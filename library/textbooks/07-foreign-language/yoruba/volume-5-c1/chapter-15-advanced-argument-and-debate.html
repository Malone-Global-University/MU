Advanced Argument and Debate · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Advanced Precision, Interpretation, and Specialization

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Build a structured argument with a clear position, supporting reasons, and organized evidence.
  2. Use concessive structures to acknowledge an opposing point before reasserting your own position.
  3. Use focus and emphasis constructions, consolidated from Chapter 6, to highlight the strongest part of an argument.
  4. Ask and respond to rhetorical questions that advance an argument rather than merely request information.
  5. Frame evidence and sources appropriately, distinguishing personal opinion from reported fact.
  6. Challenge another speaker's position politely, using hedged disagreement rather than blunt contradiction.
  7. Manage debate turn-taking: yielding the floor, holding the floor, and re-entering a discussion appropriately.
  8. Deliver and defend a short argument on a public issue in a register appropriate to a community or professional setting.

Opening Communication Scenario

The naming ceremony has passed, and the community newsletter that Adé helped Fúnmi revise in Chapter 14 has drawn attention. At the next neighborhood association meeting, a disagreement breaks out over whether the association should spend its small budget on repairing the community hall roof or on expanding the children's after-school program. Adé finds himself moderating, and Fúnmi wants to speak in favor of the after-school program without dismissing the real concerns of those who worry about the roof. This chapter equips both of them — and you — to disagree constructively: acknowledging a strong opposing point, then building a clear counter-argument supported by reasons and evidence, delivered with appropriate courtesy and confidence.

  1. This chapter builds directly on the focus and emphasis system consolidated in Chapter 6 and the register discipline practiced in Chapters 12 through 14. It does not introduce a new tense, aspect, or clause type.
  2. The productive goal is structured spoken and written argumentation: stating a position, conceding a point, providing evidence, and responding to a counter-argument, all in Standard Written/Spoken Yorùbá appropriate to a community or professional setting.
  3. Debate register in this chapter remains within ordinary public and community discourse. It does not draw on the ceremonial or oral-art registers discussed receptively in Chapter 13.

Ìpín Kìíní

Pronunciation Focus — Debate Intonation and Turn Management

Sound and Style — Holding, yielding, and re-entering a discussion

Public disagreement in Yorùbá relies on more than word choice. A speaker who wants to hold the floor slightly lengthens the final syllable before a connector rather than dropping pitch to a full stop, signaling "I am not finished." A speaker yielding the floor lets pitch fall fully and pauses, inviting the next speaker in. A speaker re-entering a discussion after being interrupted typically restates a short anchor phrase — often the concessive opener discussed in the Grammar Focus below — before continuing, rather than resuming mid-sentence as if uninterrupted.

As in Chapter 14, added emphasis for rhetorical effect must not distort the underlying tone pattern of a word. Practice holding tone accuracy steady even when raising volume or pace to make a point.

Rhetorical questions carry the same intonation contour as genuine information questions in Yorùbá; the audience recognizes a question as rhetorical from context and content, not from a different tone melody. Do not invent a special "rhetorical tone" that does not exist in natural speech.

Ìpín Kejì

Grammar Focus — Concession, Emphasis, and Evidence Framing

This section consolidates focus constructions from Chapter 6, coordination and subordination from earlier volumes, and reported-information framing from Chapter 9, applying them to structured argument. No new particle, tense, or clause type is introduced.

1. Conceding a point before reasserting your position

A concession acknowledges that an opposing view has some merit before returning to your own position. This is more persuasive than ignoring a strong counter-argument, and it signals that you have genuinely considered the other side.

Pattern: concession + reassertion
Ó tọ́ pé [opposing point] , àmọ́ / síbẹ̀ [your position]
"Ó tọ́ pé" (it is true/correct that) opens the concession. "Àmọ́" or the more formal "síbẹ̀" (nevertheless) signals the pivot back to your own reasoning.
Ó tọ́ pé òrùlé gbọ́ngàn wa nílò àtúnṣe kánkán, àmọ́ ìlọsíwájú àwọn ọmọ wa kò gbọ́dọ̀ dúró de àtúnṣe yẹn.
It's true that our hall's roof urgently needs repair, but the progress of our children shouldn't wait for that repair.
  • Bí ó tilẹ̀ jẹ́ pé ìsúná wa kéré, a lè pín in lọ́nà tí ó bọ́gbọ́n mu. Even though our budget is small, we can divide it wisely. concessive subordinator "bí ó tilẹ̀ jẹ́ pé" (even though) + reassertion clause

2. Focus for the strongest point in an argument

Chapter 6 introduced focus with ni for rhetorical emphasis. In argumentation, focus is used to place the single most important reason, participant, or consequence in the position of maximum attention.

  • Àwọn ọmọ wa ni yóò jìyà jùlọ tí a kò bá ṣe àṣàyàn tí ó tọ́. It is our children who will suffer most if we don't make the right choice. "àwọn ọmọ wa ni" = subject focus, foregrounding the party most affected

Use focus sparingly, at the point in your argument where the emphasis genuinely matters — the same selectivity principle practiced with ideophones and reduplication in Chapter 14.

3. Rhetorical questions that advance an argument

A rhetorical question does not request new information; it invites the listener to reach the speaker's intended conclusion. In Yorùbá, rhetorical questions in argument often use ordinary question forms combined with content the audience is expected to already agree with.

Pattern: rhetorical question in argument
[ordinary question form] + [content pointing to speaker's conclusion]
The grammar is identical to a genuine information question; only the shared context marks it as rhetorical.
Ǹjẹ́ a lè fi ìlọsíwájú àwọn ọmọdé wéwu fún ohun tí a lè tún tọ́jú ní ọdún tí ń bọ̀?
Can we put children's progress at risk for something we could still address next year?

4. Framing evidence and distinguishing opinion from report

Advanced argument requires the listener to know whether a claim is the speaker's own opinion, a fact the speaker is reporting from elsewhere, or a shared community observation. Chapter 9's reported-information framing is consolidated here for argumentative use.

  • Mo gbọ́ pé ìgbìmọ̀ ìlú ti sọ pé owó fún àtúnṣe gbọ̀ngàn ń bọ̀ ní oṣù mẹ́ta tó ń bọ̀. I heard that the town council has said that funding for the hall repair is coming in the next three months. "mo gbọ́ pé...ti sọ pé" frames the claim as reported, not personal opinion
  • Mo rò pé ó yẹ kí a kọ́kọ́ fi àkókò sí àwọn ọmọdé. I think we should prioritize the children first. "mo rò pé" clearly marks personal opinion, distinct from the reported claim above

5. Polite challenge: disagreeing without confrontation

Directly contradicting another speaker in a public or community setting can damage relationships and close down discussion. Yorùbá argumentative register favors hedged disagreement that questions a claim's completeness or applicability rather than its truth outright.

Pattern: hedged disagreement
Mo lóye ohun tí o ń sọ, síbẹ̀ mo rò pé...
"Mo lóye ohun tí o ń sọ" (I understand what you're saying) acknowledges the other speaker's position before "síbẹ̀ mo rò pé" (still, I think that) introduces the challenge, keeping the exchange respectful.
Mo lóye ohun tí o ń sọ, síbẹ̀ mo rò pé a kò gbọ́dọ̀ fi gbogbo owó sí ohun kan ṣoṣo.
I understand what you're saying, still I think we shouldn't put all the money into just one thing.

Avoid blunt forms such as flatly stating that another speaker is wrong (o ṣìnà used without hedging) in a formal community setting. Reserve such direct correction for contexts where directness is expected, such as close peer discussion, as established by the register policy in Chapter 2.

Model Dialogue — A Community Meeting Disagreement

Context: A neighborhood association meeting. Adé moderates as Fúnmi and Bàbá Ṣadé disagree about how to spend a small shared budget.

Bàbá Ṣadé: Ó yẹ kí a lo owó náà fún àtúnṣe òrùlé gbọ̀ngàn, torí pé bí òjò bá rọ̀, gbogbo wa ni yóò ní ìṣòro.We should use the money for the hall roof repair, because if it rains, all of us will have a problem.
Fúnmi: Ó tọ́ pé òrùlé gbọ̀ngàn wa nílò àtúnṣe kánkán, àmọ́ ǹjẹ́ a lè fi ìlọsíwájú àwọn ọmọdé wéwu fún ohun tí a lè tún tọ́jú ní ọdún tí ń bọ̀?It's true our hall's roof urgently needs repair, but can we put children's progress at risk for something we could still address next year?
Fúnmi opens with a concession before pivoting to a rhetorical question, modeling the pattern introduced in the Grammar Focus.
Bàbá Ṣadé: Mo lóye ohun tí o ń sọ, síbẹ̀ mo rò pé a kò gbọ́dọ̀ dúró tì àwọn nǹkan pàtàkì tí ó lè bàjẹ́ sí i.I understand what you're saying, still I think we shouldn't delay on important things that could get worse.
Adé: Ẹ jọ̀wọ́, ẹ jẹ́ kí á gbọ́ ẹ̀rí tí a ní lọ́wọ́ ná kí á tó pinnu. Mo gbọ́ pé ìgbìmọ̀ ìlú ti sọ pé owó fún àtúnṣe gbọ̀ngàn ń bọ̀ ní oṣù mẹ́ta tó ń bọ̀.Please, let's hear the evidence we have before we decide. I heard that the town council has said funding for the hall repair is coming in the next three months.
Adé manages the floor as moderator and introduces reported evidence, distinguishing it clearly from personal opinion.
Fúnmi: Bí ó bá rí bẹ́ẹ̀, ó ṣeé ṣe kí a lo owó tí a ní lọ́wọ́ báyìí fún ètò àwọn ọmọdé, kí a sì dúró de owó ìjọba fún òrùlé.If that's the case, we could use the money we have now for the children's program, and wait for government funding for the roof.
Bàbá Ṣadé: Ìyẹn lè ṣiṣẹ́, àmọ́ a nílò ìdánilójú pé owó ìjọba náà yóò dájú dé.That could work, but we need assurance that the government money will definitely arrive.

  1. Write a concession-and-reassertion sentence about a public issue of your choice, using ó tọ́ pé... àmọ́....

Ìpín Kẹfà

Writing Workshop

Produce a short written argument in Standard Written Yorùbá on a public issue of your choice, using the argumentative strategies from this chapter deliberately.

  1. Choose a public issue appropriate for a community, school, or workplace setting (100–150 words for the full argument).
  2. State your position clearly in an opening sentence.
  3. Concede at least one strong opposing point before presenting your reasoning.
  4. Provide at least two supporting reasons, with at least one piece of evidence clearly framed as reported information or personal opinion.
  5. Use focus once, at the point of greatest importance in your argument.
  6. Include one rhetorical question that advances your position.
  7. Close with a brief restatement of your position.
  8. Proofread using the Chapter 3 four-pass procedure before submission.
  • The argument is fully tone-marked with correct dot-under letters throughout.
  • At least one concession appears before the writer's own reasoning is reasserted.
  • Opinion and reported information are clearly and separately framed.
  • Focus is used once, at a genuinely important point, not throughout the passage.
  • Any disagreement with an implied opposing view is hedged rather than blunt.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Disagreement and relationship in community settings

In many Yorùbá community and family settings, maintaining relationships during disagreement is often valued as highly as winning the point itself. Hedged disagreement, concession, and a respected moderator's role are common features of community meetings, family discussions, and workplace disputes, though individual settings and personalities vary.

Regional Note — Variation in directness

The degree of directness considered appropriate in argument can vary by region, generation, and setting, consistent with the variation categories introduced in Chapter 12. Some professional and academic settings tolerate more direct challenge than a family or neighborhood context. Observe your specific setting rather than assuming one fixed rule for all Yorùbá-speaking contexts.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Skipping the concession and going straight to contradiction

Error: O ṣìnà. Kò sí ìdí kan tí a fi ní láti tún òrùlé ṣe ní báyìí. — a flat, unhedged contradiction with no acknowledgment of the other speaker's point.

Correction: Open with a concession such as Ó tọ́ pé... before presenting your counter-reasoning, as modeled in the Grammar Focus and the dialogue above.

Common Mistake — Using focus on every sentence instead of the strongest point

Error: Placing ni-focus in nearly every sentence of an argument, weakening its rhetorical impact through overuse.

Correction: Reserve focus for the single most important claim, echoing the selectivity principle for expressive resources practiced in Chapter 14.

Common Mistake — Blurring opinion and reported fact

Error: Presenting a personal opinion with the same framing as a reported fact, misleading the listener about the claim's source or certainty.

Correction: Use clear framing such as mo rò pé for opinion and mo gbọ́ pé...ti sọ pé for reported information, as shown in the Grammar Focus.

Chapter Summary

This chapter introduced no new tense, aspect, particle, or clause system. It consolidated focus and emphasis from Chapter 6, coordination and subordination from earlier volumes, and reported-information framing from Chapter 9 into a structured toolkit for spoken and written argumentation: concession and reassertion, selective focus on the strongest point, rhetorical questions, clear opinion-versus-report framing, and polite, hedged disagreement. Learners also practiced managing debate turn-taking through intonation, and delivering and defending a short argument on a public issue in a register appropriate to community and professional settings.

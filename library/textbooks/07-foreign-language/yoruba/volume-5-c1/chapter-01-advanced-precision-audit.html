Àyẹ̀wò Ìpéye Gíga

Advanced Precision Audit · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Advanced Precision, Interpretation, and Specialization

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Produce a diagnostic self-assessment of your own tone, orthography, particle, serial-verb, focus, and discourse control at the close of Volume IV.
  2. Identify specific patterns of tone loss, diacritic slippage, or register drift in your own speech and writing.
  3. Read and interpret a diagnostic passage that reuses the full range of consolidated Volume IV systems at higher density.
  4. Describe your own professional, academic, or community identity in Yorùbá using precise, self-selected vocabulary.
  5. Conduct a recorded accent and fluency audit and draft a personal repair plan for the volume.
  6. Distinguish which of your own errors are tone-based, orthography-based, grammar-based, or register-based.
  7. State, in functional terms, what "advanced precision" will mean for your own goals across Volume V.
  8. Select a provisional specialization domain to carry through the volume's later chapters.

Opening Communication Scenario

Adé and Kúnlé have both submitted their Volume IV capstone portfolios and received feedback from their community education network's coordinator. Before starting Volume V, the coordinator asks each of them to complete a short recorded self-audit: read a short passage aloud, describe their own background and goals, and then listen back and mark, line by line, where their tone, diacritics, or register slipped. The coordinator explains that Volume V will not introduce a large amount of brand-new grammar; instead, it will sharpen everything they already know, and that sharpening has to start with an honest, specific picture of where each learner currently stands.

  1. This chapter introduces no major new grammar. It establishes a diagnostic baseline for the whole volume.
  2. The audit covers tone, orthography, particles, serial verbs, focus, word order, noun phrases, and register together, rather than one system at a time.
  3. By the end of the chapter, you will have a recorded self-audit and a written repair plan that names your own priority areas for Volume V.

Ohùn Ìfihàn

Pronunciation Focus — Accent and Fluency Audit

Focus — Building a personal repair plan from a recorded baseline

Across Volumes I–IV, tone and vowel-quality feedback has come mostly from instructors, partners, or answer keys. Volume V asks learners to become their own primary diagnostician, since advanced and specialized Yorùbá use will often happen without anyone available to correct a slip in the moment.

  • Record yourself reading the Chapter 1 diagnostic passage aloud once, without stopping to self-correct, so the recording reflects your natural baseline rather than a rehearsed best attempt.
  • Listen back with the text in front of you and mark every syllable where the tone you produced does not match the tone marked in the text.
  • Separate two different kinds of slips: places where you know the correct tone but produced the wrong one under speaking pressure, and places where you are genuinely unsure what the correct tone is.
  • Note whether tone loss clusters at particular points, such as the ends of long sentences, transitions between clauses, or particles you rarely stop to think about.
Gírámà — Àyẹ̀wò Gbogbo Ìlànà

Grammar — Full-System Diagnostic Review

This chapter introduces no new grammar system. It requires learners to apply, in a single diagnostic pass, the full range of systems consolidated across Volumes I–IV: tone and orthography, particle precision, negation, serialization, focus, word order, noun phrase structure, and register consistency. The purpose is accurate self-labeling, not new instruction.

A Diagnostic Self-Description

Pattern

Ìdánimọ̀ mi → Ohun tí mo ti dá lórí i → Ohun tí mo ṣì fẹ́ tún wò → Àyè àkànṣe tí mo yàn

My identity → what I have already mastered → what I still need to revisit → the specialization area I have chosen. This pattern reuses subordination from Chapter 11 of Volume IV, the cause/result frames from earlier volumes, and the self-assessment register introduced in the Volume IV capstone, now applied to a personal diagnostic genre.

Orúkọ mi ni Adé, mo sì ń ṣiṣẹ́ ní ilé-iṣẹ́ kan tí ó jẹ mọ́ ìtọ́jú ìlera àwùjọ. Mo ti dá lórí kíkọ lẹ́tà aláṣẹ àti dídá ọ̀rọ̀ àti ìhùwàsí sí ara wọn, àmọ́ mo ṣì fẹ́ tún wo bí mo ṣe ń pa ohun orin ìsọ̀rọ̀ mi mọ́ nígbà tí mo bá ń dáhùn ìbéèrè lójú ẹsẹ̀. Nítorí náà, mo yàn láti fi àyè àkànṣe mi sí èdè ìlera àti ìbánisọ̀rọ̀ pẹ̀lú àwùjọ.

My name is Adé, and I work at an organization concerned with community health. I have already mastered writing formal letters and separating claim from stance, but I still want to work on how I keep my spoken tone steady when answering questions on the spot. So I have chosen to make my specialization area health language and community communication.

Naming Errors by Matrix Category

  • Àṣìṣe yìí kì í ṣe ti gírámà; ohun orin ni mo ṣì ń rẹ̀wẹ̀sì lé lórí nígbà tí ọ̀rọ̀-ìṣe náà bá gùn. This error is not a grammar error; it's tone that I still keep flattening when the verb phrase gets long. self-diagnosis + negation + subordinate cause clause, consolidated from Volumes III–IV

Precise self-diagnosis requires naming which matrix category an error belongs to. Tone Orthography Particles Register — a learner who cannot say which category an error falls into cannot build a targeted repair plan.

Recognition note — Diagnostic use, not new instruction

Every structure in this chapter's examples has been taught in an earlier volume. The task here is to apply the Chapter 18 (Volume IV) editing-checklist habit to your own spontaneous speech and writing, and to sort your own errors into tone, orthography, particle, pronoun, serialization, word-order, or register categories before Volume V begins refining any one of them in depth.

Model Dialogue

Ipò: Adé àti Kúnlé ń gbọ́ àkọsílẹ̀ ohùn ara wọn papọ̀ lẹ́yìn tí wọ́n parí àyẹ̀wò ìpéye kìíní wọn.
Setting: Adé and Kúnlé are listening back to their own recordings together after completing their first precision audit.

ADÉ Nígbà tí mo gbọ́ ohùn mi padà, mo rí i pé ohun orin mi ń rẹ̀wẹ̀sì ní gbogbo ìgbà tí gbólóhùn mi bá gùn ju ìlà kan lọ. When I listened back to myself, I noticed my tone flattens every time my sentence goes longer than one line.
KÚNLÉ Èmi náà rí irú ìṣòro kan náà, àmọ́ ti èmi kì í ṣe ti ohun orin; ó jẹ́ pé mo máa ń pa dídiwọ̀n `kò` àti `kì í` mọ́ nígbà tí mo bá ń sọ̀rọ̀ láìdúró. I found a similar problem, but mine isn't tone; it's that I mix up `kò` and `kì í` when I'm speaking without stopping.
ADÉ Ǹjẹ́ ìwọ ti kọ ètò àtúnṣe rẹ sílẹ̀, tàbí o kàn ṣàkíyèsí àṣìṣe náà lọ́rọ̀ ẹnu? Have you written down your repair plan, or did you just notice the error verbally?
KÚNLÉ Mo ti kọ ọ́ sílẹ̀: ojú tí mo ṣì fẹ́ tún wò ni ìyàtọ̀ láàrin àwọn ìró àìṣe méjì yìí, mo sì yàn láti ṣàyẹ̀wò ìlò wọn ní gbogbo ìgbà tí mo bá ń kọ̀wé. I've written it down: the area I still need to revisit is the difference between these two negation forms, and I've chosen to check my use of them every time I write.
ADÉ Kí ni àyè àkànṣe tí o yàn fún ìdìpọ̀ yìí? What specialization area have you chosen for this volume?
KÚNLÉ Mo yàn èdè iṣẹ́ ìbánisọ̀rọ̀ àti ìtàn ẹnu, nítorí pé ó bá ohun tí mo fẹ́ ṣe lọ́jọ́ iwájú mu. I've chosen media and communication language along with oral tradition, because it fits what I want to do in the future.

Writing Workshop

Produce your own diagnostic self-audit in writing. Full tone marks and dot-under letters are required throughout, as in every prior volume's formal writing tasks.

  • Write a short self-description (80–120 words) covering your identity, background, and current use of Yorùbá, following the pattern modeled in this chapter.
  • List at least three specific things you have already mastered, each labeled with a matrix category (tone, orthography, particles, negation, serialization, focus, word order, noun phrases, register, or vocabulary).
  • List at least three specific things you still want to revisit, each labeled with a matrix category and a brief example from your own speech or writing.
  • State one provisional specialization area you would like to pursue across Volume V, and explain briefly why it matters to you.
  • Record yourself reading your own self-description aloud, then listen back and mark any place where your spoken tone did not match your written tone marks.
  • Write a short repair plan (60–100 words) stating what you will specifically do, and how often, to address your top revision priority.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Self-assessment as a professional habit

    Structured self-review before beginning a new stage of study or work is common across professional, academic, and community settings where continued growth is expected rather than assumed. Naming specific strengths and gaps precisely, rather than describing performance only in general terms, is widely valued as a marker of maturity in a skill.

    Regional Note — Variation in self-assessment conventions

    How directly a learner is expected to name their own weaknesses, and how much of that self-assessment is shared publicly versus kept private, varies across institutions and regions. The pattern of pairing every named strength with a named area for growth introduced in this chapter is a widely applicable habit rather than a single fixed rule.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Naming an error without a matrix category

    Error: Describing a problem only as "something is wrong" without identifying whether it is a tone, orthography, particle, negation, serialization, focus, word-order, or register issue.

    Correction: Name the category explicitly, for example àṣìṣe ohun orin ni, kì í ṣe ti gírámà ("this is a tone error, not a grammar error"), so the repair plan can target it precisely.

    Common Mistake — A repair plan with no specific action or frequency

    Error: Writing a repair plan that only states an intention, such as "I will try to do better with tone," without a concrete method or schedule.

    Correction: State a specific checking behavior and how often it will happen, following the pattern Kúnlé uses in this chapter's model dialogue: naming the exact confusion and a concrete self-check habit.

    Independent Study Tools

    Fún akẹ́kọ̀ọ́ tí ń kọ́ ẹ̀kọ́ fúnra rẹ̀ — For the independent learner

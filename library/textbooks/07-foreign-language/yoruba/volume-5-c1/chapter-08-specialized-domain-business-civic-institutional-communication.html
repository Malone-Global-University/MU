Specialized Domain II: Business, Civic, and Institutional Communication · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Advanced Precision, Interpretation, and Specialization

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Recognize the structural features of Yorùbá professional, civic, and institutional documentation, including memos, notices, and formal proposals.
  2. Use obligation language to state requirements, duties, and expectations with appropriate formal weight.
  3. Use conditional structures to present terms, conditions, and contingencies in a negotiation or agreement.
  4. Draft a short professional briefing appropriate to a business, civic, or institutional audience.
  5. Adapt the same information for two different audiences — an internal colleague and an external official body.
  6. Apply the hedging and synthesis strategies from Chapter 7 within a business or civic register.
  7. Distinguish formal institutional register from both the academic register of Chapter 7 and the public rhetorical register of Chapter 6.
  8. Produce a professional briefing that states obligations, conditions, and audience-appropriate recommendations.

Opening Communication Scenario

Adé's research brief from Chapter 7 has caught the attention of a community development office, which now wants a different kind of document: a professional briefing recommending whether the local council should fund a youth mentorship program addressing the greeting-practice concerns Adé identified. This is not an academic brief. The council needs to know exactly what is required of them, under what conditions funding should proceed, and what obligations the mentorship program would carry if approved. Adé must state duties plainly, lay out conditions clearly, and write two versions of the same recommendation: one internal memo for the office director, and one formal notice suitable for the council itself. The goal is no longer scholarly caution alone; it is precision about who must do what, and under what terms.

  1. This chapter treats business, civic, and institutional writing as a register built on stated obligations and explicit conditions, not on persuasion alone.
  2. You will practice obligation particles, conditional clauses, and audience-specific adaptation of the same content.
  3. By the end of the chapter, you will be able to produce a professional briefing appropriate for both an internal and an external institutional audience.

Pronunciation Focus — Formal Meeting and Briefing Delivery

Focus — Firm, deliberate delivery for obligation and conditional statements

Formal meeting and briefing delivery differs from both the cautious pacing of academic register in Chapter 7 and the persuasive emphasis of Chapter 6. When stating an obligation with ó gbọ́dọ̀, learners should give the phrase firm, even weight rather than rushing past it, since obligation language carries institutional authority and should not sound tentative.

  • Give the obligation particle gbọ́dọ̀ a clear, unhurried pronunciation, since flattening its tone can make a requirement sound like a mere suggestion.
  • When reading a conditional clause aloud, place a brief pause after the condition itself, before the main clause, so listeners register the two-part structure of the sentence.
  • Keep the pace measured and deliberate throughout a briefing; obligation and condition language benefits from clarity over speed.
  • When addressing an external institutional audience, adopt a slightly more formal, level pitch than when addressing an internal colleague, reflecting the audience distinction practiced later in this chapter.

Grammar — Obligation, Conditions, and Professional Documentation

This chapter introduces business, civic, and institutional discourse structure — obligation, conditional terms, and audience-specific adaptation — as a formal register built on the hedging and synthesis strategies consolidated in Chapter 7 and the focus systems from Chapter 6. It does not introduce a new tone, orthography, or core clause system.

Stating Obligation

Pattern

[Ẹni tí ó ní ojúṣe] ó gbọ́dọ̀ [ìṣe tí a ń béèrè], kí [àbájáde tàbí ìdí].

A formal obligation statement names who carries the duty, uses the obligation particle "gbọ́dọ̀" to mark the requirement as binding rather than optional, and states the expected action along with its purpose or consequence. This distinguishes an institutional requirement from the modal suggestions used at less formal levels.

Ọ́fíìsì náà ó gbọ́dọ̀ gbé ìròyìn ìnáwó kalẹ̀ ṣáájú ìpàdé ìgbìmọ̀ ìlú, kí àwọn mẹ́ńbà lè ṣàyẹ̀wò rẹ̀ dáadáa.

The office must present the financial report before the council meeting, so that members can review it properly. Notice that "gbọ́dọ̀" marks the requirement as binding, and the purpose clause explains why the obligation exists.

Adapting the Same Content for Two Audiences

  • (Sí olùdarí ọ́fíìsì — internal memo) A rò pé ó yẹ kí a fowo si ètò náà, ṣùgbọ́n a nílò láti mọ irú owó tí a ní lọ́wọ́ ṣáájú. (To the office director — internal memo) We think it would be advisable to approve the program, but we need to know what funds are available beforehand. softened obligation ("yẹ kí") + internal, collegial tone using "a" (we)
  • (Sí ìgbìmọ̀ ìlú — formal notice) Ọ́fíìsì ìdàgbàsókè àwùjọ ó gbọ́dọ̀ gbé ìmọ̀ràn kalẹ̀ fún ìgbìmọ̀ náà, àti pé bí ìgbìmọ̀ náà bá fọwọ́sí i, gbogbo ìlànà tí a ti kọ sílẹ̀ ní ìdáwọ́lé ìnáwó gbọ́dọ̀ di ìmúṣẹ. (To the local council — formal notice) The community development office must present the recommendation to the council, and if the council approves it, all terms written into the funding proposal must be fulfilled. firm obligation ("gbọ́dọ̀") + conditional clause + formal, institutional third-person distance

The same underlying recommendation changes shape depending on audience: a memo to a colleague may soften obligation and speak collegially as "we," while a formal notice to a council states obligations directly and refers to institutions by name rather than by shared identity.

Structuring a Professional Briefing

Pattern

Ìṣòro / Ipò → Ìmọ̀ràn tí a gbé kalẹ̀ → Ojúṣe àwọn ẹgbẹ́ → Ìlànà àti ìdí.

A professional briefing states the situation, puts forward a recommendation, assigns obligations to specific parties, and lays out the conditions and reasons attached to the recommendation. This four-part structure keeps a briefing organized and audience-appropriate, whether internal or external.

Ìpò náà ni pé ìkíni ń yí padà láàrín àwọn ọ̀dọ́ ìlú ńlá. Ìmọ̀ràn tí a gbé kalẹ̀ ni pé kí a dá ètò amọ̀nà ọ̀dọ́ sílẹ̀. Ọ́fíìsì ìdàgbàsókè ó gbọ́dọ̀ darí ètò náà, tí ìgbìmọ̀ ìlú bá sì fọwọ́sí ìnáwó náà, ẹgbẹ́ àwọn àgbà ìdílé gbọ́dọ̀ kópa nínú ìkọ́nilẹ́kọ̀ọ́ ọ̀sọ̀ọ̀sẹ̀.

The situation is that greeting practices are changing among urban young people. The recommendation put forward is that a youth mentorship program be established. The development office must direct the program, and if the council approves the funding, the group of family elders must participate in weekly instruction. Notice how each of the four parts of the pattern appears in sequence.

As in Chapter 7, precision about certainty still matters here: a briefing should state obligations and conditions plainly, while reserving hedging language for any part of the recommendation that remains uncertain, such as projected outcomes rather than stated requirements.

Model Dialogue

Ipò: Adé àti Olùdarí Ọ́fíìsì, Ìyá Adùké, ń jíròrò báwo ni a ṣe lè gbé ìmọ̀ràn ètò amọ̀nà ọ̀dọ́ kalẹ̀ fún ìgbìmọ̀ ìlú.
Setting: Adé and Office Director Ìyá Adùké are discussing how to present the youth mentorship program recommendation to the local council.

ÌYÁ ADÙKÉ Adé, ṣé o ti pinnu ohun tí ìgbìmọ̀ ìlú gbọ́dọ̀ ṣe kí a tó gba owó náà? Adé, have you decided what the local council must do before we receive the funding?
ADÉ Bẹ́ẹ̀ ni. Bí ìgbìmọ̀ náà bá fọwọ́sí ìdáwọ́lé ìnáwó, ọ́fíìsì wa ó gbọ́dọ̀ gbé ìlànà iṣẹ́ mẹ́ta kalẹ̀ láàrín oṣù kan. Yes. If the council approves the funding proposal, our office must present a three-part work plan within one month.
ÌYÁ ADÙKÉ Àyàfi tí a bá ti rí àwọn àgbà tí wọ́n fẹ́ kópa, a kò lè bẹ̀rẹ̀ ìkọ́nilẹ́kọ̀ọ́ náà. Ṣé o ti kàn wọ́n? Unless we have found elders who are willing to participate, we cannot begin the instruction. Have you contacted them?
ADÉ Mo ti bá àwọn mẹ́rin sọ̀rọ̀ tẹ́lẹ̀, wọ́n sì gbà. Ṣùgbọ́n mo rò pé ó yẹ kí a fi ìdí rẹ̀ mulẹ̀ nínú àkọsílẹ̀ iṣẹ́ wa fún ọ, kí ìgbìmọ̀ ìlú má bàa rí i nínú ìtọ́nisọ́nà ìlànà ṣáájú àkókò. I have already spoken with four of them, and they agreed. But I think we should confirm it in our internal memo to you first, so the council doesn't see it in the formal notice before its time.
ÌYÁ ADÙKÉ Ìyẹn dára. Nínú ìtọ́nisọ́nà tí a fẹ́ fi ránṣẹ́ sí ìgbìmọ̀, sọ ọ̀rọ̀ náà ní ọ̀nà tí ó bọ́gbọ́n mu, kì í ṣe bí i "a" ṣùgbọ́n bí i "ọ́fíìsì ìdàgbàsókè àwùjọ." That's good. In the notice we intend to send to the council, phrase things properly — not like "we," but like "the community development office."
ADÉ Mo yé, Ìyá Adùké. Ǹjẹ́ ó yẹ kí n kọ ìdí tí a fi ń fẹ́ ètò yìí sínú ìtọ́nisọ́nà náà pẹ̀lú, tàbí kí n fi sí àkọsílẹ̀ iṣẹ́ nìkan? I understand, Ìyá Adùké. Should I also write the reason we want this program into the formal notice, or should I keep it only in the memo?
ÌYÁ ADÙKÉ Kọ ọ́ sínú méjèèjì, ṣùgbọ́n ní ṣókí nínú ìtọ́nisọ́nà, kí ó sì gùn díẹ̀ nínú àkọsílẹ̀ iṣẹ́, nítorí pé a mọ̀ ara wa dáadáa níbẹ̀. Write it in both, but briefly in the notice, and let it be a bit longer in the memo, since we know each other well there.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Institutional address and collective responsibility

    In many Yorùbá civic and organizational settings, formal communication to a council, board, or institution tends to name the responsible office or body directly rather than relying on informal collective pronouns, reflecting a broader value placed on clear institutional accountability. This does not mean personal relationships are absent from institutional life; rather, the register shifts depending on whether the audience is a known colleague or a formal body, exactly as practiced in this chapter.

    Regional Note — Institutional Yorùbá varies by organization and setting

    Business, civic, and institutional register, terminology, and documentation conventions can vary across organizations, local councils, and professional settings, and some institutions borrow more English administrative terminology than others. The dialogue and reading passage in this chapter are adapted, composite illustrations for instructional purposes and should not be treated as a single fixed or universal account of Yorùbá institutional writing conventions.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Softening a firm institutional obligation

    Error: Writing Ó yẹ kí ọ́fíìsì náà gbé ètò iṣẹ́ kalẹ̀ in a formal notice where a binding requirement is intended, using the softer "yẹ kí" where "gbọ́dọ̀" is required.

    Correction: Write Ọ́fíìsì náà ó gbọ́dọ̀ gbé ètò iṣẹ́ kalẹ̀ when the requirement is binding and non-negotiable, reserving "yẹ kí" for genuinely optional or advisory recommendations, exactly as distinguished in the Grammar section.

    Common Mistake — Using the same register for internal and external audiences

    Error: Addressing a formal notice to the council using collegial "a" (we) language identical to an internal memo, as though the council shared the office's internal familiarity.

    Correction: Shift to institutional third-person reference for the external notice, naming the office or body directly, following the audience-adaptation pattern shown in the Grammar section and Model Dialogue.

    Chapter Summary

    This chapter introduced Yorùbá business, civic, and institutional discourse as a distinct formal register, developing the ability to state obligations plainly, present conditional terms clearly, and adapt the same content for internal and external institutional audiences. No new tone, orthography, or core clause system was introduced; the chapter built institutional register control on systems already consolidated through Volume IV and earlier Volume V chapters.

    • A 100–150 word internal memo stating the situation, a softened or firm obligation as appropriate, and a conditional term, using collegial address, fully proofread using the Chapter 3 procedure.

    Component 2 — Formal Notice and Spoken Briefing

    • A 100–150 word formal notice adapting the same content for an institutional audience, plus a one-minute spoken briefing delivered at a measured, deliberate pace with firm stress on obligation phrases.

    Assessment note: This task builds on the editing precision developed in Chapter 3, the focus systems developed in Chapter 6, and the hedging and synthesis strategies developed in Chapter 7, and will be referenced again during the Unit 2 checkpoint at Chapter 10.

    Answer Key

    Listening Practice Questions

    1. If the council approves the funding, the community development office must present a work plan within one month.
    2. They identified that the elders must approve their participation before instruction can begin at all.
    3. Ìyá Adùké says the notice must differ because the council does not know the office's internal history as well as the office's own staff do.

    Reading Practice Questions

    1. The sentence is: "Bí ìgbìmọ̀ náà bá fọwọ́sí ìdáwọ́lé ìnáwó yìí, ọ́fíìsì náà ó gbọ́dọ̀ gbé ètò iṣẹ́ mẹ́ta kalẹ̀ láàrín oṣù kan" (If the council approves this funding proposal, the office must present a three-part work plan within one month).
    2. The precondition is that the four elders who were consulted must approve their participation in writing; it is marked with the exceptive conditional "àyàfi tí... bá."
    3. The softened obligation is: "Ó yẹ kí ìgbìmọ̀ ìlú fún ọ́fíìsì yìí ní ìdáhùn láàrín ọ̀sẹ̀ méjì" (The council should give this office a response within two weeks); it is weaker than "gbọ́dọ̀" because it recommends rather than binds the council to act.

    Guided Practice — Sample Response

    4. Sample response: Ẹ̀dà tí ó fẹ́ẹ́rẹ́ fún ẹgbẹ́ inú ọ́fíìsì ni: "A rò pé ó yẹ kí a gbé ètò iṣẹ́ kalẹ̀ láàrín oṣù kan." Ẹ̀dà tí ó le jù fún ìgbìmọ̀ ìlú ni: "Ọ́fíìsì náà ó gbọ́dọ̀ gbé ètò iṣẹ́ kalẹ̀ láàrín oṣù kan," nítorí pé ìgbìmọ̀ náà nílò ìdáhùn tí ó dájú, kì í ṣe àbá.

    Independent Study Tools

    Fún akẹ́kọ̀ọ́ tí ń kọ́ ẹ̀kọ́ fúnra rẹ̀ — For the independent learner

    • Collect three obligation or conditional statements you encounter in institutional writing, and evaluate whether each names a responsible party clearly.
    • When reading a memo or notice, note whether the register matches the audience — internal and collegial, or external and institutional.
    • Practice writing one briefing per week on a civic or workplace topic that interests you, checking that the four-part structure (situation, recommendation, obligation, condition) is present.
    • Continue applying the Chapter 3 four-pass editing procedure to any Yorùbá writing you produce, including business and civic texts.

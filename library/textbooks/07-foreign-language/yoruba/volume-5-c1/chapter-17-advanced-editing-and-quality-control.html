Advanced Editing and Quality Control · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Advanced Precision, Interpretation, and Specialization

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Diagnose an error in a Yorùbá text by matrix category — tone, orthography, particles, pronouns, serialization, focus, syntax, or register — rather than labeling it only as "wrong."
  2. Explain why a given correction is required, using the relevant grammar or orthography rule rather than intuition alone.
  3. Apply the extended four-pass proofreading procedure from Chapters 3 and 16 to a text written by someone else.
  4. Distinguish an error from a stylistic choice, and a stylistic weakness from a genuine grammatical error.
  5. Give peer feedback that names the category of an issue, explains the rule, and suggests a specific revision, without rewriting the author's ideas for them.
  6. Receive and respond to feedback on your own writing by revising rather than only acknowledging the comment.
  7. Read a text aloud specifically to test tone and phrasing accuracy as a step in quality control, consolidating pronunciation skills from earlier volumes.
  8. Prepare a text for publication-level readiness by completing a full editing pass across all matrix categories.

Opening Communication Scenario

Fúnmi's community report from Chapter 16 impressed Adé enough that he has asked her to help proofread other members' newsletter submissions before they go out. Fúnmi realizes quickly that "check it for mistakes" is not a single skill — a wrong tone mark, a missing dot-under letter, a misplaced particle, and an unclear pronoun reference are different kinds of problems, and pointing at a sentence and saying "this sounds off" does not help the writer understand what to change. She needs a method for naming what kind of problem she is looking at, explaining the rule behind it, and suggesting a fix — without simply rewriting someone else's report in her own words.

  1. This chapter shifts from producing and revising your own writing (Chapter 16) to diagnosing errors in any text — your own or someone else's — by matrix category.
  2. No new tense, aspect, particle, or clause type is introduced. This chapter organizes and applies systems already consolidated through Chapter 16.
  3. The Chapter 3 four-pass proofreading procedure, already extended to whole documents in Chapter 16, is now extended further into a shared diagnostic vocabulary for giving and receiving feedback.

Ìpín Kìíní

Pronunciation Focus — Proofreading Aloud

Sound and Style — Reading someone else's draft aloud

Chapter 16 established that reading your own draft aloud reveals problems silent reading misses. Reading someone else's draft aloud as a reviewer is even more revealing, because you have no memory of what the writer intended — you can only respond to what is actually on the page. If a sentence's tone contour does not resolve naturally when spoken, or a phrase forces an unnatural pause, that is diagnostic evidence, not a matter of taste.

When proofreading aloud for someone else, read the full sentence once at a natural pace before stopping to mark anything. Marking every hesitation mid-sentence trains you to hear individual words rather than the sentence's overall tone and rhythm, which is what a reader will actually experience.

As in Chapters 15 and 16, tone accuracy is hardest to sustain over a long stretch of speech. When proofreading aloud, listen specifically for places where tone flattens toward the end of a sentence or paragraph — this is often where a writer's own attention had also begun to drift while drafting.

Grammar Focus — Diagnosing Errors by Matrix Category

This section organizes systems consolidated across all five volumes — tone, orthography, particles, pronouns, serialization, focus, syntax, and register — into a shared diagnostic framework for identifying and explaining errors. No new grammar system is introduced.

1. Eight matrix categories for diagnosis

An effective reviewer names the category of a problem before proposing a fix. The eight categories below cover the great majority of issues in an advanced learner's extended writing.

Diagnostic categories, what each one covers, and a brief example
CategoryCoversExample of the issue
Ohùn (Tone)Wrong or missing tone mark changing or obscuring meaningwrote "owo" where "owó" (money) or "òwò" (trade) was intended
Àmì-àyà (Orthography)Dot-under letters, capitalization, punctuation, spacingwrote "eko" where "ẹ̀kọ́" (lesson) was intended
Particles (TAM)Wrong or missing tense-aspect-mood particle, or wrong scopeused bare verb where ti (perfective) was needed to show a completed event
Pronouns / ReferenceUnclear, missing, or inconsistent pronoun referencea pronoun several sentences from its noun with no restated reference
SerializationSerial verb sequence forced into English-style coordination, or argument-sharing brokenoveruse of a connector where a serial verb chain was more natural
FocusMisused or missing ni focus construction, or focus that destabilizes word orderflat declarative used where contrastive focus was the intended meaning
Syntax / Word orderParticle, object, or adverbial placed outside its authorized clause positionadverbial phrase inserted between verb and object where it does not belong
RegisterFormality, audience, or genre mismatchcolloquial expression used inside a formal institutional report

2. The diagnostic comment: category, rule, fix

A usable revision comment has three parts: it names the category, states the rule or reason briefly, and proposes or requests a specific fix. A comment that only says a sentence is wrong gives the writer nothing to act on.

Pattern: a three-part diagnostic comment
Irú àṣìṣe: [category] | Ìdí: [rule, one sentence] | Àbá àtúnṣe: [specific fix]
Category names what kind of problem it is; the rule explains why it is a problem; the proposed fix gives the writer something concrete to change.
Irú àṣìṣe: Ohùn. Ìdí: " найдено" àti "kò rí i" ní ohùn yàtọ̀ sí "kò rí í" ní ìtumọ̀. Àbá àtúnṣe: Fi àmì ohùn síwájú sí i, kí ó jẹ́ "kò rí í."
Category: Tone. Rule: the marked and unmarked forms carry different meanings. Proposed fix: add the tone mark so it reads correctly.

3. Error versus stylistic choice

Not every unusual sentence is an error. A grammatically correct sentence written in an unexpected style, register, or word order may reflect a deliberate choice rather than a mistake. A reviewer must distinguish a genuine rule violation, which requires correction, from a stylistic weakness, which may only need a suggestion, from a stylistic choice, which may need no comment at all.

  • "Owó ni a nílò, kì í ṣe àwáwí" — fronting "owó" with "ni" for contrastive focus is a deliberate rhetorical choice, not an error, if the writer intends emphasis. "It's money we need, not excuses" — focus construction used for rhetorical effect. correct focus construction; a stylistic choice, not an error

When uncertain whether something is an error or a choice, a reviewer should ask the writer what meaning was intended, rather than assuming a rule violation.

4. Applying the extended four-pass procedure to another writer's text

The Chapter 3 four-pass procedure — tone, dot-under spelling, grammar, and overall clarity — extended in Chapter 16 to whole-document organization, now becomes a shared tool a reviewer applies to someone else's draft, pass by pass, marking each issue with a category before moving to the next pass.

Pattern: four-pass peer review
Ìwò 1: Ohùn | Ìwò 2: Àmì-àyà | Ìwò 3: Gírámà àti Ìlò-ọ̀rọ̀ | Ìwò 4: Ìṣètò àti Ìdàpọ̀
Each pass isolates one category so the reviewer does not conflate a tone problem with a grammar problem or an organizational one.

5. Responding to feedback by revising

Receiving a diagnostic comment is only useful if it leads to a specific revision. An author's response should state what was changed and, where relevant, why the original was written that way, rather than only thanking the reviewer.

  • Mo yí "kò rí i" padà sí "kò rí í" gẹ́gẹ́ bí o ṣe dámọ̀ràn, nítorí pé mo fẹ́ sọ pé kò rí ohun kan pàtó, kì í ṣe pé kò rí lápapọ̀. I changed "kò rí i" to "kò rí í" as you suggested, because I meant that he didn't see a specific thing, not that he couldn't see at all. response names the change made and the intended meaning behind the original choice

Model Dialogue — Peer Feedback on a Draft

Context: Fúnmi reviews a newsletter submission drafted by another community member, Tọ́pẹ́, and gives him feedback in person.

Tọ́pẹ́: Ṣé o ti wo àkọsílẹ̀ mi? Kí ni ó dàbí?Have you looked at my draft? What does it look like?
Fúnmi: Mo ti wò ó lẹ́ẹ̀mẹ́rin, ìwò kọ̀ọ̀kan fún irú àṣìṣe kan. Ní ìwò kìíní, mo rí àṣìṣe ohùn méjì.I've looked at it four times, one pass for each type of issue. In the first pass, I found two tone errors.
Fúnmi begins by naming her method — four passes, each for one category — before listing specific issues.
Tọ́pẹ́: Ibo ni wọ́n wà?Where are they?
Fúnmi: Ní gbolóhùn kejì, o kọ "owo" láìsí àmì ohùn, àmọ́ o túmọ̀ sí "owó," owó ìnáwó. Fi àmì náà síi.In the second sentence, you wrote "owo" without a tone mark, but you meant "owó," money. Add the mark.
Tọ́pẹ́: Ó yé mi. Ǹjẹ́ ìsọ̀rí ọ̀rọ̀ kẹta dára?I understand. Is the third paragraph okay?
Fúnmi: Gírámà rẹ̀ dára, àmọ́ ó ní àṣìṣe ìtọ́kasí: o lo "wọ́n" ní ìsọ̀rí ọ̀rọ̀ yẹn láìsí sọ ẹni tí "wọ́n" ń tọ́ka sí mọ́. Ẹ jọ̀wọ́ tún dárúkọ ìgbìmọ̀ náà kí o tó lo "wọ́n" lẹ́ẹ̀kansí.Its grammar is fine, but it has a reference error: you use "wọ́n" in that paragraph without stating anymore who "wọ́n" refers to. Please name the committee again before using "wọ́n" again.
Tọ́pẹ́: Mo lóye. Ǹjẹ́ èyí jẹ́ àṣìṣe pàtàkì, tàbí àṣàyàn ọ̀nà-kíkọ̀wé ni?I understand. Is that a real error, or is it a stylistic choice?
Fúnmi: Àṣìṣe ni, kì í ṣe àṣàyàn, nítorí pé olùkàwé kò lè mọ ẹni tí a ń sọ̀rọ̀ nípa rẹ̀ mọ́.It's an error, not a choice, because the reader can no longer tell who is being discussed.

Guided Practice

Complete these tasks using only systems consolidated through Chapter 16, applied to error diagnosis.

  1. Given a short sentence with a missing tone mark, name the category, state the rule, and propose the fix using the three-part comment pattern.
  2. Given a paragraph with an unclear pronoun reference, identify the sentence where the reference breaks down and propose a specific fix.
  3. Given two sentences joined awkwardly with an explicit connector, rewrite them using a serial verb construction where appropriate, and explain why the serial form is more natural.
  4. Given one sentence written in a colloquial register inside a formal paragraph, identify the register mismatch and propose a formal alternative.
  5. Identify one sentence that is unusual in structure but not actually an error, and explain why it should be left as a stylistic choice.

Writing Workshop

Exchange a completed long-form text with a partner (or work with your own Chapter 16 draft after a delay) and produce a full editing pass using the matrix-category framework from this chapter.

  1. Select a completed multi-paragraph text: your own from Chapter 16, or a partner's draft.
  2. Complete Pass 1 (tone) and list every issue found, tagged by category.
  3. Complete Pass 2 (dot-under orthography and Unicode accuracy) and list every issue found.
  4. Complete Pass 3 (particles, pronouns, serialization, focus, and syntax) and list every issue found.
  5. Complete Pass 4 (organization, coherence, and register) and list every issue found.
  6. Write a three-part diagnostic comment (category, rule, fix) for at least six issues across the four passes.
  7. If working with a partner, exchange comments and revise your own text in response to the comments you receive.
  8. Keep a short revision log noting what was changed in response to each comment.
  9. Read the finished, corrected text aloud as a final check before considering it publication-ready.
  • Every issue found is tagged with one of the eight matrix categories.
  • Every comment states the rule, not only the correction.
  • At least one genuine stylistic choice is identified and left uncorrected, with reasoning given.
  • The revision log records what changed and why for each comment addressed.
  • The final text has been read aloud as a last quality-control step.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Editorial roles in community publishing

Community newsletters, congregation bulletins, school publications, and organizational reports in many Yorùbá-speaking contexts are commonly reviewed by a trusted member before circulation, often someone respected for careful language use rather than someone holding a formal editorial title. This informal editorial role carries real responsibility: a reviewer's comments shape how the community receives written information, so care and specificity in feedback are valued alongside honesty.

Regional Note — Diacritic conventions in casual proofreading

Consistent with the digital and dialect variation discussed in Chapters 3 and 12, some casual or quickly circulated community writing reduces tone marks and dot-under letters, while formal publications typically restore full diacritics before release. When proofreading, confirm which standard the specific publication expects rather than assuming one fixed rule applies everywhere.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Flagging a sentence as "wrong" without naming a category

Error: Writing a comment such as "this sentence is awkward" without specifying whether the issue is tone, orthography, grammar, reference, or register.

Correction: Use the three-part comment pattern from the Grammar Focus: name the category, state the rule, and propose a specific fix.

Common Mistake — Treating every unusual sentence as an error

Error: Correcting a deliberate focus construction, expressive form, or stylistic word order as though it were a grammatical mistake.

Correction: Ask what meaning the writer intended before assuming a rule violation, and leave genuine stylistic choices uncorrected, as modeled in the Grammar Focus.

Common Mistake — Rewriting the author's sentence instead of proposing a fix

Error: Replacing an entire sentence with the reviewer's own preferred wording rather than pointing to the specific problem.

Correction: Identify the specific error and propose a targeted fix, leaving the author's remaining wording and ideas intact wherever they are not the source of the problem.

Independent Study Tools

Fún akẹ́kọ̀ọ́ tí ń kọ́ ẹ̀kọ́ fúnra rẹ̀ — For the independent learner

  • Set aside one of your own earlier chapter writing tasks for at least a week, then review it cold using the four-pass procedure and the eight matrix categories, as if it were someone else's text.

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Assemble the components built in Chapters 16 through 18 — a long-form written text, an edited and matrix-diagnosed revision, and a domain glossary, report, and two audience-adapted presentations — into one coherent capstone portfolio.
  2. Identify remaining gaps across the portfolio's components and prioritize a targeted repair plan rather than re-editing every component equally.
  3. Apply the four-pass proofreading procedure and matrix-category diagnosis from Chapter 17 to the portfolio as a unified whole, not only to its individual parts.
  4. Rehearse the oral components of the portfolio — including translation-aware commentary and audience-adapted presentation — for delivery fluency, tone accuracy, and register control under time constraints.
  5. Write a short reflective statement connecting the portfolio's components to specific systems consolidated across Volume V.
  6. Plan the structure and timing of the Chapter 20 capstone oral defense.
  7. Give and receive structured peer or self-feedback on a rehearsed portfolio component, using matrix categories rather than vague impressions.

Opening Communication Scenario

Fúnmi now has several finished pieces of work scattered across her notebooks and files: the long-form essay she drafted and revised in Chapter 16, the edited passage she diagnosed line by line in Chapter 17, and the glossary, report, and two presentation outlines she built for the education committee in Chapter 18. Adé tells her that none of this is wasted — it is, in fact, almost the entire capstone. What remains is not more writing from scratch, but assembly: choosing what belongs in the final portfolio, repairing the weakest spots first, rehearsing the spoken components until they are fluent under time pressure, and writing a short reflection that shows she understands why each piece demonstrates what it demonstrates.

  1. This chapter introduces no new tense, aspect, particle, or clause system. It is a studio chapter: assembly, targeted repair, rehearsal, and reflection.
  2. The portfolio draws directly on Chapter 16 (long-form writing), Chapter 17 (editing and matrix-category diagnosis), and Chapter 18 (the domain glossary, report, and two presentations).
  3. Chapter 19 prepares every component that Chapter 20's capstone performance and oral defense will draw upon.

Ìpín Kìíní

Pronunciation Focus — Final Delivery Repair

Sound and Style — Repairing pronunciation under time pressure

Rehearsing a spoken piece against a time limit tends to expose two separate problems: places where tone accuracy breaks down when a presenter speeds up, and places where a presenter slows to a crawl on a term they still find difficult. Both problems call for the same remedy used throughout this volume — isolate the difficult word or phrase, restore full tone accuracy at a slow, deliberate pace, then reintegrate it into the timed rehearsal rather than avoiding it.

A useful studio practice is the double rehearsal: deliver the piece once without a timer, marking every place where tone, vowel quality, or fluency felt unstable; then deliver it again against the actual time limit, focusing only on the marked places rather than re-monitoring the entire piece equally. This mirrors the matrix-category diagnosis used in writing, applied to spoken delivery.

Grammar Focus — Assembling the Capstone Portfolio

This section organizes the assembly, repair, rehearsal, and reflection tasks that turn separate pieces of prior work into one coherent capstone portfolio. No new grammar system is introduced; this section controls how systems already consolidated through Chapter 18 are combined and finished.

1. Taking inventory of the portfolio

Before repairing anything, list what already exists and what condition it is in. A portfolio inventory names each component, its source chapter, and its current status.

2. Prioritizing targeted repair

Not every component needs equal attention. Repair the components with the clearest, most concrete gaps first — an unmarked uncertain claim, an unclear register note, an untimed presentation — rather than re-reading every finished piece from the beginning.

Pattern: a targeted repair note
Apá: [component] | Àṣìṣe: [matrix category] | Àtúnṣe tí a gbé kalẹ̀: [specific fix]
A repair note names the component, sorts the problem by matrix category as in Chapter 17, and states one concrete fix rather than a vague intention to "review it again."
Apá: Ìròyìn àyíká | Àṣìṣe: ìjẹ́rìí orísun (sourcing) | Àtúnṣe tí a gbé kalẹ̀: fi kún gbólóhùn kejì pé "gẹ́gẹ́ bí ọ̀gá ìgbìmọ̀ ti sọ," kí ó má baà dà bí ẹni pé a ti fi ìdí rẹ̀ múlẹ̀ pátápátá.
Component: domain report. Problem: sourcing. Planned fix: add "as the committee head stated" to the second sentence, so it does not appear fully confirmed.

3. Rehearsing spoken components against time

Every spoken component intended for the capstone — the translation-aware commentary style introduced in Chapter 11, the audience-adapted presentations from Chapter 18, and any narrative or argument piece drawn from earlier chapters — should be rehearsed at least twice: once untimed for content and accuracy, once timed for delivery.

  • Ìdánwò àkọ́kọ́: kà á sókè láìsí àago, kí o sì kọ àwọn ibi tí ohùn rẹ ti yí padà tàbí tí o dúró jù. First attempt: read it aloud without a timer, and note the places where your tone shifted or you paused too long. untimed accuracy pass
  • Ìdánwò kejì: lo àago gẹ́gẹ́ bí àkókò tí a yàn, kí o sì fojú sí àwọn ibi tí o ti kọ sílẹ̀ nínú ìdánwò àkọ́kọ́. Second attempt: use a timer for the allotted time, and focus on the places you noted in the first attempt. timed delivery pass, targeted focus

4. Writing the reflective statement

The reflective statement is a short piece, roughly one paragraph per major portfolio component, that names which Volume V systems the component demonstrates and briefly explains why. It is evidence of self-awareness, not a restatement of the component's content.

Pattern: one reflective statement paragraph
Apá yìí ([component]) fi hàn pé mo ti [system 1] àti [system 2], nítorí pé...
Name the component, name one or two matrix systems it demonstrates, and give a one-sentence reason drawn from the actual work.
Àkójọpọ̀ ọ̀rọ̀-àyíká mi fi hàn pé mo ti ṣàkóso ọ̀rọ̀ pàtàkì àti ìkọ àkọsílẹ̀ nípa ìforígbárí àwùjọ, nítorí pé olúkúlùkù ọ̀rọ̀ nínú rẹ̀ ní àkọsílẹ̀ tí ó ṣàlàyé bóyá ó nílò àlàyé fún àwùjọ àdúgbò.
My domain glossary shows that I have controlled specialized vocabulary and register-notation, because every term in it carries a note explaining whether it needs explanation for a community audience.

5. Structuring the Chapter 20 oral defense

The oral defense is not a new task type; it is a short spoken account of the choices made across the portfolio, drawing on the same explanatory language used throughout Volume V for editing justification and translation-aware commentary. Plan it now; deliver it in Chapter 20.

Model Dialogue — A Studio Rehearsal Session

Context: Adé listens to Fúnmi rehearse her general-audience presentation from Chapter 18 and gives structured feedback.

Adé: Ṣe kí n máa fetí sí ọ pẹ̀lú àago, tàbí kí a kọ́kọ́ ṣe é láìsí àago?Should I listen with a timer, or should we first do it without a timer?
Fúnmi: Jẹ́ ká kọ́kọ́ ṣe é láìsí àago, kí n lè rí i bóyá gbogbo ọ̀rọ̀ tó yẹ ṣì wà níbẹ̀.Let's first do it without a timer, so I can see whether all the necessary words are still there.
Fúnmi delivers the presentation once without timing herself.
Adé: Ó dára jùlọ, àmọ́ ohùn rẹ yí padà lórí ọ̀rọ̀ "àjọ ìfọwọ́sowọ́pọ̀" ní ìgbà méjì. Ìró rẹ̀ kò dúró ṣinṣin.It's quite good, but your tone shifted on the word "cooperative" twice. It wasn't stable.
Fúnmi: Mo mọ̀, ọ̀rọ̀ náà ṣì ń ṣòro fún mi díẹ̀. Jẹ́ kí n kọ́kọ́ sọ ọ́ lọ́tọ̀ọ̀tọ̀ ní bíra kí n tó tún gbogbo rẹ̀ sọ.I know, that word is still a bit difficult for me. Let me say it separately, slowly, before I say the whole thing again.
Fúnmi isolates the word, repeats it slowly with full tone accuracy, then reinserts it into the sentence.
Adé: Dára. Nísinsìnyí, jẹ́ ká lo àago fún àkókò tí a yàn, kí a rí bóyá o lè pa ìró náà mọ́ nígbà tí o bá ń yára.Good. Now, let's use the timer for the allotted time, to see whether you can keep the tone steady when you're going faster.

Speaking Practice

Tier 1 — Untimed Accuracy Rehearsal

Deliver one spoken portfolio component aloud without a timer, marking every place where tone, vowel quality, or fluency felt unstable.

Tier 2 — Timed Delivery Rehearsal

Deliver the same component again against a realistic time limit, focusing only on the places marked in Tier 1.

Tier 3 — Structured Peer or Self-Feedback

Ask a partner, or review a recording of yourself, and identify feedback by matrix category — tone, orthography read aloud, register, fluency — rather than a general impression such as "it sounded fine."

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Rehearsal and communal preparation

Public presentation, oral performance, and formal address in many Yorùbá-speaking communities are commonly prepared through rehearsal in front of trusted others — family members, colleagues, or community elders — rather than in isolation. Practicing a capstone presentation in front of a partner and receiving direct, specific feedback reflects a widely shared expectation that public speech is refined communally before it is delivered publicly, not simply performed as a private first attempt.

Regional Note — Time expectations for public speaking vary

Expectations about how strictly a speaker should keep to an allotted time can vary by setting and community. Some formal or institutional contexts expect strict adherence to a stated time limit; some community or ceremonial contexts allow more flexibility. When rehearsing for a specific real audience, confirm local expectations rather than assuming one standard applies everywhere.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Re-editing every component equally instead of prioritizing

Error: Spending equal time re-reading and revising every portfolio component, including ones that are already complete.

Correction: Use the portfolio inventory to identify the components with concrete, named gaps, and prioritize repair there, as modeled in the Grammar Focus.

Common Mistake — Rehearsing only once, without a timed pass

Error: Practicing a spoken component only in an untimed, relaxed setting and assuming performance under real time pressure will be the same.

Correction: Always follow an untimed accuracy rehearsal with a timed delivery rehearsal, as shown in the Model Dialogue and Speaking Practice.

Common Mistake — Writing a reflective statement that restates content instead of naming systems

Error: Summarizing what a component says rather than explaining what language system it demonstrates.

Correction: Follow the reflective-statement pattern in the Grammar Focus, naming one or two specific matrix systems and giving a concrete reason drawn from the actual work.

Independent Study Tools

Fún akẹ́kọ̀ọ́ tí ń kọ́ ẹ̀kọ́ fúnra rẹ̀ — For the independent learner

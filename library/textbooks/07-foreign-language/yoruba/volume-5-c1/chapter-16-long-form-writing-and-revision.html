Long-Form Writing and Revision · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Advanced Precision, Interpretation, and Specialization

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Plan an extended piece of Yorùbá writing using an outline that organizes position, reasons, evidence, and conclusion.
  2. Maintain coherence across paragraphs through reference chains, consistent pronoun use, and discourse connectors.
  3. Organize a long-form text into clearly signposted paragraphs, each carrying one main idea.
  4. Integrate reported information, personal opinion, and concession consistently across an extended text, consolidating Chapters 9 and 15.
  5. Draft, then revise a text in stages, distinguishing content revision from sentence-level editing.
  6. Apply the Chapter 3 four-pass proofreading procedure to an extended text, extending it to include organization and cohesion.
  7. Read a completed draft aloud with attention to tone accuracy and natural phrase rhythm, consolidating pronunciation systems from earlier volumes.
  8. Produce a polished, multi-paragraph Yorùbá text suitable for a report, extended commentary, or project submission.

Opening Communication Scenario

The neighborhood association from Chapter 15 has asked Fúnmi to prepare a short written report summarizing the meeting's decision and explaining the reasoning behind it, for circulation to residents who could not attend. What began as a two-sentence request from Adé has grown into a task Fúnmi has not done before in Yorùbá: organizing several paragraphs of connected argument and explanation into a single coherent document, rather than speaking a few sentences at a time. She realizes that writing at length requires more than knowing individual sentence patterns — it requires planning, clear paragraph organization, and a final revision pass that checks the whole document, not just isolated lines.

  1. This chapter builds directly on the argumentation toolkit from Chapter 15 and the reported-information framing from Chapter 9. It does not introduce a new tense, aspect, or clause type.
  2. The productive goal is planning, drafting, and revising an extended written text — several connected paragraphs — rather than isolated sentences.
  3. This chapter extends the Chapter 3 four-pass proofreading procedure to the paragraph and whole-document level, adding checks for coherence and organization.

Ìpín Kìíní

Pronunciation Focus — Reading Drafts Aloud

Sound and Style — Hearing your own writing

Reading a draft aloud is one of the most reliable ways to catch problems that silent reading misses: a missing connector, a sentence that runs too long, a reference chain that has become unclear, or a tone mark that does not match the word you intended. When you read your own draft aloud, read at a natural, unhurried pace and let each sentence's tone contour come through fully, rather than rushing to finish.

Pay particular attention to sentence-final particles and connectors at paragraph boundaries — these carry the tone patterns most likely to be flattened when a writer reads quickly. If a phrase feels awkward to say aloud, it is often a sign that the written sentence needs revision, not only that the reader needs more practice.

As in Chapter 15, maintaining tone accuracy under the pressure of a long text is more difficult than maintaining it in a single sentence. Practice reading a full paragraph aloud without letting tone flatten toward the end.

Ìpín Kejì

Grammar Focus — Coherence, Reference, and Revision

This section consolidates pronoun and reference systems from earlier volumes, discourse connectors, and the argumentation and evidence-framing patterns from Chapter 15, applying them at paragraph and whole-document scale. No new particle, tense, or clause type is introduced.

1. Planning before drafting

An extended text is easier to write, and far easier to revise, when it follows a simple plan before any sentences are written. A basic plan for a report or extended commentary states the opening position, lists supporting reasons in order, notes where evidence or concession will appear, and states the intended conclusion.

Pattern: a simple four-part writing plan
Ìpele 1: Ìpolongo | Ìpele 2: Àwọn ìdí | Ìpele 3: Ẹ̀rí àti ìjẹ́wọ́-apá-kan | Ìpele 4: Àkópọ̀
Stage 1 states the position; Stage 2 lists reasons; Stage 3 places evidence and any concession; Stage 4 restates the position as a conclusion.
Ìpolongo: Ó yẹ kí a pín ìsúná wa sí ìdajì méjì. Àwọn ìdí: (1) òrùlé nílò àtúnṣe kánkán, (2) ìlọsíwájú àwọn ọmọ kò gbọ́dọ̀ dúró.
Position: We should divide our budget into two halves. Reasons: (1) the roof needs urgent repair, (2) the children's progress should not wait.

2. Reference chains across paragraphs

In a single sentence, a pronoun's reference is usually clear from context. Across several paragraphs, a writer must actively manage which noun a pronoun points back to, so the reader is never left uncertain who or what is being discussed.

  • Ìgbìmọ̀ àdúgbò pàdé ní ọ̀sẹ̀ tó kọjá. Wọ́n jíròrò lórí bí a ṣe lè lo ìsúná wọn. Ní òpin ìpàdé, wọ́n pinnu láti pín in sí ẹ̀ẹ̀mejì. The neighborhood committee met last week. They discussed how to use their budget. At the end of the meeting, they decided to divide it in two. "wọ́n" consistently tracks "ìgbìmọ̀ àdúgbò" across three sentences

When a paragraph introduces a new participant, restate the noun once before returning to pronouns, so the reader's reference chain resets clearly rather than guessing.

3. Discourse connectors between paragraphs

Paragraph-level connectors signal how one part of a text relates to the next: addition, contrast, result, or sequence. These connectors do not introduce new grammar; they organize material already available from earlier volumes.

  • Ní àkọ́kọ́... Lẹ́yìn náà... Nítorí náà... Ní ìparí... First... After that... Therefore... Finally... sequencing connectors marking the stages of an extended explanation
  • Bí ó tilẹ̀ jẹ́ bẹ́ẹ̀... síbẹ̀síbẹ̀... Even so... nevertheless... contrast connector extending the concessive pattern from Chapter 15 to paragraph scale

4. Content revision versus sentence-level editing

Revising a long text happens in two distinct stages, and conflating them slows a writer down. Content revision asks whether the ideas are complete, ordered well, and adequately supported. Sentence-level editing asks whether each sentence is grammatically accurate, correctly toned, and clearly written.

Pattern: two-stage revision
Àtúnṣe àkóónú kọ́kọ́, àtúnṣe gírámà àti ìsọ̀rọ̀ lẹ́yìn
Content revision first, grammar and sentence-level editing after — checking sentence accuracy before the ideas are settled wastes effort on sentences that may be cut or reordered.

Only after the ideas, order, and evidence are settled should a writer apply the Chapter 3 four-pass proofreading procedure — checking tone, dot-under spelling, grammar, and overall clarity — to the whole document.

5. Signposting paragraph purpose

Each paragraph in an extended text should carry one main idea, and its purpose should be recoverable from its opening sentence. A reader should be able to state what each paragraph is doing — introducing the issue, giving a reason, presenting evidence, addressing a counter-argument, or concluding — without reading the whole paragraph.

  • Ìdí kejì tí ó yẹ kí a gbé yẹ̀ wò ni pé... The second reason we should consider is that... opening sentence signals the paragraph's single purpose: presenting the second reason

Model Dialogue — Planning a Long-Form Piece

Context: Fúnmi asks Adé for advice before she begins writing the community report.

Fúnmi: Mi ò tíì kọ ìjábọ̀ gígùn rí ní èdè Yorùbá. Kí ni mo lè ṣe kí n má bàa dàrú?I've never written a long report in Yorùbá before. What can I do so I don't get confused?
Adé: Ṣe ètò-ìkọ̀wé kékeré kan kọ́kọ́. Kọ ìpolongo rẹ sílẹ̀, lẹ́yìn náà àwọn ìdí rẹ ní ọ̀wọ̀ọ̀wọ́, kí o tó ronú nípa gbolóhùn kọ̀ọ̀kan.Make a small writing plan first. Write down your position, then your reasons in order, before you think about each individual sentence.
Adé recommends the four-part plan from the Grammar Focus before Fúnmi begins drafting sentences.
Fúnmi: Ó dára. Àmọ́ báwo ni n ó ṣe rí i dájú pé ìsọ̀rí ọ̀rọ̀ kọ̀ọ̀kan ń so mọ́ ara wọn dáadáa?Good. But how do I make sure each paragraph connects well to the others?
Adé: Lo àwọn ọ̀nà ìsopọ̀ bí "ní àkọ́kọ́," "lẹ́yìn náà," àti "nítorí náà" láàrin ìsọ̀rí ọ̀rọ̀ rẹ. Kí ìsọ̀rí ọ̀rọ̀ kọ̀ọ̀kan sì máa gbé èrò kan ṣoṣo.Use connectors like "first," "after that," and "therefore" between your paragraphs. And let each paragraph carry only one idea.
Fúnmi: Ẹ̀yin ni mo ó kọ́kọ́ fi àkọsílẹ̀-àkọ́kọ́ mi hàn kí n tó ṣe àtúnṣe rẹ̀?Should I show you my first draft before I revise it?
Adé: Bẹ́ẹ̀ni. Àti pé kà á sókè fún ara rẹ kí o tó fi hàn mí — ìyẹn máa ń fi àṣìṣe tí ojú ò rí hàn.Yes. And read it aloud to yourself before you show me — that often reveals mistakes the eye doesn't catch.

Ìpín Kẹrin

Speaking Practice

Tier 1 — Draft Read-Aloud Drill

Read a paragraph you have written aloud twice: once at a slow, careful pace checking tone accuracy on every word, then once at a natural pace, listening for places where the phrasing feels awkward.

Tier 2 — Oral Outline Presentation

Before writing, present your four-part plan (position, reasons, evidence/concession, conclusion) aloud to a partner or recording, in full sentences, so the structure is tested in speech before it becomes text.

Tier 3 — Independent Draft Reading and Self-Correction

Record yourself reading your completed long-form draft aloud from beginning to end. Afterward, listen to the recording once and note any place where tone, phrasing, or paragraph connection sounded unclear, then revise those spots in the written text.

Ní ìpàdé àdúgbò tí ó kọjá, àwọn olùgbé pinnu láti pín ìsúná kékeré tí wọ́n ní sí ẹ̀ẹ̀mejì. Ìpín Kẹfà

Writing Workshop

Produce a short multi-paragraph report or extended commentary in Standard Written Yorùbá, applying the planning, coherence, and revision strategies from this chapter.

  1. Choose a topic appropriate for a report, project update, or extended commentary (200–300 words across three to four paragraphs).
  2. Write a four-part plan: position, reasons in order, evidence/concession, conclusion.
  3. Draft an opening paragraph that states your position clearly.
  4. Draft one paragraph per reason, each with a clear opening sentence signaling its purpose.
  5. Include at least one piece of evidence framed as reported information, and one concession, consolidating Chapters 9 and 15.
  6. Use at least three discourse connectors to link your paragraphs.
  7. Draft a closing paragraph that restates your position as a conclusion.
  8. Revise in two passes: first content and organization, then the Chapter 3 four-pass proofreading procedure for tone, spelling, and grammar.
  9. Read the final draft aloud before submission.
  • The report is fully tone-marked with correct dot-under letters throughout.
  • Each paragraph carries one clearly identifiable main idea.
  • Pronoun reference remains clear across paragraph boundaries.
  • At least three discourse connectors link paragraphs.
  • Opinion and reported information are clearly and separately framed.
  • The conclusion clearly restates the opening position.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Written reports in community life

Community associations, religious organizations, workplaces, and schools in many Yorùbá-speaking contexts often rely on short written reports to keep members informed between meetings. These reports are commonly read aloud at a following gathering as well as circulated in writing, so a written report is often composed with both silent readers and a listening audience in mind.

Regional Note — Formality of written reports

The expected length, formality, and structure of a community report can vary by setting, generation, and organization, consistent with the register variation discussed in Chapter 12. A workplace or institutional report may expect a more formal structure than a neighborhood update. Observe your specific setting rather than assuming one fixed format for all contexts.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Drafting sentence by sentence without a plan

Error: Beginning to write full sentences immediately, without a stated position, ordered reasons, or intended conclusion, resulting in a text that wanders or repeats itself.

Correction: Write the four-part plan from the Grammar Focus before drafting any sentences, as modeled in the dialogue above.

Common Mistake — Losing reference across paragraphs

Error: Continuing to use a pronoun such as wọ́n or ó several paragraphs after the noun it refers to, leaving the reader uncertain who or what is meant.

Correction: Restate the relevant noun once at the start of a new paragraph before returning to pronoun reference, resetting the reference chain clearly.

Common Mistake — Editing sentences before the content is settled

Error: Polishing the tone marks and grammar of a sentence that is later deleted or reordered because the underlying idea changes during revision.

Correction: Complete content revision first — checking ideas, order, and evidence — and apply sentence-level editing only afterward, as explained in the Grammar Focus.

Chapter Summary

This chapter introduced no new tense, aspect, particle, or clause system. It extended pronoun and reference systems, discourse connectors, and the argumentation and evidence-framing patterns from Chapter 15 to the scale of a full multi-paragraph text: planning before drafting, maintaining reference chains and paragraph-to-paragraph connectors, signposting each paragraph's single main idea, and separating content revision from sentence-level editing using an extended version of the Chapter 3 four-pass proofreading procedure. Learners also practiced reading a completed draft aloud to catch tone, phrasing, and coherence problems before submission.

Àkànṣe Àyíká Ìkẹ́kọ̀ọ́ Tí Akẹ́kọ̀ọ́ Yàn

Specialized Learner-Selected Domain · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Advanced Precision, Interpretation, and Specialization

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Select and clearly scope one specialized domain — professional, academic, cultural, or civic — for a sustained communication package.
  2. Build a domain-specific glossary of at least twenty tone-marked, fully diacritized terms with grammatical and register notes.
  3. Identify and apply the register expectations of the chosen domain, distinguishing them from the general register practiced in earlier chapters.
  4. Draft a domain-specific report or analytical text that integrates the domain glossary and applies the long-form writing and editing skills from Chapters 16 and 17.
  5. Plan and rehearse a short presentation appropriate to the domain and its intended audience.
  6. Adapt the same domain content for two different audiences — for example, an expert audience and a general audience — without changing the underlying facts.
  7. Apply the four-pass proofreading procedure and matrix-category diagnosis from Chapter 17 to the domain package before considering it complete.
  8. Explain the sourcing and evidentiary basis for domain claims made in the package, without fabricating information.

Opening Communication Scenario

Fúnmi's editorial work with Adé's newsletter has earned her a new invitation: the community education committee has asked her to prepare a short informational package — a glossary, a written report, and a short presentation — on a subject of her choosing, to be delivered first to the committee and then adapted for a general community meeting. Fúnmi realizes this task is different from anything she has done before. It is not one skill in isolation; it requires selecting a domain narrow enough to treat with real precision, building the vocabulary that domain requires, writing about it accurately and in the right register, and then presenting the same material twice, for two different audiences, without changing what is actually true.

  1. This chapter shifts from general-purpose editing (Chapter 17) to building a complete, domain-specific communication package from planning through delivery.
  2. No new tense, aspect, particle, or clause type is introduced. This chapter applies systems already consolidated through Chapter 17 to a learner-selected domain.
  3. The long-form writing skills from Chapter 16 and the editing and diagnostic skills from Chapter 17 are both required components of the package built in this chapter.

Ìpín Kìíní

Pronunciation Focus — Domain Terminology Aloud

Sound and Style — Speaking specialized terms with confidence

Specialized or technical vocabulary is often longer and less familiar than everyday vocabulary, and learners frequently let tone accuracy slip on unfamiliar words even when their tone control is otherwise strong. Before using a new domain term in a presentation, isolate it, pronounce it slowly with full tone accuracy, then practice it inside a short carrier sentence at natural speed.

As in Chapter 17's proofreading-aloud practice, the goal is to notice where fluency and tone accuracy compete. A presenter who slows down dramatically only for technical terms draws attention to insecurity with the vocabulary; the target is even, confident delivery across both familiar and specialized words.

When presenting to an expert audience, precise terminology matters more than simplified paraphrase. When presenting to a general audience, the same term may need a brief spoken gloss immediately after its first use, delivered smoothly rather than as an apologetic aside.

Grammar Focus — Building a Domain Package

This section organizes the planning, drafting, editing, and presentation systems consolidated through Chapter 17 into a sequence for producing one complete, learner-selected domain package. No new grammar system is introduced; this section controls how existing systems are applied to a new kind of sustained, multi-audience task.

1. Choosing and scoping a domain

A workable domain is specific enough to support twenty precise glossary terms and a focused report, and broad enough to sustain a report and two presentations. "Cooperative farming in my hometown" is scoped appropriately; "agriculture" is too broad, and "the name of one tool" is too narrow.

2. Building the domain glossary

Each glossary entry requires the same information demanded of vocabulary throughout this series: the tone-marked form, the English meaning, grammatical information where relevant, and a register or usage note. A domain glossary entry additionally states which register the term belongs to and, where useful, a plain-language paraphrase for a general audience.

Pattern: a domain glossary entry
Ọ̀rọ̀: [term] | Ìtumọ̀: [meaning] | Ìṣàkóso: [grammar note] | Ìforígbárí àwùjọ: [register note, plain-language gloss if needed]
The fourth field is unique to domain glossaries: it tells a presenter whether the term needs a spoken or written gloss for a general audience.
Ọ̀rọ̀: àjọ ìfọwọ́sowọ́pọ̀ | Ìtumọ̀: cooperative, cooperative society | Ìṣàkóso: orúkọ, oun tí a ń ṣàkópọ̀ | Ìforígbárí àwùjọ: formal/civic; fún àwùjọ àdúgbò, ṣàlàyé rẹ̀ ní ṣókí gẹ́gẹ́ bí "àwọn tó ń ṣiṣẹ́ pọ̀ láti ṣe àǹfààní fún gbogbo èèyàn."
Term: cooperative. Meaning: cooperative, cooperative society. Grammar: noun, a collective entity. Register note: formal/civic; for a general audience, gloss briefly as "people who work together for everyone's benefit."

3. Drafting the domain report

The domain report applies the long-form writing process from Chapter 16 — planning, drafting, revising, and editing — to domain-specific content. It should state its scope clearly in an opening paragraph, integrate at least ten glossary terms in context, and distinguish factual claims the writer can support from claims that would need outside verification.

Where a report makes a factual claim the writer cannot personally verify or support with a known source, it must be marked clearly rather than stated with false confidence.

  • Gẹ́gẹ́ bí àwọn àgbà nínú àdúgbò ti sọ, ẹgbẹ́ ìfọwọ́sowọ́pọ̀ àkọ́kọ́ ní agbègbè yìí bẹ̀rẹ̀ ní nǹkan bí ọgbọ̀n ọdún sẹ́yìn, bí ó tilẹ̀ jẹ́ pé a kò rí àkọsílẹ̀ tí a fi ìdí ọjọ́ náà múlẹ̀ pátápátá. According to community elders, the first cooperative in this area began roughly thirty years ago, though no record fully confirms that exact date. source attributed; certainty explicitly bounded

4. One report, two presentations

The same underlying report must be adapted for two different audiences without altering the facts it presents. Adaptation changes vocabulary density, sentence length, background explanation, and register — not the substance of what is claimed.

Adapting the same content for two audiences
FeatureExpert audienceGeneral community audience
TerminologyUsed directly, without glossGlossed briefly at first use
Background explanationMinimal; assumes shared knowledgeFuller; explains context before detail
RegisterFormal, institutionalFormal but accessible; warmer address
Sentence lengthLonger, more subordinate structureShorter, clearer sentence boundaries
Underlying factsIdenticalIdentical

5. Presentation planning

A short oral presentation for each audience should state the domain and its scope, present three to five key points supported by the glossary and the report, and close with a clear takeaway appropriate to that audience's interests.

Model Dialogue — Choosing and Scoping a Domain

Context: Fúnmi discusses her domain choice with Adé before beginning her glossary and report.

Adé: Kí ni o fẹ́ kọ̀wé nípa rẹ̀ fún ìgbìmọ̀ ẹ̀kọ́?What do you want to write about for the education committee?
Fúnmi: Mo ń rò pé kí n kọ̀wé nípa iṣẹ́ ìgbìmọ̀ àdúgbò nínú ìtọ́jú omi mímu, àmọ́ mo rò pé ó tóbi jù.I was thinking of writing about the community committee's work on drinking water maintenance, but I think it's too big.
Fúnmi recognizes on her own that her initial idea needs narrowing before Adé raises the issue.
Adé: Kí ni apá kan nínú rẹ̀ tí o mọ̀ dáadáa jù?What one part of it do you know best?
Fúnmi: Bóyá bí wọ́n ṣe ń rí owó fún àtúnṣe àwọn kànga.Maybe how they raise money for well repairs.
Adé: Ìyẹn dára. Ó ṣe é ṣe fún ìwádìí ọ̀rọ̀ ogún, ìròyìn kan, àti ìfihàn méjì. Ṣé o ní ẹnikẹ́ni tí o lè bèèrè lọ́wọ́ rẹ̀ fún ìjẹ́rìí, tàbí ṣé gbogbo rẹ̀ máa jẹ́ ohun tí o ti mọ̀ tẹ́lẹ̀?That's good. It's workable for twenty vocabulary terms, one report, and two presentations. Do you have anyone you can ask for evidence, or will it all be things you already knew?
Fúnmi: Mo lè bá àwọn tó wà nínú ìgbìmọ̀ náà sọ̀rọ̀, kí n má baà kọ ohun tí n kò lè fi ìdí rẹ̀ múlẹ̀.I can speak with people on the committee, so I don't write things I can't support with evidence.

Ìpín Kẹfà

Writing Workshop

Build your complete domain package across the steps below, applying the long-form writing process from Chapter 16 and the editing procedure from Chapter 17.

  1. Choose and scope your domain in one clear sentence, checking it against the examples in the Grammar Focus.
  2. Identify at least two sources of information for your domain: personal knowledge, a named person you can consult, or a known community record.
  3. Build a glossary of at least twenty terms using the four-field entry pattern.
  4. Draft a domain report of at least four paragraphs that states its scope, integrates at least ten glossary terms, and marks the certainty of any unverifiable claims.
  5. Apply the Chapter 17 four-pass editing procedure to the report, tagging every issue found by matrix category.
  6. Revise the report in response to your own editing pass, keeping a short revision log.
  7. Adapt the finished report into two short presentation outlines: one for an expert audience, one for a general community audience.
  8. Read both presentation outlines aloud as a final quality-control step before considering the package complete.
  • The domain is scoped neither too broadly nor too narrowly for a twenty-term glossary and a four-paragraph report.
  • Every glossary entry includes term, meaning, grammar note, and register/audience note.
  • Every unverifiable claim in the report is clearly marked as such.
  • The four-pass edit has been completed and tagged by matrix category.
  • The two presentation outlines differ in terminology, background explanation, and register while stating identical underlying facts.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Community knowledge as a legitimate source

In many Yorùbá-speaking communities, elders, committee members, and long-standing residents serve as recognized sources of information about local history, institutions, and practices, even where no written record exists. Treating an elder's or committee member's account as a named, attributed source — while still marking where certainty is limited — respects both the value of oral community knowledge and the standard of honest sourcing this chapter requires.

Regional Note — Institutional vocabulary varies by community structure

Terms for community committees, cooperatives, and civic bodies can vary by region and by the specific structure of local governance. When building a domain glossary about civic or institutional topics, confirm the term used by the specific community or institution being described rather than assuming one nationwide standard term applies everywhere.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Choosing a domain that is too broad to treat with precision

Error: Selecting a domain such as "education" or "government" that cannot be covered meaningfully in twenty glossary terms and a short report.

Correction: Narrow the domain to a specific practice, institution, or process, following the scoping examples in the Grammar Focus.

Common Mistake — Stating unverifiable claims with false confidence

Error: Presenting a fact learned informally or uncertainly as though it were fully confirmed and documented.

Correction: Attribute the claim to its actual source and mark the limits of its certainty, as modeled in the Grammar Focus and Reading Practice.

Common Mistake — Changing the facts rather than the delivery when adapting for a new audience

Error: Simplifying content for a general audience by dropping or altering facts rather than adjusting vocabulary, background explanation, and register.

Correction: Keep the underlying facts identical across both audience versions; adapt only terminology, explanation, sentence length, and register, as shown in the audience-adaptation table.

Independent Study Tools

Fún akẹ́kọ̀ọ́ tí ń kọ́ ẹ̀kọ́ fúnra rẹ̀ — For the independent learner

  • If no domain expert is available to consult, select a domain drawn from your own direct experience or a well-known community practice, and mark clearly which claims rest on personal knowledge rather than outside sourcing.
  • Record yourself delivering both presentation versions and compare the two recordings for terminology, pacing, and register differences.
  • Keep your domain glossary in a running document; specialized vocabulary built here will be reusable in Chapters 19 and 20.
  • Ask a study partner unfamiliar with your domain to listen to your general-audience presentation and tell you which terms still needed a clearer gloss.

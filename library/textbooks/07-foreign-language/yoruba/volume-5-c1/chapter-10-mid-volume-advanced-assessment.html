Mid-Volume Advanced Assessment · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Advanced Precision, Interpretation, and Specialization

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Demonstrate integrated command of the tone, orthography, register, hedging, obligation, and reported-information systems consolidated across Chapters 1 through 9.
  2. Interpret an unfamiliar listening passage that mixes register shifts, stance-marking, and reported information without new instructional support.
  3. Interpret and edit an unfamiliar reading passage, diagnosing errors by matrix category — tone, orthography, particle, pronoun, serialization, focus, syntax, or register.
  4. Produce a short register-adapted writing package on an assigned prompt, matching the audience-adaptation strategies developed in Chapters 8 and 9.
  5. Defend a completed piece of writing orally, explaining specific grammatical, tonal, or register choices when questioned.
  6. Self-assess performance against the assessment rubrics for interpretation, editing, writing, and oral defense.
  7. Identify which consolidated systems remain reliable and which require further review before continuing into Chapter 11.

Apá A

Section A — Listening Interpretation

Ask an instructor, study partner, or qualified Yorùbá speaker to read the passage below aloud once at a natural pace, then a second time more slowly, or read it aloud yourself and cover it before answering. Do not read the passage silently before listening.

Assessment Listening Script. Machine text-to-speech must be checked by a qualified Yorùbá speaker before use as a pronunciation model.

"Gẹ́gẹ́ bí olùkọ́ni ilé-ìwé alákọ̀ọ́bẹ̀rẹ̀ àdúgbò ṣe sọ, àwọn òbí kan ti fi ẹ̀dùn ọkàn hàn nípa iye àkókò tí àwọn ọmọdé ń lò lórí ẹ̀rọ ìbánisọ̀rọ̀ ní alẹ́. Wọ́n ní kí ilé-ìwé wá ọ̀nà láti ràn àwọn òbí lọ́wọ́ láti ṣàkóso ìlò ẹ̀rọ náà, kí wọ́n sì rán àwọn ìwé àlàyé sí ilé wọn. Èrò mi ni pé àníyàn àwọn òbí yìí yẹ láti gbọ́, bí ó tilẹ̀ jẹ́ pé a kò tíì rí ìwádìí tí ó fi hàn dájúdájú bí ẹ̀rọ ìbánisọ̀rọ̀ ṣe ń nípa lórí ìkẹ́kọ̀ọ́ àwọn ọmọdé ní pàtó. Bí ó ti wù kí ó rí, ó yẹ kí ilé-ìwé àti àwọn òbí jọ wá ojútùú kan tí kò ní dí ìbáraẹnisọ̀rọ̀ ìdílé lọ́wọ́ pátápátá, nítorí pé àwọn ọmọdé náà tún nílò ẹ̀rọ náà fún ìṣẹ́ ilé-ìwé wọn lẹ́ẹ̀kọ̀ọ̀kan. Àṣepé, mo dábàá pé ilé-ìwé pe àwọn òbí sí ìpàdé kan láti jíròrò ọ̀rọ̀ yìí ṣáájú kí wọ́n tó gbé ìpinnu tí ó lágbára kalẹ̀."

1. What did parents ask the school to do?

2. Identify the sentence where the speaker's personal stance begins, and state how it is marked apart from the reported concern.

3. What concessive qualification does the speaker introduce with "bí ó tilẹ̀ jẹ́ pé," and why does it matter for how the report should be trusted?

4. What does the speaker propose as a next step, and what serial or clause structure signals that this is a recommendation rather than a report?

Iṣẹ́ rẹ — Your task: Answer all four questions in complete Yorùbá sentences, fully tone-marked.

Apá B

Section B — Reading Interpretation and Editing

Read the passage below. It contains six deliberate errors distributed across tone, orthography, particle choice, pronoun reference, serialization, and register. Identify each error, name its matrix category, and supply the correction, following the four-pass editing procedure from Chapter 3.

Àyọkà fún Àtúnṣe — Passage for Correction

Igbimo ilu ti fowosi eto amona odo kan ti yio berè laarin osu kan. Woн ni pe àwọn àgbà mẹ́rin ti gbà láti kópa nínú ìkọ́nilẹ́kọ̀ọ́, ó sì lọ sí ọjà kí ó rí ẹrù. Èrò mi ni pé ìpinnu yìí kò ṣe pàtàkì rárá, torí náà a kò nílò láti ṣàníyàn nípa rẹ̀. Kín ni ìwọ o rò nípa ìròyìn yìí, ẹ̀gbọ́n?

1. Locate and correct the missing tone marks and dot-under letters in the first sentence. Name the matrix category.

2. Locate the Cyrillic character that has silently replaced a Latin letter in the reporting frame. Name the matrix category and supply the correct spelling.

3. The second sentence contains an unmotivated serial-verb clause ("ó sì lọ sí ọjà kí ó rí ẹrù") that does not belong with the reported claim about the mentorship program. Explain why this breaks discourse coherence and propose a correction.

4. The stance sentence claims the decision "kò ṣe pàtàkì rárá" (is not important at all) and that "a kò nílò láti ṣàníyàn" (we need not be concerned). Compare this stance to the stance expressed in Chapter 9's reading passage on the same topic, and explain why this version reads as an inconsistent or unsupported evaluation.

5. The closing question shifts from formal reporting register into a mixed register ("Kín ni ìwọ o rò," addressed familiarly as "ẹ̀gbọ́n"). Identify the register mismatch and explain what a more consistent formal-register closing would look like.

Iṣẹ́ rẹ — Your task: Produce a fully corrected version of the passage, then list each correction with its matrix category in a short table.

Apá D

Section D — Oral Defense

Deliver your Section C formal-register text aloud, then answer the following defense questions from a partner, instructor, or your own recorded self-review. Answer each question aloud in Yorùbá before checking the Answer Key.

Defense Question Set

  1. Which sentence in your text uses a reporting frame, and why did you choose that specific frame rather than a plainer assertion?
  2. Where does your stance begin, and how would a listener know it is your opinion rather than part of the report?
  3. Point to one place where you used focus (`ni`) or contrastive emphasis, and explain what it emphasizes.
  4. Identify one serial-verb construction in your text and explain what would be lost if you replaced it with a simple coordinated clause using "àti."
  5. Explain one specific difference between your casual and formal versions beyond vocabulary alone — for example, pacing, source detail, or hedging.

This oral defense format mirrors the interview and interpretive-commentary skills built in Chapters 4, 6, and 9, and previews the mediation and defense tasks that recur in the Chapter 19 capstone studio.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Shared decision-making between institutions and families

The listening passage in this chapter reflects a common pattern in many Yorùbá-speaking communities, where schools, clinics, and other local institutions often prefer to consult families directly before adopting a firm policy, rather than announcing a decision unilaterally. This preference for consultation shapes how proposals are worded — provisionally, with room for shared discussion — rather than as closed conclusions.

Regional Note — Assessment register conventions

The specific balance of formality expected in a "formal" community bulletin or memo can vary by institution, region, and generation. Treat the casual/formal contrast in this chapter as an instructional model for practicing register control, not as a fixed universal standard for every Yorùbá-speaking institution.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Treating the assessment as a chance to introduce new grammar

Error: Attempting to use an unfamiliar particle, focus construction, or serial-verb pattern not previously consolidated, in order to sound more advanced.

Correction: Use only the systems consolidated in Chapters 1–9. This chapter measures control of what has already been taught, not exposure to new material.

Common Mistake — Writing identical casual and formal versions

Error: Submitting two texts that differ only in a greeting line, with source detail, pacing, and hedging left unchanged.

Correction: Apply the concrete register differences practiced in Chapters 8 and 9: adjust source-naming precision, pacing, and the density of hedging or qualification between the two versions.

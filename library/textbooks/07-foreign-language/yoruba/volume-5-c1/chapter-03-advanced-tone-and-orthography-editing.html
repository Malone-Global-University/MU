Ìṣàtúnṣe Amì Ohùn Àti Àkọ̀tọ́ Gíga

Advanced Tone and Orthography Editing · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Advanced Precision, Interpretation, and Specialization

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Locate missing, misplaced, or incorrect tone marks in a long or multi-paragraph Yorùbá text.
  2. Diagnose whether a tone error changes meaning, creates ambiguity, or merely looks careless.
  3. Correct dot-under letter errors (e/ẹ, o/ọ, s/ṣ) in formal prose and explain why each correction matters.
  4. Identify nasal-vowel spelling problems and distinguish them from ordinary vowel-plus-n sequences.
  5. Convert undiacritized or informally typed digital text into fully marked Standard Written Yorùbá.
  6. Apply a consistent proofreading procedure to a text you did not write yourself.
  7. Explain, using precise terms, why a specific correction was necessary rather than simply supplying the fixed form.
  8. Distinguish scheduled recognition of undiacritized text from acceptable production in formal writing tasks.

Opening Communication Scenario

Adé's organization has asked her to prepare the final, publishable version of last week's three vaccination-schedule messages before they go on the official website and printed handouts. The drafts came from several different staff members, some of whom typed quickly on their phones without full tone marks, and one of whom mixed up ẹ and e throughout an entire paragraph. Adé's supervisor tells her plainly: nothing goes out to the public with a missing tone mark or a wrong dot-under letter, because a single missing mark can quietly change a word's meaning, and a printed institutional document is expected to model correct Yorùbá. Adé now has to read every draft slowly, mark every problem, and be ready to explain each fix — not just apply it silently.

  1. This chapter treats editing as a distinct skill from writing: you are reading someone else's text critically, not producing your own from scratch.
  2. You will practice a repeatable proofreading procedure that checks tone, dot-under letters, and nasal vowels in a fixed order rather than randomly.
  3. By the end of the chapter, you will be able to justify every correction you make, not only produce a corrected version.

Pronunciation Focus — Tone Repair in Long Texts

Focus — Reading aloud reveals what silent proofreading misses

A missing tone mark is easy to miss when scanning silently, because the eye supplies the expected word from context. Reading a text aloud, syllable by syllable, forces the tone actually written on the page to surface, exposing places where the mark is missing, wrong, or inconsistent with the rest of the passage.

  • Read a long passage aloud slowly, pausing at every word to say its tone out loud before continuing, rather than reading at natural conversational speed.
  • When your spoken tone does not match what is written, mark the spot rather than silently correcting your pronunciation to fit what you assume the writer meant.
  • Practice reading the same corrected passage twice: once at editing speed (slow, syllable-focused) and once at natural reading speed, to confirm the correction sounds right in connected speech, not only in isolation.
  • Note that a long text with many short, similarly toned words is especially prone to tone drift; read such passages in shorter chunks and check each chunk separately.

Grammar — Editing Tone and Orthography

This chapter introduces no new tone system or new letters. It develops a systematic editing procedure for the tone, dot-under, and nasal-vowel systems already consolidated through Volume IV, and extends recognition of undiacritized digital text so that it can be converted into correct formal writing.

A Four-Pass Proofreading Procedure

Recommended editing order and what each pass checks
PassChecksTypical problem caught
1. Dot-under passẹ/e, ọ/o, ṣ/sWrong vowel or consonant quality, often from fast phone typing
2. Tone-mark passHigh, low, and unmarked mid tone on every syllableMissing marks, especially on short grammatical words
3. Nasal-vowel passTrue nasal vowels vs. plain vowel + n, syllabic nasalsConfusing a nasal vowel spelling with an ordinary consonant sequence
4. Meaning-impact passWhether any remaining uncertainty changes meaningAmbiguous or minimal-pair words that need special attention

Diagnosing Whether an Error Changes Meaning

Pattern

Ọ̀rọ̀ tí a kọ (word as written) → Ǹjẹ́ ó yí ìtumọ̀ padà? (does it change meaning?) → Ìpele àṣìṣe (severity level)

Not every tone or spelling slip is equally serious. Some errors create a real minimal-pair confusion (a different existing word), some create a non-word that is merely wrong, and some are acceptable informal shortenings in the right context. An editor must classify the error before deciding how urgently to flag it.

Ìṣírò: "Mo ra ọkọ̀" dípò "Mo rà á kọ̀" — àìsí àmì ohùn níbí kì í ṣe àṣìṣe ìtumọ̀ tí ó le, àmọ́ "ó ṣe" dípò "ó ṣé" lè yí ìtumọ̀ gbólóhùn padà pátápátá.

Example: "I bought a car" instead of a different reading — a missing mark here is not a severe meaning error, but writing one grammatical word instead of a similarly spelled one can change a sentence's meaning entirely.

Converting Undiacritized Digital Text

  • Ìwé àìlàmì: "Mo lo ile ana, mo si ra ounje fun awon omo." Undiacritized draft: "I went home yesterday, and I bought food for the children." recognition-only informal digital form; must be fully restored before formal use
  • Ẹ̀dà tí a ti tún ṣe: "Mo lọ ilé àná, mo sì ra oúnjẹ fún àwọn ọmọ." Corrected version, with full tone marks and dot-under letters restored. formal Standard Written Yorùbá production target

Undiacritized text of this kind may still be read for comprehension in informal digital contexts, consistent with its receptive status from earlier volumes. It must never be the final form of a document intended for formal, institutional, or published use.

Recognition note — Recognizing an error is not the same as knowing why it matters

A learner who fixes an error correctly but cannot say why it was wrong has repaired the surface but not built the underlying editing skill. Every correction in this chapter's exercises should be paired with a short explanation naming the specific system involved — tone, dot-under quality, or nasal-vowel spelling — rather than a bare fixed form.

Writing Workshop

Produce a fully edited version of a supplied undiacritized or error-containing text, together with a short written justification for each correction. Full tone marks and dot-under letters are required in every corrected version.

  • Take an undiacritized or partially marked draft (your own earlier informal note, a text message, or a passage supplied by an instructor) of at least 60–100 words.
  • Apply the four-pass procedure in order: dot-under letters, tone marks, nasal vowels, then meaning-impact review.
  • Produce a clean, fully corrected version of the text with every tone mark and dot-under letter restored.
  • For at least five individual corrections, write one sentence each explaining which system was involved (tone, dot-under quality, or nasal vowel) and whether the original error changed meaning, created a non-word, or was merely informal.
  • Identify any place where you were genuinely uncertain about the intended word, and explain what context clue you used to decide.
  • Read your final corrected version aloud and confirm it sounds natural at normal speaking speed, not only correct on the page.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Published Yorùbá as a model of care

    Many Yorùbá-language institutions, publishers, and community organizations treat fully diacritized, carefully proofread writing as a sign of respect for readers and for the language itself, particularly in materials meant for public distribution. A document that reaches the public with missing tone marks or dot-under errors can be read as rushed or careless, even when its informational content is accurate.

    Regional Note — Informal digital norms vary

    How strictly diacritics are used in everyday texting, social media, and informal digital communication varies widely among individuals, communities, and platforms. The strict editing standard in this chapter applies to formal, institutional, and published writing; it is not a claim that all informal digital Yorùbá must look identical.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Correcting without checking meaning impact

    Error: Treating every tone or spelling irregularity as equally urgent, spending equal effort on a cosmetic slip and a genuine meaning-changing error, or worse, fixing the cosmetic issue while missing the meaning-changing one.

    Correction: Always run the meaning-impact pass last and prioritize corrections that could cause a reader to understand a different word or meaning than intended, such as distinguishing ọkọ̀ from ọ̀kọ̀ when the intended word is ambiguous from context alone.

    Common Mistake — Fixing the letter but not explaining the system

    Error: Supplying a corrected form (for example, changing ise to iṣẹ́) without being able to say whether the fix addressed a dot-under letter, a tone mark, or both.

    Correction: State explicitly which system was involved, for example èyí jẹ́ àṣìṣe ààmì ìsàlẹ̀ àti àmì ohùn papọ̀ ("this was both a dot-under error and a tone-mark error together"), so the underlying pattern is understood, not just the single instance.

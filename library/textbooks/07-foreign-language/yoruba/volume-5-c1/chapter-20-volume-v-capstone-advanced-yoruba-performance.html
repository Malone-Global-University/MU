Volume V Capstone: Advanced Yorùbá Performance · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Advanced Precision, Interpretation, and Specialization

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Deliver the studio-ready capstone portfolio assembled in Chapter 19 as a complete, integrated performance.
  2. Present long-form writing, an edited and matrix-diagnosed revision, a domain glossary and report, and audience-adapted presentations as one coherent body of evidence rather than as separate assignments.
  3. Interpret and comment on a literary, oral-tradition, or media excerpt under assessment conditions, applying the interpretive skills consolidated since Chapter 4.
  4. Produce a translation-aware mediation of a short passage, explaining meaning without imposing English structure onto Yorùbá.
  5. Deliver an oral defense that explains the choices made across the portfolio, using matrix-category language rather than vague self-praise.
  6. Respond to follow-up questions in real time, maintaining tone accuracy, register control, and fluency under assessment pressure.
  7. Self-assess the capstone performance against the full range of Volume V systems and identify realistic next steps beyond the textbook.

Pronunciation Focus — Sustained Delivery Under Assessment

Sound and Style — Holding tone and fluency across a long performance

Chapter 19's timed rehearsal prepared individual components. The capstone adds a new demand: sustaining tone accuracy, vowel quality, and register control across an entire performance that moves between reading aloud, spontaneous explanation, and responses to follow-up questions. Fatigue and adrenaline both tend to flatten tone toward the end of a long performance, exactly where a presenter is least likely to notice it happening.

Two habits from earlier chapters remain the primary defense against this. First, the isolate-and-repair habit from Chapter 19: if a specific word or phrase becomes unstable, slow down on it rather than rushing past it. Second, the pacing habit from Chapter 9's media delivery work: build in brief, natural pauses between sections of the performance, which protect tone accuracy far more reliably than trying to speak faster to save time.

1. Interpretation under assessment conditions

Chapter 4 introduced literary and oral-tradition interpretation; Chapter 13 added proverbs and praise names; Chapter 9 added media commentary. The capstone asks for a short interpretive commentary on an unfamiliar excerpt, produced without the extended preparation time earlier chapters allowed.

Pattern: rapid interpretive commentary
Ọ̀rọ̀ yìí ń sọ̀rọ̀ nípa [ohun àkọ́kọ́], ó sì lo [ẹ̀rọ èdè kan] láti fi hàn pé [ìtumọ̀ tí ó jinlẹ̀].
Name the surface subject, name one specific language device (tone, proverb structure, reduplication, focus, and so on), and state the deeper meaning it carries — in that order, without excessive hedging.
Ọ̀rọ̀ yìí ń sọ̀rọ̀ nípa sùúrù, ó sì lo àkànlò-ọ̀rọ̀ àti àtúnsọ ọ̀rọ̀ láti fi hàn pé sùúrù gbọdọ̀ jẹ́ ìwà tí a ń kọ́ dídi, kì í ṣe èyí tí a ní láti ìbí.
This passage is about patience, and it uses figurative language and repetition to show that patience must be an attitude one builds gradually, not one a person is simply born with.

2. Mediating meaning without imposing English structure

Chapter 11 established translation-aware mediation as a discipline: explaining what a Yorùbá passage communicates without forcing an English clause pattern onto it, and vice versa. The capstone requires this skill applied on the spot to a short unfamiliar sentence or exchange.

  • Dípò kí a túmọ̀ gbólóhùn ọ̀rọ̀-ìṣesí ní ọ̀nà kan-ṣoṣo, a lè ṣàlàyé ìdí tí a fi lò ó, àti ohun tí kì bá ti yẹ tó bí a bá ti tú u ní ọ̀rọ̀ Gẹ̀ẹ́sì tààrà. Rather than translating a serial-verb sentence in only one way, we can explain why it was used, and what would be lost if it were rendered in direct English word order. mediation, not literal transfer

4. Responding to follow-up questions

Follow-up questions test whether explanations were genuinely understood rather than memorized. A strong response restates the question briefly, answers it directly, and gives one piece of specific evidence — the same discipline used in Chapter 15's argument and debate work.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Defending one's work before a community

The practice of explaining and defending one's work before others — whether family elders, teachers, or a professional panel — has long been a valued form of accountability in many Yorùbá-speaking communities, closely tied to the value placed on articulate, respectful public speech. A capstone oral defense stands in this tradition: performance is expected to be accompanied by the ability to explain, in one's own words, why particular choices were made.

Regional Note — Assessment expectations vary by institution

The format, length, and formality of an oral defense can vary considerably across educational institutions, community programs, and professional settings, both within Nigeria and across the Yorùbá diaspora. Learners preparing for a specific real assessment should confirm its actual expectations rather than assuming every institution follows the same model presented here.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Treating interpretation as theme-naming only

Error: Stating only the general topic of a passage ("this is about patience") without identifying any specific language device.

Correction: Follow the full rapid interpretive commentary pattern from the Grammar Focus, naming a specific device such as tone, reduplication, proverb structure, or focus.

Common Mistake — Mediating by literal translation alone

Error: Rendering a Yorùbá sentence word-for-word into English and calling that a mediation.

Correction: Explain what would be lost by literal translation and offer the natural meaning instead, as practiced since Chapter 11.

Common Mistake — Rushing follow-up answers or over-explaining them

Error: Answering a follow-up question either too briefly to be useful or with a long, unfocused response.

Correction: Restate the question briefly, answer directly, and give one piece of specific evidence, as modeled in the Model Dialogue and Speaking Practice.

Independent Study Tools

Fún akẹ́kọ̀ọ́ tí ń kọ́ ẹ̀kọ́ fúnra rẹ̀ — For the independent learner

  • If no panel is available, record your full capstone performance in one continuous take, including simulated follow-up questions you write for yourself in advance.
  • Keep a running list of proverbs, praise names, or short literary excerpts you have not yet analyzed, and practice rapid interpretive commentary on one per week.
  • Revisit your Chapter 1 diagnostic profile and compare it honestly to your capstone performance; this comparison is often more informative than any single external score.
  • Identify one realistic next step for continued Yorùbá study — a reading circle, a conversation partner, specialized coursework, or further independent reading in a chosen domain — and write it down as a concrete plan rather than a vague intention.

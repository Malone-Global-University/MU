Ìtúmọ̀-Mímọ̀ àti Ìṣàlàyé Alátúnkọ̀

Translation-Aware Mediation and Commentary · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Advanced Precision, Interpretation, and Specialization

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify structures in Yorùbá and English that do not correspond word for word, and explain why literal translation misrepresents their meaning.
  2. Mediate between an English source and a Yorùbá audience (or the reverse) by restating meaning rather than transferring word order.
  3. Select register-equivalent expressions when moving content between a casual, formal, or institutional Yorùbá register and English.
  4. Explain cultural-pragmatic translation issues, such as greeting routines, indirectness, and address terms, that a literal rendering would distort.
  5. Produce a short bilingual commentary that presents the same content to a Yorùbá-speaking and an English-speaking audience without one version simply being a translation of the other.
  6. Deliver a mediated oral explanation with prosody appropriate to the register of each audience.
  7. Diagnose and correct translation errors that come from forcing English syntax, serial-verb equivalents, or focus structures onto Yorùbá, or the reverse.

Opening Communication Scenario

Having completed the Chapter 10 mid-volume assessment, Adé is asked by the community development office to help two groups understand each other: an English-speaking regional health partner and a Yorùbá-speaking neighborhood council. The health partner has sent a short English summary of a proposed vaccination outreach schedule. The council needs to understand it, ask questions, and respond — but a word-for-word translation would misrepresent both the tone of the English memo and the expectations of the council meeting. Adé must mediate the content for each audience: restating it naturally in Yorùbá for the council, and later restating the council's response naturally in English for the partner, without letting either version read as a mechanical translation of the other.

  1. This chapter does not introduce a new tense, aspect, or clause system. It builds a mediation skill on top of the register, reporting, and serialization systems already consolidated in Chapters 1 through 10.
  2. The central task is deciding what must change in form so that the meaning, tone, and social effect survive the move between languages.
  3. Every mediated text in this chapter must remain within the tone, orthography, particle, and register systems already active in this volume.

Ìpín Kìíní

Pronunciation Focus — Prosody in Mediated Explanation

Sound and Style — Carrying tone across a register shift

When you mediate the same content for two audiences, the words change, but tone accuracy must not slip in either version. A common error at this level is to relax tone marking in the "casual" rendering, as if informality excused imprecise pronunciation. It does not: high, mid, and low tone remain meaning-bearing in every register.

What does change between registers is pacing and pause placement. A formal mediated explanation typically uses longer pauses at clause boundaries, more deliberate stress on the reporting frame ("Gẹ́gẹ́ bí... ṣe sọ"), and a slightly slower delivery of names, dates, and figures. A casual mediated explanation compresses these pauses and allows more overlap between the reporting frame and the stance that follows it.

Practice reading the same sentence twice: once with formal pacing (clear pause after the reporting frame, deliberate stress on the source) and once with casual pacing (reporting frame and content spoken as a single breath group). Keep every tone mark accurate in both readings.

Ìpín Kejì

Grammar Focus — Non-Literal Structures and Mediation Strategies

This section develops mediation as a controlled application of systems already consolidated: serial verbs, focus, reporting frames, and register markers. No new particle, tense, or clause type is introduced.

1. Why word-for-word translation fails

A literal rendering imports English word order, English discourse connectors, and English information structure into Yorùbá, or the reverse. This is not a minor stylistic issue — it produces sentences that Yorùbá speakers recognize as foreign-shaped even when every individual word is correct.

  • English source: "The council should consider the proposal and then respond within two weeks." Literal, rejected: "Ìgbìmọ̀ náà yẹ kí ó gbé ìdábàá náà yẹ̀wò àti lẹ́yìn náà dáhùn láàrín ọ̀sẹ̀ méjì." — this string uses "àti" ("and") to connect two verb phrases the way English does, instead of Yorùbá serialization. issue: English coordination transferred directly instead of using Yorùbá event-chaining
  • Mediated, accepted: "Ó yẹ kí ìgbìmọ̀ náà gbé ìdábàá náà yẹ̀wò, kí ó sì dáhùn láàrín ọ̀sẹ̀ méjì." The council should look at the proposal and (then) respond within two weeks. Yorùbá event-chain with "kí ó sì" links the two actions as a single planned sequence rather than an English-style "and then."

2. Mediation strategy: meaning-for-meaning, not word-for-word

A mediator identifies the communicative function of a sentence — is it a request, an obligation, a reported claim, a hedge, a piece of praise, an apology — and reconstructs that function using the target language's own resources, rather than mapping each word.

Mediation move: reframing an English hedge
English: "It seems the schedule may need to change."
A word-for-word version would import an English modal-plus-adverb hedge structure Yorùbá does not build the same way. A meaning-for-meaning version identifies the function (a polite hedge softening a possible change) and expresses it with a Yorùbá hedge frame.
Ó dàbí ẹni pé ìtòlẹ́sẹẹsẹ náà lè yí padà.
It appears as though the schedule might change. (hedge frame "ó dàbí ẹni pé" + modal "lè")

3. Register equivalence across languages

Formality does not map onto the same lexical or grammatical choices in both languages. English formality often relies on Latinate vocabulary and passive constructions; Yorùbá formality often relies on plural/respect address forms, elaborated greetings, and careful reporting frames. A mediator matches the social effect of formality, not its surface markers.

Register-equivalence examples
English formal markerYorùbá formal equivalentWhy they match
Passive voice ("It has been decided that...")Impersonal reporting frame ("A ti pinnu pé...")Both remove a named agent to sound institutional.
Modal hedging ("It would be advisable to...")Respect-marked suggestion with plural address ("Ó yẹ kí ẹ jọ̀wọ́ gbé e yẹ̀wò...")Both soften a directive while keeping it clear.
Formal salutation ("Dear Committee Members,")Extended greeting sequence naming the audience with respectBoth signal the relationship and setting before content begins.

4. Cultural-pragmatic issues a literal version would distort

Some content cannot be mediated by grammar substitution alone; it requires a pragmatic decision.

  • An English memo that opens directly with the topic, with no greeting, would read as abrupt if mediated directly into a Yorùbá council announcement. A mediator adds an appropriate greeting frame that the English original does not need, without inventing new information.
  • An English reply that says only "Thanks, that's fine" may need to be mediated into Yorùbá with a fuller acknowledgment and closing courtesy, since a bare short reply can read as curt in the target context.
  • Conversely, an elaborate Yorùbá greeting and blessing sequence mediated into an English business email should usually be condensed to a brief courteous opening, since reproducing every clause would read as excessive in that context.

In each case, the mediator's job is to preserve the function (politeness, respect, closure) using the register conventions the receiving audience actually expects.

Model Dialogue — Mediating a Misunderstanding

Context: Adé meets with two council elders to explain the health partner's English memo before the council responds. Elder-respectful address is expected throughout.

Àgbà Bísí: Kíni wọ́n kọ sínú ìwé náà, ọmọ mi?What did they write in the letter, my child?
Adé: Ẹ káàbọ̀, àgbà mi. Gẹ́gẹ́ bí ìwé náà ṣe sọ, wọ́n fẹ́ bẹ̀rẹ̀ ìtòlẹ́sẹẹsẹ àjẹsára ní àdúgbò yìí láàrín oṣù kan tí ń bọ̀.Welcome, my elder. According to the letter, they want to begin a vaccination program in this neighborhood within the coming month.
Adé pauses after the reporting frame, giving the elders time to register that this is a quoted claim, not Adé's own proposal.
Àgbà Túndé: Ṣé wọ́n béèrè lọ́wọ́ wa àṣẹ ni, tàbí wọ́n ti pinnu tán?Are they asking our permission, or have they already decided?
Adé: Ìwé Gẹ̀ẹ́sì náà kò fi bẹ́ẹ̀ béèrè lọ́wọ́ ẹnikẹ́ni ní ọ̀nà tààrà, torí náà mi ò lè ṣe àdàkọ ọ̀rọ̀ dé ọ̀rọ̀ láti ọ̀dọ̀ wọn; ṣùgbọ́n èrò wọn ni pé ẹ gbọ́dọ̀ jọ wo ìtòlẹ́sẹẹsẹ náà kí ẹ tó fọwọ́sí i, kì í sì í ṣe pé wọ́n ti bẹ̀rẹ̀ sí í ṣe é.The English letter does not directly ask anyone in that way, so I cannot give you a word-for-word rendering from them; but their intention is that you all should review the schedule together before you approve it, and they have not yet begun doing it.
Adé explicitly names the mediation move — "mi ò lè ṣe àdàkọ ọ̀rọ̀ dé ọ̀rọ̀" — flagging that this is an interpretation of intent, not a literal quote, before stating what the letter's function actually is.
Àgbà Bísí: Ó dára. Kí ni a óò dá wọn lóhùn, àti bí wo ni a óò ṣe kọ ọ́ fún wọn ní èdè Gẹ̀ẹ́sì kí a má bàa sọ ọ̀rọ̀ tí kò bá ọ̀wọ̀ mú?Good. What shall we answer them, and how will we write it to them in English so we don't say something that lacks respect?
Adé: Ẹ jẹ́ kí a kọ́kọ́ gbé ohun tí ẹ fẹ́ sọ kalẹ̀ ní Yorùbá, kí n sì ṣe àyẹ̀wò rẹ̀ pẹ̀lú yín láti mọ bí a óò ṣe sọ ọ́ ní Gẹ̀ẹ́sì tí kì yóò gùn jù, tí yóò sì tún jẹ́ ọ̀wọ̀-ọ̀wọ̀.Let us first lay out what you want to say in Yorùbá, and I will review it with you to know how we will say it in English in a way that won't be too long, and that will still be respectful.

Writing Workshop

Produce a short bilingual mediation package: a Yorùbá version and an English version of the same content, plus a brief translation-aware commentary explaining your choices.

  1. Choose one scenario: the vaccination-schedule exchange from this chapter, or an alternative institution-and-community exchange approved by your instructor.
  2. Draft the Yorùbá version first (80–110 words), including a reporting frame, an explicit stance marker, and at least one culturally appropriate courtesy element.
  3. Mediate — do not literally translate — the content into an English version (80–110 words) suited to an institutional reader unfamiliar with Yorùbá greeting conventions.
  4. Write a short commentary (60–90 words) identifying at least three specific points where you changed structure, register marker, or cultural framing rather than translating directly, and explain why.
  5. Proofread the Yorùbá version using the Chapter 3 four-pass procedure before submission.
  • Both versions preserve the same core factual content and the same stance.
  • The Yorùbá version is fully tone-marked with correct dot-under letters throughout.
  • The English version reads naturally in English register, not as a literal rendering.
  • The commentary names specific mediation choices using this chapter's vocabulary.
  • No sentence in either version exceeds the register consistency established at its opening.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Greetings, blessings, and institutional correspondence

In many Yorùbá-speaking communities, a formal reply from a group of elders or a community body commonly includes a blessing or well-wish, particularly when addressing a matter that concerns children's health or wellbeing. An institution unfamiliar with this convention may not expect it, and a mediator often needs to explain its presence rather than omit it silently or reproduce it at full length in a context where it would read as unusual.

Regional Note — Institutional mediation practices vary

The specific balance a professional mediator strikes between preserving cultural framing and adapting to an unfamiliar institutional audience can vary by organization, region, and the relationship between the parties involved. Treat the strategies in this chapter as a starting framework for practicing mediation, not as a single fixed protocol used identically everywhere.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Treating mediation as literal translation with better vocabulary

Error: Producing an English or Yorùbá version that maps each source sentence one-to-one, even when the target language would not naturally organize the information that way.

Correction: Identify the communicative function of each sentence first — request, obligation, hedge, stance — and reconstruct that function using the target language's own resources, as modeled in the Grammar Focus.

Common Mistake — Silently deleting cultural content instead of mediating it

Error: Dropping a greeting sequence, blessing, or respect marker entirely when moving into English, without explaining its presence or function.

Correction: Either condense the element to a natural English equivalent (a brief courteous opening) or, when its content matters, add a short framing note explaining what it conveys, rather than removing it without comment.

Chapter Summary

This chapter introduced no new tense, aspect, particle, or clause system. It developed translation-aware mediation as an applied skill built on systems already consolidated: serial verbs and event-chaining, reporting frames and stance-marking, register-switching, and cultural-pragmatic competence. Learners practiced identifying non-literal structures, choosing meaning-for-meaning strategies over word-for-word transfer, matching register equivalence across English and Yorùbá, and handling cultural-pragmatic elements such as greetings and blessings that a literal rendering would distort. Answer Key

Listening Practice

  1. Adé gives the example that a short English letter without an elaborate greeting is acceptable in English, but the same brevity between elders and young people in Yorùbá would seem disrespectful.
  2. The mediator's job is not to copy each word the same way into the other language, but to make sure the meaning and the respect contained in the message are not lost, even though new wording is used to express it.
  3. The concessive clause is "bí ó tilẹ̀ jẹ́ pé ọ̀rọ̀ tuntun ni a lò láti sọ ọ́" ("even though new wording is used to say it"); it concedes that the surface wording changes while insisting that meaning and respect must still be preserved.

Reading Practice

  1. The elders required ten days' notice to parents before the program begins, and assurance that students would not lose ordinary instructional hours.
  2. The elders want a blessing/well-wish added to the reply, as local custom expects; the mediation challenge is that the health office is unfamiliar with this convention, so Adé must decide how to represent or explain it in English without making the letter read as unusually long or unclear.
  3. Responses will vary; a strong answer explains that Adé must keep enough of the respectful framing to honor the elders' intent while condensing or reframing it so the English letter remains clear and appropriately concise for an unfamiliar institutional reader.

Focus, Information Structure, and Rhetoric · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Advanced Precision, Interpretation, and Specialization

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify the information-structural work performed by "ni" in identification, emphasis, and contrast.
  2. Distinguish subject focus, object focus, and predicate/action focus, and explain how each reshapes word order without disturbing the clause's basic grammar.
  3. Use contrastive focus to correct, challenge, or sharpen a claim in argument and debate.
  4. Interpret and produce question-focus interactions, including focused wh-questions and their focused answers.
  5. Coordinate strong pronouns with focus constructions in connected speech and writing without destabilizing reference.
  6. Apply focus constructions rhetorically to build a nuanced, persuasive argument rather than treating focus as decorative emphasis.
  7. Explain, in writing, how a focused Yorùbá sentence differs in information structure from its most natural English translation.
  8. Edit a passage containing focus constructions for accuracy of word order, tone, and pronoun coordination, building on the editing habits from Chapters 3 and 5.

Opening Communication Scenario

Adé has been invited to a community radio program to respond to a caller who claimed that young people are entirely responsible for a decline in respectful greeting customs. Adé disagrees with the caller's framing, but not with every part of the claim: some responsibility does lie with younger speakers, but Adé believes the caller has placed the emphasis in the wrong place. Rather than simply denying the claim outright, Adé wants to redirect it — to say, in effect, "it is not only young people who are responsible; it is also the pace of modern life that is responsible." Making this kind of precise, persuasive correction in Yorùbá depends on controlling focus: choosing exactly which part of a sentence "ni" identifies, emphasizes, or contrasts, and doing so with stable tone and accurate word order under the pressure of live, spontaneous speech.

  1. This chapter treats focus and emphasis as tools for shaping an argument, not as optional stylistic flourishes.
  2. You will practice identifying what a speaker has chosen to focus, and why, in argument, correction, and explanation.
  3. By the end of the chapter, you will be able to build and deliver a short focused argument responding to a public claim.

Pronunciation Focus — Focused Delivery and Contrastive Tone

Focus — Marking emphasis without distorting tone

A focused constituent in Yorùbá is often produced with slightly greater intensity, a small pitch adjustment, and a brief pause immediately after "ni," but none of this is permitted to override the underlying high, mid, or low tone of the words involved. Learners under the pressure of argument or debate sometimes compensate for emphasis by flattening tone or by inserting an English-style stressed syllable, which can blur meaning rather than sharpen it.

  • Practice saying a focused constituent first at normal conversational pace with full tone accuracy, then again with the extra emphasis of a debate setting, checking that no tone has shifted.
  • Notice the brief, natural pause after "ni" in a focus construction, and avoid replacing it with an English-style vocal stress on a single syllable.
  • In contrastive focus, keep the two contrasted constituents tonally distinct and clearly separated, rather than running them together at speed.
  • Remember that persuasive delivery in Yorùbá depends on clear word order and stable tone working together, not on volume or pitch alone.

Grammar — Focus, Information Structure, and Rhetoric

This chapter develops advanced control of "ni" focus constructions and their rhetorical application in argument, correction, and debate. It builds on focus systems already consolidated through Volume IV and on the clause-level precision developed in Chapter 5, rather than introducing a new tone, orthography, or core clause system.

"Ni" Identification, Emphasis, and Fronting

Pattern

Ìdí-fojúsùn + ni + (ìyókù gbólóhùn)

To focus a constituent, that constituent is fronted to the beginning of the clause and followed by "ni." The rest of the clause continues in its ordinary structure, often with a resumptive pronoun left in the original position of the focused element when that element was the subject. Fronting and "ni" together signal that the fronted element — not the rest of the sentence — carries the new or emphasized information.

Adé ni ó ka ìwé náà. Ìwé náà ni Adé ka.

It is Adé who read the book (subject focus, with resumptive "ó" in subject position). It is the book that Adé read (object focus, no resumptive pronoun needed for the object). Notice how fronting a different constituent changes what the sentence emphasizes without changing its basic truth.

Subject Focus, Object Focus, and Predicate Focus

Focus types and their structural behavior
Focus typeStructural descriptionRhetorical use
Subject focusThe subject is fronted before "ni," and a resumptive subject pronoun remains in the clause.Identifies or singles out who performed an action, often to correct a misattribution.
Object focusThe object is fronted before "ni," and the object position after the verb is left empty.Identifies or singles out what was affected, chosen, or referred to.
Predicate/action focusThe verb or a verb phrase is repeated or nominalized and fronted with "ni," followed by the full clause.Emphasizes that an action did occur, often to counter a denial or doubt.

Contrastive Focus and Correction

  • Kì í ṣe àwọn ọ̀dọ́ nìkan ni ó jẹ̀bi; àyíká ìgbésí ayé òde-òní náà ni ó tún jẹ̀bi. It is not only young people who are at fault; the pace of modern life is also at fault. negated focus ("kì í ṣe... ni") paired with a second affirmative focus ("...ni ó tún jẹ̀bi") to redirect emphasis
  • Kì í ṣe Túndé ni ó sọ bẹ́ẹ̀; Adé ni. It is not Túndé who said that; it is Adé. contrastive focus correcting a misattributed subject; "ni" alone can stand as a short focused answer

Contrastive focus does not merely restate information; it actively rejects one candidate and substitutes another. In argument and debate, this structure allows a speaker to correct a claim precisely, rather than issuing a vague denial.

Question Focus and Focused Answers

Pattern

Ta/Kí/Níbo/Ọjọ́ wo + ni + (ìyókù ìbéèrè)? → Ìdáhùn-fojúsùn + ni + (ìyókù gbólóhùn).

Wh-words such as "ta" (who), "kí" (what), and "níbo" (where) combine with "ni" to form focused questions, which anticipate a focused answer built on the same pattern. A learner who answers a focused question with an unfocused clause produces a grammatically possible but rhetorically flat response, missing the parallel structure the question invites.

Ta ni ó kọ́kọ́ sọ ọ̀rọ̀ yìí? Ìyá Àgbà ni ó kọ́kọ́ sọ ọ́.

Who first said this? It was Ìyá Àgbà who first said it. The focused question and the focused answer share the same fronting-plus-"ni" structure, making the exchange feel sharply targeted rather than generic.

Focus with Strong Pronouns and Discourse Coordination

Recognition note — Strong pronouns carry the emphatic load

When the focused constituent is a pronoun rather than a full noun phrase, Yorùbá typically uses a strong or emphatic pronoun rather than a weak subject or object pronoun, since weak pronouns cannot bear the emphatic weight of a focus construction. Tracking which pronoun form is required — and keeping reference clear across a longer argument that focuses different participants in turn — is part of advanced discourse control, not a separate grammar topic.

As in Chapter 5's treatment of serialization, choosing whether to focus a constituent is not a stylistic option layered on top of a finished sentence; it is a decision about which piece of information the sentence is organized to deliver. A persuasive Yorùbá argument depends on choosing focus deliberately, sentence by sentence, rather than defaulting to plain declarative word order throughout.

Model Dialogue

Ipò: Adé wà lórí rédíò àdúgbò, ó ń dáhùn ìbéèrè olùgbọ́ kan nípa ìdí ìhùwàsí ìkíni.
Setting: Adé is on a community radio program, responding to a listener's question about the reasons behind greeting behavior.

OLÙDÁ RÉDÍÒ Ẹnìkan pè wá lóní, ó sọ pé àwọn ọ̀dọ́ nìkan ni ó jẹ̀bi ìyípadà tí ó ṣẹlẹ̀ sí bí àwọn èèyàn ṣe ń kí ara wọn. Kí ni o rò nípa èyí, Adé? Someone called us today and said it is only young people who are to blame for the change in how people greet one another. What do you think about this, Adé?
ADÉ Kì í ṣe àwọn ọ̀dọ́ nìkan ni ó jẹ̀bi; àyíká ìgbésí ayé òde-òní náà ni ó tún jẹ̀bi. Ìyára tí gbogbo ẹni ń gbé báyìí ni ó ń dín àkókò ìkíni kù, kì í ṣe àìbìkítà àwọn ọ̀dọ́ nìkan. It is not only young people who are at fault; the pace of modern life is also at fault. It is the speed at which everyone now lives that is reducing the time given to greeting, not only the carelessness of young people.
OLÙDÁ RÉDÍÒ Ta ni o rò pé ó lè yí ipò yìí padà? Who do you think can change this situation?
ADÉ Àwọn òbí àti àwọn olùkọ́ ni ó kọ́kọ́ ní ojúṣe náà, àmọ́ àwa fúnra wa pẹ̀lú ni ó yẹ kí a máa fi àpẹẹrẹ hàn ní ojoojúmọ́. It is parents and teachers who first bear that responsibility, but it is we ourselves too who ought to be setting the example every day.
OLÙDÁ RÉDÍÒ Kí ni ohun kan ṣoṣo tí o fẹ́ kí olùgbọ́ wa rántí lẹ́yìn ìjíròrò yìí? What is the one thing you want our listeners to remember after this discussion?
ADÉ Ìbọ̀wọ̀ ni kì í yí padà pẹ̀lú àkókò; ọ̀nà tí a ń gbà fi hàn án ni ó lè yí padà. It is respect that does not change with time; it is the way we choose to show it that can change.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Correction through redirection, not blunt denial

    In many Yorùbá public and community settings, correcting or disagreeing with a claim is often done through careful redirection rather than blunt denial, particularly when speaking to or about elders, officials, or a wider audience. Contrastive focus constructions are well suited to this style: they allow a speaker to acknowledge part of a claim while precisely redirecting where the emphasis should fall, as Adé does in the Model Dialogue. This chapter's grammar reflects a genuinely valued rhetorical style, not only a syntactic option.

    Regional Note — Debate and rhetorical style vary

    The pacing, directness, and formality of public argument can vary by region, setting, and speaker community; some contexts favor rapid, layered contrastive focus, while others favor slower, more measured exchanges with longer pauses between turns. The radio dialogue and community-meeting passage in this chapter are adapted, composite illustrations for instructional purposes and should not be treated as a single fixed or universal account of Yorùbá public debate.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Dropping the resumptive pronoun in subject focus

    Error: Writing Adé ni ka ìwé náà, fronting the subject with "ni" but failing to leave the required resumptive subject pronoun in the clause.

    Correction: Write Adé ni ó ka ìwé náà, keeping the resumptive "ó" in subject position exactly as shown in the Grammar section.

    Common Mistake — Using a weak pronoun where focus requires a strong pronoun

    Error: Attempting to focus a pronoun with a weak form, producing an unnatural or ungrammatical fronted construction.

    Correction: Use the appropriate strong or emphatic pronoun whenever the focused constituent is a pronoun, since weak pronouns cannot carry the emphatic weight of a focus construction.

    Fún akẹ́kọ̀ọ́ tí ń kọ́ ẹ̀kọ́ fúnra rẹ̀ — For the independent learner

    • Collect three examples of "ni" focus constructions you encounter in reading, conversation, or media, and identify whether each is subject focus, object focus, predicate focus, or contrastive focus.
    • When listening to any argument, debate, or public discussion, note where a speaker uses focus to redirect a claim rather than simply denying it.
    • Practice converting a plain declarative sentence into subject-focus, object-focus, and predicate-focus versions, and compare their rhetorical effects.
    • Continue applying the Chapter 3 four-pass editing procedure to any Yorùbá writing you produce, including persuasive and argumentative passages.

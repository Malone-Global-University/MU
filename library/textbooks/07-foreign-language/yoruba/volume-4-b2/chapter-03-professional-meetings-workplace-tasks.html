Professional Meetings and Workplace Tasks · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Professional, Academic, Civic, and Cross-Regional Yorùbá

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Participate in a workplace meeting by proposing, agreeing with, and responding to ideas.
  2. Report decisions made in a meeting using consolidated reported-speech structures developed for professional contexts.
  3. Express obligation and necessity using modal particles such as gbọ́dọ̀ and yẹ kí.
  4. State future plans and assigned tasks using consolidated future forms.
  5. Use serial verb constructions to describe multi-step workplace procedures.
  6. Listen to and read meeting talk and minutes, identifying decisions, obligations, and assigned tasks.
  7. Write meeting minutes and a short task summary in Standard Written Yorùbá.

Opening Communication Scenario

Adé's request letter from Chapter 2 was approved, and on his return from the training he attends his first full staff meeting at the community development office. The team discusses which tasks from the training should be shared with the wider office, who will be responsible for each task, and by what date the work must be finished. Ẹnjinia Kúnlé asks Adé to take notes and later circulate a short written summary of what was decided.

  1. This chapter teaches how to participate in a meeting: proposing, agreeing, and assigning responsibility.
  2. It develops reported decisions, obligation and necessity, future plans, and serial procedures inside the functional task of producing minutes.
  3. By the end of the chapter, you will produce meeting minutes and a task summary of your own, following the model Adé uses.

Pronunciation Focus

Focus — Meeting speech rhythm and turn-taking

Meeting speech moves faster than formal letter reading, and speakers frequently interrupt each other briefly to agree, propose, or confirm understanding. This chapter's pronunciation focus is producing short, tone-accurate turns at a natural conversational pace, without flattening tone under time pressure.

  • Practice the short agreement turn Ó yẹ; ẹ jẹ́ ká ṣe bẹ́ẹ̀ ("Fine; let us do so") with clear, unhurried tone despite its brevity.
  • Pay special attention to gbọ́dọ̀, where the high tone on both syllables must remain audible even in fast meeting speech.
  • Practice reading a list of assigned tasks aloud with a brief pause between each item, as in real minutes read back to a group.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter develops reported decisions, obligation and necessity with modal particles, future-oriented task assignment, and serial verb constructions for describing multi-step workplace procedures.

Reporting a decision

Pattern

A pinnu pé [ẹnikẹ́ni] yóò [ìṣe] kí ó tó [ọjọ́ ìparí].

Meeting decisions are reported using the impersonal subject "a" ("one/we") plus pinnu pé ("decide that"), followed by a complement clause naming who will do what and by when.

A pinnu pé Adé yóò kọ àkọsílẹ̀ ìpàdé kí ó tó di ọjọ́ Jimọ́.

It was decided that Adé would write the meeting minutes before Friday.

Obligation and necessity with `gbọ́dọ̀` and `yẹ kí`

  • Olúkúlùkù wa gbọ́dọ̀ fi ìjábọ̀ rẹ̀ ránṣẹ́ kí ọjọ́ ìparí tó dé. Each of us must submit their report before the deadline arrives. gbọ́dọ̀ + verb, strong obligation, introduced this chapter
  • Ó yẹ kí a jíròrò lórí ọ̀ràn yìí kí a tó pinnu ohunkóhun. It is necessary that we discuss this matter before we decide anything. yẹ kí + subjunctive-like clause, softer necessity, introduced this chapter
Introduced — Future plans and assigned tasks

Task assignment in meetings relies on the consolidated future particle yóò, now applied specifically to naming who is responsible for what and by when: Kúnlé yóò rí i dájú pé a fi gbogbo ohun èlò ránṣẹ́ ní ọ̀sẹ̀ tí ń bọ̀ ("Kúnlé will make sure all the materials are sent next week"). Learners combine this future form with ojúṣe ("responsibility") to state ownership of a task clearly.

Introduced — Serial verbs in workplace procedures

Multi-step office procedures are described through serial verb chains rather than a string of English-style "and" clauses: a óò kọ ìwé, tẹ̀ ẹ́, kí a sì fi ránṣẹ́ sí gbogbo èèyàn ("we will write the document, print it, and send it to everyone"). Each verb shares the same subject and moves the procedure forward one step at a time. This resultative and event-chain serialization, receptive since Volume III, is now developed for controlled workplace production.

Consolidated — Reference chains continue into workplace writing

The reference-chain control developed in Chapter 2 for formal letters continues here: in minutes, each named person's tasks and responsibilities must remain traceable across every sentence in which they are mentioned, exactly as Ẹnjinia Kúnlé requires of Adé's notes.

Model Dialogue

Ipò: Àwọn òṣìṣẹ́ ọ́fíìsì ìdàgbàsókè àdúgbò ń jíròrò lórí ohun tí wọ́n kọ́ ní ìdánilẹ́kọ̀ọ́, àti bí wọ́n ṣe lè pín iṣẹ́ láàrin ara wọn.
Setting: The community development office staff are discussing what they learned at the training and how to divide the resulting tasks among themselves.

ẸNJ. KÚNLÉ Ẹ jọ̀wọ́, ẹ jẹ́ ká bẹ̀rẹ̀ ìpàdé yìí. Adé, kí ni ìmọ̀ràn rẹ lórí bí a ṣe lè lo ohun tí o kọ́ ní ìdánilẹ́kọ̀ọ́ náà? Please, let us begin this meeting. Adé, what is your proposal for how we might use what you learned at the training?
ADÉ Mo rò pé ó yẹ kí a kọ ìwé kúkúrú kan, kí a sì fi ránṣẹ́ sí gbogbo àwọn akẹ́kọ̀ọ́ iṣẹ́ mìíràn. I think it would be fitting for us to write a short document and send it to all the other interns.
ẸNJ. KÚNLÉ Ó yẹ; ẹ jẹ́ ká ṣe bẹ́ẹ̀. Adé, ìwọ gbọ́dọ̀ kọ ìwé náà, kí Bọ́lá sì ṣe àyẹ̀wò rẹ̀ kí ó tó jáde. Fine; let us do so. Adé, you must write the document, and Bọ́lá must review it before it goes out.
BỌ́LÁ Mo gbà. Ǹjẹ́ a le pinnu ọjọ́ ìparí fún iṣẹ́ yìí? I agree. Can we decide on a deadline for this task?
ẸNJ. KÚNLÉ A pinnu pé Adé yóò kọ ìwé náà kí ó tó di ọjọ́ Ẹtì, Bọ́lá yóò sì parí àyẹ̀wò rẹ̀ ní ọjọ́ Jimọ́. Adé, ìwọ ni yóò gba ojúṣe láti kọ àkọsílẹ̀ ìpàdé yìí pẹ̀lú. It is decided that Adé will write the document before Wednesday, and Bọ́lá will finish her review on Friday. Adé, you will take the responsibility to write these meeting minutes as well.

    • Include

    • a reported decision with pinnu pé
    • an obligation with gbọ́dọ̀
    • a necessity with yẹ kí
    • at least one serial verb chain describing a procedure

    • Every vowel that should carry a high or low tone mark has one.

    • gbọ́dọ̀ and yẹ kí are both used correctly and distinctly.
    • Every task has a named person responsible and a stated deadline.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Meetings and consensus in Yorùbá workplaces

    In many Yorùbá-speaking workplaces, decisions are commonly presented as reached collectively, even when one person has guided the discussion, and open disagreement with a senior colleague is often expressed indirectly or softened with phrases such as mo rò pé ("I think that") rather than stated as flat contradiction. Meeting minutes typically preserve this collective framing by using the impersonal a ("we/one") for decisions rather than singling out an individual as having decided alone.

    Regional Note — Meeting formality across institutions

    Some larger institutions and government offices use additional formal meeting conventions, such as reading previous minutes aloud before new business, beyond what a small community office typically requires. These additional conventions remain recognition-only for now; Chapter 5 will return to public-service and institutional settings in more depth.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Confusing `gbọ́dọ̀` and `yẹ kí`

    Error: Using gbọ́dọ̀ for every kind of recommendation, including soft suggestions, for example writing "A gbọ́dọ̀ jíròrò lórí ọ̀ràn yìí" when only a mild suggestion is meant.

    Correction: Reserve gbọ́dọ̀ for strong obligation, and use yẹ kí when the meaning is closer to "it would be fitting" or "it is advisable," as in "Ó yẹ kí a jíròrò lórí ọ̀ràn yìí."

    Common Mistake — Coordinating instead of serializing

    Error: Describing a multi-step procedure with a repeated coordinator, treating each step as a fully separate clause rather than a shared-subject serial chain.

    Correction: Where the same subject performs each step in sequence, link the verbs directly in a serial chain, as Ẹnjinia Kúnlé does in the model minutes: "a óò kọ ìwé náà, ṣe àtúnṣe sí i, kí a sì tẹ̀ ẹ́ jáde."

    Practical Performance Task

    Iṣẹ́ aṣeyọrí — Abala 3

    Àkọsílẹ̀ Ìpàdé àti Ìjábọ̀ Iṣẹ́ — Meeting Minutes and Task Summary

    Produce complete meeting minutes and a short task summary, entirely in Standard Written Yorùbá, following the structure taught in this chapter.

    Component 1 — Meeting Minutes

    • 150–200 words, including a proposal, a reported decision, an obligation, a necessity, and a serial verb procedure.
    • Clear responsibility and deadline for each assigned task.

    Component 2 — Task Summary

    • A short summary (60–90 words) listing who must do what and by when, based on the minutes.

    Component 3 — Spoken Explanation

    • Explain the meeting's decisions and assignments aloud (60–90 seconds) to a partner, instructor, or recording.

    Assessment note: This task provides evidence of reported-decision accuracy, obligation/necessity control, serial-verb production, reference-chain clarity, and tone/orthography precision, drawing on consolidated Volume III systems plus this chapter's newly introduced structures.

    Answer Key

    Listening Practice Questions

    1. Adé says the most important elements are stating the decision clearly, assigning responsibility to the right person, and stating a deadline for each task.
    2. Adé explains that gbọ́dọ̀ signals strong necessity, something that must be done, while yẹ kí signals something fitting or advisable, a softer recommendation.
    3. Adé plans to use these principles when he writes his meeting minutes and short task summary.

    Reading Practice Questions

    1. Adé proposes writing a short document about the training and sending it to other interns; everyone present agrees.
    2. Adé must finish writing the document by Wednesday; Bọ́lá must finish reviewing it by Friday.
    3. Ẹnjinia Kúnlé describes writing the document, revising it, and printing it before the following Saturday, using a serial verb chain.
    4. Adé is also given the task of writing the meeting minutes and sending them to everyone present.

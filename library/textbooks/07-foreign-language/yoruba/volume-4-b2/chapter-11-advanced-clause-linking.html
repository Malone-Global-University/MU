Ìsopọ̀ Gbólóhùn Onípele Gíga

Advanced Clause Linking · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Professional, Academic, Civic, and Cross-Regional Yorùbá

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Link clauses accurately using reason connectors such as nítorí pé and nítorí náà.
  2. Express contrast and concession using connectors such as ṣùgbọ́n, bí ó tilẹ̀ jẹ́ pé, and síbẹ̀síbẹ̀.
  3. Express purpose using kí-clauses and result using tó bẹ́ẹ̀ tí and related frames.
  4. Sequence time clauses using connectors such as nígbà tí, kí ó tó, and lẹ́yìn tí.
  5. Distinguish formal, academic-register connectors from everyday spoken connectors.
  6. Punctuate long, multi-clause Yorùbá sentences clearly without losing tone accuracy.
  7. Recognize, without independent production, how these connectors interact with reported and complement clauses.
  8. Produce a structured analytical response that links several clause types in a single organized text.

Opening Communication Scenario

Adé and Ẹ̀gbọ́n Fúnmi's radio station has been invited to submit a short written analysis to a regional media association, explaining why community radio remains important even as more listeners move to online platforms. The analysis must connect reasons, contrasts, purposes, and outcomes into clearly organized sentences rather than a list of short, disconnected statements.

  1. This chapter develops the subordination and coordination systems that have been building since earlier chapters, applying them to longer, more formal sentences.
  2. No entirely new clause type is introduced. Instead, reason, contrast, concession, purpose, result, and time clauses are expanded, combined, and punctuated at a more advanced level.
  3. By the end of the chapter, you will write a structured analytical response that uses several of these connectors together.

Pronunciation Focus — Long-Sentence Rhythm

Focus — Maintaining tone across long, multi-clause sentences

As sentences grow longer through clause linking, learners often flatten tone toward the end of the sentence or rush through connector phrases. This chapter's pronunciation focus is not a new sound feature but a fluency skill: preserving accurate high, mid, and low tone across an entire multi-clause sentence.

  • Read each connector phrase — nítorí pé, bí ó tilẹ̀ jẹ́ pé, síbẹ̀síbẹ̀, tó bẹ́ẹ̀ tí, nígbà tí, kí ó tó, lẹ́yìn tí — slowly and clearly before attempting a full sentence.
  • When reading a long sentence aloud, pause briefly at each clause boundary rather than letting tone blur across the pause.
  • Practice the model dialogue and reading passage below at a measured pace before increasing speed.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter develops subordination and coordination that were introduced and expanded in earlier chapters. It does not introduce a new independent clause type; it develops connector range, register distinctions, and punctuation control for reason, contrast, concession, purpose, result, and time relationships.

Reason: nítorí pé / nítorí náà

Pattern

[Clause 1] nítorí pé [Clause 2].

nítorí pé introduces the reason clause; it may follow the main clause or, in more formal writing, the reason may be stated first with nítorí náà linking to the result.

Ilé-iṣẹ́ rédíò wa ṣì ṣe pàtàkì nítorí pé kì í ṣe gbogbo ènìyàn ní àdúgbò wa ló ní ìjápọ̀ ìjìnlẹ̀ tí ó dúró ṣinṣin.

Our radio station is still important because not everyone in our community has stable internet access.

Contrast and Concession: ṣùgbọ́n / bí ó tilẹ̀ jẹ́ pé / síbẹ̀síbẹ̀

  • Ọ̀pọ̀ èèyàn ń gbọ́ ìròyìn lórí ẹ̀rọ ayélujára ní báyìí, ṣùgbọ́n ọ̀pọ̀ tún ń gbọ́ rédíò lójoojúmọ́. Many people now hear news online, but many still listen to the radio every day. ṣùgbọ́n = everyday-register contrast connector
  • Bí ó tilẹ̀ jẹ́ pé ìtànkálẹ̀ ìjìnlẹ̀ ti pọ̀ sí i, ìgbẹ́kẹ̀lé àdúgbò fún rédíò kì í yẹ̀ padà. Even though online reach has increased, community trust in radio does not decline. bí ó tilẹ̀ jẹ́ pé = formal concession connector
  • Síbẹ̀síbẹ̀, ilé-iṣẹ́ wa ń wá ọ̀nà láti fi kún iṣẹ́ rédíò pẹ̀lú ìtàkurọ̀sọ orí ayélujára. Nevertheless, our station is looking for ways to add online broadcasting to its radio work. síbẹ̀síbẹ̀ = formal sentence-initial contrast connector
Register note — ṣùgbọ́n vs. bí ó tilẹ̀ jẹ́ pé / síbẹ̀síbẹ̀

ṣùgbọ́n is appropriate across most registers, including everyday conversation. Bí ó tilẹ̀ jẹ́ pé and síbẹ̀síbẹ̀ belong to a more formal, written, and academic register and are used when a text needs a more deliberate, analytical tone.

Purpose: kí-clauses

Pattern

[Clause 1] kí [Subject] [Verb Phrase].

kí introduces a purpose clause; the verb phrase that follows kí does not take the same tense/aspect marking as an ordinary main clause.

A ń kọ́ àwọn òṣìṣẹ́ wa nípa ìtàkurọ̀sọ orí ayélujára kí wọ́n lè bá àwọn ọ̀dọ́ sọ̀rọ̀ dáadáa.

We are training our staff in online broadcasting so that they can communicate well with young people.

Result: tó bẹ́ẹ̀ tí

  • Iye àwọn tí ń tẹ̀lé wa lórí ẹ̀rọ ayélujára ti pọ̀ tó bẹ́ẹ̀ tí a fi ń rò ọ̀nà láti fi kún ìsìn wa. The number of our online followers has grown to such an extent that we are considering ways to expand our service. tó bẹ́ẹ̀ tí = "to such an extent that" (formal result connector)

Time Clauses: nígbà tí / kí ó tó / lẹ́yìn tí

  • Nígbà tí a bẹ̀rẹ̀ ilé-iṣẹ́ yìí, kì í ṣe gbogbo àdúgbò ni ó gba wa gbọ́. When we started this station, not everyone in the community believed in us.
  • Kí ó tó di ọdún márùn-ún, ilé-iṣẹ́ wa ti di orísun ìròyìn tí ọ̀pọ̀ ń gbẹ́kẹ̀lé. Before five years had passed, our station had become a news source many relied on.
  • Lẹ́yìn tí a ti fi ìtàkurọ̀sọ orí ayélujára kún iṣẹ́ wa, iye àwọn olùgbọ́ wa ti fẹ̀ sí i. After we added online broadcasting to our work, the number of our listeners has expanded.
Punctuation in long sentences

When a sentence links several clauses, place a comma before the connector when the reason, contrast, purpose, or result clause is fronted, and generally omit the comma when the connector clause follows the main clause and remains short. Long sentences with more than two linked clauses should be reviewed to confirm each clause boundary is clear to the reader, and split into two sentences if a single sentence becomes difficult to follow.

Recognition note — Complement and reported clauses

The passages and dialogue below may include clauses following verbs of saying, thinking, or believing (complement and reported clauses). These are receptive at this stage; you are not required to produce new complement-clause structures independently in this chapter. Focus your own production on the reason, contrast, concession, purpose, result, and time connectors introduced above.

Model Dialogue

Ipò: Adé àti Ẹ̀gbọ́n Fúnmi ń jíroro àkọlé fún ìwádìí kúkúrú tí wọn yóò fi ránṣẹ́ sí àjọ àwọn ilé-iṣẹ́ agbéròyìnjáde àgbègbè.
Setting: Adé and Ẹ̀gbọ́n Fúnmi are discussing the topic for a short analysis they will submit to the regional media association.

ẸGBỌ́N FÚNMI Kí ni a fẹ́ sọ nípa ìdí tí rédíò àdúgbò fi ṣì ṣe pàtàkì? What do we want to say about why community radio is still important?
ADÉ Ó yẹ kí a bẹ̀rẹ̀ pẹ̀lú ìdí, nítorí pé kì í ṣe gbogbo ènìyàn ní àdúgbò wa ló ní ìjápọ̀ ìjìnlẹ̀ tí ó dúró ṣinṣin. We should begin with the reason, because not everyone in our community has stable internet access.
ẸGBỌ́N FÚNMI Bí ó tilẹ̀ jẹ́ pé ìtànkálẹ̀ ìjìnlẹ̀ ti pọ̀ sí i, ǹjẹ́ ìgbẹ́kẹ̀lé àdúgbò fún rédíò ti yẹ̀ padà? Even though online reach has increased, has community trust in radio declined?
ADÉ Rárá o, síbẹ̀síbẹ̀, ó dà bí ẹni pé ìgbẹ́kẹ̀lé wọn ti pọ̀ sí i, tó bẹ́ẹ̀ tí a fi ń rò ọ̀nà láti fi kún iṣẹ́ wa. No, nevertheless, it seems their trust has actually increased, to such an extent that we are considering ways to expand our work.
ẸGBỌ́N FÚNMI Nígbà tí a bẹ̀rẹ̀, a kò rí bẹ́ẹ̀ mọ́. Kí ni ó yí padà lẹ́yìn tí a fi ìtàkurọ̀sọ orí ayélujára kún iṣẹ́ wa? When we started, we did not see it that way. What changed after we added online broadcasting to our work?
ADÉ A gbọ́dọ̀ ṣàlàyé gbogbo èyí ní kedere kí àwọn kà á lè lóye ìdàgbàsókè wa dáadáa. We must explain all of this clearly so that readers can understand our growth well.

Listening Practice

Ask an instructor, study partner, or qualified Yorùbá speaker to read the passage below aloud, or read it aloud yourself and then cover it before answering.

If no speaker is available, this is a Listening Script for Independent Use. Machine text-to-speech must be checked by a qualified Yorùbá speaker before use as a pronunciation model.

"Ilé-iṣẹ́ rédíò wa ti wà fún ọdún mẹ́wàá, nítorí náà a ti rí ọ̀pọ̀lọpọ̀ ìyípadà nínú bí àwọn ènìyàn ṣe ń gba ìròyìn. Bí ó tilẹ̀ jẹ́ pé ọ̀pọ̀ ọ̀dọ́ ń lo ẹ̀rọ ayélujára ju rédíò lọ ní báyìí, síbẹ̀síbẹ̀, àwọn àgbà nínú àdúgbò ṣì ń gbẹ́kẹ̀lé rédíò fún ìròyìn tí ó ṣe pàtàkì. Lẹ́yìn tí a ti rí ìdàgbàsókè yìí, a pinnu láti kọ́ àwọn òṣìṣẹ́ wa síi kí wọ́n lè bá àwọn ọ̀dọ́ àti àgbà méjèèjì sọ̀rọ̀ dáadáa."

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Analytical writing and measured claims

    In many Yorùbá-language professional and media contexts, an analytical piece is considered stronger when it acknowledges a contrasting point before stating a conclusion, rather than presenting only one side. Connectors such as bí ó tilẹ̀ jẹ́ pé and síbẹ̀síbẹ̀ allow a writer to show this kind of balanced reasoning.

    Regional Note — Connector choice in spoken registers

    In many everyday spoken contexts across Yorùbá-speaking regions, speakers favor ṣùgbọ́n over bí ó tilẹ̀ jẹ́ pé or síbẹ̀síbẹ̀, which are more common in formal writing, broadcast journalism, and academic speech. This is a register difference, not a regional error.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Using a formal connector in a casual exchange

    Error: Opening a casual spoken remark to a close friend with bí ó tilẹ̀ jẹ́ pé, which sounds unnaturally formal in that setting.

    Correction: Use ṣùgbọ́n in everyday spoken exchanges, and reserve bí ó tilẹ̀ jẹ́ pé and síbẹ̀síbẹ̀ for formal writing and speech.

    Common Mistake — Overloading a single sentence with too many linked clauses

    Error: Combining a reason, a contrast, a purpose, and a result clause into one long, unbroken sentence, which becomes difficult for a reader to follow.

    Correction: Distribute linked clauses across two or three shorter, clearly punctuated sentences, as modeled in the reading passage above.

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Select and define a professional or civic project appropriate to a specific audience.
  2. Produce a structured project report using the formal writing conventions developed across this volume.
  3. Prepare and deliver a structured formal presentation summarizing the project.
  4. Write a concise executive summary that orients a reader before the full report.
  5. Anticipate and respond to clarifying and challenging questions in a formal question-and-answer session.
  6. Apply the full editing checklist from Chapter 18 to the written components of the project.
  7. Integrate tone, orthography, particles, pronoun/reference control, serialization, focus, word order, and register across one sustained piece of work.
  8. Maintain the Standard Written Yorùbá production baseline and full diacritics throughout every project component.

Opening Communication Scenario

Adé has been asked by a community education network to lead a small project: choose a professional or civic topic relevant to the network's work, prepare a written report and an executive summary, and present findings to a panel that includes both fluent Yorùbá speakers and colleagues who prefer English for technical detail. Kúnlé, having just revised his own report with Adé's help in Chapter 18, is preparing a parallel project on a different topic and the two agree to review each other's drafts and rehearse their question-and-answer sessions together.

  1. This chapter introduces no major new grammar. It integrates every system activated across Volume IV into one sustained project.
  2. Learners choose their own professional or civic topic, drawing on vocabulary and structures from earlier chapters in this volume.
  3. By the end of the chapter, you will produce a project report, an executive summary, a presentation, and a rehearsed question-and-answer exchange.

Pronunciation Focus — Delivering a Formal Q&A

Focus — Tone and pacing under unscripted pressure

A rehearsed presentation is easier to keep tonally accurate than an unscripted answer to an unexpected question. This chapter's pronunciation focus is maintaining tone and vowel quality when responding spontaneously, not only when reading prepared text.

  • Slow down slightly at the start of an unscripted answer; rushed speech is where tone flattening and vowel substitution are most likely to appear.
  • Use the acknowledgment frame ìbéèrè yẹn ṣe pàtàkì as a brief pause that buys a moment to organize a tonally accurate response rather than an empty filler.
  • If you hear yourself flatten a tone mid-answer, it is acceptable to restate the word correctly and continue; a brief self-correction is more professional than an uncorrected error.

Grammar and Project Integration

This chapter introduces no major new grammar. Instead, it develops the discourse skill of integrating every system activated in Volume IV — tone, orthography, particles, pronoun/reference, serialization, focus, word order, and register — across the sustained, multi-part structure of a single project.

Structuring an Executive Summary

Pattern

Àfojúsùn → Ọ̀nà tí a gbà ṣe é → Àbájáde àkọ́kọ́ → Àbá tí a fi lélẹ̀

Goal → method used → main finding → recommendation offered. An executive summary must orient a reader in a few sentences before the full report develops each point. This pattern reuses source-attribution and cause/result structures already consolidated earlier in the volume, applied here to a compressed genre.

Àfojúsùn iṣẹ́ yìí ni láti wo bí àwọn ọ̀dọ́ ní àdúgbò kan ṣe ń lo Yorùbá ní ilé-iṣẹ́. A lo ìbéèrè àti ìjíròrò kúkúrú láti kó ìmọ̀ jọ. Àbájáde fi hàn pé ọ̀pọ̀ ń lo Yorùbá ní ilé àmọ́ Gẹ̀ẹ́sì ní ibi iṣẹ́. Nítorí náà, a dábàá kí ilé-iṣẹ́ náà pèsè àwọn ìdánilẹ́kọ̀ọ́ kúkúrú ní èdè Yorùbá.

This project's goal was to examine how young people in one neighborhood use Yorùbá at work. We used interviews and short discussions to gather information. Findings showed that many use Yorùbá at home but English at work. Therefore, we recommend that the company provide short Yorùbá-language workshops.

Cross-Referencing Between Summary and Report

  • Gẹ́gẹ́ bí mo ti sọ nínú àkọsílẹ̀, apá kẹta ṣàlàyé ìdí tí àbájáde yìí fi ṣe pàtàkì. As I stated in the report, the third section explains why this finding matters. discourse-linking frame connecting summary claims back to specific report sections

Cross-referencing requires the same reference-chain clarity developed in Chapter 18: a phrase like apá kẹta ("the third section") must point unambiguously to one identifiable part of the report, not to a vague "somewhere in here."

Managing a Question-and-Answer Exchange

Weaker Response

Mi ò mọ̀ dájú, àmọ́ ó lè jẹ́ bẹ́ẹ̀.

Vague, unanchored, and does not point back to any part of the report.

Stronger Response

Ìbéèrè yẹn ṣe pàtàkì. Gẹ́gẹ́ bí mo ti sọ nínú àkọsílẹ̀, a kò lè jẹ́rìí sí i pátápátá nítorí iye ènìyàn tí a bèèrè lọ́wọ́ wọn kò tó; ẹ jẹ́ kí n ṣàlàyé síwájú sí i.

Acknowledges the question, cross-references the report, states the limitation precisely, and signals continued explanation.

A strong Q&A answer applies the same register consistency and reference clarity required of the written report. Reference Matrix Register/Style — an answer that shifts into vague or overly casual language undermines a presentation that was otherwise formally consistent.

Recognition note — Integration, not addition

This chapter's grammar work is not about learning something new. It is about noticing which matrix category is at stake in each sentence of your own project — tone, orthography, particles, reference, serialization, word order, or register — and applying Chapter 18's diagnostic habit to your own writing before it is finished, rather than only after.

Model Dialogue

Ipò: Adé àti Kúnlé ń darapọ̀ mọ́ra láti ṣe àdàánà fún ìgbà tí wọn yóò dá ìgbìmọ̀ lóhùn ìbéèrè lẹ́yìn ìgbékalẹ̀ iṣẹ́ wọn.
Setting: Adé and Kúnlé are rehearsing together for the question-and-answer session that will follow their project presentations.

KÚNLÉ Bí wọ́n bá bi mí pé kí ni àfojúsùn iṣẹ́ mi gan-an, kí ni ó yẹ kí n dáhùn? If they ask me what exactly my project's objective is, what should I answer?
ADÉ Bẹ̀rẹ̀ pẹ̀lú gbólóhùn kan ṣoṣo tí ó ṣàlàyé àfojúsùn náà, gẹ́gẹ́ bí ó ti wà nínú àkótán ọ̀rọ̀ rẹ; má ṣe fi ọ̀pọ̀lọpọ̀ kúlẹ̀kúlẹ̀ bẹ̀rẹ̀, kí o tó lọ sí ìsọfúnni tó jinlẹ̀ jù. Start with just one sentence that explains the objective, as it appears in your executive summary; don't begin with too much detail before you move to deeper information.
KÚNLÉ Bí ẹnì kan bá tako àbájáde mi, kí ni mo lè sọ tí kò ní jẹ́ kí n dà rú? If someone challenges my finding, what can I say that won't make me flustered?
ADÉ Lo "ìbéèrè yẹn ṣe pàtàkì" láti fún ara rẹ ní àyè díẹ̀, lẹ́yìn náà tọ́ka padà sí apá kan pàtó nínú àkọsílẹ̀ rẹ tí ó ṣe àtìlẹ́yìn fún ohun tí o sọ. Má ṣe dáhùn ní àìdánilójú tí kò tọ́ka sí ohunkóhun. Use "that is an important question" to give yourself a little room, then point back to a specific section in your report that supports what you said. Don't answer vaguely without pointing to anything.
KÚNLÉ Ó yé mi. Ǹjẹ́ a lè ṣe àdàánà ìbéèrè àti ìdáhùn pẹ̀lú ara wa lẹ́ẹ̀kan sí i kí ó tó di ọ̀la? I understand. Can we rehearse the question-and-answer exchange with each other once more before tomorrow?
ADÉ Dájúdájú. Ẹ jẹ́ kí a bẹ̀rẹ̀ pẹ̀lú àkótán ọ̀rọ̀ rẹ, kí n sì bi ọ́ ní ìbéèrè méjì tí ó lè le fún ọ. Certainly. Let's start with your executive summary, and I'll ask you two questions that might be difficult for you.

Writing Workshop

Choose a professional or civic topic relevant to your own interests or community. Write a project report (250–350 words) with clear sections, an executive summary (80–120 words), and prepare notes for a formal presentation and question-and-answer session. Full tone marks and dot-under letters are required throughout every component.

  • Choose a professional or civic topic and state its objective in one sentence.
  • Draft your project report with clear sections: introduction, method, findings, and recommendation.
  • Write your executive summary using the pattern introduced in this chapter's Grammar section.
  • Apply the full editing checklist from Chapter 18 to both the report and the summary, in order: tone, orthography, particles, pronouns/reference, serialization, word order/clause, register/style.
  • Prepare brief presentation notes and anticipate at least two challenging questions.
  • Draft a rehearsed answer to each anticipated question using the acknowledgment-and-cross-reference pattern.
  • Apply the Project Integration Checkpoint before finalizing.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Presenting work to a mixed panel

    Presenting to a panel that includes both Yorùbá speakers and colleagues who rely on English for technical precision is common in many professional and civic settings. Speakers often acknowledge this directly, offering to clarify a term in either language rather than assuming the audience's linguistic background is uniform.

    Regional Note — Variation in formal presentation norms

    Expectations around how much detail to give before opening the floor to questions, and how directly a challenging question may be phrased, vary across institutions and regions. This chapter's pattern of a brief acknowledgment followed by a specific cross-reference is a widely applicable strategy rather than a single fixed rule.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Opening the executive summary with detail instead of objective

    Error: Beginning the executive summary with background detail or method before stating what the project actually set out to do.

    Correction: State the objective in one clear sentence first, following the pattern Àfojúsùn iṣẹ́ yìí ni láti..., then proceed to method, finding, and recommendation.

    Common Mistake — Answering a challenging question with no anchor

    Error: Responding to a difficult question with a vague statement that does not point back to any specific part of the report.

    Correction: Use ìbéèrè yẹn ṣe pàtàkì to acknowledge the question, then cross-reference a specific section using gẹ́gẹ́ bí mo ti sọ nínú àkọsílẹ̀.

    Answer Key

    Listening Practice Questions

    1. The student first reads the executive summary, which contains a short sentence explaining the project's objective and the main finding.
    2. A panel member asks how the student can be certain the finding is accurate.
    3. The student thanks the panel member for the question and points back to the third section of the report, where the data-gathering method is explained; the panel praises the clarity of the answer and notes that it did not fluster the student.

    1. The group's report contains four sections: introduction, method, findings, and recommendation.
    2. Other students ask three questions, and the group answers each one respectfully, pointing back to a specific section of their report every time.
    3. The teacher praises the group for keeping every part of the project consistent from beginning to end.

    Independent Study Tools

    Fún akẹ́kọ̀ọ́ tí ń kọ́ ẹ̀kọ́ fúnra rẹ̀ — For the independent learner

    • Record your presentation aloud and listen back for tone accuracy under the pressure of continuous speech, not just in isolated practice sentences.
    • Write out three questions you expect to be asked and draft an anchored answer for each before you present.
    • Ask a study partner, or a Yorùbá-speaking contact if available, to ask you an unplanned question so you can practice responding without a script.
    • Apply the Chapter 18 editing checklist to your project report a second time after a short break, since errors are often easier to catch after time away from the text.

Mid-Volume Advanced Performance Assessment · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Professional, Academic, Civic, and Cross-Regional Yorùbá

Chapter Objectives

This chapter introduces no new grammar. By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Demonstrate formal correspondence and institutional request skills from Chapter 2 in a new context.
  2. Report workplace decisions, obligations, and plans using patterns consolidated in Chapter 3.
  3. Explain ideas with source attribution and hedged evidence language from Chapter 4.
  4. Discuss public services, rights, and responsibilities using Chapter 5 patterns.
  5. Summarize and respond to media content using the reported-speech and stance patterns from Chapter 6.
  6. Discuss policy-like material responsibly, without overclaiming expertise, as in Chapter 7.
  7. Produce a public-facing health or environmental explanation as in Chapter 8.
  8. Write a workplace report or proposal using requests, deadlines, numbers, comparisons, conditionals, and hedged modality from Chapter 9.
  9. Demonstrate integrated tone, orthography, register, and fluency control across formal, professional, and civic domains.

Opening Communication Scenario

Adé's radio station has been asked to prepare a short mid-year portfolio for a community advisory board. The portfolio must show that station staff can write formally, report on meetings, explain a topic with sources, discuss a public-service matter, summarize media content, and produce workplace documentation — all in Standard Written Yorùbá.

  1. This chapter does not teach new grammar. It brings together the formal, professional, academic, civic, and public-communication systems from Chapters 1–9.
  2. Each section of this chapter mirrors a skill from an earlier chapter, applied to a new task inside the same mid-year portfolio scenario.
  3. By the end of the chapter, you will complete a cumulative performance task that draws on all nine preceding chapters.

Cumulative Grammar Review

This chapter introduces no new grammar. It reviews and integrates the systems consolidated in Chapters 1–9, applying them to a single mid-year portfolio task.

Review — Formal correspondence and institutional requests (Ch. 2)

Formal openings and closings, politeness, reasons, conditions, and reference chains remain available for any formal letter or request in this chapter's tasks.

Review — Reported decisions and obligations (Ch. 3)

Reported decisions, obligations, future plans, modal particles, and serial procedures remain available for summarizing a meeting or task assignment.

Review — Source attribution and hedging (Ch. 4)

gẹ́gẹ́ bí ... ṣe sọ and related complement, contrast, reason, and hedging frames remain available for explaining ideas using evidence language.

Review — Civic and public-service language (Ch. 5)

Formal questions, conditions, obligations, and institutional vocabulary remain available for discussing rights, responsibilities, and public services.

Review — Reported speech and stance (Ch. 6)

Reported speech, source attribution, stance, focus, and time/aspect tracking remain available for producing a media summary and response.

Review — Careful modality with policy language (Ch. 7)

Conditionals, obligations, prohibitions, and careful modality remain available for discussing rule- or policy-like material without overclaiming expertise.

Review — Cause/result and public advisories (Ch. 8)

Cause/result, advice, warnings, and conditions remain available for producing a public health or environmental explanation.

Review — Requests, deadlines, numbers, and comparisons (Ch. 9)

a ń bèèrè pé kí, ààlà ọjọ́, number and quantity expressions, ju ... lọ, bí ... bá, and ó ṣeéṣe kí remain available for producing a workplace report or proposal.

Model Dialogue

Ipò: Adé àti Ẹ̀gbọ́n Fúnmi ń jíròrò àkójọpọ̀ ìjábọ̀ àárín ọdún fún ìgbìmọ̀ àwùjọ, tí ó nílò àpẹẹrẹ láti abala kọ̀ọ̀kan láti ìbẹ̀rẹ̀pẹ̀pẹ̀ ìdìpọ̀ yìí.
Setting: Adé and Ẹ̀gbọ́n Fúnmi are discussing the mid-year portfolio for the community advisory board, which needs an example drawn from each earlier chapter in this volume.

ẸGBỌ́N FÚNMI Ǹjẹ́ a ti kọ ìwé ìbéèrè náà sí ọ́fíìsì àgbègbè? Have we written the formal request letter to the district office?
ADÉ Bẹ́ẹ̀ni, mo ti kọ ọ́ tán, mo sì ti kọ ìjábọ̀ ìpàdé wa lẹ́yìn ìpinnu tí a ṣe ní ọ̀sẹ̀ tó kọjá. Yes, I have finished writing it, and I have also written the report of our meeting following the decision we made last week.
ẸGBỌ́N FÚNMI Ṣé o ti ṣàlàyé àkọlé ìròyìn wa gẹ́gẹ́ bí àwọn orísun ìwádìí ṣe sọ? Have you explained our report topic according to what the research sources say?
ADÉ Mo ti ṣe bẹ́ẹ̀, mo sì ti fi kún un pẹ̀lú àkọsílẹ̀ nípa ẹ̀tọ́ àti ojúṣe onítòhún nínú ètò ìlera àwùjọ. I have done that, and I have also added a note about the relevant rights and responsibilities in the community health program.
ẸGBỌ́N FÚNMI Ó dára. Ó kù ìwé ìtọ́rẹ̀ ẹ̀rọ náà àti àkópọ̀ ìròyìn tẹlifíṣàn tí a gbọ́ ní ọ̀sẹ̀ yìí. Good. What remains is the equipment proposal and the summary of the television news we heard this week.
ADÉ Mo ti fẹ́rẹ̀ parí wọn. Ó ṣeéṣe kí n parí gbogbo rẹ̀ kí ó tó ọjọ́ Jimọ́, bí kò bá sí ìdíwọ́ mìíràn. I am nearly finished with them. It is possible that I will finish everything before Friday, if there is no other obstacle.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Presenting a portfolio to a community body

    When presenting a mid-year portfolio to a community advisory board or similar body, it is common in many Yorùbá-speaking professional settings to acknowledge the group's oversight role respectfully, to present information in a clear and organized order, and to invite further questions rather than presenting the portfolio as a closed, final account.

    Regional Note — Community advisory structures

    The specific name, structure, and authority of a community advisory board can vary between institutions and regions. This chapter's patterns describe a widely usable formal-register model; learners working within a different institutional structure should treat such differences as labeled variation rather than as errors.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Mixing up source attribution and personal opinion

    Error: Presenting a personal opinion using gẹ́gẹ́ bí ... ṣe sọ as if it were sourced information, which misrepresents the origin of the claim.

    Correction: Reserve gẹ́gẹ́ bí ... ṣe sọ for genuinely sourced information, and mark personal opinion separately, as taught in Chapter 6.

    Common Mistake — Overloading one sentence with too many cumulative elements

    Error: Attempting to combine a formal request, a deadline, a comparison, and a hedge all inside a single overloaded sentence, which reduces clarity.

    Correction: Distribute the required elements across several clear, well-organized sentences, as modeled in the reading passage above.

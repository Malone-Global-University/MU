Media and News Analysis · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Professional, Academic, Civic, and Cross-Regional Yorùbá

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Report what someone else said using consolidated reported-speech frames.
  2. Attribute information to a source using clear attribution phrases.
  3. Recognize and produce stance markers that show a speaker's or writer's attitude toward information.
  4. Use focus constructions to highlight who said something, what was said, or what happened.
  5. Track time and aspect accurately across a reported news item or interview extract.
  6. Listen to and read short news items, interview extracts, and public announcements, identifying source, stance, and sequence.
  7. Produce a media summary and a brief personal response to a news item.

Opening Communication Scenario

Adé volunteers at a community radio station that rebroadcasts short Yorùbá-language news items for residents who prefer to follow public affairs in Yorùbá. This week, Adé must listen to a radio news item about a new community health campaign, identify what the reporter said directly and what officials were quoted as saying, and then prepare a short spoken summary and a written response for the station's community bulletin — noting clearly which parts are the reporter's own words and which parts are reported from an official's statement.

  1. This chapter teaches how to report what someone said, attribute information to its source, and track a speaker's stance toward that information.
  2. It develops focus and time/aspect control inside the functional task of following and retelling a news item accurately.
  3. By the end of the chapter, you will produce a media summary and response of your own, following the same process Adé uses at the radio station.

Pronunciation Focus

Focus — Listening to media tempo and style

Broadcast news is typically read faster and with more even intonation than an official announcement, and a reporter's own commentary often shifts pace slightly when moving from reported fact to a quoted statement. This chapter's pronunciation focus is following that shift in tempo and correctly hearing tone on attribution and stance words, where a missed tone can blur the line between the reporter's voice and a quoted source.

  • Practice the attribution phrase gẹ́gẹ́ bí ... ṣe sọ ("according to...") with clear tone on each syllable, since it marks the boundary between reported material and the reporter's own words.
  • Pay special attention to ó ní pé versus ó rò pé, where tone and vowel quality distinguish "said that" from "believes that."
  • Practice reading a short news item at a faster, even pace, then slowing slightly and adjusting pitch when introducing a direct quotation.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter develops reported speech, source attribution, stance marking, focus for highlighting sources or events, and careful time/aspect tracking across a reported news item.

Reported speech with `ó ní pé`

Pattern

[Ẹni tí ó sọ] ó ní pé [ọ̀rọ̀ tí a ròyìn]

Reported speech is built with the complementizer pé after a speech verb such as ní ("say"). The reported clause keeps its own subject, tense/aspect, and polarity; it is not compressed into an English-style indirect-question shape.

Ọ̀gá ilé-iṣẹ́ ìlera ìjọba ní pé àjàkálẹ̀ àrùn kò ì tí ì bẹ̀rẹ̀ ní agbègbè yìí.

The head of the government health office said that an epidemic has not yet begun in this area.

Source attribution with `gẹ́gẹ́ bí ... ṣe sọ`

  • Gẹ́gẹ́ bí òṣìṣẹ́ ilé-iṣẹ́ ìlera ṣe sọ, wọ́n ti bẹ̀rẹ̀ sí í fún àwọn ọmọdé ní abẹ́rẹ́ àjẹsára. According to the health-office worker, they have begun giving children vaccinations. gẹ́gẹ́ bí ... ṣe sọ attribution frame, developed this chapter
  • Gẹ́gẹ́ bí ìròyìn rédíò ṣe sọ, ìpolongo náà yóò wà fún ọ̀sẹ̀ mẹ́ta. According to the radio report, the campaign will run for three weeks. gẹ́gẹ́ bí ... ṣe sọ attribution frame, developed this chapter
Developed — Stance marking with `ó rò pé` and `wọ́n gbà pé`

News and commentary distinguish a plain report from a source's belief or interpretation. ó rò pé ("he/she believes/thinks that") and wọ́n gbà pé ("they accept/hold that") introduce stance rather than settled fact: àwọn amòye ìlera rò pé ìpolongo náà yóò ṣe ìrànlọ́wọ́ ńlá ("health experts believe that the campaign will help a great deal"). Learners must distinguish reported fact (ó ní pé) from reported belief or opinion (ó rò pé) when summarizing a source.

Introduced — Focus for highlighting the source or the event

The focus particle ni, consolidated for identification in earlier chapters, is now applied to highlight who is responsible for a statement or what specifically happened: ìjọba ni ó sọ pé ìpolongo náà yóò bẹ̀rẹ̀ ní ọ̀sẹ̀ tí ń bọ̀ ("it is the government that said the campaign will begin next week") places emphasis on the government as the source, distinct from a plain statement without focus. Question-focus frames such as ta ni ó sọ pé...? ("who is it that said...?") support source-checking in interviews and discussions.

Consolidated — Time/aspect tracking across reported material

A single news item may move between a past event, a present situation, and a future plan. The perfective ti, the progressive ń, and the future yóò, all consolidated from earlier volumes, must remain accurate inside a reported clause: wọ́n ní pé wọ́n ti bẹ̀rẹ̀ sí í pín àwọn ìwé ìsọfúnni, wọ́n sì ń múra sílẹ̀ fún ìpele tí ń bọ̀ ("they said that they have begun distributing information sheets, and they are preparing for the next stage"). Reference chains developed since Chapter 2 must keep the office, the reporter, and the source clearly distinguished throughout.

Model Dialogue

Ipò: Adé àti Ṣadé, akẹ́kọ̀ọ́ ẹlẹgbẹ́ rẹ̀, ń jíròrò ìròyìn rédíò kan tí wọ́n gbọ́ nípa ìpolongo ìlera ìjọba tuntun.
Setting: Adé and Ṣadé, a fellow student, are discussing a radio news item they heard about a new government health campaign.

ADÉ Ǹjẹ́ o gbọ́ ìròyìn rédíò lánàá nípa ìpolongo ìlera tuntun náà? Did you hear the radio news yesterday about the new health campaign?
ṢADÉ Mo gbọ́ ọ́. Agbéròyìnjáde náà ní pé ìjọba ti bẹ̀rẹ̀ sí í pín abẹ́rẹ́ àjẹsára ọ̀fẹ́ fún àwọn ọmọdé. I heard it. The reporter said that the government has begun distributing free vaccinations for children.
ADÉ Gẹ́gẹ́ bí ọ̀gá ilé-iṣẹ́ ìlera ṣe sọ, kí ni yóò ṣẹlẹ̀ sí àwọn tí kò tí ì gba abẹ́rẹ́ náà? According to the head of the health office, what will happen to those who have not yet received the vaccination?
ṢADÉ Ó ní pé ọ́fíìsì ìlera yóò máa lọ sí àwọn àgbègbè náà fúnra wọn ní ọ̀sẹ̀ mẹ́ta tí ń bọ̀. Àwọn amòye ìlera sì rò pé ìpolongo náà yóò ṣe ìrànlọ́wọ́ ńlá. He said that the health office itself will go to those areas in the next three weeks. Health experts also believe that the campaign will help a great deal.
ADÉ Ìjọba ni ó sọ ìdí rẹ̀, àbí àwọn amòye ni? Is it the government that stated the reason, or is it the experts?
ṢADÉ Àwọn amòye ni ó sọ ìdí rẹ̀; ìjọba kàn kéde ọjọ́ tí ìpolongo náà yóò bẹ̀rẹ̀ nìkan. It is the experts who stated the reason; the government only announced the date the campaign will begin.

Gẹ́gẹ́ bí àwọn agbéròyìnjáde ṣe sọ, ọ̀pọ̀lọpọ̀ ìlú ní agbègbè náà ti bẹ̀rẹ̀ sí í rí ìtẹ̀síwájú nínú iṣẹ́ omi mímọ́, lẹ́yìn tí ìjọba kéde ètò tuntun ní oṣù tí ó kọjá. Àwọn olùgbé kan rò pé iṣẹ́ náà ń lọ dáradára, ṣùgbọ́n àwọn mìíràn gbà pé àkókò náà ti pẹ́ jù. 2. What did the water-office head say about the towns already completed?

3. How do residents' views differ regarding the pace of the work?

4. Identify one direct quotation in the passage and explain whose stance it expresses.

Apá Kíkọ

Writing Workshop

Write a short media summary and response (150–200 words) about a news item, with full tone marks and dot-under letters throughout. Your summary must include at least one reported-speech sentence with ó ní pé, at least one source attribution with gẹ́gẹ́ bí ... ṣe sọ, at least one stance statement with rò pé or gbà pé, and at least one focus construction with ni highlighting a source or an event.

Sample summary shell — for reference only

Gẹ́gẹ́ bí [orísun] ṣe sọ, [ẹni tí ó sọ] ní pé [ọ̀rọ̀ tí a ròyìn].

According to [source], [speaker] said that [reported statement].

Àwọn [ẹgbẹ́ mìíràn] rò/gbà pé [èrò wọn], [ẹni] ni ó [ìṣe kan pàtó].

[Another group] believe/hold that [their view]; it is [someone] who [did a specific thing].
  • Reread the model dialogue and the model commentary above.
  • Choose a real or realistic news item with at least one quoted or reported statement.
  • Draft the reported speech, attribution, stance, and focus sentences.
  • Check that each reference to the reporter, the official, and the residents remains clear throughout.
  • Apply the Media Summary Editing Checkpoint before finalizing.

    • Include

    • at least one reported-speech sentence with ó ní pé
    • a source attribution with gẹ́gẹ́ bí ... ṣe sọ
    • a stance statement with rò pé or gbà pé
    • a focus construction with ni highlighting a source or event

    Avoid

    • blending reported fact and personal stance without a clear marker
    • losing track of which source said which statement
    • omitting tone marks or dot-under letters
    Media Summary Editing Checkpoint
    • Every vowel that should carry a high or low tone mark has one.
    • ẹ, ọ, and ṣ are distinguished from e, o, and s throughout.
    • Each reported statement is clearly attributed to its source.
    • Stance is marked separately from plain reported fact.
    • At least one focus construction correctly highlights a source or event.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Community radio and Yorùbá-language news

    In many Yorùbá-speaking communities, community and regional radio stations play an important role in delivering public-service news in Yorùbá alongside English-language national broadcasts, and listeners often follow both to compare how a story is presented in each language.

    Regional Note — Variation in news register

    The formality of broadcast Yorùbá and the specific attribution phrases used can vary somewhat between stations, regions, and program styles. This chapter's patterns describe a widely usable formal-register model; learners encountering other styles receptively should treat them as labeled variation rather than as errors.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Collapsing reported fact and stance

    Error: Reporting an expert's opinion as if it were a plain reported fact, for example saying "ó ní pé ìpolongo náà yóò ṣe ìrànlọ́wọ́ ńlá" when the source only expressed a belief, not a certainty.

    Correction: Mark belief or interpretation with rò pé or gbà pé, as in "àwọn amòye rò pé ìpolongo náà yóò ṣe ìrànlọ́wọ́ ńlá."

    Common Mistake — Losing the source in a long summary

    Error: Reporting several statements from different sources without repeating or clarifying who said each one, leaving the listener unsure which source is responsible for which claim.

    Correction: Repeat or clearly re-establish the source with attribution phrases or focus, as in ìjọba ni ó kéde ọjọ́ náà, ṣùgbọ́n àwọn amòye ni ó ṣàlàyé ìdí rẹ̀.

    Practical Performance Task

    Iṣẹ́ aṣeyọrí — Abala 6

    Àkópọ̀ àti Ìdáhùn sí Ìròyìn — Media Summary and Response

    Produce a complete short summary of and response to a news item, entirely in Standard Written Yorùbá, following the structure taught in this chapter.

    Component 1 — Draft Summary

    • 150–200 words, summarizing a news item's main statement, source, and one differing stance, with reported speech, attribution, stance, and focus.

Volume IV Capstone: Advanced Public Communication · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Professional, Academic, Civic, and Cross-Regional Yorùbá

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Produce a complete formal correspondence item appropriate to an institutional or professional audience.
  2. Summarize and respond to a media-style text with accurate source attribution and stance language.
  3. Write an analytical report that integrates evidence, contrast, concession, and a clearly supported conclusion.
  4. Deliver a formal presentation and manage a question-and-answer exchange under real-time pressure.
  5. Edit an extended piece of writing using the full Chapter 18 checklist across tone, orthography, particles, reference, serialization, word order, and register.
  6. Demonstrate stable control of tone and diacritic accuracy across sustained formal writing and speech.
  7. Assemble a portfolio that provides integrated evidence of Volume IV's advanced grammar, register, and communicative range.
  8. Identify which Volume IV systems are strongest and which require continued attention before Volume V.

Opening Communication Scenario

Adé and Kúnlé have each completed their Chapter 19 projects and rehearsed their question-and-answer exchanges together. Their community education network has now asked both of them, along with several other participants, to submit a complete capstone portfolio: a formal letter to a partner organization, a short written response to a recent media report, an analytical report on a topic of their choice, and a recorded or live presentation with a question-and-answer session. The network's coordinator reminds everyone that the portfolio will be read and heard by people with a wide range of backgrounds, so consistency of register and clarity of reference matter as much as accuracy at the sentence level.

  1. This chapter introduces no major new grammar. It assesses the full range of systems activated across Volume IV.
  2. The capstone brings together formal correspondence, media response, analytical writing, and formal speaking into one integrated portfolio.
  3. By the end of the chapter, you will have produced and self-evaluated a complete Volume IV capstone portfolio.

Pronunciation Focus — Sustained Formal Delivery

Focus — Maintaining tone across a long, multi-part performance

Chapter 19 addressed tone accuracy under the pressure of a single unscripted answer. The capstone adds a further challenge: keeping tone and vowel quality stable across a longer performance that shifts between reading, presenting, and answering questions in sequence.

  • Pace transitions between portfolio components deliberately; moving too quickly from reading a summary into spontaneous speech is a common point where tone control slips.
  • If early tone flattening is not corrected in the moment, it tends to continue rather than recover on its own; a brief self-correction early in a performance is more effective than continuing to the end and reflecting on it afterward.
  • Recording a full rehearsal of all portfolio components in one sitting, rather than practicing each piece separately, gives the most realistic picture of where sustained delivery breaks down.

Grammar and Volume-Wide Integration

This chapter introduces no major new grammar. It requires learners to apply, in a single sustained portfolio, the full range of systems consolidated across Volume IV: advanced tone and orthographic control, particle precision, negation and modality, serialization, focus, word order and clause architecture, noun phrase structure, reference tracking, and register consistency.

Structuring an Analytical Report with Contrast and Concession

Pattern

Ọ̀rọ̀ ìṣáájú → Ẹ̀rí kìíní àti ìkejì → Ìyàtọ̀ tàbí àtakò → Ìparí àti àbá

Introduction → first and second pieces of evidence → contrast or counterpoint → conclusion and recommendation. This structure reuses the source-attribution and cause/result frames from Chapter 4 and the subordination developed in Chapter 11, now applied to an extended analytical genre with an explicit concession move.

Ọ̀pọ̀ ènìyàn gbà pé kíkọ́ Yorùbá ní ilé-ìwé gbọ́dọ̀ dá lórí sísọ̀rọ̀ nìkan. Bí ó tilẹ̀ jẹ́ pé sísọ̀rọ̀ ṣe pàtàkì, ìwádìí fi hàn pé àwọn akẹ́kọ̀ọ́ tí wọ́n tún kọ́ láti kọ ìwé ní Yorùbá ń tẹ̀síwájú yára jù. Ní ìyàtọ̀ sí i, àwọn tí kò kọ́ láti kà àti kọ ìwé sábà máa ń dúró sí ìwọ̀nba kan. Nítorí náà, a dábàá kí ilé-ìwé kọ́kọ́ so kíkọ́ ọ̀rọ̀ sísọ pọ̀ mọ́ kíkà àti kíkọ ìwé láti ìbẹ̀rẹ̀.

Many people believe that teaching Yorùbá in schools should rely on speaking alone. Although speaking matters, research shows that students who also learn to write in Yorùbá progress faster. In contrast, those who do not learn to read and write often plateau at a certain level. Therefore, we recommend that schools combine spoken instruction with reading and writing from the beginning.

Responding to a Media Text with Stance

  • Gẹ́gẹ́ bí ìròyìn náà ti sọ, iye àwọn tí ń lo Yorùbá ní ìlú ń dín kù; síbẹ̀, mo rò pé àlàyé yìí kò kàyé gbogbo ipò náà. According to the report, the number of Yorùbá speakers in the city is declining; still, I think this explanation does not capture the whole situation. source attribution + stance marker + hedged disagreement, consolidated from Chapters 4 and 6

A media response must clearly separate what the source claims from what the writer thinks. Reported Speech Stance/Register — collapsing the two into a single unmarked statement is one of the most common register errors at this level.

Portfolio-Wide Register Consistency

Inconsistent Register

Àkọsílẹ̀ mi ṣàlàyé ọ̀rọ̀ náà dáadáa gan-an, àmọ́ nígbà tí wọ́n bi mí, mo kàn sọ pé "wèrè ni ìsọ̀rọ̀ yìí."

Formal report register collapses into unguarded colloquial speech during the Q&A.

Consistent Register

Àkọsílẹ̀ mi ṣàlàyé ọ̀rọ̀ náà dáadáa gan-an, àti pé nígbà tí wọ́n bi mí, mo dáhùn pé àlàyé náà kò bá gbogbo ẹ̀rí tí mo rí mu pátápátá.

Formal register is maintained from the written report into the spoken answer.

A capstone portfolio is read and heard as one connected performance. Register/Style — a register shift between components is noticeable precisely because the rest of the portfolio has established a formal baseline.

Recognition note — Assessment, not new instruction

Every pattern in this chapter has appeared in an earlier Volume IV chapter. The task here is to apply the Chapter 18 diagnostic habit across an entire portfolio rather than a single text, catching register drift, reference ambiguity, and tone inconsistency wherever they occur across four different components.

Model Dialogue

Ipò: Adé àti Kúnlé ń ṣe àyẹ̀wò ìkẹyìn lórí àpapọ̀ iṣẹ́ àṣepé wọn ṣáájú kí wọ́n tó fi í ránṣẹ́ sí ìgbìmọ̀ àjọ àgbègbè náà.
Setting: Adé and Kúnlé are doing a final review of their capstone portfolios before submitting them to the community network's committee.

ADÉ Mo ti ka lẹ́tà mi àti àkọsílẹ̀ ìtúpalẹ̀ mi ní ìgbà mẹ́ta, àmọ́ mi ò dá mi lójú pé ohun orin ìsọ̀rọ̀ mi bára mu láti ìbẹ̀rẹ̀ dé òpin. I've read my letter and my analytical report three times, but I'm not sure my spoken tone stays consistent from beginning to end.
KÚNLÉ Ṣe àkọsílẹ̀ ohùn gbogbo iṣẹ́ rẹ ní ìgbà kan ṣoṣo, kí o sì tẹ́tí sí i láti ìbẹ̀rẹ̀ dé òpin láìdúró; ìyẹn ni ọ̀nà tí mo lò tí mo fi rí i pé ohùn mi ń dín kù lẹ́yìn ìṣẹ́jú kan. Record your whole performance in one sitting, and listen to it from start to finish without stopping; that's how I discovered my voice was dropping after about a minute.
ADÉ Bí wọ́n bá bi mí ní ìbéèrè tí kò bá àkọsílẹ̀ mi mu rárá, kí ni mo lè ṣe? If they ask me a question that doesn't match my report at all, what can I do?
KÚNLÉ Gba ìbéèrè náà, sọ ohun tí àkọsílẹ̀ rẹ ti bo, kí o sì sọ ní kedere ibi tí ó ti kọjá àkọsílẹ̀ náà; má ṣe díbọ́n pé o ti bo ohun tí o kò bò. Accept the question, state what your report did cover, and say clearly where it goes beyond your report; don't pretend you covered something you didn't.
ADÉ Ó dára. Ǹjẹ́ ìwọ ti parí ṣíṣàyẹ̀wò ìdáhùn rẹ sí ìròyìn náà? Good. Have you finished reviewing your response to the news report?
KÚNLÉ Rárá jì, mo ṣì fẹ́ rí i dájú pé mo yà ohun tí ìròyìn náà sọ sọ́tọ̀ sí ohun tí èmi fúnra mi rò nípa rẹ̀. Not yet, I still want to make sure I've clearly separated what the report says from what I myself think about it.

Writing Workshop

Assemble your capstone portfolio. Choose one professional or civic thread that can run across all four components, or select four related topics of your own choosing. Full tone marks and dot-under letters are required throughout every component.

  • Write a formal letter (120–180 words) to an institution or organization, using the correspondence conventions from Chapter 2.
  • Choose a short media-style text (your own or an instructor-provided one) and write a response (100–150 words) that clearly separates the source's claim from your own stance.
  • Write an analytical report (300–400 words) with an introduction, at least two pieces of evidence, one contrast or concession, and a conclusion with a recommendation.
  • Prepare presentation notes covering all three written components and anticipate at least three questions, including one that falls outside your report's scope.
  • Apply the full editing checklist from Chapter 18 to all three written components, in order: tone, orthography, particles, pronouns/reference, serialization, word order/clause, register/style.
  • Record or rehearse a full run-through of the presentation and question-and-answer session in one sitting.
  • Complete the Capstone Integration Checkpoint before submitting your portfolio.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Public accountability across a body of work

    Presenting several related pieces of work together, rather than a single isolated document, is common in professional and civic settings where a committee or partner organization wants to evaluate consistency and judgment over time, not just performance on one task.

    Regional Note — Variation in portfolio and reporting conventions

    Expectations about how formally a media response should be framed, and how much personal stance a writer may include, vary across institutions and regions. The pattern of clearly separating source claim from personal stance introduced in this chapter is a widely applicable strategy rather than a single fixed rule.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Merging source claim and personal stance

    Error: Stating a source's claim and the writer's own opinion in a single unmarked sentence, so a reader cannot tell where the source ends and the writer's view begins.

    Correction: Attribute the claim explicitly with a frame such as gẹ́gẹ́ bí ìròyìn náà ti sọ, then mark the personal stance separately, for example with síbẹ̀, mo rò pé...

    Common Mistake — Overreaching beyond the report's scope in Q&A

    Error: Answering a question outside the report's scope as though it had already been addressed, rather than acknowledging the gap.

    Correction: State clearly what the report does cover, acknowledge what it does not, and offer to address the gap separately, following Kúnlé's advice in this chapter's model dialogue.

    Chapter Summary

    This chapter assessed the full range of Volume IV systems through a four-part capstone portfolio: a formal letter, a media response, an analytical report, and a formal presentation with a question-and-answer session. Learners practiced separating source claims from personal stance, structuring analytical writing with contrast and concession, and maintaining register consistency across written and spoken components under real-time pressure. No major new grammar was introduced; the chapter consolidated tone, orthography, particles, negation, serialization, focus, word order, noun phrase structure, reference, and register across a sustained body of work.

Volume IV Opening: Advanced Re-entry: Formal Tone, Orthography, and Register · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Professional, Academic, Civic, and Cross-Regional Yorùbá

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Audit your own tone marking and dot-under orthography across formal and everyday Yorùbá writing samples.
  2. Identify and correct particle, pronoun, and word-order errors using the consolidated systems from Volume III.
  3. Distinguish everyday register from the more formal, public-facing register that Volume IV will require.
  4. Recognize the professional, academic, and civic vocabulary domains this volume will develop.
  5. Read and listen to a short formal passage and identify its register markers.
  6. Write a short self-assessment paragraph describing your own tone, orthography, and grammar strengths and gaps.
  7. Explain, in general terms, how Volume IV will extend rather than replace the systems built in Volumes I–III.

Opening Communication Scenario

Adé has finished his Volume III capstone portfolio and is about to start an internship at a community development office in Ibadan. Before his first day, his supervisor, Ẹnjinia Kúnlé, asks him to review a short formal notice and to introduce himself in writing using more formal language than he has used in family or classroom settings. Adé realizes that the grammar, tone, and vocabulary he built across Volumes I–III are still the foundation — but he now needs to apply them with greater precision and in a more formal register.

  1. This chapter introduces no major new grammar system. It audits and re-activates tone, orthography, particle, pronoun, and clause systems consolidated in Volume III.
  2. The chapter also previews the formal, professional, academic, and civic register that will be developed across Volume IV.
  3. By the end of this chapter, you should have a clear picture of your own strengths and gaps, and understand what "formal register" means in practical terms for Standard Written Yorùbá.

Pronunciation Focus

Focus — Auditing tone and fluency before formal-register work begins

Volume IV assumes the consolidated tone, vowel, and syllable control built across Volumes I–III. This chapter is a checkpoint, not a new introduction: it asks you to listen critically to your own speech and identify where tone or rhythm weakens under pressure.

  • Record or read aloud a short passage, then check whether tone flattens on particles and pronouns, the pattern most likely to slip during fast or formal speech.
  • Practice sustaining clear high/mid/low tone contrasts while reading a formal notice at a measured, public-facing pace.
  • Notice that formal register in Yorùbá does not mean speaking more slowly in an unnatural way; it means maintaining full tone and vowel accuracy at a natural professional pace.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter introduces no major new productive grammar system. It re-activates and audits consolidated Volume III systems: tense-aspect particles, negation, serial verbs, `ni` focus, `tí` relative clauses, and reference tracking. It introduces, for the first time as a labeled category, the distinction between everyday register and formal register, which Volume IV will develop system by system.

Everyday register versus formal register

Pattern

Ojoojúmọ́: [gbólóhùn kúkúrú, ìsopọ̀ àìfẹ́sẹ̀múlẹ̀]. Aláṣẹ: [gbólóhùn tí ó gùn sí i, ìsopọ̀ fẹ́sẹ̀múlẹ̀, àkọlé ọlá].

This pattern contrasts a short everyday sentence with its more formal counterpart, showing that formal register in Yorùbá is achieved mainly through fuller clause structure, explicit titles/address forms, and careful discourse connectors, rather than through different core grammar.

Ojoojúmọ́: Mo dé ọ́fíìsì ni àkọ́kọ́ ọjọ́. Aláṣẹ: Mo fi ìdùnnú dé ọ́fíìsì yín ní ọjọ́ àkọ́kọ́ ìkẹ́kọ̀ọ́ iṣẹ́ mi, mo sì fẹ́ dúpẹ́ fún àǹfààní tí ẹ fi fún mi.

Everyday: I arrived at the office on the first day. Formal: I arrived at your office with pleasure on the first day of my internship, and I want to thank you for the opportunity you have given me.

Diagnostic review: particles, negation, and focus

  • Mo ti parí ìdìpọ̀ kẹta, mo sì ń múra sílẹ̀ fún ìkẹ́kọ̀ọ́ iṣẹ́. I have finished Volume III, and I am preparing for the internship. perfective `ti` + progressive `ń`, consolidated since Volume II–III
  • Kì í ṣe pé n kò múra; ọ̀rọ̀ tí mo bẹ̀rù jù ni bí n ó ṣe kọ ìkéde àìforígbárí kan. It is not that I am unprepared; the thing I fear most is how I will write a formal notice. negation `kì í ṣe` + `ni` focus, consolidated since Volume III
Consolidated — Serial verbs and relative clauses continue

The formal notice below continues to rely on productive `tí` relative clauses such as ọ̀dọ̀ akẹ́kọ̀ọ́ tí ó ń kẹ́kọ̀ọ́ iṣẹ́ ("the intern who is learning the work"), consolidated since Volume III Chapter 5, and on ordinary serial verb constructions for arriving, bringing, and taking documents, consolidated since Volume II–III.

Recognition only — Formal register markers preview Volume IV

The reading passage below contains formal address terms, an institutional greeting formula, and a policy-reference phrase (gẹ́gẹ́ bí ìlànà ọ́fíìsì). These remain receptive for now; Volume IV will introduce their productive use system by system, beginning with Chapter 2's formal correspondence work.

Model Dialogue

Ipò: Adé ń bá alábòójútó rẹ̀, Ẹnjinia Kúnlé, sọ̀rọ̀ ní ọjọ́ àkọ́kọ́ ìkẹ́kọ̀ọ́ iṣẹ́ rẹ̀ ní ọ́fíìsì ìdàgbàsókè àdúgbò.
Setting: Adé is speaking with his supervisor, Engineer Kúnlé, on his first day of internship at a community development office.

ẸNJ. KÚNLÉ Ẹ káàbọ̀, Adé. Báwo ni ìrìn àjò rẹ ṣe rí? Welcome, Adé. How was your journey?
ADÉ Ó dára, ẹ ṣé, sà. Mo dé ọ́fíìsì yín ní àkókò tí a yàn. It was fine, thank you, sir. I arrived at your office at the appointed time.
ẸNJ. KÚNLÉ Rere ni. Ṣé o ti kà ìkéde àìforígbárí tí mo fi ránṣẹ́ sí ọ lánàá? Good. Have you read the formal notice I sent you yesterday?
ADÉ Mo ti kà á, ṣùgbọ́n èdè inú rẹ̀ le fún mi níwọ̀nba; mo mọ ọ̀rọ̀ inú rẹ̀ lẹ́ẹ̀kọ̀ọ̀kan, ṣùgbọ́n ìlànà gbólóhùn rẹ̀ yàtọ̀ sí ohun tí mo mọ̀ tẹ́lẹ̀. I have read it, but the language inside it was somewhat difficult for me; I know the individual words, but its sentence structure is different from what I already know.
ẸNJ. KÚNLÉ Má ṣàníyàn. Gbogbo ohun tí o ti kọ́ láti Ìdìpọ̀ Kìíní dé Ẹ̀kẹta ṣì wà ní ìpìlẹ̀; a óò kàn máa fi kún un, a kì yóò yí i padà. Don't worry. Everything you learned from Volume I through Volume III is still the foundation; we will only add to it, we will not replace it.

  4. Write one sentence of your own contrasting an everyday version and a more formal version of the same idea, following the Everyday/Formal pattern above.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Formality in Yorùbá professional life

    In many Yorùbá-speaking professional and institutional settings, moving between registers is an expected and valued skill: the same speaker may use relaxed, informal Yorùbá with age-mates and a noticeably fuller, more respectful register with supervisors, elders, or officials. This is generally understood as a sign of social competence rather than insincerity, and it builds directly on the respect and address patterns introduced in earlier volumes.

    Volume IV Domain Preview — Suggestions, not requirements
    • Formal correspondence and institutional requests
    • Professional meetings and workplace tasks
    • Academic discussion and source-based explanation
    • Public services and civic life
    • Media and news analysis
    • Health, environment, and public communication
    • Cross-regional and diaspora listening
    • Religion, culture, and public ceremony

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Treating formal register as a different language

    Error: Assuming that formal Yorùbá requires an entirely different grammar system from what was learned in Volumes I–III.

    Correction: Recognize, as Ẹnjinia Kúnlé explains, that Volume IV adds fuller structure, address forms, and vocabulary to the same consolidated grammar; it does not replace it.

    Common Mistake — Skipping the diagnostic step

    Error: Moving directly into Volume IV content without honestly auditing tone, orthography, and grammar gaps from Volume III.

    Correction: Complete the self-assessment writing task and the diagnostic listening and reading tasks before Chapter 2, so that any Volume III gaps are addressed early rather than compounding later.

    Answer Key

    Listening Practice Questions

    1. Adé notices that office language differs somewhat from the language used at home or in class.
    2. Every office notice must include a title of respect, a formal greeting, and a stated reason for writing.
    3. No new verbs or grammar tools are required; what differs is how the words are organized and how respect is shown to the addressee.
    4. Adé decides to do a short self-assessment to identify which areas have already developed and which still need attention.

    Reading Practice Questions

    1. All interns beginning work that week must submit a short self-introduction by Friday.
    2. The self-introduction must include the intern's name, the school where they studied Yorùbá, and the area of work they are most interested in.

    4. The office asks interns to use formal language when speaking or writing to senior staff.

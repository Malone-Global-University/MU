Ìlànà, Òfin, àti Èdè Onídùúró

Policy, Rules, and Responsible Language · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Professional, Academic, Civic, and Cross-Regional Yorùbá

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Express obligation, requirement, and necessity using consolidated modal frames.
  2. Express prohibition in formal, rule-like language, distinct from ordinary negative commands.
  3. Form conditional sentences that describe what happens if a rule is or is not followed.
  4. Use careful, hedged modality to summarize a policy-like text without overstating certainty or specialist expertise.
  5. Recognize formal vocabulary associated with policies, rules, safety notices, and institutions.
  6. Pronounce specialist and institutional terms carefully and accurately.
  7. Listen to and read short rule- or policy-like texts, identifying obligations, prohibitions, and conditions.
  8. Summarize a policy-like text responsibly, in writing and aloud, without overclaiming expertise.

Opening Communication Scenario

Adé's community radio station has asked him to prepare a short segment explaining a new municipal safety notice about market-stall fire regulations. Adé is not a fire-safety official, so he must read the notice carefully, identify what it requires, what it forbids, and what happens under certain conditions, and then summarize it for listeners in careful, responsible language — without adding his own opinions or claiming knowledge the notice itself does not state.

  1. This chapter teaches how to express obligation, prohibition, and conditional consequence in formal Yorùbá.
  2. It develops careful, hedged modality so that a summary reflects only what a policy-like text actually says.
  3. By the end of the chapter, you will summarize a policy-like text responsibly, following the same process Adé uses for his safety-notice segment.

Pronunciation Focus

Focus — Careful pronunciation of specialist terms

Policy and safety vocabulary often contains longer compound terms built from familiar words, and a rushed or flattened pronunciation can obscure which term is being used. This chapter's pronunciation focus is producing these compound terms with full tone accuracy and even syllable spacing, so a listener can distinguish, for example, a notice about a rule from a notice about a penalty.

  • Practice ìtọ́ni ààbò ("safety notice") syllable by syllable before speeding up, since the double vowel in ààbò must remain long and clearly tone-marked.
  • Pay special attention to ó jẹ́ dandan pé versus a kò gbọdọ̀, where tone and vowel quality distinguish obligation from prohibition.
  • Practice reading a rule-like sentence at a measured, formal pace, giving each modal or conditional word its own clear stress.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter develops obligation, prohibition, conditional consequence, and careful hedged modality inside the functional task of reading and summarizing a policy-like text responsibly.

Obligation with `ó jẹ́ dandan pé`

Pattern

Ó jẹ́ dandan pé [ẹnikẹ́ni] [ó ṣe ìṣe kan]

Obligation is built with the impersonal frame ó jẹ́ dandan pé ("it is required that") followed by a complement clause with its own subject and verb. This keeps the requirement stated as a rule rather than as a command aimed at one specific listener.

Ó jẹ́ dandan pé gbogbo alágbàṣe ọjà ní kí wọ́n pa iná mọ́tò wọn nígbà tí wọn kò bá ń lò ó.

It is required that every market vendor must turn off their generator engine when they are not using it.

Formal prohibition with `a kò gbọdọ̀`

  • A kò gbọdọ̀ fi iná ṣe oúnjẹ nínú àgọ́ ọjà lẹ́yìn wákàtí méjọ́ tí a ti gbà láṣẹ. One must not cook with fire inside a market stall after the eight authorized hours. a kò gbọdọ̀ formal prohibition frame, developed this chapter
  • A kò gbọdọ̀ dí ọ̀nà àbáyọ ní àsìkò tí iná bá jó. One must not block the escape route in the event of a fire. a kò gbọdọ̀ formal prohibition frame, developed this chapter
Developed — Conditionals with `bí ... bá`

Policy-like texts often describe what happens under a stated condition. The frame bí ... bá ("if...") was previewed in Chapter 3 and is now developed for rule-and-consequence sentences: bí ẹnikẹ́ni bá rú òfin yìí, ó lè jẹ ìjìyà owó tàbí kí wọ́n gba ìwé àṣẹ ọjà rẹ̀ padà ("if anyone breaks this rule, they may face a fine or have their market permit withdrawn"). The condition clause states the circumstance; the result clause states the consequence, often with a modal such as lè ("may") rather than a flat certainty.

Introduced — Careful, hedged modality with `ó ṣeéṣe kí`

When summarizing a policy or safety text, a learner who is not a specialist should avoid stating consequences as flat certainties unless the text itself does so. ó ṣeéṣe kí ("it is possible that") introduces a hedged, responsible reading: ó ṣeéṣe kí ọ́fíìsì aláṣẹ ṣe àyẹ̀wò àfikún bí a bá rí àwọn ohun tí kò bá òfin mu ("it is possible that the authority office may carry out an additional inspection if items not in compliance with the rule are found"). This hedge frame is distinct from the stance frame ó rò pé from Chapter 6: ó ṣeéṣe kí signals uncertainty about an outcome, while ó rò pé signals a source's belief.

Consolidated — Attribution and reference inside rule summaries

The attribution frame gẹ́gẹ́ bí ... ṣe sọ, developed in Chapter 6, is now applied to notices and documents rather than only to people: gẹ́gẹ́ bí ìtọ́ni ààbò náà ṣe sọ, gbogbo aṣáájú ọjà ní láti kọ́ àwọn olùtajà nípa ọ̀nà àbáyọ ("according to what the safety notice states, every market leader must instruct sellers about the escape route"). Reference chains, consolidated since earlier volumes, must keep the notice, the authority office, and the vendors clearly distinguished throughout a summary.

Ìjíròrò Àpẹẹrẹ

Model Dialogue

Ipò: Adé àti Bùkúnmi, aṣáájú ẹgbẹ́ àwọn olùtajà ọjà, ń jíròrò ìtọ́ni ààbò tuntun nípa iná ní ọjà.
Setting: Adé and Bùkúnmi, a leader of the market sellers' association, are discussing a new fire-safety notice for the market.

ADÉ Ẹ ti rí ìtọ́ni ààbò tuntun tí ọ́fíìsì aláṣẹ fi ránṣẹ́ sí ọjà yìí? Have you seen the new safety notice the authority office sent to this market?
BÙKÚNMI Mo ti rí i. Ó jẹ́ dandan pé gbogbo wa ní kí a pa iná mọ́tò wa nígbà tí a kò bá ń lò ó. I have seen it. It is required that all of us must turn off our generators when we are not using them.
ADÉ Kí ni ìtọ́ni náà sọ nípa ọ̀nà àbáyọ? What does the notice say about the escape route?
BÙKÚNMI A kò gbọdọ̀ dí ọ̀nà àbáyọ pẹ̀lú ẹrù rárá. Bí ẹnikẹ́ni bá rú òfin yìí, ó lè jẹ ìjìyà owó. One must not block the escape route with goods at all. If anyone breaks this rule, they may face a fine.
ADÉ Ǹjẹ́ ọ́fíìsì aláṣẹ yóò máa ṣe àyẹ̀wò déédéé? Will the authority office carry out regular inspections?
BÙKÚNMI Ìtọ́ni náà kò sọ àkókò tí wọn yóò máa ṣe àyẹ̀wò déédéé, ṣùgbọ́n ó ṣeéṣe kí wọ́n ṣe àyẹ̀wò àfikún bí a bá rí ohun tí kò bá òfin mu. The notice does not state a fixed schedule for regular inspections, but it is possible that they may carry out an additional inspection if something not in compliance with the rule is found.
ADÉ Ó dára. Èmi kì í ṣe ọ̀gá ààbò iná, torí náà mo fẹ́ ṣàlàyé ohun tí ìtọ́ni náà sọ nìkan, kì í ṣe èrò tèmi. Good. I am not a fire-safety official, so I only want to explain what the notice states, not my own opinion.

Writing Workshop

Write a short policy summary (150–200 words) about a rule, policy, or safety notice, with full tone marks and dot-under letters throughout. Your summary must include at least one obligation sentence with ó jẹ́ dandan pé, at least one prohibition with a kò gbọdọ̀, at least one conditional sentence with bí ... bá, and at least one hedged statement with ó ṣeéṣe kí.

Sample summary shell — for reference only

Gẹ́gẹ́ bí [ìtọ́ni/òfin] ṣe sọ, ó jẹ́ dandan pé [ẹnikẹ́ni] [ó ṣe ìṣe kan]; a kò sì gbọdọ̀ [ìṣe tí a kọ̀].

According to [the notice/rule], it is required that [someone] [does something]; and one must not [a forbidden action].

Bí ẹnikẹ́ni bá rú òfin yìí, ó lè [àbájáde kan]. Ó ṣeéṣe kí [àbájáde tí kò dá jáde ní gbangba].

If anyone breaks this rule, they may [a consequence]. It is possible that [an outcome not explicitly stated].

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Community leaders and rule communication

    In many Yorùbá-speaking communities, market associations, school leaders, and local government offices commonly share responsibility for explaining rules and notices to the people they affect, often relaying information through trusted community leaders in addition to official written notices.

    Regional Note — Variation in formal rule language

    The specific wording of obligation and prohibition frames can vary somewhat between government offices, institutions, and regions. This chapter's patterns describe a widely usable formal-register model; learners encountering other wordings receptively should treat them as labeled variation rather than as errors.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Stating an uncertain outcome as certain

    Error: Summarizing a notice by saying "ọ́fíìsì aláṣẹ yóò ṣe àyẹ̀wò àfikún" ("the authority office will carry out an additional inspection") when the source text only states this as a possibility, not a certainty.

    Correction: Use the hedge frame when the source itself is not certain, as in "ó ṣeéṣe kí ọ́fíìsì aláṣẹ ṣe àyẹ̀wò àfikún."

    Common Mistake — Blurring obligation and prohibition

    Error: Using the same negative pattern for both "must not" and ordinary casual refusal, so that a formal rule sounds like a personal preference.

    Correction: Reserve a kò gbọdọ̀ for formal, rule-based prohibition, as in a kò gbọdọ̀ dí ọ̀nà àbáyọ pẹ̀lú ẹrù, distinct from ordinary conversational negation.

Ìjíhìnwá Onítọ́nà-Ìtúmọ̀

Translation-Aware Mediation · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Professional, Academic, Civic, and Cross-Regional Yorùbá

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Explain the meaning of a Yorùbá passage in English without relying on word-for-word translation.
  2. Explain the meaning of an English passage in Yorùbá, restructuring it to fit natural Yorùbá clause and focus patterns.
  3. Identify culture-bound terms that resist direct translation and explain them through description rather than substitution.
  4. Use mediation frames to introduce an explanation, a paraphrase, or a clarification for a listener or reader from the other language.
  5. Distinguish register equivalence from literal lexical equivalence when mediating between English and Yorùbá.
  6. Recognize, without independent production, how professional interpreters and translators handle ambiguity and untranslatable idioms.
  7. Maintain natural prosody when speaking a mediated explanation aloud, rather than reading it as a flat rendering of the source.
  8. Produce a short translation-aware commentary that explains, rather than merely converts, a passage from one language into the other.

Opening Communication Scenario

The regional media association enjoyed the radio station profile that Adé and Ẹ̀gbọ́n Fúnmi wrote in Chapter 12, and they have now asked the station to prepare a short bilingual segment: a Yorùbá community announcement that also needs an English explanation for a partner organization, and an English planning note that needs to be explained to Yorùbá-speaking staff who were not part of the original meeting. Neither task can be handled by translating word for word — some expressions carry meanings and social weight that only make sense with explanation.

  1. This chapter treats translation not as word-for-word substitution but as mediation: explaining what a message means and does in one language to someone who only has access to the other.
  2. No new independent clause type is introduced. Mediation builds on the focus, relative-clause, and reference systems already active from earlier chapters, and adds frames for introducing an explanation or a paraphrase.
  3. By the end of the chapter, you will produce a short translation-aware commentary explaining a passage between English and Yorùbá, rather than simply converting it.

Pronunciation Focus — Prosody in Mediated Explanation

Focus — Natural delivery when explaining rather than reading a source

When a speaker mediates between two languages, the delivery should sound like an explanation offered to a real listener, not like a flat oral rendering of a written source. This chapter's pronunciation focus builds on the long-phrase tone control developed in Chapter 12, applied to the frames used to introduce an explanation.

  • Give mediation frames such as ohun tí ó túmọ̀ sí and ní èdè Gẹ̀ẹ́sì, a lè sọ pé a slightly slower, clearer delivery than the surrounding sentence, since they signal a shift into explanation.
  • Keep tone accurate on the explanation itself; do not let it flatten simply because it follows a frame.
  • Pause briefly before and after a culture-bound term you are about to explain, so a listener understands that a definition, not a translation, is coming.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter develops mediation frames that build on the focus, relative-clause, and reference systems already active from earlier chapters. It does not introduce a new independent clause type; it develops how those systems combine to introduce an explanation, a paraphrase, or a clarification across two languages.

Introducing an Explanation of Meaning

Pattern

Ohun tí [ọ̀rọ̀/gbólóhùn] túmọ̀ sí ni pé...

A relative clause built with tí ("ohun tí X túmọ̀ sí," "what X means") becomes the focused element before ni, followed by pé introducing the explanation itself. This keeps the explanation grammatically separate from the term or phrase being explained.

Ohun tí "ẹni tí ó dá ilé-iṣẹ́ sílẹ̀" túmọ̀ sí ni pé kì í ṣe pé ẹnì kan kàn ṣiṣẹ́ níbẹ̀; ó ní í ṣe pẹ̀lú ẹni tí ó bẹ̀rẹ̀ ìṣẹ̀lẹ̀ náà láti ìbẹ̀rẹ̀pẹ̀pẹ̀.

What "the person who founded the company" means is not just that someone worked there; it has to do with the person who started the whole undertaking from the very beginning.

Explaining a Yorùbá Passage in English (Mediation Direction 1)

  • Ní èdè Gẹ̀ẹ́sì, a lè sọ pé ọ̀rọ̀ yìí ń tọ́ka sí ọ̀wọ̀ tí a ń fi hàn sí àgbà nínú ìjíròrò, kì í ṣe àìsọ̀rọ̀ tàbí ẹ̀rù. In English, one could say this expression refers to the respect shown toward an elder in a conversation, not silence or fear. ní èdè Gẹ̀ẹ́sì, a lè sọ pé = mediation frame introducing an English-facing explanation
  • Kò sí ọ̀rọ̀ kan náà ní èdè Gẹ̀ẹ́sì tí ó lè fi gbogbo ìjẹ́pàtàkì "ìjùbà" hàn; ó máa ń nílò àlàyé dípò ìtúmọ̀ kan ṣoṣo. There is no single word in English that can show the full significance of "ìjùbà" (paying respect/homage); it usually requires an explanation rather than one translation. kò sí ọ̀rọ̀ kan náà ní èdè Gẹ̀ẹ́sì = frame flagging a culture-bound term

Explaining an English Passage in Yorùbá (Mediation Direction 2)

Pattern

Ní èdè Yorùbá, a ó lè sọ ọ́ pé... dípò kí a túmọ̀ ọ̀rọ̀ kọ̀ọ̀kan taara.

This pattern signals that the Yorùbá version restructures the English meaning into a natural Yorùbá clause, rather than substituting a Yorùbá word for every English word.

Ní èdè Yorùbá, a ó lè sọ ọ́ pé àwọn òṣìṣẹ́ gbọ́dọ̀ parí iṣẹ́ náà kí ọ̀sẹ̀ tó parí, dípò kí a túmọ̀ ọ̀rọ̀ Gẹ̀ẹ́sì "deadline" ní ọ̀nà kan ṣoṣo.

In Yorùbá, one could say it as: the workers must finish the task before the week ends, rather than translating the English word "deadline" in only one way.

Register equivalence versus literal equivalence

A mediated explanation must match the register and function of the source, not only its dictionary meaning. A formal English notice mediated into Yorùbá should sound like a formal Yorùbá notice, even where the sentence structure changes considerably. Ask: what would a Yorùbá speaker say in this same situation, rather than: what is the closest Yorùbá word for each English word?

Recognition note — Professional interpretation strategies

Professional interpreters and translators sometimes leave a term unexplained when context makes it clear, sometimes insert a short parenthetical gloss, and sometimes restructure an entire sentence around a single untranslatable idiom. These are receptive strategies at this stage: notice them when they appear in the readings below, but continue to build your own mediated explanations from the frames modeled above.

Model Dialogue

Ipò: Adé ń ṣàlàyé ọ̀rọ̀ kan nínú ìkéde Yorùbá kan fún alábàáṣiṣẹ́ àjọ ìkànsí kan tí kò gbọ́ èdè Yorùbá dáadáa, nígbà tí Ẹ̀gbọ́n Fúnmi ń ṣe kànnáà pẹ̀lú àkọsílẹ̀ Gẹ̀ẹ́sì kan fún òṣìṣẹ́ tí kò lọ sí ìpàdé náà.
Setting: Adé is explaining a term from a Yorùbá announcement to a partner-organization colleague who doesn't speak Yorùbá well, while Ẹ̀gbọ́n Fúnmi does the same with an English planning note for a staff member who missed the meeting.

PATTNER (EN) What does "ìjùbà" mean exactly? I saw it in the announcement but the dictionary translation doesn't quite make sense here. (spoken in English within the scenario)
ADÉ Ó dára pé o béèrè. Kò sí ọ̀rọ̀ kan náà ní èdè Gẹ̀ẹ́sì tí ó lè fi gbogbo ìjẹ́pàtàkì rẹ̀ hàn. Ohun tí ó túmọ̀ sí ni pé ẹni náà ń fi ọ̀wọ̀ hàn sí ẹni tí ó lọ́lá ju òun lọ, kì í ṣe pé ó ń bẹ̀rù ẹni náà. I'm glad you asked. There is no single word in English that can show its full significance. What it means is that the person is showing respect to someone of higher standing than themselves, not that they are afraid of that person.
PARTNER (EN) So it's closer to "paying respect" than to "fear" — thank you, that changes how I'll read the rest of the announcement. (spoken in English within the scenario)
ẸGBỌ́N FÚNMI Nínú àkọsílẹ̀ Gẹ̀ẹ́sì náà, wọ́n kọ̀wé pé "the deadline is non-negotiable." Ní èdè Yorùbá, a ó lè sọ ọ́ pé kò sí ààyè fún àyípadà ọjọ́ tí a yàn fún ìparí iṣẹ́ náà, dípò kí a túmọ̀ ọ̀rọ̀ kọ̀ọ̀kan taara. In the English note, they wrote "the deadline is non-negotiable." In Yorùbá, one could say it as: there is no room to change the date chosen for finishing the task, rather than translating each word directly.
STAFF MEMBER Mo ti gbọ́ dáadáa. Ṣé wọ́n sọ ìdí tí ọjọ́ náà fi wà báyìí? I understand well now. Did they say why the date is fixed this way?

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Meaning carried by social relationship, not just words

    Many Yorùbá expressions of respect, address, and social positioning encode a relationship between speaker and listener that a single English word cannot carry on its own. Explaining such expressions well often requires describing who is speaking to whom, and why, rather than offering a single translated equivalent.

    Regional Note — Mediation practice across professional contexts

    Bilingual professionals across different regions and workplaces regularly mediate between Yorùbá and English in meetings, media, and public communication. Approaches to explaining a given term may vary somewhat by speaker and setting, but the underlying goal — helping a listener from the other language understand meaning and use, not just wording — remains consistent.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Treating translation as one-to-one word substitution

    Error: Translating "ìjùbà" as simply "fear" or "respect" without explaining the social relationship the term encodes, leaving the English-speaking reader with an incomplete or misleading impression.

    Correction: Use a mediation frame such as ohun tí ó túmọ̀ sí ni pé to explain the social relationship and context, rather than offering a single English word as if it were an exact equivalent.

    Common Mistake — Mismatched register when mediating

    Error: Explaining a formal English notice ("the deadline is non-negotiable") in casual, conversational Yorùbá that would sound out of place in a formal announcement.

    Correction: Match the formality of the explanation to the formality of the source, restructuring the sentence in natural, appropriately formal Yorùbá rather than preserving only the literal content.

    Fún akẹ́kọ̀ọ́ tí ń kọ́ ẹ̀kọ́ fúnra rẹ̀ — For the independent learner

    • Keep a running list of Yorùbá and English expressions you find difficult to translate directly, along with your best mediation-frame explanation of each.

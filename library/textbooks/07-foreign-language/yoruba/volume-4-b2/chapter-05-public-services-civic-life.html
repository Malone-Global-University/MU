Public Services and Civic Life · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Professional, Academic, Civic, and Cross-Regional Yorùbá

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Ask formal questions about public services, procedures, and requirements.
  2. Express obligation and requirement using consolidated obligation particles and frames.
  3. State conditions under which a public-service outcome applies, using condition clauses.
  4. Use passive-like paraphrase strategies to describe institutional actions without naming a specific agent.
  5. Recognize and use core institutional and civic vocabulary appropriately.
  6. Listen to and read short public-service announcements and civic texts, identifying rules, conditions, and obligations.
  7. Analyze and summarize public-service information in a short written or spoken response.

Opening Communication Scenario

Adé's neighbor, Ìyá Rèmí, has just moved into the area and needs help understanding a notice from the local government office about registering for a new water connection. She does not know what documents are required, what the deadline is, or what happens if a resident misses the deadline. Adé must read the notice carefully, ask the office clerk clarifying questions, and then explain the requirements, conditions, and consequences back to Ìyá Rèmí in clear, accurate Yorùbá.

  1. This chapter teaches how to ask formal questions about public services and how to state requirements and conditions accurately.
  2. It develops obligation, condition, and passive-like paraphrase language inside the functional task of understanding and explaining civic information.
  3. By the end of the chapter, you will analyze and summarize a public-service notice of your own, following the same process Adé uses.

Pronunciation Focus

Focus — Announcements and official-register listening

Public-service announcements are typically read at a controlled, deliberate pace, with a distinct pause before key details such as dates, requirements, and locations, so listeners can note them accurately. This chapter's pronunciation focus is following that controlled pace and correctly hearing tone on rule-bearing words, where a missed tone can change a requirement into something else entirely.

  • Practice the obligation phrase ó yẹ kí ("it is necessary that") with clear tone on each syllable, since it introduces the core of every requirement.
  • Pay special attention to ọjọ́ ìkẹyìn, where the tone pattern distinguishes it clearly from similar-sounding everyday words.
  • Practice pausing briefly before stating a date, a document name, or a fee, as an official announcer would when reading a notice aloud.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter introduces formal questions about public services, obligation frames, condition clauses, and passive-like paraphrase strategies for describing institutional actions.

Formal questions about services and procedures

Pattern

Kí ni ohun tí ó yẹ kí a ṣe láti [ète]? / Ǹjẹ́ [ìbéèrè fọ́ọ̀mù]?

Formal service questions often use kí ni ohun tí ó yẹ kí a ṣe láti... ("what is it necessary to do in order to...") or a polite yes/no frame beginning with ǹjẹ́ to request confirmation of a procedure.

Kí ni ohun tí ó yẹ kí a ṣe láti forúkọsílẹ̀ fún omi mímọ́ tuntun?

What is it necessary to do in order to register for a new clean water connection?

Obligation with `ó yẹ kí` and `ó ṣe pàtàkì kí`

  • Ó yẹ kí aráàlú kọ̀ọ̀kan mú ìwé ẹ̀rí ìdánimọ̀ rẹ̀ wá sí ọ́fíìsì náà. Each citizen is required to bring their identity document to the office. ó yẹ kí obligation frame, introduced this chapter
  • Ó ṣe pàtàkì kí a parí ìforúkọsílẹ̀ kí ọjọ́ ìkẹyìn tó dé. It is important that we complete the registration before the deadline arrives. ó ṣe pàtàkì kí necessity frame, introduced this chapter
Introduced — Condition clauses with `bí ... bá`

Public-service rules commonly depend on a condition. Yorùbá marks this with bí at the start of the clause and the particle bá after the subject: bí ẹnikẹ́ni kò bá parí ìforúkọsílẹ̀ ṣáájú ọjọ́ ìkẹyìn, a óò ṣe é ní oṣù tí ó tẹ̀ lé e ("if anyone does not complete registration before the deadline, it will be done in the following month"). The condition clause is stated first, followed by the result.

Introduced — Passive-like paraphrase for institutional action

Yorùbá does not form a true grammatical passive the way English does. Instead, formal civic language paraphrases institutional action using an unspecified subject a ("one, we, they, it") with the verb, as in a óò ṣe é ("it will be done") or a kò ní gba ìwé tí kò pé ("incomplete forms will not be accepted," literally "one will not accept an incomplete document"). Learners must not translate the English passive word for word; the paraphrase keeps the action in focus while leaving the responsible office unnamed or generalized.

Consolidated — Reference chains continue into civic explanation

The reference-chain control developed across Chapters 2 through 4 continues here: when a notice mentions "the office," "the resident," or "the applicant" several times, each reference must remain unambiguous so that a listener understands exactly who must do what and by when.

Model Dialogue

Ipò: Adé wà ní ọ́fíìsì ìjọba, ó ń bi òṣìṣẹ́ ọ́fíìsì náà léèrè nípa bí Ìyá Rèmí ṣe lè forúkọsílẹ̀ fún omi mímọ́ tuntun.
Setting: Adé is at the government office, asking the office clerk about how Ìyá Rèmí can register for a new clean water connection.

ADÉ Ẹ káàbọ̀. Kí ni ohun tí ó yẹ kí a ṣe láti forúkọsílẹ̀ fún omi mímọ́ tuntun? Welcome. What is it necessary to do in order to register for a new clean water connection?
ÒṢÌṢẸ́ Ó yẹ kí ẹ mú ìwé ẹ̀rí ìdánimọ̀ yín àti ìwé tí ó fi hàn pé ilé náà jẹ́ tiyín wá sí ọ́fíìsì yìí. You are required to bring your identity document and a document showing that the house belongs to you to this office.
ADÉ Ǹjẹ́ ọjọ́ ìkẹyìn kan wà fún ìforúkọsílẹ̀ yìí? Is there a deadline for this registration?
ÒṢÌṢẸ́ Bẹ́ẹ̀ ni. Bí ẹnikẹ́ni kò bá parí ìforúkọsílẹ̀ ṣáájú ọjọ́ kẹwàá oṣù tí ń bọ̀, a óò ṣe é ní oṣù tí ó tẹ̀ lé e, ṣùgbọ́n owó ìdíyelé yóò pọ̀ síi. Yes. If anyone does not complete registration before the tenth day of next month, it will be done in the following month, but the fee will increase.
ADÉ Mo yé mi. Ẹ ṣé pé ẹ ṣàlàyé ojúṣe àwọn aráàlú àti ẹ̀tọ́ tí wọ́n ní nínú ọ̀ràn yìí kedere. I understand. Thank you for explaining citizens' responsibility and the right they have in this matter clearly.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Patience and procedure in public offices

    In many Yorùbá-speaking communities, interactions with government or institutional offices are generally approached with patience and respectful persistence, and residents commonly ask clarifying questions rather than assuming they understand a notice on first reading. Asking an office clerk to restate a requirement is typically seen as responsible rather than bothersome.

    Regional Note — Variation in public-service procedure

    Specific documents, deadlines, and fees required by public offices vary by locality and by the particular institution involved. This chapter's vocabulary and patterns describe how such notices are typically communicated in Yorùbá; learners should always confirm current local requirements rather than relying on any single example as universal.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Stating obligation as a mere suggestion

    Error: Softening a firm requirement into a suggestion, for example saying "ó dára kí a mú ìwé ẹ̀rí wá" ("it is good to bring the document") when the notice states the document is mandatory.

    Correction: Preserve the notice's actual level of obligation by using ó yẹ kí, as in "Ó yẹ kí a mú ìwé ẹ̀rí wá."

    Common Mistake — Forcing an English passive construction

    Error: Attempting to build an English-style passive directly, producing an unnatural sentence instead of the Yorùbá paraphrase.

    Correction: Use the unspecified-subject paraphrase with a, as in a óò ṣe é ("it will be done") or a kò ní gba ìwé tí kò pé ("incomplete documents will not be accepted").

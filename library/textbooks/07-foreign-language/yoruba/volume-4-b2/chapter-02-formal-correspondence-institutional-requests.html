Ìwé Fẹ́sẹ̀múlẹ̀ àti Ìbéèrè Ilé-Iṣẹ́

Formal Correspondence and Institutional Requests · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Professional, Academic, Civic, and Cross-Regional Yorùbá

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Use formal openings and closings appropriate to institutional letters and formal messages.
  2. Apply consolidated politeness strategies to soften requests and show respect to an addressee.
  3. Construct reason clauses with nítorí pé and condition clauses with bí / tí constructions inside formal requests.
  4. Maintain clear reference chains across a multi-sentence formal letter so that the reader can track who is being discussed.
  5. Read and interpret a formal institutional letter, identifying its structural components.
  6. Write and revise a complete formal request letter using an editing checklist.
  7. Distinguish formal letter register from the everyday register reviewed in Chapter 1.

Opening Communication Scenario

One week into his internship, Adé must write his first formal letter: a request to the head office of the community development program asking for permission to attend a weekend training workshop in another town. His supervisor, Ẹnjinia Kúnlé, agrees to review the letter before it is sent, and reminds Adé that a formal request must open and close correctly, state its reason clearly, and keep every reference to people and documents easy to follow from the first line to the last.

  1. This chapter teaches the formal opening and closing formulas that Chapter 1 previewed as receptive material.
  2. It develops reason and condition clauses, and reference chains, inside a single functional task: the formal request letter.
  3. By the end of the chapter, you will produce and revise your own formal request letter, using the same structure Adé uses.

Pronunciation Focus

Focus — Formal reading aloud and spelling precision

Formal letters are read aloud in meetings, at counters, and in official proceedings more often than everyday messages are. This chapter's pronunciation focus is reading a formal letter clearly at a measured pace while keeping tone fully accurate on titles, greeting formulas, and reason connectors.

  • Practice reading the opening line Sí ẹni tí ọ̀rọ̀ yìí bá kàn ("To whom this concerns") and the closing pẹ̀lú ọ̀wọ̀ ("with respect") with steady, unhurried tone.
  • Pay special attention to nítorí pé, where the tone on each syllable must remain distinct even at natural reading speed.
  • Check your own spelling of formal vocabulary against the Core Vocabulary table, since formal correspondence is judged partly on visible orthographic accuracy.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter activates formal letter openings and closings, previewed receptively in Chapter 1, for controlled productive use. It develops reason clauses with nítorí pé and condition clauses with bí...bá, and it develops reference chains so that pronouns and names remain traceable across a formal letter.

Formal openings and closings

Pattern

Sí [àkọlé ọlá + orúkọ], ... [ìdí ìwé] ... Pẹ̀lú ọ̀wọ̀, [orúkọ olùkọ̀wé].

A formal letter opens with an address term and closes with a fixed respect formula. Between the opening and closing, the body states the reason for writing and any request, using consolidated Volume III clause structures.

Sí Ààrẹ Ọ́fíìsì Ìdàgbàsókè Àdúgbò, mo ń kọ ìwé yìí nítorí pé mo fẹ́ béèrè àṣẹ láti lọ sí ìdánilẹ́kọ̀ọ́ kan ní ìlú mìíràn. Pẹ̀lú ọ̀wọ̀, Adé Adéyẹmí.

To the Head of the Community Development Office, I am writing this letter because I would like to request permission to attend a training in another town. With respect, Adé Adéyẹmí.

Reason clauses with `nítorí pé`

  • Mo fẹ́ béèrè ìgbàṣẹ́ nítorí pé ìdánilẹ́kọ̀ọ́ náà yóò ràn mí lọ́wọ́ nínú iṣẹ́ mi. I would like to request permission because the training will help me in my work. nítorí pé + reason clause, introduced this chapter
  • Bí ẹ bá fún mi ní àṣẹ, n ó padà sí iṣẹ́ ní ọjọ́ Aje tí ó tẹ̀lé e. If you grant me permission, I will return to work on the following Monday. bí...bá condition clause, introduced this chapter; future `n ó`, consolidated since Volume III
Developing — Reference chains across a formal letter

A formal letter must let the reader track who is being discussed from the opening to the closing. In the model letter below, ọ̀gá ("the officer"), ẹ ("you," respectful), and èmi/mo ("I") are used consistently so that no sentence leaves the reader guessing who is doing or receiving an action. This reference control, first introduced in Volume III, is now developed specifically for extended formal writing.

Consolidated — `ni` focus and `tí` relative clauses continue

The model letter continues to use consolidated `ni` focus (ọjọ́ Sátidé ni ìdánilẹ́kọ̀ọ́ náà máa wáyé, "it is on Saturday that the training will take place") and `tí` relative clauses (ìdánilẹ́kọ̀ọ́ tí a pè mí sí, "the training that I have been invited to"), both consolidated since Volume III.

Ìjíròrò Àpẹẹrẹ

Model Dialogue

Ipò: Adé ń fi ìwé béèrè àṣẹ tí ó ti kọ hàn Ẹnjinia Kúnlé kí ó tó fi ránṣẹ́ sí ọ́fíìsì àgbà.
Setting: Adé is showing Engineer Kúnlé the request letter he has written before sending it to the head office.

ADÉ Sà, mo ti parí ìwé béèrè àṣẹ mi. Ṣé ẹ lè wò ó fún mi? Sir, I have finished my request letter. Could you look at it for me?
ẸNJ. KÚNLÉ Dájúdájú. Ǹjẹ́ o ti fi àkọlé ọlá sí orí ìwé náà àti ìkíni fẹ́sẹ̀múlẹ̀? Certainly. Have you put a title of respect at the top of the letter and a formal greeting?
ADÉ Bẹ́ẹ̀ni. Mo tún ti sọ ìdí tí mo fi ń kọ ìwé náà pẹ̀lú "nítorí pé," àti ohun tí n ó ṣe bí wọ́n bá fún mi ní àṣẹ. Yes. I have also stated the reason I am writing the letter using "because," and what I will do if they grant me permission.
ẸNJ. KÚNLÉ Dára. Ṣùgbọ́n wo ìpínrọ̀ kejì: níbi tí o ti sọ "ọ̀gá," rí i pé ó ye kí a mọ̀ dájú pé ẹni tí o ń tọ́ka sí ni ọ̀gá ọ́fíìsì, kì í ṣe ẹnì kejì. Good. But look at the second paragraph: where you say "the officer," make sure it is clear that you are referring to the office officer, not someone else.
ADÉ Mo yé mi. N ó ṣàtúnṣe sí i kí ìtọ́kasí náà ó túbọ̀ ṣe kedere kí n tó fi ránṣẹ́. I understand. I will revise it so the reference is clearer before I send it.

Guided Practice

  1. Identify the sentence where Ẹnjinia Kúnlé checks whether Adé included a title of respect and a formal greeting.
  2. Find the sentence where Adé explains his reason for writing, and underline the connector he uses.
  3. Locate the sentence where Ẹnjinia Kúnlé points out a reference problem, and explain in your own words what the problem is.
  4. Write one condition clause of your own using bí...bá, describing what you would do if a request were granted.

Bí ẹ bá fún mi ní àṣẹ yìí, n ó fi gbogbo ohun tí mo bá kọ́ ní ìdánilẹ́kọ̀ọ́ náà kọ ìròyìn kúkúrú fún ọ̀gá mi àti fún gbogbo àwọn akẹ́kọ̀ọ́ iṣẹ́ ẹlẹgbẹ́ mi. Apá Kíkọ

Writing Workshop

Write a formal request letter (120–180 words) with full tone marks and dot-under letters throughout. Your letter must include an address term and formal greeting, a reason clause with nítorí pé, a condition clause with bí...bá, at least one clear reference chain across two or more sentences, and a closing formula.

Sample letter shell — for reference only

Sí [àkọlé ọlá àti orúkọ ẹni tí a ń kọ̀wé sí]. Ẹ káàbọ̀, sà/màmá. Orúkọ mi ni [orúkọ rẹ]. Mo ń kọ ìwé yìí nítorí pé [ìdí ìbéèrè rẹ].

To [title and name of addressee]. Welcome, sir/madam. My name is [your name]. I am writing this letter because [your reason for the request].

Bí ẹ bá [ipò kìíní], n ó [ohun tí o máa ṣe]. Mo dúpẹ́ lọ́wọ́ yín tẹ́lẹ̀ fún àyẹ̀wò yín lórí ìbéèrè mi. Pẹ̀lú ọ̀wọ̀, [orúkọ rẹ].

If you [grant the request], I will [what you will do]. I thank you in advance for your consideration of my request. With respect, [your name].
  • Reread the model dialogue and the model letter above.
  • Choose a real or realistic request (leave, permission, resources, information).
  • Draft the opening, reason clause, condition clause, and closing.
  • Check that every mention of a person or institution is clear from first mention to last.
  • Apply the Formal Letter Editing Checkpoint before finalizing.

    • Include

    • an address term and formal greeting at the opening
    • a reason clause with nítorí pé
    • a condition clause with bí...bá
    • a closing formula such as pẹ̀lú ọ̀wọ̀

    Avoid

    • introducing new particles or clause types beyond consolidated Volume III material plus this chapter's reason/condition clauses
    • ambiguous references to "he/she/they" without a clear antecedent
    • omitting tone marks or dot-under letters
    Formal Letter Editing Checkpoint
    • Every vowel that should carry a high or low tone mark has one.
    • ẹ, ọ, and ṣ are distinguished from e, o, and s throughout.
    • The letter opens with an address term and formal greeting, and closes with a respect formula.
    • The reason clause and condition clause are both present and clearly connected to the request.
    • Every person or institution named in the letter is easy to identify from first mention to last.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Formal requests in Yorùbá institutional life

    In many Yorùbá-speaking institutional settings, a written request is expected to open with clear respect for the addressee's position before stating the request itself, and to close by acknowledging the addressee's time and consideration. This ordering, respect before request, is generally understood as good manners rather than excessive formality, and it applies whether the addressee is a supervisor, a school administrator, or a government office.

    Regional Note — Letter formulas across contexts

    Some institutions use additional fixed formulas beyond pẹ̀lú ọ̀wọ̀ at the close of a letter, particularly in religious, legal, or government correspondence. These additional formulas remain recognition-only for now; Chapter 7 will introduce policy- and rule-related formal register in more depth.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Omitting the reason clause

    Error: Stating a request directly without explaining why it is being made, for example writing only "Mo fẹ́ béèrè àṣẹ" without any nítorí pé clause.

    Correction: Always follow a formal request with a reason clause, since Standard Written Yorùbá formal correspondence expects the addressee to understand why the request is reasonable.

    Common Mistake — Losing the reference chain

    Error: Switching between vague pronouns and titles ("ọ̀gá," "wọ́n," "ẹ") without making clear whether the same person or institution is meant each time.

    Correction: Reread each paragraph and confirm that every pronoun or title clearly points back to a previously named person or institution, as Ẹnjinia Kúnlé demonstrates in the model dialogue.

    Answer Key

    Listening Practice Questions

    1. Adé says every formal letter needs a fitting address term and greeting, a clear reason for writing, and a statement of what will happen or what the writer will do afterward.
    2. Ẹnjinia Kúnlé adds that every reference to people or institutions in the letter must be clear from beginning to end.
    3. Clear reference matters throughout the letter so that the reader is never confused about who is being discussed.

Research, Sources, and Synthesis · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Professional, Academic, Civic, and Cross-Regional Yorùbá

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify the main claim, supporting detail, and source type of a short informational text.
  2. Use citation and attribution language to state where a piece of information came from.
  3. Use summary frames to condense a short source into one or two accurate sentences.
  4. Use contrast and concession clauses to show agreement, disagreement, or partial agreement between two short sources.
  5. Organize a short synthesis using a recognizable report structure: introduction, comparison of sources, and conclusion.
  6. Evaluate a source's reliability in general terms, without overclaiming specialist or academic authority.
  7. Maintain the Standard Written Yorùbá production baseline and full diacritics while writing about research topics.
  8. Produce a short source-based synthesis that fairly represents at least two source texts.

Opening Communication Scenario

Adé and Ẹ̀gbọ́n Fúnmi have been asked to prepare a short segment on how young people in different regions are learning Yorùbá today. They have two short source texts: a brief interview excerpt and a short summary of a community survey. Their task is not to decide which source is "right," but to represent both fairly, note where they agree and disagree, and draw a careful, limited conclusion.

  1. This chapter treats research as a communicative skill: finding, attributing, summarizing, and comparing information from more than one source.
  2. No major new independent clause type is introduced. The chapter develops citation frames, summary frames, and contrast/concession clauses building on subordination already active from Chapter 11.
  3. By the end of the chapter, you will write a short source-based synthesis that accurately represents two sources and states a careful, limited conclusion.

Pronunciation Focus — Academic Presentation Rhythm

Focus — Pacing citation and comparison clauses clearly

This chapter's pronunciation focus is the deliberate pacing used when presenting research findings aloud: a short pause before a citation frame, a slight lengthening on the contrast connector, and clear tone production on names of sources so the listener does not confuse the source with the claim being made.

  • Pause briefly before frames such as gẹ́gẹ́ bí ... ṣe sọ to signal that an attribution is coming.
  • Keep tone accuracy on ní ìyàtọ̀ sí and bí ó tilẹ̀ jẹ́ pé even when speaking quickly, since flattened tone can blur the distinction between agreement and contrast.
  • Slow slightly when stating a conclusion, to mark it clearly as the speaker's own careful synthesis rather than a direct quotation from either source.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter does not introduce a new independent clause type. It develops citation language, summary frames, and contrast/concession clauses, extending the subordination and complement-clause systems already active, and applies them to a recognizable report structure.

Citing a Source

Pattern

Gẹ́gẹ́ bí [orísun] ṣe sọ, ...

This frame attributes a claim to a named source before the claim itself is stated, keeping attribution and content clearly separated.

Gẹ́gẹ́ bí ìwádìí àwùjọ náà ṣe sọ, ọ̀pọ̀lọpọ̀ àwọn ọ̀dọ́ ní ìlú ńlá ń kọ́ Yorùbá ní ilé-ìwé nísinsìnyí ju ti ìgbà kan rí lọ.

According to the community survey, many young people in the city are now learning Yorùbá in school more than before.

Summarizing a Source Briefly

  • Àwòkẹ́kọ̀ọ́ náà fi hàn pé àwọn ọ̀dọ́ kan máa ń kọ́ Yorùbá lọ́wọ́ àwọn òbí wọn ní ilé, kì í ṣe ní ilé-ìwé nìkan. The interview shows that some young people learn Yorùbá from their parents at home, not only in school. summary frame: [orísun] fi hàn pé + one condensed claim
  • Ní ṣókí, orísun méjèèjì gbà pé ìfẹ́ láti kọ́ Yorùbá ń pọ̀ sí i láàrin àwọn ọ̀dọ́. In short, both sources agree that interest in learning Yorùbá is increasing among young people. ní ṣókí = summary-closing frame across two sources

Contrast and Concession Between Sources

Pattern

Bí ó tilẹ̀ jẹ́ pé [ẹ̀tọ́ ọ̀rọ̀ àkọ́kọ́], ní ìyàtọ̀ sí i, [ẹ̀tọ́ ọ̀rọ̀ kejì].

The concession clause acknowledges the first source's claim before the contrast connector introduces how the second source differs, avoiding a flat "both agree" or "both disagree" oversimplification.

Bí ó tilẹ̀ jẹ́ pé ìwádìí àwùjọ náà rí i pé ọ̀pọ̀ ọ̀dọ́ ń kọ́ Yorùbá ní ilé-ìwé, ní ìyàtọ̀ sí i, àwòkẹ́kọ̀ọ́ náà tẹnu mọ́ ipa tí ìdílé ń kó nínú kíkọ́ èdè náà.

Although the community survey found that many young people learn Yorùbá in school, in contrast, the interview emphasizes the role family plays in learning the language.

Careful conclusions, not overclaiming

A synthesis conclusion should stay close to what the two sources actually support. Use hedged frames such as ó dà bí ẹni pé ("it seems that") or a lè sọ pé, láti inú orísun méjèèjì... ("we can say that, from both sources...") rather than presenting a limited comparison as a settled, general fact about all Yorùbá speakers or all regions.

Recognition note — Report structure as a discourse pattern

The introduction–comparison–conclusion structure used in this chapter is a discourse-organization pattern, not a new grammatical system. It reuses subordination, focus, and reference systems already active and arranges them in a recognizable order suited to research-style writing and speaking.

Model Dialogue

Ipò: Adé àti Ẹ̀gbọ́n Fúnmi ń fiwé àwọn orísun méjì kí wọ́n tó kọ àkópọ̀ ọ̀rọ̀ wọn.
Setting: Adé and Ẹ̀gbọ́n Fúnmi are comparing two sources before writing their synthesis.

ADÉ Gẹ́gẹ́ bí ìwádìí àwùjọ náà ṣe sọ, iye àwọn ọ̀dọ́ tí ń kọ́ Yorùbá ní ilé-ìwé ti pọ̀ sí i ní ọdún márùn-ún sẹ́yìn. According to the community survey, the number of young people learning Yorùbá in school has increased over the last five years.
ẸGBỌ́N FÚNMI Ó dára, ṣùgbọ́n àwòkẹ́kọ̀ọ́ tí a ní kò sọ nípa ilé-ìwé rárá; ó ṣe pàtàkì sí bí àwọn òbí ṣe ń kọ́ ọmọ wọn ní ilé. Good, but the interview we have doesn't discuss school at all; it emphasizes how parents teach their children at home.
ADÉ Ǹjẹ́ a lè sọ pé orísun méjèèjì takò ara wọn, tàbí wọ́n kàn ń sọ nípa ọ̀nà ọ̀tọ̀ọ̀tọ̀? Can we say the two sources contradict each other, or are they just talking about different paths?
ẸGBỌ́N FÚNMI Mo rò pé wọn kò takò ara wọn; bí ó tilẹ̀ jẹ́ pé ìwádìí àwùjọ náà tẹnu mọ́ ilé-ìwé, ní ìyàtọ̀ sí i, àwòkẹ́kọ̀ọ́ náà kàn ń fi ọ̀nà mìíràn tí àwọn ọ̀dọ́ ń gbà kọ́ èdè náà hàn. I don't think they contradict each other; although the community survey emphasizes school, in contrast, the interview simply shows another path young people use to learn the language.
ADÉ Ó yẹ, nítorí náà a lè parí ọ̀rọ̀ wa pé, láti inú orísun méjèèjì, ó dà bí ẹni pé ọ̀nà tí ọ̀dọ́ ń gbà kọ́ Yorùbá ti gbòòrò sí i, kì í ṣe pé ọ̀nà kan ṣoṣo ni ó ṣẹ́kù. Right, so we can close our piece by saying that, from both sources, it seems the paths young people use to learn Yorùbá have broadened, not that only one path remains.
ẸGBỌ́N FÚNMI Bẹ́ẹ̀ ni, ṣùgbọ́n kí a rántí láti sọ pé orísun méjèèjì kéré, nítorí náà a kò lè sọ pé èyí ni ipò gbogbo àdúgbò Yorùbá. Yes, but let's remember to note that both sources are small, so we can't claim this reflects the situation of every Yorùbá community.

Speaking Practice

Tier 1 — Supported

Tier 2 — Guided

Writing Workshop

Produce a short source-based synthesis (220–270 words) that introduces a research question, cites and summarizes two sources (provided, researched, or imagined), notes at least one point of agreement or contrast using a concession/contrast clause, and closes with a carefully hedged conclusion. Full tone marks and dot-under letters are required throughout.

  • State the research question or topic in one introductory sentence.
  • Cite and summarize the first source in one or two sentences, using a citation frame.
  • Cite and summarize the second source in one or two sentences, using a citation frame.
  • Write one sentence comparing the sources, using a contrast or concession clause.
  • Write a closing conclusion of two or three sentences, using a hedged frame and noting any limitation of the sources used.
  • Apply the Synthesis Checkpoint before finalizing.

    Include

    • a clear research question or topic statement
    • a citation and summary for each of two sources
    • at least one contrast or concession clause comparing the sources
    • a hedged conclusion that notes a limitation

    Avoid

    • presenting one source's claim as the only truth

    Synthesis Checkpoint
    • The research question or topic is stated clearly in the introduction.
    • Each source is cited and summarized accurately, not quoted at length.
    • At least one contrast or concession clause compares the two sources.
    • The conclusion is hedged appropriately and notes a limitation of the sources.
    • Every vowel that should carry a high or low tone mark has one, and ẹ, ọ, and ṣ are distinguished throughout.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Community knowledge as a legitimate source

    Research about language use among Yorùbá speakers often draws on community surveys, interviews, and lived observation, alongside published academic studies. This chapter treats community-based sources as legitimate research material worth citing accurately, not as anecdotal filler beneath formal citation.

    Regional Note — Variation across communities in research findings

    Findings about language habits, education, or culture in one Yorùbá-speaking community should not be generalized to all Yorùbá speakers everywhere. This chapter reinforces the labeling habits developed in Chapter 14 by requiring learners to state clearly which community or source a finding comes from.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Dropping the citation and presenting a source's claim as the writer's own fact

    Error: Stating a source's finding as a plain fact without a citation frame, so it appears to be the writer's own general claim.

    Correction: Use a citation frame such as gẹ́gẹ́ bí ... ṣe sọ so the reader knows exactly where the claim comes from.

    Common Mistake — Treating two different findings as a flat contradiction

    Error: Describing two sources that address different aspects of a topic as though they directly contradict one another.

    Correction: Use a concession/contrast clause to state precisely what differs, and consider whether the sources might be complementary rather than contradictory.

    This chapter developed the ability to use citation language, summary frames, and contrast/concession clauses to compare short sources and produce a careful, hedged synthesis. Learners practiced identifying a source's main claim, attributing it accurately, comparing it with a second source, and organizing findings using an introduction–comparison–conclusion structure, while maintaining the Standard Written Yorùbá production baseline.

    Practical Performance Task

    Iṣẹ́ aṣeyọrí — Abala 16

    Àkópọ̀ Ọ̀rọ̀ Orísun Méjì — A Short Source-Based Synthesis

    Produce a complete short synthesis comparing two sources, entirely grounded in Standard Written Yorùbá.

    Component 1 — Source Planning Table

    • Two sources, each with its main claim and its relation to the other source noted.
    • Full tone marks and dot-under letters throughout.

    Component 2 — Written Synthesis

    • 220–270 words citing and summarizing both sources, comparing them with a contrast or concession clause, and closing with a hedged conclusion.
    • At least one explicit statement of the sources' limitations.

    Component 3 — Spoken Presentation

    • Present the synthesis aloud (90–120 seconds) to a partner, instructor, or recording, maintaining measured presentation pacing, then respond to one follow-up question asking you to justify your conclusion.

    Assessment note: This task provides evidence of accurate citation, fair summarization, appropriate contrast/concession use, and a carefully hedged conclusion. It does not assess independent academic research methodology beyond this chapter's scope.

    Answer Key

    Listening Practice Questions

    1. Before comparing two or more sources, one should first summarize what each source says on its own.
    2. The instructor warns against favoring one preferred source and describing the other source unfairly.
    3. A fair conclusion should be one that both sources can support, not one that goes beyond what they actually say.

    Reading Practice Questions

    1. Young people often omit tone marks and dot-under letters when texting because of time constraints.
    2. Secondary school students use full diacritics in school assignments, though not in casual conversation with friends.
    3. Both sources agree that context determines whether someone uses full diacritics.
    4. The passage concludes that full diacritic use remains expected in appropriate formal writing, even though young people may write quickly without them in informal contexts.

    Guided Practice — Sample Response

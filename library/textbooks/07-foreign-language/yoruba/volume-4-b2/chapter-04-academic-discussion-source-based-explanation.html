Academic Discussion and Source-Based Explanation · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Professional, Academic, Civic, and Cross-Regional Yorùbá

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Attribute an idea or finding to a source using consolidated attribution frames.
  2. Use complement clauses after verbs of saying, thinking, knowing, and showing to report source content.
  3. Express contrast between two sources or ideas using consolidated contrast connectors.
  4. Give a reason for a claim using reason clauses appropriate to academic explanation.
  5. Use hedging frames to present information cautiously rather than as absolute fact.
  6. Listen to and read short source-based passages, identifying claims, reasons, and attributed sources.
  7. Write a short source-based explanation that attributes, contrasts, and hedges appropriately.

Opening Communication Scenario

Adé's supervisor, Ẹnjinia Kúnlé, asks him to prepare a short written explanation for the office newsletter, comparing what two different reports say about community water access in the area. Adé must present each report's findings accurately, note where the two reports disagree, and avoid stating either report's claims as flatly certain when the reports themselves are cautious.

  1. This chapter teaches how to attribute ideas to sources and report their content using complement clauses.
  2. It develops contrast, reason, and hedging language inside the functional task of a source-based explanation.
  3. By the end of the chapter, you will write your own short source-based explanation, following the same structure Adé uses.

Pronunciation Focus

Focus — Academic presentation tone and pacing

Academic explanation is typically delivered more slowly and evenly than meeting talk, with clear pauses between a claim, its source, and its reason. This chapter's pronunciation focus is producing a measured, evenly paced explanation that gives the listener time to track each new idea.

  • Practice the attribution phrase gẹ́gẹ́ bí ìjábọ̀ yìí ṣe sọ ("according to what this report says") with steady tone across all four words.
  • Pay special attention to ó ṣeé ṣe kí, where tone must remain distinct on each syllable even though the phrase is spoken quickly as a unit.
  • Practice pausing briefly between a claim and its attributed source, as a speaker would when citing evidence aloud.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter introduces source attribution and complement clauses for reporting source content, and develops contrast, reason, and hedging frames for academic explanation.

Source attribution and complement clauses

Pattern

Gẹ́gẹ́ bí [orísun] ṣe sọ, [ọ̀rọ̀ àsọyé] pé [àwárí].

Source attribution opens with gẹ́gẹ́ bí ("according to") plus the source, followed by a reporting verb such as sọ ("say") or fi hàn ("show") and a complement clause introduced by pé ("that").

Gẹ́gẹ́ bí ìjábọ̀ àkọ́kọ́ ṣe sọ, omi mímọ́ kò tí ì tó fún gbogbo agbègbè náà.

According to the first report, clean water is not yet sufficient for the whole area.

Contrast between sources with `ṣùgbọ́n` and `bí ó tilẹ̀ jẹ́ pé`

  • Ìjábọ̀ àkọ́kọ́ sọ pé omi kò tó; ṣùgbọ́n ìjábọ̀ kejì fi hàn pé ipò náà ti túbọ̀ sunwọ̀n sí i ní ọdún tí ó kọjá. The first report says water is insufficient; but the second report shows that the situation has improved in the past year. ṣùgbọ́n contrast connector, introduced this chapter
  • Bí ó tilẹ̀ jẹ́ pé àwọn ìjábọ̀ méjèèjì gbà pé iṣẹ́ kan wà láti ṣe, wọn kò fohùn ṣọ̀kan lórí bí a ṣe lè yanjú rẹ̀. Even though both reports agree that there is work to be done, they do not agree on how to solve it. bí ó tilẹ̀ jẹ́ pé concessive contrast, introduced this chapter
Introduced — Reason clauses in academic explanation

Reason clauses with nítorí pé, introduced for formal requests in Chapter 2, are now applied to academic explanation: àwọn ọ̀mọ̀wé gbà pé àbájáde yìí ṣeé gbára lé nítorí pé wọ́n lo ọ̀nà ìwádìí tí ó bọ́gbọ́n mu ("the scholars accept this result as reliable because they used a sound research method"). In academic use, the reason clause typically explains why a finding should be trusted, not only why a request is being made.

Introduced — Hedging frames

Academic explanation must distinguish confirmed findings from tentative ones. Learners use ó ṣeé ṣe kí ("it is possible that") and bí a ṣe fẹ́ mọ̀ ("as far as we know") to present information cautiously: ó ṣeé ṣe kí iye owó náà yí padà ní àwọn oṣù tí ń bọ̀ ("it is possible that the amount may change in the coming months"). Hedging must not be omitted when the source itself is uncertain.

Consolidated — Reference chains continue into source-based writing

The reference-chain control developed in Chapters 2 and 3 continues here: when two reports are compared, each mention of "the first report," "the second report," or a named researcher must remain unambiguous from first mention to last, so the reader never confuses which source is being described.

Model Dialogue

Ipò: Adé àti Bọ́lá ń jíròrò àwọn ìjábọ̀ méjì tí wọ́n nílò láti fi wé ara wọn ṣáájú kí Adé tó kọ àlàyé rẹ̀ fún ìwé ìròyìn ọ́fíìsì.
Setting: Adé and Bọ́lá are discussing two reports they need to compare before Adé writes his explanation for the office newsletter.

ADÉ Bọ́lá, kí ni ìjábọ̀ àkọ́kọ́ sọ nípa omi mímọ́ ní agbègbè wa? Bọ́lá, what does the first report say about clean water in our area?
BỌ́LÁ Gẹ́gẹ́ bí ìjábọ̀ àkọ́kọ́ ṣe sọ, omi mímọ́ kò tí ì tó fún gbogbo agbègbè náà, nítorí pé kànga tí ó wà kò tó iye tí a nílò. According to the first report, clean water is not yet sufficient for the whole area, because the wells that exist are not enough for what is needed.
ADÉ Àti ìjábọ̀ kejì ńkọ́? Ǹjẹ́ ó fohùn ṣọ̀kan pẹ̀lú èyí? And what about the second report? Does it agree with this?
BỌ́LÁ Rárá o. Ìjábọ̀ kejì fi hàn pé ipò náà ti túbọ̀ sunwọ̀n sí i ní ọdún tí ó kọjá, ṣùgbọ́n ó ṣeé ṣe kí àwọn ìsọfúnni tí wọ́n lò má tí ì ní gbogbo agbègbè náà nínú. Not at all. The second report shows that the situation has improved in the past year, but it is possible that the information they used does not yet include the whole area.
ADÉ Mo yé mi. Bí ó tilẹ̀ jẹ́ pé àwọn ìjábọ̀ méjèèjì gbà pé iṣẹ́ kan wà láti ṣe, n ó ṣàlàyé ìyàtọ̀ tí ó wà láàrin wọn kí n má ba à sọ ọ̀kan nínú wọn di ohun tí ó dá gbáradi jù. I understand. Even though both reports agree that there is work to be done, I will explain the difference between them so that I do not present one of them as more certain than it really is.

Writing Workshop

Write a short source-based explanation (150–200 words) with full tone marks and dot-under letters throughout. Your explanation must attribute at least two claims to named sources using gẹ́gẹ́ bí, include at least one contrast between the sources using ṣùgbọ́n or bí ó tilẹ̀ jẹ́ pé, include at least one reason clause with nítorí pé, and include at least one hedge using ó ṣeé ṣe kí or bí a ṣe fẹ́ mọ̀.

Sample explanation shell — for reference only

Gẹ́gẹ́ bí [orísun kìíní] ṣe sọ, [àwárí kìíní], nítorí pé [ìdí]. Ṣùgbọ́n gẹ́gẹ́ bí [orísun kejì] ṣe fi hàn, [àwárí kejì].

According to [first source], [first finding], because [reason]. But according to [second source], [second finding].

Bí ó tilẹ̀ jẹ́ pé àwọn orísun méjèèjì [ohun tí wọ́n fohùn ṣọ̀kan lé lórí], ó ṣeé ṣe kí [ohun tí a kò tí ì mọ̀ dájú].

Even though both sources [what they agree on], it is possible that [what remains uncertain].

  • Choose two real or realistic sources with at least one point of agreement and one point of contrast.
  • Draft the attributions, the contrast, the reason, and the hedge.
  • Check that each source remains clearly identifiable across the whole explanation.
  • Apply the Source-Based Explanation Editing Checkpoint before finalizing.

    • Include

    • at least two attributed claims with gẹ́gẹ́ bí
    • a contrast with ṣùgbọ́n or bí ó tilẹ̀ jẹ́ pé
    • a reason clause with nítorí pé
    • a hedge with ó ṣeé ṣe kí or bí a ṣe fẹ́ mọ̀

    Avoid

    • stating an uncertain source claim as flat fact
    • losing track of which source said what
    • omitting tone marks or dot-under letters
    Source-Based Explanation Editing Checkpoint
    • Every vowel that should carry a high or low tone mark has one.
    • ẹ, ọ, and ṣ are distinguished from e, o, and s throughout.
    • Each claim is clearly attributed to its source.
    • At least one contrast and one hedge appear and are used accurately.
    • No source's claim is presented as more certain than the source itself states.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Caution and consensus in shared knowledge

    In many Yorùbá-speaking academic and professional settings, presenting information with appropriate caution is generally viewed as a sign of careful thinking rather than weakness, and speakers commonly acknowledge points of shared agreement before raising a disagreement. This ordering, agreement before contrast, tends to keep academic and professional discussion collegial even when sources conflict.

    Regional Note — Academic register across institutions

    Some universities and research institutions use additional specialized academic conventions, such as formal citation formats, beyond what a workplace newsletter explanation typically requires. These additional conventions remain recognition-only for now; Chapter 16 will return to research, sources, and synthesis in more depth.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Dropping the hedge

    Error: Reporting a tentative finding as certain fact, for example writing "Iye owó náà jẹ́ ọgọ́rùn-ún kan" when the source itself states this only as a possibility.

    Correction: Preserve the source's own level of certainty by using a hedge such as ó ṣeé ṣe kí, as in "Ó ṣeé ṣe kí iye owó náà jẹ́ ọgọ́rùn-ún kan."

    Common Mistake — Merging two sources into one voice

    Error: Presenting two different reports' findings in a single sentence without attribution, so the reader cannot tell which claim came from which source.

    Correction: Attribute each claim separately with gẹ́gẹ́ bí, and use a contrast connector such as ṣùgbọ́n to mark where the sources diverge, as in the model explanation above.

    Answer Key

    Listening Practice Questions

    1. Adé says it is important to state each report's claim clearly and to establish what kind of information the report gives.
    2. If two reports disagree, the writer should state the difference clearly rather than choosing one source and presenting it as the only truth.
    3. Ẹnjinia Kúnlé adds that uncertain information should be expressed with phrases such as "ó ṣeé ṣe kí" rather than stated as completely certain.

    Reading Practice Questions

    1. The first report says clean water is insufficient, because the existing wells do not meet the need.
    2. The second report shows the situation has improved since three new wells were built.
    3. The two reports disagree on how much money will be needed to finish the work.
    4. Sample: "Bí a ṣe fẹ́ mọ̀" is used because the actual cost is not yet certain, since the two reports used different information.

    Guided Practice — Sample Responses

    1. "Gẹ́gẹ́ bí ìjábọ̀ àkọ́kọ́ ṣe sọ" attributes the finding. 2. "Ó ṣeé ṣe kí àwọn ìsọfúnni tí wọ́n lò má tí ì ní gbogbo agbègbè náà nínú" is the hedge. 3. "Bí ó tilẹ̀ jẹ́ pé àwọn ìjábọ̀ méjèèjì gbà pé iṣẹ́ kan wà láti ṣe" is the contrast connector. 4. Sample: Gẹ́gẹ́ bí olùkọ́ wa ṣe sọ, ìdánwò yìí máa ṣòro díẹ̀ ju ti ọdún tí ó kọjá lọ.

Presentations and Question Handling · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Professional, Academic, Civic, and Cross-Regional Yorùbá

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Open, structure, and close a formal presentation using recognizable presentation frames.
  2. Signal emphasis on a key point using controlled focus and intonation strategies.
  3. Ask for clarification and respond to a clarification request during a Q&A exchange.
  4. Disagree politely with a questioner's premise or a colleague's claim without abandoning your position.
  5. Use repair strategies to recover smoothly when a listener misunderstands or a speaker misspeaks.
  6. Maintain measured formal delivery pacing and accurate tone production under the pressure of live questions.
  7. Maintain the Standard Written Yorùbá production baseline and full diacritics in presentation notes and slides.
  8. Deliver a structured presentation and handle at least two follow-up questions appropriately.

Opening Communication Scenario

Adé has been asked to present the source-based synthesis from Chapter 16 to a small community education committee. Ẹ̀gbọ́n Fúnmi will attend as a colleague and may ask a follow-up question. Adé must open the presentation clearly, walk through the findings without reading a script word for word, and handle at least one question that challenges the conclusion — without becoming defensive or abandoning the careful, hedged position built in the previous chapter.

  1. This chapter treats presenting and being questioned as a single connected skill, not two separate tasks.
  2. No major new independent clause type is introduced. The chapter consolidates presentation-opening frames, focus for emphasis, clarification questions, polite disagreement, and repair strategies, building on systems already active from earlier chapters.
  3. By the end of the chapter, you will deliver a structured presentation and respond to follow-up questions while maintaining formal register and tone accuracy.

Pronunciation Focus — Formal Delivery and Q&A

Focus — Steady pacing under the pressure of live questions

This chapter's pronunciation focus is maintaining measured, formal delivery pacing even when a question is unexpected or challenging. Nervous speech tends to flatten tone and rush through particles that carry meaning; deliberate pacing protects both.

  • Pause briefly after an opening frame such as ohun tí ó ṣe pàtàkì jùlọ ni pé to let the emphasis land before continuing.
  • Keep full tone accuracy on mo gbọ́ ohun tí o ń sọ, ṣùgbọ́n even when responding quickly to a challenging question, since a flattened tone can make polite disagreement sound curt.
  • Slow down slightly at the start of a repair strategy such as jẹ́ kí n tún un sọ ní ọ̀nà mìíràn, signaling to the listener that a clearer restatement is coming.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter does not introduce a new independent clause type. It consolidates presentation-opening and closing frames, focus for emphasis, clarification questions, polite disagreement, and repair strategies, extending the focus, subordination, and reference systems already active.

Opening a Presentation

Pattern

Ẹ ku ọ̀sán/àárọ̀, orúkọ mi ni [orúkọ], mo sì fẹ́ bá yín sọ̀rọ̀ nípa [àkòrí].

This frame names the speaker and the topic before any claim is made, giving the audience a clear frame for what follows.

Ẹ ku ọ̀sán, orúkọ mi ni Adé, mo sì fẹ́ bá yín sọ̀rọ̀ nípa bí àwọn ọ̀dọ́ ṣe ń kọ́ Yorùbá lónìí.

Good afternoon, my name is Adé, and I would like to speak to you about how young people are learning Yorùbá today.

Marking Emphasis

  • Ohun tí ó ṣe pàtàkì jùlọ ni pé orísun méjèèjì gbà pé ìfẹ́ láti kọ́ Yorùbá ń pọ̀ sí i. The most important thing is that both sources agree that interest in learning Yorùbá is increasing. emphasis frame + ni-focus on the claim itself
  • Ẹ jọ̀wọ́ ẹ ṣàkíyèsí pé kì í ṣe ọ̀nà kan ṣoṣo ni àwọn ọ̀dọ́ ń gbà kọ́ èdè náà. Please note that it is not only one path that young people use to learn the language. directive attention-frame + negative focus

Asking and Answering Clarification Questions

Pattern

Ǹjẹ́ mo lè ṣàlàyé ohun tí o ní lọ́kàn, tàbí ǹjẹ́ o fẹ́ kí n tún un sọ?

This frame lets a speaker check the listener's meaning before answering, avoiding an answer built on a misunderstood question.

Ǹjẹ́ mo lè ṣàlàyé ohun tí o ní lọ́kàn nípa "ọ̀nà tí ó gbòòrò sí i", tàbí ǹjẹ́ ìbéèrè rẹ jẹ́ nípa iye ọ̀dọ́ tí ó lọ́wọ́ nínú rẹ̀?

May I clarify what you mean by "the path that has broadened," or is your question about the number of young people involved?

Disagreeing Politely

Pattern

Mo gbọ́ ohun tí o ń sọ, ṣùgbọ́n [ìdí tí a fi ní ojú-ìwòye mìíràn].

The frame acknowledges the questioner's point before the contrast connector introduces a different position, so disagreement does not sound dismissive.

Mo gbọ́ ohun tí o ń sọ, ṣùgbọ́n orísun méjèèjì tí a lò kéré jù láti fi parí ọ̀rọ̀ pé ọ̀nà kan ṣoṣo ni ó ṣẹ́kù.

I hear what you're saying, but the two sources we used are too small to conclude that only one path remains.

Repair Strategies and Closing

Pattern

Jẹ́ kí n tún un sọ ní ọ̀nà mìíràn: ... Ní parí, a dúpẹ́ fún àkókò yín.

A repair frame restates a point more clearly without apologizing excessively; a closing frame signals the presentation's end and offers courtesy without dragging the ending out.

Jẹ́ kí n tún un sọ ní ọ̀nà mìíràn: kì í ṣe pé ilé-ìwé ti dín kù, bí kò ṣe pé àwọn ọ̀nà mìíràn ti bẹ̀rẹ̀ sí í gbilẹ̀ pẹ̀lú rẹ̀.

Let me put that another way: it's not that school has declined, but that other paths have begun to flourish alongside it.

Recognition note — Q&A as a discourse pattern, not a new grammar system

The clarification, disagreement, and repair frames in this chapter reuse focus, negation, and contrast systems already active. What is new is the discourse skill of applying them under the real-time pressure of an audience question, not a new grammatical structure.

Ìjíròrò Àpẹẹrẹ

Model Dialogue

Ipò: Adé ń fi ìwádìí rẹ̀ hàn fún ìgbìmọ̀ àgbègbè kan; Ẹ̀gbọ́n Fúnmi wà nínú àwọn olùgbọ́.
Setting: Adé is presenting the research to a community committee; Ẹ̀gbọ́n Fúnmi is among the audience.

ADÉ Ẹ ku ọ̀sán, orúkọ mi ni Adé, mo sì fẹ́ bá yín sọ̀rọ̀ nípa bí àwọn ọ̀dọ́ ṣe ń kọ́ Yorùbá lónìí, láti inú orísun méjì tí a ṣàyẹ̀wò. Good afternoon, my name is Adé, and I would like to speak with you about how young people are learning Yorùbá today, based on two sources we examined.
(Adé fi àwòrán àkópọ̀ rẹ̀ hàn kí ó tó parí, ó sì ké sí ìbéèrè.)
(Adé shows a summary slide before closing and invites questions.)
ẸGBỌ́N FÚNMI Ǹjẹ́ mo lè bèèrè ìbéèrè kan? Ǹjẹ́ ìwádìí méjì ni ó tó láti fi parí ọ̀rọ̀ bẹ́ẹ̀? May I ask a question? Are two sources enough to draw such a conclusion?
ADÉ Mo gbọ́ ohun tí o ń sọ, ṣùgbọ́n mo ti sọ tẹ́lẹ̀ pé orísun méjèèjì kéré, nítorí náà a kò lè sọ pé èyí ni ipò gbogbo àdúgbò Yorùbá; a kàn ń fi àpẹẹrẹ hàn. I hear what you're saying, but I already noted that both sources are small, so we cannot say this reflects every Yorùbá community; we are only showing an example.
ẸGBỌ́N FÚNMI Ó dára, ṣùgbọ́n ǹjẹ́ mo lè ṣàlàyé ohun tí mo ní lọ́kàn síi? Mo fẹ́ mọ̀ bóyá ìjọba yẹ kí ó lo àbájáde yìí láti yí ètò ẹ̀kọ́ padà. Alright, but may I clarify further? I want to know whether the government should use this finding to change education policy.
ADÉ Jẹ́ kí n tún un sọ ní ọ̀nà mìíràn: ìwádìí wa kò tó láti dábàá ìyípadà ètò ìjọba; ó kàn fi hàn pé ó yẹ kí ìwádìí tí ó gbòòrò sí i ṣẹlẹ̀ kí a tó dábàá bẹ́ẹ̀. Let me put that another way: our research is not sufficient to propose a policy change; it simply shows that broader research should happen before such a proposal is made.
ADÉ Ní parí, mo dúpẹ́ fún àkókò yín, mo sì ṣetán láti dáhùn àwọn ìbéèrè mìíràn lẹ́yìn ìpàdé yìí. In closing, I thank you for your time, and I am ready to answer other questions after this meeting.

Guided Practice

  1. Underline the opening frame and the closing frame in the dialogue above, and state what function each performs.
  2. Identify the clarification question Ẹ̀gbọ́n Fúnmi asks the second time, and explain why Adé chooses to restate rather than simply repeat the same sentence.
  3. Explain how Adé disagrees with the idea of using the research to justify a policy change without dismissing the question.
  4. Rewrite Adé's second response using a different repair-strategy opening of your own choice, keeping the same meaning.
  5. Write one sentence of your own using ohun tí ó ṣe pàtàkì jùlọ ni pé to emphasize a point about any topic of your choice.

Listening Practice

Ask an instructor, study partner, or qualified Yorùbá speaker to read the passage below aloud at a measured, presentation-style pace, or read it aloud yourself and then cover it before answering.

If no speaker is available, this is a Listening Script for Independent Use. Machine text-to-speech must be checked by a qualified Yorùbá speaker before use as a pronunciation model.

"Ní àpérò kan tí ó ṣẹlẹ̀ láìpẹ́ yìí, olùdámọ̀ràn ètò-ẹ̀kọ́ kan fi ìwádìí rẹ̀ hàn fún àwọn olùkọ́ ilé-ìwé gíga. Ní ìbẹ̀rẹ̀, ó sọ orúkọ rẹ̀ àti àkòrí ọ̀rọ̀ rẹ̀ kí ó tó bẹ̀rẹ̀. Nígbà tí ó parí ọ̀rọ̀ rẹ̀, olùkọ́ kan bi í pé kí ló dé tí ìwádìí rẹ̀ kò fi ilé-ìwé gbogbo hàn. Ó dáhùn pé, 'Mo gbọ́ ohun tí o ń sọ, ṣùgbọ́n àkókò tí a ní kò tó láti ṣàyẹ̀wò gbogbo ilé-ìwé; a yàn àwọn tí ó ṣe ìtọ́kasí dáradára fún ìwádìí yìí.' Olùkọ́ mìíràn sì bi í pé bóyá ó ṣeé ṣe kí ó ṣàlàyé ohun tí ó túmọ̀ sí nípa 'ìtọ́kasí dáradára'. Ó dá a lóhùn pé, 'Ǹjẹ́ mo lè ṣàlàyé pé mo túmọ̀ sí àwọn ilé-ìwé tí ó ní iye akẹ́kọ̀ọ́ àti olùkọ́ tí ó tó láti fi àpẹẹrẹ hàn ní ọ̀nà tí ó bójú mu.' Ní parí, ó dúpẹ́ fún àkókò àwọn olùkọ́ náà, ó sì ṣetán láti dáhùn ìbéèrè mìíràn lẹ́yìn náà."

1. How does the advisor open the presentation?

2. What question does the first teacher ask, and how does the advisor respond?

3. What does the advisor do when asked to clarify "well-suited"?

Writing Workshop

Produce a short presentation script (180–230 words) that opens with a naming/topic frame, states one emphasized main point, anticipates one likely challenging question with a prepared polite-disagreement or clarification response, and closes with a courtesy closing frame. Full tone marks and dot-under letters are required throughout.

  • Write an opening line naming yourself and your topic.
  • Write one or two sentences presenting your main point, using an emphasis frame.
  • Write one anticipated challenging question a listener might ask.
  • Write your prepared response to that question, using a clarification, polite-disagreement, or repair frame.
  • Write a closing sentence using a closing frame and a thanks formula.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Respectful challenge in public discussion

    In many Yorùbá-speaking professional and community settings, challenging a presenter's conclusion is treated as a normal, valued part of public discussion, provided it is done with courtesy toward the speaker and the audience. The frames in this chapter model that balance: direct enough to raise a genuine concern, courteous enough to preserve the relationship.

    Regional Note — Variation in Q&A conventions

    Some formal settings expect all questions to be routed through a moderator (alábòójútó ìjíròrò), while smaller community meetings often allow direct audience questions to the speaker. This chapter's frames work in both settings; only the sequencing of who is addressed changes.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Answering before confirming what was actually asked

    Error: Launching into a full answer immediately, based on a guess about what the questioner meant, when the question was ambiguous.

    Correction: Use a clarification frame such as ǹjẹ́ mo lè ṣàlàyé ohun tí o ní lọ́kàn before answering an ambiguous question.

    Common Mistake — Treating disagreement as an attack to be deflected

    Error: Responding to a challenging question by dismissing it or repeating the original claim more forcefully, without acknowledging the questioner's point.

    Correction: Acknowledge the point first with a frame such as mo gbọ́ ohun tí o ń sọ, then explain the difference in position calmly.

    Practical Performance Task

    Iṣẹ́ aṣeyọrí — Abala 17

    Ìfihàn Tí a Ṣètò àti Àkókò Ìbéèrè — A Structured Presentation with Q&A

    Deliver a complete structured presentation, entirely grounded in Standard Written Yorùbá, and handle live follow-up questions.

    Component 1 — Presentation Script

    • 180–230 words including an opening frame, one emphasized main point, and a closing frame.
    • Full tone marks and dot-under letters throughout.

    Component 2 — Anticipated Q&A Plan

    • One anticipated clarification question and one anticipated challenging question, each with a prepared response.

    Component 3 — Spoken Delivery

    • Deliver the presentation aloud (90–150 seconds) to a partner, instructor, or recording at a measured, formal pace, then respond to at least two follow-up questions, including one unrehearsed question, using clarification, polite disagreement, or repair strategies as needed.

    Assessment note: This task provides evidence of clear presentation structure, appropriate emphasis, accurate clarification and disagreement frames, tone accuracy under real-time pressure, and courteous closing. It does not assess independent public-speaking training beyond this chapter's scope.

    Answer Key

    Listening Practice Questions

    1. The advisor opens by stating their name and the topic of the presentation before beginning.
    2. The first teacher asks why the research did not include every school; the advisor responds that time did not allow examining every school, so schools that provided good reference points for the research were selected.
    3. When asked to clarify "well-suited," the advisor explains that it means schools with enough students and teachers to provide an appropriate example.

    Reading Practice Questions

    1. The club member asks what will happen if there is not enough money for the new program.
    2. The founder asks a clarification question first to confirm whether the member is asking specifically how many months the program can run without new funding.
    3. Once clarified, the founder answers that funding has been arranged for six months, and if funds run short before then, an alternative will be announced quickly.
    4. The founder closes by thanking everyone for their questions and reminding members that each question helps improve the program.

    Guided Practice — Sample Response

    5. Sample response: Ohun tí ó ṣe pàtàkì jùlọ ni pé kí a máa mú omi tútù ní gbogbo ìgbà tí oòrùn bá mú janjan. (The most important thing is that we always drink cool water whenever the sun is very hot.)

Advanced Focus, Relatives, and Reference · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Professional, Academic, Civic, and Cross-Regional Yorùbá

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Use ni focus constructions to identify and emphasize a subject or object within a longer, formal sentence.
  2. Build relative clauses with tí to add identifying or descriptive detail to a noun phrase.
  3. Combine focus and relative clauses to track a single participant across several linked sentences.
  4. Use strong pronouns for contrast and emphasis without destabilizing ordinary word order.
  5. Maintain clear reference chains across an extended passage, avoiding ambiguous or dropped reference.
  6. Recognize, without independent production, how question focus and negative focus interact with relative clauses.
  7. Preserve tone accuracy across long phrases containing focus markers and relative clauses.
  8. Edit a passage for reference clarity, correcting ambiguous pronouns and unclear participant tracking.

Opening Communication Scenario

The regional media association has replied to Adé and Ẹ̀gbọ́n Fúnmi's analysis from Chapter 11 with interest, and has asked them to prepare a short profile of the radio station for publication. A profile of this kind must clearly identify who did what, when, and why, across several linked sentences, without leaving the reader unsure which person or event a pronoun refers back to.

  1. This chapter develops the focus system and the relative-clause system that have been active since earlier chapters, applying them to longer stretches of connected discourse.
  2. No entirely new clause type is introduced. Instead, focus and relative clauses are combined with pronoun choice to keep reference clear across several sentences.
  3. By the end of the chapter, you will edit a short profile for reference clarity, correcting places where a reader might lose track of who or what is being discussed.

Pronunciation Focus — Long-Phrase Tone Control

Focus — Tone across focus markers, relative clauses, and reference chains

Focus markers and relative clauses often sit in the middle of a longer sentence, and learners sometimes let the tone flatten just at these points, because attention shifts to word order rather than to pitch. This chapter's pronunciation focus continues Chapter 11's long-sentence rhythm skill, applied specifically to phrases built around ni and tí.

  • Practice ni and tí in short phrases first, keeping their tone crisp and distinct from surrounding words.
  • When a relative clause interrupts a main clause, keep the tone of the main clause stable both before and after the interruption.
  • Read the model dialogue and profile passage below slowly at first, then again at a natural conversational pace.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter develops the focus and relative-clause systems that have been active since earlier chapters. It does not introduce a new independent clause type; it develops how focus, relative clauses, and pronoun choice work together to track participants clearly across several linked sentences.

Reviewing ni Focus in Longer Sentences

Pattern

[Focused element] ni [rest of clause].

The focused element is fronted before ni; the rest of the clause follows in ordinary word order. In longer, formal sentences, the focused element is often a full noun phrase rather than a single word.

Adé ni ó dá ilé-iṣẹ́ rédíò yìí sílẹ̀ ní ọdún mẹ́wàá sẹ́yìn.

It is Adé who founded this radio station ten years ago.

Relative Clauses with tí

  • Adé, ẹni tí ó dá ilé-iṣẹ́ yìí sílẹ̀, ń ṣiṣẹ́ gẹ́gẹ́ bí alábòójútó títí di òní. Adé, the person who founded this station, has worked as its manager to this day. ẹni tí = "the person who" (relative clause modifying Adé)
  • Ohun tí ó ṣe pàtàkì jùlọ nínú ìtàn ìdásílẹ̀ ni bí Adé ṣe rí owó láti bẹ̀rẹ̀. What is most important in the founding history is how Adé found money to begin. ohun tí = "the thing that" (relative clause as focused subject before ni)
Focus-relative interaction

A relative clause with tí can itself become the focused element before ni, as in ohun tí ó ṣe pàtàkì jùlọ ni... ("what is most important is..."). Here the whole relative clause — not just one word — is fronted for focus. This is a recognition-level pattern in this chapter: notice it when it appears in the readings, but continue to build your own sentences from the simpler focus and relative patterns modeled above.

Strong Pronouns for Contrast and Emphasis

Pattern

Òun ni [rest of clause]. / Àwọn ni [rest of clause].

Òun and àwọn are strong pronouns used with ni to emphasize or contrast a person or group already introduced in the discourse, rather than to introduce someone new.

Ẹ̀gbọ́n Fúnmi bẹ̀rẹ̀ sí ṣiṣẹ́ ní ilé-iṣẹ́ yìí ní ọdún mẹ́ta sẹ́yìn; òun ni ó kọ́kọ́ dábàá iṣẹ́ ìtàkurọ̀sọ orí ayélujára.

Ẹ̀gbọ́n Fúnmi began working at this station three years ago; it is she who first proposed the online broadcasting work.

Keeping Reference Chains Clear

  • Adé àti Ẹ̀gbọ́n Fúnmi ń ṣiṣẹ́ pọ̀ láti ọdún mẹ́ta sẹ́yìn; ẹni akọ́kọ́ ni olùdásílẹ̀, ẹni kejì sì ni alábòójútó ìtàkurọ̀sọ orí ayélujára. Adé and Ẹ̀gbọ́n Fúnmi have worked together since three years ago; the first person is the founder, and the second is the online-broadcasting manager. ẹni akọ́kọ́ / ẹni kejì = explicit reference labels avoiding pronoun ambiguity
  • Nígbà tí Adé bá ń sọ̀rọ̀ nípa ìdàgbàsókè ilé-iṣẹ́, ẹni náà máa ń mẹ́nu kan iṣẹ́ Ẹ̀gbọ́n Fúnmi pẹ̀lú. Whenever Adé speaks about the station's growth, that same person also mentions Ẹ̀gbọ́n Fúnmi's work. ẹni náà = "that same person," repeating the subject explicitly to avoid ambiguity after a time clause
Editing for ambiguity

When a passage introduces two or more people, avoid a bare pronoun immediately after both have been mentioned, since a reader cannot always tell which person it points to. Prefer an explicit noun phrase, a relative clause, an ordinal label such as ẹni akọ́kọ́ or ẹni kejì, or a repeated name at the point where ambiguity would otherwise arise. Once a single participant has been clearly established as the topic of a stretch of discourse, a pronoun may again be used safely.

Recognition note — Question focus and negative focus

The passages and dialogue below may include focus combined with questions (asking specifically "who" or "what" through fronting) or with negation (denying that a particular person or thing is responsible for something). These combinations are receptive at this stage. Focus your own production on the identification, contrast, and reference-tracking patterns introduced above.

Model Dialogue

Ipò: Adé àti Ẹ̀gbọ́n Fúnmi ń gbìmọ̀ràn nípa bí wọn yóò ṣe kọ àpèjúwe ilé-iṣẹ́ rédíò wọn fún àjọ àwọn ilé-iṣẹ́ agbéròyìnjáde àgbègbè.
Setting: Adé and Ẹ̀gbọ́n Fúnmi are planning how to write a profile of their radio station for the regional media association.

ẸGBỌ́N FÚNMI Ta ni ó yẹ kí a mẹ́nu kan kọ́kọ́ nínú àpèjúwe yìí? Who should we mention first in this profile?
ADÉ Mo rò pé ó yẹ kí a bẹ̀rẹ̀ pẹ̀lú ẹni tí ó dá ilé-iṣẹ́ yìí sílẹ̀, ìyẹn ni, èmi fúnra mi. I think we should begin with the person who founded this station — that is, myself.
ẸGBỌ́N FÚNMI Ó dára. Lẹ́yìn náà, a lè sọ nípa mi, ẹni tí ó bẹ̀rẹ̀ sí ṣiṣẹ́ ní ilé-iṣẹ́ yìí ní ọdún mẹ́ta sẹ́yìn gẹ́gẹ́ bí alábòójútó ìtàkurọ̀sọ orí ayélujára. Good. After that, we can talk about me, the person who began working at this station three years ago as the online-broadcasting manager.
ADÉ Ohun tí ó ṣe pàtàkì jùlọ ni pé àwọn òǹkàwé máa mọ̀ pé ẹni akọ́kọ́ ni olùdásílẹ̀, ẹni kejì sì ni alábòójútó ìtàkurọ̀sọ orí ayélujára, kí àṣìṣe má bàa wáyé. What is most important is that readers will know that the first person is the founder, and the second is the online-broadcasting manager, so that no mistake arises.
ẸGBỌ́N FÚNMI Bẹ́ẹ̀ ni. Nígbà tí a bá ń sọ̀rọ̀ nípa ìdàgbàsókè ilé-iṣẹ́, a gbọ́dọ̀ mẹ́nu kan ẹni náà tí ó dábàá iṣẹ́ ìtàkurọ̀sọ orí ayélujára pẹ̀lú, kí á má sọ ọ̀rọ̀ tí kò yé ẹnikẹ́ni. Yes. When we speak about the station's growth, we must also mention that same person who proposed the online broadcasting work, so that we don't say something no one can follow.
ADÉ Òun ni, kì í ṣe èmi. Ẹ jẹ́ ká rí i pé a ṣe kedere nípa ẹni tí a ń tọ́ka sí ní gbogbo ibi tí a bá kọ pé "ẹni náà." It is she, not I. Let's make sure we are clear about who we are referring to everywhere we write "that same person."

Writing Workshop

Write a short profile (200–250 words) of two people connected to the same organization, project, or family, with full tone marks and dot-under letters throughout. Your profile must include at least one ni focus construction, one relative clause with tí, one strong pronoun (òun or àwọn) used for contrast, and at least one explicit reference label (such as ẹni akọ́kọ́, ẹni kejì, or ẹni náà) used to prevent ambiguity.

  • Reread the model dialogue and the reading passage above.
  • List the two people you will profile and note one clearly distinguishing role for each.
  • Draft the profile, introducing each person with a relative clause and using focus to highlight their key contribution.
  • Check every pronoun: could a reader mistake who it refers to? If so, replace it with a name, a relative clause, or an explicit reference label.
  • Apply the Reference Clarity Checkpoint before finalizing.

    • Include

    • a ni focus construction identifying a key contribution
    • a relative clause with tí describing a person
    • a strong pronoun (òun or àwọn) for contrast or emphasis
    • at least one explicit reference label preventing pronoun ambiguity

    Avoid

    • a bare pronoun immediately after two people have both been introduced
    • overusing names when a relative clause or reference label would read more naturally

    Reference Clarity Checkpoint
    • Every pronoun in the passage points clearly to one identifiable person or thing.
    • Wherever two people could be confused, an explicit label, name, or relative clause is used instead of a bare pronoun.
    • At least one ni focus construction and one relative clause with tí appear.
    • Strong pronouns are used only for contrast or emphasis, not as ordinary subject pronouns.
    • Every vowel that should carry a high or low tone mark has one, and ẹ, ọ, and ṣ are distinguished throughout.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Naming roles before naming people

    In many Yorùbá professional and institutional contexts, a formal profile often introduces a person's role or contribution clearly before or alongside their name, rather than relying on the name alone to carry meaning for readers unfamiliar with the organization. Focus constructions and relative clauses support this pattern by letting a writer foreground the contribution itself.

    Regional Note — Ordinal reference labels in written Yorùbá

    Explicit ordinal labels such as ẹni akọ́kọ́ and ẹni kejì are especially common in formal written Yorùbá across regions, including reports, profiles, and academic writing, precisely because they remove ambiguity that spoken conversation can often resolve through gesture, shared context, or immediate clarification.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Ambiguous pronoun after introducing two people

    Error: Introducing Adé and Ẹ̀gbọ́n Fúnmi in one sentence, then writing "ó ṣe é" ("he/she did it") in the next sentence without making clear which person is meant.

    Correction: Replace the bare pronoun with a name, a relative clause, or an explicit label such as ẹni akọ́kọ́ or ẹni náà until the reference is unmistakably clear.

    Common Mistake — Overusing strong pronouns as ordinary subjects

    Error: Using òun as a default subject pronoun in every sentence, even where no contrast or emphasis is intended.

    Correction: Reserve òun and àwọn for moments of genuine contrast or emphasis, and use ordinary weak subject pronouns or explicit reference labels elsewhere.

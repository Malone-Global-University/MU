Àtúnṣe Ohùn, Àkọtọ́, àti Ọ̀nà Kíkọ

Editing for Tone, Orthography, and Style · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Professional, Academic, Civic, and Cross-Regional Yorùbá

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Apply a full grammar and style editing checklist to a long, advanced Yorùbá text.
  2. Diagnose an error by matrix category — tone, orthography, particle, pronoun/reference, serialization, word order, or register — rather than correcting by instinct alone.
  3. Distinguish a tone error from a dot-under spelling error, even when both affect the same syllable.
  4. Identify and repair ambiguous or overloaded reference chains across several sentences.
  5. Recognize when a sentence is grammatically correct but stylistically inconsistent with formal register.
  6. Explain a revision decision clearly, citing the matrix category the error belongs to.
  7. Maintain the Standard Written Yorùbá production baseline and full diacritics while editing and while explaining edits.
  8. Edit a long text and produce a written explanation of the revisions made.

Opening Communication Scenario

Adé has been asked to proofread a colleague's draft report before it is submitted to the community education committee that heard the Chapter 17 presentation. The draft is competent but uneven: some sentences drop tone marks under time pressure, one paragraph loses track of who "they" refers to, and one section slides into a casual register that does not match the rest of the report. Adé must find and name each problem precisely, not just make the text "sound better," so the colleague can learn from the correction.

  1. This chapter treats editing as a diagnostic skill: naming what kind of error occurred, not only fixing it.
  2. No major new independent clause type is introduced. The chapter consolidates a full editing checklist across every matrix category active in this volume.
  3. By the end of the chapter, you will edit a long text and explain your revisions by matrix category.

Pronunciation Focus — Reading Aloud for Proofreading

Focus — Hearing tone errors by reading a draft aloud

This chapter's pronunciation focus is using reading aloud as a proofreading tool. A tone error that the eye skips over on a page is often audible immediately when the sentence is spoken naturally, because the wrong tone produces the wrong word or a wrong-sounding phrase.

  • Read each sentence aloud at ordinary speaking speed before checking it silently; a flattened or misplaced tone often reveals itself in the mouth before it is caught by the eye.
  • When a sentence "sounds wrong" but the grammar looks correct on paper, suspect a tone or dot-under spelling issue rather than a word-choice issue.
  • Read a corrected sentence aloud a second time after editing to confirm the revision actually resolves the ambiguity or awkwardness, rather than only looking correct in writing.

Grammar and the Editing Checklist

This chapter does not introduce a new independent clause type. It consolidates every language-system matrix active in this volume into a single editing checklist, and it develops the discourse skill of diagnosing an error by matrix category before revising it.

The Full Editing Checklist

Checklist categories

Tone → Orthography → Particles → Pronouns/Reference → Serialization → Word Order/Clause → Register/Style

Working through categories in a fixed order prevents an editor from fixing an obvious surface error while missing a subtler structural one underneath it. Each category corresponds directly to a Part III matrix from the governing scope.

Distinguishing a Tone Error from an Orthography Error

Draft

Ó wá sí ilé ìwé láti ko àwọn ọmọ.

Revised

Ó wá sí ilé ìwé láti kọ́ àwọn ọmọ.

Here the draft's ko lacks both the mid-to-high tone shift and the dot-under vowel needed for "to teach"; without correction it reads as an unrelated or incomplete form. Tone Matrix Orthography Matrix — this is a combined tone-plus-dot error, not a grammar error, since the sentence's particle placement and word order are already correct.

Identifying a Reference Chain Error

  • Olùkọ́ náà bá akẹ́kọ̀ọ́ méjì sọ̀rọ̀. Wọ́n sọ pé òun kò mọ ìdáhùn náà. The teacher spoke with two students. They said that they/he/she did not know the answer. ambiguous pronoun — unclear whether "òun" refers to the teacher or a student
  • Olùkọ́ náà bá akẹ́kọ̀ọ́ méjì sọ̀rọ̀. Àwọn akẹ́kọ̀ọ́ náà sọ pé àwọn kò mọ ìdáhùn náà. The teacher spoke with two students. The students said that they did not know the answer. repaired — the noun phrase is restated instead of relying on an ambiguous pronoun

This is a Pronoun/Reference Matrix error. The grammar of each individual sentence is correct; the problem is that the pronoun does not clearly point back to one participant once a second potential referent has been introduced.

Identifying a Register Error

Draft — mid-report

Ẹ jọ̀wọ́ ẹ ṣàkíyèsí pé kì í ṣe ọ̀nà kan ṣoṣo ni àwọn ọ̀dọ́ ń gbà kọ́ èdè náà. Àmọ́ sá, ó máa ń wá bá wọn lọ́nà tó yàtọ̀.

The second sentence shifts into a colloquial connector and particle usage that does not match the formal register of the first.

Revised

Ẹ jọ̀wọ́ ẹ ṣàkíyèsí pé kì í ṣe ọ̀nà kan ṣoṣo ni àwọn ọ̀dọ́ ń gbà kọ́ èdè náà. Ṣùgbọ́n, ó máa ń dé ọ̀dọ̀ wọn ní ọ̀nà tí ó yàtọ̀ síra.

Both versions of the second sentence are grammatically well-formed. This is a Register/Style issue: the informal connector and phrasing are appropriate in conversation but inconsistent with the formal report register established in the surrounding paragraph.

Recognition note — Editing as diagnosis, not instinct

A skilled editor names the category of an error before fixing it. "This sounds wrong" is a starting point, not an explanation. Naming the category — tone, orthography, particle, reference, serialization, word order, or register — is what allows a writer to learn from the correction rather than simply receiving a different sentence.

Model Dialogue

Ipò: Adé ń ṣàyẹ̀wò àkọsílẹ̀ Kúnlé kí ó tó fi ránṣẹ́ sí ìgbìmọ̀ àgbègbè.
Setting: Adé is reviewing Kúnlé's draft report before it is sent to the community committee.

KÚNLÉ Ǹjẹ́ o ti wo àkọsílẹ̀ mi? Kí ni o rí nínú rẹ̀? Have you looked at my draft? What did you find in it?
ADÉ Bẹ́ẹ̀ ni, ẹ jẹ́ kí n ṣàyẹ̀wò rẹ̀ padà pẹ̀lú rẹ ní báyìí. Ní ìpínrọ̀ kejì, o kọ "ko" nínú "láti ko àwọn ọmọ", àmọ́ ohun tí o ní lọ́kàn ni "kọ́", pẹ̀lú àmì ohùn àti àmì ojú. Yes, let me review it with you now. In the second paragraph, you wrote "ko" in "to ko the children," but what you meant was "kọ́," with the tone mark and the dot.
KÚNLÉ Ah, mo rí i báyìí. Kí ni mìíràn? Ah, I see it now. What else is there?
ADÉ Ní ìpínrọ̀ kẹta, o sọ nípa olùkọ́ àti akẹ́kọ̀ọ́ méjì, o sì lo "òun" lẹ́yìn náà; a kò lè mọ̀ dájú bóyá olùkọ́ ni tàbí ọ̀kan lára àwọn akẹ́kọ̀ọ́. Ó dára láti tún orúkọ wọn sọ dípò lílo arọ́pò orúkọ tí kò ṣe kedere. In the third paragraph, you mention the teacher and two students, and then you use "he/she"; we cannot be sure whether it's the teacher or one of the students. It's better to restate their names instead of using an unclear pronoun.
KÚNLÉ Ó yé mi. Ǹjẹ́ ìṣòro kan sí wà nípa ọ̀nà tí mo fi kọ̀wé rẹ̀? I understand. Is there also a problem with the style I wrote it in?
ADÉ Bẹ́ẹ̀ ni. Ní ìpínrọ̀ kẹrin, gbólóhùn kan yí padà sí ọ̀rọ̀ àsọyé lásán tí kò bára mu pẹ̀lú ìyókù àkọsílẹ̀ náà, tí ó jẹ́ ti ìwé àṣẹ. Mo dábàá kí a mú un padà bọ̀ sí ọ̀nà kíkọ tí ó bára mu. Yes. In the fourth paragraph, one sentence shifts into ordinary casual speech that doesn't match the rest of the report, which is a formal document. I suggest we bring it back into a consistent style.
KÚNLÉ Ó dára, mo fẹ́ ṣe àtúnṣe wọ̀nyí kí n tó fi ránṣẹ́ sí ìgbìmọ̀ náà. Alright, I want to make these revisions before I send it to the committee.

    • Every vowel that should carry a high or low tone mark has one, and ẹ, ọ, and ṣ are distinguished throughout.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Editing as a collaborative, respectful act

    In many Yorùbá-speaking professional and academic settings, offering a correction to a colleague's writing is approached carefully, often paired with acknowledgment of what the draft already does well. The explanation frame in this chapter models that balance: precise about the error, respectful toward the writer.

    Regional Note — Variation in formal editing conventions

    Institutions differ in how strictly they apply full diacritics in internal drafts versus final published documents; some accept lightly marked internal drafts if the final version is fully corrected. This chapter's checklist applies to the final formal version regardless of internal drafting habits.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Correcting a sentence without naming the error type

    Error: Silently rewriting a sentence to sound better without identifying whether the underlying problem was tone, orthography, reference, or register.

    Correction: Use the explanation frame ìdí tí mo fi ṣe àtúnṣe yìí ni pé... and name the specific matrix category for every revision.

    Common Mistake — Treating a register shift as acceptable because the grammar is correct

    Error: Leaving a casual phrase or connector inside an otherwise formal report because each individual sentence is grammatically valid on its own.

    Correction: Check register consistency across the whole text, not sentence by sentence in isolation, using ó bára mu as the test question for each paragraph.

    Àtúnṣe Àkọsílẹ̀ Gígùn kan — Editing a Long Text

    Edit a long text entirely grounded in Standard Written Yorùbá and produce a clear explanation of your revisions.

    • 150–220 words, fully corrected, with full tone marks and dot-under letters throughout.

    Answer Key

    Listening Practice Questions

    1. The first error is a missing or incorrect tone mark on a word that completely changes its meaning; this is a tone-category error.
    2. The second error is an unclear pronoun reference in three sentences in a row, which prevents readers from knowing who is being discussed; this is a reference-category error.

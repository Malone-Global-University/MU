Community Interaction Project · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Move through a realistic day of Yorùbá community interaction — greetings, errands, market and service tasks, and family communication — using systems active since Chapter 1.
  2. Integrate tone, orthography, SVO word order, subject and object pronouns, aspect and tense particles (ń, ti, yóò), negation (kò, má), noun phrases, and courtesy formulas within a single connected sequence of tasks.
  3. Monitor and repair your own pronunciation and tone as you speak, without relying on a partner to catch every error.
  4. Read and respond to a multi-part community text that mixes greetings, errands, schedules, and messages.
  5. Write a connected account of a full day's community interactions.
  6. Complete a multi-step speaking and writing project that draws on vocabulary and grammar from every chapter of Volume I.

Communication Scenario

Adé has now arrived to visit Yẹmi, as planned at the end of Chapter 18. This chapter follows Adé through one full day in Yẹmi's community: a morning of greetings and a small errand, an afternoon of market and service tasks, and an evening of family conversation and messages. No new grammar is introduced in this chapter. Instead, every system you have built since Chapter 1 — tone, diacritics, word order, pronouns, particles, negation, noun phrases, courtesy, and formulaic serialization — comes together in one connected project.

  1. Before you begin, skim the Core Vocabulary Review below and notice how many of the words are already familiar to you from earlier chapters.
  2. As you move through the day's three stages, keep track of which particle (ń, ti, or yóò) each sentence uses and why.
  3. This chapter is also a rehearsal for the Chapter 20 capstone. Treat every task here as practice for demonstrating what you can do with Volume I Yorùbá.

Pronunciation Self-Monitoring

Chapter 19 does not introduce a new sound or spelling target. Instead, it asks you to monitor and repair your own pronunciation — a skill you will need for independent, real-world communication.

Pronunciation Focus — Self-monitoring checklist

As you read each stage of the project aloud, pause after every sentence and check the following, in order:

  1. Did I keep ẹ, ọ, and ṣ distinct from e, o, and s?
  2. Did I mark high and low tone clearly, and leave mid tone unmarked but level?
  3. Did I keep the particle's tone separate from the verb's tone (as in ti parí)?
  4. Did I avoid inserting an English-style consonant cluster into a Yorùbá word?
  5. Did I keep gb as a single sound rather than two English letters?

If you catch an error, stop, correct it, and repeat the sentence before continuing. This repair habit matters more than perfect accuracy on the first attempt.

Stage 1 — Àárọ̀: Greetings and an Errand

Morning

Adé greets Yẹmi's neighbors and runs a small errand

greetings (Ch. 1) subject pronouns (Ch. 2) locatives with ní (Ch. 5) questions (Ch. 3–5)

Yẹmi's neighbor, Bàbá Tùndé, greets Adé outside the house. Adé responds with the extended greeting sequence active since Chapter 1, then asks a simple location question to find the nearest shop.

  • Bàbá Tùndé: Ẹ káàárọ̀, ọmọ mi. Ṣé dáadáa ni? Good morning, my child. Is all well?
  • Adé: Ẹ káàárọ̀, sà. Dáadáa ni, ẹ ṣé. Good morning, sir. All is well, thank you.
  • Adé: Ǹjẹ́ ilé ìtajà wà ní tòsí ibí? Q + shop + be.located + at + near + here Is there a shop nearby?
Grammar Note — No new system, only integration

Every form in Stage 1 was already active before this chapter: the greeting sequence and respect address from Chapter 1, subject pronouns from Chapter 2, the locative verb wà with ní from Chapter 5, and yes/no questions with ǹjẹ́/ṣé from Chapter 3. Stage 1 simply asks you to combine them fluently in one short exchange.

Stage 2 — Ọ̀sán: Market and Service Tasks

Afternoon

Adé buys food at the market and stops at a service window

market courtesy (Ch. 7) progressive ń (Ch. 6, 8) negation with kò (Ch. 7–9) plural àwọn (Ch. 4)

At the market, Adé asks a price, negotiates politely, and negates a preference. At the service window, Adé explains that a document is not yet ready, using the progressive and negative forms active since earlier chapters.

  • Adé: Ẹ ṣé, ẹ̀wo ni owó ẹ̀fọ́ yìí? Excuse me, what is the price of these vegetables?
  • Oníṣòwò: Ọgọ́rùn-ún náírà ni. It is one hundred naira.
  • Adé: Mi ò fẹ́ èyí; mo fẹ́ àwọn tí ó wà lápá ọ̀tún yẹn. I + NEG + want + this; I + want + PL + REL + be.located + right side + that I don't want this one; I want the ones on that right side.
  • Adé: Ǹjẹ́ ìwé mi ti ṣetán? Is my document ready yet?
  • Òṣìṣẹ́: Rárá o, a ń ṣe é báyìí; kò tíì parí. no; we + PROG + do + it + now; NEG + finished Not yet; we are working on it now; it is not finished.
Grammar Note — Three particles doing familiar work

ń ṣe é (ongoing, Ch. 6/8), kò tíì parí (negative + tíì, recognized receptively since Ch. 18), and ti ṣetán (completed/resultative, Ch. 11/18) all appear in this single short exchange. No form here goes beyond what earlier chapters authorized for production; kò tíì remains recognition-only, exactly as it was introduced in Chapter 18.

Stage 3 — Ìrọ̀lẹ́: Family and Messages

Evening

Adé talks with Yẹmi's family and sends a message home

kinship terms (Ch. 3) object pronouns (Ch. 17) future yóò (Ch. 9) messaging register (Ch. 17)

In the evening, Adé sits with Yẹmi's family, answers questions about his own family, and sends a short text message home reporting on the day and stating tomorrow's plan.

  • Ìyá Yẹmi: Ẹbí rẹ ń ṣe kí? How is your family doing?
  • Adé: Wọ́n wà dáadáa, ẹ ṣé. Màmá mi ti béèrè nípa yín gan-an. They are well, thank you. My mother has asked about you a lot.
Ìránṣẹ́ sí ilé — Message home
Mo ti dé láfòyà, mo sì ti rí ẹbí Yẹmi. Wọ́n tọ́jú mi dáadáa. Ní ọ̀la, a ó lọ sí ọjà pọ̀, a ó sì kí àwọn ẹbí mìíràn. I have arrived safely, and I have met Yẹmi's family. They are taking good care of me. Tomorrow, we will go to the market together, and we will greet other family members.
Grammar Note — Reference and courtesy across a full evening

The message combines ti for what has already happened, yóò/á/n ó for tomorrow's plan, and the plural/respect address yín from Chapter 3, all inside the short, practical text-message register established in Chapter 17.

Writing Workshop

Chapter 19's writing product is a connected account of a full day of community interaction, modeled on Adé's journal entry. It must use Standard Yorùbá orthography with full tone marks and underdots and must draw only on systems already active in Volume I.

Ọjọ́ Mi Nínú Àwùjọ — My Day in the Community

Write a connected account (80–110 words) of a day that includes at least three of the following: a greeting exchange, an errand, a market or service task, and a family or messaging moment. Your writing must include:

  • At least one extended greeting exchange, using respect address where appropriate.
  • At least one sentence each using ń, ti, and yóò/á/n ó.
  • At least one negative sentence with kò.
  • At least one noun phrase with a plural marker, demonstrative, or possessive.
  • At least one courtesy formula (thanks, request, or apology).
  1. Choose your day. Base it on a real day or invent a realistic one, following Adé's model.
  2. Open with a greeting. Set the scene with a time-of-day greeting and a respectful exchange.
  3. Add an errand or task. Describe something you did, are doing, or will do, using the appropriate particle.
  4. Include a negative statement. Use kò naturally within the account.
  5. Close with a plan or message. End with a future statement or a short message to someone else.
  6. Apply the editing checklist. Verify every tone mark, underdot, and particle choice before submitting.

Àtúnṣe àtúnyẹ̀wò — Editing checklist:

  • The greeting exchange matches the time of day and the relationship between speakers.
  • ń, ti, and yóò/á/n ó are each used at least once and match their intended time meaning.
  • kò is placed correctly before the verb it negates.
  • At least one noun phrase correctly orders noun, modifier, and any plural marker or demonstrative.
  • No form appears that has not been activated somewhere in Chapters 1–18.
  • Every ẹ, ọ, and ṣ carries its underdot.
  • High and low tones are marked on every required syllable. Mid tones are left unmarked.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Greeting every stage of the day

Many Yorùbá-speaking communities use distinct greetings for morning, afternoon, and evening, and greeting a neighbor or elder before beginning an errand is often expected rather than optional. Skipping this step, even for a quick task, can come across as abrupt.

Culture in Context — Patience with service delays

The exchange at the government office reflects a common real-world pattern: a task is genuinely in progress but not yet finished, and the appropriate response is polite patience rather than frustration. Expressions like a ń ṣe é báyìí ("we are working on it now") are common, courteous ways of managing such moments.

Regional Variation — Market bargaining style

The polite refusal-and-redirection pattern in the market scene (mi ò fẹ́ èyí; mo fẹ́ àwọn tí...) is one common style. Bargaining tone, pacing, and directness vary by region and by relationship between buyer and seller; this course models one broadly usable, courteous approach.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Skipping the greeting before a task

Error: Beginning an errand or request with *Ẹ̀wo ni owó ẹ̀fọ́ yìí? alone, with no greeting or courtesy opener.

Correction: Ẹ ṣé, ẹ̀wo ni owó ẹ̀fọ́ yìí? — open transactional and service exchanges with a courtesy formula, as modeled throughout this chapter.

Common Mistake — Overusing one particle across the whole day

Error: Writing an entire day's account using only ti, even for events still in progress or still to come.

Correction: Match each clause's particle to its real time meaning, as in Grammar 18.2: ti for completed/resultative, ń for ongoing, yóò/á/n ó for future.

Common Mistake — Producing kò tíì independently

Error: Attempting to freely construct new sentences with kò tíì beyond the fixed phrases modeled in Chapters 18–19.

Correction: Recognize kò tíì parí and similar forms when you meet them, but rely on simple kò negation for your own independent production, as scheduled by the Version 5 matrices.

You can move through a full day of realistic Yorùbá community interaction, combining greetings, errands, market and service tasks, and family communication. You can integrate tone, orthography, word order, pronouns, particles, negation, and noun phrases in a single connected sequence, and you can monitor and repair your own pronunciation as you speak and write.

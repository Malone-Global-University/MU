Ní Kíláàsì

In the Classroom · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify common classroom objects by name and point to them using near and far demonstratives.
  2. Ask what an object is and answer using the identification pattern with ni.
  3. Distinguish one object from several using àwọn.
  4. Ask for help, repetition, and clarification in the classroom.
  5. Follow and give common classroom instructions.
  6. Recognize that Yorùbá nouns have no grammatical gender and do not ordinarily change form to mark plural.
  7. Count from 1 to 20 in Yorùbá.
  8. Recognize and produce the seven oral vowel qualities and begin distinguishing e from ẹ and o from ọ.
  9. Write classroom vocabulary with correct tone marks and underdots.

Communication Scenario

It is your first Yorùbá lesson. The teacher has arranged classroom objects on a table — books, pens, chairs, notebooks, and a board. She holds up each one and asks the class to name it. You do not yet know all the words, but you have just enough language to ask when you are lost, say when you understand, and point to what you mean. By the end of this chapter, you will be able to navigate a Yorùbá classroom confidently.

  1. What phrases would you need most urgently in a language class where you barely understand the teacher? Write three in English. You will learn the Yorùbá equivalents today.
  2. Look at the objects around you right now. How many can you already name in any language? Notice which ones you reach for first — those are often the highest-frequency words.

Important note on numbers 11–20: Yorùbá number formation is distinctive. Numbers 11–15 are expressed as "unit + lé lógún" (added onto twenty), while 16–19 count downward from twenty: ẹ̀rìndílógún means "four subtracted from twenty" (16). This subtractive pattern is characteristic of Yorùbá and should not be replaced with a simpler addition-only model. The full traditional numeral system is complex; only this essential range is introduced here as a productive requirement. Larger numbers will be staged across later chapters.

Pronunciation and Orthography

The seven oral vowels of Yorùbá

Yorùbá has seven distinct oral vowel sounds. This is more than English typically uses, and the differences between them carry meaning. You must learn to hear and produce all seven. The table below introduces each vowel with its standard Yorùbá letter, a description, and a sample word from this chapter's vocabulary.

LetterQualityApproximate guideExample word
aopen centralLike a in father, never like a in catàga (chair)
emid front, closeLike e in they without the glide — a pure, steady vowelfèrèsé (window)
ẹmid front, openLike e in bed — more open than the letter abovepẹ̀ńsù (pencil)
ihigh frontLike ee in feet — pure and held steadyìwé (book)
omid back, closeLike o in go without the w-glide — pure and steadydòòmù (door)
ọmid back, openLike aw in law — more open than the letter aboveọ̀wọ̀ (ruler)
uhigh backLike oo in food — lips rounded, no glidepẹ̀ńsù (pencil)
Sound Focus — e versus ẹ, and o versus ọ

The contrast between e and ẹ, and between o and ọ, is one of the most important distinctions in Standard Yorùbá. The underdot is part of the letter — it signals a different sound, not a decorated version of the same sound.

  • e (as in they) is a closer, higher vowel. Example: fèrèsé.
  • ẹ (as in bed) is a more open vowel. Example: pẹ̀ńsù, ìgbáfọ̀.
  • o (as in go) is a closer, higher vowel. Example: dòòmù.
  • ọ (as in law) is a more open vowel. Example: ọ̀wọ̀, àpótí.

In speech, producing the wrong vowel can change the meaning of a word entirely. In writing, omitting the underdot is an orthographic error, even in quick notes. Practice these contrasts every day until they become automatic.

Two special consonants: ṣ, p, and gb

Three consonant sounds in Standard Yorùbá require special attention from English speakers.

ṣ is a retroflex or palato-alveolar sound, similar to the English sh in shoe. It is not the same as ordinary English s. In classroom vocabulary it appears in kíláàsì and in many other high-frequency words. The underdot on ṣ, like the underdot on ẹ and ọ, marks a distinct letter and must always be written.

p in Yorùbá is a labial-velar consonant: both the lips and the back of the tongue close simultaneously. It sounds unlike the ordinary English p. English speakers sometimes mishear it as a combination of English kp. It appears in pátákó (board) and pẹ̀ńsù (pencil).

gb is a single coordinated consonant in which both the bilabial stop and the velar stop are released together. Think of it not as two separate sounds (g + b) but as one simultaneous articulation. It does not appear in this chapter's core vocabulary but is introduced here because it will appear in vocabulary throughout the course.

Pronunciation Practice — Tone marks on underdotted vowels

When a tone mark appears on a vowel that also carries an underdot, both marks must be written. Common combinations from this chapter:

  • ẹ̀ — low tone on the open-e vowel (as in pẹ̀ńsù)
  • ẹ́ — high tone on the open-e vowel
  • ọ̀ — low tone on the open-o vowel (as in ọ̀wọ̀)
  • ọ́ — high tone on the open-o vowel (as in àpótí)

Practice writing these combinations. Say each one aloud as you write it: the tone mark tells you the pitch, and the underdot tells you the vowel quality. Neither can be omitted.

Grammar Instruction

2.1 Nouns without grammatical gender

Purpose: understand that Yorùbá nouns do not carry the masculine or feminine categories that French, Spanish, or German nouns do.

In Yorùbá, a noun such as ìwé (book) has no inherent gender. There is no equivalent of the French le or la, and there is no need to learn a separate article for each noun. This removes one significant source of difficulty for English speakers learning gendered European languages.

Because pronouns in Yorùbá also do not grammatically distinguish "he" and "she" in the third person (a pattern introduced in Chapter 1), Yorùbá simply does not build gender into its noun or pronoun system the way many European languages do.

Grammar Note — No articles either

Yorùbá also has no words that function exactly like the English articles a and the. A noun by itself — ìwé — can mean "a book," "the book," or "books," depending on context. Do not try to add an equivalent of "the" or "a" before every Yorùbá noun you write. Context, demonstratives, and pronouns supply the specificity that English articles provide.

2.2 Plural reference with àwọn

Purpose: mark plural reference without inflecting the noun itself.

Yorùbá nouns do not change their form to become plural. A single form — ìwé — can refer to one book or several books. When you need to make plurality explicit, the most common strategy at this stage is to place àwọn before the noun.

  • ìwé a book / the book / books (context determines)
  • àwọn ìwé the books / books (explicitly plural)
  • àga a chair / the chair / chairs
  • àwọn àga the chairs / chairs (explicitly plural)
Grammar Note — àwọn is not always required

àwọn marks plurality explicitly, but it is not required every time English would use a plural noun. When context already makes plurality clear — through a number, a quantifier, or shared understanding — a bare noun is natural. Do not insert àwọn before every noun that translates as plural in English. Use it when you genuinely need to signal or emphasize that you are referring to more than one item.

2.3 Near and far demonstratives

Purpose: point to objects nearby or at a distance.

Yorùbá uses two common demonstratives at this level:

DemonstrativeMeaningUse
èyíthis (near)An object close to the speaker
ìyẹnthat (far)An object away from the speaker

For plural demonstratives at this stage, use àwọn + noun + (near or far reference). Full demonstrative agreement will be developed in Chapter 6. For now, practice èyí and ìyẹn in identification questions and answers:

  • Kí ni èyí? What is this?
  • Èyí ni ìwé. This is a book.
  • Kí ni ìyẹn? What is that?
  • Ìyẹn ni pátákó. That is the board.

2.4 Identification with ni — review and classroom use

Purpose: name objects using the identification construction introduced in Chapter 1.

In Chapter 1 you learned the personal identification pattern Orúkọ mi ni… (My name is…). The same particle ni — with mid tone — appears in object identification. The basic pattern for answering "What is this?" is:

  • Èyí ni àga. this + ni + chair This is a chair.
  • Ìyẹn ni pẹ́ńsẹ́lì. that + ni + pen That is a pen.
  • Àwọn ìyẹn ni àwọn ìwé. Those are books.
Grammar Note — ni with mid tone

The identification and focus particle used in this chapter is ni with mid tone — it carries no tone mark in standard orthography because mid tone is normally unmarked. Do not confuse this with high-tone ní, which signals possession and prepositional uses introduced in Chapter 3. This distinction — mid-tone ni versus high-tone ní — will be one of the most important contrasts in the course. For now, learn the identification use thoroughly before the contrast is introduced.

2.5 Noun-qualifier order — color after noun

Purpose: place color words and other qualifiers in the natural Yorùbá position: after the noun they describe.

In English, adjectives generally come before the noun: a red book. In Yorùbá, many qualifiers — including color words — follow the noun they describe:

  • ìwé pupa book + red a red book
  • àga funfun chair + white a white chair
  • pẹ̀ńsù dúdú pencil + black a black pencil
  • àwọn ìwé búlúù àwọn + book + blue the blue books

This noun-first pattern is a fundamental feature of Yorùbá noun-phrase structure. Resisting the English habit of placing the adjective before the noun is one of the most important adjustments for English speakers. The full order of multiple qualifiers will be developed across Chapters 4 and 6.

Language Focus

Yorùbá nouns carry no grammatical gender, and they do not change form to become plural. This is fundamentally different from French, Spanish, German, Arabic, or many other languages a learner may have encountered. In those languages, you must memorize whether a noun is masculine or feminine and produce a different form for singular and plural. In Yorùbá, the noun ìwé is always ìwé, whether you are talking about one book or twenty. Plurality is signaled by context, by numbers, by àwọn, or by pronouns — never by changing the noun itself.

A predictable trap: English speakers sometimes add an English-style -s to make a Yorùbá noun plural, or try to invent a separate plural form by analogy. Neither works. Learn the noun once in its single, invariant form, and let àwọn or context do the work of marking plurality when it matters.

Model Dialogue

Ipò: Kíláàsì Yorùbá àkọ́kọ́. Olùkọ́ Àdùnọlá n ṣe iṣẹ́ pẹ̀lú akẹ́kọ̀ọ́ mẹta: Seun, Yemi, àti Kémi. Èdè: Standard Yorùbá tí a ń kọ́, ìpele àkọ́bẹ̀rẹ̀.
Setting: A first Yorùbá class. Teacher Àdùnọlá is working with three students: Seun, Yemi, and Kémi. Language: Standard Yorùbá at beginner level.

ÀDÙNỌLÁ Ẹ káàárọ̀, àwọn akẹ́kọ̀ọ́. Good morning, students.
ÀWỌN Káàárọ̀, olùkọ́. Good morning, teacher.
ÀDÙNỌLÁ Jókòó. Ẹ jọ̀wọ́, ṣíi àwọn ìwé yín. Sit down. Please open your books.

(Àdùnọlá gba ìwé kan dé wó — Àdùnọlá picks up a book.)

ÀDÙNỌLÁ Kí ni èyí? What is this?
SEUN Èyí ni ìwé. This is a book.
ÀDÙNỌLÁ Ìdí nì. Dáadáa. Àwọn ìwé mélòó ni ó wà níbí? That's right. Good. How many books are here?
YEMI Àwọn ìwé mẹ́ta. Three books.
ÀDÙNỌLÁ Dáadáa. Kémi, kí ni ìyẹn? Good. Kémi, what is that?
KÉMI Ẹ jọ̀wọ́, tún un sọ. Please say it again.
ÀDÙNỌLÁ Kí ni ìyẹn? Kí ni orúkọ rẹ̀ ní Yorùbá? What is that? What is its name in Yorùbá?
KÉMI Ìyẹn ni… ìgbáfọ̀? That is… an eraser?
ÀDÙNỌLÁ Ìdí nì. Dáadáa, Kémi! Ìyẹn ni ìgbáfọ̀ funfun. That's right. Well done, Kémi! That is a white eraser.

(Àdùnọlá tọ́ka sí pátákó — Àdùnọlá points to the board.)

ÀDÙNỌLÁ Seun, kí ni orúkọ èyí ní Yorùbá? Seun, what is the name of this in Yorùbá?
SEUN Ìgbáfọ̀? Rárá — jọ̀wọ́, sọ ó di èlò. An eraser? No — please speak more slowly.
ÀDÙNỌLÁ Pá-tá-kó. Board. (spoken syllable by syllable)
SEUN Pátákó! Mo gbọ́. Board! I understand.
ÀDÙNỌLÁ Ìdí nì. Dáadáa, gbogbo yín. That's right. Well done, all of you.

Àwọn ìbéèrè nípa ọ̀rọ̀ àdáhùnṣe — Comprehension questions:

  1. Kí ni Yemi sọ nígbà tí olùkọ́ béèrè nípa àwọn ìwé? (What did Yemi say when the teacher asked about the books?)
  2. Kémi mọ̀ orúkọ nǹkan tí olùkọ́ tọ́ka sí bí? (Did Kémi know the name of the object the teacher pointed to?)
  3. Irú ohun tí Seun béèrè fún olùkọ́ ni kí? (What did Seun ask the teacher for?)
  4. Àwọ̀ ìgbáfọ̀ tí a sọ̀rọ̀ rẹ̀ ní kíláàsì ni kí? (What color was the eraser mentioned in class?)
  5. Tọ́ka sí ọ̀rọ̀ àdáhùnṣe kan níbí tí a fi àwọn ṣàpèjúwe ọ̀pọ̀lọpọ̀. (Find one place in the dialogue where àwọn marks plurality.)
Grammar Note — One expression to notice

In the dialogue, the teacher asks: Àwọn ìwé mélòó ni ó wà níbí? (How many books are here?) This sentence contains wà ní, a location construction taught formally in Chapter 3, and mélòó, a number question word beyond the current chapter scope. Treat this line as a formulaic expression and recognize its meaning from context. You are not yet expected to build this sentence type independently.

Writing Workshop

In this chapter you will practice labeling objects with full tone marks and underdots, then move to writing complete identification sentences about your own classroom or study space.

  1. Labeling practice. Draw or imagine five objects in a classroom. Write the fully marked Yorùbá label for each one. Include the tone marks on every vowel that carries high or low tone, and the underdot on ẹ, ọ, and ṣ wherever they appear.
  2. Identification sentences. For each labeled object, write a complete sentence using Èyí ni ___ or Ìyẹn ni ___. Vary your choice of near and far demonstrative.
  3. Color description. Choose three of your objects and add a color. Remember to place the color word after the noun: ìwé pupa, not pupa ìwé. Write a sentence for each.
  4. Singular and plural. Take two of your labeled nouns and write both a singular identification sentence and a plural version using àwọn.
  5. Revise. Check your draft against the editing checklist below before finalizing.

Àtúnyẹ̀wò àtúnṣe — Editing checklist:

  • Every high tone is marked with an acute accent (´).
  • Every low tone is marked with a grave accent (`).
  • Vowels with no accent carry mid tone — I did not add an accent to them.
  • Every ẹ, ọ, and ṣ is correctly spelled with an underdot — not replaced by plain e, o, or s.
  • Color words appear after the noun, not before it.
  • Plural phrases use àwọn + noun — the noun itself did not change form.
  • I used ni (mid tone, no accent) for identification — not a verb.
  • I started each sentence with a capital letter and ended it with a period or question mark.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Address in the Yorùbá classroom

In many Yorùbá-speaking educational settings, the relationship between teacher and student is marked by clear respect conventions. A teacher is typically addressed with an honorific title — commonly olùkọ́ (teacher) — rather than by personal name alone. In some formal contexts, the title Sọ̀fọ́ or a professional title may be used. Students generally use the plural or honorific form ẹ when addressing the teacher, rather than the singular familiar form o.

These conventions vary by institution, region, religious affiliation, and the personal preferences of the teacher. A private language class, a church school, and a government secondary school may each have distinct norms. When entering any new learning environment, pay attention to how other students address the teacher and follow that lead rather than assuming one universal rule applies.

Culture in Context — Classroom language as relationship

The clarification expressions in this chapter — asking for repetition, slowing speech, checking understanding — are not signs of weakness. In Yorùbá-speaking educational contexts, showing that you are actively engaged and willing to clarify is generally seen as positive participation. Asking Ẹ jọ̀wọ́, tún un sọ (Please say it again) or Jọ̀wọ́, sọ ó di èlò (Please speak more slowly) communicates attentiveness, not confusion.

Notice that both of these expressions use jọ̀wọ́ (please), which signals courtesy and respect. Omitting courtesy markers in a request directed at a teacher or elder can read as abrupt. Building polite request frames into your classroom language from the start will serve you throughout the course.

Regional Variation — Loanwords in the classroom

Several words in this chapter's vocabulary — including kíláàsì (classroom) and pẹ́ńsẹ́lì (pen/pencil) — are loanwords adapted from English into Standard Yorùbá. This adaptation is standard practice: Yorùbá has borrowed and phonologically adapted vocabulary from English, Arabic, Portuguese, and other languages for centuries. The adapted forms have verified Yorùbá spellings, tone patterns, and vowel qualities. They are genuine Yorùbá vocabulary, not incomplete English words, and should not be pronounced using English phonology.

In some contexts, speakers use alternative terms for classroom items. The word bóòdù is also heard for "board" alongside pátákó. The course uses pátákó as the productive standard. Where you encounter alternatives, note them as recognition vocabulary rather than mixing them into your core production.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Adding an English-style plural suffix

Error: Writing *ìwés, *àgas, or *olùkọ́s to mean "books," "chairs," or "teachers."

Correction: Yorùbá nouns never add -s or any other suffix to form a plural. The noun is always ìwé, àga, olùkọ́. To signal plurality explicitly, use àwọn before the noun: àwọn ìwé, àwọn àga, àwọn olùkọ́.

Common Mistake — Placing color before the noun

Error: *pupa ìwé (intended meaning: red book).

Correction: In Yorùbá, the color word comes after the noun: ìwé pupa. This noun-first pattern is the opposite of English adjective-before-noun order. Every time you want to say "red book" in Yorùbá, think: noun first, then color.

Common Mistake — Omitting the underdot

Error: Writing *pensù for pẹ̀ńsù, or *owo for ọ̀wọ̀.

Correction: The underdot is part of the letter, not an optional decoration. ẹ and e are two distinct letters representing two distinct sounds. Omitting the underdot is an orthographic error that can change the meaning of a word and will obscure your Yorùbá writing for any reader. Practice writing underdotted letters from the start of this course — they must become automatic.

Common Mistake — Adding àwọn before every plural noun

Error: Treating àwọn as the required Yorùbá equivalent of English -s and inserting it before every noun you want to be plural.

Correction: àwọn marks plurality when you need to make it explicit — for example, to clarify that you mean several things rather than one, or to introduce a group that will be referred to again. When a number or quantifier already establishes plurality (as in ìwé mẹ́ta, three books), or when context makes it clear, a bare noun is often more natural. Observe carefully how àwọn is used in the model dialogue and readings.

Common Mistake — Mispronouncing Yorùbá p as English p

Error: Pronouncing pátákó or pẹ̀ńsù with an ordinary English p sound (a bilabial stop with a small puff of air).

Correction: Yorùbá p is a labial-velar consonant: both the lips and the back of the tongue close simultaneously as one coordinated gesture. It is distinct from English p. Producing an English-style p in Yorùbá words may cause comprehension difficulties. Listen carefully to model audio or a qualified speaker, and practice the articulation separately before using these words in conversation.

Chapter Performance Task

Iṣẹ́ aṣeyọrí

Àsọdúnkẹ́ kíláàsì — Classroom information gap

Working with a partner, complete a classroom identification task in three parts:

  1. Oral identification: Your partner places five objects on a table (or describes five objects in an imagined classroom). Without looking, ask about each object using Kí ni èyí? or Kí ni ìyẹn?. Your partner answers. Use Ẹ jọ̀wọ́, tún un sọ if needed.
  2. Numbers and colors: Your partner asks how many of each object there are (mélòó?) and what color they are (àwọ̀ kí?). You answer using a Yorùbá number and a color word, in the correct noun-phrase order.
  3. Clarification and commands: One partner gives three classroom commands from the list in the vocabulary section. The other partner performs the action and confirms: Mo gbọ́ or asks Ẹ jọ̀wọ́, tún un sọ if needed.

Written component: After the oral task, write a five- to seven-sentence description of the objects used in the activity. Use àwọn for plural reference in at least two sentences, include a color in at least two sentences, and use at least one number. Submit with full tone marks and underdots.

Ohun tí o lè ṣe báyìí — What you can do now

You can name common classroom objects in Standard Yorùbá with correct tone marks and underdots.

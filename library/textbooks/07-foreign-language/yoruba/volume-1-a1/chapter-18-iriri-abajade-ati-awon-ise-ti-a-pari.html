Ìrírí, Àbájáde, àti Àwọn Iṣẹ́ tí a Parí

Experiences, Results, and Completed Tasks · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Use the perfective particle ti with confidence and independence to describe completed experiences, finished tasks, and present results.
  2. Distinguish clearly among ti (completed/resultative), ń (ongoing), and yóò (future) in connected sentences.
  3. Name common vocabulary for travel experiences, completed tasks, and achievements.
  4. Describe what has been done and what result that action has produced in the present.
  5. Read a short experience note that mixes completed, ongoing, and future time reference.
  6. Write a short account of something you have done, using full tone marks and underdots.

Communication Scenario

A week has passed since Yẹmi's birthday party. Adé, who could not attend, is now planning the trip to see Yẹmi that he mentioned in his message to Dúpé in Chapter 17. Dúpé checks in with Adé about what he has already arranged, what he is still working on, and what he will do once he arrives. Their exchange moves constantly among three time frames — what is already finished, what is happening now, and what is still to come — and Dúpé must keep these distinct so that Adé's plans stay clear. You will follow their exchange, then describe your own recent experiences and achievements.

  1. Before you open this chapter, think of something you completed recently — a trip, a task at work or school, or a personal errand.
  2. Recall the particle ti, introduced formulaically in Chapter 5 and developed since Chapter 11 for narrating yesterday's events. This chapter consolidates ti for independent use and contrasts it directly with ń, active since Chapter 6, and yóò, active since Chapter 9.

Pronunciation and Orthography Focus

Focus 1 — Listening for the particle contrasts

Pronunciation Focus — ti, ń, and yóò are short but meaning-changing

Each particle in this chapter is short, but changing it changes the meaning of the whole sentence. Say each set aloud slowly, then at a natural pace, listening for the particle rather than the verb:

  • Ó ti dé. — He/She has arrived.
  • Ó ń dé. — He/She is arriving (right now).
  • Yóò dé. — He/She will arrive.

Focus 2 — Tone stability in result statements

Pronunciation and Orthography Focus — Keeping tone steady across ti + verb

When ti combines with a verb, both the particle and the verb keep their own tone marks; the tone of one does not erase the tone of the other. Practice reading these pairs aloud, keeping each syllable's tone distinct: ti parí, ti ṣetán, ti rìnrìn àjò.

Focus 3 — Reading full sentences with all three particles

Say each sentence aloud, then identify aloud whether it describes something finished, something ongoing, or something still to come.

  1. Mo ti kọ ìránṣẹ́ náà. — I have written that message. (finished)
  2. Mo ń kọ ìránṣẹ́ mìíràn. — I am writing another message. (ongoing)
  3. N ó kọ ìránṣẹ́ mìíràn lọ́la. — I will write another message tomorrow. (future)

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

18.1 Ti consolidated: completed experiences and current results

Purpose: describe an action as finished, and, where relevant, point to the present result of that finished action.

ti has been active since Chapter 5 as a receptive form and developed since Chapter 11 for narrating single completed events. This chapter consolidates ti for independent, flexible use, including its resultative meaning — a completed action that produces a state still true now.

  • Adé ti dé. Adé + PERF + arrive Adé has arrived. (He is here now, as a result.)
  • Mo ti parí iṣẹ́ mi. I + PERF + finish + work + my I have finished my work. (It is done now.)
  • A ti múra sílẹ̀ fún ìrìnàjò náà. We have prepared for that trip. (We are ready now, as a result.)
Grammar Note — Ti reports a finished action and often implies a present result

Unlike Chapter 11, where ti mainly narrated single events in a past sequence, this chapter emphasizes the connection between the finished action and its result right now. Ó ti dé is not only "he arrived" but also "he is here now." This resultative sense is central to describing achievements and current situations.

18.2 Contrasting ti, ń, and yóò

Purpose: keep completed, ongoing, and future meanings clearly distinct within the same conversation or passage.

ParticleTime meaningExampleGloss
ticompleted, resultativeÓ ti kọ ìwé náà.He/She has written that book/letter.
ńongoing, in progressÓ ń kọ ìwé náà.He/She is writing that book/letter.
yóò / á / n ófutureYóò kọ ìwé náà.He/She will write that book/letter.
  • Adé ti kúrò ní ilé, ó sì ń wakọ̀ lọ sí pápá ọkọ̀ òfurufú. Yóò dé láàrin wákàtí méjì. Adé + PERF + leave + house, he + PROG + drive + to + airport. FUT + arrive + within + hours + two Adé has left home, and he is driving to the airport. He will arrive within two hours.
Grammar Note — One sentence, three time frames

Yorùbá speakers often move through completed, ongoing, and future meanings within the same short passage, exactly as the example above does. Learners should not force every sentence into a single time frame; instead, choose the particle that matches the meaning of each individual clause.

18.3 Result statements with síbẹ̀síbẹ̀

Purpose: contrast a finished action with something that remains unfinished or ongoing.

  • Mo ti rà tikẹ́ẹ̀tì mi, síbẹ̀síbẹ̀, n kò tí ì múra sílẹ̀ pátápátá. I have bought my ticket; even so, I have not fully prepared yet.
Grammar Note — Negative perfective is receptive only in Volume I

The form kò tí ì ("has not yet") appears in this chapter's reading and dialogue for recognition only. You are not required to produce the negative perfective independently in Volume I; simply understand it when you meet it in context.

Language Focus

This chapter's genuinely new productive material is the resultative use of ti and its three-way contrast with ń and yóò. The particle ti itself is not new — it has been active in some form since Chapter 5 — but this chapter asks you to use it independently, alongside ń and yóò, in a single connected passage.

[Subject] + ti/ń/yóò + [Verb] + (Object)

Model Dialogue

Read the exchange carefully. Notice how Dúpé and Adé move among completed, ongoing, and future meanings as they discuss Adé's trip. Comprehension questions follow.

Ipò: Dúpé ń pe Adé lórí fóònù láti bèèrè nípa ìrìnàjò rẹ̀ sí ibi tí Yẹmi ń gbé.
Setting: Dúpé is calling Adé to ask about his trip to where Yẹmi lives.

DÚPÉ Adé, ṣé o ti múra sílẹ̀ fún ìrìnàjò rẹ? Adé, have you prepared for your trip?
ADÉ Mo ti rà tikẹ́ẹ̀tì mi, síbẹ̀síbẹ̀, n kò tí ì kó ẹrù mi. Mo ń ṣe bẹ́ẹ̀ báyìí. I have bought my ticket; even so, I haven't packed my bags yet. I'm doing that now.
DÚPÉ Ó dára. Ìgbà wo ni o ó dé? Good. When will you arrive?
ADÉ Ọkọ̀ òfurufú mi yóò gbéra ní àárọ̀ ọ̀la, n ó sì dé ní ọ̀sán. My plane will take off tomorrow morning, and I will arrive in the afternoon.
DÚPÉ Ǹjẹ́ o ti sọ fún Yẹmi pé o ti rà tikẹ́ẹ̀tì rẹ? Have you told Yẹmi that you've bought your ticket?
ADÉ Bẹ́ẹ̀ni, mo ti sọ fún un lánàá. Inú rẹ̀ dùn gan-an, ó sì ń múra sílẹ̀ láti pàdé mi ní pápá ọkọ̀ òfurufú. Yes, I told her yesterday. She was very happy, and she is preparing to meet me at the airport.
DÚPÉ Àṣeyọrí ni pé ẹ máa pàdé lẹ́yìn ọ̀pọ̀ oṣù! Kí ni o ó ṣe nígbà tí o bá dé? It's a real achievement that you'll be meeting after so many months! What will you do when you arrive?
ADÉ A ó kọ́kọ́ lọ sí ilé oúnjẹ, a ó sì sọ̀rọ̀ nípa gbogbo ìrírí wa láti ìgbà tí a ti pàdé kẹ́yìn. We'll first go to a restaurant, and we'll talk about all our experiences since we last met.
DÚPÉ Ìyẹn dára! Rìnrìn àjò dáadáa, Adé. That's good! Have a good trip, Adé.
Grammar Note — Three time frames across the dialogue
  1. Mo ti rà tikẹ́ẹ̀tì mi. — completed, with a present result (Adé now holds a ticket).
  2. Mo ń ṣe bẹ́ẹ̀ báyìí. — ongoing, happening at the moment of speaking.
  3. Ọkọ̀ òfurufú mi yóò gbéra ní àárọ̀ ọ̀la. — future, scheduled for tomorrow.
  4. ó ń múra sílẹ̀ láti pàdé mi — ongoing, describing Yẹmi's current preparation.

Àwọn ìbéèrè nípa ọ̀rọ̀ àdáhùnṣe — Comprehension questions:

  1. Kí ni Adé ti ṣe fún ìrìnàjò rẹ̀? (What has Adé already done for his trip?)
  2. Kí ni Adé kò tí ì ṣe? (What has Adé not yet done?)
  3. Nígbà wo ni ọkọ̀ òfurufú Adé yóò gbéra? (When will Adé's plane take off?)
  4. Kí ni Adé àti Yẹmi yóò ṣe nígbà tí Adé bá dé? (What will Adé and Yẹmi do when Adé arrives?)

Ìrírí Ìrìnàjò Adé — Adé's Travel Experience

Mo ti rìnrìn àjò lọ sí ibi tí Yẹmi ń gbé, mo sì ti dé láìsí wàhálà kankan. Nígbà tí mo dé, Yẹmi ti dúró de mi ní pápá ọkọ̀ òfurufú, ó sì dùn mí gidigidi láti rí i lẹ́yìn ọ̀pọ̀ oṣù. A ti lọ sí ilé oúnjẹ kan pọ̀, a sì ti sọ̀rọ̀ nípa gbogbo ìrírí wa. Báyìí, a ń múra sílẹ̀ láti lọ sí ilé Yẹmi, a ó sì sinmi níbẹ̀ fún ọjọ́ mẹ́ta. Writing Workshop

Chapter 18's writing product is a short experience note describing something you have done, its present result, and one thing you still plan to do. It must use Standard Yorùbá orthography with full tone marks and underdots.

Ìrírí Mi — My Experience

Write a short account (50–70 words) of a trip, a completed task, or an achievement. Your writing must include:

  • At least two sentences using ti to describe completed actions.
  • At least one sentence showing a present result of a completed action.
  • At least one sentence using ń to describe something ongoing.
  • At least one sentence using yóò, á, or n ó to describe a future plan connected to your experience.
  1. Choose your experience. Decide on a trip, task, or achievement to describe.
  2. State what you have completed. Use ti for the main finished actions.
  3. Show the present result. Add a sentence showing what is true now because of that completed action.
  4. Add something ongoing. Use ń for something still in progress.
  5. Add a future plan. Use yóò, á, or n ó for what comes next.
  6. Apply the editing checklist. Verify every tone mark, underdot, and particle choice before submitting.

Àtúnṣe àtúnyẹ̀wò — Editing checklist:

  • Every completed action uses ti, not ń or a bare verb alone.
  • At least one sentence makes clear what present result follows from a completed action.
  • ń is used only for something genuinely ongoing, not for something already finished.
  • yóò, á, or n ó is used only for something not yet begun.
  • A reader could clearly tell which parts of the account are finished, ongoing, and future.
  • Every ẹ, ọ, and ṣ carries its underdot.
  • High and low tones are marked on every required syllable. Mid tones are left unmarked.

Speaking Practice

Àdáwọlé àkọ́kọ́ — Controlled practice: particle substitution

Working with a partner, Speaker A says a sentence with a bare verb and a time cue in English. Speaker B produces the correct sentence with ti, ń, or yóò. Switch roles after six exchanges each.

Example:

  • Speaker A: parí iṣẹ́ — "already finished"
  • Speaker B: Mo ti parí iṣẹ́. ("I have finished the work.")

Ìrírí àìpẹ́ yìí — A recent experience

Tell a partner about something you have recently completed, what result it has produced, and one thing you are still preparing to do. Your partner should ask at least two follow-up questions using ti, ń, or yóò. Switch roles after each account. If no partner is available, record yourself and check that all three time frames are clearly distinguished.

Fún àwọn tí ń kọ́ nìkọ̀ọ̀kan — Solo alternative: narrated achievement

Record yourself narrating an achievement — real or invented — in one to two minutes of continuous Yorùbá. Include at least three uses of ti, one use of ń, and one use of yóò. Listen back and check:

  • Did you clearly distinguish completed, ongoing, and future actions?
  • Did you state at least one present result of a completed action?
  • Did you keep tone accurate on both the particle and the verb?

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Sharing news of arrival

Announcing safe arrival after a journey is an important courtesy in many Yorùbá-speaking families and communities. A message or call such as mo ti dé ("I have arrived") reassures family and friends and is often expected soon after travel, even for short trips.

Culture in Context — Marking achievements and reunions

A reunion after a long separation, such as Adé and Yẹmi's meeting in this chapter, is often marked with warmth, shared food, and extended conversation, as reflected in a ó kọ́kọ́ lọ sí ilé oúnjẹ ("we'll first go to a restaurant"). Recognizing an achievement or milestone verbally, as Dúpé does with àṣeyọrí ni ("it's an achievement"), is a valued way of honoring someone's effort.

Regional Variation — Future particle forms

This course uses yóò, á, and n ó as controlled future forms depending on the subject pronoun, following the pattern established since Chapter 9. Some speakers favor one form over another in casual speech; all three remain within the productive standard of this course.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using ń for a completed action

Error: Writing *Ó ń dé to mean "he has arrived," when the person is already present and the action is finished.

Correction: Ó ti dé. — use ti, not ń, when the action is complete and its result holds now. Ó ń dé means "he is arriving," describing the arrival as still in progress.

Common Mistake — Dropping ti in a completed-action sentence

Error: Writing *Mo parí iṣẹ́ mi alone to mean "I have finished my work," omitting the particle that signals completion.

Correction: Mo ti parí iṣẹ́ mi. — ti is required to mark the resultative, completed meaning; a bare verb alone does not reliably signal that the action is finished.

Common Mistake — Mixing ti and yóò in the same clause

Error: Writing *Mo ti yóò lọ sí ilé oúnjẹ, combining the completed particle and the future particle in a single clause.

Correction: N ó lọ sí ilé oúnjẹ. — choose one particle per clause according to the intended time meaning; ti and yóò do not combine to form a single tense in this course.

Common Mistake — Omitting the present-result meaning

Error: Translating Ó ti dé only as "he arrived" and missing that it also communicates "he is here now."

Correction: When explaining or glossing ti sentences, note both the completed action and its present result where relevant, as shown in Grammar 18.1.

Performance Task

Iṣẹ́ aṣeyọrí — Abala 18

Ìtàn ìrírí mi tí ó ṣe kedere — A clear personal experience narrative

The Chapter 18 Performance Task has two parts. All spoken and written Yorùbá must use full tone marks and underdots.

Part 1 — Oral narration (1–2 minutes):
Narrate a real or invented experience — a trip, a completed task, or an achievement — describing what you have done, its present result, and what you still plan to do. Your narration must include:

  1. At least three uses of ti to describe completed actions.
  2. At least one clear statement of a present result following from a completed action.

Part 2 — Written experience note (50–70 words):

Assessment note: The spoken task is evaluated on task completion, comprehensibility, correct choice among ti, ń, and yóò, clear expression of a present result, and overall tone accuracy. The written task is evaluated on accuracy of particle choice, clarity of the completed/ongoing/future contrast, and orthographic completeness (underdots and tone marks).

Reading — Comprehension

  1. Bẹ́ẹ̀ni, ó dé láìsí wàhálà. (Yes, he arrived without trouble.)
  2. Wọ́n ti lọ sí ilé oúnjẹ kan, wọ́n sì ti sọ̀rọ̀ nípa ìrírí wọn. (They have gone to a restaurant and talked about their experiences.)
  3. Wọ́n ń múra sílẹ̀ láti lọ sí ilé Yẹmi. (They are preparing to go to Yẹmi's house.)
  4. Yẹmi ti dúró de mi ní pápá ọkọ̀ òfurufú. — result: Yẹmi was already waiting when Adé arrived.

Chapter Review — Apá A

  1. ìrìnàjò; dé; ìrírí; parí; àbájáde; àṣeyọrí
  2. has finished, is finished; is ready, has become ready; to prepare, to get ready; still, even so
  3. Accept any natural phrase using each item; examples: Mo ti parí ìrìnàjò mi. / Àbájáde iṣẹ́ náà dára. / Ó jẹ́ àṣeyọrí ńlá.

Chapter Review — Apá B

  1. (a) Mo ti rà tikẹ́ẹ̀tì. (b) Wọ́n ń sinmi. (c) A ó rí ọ.
  2. Accept any accurate pair, such as: Ó ti dé. (completed) / Ó wà ní ilé báyìí. (present result).
  3. Corrections: (i) Ó ti dé. — ti, not ń, is required for a completed arrival. (ii) Mo ti parí iṣẹ́ mi. — ti is required to mark completion. (iii) N ó lọ sí ilé oúnjẹ. — only one particle may be used per clause.
  4. Accept any three-sentence account using ti, ń, and yóò once each. Example: Mo ti parí iṣẹ́ mi. Mo ń sinmi báyìí. N ó jẹun ní alẹ́.

Chapter Review — Apá C

  1. Correct forms: ìrìnàjò; parí; àbájáde; àṣeyọrí.
  2. Accept accurate, natural pronunciation with each particle's tone kept distinct from the verb's tone.
  3. Accept any accurate contrastive pair, such as: Mo ti kọ ìwé náà. / Mo ń kọ ìránṣẹ́ mìíràn.

Looking Ahead

Ohun tí o lè ṣe báyìí — What you can do now

You can use ti independently to describe completed experiences and present results, and you can keep ti, ń, and yóò clearly distinct within a single connected passage. You can also describe travel experiences, completed tasks, and achievements, and write a short experience note with full diacritics that moves clearly among completed, ongoing, and future time.

Ẹ ń lẹ́! First Encounters

Greetings, names, and introductions · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Greet people appropriately for the time of day and your relationship to them.
  2. Ask and state a person's name.
  3. Introduce yourself using basic identity statements.
  4. Use core courtesy expressions including ẹ ṣé, jọ̀wọ́, and má bínú.
  5. Say goodbye appropriately.
  6. Recognize the subject pronouns mo, o, ó, a, ẹ, and wọ́n in greetings and identity statements.
  7. Distinguish the familiar singular form o from the plural or honorific form ẹ.
  8. Recognize and produce the three tones of Yorùbá and read basic tone marks correctly.
  9. Distinguish the letters ẹ from e, ọ from o, and ṣ from s in written Yorùbá.

Communication Scenario

You have just arrived at a community Yorùbá language program in Lagos. People are filtering in and sitting down. Before the session begins, the instructor asks everyone to introduce themselves to a neighbor. You have never spoken a word of Yorùbá — but by the time this chapter ends, you will be able to handle that entire opening exchange: greeting someone appropriately, asking and giving a name, and saying goodbye.

  1. In English, a greeting like "How are you?" is often answered automatically, without anyone really listening. In many Yorùbá-speaking communities, greetings function differently — they are small, structured conversations that signal respect and attentiveness. Keep this in mind as you learn the expressions that follow.
  2. Yorùbá uses pitch — high, mid, and low — to distinguish the meanings of words and sentences. This is called tone. Before you say or read any Yorùbá, notice that some words in this chapter carry small marks above certain vowels. Those marks are part of the word. Hold that observation as you begin.

Pronunciation and Orthography

Standard Yorùbá Orthography

Yorùbá is written using the Latin alphabet with several letters that are specific to the language. Three pairs of letters require special attention from the start:

Standard letterApproximate soundExampleCommon error to avoid
ẹlike the e in "bet" — open, mid-frontẹ ṣé (thank you)Do not write as plain e; the underdot is part of the letter
ọlike the o in "off" — open, mid-backọmọ (child)Do not write as plain o; these are different sounds and different letters
ṣlike the sh in "shoe"ẹ ṣé (thank you)Do not write as plain s; ṣ and s represent distinct consonant sounds
Orthography Note — The Underdot is Not Optional

The underdot beneath ẹ, ọ, and the cedilla-like mark beneath ṣ are not decorations and not accents. They are part of the letter. Leaving them out produces a different letter — one that represents a different sound and often a different word. Standard Yorùbá orthography requires them in every context, from vocabulary lists to personal messages. In informal digital writing, some speakers omit these marks, but the textbook standard does not permit this. You will write fully marked Yorùbá throughout this course.

A tone mark may appear above an underdotted vowel: ẹ́, ẹ̀, ọ́, ọ̀. The underdot and the tone mark carry separate information and both must be preserved.

The Three Tones

Yorùbá is a tonal language. This means that pitch — the relative height of your voice — is part of the meaning of every syllable. The same sequence of consonants and vowels can mean different things at different pitches. Tone is not emphasis or emotion; it is part of the word itself.

Yorùbá has three level tones:

á High acute accent ´
a Mid unmarked
à Low grave accent `
ọ́ High + ọ underdot + acute
ẹ̀ Low + ẹ underdot + grave

Sound Focus — Tone as Meaning, Not Emphasis

English uses pitch to express emotion, surprise, or question intonation — but English pitch does not change the dictionary meaning of a word. In Yorùbá, pitch is part of the word. Ọ̀bẹ̀ (low-low-low) means "knife," while ọbẹ (mid-mid) means "soup." Different tones, different meanings, same letters.

Do not try to produce Yorùbá tones using English habits of pitch (stress, volume, or question intonation). Instead, listen carefully to each word as a unit of pitch and meaning. In this course, every marked tone in every word is verified — trust the marks and practice them consistently.

First listening exercise: Your instructor or audio model will say three words. Listen for whether the pitch is level and high, level and mid, or level and low. Do not guess — train your ear before your mouth.

Grammar Instruction

1.1 Subject Pronouns

Purpose: refer to people in greetings and identity statements.

Yorùbá subject pronouns are short, high-frequency forms. Their tone and vowel quality are essential — a different tone can produce a different word. The table below introduces all six subject pronouns in controlled patterns. You will build productive control of them across the first five chapters.

Pronoun

o2nd person singular familiar — mid tone

ó3rd person singular — high tone

a1st person plural — mid tone

Grammar Note — Familiar o versus Honorific ẹ

The distinction between familiar o and plural or honorific ẹ is one of the most important choices in everyday Yorùbá interaction. Use o with someone your own age, a close friend, or a younger person. Use ẹ when addressing an elder, a person of higher social status, a teacher, a relative deserving of respect, or a group of any size.

This distinction shows up not only in pronouns but also in greetings: Ẹ káàárọ̀ (respectful — note ẹ) versus Káàárọ̀ (familiar, with ẹ removed). Many greetings in this chapter already use ẹ because they are respectful forms — appropriate for meeting people you do not yet know well.

Age and status together influence the choice, but other factors — relationship, familiarity, setting, region, and individual preference — also matter. Choosing the wrong level is a common and correctable error; this course will help you develop a reliable sense of context.

Grammar Note — Third-Person Singular Has No Grammatical Gender

The subject pronoun ó refers to one person, animal, or thing without specifying gender. Yorùbá does not have grammatical gender. Ó corresponds to English "he," "she," or "it" depending on context. Do not invent separate Yorùbá equivalents for "he" and "she" — no such distinction exists in Standard Yorùbá subject pronouns. Context and the noun in the sentence make reference clear.

1.2 Identification with ni

Purpose: state who or what someone is — name, identity, and role.

Yorùbá uses the particle ni (mid tone — unmarked) to link a subject to an identity or name. It corresponds to English "am / is / are" when you are identifying what someone is. The basic pattern is:

  • Orúkọ mi ni Tọpẹ.My name is Tọpẹ.Structure: orúkọ mi (subject phrase) + ni + name
  • Orúkọ rẹ ni nni?What is your name? (familiar)Structure: orúkọ rẹ (subject phrase) + ni + nni (what)
  • Mo jẹ́ akẹ́kọ̀ọ́.I am a student.Alternative identity pattern using jẹ́ — introduced here as a formulaic expression
Grammar Note — ni and ní Are Different

Mid-tone ni (unmarked) is the identification particle. High-tone ní (marked with an acute accent) is a different word used for possession and some prepositional meanings — for example, "he has" or "at a location." You will learn ní fully in Chapter 3. For now, use ni only in the identification patterns shown here. The tone difference is real and meaningful: do not substitute one for the other.

Practice — Complete with the correct form:

  3. Kí _____ orúkọ yín? (What is your name? — respectful)

1.3 Formulaic Greetings Containing Untaught Grammar

Purpose: recognize and use common greetings as complete communication units before analyzing their internal structure.

Several greetings in this chapter are used as complete expressions even though some of their grammar — aspect particles, specific contractions, and the full greeting verb structure — has not yet been taught. This is normal in language learning. Learn them as meaningful units now; you will understand their internal structure as the course progresses.

  • Ẹ ń lẹ́!Greetings! (literal meaning involves a respectful greeting verb — treated here as a formulaic expression)

Language Focus

Language Focus — How Yorùbá Differs from English in This Chapter

1. Tone is part of the word, not an add-on. Every syllable in Yorùbá carries a tone — high, mid, or low. Tone marks are not decorative accents. They encode part of the word's meaning. Omitting a tone mark is like omitting a letter: the word becomes incomplete or changes meaning. From this chapter onward, you will read, write, hear, and produce tones as an inseparable part of every Yorùbá word.

2. The underdots on ẹ, ọ, and ṣ distinguish separate sounds and letters. These are not decorated versions of e, o, and s. They are different letters representing different sounds. Ẹ and e are as different in Yorùbá as b and d are in English. Omitting the underdot is a spelling error.

3. Yorùbá does not assign grammatical gender to nouns. There is no "masculine" or "feminine" noun class. The third-person singular subject pronoun ó covers what English expresses as "he," "she," and "it." Do not create separate Yorùbá forms for "he" and "she" — none exist in Standard Yorùbá.

4. The same pronoun ẹ can mean "you (plural)" or "you (respectful singular)." Context, relationship, and social setting determine which meaning applies. This is not ambiguity — it is a system. You will develop reliable judgment through practice and cultural guidance across the course.

5. Many greetings are used as complete formulaic units before their grammar is taught. This is not a shortcut. It reflects how Yorùbá speakers learn and use their language. Later chapters will reconnect these formulaic expressions to the grammar that underlies them.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — The Greeting as Its Own Conversation

In many Yorùbá-speaking communities, a greeting is not a brief formality before the "real" conversation begins. The greeting exchange itself — asking after well-being, inquiring about one's household, acknowledging the time of day or a recent event — is the opening act of any respectful interaction. To skip it, or to rush through it, can signal indifference or disrespect. Taking time for the greeting communicates that you see and value the other person.

This pattern varies by setting, relationship, generation, and individual preference. A greeting with a close friend while both are in a hurry looks different from a greeting offered to an elder at a family gathering. Learning to read the context is part of communicative competence in Yorùbá, just as it is in any language. This course will give you the tools to navigate a range of situations — not one fixed set of rules.

Culture in Context — Time-Based Greetings

Yorùbá greetings commonly acknowledge the time of day: morning (Ẹ káàárọ̀), afternoon (Ẹ káàsán), and evening (Ẹ káalẹ̀). The time-word is part of the greeting, not an afterthought. Using the wrong time-greeting is a small but noticeable error — it is worth developing the habit of choosing correctly from the start.

Beyond time of day, Yorùbá has a rich system of situation-specific greetings for activities and events: returning from travel, waking from sleep, completing a task, eating a meal, working, returning after a long absence, experiencing a loss. You will encounter many of these in later chapters as the communication domains expand.

Culture in Context — Yorùbá Names

Yorùbá personal names often carry meaning — they may describe the circumstances of a birth, express gratitude, state a belief, announce a family identity, or invoke a hope. For example, Àbíọ́dún can be understood as "born at the time of a festival," and Fúnmiláyọ̀ as "give me joy." Names require their full tone marks and underdots: these are part of the name's form and its meaning.

When learning another person's name, pronounce it with care. Mispronouncing a Yorùbá name — especially the tones — changes what is being said. In this course, all names used in examples and dialogues have been orthographically verified. Use them as models for learning how tone and underdotted letters work in practice.

Do not assume that a particular name belongs to a speaker of one specific religion, region, or community. Yorùbá names are used across a wide and diverse population.

Regional Note — Greetings Across Yorùbá-Speaking Communities

Standard Nigerian Yorùbá forms such as Ẹ káàárọ̀ are broadly intelligible across Nigeria, the Republic of Benin, and Togo, and among diaspora communities. However, specific pronunciation, vocabulary, and greeting conventions vary across regions. Speakers in Ìjẹ̀bú, Ọ̀yọ́, Ẹ̀gbá, Ẹ̀kìtì, Ìlàjẹ, Ìfẹ̀, and other areas of the Yorùbá-speaking world use forms of these greetings that may differ in pronunciation or word choice from the standard forms taught here. This textbook teaches Standard Nigerian Yorùbá as the productive model; regional and community variation will be introduced receptively and accurately labeled as the course develops. No variety is presented as more "correct" or more authentically Yorùbá than another.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake

Error: Omitting tone marks: writing Oruko mi ni Kola instead of Orúkọ mi ni Kọ́lá.

Correction: Every high and low tone must be marked in all learner-facing Yorùbá. Mid tones are intentionally left unmarked. Omitting marks is a spelling error, not a shortcut. Tone marks are part of the word. Write: Orúkọ mi ni Kọ́lá.

Common Mistake

Error: Writing e se instead of ẹ ṣé — replacing the underdotted letters with plain equivalents.

Correction: ẹ and e are different letters. ṣ and s are different letters. The underdot is not optional and is not a decoration. Write: Ẹ ṣé.

Common Mistake

Error: Using the familiar pronoun o / rẹ when addressing an elder or a stranger: Kí ni orúkọ rẹ? to someone you should address with respect.

Correction: When greeting someone older, of higher status, or a person you do not know well, use the honorific or plural form: Kí ni orúkọ yín? The distinction between familiar o / rẹ and honorific ẹ / yín is socially important. When in doubt, default to the respectful form.

Common Mistake

Error: Adding tone marks to every vowel, including mid-tone ones: writing Móo wàá or marking every syllable of àwọn with a high accent.

Correction: Mid tone is deliberately left unmarked in Standard Yorùbá orthography. An unmarked vowel does not mean "no tone" — it means mid tone. Do not place an acute or grave accent on a mid-tone syllable. Write: Mo wà. (Mo = mid; wà = low.)

Common Mistake

Error: Inventing a separate Yorùbá word for "she" or "he" by analogy with English.

Correction: Standard Yorùbá does not grammatically distinguish "he," "she," and "it" in the third-person singular subject pronoun. The form is ó for all three. Context — the noun or name mentioned earlier in the conversation — makes reference clear. Do not create forms such as òun naa or any other invented pronoun as a regular substitute for "she." You will learn the emphatic third-person form òun later, in its correct context.

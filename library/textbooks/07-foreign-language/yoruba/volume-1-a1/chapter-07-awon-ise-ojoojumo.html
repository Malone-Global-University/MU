Àwọn Ìṣe Ojoojúmọ́

Daily Routines · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Describe an activity that is happening right now using the progressive marker ń.
  2. Describe activities that happen regularly or habitually using the habitual marker máa ń.
  3. Contrast an ongoing event with a regular pattern by selecting the correct aspect marker.
  4. Ask and tell the time in Yorùbá, using the approved clock-time expressions.
  5. Name the days of the week and common parts of the day.
  6. Sequence a simple routine using basic time connectors.
  7. Write a fully marked 80–100-word description of a daily routine.
  8. Read a schedule, calendar entry, or short routine description with accurate recognition of tone marks and underdots.
  9. Produce the tone of ń and máa ń correctly and distinguish them from each other in listening and speaking.
  10. Apply cumulative Unit I grammar — pronouns, ni/ní, wà, plurality — in new routine contexts.

Communication Scenario

It is Tuesday morning. Rèmí, a secondary school teacher in Ìbàdàn, is showing his schedule to his new Yorùbá-language pen pal, Àdésọlá, who lives in Cotonou, Benin. Àdésọlá wants to know what Rèmí is doing at any given time during the day and what his typical week looks like. Rèmí needs to explain both what he is doing right now and what he usually does — and those require two different Yorùbá expressions. By the end of this chapter, you will be able to hold exactly this kind of conversation.

  1. In English, "I am eating" and "I eat (every day)" are expressed differently. Do you think Yorùbá handles this distinction the same way English does? What do you expect might be different?
  2. Look at the chapter title: Àwọn Ìṣe Ojoojúmọ́. You already know àwọn marks plural reference. The word ojoojúmọ́ means "every day / daily." What do you think ìṣe might mean? What clue does the chapter topic give you?
  3. Think of five activities that are part of your own daily routine. Jot them down. You will use them in the speaking task at the end of this chapter.

Pronunciation Focus

The syllabic nasal ń — tone and identity

Pronunciation Focus — ń as a tone-bearing syllable

Chapter 4 introduced the concept of the syllabic nasal. In Chapter 7, the syllabic nasal ń becomes a major grammatical tool: it is the progressive aspect marker, placed between the subject pronoun and the verb to signal an ongoing action.

Two facts about ń are essential:

  • It is a complete syllable, not a consonant. The symbol ń represents a nasal sound — produced through the nose with the mouth closed or nearly closed — that stands alone as a syllable. It does not attach to the following vowel to form a cluster.
  • It carries high tone. The acute accent on ń is a tone mark, not a decoration. Pronounce it at the top of your comfortable speaking pitch. In connected speech this high tone must remain audible.

Practice these syllable sequences:

  • mo ń jẹun — three syllables after mo: /ń/ · /jẹ/ · /un/
  • ó ń wẹ̀ — /ó/ · /ń/ · /wẹ̀/ — the high-tone ń rises between the high-tone subject pronoun and the low-tone verb
  • a ń kọ̀wé — /a/ · /ń/ · /kọ̀/ · /wé/ — the mid-tone subject pronoun is followed by high-tone ń

A common error for English speakers is to swallow or reduce ń, treating it as a weak linking sound. It must be produced as a full, high-pitched nasal syllable.

Tone sequence in aspect markers — ń versus máa ń

Pronunciation Focus — Hearing the progressive and habitual in speech

Progressive ń (one syllable, high tone) and habitual máa ń (two syllables, high-high) are audibly distinct. The difference is not only in meaning but in how many syllables separate the subject from the verb:

  • Progressive: Mo ń jẹun. — subject + ONE high-tone syllable + verb
  • Habitual: Mo máa ń jẹun. — subject + TWO high-tone syllables + verb

Both máa and ń carry high tone. Say máa ń as a smooth two-syllable unit at a consistently high pitch before dropping into the verb.

Listen for this difference in the model dialogue and reading texts in this chapter, and practise producing it until the two patterns feel automatic.

Tone on time vocabulary

Pronunciation Focus — Parts of the day and days of the week

Several time words introduced in this chapter require careful attention to tone:

  • àárọ̀ — low-high-low (three syllables): do not pronounce it as two syllables
  • ọ̀sán — low-high: a common error is to give both syllables high tone
  • ìrọ̀lẹ́ — low-low-high: three distinct syllables
  • lóni — high-mid: the contraction of ní + oni shifts to a single word with high tone on the first syllable
  • Ọjọ́ (day) — high-high: maintain both tones clearly when combining with the day name

Practise each part-of-day expression by saying a complete sentence: Mo máa ń jí ní àárọ̀. (I usually wake up in the morning.) The time phrase ní àárọ̀ gives you three opportunities to practice: the high-tone ní, the low first syllable of àárọ̀, and the high-low sequence that follows.

Grammar

7.1 The progressive marker ń — what is happening right now

Purpose: describe an action that is in progress at the moment of speaking.

To say that someone is doing something right now — that the action is currently in progress — place ń (high tone) between the subject pronoun and the verb.

The pattern is:

subject pronoun + ń + verb (+ object or complement)

  • Mo ń jẹun. subject: mo · progressive: ń · verb: jẹun I am eating.
  • O ń wẹ̀. subject: o · progressive: ń · verb: wẹ̀ You are bathing.
  • Ó ń kẹ́kọ̀ọ́. subject: ó · progressive: ń · verb: kẹ́kọ̀ọ́ He / she is studying.
  • A ń sinmi. subject: a · progressive: ń · verb: sinmi We are resting.
  • Àwọn akẹ́kọ̀ọ́ ń ka ìwé. subject: àwọn akẹ́kọ̀ọ́ · progressive: ń · verb: ka · object: ìwé The students are reading (a book).
  • Ẹ ń kọ́? subject: ẹ (honorific/plural) · progressive: ń · verb: kọ́ Are you teaching? (honorific)
Grammar Note — Three things ń does NOT do
  1. ń does not change form for person or number. It is the same syllable whether the subject is mo, ó, a, or wọ́n. Yorùbá verbs do not conjugate for person; the subject pronoun and the aspect marker together carry that information.
  2. ń does not translate every English "-ing" form. English uses "-ing" for ongoing events, future plans, and sometimes habits. Yorùbá ń is specifically for events happening right now or at a contextually established present moment. Do not use it for habitual actions (use máa ń) or future plans (use máa, introduced in Chapter 14).
  3. ń must not lose its tone mark in writing. Writing n without the acute accent is an error — it represents a different sound in other contexts. Always write ń.

7.2 The habitual marker máa ń — what happens regularly

Purpose: describe activities that happen regularly, repeatedly, or as part of an established routine.

To say that someone habitually or regularly does something — every day, on Mondays, generally — place máa ń between the subject pronoun and the verb.

The pattern is:

subject pronoun + máa ń + verb (+ object or complement)

  • Mo máa ń jí ní àárọ̀. subject: mo · habitual: máa ń · verb: jí · time: ní àárọ̀ I usually wake up in the morning.
  • Ó máa ń lọ sí iṣẹ́ ní ago mẹjọ. subject: ó · habitual: máa ń · verb: lọ · direction: sí iṣẹ́ · time: ní ago mẹjọ He / she usually goes to work at eight o'clock.
  • A máa ń jẹun papọ̀ ní ọ̀sán. subject: a · habitual: máa ń · verb: jẹun · manner: papọ̀ · time: ní ọ̀sán We usually eat together in the afternoon.
  • Wọ́n máa ń sinmi lẹ́yìn oúnjẹ ìrọ̀lẹ́. subject: wọ́n · habitual: máa ń · verb: sinmi · time: lẹ́yìn oúnjẹ ìrọ̀lẹ́ They usually rest after the evening meal.
  • Ẹ máa ń kọ̀wé báwo? subject: ẹ (honorific/plural) · habitual: máa ń · verb: kọ̀wé · question: báwo How often do you write? (honorific)
Grammar Note — máa ń is not the same as máa

This chapter introduces máa ń as the habitual marker. A later chapter (Chapter 14) will introduce máa — without the following ń — as a future marker. These are distinct constructions:

  • Mo máa ń jẹun ní àárọ̀. — I habitually eat in the morning. (habitual — current stage)
  • Mo máa jẹun ní àárọ̀. — I will eat in the morning. (future — Chapter 14)

The difference is the presence or absence of ń after máa. Do not attempt to produce or analyze future máa at this stage. The contrast is introduced here only to prevent confusion when you encounter both later.

7.3 Contrasting progressive and habitual

Purpose: select the correct aspect marker based on whether an event is ongoing right now or regularly occurring.

The following pairs show how the same verb and the same person produce different meanings depending on the aspect marker:

Progressive ń — happening now
Habitual máa ń — regularly / usually
Mo ń jẹun.
I am eating (right now).
Mo máa ń jẹun ní àárọ̀.
I usually eat in the morning.
Ó ń sinmi.
He / she is resting (now).
Ó máa ń sinmi ní ọ̀sán.
He / she usually rests in the afternoon.
A ń ka ìwé.
We are reading (right now).
A máa ń ka ìwé ní ìrọ̀lẹ́.
We usually read in the evening.
Wọ́n ń ṣiṣẹ́.
They are working (right now).
Wọ́n máa ń ṣiṣẹ́ lóni.
They usually work today (i.e., on a day like today).

A useful question to ask yourself before choosing: Is this happening at this very moment, or is it a recurring pattern? If it is happening right now, use ń. If it is a regular or general pattern, use máa ń.

7.4 Asking and telling the time

Purpose: ask what time it is and state the time using the approved clock-time patterns.

The standard question for asking the time is:

  • Àgọ̀ mélòó ni báyìí? What time is it now?

To answer, state the hour followed by the part of day if needed:

  • Ago kan ni. It is one o'clock.
  • Ago méjì àárọ̀ ni. It is two in the morning.
  • Ago mẹ́ta àti ìdajì ni. It is half past three.
  • Ago mẹ́fà ìrọ̀lẹ́ kọ mẹ́ẹ̀ẹ́dógún ni. It is a quarter to six in the evening.
Grammar Note — ni in time statements

Notice that time statements end with ni (mid tone): Ago méjì àárọ̀ ni. This is the same identification particle you learned in Unit I. The pattern names or identifies what the time is, just as Orúkọ mi ni Títí names a person. The particle ni does not change; no accent mark appears on it.

7.5 Sequencing a routine — time expressions and connectors

Purpose: arrange activities in order using time phrases and sequencing words.

  • Mo máa ń jí ní àárọ̀. Lẹ́yìn náà, mo máa ń wẹ̀. I usually wake up in the morning. After that, I usually bathe.
  • Àkọ́kọ́, ó máa ń jẹun. Lẹ́yìn náà, ó máa ń lọ sí iṣẹ́. First, he / she usually eats. After that, he / she usually goes to work.
  • A máa ń kẹ́kọ̀ọ́ ní àárọ̀ àti ní ọ̀sán. We usually study in the morning and in the afternoon.

The connectors àkọ́kọ́ (first) and lẹ́yìn náà (after that) may appear at the beginning of a sentence or clause. They are the primary sequencing tools for the routine-writing task in this chapter. Additional connectors are introduced in Chapter 16.

Language Focus

Yorùbá verbs do not conjugate for person. In English, "I eat," "she eats," and "they eat" change the verb ending for the third-person singular. In Yorùbá, the verb jẹun (eat) does not change regardless of who is doing the eating. What changes is the subject pronoun before the verb and the aspect marker between the pronoun and the verb. This means that two pieces of information — who and what kind of event — are carried by the elements before the verb, not by the verb itself. As a result, omitting the subject pronoun or the aspect marker leaves a sentence incomplete or ambiguous. Neither can be dropped casually the way English sometimes drops the subject in informal speech.

This is one of the most important structural differences between Yorùbá and English, and it shapes how every sentence in this chapter is built.

Model Dialogue

The dialogue below shows Rèmí and Àdésọlá exchanging messages about their daily schedules — one in real time (progressive) and one describing regular patterns (habitual). Read it carefully, then answer the comprehension questions. Pay close attention to which aspect marker each speaker uses and why.

Ipò: Àwọn ìfọ̀rọ̀wánilẹ́nuwò tẹlifóònù. Rèmí jẹ́ olùkọ́ ní Ìbàdàn; Àdésọlá jẹ́ akẹ́kọ̀ọ́ ní Cotonou, Benin Republic. Ọjọ́ Ìṣẹgun ni, ní ago mẹ́jọ àárọ̀.
Setting: A phone message exchange. Rèmí is a teacher in Ìbàdàn; Àdésọlá is a student in Cotonou, Benin Republic. It is Tuesday, 8 a.m.

ÀDÉSỌLÁ Ẹ káàárọ̀, Rèmí! Báwo ni? Kí ni ẹ ń ṣe ní àkókò yìí? Good morning, Rèmí! How are you? What are you doing at this time? (honorific)
RÈMÍ Káàárọ̀, Àdésọlá! Mo wà dára. Mo ń jẹun báyìí — oúnjẹ àárọ̀ mi. Ẹ ńkọ́? Kí ni ẹ ń ṣe? Good morning, Àdésọlá! I am fine. I am eating right now — my morning meal. And you? What are you doing?
ÀDÉSỌLÁ Mo ń kẹ́kọ̀ọ́ Yorùbá! Mo ń ka ìwé Yorùbá mi àti mo ń kọ̀wé àwọn ọ̀rọ̀ tuntun. Rèmí, àwọn ìṣe rẹ ojoojúmọ́ ṣe bí? I am studying Yorùbá! I am reading my Yorùbá book and writing new words. Rèmí, what does your daily routine look like?
RÈMÍ Ó dára! Mo máa ń jí ní ago méfà àárọ̀. Àkọ́kọ́, mo máa ń wẹ̀ àti wo aṣọ. Lẹ́yìn náà, mo máa ń jẹun. Ní ago méjọ, mo máa ń lọ sí ilé-ẹ̀kọ́. Alright! I usually wake up at six in the morning. First, I usually bathe and dress. After that, I usually eat. At eight o'clock, I usually go to school.
ÀDÉSỌLÁ Ẹ máa ń kọ́ àwọn akẹ́kọ̀ọ́ tí ó tó mẹ́lòó ní ọjọ́ kan? How many students do you usually teach in a day? (honorific)
RÈMÍ Mo máa ń kọ́ àwọn akẹ́kọ̀ọ́ ọgbọ̀n ní kíláàsì kọ̀ọ̀kan. Mo ní kíláàsì mẹ́rin. Ó rẹ mi, ṣùgbọ́n mo nífẹ̀ẹ́ iṣẹ́ mi. I usually teach thirty students in each class. I have four classes. It tires me, but I love my work.
ÀDÉSỌLÁ Ẹ máa ń padà sí ilé ní ago mélòó? At what time do you usually return home? (honorific)
RÈMÍ Mo máa ń padà sí ilé ní ago mérin ìrọ̀lẹ́. Lẹ́yìn náà, mo máa ń sinmi díẹ̀. Ní ìrọ̀lẹ́, mo máa ń jẹun pẹ̀lú ẹbí mi. Ní òru, mo máa ń ka ìwé tàbí wo tẹlifísọ̀n kékeré. I usually return home at four in the evening. After that, I usually rest a little. In the evening, I usually eat with my family. At night, I usually read or watch a little television.
ÀDÉSỌLÁ Ẹ máa ń sùn ní ago mélòó? At what time do you usually sleep? (honorific)
RÈMÍ Mo máa ń sùn ní ago mẹ́wàá tàbí ago mọ́kànlá òru. Ó dára — ẹ tún sọ̀rọ̀ fún mi nípa ìgbésí ayé rẹ ní Cotonou! I usually sleep at ten or eleven at night. Alright — tell me again about your life in Cotonou!
ÀDÉSỌLÁ Ó dára! Ẹ jọ̀wọ́ dúró — mo ń pé ẹnìkan mìíràn báyìí. A ó sọ̀rọ̀ lẹ́yìn! Alright! Please wait — I am calling someone else right now. We will talk later!
RÈMÍ Ó dàbọ̀ fún ìsinsìnyí! Goodbye for now!
Grammar Note — Two expressions to notice

Ó rẹ mi (It tires me / I am tired by it) and mo nífẹ̀ẹ́ iṣẹ́ mi (I love my work) both use constructions that will be analyzed more fully in later chapters. Treat them as formulaic expressions for now: learn each as a complete, useful phrase rather than analyzing their grammatical parts. Similarly, A ó sọ̀rọ̀ lẹ́yìn (We will talk later) contains a future construction not yet taught productively; learn it as a fixed leave-taking formula.

Àwọn ìbéèrè nípa ọ̀rọ̀ àdáhùnṣe — Comprehension questions:

  1. Ago mélòó ni Rèmí máa ń jí? Ní ìpín ọjọ́ wo? (What time does Rèmí usually wake up? What part of the day?)

Writing Workshop

The major writing product for Chapter 7 is an 80–100-word description of your own daily routine on a typical weekday. This is the most extended piece of connected Yorùbá writing you have attempted, building on the profile from Chapter 5. Follow the five steps below.

  1. Plan your routine in English or any language. Think through a single typical weekday — a Monday or Tuesday works well. List at least six activities in order, with the time at which each happens. Include at least two parts of the day (morning, afternoon, evening, night).
  2. Draft in Yorùbá. Convert your plan into Yorùbá sentences. Use máa ń for habitual activities (your routine). Use ń at least once to describe something you are doing right now as you write. Add time phrases from the vocabulary list. Use at least two sequencing connectors (àkọ́kọ́, lẹ́yìn náà).
  3. Check your pronouns. Are you using the correct subject pronoun before ń or máa ń? Remember that subject mo is not the same as possessive mi. Check every pronoun in the draft.
  4. Mark every tone. Go through the draft syllable by syllable. Add an acute accent for every high tone and a grave accent for every low tone. Leave mid-tone vowels unmarked. Check the underdot on every ẹ, ọ, and ṣ.
  5. Count and revise. Count your words. If you have fewer than 80, add one more activity sentence. Apply the editing checklist before the final version.

Àtúnṣe àtúnyẹ̀wò — Editing checklist:

  • Every habitual activity uses máa ń between the subject pronoun and the verb.
  • Any activity described as happening right now uses ń alone — not máa ń.
  • ń has an acute accent in every instance. Plain n without the accent is not the aspect marker.
  • All subject pronouns are correct. I have not used mo where the possessive mi is needed.
  • Every high tone carries an acute accent; every low tone carries a grave accent; mid-tone vowels are unmarked.
  • Every ẹ, ọ, and ṣ has its underdot.
  • At least one time phrase (part of day or clock time) appears in the text.
  • At least two sequencing connectors are used correctly.
  • Every sentence begins with a capital letter and ends with a full stop or question mark.
  • The text is between 80 and 100 words.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Daily schedules and lifestyle diversity

The daily routines in this chapter's texts reflect a range of contexts: a market trader in Ìlọrin who opens at eight, a university student in Cotonou who walks to campus, a teacher in Ìbàdàn with four classes a day, and a technician near Ìbàdàn who walks with his wife in the evening. This variety is deliberate. There is no single "Yorùbá daily routine." Urban and rural settings, different occupations, children's school schedules, religious practices, and personal preferences all shape how people organise their days.

When you discuss routines with Yorùbá speakers, you will likely find wide variation in wake-up times, meal patterns, commuting distances, and evening habits. Approach these conversations with genuine curiosity rather than with a fixed expectation of what a "typical" day looks like.

Culture in Context — Mealtimes and food patterns

The vocabulary in this chapter introduces oúnjẹ àárọ̀ (morning food), but the concept of a quick early breakfast before work or school is not universal across Yorùbá-speaking communities. Some households eat their main meal at midday; others organise the day around a large evening meal shared with the family. Street food, canteen lunches, and home cooking may all feature depending on the day, the person, and the setting.

The expression ṣe oúnjẹ (to cook / prepare food) covers a wide range of food-preparation activities, from simple reheating to elaborate multi-dish meals. Do not assume that the person who cooks is always the same household member every day, or that gender automatically determines who prepares food.

Culture in Context — Time and punctuality

Clock time and punctuality practices vary significantly across Yorùbá-speaking contexts — as they do everywhere. Formal institutional settings (universities, government offices, schools) may operate on relatively strict schedules; social events and informal visits may follow more flexible conventions. Do not apply a single rule about what "on time" means across all situations.

The expression ó fẹrẹ tó (almost / it is nearly time) reflects the natural human practice of rounding or approximating time — something that occurs in any language. When telling the time in Yorùbá, both exact and approximate expressions are common and useful.

Regional Variation — Day names and everyday vocabulary

As noted in the vocabulary section, the days of the week have both traditional Yorùbá names and widely used adapted forms: Ọjọ́ Sátidé for Saturday and Ọjọ́ Sọ́ndé for Sunday are heard frequently in everyday conversation across southwestern Nigeria. In educational and formal writing, the traditional names are standard. In Benin and Togo, some day names may differ slightly from Nigerian Standard Yorùbá, and French-influenced time expressions may appear in everyday speech. This textbook uses Nigerian Standard Yorùbá as the productive model. When you encounter a day name or time expression that does not match the forms above, ask a qualified speaker about the community and context in which it is used.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using ń for habitual actions

Error: Writing *Mo ń jẹun ní àárọ̀ nígbà gbogbo when the meaning is "I always eat in the morning" — using the progressive marker for a habitual action.

Correction: Mo máa ń jẹun ní àárọ̀ nígbà gbogbo. The key question is: is this happening right now, or does it happen regularly? "Always" (nígbà gbogbo) signals a habitual pattern, which requires máa ń. Reserve ń alone for events actually in progress at the moment of speaking.

Common Mistake — Omitting the tone mark from ń

Error: Writing *Mo n jẹun or *Mo n jẹun with a plain n instead of ń.

Correction: Mo ń jẹun. The acute accent on ń is a tone mark, not optional decoration. Without it, the written form is incomplete and potentially ambiguous. Every instance of the progressive marker must be written as ń with the acute accent. Develop the habit of checking every ń in your writing before you submit.

Common Mistake — Treating máa ń as a single word meaning "usually"

Error: Treating máa ń as a single adverb equivalent to English "usually" and placing it anywhere in the sentence — for example, *Mo jẹun máa ń ní àárọ̀.

Correction: Mo máa ń jẹun ní àárọ̀. The habitual construction is a fixed subject-marker-verb pattern. máa ń occupies the aspect-marker slot between the subject pronoun and the verb. It cannot be moved to the end of the sentence or inserted after the verb. Think of it as part of the predicate structure, not a floating adverb.

Common Mistake — Translating English simple present with ń

Error: Translating "I eat breakfast every day" as *Mo ń jẹ oúnjẹ àárọ̀ lójoojúmọ́ — using the progressive because English uses a simple present form that looks "basic."

Correction: Mo máa ń jẹ oúnjẹ àárọ̀ lójoojúmọ́. English simple present (I eat, she goes, they study) often expresses habitual or general meaning. Yorùbá habitual meaning requires máa ń. Do not let the absence of "-ing" in English mislead you into choosing the progressive. Ask yourself what the sentence means, not what the English form looks like.

Common Mistake — Confusing wà and wá

Error: Using *wà (to be / exist at a place) when the intended meaning is movement toward the speaker — for example, writing *Ó ń wà to mean "He / she is coming."

Correction: Ó ń wá. — He / she is coming (toward here). wà (low-high) describes presence at a location: Ó wà ní ilé (He / she is at home). wá (high) is the verb meaning "to come." The two verbs sound similar but are tonally and semantically distinct. Pay attention to tone when using these two words.

  4. A ń jẹun papọ̀ ní ọ̀sán lóni. — We are eating together this afternoon. (progressive — happening at this moment)
  5. Ẹ máa ń lọ sí ọjà ní Ọjọ́ Ajé? — Do you usually go to the market on Mondays? (habitual — every Monday; honorific)
  6. Wọ́n ń ka ìwé. — They are reading right now. (progressive)

Practice — Building routine sentences (sample answers)

  1. O máa ń jí ní àárọ̀. (You usually wake up in the morning.)
  2. Ó máa ń lọ sí iṣẹ́ ní ago mẹjọ. (He/she usually goes to work at eight o'clock.)
  3. A máa ń jẹun papọ̀ ní ọ̀sán. (We usually eat together in the afternoon.)
  4. Wọ́n máa ń kẹ́kọ̀ọ́ ní ìrọ̀lẹ́. (They usually study in the evening.)
  5. Ẹ máa ń sinmi lẹ́yìn iṣẹ́. (You usually rest after work. — honorific)

Accept any well-formed sentence using the correct subject pronoun and máa ń followed immediately by the verb, with full diacritics. Time phrases may vary as long as the meaning is appropriate.

Practice — Telling the time

  1. Ago méjọ àárọ̀ ni. (7:00 a.m. — mẹjọ = 7 — note: review numeral 7 if needed: mẹjọ = 8; the correct Yorùbá for 7 is méjọ — confirm with your instructor)
  2. Ago méjìlá ọ̀sán ni. (12:00 noon — méjìlá = 12)
  3. Ago mẹ́ta àti ìdajì ọ̀sán ni. (3:30 p.m.)
  4. Ago mẹ́wàá òru kọ mẹ́ẹ̀ẹ́dógún ni. (9:45 p.m. — a quarter to ten at night)
  5. Ago mẹ́fà àárọ̀ ni. (6:00 a.m.)

Numeral forms above 10 should be verified with a qualified speaker. Items flagged here require human Yorùbá-language review before finalization.

Dialogue — Comprehension

  1. Rèmí máa ń jí ní ago méfà àárọ̀. (Rèmí usually wakes up at six in the morning.)
  2. Rèmí ń jẹun ní àkókò tí Àdésọlá kọ̀wé sí — oúnjẹ àárọ̀ rẹ̀. (Rèmí is eating — his morning meal.)
  3. Ó máa ń kọ́ àwọn akẹ́kọ̀ọ́ ọgbọ̀n ní kíláàsì kọ̀ọ̀kan. (He usually teaches thirty students per class.)
  4. Rèmí máa ń padà sí ilé ní ago mérin ìrọ̀lẹ́. (Rèmí usually returns home at four in the evening.)
  5. Àdésọlá ń kẹ́kọ̀ọ́ Yorùbá — ó ń ka ìwé Yorùbá rẹ̀ àti kọ̀wé àwọn ọ̀rọ̀ tuntun. (Àdésọlá is studying Yorùbá — reading her Yorùbá book and writing new words.)
  6. Progressive sentences (three examples): Mo ń jẹun báyìí (I am eating now); Àdésọlá: Mo ń kẹ́kọ̀ọ́ Yorùbá / Mo ń ka ìwé Yorùbá mi / Mo ń kọ̀wé àwọn ọ̀rọ̀ tuntun; Mo ń pé ẹnìkan mìíràn báyìí (I am calling someone else now). Accept any three correctly identified progressive sentences with ń only (no máa before it).
  7. Àdésọlá uses ẹ (honorific subject) and yín-pattern questions (Ẹ ń kọ́?, Ẹ máa ń kọ́ àwọn akẹ́kọ̀ọ́ tí ó tó mẹ́lòó?) because Rèmí is a teacher and is older or in a position of respect relative to the student Àdésọlá. The honorific form shows appropriate deference.

Listening — Comprehension table (expected answers)

  • Ìféolúwakúnlé — trader (oníṣòwò) in Ìlọrin; wakes up at 5 a.m.; bathes, dresses, prepares breakfast; right now: listening to the radio at the market because the market has not yet opened
  • Olúwatọbí — university student in Lagos; Monday classes start at 8 a.m., Tuesday classes start at 10 a.m.; right now: resting in his room because the weather is keeping him from going outside
  • Àgbàdo — technician near Ìbàdàn; works from 8 a.m. to 5 p.m.; after work walks with his wife; right now: implied evening context (watches TV with wife or talks about the day)

Bonus: (1) Examples of progressive: Ìféolúwakúnlé — mo ń gbọ́ rédíò; Olúwatọbí — mo ń sinmi ní yàrá mi; Àgbàdo — context implies ongoing evening activity. (2) Olúwatọbí's Tuesday class starts later (ago mẹ́wàá) than Monday (ago méjọ). (3) A máa ń wo tẹlifísọ̀n papọ̀ tàbí a máa ń sọ̀rọ̀ nípa ọjọ́ wa.

Reading — Comprehension

  1. Rèmí máa ń lọ sí ọjà ní Ọjọ́ Bọ, ní àárọ̀ ṣáájú iṣẹ́. (Rèmí usually goes to the market on Thursday, in the morning before work.)
  2. Rèmí ní àkókò ọfẹ́ ní Ọjọ́ Rú ní ọ̀sán. (Rèmí has free time on Wednesday afternoon.)
  3. Àdésọlá máa ń jí ní ago mẹ́fà ìdajì àárọ̀. (Àdésọlá usually wakes up at half past six in the morning.)
  4. Àdésọlá máa ń rìn nítorí pé yunifásítì rẹ̀ kò jìnnà. (Àdésọlá usually walks because her university is not far.)
  5. Àdésọlá máa ń jẹun pẹ̀lú àwọn ọ̀rẹ́ rẹ̀. Lẹ́yìn jíjẹun, wọ́n máa ń sọ̀rọ̀ nínú àgbàlá yunifásítì. (Àdésọlá usually eats with her friends. After eating, they usually talk in the university yard.)
  6. Sequencing examples: Àkọ́kọ́, mo máa ń wẹ̀ àti wọ aṣọ (First, I usually bathe and dress) — àkọ́kọ́ opens the sequence; Lẹ́yìn náà, mo máa ń mu tii (After that, I usually drink tea) — lẹ́yìn náà connects to the next activity. Accept any well-identified pair with translation.
  7. Time-phrase ní example: Mo máa ń lọ sí yunifásítì ní ago mẹjọ àárọ̀ — ní (high tone) is the time preposition marking when the action occurs, equivalent to English "at." This is the same high-tone ní as possession/location, here used in a time-phrase context.

  2. Ó máa ń jí ní ago márùn-ún àárọ̀. (She/he usually wakes up at five in the morning.)

Chapter Review — Apá B, item 3 (error corrections)

  • *Mo n sinmi báyìí → Mo ń sinmi báyìí. Error: the acute accent is missing from ń. The progressive marker must always be written as ń with the high-tone mark.
  • *Ó máa ń lọ sí iṣẹ́ ní ago méjọ báyìí → Ó ń lọ sí iṣẹ́ ní ago méjọ báyìí. Error: báyìí (right now) signals a progressive event in progress; máa ń (habitual) is incorrect here. Use ń alone when the event is happening at the moment of speaking.
  • *A máa ń jẹun — ẹ jọ̀wọ́ dúró → A ń jẹun — ẹ jọ̀wọ́ dúró. Error: the request "please wait" implies the action is happening right now and is the reason for the request; the progressive ń is correct, not the habitual máa ń.

Looking Ahead

Ohun tí o lè ṣe báyìí — What you can do now

You can describe both ongoing and habitual activities in Yorùbá, ask and tell the time, name the days of the week, sequence a daily routine using connectors, and write an organized routine paragraph of 80–100 words with correct tone marks and underdots. You can distinguish the progressive ń from the habitual máa ń in both listening and writing. These two aspect markers will appear in every subsequent chapter — they are among the most frequently used grammatical constructions in the course.

Abala 8: Oúnjẹ àti Ohun Mímu — Food and Drink

Chapter 8 moves into the world of food and drink. You will learn to identify common Yorùbá foods and beverages, discuss meal preferences, make polite requests, and express what you want or do not want. Two important grammatical systems take centre stage: basic verbal negation with kò — your first systematic encounter with negating a predicate — and high-frequency preference predicates. You will also meet object pronouns in practical food-ordering contexts, building on the possession and object forms introduced in Unit I. The communicative goal: order a simple meal, state preferences, and respond to questions about food.

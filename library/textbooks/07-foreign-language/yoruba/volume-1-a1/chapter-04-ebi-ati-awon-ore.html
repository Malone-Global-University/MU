Ẹbí àti Àwọn Ọ̀rẹ́

Family and Friends · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify and name immediate and extended family members and close friends in Yorùbá.
  2. State and ask about family relationships using possessive pronouns after the noun.
  3. Ask and state ages using approved number expressions.
  4. Use object pronouns in selected high-frequency patterns.
  5. Give simple physical and personality descriptions using stative predicates.
  6. Describe a family member or friend in connected sentences.
  7. Recognize that possessive pronouns follow the noun they modify in Yorùbá.
  8. Recognize and produce nasal vowels in family vocabulary.
  9. Begin to distinguish syllabic nasals from nasal vowels.
  10. Write a short, fully marked paragraph about family or a chosen social network.

Communication Scenario

You have just started attending a Yorùbá language evening class in Lagos. After the lesson, a classmate named Tólú invites you for tea and pulls out her phone to show you photos. "Ìdílé mi," she says — "My family." She points to each face and tells you who the person is and something about them. You want to do the same: to talk about the people who matter most to you, describe what they are like, and ask about hers in return. The language of family is among the most personally meaningful a learner can acquire — and it will reappear in nearly every chapter that follows.

  1. In English, jot down the names of four people in your family or social circle. What one word would you use to describe each person? Keep this list nearby — you will use it in the writing workshop.
  2. How do you think Yorùbá handles the difference between "my mother" and "the mother"? Make a prediction, then compare it with what you learn in the Grammar section.

In Yorùbá, many meanings expressed in English as adjectives ("tall," "clever") are expressed through stative predicates — verb-like forms that describe a state. The basic pattern is subject pronoun + predicate: ó ga means "he/she is tall" (literally, "he/she be-tall"). You will study this pattern in detail in the Grammar section. Do not place these forms before the noun as you would in English.

Pronunciation and Orthography

Nasal vowels

Yorùbá distinguishes oral vowels — vowels produced with air flowing only through the mouth — from nasal vowels, in which air also flows through the nose during the vowel itself. This is not the same as an English vowel followed by the consonant n; the nasality is a quality of the vowel, not a separate sound after it.

Several family vocabulary words in this chapter contain nasal vowels. Listen carefully to the difference between ọkọ (husband) — which has oral vowels — and words in which the vowel itself carries nasal resonance. Your instructor or audio model will demonstrate these contrasts.

Yorùbá wordEnglishNote on nasalization
ìdíléfamilyOral vowels throughout; practice for contrast
ọmọchildOral vowels; note the open-o quality of both vowels
ọ̀rẹ́friendOral vowels; note the open-o quality of ọ
ìyámotherVerify nasalization of the vowels with a qualified speaker
ọmọkùnrinboy; sonVerify nasal quality of any nasal vowels in this compound with a qualified speaker

The specific nasal vowels in each word must be verified by a qualified Yorùbá speaker or authoritative resource. Do not assume that any vowel followed by a written consonant n or m in a Yorùbá word is automatically nasal, and do not add English-style n sounds after a nasal vowel. Listen first; then produce.

Introduction to syllabic nasals

Yorùbá has syllabic nasals: sounds written as n or m that function as a full syllable on their own, capable of carrying a tone. They are not mere consonants closing a previous syllable, as in English. The syllabic nasal is its own tone-bearing unit.

Pronunciation Focus — Syllabic nasal versus nasal vowel

These two features must not be confused:

  • A nasal vowel is a full vowel (a, e, ẹ, i, o, ọ, u) pronounced with nasal airflow. The vowel carries the tone.
  • A syllabic nasal (n or m) is a nasal consonant that itself forms a complete syllable. It carries its own tone.

In the word pẹ̀ńsù from Chapter 2, the ń between the two vowels is a syllabic nasal — it forms its own syllable and carries a high tone (marked by the acute accent). You will encounter syllabic nasals in subject pronouns and grammatical particles throughout the course. For now, your goal is to distinguish the two types by listening carefully to model pronunciation.

Tone in possessive pronouns

The possessive pronouns introduced in this chapter are short forms that carry tone. Tone on pronouns is just as meaningful as tone on content words, and must not be omitted in writing or flattened into English-style stress in speech.

For example, the possessive pronoun meaning "my" is mi with mid tone — no tone mark is written because mid tone is normally unmarked. The possessive meaning "your" (singular familiar) is rẹ with mid tone, but note the underdot on the vowel. These details are part of accurate Standard Yorùbá and must be practiced alongside the meaning of each pronoun.

Grammar Instruction

4.1 Possessive pronouns — noun first, possessive second

Purpose: express whose something is by placing the possessive pronoun after the noun.

In English, possessive words come before the noun: my mother, her friend, their family. In Yorùbá, the possessive pronoun follows the noun:

Person Possessive pronoun Example English
1st singular (I/my) mi bàbá mi my father
2nd singular familiar (you/your) rẹ ìyá rẹ your mother
3rd singular (his/her/its) rẹ̀ ọ̀rẹ́ rẹ̀ his / her friend
1st plural (our) wa ìdílé wa our family
2nd plural / honorific singular yín bàbá yín your father (plural / honorific)
3rd plural (their) wọn ọmọ wọn their child

Tone note: The second-singular familiar possessive is rẹ (mid tone), while the third-singular possessive is rẹ̀ (low tone). Both use the open-e vowel (underdot required). This tonal distinction is essential: the difference between "your friend" (ọ̀rẹ́ rẹ) and "her friend" (ọ̀rẹ́ rẹ̀) is carried by the tone of the possessive pronoun alone. The underdot applies to both; neither may be written as plain re.

  • bàbá mi father + my my father
  • ẹgbọ́n rẹ older sibling + your (singular familiar) your older sibling
  • àbúrò rẹ̀ younger sibling + his/her his younger sibling / her younger sibling
  • ọ̀rẹ́ wa friend + our our friend
  • ìdílé yín family + your (honorific / plural) your family (said respectfully to one person, or to a group)
Grammar Note — Third-person singular possessive and gender

The third-person singular possessive pronoun rẹ̀ is used for people of any gender, as well as for animals and objects. There is no separate "his" and "her" in Yorùbá grammar. When context identifies the person's gender, your translation can reflect that: ẹgbọ́n rẹ̀ may be "his older sibling" or "her older sibling" depending on who you are talking about. Do not invent a gender-specific third-person possessive pronoun.

4.2 Subject pronouns used in description

Purpose: produce simple statements about what a person is like, using subject pronoun plus stative predicate.

Many meanings that English expresses through adjectives placed before a noun — "a tall man," "a clever student" — are expressed in Yorùbá through predicates: verb-like forms that follow the subject pronoun and describe a state or quality.

The basic pattern is:

Pattern
Subject pronoun + stative predicate
The predicate follows the subject. No additional copula like "is" is inserted.
Ó ga.  |  Wọ́n gbọ́n.  |  Ìyá mi dára.
He/she is tall.  |  They are clever.  |  My mother is good / beautiful.
  • Bàbá mi ga. father + my + is-tall My father is tall.
  • Ẹgbọ́n mi gbọ́n. older sibling + my + is-clever My older sibling is clever.
  • Àwọn ọmọ wa ṣírè. àwọn + children + our + are-playful Our children are playful.
  • Ọ̀rẹ́ mi dára. friend + my + is-good / is-fine / is-beautiful My friend is wonderful / lovely.
Grammar Note — Do not put the description before the noun

A common mistake at this stage is to translate "a clever student" by placing gbọ́n before the noun, producing something like *gbọ́n akẹ́kọ̀ọ́. This is not natural Standard Yorùbá. The description is made through a predicate in a complete sentence — Akẹ́kọ̀ọ́ náà gbọ́n (That student is clever) — or through constructions that will be introduced in later chapters. At this stage, always use the subject-pronoun-plus-predicate pattern.

4.3 Object pronouns — introduction in fixed patterns

Purpose: begin using object pronouns in high-frequency expressions, without yet requiring full paradigm control.

In Chapter 1 you learned subject pronouns. A subject pronoun does the action; an object pronoun receives it. The forms are different and must not be exchanged. At this stage, you will learn object pronouns in fixed, high-frequency expressions involving fẹ́ràn (to like, to love) and a small number of other common verbs. Full productive control of the object pronoun system is developed in Chapter 8.

Person Object pronoun Example with fẹ́ràn English
1st singular (me) mi Ó fẹ́ràn mi. He/she likes me.
2nd singular familiar (you) ọ Mo fẹ́ràn ọ. I like you.
3rd singular (him/her/it) ọ / un Mo fẹ́ràn un. I like him/her/it.
1st plural (us) wa Ó fẹ́ràn wa. He/she likes us.
2nd plural / honorific (you) yín A fẹ́ràn yín. We like you (all / respectfully).
3rd plural (them) wọn Mo fẹ́ràn wọn. I like them.

Important note on the third-person singular object: The third-person singular object form involves variation and vowel interaction that will be analyzed in detail in Chapters 8 and 9. At this stage, use the examples in the table as fixed models. Do not try to construct new patterns independently using these forms — learn the specific, verified expressions provided here. The full paradigm and its alternations will be taught when the communication domain requires them.

Grammar Note — Subject and object pronouns are not the same

Compare:

  • Mo fẹ́ràn ọ. — I like you. (Mo = subject; ọ = object)
  • O fẹ́ràn mi. — You like me. (o = subject; mi = object)

The subject form "I" is mo; the object form "me" is mi. These are not interchangeable. Similarly, the subject "you" is o (familiar singular), while the object "you" is ọ (note the underdot). In English, "you" works for both subject and object. In Yorùbá, subject and object forms are distinct and must be learned separately in their correct syntactic positions.

4.4 Expressing age

Purpose: ask and state how old someone is using the approved formulaic construction.

To ask someone's age, use the formulaic question:

  • Ọmọ ọdún mélòó ni ó? How old is he/she? (lit. Child of how many years is he/she?)

To answer, give the number of years:

  • Ọmọ ọdún mẹ́ta ni ó. He/she is three years old.
  • Ọmọ ọdún ogún ni mi. I am twenty years old.
  • Ọmọ ọdún mẹ́ẹ̀ẹ́dógún ni bàbá mi. My father is fifteen years old.

Use numbers from 1 to 20 that you learned in Chapter 2. Additional numbers will be introduced in Chapter 3. For now, practice age expressions within the 1–20 range.

4.5 Àwọn review — family groups

Purpose: apply the plurality marker from Chapter 2 to family vocabulary.

The plural marker àwọn works with family nouns exactly as it does with classroom nouns:

  • àwọn ọmọ mi my children
  • àwọn ọ̀rẹ́ rẹ your friends (singular familiar)
  • àwọn àbúrò wọn their younger siblings

When àwọn appears, it comes before the noun. The possessive pronoun still follows the noun, as always:

Language Building Block
noun + possessive pronoun
Possessive pronoun follows the noun — the opposite of English word order
bàbá mi  |  ìyá rẹ  |  àwọn ọmọ wa  |  ọ̀rẹ́ yín
my father  |  your mother  |  our children  |  your friend (honorific)
Language Focus

Subject pronouns, object pronouns, and possessive pronouns are three distinct systems in Yorùbá. In English, "you" serves as subject, object, and possessive (with just "your" added for possession). In Yorùbá, each function has its own form, and those forms differ in vowel quality, tone, and position in the sentence. Using a subject pronoun where an object pronoun is needed — or placing a possessive pronoun before the noun instead of after it — produces errors that will be noticed by any Yorùbá speaker.

A second key principle: many meanings English expresses through adjectives placed before nouns — "a tall man," "a clever child" — are expressed in Yorùbá through stative predicates in full sentences: Ọkùnrin náà ga. (That man is tall.) This is not simply a word-order difference: it reflects a fundamental difference in how the two languages categorize words and build descriptions. Learning to think in predicate patterns from the start will prevent persistent errors in later chapters.

Model Dialogue

Ipò: Ìjókòó lẹ́yìn kíláàsì, Tólú àti Adébísí ń sọ̀rọ̀ nípa ìdílé wọn. Tólú ń fi fótò hàn Adébísí. Ẹ̀gbọ́n Tólú kò sí ní Nàìjíríà; ó wà ní United Kingdom. Ìbáṣepọ̀: àwọn ọ̀rẹ́ kíláàsì.
Setting: After class. Tólú and Adébísí are talking about their families. Tólú is showing Adébísí photos. Tólú's older sibling is not in Nigeria; she is in the United Kingdom. Relationship: classmates and friends.

TÓLÚ Adébísí, jẹ́ kí n fihàn ọ ìdílé mi. Adébísí, let me show you my family. [formulaic expression — the full grammar of jẹ́ kí will be taught in Chapter 17]
ADÉBÍSÍ Àánú! Ẹ jọ̀wọ́ fihàn mi. How lovely! Please show me.

(Tólú fi fótò kan hàn — Tólú shows a photo.)

TÓLÚ Èyí ni bàbá mi. Ó ga àti lágbára. Orúkọ rẹ̀ ni Kúnlé. This is my father. He is tall and strong. His name is Kúnlé.
ADÉBÍSÍ Dáadáa. Ọmọ ọdún mélòó ni bàbá rẹ? Good. How old is your father?
TÓLÚ Ọmọ ọdún àádọ́ta ni ó. He is fifty years old. [àádọ́ta = 50; receptive only at this stage — do not produce independently]

(Tólú fi fótò kejì hàn — Tólú shows a second photo.)

TÓLÚ Èyí ni ìyá mi. Orúkọ rẹ̀ ni Àdùkẹ́. Ó dára gan-an. This is my mother. Her name is Àdùkẹ́. She is very beautiful / wonderful.
ADÉBÍSÍ Ìdí nì. Ṣé ó ni àwọn ọmọ mélòó? That's right. Does she have how many children? / How many children does she have?
TÓLÚ Ìyá mi ní àwọn ọmọ mẹ́ta — àwọn ọmọbìnrin èjì àti ọmọkùnrin ọ̀kan. Màá ṣe ọ̀kan nínú wọn. My mother has three children — two daughters and one son. I am one of them.
ADÉBÍSÍ Ìdí nì. Ẹgbọ́n rẹ wà ní Nàìjíríà bí? That's right. Is your older sibling in Nigeria?
TÓLÚ Ẹgbọ́n mi obìnrin wà ní United Kingdom báyìí. Mo fẹ́ràn un gan-an. My older sister is in the United Kingdom now. I love her very much.
ADÉBÍSÍ Ó yá. Ẹ̀ mi náà ní ẹgbọ́n ọkùnrin kan. Ó gbọ́n àti dára. I see. I also have an older brother. He is clever and wonderful.
TÓLÚ Ó wù mí. Ọjọ́ kan, fihàn mi fótò rẹ̀. That's nice to hear. One day, show me his photo. [formulaic — vocabulary only at this stage]

They are included as formulaic recognition items to make the dialogue sound natural. You are not expected to produce these independently. Focus your productive study on the items in the Grammar section.

Ìdílé Àmùṣà

Àtúnyẹ̀wò àtúnṣe — Editing checklist:

  • Every high tone is marked with an acute accent (´).
  • Every low tone is marked with a grave accent (`).
  • Mid-tone vowels have no accent mark — I did not add one.
  • Every ẹ, ọ, and ṣ is correctly written with an underdot.
  • Possessive pronouns follow the noun: bàbá mi, not *mi bàbá.
  • Descriptions use the subject-pronoun-plus-predicate pattern: Ó ga, not *ga ọkùnrin.
  • I used the correct object pronoun (not the subject pronoun) after fẹ́ràn.
  • I used àwọn before the noun, not after it, to mark plurality.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Family and kinship in Yorùbá communities

Among many Yorùbá-speaking families, the concept of family (ìdílé) extends well beyond the nuclear household of two parents and their children. Grandparents, aunts, uncles, and cousins may live together or in close proximity, share childcare responsibilities, and be addressed with the same terms of closeness — bàbá (father) and ìyá (mother) — that are used for biological parents. An uncle may be called bàbá, an aunt ìyá, depending on the relationship and community convention.

Kinship terms also frequently function as address forms showing respect to people who are not biological relatives. An older woman in the neighborhood may be called Ìyá, and an older man Bàbá, as a sign of respect and social closeness. This use does not imply a biological relationship and should not be translated literally in every case. Pay attention to context when you hear these terms used in conversation.

Family structures vary widely across Yorùbá-speaking communities, as they do across all communities. Avoid assuming that every family looks the same, or that Western nuclear-household assumptions apply universally. The vocabulary in this chapter covers the most broadly used Standard Yorùbá terms; many families will use additional or alternative terms specific to their region, religious community, or lineage.

Culture in Context — Asking about age and family

In many Yorùbá-speaking contexts, asking about someone's family — their parents, siblings, and children — is a natural and warm part of getting to know someone. Questions about family often signal genuine interest and care rather than intrusiveness. However, as with any community, context matters: a new acquaintance, a formal setting, or a community where family matters are private may call for more restraint.

Age is often discussed openly and may be referenced in terms of relative seniority: whether you are the ẹgbọ́n (older sibling) or àbúrò (younger sibling) in a relationship can affect how two people address each other, even if they are not biological siblings. Seniority carries social weight in many contexts, and the terms ẹgbọ́n and àbúrò are sometimes extended to any relationship where one person is meaningfully older than the other.

Regional Note — Kinship terminology variation

Yorùbá kinship vocabulary varies across communities in Nigeria, Benin, Togo, and the diaspora. Some communities use additional or alternative terms for relatives that do not appear in this chapter. For example, the word for "grandfather" may vary, and extended family terms for father's brother versus mother's brother may differ significantly from the simplified terms introduced here. The terms ìyá àgbà and bàbá àgbà (grandmother and grandfather) are widely understood in Standard Yorùbá; your own community or family may use more specific or different terms.

If you are learning Yorùbá to connect with a specific family or community, ask members of that community what terms they use. The forms in this textbook are productive standards for cross-community communication, not a complete guide to any one community's kinship system.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Placing the possessive before the noun

Error: Writing *mi bàbá to mean "my father," by direct transfer from English word order.

Correction: In Yorùbá, the possessive pronoun follows the noun: bàbá mi. This is the opposite of English order. Every time you want to say "my ___" or "your ___" in Yorùbá, think: noun first, possessive second. Practice this until it becomes automatic, because the same pattern applies to demonstratives, color words, and many other qualifiers.

Common Mistake — Using a subject pronoun as a possessive

Error: Writing *bàbá ó (using the subject pronoun ó) to mean "his/her father," instead of the possessive bàbá rẹ̀.

Correction: Subject pronouns and possessive pronouns are different forms. The third-person singular subject pronoun is ó; the possessive is rẹ̀ (with low tone and an underdot). These forms occur in different positions and cannot be substituted for each other. Review the pronoun table in section 4.1 and use the possessive form whenever you mean "his," "her," or "its."

Common Mistake — Placing a description word before the noun

Error: Writing *gbọ́n ẹgbọ́n mi (intended meaning: my clever older sibling), placing the stative predicate before the noun as though it were an English adjective.

Correction: Descriptions in Yorùbá are expressed through predicates in complete sentences: Ẹgbọ́n mi gbọ́n. (My older sibling is clever.) The stative predicate comes after the subject — not before the noun. Building descriptions as full predicate sentences is one of the most important patterns in Yorùbá and will recur throughout the course.

Common Mistake — Confusing second-singular and third-singular possessives

Error: Writing rẹ (mid tone) when the meaning is "his/her" rather than "your (familiar)," or conversely writing rẹ̀ (low tone) when addressing the person directly.

Correction: The second-person singular familiar possessive is rẹ (no tone mark = mid tone): used when saying "your ___" to a peer. The third-person singular possessive is rẹ̀ (grave accent = low tone): used when saying "his ___" or "her ___" about someone. Both use the open-e vowel with an underdot. The difference is entirely in tone. Practice these two forms in contrasting sentences until the tonal distinction is automatic.

Common Mistake — Omitting the underdot on possessive pronoun vowels

Error: Writing *re or *rè instead of rẹ or rẹ̀.

Correction: Both the second-singular and the third-singular possessive use the open-e vowel ẹ, which must always be written with an underdot. Omitting the underdot is an orthographic error, even in handwritten or informal notes. Practice writing rẹ and rẹ̀ from the start of this chapter until the underdot is automatic.

Ìpín II — Ìgbésí Ayé Ojoojúmọ́ · Unit II — Daily Life
8

Oúnjẹ àti Ohun Mímu

Food and Drink · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify and name common Yorùbá foods, drinks, and meal contexts.
  2. Ask and state what you want, like, or do not want to eat or drink.
  3. Use basic verbal negation with kò to negate common predicates and actions.
  4. Use general or habitual negation with kì í to say what you or others normally do not do.
  5. Recognize and use the formulaic negative identification pattern kì í ṣe in controlled examples.
  6. Distinguish subject pronouns from object pronouns in high-frequency patterns, and use third-person singular object forms in verified complete phrases.
  7. Make polite requests for food and drink in appropriate contexts.
  8. Express preferences using high-frequency preference predicates.
  9. Read a fully marked menu, food notice, or short meal description.
  10. Write a short, fully marked order, shopping note, or meal description.

Communication Scenario

You have been invited to eat with the family of your Yorùbá conversation partner, Adéwálé, at their home in Ìbàdàn. After greetings, his mother, Màmá Adéwálé, brings out several dishes and asks what you would like. You need to say what you want, express appreciation, ask what something is, and politely decline dishes you prefer not to eat. Later, you help Adéwálé write a quick shopping note for tomorrow's breakfast items. Everything in this chapter belongs to that evening and the note that follows it.

  1. Think about the last meal you ate. Can you name three of those foods or drinks in Yorùbá? If not yet, that is exactly what this chapter will give you.
  2. In English, you can say "I don't eat meat" or "I don't usually drink coffee." How might you signal that difference — a specific choice versus a general habit — in a language that approaches time and negation differently from English? Keep this question in mind as you work through the grammar section.
  3. What polite phrase would you want to have ready when someone offers you food you cannot eat or simply do not want? Write a note to yourself and check your answer once you reach the vocabulary section.

Pronunciation and Orthography Focus

Chapter 8 introduces a set of nasal vowels and tone sequences that are especially common in food and drink vocabulary. It also continues the cumulative work on distinguishing ẹ from e and ọ from o within new vocabulary.

Focus 1 — Nasal vowels in food vocabulary

Pronunciation Focus — Nasal vowels in context

Several food words in this chapter contain nasal vowels — vowels pronounced with air flowing through the nose. The key is to produce the nasalization on the vowel itself, not by adding a separate English-style n sound after the vowel.

Practise these words aloud. Hold the vowel and notice whether the airflow is nasal throughout, rather than ending with a released consonant:

  • ìyán — pounded yam. The final syllable has a nasal vowel at high tone.
  • àmàlà — amala. Note the low-mid-low tone pattern.
  • ọbẹ̀ — soup. Open-mid back vowel ọ, followed by open-mid front ẹ with low tone.
  • ẹran — meat. The vowel in the second syllable carries nasal quality and a mid tone. Do not add a full English -ran ending.
  • ẹwà — beans. Two-syllable word: open-mid ẹ (mid tone) + wà (low tone).

If you are unsure whether a vowel is nasal, do not guess from spelling alone. Listen to a qualified Yorùbá speaker or a verified audio recording. Nasalization must be verified for every new lexical item.

Focus 2 — Tone sequences on negation markers

Pronunciation Focus — Tone on kò and kì í

The two negation markers introduced in this chapter must be produced with their correct tones from the very first practice session. An incorrect tone on a grammatical particle changes the sentence's meaning or makes it ungrammatical.

  • kò — low tone on the vowel. This is the basic verbal negation marker. Say it at the bottom of your comfortable speaking pitch.
  • kì í — low tone on kì, high tone on í. This is the habitual or general negation marker. It is two syllables. Do not reduce it to one syllable or treat the high-tone í as a suffix on the low-tone kì.

Compare these two sentences aloud and hear the difference in both tone and meaning:

  • Mi ò jẹ ẹran. (low-high on mi ò) — I am not eating meat. / I did not eat meat.
  • Mi kì í jẹ ẹran. (kì low, í high) — I do not eat meat. (general habit)

The distinction in meaning maps broadly onto specific instances versus habitual patterns, but the grammatical contrast is carried by the choice of negation marker, not only by a time expression. Practice each form in complete sentences from the start.

Focus 3 — Object-pronoun tone

Pronunciation Focus — Object pronouns in context

Object pronouns introduced in this chapter are short, high-frequency forms that carry tone and interact with the sounds around them. Two are especially important for the food context:

  • mi (mid tone) — object form for first person: fún mi ní ___ (give me ___)
  • rẹ̀ (low tone) — object form for third person singular: used after a verb when referring back to a person already named

Do not substitute the subject pronoun mo or ó for these object forms. The forms are distinct and the difference is audible to any Yorùbá speaker. In the request phrase ẹ jọ̀wọ́, fún mi ní omi, the mid-tone mi is essential — it identifies you as the recipient.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter introduces three interconnected negative constructions and expands your object-pronoun knowledge. The negation patterns are not interchangeable. Each one serves a specific communicative function, and the wrong choice will change the meaning of your sentence or mark it as ungrammatical in context.

8.1 Basic verbal negation with kò

Purpose: say that something is not happening, did not happen, or is not the case — for specific statements.

The basic verbal negation marker kò is placed between the subject pronoun and the verb or predicate. The subject pronoun often contracts or changes form slightly in negative contexts. The most important first-person negative form is mi ò (a contraction of mo kò), which you should learn as a single unit before the full system is analyzed.

  • Mi ò fẹ́ ẹran. mi ò = 1st-person subject + kò (contracted) · fẹ́ = want · ẹran = meat I do not want meat. / I don't want meat.
  • Ó kò jẹ ẹyan. Ó = 3rd sg subject · kò = negation · jẹ = eat · ẹyan = pounded yam He/she is not eating pounded yam. / He/she did not eat pounded yam.
  • A kò mu ọtí. A = 1st pl subject · kò = negation · mu = drink · ọtí = alcohol We do not drink alcohol. / We are not drinking alcohol.
  • Oúnjẹ náà kò dára. Oúnjẹ náà = the food · kò = negation · dára = be good The food is not good.
Grammar Note — mi ò: the essential first-person contraction

In natural Standard Yorùbá speech and writing, the first-person singular negative is regularly expressed as mi ò rather than the full mo kò. Both forms exist, but mi ò is far more frequent in speech and in many written texts.

Learn mi ò as a productive unit. The full form mo kò helps you understand the grammar — subject + negation marker — but you will hear and use mi ò in natural communication. The two forms are given here so you can connect the analysis to the spoken reality.

Do not guess at contractions for other persons. Use the fully marked forms in the examples above and in the content ledger. Other contractions will be introduced and verified as they are needed.

8.2 Habitual or general negation with kì í

Purpose: say that something is not normally done, is not a general habit, or is not characteristically true.

Kì í is used when you are making a general or habitual statement — saying what someone does not usually do, or what is not characteristically true of someone or something. It is not used for a specific event in progress or in the immediate past.

  • Mi kì í jẹ ẹran. Mi = 1st sg subject · kì í = habitual negation · jẹ = eat · ẹran = meat I don't eat meat. (I never eat it / it's not my habit.)
  • Ó kì í mu kọfí lẹ́yìn oúnjẹ alẹ́. Ó = 3rd sg · kì í = habitual neg · mu = drink · kọfí = coffee · lẹ́yìn = after · oúnjẹ alẹ́ = dinner He/she doesn't drink coffee after dinner. (habit)
  • Wọ́n kì í jẹ àmàlà ní ọ̀sán. Wọ́n = 3rd pl · kì í = habitual neg · jẹ = eat · àmàlà = amala · ní ọ̀sán = at midday They don't eat amala at midday. (not their habit)
Grammar Note — kò versus kì í: which to choose

The distinction is about what kind of statement you are making, not about a single English tense:

  • Use kò when your statement is about a specific event, current situation, or particular instance: Mi ò fẹ́ ẹran báyìí — I don't want meat right now.
  • Use kì í when your statement is about what someone generally does not do, or what is not characteristically true: Mi kì í fẹ́ ẹran — I don't like meat (in general).

English sometimes uses "don't" for both meanings — context separates them. Yorùbá uses two grammatically distinct markers. Choosing the wrong one signals a different kind of negation than you intend, so it is important to think about what you mean before choosing.

Do not use kò and kì í interchangeably. They are not stylistic variants of each other.

8.3 Negative identification with kì í ṣe

Purpose: say what someone or something is not — the negative of the identification pattern with ni.

You learned identification with ni in Chapter 1: Orúkọ mi ni Títí (My name is Títí). The negative of this pattern uses kì í ṣe. Learn the following examples as productive formulaic units — the internal grammar of this construction will be expanded in later chapters.

  • Èyí kì í ṣe ẹran. Èyí = this · kì í ṣe = negative identification · ẹran = meat This is not meat.
  • Ó kì í ṣe ọ̀rẹ́ mi. Ó = he/she · kì í ṣe = negative identification · ọ̀rẹ́ mi = my friend He/she is not my friend.
  • Omi yìí kì í ṣe kọfí. Omi yìí = this water/liquid · kì í ṣe = negative ID · kọfí = coffee This is not coffee.

The expression kì í ṣe contains the habitual negation marker kì í plus the verb ṣe. The full grammar of this construction becomes productive in Chapter 8 for familiar nouns and identity expressions only. Do not yet generalize it freely to all contexts.

Grammar Note — Three rules for object pronouns
  1. Subject pronouns cannot replace object pronouns. Writing *Fún mo ní omi instead of Fún mi ní omi is an error that every Yorùbá speaker will notice. The forms are distinct in every person.
  2. The third-person singular object pronoun is rẹ̀ (low tone). Do not confuse it with the possessive rẹ (mid tone, used in ìwé rẹ — your book) or the subject ó (high tone). These three forms refer to the same person but in different grammatical roles.
  3. Object pronouns in this chapter are taught in verified complete phrases. Do not place object pronouns into new positions or sentence types that have not been modeled here. Their full distribution will be expanded in Chapter 9.

8.5 Preference predicates

Purpose: express liking, wanting, loving, and enjoying in a food context.

  • Mo fẹ́ ìyán. I want pounded yam. / I would like pounded yam.
  • Mo fẹ́ràn ọbẹ̀ egúsí. I like egusi soup. (habitual liking)
  • Ó nífẹ̀ẹ́ àmàlà. He/she loves amala.
  • Mo gbádùn oúnjẹ rẹ̀ gan-an. I really enjoyed his/her food.

Note that fẹ́ (want) and fẹ́ràn (like habitually) are different predicates. In a food request, fẹ́ expresses what you want right now. When discussing regular preferences, fẹ́ràn is more natural.

Language Focus

Yorùbá negation is construction-specific: there is no single all-purpose word for "not." English speakers often expect to find one Yorùbá equivalent of "don't" or "not" that works across every sentence type. Instead, Yorùbá uses different negation markers for different constructions — kò for specific verbal statements, kì í for general or habitual patterns, and kì í ṣe for identification. This is not unusual in the world's languages: it reflects the way Yorùbá encodes the distinction between what is happening right now and what is characteristically true. Each time you learn a new Yorùbá construction, you must also learn its specific negative partner. This chapter's three patterns are the beginning of a system that will expand in Chapter 11 (prohibitive má) and Chapter 14 (negative future).

Model Dialogue

Read the dialogue below carefully, then answer the comprehension questions. Notice how the speakers manage food offers, preferences, requests, and polite refusals. Also observe how the negation markers and object pronouns are used in natural context.

Ipò: Oúnjẹ alẹ́ ní ilé Adéwálé ní Ìbàdàn. Màmá Adéwálé, Adéwálé àti àlejò rẹ̀, Kẹ́mi, jókòó láti jẹ oúnjẹ. Kẹ́mi jẹ́ akẹ́kọ̀ọ́ Yorùbá tó máa ń gbé ní àgbègbè mìíràn ní Nàìjíríà.
Setting: Dinner at Adéwálé's home in Ìbàdàn. Màmá Adéwálé, Adéwálé, and his guest Kẹ́mi sit down to eat. Kẹ́mi is a Yorùbá learner who usually lives in another part of Nigeria.

MÀMÁ Ẹ káàbọ̀, Kẹ́mi. Ẹ jókòó. Oúnjẹ ti ṣetan. Welcome, Kẹ́mi. Please sit down. The food is ready.
KẸMI Ẹ ṣé, Màmá. Ó dára jù. Ìrísí oúnjẹ náà dára gan-an. Thank you, Màmá. That is very good. The food looks wonderful.
MÀMÁ Ẹ fẹ́ ìyán bí? Tàbí ẹ fẹ́ àmàlà? Do you want pounded yam? Or do you want amala?
KẸMI Ẹ jọ̀wọ́, mo fẹ́ ìyán. Mo nífẹ̀ẹ́ ìyán púpọ̀. Please, I would like pounded yam. I really love pounded yam.
MÀMÁ Ó yá. Ẹ fẹ́ ọbẹ̀ egúsí tàbí ọbẹ̀ ilá? Alright. Do you want egusi soup or okra soup?
KẸMI Ẹ jọ̀wọ́, fún mi ní ọbẹ̀ egúsí. Mo fẹ́ràn rẹ̀ jù ọbẹ̀ ilá lọ. Please give me egusi soup. I like it more than okra soup.
MÀMÁ Ó dára. Ẹ fẹ́ ẹran bí? Good. Do you want meat?
KẸMI Ẹ ṣé, Màmá, ṣùgbọ́n mi kì í jẹ ẹran. Mo fẹ́ ẹja díẹ̀ dípò rẹ̀, tí ó bá wà. Thank you, Màmá, but I don't eat meat. I would like a little fish instead, if it is available.
MÀMÁ Bẹ́ẹ̀ ni, ẹja wà. Àwọn ẹja tuntun ni. Ẹ jẹ́, ẹ jẹ́. Yes, there is fish. They are fresh fish. Please eat, please eat.
ADÉWÁLÉ Kẹ́mi, ẹ fẹ́ ohun mímu? Omi bí? Tàbí omi ọ̀sàn? Kẹ́mi, do you want a drink? Water perhaps? Or orange juice?
KẸMI Ẹ jọ̀wọ́, fún mi ní omi. Mi kì í mu ọtí, àti mi ò fẹ́ omi ọ̀sàn ní alẹ́. Please give me water. I don't drink alcohol, and I don't want orange juice in the evening.
MÀMÁ Ẹ mu omi yìí. Ẹ jẹ oúnjẹ yín dáadáa. Drink this water. Eat your food well.

(Wọ́n jẹ oúnjẹ. — They eat.)

KẸMI Màmá, mo gbádùn oúnjẹ yín jù jù. Ọ̀pẹ lọ́wọ́ yín. Màmá, I really enjoyed your food so much. I am very grateful to you.
MÀMÁ Ẹ kú oúnjẹ! Ẹ máa wá sí ilé wa léraléra. Well done on eating! Come to our home often.
Grammar Note — Two expressions to notice

Mo fẹ́ràn rẹ̀ jù ọbẹ̀ ilá lọ (I like it more than okra soup) uses a comparison construction with jù ... lọ that will be taught productively in Chapter 18. Learn this sentence as a useful formulaic expression now. The internal grammar will be analyzed later.

Ẹ kú oúnjẹ! is a conventional expression said after eating — literally "well done on eating." It belongs to the set of situational Yorùbá greetings that acknowledge a shared activity. Like ẹ kú iṣẹ́ (well done on your work) and others you will meet later, these expressions mark social awareness and courtesy. Learn this one as a formulaic unit.

Also notice Kẹ́mi's use of mi kì í jẹ ẹran (habitual: I don't eat meat) contrasted with mi ò fẹ́ omi ọ̀sàn ní alẹ́ (specific: I don't want orange juice this evening). The choice between kì í and kò (contracted to ò) reflects exactly the distinction in Grammar section 8.2.

Àwọn ìbéèrè nípa ọ̀rọ̀ àdáhùnṣe — Comprehension questions:

  1. Kẹ́mi fẹ́ oúnjẹ wo? (What food does Kẹ́mi want?)
  2. Kí ni ọbẹ̀ tí Kẹ́mi fẹ́ràn? (Which soup does Kẹ́mi prefer?)
  3. Kẹ́mi jẹ ẹran bí? Ìdí nìyí? (Does Kẹ́mi eat meat? Why or why not?)
  4. Kẹ́mi mu ohun mímu wo? (What does Kẹ́mi drink?)
  5. Find two sentences in the dialogue that use kì í. Write them out and explain what each one means.
  6. Find one sentence that uses the object pronoun mi and one that uses the object pronoun rẹ̀. Write each sentence and identify the role of the pronoun.
  7. What does Màmá say after the meal is finished? What social function does this expression perform?

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Food hospitality

Offering food and drink to guests is a widely valued practice in many Yorùbá-speaking households and communities. The expression Ẹ jẹ́, ẹ jẹ́ (literally "eat, eat" — meaning "please help yourself / eat freely") is a common and warm invitation at the table. Guests who decline food entirely — particularly without explanation — may inadvertently create an impression of discomfort or displeasure. A polite explanation using kì í jẹ ___ (I don't eat ___) allows the host to offer an alternative and maintains the warm social atmosphere of the meal.

The expression Ẹ kú oúnjẹ (well done on eating) that Màmá uses in the model dialogue is one of a set of Yorùbá expressions that acknowledge a shared activity or mark the completion of a task. These expressions reflect a social awareness that extends beyond the immediate exchange of words.

Culture in Context — Food diversity across Yorùbá-speaking areas

The foods in this chapter are widely eaten across Yorùbá-speaking areas of Nigeria, but food cultures vary by family, region, religion, economic context, and individual preference. Àmàlà is particularly associated with Ìbàdàn and much of the interior of Yorùbáland, while ẹ̀bà is ubiquitous across southwestern Nigeria. Mọ́ìn-mọ́ìn and akàrà are common breakfast and street foods in many settings. Ìrẹsì àti ẹwà (rice and beans) is eaten widely in schools, offices, and families across different socioeconomic contexts.

Do not assume that all Yorùbá-speaking families eat the same dishes or that the foods listed here are the only options. Benin and Togo have their own food vocabulary and practices, and urban contexts in Lagos or Cotonou include food from many other Nigerian and West African culinary traditions. The vocabulary in this chapter gives you a productive foundation; later chapters and personal cultural inquiry will deepen it.

Regional Variation — Food terms across settings

Some of the food vocabulary in this chapter has both established Standard Yorùbá forms and widely used alternative expressions. Two examples worth noting:

  • ìrẹsì (rice) is a loanword from English that is fully integrated into Standard Yorùbá. In some contexts and generations, iresi may appear in informal writing without tone marks. In this textbook, the marked form ìrẹsì is always used.
  • ẹwà (beans) is the Standard Yorùbá form used throughout this course. In some informal or regional usage, the word for beans may appear differently. Accept the form ẹwà as the core productive form.

For Yorùbá communities in Benin and Togo, some food names may differ or carry slightly different associations. These differences are receptive notes — the productive standard for this textbook remains Standard Nigerian Yorùbá.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using kò where kì í is needed (or vice versa)

Error: Writing *Mi ò jẹ ẹran when you mean "I never eat meat" (a general habit), or writing *Mi kì í fẹ́ omi báyìí when you mean "I don't want water right now" (a specific refusal).

Correction: Mi kì í jẹ ẹran — I don't eat meat (general, habitual). Mi ò fẹ́ omi báyìí — I don't want water right now (specific instance). Ask yourself: am I making a statement about a habit or a general truth? Use kì í. Am I making a statement about a specific moment or event? Use kò (or mi ò for first person).

Common Mistake — Using a subject pronoun as an object pronoun

Error: Writing *Fún mo ní omi (give I water) instead of Fún mi ní omi (give me water), or *Mo fún ó ní ẹja instead of Mo fún rẹ̀ ní ẹja (I gave him/her fish).

Correction: The subject pronoun appears before the verb. The object pronoun appears after the verb or in a request frame after fún. The forms are different: mo / mi, o / ọ, ó / rẹ̀. A Yorùbá speaker will understand your meaning but will hear the error. Make the correct form automatic through the practice exercises in Section 8.4.

Common Mistake — Omitting the underdot on ọbẹ̀, ẹran, ẹja, ẹwà

Error: Writing *obe, *eran, *eja, or *ewa instead of the correct forms with underdotted vowels.

Correction: ọbẹ̀, ẹran, ẹja, ẹwà. All four of these high-frequency food words contain the letter ẹ or ọ with an underdot. The underdot is part of the letter, not a decoration. Omitting it changes the vowel quality and is always an orthographic error. Use the editing checklist after every writing task until marking underdots is automatic.

Common Mistake — Confusing fẹ́ (want) with fẹ́ràn (like habitually)

Error: Using *Mo fẹ́ ẹran nígbà gbogbo when the meaning is "I always like meat" (habitual preference), or using *Mo fẹ́ràn omi báyìí when the meaning is "I want water right now."

Correction: Mo fẹ́ omi báyìí — I want water now (immediate desire). Mo fẹ́ràn ẹran — I like meat (habitual preference). The difference between wanting something in the moment and liking something in general is grammatically significant in Yorùbá. Choose the predicate that matches your meaning.

Common Mistake — Losing the tone on the particle kì í

Error: Producing kì í with both syllables at the same pitch, or writing it as *ki i without tone marks.

Correction: kì í must be written and pronounced with a low tone on kì and a high tone on í. The tonal contrast between the two syllables is part of the grammatical form. Without it, the particle becomes unrecognizable. Practice this particle in complete sentences — Mi kì í jẹ ẹran — until the pitch movement is automatic. The full marked form is always: kì í.

Answer Key

Practice — Choosing the right negation

  1. Màmá mi kì í jẹ ẹja. — My mother never eats fish. (habitual)
  2. Oúnjẹ náà kò dára. — The food is not good. (specific statement)
  3. Omi yìí kì í ṣe wàrà. — This is not milk. (identity negation)
  4. Mi ò fẹ́ omi báyìí. — I don't want water right now. (specific / contracted kò)
  5. Wọ́n kì í gbádùn ẹwà. — They don't enjoy beans. (habitual)

Practice — Object pronoun selection

  1. Ẹ jọ̀wọ́, fún mi ní omi.
  2. Mo fún rẹ̀ ní oúnjẹ àárọ̀.
  3. Olùkọ́ wa fún wa ní ìwé.
  4. Bàbá mi fún yín ní àmàlà àti ọbẹ̀.
  5. A fún wọn ní ẹja.

Practice — Affirmative and negative preferences

  1. Affirmative: Mo fẹ́ ẹwà báyìí. / Negative (specific): Mi ò fẹ́ ẹwà báyìí.
  2. Affirmative: Ó fẹ́ràn ọtí. / Negative (habitual): Ó kì í mu ọtí.
  3. Affirmative: A fẹ́ràn ìyán. / Negative (habitual): A kì í fẹ́ràn ìyán. — Accept any well-formed habitual negative.
  4. Affirmative: Ó fẹ́ kọfí. / Negative (specific): Ó kò fẹ́ kọfí báyìí.

Dialogue — Comprehension

  1. Kẹ́mi fẹ́ ìyán. (Kẹ́mi wants pounded yam.)
  2. Kẹ́mi fẹ́ràn ọbẹ̀ egúsí jù ọbẹ̀ ilá lọ. (Kẹ́mi prefers egusi soup over okra soup.)
  3. Rárá, Kẹ́mi kì í jẹ ẹran. Ó kì í jẹ ẹran — ó ní ìdí rẹ̀ ti ara rẹ̀. (No, Kẹ́mi does not eat meat — it is a general habit.)
  4. Kẹ́mi mu omi. (Kẹ́mi drinks water.)
  5. Sentences with kì í: Mi kì í jẹ ẹran (I don't eat meat — general habit); Mi kì í mu ọtí (I don't drink alcohol — general habit). Accept both sentences with correct glosses.
  6. mi (object, first person): Ẹ jọ̀wọ́, fún mi ní ọbẹ̀ egúsí — mi is the recipient of the action (give me). rẹ̀ (object, third person): Mo fẹ́ràn rẹ̀ jù ọbẹ̀ ilá lọ — rẹ̀ refers back to ọbẹ̀ egúsí already mentioned.
  7. Màmá says Ẹ kú oúnjẹ — this is a situational courtesy expression acknowledging the completion of eating, functioning like a social affirmation at the end of a shared meal.

  • Àṣàbí — Favourite: ìyán àti ọbẹ̀ ilá with ẹja; eats akàrà and bread for breakfast; does not particularly like ẹwà but eats it at lunch because there is no other option.
  • Dọlápọ̀ — Favourite: mọ́ìn-mọ́ìn made by mother; does not eat ọ̀gẹ̀dẹ̀ in the morning; four children enjoy the mọ́ìn-mọ́ìn; drinks water or orange juice in the morning.

Bonus: In Passage 1, Mi kì í jẹ ẹran (habitual: I don't eat meat) versus Kọfí kì í dára fún mi (habitual: coffee is not good for me — a general truth). Both use kì í; for a specific instance, the speaker would use kò. In Passage 3, Dọlápọ̀'s mother makes mọ́ìn-mọ́ìn every day; the signal of regularity is máa ń ṣe — the habitual marker máa ń from Chapter 7.

Reading — Comprehension

  1. Ìyán àti ọbẹ̀ egúsí costs ₦1,500. The cheapest item is omi at ₦100.
  2. Àmàlà àti ọbẹ̀ ilá (₦1,200) + ẹja tuntun (₦700) + omi (₦100) = ₦2,000. In Yorùbá: ẹgbẹ̀rún èjì nàírà (or accept: ẹgbẹ̀rún méjì).
  3. Ìyá Adéwálé fẹ́ ṣe akàrà àti mọ́ìn-mọ́ìn ní àárọ̀ ọjọ́ Àìkú. (Adéwálé's mother plans to make akara and moin-moin on Sunday morning.)
  4. Adéwálé kò mọ̀ bóyá Kẹ́mi jẹ ẹran tàbí bẹ́ẹ̀ kọ̀. Ó béèrè kí Kẹ́mi sọ fún un ní àárọ̀. (He does not know if Kẹ́mi eats meat. He asks Kẹ́mi to tell him in the morning.)
  5. Sentence with kì í: Tí o bá kì í jẹ ohun kan — If you don't eat something (general — referring to a dietary habit). Kì í is used rather than kò because this refers to a habitual pattern (something Kẹ́mi generally does not eat), not a specific refusal in the moment.
  6. Máa ń ṣe is the habitual aspect construction: máa ń (habitual marker) + ṣe (make/do). Introduced in Chapter 7 as the marker for regular or characteristic activity.

Errors in the text: (1) Mi o → Mi ò (grave accent on the contracted negative vowel); (2) je → jẹ (underdot missing); (3) ki i → kì í (both tones missing); (4) mu is correct but ọti → ọtí (high tone missing); (5) Fún mo → Fún mi (subject pronoun used instead of object pronoun); (6) ẹ jọwọ → ẹ jọ̀wọ́ (tone marks missing); (7) Ọbẹ́ → ọbẹ̀ (wrong tone — should be low, not high); (8) egusi → egúsí (tone marks missing). Chapter Review — Apá C

  1. Correct forms: ọbẹ̀, ẹja, ẹran, àmàlà, mọ́ìn-mọ́ìn, ẹwà, ìyán.
  2. kò = low tone on the vowel, produced at the bottom of comfortable speaking pitch. kì í = two syllables: low tone on kì, then high tone on í, producing a rising pitch pattern.
  3. A model answer: In ẹran, the vowel carries nasal quality throughout. Producing it as though it ends with a separate English -n consonant (like "ehh-rahn") adds an extra sound that is not part of the Yorùbá word. Instead, hold the nasal quality on the vowel itself and end the syllable there — the nasalization is on the vowel, not a following consonant.

Looking Ahead

Ohun tí o lè ṣe báyìí — What you can do now

You can now discuss food, drink, meals, and preferences in Yorùbá — accepting and declining with the correct negative construction for the situation, making polite requests using object pronouns, and reading and writing common food vocabulary with full diacritics. You have learned that Yorùbá uses different negation markers for specific events (kò), general habits (kì í), and identity negation (kì í ṣe), and that this distinction matters every time you speak or write. These abilities are essential for all practical transactions ahead.

Ilé-Ẹ̀kọ́, Iṣẹ́, àti Agbára

School, Work, and Ability · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Name common school subjects, jobs, and workplaces using fully marked Yorùbá vocabulary.
  2. State what you study or what your occupation is using the productive pattern [subject/job] ni mi, developed from the identification pattern first learned in Chapter 2.
  3. Describe ongoing or habitual study and work activities using ń in a new domain, distinguishing "right now" from general routine through context.
  4. Express ability using lè plus a simple verb complement, and negate ability with N kò lè.
  5. Name languages and skills you can perform, using sọ, kọ, and ka productively.
  6. Recognize and use appropriate tone in longer school- and work-related words.
  7. Understand and complete a simple interview form about school and work.
  8. Write a short, fully marked description of your studies or work, and carry out an interview exchange with a partner.

Communication Scenario

Kúnlé has recovered from the illness described in Chapter 12. Dúpé is preparing a short class project about her friends' studies and daily work, and she wants to interview Kúnlé about what he is learning at university, what he can already do, and what kind of work he hopes to do in the future. You will follow their conversation, then prepare to talk about your own studies, work, and abilities in the same way.

  1. Before you open this chapter, think of three things you already know how to do — in any language — and three things you are currently learning. You will need this same information in Yorùbá by the end of the chapter.
  2. Recall the identification pattern X ni Y from Chapter 2 (Orúkọ mi ni Kúnlé — "My name is Kúnlé"). This chapter extends the same pattern to occupations and roles.
  3. Recall the progressive marker ń, first introduced in Chapter 6 for daily schedules and developed through Chapters 8–12. This chapter applies it to a new domain: study and work.

kọ (to write) and kọ́ (to learn/study) are a tone-based minimal pair. Confusing them changes the meaning of a sentence — see Pronunciation Focus 1.

Pronunciation and Orthography Focus

Focus 1 — The kọ / kọ́ minimal pair

Pronunciation Focus — Tone distinguishes "write" from "learn"

kọ (mid tone, unmarked) means "to write." kọ́ (high tone, marked with an acute accent) means "to learn" or "to study." These two verbs are pronounced identically except for tone, and confusing them changes the meaning of a sentence:

  • Mo lè kọ Yorùbá. — I can write Yoruba.
  • Mo lè kọ́ Yorùbá. — I can learn Yoruba.

Practice both forms slowly and at natural speed until the tone contrast is clear and consistent.

Focus 2 — Syllable segmentation in longer school and work words

Pronunciation Focus — Breaking longer words into syllables

Several words in this chapter's vocabulary are longer than most words learners have met so far. Segment each into syllables before attempting the full word at natural speed:

  • akẹ́kọ̀ọ́ — a-kẹ́-kọ̀-ọ́ (student)
  • ìdánwò — ì-dán-wò (exam)
  • ilé-iṣẹ́ — i-lé-i-ṣẹ́ (workplace, company)
  • oníṣòwò — o-ní-ṣò-wò (trader)

Keep each syllable short and even. Do not let English stress patterns pull extra weight onto one syllable at the expense of the others.

Focus 3 — Tone in job and school words for practice

Say each word aloud and mark the tone pattern (H = high, M = mid, L = low) for each syllable.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

13.1 Naming a role or occupation: [subject/job] ni mi

Purpose: state what you are — a student, a teacher, a particular kind of worker — by developing the identification pattern ni first learned in Chapter 2.

In Chapter 2, you learned Orúkọ mi ni Kúnlé (My name is Kúnlé), where ni identifies one thing as another. This chapter develops the same pattern to identify a person's role or occupation. The identifying noun comes first, followed by ni and the person being identified:

  • Akẹ́kọ̀ọ́ ni mi. student + identify + me I am a student.
  • Olùkọ́ ni í. teacher + identify + him/her He/she is a teacher.
  • Dókítà ni bàbá mi. My father is a doctor. (reviewed dókítà from Ch. 12, possessive mi from Ch. 3)
  • Ẹnjinia ni ẹ̀gbọ́n mi. My older sibling is an engineer.
Grammar Note — Same pattern, new domain

The structure [identifying noun] ni [person] does not change between Chapter 2 and this chapter — only the vocabulary domain changes, from names to occupations. This is an example of spiral development: a pattern you already control is extended to new, useful content rather than replaced with a new rule.

13.2 Ongoing and habitual study or work: ń in a new domain

Purpose: say what you are studying right now, or what you generally do for work, using the progressive marker ń you have used since Chapter 6.

ń can describe an action happening right now or a routine, habitual action, depending on context — you have already seen both readings in earlier chapters. This chapter applies the same marker to study and work:

  • Mo ń kọ́ ìṣirò àti sáyẹ́nsì. I + progressive + learn + math + and + science I study math and science. (general, ongoing enrollment)
  • Ó ń ṣiṣẹ́ ní ọ́fíìsì. He/she works at an office. (habitual, routine work)
  • A ń kọ́ ìṣirò ní ilé-ẹ̀kọ́ báyìí. We are studying math at school right now. (immediate, in-progress reading)
Grammar Note — Context, not a new particle, controls the meaning

Yorùbá does not require a separate habitual marker distinct from the ongoing marker at this stage. The word báyìí (right now) or a time expression can signal an immediate reading; the absence of such a marker, together with the general nature of the statement, favors a habitual or routine reading. Learners should rely on context rather than expecting a separate grammatical form for "always" versus "right now."

13.3 Expressing ability: lè + verb

Purpose: say what you can do, using lè followed directly by a bare verb.

lè (to be able to, can) is placed before the main verb, with no additional marking on the verb itself. This is a simple complement structure: lè takes a following verb, optionally followed by an object or an adverb such as dáadáa (well):

  • Mo lè sọ èdè méjì. I + can + speak + language + two I can speak two languages.
  • Ó lè kọ Yorùbá dáadáa. He/she can write Yoruba well.
  • A lè ka ìwé Yorùbá. We can read Yoruba books.

To negate ability, combine the familiar negation particle kò with lè. You have already seen this combination receptively in Chapter 12's reading passage (N kò lè lọ sí iṣẹ́). This chapter treats it as a fully productive frame:

  • N kò lè sọ Faransé. I + not + can + speak + French I cannot speak French.
  • Kò lè kọ́ ìtàn ní ọdún yìí. He/she cannot study history this year.
Grammar Note — kò and lè keep their separate jobs

kò still functions exactly as it has since Chapter 2: it negates a declarative statement. lè still expresses ability. Combined, kò lè negates the ability itself — "cannot" — rather than negating the following verb directly. Do not insert a second negative word before the main verb; kò appears once, immediately before lè.

Language Focus

This chapter does not introduce new particles — it extends two systems you already control into a new communicative domain. The identification pattern X ni Y (Chapter 2) now names roles and occupations. The progressive marker ń (Chapter 6, developed through Chapters 8–12) now describes study and work. The one genuinely new element is lè, a simple modal verb that takes a bare verb complement and negates with the familiar particle kò. Recognizing which parts of a new topic are actually new, and which parts are familiar tools applied to new vocabulary, will help you learn faster as the course continues.

Language Building Block
(N kò) + lè + [verb] (+ object) (+ dáadáa)
Productive ability frame · lè takes a bare verb directly · kò negates the whole ability, placed immediately before lè
Mo lè sọ èdè Yorùbá dáadáa.
I can speak Yoruba well.

Model Dialogue

Read the dialogue carefully. Notice how Dúpé uses the ni pattern and ń to ask about Kúnlé's studies, and how Kúnlé uses lè to describe his abilities. Comprehension questions follow.

Ipò: Kúnlé ti yá báyìí. Dúpé ń ṣe iṣẹ́ ilé-ẹ̀kọ́ kan nípa àwọn ọ̀rẹ́ rẹ̀.
Setting: Kúnlé has recovered. Dúpé is doing a school project about her friends.

DÚPÉ Kúnlé, ǹjẹ́ o ti yá? Kúnlé, are you well now?
KÚNLÉ Bẹ́ẹ̀ni, mo ti yá. Ẹ ṣé. Yes, I am well now. Thank you.
DÚPÉ Ó dára! Kí ni o ń kọ́ ní yunifásítì? Great! What are you studying at university?
KÚNLÉ Mo ń kọ́ ìṣirò àti sáyẹ́nsì. Akẹ́kọ̀ọ́ ni mi ní kíláàsì ọdún kejì. I am studying math and science. I am a second-year student.
DÚPÉ Ǹjẹ́ o lè sọ èdè mìíràn yàtọ̀ sí Yorùbá? Can you speak another language besides Yoruba?
KÚNLÉ Bẹ́ẹ̀ni, mo lè sọ Gẹ̀ẹ́sì dáadáa. Mo tún ń kọ́ Faransé díẹ̀, ṣùgbọ́n n kò lè sọ ọ dáadáa síbẹ̀. Yes, I can speak English well. I'm also learning a little French, but I can't speak it well yet.
DÚPÉ Kí ni o fẹ́ ṣe lẹ́yìn ilé-ẹ̀kọ́? What do you want to do after school?
KÚNLÉ Mo fẹ́ ṣe iṣẹ́ ẹnjinia. Bàbá mi ń ṣe iṣẹ́ yìí báyìí, mo sì fẹ́ kọ́ ọ lọ́wọ́ rẹ̀. I want to do engineering work. My father does this work now, and I want to learn it from him.
DÚPÉ Ó yanu! Ǹjẹ́ o lè ràn mí lọ́wọ́ pẹ̀lú iṣẹ́ ilé-ẹ̀kọ́ mi ní ìṣirò? Wonderful! Can you help me with my math homework?
KÚNLÉ Bẹ́ẹ̀ni, mo lè ràn ọ́ lọ́wọ́ ọ̀la. Yes, I can help you tomorrow.
Grammar Note — Three patterns working together in the dialogue
  1. Akẹ́kọ̀ọ́ ni mi. — the ni identification pattern, developed from Chapter 2.
  2. Mo ń kọ́ ìṣirò àti sáyẹ́nsì. — ongoing/habitual study with ń, developed from Chapter 6.
  3. N kò lè sọ ọ dáadáa síbẹ̀. — negated ability with kò lè, this chapter's new frame.

Àwọn ìbéèrè nípa ọ̀rọ̀ àdáhùnṣe — Comprehension questions:

  1. Kí ni Kúnlé ń kọ́ ní yunifásítì? (What is Kúnlé studying at university?)
  2. Ǹjẹ́ Kúnlé lè sọ Faransé dáadáa? (Can Kúnlé speak French well?)
  3. Kí ni Kúnlé fẹ́ ṣe lẹ́yìn ilé-ẹ̀kọ́? (What does Kúnlé want to do after school?)
  4. Ta ni ń ṣe iṣẹ́ ẹnjinia báyìí? (Who currently does engineering work?)
  5. Wa gbolohùn kan nínú ìjíròrò tí ó lo kò lè. Kọ ọ̀ jáde, sọ ìtumọ̀ rẹ̀. (Find one sentence in the dialogue that uses kò lè. Write it out and give its meaning.)

Writing Workshop

Chapter 13 has two writing products: a short school/work description and a completed interview form. Both must use Standard Yorùbá orthography with full tone marks and underdots.

Àpín A — Nípa Èmi (About me)

Write a short description (50–70 words) about your own studies or work. Your description must include:

  • An identification statement using ni (e.g., Akẹ́kọ̀ọ́ ni mi or a job title).
  • At least one sentence describing what you study or do using ń.
  • At least two abilities using lè, including one negative ability with N kò lè.
  • A sentence about what you want to do in the future, using fẹ́ (reviewed from Ch. 7 and Ch. 12).

Àpín B — Fọ́ọ̀mù Ìfọ̀rọ̀wánilẹ́nuwò (Interview form)

Using the model form in the Reading section as a template, complete the form for yourself or an invented person. Then write two additional interview entries for classmates, using invented but plausible details.

  1. List your facts. Note your role or occupation, subjects or duties, languages you can speak, and one future goal.
  2. Draft each sentence. Write each fact as a separate short sentence using the correct pattern — ni for identity, ń for activity, lè for ability.
  3. Check tone consistency. Pay special attention to kọ versus kọ́, and to tone on longer words such as akẹ́kọ̀ọ́.
  4. Apply the editing checklist. Verify every tone mark, underdot, and pattern choice before submitting.

Àtúnṣe àtúnyẹ̀wò — Editing checklist:

  • The identification pattern places the identifying noun first, followed by ni and the person: Akẹ́kọ̀ọ́ ni mi, not *Mi ni akẹ́kọ̀ọ́.
  • kọ (write) and kọ́ (learn) are marked with the correct tone in every instance.
  • lè is followed directly by a bare verb, with no extra marking on the verb.
  • kò appears once, immediately before lè, when negating an ability.
  • Every ẹ, ọ, and ṣ carries its underdot.
  • High and low tones are marked on every required syllable. Mid tones are left unmarked.
  • Every sentence begins with a capital letter and ends with appropriate punctuation.

Speaking Practice

Àdáwọlé àkọ́kọ́ — Controlled practice: subject and job check

Working with a partner, take turns asking and answering about study or work. Speaker A asks Kí ni o ń kọ́? (What are you studying?) or Kí ni iṣẹ́ rẹ? (What is your job?). Speaker B answers with an identification statement and one activity statement using ń. Switch roles after five exchanges each.

Every exchange must include:

  1. An opening question using kí ni.
  2. An identification statement using ni.
  3. At least one activity statement using ń.

Iṣẹ́ pẹ̀lú alábàápàdé — Interview role-play

One partner interviews the other about school and work, following the model dialogue and interview form. The interviewer asks about subjects or duties, abilities, and future plans. The interviewee answers using at least two abilities with lè, including one negative ability. If no partner is available, perform both roles aloud using the model dialogue as a guide, then create a new version with different details.

  • Interviewer asks: Kí ni o ń kọ́? and Ǹjẹ́ o lè sọ èdè mìíràn?
  • Interviewee answers with an identification statement and at least two abilities.
  • Interviewee includes one statement about a future goal using fẹ́.

Fún àwọn tí ń kọ́ nìkọ̀ọ̀kan — Solo alternative: recorded self-report

Record yourself describing your own studies or work in one to two minutes of continuous Yorùbá. Include an identification statement, at least two activity statements with ń, and at least two abilities with lè — one positive and one negative. Listen back and check:

  • Did you correctly distinguish kọ from kọ́ by tone?
  • Did you place kò once, immediately before lè, in your negative ability statement?
  • Did you keep the tone on longer words such as akẹ́kọ̀ọ́ and ilé-iṣẹ́ clear and even?
  • Did your identification statement place the identifying noun before ni?

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Learning within the family and community

Alongside formal schooling, many Yorùbá-speaking families place strong value on learning a trade or profession directly from a relative or community member, as Kúnlé hopes to learn engineering-related skills from his father. This kind of hands-on, relationship-based learning is viewed as a normal and respected complement to classroom education, not a substitute for it.

Respect for teachers, both formal and informal, is an important part of everyday interaction. Learners should expect the honorific ẹ and other respect markers you learned in Chapters 3 and 11 to be used routinely when speaking with or about a teacher.

Culture in Context — A wide range of occupations in daily conversation

Ordinary conversation about work in Yorùbá-speaking communities often spans a wide range of occupations, from formal professions such as dókítà, amòfin, and ẹnjinia to widely respected occupations such as agbẹ̀ (farmer) and oníṣòwò (trader). No single occupation is treated as more worthy of discussion than another, and it is common and polite to ask someone directly about their work as part of ordinary conversation.

Regional Variation — Naming modern professions

Some modern professions, such as ẹnjinia (engineer), are commonly referred to using adapted loanwords across most Yorùbá-speaking regions, while more established professions such as agbẹ̀ (farmer) and oníṣòwò (trader) have long-standing Yorùbá terms. Formal or written Yorùbá occasionally offers alternative, less loanword-dependent terms for modern professions; these formal alternatives are introduced in later volumes.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Reversing the identification pattern

Error: Writing *Mi ni akẹ́kọ̀ọ́ when you mean "I am a student," placing the pronoun before ni.

Correction: Akẹ́kọ̀ọ́ ni mi. — The identifying noun comes first, followed by ni and then the person, exactly as in Chapter 2's Orúkọ mi ni Kúnlé. Keep the identifying noun in first position.

Common Mistake — Confusing kọ and kọ́

Error: Writing *Mo lè kọ Yorùbá when you mean "I can learn Yoruba," omitting the tone mark that distinguishes "learn" from "write."

Correction: Mo lè kọ́ Yorùbá. — The high tone on the final syllable of kọ́ is required to mean "learn." Without it, the sentence means "I can write Yoruba," which is a different, equally correct sentence with a different meaning. Always check which verb you intend before writing.

Common Mistake — Doubling the negative in an ability statement

Error: Writing *N kò lè kò sọ Faransé, placing a second kò before the main verb.

Correction: N kò lè sọ Faransé. — kò appears once, immediately before lè. It negates the ability as a whole; the main verb that follows lè does not take its own separate negative marker.

Common Mistake — Flattening tone on longer words

Error: Pronouncing or writing *akekoo instead of akẹ́kọ̀ọ́, dropping both the underdots and the tone marks on a longer word.

Correction: akẹ́kọ̀ọ́ requires the underdotted ẹ and ọ as well as its full high-low-high tone pattern. Longer words are not exempt from tone and diacritic rules; segment the word by syllable (Pronunciation Focus 2) before writing or pronouncing it in full.

Performance Task

Iṣẹ́ aṣeyọrí — Abala 13

Ìròyìn ẹ̀kọ́ àti iṣẹ́, àti ìfọ̀rọ̀wánilẹ́nuwò — A school/work report and interview

The Chapter 13 Performance Task has two parts. All spoken and written Yorùbá must use full tone marks and underdots.

Part 1 — Oral self-report (1–2 minutes):
Describe your studies or work to a partner. Your description must include:

  1. An identification statement using ni.
  2. At least two activity statements using ń.
  3. At least two ability statements using lè, including one negative ability with N kò lè.
  4. One statement about a future goal using fẹ́.
  5. Correct, clear tone throughout, including the kọ/kọ́ contrast if either verb is used.

Part 2 — Completed interview form and written description (50–70 words for the description):
Complete an interview form for yourself or an imagined person, then write a short description covering the same information. Your description must include at least one identification statement, one activity statement, and two ability statements. Use fully marked Standard Yorùbá throughout.

Assessment note: The spoken task is evaluated on task completion, comprehensibility, correct use of the identification and ability patterns, correct placement of kò before lè, and intelligible tone, including the kọ/kọ́ contrast. The written task is evaluated on accuracy of the identification, activity, and ability patterns, orthographic completeness (underdots and tone marks), and overall clarity.

  2. Ó ń ṣiṣẹ́ ní ilé-iṣẹ́ kan.
  3. A ń kọ́ ìtàn ní ilé-ẹ̀kọ́ báyìí.

Practice — Ability with lè

  1. Ability: Mo lè sọ Yorùbá. Negative: N kò lè sọ Yorùbá.
  2. Ability: Ó lè kọ ìwé. Negative: Kò lè kọ ìwé.
  3. Ability: A lè ka ìtàn. Negative: A kò lè ka ìtàn.

Dialogue — Comprehension

  1. Ó ń kọ́ ìṣirò àti sáyẹ́nsì. (He is studying math and science.)
  2. Rárá, n kò lè sọ Faransé dáadáa síbẹ̀. (No, he cannot speak French well yet.)
  3. Ó fẹ́ ṣe iṣẹ́ ẹnjinia. (He wants to do engineering work.)
  4. Bàbá Kúnlé ni ń ṣe iṣẹ́ ẹnjinia báyìí. (Kúnlé's father currently does engineering work.)
  5. Accept any of: N kò lè sọ ọ dáadáa síbẹ̀. — Meaning: "I cannot speak it well yet." kò lè negates the ability to speak French well.

Listening — Comprehension table

  • Exchange 1: Subject: history and language; Ability: can speak English and Yoruba.
  • Exchange 2: Job: teacher, works at a school; Ability: cannot teach math, teaches history only.
  • Exchange 3: Status: student; Ability/goal: wants to do lawyer's work after school.

Bonus: (1) English and Yoruba. (2) Teacher; cannot teach math. (3) Wants to become a lawyer after school.

Reading — Comprehension

  1. Ó ń kọ́ ìṣirò àti sáyẹ́nsì. (He is studying math and science.)
  2. Ó lè sọ Yorùbá àti Gẹ̀ẹ́sì dáadáa. (He can speak Yoruba and English well.)
  3. Ẹnjinia ni bàbá rẹ̀, ó sì ń ṣiṣẹ́ ní ilé-iṣẹ́ kan. (His father is an engineer and works at a company.)
  4. Olùkọ́ ni ìyá rẹ̀; ó ń kọ́ àwọn ọmọdé ní èdè Yorùbá. (His mother is a teacher; she teaches the children the Yoruba language.)
  5. Accept any of: Bàbá mi jẹ́ ẹnjinia. or Ìyá mi jẹ́ olùkọ́. (Receptive note: jẹ́ appears here as a natural variant; the productive target for this chapter remains the ni pattern.)

Looking Ahead

Ohun tí o lè ṣe báyìí — What you can do now

You can name common school subjects, jobs, and workplaces, state your role or occupation using the ni identification pattern, describe ongoing or habitual study and work with ń, and express ability and inability using lè and N kò lè. You can also understand and complete a simple interview form and describe your own studies or work in speech and writing.

Abala 14: Ìfẹ́ràn, Àyànfẹ́, àti Eré Ìnàjú — Likes, Preferences, and Hobbies

Chapter 14 moves from studies and work to leisure: what you like, dislike, and prefer, and how you spend your free time. You will learn fẹ́ràn (to like/love) and fẹ́ alongside dára for simple comparisons, name common hobbies and entertainment activities, and expand your noun phrases with descriptive adjectives and the plural marker àwọn. The writing target for Chapter 14 is a short recommendation expressing your preferences to a friend.

6

Ní Ilé

Around the Home · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Name the main rooms and areas of a home in Yorùbá with correct tone marks and underdots.
  2. Identify common furniture and household objects and use them in sentences.
  3. State that something or someone exists or is present using wà.
  4. State where something or someone is located using wà ní with a place expression.
  5. Express what someone has or owns using high-tone ní.
  6. Use near demonstratives (èyí, àwọn èyí) and far demonstratives (ìyẹn, àwọn ìyẹn) to point out objects.
  7. Build expanded noun phrases in the correct order: noun → possessive pronoun → color or descriptor → numeral.
  8. Write a fully marked 80–100-word paragraph describing a room or home.
  9. Produce accurate tone, underdots, and nasal vowels in household vocabulary.

Communication Scenario

Yẹ́túndé has just moved into a new flat in Ìbàdàn. Her cousin Kúnlé is visiting for the first time. Yẹ́túndé is giving Kúnlé a tour — pointing out each room, telling him where things are, and explaining what she has and what she still needs. At the end of the visit, Kúnlé admires a colorful rug and asks Yẹ́túndé about the furniture in the sitting room. Everything they need to have that conversation is in this chapter.

  1. Think about how you would give someone a tour of your own home or room in English. What are the first five things you would point out? Jot them down in English, then see which you already know how to say in Yorùbá from Unit I vocabulary.
  2. In Chapter 3 you learned that location is expressed with wà ní. In Chapter 5 you reviewed that possession uses high-tone ní. How confident do you feel telling the difference? This chapter will give you extensive practice with both in a home context.
  3. Chapter 6 opens Unit II. What new communication goals do you have for this unit compared with Unit I?

Pronunciation Focus

Tone in location phrases and expanded noun phrases

Pronunciation Focus — Phrase-level tone sequences

Up to now you have practised tone on individual syllables and single words. In Chapter 6 you will begin connecting multiple words into location phrases and expanded noun phrases. Maintaining correct tone across word boundaries requires attention to three things:

  • Each word keeps its own tones. When you say wà ní yàrá ìjókòó, the tone of wà (mid), ní (high), yàrá (low-high), and ìjókòó (low-high-high) must each remain distinct. Do not let the tones of one word blur into the next.
  • Short grammatical words carry tone too. The location particle ní is high-toned. The existence verb wà is mid-toned. Neither word is "unstressed" in the English sense. Do not reduce them to a quick murmur.
  • Demonstratives follow the noun and keep their tones. Àga (low-mid) followed by èyí (low-high) produces a low-mid | low-high sequence: do not raise the first syllable of àga to match the high of èyí.

Practise these phrases aloud, pausing after each slash to confirm you have produced the tones correctly:

  • Tábìlì wà / nínú yàrá oúnjẹ. — The table is in the dining room.
  • Àga èyí / wà ní / yàrá ìjókòó. — This chair is in the sitting room.
  • Ibùsùn mi / wà ní / yàrá sùn. — My bed is in the bedroom.

Nasal vowels in household vocabulary

Pronunciation Focus — Nasal vowels

Several household words in this chapter contain nasal vowels — vowels pronounced with air flowing simultaneously through the mouth and the nose. In Standard Yorùbá, nasalization is a property of the vowel itself, not a separate nasal consonant that follows it. Compare:

  • ibùsùn — the first syllable i and the vowel in sùn are produced with nasal airflow. Do not pronounce this as three entirely oral syllables followed by an English n.
  • nínú — both vowels carry nasal quality. The spelling n at the start of nínú and between the two vowels is part of the approved orthographic convention for this word, not a separate English-style consonant sound between two oral vowels.

Listen carefully to any verified audio model or qualified speaker pronunciation of these words. When practising aloud, try letting the nasal sound pervade the whole vowel rather than pinching your nose at the end.

Grammar

6.1 Existence and location — wà and wà ní

Purpose: express that something or someone is present, exists, or is found at a particular place.

The verb wà (mid tone) performs two closely related functions. First, it asserts that something exists or is present without specifying where. Second, combined with high-tone ní and a place expression, it locates that thing. You encountered both uses in earlier chapters; this chapter makes them productive in the context of the home.

  • Firiji wà níbí. subject + wà + location word The fridge is here. / There is a fridge here.
  • Ibùsùn mi wà ní yàrá sùn. subject noun phrase + wà + ní + place My bed is in the bedroom.
  • Àwọn àga méta wà ní yàrá ìjókòó. plural noun phrase + wà + ní + place Three chairs are in the sitting room.
  • Omi wà nínú agolo. subject + wà + nínú + container Water is in the tin / can.
Grammar Note — wà versus ní: two different constructions

These two constructions look similar but do different jobs:

  • wà ní + place — locates something: Tábìlì wà ní yàrá oúnjẹ. (The table is in the dining room.)
  • subject + ní + thing owned — expresses possession: Yẹ́túndé ní tábìlì tuntun. (Yẹ́túndé has a new table.)

The key test: ask yourself whether the sentence answers Where is X? or Does someone have X? Location needs wà before the high-tone ní. Possession needs only the high-tone ní directly after the subject.

6.2 Possession — ní (high tone)

Purpose: state that someone has or owns something.

  • Kúnlé ní ibùsùn tuntun. subject + ní (high) + object owned Kúnlé has a new bed.
  • Ẹbí wa ní ilé nla. subject noun phrase + ní (high) + noun phrase Our family has a big house.
  • Mo ní àwọn àga méjì nínú yàrá mi. 1st-person subject + ní (high) + expanded noun phrase + locative I have two chairs in my room.

At this stage the negative of possession is introduced in controlled fixed patterns only. Productive negative possession is scheduled for Chapter 8. For now, focus on the affirmative pattern.

6.3 Demonstratives — pointing out objects near and far

Purpose: distinguish a nearby object from one further away using demonstrative words that follow the noun.

  • Àga èyí dára. noun + near demonstrative + quality predicate This chair is good / nice.
  • Ibùsùn ìyẹn tóbi. noun + far demonstrative + quality predicate That bed is big.
  • Àwọn fèrèsé èyí ga. àwọn + noun + near plural demonstrative + predicate These windows are tall / high.
  • Mo fẹ́ àwọn àga ìyẹn. subject + verb + àwọn + noun + far plural demonstrative I want those chairs.
Grammar Note — Demonstrative order: noun first, then demonstrative

In English, demonstratives come before the noun: "this chair," "those tables." In Yorùbá the order is reversed: the noun comes first, then the demonstrative: àga èyí, àwọn tábìlì ìyẹn. This continues the general principle you learned in Unit I — in Yorùbá, qualifiers follow the noun rather than preceding it.

Do not write *èyí àga or *ìyẹn tábìlì. These orders are incorrect in standard productive Yorùbá.

6.4 Expanded noun phrases — building longer descriptions

Purpose: describe objects using multiple qualifiers placed in the correct order after the noun.

When you want to describe an object with more than one qualifier — for example, "my two blue chairs" — Yorùbá organizes the qualifiers in a specific post-noun order. The general sequence for productive use at this stage is:

Language Building Block
noun + possessive pronoun + descriptor + numeral
Head noun first · then whose · then what quality · then how many
Àwọn àga mi méjì wà ní yàrá ìjókòó.
My two chairs are in the sitting room.
  • tábìlì mi tuntun noun + possessive + descriptor (new) my new table
  • àwọn ìkòtò wọn mẹ́ta àwọn + noun + possessive + numeral their three wardrobes
  • àwọn àga pupa méjì àwọn + noun + color + numeral two red chairs
  • àwọn àwọ̀ mi funfun mẹ́rin àwọn + noun + possessive + descriptor + numeral my four white bowls / plates

Not every combination of qualifiers will always appear in one noun phrase in natural speech. These patterns represent the approved order when multiple qualifiers are used together. You will encounter further conventions in later chapters.

Language Focus

Yorùbá uses three distinct constructions where English often uses just one word: "is." Compare: "The table is in the kitchen" — location, requiring wà ní. "My sister is a doctor" — identification, requiring ni (mid tone). "Kúnlé has a new bed" — possession, requiring ní (high tone) without wà. Whenever you want to say something "is" something in Yorùbá, pause and ask: am I locating, identifying, or expressing possession? Each answer leads to a different construction. Building this habit now will prevent the most common errors learners make in this chapter and throughout the course.

Model Dialogue

Read the dialogue below carefully. Notice how Yẹ́túndé uses wà ní to locate things, ní to express what she has, and demonstratives to point things out. The dialogue also recycles identification (ni), pronouns, and quality predicates from Unit I.

Ipò: Ọ̀sán ọjọ́ Àárọ̀ ní àpáta tuntun Yẹ́túndé ní Ìbàdàn. Kúnlé (ará ẹbí Yẹ́túndé, ọ̀rẹ́ rẹ̀ pẹ̀lú) wọlé fún igba àkọ́kọ́. Yorùbá ìpele àkọ́bẹ̀rẹ̀.
Setting: Afternoon at Yẹ́túndé's new flat in Ìbàdàn. Kúnlé (her cousin and friend) enters for the first time. Beginner-level Standard Yorùbá.

KÚNLÉ Ẹ káàsán, Yẹ́túndé. Ilé rẹ dára gan-an! Good afternoon, Yẹ́túndé. Your home is really nice!
YẸ́TÚNDÉ Káàsán, Kúnlé. Ẹ ṣé. Ẹ wọlé. Èyí ni yàrá ìjókòó wa. Good afternoon, Kúnlé. Thank you. Come in. This is our sitting room.
KÚNLÉ Àwọn àga rẹ dára. Àga èyí tóbi àti rọ̀. Your chairs are nice. This chair is big and soft.
YẸ́TÚNDÉ Bẹ́ẹ̀ ni. Mo ní àwọn àga àgbàdo méta ní yàrá ìjókòó. Àpẹtẹ ìyẹn wà lórí ilẹ̀ níwájú tábìlì. Yes. I have three sofas in the sitting room. That rug is on the floor in front of the table.
KÚNLÉ Àpẹtẹ ìyẹn ẹlẹ́wà. Tá ni o rà á? That rug is beautiful. Where did you buy it?
YẸ́TÚNDÉ Mo rà á ní ọjà Bòdìjà. Jẹ́ kí n mú ọ wo ilé ìdáná pẹ̀lú. I bought it at Bòdìjà market. Let me take you to see the kitchen too.

(Wọ́n lọ sí ilé ìdáná. — They go to the kitchen.)

KÚNLÉ Ilé ìdáná rẹ tóbi! Firiji wà níbí àti ẹrọ ìgbóná pẹ̀lú. Your kitchen is big! A fridge is here and a cooker too.
YẸ́TÚNDÉ Bẹ́ẹ̀ ni. Àwọn ìkòkò mi wà ní ẹ̀gbẹ́ ẹrọ ìgbóná. Mo ní àwọn àwọ̀ pupa mẹ́fà pẹ̀lú. Yes. My pots are beside the cooker. I also have six red bowls.
KÚNLÉ Omi wà nínú firiji bí? Is there water in the fridge?
YẸ́TÚNDÉ Bẹ́ẹ̀ ni! Omi àti ohun mímu mìíràn wà nínú firiji. Ẹ jọ̀wọ́ jókòó, Kúnlé. Ó dára. Yes! Water and other drinks are in the fridge. Please sit down, Kúnlé. Good.
Grammar Note — Two expressions to notice

Jẹ́ kí n mú ọ wo (Let me take you to see) contains the suggestion pattern jẹ́ ká in a first-person singular form and the serial verbs mú and wo. These patterns will be taught productively in Chapters 17 and 11 respectively. Treat the whole expression as a formulaic unit for now.

Tá ni o rà á? (Where / from whom did you buy it?) contains a completed-event clause that will be formally taught in Chapter 16. Use it here as a recognition-only expression. The object pronoun á in rà á is a contracted form; full object-pronoun forms and their contractions will be consolidated in Chapter 8.

Àwọn ìbéèrè nípa ọ̀rọ̀ àdáhùnṣe — Comprehension questions:

  1. Kúnlé wọ yàrá wo àkọ́kọ́? (What room did Kúnlé enter first?)
  2. Àwọn àga mélòó ni Yẹ́túndé ní ní yàrá ìjókòó? (How many sofas does Yẹ́túndé have in the sitting room?)
  3. Àpẹtẹ wà níbo? (Where is the rug?)
  4. Yẹ́túndé rà àpẹtẹ rẹ̀ níbo? (Where did Yẹ́túndé buy her rug?)
  5. Àwọn ohun wo ni ó wà nínú ilé ìdáná Yẹ́túndé? Kọ ohun méta. (What things are in Yẹ́túndé's kitchen? Write three.)
  6. Find two sentences in the dialogue that use ní for possession. Write them out and translate them.
  7. Find one sentence that uses wà for existence or location. Write it and state whether it expresses location at a place, location inside something, or simple presence.

Writing Workshop

The writing product for Chapter 6 is a fully marked home description of 80–100 words. You may describe your actual home, an imagined home, or the home of someone you know. Follow the steps below carefully.

  1. Choose your home and plan. Decide which rooms you will describe and pick at least four objects to mention. For each object, decide where it is (location) or whether someone has it (possession). Jot down your plan in English or Yorùbá before you write.
  2. Draft your description. Begin with a general sentence about the home — how big it is, where it is, or who lives there. Then describe at least two rooms. Use wà ní or wà nínú for at least three location sentences. Use high-tone ní for at least two possession sentences. Include one demonstrative (èyí or ìyẹn).
  3. Expand one noun phrase. In at least one sentence, use a noun phrase with two qualifiers — for example, a possessive and a numeral, or a color and a numeral. Check that the qualifiers appear after the noun in the correct order.
  4. Mark all tones and underdots. Go through every vowel. High tone gets an acute accent. Low tone gets a grave accent. Mid tone stays unmarked. Add underdots to every ẹ, ọ, and ṣ. Remember: the underdot is part of the letter, not optional decoration.
  5. Count and revise. Count your words. If you have fewer than 80, add one more sentence. Check against the editing checklist before you submit.

Àtúnṣe àtúnyẹ̀wò — Editing checklist:

  • I used wà ní or wà nínú for location — not ní alone.
  • I used high-tone ní directly after the subject for possession — not wà ní.
  • I used mid-tone ni (no accent) for identification — saying what something is.
  • Demonstratives appear after the noun, not before it.
  • Qualifiers in expanded noun phrases appear after the noun in the order: possessive → descriptor → numeral.
  • Every high tone carries an acute accent. Every low tone carries a grave accent. Mid-tone vowels have no accent mark.
  • Every ẹ, ọ, and ṣ has an underdot.
  • Tone marks appear correctly on underdotted vowels: ẹ̀, ẹ́, ọ̀, ọ́.
  • Subject and possessive pronouns are the correct forms.
  • Every sentence begins with a capital letter and ends with appropriate punctuation.
  • My description is between 80 and 100 words.

Àfojúsùn àfikún — Optional extension: Write a 30–40-word description of one room only, imagining you are writing the text for a flat advertisement like the one in the Reading section. Try to make it sound appealing to a prospective tenant.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Yorùbá homes and household arrangements

The word ilé in Yorùbá carries meanings that extend beyond the physical structure. It can mean a house, a home, a household, a compound, or even a lineage — the phrase ará ilé (people of the house) can refer to immediate family, extended kin, or members of a shared compound. The layout of homes varies considerably across Yorùbá-speaking communities:

  • Àpáta (a flat or apartment) is common in urban centers like Lagos, Ìbàdàn, and Ìlọrin, especially for people living away from extended family.
  • Ilé agbo (a compound house) is a larger structure shared by multiple family units, each with their own rooms but sharing a communal courtyard or sitting area. This arrangement remains common in older parts of Ìbàdàn, Abẹ́òkúta, and towns across Yorùbáland.
  • Ilé ara (a private house) with a compound, a veranda, and a fenced yard is a common aspiration for families who have built their own home.

These different arrangements affect vocabulary, spatial descriptions, and who is referred to when someone speaks of people "at home." Avoid assuming that every speaker lives in the same kind of dwelling or shares the same household structure.

Culture in Context — Entering someone's home

When visiting a Yorùbá-speaking household, greeting practices are important. A visitor typically greets loudly from outside — at the gate or entrance — before entering. The expression Ẹ káàbọ̀ (You are welcome) is the conventional response from those inside, signaling that the visitor may proceed. Entering without calling out, or walking straight into an inner room without an invitation, can be considered impolite.

Notice that in the model dialogue, Yẹ́túndé says Ẹ wọlé (Come in) only after greeting Kúnlé — an invitation that formally opens the door to the rest of the home. This small exchange models a common pattern of hospitality across many Yorùbá-speaking settings, though practices vary by region, relationship, and household.

Regional Variation — Household vocabulary

Some household words vary across regions and generations:

  • ilé ìdáná (kitchen) is the Standard Yorùbá form used in this textbook. In some households and dialects, you may also hear yàrá ìdáná or simply ìdáná. These forms should be recognized but the standard form used productively.
  • ilé ìwẹ̀ (bathroom) and yàrá ìtura are both in broad use. Contexts differ: ilé ìwẹ̀ often emphasizes bathing; yàrá ìtura can cover the toilet facility as well. In formal or written contexts, use the form that best fits the specific facility being named.
  • firiji — borrowed from English — is the widely used everyday term across Nigeria. Some speakers use ẹrọ ìtọ́jú oúnjẹ (lit. food-preservation device) in more formal contexts, but firiji is standard for productive use in this course.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using ní for location without wà

Error: Writing *Tábìlì ní yàrá oúnjẹ to mean "The table is in the dining room."

Correction: Tábìlì wà ní yàrá oúnjẹ. Location requires the existence verb wà before high-tone ní. The construction subject + ní (without wà) expresses possession — "The table has something" — which is not the intended meaning here. Whenever you are answering the question Níbo ni X wà? (Where is X?), the answer must include wà.

Common Mistake — Placing demonstratives before the noun

Error: Writing *èyí àga (this chair) or *ìyẹn tábìlì (that table).

Correction: Àga èyí and tábìlì ìyẹn. In Yorùbá the demonstrative follows the noun: noun first, then èyí or ìyẹn. This is the same post-noun principle that applies to possessive pronouns and other qualifiers. English adjective-before-noun order does not transfer to Yorùbá demonstratives.

Common Mistake — Putting qualifiers in the wrong order

Error: Writing *àwọn méjì àga mi or *àwọn àga méjì mi to mean "my two chairs."

Correction: Àwọn àga mi méjì. The approved order is: àwọn (if plural marker is used) + noun + possessive + numeral. The possessive pronoun comes before the numeral. Placing the numeral before the possessive, or placing either qualifier before the noun, is an error.

Common Mistake — Omitting tone marks on wà and ní

Error: Writing *Ibùsùn wà ni yàrá sùn (no accent on the second ni).

Correction: Ibùsùn wà ní yàrá sùn. The high-tone accent on ní is not optional. Without it, the sentence uses mid-tone ni (identification) rather than high-tone ní (location / possession), which produces a grammatically incorrect or misleading sentence. Both wà and ní must carry their correct tones in every sentence.

Common Mistake — Treating àwọn as mandatory before every plural noun

Error: Adding àwọn before every noun that would be plural in English, even when context and a numeral already make plurality clear — or omitting àwọn in sentences where no other plural marker is present.

Correction: Àwọn is used to signal plurality explicitly when no numeral or other quantifier makes plurality obvious from context, or when the sentence requires clear group reference. When a numeral is present — àga mẹ́ta (three chairs) — àwọn may or may not appear depending on the communicative context. For this chapter, include àwọn when you are grouping objects without an immediately following numeral, and use your judgment when a numeral is already present. You will develop greater precision in later chapters.

Answer Key

Practice — Location, possession, or identification?

  1. Firiji wà ní ilé ìdáná. — The fridge is in the kitchen. (location)
  2. A ní àwọn àga mẹ́rin. — We have four chairs. (possession)
  3. Yàrá èyí ni yàrá ìjókòó. — This room is the sitting room. (identification)
  4. Ìkòtò mi wà ní yàrá sùn mi. — My wardrobe is in my bedroom. (location)
  5. Àbíké ní ìkòkò nla. — Àbíké has a big pot. (possession)
  6. Àwọ̀ funfun ìyẹn ni ti Yẹ́túndé. — That white bowl is Yẹ́túndé's. (identification — accept reasonable alternatives)

Practice — Building noun phrases

  2. àwọn ibùsùn pupa mẹ́ta — three red beds
  3. àwọn ìkòtò yín méjì — your two wardrobes (honorific)
  4. àpẹtẹ wọn bùláù — their blue rug (note: no numeral here, so just noun + possessive + color)

Dialogue — Comprehension

  1. Kúnlé wọ yàrá ìjókòó àkọ́kọ́. (Kúnlé entered the sitting room first.)
  2. Yẹ́túndé ní àwọn àga àgbàdo mẹ́ta. (Yẹ́túndé has three sofas.)
  3. Àpẹtẹ wà lórí ilẹ̀ níwájú tábìlì. (The rug is on the floor in front of the table.)
  4. Yẹ́túndé rà àpẹtẹ rẹ̀ ní ọjà Bòdìjà. (She bought it at Bòdìjà market.)
  5. Accept any three of: firiji, ẹrọ ìgbóná, àwọn ìkòkò, àwọn àwọ̀ pupa mẹ́fà, omi, ohun mímu.
  6. Possession sentences: Mo ní àwọn àga àgbàdo méta ní yàrá ìjókòó and Mo ní àwọn àwọ̀ pupa mẹ́fà pẹ̀lú. (Accept either; both use ní for possession.)
  7. Accept any of: Firiji wà níbí àti ẹrọ ìgbóná pẹ̀lú (presence/existence — wà níbí); Àwọn ìkòkò mi wà ní ẹ̀gbẹ́ ẹrọ ìgbóná (location beside something); Omi àti ohun mímu mìíràn wà nínú firiji (location inside something).

Listening — Comprehension table

  • Àríkẹ́ — house (ilé); rooms: yàrá ìjókòó (mentioned explicitly); objects: àwọn àga méfà, tábìlì funfun tuntun, ìmọ́lẹ̀ méjì, àwọn ìkòtò méjì

Looking Ahead

Ohun tí o lè ṣe báyìí — What you can do now

You can name the main rooms and objects of a home, state where things are using wà ní and wà nínú, express what someone has using high-tone ní, point out near and far objects with demonstratives, and build expanded noun phrases in the correct order. You can also give and understand a short home tour in Yorùbá, and write a fully marked 80–100-word home description. These location and possession patterns are the foundation for much of Unit II communication.

Abala 7: Àwọn Ìṣe Ojoojúmọ́ — Daily Routines

Chapter 7 moves from the spaces of the home to what happens inside them throughout the day. You will learn to describe activities that are happening right now using the progressive aspect marker ń, and activities that happen regularly using the habitual marker máa ń. You will also learn to tell the time, name the days of the week, and sequence a simple daily routine. Chapter 7 introduces one of Yorùbá's most important grammatical contrasts — progressive versus habitual viewpoint — and shows how subject pronouns and aspect particles work together to express meaning that English handles through tense endings.

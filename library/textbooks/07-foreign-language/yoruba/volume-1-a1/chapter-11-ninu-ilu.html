Getting Around Town · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify common buildings, public services, and forms of transportation using fully marked Yorùbá vocabulary.
  2. Ask where a place is located using the approved direction-question pattern.
  3. Give step-by-step directions using affirmative imperatives, the prepositions sí and láti, and sequenced instructions.
  4. Use the prohibitive má to give a negative instruction and distinguish it from the habitual particle máa.
  5. Use productive serial verb constructions for motion, direction, and result — including lọ, wá, ká, yí, and gba in approved patterns.
  6. State origin and destination using láti and sí.
  7. Recognize common public signs and brief written travel instructions.
  8. Write a fully marked set of step-by-step directions in Standard Yorùbá orthography.

Communication Scenario

A colleague named Tópé has just arrived at the motor park on the edge of town. She calls you on the phone. She needs to get from the motor park to the local government health clinic, but she does not know the area well. You live nearby and know the route. Your task is to describe the route clearly in Yorùbá — naming landmarks, streets, and turning points — so that Tópé can find the clinic without getting lost.

  1. Before you open this chapter, write down five expressions in Yorùbá that you already know and could use in a phone conversation: a greeting, a question about location, a command, and two position or movement expressions. Use vocabulary from Chapters 1–10.
  2. Think about the kinds of places a person might pass through when crossing a town — a market, a school, a filling station, a junction. Which of these do you already know how to say in Yorùbá? List them, then check against the vocabulary in this chapter.
  3. In English, a typical direction includes several movements joined by "then": "Turn left, then go straight, then pass the church on your right." How might Yorùbá handle this sequence? Think about what you know of serial verb constructions from Chapter 9. Keep your prediction in mind as you study Section 11.5.

Pronunciation and Orthography Focus

Focus 1 — Tone in commands and prohibitions: má versus máa

Pronunciation Focus — Distinguishing má (prohibitive) from máa (habitual/future)

This chapter introduces the prohibitive particle má. It looks almost identical in writing to the habitual/future particle máa that you learned in Chapters 7 and 14. The difference is one syllable and one tone, but the meaning contrast is significant:

  • má — one syllable, high tone — do not (negative command)
  • máa — two syllables, high-high — habitually / will (habitual or future marker)

Compare these two sentences aloud:

  • Má lọ. — Do not go.
  • Ó máa ń lọ. — He/she habitually goes.

The prohibitive má is always a single high-tone syllable and always precedes a verb directly. It is never followed by ń. Practice the contrast until the two forms feel completely separate.

Focus 2 — Tone across serial verb sequences in directions

Pronunciation Focus — Rhythm and tone in direction sequences

Direction giving in Yorùbá involves sequences of two or more verbs describing connected movements. Each verb retains its own tone in the sequence. Do not flatten the sequence into a single uniform pitch or pause heavily between the verbs as if they were separate sentences. The movement should feel continuous. Practice these sequences:

  • Lọ títọ̀, wá dé ìta ọ̀nà. — Go straight, and arrive at the junction.
  • Yí sí ọ̀tún, gba ọ̀nà náà lọ. — Turn right, take that road (and go).
  • Kọjá ilé ẹ̀kọ́, dúró níwájú banki. — Pass the school, stop in front of the bank.

Note that lọ (go) carries mid tone, wá (come) carries high tone, and dé (arrive) carries high tone. Each must be clearly pronounced even in a fast sequence.

Focus 3 — Direction-question intonation

Pronunciation Focus — Asking for directions: question intonation

The most common direction question uses the pattern Níbo ni ___ wà? (Where is ___?). The question word níbo carries two high tones (high-high), and the sentence ends with the familiar location verb wà. Practice these question patterns:

  • Níbo ni ilé ìwòsàn wà? — Where is the hospital/clinic?
  • Níbo ni pákì ọkọ̀ wà? — Where is the motor park?
  • Ọ̀nà wo ni mo gbọdọ̀ gbà lọ sí banki? — Which road should I take to the bank? (receptive; contains gbọdọ̀ from Ch. 13 — treat as a formulaic unit here)

The full question Níbo ni ___ wà? is the productive form for this chapter. The shorter colloquial ___ wà níbo? (with the question word at the end) is also heard in natural speech — treat it as a recognized alternative but use the full form in your own production.

Focus 4 — Place names and transportation vocabulary

The following place names and transportation terms contain important phonological features to practice. Say each one aloud and mark the tone pattern (H = high, M = mid, L = low) for each syllable.

  1. ìdúró ọkọ̀ — bus stop (syllabic nasal at the start; low tone on ìdúró)
  2. àárín gbùngbùn ìlú — town centre (notice the double vowel àá and nasal consonants)
  3. ojúbọ epo — filling station (oral vowel o versus underdotted ọ in ojúbọ)
  4. ìta ọ̀nà — junction (underdotted ọ̀ with low tone in ọ̀nà)
  5. ilé ìwòsàn — clinic/hospital (ìwòsàn: low-low-high tone)

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

11.1 Asking where a place is: Níbo ni ___ wà?

Purpose: ask for the location of a building, landmark, or transport stop.

You learned wà (to be located, to exist) with the preposition ní in Chapter 6. The direction question combines this with the question word níbo (where) in a focus-like pattern:

  • Níbo ni ilé ìwòsàn wà? where + focus-ni + clinic + wà? Where is the clinic?
  • Níbo ni ìdúró ọkọ̀ wà? where + focus-ni + bus stop + wà? Where is the bus stop?
  • Níbo ni pákì ọkọ̀ wà? Where is the motor park?
Grammar Note — níbo ni and the focus pattern

The structure Níbo ni ___? is a question pattern you may recognize as related to the focus construction with ni that first appeared in Chapter 1 and has been practiced in formulaic form since then. The word níbo (where) combines ní (at/in) and an interrogative element, and the particle ni follows it to mark the focus of the question. You do not yet need to analyze the full focus system — that is the work of Chapter 19. For now, learn Níbo ni ___ wà? as a complete productive question frame and use it whenever you want to ask where something is located.

11.2 Stating origin and destination: láti and sí

Purpose: say where a journey starts and ends.

Two prepositions mark origin and destination in spatial and movement contexts:

láti — from (marks the starting point)

sí — to, toward (marks the destination or direction of movement)

  • Mo ń lọ láti ọjà sí ilé ìwòsàn. I + progressive + go + from + market + to + clinic I am going from the market to the clinic.
  • Ọkọ̀ wá láti pákì ọkọ̀. The vehicle came from the motor park.
  • Ẹ lọ sí ìdúró ọkọ̀ àkọ́kọ́. Go to the bus stop first. (honorific/plural command)
  • Jìnnà ni láti banki sí ojúbọ epo? Is it far from the bank to the filling station?
Grammar Note — láti in two different uses

You will encounter láti again in Chapter 14, where it introduces a purpose clause ("in order to"). In that use, láti precedes a verb. In this chapter, láti functions as a spatial preposition meaning "from" and is followed by a place noun. The two uses share the same form but different grammatical positions: láti ọjà (from the market) is spatial, while láti rí i (in order to see it) is purposive. Context and the following word — noun versus verb — tell you which meaning is intended. Do not let this future knowledge confuse the spatial use you need now.

11.3 Affirmative commands and familiar versus honorific address

Purpose: give direction instructions appropriate to the relationship and formality.

In Chapters 2 and 9, you learned fixed command forms. Chapter 11 makes commands fully productive for giving directions. A Yorùbá affirmative command uses the bare verb form. The choice between familiar and honorific or plural address depends on your relationship with the person:

AddressPatternExampleEnglish
Familiar (singular o)bare verbLọ títọ̀.Go straight.
Honorific / plural (ẹ)ẹ + bare verbẸ lọ títọ̀.Go straight. (polite / to a group)
Polite request (either)ẹ jọ̀wọ́ + verbẸ jọ̀wọ́ dúró.Please stop.
  • Kọjá ọjà náà. Pass that market. (familiar)
  • Ẹ dúró níwájú ilé ẹ̀kọ́. Stop in front of the school. (honorific)
  • Gba ọ̀nà àkọ́kọ́ sí ọ̀tún. Take the first road on the right. (familiar)
  • Ẹ wọ inú ọfíìsì náà. Enter that office. (honorific)

11.4 Prohibitive má

Purpose: give a negative instruction — tell someone not to do something.

The prohibitive particle má (high tone, one syllable) precedes the verb directly to create a negative command. It works with both familiar and honorific address:

  • Má lọ ọ̀nà yẹn. prohibitive + go + road + that Do not go that way. (familiar)
  • Ẹ má kọjá afárá náà. honorific + prohibitive + cross + bridge + that Do not cross that bridge. (honorific)
  • Má dúró níbẹ̀. Do not stop there.
  • Ẹ má yí sí òsì ní ìta ọ̀nà yìí. Do not turn left at this junction.
Grammar Note — Three negative particles compared

You now know three different negative particles. They are not interchangeable:

  • kò — negates an action or predicate in a declarative clause: Ó kò lọ. (He/she did not go.)
  • kì í — negates a habitual or general statement: A kì í lọ níbẹ̀. (One does not go there. / We never go there.)
  • má — negates a command (prohibitive): Má lọ níbẹ̀. (Do not go there.)

When you want to tell someone not to do something, use má, not kò. Using kò in a command slot produces a declarative statement, not a prohibition.

11.5 Serial verbs of motion, direction, and result

Purpose: describe a connected movement or route using two or more verbs in a single event sequence.

Chapter 9 introduced the serial verb construction with transfer verbs (mú ___ fún mi). This chapter makes serial verbs fully productive for motion and direction. Yorùbá direction-giving commonly uses sequences of verbs with no conjunction between them. The verbs share the same subject and describe one continuous movement.

Serial Pattern 1 — lọ + direction (go in a direction)
Lọ títọ̀ dé ìta ọ̀nà.
verb₁ (go) + manner/path + verb₂ (arrive) + destination
Go straight until you reach the junction.
Serial Pattern 2 — yí sí + direction (turn toward)
Yí sí ọ̀tún, gba ọ̀nà náà lọ.
verb₁ (turn) + sí + direction + verb₂ (take) + road + verb₃ (go)
Turn right and take that road (and continue).
Serial Pattern 3 — kọjá + landmark + lọ (pass a landmark and continue)
Kọjá banki náà lọ.
verb₁ (pass) + landmark + verb₂ (go / continue on)
Pass that bank and keep going. / Go past the bank.
Serial Pattern 4 — wọ inú (enter into)
Wọ inú ọfíìsì náà, béèrè fún ìrànlọ́wọ́.
verb₁ (enter) + inú (inside) + noun + verb₂ (ask) + purpose
Enter that office and ask for help.
Grammar Note — lọ as a directional element in serial constructions

In many of the direction sequences above, lọ (go) appears at the end of a serial clause where English would use "and continue" or nothing at all. In Yorùbá, lọ at the end of a serial sequence signals continued movement away from the speaker's reference point. Compare:

  • Kọjá banki náà. — Pass that bank. (one action, completed at the bank)
  • Kọjá banki náà lọ. — Pass that bank and keep going. (movement continues past the bank)

Similarly, wá (come) at the end of a serial sequence signals movement toward the speaker. These directional verbs are an important feature of Yorùbá motion expressions and appear frequently in natural directions.

11.6 Sequencing directions with connectors

Purpose: connect direction steps into a clear, ordered sequence.

When giving multi-step directions, use the following connectors and time-like sequence words to organize the steps. These are reviewed from Chapter 7 and used here in a new context:

  • Àkọ́kọ́, lọ títọ̀ dé ìta ọ̀nà àkọ́kọ́. Lẹ́yìn náà, yí sí ọ̀tún. Tí o bá dé ojúbọ epo, dúró níbẹ̀. First, go straight to the first junction. Then turn right. When you reach the filling station, stop there.
Language Focus

Yorùbá direction-giving uses a sequence of verbs rather than a single complex sentence. Where English might say "Turn left and then go straight until you pass the church," Yorùbá coordinates short verbal clauses: Yí sí òsì. Lọ títọ̀. Kọjá ilé ijọsìn náà lọ. Each clause describes one part of the movement. No conjunction is required between the verbs within a single serial event, but sequence connectors like lẹ́yìn náà (after that) and àkọ́kọ́ (first) link the separate steps of the route. Learners who insert àti (and) inside every serial clause produce unnatural Yorùbá. Save àti for genuinely parallel items — not for the joints in a serial motion sequence.

The prohibitive má differs from the habitual particle máa (Chapters 7 and 14) by exactly one syllable and always precedes a verb without ń. Confusing them produces either an unintended ban on an activity or an unintended description of a habit. Practice the contrast through listening and writing, not only through reading.

Model Dialogue

Read the dialogue carefully. Notice how Tópé asks for directions politely, how Àlàbá gives step-by-step instructions using serial verbs and sequencing words, and how she uses the prohibitive má to prevent a common wrong turning. Comprehension questions follow.

Ipò: Tópé pè Àlàbá ní fóònù. Tópé ti dé pákì ọkọ̀ ìlú, ṣùgbọ́n ó fẹ́ lọ sí ilé ìwòsàn ìjọba kan ní àárín gbùngbùn ìlú. Àlàbá mọ ìlú náà dáadáa.
Setting: Tópé calls Àlàbá by phone. Tópé has arrived at the town motor park but wants to go to the government clinic in the town centre. Àlàbá knows the town well.

TÓPÉ Ẹ káàárọ̀, Àlàbá. Mo wà ní pákì ọkọ̀ báyìí, ṣùgbọ́n n kò mọ ọ̀nà sí ilé ìwòsàn ìjọba. Ẹ lè ràn mí lọ́wọ́? Good morning, Àlàbá. I am at the motor park now, but I do not know the way to the government clinic. Can you help me?
ÀLÀBÁ Ó yá, Tópé. Mo máa ń sọ ọ̀nà fún ọ. Àkọ́kọ́, jáde láti pákì ọkọ̀, lọ sí ọ̀nà ńlá. Alright, Tópé. I will explain the route to you. First, exit the motor park and go to the main road.
TÓPÉ Ó yá. Mo jáde tán. Mo wà ní ọ̀nà ńlá báyìí. Alright. I have exited. I am on the main road now.
ÀLÀBÁ Dára. Lọ títọ̀ dé ìta ọ̀nà àkọ́kọ́. Ní ìta ọ̀nà yẹn, má yí sí òsì — yí sí ọ̀tún nìkan. Good. Go straight until you reach the first junction. At that junction, do not turn left — only turn right.
TÓPÉ Má yí sí òsì; yí sí ọ̀tún. Mo gbọ́. Lẹ́yìn náà? Do not turn left; turn right. I understand. After that?
ÀLÀBÁ Lẹ́yìn tí o bá yí sí ọ̀tún, gba ọ̀nà náà lọ títọ̀. O máa ń kọjá ojúbọ epo kan àti ilé ẹ̀kọ́ kan. Ṣùgbọ́n má dúró níbẹ̀ — mú ẹsẹ̀ rẹ lọ. After you turn right, take that road straight. You will pass a filling station and a school. But do not stop there — keep walking.
TÓPÉ Ó dára. Kọjá ojúbọ epo àti ilé ẹ̀kọ́. Lẹ́yìn náà? Fine. Pass the filling station and the school. After that?
ÀLÀBÁ Tí o bá kọjá ilé ẹ̀kọ́, o máa ń rí afárá kékeré kan níwájú rẹ. Rekọjá afárá náà, yí sí òsì ní ipò kejì. Ilé ìwòsàn ìjọba wà ní apa ọ̀tún, nítòsí banki. When you pass the school, you will see a small bridge ahead. Cross that bridge, turn left at the second turning. The government clinic is on the right side, close to the bank.
TÓPÉ Mo tún gbọ́: rekọjá afárá, yí sí òsì ní ipò kejì, ilé ìwòsàn wà ní apa ọ̀tún nítòsí banki. Ẹ ṣé gan-an, Àlàbá! Let me repeat: cross the bridge, turn left at the second turning, the clinic is on the right near the bank. Thank you very much, Àlàbá!
ÀLÀBÁ Kò tọ́pẹ́. Pẹ̀lé, ó dàbọ̀. Don't mention it. Take care, goodbye.
Grammar Note — Three serial constructions in the dialogue
  1. jáde láti pákì ọkọ̀, lọ sí ọ̀nà ńlá — jáde (exit) + lọ (go): exit the motor park and proceed to the main road. Two verbs, one connected departure.
  2. gba ọ̀nà náà lọ títọ̀ — gba (take/follow) + lọ (go): take that road and keep going straight. The trailing lọ signals continued motion.
  3. rekọjá afárá náà, yí sí òsì — rekọjá (cross) + yí (turn): cross the bridge and turn left. Two distinct movements presented as a single instruction unit without an inserted conjunction.

Àwọn ìbéèrè nípa ọ̀rọ̀ àdáhùnṣe — Comprehension questions:

  1. Tópé wà níbo ní ìbẹ̀rẹ̀ ìjíròrò? (Where is Tópé at the beginning of the dialogue?)
  2. Kí ni Àlàbá sọ fún Tópé pé kò gbọdọ̀ ṣe ní ìta ọ̀nà àkọ́kọ́? (What does Àlàbá tell Tópé not to do at the first junction?)
  3. Àwọn ilé méló ni Tópé máa ń kọjá ṣáájú kí ó tó dé afárá? (How many buildings does Tópé pass before the bridge?)
  4. Ilé ìwòsàn ìjọba wà ní apa wo — ọ̀tún tàbí òsì? (Which side is the government clinic on — right or left?)
  5. Wa gbolohùn àkọ́kọ́ pẹ̀lú má nínú ìjíròrò. Kọ ọ̀ jáde, sọ ìtumọ̀ rẹ̀, kí o sì ṣàlàyé ìyàtọ̀ rẹ̀ sí máa. (Find the first sentence with má in the dialogue. Write it out, give its meaning, and explain the difference from máa.)
  6. Tópé tún àwọn ìtọ́nisọ̀nà náà sọ fún Àlàbá nígbà tó sọ Mo tún gbọ́. Kí ni àbájáde ìgbésẹ̀ yìí nínú ìbánisọ̀rọ̀? (Tópé repeats the directions back when she says Mo tún gbọ́. What is the purpose of this move in the conversation?)

Writing Workshop

Chapter 11 has two writing products: a set of step-by-step directions and a short direction message. Both must use Standard Yorùbá orthography with full tone marks and underdots.

Àpín A — Àwọn ìtọ́sọ́nà tó ní ìgbésẹ̀ (Step-by-step directions)

Choose a starting point and a destination in a real or imagined town. Write at least five direction steps in Yorùbá. Each step must:

  • Use at least one command (affirmative or prohibitive).
  • Name at least two real building or landmark types from this chapter's vocabulary.
  • Use at least one serial verb pattern.
  • Use sequencing words to connect the steps (àkọ́kọ́, lẹ́yìn náà, etc.).
  • State the destination clearly at the end.

Àpín B — Ìfitónilétí ọ̀nà (Direction message)

Write a short message (50–70 words) to a friend or colleague explaining how to get from the motor park to a place of your choice. Use the letter format modeled in the Reading section. Your message must include:

  • A greeting and opening line.
  • At least three direction steps using serial verb constructions.
  • At least one prohibitive má.
  • The position of the destination (right, left, in front of, near).
  • A closing.
  1. Plan your route. Sketch the route quickly in any language or as a simple map. Identify the major turning points, landmarks, and any common wrong turn to warn against.
  2. Draft in steps. Write each step as a short sentence or serial sequence. Do not worry yet about connectors — just get the movement sequence right first.
  3. Add connectors and warnings. Insert àkọ́kọ́, lẹ́yìn náà, and any prohibitive warnings. Check that má precedes the verb directly and does not carry an extra syllable.
  4. Apply the editing checklist. Verify every tone mark, underdot, and serial verb construction before submitting.

Àtúnṣe àtúnyẹ̀wò — Editing checklist:

  • Every ẹ, ọ, and ṣ carries its underdot: ọ̀nà, not *ọna or *ona.
  • High and low tones are marked on every required syllable. Mid tones are left unmarked.
  • The prohibitive má is one syllable with a high tone and is followed immediately by a verb.
  • I did not insert àti between verbs in a serial construction.
  • Commands use the bare verb; the honorific ẹ precedes the verb when the addressee requires it.
  • Position words (ní apa ọ̀tún, níwájú, nítòsí) follow the noun they modify.
  • Steps are clearly sequenced with àkọ́kọ́, lẹ́yìn náà, or equivalent connectors.
  • The destination is clearly named at the end with a location statement.
  • Every sentence begins with a capital letter and ends with appropriate punctuation.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Asking for and giving directions

In many Yorùbá-speaking communities, asking a stranger for directions typically begins with a greeting before the request itself — to launch directly into a question without an initial greeting can feel abrupt. Even a brief Ẹ káàsán or Ẹ jọ̀wọ́ dákọ (Excuse me, please) before the question is a widely appreciated courtesy. After receiving directions, it is customary to thank the person warmly before continuing on your way.

Direction-givers commonly use landmarks — a well-known market, a mosque or church, a specific junction, a large tree, or even a notable person's house — rather than street names alone. This is not because formal addresses do not exist, but because landmark-based directions are often more practically reliable in communities where not all roads carry clear signage or where a street may be known by multiple names. Learners should be comfortable both using landmark references and asking for them: Àmì wo ni ó wà nítòsí? (What landmark is near it?)

Culture in Context — Transportation systems across Yorùbá-speaking areas

Transportation practices vary considerably across Yorùbá-speaking communities. In Lagos and other major Nigerian cities, the range of options includes BRT buses, shared minibuses (often called danfo), motorcycle taxis (often called okada), and ride-hailing apps. In smaller cities and towns such as Ìbàdàn, Ìlọrin, or Àbéòkúta, shared minibuses and motorcycle taxis remain central to daily movement. In Benin and Togo, collective taxis and mototaxis are common alternatives, sometimes called by French-influenced names in those contexts.

The vocabulary in this chapter provides Standard Yorùbá terms for common vehicle types. In practice, the specific informal names used for these vehicles vary by city and may shift over time. When traveling to a new Yorùbá-speaking area, it is worth asking a local which term is used for which vehicle type rather than assuming one label covers all contexts.

Culture in Context — Respectful interaction in public space

When asking for directions from a passerby who is clearly older than you, using ẹ (honorific) rather than the familiar o is generally the appropriate choice. Similarly, when a person of authority — a security guard, a market warden, or a transport official — gives you instructions, responding with Ó dára (Fine) or Mo gbọ (I understand) acknowledges the instruction respectfully. A curt silence or an immediate counter-question can read as dismissive. These courtesies are not mechanical rules but natural parts of the relational texture of most Yorùbá public interactions.

Regional Variation — Direction vocabulary across communities

Some direction-related terms differ between Nigerian, Beninese, and Togolese Yorùbá communities or between urban and rural settings. For example, the term used for a filling station (ojúbọ epo in this textbook) may be replaced colloquially in Lagos with the English-derived filling station or the widely heard petrol station. Motor parks may be called garage in some Nigerian urban areas, drawn from Yorùbá adaptation of the English word. These informal or contact-language terms are noted here for recognition purposes. The productive default for this course remains the Standard Yorùbá vocabulary listed in the vocabulary tables.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Confusing má (prohibitive) with máa (habitual/future)

Error: Writing *Máa lọ ọ̀nà yẹn when you mean "Do not go that way."

Correction: Má lọ ọ̀nà yẹn. — The prohibitive is má, one syllable, high tone. The habitual/future particle is máa, two syllables, high-high. Inserting an extra syllable here turns a prohibition into a statement about habitual activity, which communicates a completely different meaning. Practice the pair Má lọ (Do not go) versus Ó máa ń lọ (He/she habitually goes) until the contrast is automatic.

Common Mistake — Inserting àti between serial verbs in a direction sequence

Error: Writing *Lọ títọ̀ àti yí sí ọ̀tún — inserting àti (and) between the two direction verbs.

Correction: Lọ títọ̀, yí sí ọ̀tún. A serial direction sequence does not need a conjunction between the verbs. The two verbs describe one continuous movement event. àti coordinates parallel nouns or independent clauses — it is not the connector between verbs in a serial construction. If you want to link two separate instruction steps, use lẹ́yìn náà (after that) between the sentences, not àti between the verbs.

Common Mistake — Using kò instead of má for a prohibition

Error: Writing *Kò yí sí òsì to mean "Do not turn left."

Correction: Má yí sí òsì. — The particle kò negates a declarative predicate: Ó kò yí sí òsì means "He/she did not turn left" — a statement about what happened. To give a negative instruction — to tell someone not to do something — use the prohibitive má. Using kò in a command context produces a declaration, not a prohibition, and the listener will likely be confused about what you are trying to communicate.

Common Mistake — Placing sí before a direction word without a motion verb

Error: Writing *Sí ọ̀tún alone to mean "Turn right," using the preposition without a verb.

Correction: Yí sí ọ̀tún. — The preposition sí (toward, to) marks a direction or destination but requires a motion verb to precede it. A complete turning instruction requires the verb yí (turn) followed by sí and the direction word. Similarly, lọ sí (go to), wá sí (come to), and kọjá sí (pass to/toward) all need their motion verb. Do not use sí as a standalone direction command.

Common Mistake — Omitting underdots on key direction vocabulary

Error: Writing *ona instead of ọ̀nà, or *ose instead of ẹsẹ̀.

Correction: ọ̀nà (road) requires the underdotted ọ̀ with low tone; ẹsẹ̀ (foot; on foot) requires underdotted ẹ on the first syllable and low tone on the second. Many direction words in this chapter contain ọ or ẹ and must be written with underdots at all times. Check every direction-related word in your written work against the vocabulary tables before submission.

  3. Tópé kọjá ilé méjì: ojúbọ epo àti ilé ẹ̀kọ́. (Two buildings: a filling station and a school.)
  4. Ilé ìwòsàn ìjọba wà ní apa ọ̀tún. (The clinic is on the right side.)
  5. First sentence with má: Ní ìta ọ̀nà yẹn, má yí sí òsì. — Meaning: Do not turn left at that junction. The prohibitive má is one syllable, high tone, directly before the verb yí. It differs from habitual máa, which is two syllables and precedes the progressive marker ń. Accept any of the three má sentences in the dialogue with a correct explanation.
  6. Tópé repeats the directions back (Mo tún gbọ́ — I heard / Let me repeat) to confirm she has understood correctly. This communication repair strategy helps prevent errors and signals attentive listening.

Listening — Comprehension table

  • Exchange 1: Starting point: not specified (on the road); Destination: large shop (ilé ìtajà ńlá); Key direction: pass the bus stop and turn left; destination on the right in front of a bank.
  • Exchange 2: Starting point: current street; Destination: the first mosque in town (mọ́sálásì àkọ́kọ́); Key direction: pass the school, go to the bus stop, take a minibus to the town centre, exit at Àmáládé junction; mosque on the left.
  • Exchange 3: Starting point: current position; Destination: nearest filling station (ojúbọ epo); Key direction: turn back (yí padà), cross the bridge, turn right at the first junction; filling station on the left.

Bonus: (1) Rárá. O lè lọ ní ẹsẹ̀. Ó nítòsí. — Not far; can walk; it is nearby. (2) Take a minibus (ọkọ̀ kékeré), recommended because it is faster (ó yára jù). (3) Yí padà — turn around/turn back (serial: yí + padà).

Reading — Comprehension

  1. Ẹnu-bọ̀dọ̀ wọlé wà ní apa ọ̀tún; ẹnu-bọ̀dọ̀ jáde wà ní apa òsì. (Entry gate on the right; exit gate on the left.)
  2. Do not cross the red line. / No entry beyond the red line.
  3. Kúnlé sọ fún un pé kò gbọdọ̀ yí sí ọ̀tún ní ìta ọ̀nà Àgbọ̀dọ̀. (He says not to turn right at the Àgbọ̀dọ̀ junction — take the road going uphill instead.)
  4. Ẹnu-bọ̀dọ̀ àkọ́kọ́ wà fún àwọn oṣiṣẹ́ nìkan. (The first gate is for staff only.)
  5. Three sentences with má: (from notices) Má gbé ọkọ̀ níwájú ẹnu-bọ̀dọ̀. / Ẹ má kọjá ìlà pupa. (from directions letter) Má yí sí ọ̀tún ní ìta ọ̀nà náà and Má rẹ̀rẹ̀ tọ ẹnu-bọ̀dọ̀ àkọ́kọ́. Any three of these are acceptable.
  6. First serial construction in the polytechnic directions: jáde láti ọjà, lọ sí ọ̀nà ńlá — jáde (exit) and lọ (go): exit the market and go to the main road.

  1. ilé ìwòsàn; ojúbọ epo; ìta ọ̀nà; ìdúró ọkọ̀; pákì ọkọ̀; afárá; mọ́sálásì; ilé ẹ̀kọ́; ọfíìsì; ẹsẹ̀ (or: lọ ní ẹsẹ̀)
  2. right; left; in front of / ahead; behind / after; straight ahead; on the right side; near; far from; uphill; downhill
  3. (a) Exit → Jáde; (b) No entry / Do not cross → Má kọjá; (c) Stop → Dúró; (d) Enter → Wọlé; (e) Do not park → Má gbé
  4. Accept any natural, well-formed phrase using each word; examples: Mo wá láti ọjà. / Lọ sí ilé ìwòsàn. / Banki wà nítòsí. / Ìdúró ọkọ̀ wà ní apa òsì.

Chapter Review — Apá B

  1. (a) Níbo ni banki wà? (b) Níbo ni ìdúró ọkọ̀ wà? (c) Níbo ni àgọ́ olóṣà wà? (d) Níbo ni àárín gbùngbùn ìlú wà?
  2. Go straight: Familiar: Lọ títọ̀. Honorific: Ẹ lọ títọ̀. Prohibition: Má yí padà. — Turn right: Familiar: Yí sí ọ̀tún ní ìta ọ̀nà. Honorific: Ẹ yí sí ọ̀tún ní ìta ọ̀nà. Prohibition: Má yí sí òsì ní ìta ọ̀nà yìí. — Enter: Familiar: Wọ inú ilé náà. Honorific: Ẹ wọ inú ilé náà. Prohibition: Má wọ ẹnu-bọ̀dọ̀ àkọ́kọ́. — Cross the bridge: Familiar: Rekọjá afárá náà. Honorific: Ẹ rekọjá afárá náà. Prohibition: Má rekọjá afárá yẹn.
  3. Corrections: (i) Má lọ ọ̀nà yẹn. — máa is habitual/future; the prohibitive is má (one syllable). (ii) Lọ, yí sí ọ̀tún ní ìta ọ̀nà. — Remove àti from inside the serial sequence. (iii) Lọ sí banki. — The preposition sí requires a motion verb (lọ) to precede it. (iv) Má kọjá afárá. — kò negates a declarative; the prohibitive má gives a negative instruction.
  4. Accept any three-step sequence that correctly uses a serial verb, a prohibitive, two sequencing words, and names a plausible landmark route. Example: Àkọ́kọ́, jáde láti ọjà, lọ sí ọ̀nà ńlá. Lẹ́yìn náà, yí sí ọ̀tún. Má dúró ní banki — kọjá lọ. Ilé ìwòsàn wà nítòsí, ní apa òsì.

Chapter Review — Apá C

  1. Correct forms: ọ̀nà títọ̀; ní apa ọ̀tún; ìta ọ̀nà; ilé ìwòsàn; pákì ọkọ̀.
  2. Tone patterns (accept phonetically motivated alternatives; flag for human review): ọ̀nà títọ̀ = L-M · H-L; ilé ìwòsàn = H-M · L-L-H; pákì ọkọ̀ = H-L · M-L; yí sí ọ̀tún = H · H · L-H.
  3. Example pair: Prohibitive: Má lọ ọ̀nà yẹn. (Do not go that way.) — Habitual: Ó máa ń lọ ọ̀nà yẹn. (He/she habitually goes that way.) Label each clearly.

Looking Ahead

Ohun tí o lè ṣe báyìí — What you can do now

You can ask where a building or transport stop is located, give step-by-step directions using serial verbs of motion and direction, use the prohibitive má to warn against a wrong turning, state the origin and destination of a journey with láti and sí, and write a fully marked set of directions or a direction message in Standard Yorùbá. You can also read common public signs and transit notices.

Abala 12: Ojú Ọjọ́ àti Àwọn Àkókò Ọdún — Weather and Seasons

Chapter 12 moves from getting around town to describing the world outside: weather, conditions, and the seasons. You will learn how to describe what the weather is like now and what it is typically like at different times of year, how to connect weather to clothing choices and travel decisions, and how to make a simple conditional plan using the pattern bí ___, ___ (if ___, ___). The future particle máa will appear in formulaic receptive examples as weather-related prediction language — its full productive treatment follows in Chapter 14. The writing target for Chapter 12 is a short, fully marked forecast and conditional plan.

Ara àti Ìlera

Body, Health, and Asking for Help · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Name common parts of the body using fully marked Yorùbá vocabulary.
  2. Describe a simple symptom using the productive pattern [body part] ń dun [mi/ọ/e/wọn].
  3. State a general illness or condition using formulaic expressions such as Mo ṣàìsàn and Mo ní ibà.
  4. Use kò in health-specific negative statements, including Ara mi kò yá and Kò dára.
  5. Express need using nílò and ask for help using ràn ___ lọ́wọ́ with object pronouns.
  6. Recognize and use polite, urgent intonation appropriate to describing a problem or asking for help.
  7. Understand and complete a simple clinic intake form.
  8. Write a short, fully marked message explaining a health problem and requesting help.

Communication Scenario

You have been feeling unwell since yesterday. A friend, Dúpé, notices that something is wrong and asks about it. You need to describe your symptoms clearly enough that she can decide whether you should go to the clinic — the same ilé ìwòsàn you learned to give directions to in Chapter 11. Later in the chapter, you will also practice the other side of this exchange: understanding what a nurse asks you when you arrive, and filling out a simple intake form.

  1. Before you open this chapter, list any parts of the body you may already know in Yorùbá from earlier chapters or general exposure. You are not expected to know many — this is simply a starting check.
  2. Think of three ways you might describe not feeling well in English: "My head hurts," "I have a fever," "I feel sick." Notice that these use different grammatical patterns in English (a possessive subject, a possessive object, and a simple adjective). Keep this in mind — Yorùbá does not map onto these English patterns one-to-one.
  3. Recall the honorific command form ẹ + verb from Chapter 11, and the polite request frame ẹ jọ̀wọ́ + verb. You will use both again in this chapter when asking for help.

Pronunciation and Orthography Focus

Focus 1 — Nasal vowels and syllabic nasals in health vocabulary

Pronunciation Focus — Nasal vowels and syllabic nasals in body and illness words

Health vocabulary contains several important nasal features. Practice each type carefully:

  • Nasal vowels: ọrùn (neck) and ẹnu (mouth) both contain vowels followed by n that nasalize the preceding vowel sound rather than adding a separate consonant sound. Do not pronounce the n as a full English "n" — let it color the vowel instead.
  • Syllabic nasals: ìrànlọ́wọ́ (help) contains a syllabic n that forms its own syllable, distinct from the nasal vowels above. Say it slowly: ì-rà-n-lọ́-wọ́.
  • Oral vs. nasal contrast: Compare oògùn (medicine, oral vowels throughout) with ọrùn (neck, nasalized vowel). Confusing oral and nasal vowels can change or obscure meaning.

Focus 2 — Polite, urgent intonation when describing a problem

Pronunciation Focus — Sounding concerned without sounding alarmed

When describing a health problem or asking for help, Yorùbá speakers typically maintain politeness even while conveying urgency. This is done through vocabulary choice and pacing rather than through raised pitch or volume. Practice these contrasts:

  • Orí ń dun mi. — spoken evenly, at a normal pace: a mild, ordinary complaint.
  • Orí ń dun mi gan-an. Ẹ jọ̀wọ́, ẹ ràn mí lọ́wọ́. — spoken slightly slower, with clear emphasis on gan-an (very much) and a brief pause before the request: a more serious complaint paired with a polite request for help.

Notice that urgency is added through the intensifier gan-an and the explicit request ẹ ràn mí lọ́wọ́, not by shouting or dropping the honorific ẹ. Even in a genuinely urgent situation, dropping courtesy markers is not the way Yorùbá speech typically signals seriousness.

Focus 3 — Tone in the dun pattern

Pronunciation Focus — Tone stability in [body part] ń dun [pronoun]

The verb dun (to hurt) carries mid tone and must remain clearly audible even though it sits between the high-tone progressive marker ń and the pronoun that follows. Practice these forms slowly, then at natural speed:

  • Orí ń dun mi. — My head hurts.
  • Ẹsẹ̀ ń dun un. — His/her leg hurts.
  • Ọwọ́ ń dun wa. — Our hand(s) hurt.

Do not flatten dun toward the high tone of ń. The three syllables remain tonally distinct: high–mid–(pronoun tone).

Focus 4 — Body-part and illness words for tone marking practice

Say each word aloud and mark the tone pattern (H = high, M = mid, L = low) for each syllable.

  1. ọrùn — neck (underdotted ọ, low tone throughout)
  2. ìrànlọ́wọ́ — help (low–mid–low–high–high; contains the syllabic nasal n)
  3. àìsàn — illness (low–low–low)
  4. oògùn — medicine (mid, long vowel–low)
  5. ẹ̀yìn — back (low–low, underdotted ẹ)

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

12.1 Describing a symptom: [body part] ń dun [pronoun]

Purpose: describe where something hurts.

Yorùbá describes physical pain with the body part as the grammatical subject and the person who feels the pain as an object pronoun after the verb dun (to hurt). This is the opposite of the English pattern "My head hurts," where "head" is possessed by "my." Learn this pattern as a fixed frame rather than translating word for word:

  • Orí ń dun mi. head + progressive + hurt + me My head hurts. (lit. "Head is hurting me.")
  • Inú ń dun un. stomach + progressive + hurt + him/her His/her stomach hurts.
  • Ẹ̀yìn ń dun mi gan-an. My back really hurts.
Grammar Note — Why "my head" is not possessed here

You learned possession with mi, rẹ, and rẹ̀ in Chapter 3 (orí mi — "my head," as a plain noun phrase). That possessive pattern is still correct when you are simply naming a body part, such as pointing to a diagram. But when you are describing a symptom, Yorùbá uses the object-pronoun-after-the-verb pattern shown above: Orí ń dun mi, not *Orí mi ń dun. The body part is the subject that "does" the hurting; the person experiencing it appears as the object of dun. This chapter treats the pattern formulaically — you do not yet need to analyze why Yorùbá organizes the sentence this way, only to produce the frame correctly.

12.2 Naming a general illness or condition

Purpose: state that you are sick, or name a specific condition such as fever or a cold.

  • Mo ṣàìsàn. I + be.sick I am sick.
  • Mo ní ibà. I + have + fever I have a fever.
  • Mo ní òtútù. I have a cold.
  • Ó ní ikọ́. He/she has a cough.

The verb ní (to have) was introduced formulaically for possession in earlier chapters and is developed further here for naming conditions. Ṣàìsàn (to be sick) is learned as a fixed formulaic verb at this stage; its relationship to the noun àìsàn is not analyzed further in Volume I.

12.3 Negation in health contexts: kò yá and related expressions

Purpose: say that you are not well, using the negation system from Chapters 2–10 in a new domain.

You have used kò to negate declarative statements since Chapter 2 and have practiced it steadily through Chapter 10. This chapter develops kò specifically for the domain of health:

  • Ara mi kò yá. body + my + not + be.well I am not well. (lit. "My body is not well.")
  • Kò dára. It's not good. / I'm not doing well. (general response to "How are you?")
  • N kò lera. I am not strong / not in good health.
Grammar Note — kò versus má in a health context

Remember the distinction from Chapter 11: kò negates a declarative statement, while má negates a command. In a health context, this distinction still holds. Ara mi kò yá (I am not well) is a statement about your condition. If a nurse tells you Má jẹun kí o tó dé (Do not eat before you arrive — receptive at this stage), that is a prohibitive instruction, not a declarative statement. Keep the two systems separate even in this new vocabulary domain.

12.4 Expressing need: nílò

Purpose: say what you need.

The verb nílò (to need) is followed directly by a noun, with no preposition:

  • Mo nílò oògùn. I + need + medicine I need medicine.
  • Mo nílò ìrànlọ́wọ́. I need help.
  • A nílò dókítà. We need a doctor.

12.5 Asking for help: ràn ___ lọ́wọ́ with object pronouns

Purpose: ask someone to help you, using a serial verb pattern reviewed from Chapters 9 and 11 and now developed with a fuller set of object pronouns.

The pattern ràn ___ lọ́wọ́ (help ___) places an object pronoun between ràn and lọ́wọ́. You have seen this pattern formulaically as ràn mí lọ́wọ́ since Chapter 9. This chapter treats the object-pronoun slot as a productive frame for high-frequency help requests:

  • Ẹ ràn mí lọ́wọ́, ẹ jọ̀wọ́. honorific + help + me + hand-part + please Please help me. (honorific)
  • Mo fẹ́ ràn ọ́ lọ́wọ́. I want to help you. (familiar)
  • Ta ni yóò ràn wá lọ́wọ́? Who will help us? (contains future yóò, reviewed formulaically from Ch. 9)
Serial Pattern — ràn + object pronoun + lọ́wọ́ (help someone)
Ẹ jọ̀wọ́, ẹ ràn mí lọ́wọ́ — orí ń dun mi gan-an.
please + honorific + help + me + hand-part — head + progressive + hurt + me + very
Please help me — my head really hurts.
Grammar Note — Object pronouns remain a formulaic slot in this frame

Your Yoruba Pronoun System is already consolidated for ordinary post-verbal object use (Chapters 3–10). In the specific frame ràn ___ lọ́wọ́, however, treat the object pronoun as part of a fixed help-request formula rather than as a fully generalizable grammar rule to apply elsewhere. This keeps the high-frequency expression usable immediately while more complex serial-verb argument structure remains scheduled for later volumes.

Language Focus

Health language in Yorùbá often organizes the sentence differently from English, even when the vocabulary is familiar. The pattern [body part] ń dun [person] places the source of the sensation, not the person, as the grammatical subject. Meanwhile, general condition statements like Mo ṣàìsàn and Mo ní ibà place the person as the subject, much like English. Learners who try to force every health sentence into a single template will produce unnatural Yorùbá. Learn each frame — dun, ṣàìsàn, ní + condition, kò yá, nílò, and ràn ___ lọ́wọ́ — as its own pattern with its own word order.

Language Building Block
[body part] + ń + dun + [object pronoun] (+ gan-an)
Productive symptom frame · body part is subject · dun carries mid tone · gan-an optionally intensifies
Ọrùn ń dun mi gan-an.
My neck really hurts.

Model Dialogue

Read the dialogue carefully. Notice how Dúpé notices something is wrong, how Kúnlé describes his symptom using the dun pattern, and how the conversation moves toward a polite request for help. Comprehension questions follow.

Ipò: Dúpé àti Kúnlé jẹ́ ọ̀rẹ́. Wọ́n wà ní ilé Kúnlé. Dúpé ṣàkíyèsí pé ojú Kúnlé kò dára.
Setting: Dúpé and Kúnlé are friends. They are at Kúnlé's house. Dúpé notices that Kúnlé does not look well.

DÚPÉ Kúnlé, kí ni ó ṣẹlẹ̀ sí ọ? Ojú rẹ kò dára rárá. Kúnlé, what happened to you? You don't look well at all.
KÚNLÉ Ó ṣe pàtàkì pé kí n sọ ọ́ fún ọ. Orí ń dun mi gan-an láti àná. It's important that I tell you. My head has really been hurting since yesterday.
DÚPÉ Ǹjẹ́ o ní ibà pẹ̀lú? Do you have a fever too?
KÚNLÉ Bẹ́ẹ̀ni, mo ní ibà. Inú mi náà ń dun mi díẹ̀. Ara mi kò yá rárá. Yes, I have a fever. My stomach hurts a little too. I am not well at all.
DÚPÉ Ó yẹ kí o lọ sí ilé ìwòsàn. Mo lè ràn ọ́ lọ́wọ́ láti dé ibẹ̀ — ẹ jọ̀wọ́, jẹ́ kí n pè ọ́ ní tákísì. You should go to the clinic. I can help you get there — please, let me call you a taxi. (contains formulaic frames not yet fully productive)
KÚNLÉ Mo nílò ìrànlọ́wọ́ rẹ gan-an. Ẹ ṣé, Dúpé. I really need your help. Thank you, Dúpé.
(Ní ilé ìwòsàn — At the clinic)
NỌ́Ọ̀SÌ Ẹ káàsán. Kí ni orúkọ yín? Ibi wo ni ó ń dun yín? Good afternoon. What is your name? Where does it hurt?
KÚNLÉ Orúkọ mi ni Kúnlé. Orí àti inú mi ni ó ń dun mi. Mo tún ní ibà. My name is Kúnlé. It's my head and stomach that hurt. I also have a fever.
NỌ́Ọ̀SÌ Ẹ jọ̀wọ́ dúró níbí. Dókítà máa wá kíákíá. Please wait here. The doctor will come shortly. (contains formulaic future use, reviewed from Ch. 9)
Grammar Note — Three symptom-related patterns in the dialogue
  1. Orí ń dun mi gan-an. — the core symptom pattern with intensifier.
  2. Ara mi kò yá rárá. — negated health statement, intensified with rárá (not at all).
  3. Mo nílò ìrànlọ́wọ́ rẹ gan-an. — need expression combined with the possessive rẹ (your) reviewed from Chapter 3.

Àwọn ìbéèrè nípa ọ̀rọ̀ àdáhùnṣe — Comprehension questions:

  1. Kí ni ó ń dun Kúnlé nígbà tí Dúpé bẹ̀rẹ̀ sí bi í léèrè? (What is hurting Kúnlé when Dúpé first asks him?)
  2. Ǹjẹ́ Kúnlé ní ibà? (Does Kúnlé have a fever?)
  3. Kí ni Dúpé fẹ́ ṣe láti ràn Kúnlé lọ́wọ́? (What does Dúpé want to do to help Kúnlé?)
  4. Ní ilé ìwòsàn, kí ni ìbéèrè àkọ́kọ́ tí nọ́ọ̀sì bi Kúnlé? (At the clinic, what is the nurse's first question to Kúnlé?)
  5. Wa gbolohùn kan nínú ìjíròrò tí ó lo kò ní ọ̀rọ̀ ìlera. Kọ ọ̀ jáde, sọ ìtumọ̀ rẹ̀. (Find one sentence in the dialogue that uses kò in a health statement. Write it out and give its meaning.)

Writing Workshop

Chapter 12 has two writing products: a completed intake form and a short help-request message. Both must use Standard Yorùbá orthography with full tone marks and underdots.

Àpín A — Ìwé àyẹ̀wò (Intake form)

Using the model form in the Reading section as a template, invent a plausible patient and complete the form in Yorùbá. Include:

  • A name and age.
  • At least two body parts marked as hurting.
  • A yes/no answer for fever and for cough.
  • A brief note on when the illness began.

Àpín B — Ìfitónilétí ìrànlọ́wọ́ (Help-request message)

Write a short message (50–70 words) to a friend describing a health problem and asking for help. Your message must include:

  • A greeting and opening line.
  • At least two symptoms using the [body part] ń dun [pronoun] pattern.
  • At least one negative health statement with kò.
  • A request for help using nílò or ràn ___ lọ́wọ́.
  • A closing.
  1. Plan your symptoms. Choose two or three body parts and decide how serious each symptom is.
  2. Draft each sentence. Write each symptom, condition, and request as a separate short sentence using the correct pattern for each.
  3. Add courtesy and intensity. Insert ẹ jọ̀wọ́, gan-an, or rárá where they make the message sound natural and appropriately urgent.
  4. Apply the editing checklist. Verify every tone mark, underdot, and pattern choice before submitting.

Àtúnṣe àtúnyẹ̀wò — Editing checklist:

  • Every ẹ, ọ, and ṣ carries its underdot: ọrùn, not *orun.
  • High and low tones are marked on every required syllable. Mid tones are left unmarked.
  • The symptom pattern places the body part first, followed by ń dun and the object pronoun — not the reverse.
  • I did not write *[body part] mi ń dun when describing a symptom.
  • kò is used only for declarative negation, never for a command.
  • nílò is followed directly by a noun, with no extra preposition.
  • The honorific ẹ is used when the situation calls for polite or respectful address.
  • Every sentence begins with a capital letter and ends with appropriate punctuation.

Speaking Practice

Àdáwọlé àkọ́kọ́ — Controlled practice: symptom check

Working with a partner, take turns asking and answering about how you feel. Speaker A asks Ǹjẹ́ ara rẹ dára? (Are you well?). Speaker B answers with at least one symptom using the dun pattern and one general condition statement. Switch roles after five exchanges each.

Every exchange must include:

  1. The opening question Ǹjẹ́ ara rẹ dára?
  2. At least one symptom in the [body part] ń dun [pronoun] pattern.
  3. A closing response, either Ó yẹ kí o sinmi (You should rest — formulaic) or Ẹ ṣé, mo dára báyìí (Thank you, I am fine now).

Iṣẹ́ pẹ̀lú alábàápàdé — Clinic role-play

One partner plays a patient; the other plays a nurse. The nurse asks for the patient's name and where it hurts, following the model dialogue. The patient answers using at least two symptoms and one need or help expression. If no partner is available, perform both roles aloud using the model dialogue as a guide, then create a new version with different symptoms.

  • Nurse asks: Kí ni orúkọ yín? Ibi wo ni ó ń dun yín?
  • Patient answers with at least two body parts and one general condition (ibà or ikọ́).
  • Patient includes one request using nílò or ràn ___ lọ́wọ́.

Fún àwọn tí ń kọ́ nìkọ̀ọ̀kan — Solo alternative: recorded self-report

Record yourself describing an imagined illness in one to two minutes of continuous Yorùbá. Include at least three symptoms, one negative health statement, and one help request. Listen back and check:

  • Did you keep dun at mid tone, clearly distinct from the high tone of ń?
  • Did you use kò correctly for a declarative negative statement?
  • Did your request for help sound polite, using ẹ jọ̀wọ́ or the honorific ẹ?
  • Did you use nasal vowels and syllabic nasals accurately in words like ọrùn and ìrànlọ́wọ́?

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Discussing illness with courtesy

In many Yorùbá-speaking communities, asking after someone's health is a routine part of greeting, not reserved only for moments of visible illness. A simple Ǹjẹ́ ara rẹ dára? may appear even in an ordinary greeting sequence. When someone does report feeling unwell, a common and warmly received response is Kò burú o, ó máa dára (It's not too bad, it will get better — formulaic; a comforting phrase rather than a medical claim), offered before any practical help is arranged.

It is also common for a friend, neighbor, or family member to take an active role in getting someone to care — accompanying them to a clinic, calling on their behalf, or checking in afterward. Offering this kind of help, as Dúpé does in the model dialogue, is viewed as an ordinary expression of care rather than an imposition.

Culture in Context — Clinics, pharmacies, and informal care

Across Yorùbá-speaking areas, people commonly draw on a range of care options depending on the situation, cost, and severity: government clinics and hospitals (ilé ìwòsàn ìjọba), private clinics, neighborhood pharmacies (often simply called chemist in Nigerian English usage), and, for some conditions, traditional or herbal practitioners. This range of options is a practical reality of daily life rather than a sign that any one option is inadequate. Learners visiting a Yorùbá-speaking area should be prepared to hear all of these referenced in ordinary conversation about health.

Regional Variation — Vocabulary for common ailments

Some everyday illness terms vary in informal use across regions and generations. For example, ibà (fever) is sometimes used broadly to refer to malaria-like symptoms in casual conversation, while more formal or clinical settings distinguish ibà from a specific diagnosis such as ibà ẹ̀fọn (malaria, lit. "mosquito fever"). The productive default for this course remains the general terms listed in the vocabulary tables; more specific medical terminology is introduced in later volumes.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Possessing the body part instead of using the dun pattern

Error: Writing *Orí mi ń dun when you mean "My head hurts," modeled directly on the English possessive structure.

Correction: Orí ń dun mi. — The body part is the subject of dun; the person is the object pronoun that follows the verb. Do not attach a possessive pronoun to the body part in this pattern. Compare with Chapter 3's possessive pattern orí mi (my head, as a plain noun phrase) — that pattern is correct for naming a body part, but not for describing a symptom with dun.

Common Mistake — Using kò to make a prohibition

Error: Writing *Kò jẹun kí o tó dé to tell someone not to eat before arriving.

Correction: Má jẹun kí o tó dé. — As established in Chapter 11, kò negates a declarative statement, while má negates a command. This distinction applies in health contexts exactly as it does elsewhere. Use kò only to state a fact, such as Ara mi kò yá (I am not well).

Common Mistake — Adding a preposition after nílò

Error: Writing *Mo nílò fún oògùn to mean "I need medicine," inserting an unnecessary preposition.

Correction: Mo nílò oògùn. — Nílò takes its object directly, with no preposition in between. This differs from English "need for," which some learners try to translate literally.

Common Mistake — Omitting underdots and nasal marking in body vocabulary

Error: Writing *orun instead of ọrùn, or *enu instead of ẹnu.

Correction: ọrùn (neck) requires the underdotted ọ with low tone; ẹnu (mouth) requires the underdotted ẹ on the first syllable. Many body-part words in this chapter contain ọ or ẹ and must be written with underdots at all times. Check every body-part and illness word in your written work against the vocabulary tables before submission.

Performance Task

Iṣẹ́ aṣeyọrí — Abala 12

Ìròyìn àìsàn àti ìbéèrè ìrànlọ́wọ́ — A health report and request for help

The Chapter 12 Performance Task has two parts. All spoken and written Yorùbá must use full tone marks and underdots.

Part 1 — Oral self-report (1–2 minutes):
Describe an imagined illness to a friend and ask for help. Your description must include:

  1. An appropriate opening greeting or response to a health inquiry.
  2. At least three symptoms, using the [body part] ń dun [pronoun] pattern and/or Mo ní ibà/ikọ́/òtútù.
  3. At least one negative health statement using kò.
  4. A clear request for help using nílò or ràn ___ lọ́wọ́.
  5. Appropriate polite, unhurried but concerned intonation.
  6. A polite closing, such as ẹ ṣé.

Part 2 — Completed intake form and written message (50–70 words for the message):
Complete a clinic intake form for an imagined patient, then write a short message to a friend explaining the same health problem and asking for help. Your message must include at least two symptoms, one negation with kò, and one help or need expression. Use fully marked Standard Yorùbá throughout.

Assessment note: The spoken task is evaluated on task completion, comprehensibility, correct use of the symptom pattern, correct distinction of kò from má, appropriate honorific or polite address, and intelligible tone and nasal production. The written task is evaluated on accuracy of the symptom and negation patterns, correct use of nílò and ràn ___ lọ́wọ́, orthographic completeness (underdots and tone marks), and overall clarity.

Answer Key

Practice — Describing symptoms

  1. Inú ń dun mi.
  2. Ẹsẹ̀ ń dun un.
  3. Etí ń dun wa.
  4. Ẹ̀yìn ń dun ọ gan-an.
  5. Orí ń dun wọn.

Practice — Need and help

  1. Mo nílò oògùn.
  2. Ẹ jọ̀wọ́, ẹ ràn mí lọ́wọ́.
  3. A nílò dókítà.
  4. Mo fẹ́ ràn ọ́ lọ́wọ́.
  5. Mo nílò ìrànlọ́wọ́ — orí ń dun mi gan-an.

Practice — Negation in health contexts

  1. Ara mi kò yá.
  2. N kò lera.
  3. Kò dára.

Dialogue — Comprehension

  1. Orí Kúnlé ni ó ń dun ún, láti àná. (His head, since yesterday.)
  2. Bẹ́ẹ̀ni, ó ní ibà. (Yes, he has a fever.)
  3. Dúpé fẹ́ ràn Kúnlé lọ́wọ́ láti dé ilé ìwòsàn, ó sì fẹ́ pe tákísì fún un. (She wants to help him get to the clinic and call him a taxi.)
  4. Nọ́ọ̀sì bi Kúnlé pé kí ni orúkọ rẹ̀ àti ibi tí ó ń dun ún. (Name and where it hurts.)
  5. Accept any of: Ara mi kò yá rárá. — Meaning: "I am not well at all." Kò negates the declarative statement about his condition.

Listening — Comprehension table

  • Exchange 1: Symptom: leg hurts a lot (ẹsẹ̀ ń dun mi gan-an); general condition: not well; Need: to rest (suggested, not requested).
  • Exchange 2: Symptom/condition: fever and cough since yesterday; Need: help getting to the clinic.
  • Exchange 3: Symptom: ear and neck hurt; Need: none directly stated — offered help to wait for the doctor.

Bonus: (1) The leg; described with gan-an (a lot). (2) Fever and cough; needs help reaching the clinic. (3) Two body parts (ear and neck); told to wait, as the doctor will come shortly.

Reading — Comprehension

  1. Orí, ọrùn, inú, ẹ̀yìn, ẹsẹ̀, tàbí mìíràn. (Head, neck, stomach, back, leg, or other.)
  2. Orí àti ẹ̀yìn rẹ̀ ni ó ń dun ún, ó sì ní ibà. (Her head and back, and she has a fever.)
  3. Ó béèrè pé kí Fúnmi pe dókítà fún un. (She asks Fúnmi to call a doctor for her.)
  4. Nítorí pé ara rẹ̀ kò yá, orí àti ẹ̀yìn rẹ̀ sì ń dun ún gan-an. (Because she is not well, and her head and back hurt a lot.)
  5. Mo nílò ìrànlọ́wọ́ rẹ.

Chapter Review — Apá B

  1. (a) Etí ń dun mi. (b) Inú ń dun un. (c) Ẹsẹ̀ ń dun wa. (d) Ẹ̀yìn ń dun un gan-an.
  2. (a) Ara mi kò yá. (b) N kò lera. (c) Kò dára.
  3. Corrections: (i) Orí ń dun mi. — the body part is the subject; do not possess it with mi before ń dun. (ii) Ara mi kò yá. — kò negates a declarative statement; má is only for commands. (iii) Mo nílò ìrànlọ́wọ́. — nílò takes its object directly, with no preposition.
  4. Accept any three-sentence self-report that correctly uses the symptom pattern, a negation with kò, and a need or help expression. Example: Orí àti ẹsẹ̀ mi ń dun mi. Ara mi kò yá rárá. Mo nílò oògùn.

Chapter Review — Apá C

  1. Correct forms: ọrùn; ẹnu; eyín; àìsàn; ìrànlọ́wọ́.
  2. Tone patterns (accept phonetically motivated alternatives; flag for human review): ọrùn = L-L; àìsàn = L-L-L; oògùn = M(long)-L; ẹ̀yìn = L-L.
  3. Syllabic nasal example: ìrànlọ́wọ́ (the medial n). Nasal vowel examples: ọrùn, ẹnu. Accept any correctly identified examples from the vocabulary lists.

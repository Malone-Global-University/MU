Ìjíròrò Wa Àkọ́kọ́

Our First Conversations · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Sustain a short introductory conversation with a new acquaintance, covering greetings, identity, origin, family, and classroom topics.
  2. Introduce a third person, giving their name, origin, language, and relationship to you.
  3. Describe a family member or friend using possession, quality predicates, and noun-qualifier order.
  4. Identify classroom objects and ask for clarification when you do not know a word.
  5. Select the correct subject, possessive, object, emphatic, plural, and honorific pronoun for a given context.
  6. Consistently distinguish mid-tone ni (identification) from high-tone ní (possession and location).
  7. Use àwọn naturally to signal explicit plurality.
  8. Write a fully marked, organized 60–80-word personal profile using all Unit I grammar.
  9. Produce high, mid, and low tones, the seven oral vowels, and the key contrasts e/ẹ, o/ọ, and s/ṣ with developing accuracy.
  10. Complete a dictation passage with correct tone marks and underdots.

Communication Scenario

A community center in Lagos is hosting its first Yorùbá language and culture evening. People of many backgrounds — Nigerians who grew up speaking other first languages, members of the diaspora, and international visitors — have come to meet one another and practise their Yorùbá. You are one of them. By the end of the evening, you will have greeted several people, introduced a friend, described your family, asked the Yorùbá name of something you did not know, and swapped contact details. Everything in that evening belongs to what you have already learned.

  1. Look back at the vocabulary and grammar from Chapters 1 through 4. Write down five things you can now say in Yorùbá that you could not say four chapters ago.
  2. Think about the moment when a real conversation shifts from a greeting into a real exchange. What question usually opens that door? What is the Yorùbá equivalent you have already learned?
  3. Chapter 5 introduces no major new grammar. Instead, it gives you the chance to combine everything you have learned into flowing communication. What aspect of Unit I do you feel most confident about? What do you want to practise most?

Pronunciation and Orthography Review

Chapter 5 introduces no new pronunciation system. Instead, this section consolidates the four most important contrasts from Chapters 1 through 4 and provides a dictation exercise to assess cumulative orthographic accuracy. Use the review to identify which contrasts still need practice before you move into Unit II.

Consolidation 1 — The three tones

Pronunciation Review — High, mid, and low tone

Every syllable in Yorùbá carries a tone. The three tones are:

  • High tone — marked with an acute accent: á, é, í, ó, ú, ẹ́, ọ́. Produce it at the top of your comfortable speaking pitch.
  • Mid tone — unmarked in standard orthography: a, e, i, o, u, ẹ, ọ. Produce it at a steady, comfortable middle pitch. An unmarked vowel is not "neutral" — it is an active tone that carries meaning.
  • Low tone — marked with a grave accent: à, è, ì, ò, ù, ẹ̀, ọ̀. Produce it at the bottom of your comfortable speaking pitch.

Read these pairs aloud and notice how tone changes meaning:

  • orúkọ (name) — high-mid-low
  • olùkọ́ (teacher) — mid-low-high
  • ìwé (book) — low-high
  • ìyá (mother) — low-high
  • bàbá (father) — low-high
  • ẹbí (family) — low-high

Key rule: Tone is part of the word. Omitting tone marks is not a stylistic choice — it removes information that readers need. In formal Yorùbá writing, and throughout this textbook, every high and low tone must be marked.

Consolidation 2 — e/ẹ and o/ọ

Pronunciation Review — Underdotted vowels

Review these minimal contrasts from vocabulary already introduced. Say each pair aloud and feel the vowel quality change:

  • e (closer, like they) vs. ẹ (more open, like bed): compare fèrèsé (window) and pẹ̀ńsù (pencil)
  • o (closer, like go) vs. ọ (more open, like law): compare dòòmù (door) and ọ̀wọ̀ (ruler)

The underdot is part of the letter, not a decoration. ẹ and e are two distinct letters representing two distinct sounds. Omitting the underdot in writing is always an error. Practice writing words from Unit I that contain underdotted vowels until the habit is automatic.

Consolidation 3 — s and ṣ

Pronunciation Review — ṣ versus s

ṣ is a palato-alveolar sound similar to English sh in shoe. It is a separate letter from s, which sounds like the ordinary English s in sun. Both appear in Unit I vocabulary:

  • ṣ: kíláàsì (classroom), ṣíi (open), ẹ ṣé (thank you — honorific)
  • s: Báwo ni? (How are you?), sọ (speak / say)

As with the underdotted vowels, the underdot on ṣ must always be written. Do not replace ṣ with s in any learner-facing text.

Consolidation 4 — Nasal vowels and syllabic nasals

Pronunciation Review — Nasal vowels and syllabic n

Two features introduced in Chapters 3 and 4 need ongoing practice:

  • Nasal vowels are vowels pronounced with air flowing through the nose. In Standard Yorùbá orthography, nasalization is indicated by the spelling convention of the word, not by a separate diacritic for the learner. Listen carefully to the pronunciation of words such as ìbàdàn, orúnmìlà, and names ending in nasal sounds, and practise producing the vowel with nasal airflow rather than as a vowel followed by a separate English n.
  • Syllabic nasal: the sound spelled ń or n̄ in certain contexts forms a complete syllable on its own and carries a tone. In the question word Báwo ni? the final ni is two syllables, not one. The syllabic nasal will become even more important in Chapter 7 when the progressive aspect marker ń is introduced.

Grammar Note — Four things pronouns never do in Yorùbá
  1. They do not grammatically distinguish "he" from "she." Context identifies the person being referred to. Do not invent a separate feminine pronoun.
  2. Subject and possessive forms are not interchangeable. mo is the subject; mi is possessive or object in many patterns. ó is the subject; rẹ̀ is possessive.
  3. Tone is part of the pronoun. wọ́n (high, subject) and wọn (mid, possessive) look similar in unmarked text but are different forms. Always write tone marks on pronouns.
  4. The plural-or-honorific form ẹ (subject) and yín (possessive / object) must be used consistently throughout a conversation with the same person. Do not switch between o/rẹ and ẹ/yín for the same addressee.

5.2 ni versus ní — the most important contrast in Unit I

Purpose: consolidate accurate distinction of the identification particle from the possession and location particle.

  • Orúkọ mi ni Títí. ni — mid tone — identification My name is Títí.
  • Ìyá mi ní ọmọ mẹ́ta. ní — high tone — possession My mother has three children.
  • Bàbá mi wà ní Àbéòkúta. ní — high tone — location My father is in Àbéòkúta.
  • Kò ṣe ọmọ Nàìjíríà ni. ni — mid tone — identification (negative) He/she is not a Nigerian. (formulaic negative)
Grammar Note — How to remember the contrast

One reliable strategy is to ask yourself what the sentence is doing:

  • If the sentence is naming or identifying something — saying what something is or who someone is — use ni (mid tone, no accent mark).
  • If the sentence is expressing possession (someone has something) or location (someone or something is at a place), use ní (high tone, acute accent).

The contrast is carried entirely by tone. In unmarked informal digital writing, both words look the same — which is one of several reasons this textbook insists on marking every tone. Without the acute accent on ní, a reader cannot distinguish the two constructions.

5.3 àwọn and noun-phrase order

Purpose: confirm the placement of àwọn and qualifiers relative to the noun.

  • àwọn ọ̀rẹ́ mi àwọn + noun + possessive pronoun my friends
  • ìyá rẹ̀ tó dára noun + possessive pronoun + quality predicate his/her mother who is good / his/her kind mother
  • àwọn ìwé pupa méta àwọn + noun + color + numeral three red books

The head noun comes first. Qualifiers — including possessives, colors, and numerals — follow in the order: noun → possessive pronoun → color or other descriptor → numeral or quantifier. Full multi-modifier order will be analyzed in Chapter 6.

5.4 Description — quality predicates review

Purpose: use simple predicates to describe people and things without imposing English adjective-before-noun order.

  • Ọ̀rẹ́ mi ga. My friend is tall.
  • Bàbá rẹ̀ dára. His/her father is good / kind / fine.
  • Ìwé yín tóbi. Your book (honorific) is big.
  • Kíláàsì wa kéré. Our classroom is small.

In these sentences, the quality word follows the noun phrase it describes rather than preceding it as an adjective would in English. These are predicate constructions, not adjective-before-noun phrases. The quality word does not change form for number or gender.

Language Focus

Yorùbá organizes the same communication that English handles through several different verbs — "be," "have," "is at," "is in" — through distinct constructions with different particles and different tones. You have now learned three of the most important: identification with mid-tone ni, possession and location with high-tone ní, and existence with wà. In English, "My sister is a doctor," "My sister has a car," and "My sister is in Abuja" all use the word "is" — but in Yorùbá, each requires a different construction. The key to accurate production is to ask what the sentence is doing, not to look for a single Yorùbá equivalent of the English word "is."

This principle — that Yorùbá and English organize meaning through different structures — will be one of the most important habits to develop throughout the course. Chapters 1 through 4 have given you three major examples of it. Unit II will add more.

Grammar Note — Two expressions to notice

Ó dára láti pàdé yín (It is good to meet you) and Ẹ jẹ́ kí a sọ Yorùbá pọ̀ (Let us speak Yorùbá together) contain grammar — specifically the infinitive-like use of láti and the suggestion pattern jẹ́ ká — that will be taught productively in Chapters 14 and 17 respectively. Treat both expressions as formulaic units for now and learn their overall meaning. Do not yet try to build new sentences by analogy with their internal structure.

Also note: Ó dàbọ̀ fún ìsinsìnyí (Goodbye for now) is a formulaic leave-taking expression. Use it as a complete unit.

Writing Workshop

The major writing product for Chapter 5 is a fully marked personal profile of 60–80 words. This is the most extended and independent piece of Yorùbá writing you have attempted so far. Follow the steps below carefully.

  1. Plan your content. Decide what you will include. You need at least: your name, your origin, one or two languages you speak, one person in your family or social circle (with their relationship to you, one quality, and their origin or location), and one closing sentence about yourself or your reason for learning Yorùbá. Jot these facts in English or any language first.
  2. Draft in Yorùbá. Write your profile in Yorùbá. Begin with a greeting or a direct introduction. Use at least one example of ni (identification), one example of ní (possession or location), and one plural noun phrase with àwọn.
  3. Check your noun-phrase order. Scan every noun phrase. Is the qualifier — color, possessive pronoun, or quality word — placed after the noun? Fix any item where you have used English adjective-before-noun order.
  4. Write the tone marks. Go through every vowel. Mark every high and low tone. Leave mid-tone vowels unmarked. Add the underdot wherever ẹ, ọ, or ṣ appears.
  5. Count and revise. Count your words. If you have fewer than 60, add one more sentence. If you have more than 80 and the chapter requirement is strict, trim carefully without removing accuracy. Make a final check against the editing checklist.

Àtúnṣe àtúnyẹ̀wò — Editing checklist:

  • I used ni (mid tone, no accent) for identification — naming or saying what something is.
  • I used ní (high tone, acute accent) for possession or location — not as a general substitute for "is."
  • Every high tone carries an acute accent. Every low tone carries a grave accent. Mid-tone vowels carry no accent mark.
  • Every ẹ, ọ, and ṣ is spelled with an underdot — not replaced by plain e, o, or s.
  • Tone marks appear correctly on underdotted vowels: ẹ̀, ẹ́, ọ̀, ọ́.
  • All qualifiers — possessive pronouns, colors, quality words — appear after the noun, not before it.
  • Where I used àwọn, the noun itself has not changed form.
  • Subject and possessive pronouns are the correct forms — I did not use mo where mi is needed, or vice versa.
  • I maintained a consistent register — either familiar or honorific — when addressing or referring to the same person.
  • Every sentence begins with a capital letter and ends with a full stop or question mark.
  • My profile is between 60 and 80 words.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Selecting the right greeting

Unit I has introduced several Yorùbá greetings, and Chapter 5 is a good moment to review what governs the choice among them. The key variables are:

  • Time of day — morning (káàárọ̀), afternoon (káàsán), evening (káalẹ̀). Using the wrong time-of-day greeting is a minor but noticeable slip.
  • Relationship and status — the honorific prefix Ẹ signals respect. Using the bare form Káàárọ̀ without Ẹ is appropriate among peers or to a younger person, but using it to an elder or a teacher without other mitigating factors can read as abrupt.
  • Setting and purpose — there are greetings specific to someone working, someone who has returned from a journey, someone who has recently lost a family member, and someone who is ill. These situational greetings are an important part of Yorùbá communicative culture and will be introduced progressively across the course.

The general greeting Ẹ ń lẹ́ — introduced in Chapter 1 — is broadly usable across most daytime settings and relationships, which is why it is a useful formula for beginners. As your vocabulary and awareness grow, you will be able to select more precisely.

Culture in Context — Showing interest in a conversation

In many Yorùbá-speaking contexts, a good conversationalist does more than answer questions — they show active interest through follow-up questions, affirmations, and occasional exclamations. Expressions such as À! (Ah!), Ó yá! (Alright! / Oh, really!), Ó dára! (Good! / That's wonderful!), and Ìdí nì! (That's right! / Exactly!) signal that you are engaged. Using these small responses at appropriate moments will make your Yorùbá conversations feel much more natural.

Notice how Fọlákẹ́ uses Ó dára, Dávídì! in the model dialogue to encourage Dávídì's efforts. This kind of warm affirmation is common in language-learning interactions and in everyday Yorùbá conversation more broadly.

Culture in Context — Introduction and leave-taking

When introducing someone in Yorùbá, the standard pattern is to name the person and state their relationship to you, their origin, and one or two relevant facts. The expression Ẹ jẹ́ kí n mú yín pàdé ___ (Let me introduce you to ___) is a natural way to frame a third-party introduction, though learners should use it as a formulaic unit for now rather than building from its parts.

Leave-taking in Yorùbá often involves more than a single word. Ó dàbọ̀ (Goodbye) is the most common general farewell. Ẹ wá pẹ̀lé (Go safely — honorific or plural) and Ó dàbọ̀ fún ìsinsìnyí (Goodbye for now) acknowledge that the separation may be temporary. In many Yorùbá-speaking contexts, a goodbye that feels too abrupt — particularly with an elder — may be softened by a brief expression of good wishes before the final farewell.

Regional Variation — Names and identity across communities

The dialogue and reading texts in this chapter introduce speakers from Ìbàdàn, New York, Kano, Ògbómọ̀ṣọ́, and Àbéòkúta. This breadth is deliberate. Yorùbá is spoken not only in southwestern Nigeria but in communities across Benin, Togo, and the global diaspora, and many Nigerians from non-Yorùbá ethnic backgrounds speak Yorùbá as a second or third language — as Àmíná's character illustrates.

When you encounter a Yorùbá name in reading or conversation, do not assume that the speaker's only language is Yorùbá or that their identity is solely defined by their ethnic heritage. Language use, ethnicity, nationality, religion, and family history are distinct and sometimes overlapping categories. Accurate cultural understanding requires keeping these distinctions in mind.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Confusing ni (mid) with ní (high)

Error: Writing or saying *Ìyá mi ni ọmọ mẹ́ta when the meaning is "My mother has three children," or writing *Orúkọ mi ní Kọ́lá when the meaning is "My name is Kọ́lá."

Correction: Ìyá mi ní ọmọ mẹ́ta (possession — ní with high tone). Orúkọ mi ni Kọ́lá (identification — ni with mid tone, no accent). Ask yourself: is the sentence naming or identifying something? Use mid-tone ni. Is it expressing that someone has something or is at a place? Use high-tone ní.

Common Mistake — Using a subject pronoun where a possessive is needed

Error: Writing *ìwé mo or *ọ̀rẹ́ ó instead of ìwé mi (my book) or ọ̀rẹ́ rẹ̀ (his/her friend).

Correction: Subject and possessive pronouns are different forms. The possessive form follows the noun it modifies. mo and ó are subject pronouns used before verbs; mi and rẹ̀ are the possessive forms that follow nouns. Review the pronoun table in Section 5.1 until each pair is automatic.

Common Mistake — Switching register mid-conversation

Error: Beginning a conversation with the honorific form Ẹ and yín, then switching to the familiar o and rẹ without a change in relationship or context.

Correction: Choose a register — familiar or honorific — based on your relationship with the person and the setting, and maintain it consistently throughout the conversation. If you begin with Orúkọ yín ni kí? (honorific), follow up with Níbo ni ẹ ti wà? and refer to their possessions as yín, not rẹ. Inconsistent register is noticed by Yorùbá speakers and can create an impression of inattention or social miscalibration.

Common Mistake — Omitting tone marks in writing

Error: Writing *Oruko mi ni Titi. Iya mi wa ni Ibadan. Baba mi ni omo meta. — omitting all tone marks in a personal profile.

Correction: Every high and low tone must be marked, even in quickly written drafts. Orúkọ mi ni Títí. Ìyá mi wà ní Ìbàdàn. Bàbá mi ní ọmọ mẹ́ta. Unmarked text is incomplete text in Standard Yorùbá. The editing checklist in the Writing Workshop is designed to catch every missing mark before you submit.

Common Mistake — Translating "he" and "she" as different Yorùbá pronouns

Error: Inventing *o for "she" and *e for "he," or using the second-person form o to mean "she" or "he."

Correction: Yorùbá does not grammatically distinguish "he" from "she" in the third person. The third-person singular subject pronoun is ó (high tone) regardless of gender. Context — a name, a title, a previous mention — supplies the information that English expresses through gendered pronouns. Do not invent a gendered pronoun system that does not exist in the language.

  6. Sentences with rẹ̀: Orúkọ rẹ̀ ni Àmíná (His/her name is Àmíná) and Yorùbá rẹ dára (Your Yorùbá is good — note: rẹ here with mid tone addresses Dávídì in familiar register; rẹ̀ with low tone is the third-person possessive). Accept any well-argued identification from the text.
  7. Dávídì uses ẹ and yín when addressing Fọlákẹ́ — for example, Níbo ni ẹ ti wà? — signalling respect to someone he has just met who is also older and a professional.

  • Kọ́bí — from Ghana, father is Yorùbá and lives in Ìbàdàn; student (implied); has a paternal uncle and a grandmother in Nigeria; is studying Yorùbá to speak with family

Unit I Review — Apá C

  1. Correct forms: orúkọ, ìyá, bàbá, oníṣòwò, àwọn ọ̀rẹ́, kíláàsì, pẹ̀ńsù, ọ̀wọ̀.
  2. Accept any well-formed, fully marked sentences using Unit I vocabulary that correctly contain ẹ and ọ. Example: Ìyá mi ní pẹ̀ńsù ẹ̀fà. / Ọ̀rẹ́ mi wà ní Ìbàdàn.
  3. A model answer: In Yorùbá, tone is not expressive or emotional — it is part of the word's identity. The particle ni (mid tone, no accent) identifies or names something: Orúkọ mi ni Kọ́lá means "My name is Kọ́lá." The particle ní (high tone, acute accent) expresses possession or location: Mo ní ìwé means "I have a book." The two words look identical without tone marks, which is why marking every high and low tone is required in this textbook. Omitting the accent on ní makes it impossible to distinguish the two constructions.

Looking Ahead

Ohun tí o lè ṣe báyìí — What you can do now

You can conduct a short introductory conversation in Yorùbá — greeting, exchanging names and personal information, introducing another person, describing a family member, asking for clarification, and closing politely. You can write a fully marked personal profile using correct tone marks, underdots, pronoun forms, and noun-phrase order. You consistently distinguish mid-tone ni from high-tone ní. These abilities are the foundation for everything in Unit II and beyond.

Abala 6: Ní Ilé — Around the Home

Unit II opens with the world of the home. In Chapter 6 you will learn to name rooms, furniture, and household objects; state what is present, absent, or owned; and describe where people and things are located. Two closely related grammar systems take center stage: existence with wà and location with wà ní, building directly on your knowledge of high-tone ní from Chapters 3 and 5. You will also expand noun phrases with demonstratives, possessives, and qualifiers in their correct order. The communicative goal: describing your home or living space to someone who has never seen it.

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Combine home, routine, food, and market vocabulary in connected Yorùbá sentences and exchanges.
  2. Ask and answer follow-up questions using grammar from Chapters 6 through 9.
  3. Resolve a simple misunderstanding using repair expressions and clarification strategies.
  4. Describe preferences and practical daily needs using fẹ́, nífẹ̀ẹ́ sí, and negation patterns.
  5. Sustain a short transaction entirely in Yorùbá, moving from greeting through purchase to farewell.

Chapter 10 is the Unit II consolidation chapter. It introduces no major new grammar. Everything in this chapter — the vocabulary, the grammar patterns, the sentence structures, and the communication strategies — comes from Chapters 6 through 9. Your goal is to bring those building blocks together into fluent, connected everyday communication. Think of this chapter as a dress rehearsal: the language you practice here is the language you will need in every chapter from Unit III onward.

Communication Scenario

It is a weekday morning. Kẹ́hìndé, a university student who lives in a shared house in Ìbàdàn, is getting ready for the day. She checks what food is in the kitchen, realizes the household needs supplies, writes a quick message to her housemate Túndé, and heads to a nearby market stall. At the stall, she asks about two items, finds one price too high, and negotiates before completing her purchase. After returning home, she tells Túndé what she bought, what it cost, and what they still need before the weekend.

  1. Kẹ́hìndé's day involves four distinct situations: a home inventory check, a written message, a market transaction, and a spoken report. Which Unit II grammar patterns will she need in each situation? List them in English before you begin the chapter.
  2. Think about what can go wrong in a market transaction — an item is out of stock, a price is unclear, or you misheard the quantity. What would you say in Yorùbá if you needed to ask the seller to repeat something or clarify a price?
  3. How would Kẹ́hìndé's language change depending on whether she is talking to Túndé (a peer) or to an older market seller? Identify at least two specific differences in pronoun choice or politeness expressions from Chapters 1 through 9.

Pronunciation and Orthography Focus

Focus 1 — Tone across connected phrases

Pronunciation Focus — Linking words in connected speech

When you speak in sentences rather than single words, the tones of adjacent syllables interact. This chapter is a good opportunity to consolidate the most important patterns you have met across Unit II. Practise reading each phrase below as one connected unit, not as isolated words:

  • Mo ń jẹun ní ilé. — I am eating at home.
  • Ó máa ń jí ní òwúrọ̀. — She/He usually wakes up in the morning.
  • A kò fẹ́ oúnjẹ yẹn. — We do not want that food.
  • Wọ́n ń rà nǹkan ní ọjà. — They are buying things at the market.

Pay special attention to the particles ń (progressive, high tone) and kò (negation, low tone). Their tones must be distinct from one another and from the verbs that follow them.

Focus 2 — Pronoun and particle discrimination

Pronunciation Focus — Distinguishing short grammatical forms

Short grammatical words carry enormous meaning in Yorùbá. The following contrasts have been taught across Unit II. Read each pair aloud and make sure the tones are distinct:

  • ń (high — progressive marker) versus kò (low — negation)
  • mi (mid — first-person object: me) versus mo (mid — first-person subject: I)
  • rẹ (mid — second-person possessive: your) versus rẹ̀ (low — third-person object: it/him/her)
  • ẹ (mid — honorific/plural subject) versus ó (high — third-person singular subject)
  • ni (mid — identification) versus ní (high — location/possession preposition)

Dictation practice: ask a partner to say one item from each pair at random while you write the correct form including all diacritics. Switch roles and repeat.

Focus 3 — Nasal vowels and syllabic nasals

Pronunciation Focus — Nasal sounds in everyday vocabulary

Unit II introduced several words with nasal vowels and syllabic nasals. Read the following words aloud and make sure you are producing the nasal quality as part of the vowel rather than as a separate consonant following it:

  • oúnjẹ — food (the n in un is part of a nasal vowel sequence)
  • nǹkan — something, thing (syllabic nasal ǹ with low tone)
  • ìjókòó — sitting (nasal effect in context)
  • ń — the progressive marker itself is a syllabic nasal with high tone

A syllabic nasal (ń or ǹ) is a nasal consonant that forms a syllable on its own and carries its own tone. It is not a vowel, but it functions as the tone-bearing unit of its syllable. When you say Mo ń jẹun, the ń is a complete syllable with high tone standing between mo and jẹun.

Grammar Review

This section reviews the core grammar systems introduced in Unit II. No new grammar is presented. Use the review as a diagnostic: if any pattern feels uncertain, reread the relevant section in Chapters 6–9 before attempting the extended exercises later in this chapter.

Review 1 — Location, existence, and possession

From Chapter 6: wà, wà ní, and ní for location, existence, and possession.

  • Àwọn ẹyin wà nínú ìkòkò. location: wà + nínú (inside) The eggs are inside the pot.
  • Bísí ní ìgò epo méjì. possession: ní + noun phrase Bísí has two bottles of oil.
  • Kẹ́hìndé wà níbí báyìí. location + time expression Kẹ́hìndé is here now.
  • Oúnjẹ kò wà. negative existence: kò + wà There is no food. / Food is not available.
Grammar Reminder — Three meanings of ní

You have met ní (high tone) in three overlapping uses:

  1. Possession: Kẹ́hìndé ní àpò kékeré. — Kẹ́hìndé has a small bag.
  2. Location preposition: Mo wà ní ilé. — I am at home.
  3. Introducing an object after certain verbs: Fún mi ní àtàrí mẹ́ta. — Give me three tomatoes.

In all three uses, the tone is high. Contrast with mid-tone ni, which marks identity or focus (Olùkọ́ ni Bísí. — Bísí is a teacher).

Review 2 — Progressive and habitual aspect

From Chapter 7: ń for ongoing events and máa ń for regular activities.

  • Túndé ń jẹun báyìí. subject + ń + verb (ongoing right now) Túndé is eating right now.
  • Kẹ́hìndé máa ń jí ní àárọ̀. subject + máa ń + verb (habitual / regular) Kẹ́hìndé usually wakes up early in the morning.
  • A máa ń jẹ ẹbọ ní Saturday. habitual with day of week We usually eat àmàlà on Saturdays.
Grammar Reminder — ń versus máa ń

These two particles are never interchangeable. Use ń when an event is happening right now, at this very moment. Use máa ń when an action is characteristic, habitual, or regularly recurring. Ask yourself: is the event happening now or is it something that usually happens? The answer determines which marker to use.

Compare: Mo ń kọ̀ láti ọwọ́ báyìí (I am writing right now) versus Mo máa ń kọ̀ ní òwúrọ̀ (I usually write in the morning).

Review 3 — Negation: kò and kì í

From Chapter 8: kò for basic verbal negation; kì í for habitual or general negation.

  • Mo kò fẹ́ oúnjẹ yẹn. kò negates the verb fẹ́ (want) in this moment I don't want that food.
  • Ó kì í jẹ ẹran. kì í negates a general or habitual pattern She/He does not eat meat. (general — never or habitually not)
  • Wọ́n kò wà níbí. kò negates wà (existence/location) They are not here.
  • Kẹ́hìndé kì í mu ọtí. general negative — characteristic behaviour Kẹ́hìndé does not drink alcohol. (habitually)
Grammar Reminder — kò versus kì í

kò negates a specific or currently relevant state or action. kì í negates something general, habitual, or characteristic — a permanent or usual pattern. The same distinction mirrors the affirmative contrast between a bare verb or ń (current) and máa ń (habitual). When in doubt, ask: is this negation about right now or about generally/always?

Review 4 — Object pronouns and noun-phrase order

From Chapters 8–9: object forms mi, rẹ̀, yín; noun before qualifier.

  • Fún mi ní àtàrí mẹ́ta. mi = first-person object; noun + numeral order Give me three tomatoes.
  • Mo fẹ́ rẹ̀. rẹ̀ = third-person singular object (it), low tone I want it.
  • aṣọ pupa tóbi méjì noun + color + size + numeral two large red cloths

Review 5 — Introductory serial verb pattern

From Chapter 9: mú ___ fún mi and mú ___ wá.

  • Mú aṣọ yìí fún mi. Take this cloth and give it to me. / Bring me this cloth.
  • Ẹ mú omi díẹ̀ wá. Bring some water. (honorific)
Language Focus

Yorùbá expresses time and viewpoint through particles and context, not through verb endings. By the end of Unit II, you have four central ways of framing an action's time and character: the bare verb (for general or completed events in clear context), ń (ongoing now), máa ń (habitual or regular), and negation with kò or kì í. English speakers often expect a one-to-one mapping from English tense to Yorùbá form. That mapping does not exist. A single English sentence may correspond to different Yorùbá constructions depending on whether the action is happening right now, usually happens, or generally does not happen. The question to ask is always: what is the meaning and viewpoint I want to communicate?

Communication Repair

Every language learner encounters moments when communication breaks down — you mishear something, the speaker talks too fast, or your Yorùbá sentence comes out wrong and the listener looks confused. Skilled communicators in any language manage these moments through repair: they signal the problem, request help, and confirm that understanding has been restored. This section teaches you the key repair moves in Yorùbá daily communication.

Repair moves in everyday Yorùbá

Repair Move 1 — Asking for repetition
Ẹ tún sọ ọ, ẹ jọ̀wọ́. / Tún sọ ọ, jọ̀wọ́.
Please say it again. (honorific / familiar)
Repair Move 2 — Signalling that you missed something
Mi ò gbọ́.
I didn't hear / I didn't catch that.
Repair Move 3 — Requesting slower speech
Ẹ sọ ọ lọ́ra díẹ̀, ẹ jọ̀wọ́.
Please speak a little more slowly. (honorific)
Repair Move 4 — Signalling non-understanding
Mi ò yé mi.
I don't understand.
Repair Move 5 — Confirming understanding
Ó ṣe kedere.
That is clear. / I understand now.
Repair Move 6 — Gently correcting a misunderstanding
Rárá, kì í ṣe bẹ́ẹ̀.
No, that is not right. / No, that is not what I meant.
Language Note — Repair and respect

When asking an older person or a market seller to repeat something or speak more slowly, always use the honorific forms: ẹ tún sọ ọ rather than tún sọ ọ, and add ẹ jọ̀wọ́ (please — honorific). With a peer or younger person, the familiar form is natural. Repair requests are not rude — they are a sign that you are taking communication seriously. A Yorùbá speaker will generally appreciate being asked to clarify rather than being misunderstood.

Practice — Repair in context

For each situation, write the most appropriate repair expression in Yorùbá. Choose between honorific and familiar forms based on who is speaking.

  1. You are at the market. The seller states the price, but you did not catch it. (You are speaking to an older seller.)
  2. Your housemate Túndé tells you something about the weekend, but he spoke too fast. (Peer relationship.)
  3. A market seller has just explained something in Yorùbá that you did not fully understand. (Older seller.)
  4. After asking the seller to repeat the price, you now understand it clearly. What do you say?
  5. Túndé thinks you said you do not want dinner, but you actually said you do want it. How do you correct him gently?

Model Dialogue

The dialogue below brings together home, routine, food, and market language from all four chapters of Unit II. It also includes two repair moves. Read it carefully before answering the comprehension questions.

Ipò: Ilé Kẹ́hìndé àti Túndé ní Ìbàdàn — owúrọ̀ ọjọ́ Ọjọ́bọ. Kẹ́hìndé ń sọ̀rọ̀ pẹ̀lú Túndé ṣáájú kí ó tó lọ sí ọjà. Túndé jókòó ní yàrá ìjókòó ó sì ń mu kọfí.
Setting: Kẹ́hìndé and Túndé's house in Ìbàdàn — Thursday morning. Kẹ́hìndé is speaking with Túndé before going to the market. Túndé is sitting in the living room and drinking coffee.

KẸHÌNDÉ Túndé, epo àti àtàrí kò wà nínú ilé ìdáná mọ́. Túndé, there is no more oil or tomatoes in the kitchen.
TÚNDÉ Bẹ́ẹ̀ ni? Mo máa ń ra wọn ní ọjà Àmáládé ní gbogbo ọ̀sẹ̀. Àmọ́ mi ò lọ lóòtọ́ ọ̀sẹ̀ yìí. Really? I usually buy them at Àmáládé market every week. But I did not actually go this week.
KẸHÌNDÉ Ó dára. Màá lọ báyìí. Mo fẹ́ rà epo kan, àtàrí mẹ́fà, àti ẹja. A ní owó tó bí? Alright. I will go now. I want to buy one bottle of oil, six tomatoes, and fish. Do we have enough money?
TÚNDÉ Bẹ́ẹ̀ ni, owó wà. Ẹgbẹ̀rún mẹ́ta náírà wà nínú àpò mi. Mú rẹ̀. Yes, there is money. There are three thousand naira in my bag. Take it.
KẸHÌNDÉ Ẹ̀ẹ́, o ṣeun. Màá padà wá ní kíkánkíkán. Thank you. I will come back quickly.

(Kẹ́hìndé ń lọ sí ọjà. Ó rí Ìyá Tàyọ̀ ní ojú ọnà. — Kẹ́hìndé goes to the market. She sees Ìyá Tàyọ̀ at a stall.)

KẸHÌNDÉ Ẹ káàárọ̀, Ìyá. Ẹ ní ẹja tútù bí? Good morning, Ma. Do you have fresh fish?
ÌYÁ TÀYỌ̀ Káàárọ̀, ọmọ mi. Bẹ́ẹ̀ ni, ẹja tútù wà. Ẹja tóbi kan jẹ́ ẹgbẹ̀rún méjì náírà. Good morning, my child. Yes, there is fresh fish. One large fish costs two thousand naira.
KẸHÌNDÉ Ẹ jọ̀wọ́ — iye rẹ̀ jẹ́ mélòó ẹ ní? Mi ò gbọ́ dáadáa. Excuse me — how much did you say the price is? I did not hear properly.
ÌYÁ TÀYỌ̀ Ẹgbẹ̀rún méjì náírà, ọmọ mi. Two thousand naira, my child.
KẸHÌNDÉ Àá, ó pọ̀ jù. Ẹgbẹ̀rún kan àti igba náírà bí? Ah, it is too expensive. How about one thousand two hundred naira?
ÌYÁ TÀYỌ̀ Ẹgbẹ̀rún kan àti ọgọ́rùn-ún márùn-ún náírà. Ó pẹ tán, ọmọ mi. One thousand five hundred naira. That is final, my child.
KẸHÌNDÉ Ó dára. Mo gbà. Fún mi ní ẹja yẹn àti epo kan àti àtàrí mẹ́fà pẹ̀lú. Alright. I accept. Give me that fish and also one oil and six tomatoes.
ÌYÁ TÀYỌ̀ Ó yá. Gbogbo rẹ̀ jẹ́ ẹgbẹ̀rún méjì àti igba náírà ní àpapọ̀. Alright. Everything comes to two thousand two hundred naira in total.
KẸHÌNDÉ Ẹ gba owó yìí. Ẹ ṣé púpọ̀, Ìyá. Take this money. Thank you very much, Ma.
ÌYÁ TÀYỌ̀ Ẹ ṣé, ọmọ mi. Ẹ wá padà. Thank you, my child. Come again.

Àwọn ìbéèrè nípa ọ̀rọ̀ àdáhùnṣe — Comprehension questions:

  1. Kí ni kò wà nínú ilé ìdáná Kẹ́hìndé àti Túndé? (What is not in the kitchen?)
  2. Túndé máa ń rà ohun tó nílò wọn ní ọjà wo? (Which market does Túndé usually shop at?)
  3. Kẹ́hìndé fẹ́ rà kí? Ṣe àkójọ. (What does Kẹ́hìndé want to buy? List the items.)
  4. Kẹ́hìndé lo irinṣẹ́ àtúntọ̀ wo nígbà tí ó kò gbọ́ iye ẹja dáadáa? Kọ gbolohùn náà. (Which repair expression did Kẹ́hìndé use when she did not hear the fish price properly? Write the sentence.)
  5. Iye ẹja àkọ́kọ́ àti iye tí àwọn méjì wọn gbà ní ìparí jẹ́ mélòó? (What was the original fish price and what did they agree on?)
  6. Owó àpapọ̀ gbogbo ohun tí Kẹ́hìndé rà jẹ́ mélòó? (What was the total amount Kẹ́hìndé paid for everything?)
  7. Find all the Unit II grammar patterns used in the dialogue: location with wà, progressive ń, habitual máa ń, negation with kò, object pronouns, and a serial pattern. Write one example of each from the dialogue.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Shared houses and household management

Shared houses — known in different registers as ilé àjọṣe or simply a compound — are a common living arrangement across many Yorùbá-speaking urban areas. University students, young professionals, and extended family members often share kitchen facilities, courtyards, and household responsibilities. Communication about shared supplies, chores, expenses, and schedules is part of everyday life.

The model dialogue in this chapter reflects one common scenario: a household member discovers supplies are missing, communicates this to a housemate, and goes to the market. This kind of practical household communication is an important register for A1–A2 learners and one that is rarely covered in textbooks that focus exclusively on tourist or business contexts.

Culture in Context — Politeness and repair in Yorùbá communication

Requests for clarification and repetition are not considered rude in Yorùbá conversational culture when expressed appropriately. Using ẹ jọ̀wọ́ (please — honorific) or jọ̀wọ́ (please — familiar) signals respect and communicative good faith. Speakers of Yorùbá generally appreciate a learner who takes the language seriously enough to ask for help rather than nodding and guessing.

Saying Mi ò yé mi (I don't understand) is direct but not impolite. If you want to be more indirect, you can say Ẹ jọ̀wọ́, ẹ sọ ọ lọ́ra díẹ̀ (Please speak a little more slowly), which signals comprehension difficulty without explicitly saying you did not understand — a slightly softer approach that some learners prefer in formal market contexts.

Culture in Context — Food, household, and community purchasing patterns

Household food purchasing in many Yorùbá-speaking communities combines daily fresh-market trips, weekly large-volume shopping, and informal neighborhood exchanges. Not every household follows the same pattern, and purchasing habits vary by income, occupation, location, household structure, and individual preference.

The term ọjà covers a range of market types — from large daily markets in urban centers to small roadside stalls, periodic weekly markets, and, increasingly, fixed-price shops and supermarkets. Learners should not assume that every market transaction involves negotiation or that every household shops at an open-air market. The language you have learned in Unit II transfers across all these contexts.

Regional Variation — Household vocabulary across Yorùbá-speaking areas

Some household terms vary across Nigerian, Beninese, and Togolese Yorùbá-speaking areas and across generations. The terms taught in this course — ilé ìdáná (kitchen), yàrá ìjókòó (sitting room), àgbàlá (compound/backyard) — reflect Standard Nigerian Yorùbá usage. In informal speech and in some regional varieties, you may encounter alternative terms. When you come across a household word that does not match what you have learned, note it, identify the source or region if possible, and keep it separate from your core productive vocabulary.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Confusing ń (progressive) with ní (preposition)

Error: Writing *Mo ní jẹun báyìí — using the possession/location preposition ní (high tone) where the progressive marker ń (high-tone syllabic nasal) is needed.

Correction: Mo ń jẹun báyìí. The progressive marker ń is a syllabic nasal — it is a consonant acting as a syllable, not a vowel. It appears directly after the subject pronoun and before the verb. The preposition ní introduces a location or object; it does not precede the main verb. These two forms look similar but are grammatically and phonologically distinct.

Common Mistake — Using kò for habitual negation when kì í is needed

Error: Writing *Ó kò jẹ ẹran rárá — using kò to express that someone habitually or generally does not eat meat.

Correction: Ó kì í jẹ ẹran rárá. When you want to express a general, permanent, or habitual negation — something that is characteristic or always true — use kì í. Reserve kò for specific events: something is not happening right now, or did not happen at a particular time. The distinction directly mirrors the affirmative contrast between ń (happening now) and máa ń (habitually).

Common Mistake — Omitting the tone mark on kò

Error: Writing *Mo ko fẹ́ oúnjẹ yẹn — leaving off the grave accent on kò.

Correction: Mo kò fẹ́ oúnjẹ yẹn. The low tone on kò is part of its identity as a negation marker. An unmarked ko is not Standard Yorùbá. The same applies to ń (progressive) and every other grammatical particle in this course: a particle without its tone mark is an incomplete word. In fully marked Standard Yorùbá, no tone mark may be omitted from learner-facing content.

Common Mistake — Responding to a repair request by speaking faster

Communication error: When a learner says Ẹ tún sọ ọ, ẹ jọ̀wọ́ and the speaker speeds up, repeats louder, or switches to English rather than repeating clearly.

Strategy: Ẹ sọ ọ lọ́ra díẹ̀, ẹ jọ̀wọ́. If repeating did not help, explicitly ask for slower speech. In a market setting, it is perfectly appropriate to say both Ẹ tún sọ ọ (say it again) and then Ẹ sọ ọ lọ́ra díẹ̀ (speak more slowly) if needed. Sellers in Yorùbá-speaking markets are generally familiar with learners and will usually oblige a polite request to slow down.

Common Mistake — Writing ìgbésí ayé without tone marks

Error: Writing *igbesi aye — the chapter title and a key vocabulary item stripped of all diacritics.

Correction: ìgbésí ayé — the low tone on ì, the high tone on é, and the high tone on the í of ísí must all be present. The phrase means "life" or "the way one lives." Writing it without diacritics is not informal Yorùbá spelling — it is an error in any learning or literacy context. The chapter title of this course also uses the fully marked form: Ìgbésí Ayé Ojoojúmọ́.

Performance Task

Iṣẹ́ aṣeyọrí — Abala 10 àti Ìpín II

Ìjíròrò ìgbésí ayé ojoojúmọ́ — Everyday life in Yorùbá

The Chapter 10 Performance Task is also the Unit II Assessment. It has three parts and must be completed without preparation notes for the spoken sections. All written Yorùbá must carry full tone marks and underdots.

Part 1 — Oral scenario (4–5 minutes):
Your instructor or partner will play the role of a housemate and then a market seller. You will move through the following connected sequence without stopping:

  1. Tell your housemate what supplies are missing and what you plan to buy. Use at least one wà/kò wà statement and one habitual statement (máa ń).
  2. At the market, greet the seller appropriately, ask about and purchase at least two items, use a price question and a repair move, negotiate or accept at least one price, and complete the transaction with a serial pattern and a farewell.
  3. Return home and report to your housemate: what you bought, what each item cost, and what the household still needs.

Part 2 — Written description (100–120 words):
Submit the final draft of your everyday life description from the Writing Workshop. It must cover all four Unit II domains (home, routine, food, shopping) and include all required grammar patterns.

Part 3 — Reading comprehension (5 minutes):
Your instructor will provide a short unseen text — a menu, message, or market notice — in fully marked Standard Yorùbá. Answer four comprehension questions in Yorùbá. You may not use a dictionary.

Assessment criteria: The spoken task is evaluated on task completion across all three stages, comprehensibility, appropriate register with the housemate (familiar) and seller (honorific), correct use of progressive/habitual/negation markers, tone accuracy on particles, correct object pronouns, repair move usage, and serial verb naturalness. The written task is evaluated on domain coverage, aspect marker accuracy, negation accuracy, noun-phrase order, tone and underdot accuracy, and organization. The reading task is evaluated on accuracy of comprehension in each answer.

Reading — Comprehension

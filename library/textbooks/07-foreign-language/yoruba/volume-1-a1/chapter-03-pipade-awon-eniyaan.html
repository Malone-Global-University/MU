Pípàdé Àwọn Ènìyàn

Meeting People · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Introduce another person by name, origin, language, and profession.
  2. Ask and answer questions about where someone comes from or lives.
  3. State your nationality, the languages you speak, and your profession.
  4. Distinguish mid-tone ni (identification) from high-tone ní (possession and location).
  5. Use the location construction wà ní to say where someone or something is.
  6. Use emphatic pronouns in controlled identity and contrast patterns.
  7. Recognize the formulaic negative identification pattern kì í ṣe.
  8. Count and use numbers from 21 to 100.
  9. Produce tone correctly on short pronouns and particles.
  10. Write personal profiles with full tone marks, underdots, and Standard Yorùbá orthography.

Communication Scenario

A community language event is taking place in Lagos. People from different countries, cities, and professional backgrounds have gathered to practice Yorùbá. You are paired with two strangers. You know their names but nothing else. Your task is to find out where each person is from, what languages they speak, and what they do for work — and to share the same information about yourself. By the end of this chapter, you will have the language to do exactly that.

  1. Think of three questions you would want to ask someone you have just met. Write them in English. You will learn the Yorùbá equivalents in this chapter.
  2. In English, we often say "She is a teacher" and "She is in Lagos" using the same word — is. Do you think Yorùbá handles both meanings the same way? Hold that question as you work through the grammar today.

Pronunciation and Orthography

Tone on pronouns and particles

Short function words — pronouns, question particles, and aspect markers — carry essential tone in Yorùbá. The tone on these forms is part of their meaning and grammatical identity. Losing the tone or substituting a different pitch can change what a sentence communicates.

This chapter introduces a critical contrast: mid-tone ni versus high-tone ní. Both are spelled with the same two letters, but their tone and function are completely different. Attending to tone on short words is a skill you must develop from this chapter onwards.

FormToneMarkFunctionExample
niMidNo markIdentification and focusDókítà ni ó ṣe. — It is a doctor that she is.
níHighAcute accent on iPossession; locationMo ní ìwé. — I have a book. / Ó wà ní Èkó. — She is in Lagos.
wàLowGrave accent on aExistence; location predicateÓ wà ní Ìbàdàn. — He is in Ibadan.
moMidNo markFirst-person singular subjectMo jẹ́ olùkọ́. — I am a teacher.
óHighAcute accent on oThird-person singular subjectÓ ti wá láti Gánà. — She/he comes from Ghana.
Pronunciation Focus — ni versus ní

The two most easily confused particles in this chapter are ni (mid tone, no mark) and ní (high tone, acute accent on i). In speech, mid tone is a steady, medium pitch. High tone is noticeably higher. In writing, mid tone is unmarked on an ordinary vowel, while high tone always carries an acute accent.

  • Ìyẹn ni ìwé. — That is a book. (identification: mid-tone ni)
  • Mo ní ìwé. — I have a book. (possession: high-tone ní)
  • Ó wà ní Èkó. — She is in Lagos. (location: high-tone ní)

Practice these three sentences aloud until the pitch difference feels natural. The distinction is not a detail — it determines what the sentence means.

Oral versus nasal vowels — awareness practice

Several vocabulary items in this chapter contain nasal vowels. A nasal vowel is produced with airflow through both the mouth and the nose simultaneously. It is not the same as an oral vowel followed by a separately pronounced n. Nasal vowels must be heard and practiced as single vowel sounds with nasal airflow, not as two-part sequences.

The following words from this chapter contain nasal vowels or nasal influence. They are flagged here for awareness practice. Your instructor or audio resource should model each one carefully.

Syllable structure and vowel-initial words

Yorùbá syllables are typically open — they end in a vowel rather than a consonant. When a word begins with a vowel, it follows directly after the preceding word with no inserted consonant. This is different from English, which sometimes inserts a glottal stop before vowel-initial words in careful speech.

Practice linking the following words smoothly to preceding pronouns or particles:

  • Mo ń sọ Ìgbò. I speak Igbo. Smooth transition from sọ to Ìgbò — no inserted consonant
  • Ó wà ní Abúùjá. She is in Abuja. The vowel in Abúùjá follows ní without a break

Grammar Instruction

3.1 Identification with ni — review and expansion

Purpose: use the mid-tone identification particle to name, classify, or specify a person, thing, or category.

In Chapter 1 you learned the pattern Orúkọ mi ni ___ (My name is ___) and in Chapter 2 you used Èyí ni ___ (This is ___). The same mid-tone particle ni appears whenever you are identifying or classifying someone or something. The pattern is:

  • subject or demonstrative + ni + identity or classification X is [identity]
  • Olùkọ́ ni ó ṣe. teacher + ni + she/he does/is It is a teacher that she is. / She is a teacher. (focus)
  • Ìyẹn ni Kọ̀tónù. That is Cotonou.
  • Yorùbá ni èdè tí mo ń sọ. Yorùbá is the language I speak.
Grammar Note — ni does not mean "is"

It is tempting to translate ni as "is," but this leads to errors because English "is" covers several functions that Yorùbá handles differently. Specifically, ni is an identification and focus particle — it connects a subject or demonstrative to its identity or classification. For location ("She is in Lagos") and possession ("I have a book"), Yorùbá uses completely different structures. Treat ni as its own particle with its own job, not as a translation of the English verb "to be."

3.2 High-tone ní — possession and location

Purpose: express that someone has something, or that something is at a certain place, using high-tone ní.

High-tone ní functions in two related ways at this stage. First, it can signal possession — similar to the English verb "have." Second, it follows location predicates to indicate where something or someone is. Both uses carry the same high tone and the same spelling, but context makes the meaning clear.

Possession with ní:

  • Mo ní ìwé. I + ní + book I have a book.
  • Ó ní àpótí pupa. She has a red bag.
  • Àwọn akẹ́kọ̀ọ́ ní àwọn pẹ̀ńsù. The students have pencils.

Location with wà ní:

The location predicate wà (low tone) signals existence or presence. It is followed by high-tone ní and the place name. The full pattern is subject + wà ní + place.

  • Ó wà ní Èkó. she/he + wà + ní + Lagos She is in Lagos. / He is in Lagos.
  • Mo wà ní Ìbàdàn. I am in Ibadan.
  • Àwọn akẹ́kọ̀ọ́ wà ní kíláàsì. The students are in the classroom.
  • Dókítà Àjọké wà ní Abúùjá. Doctor Àjọké is in Abuja.
Language Focus

Tone distinguishes mid-tone ni from high-tone ní — and the two do completely different jobs. English speakers find this contrast challenging because English uses the single word "is" for identification (She is a doctor), location (She is in Lagos), and possession (She has a book). Yorùbá distributes these meanings across three distinct constructions:

  • Identification: mid-tone ni — Dókítà ni ó ṣe (She is a doctor)
  • Location: wà ní — Ó wà ní Èkó (She is in Lagos)
  • Possession: high-tone ní — Mo ní ìwé (I have a book)

Never use mid-tone ni to say where something is, and never use wà ní to identify what something is. Mixing these structures is one of the most common errors for English-speaking learners of Yorùbá, and learning the contrast now will prevent persistent mistakes throughout the course.

3.3 Emphatic pronouns in identity patterns

Purpose: use emphatic pronouns to introduce, contrast, or emphasize the subject of a statement.

Yorùbá has a set of emphatic or independent pronouns used when you want to bring special attention to the participant in a statement — for example, to say "As for me, I am a teacher" or to contrast "She is a doctor, but he is a lawyer." These are different from the subject pronouns you have already learned. At this stage, learn the emphatic pronouns in the table below in the controlled patterns provided.

2nd singular (familiar)oìwọ
3rd singularóòun
1st pluralaàwa
2nd plural / honorificẹyín
3rd pluralwọ́nàwọn

The emphatic pronoun àwọn for the third-person plural is the same form as the plurality marker introduced in Chapter 2. Context determines whether it refers to a group of people ("they," emphatic) or marks a noun as plural. At this stage, use emphatic pronouns in the two controlled patterns shown below. A fuller analysis of emphasis and contrast will be developed in Chapter 19.

Pattern 1 — Emphatic subject with identification:

  • Èmi ni olùkọ́. emphatic-I + ni + teacher I am the teacher. / It is I who is the teacher.
  • Ìwọ ni Àjọké bí? Are you Àjọké?
  • Òun ni dókítà. She / he is the doctor. (with emphasis or contrast)

Pattern 2 — Contrast using emphatic pronouns:

  • Èmi jẹ́ olùkọ́; òun jẹ́ dókítà. I am a teacher; she is a doctor.
  • Ìwọ wà ní Èkó; àwa wà ní Ìbàdàn. You are in Lagos; we are in Ibadan.

3.4 Formulaic negative identification — kì í ṣe

Purpose: recognize and use the negative identification pattern to say what someone or something is not.

The formulaic pattern kì í ṣe negates an identification statement. It is the negative counterpart of the ni identification pattern and is introduced here as a productive formulaic expression. Its full grammatical analysis — including the negation system and the functions of ṣe — is addressed in Chapter 8.

  • Kì í ṣe dókítà ni mo jẹ́. I am not a doctor.
  • Kì í ṣe olùkọ́ ni ó ṣe. She is not a teacher.
  • Kì í ṣe Nàìjíríà ni ó ti wá. She does not come from Nigeria.
Grammar Note — Treat kì í ṣe as a complete unit for now

kì í ṣe contains the habitual or general negation marker kì í and the verb ṣe. Attempting to take this apart and recombine it freely at this stage can lead to errors. Learn it as a reliable negative identification phrase and practice the examples above. The negation system will be taught step by step starting in Chapter 8.

Note also the tone on ṣe: it begins with ṣ (the retroflex consonant introduced in Chapter 2), not ordinary s. Omitting the underdot changes the letter and the sound.

3.5 Asking and answering personal information questions

Purpose: combine the constructions above to exchange origin, language, and profession information.

The following question-and-answer sequences bring together all the grammar in this chapter. Practice each one as a complete exchange until it is fluent.

  • Q: Níbo ni o ti wá? — A: Mo ti wá láti Gánà. Where do you come from? — I come from Ghana.
  • Q: Níbo ni o ń gbé? — A: Mo ń gbé ní Abúùjá. Where do you live? — I live in Abuja.
  • Q: Èdè wo ni o ń sọ? — A: Mo ń sọ Yorùbá àti Gẹ̀ẹ́sì. What language(s) do you speak? — I speak Yorùbá and English.
  • Q: Iṣẹ́ wo ni o ń ṣe? — A: Mo jẹ́ oníṣòwò. What work do you do? — I am a trader.

Model Dialogue

Ipò: Ìgbàdùn èdè ní Èkó. Àwọn ènìyàn tó ti wá láti orílẹ̀-èdè oríṣiríṣi ń pàdé ara wọn. Ṣéun àti Bísí pàdé Àjọké tuntun.
Setting: A language event in Lagos. People from various countries are meeting one another. Ṣéun and Bísí meet Àjọké for the first time.

ṢÉUN Ẹ káàbọ̀, ẹ jọ̀wọ́. Orúkọ mi ni Ṣéun. Tó o bá yá, kí ni orúkọ rẹ? Welcome, please. My name is Ṣéun. If you don't mind, what is your name?
ÀJỌKÉ Orúkọ mi ni Àjọké. Mo dúpẹ́ lórí ìgbàlà rẹ. Ẹ káàárọ̀. My name is Àjọké. Thank you for your welcome. Good morning.
ṢÉUN Níbo ni o ti wá, Àjọké? Where do you come from, Àjọké?
ÀJỌKÉ Mo ti wá láti Bẹ̀nẹ̀, ṣùgbọ́n mo ń gbé ní Èkó báyìí. I come from Benin, but I live in Lagos now.
BÍSÍ Ó yẹ, ó yẹ! Orúkọ mi ni Bísí. Mo ti wá láti Ìbàdàn. Àjọké, iṣẹ́ wo ni o ń ṣe? How nice! My name is Bísí. I come from Ibadan. Àjọké, what work do you do?
ÀJỌKÉ Mo jẹ́ nọ́ọ̀sì. Mo ń ṣiṣẹ́ ní ilé-ìwòsàn tó wà ní Èkó. Ẹ ńkọ́? Iṣẹ́ wo ni ẹ ń ṣe? I am a nurse. I work at a hospital in Lagos. And you? What work do you do?
ṢÉUN Èmi jẹ́ olùkọ́. Mo ń kọ́ ẹ̀kọ́ Yorùbá. I am a teacher. I teach Yorùbá.
BÍSÍ Àti èmi, mo jẹ́ akẹ́kọ̀ọ́. Mo ń kọ́ Yorùbá ní Yunifásítì. And I am a student. I am studying Yorùbá at university.
ÀJỌKÉ Ó dára! Èdè wo ni ẹ ń sọ? Good! What languages do you speak?
ṢÉUN Mo ń sọ Yorùbá, Gẹ̀ẹ́sì, àti Hausa díẹ̀. I speak Yorùbá, English, and a little Hausa.
BÍSÍ Mo ń sọ Yorùbá àti Gẹ̀ẹ́sì. Kì í ṣe Hausa ni mo ń sọ. I speak Yorùbá and English. I do not speak Hausa.
ÀJỌKÉ Àwa ọ̀tọ̀ọ̀tọ̀ ni, ṣùgbọ́n Yorùbá ló so wọ́n pọ̀ fún wa. We are different, but Yorùbá is what connects us.

(Gbogbo wọn rẹ́rìn-ín — They all laugh.)

Grammar Note — Expressions to notice in the dialogue

Two expressions in the dialogue contain grammar or vocabulary beyond this chapter's productive scope. Treat them as formulaic expressions for now:

  • Ẹ ńkọ́? — "And you?" This is a common conversational expression. Its structure involves the honorific pronoun ẹ and the particle ńkọ́. Use it as a complete, reliable turn-passing phrase without breaking it apart.
  • Yorùbá ló so wọ́n pọ̀ fún wa. — "Yorùbá is what connects us." This sentence contains a focus construction and serial verb pattern. Recognize its meaning but do not try to build similar sentences independently yet.

Àwọn ìbéèrè nípa ọ̀rọ̀ àdáhùnṣe — Comprehension questions:

  1. Níbo ni Àjọké ti wá? Níbo ni ó ń gbé báyìí? (Where does Àjọké come from? Where does she live now?)
  2. Iṣẹ́ wo ni Àjọké ń ṣe? (What is Àjọké's profession?)
  3. Ṣé Bísí jẹ́ olùkọ́? Tó bá kọ́, iṣẹ́ wo ni ó ṣe? (Is Bísí a teacher? If not, what is she?)
  4. Àwọn èdè wo ni Ṣéun ń sọ? (What languages does Ṣéun speak?)
  5. Ṣe Bísí ń sọ Hausa? Kọ ìdáhùn rẹ ní Yorùbá nínú gbólóhùn kúkúrú kan. (Does Bísí speak Hausa? Write your answer in Yorùbá in a short sentence.)

Writing Workshop

In this chapter you will write a personal profile about yourself and a short introduction about another real or invented person. Both pieces should use full tone marks and underdots throughout.

  1. Plan your content. Answer these questions in notes before you write: What is your name? Where do you come from? Where do you live? What is your profession or student status? What languages do you speak?
  2. Write your profile. Write a paragraph of four to six sentences introducing yourself in Yorùbá. Use the vocabulary and patterns from this chapter. Include at least one sentence with wà ní or ń gbé ní, one sentence with Mo jẹ́ ___, and one sentence with Mo ń sọ ___.
  3. Introduce another person. Write two to three sentences introducing a real or invented person. Include their name, origin, and one other piece of information. Use Orúkọ rẹ̀ ni ___ and at least one third-person subject pronoun ó.
  4. Add a negative sentence. Write one sentence using Kì í ṣe ___ ni mo jẹ́ or the same pattern for the third person.
  5. Revise and check. Use the editing checklist below before submitting.

Àtúnyẹ̀wò àtúnṣe — Editing checklist:

  • Every high tone is marked with an acute accent (´).
  • Every low tone is marked with a grave accent (`).
  • Mid-tone vowels carry no accent mark.
  • Every ẹ, ọ, and ṣ has its underdot — not replaced by plain e, o, or s.
  • I used mid-tone ni for identification and high-tone ní for possession or location.
  • Location sentences use the pattern wà ní + place.
  • Third-person singular pronoun is ó (high tone) — no separate he/she distinction.
  • Each sentence starts with a capital letter and ends with a period or question mark.
  • Country, city, and language names are spelled with correct tones and underdots.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Language, ethnicity, and identity

Yorùbá is spoken by tens of millions of people across Nigeria, Benin, Togo, and diaspora communities worldwide. Speaking Yorùbá is not the same as being Yorùbá by ethnicity, and being Yorùbá by ethnicity does not mean that a person speaks Standard Yorùbá — or speaks it as a first language. Many people who identify as Yorùbá speak regional dialects, mix Yorùbá with English or French in daily conversation, or use Yorùbá only in specific social contexts.

When you learn to ask Níbo ni o ti wá? (Where do you come from?) or Èdè wo ni o ń sọ? (What language do you speak?), you are gathering specific information about a specific person — not confirming a general cultural profile. Avoid drawing conclusions about a person's language based on their country or city of origin alone, or about their identity based on the language they speak. Ethnicity, language, nationality, religion, and individual practice are distinct and should not be treated as a single package.

Culture in Context — Multilingualism as the norm

In the dialogue, Ṣéun speaks Yorùbá, English, and some Hausa. Fúnmiláyọ̀ in the listening scripts speaks French, Yorùbá, and some English. This multilingualism is not exceptional — it is normal across Yorùbá-speaking communities. Nigeria's linguistic situation includes hundreds of recognized languages alongside English as an official language, and communities in Benin and Togo navigate French, Yorùbá, and local languages routinely.

When you tell someone Mo ń sọ Yorùbá díẹ̀ (I speak a little Yorùbá), you are participating in a multilingual social world where this kind of disclosure is entirely natural. Yorùbá speakers are accustomed to people at various levels of fluency, and many will appreciate and encourage the effort.

Regional Variation — Yorùbá in Benin and Togo

Yorùbá communities in Benin and Togo have developed alongside distinct national histories, government languages (French), and local dialects. The Yorùbá spoken in Cotonou or Lomé may differ from Lagos Yorùbá in pronunciation, vocabulary, and certain grammatical features. Some loanwords reflect French influence rather than English, and written conventions may vary slightly when these communities produce materials in their own orthographic traditions.

For this course, Standard Nigerian Yorùbá is the productive model. Where Beninese or Togolese contexts appear in the course — as they do in the listening scripts and cultural examples — they are included to show that Yorùbá is not limited to Nigeria. The differences are noted for recognition. Learners should not mix forms from different contexts without labeling them clearly.

Culture in Context — Personal names in Yorùbá

Yorùbá personal names often carry meaning related to family circumstance, birth conditions, religious identity, or aspirations. Names such as Àjọké (one who is cared for by all), Fúnmiláyọ̀ (give me joy), Àbídèmí (born during my presence), and Kẹ́hìndé (the second of twins) reflect real naming practices. Tone is part of the name's correct pronunciation, and underdots are part of its correct spelling.

Do not treat names as interchangeable labels or strip them of their diacritics in writing. When you write someone's name in Yorùbá, you are spelling it as they spell it. If you encounter a name you do not know how to pronounce, the expressions Ẹ jọ̀wọ́, tún orúkọ rẹ sọ (Please say your name again) or Kí ni ọ̀nà tí a máa ń pe orúkọ rẹ? (How is your name pronounced?) are appropriate ways to ask for clarification.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using ni for location

Error: *Mo ni Èkó or *Ó ni Ìbàdàn to mean "I am in Lagos" or "She is in Ibadan."

Correction: Location requires the construction wà ní + place: Mo wà ní Èkó, Ó wà ní Ìbàdàn. Mid-tone ni identifies what something is, not where something is. High-tone ní appears after wà as a location preposition. The two particles have different tones and different jobs.

Common Mistake — Writing ní without the accent in location phrases

Error: Writing *Ó wà ni Èkó (mid-tone ni) instead of Ó wà ní Èkó (high-tone ní).

Correction: The location and possession preposition always carries high tone: ní with an acute accent on the vowel. Omitting the accent changes the particle's meaning and produces a grammatical error. Every time you write wà ní, check that the acute accent is on the í.

Common Mistake — Translating ó as "he" or "she" based on the person described

Error: Producing *hé jẹ́ dókítà or *ṣhé jẹ́ dókítà, or mentally assigning a gendered English pronoun and then being uncertain which Yorùbá form to use.

Correction: The third-person singular subject pronoun in Yorùbá is ó (high tone) regardless of whether the person is male, female, or any other identity. English "he," "she," and "it" all correspond to Yorùbá ó in the third-person singular. Context — a person's name, prior mention, or shared knowledge — tells listeners who is being discussed. There is no need to invent a gender distinction in Yorùbá where none exists.

Common Mistake — Omitting the tone on ó

Error: Writing *o jẹ́ dókítà (familiar singular subject) when referring to a third person.

Correction: o (mid tone, no accent) is the second-person singular familiar subject pronoun — "you." ó (high tone, acute accent) is the third-person singular subject pronoun — "she," "he," or "it." These are different pronouns. o jẹ́ dókítà means "you are a doctor." ó jẹ́ dókítà means "she/he is a doctor." The accent on ó is not optional.

Common Mistake — Stripping diacritics from country and city names

Error: Writing *Ibadan, *Ekó, or *Naijiriya instead of Ìbàdàn, Èkó, or Nàìjíríà.

Correction: Yorùbá place names require their full tone marks and underdots just as any other Yorùbá word does. The spelling Ibadan is an English-language convention used in English-medium writing. When you write in Standard Yorùbá, use the Yorùbá form with all its diacritics. Dropping the marks is an orthographic error that compromises both meaning and respect for the language's writing system.

Looking Ahead

Ohun tí o lè ṣe báyìí — What you can do now

You can introduce yourself and another person, ask and answer questions about origin, residence, profession, and languages, and distinguish the mid-tone identification particle ni from the high-tone location and possession particle ní. You can use the location construction wà ní, use emphatic pronouns in controlled patterns, and produce the formulaic negative identification kì í ṣe. You can count from 1 to 100 and write personal profiles with full Standard Yorùbá orthography. These skills — especially the ni/ní distinction and third-person singular ó — will recur in every subsequent chapter.

Abala 4: Ẹbí àti Àwọn Ọ̀rẹ́ — Family and Friends

Chapter 4 moves from public introductions to personal relationships. You will learn family vocabulary for immediate and extended relatives, express possession using noun-plus-possessive-pronoun patterns, describe people physically and personally, and ask and state ages. Two new grammatical systems take center stage: possessive pronouns (which follow the noun, not precede it) and object pronouns in their first controlled patterns. You will also encounter your first nasal vowels as a formal phonological category and continue building your number vocabulary for expressing ages. The communicative goal: presenting a family or social network in connected Yorùbá speech and writing.

Ní Ọjà

At the Market · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Ask for the price of a market item using the approved question pattern.
  2. State prices in Nigerian naira using correctly marked number expressions.
  3. Request a specific quantity, size, or color of a market good.
  4. Identify clothing items, containers, and common market goods using fully marked vocabulary.
  5. Evaluate options and make a simple choice with fẹ́ (want / prefer) and related expressions.
  6. Complete a courteous purchase exchange from greeting to closing.
  7. Use direct-object pronouns in controlled market contexts.
  8. Recognize and use one approved serial verb pattern for taking, giving, or transferring.
  9. Ask an information question using the ni focus pattern.
  10. Write a shopping list and a short transactional exchange with full tone marks and underdots.

Communication Scenario

It is Saturday morning. You are at Ọjà Ìbàdàn — a busy open-air market in Ìbàdàn — with a friend who has just arrived from abroad. You want to buy two items: a wrapper of fabric and some tomatoes for the evening meal. Your friend wants to look at clothing. The stall holders speak Yorùbá and some English. Your goal is to ask about the goods, find out prices, request the quantities and sizes you need, and complete the purchase politely.

  1. Think about what you can already say in Yorùbá: you know colors, numbers, quantity expressions, negation, and polite requests. Write down five sentences you could already use in a market.
  2. What new things do you need in order to complete a purchase? Make a list in English. By the end of this chapter, every item on that list should have a Yorùbá expression next to it.
  3. When you shop in any language, what do you say when something is too expensive? What do you say when you want to see a different size or color? Think about the communicative moves — not just the words — that make up a successful transaction.

Pronunciation and Orthography Focus

Focus 1 — Tone in numbers and prices

Pronunciation Focus — Numbers, prices, and tone

Numbers in Yorùbá carry specific tone patterns that must be memorized as part of each form. A wrong tone on a number can cause confusion about the amount. Practice the following forms until the tone sequence feels natural:

  • ẹgbẹ̀rún — one thousand: low-low-high
  • ọgọ́rùn-ún — one hundred: mid-high-low-high
  • igba — two hundred: mid-mid
  • irinwó — four hundred: mid-mid-high
  • ọ̀ọ́dúnrún — three hundred: low-high-high-high

When saying a price, the pattern is typically: number + náírà. Practise these as complete phrases:

  • ẹgbẹ̀rún méjì náírà — two thousand naira
  • igba náírà — two hundred naira
  • ọgọ́rùn-ún àti àádọ́ta náírà — one hundred and fifty naira

Always mark the tone on náírà. The high tones on the first two syllables are essential for distinguishing this word from other similar-sounding words.

Focus 2 — Tone and vowel interaction with object pronouns

Pronunciation Focus — Object pronouns in market requests

Market requests frequently use object pronouns. The most important for this chapter is the third-person singular object form rẹ̀ (low tone) — "it" or "him/her" — which often follows directly after the verb. Practice the following patterns:

  • Fún mi ní ẹ̀jọ àtàrí. — Give me eight tomatoes. (mi = first-person object)
  • Mo fẹ́ rẹ̀. — I want it. (rẹ̀ = third-person singular object, low tone)
  • Ẹ jẹ́ kí n rí i. — Let me see it. (i = reduced object form after some verbs)

Notice that rẹ̀ (object) has low tone, while rẹ (possessive, familiar 2nd person) has mid tone. This contrast matters. When you say Mo fẹ́ rẹ̀, the low tone on the final word identifies "it" as the object you want, not a possessive reference.

Focus 3 — Contractions in high-frequency market questions

Pronunciation Focus — Natural contractions in market speech

In natural Yorùbá market speech, certain question and request patterns are commonly contracted. Learn the full form first, then recognise the contracted version:

  • Full: Iye rẹ̀ jẹ́ mélòó? — How much does it cost? (literally: its price is how many?)
  • Common spoken alternative: Iye rẹ̀ fún mi ni? — How much for me? (formulaic inquiry)
  • Full: Fún mi ní àtàrí mẹ́ta. — Give me three tomatoes.
  • Natural spoken: Fún mi àtàrí mẹ́ta. — the ní may be reduced in rapid speech

Do not invent contractions by deleting syllables at random. The contracted forms here are verified patterns from natural market Yorùbá. The full forms are always acceptable and are the baseline for writing.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

9.1 Numerals and noun-qualifier order in the market

Purpose: use numbers, colors, sizes, and quantities in natural noun phrases to describe market goods.

You learned in Chapter 2 that qualifiers follow the noun in Yorùbá noun phrases. Market language builds on this pattern. The general order for expanded noun phrases is:

àwọn (if plural) + noun + possessive or demonstrative + color or quality + numeral or quantifier

  • aṣọ pupa méjì noun + color + numeral two red cloths
  • àwọn àtàrí mẹ́ta àwọn + noun + numeral three tomatoes
  • bàtà dúdú tóbi kan noun + color + size + numeral (one) one large black shoe / a large black shoe
  • ìgò omi kékeré méfàá noun + noun-modifier (of water) + size + numeral six small bottles of water
Grammar Note — Numeral position and the word for "one"

In Yorùbá, the number kan (one) often follows the noun and any other qualifiers, functioning somewhat like an indefinite article in English. Compare:

  • ẹwù kan — a garment / one garment
  • ẹwù pupa kan — a red garment / one red garment

Numbers greater than one also follow the noun and any color or quality word. Do not put the number before the noun as you would in English.

9.2 Asking and stating prices

Purpose: use the price-question pattern and state a price correctly.

The standard price question uses jẹ́ (to cost, to be priced at) and the question word mélòó (how much/many):

  • Iye rẹ̀ jẹ́ mélòó? price/amount of-it costs how-much? How much does it cost? / What is the price?
  • Ó jẹ́ ẹgbẹ̀rún méjì náírà. it costs thousand two naira It costs two thousand naira.
  • Aṣọ yìí jẹ́ mélòó? cloth this costs how-much? How much does this cloth cost?
  • Àwọn àtàrí mẹ́ta jẹ́ igba náírà. tomatoes three cost two-hundred naira Three tomatoes cost two hundred naira.
Grammar Note — jẹ́ for price versus jẹ́ for identity

You have already met jẹ́ in the identity pattern Mo jẹ́ olùkọ́ (I am a teacher). In market contexts, jẹ́ also means "to cost" — the price of something is what it "amounts to." The two uses share the same form and tone. Context makes the meaning clear: when followed by a naira amount, jẹ́ expresses cost; when followed by a profession or identity noun, it expresses identity or classification.

9.3 Direct-object pronouns in market requests

Purpose: use first- and third-person object pronouns to complete market requests naturally.

Object pronouns allow you to refer back to an item already mentioned without repeating the full noun. The most important forms for this chapter are:

PersonObject pronounToneExample
1st singular (me)mimidFún mi ní ___. — Give me ___.
3rd singular (it / him / her)rẹ̀lowMo fẹ́ rẹ̀. — I want it.
2nd honorific / plural (you)yínhighMàá san owó fún yín. — I will pay you.
  • Fún mi ní ìró pupa méjì. Give me two red wrappers.
  • Iye rẹ̀ jẹ́ mélòó? Mo fẹ́ rẹ̀. How much does it cost? I want it.
  • Màá san owó fún yín lẹ́sẹ̀kẹsẹ̀. I will pay you immediately.
Grammar Note — mi (object) versus mi (possessive)

The form mi appears both as a first-person object pronoun (in Fún mi — give me) and as a first-person possessive pronoun (in àpótí mi — my bag). These are the same surface form in Standard Yorùbá, and context disambiguates them. In Fún mi ní aṣọ, mi is the object of the verb fún. In aṣọ mi, mi follows the noun and marks possession. Learners need not worry about a formal distinction — just learn each pattern as a whole expression.

9.4 Commands and polite requests in market contexts

Purpose: make direct requests to a seller using affirmative commands and polite framing.

You learned fixed command forms in Chapter 2. In market settings, commands to sellers take the bare verb form. Because sellers are typically addressed with the honorific or plural ẹ, a polite honorific frame is common:

  • Fún mi ní àtàrí mẹ́ta. verb + object-pronoun + ní + item Give me three tomatoes. (direct, neutral)
  • Ẹ jọ̀wọ́, fún mi ní àtàrí mẹ́ta. honorific-please + verb + object-pronoun + ní + item Please give me three tomatoes. (polite, respectful)
  • Ẹ jẹ́ kí n rí aṣọ yẹn. honorific + let + 1st-person + see + cloth + that Please let me see that cloth. (formulaic — treat as a unit)
  • Ẹ dín iye rẹ̀ kù. honorific + reduce + price + remain Please reduce the price. (polite negotiation)
Grammar Note — Using ẹ jọ̀wọ́ versus a bare command

A bare command is not rude in itself — it is a natural request form in Yorùbá market contexts where the relationship is service-oriented. Adding ẹ jọ̀wọ́ (please — honorific) or addressing the seller as ẹ makes the request more courteous and is often heard from shoppers who wish to maintain a respectful relationship. You will not cause offence by using ẹ jọ̀wọ́, and it is always a safe choice.

9.5 Introductory serial verb construction: fún (give) and gbà (take/receive)

Purpose: use one approved serial verb pattern that expresses transfer of goods from seller to buyer.

A serial verb construction is a sequence of two or more verbs that share the same subject (and sometimes the same object) to describe one connected event. The conjunction "and" is not inserted between the verbs in Yorùbá.

The most important serial pattern for the market involves mú (take, pick up) combined with a directional or transfer verb:

Serial Pattern 1 — mú + fún (take and give)
Mú aṣọ yìí fún mi.
verb₁ + object + verb₂ + indirect-object
Take this cloth and give it to me. / Bring me this cloth.
Serial Pattern 2 — gbà + owó (take + money)
Gbà owó rẹ̀.
verb + object
Receive your money. / Take your money. (seller receiving payment)
Serial Pattern 3 — mú wá (take and bring)
Ẹ mú díẹ̀ wá.
honorific + verb₁ + quantity + verb₂
Bring some. / Bring a little (of it).
Grammar Note — Why there is no "and" in a serial construction

English speakers naturally want to insert "and" between two action verbs: take and give. Yorùbá serial verb constructions express this as a single coordinated event rather than two separate linked events. The two verbs share the same subject and describe one flowing action. Inserting àti (and) between mú and fún would produce an unnatural result — it would imply two independent actions rather than one transfer event.

For now, learn mú ___ fún mi and mú ___ wá as complete, unanalyzed productive patterns. The serial verb system will be analyzed and expanded in Chapter 11.

9.6 Information questions with ni focus

Purpose: use approved question patterns with ni to ask about price and identity of market goods.

You have already used the focus particle ni in identity statements. In market questions, it appears in the patterns kí ni ___? (what is ___?) and price questions. These are treated as formulaic productive expressions at this stage:

  • Kí ni orúkọ rẹ̀ ní Yorùbá? What is its name in Yorùbá?
  • Iye rẹ̀ jẹ́ mélòó? How much does it cost?
  • Àwọn ẹwù wọ̀nyí jẹ́ mélòó? How much are these garments?
Language Focus

Yorùbá number formation is staged and must not be generated by analogy from English patterns. The numbers you have learned in this course — including the forms for 100, 200, 1,000, and their combinations — are verified high-frequency forms appropriate to market and everyday use. However, the full traditional Yorùbá numeral system uses a vigesimal (base-20) structure with forms such as ẹgbẹ̀ta (600), ẹgbẹ̀rin (1,600), and complex subtraction patterns for numbers like 19, 39, or 190. These are outside the productive scope of this course. Do not attempt to generate traditional number forms by analogy — look them up and verify the tone and form each time.

Price expressions also vary across setting, generation, and community. Prices in Nigerian naira are standard for transactions in Nigeria, while Beninese and Togolese Yorùbá-speaking communities use different currencies. When practicing market dialogues, use naira amounts unless your scenario specifically calls for another context.

Model Dialogue

Read the dialogue carefully. Notice how the buyer greets the seller, asks about the goods, negotiates politely, and completes the purchase. Pay attention to the serial verb patterns, price expressions, and object pronouns. The comprehension questions follow.

Ipò: Ọjà Ìbàdàn, owúrọ̀ Ọjọ́ Àbámẹ́ta. Adélékè (olùra — buyer) ń wá aṣọ. Àwọn ẹní ta (ọlọ́jà — seller) orúkọ rẹ̀ ni Màmá Títí. Adélékè ò mọ Màmá Títí tẹ́lẹ̀, nítorí náà ó ń lo ìkíni ti ọmọlúwàbí.
Setting: Ìbàdàn market, Wednesday morning. Adélékè (buyer) is looking for cloth. The seller is called Màmá Títí. Adélékè does not know Màmá Títí, so he uses polite address from the start.

ADÉLÉKÈ Ẹ káàárọ̀, Màmá. Good morning, Ma.
MÀMÁ TÍTÍ Káàárọ̀, ọmọ mi. Ẹ wọlé. Kí ni ẹ fẹ́? Good morning, my child. Come in. What do you want?
ADÉLÉKÈ Mo fẹ́ rà aṣọ kan. Ẹ ní ìró pupa bí? I want to buy a cloth. Do you have a red wrapper?
MÀMÁ TÍTÍ Bẹ́ẹ̀ ni, mo ní. Àwọn ìró pupa wà níbí. Ẹ jọ̀wọ́, wo ìró wọ̀nyí. Yes, I have. Red wrappers are here. Please look at these wrappers.
ADÉLÉKÈ Ó dára. Ẹ jẹ́ kí n rí ìró pupa tóbi yẹn. Good. Let me see that large red wrapper.

(Màmá Títí mú ìró náà fún Adélékè. — Màmá Títí takes the wrapper and gives it to Adélékè.)

ADÉLÉKÈ Ó rẹwà gan-an. Iye rẹ̀ jẹ́ mélòó? It is very beautiful. How much does it cost?
MÀMÁ TÍTÍ Ìró yẹn jẹ́ ẹgbẹ̀rún mẹ́rin náírà. That wrapper costs four thousand naira.
ADÉLÉKÈ Àá, ó pọ̀ jù! Ẹ dín iye rẹ̀ kù díẹ̀, ẹ jọ̀wọ́. Ah, it is too expensive! Please reduce the price a little.
MÀMÁ TÍTÍ Ó yá. Ẹgbẹ̀rún mẹ́ta àti igba náírà. Ó pẹ tán. Alright. Three thousand two hundred naira. That is final.
ADÉLÉKÈ Ẹgbẹ̀rún mẹ́ta náírà. Ẹ gba. Three thousand naira. Take it.
MÀMÁ TÍTÍ Ó dára, ọmọ mi. Mú ìró rẹ lọ. Ẹ ṣé. Alright, my child. Take your wrapper and go. Thank you.
ADÉLÉKÈ Ẹ ṣé púpọ̀, Màmá. Ó dàbọ̀. Thank you very much, Ma. Goodbye.
Grammar Note — Three serial constructions in the dialogue
  1. Màmá Títí mú ìró náà fún Adélékè (stage direction) — mú (take) + fún (give): Màmá Títí picks up the wrapper and gives it. Two verbs, one event.
  2. Mú ìró rẹ lọ — mú (take) + lọ (go): take your wrapper and go. The buyer is told to take the item and depart. Again, no "and" is inserted.
  3. Ẹ gba (Take it) — a single-verb command in a transfer context; the serial element is implied by the prior offer of money.

Notice how lọ (to go) functions as the second verb in a serial sequence. You will learn more about motion verbs in serial constructions in Chapter 11.

Àwọn ìbéèrè nípa ọ̀rọ̀ àdáhùnṣe — Comprehension questions:

  1. Kí ni Adélékè fẹ́ rà? (What does Adélékè want to buy?)
  2. Iye ìró pupa tóbi yẹn jẹ́ mélòó ní àkọ́kọ́? (How much did the large red wrapper cost at first?)
  3. Kí ni Adélékè sọ nígbà tí ó gbọ́ iye náà? (What did Adélékè say when he heard the price?)
  4. Àwọn méjì wọn gbà iye mélòó ní ìparí? (What price did the two of them agree on in the end?)
  5. Find the serial verb construction in the stage direction. Write it out, identify the two verbs, and explain what single event it describes.
  6. What form of address does Adélékè use throughout — familiar or honorific? Find two examples to support your answer.

Àtúnṣe àtúnyẹ̀wò — Editing checklist:

  • Every number has its correct tone pattern. I did not leave numbers unmarked.
  • The word náírà carries its two high tones: náírà.
  • Every ẹ, ọ, and ṣ is spelled with an underdot.
  • Object pronoun mi appears after fún, not the subject form mo.
  • Object pronoun rẹ̀ (for "it") carries a low tone, not a mid tone.
  • I used the honorific address form ẹ (or ẹ jọ̀wọ́) when speaking to the seller.
  • My serial verb construction has no conjunction between the two verbs.
  • Noun qualifiers (color, size, number) appear after the noun, not before it.
  • Every sentence begins with a capital letter and ends with a full stop or question mark.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Markets in Yorùbá-speaking communities

Markets occupy a central place in the social and economic life of many Yorùbá-speaking communities. In southwestern Nigeria, large daily markets such as Ọjà Ìbàdàn, Balogun Market in Lagos, and Ọjà Ìgbẹ́kọ̀ serve not only as commercial spaces but as places where people catch up on news, renew social ties, and exchange information. Weekly markets on a four- or eight-day cycle also exist in many towns, coordinating trade across a wider area.

In Benin and Togo, Yorùbá-speaking traders similarly participate in local and regional market systems, though the currency, some vocabulary, and specific market names differ. Do not assume that every market practice in Ìbàdàn applies identically in Cotonou or Lomé.

Culture in Context — Negotiation and fixed prices

Price negotiation (ìdójútí iye or ìdíje iye) is common in open-air markets and stalls but is not universal. Fixed-price retail stores, supermarkets, and many urban commercial outlets do not negotiate. Even in open markets, not every seller expects or welcomes negotiation, and the degree of flexibility varies by commodity, relationship, and context.

The key politeness moves in Yorùbá market negotiation are: expressing appreciation for the goods before questioning the price, using respectful address throughout, making a reasonable counter-offer rather than drastically undercutting, and accepting "Ó pẹ tán" (That is final) gracefully if the seller is not willing to reduce further. Avoid characterizing negotiation as a Yorùbá cultural universal or as compulsory behavior in every shopping context.

Culture in Context — Address and respect in market interactions

Addressing a market seller requires attention to age, relationship, and status. A seller who is clearly older than the buyer is typically addressed with the honorific ẹ, often combined with kinship-based address terms such as Màmá, Bàbá, Ìyá, or Ègbón (older sibling). These terms signal respect and also establish a warm relational tone that may ease negotiation.

A seller who appears to be a peer or younger may be addressed with the familiar o, though using the honorific is never inappropriate. Learners who are uncertain about the right register should default to the honorific — it is always safe.

Regional Variation — Currency across Yorùbá-speaking areas

This chapter uses Nigerian naira (náírà) as the currency for price examples, which is standard for transactions in Nigeria. Yorùbá-speaking communities in Benin use the West African CFA franc (fíráńsì), and those in Togo use the same CFA franc under a different central bank. The Yorùbá terms for these currencies differ, and price scales differ dramatically from naira amounts.

If you are using this textbook in a Beninese or Togolese context, your instructor may provide localized price examples. The transactional grammar — questions, requests, serial patterns, and politeness moves — transfers across all three contexts. Only the currency names and amounts need to be adapted.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Putting numbers before the noun

Error: Writing *mẹ́ta àtàrí (three tomatoes) by placing the number before the noun, as in English.

Correction: àtàrí mẹ́ta. In Yorùbá, the numeral follows the noun. The pattern is noun first, qualifier or number after. This applies to all qualifying words, including colors and size adjectives. Think of the noun as announcing the category and the qualifiers as narrowing it down.

Common Mistake — Using rẹ (mid tone) instead of rẹ̀ (low tone) for "it"

Error: Saying or writing *Mo fẹ́ rẹ (mid tone on the final word) when referring to an item as the object of the verb.

Correction: Mo fẹ́ rẹ̀ — the third-person singular object pronoun "it/him/her" carries low tone. Mid-tone rẹ is the familiar second-person possessive ("your"), as in ìwé rẹ (your book). Confusing the two tones produces a different meaning. Practice the minimal pair: rẹ (your) vs. rẹ̀ (it/object) until the distinction is automatic.

Common Mistake — Inserting àti (and) between serial verbs

Error: Writing *Mú ìró yẹn àti fún mi — inserting àti between the two verbs of a serial construction.

Correction: Mú ìró yẹn fún mi. A serial verb construction in Yorùbá does not require a conjunction between the verbs. The two verbs form a single event sequence. Adding àti disrupts the construction and sounds unnatural. Remember: the absence of "and" is not a grammatical error — it is the correct form.

Common Mistake — Writing náírà without tone marks

Error: Writing *naira or *naírà — using the English spelling or omitting the first high tone.

Correction: náírà — both high tones on the first two syllables must be marked. The Yorùbá word for the Nigerian currency is an adapted loanword; in Standard Yorùbá orthography, it carries fully specified tones and the adapted vowel pattern. Do not transfer the English spelling naira into your Yorùbá text.

Common Mistake — Using mo (subject) instead of mi (object) after fún

Error: Writing *Fún mo ní àtàrí mẹ́ta — using the subject pronoun mo instead of the object pronoun mi.

Correction: Fún mi ní àtàrí mẹ́ta. After a verb in a request or command, the object form is required. Subject pronouns (mo, o, ó, a, ẹ, wọ́n) precede the verb; object pronouns (mi, ọ, un/rẹ̀, wa, yín, wọn) follow the verb as its complement. The pattern Fún mi (give me) is one of the most frequent object-pronoun constructions in market language — practise it until it sounds automatic.

Practice — Serial verb and request patterns

  1. Mú aṣọ ewé fún mi. — Take the green cloth and give it to me. (Bring me the green cloth.)
  2. Fún mi ní àwọn ìró pupa méjì. or Mú àwọn ìró pupa méjì fún mi.
  3. Ẹ mú omi díẹ̀ wá. — Bring a little water.
  4. Ẹ jẹ́ kí n rí àgbọ̀n tóbi náà. — Let me see the large basket. (formulaic unit)

Dialogue — Comprehension

  1. Adélékè fẹ́ rà aṣọ kan — specifically, a large red wrapper. (He wants to buy a cloth.)
  2. Ìró pupa tóbi yẹn jẹ́ ẹgbẹ̀rún mẹ́rin náírà ní àkọ́kọ́. (₦4,000 at first.)

Chapter Review — Apá B

  1. (a) Q: Iye ìró tóbi jẹ́ mélòó? A: Ó jẹ́ ẹgbẹ̀rún mẹ́rin náírà. (b) Q: Àwọn àtàrí mẹ́fà jẹ́ mélòó? A: Àwọn àtàrí mẹ́fà jẹ́ ọgọ́rùn-ún mẹ́ta náírà. (c) Q: Àgbọ̀n jẹ́ mélòó? A: Ó jẹ́ ọgọ́rùn-ún mẹ́jọ náírà. (Accept reasonable expressions for ₦800.)
  2. Fún mi ní aṣọ pupa kan. — Mo fẹ́ rẹ̀. — Màá san owó fún yín. — Mú ẹja náà fún mi.
  3. Corrections: (i) Àtàrí mẹ́ta jẹ́ igba náírà. (noun before numeral); (ii) Mo fẹ́ rẹ̀. (low tone on object pronoun, no noun repeated); (iii) Mú ìró yẹn fún mi. (no àti between verbs); (iv) Iye rẹ̀ jẹ́ ẹgbẹ̀rún méjì náírà. (low tone on rẹ̀, correct spelling of náírà).
  4. Accept any well-formed four-turn exchange with a serial pattern, correct tone marks, and natural register.

Chapter Review — Apá C

  1. Correct forms: ẹgbẹ̀rún méjì náírà; irinwó náírà; ọgọ́rùn-ún márùn-ún náírà; igba àti àádọ́ta náírà.
  2. (a) rẹ: e.g., Ìwé rẹ dára. (Your book is good.) — rẹ̀: e.g., Mo fẹ́ rẹ̀. (I want it.) (b) Accept any well-formed sentence with fún in a different context, e.g., Ẹ ṣé fún yín — in many farewell expressions. Note: fún can mean "give" (verb) or mark a beneficiary ("for"). Contextual disambiguation is acceptable.
  3. Tone pattern for ẹgbẹ̀rún mẹ́ta náírà: L-L-H · H-M · H-H-M (syllables: ẹg-bẹ̀-rún · mẹ́-ta · ná-í-rà). Accept phonetically motivated alternatives; flag for human review if uncertain.

Looking Ahead

Ohun tí o lè ṣe báyìí — What you can do now

You can ask the price of market goods, state quantities and describe items using colors and sizes, make polite requests using object pronouns, complete a purchase with a serial verb pattern, and write a shopping list and transactional exchange in fully marked Standard Yorùbá. You can use the approved number forms for naira prices and recognize when a price is being negotiated or accepted.

Abala 10: Ìgbésí Ayé Ojoojúmọ́ — Everyday Life in Action

Chapter 10 is the Unit II consolidation chapter. It introduces no major new grammar. Instead, you will combine everything from Chapters 6 through 9 — home and location, daily routines, food and preferences, and market transactions — into sustained, connected interaction. You will practise resolving misunderstandings, describing preferences and practical needs, and sustaining a short transaction entirely in Yorùbá. The writing target for Chapter 10 is a fully marked 100–120-word description of everyday life that draws on all four skill areas from Unit II.

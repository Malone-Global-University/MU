Ìránṣẹ́, Ìtọ́kasí, àti Àwọn Arọ́pò Orúkọ Àṣepé

Messaging, Reference, and Object Pronouns · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Name common vocabulary for phones, messages, and basic email communication using fully marked Yorùbá.
  2. Use object pronouns after high-frequency verbs to refer to people and things without repeating a full noun.
  3. Use the demonstratives yìí ("this") and náà ("that, the aforementioned") to refer back to something already mentioned.
  4. Distinguish new information from already-mentioned information in a short conversation or message.
  5. Write a short text message that refers clearly to people or things mentioned earlier in the same message.
  6. Understand who is contacting whom in a short phone or messaging exchange.

Communication Scenario

Yẹmi's birthday party from Chapter 16 is over, and it went well. Now Dúpé wants to send Kúnlé a short thank-you message, and she also needs to pass along a message from Yẹmi to Adé, who could not attend. As the messages go back and forth, Dúpé must be careful to make clear who she means each time she says "him," "her," "it," "this," or "that" — the people and things being discussed are not always repeated by name. You will follow these exchanges, then write your own short message using object pronouns and reference words.

  1. Before you open this chapter, think of a short message you might send a friend after an event: a thank-you, a follow-up question, or a piece of news.
  2. Recall the subject pronouns you have used since Chapter 2 — mo, o, ó, a, ẹ, wọ́n — and the demonstrative yìí, formulaic since Chapter 4. This chapter adds object pronouns and a second demonstrative, náà, to that foundation.

Pronunciation and Orthography Focus

Focus 1 — Connected-speech rhythm in message formulas

Pronunciation Focus — Object pronouns run smoothly into the verb

Object pronouns are pronounced as a smooth continuation of the verb, not as a separate, heavily stressed word. Say each pair aloud, letting the object pronoun flow directly out of the verb with no pause:

  • rí i — see him/her/it (compare with the pause-free á rí ọ, "see you," from Chapter 16)
  • pè é — call him/her
  • gbọ́ ọ — hear it

Focus 2 — Diacritics in digital text

Pronunciation and Orthography Focus — Full diacritics in written messages

Formal instructional writing in this course always includes full tone marks and underdots, even in a short text message. Some Yorùbá speakers write informal digital messages without diacritics, but this course keeps every message you write fully marked, so that tone and vowel quality remain visible and unambiguous.

Focus 3 — Distinguishing yìí and náà

Say each phrase aloud, then say an English gloss, keeping the two demonstratives clearly separate in your mind.

  1. ìránṣẹ́ yìí — this message (a message right in front of you)
  2. ìránṣẹ́ náà — that message, the message just mentioned (already introduced in the conversation)
  3. ẹni náà — that person, the person just mentioned
  4. ohun náà — that thing, the thing just mentioned

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

17.1 Object pronouns after high-frequency verbs

Purpose: refer to a person or thing already mentioned, without repeating the full noun, immediately after a verb.

Yorùbá object pronouns come directly after the verb, in the position a noun object would occupy. You have already produced full noun objects since Chapter 4; this chapter replaces some of those nouns with object pronouns in familiar sentence patterns.

PersonObject pronounExampleGloss
memiÓ rí mi.He/She saw me.
you (sg.)ọMo rí ọ.I saw you.
him/her/iti / rẹ̀Mo rí i.I saw him/her/it.
uswaÓ rí wa.He/She saw us.
you (pl./respect)yínMo rí yín.I saw you (all/respectfully).
themwọnÓ rí wọn.He/She saw them.
  • Mo pè é. I + call + him/her [object pronoun] I called him/her.
  • Ó ránṣẹ́ sí mi. He/She sent me a message.
  • N ó sọ fún un. I will tell him/her.
Grammar Note — Object pronouns are limited to high-frequency contexts here

Volume I activates object pronouns only in the high-frequency verb contexts shown in this chapter: rí ("to see"), pè ("to call"), sọ fún ("to tell"), and ránṣẹ́ sí ("to send a message to"). You are not yet asked to produce object pronouns independently with every verb you know. Broader, more independent object pronoun use is developed in a later volume.

17.2 Referring back with náà

Purpose: refer to a person, thing, or event already mentioned earlier in the same conversation or message, distinguishing it from something new.

náà follows a noun, just as yìí does, but it points back to something already introduced rather than to something physically present:

  • Mo rí Kúnlé. Ọkùnrin náà dára púpọ̀. I + saw + Kúnlé. Man + that/the-aforementioned + is-good + very I saw Kúnlé. That man (the one just mentioned) is very kind.
  • Mo gba ìránṣẹ́ kan. Ìránṣẹ́ náà wá láti ọ̀dọ̀ Yẹmi. I received a message. That message (the one just mentioned) came from Yẹmi.
Grammar Note — yìí points forward or at hand; náà points back

yìí, formulaic since Chapter 4, marks something close at hand or currently being shown, such as ìwé yìí ("this book," the one you are holding). náà marks something the listener or reader already knows about because it was mentioned earlier, such as ìwé náà ("that book," the one already discussed). Both words follow the noun they modify, and neither changes form for singular or plural.

17.3 Tracking who is speaking about whom

Purpose: keep subject and object clearly distinct in a short exchange, so that the listener or reader always knows who is doing what to whom.

A message or dialogue often mixes subject pronouns, object pronouns, and demonstratives in the same short passage. Reading or listening carefully for each one keeps the participants straight:

  • Yẹmi ránṣẹ́ sí mi. Mo dá a padà lẹ́sẹ̀kẹsẹ̀. Yẹmi[subj] + sent + to + me[obj]. I[subj] + replied + him/her[obj] + immediately Yẹmi sent me a message. I replied to him/her immediately.
Grammar Note — No new subject pronoun forms

The subject pronouns in this section — mo, ó, and the others — have been active since Chapter 2. This section does not add new subject forms; it asks you to track subject and object together in a single short passage, a skill developed gradually since Chapter 3.

Language Focus

This chapter's genuinely new productive material is the object pronoun set and the demonstrative náà. Both attach to sentence patterns and verbs you already control. Subject pronouns, the verbs rí, pè, and sọ fún, and the demonstrative yìí have all been active since earlier chapters and are recombined here rather than retaught from the beginning.

Model Dialogue

Read the exchange carefully. Notice how Dúpé and Kúnlé use object pronouns instead of repeating names, and how náà refers back to Yẹmi's message once it has already been mentioned. Comprehension questions follow.

Ipò: Dúpé ń rán ìránṣẹ́ sí Kúnlé lẹ́yìn àríyá ọjọ́ ìbí Yẹmi.
Setting: Dúpé is sending Kúnlé a message after Yẹmi's birthday party.

DÚPÉ Kúnlé, ẹ ṣé púpọ̀ tí o wá sí àríyá náà! Kúnlé, thank you very much for coming to that party (the one we discussed).
KÚNLÉ Kò tọ́pá, ó dùn mí púpọ̀. Ǹjẹ́ Yẹmi rí ẹ̀bùn mi? No problem, I was delighted. Did Yẹmi see my gift?
DÚPÉ Bẹ́ẹ̀ni, ó rí i, ó sì fẹ́ràn rẹ̀ púpọ̀. Ó fẹ́ ránnti ọ pé ó dúpẹ́. Yes, she saw it, and she liked it very much. She wants to remind you that she's grateful.
KÚNLÉ Ó dára! Ǹjẹ́ o gbọ́ láti ọ̀dọ̀ Adé lẹ́yìn àríyá náà? Good! Did you hear from Adé after that party?
DÚPÉ Bẹ́ẹ̀ni, ó ránṣẹ́ sí mi lánàá. Mo dá a padà lẹ́sẹ̀kẹsẹ̀. Yes, he sent me a message yesterday. I replied to him immediately.
KÚNLÉ Kí ni ìránṣẹ́ náà sọ? What did that message (the one you just mentioned) say?
DÚPÉ Adé ní ó ṣàánú pé kò lè wá, ṣùgbọ́n ó fẹ́ rí Yẹmi ní ọ̀sẹ̀ tí ń bọ̀. Adé said he's sorry he couldn't come, but he wants to see Yẹmi next week.
KÚNLÉ Ó yé mi. Sọ fún un pé mo kí i. I understand. Tell him I say hello.
DÚPÉ Mo ó sọ fún un. Ẹ ṣé, Kúnlé! I'll tell him. Thanks, Kúnlé!
Grammar Note — Four reference patterns working together in the dialogue
  1. Ó rí i. — object pronoun i refers back to the gift already mentioned.
  2. àríyá náà and ìránṣẹ́ náà — náà refers back to a party and a message already introduced in the conversation.
  3. Mo dá a padà. — object pronoun a refers back to Adé without repeating his name.
  4. Sọ fún un pé mo kí i. — object pronoun un ("him," a variant of i after certain sounds) again refers back to Adé.

Àwọn ìbéèrè nípa ọ̀rọ̀ àdáhùnṣe — Comprehension questions:

  1. Ǹjẹ́ Yẹmi rí ẹ̀bùn Kúnlé? (Did Yẹmi see Kúnlé's gift?)
  2. Ta ni ó ránṣẹ́ sí Dúpé lánàá? (Who sent Dúpé a message yesterday?)
  3. Kí nìdí tí Adé kò fi wá sí àríyá náà? (Why didn't Adé come to that party?)
  4. Kí ni Kúnlé béèrè lọ́wọ́ Dúpé láti sọ fún Adé? (What does Kúnlé ask Dúpé to tell Adé?)

Writing Workshop

Chapter 17's writing product is a short text message exchange: one message and one reply, each referring clearly to a person or thing mentioned in the other. It must use Standard Yorùbá orthography with full tone marks and underdots.

Ìránṣẹ́ àti Ìdáhùn — A message and a reply

Write a short message (30–50 words) to a friend about a recent event, a plan, or a piece of news. Then write a short reply (20–30 words). Your writing must include:

  • At least one object pronoun after a verb such as rí, pè, or sọ fún.
  • At least one use of náà, referring back to something already mentioned in your own message.
  • At least one subject pronoun, correctly distinguished from the object pronoun(s) in the same sentence or nearby sentences.
  • In the reply: at least one object pronoun or one use of náà referring back to the first message.
  1. Choose a topic. Decide what event, plan, or news you are messaging your friend about.
  2. Introduce the person or thing. Name it once with a full noun.
  3. Refer back to it. Use an object pronoun or náà instead of repeating the noun.
  4. Write the reply. Respond, referring back to something in the first message with a pronoun or náà.
  5. Apply the editing checklist. Verify every tone mark, underdot, and reference choice before submitting.

Àtúnṣe àtúnyẹ̀wò — Editing checklist:

  • Every object pronoun appears immediately after its verb, in the position of the noun it replaces.
  • náà follows a noun that was already mentioned earlier in the message, not a brand-new noun.
  • yìí is used only for something close at hand, not for something already discussed.
  • Subject pronouns and object pronouns are kept distinct; no object pronoun is used in subject position.
  • A reader unfamiliar with the situation could still tell who "he," "she," "it," or "that" refers to.
  • Every ẹ, ọ, and ṣ carries its underdot.
  • High and low tones are marked on every required syllable. Mid tones are left unmarked.

Speaking Practice

Àdáwọlé àkọ́kọ́ — Controlled practice: object pronoun substitution

Working with a partner, Speaker A says a sentence with a full noun object. Speaker B repeats the sentence, replacing the noun with the correct object pronoun. Switch roles after six exchanges each.

Example:

  • Speaker A: Mo rí Kúnlé. ("I saw Kúnlé.")
  • Speaker B: Mo rí i. ("I saw him.")

Ta ni ó ń pè ta? — Who is calling whom?

One partner describes a short phone or messaging exchange using subject pronouns, object pronouns, and náà. The other partner listens and then states, in their own words, exactly who contacted whom and what was said. Switch roles after three rounds each. If no partner is available, record yourself describing the exchange, then listen back and check that a listener could follow who is who.

Fún àwọn tí ń kọ́ nìkọ̀ọ̀kan — Solo alternative: narrated message exchange

Record yourself narrating a short messaging exchange between two people you invent, in one to two minutes of continuous Yorùbá. Include at least three object pronouns and two uses of náà. Listen back and check:

  • Did you place each object pronoun directly after its verb?
  • Did you use náà only for something already mentioned?
  • Could a listener tell who sent the message and who replied?
  • Did you keep every tone accurate while speaking at a natural pace?

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Phone calls and messages among family and friends

Among many Yorùbá-speaking families and communities, a phone call is often preferred over a written message for important news, thanks, or condolences, since it allows for a fuller exchange of greetings and warmth. A short written message is common for practical, quick information, but a follow-up call is not unusual even after a message has been sent, especially between close friends and family.

Culture in Context — Sending greetings through a third person

Asking a friend to pass along a greeting, as Kúnlé does in this chapter's dialogue and message when he says sọ fún un pé mo kí i ("tell him I say hello"), is a common and valued gesture in Yorùbá-speaking communities. It keeps relationships warm even when direct contact is not immediately possible.

Regional Variation — Written diacritics in everyday digital messages

In everyday digital communication, some Yorùbá speakers write with reduced or no diacritics because of keyboard limitations or habit, while others maintain full diacritics, especially in more formal digital writing. This chapter keeps all model messages fully marked as the stable instructional standard; recognizing informal, reduced-diacritic text is developed later in the series as a scheduled receptive skill.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using a subject pronoun in object position

Error: Writing *Mo rí ó when you mean "I saw him," using the subject form ó instead of the object form.

Correction: Mo rí i. — the object pronoun for "him/her/it" is i (or rẹ̀ in some contexts), not the subject pronoun ó. Keep subject and object forms distinct, as shown in Grammar 17.1.

Common Mistake — Using yìí for something already mentioned

Error: Writing *Ìránṣẹ́ yìí wá láti ọ̀dọ̀ Yẹmi to refer back to a message discussed several sentences earlier, when the message is not physically present or currently being shown.

Correction: Ìránṣẹ́ náà wá láti ọ̀dọ̀ Yẹmi. — use náà, not yìí, for something already mentioned rather than something close at hand.

Common Mistake — Repeating the full noun instead of using a pronoun

Error: Writing *Mo rí Kúnlé. Mo pè Kúnlé., repeating the full name where an object pronoun would sound more natural.

Correction: Mo rí Kúnlé. Mo pè é. — once a person has been named, an object pronoun is usually preferred over repeating the name in the next closely connected sentence.

Common Mistake — Dropping diacritics in informal writing

Error: Writing *Mo ri e, o dun mi for a text message assignment, omitting tone marks and underdots because the content is "just a text message."

Correction: Mo rí ẹ, ó dùn mí. — this course requires full diacritics in every formal writing task, including short messages, unless an exercise specifically asks for undiacritized recognition practice.

Ìránṣẹ́ àti ìdáhùn tí ó ṣe kedere — A clear message and reply

The Chapter 17 Performance Task has two parts. All spoken and written Yorùbá must use full tone marks and underdots.

Part 1 — Oral narration (1–2 minutes):
Narrate a short phone or messaging exchange between two people, describing who contacted whom, what was said, and how the other person replied. Your narration must include:

  1. At least three object pronouns used correctly after their verbs.
  2. At least two uses of náà referring back to something already mentioned.
  3. Clear tracking of subject and object throughout, so a listener always knows who is doing what to whom.

Part 2 — Written message and reply (30–50 words plus 20–30 words):
Write a short message to a friend and a short reply, following the model in the Reading section. Your writing must include at least two object pronouns, one use of náà, and correct use of full diacritics throughout.

Assessment note: The spoken task is evaluated on task completion, comprehensibility, correct placement and choice of object pronouns, correct use of náà versus yìí, and clear tracking of who is speaking about whom. The written task is evaluated on accuracy of pronoun and reference choices, clarity for a reader unfamiliar with the situation, and orthographic completeness (underdots and tone marks).

Looking Ahead

Ohun tí o lè ṣe báyìí — What you can do now

You can name vocabulary for phones, messages, and basic email, use object pronouns after high-frequency verbs such as rí, pè, and sọ fún, and use náà to refer back to a person or thing already mentioned, keeping it clearly distinct from yìí. You can also write a short text message that a reader can follow clearly, and understand who is contacting whom in a short phone or messaging exchange.

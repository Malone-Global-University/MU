Volume I Capstone: A2 Practical Communication · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Demonstrate the full range of Volume I productive systems — tone, orthography, SVO word order, subject and object pronouns, aspect and tense particles (ń, ti, yóò), negation (kò, má), noun phrases, and formulaic serialization — inside one formal portfolio.
  2. Produce a written personal profile that introduces yourself with accurate, fully diacritized Yorùbá.
  3. Write and perform a short dialogue appropriate to a familiar everyday situation.
  4. Write a short practical message appropriate to its audience and register.
  5. Write a short connected narrative describing a sequence of past, ongoing, and future events.
  6. Complete a listening task that requires identifying particles, negation, and key details in connected speech.
  7. Complete an oral interview that demonstrates spontaneous, self-monitored spoken Yorùbá.
  8. Self-assess your Volume I portfolio against the completion standard for Pre-A1 → A2 practical communication.

Communication Scenario

Adé's visit to Yẹmi's town, followed across Chapters 18 and 19, is now complete. Before Adé returns home, he assembles a small portfolio of Yorùbá writing and speaking to show what he can now do — much as you will assemble your own Volume I portfolio in this chapter. The six parts of the capstone below mirror the six parts of Adé's own portfolio: a profile, a dialogue, a message, a short narrative, a listening task, and an oral interview.

  1. No new grammar, vocabulary, tone rule, or orthographic rule appears in this chapter. Every task draws only on systems already active in Chapters 1–19.
  2. Work through the six portfolio parts in order. Each part reuses and combines systems from earlier chapters rather than testing them in isolation.
  3. Use the Cumulative Vocabulary Review and Cumulative Grammar Review below as a self-check before you begin producing your own portfolio.

Cumulative Grammar Review

Pattern 1 — Simple SVO clause with a subject pronoun
Subject + Verb + Object
Established Ch. 2–4; consolidated throughout Volume I.

Pattern 2 — Three particles, three time meanings
ń (ongoing) · ti (completed/resultative) · yóò / á / n ó (future)

Pronunciation and Orthography Review

Chapter 20 does not introduce a new sound, spelling, or tone rule. It asks you to apply, without support, every pronunciation and orthography target activated across the volume.

Pronunciation Focus — Full self-monitoring checklist

Apply this checklist to every spoken and written task in this chapter:

  1. Are ẹ, ọ, and ṣ distinct from e, o, and s in both speech and writing?
  2. Is every high and low tone marked, with mid tone correctly left unmarked?
  3. Is gb produced and written as a single Yorùbá sound, not two English letters?
  4. Does nasal vowel spelling with n match nasal pronunciation?
  5. Is CV syllable rhythm maintained, with no inserted English-style consonant clusters?

Part 1 — Personal Profile

Portfolio Part 1

Ìwé ìdánimọ̀ — Personal profile

names and identity (Ch. 2) family (Ch. 3) preferences (Ch. 14) work/school (Ch. 13)

Adé's profile, written for a community welcome book, models the task you will complete.

Àpẹẹrẹ — Model profile

Orúkọ mi ni Adé. Mo wá láti ìlú ńlá kan, mo sì jẹ́ akẹ́kọ̀ọ́. Ẹbí mi wà ní ìlú mi; màmá mi àti bàbá mi ń ṣiṣẹ́ ní ilé ìwòsàn kan. Mo fẹ́ràn kíkàwé àti sísọ èdè tuntun. Ní báyìí, mo ń kọ́ Yorùbá, mo sì ti kọ́ ọ̀pọ̀lọpọ̀ ọ̀rọ̀ tuntun. Ní ọdún tí ń bọ̀, mo fẹ́ ṣèbẹ̀wò sí àwọn ọ̀rẹ́ mi ní ìlú mìíràn.

Gloss: My name is Adé. I come from a large town, and I am a student. My family lives in my town; my mother and father work at a clinic. I like reading and speaking new languages. Right now, I am studying Yorùbá, and I have already learned many new words. Next year, I want to visit my friends in another town.

Iṣẹ́ rẹ — Your task: Write your own profile (60–90 words) that includes your name, where you are from, at least one family or work/school detail, one preference, and one sentence each using ń and ti or yóò/á/n ó. Use full tone marks and underdots throughout.

Part 2 — Dialogue

Portfolio Part 2

Ìjíròrò — A short familiar dialogue

questions (Ch. 3–5) negation (Ch. 7–9) courtesy (Ch. 1, 16)

Adé models a short leave-taking dialogue with Yẹmi as he prepares to travel home.

Ipò: Adé ń dá kú sí Yẹmi ṣáájú kí ó tó padà lọ sílé.
Setting: Adé says goodbye to Yẹmi before returning home.

YẸMI Ǹjẹ́ o ti múra sílẹ̀ fún ìrìnàjò rẹ? Have you prepared for your journey?
ADÉ Bẹ́ẹ̀ni, mo ti múra sílẹ̀. Mi ò gbàgbé nǹkan kankan. Yes, I have prepared. I haven't forgotten anything.
YẸMI Ẹ jọ̀wọ́, ẹ máa bá mi kí ẹbí yín nígbà tí ẹ bá dé ilé. Please greet your family for me when you arrive home.
ADÉ Dájúdájú, n ó ṣe bẹ́ẹ̀. Ẹ ṣé fún ìtọ́jú yín gbogbo. Certainly, I will do that. Thank you for all your care.

Iṣẹ́ rẹ — Your task: Write and perform (with a partner, or solo as a recorded monologue with implied responses) a short dialogue of 6–8 lines set in a familiar everyday situation of your choice. It must include one question, one negative statement, and one courtesy formula.

Ìránṣẹ́ — A short practical message

Ìtàn kékeré — A short connected narrative

Àpẹẹrẹ — Model narrative

Ní ọ̀sẹ̀ tí ó kọjá, mo lọ sí ìlú Yẹmi láti ṣèbẹ̀wò. Mo kí àwọn aládùúgbò rẹ̀, mo sì lọ sí ọjà pẹ̀lú rẹ̀. Ní báyìí, mo ń múra sílẹ̀ láti padà lọ sílé. Ní ọ̀la, n ó dé ilé, n ó sì kí ẹbí mi.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Leave-taking and continuing relationships

A proper Yorùbá leave-taking often includes thanks for hospitality, a request to greet the other person's family, and a promise to stay in touch, as modeled in Part 2. Ending a visit abruptly, without these steps, can seem incomplete even when the practical goodbye is brief.

Culture in Context — Reassurance in messages

Short messages to family, like the one in Part 3, commonly include a brief reassurance (ẹ má ṣàníyàn, "don't worry") alongside practical information. This reflects a broader communicative habit of caring for the listener's peace of mind, not only relaying facts.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Dropping diacritics under portfolio pressure

Error: Writing *Oruko mi ni Ade without tone marks or underdots when working quickly on a portfolio task.

Correction: Orúkọ mi ni Adé. Formal capstone writing requires full diacritics exactly as in every earlier chapter; portfolio conditions do not relax this standard.

Common Mistake — Using only one particle across the whole portfolio

Error: Writing every profile, message, and narrative sentence with ti, regardless of actual time meaning.

Correction: Match each clause's particle to its real time meaning, as consolidated in Chapter 18: ti for completed/resultative, ń for ongoing, yóò/á/n ó for future.

Common Mistake — Reaching for unscheduled focus or relative structures

Error: Attempting to build a full ni-focus construction or a complex relative clause to sound more advanced during the oral interview.

Correction: Stay within Volume I's activated systems. Simple SVO clauses, controlled particles, negation, and basic noun phrases are sufficient — and expected — for this capstone.

Answer Key

Part 5 — Listening Task

  • 1: ń — ongoing ("I am preparing to go"). Negative: Rárá.
  • 2: kò tíì (recognition-level negative, "has not yet arrived"). Negative: Bẹ́ẹ̀ni.
  • 3: á/yóò/n ó — future ("we will meet tomorrow"). Negative: Rárá.
  • 4: ti — completed/resultative ("they have eaten"). Negative: Rárá.
  • 5: kò/mi ò — negative preference ("I don't like rain"). Negative: Bẹ́ẹ̀ni.

Chapter Review — Apá B

  1. Accept any accurate set of three sentences correctly matching particle to time meaning, e.g.: Mo ń kọ́ Yorùbá (ongoing); Mo ti parí iṣẹ́ mi (completed/resultative); N ó lọ sí ilé ẹ̀kọ́ ní ọ̀la (future).
  2. Mi ò fẹ́ràn òjò.
  3. Accept any accurate set, e.g.: àwọn ọmọdé (plural marker); ilé yìí (demonstrative); ìwé mi (possessive).

Chapter Review — Apá C

  1. Correct forms: orúkọ mi ni; ẹbí mi wà; mo ń lọ sí ọjà.
  2. Accept accurate, natural pronunciation with tone kept steady across particle-plus-verb pairs and underdots preserved throughout.

Directions, Movement, and Serial Verbs · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Expanding Everyday and Community Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Recall and apply níbo, formulaic direction phrases, àwọn, numerals, and élòó from Chapters 3–4 and all Volume I systems.
  2. Recognize and use lọ ("go"), wá ("come"), mú ("take/bring"), and gbé ("carry/take") as verbs in motion serial verb constructions.
  3. Build simple serial verb sentences that narrate a sequence of connected actions, such as going somewhere and bringing something back.
  4. Explain, at a basic level, why a Yorùbá serial verb sequence is not simply translated with English "and."
  5. Narrate a short multi-step errand orally and in writing, using serial verbs in controlled sequence.
  6. Give and follow multi-step directions that combine Chapter 3's formulaic direction phrases with this chapter's motion verbs.
  7. Understand a short narrated errand at natural pace well enough to identify the sequence of actions and places.
  8. Write a short paragraph describing an errand, keeping full tone marks and dot-under letters throughout.

Communication Scenario

With bread and bananas bought, Adé and Yẹmi still have more errands before they return home. Adé must go to a second stall, come back, and carry what he bought, all in one connected sequence of actions. Yorùbá speakers do not usually chain these actions together with a word for "and" between every step; instead, several verbs are placed one after another to describe one connected event.

  1. Every task in this chapter reuses Chapter 3's níbo question and formulaic direction phrases, Chapter 4's àwọn, numerals, and élòó, Chapter 2's object pronouns and request frame, and all systems consolidated in Volume I.
  2. One new system is introduced for the first time in controlled form: motion serial verb constructions built from lọ, wá, mú, and gbé.
  3. Only short, two- and three-verb sequences describing errands are required here. Longer or more complex serial chains, instrumental and resultative serialization, and full TAM-scope analysis across a series are reserved for later chapters and are not required now.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Tone across a verb series

When two or three verbs are pronounced together in a serial construction, each verb keeps its own tone. Learners sometimes flatten the tone of the second or third verb in a sequence, as if it were unstressed. Keep lọ, wá, mú, and gbé each clearly mid or high, even when speaking at natural speed.

  • lọ — mid tone
  • wá — high tone
  • mú — high tone
  • gbé — high tone

This is a review application of the Volume I skill "tone in connected speech," applied here to a short sequence of separate verbs rather than to a single word.

Grammar Focus

This chapter opens one new system: motion serial verb constructions built from lọ, wá, mú, and gbé. These verbs are placed one after another, without a connecting word, to describe a single connected sequence of actions. This is different from English, which usually joins separate actions with "and" or "then." Only short, controlled two- and three-verb sequences describing errands are required here; instrumental and resultative serialization are reserved for later chapters.

New Pattern 1 — Two-verb motion sequence
Subject + Verb₁ + (Place) + Verb₂
Two verbs describing one connected movement are placed side by side with no linking word. The subject is stated once and applies across the whole sequence.
Adé lọ sí ilé-ìtajà rà búrẹ́dì.
Adé went to the shop [and] bought bread. (Literally: Adé go to shop buy bread.)
New Pattern 2 — mú / gbé with wá or lọ
Subject + mú/gbé + Object + wá/lọ
Mú and gbé describe carrying or taking something; wá and lọ add the direction, "toward here" or "away from here." The object of mú or gbé stays between the two verbs.
Ẹni tí ń ta mú búrẹ́dì wá fún wọn.
The seller brought the bread to them. (Recycled from Chapter 4's dialogue; now explained as a serial verb pattern.)
New Pattern 3 — Three-verb errand sequence
Subject + lọ + (Place) + mú/gbé + Object + padà/wá
A short narrated errand may chain three verbs: going, taking or carrying something, and returning. Each verb keeps its own tone and its own place in the sequence; no word for "and" is needed between them.
Yẹmi lọ sí ọjà, ó mú oúnjẹ, ó sì padà sí ilé.
Yẹmi went to the market, took [some] food, and returned home.
Language Focus — Serial verbs are not English "and"

English usually links separate actions with "and" or "then": "He went and bought bread." Yorùbá can place the verbs directly next to each other instead, treating the whole sequence as one connected event: ó lọ rà búrẹ́dì. Where the example above uses ó sì padà, the particle sì is a light connector sometimes added between steps in a longer narrated sequence; it does not turn the construction into ordinary English-style coordination, and its full range of uses is developed in later chapters.

  • Mo fẹ́ lọ sí ilé-ìtajà, mo sì fẹ́ mú búrẹ́dì wá. I want to go to the shop, and I want to bring bread. fẹ́ + Verb₁...Verb₂ — desire particle scoping over a short serial sequence
  • Ẹ jọ̀wọ́, ẹ gbé àwọn ìwé yìí lọ fún mi. Please, carry these books away for me. gbé + Object + lọ — carrying with a direction verb; recycled request frame from Chapter 2

Model Dialogue

Ipò: Adé àti Yẹmi ti rà búrẹ́dì àti ọ̀gẹ̀dẹ̀ tán; wọ́n ń rò bí wọ́n ṣe lè lọ sí ilé-ìtajà kejì kí wọ́n sì padà sí ilé.
Setting: Adé and Yẹmi have finished buying bread and bananas; they are thinking about how to go to a second shop and return home.

YẸMI Ẹ jọ̀wọ́, níbo ni ilé-ìtajà tí a ti lè rí oúnjẹ míràn wà? Please, where is a shop where we can find other food?
ẸNI TÍ Ń TA Ẹ tẹ̀síwájú, ẹ yà sí òsì; ilé-ìtajà náà tòsí. Keep going, turn left; the shop is nearby.
ADÉ Ó dára. Àwa yóò lọ sí ibẹ̀, a sì mú oúnjẹ díẹ̀ wá. Good. We will go there, and we will bring back a little food.
Adé àti Yẹmi lọ sí ilé-ìtajà kejì; wọ́n rí oúnjẹ tí wọ́n fẹ́.
YẸMI Mo fẹ́ mú búrẹ́dì àti wàrà wá; ǹjẹ́ o lè gbé àpò yìí fún mi? I want to bring bread and milk; can you carry this bag for me?
ADÉ Bẹ́ẹ̀ni, mo lè gbé e. Ẹ jẹ́ kí a padà sí ilé nísinsìnyí. Yes, I can carry it. Let's return home now.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Errands and shared carrying

    In many Yorùbá communities, running errands together and sharing the physical task of carrying goods, as Adé and Yẹmi do with their bag, is a common and expected form of everyday cooperation, especially among family members, neighbors, and close friends. Offering to carry something for someone, using a frame such as ẹ jọ̀wọ́, ẹ gbé...fún mi, is generally seen as courteous rather than presumptuous.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Inserting an English-style "and" between every verb

    Error: *Adé lọ àti rà búrẹ́dì, inserting a separate connecting word between the two verbs as English would.

    Correction: Adé lọ rà búrẹ́dì. Motion serial verbs are placed directly next to each other without a connecting word; the sequence itself signals that the actions are part of one connected event.

    Common Mistake — Confusing mú and gbé

    Error: Using mú for a large or heavy item, e.g. *Ó mú àpò ńlá náà, when carrying, not handheld taking, is intended.

    Correction: Use gbé for carrying larger or heavier items, and reserve mú for smaller items taken or brought by hand, as in Chapter 4's mú...wá frame.

Ní Àdúgbò — Iṣẹ́ àti Ìtọ́ni Rọrùn

Around the Neighborhood — Errands and Simple Directions · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Expanding Everyday and Community Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Recall and apply the object-pronoun, request, and habitual-particle patterns from Chapter 2, alongside all Volume I and Chapter 1 systems.
  2. Ask where something or someone is using the question word níbo.
  3. Understand and give short, formulaic directions using yà sí ọ̀tún, yà sí òsì, and tẹ̀síwájú.
  4. Name common neighborhood places using vocabulary reviewed from Volume I and expanded here.
  5. Run a simple errand: state what you need to buy or do, and where you are going.
  6. Understand a short direction-giving exchange at natural pace well enough to identify the destination and the path described.
  7. Write a short note describing an errand, including where you went and what you did there.

Communication Scenario

It is now Adé's third day at Yẹmi's house. After finishing the morning chores from Chapter 2, Yẹmi invites Adé to come along on a short errand around the neighborhood — buying a few things and dropping off a message for a neighbor. Along the way, Adé must ask where places are, follow simple directions, and describe what he is doing, using the household and request language from Chapter 2 together with new neighborhood and direction vocabulary.

  1. Every task in this chapter reuses Chapter 2's object pronouns, request frame, and habitual particle máa ń, together with all systems consolidated in Volume I.
  2. Two new systems are introduced for the first time: the location question word níbo, and a small set of formulaic direction phrases.
  3. Direction-giving remains formulaic in this chapter. Full serial-verb analysis of motion verbs is reserved for a later chapter and is not required here.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Tone in question and direction phrases

Níbo carries a rising question shape without losing its own low-high-mid tone pattern on the syllables themselves. Keep the tone of each syllable distinct from the rising intonation of the whole question.

  • níbo — high, mid
  • yà sí ọ̀tún — mid, high, low-high
  • yà sí òsì — mid, high, low-mid

This is a review application of the Volume I skill "tone in connected speech," applied here to short direction and question phrases.

Grammar Focus

This chapter opens two systems: the location question word níbo, and a small set of formulaic direction phrases. Both build on the demonstrative and location vocabulary consolidated in Volume I, without introducing full serial-verb analysis of motion, which is reserved for a later chapter.

New Pattern 1 — Location questions with níbo
Níbo ni + Noun/Pronoun + wà?
Uses the familiar ni identification word from everyday speech together with the stative verb wà ("to be located") to ask where something or someone is. Learners produce this frame with nouns already known from Volume I and Chapter 1; no new pronoun forms are required.
Níbo ni ọjà wà?
Where is the market?
New Pattern 2 — Formulaic direction phrases
Tẹ̀síwájú… / Yà sí ọ̀tún… / Yà sí òsì…
These phrases are introduced as fixed, formulaic units for giving and following short directions. Learners may use and recognize them productively in this chapter without analyzing their internal serial-verb structure, which the Verb Serialization system schedules for later, controlled treatment.
Tẹ̀síwájú, lẹ́yìn náà, ẹ yà sí ọ̀tún.
Keep going, and after that, turn right.
New Pattern 3 — Near and far
Ó tòsí. / Ó jìnnà.
Simple stative descriptions that answer a níbo question with distance rather than a full route, useful for short, practical exchanges.
Ọjà kò jìnnà; ó tòsí.
The market is not far; it is near.
  • Níbo ni ilé ìwòsàn wà, ẹ jọ̀wọ́? Where is the clinic, please? Níbo ni + N + wà — location question
  • Ẹ jọ̀wọ́, mo fẹ́ ránṣẹ́ sí aládùúgbò wa. Please, I want to send word to our neighbor. fẹ́ + V — stated intention, recycled from Volume I

Model Dialogue

Ipò: Lẹ́yìn iṣẹ́ ilé àárọ̀, Yẹmi àti Adé ń rìn ní àdúgbò láti ra búrẹ́dì àti láti fi ìránṣẹ́ ránṣẹ́ sí aládùúgbò kan.
Setting: After the morning chores, Yẹmi and Adé are walking around the neighborhood to buy bread and to send a message to a neighbor.

ADÉ Níbo ni ibi tí ó ta búrẹ́dì wà? Where is the place that sells bread?
YẸMI Kò jìnnà. Tẹ̀síwájú lórí ọ̀nà yìí, lẹ́yìn náà, a máa yà sí ọ̀tún. It's not far. Keep going on this road, and after that, we'll turn right.
Wọ́n tẹ̀síwájú, wọ́n sì yà sí ọ̀tún ní orí ìsopọ̀ ọ̀nà.
ADÉ Àti ilé aládùúgbò tí a fẹ́ ránṣẹ́ sí i ńkọ́, níbo ni ó wà? And the house of the neighbor we want to send word to, where is it?
YẸMI Ó tòsí ilé búrẹ́dì; a máa yà sí òsì lẹ́ẹ̀kan síi. It's near the bread place; we'll turn left one more time.
ADÉ Mo yé mi báyìí. Ẹ jọ̀wọ́, ṣé mo lè kí wọn fún ọ? I understand now. Please, may I greet them for you?
YẸMI Bẹ́ẹ̀ni, ó dára. Ẹ ṣé, Adé. Yes, that's good. Thank you, Adé.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Errands, messages, and neighborly ties

    In many Yorùbá neighborhoods, running an errand often doubles as a chance to maintain relationships: greeting neighbors along the way, delivering a short message on someone's behalf, and asking after a family's wellbeing are commonly seen as ordinary and expected parts of moving through the community, not as separate, formal visits.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Dropping wà in a location question

    Error: *Níbo ni ọjà?, omitting the stative verb.

    Correction: Níbo ni ọjà wà? The frame Níbo ni... wà? requires wà to state that something is located somewhere.

    Common Mistake — Treating direction phrases as separately conjugated verbs

    Error: Attempting to analyze yà sí ọ̀tún word-by-word as if each part could be freely rearranged.

    Correction: Learn yà sí ọ̀tún and yà sí òsì as fixed, formulaic units for now. Full analysis of the underlying verb structure comes later, once the Verb Serialization system is formally activated.

    2. Keep going on the road (tẹ̀síwájú), turn right (yà sí ọ̀tún) to reach the bread place, then turn left (yà sí òsì) once more to reach the neighbor's house, which is near the bread place (tòsí).

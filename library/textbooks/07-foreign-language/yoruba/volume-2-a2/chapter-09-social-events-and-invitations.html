Àwọn Ìṣẹ̀lẹ̀ Àwùjọ àti Ìpèníjà

Social Events and Invitations · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Expanding Everyday and Community Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Recall and apply the future particle yóò, modal intention with fẹ́, and the formulaic complement frames fẹ́ láti and gbèrò láti from Chapter 8, and all systems consolidated in Chapters 1–7 and Volume I.
  2. Talk about upcoming social events, dates, and celebrations.
  3. Invite someone to an event and respond to an invitation.
  4. Explain availability using a small set of formulaic time-clause frames such as nígbà tí and ṣáájú kí, without analyzing their full internal grammar.
  5. Contrast the perfective/resultative particle ti with the ongoing particle ń when planning and reporting on events.
  6. Decline an invitation politely, softening a negative response with courteous language.
  7. Write a short event plan or invitation message.
  8. Maintain full tone marks, dot-under letters, and accurate connected-speech tone in invitations and refusals.

Communication Scenario

Yẹmi's exam is behind her, and she wants to celebrate with friends before her next busy stretch of work and study. She calls Adé to invite him to a small gathering at her house. Adé is glad to hear from her, but he already has a commitment that evening and has to explain why he cannot come, while proposing another time. Their conversation moves between talking about the event itself, what has already been arranged, what is currently being prepared, and what each of them is free to do. This chapter gives Adé, Yẹmi, and you the controlled language to plan events, extend invitations, and respond to them courteously.

  1. Every task in this chapter reuses Chapter 8's yóò, fẹ́ láti, and gbèrò láti patterns, Chapter 7's object pronoun, lè, and gbọ́dọ̀ patterns, and all systems consolidated in Chapters 1–6 and Volume I.
  2. A small set of formulaic time-clause frames is introduced here for the first time, alongside a contrast between ti (already arranged) and ń (currently being arranged).
  3. Time clauses with nígbà tí and ṣáájú kí are introduced only as formulaic frames; their fuller grammar is developed in later volumes.
  4. Only short, familiar-register invitations, acceptances, and courteous refusals are required here. Detailed formal event correspondence is reserved for later volumes.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Connected speech in invitations and refusals

Invitations and refusals are usually spoken quickly and courteously, which puts pressure on tone accuracy. Ìpèníjà ("invitation") has four tone-bearing syllables that must stay distinct even at a natural conversational pace. When declining, the softening phrase ṣùgbọ́n ("but") often blends into the surrounding words; keep its rising tone on the final syllable clear so the listener hears the contrast coming. Àyè ("availability") begins with a low tone that is easy to drop when the word is said casually.

  • ìpèníjà — low-mid-high-low; keep all four syllables distinct at natural speed
  • àyè — low-mid; initial low tone must be held
  • ṣáájú — high-high-mid; do not flatten the long vowel's tone
  • ṣùgbọ́n — low-mid-high; keep the final high tone clear when softening a refusal

This is a review application of the Volume II skill "tone in connected speech," applied here to courteous invitations and refusals.

Grammar Focus

This chapter introduces a small set of formulaic time-clause frames for talking about when things happen, and develops the contrast between ti (already arranged) and ń (currently being arranged) in the specific context of planning events. Only short, familiar-register comparisons are required; a full time-clause and complement-clause paradigm is reserved for later volumes.

New Pattern 1 — Formulaic time clauses with nígbà tí and ṣáájú kí
Nígbà tí + Clause, ... · Ṣáájú kí + Clause, ...
Nígbà tí ("when, at the time that") and ṣáájú kí ("before") link two events in time. At this stage, learners should treat each frame as a fixed formulaic unit rather than analyzing the internal clause structure. Full time-clause and subordination grammar is developed in Volume III.
Nígbà tí o bá dé, a máa jẹun papọ̀.
When you arrive, we will eat together.
New Pattern 2 — ti (already arranged) versus ń (currently being arranged)
Subject + ti + Verb (already done/arranged) · Subject + ń + Verb (being done now)
Ti, consolidated since Chapter 5, marks something already completed or already arranged. Ń, consolidated since Chapter 3, marks something happening right now. In event planning, this contrast tells the listener whether a detail is settled or still in progress.
Mo ti pe àwọn ọ̀rẹ́ mi; mo sì ń ṣe oúnjẹ báyìí.
I have already invited my friends; and I am cooking now.
New Pattern 3 — Courteous refusal frame
Ẹ ṣé fún ìpèníjà náà, ṣùgbọ́n ... + Reason
A polite refusal in Yorùbá first thanks the person for the invitation, then softens the refusal with ṣùgbọ́n ("but") before giving a brief reason. This frame should be learned and used as a fixed courteous unit, not built freely from separate parts.
Ẹ ṣé fún ìpèníjà náà, ṣùgbọ́n mo ti ní ìpàdé ní alẹ́ yìí.
Thank you for the invitation, but I already have an engagement tonight.
Language Focus — Settled versus in-progress event details

When Yorùbá speakers plan an event, they regularly distinguish what is already settled from what is still being worked on. Mo ti pe àwọn ọ̀rẹ́ mi ("I have already invited my friends") reports a completed, settled action with ti. Mo ń ṣe oúnjẹ báyìí ("I am cooking now") reports an action still in progress with ń. Learners should choose ti for arrangements that are finished and ń for arrangements still underway, rather than defaulting to one particle for all planning language.

  • Ǹjẹ́ o lè wá sí àpèjọ mi ní ọjọ́ Sátidé? Can you come to my gathering on Saturday? lè + Verb — recycled Ch. 7 ability/possibility question
  • Mo ti ṣètò gbogbo nǹkan fún ìṣẹ̀lẹ̀ náà. I have already arranged everything for the event. ti + Verb — completed arrangement
  • A ń múra sílẹ̀ fún ọjọ́ ìbí Yẹmi báyìí. We are preparing for Yẹmi's birthday right now. ń + Verb — ongoing preparation

Model Dialogue

Ipò: Yẹmi pe Adé lórí fóònù láti pè é sí àpèjọ kékeré kan lẹ́yìn ìdánwò rẹ̀.
Setting: Yẹmi calls Adé on the phone to invite him to a small gathering after her exam.

YẸMI Ẹ kú alẹ́, Adé. Mo fẹ́ pè ẹ́ sí àpèjọ kékeré kan ní ilé mi ní ọjọ́ Sátidé. Good evening, Adé. I want to invite you to a small gathering at my house on Saturday.
ADÉ Ó dùn mí láti gbọ́! Kí ni ìṣẹ̀lẹ̀ náà fún? I'm glad to hear that! What is the event for?
YẸMI Mo ti parí ìdánwò mi, mo sì fẹ́ ṣe àyẹyẹ pẹ̀lú àwọn ọ̀rẹ́ mi. Mo ti pe àwọn kan tẹ́lẹ̀; mo sì ń ṣe oúnjẹ báyìí. I have finished my exam, and I want to celebrate with my friends. I have already invited some people; and I am cooking now.
ADÉ Ẹ ṣé fún ìpèníjà náà, ṣùgbọ́n mo ti ní ìpàdé pẹ̀lú agbanisíṣẹ́ mi ní alẹ́ ọjọ́ Sátidé. Thank you for the invitation, but I already have a meeting with my employer on Saturday evening.
YẸMI Kò burú. Ǹjẹ́ o ní àyè ní ọjọ́ Sọ́ndè? That's alright. Do you have availability on Sunday?
ADÉ Bẹ́ẹ̀ni, mo ní àyè ní ọjọ́ Sọ́ndè. Nígbà tí mo bá parí ẹ̀kọ́ mi, mo lè wá sí ọ̀dọ̀ rẹ. Yes, I have availability on Sunday. When I finish my lesson, I can come to you.
YẸMI Ó dára gan-an! A máa dúró de ẹ ní ọjọ́ Sọ́ndè. Very good! We will wait for you on Sunday.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Declining an invitation with care

    Among many Yorùbá speakers, declining an invitation directly without softening language can sound abrupt, even among close friends. A courteous refusal typically begins by thanking the host for the invitation, offers a brief and honest reason, and often proposes an alternative time or gesture of goodwill. Learners should treat the thank-you-plus-reason structure introduced in this chapter as the default polite pattern, rather than a bare "no," and should be prepared to hear a proposed alternative in return, as Yẹmi does in the model dialogue.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Using ń for an already-settled arrangement

    Error: *Mo ń pe àwọn ọ̀rẹ́ mi to report that the invitations were already sent out.

    Correction: Mo ti pe àwọn ọ̀rẹ́ mi. Ti marks an arrangement that is already settled or completed; ń marks something still being done. Mixing the two makes it unclear whether a detail is finished or still open.

    Common Mistake — Declining without softening language

    Error: *Rárá, n kò lè wá as a bare, unsoftened refusal to an invitation.

    Correction: Ẹ ṣé fún ìpèníjà náà, ṣùgbọ́n mo ti ní ìpàdé. At this stage, learners should use the thank-you-plus-ṣùgbọ́n-plus-reason frame as a fixed courteous unit whenever declining an invitation.

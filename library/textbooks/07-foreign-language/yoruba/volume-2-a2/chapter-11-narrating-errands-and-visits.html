Ìtàn Iṣẹ́ àti Ìbẹ̀wò

Narrating Errands and Visits · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Expanding Everyday and Community Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Tell a connected story about errands and visits using ti, ń, and yóò in sequence rather than in isolated sentences.
  2. Use time connectors such as lẹ́yìn náà, nígbà tí, and kí to link events in the order they happened.
  3. Expand motion serial verbs from Chapter 5 into event-chain serialization that packages several linked actions into one clause sequence.
  4. Distinguish a completed event, an event still in progress, and a planned future event within the same narrative.
  5. Report the outcome of an errand or visit, not only the actions leading up to it.
  6. Maintain full tone marks and dot-under orthography across a multi-sentence narrative.
  7. Tell and write a short connected narrative about errands, visits, and their outcomes.

Communication Scenario

The morning after Chapter 10's conversation, Bùnmi wants to know exactly how Adé's errands went the day before — not just what Adé planned to do, but what actually happened, in what order, and how it turned out. Adé retells the whole sequence: going to the market, stopping at the tailor's, visiting a friend along the way, and coming home again. This chapter moves from talking about a single moment (Chapter 10) to narrating a connected chain of events that happened one after another.

  1. Chapters 1–10 controlled particles and serial verbs mostly one clause at a time. This chapter links several clauses into a single narrative using time connectors and event-chain serialization.
  2. The distinction between ti (already completed), ń (still ongoing), and yóò (still to come) becomes the backbone of narrative sequencing.
  3. Every narrative task in this chapter must end with an outcome — what happened as a result — not simply a list of actions.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation — Tone across connected narrative

Narrating several linked events in a row is one of the first places learners flatten tone under speaking pressure. Two words in this chapter deserve special attention:

  • lẹ́yìn náà — high-low-mid-mid pattern; do not let the phrase collapse into an unmarked "leyin na."
  • nígbà tí — high-low-mid; keep the high tone on the first syllable distinct from the low tone that follows.

Practice each connector on its own first, then inside a full sentence, then inside a three-sentence narrative, checking that tone does not degrade as the sentence chain grows longer.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter develops three systems already active since Chapters 3, 5, and 8–9: the particles ti, ń, and yóò; time connectors; and event-chain serialization. None of these is newly introduced — all are developed from formulaic or introduced status toward connected, independent narrative use.

Ti, ń, and yóò in narrative sequence

Building Block
Subject + ti/ń/yóò + Verb (+ Object)
SUBJ + already-done / ongoing / future + VERB (+ OBJ)
Mo ti lọ sí ọjà; mo ń padà wá; màá lọ sí ilé Tọ́pẹ́ lẹ́yìn náà.
I have already gone to the market; I am coming back now; I will go to Tọ́pẹ́'s house after that.

In a narrative, these three particles work together to establish a timeline: ti marks what is finished, ń marks what is happening right now within the story, and yóò (often contracted to máa or á in fast speech, introduced here receptively) marks what still lies ahead in the sequence.

  • Mo ti ra ẹ̀wù ní ọjà. I already bought a garment at the market. mo=I ti=already ra=buy ẹ̀wù=garment ní=at ọjà=market
  • Bùnmi ń dúró de mi ní ilé alágbàtàjà. Bùnmi is waiting for me at the tailor's. Bùnmi ń=ongoing dúró de=wait for mi=me ní=at ilé alágbàtàjà=tailor's house
  • Èmi yóò padà sí ilé kí oòrùn tó wọ̀. I will return home before the sun sets. Èmi=I(strong) yóò=will padà=return sí ilé=to house kí...tó=before

Time connectors: lẹ́yìn náà, nígbà tí, kí

Building Block
Clause 1 + lẹ́yìn náà + Clause 2
EVENT 1 + after that + EVENT 2
Mo lọ sí ọjà; lẹ́yìn náà, mo lọ sí ilé alágbàtàjà.
I went to the market; after that, I went to the tailor's.

Lẹ́yìn náà links two completed events in sequence and is the most frequent narrative connector at this level. Nígbà tí introduces a background event during which something else happened. Kí here links two events where one must finish before the other, as in the sun-setting example above.

  • Nígbà tí mo wà ní ọjà, mo rí ọ̀rẹ́ mi, Kúnlé. When I was at the market, I saw my friend, Kúnlé. Nígbà tí=when mo wà=I was ní ọjà=at market mo rí=I saw
  • Mo kí Kúnlé; lẹ́yìn náà, àwa méjèèjì jọ lọ sí ilé Tọ́pẹ́. I greeted Kúnlé; after that, the two of us went together to Tọ́pẹ́'s house. àwa méjèèjì=the two of us jọ=together lọ=go

Event-chain serialization

Building Block
Subject + Verb₁ + Verb₂ + Verb₃ (+ Object)
SUBJ + go/take/give-style verb chain sharing one subject and one timeline
Mo lọ ra ẹ̀wù mú padà wá sílé.
I went and bought a garment and brought it back home. (literally: I went, bought garment, took, returned, came, to-house)

Chapter 5 introduced motion serial verbs as short, two-verb chains (lọ ra, "go and buy"). This chapter expands the same principle into longer event chains of three or more verbs describing a single connected outcome, still sharing one subject and one overall timeline. English speakers often want to break these into separate sentences joined by "and then"; Yorùbá instead packages the whole sequence into one flowing clause.

  • Ó lọ sí ilé-iṣẹ́ ìfìwéránṣẹ́ fi lẹ́tà ránṣẹ́ padà wá. He went to the post office, sent the letter, and came back. lọ=go fi...ránṣẹ́=send padà wá=return-come
  • A lọ ra oúnjẹ mú wá sí ilé fún oúnjẹ àárọ̀. We went and bought food and brought it home for breakfast. lọ ra=go-buy mú wá=take-come

Do not insert English-style "and" (àti) between the verbs in an event chain; àti links nouns, not a shared-subject verb sequence.

Model Dialogue

Ipò: Ní òwúrọ̀ ọjọ́ kejì, Bùnmi bi Adé nípa ọjọ́ tí ó kọjá.
Setting: The next morning, Bùnmi asks Adé about the day before.

BÙNMI Ẹ kú àárọ̀. Ṣé ohun gbogbo dára lánàá, nígbà tí o lọ sí ọjà? Good morning. Did everything go well yesterday, when you went to the market?
ADÉ Bẹ́ẹ̀ni. Láti ìbẹ̀rẹ̀, mo kọ́kọ́ lọ sí ọjà, mo ra ẹ̀wù kan fún ẹ̀gbọ́n mi. Yes. From the beginning, I first went to the market and bought a garment for my older sibling.
BÙNMI Ó dára. Kín ni o ṣe lẹ́yìn náà? Good. What did you do after that?
ADÉ Lẹ́yìn náà, mo lọ sí ilé alágbàtàjà láti fi ẹ̀wù náà sílẹ̀. Nígbà tí mo wà níbẹ̀, mo rí ọ̀rẹ́ mi, Kúnlé. After that, I went to the tailor's to leave the garment. While I was there, I saw my friend, Kúnlé.
BÙNMI Ǹjẹ́ ẹ jọ lọ síbikan? Did the two of you go somewhere together?
ADÉ Bẹ́ẹ̀ni, àwa méjèèjì jọ lọ bẹ Tọ́pẹ́ wò kí á tó padà sílé. A dúró síbẹ̀ fún ìṣẹ́jú díẹ̀ nìkan. Yes, the two of us went together to visit Tọ́pẹ́ before returning home. We only stayed there for a few minutes.
BÙNMI Ní ìparí, ǹjẹ́ ẹ̀wù náà ti ṣetán? In the end, is the garment ready?
ADÉ Kò tíì ṣetán, ṣùgbọ́n alágbàtàjà sọ pé yóò ṣetán ní ọjọ́ Jimọ́. Nítorí náà, màá padà lọ gbà á ní ọjọ́ Jimọ́. It is not ready yet, but the tailor said it will be ready on Friday. So, I will go back and collect it on Friday.

Guided Practice

  1. Write one sentence with ti describing an errand you already completed.
  2. Write one sentence with ń describing something happening in the middle of that same story.
  3. Write one sentence with yóò (or máa) describing what will happen next in the story.
  4. Join two of your sentences with lẹ́yìn náà.
  5. Write one sentence using nígbà tí to describe something that happened while you were somewhere.
  6. Write one event-chain sentence with at least three verbs describing a single errand from start to finish (for example: go, buy, bring, return).
  7. State the outcome of your errand or visit in one final sentence, using ní ìparí or ní òpin ọjọ́.

Reading Practice

Àpẹẹrẹ — Ọjọ́ tí ó kọjá

Láti ìbẹ̀rẹ̀, Adé kọ́kọ́ lọ sí ọjà, ó sì ra ẹ̀wù kan fún ẹ̀gbọ́n rẹ̀. Lẹ́yìn náà, ó lọ sí ilé alágbàtàjà láti fi ẹ̀wù náà sílẹ̀. Nígbà tí ó wà níbẹ̀, ó rí ọ̀rẹ́ rẹ̀, Kúnlé, wọ́n sì jọ lọ bẹ Tọ́pẹ́ wò kí wọ́n tó padà sílé. Ẹ̀wù náà kò tíì ṣetán, ṣùgbọ́n alágbàtàjà sọ pé yóò ṣetán ní ọjọ́ Jimọ́. Ní ìparí, Adé padà sílé pẹ̀lú ìtẹ́lọ́rùn, ó sì gbèrò láti padà lọ gba ẹ̀wù náà ní ọjọ́ Jimọ́.

Gloss: From the beginning, Adé first went to the market and bought a garment for his older sibling. After that, he went to the tailor's to leave the garment. While he was there, he saw his friend, Kúnlé, and they went together to visit Tọ́pẹ́ before returning home. The garment is not ready yet, but the tailor said it will be ready on Friday. In the end, Adé returned home satisfied, and he plans to go back and collect the garment on Friday.

1. Put Adé's four main actions in order: (a) visited Tọ́pẹ́, (b) went to the market, (c) left the garment at the tailor's, (d) saw Kúnlé.

2. What is the outcome of the errand, and what does Adé plan to do next?

Writing Workshop

  • Choose a real or invented day involving at least two errands and one visit to a person.
  • Open your narrative with láti ìbẹ̀rẹ̀ and describe the first completed action using ti.
  • Use lẹ́yìn náà to move to the second action, and nígbà tí to describe something happening in the background of one of your errands.
  • Include at least one event-chain sentence with three or more linked verbs.
  • Close your narrative with an outcome sentence using ní ìparí or ní òpin ọjọ́, including a yóò or máa sentence about what comes next.
  • Read your paragraph aloud and apply the Chapter 10 Tone and Orthography Checkpoint before finishing.

    • Iṣẹ́ rẹ — Your task: Write the connected narrative described above (70–100 words).

    • Every ẹ, ọ, and ṣ is correctly dot-underlined.
    • Every required high and low tone is marked, including on particles and connectors.
    • ti, ń, and yóò/máa each appear at least once and are used correctly.
    • At least one event-chain sentence with three or more verbs appears.
    • The narrative ends with a clear outcome.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Telling the whole story

    When recounting a day's errands, Yorùbá speakers commonly narrate the full arc of events, including small detours such as an unplanned greeting or visit along the way, before stating how things turned out. A listener who asks "Kín ni o ṣe lánàá?" is usually inviting a connected account, not a single-sentence summary. Ending a narrative without stating the outcome — whether the errand succeeded, was delayed, or requires a follow-up visit — can leave a Yorùbá listener feeling the story is unfinished.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Breaking event chains with àti

    Error: *Mo lọ àti ra ẹ̀wù àti mú padà wá, inserting àti ("and") between each verb in a single-subject event chain.

    Correction: Mo lọ ra ẹ̀wù mú padà wá. When one subject performs a connected sequence of actions with one shared timeline, Yorùbá links the verbs directly, without àti between them. Reserve àti for joining nouns or separate clauses, not verbs sharing a subject in a serial chain.

    Common Mistake — Narrating without an outcome

    Error: Ending a narrative after the last action ("...mo padà sílé.") without saying how things turned out.

    Correction: Add a closing outcome sentence, such as Ní ìparí, ohun gbogbo dára. ("In the end, everything went well.") A Yorùbá narrative of this kind is expected to resolve, not simply stop.

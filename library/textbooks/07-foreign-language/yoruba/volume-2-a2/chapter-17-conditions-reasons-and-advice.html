Ipò, Ìdí, àti Ìmọ̀ràn

Conditions, Reasons, and Advice · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Expanding Everyday and Community Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. State a simple condition using bí ("if") and a simple time relationship using nígbà tí ("when").
  2. Give a simple reason using nítorí ("because") and torí náà ("so, therefore").
  3. Express obligation and necessity using gbọ́dọ̀ ("must") with independent control in familiar contexts.
  4. Express ability and permission using lè ("can, may") with independent control in familiar contexts.
  5. Give a warning or a prohibition using má, extended to safety and advice contexts.
  6. Write and speak a short piece of practical advice that combines a condition or reason with an obligation, ability, or prohibition.

Communication Scenario

Adé's neighbor, Ìyá Kúmọ́lá, is preparing to travel by bus to a nearby town for the first time in a while. She stops by to ask Adé's family for advice about the trip: what she must bring, what she should avoid, and what to do if something goes wrong along the way.

  1. Chapter 16 consolidated ni identification, subject focus, and object focus, and introduced clarification questions with ta ni, kí ni, and níbo ni.
  2. This chapter turns from identifying and clarifying facts toward giving advice: stating conditions, reasons, obligations, abilities, and warnings.
  3. Basic if/when/reason connectors are introduced here as controlled formulas. Gbọ́dọ̀ and lè move from formulaic to consolidated productive use, and prohibitions with má are developed beyond simple household commands into advice and safety contexts.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation — Careful tone in warnings and advice

Warnings and advice in Yorùbá often rely on precise tone to distinguish urgency from ordinary statement. Two points deserve attention:

  • The prohibition particle má carries a high tone and is typically spoken with a firmer, slightly slower delivery than an ordinary statement, without becoming harsh.
  • Gbọ́dọ̀ and lè must keep their tone pattern steady even when spoken quickly in a string of advice; flattening their tone can blur the difference between "must" and "can."

Practice reading the Chapter 17 building-block examples aloud slowly first, then at a natural advice-giving pace, keeping tone steady throughout.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter introduces basic if/when/reason connectors as controlled formulas, consolidates gbọ́dọ̀ and lè for independent productive use, and develops prohibitions with má into advice and safety contexts.

Conditions and time with bí and nígbà tí

Building Block
Bí + CLAUSE, (ó / kí ó) + RESULT / Nígbà tí + CLAUSE, RESULT
Condition or time clause first, result clause second, connected without an extra "then"
Bí òjò bá rọ̀, má jáde lọ láìsí gbòngbò.
If it rains, do not go out without an umbrella.

Bí introduces a condition, and nígbà tí introduces a time relationship. Both connectors are used here as controlled formulas: learners state the condition or time clause, then follow directly with the result, without needing to master the full range of subordinate-clause structures yet.

  • Bí o bá dé ibùdókọ̀, pe mí lẹ́sẹ̀kẹsẹ̀. If you arrive at the bus station, call me right away. bí...bá=if (conditional formula)
  • Nígbà tí o bá ń múra sílẹ̀, mú ẹ̀rí ìdánimọ̀ rẹ pẹ̀lú. When you are getting ready, bring your identification with you. nígbà tí=when (time formula)

Giving reasons with nítorí and torí náà

Building Block
STATEMENT + nítorí + REASON / REASON, torí náà + RESULT
Reason connector links a statement to its cause, or a cause to its consequence
Ọkọ̀ akérò lè pẹ́, nítorí ọ̀nà náà máa ń dí. Torí náà, mú oúnjẹ díẹ̀ lọ.
The bus may be late, because the road is often congested. So, bring a little food.

Nítorí gives a reason for a statement that came before it. Torí náà introduces a result or a piece of advice that follows from a reason already given. Together they let learners build short, connected pieces of practical advice.

  • Ẹ gbọ́dọ̀ dé ní kùtùkùtù, nítorí àwọn ọkọ̀ kì í dúró pẹ́. You must arrive early, because the buses do not wait long. nítorí=because (reason formula)
  • Ọ̀nà náà le máa dí lálẹ́, torí náà, má lọ nìkan ṣoṣo lálẹ́. The road can get congested at night, so do not go alone at night. torí náà=so, therefore (result formula)

Obligation with gbọ́dọ̀ and ability/permission with lè

Building Block
SUBJECT + gbọ́dọ̀ + VERB (must) / SUBJECT + lè + VERB (can, may)
Pre-verbal modal particles placed directly before the main verb
O gbọ́dọ̀ mú owó tó tó. O lè bèèrè ìrànlọ́wọ́ ní ibùdókọ̀.
You must bring enough money. You can ask for help at the bus station.

Gbọ́dọ̀ expresses necessity or strong obligation. Lè expresses ability or permission. Both particles are now consolidated for independent use: learners should be able to use them accurately in new, familiar-type advice situations without a model sentence to copy.

  • Ẹ gbọ́dọ̀ ṣọ́ra pẹ̀lú àwọn nǹkan yín ní ọkọ̀ akérò. You must be careful with your belongings on the bus. gbọ́dọ̀=must (obligation)
  • O lè jókòó ní ẹ̀gbẹ́ fèrèsé bí o bá fẹ́. You can sit by the window if you like. lè=can (ability/permission)

Warnings and prohibitions with má

Building Block
Má + VERB (+ OBJECT)
Negative-command particle placed directly before the verb, extended here to advice and safety warnings
Má gbàgbé ẹ̀rí ìdánimọ̀ rẹ. Má sùn ní ọkọ̀ akérò nígbà tí ẹrù rẹ wà ní ẹ̀gbẹ́ rẹ.
Do not forget your identification. Do not sleep on the bus while your luggage is beside you.

Má was introduced earlier for simple household prohibitions. This chapter develops má further into advice and safety warnings, where it is used to caution someone about a real risk rather than to issue a simple household command.

  • Má fi ẹrù rẹ sílẹ̀ láìsí àkíyèsí, torí ewu wà. Do not leave your luggage unattended, because there is danger. má=do not (safety prohibition)
  • Má bẹ̀rù láti béèrè ìrànlọ́wọ́ bí ohunkóhun bá ṣẹlẹ̀. Do not be afraid to ask for help if anything happens. má=do not (advice, not a strict command)

Full negative-focus interaction, embedded conditional clauses, and advanced subordination remain deferred to a later volume; this chapter keeps condition, reason, obligation, ability, and prohibition at the level of controlled, connected advice-giving.

Model Dialogue

Ipò: Ìyá Kúmọ́lá wá sí ilé Adé láti bi ìmọ̀ràn nípa ìrìn àjò rẹ̀ lọ sí ìlú mìíràn.
Setting: Ìyá Kúmọ́lá comes to Adé's house to ask for advice about her trip to another town.

ÌYÁ KÚMỌ́LÁ Ẹ ku iṣẹ́ o. Kí ni mo gbọ́dọ̀ mú, bí mo bá fẹ́ rin ìrìn àjò lọ sí Ìbàdàn? Well done. What must I bring, if I want to travel to Ibadan?
ADÉ Ẹ gbọ́dọ̀ mú ẹ̀rí ìdánimọ̀ yín àti owó tó tó, nítorí àwọn ohun kan lè yá mọ́ọ̀ré ní ọ̀nà. You must bring your identification and enough money, because some things can come up unexpectedly along the way.
ÌYÁ KÚMỌ́LÁ Bí ọkọ̀ akérò náà bá pẹ́, kí ni mo lè ṣe? If the bus is late, what can I do?
ADÉ O lè dúró ní ibùdókọ̀ pẹ̀lú sùúrù, torí náà má yára bínú. You can wait at the station patiently, so do not be quick to get upset.
ÌYÁ KÚMỌ́LÁ Ẹ ṣé púpọ̀. Ǹjẹ́ ohunkóhun mìíràn tí mo gbọ́dọ̀ ṣọ́ra sí? Thank you very much. Is there anything else I must be cautious about?
ADÉ Má fi ẹrù yín sílẹ̀ láìsí àkíyèsí, nítorí ewu wà. Nígbà tí ẹ bá dé, ẹ pe wa. Do not leave your luggage unattended, because there is danger. When you arrive, call us.

Nígbà tí ọkọ̀ akérò bá ń múra láti kúrò, ẹnìkan lè bèèrè ìrànlọ́wọ́ lọ́wọ́ àwọn òṣìṣẹ́ ibùdókọ̀ bí ó bá dàrú. Má fi ẹrù rẹ sílẹ̀ láìsí àkíyèsí ní ìgbà kankan, nítorí ewu wà. Torí náà, ẹnìkan gbọ́dọ̀ ṣọ́ra, kó sì máa bá àwọn ẹbí rẹ̀ sọ̀rọ̀ nígbà tí ó bá dé, kí wọ́n má bàa ṣàníyàn.

Gloss: If anyone wants to travel to another town, they must prepare well. One must bring one's identification and enough money, because something unexpected can happen along the way. When the bus is preparing to leave, one can ask the station workers for help if something goes wrong. Do not ever leave your luggage unattended, because there is danger. So, one must be careful, and one should speak to one's family when one arrives, so that they will not worry.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Giving advice respectfully

    In many Yorùbá-speaking communities, advice to someone preparing for a journey, a new responsibility, or an uncertain situation is commonly framed with care for the listener's wellbeing rather than as a list of blunt commands. Speakers often pair an obligation or warning with a reason, so that the advice sounds protective rather than controlling. Closing well-wishes, such as a short blessing for safe travel, commonly accompany practical advice of this kind, though the specific wording varies by family, region, and religious background.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Confusing gbọ́dọ̀ and lè

    Error: *O lè mú ẹ̀rí ìdánimọ̀ rẹ. when the intended meaning is a strict requirement, not an option.

    Correction: O gbọ́dọ̀ mú ẹ̀rí ìdánimọ̀ rẹ. Gbọ́dọ̀ expresses a requirement or obligation, while lè expresses ability or permission; the two particles are not interchangeable, even though both come directly before the verb.

    Common Mistake — Placing má after the verb

    Error: *Fi ẹrù rẹ sílẹ̀ má láìsí àkíyèsí. inserting má in the middle of the verb phrase.

    Correction: Má fi ẹrù rẹ sílẹ̀ láìsí àkíyèsí. Má must come directly before the main verb at the start of the prohibition, not after it or in the middle of the verb phrase.

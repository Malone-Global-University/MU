Àyẹ̀wò Ìparí Ìdìpọ̀ Kejì

Volume II Capstone: Community Communication · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Expanding Everyday and Community Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Demonstrate sustained tone and diacritic accuracy across speaking, listening, reading, and writing tasks drawn from Chapters 1–19.
  2. Participate in an extended interview covering family, routines, markets, directions, hospitality, health, work, events, narration, description, public services, opinions, media, focus/clarification, advice, and community problem solving.
  3. Comprehend a connected listening passage at natural Volume II pace and answer detailed and inferential questions about it.
  4. Read a paragraph-length text and demonstrate main-idea, detail, and inference comprehension.
  5. Produce a written portfolio piece that integrates particles, pronouns, negation, plural and quantifier noun phrases, possession, demonstratives, routine serial verbs, and basic ni focus.
  6. Self-assess growth from A2 toward B1 and identify systems to continue developing in Volume III.

Communication Scenario

The community leader has read the proposal from Adé and Ìyá Kúmọ́lá and has invited them, along with a few other neighbors, to a short meeting to discuss it further and to hear about how each person has been managing daily life in the neighborhood. This capstone chapter places you in a parallel position: an interviewer wants to hear how you communicate across the everyday and community domains you have built throughout Volume II.

  1. This chapter introduces no new grammar. It assesses the productive Volume II systems established across Chapters 1–19: strong and stage-appropriate tone and diacritic control, particles (ń, ti, future/modal formulas), pronouns, negation, plural and quantifier noun phrases, possession, demonstratives, routine serial verbs, and basic ni focus.
  2. The capstone has five parts: a speaking interview, a listening assessment, a reading assessment, a writing portfolio, and a Tone and Orthography Checkpoint review applied across all of the above.
  3. Advanced literary style, unrestricted dialect mixing, dense embedded clauses, contrastive or negative focus, relative clauses with tí as an independent production target, and resultative/event-chain serialization beyond the scheduled preview remain outside this chapter's assessment and are deferred to Volume III.

Apá A

Speaking Interview

Interview format

Complete this interview with an instructor, study partner, or by recording yourself answering each question aloud. Answer in connected sentences rather than single words wherever the question allows it.

tone & diacritics pronouns particles negation serial verbs (routine) ni focus (basic)

Ipò: Aṣèwádìí ń bèèrè lọ́wọ́ akẹ́kọ̀ọ́ nípa ìgbé ayé rẹ̀ àti àdúgbò rẹ̀.
Setting: An interviewer is asking the learner about their life and neighborhood.

AṢÈWÁDÌÍ Ǹjẹ́ o lè sọ fún mi nípa ìdílé rẹ àti àwọn aládùúgbò rẹ? Can you tell me about your family and your neighbors?
AṢÈWÁDÌÍ Kí ni o máa ń ṣe ní ọjọ́ kọ̀ọ̀kan ní ilé rẹ? What do you usually do each day at your house?
AṢÈWÁDÌÍ Sọ fún mi nípa àkókò kan tí o lọ sí ọjà tàbí tí o lọ sí ibi iṣẹ́. Tell me about a time you went to the market or went to work.
AṢÈWÁDÌÍ Kí ni ìṣòro kan tí àdúgbò rẹ ń dojú kọ, kí sì ni ìdí rẹ̀? What is a problem your neighborhood is facing, and what is the reason for it?
AṢÈWÁDÌÍ Ta ni ó lè ràn ọ́ lọ́wọ́ láti yanjú ìṣòro yìí? Who can help you resolve this problem?
AṢÈWÁDÌÍ Kí ni o máa ṣe ní ọjọ́ iwájú láti mú ìgbé ayé rẹ dára sí i? What will you do in the future to make your life better?

Iṣẹ́ rẹ — Your task: Respond to each interview question in full, connected sentences. Include at least one negative statement, one plural or quantified noun phrase, one routine serial verb, and one formulaic future step across your answers.

Apá B

Listening Assessment

Ask an instructor, study partner, or qualified Yorùbá speaker to read the passage below aloud at a natural pace, or read it aloud yourself and then cover it before answering.

If no speaker is available, this is a Listening Script for Independent Use. Machine text-to-speech must be checked by a qualified Yorùbá speaker before use as a pronunciation model.

"Ní àdúgbò wa, a ti pinnu láti bẹ̀rẹ̀ iṣẹ́ àtúnṣe fọ́ntí omi ní ọjọ́ Sátidé tí ń bọ̀. Ẹgbẹ́ àdúgbò ti kó owó jọ, àwọn ọ̀dọ́ sì ti gbà láti wá ṣe iṣẹ́ náà pẹ̀lú ọwọ́ wọn. Bí ọ̀rọ̀ bá dára, a óò tún bẹ̀rẹ̀ sí wo bí a ṣe lè tún àwọn ọ̀nà tí ó bàjẹ́ ṣe pẹ̀lú. Olórí àdúgbò ti kí gbogbo aládùúgbò kí wọ́n wá sí ìpàdé ní ọjọ́ Sátidé kí wọ́n lè rí bí iṣẹ́ náà ṣe ń lọ."

1. What has the community already decided to begin, and when?

2. Who has agreed to do the work, and how?

3. What might the community look into next, if things go well?

4. What has the community leader asked neighbors to do?

Iṣẹ́ rẹ — Your task: Answer the four questions above in complete Yorùbá sentences.

Apá C

Reading Assessment

Ìwé Ìròyìn Àdúgbò

Ní oṣù tí ó kọjá, àwọn ọmọ ilé-ìwé àdúgbò wa ṣe eré ìdárayá kan láti ṣe ìrànwọ́ fún ilé-ìwé wọn. Wọ́n kó owó jọ, wọ́n sì lo owó náà láti ra àwọn ìwé àti pátákó dúdú tuntun. Olùkọ́ wọn sọ pé inú òun dùn gan-an, torí àwọn akẹ́kọ̀ọ́ náà ṣiṣẹ́ pọ̀ dáadáa, wọn kò sì fi ọ̀lẹ̀yà ṣe iṣẹ́ náà. Àwọn òbí tí wọ́n wá wo eré náà sọ pé àwọn fẹ́ ràn án lọ́wọ́ láti ṣe irú eré bẹ́ẹ̀ lẹ́ẹ̀kansì ní ọdún tí ń bọ̀.

1. Who organized the fundraising activity, and why?

2. What did they buy with the money they gathered?

3. What does the teacher's comment suggest about the students' attitude?

4. What do the parents want to happen next year?

Iṣẹ́ rẹ — Your task: Answer the four questions above. For Question 3, explain your inference in one or two sentences.

Apá D

Writing Portfolio

Select one of the following two writing tasks and produce a connected text of 100–140 words with full tone marks and dot-under letters throughout.

Option 1 — Descriptive Narrative

Describe a person, place, or object in your neighborhood in detail, then narrate a short event connected to it (something that happened, is happening, or will happen).

  • at least one possessive noun phrase
  • at least one demonstrative
  • at least one plural or quantifier phrase
  • at least one routine serial verb

Option 2 — Community Report

Write a short report about a community matter (real or invented): state the situation, give a reason, and describe a proposed or completed solution.

  • at least one negative statement
  • at least one formulaic future step
  • at least one basic ni focus statement
  • at least one routine serial verb

Iṣẹ́ rẹ — Your task: Write your chosen portfolio piece, then complete the Tone and Orthography Checkpoint review in Part E before submitting.

Apá E

Tone and Orthography Checkpoint

Self-review — apply to Parts A, C, and D

Before you submit your interview notes, reading answers, and writing portfolio, review each one against the checklist below.

  • Every vowel that should carry a high or low tone mark has one.
  • Every mid-tone syllable is left correctly unmarked.
  • ẹ, ọ, and ṣ are distinguished from e, o, and s throughout.
  • Syllabic nasals carry the correct tone mark.
  • Names and titles preserve their correct tone-marked spelling.
  • No unscheduled contrastive focus, relative clauses with tí, or full future/modal paradigms have been introduced as independent production.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Community accountability and shared progress

Neighborhood meetings that report on shared projects, such as the water pump repair described above, often serve two purposes at once: giving practical updates and reaffirming that the community is working together. When describing community progress in Yorùbá, it can be effective to name the group responsible for an action (the community association, the young people, the leader) rather than describing the outcome in a way that leaves the contributors unclear, though specific conventions vary by community and context.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Treating the interview as a memorized script

Error: Reciting fixed sentences from earlier chapters without adapting them to answer the specific interview question asked.

Correction: Use the vocabulary and structures from Chapters 1–19 as flexible tools, adapting them to answer each question directly and specifically rather than repeating a memorized paragraph regardless of the question.

Common Mistake — Letting tone accuracy drop in longer capstone writing

Error: Writing a longer 100–140 word portfolio piece with fewer tone marks or dot-under letters than in shorter Chapter 1–18 exercises.

Correction: Apply the Part E Tone and Orthography Checkpoint to the entire portfolio piece, sentence by sentence, exactly as you would to a single short sentence.

Answer Key

Part B — Listening Comprehension Questions

  1. The community has decided to begin repairing the water pump, starting the coming Saturday.
  2. The community association has gathered money, and the young people have agreed to do the work with their own hands.
  3. If things go well, they may also look into repairing the damaged roads.
  4. The community leader has asked all neighbors to come to a meeting on Saturday to see how the work is progressing.

Part C — Reading Comprehension Questions

  1. The school students organized the fundraising activity to help their school.
  2. They used the money to buy new books and a new blackboard.
  3. The teacher's comment suggests the students worked together well and cooperatively, without laziness, indicating a positive and diligent attitude.
  4. The parents want to help organize a similar activity again next year.

Looking Ahead

Ohun tí o lè ṣe báyìí — What you can do now

You have completed Volume II. You can now speak, listen, read, and write about everyday and community life with growing independence, moving from A2 toward B1, while maintaining sustained tone and orthographic accuracy across connected texts and extended interaction.

Volume III: Independent Communication and Connected Discourse

Volume III develops stronger tone control in connected speech, expanded serial verbs, focus and contrast in information structure, relative clauses with tí, subordinate clauses, reported speech, expressive language, and independent essays, letters, and narratives, building B1-oriented integrated performance toward B2.

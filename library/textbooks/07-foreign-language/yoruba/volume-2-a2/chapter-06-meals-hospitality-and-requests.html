Oúnjẹ, Àlejòrí, àti Ìbéèrè

Meals, Hospitality, and Requests · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Expanding Everyday and Community Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Recall and apply motion serial verbs with lọ, wá, mú, gbé, Chapter 3's direction phrases, Chapter 4's quantities, and all Volume I systems.
  2. Use fẹ́ ("want, would like") and lè ("can, may") to make and respond to polite requests and offers around a meal.
  3. Soften requests and offers using jọ̀wọ́, questions with ǹjẹ́, and other controlled polite frames.
  4. Accept and decline an invitation or offer courteously.
  5. Give a simple courteous warning or caution using má in a hospitality context.
  6. Perform a short hospitality dialogue as both host and guest.
  7. Write a short thank-you note using controlled request, offer, and courtesy language.
  8. Maintain full tone marks and dot-under letters throughout all spoken and written practice.

Communication Scenario

Adé and Yẹmi carry their bread, bananas, milk, and other food back home. Yẹmi's aunt, Ìyá Bísí, is waiting to host them for a shared meal before they part ways for the day. Around the table, family and guests constantly request, offer, accept, decline, and gently warn one another — "would you like more?", "please pass the water," "be careful, it is still hot." This chapter gives Adé and Yẹmi, and you, the controlled language to do all of this politely.

  1. Every task in this chapter reuses Chapter 5's motion serial verbs, Chapter 4's àwọn, numerals, and élòó, Chapter 3's direction phrases, Chapter 2's object pronouns and request frame, and all systems consolidated in Volume I.
  2. Two systems move from formulaic recognition into controlled productive use for the first time: request/offer frames built on fẹ́ and lè with polite softening, and prohibitions/warnings with má applied to a hospitality context.
  3. Only short, familiar-register hospitality exchanges are required here. Formal institutional requests, negative focus, and advanced modal meanings are reserved for later chapters and are not required now.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Tone and intonation in polite formulas

Polite requests and offers in Yorùbá keep their tone marks fully intact; politeness is signaled by word choice and softeners such as jọ̀wọ́, not by flattening tone. Learners sometimes lower or "swallow" the tone of fẹ́ and lè when trying to sound gentle. Keep both words clearly high tone, and let the overall sentence melody, not the individual word tones, carry the polite effect.

  • fẹ́ — high tone
  • lè — low tone
  • jọ̀wọ́ — low-high tone, recycled from Chapter 2
  • ǹjẹ́ — a question-opening word; keep its rising, question-inviting intonation across the whole clause it introduces

This is a review application of the Volume I skill "tone in connected speech," applied here to short courteous exchanges.

Grammar Focus

This chapter opens two systems for controlled productive use: request and offer frames built on fẹ́ ("want, would like") and lè ("can, may"), softened with jọ̀wọ́ or ǹjẹ́; and prohibitions or warnings with má, applied here to a hospitality context. Only short, familiar-register hospitality exchanges are required; formal institutional requests and advanced modal meanings are reserved for later chapters.

New Pattern 1 — Polite request with fẹ́
(Ẹ jọ̀wọ́,) Mo fẹ́ + Noun/Verb
Fẹ́ states a want or wish. Adding ẹ jọ̀wọ́ ("please," recycled from Chapter 2) at the front softens the request without changing its grammar.
Ẹ jọ̀wọ́, mo fẹ́ omi díẹ̀.
Please, I would like a little water. (Literally: Please, I want water a-little.)
New Pattern 2 — Offer with ṣé o fẹ́ ...?
Ṣé o/ẹ fẹ́ + Noun?
Ṣé opens a yes/no question; combined with fẹ́, it becomes a polite offer. Use o for one familiar person and ẹ for a respected or plural addressee, recycling the honorific/plural pronoun distinction from Chapter 2.
Ṣé ẹ fẹ́ oúnjẹ díẹ̀ sí i?
Would you like a little more food?
New Pattern 3 — Ability/permission with lè, and warnings with má
Subject + lè + Verb · Má + Verb
Lè expresses ability or permission and is often used to accept an offer or make a soft request. Má, already active since Chapter 2, is developed here as a courteous warning rather than a harsh command, usually softened with ẹ jọ̀wọ́ or a following reason.
Mo lè ràn ẹ́ lọ́wọ́. Ẹ jọ̀wọ́, ẹ má fọwọ́ kan ìpẹjẹ yìí; ó ṣì gbóná.
I can help you. Please, don't touch this plate; it is still hot.
Language Focus — Accepting and declining courteously

A direct "no" can sound abrupt in a hospitality setting. To accept, Yorùbá speakers commonly say ó dára, ẹ ṣé ("good, thank you") or simply restate the item with mo fẹ́. To decline, a softened response such as ó dára, ṣùgbọ́n mo ti yó ("it's fine, but I am already full") is more natural than an unsoftened rárá ("no"). This softened declining pattern is introduced here only as a formulaic exchange; its full range of connector and reason-clause structures is developed in later chapters.

  • Ẹ jọ̀wọ́, mo fẹ́ ago omi kan. Please, I would like a glass of water. jọ̀wọ́ + mo fẹ́ + Object — softened request frame
  • Ǹjẹ́ mo lè mú síbí wá fún ẹ? May I bring you a spoon? ǹjẹ́ + mo lè + Verb₁ + Object + wá — question-softened offer, recycling Ch. 5's mú...wá pattern

Ipò: Adé àti Yẹmi ti dé ilé; Ìyá Bísí ti pèsè oúnjẹ àlẹ́ sílẹ̀ fún gbogbo wọn.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Hospitality and repeated offers

    In many Yorùbá households, a host will often offer food or drink more than once, as Ìyá Bísí does, and a guest's first polite refusal is sometimes only a formality before accepting. At the same time, a guest who is truly full, as Yẹmi is, can decline a second or third offer respectfully by giving a brief reason, such as mo ti yó ("I am already full"), rather than a bare "no."

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Dropping jọ̀wọ́ or ǹjẹ́ and sounding blunt

    Error: *Mo fẹ́ omi used alone as an unsoftened demand in a formal or unfamiliar hospitality setting.

    Correction: Ẹ jọ̀wọ́, mo fẹ́ omi díẹ̀, or ǹjẹ́ mo lè ní omi díẹ̀? Adding jọ̀wọ́ or opening with ǹjẹ́ keeps the request courteous without changing what is being asked for.

    Common Mistake — Using má as a harsh command

    Error: *Má fọwọ́ kan ìkòkò! spoken bluntly with no softening, as if issuing a sharp order to a guest.

    Correction: Ẹ jọ̀wọ́, ẹ má fọwọ́ kan ìkòkò náà; ó ṣì gbóná. Adding ẹ jọ̀wọ́ and a reason turns a bare prohibition into a courteous warning appropriate for hospitality contexts.

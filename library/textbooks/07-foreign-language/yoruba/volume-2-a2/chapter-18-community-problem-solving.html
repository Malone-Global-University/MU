Ìṣòro Àdúgbò àti Ìjíròrò

Community Problem Solving · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Expanding Everyday and Community Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Describe a practical community problem using vocabulary for community issues and services.
  2. Combine a condition or reason with an obligation, ability, or warning to explain why a problem matters.
  3. Refer to a future step or a possible solution using yóò and modal meanings such as intention and possibility, at a controlled, receptive-to-formulaic level.
  4. Use a routine serial verb construction to describe carrying out or resolving a step in a solution.
  5. Identify who or what is central to a problem using basic ni focus.
  6. Produce a short spoken and written proposal that connects a problem, a reason, and a proposed solution.

Communication Scenario

The residents of Adé's street have noticed that the local water pump has been broken for two weeks and that rubbish is collecting near the market corner. A small group of neighbors, including Adé and Ìyá Kúmọ́lá, meet to discuss the problem and decide what to propose to the local community leader.

  1. Chapter 17 developed conditions and reasons with bí, nígbà tí, and nítorí, and consolidated gbọ́dọ̀ and lè for independent advice-giving, alongside safety warnings with má.
  2. This chapter brings those systems together with previously previewed material — future/modal reference, routine serial verbs, and basic focus — to discuss a shared community problem rather than give advice to one traveler.
  3. No major new grammar is introduced. Future and modal particles, resultative serial verbs, and contrastive focus remain at their scheduled preview level (formulaic or receptive); this chapter integrates only the systems already active for independent production.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation — Presentation rhythm and repair

Proposing a solution to a group calls for a slightly slower, clearer rhythm than casual conversation. Two points deserve attention:

  • When listing more than one step of a solution, pause briefly between clauses so that ní àkọ́kọ́ ("first"), and lẹ́yìn náà ("afterward") stand out clearly, without losing the tone of the words around them.
  • When self-correcting mid-sentence, restate the corrected phrase at normal tone and speed rather than rushing or dropping tone — repair should sound natural, not apologetic.

Practice reading the Chapter 18 building-block examples aloud slowly first, then at a natural meeting-discussion pace, keeping tone steady through pauses.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter integrates reason, condition, obligation, ability, and warning language from Chapter 17 with previously previewed future/modal reference, routine serial verbs, and basic focus, applying them to describing a community problem and proposing a solution. No major new system is introduced.

Stating the problem with condition and reason (review and integration)

Building Block
Nítorí + REASON, ìṣòro wà / Bí a kò bá ṣe X, Y máa ṣẹlẹ̀
Reason and condition connectors from Chapter 17, applied to a shared community issue rather than personal advice
Nítorí fọ́ntí omi ti bàjẹ́ fún ọ̀sẹ̀ méjì, gbogbo àdúgbò ń ṣòro omi mímu.
Because the water pump has been broken for two weeks, the whole neighborhood is struggling for drinking water.

The condition and reason connectors from Chapter 17 (bí, nígbà tí, nítorí, torí náà) are the backbone of describing why a community problem matters and what will happen if it is not addressed.

  • Bí a kò bá tún fọ́ntí omi náà ṣe kíákíá, ọ̀pọ̀ ènìyàn lè ṣàìsàn. If we do not repair the water pump quickly, many people may fall sick. bí...bá=if (review); lè=may (review)
  • Ìdọ̀tí náà ń kó ewu jọ ní igun ọjà, torí náà a gbọ́dọ̀ sọ̀rọ̀ nípa rẹ̀ ní ìpàdé àdúgbò. The rubbish is gathering danger at the market corner, so we must talk about it at the community meeting. torí náà=so (review); gbọ́dọ̀=must (review)

Pointing toward a future step (formulaic, previewed)

Building Block
A yóò + VERB (we will) — formulaic future reference
Future particle used here only in a small set of fixed proposal expressions, not as a fully analyzed paradigm
A yóò sọ ìṣòro yìí fún olórí àdúgbò ní ọ̀la.
We will tell the community leader about this problem tomorrow.

Yóò is used here as a controlled, formulaic way to state a next step in a proposal. Learners are expected to recognize and use the pattern a yóò + VERB in a small number of familiar proposal expressions; broader future and modal paradigms remain scheduled for later development.

  • A yóò kọ àwíyé kan, a ó sì fi ránṣẹ́ sí ẹgbẹ́ àdúgbò. We will write a proposal, and we will send it to the community association. a yóò / a ó=we will (formulaic future)

Describing a solution step with a routine serial verb

Building Block
VERB₁ + VERB₂ (+ OBJECT) — routine serial verb, motion/transfer/result type
Two verbs sharing one subject and describing one connected action, as developed since Chapter 5
A máa mú owó jọ láti tún fọ́ntí omi náà ṣe.
We will gather money together to repair the water pump.

Routine serial verbs, developed since Chapter 5 for motion, transfer, and result, are consolidated here to describe carrying out a solution step. Tún...ṣe ("to repair") is treated as a fixed formula, not as a new discontinuous-verb pattern to analyze.

  • Àwọn ọ̀dọ́ ń wá kó ìdọ̀tí náà kúrò ní igun ọjà. The young people are gathering to remove the rubbish from the market corner. wá + kó...kúrò=come to remove (routine serial verb)

Identifying who is responsible with basic ni focus (review)

Building Block
PARTICIPANT + ni + REST OF CLAUSE
Basic identification focus from Chapter 16, applied to assigning responsibility in a proposal
Olórí àdúgbò ni ó gbọ́dọ̀ pe àwọn òṣìṣẹ́ ìjọba wá.
It is the community leader who must call the government workers.

Basic ni identification, consolidated in Chapter 16, is reused here to state clearly who is responsible for a step, without introducing contrastive or negative focus, which remain at a formulaic/receptive level until a later volume.

  • Ẹgbẹ́ àdúgbò ni ó lè kó owó jọ fún ìtọ́jú fọ́ntí náà. It is the community association that can gather money for the pump's maintenance. ni=focus marker (review)

Contrastive focus, question-focus interaction, negative focus, and fully productive future/modal paradigms remain formulaic or receptive; this chapter keeps them at preview level while integrating the productive Chapters 1–17 systems into extended discourse.

Model Dialogue

Ipò: Adé, Ìyá Kúmọ́lá, àti àwọn aládùúgbò péjọ láti sọ̀rọ̀ nípa fọ́ntí omi tí ó bàjẹ́ àti ìdọ̀tí tí ó kó jọ ní igun ọjà.
Setting: Adé, Ìyá Kúmọ́lá, and neighbors gather to talk about the broken water pump and the rubbish gathering at the market corner.

ÌYÁ KÚMỌ́LÁ Nítorí fọ́ntí omi ti bàjẹ́ fún ọ̀sẹ̀ méjì, gbogbo wa ń ṣòro omi mímu. Because the water pump has been broken for two weeks, all of us are struggling for drinking water.
ADÉ Bí a kò bá tún un ṣe kíákíá, ìṣòro náà lè burú sí i. Ẹgbẹ́ àdúgbò ni ó lè kó owó jọ fún ìtọ́jú rẹ̀. If we do not repair it quickly, the problem may get worse. It is the community association that can gather money for its maintenance.
ALÁDÙÚGBÒ KAN Kí ni nípa ìdọ̀tí tí ó kó jọ ní igun ọjà? Ewu wà níbẹ̀ pẹ̀lú. What about the rubbish gathering at the market corner? There is danger there too.
ÌYÁ KÚMỌ́LÁ Àwọn ọ̀dọ́ lè wá kó ìdọ̀tí náà kúrò ní ọjọ́ Sátidé. Torí náà, a gbọ́dọ̀ sọ fún wọn ní kùtùkùtù. The young people can come remove the rubbish on Saturday. So, we must tell them early.
ADÉ Ó dára. A yóò kọ àwíyé kan, a ó sì fi ránṣẹ́ sí olórí àdúgbò ní ọ̀la. Good. We will write a proposal, and we will send it to the community leader tomorrow.
ÌYÁ KÚMỌ́LÁ Olórí àdúgbò ni ó gbọ́dọ̀ pe àwọn òṣìṣẹ́ ìjọba wá fún fọ́ntí náà. Torí náà, kí àwíyé wa ṣe kedere. It is the community leader who must call the government workers for the pump. So, let our proposal be clear.

Guided Practice

  1. Write one sentence stating a community problem, using nítorí to explain why it matters.
  2. Write one sentence with a condition using bí that shows what will happen if the problem is not solved.
  3. Write one sentence using the formulaic pattern a yóò + VERB to state a next step.
  4. Write one sentence using a routine serial verb (for example, wá kó...kúrò or mú...jọ) to describe carrying out a solution step.
  5. Write one sentence using basic ni focus to say who is responsible for a step.
  6. Combine at least three of the sentences above into a short, connected proposal.

Reading Practice

Àpẹẹrẹ — Àwíyé Àdúgbò

Fún ọ̀sẹ̀ méjì sẹ́yìn, fọ́ntí omi àdúgbò wa ti bàjẹ́, torí náà ọ̀pọ̀ ẹbí ń ṣòro omi mímu. Bí a kò bá tún un ṣe kíákíá, àwọn ọmọdé àti àgbàlagbà lè ṣàìsàn. Ní àfikún sí i, ìdọ̀tí ń kó jọ ní igun ọjà, ó sì lè fa ewu fún àwọn tí ń kọjá níbẹ̀. Ẹgbẹ́ àdúgbò ni ó lè kó owó jọ fún ìtọ́jú fọ́ntí náà, àwọn ọ̀dọ́ sì lè wá kó ìdọ̀tí náà kúrò ní ọjọ́ Sátidé tí ó ń bọ̀. A yóò kọ àwíyé yìí fún olórí àdúgbò, a ó sì bèèrè lọ́wọ́ rẹ̀ pé kó pe àwọn òṣìṣẹ́ ìjọba wá láti ràn wá lọ́wọ́. Bí gbogbo wa bá ṣiṣẹ́ pọ̀, a lè yanjú ìṣòro yìí láìpẹ́.

Gloss: Two weeks ago, our neighborhood's water pump broke, so many families are struggling for drinking water. If we do not repair it quickly, children and elders may fall sick. In addition, rubbish is gathering at the market corner, and it may cause danger to those passing by. It is the community association that can gather money for the pump's maintenance, and the young people can come remove the rubbish this coming Saturday. We will write this proposal for the community leader, and we will ask him to call the government workers to help us. If all of us work together, we can resolve this problem soon.

1. What two problems does the neighborhood face, according to the passage?

2. Who is responsible for gathering money for the pump's maintenance?

3. What will the neighbors ask the community leader to do?

Writing Workshop

Choose one real or invented community problem (a broken facility, an unsafe area, a service that is missing or unreliable).

  • State the problem clearly in one or two sentences.
  • Include at least one reason using nítorí and one condition using bí showing why the problem matters.
  • Include at least one formulaic future step using a yóò + VERB.
  • Include at least one routine serial verb describing a solution step.
  • Include at least one statement of responsibility using basic ni focus.
  • Read your paragraph aloud and apply the Chapter 10 Tone and Orthography Checkpoint before finishing.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Community meetings and collective responsibility

    In many Yorùbá-speaking communities, practical problems affecting shared spaces or resources are commonly discussed collectively, often through a community association, an elders' council, or a similar local structure, rather than left to a single individual. Proposals to a community leader are often framed to show that many people share the concern and are prepared to contribute, whether through labor, funds, or coordination, though the specific structures and customs vary by community, region, and context.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Overusing yóò for every future-sounding idea

    Error: *A yóò lè tún fọ́ntí náà ṣe bí a yóò bá kó owó jọ. stacking yóò inside a condition clause where the condition already carries its own time reference.

    Correction: A lè tún fọ́ntí náà ṣe bí a bá kó owó jọ. The condition clause after bí does not need its own yóò; the future/possibility meaning is already carried by bí...bá and lè in the result clause.

    Common Mistake — Dropping ni when assigning responsibility

    Error: *Olórí àdúgbò gbọ́dọ̀ pe àwọn òṣìṣẹ́ ìjọba wá, when the speaker specifically wants to identify the community leader, and only the community leader, as responsible.

    Correction: Olórí àdúgbò ni ó gbọ́dọ̀ pe àwọn òṣìṣẹ́ ìjọba wá. Without ni, the sentence is still correct Yorùbá, but it no longer highlights the community leader as the specific person responsible; ni focus is needed to make that identification clear.

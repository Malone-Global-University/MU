Gbígbé Sí Ilé — Àwọn Ìṣẹ́ Ilé àti Ìbéèrè

Settling In — Household Routines and Requests · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Expanding Everyday and Community Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Recall and apply the pronoun, noun-phrase, and formulaic serial-verb patterns introduced in Chapter 1, alongside all Volume I systems.
  2. Use object pronouns after common household verbs to make and respond to everyday requests.
  3. Use the habitual particle máa ń to describe routines that happen regularly, distinguishing it from the ongoing-action use of ń alone.
  4. Ask someone to do something politely and agree to, delay, or decline the request appropriately.
  5. Describe two or three household routines using máa ń and time expressions from Volume I.
  6. Understand a short household dialogue at natural pace well enough to identify who is asked to do what, and how they respond.
  7. Write a short set of household-routine sentences and one polite request, correctly placing object pronouns and the habitual particle.

Communication Scenario

A few days have passed since Adé was introduced to Yẹmi's household. He is now staying in a small room near the family, and daily life has begun in earnest. Yẹmi's mother asks Adé to help with small household tasks, and Adé must learn to understand requests, respond to them, and describe the household's daily routine — using the new neighbors, family members, and household vocabulary from Chapter 1.

  1. Every task in this chapter reuses Chapter 1's pronoun and noun-phrase systems, together with all systems consolidated in Volume I, Chapter 20.
  2. Two new systems are introduced for the first time: object pronouns after household verbs, and the habitual particle máa ń.
  3. Vocabulary domain, sentence length, and tone burden remain within reach of a learner who has just completed Chapter 1 of Volume II.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Tone in verb-object sequences

Phrases like gbá ilẹ̀ and fọ àwo combine a mid- or high-tone verb with a low-tone or mixed-tone noun. Keep each syllable's tone distinct even when the phrase is spoken quickly.

  • gbá ilẹ̀ — high, low-low
  • fọ àwo — mid, low-mid

This is a review application of the Volume I skill "tone in connected speech" (Chapter 8 onward), applied here to verb-object phrases rather than compound nouns.

Grammar Focus

This chapter opens two systems reserved from Volume I: object pronouns after high-frequency verbs, and the habitual particle máa ń, which builds on the ongoing/progressive ń consolidated across Volume I.

New Pattern 1 — Object pronouns after household verbs
Subject + Verb + Object pronoun (mi, ẹ, wọn, mí, ọ, é...)
Introduced here only in high-frequency request and household contexts, following the Yoruba Pronoun System Matrix control on object pronouns and post-verbal reference. Full paradigm analysis is not required at this stage; learners practice the forms that occur in requests and responses.
Ẹ jọ̀wọ́, gbá ilẹ̀ fún mi.
Please, sweep the floor for me.
New Pattern 2 — Habitual máa ń
Subject + máa ń + Verb (+ Object)
Marks an action that happens regularly or as a routine, distinct from the ongoing/right-now meaning of ń alone (Volume I, Chapters 6 and 8). Contrast: Mo ń gbá ilẹ̀ ("I am sweeping the floor right now") versus Mo máa ń gbá ilẹ̀ ní àárọ̀ ("I usually sweep the floor in the morning").
Màmá Yẹmi máa ń fọ àwo ní alẹ́.
Yẹmi's mother usually washes the dishes in the evening.
New Pattern 3 — Polite request frame with kí
Ẹ jẹ́ kí n + Verb... / Jọ̀wọ́, ṣé o lè + Verb...?
Recombines the Volume I courtesy particle jọ̀wọ́ and the formulaic jẹ́ kí n frame from Chapter 1 with everyday household verbs, so learners can ask for and grant small favors.
Jọ̀wọ́, ṣé o lè rà búrẹ́dì fún wa?
Please, can you buy bread for us?
  • Ẹ jọ̀wọ́, ẹ máa ń jí ní àárọ̀ báwo? Please, what time do you usually wake up in the morning? máa ń + V — habitual question
  • Bẹ́ẹ̀ni, màá gbá ilẹ̀ fún ẹ. Yes, I will sweep the floor for you. V + object pronoun — agreeing to a request

Model Dialogue

Ipò: Ó jẹ́ àárọ̀ ọjọ́ kejì Adé ní ilé Yẹmi. Màmá Yẹmi ń sọ̀rọ̀ nípa ìsẹ́ ilé pẹ̀lú Adé àti Yẹmi.
Setting: It is the morning of Adé's second day at Yẹmi's house. Yẹmi's mother is talking about housework with Adé and Yẹmi.

MÀMÁ YẸMI Ẹ káàárọ̀. Ní ilé wa, a máa ń gbá ilẹ̀ àti fọ àwo ní àárọ̀ gbogbo. Good morning. In our house, we usually sweep the floor and wash the dishes every morning.
ADÉ Mo yé mi. Ǹjẹ́ mo lè ràn yín lọ́wọ́? I understand. Can I help you (all)?
MÀMÁ YẸMI Ẹ jọ̀wọ́, ṣé o lè gbá ilẹ̀ fún mi lónìí? Please, can you sweep the floor for me today?
ADÉ Bẹ́ẹ̀ni, màá gbá ilẹ̀ báyìí. Yes, I will sweep the floor right now.
YẸMI Bàbá mi máa ń rà búrẹ́dì ní àárọ̀; ó ti lọ sí ọjà. My father usually buys bread in the morning; he has already gone to the market.
Adé gbá ilẹ̀, Yẹmi sì fọ àwo pẹ̀lú màmá rẹ̀.
MÀMÁ YẸMI Ẹ ṣé, Adé. O ń kọ́ ìsẹ́ ilé wa dáadáa. Thank you, Adé. You are learning our household's chores well.

  2. Rewrite each sentence using máa ń to show it is a routine: Mo ń fọ àwo. → a habitual statement.
  3. Answer a request with agreement, then with a polite delay, using object pronouns both times.
  4. Write two original sentences contrasting ń (right now) and máa ń (usually) with the same verb.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Sharing household tasks with a guest

    In many Yorùbá households, a guest who stays for an extended period is often gradually invited into small household tasks as a sign of being welcomed as part of the family, rather than being kept apart as a formal visitor. Offering to help, as Adé does, is commonly seen as respectful and appreciated, though a guest should typically wait to be asked or to be clearly invited before taking on larger tasks.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Confusing ń and máa ń

    Error: *Mo ń fọ àwo ní àárọ̀ gbogbo, when describing a routine rather than what is happening right now.

    Correction: Mo máa ń fọ àwo ní àárọ̀ gbogbo. Use ń alone only for an action in progress at the moment of speaking; use máa ń for something that happens regularly.

    Common Mistake — Omitting the object pronoun in a request

    Error: *Ẹ jọ̀wọ́, gbá ilẹ̀, without indicating for whom, when the context requires it.

    Correction: Ẹ jọ̀wọ́, gbá ilẹ̀ fún mi. When a request benefits a specific person, include the object pronoun after fún so the listener knows who benefits.

    Practical Performance Task

    Iṣẹ́ aṣeyọrí — Abala 2

    Ìsẹ́ ilé àti ìbéèrè — Household routines and requests

    Orally describe (live or recorded) two or three household routines, then make and respond to one polite request with a partner or listener. Afterward, write the same content as a short paragraph (40–60 words).

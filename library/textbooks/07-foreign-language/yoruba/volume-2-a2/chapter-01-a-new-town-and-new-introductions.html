Ìlú Tuntun àti Ìfarahàn Tuntun

A New Town and New Introductions · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Expanding Everyday and Community Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Recall and apply, without support, the core tone, orthography, SVO word order, pronoun, particle, negation, and noun-phrase systems consolidated in Volume I.
  2. Use the honorific/plural second-person pronoun ẹ̀yin and the first-person plural àwa productively in formal group introductions.
  3. Introduce yourself to a small group and introduce two people to each other, using appropriate respect language.
  4. Use the plural marker àwọn together with a numeral or quantifier in a single noun phrase.
  5. Recognize and begin to use one controlled motion-serialization pattern (lọ mú...wá, "go get...and bring") in a formulaic, everyday context.
  6. Understand a short community-introduction dialogue at natural pace well enough to identify who is being introduced to whom.
  7. Write a short paragraph introducing a small group of people, correctly distinguishing singular and plural, formal and familiar address.

Communication Scenario

Adé did not, in the end, return home for good. A short-term work opportunity has brought him back to Yẹmi's town — this time to stay for several months. In this chapter, Yẹmi takes Adé to meet her extended household and a few neighbors for the first time, all at once. Where Volume I let Adé greet and describe one person at a time, Volume II opens with Adé needing to greet, introduce, and be introduced to a small group — and to do so with the level of respect a first meeting with elders and strangers requires.

  1. Every task in this chapter reuses Volume I's tone, orthography, pronoun, particle, negation, and noun-phrase systems exactly as consolidated in Chapter 20.
  2. One new pronoun set (ẹ̀yin, àwa), one new noun-phrase pattern (plural + numeral), and one new formulaic serial-verb expression are introduced for the first time.
  3. Everything else in this chapter — vocabulary domain, sentence length, and tone burden — stays within reach of a learner who has just finished the Volume I capstone.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Tone across compound nouns

Words like aládùúgbò and ìdílé carry tone across several syllables without a pause. Practice each word slowly, syllable by syllable, keeping every marked high and low tone distinct, then again at natural speed without flattening the tone pattern.

  • a-lá-dùú-gbò — mid, high, low-low, mid-low
  • ì-dí-lé — low, high, high

This is a review application of the Volume I skill "tone in connected speech" (Chapter 8 onward), not a new pronunciation system.

Grammar Focus

Volume II opens by expanding two systems that Volume I deliberately kept narrow: pronoun range and noun-phrase quantity. A third system, motion serialization, appears here only as a single fixed expression.

New Pattern 1 — Strong/honorific pronouns for group address
Ẹ̀yin (you-plural/formal) · Àwa (we, emphatic)
Builds on the respect/plural ẹ introduced in Ch. 3 of Volume I. Ẹ̀yin and àwa are strong forms used for emphasis or when addressing/including a full group, not for every ordinary sentence.
Ẹ̀yin ni màmá àti bàbá Yẹmi. Àwa ń gbé ní àdúgbò yìí.
You (all) are Yẹmi's mother and father. We live in this neighborhood.
New Pattern 2 — Plural marker + numeral in one noun phrase
àwọn + Noun + Numeral
Extends the Ch. 4/23 noun-phrase pattern by adding a numeral after the noun, without introducing agreement morphology.
àwọn aládùúgbò mẹ́ta
three neighbors
New Pattern 3 — Formulaic motion serialization
Subject + lọ mú Noun wá
Introduced only as a fixed expression ("go get [something] and bring [it]"). Learners use this pattern as a unit; its internal serial-verb structure is analyzed later in Volume II.
Yẹmi lọ mú aga wá fún Adé.
Yẹmi went and brought a chair for Adé.
  • Ẹ jọ̀wọ́, jẹ́ kí n fi ọ̀rẹ́ mi hàn yín. Please, let me introduce my friend to you (all). fi [obj] hàn [addressee] — introduction frame
  • Àwọn àgbà mẹ́ta wà nínú ìdílé yìí. There are three elders in this household. àwọn + N + numeral

Model Dialogue

Ipò: Yẹmi mú Adé lọ sí ilé ìdílé rẹ̀ láti fi í hàn àwọn òbí àti aládùúgbò rẹ̀.
Setting: Yẹmi takes Adé to her household to introduce him to her parents and neighbors.

YẸMI Ẹ jọ̀wọ́, ẹ̀yin ni màmá àti bàbá mi. Èyí ni Adé, ọ̀rẹ́ mi. Please — this is my mother and father. This is Adé, my friend.
MÀMÁ YẸMI Ẹ káàbọ̀, Adé. Ǹjẹ́ o ti wá sí ìlú yìí rí? Welcome, Adé. Have you ever come to this town before?
ADÉ Bẹ́ẹ̀ni, mo ti wá rí, ṣùgbọ́n báyìí ni mo kọ́kọ́ pàdé ẹbí Yẹmi. Yes, I have come before, but this is the first time I am meeting Yẹmi's family.
YẸMI Àwa ń gbé pẹ̀lú àwọn aládùúgbò mẹ́ta tí ó dára gan-an. We live alongside three very good neighbors.
Yẹmi lọ mú aga wá fún Adé, kí ó lè jókòó.
YẸMI Ẹ jọ̀wọ́, ẹ jókòó, Adé. Please, sit down, Adé.

Reading Practice

Àpẹẹrẹ — A household introduction

Orúkọ ìdílé Yẹmi ni ìdílé Adédàpọ̀. Ẹ̀yin ti mọ̀ màmá àti bàbá Yẹmi. Àwọn aládùúgbò mẹ́ta tún ń gbé nítòsí ilé wọn; wọ́n máa ń wá kí ìdílé náà ní gbogbo ọ̀sán. Ní òní, Yẹmi fi Adé hàn gbogbo àwọn tí ó wà nílé.

Gloss: Yẹmi's household is the Adédàpọ̀ household. You now know Yẹmi's mother and father. Three neighbors also live near their house; they usually come to greet the household every afternoon. Today, Yẹmi introduced Adé to everyone who was at home.

1. How many neighbors are mentioned, and how is that number expressed in the noun phrase?

2. Which sentence uses the introduction frame fi...hàn, and who is introduced to whom?

Writing Workshop

  • List three to four people you would introduce together as a group (real or invented).
  • Write one sentence using ẹ̀yin or àwa about this group.
  • Write one noun phrase combining àwọn, a noun, and a numeral to describe the group's size.
  • Write one sentence using the fi...hàn frame to introduce one person to the others.
  • Combine your sentences into a short paragraph (40–60 words), keeping full tone marks and underdots throughout.

    • Iṣẹ́ rẹ — Your task: Write the short group-introduction paragraph described above.

    • Every ẹ, ọ, and ṣ is correctly dot-underlined.
    • Every required high and low tone is marked.
    • Ẹ̀yin/àwa is used correctly for a group, not a single person.
    • The àwọn + noun + numeral phrase is present and correctly ordered.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Introducing a guest to a household

    When a guest is introduced to a Yorùbá household for the first time, it is common for the introduction to move from the most senior members of the household to others present, and for the guest to greet each person individually even after a group introduction has been made. A single group greeting is not usually treated as sufficient on its own.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Using ẹ for one person

    Error: *Ẹ̀yin ni ọ̀rẹ́ mi when introducing a single friend to one other person.

    Correction: Èyí ni ọ̀rẹ́ mi. Reserve ẹ̀yin for addressing or referring to more than one person, or a single person formally, exactly as ẹ already works in Volume I.

    Common Mistake — Reversing the numeral-noun order

    Error: *àwọn mẹ́ta aládùúgbò, placing the numeral before the noun.

    Correction: àwọn aládùúgbò mẹ́ta. The numeral follows the noun, consistent with the noun + modifier order established in Volume I, Chapter 4.

    Practical Performance Task

    Iṣẹ́ aṣeyọrí — Abala 1

    Ìfarahàn ìdílé — A household introduction

    Orally introduce (live or recorded) an invented group of three or four people to a partner or listener, and then write the same introduction as a short paragraph (40–60 words).

    1. Use ẹ̀yin or àwa at least once.
    2. Use one àwọn + noun + numeral phrase.
    3. Use the fi...hàn introduction frame at least once.
    4. Keep all writing fully tone-marked and dot-underlined.

    Assessment note: This task is evaluated on accurate use of the new pronoun set, correct numeral-noun ordering, correct use of the introduction frame, and continued accuracy in tone, orthography, particles, and negation carried over from Volume I. Serial verbs beyond the single fixed expression lọ mú...wá are not assessed in this chapter.

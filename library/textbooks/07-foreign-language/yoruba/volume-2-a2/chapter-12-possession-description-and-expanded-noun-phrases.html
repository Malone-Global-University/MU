Ohun-ìní, Àpèjúwe àti Gbólóhùn Orúkọ Gbígbòòrò

Possession, Description, and Expanded Noun Phrases · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Expanding Everyday and Community Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Express possession accurately using the Yorùbá possessive pattern, including possession with pronouns and with full names.
  2. Use demonstratives yìí and yẹn to point out specific belongings, people, and places within a description.
  3. Stack descriptors, quantifiers, and possessives in the correct post-nominal order within a single noun phrase.
  4. Describe the appearance and condition of belongings, people, rooms, and places using controlled adjective and stative-verb patterns.
  5. Combine possession, description, and quantity into one expanded noun phrase without breaking Yorùbá word order.
  6. Maintain full tone marks and dot-under orthography across multi-word descriptive phrases.
  7. Write a descriptive paragraph about a belonging, a person, or a place using expanded noun phrases.

Communication Scenario

A few days after Adé's errand story in Chapter 11, Bùnmi is helping Adé's older sibling get ready to move into a new room at the family house. Bùnmi has never seen the new room or the sibling's things, so she asks Adé to describe them in detail — whose things they are, what they look like, how many there are, and which ones she means when several similar items are in the room. Chapters 1–11 allowed learners to name people, places, and objects and to attach a single modifier to a noun. This chapter expands that single-word description into a full, stacked noun phrase.

  1. Chapters 2 and 4 introduced possession and plural marking as separate, single-purpose patterns. This chapter develops them together inside one expanded noun phrase.
  2. Demonstratives, quantifiers, descriptors, and possessives now combine in a fixed order rather than appearing one at a time.
  3. Every descriptive task in this chapter should let a listener or reader identify exactly which item, person, or place is meant — not just what it is generally like.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation — Tone across stacked modifiers

When several modifiers follow a single noun, learners often let tone flatten toward the end of the phrase. Two patterns deserve special attention:

  • ilé ńlá funfun mi — each word keeps its own tone pattern; do not let ńlá and funfun blur into a single unmarked run of syllables.
  • àpótí aṣọ tuntun yìí — the demonstrative yìí at the end still carries its own rising tone shape and must not be swallowed after a long noun phrase.

Practice building the phrase word by word — noun, then possessive or descriptor, then quantifier, then demonstrative — checking tone accuracy at each stage before saying the full phrase at natural speed.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter develops three systems active since Chapters 2 and 4: possessive noun phrases, plural and quantifier marking, and single-word description. It combines them, together with demonstratives, into one expanded and correctly ordered noun phrase.

Possessive noun phrases

Building Block
Noun + Possessor (pronoun or name)
NOUN + POSSESSOR, possessor follows the possessed noun directly
ilé mi; aṣọ Adé; ọkọ̀ ẹ̀gbọ́n rẹ̀.
my house; Adé's clothing; his/her older sibling's vehicle.

Yorùbá possession places the possessor immediately after the possessed noun, with no separate possessive marker in most everyday cases. Pronoun possessors use the same short forms learners already control from Chapter 2; name and title possessors follow the noun directly.

  • Yàrá tuntun ẹ̀gbọ́n mi tóbi púpọ̀. My older sibling's new room is very big. yàrá=room tuntun=new ẹ̀gbọ́n mi=my older-sibling tóbi púpọ̀=is very big
  • Àpótí aṣọ Adé wà nínú yàrá náà. Adé's clothing box is inside the room. àpótí aṣọ=clothing box Adé=possessor wà nínú=is inside

Demonstratives: yìí and yẹn

Building Block
[Noun Phrase] + yìí / yẹn
FULL NOUN PHRASE + this / that, demonstrative closes the phrase
àpótí aṣọ funfun yìí; yàrá ńlá yẹn.
this white clothing box; that big room.

A demonstrative in Yorùbá comes after the entire noun phrase it points to, including any descriptors, possessives, or quantifiers already attached. Yìí points to something close to the speaker; yẹn points to something farther away or already mentioned.

  • Ṣé àwọn aṣọ tuntun yìí jẹ́ tirẹ? Are these new clothes yours? àwọn=plural aṣọ tuntun=new clothes yìí=this jẹ́ tirẹ=is yours
  • Yàrá kékeré yẹn ni ti ẹ̀gbọ́n mi. That small room is my older sibling's. yàrá kékeré=small room yẹn=that ni=focus/copula ti ẹ̀gbọ́n mi=belongs-to my older-sibling

Stacking descriptors, quantifiers, and possessives

Building Block
Noun + Descriptor(s) + Possessor + Quantifier + Demonstrative
NOUN + description + possession + quantity + this/that, in that order
àpótí aṣọ funfun ẹ̀gbọ́n mi méjì yìí.
these two white clothing boxes of my older sibling's.

When several modifiers describe the same noun, Yorùbá stacks them in a fixed order after the noun: color or quality descriptors first, then the possessor, then a numeral or quantifier, then a demonstrative if one is needed. Reversing this order is a common source of confusion, so build longer phrases piece by piece rather than translating the English order directly.

  • Mo fẹ́ mú aṣọ funfun ẹ̀gbọ́n mi méjì yẹn wá. I want to bring those two white clothes of my older sibling's. aṣọ funfun=white clothes ẹ̀gbọ́n mi=my older-sibling méjì=two yẹn=that
  • Àwọn ìwé kékeré rẹ̀ mẹ́ta wà lórí tábìlì. His/her three small books are on the table. àwọn=plural ìwé kékeré=small books rẹ̀=his/her mẹ́ta=three

Full relative-clause modification of this kind of noun phrase (for example, "the box that Adé bought") is introduced only receptively in this chapter and becomes a productive target in a later chapter.

Model Dialogue

Ipò: Ní ilé, Bùnmi ń béèrè lọ́wọ́ Adé nípa yàrá tuntun ẹ̀gbọ́n rẹ̀.
Setting: At home, Bùnmi is asking Adé about his older sibling's new room.

BÙNMI Ṣé o ti rí yàrá tuntun ẹ̀gbọ́n rẹ? Have you seen your older sibling's new room?
ADÉ Bẹ́ẹ̀ni, mo ti rí i. Yàrá náà tóbi púpọ̀, ó sì funfun jùlọ. Yes, I have seen it. The room is very big, and it is quite white.
BÙNMI Kín ni ó wà nínú rẹ̀? Ṣé ó ti kó ohun-ìní rẹ̀ lọ síbẹ̀? What is inside it? Has he already moved his belongings there?
ADÉ Àpótí aṣọ funfun rẹ̀ méjì wà nínú yàrá náà, àti ìwé kékeré rẹ̀ mẹ́ta lórí tábìlì. His two white clothing boxes are in the room, and his three small books are on the table.
BÙNMI Àpótí wo nínú méjì náà ni ó dàgbà jùlọ? Which of the two boxes is the older one?
ADÉ Àpótí àtijọ́ yẹn, ní ìdọ̀tí kékeré. Àpótí tuntun yìí lẹ́wà ju ti àtijọ́ náà lọ. That old box, over there, is a little worn. This new box is more attractive than the old one.
BÙNMI Ṣé yàrá kékeré tí ó wà lẹ́gbẹ̀ẹ́ náà tún jẹ́ ti ẹ̀gbọ́n rẹ? Is the small room next to it also your older sibling's?
ADÉ Rárá, yàrá kékeré yẹn ti mi ni; yàrá ńlá yìí ni ti ẹ̀gbọ́n mi. No, that small room is mine; this big room is my older sibling's.

Guided Practice

  1. Write one noun phrase naming a belonging and its possessor (for example, ìwé Adé).
  2. Add one descriptor to the same noun phrase, keeping the descriptor after the noun.
  3. Add a quantifier (a number or àwọn) to the same phrase.
  4. Close the phrase with yìí or yẹn, checking the required word order.
  5. Write two full sentences describing a room or place, using at least one stacked noun phrase in each.
  6. Write one sentence contrasting two similar belongings using yìí for one and yẹn for the other.
  7. Describe one person's appearance in one sentence, using a possessive and a descriptor together.

Speaking Practice

Pẹ̀lú alábàáṣepọ̀ — With a partner

One partner describes a real or imagined room, naming three belongings inside it with a descriptor, a possessor, and a demonstrative for each. The other partner draws or lists what is described, then checks the description against the room. Switch roles.

Fún àwọn tí ń kọ́ nìkọ̀ọ̀kan — Solo alternative

Record yourself describing your own room or a photograph of a room, naming at least four belongings with stacked noun phrases that include a descriptor, a possessor where relevant, and a demonstrative. Self-check your recording against the tone points in the Pronunciation Focus box above.

Reading Practice

Àpẹẹrẹ — Yàrá tuntun ẹ̀gbọ́n mi

Ẹ̀gbọ́n mi ní yàrá tuntun kan ní ilé wa. Yàrá náà tóbi púpọ̀, ó sì funfun jùlọ. Nínú yàrá náà, àpótí aṣọ funfun rẹ̀ méjì wà lẹ́gbẹ̀ẹ́ ògiri, àti ìwé kékeré rẹ̀ mẹ́ta wà lórí tábìlì kan. Àpótí àtijọ́ yẹn kò lẹ́wà mọ́, ṣùgbọ́n àpótí tuntun yìí lẹ́wà púpọ̀. Yàrá kékeré tí ó wà lẹ́gbẹ̀ẹ́ yàrá ńlá yẹn jẹ́ tèmi, kì í ṣe ti ẹ̀gbọ́n mi.

Gloss: My older sibling has a new room in our house. The room is very big, and it is quite white. Inside the room, his two white clothing boxes are next to the wall, and his three small books are on a table. That old box is no longer attractive, but this new box is very attractive. The small room next to that big room is mine, not my older sibling's.

1. List the three belongings mentioned, with their descriptors and possessors.

2. Whose room is the small room, and how do you know?

Writing Workshop

  • Choose a real or invented room, person, or place to describe.
  • Open your paragraph by naming the room, person, or place and its possessor, if relevant.
  • Describe at least three belongings, features, or details, each using a stacked noun phrase with a descriptor.
  • Use yìí for at least one item close to you and yẹn for at least one item farther away or already mentioned.
  • Include at least one quantifier (a number or àwọn) inside a stacked noun phrase.
  • Read your paragraph aloud and apply the Chapter 10 Tone and Orthography Checkpoint before finishing.

    • Iṣẹ́ rẹ — Your task: Write the descriptive paragraph described above (70–100 words).

    • Every ẹ, ọ, and ṣ is correctly dot-underlined.
    • Every required high and low tone is marked, including on descriptors, possessives, and demonstratives.
    • Descriptor, possessor, quantifier, and demonstrative appear in the correct post-nominal order.
    • At least one stacked noun phrase contains three or more modifiers.
    • yìí and yẹn each appear at least once and are used correctly.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Describing shared spaces

    In many Yorùbá households, rooms and belongings are closely tied to family roles and relationships, so a description of a room often includes whose it is and how it relates to the rest of the household, not only its size or color. When describing someone else's belongings, it is common and polite to name the owner clearly, using the possessive pattern from this chapter, rather than leaving ownership ambiguous.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Placing descriptors before the noun

    Error: *funfun àpótí, placing the descriptor before the noun as in English "white box."

    Correction: àpótí funfun. Yorùbá descriptors, possessors, quantifiers, and demonstratives all follow the noun. Build the phrase starting with the noun, then add each modifier after it in the correct order.

    Common Mistake — Stacking modifiers in the wrong order

    Error: *àpótí yìí funfun mi méjì, placing the demonstrative directly after the noun instead of at the end of the phrase.

    Correction: àpótí funfun mi méjì yìí. The demonstrative closes the entire noun phrase, after any descriptors, possessors, and quantifiers, not immediately after the noun.

    Answer Key

    Part 12 — Listening Task

    • 2: Noun: yàrá ("room"); Descriptors: ńlá, tuntun ("big, new"); Demonstrative: yẹn ("that").
    • 3: Noun: ìwé ("book"); Descriptor: kékeré ("small"); Possessor: rẹ̀ ("his/her"); Quantifier: mẹ́ta ("three").
    • 4: Noun: aṣọ ("clothing"); Descriptors: àtijọ́, dúdú ("old, black"); Possessor: Adé.

    Reading — Comprehension Questions

    1. Two white clothing boxes (his), next to the wall; three small books (his), on a table; one old box (no longer attractive) and one new box (very attractive).
    2. The small room belongs to the narrator ("mine"), not the older sibling; this is stated directly in the final sentence.

    Chapter Review — Apá B

    1. Accept any correctly ordered stacked phrase, e.g.: ìwé funfun mi mẹ́ta yìí ("these three white books of mine").
    2. Accept any accurate contrastive pair, e.g.: Aṣọ yìí jẹ́ tuntun. Aṣọ yẹn jẹ́ àtijọ́.
    3. Accept any accurate descriptive sentence, e.g.: Ẹ̀gbọ́n mi ga, ó sì rẹwà.

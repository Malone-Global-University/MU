Iṣẹ́ Ìjọba, Ilé-Iṣẹ́, àti Fọ́ọ̀mù

Public Services and Forms · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Expanding Everyday and Community Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Ask and answer information questions needed to complete a clinic, school, office, bank, or transport-service form.
  2. Use controlled formal courtesy formulas when addressing clerks, officials, and service staff.
  3. Track personal reference (name, age, address, relationship, purpose) accurately across a multi-turn service exchange.
  4. Read a simple form or public announcement aloud with accurate tone and orthography.
  5. Complete a simple form using personal information vocabulary and expanded noun phrases from Chapter 12.
  6. Distinguish formal institutional register from the everyday register used in Chapters 1–12.
  7. Explain, in writing, the purpose and content of a completed form.

Communication Scenario

Now that Adé's older sibling has moved into the new room described in Chapter 12, the family needs to register several things: a change of address at the local government office, a new patient form at the clinic, and a school enrollment form for a younger cousin. Bùnmi and Adé accompany different family members to these offices over the course of a busy week. Each office asks similar information-gathering questions, but each requires a different level of formality and a different form.

  1. Chapters 1–12 built vocabulary, questions, possession, and description for personal and household life. This chapter turns that language toward formal institutional settings.
  2. Every service encounter in this chapter follows the same core sequence: greeting, stating purpose, providing information, confirming information, and closing courteously.
  3. Learners must track who is being asked about (the visitor, a family member, or a third party) throughout each exchange.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation — Reading forms and announcements aloud

Forms and public announcements are read more slowly and evenly than casual conversation, but tone must not be dropped for the sake of a formal-sounding rhythm. Two patterns deserve attention:

  • orúkọ, àdírẹ́sì, ọjọ́ ìpàdé — when reading a list of form fields aloud, keep a brief pause between items without flattening the tone of any single word.
  • ẹ jọ̀ọ́, ẹ dúró díẹ̀ — formal courtesy formulas are often spoken slightly more slowly than everyday speech; slower speed must not blur the low tone on jọ̀ọ́ or the mid-low sequence in dúró díẹ̀.

Practice reading the Chapter 13 sample form aloud, field by field, before reading it as a connected announcement.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter develops information questions and reference tracking from earlier chapters into a controlled formal-register question sequence, and adds formal courtesy formulas as scheduled formulaic language.

Formal information questions

Building Block
Kín ni orúkọ rẹ? / Níbo ni o ń gbé? / Kí ni ìdí ìbẹ̀wò rẹ?
QUESTION WORD + ni/verb + POSSESSED NOUN + rẹ (your), formal information-gathering frame
Kín ni orúkọ rẹ, ẹ jọ̀ọ́?
What is your name, please?

Information questions used in service settings follow the same question patterns learners already control, but they are consistently softened with ẹ jọ̀ọ́ and directed using the respectful second-person forms developed in Chapter 2. The clerk asks; the visitor answers with a short, complete phrase rather than a full sentence when the context makes the answer clear.

  • Oníṣẹ́: Kín ni orúkọ rẹ ní kíkún? Clerk: What is your full name? kín ni=what is orúkọ rẹ=your name ní kíkún=in full
  • Àlejò: Orúkọ mi ni Túndé Ọlá. Visitor: My name is Túndé Ọlá. orúkọ mi=my name ni=focus/copula

Stating purpose with ìdí ìbẹ̀wò

Building Block
Mo wá láti + VERB PHRASE
I came in-order-to + verb phrase, formal purpose statement
Mo wá láti forúkọ sílẹ̀ fún ilé-ìwé.
I came to register for school.

Stating a reason for a visit uses a controlled purpose frame rather than a full subordinate clause. This frame is introduced here as a formulaic pattern; full purpose-clause analysis is deferred to a later volume.

  • Mo wá láti ṣí àkọsílẹ̀ tuntun ní ilé-ìfowópamọ́. I came to open a new record at the bank. mo wá=I came láti=in-order-to ṣí àkọsílẹ̀ tuntun=open a new record
  • À wá láti yí àdírẹ́sì ìdílé wa padà. We came to change our family's address. à wá=we came yí...padà=change àdírẹ́sì ìdílé wa=our family's address

Formal courtesy formulas

Building Block
Ẹ jọ̀ọ́ ... / Ẹ dúró díẹ̀ / Ẹ ṣé / Ẹ fọwọ́ sí ibí
Fixed formal-register expressions used at each stage of a service exchange
Ẹ jọ̀ọ́, ẹ fọwọ́ sí ibí.
Please, sign here.

These formulas are used as fixed expressions in this chapter. Learners should recognize where each formula fits in a service exchange — opening a request, asking someone to wait, thanking, or directing an action — without analyzing their internal grammar in detail.

  • Ẹ jọ̀ọ́, ẹ dúró díẹ̀ kí n lè wá fọ́ọ̀mù náà. Please wait a moment so I can bring the form. ẹ dúró díẹ̀=please wait a moment kí n lè wá=so that I can bring
  • Ẹ ṣé púpọ̀ fún ìrànlọ́wọ́ yín. Thank you very much for your help. ẹ ṣé=thank you (formal) fún ìrànlọ́wọ́ yín=for your help

Complement clauses such as kí n lè wá ("so that I can bring") appear here only as part of a fixed formula. Independent production of complement clauses is not required until a later chapter activates it.

Model Dialogue

Ipò: Ní ilé-ìwòsàn, Bùnmi ń tẹ̀lé ẹ̀gbọ́n rẹ̀ láti forúkọ sílẹ̀ gẹ́gẹ́ bí aláìsàn tuntun.
Setting: At the clinic, Bùnmi accompanies her older sibling to register as a new patient.

ONÍṢẸ́ Ẹ káàbọ̀. Kín ni ìdí ìbẹ̀wò yín lónìí? Clerk: Welcome. What is the reason for your visit today?
BÙNMI A wá láti forúkọ ẹ̀gbọ́n mi sílẹ̀ gẹ́gẹ́ bí aláìsàn tuntun. We came to register my older sibling as a new patient.
ONÍṢẸ́ Ẹ jọ̀ọ́, kín ni orúkọ rẹ ní kíkún? Please, what is your full name?
ẸGBỌ́N Orúkọ mi ni Kúnlé Adéyẹmí. My name is Kúnlé Adéyẹmí.
ONÍṢẸ́ Níbo ni o ń gbé báyìí? Where do you live now?
ẸGBỌ́N Mo ń gbé ní yàrá tuntun ní ilé wa, ní òpópónà kejì lẹ́yìn ọjà náà. I live in the new room at our house, on the second street behind the market.
ONÍṢẸ́ Ẹ dúró díẹ̀ kí n lè wá fọ́ọ̀mù náà. Ẹ jọ̀ọ́, ẹ fọwọ́ sí ibí nígbà tí ẹ bá parí. Please wait a moment so I can bring the form. Please sign here when you finish.
BÙNMI Ẹ ṣé púpọ̀ fún ìrànlọ́wọ́ yín. Thank you very much for your help.

Reading Practice

Àpẹẹrẹ — Ìkéde ní Ilé-Iṣẹ́ Ìjọba

Ẹ káàbọ̀ sí ilé-iṣẹ́ ìjọba wa. Ẹ jọ̀ọ́, ẹ mú ìwé-ẹ̀rí yín àti fọ́ọ̀mù tí ẹ ti kọ tán wá. Bí ẹ bá ń wá láti forúkọ sílẹ̀ fún àdírẹ́sì tuntun, ẹ jọ̀ọ́ ẹ dúró ní ìlà kejì. Bí ẹ bá ní ìbéèrè kankan, ẹ lè bèèrè lọ́wọ́ oníṣẹ́ tí ó wà ní tábìlì àkọ́kọ́. A dúpẹ́ fún sùúrù yín.

Gloss: Welcome to our government office. Please bring your document and the form you have already completed. If you are coming to register a new address, please wait in the second line. If you have any questions, you may ask the clerk at the first table. We thank you for your patience.

2. Where should someone registering a new address wait?

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Formality in public offices

    In many Yorùbá-speaking communities, interactions with clerks, health workers, bank staff, and school administrators call for a noticeably more formal register than family or market conversation, including the respectful plural/honorific address forms introduced in Chapter 2. Patience and unhurried courtesy are commonly valued in these settings; a visitor who greets fully, states their purpose clearly, and thanks the staff member is generally seen as well mannered, even when a wait is required.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Using everyday register with officials

    Error: *Kín ni orúkọ rẹ? spoken bluntly to a clerk without any softening or respectful address.

    Correction: Ẹ jọ̀ọ́, kín ni orúkọ rẹ? Formal settings call for ẹ jọ̀ọ́ and, where relevant, the respectful plural/honorific forms from Chapter 2, even when asking a simple information question.

    Common Mistake — Omitting the purpose statement

    Error: Announcing an action (such as signing a form) without first stating a reason for the visit.

    Correction: Begin a service exchange with a clear purpose statement such as Mo wá láti... before moving to specific requests or questions. This mirrors the expected sequence of a Yorùbá service encounter: greeting, purpose, information exchange, closing.

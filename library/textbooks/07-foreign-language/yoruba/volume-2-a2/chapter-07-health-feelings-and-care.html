Ìlera, Ìmọ̀lára, àti Ìtọ́jú

Health, Feelings, and Care · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Expanding Everyday and Community Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Recall and apply request, offer, and warning frames with fẹ́, lè, and má from Chapter 6, and all systems consolidated in Chapters 1–5 and Volume I.
  2. Describe how you and others feel using expanded object pronouns after feeling and care verbs.
  3. Name simple body parts and common symptoms.
  4. Use lè to give simple health advice, extending its ability/permission meaning from Chapter 6 into recommendation.
  5. Use gbọ́dọ̀ ("must, have to") as a formulaic expression of obligation in health contexts.
  6. Negate simple recommendations and obligations using kò lè and kò yẹ kí as controlled, familiar-register formulas.
  7. Explain a symptom and give simple advice in a short spoken exchange.
  8. Maintain full tone marks, dot-under letters, and nasal-vowel and syllabic-nasal accuracy in health and emotion vocabulary.

Communication Scenario

A few days after the shared meal at Ìyá Bísí's house, Yẹmi wakes up feeling unwell. Her head aches and she has a mild fever. Adé stops by to check on her, and Yẹmi's mother, Màmá Yẹmi, offers care and simple recommendations. Around the house, family members ask how someone feels, name what hurts, offer comfort, and give gentle advice about rest, medicine, and seeing a doctor. This chapter gives Adé, Yẹmi, and you the controlled language to talk about health and feelings and to give and receive simple care.

  1. Every task in this chapter reuses Chapter 6's request, offer, and warning frames with fẹ́, lè, and má, Chapter 5's motion serial verbs, Chapter 4's quantities, Chapter 3's direction phrases, Chapter 2's object pronouns, and all systems consolidated in Volume I.
  2. Object pronouns are developed further here, appearing productively after feeling and care verbs such as dá ... lágara ("ache/trouble someone") and ràn ... lọ́wọ́ ("help someone," recycled from Chapter 6).
  3. Lè is extended from Chapter 6's ability/permission meaning into simple recommendation. Gbọ́dọ̀ ("must") is introduced here only as a formulaic expression; its fuller modal paradigm is developed in later volumes.
  4. Only short, familiar-register health exchanges about common, everyday symptoms are required here. Medical terminology, formal clinical registers, and detailed diagnosis are reserved for later chapters and are not required now.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Nasal vowels and syllabic nasals in health and emotion words

Several health and emotion words in this chapter carry nasal vowels or syllabic nasals that must not collapse into their oral counterparts. Ibà ("fever") ends in a nasal low-tone à, distinct from an oral a. Àárẹ̀ ("tiredness") has a long nasal vowel across its first syllable that must be held, not shortened. Ìsinmi ("rest") contains a syllabic nasal in its final syllable that must be pronounced as its own beat, not merged into the preceding consonant.

  • ibà — nasal vowel; do not pronounce as oral ìbá
  • àárẹ̀ — nasal vowel held across two tone-bearing units
  • ìsinmi — syllabic nasal as its own syllable
  • ìrora — three clear oral vowels; do not compress into two syllables

This is a review application of the Volume I skill "nasal vowels and syllabic nasals," applied here to a new vocabulary domain.

Grammar Focus

This chapter develops object pronoun use with feeling and care verbs, extends lè from Chapter 6's ability/permission meaning into simple recommendation, and introduces gbọ́dọ̀ ("must") formulaically for obligation. Only short, familiar-register health exchanges are required; formal clinical language and a full modal paradigm are reserved for later chapters.

New Pattern 1 — Object pronouns with feeling and care verbs
Noun/Subject + dá + Object Pronoun + lágara
The frame dá ... lágara ("to ache/trouble someone") places an object pronoun directly after the verb, developing the object pronoun system introduced in Chapter 2 into a new communicative use: describing symptoms.
Orí ń dá mi lágara.
My head is aching. (Literally: Head is troubling me.)
New Pattern 2 — Simple recommendation with lè
Subject + lè + Verb (recommendation)
Lè, already active since Chapter 6 for ability and permission, is extended here to give gentle, non-forceful advice — softer than an outright command, similar in effect to English "you could" or "you might."
O lè sinmi díẹ̀, kí o sì mu omi púpọ̀.
You could rest a little, and drink plenty of water.
New Pattern 3 — Obligation with gbọ́dọ̀ (formulaic)
Subject + gbọ́dọ̀ + Verb
Gbọ́dọ̀ expresses a stronger obligation than lè. At this stage, learners should treat gbọ́dọ̀ as a fixed formulaic expression meaning "must" in a small set of health contexts, without analyzing its full modal range. Negative obligation is expressed with the formulaic frame kò yẹ kí ("it is not fitting that").
O gbọ́dọ̀ lọ sí ilé ìwòsàn tí ibà náà kò bá dín kù.
You must go to the hospital if the fever doesn't go down.
Language Focus — Asking how someone feels

The most common way to ask about wellbeing is báwo ni ara rẹ? ("how is your body?", i.e. "how are you feeling?"), recycling the question-word pattern from Volume I. A simple answer states the body part and the feeling verb, as in orí ń dá mi lágara above, or a general state such as inú mi ń bàjẹ́ ("I feel unwell"). Learners are not expected to produce detailed medical description at this stage — only common, everyday symptom language.

  • Báwo ni ara rẹ, Yẹmi? How are you feeling, Yẹmi? báwo ni + ara + Object Pronoun — recycled Volume I question frame
  • Ó dára, ṣùgbọ́n orí ń dá mi lágara díẹ̀. I'm fine, but my head is aching a little. dá + mi + lágara — object pronoun with feeling verb
  • O lè sinmi, kò sì yẹ kí o jáde lónìí. You could rest, and you shouldn't go out today. lè + Verb (recommendation); kò yẹ kí + Verb (negative obligation, formulaic)

Model Dialogue

Ipò: Adé wá sí ilé Yẹmi láti bẹ ẹ wò; ara Yẹmi kò yá dáadáa lónìí.
Setting: Adé has come to Yẹmi's house to check on her; Yẹmi isn't feeling well today.

ADÉ Ẹ kú àárọ̀, Yẹmi. Báwo ni ara rẹ? Good morning, Yẹmi. How are you feeling?
YẸMI Ẹ ṣé, Adé. Orí ń dá mi lágara, ó sì dà bíi pé ibà díẹ̀ ń bẹ. Thank you, Adé. My head is aching, and it seems there's a little fever.
MÀMÁ YẸMI Ẹ jọ̀wọ́, ẹ jókòó. O lè sinmi díẹ̀, kí o sì mu omi púpọ̀. Please, sit down. You could rest a little, and drink plenty of water.
ADÉ Mo lè mú oògùn wá fún ẹ láti ilé ìtajà, tí o bá fẹ́. I can bring medicine for you from the store, if you'd like.
YẸMI Ó dára, ẹ ṣé púpọ̀. That's good, thank you very much.
Adé lọ sí ilé ìtajà, ó sì padà pẹ̀lú oògùn.
MÀMÁ YẸMI Ẹ jọ̀wọ́, Yẹmi, o gbọ́dọ̀ sinmi lónìí; kò sì yẹ kí o jáde. Please, Yẹmi, you must rest today; and you shouldn't go out.
YẸMI Mo gbọ́, màmá. Bí ibà náà kò bá dín kù ní ọ̀la, mo gbọ́dọ̀ lọ sí ilé ìwòsàn. I hear you, mother. If the fever doesn't go down by tomorrow, I must go to the hospital.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Checking in and communal care

    Among many Yorùbá families and communities, checking in on someone who is unwell is treated as a shared responsibility rather than a private matter. A relative, neighbor, or friend such as Adé visiting Yẹmi is common and expected, and it is normal for more than one person, such as both Adé and Màmá Yẹmi here, to offer advice or care at the same time. Directly refusing an elder's health advice is generally avoided; a softened acknowledgment such as mo gbọ́ ("I hear you") is more typical than an outright disagreement, even if the person plans to make their own decision afterward.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Omitting the object pronoun with dá ... lágara

    Error: *Orí ń dá lágara, leaving out the object pronoun after the verb.

    Correction: Orí ń dá mi lágara. The frame dá ... lágara requires an object pronoun between the verb and lágara to show who is affected; without it, the sentence does not state who is aching.

    Common Mistake — Treating gbọ́dọ̀ and lè as interchangeable

    Error: *O lè lọ sí ilé ìwòsàn used when the situation actually requires strong obligation, weakening the urgency of the message.

    Correction: O gbọ́dọ̀ lọ sí ilé ìwòsàn. Lè offers a gentle suggestion; gbọ́dọ̀ expresses a stronger "must." Choosing the wrong one can make urgent advice sound optional, or make a gentle suggestion sound like a command.

Amóhùnmáwòrán, Fóònù, àti Ìròyìn Ojoojúmọ́

Media, Phone, and Everyday News · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Expanding Everyday and Community Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Understand and answer a simple phone call about everyday plans.
  2. Understand short announcements about local events and services.
  3. Report what someone else said using a controlled formula for reported information.
  4. Distinguish an event that is happening now from one that has already happened when listening to news or announcements.
  5. Identify the source and topic of a short news item.
  6. Read a short announcement or news item and identify its main point.
  7. Summarize a short announcement in your own words, in speech and in writing.

Communication Scenario

Bùnmi's phone rings early one morning: it is her cousin calling about a change of plan for the weekend. Later, the radio in the kitchen announces that the water supply in the neighborhood will be interrupted for repairs, and Adé reads a notice posted near the market about a new bus schedule. By the end of the day, the family has passed each piece of news along to someone else, each time explaining what they heard and who said it.

  1. Chapter 14 developed opinions, reasons, and comparisons. This chapter turns to understanding and passing along information from phone calls, radio, and public notices.
  2. Every announcement or report in this chapter is short, clearly sourced, and supported by familiar vocabulary and grammar.
  3. Reporting what someone said is introduced only as a controlled formula; full reported-speech clause analysis remains deferred to a later volume.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation — Listening to phone and media speech

Phone calls and radio announcements often move faster than classroom dialogue and may include brief static, overlap, or clipped openings and closings. Two points deserve attention when listening:

  • Tone must still be tracked even when the speech is fast; a rising or falling pitch on a short phrase such as ó ní can be the only signal that a report is beginning.
  • Announcements often open with a short attention-getting phrase, then slow slightly for the main point, and speed up again for closing details; listen for this change of pace as a guide to what matters most.

Practice listening to the Chapter 15 phone dialogue and announcement first at a slow, exaggerated pace, then at a natural conversational pace.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter introduces controlled formulas for reporting information and its source, and develops the progressive/perfective contrast so learners can tell an ongoing situation from a completed one in announcements and news.

Reporting what someone said with ó ní

Building Block
PERSON + ó ní + REPORTED INFORMATION
Person + he/she said + reported information, formulaic reporting frame
Ẹ̀gbọ́n mi ó ní òun kò lè wá mọ́.
My older sibling said that they can no longer come.

Ó ní is used here as a fixed frame for reporting what someone said on the phone or in person. Learners are not expected to analyze the internal clause structure after ní; independent complement-clause production remains deferred to a later volume.

  • Bùnmi pe mi lórí fóònù, ó ní a ó pàdé ní ọjà ní ọ̀sán. Bùnmi called me on the phone; she said we will meet at the market in the afternoon. pe...lórí fóònù=called...on the phone ó ní=she said
  • Bàbá ní kí a máa dúró de ìpè rẹ̀. Father said we should wait for his call. ní kí a máa dúró de=said that we should wait for

Public announcements with wọ́n kéde pé and source with gẹ́gẹ́ bí ... ṣe sọ

Building Block
Wọ́n kéde pé + ANNOUNCEMENT / gẹ́gẹ́ bí ORÍSUN ṣe sọ, + ÌRÒYÌN
They announced that + announcement / according to what source said, + report, formulaic announcement and source-attribution frames
Wọ́n kéde pé kò ní sí omi ní àdúgbò yìí lọ́la.
They announced that there will be no water in this neighborhood tomorrow.

Wọ́n kéde pé introduces a public announcement without naming a single speaker. Gẹ́gẹ́ bí ... ṣe sọ names the source of a report and is placed before the reported information.

  • Gẹ́gẹ́ bí rédíò ṣe sọ, ọkọ̀ akérò yóò máa gòkè ní àkókò tuntun láti ọ̀sẹ̀ ọdẹ. According to what the radio said, the bus will run on a new schedule from next week. gẹ́gẹ́ bí...ṣe sọ=according to what...said
  • Wọ́n kéde pé iṣẹ́ àṣesílẹ̀ ń lọ lọ́wọ́ ní òpópónà wa, nítorí náà kí a máa ṣọ́ra. They announced that repair work is going on in our street, so we should be careful. wọ́n kéde pé=they announced that ń lọ lọ́wọ́=is going on

Progressive/perfective contrast in announcements

Building Block
SUBJECT + ń + VERB (ongoing) vs. SUBJECT + ti + VERB (completed)
Ongoing marker ń versus completed/resultative marker ti, contrast developed from Chapters 3 and 5
Iṣẹ́ náà ń lọ lọ́wọ́. / Iṣẹ́ náà ti parí.
The work is going on. / The work has finished.

This chapter develops the ń and ti contrast specifically for interpreting announcements: whether a repair, an event, or a service change is still happening or has already happened often decides what a listener should do next.

  • Wọ́n ti tún ojú títì ṣe, nítorí náà ọkọ̀ akérò lè gba ibẹ̀ kọjá báyìí. They have already repaired the road, so buses can now pass through there. ti tún...ṣe=have already repaired
  • Wọ́n ń tún ojú títì ṣe ní bẹ́ẹ̀ẹ̀ni, nítorí náà a gbọ́dọ̀ lọ ọ̀nà mìíràn. They are still repairing the road right now, so we must go another way. ń tún...ṣe=are still repairing

Reported speech beyond these formulas, and negative or future reporting frames, are not required until a later volume activates them.

Model Dialogue

Ipò: Fóònù ń dún ní ilé Bùnmi ní òwúrọ̀; Adé dáhùn.
Setting: The phone is ringing at Bùnmi's house in the morning; Adé answers.

ADÉ Hàlò, ta ni yìí? Hello, who is this?
ẸGBỌ́N BÙNMI Èmi ni ẹ̀gbọ́n Bùnmi. Ṣé Bùnmi wà níbẹ̀? It's Bùnmi's older sibling. Is Bùnmi there?
ADÉ Kò sí níbí báyìí. Kí ni mo lè sọ fún un? She is not here right now. What can I tell her?
ẸGBỌ́N BÙNMI Ó ní òun kò lè wá sí ọjà mọ́ ní ọ̀sán yìí, nítorí iṣẹ́ àṣesílẹ̀ ń lọ lọ́wọ́ ní òpópónà rẹ̀. Tell her they can no longer come to the market this afternoon, because repair work is going on in their street.
ADÉ Ó dára, màá sọ fún un. Ṣé wọ́n ti mọ ìgbà tí iṣẹ́ náà á parí? Good, I will tell her. Do they know when the work will finish?
ẸGBỌ́N BÙNMI Gẹ́gẹ́ bí wọ́n ṣe kéde, á parí ní ọjọ́ Jimọ́. According to what they announced, it will finish on Friday.
ADÉ Ẹ ṣé fún ìròyìn náà. Màá sọ fún Bùnmi kété tí ó bá dé. Thank you for the information. I will tell Bùnmi as soon as she arrives.

Speaking Practice

Pẹ̀lú alábàáṣepọ̀ — With a partner

One partner plays a caller who leaves a short message with news for a third person, using ó ní or wọ́n kéde pé. The other partner takes the message, then reports it back in their own words as if telling the third person, naming the source with gẹ́gẹ́ bí ... ṣe sọ if known. Switch roles with a new piece of news.

Fún àwọn tí ń kọ́ nìkọ̀ọ̀kan — Solo alternative

Record yourself giving a short phone message that reports a real or invented piece of local news. State whether the situation is still ongoing or already completed, then perform the message again as if reporting it to someone else who missed the call.

Reading Practice

Àpẹẹrẹ — Ìkéde Nípa Omi àti Ọkọ̀

Wọ́n kéde lórí rédíò ní òwúrọ̀ yìí pé kò ní sí omi ní àwọn àdúgbò kan ní ìlú wa láti òwúrọ̀ di alẹ́, nítorí iṣẹ́ àṣesílẹ̀ ń lọ lọ́wọ́ ní ibùdó omi ńlá. Gẹ́gẹ́ bí wọ́n ṣe sọ, iṣẹ́ náà á parí kí alẹ́ tó lé, kí omi sì padà wá bí ó ti ṣe rí tẹ́lẹ̀. Ní àkókò kan náà, wọ́n tún kéde pé àkókò ọkọ̀ akérò ti yí padà: ọkọ̀ yóò máa gòkè ní gbogbo ìṣẹ́jú ọgbọ̀n dípò wákàtí kan, láti òní lọ. Àwọn tó ń lọ síbi iṣẹ́ ni a gbà kí wọ́n kíyè sí àwọn ìyípadà méjèèjì yìí.

Gloss: They announced on the radio this morning that there will be no water in certain neighborhoods of our town from morning until evening, because repair work is going on at the large water station. According to what they said, the work will finish before evening falls, and the water will return as it was before. At the same time, they also announced that the bus schedule has changed: the bus will now run every thirty minutes instead of every hour, starting today. Those going to work are advised to pay attention to both of these changes.

1. What is the source of the two announcements in this passage?

2. Is the water interruption already finished or still ongoing at the time of the announcement? How do you know?

3. What has changed about the bus schedule?

Writing Workshop

Choose one real or invented piece of local news: a service interruption, a schedule change, a community event, or a phone message passed along from someone else.

  • Identify the source of your news at the start of your paragraph, using gẹ́gẹ́ bí ... ṣe sọ or wọ́n kéde pé.
  • State the main point of the news clearly in one or two sentences.
  • Include at least one sentence that makes clear whether the situation is ongoing (ń) or completed (ti).
  • If your news includes something someone said directly to you or to someone else, report it using ó ní.
  • Add one sentence advising the reader what to do in response to the news.
  • Read your paragraph aloud and apply the Chapter 10 Tone and Orthography Checkpoint before finishing.

    • Iṣẹ́ rẹ — Your task: Write a short news summary of 60–80 words following the steps above.

    • Every ẹ, ọ, and ṣ is correctly dot-underlined.
    • Every required high and low tone is marked, including in the reporting and announcement frames.
    • The paragraph names a source and states a clear main point.
    • The paragraph distinguishes an ongoing situation from a completed one using ń or ti.
    • The paragraph includes practical advice for the reader.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Passing along news responsibly

    In many Yorùbá-speaking communities, radio remains a widely trusted source for local announcements such as service interruptions, transport changes, and community events, and neighbors often relay this information to one another by phone or in person rather than assuming everyone heard the original broadcast. When passing along news, it is common to name the source clearly, using a phrase such as gẹ́gẹ́ bí rédíò ṣe sọ ("according to what the radio said"), so that the listener knows where the information came from and can judge how reliable or current it is.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Confusing ń and ti when reporting news

    Error: *Iṣẹ́ náà ti lọ lọ́wọ́. using ti to describe work that is still happening.

    Correction: Iṣẹ́ náà ń lọ lọ́wọ́. Use ń for a situation that is still ongoing, and reserve ti for a situation that has already been completed, such as iṣẹ́ náà ti parí.

    Common Mistake — Reporting news without naming a source

    Error: *Kò ní sí omi lọ́la. stated as if it were the speaker's own claim, with no indication of where the information came from.

    Correction: Wọ́n kéde pé kò ní sí omi lọ́la. This chapter expects announcements and reports to be attributed to a source, using wọ́n kéde pé or gẹ́gẹ́ bí ... ṣe sọ, whenever the task calls for reporting information rather than a personal opinion.

Ní Ọjà — Iye àti Owó

At the Market — Quantities and Prices · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Expanding Everyday and Community Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Recall and apply the location question níbo, formulaic direction phrases, and all Chapter 1–3 and Volume I systems.
  2. Use the plural marker àwọn to talk about more than one person or thing.
  3. Count and ask about quantities using numerals with everyday market goods.
  4. Ask and state a price using Élòó ni...? and simple number-plus-owó phrases.
  5. Use basic quantifiers such as díẹ̀ and púpọ̀ to describe amounts.
  6. Make a polite request for a quantity of something, and politely decline or state a preference.
  7. Understand a short market exchange at natural pace well enough to identify the item, quantity, and price.
  8. Write a short shopping note listing items, quantities, and prices.

Communication Scenario

Following the directions they practiced in Chapter 3, Adé and Yẹmi arrive at the market. Now Adé must do more than find the right stall — he must ask how much things cost, decide how many he needs, and politely negotiate small quantities, all while continuing to greet sellers respectfully and use the request patterns from Chapter 2.

  1. Every task in this chapter reuses Chapter 3's níbo question and formulaic direction phrases, Chapter 2's object pronouns, request frame, and habitual particle máa ń, and all systems consolidated in Volume I.
  2. Three new systems are introduced for the first time: the plural marker àwọn, numerals with quantity/measure phrases, and the price question Élòó ni...? together with basic quantifiers díẹ̀ and púpọ̀.
  3. Bargaining remains simple and polite in this chapter. Extended negotiation language and comparison structures (dára ju... lọ) are reserved for later chapters and are not required here.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Numbers and nasal vowels in quantities

Several numerals and quantity words contain nasal vowels and syllabic nasals. Keep the nasal vowel in mẹ́rin and márùn-ún distinct from an oral vowel followed by a plain n, and keep each syllable's tone clear even when counting quickly.

  • mẹ́ta — mid, high
  • mẹ́rin — mid, high, nasal low-mid
  • élòó — high, low, mid
  • púpọ̀ — high, low

This is a review application of the Volume I skills "nasal vowels" and "tone in connected speech," applied here to numerals and quantity phrases.

Grammar Focus

This chapter opens three systems: the plural marker àwọn, numerals used with quantity/measure phrases, and the price question Élòó ni...? together with the quantifiers díẹ̀ and púpọ̀. All three build on the noun phrases and requests consolidated in Volume I and Chapters 1–3, without introducing comparison structures or extended bargaining language, which later chapters schedule.

New Pattern 1 — Plural marker àwọn
Àwọn + Noun
Àwọn is placed directly before a noun to mark it as plural. It does not change the noun's spelling and is not attached to the noun as an ending. Learners apply it to nouns already known from Volume I and this chapter's vocabulary.
Àwọn ọ̀gẹ̀dẹ̀ yìí a dùn.
These bananas are sweet.
New Pattern 2 — Numerals with quantity phrases
Mo fẹ́ + Numeral + Noun
Numerals combine directly with a noun to state a wanted quantity. Learners produce numerals one through five with familiar market nouns; larger numbers and complex measure-word systems are reserved for later volumes.
Mo fẹ́ ọ̀gẹ̀dẹ̀ mẹ́ta.
I want three bananas.
New Pattern 3 — Asking and stating price with élòó
Élòó ni + Noun? · Ó jẹ́ + Number + owó.
Uses the question word élòó ("how much") together with the identification word ni to ask the price of an item, and the frame Ó jẹ́...owó to state it. Quantifiers díẹ̀ and púpọ̀ may be added to describe amount rather than exact number.
Élòó ni ọ̀gẹ̀dẹ̀ yìí? — Ó jẹ́ ọgọ́rùn-ún owó.
How much are these bananas? — It is one hundred naira.
  • Ẹ jọ̀wọ́, ṣé ẹ ní àwọn ọ̀gẹ̀dẹ̀ díẹ̀? Please, do you have a few bananas? àwọn + N + díẹ̀ — plural noun with small quantity
  • Kò wọ́n; owó rẹ̀ kò pọ̀ jù. It's not expensive; its price is not too much. kò + Adj — negation with a stative description, recycled from Volume I

Model Dialogue

Ipò: Adé àti Yẹmi ti dé ọjà; wọ́n dúró ní iwájú ẹni tí ń ta ọ̀gẹ̀dẹ̀ àti búrẹ́dì.
Setting: Adé and Yẹmi have arrived at the market; they stop in front of a seller of bananas and bread.

YẸMI Ẹ jọ̀wọ́, élòó ni àwọn ọ̀gẹ̀dẹ̀ yìí? Please, how much are these bananas?
ẸNI TÍ Ń TA Ó jẹ́ ọgọ́rùn-ún owó fún márùn-ún. It is one hundred naira for five.
ADÉ Mo fẹ́ ọ̀gẹ̀dẹ̀ mẹ́ta àti búrẹ́dì díẹ̀, ẹ jọ̀wọ́. I want three bananas and a little bread, please.
ẸNI TÍ Ń TA Ó dára. Kò wọ́n; owó rẹ̀ kò pọ̀ jù. Good. It's not expensive; its price is not too much.
Ẹni tí ń ta mú búrẹ́dì àti ọ̀gẹ̀dẹ̀ wá fún wọn.
YẸMI Ẹ ṣé púpọ̀. Ẹ jọ̀wọ́, ṣé ẹ ní búrẹ́dì díẹ̀ síi? Thank you very much. Please, do you have a little more bread?
ẸNI TÍ Ń TA Bẹ́ẹ̀ni, mo ní díẹ̀ síi. Yes, I have a little more.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Politeness in market exchanges

    In many Yorùbá markets, asking a price and requesting a quantity are commonly softened with ẹ jọ̀wọ́ ("please") and closed with thanks such as ẹ ṣé or ẹ ṣé púpọ̀. Gentle negotiation over price is often expected and is not usually seen as rude, but it is typically conducted with courtesy rather than insistence, especially between a buyer and seller who do not yet know each other.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Placing àwọn after the noun

    Error: *ọ̀gẹ̀dẹ̀ àwọn, placing the plural marker after the noun.

    Correction: àwọn ọ̀gẹ̀dẹ̀. The plural marker àwọn always comes directly before the noun it pluralizes.

    Common Mistake — Confusing élòó with níbo

    Error: Using níbo ni to ask a price, e.g. *Níbo ni ọ̀gẹ̀dẹ̀ yìí? when a price is intended.

    Correction: Use Élòó ni ọ̀gẹ̀dẹ̀ yìí? for price. Níbo asks only about location, as reviewed from Chapter 3.

Ìṣọ̀kan Àárín Ìdìpọ̀ — Àwọn Iṣẹ́ Àwùjọ

Mid-Volume Integration: Community Tasks · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Expanding Everyday and Community Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Combine the family, routine, market, direction, hospitality, health, work, and event systems from Chapters 1–9 and Volume I in a single integrated task sequence.
  2. Introduce yourself and describe extended family and community relationships with respect language.
  3. Describe a habitual routine and negotiate quantities and prices at a market.
  4. Give and follow multi-step directions using motion serial verbs.
  5. Host or accept hospitality, describe a health concern, and give simple advice.
  6. State a work or study plan and extend, accept, or courteously decline an invitation.
  7. Maintain full tone marks and dot-under orthography across every task in this chapter.
  8. Complete a role-play and a written task sequence that together serve as diagnostic evidence for the second half of Volume II.

Communication Scenario

Adé is hosting a visiting cousin, Bùnmi, for a week. Over the course of a single busy day, Adé and Bùnmi move through nearly every kind of everyday communication this volume has built so far: greeting family, describing the household routine, going to the market, giving directions to a friend's house, offering hospitality, checking on a relative's health, discussing Adé's work schedule, and finally planning a small gathering for the weekend. No new grammar appears in this chapter. Instead, the chapter asks you to move fluidly between the systems introduced in Chapters 1 through 9, choosing the right particle, pronoun, serial verb, and register for each moment as it happens.

  1. This chapter introduces no new grammar, vocabulary domain, or orthographic burden. Every task below draws only on Volume I and Chapters 1–9.
  2. The chapter functions as a checkpoint: it tells you and your instructor which Chapters 1–9 systems are solid and which need review before Chapter 11 begins developed narration.
  3. Because this is an integration chapter, tasks are organized by communicative situation rather than by a single new pattern, and each task names which chapter's system it draws on.

Àyẹ̀wò Gírámà — Mid-Volume Systems Checkpoint

Grammar and Systems Checkpoint

No new grammar is introduced in this chapter. The four cards below summarize the systems Chapters 1–9 have made consolidated or developed, so you can check your own control of each before the integration tasks.

Particles: ń, ti, yóò

  • ń — ongoing or habitual (Ch. 3)
  • ti — completed or already arranged (Ch. 5, Ch. 9)
  • yóò — future (Ch. 8)

Negation and modality

  • kò — main-clause negation (Ch. 1–3)
  • lè — ability or possibility (Ch. 6–7)
  • gbọ́dọ̀ — obligation (Ch. 7)

Serial verbs and noun phrases

  • Motion serials with lọ, wá, mú, gbé (Ch. 5)
  • Plural àwọn, numerals, quantifiers (Ch. 4)
  • Possession and demonstratives (Ch. 2, Ch. 12 preview)

Pronouns and courtesy

  • Weak subject/object pronouns (Ch. 2–3)
  • Strong pronouns for emphasis (Ch. 2)
  • Thank-you-plus-ṣùgbọ́n refusal frame (Ch. 9)

If any card feels shaky, review the named chapter before attempting the corresponding task below.

Tone and Orthography Checkpoint

Checkpoint — Self-correction pass

Before recording or submitting any task in this chapter, read your own written Yorùbá aloud and check each of the following. This is a review application of tone and orthography skills already consolidated in Chapters 1–9; no new tone or spelling rule is introduced here.

  • Every ẹ, ọ, and ṣ is dot-underlined where required.
  • Every high and low tone is marked; unmarked syllables are genuinely mid tone.
  • Particles (ń, ti, yóò, kò) carry their correct tone marks.
  • Names and kinship terms are spelled and tone-marked as taught in Chapter 2.

Model Dialogue

Ipò: Bùnmi ń bẹ Adé wò fún ọ̀sẹ̀ kan. Ní òwúrọ̀ ọjọ́ kan, wọ́n ń sọ̀rọ̀ nípa ìdílé, ọjà, ọ̀nà sí ilé ọ̀rẹ́ kan, àti ètò ọjọ́ Sátidé.
Setting: Bùnmi is visiting Adé for a week. One morning, they talk about family, the market, the way to a friend's house, and plans for Saturday.

ADÉ Ẹ kú àárọ̀, Bùnmi. Ṣé o sùn dáadáa? Good morning, Bùnmi. Did you sleep well?
BÙNMI Ẹ ṣé, mo sùn dáadáa. Báwo ni ìdílé rẹ? Thank you, I slept well. How is your family?
ADÉ Wọ́n wà dáadáa. Ẹ̀gbọ́n mi ń ṣe iṣẹ́ ilé báyìí; a sì ń lọ sí ọjà lẹ́yìn náà láti ra oúnjẹ díẹ̀. They are well. My older sibling is doing housework now; and we are going to the market afterward to buy a little food.
BÙNMI Ó dára. Lẹ́yìn ọjà, mo fẹ́ lọ wo ọ̀rẹ́ mi, Tọ́pẹ́. Ǹjẹ́ o mọ ọ̀nà sí ilé rẹ̀? Good. After the market, I want to go see my friend, Tọ́pẹ́. Do you know the way to her house?
ADÉ Bẹ́ẹ̀ni. Lọ tààrà, kí o sì yíjú sí ọwọ́ ọ̀tún ní ilé ìtajà náà; ilé rẹ̀ wà nítòsí. Yes. Go straight, and turn right at the shop; her house is nearby.
BÙNMI Ẹ ṣé púpọ̀. Mo gbọ́ pé o ní ìpàdé iṣẹ́ ní ọjọ́ Jimọ́; ṣé gbogbo nǹkan ń lọ dáadáa? Thank you very much. I heard you have a work meeting on Friday; is everything going well?
ADÉ Bẹ́ẹ̀ni, mo ti ṣètò ohun gbogbo. Lẹ́yìn ìpàdé náà, mo fẹ́ pè ẹ́ àti Tọ́pẹ́ sí àpèjọ kékeré kan ní ilé mi ní ọjọ́ Sátidé. Yes, I have already arranged everything. After the meeting, I want to invite you and Tọ́pẹ́ to a small gathering at my house on Saturday.
BÙNMI Ó dùn mí gan-an! Èmi àti Tọ́pẹ́ máa wá. That delights me greatly! Tọ́pẹ́ and I will come.

  4. Give two-step directions using a motion serial verb with lọ or wá (Ch. 5).
  5. Offer a guest something to eat or drink and accept or decline the offer politely (Ch. 6).
  6. Describe a minor health concern and give one piece of advice with gbọ́dọ̀ or lè (Ch. 7).
  7. State one work or study plan using yóò or gbèrò láti (Ch. 8).
  8. Invite someone to an event and either accept or courteously decline (Ch. 9).

Adé ní ìpàdé iṣẹ́ ní ọjọ́ Jimọ́, ṣùgbọ́n ó ti ṣètò ohun gbogbo tẹ́lẹ̀. Lẹ́yìn ìpàdé náà, ó fẹ́ pe Bùnmi àti Tọ́pẹ́ sí àpèjọ kékeré kan ní ilé rẹ̀ ní ọjọ́ Sátidé. Àwọn méjèèjì gbà láti wá.

Both of them agree to come.

1. What is Adé's sibling doing, and what are Adé and Bùnmi planning to do afterward?

2. What directions does Adé give Bùnmi, and what has Adé already arranged for Friday?

  • Choose a day involving a family member, a market errand, a short trip somewhere, a moment of hospitality, a health mention, a work or school plan, and an event invitation.
  • Write one sentence for each of the eight domains practiced in this chapter's Guided Practice, in any natural order.
  • Combine your eight sentences into a single connected paragraph (60–90 words), adding simple connectors where needed.
  • Read your paragraph aloud and apply the Tone and Orthography Checkpoint before finishing.

    • Every ẹ, ọ, and ṣ is correctly dot-underlined.
    • Every required high and low tone is marked, including on particles.
    • All eight Chapters 1–9 domains appear at least once.
    • No grammar or vocabulary beyond Chapters 1–9 and Volume I is used.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Hosting across a single day

    Many Yorùbá households treat hosting a visiting relative as a series of small, connected courtesies rather than one formal event: a warm greeting in the morning, an offer of food or drink, help running errands, and an invitation to meet other friends or family before the visit ends. Notice how Adé and Bùnmi's conversation moves naturally between checking on family, sharing tasks, giving directions, and planning a gathering — the same register and courtesy patterns from Chapters 2, 6, and 9 simply carry across the whole day rather than appearing only in isolated exchanges.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Flattening particle contrasts under pressure

    Error: *Mo ń ṣètò ohun gbogbo to report that Friday's meeting arrangements are already finished.

    Correction: Mo ti ṣètò ohun gbogbo. When moving quickly between many tasks in a single conversation, learners sometimes default to ń for everything. Keep the ti/ń contrast from Chapters 5 and 9 active even in fast, integrated speech.

    Common Mistake — Dropping courtesy formulas in longer exchanges

    Error: Skipping ẹ ṣé or the thank-you-plus-ṣùgbọ́n frame when a conversation covers many topics in a row.

    Correction: Courtesy formulas from Chapters 2, 6, and 9 remain required in every domain, not only in short isolated exchanges. A long, multi-topic conversation still needs greetings, thanks, and softened refusals at the appropriate moments.

    Answer Key

    Part 10 — Listening Task

    • 1: Family/routine — someone is doing housework right now.
    • 2: Market — asking the price of tomatoes.
    • 3: Directions — go straight, then turn right.
    • 4: Health/work/events — arrangements already made for a Friday work meeting.

    Reading — Comprehension Questions

    1. Adé's older sibling is doing housework (ń ṣe iṣẹ́ ilé); afterward, Adé and Bùnmi plan to go to the market to buy a little food.
    2. Adé tells Bùnmi to go straight and turn right at the shop; he has already arranged everything (ti ṣètò ohun gbogbo) for Friday's work meeting.

    Chapter Review — Apá B

    1. Accept any three accurate sentences correctly distinguishing ń, ti, and yóò, e.g.: Mo ń ṣe oúnjẹ. / Mo ti pe àwọn ọ̀rẹ́ mi. / Èmi yóò lọ sí ọjà.

    3. Accept any accurate refusal, e.g.: Ẹ ṣé fún ìpèníjà náà, ṣùgbọ́n mo ti ní iṣẹ́ mìíràn.

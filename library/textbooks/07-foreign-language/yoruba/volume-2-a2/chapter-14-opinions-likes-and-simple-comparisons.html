Èrò, Ìfẹ́ràn, àti Ìfiwéra Rírọrùn

Opinions, Likes, and Simple Comparisons · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Expanding Everyday and Community Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. State a personal opinion about entertainment, shopping, or everyday decisions.
  2. Give a reason for an opinion using a controlled reason connector.
  3. Compare two options using the comparison frame dára ju... lọ.
  4. Ask another person for their opinion and respond to disagreement politely.
  5. Read a short passage comparing two options and identify the writer's preference and reason.
  6. Write a short paragraph that states and supports a preference between two things.
  7. Distinguish stating a fact from stating an opinion in Yorùbá.

Communication Scenario

After a busy week of office and clinic visits, Adé's family takes a lighter weekend. Bùnmi and Adé argue cheerfully about which market stall sells the best sùyà, whether the new radio drama is better than the old one, and which of two shirts their cousin should buy for a party. Their grandmother, listening nearby, offers her own opinion — and a reason for it — about almost everything.

  1. Chapters 1–13 built description, narration, and formal service language. This chapter turns that language toward everyday opinions and choices.
  2. Every opinion in this chapter is expected to be supported by at least one reason, not stated alone.
  3. Comparisons in this chapter involve exactly two options at a time; comparing three or more options is left to a later volume.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation — Contrastive intonation in opinions and comparisons

When Yorùbá speakers compare two things or disagree politely, the two contrasted items usually receive slightly more prominence in the phrase, but this prominence must never override the underlying tone of the words. Two patterns deserve attention:

  • dára ju... lọ — the compared item after ju often carries a small rise in pitch to mark contrast, while the low tone on jù-related syllables must remain audible.
  • mo rò pé... ṣùgbọ́n... — when a speaker states an opinion and then offers a contrasting view, a brief pause before ṣùgbọ́n ("but") signals the contrast; the pause must not be filled with flattened, toneless speech.

Practice reading the Chapter 14 comparison sentences aloud, first slowly with exaggerated contrast, then at a natural conversational pace.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter introduces a controlled reason connector and a comparison frame, and develops the opinion-stating pattern mo rò pé into fuller, reason-supported statements.

Stating an opinion with mo rò pé

Building Block
Mo rò pé + CLAUSE
I think that + clause, formulaic opinion-introducing frame
Mo rò pé fíìmù yìí dára.
I think that this film is good.

Mo rò pé is used here as a fixed opening frame for opinions. Learners are not expected to analyze the internal structure of the complement clause after pé; independent complement-clause production remains deferred to a later volume.

  • Mo rò pé ẹ̀wù pupa yìí dára ju èyí aláwọ̀ dúdú lọ. I think that this red shirt is better than that black one. mo rò pé=I think that ẹ̀wù pupa yìí=this red shirt
  • Ìyá àgbà sọ pé eré orí rédíò tuntun kò dára tó èyí àtijọ́. Grandmother says that the new radio drama is not as good as the old one. sọ pé=says that kò dára tó=is not as good as

Giving a reason with nítorí

Building Block
OPINION + nítorí + REASON
Opinion clause + because + reason clause, controlled reason connector
Mo fẹ́ràn ọjà yìí nítorí owó rẹ̀ kò wọ́n.
I like this market because its prices are not expensive.

Nítorí is introduced this chapter as a controlled connector for giving one clear reason after an opinion. Learners should place the opinion first and the reason second, and should keep the reason clause short and complete.

  • Mo fẹ́ràn sùyà níbí ju ibòmíràn lọ nítorí ó gbóná dáadáa. I like the sùyà here more than anywhere else because it is properly spicy. mo fẹ́ràn...ju...lọ=I like...more than... nítorí=because
  • Adé kò fẹ́ràn fíìmù náà nítorí ó gùn jù. Adé does not like the film because it is too long. kò fẹ́ràn=does not like nítorí ó gùn jù=because it is too long

Comparing two options with dára ju... lọ

Building Block
X dára ju Y lọ
X is good beyond Y go = X is better than Y, two-item comparison frame
Ẹ̀wù aláwọ̀ búlúù yìí dára ju aláwọ̀ pupa náà lọ.
This blue shirt is better than the red one.

The comparison frame dára ju... lọ is controlled and productive in this chapter, but only for comparing exactly two options at a time. The adjective before ju may change (for example wọ́n "expensive", rọrùn "easy"), but the frame itself stays fixed.

  • Ṣóòbù yìí wọ́n ju èyí tí a lọ sí lánàá lọ. This shop is more expensive than the one we went to yesterday. wọ́n ju...lọ=is more expensive than
  • Kíkọ ní ilé rọrùn ju kíkọ ní kíláàsì lọ, ní èrò mi. Studying at home is easier than studying in class, in my opinion. rọrùn ju...lọ=is easier than ní èrò mi=in my opinion

Comparisons involving three or more items, or comparisons of equality beyond simple kò...tó patterns, are not required until a later volume activates them.

Model Dialogue

Ipò: Ní ilé, Bùnmi àti Adé ń wo àwọn ẹ̀wù méjì tí ẹ̀gbọ́n wọn máa rà fún àríyá kan.
Setting: At home, Bùnmi and Adé are looking at two shirts their older sibling might buy for a party.

BÙNMI Èwo lára àwọn ẹ̀wù méjèèjì lo fẹ́ràn jù? Which of the two shirts do you like the most?
ADÉ Mo rò pé aláwọ̀ búlúù dára ju aláwọ̀ pupa náà lọ. I think the blue one is better than the red one.
BÙNMI Kí ló dé? Why?
ADÉ Nítorí aláwọ̀ búlúù kò wọ́n ju èkejì lọ, ó sì bá àwọn bàtà rẹ̀ mu. Because the blue one is not more expensive than the other, and it matches his shoes.
BÙNMI Mo lóye, ṣùgbọ́n mo fẹ́ràn aláwọ̀ pupa ju lọ nítorí ó dán mọ́rán fún àríyá. I understand, but I like the red one more because it is sharper for a party.
ADÉ Ó yẹ kí a béèrè lọ́wọ́ ẹ̀gbọ́n wa, nítorí tirẹ̀ ni. We should ask our older sibling, because it is theirs to decide.
BÙNMI Ó dára. Ẹ jọ̀ọ́, jẹ́ ká béèrè lọ́wọ́ rẹ̀ pọ̀. Good. Please, let's ask them together.

Speaking Practice

Pẹ̀lú alábàáṣepọ̀ — With a partner

One partner names two options (two films, two shops, two dishes, or two shirts). The other states a preference with mo rò pé or mo fẹ́ràn... ju... lọ, gives a reason with nítorí, and compares the two options with dára ju... lọ. Switch roles and choose two new options.

Fún àwọn tí ń kọ́ nìkọ̀ọ̀kan — Solo alternative

Record yourself comparing two real items you know well (two restaurants, two songs, two brands). State your opinion, give a reason, and use the comparison frame at least once. Then perform the exchange again, arguing the opposite opinion.

Reading Practice

Àpẹẹrẹ — Èrò Nípa Ọjà Méjì

Mo máa ń lọ sí ọjà méjì láti rà oúnjẹ: ọjà tí ó wà nítòsí ilé wa, àti ọjà ńlá tí ó jìnnà sí i. Mo rò pé ọjà tí ó wà nítòsí ilé wa dára ju ọjà ńlá náà lọ, nítorí kò gba àkókò púpọ̀ láti dé ibẹ̀, owó rẹ̀ kò sì wọ́n jù. Ṣùgbọ́n ìyá mi fẹ́ràn ọjà ńlá ju èkejì lọ, nítorí a lè rí onírúurú ẹrù níbẹ̀ tí a kò lè rí ní ọjà kékeré. Ẹnì kọ̀ọ̀kan ní èrò rẹ̀, ṣùgbọ́n a máa ń gbà pé àwọn ọjà méjèèjì wúlò.

Gloss: I usually go to two markets to buy food: the market near our house, and the large market that is farther away. I think the market near our house is better than the large market, because it does not take much time to get there, and its prices are not too expensive. But my mother likes the large market more than the other, because we can find various goods there that we cannot find in the small market. Each person has their own opinion, but we usually agree that both markets are useful.

1. Which market does the writer prefer, and what reason does the writer give?

2. Which market does the mother prefer, and what reason does she give?

Writing Workshop

Choose two real or invented options in one category: two films, two markets, two shirts, two restaurants, or two radio programs.

  • Name the two options you are comparing at the start of your paragraph.
  • State your overall opinion using mo rò pé or mo fẹ́ràn... ju... lọ.
  • Give at least one reason for your opinion using nítorí.
  • Include at least one direct comparison sentence using dára ju... lọ or a related pattern such as wọ́n ju... lọ or rọrùn ju... lọ.
  • Acknowledge, in one sentence, that someone else might reasonably disagree, using ṣùgbọ́n.
  • Read your paragraph aloud and apply the Chapter 10 Tone and Orthography Checkpoint before finishing.

    • Iṣẹ́ rẹ — Your task: Write a supported preference paragraph of 60–80 words following the steps above.

    • Every ẹ, ọ, and ṣ is correctly dot-underlined.
    • Every required high and low tone is marked, including in the comparison frame.
    • The paragraph states a clear opinion, at least one reason, and at least one comparison sentence.
    • The paragraph acknowledges a possible disagreement using ṣùgbọ́n.
    • The comparison involves exactly two options, not three or more.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Disagreeing politely

    In many Yorùbá-speaking communities, disagreement, especially with someone older or more senior, is commonly softened rather than stated bluntly. A phrase such as mo lóye, ṣùgbọ́n... ("I understand, but...") acknowledges the other person's view before offering a different one. Younger speakers addressing elders often frame disagreement as a question or a gentle observation rather than a flat contradiction, even in casual conversations about everyday preferences like food, shopping, or entertainment.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Giving an opinion without a reason

    Error: *Mo rò pé fíìmù yìí dára. left as the entire response, with no supporting reason.

    Correction: Mo rò pé fíìmù yìí dára nítorí ó kúkúrú tí ó sì yára. This chapter expects opinions to be supported with a reason introduced by nítorí whenever the task calls for a supported opinion.

    Common Mistake — Dropping lọ from the comparison frame

    Error: *Ẹ̀wù búlúù dára ju pupa. with lọ omitted at the end of the comparison.

    Correction: Ẹ̀wù búlúù dára ju pupa lọ. The comparison frame dára ju... lọ requires both parts; omitting lọ leaves the comparison incomplete.

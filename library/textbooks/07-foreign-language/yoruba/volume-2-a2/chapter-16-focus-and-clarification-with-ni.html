Ìdámọ̀ àti Àlàyé pẹ̀lú Ní

Focus and Clarification with Ní · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Expanding Everyday and Community Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify a person, thing, or place clearly using ni focus.
  2. Confirm or correct someone's identity or a fact using ni.
  3. Front a subject or an object for emphasis using controlled ni focus patterns.
  4. Ask and answer basic clarification questions with ta ni, kí ni, and níbo ni.
  5. Correct a misunderstanding politely using a focused statement.
  6. Read and write short exchanges built around identification, correction, and clarification.

Communication Scenario

Adé cannot find his phone charger, and everyone in the house has a different idea about where it is and who used it last. As the family sorts through the confusion, they keep pointing to exactly who did what and exactly where things are, using short, pointed statements rather than long explanations.

  1. Chapter 15 developed reported information and the ongoing/completed contrast in news and announcements. This chapter turns to identifying, confirming, and correcting information using ni focus.
  2. Ni focus for identification and subject/object emphasis is now consolidated for independent use in familiar contexts.
  3. Clarification questions with ta ni, kí ni, and níbo ni are introduced here as controlled formulas; contrastive focus, question-focus interaction, and focus with pronouns remain formulaic and are not yet independently analyzed.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation — Tone and rhythm after fronting

When a word is fronted before ni for focus, the tone of the fronted word does not change, but the rhythm of the sentence often shifts, with a brief pause before ni and a slightly stronger stress on the fronted item. Two points deserve attention:

  • Keep the tone of the fronted word exactly as it is pronounced in its ordinary position; fronting does not flatten or alter tone.
  • Listen for the short pause and slightly heavier emphasis just before ni, which signals to the listener that identification or correction is coming.

Practice reading the Chapter 16 building-block examples aloud slowly first, then with the natural pause-and-emphasis pattern before ni.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter consolidates ni focus for identification, subject focus, and object focus for independent use in familiar contexts, and introduces clarification questions with ta ni, kí ni, and níbo ni as controlled formulas.

Identification with ni

Building Block
ITEM/PERSON + ni + IDENTIFICATION
Fronted item + focus marker ni + identifying information
Ẹ̀rọ ìdọ́gba mi ni èyí.
This is my charger.

Ni identifies exactly who or what something is. It is used to answer questions such as "who is this" or "what is that," and to state plainly that one specific person or thing, and not another, fits the description.

  • Adé ni ẹni tó mú ẹ̀rọ ìdọ́gba náà lọ́wọ́ tẹ́ràn. Adé is the one who took the charger last. Adé ni=it is Adé (identification)
  • Yàrá ìjẹun ni ibi tí mo ti rí i. The dining room is where I saw it. Yàrá ìjẹun ni=it is the dining room

Subject and object focus with ni

Building Block
SUBJECT + ni + REST OF CLAUSE (subject focus) / OBJECT + ni + SUBJECT + VERB (object focus)
Fronted subject or object emphasized with ni, without disturbing the rest of the clause
Bùnmi ni ó fọ àwo náà. / Àwo náà ni Bùnmi fọ̀.
It is Bùnmi who washed the plate. / It is the plate that Bùnmi washed.

Fronting the subject before ni emphasizes who performed the action. Fronting the object before ni emphasizes what was acted upon. Both patterns keep the rest of the clause in its ordinary word order.

  • Èmi ni mo rí ẹ̀rọ ìdọ́gba náà nísàlẹ̀ ìjókòó. I am the one who found the charger under the seat. Èmi ni=it is I (subject focus)
  • Fóònù rẹ ni ó sọnù, kì í ṣe ẹ̀rọ ìdọ́gba. It is your phone that is missing, not the charger. Fóònù rẹ ni=it is your phone (object focus, contrast)

Clarification questions: ta ni, kí ni, níbo ni

Building Block
Ta ni...? / Kí ni...? / Níbo ni...?
Who is...? / What is...? / Where is...?, controlled clarification formulas built on ni
Ta ni ó mú un lọ? Kí ni ó sọnù? Níbo ni ó wà?
Who took it? What is missing? Where is it?

These three question formulas are used to ask for identification directly. Learners are not expected to analyze how ni interacts with question words internally; independent question-focus production remains deferred to a later volume.

  • Ta ni ó kẹ́yìn lo ẹ̀rọ ìdọ́gba náà? Who used the charger last? Ta ni=who is it (clarification formula)
  • Níbo ni o fi í sí lẹ́yìn tí o parí? Where did you put it after you finished? Níbo ni=where is it (clarification formula)

Contrastive focus, focus combined with pronouns beyond simple identification, and full question-focus analysis are not required until a later volume activates them.

Model Dialogue

Ipò: Adé ń wá ẹ̀rọ ìdọ́gba fóònù rẹ̀ ní ilé, ó sì ń bi Bùnmi àti ẹ̀gbọ́n rẹ̀ léèrè.
Setting: Adé is looking for his phone charger at home and is asking Bùnmi and their older sibling.

ADÉ Ta ni ó kẹ́yìn lo ẹ̀rọ ìdọ́gba mi? Who used my charger last?
BÙNMI Kì í ṣe èmi. Ẹ̀gbọ́n wa ni ó lò ó lánàá. It's not me. It's our older sibling who used it yesterday.
ẸGBỌ́N Rárá o, kì í ṣe èmi. Fóònù tèmi ni mo dọ́gba, kì í ṣe tirẹ. Not at all, it wasn't me. It's my own phone that I charged, not yours.
ADÉ Kí ni mo rí, torí náà? Níbo ni ẹ̀rọ náà wà báyìí? So what did I see, then? Where is the charger now?
BÙNMI Yàrá ìjẹun ni ibi tí mo ti rí i, lórí tábìlì. The dining room is where I saw it, on the table.
ADÉ Bẹ́ẹ̀ ni! Ẹ ṣé, mo ti rí i báyìí. That's right! Thank you, I've found it now.

Speaking Practice

The other partner asks clarification questions using ta ni, kí ni, and níbo ni, and the first partner answers using ni to identify the person, item, or place clearly. Switch roles with a new mix-up.

Fún àwọn tí ń kọ́ nìkọ̀ọ̀kan — Solo alternative

Reading Practice

Àpẹẹrẹ — Ìsọdipúpọ̀ Nípa Ẹ̀rọ Ìdọ́gba

Ní òwúrọ̀ yìí, ìsọdipúpọ̀ kékeré kan wà ní ilé wa nípa ẹ̀rọ ìdọ́gba fóònù. Adé ní pé Bùnmi ni ẹni tó kẹ́yìn lò ó, ṣùgbọ́n Bùnmi ní pé kì í ṣe òun, ẹ̀gbọ́n wọn ni ó lò ó lánàá alẹ́. Nígbà tí wọ́n bi ẹ̀gbọ́n wọn léèrè, ó ní fóònù tirẹ̀ ni òun dọ́gba, kì í ṣe ti Adé. Níkẹyìn, Bùnmi rántí pé yàrá ìjẹun ni ibi tí òun ti rí ẹ̀rọ náà, lórí tábìlì, kété lẹ́yìn tí Adé ti lọ sí ilé ìwé ní òwúrọ̀. Nígbà tí Adé wọlé, ó lọ sí yàrá ìjẹun, ó sì rí ẹ̀rọ ìdọ́gba rẹ̀ gan-an lórí tábìlì, gẹ́gẹ́ bí Bùnmi ṣe sọ.

Gloss: This morning, there was a small mix-up at our house about the phone charger. Adé said Bùnmi was the one who used it last, but Bùnmi said it wasn't her, their older sibling used it last night. When their older sibling was asked, they said it was their own phone that they charged, not Adé's. Finally, Bùnmi remembered that the dining room was where she had seen the charger, on the table, shortly after Adé had left for school in the morning. When Adé came home, he went to the dining room and found his charger right there on the table, just as Bùnmi had said.

1. Who did Adé first think used the charger last?

2. What did the older sibling say they charged instead?

3. Where did Bùnmi say the charger could be found, and was she correct?

Writing Workshop

Choose one real or invented mix-up at home, school, or work involving a lost item, a mistaken identity, or a misplaced object.

  • Include at least one clarification question using ta ni, kí ni, or níbo ni.
  • Include at least one sentence with subject focus and one with object focus, both using ni.
  • Include one correction using rárá o or one confirmation using bẹ́ẹ̀ ni.
  • State clearly how the mix-up was resolved, identifying the person, item, or place with ni.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Correcting politely within the family

    When correcting a mistaken assumption within a Yorùbá-speaking family or community, speakers often soften a direct correction with a polite opener such as rárá o ("not at all") rather than a blunt denial, and they commonly follow the correction immediately with the accurate information rather than leaving the correction unexplained. Naming the correct person, item, or place clearly with ni is considered helpful rather than confrontational, since it resolves the confusion quickly and respectfully.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Omitting ni in identification

    Error: *Adé ẹni tó mú un lọ. stating identification without the focus marker.

    Correction: Adé ni ẹni tó mú un lọ. The focus marker ni is required to identify who, what, or where in this pattern; it must not be dropped even when the meaning seems clear from context.

    Common Mistake — Fronting without keeping ordinary word order after ni

    Error: *Àwo náà ni fọ Bùnmi. disturbing the verb-object order after fronting.

    Correction: Àwo náà ni Bùnmi fọ̀. The fronted item is followed by ni, and the rest of the clause keeps its ordinary subject-verb order; only the focused element moves to the front.

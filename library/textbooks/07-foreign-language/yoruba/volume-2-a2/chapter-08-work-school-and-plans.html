Iṣẹ́, Ilé-Ìwé, àti Ètò

Work, School, and Plans · Speak and Write Yorùbá: Expanding Everyday and Community Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Recall and apply the object pronoun frame dá ... lágara, the recommendation use of lè, and the formulaic obligation gbọ́dọ̀ from Chapter 7, and all systems consolidated in Chapters 1–6 and Volume I.
  2. Describe study and work responsibilities, skills, and daily schedules.
  3. Use the future particle yóò to talk about plans and intentions.
  4. Use fẹ́ with a following verb to express modal intention ("want to"), extending its Chapter 6 request meaning.
  5. Use a small set of formulaic complement-clause frames, such as mo fẹ́ láti and mo gbèrò láti, without analyzing their full internal grammar.
  6. Distinguish an ongoing routine marked with ń from a future plan marked with yóò.
  7. Write a short study or work plan.
  8. Maintain full tone marks, dot-under letters, and accurate tone on formal titles and school/work vocabulary.

Communication Scenario

Yẹmi has recovered from her fever and is back at school. Adé, who works part of the week and studies part of the week, stops by after his lessons to compare schedules and talk about what each of them is working toward. Yẹmi is preparing for an exam; Adé is thinking about a new skill he wants to learn for his job. As they talk, they move between what they usually do, what they are doing this week, and what they plan to do soon. This chapter gives Adé, Yẹmi, and you the controlled language to discuss study, work, skills, and plans.

  1. Every task in this chapter reuses Chapter 7's object pronoun, lè, and gbọ́dọ̀ patterns, Chapter 6's request and offer frames, and all systems consolidated in Chapters 1–5 and Volume I.
  2. The future particle yóò is developed here for the first time, alongside modal intention with fẹ́ + Verb.
  3. Complement clauses with láti after verbs such as fẹ́ and gbèrò are introduced only as formulaic frames; their fuller grammar is developed in later volumes.
  4. Only short, familiar-register comparisons between routine (ń), current activity, and plan (yóò) are required here. Detailed academic or professional discourse is reserved for later volumes.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Tone in formal titles and school/work terms

Several new words in this chapter are formal titles or compound nouns whose tone pattern must be kept exact, since a shifted tone changes or obscures the meaning. Olùkọ́ ("teacher") carries a low-low-high pattern that must not flatten to all-mid. Agbanisíṣẹ́ ("employer") is a longer compound; keep each syllable's tone distinct rather than rushing the word into a single blur. Ìdánwò ("exam") begins with a low tone on the initial vowel, which is easy to drop when speaking quickly.

  • olùkọ́ — low-low-mid-high; do not flatten to mid-mid-mid-high
  • agbanisíṣẹ́ — keep each syllable's tone distinct in the compound
  • ìdánwò — initial low tone must be held, not dropped
  • ìṣètò — three tone-bearing units, low-low-mid, before the final low

This is a review application of the Volume I skill "tone on nouns," applied here to formal titles and compound nouns.

Grammar Focus

This chapter develops the future particle yóò for plans and intentions, extends fẹ́ into modal intention, and introduces a small set of formulaic complement-clause frames with láti. Only short, familiar-register comparisons between routine, current activity, and plan are required; a full modal and complement-clause paradigm is reserved for later volumes.

New Pattern 1 — Future plans with yóò
Subject + yóò + Verb
Yóò marks a future action or plan. It contrasts with the bare verb (habitual/general) and with ń (ongoing), which were both consolidated in earlier chapters. Learners should keep the three time frames distinct: bare verb for routine, ń for now, yóò for later.
Mo yóò kẹ́kọ̀ọ́ ní alẹ́ yìí.
I will study tonight.
New Pattern 2 — Modal intention with fẹ́
Subject + fẹ́ + Verb (intention)
Fẹ́, already active since Chapter 6 for polite requests, is extended here to express what a person wants to do, not only what they want to have. This is a controlled extension of an already-consolidated form, not a new particle.
Adé fẹ́ kọ́ ìmọ̀ràn iṣẹ́ tuntun.
Adé wants to learn a new skill.
New Pattern 3 — Formulaic complement clauses with láti
Subject + fẹ́ / gbèrò + láti + Verb
The frame láti + Verb links a main verb such as fẹ́ ("want") or gbèrò ("plan, intend") to a following action. At this stage, learners should treat fẹ́ láti and gbèrò láti as fixed formulaic units meaning "want to" and "plan to," without analyzing the internal structure of the complement clause. Full complement-clause grammar is developed in Volume III.
Mo gbèrò láti kọ́ ìmọ̀ràn iṣẹ́ tuntun ní ọdún tí ń bọ̀.
I plan to learn a new skill next year.
Language Focus — Comparing routine, now, and plan

Yorùbá keeps these three time frames grammatically distinct through particle choice rather than verb endings. A bare verb states a general fact or habitual routine, as in mo máa ń kẹ́kọ̀ọ́ ní alẹ́ ("I usually study in the evening"). Ń marks what is happening right now or these days, as in mo ń múra sílẹ̀ fún ìdánwò ("I am preparing for the exam"). Yóò marks a plan or future action, as in mo yóò kẹ́kọ̀ọ́ ní alẹ́ yìí ("I will study tonight"). Learners should practice choosing the correct particle for the intended time frame rather than defaulting to one form.

  • Kí ni o ń ṣe lọ́wọ́ báyìí? What are you doing right now? ń + Verb — recycled Ch. 3 progressive, ongoing action
  • Mo ń múra sílẹ̀ fún ìdánwò mi. I am preparing for my exam. ń + múra sílẹ̀ fún — ongoing preparation
  • Mo yóò parí ìṣẹ́ yìí láìpẹ́. I will finish this work soon. yóò + Verb — future plan

Model Dialogue

Ipò: Adé wá bẹ Yẹmi wò lẹ́yìn ẹ̀kọ́; wọ́n ń sọ̀rọ̀ nípa iṣẹ́, ilé-ìwé, àti ètò wọn.
Setting: Adé visits Yẹmi after lessons; they talk about work, school, and their plans.

ADÉ Ẹ kú ọ̀sán, Yẹmi. Kí ni o ń ṣe lọ́wọ́ báyìí? Good afternoon, Yẹmi. What are you doing right now?
YẸMI Ẹ ṣé, Adé. Mo ń múra sílẹ̀ fún ìdánwò mi; yóò wáyé ní ọ̀la. Thank you, Adé. I'm preparing for my exam; it will take place tomorrow.
ADÉ Ó dára. Mo máa ń ṣiṣẹ́ ní àárọ̀, mo sì máa ń kẹ́kọ̀ọ́ ní alẹ́. Ṣùgbọ́n mo fẹ́ láti kọ́ ìmọ̀ràn iṣẹ́ tuntun láìpẹ́. That's good. I usually work in the morning, and I usually study in the evening. But I want to learn a new skill soon.
YẸMI Kí ni ìmọ̀ràn iṣẹ́ náà? What is the skill?
ADÉ Agbanisíṣẹ́ mi fẹ́ kí n kọ́ ohun tuntun. Mo gbèrò láti bẹ̀rẹ̀ ní ọ̀sẹ̀ tí ń bọ̀. My employer wants me to learn something new. I plan to start next week.
YẸMI Ó dára gan-an. Mo yóò kọ́ nǹkan mi sílẹ̀ fún ìdánwò yìí, mo sì yóò kẹ́kọ̀ọ́ ní alẹ́ yìí pẹ̀lú. That's very good. I will prepare my things for this exam, and I will study tonight too.
ADÉ Ẹ jọ̀wọ́, ẹ máa gbìyànjú. Ìdánwò náà kò ní le fún ẹ. Please, keep trying. The exam won't be hard for you.

Writing Workshop

  • Think of your usual weekly routine for work or school.
  • Write one sentence describing that routine using máa ń or a bare-verb habitual pattern.
  • Write one sentence describing what you are doing this week using ń.
  • Write one sentence about a plan for the near future using yóò, and one using fẹ́ láti or gbèrò láti.
  • Combine your sentences into a short study or work plan (40–60 words), keeping full tone marks and underdots throughout.

    • Iṣẹ́ rẹ — Your task: Write the short plan described above.

    • Every ẹ, ọ, and ṣ is correctly dot-underlined.
    • Every required high and low tone is marked, including on formal titles and compound nouns.
    • Routine, current activity, and plan are each expressed with the correct particle.
    • At least one formulaic complement clause with láti appears.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture in Context — Discussing plans and modesty

    When discussing future plans or ambitions, many Yorùbá speakers pair a stated intention with an expression acknowledging that outcomes are not fully within human control, such as bí Ọlọ́run bá fẹ́ ("God willing") or a similarly modest closing remark. This is not treated as doubt about the plan itself, but as a customary way of speaking about the future respectfully. Learners are not required to produce this expression at this stage, but should recognize it if they encounter it in conversation, since its absence can sometimes sound overconfident in certain contexts.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake — Using ń for a future plan

    Error: *Mo ń kẹ́kọ̀ọ́ ní ọ̀la, using the ongoing particle ń to describe something planned for tomorrow.

    Correction: Mo yóò kẹ́kọ̀ọ́ ní ọ̀la. Ń marks an activity happening now or these days; yóò marks a plan or future action. Mixing the two blurs the time frame the speaker intends.

    Common Mistake — Dropping láti in a complement clause

    Error: *Mo gbèrò bẹ̀rẹ̀ ní ọ̀sẹ̀ tí ń bọ̀, joining gbèrò directly to the following verb without láti.

    Correction: Mo gbèrò láti bẹ̀rẹ̀ ní ọ̀sẹ̀ tí ń bọ̀. At this stage, fẹ́ láti and gbèrò láti should be learned and used as fixed formulaic units; láti is not optional in this frame.

रिश्ते और सामाजिक भाषा

Volume II, Chapter 2: Relationships and Social Language · Speak and Write Hindi: Everyday Independence and Connected Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Describe relationships among family members, friends, classmates, coworkers, and community members in connected sentences.
  2. Refer to people across several sentences using pronouns and possessives instead of repeating names unnecessarily.
  3. Use आप and तुम with clearer pragmatic judgment, and recognize तू as a highly context-dependent form rather than a default choice.
  4. Use जी, titles, and kinship terms appropriately with people you know well and people you have just met.
  5. Describe people using expanded adjectives and short descriptive clauses.
  6. Recognize honorific agreement patterns in common spoken and written descriptions of respected people.

Communication Scenario

A week after her language-exchange conversation with Aman, Priya invites him to meet some people in her life: her older cousin Neha, her Hindi teacher Sharma ji, and her classmate Vikas. As Priya introduces each person, she must choose her words carefully — a respectful address for her teacher, an easy familiarity with her cousin, and a friendly but still polite tone with a classmate she does not know very well yet.

  1. Notice that Priya does not use the same address form for every person. Her choice depends on age, familiarity, and role.
  2. Notice how Priya refers back to people she has already introduced using वह, उसका/उसकी/उसके, and उनका/उनकी/उनके, instead of repeating each name in every sentence.
  3. Notice that no verb tense or sentence pattern here is new. What is new is precision: choosing the right pronoun, the right possessive, and the right level of respect for each relationship.

Where This Chapter Fits

Chapter 2 continues Unit I, संबंध और दिनचर्या. Chapter 1 reactivated Volume I's verb systems and introduced paragraph-level pronoun reference for the first time, using वह and उसका/उसकी/उसके in a single controlled example about a Hindi teacher. Chapter 2 takes that single example and develops it into a full system: expanded pronoun reference, honorific plural agreement, and a clearer pragmatic map of आप, तुम, and तू.

Coming Into This Chapter

You can describe your family and background using possessives and basic adjective agreement, you default to आप for safe respectful address, and you can refer back to one person in a sentence using वह and a simple possessive, as practiced briefly in Chapter 1.

New in This Chapter

Expanded pronoun and possessive reference across several people in one description (वह/वे/ये, उन्हें, उनका/उनकी/उनके, इसका/उसका); honorific plural agreement for respected people (वे आते हैं rather than वह आता है); clearer pragmatic boundaries among आप, तुम, and तू; and a wider set of titles, kinship terms, and personality adjectives.

Looking Forward

Chapter 3, घर, मोहल्ला, और समुदाय, shifts from describing people to describing places, expanding oblique forms and introducing a wider set of locative postpositions such as के सामने, के पीछे, and के बीच.

Devanagari and Pronunciation Focus

This chapter introduces no new letters or matras. It develops listening awareness of honorific verb agreement and continues schwa deletion practice with names, titles, and kinship terms.

Focus — Listening for honorific plural agreement

When Hindi speakers describe someone they address respectfully with आप, the verb often takes plural agreement even though only one person is meant: वे आते हैं (he/she comes, respectful) rather than वह आता है. Listen for हैं, आते हैं, करते हैं, and रहते हैं in the model dialogue below and notice which sentences describe Sharma ji this way.

Focus — Schwa deletion in names, titles, and kinship terms

Read रिश्तेदार, पड़ोसी, चचेरा भाई, and मिलनसार as natural spoken forms rather than sounding out every written vowel. Notice that रिश्तेदार is pronounced roughly rishtedaar, with the inherent vowel after त dropped.

Focus — Respectful intonation versus familiar intonation

Reread the model dialogue aloud, using a slightly more formal, measured tone when Priya speaks to or about Sharma ji, and a warmer, more relaxed tone when she speaks to Neha or Vikas. This is the same natural rhythm and phrase-grouping practiced since Volume I, now applied deliberately to signal respect.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter develops two closely related systems: expanded pronoun and possessive reference for describing several people, and a clearer pragmatic map of address forms.

2.5 Language Focus — Expanded pronoun reference for several people

In Chapter 1, you referred back to one person using वह and उसका/उसकी/उसके. When a description involves more than one person, Hindi uses वे or ये for "they," उन्हें for "them" as an object, and उनका/उनकी/उनके for "their." The same forms — वे, उन्हें, उनका/उनकी/उनके — are also used respectfully for a single person addressed with आप.

Language Building Block — Referring to several people, and to one respected person
मेरे दो सहपाठी हैं। वे दोनों बहुत मेहनती हैं, और मैं उनके साथ अक्सर पढ़ती हूँ। मेरी हिंदी अध्यापिका शर्मा जी बहुत धैर्यवान हैं; वे हर हफ़्ते हमें नई कहानियाँ पढ़ाती हैं।
I have two classmates. They are both very hardworking, and I often study with them. My Hindi teacher, Sharma ji, is very patient; she teaches us new stories every week.

Notice that इसका/उसका distinguish nearer and farther reference the same way यह/वह do: यह मेरी सहेली है; इसका नाम नेहा है (this is my close friend; her name is Neha) versus वह मेरा सहपाठी है; उसका नाम विकास है (that is my classmate; his name is Vikas).

2.6 Language Focus — Mapping आप, तुम, and तू

आप remains your safe default with anyone older, anyone in authority, anyone you have just met, and anyone in a formal or professional context. तुम is common among friends, classmates, younger relatives, and peers once a relationship is established. तू is intimate, and its appropriateness depends heavily on relationship, age, and region; it can also sound insulting or overly familiar if used with the wrong person. This chapter asks you only to recognize तू and to continue using आप and तुम productively.

Language Building Block — Choosing the right address form
शर्मा जी, आप कैसी हैं? — नेहा, तुम कैसी हो? — विकास, तुम कहाँ रहते हो?
Sharma ji, how are you? (respectful) — Neha, how are you? (familiar, to a close cousin) — Vikas, where do you live? (familiar, to a classmate)

2.7 Language Focus — जी and titles in context

जी attaches to a surname, first name, or kinship term to mark respect: शर्मा जी, पिताजी. It is not automatic with every noun, and it is not usually added to a first name used with a close friend or younger relative. सर and मैडम are common alternatives in school, university, and workplace settings.

Boundary

This chapter does not introduce new postposition functions, new oblique patterns, or advanced honorific verb morphology. It also does not require productive use of तू or full command of every kinship term. Broader oblique forms and locative postpositions begin developing in Chapter 3.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Priya introduces Aman to her cousin Neha, her Hindi teacher Sharma ji, and her classmate Vikas at a small gathering.

प्रिया अमन, यह मेरी चचेरी बहन नेहा है। यह मुझसे दो साल बड़ी है, और हम बहुत क़रीब हैं। Aman, yah meree chacheree bahan Nehaa hai. Yah mujhase do saal baड़ee hai, aur ham bahut qareeb hain. Aman, this is my cousin Neha. She is two years older than me, and we are very close.
अमन नमस्ते, नेहा जी! प्रिया अक्सर आपके बारे में बात करती है। Namaste, Nehaa jee! Priyaa aksar aapake baare meँ baat karatee hai. Hello, Neha ji! Priya often talks about you.
नेहा अरे, आप जी मत बोलिए! बस नेहा बोलो, हम दोनों तो लगभग हमउम्र हैं। Are, aap 'jee' mat boliye! Bas Nehaa bolo, ham donoँ to lagabhag hamumr hain. Oh, don't say "ji"! Just call me Neha — the two of us are almost the same age anyway.
प्रिया और यह मेरी हिंदी अध्यापिका, शर्मा जी हैं। वे बहुत धैर्यवान हैं और हर हफ़्ते हमें नई कहानियाँ पढ़ाती हैं। Aur yah meree Hindee adhyaapikaa, Sharmaa jee hain. Ve bahut dhairyavaan hain aur har haफ़्ते hameँ naee kahaaniyaaँ paढ़aatee hain. And this is my Hindi teacher, Sharma ji. She is very patient and teaches us new stories every week.
अमन नमस्ते, शर्मा जी। आप कैसी हैं? प्रिया की हिंदी वाक़ई अच्छी है। Namaste, Sharmaa jee. Aap kaisee hain? Priyaa kee Hindee vaaqee achchhee hai. Hello, Sharma ji. How are you? Priya's Hindi is really good.
शर्मा जी धन्यवाद, मैं ठीक हूँ। प्रिया बहुत मेहनती छात्रा है, और मुझे उस पर पूरा भरोसा है। Dhanyavaad, maiँ theek hooँ. Priyaa bahut mehanatee chhaatraa hai, aur mujhe us par pooraa bharosaa hai. Thank you, I'm fine. Priya is a very hardworking student, and I trust her completely.
प्रिया और अमन, यह मेरा सहपाठी विकास है। हम दोनों साथ में हिंदी क्लास लेते हैं। विकास, तुम कहाँ रहते हो? Aur Aman, yah meraa sahapaathee Vikaas hai. Ham donoँ saath meँ Hindee klaas lete hain. Vikaas, tum kahaँ rahate ho? And Aman, this is my classmate Vikas. We both take Hindi class together. Vikas, where do you live?
विकास मैं यहाँ पास ही रहता हूँ। मैं थोड़ा गंभीर लगता हूँ, लेकिन असल में मैं काफ़ी मज़ाकिया हूँ! Maiँ yahaँ paas hee rahataa hooँ. Maiँ thoड़aa gambheer lagataa hooँ, lekin asal meँ maiँ kaafee mazaakiyaa hooँ! I live nearby. I seem a little serious, but I'm actually quite funny!

समझ के प्रश्न — Comprehension questions:

  1. नेहा प्रिया से कैसे संबंधित है, और वह प्रिया से बड़ी है या छोटी? (How is Neha related to Priya, and is she older or younger?)
  2. प्रिया शर्मा जी के बारे में क्या बताती है? (What does Priya say about Sharma ji?)
  3. नेहा अमन से क्या कहती है जब वह उसे "जी" कहता है? (What does Neha say when Aman calls her "ji"?)
  4. विकास अपने बारे में क्या कहता है? (What does Vikas say about himself?)

Writing Workshop

Write a connected paragraph describing three people in your life — real or imagined — and your relationships with them.

लेखन कार्य — Writing task: Write seven to nine sentences describing three people: one family member, one friend or classmate, and one teacher, coworker, or older acquaintance. Your paragraph must include: (1) how each person is related to you, (2) at least one personality adjective for each person, (3) at least one sentence that refers back to a person using a pronoun or possessive instead of repeating the name, (4) appropriate address-form awareness for at least one person (mention how you address them), and (5) one sentence connecting two ideas with और, लेकिन, or इसलिए.

  1. Introduce person one. Say who they are and how they relate to you.
  2. Describe them. Use one or two personality adjectives, with correct agreement.
  3. Refer back. Use वह/वे or उसका/उनका instead of repeating the name.
  4. Introduce person two and three the same way, varying your connectors.
  5. Note address. Mention how you address at least one person and why (आप, तुम, or जी).

संपादन सूची — Editing checklist:

  • Pronouns and possessives (वह/वे, उसका/उनका) are used correctly to avoid repeating names.
  • Honorific plural agreement is used correctly for any respected person (वे हैं, not वह है, for someone addressed with आप).
  • Adjective agreement is checked for variable adjectives such as बड़ा/बड़ी/बड़े.
  • जी or a title is used appropriately, not mechanically added to every name.
  • Spelling, matras, and word spacing are correct, and a danda (।) ends each sentence.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — What shapes address choice

Whether someone is addressed with आप, तुम, or occasionally तू depends on age, familiarity, hierarchy, affection, region, and setting — not on a single fixed rule. Two cousins close in age, like Priya and Neha, may quickly move to तुम and drop जी, while a teacher or an older acquaintance is addressed respectfully regardless of how warm the relationship feels.

Culture in Context — Kinship terms beyond biology

Kinship terms in Hindi-speaking communities are often extended to close family friends, neighbors, and community elders as a sign of warmth and respect, not only to biological relatives. A close family friend might be called मामा or चाची even without a blood relationship. When in doubt about a new relationship, defaulting to a respectful form is safer than assuming familiarity.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using singular agreement for a respected person

Error: Writing शर्मा जी अच्छा अध्यापक है for a teacher addressed with आप.

Correction: शर्मा जी अच्छे अध्यापक हैं। Use plural verb and adjective agreement for anyone addressed respectfully, even when referring to one person.

Common Mistake — Repeating a name instead of using a pronoun

Error: Writing नेहा मेरी बहन है। नेहा बहुत मिलनसार है। नेहा के बहुत दोस्त हैं। as three disconnected sentences.

Correction: नेहा मेरी बहन है। वह बहुत मिलनसार है, और उसके बहुत दोस्त हैं। Use वह and उसके after the first mention.

Common Mistake — Adding जी mechanically to every name

Error: Calling a close cousin or a young classmate by name plus जी, as Aman initially does with Neha.

Correction: Reserve जी for people who are older, unfamiliar, or in a position of respect. Among peers and younger relatives, a first name alone is usually appropriate.

Looking Ahead

अब आप क्या कर सकते हैं — What you can do now

You can describe several people and their relationships to you in connected sentences, refer back to people using pronouns and possessives instead of repeating names, use honorific plural agreement for someone addressed respectfully, and make more deliberate choices among आप, तुम, and जी depending on the relationship.

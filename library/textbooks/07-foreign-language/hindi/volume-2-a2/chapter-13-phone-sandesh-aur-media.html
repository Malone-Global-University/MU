फ़ोन, संदेश, और मीडिया

Volume II, Chapter 13: Phone, Messages, and Media · Speak and Write Hindi: Everyday Independence and Connected Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Make and respond to phone calls and voice messages in everyday and semi-formal situations.
  2. Report what someone said using कि, as in उन्होंने कहा कि... and उसने बताया कि....
  3. Distinguish a direct quotation from a reported message and adjust pronoun and reference accordingly.
  4. Ask confirmation and clarification questions when a message is unclear.
  5. Understand short media captions, posts, and public announcements.
  6. Distinguish informal messaging style from standard written Hindi, and choose the appropriate register for a given task.
  7. Write a message that summarizes and reports information accurately.

Communication Scenario

Arjun's professor approved his extension, and his manager agreed to rearrange his shift. Before he leaves town, Priya calls him to talk about the project. He misses her call and she leaves a voice message. Later, she also sends a short text and posts an update in their class group chat. Arjun needs to listen to the voice message, read the text and the group post, and then report the key information to a classmate who was not online.

  1. Notice how Priya's spoken voice message differs in pace and phrasing from her typed text message.
  2. Notice how Arjun reports Priya's words using कि rather than repeating her exact sentence.
  3. Notice how the group chat post uses a more casual, abbreviated style than the earlier formal email in Chapter 12.

Pronunciation and Schwa Deletion

Focus — Listening to natural phone and voice-message speech

Voice messages are often faster and less carefully articulated than a classroom dialogue. Speakers may pause mid-sentence, restart a phrase, or run words together. Listen first for the overall message, then listen again for specific details; do not expect to catch every word on the first pass.

Focus — Schwa deletion in digital and media vocabulary

Notice that सूचना is read as approximately soo-ch-na, with the middle inherent vowel dropped, while घोषणा is read as approximately gho-shuh-na, retaining a light middle vowel. Compound English-origin words such as ग्रुप चैट generally keep their loanword pronunciation rather than following native Hindi schwa patterns.

Focus — Intonation for confirmation and repair

A confirmation question such as क्या आपने कहा कि सोमवार को? rises gently at the end to invite a yes/no response, while a simple clarification word like मतलब? is spoken with a short rising pitch and a slightly puzzled tone.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

13.5 Reported information with कि

When you report what someone said, you connect the reporting verb to the reported content with कि ("that"). This chapter introduces the pattern in simple, direct structures.

Language Building Block — Reporting with कि
उन्होंने कहा कि वह कल आएगी। मुझे पता है कि प्रोजेक्ट सोमवार को जमा करना है। उसने बताया कि मीटिंग रद्द हो गई है।
She said that she would come tomorrow. I know that the project has to be submitted on Monday. She told me that the meeting was cancelled.

Form: [subject] + [reporting verb: कहना, बताना, पता होना, etc.] + कि + [reported clause]. The reported clause keeps its own subject, verb, and tense; Hindi does not shift the tense inside the reported clause the way some English reporting patterns do. Keep the reported clause as a complete, natural Hindi sentence.

13.6 Direct versus reported message

Language Building Block — Direct quote versus reported version
प्रिया ने कहा, "मैं कल कॉल करूँगी।" → प्रिया ने कहा कि वह कल कॉल करेगी।
Priya said, "I will call tomorrow." → Priya said that she would call tomorrow.

Meaning and use: A direct quotation repeats the speaker's exact words, usually inside quotation marks. A reported version restates the content in the reporter's own words, without quotation marks, introduced by कि. At this stage, keep reported versions simple: change first-person pronouns and possessives to match the new speaker's perspective, and adjust the verb form only when the subject changes (मैं करूँगी becomes वह करेगी).

13.7 Pronoun and reference shift in reported information

Language Building Block — Shifting reference
प्रिया: "मुझे तुम्हारी मदद चाहिए।" → अर्जुन को बताते हुए: उसने कहा कि उसे मेरी मदद चाहिए।
Priya: "I need your help." → Telling someone else: She said that she needed my help.

Form: When the reporter is not one of the original speakers, first-person forms (मैं, मुझे, मेरा) in the direct quote typically become third-person forms (वह, उसे, उसका) in the reported version, and second-person forms shift to match whoever is actually being referred to. Practice this shift with short, controlled examples before attempting it in free conversation.

13.8 Confirmation and clarification questions

Language Building Block — Checking understanding
क्या आपने कहा कि सोमवार को मिलना है? माफ़ कीजिए, मतलब? क्या आप फिर से बोल सकते/सकती हैं? ठीक है, मैं समझ गया/गई।
Did you say that we're meeting on Monday? Sorry, what do you mean? Could you say that again? Okay, I understood.

Use क्या आपने कहा कि...? to confirm a specific piece of reported information. Use मतलब? informally among friends, and क्या आप फिर से बोल सकते/सकती हैं? in more polite or institutional contexts.

13.9 Informal message fragments versus complete standard sentences

Casual texting and group-chat posts often use short fragments, abbreviations, and Hindi-English mixing, while standard written Hindi in messages, reports, or emails uses complete sentences and consistent Devanagari spelling.

Language Building Block — Register contrast
कैज़ुअल: मीटिंग कैंसिल, सोमवार को नई डेट। मानक: मुझे बताया गया है कि मीटिंग रद्द हो गई है और अब वह सोमवार को होगी।
Casual: Meeting cancelled, new date Monday. Standard: I have been informed that the meeting has been cancelled and it will now take place on Monday.

This course's productive writing target remains complete, standard written Hindi. Learners should recognize casual fragments in reading and listening but should write in complete sentences unless a task specifically asks for an informal message.

Boundary

This chapter does not introduce complex indirect speech with multiple embedded clauses, media rhetorical style, political discourse, or full colloquial romanized Hindi as a productive writing standard. Those extensions belong to later chapters and volumes.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Arjun listens to Priya's voice message, then talks with his classmate Rohan.

प्रिया (आवाज़ी संदेश) अर्जुन, मैं प्रिया बोल रही हूँ। सुनो, मीटिंग सोमवार की जगह मंगलवार को होगी, क्योंकि प्रोफ़ेसर बाहर गए हैं। मुझे तुम्हारी प्रेज़ेंटेशन वाली स्लाइड्स चाहिए, अगर हो सके तो आज भेज देना। ठीक है, बाद में बात करते हैं। Arjun, this is Priya speaking. Listen, the meeting will be on Tuesday instead of Monday, because the professor is out of town. I need your presentation slides — if possible, send them today. Okay, we'll talk later.
अर्जुन रोहन, प्रिया ने मैसेज छोड़ा था। उसने कहा कि मीटिंग सोमवार की जगह मंगलवार को होगी। Rohan, Priya left a message. She said that the meeting would be on Tuesday instead of Monday.
रोहन अच्छा, क्या उसने कोई कारण भी बताया? Okay, did she also give a reason?
अर्जुन हाँ, उसने बताया कि प्रोफ़ेसर बाहर गए हैं, इसलिए मीटिंग टल गई है। और उसने यह भी कहा कि उसे मेरी प्रेज़ेंटेशन वाली स्लाइड्स चाहिए। Yes, she told me that the professor is out of town, so the meeting has been postponed. And she also said that she needs my presentation slides.
रोहन ठीक है, समझ गया। क्या तुमने उसे स्लाइड्स भेज दीं? Okay, understood. Did you send her the slides?
अर्जुन अभी नहीं, लेकिन आज शाम तक भेज दूँगा। Not yet, but I'll send them by this evening.

समझ के प्रश्न — Comprehension questions:

  1. प्रिया ने अपने आवाज़ी संदेश में क्या बताया? (What did Priya tell Arjun in her voice message?)
  2. मीटिंग क्यों टल गई? (Why was the meeting postponed?)
  3. अर्जुन रोहन को क्या बताता है? (What does Arjun tell Rohan?)

Writing Workshop

Write a short message titled जानकारी की रिपोर्ट, reporting information from a phone call, voice message, or group chat to someone who was not present.

लेखन कार्य — Writing task: Write a connected message of 100-150 words in standard Devanagari, in complete sentences. Your message must include: (1) a brief statement of who told you the information, (2) at least two pieces of reported information using कि, (3) correct pronoun/reference shift where needed, (4) one reason using क्योंकि or इसलिए, and (5) a closing statement about what you or the reader should do next.

  1. Choose your source. Pick a phone call, voice message, or chat post to report.
  2. Identify the speaker. State who told you the information.
  3. Report the content. Use कहा कि or बताया कि for each key point.
  4. Check your pronouns. Make sure reference shifts correctly from the original speaker's perspective.
  5. Close with next steps. State what should happen next.

संपादन सूची — Editing checklist:

  • कि-clauses are complete, natural Hindi sentences.
  • Pronoun and reference shifts match the reporting perspective correctly.
  • The message uses complete standard sentences, not chat-style fragments.
  • At least one reason is expressed with क्योंकि or इसलिए.
  • Spelling, matras, word spacing, and dandas are correct throughout.

Speaking Practice

नियंत्रित अभ्यास — Controlled practice: reporting a message

With a partner, one person reads a short direct statement aloud; the other reports it back using उसने कहा कि... or उसने बताया कि..., adjusting pronouns correctly.

संचार कार्य — Communicative task: फ़ोन पर संदेश

Role-play a short phone call with a partner in which one person leaves a voice message about a schedule change, and the other later reports the message to a third person (played by the same partner or imagined).

अकेले अभ्यास — Solo alternative

Record a short voice message about a plan or schedule change, then record yourself reporting your own message as if telling someone else what "you" said.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Register in digital communication

Everyday digital Hindi communication may include abbreviations, English words, emojis, and even romanized Hindi spellings, especially among friends and classmates. This is normal, real usage and not a sign of poor Hindi. However, this course's productive writing target remains standard Devanagari in complete sentences; learners should recognize casual digital style without adopting it as their default written form.

Culture in Context — Register shifts quickly

The same person may write a casual group-chat message to friends and a formal email to a professor within the same hour. Recognizing which register a situation calls for is an important practical skill, and it is normal to shift between them depending on audience and purpose.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Forgetting to shift pronouns when reporting

Error: Reporting Priya's statement "मुझे मदद चाहिए" as प्रिया ने कहा कि मुझे मदद चाहिए when telling a third person.

Correction: प्रिया ने कहा कि उसे मदद चाहिए। The first-person मुझे in the original quote becomes third-person उसे because the reporter is not Priya.

Common Mistake — Treating कि as optional punctuation

Error: Writing उसने कहा वह कल आएगी without कि.

Correction: उसने कहा कि वह कल आएगी। कि is required to introduce the reported clause in standard written Hindi.

Common Mistake — Writing formal messages in chat-style fragments

Error: Writing a reporting message as मीटिंग कैंसिल, मंगलवार को नई डेट when a complete standard sentence is expected.

Correction: मुझे बताया गया है कि मीटिंग रद्द हो गई है और अब वह मंगलवार को होगी। Reserve fragment style for genuinely informal chat contexts.

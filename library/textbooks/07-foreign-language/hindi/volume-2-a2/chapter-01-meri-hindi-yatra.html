मेरी हिंदी यात्रा

Volume II, Chapter 1: My Hindi Journey · Speak and Write Hindi: Everyday Independence and Connected Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Describe your Hindi-learning background in a connected paragraph rather than isolated sentences.
  2. Introduce yourself and summarize familiar personal information without relying on sentence-by-sentence prompts.
  3. Explain, in connected Hindi, what you can already do: your abilities, your habits, and your plans.
  4. Ask and answer follow-up questions in a simple but connected conversation.
  5. Read a short learner profile written primarily in Devanagari, with romanization reduced to occasional support.
  6. Recognize what Volume II expects of you: broader postpositions, developed ने, and more connected discourse.

Communication Scenario

Priya, who studied Hindi throughout Volume I, meets a new Hindi conversation partner, Aman, at a language exchange meetup. Aman asks about her Hindi journey so far: why she started, what she can do now, what is still difficult, and what she hopes to do next. Priya answers not with single words but with a connected account of her learning story.

  1. Notice that Priya no longer answers with single words or short fragments. She connects several sentences into one flowing account.
  2. Notice that every grammar system Priya uses — habitual, progressive, ability, need, future, and simple perfective — was already taught in Volume I. Nothing here is new grammar.
  3. Notice how this chapter sets the tone for all of Volume II: familiar systems used more independently and more connectedly.

Where This Chapter Fits

Chapter 1 opens Unit I, संबंध और दिनचर्या, and Hindi Volume II as a whole. Volume I Chapter 20 closed with an integrated CEFR A2 performance project drawing on greetings, family, routine, shopping, health, directions, past narration, plans, and connected reason-result-contrast-condition language. Chapter 1 of Volume II does not reteach any of that. Instead, it reactivates it as the operating infrastructure for a new kind of task: sustained, connected self-description.

Coming Into This Chapter

You can already introduce yourself, describe routines with habitual and progressive forms, express ability with सकना and need with चाहिए, narrate a simple completed event with controlled ने, make future plans, and connect ideas with क्योंकि, इसलिए, लेकिन, and अगर...तो.

New in This Chapter

No new grammar system is introduced. This chapter is a reactivation and connection chapter: it asks you to combine already-known systems into longer, connected paragraphs and conversations, and it introduces the vocabulary of language learning itself — skills, confidence, difficulty, and goals.

Looking Forward

Chapter 2, रिश्ते और सामाजिक भाषा, develops honorific agreement and pronoun reference across connected sentences. Later chapters in this unit expand oblique forms, locative postpositions, and the habitual-progressive-obligation system before Unit I closes with an integrated review in Chapter 5.

Devanagari and Pronunciation Focus

This chapter introduces no new letters, matras, or pronunciation rules. It reactivates Volume I pronunciation systems at the level of a full connected paragraph rather than isolated words or short sentences.

Focus — Schwa deletion in learning vocabulary

Read सीखना, समझना, बोलना, and सुधार as natural spoken forms. Notice that सीखना is pronounced roughly seekhnaa, not seekhanaa — the inherent vowel after ख is not pronounced. This is the same schwa deletion awareness developed since Volume I, Chapter 9, now applied to new vocabulary.

Focus — Aspiration and retroflexion in connected speech

Reread the model dialogue below aloud. Pay attention to aspirated consonants in मुश्किल, धाराप्रवाह, and थोड़ा-थोड़ा, and to the retroflex ड़ in थोड़ा, which is distinct from dental द.

Focus — Paragraph-level rhythm

Volume II asks you to read and speak in longer connected units. Practice grouping the reading passage below into natural phrases — pausing at commas and dandas, not after every word — rather than reading word by word.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter introduces no new grammar. It reactivates the Volume I verb and pronoun systems you will use constantly across Volume II, now practiced at the level of a connected paragraph.

1.3 Language Focus — Connecting habitual, ability, and future in one account

A connected account of a learning journey typically moves through several verb systems in sequence: habitual for what you regularly do, ability with सकना for what you can now do, need with चाहिए for what still requires work, and future for what you plan to do next. None of these forms is new. What is new in Volume II is expecting you to move among them smoothly, in one paragraph, without a fresh prompt for each sentence.

Language Building Block — A connected learning account
मैं पिछले साल से हिंदी सीख रही हूँ। मैं हर दिन थोड़ा अभ्यास करती हूँ। अब मैं आसान बातचीत समझ सकती हूँ, लेकिन मुझे व्याकरण में अभी भी सुधार चाहिए। अगले साल मैं भारत जाऊँगी और वहाँ ज़्यादा बोलने की कोशिश करूँगी।
I have been learning Hindi since last year. I practice a little every day. Now I can understand easy conversation, but I still need improvement in grammar. Next year I will go to India and try to speak more there.

1.4 Language Focus — Paragraph-level pronoun reference

In Volume I, most sentences stood alone. In Volume II, you will describe people, places, and experiences across several connected sentences. This means using वह, वे, उसका/उसकी/उसके, and similar forms to refer back to someone or something you already mentioned, instead of repeating the full noun every time.

Language Building Block — Referring back without repeating the noun
मेरी एक दोस्त है जो हिंदी पढ़ाती है। वह बहुत धैर्यवान है, और उसकी क्लास में सीखना आसान लगता है।
I have a friend who teaches Hindi. She is very patient, and learning feels easy in her class.
Boundary

This chapter does not introduce new postposition functions, new oblique patterns, expanded ने, or compound verbs. Those systems begin developing in Chapters 3, 4, and 7. Here, only recombination and connection are new.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Priya meets Aman, a new conversation partner, at a Hindi-language exchange meetup.

अमन नमस्ते! आप कब से हिंदी सीख रही हैं? Namaste! Aap kab se Hindi seekh rahee hain? Hello! Since when have you been learning Hindi?
प्रिया नमस्ते, अमन जी। मैं पिछले साल से हिंदी सीख रही हूँ। मैंने एक किताब से शुरू किया, और अब मैं हर दिन थोड़ा अभ्यास करती हूँ। Namaste, Aman jee. Maiँ pichhale saal se Hindi seekh rahee hooँ. Maiँne ek kitaab se shuroo kiyaa, aur ab maiँ har din thoड़aa abhyaas karatee hooँ. Hello, Aman ji. I have been learning Hindi since last year. I started with a book, and now I practice a little every day.
अमन बहुत अच्छा! अब आप क्या-क्या कर सकती हैं? Bahut achchhaa! Ab aap kyaa-kyaa kar sakatee hain? Very good! What all can you do now?
प्रिया मैं आसान बातचीत समझ सकती हूँ, और मैं अपने परिवार, अपने घर, और अपनी दिनचर्या के बारे में बात कर सकती हूँ। मैंने पिछले हफ़्ते एक छोटी कहानी भी पढ़ी। Maiँ aasaan baatacheet samajh sakatee hooँ, aur maiँ apane parivaar, apane ghar, aur apanee dinacharyaa ke baare meँ baat kar sakatee hooँ. Maiँne pichhale haफ़्ते ek chhotee kahaanee bhee paढ़ee. I can understand easy conversation, and I can talk about my family, my house, and my daily routine. I even read a short story last week.
अमन शानदार! क्या कुछ अभी भी मुश्किल लगता है? Shaanadaar! Kyaa kuchh abhee bhee mushkil lagataa hai? Wonderful! Is anything still difficult?
प्रिया हाँ, व्याकरण में मुझे अभी भी सुधार चाहिए, ख़ासकर ने के साथ। कभी-कभी मैं भूल जाती हूँ। Haaँ, vyaakaran meँ mujhe abhee bhee sudhaar chaahie, khaasakar 'ne' ke saath. Kabhee-kabhee maiँ bhool jaatee hooँ. Yes, I still need improvement in grammar, especially with ने. Sometimes I forget.
अमन कोई बात नहीं, सबको समय लगता है। आपकी अगली योजना क्या है? Koee baat naheeँ, sabako samay lagataa hai. Aapakee agalee yojanaa kyaa hai? No problem, everyone needs time. What's your next plan?
प्रिया अगले साल मैं भारत जाऊँगी, इसलिए मैं ज़्यादा बोलने की कोशिश करना चाहती हूँ। मुझे लगता है कि रोज़ अभ्यास करने से मेरी प्रगति होगी। Agale saal maiँ Bhaarat jaaoँgee, isalie maiँ zyaadaa bolane kee koshish karanaa chaahatee hooँ. Mujhe lagataa hai ki roz abhyaas karane se meree pragati hogee. Next year I will go to India, so I want to try to speak more. I think that practicing daily will help my progress.

समझ के प्रश्न — Comprehension questions:

  1. प्रिया कब से हिंदी सीख रही है? (Since when has Priya been learning Hindi?)
  2. प्रिया अब क्या-क्या कर सकती है? (What can Priya do now?)
  3. प्रिया को क्या अभी भी मुश्किल लगता है? (What does Priya still find difficult?)
  4. प्रिया की अगली योजना क्या है? (What is Priya's next plan?)

Writing Workshop

Write a connected paragraph describing your own Hindi journey — or, if you prefer, an imagined learner's journey.

लेखन कार्य — Writing task: Write six to eight sentences describing a Hindi-learning journey. Your paragraph must include: (1) how long you have been learning and why you started, (2) at least one thing you can already do, using ability with सकना, (3) one completed learning event using perfective (with controlled ने if the verb is transitive), (4) one thing that is still difficult, using चाहिए or मुश्किल लगना, and (5) a future plan or goal.

  1. Open your story. Say how long you have been learning and why, using से for duration.
  2. Describe your ability. Use सकना to say what you can do now.
  3. Narrate one event. Describe one thing you did or read, using perfective and ने if transitive.
  4. Name a difficulty. Use चाहिए or मुश्किल लगना to name something still hard.
  5. Look ahead. State a future plan or goal.

संपादन सूची — Editing checklist:

  • Habitual, ability, perfective, and future verb forms are each used correctly where intended.
  • ने is used correctly with transitive perfective verbs, and gender/number agreement is checked.
  • Pronoun reference is clear across sentences — you are not repeating your own name unnecessarily.
  • Spelling, matras, and word spacing are correct, and a danda (।) ends each sentence.
  • Romanization is not used for ordinary sentences; Devanagari stands on its own.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Why people learn Hindi

Learners come to Hindi for many different reasons: family connection, travel, work, scholarship, media and film, religious or cultural interest, or simple personal curiosity. None of these reasons is more legitimate than another, and a learner's motivation does not need to involve a single "authentic" Indian identity or experience.

Culture in Context — Talking about progress politely

When Hindi speakers ask about someone's language ability, modest, encouraging responses are common, as are honest acknowledgments of what is still difficult. Overstating fluency or dismissing one's own progress entirely are both less natural than a balanced, connected account like the ones modeled in this chapter.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Restarting every sentence with the same subject

Error: Repeating मैं or a personal name at the start of every single sentence in a connected paragraph, producing a choppy list rather than a connected account.

Correction: Vary sentence connection using और, लेकिन, इसलिए, and simple pronoun reference so the paragraph flows as one account: मैं हिंदी सीख रही हूँ और हर दिन अभ्यास करती हूँ, लेकिन व्याकरण अभी भी मुश्किल लगता है।

Common Mistake — Forgetting ने when narrating a learning event

Error: Writing मैं एक कहानी पढ़ी instead of using ने with a transitive perfective verb.

Correction: मैंने एक कहानी पढ़ी। Check every past-tense transitive verb in your journey paragraph for ने, exactly as practiced in Volume I, Chapter 16.

Common Mistake — Confusing सकना and चाहिए

Error: Using चाहिए to describe ability, as in मुझे हिंदी बोलनी चाहिए when the intended meaning is "I can speak Hindi."

Correction: Use सकना for ability (मैं हिंदी बोल सकती हूँ) and reserve चाहिए for need or obligation (मुझे व्याकरण में सुधार चाहिए).

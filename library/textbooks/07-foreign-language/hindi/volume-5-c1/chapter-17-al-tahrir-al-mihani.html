التَّحْرِير المِهْنِيّ وَتَحْسِين الأُسْلُوبChapter 17: Professional Editing and Style Improvement

Continuing Unit IV · Speak and Write Arabic: Mastery, Precision, Rhetoric, and Specialized Expression
Unit IV Continues

Chapter Overview

Chapter 16 closed with هَنَاء winning a postponement rather than an outright decision: the council agreed to review the neighborhood renewal project again after further evidence is presented. That next submission now has to be written, and it has arrived on هَنَاء's desk not as a blank page but as someone else's draft — a colleague on the residents committee has produced a report for the follow-up review, and it needs an editor's eye before it goes back to the council. This chapter turns from delivering an argument aloud to revising one that already exists on paper. It develops vocabulary and method for editing someone else's Arabic for clarity, cohesion, precision, and rhetorical effect, while relying entirely on the nominalization, content clauses, contrast connectors, and passive voice that reached mastery status by Chapter 5 and the concession-rebuttal and anticipation patterns introduced in Chapter 16. No new grammar system is introduced here; editing is a discipline applied to grammar already under the learner's control, not a occasion for new structures.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Use editing and style vocabulary to name specific problems in a draft (repetition, vagueness, weak cohesion, inconsistent register, buried claims).
  2. Distinguish a copyediting correction (spelling, agreement, mechanics) from a substantive edit (reorganizing, cutting, sharpening an argument).
  3. Tighten a wordy or diffuse sentence without losing its meaning, using nominalization and cutting rather than new grammar.
  4. Improve cohesion between sentences and paragraphs by adjusting connectors, reference, and topic continuity already at mastery status.
  5. Give another writer's argument a stronger rhetorical shape by relocating a claim, sharpening a rebuttal, or tightening a concession, without changing what the writer intended to say.
  6. Demonstrate independent command of editing and style-improvement vocabulary at the level authorized for this chapter, applied entirely through grammar already at mastery status.

Opening Communication Scenario

The council's follow-up review is three weeks away. سَامِر, a newer member of the residents committee, has drafted the written submission that will accompany هَنَاء's next appearance before the council. His research is sound and his numbers are accurate, but the draft repeats its central claim four times without ever sharpening it, buries its strongest piece of evidence in the middle of a long paragraph, and shifts without warning from a formal institutional register to a more personal, conversational one. هَنَاء has been asked to edit the draft before it is submitted — not to rewrite سَامِر's argument, but to help his existing argument reach the council in its clearest, most persuasive form.

  1. You will learn vocabulary for describing what a draft needs and for naming specific kinds of edits.
  2. You will study a method for tightening a wordy sentence without losing its meaning.
  3. You will study a method for repositioning a strong piece of evidence so it carries more rhetorical weight.
  4. You will read and listen to a model exchange between هَنَاء and سَامِر as she explains her edits.
  5. You will edit a short draft yourself and justify each change you make.

قِرَاءَة التَّعْدِيلَات بِصَوْت مَسْمُوع — Reading Edits Aloud Without Marking Blame

Reading Edits Aloud: Marking Change Without Marking Blame

Sound and Delivery — Neutral, collegial delivery of editorial comments

No new sound or pausing pattern is introduced in this chapter. The relevant skill is delivering an editorial comment — spoken aloud to a colleague, as هَنَاء does with سَامِر — in a level, collegial tone that separates the comment from personal criticism. A flat, even pace on a phrase such as هَذِه الجُمْلَة تَحْتَاج إِلَى إِيجَاز ("this sentence needs concision") signals a professional observation about the text; a sharper or more clipped delivery of the same words can unintentionally sound like a judgment of the writer. Practice reading this chapter's editorial comments aloud at a steady, even pace, as one colleague speaking to another about a shared piece of work.

التَّمَاسُك وَالدِّقَّة وَالإِيجَاز — Cohesion, Precision, and Economy

Rhetorical Focus: Cohesion, Precision, and Economy

This chapter introduces no new grammar system. Nominalization, content clauses, contrast connectors, and passive voice remain at the mastery status reached by Chapter 5, and the concession-rebuttal and anticipation patterns from Chapter 16 remain available for editing an argument's structure. What is new is the disciplined application of this stable grammar to someone else's writing: tightening a sentence, strengthening cohesion between sentences, and repositioning a claim for greater rhetorical force.

Editorial Move: Tightening Through Nominalization and Cutting

[repeated finite clause] → [single nominalized phrase]

a sentence that restates the same claim across two or three finite clauses can often be tightened into a single nominalized phrase carrying the same meaning; this is not a new grammar system, since nominalization has been at mastery status since Chapter 3, but a targeted application of it to reduce repetition

قَبْل: المَشْرُوع أَفَادَ العَائِلَات، وَهَذِه الفَائِدَة كَانَت كَبِيرَة، وَقَد اِسْتَمَرَّت هَذِه الفَائِدَة الكَبِيرَة لِثَلَاث سَنَوَات. → بَعْد: اِسْتَمَرَّت فَائِدَة المَشْرُوع الكَبِيرَة لِلْعَائِلَات ثَلَاث سَنَوَات.

Before: "The project benefited families, and this benefit was large, and this large benefit continued for three years." After: "The project's large benefit to families continued for three years."

Editorial Move: Repositioning the Strongest Claim

[strongest evidence, buried mid-paragraph] → [strongest evidence, foregrounded at the paragraph's opening or close]

no new sentence-level grammar is required to move a claim; the editor identifies the paragraph's strongest piece of evidence using vocabulary such as أَقْوَى نُقْطَة (recycled from Chapter 16) and relocates it to a position of greater rhetorical weight, usually the beginning or the end of the paragraph, while leaving the underlying grammar of each sentence unchanged

تَعْلِيق تَحْرِيرِيّ: أَقْوَى نُقْطَة فِي هَذِه الفِقْرَة — نِسْبَة العَائِلَات المُسْتَفِيدَة — مَدْفُونَة فِي مُنْتَصَف الجُمْلَة الثَّالِثَة. اِقْتِرَاحِي: اِنْقُلْهَا إِلَى بِدَايَة الفِقْرَة.

Editorial comment: "This paragraph's strongest point — the proportion of benefiting families — is buried in the middle of the third sentence. My suggestion: move it to the beginning of the paragraph."

Both moves reuse nominalization, cohesion devices, and evidence-vocabulary already at mastery or consolidated status; neither requires new grammar. Learners should notice that professional editing changes the shape and placement of existing material far more often than it changes the grammar itself.

  • هَذِه الجُمْلَة تُكَرِّر الفِكْرَة الَّتِي وَرَدَت فِي الفِقْرَة السَّابِقَة؛ أَقْتَرِح حَذْفَهَا أَو دَمْجَهَا مَع الجُمْلَة التَّالِيَة.This sentence repeats the idea that appeared in the previous paragraph; I suggest deleting it or merging it with the following sentence.naming repetition and proposing a specific cut
  • الاِنْتِقَال بَيْن هَذِه الفِقْرَة وَالَّتِي تَلِيهَا غَيْر وَاضِح؛ يَحْتَاج الأُسْلُوب إِلَى رَابِط يُوَضِّح العَلَاقَة بَيْن الفِكْرَتَيْن.The transition between this paragraph and the one following it is unclear; the style needs a connector that clarifies the relationship between the two ideas.diagnosing weak cohesion and requesting a specific connector
  • هَذِه الفِقْرَة تَبْدَأ بِأُسْلُوب رَسْمِيّ ثُمَّ تَنْتَقِل فَجْأَة إِلَى أُسْلُوب أَقْرَب إِلَى الحَدِيث اليَوْمِيّ؛ يَجِب تَوْحِيد التَّنَاسُق عَلَى مُسْتَوَى الفِقْرَة كُلّهَا.This paragraph begins in a formal style, then suddenly shifts to a style closer to everyday speech; the consistency must be unified across the entire paragraph.naming a register inconsistency across a single paragraph
Grammar Note — Editing changes shape, not grammar

It might seem that professional editing calls for new grammatical tools. It does not. Every editorial move in this section — tightening through nominalization, strengthening cohesion, repositioning a claim — works entirely with structures already at mastery status by Chapter 5. What editing adds is judgment: the ability to see where existing grammar is being used inefficiently, redundantly, or in a rhetorically weak position, and the vocabulary to describe that judgment clearly and respectfully to another writer.

حِوَار نَمُوذَجِيّ — Model Exchange

Model Exchange: Editing the Draft

Setting: The residents committee office. هَنَاء is reviewing سَامِر's draft submission with him before it goes back to the council.

سَامِر: قَرَأْتِ المُسَوَّدَة؟ أَعْتَقِد أَنَّهَا جَاهِزَة لِلْإِرْسَال.Did you read the draft? I think it's ready to send.
هَنَاء: قَرَأْتُهَا، وَالبَحْث فِيهَا قَوِيّ جِدًّا. لَكِن أَقْوَى نُقْطَة عِنْدَك — نِسْبَة العَائِلَات المُسْتَفِيدَة — مَدْفُونَة فِي مُنْتَصَف الفِقْرَة الثَّالِثَة. أَقْتَرِح أَن تَنْقُلَهَا إِلَى بِدَايَة الفِقْرَة.I read it, and the research in it is very strong. But your strongest point — the proportion of benefiting families — is buried in the middle of the third paragraph. I suggest you move it to the beginning of the paragraph.
سَامِر: مَعْقُول. وَمَاذَا عَن الفِقْرَة الثَّانِيَة؟ أَشْعُر أَنَّهَا طَوِيلَة أَكْثَر مِن اللَّازِم.That's reasonable. And what about the second paragraph? I feel it's longer than it needs to be.
هَنَاء: صَحِيح. الجُمْلَة الأُولَى تُكَرِّر نَفْس الفِكْرَة ثَلَاث مَرَّات بِصِيَغ مُخْتَلِفَة. يُمْكِن إِيجَازُهَا فِي جُمْلَة وَاحِدَة عَن طَرِيق الاِسْم بَدَل الفِعْل المُتَكَرِّر.True. The first sentence repeats the same idea three times in different phrasings. It can be made concise into one sentence by using a noun instead of the repeated verb.
سَامِر: وَهَل لَاحَظْتِ تَغَيُّر الأُسْلُوب فِي الفِقْرَة الأَخِيرَة؟And did you notice the style change in the last paragraph?
هَنَاء: نَعَم. تَبْدَأ الفِقْرَة بِأُسْلُوب رَسْمِيّ ثُمَّ تَنْتَقِل فَجْأَة إِلَى عِبَارَات أَقْرَب إِلَى الحَدِيث اليَوْمِيّ. يَجِب أَن يَبْقَى الأُسْلُوب رَسْمِيًّا حَتَّى النِّهَايَة، لِأَنَّ هَذَا نَصّ سَيُقْرَأ أَمَام المَجْلِس.Yes. The paragraph begins in a formal style, then suddenly shifts to phrasing closer to everyday speech. The style should stay formal until the end, since this is a text that will be read before the council.
سَامِر: شُكْرًا لِتَعْلِيقَاتِك. سَأُعِيد تَرْتِيب الفِقْرَة الثَّالِثَة وَأُوجِز الثَّانِيَة وَأُوَحِّد الأُسْلُوب فِي الأَخِيرَة.Thank you for your comments. I will reorganize the third paragraph, make the second concise, and unify the style in the last one.

Notice that هَنَاء never rewrites سَامِر's argument for him. Every comment names a specific problem — a buried claim, a repeated idea, an inconsistent register — and proposes a specific move, leaving the underlying grammar and the writer's ownership of the argument untouched.

Writing Workshop

Below is a second, unedited paragraph from سَامِر's draft. Edit it for concision, cohesion, and consistent register, then write three to five editorial comments explaining your changes, following the model in the Reading Practice section.

مِن مُسَوَّدَة سَامِر — فِقْرَة لِلتَّحْرِير
الغَرَض / Purpose: فِقْرَة تُنَاقِش تَكْلِفَة المَشْرُوع، تَحْتَاج إِلَى تَحْرِير

صَحِيح إِنَّ التَّكْلِفَة اِرْتَفَعَت هَذَا العَام، وَهَذَا شَيْء لَازِم نِعْتَرِف بِيه بِصَرَاحَة، بَس يَنْبَغِي أَن نُلَاحِظ أَنَّ نِسْبَة العَائِلَات المُسْتَفِيدَة اِرْتَفَعَت أَيْضًا، وَهَذَا مُهِمّ جِدًّا، وَيَجِب أَن يَعْرِفَه المَجْلِس، وَهَذَا يَعْنِي أَنَّ التَّكْلِفَة الإِضَافِيَّة كَانَت اِسْتِثْمَارًا مُفِيدًا وَلَيْسَت نَفَقَة بِلَا فَائِدَة.

"It is true that the cost rose this year, and this is something we have to admit honestly, but it should be noted that the proportion of benefiting families also rose, and this is very important, and the council should know it, and this means that the additional cost was a useful investment and not an expense without benefit."

  1. Step 1 — Diagnose register. Identify any words or phrases that shift away from a formal, public register (for example, colloquial connectors), and name the problem in one editorial comment.
  2. Step 2 — Diagnose repetition or wordiness. Identify any clause that restates a point already made, and propose a nominalized or shortened alternative.
  3. Step 3 — Diagnose cohesion. Check whether the connectors joining the sentences clearly show the relationship between ideas (concession, result, addition), and suggest a stronger connector if needed.
  4. Step 4 — Produce an edited version. Write the paragraph as you would submit it, using only grammar already at mastery status.
  5. Step 5 — Justify your edits. Write three to five editorial comments, each naming a specific problem and the specific move you made to address it.
  6. Review your work against the checklist below.

قَائِمَة المُرَاجَعَة — Checklist:

  • The edited paragraph maintains a single, consistently formal register throughout.
  • At least one repeated or wordy clause has been tightened using nominalization.
  • The connectors between ideas clearly show concession, result, or addition.
  • The writer's original claim and evidence are preserved; only shape and clarity have changed.
  • Each editorial comment names a specific problem and a specific corresponding move.
  • No grammar, case, mood, or clause system beyond Chapter 5's consolidated status appears anywhere in the edited paragraph or the comments.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Editing as a collaborative, face-saving practice

In many professional and academic settings across Arabic-speaking communities, editorial feedback is commonly delivered in a way that separates the comment from the person: the editor addresses the text (هَذِه الجُمْلَة, "this sentence") rather than the writer's ability (أَنْتَ, "you"), and frames suggestions as proposals rather than commands. This is not a universal rule, but it is a common and generally safe convention for professional editorial exchanges, particularly when editor and writer will continue working together afterward.

Regional Variation — Editorial conventions differ by institution

The specific conventions of professional editing — whether comments are delivered orally or in writing, how directly a superior may edit a subordinate's work, whether tracked changes or clean rewrites are preferred — vary by country, institution, publication, and workplace culture. This chapter models one common collegial pattern rather than a single universal standard.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Rewriting instead of editing

Error: replacing the writer's argument, evidence, or word choices wholesale rather than tightening, reorganizing, or clarifying what the writer already produced.

Correction: treat editing as improving the shape and clarity of existing material; preserve the writer's claims, evidence, and voice unless a factual or grammatical error requires correction.

Common Mistake — Vague editorial comments

Error: writing a comment such as هَذِه الفِقْرَة غَيْر جَيِّدَة ("this paragraph isn't good") without naming the specific problem or a specific fix.

Correction: name the specific issue (repetition, buried claim, weak cohesion, inconsistent register) and propose a specific, actionable move, as modeled throughout this chapter.

Common Mistake — Personalizing the comment

Error: directing an editorial comment at the writer's ability or effort (أَنْتَ لَم تُفَكِّر جَيِّدًا, "you didn't think this through well") rather than at the text itself.

Correction: keep editorial vocabulary attached to the sentence, paragraph, or draft (هَذِه الجُمْلَة تَحْتَاج إِلَى..., "this sentence needs..."), consistent with the collegial register this chapter models.

Chapter Review — Model responses

Section A: تَمَاسُك names how well sentences and paragraphs connect to one another; غُمُوض names a claim or phrase that is not specific enough to be persuasive; إِعَادَة تَرْتِيب names moving a claim or paragraph to a stronger rhetorical position. A copyediting correction fixes mechanics (for example, correcting a spelling or agreement error); a substantive edit changes organization, concision, or rhetorical force (for example, repositioning a buried claim) without necessarily correcting any grammatical error.

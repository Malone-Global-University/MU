الوَسَاطَة وَالتَّرْجَمَة وَنَقْل المَعْنَىChapter 4: Mediation and Translation

Continuing Unit I · Speak and Write Arabic: Mastery, Precision, Rhetoric, and Specialized Expression

Chapter Overview

Chapter 3 taught هَنَاء to phrase the same argument at two densities for two readers of the same language. This chapter asks her to carry meaning across a harder boundary: from one language into another, and from one register into a different one, for a reader who cannot access the original directly. Her office has begun receiving requests to summarize English source material in Arabic for local partners, and to render Arabic decisions in plain English for an international office. Mediation is not word-for-word translation. It is a purposeful act of selecting, adapting, and re-expressing meaning so that a specific reader, in a specific context, understands what they need to understand. This chapter teaches functional translation, paraphrase across registers, and summary as tools of mediation, building directly on the density control from Chapter 3 and the argument architecture from Chapter 2. No new clause grammar, case system, or verb form is introduced; the task is judgment about what to keep, what to compress, what to expand, and what to leave out.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Distinguish functional (meaning-based) translation from literal, word-for-word rendering, and explain why literal translation often fails to mediate meaning accurately.
  2. Paraphrase a source passage in Arabic to fit a different register or audience without changing its core content.
  3. Produce a written summary in Arabic of a short English-language source, and a written summary in English of a short Arabic-language source.
  4. Identify what a mediation task requires the mediator to add, omit, or restructure for the target reader, and justify each choice.
  5. Transfer register — compressing or expanding, formalizing or informalizing — as part of a mediation task, applying the density tools from Chapter 3.
  6. Evaluate a mediated text for accuracy, completeness of essential meaning, and appropriateness to its intended reader.

Opening Communication Scenario

A regional partner office has sent هَنَاء's team a short English memo about a policy change, and her supervisor asks her to produce two things: a concise Arabic summary for local staff who need the practical outcome quickly, and a short spoken briefing in Arabic for a meeting where questions may come up. Separately, the director has asked for a plain-English summary of an Arabic internal decision, to send to colleagues abroad who read no Arabic. "Don't translate every sentence," her supervisor tells her. "Tell each reader what they actually need, in language they can use." هَنَاء must decide, for each audience, what belongs in the mediated version and what can be safely left out.

  1. You will compare a literal translation with a functional, meaning-based translation of the same passage.
  2. You will practice paraphrasing Arabic content into a different register for a different reader.
  3. You will summarize a short source text in the other language, keeping only essential meaning.
  4. You will prepare a brief spoken mediation for a meeting context.
  5. You will evaluate a mediated text against its stated audience and purpose.

صَوْت الوَسَاطَة الشَّفَهِيَّة — The Sound of Mediated Speech

The Sound of Mediated Speech

Sound and Style — Pacing a spoken mediation for a live audience

A spoken mediation — briefing a room on a source they cannot read themselves — calls for slower, more clearly grouped phrasing than either the dense or expanded written styles practiced in Chapter 3. Because listeners cannot re-read a sentence, a mediator typically signals structure aloud with clear phrase boundaries, brief pauses before key terms, and a steady pace that leaves room for questions. This recycles the phrase-grouping, pause control, and pacing awareness developed through Volume IV and Chapters 1 through 3, applied now to a context where the listener depends entirely on the speaker's clarity.

No new pronunciation target is introduced. Practice reading a short mediated summary aloud as if briefing colleagues who have not seen the source, pausing clearly at each new piece of information.

الوَسَاطَة وَإِعَادَة الصِّيَاغَة وَنَقْل السِّجِلّ — Mediation, Paraphrase, and Register Transfer

Mediation, Paraphrase, and Register Transfer

This chapter's language development is a judgment skill, not a new structural system. No new clause type, case rule, or mood environment is introduced. The target is selecting, compressing, expanding, and re-expressing meaning across languages and registers using systems already consolidated in Volume IV and the density tools from Chapter 3.

The Mediation Decision Sequence

تَحْدِيد الجُمْهُور ← تَحْدِيد المَعْنَى الجَوْهَرِيّ ← اخْتِيَار السِّجِلّ وَالكَثَافَة ← إِعَادَة الصِّيَاغَة

identify the audience → identify the essential meaning → choose register and density → re-express

هَل يَحْتَاج القَارِئ إِلَى كُلّ التَّفَاصِيل، أَم إِلَى النَّتِيجَة العَمَلِيَّة فَقَط؟

Does the reader need every detail, or only the practical outcome?

Mediation always begins with the reader, not the source text. A mediator asks what this particular reader needs in order to act or understand, then decides how much of the source to keep, compress, expand, or omit. The four-step sequence above is not new grammar; it is a decision procedure that governs how you apply grammar, vocabulary, and density choices you have already mastered.

Functional Translation versus Literal Translation

A literal, word-for-word rendering often distorts meaning, especially across idiom, collocation, and register. Functional translation asks what the source sentence is doing communicatively, then re-expresses that function naturally in the target language.

  • حَرْفِيًّا: "نَحْنُ نَأْسَف لِأَيّ إِزْعَاج قَد يَنْتُج عَن هَذَا."Literal rendering of an English apology formula, sounding stiff and overtranslated in Arabic.
  • وَظِيفِيًّا: "نَعْتَذِر عَن أَيّ إِزْعَاج."Functional rendering: a natural, idiomatic Arabic apology that performs the same communicative act.

Paraphrase Across Register

Paraphrasing for a different register applies the density and expansion tools from Chapter 3 to a translation or mediation context. A formal source passage may need to be paraphrased into a more accessible register for a general reader, or vice versa.

  • أَصْلِيّ (رَسْمِيّ): يُشْتَرَط استِيفَاء الطَّلَب لِجَمِيع المَعَايِير المُنَصُوص عَلَيْهَا قَبْل النَّظَر فِيه.Original, formal register: "The request is required to satisfy all the stipulated criteria before it may be considered."
  • مُعَاد صِيَاغَتُه (مُبَسَّط): لَن نَنْظُر فِي طَلَبِكُم إِلَّا بَعْد استِيفَاء كُلّ الشُّرُوط.Paraphrased, plainer register: "We won't consider your request until all the conditions are met."
Grammar Note — Mediation is judgment layered on existing systems

Producing a functional translation, a register-shifted paraphrase, or a summary does not require any grammar beyond the clause, verbal-noun, and nominalization systems already consolidated by the end of Volume IV, together with the density-control tools from Chapter 3. What is new in this chapter is the deliberate judgment of what to keep, compress, omit, or add for a specific reader — a rhetorical and interpretive skill built on the same foundation, exactly as Chapters 1 through 3 treated register, argument, and density as organizational skills rather than new grammar.

Summary as Selective Compression

A summary is not simply a shorter version of the source; it is a selection of the essential meaning for a stated purpose. The pair below shows a short English source memo and a mediated Arabic summary that keeps only what local staff need to act.

Source — English policy memo (excerpt)

"Effective the first of next month, all expense reports must be submitted through the new online portal rather than by email. Reports submitted by email after this date will be returned unprocessed. Staff who have not yet received portal credentials should contact IT support before the transition date. A short training session will be offered twice during the coming two weeks for anyone who would like guidance on the new system."

Mediated Arabic Summary — For local staff

اِبْتِدَاءً مِن الشَّهْر القَادِم، يَجِب تَقْدِيم تَقَارِير المَصَارِيف عَبْر البَوَّابَة الإِلِكْتْرُونِيَّة الجَدِيدَة فَقَط. مَن لَم يَحْصُل عَلَى بَيَانَات الدُّخُول، فَلْيَتَوَاصَل مَع الدَّعْم التِّقْنِيّ قَبْل ذَلِك التَّارِيخ. تُتَاح جَلْسَتَا تَدْرِيب لِمَن يَرْغَب فِي المُسَاعَدَة.

Starting next month, expense reports must be submitted through the new online portal only. Anyone who hasn't received login credentials should contact IT support before that date. Two training sessions are available for anyone who would like help.

The mediated summary omits the detail about returned email submissions, since local staff primarily need the new procedure and the support contact, not the consequence for noncompliance. This is a deliberate omission, not a translation error — a mediator decides what the target reader needs, not what the source happened to include.

Heritage Reference — Mediation as an old craft in Arabic intellectual tradition

The transmission of meaning across languages, dialects, and registers has a long history in Arabic scholarship, from early translation movements that carried scientific and philosophical works into Arabic to layered traditions of commentary and paraphrase. This chapter references that heritage only as background awareness, without requiring or assessing independent reading of Classical or historical translation texts. Chapter 13 returns to heritage texts directly, with clear boundaries; this chapter's reference remains receptive and analytical only.

حِوَار نَمُوذَجِيّ — Model Exchange

Model Exchange: Deciding What the Reader Needs

Setting: هَنَاء checks with her supervisor about how much of the English source memo to include in the Arabic summary for local staff.

هَنَاء: هَل أُتَرْجِم المُذَكِّرَة كَامِلَة، أَم أُلَخِّصُهَا؟Should I translate the memo in full, or summarize it?
المُشْرِفَة: لَخِّصِيهَا. المُوَظَّفُون يَحْتَاجُون إِلَى الإِجْرَاء الجَدِيد وَجِهَة الدَّعْم، لَا كُلّ التَّفَاصِيل الإِدَارِيَّة.Summarize it. Staff need the new procedure and the support contact, not every administrative detail.
هَنَاء: وَمَاذَا عَن تَفْصِيل رَفْض الطَّلَبَات المُرْسَلَة بِالبَرِيد؟What about the detail about rejecting requests sent by email?
المُشْرِفَة: اُحْذِفِيهِ الآن. يُمْكِنُنَا إِضَافَتُه لَاحِقًا إِذَا استَمَرَّ أَحَدٌ فِي إِرْسَال طَلَبَاتِه بِالبَرِيد.Leave it out for now. We can add it later if anyone keeps sending requests by email.
هَنَاء: إِذَن، الأَمَانَة هُنَا لَيْسَت فِي نَقْل كُلّ جُمْلَة، بَل فِي إِيصَال مَا يَحْتَاجُونَه فِعْلًا؟So fidelity here isn't about carrying every sentence, but about conveying what they actually need?
المُشْرِفَة: بِالضَّبْط. الوَسَاطَة الجَيِّدَة تَخْدِم القَارِئ، لَا النَّصّ الأَصْلِيّ.Exactly. Good mediation serves the reader, not the original text.

Note how the supervisor separates fidelity of essential meaning from completeness of every sentence — the central distinction of this chapter.

Writing Workshop

Produce a functional translation, a register-shifted paraphrase, and a summary of the same short source, for three different readers.

  1. Step 1 — Choose or receive a short source text. Use a short English paragraph (workplace, academic, or public-information content) provided by your instructor, or write a short formal Arabic paragraph of your own to translate into English.
  2. Step 2 — Draft a functional translation. Render the source into the other language, prioritizing natural, idiomatic meaning over word-for-word correspondence.
  3. Step 3 — Draft a register-shifted paraphrase. Rewrite your translation in a different register for a different reader, applying the density and expansion tools from Chapter 3.
  4. Step 4 — Draft a summary. Compress the source to only its essential meaning for a reader who needs the outcome quickly, and be ready to justify what you omitted.
  5. Step 5 — State your audience for each version. Write one sentence per version identifying its intended reader and why that treatment suits them.
  6. Review your three versions against the checklist below.

قَائِمَة المُرَاجَعَة — Checklist:

  • The functional translation avoids literal, word-for-word phrasing where it would sound unnatural.
  • The register-shifted paraphrase changes density or formality without changing essential meaning.
  • The summary preserves only essential meaning and clearly omits nonessential detail.
  • Every omission is a deliberate choice you can justify by audience need, not an accidental loss of meaning.
  • No untaught grammar or vocabulary domain was required.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Mediation as a valued professional skill

In many Arabic-speaking professional, diplomatic, and institutional settings, the ability to mediate meaning accurately between languages, registers, and audiences is treated as a distinct and valued professional skill, separate from bilingual fluency alone. A capable bilingual speaker is not automatically a capable mediator; mediation requires judgment about audience need that goes beyond language competence. Learners preparing for professional contexts should recognize mediation as a skill to be developed deliberately, not assumed automatically from language ability.

Regional Variation — Expectations for summary length and detail

How much detail readers expect in a mediated summary — a brief bulletin versus a fuller digest — varies across institutions, media outlets, and professional cultures within the Arabic-speaking world. Some organizations favor very brief bulletin-style mediation; others expect fuller contextual summaries even for routine updates. Learners preparing for a specific professional or regional context should observe that context's conventions for mediated content rather than assuming one universal standard of length or detail.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Literal translation that distorts natural meaning

Error: rendering idiomatic or formulaic source language word for word, producing an Arabic or English sentence that is grammatically possible but unnatural or misleading.

Correction: identify the communicative function of the source sentence first, then re-express that function naturally in the target language, even if the wording differs substantially.

Common Mistake — Treating summary as simple deletion

Error: shortening a source text by cutting sentences at random or in source order, without judging what the target reader actually needs.

Correction: identify the essential meaning for the stated reader first, then build the summary from that meaning rather than trimming the source mechanically.

Common Mistake — Losing fidelity while shifting register

Error: changing the register or density of a mediated text so much that the core facts, obligations, or claims of the source are altered or overstated.

Correction: apply Chapter 3's density and expansion tools to phrasing only; check every register-shifted version against the source to confirm essential meaning has not changed.

Answer Key

Listening Practice

  • Essential meaning: the annual report deadline has been pushed back two weeks because some branches' data has been delayed; the required format and content stay the same.
  • Omittable detail for a brief spoken mediation: the specific reason (delayed branch data) could be omitted if the listener only needs the new deadline.
  • Nominalized phrase: تَأَخُّر وُصُول البَيَانَات; expanded equivalent: عِنْدَمَا تَتَأَخَّر البَيَانَات فِي الوُصُول.
  • Sample thirty-second mediation: تَأَجَّل مَوْعِد التَّقَارِير السَّنَوِيَّة أُسْبُوعَيْن، وَالشَّكْل وَالمُحْتَوَى كَمَا هُمَا.

Reading Practice — Comprehension questions

  1. Mediation (1) preserves the fact that three proposals were reviewed, the cost concern about Proposal A, and the one-month timeline; Mediation (2) preserves only the timeline.
  2. The detail that all three proposals were judged technically sound appears in neither mediation; a mediator might omit it because it does not change what either audience needs to do or expect next.
  3. Nominalized phrase: تَجْدِيد المَرْكَز المُجْتَمَعِيّ; expanded equivalent: عِنْدَمَا يُجَدَّد المَرْكَز المُجْتَمَعِيّ.
  4. Mediation (2) suits a brief public notice board, since it gives only the timeline; Mediation (1) suits an internal staff update, since staff likely need to know a cost concern exists.

Chapter Review — Model responses

Section A: A literal translation reproduces source wording closely even when unnatural; a functional translation re-expresses the same communicative act naturally in the target language. An accurate example pair showing this contrast is acceptable. Any accurately identified deliberate omission with a plausible audience-based justification is acceptable.

Section B: Open-ended production; any accurate short passage, its natural functional translation, and a summary that preserves essential meaning while omitting nonessential detail are acceptable.

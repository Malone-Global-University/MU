المَشْرُوع المُتَخَصِّصChapter 19: The Specialized Project

Continuing Unit IV · Speak and Write Arabic: Mastery, Precision, Rhetoric, and Specialized Expression
Unit IV Continues

Chapter Overview

Chapter 18 gave هَنَاء a working method for carrying one message across four audiences without contradiction. The council has now asked for something larger: a bounded specialized dossier on the neighborhood renewal project's water-and-sanitation component, written for the technical panel, drawing on the engineer's figures, a public-health note, and the original committee report, and presented at the next council session. This is no longer a single adapted passage but a sustained piece of work: a specialized project that selects a bounded field, integrates more than one source without blurring where each claim comes from, and organizes the result for oral presentation as well as written submission. This chapter develops vocabulary and method for exactly that: naming a specialization, integrating sources responsibly, and designing a presentation that serves both the page and the room. No new grammar system is introduced; the project is carried entirely by nominalization, content clauses, contrast connectors, passive voice, and source attribution already at mastery or consolidated status, together with the audience-adaptation method from Chapter 18.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Use specialized-project vocabulary to name a bounded field, a source, and the act of integrating multiple sources into one coherent text.
  2. Distinguish a claim drawn from a named source from the writer's own synthesis or conclusion, and mark that distinction explicitly in Arabic.
  3. Integrate two or three short sources into a single paragraph without contradiction, repetition, or unattributed borrowing.
  4. Select and label visual or structural elements (headings, a short table, a closing recommendation) appropriate to a bounded specialized dossier.
  5. Plan a short oral presentation of a specialized project that reuses, rather than rewrites, the written dossier's content.
  6. Demonstrate independent command of specialized-project vocabulary and method at the level authorized for this chapter, applied entirely through grammar already at mastery or consolidated status.

Opening Communication Scenario

The council has approved the renewal project's cost-benefit case and now wants a single specialized dossier on its water-and-sanitation component, to be submitted in writing and presented briefly at the next session. هَنَاء has three sources in hand: the technical committee's engineering figures, a short public-health note from a visiting specialist, and the original committee report سَامِر edited in Chapter 17. Her task is not to write one more adapted version of an existing passage, as in Chapter 18, but to select a bounded specialization, integrate all three sources into one coherent text that keeps each source's contribution clear, and design both a written dossier and a short spoken presentation from the same underlying material.

  1. You will learn vocabulary for naming a specialization, a source, and the act of integrating sources.
  2. You will study a method for combining claims from more than one source into a single paragraph without blurring attribution.
  3. You will study a method for designing a bounded dossier's structure and a matching short presentation.
  4. You will read and listen to a model exchange as هَنَاء and سَامِر plan the dossier together.
  5. You will integrate two short sources of your own into one paragraph and outline a short presentation based on it.

إِلْقَاء العَرْض المُتَخَصِّص — Delivery for Presentation

Delivery for Presentation: Pacing a Specialized Talk

Sound and Delivery — Signaling structure aloud

No new sound or pausing pattern is introduced in this chapter. The relevant skill is using pace and pause to signal a dossier's structure when presenting it aloud, not phonology: a brief pause before naming a new section (the field, the sources, the recommendation) helps a listening audience track where the presentation is in its structure, the way a heading does for a reader. When moving from reporting a source's claim to offering the writer's own synthesis, a slightly slower pace and a short pause beforehand mark the shift from someone else's finding to the presenter's own conclusion. Practice reading the model paragraph in this chapter's Reading Practice section aloud, pausing briefly before each shift from one source to the next and before the closing recommendation.

دَمْج المَصَادِر بِلَا قَوَاعِد جَدِيدَة — Integrating Sources Without New Grammar

Rhetorical Focus: Integrating Sources Without New Grammar

This chapter introduces no new grammar system. Source attribution, content clauses, contrast connectors, nominalization, and passive voice remain at the status reached earlier in the volume, and the audience-adaptation method from Chapter 18 remains available for shaping the dossier's public-facing summary. What is new is a method for combining more than one source's claims into a single paragraph, keeping each source's contribution traceable and clearly separating reported claims from the writer's own synthesis.

Integration Move: Two Sources, One Point, Kept Distinct

وِفْقًا لِـ [مصدر ١]، ... ؛ وَفِي المُقَابِل، يُشِير [مصدر ٢] إِلَى أَنَّ ...

this pattern reuses source attribution and the contrast connector فِي المُقَابِل, both already consolidated, to report two sources on the same point without merging their claims into one voice; each clause remains attributed to its own source

وِفْقًا لِتَقْرِير اللَّجْنَة الفَنِّيَّة، اِرْتَفَعَت نِسْبَة العَائِلَات المُسْتَفِيدَة إِلَى ٤٧٪؛ وَفِي المُقَابِل، تُشِير المُلَاحَظَة الصِّحِّيَّة إِلَى أَنَّ عَدَد حَالَات التَّلَوُّث اِنْخَفَض بِنِسْبَة أَقَلّ مِمَّا كَانَ مُتَوَقَّعًا.

"According to the technical committee's report, the proportion of benefiting families rose to 47%; in contrast, the health note indicates that the number of contamination cases decreased by a smaller margin than expected."

Integration Move: Moving from Sources to the Writer's Own Synthesis

وَبِنَاءً عَلَى مَا سَبَق، يُمْكِن القَوْل إِنَّ ... [تركيب الكاتب]

this pattern marks a visible boundary between reported source claims and the writer's own conclusion; the phrase وَبِنَاءً عَلَى مَا سَبَق reuses cause/result connection already consolidated, and the impersonal يُمْكِن القَوْل إِنَّ frames what follows as the writer's synthesis rather than a fourth source

وَبِنَاءً عَلَى مَا سَبَق، يُمْكِن القَوْل إِنَّ المَكَاسِب الاجْتِمَاعِيَّة لِلْمَشْرُوع تَفُوق حَتَّى الآن مَخَاوِفَه الصِّحِّيَّة الجُزْئِيَّة، دُون أَن يُلْغِي ذَلِكَ الحَاجَة إِلَى مُتَابَعَة إِضَافِيَّة.

"Based on the foregoing, it can be said that the project's social gains so far outweigh its partial health concerns, without this eliminating the need for additional follow-up."

Both moves reuse attribution, contrast, and cause/result connectors already at mastery or consolidated status; neither requires new grammar. Learners should notice that source integration is a matter of sequencing and labeling — which source is named first, how a contrast is marked, and where the writer's own voice visibly begins — rather than a new sentence type.

  • هُنَاك تَعَارُض بَيْن المَصْدَرَيْن فِي هَذِه النُّقْطَة؛ يَجِب ذِكْر الاِثْنَيْن بَدَلًا مِن اِخْتِيَار وَاحِد فَقَط.There is a conflict between the two sources on this point; both must be mentioned rather than choosing only one.naming a source conflict and the required response
  • هَذِه الجُمْلَة لَا تُسْنَد إِلَى أَيّ مَصْدَر؛ يَجِب تَحْدِيد مَصْدَرِهَا أَو حَذْفُهَا.This sentence is not attributed to any source; its source must be identified, or it must be removed.flagging unattributed content in a specialized draft
  • هَذِه الفِقْرَة تَخْلِط بَيْن رَأْي الكَاتِب وَنَتِيجَة التَّقْرِير الفَنِّيّ؛ يَجِب الفَصْل بَيْنَهُمَا بِوُضُوح.This paragraph mixes the writer's opinion with the technical report's finding; the two must be clearly separated.diagnosing a boundary failure between source claim and synthesis
Grammar Note — Integration is sequencing and labeling, not new grammar

It might seem that combining three sources into one dossier calls for more advanced sentence structures than a single-source summary. It does not. Every integration move in this section works entirely with attribution, contrast connectors, cause/result connectors, and nominalization already active by this point in the volume. What a specialized project adds is method: deciding which source to name first, how to mark agreement or conflict between sources, and where the writer's own synthesis begins, so that a reader or listener can always tell whose claim they are hearing.

حِوَار نَمُوذَجِيّ — Model Exchange

Model Exchange: Planning the Dossier

Setting: The residents committee office. هَنَاء and سَامِر are planning the specialized dossier on the water-and-sanitation component before the next council session.

سَامِر: عِنْدَنَا ثَلَاثَة مَصَادِر: تَقْرِير اللَّجْنَة الفَنِّيَّة، وَمُلَاحَظَة الأَخْصَائِيّ الصِّحِّيّ، وَتَقْرِيرِي الأَصْلِيّ. كَيْف نَبْدَأ؟We have three sources: the technical committee's report, the health specialist's note, and my original report. How do we begin?
هَنَاء: أَوَّلًا، نُحَدِّد نِطَاق المَشْرُوع: قِسْم المِيَاه وَالصَّرْف الصِّحِّيّ فَقَط، لَا المَشْرُوع كُلّه. هَذَا يَحْمِينَا مِن تَوْسِيع المَجَال بِلَا دَاعٍ.First, we define the project's scope: only the water-and-sanitation component, not the whole project. This protects us from expanding the field unnecessarily.
سَامِر: وَإِذَا اِخْتَلَف المَصْدَرَان فِي نُقْطَة مُعَيَّنَة؟And if the two sources disagree on a particular point?
هَنَاء: نَذْكُر التَّعَارُض بِوُضُوح، وَنُسْنِد كُلّ رَأْي إِلَى مَصْدَرِه، وَلَا نَخْتَار أَحَدَهُمَا مِن دُون تَبْرِير. هَذَا أَدَقّ مِن إِخْفَاء الاِخْتِلَاف.We state the conflict clearly, attribute each view to its source, and don't choose one without justification. This is more precise than hiding the disagreement.
سَامِر: وَمَتَى نَكْتُب رَأْيَنَا نَحْنُ، لَا رَأْي المَصَادِر؟And when do we write our own opinion, not the sources' opinion?
هَنَاء: فِي القِسْم الأَخِير فَقَط: "وَبِنَاءً عَلَى مَا سَبَق، يُمْكِن القَوْل إِنَّ..." هُنَاك تَرْكِيبنَا نَحْنُ، بَعْد أَن يَكُون القَارِئ قَد رَأَى كُلّ مَصْدَر بِوُضُوح.Only in the final section: "Based on the foregoing, it can be said that..." That is our own synthesis, after the reader has clearly seen every source.
سَامِر: وَلِلْعَرْض الشَّفَهِيّ فِي الجَلْسَة، هَل نَكْتُب نَصًّا مُنْفَصِلًا؟And for the oral presentation at the session, do we write a separate text?
هَنَاء: لَا. نُصَمِّم العَرْض مِن نَفْس التَّقْرِير: عُنْوَان لِكُلّ قِسْم، وَجَدْوَل صَغِير بَدَل الأَرْقَام الطَّوِيلَة، وَالتَّوْصِيَة فِي النِّهَايَة، تَمَامًا كَمَا فِي المَلَفّ المَكْتُوب.No. We design the presentation from the same report: a heading for each section, a small table instead of long figures, and the recommendation at the end, exactly as in the written dossier.

Notice that هَنَاء never lets a claim stand without a named source, and reserves the writer's own synthesis for one clearly marked closing move. The presentation is designed, not rewritten, from the same underlying dossier.

Speaking Practice

تَدْرِيب مُوَجَّه — Controlled practice: Naming the source

Your instructor or partner will read a short claim aloud without naming its source. Respond with one sentence asking which source it comes from, using a question built on إِسْنَاد or مَصْدَر, and one sentence stating why attribution matters for that claim.

مُهِمَّة تَوَاصُل — Communicative task: Two-source relay

Working in a small group, one learner states a fact attributed to a first invented source. The next learner adds a related but different fact attributed to a second invented source, using فِي المُقَابِل if the facts contrast. The last learner closes with a one-sentence synthesis beginning وَبِنَاءً عَلَى مَا سَبَق. Compare how clearly each speaker's contribution stayed attributed to its source.

لِلْمُتَعَلِّمِينَ المُسْتَقِلِّينَ — Solo alternative

Choose two short facts about a topic you know well, treat them as if they came from two different sources, and record yourself stating each fact with its attribution, followed by a one-sentence synthesis. Listen back and check that a listener could tell where each fact came from.

Writing Workshop

Below are two short, separate source notes on a different part of the renewal project. Integrate them into a single paragraph following the model in the Reading Practice section, then design a short presentation outline from your paragraph.

مَصْدَرَان لِلدَّمْج — Two Sources to Integrate
الغَرَض / Purpose: فِقْرَة تَدْمُج مَصْدَرَيْن حَوْل إِضَاءَة الشَّوَارِع فِي الحَيّ

المَصْدَر ١ (تَقْرِير اللَّجْنَة الفَنِّيَّة): «اِرْتَفَع عَدَد الشَّوَارِع المُضَاءَة مِن ١٢ إِلَى ٣٠ شَارِعًا خِلَال عَامَيْن.» المَصْدَر ٢ (مُلَاحَظَة سُكَّان الحَيّ): «يَرَى بَعْض السُّكَّان أَنَّ الإِضَاءَة الجَدِيدَة قَوِيَّة جِدًّا بِالقُرْب مِن المَنَازِل السَّكَنِيَّة.»

Source 1 (Technical committee report): "The number of lit streets rose from 12 to 30 over two years." Source 2 (Residents' note): "Some residents feel the new lighting is too strong near residential homes."

  1. Step 1 — State the scope. Write one sentence naming the bounded topic your paragraph covers (street lighting), before citing either source.
  2. Step 2 — Report the first source. Write one sentence attributed to Source 1, using وِفْقًا لِـ or a similar attribution phrase.
  3. Step 3 — Report the second source, marking the relationship. Write one sentence attributed to Source 2, using فِي المُقَابِل if the two sources are in tension, or a different connector if they agree.
  4. Step 4 — Add your own synthesis. Write one sentence beginning وَبِنَاءً عَلَى مَا سَبَق that offers your own conclusion, clearly distinct from either source's claim.
  5. Step 5 — Outline the presentation. List, in order, the headings you would use to present this same content aloud (for example: scope, first finding, second finding, synthesis, recommendation), reusing the paragraph's content rather than rewriting it.
  6. Review your work against the checklist below.

قَائِمَة المُرَاجَعَة — Checklist:

  • The paragraph states the bounded scope before citing any source.
  • Each source's claim is clearly attributed and kept distinct from the other source's claim.
  • Any conflict or contrast between the two sources is named explicitly, not hidden or resolved by silently choosing one source.
  • The writer's own synthesis is clearly marked and appears only after both sources have been reported.
  • The presentation outline reuses the paragraph's content and order rather than introducing new material.
  • No grammar, case, mood, or clause system beyond what is already at mastery or consolidated status appears anywhere in the paragraph or the outline.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Bounded specialized dossiers in community and institutional life

In many Arabic-speaking institutional and community settings, a specialized dossier that clearly bounds its scope, names each source it draws on, and closes with an explicit recommendation is commonly read as more trustworthy and easier to act on than a longer, unbounded report that tries to cover an entire project at once. Committees, municipal bodies, and professional panels often prefer receiving one narrowly scoped, well-sourced document over a broader document whose claims are harder to trace back to their origin.

Regional Variation — Citation and source-naming conventions vary by institution

How formally a source must be cited — a full bibliographic reference, a short in-text mention, or an oral acknowledgment during a presentation — varies by country, sector, and institutional convention. This chapter models a general, widely usable pattern of naming sources clearly in running prose rather than a single universal citation standard.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Blurring the boundary between source and synthesis

Error: presenting the writer's own conclusion as if it were a direct finding of one of the sources, or presenting a source's finding as if it were the writer's own original insight.

Correction: attribute every source claim explicitly, and mark the writer's own synthesis with a clear phrase such as وَبِنَاءً عَلَى مَا سَبَق, يُمْكِن القَوْل إِنَّ, placed only after the relevant sources have been reported.

Common Mistake — Hiding a conflict between sources

Error: silently choosing one source's figure or claim over another's without acknowledging that they disagree.

Correction: name the conflict explicitly, attribute each view to its own source, and let the writer's synthesis address the conflict rather than erase it.

Common Mistake — Rewriting the presentation as a separate document

Error: drafting the oral presentation as an entirely new text, unrelated in wording and order to the written dossier it is meant to represent.

Correction: design the presentation directly from the dossier's own structure and content, using headings, a compact visual element, and the same closing recommendation, consistent with this chapter's model.

Listening Practice

  • The first sentence is attributed to the technical committee's report; it gives the figure of a rise to 47%.
  • The second sentence is attributed to the health note; it indicates that contamination cases decreased, though by less than expected.
  • The third sentence is the writer's own synthesis; it is marked by the phrase وَبِنَاءً عَلَى مَا سَبَق, يُمْكِن القَوْل إِنَّ.

Reading Practice — Comprehension questions

  1. The first sentence, naming the water-and-sanitation component as the section's scope, defines the boundary; it appears first so that no source claim is read before the reader knows what the dossier does and does not cover.
  2. The claim about the rise from 32% to 47% is attributed to the technical committee's report, marked by وِفْقًا لِتَقْرِير اللَّجْنَة الفَنِّيَّة; the claim about the smaller-than-expected drop in contamination cases is attributed to the health specialist's note, marked by وَفِي المُقَابِل، تُشِير مُلَاحَظَة الأَخْصَائِيّ الصِّحِّيّ.
  3. The sentence beginning وَبِنَاءً عَلَى مَا سَبَق، يُمْكِن القَوْل إِنَّ is the writer's own synthesis; the reader recognizes it because it follows both sources rather than naming either one, and the phrase itself signals a conclusion rather than a citation.
  4. The recommendation is the final sentence proposing quarterly health follow-up; unlike a summary, which would restate what came before, the recommendation proposes a specific future action not stated by either source.

Chapter Review — Model responses

Section A: دَمْج المَصَادِر names the general act of combining more than one source into a single coherent text; إِسْنَاد names the specific move of marking which source a given claim came from; تَرْكِيب المَصَادِر names the writer's own conclusion built from the combined sources, distinct from any single source's claim.

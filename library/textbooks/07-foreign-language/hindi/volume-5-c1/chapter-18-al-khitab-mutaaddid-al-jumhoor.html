الخِطَاب مُتَعَدِّد الجُمْهُورChapter 18: Multi-Audience Communication

Continuing Unit IV · Speak and Write Arabic: Mastery, Precision, Rhetoric, and Specialized Expression
Unit IV Continues

Chapter Overview

Chapter 17 left هَنَاء and سَامِر with a single, polished submission: one report, edited for clarity, cohesion, and consistent formal register, ready to go back to the council. But the council's follow-up review is now generating interest beyond the council chamber itself. A local reporter wants a short public statement. An engineer on the review panel wants the technical basis for the project's cost figures explained precisely. A group of newer committee volunteers, still building their command of institutional Arabic, need the same information explained more plainly so they can support the submission in discussion. And the council itself still needs the original formal report. هَنَاء now faces a new problem: the same underlying facts and the same argument must reach four different audiences — public, expert, learner, and professional — without contradicting itself and without being rewritten from scratch each time. This chapter develops vocabulary and method for audience adaptation: identifying what a given audience needs, keeping the underlying facts and stance constant, and adjusting vocabulary density, explicitness, and register accordingly. No new grammar system is introduced; every adaptation in this chapter is carried by nominalization, content clauses, contrast connectors, and passive voice at mastery status since Chapter 5, together with the editing vocabulary and method from Chapter 17.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Use audience-adaptation vocabulary to identify what a public, expert, learner, or professional audience needs from the same core message.
  2. Distinguish adapting a message (adjusting vocabulary density, explicitness, and register) from changing its content (altering the facts, claims, or stance).
  3. Rewrite a technical or institutional passage for a general public audience without oversimplifying to the point of inaccuracy.
  4. Rewrite the same passage for an expert audience, restoring precision and specialized vocabulary the public version omitted.
  5. Identify and supply the additional scaffolding a learner audience needs (glosses, restated terms, simpler connectors) without lowering the accuracy of the content.
  6. Demonstrate independent command of audience-adaptation vocabulary and method at the level authorized for this chapter, applied entirely through grammar already at mastery status.

Opening Communication Scenario

The edited report from Chapter 17 has done its job: the council's technical panel is satisfied, and word of the neighborhood renewal project's renewed cost-benefit case has reached beyond the council chamber. هَنَاء now needs four versions of essentially one message: a short public statement for a local reporter, a precise technical note for the panel's engineer, a plain-language briefing for newer committee volunteers, and the polished formal report the council itself expects. The facts do not change — the same cost figures, the same benefit ratio, the same three-year track record — but what counts as a clear, appropriate, and persuasive version of those facts changes sharply from one audience to the next.

  1. You will learn vocabulary for naming audiences and the adaptation each one requires.
  2. You will study a method for producing a public-facing version of a technical claim.
  3. You will study a method for restoring precision when writing for an expert audience.
  4. You will read and listen to a model exchange as هَنَاء plans the four versions with سَامِر.
  5. You will adapt a single passage for two different audiences yourself and justify your choices.

إِلْقَاء عَبْر مُسْتَوَيَات مُخْتَلِفَة — Delivery Across Registers

Delivery Across Registers: One Voice, Four Audiences

Sound and Delivery — Matching pace and emphasis to audience

No new sound or pausing pattern is introduced in this chapter. The relevant skill is adjusting delivery, not phonology: the same speaker reading a public statement aloud should use a slower pace, clearer phrase grouping, and more emphatic stress on key facts than when reading a technical note to an expert, where a brisker, more even pace signals confidence and shared background knowledge. When reading aloud to a learner audience, pause slightly longer after any specialized term that receives a gloss, giving the added explanation room to land. Practice reading the same short passage aloud three times, at the three different paces this chapter describes, without changing a single word.

التَّكَيُّف بِلَا قَوَاعِد جَدِيدَة — Adapting Content Without New Grammar

Rhetorical Focus: Adapting Content Without New Grammar

This chapter introduces no new grammar system. Nominalization, content clauses, contrast connectors, and passive voice remain at the mastery status reached by Chapter 5, and the editorial vocabulary and method from Chapter 17 remain available for adjusting a text's shape. What is new is applying that stable grammar with a different vocabulary density and explicitness for each of four audiences, while keeping the underlying facts and stance identical across all four versions.

Adaptation Move: Public Audience — Trading Density for Clarity

[technical nominalized phrase, dense] → [shorter clause or simpler noun, same fact]

for a public audience, a technical nominalized construction can often be unpacked into a shorter, plainer noun or verb while preserving the exact fact; this is not a new grammar system, since simple nominal and verbal sentences have been productive since Volume I, but a targeted choice of the simpler available structure

مُتَخَصِّص: اِسْتِفَادَة العَائِلَات الوَاضِحَة مِن المَشْرُوع اِسْتَمَرَّت لِمُدَّة ثَلَاث سَنَوَات مُتَتَالِيَة. → عَامّ: اِسْتَفَادَت العَائِلَات مِن المَشْرُوع بِوُضُوح، وَهَذَا مُسْتَمِرّ مُنْذُ ثَلَاث سَنَوَات.

Expert: "The families' clear benefit from the project has continued for three consecutive years." Public: "Families have clearly benefited from the project, and this has continued for three years."

Adaptation Move: Expert Audience — Restoring Precision

[general public claim] → [precise figure, named method, or bounded claim]

for an expert audience, a general public statement is restored to precision by naming the specific figure, method, or scope the public version omitted; no new sentence-level grammar is required, only the reintroduction of specialized vocabulary and exact quantification within grammar already at mastery status

عَامّ: اِرْتَفَعَت نِسْبَة العَائِلَات المُسْتَفِيدَة هَذَا العَام. → مُتَخَصِّص: اِرْتَفَعَت نِسْبَة العَائِلَات المُسْتَفِيدَة مِن ٣٢٪ إِلَى ٤٧٪ وِفْقًا لِتَقْرِير اللَّجْنَة الفَنِّيَّة لِهَذَا العَام.

Public: "The proportion of benefiting families rose this year." Expert: "The proportion of benefiting families rose from 32% to 47% according to the technical committee's report for this year."

Both moves reuse nominalization, clause structure, and connectors already at mastery status; neither requires new grammar. Learners should notice that audience adaptation changes vocabulary density and explicitness far more often than it changes the grammar itself — and that a learner-facing version simply adds a gloss to the more technical wording rather than replacing it.

  • هَذِه الصِّيَاغَة مُنَاسِبَة لِلْجُمْهُور المُتَخَصِّص، لَكِنَّهَا تَحْتَاج إِلَى تَبْسِيط لَو وُجِّهَت إِلَى الجُمْهُور العَامّ.This phrasing is suitable for the specialized audience, but it needs simplification if directed toward the general audience.naming an audience mismatch and the required move
  • النُّسْخَة المُوَجَّهَة لِلْجُمْهُور العَامّ فَقَدَت دِقَّة الرَّقْم الأَصْلِيّ؛ يَجِب إِعَادَة الرَّقْم الدَّقِيق فِي النُّسْخَة المِهْنِيَّة.The version directed toward the general audience lost the precision of the original figure; the exact figure must be restored in the professional version.diagnosing lost precision and requiring its restoration for a different audience
  • هَذَا المُصْطَلَح يَحْتَاج إِلَى شَرْح إِضَافِيّ لَو ظَهَر فِي نُسْخَة مُوَجَّهَة لِلْجُمْهُور المُتَعَلِّم.This term needs an additional explanation if it appears in a version directed toward the learner audience.flagging a term that requires added scaffolding for a learner audience without removing it
Grammar Note — Adaptation changes vocabulary and scaffolding, not grammar

It might seem that writing for four different audiences calls for four different sets of grammatical tools. It does not. Every adaptation move in this section — trading density for clarity, restoring precision, adding a gloss — works entirely with structures already at mastery status by Chapter 5. What audience adaptation adds is judgment: the ability to identify what a specific audience already knows, what it needs stated plainly, and what it needs explained further, and the vocabulary to make that judgment explicit and consistent across four versions of one message.

حِوَار نَمُوذَجِيّ — Model Exchange

Model Exchange: Planning Four Versions

Setting: The residents committee office. هَنَاء and سَامِر are planning how to adapt the edited report for four different audiences.

سَامِر: أَرْسَلَت الصَّحَفِيَّة تَطْلُب بَيَانًا مُخْتَصَرًا. هَل نُرْسِل لَهَا التَّقْرِير نَفْسَه؟The reporter sent a request for a brief statement. Should we send her the same report?
هَنَاء: لَا. التَّقْرِير مُوَجَّه لِلْمَجْلِس، وَكَثَافَة مُفْرَدَاتِه التِّقْنِيَّة عَالِيَة جِدًّا لِلْجُمْهُور العَامّ. نَحْتَاج إِلَى تَبْسِيط الحَقَائِق نَفْسِهَا، لَا تَغْيِيرَهَا.No. The report is directed at the council, and its technical vocabulary density is much too high for the general audience. We need to simplify the same facts, not change them.
سَامِر: وَمَاذَا عَن المُهَنْدِس فِي اللَّجْنَة الفَنِّيَّة؟ هُوَ طَلَب الأَرْقَام الدَّقِيقَة.And what about the engineer on the technical committee? He asked for the exact figures.
هَنَاء: لِلْجُمْهُور المُتَخَصِّص، نُعِيد الدِّقَّة الَّتِي حَذَفْنَاهَا مِن النُّسْخَة العَامَّة: النِّسْبَة بِالضَّبْط، وَمَصْدَر الرَّقْم، وَاسْم التَّقْرِير الفَنِّيّ.For the specialized audience, we restore the precision we omitted from the public version: the exact proportion, the source of the figure, and the name of the technical report.
سَامِر: وَالمُتَطَوِّعُونَ الجُدُد؟ بَعْضُهُم لَا يَعْرِف مُصْطَلَحَات مِثْل "نِسْبَة العَائِلَات المُسْتَفِيدَة".And the new volunteers? Some of them don't know terms like "the proportion of benefiting families."
هَنَاء: لِلْجُمْهُور المُتَعَلِّم، نُبْقِي المُصْطَلَح، لَكِن نُضِيف شَرْحًا إِضَافِيًّا بَعْدَه مُبَاشَرَة، حَتَّى يَتَعَلَّمُوا المُصْطَلَح وَلَا يَتَوَقَّفُوا عِنْدَه.For the learner audience, we keep the term, but we add an additional explanation right after it, so they learn the term instead of getting stuck on it.
سَامِر: إِذَن الحَقَائِق تَبْقَى وَاحِدَة، لَكِن الصِّيَاغَة تَتَكَيَّف مَع كُلّ جُمْهُور.So the facts stay the same, but the phrasing adapts to each audience.
هَنَاء: بِالضَّبْط. وَهَذَا تَوْحِيد الرِّسَالَة: أَرْبَع نُسَخ مُخْتَلِفَة، لَكِن رِسَالَة وَاحِدَة لَا تَتَنَاقَض.Exactly. And this is message consistency: four different versions, but one message that does not contradict itself.

Notice that هَنَاء never changes what the facts are — only how explicit, dense, or scaffolded their phrasing becomes for each audience. Every adaptation names a specific audience and a specific adjustment, leaving the underlying claim intact.

Speaking Practice

تَدْرِيب مُوَجَّه — Controlled practice: Naming the audience

Your instructor or partner will read a short passage aloud. Respond with one sentence identifying which of the four audiences (public, expert, learner, professional) the passage is best suited for, and one sentence naming what would need to change for a different named audience.

مُهِمَّة تَوَاصُل — Communicative task: Four-audience relay

Working in a small group, one learner reads a technical sentence aloud. The next learner restates it for the general public. The next restates the public version for a learner audience, adding a gloss. The last restates it again for a professional audience, restoring institutional register. Compare how much the wording changed while the underlying fact stayed constant.

لِلْمُتَعَلِّمِينَ المُسْتَقِلِّينَ — Solo alternative

Record yourself stating one fact from your own life (for example, how long you have studied Arabic) in four different versions: as you would tell a close friend, a teacher, a stranger who does not study languages, and a fellow advanced learner. Compare the four recordings for vocabulary density and explicitness.

قَائِمَة المُرَاجَعَة — Checklist:

  • Both adapted versions preserve the same underlying facts and figures as the original, or clearly state where a figure has been intentionally omitted for a public audience.
  • At least one version simplifies vocabulary density appropriately for its audience.
  • At least one version restores or adds precision, or adds a gloss, appropriately for its audience.

  • No grammar, case, mood, or clause system beyond Chapter 5's consolidated status appears anywhere in either version or the comments.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — One message, several audiences, in public life

In many Arabic-speaking public and institutional settings, a single civic or organizational matter routinely produces several parallel texts: a press statement for general media, a technical annex for specialists, an internal briefing for staff or volunteers, and a formal submission for the responsible body. Preparing all four from one underlying position, rather than treating each as a separate improvisation, is commonly seen as a sign of a well-organized and trustworthy communicator, particularly in community and institutional contexts where the same audience may later compare the versions.

Regional Variation — Expectations for audience adaptation vary by institution

How explicitly institutions separate public, expert, learner, and professional communication — whether through distinct written documents, oral briefings, or a single flexible spokesperson — varies by country, sector, and organizational culture. This chapter models one common practical pattern rather than a single universal standard for public communication.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Changing the facts instead of the phrasing

Error: altering a figure, softening a claim, or adding an unsupported detail while adapting a message for a new audience, rather than adjusting only vocabulary density and explicitness.

Correction: keep the underlying facts, figures, and stance identical across all audience versions; adjust only how explicit, dense, or scaffolded the phrasing is.

Common Mistake — Oversimplifying past accuracy

Error: simplifying a public-facing version so far that the remaining claim becomes misleading or loses the qualification it needs (for example, dropping "over three years" entirely rather than keeping a simplified time reference).

Correction: simplify vocabulary and structure, but keep every fact that changes the meaning of the claim, even in plainer wording.

Common Mistake — Treating the learner version as a lesser version

Error: assuming a learner-facing version should contain less information or lower-quality content than other audiences receive.

Correction: keep the same content and specialized terms for the learner audience, and add explanation rather than removing substance, consistent with this chapter's model.

You learned to identify what public, expert, learner, and professional audiences each need from the same underlying message, and to adapt a passage by adjusting vocabulary density, restoring or adding precision, and supplying additional explanation, without changing the facts or stance the message carries. No new grammar system was introduced; nominalization, content clauses, contrast connectors, and passive voice, all at mastery status since Chapter 5, together with the editing vocabulary and method from Chapter 17, carried every adaptation in this chapter. Chapter 19 continues Unit IV with the Specialized Project, in which field-specific Arabic, source integration, and presentation design come together in a substantial bounded project.

Practical Performance Task

مُهِمَّة الأَدَاء العَمَلِيّ — Performance Task

رِسَالَة وَاحِدَة، أَرْبَعَة جَمَاهِير — One Message, Four Audiences

Take a short passage (three to five sentences) from an earlier chapter's writing task, or use the sample passage provided by your instructor. Produce:

  • a version adapted for the public audience;
  • a version adapted for the expert audience, restoring or adding precision as needed;
  • a version adapted for the learner audience, with additional explanation for at least one term;
  • three to five specific comments justifying each adaptation, naming the audience and the adjustment; and
  • confirmation that the underlying facts and stance remain identical across all three versions.

This task is not a unit assessment. It assesses independent command of this chapter's audience-adaptation vocabulary and method, applied entirely through grammar already at mastery status. Unit IV's next synthesis checkpoint falls at Chapter 20.

Answer Key

Listening Practice

  • The first sentence names the public audience's problem with the technical report's vocabulary density; the proposed solution is to simplify the facts without changing them.
  • The second sentence describes the move for the expert audience: restoring the precision (exact figures, sources) omitted from the public version.
  • The third sentence describes the move for the learner audience: keeping the specialized term and adding an explanation directly after it, rather than removing the term.

Reading Practice — Comprehension questions

  1. The public version omits the exact percentages (32% to 47%) and the source citation ("according to the technical committee's report"); this omission is appropriate because a general audience needs the overall trend, not the precise sourcing.
  2. The learner version adds a parenthetical gloss explaining what "the proportion of benefiting families" means in plainer terms; this addresses a learner's need to understand a specialized construct phrase without losing exposure to it.
  3. Both versions change only phrasing and scaffolding, not the underlying claim: families benefited, the benefit grew, and it did so over three years in all three versions.
  4. Model: تَعْلِيق: لَو وُجِّهَت هَذِه الجُمْلَة لِلْجُمْهُور المُتَخَصِّص، يَجِب إِعَادَة النِّسْبَتَيْن الدَّقِيقَتَيْن (٣٢٪ وَ٤٧٪) وَذِكْر مَصْدَر الرَّقْم. ("Comment: if this sentence were directed at the specialized audience, the two exact percentages [32% and 47%] would need to be restored, and the source of the figure named.")

Chapter Review — Model responses

Section A: تَكَيُّف names the general process of adjusting a message to an audience; تَبْسِيط names the specific move of reducing vocabulary density and complexity for a less specialized audience; تَوْحِيد الرِّسَالَة names the requirement that the underlying facts and stance remain identical across all adapted versions. Adapting a message changes vocabulary density, explicitness, or scaffolding (for example, adding a gloss for a learner audience); changing content alters the facts, figures, or stance themselves (for example, dropping a qualifying detail that changes the claim's meaning), which this chapter's method does not permit.

Section B: Open-ended production; any accurate public-adaptation-with-named-omission pair and learner-adaptation-with-named-gloss pair that preserve the underlying claim and stay within Chapter 5's consolidated grammar are acceptable.

Looking Ahead

مَا الَّذِي تَسْتَطِيعُ فِعْلَهُ الآن — What you can do now

You can now identify what public, expert, learner, and professional audiences each need from a shared message, adapt vocabulary density and explicitness accordingly, restore or add precision for specialists, and supply scaffolding for learners, all while keeping the underlying facts and stance consistent across every version.

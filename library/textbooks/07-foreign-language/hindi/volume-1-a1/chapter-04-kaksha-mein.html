कक्षा में

Volume I, Chapter 4: Classroom and Script Building · Speak and Write Hindi: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify common classroom objects and study materials using scheduled vocabulary.
  2. Ask and answer यह क्या है? and वह क्या है? about familiar objects.
  3. Count classroom items using numbers 0–50 as reference support.
  4. Express possession of classroom objects using the formula मेरे पास ... है.
  5. Follow common classroom instructions and ask for repetition, help, or clarification.
  6. Recognize the four scheduled consonants स, श, ष, and ह, and read the three matras ऐ, ओ, and औ inside familiar words.
  7. Recognize the halant/virama as a support symbol in familiar conjunct words such as स्कूल and अच्छा.
  8. Identify, count, and label a small classroom set using correct Devanagari for scheduled words.

Communication Scenario

It is the first full week of class, and Mr. Sharma is setting up routines that will repeat all year. He holds up a pencil and asks यह क्या है? A student answers, and the class practices the word together. Later, students check their bags and tell a partner what they have, using मेरे पास ... है. When someone doesn't catch an instruction, they raise a hand and ask politely for it to be repeated.

  1. This chapter gives you the language to name classroom objects, ask what something is, count small quantities, and manage classroom communication.
  2. It also expands your Devanagari consonant and matra set so you can read longer, more familiar syllable chains.

Where This Chapter Fits

Chapter 4 builds on the identification patterns from Chapter 2 and the possessive patterns from Chapter 3. It adds a new question pattern for things rather than people, a formulaic possession pattern with के पास, and classroom-management language. It does not introduce a full postposition system, a full imperative-level system, or a full conjunct catalog — these continue to grow gradually across the volume.

Coming Into This Chapter

You can already identify people with यह/वह, use मेरा/मेरी/मेरे to show possession of family and friends, and add a simple adjective such as अच्छा/अच्छी/अच्छे.

New in This Chapter

यह/वह क्या है? for objects; classroom and study vocabulary; numbers 0–50 as reference support; the possession formula मेरे पास ... है; classroom instructions and clarification expressions; four new consonants; three new matras; and the halant/virama as a support symbol.

Looking Forward

Chapter 5 consolidates Chapters 1–4 without introducing new grammar, preparing you for the Unit I assessment.

Devanagari and Pronunciation

Chapters 1–3 gave you the ten independent vowels, twenty-seven consonants, and five matras. This chapter adds four more consonants, bringing your consonant total to thirty-one, and three more matras, bringing your matra total to eight.

4.1 Four new consonants

सsa
शsha
षsha (retroflex)
हha
Pronunciation Focus — Three sibilants that sound similar

स, श, and ष can all sound close to English "s" or "sh" to a new learner. स is a plain dental/alveolar "s," as in स्कूल. श is a palatal "sh," as in भाषा-type words you will meet later. ष is historically retroflex and, in most contemporary Standard Hindi speech, is pronounced the same as श; you mainly need to recognize its spelling in words like भाषा and कक्षा rather than produce a separate sound for it.

4.2 Three new matras: ऐ, ओ, and औ

कैkai
कोko
कौkau

All three of these matras attach to the right of the consonant and are pronounced immediately after it, in the order they are spoken. You can hear the ऐ matra in बैग from this chapter's vocabulary.

4.3 The halant/virama as a support symbol

The halant, or virama (्), is a small mark that removes the inherent vowel from a consonant so that it can combine directly with the next consonant, forming a conjunct. You have already been reading conjuncts formulaically in words such as स्कूल, अच्छा, and कक्षा. This chapter names the halant explicitly as a support symbol so that you can recognize it, but you are not yet expected to build new conjuncts on your own — that skill develops gradually across the volume.

4.4 Listening for classroom commands and numbers

Listen for the classroom instructions सुनिए, देखिए, बोलिए, and लिखिए, and for numbers used to count objects.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

4.5 यह क्या है? and वह क्या है?

Purpose: ask what an object is, and answer with its name.

You already use यह/वह कौन है? to ask about people. The same demonstratives combine with क्या instead of कौन to ask about things.

Language Building Block — Object identification
यह क्या है? · वह क्या है?
Use यह for something near you and वह for something farther away, exactly as you already do for people.
यह क्या है? — यह किताब है।
What is this? — This is a book.

4.6 Simple noun phrases with classroom nouns

Purpose: describe a classroom object briefly.

  • यह मेरी लाल कॉपी है। Yah meree laal copy hai. This is my red notebook.
  • वह नीला बैग है। Vah neelaa baig hai. That is a blue bag.

Plural classroom objects, such as किताबें ("books"), are introduced here only lexically as whole words to recognize. A full plural paradigm is not required yet.

4.7 Possession with मेरे पास ... है

Purpose: say what you have with you.

Language Focus — के पास as a possession formula

के पास literally relates to being "near" someone, and Hindi uses it to express possession: मेरे पास किताब है means "I have a book" (literally, "near me a book is"). This chapter introduces मेरे पास ... है as a fixed formula for classroom objects. You are not yet expected to analyze के पास as a full postposition with oblique rules — that system develops beginning in Chapter 7.

मेरे पास किताब है। मेरे पास किताब नहीं है।
I have a book. I don't have a book.

4.8 Classroom imperatives as formulas

Purpose: follow and give simple classroom instructions politely.

Grammar Note — Polite instruction forms

सुनिए, देखिए, बोलिए, and लिखिए are polite instruction forms built on the respectful आप address level, matching the safe default address you already use. Treat them as fixed classroom formulas for now; the fuller imperative system, including other politeness levels, develops later in the volume.

4.9 कहाँ? in classroom-location contexts

Purpose: recognize a location question about classroom objects.

You will hear कहाँ? used to ask where something is, such as किताब कहाँ है? ("Where is the book?"). This chapter introduces कहाँ? only receptively; you will build full location answers beginning in Chapter 7.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Mr. Sharma checks classroom supplies with his students on the first full day of Hindi class.

शर्मा जी यह क्या है, अनिल? Yah kyaa hai, Anil? What is this, Anil?
अनिल यह मेरी किताब है। Yah meree kitaab hai. This is my book.
शर्मा जी अच्छा। क्या आपके पास कलम है? Achchhaa. Kyaa aapke paas kalam hai? Good. Do you have a pen?
अनिल माफ़ कीजिए, फिर से बोलिए? Maaf keejiye, phir se boliye? Sorry, please say that again?
शर्मा जी क्या आपके पास कलम है? Kyaa aapke paas kalam hai? Do you have a pen?
अनिल जी हाँ, मेरे पास एक लाल कलम है। Jee haaँ, mere paas ek laal kalam hai. Yes, I have a red pen.
शर्मा जी बहुत अच्छा। सुनिए और लिखिए, कक्षा! Bahut achchhaa. Suniye aur likhiye, kakshaa! Very good. Listen and write, class!

क्या at the start of क्या आपके पास कलम है? is the familiar yes/no question marker from Chapter 1, here recycled before a new pattern; एक ("one/a") is glossed for context only and is not analyzed as a number word yet.

समझ के प्रश्न — Comprehension questions:

  1. अनिल के पास क्या है? (What does Anil have?)
  2. अनिल का कलम किस रंग का है? (What color is Anil's pen?)
  3. Why does Anil say माफ़ कीजिए, फिर से बोलिए?

Writing Workshop

Create your own short classroom inventory or object description, following the models above. Your writing should name at least three objects, use मेरे पास ... है at least once, and include one color word.

लेखन कार्य — Writing task: Write three or four short sentences or a labeled list in Devanagari, copying letter shapes and matras carefully.

  1. Choose your objects. Pick three or more classroom items to name.
  2. Use the possession formula. Write at least one sentence with मेरे पास ... है or मेरे पास ... नहीं है.
  3. Add one color. Describe at least one object using a color word from this chapter.
  4. Check your letters and matras. Compare each Devanagari letter and matra you wrote against the charts above, paying attention to the new ऐ, ओ, and औ matras.

संपादन सूची — Editing checklist:

  • At least three classroom objects are named correctly.
  • मेरे पास ... है is used at least once, correctly.
  • At least one color word is used correctly.
  • Devanagari letters and matras are copied carefully and clearly.
  • A danda (।) ends each sentence.

Speaking Practice

नियंत्रित अभ्यास — Controlled practice: reading the model dialogue aloud

Read the model dialogue above aloud twice: once slowly, sounding out each word, and once at a more natural pace. Pay attention to the three sibilants स, श, and ष, and to the new ऐ matra in बैग.

संचार कार्य — Communicative task: what's in your bag?

With a partner, take turns holding up or naming real or imagined classroom objects. Ask यह/वह क्या है? and answer. Then ask your partner क्या आपके पास ... है? and answer using मेरे पास ... है or मेरे पास ... नहीं है. Practice asking for repetition with फिर से बोलिए? at least once.

अकेले अभ्यास — Solo alternative

Record yourself naming five objects from your own bag or desk, saying what you have and do not have, and describing at least one object by color. Listen back and check your pronunciation of the sibilants and the new matras.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Asking for repetition is normal

Asking a teacher, colleague, or stranger to repeat something, as Anil does with फिर से बोलिए?, is a normal and expected classroom skill, not a sign of weakness. Politely requesting clarification is valued across many Hindi-speaking educational settings.

Culture in Context — Classrooms vary widely

Classroom norms, routines, and levels of formality vary by institution, age group, medium of instruction, and region. Some classrooms mix Hindi and English vocabulary freely, especially for items like पेंसिल and कॉपी; this reflects real, widespread usage rather than incorrect Hindi.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Confusing कौन and क्या

Error: Asking यह कौन है? about an object, or यह क्या है? about a person.

Correction: Use कौन only for people and क्या only for things. Compare यह कौन है? — यह मेरी बहन है। with यह क्या है? — यह किताब है।

Common Mistake — Treating मेरे पास as "near me" literally

Error: Translating मेरे पास किताब है word-for-word as "near me a book is" and expecting it to work like a location sentence.

Correction: Learn मेरे पास ... है as a fixed formula meaning "I have ...". Its deeper grammar is explained gradually starting in Chapter 7.

Common Mistake — Reading हर written अ aloud

Error: Pronouncing every letter in कलम with a full vowel sound, producing something like "ka-la-ma."

Correction: Hindi often drops the final inherent vowel in speech. कलम is pronounced closer to "kalam," not "kalama." You will keep building this schwa deletion awareness gradually across the volume.

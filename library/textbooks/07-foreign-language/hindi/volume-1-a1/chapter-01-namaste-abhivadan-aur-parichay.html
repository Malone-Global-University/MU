नमस्ते! अभिवादन और परिचय

Volume I, Chapter 1: First Encounters · Speak and Write Hindi: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Greet people and take leave politely in Hindi.
  2. Introduce yourself and ask another person's name.
  3. Use basic courtesy expressions, including please, thank you, and excuse me.
  4. Recognize आप as the safe, respectful default form of address.
  5. Recognize the Devanagari independent vowels and a first small set of consonants in familiar words.
  6. Read and copy a small set of high-frequency Devanagari greeting and courtesy words.
  7. Complete a short, supported greeting and self-introduction exchange, in speech and in writing.

Communication Scenario

Priya is starting a community Hindi class in the evenings after work. On the first night, the instructor, Mr. Sharma, greets each new student at the door, asks for their name, and shows them a small name card written in Devanagari to copy. Everyone in the room is a stranger to everyone else, so the whole evening is built out of short, repeated exchanges: नमस्ते, a name, a "thank you," and a seat.

  1. This chapter gives you the exact language Priya and her classmates use on that first night: greetings, names, and a small set of courtesy words.
  2. It also gives you your very first look at Devanagari, the script you will read and write throughout the whole series.

Where This Chapter Fits

Chapter 1 opens Volume I and Unit I. It introduces no full grammar system, no gender agreement, and no verb conjugation. Instead, it gives you a small number of complete, usable formulas and your first Devanagari letters, so that Chapters 2 through 4 can build identity, family, and classroom language on a stable first step.

Coming Into This Chapter

No prior Hindi knowledge is assumed. This is the very first chapter of the very first volume.

New in This Chapter

नमस्ते, धन्यवाद, and other courtesy formulas; मैं ... हूँ for giving your name; आपका नाम क्या है? for asking a name; आप as the safe default address form; the Devanagari independent vowels; and a first set of consonants, including क, ख, ग, घ, म, न, and ह.

Looking Forward

Chapter 2 uses यह and वह to identify other people, introduces nominal sentences with है, and continues building the Devanagari consonant set.

Devanagari and Pronunciation

Devanagari is the primary writing system of this course. It is an abugida: each basic consonant letter carries a built-in, or "inherent," vowel sound (a short a, as in "sofa") unless another vowel sign is attached or the vowel is deliberately removed. You will meet this inherent vowel again soon, but for now, simply notice that Hindi letters are not silent shapes — every letter you see in this chapter is meant to be pronounced.

Pronunciation Focus — Aspiration in क/ख and ग/घ

Hindi distinguishes aspirated consonants, said with a small puff of air, from unaspirated ones. क (ka) is unaspirated; ख (kha) is aspirated. ग (ga) is unaspirated; घ (gha) is aspirated. Hold a hand or a small piece of paper in front of your mouth: for the aspirated sound, you should feel or see a clear puff of air; for the unaspirated sound, almost none. This is a real, meaning-changing contrast in Hindi, not a minor accent detail, and you will keep practicing it across every chapter.

1.3 Listening for greetings and names

Listen for the rise and fall of a greeting exchange, and for the shape of the question आपका नाम क्या है?, before you try to read every letter. Hindi statement and question intonation both matter here: a question rises gently toward the end, while a statement or a name generally falls.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

1.4 Greeting formulas

Purpose: greet someone and take leave, using one all-purpose word.

नमस्ते is used for both "hello" and "goodbye," at any time of day, with people you have just met as well as people you know well. Because it is so flexible, you can use it confidently in almost any first encounter.

  • नमस्ते! Namaste! Hello! / Greetings!
  • नमस्ते, फिर मिलेंगे। Namaste, phir milenge. Goodbye, see you again.

1.5 मैं ... हूँ for giving your name

Purpose: say who you are, using one fixed frame.

Treat मैं ... हूँ as a complete, memorized frame for now: मैं means "I," and हूँ is a form of "to be" that goes with मैं. You are not yet expected to change हूँ to agree with other subjects — that fuller system develops gradually across the volume.

Language Focus — मैं ... हूँ

Fill the blank in the middle with your name, and keep मैं at the start and हूँ at the end: मैं [नाम] हूँ। This is a formulaic pattern. Full explanation of Hindi's pronoun and copula system will come gradually, starting in Chapter 2.

  • मैं प्रिया हूँ। Main Priya hoon. I am Priya.
  • मैं शर्मा हूँ। Main Sharma hoon. I am Sharma.

1.6 Asking a name: आपका नाम क्या है?

Purpose: ask another person's name, respectfully.

Language Building Block — Asking a name
आपका नाम क्या है?
Treat this whole question as one memorized formula: literally, "your name what is?" You do not need to take it apart word by word yet. A simple reply repeats the frame from 1.5 above.
मेरा नाम राज है।
My name is Raj. (an alternative reply frame, also formulaic at this stage)

1.7 आप as your safe default address form

Purpose: address anyone respectfully, without needing to judge the relationship first.

Grammar Note — आप, तुम, and तू

Hindi has three words that can correspond to English "you": आप, तुम, and तू. They are not interchangeable — each signals a different level of formality and closeness. आप is respectful and safe with strangers, elders, teachers, and most people you do not know well. तुम is familiar, used among friends, peers, and some family members. तू is very intimate, and can also be affectionate, poetic, religious, or, in the wrong context, insulting.

In this chapter, use आप as your default. You will begin to recognize तुम and तू in later chapters, but you are not expected to produce them yet.

1.8 Yes/no and questions, formulaically

Purpose: answer a simple question and recognize क्या in a memorized frame.

For now, treat जी हाँ and जी नहीं as complete formulas for "yes" and "no," and treat क्या only as it appears inside आपका नाम क्या है?. A full explanation of Hindi question formation begins later in the volume.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Mr. Sharma greets Priya at the door on the first night of the community Hindi class.

शर्मा जी नमस्ते! स्वागत है। Namaste! Swaagat hai. Hello! Welcome.
प्रिया नमस्ते! धन्यवाद। Namaste! Dhanyavaad. Hello! Thank you.
शर्मा जी आपका नाम क्या है? Aapka naam kya hai? What is your name?
प्रिया मैं प्रिया हूँ। और आप? Main Priya hoon. Aur aap? I am Priya. And you?
शर्मा जी मैं शर्मा हूँ। कृपया बैठिए। Main Sharma hoon. Kripaya baithiye. I am Sharma. Please sit down.
प्रिया धन्यवाद, शर्मा जी। Dhanyavaad, Sharma jee. Thank you, Mr. Sharma.

Notice that जी is added after शर्मा as a mark of respect. Not every name needs जी in every situation — context and relationship matter, as you will see again in later chapters.

समझ के प्रश्न — Comprehension questions:

  1. प्रिया का नाम क्या है? (What is Priya's name?)
  2. शर्मा जी क्या कहते हैं जब प्रिया आती है? (What does Mr. Sharma say when Priya arrives?)
  3. How does Priya thank Mr. Sharma at the end of the exchange?

Writing Workshop

Create your own short name card, following the model cards above. Your card should use a greeting, one of the two self-introduction frames, and one courtesy word.

लेखन कार्य — Writing task: Write two or three short sentences in Devanagari, copying letter shapes carefully.

  1. Choose a greeting. Start with नमस्ते.
  2. Give your name. Use either मैं [नाम] हूँ। or मेरा नाम [नाम] है।
  3. Add one courtesy word. Close with धन्यवाद। or स्वागत है।
  4. Check your letters. Compare each Devanagari letter you wrote against the vowel and consonant charts above.

संपादन सूची — Editing checklist:

  • The card opens with a greeting.
  • The card gives a name, using one of the two taught frames.
  • The card includes at least one courtesy word.
  • Devanagari letters are copied carefully and clearly.
  • A danda (।) or period ends each sentence.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — नमस्ते across settings

नमस्ते is widely understood across Hindi-speaking communities, but greeting practices are not identical everywhere. Some speakers greet with a slight bow or a folded-hands gesture; others simply say the word. Greetings may also vary by religion, region, generation, and setting. Do not assume that one greeting routine represents every Hindi-speaking person.

Culture in Context — जी and respectful address

जी added after a name or title, as in शर्मा जी, signals respect. It is common with teachers, elders, and people you have just met, but it is not automatically attached to every name in every context. As you meet more names and titles in later chapters, you will develop a better sense of when जी fits naturally.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Reading क as a plain English "k"

Error: Pronouncing क and ख identically, without any breath difference.

Correction: Practice the puff-of-air test from the Pronunciation Focus box above. ख should produce a clearly noticeable puff of air; क should not.

Common Mistake — Using तुम or तू with someone you have just met

Error: Assuming that any word for "you" is equally safe with any person.

Correction: Use आप as your default in this chapter. Save तुम and तू for later chapters, once you understand more about the relationships they signal.

Common Mistake — Changing हूँ to match other subjects

Error: Trying to guess a different ending for "I am," "you are," or "he/she is," because English changes its verb forms.

Correction: For now, treat मैं ... हूँ as one fixed frame. The fuller copula system, including है and हैं, begins in Chapter 2.

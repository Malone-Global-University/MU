यह कौन है?

Volume I, Chapter 2: People and Identity · Speak and Write Hindi: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify a person using यह ("this") and वह ("that").
  2. Ask who someone is with यह कौन है? and answer in a short nominal sentence.
  3. State a person's basic role, occupation, or relationship using है.
  4. Recognize that Hindi nouns for people carry grammatical gender, without yet assigning gender productively to every noun.
  5. Recognize twelve new Devanagari consonants and read them inside familiar words.
  6. Read and copy short identity labels and classroom captions.
  7. Introduce two people to a partner using controlled nominal sentences.

Communication Scenario

It is the second week of Priya's Hindi class. Mr. Sharma has brought in photographs of his own family and a few classroom objects to help everyone practice pointing things out and asking who is who. Priya sits next to a new classmate, Anil, and the two of them begin comparing photos: a picture of Anil's sister, a picture of Mr. Sharma's colleague, a picture of a family friend. Almost every sentence in the room this week follows the same small shape: point at something, say यह or वह, and finish with है.

  1. This chapter gives you the language Priya and Anil use to point at people and ask who they are.
  2. It also expands your Devanagari consonant set so that you can read more of the words already appearing around you in class.

Where This Chapter Fits

Chapter 2 builds directly on the greetings, names, and courtesy formulas from Chapter 1. It adds a new tool for identifying people, a first nominal sentence pattern with है, and a first, careful look at grammatical gender through people-words. It does not require full pronoun analysis, full copula analysis, or a plural system — those systems continue to grow gradually across the volume.

Coming Into This Chapter

You can already greet someone, introduce yourself with मैं ... हूँ, ask a name with आपका नाम क्या है?, and read the Devanagari independent vowels and your first seven consonants.

New in This Chapter

यह and वह for identification; यह कौन है? and short answers; nominal predicates with है; a first look at masculine and feminine nouns through people-words; and twelve new consonants, including च, छ, ज, झ, ट, ठ, ड, ढ, त, थ, द, and ध.

Looking Forward

Chapter 3 uses possessives such as मेरा and मेरी to talk about family and friends, and begins to connect adjective agreement to the gender you notice here.

Devanagari and Pronunciation

Chapter 1 introduced the ten independent vowels and your first seven consonants. This chapter adds twelve more consonants and your first two dependent vowel signs, or matras. A matra is a mark attached to a consonant to show a vowel other than the consonant's built-in "a" sound.

Pronunciation Focus — Dental versus retroflex: त/ट and द/ड

Hindi distinguishes two families of consonants that English speakers often hear as the same sound. Dental consonants, such as त and द, are made with the tongue tip touching the back of the upper front teeth. Retroflex consonants, such as ट and ड, are made with the tongue tip curled back to touch the roof of the mouth. This is a real, meaning-changing contrast, not a matter of accent. Say तीन ("three") and then टीका ("mark, vaccine") slowly, and notice where your tongue touches each time.

2.2 First matras: आ, इ, and ई

A matra attaches to a consonant to replace its inherent "a" vowel with a different vowel sound. The independent vowel and its matra form look different, but the sound relationship is the same one you already practiced in Chapter 1.

कka
काkaa
किki
कीkee

Look closely at कि: the small hook for the इ matra is written before the consonant, on its left side, but it is pronounced after the consonant sound. Do not read कि as "ik" — it is pronounced "ki." You will meet this left-side matra again throughout the course, so it is worth noticing carefully now.

2.3 Listening for कौन, यह, and वह

Listen for the question word कौन rising gently at the end of a question, and for the short, level statement intonation of an answer built with यह or वह and है.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

2.4 Identifying people with यह and वह

Purpose: point out a person or thing that is near or far.

यह identifies someone or something close to you; वह identifies someone or something farther away. Both words work the same way whether you are pointing at a person, a photo, or an object.

  • यह मेरा दोस्त है। Yah meraa dost hai. This is my friend.
  • वह अध्यापक है। Vah adhyaapak hai. That is the teacher.

मेरा ("my") appears here in a fixed phrase only; its full pattern of agreement is introduced in Chapter 3.

2.5 Asking who: यह कौन है?

Purpose: ask who a nearby person is, and answer briefly.

Language Building Block — Asking who someone is
यह कौन है?
Literally, "this who is?" Use वह कौन है? in the same way for someone farther away. Answer with यह/वह plus a name or role, and close with है.
यह सुनीता है।
This is Sunita.

2.6 Nominal predicates with है

Purpose: state who or what someone is, using one fixed pattern.

Language Focus — यह/वह + noun + है

A Hindi sentence that identifies or describes someone often has no separate word for "a" or "an," and simply places the noun before है: यह/वह [नाम या भूमिका] है। Treat है as a formulaic form for now, matching यह and वह. You already met हूँ in Chapter 1 for "I am"; the fuller system connecting हूँ, है, and हैं continues to develop gradually across the volume.

  • यह लड़का है। Yah ladkaa hai. This is a boy.
  • वह छात्रा है। Vah chhaatraa hai. That is a (female) student.

2.7 A first look at gender through people-words

Purpose: notice that many Hindi nouns for people come in matched masculine and feminine forms.

Grammar Note — Gender is grammatical, not just biological

Every Hindi noun has a grammatical gender, masculine or feminine. For words describing people, gender usually lines up with the person's gender, as in अध्यापक (male teacher) and अध्यापिका (female teacher), or छात्र and छात्रा. Some people-words, such as दोस्त and डॉक्टर, keep the same form for any gender, with gender shown elsewhere in the sentence once you learn more agreement patterns. You are not yet expected to assign gender to every noun you meet — this chapter only asks you to notice gender in these specific, familiar words.

2.8 आप, honorific हैं, and respectful reference

Purpose: recognize that respectful reference sometimes uses a plural-looking verb form.

You may hear हैं used respectfully for a single person, especially an elder or someone addressed with आप. Treat this as recognition-only for now; you are not expected to produce हैं independently until later chapters activate it.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Priya and Anil compare family photographs during a break in class.

अनिल यह कौन है, प्रिया? Yah kaun hai, Priya? Who is this, Priya?
प्रिया यह मेरी दोस्त है। यह सुनीता है। Yah meree dost hai. Yah Suneetaa hai. This is my friend. This is Sunita.
अनिल और वह कौन है? Aur vah kaun hai? And who is that?
प्रिया वह मेरा अध्यापक है। Vah meraa adhyaapak hai. That is my teacher.
अनिल अच्छा। यह लड़की कौन है? Achchhaa. Yah ladkee kaun hai? I see. Who is this girl?
प्रिया यह छात्रा है। यह भी हमारी कक्षा में है। Yah chhaatraa hai. Yah bhee hamaaree kakshaa mein hai. This is a student. She is also in our class.

भी ("also") and हमारी कक्षा में ("in our class") appear here receptively, glossed for context only; their grammar is not analyzed until later chapters.

समझ के प्रश्न — Comprehension questions:

  1. सुनीता कौन है? (Who is Sunita?)
  2. प्रिया का अध्यापक कहाँ है — पास में या दूर? (Is Priya's teacher near or far, based on the word she uses?)
  3. What does Priya say about the girl in the last photo?

Writing Workshop

Create your own short identity label for a real or imagined person, following the model labels above. Your label should identify the person with यह or वह, give a name, and state a role or relationship.

लेखन कार्य — Writing task: Write two or three short sentences in Devanagari, copying letter shapes and matras carefully.

  1. Choose यह or वह. Decide whether the person is near or far in your description.
  2. Give a name. Use the pattern यह/वह [नाम] है।
  3. Add a role. Use one role word from this chapter, such as दोस्त, छात्र, or अध्यापिका.
  4. Check your letters and matras. Compare each Devanagari letter and matra you wrote against the charts above, paying special attention to the left-side कि-type matra.

संपादन सूची — Editing checklist:

  • The label uses यह or वह correctly for the situation described.
  • The label gives a name and a role or relationship.
  • Devanagari letters and matras are copied carefully and clearly.
  • A danda (।) ends each sentence.

Speaking Practice

नियंत्रित अभ्यास — Controlled practice: reading the model dialogue aloud

Read the model dialogue above aloud twice: once slowly, sounding out each word, and once at a more natural pace. Pay attention to the dental-retroflex contrast in दोस्त and छात्रा.

संचार कार्य — Communicative task: introduce two people

With a partner, use two photos, drawings, or classroom objects standing in for people. Point at the first and say यह ... है।, then point at the second and say वह ... है। Ask your partner यह कौन है? or वह कौन है? about their own choices.

अकेले अभ्यास — Solo alternative

Record yourself identifying two people from photos or your imagination, using both यह and वह at least once each. Listen back and check your dental versus retroflex pronunciation in any त/ट or द/ड words that appear.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Occupations and social roles

Hindi speakers work in every kind of occupation, in cities, towns, and villages, across India and around the world. When you practice identifying people by role, such as अध्यापक, छात्रा, or डॉक्टर, avoid assuming that any one occupation, community, or setting represents all Hindi speakers.

Culture in Context — Respectful identification

Even a simple identification, such as introducing a teacher or an elder, often benefits from respectful address. Notice how जी appears after सुनीता in the reading passage above. As in Chapter 1, जी is not automatic with every name — it depends on relationship, age, and context.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Reading कि as "ik"

Error: Reading the left-side matra in कि in the visual order it is written, producing "ik" instead of "ki."

Correction: Remember that this matra is written before the consonant but pronounced after it. कि is always "ki," never "ik."

Common Mistake — Confusing dental and retroflex consonants

Error: Pronouncing त and ट, or द and ड, identically.

Correction: Practice the tongue-position contrast from the Pronunciation Focus box above. Dental consonants touch the teeth; retroflex consonants curl the tongue tip back.

Common Mistake — Assuming every noun's gender from its ending

Error: Guessing that every word ending in an आ-type sound must be masculine, or every word ending in an ई-type sound must be feminine.

Correction: These patterns are common tendencies, not fixed rules. For now, simply learn the gender of each people-word in this chapter as you meet it. Broader gender patterns develop gradually in later chapters.

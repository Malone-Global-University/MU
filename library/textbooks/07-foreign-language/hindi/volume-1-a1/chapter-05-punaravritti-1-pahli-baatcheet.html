पुनरावृत्ति 1: पहली बातचीत

Volume I, Chapter 5: Unit I Review — First Conversations · Speak and Write Hindi: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Greet a person, take leave, and manage basic courtesy routines using आप as the safe default address form.
  2. Introduce yourself and exchange names using मैं ... हूँ and आपका नाम क्या है?
  3. Identify and introduce another person using यह/वह and कौन?
  4. Describe a family member or friend using मेरा/मेरी/मेरे and one adjective.
  5. Identify and count classroom objects using यह/वह क्या है? and numbers 0–50 as reference support.
  6. Express possession with मेरे पास ... है and ask politely for repetition or clarification.
  7. Read familiar Devanagari words and short sentences using the vowels, consonants, and matras introduced in Chapters 1–4.
  8. Write a short, supported personal profile in Devanagari.

Communication Scenario

It is the end of the first unit of Hindi class. Mr. Sharma has invited a visiting student, Meera, to join the class for a day. The students greet Meera, introduce themselves and their families, show her around the classroom, and help her feel welcome. Meera asks questions, and the students practice everything they have learned so far — from a first "नमस्ते" to naming the objects in their bags.

  1. This chapter does not teach any new grammar, vocabulary domain, or Devanagari system. Instead, it brings together everything from Chapters 1–4 into connected, realistic communication.
  2. Use this chapter to check your own progress before the Unit I assessment tasks at the end of the chapter.

Where This Chapter Fits

Chapter 5 is a review and assessment chapter. It introduces no new grammar, no new vocabulary domain, and no new Devanagari system. It consolidates the introductory language from Chapters 1–4 so that you can move into Unit II with a secure foundation.

Coming Into This Chapter

You can greet people, introduce yourself and others, describe a family member or friend, identify and count classroom objects, express possession, and ask for repetition — all using the scheduled Devanagari letters and matras from Chapters 1–4.

New in This Chapter

Nothing new. This chapter recombines Chapters 1–4 into longer, connected exchanges and includes your Unit I assessment tasks.

Looking Forward

Pronunciation Focus — Schwa deletion so far

Chapter 4 named schwa deletion explicitly: many Hindi words drop the final inherent vowel in speech, so कलम sounds closer to "kalam" than "kalama." Practice this awareness with familiar words such as नमस्ते, घर-type words you will meet soon, and कलम itself. This remains an awareness system, not a complete rule, and will keep developing gradually across the volume.

Grammar Review

This section reviews the patterns from Chapters 1–4. No new grammar system is introduced.

5.4 Greetings and identity (Chapter 1)

Language Building Block Review — Introducing yourself
नमस्ते। मैं ... हूँ। आपका नाम क्या है?
Hello. I am ... . What is your name?

5.5 Identifying people (Chapter 2)

Language Building Block Review — यह/वह and कौन?
यह कौन है? — यह मेरे दोस्त हैं।
Who is this? — This is my friend.

5.6 Possessives and family description (Chapter 3)

Language Building Block Review — मेरा/मेरी/मेरे with an adjective
यह मेरी बहन है। वह अच्छी है।
This is my sister. She is nice.

5.7 Objects and possession in the classroom (Chapter 4)

Language Building Block Review — यह/वह क्या है? and मेरे पास ... है
यह क्या है? — यह किताब है। मेरे पास किताब है।
What is this? — This is a book. I have a book.
Grammar Note — What stays formulaic

All four patterns above remain formulaic or controlled at this stage. You are not yet expected to analyze the full copula system, full pronoun system, full oblique forms, or के पास as a complete postposition. Those systems begin to expand starting in Chapter 6 and develop fully beginning in Chapter 7.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Meera, a visiting student, joins Mr. Sharma's class for the day. Anil welcomes her and introduces his classroom and his family.

अनिल नमस्ते! मेरा नाम अनिल है। आपका नाम क्या है? Namaste! Meraa naam Anil hai. Aapkaa naam kyaa hai? Hello! My name is Anil. What is your name?
मीरा नमस्ते, अनिल! मेरा नाम मीरा है। Namaste, Anil! Meraa naam Meeraa hai. Hello, Anil! My name is Meera.
अनिल यह मेरी बहन है। यह अच्छी है। Yah meree bahan hai. Yah achchhee hai. This is my sister. She is nice.
मीरा अच्छा! और यह क्या है? Achchhaa! Aur yah kyaa hai? Good! And what is this?
अनिल यह मेरी किताब है। मेरे पास एक कलम भी है। Yah meree kitaab hai. Mere paas ek kalam bhee hai. This is my book. I also have a pen.
मीरा माफ़ कीजिए, फिर से बोलिए? Maaf keejiye, phir se boliye? Sorry, please say that again?
अनिल मेरे पास एक कलम भी है। धन्यवाद, मीरा! Mere paas ek kalam bhee hai. Dhanyavaad, Meeraa! I also have a pen. Thank you, Meera!

भी ("also/too") is glossed for context here and is not analyzed as a new grammar point.

समझ के प्रश्न — Comprehension questions:

  1. मीरा का नाम क्या है? (What is Meera's name?)
  2. अनिल के पास क्या-क्या है? (What does Anil have?)
  3. Why does Meera say माफ़ कीजिए, फिर से बोलिए?

Writing Workshop

Write a short personal profile that combines everything from Chapters 1–4. This is the same kind of task Meera's profile modeled above.

लेखन कार्य — Writing task: Write a supported personal profile of four to six sentences in Devanagari.

  1. Greet and introduce yourself. Begin with नमस्ते and your name.
  2. Introduce one family member or friend. Use यह/वह, मेरा/मेरी/मेरे, and one adjective.
  3. Name one or two classroom objects you have. Use मेरे पास ... है.
  4. Add one color word. Describe one object or belonging using a color from Chapter 4.
  5. Check your letters and matras. Compare your Devanagari against the review charts above.

संपादन सूची — Editing checklist:

  • A greeting and self-introduction appear at the start.
  • One family member or friend is introduced with यह/वह, a possessive, and an adjective.
  • मेरे पास ... है is used at least once, correctly.
  • At least one color word is used correctly.
  • A danda (।) ends each sentence, and Devanagari letters and matras are copied carefully.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Welcoming a guest

Welcoming a visitor with a greeting, an introduction, and small courtesies is valued in many Hindi-speaking settings, whether in a classroom, a home, or a community gathering. The specific routine varies by relationship, age, region, and setting, but the underlying courtesy is widely shared.

Culture in Context — Reviewing without one "correct" family or classroom

The people, families, and classrooms in this chapter's practice materials are examples, not a single model for every Hindi-speaking family or school. As you continue through the series, you will meet a wider range of people, households, and settings.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Overusing तू or तुम

Error: Using तू or तुम with a new acquaintance, teacher, or elder because it feels shorter or more casual.

Correction: Keep using आप as your safe default, exactly as you have since Chapter 1, until later volumes teach you when तुम is appropriate.

Common Mistake — Mixing up कौन and क्या under pressure

Error: Reverting to क्या for people or कौन for objects when speaking quickly in a mixed review task.

Correction: Pause and check: कौन asks about a person, क्या asks about a thing. Both questions from Chapter 2 and Chapter 4 continue to matter together.

Common Mistake — Forgetting schwa deletion in familiar words

Error: Reading familiar words letter-by-letter again, such as pronouncing नमस्ते with a full vowel after every consonant.

Correction: Keep applying the schwa deletion awareness from Chapter 4 to every word you already know, not only to new vocabulary.

अंतिम कार्य: हिंदी व्यवहार में

Volume I, Chapter 20: Final Task — Hindi in Practice · Speak and Write Hindi: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Introduce yourself and others, and manage greetings and courtesy routines using safe standard address.
  2. Describe your family, home, neighborhood, daily routine, and belongings.
  3. Order food, shop for an item, and manage a practical everyday transaction.
  4. Ask for and give directions, describe a health need, and make a polite institutional request.
  5. Narrate a simple completed event and make a future plan, including an invitation and response.
  6. Compare two things, give a supported opinion, and connect ideas with reason, result, contrast, and condition.
  7. Read practical Devanagari texts and write a short connected A2 paragraph with limited support.
  8. Demonstrate awareness of register, honorifics, and Hindi-English and Hindi-Urdu/Hindustani variation.

Communication Scenario

Arjun, an exchange student, spends a full day in Hindi: he greets his host family, describes his routine, buys something at the market, asks for directions to a clinic because he feels unwell, explains why he missed a plan last week, makes a new plan with a friend, and writes a short message to his host mother summarizing his day. This final chapter follows Arjun's day as a single connected performance project that draws on every major system from Volume I.

  1. Notice how the day moves from a simple morning greeting to a full connected written paragraph by evening.
  2. Notice how earlier chapters' language — greetings, family, food, home, shopping, routine, directions, health, work, past events, plans, and opinions — all appear together in one natural sequence.
  3. Notice that no new grammar appears anywhere in this chapter. Everything here has already been taught.

Where This Chapter Fits

Chapter 20 is the final chapter of Unit IV, अनुभव, योजना और कहानी, and of Hindi Volume I as a whole. Chapter 19 developed connected explanation with क्योंकि, इसलिए, and लेकिन, added controlled condition with अगर...तो and reported information with कि, and introduced receptive recognition of जो...वह. Chapter 20 introduces no new grammar. Instead, it integrates every active system from Chapters 1 through 19 into one connected CEFR A2 performance project and evaluates readiness for Hindi Volume II.

Coming Into This Chapter

You can greet and introduce people, describe family and home, manage food and shopping transactions, describe a daily routine, ask for directions, describe a health need, make a polite request, narrate a simple past event with controlled ने, make future plans, compare options, give opinions, and connect ideas with reason, result, contrast, condition, and reported information.

New in This Chapter

No new grammar, vocabulary domain, or pronunciation system is introduced. This chapter integrates all active systems into a single connected performance and assesses overall readiness for Volume II.

Looking Forward

Hindi Volume II, रोज़मर्रा की स्वतंत्रता और जुड़ा हुआ संचार (Everyday Independence and Connected Communication), builds on this foundation to move learners from CEFR A2 toward B1, with stronger postposition control, developed ergativity, common compound verbs, and more independent connected discourse.

Devanagari and Pronunciation Review

This chapter introduces no new letters, matras, or pronunciation rules. It reviews the cumulative Devanagari and pronunciation systems active across Volume I in a single connected checkpoint.

Review Focus — Aspiration and dental-retroflex contrast

Reread the model dialogue below aloud, paying attention to aspirated versus unaspirated consonants (क/ख, त/थ) and dental versus retroflex consonants (त/ट, द/ड) in familiar words such as दोस्त, ठीक, and तबियत.

Review Focus — Schwa deletion across the volume

Read नमस्ते, घर, दफ़्तर, सकता/सकती, and पसंद as natural spoken forms, not as a string of individually sounded written vowels. This is the same schwa deletion awareness first introduced in Chapter 9 and developed since.

Review Focus — Nasalization, anusvara, and chandrabindu

Compare मैं, हूँ, कहाँ, and नहीं. Notice that anusvara and chandrabindu both mark nasalization but attach differently depending on the vowel sign, as reviewed since Chapter 3.

Review Focus — Rhythm across a full connected day

Read the full listening passage below at a natural pace, grouping words into phrases rather than reading word by word, as practiced since Chapter 11.

Grammar and Sentence Pattern Review

This chapter introduces no new grammar. The patterns below are reviewed together because the performance project in this chapter requires learners to move fluidly among them within a single connected day.

20.5 Language Focus — Reviewing the full Volume I verb system in sequence

A single connected account of a day may require several verb systems in close succession: habitual for routine, progressive for a current action, ability with सकना, perfective for something already completed, and future for a plan. None of these forms is new; what is new in this chapter is using them together smoothly.

Language Building Block — Verb systems in one connected passage
मैं हर दिन सुबह चाय पीता हूँ। आज मैं बाज़ार जा रहा हूँ। मैं हिंदी में बात कर सकता हूँ। कल मैंने एक किताब पढ़ी। अगले हफ़्ते मैं अपने दोस्त से मिलूँगा।
Every day I drink tea in the morning. Today I am going to the market. I can speak in Hindi. Yesterday I read a book. Next week I will meet my friend.

20.6 Language Focus — Reviewing postpositions and oblique forms in one description

में, पर, के पास, से, and का/की/के all recur naturally when describing a home, a route, and a plan in the same paragraph. Oblique singular forms before these postpositions remain active from Chapter 7 onward.

Language Building Block — Postpositions in one description
मेरे घर के पास एक अच्छा बाज़ार है। मेरा कमरा घर में है। मैं स्कूल से घर आता हूँ।
There is a good market near my house. My room is in the house. I come home from school.

20.7 Language Focus — Reviewing connection: reason, result, contrast, and condition together

Chapter 19's connectors — क्योंकि, इसलिए, लेकिन, and अगर...तो — combine with earlier past-narration and future-planning language to build a connected explanation of an entire day.

Language Building Block — Connected explanation
मुझे सुबह तबियत ठीक नहीं लगी, इसलिए मैं क्लिनिक गया। डॉक्टर ने कहा कि मुझे आराम चाहिए, लेकिन मुझे शाम को अपने दोस्त से मिलना था। अगर मुझे थोड़ा अच्छा लगा, तो मैं ज़रूर मिलूँगा।
I didn't feel well in the morning, so I went to the clinic. The doctor said that I need rest, but I had to meet my friend in the evening. If I feel a little better, I will definitely meet him.
Boundary

This chapter does not introduce any new grammar, postposition function, verb form, or connector. It requires learners to combine already-active Volume I systems within one connected day. जो...वह remains receptive only, as established in Chapter 19.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Arjun speaks with his host mother, Sunita ji, in the evening about his day, after visiting a clinic and making plans with a friend.

सुनीता जी अर्जुन, आज तुम्हारा दिन कैसा रहा? तुम सुबह जल्दी घर से निकल गए थे। Arjun, aaj tumhaaraa din kaisaa rahaa? Tum subah jaldee ghar se nikal gae the. Arjun, how was your day today? You left the house early in the morning.
अर्जुन जी, सुनीता जी, सुबह मुझे तबियत ठीक नहीं लगी, इसलिए मैं क्लिनिक गया। डॉक्टर ने कहा कि यह कुछ खास नहीं है, लेकिन मुझे थोड़ा आराम करना चाहिए। Jee, Suneetaa jee, subah mujhe tabiyat theek naheeँ lagee, isalie maiँ klinik gayaa. Doctar ne kahaa ki yah kuchh khaas naheeँ hai, lekin mujhe thoड़aa aaraam karanaa chaahie. Yes, Sunita ji, in the morning I didn't feel well, so I went to the clinic. The doctor said it's nothing serious, but I should rest a little.
सुनीता जी अच्छा, यह सुनकर अच्छा लगा। फिर क्या हुआ? तुम बाज़ार भी गए थे न? Achchhaa, yah sunakar achchhaa lagaa. Phir kyaa huaa? Tum baazaar bhee gae the na? Okay, good to hear that. Then what happened? You also went to the market, right?
अर्जुन हाँ, क्लिनिक के बाद मैं बाज़ार गया और मैंने अपने लिए एक नई किताब ख़रीदी। यह किताब उस किताब से ज़्यादा अच्छी है जो मैंने पिछले हफ़्ते ख़रीदी थी। Haaँ, klinik ke baad maiँ baazaar gayaa aur maiँne apane lie ek naee kitaab khareedee. Yah kitaab us kitaab se zyaadaa achchhee hai jo maiँne pichhale haफ़्ते khareedee thee. Yes, after the clinic I went to the market and I bought a new book for myself. This book is better than the book I bought last week.
सुनीता जी बहुत अच्छा। और आज शाम को तुम कहाँ जा रहे हो? Bahut achchhaa. Aur aaj shaam ko tum kahaँ jaa rahe ho? Very good. And where are you going this evening?
अर्जुन मुझे अपने दोस्त रोहन से मिलना है। अगर मुझे थोड़ा अच्छा लगा, तो मैं ज़रूर जाऊँगा, क्योंकि हमने पहले से योजना बनाई थी। Mujhe apane dost Rohan se milanaa hai. Agar mujhe thoड़aa achchhaa lagaa, to maiँ zaroor jaaoँgaa, kyoँki hamane pahale se yojanaa banaaee thee. I have to meet my friend Rohan. If I feel a little better, I will definitely go, because we had already made a plan.
सुनीता जी ठीक है, लेकिन अपना ध्यान रखना। मुझे लगता है कि थोड़ा आराम करना अच्छा रहेगा। Theek hai, lekin apanaa dhyaan rakhanaa. Mujhe lagataa hai ki thoड़aa aaraam karanaa achchhaa rahegaa. Okay, but take care of yourself. I think that resting a little will be good.

समझ के प्रश्न — Comprehension questions:

  1. अर्जुन सुबह क्लिनिक क्यों गया? (Why did Arjun go to the clinic in the morning?)
  2. डॉक्टर ने क्या कहा? (What did the doctor say?)
  3. अर्जुन ने बाज़ार में क्या ख़रीदा, और वह पिछली किताब से कैसी है? (What did Arjun buy at the market, and how does it compare to the previous book?)
  4. अर्जुन शाम को क्या करने की योजना बना रहा है, और यह किस बात पर निर्भर करता है? (What is Arjun planning to do in the evening, and what does it depend on?)

Reading Practice

एक संदेश — A Message
नमस्ते विकास, कैसे हो? मैं ठीक हूँ, लेकिन पिछले हफ़्ते मुझे थोड़ी तबियत ख़राब थी, इसलिए मैं तुमसे नहीं मिल पाया। मेरे दोस्त ने कहा कि तुम भी बीमार थे — क्या यह सच है? अब मैं बिल्कुल ठीक हूँ। मेरा घर बाज़ार के पास है, और वहाँ से स्कूल तक पैदल दस मिनट लगते हैं। मैं हर दिन सुबह पैदल जाता हूँ, क्योंकि मुझे यह अच्छा लगता है। अगर तुम इस हफ़्ते के अंत में खाली हो, तो कृपया मुझे बताओ; हम साथ में बाज़ार जा सकते हैं और कुछ खा सकते हैं। मुझे लगता है कि यह हमारे लिए एक अच्छी योजना होगी। तुम्हारा दोस्त, अर्जुन।
Hello Vikas, how are you? I'm fine, but last week I was a little unwell, so I couldn't meet you. My friend said that you were also sick — is that true? Now I'm completely fine. My house is near the market, and it takes ten minutes to walk from there to school. I walk every day in the morning, because I like it. If you're free this weekend, please tell me; we can go to the market together and eat something. I think this would be a good plan for us. Your friend, Arjun.

  1. Why couldn't Arjun meet Vikas last week?
  2. How far is Arjun's school from his house, and how does he usually get there?
  3. What does Arjun suggest for the weekend, and what does it depend on?

Writing Workshop

Write a connected paragraph describing one full day, real or imagined, that brings together several systems from across Volume I.

लेखन कार्य — Writing task: Write eight to ten sentences describing a full day. Your paragraph must include: (1) a morning routine description, (2) at least one location or family/home description with a postposition, (3) one shopping, food, or health event, (4) one completed past event using controlled ने, (5) a reason and result using क्योंकि or इसलिए, (6) a future plan or invitation, and (7) a supported opinion or comparison.

  1. Open the day. Describe your morning routine using habitual present.
  2. Describe a place. Use a postposition to locate your home, room, or neighborhood.
  3. Narrate an event. Describe one thing that happened using perfective forms, including controlled ने if the verb is transitive.
  4. Explain why. Connect two parts of your day with क्योंकि or इसलिए.
  5. Look ahead. State a future plan, invitation, or condition with अगर...तो.
  6. Give an opinion. Compare two things or share an opinion using मुझे लगता है कि... or a comparison pattern from Chapter 18.

संपादन सूची — Editing checklist:

  • Habitual, progressive, perfective, and future verb forms are each used correctly where intended.
  • Postpositions and oblique forms are correct before में, पर, and के पास.
  • ने is used correctly with transitive perfective verbs, and object agreement is checked where relevant.
  • क्योंकि, इसलिए, and अगर...तो are each used in the correct direction.
  • Spelling, matras, and word spacing are correct, and a danda (।) ends each sentence.
  • Romanization is not used for ordinary sentences; Devanagari stands on its own.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Checking in and showing care

Asking about someone's day, health, and well-being, as Sunita ji does with Arjun, is a common way to show care in many Hindi-speaking households and communities, though the exact phrasing, frequency, and formality vary by relationship, generation, and region.

Culture in Context — Hindi as one part of a larger linguistic picture

Across this volume, Hindi has appeared alongside Hindustani, Perso-Arabic-origin vocabulary, Sanskritic alternatives, and English loanwords, and it has been used by people from varied regions, households, and walks of life. As you continue into Volume II, remember that Hindi is one part of a multilingual reality across India and the diaspora, not the only language of any single community.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Switching verb systems without adjusting agreement

Error: Moving from habitual to perfective within one paragraph but keeping the same gender/number agreement throughout without checking it, for example writing वह जाता है and then वह गया for a female subject.

Correction: Recheck gender and number agreement every time you switch from one verb system to another: वह जाती है and वह गई for a female subject.

Common Mistake — Forgetting ने when narration shifts to a transitive verb

Error: Writing मैं किताब पढ़ी instead of using ने with a transitive perfective verb.

Correction: मैंने किताब पढ़ी। Check every past-tense transitive verb in your connected writing for ने.

Common Mistake — Overusing one connector for every link in a long paragraph

Error: Connecting every sentence in a paragraph with और or क्योंकि alone.

Correction: Vary connectors deliberately: use क्योंकि for reason, इसलिए for result, लेकिन for contrast, and अगर...तो for condition, as appropriate to what each sentence needs.

Answer Key

Model Dialogue — Comprehension

  1. Arjun went to the clinic in the morning because he didn't feel well, and afterward he went to the market and bought a new book.
  2. Arjun's evening plan to meet Rohan depends on whether he feels a little better (अगर मुझे थोड़ा अच्छा लगा, तो मैं ज़रूर जाऊँगा).

Listening

  • Priya woke up late because she couldn't sleep the night before.
  • In the morning, Priya and her mother went to the market and bought vegetables.
  • In the afternoon Priya got a headache while working, so she rested a little.
  • Meera told Priya that she is coming to their city next week.
  • If Meera gets time, they will eat together.

Reading — Comprehension

  1. Arjun couldn't meet Vikas last week because he was a little unwell.
  2. Arjun's school is a ten-minute walk from his house near the market; he usually walks there.
  3. Arjun suggests going to the market together and eating something this weekend, if Vikas is free.

Looking Ahead

अब आप क्या कर सकते हैं — What you can do now

You have completed Hindi Volume I. You can greet people and manage courtesy routines, introduce yourself and others, describe family, home, and daily life, manage food and shopping transactions, ask for directions, describe a health need, make a polite request, narrate a simple past event with controlled ने, make future plans and invitations, compare options and give supported opinions, connect ideas with reason, result, contrast, and condition, read practical Devanagari texts, and write a connected A2 paragraph — all while remaining aware that Hindi varies by register, region, script tradition, media, and community.

आगे बढ़ते हुए: हिंदी खंड II — Continuing on: Hindi Volume II

Hindi Volume II, रोज़मर्रा की स्वतंत्रता और जुड़ा हुआ संचार (Everyday Independence and Connected Communication), builds directly on this foundation. It develops stronger Devanagari reading fluency, broader postposition and oblique control, developed ergativity with ने, common compound verbs, expanded negation and questions, and more independent connected narration and explanation, moving learners from CEFR A2 toward B1.

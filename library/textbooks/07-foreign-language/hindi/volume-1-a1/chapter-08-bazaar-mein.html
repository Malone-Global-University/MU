बाज़ार में

Volume I, Chapter 8: Shopping · Speak and Write Hindi: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify common shopping items, clothing, and market goods in Devanagari.
  2. Ask for an item and ask its price using कितना and कितनी.
  3. State simple quantities using numbers with familiar nouns.
  4. Describe color, size, and quality using adjective agreement in scheduled noun phrases.
  5. Make polite shopping requests with दीजिए, बताइए, and दिखाइए.
  6. Accept, decline, or complete a simple purchase politely.
  7. Recognize nukta letters in common shopping vocabulary.
  8. Read a short advertisement, receipt, or shop sign in Devanagari.
  9. Write a short shopping list or simple product comparison.

Communication Scenario

Priya goes to a neighborhood market to buy a few things for her home. She stops at a clothing stall to ask about a kurta, then at a general store to ask the price of a few household items. The shopkeeper shows her items, states prices, and helps her complete a small purchase.

  1. This chapter introduces कितना and कितनी for asking about price and quantity, following the adjective-agreement pattern you began noticing with अच्छा/अच्छी/अच्छे in Chapter 3.
  2. You will also see your first nukta letters, such as ज़ and फ़, in familiar shopping words. For now, you only need to recognize these letters and understand why they look slightly different from their non-nukta counterparts.

Where This Chapter Fits

Chapter 8 continues Unit II, रोज़मर्रा की ज़िंदगी. It builds on the home and location language of Chapter 7 and introduces shopping, prices, and quantities.

Coming Into This Chapter

You can describe rooms and belongings, locate things with में, पर, and के पास, and expand मेरे पास ... है/नहीं है. You also know numbers up to 50 from Chapter 4.

Devanagari and Pronunciation

Chapter 8 introduces no new independent vowels or matras. It introduces nukta letters for the first time and continues building schwa deletion awareness through shopping vocabulary.

8.5 First look at nukta letters

Pronunciation Focus — What is a nukta?

A nukta is a small dot placed under certain Devanagari consonants to represent a sound not found in the original set of Hindi letters, often borrowed from Persian, Arabic, or English. You have already seen फ़ोन in Chapter 7. This chapter introduces ज़ (as in ज़्यादा) and reviews फ़ (as in फ़ोन). For now, simply recognize that the dot changes the sound: ज sounds like English "j," while ज़ sounds like English "z." A fuller set of nukta letters continues to develop across Chapters 9–18.

ज़्यादा
फ़ोन
सफ़ेद

Notice that ज/ज़ and फ/फ़ are written almost identically, differing only by the small dot. This chapter asks only for recognition of the difference, not full independent production of every nukta letter.

Pronunciation Focus — Schwa deletion in shopping vocabulary

Notice schwa deletion in familiar-looking words again: सस्ता is pronounced closer to "sastaa," and कितना keeps a light final vowel sound rather than a full extra syllable. This remains an awareness system built up gradually since Chapter 6, not a complete rule.

Pronunciation Focus — Number rhythm

Hindi numbers above twenty are not always predictable from their parts, and their rhythm can differ from English number pronunciation. Practice reading साठ, सत्तर, अस्सी, and नब्बे aloud slowly before using them in a price context.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter introduces price and quantity questions, adjective agreement with color and size words, a formulaic use of को, and polite request forms for shopping. You are not yet expected to control every function of को — only the controlled pattern shown here.

8.6 Asking price and quantity with कितना/कितनी

Language Building Block — कितना/कितनी
यह कुर्ता कितने का है? यह साड़ी कितने की है?
How much is this kurta? How much is this sari?
Language Focus — कितना agrees like an adjective

कितना changes form to agree with the noun it describes, the same way अच्छा/अच्छी/अच्छे did in Chapter 3: कितना (m.), कितनी (f.), कितने (m. pl. or before some postpositions). Use कितने का/कितने की for price questions and plain कितना/कितनी for quantity questions such as आपको कितना दूध चाहिए? ("How much milk do you need?").

8.7 Numbers with nouns

Language Building Block — Numeral-noun phrases
मुझे दो कुर्ते चाहिए। मुझे एक साड़ी चाहिए।
I need two kurtas. I need one sari.

Place the number directly before the noun, as in English. Continue using मुझे ... चाहिए from Chapter 6 to request items by quantity.

8.8 Describing color and size

Language Building Block — Color and size agreement
मुझे एक बड़ा नीला बैग चाहिए। मुझे एक छोटी सफ़ेद साड़ी चाहिए।
I want a big blue bag. I want a small white sari.
Language Focus — Variable and invariable color words

Some color and size words change with gender and number, like बड़ा/बड़ी/बड़े and नीला/नीली/नीले. Others do not change at all, such as लाल and सफ़ेद. Compare बड़ा बैग (m.) with बड़ी घड़ी (f.), but notice लाल बैग and लाल घड़ी stay the same. This distinction was introduced generally in Chapter 3 and continues to develop here with shopping vocabulary.

8.9 A formulaic look at को

Language Focus — को in आपको कितना चाहिए?

को is a postposition with several important functions in Hindi, most of which are developed in later chapters. For now, learn आपको and मुझको/मुझे as formulaic patterns in quantity questions and requests, such as आपको कितने चाहिए? ("How many do you need?"). Do not attempt to apply को productively beyond these controlled patterns yet; its fuller range of uses begins in Chapter 12.

8.10 Polite shopping requests

Language Building Block — दीजिए, बताइए, and दिखाइए
यह मुझे दीजिए। कीमत बताइए। वह साड़ी दिखाइए।
Please give me this. Please tell me the price. Please show me that sari.

Learn these as complete polite formulas for now, following the same pattern as कृपया from Chapter 1. Full imperative-level analysis continues to develop in later chapters.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Priya stops at a clothing stall in the market and speaks with the shopkeeper.

प्रिया नमस्ते! यह कुर्ता कितने का है? Namaste! Yah kurtaa kitne kaa hai? Hello! How much is this kurta?
दुकानदार यह तीन सौ रुपये का है। बहुत अच्छा कपड़ा है। Yah teen sau rupaye kaa hai. Bahut achchhaa kapḍaa hai. This one is three hundred rupees. It's very good fabric.
प्रिया क्या आपके पास छोटा नीला कुर्ता है? Kyaa aapke paas chhotaa neelaa kurtaa hai? Do you have a small blue kurta?
दुकानदार जी हाँ, दिखाता हूँ। यह देखिए — यह सस्ता भी है। Jee haaँ, dikhaataa hooँ. Yah dekhie — yah sastaa bhee hai. Yes, let me show you. Here — this one is cheap too.
प्रिया अच्छा। मुझे यह चाहिए। और यह घड़ी कितने की है? Achchhaa. Mujhe yah chaahie. Aur yah ghaḍee kitne kee hai? Good. I want this one. And how much is this watch?
दुकानदार यह घड़ी थोड़ी महँगी है — पाँच सौ रुपये की। Yah ghaḍee thoḍee mahँgee hai — paaँch sau rupaye kee. This watch is a bit expensive — five hundred rupees.
प्रिया ठीक है, सिर्फ़ कुर्ता दीजिए। धन्यवाद! Ṭheek hai, sirf kurtaa deejie. Dhanyavaad! Okay, just give me the kurta. Thank you!

समझ के प्रश्न — Comprehension questions:

  1. कुर्ता कितने का है? (How much is the kurta?)
  2. घड़ी कितने की है? (How much is the watch?)
  3. प्रिया क्या खरीदती है? (What does Priya buy?)

Writing Workshop

Write a short shopping list or product comparison, combining this chapter's shopping language with vocabulary and patterns from earlier chapters.

लेखन कार्य — Writing task: Write four to six sentences in Devanagari listing what you want to buy, describing color or size, and comparing prices of two items.

  1. State what you want. Use मुझे ... चाहिए from Chapter 6.
  2. Describe one item's color or size. Use an agreement pattern such as बड़ा/बड़ी or नीला/नीली.
  3. Ask or state a price. Use कितने का/की है? or a price statement such as ... रुपये का/की है.
  4. Compare two items. Use सस्ता/सस्ती and महँगा/महँगी.
  5. Check your Devanagari. Compare your spelling of shopping words against the vocabulary tables above, and check any nukta letters like ज़ and फ़.

संपादन सूची — Editing checklist:

  • कितना/कितनी appears at least once, correctly agreeing with its noun.
  • At least one color or size word agrees correctly with its noun.
  • मुझे ... चाहिए appears at least once.
  • Shopping and price vocabulary is spelled correctly in Devanagari, including nukta letters where needed.
  • A danda (।) ends each sentence.

Speaking Practice

नियंत्रित अभ्यास — Controlled practice: reading the model dialogue aloud

Read the model dialogue above aloud twice: once slowly, sounding out each word, and once at a more natural pace. Pay attention to the nukta sound in सिर्फ़ and to number rhythm in तीन सौ and पाँच सौ.

संचार कार्य — Communicative task: shopping role-play

With a partner, act out a shopping exchange. One person plays the customer and asks for an item, its price, and a color or size preference. The other plays the shopkeeper and answers with a price, using at least one polite expression such as दिखाइए or बताइए.

अकेले अभ्यास — Solo alternative

Record yourself reading the advertisement in the Reading Practice section aloud, then describe which item you would buy and why, using at least one price comparison with सस्ता or महँगा.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Market interactions vary

Shopping customs differ widely across Hindi-speaking communities. Some shops use fixed prices, while informal markets may allow some negotiation. This varies by city, region, store type, and relationship with the shopkeeper. This chapter does not assume that bargaining is universal or expected everywhere.

Culture in Context — Polite requests in service settings

Using polite forms such as दीजिए and बताइए rather than blunt commands is generally expected in shops, markets, and other service interactions, regardless of the customer's familiarity with the shopkeeper.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Forgetting कितना agreement

Error: Saying यह साड़ी कितना का है? instead of यह साड़ी कितने की है?

Correction: कितना agrees with the noun's gender, just like अच्छा/अच्छी. Since साड़ी is feminine, use कितने की.

Common Mistake — Confusing ज and ज़

Error: Reading ज़्यादा as if it were spelled with a plain ज.

Correction: The dot under ज़ changes the sound from English "j" to English "z." Look carefully for the nukta dot in shopping and quantity words.

Common Mistake — Putting the color or size word after the noun

Error: Saying बैग नीला for "blue bag" as if it were a full sentence.

Correction: As a noun-phrase description, place the adjective before the noun: नीला बैग. Use बैग नीला है only when making a complete sentence, "The bag is blue."

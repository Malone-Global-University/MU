पुनरावृत्ति 2: रोज़मर्रा की ज़िंदगी

Volume I, Chapter 10: Unit II Assessment · Speak and Write Hindi: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Order food and drink politely, stating preferences and needs.
  2. Describe a home and neighborhood and locate people and objects within it.
  3. Shop for an item, ask its price and quantity, and complete a polite transaction.
  4. Describe a daily routine using the habitual present with correct gender agreement.
  5. Tell time and use पर, से, and तक in controlled time expressions.
  6. Use नहीं productively to negate habitual statements.
  7. Read practical Devanagari texts, including a menu, a housing note, an advertisement, and a schedule.
  8. Write a short connected message describing a practical daily plan.

Communication Scenario

Priya is writing to a new pen pal, Vikram, describing an ordinary day: what she eats, where she lives, an errand she runs at the market, and her regular schedule. Vikram writes back with questions and shares a few details about his own day. Their exchange draws together everything you have practiced since Chapter 6.

  1. This chapter introduces no new grammar, vocabulary domain, or pronunciation system. It integrates Chapters 6-9 into one connected performance.
  2. Everything you complete in this chapter should already be familiar. If a pattern feels unfamiliar, return to the chapter where it was introduced before continuing.

Where This Chapter Fits

Chapter 10 closes Unit II, रोज़मर्रा की ज़िंदगी. It does not introduce new material. Instead, it consolidates food and drink (Chapter 6), home and location (Chapter 7), shopping (Chapter 8), and daily routine (Chapter 9) into a single integrated performance, and prepares you for Unit III, which begins with directions and city life.

Coming Into This Chapter

You can request food politely, describe a home and locate things within it, shop and ask prices with कितना/कितनी, describe a daily routine with the habitual present, tell time with पर, से, and तक, and negate a habitual statement with नहीं.

New in This Chapter

No new grammar, vocabulary domain, or pronunciation system. This chapter integrates and assesses Chapters 6-9.

Looking Forward

Devanagari and Pronunciation Review

Chapter 10 introduces no new letters, matras, or nukta forms. It reviews and consolidates the pronunciation systems active across Chapters 6-9.

Pronunciation Review — Schwa deletion across four chapters

Reread the food vocabulary of Chapter 6, the home vocabulary of Chapter 7, and the routine verbs of Chapter 9 aloud. In each case, remember that the written अ is not always pronounced: उठना is closer to "uṭhnaa" than "u-ṭha-na," and कमरा keeps a light final vowel rather than a full extra syllable.

Pronunciation Review — Nukta letters from Chapter 8

Review the nukta letters introduced receptively in Chapter 8, such as फ़ in loanwords. Recognizing nukta spelling remains receptive at this stage; you are not expected to produce every nukta contrast independently yet.

Pronunciation Review — Aspiration and dental-retroflex contrast

Practice contrasts you have built since Chapter 2, such as पढ़ना versus पड़ना, and तक versus टक-type retroflex environments in the routine and shopping vocabulary.

Pronunciation Review — Number and time rhythm

Review the pronunciation rhythm of prices from Chapter 8 and clock times from Chapter 9, such as साढ़े छह बजे. Read phrases as connected units rather than isolated words.

Grammar Review

This chapter introduces no new grammar. It reviews and integrates the systems activated in Chapters 6-9.

10.2 Preferences and needs

Language Building Block — Review: preferences and needs
मुझे चाय पसंद है। मुझे पानी चाहिए।
I like tea. I need water.

Review मुझे ... पसंद है and मुझे ... चाहिए from Chapter 6 for stating preferences and needs politely.

10.3 Location and possession

Language Building Block — Review: postpositions of location
किताब मेज़ पर है। घर के पास एक बाज़ार है।
The book is on the table. There is a market near the house.

Review में, पर, के पास, के सामने, के पीछे, and के अंदर from Chapter 7, along with oblique singular awareness before these postpositions.

10.4 Quantity and price

Language Building Block — Review: कितना/कितनी
यह कितने का है? यह किताब कितनी अच्छी है?
How much is this? How good is this book?

Review कितना/कितनी from Chapter 8 for asking about price and quantity in a shopping context.

10.5 Habitual present, time, and negation

Language Building Block — Review: habitual present with time and negation
मैं सुबह सात बजे उठती हूँ। मैं रात को देर तक नहीं जागती।
I get up at seven o'clock in the morning. I do not stay awake late at night.
Language Focus — Integrating the habitual present, time, and negation

Review the habitual present from Chapter 9, including gender agreement in -ता/-ती/-ते forms, the time postpositions पर, से, and तक, frequency adverbs such as हर दिन and कभी-कभी, and the placement of नहीं directly before the verb phrase.

Boundary: This chapter does not introduce the progressive as a full system, the perfective, ergative ने, new postposition functions, or any new oblique pattern. Those systems remain scheduled for Unit III and beyond.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Priya reads part of her letter aloud to her roommate before sending it to her pen pal Vikram.

प्रिया प्रिय विक्रम, मैं हर दिन साढ़े छह बजे उठती हूँ। नाश्ते में मुझे चाय और थोड़ी रोटी पसंद है। Priy Vikram, maiँ har din saaḍhe chhah baje uṭhtee hooँ. Naashte meँ mujhe chaay aur thoḍee roṭee pasand hai. Dear Vikram, I get up at half past six every day. For breakfast, I like tea and a little bread.
रूममेट तुम अपने घर के बारे में भी लिखो। तुम्हारा कमरा कैसा है? Tum apne ghar ke baare meँ bhee likho. Tumhaaraa kamraa kaisaa hai? Write about your home too. What is your room like?
प्रिया मेरा कमरा छोटा है, लेकिन खिड़की के पास एक मेज़ है। घर के सामने एक छोटा बाज़ार है। Meraa kamraa chhoṭaa hai, lekin khiḍkee ke paas ek mez hai. Ghar ke saamne ek chhoṭaa baazaar hai. My room is small, but there is a table near the window. There is a small market in front of the house.
रूममेट क्या तुमने आज बाज़ार से कुछ ख़रीदा? Kyaa tumne aaj baazaar se kuchh ḳhareedaa? Did you buy something from the market today?
प्रिया हाँ, मैंने एक कमीज़ देखी। दुकानदार ने कहा, "यह तीन सौ रुपये की है।" मैं शाम को काम पर जाती हूँ, इसलिए मैंने जल्दी ख़रीदा। Haaँ, maiँne ek kameez dekhee. Dukaandaar ne kahaa, "Yah teen sau rupaye kee hai." Maiँ shaam ko kaam par jaatee hooँ, isalie maiँne jaldee ḳhareedaa. Yes, I saw a shirt. The shopkeeper said, "This is three hundred rupees." I go to work in the evening, so I bought it quickly.
रूममेट अच्छा! तुम्हारा दिन तो व्यस्त है। Achchhaa! Tumhaaraa din to vyast hai. Good! Your day sounds busy.
Note on this dialogue

The verb forms देखी and ख़रीदा, and the quoted दुकानदार ने कहा, appear here only as unanalyzed, glossed color for a natural shopping story. They are not taught, practiced, or assessed in this chapter. Perfective forms and ने remain scheduled for Chapters 14 and 16.

समझ के प्रश्न — Comprehension questions:

  1. प्रिया नाश्ते में क्या पसंद करती है? (What does Priya like for breakfast?)
  2. प्रिया के घर के सामने क्या है? (What is in front of Priya's house?)
  3. प्रिया शाम को कहाँ जाती है? (Where does Priya go in the evening?)

Reading Practice

Read each of the four short practical texts below, sounding out the Devanagari carefully, then answer the questions. Together they draw on food, home, shopping, and routine language from Chapters 6-9.

मेन्यू — Café Menu
चाय — 20 रुपये
कॉफ़ी — 30 रुपये
समोसा — 15 रुपये
रोटी और सब्ज़ी — 60 रुपये
Tea — 20 rupees. Coffee — 30 rupees. Samosa — 15 rupees. Bread and vegetables — 60 rupees.
कमरा किराए पर — Room for Rent
एक कमरा किराए पर है। कमरा घर के अंदर, रसोई के पास है। बाज़ार घर के सामने है।
A room is for rent. The room is inside the house, near the kitchen. The market is in front of the house.
विज्ञापन — Advertisement
नई कमीज़ें! रंग: नीला, लाल, सफ़ेद। दाम: 300 रुपये से 500 रुपये तक।
New shirts! Colors: blue, red, white. Price: from 300 rupees to 500 rupees.
समय-सारिणी — Schedule
सोमवार से शनिवार:
सुबह आठ बजे से दोपहर एक बजे तक — कक्षा
दोपहर दो बजे से शाम छह बजे तक — काम

  1. How much does a samosa cost on the café menu?
  2. Where exactly is the room for rent located?
  3. What is the price range for the shirts in the advertisement?
  4. According to the schedule, from what time to what time is class held?

Writing Workshop

Write a short connected message that combines food, home, shopping, and routine language from Chapters 6-9. No new grammar or vocabulary is required or expected.

लेखन कार्य — Writing task: Write a short letter or message of six to nine sentences in Devanagari to a pen pal, describing a typical day. Include at least one food preference or need, one description of your home or neighborhood, one shopping detail, and one routine sentence with a time expression and a negative statement.

  1. Greet your pen pal and say when you get up. Use a habitual present form with a time expression.
  2. Describe what you eat or drink and one preference or need. Use मुझे ... पसंद है or मुझे ... चाहिए.
  3. Describe your home or neighborhood. Use at least one postposition of location, such as में, पर, or के पास.
  4. Mention something you bought or want to buy. Use कितना/कितनी or a price expression.
  5. Describe your work or study schedule. Use से and तक.
  6. Include one negative sentence. Use नहीं to say something you do not do.
  7. Close your message politely. Use a courtesy formula from Chapter 1.

संपादन सूची — Editing checklist:

  • Habitual present verbs agree correctly with the subject's gender.
  • Postpositions of location are used correctly with oblique awareness.
  • At least one price or quantity expression with कितना/कितनी appears.
  • At least one correctly placed negative sentence with नहीं appears.
  • A danda (।) ends each sentence, and spelling matches vocabulary from Chapters 6-9.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Everyday life is not uniform

The food, housing, shopping, and routine patterns practiced across Chapters 6-9 vary widely across Hindi-speaking communities by region, income, family structure, and setting. No single practical day represents all Hindi-speaking life.

Culture in Context — Politeness across everyday transactions

Notice how the same courtesy expectations from Chapter 1 continue to apply across food requests, shopping exchanges, and casual conversation about routines. Politeness is not confined to formal situations; it appears throughout ordinary daily interaction.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Mixing up पसंद है and चाहिए

Error: Saying मुझे पानी पसंद है when asking for water to be brought right now.

Correction: Use चाहिए for a current need or request, and पसंद है for a general preference: मुझे पानी चाहिए।

Common Mistake — Forgetting gender agreement across a longer message

Error: A woman writing मैं उठता हूँ in the first sentence and correctly writing जाती हूँ later in the same message.

Correction: Check every habitual verb in a connected text for consistent gender agreement, not only the first one.

Common Mistake — Reversing से and तक in a written schedule

Error: Writing छह बजे तक नौ बजे से when describing a work schedule.

Correction: Keep the starting point with से and the ending point with तक in that order: नौ बजे से छह बजे तक.

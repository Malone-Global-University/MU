स्वास्थ्य और भावनाएँ

Volume I, Chapter 12: Health and Feelings · Speak and Write Hindi: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Name common body parts.
  2. Describe a simple symptom or discomfort.
  3. Say how you feel using the experiencer construction with को.
  4. Express hunger, thirst, pain, and basic need using patterns such as मुझे भूख लगी है and मुझे दर्द है.
  5. Ask for help or request a health-related appointment politely.
  6. Understand simple advice given with चाहिए.
  7. Read a clinic form, appointment note, or simple health instruction.
  8. Write a brief health message or advice note.

Communication Scenario

Anil wakes up feeling unwell before work. He calls his friend Meena, who happens to have some free time, and she suggests he visit a nearby clinic. At the clinic, Anil describes his symptoms to the receptionist and receives simple advice.

  1. Notice how Anil describes what he feels using मुझे rather than मैं as the subject.
  2. Notice how Meena and the receptionist give advice using चाहिए.

Where This Chapter Fits

Chapter 12 continues Unit III, शहर, स्वास्थ्य, पढ़ाई और काम. It builds on the movement and polite-request language of Chapter 11 and extends the formulaic मुझे ... चाहिए pattern from Chapter 6 into a fuller experiencer system.

Coming Into This Chapter

You can ask for and give directions, make polite requests with क्या आप ... सकते हैं?, and use मुझे ... पसंद है and मुझे ... चाहिए from earlier chapters.

New in This Chapter

Body parts and symptoms; the experiencer construction with को (मुझे, आपको); patterns such as मुझे दर्द है, मुझे भूख लगी है, and मुझे प्यास लगी है; चाहिए expanded for advice; and the progressive introduced formulaically in feeling expressions such as मुझे अच्छा लग रहा है.

Looking Forward

Chapter 13, पढ़ाई और काम, develops the progressive as a full productive system and introduces ability with सकना in school and work contexts.

Devanagari and Pronunciation

This chapter introduces no new letters or matras. It revisits nasalization, anusvara, and chandrabindu contrast, and develops schwa deletion in familiar health vocabulary.

Pronunciation Focus — Nasalization review: आँख and दाँत

The chandrabindu in आँख and दाँत marks nasalization on the vowel. Practice these words alongside their non-nasalized look-alikes from earlier chapters to keep the contrast clear.

Pronunciation Focus — Schwa deletion in health vocabulary

Practice reading थकान and अपॉइंटमेंट aloud. Remember that दर्द is read as a single tight syllable cluster rather than "da-ra-da."

Pronunciation Focus — Concern, request, and advice intonation

Read मुझे बुखार है। with a falling, concerned tone, and आपको आराम करना चाहिए। with a gentle, advising tone rather than a flat command.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

12.4 The experiencer construction with को

Language Building Block — मुझे and आपको
मुझे सिरदर्द है। आपको बुखार है। उसे खाँसी है।
I have a headache. You have a fever. He/she has a cough.
Language Focus — Why Hindi says "to me" rather than "I have"

Hindi describes many physical sensations, needs, and feelings from the experiencer's point of view rather than treating the person as the grammatical subject of "have." मुझे is the contracted form of मुझको ("to me"), and आपको means "to you." The thing experienced, such as दर्द or बुखार, functions as the grammatical subject, and है or हैं agrees with it. Do not translate this pattern word-for-word using English "have."

12.5 Expanded formulas with लगना

Language Building Block — भूख लगना and प्यास लगना
मुझे भूख लगी है। मुझे प्यास लगी है। मुझे ठंड लग रही है।
I am hungry (literally: hunger is felt to me). I am thirsty. I feel cold.

You met मुझे ... चाहिए formulaically in Chapter 6. This chapter develops the related को + लगना pattern for hunger, thirst, and temperature sensations. मुझे ठंड लग रही है uses the progressive formulaically to describe a feeling in progress; it is a fixed expression at this stage, not yet a fully analyzed progressive system.

12.6 Feeling good or bad: अच्छा/बुरा लगना

Language Building Block — मुझे अच्छा/बुरा लग रहा है
मुझे आज अच्छा नहीं लग रहा है। मुझे बेहतर लग रहा है।
I am not feeling well today. I am feeling better.

This pattern is controlled and formulaic. Learners should recognize and use the whole expression rather than analyzing लगना as a general verb yet.

12.7 Advice and need with expanded चाहिए

Language Building Block — चाहिए for advice
आपको आराम करना चाहिए। आपको डॉक्टर के पास जाना चाहिए।
You should rest. You should go to the doctor.

चाहिए was introduced with nouns in Chapter 6 (मुझे पानी चाहिए). This chapter extends it to infinitive-plus-चाहिए patterns for giving advice, following the same को experiencer subject.

12.8 Polite help requests

Language Building Block — Asking for help
क्या आप मेरी मदद कर सकते हैं? मुझे मदद चाहिए।
Can you help me? I need help.

Combine this chapter's को patterns with the polite request formula from Chapter 11 (क्या आप ... सकते हैं?) to ask for help in a health context.

Boundary: This chapter does not introduce a full medical register, complex advice grammar, or the subjunctive or imperative as full systems. लगना in feeling expressions and the progressive in लग रहा है remain formulaic, not analyzed.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Anil calls his friend Meena because he is not feeling well.

अनिल नमस्ते मीना जी। मुझे माफ़ कीजिए, पर मुझे आज अच्छा नहीं लग रहा है। Namaste Meenaa jee. Mujhe maaf keejie, par mujhe aaj achchaa naheeँ lag rahaa hai. Hello Meena ji. Excuse me, but I'm not feeling well today.
मीना अरे, क्या हुआ? क्या आपको बुखार है? Are, kyaa huaa? Kyaa aapko bukhaar hai? Oh, what happened? Do you have a fever?
अनिल हाँ, थोड़ा बुखार है, और मुझे सिरदर्द भी है। गला भी थोड़ा ख़राब है। Haaँ, thoḍaa bukhaar hai, aur mujhe siradard bhee hai. Galaa bhee thoḍaa ḳharaab hai. Yes, I have a little fever, and I also have a headache. My throat is also a bit bad.
मीना आपको आराम करना चाहिए, और पानी ज़्यादा पीना चाहिए। क्या आप डॉक्टर के पास जाएँगे? Aapko aaraam karnaa chaahie, aur paanee zyaadaa peenaa chaahie. Kyaa aap ḍoktar ke paas jaaeँge? You should rest, and you should drink more water. Will you go to the doctor?
अनिल हाँ, मैं क्लिनिक जाऊँगा। धन्यवाद, मीना जी। Haaँ, maiँ klinik jaaooँgaa. Dhanyavaad, Meenaa jee. Yes, I will go to the clinic. Thank you, Meena ji.

Setting: At the clinic, a receptionist asks Anil about his symptoms.

रिसेप्शनिस्ट नमस्ते। आपको क्या तकलीफ़ है? Namaste. Aapko kyaa takleef hai? Hello. What is bothering you?
अनिल मुझे बुखार और सिरदर्द है, और गला भी ख़राब है। Mujhe bukhaar aur siradard hai, aur galaa bhee ḳharaab hai. I have a fever and a headache, and my throat is also bad.
रिसेप्शनिस्ट ठीक है। आपको थोड़ा आराम करना चाहिए। डॉक्टर आपको अभी देखेंगे। Ṭheek hai. Aapko thoḍaa aaraam karnaa chaahie. Ḍoktar aapko abhee dekheँge. Okay. You should rest a little. The doctor will see you now.
Note on this dialogue

Meena's closing question, क्या आप डॉक्टर के पास जाएँगे?, and the receptionist's डॉक्टर आपको अभी देखेंगे, use future forms. Future forms are not taught productively until Chapter 17; here they appear only as natural, glossed lines and are not tested in this chapter.

समझ के प्रश्न — Comprehension questions:

  1. अनिल को क्या तकलीफ़ है? (What is bothering Anil?)
  2. मीना अनिल को क्या सलाह देती है? (What advice does Meena give Anil?)
  3. अनिल कहाँ जाएगा? (Where will Anil go?)

Writing Workshop

Write a short health message in Devanagari to a friend or a clinic.

लेखन कार्य — Writing task: Write five to seven sentences describing how you feel today (real or imagined), naming at least one symptom, and asking for or giving one piece of advice.

  1. Greet the reader. Use a courtesy formula from Chapter 1.
  2. Say how you feel. Use मुझे अच्छा नहीं लग रहा है or a similar formula.
  3. Name a symptom. Use the को experiencer pattern, such as मुझे ... है.
  4. Give or ask for advice. Use चाहिए with an infinitive.
  5. Ask for help if appropriate. Use क्या आप ... सकते हैं? from Chapter 11.
  6. Close politely. Use a closing courtesy formula.

संपादन सूची — Editing checklist:

  • The experiencer construction with मुझे or आपको is used correctly, not translated word-for-word from English "have."
  • At least one symptom or feeling word from this chapter's vocabulary appears.
  • चाहिए is used correctly with an infinitive for advice.
  • A polite request formula from Chapter 11 is used correctly, if included.
  • A danda (।) ends each sentence, and spelling matches this chapter's vocabulary.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Health communication requires tact

Describing symptoms and asking for help calls for polite, measured language, often softened with courtesy formulas. Family members, friends, pharmacists, and clinic staff may all be involved in a health conversation, and the relationship between speakers shapes how directly advice is given.

Culture in Context — Clinics, pharmacies, and family care vary widely

Health-seeking practices differ by region, household, income, and setting. Some people first consult family members or a local pharmacist, while others go directly to a clinic or hospital. No single pattern represents all Hindi-speaking communities.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Translating "I have a fever" with मैं

Error: Saying मैं बुखार हूँ, translating English "I have" directly with मैं as the subject.

Correction: Use the experiencer construction: मुझे बुखार है. The symptom, not the person, is the grammatical subject.

Common Mistake — Wrong agreement with है/हैं in experiencer sentences

Error: Saying मुझे बुखार हैं when बुखार is a singular noun.

Correction: Match है or हैं to the experienced noun's number: मुझे बुखार है (singular).

Common Mistake — Overusing चाहिए as a blunt command

Error: Using चाहिए in a flat, commanding tone as if it were an order.

Correction: Use चाहिए with a gentle, advising tone, and pair it with courtesy formulas where appropriate, such as शायद आपको आराम करना चाहिए.

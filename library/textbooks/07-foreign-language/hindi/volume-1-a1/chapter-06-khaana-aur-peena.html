खाना और पीना

Volume I, Chapter 6: Food and Drink · Speak and Write Hindi: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify common foods and drinks in Devanagari.
  2. Say what you like and do not like using मुझे ... पसंद है and मुझे ... पसंद नहीं है.
  3. Express a need or make a polite request using मुझे ... चाहिए.
  4. Talk about ordinary eating and drinking habits using मैं ... खाता/खाती हूँ and मैं ... पीता/पीती हूँ.
  5. Order food or drink politely and accept or decline an offer.
  6. Ask for a basic quantity using familiar numbers.
  7. Read a short menu or meal list in Devanagari.
  8. Write a short statement of preferences and a simple meal plan.

Communication Scenario

Anil's family is having a small evening meal at home, and his friend Priya has come to visit. Anil's mother offers Priya something to eat and drink. Priya says what she likes, asks a question about the food, and politely says what she needs. Later, at a small café near school, Anil and Priya order tea and a snack together.

  1. This chapter introduces food and drink vocabulary together with three new formulaic patterns: पसंद है, चाहिए, and simple habitual sentences with खाना and पीना.
  2. You are not yet expected to control the full present-habitual verb system for every person, or any object-marking system. Keep your sentences close to the models below.

Where This Chapter Fits

Chapter 6 opens Unit II, रोज़मर्रा की ज़िंदगी. It builds directly on the greetings, identity, possessives, and classroom language from Unit I, and it introduces the first controlled use of a habitual-present verb pattern.

Coming Into This Chapter

You can greet people, introduce yourself and others, describe a family member or friend with मेरा/मेरी/मेरे, identify classroom objects, express possession with मेरे पास ... है, and read short Devanagari sentences.

New in This Chapter

Food and drink vocabulary; मुझे ... पसंद है and मुझे ... पसंद नहीं है; मुझे ... चाहिए; a first, limited look at habitual-present verb forms with खाना and पीना; and polite service expressions with कृपया and दीजिए.

Looking Forward

Pronunciation Focus — Schwa deletion in food words

Chapter 4 introduced schwa deletion as an awareness system. Notice it again here: खाना is pronounced closer to "khaanaa," and दोपहर का खाना drops the final inherent vowel in ordinary speech in the same way कलम did in Chapter 4. This awareness continues to build gradually and is not yet a complete rule.

Pronunciation Focus — फ versus फ़ (receptive)

फल ("fruit") uses the plain aspirated फ. Some food and loanwords you will meet later use the nukta letter फ़, which is closer to an English "f" sound. You are only asked to recognize this difference for now; nukta letters become a scheduled system starting in Chapter 8.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter introduces three closely related formulaic and controlled patterns for talking about food, drink, needs, and habits. None of these patterns requires you to analyze the full Hindi verb or postposition system yet.

6.5 Expressing likes and dislikes

Language Building Block — मुझे ... पसंद है
मुझे चाय पसंद है। मुझे चावल पसंद नहीं है।
I like tea. (literally: "to me tea is pleasing") I do not like rice.
Language Focus — Why मुझे, not मैं?

Hindi does not say "I like tea" the way English does. It says something closer to "to me, tea is pleasing," using मुझे ("to me") instead of मैं ("I"). This is called an experiencer construction, and you will meet more examples like it later in the volume. For now, simply learn मुझे ... पसंद है and मुझे ... पसंद नहीं है as complete formulas with familiar food words.

6.6 Expressing need with चाहिए

Language Building Block — मुझे ... चाहिए
मुझे एक कप चाय चाहिए, कृपया।
I need/would like a cup of tea, please.

चाहिए works like पसंद है: it follows मुझे and the thing you need or want. Adding कृपया makes the request polite, in the same way कृपया and माफ़ कीजिए softened requests in Chapter 4.

6.7 Talking about ordinary eating and drinking

Language Building Block — खाना and पीना in limited habitual frames
मैं चावल खाता हूँ। मैं चाय पीती हूँ।
I eat rice. (male speaker) I drink tea. (female speaker)
Grammar Note — A limited, controlled frame only

Notice that खाता changes to खाती depending on whether the speaker is male or female — this is the same kind of gender agreement you saw with अच्छा/अच्छी in Chapter 3. In this chapter, practice only first-person sentences (मैं ... खाता/खाती हूँ, मैं ... पीता/पीती हूँ) with familiar food words. The full present-habitual system, with all persons and more verbs, is introduced in Chapter 9. Do not extend this pattern to other verbs or other subjects yet.

6.8 Accepting and declining politely

Language Building Block — Accepting or declining an offer
जी हाँ, धन्यवाद। / जी नहीं, धन्यवाद।
Yes, thank you. / No, thank you.

Direct objects such as चाय and चावल appear in these sentences without any object marker. This is intentional: broad object-marking with को is not introduced until later chapters, so keep your object nouns simple and unmarked for now.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Priya visits Anil's home in the evening. Anil's mother offers her food and drink.

अनिल की मम्मी नमस्ते, प्रिया! आपको चाय पसंद है? Namaste, Priyaa! Aapko chaay pasand hai? Hello, Priya! Do you like tea?
प्रिया जी हाँ, मुझे चाय बहुत पसंद है। धन्यवाद! Jee haaँ, mujhe chaay bahut pasand hai. Dhanyavaad! Yes, I like tea very much. Thank you!
अनिल की मम्मी और खाने में क्या पसंद है? रोटी, चावल, या दोनों? Aur khaane meँ kyaa pasand hai? Rotee, chaaval, yaa donoँ? And what do you like to eat? Bread, rice, or both?
प्रिया मुझे रोटी पसंद है। मुझे चावल पसंद नहीं है। Mujhe rotee pasand hai. Mujhe chaaval pasand naheeँ hai. I like bread. I don't like rice.
अनिल की मम्मी ठीक है। और कुछ चाहिए? Ṭheek hai. Aur kuchh chaahiye? Okay. Do you need anything else?
प्रिया मुझे थोड़ा पानी चाहिए, कृपया। बस, धन्यवाद। Mujhe thoṛaa paanee chaahiye, kripayaa. Bas, dhanyavaad. I need a little water, please. That's all, thank you.

समझ के प्रश्न — Comprehension questions:

  1. प्रिया को क्या पसंद है? (What does Priya like?)
  2. प्रिया को क्या पसंद नहीं है? (What does Priya not like?)
  3. प्रिया को और क्या चाहिए? (What else does Priya need?)

Writing Workshop

Write a short statement of your food and drink preferences, combining this chapter's patterns with the possessives and adjectives you already know from Unit I.

लेखन कार्य — Writing task: Write four to six sentences in Devanagari describing what you like, what you do not like, what you need, and what you eat or drink regularly.

  1. State one thing you like. Use मुझे ... पसंद है.
  2. State one thing you do not like. Use मुझे ... पसंद नहीं है.
  3. State one thing you need. Use मुझे ... चाहिए, with कृपया if it is a request.
  4. Describe one habit. Use मैं ... खाता/खाती हूँ or मैं ... पीता/पीती हूँ with a familiar food word.
  5. Check your Devanagari. Compare your spelling of food words against the vocabulary tables above.

संपादन सूची — Editing checklist:

  • मुझे ... पसंद है and मुझे ... पसंद नहीं है each appear at least once, correctly.
  • मुझे ... चाहिए appears at least once, correctly.
  • One habitual sentence with खाना or पीना agrees correctly with the speaker's gender.
  • Food and drink words are spelled correctly in Devanagari, without unnecessary romanization.
  • A danda (।) ends each sentence.

Speaking Practice

नियंत्रित अभ्यास — Controlled practice: reading the model dialogue aloud

Read the model dialogue above aloud twice: once slowly, sounding out each word, and once at a more natural pace. Pay attention to schwa deletion in खाना, चाहिए, and धन्यवाद.

संचार कार्य — Communicative task: offering and requesting food

With a partner, act out a host offering food and drink and a guest responding. The host should ask आपको ... पसंद है? for at least two items. The guest should accept at least one item, decline at least one item, and make one polite request with चाहिए.

अकेले अभ्यास — Solo alternative

Record yourself ordering from the café menu in the Reading Practice section above. State one thing you like, one thing you need, and thank the "server" politely. Listen back and check your schwa deletion and word stress.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Offering food as hospitality

Offering a guest something to eat or drink is a common courtesy in many Hindi-speaking homes, though the specific foods, timing, and level of insistence vary by family, region, and setting. Accepting or politely declining, as Priya does above, is a normal part of the exchange.

Culture in Context — Food practices are not one single pattern

Food habits among Hindi-speaking communities vary widely by region, religion, household, and personal choice. This chapter's foods and preferences are examples for practicing the language, not a description of what every Hindi-speaking person eats or avoids.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Saying मैं पसंद हूँ for "I like"

Error: Translating "I like tea" word-for-word as मैं पसंद हूँ चाय.

Correction: Use मुझे चाय पसंद है. The person who likes something is marked with मुझे ("to me"), not मैं.

Common Mistake — Forgetting gender agreement in खाता/खाती

Error: A female speaker saying मैं चावल खाता हूँ.

Correction: A female speaker should say मैं चावल खाती हूँ. This is the same gender agreement pattern as अच्छा/अच्छी from Chapter 3.

Common Mistake — Overusing चाहिए for simple preferences

Error: Using मुझे चाय चाहिए when you simply mean you enjoy tea in general.

Correction: Use मुझे चाय पसंद है for a general preference and reserve मुझे चाय चाहिए for when you want or need tea right now.

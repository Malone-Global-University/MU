मेरा दिन

Volume I, Chapter 9: Daily Routine · Speak and Write Hindi: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Describe a daily routine using the habitual present with common verbs.
  2. Use correct gender agreement in habitual verb forms such as जाता/जाती/जाते.
  3. Ask others about their routines with क्या आप ... हैं? patterns.
  4. Tell time in controlled contexts and use पर, से, and तक with time expressions.
  5. State when activities occur using days, parts of the day, and clock time.
  6. Use frequency expressions such as हर दिन, कभी-कभी, and हमेशा.
  7. Say what you usually do or do not do, using fuller productive negation with नहीं.
  8. Read a simple schedule or routine account in Devanagari.
  9. Write a short daily routine paragraph.

Communication Scenario

Priya's cousin Arjun calls to ask about her new routine now that she has moved to a new neighborhood. Priya describes what she does each day, from morning to evening, and asks Arjun about his own schedule. They compare when they wake up, go to work or school, and rest.

  1. This chapter introduces the habitual present as your first fully productive verb system, building on the copula and formulaic patterns you have used since Chapter 1.
  2. You will also use नहीं productively for the first time to say what someone does not do as part of a routine, beyond the formulaic negative you saw briefly in Chapter 1.

Devanagari and Pronunciation

Chapter 9 introduces no new independent vowels, matras, or nukta letters. It develops schwa deletion awareness in routine verbs and reviews aspiration and the dental-retroflex contrast in high-frequency verb forms.

Pronunciation Focus — Schwa deletion in routine verbs

Verb infinitives like उठना and पढ़ना are not read as three full syllables with every अ pronounced. Notice that उठना is closer to "uṭhnaa" than "u-ṭha-na," and सोना keeps a light final vowel rather than a full extra syllable. This continues the awareness system you began building in Chapter 6.

Pronunciation Focus — Aspiration and retroflex review in verb phrases

Practice the contrast between पढ़ना (to study, with a retroflex flap) and पड़ना (to fall, happen), and between जाता and चाहता for aspiration. Read the routine verbs in the vocabulary table aloud slowly, then at a natural pace.

Pronunciation Focus — Rhythm of habitual verb phrases

A habitual sentence such as मैं सुबह आठ बजे उठता हूँ is read as one connected phrase, not as separate isolated words. Group the time expression and verb phrase together when reading aloud, and let the sentence fall naturally on हूँ.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter introduces the habitual present as your first fully productive verb system, along with time expressions, frequency adverbs, and fuller negation with नहीं.

9.5 The habitual present

Language Building Block — Habitual present
मैं सुबह आठ बजे उठता हूँ। मेरी बहन सात बजे उठती है। हम हर दिन हिंदी पढ़ते हैं।
I get up at eight o'clock in the morning. My sister gets up at seven o'clock. We study Hindi every day.
Language Focus — Building the habitual present

The habitual present describes routines and general actions. It is built from a verb stem plus a gender- and number-agreeing participle ending in -ता (m. sg.), -ती (f. sg./pl.), or -ते (m. pl.), followed by the correct form of the auxiliary हूँ, है, or हैं: जाता हूँ (I go, m.), जाती हूँ (I go, f.), जाता है (he goes), जाती है (she goes), जाते हैं (we/they go, m., or respectful singular). The participle agrees with the subject's gender and number, the same agreement principle you have practiced with adjectives since Chapter 3.

9.6 Asking about someone's routine

Language Building Block — Routine questions
आप सुबह कितने बजे उठते हैं? आप कहाँ काम करते हैं?
What time do you get up in the morning? Where do you work?

Combine the habitual present with question words you already know, such as कहाँ from Chapter 7 and कितना/कितने from Chapter 8, to ask about another person's day.

9.7 Telling time and time expressions

Language Building Block — Time with पर, से, and तक
मैं आठ बजे पर उठता हूँ। मैं नौ बजे से पाँच बजे तक काम करता हूँ।
I get up at eight o'clock. I work from nine o'clock until five o'clock.
Language Focus — पर, से, and तक in time contexts

In this chapter, use पर to mark a specific point in time, से to mark a starting point, and तक to mark an ending point. These postpositions have other functions that develop in later chapters; for now, learn them only in the controlled time-expression pattern shown here.

9.8 Frequency adverbs

Language Building Block — Frequency expressions
मैं हर दिन चाय पीता हूँ। मैं कभी-कभी देर से उठता हूँ।
I drink tea every day. I sometimes get up late.

Frequency adverbs such as हर दिन, हमेशा, अक्सर, and कभी-कभी normally appear before the verb phrase.

9.9 Negation with नहीं

Language Building Block — Negating the habitual present
मैं सुबह जल्दी नहीं उठता। मेरा भाई काम नहीं करता।
I do not get up early in the morning. My brother does not work.
Language Focus — Placing नहीं

नहीं normally appears directly before the verb phrase it negates. You saw नहीं formulaically in earlier chapters, such as in मुझे ... पसंद नहीं है (Chapter 6) and मेरे पास ... नहीं है (Chapter 7). This chapter extends नहीं to full productive use with the habitual present. Note that the auxiliary may be dropped in casual negative statements, as in मैं जल्दी नहीं उठता (हूँ), though including it remains correct and clear for learners at this stage.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Arjun calls his cousin Priya to ask about her new daily routine.

अर्जुन नमस्ते प्रिया! तुम्हारा दिन कैसा होता है? Namaste Priyaa! Tumhaaraa din kaisaa hotaa hai? Hello Priya! What's your day usually like?
प्रिया मैं हर दिन साढ़े छह बजे उठती हूँ। फिर मैं नहाती हूँ और नाश्ता करती हूँ। Maiँ har din saaḍhe chhah baje uṭhtee hooँ. Phir maiँ nahaatee hooँ aur naashtaa kartee hooँ. I get up at half past six every day. Then I bathe and have breakfast.
अर्जुन तुम कितने बजे काम पर जाती हो? Tum kitne baje kaam par jaatee ho? What time do you go to work?
प्रिया मैं नौ बजे से शाम पाँच बजे तक काम करती हूँ। मैं कभी-कभी देर से पहुँचती हूँ। Maiँ nau baje se shaam paaँch baje tak kaam kartee hooँ. Maiँ kabhee-kabhee der se pahooँchtee hooँ. I work from nine o'clock until five o'clock in the evening. I sometimes arrive late.
अर्जुन और शाम को क्या करती हो? Aur shaam ko kyaa kartee ho? And what do you do in the evening?
प्रिया मैं घर लौटती हूँ और थोड़ा आराम करती हूँ। मैं रात को देर तक टीवी नहीं देखती। Maiँ ghar lauṭtee hooँ aur thoḍaa aaraam kartee hooँ. Maiँ raat ko der tak ṭeevee naheeँ dekhtee. I return home and rest a little. I don't watch television late at night.
अर्जुन यह अच्छा है। मैं भी हर दिन जल्दी सोता हूँ। Yah achchhaa hai. Maiँ bhee har din jaldee sotaa hooँ. That's good. I also go to sleep early every day.

समझ के प्रश्न — Comprehension questions:

  1. प्रिया कितने बजे उठती है? (What time does Priya get up?)
  2. प्रिया कितने बजे से कितने बजे तक काम करती है? (From what time to what time does Priya work?)
  3. प्रिया रात को क्या नहीं करती? (What does Priya not do at night?)

Writing Workshop

Write a short daily routine paragraph, combining this chapter's habitual present and time language with vocabulary and patterns from earlier chapters.

लेखन कार्य — Writing task: Write five to seven sentences in Devanagari describing a weekday from morning to evening, including at least one time expression, one frequency adverb, and one negative sentence.

  1. Say when you get up. Use the habitual present with उठना and a time expression.
  2. Describe your morning routine. Use verbs such as नहाना, खाना, or पीना.
  3. Say where you go and when. Use जाना with से and तक.
  4. Include one frequency adverb. Use हर दिन, अक्सर, or कभी-कभी.
  5. Include one negative sentence. Use नहीं to say something you do not do.
  6. Check your agreement. Confirm that your habitual verb forms agree with the subject's gender, and that a danda (।) ends each sentence.

संपादन सूची — Editing checklist:

  • Habitual present verbs agree correctly with the subject's gender.
  • At least one time expression with पर, से, or तक appears.
  • At least one frequency adverb appears.
  • At least one correctly placed negative sentence with नहीं appears.
  • A danda (।) ends each sentence.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Schedules vary widely

School routines, work routines, household routines, meal times, and commuting patterns vary widely across Hindi-speaking communities, by city, region, occupation, and family structure. This chapter does not assume that one daily schedule represents all Hindi-speaking life.

Culture in Context — Talking about time in different settings

Time may be discussed differently in casual conversation, institutional settings, travel contexts, and business contexts. A casual exchange about a daily schedule, like the one in this chapter's dialogue, is generally more flexible in tone than an appointment discussion in a school or workplace.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Forgetting gender agreement in the habitual present

Error: A woman saying मैं जाता हूँ instead of मैं जाती हूँ.

Correction: The habitual participle agrees with the speaker's or subject's gender, not with a fixed default form. Use -ता for masculine subjects and -ती for feminine subjects.

Common Mistake — Misplacing नहीं

Error: Saying नहीं मैं जाता हूँ to mean "I don't go."

Correction: Place नहीं directly before the verb phrase it negates: मैं नहीं जाता। or मैं नहीं जाता हूँ।

Common Mistake — Confusing से and तक

Error: Saying नौ बजे तक पाँच बजे से and reversing the order of the starting and ending points.

Correction: से marks where a time period begins and तक marks where it ends: नौ बजे से पाँच बजे तक ("from nine o'clock until five o'clock").

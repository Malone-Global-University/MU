मेरा परिवार और दोस्त

Volume I, Chapter 3: Family and Friends · Speak and Write Hindi: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify immediate family members and close friends using scheduled vocabulary.
  2. Use the possessives मेरा, मेरी, and मेरे correctly with familiar nouns.
  3. State a basic relationship using यह/वह मेरा/मेरी ... है/हैं.
  4. Describe a person with one or two familiar adjectives, such as अच्छा/अच्छी/अच्छे, in a controlled formulaic pattern.
  5. Recognize respectful plural हैं in reference to a single respected person.
  6. Recognize eight new Devanagari consonants and three new matras, and read them inside familiar words.
  7. Present two or more people, stating their relationship, identity, and one basic characteristic.

Communication Scenario

Mr. Sharma's class has a new assignment this week: bring a photo of someone from home and be ready to introduce them. Priya brings a picture of her mother, her brother, and her best friend. Anil brings a picture of his grandfather and his sister. As students go around the room, the same small set of tools reappears again and again — a possessive word like मेरा or मेरी, a family or friend word, and, once in a while, a simple word of description like अच्छा or अच्छी.

  1. This chapter gives you the language to talk about your own family and friends using possessives and simple description.
  2. It also expands your Devanagari consonant and matra set so that you can read and write more of this personal vocabulary.

Where This Chapter Fits

Chapter 3 builds directly on the identification and nominal sentences from Chapter 2. It adds possessive forms, a first controlled adjective pattern, and a deeper look at kinship vocabulary. It does not introduce a full adjective declension system, a full plural paradigm, or oblique forms — those systems continue to grow gradually across the volume.

Coming Into This Chapter

You can already identify people with यह and वह, ask यह/वह कौन है?, build a nominal sentence with है, and notice gender in familiar people-words.

New in This Chapter

Possessives मेरा, मेरी, and मेरे; family and friend vocabulary; a first formulaic adjective agreement pattern with अच्छा/अच्छी/अच्छे; respectful हैं in recognition and limited use; and eight new consonants and three new matras.

Looking Forward

Chapter 4 moves into the classroom, adding के पास for possession and expanding your Devanagari toward longer syllable chains.

Devanagari and Pronunciation

Chapters 1 and 2 gave you the ten independent vowels, nineteen consonants, and your first two matras. This chapter adds eight more consonants and three more matras, bringing your total matra set to five.

3.1 Eight new consonants

पpa
फpha
बba
भbha
यya
रra
लla
वva
Pronunciation Focus — व is neither English "v" nor English "w"

व sits between the English sounds "v" and "w." Many speakers produce it with the lower lip near the upper teeth, similar to "v," while others produce it closer to "w," especially before certain vowels. Listen for it in पत्नी and later words rather than forcing either English sound onto it.

3.2 Three new matras: उ, ऊ, and ए

कुku
कूkoo
केke

Unlike the left-side matra you met in Chapter 2, the उ and ऊ matras attach below the consonant, and the ए matra attaches to the left as a single stroke. All three are pronounced immediately after the consonant, in the order they are spoken.

3.3 Nasalization in familiar forms: माँ and मैं

The chandrabindu mark (ँ) over a vowel shows that the vowel is nasalized — pronounced partly through the nose. You already produce this sound in मैं from Chapter 1. Listen for the same nasal quality in माँ.

3.4 Listening for possessives and respectful हैं

Listen for मेरा, मेरी, and मेरे before family and friend words, and for the respectful use of हैं when a single person is described with special respect.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

3.5 Possessives मेरा, मेरी, and मेरे

Purpose: say that something or someone belongs to you, or is related to you.

Hindi possessives agree with the noun they describe, not with the speaker. मेरा is used before masculine singular nouns, मेरी before feminine nouns, and मेरे before masculine plural or certain respectful references.

Language Building Block — Possessive agreement with familiar nouns
मेरा भाई · मेरी बहन · मेरे दादा
Match the possessive to the gender and number of the noun that follows it, not to your own gender. मेरे दादा uses the respectful plural-looking form because grandparents are typically referred to respectfully.
यह मेरी बहन है। यह मेरे दादा हैं।
This is my sister. This is my grandfather.

This chapter treats possessive agreement as a controlled pattern with the specific nouns listed above. A fuller adjective declension system is not introduced yet.

3.6 Stating relationships

Purpose: introduce a family member or friend with a controlled sentence.

  • यह मेरा भाई है। Yah meraa bhaaee hai. This is my brother.
  • यह मेरी दोस्त है। Yah meree dost hai. This is my (female) friend.
  • यह मेरे दोस्त हैं। Yah mere dost hain. These are my friends. / This is my friend (respectful).

3.7 A first formulaic adjective pattern: अच्छा/अच्छी/अच्छे

Purpose: add one simple description to a person you introduce.

Language Focus — Variable adjectives agree with the noun

Some Hindi adjectives, including अच्छा, change their ending to match the gender and number of the noun they describe: अच्छा लड़का, अच्छी लड़की, अच्छे लड़के. This chapter introduces this pattern formulaically with अच्छा/अच्छी/अच्छे and, receptively, with बड़ा/बड़ी and छोटा/छोटी. Do not yet generalize this ending pattern to every adjective you encounter — some Hindi adjectives never change form, and that distinction is developed gradually in later chapters.

  • यह मेरी अच्छी दोस्त है। Yah meree achchhee dost hai. This is my good friend.
  • यह मेरा बड़ा भाई है। Yah meraa badaa bhaaee hai. This is my older brother.

3.8 Respectful हैं with family terms

Purpose: recognize that a single respected family member may be referred to with a plural-looking verb form.

Grammar Note — One person, respectful हैं

You met हैं receptively in Chapter 2. In family contexts, it is common to describe a single respected person, such as a grandparent or parent, with हैं instead of है: मेरे दादा अच्छे हैं ("My grandfather is good," respectfully). Treat this pattern as formulaic and limited to the family words in this chapter. The fuller honorific agreement system develops later in the volume.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Priya and Anil show each other family photographs during the family-and-friends assignment.

अनिल यह कौन है, प्रिया? Yah kaun hai, Priya? Who is this, Priya?
प्रिया यह मेरी माँ है। और यह मेरा भाई है। Yah meree maa hai. Aur yah meraa bhaaee hai. This is my mother. And this is my brother.
अनिल आपका भाई बड़ा है या छोटा? Aapkaa bhaaee badaa hai yaa chhotaa? Is your brother older or younger?
प्रिया वह छोटा है। और यह मेरी अच्छी दोस्त है, सुनीता। Vah chhotaa hai. Aur yah meree achchhee dost hai, Suneetaa. He is younger. And this is my good friend, Sunita.
अनिल अच्छा! और यह कौन हैं? Achchhaa! Aur yah kaun hain? I see! And who is this? (respectful)
प्रिया यह मेरे दादा हैं। वह बहुत अच्छे हैं। Yah mere daadaa hain. Vah bahut achchhe hain. This is my grandfather. He is very good.

या ("or") and बहुत ("very") appear here receptively, glossed for context only; their grammar is not analyzed until later chapters.

समझ के प्रश्न — Comprehension questions:

  1. प्रिया का भाई बड़ा है या छोटा? (Is Priya's brother older or younger?)
  2. प्रिया की दोस्त का नाम क्या है? (What is Priya's friend's name?)
  3. Why does Anil use हैं instead of है when asking about Priya's grandfather?

Writing Workshop

Create your own short family or friend profile, following the model profiles above. Your profile should introduce at least two people, use a correctly agreeing possessive, and include one adjective from this chapter.

लेखन कार्य — Writing task: Write three or four short sentences in Devanagari, copying letter shapes and matras carefully.

  1. Choose your people. Pick two or more family members or friends to introduce.
  2. Match the possessive. Use मेरा, मेरी, or मेरे to match the gender or respect level of each noun.
  3. Add one description. Use अच्छा/अच्छी/अच्छे, बड़ा/बड़ी, or छोटा/छोटी for at least one person.
  4. Check your letters and matras. Compare each Devanagari letter and matra you wrote against the charts above, paying attention to the new उ, ऊ, and ए matras.

संपादन सूची — Editing checklist:

  • Each possessive matches the gender or respect level of the noun that follows it.
  • At least one adjective from this chapter is used correctly.
  • Devanagari letters and matras are copied carefully and clearly.
  • A danda (।) ends each sentence.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Family vocabulary and respect

Hindi kinship terms often carry more than a simple English equivalent. Referring to a grandparent or parent with the respectful हैं form, as in this chapter's dialogue, reflects ordinary respectful practice rather than describing more than one person. Family structures, terms of address, and the degree of formality used with different relatives vary across households, regions, and communities.

Culture in Context — Not one family model

Family life among Hindi speakers takes many forms, including nuclear and extended households, multigenerational homes, and blended families, across urban, rural, and diaspora settings. Avoid assuming that any single family structure represents all Hindi-speaking communities.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Matching the possessive to yourself instead of the noun

Error: A male speaker saying मेरा बहन because he is male, instead of matching the possessive to बहन (feminine).

Correction: The possessive always agrees with the noun that follows it, never with the speaker. Use मेरी बहन regardless of the speaker's own gender.

Common Mistake — Overgeneralizing अच्छा-type agreement

Error: Assuming every Hindi adjective changes its ending the way अच्छा does.

Correction: Some adjectives are invariable and never change form. For now, learn the behavior of each adjective in this chapter individually; the broader variable-versus-invariable distinction develops in later chapters.

Common Mistake — Reading माँ without nasalization

Error: Pronouncing माँ the same as मा, ignoring the chandrabindu.

Correction: The chandrabindu mark shows that the vowel is nasalized. Practice the same nasal quality you already use in मैं.

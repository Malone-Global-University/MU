अंतिम परियोजना: मेरी दुनिया हिंदी में

Volume II, Chapter 20: Final Project: My World in Hindi · Speak and Write Hindi: Everyday Independence and Connected Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Sustain a connected, semi-structured B1 conversation covering personal background, a past experience, a practical problem, a conditional plan, and an opinion.
  2. Read a set of linked Devanagari texts — a personal profile, a narrative, a message thread, and a short public notice — and identify main ideas, details, stance, reference, and register.
  3. Write a connected 300-450 word portfolio text that draws on every communication domain from this volume.
  4. Apply gender, number, agreement, oblique forms, and core postpositions accurately across an extended piece of connected discourse.
  5. Narrate a completed event using controlled `ने` and object agreement, integrated with reasons, conditions, and relative description.
  6. Adjust address, vocabulary, and tone appropriately for at least two different audiences within one project.
  7. Reflect, in English or simple Hindi, on your own progress from A2 controlled communication to B1 practical independence.

Communication Scenario

Kabir has decided to build a small Hindi portfolio, मेरी दुनिया हिंदी में ("My World in Hindi"), to share with his teacher and to keep for himself as a record of what he can now do. Priya suggests he treat it like a real project: not a single essay, but a connected set of texts and a recorded conversation that together show his life, his community, one memorable event, a problem he solved, an opinion he holds, and a plan he is considering.

  1. Notice how Kabir's project moves across many earlier chapters — his learner profile from Chapter 1, his neighborhood from Chapter 3, his travel story from Chapter 8, his phone message from Chapter 13, and his register choices from Chapter 19 — without restarting any of them.
  2. Notice how Priya reminds him that a portfolio should feel like one coherent picture of his world, not a list of disconnected homework answers.
  3. Notice how Kabir chooses one audience for a formal piece and a different audience for an informal piece, applying what he practiced in Chapter 19.
  4. Notice how the final conversation with his teacher moves naturally between topics, the way real B1 conversations do.

Where This Chapter Fits

Chapter 20 closes Unit IV, जुड़ा हुआ संवाद, and closes Volume II as a whole. It does not introduce new grammar. It asks learners to combine everything already taught into one integrated demonstration of CEFR B1 practical communication.

Coming Into This Chapter

Learners bring forward personal and community description from Unit I, past narration and `ने` from Unit II, problem-solving and opinion language from Unit III, and conditions, relative description, public-text comprehension, and register awareness from Unit IV, including the audience-revision skills practiced in Chapter 19.

New in This Chapter

No new grammar system is introduced. What is new is integration: combining narration, advice, opinion, condition, relative description, and register choice inside one connected portfolio and one semi-structured conversation.

Looking Forward

Hindi Volume III, स्वतंत्र संवाद और उन्नत अभिव्यक्ति ("Independent Communication and Advanced Expression"), begins from the B1 foundation demonstrated here and expands it into independent B2 communication across academic, professional, media, and cultural contexts.

Pronunciation and Orthography Focus

Sustained paragraph rhythm

When reading a longer connected text aloud — several sentences in a row, as in a portfolio piece — group words into natural phrases rather than pausing after every word. Practice reading the model dialogue and reading passage below at a steady, natural pace, breathing at commas and dandas rather than in the middle of a phrase.

Schwa deletion across a full paragraph

Longer texts combine many of the schwa-deletion patterns practiced since Chapter 1: infinitives such as करना and पढ़ना, perfective forms such as देखा and लिखा, and compound words such as ज़िम्मेदारियाँ. Read slowly first, then at a natural pace, checking that you are not reading every written अ aloud.

Shifting rhythm and register together

As in Chapter 19, a more formal passage — such as the public notice below — is usually read slightly slower and more evenly, while a friendly message is read more quickly and with more natural contraction. Practice shifting your reading pace as you move between the informal and formal parts of your own portfolio.

Language Building Block
जो जगह मुझे सबसे पसंद है, वहाँ मैंने पिछले महीने एक मुश्किल फ़ैसला लिया, और मुझे लगता है कि अगर मुझे फिर मौक़ा मिला, तो मैं वही फ़ैसला फिर से लूँगा।
In the place I like most, I made a difficult decision last month, and I think that if I get the chance again, I would make the same decision again.

Form: This single sentence combines relative description (`जो...वह`), transitive perfective narration with `ने`, an opinion frame, and a conditional — four systems from four different units.

Meaning: Integration does not mean adding new grammar. It means using familiar systems together to express a more complete thought, the way fluent connected speech and writing normally work.

Use: When planning your own portfolio and conversation, look for natural places to connect two or three systems in one sentence, rather than keeping every sentence short and isolated.

Boundary

This chapter does not require new subordination types, advanced literary style, full Hindi-Urdu comparative analysis, or specialized professional or academic registers. Integration means combining Volume II systems accurately and naturally, not exceeding the B1 target. Advanced independent B2 integration is the work of Volume III.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Kabir's teacher, Meera जी, asks him about his final portfolio during a short conversation after class.

मीरा जी कबीर, आपका संग्रह कैसा बन रहा है? इसमें क्या-क्या शामिल है? Kabir, how is your portfolio coming along? What all is included in it?
कबीर जी, इसमें मेरा परिचय है, मेरे मोहल्ले का विवरण है, और एक घटना जो पिछले महीने हुई थी। Yes, it includes my introduction, a description of my neighborhood, and an event that happened last month.
मीरा जी वह घटना क्या थी? मुझे बताइए। What was that event? Tell me.
कबीर मैंने एक नई नौकरी के लिए आवेदन दिया था, लेकिन साक्षात्कार के दिन मेरी बस देर से आई। मुझे बहुत टेंशन हुआ, इसलिए मैंने अपने दोस्त को फ़ोन किया और उसने मुझे सलाह दी कि मैं कंपनी को फ़ोन करके बता दूँ। I had applied for a new job, but on the day of the interview my bus came late. I got very stressed, so I called my friend, and he advised me to call the company and let them know.
मीरा जी फिर क्या हुआ? Then what happened?
कबीर मैंने फ़ोन किया, उन्होंने कहा कि कोई बात नहीं, और मुझे थोड़ी देर बाद बुलाया। मुझे लगता है कि अगर मैंने फ़ोन नहीं किया होता, तो शायद मुझे वह नौकरी नहीं मिलती। I called, they said it's no problem, and they called me in a little while later. I think that if I hadn't called, I probably wouldn't have gotten that job.
मीरा जी बहुत अच्छा! और जो लोग आपके इस संग्रह को पढ़ेंगे, उनके लिए आपने कैसे लिखा है — औपचारिक या अनौपचारिक? Very good! And for the people who will read this portfolio of yours, how have you written it — formally or informally?
कबीर आपके लिए मैंने ज़्यादा औपचारिक भाषा रखी है, पर अपने दोस्त प्रिया के लिए मैंने एक संदेश भी लिखा है, वह थोड़ा अनौपचारिक है। For you I've kept more formal language, but for my friend Priya I've also written a message, which is a bit informal.
मीरा जी बहुत बढ़िया, कबीर। आपने इस साल हिंदी में बहुत तरक़्क़ी की है। Very well done, Kabir. You have made a lot of progress in Hindi this year.

समझ के प्रश्न — Comprehension questions:

  1. कबीर के संग्रह में क्या-क्या शामिल है? (What all is included in Kabir's portfolio?)
  2. साक्षात्कार के दिन क्या समस्या हुई? (What problem occurred on the day of the interview?)
  3. कबीर ने मीरा जी के लिए और प्रिया के लिए अलग-अलग भाषा क्यों इस्तेमाल की? (Why did Kabir use different language for Meera ji and for Priya?)

Reading Practice

एक अनुभव और दो संदेश — One Experience and Two Messages
कबीर की डायरी से:
पिछले महीने मैंने एक नई नौकरी के लिए आवेदन दिया। साक्षात्कार के दिन मेरी बस देर से आई, और मुझे बहुत टेंशन हुआ। मैंने अपने दोस्त को फ़ोन किया, और उसने मुझे सलाह दी कि मैं कंपनी को बता दूँ। मैंने वैसा ही किया, और सब ठीक हो गया। मुझे लगता है कि यह एक ज़रूरी सबक़ था।

कबीर से प्रिया को (दोस्त):
यार, याद है मैंने तुम्हें अपनी नौकरी वाली बात बताई थी? आख़िरकार मुझे वह मिल गई! अगली बार मिलेंगे तो पूरी कहानी सुनाऊँगा।

कबीर से मीरा जी को (शिक्षक, औपचारिक):
आदरणीय मीरा जी, मैं आपको सूचित करना चाहता था कि मुझे नई नौकरी मिल गई है। इस पूरे अनुभव के दौरान हिंदी में जो कुछ मैंने सीखा, उसने मेरी बहुत मदद की। धन्यवाद।
An Experience and Two Messages. From Kabir's diary: Last month I applied for a new job. On the day of the interview my bus came late, and I got very stressed. I called my friend, and he advised me to tell the company. I did just that, and everything worked out. I think this was an important lesson. From Kabir to Priya (friend): Buddy, remember I told you about my job thing? I finally got it! Next time we meet I'll tell you the whole story. From Kabir to Meera ji (teacher, formal): Respected Meera ji, I wanted to inform you that I have gotten a new job. Throughout this whole experience, what I learned in Hindi helped me a great deal. Thank you.

समझ के प्रश्न — Comprehension questions:

  1. What problem does the diary entry describe, and how was it resolved?
  2. Identify one word or phrase that shows the message to Priya is informal.
  3. Identify one word or phrase that shows the message to Meera ji is formal, and explain why it fits that audience.
  4. What core information is identical across all three texts?

Writing Workshop

Write your own connected portfolio, titled मेरी दुनिया हिंदी में ("My World in Hindi"), as the capstone product of Volume II.

लेखन कार्य — Writing task: Write a connected 300-450 word portfolio in standard Devanagari, in complete sentences, that includes:

  1. A personal and community profile update (building on Chapters 1-5): who you are, your relationships, and your home or community.
  2. One narrative of a completed event or experience (building on Chapters 6-10): what happened, using controlled `ने` and sequencing.
  3. One practical problem and proposed solution (building on Chapters 11-15): the problem, the advice you received or gave, and the outcome.
  4. One opinion or recommendation with reasons (building on Chapter 14): your view, supported by at least one reason.
  5. One conditional plan (building on Chapter 16): what you will do if a certain condition occurs.
  6. A short register-adjusted message (building on Chapter 19): the same core information written for two different audiences, with a clearly different address form and at least one vocabulary shift.
  7. A short reflection, in English or simple Hindi, on your progress since Chapter 1.

संपादन सूची — Editing checklist:

  • Every required part listed above is present and clearly identifiable.
  • Gender, number, agreement, and oblique forms are applied correctly throughout.
  • `ने` and object agreement are used correctly in the narrative section.
  • Reasons, opinions, conditions, and relative description are each represented at least once.
  • Address forms and vocabulary shift consistently between the two versions of the register-adjusted message.
  • Spelling, matras, conjuncts, word spacing, and dandas are correct throughout.
  • No untaught grammar is invented; only patterns already studied are reused.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Sharing a personal story across audiences

Across many Hindi-speaking communities, the same personal news — a new job, a difficulty solved, a plan for the future — is often shared differently with close friends, family elders, teachers, and institutions. This is not dishonesty; it reflects ordinary, respectful awareness of relationship and context, the same skill practiced throughout this volume.

Culture in Context — Recognizing your own progress

Reflecting on language progress is common in many educational traditions connected to Hindi-speaking communities, from classroom settings to informal mentorship. Describing what you have learned, and what still challenges you, is itself a valuable B1-level communicative skill, not merely a formality at the end of a course.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Writing a list of unconnected sentences instead of a connected portfolio

Error: Producing seven short, disconnected sentences that each satisfy one requirement but do not read as one coherent piece.

Correction: Use connectors already learned — इसलिए, इसके अलावा, फिर, जो...वह — to link ideas, the way the integrated example sentence in this chapter does.

Common Mistake — Losing register consistency across a long text

Error: Starting the formal message to a teacher with आदरणीय and `आप`, then slipping into यार or `तुम` later in the same message.

Correction: Check each section of the portfolio separately for consistent address and vocabulary, as practiced in Chapter 19.

Common Mistake — Overreaching beyond Volume II grammar

Error: Attempting advanced subjunctive-like hypotheticals, full passive constructions, or complex embedded clauses not yet taught in this course.

Correction: Use the controlled `अगर...तो` and `जो...वह` patterns from Chapters 16 and 17. Full hypothetical and passive systems are the responsibility of Volume III.

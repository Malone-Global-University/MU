सारांश, टिप्पणी, और प्रतिक्रिया

Volume IV, Chapter 7: Summary, Commentary, and Response · Speak and Write Hindi: Professional, Academic, and Cultural Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Summarize a long Hindi text accurately without copying its original wording.
  2. Paraphrase key points using different sentence structures from the source.
  3. Distinguish summary, commentary, and personal response as three separate tasks.
  4. Agree, disagree, or partially agree with a text's argument in respectful, qualified Hindi.
  5. Evaluate a text's strengths and limitations without dismissing it.
  6. Write a short analytical response that clearly separates the author's claim from your own reaction.

Communication Scenario

Priya's structural summary of the rural-training-centers article impressed Ms. Ahuja, who now asks for a second, shorter document: a summary-response memo that a program director can read in five minutes before the next funding meeting. This time, Ms. Ahuja wants more than structure — she wants Priya's professional judgment. Does the article's argument hold up? Where is it convincing, and where might it be incomplete? Priya realizes she must now do two things at once: represent the article fairly, in her own words, and add a reaction that is useful without sounding either falsely agreeable or needlessly critical.

  1. Notice how Priya keeps her summary of the article separate from her own evaluation, even though both appear in the same short memo.
  2. Notice that her paraphrase reorganizes the article's sentences rather than lightly editing them.
  3. Notice how she qualifies her agreement — she does not simply say the article is right or wrong.
  4. Notice that she supports her response with something specific from the text, not a general impression.

Where This Chapter Fits

Chapter 7 continues Unit II, अकादमिक पढ़ना, विश्लेषण, और लेखन, using the same article type introduced in Chapter 6 but shifting from pure structure toward summary, paraphrase, and evaluated response.

Coming Into This Chapter

Learners bring structural reading vocabulary, the five reference postpositional frames, and the recognize-then-restate skill from Chapter 6, along with the professional register established in Unit I.

What This Chapter Does

This chapter separates three tasks that learners often blur together — summarizing, commenting on style or approach, and reacting personally — and gives each its own vocabulary and sentence frames, with particular attention to paraphrase that does not mirror the source.

Looking Forward

Note on Summary Versus Paraphrase

सारांश condenses an entire text into its main points; व्याख्या restates a specific sentence or passage in different words at similar length. A good response memo usually contains one short summary and several small paraphrases, not one long paraphrase of everything.

Pronunciation and Listening Focus

Schwa deletion in evaluative and abstract vocabulary

Words such as मूल्यांकन (mūl-yāṅ-kan), विश्वसनीयता (vish-vas-nī-ya-tā), and निहितार्थ (ni-hi-tārth) combine several morphemes, and schwa deletion applies at each internal boundary, not only at the end of the word. Break these words into their parts before reading them aloud in a sentence, as in Chapter 6.

Intonation for qualified agreement

Hedged agreement such as मैं इस बात से आंशिक रूप से सहमत हूँ should not fall flatly at the end like a simple statement. A slight rise-then-fall around आंशिक रूप से signals that a qualification, not a flat yes or no, is coming — and prepares the listener for the explanation that follows.

Listening for summary versus opinion

In the listening passage below, identify the moment where the speaker shifts from restating the article's content to offering a personal reaction. Listen once for this shift, then a second time to note the specific hedging or evaluative words that mark it.

7.5 Stance and Hedging Language

Formal Hindi response writing rarely states full agreement or disagreement. Instead, it calibrates a position with hedging expressions:

HindiDegree
पूरी तरह सहमत हूँfull agreement
काफ़ी हद तक सहमत हूँstrong but not complete agreement
आंशिक रूप से सहमत हूँpartial agreement
संभवतः सही है, लेकिन...probably correct, with a qualification
पूरी तरह सहमत नहीं हूँdisagreement, softened
Language Building Block
लेखक का कहना है कि [paraphrased claim]। मैं इस बात से [hedging degree] सहमत हूँ, क्योंकि [reason]। हालाँकि, इस तर्क की एक सीमा यह है कि [limitation]।
The author says that [paraphrased claim]. I [degree] agree with this, because [reason]. However, one limitation of this argument is that [limitation].

7.6 Separating Quotation, Report, Paraphrase, and Commentary

These four ways of engaging with a source look similar but serve different purposes. Compare how the same idea appears in each:

Reported claim (attributed, close to original): लेखक कहते हैं कि केंद्रों की संख्या बढ़ाना अकेले पर्याप्त नहीं है।

Paraphrase (own words, same meaning): दूसरे शब्दों में, सिर्फ़ ज़्यादा केंद्र खोलने से समस्या हल नहीं होगी।

Commentary (about the argument's method): लेखक अपनी बात सीधे कहने के बजाय आँकड़ों के ज़रिए दिखाते हैं, जो तर्क को अधिक विश्वसनीय बनाता है।

Personal response (learner's own reaction): मुझे यह तर्क काफ़ी हद तक सही लगता है, हालाँकि मुझे लगता है कि लेखक ने लागत का मुद्दा नहीं उठाया।

A response memo normally moves through all four in order: report or paraphrase the claim, comment on how it is made, then give a personal, supported reaction.

Boundary Reminder

This chapter does not require full source-based argumentation with multiple sources and formal rebuttal — that begins in Chapter 9. Here, the goal is a single-source summary-response that keeps content, commentary, and personal reaction visibly separate.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Priya is drafting her summary-response memo and checks her draft with Deepak before sending it to Ms. Ahuja.

दीपक तुमने अपनी राय भी लिखी, या सिर्फ़ सारांश? Did you also write your opinion, or just a summary?
प्रिया दोनों, लेकिन अलग-अलग हिस्सों में। पहले मैंने लेख का सारांश अपने शब्दों में लिखा, फिर अपनी प्रतिक्रिया जोड़ी। Both, but in separate sections. First I wrote a summary of the article in my own words, then I added my response.
दीपक तुम लेख के तर्क से सहमत हो? Do you agree with the article's argument?
प्रिया काफ़ी हद तक सहमत हूँ। लेखक का कहना है कि सिर्फ़ केंद्र बनाना काफ़ी नहीं है — दूसरे शब्दों में, पहुँच के बिना संख्या का कोई मतलब नहीं। यह तर्क आँकड़ों पर आधारित है, इसलिए मुझे विश्वसनीय लगता है। I largely agree. The author says that just building centers isn't enough — in other words, numbers mean nothing without access. This argument is based on data, so it seems credible to me.
दीपक तो फिर तुमने केवल सहमति ही लिखी? So did you only write agreement?
प्रिया नहीं, मैंने एक सीमा भी बताई। इस तर्क की एक सीमा यह है कि लेखक लागत के बारे में कुछ नहीं कहते — नए रास्ते बनाना या केंद्र स्थानांतरित करना, दोनों महँगे हो सकते हैं। No, I also pointed out a limitation. One limitation of this argument is that the author says nothing about cost — building new roads or relocating centers could both be expensive.
दीपक यह अच्छा बिंदु है। क्या तुमने लेख का कोई वाक्य सीधे कॉपी किया? That's a good point. Did you copy any of the article's sentences directly?
प्रिया नहीं, हर वाक्य अपने ढंग से लिखा। शुरू में मैं लेख के वाक्यों के करीब चली गई थी, लेकिन फिर मैंने वाक्य-संरचना पूरी तरह बदल दी। No, I wrote every sentence in my own way. At first I stayed close to the article's sentences, but then I changed the sentence structure completely.
दीपक आहूजा जी को यह पसंद आएगा — संतुलित प्रतिक्रिया है। Ms. Ahuja will like this — it's a balanced response.

समझ के प्रश्न — Comprehension questions:

  1. प्रिया लेख के तर्क से कितनी सहमत हैं? (How much does Priya agree with the article's argument?)
  2. प्रिया कौन-सी सीमा बताती हैं? (What limitation does Priya point out?)
  3. प्रिया ने अपने पहले ड्राफ्ट में क्या समस्या पाई और उसे कैसे ठीक किया? (What problem did Priya find in her first draft, and how did she fix it?)

Reading Practice

Read the commentary piece below, written by a second education researcher responding to the same training-centers article from Chapter 6, then answer the questions.

टिप्पणी — Commentary: पहुँच के तर्क पर एक प्रतिक्रिया
हाल में प्रकाशित लेख का तर्क है कि ग्रामीण प्रशिक्षण केंद्रों की समस्या संख्या नहीं, बल्कि पहुँच है। दूसरे शब्दों में, केंद्र जितना दूर होगा, उपस्थिति उतनी ही अस्थिर होगी। यह तर्क तीन ज़िलों के आँकड़ों पर आधारित है, और आँकड़े काफ़ी स्पष्ट हैं — इसलिए मैं इस बात से काफ़ी हद तक सहमत हूँ कि पहुँच एक गंभीर मुद्दा है।

हालाँकि, इस विश्लेषण की एक सीमा यह है कि यह केवल तीन ज़िलों पर आधारित है, और लेखक यह नहीं बताते कि ये ज़िले पूरे देश की स्थिति का कितना प्रतिनिधित्व करते हैं। इसके अलावा, लेख यह भी नहीं बताता कि परिवहन में सुधार की लागत कितनी होगी, और क्या यह लागत केंद्र बनाने की लागत से कम या ज़्यादा है। इसका मतलब यह है कि लेख का सुझाव व्यावहारिक रूप से कितना संभव है, यह अभी स्पष्ट नहीं है।

मेरी प्रतिक्रिया यह है कि लेख का मूल तर्क — कि पहुँच मायने रखती है — ठोस और महत्वपूर्ण है, लेकिन नीति-निर्माताओं को इसे अपनाने से पहले लागत और व्यापकता, दोनों पर और शोध करने की ज़रूरत होगी। एक अच्छा तर्क होने के बावजूद, यह लेख पूरी कहानी नहीं बताता।
Commentary: "A Response to the Access Argument." The recently published article argues that the problem with rural training centers is not their number but their access — in other words, the farther a center, the more unstable attendance becomes. This argument is based on data from three districts, and the data is fairly clear, so I largely agree that access is a serious issue. However, one limitation of this analysis is that it is based on only three districts, and the author does not say how representative these districts are of the country as a whole. Furthermore, the article also does not address how much improving transportation would cost, or whether that cost is lower or higher than the cost of building centers. This means it isn't yet clear how practically feasible the article's suggestion is. My response is that the article's core argument — that access matters — is solid and important, but policymakers would need further research on both cost and generalizability before adopting it. Despite being a good argument, this article does not tell the whole story.

Writing Workshop

Complete all parts in standard Devanagari.

लेखन कार्य — Writing task:

  1. Write a two- or three-sentence summary of the training-centers article (Chapter 6) or the commentary above, entirely in your own words, without copying any sentence structure from the source.
  2. Write one sentence of commentary on how the argument is made — for example, whether it relies on data, direct statement, or example — using the frame लेखक अपनी बात...के ज़रिए दिखाते हैं.
  3. State your own position using a hedging expression from Section 7.5 (पूरी तरह सहमत हूँ / काफ़ी हद तक सहमत हूँ / आंशिक रूप से सहमत हूँ), followed by a reason.
  4. Identify one limitation of the argument, using the frame इस तर्क की एक सीमा यह है कि....
  5. Keep your summary, commentary, and personal response in clearly separate sentences or short paragraphs so a reader could label each part.

संपादन सूची — Editing checklist:

  • The summary restates content in different sentence structure from the original, not just different individual words.
  • Commentary on method is separated from personal response.
  • A hedging expression accurately reflects the degree of agreement, not simply full agreement or full disagreement.
  • The stated limitation is a genuine gap in the argument, not a restatement of the argument itself.
  • At least one paraphrase frame from Section 7.4 is used correctly.
  • Spelling, matras, conjuncts, word spacing, and punctuation are correct throughout.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Disagreement through qualification

In much professional and academic-adjacent Hindi writing, disagreement is rarely stated as a flat rejection. Writers more often qualify their agreement, name a specific limitation, and let the reader draw the stronger conclusion. Priya's memo and the sample commentary both model this pattern.

Culture in Context — Separating the person from the argument

इस तर्क की एक सीमा यह है कि... critiques the argument, not the author. Formal Hindi commentary generally keeps this distinction visible, especially in workplace and institutional writing where the author of a text may be a colleague or a known public figure.

Culture in Context — Why summary comes before response

A reader who trusts your summary is more likely to trust your response. Placing an accurate, fair summary before any evaluation — as both the Model Dialogue and the Reading Practice piece do — is a common professional convention, not merely a stylistic preference.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Paraphrasing by changing only a few words

Error: Keeping the article's sentence structure almost entirely intact and swapping in one or two synonyms, as Priya's own first draft did.

Correction: Rebuild the sentence from its meaning outward — decide what the sentence says, then construct a new sentence with different structure and word order, as Priya does after Deepak's question.

Common Mistake — Stating only full agreement or full disagreement

Error: Writing मैं सहमत हूँ or मैं असहमत हूँ without any qualification or reason.

Correction: Use a graded hedging expression from Section 7.5 and follow it with a specific reason, as Priya does when she says काफ़ी हद तक सहमत हूँ and explains why.

Common Mistake — Treating a limitation as an attack on the author

Error: Framing a limitation personally, such as suggesting the author was careless or did not do their job properly.

Correction: Frame the limitation as a property of the argument, using इस तर्क की एक सीमा यह है कि..., as modeled in both the dialogue and the Reading Practice commentary.

  • The speaker notes that the article does not explain how skills-training programs would be funded.

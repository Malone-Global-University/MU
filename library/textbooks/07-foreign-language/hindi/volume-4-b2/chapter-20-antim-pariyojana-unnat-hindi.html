Volume IV, Chapter 20: Final Project: Advanced Hindi · Speak and Write Hindi: Professional, Academic, and Cultural Communication

Chapter Overview and Objectives

The board meeting from Chapter 19 ended with a question nobody on the team had fully answered: could the Saturday-classes model be written up and shared with a sister community center in another city, in a form that both a Hindi-speaking coordinator and an English-speaking board member could use? Priya, Arjun, and Kamla-ji now have one final task that draws together everything from this volume — the professional register from Unit I, the reading and evidence work from Unit II, the media and cultural awareness from Unit III, and the report writing, mediation, and editing from Unit IV. This chapter does not teach anything new. It asks you to bring one substantial topic of your own through every skill this volume has developed, exactly as Priya's team must now do with theirs, and to produce a finished C1 portfolio.

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Read and synthesize long Devanagari texts on a single substantial topic.
  2. Listen to a presentation, interview, or report and identify its main claims and evidence.
  3. Participate in professional or public discussion about that topic.
  4. Write a formal report, analytical response, or position paper on the topic.
  5. Mediate the topic's meaning for a different audience.
  6. Revise your own writing for register and clarity.
  7. Deliver an extended presentation on the topic and respond to questions.
  8. Reflect on the register, audience, and cultural choices you made throughout the portfolio.

Communication Scenario

Two weeks after the board meeting, the community center's director asks Priya, Arjun, and Kamla-ji to turn their Saturday-classes project into a portfolio that a sister organization in Lucknow can use: something to read, something to hear, a written report, a short mediation note for the Lucknow center's two English-speaking trustees, and a recorded presentation the Lucknow coordinator can show at her own board meeting. Nothing about the underlying project is new — the data, the survey, the recommendation are all familiar from Chapters 4, 8, 16, and 19. What is new is holding all of it together as one coherent, portable body of work, in a register that will travel to a different city, a different institution, and a partly different audience.

  1. Notice how the same underlying facts are reshaped for five different purposes: reading synthesis, listening, formal writing, mediation, and presentation.
  2. Notice which register choices stay constant across all five and which ones shift for a new, partly unfamiliar audience.
  3. Notice how the team reflects afterward on what worked and what they would do differently for a still different audience.

Schwa Deletion Note

Watch संश्लेषण (sanshleshan), where the internal cluster keeps both consonants clearly audible rather than collapsing them, and समेकित (samekit), where the final इ is pronounced, not dropped, because it carries the adjective ending. प्रतिबिंब (pratibimb) keeps its final consonant cluster without an added vowel sound.

Apply the steady rhythm, breath-group pausing, and phrase grouping practiced in Chapter 19 to a longer, multi-part recording. A five-part portfolio presentation needs the same clarity at minute eight as at minute one, so pace yourself rather than accelerating toward the end.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

No new grammar system is introduced in this chapter. The patterns below are reviewed together because the portfolio requires moving fluidly among them within a single body of work.

20.5 Moving Between Report Style and Mediation Style

A formal report (Chapter 16) uses nominalization, passive-like structures, and impersonal recommendation frames. A mediation note for a different-language audience (Chapter 17) uses simpler, more explanatory sentences built around the reader's likely questions. The same fact can be expressed in either style.

  • रिपोर्ट शैली: विश्लेषण से पता चलता है कि उपस्थिति में साठ प्रतिशत की वृद्धि हुई है, और यह वृद्धि मौसमी प्रभाव से स्वतंत्र प्रतीत होती है। (Report style: The analysis shows a sixty-percent increase in attendance, and this increase appears independent of seasonal effect.)
  • मध्यस्थता शैली: सीधे शब्दों में कहें तो, पहले जितने लोग शनिवार को आते थे, अब उससे लगभग डेढ़ गुना लोग आते हैं — और यह सिर्फ़ गर्मी के मौसम की वजह से नहीं है। (Mediation style: Put simply, roughly one and a half times as many people now attend on Saturdays as before — and this isn't simply because of the summer season.)

20.6 Moving Between Written Report and Spoken Presentation

The report's executive-summary compression (Chapter 16) becomes a spoken opening frame in presentation (Chapter 19). The underlying claim stays the same; the sentence shape changes for the ear rather than the eye.

  • लिखित सारांश: इस रिपोर्ट का मुख्य निष्कर्ष यह है कि भाषा-सहायता सत्रों और नए समय ने मिलकर उपस्थिति बढ़ाई है। (Written summary: This report's main finding is that language-support sessions and the new schedule together increased attendance.)
  • बोला गया परिचय: आज मैं आपको बताऊँगी कि हमारी शनिवार कक्षाओं में उपस्थिति क्यों बढ़ी — और मुख्य कारण दो हैं। (Spoken introduction: Today I will tell you why attendance in our Saturday classes increased — and there are two main reasons.)

20.7 Register Consistency Across a Portfolio

A portfolio built for one audience — here, a sister organization and its two English-speaking trustees — should make deliberate, consistent register choices across every piece, even though each piece has a different form.

  • इस पूरे पोर्टफ़ोलियो में हमने एक ही रजिस्टर बनाए रखने की कोशिश की है — पेशेवर, लेकिन अत्यधिक औपचारिक नहीं, ताकि लखनऊ की टीम इसे आसानी से अपना सके। (Across this whole portfolio, we have tried to maintain one consistent register — professional, but not excessively formal, so that the Lucknow team can adopt it easily.)

20.8 Reflecting on Choices

A final reflective statement uses first-person stance language (Chapter 1) together with conditional and hypothetical reasoning to evaluate one's own portfolio.

  • इस अनुभव से मैंने सीखा कि एक ही जानकारी को अलग-अलग रूपों में ढालना जितना दिखता है उससे कहीं अधिक समय माँगता है। अगर मैं इसे दोबारा करूँ, तो मैं मध्यस्थता वाला भाग सबसे पहले लिखूँगी, ताकि बाकी हिस्सों का रजिस्टर उसी के अनुसार तय हो सके। (From this experience I learned that shaping the same information into different forms takes far more time than it appears to. If I were to do this again, I would write the mediation section first, so that the register of the remaining parts could be set accordingly.)

Model Dialogue

Setting: The community center, one week before the portfolio is due to be sent to Lucknow. Priya, Arjun, and Kamla-ji are dividing the final tasks.

प्रिया इस परियोजना के विभिन्न पहलुओं को देखें तो, हमारे पास पहले से ही ज़्यादातर सामग्री है — रिपोर्ट, आँकड़े, और बोर्ड की प्रस्तुति। सवाल यह है कि इसे लखनऊ की टीम के लिए कैसे समेकित करें। Looking at the different aspects of this project, we already have most of the material — the report, the data, and the board presentation. The question is how to consolidate it for the Lucknow team.
अर्जुन मुझे लगता है कि पहले मध्यस्थता वाला हिस्सा तय करना चाहिए — यानी, ट्रस्टियों के लिए हम भावार्थ किस तरह समझाएँगे। बाकी सब उसी रजिस्टर के आसपास बन सकता है। I think we should decide the mediation part first — that is, how we will explain the gist for the trustees. Everything else can be built around that same register.
कमला जी सही कहा। लेकिन एक बात स्पष्ट करना चाहूँगी — विभिन्न स्रोतों को मिलाकर एक ही सुसंगति बनाए रखना आसान नहीं होगा। हमें बार-बार जाँचना होगा कि लेखन और प्रस्तुति एक-दूसरे से टकरा तो नहीं रहे। Well said. But I'd like to clarify one thing — bringing different sources together while keeping the same coherence won't be easy. We'll need to check repeatedly that the writing and the presentation don't contradict each other.
प्रिया इसे समग्र रूप से देखें तो, हमारा काम चार हिस्सों में बँटेगा — पठन और सारांश, एक श्रव्य साक्षात्कार, अंतिम लिखित रिपोर्ट, और प्रस्तुति। मैं रिपोर्ट और सारांश सँभालती हूँ। Looking at it as a whole, our work will be divided into four parts — reading and summary, an audio interview, the final written report, and the presentation. I'll handle the report and the summary.
अर्जुन ठीक है, मैं मध्यस्थता का नोट लिखता हूँ और साक्षात्कार की तैयारी करता हूँ। कमला जी, आप प्रस्तुति सँभालेंगी? All right, I'll write the mediation note and prepare the interview. Kamla-ji, will you handle the presentation?
कमला जी हाँ, और अंत में हम तीनों मिलकर पूरे पोर्टफ़ोलियो का संपादन करेंगे, ताकि रजिस्टर पूरे दस्तावेज़ में एक जैसा रहे। Yes, and at the end the three of us will edit the whole portfolio together, so that the register stays consistent throughout the document.
प्रिया इस अनुभव से मैं पहले ही सीख रही हूँ कि एक ही जानकारी को अलग-अलग रूपों में ढालना जितना दिखता है उससे कहीं अधिक समय माँगता है। लेकिन इससे हमारा काम लखनऊ की टीम के लिए कहीं ज़्यादा उपयोगी होगा। I'm already learning from this experience that shaping the same information into different forms takes far more time than it appears to. But this will make our work far more useful to the Lucknow team.

समझ के प्रश्न — Comprehension questions:

  1. टीम ने पोर्टफ़ोलियो को कितने हिस्सों में बाँटा, और कौन किस हिस्से के लिए ज़िम्मेदार है? (Into how many parts did the team divide the portfolio, and who is responsible for which part?)
  2. अर्जुन क्यों सोचता है कि मध्यस्थता वाला हिस्सा पहले तय होना चाहिए? (Why does Arjun think the mediation part should be decided first?)
  3. कमला जी को किस बात की चिंता है? (What is Kamla-ji concerned about?)

Reading Practice

Read the draft mediation note Arjun has prepared for the Lucknow center's two English-speaking trustees, then answer the questions.

मध्यस्थता नोट — Mediation Note (Draft)
हमारी टीम ने पिछले छह महीनों में शनिवार की कक्षाओं में उपस्थिति में साठ प्रतिशत की वृद्धि देखी है। सीधे शब्दों में कहें तो, पहले हर शनिवार लगभग बीस लोग आते थे, अब लगभग बत्तीस आते हैं। यह वृद्धि तीन बदलावों से जुड़ी है: नई भाषा-सहायता सत्र, बेहतर समय, और मुँह-ज़बानी प्रचार। हमने यह भी जाँचा कि क्या यह वृद्धि केवल गर्मी के मौसम की वजह से है — पिछले साल के जून से तुलना करने पर हमें पता चला कि ऐसा नहीं है। इसलिए हमारी सिफ़ारिश है कि लखनऊ केंद्र भी अपनी स्थानीय परिस्थितियों के अनुसार इन्हीं तीन बदलावों को आज़माए, लेकिन ध्यान रखे कि हर समुदाय की भाषा-आवश्यकताएँ और समय-सारणी अलग हो सकती हैं।
Mediation Note: Our team has seen a sixty-percent increase in attendance at Saturday classes over the past six months. Put simply, about twenty people used to come each Saturday before, and about thirty-two come now. This increase is linked to three changes: new language-support sessions, a better schedule, and word-of-mouth promotion. We also checked whether this increase is simply due to the summer season — comparing with last year's June, we found this is not the case. We therefore recommend that the Lucknow center also try these same three changes, adapted to its own local circumstances, while keeping in mind that each community's language needs and schedule may differ.

  2. Which three changes does the note recommend, and are they the same three reasons given in Chapter 19's presentation outline?
  3. What caution does the note add at the end, and why is that caution important for mediation across communities?
  4. Rewrite the note's first two sentences in a more formal report register, as they might appear in the written report itself.

Writing Workshop

Complete all parts in standard Devanagari, except where the task specifically asks for English. This workshop is the written core of your final portfolio.

लेखन कार्य — Writing task:

  1. Choose one substantial topic from your own earlier work in this volume — the proposal (Ch. 4), the data commentary (Ch. 8), the source-based argument (Ch. 9), the formal report (Ch. 16), or a new but comparably substantial topic of your choice.
  2. Write a reading synthesis: a short paragraph in Hindi summarizing what you already know and have written about this topic across the volume, citing at least two earlier chapters' work.
  3. Write a formal report or position paper on the topic, using the structure from Chapter 16: background, findings, analysis, and recommendation.
  4. Write a mediation note adapting the same content for a reader unfamiliar with the topic's background, using plain, explanatory Hindi as modeled in this chapter's Reading Practice.
  5. Revise both documents using the editing checklist from Chapter 18, checking especially that register stays consistent within each document and appropriately different between the two.
  6. Write a short reflection in Hindi (four to six sentences) on what you learned from producing the portfolio, using the frames from Section 20.8.

संपादन सूची — Editing checklist:

  • The reading synthesis accurately references at least two earlier pieces of your own work.
  • The formal report follows the background/findings/analysis/recommendation structure.
  • The mediation note uses simpler, explanatory sentences rather than report-style nominalization.
  • Register is consistent within each document and deliberately different between the two.
  • The reflection paragraph uses at least one stance frame and one conditional or hypothetical frame.
  • Spelling, matras, conjuncts, nukta, and punctuation are correct throughout.

संचार कार्य — Communicative task: अंतिम प्रस्तुति — Final Presentation

अकेले अभ्यास — Solo alternative

Record your full presentation. Then record a short spoken reflection, in Hindi, on the choices you made across the portfolio's different pieces — what stayed the same, what changed, and what you would do differently for a new audience.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Sharing work across institutions

When one community organization shares its practices with another, respectful communication acknowledges that local conditions differ rather than presenting one center's experience as a universal template — a caution the mediation note in this chapter makes explicit.

Culture in Context — Consistency of voice across a body of work

A multi-part portfolio produced by several people — as Priya, Arjun, and Kamla-ji do here — benefits from a final shared editing pass, since institutional credibility depends on the whole document reading as one coherent voice rather than several disconnected ones.

Culture in Context — Reflection as part of professional practice

Ending a substantial project with an honest reflection on what worked and what could be improved is a valued professional habit in many Hindi-speaking institutional settings, and is treated here as a genuine part of the task rather than an afterthought.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using report style for the mediation note

Error: Writing the mediation note with the same dense nominalizations and passive-like structures as the formal report.

Correction: Rebuild the sentences in plainer, explanatory language, as modeled in Section 20.5 and this chapter's Reading Practice.

Common Mistake — Treating each portfolio piece as unrelated

Error: Writing the report, mediation note, and presentation as if they were three separate assignments with no shared facts or register plan.

Correction: Draft one shared set of facts and one register plan first, as Arjun proposes in the Model Dialogue, then adapt that shared material into each form.

Common Mistake — Skipping the reflection

Error: Treating the reflection paragraph as optional or filler after the "real" writing is done.

Correction: Write a specific, honest reflection that names one concrete choice and one concrete thing you would change, using the frames in Section 20.8.

लंबी प्रस्तुति और प्रश्नोत्तर

Volume IV, Chapter 19: Extended Presentation and Q&A · Speak and Write Hindi: Professional, Academic, and Cultural Communication

Chapter Overview and Objectives

The community center's board has scheduled a fifteen-minute agenda item for Priya, Arjun, and Kamla-ji to present their project: the findings from the formal report, the mediation work with English-speaking board members, and the newly edited, audience-appropriate materials from Chapter 18. This is no longer a short update — it is an extended, organized presentation that must introduce the topic, walk through evidence, and survive real questions from board members who were not part of the project. This chapter teaches learners to plan and deliver a sustained Hindi presentation, and — just as importantly — to handle the unpredictable part: the questions and objections that follow.

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Plan and deliver an extended, organized presentation in Hindi.
  2. Introduce a topic, define its scope, present evidence, explain examples, and conclude clearly.
  3. Handle audience questions, challenges, and requests for clarification with composure.
  4. Reformulate a point in different words when it has been misunderstood.
  5. Defend a recommendation or interpretation using specific evidence.
  6. Prepare presentation notes and a short supporting handout in Hindi.

Communication Scenario

The board meeting is in four days. Priya has been asked to open the presentation, Arjun will walk through the data and recommendations from the report, and Kamla-ji will field questions about community reaction, since she has spoken with the most families. None of them has given a presentation this long before — most of their community-center Hindi has been meetings, memos, and one-on-one messages. They realize a fifteen-minute slot is not simply a longer version of a meeting update: it needs a clear shape, a plan for questions they cannot predict, and a way to stay calm if someone disagrees in front of the whole board.

  1. Notice how the group divides the presentation into clear, signposted sections rather than one continuous block of speech.
  2. Notice the difference between answering a genuine clarification question and responding to a skeptical objection.
  3. Notice how a speaker reformulates the same point in simpler words when a board member looks confused.
  4. Notice that defending a recommendation calmly, with evidence, is different from simply repeating it more loudly.

Schwa Deletion Note

Watch श्रोतागण (shrotaagan), where the compound joins श्रोता and गण without an extra vowel between them, and विरोधाभास (virodhaabhaas), a Sanskritic compound where the internal आ vowels stay long and clear even though the word is long. Also watch पुनर्कथन (punarkathan), where पुनर् keeps its final consonant sound before the following word rather than dropping it.

Pronunciation and Listening Focus

Public presentation rhythm

A sustained presentation needs a slower, steadier rhythm than conversation, with a clear pause at the end of each major point. Rushing from one point to the next makes the structure invisible to listeners, even if the content is well organized.

Breath groups in long sentences

Long presentation sentences should be broken into natural breath groups at postpositional phrase boundaries and clause boundaries, not at random points. Practice reading a long sentence aloud, marking where a natural pause falls before speaking it to an audience.

Handling questions in real time

When a question arrives, a brief pause before answering is natural and useful — it signals that the question is being taken seriously, not dismissed. Listen for whether a question's intonation signals genuine curiosity, polite doubt, or a stronger challenge, since this shapes an appropriate response.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

19.5 Organizing Extended Discourse

A presentation longer than a few sentences needs explicit signposting so listeners can track where they are. Use clear transitions to mark the introduction, each main point, and the conclusion.

  • आज मैं समुदाय केंद्र की परियोजना पर बात करूँगी। इस प्रस्तुति का उद्देश्य तीन बातें बताना है — रिपोर्ट के निष्कर्ष, समुदाय की प्रतिक्रिया, और आगे के कदम। (Today I will talk about the community center project. The purpose of this presentation is to explain three things — the report's findings, the community's response, and the next steps.)
  • पहला बिंदु आँकड़ों से जुड़ा है; दूसरा बिंदु उन आँकड़ों के पीछे के कारणों से; और तीसरा बिंदु हमारी सिफ़ारिशों से। (The first point relates to the data; the second point to the reasons behind that data; and the third point to our recommendations.)

19.6 Reformulation When Misunderstood

When a listener looks confused or asks for the same information again, restate the point using different vocabulary and simpler sentence structure rather than simply repeating the original sentence more loudly or slowly.

  • मैं इसे दूसरे तरीके से समझाता हूँ: जब हमने कहा "सहभागिता बढ़ी," हमारा मतलब था कि पहले से दुगने लोग कार्यक्रमों में आने लगे। (Let me explain this a different way: when we said "participation increased," we meant that twice as many people started attending programs as before.)
  • दूसरे शब्दों में, यह आँकड़ा सिर्फ़ एक महीने का नहीं है — यह पूरे साल के रुझान को दिखाता है। (In other words, this figure is not just for one month — it shows the trend across the whole year.)

19.7 Distinguishing Clarification from Challenge

A clarification question asks for more information about something already said; a challenge questions whether a claim or recommendation is correct. Each deserves a different kind of response.

  • स्पष्टीकरण के लिए: अगर मैं आपका प्रश्न सही समझ पाई हूँ, तो आप जानना चाहते हैं कि यह आँकड़ा किस महीने का है। (For clarification: If I have understood your question correctly, you want to know which month this figure is from.)
  • आपत्ति के लिए: इस पर मेरी प्रतिक्रिया यह होगी कि हमने यह निष्कर्ष केवल एक स्रोत से नहीं, बल्कि तीन अलग-अलग सर्वेक्षणों से निकाला है। (For an objection: My response to that would be that we drew this conclusion not from a single source, but from three separate surveys.)

19.8 Defending a Recommendation with Evidence

Defending a position calmly means returning to specific evidence rather than restating the recommendation more forcefully or becoming defensive.

  • यह एक अच्छा सवाल है। हमारी सिफ़ारिश केवल राय पर आधारित नहीं है — इसके पीछे साठ परिवारों का सर्वेक्षण और तीन महीनों का उपस्थिति रिकॉर्ड है। (That's a good question. Our recommendation isn't based only on opinion — behind it is a survey of sixty families and three months of attendance records.)
  • मैं मानता हूँ कि बजट एक चिंता का विषय है, फिर भी आँकड़े दिखाते हैं कि इस निवेश से दीर्घकालिक लाभ होगा। (I acknowledge that budget is a concern, and yet the data shows that this investment will bring long-term benefit.)
Boundary Reminder

This chapter does not require conference-level specialization, a memorized speech, or theatrical delivery. A clearly organized, honestly delivered fifteen-minute presentation with genuine engagement with questions meets the chapter's goals.

Language Building Block — Question-Response Frame
आपका प्रश्न महत्वपूर्ण है। [स्पष्टीकरण/आपत्ति के प्रकार के अनुसार उत्तर]। इसके पीछे [प्रमाण] है, इसलिए [निष्कर्ष/सिफ़ारिश] अब भी सही लगती है।
Your question is important. [answer according to whether it is clarification or objection]. Behind this is [evidence], so the [conclusion/recommendation] still seems correct.

Model Dialogue

Setting: The board meeting room. Priya has just finished the introduction and Arjun is presenting the findings when a board member, Suresh-ji, raises his hand.

अर्जुन ...और इसीलिए हमारी सिफ़ारिश है कि शनिवार की कक्षाएँ जारी रखी जाएँ। अब मैं प्रश्नों के लिए रुकता हूँ। ...and that is why our recommendation is that the Saturday classes should continue. I'll pause here for questions.
सुरेश जी यह आँकड़ा — साठ प्रतिशत की बढ़ोतरी — किस अवधि का है? मुझे यह थोड़ा ज़्यादा लग रहा है। This figure — a sixty percent increase — from what period? It seems a bit high to me.
अर्जुन आपका प्रश्न महत्वपूर्ण है। अगर मैं सही समझ पाया हूँ, आप जानना चाहते हैं कि यह आँकड़ा एक ही हफ़्ते का है या पूरे सत्र का। Your question is important. If I've understood correctly, you want to know whether this figure is from a single week or the whole session.
सुरेश जी हाँ, बिल्कुल यही। Yes, exactly that.
अर्जुन मैं इसे दूसरे तरीके से समझाता हूँ — यह छह महीने की तुलना है, एक हफ़्ते की नहीं। जनवरी में औसतन बीस लोग आते थे, और जून में औसतन बत्तीस। यही साठ प्रतिशत की बढ़ोतरी है। Let me explain this a different way — this is a six-month comparison, not a one-week one. In January, an average of twenty people attended, and in June, an average of thirty-two. That is the sixty percent increase.
सुरेश जी ठीक है, अब स्पष्ट है। लेकिन एक आपत्ति भी है — क्या यह बढ़ोतरी सिर्फ़ इसलिए नहीं है क्योंकि गर्मियों में लोग वैसे भी ज़्यादा बाहर निकलते हैं? Okay, that's clear now. But I also have an objection — isn't this increase simply because people go out more in the summer anyway?
कमला जी यह एक अच्छा सवाल है, सुरेश जी। इस पर मेरी प्रतिक्रिया यह होगी कि हमने पिछले साल का जून भी देखा — उस समय उपस्थिति में इतनी बढ़ोतरी नहीं थी। इसलिए हम मानते हैं कि यह वृद्धि सिर्फ़ मौसम की वजह से नहीं है। That's a good question, Suresh-ji. My response to that would be that we also looked at last year's June — attendance did not rise this much at that time. So we believe this increase is not simply due to the season.
सुरेश जी मैं मानता हूँ, यह अच्छा जवाब है। I accept that — that's a good answer.
प्रिया संक्षेप में कहूँ तो, हमारी सिफ़ारिश तीन ठोस प्रमाणों पर टिकी है — छह महीने का आँकड़ा, पिछले साल से तुलना, और साठ परिवारों का सर्वेक्षण। निष्कर्ष यह है कि कक्षाएँ जारी रखने से समुदाय को स्पष्ट लाभ होगा। समय देने के लिए धन्यवाद। To put it briefly, our recommendation rests on three solid pieces of evidence — six months of data, a comparison with last year, and a survey of sixty families. The conclusion is that continuing the classes will bring clear benefit to the community. Thank you for your time.

समझ के प्रश्न — Comprehension questions:

  1. सुरेश जी का पहला प्रश्न स्पष्टीकरण था या आपत्ति? कैसे पता चलता है? (Was Suresh-ji's first question a clarification or an objection? How can you tell?)
  2. अर्जुन ने साठ प्रतिशत के आँकड़े को दूसरे तरीके से कैसे समझाया? (How did Arjun explain the sixty-percent figure a different way?)
  3. कमला जी ने सुरेश जी की आपत्ति का जवाब किस प्रमाण से दिया? (What evidence did Kamla-ji use to answer Suresh-ji's objection?)

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Respect and patience with audience questions

In many Hindi-speaking institutional and community settings, a presenter is expected to treat every question — however basic or skeptical — with visible patience and respect, since a dismissive tone toward a questioner can be read as a lack of respect for the audience as a whole, not just for one individual.

Culture in Context — Disagreement without bluntness in public settings

Public disagreement in a board or community meeting is often handled indirectly: acknowledging part of the objection before presenting counter-evidence, rather than flatly declaring the questioner wrong. This softens the exchange while still defending the position with evidence.

Culture in Context — Presentation style varies by setting

A board presentation, an academic seminar, and a community workshop each call for different pacing, formality, and audience-interaction norms. This chapter's board-meeting scenario is one instance of a broader C1 skill that transfers across professional and academic contexts.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Treating every question as an objection

Error: Responding defensively to a simple clarification question as though it were a challenge to the recommendation.

Correction: Identify whether the question asks for more information or questions a claim, and respond accordingly — a clarification usually only needs a clearer restatement.

Common Mistake — Repeating instead of reformulating

Error: Answering "I didn't understand" by repeating the exact same sentence, only louder or slower.

Correction: Restate the point using different vocabulary, a simpler sentence structure, or a concrete example, as modeled in Section 19.6.

Common Mistake — Defending a recommendation without evidence

Error: Responding to an objection only by restating the recommendation more firmly, without pointing to specific supporting evidence.

Correction: Return to a concrete piece of evidence — a figure, a comparison, a source — as shown in Section 19.8, rather than relying on repetition or tone.

समीक्षा 1: पेशेवर संवाद

Volume IV, Chapter 5: Unit I Assessment · Speak and Write Hindi: Professional, Academic, and Cultural Communication

Chapter Objectives

This review chapter introduces no new grammar. By the end of it, you will be able to:

  1. Present yourself professionally, describing your role, experience, and goals with precision.
  2. Exchange role and project information and adjust formality across email, meeting, and informal professional conversation.
  3. Participate in a structured meeting: state an agenda item, agree, disagree, clarify, and summarize a decision.
  4. Identify an institutional problem, compare two options, and recommend a course of action.
  5. Use passive-like and impersonal constructions to foreground process rather than agent.
  6. Write a register-appropriate follow-up message or recommendation memo.
  7. Complete an integrated professional scenario that draws on Chapters 1 through 4 together.

Communication Scenario

The board meeting is now three days away. Ms. Ahuja has asked Priya to lead a short segment of the meeting herself: introduce herself and her role to two new board members who have never met her, walk the group through the transportation problem and the recommended combined solution, and be ready to answer questions and confirm next steps. Priya spends an evening preparing — rehearsing her self-introduction, reviewing the meeting language she used with Neha, and rereading the recommendation memo one more time so that she can present it confidently and answer questions without notes.

  1. Notice how Priya's opening self-introduction (Chapter 2) sets up the more technical discussion that follows.
  2. Notice how she uses meeting language (Chapter 3) to manage the discussion, invite questions, and confirm agreement.
  3. Notice how she returns to the problem-cause-consequence chain and the softened recommendation frames (Chapter 4) when she reaches the proposal itself.
  4. Notice how the whole segment moves from professional identity, to structured discussion, to a concrete recommendation — the same progression Unit I has followed across four chapters.

Where This Chapter Fits

Chapter 5 consolidates Unit I, पेशेवर संवाद और संस्थागत भाषा. It does not introduce new grammar, vocabulary systems, or text types. Instead, it asks learners to combine professional self-presentation (Chapter 2), meeting participation (Chapter 3), and problem-solution writing (Chapter 4) into one connected performance.

Coming Into This Chapter

Learners bring professional identity and networking language, reported-decision and hedging frames from meetings, and the passive-like, impersonal, and recommendation frames used to describe problems and propose solutions.

What This Chapter Does

This chapter reviews and integrates Chapters 1-4 through a single extended scenario, testing whether learners can move fluidly between introduction, discussion, and recommendation without being told which system to use at each step.

Looking Forward

Unit II begins in Chapter 6 with long-form academic-adjacent reading. The evidence-framing and impersonal-style habits reinforced here reappear there in source-based argument and analytical writing.

Note on This Review

Look at how naturally these three groups connect: a professional introduction opens a meeting, meeting language manages a discussion, and problem-solution language resolves it with a recommendation. This chapter's tasks will move you through all three in a single sequence, the way an actual workplace conversation would.

Pronunciation and Listening Review

Professional pace and clarity across contexts

Review the pacing differences you have practiced: introductions (Chapter 2) are typically warm and slightly slower; meeting turns (Chapter 3) are brisk and clearly bounded; a recommendation (Chapter 4) slows again and gains a slight pitch rise at the key phrase. Practice moving between these three paces within a single rehearsal.

Schwa deletion in institutional and formal vocabulary

Reread व्यवहार्यता, कार्यान्वयन, and ज़िम्मेदारी aloud, breaking each into its component parts before reading it inside a full sentence, as you practiced in Chapter 4.

Listening for four signal types

In the listening passage below, track four signal types in sequence: a self-introduction, a meeting-style clarification, a problem-cause statement, and a softened recommendation. This is the same four-part tracking skill introduced across Chapters 2-4, now combined in one passage.

Boundary Reminder

This chapter does not introduce a new grammar system. Do not expand the passive-like pattern into new tenses, and do not introduce new postpositional functions beyond what Chapters 1-4 activated. The task here is fluent, connected use of what you already know, not new coverage.

Model Dialogue

Setting: The board meeting. Priya is introduced to two new board members, Kapoor Sahib and Mrs. Verma, before presenting the transportation recommendation.

आहूजा जी कपूर साहब, वर्मा जी, यह प्रिया हैं — हमारी कार्यक्रम समन्वयक। प्रिया, ज़रा अपना परिचय दीजिए। Mr. Kapoor, Mrs. Verma, this is Priya — our program coordinator. Priya, please introduce yourself.
प्रिया आपसे मिलकर खुशी हुई। मैं पिछले दो साल से इस संस्था में कार्यक्रम समन्वयक के रूप में काम कर रही हूँ। मेरा काम मुख्य रूप से ग्रामीण प्रशिक्षण कार्यक्रमों से संबंधित है। Pleased to meet you. I have been working at this organization as program coordinator for the past two years. My work is mainly related to rural training programs.
वर्मा जी बहुत अच्छा। आज हम किस विषय पर चर्चा करेंगे? Very good. What topic will we discuss today?
प्रिया आज मैं एक समस्या और उसके समाधान पर आपकी राय लेना चाहती हूँ। समस्या यह है कि प्रशिक्षकों के लिए ग्रामीण क्षेत्रों के स्कूलों तक समय पर पहुँचना मुश्किल होता है, और इसका मुख्य कारण उपलब्ध परिवहन की कमी है। Today I would like your opinion on a problem and its solution. The problem is that it is difficult for trainers to reach rural schools on time, and its main cause is a lack of available transportation.
कपूर साहब एक बात स्पष्ट करना चाहूँगा — क्या यह समस्या सभी स्कूलों में है, या कुछ ही स्कूलों में? I would like to clarify one point — is this problem at all schools, or only some schools?
प्रिया अच्छा प्रश्न है। यह मुख्य रूप से दो स्कूलों में हुआ, जिससे प्रशिक्षण की शुरुआत में तीन हफ़्ते की देरी हुई। हमारे पास दो विकल्प हैं — साझा वैन सेवा, या आंशिक ऑनलाइन प्रशिक्षण। Good question. It mainly occurred at two schools, causing a three-week delay in the start of training. We have two options — a shared van service, or partial online training.
वर्मा जी दोनों में क्या अंतर है? What's the difference between the two?
प्रिया वैन सेवा से प्रशिक्षक व्यक्तिगत रूप से पढ़ा सकते हैं, लेकिन इसकी लागत ज़्यादा है। ऑनलाइन प्रशिक्षण के बारे में माना जाता है कि यह लागत काफ़ी कम करता है, लेकिन कुछ स्कूलों में इंटरनेट भरोसेमंद नहीं है। मेरी सिफ़ारिश है कि दोनों को मिलाया जाए — बेहतर होगा कि हम पहले एक महीने का परीक्षण करें। With the van service, trainers can teach in person, but its cost is higher. Regarding online training, it is believed that it significantly reduces cost, but internet isn't reliable at some schools. My recommendation is that both be combined — it would be better if we first run a one-month trial.
आहूजा जी मुझे लगता है कि यह एक संतुलित सुझाव है। निर्णय यह हुआ कि हम एक महीने का परीक्षण शुरू करेंगे और परिणामों की समीक्षा करेंगे। I think this is a balanced suggestion. The decision is that we will start a one-month trial and review the results.
प्रिया धन्यवाद। मैं अगले हफ़्ते तक विस्तृत योजना ईमेल कर दूँगी। Thank you. I will email the detailed plan by next week.

समझ के प्रश्न — Comprehension questions:

  1. प्रिया अपना परिचय कैसे देती हैं? (How does Priya introduce herself?)
  2. कपूर साहब क्या स्पष्ट करना चाहते हैं? (What does Mr. Kapoor want to clarify?)
  3. बैठक में अंतिम निर्णय क्या है? (What is the final decision in the meeting?)

Writing Workshop

Complete all parts in standard Devanagari. This workshop combines the three writing systems from Chapters 2-4 into one connected task.

लेखन कार्य — Writing task:

  1. Write a short professional self-introduction (2-3 sentences) stating your role and area of work, as in Chapter 2.
  2. Report a meeting decision (1-2 sentences) using a reported-decision frame from Chapter 3 (यह तय हुआ कि or निर्णय यह हुआ कि).
  3. Describe a problem using the problem-cause-consequence chain from Chapter 4, and present two compared options.
  4. Use at least one passive-like or impersonal construction and two different recommendation frames.
  5. Combine all of the above into a single follow-up email, organized the way Priya's email above is organized: greeting, context, decision, next steps, closing.

संपादन सूची — Editing checklist:

  • The self-introduction, decision report, and problem-solution sections are all present and flow naturally into one email.
  • The problem-cause-consequence chain is complete and does not assign personal blame.
  • At least one passive-like or impersonal construction is used accurately.
  • Two different recommendation frames are used with appropriate force for a formal, group-addressed email.
  • Gender, number, agreement, oblique forms, postpositions, and `ने`/`को` patterns are applied correctly throughout.
  • Spelling, matras, conjuncts, word spacing, and punctuation are correct throughout.

Speaking Practice

नियंत्रित अभ्यास — Controlled practice: तीन चरण — Three Stages

Alone or with a partner, rehearse three short stages back-to-back: (1) introduce yourself professionally, (2) report a decision from an imagined meeting, (3) describe a small problem and recommend a solution. Move directly from one stage to the next without pausing to plan.

संचार कार्य — Communicative task: बोर्ड बैठक भूमिका-अभिनय — Board Meeting Role-Play

In a small group, recreate the Model Dialogue scenario with new names and a different institutional problem. One learner plays the presenter; others play board members who ask at least one clarifying question before the group reaches a decision.

अकेले अभ्यास — Solo alternative

Record yourself delivering the full three-stage presentation (introduction, decision report, problem-recommendation) in under three minutes. Listen back and check that your pacing shifts appropriately between stages, as described in the Pronunciation and Listening Review.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Introducing a colleague before a technical discussion

In many Hindi-speaking professional settings, a senior colleague or chair will introduce a presenter before technical discussion begins, as Ms. Ahuja does for Priya. Responding with a brief, warm self-introduction rather than moving straight into the agenda is expected, not a delay.

Culture in Context — Inviting questions rather than assuming closure

Closing a formal email with an open invitation for questions (कृपया कोई प्रश्न हो तो मुझे बताइए) is a common courtesy in institutional Hindi correspondence, signaling respect for the reader's authority even after a decision has been reported.

Culture in Context — Consistency across spoken and written registers

Notice that Priya's spoken recommendation in the meeting and her written follow-up email use compatible but not identical language — the spoken version is slightly more explanatory, and the written version is more compressed. Matching register to medium, while keeping the underlying content consistent, is a mark of professional competence.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Skipping the self-introduction before a technical point

Error: Moving directly into a problem-solution presentation without any self-introduction, even when addressing people who have not met the speaker.

Correction: Begin with a brief professional self-introduction (आपसे मिलकर खुशी हुई। मैं...के रूप में काम करता/करती हूँ।) before moving to the substantive discussion, as in the Model Dialogue.

Common Mistake — Reporting a decision and a recommendation with the same frame

Error: Using चाहिए both to recommend an option and to report what the group already decided, blurring the two functions.

Correction: Use a recommendation frame (बेहतर होगा कि) before the decision is made, and a reported-decision frame (यह तय हुआ कि or निर्णय यह हुआ कि) once the group has actually decided. Keep the two functions distinct, as Priya does in the Model Dialogue and follow-up email.

Common Mistake — Writing a follow-up email as a second full memo

Error: Repeating the entire background, options, and analysis from the original memo inside the follow-up email, making it long and repetitive.

Correction: Compress the decision and next steps into a few sentences, as in the Reading passage above, and refer back to the fuller memo only if new detail is needed.

  1. Priya introduces herself by stating her role (program coordinator), her length of service (two years), and the area her work relates to (rural training programs).
  2. Mr. Kapoor wants to clarify whether the transportation problem affects all schools or only some.
  3. The board decides to begin a one-month trial combining online sessions and the van service, and to review the results afterward.

Listening

  • Rahul introduces himself by name and states that he works in the finance department.
  • The problem is that old computers have become very slow, so daily work is completed late; the cause is that the computers have not been replaced in five years.
  • Rahul recommends replacing computers gradually each month, because it seems more feasible and puts less pressure on the budget.

Reading — Comprehension

  1. The email opens with a courteous thank-you and closing (आदरणीय सभी, धन्यवाद, प्रिया) matching Chapter 2's professional message conventions.
  2. जैसा कि तय हुआ ("as was decided") echoes Chapter 3's यह तय हुआ कि pattern, compressed into an introductory phrase rather than a full clause.
  3. The sentence beginning हम अगले महीने एक संयुक्त योजना का परीक्षण शुरू करेंगे compresses the fuller memo's separate options and recommendation into a single combined-plan statement.
  4. Model answer: Inviting questions signals that the board retains final authority and that the plan may still be adjusted, which is more respectful and more accurate than presenting the matter as fully closed.

Chapter Review — भाग C

Example: जिन स्कूलों में इंटरनेट भरोसेमंद है, वहाँ ऑनलाइन सत्र चलाए जाएँगे can be rewritten as हम उन स्कूलों में ऑनलाइन सत्र चलाएँगे जहाँ इंटरनेट भरोसेमंद है, naming "हम" (we) as the agent instead of leaving the action agentless.

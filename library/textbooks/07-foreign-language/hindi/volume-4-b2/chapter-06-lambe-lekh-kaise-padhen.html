लंबे लेख कैसे पढ़ें

Volume IV, Chapter 6: Reading Long Articles · Speak and Write Hindi: Professional, Academic, and Cultural Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify a long article's thesis, structure, supporting evidence, and conclusion.
  2. Distinguish a text's main idea from supporting detail and illustrative example.
  3. Take organized notes in Hindi while reading, using section-by-section headings.
  4. Explain, in Hindi, how a text is organized and where its central claim appears.
  5. Identify an author's stance and the audience a text seems to address.
  6. Summarize the structure of an article orally and in writing without reproducing its wording.

Communication Scenario

Two weeks after the board meeting, Ms. Ahuja forwards Priya a long article from an education-policy website: a piece analyzing why rural training programs across the country struggle with attendance and completion rates. Ms. Ahuja asks Priya to read it carefully, take notes, and prepare a short structural summary for the next planning meeting — not her own opinion yet, just a clear account of what the article argues and how it builds that argument. Priya realizes this is a different kind of reading than she is used to: the article is long, uses more abstract vocabulary than a news item, and moves through several stages before reaching its conclusion.

  1. Notice how Priya first reads for overall shape — introduction, body sections, conclusion — before reading for detail.
  2. Notice how she separates the author's central claim from the examples used to support it.
  3. Notice how her notes are organized by section rather than as a running list of everything she read.
  4. Notice that Ms. Ahuja explicitly asks for structure and content first, saving evaluation and response for a later stage — the task this chapter's sister chapter, Chapter 7, will take up.

Where This Chapter Fits

Chapter 6 opens Unit II, अकादमिक पढ़ना, विश्लेषण, और लेखन. It shifts the course from Unit I's spoken professional interaction toward extended written Hindi: long articles, reports, and source-based argument.

Coming Into This Chapter

Learners bring evidence-framing language, impersonal and passive-like style, and the problem-cause-consequence chain from Unit I, along with B2 reading skills for shorter connected texts.

What This Chapter Does

This chapter teaches a reading strategy — structure before detail — and introduces the vocabulary needed to talk about how a text is built: thesis, section, evidence, example, and conclusion.

Looking Forward

Note on Nominalized Vocabulary

विकास, परिवर्तन, विश्लेषण, प्रभाव, and संबंध are all nominalized forms — nouns built from processes or relationships rather than concrete objects. Formal Hindi prose relies on nouns like these far more than everyday conversation does. Learn to recognize them as a family rather than memorizing each in isolation.

Pronunciation and Listening Focus

Schwa deletion in nominalized and Sanskritic vocabulary

Words such as विश्लेषण (vi-shlé-ṣaṇ), कार्यान्वयन (kār-yān-va-yan), and दीर्घकालिक (dīrgh-kā-lik) carry more syllables than their spelling first suggests, and schwa deletion applies at internal morpheme boundaries as well as word-final position. Break each word into its components before reading it inside a sentence, as you practiced with formal vocabulary in Chapter 4.

Phrase grouping in long written sentences

Formal written Hindi sentences are often longer than spoken ones. When reading aloud, group words by clause rather than pausing at every comma, and let your pitch fall clearly at the end of each main clause before rising again for the next.

Listening for structure, not just content

In the listening passage below, focus less on remembering every detail and more on identifying the three-part shape: how the speaker signals the article's purpose, moves through its sections, and reaches a conclusion. Listen once for this shape, then a second time for supporting detail.

Grammar and Language Systems

6.4 Postpositional Frames for Topic and Reference

Long articles frequently refer back to a topic, a source, or a preceding claim. Five postpositional frames do most of this work in formal Hindi prose:

के बारे मेंabout, concerningयह लेख ग्रामीण शिक्षा के बारे में है।
के संदर्भ मेंin the context ofइस समस्या के संदर्भ में, लेखक तीन कारण बताते हैं।

Language Building Block
लेख का मुख्य तर्क यह है कि [clause]। पहले भाग में, लेखक [topic] के बारे में बताते हैं। इसके समर्थन में, वे [example] का उदाहरण देते हैं।
The article's main argument is that [clause]. In the first section, the author explains [topic]. In support of this, they give the example of [example].

6.5 Recognizing Complex Sentences Without Reproducing Them Immediately

Long articles combine cause, contrast, and evidence within single sentences. You are expected to understand these sentences and explain their content in your own simpler words — you are not yet expected to produce equally dense sentences yourself. Compare:

Article sentence: हालाँकि कई ज़िलों में प्रशिक्षण केंद्र खोले गए हैं, फिर भी परिवहन की कमी के कारण उपस्थिति में कोई स्थायी सुधार नहीं आया है।

Your explanation: लेख कहता है कि नए केंद्र खुले, लेकिन परिवहन की समस्या की वजह से उपस्थिति अब भी नहीं सुधरी। (The article says new centers opened, but because of the transportation problem, attendance still hasn't improved.)

This is the core reading-to-speaking skill of this chapter: understand the dense original, then restate its content in language you already control productively.

Boundary Reminder

This chapter does not require productive control of every clause type found in the reading. Recognition and restatement are the goal here. Full productive control of concession, cause, and evidence clauses in your own extended writing is built gradually across Chapters 7-9.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Priya has finished a first read-through of the article and is discussing her notes with a colleague, Deepak, before the planning meeting.

दीपक वह लेख कैसा है जो आहूजा जी ने भेजा था? How is that article that Ms. Ahuja sent?
प्रिया लंबा है, लेकिन इसका ढाँचा स्पष्ट है। लेख का मुख्य तर्क यह है कि नए प्रशिक्षण केंद्रों से उपस्थिति में सुधार नहीं आया, क्योंकि असली समस्या परिवहन है, केंद्रों की संख्या नहीं। It's long, but its structure is clear. The article's main argument is that new training centers haven't improved attendance, because the real problem is transportation, not the number of centers.
दीपक यह लेख किन भागों में बँटा है? What sections is this article divided into?
प्रिया तीन भागों में। पहले भाग में, लेखक समस्या का परिचय देते हैं। दूसरे भाग में, वे तीन ज़िलों के आँकड़ों के आधार पर तर्क को मज़बूत करते हैं। तीसरे भाग में, वे नीति में बदलाव का सुझाव देते हैं। Into three sections. In the first section, the author introduces the problem. In the second section, they strengthen the argument based on data from three districts. In the third section, they suggest a policy change.
दीपक क्या लेखक किसी एक ज़िले का विशेष उदाहरण देते हैं? Does the author give a specific example from any one district?
प्रिया हाँ, उदाहरण के तौर पर, वे एक ज़िले की बात करते हैं जहाँ केंद्र तो पास में है, फिर भी उपस्थिति सबसे कम है — सिर्फ़ इसलिए क्योंकि रास्ते बरसात में बंद हो जाते हैं। यह उदाहरण उनके मुख्य तर्क के समर्थन में है, न कि एक अलग बिंदु। Yes, as an example, they talk about a district where the center is nearby, yet attendance is lowest — just because the roads close during the rainy season. This example supports their main argument, not a separate point.
दीपक और निष्कर्ष में लेखक क्या कहते हैं? And what does the author say in the conclusion?
प्रिया निष्कर्ष में, वे कहते हैं कि नीति-निर्माताओं को केंद्र बनाने से पहले परिवहन के संबंध में सोचना चाहिए। लेखक का दृष्टिकोण काफ़ी स्पष्ट है, हालाँकि वे अपनी राय को सीधे नहीं, बल्कि आँकड़ों के ज़रिए बताते हैं। In the conclusion, they say that policymakers should think about transportation before building centers. The author's viewpoint is quite clear, although they present their opinion not directly, but through data.
दीपक यह तो हमारे अपने प्रस्ताव से मिलता-जुलता है। This is quite similar to our own proposal.
प्रिया बिल्कुल। मैं आहूजा जी के लिए इसका एक संक्षिप्त संरचनात्मक सारांश लिख रही हूँ, अभी अपनी राय शामिल किए बिना। Exactly. I'm writing a short structural summary of it for Ms. Ahuja, without including my own opinion yet.

समझ के प्रश्न — Comprehension questions:

  1. लेख का मुख्य तर्क क्या है? (What is the article's main argument?)
  2. लेख किन तीन भागों में बँटा है? (What three sections is the article divided into?)
  3. प्रिया अभी अपनी राय क्यों शामिल नहीं कर रही हैं? (Why is Priya not including her own opinion yet?)

Reading Practice

Read the article Ms. Ahuja forwarded to Priya, then answer the questions.

लेख — Article: ग्रामीण प्रशिक्षण केंद्र: संख्या या पहुँच?
पिछले पाँच वर्षों में, कई राज्यों ने ग्रामीण क्षेत्रों में सैकड़ों नए व्यावसायिक प्रशिक्षण केंद्र खोले हैं। सरकार और संस्थाएँ इसे एक बड़ी उपलब्धि मानती हैं। लेकिन आँकड़े एक अलग तस्वीर दिखाते हैं: केंद्रों की संख्या बढ़ने के बावजूद, प्रशिक्षण पूरा करने वाले प्रतिभागियों का प्रतिशत लगभग वैसा ही बना हुआ है।

तीन ज़िलों के आँकड़ों के आधार पर, यह देखा गया कि जिन गाँवों में केंद्र स्कूल या बाज़ार के पास स्थित है, वहाँ उपस्थिति स्थिर बनी रहती है। इसके विपरीत, जिन गाँवों में केंद्र मुख्य सड़क से दूर है, वहाँ उपस्थिति मौसम के साथ बहुत बदलती है। उदाहरण के तौर पर, एक ज़िले में एक केंद्र सड़क से पंद्रह किलोमीटर दूर बनाया गया; बरसात के महीनों में वहाँ उपस्थिति साठ प्रतिशत तक गिर जाती है, जबकि पास के एक केंद्र में यह गिरावट केवल दस प्रतिशत होती है।

इससे यह पता चलता है कि केंद्रों की संख्या बढ़ाना अकेले पर्याप्त नहीं है। नीति-निर्माताओं को एक केंद्र बनाने से पहले परिवहन और पहुँच के संबंध में सोचना चाहिए, न कि केवल यह गिनना चाहिए कि कितने केंद्र खोले गए। निष्कर्ष में, लेखक सुझाव देते हैं कि भविष्य की योजनाओं में केंद्रों की संख्या के साथ-साथ उनकी पहुँच को भी एक मुख्य मानदंड बनाया जाए।
Article: "Rural Training Centers: Numbers or Access?" Over the past five years, several states have opened hundreds of new vocational training centers in rural areas, widely seen as a major achievement. But the data shows a different picture: despite the rising number of centers, the percentage of participants completing training has stayed roughly the same. Based on data from three districts, it was observed that in villages where the center is near a school or market, attendance stays stable; where the center is far from the main road, attendance varies greatly with the season — one center 15 km off the road saw attendance drop 60% in the rainy months, versus only 10% at a nearby center. This shows that increasing the number of centers alone is not enough. Policymakers should think about transportation and access before building a center, rather than simply counting how many centers were opened. In conclusion, the author suggests that future plans should make access, alongside number of centers, a key criterion.

समझ के प्रश्न — Comprehension questions:

  1. State the article's main argument in one Hindi sentence, in your own words.
  2. Identify the article's three sections and give each a short Hindi label.
  3. Find the specific example used in the second section, and explain what general point it supports.
  4. Is the author's conclusion a call for more centers, fewer centers, or a different kind of planning? Support your answer from the text.

लेखन कार्य — Writing task:

  3. Write two or three sentences explaining the article's structure, using at least two postpositional reference frames from Section 6.4 (के आधार पर, के संदर्भ में, के संबंध में, के अनुसार).

संपादन सूची — Editing checklist:

  • Notes are organized by section heading, not as one continuous paragraph.
  • The thesis statement is a single clear sentence, not a list of details.
  • At least two postpositional reference frames are used correctly.
  • The restated example uses different wording from the original article.
  • No personal opinion or evaluation appears anywhere in the response.
  • Spelling, matras, conjuncts, word spacing, and punctuation are correct throughout.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Structure before opinion

When a Hindi-speaking manager or colleague asks for a summary of a document, they often expect structure and content first, with evaluation offered separately or only when requested. Producing a careful structural summary before an opinion, as Ms. Ahuja asks of Priya, signals professional discipline rather than a lack of critical thinking.

Culture in Context — Register in policy-adjacent writing

Hindi articles on education, policy, and social topics often mix formal Sanskritic vocabulary (कार्यान्वयन, मानदंड) with more everyday words (केंद्र, पास). This mixture is normal in serious, well-edited Hindi prose and is not a sign of inconsistency.

Culture in Context — Indirect argument through data

As Priya notes, the article's author states an opinion mainly through data and comparison rather than direct assertion. This indirect style of argument is common in Hindi analytical and journalistic prose and should not be mistaken for a lack of a clear position.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Confusing an example with the main argument

Error: Summarizing the article by describing the fifteen-kilometer center in detail, without ever stating the general claim it supports.

Correction: State the main argument first (लेख का मुख्य तर्क यह है कि...), then introduce the example as support (उदाहरण के तौर पर...), as Priya does in the Model Dialogue.

Common Mistake — Taking notes as one continuous list

Error: Writing down every sentence of the article in the order it appears, without separating introduction, body, and conclusion.

Correction: Organize notes by section from the start, using headings such as परिचय, मुख्य भाग, and निष्कर्ष, so the structure is visible at a glance.

Common Mistake — Adding personal opinion to a structural summary

Error: Including phrases such as मुझे लगता है कि यह सही है in a task that only asks for the article's content and structure.

Correction: Keep structural summary and personal response separate, as Priya does when she tells Deepak she is writing the summary अभी अपनी राय शामिल किए बिना. Personal response is the focus of Chapter 7.

Answer Key

Model Dialogue — Comprehension

  1. The article's main argument is that new training centers have not improved attendance, because the real problem is transportation and access, not the number of centers.
  2. The article is divided into three sections: an introduction to the problem, data from three districts strengthening the argument, and a suggested policy change.
  3. Priya is not including her opinion yet because Ms. Ahuja specifically asked for a structural summary of content first; evaluation is a separate, later task.

Listening

  • The article is about rising unemployment among urban youth.
  • In the second section, the author uses a three-city survey showing most graduates cannot find work related to their degree, illustrated by an engineering graduate now working in a shop.
  • In the conclusion, the author suggests that degrees alone are not enough and that practical skills training also needs attention.

Reading — Comprehension

  1. Centers near a main road or market keep stable attendance; centers far from the main road see attendance vary sharply by season, dropping as much as 60% in the rainy months versus about 10% at a nearby, more accessible center.
  2. The author recommends that future planning treat access and transportation, not just the number of centers built, as a key criterion.
  3. Model answer: लेख का मुख्य तर्क यह है कि सिर्फ़ केंद्र बनाना काफ़ी नहीं है; परिवहन और पहुँच पर ध्यान देना भी ज़रूरी है।
  4. The conclusion calls for a different kind of planning — factoring in access and transportation — rather than simply building more or fewer centers. This is supported by the district comparison in the second paragraph.

Chapter Review — भाग B

  1. (a) के आधार पर — the decline was observed on the basis of the data, not merely "about" it. (b) के बारे में — a general topic statement ("this article is about rural education") calls for the broader "about" frame rather than the narrower "in relation to."

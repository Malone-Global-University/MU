अनुवाद-सचेत मध्यस्थता

Volume IV, Chapter 17: Translation-Aware Mediation · Speak and Write Hindi: Professional, Academic, and Cultural Communication

Chapter Overview and Objectives

Having just finished writing a formal report on the community center's panel, Priya, Arjun, and Kamla-ji now face a related but different problem: several attendees who could not come, and one board member who does not read Hindi comfortably, need to understand what happened without reading a full Hindi report. This chapter teaches learners to mediate meaning across Hindi and English audiences — summarizing, paraphrasing, and adapting register and detail rather than translating word for word. Learners will distinguish literal meaning from natural sense and function, and practice explaining why a direct translation can sometimes mislead.

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Summarize Hindi content for an English-speaking audience and English content for a Hindi-speaking audience.
  2. Paraphrase rather than translate word-for-word, preserving meaning and tone.
  3. Distinguish literal meaning, natural sense, and practical function in a mediated message.
  4. Adapt register and level of detail to a specified audience.
  5. Explain in Hindi why a direct translation may mislead a reader or listener.
  6. Produce a short mediation note or bilingual briefing without letting English syntax drive the Hindi.

Communication Scenario

The finished report on "हमारी भाषाएँ, हमारी पहचान" now needs to reach two more audiences. Mr. Fernandes, a board member who reads English much more comfortably than Hindi, wants a short briefing before the next board meeting. And several community members who missed the event have asked Arjun, in a WhatsApp group, "क्या हुआ था?" — "What happened?" — in casual spoken Hindi that expects a casual spoken answer, not a formal report read aloud. Priya and Arjun realize the same report cannot simply be repeated word for word to either audience; each needs the content reshaped for who they are and what they actually need to know.

  1. Notice what Priya keeps, drops, or reorders when explaining the report to Mr. Fernandes in English-supported Hindi.
  2. Notice how Arjun's WhatsApp answer differs in length, formality, and structure from the written report.
  3. Notice where a literal translation of a Hindi phrase would confuse an English reader, and how Priya explains it instead.
  4. Notice that mediation is not simply shortening a text — it is reshaping meaning for a new audience.

Pronunciation and Listening Focus

Switching pace between registers

When mediating the same content for two audiences, speakers often shift pace as well as vocabulary: slower and more evenly paced for a formal briefing, quicker and more casual for a WhatsApp-style spoken answer. Practice reading the same content at both paces.

Schwa deletion in mediation vocabulary

Practice संक्षिप्तीकरण, सांस्कृतिक संदर्भ, and समतुल्य slowly, then at natural speed.

Listening for what is kept and what is dropped

When a speaker mediates a message for a new audience, listen for which details are kept, which are dropped, and which frame — भावार्थ or शाब्दिक अर्थ — the speaker signals before explaining a phrase.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

17.5 Paraphrase and Explanation Frames

Paraphrase restates meaning in different words rather than copying structure. These frames signal that a restatement, not a quotation, is coming.

  • दूसरे शब्दों में, अधिकांश लोगों को कार्यक्रम पसंद आया, लेकिन कुछ छोटी समस्याएँ थीं। (In other words, most people liked the event, but there were a few small problems.)
  • अर्थात, रिपोर्ट यह सुझाव देती है कि अगली बार साइनेज बेहतर होनी चाहिए। (That is to say, the report suggests that signage should be better next time.)
  • इसका मतलब यह नहीं है कि कार्यक्रम असफल रहा — बल्कि यह कि कुछ छोटे सुधार संभव हैं। (This does not mean the event was unsuccessful — rather, that a few small improvements are possible.)

17.6 Literal Meaning, Natural Sense, and Function

Mediation requires distinguishing three layers of meaning: the शाब्दिक अर्थ (literal, word-by-word meaning), the भावार्थ (the natural sense a native speaker intends), and the प्रयोग (how the expression actually functions in context). A direct translation often reproduces only the first layer.

  • शाब्दिक अर्थ में, "ध्यान देने योग्य" का मतलब है "worth giving attention to।" लेकिन भावार्थ में, रिपोर्ट में इसका प्रयोग किसी समस्या की ओर धीरे से इशारा करने के लिए होता है। (Literally, "worth giving attention to" means just that. But in natural sense, in a report it functions as a gentle way of pointing to a problem.)
  • "मुझे जाना है" का शब्दानुवाद है "to me, going is," लेकिन इसका भावार्थ केवल "I have to go" है। (The word-for-word translation of "mujhe jaana hai" is "to me, going is," but its natural sense is simply "I have to go.")

Explaining why a direct translation misleads is itself a productive C1 skill:

  • यहाँ सीधा अनुवाद भ्रामक होगा, क्योंकि अंग्रेज़ी में "recommendation" शब्द उतना नरम नहीं लगता जितना हिंदी का "सिफ़ारिश की जाती है कि।" (A direct translation would be misleading here, because in English the word "recommendation" does not sound as soft as the Hindi "sifaarish kii jaatii hai ki.")

17.7 Audience Adaptation

  • यदि श्रोता अंग्रेज़ी में सहज हों, तो बेहतर होगा कि पूरी रिपोर्ट के बजाय केवल मुख्य निष्कर्ष और सिफ़ारिशें बताई जाएँ। (If the audience is comfortable in English, it would be better to state only the main findings and recommendations rather than the whole report.)
  • इस संदर्भ में, फ़र्नांडीस जी के लिए संक्षेप में कहें तो: कार्यक्रम अपेक्षा से बेहतर रहा, लेकिन साइनेज सुधारने की ज़रूरत है। (In this context, to put it briefly for Mr. Fernandes: the event went better than expected, but signage needs improvement.)

17.8 Selective Translation of Embedded Phrases

Full translation is rarely necessary; only specific embedded phrases usually need it, and even then, only when a plain English gloss genuinely helps.

  • "सिफ़ारिश की जाती है" — इसे अंग्रेज़ी में लगभग "it is recommended" कहा जा सकता है, हालाँकि हिंदी वाक्य उतना ही औपचारिक और कम व्यक्तिगत है। ("Sifaarish kii jaatii hai" — this can be rendered roughly as "it is recommended" in English, though the Hindi sentence is equally formal and equally impersonal.)
Boundary Reminder

Translation cautions and basic paraphrase were introduced receptively in earlier volumes. This chapter develops audience-aware mediation into full productive control. Certified translation, legal interpretation, and publication-level bilingual editing remain outside this chapter's scope.

Language Building Block — Mediation Frame
यदि श्रोता [विवरण] हों, तो [अनुकूलन]। इसका भावार्थ यह है कि [स्वाभाविक व्याख्या], न कि [शाब्दिक अर्थ]। संक्षेप में कहें तो, [संक्षिप्त सारांश]।
If the audience is [description], then [adaptation]. The natural sense of this is [natural explanation], not [literal meaning]. To put it briefly, [short summary].

Model Dialogue

Setting: The community center's small office, the day before the board meeting. Priya is preparing to brief Mr. Fernandes, while Arjun checks his phone, where the WhatsApp group is asking about the panel.

अर्जुन प्रिया, फ़र्नांडीस जी को हम पूरी रिपोर्ट पढ़कर सुनाएँ क्या? Priya, should we read the whole report aloud to Mr. Fernandes?
प्रिया नहीं, वह ठीक नहीं होगा। वे हिंदी में सहज नहीं हैं, और बोर्ड मीटिंग में समय भी कम है। बेहतर होगा कि हम संक्षेप में कहें: कार्यक्रम अपेक्षा से बेहतर रहा, प्रतिक्रिया सकारात्मक थी, और साइनेज सुधारने की ज़रूरत है। No, that wouldn't be right. They're not comfortable in Hindi, and there's little time in the board meeting too. It would be better if we put it briefly: the event went better than expected, feedback was positive, and signage needs improvement.
अर्जुन तो क्या हम "सिफ़ारिश की जाती है कि" को सीधे "it is recommended that" कहें? So should we say "sifaarish kii jaatii hai ki" directly as "it is recommended that"?
प्रिया वह ठीक अनुवाद है, लेकिन ध्यान दो — हिंदी वाक्य में यह ज़्यादा नरम और सामान्य ढंग से सुना जाता है। अगर हम सिर्फ़ शब्दानुवाद करें, तो फ़र्नांडीस जी को यह ज़्यादा औपचारिक या ठंडा लग सकता है। इसलिए बेहतर होगा कि हम कहें: "we'd suggest" या "it might help to।" That's a correct translation, but notice — in the Hindi sentence, this sounds gentler and more ordinary. If we just do a word-for-word translation, it might sound more formal or cold to Mr. Fernandes. So it would be better to say: "we'd suggest" or "it might help to."
अर्जुन समझ गया। और व्हाट्सएप ग्रुप में लोग पूछ रहे हैं "क्या हुआ था?" — उन्हें मैं क्या लिखूँ? Got it. And in the WhatsApp group people are asking "what happened?" — what should I write them?
प्रिया वहाँ रिपोर्ट की भाषा बिल्कुल मत डालो। बस बोलचाल में लिखो — जैसे कि हम खुद बात कर रहे हों। "अरे, कार्यक्रम बहुत अच्छा रहा! साठ लोग आए, सबको मज़ा आया, बस एंट्री का साइनबोर्ड थोड़ा confusing था — अगली बार ठीक कर देंगे।" Don't put report language there at all. Just write it colloquially — as if we were talking ourselves. "Hey, the event went really well! Sixty people came, everyone enjoyed it, just the entry signboard was a bit confusing — we'll fix it next time."
अर्जुन यह तो रिपोर्ट से बिलकुल अलग सुनता है! This sounds completely different from the report!
प्रिया यही मध्यस्थता है — एक ही सच्चाई, लेकिन हर श्रोता के लिए अलग रूप में। संदर्भ के अनुसार भाषा बदलती है, मतलब नहीं। This is exactly mediation — the same truth, but in a different form for each audience. Language changes according to context; the meaning doesn't.

समझ के प्रश्न — Comprehension questions:

  1. प्रिया फ़र्नांडीस जी के लिए रिपोर्ट को क्यों नहीं पूरा पढ़कर सुनाना चाहती? (Why doesn't Priya want to read the whole report aloud for Mr. Fernandes?)
  2. "सिफ़ारिश की जाती है कि" का शब्दानुवाद और भावार्थ में क्या अंतर है? (What is the difference between the word-for-word translation and the natural sense of "sifaarish kii jaatii hai ki"?)
  3. व्हाट्सएप ग्रुप के लिए भाषा रिपोर्ट की भाषा से किस तरह अलग है? (How does the language for the WhatsApp group differ from the report's language?)

Writing Workshop

Complete all parts in standard Devanagari, except where the task specifically asks for English.

लेखन कार्य — Writing task:

  1. Choose a short Hindi text you have written in an earlier chapter (a report excerpt, a proposal, or a data commentary) as your source content.
  2. Identify two different audiences for this content: one that needs a formal, detail-light briefing, and one that needs a casual, spoken-style explanation.
  3. Write a mediation note in Hindi (three to five sentences) for the formal audience, using at least one frame from Section 17.5 or 17.7.
  4. Write a second, shorter version in colloquial spoken Hindi for the casual audience.
  5. Identify one phrase from your source text where a direct, word-for-word rendering would mislead the formal audience, and explain the difference between its शाब्दिक अर्थ and भावार्थ.
  6. Write one sentence explaining, in Hindi, why the two mediated versions differ.

संपादन सूची — Editing checklist:

  • The two mediated versions differ meaningfully in register, length, and detail, not just in a few word substitutions.
  • At least one mediation frame from Section 17.5 or 17.7 is used correctly.
  • The literal-versus-natural-sense explanation clearly distinguishes the two layers of meaning.
  • Neither version reads as a direct, word-for-word translation of the source.
  • Spelling, matras, conjuncts, and punctuation are correct throughout.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Mediation as an everyday skill

In many multilingual Indian and diaspora households, workplaces, and community organizations, someone regularly mediates information across Hindi, English, and other languages — not as professional interpreters, but as a routine part of daily life. This chapter's skills reflect that ordinary, widespread practice.

Culture in Context — Register shift is not dishonesty

Priya's two versions of the same event are both accurate; they differ in register and detail, not in truth. Presenting the same facts differently for different audiences is a normal, respected communicative skill rather than a form of spin.

Culture in Context — Avoiding flattening across languages

Treating a Hindi expression as though it has one single, fixed English equivalent — or the reverse — can erase real differences in tone, formality, and social meaning. Careful mediation preserves those differences rather than flattening them.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Treating mediation as shortening only

Error: Simply deleting sentences from the report to make it shorter, without changing register or tone for the new audience.

Correction: Rebuild the message for the audience's needs and comfort level — adjust vocabulary, formality, and structure, not just length.

Common Mistake — Word-for-word translation of formal frames

Error: Translating सिफ़ारिश की जाती है कि literally into a stiff, overly formal English sentence for a casual audience.

Correction: Use a natural English equivalent such as "we'd suggest" or "it might help to," matched to the audience's expectations.

Common Mistake — Letting English syntax drive the Hindi

Error: Building a Hindi mediation note by translating an English sentence word for word, producing unnatural word order.

Correction: Start from the meaning and build a natural Hindi sentence using Hindi's own structure, then check it against the source for accuracy.

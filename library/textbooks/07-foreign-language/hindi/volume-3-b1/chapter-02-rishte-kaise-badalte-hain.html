रिश्ते कैसे बदलते हैं

Volume III, Chapter 2: Changing Relationships · Speak and Write Hindi: Independent Communication and Advanced Expression

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Describe how a relationship — with family, a friend, a mentor, or a colleague — has changed over time, in one connected account.
  2. Explain social roles, responsibilities, and expectations with tact and appropriate honorific language.
  3. Choose among आप, तुम, and recognition-only तू according to relationship, setting, and respect.
  4. Use को to mark recipients, experiencers, and human objects inside longer relationship narratives.
  5. Use के लिए, के साथ, के बारे में, की वजह से, and के कारण to explain interpersonal cause and connection.
  6. Track who did what, to whom, and for whom across a paragraph, using pronouns, titles, and honorific agreement consistently.
  7. Read and interpret a message exchange or personal column about a changing relationship.
  8. Write a reflective paragraph describing a relationship, a mentor, or a community connection.

Communication Scenario

At the second meeting of the conversation group, Meera जी asks each member to talk about one relationship that has changed since they began learning Hindi — not necessarily a Hindi-speaking relationship, but any relationship where growth, distance, or new understanding has appeared. Kabir chooses to talk about his colleague Rahul, who became a close friend; Priya chooses her grandmother, whose relationship with her has deepened as Priya's Hindi has improved.

  1. Notice how Kabir shifts his address form as the relationship with Rahul moves from formal colleague to close friend — from आप toward तुम — and how he explains that shift explicitly rather than letting it happen invisibly.
  2. Notice how Priya keeps आप and honorific agreement for her grandmother throughout, even as the emotional closeness grows, because respect and closeness are not the same thing in Hindi address.
  3. Notice how both speakers use को to mark who benefits, who is affected, and who receives something — मुझे, उसे, उन्हें — as the relationship narrative unfolds.
  4. Notice that, as in Chapter 1, no major new tense or aspect system appears here. The new material is reference, respect, and connective explanation across a longer account.

Where This Chapter Fits

Chapter 2 continues Unit I, पहचान, अनुभव, और बदलता जीवन, extending Chapter 1's connected-discourse approach from personal history into social relationships.

Coming Into This Chapter

Learners arrive having reactivated `ne` narration, aspect, and stance expressions such as मुझे लगता है कि... in a connected personal account. Volume II honorific and address awareness, `को` for recipients and experiencers, and basic postpositional frames are also assumed as available foundations.

New in This Chapter

Expanded reference tracking across a paragraph — using pronouns, names, titles, and repeated nouns without confusion — and honorific agreement in connected discourse. `को` extends from single-sentence recipients/experiencers into full relationship narratives, alongside `के लिए`, `के साथ`, `के बारे में`, `की वजह से`, and `के कारण` for interpersonal explanation.

Looking Forward

Pronunciation and Orthography Focus

Respectful address intonation

Sentences addressed to an elder, mentor, or respected figure with आप and honorific plural verb agreement tend to carry a slightly more even, unhurried rhythm than sentences addressed to a close friend with तुम. Practice saying the same idea both ways in the Model Dialogue below, and notice how your pacing naturally shifts.

Schwa deletion in common relationship terms

Words such as सहकर्मी, ज़िम्मेदारी, and भरोसा combine familiar schwa-deletion patterns with a formal or compound shape. Read each word slowly first, then in the connected sentences of the reading and dialogue.

Rhythm in longer noun phrases and honorific forms

Honorific noun phrases such as मेरी दादी जी or हमारे सहकर्मी रोहित जी should be said as one connected phrase, not word by word. Group the title and name together, with a light pause before continuing the sentence.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

2.4 Reference Tracking Across a Paragraph

When you describe a relationship across several sentences, you must keep the reader or listener clear on who is who without repeating full names constantly. Hindi typically alternates between a name or title on first mention, then pronouns (वह, उसे, उसका) or a respectful equivalent (वे, उन्हें, उनका) for later mentions, returning to the name only when ambiguity might arise.

MentionForm UsedExample
First mentionName/titleमेरी दादी जी बहुत समझदार हैं।
Later mention (respectful)वे / उन्हें / उनकावे मुझे हमेशा अच्छी सलाह देती हैं।
First mentionNameरोहित मेरा सहकर्मी है।
Later mention (familiar)वह / उसे / उसकाअब वह मेरा क़रीबी दोस्त भी है।
Language Building Block
पहले रोहित सिर्फ़ एक सहकर्मी था, लेकिन काम की वजह से हम एक-दूसरे के साथ ज़्यादा समय बिताने लगे, और धीरे-धीरे मुझे एहसास हुआ कि वह मेरा क़रीबी दोस्त बन चुका है।
At first Rohit was just a colleague, but because of work we started spending more time with each other, and gradually I realized that he had become my close friend.

Form: This sentence links a background state (था), a causal explanation (की वजह से), an accompaniment phrase (एक-दूसरे के साथ), a `को`-marked experiencer construction (मुझे एहसास हुआ), and a `कि` clause reporting a realization.

Meaning: The sentence moves from an earlier state, through a cause, to a present realization — a natural shape for describing how a relationship has changed.

Use: When describing a relationship, look for one place to combine a starting point, a cause, and a present realization, rather than stating three separate facts.

2.5 `को` for Recipients, Experiencers, and Human Objects

को continues to mark the person who receives something (मैंने उसे एक संदेश भेजा), the person who experiences a feeling or realization (मुझे एहसास हुआ कि...), and, in relationship narratives, a specific, identified human object (मैं रोहित को अच्छी तरह जानता हूँ). Review: `को`-marked human objects are common when the object is a specific, known person rather than a general category.

2.7 आप, तुम, and Recognition-Only तू in Context

Address choice communicates respect, closeness, hierarchy, and setting simultaneously. Use आप as the safe default for elders, mentors, professional contacts, and anyone whose closeness has not been explicitly established as familiar. तुम becomes appropriate among peers, close friends, and some family members once familiarity is clear. तू remains recognition-only in this course; you should recognize it in dialogue, songs, and poetry, but it is not a form you should introduce into your own speech at this level.

Boundary

This chapter does not require new relative-correlative structures, new aspect systems, or productive तू. It expands reference tracking, honorific agreement, `को`, and postpositional frames already available from Volume II into longer, connected relationship narratives. Full relative-correlative expansion begins in Chapter 11.

Model Dialogue

Setting: At the conversation group's second meeting, Kabir tells Priya and Meera जी about his colleague Rohit.

मीरा जी कबीर, आज हमें किसी ऐसे रिश्ते के बारे में बताओ जो समय के साथ बदला है। Kabir, today tell us about some relationship that has changed over time.
कबीर ज़रूर। मेरे सहकर्मी रोहित के साथ मेरा रिश्ता काफ़ी बदल गया है। पहले वह सिर्फ़ एक सहकर्मी था, और मैं हमेशा उनसे "आप" में बात करता था। Sure. My relationship with my colleague Rohit has changed quite a bit. At first he was just a colleague, and I always spoke to him using "aap."
प्रिया और अब? तुम उसे क्या कहते हो? And now? What do you call him?
कबीर अब मैं उसे "तुम" कहता हूँ, क्योंकि एक साझा परियोजना की वजह से हम एक-दूसरे के साथ बहुत समय बिताने लगे। धीरे-धीरे मुझे एहसास हुआ कि वह मेरा क़रीबी दोस्त बन चुका है, न कि सिर्फ़ सहकर्मी। Now I call him "tum," because due to a shared project we started spending a lot of time with each other. Gradually I realized that he had become my close friend, not just a colleague.
मीरा जी बहुत दिलचस्प है। और प्रिया, तुम्हारे परिवार में कोई ऐसा रिश्ता है? Very interesting. And Priya, is there such a relationship in your family?
प्रिया हाँ, मेरी दादी जी के साथ। मेरी हिंदी की वजह से अब हम पहले से कहीं ज़्यादा बात कर पाते हैं। मैं उन्हें अभी भी "आप" कहती हूँ — यह सम्मान की बात है, न कि दूरी की। Yes, with my grandmother. Because of my Hindi, we're now able to talk far more than before. I still call her "aap" — that's a matter of respect, not distance.
कबीर यह सही बात है — नज़दीकी और सम्मान अलग-अलग चीज़ें हैं। That's true — closeness and respect are different things.

समझ के प्रश्न — Comprehension questions:

  1. कबीर और रोहित का रिश्ता कैसे बदला? (How did Kabir and Rohit's relationship change?)
  2. कबीर अब रोहित को क्या कहते हैं, और क्यों? (What does Kabir call Rohit now, and why?)
  3. प्रिया अपनी दादी जी को "आप" क्यों कहती रहती है? (Why does Priya continue to call her grandmother "aap"?)

Writing Workshop

Write a reflective paragraph, रिश्ते कैसे बदलते हैं ("How Relationships Change"), about one relationship in your own life — a family member, mentor, friend, or colleague.

लेखन कार्य — Writing task: Write a connected 180-250 word account in standard Devanagari, in complete multi-sentence paragraphs, that includes:

  1. Identify the person and their role in your life, using an appropriate title or name on first mention.
  2. Describe how the relationship began, and what it was like at first, using पहले and consistent honorific or familiar reference.
  3. Explain what changed and why, using at least one of की वजह से, के कारण, के साथ, or के लिए.
  4. Reflect on the relationship now, using अब and, if appropriate, मुझे लगता है कि....

संपादन सूची — Editing checklist:

  • The person's name or title appears clearly on first mention, and pronoun reference afterward is unambiguous.
  • Address and honorific choice (`आप`/`तुम`/respectful pronoun agreement) is used consistently and appropriately for the relationship described.
  • `को` is used correctly wherever a recipient, experiencer, or specific human object appears.
  • At least one causal or relational postpositional frame from this chapter is used accurately.
  • Gender, number, agreement, and oblique forms are applied correctly throughout.
  • Spelling, matras, conjuncts, word spacing, and dandas are correct throughout.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Respect and closeness are not the same

As Priya's example shows, continuing to use आप with a grandmother, elder, or mentor does not signal emotional distance. In many Hindi-speaking contexts, respectful address and deep affection comfortably coexist, and shifting to तुम is not required for a relationship to feel close.

Culture in Context — Address shifts among peers

As Kabir's example with Rohit shows, moving from आप to तुम between colleagues or acquaintances is common as a friendship deepens, but the timing and comfort level vary by individual, workplace, region, and personality. There is no single fixed point at which the shift must happen.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Losing track of who is who across a paragraph

Error: Repeating a person's full name in every sentence, or switching unpredictably between वह and वे for the same person without reason.

Correction: Introduce the person by name or title once, then use a consistent pronoun (वह/उसे/उसका or वे/उन्हें/उनका) for later mentions, returning to the name only if confusion could arise.

Common Mistake — Mismatching address form and honorific agreement

Error: Writing वे मुझे सलाह देती है instead of वे मुझे सलाह देती हैं when referring respectfully to one person.

Correction: When using वे, आप, or a respectful reference to a single person, the verb must agree as if with a plural subject, even though only one person is meant.

Common Mistake — Treating की वजह से and के कारण as always interchangeable

Error: Assuming the two frames never differ in feel or context.

Correction: Both mean "because of," but के कारण tends to sound slightly more formal or written, while की वजह से is comfortable in both speech and writing. Either is acceptable in this chapter's tasks.

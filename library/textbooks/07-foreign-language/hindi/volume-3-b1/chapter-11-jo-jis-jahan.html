जो, जिस, जहाँ

Volume III, Chapter 11: Expanded Relative Description · Speak and Write Hindi: Independent Communication and Advanced Expression

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify and describe people, places, objects, and ideas using relative-correlative structures.
  2. Specify exactly which person, place, or thing you mean, rather than relying on a bare noun alone.
  3. Combine description and explanation in a single connected sentence.
  4. Track reference accurately across two linked clauses.
  5. Recognize expanded relative structures such as जिस, जिसका/जिसकी/जिसके, and जहाँ in reading and listening.
  6. Write connected descriptions that use at least three controlled relative-correlative structures accurately and naturally.

Communication Scenario

With the parking recommendation delivered, the conversation group's eleventh meeting turns to a new task: Meera Ji has been asked to help write short, precise descriptions for a community directory — of people, places, and services in the neighborhood — so that new residents can find the right person or place quickly. Priya, Aditya, and Kabir soon discover that vague descriptions like "the woman" or "the office" are not precise enough; they need language that pins down exactly which person, place, or thing is meant.

  1. Notice how the group moves from simple description toward sentences that identify one specific referent among several possibilities.
  2. Notice how जो and वह, already familiar from Volume II, expand into जिस, जिसका, and जहाँ forms when a postposition or possessive relationship is involved.
  3. Notice how the group keeps sentences clear rather than piling up clauses simply because the grammar allows it.

Where This Chapter Fits

Coming Into This Chapter

Learners already recognize the controlled pattern जो...वह from Volume II (for example, जो आदमी वहाँ है, वह मेरा दोस्त है) and can track a single pronoun reference across a short discourse.

What This Chapter Does

This chapter expands the familiar जो...वह pattern into three new but closely related forms: जिस...उस for postpositional relationships, जिसका/जिसकी/जिसके...उसका/उसकी/उसके for possession, and जहाँ...वहाँ for place. It stays within description and explanation; it does not introduce reported speech, conditionals, or temporal clauses.

Receptive Vocabulary Note

Forms such as जिन, जिन्हें, जिनका, जहाँ से, and जहाँ तक appear in this chapter's reading passage for recognition only. You are not expected to produce these plural or extended forms independently yet; treat them as glossed items.

Pronunciation and Listening Focus

Schwa deletion in relative-correlative forms

Practice reading these forms aloud, paying attention to schwa deletion: जिसका (JIS-kaa, not ji-sa-kaa), उसका (US-kaa), जहाँ (ja-HAA̐, with nasalization on the final vowel), and वहाँ (va-HAA̐). Compare the pair जहाँ / वहाँ aloud several times so the parallel sound pattern becomes automatic.

Phrase grouping in complex sentences

A relative-correlative sentence has two natural phrase groups: the relative clause (usually first) and the correlative clause (usually second). Pause briefly at the comma between them: जो व्यक्ति लाइब्रेरी चलाता है, || वह हमारी सड़क पर रहता है। Reading straight through without any pause makes long sentences hard to follow, even for a fluent listener.

Listening for the pair, not just one half

Before listening, remind yourself that a relative clause almost always has a matching correlative clause somewhere in the sentence. If you hear जिस, listen for उस; if you hear जहाँ, listen for वहाँ. Missing the correlative half is the most common reason learners lose the meaning of these sentences.

Grammar: Expanding जो...वह

You already know the basic relative-correlative pattern from Volume II: जो introduces a description, and वह picks it back up in the main clause. This chapter expands that same logic into three related pairs, each triggered by a different grammatical relationship.

11.4 Review: जो...वह

Review Pattern — Subject/Object Reference
जो आदमी वहाँ खड़ा है, वह हमारा नया पड़ोसी है।
The man who is standing there is our new neighbor.

Form: जो + noun/description + comma + वह + main clause. Use: identifying a specific person, place, or thing as the subject or object of the sentence.

11.5 जिस...उस — Postpositional Relationships

When the person or thing being described is connected to the rest of the sentence through a postposition — से, को, पर, में, and so on — जो becomes जिस, and वह becomes the matching oblique form उस plus the same postposition.

Building Block — जिस...उस
जिस दुकान से मैंने यह किताब ख़रीदी, उस दुकान का मालिक बहुत मददगार है।
The shop from which I bought this book — that shop's owner is very helpful.

Form: जिस + noun + postposition + clause + comma + उस + noun + postposition + main clause. Meaning: the noun described plays a specific role (source, recipient, location) marked by the postposition. Use: giving precise, single-referent descriptions in formal or careful speech and writing.

Note: The noun (दुकान above) may repeat with उस, or be dropped if context is clear: जिस दुकान से मैंने यह किताब ख़रीदी, उसका मालिक बहुत मददगार है।

11.6 जिसका/जिसकी/जिसके...उसका/उसकी/उसके — Possession

When the description involves possession — whose book, whose house, whose idea — use जिसका/जिसकी/जिसके, agreeing with the possessed noun exactly as का/की/के would.

Building Block — जिसका...उसका
जिस औरत की बेटी डॉक्टर है, उसका घर बाज़ार के पास है।
The woman whose daughter is a doctor — her house is near the market.

Form: जिस + noun + जिसका/जिसकी/जिसके + possessed noun + clause + comma + उसका/उसकी/उसके + main clause. Meaning: identifies a person or thing by what belongs to them. Use: precise identification in descriptions, directories, and introductions — exactly the task Meera Ji's group is working on in this chapter's scenario.

Agreement reminder: जिसका/उसका agrees with the possessed noun's gender and number, not with the person being described: जिस आदमी की बेटियाँ... (feminine plural possessed noun) takes जिसकी, even though आदमी is masculine.

11.7 जहाँ...वहाँ — Place

Building Block — जहाँ...वहाँ
जहाँ नया पार्क बन रहा है, वहाँ पहले एक पुराना मैदान था।
Where the new park is being built, there used to be an old field.

Form: जहाँ + clause + comma + वहाँ + main clause. Use: identifying or describing a specific location, often useful in the community-directory task of this chapter's scenario.

11.8 Reference Tracking Across the Sentence

In all three expanded patterns, the relative clause normally comes first and the correlative clause second, but natural spoken Hindi sometimes reverses the order or drops the repeated noun. Keep two clauses per sentence at this stage; do not chain three or four relative clauses together, even though advanced Hindi permits denser structures. Clarity matters more than complexity.

Do not translate English "that" or "which" as a single fixed Hindi word in every context. जो, जिस, जिसका, and जहाँ each depend on the grammatical role of the noun being described, not on the English relative pronoun used to translate it.

Model Dialogue

Setting: At the group's eleventh meeting, Meera Ji, Priya, Aditya, and Kabir draft entries for the community directory, discovering that vague descriptions need to become precise ones.

मीरा जी पहली प्रविष्टि है: "वह औरत जो नया पुस्तकालय चलाती है।" लेकिन यह अधूरा वाक्य है। हमें बताना होगा कि वह कहाँ रहती है और उसका संपर्क कैसे किया जाए। The first entry is: "The woman who runs the new library." But this is an incomplete sentence. We need to say where she lives and how to contact her.
प्रिया ठीक है, तो हम लिख सकते हैं: जो औरत नया पुस्तकालय चलाती है, वह हमारी सड़क के आख़िर में रहती है। Okay, so we can write: the woman who runs the new library lives at the end of our street.
कबीर अच्छा, लेकिन अगली प्रविष्टि थोड़ी मुश्किल है: "वह दुकान जिससे लोग साइकिलें किराए पर लेते हैं।" यहाँ तो से भी है। Okay, but the next entry is a bit harder: "the shop from which people rent bicycles." Here there's also a "from."
मीरा जी बिल्कुल सही पकड़ा। जब वाक्य में कोई परसर्ग हो — से, को, पर — तो जो की जगह जिस इस्तेमाल होता है। तो हम लिखेंगे: जिस दुकान से लोग साइकिलें किराए पर लेते हैं, उस दुकान का पता बाज़ार के सामने है। Exactly right. When there's a postposition in the sentence — from, to, on — then jis is used instead of jo. So we'll write: the shop from which people rent bicycles — that shop's address is opposite the market.
आदित्य और अगली एक: "वह आदमी जिसका बेटा हमारी गली में क्रिकेट सिखाता है।" यहाँ रिश्ता है, परसर्ग नहीं। And the next one: "the man whose son teaches cricket in our lane." Here there's a relationship, not a postposition.
कबीर तो यहाँ जिसका होगा, क्योंकि हम बता रहे हैं कि बेटा किसका है। जिस आदमी का बेटा हमारी गली में क्रिकेट सिखाता है, उसका घर मैदान के पास है। So here it will be jiskaa, because we're saying whose son it is. The man whose son teaches cricket in our lane — his house is near the field.
प्रिया और आख़िरी प्रविष्टि जगह के बारे में है: "वह जगह जहाँ हर रविवार को सामान बेचा जाता है।" And the last entry is about a place: "the place where things are sold every Sunday."
मीरा जी जगह के लिए हम जहाँ...वहाँ इस्तेमाल करते हैं। जहाँ हर रविवार को सामान बेचा जाता है, वहाँ सुबह जल्दी पहुँचना बेहतर है, क्योंकि भीड़ बहुत होती है। For place, we use jahaa̐...vahaa̐. Where things are sold every Sunday, it's better to arrive early in the morning there, because it gets very crowded.
आदित्य तो चार प्रकार हो गए: जो...वह, जिस...उस, जिसका...उसका, और जहाँ...वहाँ। मेरा मतलब है, अब मुझे फ़र्क़ साफ़ समझ आ गया। So there are four types: jo...vah, jis...us, jiskaa...uskaa, and jahaa̐...vahaa̐. I mean, now the difference is completely clear to me.

समझ के प्रश्न — Comprehension questions:

  1. पुस्तकालय चलाने वाली औरत कहाँ रहती है? (Where does the woman who runs the library live?)
  2. साइकिल की दुकान के बारे में कौन-सा परसर्ग वाक्य में इस्तेमाल हुआ है? (Which postposition is used in the sentence about the bicycle shop?)
  3. जिसका का इस्तेमाल कब किया जाता है, कबीर के अनुसार? (When is jiskaa used, according to Kabir?)

Writing Workshop

Write three or four community-directory entries, मोहल्ला निर्देशिका की प्रविष्टियाँ ("Neighborhood Directory Entries"), about real or invented people, places, or services in a neighborhood, campus, or community you know.

लेखन कार्य — Writing task: Write a connected 120-160 word text in standard Devanagari that includes:

  1. At least one जो...वह sentence identifying a person by what they do.
  2. At least one जिस...उस sentence using a postposition such as से, को, or पर.
  3. At least one जिसका/जिसकी/जिसके...उसका/उसकी/उसके sentence identifying someone by a possessive relationship.
  4. At least one जहाँ...वहाँ sentence identifying a specific place.

संपादन सूची — Editing checklist:

  • Each relative clause has a matching correlative clause; no relative form is left without its pair.
  • जिसका/जिसकी/जिसके agrees with the possessed noun, not with the person being described.
  • No sentence chains more than two clauses together.
  • A comma separates the relative clause from the correlative clause.
  • Gender, number, agreement, and oblique forms are applied correctly throughout.
  • Spelling, matras, conjuncts, word spacing, and dandas are correct throughout.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Precision in community description

Community directories, notice boards, and word-of-mouth introductions across many Hindi-speaking neighborhoods often rely on precisely this kind of relative description — identifying someone by what they do, whom they are related to, or where they are found — rather than by a formal title or address alone. Learning to build these sentences accurately is directly useful for everyday orientation in a new community.

Culture in Context — Formality and sentence density

As the chapter scope notes, denser relative-correlative sentences tend to sound more formal than everyday conversation. In casual speech, many speakers prefer two shorter sentences over one long relative-correlative construction. Reserve denser structures for writing, directories, and more formal spoken contexts, and default to simpler phrasing in relaxed conversation.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using जो when a postposition is present

Error: जो दुकान से मैंने यह किताब ख़रीदी...

Correction: जिस दुकान से मैंने यह किताब ख़रीदी... — because से is present, जो must become जिस.

Common Mistake — Mismatched agreement in जिसका/जिसकी/जिसके

Error: जिस आदमी की बेटा... (agreeing with आदमी instead of the possessed noun).

Correction: जिस आदमी का बेटा... — जिसका/जिसकी/जिसके agrees with what is possessed (बेटा, masculine), not with the person being described.

Common Mistake — Leaving the correlative clause out

Error: जहाँ हर रविवार को सामान बेचा जाता है, सुबह जल्दी पहुँचना बेहतर है। (missing वहाँ)

Correction: जहाँ हर रविवार को सामान बेचा जाता है, वहाँ सुबह जल्दी पहुँचना बेहतर है। — the correlative वहाँ completes the pair and keeps the sentence from sounding unfinished.

प्रस्तुति और चर्चा

Volume III, Chapter 17: Presentation and Discussion · Speak and Write Hindi: Independent Communication and Advanced Expression

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Give a short, organized three-to-five-minute presentation in Hindi with a clear introduction, main points, examples, and conclusion.
  2. Use discourse markers such as सबसे पहले, दूसरी बात, and अंत में to structure spoken and written presentations.
  3. Ask and respond to clarification questions using frames such as क्या आपका मतलब है कि... and मैं फिर से समझाता/समझाती हूँ.
  4. Use rhetorical questions in a controlled way to engage an audience.
  5. Manage a discussion by inviting questions, acknowledging a questioner, and giving a focused follow-up answer.
  6. Speak from notes or an outline rather than reading a script word-for-word.

Communication Scenario

The community events committee has asked Priya to give a short presentation at the next neighborhood meeting about the group's growing activities — the orientation talk, the hall-booking request, and possible future events. Kabir, meanwhile, has been invited to a short interview follow-up where he must briefly present his qualifications and answer questions. Both need the same skill: organizing spoken Hindi into a clear structure, and handling audience questions calmly, whether the audience is a room full of neighbors or a single interviewer.

  1. Notice how each speaker signals the structure of what they are about to say.
  2. Notice how examples and evidence are woven into the main points.
  3. Notice how a speaker asks for clarification without appearing confused or unprepared.
  4. Notice how a speaker closes a presentation and opens the floor to questions.

Register Note — Presentation vs. Everyday Speech

A presentation uses more explicit structure than ordinary conversation. In conversation you might simply say और एक बात ("and one more thing"); in a presentation, दूसरी बात or इसके अलावा signals the same shift more clearly for a listening audience who cannot ask you to repeat something as easily as a friend can.

17.3 Receptive Vocabulary

The following forms should be recognized in classroom, conference, or workplace discussion contexts but are not required for independent production: सत्र ("session"), परिचर्चा ("panel discussion"), प्रतिभागी ("participant"), and समय सीमित है ("time is limited," a common moderator phrase).

Pronunciation and Listening Focus

Presentation rhythm and pausing

A presentation should not sound like a single unbroken stream of speech. Practice pausing briefly after each transition marker — सबसे पहले, दूसरी बात, अंत में — so that the listener has a moment to register the shift to a new point.

Intonation for rhetorical questions and clarification

A rhetorical question such as तो हम यह समस्या कैसे हल कर सकते हैं? ("So how can we solve this problem?") is spoken with a falling, thoughtful tone rather than the sharp rising tone of a real information question, since the speaker will answer it themselves. Contrast this with the genuine, slightly rising tone of a clarification check such as क्या आपका मतलब है कि...?

Pronunciation self-monitoring in extended speech

Practice these formal presentation words: प्रस्तुति (pra-STU-ti), निष्कर्ष (nish-KARSH), and श्रोता (SHRO-taa, with the conjunct श्र pronounced as one blended sound). Reading your notes aloud slowly before presenting helps you catch and correct these in advance.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

17.4 Organizing a Presentation with Discourse Markers

Language Building Block — Presentation Structure
सबसे पहले, मैं आपको हमारे पिछले कार्यक्रम के बारे में बताऊँगी। दूसरी बात, मैं हॉल बुकिंग की योजना समझाऊँगी। अंत में, मैं आपसे कुछ सुझाव माँगूँगी।
First of all, I will tell you about our previous event. Second, I will explain the hall-booking plan. Finally, I will ask you for some suggestions.

Use सबसे पहले, दूसरी बात, and अंत में to preview the structure of a presentation at the start, and again to mark each section as you reach it. This recycles the future-tense forms from earlier chapters inside a new, more formal discourse frame.

17.5 Rhetorical Questions in Controlled Use

Language Building Block — Rhetorical Question
तो हम हर महीने कार्यक्रम क्यों नहीं करते? क्योंकि हमारे पास पर्याप्त स्वयंसेवक नहीं हैं।
So why don't we hold an event every month? Because we don't have enough volunteers.

A rhetorical question raises a question only to answer it immediately, which draws the audience's attention to the answer. Use this pattern sparingly — once or twice in a short presentation — rather than turning every point into a question.

17.6 Clarification Frames

Language Building Block — Clarification
क्या आपका मतलब है कि हमें ज़्यादा पैसे चाहिए? अगर हाँ, तो मैं फिर से समझाती हूँ...
Do you mean that we need more money? If so, let me explain again...

When a question is unclear, restate your understanding of it using क्या आपका मतलब है कि...? or क्या आप यह पूछ रहे हैं कि...? before answering. This confirms the question and buys a moment to organize a clear response. Use मैं फिर से समझाता/समझाती हूँ to signal that you are rephrasing a point rather than repeating it word-for-word.

17.7 Acknowledging and Answering Questions

Before answering an audience question, briefly acknowledge it with an expression such as अच्छा प्रश्न है or यह एक ज़रूरी सवाल है. This is a pragmatic move, not new grammar: it signals respect for the questioner and gives the speaker a moment to think. Follow the acknowledgment with a focused, direct answer rather than a second full explanation of the entire presentation.

Boundary Reminder

This chapter does not require academic conference-level argumentation, specialized disciplinary vocabulary, or advanced rhetorical devices such as extended metaphor or irony. Keep presentations clear, organized, and audience-aware rather than stylistically elaborate.

Model Dialogue

Setting: At the neighborhood meeting, Priya gives a short presentation and then takes questions from Aditya and a neighbor, Rakesh Ji.

प्रिया नमस्ते सबको। आज मैं आपको हमारी समिति की गतिविधियों के बारे में बताना चाहती हूँ। सबसे पहले, मैं पिछले कार्यक्रम के बारे में बताऊँगी। दूसरी बात, मैं हॉल बुकिंग की योजना समझाऊँगी। अंत में, मैं आपसे सुझाव माँगूँगी। Hello everyone. Today I want to tell you about our committee's activities. First, I will talk about the last event. Second, I will explain the hall-booking plan. Finally, I will ask you for suggestions.
प्रिया तो, पिछले महीने हमारे कार्यक्रम में लगभग साठ लोग आए। यह एक बड़ी सफलता थी। दूसरी बात, हमने अगले कार्यक्रम के लिए हॉल का अनुरोध भेज दिया है। अंत में, मैं यह पूछना चाहती हूँ — क्या आप में से कोई अगली बार स्वयंसेवक बनना चाहेगा? So, last month about sixty people came to our event. This was a big success. Second, we have sent a request for the hall for the next event. Finally, I want to ask — would any of you like to be a volunteer next time?
राकेश जी माफ़ कीजिए, क्या मैं एक सवाल पूछ सकता हूँ? अगला कार्यक्रम कब होगा? Excuse me, may I ask a question? When will the next event be?
प्रिया अच्छा प्रश्न है। हमें अभी हॉल की पुष्टि नहीं मिली है, लेकिन हम अगले तीन महीने में कार्यक्रम करने की योजना बना रहे हैं। That's a good question. We haven't received confirmation of the hall yet, but we're planning to hold the event within the next three months.
आदित्य क्या आपका मतलब है कि तारीख़ अभी तय नहीं है, या कि हमें तीन महीने इंतज़ार करना होगा? Do you mean the date isn't decided yet, or that we'll have to wait three months?
प्रिया मैं फिर से समझाती हूँ — तारीख़ तय नहीं है, लेकिन हम तीन महीने के अंदर करने की कोशिश कर रहे हैं। जैसे ही पुष्टि मिलेगी, मैं आप सबको बता दूँगी। Let me explain again — the date isn't decided, but we're trying to hold it within three months. As soon as we get confirmation, I'll let everyone know.
प्रिया तो, क्या कोई और प्रश्न है? नहीं? ठीक है, तो मेरे निष्कर्ष में, हमें आप सबकी मदद चाहिए, और मुझे उम्मीद है कि आप में से कुछ लोग स्वयंसेवक बनेंगे। धन्यवाद। So, are there any other questions? No? Okay, so in conclusion, we need everyone's help, and I hope some of you will become volunteers. Thank you.

समझ के प्रश्न — Comprehension questions:

  1. प्रिया अपनी प्रस्तुति को कैसे तीन भागों में बाँटती है? (How does Priya divide her presentation into three parts?)
  2. राकेश जी क्या पूछते हैं, और प्रिया शुरू में क्या जवाब देती है? (What does Rakesh Ji ask, and what does Priya initially answer?)
  3. आदित्य किस बात को स्पष्ट करना चाहते हैं, और प्रिया कैसे स्पष्ट करती है? (What does Aditya want clarified, and how does Priya clarify it?)

Writing Workshop

Write a presentation outline, प्रस्तुति की रूपरेखा ("Presentation Outline"), on one of the following topics: your community or workplace, a plan or project you are involved in, a comparison of two options relevant to your life, or a short personal or cultural topic of your choice.

लेखन कार्य — Writing task: Write a structured outline (not a full script) in standard Devanagari that includes:

  1. An introduction that previews your three main points.
  2. Three main points, each introduced with a discourse marker (सबसे पहले, दूसरी बात, अंत में) and supported by at least one example or piece of evidence, recycled from Chapters 6 and 9.
  3. One controlled rhetorical question used naturally within one of your points.
  4. A conclusion using मेरे निष्कर्ष में or an equivalent frame, plus an invitation for questions.
  5. One anticipated question with a brief planned answer, including a clarification frame if the question could be ambiguous.

संपादन सूची — Editing checklist:

  • The outline previews its structure clearly at the start.
  • Each main point is clearly marked with an appropriate discourse marker.
  • At least one example or piece of evidence supports each point.
  • The rhetorical question is used sparingly and answered immediately.
  • The conclusion and question-invitation are present and appropriately worded.
  • Gender, number, agreement, oblique forms, and postpositions are applied correctly throughout.
  • Spelling, matras, conjuncts, word spacing, and punctuation are correct throughout.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Presentation style varies by setting

A community meeting, a classroom presentation, and a professional interview each call for a somewhat different presentation style in Hindi-speaking contexts, just as they do in English. A neighborhood presentation may be warmer and more conversational, while a formal professional or academic setting typically expects a more structured, less improvisational delivery. Neither style is more "correct" Hindi; the setting determines the appropriate approach.

Culture in Context — Questioning and politeness

Asking a clarifying or challenging question of a speaker is a normal part of many Hindi-speaking discussion contexts, but the way a question is framed matters. Opening with माफ़ कीजिए, क्या मैं एक सवाल पूछ सकता हूँ? ("Excuse me, may I ask a question?"), as Rakesh Ji does in the Model Dialogue, signals respect for the speaker before interrupting their flow.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — No structural markers

Error: A presentation that moves from point to point without any transition, so the audience cannot tell when one idea ends and the next begins.

Correction: Mark each shift explicitly with सबसे पहले, दूसरी बात, इसके बाद, or अंत में, even if the shift feels obvious to the speaker.

Common Mistake — Overusing rhetorical questions

Error: A presentation in which nearly every sentence is phrased as a question, which becomes repetitive and can sound uncertain rather than persuasive.

Correction: Reserve the rhetorical question for one or two key moments, and state the rest of your points as direct, confident statements.

Common Mistake — Answering without acknowledging or clarifying

Error: Launching into a long answer immediately when a question is ambiguous, guessing at what the questioner meant instead of checking.

Correction: Use क्या आपका मतलब है कि...? to confirm an unclear question, and अच्छा प्रश्न है to acknowledge a clear one, before giving a focused answer.

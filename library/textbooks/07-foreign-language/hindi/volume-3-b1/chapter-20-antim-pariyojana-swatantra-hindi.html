Volume III, Chapter 20: Final Project: Independent Hindi · Speak and Write Hindi: Independent Communication and Advanced Expression

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Understand a multi-part spoken and written Hindi source set on a real-world topic.
  2. Summarize and discuss information from a short source with accurate attribution.
  3. Give a structured opinion or recommendation supported by evidence and examples.
  4. Describe relevant people, places, events, and conditions using relative-correlative structures, temporal and contrastive clauses, and conditional or concessive meaning.
  5. Report what someone said, asked, or wrote using appropriate reported-speech frames.
  6. Adjust speech and writing for audience, purpose, and register, including formal correspondence.
  7. Interpret a cultural, idiomatic, or media element in context.
  8. Produce an integrated oral and written portfolio that demonstrates readiness for CEFR B2 and for Hindi Volume IV.

Communication Scenario

The housing society's lift is finally being repaired. Before work begins, the management committee asks residents to share input at an open meeting, and afterward asks a few residents — including Priya — to help prepare materials for the building's multilingual, multigenerational community: an announcement email, a plain-language summary for elderly residents like Sudha Ji, a short spoken update for the building's WhatsApp voice group, and a two-minute presentation for the meeting itself. Kabir, Aditya, and Sudha Ji all reappear, each needing a different version of essentially the same information. This is the final task of Volume III: using everything learners have built — narration, opinion, explanation, complex sentences, reported speech, formal writing, presentation, cultural interpretation, and mediation — for one coherent real-world purpose.

  1. Notice how the same underlying information appears in a formal announcement, a spoken summary, and a presentation.
  2. Notice how speakers report what others said at the meeting.
  3. Notice how relative clauses, conditions, and cause-result connectors organize longer explanations.
  4. Notice how each version stays accurate to the source while adjusting register for its audience.

Pronunciation and Listening Focus

Integrating rhythm across registers

This chapter's tasks move across four registers in one project: a formal email, a plain-language explanation, a casual voice message, and a presentation. Before recording or speaking, decide which register you are in and let your pace, pausing, and word choice shift accordingly, the way Priya did across the email and the plain-language explanation in Chapter 19.

Schwa deletion and phrase grouping in extended speech

Extended speech, such as a two-minute presentation, tends to expose uneven schwa deletion and phrase grouping more than short sentences do. Read your presentation notes aloud slowly at least once, marking natural breath groups, before practicing at full speed.

Intonation across explanation, reporting, and recommendation

A reported statement (उन्होंने कहा कि...), a conditional recommendation (अगर...तो बेहतर होगा कि...), and a direct recommendation (मैं सलाह दूँगा/दूँगी कि...) each carry a slightly different intonation contour. Practice moving between them without flattening all three into the same tone.

Integrated Grammar Review

This section introduces no new grammar. It shows how Volume III's major systems combine naturally in one extended, real-world text — the kind of writing this chapter's portfolio requires.

20.3 One Update, Four Registers

Language Building Block — The Same Information, Reused Across Registers
औपचारिक: "सभी निवासियों को सूचित किया जाता है कि लिफ़्ट की मरम्मत सोमवार से शुरू होगी, जो लगभग एक सप्ताह में पूरी हो जाएगी।" सरल: "सोमवार से लिफ़्ट ठीक होना शुरू होगी। इसमें करीब एक हफ़्ता लगेगा।" बोलचाल: "अरे, सुना? सोमवार से लिफ़्ट बनना शुरू होगी, एक हफ़्ते में हो जाएगी!" प्रस्तुति: "जैसा कि बैठक में बताया गया, मरम्मत सोमवार से शुरू होगी और लगभग एक सप्ताह में पूरी हो जाएगी।"
Formal: "All residents are informed that lift repair will begin Monday, which will be completed in about a week." Plain: "The lift will start being fixed from Monday. It will take about a week." Colloquial: "Hey, did you hear? The lift's getting fixed from Monday, it'll be done in a week!" Presentation: "As stated at the meeting, repairs will begin Monday and will be completed in about a week."

Notice that the relative clause जो लगभग एक सप्ताह में पूरी हो जाएगी (Chapter 11), the reporting frame जैसा कि बैठक में बताया गया (Chapters 8, 14), and the register shifts (Chapters 16, 19) all draw on systems already active. This chapter asks learners to combine them purposefully rather than learn them for the first time.

20.4 Condition, Cause, and Recommendation Together

A realistic recommendation often needs a condition, a cause, and a recommendation frame in the same passage: अगर मरम्मत में देरी होती है, तो इसका कारण शायद ज़रूरी पुर्ज़ों की कमी होगी; इसलिए मेरी सलाह है कि सोसाइटी पहले से एक वैकल्पिक योजना बना ले। ("If the repair is delayed, the reason will probably be a shortage of necessary parts; therefore, my advice is that the society make an alternative plan in advance.") This combines the conditional pattern from Chapter 13, the cause-result connector from Chapter 7, and the recommendation frame from Chapter 9.

20.5 Reporting Multiple Speakers at a Meeting

When summarizing a meeting, keep each speaker's contribution clearly attributed: एक निवासी ने कहा कि मरम्मत जल्दी होनी चाहिए, जबकि प्रबंधन समिति ने बताया कि पुर्ज़े मंगवाने में समय लगेगा। ("One resident said that the repair should happen quickly, whereas the management committee stated that it would take time to order the parts.") This reuses reported speech (Chapter 14) together with the contrastive connector जबकि (Chapter 12).

Boundary Reminder

This chapter does not introduce new clause types, new postpositional functions, new compound verbs, or new register systems. If a task seems to require an untaught structure, simplify the sentence or use an already-active pattern instead of reaching for new grammar.

Model Dialogue

Setting: After the housing society's open meeting about the lift repair, Priya, Kabir, and Aditya compare notes on the way home, and Priya later updates her grandmother, Sudha Ji.

कबीर बैठक में तो बहुत बातें हुईं। एक निवासी ने कहा कि मरम्मत जल्दी होनी चाहिए, जबकि प्रबंधन समिति ने बताया कि पुर्ज़े मंगवाने में समय लगेगा। A lot came up in the meeting. One resident said the repair should happen quickly, whereas the management committee said it would take time to order the parts.
आदित्य तो अगर पुर्ज़े देर से आते हैं, तो मरम्मत भी देर से शुरू होगी। यह तो वही समस्या है जो हमने चैप्टर सात में पढ़ी थी — कारण और परिणाम। So if the parts arrive late, the repair will also start late. This is the same kind of problem we studied in Chapter 7 — cause and effect.
प्रिया बिल्कुल। मेरे विचार में, सोसाइटी को एक ईमेल भेजनी चाहिए जिसमें साफ़-साफ़ तारीख़ बताई जाए, ताकि जिन लोगों को सीढ़ियाँ चढ़ने में तकलीफ़ होती है, वे पहले से तैयारी कर सकें। Exactly. In my opinion, the society should send an email that clearly states the date, so that people who have difficulty climbing stairs can prepare in advance.
कबीर तुम वही ईमेल क्यों नहीं लिखतीं? और नानी के लिए एक सरल संदेश भी बना देना। Why don't you write that very email? And make a simple message for your grandmother too.

Later, at home:

सुधा जी बैठक में क्या तय हुआ, बेटा? What was decided at the meeting, dear?
प्रिया नानी, सरल शब्दों में कहूँ तो, सोमवार से लिफ़्ट ठीक होना शुरू होगी, और इसमें करीब एक हफ़्ता लगेगा। अगर देर हुई, तो वजह पुर्ज़ों की कमी होगी — इसलिए मैंने आपके लिए सीढ़ियों की एक अस्थायी व्यवस्था के बारे में भी पूछा है। Grandma, to put it simply, the lift will start being fixed from Monday, and it will take about a week. If there's a delay, the reason will be a shortage of parts — so I also asked about a temporary stair arrangement for you.
सुधा जी शुक्रिया, बेटा। तुमने पूरी बात इतनी साफ़ तरीके से समझा दी। Thank you, dear. You explained the whole matter so clearly.

समझ के प्रश्न — Comprehension questions:

  1. बैठक में निवासी और प्रबंधन समिति की राय में क्या अंतर था? (What was the difference between the resident's and the management committee's views at the meeting?)
  2. प्रिया ने अपनी नानी को क्या-क्या बताया, और उसने भाषा कैसे बदली? (What did Priya tell her grandmother, and how did she adjust her language?)
  3. प्रिया ने सोसाइटी को किस तरह का ईमेल भेजने का सुझाव दिया, और क्यों? (What kind of email did Priya suggest the society send, and why?)

Writing Workshop

This Writing Workshop is the written half of the Final B2 Portfolio Task described below. Complete all parts in standard Devanagari.

लेखन कार्य — Writing task:

  1. Write a source-based summary (3-4 sentences) of the Reading Practice email, using at least one reporting frame and correct source attribution.
  2. Write a supported recommendation (4-5 sentences) about how the society should communicate further updates to residents, using at least one cause-result connector and one recommendation frame.
  3. Write a complex description (3-4 sentences) of a resident affected by the repair (real or invented), using at least one relative-correlative structure and one conditional or concessive clause.
  4. Write a short plain-language message (2-3 sentences) explaining the update to a family member, following the register-transformation practice from Chapter 19.
  5. Write a formal follow-up email (5-6 sentences) to the management committee, asking a clarifying question and making one polite request, using the formal conventions from Chapter 16.

संपादन सूची — Editing checklist:

  • Each of the five pieces stays within its assigned register and does not drift into another register mid-text.
  • The summary attributes information to its source and does not add unstated certainty.
  • The recommendation gives at least one clear reason, not just a bare suggestion.
  • The complex description uses at least one relative-correlative structure and one conditional or concessive clause accurately.
  • The formal email includes an appropriate opening, a clear request, and an appropriate closing.
  • Gender, number, agreement, oblique forms, postpositions, and `ने`/`को` patterns are applied correctly throughout.
  • Spelling, matras, conjuncts, word spacing, and punctuation are correct throughout.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Community communication across generations

Housing societies, residential associations, and similar community bodies across Hindi-speaking regions and diaspora settings often need to reach residents of very different ages, languages, and levels of formality with the same underlying information. The skills in this chapter — register adjustment, plain-language explanation, and formal correspondence — are ordinary, practical tools for exactly this kind of everyday community life, not specialized professional skills.

Culture in Context — Register choice as respect, not just correctness

Choosing a plainer register for an elderly relative or a more formal register for an institutional email is not simply a grammar rule; it reflects care for the listener's or reader's needs. B2 Hindi communication should be judged not only on accuracy but on whether it serves its audience well.

Culture in Context — Moving into Volume IV

The public, institutional, and mediating language practiced in this chapter is a bridge to Hindi Volume IV, where similar tasks — reports, presentations, formal correspondence, and cultural interpretation — expand into academic, professional, and public communication at a more demanding, C1-oriented level.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Losing register control across a long portfolio

Error: Starting a formal email with an appropriately formal opening, then sliding into colloquial phrasing (भइया, contractions, casual particles) by the closing lines.

Correction: Reread the entire piece after drafting and check that every sentence, including the closing, matches the register established at the opening.

Common Mistake — Reporting information as personal opinion

Error: Writing मुझे लगता है कि मरम्मत सोमवार से शुरू होगी ("I think the repair will start Monday") when this is a stated fact from the meeting, not a personal guess.

Correction: Reserve मुझे लगता है कि and similar hedges for actual opinions; use बैठक में बताया गया कि or तय हुआ कि for confirmed information.

Common Mistake — Overloading one sentence with every complex structure

Error: Cramming a relative clause, a condition, a reported statement, and a cause-result connector into a single overlong sentence that becomes unclear.

Correction: Split the information across two or three clearer sentences, as modeled throughout this chapter, and prioritize clarity over structural density.

Listening

  • The lift repair will begin Monday and take about a week.
  • A possible delay would be due to necessary parts not arriving on time.
  • A temporary arrangement will be considered for residents who have difficulty climbing stairs.

Reading — Comprehension

  1. The three shared pieces of information: the start date and duration of the repair, the possible cause of delay, and the plan for residents who have difficulty with stairs.
  2. Relative clause: जिन निवासियों को सीढ़ियाँ चढ़ने में असुविधा होती है; conditional clause: यदि आवश्यक पुर्ज़े समय पर नहीं पहुँचते, तो...; reported-decision phrase: आज की बैठक में जो निर्णय लिए गए, उनके अनुसार... These correspond to Chapters 11, 13, and 8/14 respectively.
  3. Model plain-language version: "लिफ़्ट सोमवार से ठीक होना शुरू होगी, एक हफ़्ता लगेगा। अगर पुर्ज़े देर से आए, तो थोड़ा और समय लग सकता है। सीढ़ियों में दिक़्क़त वालों के लिए कुछ इंतज़ाम किया जा रहा है।"
  4. Model answer: keep the start date, duration, and the stair-difficulty arrangement; remove the formal opening, the source-attribution phrase, and the closing apology, since a 15-second voice message needs only the core facts.

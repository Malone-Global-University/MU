भाषा, शैली, और मध्यस्थता

Volume III, Chapter 19: Language, Style, and Mediation · Speak and Write Hindi: Independent Communication and Advanced Expression

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Revise a Hindi message or text for a different audience, purpose, or level of formality.
  2. Paraphrase a Hindi passage in simpler Hindi without losing its core meaning.
  3. Summarize Hindi information in English and English information in Hindi at a B2 level.
  4. Distinguish literal translation from natural meaning when moving between Hindi and English.
  5. Explain, in plain terms, register differences among formal Hindi, everyday Hindi, Hindi-English usage, and Urdu/Perso-Arabic-associated vocabulary.
  6. Mediate meaning between two speakers or texts without claiming word-for-word equivalence.

Communication Scenario

Kabir's friend Aditya has drafted a casual WhatsApp message to their building's housing society office asking about a delayed repair, and he asks Priya to help him turn it into something he can actually send to the office secretary. At the same time, Priya's grandmother, Sudha Ji, has received a formal notice from the same office and asks Priya to explain it to her "in normal words." Both situations ask the same underlying question: how do we move Hindi between registers, and between Hindi and English, without losing what the message actually means and without pretending that one word always equals another?

  1. Notice which words or phrases change when the message shifts from casual to formal.
  2. Notice how Priya explains the notice's meaning rather than reading it aloud word for word.
  3. Notice how the speakers name the register they are choosing rather than mixing silently.
  4. Notice how mediation preserves the sender's intent and the reader's need, not just individual words.

19.3 Receptive Vocabulary

The following forms should be recognized in discussions about language and translation but are not required for independent production: भाषांतरण ("act of translating," more technical than अनुवाद), मध्यस्थ ("mediator," the person who mediates), and शाब्दिक ("literal, word-based"). Specialized linguistic or translation-studies terminology should be glossed rather than taught productively.

Pronunciation and Listening Focus

Hearing register in delivery, not just words

The same sentence can sound formal or casual partly through pace and pitch, not only word choice. A formal reading of कृपया सूचित करें tends to be slower and more evenly paced than a quick, casual बता देना, ठीक है? Listen for this difference, not only for vocabulary.

Schwa deletion in Sanskritic and Perso-Arabic-associated vocabulary

Formal words such as औपचारिकता (aupcharikta, not aupcharikataa) and Perso-Arabic-associated words such as गुज़ारिश (guzaarish) both delete the inherent vowel in predictable positions. Read each new formal or borrowed word aloud slowly before using it in a sentence.

Clear oral paraphrase

When you paraphrase aloud, pause briefly after a frame like दूसरे शब्दों में before continuing. This short pause signals to the listener that a restatement, not new information, is coming.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

19.4 Paraphrase Frames

Language Building Block — Paraphrasing in Simple Hindi
नोटिस में लिखा है, "मरम्मत कार्य अस्थायी रूप से स्थगित किया गया है।" सरल शब्दों में, इसका मतलब है कि मरम्मत का काम अभी के लिए रुक गया है।
The notice says, "Repair work has been temporarily suspended." In simple words, this means the repair work has stopped for now.

Use सरल शब्दों में or दूसरे शब्दों में to introduce a paraphrase, followed by इसका मतलब है कि... and a clause built with everyday vocabulary and familiar grammar. Paraphrase should replace formal or Sanskritic vocabulary with common alternatives while keeping the same core information.

19.5 Register Transformation: Casual to Formal

Language Building Block — Revising for Register
बोलचाल में: "भइया, ये मरम्मत कब होगी? बहुत टाइम हो गया।" औपचारिक ईमेल में: "मैं यह जानना चाहता/चाहती हूँ कि मरम्मत का काम कब पूरा होगा, क्योंकि काफ़ी समय बीत चुका है।"
Colloquial: "Hey brother, when will this repair happen? It's been a long time." Formal email: "I would like to know when the repair work will be completed, as considerable time has passed."

Register transformation typically changes: address terms (भइया → omitted or replaced with a polite opening), sentence length (short and clipped → fuller and more explicit), verb choice (होगा → पूरा होगा), and the presence of hedging or politeness markers. It does not change the underlying request.

19.6 Summarizing Across Hindi and English

A neutral B2 summary compresses information using nominal phrases and reported-information frames, reactivated from Chapters 8 and 14: रिपोर्ट के अनुसार, सोसाइटी ने मरम्मत में देरी की वजह बजट की कमी बताई है। ("According to the notice, the society has cited a lack of budget as the reason for the delay in repairs.") A Hindi-to-English or English-to-Hindi summary at this level should preserve the source's stance and level of certainty, not add confidence the original text does not have.

19.7 Flagging Non-Literal Translation

When a word or phrase would mislead if translated literally, mark this explicitly with शब्द-दर-शब्द नहीं ("not word-for-word") or by naming the natural equivalent directly: "गुज़ारिश" का मतलब सिर्फ़ "request" जैसा नहीं है — इसमें थोड़ी विनम्रता और निजी अपील का भाव भी है। ("'Guzaarish' doesn't just mean 'request' — it also carries a sense of politeness and personal appeal.")

Boundary Reminder

This chapter does not require professional translation training, advanced Urdu literacy, formal linguistic theory, or specialized legal or medical translation. Mediation tasks stay at the level of everyday, institutional, and cultural texts already familiar from this volume.

Model Dialogue

Setting: At home, Priya helps her grandmother, Sudha Ji, understand a formal notice from the housing society, then helps her friend Aditya revise a casual message into a formal one for the same office.

सुधा जी प्रिया, यह नोटिस देखो। इसमें लिखा है, "मरम्मत कार्य अस्थायी रूप से स्थगित किया गया है।" मुझे तो कुछ समझ नहीं आया। Priya, look at this notice. It says, "Repair work has been temporarily suspended." I didn't understand anything.
प्रिया नानी, सरल शब्दों में, इसका मतलब है कि मरम्मत का काम अभी के लिए रुक गया है। यह भाषा थोड़ी औपचारिक है, इसलिए मुश्किल लग रही है। Grandma, in simple words, this means the repair work has stopped for now. This language is a bit formal, so it seems difficult.
सुधा जी अच्छा! तो "स्थगित" का मतलब "रुका हुआ" है? I see! So "postponed/suspended" means "stopped"?
प्रिया बिल्कुल, दूसरे शब्दों में यही मतलब है। यह शब्द अधिक औपचारिक है — बोलचाल में हम सीधे "रुक गया है" कहेंगे। Exactly, that's the same meaning in other words. This word is more formal — in everyday speech we'd just say "it's stopped."
आदित्य प्रिया, मैंने सोसाइटी ऑफ़िस को यह मैसेज लिखा है: "भइया, ये मरम्मत कब होगी? बहुत टाइम हो गया।" क्या मैं यह ऐसे ही भेज दूँ? Priya, I wrote this message to the society office: "Hey brother, when will this repair happen? It's been a long time." Should I just send it like this?
प्रिया दफ़्तर के लिए यह बहुत बोलचाल जैसा है। मैं इसे थोड़ा औपचारिक बना देती हूँ: "मैं यह जानना चाहती हूँ कि मरम्मत का काम कब पूरा होगा, क्योंकि काफ़ी समय बीत चुका है।" This is too colloquial for an office. Let me make it a bit more formal: "I would like to know when the repair work will be completed, as considerable time has passed."
आदित्य मतलब सिर्फ़ शब्द नहीं, पूरा लहजा भी बदल गया। So it's not just the words — the whole tone changed too.
प्रिया बिल्कुल सही। संक्षेप में, तुम्हारा सवाल वही है, पर संदर्भ और स्तर के हिसाब से भाषा बदलनी पड़ती है। Exactly right. In short, your question is the same, but the language has to change according to context and register.

समझ के प्रश्न — Comprehension questions:

  1. सुधा जी को नोटिस का कौन-सा वाक्य समझ नहीं आया, और प्रिया ने उसे कैसे समझाया? (Which sentence in the notice did Sudha Ji not understand, and how did Priya explain it?)
  2. आदित्य का पहला संदेश किस तरह बोलचाल जैसा था? (In what way was Aditya's first message colloquial?)
  3. प्रिया के अनुसार, बोलचाल और औपचारिक संदेश में क्या बदलता है? (According to Priya, what changes between a colloquial and a formal message?)

Writing Workshop

Choose one everyday text (a casual message, a short notice, or a piece of news) and produce two revisions: a plain-language paraphrase and a register-appropriate transformation.

लेखन कार्य — Writing task: Complete the following in standard Devanagari:

  1. Choose or write a short source text (40-60 words) in one register — casual, formal, or media style.
  2. Paraphrase it in simpler Hindi using सरल शब्दों में or दूसरे शब्दों में, preserving the core meaning.
  3. Transform it into a different register (casual → formal, or formal → casual), adjusting address, sentence length, and politeness markers.
  4. Write a short summary (2-3 sentences) of the source text in English, preserving its stance and level of certainty.
  5. Add a brief note identifying at least one word or phrase that should not be translated word-for-word, and explain its natural sense.

संपादन सूची — Editing checklist:

  • The paraphrase keeps the same core meaning as the source while using simpler vocabulary and structure.
  • The register transformation changes tone, address, and phrasing without changing the underlying request or information.
  • The English summary does not add confidence, certainty, or detail beyond what the Hindi source states.
  • At least one word or phrase is flagged as not translating literally, with its natural sense explained.
  • Gender, number, agreement, oblique forms, and postpositions are applied correctly throughout.
  • Spelling, matras, conjuncts, word spacing, and punctuation are correct throughout.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Register signals more than formality

Choosing a more Sanskritic word, a Perso-Arabic-associated word, an English loanword, or a colloquial phrase can signal education, institution, region, generation, or relationship — but no single word choice proves someone's identity, religion, or background. Learners should describe what a register choice suggests in context rather than treating it as a fixed label for a person.

Culture in Context — Mediation as a everyday skill

Many multilingual and multigenerational households, like Priya's, rely on someone who can move comfortably between formal institutional Hindi, everyday spoken Hindi, and English. This is an ordinary, valuable skill in many Hindi-speaking communities and diaspora households, not a specialized professional task.

Culture in Context — Translation preserves social meaning

A good translation or summary preserves not only dictionary meaning but also tone, politeness, and intent. A literal word-for-word rendering can be technically accurate and still mislead a reader about how polite, urgent, or certain the original message was.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Changing meaning while "simplifying"

Error: Paraphrasing "मरम्मत कार्य अस्थायी रूप से स्थगित किया गया है" as "मरम्मत नहीं होगी" ("the repair will not happen"), which removes the word "temporarily" and changes the meaning.

Correction: Preserve the core information — including time limits, conditions, and hedges — even while simplifying vocabulary: "मरम्मत का काम अभी के लिए रुक गया है।"

Common Mistake — Word-for-word translation of a register-marked phrase

Error: Translating गुज़ारिश and अनुरोध identically as "request" in every context, hiding the fact that one is more colloquial and personal and the other more formal or institutional.

Correction: Note the register difference explicitly when it matters: "both mean 'request,' but गुज़ारिश sounds more personal and अनुरोध sounds more formal."

Common Mistake — Overusing formal vocabulary in casual mediation

Error: Explaining a notice to a family member using the same Sanskritic vocabulary as the original, so the "plain-language" explanation is not actually simpler.

Correction: Replace formal or Sanskritic words with everyday equivalents when mediating for a casual audience, as in "स्थगित" → "रुका हुआ।"

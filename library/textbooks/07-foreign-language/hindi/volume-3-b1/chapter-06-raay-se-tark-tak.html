राय से तर्क तक

Volume III, Chapter 6: From Opinion to Argument · Speak and Write Hindi: Independent Communication and Advanced Expression

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. State an opinion clearly using appropriate stance expressions.
  2. Distinguish preference, belief, fact, evidence, and uncertainty in Hindi.
  3. Support an opinion with at least one reason and one example.
  4. Agree, disagree, and partially agree politely in conversation.
  5. Ask someone to explain or justify their view.
  6. Organize a short written opinion using a claim-reason-example-conclusion structure.

Communication Scenario

The conversation group's sixth meeting shifts away from personal profiles and toward public topics. Meera जी brings a short discussion prompt about smartphone use among students, and asks the group to share and defend their views. Priya, Kabir, and a new member, Aditya, each state an opinion, and the group practices agreeing, disagreeing, and asking for justification.

  1. Notice how each speaker opens with a stance expression such as मुझे लगता है कि or मेरे विचार में before stating a claim.
  2. Notice how disagreement is softened with words like शायद, हो सकता है, or फिर भी rather than stated bluntly.
  3. Notice that every opinion in the dialogue is followed by at least one reason or example, not left unsupported.
  4. This chapter's task is to move from simply having an opinion to building a short, organized argument that a listener or reader can follow and evaluate.

Where This Chapter Fits

Chapter 6 opens Unit II, विचार, प्रमाण, और सार्वजनिक संवाद. It introduces the first new grammar of the unit: कि clauses after opinion and belief verbs, hedging and stance expressions, and evidence markers used to support a claim.

Coming Into This Chapter

Learners arrive from Unit I with reflective stance markers such as मुझे लगता है (Chapter 1), reference tracking and honorific agreement (Chapter 2), reasoned explanation with क्योंकि and इसलिए (Chapters 1-4), and the impersonal/personal opinion contrast from Chapter 4.

What This Chapter Does

This chapter formalizes opinion language: it teaches कि clauses after verbs of opinion and belief, hedging with शायद and संभव है, contrast with हालाँकि, and evidence markers such as उदाहरण के लिए. It also introduces the claim-reason-example-conclusion paragraph structure used throughout Unit II.

Looking Forward

Chapter 7 builds on this chapter's evidence language to explain causes, results, and solutions to practical problems, moving from stating an opinion to organizing a full problem-solution explanation.

Pronunciation and Listening Focus

Intonation for stated opinion

When a stance expression such as मेरे विचार में opens a sentence, it typically carries a slight rise, followed by a small pause before the claim itself. Practice reading मेरे विचार में, स्कूलों में मोबाइल फ़ोन का इस्तेमाल सीमित होना चाहिए aloud with this pause after the opening phrase.

Intonation for hedged claims

शायद and संभव है कि are usually spoken with a softer, more level pitch than a confident factual claim. Contrast यह सच है (more level, more assertive) with शायद यह सच है (softer, more tentative).

Intonation for polite disagreement

Polite disagreement in Hindi often begins with a brief acknowledgment before the contrasting point, marked by a falling-then-rising contour on words like हालाँकि and फिर भी. Avoid a flat, abrupt delivery of मैं असहमत हूँ alone; pair it with a reason.

Schwa deletion in abstract nouns

Practice schwa deletion in this chapter's abstract vocabulary: विचार (vi-CHAAR, not vi-CHAA-ra), सबूत (sa-BOOT), and निष्कर्ष (nish-KARSH). Read each aloud in isolation, then inside a full sentence from the Model Dialogue below.

Listening for stance and support

While listening to the Model Dialogue and Listening Practice passage, listen specifically for (1) the stance expression that opens each opinion, (2) the reason or example that follows it, and (3) any hedge or concession the speaker uses.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter introduces two closely related systems: कि clauses after opinion and belief verbs, and hedging/contrast/evidence markers used to build a short argument. Both systems support the claim-reason-example-conclusion structure used throughout Unit II.

6.4 कि Clauses After Opinion and Belief Verbs

Form: A verb or expression of opinion, belief, or perception is followed by कि ("that"), which introduces a full clause containing the actual claim.

Language Building Block — Opinion + कि + Claim
मुझे लगता है कि छात्रों को स्कूल में मोबाइल फ़ोन कम इस्तेमाल करना चाहिए।
I feel that students should use mobile phones less in school.

Meaning: कि does not carry independent meaning here; it functions as a connector that links the opinion verb to the content of the opinion, similar to English "that" after "I think," "I believe," or "it seems."

Use: Learners already used मुझे लगता है कि and कहा जाता है कि in earlier chapters for reflection and impersonal statements (Chapters 1 and 4). This chapter expands the same कि-clause pattern to a wider set of opinion verbs, including मेरा मानना है कि, यह सच है कि, and संभव है कि.

Do not translate कि as a single fixed English word in every context; its function is structural, not lexical.

6.5 Hedging and Stance

Language Building Block — Hedged Claim
संभव है कि कुछ छात्रों के लिए मोबाइल फ़ोन पढ़ाई में मददगार भी हो।
It is possible that mobile phones are also helpful for some students' studies.

Form: शायद typically appears before the main verb or at the start of the clause; संभव है कि introduces a full कि clause, similar to opinion verbs above.

Meaning: Both expressions reduce the speaker's commitment to a claim, presenting it as likely rather than certain.

Use: Use hedging when evidence is limited, when acknowledging an opposing view, or when softening a strong claim in a mixed-opinion group. Overusing शायद in every sentence weakens an argument; reserve it for genuinely uncertain points.

6.6 Contrast and Concession

Language Building Block — Concession
यह सच है कि मोबाइल फ़ोन से कुछ फ़ायदे होते हैं, लेकिन स्कूल में इसका ज़्यादा इस्तेमाल ध्यान भटकाता है।
It's true that mobile phones have some benefits, but using them too much in school is distracting.

Form: यह सच है कि... acknowledges a point, and लेकिन, पर, हालाँकि, or फिर भी introduces the contrasting point.

Meaning: This structure grants a partial truth to an opposing view before stating the speaker's actual position, which is more persuasive and more polite than a direct denial.

Use: हालाँकि typically opens the concessive clause (हालाँकि कुछ फ़ायदे हैं, फिर भी...), while लेकिन/पर connect two independent clauses more directly. Both are appropriate in this chapter's controlled contexts.

6.7 Evidence Markers

Language Building Block — Claim + Evidence
मेरे विचार में, नियमों की ज़रूरत है। उदाहरण के लिए, मेरे स्कूल में फ़ोन बंद करने के बाद छात्रों के अंक बेहतर हुए।
In my view, rules are needed. For example, after phones were turned off at my school, students' grades improved.

Form: उदाहरण के लिए or the shorter जैसे introduces a specific supporting example after a general claim.

Meaning: Evidence markers connect a general opinion to a specific, concrete instance, making the argument easier to follow and evaluate.

Use: Every stated opinion in this chapter's tasks should be followed by at least one reason (क्योंकि) or one example (उदाहरण के लिए). An opinion without support remains incomplete at this level.

6.8 Paragraph Organization: Claim, Reason, Example, Conclusion

Organize a short spoken or written opinion using four parts:

  1. Claim — state the opinion with a stance expression: मेरे विचार में...
  2. Reason — explain why, using क्योंकि: ...क्योंकि इससे ध्यान भटकता है।
  3. Example — support the reason with a concrete case: उदाहरण के लिए,...
  4. Conclusion — close the argument: इसलिए, मुझे लगता है कि... or अंत में,...

Do not introduce advanced debate rhetoric, technical citation forms, or specialized argumentation vocabulary in this chapter; the four-part structure above is the complete Chapter 6 target.

Model Dialogue

Setting: At the conversation group's sixth meeting, Meera जी introduces a discussion prompt about smartphone use among students, and Priya, Kabir, and a new member, Aditya, share and respond to opinions.

मीरा जी आज का विषय है: क्या स्कूलों में छात्रों को मोबाइल फ़ोन इस्तेमाल करने देना चाहिए? आपकी क्या राय है? Today's topic is: should students be allowed to use mobile phones in schools? What is your opinion?
प्रिया मेरे विचार में, स्कूल में मोबाइल फ़ोन का इस्तेमाल सीमित होना चाहिए, क्योंकि इससे छात्रों का ध्यान पढ़ाई से भटकता है। In my view, mobile phone use should be limited in school, because it distracts students from their studies.
आदित्य यह सच है कि फ़ोन से ध्यान भटक सकता है, लेकिन मुझे लगता है कि फ़ोन पढ़ाई में भी मददगार हो सकता है। उदाहरण के लिए, हम कक्षा में शब्दकोश या इंटरनेट का इस्तेमाल कर सकते हैं। It's true that phones can be distracting, but I think phones can also be helpful for studying. For example, we can use a dictionary or the internet in class.
कबीर आदित्य, क्या तुम यह कह रहे हो कि फ़ोन पर कोई नियम नहीं होना चाहिए? Aditya, are you saying that there shouldn't be any rules about phones?
आदित्य नहीं, मेरा मतलब यह नहीं है। मैं कुछ हद तक प्रिया से सहमत हूँ — नियम ज़रूरी हैं। पर मेरे हिसाब से, फ़ोन पर पूरी तरह रोक लगाना सही समाधान नहीं है। No, that's not what I mean. I agree with Priya to some extent — rules are necessary. But in my view, banning phones completely isn't the right solution.
प्रिया हालाँकि फ़ोन के कुछ फ़ायदे हैं, फिर भी मुझे लगता है कि ज़्यादातर छात्र कक्षा में फ़ोन का सही इस्तेमाल नहीं करते। उदाहरण के लिए, मेरे स्कूल में फ़ोन बंद करने के बाद छात्रों के अंक बेहतर हुए। Although phones have some benefits, I still feel that most students don't use phones appropriately in class. For example, after phones were turned off at my school, students' grades improved.
कबीर शायद हर स्कूल में यह अलग होगा। मेरे स्कूल में तो फ़ोन का इस्तेमाल सिर्फ़ खास मौकों पर होता था, इसलिए यह समस्या नहीं थी। Maybe this will be different at every school. At my school, phones were only used on special occasions, so it wasn't a problem.
मीरा जी तो, अंत में, क्या हम कह सकते हैं कि नियम ज़रूरी हैं, लेकिन उनकी सीमा हर स्कूल पर निर्भर करती है? So, in conclusion, can we say that rules are necessary, but their limits depend on each school?
आदित्य जी हाँ, मेरे हिसाब से यह एक संतुलित निष्कर्ष है। Yes, I think that's a balanced conclusion.

समझ के प्रश्न — Comprehension questions:

  1. प्रिया की राय क्या है, और वह इसे किस उदाहरण से साबित करती है? (What is Priya's opinion, and what example does she use to support it?)
  2. आदित्य प्रिया से कहाँ सहमत है और कहाँ असहमत है? (Where does Aditya agree with Priya, and where does he disagree?)
  3. कबीर की राय बाक़ी दोनों से कैसे अलग है? (How is Kabir's opinion different from the other two?)

Writing Workshop

Write a short reasoned opinion paragraph, मेरी राय ("My Opinion"), on a public topic of your choice (for example, school phone rules, social media, public transportation, or another topic your instructor approves).

लेखन कार्य — Writing task: Write a connected 120-180 word paragraph in standard Devanagari that includes:

  1. A clear claim, opened with a stance expression such as मेरे विचार में or मुझे लगता है कि.
  2. At least one reason, introduced with क्योंकि.
  3. At least one concrete example, introduced with उदाहरण के लिए or जैसे.
  4. At least one hedge or concession, using शायद, संभव है कि, or हालाँकि...फिर भी.
  5. A conclusion, using अंत में or इसलिए, मुझे लगता है कि.

संपादन सूची — Editing checklist:

  • The claim, reason, example, and conclusion are all present and appear in a logical order.
  • कि is used correctly after opinion and belief verbs.
  • At least one hedge or concession softens an overly absolute claim.
  • The paragraph does not overuse शायद to the point of sounding uncertain about everything.
  • Gender, number, agreement, and oblique forms are applied correctly throughout.
  • Spelling, matras, conjuncts, word spacing, and dandas are correct throughout.

Speaking Practice

नियंत्रित अभ्यास — Controlled practice: राय और कारण

With a partner, take turns stating an opinion on a given topic (school phones, social media, public transportation) using a stance expression, followed immediately by one reason with क्योंकि.

संचार कार्य — Communicative task: सहमति और असहमति

After your partner states an opinion, respond with agreement, disagreement, or partial agreement, using at least one softening expression (शायद, कुछ हद तक, हालाँकि) and one follow-up question asking your partner to explain or give an example.

अकेले अभ्यास — Solo alternative

Record a one- to two-minute spoken opinion on a public topic, following the claim-reason-example-conclusion structure, and then listen back to check that each part is clearly present.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Softening disagreement

As the Model Dialogue shows, direct disagreement is often softened in many Hindi-speaking contexts through partial agreement (कुछ हद तक सहमत हूँ), concession (हालाँकि...फिर भी), or a shift toward shared ground (तो, अंत में, क्या हम कह सकते हैं कि...). This is not evasiveness; it reflects a common conversational strategy for maintaining relationships while still expressing a genuine view. Directness and bluntness are valued differently across relationships, settings, and individual speakers, so learners should adjust based on context rather than assuming one universal style.

Culture in Context — Evidence in everyday conversation

Ordinary conversation in Hindi, as in English, often supports opinions with personal examples (as Priya, Aditya, and the discussion-board writer do) rather than statistics or formal citations. This chapter's evidence markers are built for exactly this kind of everyday, example-based argument, not for academic-style sourcing, which is developed further in later volumes.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Stating an opinion without support

Error: Writing or saying only मुझे लगता है कि स्कूल में फ़ोन का इस्तेमाल ठीक नहीं है and stopping there, with no reason or example.

Correction: Always follow a claim with at least a reason: मुझे लगता है कि स्कूल में फ़ोन का इस्तेमाल ठीक नहीं है, क्योंकि इससे छात्रों का ध्यान भटकता है।

Common Mistake — Treating कि as optional

Error: Dropping कि after an opinion verb, producing an unclear or incomplete-sounding sentence such as मुझे लगता है, फ़ोन ठीक नहीं है without the connector in formal writing.

Correction: In connected writing and careful speech, include कि after opinion and belief verbs: मुझे लगता है कि फ़ोन ठीक नहीं है। (In very casual speech, omission is sometimes heard, but learners at this stage should use the full pattern.)

Common Mistake — Overusing शायद

Error: Hedging every single sentence with शायद, producing a paragraph that sounds unsure of its own main claim, such as शायद फ़ोन ठीक नहीं है। शायद इससे ध्यान भटकता है। शायद स्कूल में नियम होने चाहिए।

Correction: Reserve hedging for points that are genuinely uncertain or for acknowledging an opposing view, and state the main claim and reason with more confidence: मेरे विचार में, स्कूल में फ़ोन का इस्तेमाल सीमित होना चाहिए, क्योंकि इससे ध्यान भटकता है। शायद हर स्कूल के लिए यह अलग होगा, लेकिन...

Model Dialogue — Comprehension

  1. Priya believes mobile phone use should be limited in school because it distracts students; she supports this with the example that grades improved at her school after phones were turned off.
  2. Aditya agrees with Priya that rules are necessary, but disagrees that phones should be banned completely, arguing that phones can help with research (using a dictionary or the internet in class).
  3. Kabir's opinion differs because he notes that phone use varied by school (his school only allowed it on special occasions), suggesting that the right rule depends on context rather than one universal answer.

Listening

  • Sunita believes everyone should spend at least one day a week without a mobile phone.
  • She supports this with her own experience: after not using her phone for a day, she felt she thought more and slept better.
  • She uses शायद ("perhaps") to acknowledge that this might not be easy for everyone.
  • She concludes that technology is meant to help us, not to dominate us.

Reading — Comprehension

  1. The writer's main claim is that phone use should be limited for young children because they are still learning to manage their time; the example given is the writer's younger sister checking messages repeatedly while studying.
  2. The concession appears in: हालाँकि कुछ छात्र फ़ोन का ग़लत इस्तेमाल करते हैं, फिर भी बहुत-से छात्र फ़ोन का इस्तेमाल शोध और समय-सारणी बनाने के लिए करते हैं।

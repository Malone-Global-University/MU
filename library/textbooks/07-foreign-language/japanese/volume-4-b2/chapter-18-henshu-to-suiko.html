編集と推敲Editing and Revision

Drafts, feedback, register, concision, and cohesion · Speak and Write Japanese: Professional, Academic, and Cultural Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Give and receive written feedback on a draft using constructions such as 〜たほうがいい and 〜と分かりやすい.
  2. Identify and revise overly wordy passages to make writing more concise using 〜を省く and related revision vocabulary.
  3. Improve cohesion between sentences and paragraphs using topic chains, connective revision, and reference cleanup.
  4. Recognize and correct register inconsistency within a single piece of writing.
  5. Identify and correct plausible learner errors in particle choice, predicate form, and kanji/homophone conversion.
  6. Read a marked-up draft and explain what each revision accomplishes.
  7. Revise your own Chapter 17 research summary using a structured editing checklist.
  8. Discuss proposed revisions with a partner, negotiating which changes to accept.

Communication Scenario

Emma's editor at the magazine has returned her draft of the "くらしと専門知識" column about 田中さん's dialect-use research from Chapter 17, with comments in the margins. The draft is accurate, but the editor notes that some sentences repeat information, the register shifts unexpectedly in two places, and one paragraph is harder to follow than it needs to be. Before the piece can run, Emma must work through the editor's feedback, tighten the writing, and produce a clean revised draft — the same skill she will need for any piece of professional or academic Japanese writing going forward.

  1. The learner listens to an editor giving verbal feedback on a draft and notes the specific changes requested.
  2. The learner reads a marked-up draft with margin comments and identifies what kind of problem each comment addresses.
  3. The learner practices concision by shortening wordy sentences without losing meaning.
  4. The learner revises their own Chapter 17 research summary for register consistency, cohesion, and accuracy.
  5. The learner discusses proposed edits with a partner or instructor and decides which revisions to accept.

Script Strategy — Homophone traps in kanji conversion

推敲 is a first-mention term with furigana on its first appearance in this chapter and none afterward. It is worth learning as a fixed two-kanji word rather than by its individual readings, since 敲 rarely appears outside this compound. When typing, IME conversion for words in this chapter's list can easily produce a plausible-looking but incorrect homophone: しゅうせい can convert to 修正 (correction, what this chapter means) or to 終生 (one's whole life), and かんけつ can convert to 簡潔 (conciseness, what this chapter means) or to 完結 (conclusion, completion of a series). Neither error announces itself visually; both produce a grammatically valid sentence with the wrong meaning. Checking a conversion by its meaning in context, not just its shape on screen, is the core habit this chapter builds.

Then, for しゅうせい and かんけつ, write both the correct conversion for this chapter's meaning and the incorrect homophone described above, and write one sentence showing how context makes the correct choice clear.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Reading a marked-up draft aloud with revision-marker intonation

When reading a piece of writing aloud to discuss possible edits, speakers often use a slightly flatter, more deliberate phrase rhythm than in ordinary narration, pausing at clause boundaries to give a listener time to consider a proposed change. Practice reading the example sentences in the Grammar section aloud twice: once at an ordinary narrative pace, and once with the more deliberate, editorial pacing used when walking someone through a revision. Keep vowel length and moraic timing steady in both readings; only the pacing and pause placement should change. As in earlier chapters, treat any pitch-accent notation as a reference tool rather than a required marking.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter authorizes revision operations for nominalization, topic chains, particles, register, and sentence balance, building on the source-attribution, cautious-claim, and finding-report systems from Chapters 4, 16, and 17. No new clause type is introduced; the focus is using familiar structures to talk about and carry out revision.

1. Recommending a revision: 〜たほうがいい / 〜ないほうがいい

Purpose: to suggest a specific change to a draft, as feedback from one writer to another.

  • この文は長すぎるので、二つに分けたほうがいい。This sentence is too long, so it would be better to split it into two.〜たほうがいい — recommending a change
  • ここは敬語を使わないほうがいい。この段落は全体的にです・ます体だから。You shouldn't use keigo here — this paragraph is in です／ます style overall.〜ないほうがいい — recommending against a choice

2. Explaining why a revision helps: 〜と分かりやすい / 〜とつながりがよくなる

Purpose: to justify a proposed edit by naming its effect on the reader.

  • 結果を先に書くと、読者にとって分かりやすい。If you state the result first, it's easier for the reader to follow.〜と分かりやすい — effect on comprehension
  • この二つの文の間に「しかし」を入れると、つながりがよくなる。If you add "however" between these two sentences, the connection improves.〜とつながりがよくなる — effect on cohesion

3. Naming what to cut: 〜を省く / 重複を避ける

Purpose: to identify specific content that can be removed without losing meaning.

  • この部分は前の段落と重複しているので、省いてもいい。This part overlaps with the previous paragraph, so it's fine to cut it.省いてもいい — permission to cut
  • 同じ内容を二回説明しているので、重複を避けたほうが簡潔になる。You're explaining the same content twice, so avoiding the repetition would make it more concise.重複を避ける — naming the problem

4. Pointing out and confirming a fix: 〜を指摘する / 直しておきました

Purpose: to describe feedback that has been given, and to confirm a correction has already been made.

  • 編集者は、二か所の誤変換を指摘した。The editor pointed out two conversion errors.を指摘した — reporting feedback received
  • ご指摘の点は直しておきました。ご確認いただけますか。I've already fixed the point you raised. Could you take a look?直しておきました — confirming a completed fix, polite register
Language Focus — Feedback language versus register of the piece being edited

The language used about a piece of writing and the language used within that piece of writing can carry different registers at the same time. Emma and her editor may discuss the draft using ordinary polite conversation, complete with 〜たほうがいい and casual hedges, while the magazine column itself keeps a consistent, more neutral written style throughout. A common revision error is letting the conversational register of the feedback conversation leak into the piece being revised. Keeping these two registers mentally separate is itself a skill this chapter develops.

Grammar Practice

  1. Write a sentence recommending a change to a draft using 〜たほうがいい.
  2. Write a sentence justifying that recommendation using 〜と分かりやすい or 〜とつながりがよくなる.
  3. Write a sentence identifying repeated content using 重複 and 省く.
  4. Write a sentence confirming that a pointed-out issue has been fixed, using ご指摘 and 直しておきました.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Emma's editor, 山本さん, calls to walk through feedback on the draft from Chapter 17 before Emma revises it. Polite style throughout, appropriate to a professional working relationship.

山本下書き、拝見しました。全体的にとても分かりやすいんですが、三つほど気になった点があります。I've had a look at your draft. Overall it's very clear, but there were a few things I noticed.
エマはい、お願いします。Yes, please go ahead.
山本まず、二段落目と四段落目で、対象者の情報が重複しています。四段落目のほうを省いたほうがいいと思います。First, the information about the target population is repeated in the second and fourth paragraphs. I think you should cut the version in the fourth paragraph.
エマ分かりました。それから、二つ目は何でしょうか。Understood. What's the second point?
山本三段落目だけ、急に硬い文体になっています。他の段落はです・ます体なのに、そこだけ「〜である」になっているので、統一したほうがいいですね。Just the third paragraph suddenly shifts to a stiffer style. The other paragraphs are です／ます style, but only that part uses "〜である," so it would be better to make it consistent.
エマあ、確かにそうですね。すぐ直します。三つ目は何ですか。Ah, you're right. I'll fix that right away. What's the third point?
山本最後の文に誤変換があります。「限界」が「原界」になっていました。読者には分かりにくいと思います。There's a conversion error in the last sentence. "限界" came out as "原界." I don't think readers would follow it.
エマすみません、確認せずに変換してしまいました。ご指摘、ありがとうございます。全部直しておきます。I'm sorry, I converted it without checking. Thank you for pointing it out — I'll fix everything.

Emma notes three revisions: cut the duplicated target-population sentence in paragraph four, unify the third paragraph's style with です・ます, and correct the 限界 conversion error.

ないようりかい — Comprehension check: What three problems does 山本さん identify? Which paragraph has a duplication problem, and which paragraph has a register problem?

Writing Workshop

Revise your own Chapter 17 research-summary draft about 田中さん's dialect-use study, applying this chapter's editing systems.

  1. Check for duplication across sentences and paragraphs, and cut one redundant sentence using 重複 and 省く as your working vocabulary.
  2. Check register consistency throughout the piece, and correct any sentence that shifts unexpectedly between です・ます体 and である体.
  3. Check cohesion between at least two sentences, adding or adjusting a connective so the relationship between them is clearer.
  4. Check kanji conversions for at least three key terms, watching especially for homophone errors like the 限界／原界 pair from the Reading Practice passage.
  5. Write two or three margin-style comments of your own, in Japanese, explaining what you changed and why, using 〜たほうがいい, 〜と分かりやすい, or 指摘する.

チェックリスト — Editing checklist:

  • I have removed at least one instance of duplicated content.
  • I have made the writing style consistent throughout the piece.
  • I have improved the connection between at least two sentences.
  • I have checked kanji conversions for accuracy, not just visual plausibility.
  • I have written margin comments explaining my own revisions in Japanese.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Editorial feedback as a routine, ongoing conversation

In Japanese professional and academic writing settings, feedback on a draft is often delivered as a structured but conversational exchange rather than a one-time verdict, with the writer expected to respond to each point and confirm what has been changed, as Emma does at the end of the Model Dialogue. Explicitly acknowledging feedback with a phrase such as ご指摘、ありがとうございます before describing the fix is a common and expected part of this exchange, regardless of how minor the correction is.

Regional Note — Register consistency and regional or informal color in written work

The register-consistency skills in this chapter connect back to Chapter 14's discussion of regional and social variation. A general-audience magazine column normally keeps a single consistent written register throughout, even when quoting a speaker who used informal or regionally marked language in an interview; any such color from the original conversation is usually smoothed into the article's consistent style unless it is presented as a direct, clearly marked quotation.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Mixing です・ます体 and である体 within one piece

Error: Writing most of a summary in です・ます体 but slipping into である体 for a single sentence that feels more "academic," as in Comment 2's example above.

Correction: Choose one style for the whole piece and check every sentence against it during revision: この結果は非常に興味深いと言えるだろう。

Common Mistake — Trusting a kanji conversion because it looks plausible

Error: Accepting 原界 for intended 限界 because both are real-looking two-kanji words and the IME offered a confident-looking candidate.

Correction: Check every converted term against its intended meaning in context, not just against whether it looks like a real word: 対象者の数がまだ少ないという限界がある。

Common Mistake — Cutting content instead of cutting duplication

Error: Responding to a duplication comment by deleting a sentence that actually contains new information, rather than the sentence that repeats an earlier point.

Correction: Compare the two overlapping passages carefully and cut only the version that adds nothing new, keeping whichever version states the information most clearly in its context.

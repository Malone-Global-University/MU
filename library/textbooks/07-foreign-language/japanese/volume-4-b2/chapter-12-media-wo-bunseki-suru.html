メディアを分析するAnalyzing Media

Discourse, framing, and rhetoric · Speak and Write Japanese: Professional, Academic, and Cultural Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Read short news items, commentary, and online discussion excerpts and identify their basic framing choices.
  2. Recognize headline-style abbreviation and compression and expand a headline into a full sentence.
  3. Identify quotation framing and attribution patterns used to distinguish a source's words from a writer's own claims.
  4. Recognize stance markers that signal a writer's certainty, distance, or evaluation without stating it directly.
  5. Use 〜わけではない and 〜にすぎない to recognize and produce rhetorical contrast and qualification.
  6. Compare how two short pieces frame the same basic event differently.
  7. Discuss a media excerpt's framing choices in spoken Japanese with appropriate hedging.
  8. Write a short paragraph that reports information while clearly distinguishing source claims from the writer's own commentary.

Communication Scenario

Emma's reading circle from last month has decided to try something different: instead of a literary passage, this month each member brings a short news item or piece of online commentary about the same local story — a neighborhood shopping street facing declining foot traffic — and the group compares how differently each source frames it. One piece is a short newspaper-style report; another is a more opinionated online commentary; a third is a comment thread reacting to both. Before the meeting, Emma needs to read all three closely enough to notice not just what happened, but how each piece signals its stance: which claims are attributed to named sources, which are presented as the writer's own interpretation, and where a headline compresses information in a way that could be read more than one way. At the meeting, she will need to compare the three pieces aloud, and afterward she will write a short paragraph that reports the basic facts while keeping her own commentary clearly separate.

  1. The learner reads short media excerpts and identifies framing, attribution, and stance markers.
  2. The learner expands a compressed headline into a full, natural sentence.
  3. The learner listens to a short audio-style news item and identifies its main claims and their sources.
  4. The learner discusses how two or three sources frame the same event differently, using appropriate hedging.
  5. The learner writes a short paragraph that reports information while clearly separating source claims from personal commentary.

Script and Orthography Focus

Script Strategy — Reading compressed headlines

Headlines regularly omit the topic particle は and the object particle を, and frequently end on a noun or noun-derived form rather than a full predicate. For example, a headline such as 商店街、来客数減少 ("Shopping street, customer numbers decline") expands in a full sentence to 商店街の来客数が減少している。 Recognizing this pattern — noun phrase, comma or space, noun phrase — is more useful than trying to translate a headline word for word.

Common abbreviations to recognize on sight include shortened organization names and compound terms formed from the first kanji of longer phrases. Do not guess an abbreviation's meaning from its kanji alone; check it against context or a reliable source, since some abbreviations are ambiguous outside their originating domain.

れんしゅう — Practice: Expand the headline 駅前、新店続々 into a complete, natural sentence with particles and a full predicate.

Pronunciation and Listening Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Broadcast-style delivery and evaluative emphasis

Broadcast news delivery uses a measured, evenly paced rhythm with clear phrase-final pitch drops that mark the end of each reported claim, which helps listeners track where one piece of information ends and the next begins. Commentary and podcast-style speech, by contrast, often adds emphatic stress on evaluative words (such as 明らかに "clearly" or 残念ながら "regrettably") to signal the speaker's own stance. This chapter introduces listening awareness of this contrast; production is not assessed. Continue maintaining mora timing, vowel length, moraic ん, and phrase-level intonation as consolidated in earlier chapters.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter authorizes stance markers, quotation framing, rhetorical contrast, 〜わけではない, and 〜にすぎない for controlled recognition and limited guided production. These are new systems for this volume and should be treated as introduced (I), not consolidated. Systems not listed here remain receptive or formulaic only, following the Part III matrices.

1. Stance markers

Purpose: to recognize words and phrases that signal a writer's certainty, distance, or evaluation of a claim, separate from the claim's content itself.

  • 明らかに、来客数は減っている。Customer numbers are clearly declining.明らかに signals writer confidence in the claim
  • 残念ながら、対策はまだ十分ではない。Unfortunately, the measures are still not sufficient.残念ながら signals evaluative stance, not neutral report

2. Quotation framing and attribution

Purpose: to recognize how a writer marks a claim as belonging to a named source rather than to the writer, using patterns built on earlier attribution vocabulary such as によれば and とされる.

  • 商店会長は「対策を急ぎたい」と語った。The shopping association chairperson said, "We want to move quickly on countermeasures."「」+ と語った — direct quotation with named attribution
  • 一部の住民は、再開発に反対しているという。Some residents are said to oppose the redevelopment.〜という — reported claim, source less specifically named

3. Rhetorical contrast: 〜わけではない

Purpose: to soften or qualify a claim by denying a stronger implication the reader might draw, without denying the claim itself.

  • すべての店が困っているわけではない。It's not that every shop is struggling. (Some are; the claim is qualified, not the general trend denied.)
  • 反対意見がないわけではないが、賛成の声のほうが多い。It's not that there's no opposing view, but supportive voices are more numerous.

4. Rhetorical contrast: 〜にすぎない

Purpose: to downplay a claim's significance, framing it as "merely" or "no more than" something smaller than it might appear.

  • それは一つの意見にすぎない。That is merely one opinion. (Not the full picture.)
  • 今回の増加は、一時的な現象にすぎないと考えられる。This increase is thought to be nothing more than a temporary phenomenon.
Language Focus — Framing vs. fact

A single event can be reported with very different framing depending on word choice, sentence order, quotation selection, and stance markers, without any individual sentence being factually false. When comparing sources, distinguish claims that are directly attributed to a named source, claims presented as general reporting, and claims that are clearly the writer's own commentary or evaluation. Use 〜わけではない and 〜にすぎない to describe how a source qualifies or downplays a claim, rather than only describing what the source states outright.

Grammar Practice

  1. In それは一つの意見にすぎない。, what is the writer implying about the importance or scope of "that opinion"?
  2. Rewrite すべての店が困っているわけではない。 as a plain sentence that states only what is being confirmed, without the qualifying structure.
  3. Find one instance of attributed quotation and one instance of unattributed writer commentary in the Model Passage below, and identify which is which.

Model Passage

商店街、来客数減少 — A short original news-style item written for this course

＜見出し＞商店街、来客数減少　対策急務か

市内中心部の商店街で、来客数の減少が続いている。市の調査によれば、今年度の来客数は五年前と比べて約三割減少したという。

商店会長の田中たなかさんは、「対策を急ぎたい」と語った。ただし、すべての店が困っているわけではない。一部の飲食店は、むしろ来客数を伸ばしているという。

市の担当者は、今回の減少について「複数の要因が重なった結果であり、単純な一つの原因にすぎないとは考えていない」と説明している。

This passage was written for instructional purposes and is not an excerpt from a published news source. It models the systems introduced in this chapter: a compressed headline (商店街、来客数減少　対策急務か), attribution (市の調査によれば〜という, 「対策を急ぎたい」と語った), and rhetorical qualification (〜わけではない, 〜にすぎない).

ないようりかい — Comprehension questions:

  1. What is the headline's compressed claim, and how would you expand it into a full sentence?
  2. Whose words are directly quoted in this passage, and whose words are only paraphrased or reported?
  3. What does the mention of some restaurants "increasing" customer numbers do to the passage's overall framing?

Writing Workshop

In this workshop you will write a short reporting paragraph (roughly six to ten sentences) about a real or invented local topic, clearly separating attributed source claims from your own commentary.

  1. Choose a topic and at least one source — a person, organization, or survey whose claim you can attribute.
  2. Draft a compressed headline for your topic, then write it out as a full sentence beneath it.
  3. Write at least one attributed claim, using によれば, と語った, or という.
  4. Add one rhetorical qualification, using 〜わけではない or 〜にすぎない, to qualify or downplay a claim naturally.
  5. Add one clearly marked sentence of your own commentary, distinguished from the attributed claims by its lack of a source marker.
  6. Audit your paragraph for kanji accuracy, particle accuracy, and a clear line between attributed and unattributed content.

チェックリスト — Editing checklist:

  • My paragraph includes a compressed headline and its full-sentence expansion.
  • I have included at least one clearly attributed claim.
  • I have included at least one rhetorical qualification using わけではない or にすぎない.
  • My own commentary is clearly distinguishable from attributed claims.
  • My paragraph does not misrepresent any claim as more certain than the source states.
  • I have checked kanji, particles, and okurigana for accuracy.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — News framing and reader expectations

Japanese-language news outlets, commentary sites, and online discussion spaces vary widely in how directly they state an editorial position, and readers commonly compare multiple sources rather than relying on a single outlet. Distinguishing a news report from an opinion piece, and a named quotation from general reporting, is a skill readers actively practice rather than something guaranteed by the format of a publication alone. This chapter's habit of separating attributed claims from commentary reflects a broadly useful reading practice rather than a claim about any one publication's reliability.

Regional Note — Framing varies by outlet, platform, and audience

A national newspaper, a local community bulletin, a podcast, and a social media comment thread will each frame the same event differently, with different levels of formality, different assumed audience knowledge, and different conventions for marking opinion. This chapter introduces general framing and attribution patterns; recognizing how a specific outlet or platform typically signals its stance takes continued exposure to real examples over time.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Treating a headline as a complete grammatical sentence

Error: Reading 商店街、来客数減少 as if it were already a full sentence and trying to translate it word for word without supplying the missing particles or predicate.

Correction: Recognize the compressed noun-phrase pattern and expand it mentally into a full sentence, such as 商店街の来客数が減少している。, before interpreting its meaning.

Common Mistake — Confusing an attributed claim with the writer's own assertion

Error: Reporting 市の担当者は「複数の要因が重なった結果」と説明している。 as if the passage itself, rather than the named official, were making this claim.

Correction: State clearly who the claim belongs to: 市の担当者によれば、複数の要因が重なった結果だという。, keeping the attribution visible.

Common Mistake — Misreading わけではない as a full denial

Error: Interpreting すべての店が困っているわけではない。 as meaning "no shops are struggling."

Correction: Recognize that わけではない denies only the stronger claim ("all shops"), not the underlying situation; some shops may still be struggling, just not all of them.

Grammar Practice

Model answers: (1) The writer is implying that "that opinion" should not be taken as representative of a broader consensus or as the full picture — it is only one voice among possibly many. (2) A plain rewrite confirming only what is stated: 一部の店は困っていない。 ("Some shops are not struggling.") — this removes the qualifying structure but keeps only what is actually confirmed. (3) The attributed quotation is 「対策を急ぎたい」と語った (attributed to 田中さん by name); an example of writer-level reporting without a named individual source is 今回の減少について「複数の要因が重なった結果であり」と説明している, attributed more generally to "the city's representative" (市の担当者) rather than to a specific individual quote outside quotation marks.

Model Passage — Comprehension

  1. The headline's compressed claim is that the shopping street's customer numbers are declining and that countermeasures may be urgently needed; expanded: 商店街の来客数が減少しており、対策が急務かもしれない。
  2. 田中さん's words are directly quoted (「対策を急ぎたい」と語った); the city's survey findings and the restaurant trend are reported but not given as a direct quotation.
  3. Mentioning that some restaurants are increasing their customer numbers complicates the framing, showing the situation is not uniformly negative across all shop types.

Listening Practice — Model summary

The phrase 市の担当者によれば and the sentence-final という mark the claim as attributed to city officials rather than stated as established fact by the passage itself; the city plans to discuss countermeasures with the shopping association next month; the passage reports that officials attribute the customer decline to multiple factors and that a meeting with the shopping association is planned.

Reading Practice — Comprehension

  1. This passage is closer to commentary; it includes evaluative language and a first-person opinion (〜のではないか, 〜と思う) rather than only neutral reporting.
  2. 商店街全体が衰退しているわけではない。 qualifies the stronger claim that the entire shopping street is declining, rather than denying that any decline exists.
  3. ただ、地元の声に耳を傾けることが、何よりも重要だと思う。 expresses the writer's own opinion, marked by と思う.
  4. The Model Passage presents a more balanced, source-attributed account with a named quotation, while this passage leans more openly into the writer's own evaluative stance and direct opinion.

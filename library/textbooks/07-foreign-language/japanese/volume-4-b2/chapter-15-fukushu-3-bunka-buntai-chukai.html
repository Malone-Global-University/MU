Style, media, mediation, and variation · Speak and Write Japanese: Professional, Academic, and Cultural Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Read a short literary-style passage and identify features of narrative voice, ellipsis, and sentence-final style, as developed in Chapter 11.
  2. Analyze a short media excerpt for stance, framing, and rhetorical contrast, as developed in Chapter 12.
  3. Summarize and adapt a passage for a different audience while maintaining register and terminology consistency, as developed in Chapter 13.
  4. Name and contrast a regional, heritage, or youth variety with standard Japanese, and describe a speaker's style-shifting, as developed in Chapter 14.
  5. Move fluidly among these four domains within a single integrated task, choosing the language systems appropriate to each domain.
  6. Complete a listening task, a reading task, a writing task, and a spoken-interaction task that together demonstrate cumulative Unit III control.
  7. Identify which of your own productive skills from Unit III are strongest and which need continued attention before Unit IV.

Communication Scenario

A small community magazine that Emma has been reading throughout Unit III is preparing a special issue on "voices of the neighborhood," and the editor — a friend of Kenta's — has asked Emma to help review the issue before publication, since she has been following the magazine's short stories, media commentary, and interviews for several weeks. The issue includes a short literary vignette, a media commentary piece about a local festival, an interview transcript that needs a short English-language summary for the magazine's international readers, and a profile revisiting Rina and Kenta's earlier conversations about regional and heritage Japanese. Emma's task is to read each piece carefully, discuss it with the editor, mediate one section for an audience that does not read Japanese, and write a short reflective note connecting the four pieces to what she has learned this unit.

  1. The learner reads and interprets a short literary-style passage, identifying narrative voice and style.
  2. The learner analyzes a short media excerpt for stance and framing.
  3. The learner mediates part of an interview for a non-Japanese-reading audience.
  4. The learner discusses regional and heritage variation in the context of a community profile.
  5. The learner writes a short reflective note connecting all four domains and completes a listening task drawn from the same materials.

Pronunciation and Listening Checkpoint

Pronunciation Focus — Prosody across four communicative modes

This chapter reviews how phrase-level pitch and rhythm shift across communicative modes established in Unit III: a slower, more deliberate rhythm for literary narration; a brisker, more assertive rhythm for media commentary; a neutral, careful rhythm for mediation and summary; and, where a labeled regional feature appears, a pitch pattern that may depart from the Tokyo-area reference model without being an error. Continue maintaining consolidated mora timing, vowel length, moraic ん, and gemination as the stable foundation beneath all four modes.

Grammar and Sentence Pattern Review

This chapter assesses stylistic interpretation, media framing, mediation, and regional and register awareness together. No new grammar is introduced. The four patterns below each represent one chapter's central system; review each before attempting the Integrated Model Passage and its tasks.

1. Narrative style and ellipsis (Chapter 11 review)

Purpose: to recognize how a narrator's voice, omitted subjects, and sentence-final choices shape literary tone.

  • あの日のことは、今もよく覚えている。Even now, I remember that day well.omitted subject + sentence-final reflection

2. Stance and rhetorical contrast (Chapter 12 review)

Purpose: to identify a writer's stance without assuming the writer's opinion is neutral fact.

  • 今回の変更は、便利になったとは言えない。This change cannot really be called an improvement.〜とは言えない — stance marking through negated claim

3. Paraphrase and audience adaptation (Chapter 13 review)

Purpose: to restate content for a different audience while preserving meaning and register.

  • つまり、参加者の多くが今後も続けてほしいと考えているということです。In short, this means that most participants would like the event to continue.つまり〜ということです — paraphrase frame

4. Naming variation and style-shifting (Chapter 14 review)

Purpose: to name a variety, give its standard equivalent, and describe a speaker moving between forms.

  • これは方言で、標準語では〜に当たります。状況によって言葉遣いを使い分ける人も多いです。This is dialect; in standard Japanese it corresponds to ___. Many people vary their way of speaking depending on the situation.
Language Focus — Reading for genre before reading for content

A skilled reader identifies what kind of text they are reading — literary, journalistic, mediating, or descriptive of variation — before analyzing its content in detail, because each genre uses grammar, vocabulary, and stance differently. This chapter's Integrated Model Passage asks you to apply this genre awareness across four short sections in sequence, the same skill required in the Practical Performance Tasks of Chapters 11 through 14.

Integrated Model Passage

近所の声 — "Voices of the Neighborhood," written for this course

①短編（文学的文体）
商店街の灯りが一つずつ消えていく。あの店も、この店も、もう長くはないのだろう。子どものころ、ここで何を買ったのか、もう思い出せない。

②コメント（メディア）
商店街の閉店が続いていることについて、「時代の流れだから仕方ない」という論調が目立つが、それだけで済ませてよい問題だとは言えない。地域とのつながりをどう残すかという視点も必要だろう。

③インタビュー抜粋（仲介対象）
記者「商店街について、どうお考えですか。」
住民「そうですね……昔からの店がなくなるのは寂しいですが、新しい店も少しずつ増えていますし、悪いことばかりではないと思います。」

④プロフィール（地域と言語変化）
健太さんは、この商店街の近くで育った。〔関西方言〕「昔はもっと賑やかやったなあ。」〔標準語では「昔はもっと賑やかだったなあ」に当たる〕健太さんは、家族と話すときと取材で話すときとで、言葉遣いを使い分けている。

This passage was written for instructional purposes and integrates the four genres of Chapters 11–14. It is not an excerpt from a published source.

ないようりかい — Comprehension questions:

  4. In section ④, what does the bracketed label tell you, and how does Kenta's own behavior illustrate 使い分ける?

Speaking Assessment

コントロールされた練習 — Controlled practice: Four-genre sentence starters

  1. ＿＿＿＿＿のことは、今も＿＿＿＿＿。 (a literary-style reflective sentence)
  2. ＿＿＿＿＿については、＿＿＿＿＿とは言えない。 (a media-style stance sentence)
  3. つまり、＿＿＿＿＿ということです。 (a mediation-style summary sentence)
  4. これは＿＿＿＿＿で、標準語では＿＿＿＿＿に当たります。 (a variation-naming sentence)

Writing Assessment

In this assessment task you will write a short reflective note (roughly eight to twelve sentences, in standard Japanese) connecting all four sections of the Integrated Model Passage, as Emma would for the magazine editor.

  1. Comment on section ① using at least one observation about narrative voice or tone.
  2. Comment on section ② using at least one stance-marking pattern such as 〜とは言えない.
  3. Comment on section ③ using a paraphrase frame such as つまり〜ということです to summarize the resident's view.

Culture and Communication Review

Culture in Context — Community media as a meeting point of genres

Community magazines, neighborhood newsletters, and local online publications in Japan often combine several genres in a single issue: personal reflection, opinion commentary, interview transcripts, and profiles of community members, much like the fictional "近所の声" issue in this chapter. Reading such publications well requires recognizing which genre a given section belongs to, since the same neighborhood topic — a closing shop, a changing street — can be treated very differently depending on whether the writer is narrating, arguing, reporting, or profiling.

Regional Note — Revisiting variation without ranking it

As in Chapter 14, this chapter's treatment of Kenta's Kansai-dialect line is a labeled example, not a claim that all Kansai speakers talk this way in every setting. When reviewing your own writing and speaking for this chapter's tasks, continue describing regional, heritage, and youth Japanese as different, legitimate forms shaped by their communities, alongside — not beneath — the standard-Japanese productive model of this course.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Treating every section of a mixed-genre text the same way

Error: Reading a literary vignette, a media commentary, an interview transcript, and a variation profile with the same expectations, missing that each uses stance, register, and grammar differently.

Correction: Identify the genre of each section first, then apply the reading strategy suited to it: この部分は文学的な文体で、あの部分は意見を述べる記事です。

Common Mistake — Mediating word for word instead of by meaning

Error: Translating the resident's hesitant, informal interview answer in section ③ into an equally hesitant, disorganized English sentence, losing the overall point for an international reader.

Correction: Prioritize the resident's overall meaning and stance in a clear English summary, as practiced in Chapter 13: つまり、寂しさはあるが、悪いことばかりではないという意見です。

Common Mistake — Presenting a writer's stance as neutral fact

Error: Summarizing section ② as simply stating "what happened," without noting that the writer is arguing against a particular framing.

Correction: Name the stance explicitly when summarizing opinion writing: この記事の書き手は、「仕方ない」という考え方に対して批判的な立場を取っている。

Listening Assessment — Model summary

The labeled expression 昔はもっと店が多かってん is Kansai dialect, corresponding to standard 昔はもっと店が多かったんだ ("there used to be more shops"); Kenta immediately restates the same idea in standard Japanese and apologizes, illustrating conscious style-shifting; the exchange shows that speakers may shift varieties within a single sentence and self-correct depending on their sense of the listener or setting.

会議と交渉Meetings and Negotiation

Turn-taking, proposals, and compromise · Speak and Write Japanese: Professional, Academic, and Cultural Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Understand and produce the basic structure of a Japanese business meeting, including opening the meeting, working through an agenda, and closing with a summary of decisions.
  2. Make a proposal using conditional and hedged proposal language such as 〜たらどうでしょうか and 〜のはいかがでしょうか.
  3. Raise an objection or express disagreement indirectly, using softening expressions rather than blunt refusal.
  4. Offer and accept a compromise, using language that acknowledges another person's position before proposing an adjustment.
  5. Manage turn-taking in a meeting: signal that you would like to speak, yield the floor, and invite a quiet participant to contribute.
  6. Summarize a decision or set of decisions clearly at the close of a meeting, using appropriate keigo carried over from Chapter 6.
  7. Read and interpret simple meeting minutes (議事録) and identify the agenda items, decisions, and follow-up actions they record.
  8. Participate in a simulated meeting that includes at least one proposal, one objection, and one negotiated compromise.

Communication Scenario

Emma's internship has begun, and she has been invited to sit in on a planning meeting for the organization's autumn recycling awareness event. The meeting includes 田中さん (her supervisor), 佐藤さん (a colleague who tends to speak quickly and interrupt), and 鈴木さん (a quieter colleague whose opinion is rarely asked for directly). The agenda includes choosing a date, assigning responsibilities, and agreeing on a budget. Emma has been asked to take notes and, if she has a relevant idea from her research, to propose it politely at the appropriate moment. During the meeting, 佐藤さん and 田中さん disagree gently about the budget, and the group must reach a compromise. This chapter teaches you the tools Emma needs: proposing an idea without sounding pushy, disagreeing without sounding rude, managing turns in a group conversation, and summarizing what the group has decided.

  1. The learner follows the structure of a typical planning meeting from opening to close.
  2. The learner makes a hedged proposal and responds to another person's proposal.
  3. The learner expresses disagreement indirectly and works toward a compromise.
  4. The learner manages turn-taking, including yielding the floor and drawing out a quiet participant.
  5. The learner reads simple meeting minutes and writes a short summary of decisions and follow-up actions.

Script Strategy — Reading simple meeting minutes

Japanese meeting minutes (議事録) typically list, in order: the date and attendees, each agenda item (議題) with a brief note on the discussion, the decision reached (決定事項), and follow-up actions with the person responsible (担当). This chapter's Reading Practice section presents minutes in this format. Furigana appears only on 議題, 提案, 調整, and 決定 the first time each appears, matching this volume's exceptional-furigana policy.

Continue using Arabic numerals for dates, times, and budget figures, as in earlier chapters.

Pronunciation and Listening Focus

Pronunciation Focus — The prosody of hedged proposals and soft disagreement

Proposals and disagreements in a Japanese meeting are often produced with a rising, trailing-off intonation on phrases such as 〜と思うのですが… or 〜かもしれませんが…, leaving space for the other person to respond rather than stating an opinion as a flat conclusion. In this chapter's Listening Practice, listen for this trailing intonation as one signal that a speaker is offering an idea for discussion rather than announcing a final decision.

Continue maintaining mora timing, vowel length, moraic ん, and phrase-level intonation as in previous chapters. No new pitch-accent notation is introduced in this chapter.

Grammar: Meeting and Negotiation Language

This chapter activates proposal language, conditional proposals, indirect disagreement, and summarizing decisions for productive use, building on the operational keigo introduced in Chapter 6.

Making a proposal with conditional softening

Purpose: to introduce an idea for the group's consideration without presenting it as a demand or a finished decision.

Building Block — [た-form]＋らどうでしょうか　／　[noun／の]＋はいかがでしょうか
来月の第一週にしたらどうでしょうか。／　このイベントの日程を来月にするのはいかがでしょうか。
Both patterns turn a plain suggestion into a question that invites discussion rather than announcing a decision. 〜たらどうでしょうか attaches to a た-form and is slightly more direct; 〜はいかがでしょうか attaches to a noun phrase (often with の nominalizing a clause) and is somewhat more formal and indirect.
予算については、二つの案を比べてみるのはいかがでしょうか。
Regarding the budget, what would you think of comparing two options?

Disagreeing indirectly

Purpose: to express a differing opinion or concern without a blunt refusal, preserving the relationship and keeping the discussion open.

Building Block — [opinion]＋と思うのですが、[concern]
それも良い案だと思うのですが、予算が少し心配です。
Indirect disagreement in Japanese meetings often begins by acknowledging the value of the other person's proposal before introducing a concern, frequently using 〜と思うのですが, 〜かもしれませんが, or 〜のは分かるのですが to soften the transition. A flat 反対です ("I object") is grammatically correct but often sounds abrupt in this context; softened framing is the more natural default.
その日程も考えられると思うのですが、担当者の都合が合うかどうか確認が必要かもしれません。
I think that schedule could work, but we may need to confirm whether it fits the person in charge's availability.

Offering and accepting a compromise

Purpose: to move from two competing positions toward an adjusted, mutually acceptable outcome.

Building Block — [A]も[B]も＋考慮して　／　[proposal]＋で調整する
予算のことも準備の時間のことも考慮して、少し規模を小さくするのはいかがでしょうか。
Compromise language typically names both concerns before proposing an adjustment, often with 考慮する ("to take into consideration") or 調整する ("to adjust, coordinate"). Accepting a compromise commonly uses その方向で調整しましょう ("let's coordinate/adjust in that direction") rather than a simple agreement, since it signals a jointly shaped outcome rather than one side simply winning.
では、規模を小さくする方向で調整しましょう。
Then let's move forward by adjusting things toward a smaller scale.

Managing turns and summarizing decisions

Language Focus — Turn-taking and closing a meeting

To signal a wish to speak, learners can use a brief attention-getter such as すみません、よろしいでしょうか ("Excuse me, may I?") before stating an opinion. To yield the floor, a speaker may trail off with 〜と思うのですが、いかがでしょうか, explicitly inviting a response. To draw out a quieter participant, a chair might ask 鈴木さんはどう思われますか ("What do you think, Suzuki-san?"), using a respectful form for the other person's opinion. At the close of a meeting, decisions are typically summarized with 〜ということで決定いたします ("we will finalize it as ___"), followed by a statement of who is responsible for each follow-up action.

Model Dialogue

Setting: A planning meeting for the autumn recycling awareness event. 田中さん chairs the meeting; 佐藤さん and 鈴木さん are colleagues; エマ is the intern.

田中さん それでは、本日の議題は日程と予算の二つです。まず日程からお願いします。 Well then, today's agenda has two items: the schedule and the budget. Let's start with the schedule.
佐藤さん 来月の第一週にしたらどうでしょうか。会場も空いていますし。 What would you think of making it the first week of next month? The venue is also available then.
エマ すみません、よろしいでしょうか。その日程も良いと思うのですが、担当者の都合が合うかどうか、まだ確認していないかもしれません。 Excuse me, may I say something? I think that schedule is fine too, but we may not have confirmed yet whether it fits the person in charge's availability.
田中さん 確かにそうですね。では、確認してから決定いたしましょう。次に、予算について、鈴木さんはどう思われますか。 That's true. Then let's confirm before finalizing. Next, regarding the budget, what do you think, Suzuki-san?
鈴木さん 予算のことも準備の時間のことも考慮すると、規模を少し小さくするのはいかがでしょうか。 Considering both the budget and the preparation time, what would you think of making the scale a bit smaller?
佐藤さん その案も分かるのですが、規模を小さくすると、参加者が減るかもしれません。 I understand that idea too, but if we make the scale smaller, participants might decrease.
田中さん では、規模を少しだけ小さくする方向で調整しましょう。予算については、そういうことで決定いたします。エマさん、議事録をお願いできますか。 Then let's coordinate toward making the scale only slightly smaller. Regarding the budget, we'll finalize it that way. Emma, could I ask you to handle the minutes?
エマ 承知いたしました。 Understood.

In a group of three or four, hold a short meeting to plan a simple event. One participant chairs the meeting, opens the agenda, and closes with a summary of decisions; the others make at least one hedged proposal, one indirect disagreement, and one accepted compromise. Rotate roles if time allows.

ひとりでもできる練習 — Solo practice: Recorded proposal and compromise

Record a 30–45 second turn as if speaking in a meeting: make a proposal using 〜たらどうでしょうか or 〜はいかがでしょうか, respond to an imagined objection with indirect disagreement, and close by proposing a compromise. Listen back and check that no statement sounds like a blunt refusal or a flat command.

Writing Workshop

In this workshop you will write a short set of meeting minutes recording a proposal, a disagreement or concern, a compromise, and a follow-up assignment, following the minutes layout modeled above.

  1. Record the date and attendees at the top of the minutes, following the modeled layout.
  2. State the first agenda item and summarize the proposal made for it, using 提案 and related vocabulary.
  3. State the second agenda item and summarize any concern or objection raised, using softened, indirect framing.
  4. Record the compromise or decision reached, using 調整する or 決定する as appropriate.
  5. List follow-up responsibilities, naming who is responsible for each task before the next meeting.
  6. Audit your minutes for accurate vocabulary, correct kanji, consistent institutional register, and clear identification of each agenda item's outcome.

チェックリスト — Editing checklist:

  • My minutes record the date and attendees at the start.
  • Each agenda item is clearly labeled and summarized.
  • I have recorded at least one proposal, one concern or disagreement, and one compromise or decision.
  • Follow-up responsibilities are listed with the person responsible for each.
  • I have used 提案する, 調整する, and 決定する accurately.
  • I have checked kanji, particles, predicate form, and register consistency throughout.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Indirectness as a negotiation tool, not evasiveness

The softened, indirect disagreement patterns in this chapter are sometimes misread by learners as vagueness or an unwillingness to state an opinion. In many Japanese institutional settings, this indirectness functions instead as a negotiation tool: it keeps multiple options open, protects the relationship between speakers, and allows a group to move toward consensus without anyone being forced into a public loss of face. Directness and indirectness both exist in Japanese communication; the chapter's patterns reflect one common professional convention, not a claim that all Japanese speakers avoid disagreement.

Regional Note — Meeting conventions vary by organization

The specific rhythm of a meeting — how formally an agenda is announced, how strictly turns are managed, and how minutes are distributed — can vary by industry, company size, and individual organizational culture, and some workplaces have moved toward less formal meeting styles. The patterns in this chapter are broadly usable defaults for general institutional meetings, not the only acceptable approach.

Language Note — Proposal and disagreement language build on earlier systems

The conditional forms underlying 〜たらどうでしょうか and the nominalizing の in 〜のはいかがでしょうか were introduced in earlier volumes; this chapter recombines them with the keigo from Chapter 6 to create negotiation-specific patterns. If a pattern feels unfamiliar, it may help to first identify the underlying conditional or nominalized clause, then add the negotiation framing on top.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Using a flat 反対です where softened disagreement is expected

Error: その案に反対です。 (a flat, unsoftened objection in an institutional meeting)

Correction: その案も良いと思うのですが、少し心配な点があります。 — acknowledge the proposal's value before introducing a concern, unless the setting specifically calls for direct disagreement.

Common Mistake — Announcing a decision as a personal command

Error: 来月にします。 used by a participant who is not the meeting chair, presented as a unilateral decision.

Correction: 来月にしたらどうでしょうか。 — frame the idea as a proposal for the group, and reserve 〜ということで決定いたします-style closing statements for the person actually finalizing the decision, typically the chair.

Common Mistake — Interrupting without a turn-taking signal

Error: Speaking over another participant without any attention-getting phrase.

Correction: すみません、よろしいでしょうか。 — use a brief signal to request the floor before introducing a new point, especially in a formal meeting.

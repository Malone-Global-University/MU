公式文書と通知Official Documents and Notices

Formal written comprehension and production · Speak and Write Japanese: Professional, Academic, and Cultural Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Read a public notice, policy statement, or set of guidelines and identify its purpose, scope, conditions, and required actions.
  2. Recognize the written-style patterns 〜にあたり, 〜ものとする, and 〜こと in official documents and understand the function each performs.
  3. Distinguish formal written style from the spoken keigo introduced in Chapter 6, and explain why the two differ in a notice, application, or set of rules.
  4. Read a simple application form and identify what information it requests and how it should be completed.
  5. Interpret the structure of a numbered set of guidelines or rules, including headings, numbered items, and the closing note or contact information.
  6. Write a short official-style notice or set of guidelines using appropriate formal nominalization and layout conventions.
  7. Ask a colleague or institution a clarifying question about a notice or application in appropriately formal spoken Japanese.
  8. Complete a task that combines reading an official document with producing a related written or spoken response.

Communication Scenario

Following the planning meeting in Chapter 7, Emma's organization has decided to move forward with the autumn recycling awareness event. Before the event can proceed, the office must issue an internal notice announcing the date and asking staff to submit a simple participation form, and Emma has been asked to help post an updated set of building guidelines for volunteers who will be staffing the event. She also needs to read the organization's existing notice board to understand how such documents are normally written, since her own drafts will be reviewed by 田中さん before posting. This chapter gives Emma the tools she needs: reading and writing formal notices, understanding regulation-style language such as 〜ものとする and 〜こと, completing and explaining a simple application form, and asking clarifying questions about official documents without sounding uncertain or careless.

  1. The learner reads an internal notice and identifies its purpose, date, and required action.
  2. The learner reads a short set of numbered guidelines and explains what each rule requires.
  3. The learner reads and completes a simple application form.
  4. The learner writes a short notice or set of guidelines using this chapter's formal written patterns.
  5. The learner asks a clarifying question about a document in appropriately formal spoken Japanese.

Script Strategy — Reading dense official layout

Official notices and guidelines are typically denser in kanji than conversational writing and rely heavily on layout to organize meaning: a title, a short statement of purpose, numbered items, and a closing line with contact information or a deadline. Learners should scan this structure first — title, numbered items, closing line — before reading every sentence closely, since the layout itself often answers "what is this document for" and "what do I need to do." Furigana appears only on 通知, 方針, 規則, and 申請 the first time each appears, matching this volume's exceptional-furigana policy.

Numbered items in official documents commonly use full-width numerals with a period, such as 一、 or １．, depending on the organization's house style; this chapter uses Arabic numerals with a period for consistency with earlier chapters' numbering conventions.

れんしゅう — Practice: Before reading the guidelines passage in Reading Practice, scan only the title and the numbered headings and predict, in English, what topic each numbered item is likely to cover.

Pronunciation and Listening Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Reading a formal notice aloud

When a formal written notice is read aloud, such as at the start of a meeting or over an office announcement system, the reader typically uses a flatter, more even intonation than in conversation, with clear pauses at each numbered item and at the closing line. This differs from the trailing, invitational intonation of a hedged proposal from Chapter 7; a notice is being reported, not negotiated. In this chapter's Listening Practice, listen for these clear item-by-item pauses as a signal of formal read-aloud style.

Continue maintaining mora timing, vowel length, moraic ん, and phrase-level intonation as in previous chapters. No new pitch-accent notation is introduced in this chapter.

Grammar: Official Written Style

This chapter activates written-style grammar recognition for 〜にあたり, 〜ものとする, 〜こと, and formal nominalization, building on the operational keigo and negotiation language from Chapters 6 and 7.

Marking a formal occasion with 〜にあたり

Purpose: to introduce the circumstance or occasion for an announcement in a formal, written register, roughly corresponding to "on the occasion of ___" or "in ___-ing."

Building Block — [verb dictionary form／noun]＋にあたり
イベントを開催するにあたり、参加者の皆様に下記の点をご確認いただきます。
〜にあたり attaches to a dictionary-form verb or a noun and introduces the occasion for the statement that follows, most often at the opening of a notice. It is more formal than conversational connectors such as 〜とき and is recognition-level in this chapter: learners should be able to identify its function when reading, and may use it in a controlled written notice, but full manipulation of the pattern is not required for spoken production.
申請書を提出するにあたり、締め切りをご確認ください。
In submitting the application form, please confirm the deadline.

Stating a regulation with 〜ものとする

Purpose: to state a rule, policy, or provision in an impersonal, regulatory way, without naming who is responsible for enforcing it.

Building Block — [verb dictionary form]＋ものとする
参加を希望する方は、事前に申請書を提出するものとする。
〜ものとする is a formal written pattern used to state that something "shall be" the case, common in policies, bylaws, and official guidelines. It differs from a spoken instruction such as 〜てください in that it states a standing provision rather than making a direct request of the reader. This chapter treats 〜ものとする as a recognition target for reading; learners are not required to produce it independently in speech.
対象となる活動は、担当部署が別途定めるものとする。
The activities that are eligible shall be separately determined by the department in charge.

Listing requirements with 〜こと

Purpose: to state a required action as a numbered item in a list of rules or guidelines, without a full sentence-final predicate.

Building Block — [verb て-form の反対の形／plain form]＋こと
１．申請書は締め切りまでに提出すること。
Nominalizing a plain-form verb with こと and ending the item there, without です or ます, is a common way to state a requirement in a numbered list of rules. The effect is closer to "submit the form by the deadline" as a standing requirement than to a direct spoken command. This pattern is recognition-level for reading numbered guidelines; learners may use it in a controlled written task such as drafting a short set of rules, following the model closely.
２．会場内では係員の指示に従うこと。
2. Follow the instructions of staff members while inside the venue.

Formal nominalization and document structure

Language Focus — How a notice is organized

A typical internal notice or set of guidelines follows a predictable order: a title naming the document, a short opening statement (often using 〜にあたり or a similarly formal opening), a numbered list of items (often ending in 〜こと or a regulatory 〜ものとする), and a closing line giving a deadline, a contact person, or an inquiries address, frequently introduced with 問い合わせ. Recognizing this structure helps a reader locate the purpose, the requirements, and the necessary follow-up action even in a document with unfamiliar vocabulary.

Grammar Practice

  1. Identify the function of 〜にあたり in the following sentence and explain it in English: 新しい規則を導入するにあたり、説明会を行います。
  2. Rewrite the following spoken instruction as a numbered rule using 〜こと: 申請書を必ず記入してください。
  3. Identify whether the following sentence is a formal regulatory statement or a direct spoken request, and explain how you know: 対象となる方は、事前に申請するものとする。
  4. Draft one opening line and one numbered rule for a short notice about a volunteer sign-up deadline, following the models above.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Emma has just read a draft notice posted on the office board and asks 田中さん a clarifying question about it.

エマ すみません、掲示板の通知を拝見したのですが、一点よろしいでしょうか。 Excuse me, I saw the notice on the board — may I ask about one point?
田中さん ええ、どうぞ。 Yes, go ahead.
エマ 「申請書は締め切りまでに提出すること」とありますが、締め切りは何日でしょうか。通知には日付が書かれていないようです。 It says "submit the application form by the deadline," but what date is the deadline? The notice doesn't seem to have the date written on it.
田中さん ああ、失礼いたしました。締め切りは来月十日です。通知を修正して、日付を記入いたします。 Ah, my apologies. The deadline is the tenth of next month. I'll revise the notice and fill in the date.
エマ 承知いたしました。問い合わせ先も記載したほうがよいかもしれません。 Understood. It might also be good to include a contact for inquiries.
田中さん そうですね、そのようにいたします。ご指摘ありがとうございます。 You're right, I'll do that. Thank you for pointing it out.

Writing Workshop

In this workshop you will write a short internal notice announcing an activity and stating two or three requirements, following the layout modeled above.

  1. Write a title naming the notice, following the modeled layout.
  2. Write an opening statement introducing the occasion for the notice, using 〜にあたり or a similarly formal opening.
  3. State who is eligible or affected, using 対象 and related vocabulary.
  4. Write two or three numbered requirements, using 〜こと to close each item.
  5. Write a closing line giving a deadline and an inquiries contact, using 締め切り and 問い合わせ.
  6. Audit your notice for accurate vocabulary, correct kanji, consistent formal written register, and a clear, complete structure.

チェックリスト — Editing checklist:

  • My notice has a clear title.
  • The opening statement clearly introduces the occasion for the notice.
  • Eligibility or scope is clearly stated.
  • I have written at least two numbered requirements using 〜こと.
  • The closing line includes a deadline and a contact point for inquiries.
  • I have checked kanji, particles, formal written predicate forms, and register consistency throughout.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Why written notices sound different from spoken keigo

Learners who have just built strong control of spoken keigo in Chapter 6 sometimes expect a written notice to sound the same way. In practice, formal written Japanese used in notices, guidelines, and regulations favors impersonal, standing statements — patterns like 〜ものとする and 〜こと — over the interpersonal politeness markers used in speech. This is not a contradiction: spoken keigo manages a relationship between specific speakers in the moment, while written regulatory style states a rule that applies regardless of who is reading it. Both are part of an educated adult's control of Japanese, and professional contexts typically require both.

Regional Note — Document conventions vary by institution

The exact layout, numbering style, and level of formality used in a notice can vary by organization, industry, and even individual department, and some workplaces increasingly post informal digital notices alongside or instead of traditional printed ones. The patterns in this chapter are broadly usable defaults for general institutional notices, not the only acceptable format.

Language Note — Reading before producing

〜にあたり, 〜ものとする, and 〜こと are introduced here mainly as reading targets. Learners should focus first on recognizing these patterns confidently in authentic-style notices; controlled production in a guided writing task, as in this chapter's Writing Workshop, is sufficient at this stage. Full independent production of regulatory prose is developed further across later chapters of this unit.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Writing a numbered rule as a direct spoken command

Error: １．申請書を締め切りまでに提出してください。 used as a numbered item in a formal notice.

Correction: １．申請書は締め切りまでに提出すること。 — numbered rules in a formal notice normally close with こと rather than a spoken request form.

Common Mistake — Treating 申請する and 適用する as interchangeable

Error: この規則を申請する, intending to say that a rule is being applied or put into effect.

Correction: この規則を適用する。 — 申請する means "to apply" in the sense of submitting a request or application (a person applies for something); 適用する means "to apply" in the sense of putting a rule into effect.

Common Mistake — Omitting the closing inquiries line

Error: A drafted notice that states requirements but gives the reader no way to ask a question or confirm details.

Correction: Close the notice with a line such as ご不明な点がございましたら、問い合わせ先までご連絡ください。 — official notices normally tell the reader where to direct questions.

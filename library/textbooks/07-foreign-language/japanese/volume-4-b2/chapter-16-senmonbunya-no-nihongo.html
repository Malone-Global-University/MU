専門分野の日本語Japanese for Specialized Fields

Terminology, register, and domain discourse · Speak and Write Japanese: Professional, Academic, and Cultural Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Recognize and use domain-specific collocations across business, science and technology, education, the arts, finance, health communication, and policy-adjacent texts.
  2. Define and classify specialized concepts in Japanese using layered definitions appropriate to a general educated audience.
  3. Make cautious, appropriately hedged claims about specialized topics using に基づく and をめぐる.
  4. Read short specialized passages from several fields, using rare-term furigana strategically rather than relying on it for every unfamiliar word.
  5. Distinguish core general vocabulary from field-specific terminology, and identify when a term needs to be explained for a non-specialist reader.
  6. Write a short explanatory paragraph that introduces a specialized topic to a general audience without oversimplifying it inaccurately.
  7. Discuss a specialized topic in conversation, asking for clarification when unfamiliar terminology appears.

Communication Scenario

Now that the "voices of the neighborhood" issue has gone to print, the magazine's editor has invited Emma to join a small volunteer team preparing a new occasional section called "くらしと専門知識" ("Everyday Life and Specialized Knowledge"), which explains developments from different fields in plain but accurate language for general readers. Her first assignment is to sit in on a short planning meeting where a local clinic nurse, a small business owner, and a university student researcher each pitch a topic — a public-health notice about seasonal allergies, a small business's move to cashless payment, and a linguistics student's explanation of a local language survey — and Emma must take notes, ask clarifying questions about unfamiliar terminology, and draft a short introductory paragraph for one of the three topics.

  1. The learner listens to three short specialized pitches and identifies the field and central claim of each.
  2. The learner asks for clarification about unfamiliar domain terminology in a natural, level-appropriate way.
  3. The learner reads short specialized texts from at least two different fields and identifies definitions, classifications, and cautious claims.
  4. The learner writes a short explanatory paragraph introducing one specialized topic to a general audience.
  5. The learner discusses the three pitches with a partner or instructor, deciding which topic best fits the new magazine section.

Script Strategy — Furigana as a rare-term tool, not a default

In earlier chapters, furigana often supported entire sentences of unfamiliar kanji. From this chapter forward, furigana in specialized texts is reserved for genuinely rare readings, technical terms outside the active kanji set, and names — for example, 花粉症かふんしょう may appear with furigana on first mention in a health passage, then without furigana afterward, since it becomes a repeated key term. Kanji you have already consolidated, such as 対策 or 調査, should not carry furigana in this chapter's texts.

When you meet an unfamiliar specialized term without furigana, use context, surrounding definitions, and word structure — not guesswork about pronunciation alone — to understand its meaning.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Reading specialized compounds aloud fluently

Specialized vocabulary is often built from longer Sino-Japanese compounds, such as キャッシュレス決済 or 言語使用. Practice maintaining even mora timing and consistent long-vowel and geminate-consonant accuracy across these longer words, rather than slowing down and losing rhythm mid-word. Continue treating pitch-accent notation as a reference tool rather than a requirement; where this chapter's audio or read-aloud scripts model a term, listen for its natural phrase-level pitch rather than trying to mark accent yourself.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter authorizes domain collocations, definitions, classification patterns, cautious claims, and two connective patterns for citing a basis or framing a topic under discussion: に基づく and をめぐる. No new verb morphology or clause type is introduced; these patterns build on the source-attribution and stance systems already active from Chapters 4 and 12.

1. Layered definition: 〜とは〜のことです / 〜という〜です

Purpose: to define a specialized term for a general reader in a compact, natural sentence.

  • 花粉症とは、花粉が原因で起こるアレルギー症状のことです。"Kafunshō" refers to allergy symptoms caused by pollen.とは〜のことです — formal definition frame
  • キャッシュレス決済というのは、現金を使わずに支払いをする方法です。"Cashless payment" is a method of paying without using cash.というのは〜です — definition frame, review from Ch. 2

2. Classification: 〜は〜に分類される / 〜の一種である

Purpose: to place a specific case within a broader category.

  • この調査は、社会言語学の一種に分類される。This survey is classified as a type of sociolinguistics.〜の一種に分類される — classification pattern

3. Citing a basis: 〜に基づく

Purpose: to state that a claim, plan, or policy is grounded in specific data, research, or a specific source.

  • この対策は、去年の調査結果に基づいている。This countermeasure is based on last year's survey results.に基づいている — grounded claim

4. Framing a topic under discussion: 〜をめぐる

Purpose: to introduce a noun phrase as the subject of ongoing discussion, debate, or activity, without stating a personal stance.

  • キャッシュレス決済の導入をめぐる意見は、店によって様々だ。Opinions surrounding the introduction of cashless payment vary from shop to shop.をめぐる — topic-framing, neutral
Language Focus — Cautious claims in specialized explanation

When explaining a specialized topic to a general audience, precision about certainty matters as much as precision about content. に基づく signals that a claim rests on a stated source rather than on the writer's own authority, and をめぐる frames a topic as contested or actively discussed rather than settled. Both patterns let a writer explain a specialized field honestly without pretending to more certainty than the source material supports — the same cautious-claim habit introduced with とされる and と考えられる in Chapter 4.

Grammar Practice

  1. Write a definition of 専門用語 itself, using 〜とは〜のことです.
  2. Write one sentence classifying a topic of your choice using 〜の一種に分類される.
  3. Write one sentence using に基づく about a decision, plan, or policy you know of.
  4. Write one sentence using をめぐる to frame a topic currently being discussed in your own community.

Model Dialogue

Setting: The magazine's planning meeting. A clinic nurse, a small business owner, and a university student researcher each present a short pitch; Emma asks clarifying questions. Polite style throughout, appropriate to a small volunteer planning meeting among people who do not know each other well.

看護師花粉症とは、花粉が原因で起こるアレルギー症状のことです。今年は例年より症状が重い人が多いという調査結果に基づいて、簡単な対策を紹介したいと思います。Kafunshō refers to allergy symptoms caused by pollen. Based on survey results showing that more people than usual have severe symptoms this year, I'd like to introduce some simple countermeasures.
エマすみません、「対策」というのは、具体的にどういうことですか。Excuse me, what exactly do you mean by "taisaku"?
看護師ここでは、症状を軽くするための日常的な工夫という意味で使っています。Here I'm using it to mean everyday steps for lessening the symptoms.

エマなるほど。一般読者にとって、三つとも興味深いテーマだと思います。I see. I think all three would be interesting topics for a general reader.

Writing Workshop

Write a short explanatory paragraph (roughly six to ten sentences, in standard Japanese) introducing one of the three original pitches from the Communication Scenario — the health notice, the cashless-payment story, or the dialect survey — for a general magazine reader who has no background in that field.

  1. Choose your topic and identify the one or two specialized terms a general reader would not already know.
  2. Open with a definition of your central term using 〜とは〜のことです or 〜というのは〜です.
  3. Classify the topic within a broader category using 〜の一種に分類される where natural.
  4. Support one claim with a stated basis using に基づく.
  5. Frame the topic's current status using をめぐる if the topic involves ongoing discussion or change.
  6. Close with one sentence explaining why a general reader might find the topic useful or interesting.

チェックリスト — Editing checklist:

  • I have defined my central specialized term clearly for a non-specialist reader.
  • I have used at least one classification pattern and one cautious-claim pattern from this chapter.
  • I have not overused furigana on kanji I have already consolidated.
  • My paragraph does not overstate certainty beyond what my stated basis supports.
  • I have checked kanji, particles, and register for accuracy and consistency.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Community writers explaining specialized topics

Neighborhood and community publications in Japan frequently carry short, accessible explanations of specialized topics — public-health notices, changes to local business practices, or summaries of local research — written by community members rather than professional journalists or subject specialists. Writing well for this kind of audience means balancing accuracy with accessibility: a term cannot be dropped just because it is technical, but it must be defined clearly enough that a reader without background knowledge is not left behind.

Regional Note — Specialized language and local variation together

The researcher's dialect survey in this chapter's dialogue connects directly to the regional and social variation studied in Chapter 14. Specialized academic writing about dialects still requires the same respectful, non-hierarchical framing practiced earlier in the course; describing a survey's findings accurately is not the same as ranking the varieties it studies.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Leaving a specialized term undefined for a general reader

Error: Introducing キャッシュレス決済 or 花粉症 without any definition, assuming the reader already knows the term.

Correction: Open with a clear definition before using the term freely: キャッシュレス決済とは、現金を使わずに支払いをする方法のことです。

Common Mistake — Overusing furigana on already-consolidated kanji

Error: Adding furigana to familiar words such as 対策 or 調査 out of habit, which clutters a specialized text and signals that the kanji is harder than it is.

Correction: Reserve furigana for genuinely rare or first-mention terms, such as 花粉症かふんしょう on its first appearance, and remove it afterward.

Common Mistake — Stating a claim with more certainty than the source supports

Error: Writing 今年は症状がひどくなる。 as a flat, certain prediction based on a single survey.

Correction: Attach the claim to its basis and keep the hedge: 今年の調査に基づくと、症状が重くなる人が多いとされている。

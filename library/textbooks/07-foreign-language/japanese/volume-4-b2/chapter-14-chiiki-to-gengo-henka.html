地域と言語変化Regional and Social Variation

Variation, identity, and language change · Speak and Write Japanese: Professional, Academic, and Cultural Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Explain, in Japanese, the difference between standard Japanese and a regional or community variety in terms of vocabulary, pronunciation, or grammar, without ranking one as more correct than the other.
  2. Recognize a small set of labeled Kansai-dialect features when they appear in context, and explain what the standard equivalent would be.
  3. Describe heritage Japanese, youth language, and digital/informal writing as legitimate forms of Japanese shaped by their own communities and contexts.
  4. Identify style-shifting: a single speaker moving between standard, regional, or informal forms depending on setting, audience, or stance.
  5. Discuss accessibility and multilingual life in Japan, including non-Japanese residents, sign language, and multilingual public information.
  6. Read a short article or profile that discusses language variation and identify the variety, community, and register being described.
  7. Listen to a short conversation that includes a labeled regional or informal feature and identify what was said and why that form was used.
  8. Write a short reflective paragraph analyzing one instance of variation or style-shifting encountered in this chapter's materials.

Communication Scenario

Now that the visiting colleague has settled in near the shopping street, Emma runs into the colleague's Japanese coworker, Kenta, who grew up in Osaka but has lived in Tokyo for several years. Kenta answers the phone in standard Japanese with his manager, then switches into a relaxed Kansai-inflected way of talking the moment the call ends and he turns back to Emma. Later, Emma reads a short online profile about a university student who grew up speaking Japanese at home in Brazil and is now studying in Japan, and she watches a short video clip in which two teenagers use some casual, trend-driven expressions Emma has never encountered in her textbook. Emma wants to understand what is actually happening in each case — not to imitate every form she hears, but to recognize what kind of Japanese she is hearing, who uses it, and why a speaker might move between varieties within a single afternoon.

  1. The learner listens to and reads short authentic-style examples containing labeled regional, heritage, youth, or informal digital Japanese.
  2. The learner identifies the variety, likely speaker community, and setting for each example.
  3. The learner discusses, in Japanese, why a speaker might shift between standard and non-standard forms within the same conversation.
  4. The learner reads a short profile describing multilingual or heritage-language experience in Japan.
  5. The learner writes a short reflective paragraph analyzing one specific instance of variation encountered in the chapter.

Script and Orthography Focus

Script Strategy — Labeling variation clearly in a written text

When this course presents a sentence in a regional or informal form, it is marked with a short bracketed label such as 〔関西方言〕 (Kansai dialect) or 〔若者言葉〕 (youth language), followed by the standard-Japanese equivalent. This labeling convention continues throughout this chapter and should be treated as a reading aid, not as part of the sentence itself.

Place names associated with regional varieties, such as 大阪 (Osaka) or 東北地方 (the Tōhoku region), should always be verified rather than assumed, since regional labels are approximate and a single prefecture or city may contain more than one local variety.

Pronunciation and Listening Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Recognizing that pitch and rhythm vary by region

Standard Japanese pitch-accent patterns, as referenced earlier in this course, reflect a Tokyo-area reference model. Other regions, including the Kansai area, commonly realize pitch differently for many of the same words, and a speaker's regional pitch pattern does not indicate an error. This chapter asks learners only to notice, in labeled listening examples, that a speaker's pitch or rhythm may differ from the reference model without needing to reproduce that regional pattern. Continue maintaining consolidated mora timing, vowel length, moraic ん, and phrase-level intonation as established in earlier chapters; this chapter adds listening awareness of regional and informal variation on top of that foundation.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter authorizes labeled recognition of regional and social variation, explicit standard/productive distinctions, and analysis of style-shifting. These are new systems for this volume and should be treated as introduced (I) for production and receptive/labeled for the underlying regional forms themselves, following the Part III matrices. Learners are expected to talk about variation in standard Japanese, not to produce regional, heritage, or youth forms themselves.

1. Naming and contrasting a variety

Purpose: to identify a form of Japanese as belonging to a particular region, generation, or community, and to state its standard-Japanese equivalent.

  • これは関西方言で、標準語では「そうです」に当たります。This is Kansai dialect; in standard Japanese it corresponds to "sō desu."Xは〜方言で、標準語では〜に当たる — naming and equating a variety
  • 「マジで」は若者言葉としてよく使われます。"Maji de" is commonly used as youth language.

2. Style-shifting: 使い分ける

Purpose: to describe a speaker moving between two or more forms of Japanese depending on setting, audience, or relationship.

  • 健太さんは、仕事のときは標準語を、友達と話すときは関西弁を使い分けています。Kenta uses standard Japanese for work and Kansai dialect with friends, switching between the two.状況によって〜を使い分ける — style-shifting by context
  • 相手や場面によって、言葉遣いを変えるのは自然なことです。It is natural to change one's way of speaking depending on the listener and the situation.

3. Describing heritage and multilingual experience

Purpose: to talk about learning or maintaining Japanese as a heritage language, or living multilingually in or with Japan.

  • 彼女は家では日本語を、学校ではポルトガル語を使って育ちました。She grew up using Japanese at home and Portuguese at school.
  • 継承語としての日本語は、家庭や地域によって使い方が異なります。Japanese as a heritage language is used differently depending on the household and community.

4. Talking about language change

Purpose: to describe how vocabulary, expressions, or usage change over time, without treating older or newer forms as more correct.

  • 若い世代と年上の世代とでは、使う言葉が変化してきています。The vocabulary used by younger and older generations has been changing.
  • 新しい表現が生まれても、それは日本語が乱れているという意味ではありません。Even when new expressions appear, it does not mean that the Japanese language is deteriorating.
Language Focus — Variation is not a hierarchy

Regional dialects, heritage Japanese, youth language, and standard Japanese are different, coexisting forms of the same language, each shaped by the community that uses it. Standard Japanese is the productive model for this course because it is broadly intelligible across regions and contexts, not because other varieties are incorrect, careless, or lesser. A single speaker may move fluently among several of these forms in one day, choosing the form that fits the setting, the listener, and the relationship.

Writing Workshop

In this workshop you will write a short reflective paragraph (roughly six to ten sentences, in standard Japanese) analyzing one instance of variation or style-shifting encountered in this chapter.

  1. Choose one example — from the Model Passage, the Listening Practice, the Reading Practice, or your own Speaking Practice discussion.
  2. Name the variety or community involved, using vocabulary such as 方言, 若者言葉, or 継承語.
  3. State the standard-Japanese equivalent, if the example involves a non-standard form.
  4. Explain the likely reason for the speaker's choice, referring to setting, audience, relationship, or generation.
  5. Include at least one sentence using 使い分ける or another chapter grammar pattern.
  6. Audit your paragraph to confirm that it describes the variation accurately and respectfully, without ranking it against standard Japanese.

チェックリスト — Editing checklist:

  • I have named the specific example I am analyzing.
  • I have identified the variety or community involved.
  • I have given the standard-Japanese equivalent where relevant.
  • I have used at least one chapter grammar pattern, including 使い分ける if appropriate.
  • My paragraph does not describe any variety as incorrect or inferior.
  • I have checked kanji, particles, and register for accuracy.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Multilingual and accessible communication in Japan

Contemporary Japan includes long-established and growing communities who use languages other than Japanese at home, alongside residents who communicate primarily through Japanese Sign Language (日本手話) or other accessible formats. Public transportation announcements, ward-office notices, and emergency information increasingly appear in multiple languages and in accessible formats, reflecting an understanding of Japan as a multilingual society rather than a uniformly monolingual one. This awareness supports, rather than replaces, the productive standard-Japanese focus of this course.

Regional Note — Kansai Japanese as one variety among many

Kansai Japanese, associated with the Osaka-Kyoto-Kobe area, is one of the most widely recognized regional varieties in Japanese media, but it is only one of many regional varieties spoken across Japan, including distinct varieties in the Tōhoku region, Kyushu, Hokkaido, and Okinawa. Okinawan Japanese and the Ryukyuan languages are historically and linguistically distinct from mainland regional dialects and should not be treated as simply another accent of standard Japanese. This chapter introduces a small set of labeled Kansai features as a representative example; it does not attempt to cover the full range of regional Japanese.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Treating a labeled regional or youth form as something to produce freely

Error: Using a labeled expression such as ほんまに or それな in an ordinary standard-Japanese assignment or conversation with a teacher, after encountering it once in this chapter.

Correction: Recognize labeled forms receptively and discuss them accurately, but continue producing standard Japanese in ordinary coursework and formal settings: これは若者言葉で、標準語では「それ、わかる」に当たります。

Common Mistake — Describing a variety as broken or incorrect Japanese

Error: Describing Rina's heritage Japanese, or Kenta's Kansai dialect, as a mistake-filled version of "real" Japanese.

Correction: Describe each variety accurately and respectfully, naming its community and context: リナさんの日本語は、継承語として家庭の中で育った日本語です。

Common Mistake — Assuming one entertainment example represents an entire region or generation

Error: Assuming that a single dialect line heard in a television drama or anime accurately represents how everyone in that region speaks in daily life.

Correction: Treat media examples as one stylized data point, and note explicitly that real usage varies by speaker, generation, and setting: ドラマの中の方言は、実際の話し方を多少誇張していることがあります。

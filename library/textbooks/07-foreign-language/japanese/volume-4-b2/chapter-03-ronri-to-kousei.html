論理と構成Logic and Organization

Argument architecture and transitions · Speak and Write Japanese: Professional, Academic, and Cultural Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Signal a logical conclusion drawn from prior statements using したがって and 以上のことから.
  2. Present a contrasting or alternative viewpoint using 一方で.
  3. Concede a point while maintaining your own position using にもかかわらず.
  4. Organize a short position paper, report, or critique into claim, evidence, counterpoint, and conclusion.
  5. Read a short argumentative passage and identify its claim, supporting evidence, counterpoint, and conclusion.
  6. Listen to a short structured argument and track how the speaker moves from evidence to conclusion.
  7. Present a position on a topic from your own field or interest, acknowledge an opposing view, and reach a conclusion.
  8. Write a short, organized position paragraph that states a claim, supports it, addresses a counterpoint, and concludes logically.

Communication Scenario

Building on Chapter 2's definition of 地域リサイクル制度, Emma has been asked to take a position: should her local government expand the program, or is it already sufficient? She has plenty of evidence and a clear opinion, but she notices that her Japanese so far lets her state facts and definitions much more easily than it lets her connect them into an argument. She can say what is true, but not yet why one thing follows from another, or how to acknowledge a reasonable objection without abandoning her own position. 佐藤さん faces the same problem with a position of his own, drawn from his work. This chapter gives Emma and 佐藤さん the connective tools of argument: how to draw a conclusion from evidence, how to present a contrasting view, how to concede a point without losing the thread of the argument, and how to organize all of this into a coherent short position paper.

  1. The learner draws an explicit conclusion from stated evidence using したがって or 以上のことから.
  2. The learner introduces a contrasting viewpoint or alternative approach using 一方で.
  3. The learner concedes a valid point while maintaining a position using にもかかわらず.
  4. The learner reads a short argumentative passage and identifies its claim, evidence, counterpoint, and conclusion.
  5. The learner presents a position to a partner, addresses a counterpoint, and states a clear conclusion.

Pronunciation and Listening Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Intonation at logical turning points

Words like 一方で and にもかかわらず mark a turn in an argument, and speakers often signal this turn with a small shift in pitch or a brief pause immediately before the transition word, distinct from the pause you practiced in Chapter 2 at definitional boundaries. Listen for this shift in the Model Dialogue and Listening Practice passages below, and practice placing a similar small pause before したがって, 一方で, にもかかわらず, and 以上のことから in your own speech.

Continue maintaining mora timing, vowel length, moraic ん, and phrase-level intonation as in previous chapters; this chapter adds attention to the intonation contour that surrounds logical transition words specifically.

Grammar: Building an Argument

This chapter activates four connective tools for argument: したがって and 以上のことから for drawing a conclusion, 一方で for presenting a contrasting viewpoint, and にもかかわらず for conceding a point while maintaining a position. Together with Chapter 2's definitional tools, these let you build a short, organized position paper.

したがって — Drawing a direct logical conclusion

Purpose: to signal that the following statement is a conclusion that follows logically and directly from what was just said; formal and written-style, appropriate for reports, essays, and structured argument.

Building Block — [evidence]。したがって、[conclusion]。
[根拠となる文]。したがって、[結論となる文]。
したがって begins a new sentence and marks a conclusion that the speaker or writer presents as following necessarily from the prior statement. It is more formal than plain だから and is common in reports, essays, and academic writing.
この制度は資源の無駄を大きく減らしている。したがって、地域はこの制度をさらに拡大するべきだ。
This system greatly reduces wasted resources. Therefore, the community should expand this system further.
  • 参加者の多くがこの制度に満足している。したがって、制度を縮小する根拠は弱い。Many participants are satisfied with this system. Therefore, the grounds for scaling it back are weak.したがって + conclusion

以上のことから — Concluding after multiple points

Purpose: to draw a conclusion after presenting several pieces of evidence or several sentences of reasoning, rather than after a single statement; common at the close of a paragraph, report, or argument.

Building Block — [several points]。以上のことから、[overall conclusion]。
[根拠１]。[根拠２]。以上のことから、[結論]。
以上のことから literally means "from the above matters" and signals that the conclusion draws together everything stated previously, not just the immediately preceding sentence. It is well suited to closing a paragraph that has presented multiple pieces of evidence.
この制度は資源を節約している。また、住民の環境意識も高めている。以上のことから、この制度は地域にとって有益だと言える。
This system conserves resources. It has also raised residents' environmental awareness. From the above, it can be said that this system is beneficial for the community.
  • 費用は決して低くない。しかし、長期的な効果は大きい。以上のことから、この提案には十分な根拠があると考える。The cost is by no means low. However, the long-term effect is significant. From the above, I believe this proposal has sufficient grounds.以上のことから + evaluative conclusion

一方で — Presenting a contrasting viewpoint

Purpose: to introduce a contrasting fact, viewpoint, or approach alongside what was just stated, without necessarily rejecting it; useful for presenting both sides of an issue before reaching a conclusion.

Building Block — [one side]。一方で、[contrasting side]。
[Ａの視点]。一方で、[Ｂの視点]。
一方で presents a second perspective, fact, or approach that stands alongside the first rather than directly refuting it. It is commonly used to give balanced treatment to two sides of an issue before the writer states their own conclusion.
この制度には多くの利点がある。一方で、導入の費用が高いという課題も残っている。
This system has many advantages. On the other hand, there remains the challenge that the cost of introducing it is high.
  • 賛成する住民は多い。一方で、反対する声も無視できない。Many residents are in favor. On the other hand, the voices of opposition cannot be ignored.一方で presenting an opposing group's view

にもかかわらず — Conceding a point while maintaining a position

Purpose: to acknowledge a fact that would seem to argue against the writer's position, and then maintain that position anyway; stronger and more formal than でも or けど.

Building Block — [conceded fact]にもかかわらず、[maintained position]
[譲歩する事実]にもかかわらず、[話し手の主張]。
にもかかわらず connects a clause or noun phrase describing a fact that might be expected to weaken the speaker's argument, and then introduces a statement that maintains the argument regardless. This concession-and-continuation pattern is central to persuasive academic writing.
導入の費用が高いにもかかわらず、多くの自治体がこの制度を採用している。
Despite the high cost of introduction, many local governments have adopted this system.
  • 一部で反対の声があるにもかかわらず、この提案の根拠は十分だと考える。Despite some voices of opposition, I believe the grounds for this proposal are sufficient.にもかかわらず + maintained conclusion

Building a short position paper

Purpose: to combine claim, evidence, counterpoint, and conclusion into one coherent short argument, using this chapter's transitions together with Chapter 2's definitional tools.

Language Focus — Four-part argument structure

A clear short position paper typically moves through four steps: (1) state your 主張 (claim) directly; (2) support it with 根拠 (grounds or evidence); (3) acknowledge a contrasting view or a point against your position using 一方で or にもかかわらず; (4) close with a conclusion using したがって or 以上のことから that restates your position in light of everything discussed. This structure extends Chapter 2's three-step definition into a four-step argument.

地域は地域リサイクル制度をさらに拡大するべきだと主張する。この制度は資源の無駄を大きく減らし、住民の満足度も高い。一方で、導入の費用が高いという課題も残っている。しかし、費用の問題があるにもかかわらず、長期的な効果を考えれば、以上のことから、制度の拡大には十分な根拠があると言える。

I claim that the community should expand the community recycling program further. This system greatly reduces wasted resources and resident satisfaction is high. On the other hand, the challenge remains that the cost of introduction is high. However, despite the cost issue, when the long-term effect is considered, from the above, it can be said that there are sufficient grounds for expanding the system.

Grammar Practice

  1. Write one sentence of evidence, then a conclusion drawn from it using したがって.
  2. Write two sentences of evidence, then an overall conclusion using 以上のことから.
  3. Write one sentence presenting your view, then a contrasting view using 一方で.
  4. Write one sentence conceding a point against your position and maintaining your position using にもかかわらず.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Emma and 佐藤さん continue their preparation for the group discussion, this time debating whether the recycling program should be expanded.

佐藤さん エマさんは、この制度をどうするべきだと主張しますか。 Emma, what do you claim should be done with this system?
エマ 私は、この制度をさらに拡大するべきだと主張します。この制度は資源の無駄を大きく減らしていて、参加している住民の満足度も高いです。したがって、拡大する十分な根拠があると思います。 I claim that this system should be expanded further. This system greatly reduces wasted resources, and satisfaction is high among the residents who participate. Therefore, I think there are sufficient grounds for expansion.
佐藤さん なるほど。でも、導入の費用については、どう考えますか。 I see. But what do you think about the cost of introduction?
エマ 一方で、費用が高いという課題があることは認めます。にもかかわらず、長期的に見れば、この制度は地域にとって有益だと考えます。以上のことから、拡大の提案には根拠があると思います。 On the other hand, I admit there is the challenge that the cost is high. Despite that, when viewed long-term, I believe this system is beneficial for the community. From the above, I think there are grounds for the proposal to expand it.
佐藤さん 分かりました。私の分野の提案についても、同じような構成で説明してみます。 I understand. I'll try explaining the proposal from my own field using a similar structure.

Writing Workshop

In this workshop you will write a complete short position paragraph on a concept from your own field or interest, applying this chapter's grammar together with Chapter 2's definitional tools and the accuracy audit habits from Chapter 1.

  1. State your claim directly using 主張する or 主張だ.
  2. Give supporting evidence using 根拠 or specific facts, optionally drawing a first conclusion with したがって.
  3. Introduce a counterpoint using 一方で, and address it while maintaining your position using にもかかわらず.
  4. Close with an overall conclusion using 以上のことから that draws together everything you have said.
  5. Run the four-point audit from Chapter 1: particles, predicate form, register consistency, and clause connection.

チェックリスト — Editing checklist:

  • My paragraph states a clear claim using 主張する or 主張だ.
  • My paragraph gives at least one piece of supporting evidence using 根拠 or specific facts.
  • My paragraph introduces a counterpoint using 一方で.
  • My paragraph concedes and responds to that counterpoint using にもかかわらず.
  • My paragraph closes with a conclusion using したがって or 以上のことから.
  • I have checked every particle and predicate form in my paragraph.
  • My paragraph maintains one consistent register from start to finish.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Concession before conclusion

Many Japanese position papers, reports, and opinion pieces present a counterpoint or acknowledge a limitation before restating the writer's conclusion, rather than dismissing opposing views outright. Learning to build this concession-and-conclusion structure with 一方で and にもかかわらず gives you access to a common and highly valued pattern of argumentation in Japanese academic and professional writing, not a universal feature of how all Japanese speakers argue in every setting.

Regional Note — Directness varies by setting, not only by speaker

How directly a writer or speaker states disagreement with a counterpoint can vary widely by publication, workplace, academic field, and individual style, not only by broad cultural tendency. A newspaper editorial, an internal company report, and a graduate seminar paper may all handle the same counterpoint quite differently while remaining entirely appropriate within their own genre.

Language Note — にもかかわらず is stronger than でも or けど

にもかかわらず concedes a specific fact and then explicitly maintains a position despite it, which is a stronger and more formal move than the everyday contrast markers でも or けど. Using にもかかわらず in casual conversation about small matters can sound unexpectedly formal; save it for genuine argumentative concession, as modeled in this chapter.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Using したがって without a clear preceding cause

Error: *天気がいいです。したがって、この制度を拡大するべきだ。 — the weather has no logical connection to the conclusion, so したがって sounds unmotivated and confusing.

Correction: この制度は資源の無駄を減らしている。したがって、この制度を拡大するべきだ。 — したがって requires that the preceding statement function as genuine evidence for the conclusion that follows.

Common Mistake — Treating 一方で as a simple negation

Error: *この制度には利点がある。一方で、利点がない。 — 一方で introduces a contrasting perspective, not a flat contradiction of the same claim.

Correction: この制度には利点がある。一方で、費用が高いという課題もある。 — pair 一方で with a genuinely different aspect of the topic, such as a cost, a risk, or an alternative viewpoint.

Common Mistake — Omitting a conclusion after にもかかわらず

Error: *費用が高いにもかかわらず。 — にもかかわらず sets up an expectation of a maintained position that must follow; leaving the sentence there is incomplete.

Correction: 費用が高いにもかかわらず、多くの自治体がこの制度を採用している。 — always complete the concession with the position it is meant to support.

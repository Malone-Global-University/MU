資料を読むReading Source Materials

Source evaluation and synthesis · Speak and Write Japanese: Professional, Academic, and Cultural Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Attribute a claim to a specific source using によれば.
  2. Report a widely held view or common characterization using とされる.
  3. Present a reasoned judgment as your own interpretation using と考えられる.
  4. Distinguish, in a chain of attribution, what a source states, what is generally said, and what the writer concludes.
  5. Compare two or more sources on the same topic and note where they agree and disagree.
  6. Paraphrase a source's claim in your own words without copying its sentence structure.
  7. Read a report, article, or survey summary and identify its sources, claims, and evidence.
  8. Write a short source-based paragraph that attributes claims accurately and draws its own conclusion.

Communication Scenario

Emma's position paper on the 地域リサイクル制度 persuaded her study group, but 佐藤さん points out a problem: her argument in Chapter 3 rested on claims she has not yet traced back to any actual source. Where did the figure about resident satisfaction come from? Who says the cost is high, and compared to what? Before the group presents its position publicly, Emma needs to find real reports, articles, and survey summaries about the program, read them carefully, and show clearly which claims come from which source, which claims are simply "said to be true" without a named source, and which conclusions are her own judgment based on the evidence. 佐藤さん is preparing a similar review of sources for his own field. This chapter gives Emma and 佐藤さん the tools to read source materials critically and to write about them honestly: attributing a claim to where it came from, reporting what is generally said, offering their own reasoned conclusion, and keeping all three clearly distinct.

  1. The learner attributes a specific claim to a named source using によれば.
  2. The learner reports a general characterization using とされる, without claiming it as the writer's own view.
  3. The learner offers a reasoned conclusion as their own interpretation using と考えられる.
  4. The learner reads a report or article and identifies its sources, key claims, and supporting evidence.
  5. The learner compares two sources on the same topic and states where they agree and where they differ.

Script Strategy — Verified names, dates, and citation-like formatting

When you write about a source, the institution name, publication date, and any numerical figures you cite must match the source exactly. Do not adjust an organization's name to a more familiar-looking form, and do not guess at a date or a percentage. A common project convention for referring to a source in a short paragraph is: 〈組織名〉の〈報告書名〉（年）によれば、 — for example, 〇〇市の報告書（２０２５年）によれば、. Use Arabic numerals for years, percentages, and other survey figures, consistent with this volume's numeral conventions.

Pronunciation and Listening Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Distinguishing attribution from opinion in speech

When a speaker moves from reporting a source with によれば or とされる into their own judgment with と考えられる, listen for a small change in pace or emphasis that often marks this shift, similar in function to the transition-word pause you practiced in Chapter 3 but occurring here between reported information and personal interpretation rather than between two logical steps. Missing this shift can lead a listener to mistake the speaker's own opinion for something the source actually said, or vice versa.

Continue maintaining mora timing, vowel length, moraic ん, and phrase-level intonation as in previous chapters; this chapter adds attention to the boundary between reported and original content specifically.

Grammar: Attributing and Weighing Sources

This chapter activates three attribution and evaluation tools: によれば for naming a specific source, とされる for reporting a general or widely held characterization without a named source, and と考えられる for presenting your own reasoned conclusion. Together with Chapter 3's argument tools, these let you build a paragraph that is honest about where each claim comes from.

によれば — Attributing a claim to a named source

Purpose: to introduce information as coming specifically from a named person, organization, report, or document, rather than presenting it as the writer's own claim.

Building Block — [source]によれば、[reported content]（そうだ／ようだ／と言われている, etc.）。
[出典]によれば、[報告される内容]。
によれば attaches to the source noun and marks everything that follows as coming from that source, not from the writer directly. The reported content is often closed with a form such as そうだ, とのことだ, or a plain statement, depending on register; this chapter uses plain reported statements for clarity.
〇〇市の報告書（２０２５年）によれば、この制度の参加率は住民の約六十パーセントに達している。
According to the city's 2025 report, participation in this system has reached approximately sixty percent of residents.
  • 担当者によれば、費用の大部分は初期投資であり、年々減少している。According to the official in charge, most of the cost is initial investment, and it is decreasing year by year.によれば + attributed claim

とされる — Reporting a general characterization

Purpose: to report a claim, belief, or characterization that is widely held or generally stated, without attributing it to one specific named source and without presenting it as the writer's own view.

Building Block — [claim]とされる。
[一般的な見方]とされる。
とされる marks a claim as something that is generally said, believed, or treated as true by people in general, an unspecified group, or common opinion, distinct from によれば's specific named source and from と考えられる's personal judgment. It is common in reports and articles when the original source of a common view is not one identifiable document.
この種の制度は、導入初期に費用がかかりやすいとされる。
This kind of system is generally said to tend to incur costs early in its introduction.
  • 地域住民の協力がなければ、この制度は機能しないとされる。It is generally said that without the cooperation of local residents, this system will not function.とされる + general characterization

と考えられる — Presenting the writer's own reasoned conclusion

Purpose: to present a judgment or interpretation as the writer's own reasoned conclusion, based on the evidence discussed, rather than as something reported from a source or generally believed.

Building Block — [evidence]。したがって／以上のことから、[judgment]と考えられる。
[根拠]。以上のことから、[書き手の判断]と考えられる。
と考えられる combines the verb 考える (to think, consider) with the potential/passive form to soften a personal judgment into a reasoned, somewhat impersonal conclusion appropriate to reports and academic writing. It signals that this is the writer's interpretation of the evidence, not a quotation or a general belief, and pairs naturally with Chapter 3's したがって and 以上のことから.
複数の調査結果が同じ傾向を示している。以上のことから、この制度は地域にとって有益だと考えられる。
Multiple survey results show the same tendency. From the above, it can be concluded that this system is beneficial for the community.
  • 二つの報告書の数字が一致していることから、この結果は信頼性が高いと考えられる。Because the figures in the two reports agree with each other, this result is thought to be highly reliable.と考えられる + writer's judgment

Comparing sources

Purpose: to compare what two or more sources say about the same topic, noting agreement, disagreement, or difference in emphasis, using 比較する and 一致する together with attribution language.

Language Focus — Three-way attribution contrast

Keep the three attribution tools distinct as you compare sources: use によれば when you can name the exact report, article, or person a claim comes from; use とされる when a claim is commonly stated but you cannot or do not need to name one specific source; and use と考えられる only for your own conclusion after weighing the evidence. A paragraph that compares sources typically states what Source A says (によれば), what Source B says (によれば), whether the two 一致する or differ, and finally what the writer concludes (と考えられる).

〇〇市の報告書（２０２５年）によれば、この制度の参加率は約六十パーセントである。一方で、地元新聞の記事によれば、参加率は五十パーセントにとどまるという。二つの数字は完全には一致していないが、大きな傾向としては近いと言える。この違いは調査方法の違いによるものだと考えられる。

According to the city's 2025 report, participation in this system is approximately sixty percent. On the other hand, according to a local newspaper article, participation remains at only fifty percent. The two figures do not match completely, but it can be said that they are close in overall tendency. This difference is thought to be due to a difference in survey methods.

Grammar Practice

  1. Write one sentence attributing a claim to a named source using によれば.
  2. Write one sentence reporting a general characterization using とされる, without naming a specific source.
  3. Write one sentence of evidence, then a conclusion using と考えられる.
  4. Write a short comparison of two sources on the same topic, using によれば twice, noting whether they 一致する, and closing with と考えられる.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Emma and 佐藤さん meet in the library to find and evaluate sources for Emma's recycling-program position before the group presents it.

佐藤さん エマさんの主張の根拠は、どの資料から来ていますか。出典を確認しましたか。 Where does the evidence for your claim come from, Emma? Have you checked the source?
エマ はい、〇〇市の報告書（２０２５年）によれば、この制度の参加率は約六十パーセントだそうです。それから、地元新聞の記事も見つけました。 Yes, according to the city's 2025 report, participation in this system is said to be about sixty percent. I also found a local newspaper article.
佐藤さん その記事の数字は、報告書の数字と一致していますか。 Does the figure in that article agree with the figure in the report?
エマ 完全には一致していません。記事によれば、参加率は五十パーセントにとどまるとされています。ただ、この種の制度は導入初期に数字が変動しやすいとされているので、大きな違いではないと考えられます。 They don't match completely. According to the article, participation is said to remain at only fifty percent. However, this kind of system is generally said to have fluctuating figures early in its introduction, so I think this isn't a major discrepancy.
佐藤さん なるほど。それなら、報告書と記事、両方の出典を要約に入れて、違いの理由も一文で説明した方がいいですね。 I see. In that case, it would be better to include both sources—the report and the article—in your summary, and explain the reason for the difference in one sentence.

ないようりかい — Comprehension questions:

  1. What two sources does Emma cite for her participation-rate claim?
  2. Do the two sources' figures agree with each other? What does Emma say about the difference?
  3. What does 佐藤さん recommend Emma do with the two sources in her summary?

チェックリスト — Editing checklist:

  • My paragraph attributes at least one specific claim to a named source using によれば.
  • My paragraph reports at least one general characterization using とされる, without over-attributing it to one source.
  • My paragraph notes whether its sources 一致する, or explains a difference between them.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Attribution as a mark of careful writing

Clearly attributing a claim to its source, or clearly marking a claim as a general characterization rather than the writer's own view, is widely valued in Japanese reports, academic writing, and serious journalism as a sign of careful, trustworthy work. Learning to use によれば, とされる, and と考えられる precisely gives you access to this expectation; it is not a claim that every piece of Japanese writing in every genre attributes sources this formally.

Regional Note — Citation conventions vary by genre and institution

How formally a source is cited, and how much detail is expected, differs across newspapers, government reports, academic journals, and casual blogs, and also varies between institutions and fields. A government report may require a full citation of the survey name, date, and sample size, while a newspaper article may simply say 関係者によれば without further detail; both are appropriate within their own genre and context.

Language Note — とされる is not the same as agreeing with a claim

Using とされる to report a general characterization does not mean the writer personally endorses that characterization; it simply reports that the view is commonly held or stated. Do not use とされる when you mean to state your own conclusion — reserve と考えられる for that purpose, and reserve によれば for claims you can trace to a specific, identifiable source.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Using によれば without naming an identifiable source

Error: *によれば、この制度は良い。 — によれば requires a specific source noun immediately before it; leaving the source unnamed defeats its purpose.

Correction: 〇〇市の報告書によれば、この制度は資源の無駄を減らしている。 — always name the specific source that によれば attributes the claim to.

Common Mistake — Treating とされる as the writer's own opinion

Error: Writing この制度は有益だとされる。 when the writer means to state their own conclusion after weighing the evidence.

Correction: 以上のことから、この制度は有益だと考えられる。 — reserve とされる for reporting what is generally said, and と考えられる for the writer's own reasoned judgment.

Common Mistake — Copying a source's sentence structure instead of paraphrasing

Error: Reproducing a source's sentence nearly word for word and simply adding によれば at the front, which blurs the line between summary and copying.

Correction: Restate the source's claim in your own words and sentence structure — for example, 要約する — before attaching によれば, keeping only names, dates, and figures exact.

復習２：職業・制度・発表Unit II Assessment

Professional and institutional communication assessment · Speak and Write Japanese: Professional, Academic, and Cultural Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Use operational keigo accurately in requests, apologies, invitations, and institutional communication, correcting common over-politeness and under-politeness errors.
  2. Participate in a meeting-style exchange that includes a proposal, a polite objection or alternative, and a summarized decision.
  3. Read and interpret an official document or notice, identifying its formal written patterns and practical content.
  4. Write a short formal message or notice appropriate to an institutional audience.
  5. Organize and deliver a short formal presentation, using transition language, hedged projections, and source attribution.
  6. Respond politely to an unexpected question, including one that must be deferred.
  7. Identify which formal register belongs to which medium: spoken keigo, meeting language, written regulatory style, and spoken presentation style.
  8. Complete an integrated task that draws on keigo, meetings, official documents, and formal presentations from Chapters 6 through 9.

Communication Scenario

The autumn recycling awareness event is now two weeks away. Emma's supervisor has asked her to close out this stage of the project by pulling together everything covered in Chapters 6 through 9: she must respond formally to a request from another department, sit in on a short planning meeting where a scheduling conflict needs to be resolved, read and confirm the details of an official notice about the event, and deliver a brief final status presentation to the wider staff group, taking questions afterward. Nothing new is being introduced in this chapter. Instead, Emma — and the learner working alongside her — must show that keigo, meeting language, official written style, and formal presentation language can all be called on appropriately, in the right medium, for the right audience, without mixing them together.

  1. The learner reads an official notice and answers questions about its content and formal written patterns.
  2. The learner listens to a short planning-meeting exchange and identifies the proposal, the objection, and the decision.
  3. The learner writes a short formal reply to an institutional request, choosing appropriate keigo.
  4. The learner delivers a short integrated status presentation and responds to at least one audience question.
  5. The learner completes the Unit II Performance Task, which draws on all four chapters at once.

Script Strategy — Recognizing genre by layout, not just content

A single event can generate four differently formatted texts: an institutional email requesting information (Chapter 6's formulas), a set of meeting minutes with numbered decisions (Chapter 7), an official notice with numbered clauses and 〜ものとする (Chapter 8), and a slide-style presentation outline with short noun-phrase headings and ・ bullets (Chapter 9). Before reading any text closely in this chapter, first identify which of these four layouts it uses, since layout signals which grammar and register to expect.

Pronunciation and Listening Review

Pronunciation Focus — Distinguishing meeting, keigo, and presentation prosody

This chapter checks that learners can distinguish three spoken styles by ear: the careful, softened pacing of keigo requests and apologies (Chapter 6); the more conversational but still polite back-and-forth of a meeting, including brief pauses before a polite disagreement (Chapter 7); and the measured, transition-marked pacing of a formal presentation, with a small pitch reset at each section boundary (Chapter 9). No new pitch-accent notation is introduced. Continue maintaining mora timing, vowel length, moraic ん, and phrase-level intonation as in previous chapters.

Grammar Review: Unit II Systems

This chapter assesses operational keigo, meeting language, official written style, and presentation structures together. No new grammar is introduced. The comparison below reviews how the same underlying idea — a request, a statement about the future, or a piece of information — is expressed differently depending on genre.

Integrated Dialogue

Setting: A short planning meeting the week before the event. A scheduling conflict has come up, and the group must reach a decision. Emma opens with a polite request, the group negotiates, and 田中さん summarizes the decision in meeting-appropriate language.

エマ お忙しいところ恐れ入りますが、当日の会場設営の時間について、少しご相談させていただけますでしょうか。 I'm sorry to trouble you when you're busy, but could I consult with you briefly about the setup time for the venue on the day of the event?
田中さん ええ、どうぞ。議題としましょう。 Yes, please go ahead. Let's make it an agenda item.
エマ 現在の予定では午前九時からの設営となっておりますが、係員の集合が九時半になる見込みのため、少し時間がずれてしまうかもしれません。 Under the current plan, setup begins at 9:00 a.m., but since staff are projected to gather at around 9:30, the timing may end up slightly off.
同僚 それは少し厳しいですね。設営開始を九時半に調整するのはいかがでしょうか。 That sounds a bit difficult. How about we adjust the setup start time to 9:30 instead?
田中さん 確かにそれも一つの方法ですが、開場が十時ですので、九時半開始では少し慌ただしくなるのではないかと思います。 That's certainly one option, but since the doors open at 10:00, I think starting at 9:30 might make things a bit rushed.
エマ では、係員の集合時間を九時十五分に早めることを検討させていただいてもよろしいでしょうか。 In that case, would it be all right if I looked into moving the staff gathering time earlier, to 9:15?
田中さん それでよろしいかと思います。以上を踏まえ、設営開始は九時のまま、係員の集合を九時十五分に変更するということでまとめさせていただきます。 I think that would be fine. Taking the above into account, I'll summarize this as: setup start remains at 9:00, and the staff gathering time is changed to 9:15.

Writing Assessment

In this assessment you will write a short formal reply to an institutional request, integrating keigo, an acknowledgment, and a next step.

  1. Open with a polite acknowledgment of the request you are replying to, using an appropriate keigo opening from Chapter 6.
  2. State your response to the request clearly, using humble or respectful forms as appropriate to your role.
  3. Add one hedged or confirmed detail about timing or next steps, drawing on either meeting language (Chapter 7) or presentation-style hedging (Chapter 9), whichever fits your reply better.
  4. Close politely, indicating when you will follow up if anything remains unconfirmed.
  5. Audit your reply for consistent keigo register throughout, correct kanji, and no accidental mixing of spoken meeting language into the written reply.

チェックリスト — Editing checklist:

  • My reply opens with an appropriate keigo acknowledgment.
  • My response to the request is clear and uses humble or respectful forms consistently.
  • I have not mixed spoken meeting language into a written reply.
  • Any uncertain detail is hedged or clearly marked as pending confirmation.
  • The closing states a next step or a time frame for follow-up.
  • I have checked kanji, particles, and register consistency throughout.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Why register control matters more than any single pattern

Across Chapters 6 through 9, no single grammar pattern is as important to professional competence as the ability to select the right register for the right medium and audience. A perfectly formed keigo sentence used in the wrong context, such as an overly formal request in a casual internal chat, can sound as awkward as an underformal request sent to an unfamiliar department. This chapter's integrated tasks are designed to build exactly this judgment, which continues to matter well beyond this unit.

Regional Note — Institutional conventions still vary

As in Chapter 9, the exact conventions for meetings, notices, and presentations vary by organization, industry, and individual workplace culture. The patterns reviewed in this chapter are broadly usable defaults, not the only acceptable forms across every Japanese-speaking institutional context.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Using written regulatory style in speech

Error: Saying aloud in a meeting 確認するものとする。, borrowing Chapter 8's written pattern for a spoken update.

Correction: 確認させていただきます。 — spoken settings call for keigo or presentation register, not written regulatory forms.

Common Mistake — Treating a meeting proposal as a final decision

Error: Ending a meeting discussion after only one proposal, without a clear summary such as 以上を踏まえ〜ということでまとめさせていただきます.

Correction: Always close a meeting-style exchange with an explicit decision summary, so all participants share the same understanding of the outcome.

Common Mistake — Mixing keigo levels within one message

Error: Opening a written reply with a fully humble keigo pattern and then closing with a casual sentence-final form.

Correction: Maintain one consistent register throughout a single message; abrupt shifts read as inconsistent rather than natural variation.

Grammar Review Practice

Model answers: (1) 確認するのではないかと思います。 or 確認できると考えられます。 as a spoken presentation-style rewrite. (2) The written regulatory style (〜ものとする) is least appropriate for a first-time written request to an unfamiliar department; that context calls for keigo request patterns from Chapter 6, since regulatory style is meant for notices and rules, not personal requests. (3) 〜と考えられます presents the speaker's own interpretation of evidence in a spoken presentation, while 〜ものとする states a rule or official stipulation in written regulatory text; they belong to different mediums and different communicative functions, not just different politeness levels.

Looking Ahead

いまできること — What you can do now

You can move confidently among four related but distinct professional registers: keigo requests and institutional interaction, spoken meeting negotiation, formal written documents and notices, and formal spoken presentations with hedging and source attribution. You can select the appropriate register for a given medium and audience, and you can politely defer an answer you cannot yet give. This closes Unit II.

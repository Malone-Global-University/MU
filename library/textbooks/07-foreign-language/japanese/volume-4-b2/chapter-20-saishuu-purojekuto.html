Bringing together abstraction, argument, source synthesis, keigo, meetings, documents, presentation, media and literary analysis, mediation, variation, domain discourse, research, and revision · Speak and Write Japanese: Professional, Academic, and Cultural Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Carry a Chapter 19 project proposal through to a complete, multi-source, publication-ready piece of Japanese writing.
  2. Conduct and report on a short interview using operational keigo appropriate to a professional relationship.
  3. Compare findings across at least two sources and explain agreement, disagreement, and unresolved questions between them.
  4. Deliver a formal oral presentation of the project and respond to unscripted questions.
  5. Produce an audience-adapted mediation of the project's findings for a general readership.
  6. Apply the full editing and revision process from Chapter 18 to a final draft before publication.
  7. Reflect in Japanese on which language systems from this volume were most useful to the project and why.
  8. Demonstrate integrated control of Volume IV's grammar, register, vocabulary, and script systems across listening, speaking, reading, and writing.

Communication Scenario

山本さん has approved Emma's one-page proposal from Chapter 19: a feature on the role of regional dialect in workplaces that are becoming more multilingual, built on an interview with a local company and two survey reports whose methods do not fully match. Over the next two weeks, Emma must resolve her open questions, conduct the interview in appropriately formal Japanese, compare her three sources honestly, draft the full feature, adapt a version for the magazine's general readership, revise everything against the Chapter 18 checklist, and present the finished project to 山本さん and a small editorial team — fielding their questions on the spot. This chapter follows Emma through each stage and asks the learner to complete a parallel project of their own choosing.

  1. The learner listens to Emma's interview with a workplace representative and to a short editorial-meeting exchange about the findings.
  2. The learner reads Emma's completed feature article and its general-audience companion summary.
  3. The learner conducts, records, or scripts a short interview of their own related to their project topic.
  4. The learner writes a complete, multi-source, source-attributed feature article and a general-audience adaptation.
  5. The learner delivers a formal presentation of their project and responds to questions from a partner or instructor.
  6. The learner reflects in writing on which grammar, vocabulary, register, and script systems from Volume IV were most central to completing the project.

もじ — Script and Terminology Focus

Script and Terminology Focus

This chapter assesses advanced kanji recognition and production, specialized terminology from the learner's chosen field, document formatting, and Japanese digital typography, rather than introducing new characters. No new kanji are activated in Chapter 20.

Script Strategy — Formatting a finished, publication-ready document

A finished feature article should follow the same furigana and typography conventions established across Chapters 17 through 19: kanji consolidated earlier in the volume carry no furigana, new or rare readings receive furigana at first mention only, and headings never contain an unglossed reading a target reader could not recover. When two versions of the same piece exist — a specialist feature and a general-audience summary, as in Chapter 19 — each version's furigana, terminology, and heading length should be checked separately, since a single formatting pass is not sufficient for two different readerships.

れんしゅう — Practice: Before submitting your final project, list every kanji compound in your draft that is specific to your project's field (not general-purpose Volume IV vocabulary), and confirm that each one carries furigana on first mention only if it is genuinely rare or unscheduled.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Presenting and fielding questions aloud

A formal presentation followed by a question-and-answer session places different demands on delivery than reading a prepared script. During the prepared portion, maintain steady phrase-level pitch and clear sentence-final falls to mark completed points, as practiced since Chapter 9. During 質疑応答, a brief pause before answering — rather than an immediate rushed response — is both a natural processing strategy and a mark of a considered answer; it does not need to be filled with hesitation sounds. Keep mora timing, vowel length, and moraic ん steady under the added pressure of unscripted speech, and treat any pitch-accent notation you consult while preparing as a reference tool, not a required marking.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter authorizes the integration of every major system activated across Volume IV: advanced explanation and abstraction (Ch. 2), logic and organization (Ch. 3), source reading and attribution (Ch. 4), operational keigo (Ch. 6), meeting and negotiation language (Ch. 7), formal written style (Ch. 8), presentation and Q&A structures (Ch. 9), literary and media interpretation (Ch. 11–12), mediation and audience adaptation (Ch. 13, 19), labeled variation (Ch. 14), specialized domain discourse (Ch. 16), research language (Ch. 17), and revision operations (Ch. 18). No single new grammar point is introduced; the four patterns below specifically support comparing sources and closing out the project.

1. Reporting agreement and divergence: 〜と一致する / 〜と食い違う

Purpose: to state precisely how two or more sources relate to each other, extending Chapter 4's attribution language.

  • 田中さんの話は、調査報告書Aの結果とおおむね一致している。What Tanaka-san said largely agrees with the findings of Survey Report A.〜と一致する — stating agreement between sources
  • しかし、調査報告書Bの数字は、報告書Aと食い違う部分がある。However, some of Report B's figures diverge from Report A.〜と食い違う — naming a specific divergence

2. Drawing a conclusion from combined evidence: 〜ことから、〜と結論づけられる

Purpose: to move from compared evidence to a stated conclusion, connecting Chapter 3's logical connectors to Chapter 17's research language.

  • 三つの資料を総合的に見ると、若い世代の方言使用は減少しているが、職場では見直されつつあることから、単純な衰退とは結論づけられない。Taking all three sources together, dialect use among younger people is declining, but since it is being reconsidered in workplaces, it cannot simply be concluded to be in decline.〜ことから、〜と結論づけられる — evidence-based conclusion with appropriate caution

3. Naming a remaining question: 今後の課題として〜が挙げられる

Purpose: to close a project honestly by naming what remains unresolved, extending Chapter 19's 未解決の点.

  • 今後の課題として、より広い地域を対象とした調査の必要性が挙げられる。As a task for the future, there is a need for research covering a wider range of regions.今後の課題として〜が挙げられる — closing with a forward-looking open question

4. Reflecting on the process: 〜を振り返ると

Purpose: to look back on the project and identify which language systems were most useful, supporting the chapter's reflection task.

  • 今回のプロジェクトを振り返ると、資料を比較する表現と、敬語を使ったインタビューが特に役に立った。Looking back on this project, the expressions for comparing sources and the keigo I used in the interview were especially useful.〜を振り返ると — reflective framing
Language Focus — Integration is not accumulation

Combining Volume IV's systems into one project does not mean using every pattern from every chapter in a single paragraph. A strong capstone project chooses the right tool for each moment: keigo for the interview itself, attribution language when reporting what sources said, comparison language when sources disagree, and plain analytical prose when drawing conclusions. Judging which system belongs where is itself part of C1 control, and it is exactly what the final performance task assesses.

Grammar Practice

  1. Write a sentence stating that two sources agree, using 〜と一致する.
  2. Write a sentence naming a divergence between two sources, using 〜と食い違う.
  3. Write a sentence drawing a cautious conclusion from combined evidence, using 〜ことから、〜と結論づけられる.
  4. Write a sentence naming an open question for future work, using 今後の課題として〜が挙げられる.
  5. Write a sentence reflecting on your own project, using 〜を振り返ると.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Emma presents her finished project to 山本さん and a colleague, 佐藤さん, in a small editorial meeting. Formal polite style, with operational keigo appropriate to presenting work to superiors.

エマ本日は、職場の多言語化と方言の役割についての記事をご紹介させていただきます。まず、取材の内容からご説明いたします。Today I'd like to present my article on workplace multilingualization and the role of dialect. First, let me explain what I found in my interview.
エマ田中さんへの聞き取り調査では、方言が職場の雰囲気を和らげる役割を果たしているというお話をいただきました。この点は、調査報告書Aの結果とも一致しております。In my interview with Tanaka-san, I was told that dialect plays a role in softening the workplace atmosphere. This point also agrees with the findings of Survey Report A.
佐藤なるほど。では、報告書Bとは食い違う部分もあったのでしょうか。I see. So were there also parts that diverged from Report B?
エマはい。報告書Bは調査方法が異なるため、若い世代の方言使用について、より否定的な数字が出ています。この違いについては、記事の中でも正直にお伝えしました。Yes. Because Report B used a different research method, it shows more negative figures regarding younger generations' dialect use. I've been honest about this difference in the article as well.
山本その姿勢はいいと思います。三つの資料を総合的に見て、どのように結論づけましたか。I think that approach is good. Taking all three sources together, how did you conclude things?
エマ単純な衰退とは結論づけず、職場という新しい文脈で方言が見直されつつある、とまとめました。今後の課題としては、より広い地域を対象とした調査の必要性が挙げられると考えております。Rather than concluding it's simply declining, I summarized that dialect is being reconsidered in the new context of the workplace. As a task for the future, I believe there's a need for research covering a wider range of regions.
山本読み応えのある記事になりましたね。要約版のほうも、専門用語がうまく言い換えられています。このまま掲載しましょう。This has become a substantial, rewarding article. The summary version also rephrases the technical terms well. Let's run it as is.
エマありがとうございます。今回のプロジェクトを振り返ると、敬語を使ったインタビューと、資料を比較する表現が特に役に立ちました。Thank you very much. Looking back on this project, the keigo I used in the interview and the expressions for comparing sources were especially useful.

山本さん approves the article for publication. Emma's project — interview, source comparison, feature article, general-audience summary, and honest reflection — is now complete.

ないようりかい — Comprehension check: On which point do Emma's interview and Report A agree? Why does Report B diverge, according to Emma? What does Emma name as a task for the future?

Writing Workshop

Complete your capstone project by producing all of the following, drawing on your Chapter 19 proposal and any interview or source material you have gathered.

  2. Compare your sources explicitly, naming at least one point of agreement using 〜と一致する and at least one point of divergence using 〜と食い違う.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Presenting finished work to a group

Presenting completed work to colleagues or supervisors in a Japanese professional or academic setting, as Emma does in the Model Dialogue, commonly involves acknowledging limitations and unresolved points directly, rather than presenting the work as flawless. A presenter who names an honest limitation, as Emma does regarding the two survey reports' differing methods, is generally seen as more credible than one who avoids the subject, echoing the proposal-stage norm introduced in Chapter 19.

Regional Note — Closing the loop on Chapter 14

Emma's finished project returns to the regional and social variation discussed in Chapter 14: her interview subject describes dialect use as something actively chosen for effect in certain workplace situations, rather than as a fixed marker of where someone is from. A capstone project that treats variation this way — as a resource speakers draw on deliberately — reflects the volume's broader caution against treating standard Japanese as neutral and regional Japanese as marked.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Forcing every chapter's pattern into the final project

Error: Trying to include at least one example of every grammar pattern from Chapters 1 through 19 in the final project, regardless of whether it fits naturally.

Correction: Choose the systems that genuinely serve the project's content and audience, and let some chapters' patterns appear only lightly or not at all: この記事では、資料比較と結論の表現を特に活用した。

Common Mistake — Treating source disagreement as a problem to hide

Error: Quietly dropping a source, or softening a real divergence between sources, to make the project's conclusion appear cleaner than the evidence supports.

Correction: Name the divergence directly and explain why it exists, as part of the project's credibility: 二つの報告書は調査方法が異なるため、数字が食い違っている。

Common Mistake — Skipping the reflection as an afterthought

Error: Writing a brief, generic reflection such as "this chapter was useful" without naming specific systems or explaining their role.

Correction: Name specific patterns and explain concretely how each one shaped the finished project: 敬語を使ったインタビューがなければ、田中さんから率直な話を引き出せなかったと思う。

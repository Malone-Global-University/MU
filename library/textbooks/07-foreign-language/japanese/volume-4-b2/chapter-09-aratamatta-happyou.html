改まった発表Formal Presentations

Advanced oral presentation and Q&A · Speak and Write Japanese: Professional, Academic, and Cultural Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Organize a short formal presentation into an opening, a body with clearly signaled sections, and a closing.
  2. Use formal transition phrases such as まず, 次に, 最後に, and 以上を踏まえ to guide a listener through a presentation.
  3. Hedge a claim appropriately using patterns such as 〜と考えられます and 〜のではないかと思います rather than overstating certainty.
  4. Attribute information to a source aloud, distinguishing the presenter's own view from reported information.
  5. Comment on a simple chart or set of figures in formal spoken Japanese.
  6. Frame an answer to an audience question politely, including when the presenter does not know the answer.
  7. Read and interpret a set of presentation slides that use terminology lists and Japanese typographic conventions for headings and bullet points.
  8. Deliver a short formal presentation and respond to at least one follow-up question.

Communication Scenario

With the notice posted and the application forms coming in, Emma has been asked to present a short update on the autumn recycling awareness event at the organization's monthly staff meeting. The presentation needs to cover why the event is being held, what has been prepared so far, what the current volunteer numbers look like, and what remains to be done before the event date. 田中さん has told Emma that the meeting audience will include people from other departments who were not part of the original planning meeting in Chapter 7, so the presentation needs to stand on its own, with a clear structure and time set aside for questions at the end. This chapter gives Emma the tools she needs: organizing a formal presentation into clear sections, using transition language to guide the audience, commenting on simple figures, hedging claims appropriately, and answering audience questions politely and confidently, even when she does not know the answer.

  1. The learner reads a set of presentation slides and identifies their structure and terminology.
  2. The learner listens to a short formal presentation and identifies its sections and main claims.
  3. The learner writes a short presentation outline using this chapter's transition and hedging language.
  4. The learner delivers a short formal presentation using the outline.
  5. The learner responds to at least one follow-up question, including a question the learner cannot fully answer.

Script Strategy — Reading a slide-style layout

Presentation slides in Japanese typically compress ideas into short noun phrases and headings rather than full sentences, relying on bold section titles, short bullet points, and occasional numbers or simple charts to carry the content. A slide heading such as 現状と課題 ("current situation and remaining issues") functions as a section label rather than a sentence and should be read as an organizational cue, similar to the numbered items in an official notice from Chapter 8. Learners should scan slide titles first to build a mental outline of the presentation before reading each bullet point closely. Furigana appears only on 発表, 見込み, 現状, and 課題 the first time each appears, matching this volume's exceptional-furigana policy.

Bulleted slide content in this chapter uses a simple centered dot ・ to mark each item, a common convention that differs from the numbered １． style used for regulatory lists in Chapter 8; both are legitimate depending on whether items form a sequence or a simple list.

れんしゅう — Practice: Before reading the presentation slides in Reading Practice, scan only the slide titles and predict, in English, what content each slide is likely to contain.

Pronunciation and Listening Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Presentation delivery prosody

Formal spoken presentations in Japanese tend to use a measured pace with a clear pause at each transition word, such as まず, 次に, or 最後に, signaling to the audience that a new section is beginning. This differs from the flatter, item-by-item read-aloud style of a formal notice in Chapter 8; a presentation is delivered with more variation in pitch and pace to hold audience attention while remaining more controlled than casual conversation. In this chapter's Listening Practice, listen for a slight pause and a small pitch reset at each transition word as a marker of a new section.

Continue maintaining mora timing, vowel length, moraic ん, and phrase-level intonation as in previous chapters. No new pitch-accent notation is introduced in this chapter.

Grammar: Presentation Language

This chapter activates formal transition phrases, hedging language, source attribution, data commentary, and answer framing, building on the formal written style introduced in Chapter 8.

Guiding the audience with transition phrases

Purpose: to signal the structure of a presentation clearly, so the audience can follow each new section without losing track of the overall organization.

Building Block — まず／次に／最後に＋[section content]
まず、イベントの目的についてご説明いたします。
まず ("first"), 次に ("next"), and 最後に ("finally") mark the beginning, continuation, and end of a presentation's main sections. They are placed at the start of a sentence and are usually followed by a brief pause when spoken aloud. This chapter also activates 以上を踏まえ ("taking the above into account"), which introduces a concluding statement that draws on everything presented so far.
以上を踏まえ、来月の準備状況について補足いたします。
Taking the above into account, I will add some supplementary information about next month's preparation status.

Hedging a claim with 〜と考えられます

Purpose: to present an interpretation or projection carefully, without overstating certainty, appropriate to formal spoken register.

Building Block — [plain form]＋と考えられます／のではないかと思います
現在の申請数から、当日は三十人程度の参加者が見込まれると考えられます。
〜と考えられます presents a conclusion as a reasonable interpretation rather than a certain fact, and is common when a presenter draws on data such as application numbers. 〜のではないかと思います is a softer, more personal hedge, often used for a presenter's own tentative view rather than a data-based projection. Both differ from a direct assertion with plain です, which would sound overconfident for a projection based on incomplete information.
締め切りまでにもう少し申請が増えるのではないかと思います。
I think perhaps a few more applications will come in before the deadline.

Attributing information with によると

Purpose: to distinguish information drawn from a source, such as an earlier report or another department, from the presenter's own view.

Building Block — [source]＋によると、〜そうです／とのことです
総務課の資料によると、昨年の参加者は二十五人だったそうです。
〜によると, recycled and extended from the source-reading patterns in Chapter 4, marks the source of the information that follows, and the sentence typically closes with a reported-information ending such as 〜そうです or 〜とのことです rather than a plain assertion. This distinguishes reported figures from the presenter's own projection, which uses 〜と考えられます instead.
田中さんによると、会場は十分に確保できているとのことです。
According to 田中さん, the venue has reportedly been sufficiently secured.

Framing an answer during Q&A

Language Focus — Answering a question politely

When answering an audience question, a presenter typically opens with a brief acknowledgment such as ご質問ありがとうございます before answering, and may add supplementary information with 補足いたしますと. When a presenter cannot fully answer a question, an honest, polite response such as その点については確認して後日お答えいたします ("regarding that point, I will confirm and respond at a later date") is far more appropriate than guessing or appearing evasive. Learners should practice this kind of honest, face-saving framing rather than avoiding questions they cannot answer.

Grammar Practice

  1. Identify the function of 次に in the following sentence and explain it in English: 次に、現状の課題についてご説明いたします。
  2. Rewrite the following overly certain statement as an appropriately hedged projection: 当日は三十人来ます。
  3. Identify whether the following sentence reports someone else's information or the speaker's own view, and explain how you know: 田中さんによると、会場の予約は済んでいるそうです。
  4. Write one polite way to respond to an audience question you cannot fully answer, using the pattern modeled above.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Emma delivers a short update at the monthly staff meeting and takes a question from a colleague in another department.

エマ それでは、秋のリサイクル啓発イベントについて発表いたします。まず、イベントの目的についてご説明いたします。 Now then, I will give a presentation about the autumn recycling awareness event. First, I will explain the purpose of the event.
エマ 次に、現状についてです。現在、十五人のボランティアから申請書をいただいており、締め切りまでにもう少し増えるのではないかと思います。 Next, the current situation. At present, we have received application forms from fifteen volunteers, and I think a few more will come in before the deadline.
エマ 最後に、課題についてです。会場の確保は済んでおりますが、当日の係員の人数はまだ検討中です。以上を踏まえ、来週までに係員の割り当てを決定いたします。 Finally, regarding remaining issues. Securing the venue is complete, but the number of staff members needed on the day is still under consideration. Taking the above into account, I will decide on staff assignments by next week.
田中さんが手を挙げる。
同僚 質問してもよろしいでしょうか。当日雨が降った場合はどうなりますか。 May I ask a question? What happens if it rains on the day of the event?
エマ ご質問ありがとうございます。その点については、まだ担当部署と確認しておりませんので、確認して後日お答えいたします。 Thank you for your question. Regarding that point, I have not yet confirmed with the department in charge, so I will confirm and respond at a later date.

チェックリスト — Editing checklist:

  • My outline has a clear opening naming the topic.
  • Each body section is introduced with a transition word.
  • At least one claim is appropriately hedged rather than stated as certain.
  • At least one remaining issue is named clearly.
  • The closing uses 以上を踏まえ and states a next step.
  • I have checked kanji, particles, formal predicate forms, and register consistency throughout.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Why hedging is not evasiveness

Learners sometimes read hedged Japanese presentation language, such as 〜と考えられます or a deferred answer during Q&A, as a sign that the speaker lacks confidence or is avoiding the question. In professional and academic Japanese presentation contexts, careful hedging of projections and honest deferral of unconfirmed information are usually read as signs of competence and reliability rather than weakness: a presenter who states an uncertain figure as fact, or who guesses at an answer rather than confirming it, is generally seen as less trustworthy than one who hedges appropriately and follows up. This expectation exists in many professional and academic settings beyond Japanese-speaking contexts as well, though the specific language used to hedge differs.

Regional Note — Presentation conventions vary by field and organization

The exact structure, slide density, and level of formality expected in a presentation can vary by industry, academic discipline, and individual organization; some workplaces favor dense, text-heavy slides, while others favor minimal slides with more spoken explanation. The patterns in this chapter are broadly usable defaults for a general institutional status update, not the only acceptable format.

Language Note — Building on Chapter 8's written style

The formal nominalization patterns from Chapter 8, such as 〜こと and 〜ものとする, belong to written regulatory style and are not typically spoken aloud in a presentation. Spoken formal presentation language instead favors the transition, hedging, and attribution patterns introduced in this chapter, delivered with the operational keigo from Chapter 6. Recognizing which register belongs to which medium is part of controlling formal Japanese as a whole system.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Stating a projection as a certain fact

Error: 当日は三十人来ます。 used to describe an expected, not yet confirmed, number of attendees.

Correction: 当日は三十人程度になると考えられます。 — an unconfirmed projection should be hedged rather than presented as settled fact.

Common Mistake — Guessing at an answer instead of deferring it

Error: Answering an unexpected question with an invented or uncertain guess presented as fact, such as たぶん大丈夫だと思います for a question the presenter has not actually checked.

Correction: その点については確認して後日お答えいたします。 — when information has not been confirmed, defer the answer honestly rather than guessing.

Common Mistake — Omitting transition words between sections

Error: Moving directly from one topic to the next with no signal, leaving the audience unsure whether a new section has begun.

Correction: Mark each new section clearly with まず, 次に, or 最後に — formal presentations rely on explicit verbal signposting, unlike casual conversation.

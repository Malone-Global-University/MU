B2 consolidation, accuracy audit, and advanced workflow · Speak and Write Japanese: Professional, Academic, and Cultural Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Describe your own Japanese abilities and goals using vocabulary for self-assessment, kanji strategy, source management, and discourse planning.
  2. Audit a piece of your own Volume III-level Japanese for accuracy in particles, predicate forms, register consistency, and clause connection.
  3. Identify, without yet producing, the passive, causative, and keigo forms your own writing will need to develop across Volume IV.
  4. Distinguish active production from receptive recognition when reviewing your own language systems.
  5. Read an intermediate-length reflective text with reduced furigana support and identify its main organizational structure.
  6. Plan a personal kanji-learning and source-reading strategy appropriate to advanced study.
  7. Discuss your language-learning goals with a partner or instructor, using consolidated B2 grammar accurately.
  8. Write a short, organized self-assessment paragraph that models the accuracy standard expected throughout this volume.

Communication Scenario

Six months have passed since Emma delivered her capstone presentation to the recycling group. She has decided to continue her Japanese study at a local community center that offers an advanced discussion course, and today is the orientation session. Before the course begins, the instructor asks each learner to complete a short self-assessment: what can you already do in Japanese, where do your accuracy gaps still show up, and what do you want to be able to do by the end of the course? Emma reviews a paragraph she wrote for her Chapter 20 project, checks it for particle and register accuracy, and talks with another learner, 佐藤さん, about their shared goals. This chapter walks through that orientation process and gives you the same tools to audit your own Japanese and set a clear direction for Volume IV.

  1. The learner writes a short self-assessment describing current strengths, accuracy gaps, and goals.
  2. The learner audits a short passage of their own prior writing for particle, predicate, and register accuracy.
  3. The learner identifies which Volume IV systems (keigo, passive, causative, formal writing) they currently only recognize rather than produce.
  4. The learner discusses their goals with a partner using accurate, consolidated B2 grammar.
  5. The learner reads a short reflective text about starting an advanced course and identifies its structure.

Pronunciation and Listening Focus

Pronunciation Focus — A diagnostic self-check

Before beginning new pronunciation work in this volume, record yourself reading a short passage aloud — the Reading Practice passage later in this chapter works well — and listen back for mora timing, vowel length, moraic ん, and phrase-level intonation, the same priorities that have applied since Volume I.

This is a diagnostic check, not a new skill. Its purpose is to identify which of these ordinary features still need attention as you move toward more demanding presentation and formal-speech contexts later in this volume.

Grammar: B2 Accuracy Audit

Chapter 1 introduces no new grammar system. Its grammar work is an audit of everything consolidated by the end of Volume III: particles, predicate forms, register consistency, clause connection, and conditionals. This section also previews, for recognition only, several forms that will become productive later in this volume: expanded keigo, passive and causative forms in professional contexts, and formal written style. You are not expected to produce these yet.

Auditing your own Japanese

Purpose: to check a piece of your own writing against the accuracy standards you have already learned, rather than to learn something new.

Building Block — A four-point audit checklist
助詞 → 述語の形 → 文体の一貫性 → 文と文のつながり
Particles, then predicate form, then register consistency, then clause connection — check your own writing in this order, one pass per category.
この文章は敬語ではなく、丁寧体で書かれているが、最後の文だけ普通体になっている。
This passage is written in polite style rather than keigo, but only the final sentence has slipped into plain style.

Recognizing what comes next (recognition only)

Purpose: to notice, without yet producing, the systems that Chapters 6 through 9 will activate for production — operational keigo, professional passive and causative forms, and formal written grammar.

  • こちらでお待ちいただけますか。Could you wait here? (respectful request form)receptive keigo — Ch. 6
  • この規則は、来月から適用されるものとする。This rule shall be applied starting next month. (formal written style)receptive formal grammar — Ch. 8

If you can read and roughly understand these two sentences, you are exactly where this chapter expects you to be. If you cannot yet produce sentences like them yourself, that is also expected — production of these systems begins later in the volume.

Language Focus — Audit before expansion

Volume IV will ask you to control more demanding registers and more complex grammar. Before adding anything new, this chapter asks you to make sure the B2 systems underneath are solid, since keigo, passive, and formal writing all depend on accurate particles, predicate forms, and register control to sound natural rather than mechanical.

Audit Practice

  1. Take one paragraph of your own past writing and underline every particle. Confirm each one is the particle you intended.
  2. Circle every predicate and confirm its form (polite, plain, past, negative) matches your intended register.
  3. Mark any sentence that breaks register consistency with the rest of the paragraph.
  4. Draw an arrow between any two sentences whose logical connection is unclear, and add or revise a connector if needed.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Emma and 佐藤さん, another learner, talk during the orientation session for the advanced discussion course.

佐藤さん エマさんは、これまでどんな勉強をしてきましたか。 Emma, what kind of studying have you done up to now?
エマ 説明したり、意見を言ったりすることはできるようになりましたが、まだ敬語には自信がありません。今回、自己評価をしてみて、そこが一番の課題だと分かりました。 I've become able to explain things and give opinions, but I still don't feel confident with keigo. When I did my self-assessment, I realized that's my biggest issue.
佐藤さん 私も同じです。それに、資料を読むとき、専門的な言葉の出典を確認するのに時間がかかります。 Same for me. Also, when I read sources, it takes me time to check the origin of specialized terms.
エマ じゃあ、二人とも、漢字の戦略と敬語の練習を目標に立てましょうか。 Then shall we both set kanji strategy and keigo practice as our goals?
佐藤さん いいですね。まずは、今の自分の日本語を正確に見直すことから始めましょう。 Sounds good. Let's start by carefully reviewing our current Japanese for accuracy.

  2. What challenge does 佐藤さん mention about reading sources?
  3. What two goals do Emma and 佐藤さん agree to set together?

Writing Workshop

In this workshop you will complete your own self-assessment paragraph and audit it for accuracy, modeling the standard this volume expects throughout.

  1. Draft your self-assessment: in three to five sentences, describe what you can already do in Japanese, one accuracy challenge, and one goal for this volume, using the chapter's core vocabulary.
  2. Run the four-point audit from the Grammar section: particles, predicate form, register consistency, and clause connection.
  3. Check your kanji against the personal audit list you built in the Script and Kanji Focus section, adding furigana only where you genuinely remain unsure.
  4. Read your paragraph aloud once, checking mora timing, vowel length, and phrase-level intonation.
  5. Revise once more for anything the audit revealed.

チェックリスト — Editing checklist:

  • My paragraph names one strength, one challenge, and one goal.
  • I have checked every particle in my paragraph.
  • I have checked every predicate form for accuracy.
  • My paragraph maintains one consistent register from start to finish.
  • I have checked the connections between my sentences.
  • I have checked my kanji against my personal audit list.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Self-assessment as a starting practice

Many Japanese-language courses, workplaces, and study groups begin a new stage with some form of structured self-reflection, whether written or spoken. This is not unique to Japanese-language education, but the practice of naming a concrete 課題 and a concrete 目標 before beginning new work, as Emma and 佐藤さん do here, is a widely valued habit in many Japanese educational and professional settings.

Regional Note — Variation in how goals are discussed

How openly learners or colleagues discuss personal weaknesses, and how directly a goal is stated aloud versus written privately, can vary by institution, workplace, generation, and individual personality. The pattern in this chapter's dialogue reflects one common classroom approach rather than a single universal norm.

Language Note — Recognition is real progress

Being able to read and roughly understand the two recognition-only sentences in the Grammar section, even without being able to produce similar sentences yourself yet, is genuine progress. Distinguishing what you can recognize from what you can produce, as this chapter asks you to do, is itself an advanced language-learning skill.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Trying to produce keigo before it is active

Error: Attempting to write your self-assessment entirely in unpracticed keigo forms, guessing at patterns you have only just seen in this chapter.

Correction: 説明することができるようになりました。 — write this chapter's tasks in the consolidated polite style you already control; full keigo production begins in Chapter 6.

Common Mistake — Treating the audit as a grammar test rather than a self-check

Error: Rewriting an old paragraph from scratch instead of checking it line by line for the four audit categories.

Correction: Work through the four-point checklist in order on your existing draft, marking specific words and sentences rather than starting over.

Common Mistake — Vague, unmeasurable goals

Error: *もっと日本語が上手になりたいです。 is too vague to act on or check later.

Correction: 敬語を練習して、来月の発表で使ってみたいです。 — state a specific, checkable goal, as Emma does in the Reading Practice passage.

Looking Ahead

いまできること — What you can do now

You can describe your own Japanese abilities, challenges, and goals using accurate vocabulary for self-assessment and planning. You can audit a piece of your own writing for particle, predicate, and register accuracy, and you can distinguish grammar you merely recognize from grammar you can confidently produce. This audit is the foundation for everything Volume IV will build.

第二章へContinuing to Chapter 2

Chapter 2, 高度な説明 (Advanced Explanation), builds directly on this audit. It introduces abstract and technical explanation using patterns such as とは, というのは, において, and に関して, moving from the everyday explanation you consolidated in Volume III toward the more abstract, definition-based explanation that professional and academic Japanese requires.

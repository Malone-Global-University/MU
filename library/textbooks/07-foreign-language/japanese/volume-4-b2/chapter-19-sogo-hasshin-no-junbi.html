総合発信の準備Preparing Advanced Publication

Project planning, audience definition, source review, and adaptation · Speak and Write Japanese: Professional, Academic, and Cultural Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Define the audience, purpose, and format of a public-facing piece of writing using constructions such as 〜を対象とする and 〜向けに書く.
  2. Plan a multi-source project by organizing sources, findings, and open questions with attribution and comparison language from earlier chapters.
  3. Adapt the same underlying content for two different audiences, adjusting register, terminology, and explanation depth.
  4. Integrate advanced explanation, source-based argument, keigo, and revision systems from Chapters 2 through 18 into a single connected project.
  5. Explain to a partner or instructor how a specific choice in your draft was shaped by audience or purpose.
  6. Read a project proposal and evaluate whether its plan matches its stated audience and purpose.
  7. Draft the first version of your Chapter 20 final project proposal.
  8. Apply a full pre-publication checklist covering content, structure, register, and accuracy.

Communication Scenario

Emma's revised "くらしと専門知識" column on 田中さん's dialect-use research has been approved, and 山本さん asks her to prepare two more things before the issue closes: a short version of the same material for the magazine's website summary page, aimed at a general audience skimming headlines, and a one-page proposal for her next piece — a longer feature that will draw on several sources rather than a single interview. Emma has never had to plan a multi-source project or rewrite the same content for two different audiences at once, so this chapter walks through both tasks, building directly toward the final project she will complete in Chapter 20.

  1. The learner listens to an editor explaining the difference between a full article and a website summary aimed at a different audience.
  2. The learner reads a project proposal and evaluates whether the plan fits its stated audience and purpose.
  3. The learner adapts a paragraph of existing writing for a new, less specialized audience.
  4. The learner drafts a one-page proposal for their own Chapter 20 final project, naming a topic, audience, purpose, and planned sources.
  5. The learner discusses their proposal with a partner or instructor and refines it based on feedback.

Script Strategy — Ruby and layout choices for a publication-ready page

When a piece of writing moves from a private draft toward something intended for readers, ruby and layout decisions become part of the final product rather than a private study aid. A general-audience website summary typically keeps furigana on any kanji outside the most common everyday set, since a skimming reader will not stop to look up an unfamiliar reading; a specialist feature article, by contrast, can rely on its readers' background knowledge and use furigana only for names and genuinely rare readings, exactly as this textbook has done since 推敲 in Chapter 18. Headings, in particular, should never carry an unglossed reading a general reader could not recover, since a heading is often the only text a skimming reader sees before deciding whether to continue.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Reading the same content aloud for two audiences

Presenters often shift pace and emphasis, not just vocabulary, when explaining the same material to a general audience instead of a specialist one: pauses after key terms grow slightly longer, and sentence-final intonation tends to fall more clearly to signal that a point is complete before moving on. Read the sample proposal excerpt in the Reading Practice section aloud twice: once as if briefing a colleague who already knows the background, and once as if explaining it to a new listener for the first time. Keep mora timing, vowel length, and moraic ん steady in both readings; only pacing, emphasis, and pause placement should change. Treat any pitch-accent notation as a reference tool rather than a required marking, as in earlier chapters.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter authorizes the integration of advanced explanation, source use, formal register, mediation, and revision into a single connected project. No new clause type is introduced; the focus is combining Chapters 2, 4, 6, 13, and 18's systems to plan, adapt, and finalize a piece of writing for a specific audience.

1. Naming an audience and purpose: 〜を対象とする / 〜向けに書く

Purpose: to state clearly who a piece of writing is for and why, at the planning stage.

  • このコラムは、専門知識のない一般の読者を対象としている。This column is aimed at general readers without specialized knowledge.〜を対象とする — naming the audience
  • 同じ内容でも、専門家向けに書く場合と、一般読者向けに書く場合では、言葉の選び方が変わる。Even with the same content, word choice changes depending on whether you're writing for specialists or general readers.〜向けに書く — contrasting two audiences

2. Planning across multiple sources: 〜をもとに / 複数の資料を比較する

Purpose: to describe how several sources will be used together in a project, extending the attribution language from Chapter 4.

  • 今回の企画は、田中さんへのインタビューと、二つの調査報告書をもとに構成する予定です。I'm planning to build this project on the interview with Tanaka-san and two survey reports.〜をもとに — grounding a plan in sources
  • 複数の資料を比較すると、若い世代の方言使用について、意見が分かれていることが分かる。Comparing multiple sources shows that opinions differ on younger generations' dialect use.複数の資料を比較する — source comparison as project method

3. Rephrasing for a different audience: 〜を分かりやすく言い換えると / 専門用語を避ける

Purpose: to adapt specialist content into more accessible language without changing its meaning.

  • 「言語変化」という専門用語を分かりやすく言い換えると、「言葉が時間とともに変わっていくこと」となる。To put the technical term "language change" more simply, it means "language changing over time."分かりやすく言い換える — accessible rephrasing
  • 要約版では専門用語をできるだけ避け、必要な場合は簡単な説明を加える。In the summary version, avoid technical terms where possible, and add a simple explanation when one is necessary.専門用語を避ける — an explicit adaptation strategy

4. Preparing for publication: 公開する前に / 最終確認をする

Purpose: to describe the final steps before a piece becomes public, tying revision language from Chapter 18 to the publication stage.

  • 公開する前に、事実関係と敬語の使い方をもう一度確認してください。Before publishing, please check the facts and the use of keigo one more time.公開する前に — sequencing final checks
  • 最終確認では、誤変換だけでなく、読者層に合った文体になっているかも見る。The final check looks not only for conversion errors but also for whether the style suits the target readership.最終確認 — combining accuracy and audience checks
Language Focus — Same facts, different explanatory depth

Adapting content for a new audience is not primarily about shortening sentences or using easier words at random. It is a decision about which information a given reader needs in order to follow the point, and which information can be assumed, summarized, or left out entirely. A specialist version of Emma's column can name a specific survey and cite its year and sample size; a general-audience summary version may only need to say that "research shows" a pattern exists. Both versions must remain accurate — the difference is in explanatory depth and terminology, not in truthfulness.

  2. Write a sentence describing a plan built on multiple sources using 〜をもとに.
  3. Write a sentence rephrasing a technical term for a general audience using 分かりやすく言い換えると.
  4. Write a sentence describing a final pre-publication check using 公開する前に and 最終確認.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Emma meets with 山本さん to discuss the website summary version of her column and to propose her next project. Polite style throughout, appropriate to a professional working relationship.

山本コラムの記事、お疲れさまでした。次に、ウェブサイト用の要約版を作ってもらえますか。こちらは読者層が違うので、少し書き分ける必要があります。Good work on the column article. Next, could you make a summary version for the website? The readership is different there, so you'll need to write it a bit differently.
エマ分かりました。ウェブサイトのほうは、一般の読者を対象としていますよね。Understood. The website version is aimed at general readers, right?
山本そうです。専門用語はできるだけ避けて、分かりやすく言い換えてください。見出しも、記事とは別に、短くて分かりやすいものを考えてください。That's right. Avoid technical terms where possible and rephrase things simply. Please come up with a short, clear headline too, separate from the article's.
エマはい、やってみます。それから、次の企画についてもご相談したいことがあります。Yes, I'll try that. Also, there's something I'd like to discuss about my next project.
山本聞かせてください。Go ahead, I'm listening.
エマ今回は一つのインタビューだけでなく、複数の資料をもとに、もう少し長い記事を書きたいと考えています。田中さんへのインタビューに加えて、二つの調査報告書を比較しようと思っています。This time, I'm thinking of writing a somewhat longer article based on multiple sources rather than just one interview — in addition to the Tanaka-san interview, I'd like to compare two survey reports.
山本いいですね。構成案を一枚にまとめて、来週までに見せてもらえますか。未解決の点があれば、それも書いておいてください。Sounds good. Could you put together a one-page outline and show it to me by next week? If there are any open questions, note those too.
エマ承知しました。公開する前には、もちろん最終確認もお願いします。Understood. And of course, please do a final check before it's published.

Emma leaves the meeting with two tasks: adapt the column into a general-audience website summary with its own headline, and prepare a one-page proposal for a longer, multi-source feature by the following week.

企画書：多言語化する職場と方言の役割（案）

この企画は、専門知識のない一般読者を対象とした雑誌のコラムである。目的は、多言語化が進む職場で方言がどのような役割を果たしているかを、複数の資料をもとに紹介することである。資料としては、地元企業へのインタビュー一件と、過去五年間の関連調査報告書二件を使用する予定である。構成案としては、まず職場の多言語化という背景を分かりやすく説明し、次にインタビューの内容を紹介し、最後に調査報告書の結果と比較する。未解決の点としては、インタビュー対象者がまだ決まっていないことと、二つの調査報告書の調査方法が異なるため、単純に比較できるかどうか確認が必要なことが挙げられる。

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Proposing a project before writing it

In many Japanese professional and academic settings, a short written proposal — often called 企画書 in publishing or business contexts, or a research plan in academic ones — circulates for feedback before substantial writing begins, rather than after a full draft is complete. Naming open, unresolved questions honestly in such a proposal, as the sample proposal above does, is generally seen as a sign of careful planning rather than a weakness, since it lets a supervisor or editor help resolve them early.

Regional Note — Audience adaptation and the variation discussed in Chapter 14

Adapting the same content for different readerships connects back to Chapter 14's discussion of regional and social variation: a general-audience summary about workplace multilingualism, for example, may need to briefly explain a regional or community term that a specialist readership would already recognize without explanation. Deciding how much background a given audience needs is itself a form of the register and audience awareness this chapter develops.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Adapting vocabulary but not explanatory depth

Error: Rewriting a general-audience summary by simply replacing a few technical words with simpler ones, while keeping the same density of detail and assuming the same background knowledge as the specialist version.

Correction: Reconsider what the new reader actually needs to follow the point, and adjust the level of detail itself, not just the individual words: この調査では、方言を使う若者が減っていることが分かった。

Common Mistake — A proposal with no open questions

Error: Writing a proposal that presents every detail as already settled, even when the interview subject has not been confirmed or the sources have not yet been fully compared.

Correction: Name unresolved points honestly and specifically, as part of the plan rather than as a weakness to hide: インタビュー対象者はまだ決まっていない。

Common Mistake — Skipping the final check because a piece was already revised once

Error: Assuming that because a draft was thoroughly edited in Chapter 18, no further check is needed before an adapted or newly proposed version is shared.

Correction: Apply a final check to every version separately, since adaptation and new drafting can introduce new errors: 要約版も、公開する前に最終確認をしてください。

高度な説明Advanced Explanation

Definition, classification, and abstraction · Speak and Write Japanese: Professional, Academic, and Cultural Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Define and classify abstract and technical concepts using とは and というのは.
  2. Situate a discussion within a system, institution, or field using において and に関して.
  3. Build layered definitions that move from a general category to specific distinguishing features.
  4. Use abstract nominalization to turn actions and qualities into discussable topics.
  5. Read a short explanatory passage about a social or institutional concept and identify its definitional structure.
  6. Listen to a definition-style explanation and extract the category, distinguishing features, and examples given.
  7. Discuss an abstract or technical concept from your own field or interest with a partner, offering a definition and inviting clarification.
  8. Write a short, organized explanatory paragraph that defines a concept, situates it within a broader system, and gives at least one example.

Communication Scenario

In the advanced discussion course, the instructor has asked each learner to prepare a short explanation of a concept from their own field, background, or interest, to be shared with the group before it is discussed further later in the unit. Emma decides to explain what she means by "community recycling program," since she worked on this topic for her Volume III capstone project, but she quickly realizes that her Volume III Japanese does not yet let her define a concept clearly, distinguish it from related ideas, or situate it within a larger system such as local government or environmental policy. 佐藤さん, meanwhile, wants to explain a concept from his own work in a similar way. This chapter introduces the grammar of definition and abstraction that Emma and 佐藤さん need: how to say what something is, what it is not, where it belongs within a larger system, and how it relates to other concepts.

  1. The learner defines an abstract or technical concept in layered form: general category, then distinguishing features.
  2. The learner situates a concept within a system, institution, or field using において and に関して.
  3. The learner distinguishes a concept from a closely related one using というのは to clarify or restate.
  4. The learner reads a short explanatory passage and identifies its definitional structure.
  5. The learner explains a concept from their own field or interest to a partner and answers a clarifying question about it.

Script Strategy — Okurigana in explanatory verbs

Verbs like 位置づける carry meaning across both the kanji base and the okurigana ending, and the okurigana changes with each form: 位置づける (dictionary form), 位置づけられる (passive, "is positioned as"), 位置づけられている (result state, "is positioned as," ongoing). Reading only the kanji 位置 without attention to the okurigana will not tell you which of these forms is in front of you.

Pronunciation and Listening Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Pausing at definitional boundaries

Definitional sentences built with とは and というのは often contain a longer topic phrase before the definition itself arrives, for example 地域リサイクル制度というのは、 followed by a pause and then the explanation. Practice placing a brief, natural pause after the topic phrase rather than rushing straight into the definition, since this pause is part of how the sentence signals its own structure to a listener.

Continue maintaining mora timing, vowel length, moraic ん, and phrase-level intonation as in previous chapters; this chapter adds attention to phrase-boundary pausing specifically at definitional junctures.

Grammar: Defining and Explaining Abstract Concepts

This chapter activates four connected tools for advanced explanation: とは and というのは for definition, において and に関して for situating a topic within a system or field, abstract nominalization for turning actions and qualities into discussable topics, and layered definitions that move from general category to specific distinguishing feature.

とは — Stating a formal definition

Purpose: to introduce a formal, dictionary-like definition of a term, typically followed by a category noun and copula.

Building Block — とは + category + ことである／だ
[概念] とは、[カテゴリー] のことである／だ。
とは marks the term being defined; the sentence typically closes with a category noun plus こと and a copula. This pattern reads as formal and written-style even in speech.
地域リサイクル制度とは、家庭から出るごみを分別・回収し、再利用可能な資源に変える地域の仕組みのことである。
A community recycling program is a local system that sorts and collects household waste and turns it into reusable resources.
  • 制度とは、社会の中で人々の行動を支えるために作られた仕組みのことだ。An institution is a system created within a society to support people's behavior.とは + category + のことだ
  • 現象とは、実際に観察できる出来事や状態のことである。A phenomenon is an event or state that can actually be observed.とは + category + のことである

というのは — Restating, clarifying, or explaining a reason

Purpose: to introduce a clarification of a term or statement, or to explain the reasoning behind something just said; less formal and more conversational than とは.

Building Block — というのは as clarification
[話題] というのは、[説明]。
というのは can restate a term more conversationally than とは, or it can introduce an explanation for a claim that was just made, functioning like "what I mean is" or "the reason is."
地域リサイクル制度というのは、簡単に言うと、ごみを資源に変えるための地域の取り組みです。
What I mean by "community recycling program" is, simply put, a local effort to turn waste into resources.
  • この制度は複雑だ。というのは、いくつもの要素が関係しているからだ。This system is complex. That's because several elements are involved.というのは introducing a reason

において and に関して — Situating a topic

Purpose: において marks the setting, field, or context in which something holds true; に関して marks the topic that a statement concerns, closer to "regarding" or "concerning."

ParticleFunctionExample
においてmarks the field, setting, or system a statement applies within地域社会において、この制度は重要な役割を果たしている。
Within local communities, this system plays an important role.
に関してmarks the topic a statement concerns; introduces further discussion of that topicこの制度に関して、いくつか説明したいことがある。
Regarding this system, there are a few things I would like to explain.

において situates a claim inside a domain; に関して introduces or narrows a topic for further comment. Both are more formal than plain topic-marking with は and are appropriate for the explanatory, academic-adjacent register this chapter targets.

Abstract nominalization

Purpose: to turn an action, quality, or clause into a noun phrase that can serve as the topic or subject of an explanatory sentence.

  • ごみを分別することが、この制度の中心的な要素である。Sorting waste is the central element of this system.verb + こと as nominalized subject
  • 資源を無駄にしないという考え方が、この制度の背景にある。The idea of not wasting resources lies behind this system.clause + という考え方 as nominalized topic

Nominalization with こと and structures like という考え方 let you discuss an action or idea itself as a topic, which is essential for definitional and classificatory explanation.

Layered definitions

Purpose: to build a complete definition in stages, moving from general category to specific distinguishing features, rather than offering one dense sentence.

Language Focus — Three-step definition structure

A clear advanced definition typically moves through three steps: (1) name the general category the concept belongs to, using とは or というのは; (2) add the distinguishing features that separate it from other members of that category, often using 特徴 or 要素; (3) situate the concept within a broader system or field using において or に関して. Chapter 1's audit skills apply directly here: check that each step uses consistent register and that the sentences connect logically.

地域リサイクル制度とは、地域の仕組みのことである。この制度の特徴は、家庭ごみを種類ごとに分別する点にある。地域社会において、この制度は資源の無駄を減らす重要な役割を果たしている。

A community recycling program is a local system. Its distinguishing feature is that it sorts household waste by type. Within local communities, this system plays an important role in reducing wasted resources.

Grammar Practice

  1. Write a formal definition of a concept from your own field using とは + category + のことである.
  2. Rewrite the same definition more conversationally using というのは.
  3. Add one sentence situating the concept within a broader field or system using において or に関して.
  4. Add one sentence naming a distinguishing feature (特徴 or 要素) that separates your concept from a related one.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Emma explains her Volume III capstone topic to 佐藤さん before the group discussion.

佐藤さん エマさんは、どんな概念について説明する予定ですか。 Emma, what concept are you planning to explain?
エマ 地域リサイクル制度について話そうと思っています。地域リサイクル制度とは、家庭から出るごみを分別・回収し、資源に変える地域の仕組みのことです。 I'm thinking of talking about community recycling programs. A community recycling program is a local system that sorts and collects household waste and turns it into resources.
佐藤さん なるほど。それは、普通のごみ収集とはどう違うんですか。 I see. How is that different from ordinary garbage collection?
エマ というのは、ただ集めるだけでなく、分類して資源として再利用する点が特徴なんです。この制度は、環境政策において重要な役割を果たしています。 What I mean is, its distinguishing feature is that it doesn't just collect waste but classifies it and reuses it as a resource. This system plays an important role within environmental policy.
佐藤さん 面白いですね。私の分野に関しても、似たような制度があるので、後で説明します。 That's interesting. There's a similar system in my own field too, so I'll explain it later.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Definitional style in Japanese explanation

Formal definitions using とは and のことである appear frequently in Japanese reference materials, textbooks, official documents, and academic writing. Learning to recognize and produce this pattern gives you access to how many Japanese explanatory texts are actually structured, rather than relying on a looser, more conversational style throughout.

Regional Note — Variation in explanatory formality

How formal or conversational an explanation sounds can vary by setting, audience, and individual speaking style, not only by region. A conference presentation, a casual study-group conversation, and a written report may all define the same concept quite differently in register, even when the underlying content is identical.

Language Note — とは and というのは are not interchangeable

とは signals a formal, often written-style definition, while というのは more often introduces a clarification, restatement, or explanation of a reason in a more conversational register. Using とは in a casual conversation is not incorrect, but it can sound noticeably more formal than the surrounding speech; choose deliberately based on the register you intend.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Defining without a category noun

Error: *地域リサイクル制度とは、家庭のごみを集めて資源にする。 — this ends with a bare verb rather than a category noun, so it reads as an action description rather than a definition.

Correction: 地域リサイクル制度とは、家庭のごみを資源に変える地域の仕組みのことである。 — close the definition with a category noun (仕組み) plus のことである.

Common Mistake — Confusing において with に関して

Error: *この制度において、いくつか説明したいことがある。 — において does not naturally introduce "things I want to explain about" a topic.

Correction: この制度に関して、いくつか説明したいことがある。 — use に関して when introducing further discussion of a topic; reserve において for situating a claim within a field or setting.

Common Mistake — Stacking every tool into one dense sentence

Error: Cramming とは, というのは, において, and に関して into a single sentence to sound more advanced.

Correction: Follow the three-step layered structure from the Language Focus box: one sentence per step. Advanced Japanese explanation values clarity and structure over density.

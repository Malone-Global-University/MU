Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Define and classify an abstract concept using とは, というのは, において, and に関して.
  2. Build a short argument using したがって, 一方で, にもかかわらず, and 以上のことから.
  3. Attribute claims to named sources with によれば, report general characterizations with とされる, and state a reasoned conclusion with と考えられる.
  4. Combine definition, argument, and source attribution into one organized paragraph on a single topic.
  5. Read an integrated explanatory-argumentative text and identify its definitions, logical structure, and sources.
  6. Listen to a connected discussion and distinguish reported claims, general characterizations, and the speaker's own conclusions.
  7. Present and defend a short position that explains a concept, supports it with logical structure, and grounds it in named and general sources.
  8. Audit a piece of writing for particle accuracy, predicate form, register consistency, clause connection, and correct use of all three chapters' systems.

Communication Scenario

The study group's public presentation on the 地域リサイクル制度 is now three weeks away, and Emma, 佐藤さん, and their groupmates must merge everything they have built across Unit I into a single, coherent piece: Chapter 2's ability to define and classify the program itself, Chapter 3's ability to argue for a position with clear logical structure, and Chapter 4's ability to attribute claims honestly to named sources, general characterizations, and their own reasoned conclusions. Their instructor has asked each group member to produce one integrated written summary and one short spoken presentation that a general audience — not specialists — could follow from beginning to end, and to be ready to answer questions that test whether they can tell the difference between what a source says, what is commonly said, and what they themselves conclude. This chapter reviews and integrates all three chapters' tools and asks you to apply them together, exactly as Emma's group must, before moving on to Unit II's shift toward professional and institutional communication.

  1. The learner defines and classifies a concept clearly enough for a general audience to follow.
  2. The learner constructs a short argument with visible logical structure: claim, support, contrast or concession, and conclusion.
  3. The learner attributes claims accurately to named sources, general characterizations, and their own judgment, keeping the three distinct.
  4. The learner reads or listens to an integrated text and identifies its definitions, logical moves, and sources.
  5. The learner produces one written and one spoken integrated performance combining all three chapters' systems.

Grammar: Integrating Explanation, Logic, and Sources

This chapter does not introduce new grammar. It reviews and combines Chapter 2's definitional tools, Chapter 3's argument tools, and Chapter 4's attribution tools so that you can use all nine forms in a single organized text.

Chapter 2 review — Defining and classifying

Purpose: to introduce a concept, define it clearly, and situate it within a domain, before arguing about it or citing sources on it.

Building Block — [concept]とは／というのは、[definition]という意味だ。[domain]において／に関して、…
[概念]とは、[定義]。[分野]において、…
とは and というのは introduce a term for definition; において situates a claim within a field, place, or context; に関して introduces the topic a following statement concerns. Use these before moving into argument or source material, so your audience knows what you are discussing.
地域リサイクル制度とは、家庭ごみを分別し、再利用可能な資源として回収する仕組みのことだ。
The community recycling program is a system in which household waste is sorted and collected as reusable resources.

Chapter 3 review — Building an argument

Purpose: to move from a claim through supporting evidence, a contrasting or conceding point, and a conclusion.

Building Block — [claim]。したがって、[support]。一方で／にもかかわらず、[contrast]。以上のことから、[conclusion]。
[主張]。したがって、[根拠]。一方で、[反論または留保]。以上のことから、[結論]。
したがって marks a logical result; 一方で introduces a contrasting point without denying the main claim; にもかかわらず marks a concession despite an obstacle; 以上のことから signals that a conclusion follows from everything stated above. These connectors give your argument a visible shape.
この制度は資源の無駄を減らす。したがって、多くの自治体が導入を検討している。一方で、導入初期には費用がかかる。以上のことから、長期的な視点で評価する必要があると考えられる。
This system reduces the waste of resources. Therefore, many municipalities are considering introducing it. On the other hand, there are costs early in its introduction. From the above, it can be concluded that it needs to be evaluated from a long-term perspective.

Chapter 4 review — Attributing and weighing sources

Purpose: to keep what a named source says, what is generally said, and what the writer concludes clearly distinct.

Building Block — [source]によれば、[reported content]。[general claim]とされる。以上のことから、[writer's judgment]と考えられる。
[出典]によれば、[内容]。[一般的な見方]とされる。以上のことから、[書き手の判断]と考えられる。
によれば names a specific source; とされる reports a general characterization without one named source; と考えられる presents the writer's own reasoned conclusion. Never let these three collapse into one another.
〇〇市の報告書によれば、参加率は約六十パーセントに達している。この種の制度は導入初期に数字が変動しやすいとされる。以上のことから、現時点の数字だけで制度の成否を判断するのは早計だと考えられる。
According to the city's report, participation has reached about sixty percent. This kind of system is generally said to have fluctuating figures early on. From the above, it can be concluded that it would be premature to judge the program's success from the current figures alone.

Combining all three systems

Language Focus — One paragraph, three functions

A fully integrated Unit I paragraph typically moves through four stages: (1) define the concept using とは or というのは, adding において or に関して to situate it; (2) state a claim and support it using したがって, adding 一方で or にもかかわらず for balance; (3) ground the claim in at least one named source using によれば and one general characterization using とされる; and (4) close with your own conclusion using と考えられる, optionally preceded by 以上のことから. Not every sentence needs a connector, but the overall shape should be traceable from definition through argument to source-grounded conclusion.

地域リサイクル制度とは、家庭ごみを分別し、再利用可能な資源として回収する仕組みのことだ。したがって、この制度は資源の無駄を減らす効果があると考えられている。〇〇市の報告書（２０２５年）によれば、参加率は約六十パーセントに達している。一方で、この種の制度は導入初期に費用がかかりやすいとされる。にもかかわらず、複数の調査結果が同じ傾向を示していることから、以上のことから、この制度は長期的に地域にとって有益だと考えられる。

The community recycling program is a system in which household waste is sorted and collected as reusable resources. Therefore, this system is thought to have the effect of reducing the waste of resources. According to the city's 2025 report, participation has reached about sixty percent. On the other hand, this kind of system is generally said to tend to incur costs early in its introduction. Despite this, since multiple survey results show the same tendency, it can be concluded from the above that this system is beneficial for the community in the long term.

Grammar Practice

  1. Write one sentence defining a concept of your choice using とは or というのは.
  2. Write a short argument of three to four sentences using したがって, 一方で or にもかかわらず, and 以上のことから.
  3. Write one sentence attributing a claim using によれば, one using とされる, and one conclusion using と考えられる.
  4. Combine all of the above into one integrated paragraph of six to eight sentences on a single topic.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Emma and 佐藤さん rehearse Emma's integrated summary before the group presentation, checking that definition, argument, and source attribution all fit together clearly.

佐藤さん まず制度の説明から始めていますか。聞いている人が何の話か分からないまま主張に入ると、伝わりにくいですよ。 Are you starting with an explanation of the system first? If you move into your claim before listeners know what you're talking about, it's hard to follow.
エマ はい。「地域リサイクル制度とは、家庭ごみを分別し、資源として回収する仕組みのことだ」という定義から始めました。それから、この制度が資源の無駄を減らす、という主張に進みます。 Yes. I started with the definition, "The community recycling program is a system in which household waste is sorted and collected as a resource." Then I move on to the claim that this system reduces the waste of resources.
佐藤さん 主張の後に、反対の意見や制約も入れていますか。一方でとにもかかわらず、両方使えるといいですね。 After the claim, have you included an opposing view or a limitation? It would be good if you could use both 一方で and にもかかわらず.
エマ 一方で、導入初期には費用がかかりやすいとされる、という点を入れました。そのあとで、〇〇市の報告書によれば、参加率は六十パーセントに達している、というデータを出します。 I included the point that, on the other hand, costs tend to be high early in the introduction. After that, I present the data that, according to the city's report, participation has reached sixty percent.
佐藤さん 最後の結論は？と考えられるで終わっていますか。 And your final conclusion? Do you end with と考えられる?
エマ はい。「以上のことから、この制度は長期的に地域にとって有益だと考えられる」で締めくくっています。 Yes. I close with, "From the above, it can be concluded that this system is beneficial for the community in the long term."
佐藤さん それなら、定義、主張、根拠、出典、結論が全部つながっていますね。 In that case, your definition, claim, support, source, and conclusion are all connected.

ないようりかい — Comprehension questions:

  1. What definition does Emma begin with, and why does 佐藤さん say this matters?
  2. Which two contrast or concession words does Emma use, and what point does each introduce?
  3. How does Emma's presentation move from definition to claim to source to conclusion?

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Explaining before arguing

In many Japanese reports, presentations, and academic texts, a writer typically defines or situates a concept clearly before advancing a claim about it, rather than assuming the audience already shares the same understanding of the term. This ordering supports a general audience in following an argument, and it is a widely useful habit rather than a fixed rule that every genre follows identically.

Regional Note — Genre still shapes formality

As in Chapter 4, how formally a definition, argument, or source is presented differs across academic writing, government reports, journalism, and informal explanation, and also varies by institution and field. The integrated pattern in this chapter reflects a fairly formal, general-audience register; a specialist audience or a casual blog post might compress or omit some of these steps.

Language Note — Keeping nine forms distinct under pressure

When writing or speaking quickly under the pressure of a real presentation, learners sometimes let とされる and と考えられる blur together, or let したがって and 以上のことから do the same logical work twice. Slow down at the transition points in your own writing and confirm which of the nine forms each transition actually requires.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Arguing before defining

Error: Opening a paragraph directly with したがって、この制度は有益だ。 before the audience knows what "この制度" refers to.

Correction: 地域リサイクル制度とは、…という仕組みのことだ。したがって、この制度は有益だ。 — define the concept before building an argument about it.

Common Mistake — Skipping the contrast or concession step

Error: Moving directly from a claim supported by したがって to a conclusion with と考えられる, without acknowledging any contrasting point, which can make an argument sound one-sided.

Correction: Insert a 一方で or にもかかわらず clause between your claim and your conclusion, even briefly, to show that you have considered a limitation or opposing view.

Common Mistake — Ending without と考えられる after presenting sources

Error: Presenting によれば and とされる claims from sources but never stating a clear conclusion of one's own, leaving the paragraph without a final judgment.

Correction: Close with a sentence using と考えられる, ideally following 以上のことから, that states what the writer concludes from the evidence just presented.

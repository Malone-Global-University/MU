精密な文法運用Precise Grammar Control

Fine-grained clause and predicate control across modality, aspect, nominalization, embedded clauses, conditionals, and the passive/causative system · Speak and Write Japanese: Mastery, Specialization, and Expert Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Choose among と, ば, たら, and なら with precision, based on the exact relationship between condition and result.
  2. Distinguish fine shades of modality and stance, including はず, に違いない, かねない, and わけではない.
  3. Nominalize a clause with こと or の appropriately, choosing the form that fits the surrounding predicate and register.
  4. Embed a clause inside a noun phrase with the correct level of density for dense professional and academic prose.
  5. Distinguish related passive and causative-passive constructions and explain the shift in responsibility and perspective each one carries.
  6. Manage topic and focus deliberately across a paragraph, rather than sentence by sentence.
  7. Read a dense passage of professional or academic Japanese and identify which precision system each sentence relies on.
  8. Revise a piece of your own writing by replacing an approximate structure with the more precise structure the context actually requires.

Communication Scenario

エマ has begun drafting her first piece for 山本さん's magazine on regional and social language use, but her own diagnostic from Chapter 1 flagged exactly the systems this draft depends on most: the conditionals, and the fine modal distinctions that separate a cautious claim from an overstated one. When 山本さん reads her first draft, he does not tell her the Japanese is wrong — he tells her it is imprecise, and that at this level, imprecision changes what the sentence actually means. This chapter follows Emma as she revises that draft sentence by sentence, replacing approximate structures with exact ones, and asks the learner to do the same with a passage of their own.

  1. The learner listens to 山本さん explaining why one of Emma's sentences is imprecise rather than wrong.
  2. The learner reads Emma's revised paragraph alongside her first draft.
  3. The learner studies four precision systems: conditionals, modality/stance, nominalization and embedding, and passive/causative distinctions.
  4. The learner practices choosing the exact structure a context requires, rather than any structure that is merely acceptable.
  5. The learner revises a paragraph of their own writing using at least three of this chapter's precision systems.
  6. The learner explains, in Japanese, why one structure fits a given context better than a plausible alternative.

もじ — Script and Terminology Focus

Script and Terminology Focus

This chapter introduces no new kanji. Its script focus is on okurigana and kanji precision in the advanced predicates this chapter activates, since a single mistaken okurigana ending can silently change a predicate's meaning at this level.

Script Strategy — Okurigana precision in compound predicates

Several of this chapter's structures attach directly to a verb stem or add an auxiliary ending, and a small okurigana error can obscure which structure is intended. Compare 含みを持たせる (to leave an implication) with an incorrectly spelled variant that drops or misplaces okurigana — a reader can no longer tell whether the verb is transitive, causative, or something else entirely. When proofreading dense professional writing, check okurigana on every compound predicate you have modified, not only on words you consider new.

れんしゅう — Practice: Choose five compound predicates from this chapter's Grammar and Sentence Patterns section. Write each one twice — once with correct okurigana, and once with a plausible okurigana error a learner might make — and note what meaning or grammatical category the error would suggest.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Prosody and precision in spoken argument

When a spoken argument depends on a fine modal distinction — for instance, the difference between 〜はずだ and 〜に違いない — phrase-level pitch and pacing can either support or undercut that distinction. A flat, rushed delivery can make a careful hedge sound like a flat assertion, and the reverse. As you practice this chapter's spoken tasks, read each sentence aloud slowly enough that the modal ending receives its own clear phrase-final contour, while keeping mora timing, vowel length, and moraic ん steady throughout.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter authorizes fine-grained distinctions in four interconnected systems: conditionals, modality and stance, nominalization and clause embedding, and passive/causative constructions. None of these systems is new — all were introduced or developed in earlier volumes. What is new is the expectation that you choose among near-synonymous options deliberately, based on exactly what the context requires.

1. Choosing among と, ば, たら, and なら with precision

Purpose: to select the conditional whose nuance — general truth, evaluative condition, sequential result, or premise-based judgment — actually matches the intended meaning.

FormCore NuanceTypical Use in Dense Prose
〜とa general or mechanical result that follows automatically気温が下がると、方言の使用が増える傾向が見られる。
〜ばan evaluative condition, often with a value judgment attached政策を見直せば、状況は改善するはずだ。
〜たらa specific, sequential condition, often one-time or hypothetical調査が終わったら、結果を報告書にまとめる。
〜ならa premise the speaker is reacting to or building on, not a cause-effect chainその地域の言葉を研究するなら、まず現地調査が欠かせない。
  • 条件が同じなら、結果も同じになるとは限らない。Even if the conditions are the same, it isn't necessarily the case that the results will be the same.〜なら — reacting to a stated premise, not describing a mechanical sequence

2. Fine modal distinctions: はず / に違いない / かねない / わけではない

Purpose: to mark exactly how confident, how justified, and how qualified a claim is, rather than defaulting to a flat statement or an all-purpose hedge.

  • この結果は、先行研究と一致するはずだ。This result should be consistent with prior research (based on a logical expectation).〜はずだ — expectation grounded in reasoning, not direct evidence
  • この表現は、若い世代には通じないに違いない。This expression is surely incomprehensible to the younger generation (strong inference).〜に違いない — confident inference approaching certainty
  • この言い方は、相手を怒らせかねない。This way of putting it could well anger the other person.〜かねない — a negative possibility the speaker is warning against
  • 方言を使わないからといって、その地域に愛着がないわけではない。Just because someone doesn't use the dialect doesn't mean they lack attachment to the region.〜わけではない — denying an overgeneralized implication, not the fact itself
Language Focus — はず versus に違いない

はず and に違いない are frequently taught as near-synonyms, but at this level the distinction matters. はず expresses an expectation the speaker has reasoned their way to, and it can be undercut by new evidence without contradiction. に違いない expresses a stronger inference the speaker is prepared to stand behind. Choosing the wrong one in an academic or professional context either overstates or understates the writer's actual confidence.

3. Nominalization and clause embedding: こと versus の, and dense noun modification

Purpose: to package a clause as a noun phrase in the form that fits its predicate and register, and to build noun-modifying clauses dense enough for professional and academic prose without becoming unreadable.

  • 方言が失われつつあることは、多くの研究者が指摘している。Many researchers have pointed out that dialects are gradually being lost.こと — nominalizing an abstract fact for a formal predicate (指摘する)
  • 彼女が方言で話しているのを、初めて聞いた。I heard her speaking in dialect for the first time.の — nominalizing a directly perceived event for a perception verb (聞く)
  • 地域社会の中で世代を超えて受け継がれてきた言い回しには、独特のニュアンスが含まれている。Expressions handed down across generations within a regional community carry a distinctive nuance.dense noun modification — a multi-layered clause modifying 言い回し

4. Passive and causative-passive: distinguishing responsibility and perspective

Purpose: to select the construction that places responsibility and viewpoint where the context requires, rather than defaulting to an active sentence out of habit.

  • その方言は、若い世代に使われなくなりつつある。That dialect is increasingly not being used by the younger generation.plain passive — foregrounds the dialect, not the younger generation, as topic
  • 彼は、方言で話すことを恥ずかしがらせられて育った。He was made to feel ashamed of speaking dialect as he grew up.causative-passive — marks the subject as compelled into an emotional state by an outside force

5. Deliberate topic and focus management across a paragraph

Purpose: to keep a single element as topic across several sentences, or shift it deliberately, rather than letting topic choice happen by accident sentence by sentence.

  • その地域の方言は、かつて学校で使用が制限されていた。しかし近年は、逆に保存活動の対象として際立たせられるようになってきている。That region's dialect was once restricted from use in schools. In recent years, however, it has come to be foregrounded, conversely, as an object of preservation efforts.topic held across two sentences (方言), then evaluative focus shifted with 際立たせる

Grammar Practice

  1. Write two sentences about the same situation, one using 〜ば and one using 〜たら, and explain in English why the nuance differs.
  2. Write a sentence using はず and a sentence using に違いない about the same claim, and explain which one commits you to more.
  3. Nominalize the same clause once with こと and once with の, each attached to a predicate that requires it.
  4. Rewrite an active sentence about language change as a passive sentence that foregrounds the language rather than the speakers.

Model Dialogue

Setting: 山本さん reviews Emma's first draft with her at the magazine office. Polite style, mentor-colleague relationship.

山本この文なんですが、「方言が使われなくなる」と書いていますね。でも、あなたが本当に言いたいのは、断定ではなく、可能性の話ではありませんか。About this sentence — you've written that "the dialect will stop being used." But isn't what you really mean not a flat assertion, but a matter of possibility?
エマあ、そうですね。断定するには、まだ根拠が十分ではない気がします。Ah, that's right. I feel like I don't yet have enough grounds to assert it flatly.
山本では、「使われなくなりかねない」に変えてみたらどうでしょう。それなら、含みを持たせながらも、懸念はしっかり伝わります。Then how about changing it to "could well stop being used"? That would leave an implication while still conveying your concern clearly.
エマなるほど。もう一つ、この段落なんですが、主語が文ごとに変わっていて、読みにくい気がします。I see. There's one more thing — in this paragraph, the subject changes from sentence to sentence, and I feel it's hard to read.
山本いいところに気づきましたね。ここは「方言」を話題として最後まで保ったほうが、責任の所在もはっきりします。受け身を使ってみてください。Good catch. Here, it would be better to keep "the dialect" as the topic through to the end — that will also make it clearer where responsibility lies. Try using the passive.
エマわかりました。「方言は学校で使用が制限されていたが、近年は保存活動の対象として際立たせられるようになってきている」でどうでしょうか。Understood. How about: "The dialect had its use restricted in schools, but in recent years it has come to be foregrounded as an object of preservation efforts"?
山本とてもいいと思います。曖昧さがなくなって、視点も一貫しましたね。I think that's very good. The ambiguity is gone, and the viewpoint is consistent now.

Emma leaves the meeting with a revised paragraph and a clearer sense of what precision actually means at this level.

Writing Workshop

Revise a paragraph of your own writing for precision, following Emma's example.

  1. Select a paragraph. Choose three to five sentences from your own earlier Japanese writing, ideally on a topic related to your specialization interest from Chapter 1.
  2. Diagnose each sentence. For each sentence, ask whether it asserts too flatly, hedges too vaguely, uses the wrong conditional, or lets the topic shift without reason.
  3. Revise at least one sentence using a conditional chosen specifically for its nuance, not merely because it is grammatical.
  4. Revise at least one sentence using a modal expression (はず, に違いない, かねない, or わけではない) that matches your actual confidence.
  5. Revise at least one sentence using nominalization or dense noun modification to package information more efficiently.
  6. Check topic continuity across the paragraph and revise at least one sentence, if needed, using the passive to keep a consistent topic.
  7. Write a two-to-three-sentence note explaining, in Japanese, what changed and why.

チェックリスト — Revision checklist:

  • Each revised sentence uses a structure chosen for its exact nuance, not simply an acceptable alternative.
  • My conditional choices reflect the actual relationship between condition and result.
  • My modal choices reflect my actual level of confidence, not a default hedge or a default assertion.
  • My paragraph maintains a clear, deliberate topic rather than shifting by accident.
  • My explanatory note names the specific system used in each revision.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Precision and credibility in Japanese professional writing

In many Japanese professional and academic contexts, overstating a claim with excessive certainty can damage a writer's credibility as much as an outright factual error, particularly in reporting, research, and policy-adjacent writing. Careful hedging with forms such as はず, と考えられる, or とは限らない is generally read as a mark of careful thinking rather than weakness, provided the hedge itself is precise rather than vague.

Regional Note — Precision language in variation research

Emma's article topic — regional and social language use — is exactly the kind of subject where imprecise modality can cause real harm, since flat claims about who speaks a dialect and why can flatten real diversity within a community. This chapter's precision systems will return directly in Chapter 13, when the volume examines regional, historical, and multilingual Japanese in depth.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Treating と, ば, たら, and なら as interchangeable

Error: Selecting whichever conditional feels most familiar, regardless of whether the sentence describes a general truth, an evaluative condition, a sequential event, or a reaction to a premise.

Correction: Ask what relationship the sentence actually expresses, then choose accordingly: その分野を研究するなら、現地調査は欠かせない。 (reacting to a premise, not a mechanical result).

Common Mistake — Overstating confidence with 〜に違いない

Error: Using 〜に違いない for a claim that is really only a reasoned expectation, making the writing sound more certain than the evidence supports.

Correction: Reserve 〜に違いない for genuinely strong inference, and use 〜はずだ for reasoned expectation that could be revised by new evidence.

Common Mistake — Letting topic drift across a paragraph

Error: Switching the grammatical subject from sentence to sentence without a discourse reason, forcing the reader to track several shifting topics at once.

Correction: Hold a single topic across related sentences using the passive when needed, as in Emma's revision: 方言は、〜使用が制限されていた。しかし近年は、〜際立たせられるようになってきている。

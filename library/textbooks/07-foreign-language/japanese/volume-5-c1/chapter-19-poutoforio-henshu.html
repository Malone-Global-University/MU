ポートフォリオ編集Portfolio Editing

C2 portfolio assembly and reflective evidence · Speak and Write Japanese: Mastery, Specialization, and Expert Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Select representative evidence from Chapters 16 through 18 for inclusion in a C2 portfolio.
  2. Write annotations that explain why each piece of evidence was chosen and what it demonstrates.
  3. Describe your own growth using metalinguistic reflection, including naming a recurring error pattern and how it changed over time.
  4. Comment on your own register and style choices across genres, explaining why a given choice suited its audience.
  5. Write a revision narrative that traces a piece of writing from an early draft to its final form.
  6. Apply a final script, typography, and accessibility audit across all portfolio materials.
  7. Assemble a complete, consistently formatted portfolio with reflective commentary in Japanese.
  8. Distinguish active production expectations from receptive recognition across all systems introduced in this chapter.

Communication Scenario

With her presentation and Q&A now behind her, Emma must assemble a portfolio for her final departmental review. 田中先生 has asked for evidence of her full research process: her original research proposal from Chapter 16, at least one full revision cycle from Chapter 17, her presentation materials and a note on how the Q&A went from Chapter 18, and a short reflective essay commenting on her own growth, remaining weaknesses, and stylistic choices across the three chapters. She has one week to select, annotate, and format everything into a single coherent submission.

  1. The learner reviews vocabulary and discourse patterns for portfolio selection, annotation, and reflection.
  2. The learner reads a sample portfolio table of contents and a sample reflective annotation.
  3. The learner listens to a short excerpt of a learner explaining a portfolio choice to an instructor.
  4. The learner practices writing an annotation, a register comment, and a short reflective passage.
  5. The learner completes a performance task assembling and annotating a personal C2 portfolio.

もじ — Script and Typography Focus

Script and Typography Focus

This chapter authorizes final file naming, Japanese text accessibility, ruby policy, and typography consistency across the whole portfolio, drawing together the script decisions made separately in Chapters 16 through 18.

Script Strategy — A final consistency audit across portfolio documents

A portfolio assembled from several chapters' worth of documents often mixes typography choices made at different times: a term given furigana in Chapter 16 might appear without it in Chapter 18's slides, or a kanji written one way in the proposal might appear in an alternate but equally valid form in the presentation outline. Before submission, review every document for the same specialized term, and make the furigana, kanji form, and romanized name spellings (if any appear in captions or file names) consistent throughout. File names for individual portfolio documents should follow the same kebab-case, lowercase-ASCII convention used for the course's own chapter files, with macrons and apostrophes removed, so that the whole submission remains searchable and reliably openable across systems.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Explaining your own speech in reflection

This chapter does not introduce new pronunciation targets. It does, however, ask learners to talk about their own pronunciation, pacing, and delivery choices when reflecting on the Chapter 18 presentation recording. Maintain the mora timing, length contrasts, and phrase-level pitch already consolidated in earlier chapters when recording any spoken reflection, and use ordinary reflective sentence rhythm rather than presentation-style pacing, since a reflective recording addresses an instructor directly rather than an unfamiliar audience.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter authorizes metalinguistic reflection, error-pattern analysis, register commentary, and revision narrative. No new grammar paradigm is introduced beyond systems already consolidated in Units I through III; the focus here is applying that control to talking about one's own language use.

1. Naming a piece of evidence and its purpose

Purpose: to introduce a portfolio item and state what it is meant to demonstrate.

  • この抜粋は、初稿と最終稿の違いを示すために選定した。I selected this excerpt to show the difference between the first draft and the final version.〜を示すために選定した (annotation frame)

2. Naming a recurring error pattern

Purpose: to describe a mistake as a pattern across multiple pieces of work, not a single isolated error.

  • 初期の草稿では、受身形を使うべき箇所で能動形を使ってしまう誤りの傾向が見られた。In the early drafts, there was a recurring error pattern of using the active form in places where the passive form should have been used.〜という誤りの傾向が見られた (error-pattern analysis)

3. Describing growth over time

Purpose: to connect an earlier weakness to later improvement using a before-and-after structure.

  • 第１６章の時点ではこの誤りが多かったが、第１７章の改訂を経て、正しい受身形を安定して使えるようになった。This error was common as of Chapter 16, but after the revisions in Chapter 17, I became able to use the correct passive form consistently.〜時点では〜が、〜を経て〜ようになった (growth narrative)

4. Commenting on register and style choice

Purpose: to explain why a particular register or style was chosen for a given audience or genre.

  • 発表原稿では丁寧な書き言葉を用いたが、スライドでは短い名詞句を中心とした文体に統一した。これは、聞き手が話しながら同時に読むという状況を考えたためである。I used polite written language in the presentation script, but unified the slide style around short noun phrases. This was because I considered the situation of the audience reading while listening at the same time.〜文体に統一した + 〜ためである (register commentary)

5. Naming remaining room for growth

Purpose: to identify an honest, specific area for continued improvement rather than a vague self-criticism.

  • 今後の伸びしろとしては、質疑応答における即興的な言い換えの速さが挙げられる。One area of remaining growth is the speed of improvised rephrasing during Q&A sessions.今後の伸びしろとしては〜が挙げられる (specific, forward-looking reflection)

6. Closing a reflective annotation

Purpose: to summarize what a piece of evidence, taken as a whole, demonstrates about the learner's development.

  • 以上のことから、この一連の資料は、文法的な正確さだけでなく、聞き手に応じて文体を調整する力が伸びてきたことを示している。From the above, this set of materials shows not only grammatical accuracy but also a growing ability to adjust style according to the audience.以上のことから〜ことを示している (reflective summary closing)
Building Block — The reflective annotation pattern

[name the evidence and why it was selected] + [name a specific error pattern or strength it shows] + [trace the change over time] + [state a closing judgment about growth or remaining work]

This is a discourse pattern drawing on the caveats and limitations language from Chapter 16, the revision vocabulary from Chapter 17, and the concession and correction language from Chapter 18, reorganized so a learner can talk honestly about their own development rather than about a research topic or a live challenge.

この抜粋は、初稿と最終稿を並べて、受身形の使い方の変化を示すために選定した。第１６章の時点では能動形と受身形を混同する誤りの傾向が見られたが、第１７章の改訂を経て、意味に応じて適切に使い分けられるようになった。今後の伸びしろとしては、口頭でのとっさの言い換えが挙げられるが、書き言葉における文法的な正確さについては、この一連の資料が明確な伸びを示していると考える。

I selected this excerpt, placing the first and final drafts side by side, to show the change in how the passive form was used. As of Chapter 16, there was a recurring error pattern of confusing the active and passive forms, but after the revisions in Chapter 17, I became able to use each appropriately according to meaning. One area of remaining growth is spontaneous spoken rephrasing, but I believe this set of materials clearly shows growth in grammatical accuracy in writing.

Grammar Practice

  1. Write one sentence naming a piece of evidence using 〜を示すために選定した.
  2. Write one sentence naming a recurring error pattern using 〜という誤りの傾向が見られた.
  3. Write one sentence tracing growth over time using 〜時点では〜が、〜を経て〜ようになった.
  4. Write one sentence commenting on a register or style choice, explaining the reason for it.
  5. Write one sentence naming a specific remaining area for growth using 今後の伸びしろとしては〜.
  6. Combine all five into one connected reflective annotation, following the Building Block pattern above.

Writing Workshop

Assemble a table of contents and a set of reflective annotations for your own portfolio, drawing on your work from Chapters 16 through 18.

  1. Write a table of contents listing the items you will include, following the model's numbered format.
  2. Select at least three items as evidence: one from your research proposal, one from your revision work, and one from your presentation or Q&A record.
  3. Write one reflective annotation per item, following the Building Block pattern: name the evidence, describe an error pattern or strength, trace the change, and close with a judgment.
  4. Write one register or style comment explaining a stylistic choice you made differently across two genres (for example, your written proposal versus your slide phrases).
  5. Write one short overall reflective passage (four to six sentences) naming one honest remaining area for growth.
  6. Complete a final consistency audit of terminology, kanji/furigana treatment, and file naming across all portfolio documents.

チェックリスト — Revision checklist:

  • The table of contents lists all portfolio items in a clear, logical order.
  • Each annotation names the evidence, describes a pattern (not just a single error), traces a change, and closes with a judgment.
  • At least one annotation comments honestly on register or style choice.
  • The overall reflective passage names a specific, honest remaining area for growth rather than a vague generalization.
  • Specialized terms, kanji forms, and furigana treatment are consistent across all documents.
  • File names follow the kebab-case, lowercase-ASCII convention with no macrons or spaces.
  • Vocabulary and grammar remain within the systems authorized through this chapter.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Honest self-assessment as part of academic and professional practice

In many Japanese academic and professional settings, a reflective document that names a specific, honest remaining weakness alongside genuine strengths is often regarded as more credible than one that only lists accomplishments. Learners should treat naming a 伸びしろ not as an admission of failure but as an expected, valued part of a mature self-assessment, provided the growth area is specific rather than a vague or performative disclaimer.

Regional Note — Portfolio conventions vary by institution

The expected length, format, and required components of a language-learning or research portfolio differ across universities, language programs, and workplaces. The structure modeled in this chapter reflects one plausible departmental research-course convention rather than a single fixed standard that applies to every academic or professional context.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Describing a single error instead of a pattern

Error: Writing an annotation that simply says a mistake was made once, without connecting it to a broader tendency or showing change over time.

Correction: Use 誤りの傾向 language to describe a pattern across multiple pieces of work, and trace how it changed, as modeled in the Grammar section above.

Common Mistake — Vague, unfalsifiable reflection

Error: Writing a closing reflection such as "I still need to study more," which names no specific area of growth.

Correction: Name a specific, concrete remaining area for growth, such as the speed of spoken rephrasing under pressure, following the models in this chapter.

Common Mistake — Inconsistent typography across portfolio documents

Error: Submitting a portfolio where the same specialized term appears with furigana in one document and without it in another, or where file names mix conventions.

Correction: Complete a final consistency audit across all documents before submission, as described in the Script and Typography Focus section.

公開発表と質疑応答Public Presentation and Q&A

Advanced delivery, defense, and adaptation · Speak and Write Japanese: Mastery, Specialization, and Expert Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Turn a revised draft from Chapter 17 into a structured oral presentation with a clear framing for a named audience.
  2. Open a formal presentation appropriately, stating purpose, scope, and structure for listeners who have not read the draft.
  3. Deliver supported claims aloud with appropriate pacing, phrase-level pitch, and presentation prosody.
  4. Field questions after a presentation, including requests for clarification and genuinely challenging questions.
  5. Concede a valid point, correct a misunderstanding, or rephrase an unclear statement without abandoning the overall argument.
  6. Close a presentation and a question-and-answer session in a way that leaves the audience with a clear summary.
  7. Produce a full spoken presentation with a live or simulated Q&A segment.
  8. Distinguish active production expectations from receptive recognition across all systems introduced in this chapter.

Communication Scenario

Emma's revised draft on how an online reading community discusses figurative language in contemporary short fiction is now ready for a small departmental research meeting. She must present her findings in eight minutes to an audience that includes 田中先生, two other instructors, and several classmates, some of whom study different topics entirely. After her prepared remarks, she will take questions, including at least one point she had not fully anticipated: a classmate's challenge to her sample size. She has one final opportunity to clarify, concede, and rephrase before the meeting ends.

  1. The learner reviews vocabulary and discourse patterns for presentation framing, delivery, and question handling.
  2. The learner reads a presentation outline and a transcript of a question-and-answer exchange.
  3. The learner listens to a short excerpt of a presenter opening a talk and fielding a challenging question.
  4. The learner practices opening a presentation, answering a clarification question, and answering a challenging question.
  5. The learner completes a performance task delivering a full presentation with a simulated or live Q&A segment.

もじ — Script and Typography Focus

Script and Typography Focus

This chapter authorizes slide typography, handout design, a source/term list carried forward from Chapter 16 and Chapter 17, and accessibility considerations for visual presentation materials.

Script Strategy — Designing slides and handouts for a spoken presentation

Slides and handouts support spoken delivery; they should not simply repeat the full script. Use larger type for headings and key terms, keep furigana visible on any specialized or rare-reading term projected on a slide (since an audience member reading quickly does not have time to look a reading up), and keep sentence-length text off slides in favor of short labeled phrases. A handout distributed to the audience may carry more full sentences and should include the presenter's confirmed terminology list from Chapter 16, since audience members unfamiliar with a specialized term benefit from having the reading and a brief gloss available in front of them rather than only hearing it once.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Presentation prosody and audience-facing pacing

Formal presentation delivery differs from ordinary conversation in pacing rather than in pronunciation rules: pause slightly longer at the boundary between sections (for example, after finishing the 導入 and before moving into the main argument), and slow down noticeably when saying a specialized term for the first time so that an audience member can process both the sound and the meaning together. When answering a question, a brief pause before responding is a normal and useful signal that you are considering the question seriously, not a sign of hesitation to avoid.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter authorizes advanced question-and-answer frames, clarification requests, concession, correction, and rephrasing under pressure. No new grammar paradigm is introduced beyond systems already consolidated in Units I through III; the focus here is applying that control to live spoken defense of one's own work.

1. Opening a formal presentation

Purpose: to state purpose, scope, and structure for an audience that has not read the underlying draft.

  • 本日は、オンラインの読書コミュニティにおける比喩表現の解釈について発表させていただきます。Today, I will be presenting on the interpretation of figurative expressions in an online reading community.本日は〜させていただきます (formal presentation opening)

2. Requesting clarification of a question

Purpose: to confirm what a questioner is actually asking before answering, without appearing evasive.

  • ご質問の論点を確認させていただきたいのですが、サンプル数についてのご指摘でよろしいでしょうか。I'd like to confirm the point of your question — is your comment about the sample size?確認させていただきたいのですが (formal clarification request)

3. Conceding a valid point

Purpose: to acknowledge a genuine limitation without undermining the whole argument.

  • おっしゃる通り、サンプル数が三十件というのは十分とは言えず、これは今回の分析の課題として認めます。As you say, a sample size of thirty is not really sufficient, and I acknowledge this as a limitation of the current analysis.おっしゃる通り + 〜として認める (formal concession)

4. Correcting a misunderstanding without conceding the point

Purpose: to distinguish a factual correction from agreement with the questioner's underlying criticism.

  • その点については少し訂正させてください。三十件はコメント全体ではなく、分析対象とした投稿の数です。Let me correct that point slightly. Thirty is not the total number of comments, but the number of posts selected for analysis.訂正させてください (polite correction, not concession)

5. Rephrasing an unclear statement

Purpose: to restate an earlier point more clearly when a question reveals it was not fully understood.

  • 言い換えますと、読者は本文にはっきり書かれていない意味を、自分の経験に基づいて加えているということです。To put it another way, readers are adding meaning not explicitly written in the text, based on their own experience.言い換えますと (formal rephrasing marker)

6. Closing the Q&A and deferring what remains

Purpose: to summarize the session and offer a concrete way to continue the conversation afterward.

  • 本日いただいたご指摘を踏まえ、サンプル数の課題については後日、追加の分析を行った上でご報告いたします。In light of the comments received today, regarding the sample-size issue, I will conduct additional analysis and report back at a later date.〜を踏まえ + 後日〜いたします (formal closing/deferral)
Building Block — The question-response pattern

[clarify the question, if needed] + [concede what is valid or correct what is mistaken] + [rephrase or add detail] + [state a next step if the issue is unresolved]

This is a discourse pattern drawing on the hedged feedback language from Chapter 17, the advanced keigo and formal request forms from Chapter 6, and the caveats language from Chapter 16, organized so that a presenter can respond to a live question honestly and completely without either capitulating entirely or dismissing a fair criticism.

ご質問ありがとうございます。ご指摘の通り、サンプル数が三十件というのは十分とは言えず、これは課題として認めます。ただし、対象とした投稿は無作為に選んだものではなく、比喩表現への反応が特に多かった投稿を意図的に選んでいます。この点は後日、選定方法について補足資料をご用意いたします。

Thank you for your question. As you point out, a sample size of thirty is not really sufficient, and I acknowledge this as a limitation. However, the posts selected were not chosen at random; they were deliberately selected because they generated a particularly large response to the figurative expressions. I will prepare supplementary material on the selection method at a later date.

Grammar Practice

  1. Write one sentence opening a presentation using 〜させていただきます.
  2. Write one sentence requesting clarification of a question using 確認させていただきたいのですが.
  3. Write one sentence conceding a valid limitation using おっしゃる通り〜課題として認めます.
  4. Write one sentence correcting a misunderstanding without conceding the underlying point.
  5. Write one sentence rephrasing an earlier claim using 言い換えますと.
  6. Combine all five into one connected question-response exchange, following the Building Block pattern above.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Emma has just finished her eight-minute presentation at the departmental research meeting. 田中先生 opens the floor to questions, and a classmate, 木村さん, raises a challenge about her sample size.

田中先生ありがとうございました。それでは質疑応答に移りましょう。ご質問のある方はどうぞ。Thank you. Let's move on to questions and answers. Please go ahead, anyone with a question.
木村発表、興味深かったです。一つ伺いたいのですが、分析対象が三十件というのは、結論を出すには少ないのではないでしょうか。The presentation was interesting. I'd like to ask one thing — isn't thirty cases too few to draw a conclusion from?
エマご質問ありがとうございます。確認させていただきたいのですが、サンプル数そのものについてのご指摘でよろしいでしょうか。Thank you for your question. I'd like to confirm — is your point specifically about the sample size itself?
木村はい、そうです。三十件だけで、コミュニティ全体の傾向と言えるのかという点です。Yes, that's right. My point is whether thirty cases alone are enough to speak of a trend across the whole community.
エマおっしゃる通り、三十件というサンプル数は、コミュニティ全体の傾向として一般化するには十分とは言えません。これは今回の分析の課題として認めます。As you say, a sample size of thirty is not really sufficient to generalize as a trend across the whole community. I acknowledge this as a limitation of the current analysis.
エマただし、一点訂正させてください。三十件はコメント全体からの無作為抽出ではなく、比喩表現への反応が特に多かった投稿を意図的に選んだものです。ですので、傾向そのものは弱いものではないと考えています。However, let me correct one point. The thirty cases were not a random sample from all comments, but were deliberately selected because they generated a particularly large response to the figurative expressions. So I don't think the trend itself is weak.
木村なるほど、選び方が意図的だったのですね。よく分かりました。I see, so the selection was intentional. That makes sense now.
エマこの選定方法については、後日、補足資料を用意して詳しくご説明いたします。I will prepare supplementary material on this selection method and explain it in more detail at a later date.
田中先生では、他にご質問がなければ、本日はここまでにしましょう。エマさん、お疲れさまでした。Well, if there are no other questions, let's stop here for today. Emma, thank you for your hard work.

Emma models the full question-response pattern: clarifying the question, conceding what is genuinely valid, correcting a misunderstanding about her method, and deferring the remaining detail to a later date.

ないようりかい — Comprehension check: What challenge does 木村さん raise? What part of the challenge does Emma concede, and what part does she correct? What does Emma promise to do about the unresolved part of the question?

Writing Workshop

Turn your Chapter 17 revised draft into a written presentation outline and a set of anticipated question-response pairs.

  1. Write a five-part presentation outline (導入, two or three 本論 sections, and 総括) based on your revised research draft.
  2. Draft a thirty-second spoken opening stating your topic, purpose, and structure for an audience unfamiliar with your project.
  3. Anticipate two likely questions about your project's evidence or method, at least one of which should identify a genuine limitation.
  4. Write a full question-response pattern for each anticipated question: clarification (if needed), concession or correction, added detail, and a next step if the issue remains unresolved.

  6. Write a closing summary of two or three sentences using 総括 language to end the presentation.

チェックリスト — Revision checklist:

  • The presentation outline includes an introduction, main sections, and a closing summary.
  • The opening states purpose and structure clearly for an audience unfamiliar with the project.
  • At least one anticipated question addresses a genuine limitation rather than only an easy question.
  • Each question response follows the clarify / concede-or-correct / add detail / next-step pattern where relevant.
  • Slide phrases are short and readable rather than full sentences copied from the draft.
  • Vocabulary and grammar remain within the systems authorized through this chapter.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Conceding a point is a sign of credibility, not weakness

In many Japanese academic and professional presentation settings, openly acknowledging a limitation identified by a questioner, especially with a phrase such as 課題として認めます, is generally understood as a mark of a careful and trustworthy presenter rather than as an admission of failure. Learners should distinguish this from simply agreeing with every criticism raised; a skilled presenter concedes what is genuinely valid while still correcting factual misunderstandings.

Regional Note — Q&A conventions vary by setting

The pacing, formality, and directness of questions in a departmental research meeting, a large academic conference, and a workplace project review can differ considerably. The patterns modeled in this chapter reflect a small, relatively informal departmental meeting rather than a single fixed convention that applies to every presentation setting.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Answering before confirming what was actually asked

Error: Launching into a full answer without confirming the questioner's actual point, which can lead to answering the wrong question entirely.

Correction: When a question is at all ambiguous, use a clarification frame such as 確認させていただきたいのですが before answering, as modeled in the Grammar section above.

Common Mistake — Conceding everything to avoid conflict

Error: Agreeing entirely with a challenging question even when part of it rests on a factual misunderstanding, out of a desire to appear agreeable.

Correction: Separate what is genuinely valid (concede it) from what is a misunderstanding (correct it), following Emma's model in the Model Dialogue.

Common Mistake — Reading full sentences directly off a slide

Error: Filling slides with complete sentences copied from the written draft and reading them aloud word for word.

Correction: Use short phrases on slides as support for spoken delivery, and reserve full sentences for a separate handout, as described in the Script and Typography Focus section.

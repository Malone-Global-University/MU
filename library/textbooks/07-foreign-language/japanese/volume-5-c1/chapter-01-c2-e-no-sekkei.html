C2への設計Designing C2 Control

An advanced diagnostic, precision plan, style profile, error-history audit, and portfolio-planning process · Speak and Write Japanese: Mastery, Specialization, and Expert Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Describe your Japanese ability across the four skills using precise, self-diagnostic language.
  2. Name specific grammar, register, and lexical systems from Volume IV that you control confidently, and name specific systems that still need work.
  3. Discuss recurring patterns in your own errors and explain what tends to cause them.
  4. Describe your current written and spoken style and identify features you would like to refine.
  5. State a specialization interest — a professional, academic, or cultural domain — that will guide your Volume V portfolio and research project.
  6. Set concrete, honestly scoped goals for the volume using appropriate hedging and stance language.
  7. Read and interpret a diagnostic-style self-assessment text.
  8. Produce a written and spoken self-diagnostic that will open your Volume V portfolio.

Communication Scenario

エマ has finished her Volume IV capstone project and feature article on workplace dialect use. 山本さん has now invited her to take on a longer-term specialization at the magazine — but before Emma can propose a direction, she needs to take stock of exactly where her Japanese stands: which systems she controls with precision, which ones still slip under pressure, what her own writing sounds like compared to the writers she admires, and which of her recurring mistakes are worth tackling first. This chapter follows Emma through that stock-taking process and asks the learner to complete a parallel self-diagnostic of their own, which will open their own Volume V portfolio.

  1. The learner listens to Emma describing her diagnostic process to a mentor.
  2. The learner reads Emma's written self-assessment.
  3. The learner completes a structured self-diagnostic covering grammar, vocabulary, register, and error patterns.
  4. The learner writes a short style profile describing their own current spoken and written Japanese.
  5. The learner states a specialization interest that will guide their Volume V project work.
  6. The learner sets three honestly scoped goals for the volume, using appropriate hedging and stance language.

もじ — Script and Terminology Focus

Script and Terminology Focus

This chapter does not introduce new kanji. Instead, it asks you to audit your existing kanji knowledge and begin planning the specialized terminology your Volume V portfolio will require.

Script Strategy — Auditing your kanji before specializing

Before choosing a specialization for Volume V, it is worth reviewing which kanji compounds you can already read and produce reliably, and which ones you recognize only with furigana support. A simple two-column list — kanji you can produce without support, and kanji you still need furigana for — gives you an honest starting point. As your specialization becomes clearer over the next few chapters, you will begin building a dedicated terminology list for that field; this chapter's audit is the foundation for that list, not the list itself.

れんしゅう — Practice: Choose ten kanji compounds from your Volume IV notes or from Chapter 20's vocabulary. Sort them into two columns — produced without support, and still needing furigana — and note one compound from each column that you expect to use often in your chosen specialization.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Listening to your own recorded speech

A useful first step in any C2 diagnostic is listening back to a short recording of your own Japanese. Rather than judging the recording generally, listen specifically for mora timing, vowel length, consonant length, and moraic ん — the contrasts prioritized since Volume I — and note whether they stay steady when you speak quickly or under pressure. If you have access to a verified accent dictionary or pronunciation reference, you may also note one or two words whose pitch pattern surprised you, but treat this as a starting observation rather than a requirement to mark every word you say.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter introduces no new grammar paradigm. It authorizes an audit of every grammar, discourse, register, and revision system activated across Volumes I through IV, applied specifically to the task of describing your own language ability. The four patterns below support that self-diagnostic work by combining familiar systems — stance marking, comparison, cause, and goal-setting — in a reflective frame.

1. Framing a self-assessment: 〜という観点から見ると

Purpose: to state which angle or criterion a self-assessment is being made from, recycling nominalization and topic-framing from Volume IV.

  • 文法という観点から見ると、受け身と使役はまだ完全には使いこなせていない。From the standpoint of grammar, I still don't have full command of the passive and causative.〜という観点から見ると — framing a criterion for self-assessment

2. Naming a tendency in one's own errors: 〜がちだ / 〜傾向がある

Purpose: to describe a recurring, rather than one-time, pattern in one's own language use.

  • 早く話そうとすると、助詞を落としがちだ。When I try to speak quickly, I tend to drop particles.〜がちだ — a recurring tendency under a specific condition
  • 書き言葉では丁寧すぎる表現を選ぶ傾向がある。In writing, I have a tendency to choose overly polite expressions.〜傾向がある — naming a general tendency

3. Setting a goal honestly: 〜を目標に据えたいと思う

Purpose: to state a goal with appropriate hedging, avoiding overclaiming about future ability.

  • 今学期は、専門分野の語彙を増やすことを目標に据えたいと思う。This term, I'd like to set expanding my specialized vocabulary as a goal.〜を目標に据えたいと思う — stating a goal with hedged commitment

4. Speaking frankly about oneself: 率直に言うと、〜

Purpose: to open an honest, unflattering observation about one's own ability without excessive self-criticism.

  • 率直に言うと、敬語はまだ頭で考えながら使っている状態だ。To put it frankly, I'm still using keigo by consciously thinking it through, not automatically.率直に言うと、〜 — honest self-observation without self-criticism
Language Focus — Precision over praise

A C2-level self-diagnostic is not a list of compliments to yourself, and it is not a list of complaints either. It is a precise, specific account: which system, in which context, under which condition. "My Japanese needs work" is not useful information. "I tend to drop particles when I speak quickly, but not when I write" is. The patterns above exist to help you reach that level of precision, not to generate polite modesty.

Grammar Practice

  1. Write a sentence assessing your grammar from a stated angle, using 〜という観点から見ると.
  2. Write a sentence naming a recurring error tendency, using 〜がちだ or 〜傾向がある.
  3. Write a sentence setting a hedged goal, using 〜を目標に据えたいと思う.
  4. Write a sentence opening an honest self-observation, using 率直に言うと、〜.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Emma discusses her self-diagnostic with 山本さん before proposing a specialization for her ongoing work at the magazine. Polite style, mentor-colleague relationship.

山本プロジェクトお疲れさまでした。次に進む前に、自分の日本語を一度、棚卸ししてみるといいですよ。Well done on the project. Before moving forward, it might be good to take stock of your Japanese once.
エマはい。文法という観点から見ると、受け身と使役はだいぶ使えるようになりましたが、条件表現はまだ場面によって選び方に迷うことがあります。Yes. From the grammar standpoint, I've gotten fairly good with the passive and causative, but I still sometimes hesitate over which conditional to choose depending on the situation.
山本なるほど。話すときと書くときで、違いはありますか。I see. Is there a difference between when you speak and when you write?
エマはい。早く話そうとすると、助詞を落としがちです。でも、書くときは逆に、丁寧すぎる言い回しを選ぶ傾向があると思います。Yes. When I try to speak quickly, I tend to drop particles. But when writing, conversely, I think I have a tendency to choose overly polite phrasing.
山本面白い分析ですね。専門分野については、何か考えていますか。That's an interesting analysis. Have you been thinking about a field of specialization?
エマ今回の記事を書いてみて、地域や社会の中の言葉の使われ方に興味を持ちました。今後は、その分野を中心に据えたいと思っています。Writing this article, I became interested in how language is used within regions and society. Going forward, I'd like to make that field my central focus.
山本いいと思います。率直に言うと、その分野の専門用語はまだ少ないでしょうから、そこから始めましょう。I think that's good. Frankly, you probably don't have much specialized vocabulary in that field yet, so let's start there.
エマはい。まずは今学期、条件表現の使い分けと、その分野の語彙を増やすことを目標に据えたいと思います。Yes. First, for this term, I'd like to set choosing among conditionals correctly and expanding my vocabulary in that field as goals.

山本さん agrees, and Emma leaves the meeting with a concrete diagnostic and two clear, honestly scoped goals for the term ahead.

Writing Workshop

Complete your own self-diagnostic, which will open your Volume V portfolio.

  1. Review recent work. Reread two or three pieces of your own Japanese writing from Volume IV, noting repeated expressions or structures.
  2. Assess your grammar from at least two angles, using 〜という観点から見ると, naming one strength and one weakness.
  3. Name at least one recurring tendency in your own errors, using 〜がちだ or 〜傾向がある, and specify the condition under which it happens.
  4. Describe your current style in two or three sentences, comparing your spoken and written Japanese.
  5. State a specialization interest that will guide your Volume V project work, in one or two sentences.
  6. Set three honestly scoped goals for the volume, using 〜を目標に据えたいと思う for at least one of them.
  7. Close with one frank observation using 率直に言うと、〜.

チェックリスト — Self-diagnostic checklist:

  • My assessment names specific systems, not just "grammar" or "vocabulary" in general.
  • I have named at least one strength and one weakness, not only one or the other.
  • My error tendency includes the condition under which it happens.
  • My goals are specific enough to check at the end of the volume.
  • My tone is precise and honest, not falsely modest or falsely confident.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Self-assessment in Japanese professional and academic settings

In many Japanese professional and academic settings, a colleague or mentor asking someone to reflect openly on their own strengths and weaknesses, as 山本さん does with Emma, is a normal part of planning new work — not a test designed to catch someone out. A response that names specific weaknesses alongside specific strengths, as Emma's does, is generally seen as more useful and more credible than a response that avoids naming any weakness at all.

Regional Note — Looking ahead to Chapter 13

Emma's stated interest in regional and social language use previews this volume's Chapter 13, which examines regional, historical, and multilingual Japanese in more depth. Naming a specialization interest early, as Emma does here, does not lock a learner into that topic; it simply gives later chapters' research and portfolio work a clearer direction to build toward.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Vague self-assessment

Error: Writing only a general statement such as "my Japanese is pretty good but I need to study more," without naming any specific system.

Correction: Name the specific system and context: 条件表現は、書くときは選べるが、話すときはとっさに選べないことがある。

Common Mistake — Setting goals with no honest limit

Error: Listing five or six ambitious goals for the volume without acknowledging that pursuing all of them at once may not be realistic.

Correction: Narrow the list and name the tradeoff honestly, as Emma does: 率直に言うと、この二つを同時に進めるのは簡単ではないだろう。

Common Mistake — Confusing a one-time mistake with a genuine tendency

Error: Using 〜がちだ or 〜傾向がある to describe a single error made once, rather than a pattern observed across multiple instances.

Correction: Reserve these patterns for genuinely recurring behavior, and specify the condition: 早口になると助詞を落としがちだ。

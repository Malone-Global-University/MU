学術的な読む・書くAcademic Reading and Writing

Research articles, abstracts, and synthesis · Speak and Write Japanese: Mastery, Specialization, and Expert Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Recognize the structural conventions of Japanese academic prose, including abstracts, literature reviews, and discussion sections.
  2. Use core academic phrase frames such as 本稿では, 先行研究, and 示唆する to frame a paper's purpose, situate it within prior work, and state its implications.
  3. Read a short academic-style abstract and identify its research question, method, and stated contribution.
  4. Summarize a source's claim in your own words while distinguishing your voice from the source's voice.
  5. Write a short academic-style paragraph that states a claim, supports it with reasoning, and acknowledges a limitation.
  6. Apply basic keigo appropriately within an academic register, distinguishing it from the ceremonial keigo of Chapter 6.
  7. Listen to a short excerpt resembling an academic presentation and identify its main claim and supporting point.
  8. Avoid common errors such as overclaiming, uncritical citation, and mixing academic register with casual phrasing.

Communication Scenario

Encouraged by her growing comfort with institutional Japanese, Emma enrolls in a evening seminar at a local university extension program, where she is auditing a course on regional language policy. Her instructor, 中村先生, asks the auditing students to read a short abstract on dialect attrition, summarize its argument, and draft a short response paragraph situating their own view within the existing research. Emma quickly finds that academic Japanese has its own conventions: a paper opens by stating what it will do, situates itself against 先行研究, and closes by carefully hedging its conclusions rather than overstating them. 中村先生 reminds the class that summarizing a source accurately, without either copying its wording or distorting its claim, is itself a skill that takes practice.

もじ — Script and Typography Focus

Script and Typography Focus

This chapter's script focus is academic kanji and terminology, together with citation-like formatting conventions and the verification of names and titles referenced in scholarly writing. Academic Japanese typically writes technical compounds fully in kanji rather than mixing in katakana or hiragana substitutes, and it generally carries little or no furigana, since the assumed reader has advanced literacy.

Script Strategy — Verifying names in academic references

Academic Japanese frequently references authors, institutions, and prior studies by name. Because kanji readings of personal and institutional names cannot be reliably guessed from the characters alone, never invent or assume a reading for a scholar's name or a research institution. When a name matters for a task, treat it the same way you would treat any other unscheduled or unverified reading: flag it for confirmation rather than guessing.

れんしゅう — Practice: Before reading the Model Abstract below, predict which vocabulary items are likely to appear entirely in kanji without furigana, and compare your prediction against the passage.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Reading dense academic sentences aloud

Academic Japanese sentences are often long and heavily nominalized, so reading them aloud clearly requires locating the natural internal pause points, typically after a topic phrase marked by は or before a clause-final conjunction such as が or ため. Practice reading a long academic sentence by first marking where you would pause if explaining its content conversationally, then reading it aloud at a measured pace with accurate mora timing and vowel length, even through dense compounds like 分析結果.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter authorizes academic phrase frames for stating a paper's purpose, situating it against prior research, and stating implications and limitations, together with continued basic keigo appropriate to an academic register.

1. Framing purpose with 本稿では

Purpose: to state clearly, near the opening of a paper or paragraph, what the piece of writing itself will do.

Building Block
本稿では、～について論じる／検討する／明らかにする
Self-referential academic frame: "this paper" as the grammatical topic of what follows.
本稿では、方言使用の世代差について論じる。
This paper discusses generational differences in dialect use.
  • 本稿では、地方における言語政策の変化を検討する。This paper examines changes in regional language policy.本稿では + object + 検討する
  • 本稿の目的は、若年層の方言意識を明らかにすることである。The purpose of this paper is to clarify young people's awareness of dialect.本稿の目的は～ことである (nominalized purpose statement)

2. Situating a paper against 先行研究

Purpose: to connect the current discussion to existing research, showing what is already known and what remains open.

Building Block
先行研究では、～とされている／～が指摘されている
Citation-like frame: attributes a claim to prior research without naming a specific unverified source.
先行研究では、都市部での方言使用の減少が指摘されている。
Prior research has pointed out a decline in dialect use in urban areas.
  • 先行研究では、世代による意識の違いが繰り返し示されてきた。Prior research has repeatedly shown differences in awareness by generation.先行研究では + 示されてきた (passive, ongoing)
  • しかし、先行研究の多くは都市部を対象としており、地方の実態は十分に明らかではない。However, much prior research has focused on urban areas, and the actual situation in regional areas is not sufficiently clear.しかし + 先行研究の多くは (contrast, gap in the literature)

3. Stating implications with 示唆する and hedged claims

Purpose: to present a finding's implication carefully, without overstating what the evidence supports.

FunctionFormulaNotes
Stating an implicationこの結果は、～ことを示唆しているframes a finding as suggestive rather than conclusive
Hedging a claim～と考えられるsofter than ～である; signals reasoned inference
Acknowledging a limitation今後の課題として、～が挙げられるnames an open issue for future research
Closing a discussion以上のことから、～と言えるdraws a summary conclusion from the preceding discussion
  • この結果は、地域による方言意識の差が拡大している可能性を示唆している。This result suggests the possibility that regional differences in dialect awareness are widening.示唆している (hedged, possibility-marked implication)
  • 調査対象が限られているため、今後の課題として、より広い地域での調査が挙げられる。Because the research subjects were limited, a broader regional survey can be named as an issue for future research.今後の課題として + 挙げられる (naming a limitation)
Language Focus — Hedging is not weakness

Academic Japanese writing consistently favors hedged, cautious claims such as ～と考えられる or ～可能性がある over flat assertions such as ～である alone. This is not a sign of a weak argument; it is the expected convention for presenting evidence-based reasoning responsibly. A learner who writes only unhedged, absolute claims will sound overconfident rather than more authoritative.

Model Dialogue

Setting: 中村先生 and Emma discuss her draft summary of the assigned abstract during office hours. Academic register, instructor-student relationship, continued basic keigo.

中村先生エマさんの要約、よく書けていますが、最初の文が少し弱いですね。「本稿では」を使ってみてはどうでしょうか。Your summary is well written, Emma, but the first sentence is a bit weak. What about trying "本稿では"?
エマ「本稿では、方言使用の減少について論じている」でしょうか。Would "This paper discusses the decline in dialect use" work?
中村先生いいですね。次に、先行研究への言及がまだ足りないと思います。この論文はどんな先行研究を踏まえていますか。Good. Next, I think the reference to prior research is still insufficient. What prior research does this paper build on?
エマ先行研究では、都市部の方言使用の減少が指摘されている、と書いてありました。It said that prior research has pointed out a decline in dialect use in urban areas.
中村先生そうです。では、この論文は先行研究と比べて、何が新しいのでしょうか。Right. So compared to that prior research, what is new about this paper?
エマ地方における実態を調査している点が新しいと考えられます。I think what's new is that it investigates the actual situation in regional areas.
中村先生よくできました。「新しいです」ではなく「新しいと考えられます」と言えたのは、とてもいいですね。Well done. The fact that you said "it can be considered new" rather than just "it is new" is very good.

Emma revises her draft, underlining every hedged claim in one color and every reference to prior research in another.

Writing Workshop

Draft a short academic-style paragraph summarizing a source and responding to it, applying the purpose, prior-research, and hedging frames from this chapter.

  1. Choose a topic. Select a short claim about language, education, or community life that you can plausibly discuss, real or invented for practice.
  2. Open with a purpose statement. Use 本稿では or an equivalent frame to state what your paragraph will do.
  3. Reference existing understanding. Use a construction such as 先行研究では～とされている to situate your topic.
  4. State one hedged implication using ～と考えられる or ～ことを示唆している.
  5. Name one limitation using 今後の課題として～が挙げられる.
  6. Write a one-sentence note in Japanese identifying which sentence in your paragraph is the most strongly hedged, and why you hedged it that way.

チェックリスト — Revision checklist:

  • The paragraph opens with a clear purpose statement.
  • At least one sentence references prior research or existing understanding.
  • No claim is stated more strongly than the evidence described would support.
  • At least one limitation is named rather than omitted.
  • Kanji and terminology are used consistently, with furigana reserved only for genuinely rare or unscheduled readings.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Hedging as intellectual responsibility

Japanese academic writing places strong value on careful, hedged claims and explicit acknowledgment of a study's limitations, reflecting a broader convention in which overstating one's findings is viewed as a lapse in scholarly responsibility rather than a sign of weak confidence. A paper that includes a clear 今後の課題 section is generally read as more credible, not less, because it signals that the author understands the boundaries of the evidence presented.

Regional Note — Academic register across institutions

Academic writing conventions in Japanese remain broadly consistent across universities and research institutions nationwide, since scholarly publishing follows shared disciplinary norms rather than regional speech patterns. Where variation does appear, it is more likely to concern discipline-specific terminology or house style guidelines from a particular journal or institution than regional dialect.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Overclaiming without hedging

Error: Writing a flat, unhedged conclusion such as 若い世代は方言を使わない。 based on a small or limited study.

Correction: Use a hedging formula appropriate to the strength of the evidence: 若い世代において方言使用が減少している可能性があると考えられる。

Common Mistake — Summarizing a source by copying its wording

Error: Reproducing a source's sentence nearly word-for-word and presenting it as one's own summary, without restating the claim in different phrasing.

Correction: Restate the source's claim in your own words and, where relevant, attribute it explicitly with a frame such as 先行研究では～とされている, rather than echoing the original sentence structure.

Common Mistake — Mixing casual and academic register

Error: Combining an academic frame such as 本稿では with a casual predicate ending such as ～と思う or ～だと思います in the same paragraph.

Correction: Keep academic register consistent throughout a paragraph, preferring forms such as ～と考えられる or ～と言える over conversational hedges like ～と思う.

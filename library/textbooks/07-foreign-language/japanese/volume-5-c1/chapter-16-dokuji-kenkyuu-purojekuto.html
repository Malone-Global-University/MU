独自研究プロジェクトIndependent Research Project

Question, method, source base, and contribution · Speak and Write Japanese: Mastery, Specialization, and Expert Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Formulate a focused research question in Japanese, appropriate in scope for an independent project.
  2. Describe and justify a research method, including its limitations, in accurate written and spoken Japanese.
  3. Build and annotate a small source base, distinguishing primary from secondary sources and noting relevance.
  4. Identify an intended audience for the project and explain how that audience shapes tone, depth, and terminology.
  5. State caveats and limitations honestly, without overstating what a small independent project can establish.
  6. Read and interpret a model research proposal and identify its component parts.
  7. Produce a written project plan and a short spoken pitch describing the project's question, method, sources, and limitations.
  8. Distinguish active production expectations from receptive recognition across all systems introduced in this chapter.

Communication Scenario

Having completed Unit III, Emma now begins the independent project that will carry through the rest of Volume V: drafting in Chapter 17, presenting in Chapter 18, assembling a portfolio in Chapter 19, and delivering a final integrated project in Chapter 20. Her instructor, 田中先生, asks her to bring a one-page project plan to their next meeting: a research question, a short account of her method, an annotated list of three to five sources, a statement of who the project is for, and an honest note about what the project will not be able to show. Emma chooses to investigate how a specific online reading community discusses figurative language in contemporary short fiction — a topic that lets her draw on Unit III's critique and media work while building something new.

  1. The learner reviews vocabulary and discourse patterns for research questions, methods, sources, limitations, and audience.
  2. The learner reads a model research proposal and identifies its parts.
  3. The learner listens to a short spoken research pitch and identifies its question, method, and limitations.
  4. The learner writes a one-page project plan following the model.
  5. The learner completes a performance task presenting the project plan both in writing and aloud.

もじ — Script and Terminology Focus

Script and Terminology Focus

This chapter authorizes a specialized terminology list, a kanji and name verification log, and a typography plan, as tools for managing an independent project rather than as new grammar or reading systems.

Script Strategy — Keeping a terminology and verification log

An independent project introduces specialized terms and proper names — authors, titles, platforms, technical words — that must be spelled and read correctly and consistently throughout the project. Start a simple two-column log now: the term or name in Japanese script with its verified reading, and a short note on where you confirmed it (a dictionary, an official source, the source text itself). Never guess a name reading from its kanji alone. This log will be reused directly in Chapter 17 during revision and in Chapter 19 when assembling the portfolio.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Presenting a plan aloud with confident, even pacing

A short spoken research pitch differs from a casual conversation: sentences tend to be longer and more information-dense, and a presenter must maintain even phrase-level pacing so that a listener can follow the question, method, and limitations in sequence. Practice reading your written project plan aloud slowly enough to preserve mora timing and vowel length in specialized terms, pausing briefly after each major component (question, method, sources, audience, limitations) rather than running them together.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter authorizes research framing, problem statement, source synthesis, caveats, and genre planning as integrated discourse functions. No new grammar paradigm is introduced beyond the fine-grained clause and predicate control already consolidated in Unit I and the academic phrase frames introduced in Chapter 7.

1. Framing a research question

Purpose: to state clearly what the project investigates and why.

  • 本研究では、あるネット上の読書コミュニティが比喩表現をどう解釈しているかを考察する。This project examines how a particular online reading community interprets figurative expressions.本研究では + 〜かを考察する (research framing)

2. Stating the motivating problem

Purpose: to explain why the question is worth investigating.

  • これまでの批評は作品そのものの分析が中心で、読者側の反応にはあまり注目してこなかった。Criticism up to now has centered on analysis of the work itself and has not paid much attention to readers' own reactions.問題意識 framing via これまで + 〜てこなかった

3. Describing method and its limitation

Purpose: to describe how the project will be carried out and to acknowledge what the method cannot show.

  • 今回はコメント欄の投稿を分析するという手法を取るが、対象が一つのコミュニティに限られるため、結果を一般化することはできない。This time I will take the approach of analyzing comment-section posts, but since the subject is limited to one community, the results cannot be generalized.〜という手法を取る + 限界 (limitation clause)

4. Annotating and synthesizing sources

Purpose: to introduce a source and note its relevance in one connected statement.

  • この論文は二次資料だが、比喩理解に関する先行研究をよくまとめており、本研究の枠組みの参考になる。This paper is a secondary source, but it summarizes prior research on figurative-language comprehension well and is useful as a reference for this project's framework.二次資料 + 先行研究 (Ch. 7 reuse) + 参考になる

5. Naming an audience and adjusting genre

Purpose: to state who the project is for and how that shapes its presentation.

  • 対象読者は日本語学習者を含む一般の読書愛好者なので、専門用語をできるだけ避け、具体例を多く示すつもりだ。Since the target readership is general reading enthusiasts, including Japanese-language learners, I intend to avoid specialized terminology as much as possible and provide many concrete examples.対象読者 + 〜つもりだ (genre planning)
Building Block — The five-part project statement

[research question] + [motivating problem] + [method and its limitation] + [source synthesis] + [audience and genre plan]

This is a discourse pattern rather than a new grammatical structure: it draws on academic phrase frames from Chapter 7, register-transfer and audience-adaptation reasoning from Chapter 14, and the connective systems already consolidated across this volume, organized into a five-part sequence appropriate for a one-page project plan.

本研究では、あるネット上の読書コミュニティが比喩表現をどう解釈しているかを考察する。これまでの批評は作品そのものの分析が中心で、読者側の反応にはあまり注目してこなかった。今回はコメント欄の投稿を分析するという手法を取るが、対象が一つのコミュニティに限られるため、結果を一般化することはできない。参考資料としては、比喩理解に関する先行研究をまとめた論文（二次資料）と、実際のコメント欄の投稿（一次資料）を用いる。対象読者は日本語学習者を含む一般の読書愛好者なので、専門用語をできるだけ避け、具体例を多く示すつもりだ。

This project examines how a particular online reading community interprets figurative expressions. Criticism up to now has centered on analysis of the work itself and has not paid much attention to readers' own reactions. This time I will take the approach of analyzing comment-section posts, but since the subject is limited to one community, the results cannot be generalized. As reference material, I will use a paper summarizing prior research on figurative-language comprehension (a secondary source) and actual comment-section posts (a primary source). Since the target readership is general reading enthusiasts, including Japanese-language learners, I intend to avoid specialized terminology as much as possible and provide many concrete examples.

Grammar Practice

  1. Write one sentence framing a research question using 本研究では〜を考察する.
  2. Write one sentence explaining the motivating problem behind your question.
  3. Write one sentence describing your method and stating one honest limitation of it.
  4. Write one sentence introducing a source and explaining its relevance, distinguishing primary from secondary if applicable.
  5. Write one sentence naming your intended audience and how it shapes your genre choices.
  6. Combine all five into one connected project statement, following the Building Block pattern above.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Emma meets with 田中先生 to discuss her draft research question before writing the full project plan.

エマ先生、研究課題を考えてみたのですが、範囲が広すぎる気がして心配です。Sensei, I've thought of a research question, but I'm worried the scope might be too broad.
田中先生どんな課題ですか。What is the question?
エマ「現代の読者はどのように比喩を理解するか」というテーマにしようと思っていました。I was thinking of the theme "How do contemporary readers understand figurative language."
田中先生おもしろいテーマですが、「現代の読者」全体を対象にするのは、一人のプロジェクトでは扱いきれません。対象を一つのコミュニティや一つの作品に絞ってみてはどうでしょうか。It's an interesting theme, but treating "contemporary readers" as a whole isn't something a single-person project can handle. What about narrowing the subject to one community or one work?
エマでは、あるネット上の読書コミュニティのコメント欄に絞ろうと思います。でも、それだけで一般的な結論は出せますか。Then I'll narrow it to the comment section of one particular online reading community. But can I draw a general conclusion from just that?
田中先生いいえ、それは限界として正直に書いておくべきです。一つのコミュニティの分析からは、一般化できる結論ではなく、一つの事例としての貢献が期待できます。No, and you should honestly state that as a limitation. From the analysis of one community, you can't expect a generalizable conclusion — rather, a contribution as a single case study.
エマ分かりました。対象読者についても考えておいたほうがいいですか。I understand. Should I also think about the target readership?
田中先生ぜひ。対象読者が決まれば、専門用語をどの程度使うか、どんな具体例を選ぶかも自然に決まってきますよ。Please do. Once you decide on the target readership, things like how much specialized terminology to use and what kinds of examples to choose will naturally follow.

田中先生 models how to narrow an overly broad research question, name a resulting limitation honestly, and connect the audience decision to later genre choices.

Writing Workshop

Write your own one-page project plan, applying the five-part pattern from this chapter.

  1. State your research question in one clear sentence, narrowed following the model in the Model Dialogue.
  2. Explain the motivating problem in one or two sentences: why does this question matter, and what gap does it address?
  3. Describe your method in one or two sentences, and state at least one honest limitation of that method.
  4. Introduce three to five sources in an annotated list, labeling each as a primary or secondary source and noting its relevance in one sentence per source.
  5. Name your target readership and explain in one or two sentences how that readership shapes your terminology, tone, and choice of examples.
  6. Begin your terminology and verification log from the Script and Terminology Focus section, with at least three entries relevant to your project.

チェックリスト — Revision checklist:

  • The research question is narrow enough for a single independent project.
  • The motivating problem clearly explains why the question is worth investigating.
  • The method is described clearly, and at least one limitation is stated honestly.
  • Each source is labeled as primary or secondary, with its relevance explained.
  • The target readership is named, and its effect on terminology and examples is explained.
  • The terminology and verification log has at least three entries with verification notes.
  • Vocabulary and grammar remain within the systems authorized through this chapter.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Honesty about scope is a form of credibility

Across academic and professional writing in many contexts, stating a project's limitations clearly is not a weakness to be hidden but a marker of credibility: it shows the writer understands exactly what their evidence can and cannot support. A brief, honest limitation statement, such as noting that a single-community analysis cannot be generalized, is generally viewed as good practice rather than as an admission of failure.

Regional Note — Research on regional and community variation requires special care

If your project topic touches on regional Japanese, community usage, or any variety discussed in Chapter 13, continue applying the same non-ranking principle used throughout this volume: describe variation as difference, not as deficiency, and avoid treating your own limited sample as representative of an entire region or community.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — A research question that is too broad for the project

Error: Writing a research question like 「現代の読者はどのように比喩を理解するか」 without narrowing it to a specific, manageable subject.

Correction: Narrow the question to a specific community, text, or dataset, as 田中先生 models in the Model Dialogue, and state the narrower scope explicitly in the project plan.

Common Mistake — Overstating what a small project can show

Error: Presenting findings from one community or one small dataset as though they apply generally, without any limitation statement.

Correction: Include an honest limitation sentence, such as 結果を一般化することはできない, following the models in the Grammar and Discourse section above.

Common Mistake — Listing sources without annotation

Error: Providing a bare list of source titles without noting whether each is a primary or secondary source or why it is relevant.

Correction: Annotate each source with its type and its relevance in one sentence, following the source-synthesis model in the Grammar and Discourse section above.

Model Dialogue — Comprehension Check

田中先生 says Emma's original theme is too broad because treating "contemporary readers" as a whole cannot be handled by a single-person project. 田中先生 says Emma should honestly state that her findings, drawn from one community, cannot be generalized and should instead be framed as a contribution from a single case study. 田中先生 connects the audience decision to later choices by noting that once the target readership is decided, decisions about how much specialized terminology to use and which examples to choose will follow naturally from that.

Listening Practice — Model answers

(1) The research question concerns how dialect expressions are used in social-media posts by high school students in a particular region. (2) The method is analyzing social-media posts by high school students in one region; the subject is a limited group of high school students from that region. (3) The limitation is that the number of high school students studied is limited, so the results cannot represent the region as a whole. (4) The primary sources are the actual posts; the secondary sources are prior research papers on dialect. (5) The target readership is university students studying linguistics, and the speaker plans to use specialized terminology while carefully explaining concrete examples.

Reading Practice

(1) The research question concerns how dialect expressions are used in the online edition of a regional newspaper. (2) The motivating problem is that regional newspaper web articles are now readable nationwide, but how much dialect is retained varies by reporter and outlet, and this variation has rarely been studied systematically. (3) The method is analyzing thirty web articles from one regional newspaper for where dialect expressions appear and what function they serve; the limitation is that, since the subject is limited to one newspaper, the results cannot be generalized as a trend for regional newspapers as a whole. (4) The primary source is the text of the web articles; the secondary sources are existing studies on dialect and regional media. (5) The target readership is university students interested in media studies, and the writer plans to add concise annotations for specialized terminology.

草稿から完成へFrom Draft to Final

Revision, peer response, and editorial control · Speak and Write Japanese: Mastery, Specialization, and Expert Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Turn a project plan into a full first draft while preserving the five-part project statement from Chapter 16.
  2. Identify and repair structural problems in a draft, including weak transitions, unsupported claims, and misplaced source synthesis.
  3. Give and receive peer response using accurate, appropriately hedged Japanese.
  4. Edit a draft at the level of register, word choice, and sentence style, matching the audience and genre named in Chapter 16.
  5. Proofread a draft systematically, including a final kanji-conversion audit and a ruby-and-typography audit.
  6. Read and evaluate a revised draft against an earlier version, identifying what changed and why.
  7. Produce a revised written draft and a short spoken account explaining the most significant changes made during revision.
  8. Distinguish active production expectations from receptive recognition across all systems introduced in this chapter.

Communication Scenario

Emma now has an approved one-page project plan on how an online reading community discusses figurative language in contemporary short fiction. Her task this chapter is to expand that plan into a full first draft, then revise it before her next meeting with 田中先生. She exchanges a draft with a classmate, 佐藤さん, for peer response, then works through 田中先生's written comments. Along the way she keeps her terminology and verification log from Chapter 16 open, adding entries as new specialized terms and names appear in her draft.

  1. The learner reviews vocabulary and discourse patterns for drafting, revision, peer response, and proofreading.
  2. The learner reads a draft passage alongside its revised version and identifies the kinds of changes made.
  3. The learner listens to a short peer-response exchange and identifies the feedback given and the writer's response to it.
  4. The learner revises a short draft passage using the feedback techniques modeled in the chapter.
  5. The learner completes a performance task submitting a revised draft and a short spoken account of the revision process.

もじ — Script and Typography Focus

Script and Typography Focus

This chapter authorizes a final kanji-conversion audit, a ruby audit, and attention to punctuation, captions, tables, and accessibility, building directly on the terminology and verification log begun in Chapter 16.

Script Strategy — Running a kanji-conversion and ruby audit

Before submitting a revised draft, read through it specifically for 誤変換: words that IME conversion accepted but that do not match the intended meaning, since homophone pairs such as 構成 and 校正 are easy to swap by accident. Separately, check every place ruby appears: confirm each reading matches the current word-level reading rather than a leftover from an earlier draft, and confirm that no ruby has been duplicated over okurigana. Finally, confirm 表記統一 — that a term written with kanji in one place has not drifted into kana or a different kanji elsewhere in the same draft — since inconsistency of this kind is a common and avoidable sign of insufficient proofreading.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Reading a revised passage aloud to test its flow

Reading a draft aloud is a practical revision technique: a sentence that is difficult to read aloud smoothly, or that requires an unnatural breath in the middle, often has a structural problem as well. As you read your own draft aloud, maintain even phrase-level pacing and clear mora timing on specialized terms, and note any point where you stumble, since that point often marks a place where the sentence should be shortened or the transition should be strengthened.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter authorizes revision operations at the level of grammar, discourse, register, lexical choice, source integration, and typography. No new grammar paradigm is introduced beyond systems already consolidated in Units I through III; the focus here is applying that control to the task of revision.

1. Pointing out a structural problem

Purpose: to identify a weakness in organization or transition, hedged appropriately for peer or instructor comment.

  • 第二段落と第三段落のつながりが弱いように思います。It seems to me that the connection between the second and third paragraphs is weak.〜ように思う (hedged instructor/peer observation)

2. Requesting or explaining a revision

Purpose: to explain what should change and why, in a way that supports rather than merely criticizes.

  • この主張には根拠が足りないので、具体的な投稿例を一つ加えたほうがいいかもしれません。This claim doesn't have enough grounding, so it might be better to add one concrete example of a post.根拠が足りない + 〜たほうがいいかもしれない (softened suggestion)

3. Responding to feedback

Purpose: to acknowledge a comment and state how it will be addressed, without simply agreeing to everything.

  • ご指摘ありがとうございます。おっしゃる通り根拠が弱いので、次の改訂版で具体例を追加します。Thank you for pointing that out. As you say, the grounding is weak, so I will add a concrete example in the next revised version.ご指摘 + おっしゃる通り (acknowledging feedback formally)

4. Explaining a style or register change

Purpose: to justify a change made for audience or genre reasons, connecting back to the audience decision from Chapter 16.

  • 対象読者には専門家以外の読者も含まれるため、この部分の専門用語をより平易な表現に置き換えた。Since the target readership includes non-specialist readers, I replaced the specialized terminology in this section with plainer expressions.対象読者 (Ch. 16 reuse) + 〜に置き換える

5. Reporting what changed between drafts

Purpose: to summarize a revision for a reader comparing two versions.

  • 草稿では結論が唐突だったが、改訂版では前の段落との関係を明示することで、つながりを強めた。In the draft, the conclusion felt abrupt, but in the revised version, I strengthened the connection by making the relationship with the preceding paragraph explicit.草稿 vs 改訂版 (contrast) + 〜ことで (means)
Building Block — The revision-report pattern

[structural problem, hedged] + [suggested or made revision, with reason] + [response to feedback] + [before/after contrast]

This is a discourse pattern drawing on hedged observation forms, the academic and audience-adaptation reasoning built in Chapters 7 and 14, and the source-synthesis language from Chapter 16, organized so that a writer can report on and justify a set of revisions clearly and honestly.

第二段落と第三段落のつながりが弱いように思ったので、根拠となる具体例を一つ追加した。また、対象読者には専門家以外の読者も含まれるため、専門用語をより平易な表現に置き換えた。草稿では結論が唐突だったが、改訂版では前の段落との関係を明示することで、つながりを強めた。

I thought the connection between the second and third paragraphs was weak, so I added one concrete example to serve as grounding. Also, since the target readership includes non-specialist readers, I replaced the specialized terminology with plainer expressions. In the draft, the conclusion felt abrupt, but in the revised version, I strengthened the connection by making the relationship with the preceding paragraph explicit.

Grammar Practice

  1. Write one sentence hedging a structural criticism using 〜ように思う.
  2. Write one sentence suggesting a revision and its reason using 〜たほうがいいかもしれない.
  3. Write one sentence responding to feedback that both acknowledges it and states a concrete next step.
  4. Write one sentence explaining a style or register change with reference to your audience.
  5. Write one sentence contrasting a draft and a revised version using 草稿では〜が、改訂版では〜.
  6. Combine all five into one connected revision report, following the Building Block pattern above.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Emma exchanges drafts with a classmate, 佐藤さん, for peer response before meeting 田中先生 again.

佐藤エマさんの草稿、読ませてもらいました。全体的に読みやすかったですが、一つ気になった点があります。I read your draft, Emma. Overall it was easy to read, but there's one point that stood out to me.
エマどの部分ですか。ぜひ教えてください。Which part? Please tell me.
佐藤三段落目です。「読者は比喩を独自に解釈する傾向がある」と書いてありますが、具体的な投稿の例がなくて、根拠が弱いように思いました。The third paragraph. You wrote that "readers tend to interpret figurative language in their own way," but there's no concrete example of a post, so the grounding seemed weak to me.
エマご指摘ありがとうございます。おっしゃる通りです。実は手元に良い例が一つあるので、それを追加します。Thank you for pointing that out. You're right. Actually, I have a good example on hand, so I'll add it.
佐藤それから、四段落目の専門用語が少し難しいと感じました。対象読者は日本語学習者も含むんですよね。Also, I felt the specialized terminology in the fourth paragraph was a bit difficult. Your target readership includes Japanese-language learners too, right?
エマはい、そうです。専門用語をもう少し平易な表現に置き換えたほうがいいかもしれませんね。Yes, that's right. It might be better to replace the specialized terminology with somewhat plainer expressions.
佐藤最後に、結論が少し唐突に感じました。三段落目までの内容とのつながりをもう少し明示してもいいかもしれません。Lastly, the conclusion felt a bit abrupt to me. It might help to make the connection with the content up through the third paragraph a bit more explicit.
エマ分かりました。全部直してから、改訂版をまた読んでもらってもいいですか。I understand. Once I've fixed everything, could I have you read the revised version again?
佐藤もちろんです。楽しみにしています。Of course. I'm looking forward to it.

佐藤さん models hedged, specific peer feedback across three levels — evidence, register, and structure — and Emma models acknowledging feedback and stating a concrete next step for each point.

ないようりかい — Comprehension check: What three problems does 佐藤さん identify in Emma's draft? How does Emma respond to each one? What does Emma ask 佐藤さん to do after she revises?

Writing Workshop

Revise your Chapter 16 project plan into a fuller first draft, then produce a revised version applying the techniques from this chapter.

  1. Expand your project plan from Chapter 16 into a full first draft of two to three paragraphs, keeping the five-part project statement intact.
  2. Exchange drafts with a partner (or set your own draft aside for a day) and identify at least two structural, register, or evidence problems, following 佐藤さん's hedged feedback model.
  3. Respond to each comment in writing, acknowledging it and stating a concrete revision.
  4. Revise the draft, adding at least one concrete example or piece of evidence and adjusting terminology for your named audience.
  5. Run a kanji-conversion and ruby audit on the revised draft, checking specifically for homophone errors and orthographic consistency, and update your terminology and verification log.
  6. Write a short before/after summary of two or three sentences describing what changed and why, using the revision-report pattern.

チェックリスト — Revision checklist:

  • The five-part project statement from Chapter 16 is still present and intact.
  • At least one concrete example or piece of evidence has been added to support a general claim.
  • Terminology has been checked against the named target readership.
  • Transitions between paragraphs are explicit rather than abrupt.
  • A kanji-conversion and ruby audit has been completed, with no unresolved homophone errors.
  • Orthographic consistency (表記統一) has been checked for key terms.
  • The terminology and verification log has been updated with any newly confirmed terms or names.
  • Vocabulary and grammar remain within the systems authorized through this chapter.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Hedged, indirect feedback as a professional norm

In many Japanese academic and professional settings, written and spoken feedback on a draft is commonly phrased with hedges such as 〜ように思います or 〜かもしれません even when the reviewer is quite confident about the problem identified. This is generally a convention of collegial communication rather than a sign of uncertainty about the content itself, and learners should not read hedged feedback as weak or optional.

Regional Note — Revision conventions are not identical across every institution

Specific expectations for peer review, instructor comments, and manuscript revision vary by institution, discipline, and generation. The patterns modeled in this chapter reflect common classroom and academic practice rather than a single fixed procedure that applies everywhere.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Feedback that is too vague to act on

Error: Writing feedback like 分かりにくいです without naming which part is unclear or why.

Correction: Name the specific paragraph or sentence and the specific problem, following 佐藤さん's model in the Model Dialogue.

Common Mistake — Revising the surface without checking the argument

Error: Correcting spelling and word choice while leaving an unsupported claim or a weak transition unaddressed.

Correction: Treat structural and evidentiary revision as equally important as surface editing, and check both in every revision pass.

Common Mistake — Skipping the kanji-conversion audit

Error: Submitting a revised draft without checking for homophone conversion errors such as 校正 for 構成.

Correction: Run a dedicated conversion and ruby audit as a separate final step, as described in the Script and Typography Focus section above.

Model Dialogue — Comprehension Check

佐藤さん identifies three problems: weak grounding for the claim in the third paragraph, specialized terminology in the fourth paragraph that may be difficult for the named audience, and an abrupt conclusion that does not connect clearly to the preceding paragraphs. Emma responds to each by agreeing and stating a concrete next step: adding a concrete example, replacing terminology with plainer expressions, and making the connection to earlier paragraphs more explicit. Emma asks 佐藤さん to read the revised version again once she has made all the changes.

Listening Practice — Model answers

(1) The two problems are an unclear classification of dialect expressions in the second paragraph and a source introduction that summarizes a secondary source without explaining why it was chosen. (2) For the first, the reviewer suggests adding one more concrete example; for the second, the reviewer suggests adding a sentence explaining the reason the source was chosen. (3) The writer agrees with both points and states that an example will be added to the classification section and a sentence about the reason for choosing the source will be added. (4) The passage reuses 二次資料 from Chapter 16 to describe the second problem.

Reading Practice

(1) A concrete example post is added: "this expression seems to emphasize the character's sense of loneliness." (2) A count is added — more than twenty out of thirty comments showed the pattern. (3) The claim that readers add interpretations not explicit in the text becomes more explicit and specific with the added example. (4) The revision does not change the basic argument; it strengthens the same argument with concrete evidence and quantified support. (5) This is primarily a change in 根拠 (grounding/evidence), with a secondary effect on 構成, since the added evidence also makes the paragraph's internal structure clearer.

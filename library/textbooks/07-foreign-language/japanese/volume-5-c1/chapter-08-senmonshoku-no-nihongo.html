専門職の日本語Professional Domain Communication

High-level professional discourse · Speak and Write Japanese: Mastery, Specialization, and Expert Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Recognize domain register across business, technology, finance, education, law-adjacent public information, health communication, arts, administration, and science contexts.
  2. Use professional moves such as framing a recommendation, weighing risk against benefit, and summarizing a position for a busy audience.
  3. Read a short executive summary and identify its recommendation, supporting reasons, and named risk.
  4. Write a concise executive summary that states a recommendation and briefly justifies it.
  5. Distinguish domain-specific terminology and abbreviations from general academic vocabulary already active from Chapter 7.
  6. Apply basic keigo appropriately within a professional register, adjusting for audience seniority and internal versus external communication.
  7. Listen to a short excerpt resembling an internal briefing and identify its recommendation and the risk or benefit it names.
  8. Avoid common errors such as burying a recommendation, overusing academic hedges in a business context, and mismatching register between internal and external communication.

Communication Scenario

Emma's evening seminar work with 中村先生 has drawn the attention of her employer, a small language-services company preparing to expand its client base to include universities and municipal offices. Her manager, 佐藤部長, asks Emma to help draft a short internal recommendation about whether the company should offer a new consulting service to local governments interested in multilingual public communication. Emma quickly notices that professional Japanese shares some conventions with the academic writing she has just studied — a clear opening statement, careful hedging of uncertain claims — but professional documents move faster to the point, name a concrete recommendation early, and speak explicitly about risk and benefit rather than academic implication. 佐藤部長 also reminds Emma that the same recommendation must sound different depending on whether it is written for colleagues inside the company or presented to an outside client.

もじ — Script and Typography Focus

Script and Typography Focus

This chapter's script focus is specialized terminology, common business abbreviations, and the typography of charts and tables used in professional documents. Business Japanese frequently mixes established loanword terms such as リスク and メリット with kanji compounds such as 費用対効果, and it favors compact, scannable formatting — bolded headers, bulleted recommendations, and short summary lines — over the continuous prose typical of academic writing.

Script Strategy — Reading a document meant to be scanned, not read start to finish

A professional recommendation memo is usually designed so that a busy reader can find the recommendation and its main reason within the first few lines. When reading a memo or executive summary, first locate the sentence that states the recommendation itself, then look for the sentences that give reasons, risks, and next steps. Do not assume the document must be read in strict linear order the way an academic abstract typically is.

れんしゅう — Practice: Before reading the Model Executive Summary below, scan it for ten seconds and try to identify the single sentence that states the recommendation, without reading the full passage closely yet.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Delivering a recommendation with confident, measured pacing

Professional spoken Japanese, especially when stating a recommendation aloud in a meeting, tends to favor a slightly faster, more clipped pace than academic presentation speech, with a clear drop in pitch at the end of the recommendation sentence to signal that the main point has been delivered. Practice saying a short recommendation sentence such as 導入をお勧めします aloud with accurate mora timing and a controlled falling pitch at the end, then compare that delivery with the more measured, hedged pacing appropriate to an academic implication from Chapter 7.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter authorizes domain register choices, professional moves for framing a recommendation, risk/benefit language, and executive-summary structure, together with continued basic keigo appropriate to a professional register that shifts between internal and external audiences.

1. Stating a recommendation directly

Purpose: to state a course of action clearly and near the beginning of a professional document or utterance, rather than saving it for the end.

Building Block
～を（お）勧めします／～べきだと考えます
Direct professional recommendation frame: states the proposed action as the sentence's main point rather than as a hedged implication.
多言語対応サービスの導入をお勧めします。
I recommend introducing the multilingual support service.
  • 今回の調査結果を踏まえて、試験的な導入を進めるべきだと考えます。Taking this survey's results into account, I believe we should proceed with a trial introduction.～を踏まえて + べきだと考えます
  • 社内での意思決定を早めるため、来週までにご回答いただけますでしょうか。To speed up the internal decision-making, could we ask for your response by next week?ため + いただけますでしょうか (polite request tied to a business purpose)

2. Weighing メリット and デメリット

Purpose: to present the benefits and drawbacks of a course of action side by side, so that a reader or listener can judge whether the recommendation is well supported.

Building Block
メリットとしては～。一方、デメリットとしては～。
Balanced risk/benefit frame: presents an advantage first, then contrasts it explicitly with a drawback using 一方 ("on the other hand").
メリットとしては費用対効果が高いことが挙げられる。一方、デメリットとしては導入に時間がかかることが挙げられる。
One advantage is high cost-effectiveness. On the other hand, one drawback is that the introduction takes time.
  • 社外に委託する最大のメリットは、社内の負担を減らせる点である。The greatest merit of outsourcing to an outside party is that it can reduce the internal workload.最大のメリットは～点である (naming the single strongest benefit)
  • ただし、リスクとして、外部との連携がうまくいかない可能性も考慮すべきである。However, as a risk, the possibility that coordination with an outside party might not go smoothly should also be considered.リスクとして + 考慮すべきである

3. Writing an executive summary opening

Purpose: to open a professional document by stating its overall conclusion first, before the supporting reasoning, so that a reader who stops after one sentence still understands the main point.

FunctionFormulaNotes
Stating the bottom line first結論として、～を提言するopens with the recommendation rather than building up to it
Naming a benefit～というメリットがあるconcrete, businesslike, less hedged than academic 示唆する
Naming a risk～というリスクが考えられるnames a risk without overstating its likelihood
Naming a next step次のステップとして、～を予定しているcloses with a concrete, scheduled action rather than an open academic limitation
  • 結論として、来年度から多言語対応サービスを試験的に導入することを提言する。In conclusion, I recommend introducing the multilingual support service on a trial basis starting next fiscal year.結論として (bottom-line-first opening)
  • 次のステップとして、来月中に社内説明会を開催する予定である。As a next step, we plan to hold an internal briefing session sometime next month.次のステップとして + 予定である
Language Focus — Professional register is not the same as academic register

Chapter 7 taught that hedged academic claims such as ～と考えられる signal responsible scholarship. Professional Japanese respects careful reasoning too, but it generally expects the recommendation itself to be stated plainly and early, with risk and benefit named explicitly rather than folded into cautious academic hedging. A recommendation memo that hedges its central recommendation as heavily as an academic paper hedges a research finding will often read as indecisive rather than careful.

Grammar Practice

  1. Write an opening sentence using 結論として to recommend adopting a new scheduling system at a small office.

  3. Rewrite the academic-style sentence この結果は、業務の効率が上がる可能性を示唆している。 as a direct professional recommendation using ～を提言する or ～をお勧めします.

Model Dialogue

Setting: 佐藤部長 and Emma review her draft internal recommendation about the proposed multilingual consulting service. Professional register, manager-employee relationship, continued basic keigo.

佐藤部長エマさん、この提言書、よく調べてありますが、結論が最後まで出てこないですね。Emma, this recommendation document is well researched, but the conclusion doesn't appear until the very end.
エマすみません。学校で習った書き方だと、最後に結論を書く形が多かったので。I'm sorry. In the style I learned in school, the conclusion often comes at the end.
佐藤部長なるほど。でも社内の資料では、まず「結論として」で始めて、それから理由を説明する方が読みやすいですよ。I see. But for internal documents, it's easier to read if you start with "結論として" first and explain the reasons afterward.
エマ分かりました。「結論として、試験的な導入を提言する」から始めます。Understood. I'll start with "In conclusion, I recommend a trial introduction."
佐藤部長いいですね。それから、メリットとデメリットも、もう少しはっきり分けて書いてもらえますか。Good. And could you also separate the merits and drawbacks a bit more clearly?
エマはい。メリットとしては費用対効果、デメリットとしては導入にかかる時間、という形でまとめます。Yes. I'll organize it as: merit is cost-effectiveness, drawback is the time the introduction takes.
佐藤部長それと、これは社外のお客様にもお見せする資料なので、社内用よりも少し丁寧な言葉遣いにしてくださいね。Also, since we'll be showing this document to outside clients too, please make the wording a bit more polite than the internal version.

Writing Workshop

Draft a short executive summary recommending a course of action, applying the recommendation, risk/benefit, and next-step frames from this chapter.

  1. Choose a topic. Select a plausible workplace, school, or community decision that you can discuss, real or invented for practice.
  2. Open with the conclusion. Use 結論として or an equivalent frame to state your recommendation in the first sentence.
  3. Name one merit using ～というメリットがある or an equivalent construction.
  4. Name one drawback or risk using ～というリスクが考えられる or a similar frame.
  5. Name one next step using 次のステップとして～予定である.
  6. Write a one-sentence note in Japanese identifying whether your summary is intended for an internal or external audience, and why that affected your word choice.

チェックリスト — Revision checklist:

  • The summary opens with a clear, direct recommendation rather than saving it for the end.
  • At least one merit and one drawback or risk are named explicitly.
  • A concrete next step is named, not an open-ended academic limitation.
  • Register is consistent with the stated audience, internal or external.
  • Terminology and abbreviations are used consistently, with rare readings supported only where genuinely needed.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Internal consensus before external presentation

Many Japanese organizations place strong value on building internal agreement, sometimes called 根回し, before a recommendation is formally presented to outside stakeholders or decision-makers. A document like Emma's draft is often circulated internally for comment well before its conclusions are treated as final, which is one reason 佐藤部長 wants an internal explanatory meeting before the recommendation reaches a client. This practice varies by organization, industry, and generation, and it should not be treated as a fixed rule for every Japanese workplace.

Regional Note — Professional register across industries and organizations

Professional register in Japanese is shaped more by industry, company culture, and the internal-external distinction than by regional dialect. A recommendation memo written for a technology startup, a hospital, and a municipal office will differ in terminology and formality, but these differences are domain-based rather than regional. Where regional variation does appear in a professional setting, it tends to concern spoken interaction style rather than formal written documents.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Burying the recommendation at the end

Error: Following an academic-style structure that saves the recommendation for the final sentence of a business document, forcing a busy reader to read the entire text to find the main point.

Correction: Open with the recommendation using a frame such as 結論として、～を提言する, then follow with supporting reasons, risks, and next steps.

Common Mistake — Overusing academic hedging in a business recommendation

Error: Writing a business recommendation as cautiously as an academic hypothesis, for example 導入する可能性があると考えられるかもしれない。

Correction: State the recommendation plainly while still naming genuine risks separately: 導入をお勧めします。ただし、需要に関するリスクも考慮すべきです。

Common Mistake — Using the same wording for internal and external audiences

Error: Sending an external client the same casually worded internal draft without adjusting formality or softening direct statements.

Correction: Prepare a more formal version for external readers, adjusting keigo, softening direct claims where appropriate, and confirming that internal-only information has been removed.

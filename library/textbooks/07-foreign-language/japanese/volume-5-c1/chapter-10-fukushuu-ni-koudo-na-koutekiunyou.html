復習２：高度な公的運用Unit II Assessment

Advanced formal, academic, professional, and coordination use · Speak and Write Japanese: Mastery, Specialization, and Expert Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Demonstrate integrated control of the advanced keigo, institutional language, academic reading and writing, professional domain communication, and negotiation and coordination systems developed in Chapters 6 through 9.
  2. Move fluidly between respectful and humble language, academic phrase frames, professional domain register, and conditional negotiation language within a single extended task.
  3. Read a longer, multi-part professional document that combines a formal request, background information, and a proposed resolution, and identify how register shifts across its parts.
  4. Listen to a longer excerpt combining a formal announcement and a coordination exchange and extract the key commitments made by each speaker.
  5. Write a multi-part professional message that opens with appropriate institutional formality, presents information in an academic-adjacent style, and closes with a calibrated, conditionally framed proposal.
  6. Deliver a short spoken exchange that shifts register appropriately as the situation moves from formal opening, to professional explanation, to negotiated resolution.
  7. Self-assess performance against the cumulative criteria for Unit II before proceeding to Unit III.

Communication Scenario

The trial multilingual consulting project with the municipal office has reached its midpoint review. 佐藤部長 asks Emma to prepare the materials for a joint review meeting: a short formal briefing document summarizing the project's progress for the municipal office's senior staff, a spoken update for the meeting itself, and a follow-up plan addressing the translation delay from Chapter 9 and a newly identified staffing question from Chapter 8. The meeting will include people Emma has never addressed directly — some more senior than 山本様, the contact she has worked with so far — so she must judge, in real time, how formal her language should be for each part of the exchange: the opening greeting, the academic-style progress summary, the professional recommendation about staffing, and the negotiated resolution of the translation schedule. This chapter asks the learner to bring together everything practiced across Chapters 6 through 9 into a single connected task, rather than treating each system as a separate skill.

もじ ふくしゅう — Script and Typography Review

Script and Typography Review

This chapter reviews the script and typography strategies developed across Unit II: institutional formulas and official terms (Chapter 6), academic kanji, terminology, and citation-like formatting with verified names and titles (Chapter 7), specialized terminology, abbreviations, charts, and tables (Chapter 8), and notices organized by problem, cause, and response (Chapter 9). No new script conventions are introduced here.

Script Strategy — Reading a document that shifts typography across sections

A single professional document, such as the briefing below, may shift typography section by section: an opening greeting in dense keigo with few labels, a middle section using academic-style headers and enumerated points, and a closing section labeled by function, such as 現状, 原因, and 対応策. Before reading closely, scan the whole document once to notice where these shifts occur, since each section calls for a different reading strategy.

Pronunciation Review

Pronunciation Review — Shifting prosody across a register change

Across Unit II, formal openings and ceremonial keigo (Chapter 6) call for a measured, slightly slower pace with clear phrase-final lowering. Academic and professional explanation (Chapters 7 and 8) call for a more even, steady pace with clear phrase grouping around enumerated points. Apology and negotiation language (Chapter 9) calls for a deliberate slowing on the apology itself, followed by a more forward-moving pace on the proposal. This chapter's listening and speaking tasks ask you to move through all three prosodic styles within a single extended exchange, so practice noticing the shift as it happens rather than only producing each style in isolation.

Grammar and Register Review

This chapter assesses formal language, synthesis, professional register, and coordination under constraint, drawing on the authorized systems from Chapters 6 through 9. No new grammar is introduced. The building blocks below are reviewed together because a single real document or exchange often requires moving among them in sequence.

1. Review: Advanced respectful and humble patterns (Chapter 6)

Purpose: to open a formal exchange, refer to one's own actions humbly, and refer to another party's actions respectfully.

  • 本日はお時間をいただき、誠にありがとうございます。Thank you very much for taking the time today.formal opening + humble いただく
  • 資料は拝見しております。I have looked at the materials (humble).拝見する (humble form of 見る)

2. Review: Academic phrase frames and synthesis language (Chapter 7)

Purpose: to summarize progress and evidence in an organized, academic-adjacent style suitable for a briefing document.

  • 本稿では、これまでの進捗を三点にまとめて報告する。In this report, the progress so far is summarized in three points.本稿では + まとめて報告する
  • これまでのデータは、当初の想定より導入期間が長くなることを示唆している。The data so far suggest that the introduction period will be longer than originally expected.示唆している (evidentially cautious claim)

3. Review: Professional domain register and recommendation frames (Chapter 8)

Purpose: to present a professional recommendation with risk and benefit language, appropriate to an executive summary.

  • 現在の体制のままでは、担当者一名あたりの負担が大きいというリスクがあります。If the current structure is left as is, there is a risk that the burden on each staff member will be large.リスクがあります (professional risk framing)
  • 増員のメリットは負担軽減ですが、デメリットは追加コストです。The advantage of adding staff is reduced burden, but the disadvantage is additional cost.メリット／デメリット paired evaluation

4. Review: Conditional mitigation and calibrated apology (Chapter 9)

Purpose: to name a condition for cooperation and calibrate an apology to the true severity of a problem.

  • 優先度の高い部分を先にお渡しできれば、全体の遅れは最小限に抑えられます。If we can deliver the highest-priority part first, the overall delay can be kept to a minimum.できれば conditional + result clause
  • ご迷惑をおかけし、申し訳ございません。We apologize for the inconvenience caused.standard formal apology for an acknowledged problem
Language Focus — Moving among registers within one document or exchange

A single briefing document or meeting rarely stays in one register throughout. A realistic sequence often opens with the respectful and humble keigo from Chapter 6, moves into the academic-style synthesis from Chapter 7 to summarize progress, shifts into the professional risk-and-recommendation language of Chapter 8 to address a staffing question, and closes with the conditional, calibrated negotiation language of Chapter 9 to resolve an outstanding delay. Treat the boundary between sections, not the whole document, as the place where register changes.

Grammar Review Practice

  1. Write a two-sentence formal opening using humble language appropriate for greeting senior staff you have not addressed before.
  2. Write a three-point academic-style summary of a project's progress using 本稿では and at least one evidentially cautious claim.
  3. Write one sentence presenting a staffing risk and one sentence presenting its corresponding benefit, in the paired style of Chapter 8.
  4. Write a conditional proposal and a calibrated apology addressing a delay, choosing the apology formula appropriate to a clearly acknowledged, moderate problem.

Integrated Dialogue

Setting: the midpoint review meeting. Emma addresses 中村部長, a senior municipal staff member she has not met before, together with the familiar contact 山本様. The exchange moves from formal opening, to progress summary, to a staffing recommendation, to a negotiated resolution of the translation delay.

エマ本日はお時間をいただき、誠にありがとうございます。エマと申します。Thank you very much for taking the time today. My name is Emma.
中村部長こちらこそ、よろしくお願いいたします。進捗を伺えればと思います。Likewise, thank you. I'd like to hear about the progress.
エマはい。本稿では、これまでの進捗を三点にまとめてご報告いたします。まず、相談件数は当初の想定通りに推移しております。Yes. I will report on progress so far in three points. First, the number of consultations is proceeding as originally expected.
エマ一方で、データからは、担当者一名あたりの負担が想定より大きくなっていることが示唆されております。増員にはコストというデメリットもございますが、負担軽減という明確なメリットがございます。On the other hand, the data suggest that the burden per staff member has become larger than expected. Adding staff has the disadvantage of cost, but has the clear advantage of reduced burden.
中村部長なるほど。その点は検討します。ところで、翻訳資料の件はどうなっていますか。I see. We'll consider that point. By the way, what is the status of the translation document?
エマご迷惑をおかけし、申し訳ございません。優先度の高い部分は既にお渡しし、残りは今週中にお渡しできる見込みです。We apologize for the inconvenience. The highest-priority part has already been delivered, and the rest is expected to be delivered within this week.
山本様はい、優先度の高い部分は確かに受け取りました。助かりました。Yes, we did indeed receive the high-priority part. That helped a great deal.
エマ承知いたしました。それでは、増員の件についても来週までにご提案をまとめ、改めてご連絡いたします。Understood. Then, regarding the staffing matter as well, I will put together a proposal by next week and contact you again.

Emma notes three follow-up actions: complete delivery of the remaining translation materials this week, prepare a staffing proposal by next week, and follow up with both 中村部長 and 山本様.

ないようりかい — Comprehension check: Identify where the register shifts from formal opening, to academic-style summary, to professional recommendation, to negotiated resolution. What follow-up actions does Emma commit to?

Writing Assessment

Draft a multi-part professional message for the midpoint review, applying the humble/respectful opening, academic-style summary, professional risk-benefit framing, and conditional negotiation systems reviewed in this chapter.

  1. Open formally. Write a one- to two-sentence humble and respectful opening appropriate for addressing senior staff you have not met before.
  2. Summarize progress academically. Write a three-point summary using 本稿では and at least one evidentially cautious claim such as 示唆されている.
  3. Present one professional recommendation using a paired risk-and-benefit structure.
  4. Resolve one outstanding problem using a calibrated apology and a specific conditional proposal.
  5. Close with named follow-up commitments, stating what will happen and by when.
  6. Write a one-sentence note in Japanese explaining why you placed the register shifts where you did.

チェックリスト — Revision checklist:

  • The opening register is appropriately formal for addressing unfamiliar senior staff.
  • The progress summary uses academic-style framing and at least one evidentially cautious claim.
  • The recommendation presents both a risk and a benefit rather than only one side.
  • The apology matches the actual severity of the problem addressed.
  • The conditional proposal names a specific condition and a specific outcome.
  • Follow-up commitments are specific and dated.
  • Register remains internally consistent within each section of the message.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Register as a map of relationship, not a single fixed formality level

The Integrated Dialogue and Model Briefing Document above shift register several times within a single meeting or document. This is not inconsistency; it reflects that different communicative acts — greeting an unfamiliar senior person, summarizing evidence, recommending a course of action, and negotiating a resolution — carry different conventional registers even within one continuous professional interaction. Treating an entire meeting as requiring one unchanging formality level often produces language that sounds stiff in some places and too casual in others.

Regional Note — Review of organizational variation in escalation and formality

As introduced in Chapter 9, how formally a meeting proceeds and how quickly a problem is escalated to senior staff varies by organization and industry more than by region. The same principle applies to how a single meeting moves among the registers reviewed in this chapter: some organizations expect nearly uniform formality throughout, while others expect a visible shift in tone as the topic moves from ceremony to problem-solving.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Holding one register throughout an entire multi-part exchange

Error: Using the same dense keigo throughout a briefing, including inside the academic-style summary, where it becomes unnecessarily heavy, or using the same plain professional tone throughout, including the formal opening, where it sounds under-formal.

Correction: Match register to function within the same document: humble and respectful for the opening, more even academic framing for the summary, professional but direct language for recommendations, and calibrated apology and conditional language for negotiation.

Common Mistake — Presenting a recommendation with only a risk or only a benefit

Error: Stating only the risk of the current staffing structure, or only the benefit of adding staff, without acknowledging the corresponding trade-off, which can make a recommendation sound one-sided or unbalanced.

Correction: Present both sides explicitly, as in Chapter 8: 増員にはコストというデメリットがありますが、負担軽減という明確なメリットがあります。

Common Mistake — Reopening a resolved problem with a fresh apology instead of confirming status

Error: Apologizing again for a problem that has already been substantially resolved, such as re-apologizing for a delay after the priority portion has already been delivered, which can needlessly reopen a settled matter.

Correction: Briefly acknowledge the earlier issue, then move to confirming current status: 優先度の高い部分は既にお渡しし、残りは今週中にお渡しできる見込みです。

翻訳・通訳・ローカライゼーションTranslation, Interpreting, and Localization

Advanced mediation and adaptation · Speak and Write Japanese: Mastery, Specialization, and Expert Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Discuss translation, interpreting, and localization using appropriate vocabulary for literary excerpts, public notices, websites, subtitles, product text, speeches, summaries, and localization briefs.
  2. Apply register transfer, equivalence, and omission/explication strategies when moving meaning between Japanese and English.
  3. Maintain terminology consistency across a short multi-sentence text and explain why consistency matters for a mediated document.
  4. Adapt a source text for a specified audience, distinguishing translation from adaptation and summary.
  5. Describe the practical constraints of subtitle translation, including length, timing, and register compression.
  6. Interpret a short spoken passage and produce a mediated summary for a defined audience, orally and in writing.
  7. Distinguish faithful rendering of meaning from personal embellishment, and explain why unverifiable additions are a mediation error.

Communication Scenario

Emma's reading circle has decided to prepare a small multilingual welcome packet for new members, some of whom read only English and some of whom read only Japanese. The task requires more than word-for-word conversion: a short public notice about meeting times needs to sound natural and appropriately polite in both languages, a paragraph from the group's favorite short story needs a faithful but readable English rendering, and a two-line video introduction needs subtitle-length captions in Japanese. Emma also volunteers to interpret informally for a Japanese-speaking guest during the next meeting, summarizing the English discussion in real time. Reflecting on Chapter 13's lesson that description should not become ranking, Emma wants her mediation work to represent both languages faithfully, without smoothing over real differences or inventing details that were not in the source.

  1. The learner studies vocabulary for literary excerpts, public notices, websites, subtitles, product text, speeches, summaries, and localization briefs.
  2. The learner studies register transfer, equivalence, omission/explication, terminology consistency, audience adaptation, and subtitle constraints.
  3. The learner reads a short localization brief and a paired source/target text pair.
  4. The learner listens to a short spoken passage and produces a mediated summary.
  5. The learner writes a short adapted text for a specified audience.
  6. The learner completes a performance task combining all four skills around translation, interpreting, and localization.

もじ — Script and Typography Focus

Script and Typography Focus

This chapter's script focus is bilingual typography, ruby, names, measurements, dates, and localization conventions, as authorized by the volume scope. Presenting a source and target text side by side, or embedding one language's convention inside another, requires deliberate typographic choices.

Script Strategy — Presenting bilingual and localized text accurately

When a Japanese passage and its English rendering appear together, keep the two clearly distinguished, for example through labeling, spacing, or a consistent visual pattern, rather than interleaving them in a way that could blur which language says what. Use lang="ja" markup on Japanese spans in any HTML localization output, as required by the Japanese master instructions.

Measurements, dates, and numbers frequently need conversion or reformatting rather than direct transcription. A date written 2026年7月11日 in a Japanese source is conventionally rendered as "July 11, 2026" in English prose, not copied in numeral order. Prices, addresses, and honorific name order should follow the target language's convention unless the localization brief specifies otherwise. When a personal name's reading is uncertain, mark it with furigana in the Japanese version rather than guessing, following the standing project policy that name readings are never generated from kanji alone.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Prosody for spoken interpreting

Interpreting places extra demands on phrase-level pitch and rhythm, since a listener must follow meaning in real time without a written text to fall back on. Maintain clear phrase boundaries, avoid rushing through information-dense noun phrases, and use pitch and pacing to signal where one idea ends and the next begins. This chapter's target remains the contemporary standard pattern already established in this volume; the goal here is applying that pattern under the added pressure of real-time mediation, not acquiring a new pronunciation system.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter authorizes register transfer, equivalence, omission/explication, terminology consistency, audience adaptation, and subtitle constraints, building on the comparative and classification systems consolidated in Chapter 13 and the nominalization, clause-linking, and stance systems consolidated earlier in this volume. No new clause-level paradigm is introduced; the focus is on applying existing systems to describe and perform mediation between languages.

1. Register transfer: 〜調で and 〜向けに

Purpose: to specify the style or audience that a translated or localized text is being produced for.

  • この案内は丁寧な調で訳す必要がある。This notice needs to be translated in a polite register.〜調で (register specification, productive)
  • この文章は初めて日本を訪れる旅行者向けに書かれている。This text is written for travelers visiting Japan for the first time.〜向けに (audience targeting, productive)

2. Equivalence and free versus literal rendering: 〜に相当する and 意訳する

Purpose: to describe how closely a translated form corresponds to the source, and to justify a translation choice.

  • この表現は英語の "break the ice" に相当する。This expression corresponds to the English phrase "break the ice."〜に相当する (equivalence marker, productive)
  • 直訳すると不自然になるので、ここは意訳した。Because a literal translation would sound unnatural here, I translated this part more freely.意訳する (free-translation justification, productive)

3. Omission and explicitation: 〜を省略する and 〜を補足する

Purpose: to describe a deliberate choice to remove or add information during mediation, and to state the reason.

  • 字幕の文字数制限のため、細かい説明は省略した。Because of the subtitle character limit, I omitted the detailed explanation.〜を省略する (omission, productive)
  • 読者が背景を知らない可能性があるので、一文補足した。Since readers might not know the background, I added one supplementary sentence.〜を補足する (explicitation, productive)
Building Block — 〜ようにする（用語統一の「ようにする」）

[term] は一貫して〜と訳すようにする。

The consolidated intentional-effort use of ようにする, applied here to describe a deliberate ongoing practice of consistency rather than a single act. It frames terminology consistency as a standing policy the translator follows throughout a document.

この指示書では、"ローカライゼーション" は一貫してこの語で訳すようにする。

In this brief, the term is to be translated consistently using this same word throughout.

4. Subtitle and length constraints: 〜以内に収める

Purpose: to describe fitting a translation within a fixed length, time, or space constraint.

  • 字幕は一行あたり十四文字以内に収める必要がある。Subtitles need to be kept within fourteen characters per line.〜以内に収める (length constraint, productive)
Language Focus — Faithful mediation, not personal rewriting

The patterns in this chapter describe deliberate, explainable choices: a register chosen for an audience, an equivalence justified by meaning, an omission required by a length constraint, an explanation added because the target audience lacks context. They do not license inventing details that were not in the source, softening a source's actual meaning without labeling the change, or adding personal opinion to someone else's words. When you translate, interpret, or localize, keep a clear line between what the source said and what you added to make it work in the target language, and be ready to explain any addition or omission you made.

Grammar Practice

  1. Write one sentence specifying a register or audience for a translation using 〜調で or 〜向けに.
  2. Write one sentence justifying a free or literal translation choice using 〜に相当する or 意訳する.
  3. Write one sentence describing an omission or an added explanation using 〜を省略する or 〜を補足する.
  4. Write one sentence about terminology consistency using ようにする, and one sentence about a length constraint using 〜以内に収める.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Emma meets with 田中先生 to review her draft welcome-packet translations before the next reading-circle meeting.

エマ先生、この案内文なんですが、直訳すると変な感じになってしまって。「毎月第二土曜日に集まります」を英語にするとき、どう考えればいいでしょうか。Sensei, about this notice — when I translate it literally it ends up sounding strange. When I turn "We meet on the second Saturday of every month" into English, how should I think about it?
田中先生いいところに気づきましたね。ここは直訳よりも意訳のほうが自然です。大切なのは、日付の表現そのものではなく、「いつ集まるか」という情報が読者に正確に伝わることです。You've noticed something important. Here, a free translation is more natural than a literal one. What matters isn't the date expression itself, but making sure the information about when the group meets is accurately conveyed to the reader.
エマ字幕のほうも見ていただけますか。動画の紹介文を日本語字幕にしたんですが、文字数が多すぎる気がします。Could you also look at the subtitles? I turned the video's introduction into Japanese captions, but I feel like there are too many characters.
田中先生確かに少し長いですね。すべての情報を訳そうとせず、一番伝えたい部分だけを残して、あとは省略しましょう。字幕は読む時間が限られているので、簡潔さが正確さと同じくらい重要です。It is indeed a bit long. Rather than trying to translate every piece of information, let's keep only the part you most want to convey and omit the rest. Since subtitles only have limited reading time, conciseness is just as important as accuracy.
エマ最後に、短編小説の一文なんですが、原文にない説明を少し補足したくなりました。これはしてもいいことでしょうか。Lastly, about one sentence from the short story — I found myself wanting to add a bit of explanation that isn't in the original. Is this something I'm allowed to do?
田中先生補足説明は、読者の理解のために必要な場合はよくある手法です。ただし、原文にない個人的な解釈や感想を加えるのは別の問題です。補足するときは、なぜそれが必要なのかを自分で説明できるようにしておいてください。Supplementary explanation is a common technique when it's necessary for the reader's understanding. However, adding personal interpretation or impressions that aren't in the source is a different matter. When you add something, make sure you can explain to yourself why it was necessary.

田中先生 distinguishes free translation, subtitle-driven omission, and explicitation as three separate, justifiable techniques, while marking unexplained personal addition as a distinct problem from all three.

Writing Workshop

Write a short localization brief and a paired translation of your own, applying the systems from this chapter.

  1. Choose a short Japanese or English source text of two to four sentences — a notice, a product description, or a short informational passage — and state its original audience.
  2. Write a brief localization instruction in Japanese specifying the new target readership, the intended register, using 〜向けに or 〜調で, and any length constraint.
  3. Produce your translation, making at least one deliberate free-translation choice and describing it using 意訳する or 〜に相当する.
  4. Note one omission or one added explanation in your translation, using 〜を省略する or 〜を補足する, with a stated reason.
  5. Confirm terminology consistency for any term that repeats, using ようにする.
  6. Write a one-sentence note in Japanese explaining which source of information (this chapter, the Model Dialogue, your own judgment) guided your key translation decision.

チェックリスト — Revision checklist:

  • The localization brief names a target readership, register, and any length constraint.
  • At least one free-translation choice is identified and justified.
  • Any omission or added explanation is stated with a clear reason.
  • Repeated terms are translated consistently throughout.
  • No unexplained personal interpretation or invented detail has been added to the source's meaning.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Politeness levels do not always translate directly

A Japanese public notice or product description often uses a level of politeness, indirectness, or formal nominal style that does not map onto a single fixed English register. English public notices can sound natural while being considerably more direct than a comparable Japanese original, and an overly literal translation of Japanese formality markers can make an English text sound stiff or overly deferential. Skilled localization adjusts the register to what is natural and appropriate in the target language and context, rather than preserving the source's formality markers word for word.

Regional Note — Localization briefs vary by market, not just by language

A single language can require different localization choices for different markets or communities. Japanese used in a notice for a general Japan-based audience may differ from Japanese localized for a Japanese-speaking diaspora community, or for a Ryukyuan- or Ainu-language-aware regional audience such as the ones discussed in Chapter 13. A localization brief should specify not just "Japanese" but the intended readership and setting, since assuming one uniform "Japanese audience" can miss real variation in expectation and background knowledge.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Translating word for word regardless of naturalness

Error: Producing a direct, word-order-preserving rendering that sounds unnatural or confusing in the target language, on the assumption that literal equals accurate.

Correction: Prioritize 等価 ("equivalence") of meaning and effect over surface form, using 意訳する when a literal rendering would be unnatural, as modeled in the Model Dialogue above.

Common Mistake — Adding unexplained personal interpretation

Error: Inserting an opinion, emotional coloring, or extra detail that is not in the source and cannot be traced back to a stated reason, then presenting it as part of the translation.

Correction: Reserve additions for genuine 補足説明 that helps the target reader understand something the source assumed, and be ready to state the reason for every addition, as 田中先生 emphasizes in the Model Dialogue.

Common Mistake — Using inconsistent terminology across one document

Error: Translating the same recurring term differently in different sentences of the same document, for example rendering 読書会 as "reading circle" in one place and "book club" in another.

Correction: Establish one target term per source concept at the start of a project and apply it consistently, using 用語統一 and the ようにする pattern from the Building Block above.

高度な敬語と制度的言語Advanced Keigo and Institutional Language

Ceremonial, professional, and institutional nuance across executive communication, announcements, formal requests, apologies, ceremonies, and interviews · Speak and Write Japanese: Mastery, Specialization, and Expert Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Distinguish respectful language (尊敬語), humble language (謙譲語), and polite language (丁寧語) at an operational, institutional level, rather than as isolated verb substitutions.
  2. Recognize and interpret advanced keigo patterns used in executive announcements, formal requests, apologies, ceremonies, and interviews.
  3. Produce, within the authorized systems of this chapter, formally correct requests, apologies, and announcements appropriate to a professional or institutional setting.
  4. Identify nominal, indirect, and protocol-driven phrasing typical of formal institutional Japanese, and explain why it differs from ordinary polite conversation.
  5. Read a short formal announcement or notice and identify its keigo forms, its structure, and its intended audience.
  6. Listen to a formal interview excerpt and distinguish the interviewer's respectful language from the interviewee's humble self-reference.
  7. Avoid common overuse and misuse patterns in advanced keigo, including double honorifics and mismatched respectful/humble direction.
  8. Explain, in Japanese, why a particular keigo choice was appropriate for a specific audience and situation.

Communication Scenario

With her dialect article published, Emma is invited to observe a very different kind of Japanese: the language of institutions. Her workplace, a regional cultural foundation, is preparing for its annual public symposium. 山本さん asks Emma to help draft the opening announcement, a formal letter requesting a guest speaker's participation, and a short apology notice about a schedule change — all before she sits in on a recorded interview with the foundation's director. Emma quickly notices that this is a different register entirely from her earlier writing: dense with respectful and humble forms, built from nominal phrases rather than casual verbs, and governed by conventions she has only seen in passing before. 山本さん explains that mastering this register is less about memorizing a list of polite verbs and more about understanding whose action is being described, and how much social distance the situation requires.

もじ — Script and Typography Focus

Script and Typography Focus

This chapter introduces no new kanji readings beyond the vocabulary above. The focus instead is on how institutional formulas and official terms are typeset: formal notices commonly use full-width honorific prefixes (ご, お), consistent vertical or horizontal letterhead conventions, and minimal furigana, since the intended audience is assumed to be literate in the relevant institutional vocabulary.

Script Strategy — Furigana in institutional documents

Formal announcements and letters typically carry little or no furigana, even over words that would receive furigana in a general-audience newsletter, because the assumed reader is an adult professional audience. When this chapter's reading materials include furigana, it is reserved for names, rare readings, or terms genuinely uncommon outside institutional contexts, consistent with the volume's furigana-reduction policy.

れんしゅう — Practice: Before reading the Model Announcement below, predict which of this chapter's vocabulary items are likely to appear without furigana in an authentic institutional notice, and which might still carry furigana for a general audience.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Formal presentation prosody

Formal spoken Japanese in announcements, ceremonies, and interviews favors a measured pace, clear phrase-final pauses, and a narrower pitch range than everyday conversation, without becoming monotone. Long humble and respectful constructions such as お願い申し上げます are typically produced as a single connected phrase rather than broken into separate words, so practice saying them smoothly from the honorific prefix through to the final verb. Continue to maintain accurate mora timing, vowel length, and gemination throughout, even at this slower, more deliberate pace.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter authorizes advanced respectful and humble patterns, formula choice for requests and apologies, nominal style typical of formal documents, strategic indirectness, and protocol language used in ceremonies and interviews.

1. Respectful language for another's action

Purpose: to describe the actions,状態, or possessions of a person to whom respect is owed — a guest, a superior, a customer, or an audience member — without using their name repeatedly.

Building Block
お + verb stem + になる／ご + verbal noun + になる
Respectful (尊敬語) pattern: raises the status of the person performing the action.
来賓の皆様には、まもなくご到着になります。
Our honored guests will be arriving shortly.
  • 先生は、すでに資料をお読みになりました。The professor has already read the materials.お + 読み + になる (respectful)
  • 理事長がご挨拶になります。The chairperson will give the greeting.ご + 挨拶 + になる (respectful, verbal noun)

2. Humble language for one's own action

Purpose: to lower the status of the speaker's own action in order to raise the relative status of the listener or a third party, especially in requests, apologies, and formal acknowledgments.

Building Block
お／ご + verb stem or verbal noun + 申し上げる／いたす
Humble (謙譲語) pattern: lowers the speaker's own action relative to the listener.
式典へのご出席を、心よりお願い申し上げます。
We humbly and sincerely request your attendance at the ceremony.
  • この度はご迷惑をおかけし、深くお詫び申し上げます。We deeply and humbly apologize for the trouble caused this time.お + 詫び + 申し上げる (humble)
  • 資料は事前に拝見しております。I have already had the honor of looking at the materials in advance.拝見する, humble verb replacing 見る

3. Formal request and apology formulas

Purpose: to open and close formal requests and apologies with recognizable, socially expected phrasing, rather than constructing each sentence from scratch.

FunctionFormulaNotes
Opening a formal requestご多忙のところ恐縮ですがacknowledges the recipient's time before making the request
Making the request～のほど、お願い申し上げますsoftens the request through indirect nominal phrasing
Opening an apologyこの度は～につきましてintroduces the matter being apologized for formally
Closing an apology深くお詫び申し上げますstandard formal closing for a written or spoken apology

4. Nominal style and indirectness in institutional writing

Purpose: to recognize that formal institutional Japanese favors noun-centered phrasing (～のほど, ～につきまして) over direct verbal requests, softening the force of a demand or announcement.

  • 式典の日程変更につきまして、下記の通りご案内申し上げます。Regarding the change in the ceremony's schedule, we inform you as follows.につきまして (formal "regarding") + humble ご案内申し上げる
  • ご確認のほど、よろしくお願いいたします。We kindly ask for your confirmation.のほど softens a direct request into a general "matter of"
Language Focus — Direction of respect, not a fixed list

Respectful and humble forms are not simply "polite" and "very polite" versions of the same verb. Respectful language raises the person whose action is being described; humble language lowers the speaker's own action. Using a humble form for a guest's action, or a respectful form for your own action, reverses the intended meaning and is a serious error in institutional Japanese, even though both forms sound "formal" to a learner's ear.

  2. Rewrite 資料を見ました。 using a humble form appropriate for a formal report to a superior.
  3. Combine the opening and closing formulas above into a short formal request asking a guest speaker to confirm their attendance.
  4. Identify which of the two example sentences under "Nominal style and indirectness" would sound too indirect in ordinary polite conversation with a colleague.

Model Dialogue

Setting: 山本さん and Emma draft the opening lines of the symposium announcement together. Formal written register, workplace mentor-colleague relationship.

山本この案内文、まず来賓の方々への敬意を示す文から始めましょう。Let's start this announcement with a sentence that shows respect toward our honored guests.
エマ「来賓の皆様には、ご多忙のところご出席を賜り、誠にありがとうございます」でよろしいでしょうか。Would "To our honored guests, we sincerely thank you for your attendance despite your busy schedules" be appropriate?
山本いいですね。「賜り」は少し格式が高いですが、式典の冒頭にはふさわしい表現です。That's good. "賜り" is a fairly formal expression, but it's appropriate for the opening of a ceremony.
エマ次に、日程変更についてお詫びする文を入れる必要がありますよね。Next, we need to include a sentence apologizing for the schedule change, right?
山本そうですね。「この度は日程変更によりご迷惑をおかけし、深くお詫び申し上げます」としましょう。That's right. Let's write "We deeply apologize for the inconvenience caused by this schedule change."
エマ私が「ご迷惑をおかけし」と、自分たちの側の行為を下げて言うんですね。来賓側の行為には使えない、ということですか。So I use "causing you inconvenience" to lower our own side's action. Does that mean this can't be used for the guests' actions?
山本その通りです。逆にしてしまうと、意味が反対になってしまいますから、気をつけてください。Exactly right. If you reverse it, the meaning flips entirely, so please be careful.

Emma marks the draft with two colors: one for respectful forms describing the guests, and one for humble forms describing the foundation's own staff.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Keigo as social structure, not decoration

Advanced keigo in institutional settings encodes relationships among an organization, its guests, its staff, and the public, rather than simply adding formality for its own sake. A well-constructed formal notice signals who is being honored, who is speaking humbly, and what social distance the situation requires, and misjudging these relationships can affect how sincerely an apology or request is received, independent of its literal content.

Regional Note — Institutional register varies less by region than everyday speech

Formal institutional Japanese used in national organizations, government notices, and large companies tends to vary much less by region than everyday conversational speech, since it follows nationally recognized professional conventions. Regional influence is more likely to surface in the surrounding spoken delivery, such as at an informal reception after a formal ceremony, than in the written notice itself.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Reversing respectful and humble direction

Error: Using a humble form such as 拝見する to describe a guest's action, or a respectful form such as ご覧になる to describe one's own action.

Correction: Always identify whose action is being described before choosing the form: respectful forms raise another person's action; humble forms lower the speaker's own action. 来賓が資料をご覧になる (respectful, guest's action) versus 私が資料を拝見する (humble, speaker's action).

Common Mistake — Double honorifics

Error: Stacking two respectful markers on the same verb, such as お読みになられる, which combines the respectful お～になる pattern with the passive-derived respectful ～られる unnecessarily.

Correction: Choose one respectful pattern per verb. Use お読みになる or 読まれる, not both together, unless a specific institutional convention explicitly calls for the doubled form.

Common Mistake — Overusing advanced keigo in ordinary polite conversation

Error: Using ceremonial-level formulas such as 賜る or 所存でございます in an everyday polite conversation with a colleague or acquaintance.

Correction: Reserve the most formal ceremonial vocabulary for genuinely formal written notices, ceremonies, and high-stakes institutional speech; ordinary polite です・ます style remains appropriate for everyday professional interaction.

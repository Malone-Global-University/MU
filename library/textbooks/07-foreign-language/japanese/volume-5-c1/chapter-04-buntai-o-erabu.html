文体を選ぶChoosing Style

Genre, voice, persona, and audience across academic prose, correspondence, public explanation, essays, narrative, speech, commentary, and translation style · Speak and Write Japanese: Mastery, Specialization, and Expert Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify the genre expectations of academic prose, correspondence, public explanation, essays, narrative, speech, commentary, and translation, and describe how each shapes word and sentence choice.
  2. Perform a deliberate style shift on the same content for two different genres or audiences without changing its underlying meaning.
  3. Control sentence length and rhythm as a stylistic tool, distinguishing short, direct sentences from longer, layered ones.
  4. Balance nominal and verbal style, recognizing when a noun-heavy, clause-condensing style suits a genre and when a more verbal, event-by-event style suits it better.
  5. Adjust honorific density up or down according to genre and audience, without over- or under-marking respect.
  6. Distinguish authorial voice from persona, and adapt persona to a stated audience and communicative purpose.
  7. Read a short passage and identify its genre, voice, and audience design from formal and stylistic evidence alone.
  8. Revise a single piece of writing into two genuinely different genres while preserving its core content.

Communication Scenario

Emma's article on regional dialect is nearly ready, but 山本さん has a new request: the magazine wants a short, warmer version of the same material for its website's general-audience newsletter, alongside the original, denser print article. Emma is startled to realize these are not simply "the same words, shorter" — they are two different pieces of writing with two different relationships to the reader. 山本さん shows her how the print article's long, nominal, evidence-stacked sentences would sound cold and inaccessible in the newsletter, and how the newsletter's short, warmer, more verbal sentences would sound underqualified in the print edition. Over this chapter, Emma learns to hear genre the way she has learned to hear register: not as one right way to write, but as a set of deliberate choices that must match the piece's purpose and reader.

  1. The learner listens to 山本さん explaining why the same content needs two different styles for two different publications.
  2. The learner compares two versions of the same paragraph: the dense print original and the warmer newsletter rewrite.
  3. The learner studies four interacting style systems: genre and audience design, sentence length and rhythm, nominal/verbal balance, and honorific density and persona.
  4. The learner practices shifting a single passage between two genres while keeping its content intact.
  5. The learner rewrites one paragraph of their own writing into two contrasting genres.
  6. The learner explains, in Japanese, which stylistic features they changed and why each genre required that change.

もじ — Script and Typography Focus

Script and Typography Focus

This chapter introduces no new kanji. Its script focus is on how typography, punctuation, and layout themselves signal genre before a reader has read a single word.

Script Strategy — Typography as a genre signal

A dense academic article typically uses long, unbroken paragraphs, minimal furigana, and conservative punctuation, while a general-audience newsletter typically uses shorter paragraphs, occasional subheadings, more generous furigana on less common kanji, and a friendlier use of exclamation marks or ellipses. Ruby markup should be adjusted for genre: a print article aimed at educated adult readers may use furigana sparingly for rare terms only, while a general-audience web piece may support a wider set of readers with more furigana. Neither choice is an error; each is a deliberate accommodation to a stated readership.

れんしゅう — Practice: Take one sentence from Emma's print article in Chapter 3 and describe, in English, three typographic or layout choices you would change if it were reformatted for a general-audience newsletter.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Prosody of formal versus warm delivery

When the same content is read aloud in a formal register versus a warmer, more conversational register, the prosody shifts even when the words are similar. Formal delivery tends toward more even pacing, clearer phrase-final pauses, and less pitch variation between phrases, while warmer delivery allows more pitch movement, faster pacing on familiar words, and closer phrase linking. As you read this chapter's two style-shift passages aloud, notice how your own pacing and pitch range change even before you consciously alter the wording, while keeping mora timing, vowel length, and gemination accurate in both styles.

Style-Shift Systems

This chapter authorizes four interacting systems: genre and audience design, sentence length and rhythm, nominal/verbal balance, and honorific density together with persona and voice. None of these systems introduces new grammar beyond what earlier chapters and volumes have activated. What is new is the expectation that you select and combine these systems deliberately to match a stated genre and audience, and that you can perform the same content in more than one style.

1. Genre and audience design

Purpose: to recognize how genre and readership jointly determine appropriate vocabulary density, sentence complexity, and tone, and to design a piece of writing around a stated audience rather than a generic reader.

GenreTypical Reader RelationshipTypical Stylistic Tendency
Academic proseexpert or student reader seeking rigordense nominal style, high 漢語 density, minimal furigana
Correspondencea known individual or institutioncalibrated keigo, clear opening/closing formulas
Public explanationa general reader needing clarityshorter sentences, more furigana, plain structure
Essay/commentaryan engaged reader open to a stancemixed sentence length, visible authorial voice
Narrativea reader following events over timeverbal style, varied rhythm, scene-setting detail
  • 本稿では、方言使用の減少傾向について論じる。This paper discusses the declining trend in dialect use.academic register — impersonal, nominal opening formula
  • 方言、最近使われなくなってきているんです。The local dialect — it's not really being used much these days.newsletter register — conversational, verbal, warmer particle use
Language Focus — Audience design is not simplification alone

Writing for a general audience is not merely writing "easier" Japanese; it is writing Japanese that assumes a different relationship with the reader. A general-audience piece may still use sophisticated ideas, but it will typically explain them through shorter sentences, more concrete examples, and a warmer authorial presence, rather than through dense, self-contained academic phrasing.

2. Sentence length and rhythm

Purpose: to control sentence length deliberately, using short sentences for emphasis, clarity, or pace, and longer, layered sentences for nuance, qualification, or density.

  • 方言は消えつつある。それは、多くの地域で確認されている変化である。The dialect is disappearing. This is a change confirmed in many regions.two short sentences — clear, deliberate, emphatic pacing
  • 多くの地域で確認されているように、方言は、日常会話の機会が減ることによって、世代を超えてゆっくりと、しかし確実に、使われなくなりつつある。As confirmed in many regions, the dialect, as everyday opportunities to use it decrease, is slowly but surely falling out of use across generations.one long, layered sentence — academic density, multiple qualifications

3. Nominal and verbal balance

Purpose: to choose between a noun-centered (名詞的) style that condenses events and relationships into noun phrases, and a verb-centered (動詞的) style that narrates events step by step, according to genre.

  • 方言使用の減少は、世代間の会話機会の不足に起因する。The decline in dialect use is attributable to a lack of intergenerational conversation opportunities.名詞的 — nominalized cause-effect relationship, academic tone
  • 若い世代とお年寄りが話す機会が減ったから、方言はだんだん使われなくなった。Because opportunities for young people and elders to talk decreased, the dialect gradually stopped being used.動詞的 — the same relationship narrated as a sequence of events

4. Honorific density, persona, and voice

Purpose: to raise or lower honorific density according to genre and audience, and to project a consistent persona and voice appropriate to a stated communicative purpose.

  • ご覧いただいている記事は、方言の現状についてまとめたものでございます。The article you are viewing is a summary of the current state of the dialect.high honorific density — formal institutional persona
  • この記事では、方言の今について紹介します。In this article, we introduce where things stand with the dialect today.moderate honorific density — approachable, informative persona

Style-Shift Practice

  1. Take one factual sentence and write it twice: once with a nominal, condensed structure, and once with a verbal, step-by-step structure.
  2. Write the same short announcement twice: once at high honorific density for a formal institutional persona, and once at moderate honorific density for an approachable persona.
  3. Combine two short sentences into one longer, layered sentence, then explain in English what nuance the combination adds.
  4. Choose one genre from the comparison table and write two sentences that clearly reflect its typical stylistic tendency.

Model Dialogue

Setting: 山本さん and Emma compare the print article draft with a newsletter rewrite at the magazine office. Polite style, mentor-colleague relationship.

山本内容はほとんど同じなのに、この二つ、ずいぶん印象が違いますよね。The content is almost the same, but these two really give a different impression, don't they?
エマはい。雑誌の方は文が長くて、名詞で終わる文も多いです。ニュースレターの方は文が短くて、動詞で終わっていますね。Yes. The magazine version has long sentences, and a lot of them end in nouns. The newsletter version has short sentences and ends in verbs.
山本そうです。雑誌の読者は専門的な内容を求めていますから、名詞的な文体で情報を凝縮したほうが説得力があります。でも、ニュースレターの読者は、もっと気軽に読みたいはずです。That's right. Magazine readers are looking for specialized content, so a nominal style that condenses information is more persuasive. But newsletter readers presumably want to read something more casually.
エマ敬語の使い方も少し変えたほうがいいですか。Should I change how I use honorifics a bit too?
山本ええ。雑誌のほうは少しかしこまった語り口でいいですが、ニュースレターはもっと親しみやすい人格を出したほうが、読者との距離が縮まりますよ。Yes. For the magazine, a somewhat formal narrative voice is fine, but for the newsletter, projecting a friendlier persona will close the distance with readers.
エマ同じ内容でも、読み手に合わせて文体を選ばなければいけないんですね。So even with the same content, I have to choose the style to fit the reader.
山本そのとおりです。文体は、書き手が読み手とどんな関係を築きたいかを表すものですからね。Exactly right. Style is something that expresses what kind of relationship the writer wants to build with the reader.

Emma opens both drafts side by side on her laptop and begins marking each sentence with a small "N" or "V" in the margin.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Genre expectations in Japanese publishing

Japanese publishing and media commonly maintain sharp stylistic distinctions between print journalism, web journalism, institutional notices, and personal or commentary writing, and readers often develop strong expectations for how each genre should sound. A piece that violates these expectations — for example, an official notice written with too casual a tone, or a friendly web column written with too much institutional formality — can strike readers as mismatched even when its content is accurate and well organized.

Regional Note — Style and regional voice in commentary writing

Essays and commentary pieces about regional topics sometimes deliberately incorporate a small amount of regional vocabulary or phrasing into an otherwise standard-Japanese style, as a stylistic signal of authenticity or closeness to the subject. This is a genre-specific choice rather than a general writing practice, and it connects to the regional and community variation this volume returns to in Chapter 13.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Simplifying vocabulary without shifting style

Error: Rewriting a dense academic passage for a general audience by replacing a few difficult words while leaving long, nominal sentence structures and high honorific density unchanged.

Correction: Adjust sentence length, nominal/verbal balance, and honorific density together, not vocabulary alone: 方言使用の減少は世代間の会話機会の不足に起因する。 becomes 若い人とお年寄りが話す機会が減ったから、方言が使われなくなってきた。, not merely a word-for-word simplification of the original sentence.

Common Mistake — Mixing personas within a single piece

Error: Beginning a piece with a formal, distant persona and honorific density, then shifting unpredictably to a casual, warm persona partway through without a genre or audience reason.

Correction: Choose one persona and honorific density for the entire piece, or shift deliberately and visibly at a clear structural boundary, such as between a formal introduction and a quoted informal remark.

Common Mistake — Treating all long sentences as more sophisticated

Error: Assuming that a longer, more layered sentence is always more advanced or impressive, regardless of genre.

Correction: Match sentence length to genre and communicative purpose; a public-explanation genre often benefits from short, clear sentences even at an advanced proficiency level, as in 方言は消えつつある。それは多くの地域で確認されている。

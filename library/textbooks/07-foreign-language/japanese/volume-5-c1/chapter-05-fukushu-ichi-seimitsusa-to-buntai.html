復習１：精密さと文体Unit I Assessment

Integrating precision grammar, vocabulary and collocation mastery, and deliberate style-shift control from Chapters 1 through 4 · Speak and Write Japanese: Mastery, Specialization, and Expert Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Demonstrate fine-grained control of the clause- and predicate-level distinctions developed in Chapter 2, applying them accurately in connected discourse.
  2. Select precise vocabulary, collocations, and idiomatic expressions from Chapter 3 to replace vaguer or less natural alternatives.
  3. Perform a deliberate style shift on a single piece of content, applying the four systems from Chapter 4: genre and audience design, sentence length and rhythm, nominal/verbal balance, and honorific density with persona.
  4. Diagnose your own recurring errors across grammar, lexical choice, and style, and describe a specific correction for each.
  5. Read an unfamiliar passage and identify its grammatical precision, lexical choices, and stylistic design as integrated evidence of a single authorial decision.
  6. Listen to connected, natural-speed Japanese drawing on Unit I vocabulary and structures, and extract both content and stylistic information.
  7. Produce a single connected written and spoken project that displays precision, lexical control, and style-shift ability together, rather than as three separate skills.
  8. Explain, in Japanese, the reasoning behind your own grammar, vocabulary, and style choices in a finished piece of writing.

Communication Scenario

Emma's dialect article — in both its dense print form and its warmer newsletter form — is finally ready for 山本さん's final review before publication. Before he reads it, he asks Emma to walk him through her own editing history: which sentences she tightened for precision, which words she replaced because a more exact term existed, and which stylistic choices she made deliberately for each version. Emma realizes that this is not three separate reviews — a grammar check, a vocabulary check, and a style check — but one integrated act of authorial judgment. As she prepares her explanation, she reviews her work from Chapters 2 through 4 side by side, checking that her precision, her word choice, and her style all serve the same communicative goal in each version.

もじ — Script and Typography Review

Script and Typography Review

No new kanji are introduced in this chapter. This section reviews how high-density kanji, consolidated okurigana, and style-sensitive orthographic choices interact across the two versions of a text.

Script Strategy — Reviewing kanji density as a stylistic signal

Across Chapters 1 through 4, you have seen that kanji density itself communicates genre before a single word is read closely: a dense, nominal, low-furigana passage signals an academic or professional genre, while a passage with shorter sentences, more furigana, and a more even balance of kanji and kana signals a general-audience genre. As you review Unit I, check your own writing for consistency: a single piece should not mix a high-density academic typography with a warm, conversational voice, or the reverse, unless that mismatch is a deliberate device.

れんしゅう — Practice: Compare the two Emma passages under Reading Practice below and note, without rereading the sentences in detail yet, which one you expect to be denser in kanji based on typography alone. Confirm your prediction once you reach that section.

Integrated Precision and Style Review

This chapter authorizes no new grammar, vocabulary, or style system. Its purpose is to assess your ability to combine, without prompting, the fine-grained grammar control from Chapter 2, the collocational and lexical precision from Chapter 3, and the deliberate style-shift systems from Chapter 4 in a single connected task.

1. Precision grammar under review

Purpose: to confirm that fine distinctions in modality, aspect, nominalization, embedded clauses, conditionals, and reference from Chapter 2 remain accurate and available for spontaneous use, not only for isolated drills.

  • 方言が使われなくなりつつあるとはいえ、完全に消えたわけではない。Although the dialect is falling out of use, it hasn't disappeared entirely.concessive + negative scope precision (Chapter 2)
  • この調査結果からすれば、若年層への継承が課題だと言わざるを得ない。Given these survey results, one has no choice but to say that transmission to younger generations is the challenge.evidential base + obligatory-conclusion construction (Chapter 2)

2. Vocabulary and collocation under review

Purpose: to confirm that a precise word or collocation from Chapter 3 is chosen over a vaguer alternative, and that the choice matches the surrounding register.

Vaguer choiceMore precise choiceWhy it matters
変わる衰退する / 継承されるnames the direction and mechanism of change rather than change in general
大切だ課題である / 意義深いdistinguishes "a problem to solve" from "a thing of deep significance"
言う示唆する / 指摘するdistinguishes a tentative suggestion from a direct, evidenced point

3. Style-shift under review

Purpose: to confirm that genre and audience design, sentence length and rhythm, nominal/verbal balance, and honorific density are adjusted together, consistently, and deliberately, as in Chapter 4.

  • 若年層への方言継承は、会話機会の減少という構造的要因により停滞していると考えられる。The transmission of dialect to younger generations is thought to be stagnating due to the structural factor of decreasing conversation opportunities.academic register — nominal, high density, low furigana
  • 若い人に方言が伝わりにくくなっているのは、話す機会が減っているからなんです。The reason dialect isn't passing down to young people so easily is that opportunities to speak it are decreasing.newsletter register — verbal, moderate density, warmer particle use
Language Focus — Precision, lexicon, and style as one judgment

A sentence can be grammatically flawless, lexically precise, and still fail its communicative purpose if its style does not match its genre and audience — and a stylistically well-matched sentence can still fail if its grammar or word choice is imprecise. Unit I has taught these as three systems for clarity, but a finished piece of writing succeeds only when they are checked together, as a single editorial judgment, exactly as Emma is learning to do with 山本さん.

  2. Identify one sentence in your own recent writing where the grammar is correct but the word choice is vague; replace the vague word with a more precise collocation.

  4. Write two sentences — one academic, one general-audience — about a single fact, applying precision, lexical choice, and style together in each.

Model Dialogue

Setting: 山本さん and Emma review her editing history together before final publication. Polite style, mentor-colleague relationship.

山本この二つの原稿、文法も語彙も文体も、かなり丁寧に見直しましたね。You've reviewed both grammar, vocabulary, and style pretty carefully in these two drafts, haven't you.
エマはい。最初は文法だけ、次は語彙だけ、というふうに別々に直していたんですが、途中で、それでは足りないと気づきました。Yes. At first I was fixing grammar alone, then vocabulary alone, separately like that, but partway through I realized that wasn't enough.
山本どうしてですか。Why is that?
エマ文法が正しくても、語感の合わない言葉を選んでいたら、意味が伝わりにくくなります。それに、語彙が正確でも、文体が読み手に合っていなければ、印象がずれてしまいます。Even if the grammar is correct, if I choose a word whose nuance doesn't fit, the meaning becomes hard to convey. And even if the vocabulary is accurate, if the style doesn't fit the reader, the impression ends up off.
山本つまり、三つを同時に見なければならない、ということですね。In other words, you have to look at all three at the same time.
エマそうなんです。今回は、雑誌版もニュースレター版も、精密さと語彙とスタイルを一緒に確認しながら仕上げました。Exactly. This time, for both the magazine version and the newsletter version, I finished them while checking precision, vocabulary, and style together.
山本では、公開する前に、最後にもう一度、その三つの観点から読んでみましょう。Then before we publish, let's read through one more time from those three angles.

Emma pulls up both drafts and highlights three colors in the margin: one for grammar checks, one for word-choice checks, and one for style checks.

ないようりかい — Comprehension check: Why did Emma initially separate her grammar, vocabulary, and style edits? What made her realize that separation was not enough? What does 山本さん suggest doing before publication?

Speaking Practice

コントロールされた練習 — Controlled practice: Three-angle sentence check

  1. Read a given sentence aloud, then restate it correcting one grammar precision issue.
  2. Restate the corrected sentence again, replacing one vague word with a more precise collocation.
  3. Restate the sentence a third time, adjusting it for a stated genre and audience.
  4. Explain aloud, in Japanese, what changed at each step and why.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Editorial review in Japanese publishing

Japanese editorial practice often involves multiple stages of review by different readers before publication, checking not only factual accuracy but also whether the tone, vocabulary, and structure of a piece match its intended readership and outlet. A writer's ability to explain and justify their own stylistic and lexical choices to an editor, as Emma does with 山本さん, is itself a valued professional skill, distinct from simply producing correct sentences.

Regional Note — Reviewing dialect content for a general readership

When writing about regional Japanese for a general audience, editors often ask writers to confirm that any regional forms quoted or referenced are presented accurately and are not stereotyped or reduced to caricature. This concern will return more fully in Chapter 13, but Emma's article already reflects it in how carefully she attributes claims about dialect use to specific evidence rather than general impressions.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Treating precision, vocabulary, and style as separate passes

Error: Revising a piece of writing in three fully separate passes — one for grammar, one for vocabulary, one for style — without checking whether a change in one system requires a change in another.

Correction: Review all three together on a single reading, as Emma and 山本さん do: a more precise grammatical construction may call for a more formal collocation, which in turn may call for a longer, more nominal sentence structure.

Common Mistake — Over-correcting precision at the expense of style

Error: Replacing every simple word or construction with the most precise or advanced available option, regardless of whether the genre calls for that density.

Correction: Match the level of grammatical and lexical precision to the genre: 言わざるを得ない suits the academic magazine version, while ～ようです suits the newsletter version, even though both are grammatically precise.

Common Mistake — Assuming a single "correct" revision exists

Error: Assuming that only one combination of grammar, vocabulary, and style is acceptable for a given piece of content.

Correction: Recognize that different genres and audiences license different, equally valid combinations, as long as each combination is internally consistent, as shown by the magazine and newsletter versions throughout this unit.

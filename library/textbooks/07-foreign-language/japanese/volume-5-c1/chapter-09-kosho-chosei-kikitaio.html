9

交渉・調整・危機対応Negotiation, Coordination, and Crisis Communication

High-stakes clarity and cooperative control · Speak and Write Japanese: Mastery, Specialization, and Expert Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Recognize domain register for scheduling conflicts, project risks, public problems, apologies, stakeholders, and contingency plans.
  2. Use conditional mitigation such as ～ば, ～たら, and ～なら to propose solutions carefully rather than issuing blunt demands.
  3. Frame responsibility for a problem without assigning blame in a way that damages a working relationship.
  4. Deliver and receive a formal apology using escalating degrees of formality appropriate to the severity of a problem.
  5. Read a short internal notice describing a scheduling conflict and identify the problem, the proposed adjustment, and the requested response.
  6. Write a coordination message that proposes an adjustment, names a condition, and requests confirmation.
  7. Listen to a short excerpt resembling a crisis-response call and identify the problem, the immediate action, and the follow-up commitment.
  8. Avoid common errors such as over-apologizing without proposing a solution, escalating before attempting de-escalation, and using conditional language so vague that no clear commitment is made.

Communication Scenario

佐藤部長 approved Emma's recommendation, and the company has begun a trial multilingual consulting project with a municipal office. Two weeks in, the municipal office's contact person calls to say that a promised translated document will not be ready by the agreed date, because the office's own internal review has taken longer than expected. Emma is asked to help coordinate a response: she must acknowledge the delay without either overpromising a new date the team cannot meet or sounding evasive about what went wrong. At the same time, 佐藤部長 wants Emma to propose a concrete adjustment — for example, delivering part of the document on time and the rest slightly later — rather than simply apologizing and hoping the client agrees to wait. Emma discovers that negotiation and crisis language in Japanese depends heavily on naming a condition clearly, proposing a specific next step, and calibrating the apology to match the seriousness of the problem, neither more nor less.

もじ — Script and Typography Focus

Script and Typography Focus

This chapter's script focus is the typography of internal notices and timelines used to coordinate a response to a scheduling problem. Coordination notices typically separate the situation, the cause, the proposed adjustment, and the requested action into short, clearly labeled sections, often using bolded labels such as 現状 ("current situation"), 原因 ("cause"), and 対応策 ("response measure"), so that a reader under time pressure can locate each part quickly.

Script Strategy — Reading a notice organized by problem, cause, and response

When reading a coordination notice, look first for a label such as 現状 or 問題点 to find the stated problem, then for 原因 to find the stated cause, and finally for 対応策 to find the proposed response. Do not assume the cause is stated first; some notices state the proposed response before explaining why it is necessary.

れんしゅう — Practice: Before reading the Model Coordination Notice below, scan it for labeled sections and note which order — problem, cause, response — the writer actually used.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Delivering an apology and a proposal with distinct prosody

A formal apology such as 誠に申し訳ございません is typically delivered more slowly than surrounding speech, with a lower, steadier pitch and a brief pause afterward, signaling sincerity rather than haste. A proposed solution that follows, such as 代替案をご提案させていただきます, is usually delivered at a more normal pace with a clearer rising energy, signaling a shift from acknowledging the problem to moving toward resolution. Practice saying both sentences aloud, exaggerating the contrast between the slower, lower apology and the more forward-moving proposal, then reduce the exaggeration to a natural level.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter authorizes risk language, conditional mitigation, responsibility framing, apology and repair, escalation and de-escalation, and summaries of agreement, together with continued keigo appropriate to high-stakes coordination between internal colleagues and external stakeholders.

1. Proposing a solution with conditional mitigation

Purpose: to propose an adjustment carefully, so that the proposal reads as a considered option rather than an unconditional demand or a vague hope.

Building Block
～ていただければ／～ますと／～のであれば、～できます
Conditional mitigation frame: names a condition for cooperation, then states what becomes possible if that condition is met, softening the proposal.
一部を先にお渡しできれば、全体の遅れは最小限に抑えられます。
If we are able to deliver part of it first, the overall delay can be kept to a minimum.
  • ご都合がよろしければ、来週の水曜日までに残りの部分をお届けする形でいかがでしょうか。If it is convenient for you, how would it be if we delivered the remaining part by next Wednesday?よろしければ + いかがでしょうか (conditional + soft proposal)
  • こちらで担当者を一名追加できれば、スケジュールの見直しは不要になると考えております。If we can add one more staff member on our side, I believe a review of the schedule will not be necessary.できれば + と考えております

2. Framing responsibility without assigning blame

Purpose: to explain what caused a problem and who is responsible for the response, without phrasing the explanation as an accusation that damages the relationship.

Building Block
～により／～の結果、～という状況になっております
Responsibility framing: attributes the situation to a cause or process rather than directly to a person, while still taking ownership of the response.
社内確認に予定より時間がかかった結果、翻訳作業の開始が遅れるという状況になっております。
As a result of the internal review taking longer than planned, the situation is that the start of the translation work has been delayed.
  • 今回の遅延の責任は当社にあると認識しております。We recognize that the responsibility for this delay lies with our company.責任は～にあると認識しております (owning responsibility without excessive self-blame)
  • 原因を確認したところ、双方の連絡不足も一因であったことが分かりました。Upon checking the cause, it became clear that insufficient communication on both sides was also a contributing factor.双方の～も一因 (shared responsibility framing, avoids one-sided blame)

3. Calibrating apology to the severity of the problem

Purpose: to match the strength of an apology to how serious the problem actually is, since an apology that is too strong or too weak for the situation can itself create friction.

SeverityFormulaNotes
Minor inconvenienceご迷惑をおかけしておりますacknowledges ongoing minor disruption, no admission of major fault
Clear delay or errorご迷惑をおかけし、申し訳ございませんstandard formal apology for a concrete, acknowledged problem
Serious failure誠に申し訳ございませんでしたstronger, past-tense form; reserved for significant failures
After resolutionお手数をおかけしましたことをお詫び申し上げますcloses the matter formally once a resolution has been reached
  • 今回はスケジュールの調整でご迷惑をおかけしております。This time we are causing you inconvenience with the schedule adjustment.minor-inconvenience register, ongoing 迷惑
  • 納期に間に合わなかったこと、誠に申し訳ございませんでした。I am truly sorry that we did not meet the deadline.serious-failure register, past tense でした
Language Focus — De-escalation before escalation

Chapter 8 taught that a professional recommendation should state its conclusion directly and early. In a coordination or crisis exchange, the first move is usually different: acknowledge the problem, propose a concrete adjustment, and confirm agreement, escalating to a more formal or higher-level response only if the first attempt does not resolve the matter. Opening with an escalated apology or a formal complaint before attempting a direct, cooperative fix can make a manageable scheduling problem feel like a larger conflict than it is.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Emma calls the municipal office's contact person, 山本様, to address the delayed translation document. Formal register, external stakeholder, continued keigo.

エマお世話になっております。今回の翻訳資料の件で、ご連絡いたしました。Thank you for your continued support. I'm calling regarding this translation document.
山本様はい、こちらこそ。予定より遅れているとのことですが。Yes, likewise. I understand it's running behind schedule.
エマはい、ご迷惑をおかけしております。社内確認に予定より時間がかかった結果、開始が遅れるという状況になっております。Yes, we're causing you inconvenience. As a result of our internal review taking longer than planned, the situation is that the start has been delayed.
山本様そうですか。こちらとしても予定があるので、少し困りますね。I see. Since we also have our own schedule, that's a bit of a problem for us.
エマ申し訳ございません。そこで、一つご提案がございます。全体を一度にお渡しするのではなく、優先度の高い部分を今週中にお渡しし、残りを来週に段階的にお渡しするという形はいかがでしょうか。I'm sorry. So, I do have one proposal. Rather than delivering everything at once, how would it be if we delivered the highest-priority part this week, and the rest in stages next week?
山本様それなら、今週中に一番急ぐ部分だけでもいただければ、助かります。If that's the case, it would help if we could at least receive the most urgent part this week.
エマ承知いたしました。それでは、その形で進めさせていただき、金曜日までに書面でも改めて確認を取らせていただきます。Understood. Then we will proceed in that way, and I will also get this confirmed formally in writing by Friday.
山本様お願いします。Please do.

Emma notes two follow-up actions: prepare the priority section for delivery this week, and send written confirmation of the staged schedule by Friday.

ないようりかい — Comprehension check: What condition does 山本様 name for accepting the delay? What two follow-up actions does Emma commit to before ending the call?

Writing Workshop

  4. Propose a conditional solution using ～ていただければ or an equivalent construction.

チェックリスト — Revision checklist:

  • The apology matches the actual severity of the problem, neither overstated nor understated.
  • The cause is framed responsibly without assigning blame in a way that damages the relationship.
  • A concrete conditional proposal is offered, not a vague hope that the problem will resolve itself.
  • A specific next step and deadline are named.
  • Register remains consistent with a formal, external-facing coordination message.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Apology as a relationship-preserving act, not only an admission of fault

In many Japanese professional settings, an apology such as ご迷惑をおかけしております functions partly as a way of acknowledging the other party's inconvenience and maintaining the relationship, and not only as a strict legal admission that one side is entirely at fault. This is one reason a coordination message may open with an apology even when responsibility is genuinely shared between both parties. This practice varies by organization, industry, and the seriousness of the matter, and it should not be treated as a fixed rule for every workplace or every kind of problem.

Regional Note — Escalation pathways across organizations

How quickly a problem moves from a working-level contact to a more senior person varies considerably by organization and industry rather than by region. Some organizations expect a working-level attempt at resolution before any escalation; others expect earlier notification to a supervisor even for minor issues. Where regional variation does appear, it tends to affect the directness of spoken interaction rather than the formal escalation structure itself.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Apologizing repeatedly without proposing a solution

Error: Repeating 申し訳ございません several times without ever stating a concrete proposed adjustment, leaving the other party without a clear picture of what happens next.

Correction: Apologize once at an appropriate severity, then move directly to a conditional proposal: 申し訳ございません。優先度の高い部分を先にお渡しできればと存じます。

Common Mistake — Escalating before attempting de-escalation

Error: Opening a minor scheduling issue with a formal written complaint or an unusually strong apology such as 誠に申し訳ございませんでした before attempting a direct, cooperative fix.

Correction: Match the response to the actual severity, attempting a working-level conditional proposal first: 少し調整させていただければ、対応可能かと存じます。

Common Mistake — Proposing a solution so vague that no clear commitment is made

Error: Using a conditional so open-ended that it commits to nothing, for example できればなんとかしたいと思います。

Correction: Name the specific condition and the specific outcome: 優先度の高い部分を今週中にお渡しできれば、全体の遅れは最小限に抑えられます。

Model Dialogue — Comprehension Check

山本様 names the condition that receiving at least the most urgent part of the document within the week would be acceptable. Emma commits to (1) proceeding with the staged delivery as proposed, and (2) sending written confirmation of the schedule by Friday.

Listening Practice — Model answers

(1) The speaker uses ご迷惑をおかけし、申し訳ございません, suggesting a clear, acknowledged delay rather than a minor inconvenience or a major failure. (2) The named cause is that internal confirmation work took longer than expected. (3) The proposed contingency plan is to deliver the highest-priority part the next day and the remainder in stages early the following week. (4) The speaker commits to obtaining written confirmation of this plan by the end of the day.

Reading Practice

(1) The sentence beginning 現状： states that part of the translation materials will not be delivered as originally scheduled. (2) The sentence beginning 原因： names two factors: internal confirmation work taking longer than expected, and some miscommunication with the responsible department. (3) The sentence beginning 対応策： proposes delivering the higher-priority part first and the remainder in stages, rather than delivering everything at once. (4) The final sentence describes obtaining written confirmation from relevant parties within the day and sharing a final schedule by Friday.

同時代メディアとネット言語Contemporary Media and Online Language

Digital discourse, platforms, and evolving usage · Speak and Write Japanese: Mastery, Specialization, and Expert Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Discuss online posts, comments, video captions, livestream language, and platform conventions using appropriate vocabulary.
  2. Recognize common informal contractions, abbreviations, and stance markers used in Japanese digital writing, and distinguish recognition from production expectations.
  3. Interpret irony, quoting, and audience-directed cues in short digital texts.
  4. Read a set of short online posts and comments and identify stance, tone, and audience design in each.
  5. Listen to a short livestream-style excerpt and identify how the speaker addresses an unseen audience.
  6. Write a short, appropriately limited digital-style post or comment that uses only the production-authorized features of this chapter.
  7. Distinguish digital and online Japanese as one genre among many rather than as evidence about how all Japanese speakers write or speak.

Communication Scenario

A member of Emma's reading circle mentions that the author of a short story they read recently sometimes answers questions from readers on a video platform. Curious, Emma watches a short clip in which the author responds informally to comments, and later reads through a thread of comments left under the video. The language is different again from anything Emma has handled so far this volume: sentences are shortened, some words are clipped or written in unusual ways, reactions are compressed into a few characters, and stance is signaled through small particles and punctuation choices rather than full sentences. Emma wants to understand this register well enough to follow it and, cautiously, to participate a little herself — without assuming that this is how any of these speakers would write in a letter, an email, or a face-to-face conversation.

  1. The learner studies vocabulary for online posts, comments, video captions, livestream language, and platform conventions.
  2. The learner studies informal contractions, abbreviations, stance markers, quoting, and irony, with clear production limits.
  3. The learner reads a short thread of comments under a video.
  4. The learner listens to a short livestream-style excerpt.
  5. The learner writes a short, limited digital-style comment.
  6. The learner completes a performance task combining all four skills around a single short digital exchange.

もじ — Script and Typography Focus

Script and Typography Focus

This chapter's script focus is digital typography, emoji/kaomoji recognition, hashtags, mixed scripts, and searchable text, as authorized by the volume scope. Digital Japanese text frequently mixes hiragana, katakana, kanji, Latin letters, numerals, and symbols within a single short post in ways that would be unusual in formal writing.

Script Strategy — Reading compressed digital typography

A hashtag such as #読書会 combines the symbol with an ordinary kanji compound; treat the kanji portion as you would in any other text and simply recognize the symbol as a searchable tag marker, not as new grammar. Kaomoji such as （笑） or w after a sentence mark laughter or amusement; （笑） is a written convention going back to formal parenthetical notes, while a bare w (from 笑う) is a informal digital-only convention. Recognize both, but this chapter authorizes production only of （笑） in a controlled way, since w-chains and similar conventions shift quickly and are better treated as receptive literacy for now.

Elongated vowels for emphasis, such as すごーい instead of すごい, are a real informal-digital spelling convention and not a spelling error. Recognize this pattern in reading; this chapter does not require you to produce it.

れんしゅう — Practice: In the Model Reading Passage below, find one example of mixed-script compression (a hashtag, an emoji-like symbol, or an unusual spelling) and explain in one sentence what function it serves.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Prosody for addressing an unseen audience

Livestream and video-caption speech is directed at an audience the speaker cannot see, which produces a distinctive prosody: slightly heightened pitch range, more frequent rising intonation on statements to invite reaction, and audible smiling quality on positive comments. Mora timing, vowel length, and moraic ん must remain accurate even in this more animated register. Listen for how a streamer's pitch rises noticeably when reading a comment aloud, marking a shift from the streamer's own speech to quoted audience text — a boundary cue that print alone cannot always convey.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter authorizes informal contractions, abbreviations, stance markers, quoting, irony, and audience cues, with clear production limits, building on the plain-form and register-switching control consolidated earlier in this volume. No new clause-level grammar paradigm is introduced; the focus is on how existing systems compress and shift in digital contexts.

1. Common informal contractions (receptive; limited production)

Purpose: to recognize, and in a controlled way produce, contractions common in casual digital writing.

  • それ、めっちゃわかる。I totally get that. (めっちゃ = very, informal)めっちゃ (informal intensifier, receptive/limited production)
  • もう見た？おもしろかったよ。Have you seen it yet? It was interesting.plain-form question + よ (productive, within Unit II register control)

This chapter authorizes production of ordinary plain-form casual sentences with sentence-final particles already consolidated earlier in the series. Contractions such as てる for ている, or じゃん, remain receptive only in this chapter unless a specific item is marked productive below.

2. Compressed reaction and stance markers

Purpose: to identify and, in controlled form, produce short reaction expressions that stand in place of a full sentence.

  • わかる、それ。I get that. (agreement, reordered for emphasis)predicate-first reordering (productive)
  • 確かに。True. / Fair point. (a bare adverb standing alone as a full reaction)adverb-as-utterance (productive, limited to listed items)
Building Block — 〜って（引用の「って」）

[quoted words or idea]って、[reaction or comment]。

An informal quotative particle functioning much like formal と in 〜と言っていた, but shorter and typical of spoken and digital registers. It quotes a claim, post, or comment before reacting to it. This pattern is productive in this chapter.

コメントで「続編が読みたい」って言ってる人が多い。

A lot of people are saying in the comments, "I want to read a sequel."

3. Irony and stance without direct statement

Purpose: to recognize when a positive-sounding comment is being used ironically, and to produce a clearly marked ironic comment when appropriate.

  • さすが（笑）予想通りの展開。Naturally (lol). Exactly the development you'd expect.さすが + （笑） as an ironic marker
  • 「素晴らしい対応」とのことですが、実際はかなり違ったようです。Apparently it was a "wonderful response" — though in reality it seems to have been quite different.とのことですが (distancing quotation of a claim being questioned)

4. Audience-directed cues

Purpose: to address an unseen audience directly, inviting reaction or participation.

  • みなさんはどう思いますか。コメントで教えてください。What do all of you think? Let me know in the comments.みなさん (audience address) + 教えてください (invitation)
  • これ、共感する人いますか。Is there anyone who relates to this?short audience-polling question
Language Focus — Compression is a genre feature, not a grammar failure

Digital comments and captions frequently omit particles, drop full predicates, and rely on a single adverb or noun to carry a full reaction. This compression follows recognizable patterns rather than being random or ungrammatical. Learners should be able to expand a compressed comment into a full plain-form or polite-form sentence, and should understand that this expansion is a comprehension skill, not evidence that the compressed form is simply "bad Japanese."

Grammar Practice

  1. Write one sentence quoting a comment using 〜って.
  2. Write one short reaction using a bare adverb, such as 確かに or なるほど, as a complete utterance.
  3. Write one sentence that questions a claim using とのことですが.
  4. Write one audience-directed question inviting comments, using みなさん or a similar address term.

Model Dialogue

Setting: a short video clip in which an author, 中村さん, responds to comments left under an earlier video, addressing an unseen audience of viewers.

中村さんはい、今日はコメント欄から質問に答えていきます。まず、「続編が読みたい」ってコメント、結構多かったです。All right, today I'll answer questions from the comment section. First, there were quite a few comments saying, "I want to read a sequel."
中村さん正直、今のところ続編の予定はありません。でも、みなさんがそこまで気にしてくれるのは、すごく嬉しいです。Honestly, there's no sequel planned at the moment. But it makes me really happy that all of you care that much.
中村さん次に、「駅のシーン、意味が分からなかった」って人もいました。これは、わざと明言しなかった部分なので、みなさんの解釈にお任せします。Next, there were also people saying, "I didn't understand the station scene." That's a part I deliberately left unstated, so I'll leave it to everyone's interpretation.
中村さん最後に、「炎上覚悟で聞きますが」というコメントがありました（笑）。別に炎上するような質問ではなかったので、安心してください。Finally, there was a comment that said, "I'll ask even at the risk of stirring up controversy" (lol). It wasn't the kind of question that would cause any backlash, so don't worry.
中村さん今日はこのへんで。また何か思ったら、コメントで教えてください。That's it for today. If anything else comes to mind, let me know in the comments.

中村さん addresses the unseen audience directly throughout, quotes several comments using 「〜」って, and marks one comment as playfully exaggerated with （笑）rather than treating it as a genuine controversy.

ないようりかい — Comprehension check: Which comment does 中村さん decline to fully answer, and why? How does 中村さん signal that the "controversial" question was not actually controversial?

Guided Practice

タスク１ — Quote extraction. From the Model Dialogue, list each comment 中村さん quotes using って, and rewrite each one as a full formal sentence using と言っていた instead.

タスク２ — Expansion practice. Take the compressed reaction 確かに。 and expand it into a full plain-form sentence that states clearly what is being agreed with.

タスク３ — Peer or self-check. Compare your expanded sentences with a partner's, or review them yourself after a short break, and confirm that no meaning was lost in the expansion.

Listening Practice

Ask your instructor, a partner, or a text-to-speech tool to read the following short passage aloud in an animated, audience-directed style.

きくタスク — A short livestream-style excerpt

みなさん、こんばんは。今日もたくさんコメントありがとうございます。「今日の配信、いつもより早いですね」ってコメント、確かに。今日はちょっと早めに始めました。あと、「前回の話、続き気になります」という人も多かったので、また今度話しますね。皮肉っぽく聞こえたらすみませんが、正直、そんなに大した話じゃないです（笑）。それでは、また明日。

聞くタスク — Listening task: Listen at least twice. (1) What does the speaker acknowledge about the timing of today's stream? (2) What topic does the speaker promise to return to later? (3) How does the speaker soften a potentially self-deprecating comment about the previous topic? (4) What tone does the speaker use to close the stream?

Reading Practice

Read the short comment thread below and complete the questions that follow.

コメント欄（抜粋）— Comment section (excerpt)

読者A：この駅のシーン、結局どういう意味だったんでしょうか。気になって眠れません（笑）
読者B：わかる、それ。私も三回読み返しました。
読者C：「わざと明言しなかった」って作者さん言ってましたよね。だとしたら、読者それぞれの解釈でいいのかも。
読者A：確かに。押しつけがましい説明より、こういう終わり方のほうが好きです。
読者D：みなさん褒めすぎでは？普通に読みにくかったです。

  1. Identify which comment uses って to quote something the author said, and state what is being quoted.
  2. Explain what 読者B's short comment わかる、それ。 is agreeing with, and expand it into a full sentence.
  3. 読者D's comment differs in stance from the others. Identify the phrase that signals this, and explain how it differs without assuming 読者D is being rude.
  4. Is any comment here clearly ironic? Explain your answer with reference to a specific phrase.
  5. Write one sentence responding to 読者D using とのことですが to acknowledge the differing view respectfully.

Writing Workshop

Write a short, appropriately limited comment responding to the comment thread above (or a similar short digital exchange of your choice, with instructor approval), applying the digital-discourse systems from this chapter.

  1. Quote a comment or claim you are responding to, using 〜って or とのことですが.
  2. State your own reaction or stance in one or two sentences, using a full sentence rather than only a bare reaction word.
  3. If appropriate, mark irony clearly using （笑） or another authorized marker, rather than leaving your intent ambiguous.
  4. Invite further audience response, using an audience-directed question if it fits your comment.
  5. Keep your comment brief, consistent with the genre, while remaining grammatically complete enough that a reader unfamiliar with the thread could follow it.
  6. Write a one-sentence note in Japanese explaining which feature of digital-register Japanese you chose to include and why.

チェックリスト — Revision checklist:

  • The comment quotes what it is responding to, rather than assuming shared context.
  • Any irony is clearly marked rather than ambiguous.
  • The comment remains brief but grammatically complete.
  • Vocabulary and grammar remain within this chapter's authorized systems.
  • No unscheduled slang, abbreviation, or contraction is used productively.
  • Script, punctuation, and any digital typography follow the chapter's conventions.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Digital registers as one genre among many

The compressed, informal, audience-directed style seen in this chapter belongs to a specific set of digital genres: comments, livestreams, and short posts. It is not evidence about how Japanese speakers write in general, nor a replacement for the formal written registers taught earlier in this volume. The same person who writes わかる、それ。 in a comment section may write a fully formal email an hour later. Treat digital register as context-specific, not as a simplified or degraded form of "real" Japanese.

Regional Note — Digital conventions change faster than regional dialects

Online abbreviations, emoji conventions, and platform-specific slang change faster than regional or generational speech patterns and can go out of use within a few years. Treat any specific abbreviation or meme convention you encounter as a snapshot of current usage rather than a permanent feature of the language, and expect that some items in this chapter's receptive vocabulary may already sound dated by the time you read them elsewhere. Chapter 13 turns to more stable regional and historical variation for contrast.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Assuming a compressed comment is grammatically incomplete or wrong

Error: Treating 確かに。 or わかる、それ。 as sentence fragments that need to be "corrected" into full formal sentences before they can be understood.

Correction: Recognize these as complete, genre-appropriate utterances in context, and practice expanding them mentally into full sentences only as a comprehension check, not as a required correction.

Common Mistake — Using unmarked irony in a foreign-language comment

Error: Writing an ironic comment such as さすが。予想通りの展開。 with no marker at all, risking that a reader takes the positive-sounding words at face value.

Correction: As a learner, mark irony explicitly with （笑） or a clarifying follow-up sentence, as in さすが（笑）予想通りの展開。, rather than relying on tone alone, which is much harder to convey reliably in a second language.

Common Mistake — Producing unscheduled slang or contractions to "sound native"

Error: Inserting contractions such as てる for ている, or abbreviations not covered in this chapter, into writing because they were seen once online.

Correction: Recognize such forms receptively, but keep productive writing within this chapter's authorized systems until a later stage or independent study activates broader informal contractions for production.

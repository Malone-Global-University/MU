休みの日と予定Leisure and Plans

Invitations, suggestions, weather, and making plans, with 〜ませんか and 〜ましょう · Speak and Write Japanese: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify and use vocabulary for leisure activities, weather, and the days of the week and weekend.
  2. Invite someone to do something using 〜ませんか.
  3. Suggest an activity using 〜ましょう.
  4. Accept an invitation, offer a soft refusal, and propose an alternative.
  5. Describe past weather and past conditions using the past forms of い-adjectives and な-adjectives.
  6. Use the nonpast form of a verb to talk about a plan or intention in the near future.
  7. Read and interpret simple weather reports and event announcements.
  8. Recognize, read, and begin to handwrite ten new Unit III kanji for weather, time, and meeting.
  9. Hold a short guided conversation making weekend plans with a partner.

Communication Scenario

It is Thursday afternoon, and Tom's classmate Yuki wants to make plans for the weekend. She checks the weather, invites Tom to join her at a nearby park, and the two negotiate a time and place. When rain changes their plan, they discuss an indoor alternative instead. This chapter follows Tom and Yuki as they invite, suggest, accept, decline, and revise plans together.

  1. This chapter continues Unit III. It introduces the invitation pattern 〜ませんか, the suggestion pattern 〜ましょう, past-tense adjectives, and the use of the ordinary nonpast verb form to talk about upcoming plans.
  2. By the end of the chapter, you will be able to invite a friend to do something, suggest a time and place, talk about the weather, and politely decline an invitation while offering an alternative.

Script Note — 天気 as a fixed compound

The kanji 天 and 気 combine to form 天気 (weather), read てんき. Neither kanji is commonly used alone with this meaning at this level, so learn 天気 as one lexical unit rather than as two separate pieces of meaning added together.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Intonation in invitations and suggestions

〜ませんか is a question, and its final syllable rises like other yes-or-no questions you have practiced since Chapter 1. However, a warm, slightly softer rise (rather than a sharp one) makes the invitation sound welcoming rather than merely informational. Practice the contrast:

  • 公園に行きますか。 — a plain question about fact, with a clear rising question intonation
  • 公園に行きませんか。 — an invitation, with a softer, warmer rise

By contrast, 〜ましょう normally falls gently at the end, since it proposes an action rather than asking a yes-or-no question. Also review the long vowel in 映画 (えいが): keep the えい sequence smooth and connected rather than pronouncing it as two separate syllables.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

1. Inviting someone with 〜ませんか

Purpose: to invite someone to do something together.

Attach か to the negative polite form of a verb, 〜ません, to turn a plain negative statement into a warm invitation. Although the form is grammatically negative, its meaning is an invitation, not a statement about not doing something.

  • いっしょに映画を見ませんか。Would you like to watch a movie together?見ません[neg. of 見ます]+か / invitation
  • 週末、公園に行きませんか。Would you like to go to the park this weekend?

2. Suggesting an activity with 〜ましょう

Purpose: to propose doing something together, often in response to an invitation.

Replace ます with ましょう to suggest an action that includes the speaker. This form is commonly used to respond to an invitation, to propose a time or place, or to move a plan forward.

  • いいですね。行きましょう。Sounds good. Let's go.行き[stem]+ましょう[let's]
  • 三時に会いましょう。Let's meet at three o'clock.会い[stem]+ましょう
Common Mistake — Using 〜ましょう to invite someone

Error: *公園に行きましょうか。 (intending to ask a friend for the first time, "Would you like to go to the park?")

Correction: 公園に行きませんか。 — Use 〜ませんか to open an invitation. 〜ましょう is more natural once both people have already agreed to do something together, or to confirm a plan already in progress.

3. Accepting, softly declining, and offering alternatives

Purpose: to respond politely to an invitation.

An invitation may be accepted directly, or declined softly with an incomplete sentence rather than a blunt refusal. A soft refusal often begins with ちょっと and trails off, leaving the reason understood rather than stated in full.

  • いいですね。行きましょう。Sounds good. Let's go. (acceptance)
  • すみません、週末はちょっと…。Sorry, this weekend is a bit… (soft refusal, reason left unstated)
  • 週末はちょっと…。今度はどうですか。This weekend is a bit difficult. How about another time?
Language Note — Why the sentence trails off

Leaving a refusal incomplete after ちょっと is a common and polite way to decline without stating a blunt "no" or a detailed excuse. The listener understands that the answer is negative from the trailing intonation and the word ちょっと alone. This is not evasiveness; it is a recognized way of softening a refusal in many everyday situations.

4. Past tense of い-adjectives and な-adjectives

Purpose: to describe past weather and past conditions.

You have used present-tense い-adjectives and な-adjectives since Chapter 6. This chapter introduces their polite past forms, useful for describing yesterday's or last week's weather.

Building Block — Adjective past forms
い-adjective: drop い, add かったです    な-adjective: add でした after the noun-like stem
Note the irregular adjective いい ("good"), which uses its alternate stem よ before adding the past ending.
暑い → 暑かったです    寒い → 寒かったです    いい → よかったです    元気 → 元気でした
hot → was hot    cold → was cold    good → was good    well/fine → was well/fine
  • 昨日は寒かったです。It was cold yesterday.寒（い→）かったです[past]
  • 週末の天気はよかったです。The weekend's weather was good.

5. Talking about plans with the nonpast form

Purpose: to describe an intention or a plan for the near future.

The nonpast forms you already know, such as 行きます and 見ます, describe both habitual actions and future plans. Japanese does not require a separate future tense; context and time words such as 週末, 今度, and 来週 make clear that a nonpast verb refers to something not yet done.

  • 来週、パーティーに行きます。I am going to a party next week.来週[time word] establishes future reference
  • 今度、いっしょに音楽を聞きましょう。Let's listen to music together sometime soon.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Yuki invites Tom to the park on Thursday afternoon, but rain changes their plan.

YUKI トムさん、週末、いっしょに公園に行きませんか。 Tom, would you like to go to the park together this weekend?
TOM いいですね。行きましょう。天気はどうですか。 Sounds good. Let's go. How's the weather?
YUKI 今週はずっと雨でした。でも、週末はいい天気ですよ。 It's been raining all week. But the weekend has good weather.
TOM よかったです。何時に会いましょうか。 That's good. What time should we meet?
YUKI 十時はどうですか。駅で会いましょう。 How about ten o'clock? Let's meet at the station.
(On Saturday morning, it begins to rain.)
YUKI すみません、雨ですね。公園はちょっと…。映画はどうですか。 Sorry, it's raining, isn't it. The park is a bit… How about a movie?
TOM いいですね。映画を見ましょう。 Sounds good. Let's watch a movie.

ないようりかい — Comprehension questions:

  1. What does Yuki invite Tom to do, and how does he respond?
  2. What was the weather like earlier in the week, and what is it like on the weekend?
  3. Why does the plan change, and what do Tom and Yuki decide to do instead?

Writing Workshop

In this workshop you will write a short message inviting a friend to a weekend activity and responding to a change in plans.

  1. Choose an activity and a day. Pick a leisure activity from this chapter's vocabulary and a day of the weekend.
  2. List your ideas in English first. Note the activity, the place, the time, and one possible weather condition.
  3. Write an opening invitation using 〜ませんか.
  4. Write a sentence about the weather, using an adjective in either the present or past form.

チェックリスト — Editing checklist:

  • I described the weather accurately, using a present or past adjective form correctly.
  • I suggested a time or place using 〜ましょう.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Soft refusals and unstated reasons

Leaving a refusal unfinished after ちょっと, as in 週末はちょっと…, is a common way of declining an invitation in many everyday Japanese interactions without stating a detailed reason or a blunt "no." A listener typically understands this as a polite decline and does not usually press for further explanation. This pattern reflects a common social convention, not a universal rule that every speaker follows in every situation.

Regional Note — Talking about the weather

Commenting on the weather, as in いい天気ですね ("Nice weather, isn't it"), is a common way to open a casual conversation in many everyday settings across Japan, similar to small talk about weather in many English-speaking contexts. Local weather patterns vary considerably by region, from snowy winters in parts of Hokkaido to humid summers in much of the country, so specific weather comments will vary by season and location.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Treating 〜ませんか as a plain negative statement

Error: Reading 公園に行きませんか。 only as "I am not going to the park."

Correction: With rising intonation and in an invitation context, 〜ませんか means "Would you like to...?" Context, intonation, and situation distinguish the invitation meaning from a simple negative statement.

Common Mistake — Forgetting the irregular past of いい

Error: *いいかったです。

Correction: よかったです。 — いい uses the alternate stem よ before any inflected ending; this is the only common irregular い-adjective at this level.

Common Mistake — Adding だ or です directly to a な-adjective before past で

Error: *元気だでした。

Correction: 元気でした。 — A な-adjective's polite past form adds でした directly after the stem, without だ or です in between.

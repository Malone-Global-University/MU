勉強と仕事Study and Work

School, work, subjects, and skills, with plain forms, 〜ことができます、quotation と／と思います、and short noun-modifying clauses · Speak and Write Japanese: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify and use vocabulary for school subjects, workplaces, tasks, and skills.
  2. Recognize plain nonpast and plain negative verb and adjective forms used inside larger patterns.
  3. Express ability using 〜ことができます.
  4. Quote what someone said or thinks using と言いました and と思います.
  5. Build short noun-modifying clauses to describe a person, place, or thing, such as 日本語を勉強している学生.
  6. Ask about and describe a person's studies, job, and abilities.
  7. Read and interpret simple school and workplace notices and short reports.
  8. Recognize, read, and begin to handwrite twelve new Unit III kanji for study, work, and communication.
  9. Hold a short guided conversation about study, work, and ability.

Communication Scenario

Tom has recovered from his cold and returns to Japanese class. During a break, Yuki asks him about his studies and his part-time job. Tom explains what he is studying, what he can and cannot do yet, and what his manager told him at work. Later, Tom tells a classmate what Yuki said and what he himself thinks about balancing study and work. This chapter follows Tom as he describes his studies and job, reports what people have said, and talks about his abilities.

  1. This chapter continues Unit III. It introduces plain forms for grammar building, the ability pattern 〜ことができます, quotation with と and と思います, and short noun-modifying clauses.
  2. By the end of the chapter, you will be able to describe your studies and work, talk about what you can and cannot do, report what someone said or thinks, and describe a person or thing using a short modifying clause.

Script Note — 事 has more than one active reading

The kanji 事 is read じ in the compound 仕事 (job) but read こと when it appears alone as a word meaning "thing" or "matter," including inside the pattern 〜ことができます introduced later in this chapter. Learn each word and pattern as its own unit rather than assuming one reading applies everywhere 事 appears.

れんしゅう — Practice: Copy each new kanji five times, saying its reading aloud each time. Then write the words 勉強, 仕事, and 会社 from memory, checking your okurigana against the vocabulary list above.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Long vowels in 勉強 and 学校

The word 勉強 (べんきょう) contains the contracted sound きょ followed by a long う vowel, and 学校 (がっこう) contains a small っ followed by a long う vowel. Tap out each word's morae to keep the timing accurate: べ・ん・きょ・う (4 morae) and が・っ・こ・う (4 morae).

  • 勉強 べんきょう — study (4 morae)
  • 学校 がっこう — school (4 morae)

Also review the falling intonation used when reporting what someone said with と言いました, which typically completes the sentence with a level or slightly falling pitch rather than the rising pitch of a question.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

1. Plain forms for grammar building

Purpose: to recognize and begin using the plain nonpast form inside larger patterns, without yet using it as an independent polite-style substitute.

Every verb has a plain nonpast form in addition to its polite 〜ます form. From this chapter onward, the plain nonpast form is used inside larger grammar patterns such as 〜ことができます, と思います, and noun-modifying clauses. The plain form is not yet a general substitute for polite 〜ます speech in this volume; it appears only inside the patterns this chapter and later chapters authorize.

  • 話します → 話すto speak (polite → plain)
  • 読みます → 読むto read (polite → plain)
  • します → するto do (polite → plain)
Language Note — Why plain forms appear now

Patterns such as 〜ことができます and と思います attach to the plain nonpast form of a verb, not to the 〜ます form. Because these patterns are useful for describing ability and reporting thoughts, this chapter introduces the plain nonpast form as a building block. A full system of plain-style speech, including plain past and plain negative for independent conversation, develops later in the volume and more fully in Volume II.

2. Expressing ability with 〜ことができます

Purpose: to say what a person is able to do.

Attach ことができます to the plain nonpast form of a verb to express ability. This pattern nominalizes the verb phrase with こと (thing; matter) and states that "doing [verb]" is possible.

  • 日本語を話すことができます。I can speak Japanese.話す[plain]+ことができます
  • 漢字を書くことができません。I cannot write kanji.
  • ピアノを弾くことができますか。Can you play the piano?
Building Block — Ability with こと
Verb (plain nonpast) + こと + が + できます
こと turns the verb phrase into a noun phrase, which becomes the thing that is possible.
日本語を読むことができます
I can read Japanese
Common Mistake — Using 上手 and 〜ことができます interchangeably

Error: Treating 日本語が上手です and 日本語を話すことができます as always identical in meaning.

Correction: 上手です comments on skill level, while 〜ことができます states whether an action is possible at all. A person can speak a language with 〜ことができます without necessarily being described as 上手.

3. Quotation with と, 言いました, and と思います

Purpose: to report what someone said or to state a personal opinion.

Attach と to a plain-style clause, followed by 言いました to report speech or 思います to give an opinion. At this stage, quotations use short, simple clauses that learners have already studied.

  • 先生は「明日、テストがあります」と言いました。The teacher said, "There will be a test tomorrow."「発言」+と+言いました
  • ゆきさんは忙しいと言いました。Yuki said she is busy.
  • 私はこの仕事はおもしろいと思います。I think this job is interesting.
Language Note — Direct and indirect quotation at this level

Japanese does not always require a strict grammatical shift between direct and indirect quotation the way English does. At this level, keep quotations short and close to the original wording, using corner brackets 「 」 for a quoted line when useful. More flexible indirect-quotation patterns are studied in later volumes.

4. Short noun-modifying clauses

Purpose: to describe a person, place, or thing using a short clause placed directly before the noun.

In Japanese, a clause that describes a noun is placed directly before that noun, using the plain nonpast (or plain past, once available) form of the verb or adjective. There is no separate word equivalent to English "who," "which," or "that" in this position.

  • 日本語を勉強している学生a student who is studying Japanese[clause]+学生[noun]
  • 会社で働いている人a person who works at a company
  • 先生が読んでいる本the book that the teacher is reading
Common Mistake — Looking for a relative pronoun

Error: Expecting a separate word for "who" or "that" between the clause and the noun, as in English.

Correction: 日本語を勉強している学生 — Japanese places the entire descriptive clause directly before the noun it modifies, with no linking word. Practice reading the clause first, then the noun it describes, right to left in meaning even though the text reads left to right.

Grammar Practice

  1. Give the plain nonpast form of 読みます and 聞きます.
  2. Say you can speak Japanese, using 〜ことができます.
  3. Report that the teacher said there is a test tomorrow, using と言いました.
  4. Say you think this job is interesting, using と思います.
  5. Describe "a student who is studying Japanese" using a short noun-modifying clause.

Model Dialogue

Setting: During a break in Japanese class, Yuki asks Tom about his studies and his part-time job.

ゆき トムさんは大学で何を勉強していますか。 Tom, what are you studying at university?
トム 歴史を勉強しています。それから、日本語も勉強しています。 I'm studying history. And I'm also studying Japanese.
ゆき 日本語で新聞を読むことができますか。 Can you read a newspaper in Japanese?
トム いいえ、まだ読むことができません。でも、話すことはできます。 No, I can't read one yet. But I can speak.
ゆき アルバイトはどうですか。忙しいですか。 How about your part-time job? Are you busy?
トム はい、少し忙しいです。マネージャーは「来週、新しい仕事があります」と言いました。 Yes, a little busy. My manager said, "There will be new work next week."
ゆき 勉強とアルバイトは大変ですね。 Studying and working part-time must be difficult.
トム そうですね。でも、いい経験だと思います。 That's true. But I think it's good experience.

ないようりかい — Comprehension questions:

  1. What is Tom studying at university?
  2. What can Tom do in Japanese, and what can he not yet do?
  3. What did Tom's manager say, and what does Tom think about studying and working together?

Writing Workshop

In this workshop you will write a short paragraph describing your studies or work, your abilities, and your opinion about them.

  1. Choose your topic. Decide whether to focus on school, a job, or a part-time job.
  2. List your ideas in English first. Note what you study or do, one or two abilities, and something someone has said to you about it.
  3. Write a sentence describing what you study or do using vocabulary from this chapter.
  4. Write a sentence about your ability, using 〜ことができます in the affirmative or negative.
  5. Write a sentence reporting something someone said to you using と言いました.
  6. Write a sentence giving your opinion using と思います.
  7. Add a sentence describing a classmate or coworker using a short noun-modifying clause, such as 「日本語を勉強している学生」.
  8. Revise and check using the checklist below before finalizing your work.

チェックリスト — Editing checklist:

  • I described my studies or work clearly using accurate vocabulary.
  • I used 〜ことができます correctly, attached to a plain nonpast verb.
  • I reported someone's words or thought using と言いました or と思います correctly.
  • I built a short noun-modifying clause with the clause placed directly before the noun.
  • I used the new Unit III kanji correctly, with furigana.
  • I ended each sentence with 。 and organized my paragraph logically.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Part-time work and study for students

Many students in Japan and in Japanese-speaking communities elsewhere combine study with a part-time job, often called アルバイト, a term borrowed from German and adapted into Japanese. Balancing study and part-time work is a common topic of everyday conversation among students, and expressions like 忙しい and 大変 frequently appear when students describe this balance to friends, family, or teachers.

Regional Note — Workplace expectations vary

Expectations around reporting to a manager, the language used with coworkers, and typical part-time job hours vary by workplace, industry, and region. Some workplaces use more formal language toward managers than the plain and polite forms practiced in this chapter; learners preparing for a specific workplace should confirm its particular expectations rather than assuming this chapter's examples apply everywhere.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Attaching ことができます to the ます form

Error: *話しますことができます。

Correction: 話すことができます。 — 〜ことができます attaches to the plain nonpast form of the verb, not to the 〜ます form.

Common Mistake — Omitting と before 言いました or 思います

Error: *先生は忙しい言いました。

Correction: 先生は忙しいと言いました。 — The particle と must appear directly before 言いました or 思います to mark the quoted content or opinion.

Common Mistake — Placing the modifying clause after the noun

Error: *学生、日本語を勉強している。 (intending "a student who is studying Japanese," modeled on English word order)

Correction: 日本語を勉強している学生 — The entire descriptive clause must come before the noun it modifies, with no separating word or punctuation.

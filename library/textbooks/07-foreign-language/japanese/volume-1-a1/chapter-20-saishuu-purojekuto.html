Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Introduce yourself and describe your identity, family, home, and daily life using integrated Volume I systems.
  2. Narrate a sequence of past events, including at least one experience with たことがあります, using connected sentences.
  3. Describe a person or thing with a short modifying clause and report what someone said using と with 言います or 思います.
  4. Ask for and give directions, make plans, and manage a simple health or service interaction in ordinary polite Japanese.
  5. Read and interpret practical mixed-script texts, including signs, schedules, messages, and short narratives, with appropriate furigana support.
  6. Write a connected multi-paragraph account that combines description, narration, comparison, and a closing message.
  7. Demonstrate control of hiragana, katakana, and the scheduled Volume I kanji in a cumulative reading and writing task.
  8. Reflect on your own progress across the volume and identify goals for Volume II.

Communication Scenario

Tom's exchange program is ending, and his school has asked him to prepare a short capstone presentation and a written portfolio piece called 「日本語で広がる世界」("My World Expands in Japanese") for a small farewell gathering with his host family, classmates, and teacher. He will introduce himself again as he did in Chapter 1, but now with a full year of experience behind him: his home and daily life, the town he came to know, his health and studies, the trip he took, how his understanding of Japan has changed, and the people he met and stayed in touch with. This final chapter does not introduce new grammar. Instead, it asks you to bring together everything from Units I through IV into one connected performance.

  1. This chapter reviews and integrates vocabulary, kanji, and grammar from Chapters 1 through 19.
  2. By the end of the chapter, you will complete a capstone performance task that includes a written portfolio piece, a spoken presentation, an interactive conversation, and a script and kanji check.

Pronunciation Review

Pronunciation Focus — Mora timing, length, and rhythm across a full presentation

A spoken presentation that lasts several sentences requires steady mora timing throughout, not only in short exchanges. Review these contrasts before your capstone presentation:

  • Short versus long vowels, such as おばさん (aunt) versus おばあさん (grandmother)
  • Single versus geminate consonants, such as きて (come, て-form) versus きって (stamp)
  • Moraic ん held for a full mora, as in れんらく and けいけん
  • Phrase-level rhythm and falling intonation at the end of connected statements, and rising intonation for questions

Practice reading your written portfolio piece aloud slowly at first, then at a natural conversational pace, paying attention to mora count rather than to English-style stress.

Grammar Review

This section reviews the major grammar systems of Volume I in the order you will likely use them in your capstone project: introducing yourself, describing daily life, narrating the past, comparing then and now, and closing with connection.

1. Identity and description (Units I–II)

Purpose: to introduce yourself and describe people, places, and routines.

  • わたしはトムです。アメリカから来ました。I am Tom. I came from America.
  • 毎日七時に起きて、朝ご飯を食べます。Every day I wake up at seven and eat breakfast.
  • わたしの部屋は小さいですが、明るいです。My room is small, but it is bright.

2. Requests, plans, and community life (Unit III)

Purpose: to get around, make plans, and manage everyday needs.

  • 駅までまっすぐ行ってください。Please go straight to the station.
  • 週末、映画を見に行きませんか。Would you like to go see a movie this weekend?
  • 頭が痛いので、薬を飲みました。Because I had a headache, I took medicine.

3. Past narration, comparison, and experience (Unit IV)

Purpose: to narrate past events and describe change over time.

  • 先週、京都に行って、お寺を見ました。Last week I went to Kyoto and saw temples.
  • 日本のお祭りに行ったことがあります。I have been to a festival in Japan.
  • 一年前は日本語が話せませんでしたが、今は少し話せます。A year ago I could not speak Japanese, but now I can speak a little.

4. Description, quotation, and connection (Chapters 18–19)

Purpose: to describe people with a modifying clause, report speech, and connect sentences into a story.

  • これはお世話になった家族と撮った写真です。This is a photo I took with the family who took care of me.
  • 先生は「がんばりましたね」と言いました。My teacher said, "You worked hard."
  • 日本語を勉強しました。それから、たくさんの友だちができました。I studied Japanese. After that, I made many friends.
Common Mistake — Switching plain and polite style within one paragraph

Error: *京都に行きました。とても楽しかった。それから、写真を撮ります。

Correction: 京都に行きました。とても楽しかったです。それから、写真を撮りました。 — Keep one consistent style, usually polite です／ます style, across a connected written passage unless you are quoting someone or writing a private diary note.

Building Block — A four-part capstone paragraph
Self-introduction + daily life or town description + one narrated past experience + a closing sentence about connection or change
Your capstone portfolio piece can follow this four-part shape: who you are and where you have been living, what your ordinary life or town has been like, one specific memorable event told with plain or polite past forms, and a closing statement connecting your experience to the people you met.
わたしはトムです。一年間、日本で勉強しました。毎日、近所を歩いて、いろいろなお店に行きました。夏休みに沖縄に旅行して、そこで親切な人に出会いました。その人は「また来てください」と言いました。日本語を勉強して、たくさんのことを学びました。これからも、日本の友だちと連絡します。
I am Tom. I studied in Japan for one year. Every day, I walked around the neighborhood and went to various shops. During summer vacation I traveled to Okinawa, and there I met a kind person. That person said, "Please come again." Through studying Japanese, I learned many things. From now on too, I will stay in touch with my friends in Japan.

Grammar Review Practice

  1. Write two sentences introducing yourself and your origin, using です and から来ました.
  2. Write two sentences describing your daily routine or your host family's neighborhood.
  3. Write two sentences narrating one specific past event, using plain or polite past forms.
  4. Write one sentence comparing something about "then" and "now," using 前は／今は or なる.
  5. Write one sentence with a short modifying clause and one sentence quoting something someone said to you.

Model Dialogue

Setting: At the farewell gathering, Tom's teacher asks him a few questions before his presentation.

先生 トムさん、一年間、日本での生活はどうでしたか。 Tom, how was your life in Japan over the past year?
トム とても楽しかったです。最初は日本語が全然できませんでしたが、今は毎日日本語で話すことができます。 It was a lot of fun. At first I couldn't do Japanese at all, but now I can speak Japanese every day.
先生 いちばんいい思い出は何ですか。 What is your favorite memory?
トム 沖縄への旅行です。そこで出会った人が、いろいろな話をしてくれました。とても親切でした。 My trip to Okinawa. The person I met there talked with me about many things. They were very kind.
先生 これから、日本語をどう続けますか。 How will you continue with Japanese from now on?
トム 日本の友だちと連絡します。それから、来年、また日本に来たいと思います。 I'll stay in touch with my friends in Japan. And, next year, I think I want to come to Japan again.

ないようりかい — Comprehension questions:

  1. How does Tom describe his Japanese ability at the beginning of the year, compared to now?
  2. What does Tom name as his favorite memory, and why?
  3. What are Tom's plans for continuing with Japanese?

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Farewell gatherings and closing words

Many schools, host families, and community groups mark the end of a stay or a course with a small gathering, a card, a shared meal, or an exchange of messages. Expressions of thanks, mention of specific shared memories, and simple statements about staying in touch are common ways to close such a gathering, though exact customs vary by school, family, and region.

Regional Note — Your own experience is one story among many

Your particular host family, neighborhood, school, and friends reflect one set of experiences in one place. Other learners studying in different regions or communities in Japan may describe different routines, foods, dialect features, or customs. Volume II will continue to widen this picture with more regional and community variation.

Register Note — Choosing polite style for a formal presentation

A farewell presentation to teachers, host families, and classmates in a somewhat formal setting calls for the ordinary polite です／ます style you have used throughout this volume, rather than the plain forms you have learned to recognize. Save plain-form practice for informal writing, private notes, or conversations with close friends, as your future volumes will clarify further.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Listing events without connecting them

Error: *京都に行きました。お寺を見ました。買い物をしました。

Correction: 京都に行って、お寺を見ました。それから、買い物をしました。 — Use て-form linking or discourse connectors such as それから to connect events into one account rather than presenting a flat list of separate sentences.

Common Mistake — Overusing わたし and あなた in a presentation

Error: *わたしは、わたしの先生に、わたしの経験について話しました。

Correction: 先生に自分の経験について話しました。 — Once the topic is established, omit repeated instances of わたし where the subject remains clear from context, and avoid あなた toward your teacher or host family; use their title or name instead.

Common Mistake — Placing a kanji reading you have not verified

Error: Guessing a reading for a name or place not covered in this volume and writing it without furigana.

Correction: Keep furigana on any name, place, or kanji outside this volume's schedule, and check unfamiliar readings with your instructor or a reliable dictionary rather than guessing.

Final Capstone Performance Task

さいしゅうパフォーマンスタスク

日本語で広がる世界 — Your world expands in Japanese

This capstone project draws on every unit of Volume I. Complete all four components below.

  • Written portfolio piece: Write a connected passage of at least twelve sentences that includes a self-introduction, a description of your daily life or town, a narrated past experience, a comparison of "then" and "now," a short modifying clause, a quotation with と and 言いました, and a closing statement about staying connected.
  • Spoken presentation: Present a spoken version of your portfolio piece to a partner, instructor, or small group, lasting approximately two to three minutes, adding at least one detail not included in your written work.
  • Interactive conversation: Answer at least three follow-up questions about your presentation, and ask a partner at least two questions about their own year of study.

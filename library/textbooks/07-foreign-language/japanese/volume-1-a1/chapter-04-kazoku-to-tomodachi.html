家族と友だちFamily and Friends

Reaching hiragana fluency, beginning katakana, and talking about who and what exists in your life · Speak and Write Japanese: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Ask and answer "who is this/that?" using だれ.
  2. Use の to describe family relationships and possession, such as わたしのちち.
  3. State that a person, animal, or family exists using います.
  4. State that a thing exists using あります, and explain the difference between います and あります.
  5. Combine location and existence in the pattern [place]に[person/thing]が います／あります.
  6. Name immediate family members using plain forms for your own family and polite forms for someone else's family.
  7. Recognize and begin using a small set of adjectives to describe people, such as やさしい and げんき.
  8. Recognize and begin writing the first block of katakana: the ア, カ, and サ rows.
  9. Recognize the small っ and produce geminate (doubled) consonants accurately.
  10. Recognize the seven new kanji introduced in this chapter: 家いえ・族ぞく・友とも・母はは・父ちち・女おんな・男おとこ.

Communication Scenario

Tom and Keiko have agreed to study together after class. Keiko brings a small photo of her family to show Tom, and Tom brings a photo of his own. Each of them wants to say who is in the photo, how that person relates to them, and one small detail about that person. This kind of exchange — sharing a photo and explaining who is who — is a common and natural way for new acquaintances to get to know each other a little better.

  1. Think about a photo you might show a new friend: a family photo, a photo with friends, or a photo of a pet. What information would you naturally give about each person or animal in the photo?
  2. In Japanese, the words you choose to describe a family member often depend on whose family you are talking about. This chapter gives you the tools to talk about your own family and to ask politely about someone else's.

ひらがな・カタカナ・かんじ — Writing System

Hiragana Fluency, Katakana, and New Kanji

小さい「っ」— Small っ and geminate consonants

Japanese has a special small version of the hiragana つ — written っ, about half the size of the regular つ — that does not represent a sound of its own. Instead, it signals that the following consonant is held or "doubled," creating a small pause before it is released. This doubled consonant counts as one full mora, just like any other kana.

Pronunciation Note — Hearing and producing small っ

Compare these two words, which are identical except for the small っ:

  • いた (ita) — two morae: i – ta. Meaning: "was" (a form you will study later).
  • いった (itta) — three morae: i – (pause) – ta. Meaning: "went."

The small っ adds a brief silent beat — like a tiny held breath — before the consonant that follows. It does not add a vowel sound. You will meet this pattern again in common words such as がっこう (school) and いっしょ (together). Practice tapping once for the pause itself, just as you would tap for any other mora.

れんしゅう — Practice: Say each pair aloud, tapping one beat per mora: いた／いった、おと／おっと? — note that not every such pair is a real word; focus on the rhythm difference itself, then practice がっこう and いっしょ several times.

Script Note — Two kana systems, one sound system

Hiragana and katakana represent the same underlying sounds; they are two different sets of symbols for one sound system, not two different pronunciations. At this stage, focus on accurate recognition and careful copying of the ア, カ, and サ rows. A frequent point of visual confusion for new learners is シ (shi) versus ツ (tsu, not yet introduced) and ソ (so) versus ン (n, not yet introduced) — pay close attention to the direction of the short strokes as you practice. Katakana vocabulary in full words begins in Chapter 6; for now, the goal is confident recognition and accurate handwriting of the symbols themselves.

Pronunciation

Pronunciation Focus — Long vowels inside family words

This chapter's family vocabulary offers an excellent opportunity to practice vowel length, because several word pairs are distinguished only by a long versus a short vowel:

  • おじいさん (ojīsan, grandfather) — five morae: o – ji – i – sa – n, with the long い held for a full extra beat.
  • おじさん (ojisan, uncle) — four morae: o – ji – sa – n, with a short じ and no extra vowel.

The same contrast applies to おばあさん (grandmother, long vowel) versus おばさん (aunt, short vowel). Confusing these words changes who you are talking about, not just how "correct" you sound — this is a meaningful, not merely stylistic, contrast. Practice each pair slowly, tapping once per mora, before speeding up.

Pronunciation Focus — Small っ in がっこう and いっしょ

Return to the small っ words introduced in the Script section: がっこう (school) and いっしょ (together). Say each word slowly, pausing briefly where the small っ falls, before returning to a natural speaking pace. Listen for this same pattern in your instructor's or partner's speech during this chapter's dialogue and listening activities.

Grammar Instruction

4.1 だれ — Asking "who"

Purpose: ask about the identity of a person, extending the question-word pattern you know from これ・それ・あれ.

  • これはだれですか。Kore wa dare desu ka. — Who is this?
  • あれはだれですか。Are wa dare desu ka. — Who is that (over there)?
  • これはだれのペンですか。Kore wa dare no pen desu ka. — Whose pen is this?だれ + の + [noun]
Grammar Note — だれ versus なに

You already know なん／なに (what) from Chapter 2. Use だれ only for people, and なに／なん only for things. Asking ＊これはなにですか about a person, or ＊これはだれですか about an object, will sound confusing to a Japanese listener even though the sentence structure is otherwise correct.

4.2 の — Showing family relationships

Purpose: extend the possessive/connecting particle の, introduced in Chapter 2, to describe relationships between people.

  • わたしのちちです。Watashi no chichi desu. — This is my father.
  • ともだちのいえです。Tomodachi no ie desu. — It's my friend's house.
  • けいこさんのおかあさんです。Keiko-san no okāsan desu. — This is Keiko-san's mother.

The pattern is always [person/owner] + の + [related person or thing]. Word order is the reverse of English "Keiko's mother" — Japanese places the possessor first, then の, then the item or person possessed.

4.3 います and あります — Talking about existence

Purpose: state that someone or something exists, choosing the correct verb for animate versus inanimate subjects.

Japanese uses two different verbs to say "there is / there are," depending on whether the subject is animate (a person or animal) or inanimate (a thing):

  • かぞくがいます。Kazoku ga imasu. — I have a family. / There is family.animate subject → います
  • ともだちがいます。Tomodachi ga imasu. — I have a friend.
  • いえがあります。Ie ga arimasu. — There is a house. / I have a house.inanimate subject → あります
Grammar Note — います for people and animals, あります for things

います is used for people and animals — anything considered animate. あります is used for objects, plants, and other things considered inanimate. This distinction has no equivalent in English "there is/are," so it requires deliberate attention. As you build vocabulary in later chapters, sort each new noun mentally into one of these two categories.

4.4 場所に〜がいます／あります — Existence with location

Purpose: state where a person or thing exists, using the location particle に together with います or あります.

  • いえにかぞくがいます。Ie ni kazoku ga imasu. — My family is at home.[いえ] + に + [かぞく] + が + います
  • がっこうにともだちがいます。Gakkō ni tomodachi ga imasu. — My friend is at school.

The particle に marks the location where something exists. The word order is fixed: [place] + に + [person/thing] + が + います／あります. Do not reverse this order or drop に when a location is stated.

4.5 Describing people (recognition)

Purpose: begin recognizing and lightly producing descriptive words about people, using the simple pattern 〜は＿＿です.

  • ちちはやさしいです。Chichi wa yasashii desu. — My father is kind.
  • あねはげんきです。Ane wa genki desu. — My older sister is well/energetic.
Grammar Note — Two kinds of adjective, briefly

Japanese has two families of descriptive words: い-adjectives, which end in い (such as やさしい), and な-adjectives, which do not (such as げんき). In this chapter, both types simply attach to です without any special change, so the difference is not yet visible. The difference becomes important once these words are used to directly modify a noun (for example, "a kind father") or placed into past or negative forms — those patterns are developed in later chapters. For now, treat these words as recognition vocabulary that you can use lightly in the 〜は＿＿です frame.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Tom and Keiko meet at a café to study, as planned at the end of Chapter 3. Keiko shows Tom a photo on her phone.

KEIKO これはわたしのかぞくです。 This is my family.
TOM この女おんなのひとはだれですか。 Who is this woman?
(Tom points at the photo — this use of "この" is recognition-only in this chapter.)
KEIKO わたしの母ははです。母はははやさしいです。 That's my mother. My mother is kind.
TOM おとうさんもいますか。 Is your father also there (in the photo)?
KEIKO はい、父ちちもいます。父ちちはきびしいです。あにもいます。 Yes, my father is also there. My father is strict. My older brother is also there.
TOM おにいさんはいまどこにいますか。 Where is your older brother now?
KEIKO あにはいまがっこうにいます。トムさんはきょうだいがいますか。 My older brother is at school right now. Tom-san, do you have any siblings?
TOM いいえ、きょうだいはいません。いぬがいます！ No, I don't have any siblings. I have a dog!
KEIKO かわいいですね！ Cute!

ないようりかい — Comprehension questions:

  1. How does Keiko describe her mother and her father? Which two adjectives does she use?
  2. Where is Keiko's older brother right now?
  3. Does Tom have any human siblings? What does he have instead?
  4. Find every place in the dialogue where います is used with a person and every place いません (its negative) appears. What do you think いません means, based on context?
  5. Keiko says おとうさんもいますか using も rather than が. Based on what you learned about も in earlier chapters, why is that natural here?
Grammar Note — いません: a preview

いません is the negative form of います, meaning "does not exist / doesn't have." You can recognize and use this form now in simple answers such as きょうだいはいません (I don't have siblings), following the pattern of ます/ません you already know from other polite verbs. The negative of あります — ありません — follows the same pattern.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Uchi and soto: in-group and out-group language

The plain/polite distinction for family terms in this chapter (ちち versus おとうさん) reflects a broader pattern in Japanese communication sometimes described as うち (in-group) and そと (out-group) language: how you refer to someone can shift depending on whether you are speaking about your own circle to an outsider, or about someone else's circle. This same underlying idea reappears later in the course in workplace and institutional contexts. For now, the practical rule is straightforward: plain terms for your own family, polite terms for someone else's family — and for many people, polite terms when addressing their own family members directly.

Culture in Context — Families look different from household to household

The vocabulary in this chapter — 父・母・あに・あね, and so on — describes common family roles, but real families vary widely: single-parent households, blended families, extended families sharing a home, same-sex parents, and households without children are all part of everyday life in Japanese-speaking communities, as everywhere else. When practicing this chapter's material, feel free to describe your actual family structure, a family you know, or a fictional one — the language works the same way regardless of family shape.

Regional Variation — Affectionate family terms

Beyond the standard forms taught in this chapter, many regions and families use their own affectionate variants for parents. In the Kansai region — home to Keiko's Osaka, introduced in Chapter 3 — casual terms such as おかん (mom) and おとん (dad) are common in informal, familiar speech, alongside the standard おかあさん／おとうさん. These regional and familiar forms are useful to recognize if you encounter them in media or conversation, but they are not part of this chapter's productive vocabulary; continue using the standard forms taught above in your own speaking and writing for now.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using あります for people

Error: *かぞくがあります。

Correction: かぞくがいます。 — People, animals, and other animate beings always take います, never あります. Sort each noun into "animate" or "inanimate" as you learn it.

Common Mistake — Using なに instead of だれ for people

Error: *これはなにですか。 (pointing at a person)

Correction: これはだれですか。 — Reserve なに／なん for objects and だれ for people.

Common Mistake — Using polite family terms for your own family

Error: Telling a classmate *おとうさんはやさしいです when describing your own father to them.

Correction: ちちはやさしいです。 — When describing your own family to someone outside it, use the plain forms (ちち、はは, etc.). Save the polite forms (おとうさん、おかあさん) for referring to someone else's family, or for family members who use them to address each other directly.

Common Mistake — Dropping the small っ

Error: Pronouncing がっこう as "ga-ko-u," without the pause.

Correction: がっこう has four morae: ga – (pause) – ko – u. The small っ is a full mora with its own duration, even though it has no vowel sound of its own.

Common Mistake — Wrong word order in location-existence sentences

Error: *かぞくがいえにいます is not wrong in meaning, but many learners instead produce *います、いえに、かぞくが or omit に entirely: *いえかぞくがいます.

Correction: いえにかぞくがいます。 — The fixed order is [place] + に + [person/thing] + が + います／あります. Both orders shown as correct in this note ([place]に first, or the subject stated first with に still attached) are acceptable, but に must always directly follow the place word.

Answer Key

Practice — Particle choice

Practice — います or あります

1. あにがいます。   2. いえがあります。   3. ともだちがいます。   4. かぞくがいます。

Dialogue — Comprehension

  1. Keiko describes her mother as やさしい (kind) and her father as きびしい (strict).
  2. Keiko's older brother is at school right now (がっこうにいます).
  3. Tom does not have human siblings; he uses きょうだいはいません. He has a dog instead (いぬがいます).
  4. いません appears once, describing Tom's lack of siblings. Based on context, it means the negative of います — "does not exist / doesn't have," used for people and animals.
  5. も is natural here because Keiko has already mentioned her mother being in the photo and is now adding her father as another item in the same category, which is exactly the function of も ("also") introduced in earlier chapters.

Chapter Review — Section A

  1. Father: plain ちち, polite おとうさん. Mother: plain はは, polite おかあさん.
  2. おばあさん means grandmother (long vowel); おばさん means aunt (short vowel). The vowel length of the second syllable distinguishes them.
  3. あに (older brother), あね (older sister), おとうと (younger brother), いもうと (younger sister).
  4. 女のひと (woman), 男のひと (man).
  5. い-adjectives: やさしい、きびしい (also acceptable: わかい、おもしろい). な-adjective: げんき.

Chapter Review — Section B

  1. ア行: ア イ ウ エ オ; カ行: カ キ ク ケ コ; サ行: サ シ ス セ ソ.
  2. The small っ adds a brief pause before the following consonant, creating a doubled/held consonant sound that counts as one full mora. Example: がっこう.
  3. 家 — e.g. 家族; 族 — e.g. 家族; 友 — e.g. 友だち; 母 — e.g. 母 or お母さん; 父 — e.g. 父 or お父さん; 女 — e.g. 女のひと; 男 — e.g. 男のひと.
  4. A kanji's reading belongs to the specific word it appears in, not to the character alone — the same character can have entirely different readings in different words.

Chapter Review — Section C

  1. あに（います）、いえ（あります）、いぬ（います）、がっこう（あります）.
  2. ともだちはがっこうにいます。
  3. これはわたしのあねです。
  4. これはだれですか。
  5. きょうだいはいません。

Chapter Review — Section D

  1. Model answers: レイラさんのはははげんきです。あねはがっこうのせんせいです。いもうとはわかいです。 (Other accurate answers accepted.)
  2. Commas are a compact list format common on cards and forms, distinct from a spoken/written sentence. Use と when writing a full connected sentence, such as 父と母がいます.

物語とつながりStories and Staying Connected

Relative clauses, quotation, giving and receiving awareness, and discourse linkage · Speak and Write Japanese: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Describe people, photos, and messages using vocabulary for stories, contact, and staying connected.
  2. Build a short noun-modifying (relative) clause to describe a person, photo, or thing.
  3. Report what someone said using the quotation particle と with 言う and 思う.
  4. Recognize how あげる, くれる, and もらう describe the direction of a favor or gift.
  5. Link two or more sentences into a short connected account using discourse connectors.
  6. Recognize sentence-final particles such as ね and よ receptively in natural speech.
  7. Read and write a short message or story that stays connected across several sentences.
  8. Recognize and read the ten new kanji introduced in this chapter, with furigana support where required.

Communication Scenario

Tom is getting ready to leave Japan at the end of his stay. Before he goes, he looks through photos with Yuki, tells a short story about a person he met, and exchanges messages with Yuki and her grandmother about staying in touch. This chapter closes Unit IV by moving from single comparisons into short connected narratives and by introducing the language people use to describe photos, quote what someone said, and talk about small gifts and favors exchanged between friends.

  1. This chapter builds on the past-tense narration from Chapter 16 and the experience and comparison language from Chapters 17 and 18, connecting them into a short story with description and quotation.
  2. By the end of the chapter, you will be able to describe a person or photo with a short modifying clause, quote what someone said, and write a connected message about staying in touch.

Script Note — 物語 as a single lexical unit

物語 is read ものがたり as a whole word rather than as two separately readable parts. Learn compounds like this as complete lexical items, and do not assume that every kanji inside a compound keeps the same reading it has when used alone, such as 物 in お土産-type words or 語 in 日本語.

Script Note — Quotation punctuation

Direct quotations in Japanese are usually marked with corner brackets 「 」 rather than English-style quotation marks. In this chapter's dialogue and reading, watch for how 「 」 mark what a person actually said, separate from the quotation particle と that introduces reported speech in a sentence.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Moraic ん before れ, and geminate consonants in contact words

Several important words in this chapter combine the moraic nasal ん with a following consonant, or contain a small っ. Practice these carefully so that a message about contacting someone is not misunderstood.

  • 連絡 れんらく — contact (hold ん for a full mora before the following 写真 しゃしん — photograph (contracted しゃ counts as one mora, followed by moraic ん at the end)
  • 持って もって — holding (small っ creates a brief pause before て; do not skip the timing)

Also review the falling and rising intonation used when quoting someone else's words, and the gentle rise often heard before sentence-final particles such as ね in reflective or connecting statements.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This section introduces short relative clauses, quotation with と, giving and receiving awareness, discourse connectors, and sentence-final particles for recognition.

1. Short noun-modifying (relative) clauses

Purpose: to describe a person, photo, or thing using a short clause placed directly before the noun.

  • 沖縄で出会った人the person (I) met in Okinawaplain past + noun: modifying clause before head noun
  • これは沖縄で撮った写真です。This is a photo (I) took in Okinawa.
  • これはおばあさんがくれたプレゼントです。This is the present (my) grandmother gave (me).

Japanese noun modification places the describing clause before the noun it describes, using a plain form. There is no separate word for "who," "which," or "that" as in English; the noun's role inside the clause is understood from context and word order rather than marked by a relative pronoun.

Common Mistake — Adding an extra pronoun inside the modifying clause

Error: *これは、私はそれを撮った写真です。

Correction: これは私が撮った写真です。 — Do not add an extra pronoun such as それ for the noun being modified; the head noun 写真 already fills that role, and the subject of the clause, if stated, normally takes が rather than は.

2. Quotation with と

Purpose: to report what someone said or thought.

  • 「また来てください」と言いました。(She) said, "Please come again."
  • また会えると思います。I think (we) will be able to meet again.
  • おばあさんは元気だと言っていました。(She) said that (my) grandmother was well.

The particle と connects a quoted sentence to verbs such as 言います ("to say") and 思います ("to think"). A direct quotation may be marked with corner brackets 「 」, while an indirect report uses a plain-form sentence before と without brackets.

3. Giving and receiving: recognizing direction

Purpose: to recognize how あげます, くれます, and もらいます show the direction of a gift or favor between people.

  • わたしはユキさんにプレゼントをあげました。I gave Yuki a present.giver: わたし → receiver: ユキさん
  • おばあさんはわたしにプレゼントをくれました。(My) grandmother gave me a present.giver: おばあさん → receiver: わたし (speaker's side)
  • わたしはおばあさんからプレゼントをもらいました。I received a present from (my) grandmother.receiver: わたし ← giver: おばあさん

あげます describes giving away from the speaker's side, くれます describes giving toward the speaker's side, and もらいます describes receiving. At this level, focus on recognizing which direction each verb describes in a sentence rather than producing all three freely in every context.

4. Connecting sentences into a short account

Purpose: to link several sentences into one connected story or message.

  • 沖縄で人に出会いました。それから、いろいろな話をしました。I met a person in Okinawa. After that, we talked about many things.
  • その人はやさしかったです。でも、日本語が少し難しかったです。That person was kind. But the Japanese was a little difficult.
  • 今度、また連絡します。そして、写真を送ります。Next time, I will get in touch again. And, I will send photos.

Connectors such as それから ("after that"), でも ("but"), and そして ("and, then") link separate sentences into a connected passage. These connectors return from earlier chapters and now support longer, story-like writing.

Building Block — A short connected story
Setting + person description with relative clause + event + quotation + closing connector sentence
A short story can introduce a setting, describe a person using a modifying clause, narrate an event, quote something the person said, and close with a connected sentence about staying in touch.
沖縄で出会った人はとても親切でした。いろいろな話をしてくれました。「また来てください」と言いました。それから、連絡先を交換しました。今度、写真を送ります。
The person I met in Okinawa was very kind. They talked with me about many things. They said, "Please come again." After that, we exchanged contact information. Next time, I will send photos.
Language Note — Sentence-final particles ね and よ (receptive)

You will hear speakers add ね or よ at the end of many sentences, such as いい写真ですね。 or 連絡しますよ。 ね softly invites agreement or shared feeling, while よ adds emphasis or tells the listener something they may not know. At this level, focus on recognizing these particles in speech and reading; you are not yet expected to choose between them productively.

  4. Write two connected sentences using それから or でも.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Tom and Yuki look through photos from his trip before he leaves Japan.

ユキ これは沖縄で撮った写真ですか。 Is this a photo you took in Okinawa?
トム はい、そうです。これは沖縄で出会った人です。とても親切でした。 Yes, that's right. This is a person I met in Okinawa. They were very kind.
ユキ そうですか。何か話しましたか。 Is that so? Did you talk about anything?
トム はい。「また来てください」と言いました。それから、連絡先を交換しました。 Yes. They said, "Please come again." After that, we exchanged contact information.
ユキ よかったですね。おばあさんにも写真を送ってください。 That's nice. Please send photos to my grandmother too.
トム もちろんです。おばあさんはわたしにいろいろな話をしてくれました。今度、写真とメッセージを送ります。 Of course. Your grandmother told me many stories. Next time, I'll send photos and a message.

ないようりかい — Comprehension questions:

  1. What is the photo Tom shows Yuki?
  2. What did the person Tom met in Okinawa say to him?
  3. What does Tom plan to send, and to whom?

Writing Workshop

In this workshop you will write a short connected story or message about a person, photo, or memory, using this chapter's systems.

  1. Choose a topic. Pick a real or imagined photo, memory, or person you want to describe.
  2. List your ideas in English first. Note who the person is, what happened, and what was said.
  3. Write an opening sentence introducing the photo or memory.
  4. Write a sentence with a short modifying clause describing the person or photo, such as 〜で出会った人です or 〜で撮った写真です.
  5. Write a sentence quoting something the person said, using 「 」 and 言いました.
  6. Write a sentence connecting two ideas using それから, でも, or そして.
  7. Write a closing sentence about staying connected, such as a plan to send a message or photo.
  8. Revise and check using the checklist below before finalizing your work.

チェックリスト — Editing checklist:

  • I introduced the photo, memory, or person clearly.
  • I used a short modifying clause to describe a person or photo.
  • I quoted something using 「 」 and 言いました.
  • I connected at least two sentences with a discourse connector.
  • I used this chapter's vocabulary and kanji accurately, with furigana where still required.
  • I ended each sentence with 。 and used consistent style within the passage.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Exchanging contact information and small gifts

Exchanging contact information after a friendly meeting, and giving or receiving a small gift as a token of appreciation, are common ways of maintaining a new connection in many settings in Japan, as elsewhere. A short, sincere message after returning home, such as thanking someone and mentioning a shared memory, is a simple and appropriate way to stay in touch.

Regional Note — Ways of staying connected vary

How people stay in touch, and how often, varies by generation, relationship, region, and personal habit. Some people exchange messages frequently through apps, while others prefer occasional letters, calls, or visits. The patterns in this chapter are for practicing the target language, not a claim about how every person or community maintains relationships.

Register Note — Giving and receiving language carries social meaning

Choosing あげます, くれます, or もらいます is not only a grammar choice; it reflects whose perspective the speaker is taking. Using くれます for something done for the speaker often expresses warmth or gratitude toward the giver. At this level, focus on recognizing this social meaning rather than mastering every nuance.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Placing the modifying clause after the noun

Error: *人、沖縄で出会った、は親切でした。

Correction: 沖縄で出会った人は親切でした。 — The modifying clause must come directly before the noun it describes, not after it.

Common Mistake — Forgetting と before 言いました or 思います

Error: *「また来てください」言いました。

Correction: 「また来てください」と言いました。 — The particle と must connect the quoted words to 言いました.

Common Mistake — Confusing あげます and もらいます

Error: *わたしはおばあさんにプレゼントをもらいました。 (intended: "I received a present from my grandmother")

Correction: わたしはおばあさんからプレゼントをもらいました。 — もらいます uses から or に to mark the giver, and the subject is the receiver; take care not to mix up the direction with あげます.

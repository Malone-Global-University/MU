毎日の生活Daily Routine

Talking about your schedule, the time, the days of the week, and how often you do things · Speak and Write Japanese: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Tell time in Japanese, including the hour, the minute, and the half-hour.
  2. Name the days of the week and talk about a weekly schedule.
  3. Use the systematic polite verb system with ます and ません to describe habitual and planned actions.
  4. Mark the object of an action with を and the location of an action with で.
  5. Mark a specific clock time with time に and a destination with に or へ.
  6. Describe how often you do something using frequency expressions.
  7. Connect a sequence of actions using それから.
  8. Read and write the remaining katakana rows and the nine Chapter 7 kanji for time and days, with furigana support.

Communication Scenario

Tom has settled into his new apartment and is getting used to his weekly schedule at the exchange program. His neighbor Mrs. Tanaka asks him about his daily routine over tea, and later Tom writes a short weekly schedule to share with a study partner who wants to plan a time to meet.

  1. This chapter teaches you to describe a daily and weekly routine using the systematic ます form you saw in earlier chapters, now fully active for regular use.
  2. By the end of the chapter, you will be able to state what time you do things, on which days, how often, and in what order.

Vocabulary Note — Frequency words come before the verb

Frequency words such as よく, ときどき, and たいてい are placed before the verb phrase, similar to English. ぜんぜん is special: it must always be paired with a negative verb, as in ぜんぜんはたらきません ("I don't work at all").

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Counting minutes and the sound changes of 分

The reading of 分 ("minute") changes depending on the number before it. Say each of these aloud, tapping once per mora:

  • いっぷん — 1 minute (small っ plus ぷ)
  • さんぷん — 3 minutes
  • よんぷん — 4 minutes
  • ごふん — 5 minutes
  • じゅっぷん — 10 minutes

These sound changes are lexical: they belong to the specific number-plus-counter combination, not to a rule you can apply everywhere. Practice them as fixed units, the same way you practiced numbers and counters in Chapter 9's preview material.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

7.1 なんじですか — Telling time

Purpose: state and ask for the time.

  • いま、七時です。Ima, shichi-ji desu. — It's seven o'clock now.
  • 九時半です。Ku-ji han desu. — It's nine thirty.
  • すみません、いまなんじですか。Sumimasen, ima nanji desu ka. — Excuse me, what time is it now?

半 ("half") always follows the hour and means "thirty minutes past," similar to English "half past."

7.2 systematic ます／ません — The polite verb system

Purpose: describe habitual actions, daily routines, and near-future plans in ordinary polite style.

  • まいあさ七時におきます。Mai-asa shichi-ji ni okimasu. — I get up at seven every morning.time に + verb ます
  • よる十一時にねます。Yoru jūichi-ji ni nemasu. — I go to sleep at eleven at night.
  • 土よう日ははたらきません。Doyōbi wa hatarakimasen. — I don't work on Saturdays.
Grammar Note — ます forms describe habits, not only single events

The polite nonpast form (おきます, ねます, はたらきます) can describe a habitual action ("I get up at seven every day"), a fact that is generally true, or a planned or scheduled future action. Japanese does not need a separate habitual-tense marker; context and words like まいあさ make the habitual meaning clear.

7.3 時に and に／へ — Marking clock time and destination

Purpose: state exactly when something happens and where someone is going.

  • 八時に学校へ行きます。Hachi-ji ni gakkō e ikimasu. — I go to school at eight o'clock.time に + destination へ
  • 六時に家に帰ります。Roku-ji ni ie ni kaerimasu. — I go home at six.

Both に and へ may mark a destination with movement verbs such as 行きます and 帰ります; the two particles are interchangeable in this use at this level. Time に is a separate, unrelated use of the same particle and marks a specific point in time, not a place.

7.4 を and で with daily activities

Purpose: mark the object of an action and the place where an activity happens.

  • あさ、シャワーをあびます。Asa, shawā o abimasu. — I take a shower in the morning.object を + verb
  • かいしゃではたらきます。Kaisha de hatarakimasu. — I work at the office.activity で

This continues the location に versus activity で contrast from Chapter 6: に marks where something exists or a specific time, while で marks where an action takes place.

7.5 頻度のことば — Frequency words

Purpose: describe how often you do something.

  • よくコンビニで買い物をします。Yoku konbini de kaimono o shimasu. — I often shop at the convenience store.
  • ときどきこうえんであるきます。Tokidoki kōen de arukimasu. — I sometimes walk in the park.
  • ぜんぜんテレビを見ません。Zenzen terebi o mimasen. — I don't watch TV at all.

7.6 それから — Sequencing actions

Purpose: connect two or more actions in the order they happen.

  • 七時におきます。それから、シャワーをあびます。Shichi-ji ni okimasu. Sorekara, shawā o abimasu. — I get up at seven. Then I take a shower.
  • かいしゃではたらきます。それから、うちへかえります。Kaisha de hatarakimasu. Sorekara, uchi e kaerimasu. — I work at the office. Then I go home.

それから begins a new sentence and links it to the sentence before it. This chapter uses それから only between separate polite sentences; joining actions within a single sentence will be introduced with the て-form in Chapter 11.

Common Mistake — Marking clock time with で instead of に

Error: *七時ではたらきます。

Correction: 七時にはたらきます。 — A specific clock time takes に, not で. で marks the place of an activity, not the time.

Language Focus

From single sentences to a connected routine. Earlier chapters introduced individual ます verbs and the particles that support them. This chapter puts the full polite verb system to work describing a real daily schedule: what you do, at what time, where, how often, and in what order, using それから to link the steps together.

Language Building Block — Describing a daily routine
まいあさ〜時におきます。それから、〜。〜時に〜へ行きます。
Anchor the routine to a time → sequence the next action → state a destination
まいあさ七時におきます。それから、シャワーをあびます。八時半に学校へ行きます。学校でべんきょうします。よく友だちとひるごはんを食べます。
I get up at seven every morning. Then I take a shower. I go to school at eight thirty. I study at school. I often eat lunch with friends.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Mrs. Tanaka visits Tom again and asks about his daily schedule over tea.

TANAKA トムさんは毎あさなんじにおきますか。 What time do you get up every morning, Tom?
TOM 七時におきます。それから、シャワーをあびます。 I get up at seven. Then I take a shower.
TANAKA プログラムはなんじからですか。 What time does the program start from?
TOM 九時からです。八時半に学校へ行きます。 From nine o'clock. I go to school at eight thirty.
TANAKA 土よう日もはたらきますか。 Do you work on Saturdays too?
TOM いいえ、土よう日ははたらきません。ときどきこうえんであるきます。 No, I don't work on Saturdays. I sometimes walk in the park.
TANAKA よるはなにをしますか。 What do you do in the evening?
TOM うちでばんごはんを食べます。それから、へやでべんきょうします。じゅういち時にねます。 I eat dinner at home. Then I study in my room. I go to sleep at eleven.
TANAKA いそがしい毎日ですね。 That's a busy daily routine, isn't it.

ないようりかい — Comprehension questions:

  1. What time does Tom get up, and what does he do right after?
  2. What time does Tom's program start, and what time does he leave for school?
  3. Does Tom work on Saturdays? What does he do instead?
  4. Identify each use of に in this dialogue and say whether it marks a time or a destination.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — The work week and weekend

Work and school schedules vary widely across Japan by industry, employer, region, and individual circumstance. While a Monday-to-Friday pattern with Saturday and Sunday off is common in many schools and offices, many people work variable schedules, including retail, hospitality, healthcare, and shift-based occupations, so it is not accurate to describe one fixed national schedule.

Culture in Context — Talking about routines with others

Asking a new acquaintance about their daily schedule, as Mrs. Tanaka does in this chapter's dialogue, is a common and friendly way to build a relationship in many everyday Japanese conversations, though the level of detail people share can depend on how well they know each other and the setting of the conversation.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Omitting に with a specific clock time

Error: *七時おきます。

Correction: 七時におきます。 — A specific clock time requires に before the verb.

Common Mistake — Using ぜんぜん with an affirmative verb

Error: *ぜんぜんはたらきます。

Correction: ぜんぜんはたらきません。 — ぜんぜん must always be paired with a negative verb form.

Common Mistake — Confusing time に with activity で

Error: *学校でなんじに行きますか。 used to ask "what time," when the speaker means the school itself as the location of studying, not the time.

Correction: Keep the two uses separate: なんじに学校へ行きますか。 (time) versus 学校でべんきょうします。 (activity location).

復習１：自己紹介Unit I Review: Introducing Yourself

Bringing together greetings, classroom language, personal information, and family into one connected performance · Speak and Write Japanese: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Greet someone, introduce yourself, and exchange names using です, は, and か.
  2. Identify classroom objects and people, and ask for clarification using これ／それ／あれ／どれ and の.
  3. State where you are from, what language(s) you speak, and what you study, using から, を, and と.
  4. Introduce family members and friends, ask だれですか, and choose correctly between います and あります.
  5. Combine a location and an existing person or thing using [place]に[person/thing]が います／あります.
  6. Read and write complete hiragana, the first fifteen katakana symbols (ア・カ・サ rows), and the fourteen kanji introduced in Unit I.
  7. Demonstrate control of the small っ, long vowels, and moraic ん in both reading aloud and writing.
  8. Produce a connected spoken and written self-introduction that combines every system from Chapters 1 through 4.

Communication Scenario

A new international exchange program is holding its first meeting at the community center. Everyone in the room is a stranger, and the organizer has asked each participant to prepare a short self-introduction: name, origin, language(s), what they study or do, and one or two words about their family or a friend who is important to them. Tom and Keiko, who have already met through their earlier study sessions, are each preparing to introduce themselves to the wider group and to meet other new people for the first time.

  1. This chapter does not introduce anything new. Instead, it asks you to combine everything from Chapters 1 through 4 into one connected performance — exactly the kind of task a real first meeting like this would require.
  2. As you review, notice how naturally the systems from separate chapters fit together: a greeting leads into a name, a name leads into an origin, an origin leads into a family or friend, and a family member leads into a simple description.

Pronunciation Review

Pronunciation Focus — Mora timing across Unit I

Every word you have learned in Chapters 1 through 4 is built from morae of equal length: short vowels, long vowels, the moraic ん, and the small っ. Say the following words aloud, tapping once per mora, and check your rhythm against the mora count given:

  • はじめまして — 6 morae
  • がくせい — 4 morae (long vowel in せい)
  • がっこう — 4 morae (small っ, long vowel in こう)
  • おじいさん — 5 morae (long vowel) versus おじさん — 4 morae (short vowel)
Pronunciation Focus — Priority consonants and intonation

Review the Japanese ら-row tap, the vowel quality of う, and basic statement, question, and confirmation intonation. Read the Integrated Dialogue in this chapter aloud, paying attention to rising intonation on questions ending in か and level intonation on ordinary statements.

Grammar Review

No new grammar is introduced in this chapter. The four systems below are the ones this chapter's tasks require you to combine.

5.1 です／は／か — The basic sentence frame

Purpose: identify, describe, and ask about people and things.

  • トムです。Tomu desu. — I am Tom.
  • わたしはがくせいです。Watashi wa gakusei desu. — I am a student.
  • これはなんですか。Kore wa nan desu ka. — What is this?

5.2 の — Connecting nouns and showing relationships

Purpose: connect two nouns, including possession and family relationships.

  • にほんごのほんです。Nihongo no hon desu. — It's a Japanese-language book.
  • わたしのちちです。Watashi no chichi desu. — This is my father.

5.3 から／を／と — Origin, object, and connection

Purpose: state where you are from, what you speak or study, and what things go together.

  • カナダから来ました。Kanada kara kimashita. — I came from Canada.
  • にほんごをはなします。Nihongo o hanashimasu. — I speak Japanese.
  • ちちとははがいます。Chichi to haha ga imasu. — I have a father and a mother.

5.4 います／あります — Existence, with and without location

Purpose: state that a person, animal, or thing exists, and where.

  • きょうだいがいます。Kyōdai ga imasu. — I have siblings.animate → います
  • いえがあります。Ie ga arimasu. — There is a house.inanimate → あります
  • がっこうにともだちがいます。Gakkō ni tomodachi ga imasu. — My friend is at school.[place] + に + [person] + が + います
Grammar Note — Omission across the four systems

Across Chapters 1 through 4, you have repeatedly seen subjects and topics dropped once they are clear from context — for example, answering トムです。 instead of わたしはトムです。. This chapter's tasks reward natural omission. Do not add わたし or あなた to every sentence simply because English requires a subject.

Language Focus

One connected introduction, four systems working together. A complete self-introduction moves naturally from a greeting, to a name and origin, to what you study or speak, to a family member or friend, and finally to one small description. Every piece of this sequence was taught separately in Chapters 1 through 4; this chapter asks you to produce it as a single connected performance, spoken and written.

Integrated Dialogue

Setting: The first meeting of the exchange program. Tom introduces himself to Aiko, a new participant he has not met before.

TOM はじめまして。トムです。よろしくおねがいします。 How do you do. I'm Tom. Pleased to meet you.
AIKO はじめまして。あいこです。トムさんはどこから来ましたか。 How do you do. I'm Aiko. Where are you from, Tom?
TOM カナダから来ました。えいごとにほんごをはなします。 I came from Canada. I speak English and Japanese.
AIKO がくせいですか。 Are you a student?
TOM はい、がくせいです。かぞくは、ははといぬがいます。はははやさしいです。 Yes, I'm a student. As for my family, I have my mother and a dog. My mother is kind.
AIKO いぬですか！かわいいですね。わたしもかぞくがいます。ちちとあねがいます。 A dog! How cute. I also have family. I have a father and an older sister.
TOM おとうさんはどんなひとですか。 What is your father like?
AIKO ちちはきびしいです。でも、あねはやさしいです。あねはいまがっこうにいます。 My father is strict. But my older sister is kind. My older sister is at school right now.
TOM そうですか。よろしくおねがいします。 I see. Pleased to meet you (again, in closing).

ないようりかい — Comprehension questions:

  1. Where is Tom from, and what two languages does he speak?
  2. What does Tom have at home, and how does he describe his mother?
  3. Name Aiko's two family members mentioned in the dialogue.
  4. Which of Aiko's family members is described as strict, and which as kind?
  5. Identify every use of の, が, を, and に in this dialogue and explain the function of each.

Culture Review

Culture in Context — First meetings and continuing relationships

Across Chapters 1 through 4, you have learned that はじめまして and よろしくおねがいします mark a first meeting and open an ongoing relationship, not a single closed exchange. As you deliver your self-introduction in this chapter's performance task, remember that these are formulaic expressions used specifically for first encounters — you would not repeat はじめまして to someone you already know.

Culture in Context — Uchi and soto revisited

Recall the in-group/out-group pattern from Chapter 4: plain family terms (ちち、はは) for your own family when speaking to someone outside it, and polite terms (おとうさん、おかあさん) for someone else's family. This distinction is tested directly in this chapter's writing and speaking tasks, since you will both describe your own family and ask about a partner's.

Regional Variation — A reminder

Chapter 3 introduced Keiko's origin in Osaka, and Chapter 4 mentioned casual Kansai terms such as おかん and おとん as recognition-only regional vocabulary. These forms remain receptive only in this chapter; continue to use the standard forms taught in Unit I for all speaking and writing tasks.

Common Mistakes Review

Common Mistake — Confusing だれ and なに

Error: *これはなにですか when pointing at a person.

Correction: これはだれですか。 — Reserve だれ for people and なに／なん for things, as taught in Chapter 4.

Common Mistake — Using あります for people or animals

Error: *いぬがあります。

Correction: いぬがいます。 — Animate beings always take います.

Common Mistake — Overusing わたし

Error: Beginning every sentence with わたしは, as English speakers often do with "I."

Correction: Once you have stated your topic once, later sentences often omit it entirely, as in トムです。カナダから来ました。 — no repeated わたしは is needed.

Common Mistake — Mixing plain and polite family terms

Error: *おかあさんはやさしいです when describing your own mother to a classmate.

Correction: はははやさしいです。 — Plain forms for your own family; polite forms for someone else's.

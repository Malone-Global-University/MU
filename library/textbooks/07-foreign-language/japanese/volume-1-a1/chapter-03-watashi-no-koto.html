わたしのことAbout Me

Completing hiragana, talking about where you are from, and your first polite verbs · Speak and Write Japanese: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. State where you are from using the formulaic expression 〜から来ました.
  2. Name the language you speak and the country you are from using 〜語 and 〜人.
  3. Use the object particle を with common verbs such as 話します、勉強します、食べます.
  4. Use the particle と to connect nouns meaning "and" and "with."
  5. Ask and answer basic questions about origin, languages, and current study.
  6. Use から to express "from" in origin statements.
  7. Produce a short self-introduction linking information from Chapters 1, 2, and 3.
  8. Recognize and write the final rows of core hiragana: ら, わ, and ん.
  9. Recognize the four kanji introduced in this chapter: 語ご、学がく、生せい、年ねん.
  10. Write your age and school-year level in Japanese.

Communication Scenario

It is the second week of class. Yamada-sensei has arranged a short "self-introduction" activity. Each student stands, says their name, where they are from, what language they speak at home, what they are studying, and one additional piece of information. Other students can then ask follow-up questions. For the first time, the class needs to say something meaningful about themselves — not just identify objects on a desk.

  1. Think about what you usually say when you introduce yourself for the first time in a new group. What information comes first? What comes later? Does your ordering change depending on the setting?
  2. In many Japanese contexts, a self-introduction — called 自己紹介じこしょうかい — follows a fairly recognizable pattern: name, affiliation or origin, then one or two personal details. This chapter gives you the tools to produce a basic but genuine version of this pattern in Japanese.

Pronunciation Note — The Japanese ら row

The consonant of the ら row is neither the English "r" nor the English "l." It is most accurately described as a flap or tap: the tip of the tongue briefly touches the ridge behind the upper front teeth and immediately pulls away, similar to the "d" or "r" sound in the middle of an American English word like "butter" or "water." Do not curl the tongue back as in English "r," and do not press it firmly as in English "l." Listen to model audio or a native speaker carefully, and practice words such as らいねん (next year) and にほんご (Japanese language) out loud.

Script Note — を and ん: two special symbols

を is the hiragana used exclusively for the object particle. In modern standard Japanese, it is always pronounced "o" — the same vowel sound as お — but its spelling distinguishes it from the regular vowel お. Every time you see を, you know it is functioning as the object particle を and nothing else.

ん represents the moraic nasal, which you have already encountered in words like えんぴつ and わかりません. It is always one full mora in length. Its exact pronunciation shifts depending on what follows it — it can sound like "n," "m," or a nasal resonance similar to "ng" — but you do not need to engineer these variants consciously. Producing a clear nasal at the right position in the word is sufficient for intelligibility.

Script Note — 生 has many readings

The kanji 生 is one of the most versatile characters in Japanese and illustrates an important principle: the reading of a kanji belongs to the word, not the character alone. In 学生がくせい it reads せい; in 先生せんせい it also reads せい; in 一年生いちねんせい it reads せい again. But in 生いきる (to live) or 生うまれる (to be born), entirely different readings appear. For now, learn the reading of each complete word — do not try to memorize all possible readings of 生 in the abstract.

Pronunciation

Completing the sound inventory

With the addition of the ら row and ん, you now have access to the complete core Japanese sound inventory in hiragana. Before moving to new material, it is worth reviewing where English-speaking learners most often develop persistent habits that affect intelligibility.

Pronunciation Focus — Vowel sequences and long vowels

Several words in this chapter contain vowel sequences that must be spoken as separate morae, not as English diphthongs. Compare:

  • えいご (Eigo, English language) — three morae: e – i – go. Do not collapse ei into a single gliding sound like "ay." Each vowel has its own beat.
  • べんきょう (benkyō, study) — four morae: be – n – kyo – u. The final う extends the o sound; it is not a separate syllable "oo," but a lengthened o.
  • りゅうがくせい (ryūgakusei, international student) — six morae: ryu – u – ga – ku – se – i. Practice counting as you tap.

Long vowels are meaningful in Japanese — they can distinguish different words entirely. Training yourself to hold the lengthened vowel for a full extra mora is one of the highest-value pronunciation habits you can build at this stage.

Pronunciation Focus — ん in self-introductions

Several key vocabulary words in this chapter contain ん: にほんご, べんきょう, わかりません. In each case, ん counts as one full mora. The most common error is to swallow ん or merge it with a nearby vowel. Practice these words while tapping one finger per mora and you will feel where ん belongs in the rhythm of the word.

Grammar Instruction

3.1 〜から来ました — Expressing origin

Purpose: state where you are from using a formulaic polite expression.

To say where you are from, use the pattern 〜から来ました. The particle から means "from," and 来ました is the polite past form of the verb 来る (to come). At this stage, treat the whole phrase as a formulaic unit — the internal grammar of 来ました will be developed formally in later chapters.

  • アメリカから来ました。Amerika kara kimashita. — I am from America.[アメリカ] + から + 来ました
  • 日本にほんから来ました。Nihon kara kimashita. — I am from Japan.
  • どこから来ましたか。Doko kara kimashita ka. — Where are you from?
Grammar Note — から has two uses you have now seen

In Chapter 3 you meet から in the origin expression 〜から来ました, where it means "from" in a spatial sense. You will eventually also encounter から with the meaning "because" or "so" when connecting clauses. These are different uses of the same particle; for now, focus on the origin use and note that context will always clarify which meaning applies.

3.2 〜語と〜語をはなします — Languages and the particles と and を

Purpose: name the languages you speak using the object particle を and the listing particle と.

This chapter introduces two important particles used together in a natural statement about languages.

The object particle を marks the noun that receives the action of a transitive verb. The verb はなします (speak) takes a language name as its object:

  • えいごをはなします。Eigo o hanashimasu. — I speak English.[えいご] + を + はなします
  • 日本語にほんごをはなします。Nihongo o hanashimasu. — I speak Japanese.
  • 日本語にほんごを勉強べんきょうします。Nihongo o benkyō shimasu. — I study Japanese.

The particle と connects two or more nouns in a list meaning "and." It is also used to mean "with" when describing a companion:

  • えいごとスペインごをはなします。Eigo to Supeingo o hanashimasu. — I speak English and Spanish.[えいご] + と + [スペインご] + を + はなします
  • ともだちとはなします。Tomodachi to hanashimasu. — I speak with a friend.と = "with" when marking a companion
Grammar Note — を is pronounced "o," not "wo"

The hiragana を is consistently pronounced "o" in modern standard Japanese, even though it is romanized as "wo" in some systems. When you see を, pronounce it the same way you pronounce the vowel お. Its distinctive spelling is simply a convention that identifies it as the object particle rather than the syllable お.

Grammar Note — と (and) vs. や (and, among others)

You saw the particle や briefly in Chapter 2's vocabulary notes (as in ノートや教科書). Both と and や connect nouns, but they differ in an important way. と presents an exhaustive list — "A and B, and that is all." や presents a partial list — "A and B (among other things)." For now, use と when you mean to name all the items in your list. や will be developed further in Chapter 8.

3.3 〜がわかります — The particle が with わかります

Purpose: say what you understand or know, using the correct particle with わかります.

The verb わかります (to understand) uses the particle が, not を, to mark what is understood. This pattern differs from English, where "understand" takes a direct object:

  • えいごがわかります。Eigo ga wakarimasu. — I understand English. / I know English.[えいご] + が + わかります
  • 日本語にほんごがすこしわかります。Nihongo ga sukoshi wakarimasu. — I understand Japanese a little.
  • まだわかりません。Mada wakarimasen. — I don't understand yet.

The reason わかります uses が rather than を relates to the nature of the verb — it describes a state of awareness rather than an action performed on an object. The full contrast between が and を develops throughout the course; at this stage, simply memorize the frame 〜がわかります as a unit.

3.4 〜歳です / 〜年生です — Stating age and year level

Purpose: state your age and student year level using counters with the copula.

To state age, combine a number with the counter 〜歳 and the copula です. To state school year, combine a number with 〜年生:

  • じゅうはっさいです。Jūhassai desu. — I am 18 years old.
  • にねんせいです。Ninensei desu. — I am a second-year student.
  • なんさいですか。Nansai desu ka. — How old are you?
  • なんねんせいですか。Nannensei desu ka. — What year are you?
Language Focus

Building a self-introduction step by step. You now have the pieces to produce a short but genuine self-introduction. A typical beginner 自己紹介 in a classroom setting might follow this order: (1) greeting, (2) name, (3) origin, (4) languages, (5) current situation (student, year level), (6) one personal detail. Each piece slots into a simple frame you already know. Notice that the Japanese self-introduction does not require you to state every piece of information — leaving out age, for example, is perfectly natural unless the setting calls for it.

Language Building Block — Self-introduction frame
はじめまして。〜と申します。〜から来ました。〜語を話します。〜年生です。どうぞよろしくお願いします。
Greeting → Name (formulaic polite form) → Origin → Language(s) → Year/status → Closing bow phrase
はじめまして。レイラ・スミスと申します。アメリカから来ました。えいごとすこし日本語を話します。二年生です。どうぞよろしくお願いします。
Nice to meet you. I am called Layla Smith. I am from America. I speak English and a little Japanese. I am a second-year student. I look forward to your kind regard.

〜と申します (to moushimasu) is a polite and humble way to state your name, used in formal self-introductions. It will be analyzed grammatically in a later chapter; for now, use it as a formulaic unit in formal settings, and 〜といいます in more everyday contexts. どうぞよろしくお願いします is the standard closing phrase of a first meeting and will be discussed in the Culture section below.

Model Dialogue

Setting: after class in the corridor. Tom approaches Keiko, who has just given her self-introduction. He wants to ask about where she is from and what languages she speaks.

TOM けいこさん、どこから来ましたか。 Keiko-san, where are you from?
KEIKO 大阪おおさかから来ました。日本にほんの大阪おおさかです。トムさんは？ I am from Osaka. Osaka in Japan. And you, Tom-san?
TOM カナダから来ました。バンクーバーです。 I am from Canada. Vancouver.
KEIKO そうですか。えいごをはなしますか。 Is that so? Do you speak English?
TOM はい、えいごをはなします。フランスごもすこしはなします。けいこさんは？ Yes, I speak English. I also speak a little French. And you, Keiko-san?
KEIKO 日本語にほんごをはなします。えいごもべんきょうしています。でも、まだあまりわかりません。 I speak Japanese. I am also studying English. But I don't understand it very well yet.
TOM わたしも日本語にほんごをべんきょうしています！いっしょにべんきょうしましょうか。 I am also studying Japanese! Shall we study together?
KEIKO はい、ぜひ！よろしくおねがいします。 Yes, please! I am in your care.

ないようりかい — Comprehension questions:

  1. Where is Keiko from? How specific is her answer?
  2. Which two languages does Tom say he speaks? How does he add the second one?
  3. What does Keiko say about her English? What word does she use to mean "yet" or "not very well yet"?
  4. In the final exchange, Tom uses いっしょにべんきょうしましょうか. Based on the context, what do you think いっしょに and 〜ましょうか contribute to this sentence?
  5. Notice that Tom says けいこさんは？ without a verb. What function does this short-form question serve, and why is it natural?
Grammar Note — べんきょうしています: a preview

Both Tom and Keiko use the form べんきょうしています rather than べんきょうします to describe their ongoing study. The 〜ています construction, which expresses an ongoing state or continuing action, will be introduced formally in Chapter 18. For now, recognize it in the dialogue as meaning "am studying" (ongoing) rather than "study" (general habit), and treat it as recognition-only material.

Writing Workshop

In this chapter you will write a profile card for yourself, then turn it into a connected short self-introduction paragraph.

  1. Complete your profile card. Fill in the following fields with information about yourself (or a fictional persona). Write everything in Japanese, using hiragana for words you know and the vocabulary from Chapters 1–3:
    • なまえ：
    • くに：
    • ことば：
    • べんきょう：にほんごをべんきょうしています。 (copy this line)
    • しゅみ：
  2. Write a connected self-introduction. Using your profile card as a source, write a five-to-seven sentence self-introduction paragraph. Follow the framework: greeting → name → origin → languages → student status or study goal → one personal detail → closing phrase. Use full sentences with particles.
  3. Add at least one negative statement. For example: まだあまりにほんごがわかりません or 〜ではありません.
  4. Add at least one も sentence. Connect two pieces of information that share a common predicate, for example: えいごをはなします。フランスごもすこしはなします。
  5. Revise and check. Use the checklist below before finalizing your work.

チェックリスト — Editing checklist:

  • I used 〜から来ました correctly for my origin.
  • I used を with action verbs (はなします、べんきょうします) and が with わかります.
  • I used と to list nouns, not は or を.
  • I used も in place of は, not alongside it.
  • I checked my hiragana, especially the new symbols ら り る れ ろ わ を ん.
  • I ended each sentence with a Japanese full stop 。.
  • I have not used katakana that I have not yet studied for production.
  • I have not used kanji beyond the scheduled set (Chapters 1–3 kanji only).

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — どうぞよろしくおねがいします

The closing phrase of a Japanese self-introduction, どうぞよろしくおねがいします, has no clean English equivalent. Its literal meaning is something like "please regard me favorably," but its communicative function is to signal the beginning of a relationship — an invitation to the other person to treat you kindly and to look forward to working together. The phrase is typically accompanied by a bow, whose depth reflects the formality of the setting. In a classroom context, a moderate bow is appropriate. In a business or very formal context, a deeper bow with both hands at the sides or front signals greater respect. The shorter version, よろしくおねがいします, is appropriate in most non-ceremonial settings, including a language class.

Culture in Context — Names and name order

In Japanese, the family name traditionally comes before the given name: 山田けいこ means the person's family name is 山田 (Yamada) and given name is けいこ (Keiko). When reading Japanese-language documents, name cards, or official materials in Japan, expect the family-name-first order. In international or English-medium contexts, many Japanese people may switch to the given-name-first order to accommodate expectations. When you introduce yourself in Japanese, you may give your name in either order depending on your preference and context, but be aware that your instructor or conversational partner may understand your family name to be whichever comes first if you follow Japanese convention. It is always kind to clarify which is which if your name is unfamiliar in Japan.

Culture in Context — Asking about age

While the expression なんさいですか (How old are you?) is grammatically correct and widely understood, asking someone's age directly can feel intrusive in many Japanese contexts — particularly with adults, seniors, or new acquaintances. In a language classroom where age is relevant to year-level placement, the question is generally acceptable. In other settings, especially professional or social ones, it is common to either volunteer your own age first and invite the other person to share, or to avoid the topic entirely unless it comes up naturally. Among close friends and peers in casual contexts, direct age questions are less unusual.

Regional Variation — Osaka and regional identity

Keiko in this chapter's dialogue says she is from 大阪おおさか (Osaka), in the Kansai region of Japan. Osaka, along with Kyoto and Kobe, forms one of Japan's major metropolitan areas and has a distinctive regional variety of Japanese — often called 関西弁 (Kansai-ben) or more specifically 大阪弁 (Osaka-ben). Speakers from Osaka are sometimes associated with particular speech patterns, including characteristic intonation and vocabulary that differs from the Tokyo-based standard. Regional identity can be a meaningful part of self-introduction in Japan; saying you are from Osaka carries cultural associations. The standard Japanese in this textbook remains the core productive model, but regional varieties like Kansai-ben are an important part of the full picture of Japanese-language diversity that you will encounter as you progress.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using を with わかります

Error: *にほんごをわかります。

Correction: にほんごがわかります。 — The verb わかります describes a state of knowing or understanding and uses the subject-marking particle が, not the object particle を. Memorize the frame 〜がわかります as a complete unit.

Common Mistake — Confusing と (listing) and と (with/companion)

Error: Writing *ともだちとえいごをはなします when you mean "I speak English with a friend" — this is actually correct, but learners sometimes confuse this use of と (companion) with the listing use. The key is that in listing, と connects nouns of the same category (えいごとフランスごをはなします); in the companion use, と marks the person you are doing something with (ともだちとはなします).

Remember: Both uses are correct and natural. The meaning is clear from context. Practice producing both patterns so you use と confidently in each situation.

Common Mistake — Placing から after the destination instead of the origin

Error: *来ましたアメリカから。 or placing から in the wrong position.

Correction: アメリカから来ました。 — The particle から attaches directly to the origin noun. The verb comes at the end of the sentence, as in all Japanese predicate-final sentences. The pattern is: [Place] + から + 来ました, not [Verb] + [Place] + から.

Common Mistake — Treating ん as a vowel at the end of a word

Error: Pronouncing にほん (Japan) as two syllables: "nee-hon," dropping the final ん.

Correction: にほん has three morae: ni – ho – n. The final ん is a full mora with its own duration. Tap three times while saying the word. The same applies to べんきょう (four morae: be – n – kyo – u) and わかりません (six morae: wa – ka – ri – ma – se – n). Every ん counts.

Common Mistake — Using あなた as the default "you"

Error: Translating every English "you" directly as あなた — for example, asking *あなたはどこから来ましたか when speaking to a classmate by name.

Correction: In Japanese, using the person's name or title is more natural and more polite than using あなた in most conversational contexts. Notice how Tom and Keiko in the model dialogue use けいこさんは？ and トムさんは？ rather than あなたは？. Reserve あなた for situations where you genuinely do not know the other person's name, or where it is specifically appropriate. When in doubt, use the person's name plus さん.

Dialogue — Comprehension

  1. Keiko is from Osaka, Japan. She specifies both the city and the country, which is more precise than just saying "Japan."
  2. Tom speaks English and a little French. He uses も to add French as a second item: フランスごもすこしはなします.
  3. Keiko says she does not understand English very well yet. She uses まだあまりわかりません — まだ means "yet" or "still," and あまり〜ません means "not very much / not particularly."
  4. いっしょに means "together." 〜ましょうか is a suggestion form meaning "shall we 〜?" So the sentence means "Shall we study together?" This form will be introduced formally in Chapter 12.
  5. けいこさんは？ is a short-form follow-up question that omits the predicate because it was just stated by the other speaker. It means "And you, Keiko-san?" It is natural precisely because it avoids unnecessary repetition — a very typical feature of Japanese conversation.

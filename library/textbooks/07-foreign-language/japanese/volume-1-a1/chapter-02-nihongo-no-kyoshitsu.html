日本語にほんごの教室きょうしつIn Japanese Class

Classroom language, demonstratives, and your second hiragana group · Speak and Write Japanese: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Name common classroom objects using the vocabulary introduced in this chapter.
  2. Point out and identify objects using the demonstratives これ、それ、あれ、どれ.
  3. Ask "What is this?" and similar questions using 〜は何ですか.
  4. Show possession or connection between two nouns using the particle の.
  5. Add the particle も to mean "also" or "too."
  6. Say that something is not the case, using the negative copular form 〜ではありません.
  7. Ask for repetition and clarification using classroom survival expressions.
  8. Recognize and write the second group of hiragana, from な through ま.
  9. Recognize the three kanji introduced in this chapter: 日にち・ひ、本ほん、人ひと・じん.
  10. Count from 6 to 10 in Japanese.

Communication Scenario

It is the first morning of class. Yamada-sensei asks students to look around the room and name the objects on their desks. A new student, Keiko, arrives late and asks a classmate what a nearby object is. Meanwhile, everyone is still getting comfortable asking the teacher to slow down or repeat something.

  1. Think about how you might ask "What is that?" or "Whose is this?" in your first language. What words do you use to distinguish between near and far objects?
  2. In this chapter you will learn a systematic set of four Japanese pointing words — こ・そ・あ・ど — that work across many different contexts. Mastering this system will let you ask about almost any unfamiliar object, name, or concept you encounter.

Script Note — Dakuten and handakuten: voiced and semi-voiced sounds

Japanese hiragana uses two diacritical marks to create additional sounds from the basic rows. The dakuten (゛), two small strokes placed at the upper right of a symbol, turns an unvoiced consonant into its voiced counterpart:

  • は→ば (ha → ba), ひ→び (hi → bi), ふ→ぶ (fu → bu), へ→べ (he → be), ほ→ぼ (ho → bo)

The handakuten (゜), a small circle, applies only to the は row and produces a "p" sound:

  • は→ぱ (ha → pa), ひ→ぴ (hi → pi), ふ→ぷ (fu → pu), へ→ぺ (he → pe), ほ→ぽ (ho → po)

These marks must be written clearly and in the correct position. A missing dakuten changes the word entirely: はな (hana, flower/nose) is not the same word as ばな. You will encounter dakuten throughout your vocabulary; treat the mark as part of the symbol, not an optional decoration.

Pronunciation

ふ and the は row

The は-row consonants deserve particular attention from English-speaking learners.

  • ふ — This mora is often romanized "fu," but the consonant is not identical to English "f." The lips do not press together or touch the teeth; instead, the sound is produced by bringing the upper and lower lips close together and letting air pass through. Listen carefully and aim for a softer, more central friction than the English "f."
  • ひ — The consonant here is similar to English "h," but before the vowel い it sounds more like a light "sh"-like friction in some speakers' speech. Focus on the vowel quality and even mora timing rather than engineering a precise consonant.
Pronunciation Note — Dakuten and voicing

Adding dakuten to the は row creates the ば row (ba, bi, bu, be, bo). The handakuten creates the ぱ row (pa, pi, pu, pe, po). When you see dakuten, the change is not just spelling — the sound genuinely shifts from unvoiced to voiced, or from h to b to p. Practice saying minimal pairs such as はな (flower/nose) and ばな, or ほん (book) and ぼん (tray/basin), so your ear and mouth both register the difference.

Mora timing: longer words

As vocabulary grows, mora counting becomes increasingly useful. Try tapping out the mora counts of this chapter's new words:

  • えんぴつ — four morae: e-n-pi-tsu
  • ホワイトボード — seven morae: ho-wa-i-to-bō-do (the long vowel ー counts as one mora)
  • わかりません — five morae: wa-ka-ri-ma-se-n — wait, that is six. Count carefully.

Miscounting morae is common. Always count syllable-by-syllable while listening to a model, then verify by tapping.

Pronunciation Note — Particle の and its mora

The particle の, which you will study in the grammar section below, is a single mora — one even beat. In connected speech, however, の is relatively unstressed and can pass by quickly. Train yourself to hear it clearly in phrases such as わたしのほん (my book): three words, four morae (wa-ta-shi-no-ho-n — actually six; count again carefully). Developing accurate mora awareness now will pay dividends throughout the course.

Grammar Instruction

2.1 これ・それ・あれ・どれ — Demonstratives

Purpose: point out and identify objects in relation to the speaker and listener.

Japanese has a systematic set of pointing words organized around the concept of distance and perspective. Four of them — これ、それ、あれ、どれ — replace a noun when pointing at a thing:

JapaneseApproximate meaningUse
これthis oneNear the speaker; the speaker can reach it
それthat oneNear the listener; or a step away from the speaker
あれthat one over thereAway from both speaker and listener; some distance away
どれwhich one?Question word; "which of these things?"

Combined with は〜ですか, these demonstratives let you ask and answer questions about any object:

  • これはえんぴつです。Kore wa enpitsu desu. — This is a pencil.[これ] + は + [えんぴつ] + です
  • それはノートですか。Sore wa nōto desu ka. — Is that a notebook?
  • あれは何なんですか。Are wa nan desu ka. — What is that over there?
  • どれがあなたのかばんですか。Dore ga anata no kaban desu ka. — Which one is your bag?
Grammar Note — そ is not simply "further away than こ"

English "this" and "that" map roughly onto a two-way near/far distinction. Japanese uses a three-way system, and the boundary between それ and あれ is not purely about physical distance. それ often signals that the object is in the listener's zone — on their desk, in their hand, or closer to them than to the speaker. あれ typically signals something outside both parties' immediate space. As you listen to natural Japanese, pay attention to which word speakers choose and notice that perspective and shared attention matter, not only centimeters.

2.2 この・その・あの・どの + noun — Demonstrative modifiers

Purpose: use a demonstrative directly before a noun to describe it, rather than replacing it.

When you want to say "this book" or "that pencil" (combining the demonstrative with a noun), use the modifier forms, which end in 〜の:

Standalone (replaces noun)Modifier (before noun)Example
これこの〜この本ほん — this book
それその〜そのペン — that pen
あれあの〜あのかばん — that bag over there
どれどの〜どのノート — which notebook?
  • このえんぴつはだれのですか。Kono enpitsu wa dare no desu ka. — Whose pencil is this?
  • そのノートはわたしのです。Sono nōto wa watashi no desu. — That notebook is mine.

2.3 〜の〜 — The linking particle の

Purpose: connect two nouns to show possession, association, or classification.

The particle の links two nouns. It most often signals that the first noun modifies or belongs to the second noun. Think of it as a flexible connector; "of," "'s," or "—'s" in English can each translate it, depending on context:

  • わたしの本ほんwatashi no hon — my book (lit. "I's book")[わたし] + の + [本]
  • やまださんのペンYamada-san no pen — Yamada-san's pen
  • 日本語にほんごの教科書きょうかしょNihongo no kyōkasho — Japanese-language textbookClassification: a textbook of Japanese
Grammar Note — の can stand alone to replace a noun

In a sentence like そのノートはわたしのです。 (That notebook is mine), the final の after わたし stands in for the noun that was just mentioned — here, ノート. This use of の as a noun-substitute, meaning "the one belonging to me" or "mine," is common and worth recognizing early. Its fuller grammar will be developed in a later chapter.

2.4 〜も — "Also" and "too"

Purpose: express that something applies equally to another item or person, using も in place of は or が.

The particle も replaces は when you want to say that the same statement applies to an additional item. It often corresponds to English "also," "too," or "either" (in negative sentences):

  • これはえんぴつです。それもえんぴつです。This is a pencil. That is also a pencil.も replaces は to signal "also applies"
  • わたしも学生がくせいです。Watashi mo gakusei desu. — I am also a student.
  • それもちがいます。Sore mo chigaimasu. — That is also wrong / different.
Grammar Note — も replaces, does not follow, は

A very common beginner error is to write *わたしはもです, treating も as something added alongside は. In Japanese, も replaces は (or が) entirely. There is no double-particle combination はも. Compare: わたしは学生です。 (I am a student.) versus わたしも学生です。 (I am also a student.)

2.5 〜ではありません — Negative copular

Purpose: say that something is not something else, using the polite negative form of the copula.

In Chapter 1 you learned 〜です to make statements. The polite negative form is 〜ではありません:

  • これはえんぴつではありません。Kore wa enpitsu dewa arimasen. — This is not a pencil.[これ] + は + [えんぴつ] + ではありません
  • わたしは先生せんせいではありません。学生がくせいです。Watashi wa sensei dewa arimasen. Gakusei desu. — I am not a teacher. I am a student.

You may also hear the shortened form じゃありません in ordinary speech. This is a natural, common contraction. For now, practice the full form ではありません, which is always appropriate in polite interaction, and treat じゃありません as recognition-only material.

Language Focus

The こ・そ・あ・ど system. Japanese organizes many pointing and questioning words into a tidy four-way grid built around the stems こ・そ・あ・ど. You have now met two sets within this grid: the standalone demonstratives (これ・それ・あれ・どれ) and the modifier demonstratives (この・その・あの・どの). The same four stems will reappear as you learn words for places (ここ・そこ・あそこ・どこ) and directions (こちら・そちら・あちら・どちら). Recognizing the shared stems will make these new words much easier to learn when you encounter them.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Yamada-sensei's Japanese classroom, first morning. Keiko arrives late and quietly asks her neighbour, Tom, about an object on the desk. Then Yamada-sensei addresses the whole class.

KEIKO すみません、トムさん。これはなんですか。 Excuse me, Tom. What is this?
TOM それは教科書きょうかしょですよ。日本語にほんごの教科書きょうかしょです。 That's the textbook. A Japanese textbook.
KEIKO ありがとうございます。あれはなんですか。 Thank you. What is that over there?
TOM あれはホワイトボードです。 That over there is the whiteboard.

(やまだ先生が話し始めます。— Yamada-sensei begins to speak.)

YAMADA-SENSEI おはようございます。これはなんですか。 Good morning. What is this?
CLASS えんぴつです。 A pencil.
YAMADA-SENSEI そうです。では、それはなんですか。 That's right. Then, what is that?
LAYLA すみません、もういちどおねがいします。 Excuse me, once more please.
YAMADA-SENSEI はい。それはなんですか。 Yes. What is that?
LAYLA ノートです。 A notebook.
YAMADA-SENSEI そうです、よくできました。 That's right, well done.

ないようりかい — Comprehension questions:

  1. What two objects does Keiko ask about? Which demonstrative does she use for each, and why are they different?
  2. What expression does Layla use when she needs the teacher to repeat? Is this an expression from Chapter 1 or a new one?
  3. In Tom's first answer, he uses ですよ rather than plain です. What small difference in nuance might the sentence-final particle よ add? (Hint: think about whether Tom is asserting information Keiko didn't know.)
  4. What does よくできました mean, and how might a teacher use it?
Grammar Note — では in the dialogue

Yamada-sensei uses では to transition to the next question: では、それはなんですか。 This では functions as a discourse connector meaning "well then" or "in that case" — a natural way to move from one topic or activity to the next in class and in everyday conversation. It is different from the では inside the negative form ではありません, though they share the same hiragana sequence. Context makes the distinction clear.

Writing Workshop

In this chapter you will write a short classroom inventory note, using the demonstratives, particles, and negative form you have learned.

  1. Choose five objects. Look around your real or imagined classroom and choose five objects: two near you, one near a classmate, and two across the room. Write their names in Japanese from this chapter's vocabulary.
  2. Write identifying sentences. For each object, write one sentence using the appropriate demonstrative (これ・それ・あれ) and 〜は〜です. Aim for at least one sentence using の to show whose object it is.
  3. Write one negative sentence. Choose one object and write a sentence saying it is not something, using ではありません. For example: これはノートではありません。教科書きょうかしょです。
  4. Write one も sentence. Choose a second object of the same type and use も to add it to your description.
  5. Revise and check. Go through the editing checklist below before submitting your note.

チェックリスト — Editing checklist:

  • I used the correct demonstrative (これ・それ・あれ) based on where each object is.
  • I used の to connect two nouns correctly, without a space between them.
  • I used も in place of は, not alongside it.
  • I wrote ではありません in full, without mixing in じゃ.
  • I checked my hiragana shapes, especially the dakuten marks.
  • I ended each sentence with a Japanese full stop 。.
  • I have not written any kanji that have not been introduced (use hiragana or ask your instructor).

Speaking Practice

コントロールされた練習 — Controlled practice: Object identification

With a partner, take turns pointing at objects in the room (real or in a picture) and asking identification questions. Switch roles after five items each. Try to use all four demonstrative forms at least once.

  1. Ａ：これ／それ／あれはなんですか。

コミュニケーションタスク — Find the owner

Put three or four objects on a shared table without labeling them. Each person secretly notes which object is theirs. Take turns asking and answering until everyone knows who owns what. Use だれのですか and 〜のです or 〜のではありません.

  1. このかばんはだれのですか。
  2. そのえんぴつはわたしのではありません。レイラさんのです。

ひとりでもできる練習 — Solo practice: Self-guided tour

Sitting at your own desk or in any room, record yourself giving a short spoken inventory of five objects. Point at each one and produce a sentence for it. Include at least one も sentence and one ではありません sentence. Play back the recording and check your mora timing.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Asking for help in the classroom

In many Japanese educational settings, students are accustomed to listening attentively and may wait for the appropriate moment before speaking. At the same time, expressions like もういちどおねがいします and ゆっくりおねがいします are recognized, expected, and welcomed in language classrooms — particularly when the instructor knows that learners are at an early stage. Using these expressions is not seen as interrupting; it demonstrates engagement and a desire to understand correctly. If you feel uncertain about timing, a small raised hand alongside the verbal request is a safe and natural combination in a classroom context.

Culture in Context — Mixed script in everyday Japanese

The classroom labels and vocabulary in this chapter already show you something true about ordinary Japanese writing: it routinely mixes hiragana, katakana, and kanji in a single short text. This is not unusual or advanced — it is completely normal. Words like けしゴム (eraser) combine hiragana and katakana within a single word because each part has a distinct writing-system history. One reason Japanese readers process mixed-script text easily is that the different scripts serve different roles: kanji for many content words, hiragana for grammatical elements and some vocabulary, katakana primarily for loanwords and foreign names. As your hiragana becomes fluent and your kanji set grows, this layered system will gradually become natural to you as well.

Regional Variation — Classroom Japanese across Japan

The expressions in this chapter — もういちどおねがいします, わかりました, よくできました — reflect the polite standard used in educational settings across Japan. Regional varieties exist, and different instructors across different schools, cities, and generations may phrase encouragement or correction somewhat differently. The expressions here are broadly understood and broadly appropriate; they will serve you well in any standard Japanese classroom context, including formal language programs in Japan or abroad.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using も alongside は

Error: Writing *わたしはも学生です。, adding も after は rather than replacing it.

Correction: も replaces は entirely: わたしも学生です。 The particle combination はも does not exist.

Common Mistake — Confusing これ and この

Error: Using *これほんはわたしのです。 — placing the standalone demonstrative directly before a noun.

Correction: これ stands alone (replaces the noun); この is placed directly before the noun. Use このほんはわたしのです。 (This book is mine.) When you want to say "this one," use これはわたしのです。

Common Mistake — Forgetting dakuten changes meaning

Error: Writing えんひつ instead of えんぴつ, omitting the handakuten from ぴ.

Correction: Diacritical marks are part of the word. ひ and ぴ represent different sounds and different words. Always include the dakuten or handakuten whenever it belongs, writing it clearly in the upper-right area of the symbol.

Common Mistake — Treating の as meaning only possession

Error: Assuming の always means "'s" and trying to translate 日本語にほんごの教科書きょうかしょ as "Japanese's textbook."

Correction: の expresses connection broadly: possession is one use, but classification and description are equally common. 日本語の教科書 means "a textbook of Japanese" — the の connects "Japanese language" to "textbook" as a type, not as an owner. Notice whether the first noun possesses the second or simply classifies it.

Common Mistake — Reading は as "ha" when it is the topic particle

Error: Pronouncing これはえんぴつです as "kore-HA-enpitsu desu," giving は its standalone hiragana reading rather than its particle reading.

Correction: When は functions as the topic particle, it is always pronounced "wa." This rule — which you learned in Chapter 1 — applies just as consistently when the topic is a demonstrative, a name, or any other noun. これはえんぴつです is pronounced "kore-WA-enpitsu desu."

Answer Key

Practice — これ、それ、あれ or どれ?

1. これ — the book is near the speaker.   2. それ — the dictionary is near the listener.   3. あれ — the object is away from both parties.   4. どれ — asking which of several items.

Practice — Filling in は, の, or も

1. これはわたしのえんぴつです。   2. あれもペンです。   3. この本ほんはやまださんのです。   4. トムさんは学生がくせいです。レイラさんも学生がくせいです。

Dialogue — Comprehension

  1. Keiko asks about (1) an object on the table near Tom — she uses これ because it is near her; Tom responds with それ because from his perspective it is near Keiko. She then asks about (2) the whiteboard across the room, using あれ because it is away from both of them.
  2. Layla uses もういちどおねがいします. It was introduced in Chapter 1's classroom survival expressions and is recycled here.
  3. The sentence-final particle よ adds an assertive nuance — the speaker is conveying information they believe the listener does not yet know. Tom is gently informing Keiko, not simply confirming something she already knew.
  4. よくできました means "well done" or "nicely done." A teacher would use it to praise a student's correct or effective response.

Listening task — Model answers

  • Yamada line 1: object = えんぴつ, demonstrative = これ, object is near Yamada.
  • Keiko line 1: object = えんぴつ, demonstrative = それ, object is near Yamada (from Keiko's perspective).
  • Yamada line 2: object = ホワイトボード, demonstrative = あれ, object is away from both.

Reading — Comprehension

  1. ノート and えんぴつ. The particle と is used to list them (meaning "and").
  2. The book belongs to Yamada-san.
  3. The writer says the following belong to them: このノート (this notebook), このえんぴつ (this pencil).
  4. The writer says そのペンはわたしのではありません — that pen is not mine — using the full negative form ではありません.
  5. ですが signals contrast or a "but" relationship, similar to English "but." (The conjunction が used clause-finally is recognition-only at this stage.)

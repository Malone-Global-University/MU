Unit I — はじめの一歩 (First Steps)
1

はじめましてFirst Encounters

Greetings, names, and your first hiragana · Speak and Write Japanese: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Greet someone and take leave using common polite Japanese expressions.
  2. Ask and answer the question "What is your name?" using お名前は何ですか。
  3. Introduce yourself using 〜です with your name.
  4. Use 〜さん appropriately when referring to someone else by name.
  5. Form and answer simple yes/no questions using the sentence-final particle か.
  6. Use basic courtesy expressions, including please, thank you, and excuse me.
  7. Recognize and write the first group of hiragana, from あ through と.
  8. Maintain accurate mora timing when pronouncing short Japanese words and phrases.

Communication Scenario

You have just arrived for the first day of an intensive Japanese program at a language school in Osaka. Students from many countries are gathering in the school lobby before the first class begins. Your instructor has asked everyone to greet and introduce themselves to at least three new people — in Japanese — before the session starts.

  1. Think about the last time you introduced yourself to a stranger. Was the greeting formal or casual? What did you say first?
  2. You may already recognize the word はじめまして, a fixed greeting used the very first time you meet someone. By the end of this chapter, you will know several ways to greet someone in Japanese, and you will know how to write the first letters of your own hiragana vocabulary.

ひらがな — Writing System

Hiragana

Japanese is normally written using a mix of hiragana, katakana, and kanji. Hiragana is a phonetic script: each symbol represents one mora, a short, evenly timed beat of sound — not a letter the way the Roman alphabet works. This chapter introduces your first ten hiragana symbols, covering the あ row through the た row.

Script Note — Why hiragana, not "the Japanese alphabet"

Hiragana is sometimes loosely called "the Japanese alphabet," but this is not accurate. Each hiragana symbol stands for a whole mora — typically a consonant-plus-vowel combination, or a vowel alone — rather than a single consonant or vowel sound the way English letters do. There is no separate symbol for the consonant "k" alone; instead, か already means "ka" as a complete unit.

Script Note — Stroke order matters

Each hiragana symbol has a conventional stroke order and direction. Following the correct stroke order helps your handwriting look natural and makes the symbols easier to remember and to read when handwritten quickly. As a general habit, strokes typically move from top to bottom and from left to right; curved strokes are written in a single smooth motion rather than built up from short straight lines.

れんしゅう — Practice: Copy each of the ten hiragana introduced in this chapter three times, paying attention to stroke direction and to the proportions of each symbol. Then try writing あ, か, and た from memory without looking at the model.

Pronunciation

Mora timing

Japanese is a mora-timed language: each hiragana symbol (with a few exceptions you will learn later) represents one mora, and each mora normally takes roughly the same amount of time to say. This is different from English, where syllables can stretch or compress depending on stress. Compare the timing of こんにちは — five even beats, ko-n-ni-chi-wa — rather than three stressed English-style syllables.

  • あ — one mora, one even beata short, clipped "ah"
  • ありがとう — five moras, five even beatsa-ri-ga-to-u, not "ah-ri-GA-toh"

Try tapping your finger once for each mora as you say a word slowly. This habit will help you internalize Japanese rhythm from the very beginning.

The five vowels

Japanese has five vowels, and each one keeps a stable, pure quality no matter where it appears in a word:

  • あ /a/ — similar to the "a" in English "father," but shorter
  • い /i/ — similar to the "ee" in English "see," but shorter
  • う /u/ — a vowel with less lip-rounding than English "oo"; the lips stay relatively relaxed
  • え /e/ — similar to the "e" in English "bed"
  • お /o/ — similar to the "o" in English "more," but shorter and without the glide

English vowels often glide or change quality, especially at the end of a word. Japanese vowels do not glide; keep the sound the same from beginning to end of the mora.

Pronunciation Note — Vowel length can change meaning

In Japanese, the difference between a short vowel and a long vowel is not a matter of style — it can change a word's meaning entirely. A long vowel is written and counted as two morae, not one. You will study this contrast formally beginning in Chapter 2, but it is worth listening for it from the start: ここ (here) and こうこう (high school) are built from related but different mora counts.

The か row and た row consonants

The consonants in this chapter's hiragana set are close to their English counterparts:

  • か・き・く・け・こ — pronounced with a "k" similar to English, though slightly less puffed with breath than English "k" at the start of a word
  • た・と — pronounced with a "t" similar to English "t," with the tongue touching just behind the upper front teeth
Pronunciation Note — Question intonation with か

Questions formed with the sentence-final particle か are typically said with a gentle rise in pitch on the final syllable, similar to how English questions often rise at the end. Unlike English, Japanese does not usually need to invert word order to ask a question — the particle か does that work on its own, as you will see in the grammar section below.

れんしゅう — Practice: With a partner, or using a recording, practice tapping out the mora count of はじめまして (6 morae), ありがとうございます (10 morae), and your own name in katakana approximation, paying attention to keeping each beat even.

Grammar Instruction

1.1 わたしは〜です — Identifying yourself

Purpose: state your name (or identity) using a complete, basic sentence pattern.

The pattern わたしは〜です literally places three pieces in order: the topic わたし ("I"), the topic particle は, and the predicate です attached after whatever you are identifying yourself as.

  • わたしはサラです。Watashi wa Sara desu. — I am Sara.[topic] わたし + は + [predicate] サラ + です
  • わたしはユウスフです。Watashi wa Yūsufu desu. — I am Yusuf.
Grammar Note — The particle は is pronounced "wa"

The hiragana は is normally pronounced "ha," but when it functions as the topic particle, it is pronounced "wa." This is one of a small, fixed set of particle spellings in Japanese, not an exception you need to memorize word by word — it applies every time は is used as the topic particle.

Grammar Note — です is not simply English "is"

です is a polite predicate ending used with nouns and certain other word types. It does not behave exactly like the English verb "to be" — for example, it does not change form according to who is speaking, the way English "am / is / are" does. For now, treat です as the polite way to complete a basic identifying sentence; its fuller grammar will be studied across this and later chapters.

1.2 〜さん — Referring politely to someone else

Purpose: refer to or address another person respectfully by name.

〜さん attaches directly after a person's name, family name, or given name, and works for people of any gender:

  • サラさんSara-san — Sara (respectful)
  • スミスさんSumisu-san — Mr./Ms. Smith
Grammar Note — Never attach さん to your own name

A common error among beginners is attaching さん to one's own name, modeled on English titles like "Mr. Smith" used in self-introduction. In Japanese, さん is used only when referring to or addressing someone else, never to oneself. Say わたしはサラです。, not a form that includes さん attached to "Sara."

1.3 〜は何ですか — Asking with か

Purpose: form a basic yes/no or information question by adding the sentence-final particle か.

Japanese questions are typically formed by adding the particle か to the end of a statement — without changing word order, and without an extra question word like English "do" or "is":

  • お名前は何ですか。O-namae wa nan desu ka. — What is your name?[topic] お名前 + は + [predicate] 何 + です + か

The word 何 ("what") appears here in kanji with hiragana support; it will be formally added to your active kanji set in a later chapter. The honorific prefix お on お名前 is a fixed, polite way of referring to someone else's name; treat お名前 as a set phrase for now.

Language Focus

One particle, no inversion. English turns a statement into a question partly by changing word order ("You are Sara." becomes "Are you Sara?"). Japanese keeps the word order exactly the same and simply adds か at the very end. This single habit — statement, then か — will let you ask an enormous range of basic questions throughout this course, long before you learn more complex question structures.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Lobby of a language school, Osaka. First morning. Layla, an Egyptian student, meets Tom, an American student. Then their instructor, Yamada-sensei, welcomes them.

LAYLA はじめまして。わたしはレイラです。 How do you do. I am Layla.
TOM はじめまして。わたしはトムです。どうぞよろしくおねがいします。 How do you do. I am Tom. Pleased to meet you.
LAYLA こちらこそ、どうぞよろしくおねがいします。 Likewise — pleased to meet you too.

(山田先生が来ます。— Yamada-sensei arrives.)

YAMADA-SENSEI おはようございます。わたしは山田です。 Good morning. I am Yamada.
LAYLA おはようございます、山田先生。わたしはレイラです。 Good morning, Yamada-sensei. I am Layla.
YAMADA-SENSEI レイラさん、はじめまして。＿＿さんは？ Pleased to meet you, Layla. And you?
TOM わたしはトムです。どうぞよろしくおねがいします。 I am Tom. Pleased to meet you.
YAMADA-SENSEI トムさん、どうぞよろしく。じゃあ、また教室で。 Tom, pleased to meet you. Well then, see you in the classroom.
LAYLA & TOM はい、また教室で。 Yes, see you in the classroom.

ないようりかい — Comprehension questions:

  1. エジプトの学生はだれですか。 (Who is the student from Egypt?)
  2. トムさんはアメリカ人ですか。 (Is Tom American?)
  3. What title does Layla and Tom use for their instructor, and what does it tell you about the register of their interaction?
  4. Why does Yamada-sensei say レイラさん but Layla never refers to herself with さん?
  5. What does Yamada-sensei say to indicate the conversation is ending?
Grammar Note — A shortened form to notice in the dialogue

Yamada-sensei says どうぞよろしく instead of the full どうぞよろしくおねがいします. Dropping おねがいします is a common, natural shortening among people who already share a polite relationship, such as a teacher speaking to new students. Both the full and shortened forms are appropriate; you may continue using the full form, which is never wrong.

The word 先生 ("sensei") means "teacher" or "instructor" and is commonly used as a respectful title after a name, similar in function to さん but specifically signaling a teaching, medical, or other recognized professional role. Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — はじめまして is for first meetings only

はじめまして is used exactly once with any given person — the very first time you meet them. Using it again with someone you already know would sound odd, much as repeatedly saying "nice to meet you" to a longtime acquaintance would sound strange in English. After the first meeting, greetings such as おはようございます or こんにちは take over for everyday encounters.

The accompanying formula どうぞよろしくおねがいします has no single neat English equivalent. It expresses a general request for good treatment or good working relations going forward, and it is said routinely after introductions in both casual and professional Japanese settings.

Culture in Context — A linguistically diverse world of learners

Japanese is studied and spoken by people of many nationalities, religious backgrounds, and language histories, both within Japan and around the world. The four speakers in today's listening section — from varied backgrounds — reflect the genuine diversity of people who learn, speak, and study Japanese. Learning Japanese is not learning about only one city, one industry, or one set of customs; it is gaining access to a wide and varied linguistic world, including diverse communities across Japan itself.

Regional Variation — Greetings beyond the standard

This course teaches contemporary standard Japanese, the shared variety used in education, national media, and ordinary cross-regional communication throughout Japan. In everyday spoken interaction, however, regional varieties exist with their own greeting expressions, intonation patterns, and vocabulary — for example, greetings in the Kansai region (where Osaka is located) may sound noticeably different from those in Tokyo. こんにちは and おはようございます are both widely understood and used across regional varieties as well as in the standard language, which is part of why they make an excellent starting point. As the course progresses, regional forms will be introduced and clearly labeled for recognition; the productive standard taught throughout remains contemporary standard Japanese.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Attaching さん to your own name

Error: Saying a form like "わたしはレイラさんです" when introducing oneself, modeled on English titles used in self-introduction.

Correction: さん is used only for other people, never for yourself. Say simply わたしはレイラです。

Common Mistake — Mispronouncing the topic particle は

Error: Pronouncing the topic particle は as "ha," matching its pronunciation as an ordinary hiragana syllable elsewhere.

Correction: When は functions as the topic particle, it is always pronounced "wa." This rule applies consistently and is not an exception limited to certain words.

Common Mistake — Flattening mora timing

Error: Pronouncing Japanese words with English-style stress, lengthening one syllable and rushing the others — for example, saying ありがとう as "a-ri-GAH-toh" with uneven timing.

Correction: Japanese morae are normally close to equal in length. Practice tapping out each mora evenly, especially in longer words and set phrases, rather than applying English stress patterns.

Common Mistake — Using はじめまして after you already know someone

Error: Greeting a classmate you already met yesterday with はじめまして.

Correction: はじめまして is reserved strictly for the first time you ever meet someone. For everyone you already know, use おはようございます, こんにちは, or こんばんは depending on the time of day.

Common Mistake — Inserting an extra word for "am/is"

Error: Trying to add a separate word alongside です, modeled on the idea that English "is" needs reinforcing.

Correction: です already completes the sentence on its own. わたしはレイラです。 is already a complete, correct sentence meaning "I am Layla."

  • 4 — Ari — おはようございます — (no closing formula given; addresses the listener with the title 先生)

Bonus: Ari (No. 4) uses the most formal register, signaled by addressing the listener as 先生.

Reading — Comprehension

  1. The instructor's name is Yamada (山田).
  2. Benjamin Smith is a student; his badge is labeled "Student" rather than showing a teaching role.
  3. Each badge shows the person's name and their role (student or instructor).
  4. You would say 山田先生 in a classroom setting — using the professional title 先生 is more natural and respectful than the general title さん when addressing a teacher in an educational context.

Chapter Review — Section A

  1. はじめまして・さようなら・ありがとうございます・どういたしまして・すみません・どうぞよろしくおねがいします
  2. ゼロ（れい）、いち、に、さん、よん（し）、ご
  3. おはようございます means "good morning" and is used in the morning; こんにちは means "hello/good afternoon" and is used from late morning through the afternoon.
  4. どうぞよろしくおねがいします roughly expresses a request for good treatment or good relations going forward. It is said routinely right after introductions.
  5. No. はじめまして is used only the very first time you meet someone; for someone you already met yesterday, an ordinary time-of-day greeting is appropriate instead.

Chapter Review — Section B

  1. あ、い、う、え、お、か、き、く、け、こ
  2. あ、か、た、こ、と
  3. (a) No さん   (b) さん   (c) さん (or 先生, if a teacher)   (d) No さん
  4. *わたしはトムさんです。 → わたしはトムです。   *お名前は何です。 → お名前は何ですか。
  5. The hiragana は is pronounced "wa" specifically when it functions as the topic particle, marking わたし as the topic of the sentence; this is a fixed pronunciation rule for the particle use of は.

Chapter Review — Section C

  1. Yamada-sensei is a teacher of Japanese, indicated by the title 先生 and the role description on the badge.
  2. The shortened form is a natural, common simplification used among people who already share a polite relationship; both the full and shortened forms are correct.
  3. おはようございます ("good morning") would be inappropriate in the evening; こんばんは should replace it.

家と近所Home and Neighborhood

Describing homes, rooms, and neighborhoods, and saying exactly where things and people are · Speak and Write Japanese: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Name common rooms in a home and basic features of a neighborhood.
  2. Say what exists and where, using あります and います with location particles.
  3. Point to and identify places using ここ／そこ／あそこ and describe nearby items using この／その／あの.
  4. Describe the position of one thing relative to another using position nouns such as 上, 下, and 中 together with の.
  5. Say where an activity takes place using activity で.
  6. Read and write ten new katakana symbols (タ through ホ rows) and the katakana long-vowel mark ー.
  7. Recognize and read the nine Chapter 6 kanji in familiar words, with furigana support.
  8. Read a simple neighborhood map and write a short description of your own home.

Communication Scenario

Tom has just moved into a small apartment near the community center where he attends the exchange program. His neighbor, Mrs. Tanaka, stops by to welcome him and ask about his new home. Later, a new friend asks Tom for directions to a convenience store near his building. Tom needs to describe his apartment, say what is in each room, and explain what is located around his neighborhood.

  1. This chapter teaches you to describe where things are — inside your home and around your neighborhood — using the existence patterns you saw briefly in Chapter 4.
  2. By the end of the chapter, you will be able to describe your own home and give a simple description of your neighborhood to someone who has never been there.

Vocabulary Note — Position words are nouns

Words such as 上, 下, and 中 behave as nouns in Japanese, not as prepositions. This is why they connect to another noun with の, as in つくえの上 (on top of the desk) — literally, "the desk's top." You will practice this pattern in the Grammar section below.

Script Note — The katakana long-vowel mark ー

Katakana words lengthen a vowel with a straight mark, ー, rather than by repeating a vowel symbol as hiragana sometimes does. Compare スーパー (sūpā, supermarket) with アパート (apāto, apartment) — both contain a long vowel mora shown by ー. This mark always represents one full mora of length, so スーパー has four morae: す・ー・ぱ・ー.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Loanword rhythm and long vowels

Loanwords such as アパート, コンビニ, and スーパー are ordinary Japanese words with Japanese mora timing — they are not simply English words spoken faster. Say each word aloud, tapping once per mora:

  • アパート — 4 morae: あ・パ・ー・と
  • コンビニ — 4 morae: こ・ん・び・に
  • スーパー — 4 morae: す・ー・ぱ・ー

Notice that the moraic ん in コンビニ takes one full mora of time, just as it does in native Japanese words.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

6.1 ここ／そこ／あそこ — Place demonstratives

Purpose: identify and point to a specific location.

  • ここはわたしのへやです。Koko wa watashi no heya desu. — Here is my room.
  • そこにコンビニがあります。Soko ni konbini ga arimasu. — There is a convenience store there.
  • あそこはこうえんです。Asoko wa kōen desu. — Over there is a park.
Grammar Note — ここ／そこ／あそこ versus これ／それ／あれ

ここ／そこ／あそこ identify places, while これ／それ／あれ (Chapter 2) identify things. Both series follow the same near-speaker, near-listener, far-from-both pattern, and both depend on shared attention and context rather than on fixed physical distance alone.

6.2 この／その／あの — Modifying a following noun

Purpose: point out a specific item before naming it.

  • このへやはひろいです。Kono heya wa hiroi desu. — This room is spacious.
  • そのアパートはあたらしいです。Sono apāto wa atarashii desu. — That apartment is new.
  • あのやまはたかいです。Ano yama wa takai desu. — That mountain over there is tall.

この／その／あの must always be followed directly by a noun; unlike これ／それ／あれ, they cannot stand alone.

6.3 [場所]に[人／もの]が います／あります — Existence with location

Purpose: state that a person, animal, or thing exists at a specific place.

  • へやにつくえがあります。Heya ni tsukue ga arimasu. — There is a desk in the room.[place]に[thing]が あります
  • だいどころにははがいます。Daidokoro ni haha ga imasu. — My mother is in the kitchen.[place]に[person]が います
  • こうえんにいぬがいます。Kōen ni inu ga imasu. — There is a dog in the park.
Grammar Note — Reversing the pattern

The same information can also be expressed by starting with the person or thing and marking it as the topic: つくえはへやにあります。 ("As for the desk, it is in the room.") Both patterns are correct; the choice depends on what the speaker wants to introduce first — the place or the item.

6.4 [ものA]の[位置ことば] — Describing position with の

Purpose: describe exactly where something is relative to another object.

  • つくえの上にほんがあります。Tsukue no ue ni hon ga arimasu. — There is a book on top of the desk.desk の on-top-of に book が あります
  • いすの下にねこがいます。Isu no shita ni neko ga imasu. — There is a cat under the chair.
  • いえのとなりにコンビニがあります。Ie no tonari ni konbini ga arimasu. — There is a convenience store next to the house.
  • がっこうの前にこうえんがあります。Gakkō no mae ni kōen ga arimasu. — There is a park in front of the school.

Because position words such as 上 and 中 are nouns, they connect to the reference object with の, exactly as two ordinary nouns connect in にほんごのほん from Chapter 5.

6.5 活動の で — Marking where an activity happens

Purpose: state the location where an action takes place, as distinct from where something simply exists.

  • だいどころでたべます。Daidokoro de tabemasu. — I eat in the kitchen.activity で, not に
  • へやでべんきょうします。Heya de benkyō shimasu. — I study in my room.
Common Mistake — Confusing location に and activity で

Error: *だいどころにたべます。

Correction: だいどころでたべます。 — Use に only with います／あります to state that something exists somewhere. Use で with action verbs to state where an activity happens.

Language Focus

From existence to precise position. Chapter 4 introduced います and あります for simple existence. This chapter adds three tools that make your descriptions precise: place demonstratives for pointing, position nouns with の for exact location, and activity で for distinguishing "being somewhere" from "doing something somewhere."

Language Building Block — Describing a room
ここは〜です。〜があります／います。〜の〜に〜があります。
Identify the place → state what exists → give a precise position
ここはわたしのへやです。つくえといすがあります。つくえの上に本があります。ベッドはまどのとなりにあります。
Here is my room. There is a desk and a chair. There is a book on top of the desk. The bed is next to the window.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Mrs. Tanaka, Tom's new neighbor, visits Tom's apartment for the first time.

TANAKA こんにちは。ここがトムさんのアパートですか。 Hello. Is this your apartment, Tom?
TOM はい、そうです。どうぞ。 Yes, that's right. Please, come in.
TANAKA ひろいへやですね。あそこにベッドがありますね。 It's a spacious room, isn't it. There's a bed over there.
TOM はい。つくえの上にコンピューターがあります。あそこでべんきょうします。 Yes. There's a computer on top of the desk. I study over there.
TANAKA だいどころはどこですか。 Where is the kitchen?
TOM そこです。トイレはだいどころのとなりにあります。 It's right there. The bathroom is next to the kitchen.
TANAKA きんじょにコンビニがありますか。 Is there a convenience store in the neighborhood?
TOM はい、あります。アパートの前にコンビニがあります。こうえんもあります。 Yes, there is. There's a convenience store in front of the apartment. There's also a park.
TANAKA いいところですね。よろしくおねがいします。 It's a nice area, isn't it. I look forward to knowing you as a neighbor.

ないようりかい — Comprehension questions:

  1. What is on top of Tom's desk, and what does he do there?
  2. Where is the bathroom located, according to Tom?
  3. What two places does Tom mention are near his apartment?
  4. Identify each use of に and で in this dialogue and explain the difference.

Writing Workshop

In this workshop you will write a description of your own home and neighborhood.

  1. Sketch your home or room. Label the rooms or main items in English first, so you know what vocabulary you will need.
  2. Write four to six sentences describing your home. Include at least two items with あります or います, and at least two position sentences using の and a position word.
  3. Write two to three sentences describing your neighborhood. Mention at least one place using この／その／あの or ここ／そこ／あそこ.
  4. Add one sentence about an activity you do somewhere in your home, using activity で.
  5. Revise and check using the checklist below before finalizing your work.

チェックリスト — Editing checklist:

  • I chose correctly between います and あります.
  • I used の correctly to connect a position word to its reference noun.
  • I used に for existence and で for activities, without mixing them up.
  • I used この／その／あの only directly before a noun.
  • I wrote new katakana words accurately, including the long-vowel mark ー where needed.
  • I used the nine new kanji correctly, with furigana.
  • I ended each sentence with 。.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Housing and everyday space

Homes in Japan vary widely by region, household size, and city, ranging from compact urban apartments to larger houses in suburban and rural areas. Many apartments in dense city neighborhoods make efficient use of limited space, but this is a practical pattern common in many large cities worldwide, not a universal or defining feature of Japanese life.

Culture in Context — Greeting new neighbors

In many communities in Japan, it is common for new residents or their neighbors to exchange a short greeting and small gift when someone moves in, though this custom is far from universal and varies by region, building type, and generation. The dialogue in this chapter reflects one common, friendly version of this kind of first neighborly exchange.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Omitting の before a position word

Error: *つくえ上に本があります。

Correction: つくえの上に本があります。 — Because position words are nouns, they must be connected to the reference noun with の.

Common Mistake — Using に with an activity verb

Error: *へやにべんきょうします。

Correction: へやでべんきょうします。 — Activity verbs take で for location, not に.

Common Mistake — Using この／その／あの without a following noun

Error: *このはわたしのへやです。

Correction: これはわたしのへやです。 — Use これ when standing alone; reserve この for directly modifying a following noun, such as このへや.

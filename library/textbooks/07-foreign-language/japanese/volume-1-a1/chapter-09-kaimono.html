買い物Shopping

Describing products, comparing prices, and shopping with confidence · Speak and Write Japanese: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Name common shopping vocabulary, including colors, sizes, and product types.
  2. Describe things using い-adjectives and な-adjectives in the nonpast and past, affirmative and negative.
  3. Compare two items using より and ほう.
  4. Identify the most extreme item in a group using いちばん.
  5. Ask for and state prices using 円 and the kanji numerals 百・千・万.
  6. Count objects using the counters つ, 個, and 枚, and choose the correct counter for common items.
  7. Make simple purchase requests and ask about price, size, and color.
  8. Recognize and read the eleven Chapter 9 kanji in familiar words, with furigana support.

Communication Scenario

Tom needs a new bag for school and a small gift for Priya's birthday. Mrs. Tanaka takes him to a shop near the station where they look at bags, notebooks, and other items together, comparing colors, sizes, and prices before Tom decides what to buy.

  1. This chapter builds on the ordering language and the つ counter from Chapter 8, adding full adjective forms, comparison, and two new counters for shopping.
  2. By the end of the chapter, you will be able to describe products, compare two options, ask the price, and complete a simple purchase.

Vocabulary Note — Two kinds of adjectives

Words such as 大きい and 高い end in い and are called い-adjectives. Words such as きれいです and べんりです are な-adjectives; their dictionary form does not end in a predictable い, and the です shown here is part of their polite predicate form, not part of the word itself. You already used 好き and きらい as formulaic な-adjectives in Chapter 8; this chapter teaches the full conjugation pattern that both types follow.

Script Note — Adjective kanji and okurigana

Each descriptive kanji in this chapter keeps a fixed kanji base with a changing hiragana ending, exactly as 食べる and 飲む did in Chapter 8: 大きい → 大きくない → 大きかった, 高い → 高くない → 高かった. The kanji itself never changes; only the okurigana ending changes to show negative and past forms. You will practice this pattern fully in the grammar section below.

Script Note — Reading large numbers

百, 千, and 万 combine with the numbers 一 through 五 from Chapter 8 to build prices, but some combinations change their sound. 三百 is read さんびゃく, not さんひゃく, and 三千 is read さんぜん. This chapter presents only the specific price amounts used in its examples and exercises; the complete pattern of sound changes across all numbers is not required at this stage.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

9.1 い-adjective forms

Purpose: describe things in the nonpast and past, affirmative and negative.

  • このかばんは大きいです。Kono kaban wa ōkii desu. — This bag is big.nonpast affirmative: 大きい + です
  • このかばんは大きくないです。Kono kaban wa ōkikunai desu. — This bag is not big.nonpast negative: 大き + くない + です
  • このかばんは大きかったです。Kono kaban wa ōkikatta desu. — This bag was big.past affirmative: 大き + かった + です
  • このかばんは大きくなかったです。Kono kaban wa ōkikunakatta desu. — This bag was not big.past negative: 大き + くなかった + です
Grammar Note — Building い-adjective forms

Drop the final い from the dictionary form to find the stem: 大き. Add い for the plain nonpast, くない for the plain negative, かった for the plain past, and くなかった for the plain past negative. です is added after any of these forms to make the sentence polite; it does not change the adjective's own conjugation. The adjective いい ("good") is irregular: its negative and past forms are built from よく, not いく — よくない, よかった.

9.2 な-adjective forms

Purpose: describe things using な-adjectives in the nonpast and past, affirmative and negative.

  • このみせはべんりです。Kono mise wa benri desu. — This shop is convenient.nonpast affirmative: べんり + です
  • このみせはべんりじゃないです。Kono mise wa benri janai desu. — This shop is not convenient.nonpast negative: べんり + じゃないです
  • このみせはべんりでした。Kono mise wa benri deshita. — This shop was convenient.past affirmative: べんり + でした
  • このみせはべんりじゃなかったです。Kono mise wa benri janakatta desu. — This shop was not convenient.past negative: べんり + じゃなかったです

な-adjectives conjugate like です predicates: the descriptive word itself stays fixed, and only the です part changes for negative and past. This is different from い-adjectives, whose own ending changes. When a な-adjective directly modifies a noun, it takes な, as in きれいなかばん ("a pretty bag"); this noun-modifying use is presented here for recognition and will be practiced more fully in later chapters.

Common Mistake — Mixing い-adjective and な-adjective negatives

Error: *大きいじゃないです。

Correction: 大きくないです。 — い-adjectives never take じゃない. Only な-adjectives and nouns use the じゃない pattern you learned with です/じゃないです in earlier chapters.

9.3 より — Comparing two items

Purpose: say that one thing has more of a quality than another.

  • このかばんはあのかばんより高いです。Kono kaban wa ano kaban yori takai desu. — This bag is more expensive than that bag.Aは Bより + adjective
  • くろいくつはしろいくつより安いです。Kuroi kutsu wa shiroi kutsu yori yasui desu. — The black shoes are cheaper than the white shoes.

9.4 ほう — Stating a preference between two items

Purpose: say which of two items you prefer or which has more of a quality, using ほう ("side; direction").

  • あかいかばんのほうが好きです。Akai kaban no hō ga suki desu. — I like the red bag better.Nのほうが好きです (compared with the other option in context)
  • 新しいほうが高いです。Atarashii hō ga takai desu. — The new one is more expensive.adjective + ほうが + adjective
Grammar Note — より versus ほう

より attaches to the item being compared against, and the adjective describes the other item: Aは Bより 高いです ("A is more expensive than B"). ほう attaches to the item being highlighted, often when the other item is already clear from context, and always takes が before the adjective: Aのほうが 高いです ("A is the more expensive one"). The two patterns describe the same comparison from different starting points and are often combined: Aのほうが Bより 高いです.

9.5 いちばん — Saying "the most"

Purpose: identify the item with the most of a quality within a group of three or more.

  • このみせでこのかばんがいちばん安いです。Kono mise de kono kaban ga ichiban yasui desu. — In this shop, this bag is the cheapest.場所で Nが いちばん + adjective
  • どれがいちばん大きいですか。Dore ga ichiban ōkii desu ka. — Which one is the biggest?

いちばん ("number one") comes directly before the adjective and does not require の or ほう.

9.6 個 and 枚 — Two more counters

Purpose: count objects using counters suited to their shape, in addition to the general counter つ from Chapter 8.

  • ノートを二個ください。Nōto o niko kudasai. — Two notebooks, please.N を + [number]個 + ください
  • ペンを三枚ください。Pen o sanmai kudasai. — Wait, pens use a different counter — see the note below.
Grammar Note — Choosing a counter

個 counts small, roughly round or compact objects such as apples, boxes, or small bags. 枚 counts flat, thin objects such as paper, tickets, or shirts, but not most pens, which are long, thin objects using a different counter not introduced in this chapter. This chapter limits productive practice to 個 and 枚 for clearly countable, round or flat shopping items, alongside the general counter つ, which remains usable for almost any small countable item when the specific counter is unfamiliar. The complete counter system continues to develop in later volumes.

Language Focus

Model Dialogue

Setting: Tom and Mrs. Tanaka look at bags in a shop near the station.

TOM たなかさん、このかばんはどうですか。 Mrs. Tanaka, how about this bag?
TANAKA きれいですね。でも、ちょっと高いですよ。 It's pretty. But it's a little expensive.
TOM すみません、これはいくらですか。 Excuse me, how much is this?
STAFF それは五千円です。あのくろいかばんは三千円です。 That one is 5,000 yen. That black bag is 3,000 yen.
TOM くろいかばんのほうが安いですね。あかいかばんより小さいですか。 The black bag is cheaper, isn't it. Is it smaller than the red bag?
STAFF いいえ、おなじおおきさです。くろいかばんはあかいかばんよりじょうぶですよ。 No, it's the same size. The black bag is sturdier than the red bag.
TOM じゃあ、くろいかばんにします。それから、プレゼントにノートを二個ください。 Then I'll take the black bag. And also, two notebooks for a present, please.
STAFF かしこまりました。ぜんぶで三千五百円です。 Understood. That's 3,500 yen in total.

ないようりかい — Comprehension questions:

  1. How much is the red bag, and how much is the black bag?
  2. According to the staff member, how do the two bags compare in size and sturdiness?
  3. What does Tom decide to buy, and how many notebooks does he order?
  4. Identify each use of より and ほう in this dialogue and explain what each is comparing.

Writing Workshop

In this workshop you will write a short shopping note comparing two items and describing a purchase.

  1. Choose two items to compare in English first. Note at least one difference in price, size, or quality for each.
  2. Write two to three sentences describing each item. Use at least one い-adjective and one な-adjective.
  3. Write one comparison sentence using より and one preference sentence using ほう.
  4. Write a short purchase note stating what you bought, with a quantity and the correct counter, and the total price in yen.
  5. Revise and check using the checklist below before finalizing your work.

チェックリスト — Editing checklist:

  • I built い-adjective negative and past forms from the stem correctly.
  • I built な-adjective negative and past forms using じゃない and でした correctly.
  • I used より with the item being compared against, and ほう with が for the highlighted item.
  • I used いちばん correctly for the most extreme item in a group of three or more.
  • I chose an appropriate counter (つ, 個, or 枚) for each item.
  • I wrote prices accurately using 円, 百, 千, and 万.
  • I used the eleven new kanji correctly, with furigana.
  • I ended each sentence with 。.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Receipts and paying

Many shops in Japan provide a printed receipt, レシート, automatically after a purchase, and cashiers commonly place change on a small tray rather than directly into a customer's hand. Payment methods vary by shop, region, and generation: cash remains widely used in many smaller shops, while cashless payment methods are increasingly common in larger stores. It is useful to be able to ask いくらですか and understand a spoken price clearly before paying.

Culture in Context — Comparing prices and value

Shoppers in many parts of Japan commonly compare price, durability, and appearance before deciding on a purchase, much as this chapter's dialogue does. Attitudes toward spending more for durability versus choosing the cheaper option vary by individual, household, and item, so avoid assuming that any one shopping style is typical of all shoppers.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using じゃない with an い-adjective

Error: *安いじゃないです。

Correction: 安くないです。 — い-adjectives form their negative by changing い to くない, never with じゃない.

Common Mistake — Forgetting が before ほう

Error: *あかいかばんのほう好きです。

Correction: あかいかばんのほうが好きです。 — ほう always takes が before the following adjective or predicate.

Common Mistake — Using いちばん with only two items

Error: Using いちばん to compare only two bags.

Correction: With exactly two items, use より or ほう: このかばんのほうが安いです。 Reserve いちばん for groups of three or more.

11

町でGetting Around

Directions, transport, and movement, with the first introduction of the て-form · Speak and Write Japanese: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify and name common transportation and route vocabulary, including stations, trains, streets, and directional words.
  2. Describe movement to and from a place using 行きます, 来ます, and 帰ります with the particles に and へ.
  3. State the means of transportation used with the particle で.
  4. Describe a route's starting point and destination with から and まで.
  5. Form the plain て-form of familiar verbs for the first time.
  6. Make a polite request using 〜てください.
  7. Read and interpret simple station signs, maps, and directional notices.
  8. Recognize, read, and begin to handwrite the fourteen new Unit III kanji for movement, transportation, and direction.
  9. Ask for and give simple directions in a short guided interaction.

Communication Scenario

Tom has an appointment across town. He needs to find the train station near his home, ask a station employee for help, and follow a friend's spoken directions to a new coffee shop. This chapter follows Tom as he moves around his neighborhood and learns the language of asking for and giving directions.

  1. This chapter opens Unit III. It introduces new vocabulary for transportation and movement, and it introduces the て-form for the first time, used here to make polite requests with 〜てください.
  2. By the end of the chapter, you will be able to ask how to get somewhere, understand simple directions, state how you travel and where you are going, and write a short set of directions to a familiar place.

Script Note — 入り口 and 出口

Notice that 入 and 出 combine with 口 to form 入り口 (entrance) and 出口 (exit). These are extremely common words on Japanese station signs, building directories, and maps. The okurigana り appears in 入り口 but not in 出口; learn each written form as a complete word rather than assembling it from a rule.

れんしゅう — Practice: Copy each new kanji five times, saying its reading aloud each time. Then write the two words 入り口 and 出口 from memory, checking your okurigana against the example above.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Long vowels in transportation words

Several new words in this chapter contain a long vowel that changes meaning if shortened. Say each pair aloud, holding the long vowel for a full extra mora:

  • とおい (とお + い, far) — hold the long お
  • とうきょう — Tokyo, with two long vowels in sequence

Also practice the moraic ん in でんしゃ (train) and じてんしゃ (bicycle): keep it as a single, clearly timed mora rather than blending it into the following consonant.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

1. Movement verbs with に and へ

Purpose: to state a destination.

行きます (go), 来ます (come), and 帰ります (return) take a destination marked by に or へ. Both particles are acceptable for a destination in this pattern; へ emphasizes direction slightly more than に, but the difference rarely changes meaning at this level.

  • 駅に行きます。I am going to the station.駅[destination]+に / 行きます[go]
  • うちへ帰ります。I am going home.うち[home]+へ / 帰ります[return]

2. Means of transportation with で

Purpose: to state how one travels.

The particle で, already familiar as the particle for the location of an activity, also marks the means of transportation.

  • 電車で行きます。I go by train.電車[train]+で[means] / 行きます
  • 自転車で来ました。I came by bicycle.自転車+で / 来ました[came]
Common Mistake — Walking is not marked with で

Error: *歩きでうちへ帰ります。

Correction: 歩いてうちへ帰ります。 — Walking uses the て-form of 歩きます, not the particle で, because walking is treated as a manner of moving rather than a vehicle.

3. から and まで for routes

Purpose: to state a starting point and an endpoint.

You have used から for origin since Chapter 3. Paired with まで, it now marks the start and end of a route, a distance, or a duration.

  • 駅から学校まで歩きます。I walk from the station to school.駅+から[from] / 学校+まで[to] / 歩きます
  • うちから駅まで十分ぐらいです。It's about ten minutes from home to the station.
Building Block — The て-form, first introduction
Group 1 (う-verbs): stem changes according to the final sound    Group 2 (る-verbs): drop る, add て    Irregular: する→して, 来る→来て
The て-form is a connecting form of the verb. In this chapter it appears only in the request pattern 〜てください. Its other uses will be developed gradually starting in Chapter 13.
まがります → まがって    行きます → 行って    見ます → 見て
turn → turning/turn (and)    go → going/go (and)    see/look → seeing/look (and)

4. Polite requests with 〜てください

Purpose: to politely ask someone to do something.

Attach ください to the て-form of a verb to make a polite request. This is the first productive use of the て-form in the course; a fuller range of て-form meanings and connections will be introduced across Chapters 12 and 13.

  • まっすぐ行ってください。Please go straight ahead.行って[て-form of 行きます] + ください
  • 右にまがってください。Please turn right.まがって[て-form] + ください
  • ここでおりてください。Please get off here.
Language Note — Only the request pattern is active

This chapter authorizes the て-form only for building 〜てください requests. Do not use the て-form productively to connect two clauses ("I do X and then Y") yet; that use begins in Chapter 13, once more verb groups and contexts have been practiced.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Tom stops a station employee to ask how to get to the north exit.

TOM すみません、北口はどこですか。 Excuse me, where is the north exit?
EMPLOYEE まっすぐ行ってください。それから、右にまがってください。 Please go straight ahead. After that, please turn right.
TOM わかりました。ありがとうございます。バスていはどこですか。 I understand. Thank you. Where is the bus stop?
EMPLOYEE 北口を出て、道をわたってください。バスていは道の前にあります。 Exit the north gate and cross the street. The bus stop is in front of the road.
TOM わかりました。どうもありがとうございます。 I understand. Thank you very much.

ないようりかい — Comprehension questions:

  1. What two directions does the employee give first?
  2. Where does the employee say the bus stop is located?
  3. Find one example of the て-form used to make a request.

Listening Practice

Writing Workshop

In this workshop you will write a short set of directions to a place you know well.

  1. Choose a destination. Pick a place a friend might want to visit, such as your home, a shop, or a park.
  2. List the steps in English first. Note the starting point, at least three directional steps, and the destination.
  3. Write an opening sentence stating the starting point and destination with から and まで.
  4. Write three to four direction sentences using 〜てください, まっすぐ, まがります, and わたります as needed.
  5. Add a sentence about transportation or distance, using で for means or a time expression for duration.
  6. Revise and check using the checklist below before finalizing your work.

チェックリスト — Editing checklist:

  • I stated the starting point and destination with から and まで.
  • I used at least two 〜てください requests correctly formed from the て-form.
  • I used a direction word (まっすぐ, 右, 左, 東, 西, 南, or 北) accurately.
  • I stated a means of transportation with で, or noted that the route is on foot.
  • I used the new Unit III kanji correctly, with furigana.
  • I ended each sentence with 。 and ordered my directions logically.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Asking strangers for directions

Beginning a request to a stranger with すみません ("excuse me") is a common and polite way to open an interaction in many everyday Japanese contexts, whether speaking to a station employee, a shopkeeper, or a passerby. This softens the interruption and signals a polite request is coming, regardless of the specific words that follow.

Regional Note — Station layouts vary

Large stations in major cities often have multiple named exits (such as north, south, east, and west exits) with different surrounding businesses and landmarks, while smaller stations may have only one or two. When giving or asking for directions in an unfamiliar area, confirming the exit name is a practical habit, not a sign of confusion.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Confusing に/へ destination with で means

Error: *駅で行きます。 (intending "I go to the station")

Correction: 駅に行きます。 — Use に or へ for the destination itself; reserve で for the means of transportation or the location where an activity happens.

Common Mistake — Forgetting てください after the て-form

Error: *右にまがって。 (used where a full polite request is expected)

Correction: 右にまがってください。 — At this stage, always complete the pattern with ください when making a polite request; the bare て-form alone will be developed later as a separate, more casual use.

Common Mistake — Mixing up 入り口 and 出口 okurigana

Error: *出り口

Correction: 出口 — Only 入り口 takes the okurigana り; 出口 does not. Learn each word's spelling individually.

Practical Performance Task

パフォーマンスタスク

道あんない — Giving directions

You will produce a connected spoken and written set of directions to a place you know well, and respond to a partner's directions in a short interaction.

  • Written: Write at least eight sentences of directions from a stated starting point to a stated destination, including a means of transportation or a statement that the route is on foot, and at least three 〜てください requests.
  • Spoken: Give the same directions aloud from memory, without reading directly from your written text.
  • Interaction: Listen to a partner's or instructor's directions to an unfamiliar place and ask at least two clarifying questions.
  • Script check: Hand-write at least eight of the fourteen new kanji from memory, and read aloud a short station sign or note furnished by your instructor.

Assessment focus: accurate destination and means particles; correct から〜まで route phrasing; correctly formed て-form requests with ください; accurate direction vocabulary; accurate Chapter 11 kanji with furigana; and a clearly ordered, followable set of directions.

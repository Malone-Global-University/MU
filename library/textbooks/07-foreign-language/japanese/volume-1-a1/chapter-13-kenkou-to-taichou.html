健康と体調Health and Well-Being

The body, symptoms, and requests for help, with 〜てもいい、〜てはいけない、and 〜たい · Speak and Write Japanese: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify and use vocabulary for body parts, common symptoms, and clinic or pharmacy language.
  2. Ask for and grant permission using 〜てもいいですか and 〜てもいいです.
  3. State a prohibition using 〜てはいけません.
  4. Express a desire using 〜たいです.
  5. Describe a symptom using a body part as the subject, such as 頭が痛いです.
  6. Give and understand simple health advice.
  7. Read and interpret simple pharmacy labels and clinic signs.
  8. Recognize, read, and begin to handwrite ten new Unit III kanji for the body and health.
  9. Hold a short guided conversation describing symptoms and asking for help at a clinic or pharmacy.

Communication Scenario

On Monday morning, Tom wakes up with a headache and a fever. He messages Yuki to say he cannot come to class, then visits a nearby clinic. At the clinic, he describes his symptoms to the receptionist and the doctor, asks whether he may go home to rest, and stops at the pharmacy afterward to pick up medicine. This chapter follows Tom as he describes how he feels, asks for permission, receives advice, and requests help.

  1. This chapter continues Unit III. It introduces the permission pattern 〜てもいいです(か), the prohibition pattern 〜てはいけません, the desire pattern 〜たいです, and body-part subject patterns for describing symptoms.
  2. By the end of the chapter, you will be able to describe how you feel, ask permission to do or not do something, understand simple prohibitions, and request help at a clinic or pharmacy.

Script Note — 病院 as a fixed compound

The kanji 病 and 院 combine to form 病院 (hospital or clinic), read びょういん. Neither kanji is commonly used alone with this meaning at this level, so learn 病院 as one lexical unit rather than as two separate pieces of meaning added together. Note also that 病院 and 美容院 (beauty salon) share the same second kanji but refer to very different places.

れんしゅう — Practice: Copy each new kanji five times, saying its reading aloud each time. Then write the words 病院, 薬, and 体 from memory, checking your okurigana against the vocabulary list above.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Long vowels in 病院 and よう sequences

The word 病院 (びょういん) contains the contracted sound びょ followed by a long う vowel. Hold びょう for its full two morae rather than shortening it, since びょういん with a short vowel would be difficult to understand. Practice the contrast between a short and long vowel in this position by tapping out the morae: び・ょ・う・い・ん (5 morae).

  • 病院 びょういん — hospital; clinic (5 morae)
  • 病 びょう — illness (2 morae, as part of a compound)

Also review the falling intonation of 〜てもいいですか, which typically rises gently at the very end as a genuine yes-or-no question, similar to other polite requests for permission you have practiced.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

1. Body-part subject patterns with い-adjectives

Purpose: to describe a symptom by naming the body part that hurts or feels unwell.

To describe a symptom, name the body part with the particle が, followed by an い-adjective such as 痛い (painful). The body part functions as the grammatical subject of the sentence, while the whole sentence describes the speaker's overall condition.

  • 頭が痛いです。My head hurts. (I have a headache.)頭[body part]+が+痛い[adj.]+です
  • おなかが痛いです。My stomach hurts.
  • のどが痛いです。My throat hurts.
Language Note — Why が and not は

In these sentences, the body part is new or focused information within an already-understood topic (the speaker), so が marks it rather than は. Compare 頭が痛いです to a sentence with an established topic, such as 私は元気です, where は marks the speaker as topic. Both patterns are correct; they answer slightly different questions.

2. Asking and granting permission with 〜てもいいですか

Purpose: to ask whether an action is allowed, and to grant that permission.

Attach もいいですか to the て-form of a verb to ask politely whether you may do something. Answer with 〜てもいいです to grant permission, or with a softened refusal to withhold it.

  • 家に帰ってもいいですか。May I go home?帰って[て-form]+もいいですか
  • はい、帰ってもいいですよ。Yes, you may go home.
  • 水を飲んでもいいですか。May I drink some water?

3. Prohibiting an action with 〜てはいけません

Purpose: to state that an action is not allowed.

Attach はいけません to the て-form of a verb to state a prohibition. This form is direct, so it is most often used by a doctor, an instructor, a sign, or another figure with the authority to set a rule, rather than between people of equal standing without cause.

Building Block — Permission and prohibition
Permission: 〜て + もいいです(か)    Prohibition: 〜て + はいけません
Both patterns build directly on the て-form you began using in Chapter 11.
お風呂に入ってもいいです    お風呂に入ってはいけません
You may take a bath    You must not take a bath
  • 今日は学校に行ってはいけません。You must not go to school today.行って[て-form]+はいけません
  • お酒を飲んではいけません。You must not drink alcohol.
Common Mistake — Using 〜てはいけません too broadly

Error: Using 〜てはいけません for every negative preference, such as telling a friend you personally dislike an activity.

Correction: Reserve 〜てはいけません for genuine rules, medical instructions, or authority-based prohibitions. For a personal preference, use language you already know, such as あまり好きじゃありません (I don't like it very much), instead.

4. Expressing desire with 〜たいです

Purpose: to say what you want to do.

Replace ます with たいです to express a wish to do something. たい conjugates like an い-adjective, so it has its own past, negative, and past-negative forms.

  • 早く寝たいです。I want to sleep early.寝[stem]+たいです
  • 薬を飲みたくないです。I don't want to take the medicine.飲み[stem]+たくないです[neg.]
  • 今日は休みたかったです。I wanted to rest today.休み[stem]+たかったです[past]
Common Mistake — Using たい about someone else without care

Error: *先生は寝たいです。 (intending "The teacher wants to sleep.")

Correction: たい most naturally expresses the speaker's own wish. To describe someone else's apparent wish, more cautious or indirect language is needed, which you will study in a later volume. For now, use たい mainly for your own desires, or ask about another person's wish with a question such as 寝たいですか。

5. Giving simple advice

Purpose: to suggest what someone should do when they are unwell.

Combine the patterns from this chapter and earlier chapters to give simple advice: a request with 〜てください, a permission or prohibition statement, or a suggestion using vocabulary you already know.

  • 薬を飲んでください。そして、休んでください。Please take the medicine. And please rest.
  • 今日は学校を休んでもいいですよ。It's fine to miss school today.

Grammar Practice

  1. Describe a symptom: (head) → ______が______です。
  2. Ask permission to go home: 家に______もいいですか。
  3. State a prohibition about drinking alcohol using 〜てはいけません.
  4. Say you want to sleep early using 〜たいです.
  5. Say you do not want to take medicine, using the negative form of たい.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Tom visits a clinic on Monday morning and speaks with the receptionist and the doctor.

受付 どうしましたか。 What's wrong? (What brings you in today?)
TOM 頭が痛いです。それから、熱もあります。 My head hurts. And I also have a fever.
医者 のども痛いですか。 Does your throat hurt too?
TOM はい、ちょっと痛いです。せきもあります。 Yes, it hurts a little. I also have a cough.
医者 かぜですね。薬を飲んでください。そして、今日は学校に行ってはいけません。 It's a cold. Please take medicine. And you must not go to school today.
TOM 分かりました。家で休みます。お風呂に入ってもいいですか。 Understood. I'll rest at home. May I take a bath?
医者 今日はお風呂に入ってはいけません。早く寝たほうがいいですよ。 You must not take a bath today. It's better to sleep early.
(Tom stops at the pharmacy next door on his way home.)
薬剤師 この薬を一日三回飲んでください。お大事に。 Please take this medicine three times a day. Take care.

ないようりかい — Comprehension questions:

  1. What symptoms does Tom describe to the receptionist and the doctor?
  2. What does the doctor tell Tom he must not do today?
  3. What does Tom ask permission to do, and what does the doctor say?

Writing Workshop

In this workshop you will write a short message to a friend or instructor explaining that you are unwell and describing what you will do.

  1. Choose your symptoms. Pick one or two symptoms from this chapter's vocabulary.
  2. List your ideas in English first. Note the symptom, the reason you are writing, and your plan for the day.
  3. Write a sentence describing your symptom using a body-part subject pattern.
  4. Write a sentence about what you must not do today, using 〜てはいけません, or about what is fine to do, using 〜てもいいです.
  5. Write a sentence about what you want to do using 〜たいです.
  6. Add a closing sentence, such as a plan to rest or contact the reader again later.
  7. Revise and check using the checklist below before finalizing your work.

チェックリスト — Editing checklist:

  • I described my symptom using a body part plus が and an accurate い-adjective.
  • I used 〜てはいけません or 〜てもいいです correctly and appropriately.
  • I expressed a wish using 〜たいです, conjugated correctly.
  • I used the new Unit III kanji correctly, with furigana.
  • I ended each sentence with 。 and organized my message logically.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — お大事に and expressions of concern

お大事に ("Take care" or "Get well soon") is a common expression used by pharmacy staff, doctors, and acquaintances when someone is unwell. It is typically said as a closing remark rather than as a full sentence, and it appears in many everyday interactions involving illness or injury, from a brief clinic visit to a conversation between coworkers.

Regional Note — Missing school or work when unwell

Practices around missing school or work due to illness vary by institution, region, and workplace. Some schools and workplaces expect advance notice by phone or message, as Tom does in this chapter's opening scenario; others have different procedures, such as online forms or medical notes. Learners preparing to study, work, or live in a Japanese-speaking community should confirm the specific expectations of their own school or workplace rather than assuming one universal practice.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Marking the body part with は instead of が

Error: *頭は痛いです。 as a first, out-of-context statement about a headache.

Correction: 頭が痛いです。 — Use が to introduce the body part as the focus of a new symptom description. は becomes natural only when contrasting body parts, such as 頭は痛くないですが、のどは痛いです。

Common Mistake — Confusing てもいいです and てください

Error: Using 薬を飲んでもいいです when intending to instruct someone to take medicine.

Correction: 薬を飲んでください。 — 〜てもいいです grants permission for an optional action; it does not instruct or request. Use 〜てください, which you learned in Chapter 11, for an actual instruction or request.

Common Mistake — Forming the negative of たい incorrectly

Error: *飲みたじゃないです。

Correction: 飲みたくないです。 — たい conjugates like an い-adjective, so its negative form uses 〜くない, not 〜じゃない.

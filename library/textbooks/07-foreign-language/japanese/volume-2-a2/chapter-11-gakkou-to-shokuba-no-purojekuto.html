学校と職場のプロジェクトSchool and Workplace Projects

Tasks, collaboration, and progress reporting · Speak and Write Japanese: Everyday Independence and Connected Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Describe what stage a task or project is at, using 「るところです」、「ているところです」、and 「たところです」.
  2. State who is responsible for a task, using 「担当」 and related frames such as 「〜を担当しています」.
  3. State a deadline, using 「までに」, and distinguish it from a simple time expression.
  4. Give a reason for a delay or a decision in a meeting or project context, using 「ので」.
  5. Assign a task to someone and confirm an assignment, using polite request and confirmation frames appropriate to a school or workplace setting.
  6. Read and write a short progress report that names a task, a person responsible, a deadline, and a reason for the current status.

Communication Scenario

Emma has joined a small group project at her school's community center, preparing materials for an upcoming local event. Yuki is also part of the group. The team needs to divide tasks, agree on deadlines, and check on each other's progress before the event. This chapter opens Unit III, moving from Volume II's everyday problem-solving into connected explanation across school, workplace, media, and opinion contexts.

  1. Emma and her teammates decide who is responsible for which part of the project.
  2. The group sets a deadline for the first draft of the materials.
  3. During a short check-in meeting, each person reports on their progress and explains any delay.

Script Note — 締め切り and 締切

The word for deadline is commonly written 締め切り with okurigana in everyday writing, and often shortened to 締切 in headers, forms, and notices. This chapter uses 締め切り in sentences and 締切 in table headers and notices, matching ordinary practice. Both forms are read the same way: しめきり.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Long vowels and gemination in project vocabulary

Several new words in this chapter combine a long vowel with a following geminate consonant, a pattern that is easy to shorten if learners are not careful:

  • 担当 (たんとう) has a long vowel in the second syllable; keep it distinct from たんと.
  • 報告 (ほうこく) opens with a long vowel; compare it with the shorter ほこく, which is not a natural word.
  • 提出 (ていしゅつ) contains a small 「ゅ」 combined with 「つ」; keep both morae distinct and avoid collapsing them into one syllable.
  • 締め切り (しめきり) has no long vowel or gemination, but is often rushed together; keep all four morae clear.

Practice tapping out the mora count for 担当, 報告, and 提出 before using them in the Guided Practice section below.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter introduces four connected systems for describing project work: stage-of-progress expressions with ところです, responsibility expressions with 担当, deadline expressions with までに, and reason expressions with ので.

1. Describing a stage of progress — ところです

Purpose: to say that an action is about to begin, currently underway, or just finished.

  • 今から資料を作るところです。I'm just about to make the materials now.verb dictionary form + ところです → about to
  • 今、資料を作っているところです。I'm in the middle of making the materials right now.verb ている form + ところです → in progress
  • 資料はさっき作ったところです。I just finished making the materials a moment ago.verb た form + ところです → just finished

Compare these three forms carefully: the verb form immediately before ところです determines whether the action is about to start, currently happening, or just completed.

2. Stating responsibility — 担当

Purpose: to say who is responsible for a task within a project or team.

  • 田中さんが資料を担当しています。Tanaka is in charge of the materials.
  • この作業の担当は私です。I'm the one in charge of this task.
  • エマさんは会議の準備を担当してください。Emma, please take charge of the meeting preparations.

3. Stating a deadline — までに

Purpose: to state a point by which an action must be completed, in contrast to まで, which marks a continuous period up to a point.

  • 金曜日までに資料を提出してください。Please submit the materials by Friday.
  • 会議までに準備を終わらせるつもりです。I intend to finish the preparations by the time of the meeting.

までに marks a deadline for a single completed action (submit by Friday). まで, reviewed from earlier chapters, marks a continuous span (work until Friday). Confusing the two is a common error covered in the Common Mistakes section below.

4. Giving a reason — ので

Purpose: to give a reason in a way that sounds slightly softer and more explanatory than から, and that is common in meeting and reporting contexts.

  • 作業の進行が遅れているので、締め切りを一日延ばしてもらえますか。Since the work is running behind, could you extend the deadline by one day?
  • 資料はもうできているので、今、提出することができます。Since the materials are already finished, I can submit them now.
Building Block — Reporting on a task
assign responsibility (担当) → state a deadline (までに) → describe current progress (ところです) → give a reason for the status (ので)
A short progress report can move through all four patterns in a single connected statement, matching how a team member would actually update the group in a meeting.
資料の担当は私ですが、金曜日までに終わらせる予定です。実は他の作業が長引いているので、今はまだ資料を作っているところです。
I'm in charge of the materials, and my plan is to finish by Friday. Actually, another task has been taking longer than expected, so right now I'm still in the middle of making the materials.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Emma, Yuki, and a third teammate, Sato, hold a short check-in meeting about their community center project.

佐藤 それでは、会議を始めましょう。まず、資料の担当はエマさんでしたね。進行はどうですか。 All right, let's start the meeting. First, Emma, you're in charge of the materials, right? How's the progress?
エマ はい。今、資料を作っているところです。ほかの作業が長引いているので、少し遅れています。 Yes. I'm in the middle of making the materials right now. Since another task has been taking longer than expected, I'm a bit behind.
佐藤 分かりました。締め切りは金曜日ですが、間に合いそうですか。 I understand. The deadline is Friday — does it look like you'll make it in time?
エマ 木曜日までに終わらせるつもりです。もし遅れたら、すぐに報告します。 I intend to finish by Thursday. If I get delayed, I'll report right away.
佐藤 ありがとうございます。ユキさんは会場の準備を担当してもらえますか。 Thank you. Yuki, could you take charge of preparing the venue?
ユキ はい、大丈夫です。会場の準備はもう始めたところです。ファイルは共有フォルダに入れておきます。 Yes, that's fine. I've just started the venue preparations. I'll put the files in the shared folder.
佐藤 助かります。それでは、次の会議は水曜日にしましょう。 That's a great help. Well then, let's set the next meeting for Wednesday.

ないようりかい — Comprehension questions:

  1. What is Emma in charge of, and what stage is her task at?
  2. Why is Emma's task running a little behind?
  3. What deadline does Emma give for finishing, and what does she promise to do if she is delayed?
  4. What is Yuki asked to take charge of, and what stage is that task at?

Writing Workshop

In this workshop you will write a short progress report for an imagined project, following the Building Block pattern from the Grammar section.

  1. State your task and responsibility, using 担当.
  2. State a deadline, using までに.
  3. Describe the current stage of the task, using an appropriate form of ところです.
  4. Give a reason for the current status, using ので, whether the task is on schedule, ahead, or behind.
  5. Connect your sentences so the report reads as one short account rather than four separate lists.
  6. Revise and check using the checklist below before finalizing your text.

チェックリスト — Editing checklist:

  • My report clearly states who is responsible for the task.
  • I used までに correctly for a deadline, not まで for a continuous period.
  • I chose the correct form of ところです for the actual stage of the task.
  • I used ので to give a reason, not から, matching the softer explanatory tone of a report.
  • I kept furigana on this chapter's new kanji and on Chapter 8–9 kanji, and used reduced furigana on Chapter 6–7 kanji where appropriate.
  • I ended each sentence with 。 and used consistent script and punctuation.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Progress meetings in schools and workplaces

Short, regular check-in meetings are common in many Japanese schools, clubs, and workplaces when a group is working on a shared project. Team members are often expected to state clearly what they are responsible for and to report proactively on delays, rather than waiting to be asked. A phrase such as 遅れているので、報告します (since I'm behind, I'm reporting it) reflects an expectation of clear, timely communication about progress, though the exact style of a meeting varies by institution, team, and individual relationships.

Regional Note — Workplace and school terms vary by institution

Words such as 担当, 進行, and 報告 are widely used across schools, community groups, and businesses, but specific institutions often have their own preferred terms for roles, meetings, and reports. A company might use different internal terminology than a school club uses for a similar kind of task. Learners moving between contexts should expect to adjust some vocabulary while keeping the underlying grammar patterns in this chapter the same.

Register Note — Reporting to a group leader or teacher

In the Model Dialogue, all speakers remain in polite です／ます style throughout the meeting, even though they know each other. This reflects a common pattern in group or classroom settings, where polite style is maintained during structured meetings and reports even among peers, while more casual plain style might be used in the same group's private conversation afterward.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Confusing までに and まで

Error: *金曜日まで資料を提出してください。 (using まで, which suggests continuing an action up to Friday, for a one-time submission).

Correction: 金曜日までに資料を提出してください。 — までに marks the point by which a single completed action, such as submitting materials, must happen.

Common Mistake — Choosing the wrong stage of ところです

Error: *資料はもう作るところです。 (using the dictionary form with もう, "already," which contradicts the "about to start" meaning of るところです).

Correction: 資料はもう作ったところです。 — the た form with ところです correctly matches もう, since the action has just been completed.

Common Mistake — Treating 担当 as a verb by itself

Error: *私は資料を担当。 (leaving 担当 without する or a copula, so the sentence does not close naturally).

Correction: 私は資料を担当しています。 or 資料の担当は私です。 — 担当 needs する to function as a verb, or です when used as a noun stating who is responsible.

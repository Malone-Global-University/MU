ニュースとメディアNews and Media

Main ideas, source type, and opinion response · Speak and Write Japanese: Everyday Independence and Connected Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Recognize plain-form headline style in short news headlines and public notices.
  2. Report information you heard or read from another source, using hearsay 「そうです」.
  3. State what a piece of news, a report, or a conversation is about, using 「について」.
  4. Identify and label the source of information, using frames such as 「〜によると」.
  5. Understand the main idea and key details of a short news item or social media post.
  6. Give a short, connected spoken or written response to something you read or heard in the news.

Communication Scenario

Emma has started following local news to prepare for her community center project and to keep up with everyday life. She checks a news app, reads short articles and headlines, and talks with Yuki about what she has seen. This chapter continues Unit III by building the skills needed to follow, summarize, and briefly respond to news and media content at a B1 level.

  1. Emma reads a short news article and a few headlines on her phone.
  2. Emma tells Yuki what she read, being careful to say where the information came from.
  3. Yuki and Emma briefly discuss what the news means for their neighborhood.

Script Note — Headline style and dropped particles

Japanese headlines and short notices often use plain-form verbs and drop particles such as 「は」、「が」、and 「を」 to save space, for example 「事故で電車遅れる」 instead of the full sentence 「事故で電車が遅れています」. This chapter introduces headline style only for recognition: you should be able to understand a short headline written this way, but you are not expected to produce it yourself. Full sentences with particles remain the productive standard in your own speaking and writing.

れんしゅう — Practice: Write each of the eight new kanji above once from memory, then use each one in a short phrase such as 天気予報 (weather forecast) or 記事の内容 (the content of the article).

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Long vowels and devoicing in media vocabulary

Several new words in this chapter combine long vowels, gemination, or high-vowel devoicing:

  • 予報 (よほう) has a long vowel in the second syllable; keep it distinct from よほ.
  • 発表 (はっぴょう) contains a geminate consonant and a long vowel together; keep both features clear and avoid shortening it to はぴょ.
  • 情報 (じょうほう) has two long vowels in a row; count all six morae carefully.
  • 記事 (きじ) often shows devoicing of the き in casual, fast speech; this is a listening target, not something to force in your own careful speech.

Practice tapping out the mora count for 予報, 発表, and 情報 before using them in the Guided Practice section below.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter introduces three connected systems for handling news and media: reporting what you heard or read with hearsay そうです, naming a topic with について, and labeling a source with によると.

1. Reporting what you heard or read — hearsay そうです

Purpose: to report information that comes from another source, such as the news, a person, or a written report, rather than from your own direct experience or judgment.

  • 明日は雨だそうです。I heard that it's going to rain tomorrow.plain form + そうです → hearsay
  • その駅で事故があったそうです。I heard there was an accident at that station.
  • その店はもう閉まっているそうです。I heard that shop is already closed.

Hearsay そうです attaches to the plain form of a verb, adjective, or noun + だ. It signals that the information comes from somewhere else, so it should not be used for your own direct opinions or firsthand experience.

2. Naming a topic — について

Purpose: to state what a piece of news, a report, a conversation, or a piece of writing is about.

  • 今日のニュースは新しい公園について話していました。Today's news talked about a new park.
  • この記事は駅前の事故について書かれています。This article is written about the accident in front of the station.
  • その発表について、意見を聞かせてください。Please let me hear your opinion about that announcement.

3. Labeling a source — によると

Purpose: to identify where a piece of information came from, which is especially important when reporting news, since a good report always names its source.

  • 天気予報によると、明日は雨だそうです。According to the weather forecast, I heard it's going to rain tomorrow.
  • その記事によると、駅前で事故があったそうです。According to that article, I heard there was an accident in front of the station.
  • ユキさんの投稿によると、公園のイベントは来週だそうです。According to Yuki's post, I heard the park event is next week.

によると very often appears together with hearsay そうです, since naming a source and reporting secondhand information usually go together in natural Japanese.

Building Block — Reporting a piece of news
name the source (によると) → state the topic (について) → report the information (plain form + そうです) → add your own short opinion (と思います)
A short news report can move through all four elements in a connected statement, matching how someone would actually summarize a news item to a friend.
今日のニュースによると、駅前の事故について、電車が一時間遅れたそうです。私は、朝の時間は特に気をつけたほうがいいと思います。
According to today's news, regarding the accident in front of the station, I heard the trains were delayed by an hour. I think it's especially a good idea to be careful during the morning hours.

  1. Write one sentence using hearsay そうです to report something you read or heard.

  3. Write one sentence using によると to name your source.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Emma and Yuki meet at the community center and talk about something Emma read in the news that morning.

エマ 今朝のニュースを見ましたか。駅前で事故があったそうですよ。 Did you see this morning's news? I heard there was an accident in front of the station.
ユキ え、本当ですか。それはどこの情報ですか。 Oh, really? Where did you get that information?
エマ ニュースアプリの記事によると、朝、電車が一時間ぐらい遅れたそうです。 According to an article on a news app, I heard the trains were delayed by about an hour this morning.
ユキ そうですか。それは大変でしたね。ほかにも何か発表がありましたか。 I see. That must have been rough. Was there any other announcement?
エマ はい、天気予報によると、午後から雨が降るそうです。傘を持って行ったほうがいいですよ。 Yes, according to the weather forecast, I heard it's going to rain from the afternoon. You'd better bring an umbrella.
ユキ 教えてくれてありがとうございます。プロジェクトの発表について、何か新しい情報はありますか。 Thanks for telling me. Is there any new information about the announcement for the project?
エマ 佐藤さんの投稿によると、会場の準備はもう終わったところだそうです。 According to Sato's post, I heard the venue preparations have just been finished.

ないようりかい — Comprehension questions:

  1. What happened at the station this morning, and where did Emma get that information?
  2. What does the weather forecast say, and what advice does Emma give?
  3. According to Sato's post, what stage is the venue preparation at?

Writing Workshop

In this workshop you will write a short news summary of an imagined local event, following the Building Block pattern from the Grammar section.

  1. Name your source, using によると.
  2. State the topic, using について.
  3. Report the information, using an appropriate plain form + そうです.
  4. Add a short opinion or piece of advice, using と思います or ほうがいい.
  5. Connect your sentences so the summary reads as one short account rather than separate lists.
  6. Revise and check using the checklist below before finalizing your text.

チェックリスト — Editing checklist:

  • My summary clearly states where the information came from, using によると.
  • I used について to state the topic of my summary.
  • I used hearsay そうです correctly for reported information, not for my own direct opinion.
  • I kept my own opinion clearly separate from the reported information.
  • I kept furigana on this chapter's new kanji and on Chapter 8–9 kanji, and used reduced furigana on Chapter 6–7 and Chapter 11 kanji where appropriate.
  • I ended each sentence with 。 and used consistent script and punctuation.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Following local news and public announcements

Many people in Japan follow local news through television broadcasts, news apps, and community bulletin boards, especially for practical information such as train delays, weather warnings, and local events. Public institutions, including city offices and train companies, often issue short, formal announcements when something affects daily life, and residents are generally expected to check this kind of information regularly rather than wait to be told directly, though habits vary by generation, region, and individual.

Regional Note — Local news sources vary

The specific apps, stations, and community bulletins that people use for local news vary considerably by city, town, and region. A neighborhood in a large city may rely heavily on a train company's app for transit updates, while a smaller town may depend more on a community center bulletin board or a local broadcasting station. The grammar patterns in this chapter apply across these different sources, even though the specific vocabulary for a source may change.

Register Note — Plain style in written news

Written news articles, like the one in the Reading Practice section, are commonly written in plain style even though they are addressing a general public audience, because plain style is the standard convention for written reports and articles. This is different from a spoken announcement or a conversation between colleagues, which would typically use polite です／ます style. Recognizing plain style in written news is a receptive skill in this chapter; your own speaking and writing about the news should continue to use polite style unless another chapter specifically authorizes otherwise.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Using そうです for your own opinion

Error: *私はこの公園が好きだそうです。 (using hearsay そうです for the speaker's own direct feeling, which does not come from another source).

Correction: 私はこの公園が好きです。 — hearsay そうです is reserved for information that comes from someone or something else, not for your own firsthand opinion or experience.

Common Mistake — Forgetting to name a source

Error: *電車が遅れたそうです。 without ever stating where this information came from anywhere in the conversation.

Correction: ニュースによると、電車が遅れたそうです。 — a clear news report should name its source, especially the first time the information is introduced.

Common Mistake — Treating a headline as a full sentence to imitate

Error: *駅前で火事。 used as a spoken sentence in ordinary conversation, imitating headline style.

Correction: 駅前で火事があったそうです。 — headline style is for recognition when reading headlines only; your own spoken and written sentences should use full particles and a complete predicate.

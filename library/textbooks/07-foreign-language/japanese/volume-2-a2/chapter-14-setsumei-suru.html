14

説明するExplaining Processes

Steps, causes, and instructions · Speak and Write Japanese: Everyday Independence and Connected Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Explain a process in order, using 「まず」、「次に」、and 「最後に」.
  2. Describe a general result or rule using the procedural 「と」 pattern.
  3. Describe what to do in a particular situation, using 「場合」.
  4. Explain the purpose of an action, using 「ために」.
  5. Explain a goal or desired outcome, using 「ように」.
  6. Recognize instructions written in the plain imperative form without being required to produce them.
  7. Follow and give step-by-step spoken and written instructions for a familiar procedure.

Communication Scenario

With the event day decided in Chapter 13, Emma now needs to actually get the project moving. The community center requires an application form for using the meeting room, and Yuki's coworker has offered to explain how to use the center's printer to make flyers. Emma needs to understand written instructions, follow spoken directions step by step, and later explain the same steps to another volunteer. This chapter gives Emma, and you, the language for explaining and following processes: sequencing steps, stating purposes, and describing what to do in different situations.

  1. Emma reads a short set of instructions for filling out the room application form.
  2. A staff member explains, step by step, how to use the printer.
  3. Emma later explains the same printer steps to a new volunteer, using ために and ように to explain why each step matters.

Script Note — 方 in 方法 and 使い方

The character 方 appears in both 方法 (method) and 使い方 (how to use), but it is read differently in each: ほう in 方法 and かた in 使い方. In 使い方, 方 attaches to the stem of a verb to mean "the way of doing" that verb, a pattern you will also see with other verbs later in the series, such as 読み方 (how to read) or 書き方 (how to write). Learn each word as its own unit rather than assuming 方 always sounds the same.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Gemination in procedure vocabulary

Several new words in this chapter contain a geminate consonant, marked by small っ:

  • 結果 (けっか) contains small っ before か; keep the extra beat distinct from けか.
  • 申し込む (もうしこむ) has a long vowel in the first syllable (もう), which should not be shortened to もしこむ.
  • 確認する (かくにんする) contains moraic ん before a さ-row consonant; keep ん as its own mora rather than blending it into にん as a single unit with the following する.

Practice tapping out the mora count for 結果, 申し込む, and 確認する before using them in the Guided Practice section below.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter introduces five connected systems for explaining a process: sequencing steps with まず／次に／最後に, describing general results with procedural と, describing situations with 場合, explaining purpose with ために and ように, and recognizing plain imperative instructions.

1. Sequencing steps — まず／次に／最後に

Purpose: to present the steps of a process clearly, in the order they happen.

  • まず、申し込み用紙に名前を書きます。First, write your name on the application form.
  • 次に、電話番号を書きます。Next, write your phone number.
  • 最後に、受付に出します。Finally, submit it at the reception desk.

まず、次に、and 最後に are sentence-initial connectors and do not change the verb form that follows. They may be combined with other connectors, such as それから (after that), which was introduced in an earlier volume as a general-purpose sequencing word.

2. Describing general results — procedural と

Purpose: to describe what regularly or naturally happens when a condition is met, such as steps in a machine's operation or a general rule.

  • このボタンを押すと、電源が入ります。If you press this button, the power turns on.dictionary form + と → automatic/general result
  • 申し込み用紙を出すと、番号がもらえます。When you submit the application form, you receive a number.
  • この道をまっすぐ行くと、駅に着きます。If you go straight down this road, you arrive at the station.

This procedural と describes a general or automatic result, not a one-time personal plan or invitation. It is common in instructions, directions, and explanations of how something works, and it does not require です／ます after the と clause when it functions this way.

3. Describing situations — 場合

Purpose: to explain what to do, or what is true, in a particular case or situation.

  • 分からない場合は、スタッフに聞いてください。If you don't understand, please ask a staff member.
  • 紙がなくなった場合は、この棚から取ってください。If the paper runs out, please take some from this shelf.
  • 雨の場合は、イベントは中止です。In the case of rain, the event is cancelled.

場合 attaches after a plain-form verb, an adjective, or a noun (often with の), and is frequently followed by は. It is especially common in written instructions, notices, and explanations of exceptions or special cases.

4. Explaining purpose — ために and ように

Purpose: to explain why an action is done, either as a stated goal (ために) or as a desired outcome expressed with a verb that is not fully under the speaker's direct control (ように).

  • きれいに印刷するために、正しい紙を選びます。In order to print cleanly, choose the correct paper.
  • みんなが分かるように、ゆっくり説明します。So that everyone can understand, I'll explain slowly.
  • 間違えないように、もう一度確認してください。Please check once more so that you don't make a mistake.

ために typically follows a verb that expresses an intentional goal, such as 印刷する (to print) above. ように typically follows a verb describing a state, ability, or unintentional result, such as 分かる (to understand) or 間違える (to make a mistake); this is why 分かるように is natural but 分かるために would sound unusual in the same sentence. This distinction is introduced here at a practical level and will be developed further in later volumes.

5. Recognizing plain imperative instructions (recognition only)

Purpose: to recognize direct plain-form commands when they appear in signs, manuals, or recipes, without being required to produce them.

  • 押せ。Press. (blunt plain imperative — signs and manuals only)
  • 止まれ。Stop. (blunt plain imperative — traffic and safety signs)

The plain imperative is common on signs, in manuals, and in some fixed public notices, but it sounds abrupt or harsh in ordinary conversation. This chapter's productive instructions instead use polite request forms such as 〜てください, which you have used since Volume I. Recognizing the plain imperative on a sign is a reading skill; producing it in everyday speech is not expected at this stage.

Building Block — Explaining a process
state the purpose (ために／ように) → sequence the steps (まず → 次に → 最後に) → note a special case (場合は〜) → describe the natural result (〜と、〜)
A clear set of instructions usually explains why the process matters, walks through the steps in order, and warns about any exceptions or automatic results along the way.
きれいなちらしを作るために、まず紙を選びます。次に、このボタンを押すと、印刷が始まります。紙がなくなった場合は、スタッフに言ってください。最後に、印刷した紙を確認してください。
In order to make a clean flyer, first choose the paper. Next, if you press this button, printing begins. If the paper runs out, please tell a staff member. Finally, please check the printed paper.

Grammar Practice

  1. Write three sentences describing the steps of a simple process, using まず, 次に, and 最後に.
  2. Write one sentence describing a general result using procedural と.
  3. Write one sentence describing a special situation using 場合は.
  4. Write one sentence explaining a purpose, choosing ために or ように correctly for the verb you use.
  5. Combine your sentences from items 1 through 4 into one connected explanation, following the Building Block pattern.

Model Dialogue

Setting: A community center staff member, カワタさん, explains the printer to Emma.

カワタ では、プリンターの使い方を説明しますね。 All right, I'll explain how to use the printer.
エマ お願いします。 Please do.
カワタ まず、きれいに印刷するために、正しい紙のサイズを選んでください。次に、このボタンを押すと、画面に順番が出ます。 First, in order to print cleanly, please choose the correct paper size. Next, if you press this button, the order appears on the screen.
エマ 分かりました。紙がなくなった場合は、どうすればいいですか。 I understand. If the paper runs out, what should I do?
カワタ その場合は、棚から新しい紙を取ってください。それから、最後に、結果を確認してから終わってください。 In that case, please take new paper from the shelf. After that, finally, please check the result before finishing.
エマ 分かりました。この手順を後で新しいボランティアの人にも説明しなければなりません。 I understand. I also have to explain this procedure to a new volunteer later.
カワタ それなら、みんなが分かるように、ゆっくり順番に説明してくださいね。 In that case, please explain slowly and in order, so that everyone can understand.

ないようりかい — Comprehension questions:

  1. What must Emma choose first, and why?
  2. What should Emma do if the paper runs out?
  3. What does Kawata advise Emma to do when she explains the procedure to a new volunteer?

Writing Workshop

In this workshop you will write a short set of instructions for a familiar process, following the Building Block pattern from the Grammar section.

  1. State the purpose of the process, using ために or ように.
  2. List the steps in order, using まず、次に、and 最後に.
  3. Describe at least one automatic result, using procedural と.
  4. Note a special case, using 場合は, such as what to do if something goes wrong.
  5. Check your instructions by reading them aloud in order, as if following them for the first time.
  6. Revise and check using the checklist below before finalizing your text.

チェックリスト — Editing checklist:

  • My instructions include a clear purpose using ために or ように.
  • My steps are in a clear order, using まず、次に、and 最後に.
  • I included at least one automatic result using procedural と.
  • I included at least one special case using 場合は.
  • I kept furigana on this chapter's new kanji and reduced furigana on Chapter 13 kanji, following the furigana reduction schedule.
  • I ended each sentence with 。 and used consistent script and punctuation.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Written instructions in public and community spaces

Community centers, libraries, and other shared public facilities in Japan commonly post written step-by-step instructions for using shared equipment such as printers, photocopiers, and reservation systems, often alongside a staff member available to help in person. This combination of written and spoken instruction is common in many public-facility cultures around the world and is not unique to Japan, but it is a useful pattern to expect when navigating similar spaces, since the wording and level of detail can vary by facility and region.

Regional Note — Sign and manual language

Public signs, manuals, and safety notices sometimes use the blunt plain imperative form introduced in this chapter's grammar section, but the exact wording of signs can vary by facility, industry, and region. Some notices use polite request forms instead, especially in customer-facing spaces. This chapter asks you only to recognize the plain imperative when reading; you are not expected to produce it.

Register Note — Explaining a process to different audiences

The patterns in this chapter, including 〜てください, まず／次に／最後に, and 場合は, are appropriate for polite explanations to staff, coworkers, and people you do not know well. When explaining a process to a close friend or family member, some speakers shift to plain style throughout, dropping です／ます, but that fuller shift is not the focus of this chapter and should not be assumed automatically from the patterns introduced here.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Using ために where ように is expected

Error: *みんなが分かるために、ゆっくり説明します。 using ために after 分かる, a verb describing a state rather than an intentional action.

Correction: みんなが分かるように、ゆっくり説明します。 — ように is used with verbs describing states, abilities, or outcomes that are not directly controlled by the subject's will.

Common Mistake — Confusing procedural と with an invitation or plan

Error: *明日、映画を見ると、楽しいですよ。 attempting to use procedural と to invite someone to a one-time planned activity.

Correction: 映画を見ると、楽しい気持ちになります。 or, for an invitation, 明日、映画を見ませんか。 — procedural と describes a general or automatic result, not a specific one-time plan or invitation.

Common Mistake — Listing steps without any sequencing words

Error: *紙を選びます。ボタンを押します。紙を取ります。 presenting three separate sentences with no indication of order or connection.

Correction: まず、紙を選びます。次に、ボタンを押します。最後に、紙を取ります。 — まず、次に、and 最後に make the sequence of steps clear to a reader or listener who has not seen the process before.

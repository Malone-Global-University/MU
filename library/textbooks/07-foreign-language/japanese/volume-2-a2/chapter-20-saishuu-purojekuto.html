Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Review and use, in one connected project, the major grammar systems developed across Volume II, including obligation, giving and receiving, explanatory んです, conditionals, and reported and secondhand speech.
  2. Read and write mixed-script Japanese that combines Volume II kanji with appropriately reduced furigana.
  3. Interview, narrate, explain, give an opinion, and report within a single organized project.
  4. Manage topic reference across a longer piece of connected discourse without unnatural repetition.
  5. Distinguish polite and plain style and apply register consistently across a spoken and a written task.
  6. Complete a multi-part final performance task that draws on listening, speaking, reading, and writing.
  7. Identify which Volume II systems feel secure and which require further practice before beginning Volume III.

Communication Scenario

The neighborhood newsletter article from Chapter 19 was a success, and the neighborhood association has asked Emma and Yuki to put together one larger feature: a short profile of a person in the community, including their recent life, an experience they are proud of, a problem they solved, an opinion they hold about the area, and a plan for the future. To do this, Emma and Yuki must draw on everything from this volume — introducing themselves and updating others on their lives, describing routines and relationships, stating obligations, solving a practical problem, explaining a process, forming and supporting an opinion, narrating experience, discussing community and conditions, and finally reporting what they learned in one connected account. This chapter's Final Capstone Performance Task asks you to do the same for a person of your choice.

  1. The writer reviews and reuses vocabulary and grammar from across Volume II.
  2. The writer interviews someone, narrates an experience, explains a process, and gives a supported opinion.
  3. The writer organizes everything into one clear, connected profile with correct polite or plain style throughout.

Pronunciation Review

Pronunciation Focus — Maintaining accuracy across a longer connected performance

As your spoken output grows longer in this chapter's capstone task, mora timing, vowel length, gemination, and moraic ん can slip, particularly across sentence boundaries when you are focused on content rather than form. Before recording or presenting your final project, review the following pairs, all drawn from earlier Volume II vocabulary:

  • 予約 (よやく) versus a shortened, incorrect よやく without the long vowel in よう being fully absent here — the key contrast to check is 報告 (ほうこく) against an under-lengthened ほこく.
  • 経験 (けいけん): keep the long vowel in けい distinct from a plain け, and keep moraic ん fully timed before け in けん.
  • 連絡 (れんらく): moraic ん before ら, without inserting a vowel between them.

Maintain phrase-level rhythm through longer connected sentences that combine と言っていました, そうです, and connective phrases such as ので or ために, rather than rushing toward the end of a long sentence.

Topic management across a long text

Purpose: to keep a single profile subject clear as the topic across many connected sentences covering different time periods and grammar systems, as first developed in Chapter 19.

  • 田中さんは、この地域に二十年住んでいます。最近、新しいボランティア活動を始めました。前は忙しくてできなかったそうですが、去年、仕事の状況が変わってから、時間ができたと言っていました。Tanaka-san has lived in this area for twenty years. Recently, they started a new volunteer activity. They said that before, they were too busy to do it, but after their work situation changed last year, they got some time.topic established once; carried through obligation, change, and reported speech

Notice that the topic 田中さん is stated once and then tracked through several different grammar systems — duration, recent change, obligation-like circumstance, and reported speech — without being repeated in every sentence.

Polite and plain style consistency

Purpose: to maintain one consistent style, usually polite です／ます style for a public-facing profile, while quoting or reporting a subject's own plain-form words when appropriate, as modeled throughout this volume.

  • 田中さんは、「もっと早く始めればよかった」と言っていました。Tanaka-san said, "I wish I had started earlier."polite narration frame; plain-form quoted content

A profile written mainly in polite style may still quote a subject's exact plain-form words inside 「 」, since a direct quotation preserves what the person actually said; the surrounding narration should remain consistently polite unless the whole project is intentionally written in plain style throughout.

Building Block — A full community profile
introduce the subject and recent life → describe a relationship, favor, or obligation → narrate a problem and how it was solved → explain a process or sequence connected to their activity → state a supported opinion the subject holds → narrate a past experience and a change over time → describe a future condition or plan → report what the subject said and add secondhand information → close with the writer's own conclusion
This nine-stage pattern draws one representative system from each unit of Volume II and organizes them into a single coherent profile. This chapter's Final Capstone Performance Task follows this same pattern; the Model Dialogue and Reading Practice below both demonstrate shortened versions of it.
田中さんは、この地域に長く住んでいます。最近、近所の子供たちに読み聞かせを教える活動を始めました。始める前は、場所を見つけなければならなかったそうですが、公民館の人が場所を貸してくれたと言っていました。活動をする時は、まず本を選び、次に子供たちを集め、最後に一緒に読みます。田中さんは、この活動が地域をもっと元気にすると思うと言っていました。若い頃は人前で話すのが苦手だったそうですが、今はもっと自信を持って話せるようになったと言っていました。もし興味がある人がいたら、いつでも見学できるそうです。私も、この活動をもっと多くの人に知ってもらいたいと思います。
Tanaka-san has lived in this area for a long time. Recently, they started an activity teaching neighborhood children through reading aloud. Before starting, I heard they had to find a location, but they said someone from the community center let them use a space. When they do the activity, they first choose a book, then gather the children, and finally read together. Tanaka-san said they think this activity makes the community more lively. I heard that when they were young, they weren't good at speaking in front of people, but they said they've become able to speak with more confidence now. If anyone is interested, I heard they can come observe anytime. I also hope more people come to know about this activity.

Writing Workshop

In this workshop you will draft the written portion of your Final Capstone Performance Task: a community profile that integrates grammar and vocabulary from across Volume II, following the nine-stage Building Block pattern from the Grammar Review section.

  1. Introduce your subject and their recent life, establishing the topic clearly.
  2. Describe a relationship, favor, or obligation connected to your subject.
  3. Narrate a problem your subject faced and how it was solved, using explanatory んです or a conditional pattern.
  4. Explain a process or sequence connected to your subject's activity, using まず／次に／最後に.
  5. State a supported opinion your subject holds, with a reason.
  6. Narrate a past experience and a change over time in your subject's life.
  7. Describe a future condition or plan connected to your subject.

  9. Close with your own conclusion, and revise using the checklist below.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Community profiles and lifelong local involvement

Community profiles like the ones practiced in this chapter reflect a common feature of neighborhood life in many parts of Japan, where long-term residents often take on informal or semi-formal roles supporting neighbors, such as organizing activities, checking on elderly residents, or running small local events. The scale, frequency, and formality of this kind of involvement vary considerably by neighborhood, city size, and region, so profiles like these should be read as examples rather than as a single universal pattern.

Regional Note — Community structures differ across areas

Some neighborhoods rely on a formal 自治会 (neighborhood association) to organize activities like those described in this chapter, while others rely more on informal networks among neighbors, community centers, or online groups. Learners preparing an interview-based project should ask their subject directly about how their own community is organized, rather than assuming one pattern applies everywhere.

Register Note — Consistency across a longer public-facing text

A community profile intended for a newsletter or public audience is typically written in consistent polite style throughout the narration, even when it quotes a subject's own plain-form words inside direct quotations. Shifting between polite and plain narration without a clear reason, such as a deliberate change in audience or genre, can make a public-facing text feel inconsistent or unfinished.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Switching between polite and plain narration without reason

Error: *山田さんはこの地域に十年住んでいます。最近、活動を始めた。問題があったと言っていました。 mixing です／ます narration with plain-form narration in the same profile.

Correction: 山田さんはこの地域に十年住んでいます。最近、活動を始めました。問題があったと言っていました。 — keep narration consistently polite throughout a public-facing profile, reserving plain forms for direct quotations inside 「 」 or for a deliberately plain-style piece.

Common Mistake — Overloading one sentence with too many systems

Error: *山田さんは、隣の人に頼まれて、大変だったけど、慣れて、来年広げるつもりだと言っていて、いいと思います。 combining giving/receiving, past difficulty, change, future plan, reported speech, and opinion into a single overloaded sentence.

Correction: Separate these ideas into distinct sentences, as the reading passage in this chapter does, so that each grammar system remains clear and the connected account stays readable.

Common Mistake — Treating a capstone project as a list of grammar points rather than a connected text

Error: Writing eight or nine disconnected sentences, each demonstrating one grammar point in isolation, without any topic chaining, transitions, or overall structure.

Correction: Use the Building Block pattern from the Grammar Review section to connect each stage smoothly, carrying the topic forward and using appropriate connectors, so that the finished project reads as one coherent profile rather than a checklist.

Final Capstone Performance Task

さいしゅうパフォーマンスタスク

自立した日本語 — A community profile

Choose a real or imagined person to profile, real interview subject preferred where possible. Complete all four components below, drawing on grammar and vocabulary from across Volume II.

6

外で困ったときWhen Problems Happen Outside

Requests, repairs, explanations, and service problems · Speak and Write Japanese: Everyday Independence and Connected Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Talk about lost items, broken devices, delays, wrong orders, and other everyday problems that happen while you are out.
  2. Explain a situation to someone using explanatory「んです」so that your explanation sounds natural rather than abrupt.
  3. Describe an unfortunate or regrettable event using「てしまいました」.
  4. Ask someone to do something for you using an expanded request pattern such as「てもらえませんか」.
  5. Use repair phrases to ask someone to repeat, slow down, or clarify what they said.
  6. Ask for and understand basic confirmation using「確認」in a service or public setting.
  7. Read and write a short account of a problem that happened while out, including what happened and what was requested or resolved.

Communication Scenario

Emma is spending a free afternoon in town before meeting Yuki. Over the course of the afternoon, several small problems come up: her train is delayed, she thinks she may have dropped her phone charger somewhere, and a café gets her order wrong. Each time, Emma has to explain the situation and ask for help in Japanese rather than simply pointing or switching to English.

  1. This chapter opens Unit II, which focuses on solving practical problems: things that go wrong outside the home, returns and reservations, travel and lodging, and clinic or procedure situations.
  2. By the end of the chapter, you will be able to explain a problem, make a polite request for help, and confirm details with a staff member or stranger in a way that sounds natural rather than overly blunt.

Vocabulary Note — なくす versus 落とす versus こわれる

なくす is used when you no longer know where something is, without saying how it happened. 落とす specifically suggests the object was dropped. こわれる describes an object becoming broken, without stating who broke it; the object itself is the grammatical subject, as in 充電器がこわれました (The charger broke).

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Explanatory intonation with んです

Explanatory 「んです」sentences often carry a slightly falling, confiding intonation, different from a plain statement. When practicing sentences such as 財布をなくしてしまったんです, keep the following in mind:

  • maintain full mora length in words such as こしょう (breakdown) and ちえん (delay), even when speaking with an apologetic or hesitant tone;
  • keep the geminate consonant clear in words such as ちょっと (a moment; a little) when used as a softener before a request; and
  • avoid rushing through the moraic ん in じゅうでんき (charger) and かんけい (relationship).

Practice saying one sentence from the Model Dialogue first as a plain statement, then again with the softer, more explanatory tone appropriate to んです.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter authorizes explanatory「んです」,「てしまいました」, repair phrases,「確認」, and expanded requests such as「てもらえませんか」.

1. Explanatory んです

Purpose: to explain a situation, give a reason, or provide background information, especially before making a request or apology.

  • 電車が遅れているんです。You see, the train is delayed. (explaining why I am late)
  • 充電器をなくしてしまったんです。You see, I've lost my charger.
  • これは注文したものと違うんです。This is different from what I ordered, you see.

んです signals that a statement is offered as an explanation or as background for what follows, rather than as a plain, unconnected fact. It is not simply a stronger version of です.

2. てしまいました — regrettable or completed events

Purpose: to describe an event, often unfortunate or unintended, that has been completed.

  • 財布を落としてしまいました。I dropped my wallet. (unfortunately)
  • スマホの充電器がこわれてしまいました。My phone charger broke. (unfortunately)
  • バスに乗り遅れてしまいました。I missed the bus. (unfortunately)

3. Repair phrases

Purpose: to ask someone to repeat, slow down, or clarify what they said.

  • すみません、もう一度お願いします。Excuse me, could you say that once more?
  • すみません、よく聞こえませんでした。Sorry, I didn't catch that clearly.
  • もう少しゆっくり話していただけますか。Could you speak a little more slowly?

4. 確認 — asking for and offering confirmation

Purpose: to check that details are correct before proceeding.

  • 確認してもいいですか。May I confirm something?
  • お名前を確認させてください。Please let me confirm your name.
  • 注文を確認したいんですが。I'd like to confirm the order, if that's all right.

5. Expanded requests — てもらえませんか

Purpose: to make a polite request for someone to do something, slightly softer and more indirect than てください.

  • 手伝ってもらえませんか。Could you help me?
  • もう一度説明してもらえませんか。Could you explain that once more?
  • エレベーターの場所を教えてもらえませんか。Could you tell me where the elevator is?

てもらえませんか is built on もらう (Chapter 3) plus the potential and negative-question pattern, making it more indirect and generally more polite than a plain command form.

Building Block — Explaining a problem and asking for help
Attention-getter + situation with んです or てしまいました + request with てもらえませんか
A typical problem-and-request exchange opens with a phrase like すみません, explains what happened, and closes with a polite request for help or confirmation.
すみません、充電器をなくしてしまったんです。忘れ物がなかったか、確認してもらえませんか。
Excuse me, I've lost my charger. Could you check whether there were any lost items found?

Model Dialogue

Setting: Emma stops by a station information counter after realizing she may have left her phone charger on a train.

エマ すみません、忘れ物をしてしまったんですが。 Excuse me, I seem to have left something behind.
係員 何を忘れましたか。 What did you leave behind?
エマ スマホの充電器です。電車の中に落としてしまったと思うんです。 My phone charger. I think I dropped it on the train.
係員 すみません、もう一度お願いします。何線に乗っていましたか。 Sorry, could you say that once more? Which line were you riding?
エマ 中央線です。今日の午後、乗っていました。確認してもらえませんか。 The Chūō Line. I was on it this afternoon. Could you check for me?
(The station staff member checks a list on a computer screen.)
係員 少々お待ちください。…充電器が一つ届いていますね。黒いものですか。 One moment, please. ...There is a charger here. Is it a black one?
エマ はい、そうです！助かりました。ありがとうございます。 Yes, that's it! You've really helped me. Thank you very much.
係員 こちらの用紙にお名前とご連絡先を書いていただけますか。確認のためです。 Could you write your name and contact information on this form? It's for confirmation.

ないようりかい — Comprehension questions:

  1. What does Emma think she lost, and where?
  2. Which train line was Emma riding?
  3. How does Emma ask the staff member to check for the item?
  4. What does the staff member ask Emma to do at the end of the conversation?

Writing Workshop

In this workshop you will write a short account of a problem you experienced (real or invented) while out, using the Building Block pattern from the Grammar section.

  1. Open with an attention-getter, such as すみません, and identify who you are speaking to (in an email, this can be a brief greeting instead).
  2. Explain what happened, using んです or てしまいました to describe the problem naturally.
  3. Add one detail about when, where, or what was involved, recycling vocabulary from this chapter.
  4. Make a polite request, using てもらえませんか or 確認してもらえませんか.
  5. Revise and check using the checklist below before finalizing your text.

チェックリスト — Editing checklist:

  • My text clearly explains what problem happened.
  • I used んです or てしまいました naturally, not as a random addition.
  • I included one polite request using てもらえませんか.
  • I used at least three vocabulary items from this chapter's Core Vocabulary.
  • I kept furigana on this chapter's new kanji and on developing Unit I kanji.
  • I kept one consistent, polite register throughout.
  • I ended each sentence with 。 and used consistent script and punctuation.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Lost-and-found services

Many train stations, shops, and public facilities in Japan maintain organized lost-and-found procedures, and asking politely at an information counter is a common and effective first step when something goes missing. Confirming details such as the item's color, the time, and the exact train line helps staff locate an item more quickly.

Regional Note — Variation in service procedures

The exact procedure for lost items, refunds, or accessibility requests can vary by company, region, and type of facility. The dialogue and notice in this chapter describe one plausible procedure; confirming the specific steps with staff at the actual location remains the most reliable approach.

Register Note — Softening a request

Using んです to explain a situation before making a request, and choosing てもらえませんか instead of a direct command form, both soften the request and make it sound more considerate. This kind of softening is especially useful when asking a stranger or a staff member for help.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Using てしまいました for events that are not regrettable

Error: *テストに合格してしまいました。 (used to mean simply "I passed the test," with no sense of regret).

Correction: テストに合格しました。 — てしまいました is appropriate for unfortunate, unintended, or sometimes surprising events, such as losing or breaking something, not for ordinary good news.

Common Mistake — Treating こわれる and こわす as interchangeable

Error: *充電器をこわれました。 (mixing the intransitive verb こわれる with を, as if it were transitive).

Correction: 充電器がこわれました。 — こわれる is intransitive and takes が; the transitive partner こわす, which takes を and means "to break something," is introduced later in the series.

Common Mistake — Skipping the attention-getter before a request

Error: Walking up to a stranger or staff member and stating a request directly, without すみません or another opening phrase.

Correction: Begin with すみません or a similar attention-getter before explaining the situation, as shown in the Model Dialogue.

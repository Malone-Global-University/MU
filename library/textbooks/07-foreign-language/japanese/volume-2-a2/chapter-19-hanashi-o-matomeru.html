話をまとめるPutting a Story Together

Narrative cohesion, reporting, and paragraph structure · Speak and Write Japanese: Everyday Independence and Connected Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Report what someone else said, using 「と言っていました」.
  2. Pass along information you heard from another source, using 「そうです」.
  3. Recognize 「らしい」 in reading and listening as a way of expressing an impression based on indirect information.
  4. Keep a topic clear across several connected sentences without repeating the same noun unnecessarily.
  5. Interview someone, take notes, and retell what they said in your own words.
  6. Recognize and produce kanji for story, interview, summary, report, memory, and contact.
  7. Write a short, organized account that combines a topic, reported speech, hearsay, and a personal conclusion.

Communication Scenario

The community clean-up event from Chapter 18 finally happened, and the neighborhood association wants a short article about it for their newsletter. Emma and Yuki decide to interview タナカさん, the longtime resident from Chapter 17, and サトウさん, the volunteer from Chapter 18, about how the day went. As they gather everyone's comments, they need to retell what people said, pass along things they only heard secondhand, and organize their notes into one connected account — the reporting and narrative-cohesion skills this chapter develops, and the final set of tools needed before Chapter 20's integrated capstone project.

  2. Someone passes along secondhand information, using そうです.

Script Note — 記憶 and 覚える are related in meaning but different in reading and part of speech

The noun 記憶 (きおく, memory) and the verb 覚える (おぼえる, to memorize) share the idea of retaining information, but they use different characters, different readings, and different grammatical behavior. 覚えている, taught in this chapter's vocabulary, is the stative て-form of 覚える and means "to remember," while 記憶 is the noun used for the concept of memory itself, as in 記憶がある. Learn each word's reading and use individually, as this volume's kanji policy requires.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Long vowels and moraic ん in reporting vocabulary

Several words in this chapter combine a long vowel with moraic ん or with a palatalized sequence. Practice the following words carefully:

  • 要約 (ようやく): long vowel in よう followed by やく; do not shorten よう to よ.
  • 報告 (ほうこく): long vowel in ほう; keep it clearly longer than a plain ほ.
  • 連絡 (れんらく): moraic ん before a ら-row consonant; do not insert a full vowel between ん and ら.

When retelling what someone said, keep phrase-level rhythm even through と言っていました and そうです, rather than rushing the reporting phrase at the end of the sentence.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter develops practical language for retelling and organizing a story. It introduces 「と言っていました」 for reporting what someone said, expands the hearsay use of 「そうです」 introduced in Chapter 12, presents 「らしい」 receptively as a way of expressing an impression based on indirect information, and develops topic chains that keep a subject clear across several connected sentences.

1. と言っていました — reporting what someone said

Purpose: to report, in your own words, something another person said at an earlier time.

  • タナカさんは、「今年のイベントはよかった」と言っていました。Tanaka-san said that this year's event was good.quoted or paraphrased content + と言っていました
  • サトウさんは、来年も参加したいと言っていました。Sato-san said that they want to participate again next year.
  • 住民の一人は、公園がきれいになったと言っていました。One of the residents said that the park had become clean.

と言っていました reports something said in the past and often continues to be true or relevant now; the content before と can be a direct quotation in「 」or a paraphrase in plain form, and either is natural in ordinary conversation and writing.

2. そうです — passing along secondhand information

Purpose: to report information you learned from someone else or from a source, rather than something you experienced or decided yourself. This use of そうです was introduced formulaically in Chapter 12 and becomes more fully productive here.

  • 来月、もっと大きいイベントをする予定だそうです。I heard that they're planning to do a bigger event next month.
  • タナカさんによると、去年より参加者が増えたそうです。According to Tanaka-san, the number of participants increased compared to last year.

Hearsay そうです attaches to the plain form of a verb, adjective, or noun + だ, and is often introduced with a source such as 「Xによると」 or 「Xの話では」; unlike と言っていました, it reports information rather than a specific person's exact words.

3. らしい — receptive recognition of indirect impression

Purpose: to recognize an impression or conclusion based on indirect evidence, such as something read, seen, or heard secondhand. Recognition only at this stage; learners are not expected to produce らしい independently.

  • 記事によると、来年は場所が変わるらしいです。According to the article, it seems the location will change next year.
  • タナカさんの話では、住民はみんな満足しているらしいです。From what Tanaka-san says, it seems that all the residents are satisfied.

らしい often overlaps in meaning with hearsay そうです, but it emphasizes the speaker's own impression drawn from indirect evidence rather than a direct report. Learners should recognize this pattern in reading and listening but are not required to produce it independently until a later volume.

4. Topic chains — keeping the subject clear

Purpose: to connect several sentences about the same person, event, or topic without repeating the noun in every sentence, using context, particles, and verb forms to keep reference clear.

  • タナカさんに取材しました。イベントについて詳しく話してくれました。とても印象に残ったと言っていました。I interviewed Tanaka-san. They talked to me in detail about the event. They said it left a strong impression on them.topic established once, then omitted across three sentences

Once タナカさん is introduced as the topic, later sentences can omit the name entirely as long as the verb forms and context make clear who is still being discussed; repeating タナカさんは in every sentence would sound unnatural and overly explicit in connected Japanese.

Building Block — Retelling an interview
introduce who you talked to (topic chain) → report what they said (と言っていました) → add something you only heard secondhand (そうです) → note an impression from indirect evidence (らしい, receptive) → close with your own conclusion
A natural retelling moves from establishing who is being discussed, to reporting their words directly, to passing along additional information from other sources, to noting an impression based on indirect evidence, and finally to a short conclusion in the writer's own words. This chapter's Model Dialogue and Performance Task ask you to move through these stages in order.
タナカさんに取材しました。今年のイベントはよかったと言っていました。来月、もっと大きいイベントをする予定だそうです。記事によると、来年は場所も変わるらしいです。私も、この地域の活動がもっと広がってほしいと思います。
I interviewed Tanaka-san. They said this year's event was good. I heard that they're planning a bigger event next month. According to the article, it seems the location will change next year too. I also hope that this community's activities will continue to grow.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Emma and Yuki compare notes after interviewing Tanaka-san and Sato-san about the clean-up event.

ユキ タナカさんに取材した内容、もうまとめた？ Have you already organized the content of the interview with Tanaka-san?
エマ うん。タナカさんは、今年のイベントはよかったと言っていました。特に、公園がきれいになったことが印象に残ったと言っていました。 Yeah. Tanaka-san said this year's event was good. They said the fact that the park became clean especially left an impression on them.
ユキ サトウさんは何か言っていた？ Did Sato-san say anything?
エマ サトウさんは、来年も参加したいと言っていました。それから、来月、もっと大きいイベントをする予定だそうです。 Sato-san said they want to participate again next year. Also, I heard that they're planning to do a bigger event next month.
ユキ へえ、詳しいね。記憶力がいいんだね。 Wow, that's detailed. You have a good memory.
エマ ちゃんとメモしたから。あ、それから、地域のニュースの記事によると、来年は場所も変わるらしいよ。 It's because I took proper notes. Oh, and also, according to a local news article, it seems the location will change next year too.
ユキ じゃあ、その内容も入れて、一つの文章にまとめよう。連絡先も忘れないでね。 Then let's include that content too and put it together into one piece of writing. Don't forget the contact information either.
エマ うん、要約してから、報告書に書くね。 Okay, I'll summarize it and then write it into the report.

ないようりかい — Comprehension questions:

  1. What did Tanaka-san say left an impression on them?
  2. What did Sato-san say, and what did Emma hear secondhand about next month?
  3. What does the news article suggest about next year, and what does Yuki ask Emma not to forget?

Writing Workshop

In this workshop you will write a short newsletter-style report that introduces who you talked to, reports what they said, adds secondhand information, and closes with your own conclusion, following the Building Block pattern from the Grammar section.

  1. Introduce who you talked to, establishing the topic clearly in your first sentence.
  2. Report what they said, using と言っていました.
  3. Add something you heard secondhand, using そうです.
  4. Note an impression based on indirect evidence, using a らしい pattern modeled on this chapter's examples.
  5. Keep your topic clear across sentences without repeating names unnecessarily.
  6. Close with your own short conclusion, and revise using the checklist below.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Neighborhood newsletters

Many neighborhood associations and local organizations in Japan produce a regular newsletter, often called 回覧板 or 広報誌, sharing event reports, announcements, and community news. These publications commonly combine short interviews, brief reports, and practical information such as contact details, similar to the reading passage in this chapter, though their format and frequency vary by area and organization.

Regional Note — Local news sources vary in reach

Local news and community reporting, as referenced in this chapter's reading, may come from a neighborhood association, a municipal office, a local newspaper, or an online community group, depending on the area. Some communities rely heavily on printed newsletters, while others share similar information mainly online, so checking more than one source is often useful practical advice.

Register Note — Distinguishing direct and secondhand information

Notice that Emma carefully distinguishes what Tanaka-san and Sato-san told her directly, using と言っていました, from what she only read or heard from another source, using そうです and らしい. Keeping this distinction clear is considered good practice in both spoken reporting and written accounts, since it lets the listener or reader judge how reliable each piece of information is.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Using と言っていました for your own opinion

Error: *私はイベントがよかったと言っていました。 using と言っていました to describe the speaker's own current opinion.

Correction: 私はイベントがよかったと思います。 — と言っていました reports someone else's past statement; for your own opinion, use と思います instead, as taught in Chapter 13.

Common Mistake — Confusing hearsay そうです with the appearance-based そうです

Error: Treating 忙しそうです (it looks busy, based on appearance) as equivalent to 忙しいそうです (I heard that it is busy).

Correction: These are two different constructions with different connection patterns: 忙し + そうです (dropping い) describes an appearance-based guess, while 忙しい + そうです (keeping the plain form) reports something heard from a source. Check the form directly before the そうです carefully.

Common Mistake — Repeating a name in every sentence of a topic chain

Error: *タナカさんは取材に答えました。タナカさんはイベントについて話しました。タナカさんは満足していると言っていました。 repeating タナカさん in every sentence.

Correction: タナカさんは取材に答えました。イベントについて詳しく話してくれました。満足していると言っていました。 — once the topic is established, later sentences can omit the name; repeating it in every sentence sounds unnatural and overly explicit in connected Japanese.

Practical Performance Task

話をまとめる — An interview report

Interview a real or imagined person about a recent event, trip, or experience. Complete all four components below.

しなければならないことObligations and Needs

Obligation, necessity, permission, and plans · Speak and Write Japanese: Everyday Independence and Connected Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Talk about appointments, chores, deadlines, rules, forms, and things you need.
  2. Say what you must do using「なければなりません」.
  3. Say what you do not have to do using「なくてもいいです」.
  4. Say what is permitted using「てもいいです」and what is prohibited using「てはいけません」.
  5. State that something is necessary using「必要です」.
  6. Ask about and confirm obligations, permissions, and requirements in a short exchange.
  7. Read and write a short connected text about a set of obligations, such as a to-do list or a set of rules.

Communication Scenario

Yuki's schedule has gotten busier: her host family has given her a list of household chores, her school has upcoming deadlines for a form and a project, and a study-abroad office has sent updated rules about attendance and paperwork. As Yuki and Emma continue their messages, Yuki needs to sort through what she absolutely must do, what she does not need to worry about, what she is allowed to do, and what is not allowed — and Emma wants to understand which parts of Yuki's week are fixed and which are flexible.

  1. This chapter expands your ability to describe obligations, optional tasks, permissions, and prohibitions connected to appointments, chores, deadlines, rules, and forms.
  2. By the end of the chapter, you will complete a performance task that includes a written to-do list or set of rules, a spoken explanation of your own obligations, and a listening task based on a short set of instructions.

Pronunciation Review

Pronunciation Focus — Long vowels and gemination in new vocabulary

Several new words in this chapter contain long vowels and geminate consonants that are easy to shorten when speaking quickly. Practice these contrasts before the Model Dialogue:

  • Long vowel: きそく (rule) — hold the long おう-type vowel quality steady across the word
  • Geminate consonant: しめきり (deadline) does not contain a small っ, but compare it carefully with せきにん (responsibility), which contains a moraic ん that must keep its own full beat
  • Long vowel: ひつよう (necessary) — keep the long よう distinct from a short よ

Read the vocabulary tables above aloud slowly, then again at a natural pace, checking that each long vowel and each moraic ん keeps its full timing.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter authorizes 「なければなりません」for obligation, 「なくてもいいです」for lack of obligation, 「てもいいです」for permission, 「てはいけません」for prohibition, and 「必要です」for stating necessity. No additional grammar paradigm is introduced.

1. Stating obligation with 「なければなりません」

Purpose: to say that something must be done. This pattern is built from the negative plain form of a verb, with ない changed to なければ, followed by なりません.

  • 今日中に申込書を出さなければなりません。I must submit the application form by the end of today.
  • 毎週、ゴミを出さなければなりません。I have to take out the garbage every week.

なければなりません is built from the negative plain form: 出す → 出さない → 出さなければなりません. This pattern will be reviewed in more detail in later chapters as more verb types are added.

2. Stating lack of obligation with 「なくてもいいです」

Purpose: to say that something does not need to be done. This pattern also begins from the negative plain form of a verb.

  • 今週は掃除をしなくてもいいです。I don't have to do the cleaning this week.
  • サインはまだしなくてもいいです。I don't have to sign it yet.

3. Permission and prohibition with 「てもいいです」and「てはいけません」

Purpose: to say what is allowed and what is not allowed. Both patterns build on the て-form reviewed in Chapters 1 through 3.

  • この規則を読んでもいいですか。May I read this rule? (asking permission)
  • はい、読んでもいいです。Yes, you may read it.
  • 締め切りの後で提出してはいけません。You may not submit it after the deadline.

てもいいですか is commonly used to ask for permission, and てはいけません is a fairly direct way to state a prohibition. In institutional contexts such as school or host-family rules, てはいけません is natural; in casual conversation, softer alternatives exist but are not introduced in this chapter.

4. Stating necessity with 「必要です」

Purpose: to say that something is necessary, often used with nouns rather than verbs.

  • 申込書にサインが必要です。A signature is necessary on the application form.
  • この用事には時間が必要です。This errand requires time.

必要です attaches to a noun with が, describing what is needed for a situation, rather than describing an action directly.

Common Mistake — Confusing なくてもいいです and てはいけません

Error: *「今週は掃除をしてはいけません」to mean "I don't have to clean this week."

Correction: 今週は掃除をしなくてもいいです。 — なくてもいいです means an action is not required; てはいけません means an action is forbidden. These are two very different meanings.

Building Block — A short obligations account
Situation statement + obligation with なければなりません + optional task with なくてもいいです + permission or prohibition with てもいいです/てはいけません
A short account of obligations can follow this shape: name the situation, state what must be done, mention something that does not need to be done, and add a note about what is or is not allowed.
今週は締め切りが多いです。今日中に申込書を提出しなければなりません。でも、サインはまだしなくてもいいです。締め切りの後で提出してはいけません。
There are a lot of deadlines this week. I have to submit the application form by the end of today. But I don't have to sign it yet. I may not submit it after the deadline.

Grammar Review Practice

  1. Write one sentence using なければなりません to describe an obligation.
  2. Write one sentence using なくてもいいです to describe something you do not need to do.
  3. Write one sentence using てもいいです to ask for or give permission.
  4. Write one sentence using てはいけません to describe a prohibition.
  5. Write one sentence using 必要です to describe something necessary.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Yuki and Emma message each other about Yuki's household chores and an upcoming school deadline.

エマ 今週は忙しいですか。 Are you busy this week?
ユキ はい、少し。ホストファミリーの規則で、毎週、ゴミを出さなければなりません。 Yes, a little. By my host family's rules, I have to take out the garbage every week.
エマ そうですか。掃除もしなければなりませんか。 I see. Do you have to do the cleaning too?
ユキ いいえ、掃除は今週しなくてもいいです。それより、学校の申込書のほうが大変です。 No, I don't have to do the cleaning this week. More than that, the school application form is harder.
エマ 申込書ですか。何が必要ですか。 The application form? What's necessary for it?
ユキ サインが必要です。それに、今日中に提出しなければなりません。締め切りの後で提出してはいけないんです。 A signature is necessary. Also, I have to submit it by the end of today. I'm not allowed to submit it after the deadline.
エマ 大変ですね。がんばってください。 That sounds tough. Good luck.

ないようりかい — Comprehension questions:

  1. What must Yuki do every week according to her host family's rules?
  2. Does Yuki have to do the cleaning this week?
  3. What is necessary for the school application form, and when must Yuki submit it?

Writing Workshop

In this workshop you will write a short to-do list or set of rules, using the Building Block pattern from the Grammar section.

  1. Name the situation in one sentence, such as a busy week, a household, or a class.
  2. State one obligation, using なければなりません.
  3. State one optional task, using なくてもいいです.
  4. Add one permission or prohibition, using てもいいです or てはいけません.
  5. Revise and check using the checklist below before finalizing your text.

チェックリスト — Editing checklist:

  • I named the situation clearly in my first sentence.
  • I used なければなりません correctly to describe an obligation.
  • I used なくてもいいです correctly to describe something optional.
  • I used てもいいです or てはいけません correctly to describe permission or prohibition.
  • I did not confuse なくてもいいです with てはいけません.
  • I kept one consistent register throughout.
  • I kept furigana on 規則, 必要, 締め切り, 提出, and this chapter's Chapter 2 and Chapter 3 kanji, and removed it from consolidated Volume I kanji.
  • I ended each sentence with 。 and used consistent script and punctuation throughout.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Household and institutional rules vary widely

Expectations about chores, quiet hours, guests, and shared responsibilities differ from household to household and from institution to institution in Japan, just as they do elsewhere. A host family's rules, a school's requirements, and a workplace's policies each reflect their own local practice rather than a single nationwide standard, and confirming expectations directly with the people involved is normal and expected.

Regional Note — Directness in stating rules

The directness of てはいけません can vary by relationship and setting; some communities and speakers prefer softer alternatives for less serious rules, while official notices, signs, and formal household rules commonly use direct forms like the ones in this chapter. This chapter introduces the direct pattern first because it is the most transparent for beginning independent learners.

Register Note — Obligation language across settings

Yuki's polite explanation to Emma and the plain-style household rules in the Reading Practice section both use the same core obligation and permission patterns, but the choice of polite or plain style depends on the audience and the type of text, not on the grammar itself. Later chapters in this volume will continue to develop this distinction.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Confusing なくてもいいです and てはいけません

Error: *今週は掃除をしてはいけません。 (used to mean "I don't have to clean this week").

Correction: 今週は掃除をしなくてもいいです。 — なくてもいいです describes something that is not required; てはいけません describes something that is forbidden.

Common Mistake — Forgetting the negative base before なければなりません

Error: *出すなければなりません。 (attaching なければなりません directly to the dictionary form).

Correction: 出さなければなりません。 — なければなりません attaches to the negative plain form of the verb (出す → 出さない → 出さなければなりません), not to the dictionary form.

Common Mistake — Using 必要です like an English verb "need"

Error: *わたしは必要ですサインを。 (treating 必要です like a transitive verb with an object).

Correction: サインが必要です。 — 必要です describes a noun marked with が as necessary; it is not used like an English verb that takes a direct object.

Practical Performance Task

This performance task draws together the systems developed in this chapter. Complete all three components below.

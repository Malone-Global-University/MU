これまでの経験Life Experience

Narration, experience, and reflection · Speak and Write Japanese: Everyday Independence and Connected Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Talk about things you have and have not experienced, using expanded 「ことがある」.
  2. Describe a change that happened over time, using 「ようになった」.
  3. Express an ongoing personal belief or intention, using 「と思っている」.
  4. Connect a series of past events into a smooth narrative using narrative connectors such as 「そして」、「それから」、「その後」、and 「〜てから」.
  5. Read and listen to short accounts of personal history and reflect on your own experiences in Japanese.
  6. Recognize and produce kanji for experience, life, history, challenge, growth, and reflection.
  7. Narrate a connected account of a personal experience, spoken and written, that reflects on what you learned from it.

Communication Scenario

With the community event now finished, Emma, Yuki, and their volunteer team gather for a casual debrief. Someone suggests that everyone share a memorable personal experience related to volunteering, travel, study, or a challenge they once faced. As the conversation moves from the event itself into life stories, the group naturally begins comparing what they have and have not tried, describing how they changed over time, and reflecting on what they learned. This chapter opens Unit IV by moving from managing a single project into narrating a life story — the first step toward the connected, independent Japanese that will carry through to the end of the volume.

  1. Someone asks whether another person has ever done something unusual or difficult.
  2. A speaker describes how their feelings or habits changed after an experience, using 「ようになった」.
  3. The group reflects together on what they learned, using connectors to link the events of a story in order.

Script Note — Okurigana in 振り返る and related verbs

The verb 振り返る (ふりかえる, to look back on) keeps the okurigana り after the kanji 振, distinguishing the stem from the ending that changes in conjugation: 振り返ります、振り返って、振り返った. Write the full word, kanji plus okurigana, rather than dropping either part. The same principle applies to 乗り越える (のりこえる, to overcome), which keeps the okurigana り after 乗.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Long vowels and moraic ん in narrative vocabulary

Several words in this chapter combine long vowels and moraic ん in the same word. Practice the following pairs carefully:

  • 経験 (けいけん): a long vowel in the first syllable (けい) followed by moraic ん; keep both distinct from a short け and a plain な-row consonant.
  • 人生 (じんせい): moraic ん in the first syllable, then a long vowel in せい; do not shorten either.
  • 成長 (せいちょう): two long vowels in a row (せい and ちょう); keep phrase rhythm even rather than rushing through the second one.

When narrating a story aloud, maintain steady phrase-level rhythm across longer sentences, rather than speeding up toward the end of a long clause.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter develops three systems for narrating personal experience: expanded 「ことがある」 for past experience, 「ようになった」 for change over time, and 「と思っている」 for an ongoing belief or intention. It also introduces narrative connectors that link a sequence of past events smoothly.

1. Expanded ことがある — talking about experience

Purpose: to say whether you have or have not had a particular experience, using the plain past form of a verb plus ことがある.

  • 日本に住んだことがあります。I have lived in Japan (before).verb (plain past) + ことがあります
  • 一人で旅行したことがありません。I have never traveled alone.
  • こんなに大変な挑戦をしたことがありますか。Have you ever taken on such a difficult challenge?

This pattern was introduced formulaically in earlier chapters; in Chapter 16 it becomes fully productive, meaning learners are expected to form it independently with a wide range of verbs, not just memorized examples.

2. ようになった — describing a change over time

Purpose: to describe how a situation, ability, or habit changed from one state to another, usually as the result of an experience.

  • その経験をしてから、日本語で話せるようになりました。After having that experience, I became able to speak in Japanese.
  • 失敗したきっかけで、もっと注意するようになりました。Because of the failure, I came to be more careful.

ようになった most often follows a potential form (話せる) or a verb describing a habit or state (注意する). It marks a change, not a single completed action; contrast it with simple past forms, which describe one event.

3. と思っている — an ongoing belief or intention

Purpose: to express a belief, plan, or intention that the speaker holds over time, rather than a thought that occurred only once.

  • その経験のおかげで、もっと成長したいと思っています。Thanks to that experience, I've been thinking I want to grow more.
  • 来年、また挑戦しようと思っています。I'm planning to try again next year.

と思っている describes a standing belief or plan; と思う, reviewed from Chapter 13, describes an opinion at the moment of speaking. Both remain productive, but they answer slightly different questions: 「今、何を思いますか」 versus 「最近、何を考えていますか」.

4. Narrative connectors — linking a sequence of past events

Purpose: to connect a series of past events into a smooth, ordered narrative.

  • 大学を卒業してから、一年間、外国で働きました。After graduating from university, I worked abroad for a year.
  • 最初は不安でした。それから、少しずつ慣れました。At first I was anxious. After that, I gradually got used to it.
  • 友達ができました。そして、日本語が好きになりました。I made friends. And I came to like Japanese.
  • その後、日本語の勉強を続けています。After that, I have continued studying Japanese.

「てから」links two clauses within one sentence and emphasizes the first action as a starting point; 「それから」、「そして」、and 「その後」 link separate sentences within a narrative. Vary these connectors rather than repeating the same one throughout a story.

Building Block — A short life-experience narrative
time/starting point (当時／〜てから) → an experience (ことがある／初めて) → a change (ようになった) → a reflection or ongoing belief (と思っている)
A natural personal narrative moves from setting the scene, to describing what happened, to explaining how it changed the speaker, to reflecting on what it means now. This chapter's Model Dialogue and Performance Task ask you to move through all four stages in order.
大学生の当時、初めて一人で旅行したことがあります。最初は不安でしたが、それから、いろいろな人と話すようになりました。その経験のおかげで、もっといろいろな場所に挑戦したいと思っています。
Back when I was a university student, I traveled alone for the first time. At first I was anxious, but after that, I came to talk with all kinds of people. Thanks to that experience, I've been thinking I want to try more places.

Model Dialogue

Setting: After the event, Emma, Yuki, and サトウさん relax over tea and share personal stories.

ユキ エマさんは、外国でボランティアをしたことがありますか。 Emma, have you ever volunteered abroad?
エマ はい、あります。大学生の当時、初めて一人でアジアに行ったことがあります。 Yes, I have. Back when I was a university student, I went to Asia alone for the first time.
サトウ それは、どんな経験けいけんでしたか。 What kind of experience was that?
エマ 最初は言葉が分からなくて、大変でした。それから、少しずつ地域の人と話すようになりました。その挑戦ちょうせんのおかげで、いろいろな考え方を感かんじるようになりました。 At first I didn't understand the language, and it was hard. After that, I gradually came to talk with the local people. Thanks to that challenge, I came to feel different ways of thinking.
ユキ その経験けいけんを振ふり返かえってみて、今、どう思おもっていますか。 Looking back on that experience, what do you think now?
エマ あの経験けいけんがあったから、もっと成長せいちょうしたいと思おもっています。だから、来年、また新しい国に挑戦ちょうせんしようと思おもっています。 Because I had that experience, I've been thinking I want to grow more. So next year, I'm planning to challenge myself with a new country again.
サトウ すごいですね。私も、今回のボランティアのおかげで、地域の活動に興味を持つようになりました。 That's amazing. Thanks to this volunteering too, I came to take an interest in community activities.

ないようりかい — Comprehension questions:

  1. What experience did Emma have as a university student?
  2. How did Emma change as a result of that experience, and what does she now think or plan to do?
  3. How did Satō change as a result of this volunteer project?

Writing Workshop

In this workshop you will write a short connected personal narrative that combines experience, change, and reflection, following the Building Block pattern from the Grammar section.

  1. Set the scene by naming a time in your life, using 当時 or a similar time expression.
  2. Describe an experience, using ことがある or 初めて.
  3. Describe how you changed because of that experience, using ようになった.
  4. Reflect on what you think or plan now, using と思っている.
  5. Connect your sentences smoothly, using at least two different narrative connectors, such as てから、それから、そして、or その後.
  6. Revise and check using the checklist below before finalizing your text.

チェックリスト — Editing checklist:

  • My text sets a time or starting point for the narrative.
  • My text describes an experience using ことがある or 初めて.
  • My text describes a change using ようになった.
  • My text reflects on the experience using と思っている.
  • My text uses at least two different narrative connectors to link the events in order.
  • I kept furigana on this chapter's new kanji and followed the reduction schedule for earlier kanji.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Sharing personal stories in group settings

Sharing a personal experience within a group, as the volunteer team does in this chapter's dialogue, is common in many settings in Japan, including workplaces, schools, and community organizations, though the amount of personal detail considered appropriate can vary by relationship, setting, and individual comfort. A reflective closing comment, such as 「その経験のおかげで成長したと思っています」, is often valued as a way of connecting a personal story to a broader lesson, but this style of reflection is not required or universal.

Regional Note — Life-stage milestones vary by community

Milestones such as graduating, starting a first job, or traveling abroad for the first time are common topics for personal narratives, but the typical age, order, and significance of these milestones vary across regions, generations, and individual families in Japan, just as they do elsewhere. Avoid assuming that any one life path is the standard one.

Register Note — Reflective language in casual conversation

The Model Dialogue in this chapter uses reflective language such as 振り返ってみて and と思っています within an otherwise casual, polite conversation among colleagues who have become friends. This kind of reflective turn is a natural feature of many everyday conversations, not only of formal speeches or written essays, and it remains comfortably in ordinary です／ます style.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Using ことがある for a single specific past event

Error: *昨日、映画を見たことがあります。 using ことがある to describe something that happened once, at a specific, recent time.

Correction: 昨日、映画を見ました。 — use the plain past tense plus ことがある only for general life experience, not for a single event at a known, specific time; use the ordinary past tense for that instead.

Common Mistake — Confusing ようになった with a simple past action

Error: *日本語を勉強するようになりました。 intended to mean simply "I studied Japanese," but ようになった always implies a change from a previous state.

Correction: 日本語を勉強するようになりました。 is correct only if it means "I came to study Japanese (when I did not before)." If the intended meaning is simply "I studied Japanese," use 日本語を勉強しました。 instead.

Common Mistake — Narrating events with no connectors

Error: *旅行に行きました。友達に会いました。日本語が好きになりました。 presenting three events with no connectors linking them.

Correction: 旅行に行ってから、友達に会いました。それから、日本語が好きになりました。 — as this chapter teaches, connectors such as てから、それから、and そして keep a personal narrative clear and connected for a listener or reader.

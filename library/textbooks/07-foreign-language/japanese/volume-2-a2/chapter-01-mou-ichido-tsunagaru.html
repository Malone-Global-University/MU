もう一度つながるReconnecting and Review Bridge

An A2 consolidation chapter that reactivates Volume I Japanese and opens Volume II · Speak and Write Japanese: Everyday Independence and Connected Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Reintroduce yourself and give an updated account of your recent life, using Volume I systems with greater confidence.
  2. Ask about and describe changes since you last spoke with someone, using expressions such as 「お元気でしたか」and「ひさしぶり」.
  3. Recognize the difference between polite です／ます style and plain style, and identify which one a speaker is using and why.
  4. Connect two or more sentences about recent events using て-form linking and simple discourse connectors.
  5. Read a short reconnection message or profile update written in mixed script with reduced furigana on consolidated Volume I kanji.
  6. Write a short self-update message that could be sent to a teacher, host family, or friend after time apart.
  7. Identify your own strengths and goals from Volume I as you begin Volume II.

Communication Scenario

It has been a few months since Yuki last saw her pen pal Emma, who studied Japanese with Speak and Write Japanese: Foundations of Practical Communication. Emma is joining a new online conversation group with learners and native speakers from several countries, and everyone is asked to reconnect with a partner from a past exchange before the first meeting. Emma messages Yuki to say hello again, ask how she has been, and share a short update about her own life. This chapter does not introduce a large new topic. Instead, it asks you to reactivate everything from Volume I so that you can move forward with confidence into new material.

  1. This chapter reviews and reactivates vocabulary, kanji, and grammar from Volume I, especially self-introduction, daily life, and simple past narration.
  2. By the end of the chapter, you will complete a performance task that includes a written reconnection message, a short spoken update, and a polite/plain recognition check.

Pronunciation Review

Pronunciation Focus — Rebuilding steady mora timing after a break

After time away from speaking, learners often let mora timing slip, especially with long vowels and moraic ん. Before the Model Dialogue, review these contrasts:

  • Long vowels: ゆうき (a name) versus ゆき (snow) — listen for the extra mora
  • Moraic ん held for a full beat, as in れんらく and げんき
  • Phrase-level rhythm in a greeting exchange, where falling intonation closes a statement and rising intonation marks a question

Read the vocabulary tables above aloud slowly, then again at a natural pace, checking mora count rather than English-style stress.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter authorizes review of Volume I systems, stronger topic chaining across connected sentences, and recognition of the polite/plain contrast as a system rather than a single rule. No new grammar paradigm is introduced.

1. Reconnecting and asking about time apart

Purpose: to greet someone after time apart and ask how they have been.

  • 久しぶりですね。お元気でしたか。It's been a while, hasn't it? How have you been?
  • はい、元気でした。相変わらず忙しいです。Yes, I've been well. Busy as always.

2. Connecting recent events with て-form and それから

Purpose: to link two or more recent events into one connected account, recycling the て-form from Volume I.

  • 学校が終わって、アルバイトを始めました。School ended, and I started a part-time job.
  • 日本語を勉強しました。それから、新しい友だちができました。I studied Japanese. After that, I made new friends.

3. Recognizing polite and plain style side by side

Purpose: to recognize when a speaker or writer has chosen polite です／ます style versus plain style, and to understand that the choice depends on relationship and setting rather than one form being more correct.

  • （ていねい）最近、忙しいです。(polite) I've been busy recently.
  • （普通）最近、忙しい。(plain) I've been busy recently.

Both sentences are grammatically correct Japanese. The first suits a message to a teacher, host family, or someone you do not know well. The second suits a diary entry or a message to a close friend. This chapter asks you to recognize the contrast; Volume II will expand your ability to produce plain forms deliberately in later chapters.

Common Mistake — Treating plain style as simply "the casual version" with no rule

Error: Assuming plain forms are always rude and polite forms are always safe, in every context.

Correction: Polite style is the safer default with teachers, host families, and people you have just reconnected with, such as Emma writing to Yuki's family. Plain style is not rude by itself; it is simply the expected choice among close friends, in diaries, and in certain informal written genres. Choose based on relationship and setting, not on a fixed judgment about politeness.

Building Block — A four-part reconnection message
Greeting after time apart + one or two recent events, connected + a question back to the reader + a closing wish to stay in touch
A short reconnection message can follow this shape: open with 久しぶり and a question about how the reader has been, share one or two connected recent events using て-form or それから, ask a question back, and close with a wish to stay in touch, all in polite です／ます style since the relationship is not fully close yet.
久しぶりです。お元気でしたか。わたしは元気です。学校が終わって、アルバイトを始めました。それから、日本語のクラスも続けています。エマさんの近況も聞きたいです。これからもよろしくお願いします。
It's been a while. How have you been? I've been well. School ended, and I started a part-time job. Also, I'm continuing my Japanese class. I'd like to hear your recent news too. I hope we can keep in touch.

Grammar Review Practice

  1. Write one sentence greeting someone after time apart, using 久しぶり.
  2. Write two connected sentences about something that happened recently, using て-form or それから.
  3. Rewrite one of your sentences in the other style (polite to plain, or plain to polite), and note which setting each version fits.
  4. Write one question you could ask a reconnecting partner about their recent life.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Yuki calls Emma on a video chat for the first time in several months.

エマ ユキさん、久しぶりですね。お元気でしたか。 Yuki, it's been a while, hasn't it? How have you been?
ユキ はい、元気でした。相変わらず忙しいですが、楽しいです。エマさんは？ Yes, I've been well. Busy as always, but it's fun. How about you, Emma?
エマ わたしも元気です。学校が終わって、新しいアルバイトを始めました。 I'm well too. School ended, and I started a new part-time job.
ユキ そうですか。日本語の勉強は続けていますか。 Is that right? Are you continuing your Japanese studies?
エマ はい、続けています。新しい会話グループに入って、いろいろな国の人と話しています。 Yes, I'm continuing. I joined a new conversation group and I'm talking with people from various countries.
ユキ それはいいですね。これからも連絡しましょう。 That's great. Let's keep in touch from now on too.

ないようりかい — Comprehension questions:

  1. How does Emma describe her recent life change?
  2. What has Yuki been doing that keeps her busy?
  3. Why has Emma joined a new conversation group?

Writing Workshop

In this workshop you will write a short reconnection message of your own, using the four-part Building Block pattern from the Grammar section.

  1. Choose a reader. Decide whether you are writing to a teacher, a host family, or a friend you have not spoken with in a while, and choose polite or plain style accordingly.
  2. Write an opening greeting, using 久しぶり and a question about how the reader has been.
  3. Write one or two connected sentences about your own recent life, using て-form linking or それから.
  4. Write one question back to the reader about their recent news.
  5. Write a closing sentence expressing a wish to stay in touch.
  6. Revise and check using the checklist below before finalizing your message.

チェックリスト — Editing checklist:

  • I greeted the reader clearly after time apart.
  • I chose one consistent style (polite or plain) appropriate to my reader and kept it throughout.
  • I connected at least two events using て-form or それから rather than listing separate sentences.
  • I asked at least one question back to the reader.
  • I closed with a sentence about staying in touch.
  • I used furigana correctly, keeping it on kanji not yet consolidated and removing it from consolidated Volume I kanji.
  • I ended each sentence with 。 and used consistent script and punctuation throughout.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Reconnecting after time apart

When reconnecting after time apart, many Japanese speakers open with 「久しぶり」or the more formal「お久しぶりです」before moving to other topics, though the exact wording and warmth of a reconnection message can vary by relationship, generation, and platform.

Regional Note — One family, one set of habits

Yuki's family's habits around messaging, phone calls, and video chats reflect one household's practice. Other families and communities in Japan may prefer different platforms, timing, or degrees of formality when reconnecting. Later chapters in this unit will widen this picture.

Register Note — Why the same message can look different

Compare the polite written message to Yuki's family with the plain spoken voice message to Yuki herself. Both come from Emma and describe the same events, but the register shifts because the relationship and setting shift. This is normal in Japanese and will become a more explicit focus later in this volume.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Mixing polite and plain forms in one message

Error: *久しぶり。お元気でしたか。最近、忙しい。

Correction: 久しぶりです。お元気でしたか。最近、忙しいです。 — Keep one consistent style across a written message unless you are quoting someone else's words.

Common Mistake — Listing recent events without connecting them

Error: *学校が終わりました。アルバイトを始めました。会話グループに入りました。

Correction: 学校が終わって、アルバイトを始めました。それから、会話グループに入りました。 — Use て-form linking or それから to connect events into one account rather than presenting a flat list of separate sentences.

Common Mistake — Assuming furigana should stay on every kanji

Error: Adding furigana to 元気, 生活, and other consolidated Volume I kanji out of habit.

Correction: Check the Furigana / Reading-Support Matrix before deciding. Consolidated kanji from Volume I no longer need furigana in Volume II unless the specific word or reading is new, rare, or a name.

Practical Performance Task

パフォーマンスタスク

もう一度つながる — Reconnecting after time apart

This performance task draws together the systems reactivated in this chapter. Complete all three components below.

  • Written reconnection message: Write a message of at least six sentences to a chosen reader (teacher, host family, or friend), including a greeting after time apart, at least two connected recent events, one question back to the reader, and a closing wish to stay in touch, in one consistent register.

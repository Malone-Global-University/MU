人間関係Relationships and Social Life

Reference tracking, names/titles, and giving/receiving introduction · Speak and Write Japanese: Everyday Independence and Connected Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Talk about the people in your life using appropriate names, titles, and relationship terms.
  2. Describe invitations, messages, and social plans in more detail than in Chapter 2.
  3. Use「あげる」「くれる」「もらう」to describe giving and receiving from the correct point of view.
  4. Use expanded「〜てくれる」to describe a favor someone has done for you or for someone in your group.
  5. Mark the source or direction of an exchange using「に」and「から」.
  6. Track reference across several connected sentences about the same person without overusing names or pronouns.
  7. Read and write a short connected message about an invitation, a favor, or a small gift.

Communication Scenario

Yuki's birthday is coming up, and her online conversation partner Emma wants to send something small to mark the occasion. Meanwhile, Yuki mentions that a classmate has been helping her study for an upcoming test, and that her host family invited her to a small weekend gathering. As the two continue their messages, they need to talk clearly about who did what for whom — who invited someone, who helped someone, and who gave or received something — without confusing the people involved.

  1. This chapter expands your ability to describe relationships, invitations, favors, and small gifts, and to keep the people in a story straight as it develops.
  2. By the end of the chapter, you will complete a performance task that includes a written message about an invitation or favor, a spoken retelling of a small exchange, and a listening task based on a short account of a favor.

Pronunciation Review

Pronunciation Focus — Long vowels and moraic ん in new vocabulary

Several new words in this chapter contain long vowels and a moraic ん that are easy to shorten or drop when speaking quickly. Practice these contrasts before the Model Dialogue:

  • Long vowel: しょうたい (invitation) — hold the long おう sound for its full two morae
  • Moraic ん in かんけい (relationship) and れんらく (contact), held for a full beat before the next sound
  • Long vowel: ゆうじん (friend) — keep the long ゆう distinct from a short ゆ

Read the vocabulary tables above aloud slowly, then again at a natural pace, checking that each long vowel and each moraic ん keeps its full timing.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter authorizes 「あげる」「くれる」「もらう」as a set for describing giving and receiving, expanded 「〜てくれる」for describing a favor done for you or your group, 「に」and「から」for marking the source or direction of an exchange, and stronger reference omission across connected sentences about an exchange. No additional grammar paradigm is introduced.

1. Giving and receiving with 「あげる」「くれる」「もらう」

Purpose: to describe who gives something to whom, and who receives something from whom, while keeping the speaker's point of view clear. In this chapter, these three verbs are introduced together as a connected set rather than as three unrelated words for "give" and "receive."

  • わたしはユキさんにプレゼントをあげます。I give a present to Yuki. (I give to someone else.)
  • ユキさんはわたしにプレゼントをくれました。Yuki gave a present to me. (Someone gives to me or to my group.)
  • わたしはユキさんにプレゼントをもらいました。I received a present from Yuki. (I receive from someone else.)

Notice that あげる and くれる both describe giving, but the choice depends on whether the receiver is the speaker (or the speaker's group) or someone else. もらう describes the same exchange from the receiver's point of view.

2. Describing a favor with expanded 「〜てくれる」

Purpose: to describe someone doing something for you or for someone in your group, as a favor. This builds on the て-form reviewed in Chapters 1 and 2.

  • クラスメートが宿題を手伝ってくれました。A classmate helped me with my homework (as a favor to me).
  • 友人が駅まで来てくれました。A friend came to the station for me.

3. Marking source and direction with 「に」and「から」

Purpose: to mark clearly who an exchange comes from or goes to, especially with もらう.

  • 先生に日本語を習いました。I learned Japanese from my teacher. (person as source, marked with に)
  • 友人からメッセージをもらいました。I received a message from a friend. (person as source, marked with から)

With もらう, either に or から can mark the source of the exchange for a person. This chapter treats both as available options rather than choosing a single fixed rule.

4. Reference tracking across connected sentences

Purpose: to describe the same person across several sentences without repeating their name every time or overusing pronouns.

  • ユキさんは友人です。よく連絡をくれます。Yuki is a friend. She often sends me messages. (the subject of the second sentence is recoverable from context)
Common Mistake — Using あげる when the receiver is the speaker

Error: *「友人はわたしにプレゼントをあげました」to mean "my friend gave a present to me."

Correction: 友人はわたしにプレゼントをくれました。 — When the receiver is the speaker or someone in the speaker's group, use くれる rather than あげる, even though both verbs describe giving.

Building Block — A short exchange account
Relationship statement + favor or gift with てくれる/くれる/もらう + source or direction with に/から + brief reaction
A short account of an exchange can follow this shape: introduce the person and your relationship to them, describe what they did or gave using てくれる, くれる, or もらう, mark where the exchange came from with に or から if useful, and close with a brief reaction.
ユキさんはわたしの友人です。誕生日にプレゼントをくれました。友人からのプレゼントで、とてもうれしかったです。
Yuki is my friend. She gave me a present for my birthday. It was a present from a friend, and I was very happy.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Yuki and Emma message each other about Yuki's upcoming birthday and a recent favor from a classmate.

エマ 来週、誕生日ですよね。何かプレゼントをあげたいです。 Your birthday is next week, right? I want to give you a present.
ユキ ありがとうございます。実は、去年もエマさんにプレゼントをもらいました。 Thank you. Actually, I received a present from you last year too.
エマ そうでしたね。ところで、最近、クラスメートとの関係はどうですか。 That's right. By the way, how are things with your classmates lately?
ユキ いいですよ。テストの前に、友人が勉強を手伝ってくれました。とても助かりました。 Good. Before the test, a friend helped me study. It really helped.
エマ いい友人ですね。週末の集まりにも招待されましたか。 That's a good friend. Were you also invited to the weekend gathering?
ユキ はい、ホストファミリーから招待をもらいました。連絡はまだしていませんが、行くつもりです。 Yes, I received an invitation from my host family. I haven't gotten back to them yet, but I plan to go.

ないようりかい — Comprehension questions:

  1. What did Yuki receive from Emma last year?
  2. Who helped Yuki study before her test?
  3. Who invited Yuki to the weekend gathering?

Writing Workshop

In this workshop you will write a short message describing an invitation, a favor, or a small gift, using the Building Block pattern from the Grammar section.

  1. Introduce the person and your relationship to them in one sentence.
  2. Describe what they did or gave you, using てくれる, くれる, or もらう.
  3. Mark the source or direction of the exchange, using に or から, if it helps clarify who is involved.
  4. Add a brief reaction or plan, such as how you felt or what you intend to do next.
  5. Revise and check using the checklist below before finalizing your message.

チェックリスト — Editing checklist:

  • I introduced the person and my relationship to them clearly.
  • I chose あげる, くれる, or もらう correctly based on who received the gift or favor.
  • I used に or から accurately when marking a source, if needed.
  • I did not repeat the person's name more often than necessary.
  • I kept one consistent register throughout.
  • I kept furigana on 関係, 招待, 連絡, 友人, and this chapter's Chapter 2 kanji, and removed it from consolidated Volume I kanji.
  • I ended each sentence with 。 and used consistent script and punctuation throughout.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Small gifts and returned favors

Giving and receiving small gifts, such as souvenirs, seasonal presents, or thank-you items, is common among many people in Japan, and expectations about occasion, amount, and timing can vary by relationship, region, and individual family or workplace custom. The あげる/くれる/もらう system reflects how the exchange is described in language, not a single fixed social rule about when gifts must be given.

Regional Note — Relationship terms and closeness vary by community

Words like 友人 and 友だち, or the way someone refers to a classmate versus a close friend, can carry different nuances depending on age, school culture, workplace norms, and individual relationships. This chapter's vocabulary offers a starting point, not a universal scale of closeness.

Register Note — Describing favors to different audiences

Yuki's polite written reply to Emma and her plainer private reflection about the same favor describe similar facts, but the register shifts because the audience shifts from a friend she is writing to directly to a private account of her own thoughts. Volume II will continue to return to this kind of audience-based register shift in later chapters.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Confusing あげる and くれる

Error: *友人はわたしにプレゼントをあげました。 (using あげる when the receiver is the speaker).

Correction: 友人はわたしにプレゼントをくれました。 — Use くれる, not あげる, whenever the receiver is the speaker or someone in the speaker's group.

Common Mistake — Treating もらう like an English "give"

Error: *わたしはユキさんをプレゼントをもらいました。 (marking Yuki with を instead of に or から).

Correction: わたしはユキさんにプレゼントをもらいました。 — もらう describes receiving, so the source of the exchange is marked with に or から, and the item received is marked with を.

Common Mistake — Overusing names instead of tracking reference

Error: *ユキさんは友人です。ユキさんはよくユキさんにメッセージをくれます。 (repeating the name where the subject is already clear from context).

Correction: ユキさんは友人です。よく連絡をくれます。 — Once the subject is clear from context, it is more natural to omit it than to repeat the name.

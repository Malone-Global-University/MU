記事を読むReading Articles

Article structure and information extraction · Speak and Write Japanese: Independent Communication and Advanced Expression

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Recognize the typical structure of a feature article, interview, or public report, including headline, lead, body, and quoted commentary.
  2. Identify who said what in a text by tracking quotation chains and attribution phrases.
  3. Use によると and other source markers to introduce information drawn from a specific source.
  4. Extract main ideas and key details from moderately dense mixed-script texts with reduced furigana.
  5. Recognize common headline-style grammar and expand it into full sentences.
  6. Summarize an article orally and in writing, distinguishing the article's claims from your own reaction.
  7. Complete listening, speaking, reading, and writing tasks that draw on an article-like text.

Communication Scenario

Emma's community center newsletter often reprints short articles from a local paper about neighborhood events, technology in daily life, and community projects. Her supervisor has asked her to read a short feature article about a new community recycling program, identify the main points and who is quoted, and then summarize it for volunteers who prefer to hear the highlights rather than read the whole article themselves. This chapter's Practical Performance Task asks you to read an article, track its sources and quotations, and produce a clear oral and written summary.

  1. The reader identifies the headline, lead sentence, and overall topic of an article before reading in detail.
  2. The reader tracks who is being quoted or cited at each point in the article, using attribution phrases and によると.
  3. The reader distinguishes reported facts, quoted opinions, and the article's own framing from one another.
  4. The reader produces an oral or written summary that accurately reflects the article's content and sources.

Script Strategy — Reading dense mixed-script paragraphs

Feature articles pack more information into each sentence than the conversational texts of earlier chapters. Rather than reading word by word, first scan the headline and the first sentence of each paragraph to build a rough map of the article before reading closely for detail.

When you meet an unfamiliar kanji compound, check whether its components are already familiar from earlier chapters before assuming the word is entirely new; many article-style compounds combine known kanji in new combinations.

Pronunciation and Listening Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Reading formal written Japanese aloud

Formal written Japanese, including article-style text, often uses longer noun phrases and fewer pauses than ordinary conversation. When reading such text aloud, group words by phrase rather than by individual word, and pause briefly at commas and clause boundaries rather than at every particle.

  • 市の担当者によると (しのたんとうしゃによると): read as one phrase group, without a pause after 担当者.
  • 住民からの取材で分かった: keep とりくみ-style long compounds evenly paced, without dropping any mora.

Practice reading the Reading Assessment passage aloud at a natural, moderate pace, grouping phrases rather than individual words.

Grammar: Attribution and Source Markers

This chapter authorizes headline grammar recognition, quotation chains, attribution phrases, によると, and other source markers, building directly on the quotation and clause-linking systems developed earlier in this volume.

Headline grammar recognition

Purpose: to recognize the compressed, noun-heavy style used in Japanese headlines, and to expand a headline into a full sentence when reading the article body.

  • 市、リサイクル拡大へCity to expand recycling (headline style)headline drops particles and predicate endings; へ suggests future direction
  • 市がリサイクルの取り組みを拡大する予定です。The city plans to expand its recycling initiative.expanded sentence with particles and predicate restored

Recognition only: learners are not expected to produce headline-style compression, only to recognize it and expand it into ordinary sentences when reading.

Attribution and quotation chains

Purpose: to track who said or reported what across a passage, especially when a single article quotes more than one source.

  • 担当者は「参加者が増えている」と話しました。The person in charge said, "The number of participants is increasing."direct quotation + と話す attribution
  • ある住民は、この取り組みに賛成だと述べています。One resident has stated that they support this initiative.indirect quotation + と述べる attribution

When an article quotes several people, track each attribution phrase carefully; the opinion belongs to the person named, not to the article's author.

によると and other source markers

Purpose: to mark that a piece of information comes from a specific source rather than being the writer's own claim.

  • 市の発表によると、来月から新しいごみの分別が始まります。According to the city's announcement, new waste-sorting rules will begin next month.によると marks the source of the following information
  • 担当者の話では、参加者はすでに三百人を超えているそうです。According to the person in charge, participants have reportedly already exceeded three hundred.〜の話では as an alternative source marker, combined with そうです for hearsay

によると and 〜の話では both mark a source; そうです may combine with either to show that the information is reported rather than directly confirmed by the writer.

Building Block — Reading an article's source structure
headline (expanded) + source marker (によると / の話では) + reported information + quotation with attribution (と話す / と述べる)
A typical article opens with a headline that can be expanded into a full sentence, introduces information through a source marker, and then supports that information with one or more direct or indirect quotations, each clearly attributed to a named person or role.
市の発表によると、来月から新しいリサイクルの取り組みが始まります。担当者は「住民の協力がとても大切です」と話しました。ある住民は、この取り組みに賛成だと述べています。
According to the city's announcement, a new recycling initiative will begin next month. The person in charge said, "Residents' cooperation is very important." One resident has stated that they support this initiative.

Grammar Practice

  1. Expand the headline 住民、新しい取り組みに賛成 into a full sentence.
  2. Rewrite 「参加者が増えている」（担当者） as a direct-quotation sentence with correct attribution.
  3. Complete 市の発表＿＿＿＿＿、来月から新しい分別が始まります。 with the correct source marker.
  4. Identify whether the following sentence reports the writer's own claim or a quoted source, and explain why: 担当者の話では、参加者はすでに三百人を超えているそうです。
  5. Write one original sentence combining によると with a quotation and attribution.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Emma discusses a newsletter article about a new community recycling program with her supervisor, 田中さん.

田中さん エマさん、この記事はもう読みましたか。見出しだけでも分かりますか。 Emma, have you already read this article? Can you understand it even just from the headline?
エマ はい、見出しは「市、リサイクル拡大へ」でしたので、市がリサイクルの取り組みを拡大するという意味だと思います。 Yes, the headline was "City to expand recycling," so I think it means the city is expanding its recycling initiative.
田中さん その通りです。記事の中で、誰が話していましたか。 That's right. Who was quoted speaking in the article?
エマ 市の担当者によると、来月から新しい分別が始まるそうです。それから、担当者は「住民の協力がとても大切です」と話していました。 According to the city official, new sorting rules will reportedly start next month. Also, the official said, "Residents' cooperation is very important."
田中さん 住民の意見も紹介されていましたか。 Was a resident's opinion also introduced?
エマ はい、ある住民は、この取り組みに賛成だと述べていました。担当者と住民、両方の情報源が引用されていると思います。 Yes, one resident stated that they support the initiative. I think both the official and the resident are quoted as sources.

ないようりかい — Comprehension questions:

  1. What does the headline mean, and how did Emma expand it into a full sentence?
  2. According to the city official, what will begin next month?
  3. Who else is quoted in the article besides the city official?

Guided Practice

タスク１ — Headline expansion. Given three short headline-style phrases, expand each into a full sentence with particles and a predicate.

タスク２ — Source-tracking chart. Read a short passage provided by your instructor or partner and, for each sentence, note whether the information is the writer's own statement, a によると source, or a direct or indirect quotation, and who the source is.

タスク３ — Quotation identification. Underline every quotation and attribution phrase in a short passage, then state in your own words what each quoted person said.

タスク４ — Source marker substitution. Rewrite three sentences, replacing によると with 〜の話では or vice versa, keeping the meaning natural.

タスク５ — Building an article paragraph. Using the Building Block pattern from the Grammar section, write a short paragraph that includes a headline-style opening (expanded into a sentence), a source marker, and at least one quotation with attribution.

Writing Workshop

In this workshop you will draft the written portion of your Practical Performance Task: a summary of the Reading Practice article for volunteers who have not read it themselves.

  1. Reread the article in the Reading Practice section and note its main topic, sources, and quotations.
  2. State the main point of the article in one or two sentences, in your own words.
  3. Report the sources using at least one source marker, such as によると or 〜の話では.
  4. Include at least one quotation with correct attribution, either directly or indirectly.
  5. Add a brief closing sentence distinguishing the article's content from any reaction of your own, if you include one.
  6. Revise your summary using the checklist below.

チェックリスト — Editing checklist:

  • My summary states the article's main point clearly in my own words.
  • My summary includes at least one source marker used correctly.
  • My summary includes at least one quotation with correct attribution.
  • I did not confuse the article's claims with my own opinion.
  • I checked this chapter's kanji readings without relying on furigana where possible.
  • My summary would be understandable to a volunteer who has not read the original article.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Local news and community newsletters

Many neighborhoods, volunteer organizations, and municipal offices in Japan produce short community newsletters that summarize local announcements, initiatives, and resident opinions, often written in a style similar to this chapter's reading passage. Recognizing this genre helps learners identify similar community-level texts in other contexts.

Regional Note — Local reporting style and formality

The formality and structure of local newsletters and community reports can vary by region, organization, and publication. Some favor a more formal, article-like style similar to this chapter's passage, while others use a plainer, more conversational tone. The pattern taught here represents a common, moderately formal style rather than the only acceptable format.

Language Note — Attribution across languages

Many languages distinguish a writer's own claims from information attributed to someone else, though the specific markers differ. Comparing how your own language signals "according to" or introduces a quotation can help clarify why Japanese consistently marks sources with phrases such as によると rather than leaving attribution implicit.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Treating a quoted opinion as the writer's own claim

Error: Summarizing ある住民は、この取り組みに賛成だと述べています。 as "the article says the initiative is a good idea," losing the fact that this is one resident's quoted opinion, not the writer's own claim.

Correction: "According to the article, one resident supports the initiative." — always preserve who is speaking when summarizing a quoted or attributed statement.

Common Mistake — Dropping the source marker when reporting information

Error: *来月から新しい分別が始まります。 stated as a plain fact when the article actually attributes this to the city's announcement.

Correction: 市の発表によると、来月から新しい分別が始まります。 — keep the source marker when the original text attributes information to a specific source, rather than presenting it as an unattributed fact.

Common Mistake — Reading a headline literally without expanding it

Error: Misreading 市、リサイクル拡大へ as an incomplete or ungrammatical sentence, rather than recognizing it as headline-style compression.

Correction: Expand the headline mentally into 市がリサイクルの取り組みを拡大する予定です。 before continuing to read, rather than expecting headlines to follow ordinary sentence grammar.

Practical Performance Task

パフォーマンスタスク

記事を読む — Reading, tracking, and summarizing an article

Using the Reading Practice article or a similar short article-style text approved by your instructor, complete all four components below.

  • Source-tracking notes: List each source quoted or cited in the article, what each source said or reported, and which attribution or source-marking phrase introduces it.
  • Written summary: Write a summary of at least six sentences that states the article's main point, includes at least one source marker, and includes at least one quotation with correct attribution.
  • Spoken summary: With a partner, instructor, or recording, perform a thirty-to-sixty-second spoken summary of the same article, including at least one source marker and one quotation with attribution.
  • Peer or self-audit: Exchange your written summary with a partner, or review your own after a break, and check specifically that quoted opinions are not presented as your own claims and that source markers are used accurately.

Assessment focus: accurate identification of headline meaning; correct tracking of attribution across multiple sources; accurate and natural use of によると and related source markers; clear distinction between reported information and personal reaction; and comprehensible, accurately paced spoken summary.

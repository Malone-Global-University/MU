立場を調整するManaging Stance

Nuance, uncertainty, and interpersonal adjustment · Speak and Write Japanese: Independent Communication and Advanced Expression

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Recognize and use vocabulary for requests, proposals, feedback, apology, disagreement, and sensitive topics.
  2. Use かもしれない to hedge a claim as a possibility rather than a certainty.
  3. Use ようです to present an impression based on available evidence.
  4. Use みたいです to present an impression in a more conversational register.
  5. Distinguish the register differences among かもしれない, ようです, and みたいです.
  6. Use softening language to make a request, proposal, or piece of feedback less direct.
  7. Use repair language to clarify, restate, or adjust a statement that was misunderstood or too strong.
  8. Read and summarize a short passage in which a speaker adjusts stance while giving feedback or disagreeing politely.
  9. Speak and write about a sensitive interpersonal situation using only vocabulary and structures explicitly introduced or previously activated.

Communication Scenario

The volunteer meeting went well, and the data summary Emma prepared with 中村さん's help was well received. Afterward, another volunteer, 田中さん, has proposed a change to how the recycling project collects donations, and 中村さん has asked Emma for her honest opinion before the group decides. Emma has some concerns about the proposal but wants to raise them carefully, since 田中さん worked hard on the idea and the group values a comfortable atmosphere. Emma needs a way to propose a small adjustment, offer feedback without sounding too blunt, disagree gently, and soften her uncertainty about how well the new approach will actually work.

  1. The learner makes at least one hedged claim using かもしれない.
  2. The learner offers at least one impression using ようです or みたいです.
  3. The learner softens at least one request, proposal, or piece of feedback.
  4. The learner uses at least one repair expression to clarify or adjust a statement.
  5. The learner disagrees or raises a concern without sounding overly direct or confrontational.

Script Strategy — Reading softened statements as a unit

Softened statements often place the hedging expression at the very end of the sentence, as in 少し心配かもしれません. Practice reading such sentences by first identifying the core claim, then noticing how the final expression adjusts its strength, rather than translating word by word from left to right.

As with earlier chapters, learn each item such as 立場 or 調整 as a single lexical unit tied to its use in careful interpersonal communication, rather than attempting to derive its meaning from its individual characters alone.

Pronunciation and Listening Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Softened intonation on hedged statements

Hedged and softened statements are often spoken with a slightly falling, gentler intonation on the final hedging expression, rather than a strong, flat, or clipped ending. Practice letting phrases such as かもしれません and ようです trail off gently, which supports the cautious, non-confrontational meaning they carry.

  • この方法は、少し難しいかもしれません。 — this method may be a little difficult
  • 田中さんは、忙しいようです。 — it seems that Tanaka-san is busy
  • これで大丈夫みたいです。 — it seems like this is fine

Grammar: Softening and Adjusting Stance

This chapter authorizes かもしれない, ようです, and みたいです, together with hedging and softening language, repair expressions, and stance markers built on cautious-claim structures already active from Chapter 17. Together these let you propose, give feedback, disagree, and adjust an earlier statement without sounding overly blunt or overly certain.

かもしれない — hedging a claim as a possibility

Purpose: to present a statement as a possibility rather than a fact, softening its force.

  • この提案は、少し難しいかもしれません。This proposal may be a little difficult.い-adjective stem + かもしれません
  • 田中さんは、まだ気づいていないかもしれません。Tanaka-san may not have noticed yet.plain negative + かもしれません
  • 私の言い方が、少し強かったかもしれません。My way of speaking may have been a little too strong.past + かもしれません, self-directed hedge

かもしれない attaches to the plain form of verbs, adjectives, and nouns (with な dropped for な-adjectives and nouns). It is useful both for softening an opinion about someone else's idea and for softening a comment about one's own words.

ようです and みたいです — presenting an impression

Purpose: to present a conclusion as an impression based on what the speaker has seen, heard, or noticed, rather than as a confirmed fact.

  • 田中さんは、この提案に自信があるようです。It seems that Tanaka-san is confident about this proposal.plain form + ようです, neutral/formal register
  • みんな、少し心配しているみたいです。It seems like everyone is a little worried.plain form + みたいです, conversational register
  • この方法は、時間がかかるようです。It seems that this method takes time.verb plain form + ようです
Language Focus — Choosing between ようです and みたいです

ようです and みたいです both present an impression based on evidence, but they differ mainly in register. ようです fits neutral-to-formal spoken and written contexts, including meetings and reports. みたいです fits everyday conversation among friends, family, or familiar colleagues. Both attach to the plain form, though みたいです follows nouns and な-adjectives directly, without な.

Softening requests, proposals, and feedback

Purpose: to make a request, proposal, or piece of feedback feel less direct and more considerate of the listener.

Building Block — Softening a piece of feedback
[肯定的な点]。ただ、[心配・意見]かもしれません。[代わりの提案]、どうでしょうか。
Begin with something positive, add a hedged concern, then offer an alternative as a gentle question rather than a demand.
この提案、いいと思います。ただ、少し時間がかかるかもしれません。日にちを少し調整するのは、どうでしょうか。
I think this proposal is good. However, it may take a little time. How about adjusting the schedule a little?
  • 一つ、お願いしてもいいですか。May I ask you for one thing?softened request opener
  • 少し意見を言ってもいいですか。May I share a little feedback?softened feedback opener
  • もし可能なら、少し調整していただけますか。If possible, could you make a small adjustment?もし可能なら softens the request further

Repair language for clarifying or adjusting a statement

Purpose: to restate, clarify, or soften something already said, especially when it may have sounded too strong or was misunderstood.

  • すみません、言い方が悪かったです。Sorry, my phrasing was poor.repair opener + apology
  • そういう意味ではなくて、こう言いたかったんです。That's not what I meant — what I wanted to say was this.clarifying restatement
  • 少し言い直してもいいですか。May I rephrase that a little?repair request

Disagreeing gently

Purpose: to express a concern or disagreement without sounding confrontational, drawing on softening and hedging language together.

  • いいと思いますが、少し心配な点もあります。I think it's good, but I also have a small concern.concession + softened concern
  • 私は、少し違う意見かもしれません。My opinion may be a little different.hedged disagreement

  3. Write one softened request or piece of feedback using the Building Block pattern above.
  4. Write one short exchange in which a speaker uses repair language to clarify a statement that sounded too strong.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Emma speaks with 中村さん before the group discusses 田中さん's proposal about how donations are collected.

中村さん 田中さんの提案について、エマさんはどう思いますか。 What do you think about Tanaka-san's proposal?
エマ いい提案だと思います。ただ、少し心配な点もあります。 I think it's a good proposal. However, I also have a small concern.
中村さん そうですか。どんな点ですか。 Is that so. What kind of point?
エマ 新しい方法は、準備に時間がかかるかもしれません。それに、年配のボランティアの方には、少し難しいかもしれません。 The new method may take time to prepare. Also, it may be a little difficult for the older volunteers.
中村さん なるほど。田中さんに、そのまま伝えてもいいですか。 I see. Is it all right if I tell Tanaka-san that as it is?
エマ すみません、言い方が少し強かったかもしれません。田中さんは、この提案に自信があるようです。だから、批判ではなくて、調整の提案として伝えていただけますか。 Sorry, my phrasing may have been a little too strong. It seems that Tanaka-san is confident about this proposal. So could you convey it as a suggestion for adjustment rather than as criticism?
中村さん 分かりました。「準備の時間と、年配の方への説明を、少し調整しませんか」と伝えますね。 Understood. I'll convey it as "Shall we adjust the preparation time and the explanation for the older members a little?"
エマ それなら、田中さんも受け入れやすいと思います。ありがとうございます。 I think Tanaka-san will find that easier to accept. Thank you.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Softened feedback in community and group settings

In many Japanese community, volunteer, and workplace settings, feedback and disagreement are often introduced gradually, framed as a shared adjustment rather than as personal criticism, and preceded by acknowledgment of the other person's effort or intention. This chapter's softening patterns reflect one common way of managing this kind of interaction, not a rule that every speaker follows in every situation.

Regional Note — Variation in directness across groups and generations

How directly people raise concerns or disagree can vary by region, generation, relationship, and the specific group or organization involved. Some communities and individuals communicate more directly than the patterns modeled in this chapter, and directness itself is not a sign of poor social skill in every context.

Language Note — Hedging and softening across languages

Many languages have set ways of softening feedback or disagreement, such as English "it might be worth considering..." or "I could be wrong, but...". Comparing how your own language signals hesitation or diplomacy can help you notice that Japanese expressions such as かもしれません and ようです serve a similar interpersonal function, adjusted for Japanese social expectations.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Attaching ようです directly to a な-adjective or noun without だ

Error: *心配ようです。 drops the connecting な form required before ようです.

Correction: 心配なようです。 — な-adjectives take な before ようです, while nouns take の.

Common Mistake — Using みたいです in a formal meeting or written report

Error: *会議の報告書：問題があるみたいです。 uses a conversational hedge in a formal written context.

Correction: 会議の報告書：問題があるようです。 — reserve みたいです for conversational registers, and use ようです in more neutral or formal spoken and written contexts.

Common Mistake — Giving feedback with no softening at all

Error: *その提案は、時間がかかりすぎます。だめです。 states a concern as a flat, unsoftened rejection.

Correction: いいと思いますが、時間が少しかかるかもしれません。日にちを調整しませんか。 — pair a hedge with a positive opening and an alternative suggestion.

Looking Ahead

いまできること — What you can do now

You can hedge a claim using かもしれません, present an impression using ようです or みたいです with appropriate register, soften a request, proposal, or piece of feedback so it feels considerate rather than blunt, use repair language to clarify or adjust a statement, and disagree gently while acknowledging another person's effort. You can give careful feedback on a familiar proposal in both writing and speech.

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Read a feature article and extract its main information, attributed sources, and structure.
  2. Organize and deliver a short oral presentation with transitions, comparison language, and a Q&A response.
  3. Write a formal email or document using opening and closing formulas, request softening, and appropriate written style.
  4. Read and write a short personal narrative that uses plain style, a temporal shift, and a represented inner thought.
  5. Move between polite, plain, formal-written, and presentation registers appropriately within a single project.
  6. Identify attribution, quotation, and source markers across article, presentation, and narrative genres.
  7. Complete an integrated listening, speaking, reading, and writing assessment covering Chapters 11 through 14.

Communication Scenario

Emma's newsletter reflection about the recycling project storage room was well received, and the community center director asks her to help prepare a short end-of-unit update for the center's volunteers: a combination of an article summary, a two-minute spoken update, a short formal email confirming a follow-up meeting, and her personal narrative from Chapter 14, all drawn together into one small volunteer packet. This chapter's Unit III Performance Task asks you to demonstrate control of all four genres from this unit, article reading, oral presentation, formal writing, and personal narrative, using only the systems authorized through Chapter 14.

  1. The learner reads a short article and reports its main information accurately, including at least one attributed source.
  2. The learner delivers a short spoken update using presentation transitions and responds to at least one follow-up question.
  3. The learner writes a short formal email using correct opening and closing formulas and softened requests.
  4. The learner presents or revises a short personal narrative using plain style, a temporal shift, and an inner thought.

Pronunciation and Listening Review

Pronunciation Focus — Shifting prosody across genres

This unit has practiced several distinct prosodic patterns: the relatively even, informative pace of an article summary, the clearer and more deliberate pace of a presentation with pauses before transitions, the flatter and more careful reading of a formal email, and the softer, slightly slowed pace of a narrated inner thought. As you complete this chapter's Listening Assessment, listen for which of these four patterns is being used in each section, since the pattern itself is a clue to the genre.

  • 記事によると、参加者は前回より増えた。 even, informative pace — article-style reporting.
  • それでは、結果について発表します。 clear, deliberate pace with a pause before 結果 — presentation-style delivery.
  • これでいいのかな。 slower, softer, reflective pace — narrated inner thought.

Grammar: Four Genres, One Toolkit

This chapter authorizes assessed use of attribution and source marking (Chapter 11), presentation transitions and comparison language (Chapter 12), formal opening and closing formulas with request softening (Chapter 13), and narrative plain style with temporal shifts and inner thought (Chapter 14). No new grammar system is introduced; this section reorganizes Unit III's four grammar sets side by side so you can compare their register and function directly.

Comparing the four genres

GenreTypical registerKey markerFunction
Article (Ch. 11) 〜によると、〜そうだ Written, neutral-formal Attributes information to a source rather than the writer
Presentation (Ch. 12) それでは、〜について発表します Spoken, polite です／ます Signals a transition into a new part of a talk
Formal document (Ch. 13) よろしくお願いいたします Written, formal, polite Closes a request with conventional deference
Narrative (Ch. 14) 実は、〜前に〜／〜と思った Written, plain style Marks a shift in time or represents inner thought

Attribution across genres

Purpose: to recognize that attribution appears differently depending on genre, and to choose the right form for the right context.

  • 記事によると、参加者は増えている。According to the article, the number of participants is increasing.によると, written attribution, Chapter 11
  • アンケートの結果について発表します。I will present the survey results.について発表します, spoken presentation frame, Chapter 12
  • 佐藤さんからいただいたメールによりますと、鍵は倉庫にあるとのことです。According to the email I received from Sato-san, the key is said to be in the storage room.によりますと, formal written attribution, Chapter 13 register

Register switching within one project

Purpose: to move deliberately between polite spoken style, formal written style, and narrative plain style within a single multi-genre task, rather than mixing them by accident.

  • （発表）今日は、リサイクルプロジェクトについてお話しします。(Presentation) Today I will talk about the recycling project.polite spoken register
  • （メール）ご確認のほど、よろしくお願いいたします。(Email) Thank you for your confirmation.formal written register
  • （物語）あの朝のことは、今でもよく覚えている。(Narrative) I still remember that morning well, even now.plain narrative register

A single volunteer packet, like the one in this chapter's scenario, may legitimately contain all three registers, each used consistently within its own section. Switching registers between sections is expected; switching registers within one sentence or one section, without reason, is the pattern to avoid.

Building Block — A four-genre packet
article summary (によると) + spoken update (それでは／について発表します) + formal email (よろしくお願いいたします) + personal narrative (plain style + temporal shift + inner thought)
Each section keeps its own register and grammar consistent internally, while the packet as a whole moves deliberately across all four registers practiced in this unit.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Emma and the community center director, 中村さん, plan the volunteer packet together.

中村さん エマさん、ボランティア向けの資料を四つ、お願いできますか。記事の要約、発表、メール、それから物語です。 Emma, could I ask you for four items for the volunteer materials? An article summary, a presentation, an email, and a narrative.
エマ はい、大丈夫です。記事の要約はもう書いてあります。地域のリサイクルに関する記事によると、参加者が去年より増えているそうです。 Yes, that's fine. I've already written the article summary. According to an article about local recycling, the number of participants is said to have increased compared to last year.
中村さん いいですね。発表のほうはどうですか。 That's good. How about the presentation?
エマ それでは、結果について発表します、という文で始めるつもりです。グラフも一枚使う予定です。 I'm planning to start with the sentence, "Now, I will present the results." I'm also planning to use one chart.
中村さん 確認のメールもお願いしていいですか。来週の会議についてです。 Could I also ask for a confirmation email? It's about next week's meeting.
エマ 承知いたしました。よろしくお願いいたします、で締めくくります。物語のほうは、先週書いたものを少し直そうと思っています。 Understood. I'll close it with "Thank you very much." As for the narrative, I'm thinking of revising a little the one I wrote last week.
中村さん 四つとも、いい組み合わせになりそうですね。楽しみにしています。 All four sound like they'll make a good combination. I'm looking forward to it.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — One project, several documents

A single community project in Japan, as in many places, often generates several different kinds of writing: a news item summarizing it for outsiders, a presentation reporting results to insiders, formal correspondence confirming logistics, and, less often but not unusually, a personal reflection shared informally among participants. Recognizing which register a document requires, and switching cleanly between them, is itself a marker of communicative competence at this level.

Regional Note — Consistency within, flexibility across

Individual and regional variation exists within each genre, as noted in Chapters 11 through 14: presentation style can be more or less formal depending on the audience, and narrative voice can vary by writer. What generally stays consistent, across regions and individual styles, is that a single section of a single document tends to hold one register steadily, rather than mixing registers unpredictably within a paragraph.

Language Note — Genre awareness across languages

Most languages distinguish reporting, presenting, formal correspondence, and personal narrative through some combination of vocabulary, structure, and tone, even where the specific markers differ from Japanese. Reflecting on how your own language signals these four genres can help you notice why Japanese relies so heavily on specific fixed markers, such as によると or よろしくお願いいたします, to do work that other languages might do through tone alone.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Attributing an article's claim as your own opinion

Error: *参加者は増えている。 stated as a plain fact with no attribution, when the information actually came from a specific article.

Correction: 記事によると、参加者は増えている。 — mark the source clearly when reporting information you did not observe or verify yourself.

Common Mistake — Carrying presentation register into a formal email

Error: *それでは、来週の会議について発表します。 used as the opening of a written confirmation email, borrowing spoken presentation phrasing.

Correction: 来週の会議の日時と場所について確認させていただきたく、ご連絡いたしました。 — use written, formal-email phrasing for a written, formal-email genre.

Common Mistake — Slipping into polite style mid-narrative

Error: *あの朝のことは、今でもよく覚えています。 using polite です／ます inside an otherwise plain-style narrative passage.

Correction: あの朝のことは、今でもよく覚えている。 — keep narrative passages in plain style throughout, as established in Chapter 14.

Unit III Performance Task

情報と表現 — Assembling a four-genre volunteer packet

橋渡し：B1からB2へBridge to Independent Communication

B1 consolidation, discourse planning, and register audit · Speak and Write Japanese: Independent Communication and Advanced Expression

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Talk about your own Japanese learning: what feels secure, what still feels uncertain, and what a B2 learner needs to be able to do.
  2. Audit your control of polite and plain style and identify where you mix them without intending to.
  3. Review and reactivate conditionals, quotation, and relative clauses from Volume II so they are ready for expanded use in this volume.
  4. Track a single topic across several connected sentences without unnatural repetition of names or pronouns.
  5. Organize a short spoken or written account using a clear beginning, middle, and end rather than a list of disconnected sentences.
  6. Read and write mixed-script Japanese with kanji strategies for words you do not immediately recognize.
  7. Identify, from your own recent work, one or two error patterns to watch for as you begin Volume III.

Communication Scenario

Emma and Yuki's community profile project earned them a spot in an intermediate discussion group that meets online with learners and speakers from several countries. Before their first session, the group leader has asked every member to send a short self-introduction that goes beyond "hobbies and hometown": what they can already do in Japanese, what they still find difficult, and one specific goal for the next few months. Yuki, glancing over Emma's draft, notices that it slides between polite and plain sentences without any particular reason, and the two of them end up talking not just about the introduction itself but about how their Japanese has grown over the past year and where the rough edges still are. This chapter's Practical Performance Task asks you to write and present a similar self-assessment as you begin Volume III.

  1. The writer reflects honestly on strengths and weaknesses using vocabulary for self-assessment and discourse planning.
  2. The writer reactivates Volume II systems — conditionals, quotation, and relative clauses — in a short connected account.
  3. The writer maintains one consistent register throughout, auditing for accidental polite/plain mixing.

Script Strategy — What to do when a familiar kanji will not come to you

When a kanji from an earlier chapter does not come to mind immediately, three strategies are more reliable than guessing: first, check whether the surrounding sentence gives enough context to infer the word even without the exact reading; second, look up the word by typing a plausible reading into an IME and checking the conversion candidates against the meaning you intend; third, keep a running list of words that consistently slow you down, and review that list rather than re-reading entire chapters at random.

From this chapter forward, furigana on Volume II kanji is reduced further than in Volume II itself. New Volume III kanji, such as those introduced above, receive furigana at first appearance and are gradually reduced according to this volume's matrices.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Auditing your own recorded speech

As this volume moves toward longer spoken tasks, it becomes useful to listen back to your own recordings with a specific checklist rather than a general impression of "did that sound okay." Listen once for content, and listen a second time only for mora timing, vowel length, gemination, and moraic ん, since trying to judge both content and pronunciation at once tends to miss errors in both.

  • 評価 (ひょうか): check that the long vowel in ひょう is fully held, distinct from a shortened ひょか.
  • 目標 (もくひょう): moraic く and the long vowel in ひょう should both be clearly timed, not compressed into a single beat.
  • 構成 (こうせい): the long vowel in こう and the vowel sequence in せい should remain distinct from an under-lengthened こせい.

This kind of self-monitoring becomes an ongoing habit in this volume rather than a single lesson; return to it whenever you record a longer spoken task.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter introduces no new grammar system. Instead, it audits and reactivates three systems from Volume II — conditionals, quotation, and relative clauses — and adds explicit attention to topic chains, paragraph structure, and register consistency, all of which this volume will build on directly.

Auditing polite and plain style

Purpose: to notice, in your own recent writing or speech, places where polite and plain forms mix without a clear reason, and to correct them toward one consistent style.

  • *日本語は難しいですが、時々楽しいと思う。*Japanese is difficult (polite), but I sometimes think it's fun (plain).unintentional mixing: です ends the first clause, plain 思う ends the second
  • 日本語は難しいですが、時々楽しいと思います。Japanese is difficult, but I sometimes think it's fun.corrected: consistent polite style throughout

This kind of mixing is common once learners can produce both styles fluently and are thinking about content rather than form. The correction is not to avoid plain forms, but to choose one register per text or turn and hold it, exactly as Chapter 20 of Volume II practiced for the community profile.

Reactivating conditionals

Purpose: to review the contrast among と, ば, たら, and なら from Volume II before this volume expands their use into more abstract and hypothetical contexts.

  • 時間があれば、毎日練習したいです。If I have time, I want to practice every day.ば — general condition
  • 分からないことがあったら、先生に聞きます。If there's something I don't understand, I'll ask the teacher.たら — specific future situation
  • 日本語をもっと話したいなら、この会に参加するといいですよ。If you want to speak more Japanese, it's a good idea to join this group.なら — responding to the other person's stated situation

Reactivating quotation and relative clauses

Purpose: to confirm control of direct and indirect quotation and of simple relative clauses before this volume extends both into longer, more complex noun phrases.

  • 先生は「もっと練習してください」と言いました。The teacher said, "Please practice more."direct quotation with と言いました
  • 先生は、もっと練習したほうがいいと言っていました。The teacher said that it would be better to practice more.indirect quotation, plain-form content
  • 去年習った文法を、まだよく覚えています。I still remember well the grammar I learned last year.relative clause 去年習った modifies 文法

Topic chains and paragraph structure

Purpose: to organize several sentences around one clear topic, and to arrange those sentences into a recognizable beginning, middle, and end rather than a list.

Building Block — A short self-assessment paragraph
state your overall current level → name one specific strength with an example → name one specific difficulty, quoting or paraphrasing feedback if relevant → state a conditional goal for what you will do if a certain situation arises → close with one sentence connecting back to your opening statement
This five-stage pattern keeps a single topic (the writer's own Japanese) clear from beginning to end and gives this chapter's Practical Performance Task its underlying shape.
私は今、日本語でだいたいのことが話せるようになりました。特に、日常生活について話すことは得意で、去年、地域の人にインタビューをした時も、あまり困りませんでした。でも、敬語や、少し複雑な文の構成はまだ苦手です。先生には、「文が長くなると、時々分かりにくくなる」と言われたことがあります。もし敬語を使う機会があったら、失敗を恐れずに練習してみたいと思います。これからも、得意なことを生かしながら、苦手なことに少しずつ挑戦していきたいです。
I've now become able to say more or less anything in Japanese. In particular, I'm good at talking about daily life, and when I interviewed someone in my community last year, I didn't have much trouble. But I'm still weak at honorific language and slightly complex sentence structure. A teacher once told me, "Sometimes it becomes hard to understand when your sentences get long." If I get a chance to use honorific language, I want to try practicing without fearing mistakes. Going forward, I want to keep making use of what I'm good at while gradually challenging myself on what I'm weak at.

Writing Workshop

In this workshop you will draft the written portion of your Practical Performance Task: a short self-assessment introduction, following the five-stage Building Block pattern from the Grammar section.

  1. State your overall current level in one or two sentences.
  2. Name one specific strength, with a concrete example.
  3. Name one specific difficulty, optionally quoting or paraphrasing feedback you have received.
  4. State a conditional goal using ば, たら, or なら.
  5. Close with one sentence connecting back to your opening statement, and revise using the checklist below.

チェックリスト — Editing checklist:

  • My introduction states my overall level clearly.
  • My introduction names a specific strength with an example.
  • My introduction names a specific difficulty.
  • My introduction includes a conditional goal using ば, たら, or なら.
  • My introduction quotes or paraphrases feedback using an appropriate quotation pattern, if relevant.
  • I kept my topic (myself and my Japanese) clear across sentences without unnecessary repetition.
  • I maintained one consistent polite or plain style throughout, except inside direct quotations.
  • I checked unfamiliar kanji using the strategies in the Script and Kanji Strategy section.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Self-assessment in language-learning communities

Discussion groups, exchange meetups, and online language communities that bring together learners and speakers of Japanese from many countries have become increasingly common, both in Japan and elsewhere. In many such groups, brief self-introductions that name specific strengths and difficulties, rather than only hobbies or hometown, help other participants know how to communicate with a newcomer, though the level of detail expected varies by group and setting.

Regional Note — Groups vary in formality and expectation

Some discussion groups are organized formally, with structured self-introductions and moderated turn-taking, while others are informal and conversational from the start. Learners joining a new group should observe how others introduce themselves before assuming a fixed format applies everywhere.

Register Note — Consistency in a first impression

A first self-introduction to a new group is one of the few places where an inconsistent register is especially noticeable, since the audience has no prior context for the writer's usual style. Maintaining one consistent register throughout, as this chapter's Practical Performance Task requires, helps the introduction read as complete and considered rather than as a rough draft.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Mixing polite and plain style while focused on content

Error: *日常生活について話すことは得意だ。でも、意見を言うことはまだ苦手です。 switching from plain 得意だ to polite 苦手です within the same short introduction.

Correction: 日常生活について話すことは得意です。でも、意見を言うことはまだ苦手です。 — choose one style for the whole introduction and check it after writing, as modeled in this chapter's Model Dialogue.

Common Mistake — Listing strengths and weaknesses without a topic thread

Error: *得意なことは会話です。苦手なことは敬語です。目標は敬語を練習することです。 three disconnected sentences with no connectors or topic chaining.

Correction: Use the five-stage Building Block pattern to connect these ideas with appropriate connectors and a consistent topic, so the introduction reads as one account rather than three separate labels.

Common Mistake — Treating a conditional goal as a simple future statement

Error: *敬語を練習します。 stating a goal as a flat future action rather than connecting it to a specific condition, when the surrounding context calls for a conditional framing.

Correction: 敬語を使う機会があったら、練習してみたいです。 — connecting the goal to a specific situation with たら makes the statement feel more natural and realistic in a self-introduction.

Integrated independent communication · Speak and Write Japanese: Independent Communication and Advanced Expression

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Produce a complete multi-paragraph project on a learner-selected familiar or semi-specialized topic, using only Volume III systems.
  2. Combine explanation, cause and result, and supported opinion into one organized piece.
  3. Use basic keigo appropriately in an introduction, a request, or a closing formula within the project.
  4. Use passive, potential, and causative forms accurately where the content calls for them.
  5. Choose and justify a conditional form when discussing a hypothesis, a plan, or an alternative outcome.
  6. Attribute information to at least two sources and synthesize it into a connected argument.
  7. Present the project orally, respond to at least one question, and manage stance and register consistently throughout.
  8. Reflect on personal progress across Volume III and identify at least one goal for Volume IV.

Communication Scenario

The month has arrived for the recycling group's regular meeting, and Emma is ready to share the project she planned with 中村さん in Chapter 19. Using her outline, her two sources, and her revised opening paragraph, Emma will explain 田中さん's original proposal, walk the group through how it changed after discussion, and offer her own view on what the experience taught the group about making decisions together. She will speak politely to a mixed audience of volunteers and staff, answer a question from the floor, and submit a short written version afterward for the group's records. This chapter walks through the finished project and gives you the structure, language, and feedback tools you need to complete and present your own capstone.

  1. The learner writes a complete multi-paragraph project (introduction, at least two developed body paragraphs, and a conclusion) on a chosen topic.
  2. The project synthesizes at least two sources and attributes information clearly.
  3. The project uses at least one passive or causative form, one conditional, and one basic keigo expression appropriately.
  4. The learner delivers an oral presentation of the project and responds to at least one question.
  5. The learner completes a short self-assessment connecting the project to Volume III outcomes and a Volume IV goal.

Script Strategy — A final furigana check before submission

Before submitting your written project, read through it once with the sole purpose of checking furigana: consolidated kanji from earlier chapters should generally appear without furigana, while any name, rare reading, or unscheduled character you have chosen to include for authenticity should keep its reading support.

This is also a good moment to proofread for accidental homophone conversions from IME input, since a longer piece has more opportunities for an unnoticed incorrect conversion to slip through.

れんしゅう — Practice: Read your finished draft aloud once, kanji by kanji, and mark any character whose reading you are not fully confident about. Add furigana only to those marked characters.

Pronunciation and Listening Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Sustaining formal presentation prosody

A full presentation is longer than the single-paragraph pitch practiced in Chapter 19, so pacing, breathing, and paragraph-level pauses matter even more. Practice reading your entire project aloud at least once before presenting it, marking a light breath and a slightly longer pause at each of the three outline transitions まず, 次に, and 最後に, and keeping ordinary mora timing, vowel length, and moraic ん steady even as you tire toward the end of a longer passage.

Listeners rely on phrase-level intonation to tell a statement from a question and a continuing point from a completed one; keep this contrast clear even under the pressure of public speaking.

Grammar: Integrated Review

Chapter 20 authorizes all active Volume III systems for combined use: explanation, opinion, cause/result, keigo basics, passive, causative, conditionals, attribution, formal writing, narrative, data, and stance. No new grammar form is introduced here. Instead, this section reviews how these systems fit together inside one connected project, using Emma's finished piece as the model.

A four-part capstone frame

Purpose: to organize an introduction, two developed body paragraphs, and a conclusion into one coherent project, extending the three-part outline frame from Chapter 19.

Building Block — The capstone paragraph frame
導入（まず）→ 説明と原因（次に①）→ 意見と条件（次に②）→ 結論（最後に）
Introduction, explanation with cause, opinion with a conditional, and conclusion — each part uses systems already active from Units I and II.
まず、田中さんの提案について説明する。次に、なぜグループがそれを調整することにしたかを述べる。さらに、もし話し合いがなかったら、結果はどう違っていたかを考える。最後に、この経験から学んだことをまとめる。
First, I explain Tanaka-san's proposal. Next, I state why the group decided to adjust it. Furthermore, I consider how the result might have differed if there had been no discussion. Finally, I summarize what was learned from this experience.

Passive, potential, and causative in context

Purpose: to use passive, potential, and causative forms accurately when the content of your own project calls for them, rather than inserting them artificially.

  • 最初の提案は、多くのボランティアに支持された。The original proposal was supported by many volunteers.direct passive, Chapter 7
  • 話し合いのおかげで、みんなが意見を言うことができた。Thanks to the discussion, everyone was able to state an opinion.potential + おかげで, Chapters 3 and 8
  • 中村さんは、みんなに自由に意見を言わせてくれた。Nakamura-san let everyone express their opinions freely.causative-permissive, Chapter 8

Not every project will naturally require all three forms with equal weight; use each one only where the content genuinely calls for it, and avoid forcing an unnatural sentence merely to display a form.

Conditionals for hypothesis and consequence

Purpose: to choose the conditional that matches your intended meaning when discussing an alternative outcome, a plan, or advice within the project.

  • もし話し合いがなかったら、グループは今も古い方法を使っていただろう。If there had been no discussion, the group would probably still be using the old method now.counterfactual たら, Chapter 9
  • 同じような問題が起きた場合、まず全員の意見を聞くべきだ。If a similar problem occurs, one should first listen to everyone's opinions.場合, general condition, Chapter 9

Keigo, attribution, and stance together

Purpose: to combine a respectful opening or closing formula, clear attribution to your sources, and cautious stance markers into one register-consistent presentation.

  • 本日は、お時間をいただき、ありがとうございます。Thank you for your time today.formulaic keigo opening, Chapter 6
  • アンケートと話し合いのメモによると、調整は全体としてうまくいったと考えられる。According to the survey and the discussion notes, the adjustment can overall be considered to have gone well.attribution + synthesis + stance, Chapters 11, 18, 19
Language Focus — One project, one register

Across the whole capstone, hold to a single target register decided at the planning stage, most likely a polite public-presentation style with occasional formal written-style stance markers such as と考えられる or だろう. Switch to plain style only inside a clearly marked quotation, such as reporting what a colleague actually said.

Integrated Grammar Practice

  1. Underline every passive, potential, and causative form in your draft and confirm each one fits the meaning you intend.
  2. Underline your conditional sentence and identify whether it expresses a counterfactual, a general condition, or advice.
  3. Circle your keigo opening and closing formulas and confirm they match the register of the rest of the piece.
  4. Highlight every attribution phrase and confirm each source is introduced clearly at least once.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Emma delivers her presentation at the recycling group's monthly meeting and takes a question from 田中さん afterward.

エマ 本日は、お時間をいただき、ありがとうございます。今日は、田中さんの提案がどう調整されたかについて発表します。 Thank you for your time today. Today I will present on how Tanaka-san's proposal was adjusted.
（発表を通して、まず・次に・最後にを使って構成を示す）
エマ アンケートと話し合いのメモによると、最初の提案は多くのボランティアに支持されたが、時間の面で難しいという意見もあった。もし話し合いがなかったら、グループは今も古い方法を使っていただろう。 According to the survey and the discussion notes, the original proposal was supported by many volunteers, but there were also opinions that it was difficult in terms of timing. If there had been no discussion, the group would probably still be using the old method now.
エマ 以上のことから、話し合いを通して全員が意見を言えたことが、今回の調整がうまくいった一番の理由だと考えられます。 From the above, I believe the main reason this adjustment went well is that everyone was able to share their opinions through the discussion.
田中さん 発表、ありがとうございました。一つ質問なんですが、もし同じような問題がまた起きたら、どうすればいいと思いますか。 Thank you for the presentation. I have one question — if a similar problem occurred again, what do you think should be done?
エマ ご質問ありがとうございます。同じような問題が起きた場合、今回のように、まず全員の意見を聞くべきだと思います。 Thank you for the question. If a similar problem occurs, I think that, as in this case, one should first listen to everyone's opinions.
田中さん なるほど、よく分かりました。ありがとうございました。 I see, that makes good sense. Thank you very much.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Q&A as part of a presentation

In many Japanese classroom, community, and workplace settings, a short question-and-answer period after a presentation is an expected and valued part of the event, giving the audience a chance to engage directly with the presenter's ideas. Responding calmly and thoughtfully to a question, as Emma does with 田中さん in this chapter, is itself part of demonstrating communicative competence.

Regional Note — Variation in Q&A style

How many questions are typically asked, how directly they are phrased, and how much discussion follows a presentation can vary by school, workplace, region, and generation. The pattern modeled in this chapter reflects one common approach rather than a single universal standard.

Language Note — Reflecting on your own learning

Explicitly reflecting on which language systems you were able to combine in a real task, as Emma does at the end of the Reading Practice passage, is a useful habit in any language you study. Consider keeping a similar short reflection at the end of each future project as you continue into Volume IV.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Inserting grammar forms without a natural reason

Error: *このコーヒーは飲まれた。 forces a passive form onto a sentence where an ordinary active sentence is far more natural.

Correction: 私はこのコーヒーを飲んだ。 — use passive, causative, or conditional forms only where your project's actual content calls for them.

Common Mistake — Dropping register consistency near the end of a long piece

Error: A presentation that opens with careful です／ます style but drifts into plain-style endings in the final paragraph, often because the writer is tired or rushing to finish.

Correction: Reread the entire piece one final time specifically for register, as recommended in the Chapter 19 Error Analysis section, paying particular attention to the conclusion.

Common Mistake — Answering a question without acknowledging it

Error: Responding to an audience question by launching directly into an answer with no acknowledgment can feel abrupt in a formal presentation setting.

Correction: ご質問ありがとうございます。 before answering, as modeled in the Model Dialogue, softens the transition and shows attentiveness to the audience.

Looking Ahead

いまできること — What you can do now

You can plan, draft, revise, and deliver a complete multi-paragraph project that explains a topic, supports an opinion with reasons and sources, uses basic keigo, passive, causative, and conditional forms accurately, and maintains one consistent register from introduction to conclusion. You can also respond to a question in real time and reflect meaningfully on your own progress. This is the integrated, independent B2 communication that Volume III set out to build.

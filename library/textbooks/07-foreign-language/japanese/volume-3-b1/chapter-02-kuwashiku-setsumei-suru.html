詳しく説明するDetailed Description and Explanation

Complex description, classification, and examples · Speak and Write Japanese: Independent Communication and Advanced Expression

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Describe people, places, systems, processes, services, and cultural practices in greater structural detail than in Volume II.
  2. Classify something by naming its type or category using という and related patterns.
  3. Give concrete examples to support a general statement using 例えば and 〜ような.
  4. Contrast two related items or perspectives using 一方.
  5. Build longer noun phrases through expanded noun modification, stacking description before a head noun.
  6. Organize a multi-sentence explanation that moves from a general classification to specific supporting detail.
  7. Read and write mixed-script Japanese describing systems and processes, using kanji strategies for categories, examples, and structure.

Communication Scenario

In the online discussion group Emma and Yuki joined in Chapter 1, this week's task is to explain something from daily life to members who may not be familiar with it: a system, a service, or a custom from your own city or country. Emma decides to explain how recycling collection works where she lives, since a member from another country asked a question about it during the group's first session. Her first draft is accurate but reads like a list of separate facts. Yuki suggests that Emma classify the system first — what kind of system it is — and then give concrete examples before adding a contrasting point about how a nearby city handles things differently. This chapter's Practical Performance Task asks you to write and present a similarly organized explanation of a system, place, service, or practice from your own life.

  1. The writer classifies the system or practice being explained before giving details.
  2. The writer supports the general explanation with at least one concrete example.
  3. The writer includes a contrasting point using 一方, comparing the system to a related alternative.

Script Strategy — Reading compound words by their parts

Several of this chapter's new words are two-kanji compounds built from characters you may already recognize individually, even if the compound itself is new: 分類 combines 分 (divide) and 類 (kind), and 対象 combines 対 (facing; versus) and 象 (image; shape). Recognizing familiar components can help you guess a plausible meaning before checking a dictionary, but a guess from components is a starting point, not a confirmed reading — always verify the actual reading and meaning of the whole word rather than assuming it from the parts alone.

New Volume III kanji introduced in this chapter, such as those above, receive furigana at first appearance and are gradually reduced according to this volume's matrices. Kanji introduced in Chapter 1, such as 評価 and 構成, appear with reduced furigana in this chapter's readings.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Long compounds and phrase grouping

As explanations grow longer, it becomes important to group words into meaningful phrases when speaking, rather than pausing evenly between every word or running everything together without a breath. A useful habit is to pause briefly after a topic phrase marked by は, after a long noun phrase before its head noun, and before a contrastive phrase introduced by 一方, since these are the places where a listener needs a moment to process what has come before.

  • 種類 (しゅるい): keep the long vowel in しゅる clearly held, distinct from a shortened しゅる with no length contrast at all in casual speech — listen for how careful speech maintains it.
  • 特徴 (とくちょう): moraic く and the long vowel in ちょう should both be clearly timed.
  • 手順 (てじゅん): the moraic ん should be held for a full mora before a pause, not clipped short.

Practice reading the Building Block example in the Grammar section aloud, pausing at the phrase boundaries suggested above rather than at every word.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter introduces four connected tools for detailed explanation: classification with という, exemplification with 例えば and 〜ような, contrast with 一方, and expanded noun modification for building longer descriptive noun phrases.

Classifying with という

Purpose: to name the type or category something belongs to, especially when introducing an unfamiliar term or naming a system for the first time.

  • これは「分別収集」という仕組みです。This is a system called "separated collection."Xという Y — Y is classified/named as X
  • ごみを種類ごとに分けて出すという習慣があります。There is a custom of sorting trash by type before putting it out.という + noun, summarizing a described practice

という is used here to introduce or summarize a name, term, or practice, not simply to mean "say." Its use in quotation (Chapter 1's と言いました) and its use here in classification share the same underlying particle と but serve different discourse functions.

Giving examples with 例えば and 〜ような

Purpose: to support a general statement with one or more concrete examples, and to describe something by comparing it to a familiar example.

  • このサービスにはいろいろな種類があります。例えば、食料品の配達や、部屋の掃除を頼むことができます。This service has various types. For example, you can request grocery delivery or room cleaning.例えば opens a sentence giving specific examples of a prior general claim
  • 図書館のような公共施設は、多くの地域で無料で利用できます。Public facilities like libraries can be used for free in many areas.Xのような Y — Y similar in kind to example X

Contrasting with 一方

Purpose: to present two related but different situations, systems, or perspectives side by side.

  • この市ではプラスチックは週に一度回収されます。一方、隣の市では週に二度回収されるそうです。In this city, plastic is collected once a week. On the other hand, I hear that in the neighboring city it's collected twice a week.一方 introduces a contrasting second clause or sentence
  • このサービスは便利だが、料金が高いという一方もある。This service is convenient, but there is also the other side that the fee is high.という一方もある — naming a contrasting aspect within one statement

一方 can connect two full sentences, as in the first example, or appear within a single sentence to name a contrasting aspect, as in the second. Both uses are active in this chapter; the volume scope does not require more advanced concessive patterns yet.

Expanded noun modification

Purpose: to build longer, more informative noun phrases by stacking a description before the noun it modifies, rather than splitting the description into a separate sentence.

  • 週に一度、決まった曜日に回収される可燃ごみburnable trash that is collected once a week, on a fixed dayclause + clause modifying 可燃ごみ, head-final structure
  • 誰でも無料で利用できる、駅前にある図書館the library near the station that anyone can use for freetwo modifying clauses stacked before 図書館

This is the same head-final noun-modification system reactivated in Chapter 1, now extended to carry more information before the head noun. Keep each modifying clause understandable; two clearly connected clauses are more useful than a single overloaded phrase.

Building a classification-to-detail explanation

Building Block — A detailed explanation paragraph
classify the topic with という → describe its main characteristic or how it works → give one or two concrete examples with 例えば or 〜ような → add a contrasting point with 一方 → close with one sentence connecting the explanation back to the reader's likely situation
This five-stage pattern moves from general classification to specific, comparative detail, and gives this chapter's Practical Performance Task its underlying shape.
私の住んでいる市には、「分別収集」というごみの仕組みがあります。これは、ごみを種類ごとに分けて出す仕組みで、決まった曜日に、決まった種類のごみだけを出すという特徴があります。例えば、燃えるごみは月曜日と木曜日、缶やびんは水曜日に出します。一方、隣の市では、種類の分け方が少し違っていて、プラスチックも燃えるごみと一緒に出せるそうです。もしこの地域に引っ越してきたら、まず市のホームページで自分の地域のごみの出し方を確認するといいと思います。
The city I live in has a trash system called "separated collection." This is a system where you sort trash by type before putting it out, and its distinguishing feature is that you put out only a fixed type of trash on a fixed day. For example, burnable trash goes out on Mondays and Thursdays, and cans and bottles go out on Wednesdays. On the other hand, I hear that in the neighboring city, the way types are sorted is a little different, and plastic can be put out together with burnable trash. If you move to this area, I think it's good to first check the city's website for how to put out trash in your own area.

Grammar Review Practice

  1. Write one sentence classifying a system or practice you know, using という.
  2. Write one sentence giving an example of something using 例えば.
  3. Write one sentence describing something by comparison, using 〜のような.
  4. Write one sentence contrasting two related things using 一方.
  5. Write one noun phrase with at least two stacked modifying clauses before the head noun.

Writing Workshop

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Local variation in everyday systems

Many everyday systems in Japan, such as trash sorting, recycling collection, and local card-based services, are managed at the municipal level rather than nationally, so procedures can differ meaningfully between neighboring cities or wards. This local variation is a common and useful topic for detailed explanation, since it naturally requires classification, examples, and comparison rather than a single fixed description.

Regional Note — Comparable local variation elsewhere

Local variation in municipal systems is common in many countries, not only in Japan. When describing a system from your own country or region for an international discussion group, it can be helpful to note explicitly that your description applies to your specific area, since a listener from elsewhere may reasonably assume the practice is more uniform than it actually is.

Register Note — Classification language in explanatory writing

という and 一方 are common in both spoken explanation and written description, and their level of formality depends more on the surrounding sentence-final forms than on the connectors themselves. Using them consistently within one register, as this chapter's Practical Performance Task requires, helps a detailed explanation read as organized and complete.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Listing facts without classification or connection

Error: *ごみは種類ごとに出します。月曜日は燃えるごみです。水曜日は缶です。 three disconnected factual sentences with no classification or connecting language.

Correction: これは「分別収集」というごみの仕組みで、種類ごとに決まった曜日に出すという特徴があります。例えば、燃えるごみは月曜日、缶は水曜日に出します。 — open with a classification using という, then connect the details with 例えば, as modeled in this chapter's Building Block.

Common Mistake — Using 一方 to mean simply "and" or "also"

Error: *このサービスは便利です。一方、駅の近くにあります。 using 一方 to add a second unrelated fact rather than a genuine contrast.

Correction: このサービスは料金が安いです。一方、隣町のサービスは少し高いです。 — reserve 一方 for a genuine point of contrast between two comparable things, not as a general-purpose connector for additional information.

Common Mistake — Overloading a single noun phrase

Error: *駅前にあって、誰でも無料で利用できて、月曜日から日曜日まで開いていて、本や雑誌がたくさんある図書館 a single noun phrase stacking four separate modifying clauses before the head noun, making it hard to follow.

Correction: Limit a single noun phrase to one or two clearly connected modifying clauses, such as 駅前にあって、誰でも無料で利用できる図書館, and move additional details into a separate sentence.

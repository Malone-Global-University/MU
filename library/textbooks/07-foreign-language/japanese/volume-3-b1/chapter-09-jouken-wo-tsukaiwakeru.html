条件を使い分けるChoosing Conditionals

Conditional contrast and hypothetical reasoning · Speak and Write Japanese: Independent Communication and Advanced Expression

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Form the four conditional patterns と, ば, たら, and なら accurately.
  2. Distinguish the core communicative use of each conditional: automatic or habitual result (と), general or evaluative condition (ば), sequential or discovery-based condition (たら), and topic-based or advice-oriented condition (なら).
  3. Use もし to signal a hypothetical condition clearly, and use 場合 to describe a formal or procedural case.
  4. Recognize counterfactual meaning in たら and ば sentences describing situations that did not happen.
  5. Give instructions, advice, warnings, and preferences using the conditional form best suited to the situation.
  6. Read and listen to short texts containing instructions, warnings, and procedures, identifying which conditional is used and why.
  7. Write and narrate a short account of advice or a warning using at least two different conditional forms appropriately.

Communication Scenario

Emma is preparing for a weekend hiking trip with friends from her discussion group, and she is also helping a new volunteer at the community center understand the office procedures. Before the hike, one friend explains what always happens if the weather turns bad, another gives advice about what to do if Emma gets tired, and a third describes what she would have done differently if she had known about a shortcut earlier. At the community center, Emma explains to the new volunteer what to do in case a visitor arrives without an appointment, and what to do if the printer stops working. This chapter's Practical Performance Task asks you to give advice, a warning, and a procedure for handling a specific case, choosing the conditional form that fits each situation.

  1. The speaker or writer selects と, ば, たら, or なら based on whether the sentence describes an automatic result, a general condition, a sequential discovery, or a topic-based recommendation.
  2. The speaker or writer uses もし to flag a hypothetical situation and 場合 to describe a formal or procedural case.
  3. The speaker or writer recognizes and can produce a simple counterfactual sentence describing something that did not happen.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Distinguishing ば and たら endings clearly

The ば and たら conditional endings are short but consequential: mispronouncing the vowel length or mora count can shift a conditional form into a different word entirely. Careful attention to each mora keeps these endings distinct and intelligible.

  • 行けば (いけば): two distinct morae in the ending; do not lengthen the final え-row vowel.
  • 行ったら (いったら): the small っ before た must be held for its own timing unit, distinguishing 行ったら from 行たら, which is not a valid form.
  • 故障したら: practice the sequence した + ら slowly, keeping both morae of した distinct before adding ら.

Practice reading the Model Dialogue aloud, paying particular attention to keeping the small っ and each conditional ending clearly timed, even at a natural conversational pace.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns: Choosing Conditionals

This chapter contrasts と, ば, たら, and なら, and adds もし and 場合 as tools for signaling hypothetical or procedural conditions. All four conditional forms can sometimes be translated with English "if," but they are not interchangeable in Japanese; each carries its own typical range of use, and choosing among them is itself a communicative skill.

と — automatic and habitual result

Purpose: to describe a result that follows automatically or habitually whenever the condition is met, without expressing the speaker's will, invitation, or advice in the result clause.

  • 春になると、桜が咲きます。When spring comes, the cherry blossoms bloom.natural, repeatable result; と links a general condition to a predictable outcome
  • このボタンを押すと、電気が消えます。If you press this button, the light turns off.mechanical, automatic result; typical of instructions and procedures

と is not typically used when the result clause contains a request, invitation, suggestion, or command directed at the listener; ば, たら, or なら are usually chosen instead for those functions.

ば — general or evaluative condition

Purpose: to describe a general condition, especially one used for evaluation, advice, or a logical relationship between condition and result.

  • 時間があれば、手伝います。If I have time, I'll help.general condition affecting the speaker's future action
  • 早く出発すれば、道が込む前に着けます。If we leave early, we can arrive before the roads get crowded.evaluative condition supporting advice or planning

ば often appears in advice and evaluative statements, but a request or command in the result clause is more limited than with たら or なら; check the surrounding context and, where possible, favor たら or なら when the result is a direct instruction to the listener.

たら — sequential condition and discovery

Purpose: to describe a condition or event that comes first, followed by a result, often used for sequential actions, discoveries, requests, and counterfactuals.

  • 駅に着いたら、電話してください。When you arrive at the station, please call me.sequential condition + request, very natural with たら
  • 箱を開けたら、手紙が入っていました。When I opened the box, there was a letter inside.discovery use: the result was found out after the first action
  • もっと早く出発していたら、道が込む前に着けたでしょう。If we had left earlier, we probably would have arrived before the roads got crowded.counterfactual: describes a past situation that did not happen

たら is the most flexible of the four conditionals and is often the safest default for requests, sequential events, discoveries, and counterfactual situations describing something that did not actually happen.

なら — topic-based condition and advice

Purpose: to respond to a situation already mentioned or assumed, often to give advice, a recommendation, or a comment about that specific topic.

  • 疲れているなら、休んだほうがいいですよ。If you're tired, you should rest.responds to an already-mentioned or observed situation with advice
  • 山に登るなら、水をたくさん持っていってください。If you're going to climb the mountain, please bring plenty of water.advice tied to a stated plan, not a hypothetical general condition

なら typically follows something already said, known, or assumed by the listener, rather than introducing a brand-new hypothetical condition; this is the key difference between なら and もし + たら/ば, which more often introduce a new hypothesis.

Building Block — Choosing the right conditional
automatic/habitual result → と　|　general/evaluative condition → ば　|　sequence, discovery, request, counterfactual → たら　|　response to a known topic, advice → なら
Ask what kind of relationship connects the condition and the result before choosing a form; more than one conditional may be grammatically possible, but each carries a different nuance.
もし明日雨が降ったら、ハイキングは中止します。天気がよければ、予定通り出発しましょう。
If it rains tomorrow, we'll cancel the hike. If the weather is good, let's leave as planned.

もし and 場合 — flagging hypotheses and formal cases

Purpose: to mark a condition clearly as hypothetical (もし) or to describe a formal, procedural, or institutional case (場合), often independent of which conditional ending is used.

  • もし故障したら、担当者に連絡してください。If it breaks down, please contact the person in charge.もし signals the hypothetical nature of the condition before たら appears
  • 予約がない場合は、少々お待ちください。In the case that you don't have a reservation, please wait a moment.場合 frames the condition as a formal, procedural case rather than a personal hypothesis

もし and 場合 can combine with と, ば, たら, or なら; they add clarity and formality rather than replacing the conditional ending itself.

Recognizing counterfactuals

Purpose: to recognize and produce simple sentences describing a past situation that did not actually happen, most often built with たら or ば plus a past-tense-based result.

  • もっと練習していれば、うまく話せたと思います。If I had practiced more, I think I could have spoken well.counterfactual with ば; describes an unrealized past condition
  • 近道を知っていたら、もっと早く着いていたでしょう。If I had known the shortcut, I probably would have arrived earlier.counterfactual with たら; both condition and result describe something that did not happen

This chapter introduces counterfactual awareness for recognition and controlled, supported production; learners are not expected to produce complex counterfactuals independently without a model.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Emma and her friend ゆうき talk the evening before their weekend hiking trip.

ゆうき 明日、もし雨が降ったら、どうする？ If it rains tomorrow, what will we do?
エマ 天気予報によると、午前中は大丈夫みたい。でも、もし本当に降ったら、ハイキングは中止したほうがいいと思う。 According to the weather forecast, the morning should be fine. But if it really does rain, I think we should cancel the hike.
ゆうき じゃあ、天気がよければ、予定通り七時に出発しよう。疲れたら、途中で休めばいいよ。 Then if the weather is good, let's leave at seven as planned. If you get tired, we can just rest partway.
エマ ありがとう。実は、去年、近道を知らなかったから、道に迷ってしまったの。近道を知っていたら、もっと早く山頂に着いていたと思う。 Thanks. Actually, last year I didn't know the shortcut, so I ended up getting lost. If I had known the shortcut, I think I would have reached the summit much earlier.
ゆうき それなら、今回は地図をちゃんと確認しよう。何か問題があった場合は、すぐに連絡してね。 In that case, let's make sure to check the map properly this time. If there's any problem, please contact me right away.

  3. What happened last year, and what does Emma say she would have done if she had known the shortcut?

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — 場合 in signs, manuals, and public announcements

The word 場合 appears frequently in public signs, manuals, workplace notices, and institutional announcements to describe a specific case in a neutral, procedural way, such as what to do if a machine malfunctions or if a visitor arrives without a reservation. Recognizing this pattern helps learners navigate real signage and instructions in many public and institutional settings.

Regional Note — Conditional choice and personal style

While と, ば, たら, and なら each have typical communicative tendencies, individual speakers and regions may show some flexibility in which conditional they favor for a given situation, especially in casual conversation. The distinctions taught in this chapter describe reliable, broadly applicable patterns rather than rigid, universal rules that every speaker follows identically.

Language Note — Many languages mark hypotheticals differently

Many languages distinguish real conditions from hypothetical or counterfactual ones using separate verb forms, moods, or particles, though the boundaries and forms differ substantially from language to language. Comparing how your own language marks a real "if" versus a hypothetical or counterfactual "if" can help clarify why Japanese uses four distinct conditional forms rather than one all-purpose word.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Using と with a request or command in the result clause

Error: *駅に着くと、電話してください。 intended as "when you arrive at the station, please call me," using と with a direct request.

Correction: 駅に着いたら、電話してください。 — use たら, which comfortably supports a request in the result clause, rather than と, which is reserved mainly for automatic or habitual results.

Common Mistake — Using なら to introduce a brand-new hypothesis

Error: *明日雨が降るなら、ハイキングは中止します。 used as an opening statement with no prior mention of rain, when なら is more natural responding to an already-raised topic.

Correction: もし明日雨が降ったら、ハイキングは中止します。 — use もし with たら to introduce a new hypothetical condition, and reserve なら for responding to a condition the listener has already raised or that is already assumed.

Common Mistake — Missing the counterfactual meaning of たら/ば with past-based results

Error: Reading 近道を知っていたら、もっと早く着いていたでしょう。 as a simple future condition, missing that both the condition and the result describe something that did not actually happen.

Correction: Recognize that when both the condition and the result use past-tense-based forms describing an unrealized situation, the sentence is counterfactual: it means "if I had known the shortcut, I would have arrived earlier" — but I did not know it, and I did not arrive earlier.

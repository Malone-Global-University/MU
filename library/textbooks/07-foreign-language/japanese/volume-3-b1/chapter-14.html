物語を読むReading Narratives

Narrative viewpoint, time, and style · Speak and Write Japanese: Independent Communication and Advanced Expression

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Recognize plain narrative style as distinct from the polite style used in conversation and email.
  2. Follow temporal shifts in a personal narrative, including flashback and return to the present.
  3. Identify sentences that represent a character's inner thought rather than spoken dialogue or narration.
  4. Use descriptive clauses to build a scene with sensory and emotional detail.
  5. Read a short memoir-like passage and summarize its sequence of events and central feeling.
  6. Write a short personal narrative using plain style and at least one temporal shift.
  7. Complete listening, speaking, reading, and writing tasks built around a personal narrative.

Communication Scenario

佐藤さん replied to Emma's email and approved the use of the storage room, and the small repair project went smoothly. A few weeks later, the community center newsletter asks volunteers to submit a short personal reflection about their experience helping with the recycling project, to be printed alongside the volunteer profiles Emma read back in Chapter 11. Unlike the article she read then, this piece should read like a short personal story: what she remembers noticing, what she thought at the time, and how the day changed compared to how she expected it to go. This chapter's Practical Performance Task asks you to read a model narrative and then write a short personal narrative of your own about an experience, real or invented for practice, using plain narrative style and at least one shift in time.

  1. The writer sets a scene using descriptive clauses about a place, a time, or a feeling.
  2. The writer narrates a sequence of events in plain style, including at least one shift back in time or forward to the present.
  3. The writer includes at least one sentence representing inner thought at the moment it occurred.
  4. The writer closes by reflecting on how the experience compared to what was expected.

Script Strategy — Following verbs across a narrative

Personal narratives shift between plain-style narration, quoted or unquoted inner thought, and occasional direct speech, often without quotation marks announcing the change. As you read, track the verb ending at the end of each sentence: plain past forms such as 気づいた or 思い出した usually mark narration, while a short plain sentence standing alone, such as 大丈夫かな。, often represents a thought at the moment it occurred.

Kanji from this chapter, such as 記憶, 気持ち, 場面, and 変化, will continue to appear in Chapter 16's discussion of specialized and reflective topics, so treat them as durable vocabulary rather than words limited to storytelling.

Pronunciation and Listening Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Voicing a shift into thought

When a narrative moves from ordinary narration into a character's inner thought, the pitch and pace of a read-aloud passage often shift slightly: the narrating sentences move at a steady pace, while a short internal thought, such as これでいいのかな。, is often read a little more slowly, with a small pause just before it and a softer, more reflective tone.

  • エマは箱を運びながら、これで足りるかなと思った。 steady narration, no special pause.
  • これでいいのかな。 read alone, slightly slower, as an isolated thought.

Practice reading the Reading Assessment passage aloud, using a small pause and a slightly softer tone whenever the text shifts into a character's unspoken thought.

Grammar: Narrative Style and Temporal Shifts

This chapter authorizes narrative plain style, temporal shifts between past and present reference points, sentences representing inner thought, and descriptive clauses used to build a scene, building on the plain-form and clause systems developed since Chapter 1.

Narrative plain style

Purpose: to narrate a personal story the way it usually appears in writing, using plain forms rather than the polite です／ます style used in conversation and email.

  • その日、エマは朝早く倉庫に向かった。That day, Emma headed to the storage room early in the morning.向かった, plain past, narrative style
  • 箱は思ったより重かった。The boxes were heavier than she had thought.重かった, plain past い-adjective, descriptive narration
  • 佐藤さんはすぐに手伝ってくれた。Sato-san helped right away.くれた, plain past, benefactive narration

Narrative plain style is a written convention, not a sign of rudeness; it is the expected style for personal essays, diaries, and short stories, distinct from the polite style used when addressing a reader or listener directly, as in Chapter 13's emails.

Temporal shifts

Purpose: to move between the main timeline of a narrative and an earlier or later point in time, and to signal clearly when the story returns to the present moment of narration.

  • 実は、一週間前に佐藤さんからメールが届いていた。In fact, an email from Sato-san had arrived a week earlier.実は signals a shift back; 届いていた, past-of-past, flashback marker
  • あのときのことを、エマは今でもよく覚えている。Emma still remembers that moment well, even now.あのとき refers back; 今でも signals return to the present
  • それから数週間がたった。Several weeks passed after that.それから〜がたつ, time-passing formula marking a jump forward

Inner thought

Purpose: to represent what a character silently thought at a particular moment, distinct from what they said aloud or from the narrator's own commentary.

  • これで足りるかな、とエマは思った。"Will this be enough?" Emma thought.〜かな、と思った, quoted inner thought with a plain-style question particle
  • 意外に早く終わった。よかった。It finished surprisingly quickly. Good.よかった standing alone as an unquoted thought, no と思った needed

Descriptive clauses for scene-building

Purpose: to give the reader enough sensory and situational detail to picture the scene, using noun-modifying clauses and descriptive predicates rather than a bare list of facts.

  • まだ人のいない静かな朝だった。It was a quiet morning, with no one around yet.人のいない静かな朝, noun-modifying clause building a scene
  • 埃をかぶった古い箱がいくつも積まれていた。Several old, dust-covered boxes were stacked up.埃をかぶった古い箱, layered descriptive modification
Building Block — A short personal narrative's structure
scene-setting description + narrated sequence of events (plain style) + one temporal shift + one inner thought + closing reflection
A short personal narrative typically opens with a descriptive clause setting the scene, narrates a sequence of events in plain style, includes one shift backward or forward in time such as 実は〜前に〜 or それから〜がたった, represents at least one moment of inner thought using 〜と思った or an isolated plain sentence, and closes with a reflection comparing the experience to what was expected, often using 意外 or 予想.
まだ人のいない静かな朝だった。エマは箱を運びながら、これで足りるかなと思った。実は、一週間前に佐藤さんからメールが届いていて、鍵は使えると聞いていた。それでも少し心配だった。作業は意外に早く終わった。よかった。予想していたより、ずっと簡単だった。
It was a quiet morning, with no one around yet. As Emma carried the boxes, she thought, "Will this be enough?" In fact, an email from Sato-san had arrived a week earlier, and she had heard that the key would work. Even so, she was a little worried. The work finished surprisingly quickly. Good. It was much easier than she had expected.

Grammar Practice

  1. Rewrite 静かな朝でした。 in narrative plain style.
  2. Write one sentence that shifts backward in time using 実は、〜前に〜.
  3. Write one sentence representing an inner thought, following the pattern of これで足りるかな、とエマは思った。
  4. Write one descriptive clause that modifies a noun, following the pattern of 人のいない静かな朝.
  5. Write a closing reflection sentence using 意外 or 予想, comparing an experience to what you expected.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Emma talks with a fellow volunteer, 山田さん, about writing her newsletter reflection.

山田さん ニュースレターの文章、もう書き始めましたか。 Have you started writing your piece for the newsletter yet?
エマ はい。あの日の朝のことから書いています。まだ人のいない静かな朝だった、という文で始めました。 Yes. I'm writing about that morning. I started with the sentence, "It was a quiet morning, with no one around yet."
山田さん いいですね。作業のときは何を考えていましたか。 That's nice. What were you thinking during the work?
エマ これで足りるかな、と思っていました。実は、その一週間前に佐藤さんからメールが届いていて、少し安心していたんですが、それでも心配でした。 I was thinking, "Will this be enough?" Actually, an email from Sato-san had arrived a week before, which had reassured me a little, but I was still worried.
山田さん それで、実際はどうでしたか。 So, how did it actually turn out?
エマ 意外に早く終わりました。予想していたより、ずっと簡単でした。そのことを最後に書こうと思っています。 It finished surprisingly quickly. It was much easier than I had expected. I'm planning to write that at the end.
山田さん いい構成ですね。読むのが楽しみです。 That's a good structure. I'm looking forward to reading it.

  1. How does Emma's piece begin, and what kind of sentence is it?
  2. What was Emma thinking during the work, and what had happened a week earlier?
  3. How does Emma plan to end her piece?

Writing Workshop

In this workshop you will draft the written portion of your Practical Performance Task: a short personal narrative based on an experience of your choice, real or invented for practice.

  1. Choose an experience that had a moment of uncertainty followed by a result that was easier, harder, or different than expected.
  2. Write an opening description that sets the scene, using at least one descriptive noun-modifying clause.
  3. Narrate the sequence of events in plain style, as they happened.
  4. Include one temporal shift backward or forward in time, using a phrase such as 実は、〜前に〜 or それから〜がたった.

チェックリスト — Editing checklist:

  • My narrative uses plain style consistently, not polite です／ます forms.
  • My opening includes a descriptive clause that helps the reader picture the scene.
  • My narrative includes at least one clear temporal shift.
  • My narrative includes at least one sentence representing inner thought.
  • My closing reflection compares the outcome to what I expected.
  • My sentences are connected smoothly, without unnecessary repetition of names or pronouns.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Personal narrative in newsletters and community writing

Short personal reflections from volunteers, students, or community members commonly appear in Japanese newsletters, school publications, and community bulletins, often written in a quieter, more reflective register than a formal report. These pieces typically favor understated description and a modest closing reflection over strong emotional claims, and they are usually written in plain narrative style even though the writer might use polite style when speaking to the same readers directly.

Regional Note — Variation in narrative voice

Narrative style can vary somewhat by genre, generation, and individual voice: some writers favor short, plain sentences throughout, while others mix in occasional polite-style asides to the reader, especially near the beginning or end of a personal essay. Some regional or community publications also allow a more conversational tone, including regional vocabulary, when the writer's personal voice is emphasized. Treat this chapter's plain narrative style as a reliable, widely used default rather than the only acceptable choice.

Language Note — Marking thought across languages

Many languages distinguish narration, spoken dialogue, and inner thought using punctuation, verb choice, or reporting phrases. Comparing how your own language signals "what someone was thinking" without necessarily saying it aloud can help clarify why Japanese narratives so often let a short plain sentence stand alone as an unquoted thought, without an explicit と思った attached.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Mixing polite and plain style within one narrative

Error: *静かな朝だった。エマは箱を運びました。 switching from plain to polite style mid-narrative without a clear reason.

Correction: 静かな朝だった。エマは箱を運んだ。 — keep narrative passages consistently in plain style unless directly addressing the reader.

Common Mistake — Leaving a temporal shift unmarked

Error: *佐藤さんからメールが届いていた。 inserted with no signal that the story has jumped backward in time, confusing the sequence of events.

Correction: 実は、一週間前に佐藤さんからメールが届いていた。 — mark a shift backward in time clearly with a phrase such as 実は or 〜前に.

Common Mistake — Overusing と思った for every thought

Error: *よかったと思った。安心したと思った。終わったと思った。 attaching と思った to every sentence, which reads as repetitive and mechanical.

Correction: よかった。安心した。ようやく終わった。 — let some thoughts stand alone as plain sentences, reserving と思った for moments where the act of thinking itself needs emphasis.

総合プロジェクトの準備Capstone Preparation

Synthesis, revision, and genre control · Speak and Write Japanese: Independent Communication and Advanced Expression

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Recognize and use vocabulary for project planning, sources, outlines, drafts, revisions, presentations, and peer response.
  2. Use synthesis connectors to combine information from more than one source or idea into a single connected passage.
  3. Attribute information to a source using によると and related attribution language already active from Chapter 11.
  4. Use paragraph transitions to move clearly between an introduction, supporting points, and a conclusion.
  5. Revise a draft for register, adjusting between polite and plain style, or between formal and conversational stance markers, according to genre.
  6. Identify and correct common error patterns from earlier chapters in one's own writing.
  7. Read and respond to a peer's outline or draft with organized, considerate feedback.
  8. Plan the outline, sources, and format for the Chapter 20 capstone project using only authorized Volume III systems.

Communication Scenario

中村さん has asked each volunteer to prepare a short final project about some aspect of the recycling group's work, to be shared with the whole team at next month's meeting. Emma has decided to write and present about how the group's donation-collection schedule changed after 田中さん's proposal was adjusted, and what the group learned from that process. Before she can write a full draft, Emma needs to plan her sources, organize her ideas into an outline, decide on the right register for a mixed formal-and-friendly audience, and get feedback from 中村さん on a first draft. This chapter walks through exactly that preparation process, which every learner will also use to plan their own Chapter 20 capstone project.

  1. The learner selects a topic and lists at least two sources of information to draw on.
  2. The learner produces a short outline with an introduction, at least two supporting points, and a conclusion.
  3. The learner writes at least one sentence that synthesizes information from two sources or ideas.
  4. The learner revises a short passage for register consistency.
  5. The learner gives or responds to peer feedback on a draft or outline.

Script Strategy — Building a personal terminology list

Before drafting a longer project, it is useful to gather the kanji and vocabulary you expect to reuse throughout the piece into a short personal terminology list, with readings noted for anything uncertain. This is a practical strategy that professional and academic writers also use, not a special beginner accommodation.

As you build your terminology list for the Chapter 20 project, decide which words are consolidated enough to write without furigana and which still need reading support, following the same reduction logic used throughout this volume.

Pronunciation and Listening Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Presentation pacing and paragraph breaks

When reading a multi-paragraph draft aloud, a brief pause at each paragraph transition helps a listener follow the structure, in the same way a written paragraph break helps a reader. Practice reading a short outline aloud, pausing slightly longer at transitions such as まず, 次に, and 最後に than at ordinary sentence-final pauses.

  • まず、田中さんの提案について説明します。 — first, I will explain about Tanaka-san's proposal
  • 次に、グループがどう調整したかをまとめます。 — next, I will summarize how the group made adjustments
  • 最後に、そこから学んだことを話します。 — finally, I will talk about what was learned from it

Grammar: Synthesis and Revision

This chapter authorizes synthesis connectors, source attribution, paragraph transitions, register revision, and error analysis, building directly on the attribution language from Chapter 11, the presentation transitions from Chapter 12, and the stance and hedging language from Chapters 17 and 18. Together these let you combine information from more than one source, organize a multi-paragraph piece clearly, and revise a draft for consistent register.

Synthesis connectors — combining information from more than one idea or source

Purpose: to connect two related pieces of information into one organized statement, showing how they relate to each other.

  • 田中さんの提案だけでなく、他のメンバーの意見も参考にした。I referred not only to Tanaka-san's proposal but also to other members' opinions.〜だけでなく〜も, adding a second source
  • アンケートの結果と、会議での話し合いを合わせて考えると、多くの人が新しい方法に賛成しているようだ。Considering the survey results together with the discussion at the meeting, it seems that many people support the new method.〜と〜を合わせて考えると, combining two sources
  • この二つの点から、グループの調整はうまくいったと言える。From these two points, it can be said that the group's adjustment went well.〜から言える, drawing a synthesized conclusion

Synthesis connectors do not introduce new grammar forms on their own; they recombine attribution, comparison, and conclusion language already active from earlier chapters into patterns that connect two or more ideas at once.

Attribution review — によると and related source markers

Purpose: to show clearly where a piece of information came from, a skill first introduced in Chapter 11 and now applied to a longer, multi-source project.

  • 中村さんによると、今回の調整はうまくいったそうだ。According to Nakamura-san, this adjustment reportedly went well.によると + そうだ, reported information
  • アンケートによると、多くのボランティアが新しい方法を支持している。According to the survey, many volunteers support the new method.によると, written source
Language Focus — Keeping attribution consistent across a longer piece

In a short paragraph, a single attribution phrase is usually enough. In a longer, multi-paragraph piece, mark each new source clearly the first time it appears, and use context, topic continuity, and occasional repetition of the source name rather than repeating a full attribution phrase in every sentence, which can feel repetitive and heavy in natural Japanese writing.

Paragraph transitions

Purpose: to guide a reader or listener clearly from one part of an organized piece to the next.

Building Block — A three-part outline frame
まず、[導入]。次に、[支持点１と支持点２]。最後に、[結論]。
Open with an introduction marked by まず, develop supporting points marked by 次に, and close with a conclusion marked by 最後に.
まず、田中さんの提案について説明します。次に、グループがどう話し合い、調整したかをまとめます。最後に、そこから学んだことを話します。
First, I will explain Tanaka-san's proposal. Next, I will summarize how the group discussed it and made adjustments. Finally, I will talk about what was learned from it.
  • この点について、もう少し詳しく説明する。I will explain this point in a little more detail.internal transition within a section
  • 以上のことから、全体として次のように結論できる。From the above, overall it can be concluded as follows.全体として, moving toward the conclusion

Register revision — adjusting a draft for genre and audience

Purpose: to review a draft and make its register consistent throughout, given a stated audience and purpose.

ContextExampleRegister notes
Formal presentation to the whole group今回の調整はうまくいったと考えられます。です／ます style, ようです or 〜と考えられる rather than casual hedges
Casual note to a close friend in the group今回の調整、うまくいったみたい。plain style, みたい acceptable, topic particle often dropped
Written report or summary document今回の調整はうまくいったと言えるだろう。written-style stance marker, avoids conversational fillers

When revising a draft for the Chapter 20 capstone, first decide on a single target register for the whole piece based on its audience and purpose, then check every sentence against that choice rather than mixing polite, plain, and written-style forms without a clear reason.

Error analysis — reviewing a draft for common Volume III error patterns

Purpose: to identify and correct the kinds of errors most likely to appear in a longer Volume III piece.

  • 受け身の主語が抜けていないか確認する。Check whether the subject of a passive sentence has been dropped when it is actually needed.passive/perspective review, Chapter 7
  • 条件文の「と／ば／たら／なら」を正しく使い分けているか確認する。Check whether と, ば, たら, and なら have been used correctly for their different conditional meanings.conditional review, Chapter 9
  • ようですとみたいですの使い分けが場面に合っているか確認する。Check whether the choice between ようです and みたいです fits the context.register review, Chapter 18

Model Dialogue

Setting: Emma shares her outline and a short draft paragraph with 中村さん for feedback before writing the full project.

エマ 構成を考えてみました。まず提案の説明、次に調整の話し合い、最後に学んだことです。どう思いますか。 I've thought about the structure. First an explanation of the proposal, next the discussion of the adjustment, and finally what was learned. What do you think?
中村さん いい構成だと思います。資料は、何を使う予定ですか。 I think that's a good structure. What materials are you planning to use?
エマ アンケートの結果と、あの日の話し合いのメモを使うつもりです。この二つを合わせて考えると、調整はうまくいったと言えると思います。 I plan to use the survey results and the notes from that day's discussion. Considering these two together, I think it can be said that the adjustment went well.
中村さん いいですね。下書きの一部を見せてもらえますか。 That's good. Could you show me part of the draft?
エマ はい、どうぞ。「今回の調整、うまくいったと思う」と書いたんですが、これでいいですか。 Yes, here you go. I wrote "I think this adjustment went well," but is this okay?
中村さん 内容はいいんですが、発表するなら、少し丁寧な言い方に直したほうがいいかもしれません。「今回の調整はうまくいったと考えられます」のような形はどうでしょうか。 The content is good, but if you're going to present it, it might be better to revise it into a slightly more polite phrasing. How about a form like "It can be considered that this adjustment went well"?
エマ なるほど、全体をその文体に見直します。ありがとうございます。 I see, I'll review the whole piece to match that style. Thank you.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Peer review and shared preparation

In many Japanese school, community, and workplace settings, sharing an outline or draft with a colleague, mentor, or supervisor before a final presentation is a routine and expected step, rather than a sign of weak preparation. Seeking early feedback, as Emma does with 中村さん in this chapter, reflects one common and valued approach to project work.

Regional Note — Variation in presentation conventions

Expectations for how formal a presentation should sound, how much feedback is exchanged beforehand, and how directly peer feedback is given can vary by school, workplace, region, and generation. The patterns modeled in this chapter reflect one common approach rather than a single universal standard.

Language Note — Synthesis and attribution across languages

Combining information from more than one source and marking where each idea came from is a skill needed in academic and professional writing across many languages. Comparing how your own language signals synthesis and attribution can help you notice how Japanese expressions such as によると and 合わせて考えると serve a similar function, adjusted for Japanese written conventions.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Mixing polite and plain style within one formal piece

Error: *今回の調整はうまくいったと思う。それに、みんな満足しています。 mixes plain and polite predicates in the same formal passage.

Correction: 今回の調整はうまくいったと思います。それに、みんな満足しています。 — choose one register for the whole piece and check every sentence against it.

Common Mistake — Presenting synthesized information without attribution

Error: *多くの人が新しい方法に賛成している。 presents a claim from a survey as if it were the writer's own direct knowledge.

Correction: アンケートによると、多くの人が新しい方法に賛成しているようだ。 — mark the source and, where appropriate, the degree of certainty.

Common Mistake — Skipping the outline and drafting directly

Error: Writing a full draft without first listing an introduction, supporting points, and a conclusion often produces a passage that is difficult for a reader to follow, even when individual sentences are accurate.

Correction: Build a short outline using まず, 次に, and 最後に before drafting, as modeled in the Building Block box in the Grammar section, and check the finished draft against that outline.

可能・使役・依頼Ability, Causation, and Requests

Potential, causative, permission, and request nuance · Speak and Write Japanese: Independent Communication and Advanced Expression

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Form and use the potential form of verbs with confidence, distinguishing it clearly from the passive form introduced in Chapter 7.
  2. Form and use the causative form of verbs to describe making or letting someone do something.
  3. Use 〜させてください to request permission to do something yourself, distinguishing this pattern from ordinary requests that ask someone else to act.
  4. Distinguish causative sentences that express compulsion from those that express permission, based on context and verb choice.
  5. Talk about skills, rules, permissions, and workplace tasks using potential and causative forms appropriately.
  6. Read and listen to short texts about rules, permissions, and workplace procedures, identifying potential and causative forms and their meaning.
  7. Write and narrate a short account of a skill, a rule, or a permission request using potential and causative forms accurately.

Communication Scenario

Emma has started a short-term volunteer position at the same community center where her discussion group meets. On her first day, the coordinator explains what volunteers are allowed to do, what they are not allowed to do without asking, and what new skills the position will let her practice. Emma also wants to ask her supervisor for permission to leave twenty minutes early one day to attend a language exchange event, and she wants to explain to Yuki afterward what she can now do at the center and what she asked permission to do. This chapter's Practical Performance Task asks you to describe your own abilities and a set of rules or permissions in a familiar setting, then request permission to do something yourself, using potential, causative, and 〜させてください forms.

  1. The speaker or writer uses the potential form to describe what someone is or is not able to do.
  2. The speaker or writer uses the causative form to describe making or letting someone do something.
  3. The speaker uses 〜させてください to request permission to do something themselves, rather than asking someone else to act.

Script Strategy — Recognizing 可 as a productive component

The character 可, meaning roughly "permissible" or "possible," appears in several words active in this chapter and beyond: 許可 (permission) and 可能 (possibility, related to this chapter's potential forms), and later in the volume in other compounds involving permission or possibility. Recognizing 可 as a recurring component can help you predict that a compound relates to permission or possibility, but the exact reading and precise meaning of each word must still be checked individually.

In this chapter's readings, only names, unfamiliar loanwords, and this chapter's new kanji on first appearance receive furigana.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Long causative and potential endings

Potential and causative forms in this chapter often produce longer verb endings than the plain dictionary forms learners have used so far. Keeping every mora distinct, without rushing or dropping vowels, becomes especially important with these longer forms.

  • 話せる (はなせる): three distinct morae in the ending; do not shorten to はなせ.
  • やらせてください: the small っ after さ in させて must be held for its own timing unit, and the long final い of ください must not be shortened.
  • 出かけさせられました: a longer causative-passive-style sequence built from 出かける; practice this word slowly, mora by mora, before speeding up to natural pace.

Practice reading the Model Dialogue aloud, paying particular attention to keeping each mora in these longer endings distinct, even when speaking at a natural conversational pace.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns: Ability, Causation, and Requests

This chapter consolidates the potential form, introduces the causative form, and teaches 〜させてください as a way to request permission to act. All three systems describe a relationship between a person and an action — being able to do it, making or letting someone do it, or asking to be allowed to do it — and together they let you talk about skills, rules, and permissions with more precision than the plain and polite forms alone allow.

Consolidating the potential form

Purpose: to state clearly what someone is or is not able to do, distinguishing potential meaning from the passive meaning covered in Chapter 7.

  • 話す → 話せるto speak → to be able to speakGroup 1 (u-verb): change final -u to -e, add -る
  • 食べる → 食べられるto eat → to be able to eatGroup 2 (ru-verb): drop -る, add -られる (identical in form to the passive; distinguished by context)
  • する → できるto do → to be able to doirregular; suppletive form, not built with -られる
  • 来る → 来られるto come → to be able to comeirregular; identical in form to the passive 来られる, distinguished by context

For Group 2 verbs and 来る, the potential form and the passive form introduced in Chapter 7 look identical. Context, the surrounding particles, and the presence of an agent phrase (which signals passive, not potential) help distinguish the two: 田中さんに来られて困りました ("I was troubled by Tanaka coming," passive) versus 田中さんは来られます ("Tanaka is able to come," potential).

Introducing the causative form

Purpose: to describe making or letting someone do something, distinguishing compulsion from permission by context and verb choice.

  • 書く → 書かせるto write → to make/let (someone) writeGroup 1 (u-verb): change final -u to -a, add -せる
  • 見る → 見させるto see/look at → to make/let (someone) look atGroup 2 (ru-verb): drop -る, add -させる
  • する → させるto do → to make/let (someone) doirregular
  • 来る → 来させるto come → to make/let (someone) comeirregular
Building Block — Compulsion or permission?
causative sentence → ask: is the causer requiring the action, or allowing it? → context, verb meaning, and particles (を for compulsion with intransitive verbs, に for the causee with transitive verbs) guide the interpretation
The same causative form can express "made someone do it" or "let someone do it"; the surrounding context, not the verb form itself, usually makes the intended meaning clear.
担当者は新しいボランティアに規則を確認させました。一方、担当者は私に早く帰らせてくれました。
The person in charge had the new volunteers check the rules. On the other hand, the person in charge let me go home early.

確認させました reads most naturally as compulsion (having someone do a required task), while 帰らせてくれました, with the added verb くれる, signals that the causative action was a favor or permission granted to the speaker, not an imposition.

〜させてください — requesting permission to act

Purpose: to ask permission to do something yourself, using the causative te-form plus ください, rather than asking someone else to perform an action.

  • 今日は早く帰らせてください。Please let me go home early today.causative te-form of 帰る + ください; the speaker asks to be allowed to act
  • この規則について質問させてください。Please let me ask a question about this rule.causative te-form of 質問する + ください
  • その仕事を私にやらせてください。Please let me be the one to do that task.causative te-form of やる + ください, with に marking the person requesting the role

〜させてください differs from an ordinary request such as 手伝ってください ("please help me"), which asks someone else to act. 〜させてください instead asks for permission for the speaker to act, and is common in workplace, service, and institutional contexts when requesting to take on a task or leave a duty early.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Emma speaks with her volunteer supervisor, たなかさん, on her first day at the community center.

たなか エマさんは日本語の書類を読めますか。 Emma, are you able to read documents in Japanese?
エマ 簡単なものなら読めます。難しい言葉は、まだ全部は分かりません。 I can read simple ones. I still don't understand all the difficult vocabulary.
たなか 分かりました。じゃあ、最初は簡単な受付の仕事をさせますね。規則について何か質問はありますか。 I see. Then at first I'll have you do the simple front-desk work. Do you have any questions about the rules?
エマ はい、一つ質問させてください。ボランティアは何時まで働けますか。 Yes, please let me ask one question. Until what time are volunteers able to work?
たなか 普通は五時までですが、用事があれば、早く帰らせますよ。 Normally until five, but if you have something to attend to, I'll let you go home early.
エマ 実は来週、言語交流のイベントがあるので、その日は少し早く帰らせてください。 Actually, there's a language exchange event next week, so please let me go home a little early that day.

  2. What task will Tanaka have Emma do first, and what causative form does Tanaka use?
  3. What does Emma request permission to do, and what pattern does she use to ask?

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Requesting permission in workplace and volunteer settings

In many Japanese workplaces, schools, and volunteer organizations, requesting permission with 〜させてください before taking on a task, leaving early, or asking a question is a common and expected way of showing awareness of one's role and the group's expectations. This pattern is widely used across a range of settings and does not by itself signal excessive formality or hesitation; it functions as an ordinary way of framing a request as something the speaker is asking to be allowed to do, rather than assuming it.

Regional Note — Expressing "can" across languages

Many languages distinguish ability, permission, and possibility with separate words or constructions, though the boundaries differ. English "can" covers ability, permission, and possibility fairly broadly, while Japanese separates these more explicitly across the potential form, 〜てもいい for permission, and other constructions. Comparing how your own language handles these distinctions can help clarify which meaning a given Japanese sentence is expressing.

Register Note — Causative forms are not automatically harsh

A causative sentence describing a rule or requirement, such as 規則を確認させました, is not automatically harsh or authoritarian; it simply states that someone was made to do something, which is often a neutral description of ordinary workplace or institutional procedure. Tone, added expressions such as くれる, and context determine whether a causative sentence sounds neutral, considerate, or heavy-handed, not the causative form itself.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Confusing the potential and passive forms of Group 2 verbs

Error: *先生に見られる intended to mean "I am able to be seen by the teacher," when the intended meaning was actually "I am able to see," misreading a passive-shaped sentence as potential without checking the agent marker and context.

Correction: 見られる alone, without an agent phrase and in a context about ability, means "to be able to see/look at"; check for an explicit agent phrase and context before assuming a Group 2 -られる form is passive rather than potential.

Common Mistake — Using 〜させてください to ask someone else to act

Error: *これを手伝わせてください。 intended as "please help me with this," but actually meaning "please let me help with this," reversing who is expected to perform the action.

Correction: これを手伝ってください。 — use the plain request form when asking someone else to act, and reserve 〜させてください for requesting permission to act yourself.

Common Mistake — Treating every causative sentence as compulsion

Error: Reading 早く帰らせてくれました as "he forced me to go home early," missing the permission meaning signaled by くれる.

Correction: 早く帰らせてくれました means "he let me go home early (as a favor to me)"; check for くれる, context, and the nature of the action before assuming a causative sentence expresses compulsion.

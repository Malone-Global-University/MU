敬語の基礎を使うUsing Basic Keigo

Respectful and humble forms in practical contexts · Speak and Write Japanese: Independent Communication and Advanced Expression

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Explain why respectful (尊敬語), humble (謙譲語), and polite (丁寧語) language exist as separate systems within Japanese keigo.
  2. Recognize and produce common お/ご〜になる respectful forms when describing another person's actions in a formal context.
  3. Recognize and produce common お/ご〜する humble forms when describing your own actions toward someone you are treating with respect.
  4. Use a set of high-frequency fixed keigo expressions in emails, service encounters, introductions, visits, and interviews.
  5. Distinguish situations that call for keigo from situations that call for ordinary polite or plain Japanese.
  6. Read and listen to short formal exchanges and emails that use basic keigo, and identify their respectful and humble forms.
  7. Write and perform a short formal email or introduction using only the keigo patterns authorized in this chapter.

Communication Scenario

Emma has arranged a short informational interview with a staff member at a local library association, Tanaka-san, to ask about volunteer opportunities for her discussion group's community project. Before the meeting, she needs to write a formal email requesting the appointment, and during the meeting she needs to introduce herself, ask questions, and thank Tanaka-san appropriately — all without the casual language she uses with Yuki. Yuki helps her prepare by reviewing which everyday polite sentences need to become more formal, and which set phrases she can simply memorize and use as they are. This chapter's Practical Performance Task asks you to write a similar request email and perform a short formal self-introduction and interview exchange, using only the basic keigo patterns introduced here.

  1. The writer or speaker uses appropriate opening and closing formulas for a formal request.
  2. The writer or speaker uses at least one respectful (尊敬語) form to describe the other person's action.
  3. The writer or speaker uses at least one humble (謙譲語) form to describe their own action.

Script Strategy — Recognizing the respect component お／ご

The honorific prefixes お and ご attach to many nouns and verb stems in this chapter's grammar patterns. お usually attaches to native Japanese (和語) words, such as お電話 and お名前, while ご usually attaches to Sino-Japanese (漢語) words, such as ご連絡 and ご確認. This is a useful tendency for guessing which prefix a new word takes, but it is not a rule without exceptions, so check new vocabulary items individually rather than applying the pattern automatically.

In this chapter's readings, only names, unfamiliar loanwords, and this chapter's new kanji on first appearance receive furigana.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Careful articulation in formal speech

Formal spoken Japanese, such as the language used in an interview or a first visit, is often produced somewhat more slowly and clearly than casual conversation, with fuller pronunciation of vowels and endings that might otherwise be reduced. This chapter's vocabulary includes several long vowels and moraic ん that are easy to shorten under nervousness; holding them at full length supports both accuracy and a calm, formal impression.

  • 訪問 (ほうもん): two long vowels in a row, in ほう and もん's preceding vowel; do not shorten either.
  • 面接 (めんせつ): the moraic ん before せ must be held for its own mora, distinct from めせつ.
  • お世話になっております: a common fixed expression from this chapter's Grammar section; practice it as one connected phrase rather than word by word.

Practice reading the Model Dialogue aloud at a clear, moderate pace, as if greeting someone for the first time in a formal setting.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns: Basic Keigo

Japanese keigo (敬語) is not one system but three related systems: respectful language (尊敬語), which raises the status of someone else's action; humble language (謙譲語), which lowers the status of your own action to show respect toward someone else; and polite language (丁寧語), the です／ます style you have already used since Volume I. This chapter introduces the basic productive forms of respectful and humble language, along with several fixed expressions that are learned as set phrases before their internal grammar is fully analyzed. Advanced and less common keigo forms remain outside this chapter's scope and will be developed further in Volume IV.

お/ご〜になる — Respectful forms for another person's action

Purpose: to describe an action performed by someone you are treating with respect — a customer, a supervisor, an interviewer, or an unfamiliar adult — without using their action in plain です／ます form.

  • 田中さんはもう資料をお読みになりましたか。Has Tanaka-san already read the materials?お + Vます-stem + になる — respectful, for the other person's action
  • ご担当者様は何時にご出発になりますか。What time will the person in charge be departing?ご + noun/する-verb stem + になる — respectful, Sino-Japanese base

お attaches mainly to native Japanese verb stems; ご attaches mainly to Sino-Japanese する-verb stems. A small number of high-frequency verbs, such as 言う, 見る, 行く／来る, and する, use irregular respectful forms (おっしゃる, ご覧になる, いらっしゃる, なさる) that must be learned individually rather than built with this pattern; these irregular forms are introduced here for recognition and limited use in fixed contexts only.

お/ご〜する — Humble forms for your own action

Purpose: to describe your own action toward someone you are treating with respect, lowering your own action rather than raising theirs.

  • 明日の午後、資料をお送りします。I will send the materials tomorrow afternoon.お + Vます-stem + する — humble, describing the speaker's action
  • 来週、そちらにご連絡いたします。I will contact you next week.ご + noun + いたす — humble, formal variant of する

いたす is a humble equivalent of する and combines with ご〜 to sound more formal than ご〜する alone; both are acceptable in this chapter, and いたす is preferred in written formal correspondence.

Common fixed keigo expressions

Purpose: several everyday formal expressions are learned first as complete, memorized units. Their internal grammar draws on the patterns above, but they should be used as set phrases at this stage rather than freely rebuilt from parts.

  • いつもお世話になっております。Thank you for your continued support. (lit., I am always in your care.)fixed opening for business and formal email correspondence
  • よろしくお願いいたします。Thank you in advance for your kindness in this matter.fixed closing for requests and formal correspondence
  • 恐れ入りますが、〜I'm sorry to trouble you, but ~fixed softener before a formal request
  • 失礼いたします。Excuse me. / Please excuse me. (used both entering and leaving a formal setting)fixed formula for formal arrivals and departures

Building a basic keigo request email

Building Block — A short formal request email
opening formula → self-identification and reason for writing → softened request using お/ご〜 patterns → closing formula
This four-stage pattern combines the fixed opening and closing expressions with the productive お/ご〜になる and お/ご〜する patterns, and gives this chapter's Practical Performance Task its underlying shape.
いつもお世話になっております。○○大学のエマと申します。地域の図書館ボランティアについてお伺いしたく、ご連絡いたしました。恐れ入りますが、来週のご都合のよろしい日時をお知らせいただけますでしょうか。よろしくお願いいたします。
Thank you for your continued support. My name is Emma, and I'm from ○○ University. I'm contacting you because I would like to ask about local library volunteering. I'm sorry to trouble you, but could you let me know a convenient date and time for you next week? Thank you in advance for your kindness in this matter.

Writing Workshop

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Keigo as relationship management, not just politeness

Keigo is often introduced to learners simply as "being polite," but it more precisely marks social distance, role, and relationship: a person may use keigo toward a stranger, a customer, or a supervisor regardless of that person's age, and may use plain or ordinary polite forms with a close colleague of higher status. Choosing keigo is less about how much respect one personally feels and more about signaling the kind of relationship and setting the conversation is taking place in.

Regional Note — Formal address conventions elsewhere

Many languages mark social distance and formality through separate address forms, verb forms, or fixed expressions, though the details differ widely — some rely mainly on pronoun choice, others on verb conjugation, and others on vocabulary substitution as in Japanese keigo. Comparing how your own language signals formality can help clarify what is functionally similar to keigo and what is genuinely distinct to Japanese.

Register Note — Keigo is not required everywhere at once

Using keigo with a close friend or family member can sound distant or even sarcastic rather than respectful, just as failing to use keigo with a first-time business contact can sound careless. This chapter's exercises are limited to contexts where keigo is expected — emails, service encounters, visits, and interviews — so that keigo is practiced where it naturally belongs rather than as a blanket "more polite is always better" habit.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Using お/ご〜になる for your own action

Error: *私はお読みになりました。 using the respectful form お〜になる to describe the speaker's own action.

Correction: 私は拝見しました。 or 私は読みました。 — respectful forms describe someone else's action; describe your own action with a humble or ordinary polite form instead.

Common Mistake — Using お/ご〜する for someone else's action

Error: *田中さんはもうご連絡しましたか。 using the humble form ご〜する to ask about another person's action.

Correction: 田中さんはもうご連絡になりましたか。 — humble forms lower the speaker's own action; use a respectful form to describe someone else's action.

Common Mistake — Mixing keigo with casual plain-form endings

Error: *いつもお世話になっております。明日行くね。 mixing a formal opening with a casual plain-form ending in the same message.

Correction: いつもお世話になっております。明日、伺います。 — maintain one consistent formal register throughout a keigo message, just as register consistency matters across a longer essay.

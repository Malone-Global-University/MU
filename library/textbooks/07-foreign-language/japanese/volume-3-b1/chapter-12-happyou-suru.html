発表するPresenting Information

Oral presentation structure and visuals · Speak and Write Japanese: Independent Communication and Advanced Expression

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Organize a short oral presentation into an opening, body, and closing using standard presentation transitions.
  2. Introduce a chart, survey result, or set of numbers clearly enough for listeners to follow without seeing the visual in detail.
  3. Cite a survey, report, or source in simple terms appropriate to a classroom or community presentation.
  4. Compare two or more results or options using previously learned comparison patterns.
  5. State a result or conclusion clearly, distinguishing it from supporting detail.
  6. Respond to a listener's question during or after a presentation.
  7. Complete listening, speaking, reading, and writing tasks built around a short presentation.

Communication Scenario

After summarizing the recycling article for the community center newsletter, Emma has been asked to present a short update at the next volunteer meeting. She has a simple survey of how many households in the neighborhood have started using the new sorting method, and she wants to explain the results clearly, compare this month's numbers with last month's, and be ready to answer questions from volunteers. This chapter's Practical Performance Task asks you to plan, organize, and deliver a short presentation of your own, using a simple visual or set of numbers.

  1. The presenter opens by stating the topic and purpose of the presentation.
  2. The presenter introduces a chart, survey, or set of numbers using clear presentation language.
  3. The presenter compares results and states a conclusion.
  4. The presenter closes the presentation and responds to listener questions.

Pronunciation and Listening Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Presentation pacing and emphasis

A clear oral presentation groups words by phrase, pauses briefly at each transition word, and slightly emphasizes the number or result being reported. Unlike ordinary conversation, a short pause after words such as まず, 次に, and 以上です helps listeners follow the structure of the talk.

  • まず、調査の目的についてお話しします。 (まず、ちょうさのもくてきについておはなしします。): pause briefly after まず.
  • 以上です。ご質問はありますか。: keep 以上です as one phrase group, then pause before inviting questions.

Practice reading the Reading Assessment passage aloud as if giving the presentation yourself, pausing at each transition word and slightly emphasizing the reported numbers.

Grammar: Presentation Structure and Citation

This chapter authorizes presentation transitions, simple source citation, comparison using previously learned patterns, result language, and Q&A response, building on the source-marking and attribution systems developed in Chapter 11.

Presentation transitions

Purpose: to signal the structure of a presentation so listeners can follow the opening, body, and closing without seeing a written outline.

  • それでは、発表を始めます。Now then, I will begin the presentation.それでは opens a formal presentation
  • まず、調査の目的についてお話しします。次に、結果をご紹介します。最後に、まとめをお伝えします。First, I will talk about the purpose of the survey. Next, I will introduce the results. Finally, I will share a summary.まず / 次に / 最後に mark opening, body, and closing
  • 以上です。That is all. (closing a report or presentation)formulaic closing phrase

Introducing a chart or number simply

Purpose: to describe a chart, survey, or set of numbers clearly enough for a listener who cannot see the visual.

  • このグラフは、参加者の割合を表しています。This graph shows the proportion of participants.このグラフは〜を表しています as a description frame
  • 調査によると、住民の約六十パーセントが新しい分別に協力しています。According to the survey, about sixty percent of residents are cooperating with the new sorting method.によると (from Ch. 11) marking the survey as source

Comparison and result language

Purpose: to compare two results and state a conclusion clearly, using comparison patterns already active from earlier volumes.

  • 先月より今月のほうが、参加者が多いです。Compared to last month, this month has more participants.〜より〜のほうが comparison, already active
  • この調査の結果、参加者が増えていることが分かりました。As a result of this survey, it became clear that the number of participants is increasing.〜の結果 introducing a stated conclusion

Simple source citation

Purpose: to tell listeners where information comes from, without using specialized academic citation conventions.

  • この調査は、先月、ボランティアが行いました。This survey was conducted last month by the volunteers.simple statement of who conducted the source
  • 市の発表によると、来月から新しい分別が始まります。According to the city's announcement, new sorting rules will begin next month.によると, recycled directly from Chapter 11

Responding to a question (Q&A)

Purpose: to respond politely and clearly to a listener's question during or after a presentation.

  • ご質問ありがとうございます。Thank you for your question.formulaic Q&A opener
  • その点については、来月また調査する予定です。Regarding that point, we plan to survey again next month.その点については as a formal way to acknowledge a question before answering
Building Block — A short presentation's structure
opening (それでは／まず) + purpose statement + body (次に + chart/number + source) + comparison/result + closing (最後に／以上です) + Q&A response
A short classroom or community presentation typically opens with a transition phrase and a statement of purpose, introduces a chart or number together with its source, compares results and states a conclusion, closes with a formulaic phrase such as 以上です, and then responds to listener questions using a polite acknowledgment before answering.
それでは、発表を始めます。まず、調査の目的についてお話しします。この調査は、先月、ボランティアが行いました。調査によると、住民の約六十パーセントが新しい分別に協力しています。先月より今月のほうが、参加者が多いです。以上です。ご質問はありますか。
Now then, I will begin the presentation. First, I will talk about the purpose of the survey. This survey was conducted last month by the volunteers. According to the survey, about sixty percent of residents are cooperating with the new sorting method. Compared to last month, this month has more participants. That is all. Do you have any questions?

Model Dialogue

Setting: Emma rehearses her volunteer-meeting presentation with her supervisor, 田中さん, before delivering it.

田中さん エマさん、発表の準備はできましたか。まずは何について話しますか。 Emma, are you ready to present? What will you talk about first?
エマ はい。それでは、発表を始めます。まず、調査の目的についてお話しします。この調査は、先月、ボランティアが行いました。 Yes. Now then, I will begin the presentation. First, I will talk about the purpose of the survey. This survey was conducted last month by the volunteers.
田中さん いいですね。次は、結果ですね。 That's good. Next is the results, right?
エマ はい。次に、結果をご紹介します。調査によると、住民の約六十パーセントが新しい分別に協力しています。先月より今月のほうが、参加者が多いです。 Yes. Next, I will introduce the results. According to the survey, about sixty percent of residents are cooperating with the new sorting method. Compared to last month, this month has more participants.
田中さん 最後はどうしますか。 How will you close it?
エマ 最後に、まとめをお伝えします。この調査の結果、参加者が増えていることが分かりました。以上です。ご質問はありますか。 Finally, I will share a summary. As a result of this survey, it became clear that the number of participants is increasing. That is all. Do you have any questions?
田中さん よくできています。もし誰かが「来月はどうなりますか」と質問したら、どう答えますか。 Very well done. If someone asks, "What will happen next month?" how will you answer?
エマ ご質問ありがとうございます。その点については、来月また調査する予定です。 Thank you for your question. Regarding that point, we plan to survey again next month.

ないようりかい — Comprehension questions:

  1. What three transition words does Emma use to organize her presentation?
  2. Who conducted the survey Emma is presenting?
  3. How does Emma respond when 田中さん asks a practice question?

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Community and classroom presentations

Short presentations of survey results, learning reports, and project updates are common in schools, workplaces, and community organizations. The structure taught in this chapter, an opening, a body with cited information, and a formulaic closing such as 以上です, reflects a widely used pattern in these settings rather than a single fixed national convention.

Regional Note — Formality of presentations

The level of formality expected in a presentation can vary by setting, audience, and organization. A classroom presentation to classmates may use somewhat less formal language than a report to a workplace supervisor or a public institution, even when the overall structure remains similar. Adjust vocabulary and closing phrases to the audience rather than assuming one fixed register for every presentation.

Language Note — Presentation structure across languages

Many languages use similar three-part presentation structures, with an opening, body, and closing, though the specific transition words differ. Comparing how your own language signals "first," "next," and "in conclusion" can help clarify why Japanese presentations consistently mark these transitions with words such as まず, 次に, and 最後に.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Omitting presentation transitions

Error: Moving directly from the purpose statement to the results without any transition, leaving listeners unsure whether the speaker has finished the opening.

Correction: まず、調査の目的についてお話しします。次に、結果をご紹介します。 — mark each new part of the presentation with a transition word such as まず or 次に.

Common Mistake — Describing a chart without stating what it shows

Error: *これがグラフです。 stated alone, without explaining what the chart represents.

Correction: このグラフは、参加者の割合を表しています。 — always state what a chart or number represents, since listeners cannot see the visual in detail.

Common Mistake — Answering a question without acknowledging it

Error: Answering a listener's question immediately with only the answer, which can sound abrupt in a formal presentation setting.

Correction: ご質問ありがとうございます。その点については、来月また調査する予定です。 — briefly acknowledge the question before answering, especially in formal or institutional presentations.

Practical Performance Task

パフォーマンスタスク

発表する — Planning, organizing, and delivering a short presentation

Using a chart, survey, or set of numbers of your choice, real or invented for practice, complete all four components below.

  • Presentation outline: Write a short outline using まず, 次に, and 最後に (or similar transitions) that lists the purpose, source, comparison or result, and closing of your presentation.
  • Written script and handout text: Write a presentation script of at least six sentences, following the Writing Workshop steps, including a source citation, a comparison, and a stated result.
  • Spoken presentation: With a partner, instructor, or recording, deliver a thirty-to-sixty-second spoken presentation based on your script, including at least one prepared response to a listener's question.
  • Peer or self-audit: Exchange your script with a partner, or review your own after a break, and check specifically that the chart or numbers are clearly described, the source is cited, and the closing and Q&A response are included.

Assessment focus: clear presentation structure with recognizable transitions; accurate and natural description of a chart or numbers; correct use of a simple source citation; accurate comparison and clearly stated result; and comprehensible, appropriately paced spoken delivery with a polite Q&A response.

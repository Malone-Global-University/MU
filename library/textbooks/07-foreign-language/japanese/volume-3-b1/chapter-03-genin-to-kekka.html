原因と結果Cause and Consequence

Causal chains and problem analysis · Speak and Write Japanese: Independent Communication and Advanced Expression

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Explain the reason for a situation or decision using ため.
  2. Distinguish a negative outcome expressed with せいで from a positive or neutral outcome expressed with おかげで.
  3. State the result of a cause using 結果 and connect a cause to its result across two sentences.
  4. Express that one factor determines or influences an outcome using によって.
  5. Recognize and use a small set of cause/result discourse connectors to build a causal chain of more than one step.
  6. Analyze a simple problem by identifying its cause, its immediate result, and a further consequence.
  7. Read and write mixed-script Japanese describing problems, technology, environment, and decisions, using kanji strategies for cause, result, and influence.

Communication Scenario

Encouraged by the positive response to her explanation of the recycling system, Emma posts a follow-up in the discussion group: a short analysis of why a local convenience store near her apartment recently started charging for plastic bags, and what has changed as a result. Yuki reads the draft and notices that Emma has stated the cause and the result but has not clearly connected them — the two ideas sit side by side rather than forming a chain. Yuki suggests naming the cause explicitly with ため, describing the result with 結果, and adding a second-order consequence that follows from the first result. This chapter's Practical Performance Task asks you to analyze a problem, decision, or change from your own life or community in the same way: cause, immediate result, and further consequence.

  1. The writer states a clear cause using ため or a related cause expression.
  2. The writer states at least one direct result using 結果 or another result expression.
  3. The writer extends the chain with a second, further consequence that follows from the first result.

Script Strategy — Recognizing a shared component across cause-and-result words

結果 combines 結 (to tie; to conclude) and 果 (fruit; outcome) — the image of an outcome that has "borne fruit" from an earlier cause can help the compound stick in memory, though this is a memory aid rather than a rule for predicting meaning from components in general. 増加する and 減少する share the pattern of a directional verb root plus する, a pattern you will see again with other formal intransitive verbs describing change over time.

New Volume III kanji introduced in this chapter, such as those above, receive furigana at first appearance and are gradually reduced according to this volume's matrices. Kanji introduced in Chapters 1 and 2, such as 評価, 構成, 仕組み, and 特徴, appear with reduced furigana in this chapter's readings.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Geminate consonants in cause-and-result vocabulary

Several key words in this chapter contain a geminate consonant marked by small っ, and holding that consonant length clearly is important for both comprehension and meaning. 結果 (けっか) has a geminate っ before か; shortening it can make the word harder to recognize in fast speech. 増加 (ぞうか) has a long vowel in ぞう that should be held for two full morae, distinct from a shortened ぞ か with no length contrast.

  • 結果 (けっか): hold the small っ fully before releasing into か.
  • 影響 (えいきょう): both the long vowel えい and the long vowel きょう need full mora timing; rushing either changes the rhythm of the word.
  • 増加する (ぞうかする): keep the long vowel in ぞう clearly held before moving to か.

Practice reading the Building Block example in the Grammar section aloud, paying particular attention to holding these long vowels and the geminate consonant in 結果.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter introduces four connected tools for cause-and-result explanation: stating a reason with ため, distinguishing negative and positive outcomes with せいで and おかげで, naming a result with 結果, and marking an influencing factor with によって.

Stating a reason with ため

Purpose: to give the reason or cause behind a situation, decision, or change, in a relatively neutral and often written or formal register.

  • プラスチックごみを減らすため、この店ではレジ袋が有料になった。In order to reduce plastic waste, this store started charging for shopping bags.Vる/Nの + ため — purpose or reason clause before the main clause
  • 値段が上がったのは、原材料が値上がりしたためだ。The reason the price went up is that raw materials became more expensive.のは〜ためだ — naming the cause as the explanation for a stated fact

ため can express either purpose ("in order to") or reason ("because of"), and the surrounding clause usually makes the intended meaning clear. In this chapter, ため is used mainly for reason, as in the examples above.

Distinguishing せいで and おかげで

Purpose: to attribute an outcome to a cause while also signaling whether the outcome is negative (せいで) or positive/neutral (おかげで).

  • 天気が悪かったせいで、イベントは中止になった。Because of the bad weather, the event was cancelled.Vた/Nの + せいで — negative outcome attributed to a cause
  • 新しいアプリのおかげで、予約がずっと簡単になった。Thanks to the new app, making reservations has become much easier.Vた/Nの + おかげで — positive outcome attributed to a cause

Using せいで for a positive outcome, or おかげで for a clearly negative one, sounds unnatural or sarcastic to many listeners; this chapter authorizes both forms only with their standard negative/positive association, labeled clearly as shown above.

Naming a result with 結果

Purpose: to state the outcome that followed from a cause already described, connecting two sentences into a cause-then-result sequence.

  • この店ではレジ袋が有料になった。その結果、マイバッグを持ってくる客が増えた。This store started charging for shopping bags. As a result, the number of customers bringing their own bags increased.その結果、〜 — opens a sentence stating the result of the prior sentence
  • 売り上げが伸びた結果、新しい店員を雇うことになった。As a result of sales growing, they ended up hiring a new employee.Vた + 結果、〜 — within a single sentence, result follows a described cause

Marking an influencing factor with によって

Purpose: to identify the factor that determines, causes, or brings about variation in an outcome.

  • 地域によって、ごみの分別のルールが違う。The rules for sorting trash differ depending on the area.Nによって、〜が違う/異なる — variation caused by the named factor
  • 新しい技術によって、多くの作業が自動化された。Due to new technology, many tasks have become automated.Nによって + passive predicate — cause/agent of a resulting change

によって has two related uses active in this chapter: marking a factor that causes variation between cases, and marking the agent or cause behind a passive-predicate result. Both uses are illustrated above; more advanced によって uses are not required at this stage.

Building a cause-to-consequence chain

Building Block — A cause-and-result analysis paragraph
state the problem or change → give its cause with ため, せいで, or おかげで → state the direct result with 結果 → extend the chain with a further consequence → close with one sentence about how people are responding or what might happen next
This five-stage pattern builds a two-step causal chain (cause → result → further consequence) rather than stopping at a single cause-and-effect pair, and gives this chapter's Practical Performance Task its underlying shape.
最近、近所のコンビニでレジ袋が有料になった。プラスチックごみを減らすためだそうだ。その結果、マイバッグを持ってくる客が増えた。さらに、レジ袋を使わない習慣が広がった結果、店のプラスチックごみの量も少しずつ減っているらしい。地域によって対策の進み方は違うが、この変化は環境への負担を減らす一歩になっていると思う。
Recently, the convenience store in my neighborhood started charging for shopping bags. I hear it's in order to reduce plastic waste. As a result, the number of customers bringing their own bags has increased. Furthermore, as the habit of not using shopping bags has spread, it seems that the store's amount of plastic waste is also gradually decreasing. How far the countermeasures have progressed differs by area, but I think this change is a step toward reducing the burden on the environment.

Grammar Review Practice

  1. Write one sentence giving a reason using ため.
  2. Write one sentence describing a negative outcome using せいで.
  3. Write one sentence describing a positive outcome using おかげで.
  4. Write two connected sentences: a cause, followed by a result introduced with その結果.
  5. Write one sentence using によって to describe variation between areas, groups, or cases.

Writing Workshop

In this workshop you will draft the written portion of your Practical Performance Task: a cause-and-result analysis of a problem, decision, or change, following the five-stage Building Block pattern from the Grammar section.

  1. State the problem or change in one sentence.
  2. Give its cause using ため, せいで, or おかげで, choosing the form that matches whether the outcome is negative, positive, or neutral.
  3. State the direct result using 結果 or その結果.
  4. Extend the chain with one further consequence that follows from the first result.
  5. Close with one sentence about how people are responding or what might happen next, and revise using the checklist below.

チェックリスト — Editing checklist:

  • My analysis states the problem or change clearly before naming a cause.
  • My cause expression (ため, せいで, or おかげで) matches whether the outcome is negative, positive, or neutral.
  • My analysis states at least one direct result using 結果 or その結果.
  • My analysis extends the chain with at least one further consequence.
  • If I described variation between areas, groups, or situations, I used によって correctly.
  • My sentences connect to one another rather than reading as a disconnected list of facts.
  • I maintained one consistent polite or plain style throughout.
  • I checked unfamiliar kanji using the strategies in the Script and Kanji Strategy section.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Public explanations of policy changes

Businesses, municipalities, and other institutions in Japan often explain the reason behind a new rule or charge directly to customers or residents, for example through a printed notice or a short announcement, rather than leaving the reason unstated. This makes cause-and-result vocabulary such as ため and 結果 common in everyday notices, not only in academic or news writing.

Regional Note — Comparable policy explanations elsewhere

Public explanation of the reasons behind a fee, rule, or policy change is common in many countries, though the expected level of detail and the typical medium (a sign, a website, a spoken announcement) can differ. When describing a policy change from your own country or region for an international discussion group, it can be useful to note briefly how such changes are usually explained where you live.

Register Note — せいで and おかげで in spoken versus written explanation

せいで and おかげで are common in both spoken and written explanation, but they carry a noticeably personal or evaluative tone compared with the more neutral ため and によって, which appear more often in formal written analysis, notices, and reports. Choosing between them affects how opinionated or neutral an explanation sounds, even when the underlying facts are the same.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Using おかげで for a clearly negative outcome

Error: *道が混んでいたおかげで、会議に遅れてしまった。 using おかげで, which normally signals a positive or grateful outcome, for a clearly negative result.

Correction: 道が混んでいたせいで、会議に遅れてしまった。 — use せいで for a negative outcome and reserve おかげで for a positive or genuinely appreciated one, as modeled in this chapter's Grammar section.

Common Mistake — Stating a cause and a result without connecting them

Error: *レジ袋が有料になった。マイバッグを持ってくる客が増えた。 two separate sentences with no connector, leaving the cause-and-result relationship implicit and unclear.

Correction: レジ袋が有料になった。その結果、マイバッグを持ってくる客が増えた。 — mark the second sentence explicitly as a result with その結果, as modeled in this chapter's Building Block.

Common Mistake — Overusing によって as a general-purpose "because"

Error: *雨が降ったによって、イベントは中止になった。 using によって to mean simply "because," which does not match how によって functions in this chapter.

Correction: 雨が降ったため、イベントは中止になった。 — reserve によって for marking variation between cases or an agent/cause behind a passive-predicate result, and use ため for a general stated reason, as shown in the Grammar section.

Practical Performance Task

じっせんパフォーマンスタスク

原因と結果 — Analyzing a problem, decision, or change

Prepare a cause-and-result analysis, as though posting it for the discussion group described in the Communication Scenario. Complete all three components below.

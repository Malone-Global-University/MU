復習２：視点と条件Unit II Assessment

Keigo, passive, causative, and conditionals · Speak and Write Japanese: Independent Communication and Advanced Expression

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Use basic respectful and humble forms, including お/ご〜になる and お/ご〜する, in practical service, email, and introduction contexts.
  2. Form and interpret direct and indirect passive sentences, including agent marking with に, and distinguish active from passive viewpoint.
  3. Form and use potential forms with control, and use causative forms and 〜させてください to express permission, agency, and requests.
  4. Choose confidently among と, ば, たら, and なら, and use もし and 場合 appropriately.
  5. Integrate keigo, passive, causative, and conditional forms together in a single connected account.
  6. Read and listen to formal notices, procedures, and short narratives that combine these systems, extracting key information accurately.
  7. Complete a cumulative assessment demonstrating Unit II control across listening, speaking, reading, and writing.

Communication Scenario

Emma has been asked to represent her volunteer group at a short meeting with the community center's director. Before the meeting, she needs to write a polite email confirming the appointment, and during the meeting she may be asked about a recent incident at the front desk, about what she is and is not permitted to do as a volunteer, and about what she would recommend under certain conditions. Afterward, she needs to explain to a new volunteer what happened, what the director allowed her to do, and what to do if similar situations come up again. This chapter's Unit II Performance Task asks you to bring together formal language, passive description, ability and permission, and conditional advice in one connected set of tasks.

  1. The speaker or writer uses basic keigo appropriately when addressing someone of higher status or in a formal service context.
  2. The speaker or writer describes events from a passive viewpoint when the affected person, rather than the agent, is the natural focus.
  3. The speaker or writer expresses ability, permission, and requests for permission using potential and causative forms.
  4. The speaker or writer selects the conditional form, and uses もし or 場合, appropriate to each situation.

Pronunciation and Listening Check

Pronunciation Focus — Formal speech rhythm and conditional endings together

Formal keigo phrases often carry a slightly more deliberate rhythm than plain-style conversation, while conditional endings such as ば and たら require the same careful mora timing practiced in Chapter 9. This chapter's listening passages combine both, so pay attention to keeping formal phrases like いたします and よろしくお願いいたします clearly and evenly paced, without rushing through the conditional endings that may appear nearby.

  • お伺いします (おうかがいします): each mora should remain distinct; do not compress おう into a single short vowel.
  • 確認していただければ: a longer formal phrase ending in ば; keep the final ば short and clear rather than lengthening it for emphasis.

Practice reading the Model Dialogue aloud, maintaining even mora timing across both the formal phrases and the conditional endings.

Grammar Review: Keigo, Passive, Potential/Causative, Conditionals

This chapter reviews and integrates the four major grammar systems developed in Unit II. No new grammar is introduced; the goal is confident, combined control of systems that were each introduced separately in Chapters 6 through 9.

Basic keigo review

Purpose: to address someone of higher status, or to speak in a formal service or institutional context, using respectful forms for the other person's actions and humble forms for the speaker's own actions.

  • 部長はもう帰られました。The department head has already gone home.respectful form (お/ご〜になる pattern or equivalent) for another person's action
  • 私がご案内いたします。I will show you the way.humble form (ご〜いたす pattern) for the speaker's own action

Review: respectful language raises the person being discussed, while humble language lowers the speaker's own action to show respect toward the listener or a third party; both work together within the same conversation.

Passive and perspective review

Purpose: to describe an event from the viewpoint of the person affected, rather than the person acting, especially for incidents, notices, and experiences.

  • プリンターが担当者に修理されました。The printer was repaired by the person in charge.direct passive; focus on the printer rather than the repairer
  • 急に雨に降られて、困りました。I was troubled by it suddenly raining on me.indirect (affected) passive; expresses personal inconvenience

Review: choosing the passive shifts the sentence's focus onto the person affected; the agent, marked with に, is optional and often omitted when not important to the message.

Potential and causative review

Purpose: to express ability, to request permission to perform an action, and to describe who causes or allows an action to happen.

  • この機械は簡単に使えます。This machine can be used easily.potential form describing ability or capability
  • 今日は早く帰らせてください。Please let me go home early today.causative + ください requesting permission

Review: potential forms describe what is possible; 〜させてください specifically requests permission for the speaker's own action, and is distinct from a plain request directed at someone else.

Conditional review

Purpose: to choose accurately among と, ば, たら, and なら, and to use もし and 場合 to mark a hypothesis or a formal case.

  • 何か問題があった場合は、担当者にご連絡いただければと思います。If there is any problem, I would be grateful if you could contact the person in charge.場合 for a formal case, combined with ば and a keigo-adjacent request pattern
  • もし許可がいただけたら、早めに帰らせていただきます。If I could receive permission, I would like to go home early.もし + たら combined with a humble causative-based request

Review: the four conditionals, もし, and 場合 combine naturally with keigo, passive, and causative forms; nothing about combining these systems changes each system's individual rule.

Building Block — Combining Unit II systems
keigo (respect/humility) + passive (affected viewpoint) + potential/causative (ability/permission) + conditional (と/ば/たら/なら, もし, 場合)
A single formal account may use all four systems: keigo to address the listener appropriately, passive to describe what happened to someone, potential or causative to state what is possible or request permission, and a conditional to explain what should happen under a given condition.
昨日、来客に急に訪問されまして、少し困りましたが、マニュアルを確認すれば、たいてい対応できます。もし今後同じようなことがあった場合は、先に電話でご確認いただければと思います。
Yesterday I was suddenly visited by a guest and was a little troubled, but if you check the manual, you can usually handle it. If something similar happens again in the future, I would be grateful if you could confirm by phone beforehand.

Grammar Review Practice

  1. Rewrite 部長は明日来ます。 using a respectful form appropriate for referring to a superior's action.

  3. Complete 今日は早く＿＿＿＿＿ください。 with the correct causative + request form meaning "please let me go home early."

Model Dialogue

Setting: Emma meets briefly with the community center's director, 田中さん, to discuss a recent incident at the front desk.

田中さん エマさん、先週の件について、少しお伺いしてもいいですか。 Emma, may I ask you a bit about last week's matter?
エマ はい、もちろんです。実は、予約のないお客様に急に訪問されて、少し対応に困ってしまいました。 Yes, of course. Actually, I was suddenly visited by a guest without a reservation, and I had a little trouble handling it.
田中さん それは大変でしたね。もし今後同じような場合があれば、まずマニュアルを確認すれば、たいてい対応できますよ。 That must have been difficult. If a similar case comes up again in the future, if you check the manual first, you can usually handle it.
エマ 分かりました。それから、もし判断できない場合は、田中さんに直接ご連絡させていただいてもよろしいでしょうか。 I understand. Also, if I can't decide on my own, would it be all right if I contacted you directly?
田中さん もちろんです。いつでも連絡してくださって構いません。今日は他に何かありますか。 Of course. Please feel free to contact me anytime. Is there anything else today?
エマ いいえ、大丈夫です。お時間をいただき、ありがとうございました。 No, that's all. Thank you for your time.

  1. What happened to Emma last week that troubled her?
  2. What does the director suggest Emma do if a similar case happens again?
  3. What does Emma ask permission to do if she cannot decide something on her own?

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Formal reporting and shared responsibility

Formal workplace and volunteer notices in many Japanese institutional settings often describe an incident using passive or neutral phrasing rather than directly naming who was at fault, and follow with clear, procedural advice for the future. Recognizing this pattern helps learners understand why passive description and conditional procedural language appear together so often in real notices, reports, and manuals.

Regional Note — Keigo intensity and workplace norms

The degree of keigo used in a given workplace, organization, or region can vary considerably depending on industry norms, company culture, and individual relationships. The basic keigo patterns taught in this unit represent a broadly reliable, moderate level of formality rather than the single correct level for every institution.

Language Note — Viewpoint and voice across languages

Many languages offer speakers a choice between an active voice, which foregrounds who performed an action, and a passive or similar construction, which foregrounds who was affected by it, though the specific forms and typical uses differ from language to language. Comparing how your own language handles this choice can help clarify why Japanese speakers often prefer the passive viewpoint for incidents and notices.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Mixing plain and formal register within the same account

Error: *先週、来客に訪問された。でも、対応できますよ。 mixing a plain-style narration with a suddenly polite ending, producing an inconsistent register.

Correction: 先週、来客に訪問されました。でも、対応できますよ。 — keep register consistent throughout a single formal account rather than switching between plain and polite forms without reason.

Common Mistake — Using させてください for someone else's action

Error: *田中さんに早く帰らせてください。 intended as a request for Tanaka-san to go home early, incorrectly using させてください, which requests permission for the speaker's own action.

Correction: 田中さんに早く帰っていただけますか。 — use させてください only to request permission for your own action; use a request form such as 〜ていただけますか when asking someone else to do something.

Common Mistake — Using なら to introduce a new formal hypothesis

Error: *何か問題があるなら、ご連絡ください。 used as an opening statement with no prior mention of a problem, when なら is more natural responding to an already-raised topic, even in formal contexts.

Correction: もし何か問題がありましたら、ご連絡ください。 — use もし with a たら-based or equivalent formal conditional to introduce a new hypothetical case, reserving なら for responding to something already mentioned or assumed.

Listening Assessment — Model summary

Last week, a volunteer was suddenly visited by a guest without a reservation at the front desk. Going forward, if a similar case happens, volunteers should first check the manual, since that usually allows them to handle it. If a volunteer cannot decide something, they may contact the person in charge directly. If a volunteer suddenly finds themselves in a difficult situation, they should not try to handle it alone and should immediately ask a nearby staff member for help.

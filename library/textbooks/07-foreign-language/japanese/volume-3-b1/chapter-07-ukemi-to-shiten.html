受け身と視点Passive and Perspective

Passive forms, affectedness, and viewpoint · Speak and Write Japanese: Independent Communication and Advanced Expression

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Explain the difference between describing an event with an active verb and describing it with a passive verb.
  2. Form and use the direct passive to describe an event from the point of view of the person or thing it happens to.
  3. Form and use the indirect ("adversative" or affected-bystander) passive to describe how an event affected someone, even when that person is not the grammatical object of the original action.
  4. Mark the agent of a passive sentence with に, and recognize when the agent is omitted because it is unknown, unimportant, or already understood.
  5. Choose between an active and a passive sentence based on whose perspective and experience the sentence is meant to foreground.
  6. Read and listen to short notices, news items, and personal accounts that use passive forms, and identify who is affected and how.
  7. Write and narrate a short account of an incident or experience using passive forms to express affectedness and perspective.

Communication Scenario

Emma's discussion group meets at a community center, and this week the group's usual room has been closed for repairs after a small water leak. A printed notice about the change was posted on the bulletin board, and Yuki tells Emma that her bicycle was also affected — it was parked near the flooded area and got splashed, delaying her morning. Emma wants to understand the posted notice, ask Yuki about what happened, and later write a short message to the rest of the discussion group explaining the situation from everyone's point of view — what happened to the room, and how it affected the group's plans. This chapter's Practical Performance Task asks you to read or listen to an account of an incident, then narrate and write your own account of an event and its effect on you or someone else, using both direct and indirect passive forms.

  1. The writer or speaker uses a direct passive form to describe what happened to an object, place, or announcement.
  2. The writer or speaker uses an indirect passive form to describe how the event affected a specific person.
  3. The writer or speaker marks the agent with に only where the agent is relevant and known.

Script Strategy — Recognizing 発 as a productive component

The character 発, meaning roughly "to send out" or "to bring about," appears in several words active in this chapter and beyond: 発表 (announcement), 発見 (discovery), and later in the volume, 発達 (development) and other compounds. Recognizing 発 as a recurring component can help you predict the general meaning area of a new compound, but the exact reading and precise meaning of each word must still be checked individually, since the same component does not guarantee the same reading in every compound.

In this chapter's readings, only names, unfamiliar loanwords, and this chapter's new kanji on first appearance receive furigana.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Geminate consonants in news-style vocabulary

Several words in this chapter contain the small っ, which lengthens the following consonant by one full mora. This chapter's vocabulary makes these contrasts especially important, since dropping the small っ can produce a different word or an unintelligible sequence.

  • 発見 (はっけん): the small っ before け must be held as its own timing unit, distinct from はけん (which has a different meaning).
  • 発表 (はっぴょう): the small っ before ぴ, followed immediately by the contracted sound ょう; keep both features distinct and do not shorten either.
  • 迷惑をかけられました: a common phrase from this chapter's Grammar section; practice keeping the moraic ん in 迷惑 and the passive ending's own vowel length distinct from each other.

Practice reading the Model Dialogue aloud, paying particular attention to holding each moraic and geminate unit for its full length, even at a natural conversational pace.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns: Passive and Perspective

Japanese passive sentences do more than reverse the subject and object of an active sentence, as an English passive often does. Japanese has two related passive constructions: the direct passive, which describes an event from the point of view of the person or thing the action was done to, and the indirect passive (sometimes called the adversative or affected-bystander passive), which describes how an event affected someone even when that person was not directly acted upon at all. Both constructions shift the sentence's point of view toward the person who experiences the event, which is why this chapter treats passive forms and perspective together.

Forming the passive

Purpose: to build the passive form of a verb before using it to shift perspective.

  • 読む → 読まれるto read → to be readGroup 1 (u-verb): change final -u to -a, add -れる
  • 見る → 見られるto see/look at → to be seen/looked atGroup 2 (ru-verb): drop -る, add -られる
  • する → されるto do → to be doneirregular
  • 来る → 来られるto come → (someone) coming (on the speaker)irregular; frequently indirect passive

The passive form is itself a Group 2 (ru-verb) once built, so it conjugates like 見られる in all further tenses and forms: 読まれます, 読まれました, 読まれない, and so on.

The direct passive — describing what happened to something

Purpose: to describe an event from the point of view of the person, place, or thing the action was done to, especially when the agent is unknown, unimportant, or best left unstated.

  • 今朝、事件のニュースが発表されました。News of the incident was announced this morning.agent omitted — the announcer is not the focus
  • その掲示は先週、担当者によって貼られました。That notice was posted last week by the person in charge.agent marked with によって — common in formal/written passive
  • 古い自転車が公園で発見されました。An old bicycle was discovered in the park.agent omitted — the discoverer is unimportant to the point

The agent of a direct passive is normally marked with に in ordinary speech and writing (先生に褒められました, "I was praised by the teacher"), while によって is common in more formal, written, or news-style passives, especially when the agent is an organization, institution, or unspecified party rather than an individual person.

The indirect passive — describing how an event affected someone

Purpose: to describe how an event affected a person, even when that person did not directly experience the original action — often, though not always, an inconvenience, disruption, or unwelcome effect.

  • 雨に降られて、予定が変わりました。I got rained on, and my plans changed. (lit., I was rained on, and...)intransitive verb 降る + passive; the speaker is affected by the rain, not "acted upon" by it
  • 隣の部屋の工事に困っています。いつも音を出されて、集中できません。I'm troubled by the construction in the next room. Noise is constantly made, and I can't concentrate.the speaker is affected by an action performed by someone else, without being its direct target
  • 友達に急に予定を変えられて、困りました。My friend suddenly changed our plans on me, and I was troubled by it.transitive verb 変える + passive, with the affected party as the topic rather than the person who changed the plans

The indirect passive can be built from both transitive and intransitive verbs — a flexibility that the direct passive does not share, since the direct passive normally requires a transitive verb with a clear object.

Active or passive: choosing the sentence's perspective

Building Block — Choosing between active and passive
ask: whose experience does this sentence need to foreground? → the doer's experience → active sentence → the affected person's or thing's experience → passive sentence
This decision point, rather than a mechanical transformation rule, gives this chapter's writing and narration tasks their underlying logic.
先週、コミュニティセンターで水漏れがありました。いつもの部屋が修理のために閉められて、私たちのグループは困りました。ゆきさんの自転車も水に濡らされて、朝の予定が遅れてしまったそうです。
Last week there was a water leak at the community center. Our usual room was closed for repairs, and our group was troubled by it. Apparently Yuki's bicycle also got splashed with water, and her morning plans were delayed.

Notice that 閉められて foregrounds the room's condition (direct passive), while 濡らされて and 困りました foreground how the situation affected the group and Yuki personally (indirect passive) — the same underlying event, told twice from different points of view.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Emma and Yuki meet at the community center and notice the posted notice about the closed room.

エマ あれ、いつもの部屋が閉められてる。何かあったの? Huh, the usual room is closed. Did something happen?
ゆき うん、先週水漏れが発見されて、今、修理されているんだって。掲示、見た? Yeah, a water leak was discovered last week, and apparently it's being repaired now. Did you see the notice?
エマ まだ。何て書いてあるの? Not yet. What does it say?
ゆき 別の部屋が使われることになったって発表されてる。あと、私、実は水に自転車を濡らされちゃって、今朝ちょっと大変だった。 It's been announced that a different room will be used instead. Also, my bicycle actually got splashed with water, and it was a bit of a hassle this morning.
エマ それは大変だったね。センターの人に何か言われた? That sounds rough. Did someone from the center say anything to you?
ゆき うん、迷惑をかけてすみませんって謝られたよ。 Yeah, I was apologized to — they said sorry for the trouble.

ないようりかい — Comprehension questions:

  1. What was discovered last week, according to Yuki?
  2. What has been announced about the room situation?
  3. What indirect passive form does Yuki use to describe what happened to her bicycle, and what does it foreground?

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Passive voice in Japanese public notices

Japanese public notices, news reports, and institutional announcements frequently use the passive voice, often with によって, to describe decisions, discoveries, and actions without naming an individual responsible party. This is not primarily a way of avoiding responsibility; it reflects a common convention in formal and institutional writing, where the organization or process, rather than a specific person, is usually the more relevant piece of information for the reader.

Regional Note — Affected-bystander meaning across languages

Many languages have some way of expressing that someone was affected by an event without being its direct target, though the grammatical tools differ: some languages use dative or reflexive constructions, others use idiomatic phrases, and Japanese uses the indirect passive. Comparing how your own language expresses "something happened to me" without a direct object can help clarify what is functionally similar to the Japanese indirect passive and what is distinct to it.

Register Note — Passive forms are not automatically more polite

Unlike the keigo forms introduced in Chapter 6, ordinary passive forms are not inherently more formal or more polite than their active counterparts; they simply describe events from a different point of view. A casual conversation between friends can use the indirect passive just as naturally as a formal news report, so choose the passive based on perspective and affectedness, not as a general politeness strategy.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Marking every agent with に even when it is unimportant

Error: *このニュースは誰かに発表されました。 forcing an unnecessary or vague agent phrase onto a passive sentence where the agent is not relevant.

Correction: このニュースは発表されました。 — omit the agent entirely when it is unknown or unimportant to the point of the sentence.

Common Mistake — Using a direct passive where an indirect passive is needed

Error: *私は雨が降られました。 attempting to build a direct passive from an intransitive verb without the correct indirect-passive structure.

Correction: 私は雨に降られました。 — the indirect passive of an intransitive verb marks the affected person as the topic and uses に, not が, for the event itself.

Common Mistake — Confusing によって with に in ordinary conversation

Error: *友達によって助けられました。 using the formal によって to mark an ordinary personal agent in casual conversation.

Correction: 友達に助けられました。 — reserve によって mainly for formal, written, or institutional agents, and use に for ordinary personal agents in everyday speech and writing.

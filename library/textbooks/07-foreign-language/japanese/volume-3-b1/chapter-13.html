メールと文書Emails and Documents

Formal writing, requests, apology, and confirmation · Speak and Write Japanese: Independent Communication and Advanced Expression

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Open and close a formal email using standard Japanese business and institutional formulas.
  2. State a request in writing using softened, polite forms rather than a direct command.
  3. Attach or refer to a document clearly and confirm that the recipient has what they need.
  4. Write a short apology appropriate to a scheduling problem, mistake, or delay.
  5. Confirm a plan, date, or piece of information in writing and ask the reader to check it.
  6. Recognize noun-heavy written style in short business and institutional notices.
  7. Complete listening, speaking, reading, and writing tasks built around a formal email exchange.

Communication Scenario

After her presentation at the volunteer meeting, Emma has been asked to help the community center coordinate a small repair project for the recycling collection point. She needs to email the building manager, 佐藤さん, to request permission to use a storage room, attach a simple floor plan, apologize for the short notice, and confirm the date of the work. This chapter's Practical Performance Task asks you to write and respond to a short formal email exchange of your own, using a request, an attachment reference, an apology, and a confirmation.

  1. The writer opens the email with a standard greeting appropriate to a business or institutional relationship.
  2. The writer states a request using a softened, polite request pattern.
  3. The writer refers to an attached document and apologizes for any inconvenience.
  4. The writer confirms a date or detail and closes the email with a standard closing formula.

Script Strategy — Reading noun-heavy business language

Formal emails and documents often string several kanji compounds together as noun phrases rather than full sentences, for example 添付資料ご確認のほどよろしくお願いいたします. Read these phrases by first identifying each noun compound (添付資料, ご確認) and then supplying the missing verbs and particles mentally, the same expansion strategy you used for presentation slides in Chapter 12.

Kanji from this chapter, such as 文書, 添付, 依頼, and 確認, will continue to appear in Chapter 16's specialized and institutional readings, so treat them as durable vocabulary rather than words limited to email.

Pronunciation and Listening Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Reading formal formulas aloud

Formal opening and closing formulas such as いつもお世話になっております and よろしくお願いいたします are usually spoken as single, smoothly connected phrases rather than word by word. When these formulas are read aloud, for example at the start of a phone call or voicemail that follows up on a written request, keep the phrase together and avoid pausing in the middle of it.

  • いつもお世話になっております。 (いつもおせわになっております。): one connected phrase, slight pause after it.
  • よろしくお願いいたします。: keep お願いいたします together as a single closing unit.

Practice reading the Reading Assessment email aloud as if leaving a short voicemail confirming its contents, keeping each formulaic phrase connected.

Grammar: Formal Formulas and Softened Requests

This chapter authorizes standard opening and closing formulas, よろしくお願いいたします and related set forms, softened request patterns, and recognition of noun-heavy written style, building on the polite and formal-register systems developed since Chapter 6.

Opening and closing formulas

Purpose: to begin and end a formal email in a way that is recognizable and appropriate, without composing an entirely original greeting each time.

  • いつもお世話になっております。Thank you for your continued support. (formulaic email opener)お世話になる, formulaic, used regardless of literal meaning
  • 〇〇コミュニティセンターのエマです。This is Emma from the [Name] Community Center.self-identification following the opener
  • よろしくお願いいたします。Thank you in advance. (standard closing)お願いいたします, more formal than お願いします

Softened requests

Purpose: to ask for something in writing without sounding like a direct command, appropriate to a relationship with distance or formality.

  • 来週までにご確認いただけますか。Could you please check this by next week?ご＋noun＋いただけますか, polite request frame
  • 恐れ入りますが、倉庫の使用について確認していただけないでしょうか。I'm sorry to trouble you, but could you possibly confirm about using the storage room?恐れ入りますが softens the request; 〜ていただけないでしょうか is more indirect than 〜ていただけますか
  • お手数ですが、添付の文書をご確認ください。Sorry for the trouble, but please check the attached document.お手数ですが as a softening phrase before a request

Referring to an attachment

Purpose: to tell the reader that a document is attached and to direct their attention to it clearly.

  • 簡単な図面を添付いたしました。I have attached a simple floor plan.添付いたしました, humble past form of 添付する
  • 詳しい内容は添付の文書をご覧ください。Please see the attached document for the details.添付の文書 as a noun phrase referring to the file

Apologizing in writing

Purpose: to apologize briefly for short notice, an inconvenience, or a delay, without over-explaining.

  • 急なご連絡になり、申し訳ございません。I'm very sorry for the sudden message.申し訳ございません, more formal than すみません
  • ご不便をおかけして、お詫び申し上げます。I apologize for the inconvenience.お詫び申し上げます, formal written apology formula

Confirming a plan or detail

Purpose: to state a plan or piece of information clearly and invite the reader to check it, rather than assuming it is already understood.

  • 作業は来週の水曜日に行う予定です。ご都合はいかがでしょうか。The work is planned for next Wednesday. How does that work for you?ご都合はいかがでしょうか, formal way to ask about availability
  • 以上の内容でよろしければ、ご返信をお願いいたします。If the above is acceptable, please reply.以上の内容で〜, formal closing confirmation frame
Building Block — A short formal email's structure
opening formula + self-identification + softened request + attachment reference + apology (if needed) + confirmation of plan/detail + closing formula
A short formal email typically opens with いつもお世話になっております and a self-identification, states a request using a softened pattern such as 〜ていただけますか or 恐れ入りますが〜, refers to any attached document, apologizes briefly for short notice or inconvenience if relevant, confirms a date or detail and invites a reply, and closes with よろしくお願いいたします.
いつもお世話になっております。コミュニティセンターのエマです。恐れ入りますが、倉庫の使用について確認していただけないでしょうか。簡単な図面を添付いたしました。急なご連絡になり、申し訳ございません。作業は来週の水曜日に行う予定です。ご都合はいかがでしょうか。よろしくお願いいたします。
Thank you for your continued support. This is Emma from the community center. I'm sorry to trouble you, but could you possibly confirm about using the storage room? I have attached a simple floor plan. I'm very sorry for the sudden message. The work is planned for next Wednesday. How does that work for you? Thank you in advance.

Grammar Practice

  1. Rewrite 確認してください。 as a more softened request using 〜ていただけますか.
  2. Complete 恐れ入りますが、＿＿＿＿＿していただけないでしょうか。 with a request of your choice.
  3. Write one sentence referring to an attached document, following the pattern of 簡単な図面を添付いたしました。
  4. Write a short apology for short notice, following the pattern of 急なご連絡になり、申し訳ございません。
  5. Write a closing pair of sentences confirming a plan and inviting a reply, following the pattern of 以上の内容でよろしければ、ご返信をお願いいたします。

Model Dialogue

Setting: Emma discusses her draft email with her supervisor, 田中さん, before sending it to the building manager, 佐藤さん.

田中さん 佐藤さんへのメールはもう書きましたか。まずはどう始めますか。 Have you written the email to Sato-san yet? How will you start it?
エマ はい。いつもお世話になっております。コミュニティセンターのエマです、と書きました。 Yes. I wrote, "Thank you for your continued support. This is Emma from the community center."
田中さん いいですね。それから、倉庫についてのお願いはどう書きましたか。 Good. And how did you write the request about the storage room?
エマ 恐れ入りますが、倉庫の使用について確認していただけないでしょうか、と書きました。図面も添付いたしましたと書きました。 I wrote, "I'm sorry to trouble you, but could you possibly confirm about using the storage room?" I also wrote that I attached the floor plan.
田中さん 急なお願いですから、お詫びの言葉も入れたほうがいいですね。 Since it's a sudden request, it would be good to include an apology too.
エマ はい、急なご連絡になり、申し訳ございません、と書きました。作業の日も確認しました。 Yes, I wrote, "I'm very sorry for the sudden message." I also confirmed the day of the work.
田中さん 最後はどう終わりますか。 How will you end it?
エマ 以上の内容でよろしければ、ご返信をお願いいたします。よろしくお願いいたします、で終わります。 I'll end with, "If the above is acceptable, please reply. Thank you in advance."
田中さん よくできています。これで送ってください。 Well done. Please send it now.

  2. What does Emma attach to the email, and how does she refer to it?
  3. Why does 田中さん suggest that Emma include an apology?

Writing Workshop

In this workshop you will draft the written portion of your Practical Performance Task: a short formal email exchange based on a request, attachment, apology, and confirmation of your choice.

  1. Choose a scenario in which you need to make a request of an institution, workplace, or organization, real or invented for practice.
  2. Write your opening using いつもお世話になっております and a self-identification.
  3. State your request using a softened pattern such as 〜ていただけますか or 恐れ入りますが〜.
  4. Refer to an attachment relevant to your request, even if invented for practice.
  5. Include a brief apology if your request involves short notice or inconvenience.
  6. Confirm a date or detail and invite a reply.
  7. Close your email using よろしくお願いいたします, then revise your draft using the checklist below.

チェックリスト — Editing checklist:

  • My email opens with いつもお世話になっております and identifies me clearly.
  • My request uses a softened pattern rather than a direct command.
  • My email refers to an attachment clearly, using 添付 or a related phrase.
  • My email includes an apology appropriate to the situation, if needed.
  • My email confirms a date or detail and invites a reply.
  • My email closes with よろしくお願いいたします or a similarly appropriate formula.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Formulaic openings and closings

Formulas such as いつもお世話になっております and よろしくお願いいたします appear at the start and end of many Japanese business and institutional emails, regardless of whether the writer and reader have an ongoing relationship. Using these formulas signals that the writer understands professional convention; omitting them in a formal context can read as abrupt, even when the surrounding request is polite.

Regional Note — Variation by workplace and generation

The exact wording of opening and closing formulas can vary somewhat by workplace, generation, and communication platform. Some workplaces use shorter formulas in internal chat tools than in emails to outside organizations, and younger colleagues may use somewhat less elaborate formulas with each other than with senior staff or outside contacts. Treat this chapter's formulas as a reliable, widely used default rather than the only acceptable wording.

Language Note — Softening across languages

Many languages soften written requests using indirect phrasing, conditional forms, or apologetic openers rather than direct imperatives. Comparing how your own language turns "check this" into a more indirect request can help clarify why Japanese so consistently pairs a request with a softening phrase such as 恐れ入りますが or お手数ですが.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Skipping the opening formula

Error: Beginning a formal email directly with the request, such as *倉庫を貸してください。, without any opening formula or self-identification.

Correction: いつもお世話になっております。コミュニティセンターのエマです。 — open with a standard formula and identify yourself before making a request.

Common Mistake — Using a direct command instead of a softened request

Error: *確認してください。 used alone in a formal email to someone outside a close working relationship.

Correction: ご確認いただけますか。 or 恐れ入りますが、ご確認していただけないでしょうか。 — soften the request appropriately to the relationship and setting.

Common Mistake — Mentioning an attachment without referring to it in the text

Error: Attaching a file to an email without any sentence telling the reader that something is attached or what it contains.

Correction: 簡単な図面を添付いたしました。 — always state clearly in the body of the email that a document is attached and what it is.

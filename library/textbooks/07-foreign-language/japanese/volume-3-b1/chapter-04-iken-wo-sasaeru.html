意見を支えるSupporting Opinions

Argument support, concession, and stance · Speak and Write Japanese: Independent Communication and Advanced Expression

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. State a clear opinion and identify it explicitly as an opinion rather than a fact.
  2. Support an opinion with at least one reason and one concrete example.
  3. Recognize べき and use it in a small number of controlled, well-supported sentences to say what should or should not be done.
  4. Introduce a concession before disagreeing, using patterns such as 確かに〜が and 〜ものの.
  5. Cite a source of evidence in a controlled way using phrases such as 例えば and 〜というデータ／話がある.
  6. Disagree gently using softened language rather than a blunt contradiction.
  7. Read and write mixed-script Japanese about education, work, technology, community, media, travel, environment, and cultural topics, using kanji strategies for opinion, evidence, and conclusion.

Communication Scenario

Emma's cause-and-result post about the convenience store bag charge attracted several replies in the discussion group, including one from a member who argues that stores should not be allowed to charge for something that used to be free. Emma wants to respond, and she asks Yuki for help before posting. She agrees with part of the other member's concern but disagrees with the overall conclusion, and she wants her reply to sound like a considered opinion rather than a flat contradiction. Yuki suggests that Emma first acknowledge the other member's point, then explain her own opinion with a reason and an example, and finally soften her disagreement rather than stating it directly. This chapter's Practical Performance Task asks you to respond to an opinion on a topic of your choice — from education, work, technology, community, media, travel, environment, or culture — using the same pattern: acknowledge, support your own view, and disagree gently where needed.

  1. The writer states their own opinion clearly, marked as an opinion.
  2. The writer supports the opinion with at least one reason and one example.
  3. The writer acknowledges a different view before disagreeing, and disagrees gently rather than bluntly.

Script Strategy — Recognizing a shared component across opinion words

賛成 and 反対 both end in a kanji used broadly for stance (成, "to become/form," and 対, "to face/oppose"); noticing that 賛成 and 反対 are structured as opposites can help both words stick together in memory, though this is a memory aid rather than a rule for predicting meaning from components in general. 結論 shares its first kanji, 結, with 結果 from Chapter 3 — both describe an outcome that has "tied together" into a final form, one a result and one a conclusion.

New Volume III kanji introduced in this chapter, such as those above, receive furigana at first appearance and are gradually reduced according to this volume's matrices. Kanji introduced in Chapters 1 through 3, such as 原因, 結果, and 影響, appear with reduced furigana in this chapter's readings.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Long vowels and geminate consonants in opinion vocabulary

Several key words in this chapter contain a long vowel or a geminate consonant marked by small っ, and holding these clearly is important for comprehension. 賛成 (さんせい) has a long vowel in せい that should be held for its full two morae, distinct from a shortened せ with no length contrast. 結論 (けつろん) contains the moraic ん, which must keep its own timing slot before ろ.

  • 賛成 (さんせい): hold the long vowel in せい for its full two morae.
  • 反対 (はんたい): keep the moraic ん distinct in timing from the following た.
  • 結論 (けつろん): the moraic ん before ろ must occupy its own mora, not blend directly into ろ.

Practice reading the Building Block example in the Grammar section aloud, paying particular attention to the long vowel in 賛成 and the moraic ん in 反対 and 結論.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter introduces four connected tools for supporting an opinion: recommending or evaluating an action with べき, conceding a point before disagreeing, citing evidence in a controlled way, and softening disagreement.

Recognizing and using べき

Purpose: to say that an action should or should not be done, based on the speaker's judgment. This chapter authorizes べき for recognition and limited, well-supported production only.

  • 学校はもっとコンピューターの授業を増やすべきだと思う。I think schools should offer more computer classes.Vる + べきだと思う — opinion softened with と思う
  • この問題については、もっと早く対策を取るべきだった。Regarding this problem, countermeasures should have been taken sooner.Vる + べきだった — past evaluation of an action that was not taken

べき can sound strong and judgmental on its own. In this chapter, べき is authorized mainly together with と思う or in a clearly explained, well-supported opinion, not as a bare statement about other people's behavior.

Conceding a point before disagreeing

Purpose: to acknowledge that part of an opposing view has merit before explaining a different conclusion.

  • 確かに値段が上がったのは困るが、環境のためには必要な変化だと思う。It's true that the price increase is a problem, but I think it's a necessary change for the environment.確かに〜が、〜 — concede a point, then state one's own view
  • 便利なアプリが増えたものの、使い方が分からない人もまだ多い。Although convenient apps have increased, there are still many people who don't know how to use them.〜ものの、〜 — concession within a single sentence, more formal than が

確かに〜が is common in both speech and writing; ものの is more common in writing and formal speech. Both are authorized here for controlled, supported use only.

Citing evidence in a controlled way

Purpose: to support an opinion with a concrete example or a general reference to information, without overstating certainty.

  • 例えば、私の大学では在宅授業のおかげで通学時間が減った学生が多い。For example, at my university, many students have had less commuting time thanks to remote classes.例えば、〜 — introduces a concrete supporting example
  • 環境への意識が高まっているというデータもあるようだ。There also seems to be data suggesting that environmental awareness is rising.〜というデータ／話がある + ようだ — cautious reference to a source without overstating certainty

This chapter authorizes general, cautious references to evidence such as 〜というデータ／話がある; it does not require citing or verifying specific named studies or statistics.

Softening disagreement

Purpose: to disagree with part or all of another person's opinion without sounding blunt or confrontational.

  • そのご意見も分かりますが、私は少し違う考えを持っています。I understand your opinion, but I have a somewhat different view.分かりますが、〜考えを持っています — acknowledge, then present a different opinion
  • それも一つの考え方かもしれませんが、私はこう思います。That might be one way of looking at it, but this is what I think.〜かもしれませんが、〜 — soften disagreement with a hedge before stating one's view

Building an opinion-support paragraph

Building Block — An opinion-support paragraph
state the topic → give your opinion, marked clearly as an opinion → support it with one reason and one example → concede a point from an opposing view → soften your disagreement and restate your conclusion
This five-stage pattern moves from a stated opinion, to supported reasoning, to acknowledgment of another view, and finally to a gently restated conclusion, and gives this chapter's Practical Performance Task its underlying shape.
レジ袋が有料になったことについて、私は賛成だ。ごみを減らすためには必要な変化だと思う。例えば、私の近所の店でもマイバッグを持ってくる客が増えたそうだ。確かに、急に有料になって困っている人もいるものの、少し慣れれば大きな負担にはならないと思う。それも一つの考え方かもしれないが、長い目で見れば環境のためには良い変化だというのが私の結論だ。
Regarding the shopping bags becoming fee-based, I'm in favor. I think it's a necessary change in order to reduce waste. For example, I hear that at the store in my neighborhood too, the number of customers bringing their own bags has increased. It's true that some people are troubled by the sudden fee, but I think that once people get a little used to it, it won't be a big burden. That might be one way of looking at it, but my conclusion is that, in the long run, this is a good change for the environment.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Emma drafts a reply to a discussion group member who argues against the bag charge, and shows it to Yuki.

エマ その人の意見に反対したいんだけど、いきなり「違います」って書くのは失礼かな。 I want to disagree with that person's opinion, but would it be rude to just suddenly write "you're wrong"?
ユキ うん、ちょっと強すぎると思う。まず「確かに〜が」で、相手の意見にも一理あることを認めたら? Yeah, I think that's a bit too strong. First, what if you used "certainly ~ but" to acknowledge that the other person's opinion has a point too?
エマ 「確かに急に有料になって困る人もいるが」みたいな感じ? Something like "certainly there are people troubled by the sudden fee, but"?
ユキ そうそう。それから自分の意見を根拠と例を付けて説明して、最後は「かもしれませんが」で少し柔らかく反対を言うといいと思う。 Exactly. After that, explain your own opinion with a reason and an example attached, and at the end, I think it's good to disagree a bit softly using "might be, but."
エマ 「それも一つの考え方かもしれませんが、長い目で見れば良い変化だと思います」って結論を書けばいいかな。 Maybe I should write a conclusion like "that might be one way of looking at it, but I think it's a good change in the long run"?
ユキ いいと思う。「べき」も使いたいなら、意見の部分に一つだけ入れて、あとは「と思う」で全体を柔らかくするといいよ。 I think that's good. If you also want to use "should," put it in just once in the opinion part, and soften the whole thing with "I think" elsewhere.
エマ 分かった。「店はもっと分かりやすく説明するべきだと思う」って一文だけ入れてみる。 Got it. I'll try putting in just one sentence like "I think stores should explain things more clearly."

ないようりかい — Comprehension questions:

  1. What does Emma originally want to write, and why does Yuki think it might be too strong?
  2. What concession does Yuki suggest Emma include?
  3. How does Yuki suggest Emma use べき in the reply?

Writing Workshop

In this workshop you will draft the written portion of your Practical Performance Task: a response to an opinion on a topic of your choice, following the five-stage Building Block pattern from the Grammar section.

  1. State the topic in one sentence.
  2. Give your opinion, clearly marked with と思う, and use べき at most once, softened by と思う.
  3. Support your opinion with one reason and one example using 例えば.
  4. Concede a point from a different view using 確かに〜が or 〜ものの.
  5. Soften your disagreement and restate your conclusion, then revise using the checklist below.

チェックリスト — Editing checklist:

  • My opinion is clearly marked as an opinion, not stated as plain fact.
  • I supported my opinion with at least one reason and one example.
  • If I used べき, it appears at most once and is softened with と思う.
  • I conceded at least one point from a different view before disagreeing.
  • My disagreement is softened rather than blunt.
  • My sentences connect to one another rather than reading as a disconnected list of statements.
  • I maintained one consistent polite or plain style throughout.
  • I checked unfamiliar kanji using the strategies in the Script and Kanji Strategy section.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Softened disagreement in group discussion

In many Japanese group discussions, meetings, and online forums, disagreement is commonly softened through concession phrases, hedges, and indirect wording rather than expressed as a flat contradiction. This pattern is not unique to Japanese, but the specific expressions practiced in this chapter, such as 確かに〜が and 〜かもしれませんが, are common, natural ways of managing disagreement in Japanese-language discussion.

Regional Note — Softened disagreement elsewhere

Softening disagreement with a concession before stating a different view is common in many languages and communities, though the specific phrases and the expected degree of softening can differ. When responding to an opinion from an international discussion group, it can be useful to briefly compare how disagreement is typically expressed where you live with the patterns practiced in this chapter.

Register Note — べき in spoken versus written opinion

べき can sound quite direct and can be perceived as preachy or judgmental, especially when used repeatedly or without support. In both speech and writing, it is more natural when limited to a single, well-supported claim and softened with と思う, as modeled in this chapter, rather than used as a general way to tell other people what they should do.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Overusing べき without support

Error: *学校は変わるべきだ。先生も変わるべきだ。生徒も変わるべきだ。 repeating べき several times in a row without any supporting reason or example, which sounds preachy and unsupported.

Correction: 学校はもっとコンピューターの授業を増やすべきだと思う。例えば、多くの生徒がプログラミングに興味を持っている。 — use べき at most once, soften it with と思う, and follow it with a reason and an example, as modeled in this chapter's Grammar section.

Common Mistake — Disagreeing without conceding any point

Error: *その意見は間違っていると思います。 stating flatly that the other person's opinion is wrong, with no acknowledgment of their point.

Correction: 確かにそのお考えも分かりますが、私は少し違う意見を持っています。 — concede part of the other view first, then present a different opinion gently, as modeled in this chapter's Building Block.

Common Mistake — Citing evidence with false certainty

Error: *調査によると、絶対にそうだ。 presenting a vaguely cited source as absolute proof, which overstates the strength of the evidence.

Correction: そういうデータもあるようだ。 — use cautious, general reference language such as 〜というデータ／話がある together with ようだ, rather than claiming certainty from an unspecified or unverified source, as shown in the Grammar section.

Practical Performance Task

じっせんパフォーマンスタスク

意見を支える — Responding to an opinion

Prepare a response to an opinion, as though posting it for the discussion group described in the Communication Scenario. Complete all three components below.

Looking Ahead

いまできること — What you can do now

You can state an opinion clearly, support it with a reason and an example, recognize and cautiously use べき, concede a point before disagreeing, and soften disagreement so that it sounds considered rather than blunt. Combined with the cause-and-result analysis skills from Chapter 3, you can now build an organized response that explains a situation, its causes and consequences, and a supported, appropriately softened opinion about it. This prepares you for Chapter 5's cumulative review of explanation and opinion.

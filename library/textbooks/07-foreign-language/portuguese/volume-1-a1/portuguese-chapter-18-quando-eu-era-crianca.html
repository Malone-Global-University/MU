Quando eu era criança

Background and Habitual Past · Speak and Write Portuguese: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Overview

In Chapters 16 and 17, you used the preterite to narrate single completed events and lifetime experiences. This chapter introduces a second past tense, the imperfect, which describes what things were like, what used to happen, and what was going on, rather than what happened and finished at one point in time. You will learn the imperfect of ser, estar, ter, and ir, along with the regular imperfect pattern for -ar, -er, and -ir verbs, and you will begin to contrast the imperfect with the preterite you already control. By the end of the chapter, you will be able to describe what your life was like as a child, what you used to do, and what things and people were like in the past.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Conjugate the imperfect of ser, estar, ter, and ir.
  2. Form the regular imperfect of -ar, -er, and -ir verbs.
  3. Describe people, places, and conditions as they used to be.
  4. Describe habitual or repeated past actions, such as childhood routines.
  5. Begin to distinguish the imperfect from the preterite in simple contexts.
  6. Read and write a short paragraph about childhood, preserving all diacritics.

Communication Scenario

You are talking with a friend about what your childhood was like: where you lived, what your family was like, what you used to do after school, and what your daily life looked like before you grew up. This kind of description requires a different past tense from the one you have used so far, because you are not narrating a single completed event — you are painting a picture of an ongoing period of your life.

  1. This chapter activates the imperfect of ser, estar, ter, and ir, plus the regular imperfect pattern.
  2. The chapter's productive target is describing background conditions and habitual past actions, most often connected to childhood.
  3. Preterite and imperfect are introduced together only at the level of recognizing the basic contrast; full mastery of preterite/imperfect choice continues in Chapter 19 and beyond.
  4. You may describe your real childhood or invent one.

Language Note — morar versus viver

Morar and viver can both be translated as "to live," but morar refers specifically to residing at an address or in a place, while viver refers more broadly to living or being alive, or to a general way of life: Eu morava numa casa pequena ("I lived in a small house") versus A gente vivia bem, mesmo sem muito dinheiro ("We lived well, even without much money").

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Note — The imperfect ending stress

Regular imperfect forms carry stable, predictable stress. For -ar verbs, listen for the open, nasal-free -ava ending in the singular and third-person plural: morava, moravam. For -er and -ir verbs, listen for the closed -ia ending, which sounds the same for both conjugations: vivia, assistia.

The stress in every regular imperfect form falls on the syllable immediately before the ending, never on the ending itself. Compare eu moro (present, stress on the first syllable) with eu morava (imperfect, stress on the second syllable, immediately before -va).

Building Block — Describing background and habit
quando eu era criança + imperfect verb
the imperfect describes what was ongoing, repeated, or true as a background condition
Quando eu era criança, eu morava numa cidade pequena e brincava no quintal todos os dias.
When I was a child, I lived in a small city and used to play in the backyard every day.

First Contrast: Preterite versus Imperfect

You already know the preterite for completed, one-time events. The imperfect describes what was ongoing, repeated, or simply true as background, without a clear beginning or end.

  • Quando eu era criança, eu morava no Rio de Janeiro. When I was a child, I lived in Rio de Janeiro. imperfect = background condition, no single completed moment
  • Em 2015, eu me mudei para São Paulo. In 2015, I moved to São Paulo. preterite = one completed event with a clear moment
  • Todos os domingos, a gente visitava os avós. Every Sunday, we used to visit our grandparents. imperfect = habitual, repeated action
  • No domingo passado, a gente visitou os avós. Last Sunday, we visited our grandparents. preterite = one specific, completed visit
Language Note — Two questions to guide the choice

At this stage, ask two guiding questions rather than trying to memorize a full rule system. First: is this a single, completed event with a clear edge in time, like an arrival or a specific visit? If so, use the preterite. Second: is this a repeated habit, an ongoing background condition, or a description of what something was like, without a clear single completed moment? If so, use the imperfect. Full control of this contrast, including more complex cases, is the focus of Chapter 19 and later volumes.

Model Dialogue

Context: Two old friends catching up, informal register, reminiscing about childhood.
Renata Onde você morava quando era criança? Where did you live when you were a child?
Diego Eu morava numa cidade pequena perto de Belo Horizonte. A gente tinha uma casa com um quintal enorme. I lived in a small city near Belo Horizonte. We had a house with an enormous backyard.
Renata Que legal! O que você fazia lá? Cool! What did you use to do there?
Diego Eu brincava com os vizinhos todos os dias e jogava futebol no quintal. Também tinha um cachorro que corria atrás da gente. E você? I used to play with the neighbors every day and play soccer in the backyard. I also had a dog that used to run after us. And you?
Renata Eu era mais tímida. Eu ficava em casa e lia muito. Mas todo domingo, a gente ia à casa dos meus avós. I was more shy. I used to stay home and read a lot. But every Sunday, we used to go to my grandparents' house.
Diego Que bom! E vocês ainda vão lá? That's nice! And do you still go there?
Renata Não muito. Minha avó morreu há dois anos, então agora é diferente. Not much. My grandmother died two years ago, so now it's different.
  1. List three things Diego used to do as a child.
  2. What did Renata use to do at home, and where did her family go every Sunday?
  3. Find one preterite verb in the dialogue and explain why it is preterite rather than imperfect.

Listening Practice

If no audio recording is available, ask a partner, instructor, or text-to-speech tool to read the script below aloud, or read it aloud to yourself slowly.

Listening script
Quando eu era criança, eu morava com meus pais e minha irmã numa casa perto do centro. Meu pai trabalhava numa loja e minha mãe era professora. Todos os dias, eu ia à escola de manhã e brincava com os vizinhos à tarde. Nos fins de semana, a gente visitava meus avós, que moravam numa fazenda pequena. Eu adorava a fazenda, porque lá eu podia correr, ver os animais e comer frutas do quintal. Às vezes, meu avô me contava histórias antigas sobre a nossa família.
When I was a child, I lived with my parents and my sister in a house near downtown. My father worked at a store and my mother was a teacher. Every day, I went to school in the morning and played with the neighbors in the afternoon. On weekends, we used to visit my grandparents, who lived on a small farm. I loved the farm, because there I could run, see the animals, and eat fruit from the yard. Sometimes my grandfather used to tell me old stories about our family.
  1. Describe the speaker's family and where they lived as a child.
  2. What did the speaker use to do every day?
  3. What did the speaker like about the grandparents' farm?

Reading Practice

A infância de Marcos

Marcos era filho único e morava com os pais num apartamento pequeno em Recife. Ele não tinha irmãos, mas tinha muitos primos que moravam perto. Todos os sábados, os primos se encontravam no parque perto de casa e jogavam bola até o fim da tarde. Marcos também gostava muito de desenhar, e ele desenhava animais e carros em cadernos velhos. Sua avó morava com a família e sempre cozinhava para todo mundo. Quando Marcos tinha dez anos, a família se mudou para outra cidade, e ele teve que dizer adeus aos primos e ao parque que ele tanto amava.

Notice era, morava, tinha, se encontravam, jogavam, gostava, desenhava, and cozinhava — imperfect forms describing background and habit — alongside se mudou and teve, preterite forms marking the single completed event that changed everything.

Tarefa de leitura — Reading task:

  1. Describe Marcos's family and where they lived.
  2. What did Marcos and his cousins use to do on Saturdays?
  3. Find the two preterite verbs in the passage and explain why the imperfect would not fit in their place.

Writing Workshop

Write a short paragraph of six to eight sentences describing your childhood (real or invented), following the model of Marcos's story above.

  1. Choose your setting. Decide where you lived and who was in your family.
  2. Describe people and conditions. Use the imperfect of ser, estar, or ter at least twice.
  3. Describe a habit or routine. Use at least two regular imperfect verbs to describe what you used to do.
  4. Add a place detail. Use morar or viver in the imperfect.
  5. Optional: add one completed event. If something changed, use one preterite verb to mark it.
  6. Connect the sentences using antigamente, todos os dias, or às vezes.
  7. Check diacritics and spelling throughout, including forms such as éramos, íamos, and tínhamos.

Lista de revisão — Editing checklist:

  • The paragraph uses at least three imperfect verbs correctly conjugated.
  • At least one of ser, estar, ter, or ir appears in the imperfect.
  • The paragraph describes people or conditions as well as a habitual action.
  • Any preterite verb used marks a genuinely single completed event.
  • All diacritics are preserved, including éramos, íamos, and tínhamos.
  • No unscheduled grammar, such as the subjunctive, appears productively.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Extended family and childhood in Brazil

Extended family often plays a visible role in childhood memories shared in Brazilian conversation, including grandparents, aunts, uncles, and cousins who may live nearby or even in the same household. Weekly family gatherings, as in this chapter's dialogue and reading, are common in many families but not universal, and household structures vary widely by region, class, and generation. Avoid assuming that every Brazilian childhood followed the same pattern of extended-family closeness.

Regional Variation — Childhood vocabulary across Lusophone contexts

Words for children's games and toys vary across the Lusophone world and even across regions of Brazil. This chapter's vocabulary reflects common contemporary Brazilian usage; European Portuguese and African Portuguese varieties may use different terms for some toys and games, which are recognition items only at this level.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Using the preterite for ongoing background description

Error: Saying Quando eu fui criança, eu morei numa casa pequena to describe a general childhood background.

Correction: Use the imperfect for background description: Quando eu era criança, eu morava numa casa pequena. The preterite would suggest a single, completed period rather than an ongoing state.

Common Mistake — Regularizing ser, ter, or ir

Error: Producing forms like eu seia, eu tia, or eu iia by applying the regular imperfect pattern.

Correction: ser, ter, and ir are irregular in the imperfect and must be memorized as whole forms: eu era, eu tinha, eu ia.

Common Mistake — Dropping the nasal in tínhamos and íamos

Error: Writing or saying tinhamos or iamos without the accent mark.

Correction: The accented forms tínhamos and íamos mark required stress and must keep their accent marks in writing.

3

Minha família e meus amigos

Family, Friends, and Description · Speak and Write Portuguese: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Use ter in the present tense to talk about family, age, and possession.
  2. Use possessive words such as meu, minha, meus, minhas to describe people who belong to your life.
  3. Describe family members and friends with adjectives that agree in gender and number.
  4. Use ele and ela to talk about a third person already introduced.
  5. Count and use numbers 0–100 to state ages.
  6. Recognize and produce nasal vowels in common family and description words.

Communication Scenario

After class, Marcos and Camila stay behind to look at photos on Marcos's phone. Camila asks about the people in the pictures, and Marcos describes his family and friends: who they are, how old they are, and what they are like. Camila does the same with a photo of her own. Neither of them is being interviewed anymore — this is a real, ordinary exchange between two people getting to know each other.

  1. You will practice describing people who belong to your own life — family, chosen family, or friends — using the word ter ("to have") for the first time.
  2. Every adjective in this chapter must agree with the noun it describes in gender and number, so pay close attention to endings as new descriptive words appear.

Pronunciation: Nasal Vowels

This chapter's family vocabulary is full of nasal vowels and nasal diphthongs, continuing from não in Chapter 1 and também in Chapter 2.

Pronunciation Focus — Nasal vowels in family words

A nasal vowel is a vowel sound that passes partly through the nose. In Portuguese spelling, nasalization is usually signaled by a tilde (ã, õ) or by m or n at the end of a syllable, rather than by a fully pronounced consonant.

  • mãe — the tilde over ã nasalizes the vowel; the word is one syllable, not two.
  • irmão and irmã — the same family word changes ending, and both endings are nasal.
  • também and engraçado both nasalize the vowel before a following m or n plus consonant, even though only também ends the word.

Do not fully pronounce the m or n as an English consonant; let the vowel itself carry the nasal quality.

Language Focus: Ter, Possessives, Agreement, and Numbers

Purpose: describe family and friends, state ages, and show who belongs to whom.

3.1 Ter in the present tense: eu tenho, você tem, ele/ela tem

Language Focus

Ter means "to have." It is used for possession, for family relationships (eu tenho um irmão, "I have a brother"), and for stating age (ele tem vinte anos, "he is twenty years old" — literally "he has twenty years"). This age pattern is different from English and should be learned as a fixed structure with ter, not translated word for word.

PronounTerExample
eutenhoEu tenho um irmão.
vocêtemVocê tem uma irmã?
eletemEle tem trinta anos.
elatemEla tem dois filhos.
  • Eu tenho uma amiga simpática. I have a nice friend.
  • Quantos anos você tem? — Eu tenho vinte e cinco anos. How old are you? — I am twenty-five years old.

3.2 Possessives: meu, minha, meus, minhas

Language Focus

Portuguese possessives agree with the gender and number of the thing or person possessed, not with the possessor. Meu and minha both mean "my"; the choice depends on whether the noun that follows is masculine or feminine.

FormUsed withExample
meumasculine singularmeu irmão
minhafeminine singularminha irmã
meusmasculine pluralmeus amigos
minhasfeminine pluralminhas amigas

The equivalent forms of seu/sua/seus/suas ("your," referring to você) follow the same agreement pattern and are introduced here for recognition and limited production.

  • Esta é minha mãe. This is my mother.
  • Estes são meus avós. These are my grandparents. avós = plural of avô/avó, used together for "grandparents"

3.3 Adjective agreement: gender and number

Language Focus

Adjectives in Portuguese usually follow the noun they describe and must agree with it in gender and number. Adjectives ending in -o typically change to -a for feminine nouns; most other endings, such as -e or a consonant, stay the same for both genders. Plural adjectives generally add -s.

  • Meu irmão é alto e simpático. My brother is tall and nice.
  • Minha irmã é alta e simpática. My sister is tall and nice. alto → alta; simpático → simpática (gender agreement)
  • Meus amigos são inteligentes. My friends are intelligent. inteligente → inteligentes (number agreement only; same for both genders)

3.4 Ele and ela for a person already introduced

Building Block — Talking about someone else
Este é meu irmão. Ele tem vinte anos. Ele é alto.
Once a person has been introduced by name or relationship, ele or ela replaces repeating the noun, just as "he" or "she" does in English.
Esta é minha amiga Camila. Ela é professora e ela é muito simpática.
This is my friend Camila. She is a teacher and she is very nice.

Numbers 0–100 are used here mainly to state ages; they will return with prices and quantities in Chapter 9.

Model Dialogue: Esta é minha família

Context: After class, Camila and Marcos look at photos on Marcos's phone.

Camila: Quem são eles? Who are they?
Marcos: Esta é minha família! Este é meu pai, e esta é minha mãe. This is my family! This is my father, and this is my mother.
Camila: E o rapaz alto? Ele é seu irmão? And the tall guy? Is he your brother?
Marcos: Sim, ele é meu irmão Diego. Ele tem vinte e dois anos. Ele é muito engraçado. Yes, he's my brother Diego. He's twenty-two. He's very funny.

Camila shows Marcos a photo on her own phone.

Camila: Esta é minha avó. Ela tem oitenta anos e é muito inteligente. This is my grandmother. She's eighty and very intelligent.
Marcos: Que legal! Você tem irmãos também? Cool! Do you have siblings too?
Camila: Tenho, sim. Eu tenho uma irmã. Ela tem trinta anos. Yes, I do. I have a sister. She's thirty.

Notice that Camila answers tenho, sim rather than a single word for "yes" — repeating the conjugated verb, as with ser in Chapter 2, is a common way to answer a yes/no question.

Listening Practice

If no audio recording is available, ask a partner, instructor, or text-to-speech tool to read the lines below aloud, or read them aloud to yourself slowly.

Listening script
Esta é minha mãe. Ela tem cinquenta anos e é muito simpática. Este é meu irmão. Ele tem vinte anos e é muito engraçado.
This is my mother. She's fifty and very nice. This is my brother. He's twenty and very funny.
  1. Listen to (or read aloud) the script above, then state the age and one adjective for each person mentioned.
  2. Listen for the possessive words. Which one changes to agree with mãe? Which one changes to agree with irmão?
  3. Listen for the nasal vowels in mãe, irmão, and engraçado.

Speaking Practice

Prática controlada — Controlled practice: complete the sentence

Say each sentence aloud, filling the blank with your own information: Eu tenho ______. (a family member or friend) Ele/Ela tem ______ anos. (age) Ele/Ela é ______. (one adjective, agreeing correctly).

Tarefa comunicativa — Communicative task: describe a photo

Using a real or imagined photo, describe three people to a partner: who they are (using a possessive and a family or friend word), how old they are, and one thing they are like. Your partner should ask at least one follow-up question using quantos anos or como.

Prática individual — Solo alternative

Record yourself describing two people in your life in three sentences each. Listen back and check every adjective ending against the gender of the person you described.

Writing Workshop

Complete the following linked writing tasks in order.

Tarefa 1 — Possessive practice: Write the correct possessive (meu, minha, meus, or minhas) before each noun: ___ irmão, ___ irmã, ___ amigos, ___ amigas, ___ avó.

Tarefa 2 — Agreement practice: Adjust each adjective to agree with the noun given: alto → minha mãe é ______; simpático → meus amigos são ______; engraçado → minha irmã é ______.

Fill in the blank
Eu tenho ______ (número) irmãos. Meu/Minha ______ se chama ______. Ele/Ela tem ______ anos.
I have ______ (number) siblings. My ______ is named ______. He/She is ______ years old.

Tarefa 3 — Short written profile: Write five short sentences describing two people in your family or friend group, including their relationship to you, their age, and at least one adjective each.

  1. Complete the possessive practice first. This checks your control of gender agreement before you write full sentences.
  2. Complete the agreement practice. Check every adjective ending against the noun it describes.
  3. Fill in the blank paragraph. Confirm that ter is conjugated correctly for each subject.
  4. Draft your five-sentence profile. Keep every word within this chapter's vocabulary plus Chapters 1–2.
  5. Check every possessive and adjective against the vocabulary tables. Correct any form that does not match the gender or number of its noun.

Lista de revisão — Editing checklist:

  • Every possessive matches the gender and number of the noun it precedes.
  • Every adjective agrees in gender and number with the noun it describes.
  • ter is conjugated correctly for the subject used (tenho vs. tem).
  • Ages are expressed with ter plus a number plus anos.
  • All diacritics, including nasal vowels, are preserved.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Family models and disclosure

Families take many forms across Brazil and the wider Lusophone world: nuclear families, extended households, blended families, and chosen families of close friends. When describing family in Portuguese, learners are never required to disclose more personal information than they are comfortable sharing, and may describe a fictionalized family if they prefer.

Regional Variation — Talking about age

Brazilian Portuguese uses ter to state age (ele tem vinte anos). This pattern is consistent across Lusophone varieties, though some formal or written registers may occasionally use other constructions. This course treats the ter pattern as the standard production form.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using "ser" for age

Error: Saying ele é vinte anos, modeled on English "he is twenty years old."

Correction: Portuguese uses ter, not ser, for age: ele tem vinte anos.

Common Mistake — Possessive not agreeing with the noun

Error: Saying meu irmã or minha irmão, mismatching the possessive and the noun's gender.

Correction: The possessive agrees with the noun that follows it, not with the speaker: meu irmão, minha irmã.

Common Mistake — Forgetting adjective agreement

Error: Saying minha irmã é alto, leaving the adjective in its masculine form.

Correction: Adjectives must agree with the noun they describe: minha irmã é alta. Learn each adjective pair together, as in the vocabulary table.

4

Hoje é segunda-feira

Days, Dates, and Schedules · Speak and Write Portuguese: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Name the days of the week and the months of the year with correct lowercase spelling.
  2. Say today's date and ask what day or date it is.
  3. Use formulaic estar to say where and when something takes place.
  4. Use em and às to talk about days and clock times.
  5. Tell time using hours and half hours.
  6. Give and understand basic appointment and schedule information.

Communication Scenario

Camila and Marcos are comparing their weekly class schedules before deciding when to study together. Camila checks her phone calendar and reads off days, dates, and times, while Marcos matches his own schedule against hers. Neither is used to speaking Portuguese about calendars yet, so they keep the exchange short, checking each detail carefully before agreeing on a time.

  1. You will practice naming days, dates, and clock times in Portuguese, and using them to compare schedules with someone else.
  2. Days of the week and months of the year are not capitalized in Portuguese, even though they are in English, so pay attention to capitalization as new vocabulary appears.

Pronunciation: Stress in Numbers and Days

This chapter reuses the numbers from Chapter 3 for clock times and dates, and introduces compound day names built on -feira.

Pronunciation Focus — Stress in compound day names

Words ending in -feira carry their main stress on the first part of the compound, not on -feira itself: SEgunda-feira, TERça-feira. Say the day name, then add -feira without a second strong stress.

  • sábado and domingo — stress falls on the second-to-last syllable, as in most Portuguese words without a written accent.
  • reunião — nasal diphthong at the end, continuing the nasal patterns from Chapter 3.
  • horário — the written accent on á marks the stressed syllable directly.

Reading dates and times aloud slowly, syllable by syllable at first, helps keep the stress in the right place before speeding up.

Language Focus: Estar, Em/Às, and Time Expressions

Purpose: give days, dates, times, and basic appointment information.

4.1 Formulaic estar: hoje é / estamos em

Language Focus

This chapter uses only two fixed, formulaic patterns with estar, without yet teaching its full present-tense conjugation. Hoje é segunda-feira uses ser, already active since Chapter 2, to identify the day. Estamos em outubro is a fixed calendar expression meaning "it is October" (literally "we are in October"), used here as a memorized pattern rather than a productive verb form. The full present tense of estar is scheduled for a later chapter.

PatternExampleMeaning
Hoje é...Hoje é sexta-feira.Today is Friday.
Estamos em...Estamos em outubro.It's October. (fixed pattern)
  • Que dia é hoje? — Hoje é quarta-feira. What day is today? — Today is Wednesday.

4.2 Em and às for days and clock times

Language Focus

Portuguese uses different prepositions for different kinds of time. Em combines with days of the week to mean "on": na segunda-feira ("on Monday") uses the contraction na (em + a), which you will study formally as a contraction in a later chapter. Às combines with clock time to mean "at": às dez horas ("at ten o'clock"). These two patterns should not be confused, since English "at" and "on" do not map onto Portuguese the same way.

PortuguêsUsed forExample
na segunda-feiraon + day of the weekA aula é na segunda-feira.
às dez horasat + clock timeA reunião é às dez horas.
  • Minha aula de português é na terça-feira, às nove horas. My Portuguese class is on Tuesday, at nine o'clock.

4.3 Telling time

Language Focus

Use é only for one o'clock and são for all other hours. Add e meia for the half hour. Numbers you already know from Chapter 3 are reused here for clock times.

PortuguêsEnglish
É uma hora.It's one o'clock.
São quatro horas.It's four o'clock.
São sete e meia.It's seven thirty.
É meio-dia.It's noon.

Numbers above thirty and minute-by-minute time expressions are not required in this chapter; they return with prices and quantities in Chapter 9.

Model Dialogue: Que horas você tem aula?

Context: Camila and Marcos compare their weekly schedules before class.

Camila: Que dia é hoje? What day is today?
Marcos: Hoje é segunda-feira. Estamos em outubro. Today is Monday. It's October.
Camila: Que horas é sua aula de português? What time is your Portuguese class?
Marcos: Minha aula é na segunda e na quarta, às nove horas. My class is on Monday and Wednesday, at nine o'clock.

Camila checks the calendar on her phone.

Camila: Eu tenho uma reunião na sexta-feira, às dez horas. I have a meeting on Friday, at ten o'clock.
Marcos: E de tarde? Você tem um compromisso? And in the afternoon? Do you have an appointment?
Camila: Não, de tarde eu não tenho compromisso. Vamos estudar juntos? No, in the afternoon I don't have an appointment. Should we study together?
Marcos: Boa ideia! Sexta-feira, de tarde. Good idea! Friday, in the afternoon.

Notice não tenho compromisso, reusing ter from Chapter 3 with the negation não already active since Chapter 1.

Listening script
Hoje é quinta-feira. Estamos em outubro. Minha aula de português é às nove horas. Minha reunião é na sexta-feira, às duas horas.
Today is Thursday. It's October. My Portuguese class is at nine o'clock. My meeting is on Friday, at two o'clock.

  2. Listen for na and às. Which one comes before a day of the week? Which one comes before a clock time?
  3. Listen for the stress pattern in quinta-feira and sexta-feira.

Speaking Practice

Tarefa comunicativa — Communicative task: compare schedules

With a partner, name three things on your weekly schedule (a class, a meeting, an appointment), each with a day and a time. Your partner should ask que horas for at least one item and repeat it back to check.

Prática individual — Solo alternative

Record yourself stating today's day and month, then describing two items on your schedule with a day and a time each. Listen back and check that every day name is lowercase in your written notes.

Lista de revisão — Editing checklist:

  • Days of the week and months are written in lowercase.
  • na is used before a day of the week, and às is used before a clock time.
  • é is used only for one o'clock; são is used for all other hours.
  • The formulaic patterns hoje é and estamos em are used correctly and not altered as if they were fully conjugated.
  • All diacritics, including nasal vowels, are preserved.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Portuguese date and time conventions

Portuguese writes dates and calendar words differently from English. Days of the week and months are never capitalized in ordinary writing, and dates are commonly written day before month. Digital calendars and appointment apps in Brazil generally follow these same lowercase and day-first conventions, so recognizing them helps with everyday reading, not only classroom exercises.

Regional Variation — Talking about the clock

The é/são pattern for clock time and the twelve-hour system with de manhã, de tarde, and de noite are common across Lusophone communities. Some formal, institutional, or written contexts use the twenty-four-hour clock instead; this course treats the twelve-hour spoken pattern as the standard production form for this level.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Capitalizing days and months

Error: Writing Segunda-feira or Outubro with a capital letter, modeled on English capitalization.

Correction: Portuguese days and months are lowercase: segunda-feira, outubro, even at the start of a sentence about the calendar unless the sentence itself begins with that word for another grammatical reason.

Common Mistake — Confusing na and às

Error: Saying às segunda-feira or na dez horas, mixing up which preposition goes with days and which goes with clock times.

Correction: Use na with days of the week and às with clock times: na segunda-feira, às dez horas.

Common Mistake — Using é for every hour

Error: Saying é quatro horas, using the singular form for an hour other than one o'clock.

Correction: Use é only for one o'clock and são for every other hour: são quatro horas.

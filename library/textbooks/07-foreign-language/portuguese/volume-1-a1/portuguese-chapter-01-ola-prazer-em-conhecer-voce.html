Olá! Prazer em conhecer você

First Encounters · Speak and Write Portuguese: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Recognize the Portuguese alphabet and begin producing its vowel sounds, including the written accent marks that Portuguese requires from the very first word.
  2. Greet someone and say goodbye using level-appropriate expressions in Brazilian Portuguese.
  3. Introduce yourself using eu sou..., and ask someone else's name.
  4. Ask and answer a small set of controlled questions using only formulaic language.
  5. Use basic classroom survival language, including simple requests for repetition and clarification.
  6. Count from 0 to 10 in Portuguese.

Communication Scenario

Marcos is starting a beginner Portuguese class on a Tuesday evening. As students arrive, the teacher greets each one at the door, and the students greet each other before the lesson begins. Nobody in the room has met before, so the entire first evening is built from the same small handful of expressions, said again and again, until they start to feel natural. This chapter follows that first evening, greeting by greeting, name by name.

  1. You will meet the same expressions in several short exchanges, so that repetition, not memorization, builds your first working Portuguese.
  2. Every new word introduced in this chapter keeps its correct diacritics, since Portuguese spelling and pronunciation depend on them from the very beginning.

Pronunciation: The Portuguese Alphabet and Diacritics

Portuguese uses the Latin alphabet, with a small set of extra marks called diacritics: the acute accent (á, é, í, ó, ú), the circumflex (â, ê, ô), the tilde (ã, õ), the grave accent (à), and the cedilla (ç). These marks are not decoration. They change pronunciation, and sometimes they change meaning entirely.

Pronunciation Focus — Diacritics and vowel awareness

Say each vocabulary word above aloud, paying attention to any accent mark. The tilde in não signals a nasal sound: the vowel is pushed partly through the nose, something English vowels do not normally do. You do not need to master nasal vowels fully yet — that system develops gradually starting in Chapter 3 — but you should begin noticing that não sounds different from an English word like "now."

  • Portuguese vowels are generally shorter and more precise than English vowels; avoid gliding an English-style diphthong onto them.
  • Never drop an accent mark when writing, even informally in this course. Está and esta are different words, and only the accent tells them apart.
  • The letter h is silent at the start of a word; you will see this in words like hoje in a later chapter.

Language Focus: Introducing Yourself

Purpose: greet someone, introduce yourself with ser, ask a name, and answer simple yes/no questions.

1.1 The formula Eu sou...

Language Focus

The expression eu sou... uses the verb ser, one of two Portuguese verbs that can translate as "to be." At this stage, treat eu sou as a fixed formula for stating who you are. You do not yet need to conjugate ser for other subjects or contrast it with estar; that system develops gradually starting in Chapter 2.

To ask someone's name, Brazilian Portuguese commonly uses qual é o seu nome? or como você se chama?. Both are treated as fixed units for now.

  • Eu sou Marcos. I am Marcos.
  • Qual é o seu nome? — Eu sou Camila. What is your name? — I am Camila.

1.2 Yes/no questions with rising intonation

Language Focus

Brazilian Portuguese usually forms a yes/no question not by changing word order, but by using rising intonation on a statement. Você é estudante? and Você é estudante. can use the exact same words; only the rising pitch at the end signals a question in speech, and a question mark signals it in writing.

  • Tudo bem? Everything okay? (a very common informal greeting question)
  • Tudo bem! Everything's fine! (a common reply)

1.3 Classroom survival language

Building Block — Asking for repetition
Como? / Pode repetir, por favor?
These two formulas let you signal that you did not understand something, without needing any additional grammar. Use them freely from your very first class.
Como? Pode repetir, por favor?
What? Can you repeat, please?

You will meet ser again in Chapter 2, where it expands to talking about origin, nationality, and language.

Model Dialogue: Prazer em conhecer você

Context: The teacher greets a new student at the door on the first evening of class.

Professora: Boa noite! Qual é o seu nome? Good evening! What is your name?
Marcos: Boa noite! Eu sou Marcos. Good evening! I am Marcos.
Professora: Prazer em conhecer você, Marcos. Entra, por favor. Nice to meet you, Marcos. Come in, please.

Marcos sits down next to another student, who turns to greet him informally.

Camila: Oi! Como você se chama? Hi! What's your name?
Marcos: Oi! Eu sou Marcos. E você? Hi! I'm Marcos. And you?
Camila: Eu sou Camila. Prazer! I'm Camila. Nice to meet you!

Notice that Marcos greets the teacher with boa noite and greets Camila with oi, choosing his greeting according to context and relationship, not a single fixed rule.

Listening Practice

If no audio recording is available, ask a partner, instructor, or text-to-speech tool to read the lines below aloud, or read them aloud to yourself slowly, paying attention to the diacritics.

Listening script
Boa tarde! Eu sou Rafael. E qual é o seu nome?
Good afternoon! I am Rafael. And what is your name?
  1. Listen to (or read aloud) the script above, then answer, in Portuguese, with your own name using the correct formula.
  2. Listen for the greeting word at the start of the script. Which time of day does it suggest?
  3. Listen for the nasal sound in não if your partner adds it to the exchange. How is it different from an English vowel?

Speaking Practice

Prática controlada — Controlled practice: greet and respond

Practice saying each greeting and courtesy word from the vocabulary table aloud, three times each, paying attention to diacritics and vowel sounds.

Tarefa comunicativa — Communicative task: meet a classmate

With a partner, greet each other informally, ask and answer each other's names, and say goodbye. Then repeat the exchange as if meeting a teacher, using a more neutral greeting such as boa tarde or boa noite.

Prática individual — Solo alternative

Record yourself greeting an imaginary teacher and an imaginary classmate, introducing yourself in both exchanges. Listen back and check your pronunciation of any accented vowels against the vocabulary table.

Reading Practice

Oi! Eu sou Sônia. Obrigada! Tchau!

Tarefa de leitura — Reading task:

  1. What is the name of the person who wrote this note?
  2. Is this note written in an informal or a neutral style? Point to one word that tells you.
  3. Find the word that means "thank you" and the word that means "bye." Why does obrigada end in -a here rather than -o?

  2. Fill in the blank exchange. Check that your answer uses the full formula, not just your name alone.

Lista de revisão — Editing checklist:

  • Every accent mark is included where the vocabulary table shows it.
  • The formula eu sou... is used correctly and completely.
  • The greeting chosen matches the intended relationship and time of day.
  • obrigado/obrigada agrees with the writer's own gender.
  • No new vocabulary from outside this chapter has been used.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Greeting warmly

Among many Brazilian speakers, greetings often include a warm tone and, in person, a light physical gesture such as a handshake or a cheek kiss depending on region, familiarity, and gender norms. This textbook focuses on the words themselves; nonverbal greeting customs vary widely and are best learned directly from the community you are engaging with.

Regional Variation — Portuguese beyond Brazil

Portuguese is spoken as a first or official language in Brazil, Portugal, Angola, Mozambique, Cape Verde, Guinea-Bissau, São Tomé and Príncipe, Timor-Leste, and in communities in Macau, Goa, and the diaspora. This course uses contemporary Brazilian Portuguese as its production model, and it will introduce labeled comparisons with other varieties as the volumes progress. Speaking Portuguese does not by itself indicate a person's nationality, ethnicity, or political views.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Dropping accent marks

Error: Writing nao instead of não, or obrigado for a female speaker, treating diacritics as optional.

Correction: Always include the tilde, acute, circumflex, grave, or cedilla shown in the vocabulary table. These marks change pronunciation and sometimes meaning.

Common Mistake — Confusing obrigado and obrigada

Error: Using obrigado regardless of the speaker's own gender.

Correction: A speaker who identifies as a man typically says obrigado, and a speaker who identifies as a woman typically says obrigada. The word agrees with the person speaking, not with the person being thanked.

Common Mistake — Gliding English vowel sounds onto Portuguese

Error: Pronouncing Portuguese vowels as English-style diphthongs, for example stretching the o in não into an English "oh" sound.

Correction: Keep Portuguese vowels short and precise, and let the tilde signal nasalization rather than an English glide.

Minha rotina

Daily Routines and Frequency · Speak and Write Portuguese: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Overview

Describing a typical day is one of the most useful things you can do in a new language — it lets you talk about work, school, and everyday habits with people you meet. This chapter continues Unit II by teaching you to describe your daily routine using regular -er and -ir verbs in the present tense, a small set of formulaic reflexive routine expressions, the inclusive pronoun a gente, and frequency words such as sempre, nunca, and às vezes. You will also expand your control of two essential irregular verbs, fazer and ir, which recur throughout everyday Portuguese.

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Conjugate regular -er and -ir verbs in the present tense for everyday actions.
  2. Use fazer and ir to describe common daily activities.
  3. Use a small set of formulaic reflexive expressions to describe personal routines.
  4. Use a gente alongside nós to talk about shared routines.
  5. Describe how often you do something using sempre, nunca, and às vezes.
  6. Ask and answer simple questions about someone else's daily routine.

Communication Scenario

After the café meetup, two members of the conversation group decide to keep practicing by text every morning, describing what their day usually looks like. To keep the exchange going, each member needs to describe a typical day: what they do, when they do it, and how often.

  1. This chapter activates regular -er and -ir verbs, formulaic reflexive routine expressions, a gente, frequency words, and the verbs fazer and ir.
  2. You may invent your own routine if you prefer not to describe your real schedule.
  3. Chapters 1–6 systems (greetings, ser, ter, days and times, regular -ar verbs, gostar de, querer) continue to recycle throughout this chapter.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation — Open versus closed vowels in -er and -ir endings

Regular -er verbs shift vowel quality between forms: notice the more open vowel in come compared with the closed vowel in comem. Practice these pairs aloud: come / comem, bebe / bebem, dorme / dormem. This production model reflects contemporary Brazilian Portuguese.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

Regular -er and -ir verbs in the present tense

Regular -er and -ir verbs let you describe everyday actions such as eating, learning, and sleeping, expanding the -ar pattern from Chapter 6.

Building Block
comer: eu como, você/ele/ela come, nós comemos, vocês/eles/elas comem
assistir: eu assisto, você/ele/ela assiste, nós assistimos, vocês/eles/elas assistem
note: -er and -ir verbs share the same endings except in the nós form (-emos vs. -imos)
Eu como às sete horas.
I eat at seven o'clock.
  • Ele aprende português todo dia. He learns Portuguese every day. aprender → aprende
  • Nós assistimos à televisão à noite. We watch television at night. assistir → assistimos
  • Eles dormem cedo. They sleep early. dormir → dormem

Fazer and ir

Fazer and ir are two of the most frequent verbs in everyday Portuguese and appear constantly in routine descriptions.

Building Block
fazer: eu faço, você/ele/ela faz, nós fazemos, vocês/eles/elas fazem
ir: eu vou, você/ele/ela vai, nós vamos, vocês/eles/elas vão
note: both verbs are irregular in the eu form; ir is irregular throughout
Eu faço exercício de manhã.
I exercise in the morning.
  • Você vai para o trabalho de manhã? Do you go to work in the morning?
  • A gente vai para a escola juntos. We go to school together.

Formulaic reflexive routines

A small number of daily routine expressions use a reflexive pronoun in Portuguese, even though English does not mark them this way. These three forms are learned whole this chapter.

  • Eu me acordo às seis horas. I wake up at six o'clock.
  • Eu me levanto cedo. I get up early.
  • Eu me deito às dez horas. I go to bed at ten o'clock.

Other persons of these reflexive verbs, and the reflexive system in general, remain receptive until a later chapter activates them.

A gente for shared routines

A gente is an everyday, informal alternative to nós that is extremely common in spoken Brazilian Portuguese.

  • A gente estuda português duas vezes por semana. We study Portuguese twice a week. a gente + third-person singular verb form

A gente takes a third-person singular verb form even though it means "we." Nós remains fully productive alongside it; both are correct in everyday Brazilian speech.

Language Focus — Frequency words

Sempre, nunca, and às vezes typically appear before the verb: Eu sempre como às sete horas. / Eu nunca como carne. / Às vezes eu estudo à noite. This pattern is fully productive from this chapter forward.

Model Dialogue: A rotina da Camila

Context: Two friends from the conversation group text each other about their daily routines. Informal, everyday register.

Marcos: Oi, Camila! Como é a sua rotina? Hi, Camila! What's your routine like?
Camila: Oi, Marcos! Eu me acordo às seis horas e eu me levanto às seis e meia. Hi, Marcos! I wake up at six o'clock and I get up at six thirty.
Marcos: E depois? Você trabalha de manhã? And then? Do you work in the morning?
Camila: Sim, eu sempre trabalho de manhã. À tarde, eu estudo português e eu faço exercício. Yes, I always work in the morning. In the afternoon, I study Portuguese and I exercise.
Marcos: Você assiste à televisão à noite? Do you watch television at night?
Camila: Às vezes. Eu nunca assisto muito, porque eu me deito cedo. E você, Marcos? Qual é a sua rotina? Sometimes. I never watch a lot, because I go to bed early. And you, Marcos? What's your routine?
Marcos: A gente é parecido! Eu também estudo à tarde e eu também faço exercício. We're similar! I also study in the afternoon and I also exercise.

This dialogue combines regular -er/-ir verbs, formulaic reflexives, fazer, frequency words, and a gente (Ch. 7) with question words and time expressions recycled from Chapters 2 and 4.

Writing Workshop

Complete the following linked writing tasks in order to build a short daily-routine message for your morning text exchange with the conversation group.

Tarefa 1 — Waking and sleeping: Write one sentence using eu me acordo and one sentence using eu me deito, each with a time.

Tarefa 2 — Actions: Write two sentences describing things you do during the day, using regular -er/-ir verbs, fazer, or ir.

Fill in the blank
Oi! Eu me acordo às ______. De manhã, eu ______. À tarde, eu ______. Eu sempre ______, mas eu nunca ______.
Hi! I wake up at ______. In the morning, I ______. In the afternoon, I ______. I always ______, but I never ______.

Tarefa 3 — Frequency: Write one sentence with sempre and one sentence with nunca, describing your own habits.

Tarefa 4 — Full note: Combine Tarefas 1–3 into one short note of four to five sentences for the group chat.

  1. Draft each section separately. Write the routine, actions, and frequency sentences one at a time before combining them.
  2. Combine the sections into one note. Add a greeting at the start, recycled from Chapter 1.
  3. Check every verb form. Confirm that -er/-ir verbs, fazer, and ir are conjugated correctly for the subject.
  4. Check frequency word placement. Confirm that sempre, nunca, and às vezes appear in a natural position.
  5. Check diacritics. Confirm that accented forms such as às and também are written correctly.

Lista de revisão — Editing checklist:

  • The note includes a greeting, a waking/sleeping time, at least two daily actions, and one frequency word for each of sempre and nunca.
  • Regular -er/-ir verbs, fazer, and ir are conjugated correctly for their subjects.
  • Formulaic reflexive expressions are used only in the eu form taught this chapter.
  • All diacritics, including às and também, are preserved.
  • At least one frequency word is used correctly.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Daily schedules in Brazil

Work and study schedules vary widely across Brazil depending on region, profession, and household. Some people work a single continuous shift, while others take a longer midday break and return to work in the afternoon. This pattern is common but not universal; do not assume a single national schedule.

Regional Variation — A gente and nós

A gente is extremely common in everyday spoken Brazilian Portuguese across regions, though nós remains fully correct and more common in formal writing. Both forms are productive in this course; a gente is not a mistake for nós.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Using -ar endings on -er/-ir verbs

Error: Saying eu come or eu assista, borrowing endings from a different verb group.

Correction: Match the ending to the verb group: eu como (comer), eu assisto (assistir).

Common Mistake — Verb agreement with a gente

Error: Saying a gente estudamos, using the nós ending with a gente.

Correction: a gente takes a third-person singular verb: a gente estuda.

Common Mistake — Placing frequency words after the verb

Error: Saying eu como sempre às sete horas, copying English word order too closely.

Correction: Frequency words typically come before the verb: eu sempre como às sete horas.

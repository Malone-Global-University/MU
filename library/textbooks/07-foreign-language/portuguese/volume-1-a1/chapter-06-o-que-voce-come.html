O que você come?

Food, Drink, and Preferences · Speak and Write Portuguese: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Overview

Talking about food is one of the most frequent things people do in everyday Portuguese conversation, from ordering a coffee to describing what you like to eat. This chapter opens Unit II by teaching you to name common foods and drinks, say what you like and want using gostar de and querer, and place a simple order in a café or restaurant. You will also begin using regular -ar verbs in the present tense, which opens the door to describing everyday actions in the chapters ahead.

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Name common foods and drinks using correct articles and gender.
  2. Conjugate regular -ar verbs in the present tense for everyday actions.
  3. State likes and dislikes using gostar de.
  4. Express wants using querer.
  5. Order simply in a café or restaurant using Eu queria... and ..., por favor.
  6. Ask someone else about their food preferences and respond to the same questions.

Communication Scenario

After the conversation group's first video call, several members decide to meet in person at a small café before their next session. Before the group arrives, each member needs to be able to look at a simple menu, say what they like and want to eat or drink, and place an order with the server.

  1. This chapter activates regular -ar verbs, gostar de, articles with food nouns, querer, and the ordering formula N, por favor.
  2. You may invent your own food preferences if you prefer not to describe your real diet.
  3. Chapters 1–5 systems (greetings, ser, ter, days and times) continue to recycle throughout this chapter.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation — Nasal vowels in food words

Several common food words contain nasal vowels, where m or n signals that the vowel is pronounced through the nose rather than as a full consonant. Practice these words aloud: pão, feijão, também (also), and um pão. This production model reflects contemporary Brazilian Portuguese.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

Regular -ar verbs in the present tense

Regular -ar verbs are the largest group of Portuguese verbs and let you describe everyday actions such as eating, drinking, and working.

Building Block
comer → como / come / comemos / comem
note: comer is an -er verb shown for contrast; this chapter's productive pattern is -ar verbs such as falar
falar: eu falo, você/ele/ela fala, nós falamos, vocês/eles/elas falam
Eu falo português.
I speak Portuguese.
  • Eu tomo café de manhã. I drink coffee in the morning. tomar → tomo
  • Ela toma suco de laranja. She drinks orange juice. tomar → toma
  • Nós compramos pão no mercado. We buy bread at the market. comprar → compramos

Gostar de for likes and dislikes

Gostar de is the standard way to say you like something in Portuguese. The preposition de is a fixed part of the expression and does not change.

  • Eu gosto de arroz e feijão. I like rice and beans.
  • Você gosta de café? Do you like coffee?
  • Ela não gosta de peixe. She doesn't like fish.

Contraction with a definite article, such as gostar do café for a specific coffee already mentioned, is receptive only this chapter; production stays with the simple gostar de + noun pattern.

Querer for wants and ordering

Querer lets you say what you want, and its polite past-tense form queria is the standard soft way to order in a restaurant.

  • Eu quero uma água, por favor. I want a water, please.
  • Eu queria um café, por favor. I would like a coffee, please. formulaic softened order

Eu queria is taught here as a formulaic, memorized politeness pattern for ordering. Its underlying imperfect-tense grammar is not activated until Chapter 18.

Language Focus — Ordering with N, por favor

The simplest way to order in a Brazilian café or restaurant is to name the item followed by por favor: Um café, por favor. / Uma água, por favor. This pattern is fully productive from this chapter forward.

Model Dialogue: No restaurante

Context: A server and a customer at a small café. Informal, everyday service register.

Garçom: Boa tarde! O que você quer? Good afternoon! What do you want?
Cliente: Boa tarde! Eu queria um café e um pão de queijo, por favor. Good afternoon! I would like a coffee and a cheese bread, please.
Garçom: Você quer suco também? Do you want juice too?
Cliente: Não, obrigado. Eu não gosto muito de suco. Eu gosto de água. No, thank you. I don't like juice much. I like water.
Garçom: Uma água, então. Mais alguma coisa? A water, then. Anything else?
Cliente: Não, só isso, por favor. No, just that, please.
Garçom: Perfeito! Perfect!

This dialogue combines querer (Ch. 6), gostar de (Ch. 6), and courtesy language recycled from Chapter 1.

Writing Workshop

Complete the following linked writing tasks in order to build a short note about your food preferences for the conversation group's café meetup.

Tarefa 1 — Likes: Write one or two sentences about foods or drinks you like, using gostar de.

Tarefa 2 — Dislikes: Write one sentence about a food or drink you do not like, using não gostar de.

Fill in the blank
Oi! Eu gosto de ______ e de ______. Eu não gosto de ______. Eu quero ______, por favor.
Hi! I like ______ and ______. I don't like ______. I want ______, please.

Tarefa 3 — Order: Write one sentence placing an order for the café meetup, using Eu queria... or N, por favor.

Tarefa 4 — Full note: Combine Tarefas 1–3 into one short note of three to four sentences for the group chat.

  1. Draft each section separately. Write the likes, dislikes, and order sentences one at a time before combining them.
  2. Combine the sections into one note. Add a greeting at the start, recycled from Chapter 1.
  3. Check every verb form. Confirm that gostar de and querer are conjugated correctly for the subject.
  4. Check articles and gender. Confirm that food nouns have the correct article and gender.
  5. Check diacritics. Confirm that nasal vowels such as those in pão and feijão are written correctly.

Lista de revisão — Editing checklist:

  • The note includes a greeting, at least one like, one dislike, and one order.
  • gostar de and querer are used correctly for their taught functions.
  • Food nouns include the correct article and gender.
  • All diacritics, including nasal vowels, are preserved.
  • The ordering pattern N, por favor is used at least once.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Meals in Brazil

In many Brazilian households, almoço (lunch) is the largest meal of the day and commonly includes rice, beans, and a protein such as chicken or fish. This pattern is common but not universal; meal timing and composition vary by region, household, and lifestyle.

Regional Variation — Ordering language

Eu queria... is a common, polite way to order across Brazilian Portuguese contexts. In some settings, speakers may instead use the direct Eu quero... or simply name the item with por favor. All three remain within the productive Brazilian model used in this course.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Dropping "de" after gostar

Error: Saying eu gosto café, omitting the required preposition.

Correction: gostar always requires de: eu gosto de café.

Common Mistake — Wrong gender with food nouns

Error: Saying o água or a suco, guessing gender from the English equivalent.

Correction: Learn each noun with its article: a água, o suco. Portuguese gender is a property of the noun, not of the object itself.

Common Mistake — Wrong -ar ending for the subject

Error: Saying nós fala português, using the third-person singular ending with a plural subject.

Correction: Match the ending to the subject: nós falamos português.

Eu sou estudante

Identity, Origin, and Roles · Speak and Write Portuguese: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Use subject pronouns (eu, você, ele, ela) together with the present tense of ser.
  2. State your nationality, your language, and your origin using ser de....
  3. Say what your role is, such as student or worker, using correctly gendered nouns and articles.
  4. Ask and answer basic questions with quem, o que, and de onde.
  5. Distinguish nationality, ethnicity, residence, and language as separate ideas.
  6. Recognize masculine and feminine articles (o/a, um/uma) attached to new nouns.

Communication Scenario

The first class continues. After the round of greetings from Chapter 1, the teacher asks each student to say a little more about themselves: where they are from, what language or languages they speak, and what they do. The students discover that they come from different countries and different backgrounds, and that ser — the verb they used to say their name — can do much more work than they first realized.

  1. You will hear and read the same handful of identity questions repeated with different classmates, so that the pattern becomes automatic rather than memorized word by word.
  2. Every noun introduced in this chapter appears with its article, since Portuguese gender is a property of the word itself, not of the person or thing it names.

Pronunciation: Vowel Precision and Nationality Words

This chapter's vocabulary introduces two pronunciation points that recur throughout the course: the circumflex accent, and the nasal diphthong in também.

Pronunciation Focus — Circumflex and nasal diphthong

The circumflex (ê) in você and inglês marks a closed, precise vowel; keep it short and do not glide it toward an English "ay" sound.

  • Também ends in a nasal diphthong, spelled -em. As with não in Chapter 1, let the sound pass partly through the nose rather than closing on a full English "m."
  • Keep practicing full nasal-vowel mastery gradually; this system continues to develop starting in Chapter 3.
  • Português carries stress on the final syllable, shown by the acute accent on ê; say it as por-tu-GUÊS, not with English-style stress on the first syllable.

Language Focus: Ser, Pronouns, Gender, and Questions

Purpose: state identity, origin, language, nationality, and role, and ask basic questions about someone else.

2.1 Ser in the present tense: eu sou, você é, ele/ela é

Language Focus

In Chapter 1, eu sou was treated as a fixed formula. Now ser begins to expand: it changes its ending to match the subject pronoun. Ser identifies who or what someone is — a name, a nationality, a language spoken, a role, or an origin. You will meet the rest of the ser paradigm, and its contrast with estar, gradually across this volume.

PronounSerExample
eusouEu sou estudante.
vocêéVocê é brasileiro?
eleéEle é professor.
elaéEla é de Lisboa.
  • Eu sou estudante. I am a student.
  • Ele é americano. He is American.

2.2 Ser de: origin with de

Language Focus

Ser de states where someone is from. The preposition de ("from, of") attaches directly to a place name. You will meet de again as a fuller preposition system, including its contractions with articles, later in this volume.

  • De onde você é? — Eu sou do Brasil. Where are you from? — I am from Brazil. de + o → do (formulaic here; full contraction system develops later)
  • Ela é de Portugal. She is from Portugal.

2.3 Gender and articles with new nouns

Language Focus

Every Portuguese noun has a grammatical gender, masculine or feminine. This is a property of the word, not of biological sex, though many words for people do align with the gender of the person described. The definite articles o (masculine) and a (feminine) and the indefinite articles um and uma must always match the noun's gender.

Some nouns, like estudante, keep the same ending for both genders; only the article changes. Other nouns, like professor/professora, change their ending as well.

  • o estudante / a estudante the student (m.) / the student (f.)
  • um professor / uma professora a teacher (m.) / a teacher (f.)

2.4 Basic question words: quem, o que, de onde

Building Block — Asking about someone
Quem é você? / O que você é? / De onde você é?
Brazilian Portuguese usually keeps the same word order as a statement, placing the question word first and relying on the question mark or rising intonation, rather than inverting subject and verb.
De onde você é? Eu sou do Brasil, e você?
Where are you from? I'm from Brazil, and you?

You will meet quem, o que, and other question words again in Chapter 4, where the full question-word inventory develops further.

Model Dialogue: De onde você é?

Context: On the same first evening of class, the teacher asks the students to introduce themselves a little more.

Professora: Marcos, de onde você é? Marcos, where are you from?
Marcos: Eu sou dos Estados Unidos. Eu sou americano. I'm from the United States. I'm American.
Professora: E o que você é? Estudante? And what do you do? A student?
Marcos: Sim, eu sou estudante. Yes, I'm a student.

The teacher turns to Camila, who is sitting nearby.

Professora: Camila, você é brasileira? Camila, are you Brazilian?
Camila: Sou, sim! Eu sou do Brasil. Eu sou professora. Yes, I am! I'm from Brazil. I'm a teacher.
Marcos: Ah, você também é professora! Prazer. Ah, you're a teacher too! Nice to meet you.

Notice that Camila answers sou, sim rather than a single word for "yes" alone — repeating the verb is a very common way to answer a yes/no question in Brazilian Portuguese.

Listening Practice

If no audio recording is available, ask a partner, instructor, or text-to-speech tool to read the lines below aloud, or read them aloud to yourself slowly.

Listening script
Oi! Eu sou Rafael. Eu sou do Brasil. Eu sou estudante, e você?
Hi! I'm Rafael. I'm from Brazil. I'm a student, and you?
  1. Listen to (or read aloud) the script above, then answer, in Portuguese, giving your own origin and role.
  2. Listen for the word that tells you Rafael's country. What form does de take before it?
  3. Listen for the nasal diphthong in também if your partner adds it to the exchange.

Speaking Practice

Prática controlada — Controlled practice: complete the sentence

Say each sentence aloud, filling the blank with your own information: Eu sou ______. (name) Eu sou de ______. (city or country) Eu sou ______. (role, such as estudante).

Tarefa comunicativa — Communicative task: interview a classmate

With a partner, ask and answer de onde você é? and o que você é?. Report one fact about your partner to the class using ele é... or ela é....

Prática individual — Solo alternative

Record yourself stating your name, origin, and role in three short sentences. Listen back and check the ending of ser against the pronoun you used.

Reading Practice

Um perfil curto — A short profile

Oi! Eu sou Sônia. Eu sou brasileira, de São Paulo. Eu sou professora. Eu falo português e inglês.

Tarefa de leitura — Reading task:

  1. What is Sônia's nationality, and which city is she from?
  2. What is Sônia's role?
  3. Find the two language names in the passage. Why are they written with a lowercase first letter in Portuguese?

Writing Workshop

Complete the following linked writing tasks in order.

Tarefa 1 — Article practice: Write the correct definite article (o or a) before each noun: ___ estudante, ___ professor, ___ professora, ___ amigo, ___ amiga.

Tarefa 2 — Complete the exchange: Fill in your own information in the blanks.

Tarefa 3 — Short written profile: Write four short sentences about yourself, giving your name, origin, nationality, and role, using only the vocabulary and patterns from this chapter.

  1. Complete the article practice first. This checks your control of noun gender before you write full sentences.
  2. Fill in the blank exchange. Check that each answer uses a full form of ser, not just the missing word alone.
  3. Draft your four-sentence profile. Keep every word within this chapter's vocabulary plus Chapter 1.
  4. Read your profile aloud once. Listen for the circumflex in você and the nasal diphthong in também, if used.
  5. Check every article against the vocabulary tables. Correct any noun whose article does not match its gender.

Lista de revisão — Editing checklist:

  • Every noun has the correct article for its gender.
  • ser is conjugated correctly for the subject pronoun used (sou vs. é).
  • de is used correctly to state origin.
  • Language names and nationality adjectives are lowercase.
  • All diacritics from Chapter 1 vocabulary are still preserved.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Nationality, ethnicity, and residence

Nationality, ethnicity, place of residence, and language spoken are related but distinct ideas. A person may hold Brazilian nationality, identify with a particular ethnicity, live outside Brazil, and speak more than one language fluently. When learners describe themselves or others in Portuguese, they should keep these categories separate rather than assuming that one predicts the others.

Regional Variation — Naming languages and nationalities

Brazilian Portuguese and European Portuguese both lowercase language names and nationality adjectives, unlike English. This convention holds across the Lusophone world. Vocabulary for nationality may vary slightly by country of origin; this course introduces only the labels needed for the chapter's communicative tasks.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Capitalizing languages and nationalities

Error: Writing Português or Brasileiro with a capital letter, following English capitalization habits.

Correction: Portuguese lowercases language names and nationality adjectives: português, brasileiro, unless the word begins a sentence.

Common Mistake — Mismatched articles

Error: Saying a professor or o professora, mismatching the article and the noun ending.

Correction: The article must agree with the noun's own gender: o professor, a professora. Learn each noun together with its article from the start.

Common Mistake — Forgetting to conjugate ser

Error: Using sou for every subject, for example saying ele sou professor.

Correction: Sou is used only with eu. Use é with você, ele, and ela: ele é professor.

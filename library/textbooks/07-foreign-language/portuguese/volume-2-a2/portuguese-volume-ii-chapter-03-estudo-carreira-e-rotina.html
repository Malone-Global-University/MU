Estudo, carreira e rotina

Study, Career, and Routine · Speak and Write Portuguese: Independent Everyday Portuguese

Chapter Overview

Talking about school, work, and the shape of a normal day is one of the most common things adult learners need to do in Portuguese. This chapter builds your ability to describe your studies or job, explain your daily routine in more detail, and talk about the tasks and duties that fill your week. Along the way, you will strengthen the present tense for regular and reflexive verbs, learn to describe habitual actions such as waking up, getting ready, and going to bed using reflexive verbs, and add a gente to your pronoun system alongside nós. You will also begin using modal expressions such as precisar, poder, and ter que to talk about obligations and possibilities at school or work. By the end of the chapter, you will be able to describe a typical day, your responsibilities, and what you need or are able to do.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Describe your studies, job, or field of work in more detail.
  2. Narrate a typical daily routine using reflexive verbs.
  3. Distinguish a gente from nós and use each with correct agreement.
  4. Express obligation, necessity, and ability using modal expressions.
  5. Talk about duties and tasks connected to school or work.
  6. Read and write a short text describing a typical day or a set of responsibilities.

Communication Scenario

The same conversation group is now preparing short presentations about "a normal week" to share with new members. You are asked to describe your studies or job, walk through your typical daily routine, and explain what you need to do and what you are able to do during a busy week.

  1. This chapter develops the present tense, reflexive verbs, a gente/nós, and modal expressions; it recycles Volume I present-tense and routine vocabulary and expands it.
  2. The productive target is one short presentation describing studies or work and a typical daily routine.
  3. You may use a fictional or generalized routine, school, or job at any point.
  4. Only systems marked active, developed, or consolidated through this chapter are required for independent production.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Note — Reflexive pronouns in connected speech

Reflexive pronouns such as me, se, and nos are unstressed and attach rhythmically to the verb that follows them in everyday Brazilian speech. Practice saying eu me levanto, ele se arruma, and a gente se vê as smooth, connected phrases. Also continue watching stress in words such as horário, which carries stress on the antepenultimate syllable, and relatório, which follows the same pattern.

Intelligibility in connected phrases, not isolated word pronunciation, remains the goal of this chapter.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This section develops the present tense of reflexive verbs for daily routine, strengthens the choice between a gente and nós, and introduces modal expressions of necessity, obligation, and ability tied to school and work.

Reflexive Verbs for Daily Routine

  • Eu me levanto às sete horas e me arrumo rápido. I get up at seven o'clock and get ready quickly. me + levanto; me + arrumo — reflexive pronoun before conjugated verb
  • Ela se veste antes de tomar café da manhã. She gets dressed before having breakfast. se + veste — third-person reflexive
  • A gente se deita cedo durante a semana de trabalho. We go to bed early during the work week. a gente + se + deita — third-person-singular verb form with a gente

A gente and nós

  • Nós trabalhamos no mesmo escritório. We work in the same office. nós + first-person-plural verb form
  • A gente trabalha no mesmo escritório. We work in the same office. a gente + third-person-singular verb form; same meaning, more common in everyday speech
Language Note — Choosing between a gente and nós

A gente and nós both mean "we," but they behave differently. A gente is grammatically third-person singular, so the verb takes the same ending as ele or ela, even though the meaning is plural. Nós takes the standard first-person-plural verb ending. In everyday spoken Brazilian Portuguese, a gente is extremely common and generally neutral in register; nós remains fully correct in both speech and writing and is more consistently expected in formal written Portuguese. This chapter treats both as productive; later chapters continue to refine register choices between them.

Modal Expressions: Necessity, Obligation, and Ability

  • Eu preciso terminar o relatório até sexta-feira. I need to finish the report by Friday. precisar + infinitive, necessity
  • A gente tem que ir à reunião às nove horas. We have to go to the meeting at nine o'clock. ter que + infinitive, obligation
  • Você pode me ajudar com essa tarefa? Can you help me with this task? poder + infinitive, ability/permission, formulaic request

Modal expressions in this chapter are practiced in fixed frames (precisar + infinitive, ter que + infinitive, poder + infinitive) tied directly to school and work duties; broader modal nuance and register variation develop later in the volume.

Building Block — Describing a typical day
reflexive routine verb + time expression + study/work activity + modal expression of duty or ability

Eu me levanto às sete horas todos os dias. De manhã, eu tenho aula na universidade, e à tarde eu trabalho em um escritório perto do centro. A gente tem reuniões toda quarta-feira, e eu preciso terminar as tarefas antes do fim do turno. À noite, eu me deito cedo porque preciso acordar bem cedo no dia seguinte.

We have meetings every Wednesday, and I need to finish my tasks before the end of the shift. At night, I go to bed early because I need to wake up very early the next day.
Language Note — What is not assessed in this chapter

This chapter does not assess subjunctive obligation forms, formal clitic placement, or unscheduled Lusophone variation. It develops the present tense of reflexive verbs, the choice between a gente and nós, and the modal expressions precisar, ter que, and poder with the infinitive.

Model Dialogue

Context: Two colleagues talking before a meeting. Informal-neutral register, Brazilian Portuguese.
Renata Oi, Felipe! Você terminou o relatório? Hi, Felipe! Did you finish the report?
Felipe Ainda não. Eu preciso terminar até o fim do dia. E você, como está sua semana? Not yet. I need to finish it by the end of the day. And you, how's your week?
Renata Bem corrida! Eu me levanto às seis, estudo de manhã e trabalho à tarde. Quite busy! I get up at six, study in the morning, and work in the afternoon.
Felipe Nossa! E a gente ainda tem que ir à reunião às nove. Wow! And we still have to go to the meeting at nine.
Renata É verdade. Você pode enviar os slides antes da reunião? That's right. Can you send the slides before the meeting?
Felipe Posso, sim. Eu envio assim que terminar o relatório. Yes, I can. I'll send them as soon as I finish the report.
Renata Perfeito. A gente se vê às nove, então. Perfect. See you at nine, then.
  1. List the tasks and duties Felipe and Renata mention, in order.
  2. Find every reflexive verb used in this dialogue.
  3. Find the sentence that uses a gente and explain what verb form follows it.

Reading Practice

Um dia na vida de uma enfermeira

Camila trabalha como enfermeira em um hospital e tem uma rotina bem organizada. Ela se levanta às cinco da manhã, se arruma rápido e chega ao hospital antes das sete. De manhã, ela cuida dos pacientes e participa de uma reunião com a equipe médica. À tarde, ela precisa organizar os relatórios e preparar os remédios para o turno seguinte. Às vezes, ela também tem que ficar até mais tarde quando há uma emergência. Mesmo com a rotina corrida, Camila gosta do seu trabalho porque pode ajudar as pessoas todos os dias. À noite, ela volta para casa, janta com a família e se deita cedo para descansar antes do próximo turno.

Notice how this passage moves from basic facts, to morning routine, to work duties, to evening routine — the same structure your own presentation should follow.

Tarefa de leitura — Reading task:

  1. What time does Camila get up, and what does she do first?
  2. What duties does she have during the afternoon?
  3. Why does she say she likes her job, despite the busy routine?

Writing Workshop

Write a short text describing your studies or job and your typical daily routine, of six to ten sentences, following the model above.

  1. State what you study or where you work. Mention your field or job in one or two sentences.
  2. Describe your morning routine. Use at least one reflexive verb and a time expression.
  3. Describe your study or work activities. Mention at least one duty or task.
  4. Use a gente or nós at least once to describe something you share with classmates or colleagues.
  5. Add one modal expression (precisar, ter que, or poder) describing an obligation or ability.
  6. Check reflexive pronouns, verb agreement, and diacritics throughout, using the checklist below.

Lista de revisão — Editing checklist:

  • The text describes studies or work, a morning routine, at least one duty, and an evening routine in a connected way.
  • Reflexive verbs such as levantar-se, arrumar-se, and deitar-se are used correctly.
  • A gente and nós take the correct verb agreement wherever they appear.
  • At least one modal expression correctly uses the infinitive.
  • Articles, gender, and adjective agreement are controlled throughout.
  • All diacritics are preserved, including horário, relatório, and reunião.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Titles and workplace norms

Titles and workplace address vary across Lusophone settings. In many Brazilian workplaces, colleagues commonly use first names even with a supervisor, and address shifts toward more formal titles such as senhor or senhora mainly in more traditional companies, with clients, or with people who are notably older or more senior. Academic settings often use titles such as professor or professora with or without the surname. Workplace norms in Portugal, Angola, Mozambique, and other Lusophone contexts differ in their own ways and are not identical to the Brazilian pattern described here.

Regional Variation — A reminder about the declared production model

This chapter continues to use contemporary Brazilian Portuguese as your default production model, including a gente as a common everyday form alongside nós. Workplace and academic vocabulary can differ noticeably across Lusophone regions; labeled European, African, and other Lusophone terms remain recognition-level material until a later chapter schedules them productively. Continue producing your declared variety consistently in this chapter's tasks.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Mismatching a gente with a first-person-plural verb

Error: Saying A gente trabalhamos juntos, using the nós verb ending after a gente.

Correction: A gente takes a third-person-singular verb form: A gente trabalha junto. Use the first-person-plural ending only with nós: Nós trabalhamos juntos.

Common Mistake — Omitting the reflexive pronoun

Error: Saying Eu levanto às sete horas instead of including the reflexive pronoun.

Correction: Portuguese requires the reflexive pronoun with these routine verbs: Eu me levanto às sete horas. Reflexive pronouns are required, not optional, whenever the verb describes an action the subject does to itself.

Common Mistake — Confusing ter que and poder

Error: Saying Eu posso terminar o relatório hoje to describe an obligation rather than an ability.

Correction: Use ter que or precisar for obligation: Eu tenho que terminar o relatório hoje. Reserve poder for ability or permission.

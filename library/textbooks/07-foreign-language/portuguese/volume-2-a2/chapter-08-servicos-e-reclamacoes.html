Serviços e reclamações

Services and Complaints · Speak and Write Portuguese: Independent Everyday Portuguese

Chapter Overview

This chapter moves from the market of Chapter 7 into the world of everyday services: banks, delivery, repairs, and customer service. You will learn to ask for help politely, explain a problem clearly, and make a complaint that is firm without being aggressive. The chapter develops queria and gostaria as softened request forms, indirect object pronouns me and lhe, and the everyday register of service encounters. This work continues to recycle the quantity expressions, imperatives, and costumar constructions from Chapter 7, applying your growing independence to situations where something has gone wrong and needs to be resolved.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Use everyday vocabulary for banks, delivery, repairs, and customer service.
  2. Soften requests using queria and gostaria, such as Eu queria falar com um atendente.
  3. Use indirect object pronouns me and lhe to say who receives an action, such as Pode me explicar o problema?
  4. Explain a problem clearly, giving relevant details in a logical order.
  5. Make a polite but firm complaint using appropriate service register.
  6. Read a simple service notice, receipt, or complaint form and identify its main information.
  7. Write a short message requesting help or reporting a problem.

Communication Scenario

Not everything goes smoothly in daily life. This week, something needs fixing: a delivery arrived late, an appliance stopped working, or a bank charge does not look right. You need to contact a bank, request a repair, or follow up with a delivery service, and you want to sound polite but clear about what you need. This chapter walks you through the everyday language of requesting help, explaining a problem, and making a complaint that gets results.

  1. The productive target is a short service request, a clear explanation of a problem, and a polite complaint.
  2. Only systems marked active, developed, or consolidated through Chapter 8 are required for independent production; the subjunctive and advanced conditional structures are not required here.
  3. You may invent a fictional company, product, or situation for any task in this chapter.
  4. Brazilian Portuguese remains the declared production model; other Lusophone service practices mentioned here are labeled comparison only.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Note — Stress and rhythm in service language

Words like reclamação and cartão carry the nasal diphthong -ão you practiced in Chapter 7, always on the final stressed syllable. Continue to keep the airflow nasal through the entire glide rather than closing into a hard n.

Also notice the stress difference between público (stress on the first syllable) and publicar (stress on the final syllable). In service situations, stress placement can distinguish related words such as conserto (“repair,” noun) and conserto (“I fix,” first-person present) from consertou (“fixed,” past). Read these three forms aloud slowly, paying attention to where the stress falls.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter develops softened requests with queria and gostaria, indirect object pronouns me and lhe, and the register needed for polite but firm service interactions.

Queria and Gostaria for Softened Requests

Queria (from querer) and gostaria (from gostar) both soften a request compared to the plain present tense. They are common formulaic patterns in Brazilian service Portuguese and do not require you to control the full conditional or imperfect system to use them accurately.

  • Eu queria falar com um atendente, por favor. I would like to speak with a representative, please. queria + infinitive, softened request
  • Eu gostaria de resolver um problema com minha conta. I would like to resolve a problem with my account. gostaria + de + infinitive
  • A gente gostaria de saber o prazo para o conserto. We would like to know the deadline for the repair. gostaria + de + infinitive, plural subject

Indirect Objects: Me and Lhe

Me and lhe mark the person who receives an explanation, an answer, a favor, or information. In everyday spoken Brazilian Portuguese, me is extremely common; lhe is more common in written or more formal spoken contexts and is often replaced by para você or a name in casual speech. Both forms are active for recognition and controlled production in this chapter; full clitic placement across registers develops later in the volume.

  • Pode me explicar o que aconteceu? Can you explain to me what happened? me = indirect object, "to me"
  • Eu vou lhe enviar o comprovante por e-mail. I will send you the receipt by email. lhe = indirect object, more formal/written register
  • O atendente me disse que o prazo é de cinco dias. The representative told me the deadline is five days. me = indirect object with dizer
Language Note — Explaining a problem clearly

A clear problem explanation usually states what happened, when it happened, and what you need. You already control the preterite for completed events and prepositions of time; this chapter simply applies them to service situations.

  • Meu pedido chegou atrasado. — My order arrived late.
  • O produto chegou com defeito. — The product arrived defective.
  • Eu paguei duas vezes pelo mesmo produto. — I paid twice for the same product.

Model Dialogue

Context: A phone call between a customer and a bank representative about a charge on an account. Polite-neutral register, Brazilian Portuguese.
Atendente Banco Horizonte, bom dia. Em que posso ajudar? Banco Horizonte, good morning. How can I help?
Cliente Bom dia. Eu queria resolver um problema com a minha conta. Tem uma cobrança que eu não reconheço. Good morning. I'd like to resolve a problem with my account. There's a charge I don't recognize.
Atendente Entendo. Pode me confirmar o valor e a data da cobrança? I understand. Can you confirm the amount and date of the charge for me?
Cliente Foi de duzentos reais, no dia cinco. Eu já entrei em contato uma vez e ninguém me explicou nada. It was two hundred reais, on the fifth. I already contacted you once and no one explained anything to me.
Atendente Sinto muito pelo transtorno. Vou lhe enviar um comprovante por e-mail e resolver isso ainda hoje. I'm sorry for the inconvenience. I'll send you a receipt by email and resolve this today.
  1. What problem does the customer report?
  2. What does the customer say happened the first time they contacted the bank?
  3. Find one example of an indirect object pronoun in the dialogue.

Speaking Practice

Tier 1 — Supported Exchange

With a partner or on your own, role-play a short phone call to a delivery company. Use queria or gostaria to open the request, and explain that your order arrived late.

Tier 2 — Transactional Interaction

Role-play a visit to a repair shop. Explain what is wrong with an appliance (invented is fine), ask for the estimated cost, and ask Pode me dizer quando fica pronto?

Tier 3 — Independent Task

Prepare a one-minute monologue as if leaving a voicemail for a bank. State your name, explain the problem, and politely ask for a callback with a clear deadline.

Writing Workshop

Write a short message (5–7 sentences) to a company reporting a problem and requesting a solution.

  1. Open with a greeting and state your request using queria or gostaria.
  2. Explain the problem clearly: what happened and when.
  3. State what solution you would like.
  4. Ask a follow-up question about the deadline or next steps, using me or lhe.
  5. Close politely.

Revision checklist

  • I used queria or gostaria to soften my opening request.
  • I explained the problem with a clear time reference.
  • I used me or lhe correctly at least once.
  • I preserved all diacritics, including nasal vowels in words like reclamação.
  • My tone is polite but clear about what I need.

Culture and Communication

Culture and Communication — Complaint register across Lusophone contexts

In many Brazilian service settings, complaints are often introduced with a softened request such as eu queria resolver um problema before the specific issue is named directly. This is a matter of everyday register, not a universal script: workplace and institutional norms vary by company, region, and generation.

Formal written complaints in Portuguese-speaking contexts, including European Portuguese institutional settings, often use more elevated vocabulary and more consistent use of lhe-style indirect objects than casual spoken Brazilian Portuguese. This is a labeled comparison only; it is not required production for this chapter.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Confusing queria with quero

Error: Quero falar com um atendente agora! in a neutral first request.
Better: Eu queria falar com um atendente, por favor.

Quero is grammatically correct but can sound abrupt as an opening line. Queria softens the same request without changing its meaning.

Common Mistake — Dropping the indirect object

Error: Pode explicar o problema? when the speaker means "explain it to me."
Better: Pode me explicar o problema?

Without me, the sentence asks whether the problem can be explained in general, not specifically to the speaker. The indirect object clarifies who receives the explanation.

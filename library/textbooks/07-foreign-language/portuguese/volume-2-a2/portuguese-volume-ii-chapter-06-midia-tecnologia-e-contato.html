Mídia, tecnologia e contato

Media, Technology, and Contact · Speak and Write Portuguese: Independent Everyday Portuguese

Chapter Overview

This chapter opens Unit II and moves your everyday Portuguese onto the phone and the screen. You will learn to make a phone call, leave and understand a short voicemail, exchange text messages, and read simple public and media texts such as a website notice or a short online post. The chapter also introduces verb-preposition frames for communication verbs — ligar para, mandar mensagem para, escrever para — and develops your sense of register: the same request sounds different in a casual text to a friend than in a message to an institution. Everything here builds directly on the housing, routine, and well-being language you consolidated in Chapter 5.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Use everyday vocabulary for phones, texting, email, and social media.
  2. Make and respond to a simple phone call, including asking someone to repeat or speak more slowly.
  3. Use communication verbs with their correct prepositions, such as ligar para alguém and mandar mensagem para alguém.
  4. Refer to people and things using nouns and strong pronouns in messages, without relying on formal clitic placement.
  5. Distinguish an informal text message from a more formal or institutional message.
  6. Read a short public notice, website snippet, or social-media post and identify its main information.
  7. Write a short informal text-message exchange and a short, more neutral message to a service or institution.

Communication Scenario

You are settling into your new routine from Chapter 5, and now you need to stay in touch with people by phone and by text — a friend confirming plans, a landlord about the apartment, a classmate about a group project. You also want to follow a few Portuguese-language accounts and websites to practice reading. This chapter walks you through the everyday language of staying connected, on and off the phone.

  1. The productive target is a short phone exchange, a short text-message exchange, and a brief written message appropriate to its audience.
  2. Only systems marked active, developed, or consolidated through Chapter 6 are required for independent production; formal email conventions are previewed here only as recognition and return productively in a later chapter.
  3. You may invent a fictional contact, landlord, or classmate for any task in this chapter.
  4. Brazilian Portuguese remains the declared production model; any European Portuguese texting habits mentioned here are labeled comparison only.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Note — Open and closed mid vowels on the phone

Phone conversations move fast, and small vowel differences carry real meaning. Brazilian Portuguese distinguishes an open mid vowel, as in the stressed é of é (“is”), from a closed mid vowel, as in e (“and”) when unstressed, and in words such as ele where the final e is closed and often raised toward an i sound in casual speech. Listen for this contrast in the model dialogue: é (open, “is/it's”) versus the everyday connector e (closed, “and”).

Also continue practicing careful, matter-of-fact intonation for polite requests such as Pode falar mais devagar, por favor?, and the antepenultimate stress pattern in aplicativo.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter develops verb-preposition frames for communication verbs and continues to build audience-sensitive language: choosing words and structures that fit who you are talking to.

Verb-Preposition Frames for Communication

Several common communication verbs require a specific preposition before the person you are contacting. Learning the verb and its preposition together, as one unit, avoids a common English-influenced error.

  • Eu ligo para o meu colega todos os dias. I call my colleague every day. ligar + para + pessoa
  • Ela manda mensagem para a família todo domingo. She sends a message to her family every Sunday. mandar mensagem + para + pessoa
  • Eu escrevo para o proprietário sobre o aluguel. I'm writing to the landlord about the rent. escrever + para + pessoa

Notice that atender (“to answer”), postar (“to post”), curtir (“to like”), and compartilhar (“to share”) take a direct object with no preposition: Eu atendi o telefone, not a form with para.

Referring to People and Things Without Clitics

Formal object clitics such as o, a, and lhe are not yet active in this volume. For now, continue referring to people and things with full nouns or strong pronouns, exactly as spoken Brazilian Portuguese normally does.

  • Eu vou ligar para ela mais tarde. I'm going to call her later. strong pronoun after preposition, not a clitic
  • Manda uma mensagem para mim quando chegar. Send me a message when you arrive. strong pronoun mim after para
Language Note — Register-sensitive grammar in messages

The same request changes shape depending on the audience. To a friend, a short imperative such as Me liga! (“Call me!”) is completely natural. To someone you don't know well, or to an institution, the softer Você poderia me ligar, por favor? (“Could you call me, please?”) fits better. This chapter introduces that audience-sensitive choice; a full formal-register system, including formal email openings and closings, is developed later in the volume.

ligar / mandar mensagem / escrever + para + pessoa

Model Dialogue

Context: A short phone call between two friends confirming weekend plans. Informal register, Brazilian Portuguese.
Paula Alô? Oi, Rafael, tudo bem? Hello? Hi, Rafael, how's it going?
Rafael Oi, Paula! Tudo bem, e você? Desculpa, o sinal aqui não é muito bom. Hi, Paula! Good, and you? Sorry, the signal here isn't very good.
Paula Sem problema. Pode falar mais devagar? Eu estou ligando para confirmar o sábado. No problem. Can you speak more slowly? I'm calling to confirm Saturday.
Rafael Claro! Eu te mando uma mensagem com o endereço, porque a ligação está caindo. Sure! I'll send you a message with the address, because the call keeps dropping.
Paula Perfeito. Eu respondo assim que ver a mensagem. Até sábado! Perfect. I'll reply as soon as I see the message. See you Saturday!
  1. What problem interrupts the call?
  2. What does Rafael decide to do instead of continuing the call?
  3. Find one example of a communication verb with para.

Listening script — Voicemail message
Oi, é a Camila. Eu liguei porque preciso falar sobre o projeto da faculdade. Eu vou mandar uma mensagem com os detalhes, mas se você puder, me liga hoje à noite. Meu celular está com pouca bateria, então, se não atender, manda uma mensagem, tá bom? Obrigada, tchau!

Writing Workshop

Write two short pieces: (1) a text-message exchange of four to six lines with a friend, and (2) a short, more neutral message of three to five sentences to a landlord, neighbor, or classmate you don't know well, using at least one communication verb with its correct preposition in each.

  1. Plan your text-message exchange. Decide who you are messaging and why — confirming plans, asking a quick question, or sharing news.
  2. Write the exchange informally, the way friends actually text: short lines, contractions such as tá, and direct requests are appropriate here.
  3. Plan your neutral message. Decide who you are contacting and what you need from them.
  4. Write the neutral message with softer requests, such as Você poderia...?, and at least one communication verb with its preposition.
  5. Check your prepositions after ligar, mandar mensagem, and escrever.
  6. Check grammar and diacritics using the checklist below.

Lista de revisão — Editing checklist:

  • The informal exchange sounds like real texting between friends, not a formal letter.
  • The neutral message uses softer, audience-appropriate requests.
  • Ligar para, mandar mensagem para, and escrever para are used with the correct preposition.
  • People and things are referred to with nouns or strong pronouns, not with formal clitics.
  • Articles, gender, and agreement are controlled throughout.
  • All diacritics are preserved, including está, até, and é.
  • No new, unscheduled grammar or vocabulary from outside this chapter has been introduced.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Digital register differs from formal writing

In many Brazilian contexts, everyday texting is fast, informal, and full of abbreviations such as vc for você or tb for também, even among people who write quite formally in other settings. A message to an institution, a landlord you don't know, or a professor, however, is usually written out more fully and politely, closer to standard spelling. Recognizing this difference — rather than assuming one style fits every audience — is itself part of communicative competence.

Regional Variation — Texting habits and the declared production model

This chapter continues to use contemporary Brazilian Portuguese as your default production model. In some European Portuguese contexts, texting habits and abbreviations differ, and the pronoun tu appears far more often in informal messages than in most Brazilian texting. These are receptive comparison points only; you are not asked to produce them in this volume.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Dropping the preposition after a communication verb

Error: Eu liguei ela ontem.

Correction: Ligar requires para before the person: Eu liguei para ela ontem. The same applies to mandar mensagem para and escrever para.

Common Mistake — Using the same register for every audience

Error: Writing to a landlord exactly the way you would text a close friend, with the same abbreviations and blunt imperatives.

Correction: Reserve short imperatives like Me liga! for close, informal relationships, and use a softer structure such as Você poderia me ligar, por favor? for people you don't know well or institutional contacts.

Common Mistake — Reaching for formal clitics too early

Error: Attempting a formal object clitic such as lhe to sound more advanced in a text message.

Correction: Continue using full nouns and strong pronouns, as in Eu mandei uma mensagem para ele, not an unscheduled clitic construction. Formal clitics are introduced later in this volume.

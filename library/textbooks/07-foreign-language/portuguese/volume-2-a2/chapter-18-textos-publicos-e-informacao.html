Textos públicos e informação

Unit IV, Chapter 3 · Speak and Write Portuguese: Independent Everyday Portuguese

Chapter Overview

Chapter 18 turns your attention from personal conversation to the kind of Portuguese you meet on signs, websites, and official notices. You will read short public texts — opening-hours notices, building rules, informational web excerpts — and learn to summarize what they say for someone else. Along the way you will begin recognizing, at the level of comprehension only, the impersonal and passive-like se construction that is extremely common in this kind of writing (aluga-se, não se aceita, fala-se português), along with a set of formal nouns built from familiar verbs (funcionamento, atendimento, encerramento). None of this becomes a new productive grammar target; the goal is confident reading and clear, everyday summarizing.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Read and interpret short public notices, signs, and website excerpts written in an everyday formal register.
  2. Recognize the impersonal and passive-like se construction in public texts without being required to produce it independently.
  3. Recognize common formal nouns built from familiar verbs (such as funcionamento from funcionar) and connect them to meanings you already know.
  4. Summarize the key information in a public text using your own, everyday Portuguese.
  5. Ask for clarification about a rule, deadline, or instruction found in a public text.

Communication Scenario

You come across a notice, sign, or short website page — at a building, a service office, a store, or online — and a friend or family member asks you what it says. You read it, then explain the key information in plain, everyday language: what is allowed, what is required, what the hours are, and what to do next.

  1. The productive target is a clear, spoken or written summary of a public text using vocabulary and grammar already active from earlier chapters.
  2. Impersonal/passive-like se forms may be read and understood, but your own summary may restate the same information using ordinary subject-verb sentences you already control.
  3. You may invent the notice or describe a real one you have seen.
  4. Brazilian Portuguese remains the declared production model for all required production in this chapter.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Reading formal nouns aloud

Formal nouns ending in -mento and -ção carry stress on the syllable just before that ending: funcioNAMento, atendiMENto, informaÇÃO. Practice reading this sign aloud, paying attention to stress and to the nasal -ção ending: Horário de funcionamento: atendimento das nove às dezoito horas. Aos domingos, não há atendimento.

Hours of operation: service from nine to six. On Sundays, there is no service.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter develops reading strategies for public texts: recognizing the impersonal/passive-like se construction and formal nouns built from familiar verbs. Both are recognition targets in this chapter, not new productive systems; your own speaking and writing continue to rely on the subject-verb patterns already active from earlier chapters.

Recognizing Impersonal and Passive-Like Se

Public Portuguese frequently drops a named subject and uses se plus a verb instead. Two common patterns appear on signs and notices:

  • Aluga-se. For rent. (literally: "it rents itself") passive-like se — no named subject; equivalent meaning to "isto é alugado"
  • Fala-se português aqui. Portuguese is spoken here. passive-like se + object noun (português)
  • Não se aceita cartão. Cards are not accepted. / We don't accept cards. impersonal se — no specific person named as the one who doesn't accept
  • Precisa-se de atendente. Attendant needed / wanted. impersonal se — common in help-wanted notices

You are not asked to produce sentences with se in this way. When you summarize a notice like these, you may simply restate the meaning with a sentence you already control, such as Eles não aceitam cartão or Aqui as pessoas falam português.

Formal Nouns from Familiar Verbs

Public and institutional Portuguese often uses a noun built from a verb you already know, instead of the verb itself. Recognizing the connection makes these texts far easier to read.

  • funcionar → o funcionamento to function/operate → the operation, functioning
  • atender → o atendimento to assist/serve → the service, assistance
  • encerrar → o encerramento to close/end → the closing
  • informar → a informação to inform → the information

Treat this as a reading strategy: when you meet an unfamiliar formal noun, look for a familiar verb hiding inside it.

Model Dialogue

Context: Two friends look at a notice posted on a building's front door. Everyday spoken register, Brazilian Portuguese.
Paulo O que diz esse aviso na porta? What does that notice on the door say?
Renata Diz o horário de funcionamento: atendimento das nove às dezessete horas, de segunda a sexta. Diz também "não se aceita entrega depois das dezesseis horas". It says the hours of operation: service from nine to five, Monday to Friday. It also says "deliveries are not accepted after four p.m."
Paulo Então eles não recebem depois das quatro. E tem mais alguma coisa? So they don't accept deliveries after four. Is there anything else?
Renata Tem: "é obrigatório o uso de máscara na recepção" e o prazo para retirar encomendas é de cinco dias. There is: "mask use is required at the reception desk" and the deadline to pick up packages is five days.
Paulo Entendi, obrigado. Vou chegar antes das quatro, então. Got it, thanks. I'll arrive before four, then.
  1. What are the hours of service according to the notice?
  2. What rule does the notice give about deliveries?
  3. Find one impersonal/passive-like se phrase in the dialogue and restate it in your own words.

Listening Practice

If no audio recording is available, ask a partner, instructor, or text-to-speech tool to read the script below aloud, or read it aloud to yourself slowly.

Listening script — An announcement at a public office
Atenção: o horário de atendimento hoje será das nove às treze horas, por causa de um treinamento interno. Não se atende ao público depois desse horário. O prazo para entregar documentos permanece o mesmo: cinco dias úteis. É necessário apresentar um documento com foto na recepção.
Attention: today's service hours will be from nine to one, due to internal training. The public is not served after that time. The deadline to submit documents remains the same: five business days. It is necessary to present a photo ID at reception.
  1. Why are today's hours different from usual?
  2. What is the deadline mentioned, and does it change?
  3. What must a visitor bring to the reception desk?

Writing Workshop

Write a short structured summary (7–9 sentences) reporting the key information from a real or invented public notice, as if explaining it to someone who has not read it.

  1. Name the place or service the notice is about.
  2. State the hours of operation.
  3. Report one or two rules, restating any se-construction in your own everyday words.
  4. Mention a deadline or requirement if the notice includes one.
  5. Close with a practical recommendation, reusing é importante que or recomendo que from Chapter 17 if useful.

Revision checklist

  • I restated any se-construction using a subject-verb sentence I already control.
  • I used at least one formal noun (such as funcionamento or atendimento) correctly.
  • I stated the hours and any deadline clearly.
  • I did not attempt to produce a new passive or impersonal se sentence of my own.
  • I preserved all diacritics, including in informação, funcionamento, and é.

Culture and Communication

Culture and Communication — Public texts across the Lusophone world

Signs, official notices, and government websites in Brazil, Portugal, and other Lusophone countries share many conventions, including impersonal se constructions and formal nouns of the kind studied here, but the exact vocabulary, formatting, and level of formality can vary by country and by institution. A bank notice in Brazil and a municipal notice in Portugal may both use horário de funcionamento, yet differ in tone, layout, and the specific rules stated.

As in earlier chapters, you may describe a real notice you have encountered or invent one; what matters is reading it accurately and reporting its content clearly.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Trying to produce new se-constructions

Error: attempting to build your own novel passive/impersonal se sentences in speaking or writing this chapter.
Better: read and understand se-constructions when you meet them, but restate the same meaning in your summaries using ordinary sentences such as Eles não aceitam... or As pessoas falam....

This construction is a recognition target in Chapter 18, not an active production system; a fuller treatment of impersonal and passive-like se comes in a later volume.

Common Mistake — Misreading a formal noun as a verb

Error: treating o atendimento as if it meant "he/she attends."
Better: recognize o atendimento as a noun meaning "the service/assistance," derived from the verb atender.

Formal nouns ending in -mento or -ção function as nouns, not conjugated verbs, even though they are built from familiar verb stems.

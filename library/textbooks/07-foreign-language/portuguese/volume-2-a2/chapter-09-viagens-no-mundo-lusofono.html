Viagens no mundo lusófono

Travel in the Lusophone World · Speak and Write Portuguese: Independent Everyday Portuguese

Chapter Overview

This chapter moves from the service encounters of Chapter 8 into the language of travel and logistics. You will learn to plan a trip, compare destinations, and manage practical details such as lodging, transportation, and documents. The chapter reviews the preterite for completed travel events, develops future planning with ir + infinitivo and other future-reference expressions, and introduces travel-specific uses of por and para. Along the way, you will encounter other Lusophone countries as real travel destinations, with variation labeled clearly rather than treated as interchangeable with Brazil. This chapter prepares you for the Chapter 10 unit assessment, which asks you to resolve an integrated service or travel problem.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Use everyday vocabulary for travel, lodging, documents, and transportation.
  2. Review and apply the preterite to narrate completed travel events, such as Eu viajei para Lisboa em março.
  3. Talk about upcoming travel plans using ir + infinitivo and other future-planning expressions.
  4. Use para to express destination and purpose, and por to express route, duration, and means, in travel contexts.
  5. Compare destinations and travel options using comparatives you have already studied.
  6. Read a simple itinerary, ticket, or travel notice and identify key information.
  7. Write a short message describing travel plans or asking about travel logistics.

Communication Scenario

You are planning a trip somewhere in the Lusophone world. Maybe you already went somewhere last year and want to tell a friend about it, or maybe you are comparing two destinations for an upcoming vacation and need to sort out flights, lodging, and documents. This chapter walks you through the everyday language of talking about past trips, making future plans, and handling the practical logistics of travel.

  1. The productive target is a short narration of a past trip, a description of upcoming travel plans, and a comparison between two destinations or options.
  2. Only systems marked active, developed, or consolidated through Chapter 9 are required for independent production; the full por/para contrast and the subjunctive are not required here.
  3. You may invent a fictional trip, itinerary, or travel companion for any task in this chapter.
  4. Brazilian Portuguese remains the declared production model; other Lusophone destinations and practices mentioned here are labeled comparison and cultural content, not required production models.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Note — Nasal vowels in travel vocabulary

Travel vocabulary offers more practice with nasal sounds. Viagem and passagem both end in the nasal diphthong spelled -agem; keep the vowel nasalized through the glide rather than closing into a full m sound. Embarcar begins with a nasal em-, and rodoviária and fronteira both carry a stressed diphthong that should be pronounced as a single smooth glide, not two separate vowel sounds.

Also review the contrast between open and closed vowels in words like avó (“grandmother,” open) and avô (“grandfather,” closed), which you first met earlier in the volume. This same open/closed distinction appears in porto (a generic “port,” closed o) versus the place name Porto, which is pronounced the same way but capitalized as a proper noun.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter reviews the preterite for narrating completed trips, develops future planning with ir + infinitivo, and introduces travel-specific uses of por and para.

Preterite Review: Narrating a Past Trip

You have already developed the preterite to narrate completed events. This chapter recycles that system for travel narration, combining it with prepositions of time and place.

  • Eu viajei para Cabo Verde no ano passado. I traveled to Cape Verde last year. viajar, preterite, 1sg + para + destination
  • Nós ficamos numa pousada por dez dias. We stayed in a guesthouse for ten days. ficar, preterite, 1pl + por + duration
  • Eles perderam o voo e tiveram que remarcar a passagem. They missed the flight and had to rebook the ticket. perder, ter, preterite, 3pl

Future Planning with Ir + Infinitivo

Ir + infinitivo remains the primary way to talk about future plans in everyday Brazilian Portuguese. This chapter applies it specifically to travel planning, alongside expressions such as pretender and pensar em for less certain plans.

  • Eu vou viajar para Moçambique em setembro. I am going to travel to Mozambique in September. ir (present) + infinitive
  • A gente vai reservar o hotel esta semana. We're going to book the hotel this week. ir (present, a gente) + infinitive
  • Eu penso em visitar Timor-Leste um dia. I'm thinking about visiting Timor-Leste someday. pensar em + infinitive, less certain plan
Language Note — Por and para in travel contexts

Para and por are not interchangeable, and this chapter introduces only the travel meanings you need right now. A full contrast between the two words develops later in the volume.

  • para + destination: O voo é para Luanda. — The flight is to Luanda.
  • para + purpose: Preciso do passaporte para viajar. — I need my passport to travel.
  • por + route/path: Nós vamos passar por Lisboa antes de chegar ao Porto. — We're going to pass through Lisbon before arriving in Porto.
  • por + duration: Vamos ficar por duas semanas. — We're going to stay for two weeks.

Do not treat these as a single English equivalent. Para generally points toward a destination or goal; por generally describes a route, a duration, or movement through something.

Model Dialogue

Context: Two friends compare travel plans over coffee. Everyday register, Brazilian Portuguese.
Bruno Você já decidiu para onde vai viajar nas férias? Have you already decided where you're going to travel on vacation?
Marisa Eu penso em ir para Cabo Verde, mas ainda não reservei nada. E você? I'm thinking about going to Cape Verde, but I haven't booked anything yet. And you?
Bruno Eu já comprei a passagem. Vou viajar para Lisboa em julho e depois vou passar por Coimbra. I already bought the ticket. I'm going to travel to Lisbon in July and then pass through Coimbra afterward.
Marisa Que legal! Quanto tempo você vai ficar? Nice! How long are you going to stay?
Bruno Vou ficar por duas semanas. No ano passado eu só fiquei por cinco dias, e não foi suficiente. I'm going to stay for two weeks. Last year I only stayed for five days, and it wasn't enough.
  1. Where is Marisa thinking about traveling, and has she booked anything?
  2. Where is Bruno going to travel, and what city will he pass through?
  3. Find one example of por used for duration in the dialogue.

Listening script — A traveler describes an upcoming trip
No mês que vem, eu vou viajar para Angola. Vou ficar em Luanda por uma semana e depois vou passar por outra cidade antes de voltar. Já comprei a passagem, mas ainda preciso reservar a hospedagem. No ano passado eu viajei para Portugal e fiquei por duas semanas; foi uma viagem incrível.

  2. What has the speaker already done, and what do they still need to do?
  3. Where did the speaker travel last year, and for how long?

Writing Workshop

Write a short message (5–7 sentences) to a friend describing a past trip and your plans for an upcoming one.

  1. Open with a greeting and mention a trip you took in the past, using the preterite.
  2. Say where you went, how long you stayed, and one detail about the trip.
  3. Describe an upcoming trip using ir + infinitivo.
  4. Include at least one sentence using por or para correctly.
  5. Close politely, asking your friend about their own travel plans.

Revision checklist

  • I used the preterite correctly to narrate the past trip.
  • I used ir + infinitivo to describe the upcoming trip.
  • I used por or para correctly at least once.
  • I preserved all diacritics, including nasal vowels in words like viagem and passagem.
  • My message flows naturally between past narration and future plans.

Culture and Communication

Culture and Communication — Travel across the Lusophone world

Portuguese is spoken across a wide range of travel destinations, including Brazil, Portugal, Angola, Mozambique, Cape Verde, Guinea-Bissau, São Tomé and Príncipe, and Timor-Leste, as well as communities in Macau and Goa. These places differ enormously in geography, culture, cost of travel, entry requirements, and everyday Portuguese usage. Do not treat them as interchangeable options that differ only in name.

Travel logistics vocabulary such as passagem, hospedagem, and roteiro is understood across Brazilian and European Portuguese, though some everyday words for transportation, such as ônibus in Brazil and autocarro in Portugal, differ by region. This chapter uses the Brazilian terms as the default production model and labels European alternatives as receptive comparison only.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Confusing por and para with destination

Error: O voo é por Lisboa. when the flight's destination is Lisbon.
Better: O voo é para Lisboa.

Use para when naming the destination itself. Por is reserved for a place you pass through, a duration, or a route, not the final destination.

Common Mistake — Mixing preterite and future forms

Error: No ano passado, eu vou viajar para Portugal.
Better: No ano passado, eu viajei para Portugal.

A completed past trip requires the preterite, not ir + infinitivo. Reserve ir + infinitivo for plans that have not happened yet, and check that your time expression, such as no ano passado or no próximo ano, matches the verb form you chose.

Comida, mercado e hábitos

Food, Market, and Habits · Speak and Write Portuguese: Independent Everyday Portuguese

Chapter Overview

This chapter takes your independent everyday Portuguese to the market and the kitchen table. You will learn to shop for food, ask about quantities, follow and give simple recipe instructions, and talk about your eating and shopping habits. The chapter develops quantity expressions and partitives (um quilo de, um pouco de, alguns/algumas), the imperative for instructions and requests, and costumar for describing habitual actions. This work continues to recycle the register awareness and communication verbs you developed in Chapter 6, applying them to a new domain: food, markets, and daily routines around eating.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Use everyday vocabulary for markets, food, meals, and shopping.
  2. Express quantities and partitives, such as um quilo de arroz and um pouco de sal.
  3. Give simple instructions and requests using the imperative, such as Corte a cebola and Me dá meio quilo de tomate, por favor.
  4. Describe habitual actions using costumar, such as Eu costumo fazer compras aos sábados.
  5. Ask about and state prices, availability, and freshness at a market or store.
  6. Read a simple recipe or market flyer and identify its main information.
  7. Write a short shopping list and a simple set of recipe instructions.

Communication Scenario

You have settled into your routine and your new way of staying connected from Chapter 6, and now you want to cook more at home and shop like a local. This week you are going to a neighborhood market, texting a friend about a recipe you both like, and thinking about your own food habits — what you usually buy, how often you cook, and what you prefer to eat. This chapter walks you through the everyday language of shopping, cooking, and talking about habits.

  1. The productive target is a short market exchange, a simple set of recipe instructions, and a few sentences describing your own food habits.
  2. Only systems marked active, developed, or consolidated through Chapter 7 are required for independent production; the subjunctive and advanced conditional structures are not required here.
  3. You may invent a fictional market, vendor, or recipe for any task in this chapter.
  4. Brazilian Portuguese remains the declared production model; other Lusophone food practices mentioned here are labeled comparison only.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Note — Nasal diphthong -ão at the market

Several high-frequency food words end in the nasal diphthong -ão, including feijão, pão, and the plural marker in words like limões. Keep the airflow moving through the nose for the entire glide, without a hard final consonant. Practice the contrast between pão (“bread”) and pau (“stick”): the nasalization is the only thing that separates them.

Also continue practicing open versus closed mid vowels from Chapter 6, now in words such as pesar (closed e) and the everyday market phrase é fresco? (open é, “is it fresh?”).

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter develops quantity expressions and partitives, the imperative for instructions and requests, and costumar for describing habitual actions.

Quantity Expressions and Partitives

To ask for a specific amount of something, use a quantity expression followed by de and the item. Note that de does not contract with the noun's article in these frames.

  • Eu queria um quilo de arroz e meio quilo de feijão. I'd like a kilo of rice and half a kilo of beans. quantity + de + item
  • Você pode colocar só um pouco de sal na receita. You can put just a little salt in the recipe. um pouco de + item (uncountable)
  • Tem alguns tomates bem maduros hoje. There are some very ripe tomatoes today. alguns + item (countable, masculine plural)

The Imperative for Instructions and Requests

The imperative is essential for recipes and for polite requests at a market stall. In everyday spoken Brazilian Portuguese, the você-based affirmative imperative generally uses the same form as the present-tense ele/ela/você conjugation.

  • Corte a cebola em pedaços pequenos. Cut the onion into small pieces. imperative, recipe instruction
  • Misture o arroz com o tempero. Mix the rice with the seasoning. imperative, recipe instruction
  • Me dá meio quilo de tomate, por favor. Give me half a kilo of tomatoes, please. imperative, market request, softened by por favor
Language Note — Costumar for habitual actions

Costumar followed by an infinitive expresses what someone usually or typically does. It works alongside the present tense you already control, adding a clear habitual meaning without changing the verb that follows.

  • Eu costumo fazer compras aos sábados. — I usually go grocery shopping on Saturdays.
  • A gente costuma comer peixe às sextas. — We usually eat fish on Fridays.
Building Block — Quantity plus de plus item, and costumar plus infinitive
quantidade + de + item · costumar + infinitivo
de does not contract with the article after a quantity expression; costumar is followed directly by the infinitive, with no preposition
Eu costumo comprar um quilo de arroz e alguns legumes na feira todo sábado.
I usually buy a kilo of rice and some vegetables at the market every Saturday.

Prática guiada — Guided practice: Complete each sentence with the correct quantity expression or imperative form.

  1. Eu queria ___ (a kilo) de feijão, por favor.
  2. ___ (Cut, imperative) o tomate antes de temperar.
  3. Tem ___ (some, fem. pl.) frutas bem doces hoje?
  4. Nós ___ (costumar) fazer compras juntos aos domingos.

Model Dialogue

Context: A short exchange at a market stall between a customer and a vendor. Informal-neutral register, Brazilian Portuguese.
Vendedor Bom dia! O que você vai levar hoje? Good morning! What will you take today?
Cliente Bom dia! Me dá um quilo de tomate e meio quilo de cebola, por favor. Good morning! Give me a kilo of tomatoes and half a kilo of onions, please.
Vendedor Claro. O tomate está bem fresco hoje. Quer alguns limões também? Sure. The tomatoes are very fresh today. Do you want some lemons too?
Cliente Pode ser, uma dúzia. Eu costumo fazer suco de manhã. Sure, a dozen. I usually make juice in the morning.
Vendedor Boa escolha. São vinte e cinco reais no total. Good choice. That's twenty-five reais total.
  1. What three items does the customer buy?
  2. What habit does the customer mention?
  3. Find one example of a quantity expression with de.

Guided Practice

Exercício A — Write a market request using a quantity expression for each item.

  1. arroz (1 kg)
  2. tomate (meio kg)
  3. limões (uma dúzia)

Exercício B — Turn each verb into an imperative recipe instruction.

  1. cortar / a cebola
  2. misturar / o arroz e o feijão
  3. temperar / o peixe com sal e limão

Exercício C — Answer in a complete sentence, using costumar and vocabulary from this chapter.

  1. Where do you usually buy food?
  2. What do you usually cook during the week?
  3. Do you prefer to shop at a market or a supermarket? Why?

Listening Practice

If no audio recording is available, ask a partner, instructor, or text-to-speech tool to read the script below aloud, or read it aloud to yourself slowly.

Listening script — A quick recipe explained aloud
Olha, é bem simples. Primeiro, corte a cebola e o tomate em pedaços pequenos. Depois, misture tudo com um pouco de sal e um pouquinho de limão. Eu costumo comer isso com arroz e feijão. Não precisa de muita coisa, só uns legumes frescos da feira.

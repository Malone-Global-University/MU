Identidade, trajetória e objetivos

Unit I, Chapter 1 · Speak and Write Portuguese: Connected Discourse and Public Life

Chapter Overview

Welcome to Volume III. This chapter reopens your Brazilian Portuguese production model at a more demanding level: instead of simple biographical facts, you will present a fuller personal or professional trajectory that connects where you started, what you have done, and where you are heading. You will review and sharpen the preterite/imperfect narration, connectors, and discourse-reference systems consolidated in Volume II, and you will begin using them with more independence, more subordination, and more attention to formal address. No unfamiliar grammar architecture is introduced today; the goal is to raise the ceiling on systems you already control.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Present a detailed personal or professional trajectory that connects background, key events, and current goals.
  2. Use preterite and imperfect together with greater independence to narrate a life story rather than isolated events.
  3. Use coordinating and subordinating connectors and relative clauses to link ideas into longer, more cohesive sentences.
  4. Track a person's identity across a paragraph using pronouns, possessives, and demonstratives without ambiguity.
  5. Choose formal or informal address appropriately when introducing yourself in different settings.
  6. Ask for clarification, repetition, confirmation, or repair when a conversation partner's account is unclear.
  7. Maintain consistent Brazilian Portuguese production while labeling any Lusophone variation you introduce.

Communication Scenario

You are meeting someone new in a setting that matters to your future: a job interview, a university orientation, a professional networking event, or a first meeting with a mentor. The other person asks you to introduce yourself properly — not just your name, but your story: where you are from, what you have done, and what you hope to do next. You respond with a connected trajectory, and you close the conversation with a short written follow-up, such as a message or a brief email, restating your goals.

  1. The productive target is a connected spoken trajectory followed by a short written follow-up, entirely in Brazilian Portuguese.
  2. You may invent every detail of your trajectory or adapt it from your own life.
  3. Every tool required for this chapter was already active by the end of Volume II; this chapter raises the level at which you use it.
  4. Any Lusophone variation you choose to mention must remain clearly labeled and must not be produced as an unmarked mix with Brazilian Portuguese.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Re-establishing the Brazilian production model for longer speech

Volume III asks you to speak in longer, connected turns, so this chapter reconfirms your Brazilian Portuguese pronunciation model rather than introducing anything new. Pay attention to stress placement in words newly active this chapter, such as trajetória, formação, and experiência, and keep the nasal vowel in formação and decisão-type words fully nasalized rather than reduced to an English-style final consonant. As your sentences grow longer, keep unstressed vowels light and let sentence stress fall naturally on the word that carries new information, not on every syllable equally.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter introduces no new productive paradigm. It raises your independent control of preterite/imperfect narration, connectors, subordination, relative clauses, and pronoun/possessive reference tracking — all consolidated in Volume II — so that you can sustain a longer connected account.

Narrating a Trajectory: Preterite and Imperfect Together

  • Eu nasci em uma cidade pequena, onde meus pais trabalhavam na área da educação. I was born in a small city, where my parents worked in education. preterite (nasci) + imperfect (trabalhavam); relative onde
  • Quando eu tinha dezoito anos, decidi me mudar para estudar administração. When I was eighteen, I decided to move to study business administration. imperfect background + preterite turning point

Connecting Ideas: Subordination and Relative Clauses

  • O curso que eu escolhi mudou completamente a minha trajetória. The course that I chose completely changed my trajectory. relative que; discourse reference to "curso"
  • Embora eu não tivesse certeza no começo, percebi que aquela era a área certa para mim. Although I wasn't sure at first, I realized that was the right field for me. subordination (embora + imperfect subjunctive, recognition-level here); que-clause

The embora example is presented for recognition and natural input; independent production of the subjunctive after embora is not assessed until this system is formally activated later in the volume.

Tracking Identity Across a Paragraph

  • Meu mentor, que trabalha na mesma empresa há vinte anos, sempre me disse que eu deveria confiar no meu próprio percurso. My mentor, who has worked at the same company for twenty years, always told me I should trust my own path. possessive "meu"; relative "que"; discourse-reference chain
Building Block — The trajectory frame
Eu nasci/cresci em... Quando eu [imperfeito], eu [pretérito]. O que mudou tudo foi... Hoje, o meu objetivo é...
a scaffold combining background, turning point, and present goal without any new grammar
Eu cresci em Recife, onde meus pais tinham uma pequena loja. Quando eu tinha vinte anos, comecei a trabalhar com eles e percebi que gostava de administrar negócios. O que mudou tudo foi um curso de gestão que fiz na faculdade. Hoje, o meu objetivo é abrir a minha própria empresa.
I grew up in Recife, where my parents had a small shop. When I was twenty, I started working with them and realized I liked managing businesses. What changed everything was a management course I took in college. Today, my goal is to open my own company.

Prática guiada — Guided practice: Complete each sentence using a system already active from Volume II.

  1. Quando eu ___ (imperfeito de "ser") criança, eu ___ (pretérito de "morar") em outra cidade.
  2. A pessoa ___ (relativo) me ajudou a escolher a faculdade foi minha tia.
  3. Meu primeiro emprego ___ (pretérito de "ser") difícil, mas eu ___ (pretérito de "aprender") muito.

Model Dialogue

Context: A candidate introduces her trajectory at a job interview. Semi-formal spoken register, Brazilian Portuguese.
Recrutador Pode me contar um pouco sobre a sua trajetória até aqui? Could you tell me a bit about your trajectory up to this point?
Fernanda Claro. Eu me formei em administração há cinco anos e comecei a trabalhar em uma empresa pequena, onde aprendi muito sobre gestão de projetos. Depois de dois anos, decidi fazer uma pós-graduação para me especializar. Of course. I graduated in business administration five years ago and started working at a small company, where I learned a lot about project management. After two years, I decided to do a graduate program to specialize.
Recrutador Desculpe, você pode repetir o nome da especialização? Sorry, could you repeat the name of the specialization?
Fernanda Claro, foi uma pós-graduação em gestão de projetos. Foi um desafio grande, porque eu trabalhava e estudava ao mesmo tempo, mas valeu muito a pena. Of course, it was a graduate program in project management. It was a big challenge, because I was working and studying at the same time, but it was very much worth it.
Recrutador E qual é o seu objetivo agora? And what is your goal now?
Fernanda Meu objetivo é assumir mais responsabilidade em projetos internacionais, e é por isso que essa vaga me interessa tanto. My goal is to take on more responsibility in international projects, and that's why this position interests me so much.
  1. What did Fernanda do after two years at her first job?
  2. What clarification does the recruiter ask for, and how does Fernanda respond?
  3. What is Fernanda's stated goal, and how does she connect it to the interview?

Listening Practice

Listening script — A mentor's trajectory
Eu cresci em uma família de professores, então sempre gostei de estudar. Quando eu tinha dezessete anos, entrei na universidade para estudar engenharia, mas, no segundo ano, percebi que a área não era bem o que eu queria. Decidi mudar de curso, o que foi um grande desafio para a minha família no começo. Hoje, depois de quinze anos trabalhando com tecnologia, meu objetivo é ajudar jovens profissionais a encontrar o próprio caminho, assim como um mentor me ajudou há muitos anos.
I grew up in a family of teachers, so I always liked studying. When I was seventeen, I entered university to study engineering, but, in the second year, I realized the field wasn't quite what I wanted. I decided to change majors, which was a big challenge for my family at first. Today, after fifteen years working in technology, my goal is to help young professionals find their own path, just as a mentor helped me many years ago.
  1. What field did the speaker originally study, and what did she realize in the second year?
  2. What decision followed, and how did the family react?
  3. What is the speaker's current goal, and how does she connect it to her own past?

Writing Workshop

Write a short follow-up message (8–10 sentences) to a recruiter, mentor, or program coordinator, restating your trajectory and your goal after your spoken introduction.

  1. Open with a polite, appropriately formal or semi-formal greeting.
  2. Briefly restate your background using the imperfect for context.
  3. Mention one turning point or achievement using the preterite.
  4. Connect your background to your present goal using a relative clause or connector.
  5. Close with an appropriately formal or semi-formal closing line.

Revision checklist

  • I distinguished preterite and imperfect correctly throughout the narration.
  • I used at least one relative clause and one connector to link ideas.
  • I tracked my own identity clearly using pronouns and possessives, without ambiguity.
  • I maintained one consistent register (formal or semi-formal) from greeting to closing.
  • I preserved all diacritics, agreement, and punctuation conventions.

Culture and Communication

Culture and Communication — Presenting a trajectory across the Lusophone world

How people are expected to present their trajectory varies by setting, generation, and country. In many Brazilian professional contexts, a somewhat personal note — mentioning family, a turning point, or a personal motivation — is welcome alongside professional facts, though this varies by industry, region, and individual style. In some European Portuguese-speaking professional contexts, self-presentation may lean more strictly factual in a first formal exchange; this is a tendency, not a rule, and should be labeled as such rather than treated as a fixed contrast. Across Lusophone countries and communities, the balance between personal narrative and formal fact varies further still, and no single pattern should be treated as representative of Brazil, Portugal, Angola, Mozambique, Cape Verde, Guinea-Bissau, São Tomé and Príncipe, Timor-Leste, or diaspora communities as a whole.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Losing preterite/imperfect contrast in a longer trajectory

Error: Quando eu tinha dezoito anos, eu decidia estudar administração. (imperfect used for a one-time decision)
Better: Quando eu tinha dezoito anos, eu decidi estudar administração. (preterite marks the single completed decision; imperfect marks the ongoing age/background)

In a longer trajectory, it is easy to let every verb slide into the imperfect because it feels more descriptive. Reread your narration and ask, for each verb, whether it names a completed turning point or an ongoing background state.

Common Mistake — Redundant pronoun after a relative clause

Error: O professor que me ajudou, ele trabalha na universidade há vinte anos. (unnecessary repeated subject pronoun)
Better: O professor que me ajudou trabalha na universidade há vinte anos.

Once a relative clause identifies the subject, Brazilian Portuguese does not normally repeat it with a redundant pronoun in careful spoken or written narration. Read your sentence back and remove the extra pronoun if the subject is already clear.

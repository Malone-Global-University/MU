Wortbildung und Nominalstil

Word Formation and Nominal Style · Speak and Write German: Independent Communication and Advanced Expression · Volume III, Chapter 18

Chapter Overview and Objectives

Chapter 18 turns from the hypothetical and negotiated language of Chapter 17 to the dense, compact vocabulary that formal and technical German often uses. The compromise that Tobias and his team proposed is now working its way through the company as a written policy, and Tobias finds himself reading and drafting texts full of long compounds and nominalized phrases. This chapter teaches you to take apart unfamiliar compound nouns to find their head and meaning, to recognize how nouns are built from verbs and adjectives through common suffixes, and to read and revise the nominal style found in reports, notices, and academic prose. The goal is not to write in nominal style by default, but to understand it when you meet it and to move between nominal and more direct, verbal phrasing as the audience requires.

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Break down unfamiliar compound nouns into their components, identify the head noun, and determine gender and meaning from it.
  2. Recognize and use common linking elements that join the parts of a compound.
  3. Recognize and produce nominalized forms of verbs and adjectives using suffixes such as -ung, -heit, -keit, and -nis, and understand nominalized infinitives.
  4. Use genitive and prepositional noun phrases to express relationships that a subordinate clause might otherwise express.
  5. Distinguish nominal style from verbal style and revise a dense nominal sentence into clearer, more direct German.
  6. Check unfamiliar or specialized compounds and derivations against attested usage rather than inventing new ones.

Opening Scenario

Frau Ecker has approved the compromise that Tobias's team proposed, and the human resources department sends around an internal memo describing the new arrangement. The memo is written in the dense nominal style typical of German administrative prose: long compound nouns, nominalized verbs, and genitive phrases replace many of the verbs and clauses that a spoken explanation would use. Grammatik

Grammar Focus: Wortbildung

German builds much of its formal and technical vocabulary by combining existing words rather than borrowing new ones. Two processes matter most here: compounding, which you have already met as a reading strategy, and nominalization, which turns verbs and adjectives into nouns.

Grammar Note — Analyzing Compounds

Head and modifier: A German compound noun is read from back to front. The last element, the Grundwort, carries the core meaning, the gender, and the plural form. Everything before it, the Bestimmungswort or Bestimmungswörter, narrows or specifies that meaning.

Linking elements: Many compounds insert a linking element (Fugenelement) between parts, most often -s-, -n-, -es-, or -er-. Linking elements do not change the gender of the compound; they only connect the parts smoothly. Some linking elements come from an old genitive or plural ending, but a modern reader should treat them as connectors, not as active case or number marking.

die Arbeitszeitregelung
Arbeit + s + Zeit + Regelung → [[Arbeit-s-Zeit]-Regelung]
"working-time arrangement" — head: die Regelung (feminine, plural die Regelungen); modifiers: Arbeitszeit ("working time")
der Kundentermin
Kunde + n + Termin
"client appointment" — head: der Termin (masculine); modifier: Kunde, linked with -n-
das Bürotagemodell
Büro + Tage + Modell → [[Büro-Tage]-Modell]
"office-day model" — head: das Modell (neuter); modifiers: Bürotage ("office days")
Grammar Note — Nominalization

Suffixes from verbs and adjectives: German regularly turns verbs and adjectives into nouns with suffixes such as -ung (from verbs: regeln → die Regelung), -heit and -keit (from adjectives: möglich → die Möglichkeit, frei → die Freiheit), and -nis (from verbs or adjectives: erlauben → die Erlaubnis). Nouns formed with these suffixes are feminine.

Nominalized infinitives: Any infinitive can function as a neuter noun referring to the action itself, such as das Arbeiten ("the working, the act of working") or das Verhandeln ("the negotiating"). These are common in formal descriptions of processes and activities.

Why it matters: Nominalization lets a writer compress an action or quality into a single noun phrase, which is central to nominal style. Recognizing the suffix and the underlying verb or adjective is often the fastest way to understand an unfamiliar formal noun.

  • die Umsetzung der neuen Regelung the implementation of the new arrangement umsetzen (verb) → die Umsetzung (nominalization with -ung)
  • die Zuständigkeit der einzelnen Abteilungen the area of responsibility of the individual departments zuständig (adjective) → die Zuständigkeit (nominalization with -keit)
  • das Arbeiten von zu Hause aus wird flexibler geregelt. Working from home is being regulated more flexibly. arbeiten (infinitive) used as a neuter noun, das Arbeiten
Frequent Confusion — Inventing Compounds and Derivations

Error: *die Bürotageflexibilisierungsregelungsmitteilung (a long invented chain that is not an attested German word, built by stacking compounds the way an English noun-string might be translated word for word)

Correction: die Mitteilung zur flexiblen Regelung der Bürotage — attested German prefers a shorter compound or noun phrase supported by prepositions and genitive, not an unlimited chain of components. When in doubt, break a long invented compound into a noun phrase and check any compound or derived noun against a current dictionary before using it productively.

Nominalstil

Nominalstil and Genitive Phrases

Formal and administrative German often prefers noun phrases over full clauses. Genitive and prepositional noun phrases let a writer pack a relationship that a subordinate clause would otherwise express into a single phrase.

Grammar Note — Genitive and Prepositional Noun Phrases

Genitive of specification: A genitive phrase after a nominalized noun often expresses what a subordinate clause with dass or an infinitive clause with zu would otherwise express: die Umsetzung der Regelung ("the implementation of the arrangement") compresses dass man die Regelung umsetzt.

Formal prepositional phrases: Fixed formal prepositional phrases such as im Hinblick auf, in Bezug auf, and im Rahmen von/des introduce a noun phrase where a spoken register might instead use a clause with weil, wenn, or da.

Register, not requirement: Nominal style is a register choice associated with reports, notices, academic prose, and some professional writing. It is not more correct or more advanced German than a well-formed verbal sentence; it simply serves different communicative purposes, and dense nominal style is a register choice, not a universal ideal for good German.

  • Im Hinblick auf die Zuständigkeiten der Abteilungen wird die Regelung individuell angepasst. With regard to the departments' areas of responsibility, the arrangement is adjusted individually. nominal style — formal prepositional phrase + passive
  • Weil jede Abteilung andere Zuständigkeiten hat, passen wir die Regelung individuell an. Because each department has different areas of responsibility, we adjust the arrangement individually. verbal style — subordinate clause with weil, active voice
Baustein — Nominalstil in Verbalstil umwandeln
Nominalphrase (Nominalisierung + Genitiv) → Nebensatz (Konjunktion + Subjekt + ... + Verb)
unpack the nominalized noun back into its underlying verb, supply a subject, and choose a suitable conjunction; the genitive phrase usually becomes the object or subject of the new clause
die Prüfung der Unterlagen durch die Personalabteilung → dass die Personalabteilung die Unterlagen prüft
the review of the documents by the HR department → that the HR department reviews the documents
Grammar Note — Complex Clause Hierarchy (Recognition)

Dense nominal prose sometimes appears alongside long sentences with several layered subordinate and relative clauses, each with its own comma. At this level, learn to recognize the hierarchy of such sentences when reading — identifying which clause depends on which, and where each begins and ends — rather than producing highly layered sentences yourself. This recognition skill supports the revision work in this chapter's writing tasks.

Modelldialog

Model Dialogue

Setting: a colleague asks Tobias to help explain a dense internal memo. Context: colleague to colleague, informal spoken register used to discuss a formal written text.

Sabine hat die Mitteilung der Personalabteilung bekommen und versteht einen Satz nicht.
Sabine: Was bedeutet "die Umsetzung der Arbeitszeitregelung erfolgt im Rahmen der bestehenden Zuständigkeiten"? Das klingt so kompliziert. What does "the implementation of the working-time arrangement takes place within the framework of the existing areas of responsibility" mean? That sounds so complicated.
Tobias: Ganz einfach gesagt: Jede Abteilung setzt die neue Regelung selbst um, so wie es für sie passt. Simply put: each department implements the new arrangement itself, in whatever way suits it.
Sabine: Ah, das Grundwort im ersten langen Wort ist also "Regelung", oder? Ah, so the head word in the first long word is "Regelung", right?
Tobias: Genau. "Arbeitszeitregelung" — die Regelung der Arbeitszeit. Wenn man das Grundwort findet, versteht man meistens den Rest auch. Exactly. "Arbeitszeitregelung" — the arrangement of working time. Once you find the head word, you usually understand the rest too.
Sabine: Gut, dann schreibe ich mir das so um: "Jede Abteilung setzt die Regelung selbst um." Viel klarer! Good, then I'll rewrite it for myself as: "Each department implements the arrangement itself." Much clearer!

Schreiben

Writing Workshop

Choose two sentences from the reading passage above that use nominal style. Rewrite each one in clearer verbal style, following the pattern practiced in this chapter. Then write two to three sentences of your own, describing a workplace process or rule, first in nominal style and then rewritten in verbal style.

  1. Select a nominal-style sentence and identify its nominalized noun(s) and genitive or prepositional phrase(s).
  2. Unpack the nominalized noun into its underlying verb, and supply a subject.
  3. Choose a natural conjunction (weil, wenn, dass, or simply a new main clause) to rebuild the sentence.
  4. Write your own short description of a process or rule in nominal style, then rewrite it in verbal style.
  5. Check every compound and derived noun you use against a current dictionary rather than inventing new ones.

Revision checklist:

  • Is each nominal-style sentence correctly unpacked into a clear verbal-style sentence?
  • Are all compound nouns attested rather than invented?
  • Are nouns and nominalized forms correctly capitalized?
  • Is gender and case marking correct throughout?

Culture and Communication

Culture and Communication — Nominal Style in Institutional Writing

Nominal style is common in German administrative, academic, and some professional writing, where it can compress a great deal of information into a compact noun phrase. This density can make such texts feel formal or impersonal, which is one reason many organizations and style guides now recommend a more direct, verbal style, especially in communication meant for a general audience.

Regional Note — Style Preferences Vary by Field and Institution

The degree of nominal style used in workplace and institutional writing varies by field, organization, and country across the DACH region, and individual writers and editors within the same institution may also differ in how densely they write. Dense nominal style is not a mark of better German, and clear verbal style is not a mark of weaker German; both are legitimate register choices suited to different audiences and purposes.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Guessing gender from a modifier instead of the head noun

Error: *der Arbeitszeitregelung (assuming masculine gender from "der Arbeit" instead of checking the head noun)

Correction: die Arbeitszeitregelung — gender always comes from the final element, die Regelung, regardless of the gender of any earlier component.

Inventing unattested compounds

Error: *die Homeofficetagenverteilungsplanung (a long invented chain modeled on an English noun string)

Correction: die Planung der Homeoffice-Tage — prefer a shorter, attested compound supported by a genitive or prepositional phrase, and verify any unfamiliar compound before using it productively.

Overusing nominal style in every context

Error: Writing a casual email to a colleague entirely in dense nominal style, as if imitating an official notice.

Correction: Match style to audience and purpose: use verbal style for everyday communication and reserve dense nominal style for the formal genres where it is conventional.

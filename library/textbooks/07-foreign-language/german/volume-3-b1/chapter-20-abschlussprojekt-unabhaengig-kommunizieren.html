Chapter Overview and Objectives

Chapter 20 is the capstone of Volume III. Tobias's team has spent the past several chapters building the language they need to narrate, explain, argue, communicate formally, and mediate information — from the relative clauses of Chapter 6 through the reporting frames of Chapter 19. In this chapter, that same workplace project continues one final step: the working-time report from Chapter 19 has gone to a wider department meeting, and Tobias's team must now present it, defend it under questions, extend it into a short written recommendation, and explain its outcome to an English-speaking regional colleague who was not in the meeting. This chapter does not teach new grammar. Instead, it asks you to draw together the narrative control, relative clauses, passive and impersonal constructions, connectors, compounds, register choices, Konjunktiv II, and reporting language developed across Volume III into a single connected portfolio of reading, writing, speaking, listening, and mediation tasks.

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Read and synthesize information from more than one connected text at the B2 level.
  2. Narrate, explain, and argue using accurate case, verb position, and clause structure without conscious translation from English.
  3. Write a structured formal document that combines summary, explanation, and a clearly marked recommendation.
  4. Participate in an extended spoken discussion that includes narration, explanation, argument, and response to questions.
  5. Mediate the content of a German discussion or document for an English-speaking audience, preserving meaning and nuance.
  6. Revise your own German for accuracy, cohesion, and register across a multi-skill project.

Opening Scenario

The short report that Tobias and Frau Weiss wrote in Chapter 19 has been well received, and Frau Ecker now asks the team to present it at a wider department meeting, answer questions from colleagues who were not involved in drafting it, and prepare a short follow-up recommendation in writing. Herr Novak, a regional colleague based in Zurich who does not read German fluently, also needs a spoken summary in English of what was decided and why, so that his team can align their own scheduling. Tobias must move fluidly between summarizing what colleagues said, defending the team's own reasoning, imagining what might happen under a longer trial period, and explaining the whole outcome clearly to someone outside the German-language conversation entirely.

  1. Review the cumulative vocabulary and grammar overview below.
  2. Study the model dialogue, which shows the department meeting in progress.
  3. Complete the Listening, Speaking, Reading, Writing, and Mediation components in order.
  4. Finish with the B2 Capstone Portfolio, which integrates all five components into one connected project.

Grammatik

Grammar Focus: Systeme im Überblick

This chapter introduces no new grammar. The overview below simply reminds you which consolidated systems the capstone project expects you to draw on together, in connected discourse rather than in isolated drills.

Grammar Note — Systems in Integrated Use

Clause architecture: verb-second main clauses, verb-final subordinate clauses, and relative clauses (Ch. 1–6) must work together smoothly in a single paragraph, without reverting to English-style ordering under the pressure of longer discourse.

Passive and impersonal alternatives: the choice among active voice, man, and the werden-passive (Ch. 7) should reflect what the sentence is really about, not habit.

Konjunktiv II: polite requests, hypotheticals, and advice (Ch. 17) remain available whenever the discussion turns to what might happen under a longer trial or a different arrangement.

Reporting frames and attribution: laut, nach Angaben von, and angeben/berichten, dass (Ch. 19) remain the default way to distinguish what others said from what the team itself concludes.

  • Obwohl die Regelung laut den meisten Rückmeldungen gut funktioniert, sollte man einzelne Ausnahmen, die besonders viele Kundentermine haben, gesondert betrachten. Although the arrangement works well according to most feedback, one should consider individual exceptions that have especially many client appointments separately. concessive connector + reporting frame + relative clause + man-construction
  • Wenn die Testphase verlängert würde, könnte die Abteilung genauer feststellen, ob sich die Zusammenarbeit langfristig verbessert. If the trial phase were extended, the department could determine more precisely whether teamwork improves in the long term. Konjunktiv II conditional + modal + subordinate clause
Frequent Confusion — Losing Control Under Discourse Pressure

Error: *Ich denke die Regelung ist gut aber wenn wir würden verlängern die Testphase dann könnten wir mehr Daten haben. (word order collapses toward English patterns as the sentence grows longer)

Correction: Ich denke, dass die Regelung gut ist, aber wenn wir die Testphase verlängern würden, hätten wir mehr Daten. — keep verb-final order in the dass-clause and verb-final order in the wenn-clause, with the conjugated verb only in second position in the following main clause.

Modelldialog

Model Dialogue

Setting: the wider department meeting. Context: Tobias presents the report; colleagues who were not involved ask questions; the register is more formal than the planning conversation in Chapter 19.

Tobias stellt den Bericht in der Abteilungssitzung vor. Kolleginnen und Kollegen stellen Fragen.
Tobias: Laut den zehn Rückmeldungen, die wir gesammelt haben, wird die neue Regelung im Großen und Ganzen positiv bewertet. Besonders die festen Bürotage haben laut den meisten Kolleginnen und Kollegen die Zusammenarbeit verbessert. According to the ten pieces of feedback we collected, the new arrangement is rated positively on the whole. In particular, the fixed office days have, according to most colleagues, improved teamwork.
Herr Brandt: Und was ist mit Kolleginnen und Kollegen, die viele Kundentermine außerhalb des Büros haben? Wurde das berücksichtigt? And what about colleagues who have many client appointments outside the office? Was that taken into account?
Tobias: Ja. Ein Kollege, dessen Arbeitsablauf durch die festen Tage erschwert wird, hat das ausdrücklich erwähnt. Deshalb empfiehlt das Team, die Regelung nach drei weiteren Monaten erneut zu überprüfen, statt sie sofort für alle verbindlich zu machen. Yes. One colleague whose workflow is made harder by the fixed days mentioned that explicitly. That's why the team recommends reviewing the arrangement again after three more months, instead of making it binding for everyone immediately.
Frau Ecker: Wenn wir die Testphase verlängern würden, wie würde das den Zeitplan für die Kundenbetreuung beeinflussen? If we were to extend the trial phase, how would that affect the schedule for client support?
Tobias: Nach Angaben der Abteilungsleitung hat die Kundenbetreuung bisher nicht gelitten, aber wir würden die betroffenen Kolleginnen und Kollegen natürlich weiter beobachten. According to the department management, client support has not suffered so far, but we would of course continue to monitor the colleagues affected.

Guided Practice

For each item, combine the two shorter sentences into one sentence that uses a relative clause, a concessive connector, or a Konjunktiv II conditional, as indicated. This is a bridging task before the full portfolio components below.

  1. Ein Kollege hat viele Kundentermine. Sein Arbeitsablauf wird durch die festen Bürotage erschwert. (relative clause with dessen)
  2. Die Regelung funktioniert laut den meisten Rückmeldungen gut. Ein Kollege ist unzufrieden. (concessive connector)
  3. Wir verlängern die Testphase. Wir haben mehr Daten. (Konjunktiv II conditional)

Listening Component

Ask a partner, instructor, or text-to-speech tool to read the following short passage aloud. Listen once for the overall content, then again for how narration, explanation, and recommendation are distinguished.

Sitzungsprotokoll — Listening Script

In der Abteilungssitzung stellte Tobias den Bericht zur neuen Arbeitszeitregelung vor. Laut den Rückmeldungen wird die Regelung insgesamt positiv bewertet, besonders wegen der festen Bürotage. Ein Kollege, dessen Arbeitsablauf durch feste Termine erschwert wird, äußerte Bedenken. Auf die Frage von Frau Ecker, wie sich eine längere Testphase auf die Kundenbetreuung auswirken würde, antwortete Tobias, dass die Kundenbetreuung bisher nicht gelitten habe, man die betroffenen Kolleginnen und Kollegen aber weiter beobachten würde. Das Team empfiehlt, die Regelung nach drei weiteren Monaten erneut zu überprüfen.

Verständnisfragen

1. Welche zwei Reaktionen auf die Regelung werden im Protokoll genannt?

2. Was empfiehlt das Team am Ende?

3. Identify one relative clause, one concessive structure, and one Konjunktiv II form in the passage, and explain what each contributes to the report.

Speaking Component

Aufgabe: Die Sitzung fortsetzen

Work with a partner. Continue the department meeting from the Model Dialogue. One of you plays Tobias, presenting one further point from the Chapter 19 report and defending it. The other plays a skeptical colleague who raises at least one objection and one hypothetical question ("What would happen if ...?").

Rolle: Tobias

  • Present one further finding using a reporting frame.
  • Respond to the objection with a relative clause or concessive connector.
  • Answer the hypothetical question using Konjunktiv II.

Rolle: Skeptische Kollegin/Skeptischer Kollege

  • Raise one specific objection about a group not represented in the feedback.
  • Ask one hypothetical question about a longer trial period or a different schedule.
  • Confirm whether Tobias's answer addressed the concern.

Bewertungshinweise — Speaking Criteria

  • Clear narration, explanation, and argument, each distinguishable from the others.
  • At least one relative clause and one Konjunktiv II form used accurately.
  • Appropriate formal register for a department meeting.
  • Comprehensible, natural pronunciation and pacing under extended interaction.

Solo alternative: Record both sides of the exchange yourself, pausing between turns to plan your response.

2. Wie reagiert Tobias auf die Frage von Frau Ecker?

Schreiben

Writing Component

Using the Reading Component passage and the Chapter 19 report as your sources, write a short formal follow-up recommendation of five to seven sentences addressed to Frau Ecker. Your text should summarize the meeting's outcome, address the objection raised by Herr Brandt, and state the team's recommendation clearly, using formal Sie-register throughout.

  1. Open with a brief formal salutation and one sentence stating the purpose of the message.
  2. Summarize the meeting's general outcome using a reporting frame.
  3. Address Herr Brandt's objection directly, using a relative clause or concessive connector.
  4. State the team's recommendation clearly, marked as the team's own view.
  5. Close with an appropriate formal closing.
  6. Review your text against the checklist below.

Revision checklist:

  • Is the formal Sie-register maintained consistently, including capitalization?
  • Is the general outcome clearly distinguished from Herr Brandt's individual objection?
  • Is the recommendation clearly marked as the team's own view?
  • Are verb position, case, and relative-clause punctuation correct throughout?
Vermitteln

Mediation Component

Herr Novak in Zurich does not read German fluently and was not at the meeting. Using the Reading Component passage, prepare a brief spoken or written summary in English that explains, for his team's planning purposes: what the department decided, why Herr Brandt's concern mattered, and what will happen over the next three months. Your mediation should preserve the meaning and nuance of the German source without translating it word for word or quoting long passages directly.

Mediation Reminder

Good mediation explains what matters to the listener rather than reproducing the source sentence by sentence. Keep the distinction between the general finding, the individual objection, and the team's recommendation clear in your English summary, just as the German report kept them clear.

Culture and Communication

Culture and Communication — Meetings, Objections, and Consensus

Raising a specific, well-reasoned objection in a department meeting, as Herr Brandt does, is a normal and expected part of professional discussion in German-speaking workplaces, not a sign of conflict. A well-prepared presenter is expected to have anticipated likely questions and to respond calmly and specifically, as Tobias does, rather than treating the objection as a personal challenge.

Regional Note — Working Across the DACH Region

Coordinating schedules and procedures across German-speaking offices in different countries, as Tobias's team does with a Zurich-based colleague, is common in DACH-region workplaces. Meeting conventions, working-time norms, and communication style can vary somewhat between Germany, Austria, and Switzerland, and a careful mediator adjusts explanation, not content, for a colleague working in a different national context.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Dropping register under discourse pressure

Error: Sliding into du-forms or informal phrasing partway through a formal written recommendation because the surrounding project has felt collaborative.

Correction: Maintain formal Sie-register consistently across the entire written recommendation, regardless of how informal the underlying team discussion was.

Flattening mediation into translation

Error: Translating the German report to Herr Novak sentence by sentence, including details irrelevant to his team's planning.

Correction: Select and explain only what Herr Novak's team needs — the outcome, the reason for the delay, and the next review date — rather than reproducing the full report in English.

Letting sentence length break clause architecture

Error: *Der Kollege der viele Kundentermine hat und der deswegen Probleme hat wollte wissen ob man das ändern kann. (relative clauses stacked without commas or clear case marking)

Correction: Der Kollege, der viele Kundentermine hat und deshalb Probleme mit der Regelung hat, wollte wissen, ob man das ändern kann. — punctuate each relative clause clearly and keep each verb in its correct final position.

Listening Component — Comprehension Questions

  1. Die Regelung wird insgesamt positiv bewertet, aber ein Kollege äußerte Bedenken wegen erschwerter Termine.
  2. Das Team empfiehlt, die Regelung nach drei weiteren Monaten erneut zu überprüfen.
  3. Relative clause: ein Kollege, dessen Arbeitsablauf durch feste Termine erschwert wird; Konjunktiv II: wie sich eine längere Testphase ... auswirken würde and man ... weiter beobachten würde, marking these as hypothetical/reported rather than settled fact.

Reading Component — Comprehension Questions

  1. Er fragt, ob Kolleginnen und Kollegen mit ähnlichen externen Terminen ausreichend berücksichtigt wurden; die Frage ist für ihn relevant, weil er selbst häufig solche Termine hat.
  2. Er erklärt, dass die Kundenbetreuung laut der Abteilungsleitung bisher nicht gelitten habe, das Team die betroffenen Kolleginnen und Kollegen aber weiter beobachten werde.
  3. Reporting frames: laut den meisten Rückmeldungen, laut der Abteilungsleitung; reported-speech forms: habe ... gemeldet, empfehle, habe ... nicht gelitten, werde ... beobachten — each signals that the information is being reported from someone else's statement rather than asserted directly by the writer of the protocol.

Umwelt, Wissenschaft und Alltag

Environment, Science, and Daily Life · Speak and Write German: Independent Communication and Advanced Expression · Volume III, Chapter 8

Chapter Overview and Objectives

Chapter 8 moves from describing how processes work — the passive-voice explanations you practiced in Chapter 7 — to explaining why things happen the way they do. Everyday environmental and scientific topics, from energy use to sleep habits, constantly call for cause-and-effect reasoning and for comparing data: which option uses less energy, which habit is more common, which change had the biggest effect. This chapter introduces connectors that link causes to results, develops comparative and superlative forms for talking about data, and strengthens your ability to analyze compound nouns so that denser informational vocabulary becomes easier to read. Now that Julia is settled and understands the building's basic processes, the group turns to a new topic: the building management has proposed some energy-saving changes, and everyone has opinions about the data behind them.

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Use cause-and-result connectors such as weil, deshalb, daher, and sodass to explain everyday environmental and scientific topics.
  2. Form and use comparative and superlative adjectives to discuss data and trends.
  3. Analyze unfamiliar compound nouns by identifying their head noun and components.
  4. Use common prepositional verbs in abstract, cause-related contexts.
  5. Read an informational text containing simple data and identify cause-and-effect relationships.
  6. Write and present a short explanation of a familiar science or environment topic.

  • die Heizung, die Heizungen — heating (system)
  • die Dämmung, die Dämmungen — insulation

Klima und Wetter

  • der Durchschnitt, die Durchschnitte — average
  • der Anstieg, die Anstiege — rise, increase

Grammar: Umwelt, Wissenschaft und Alltag

Cause and Result: Two Kinds of Connector

German expresses cause and result with two structurally different tools, and it is important not to confuse them:

  1. Subordinating connectors like weil ("because") and sodass ("so that / with the result that") introduce a subordinate clause and send the finite verb to the end.
  2. Adverbial connectors like deshalb, daher, and deswegen ("therefore, for that reason") occupy the front field of a main clause and trigger verb-second inversion, just like any other fronted element.

Both express the same logical relationship, but they build the sentence differently.

weil — Subordinate Cause

  • Die Heizkosten sind gestiegen, weil die alten Fenster schlecht gedämmt sind.Heating costs have risen because the old windows are poorly insulated.weil ... sind = subordinate clause, verb-final
  • Viele Leute schlafen schlechter, weil sie abends zu lange auf den Bildschirm schauen.Many people sleep worse because they look at the screen too long in the evening.

deshalb / daher / deswegen — Adverbial Result

  • Die alten Fenster sind schlecht gedämmt. Deshalb sind die Heizkosten gestiegen.The old windows are poorly insulated. Therefore, heating costs have risen.deshalb = position 1, sind = verb-second
  • Die neue Dämmung spart Energie. Daher plant die Hausverwaltung die Renovierung.The new insulation saves energy. That's why the building management is planning the renovation.
Baustein — Satzklammer mit sodass
Hauptsatz, + sodass + ... + finites Verb.
sodass introduces a result clause; the finite verb goes to the end, exactly like weil
Die neuen Bewegungsmelder schalten das Licht automatisch aus, sodass weniger Energie verschwendet wird.
The new motion sensors switch off the light automatically, with the result that less energy is wasted.

Compare: weil gives a reason for something already stated; sodass states a result that follows from something already stated. Deshalb/daher/deswegen can express the same result relationship as sodass, but as a separate main clause rather than a subordinate clause.

Comparatives and Superlatives with Data

When comparing numbers, trends, or options, German adjectives form the comparative with -er and the superlative with am -sten (predicate position) or the appropriate ending after an article (attributive position).

BaseComparativeSuperlative (predicate)Gloss
niedrigniedrigeram niedrigstenlow / lower / lowest
hochhöheram höchstenhigh / higher / highest (irregular stem)
geringgeringeram geringstenslight / slighter / slightest
großgrößeram größtenlarge / larger / largest (irregular stem)
  • Der Energieverbrauch war im Nachbargebäude niedriger als hier.Energy consumption was lower in the neighboring building than here.comparative + als = "than"
  • Im Winter ist der Verbrauch am höchsten.In winter, consumption is at its highest.
  • Von allen drei Gebäuden hat unseres den größten Anstieg gezeigt.Of all three buildings, ours showed the largest increase.attributive superlative with definite article

Use als after a comparative to mean "than." Do not use wie for this comparison; wie corresponds to "as ... as" with so ... wie for equal comparisons (e.g., so hoch wie).

Analyzing Compound Nouns

Environmental and scientific vocabulary is full of compounds. The last component is always the head noun and determines gender and plural; earlier components modify it.

Baustein — Kompositum
Bestimmungswort(-e) + Grundwort (= Genus/Plural)
der Energieverbrauch = die Energie + der Verbrauch → gender/plural from "der Verbrauch"
der Bewegungsmelder = die Bewegung + der Melder
motion sensor: "movement" + "detector/reporter"
  • die Heizkosten = die Heizung + die Kostenheating costs
  • der Klimawandel = das Klima + der Wandelclimate change
  • die Bildschirmzeit = der Bildschirm + die Zeitscreen time
Prepositional Verbs in Abstract, Causal Contexts

Several common verbs pair with a fixed preposition to express cause, attention, or effect. The preposition's case must be learned with the verb.

  • führen zu + Dativ — Die schlechte Dämmung führt zu höheren Heizkosten.Poor insulation leads to higher heating costs.
  • achten auf + Akkusativ — Die Hausverwaltung achtet jetzt stärker auf den Energieverbrauch.The building management now pays closer attention to energy consumption.
  • sich auswirken auf + Akkusativ — Die neue Heizung wirkt sich positiv auf die Kosten aus.The new heating system has a positive effect on costs.

Context: Julia, Markus, and Anna look over the building management's notice about proposed energy-saving measures.

Julia:Hier steht, dass der Verbrauch im Nachbargebäude viel niedriger war als bei uns.It says here that consumption in the neighboring building was much lower than ours.

Anna:Die Hausverwaltung sollte einfach mehr auf vergleichbare Zahlen achten, bevor sie so einen Vergleich zeigt.Building management should simply pay more attention to comparable numbers before showing a comparison like that.

Writing Workshop — Eine Erklärung schreiben

Write a short explanation (120–180 words) of a familiar environmental, scientific, or health-related everyday topic, comparing at least two options or data points and explaining a cause-and-effect relationship.

  1. Introduce the topic. State briefly what you are comparing or explaining.
  2. Give a reason. Use a weil-clause to explain a cause.
  3. State a result. Use deshalb, daher, or sodass to connect a cause to its effect.
  4. Compare. Use at least one comparative and one superlative form to discuss data or options.
  5. Revise using the checklist below.

Checkliste — Editing Checklist:

  • At least one accurate weil-clause with the verb in final position.
  • At least one accurate adverbial connector (deshalb, daher, or deswegen) with verb-second order.
  • At least one accurate comparative and one superlative form.
  • Any compound nouns used are natural and correctly spelled as one word.

Culture and Communication

Culture and Communication — Talking About Data Responsibly

Everyday German communication about environment, health, and science regularly involves comparing numbers — energy bills, weather averages, study results — in notices, articles, and casual conversation. Comparing responsibly means noticing what a comparison leaves out, as Markus and Anna do with the mismatched building sizes, rather than accepting every number at face value. Scientific and statistical claims deserve appropriate uncertainty; avoid stating conclusions more confidently than the underlying information supports.

Regional Note — Energy and Housing Data Vary

Energy costs, climate data, building standards, and public health figures vary across Germany, Austria, Switzerland, and other German-speaking regions, and change over time. Any specific figures, regulations, or study results used in real communication should be checked against a current, reliable source rather than assumed from one example or one building.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Using deshalb with verb-final order

Error: *Die Fenster sind alt, deshalb die Heizkosten hoch sind.

Correction: Deshalb is an adverbial connector, not a subordinating conjunction; it occupies position one and triggers verb-second order: Die Fenster sind alt. Deshalb sind die Heizkosten hoch.

Using wie instead of als after a comparative

Error: *Der Verbrauch war höher wie im letzten Jahr.

Correction: A comparative form takes als, not wie: Der Verbrauch war höher als im letzten Jahr. Reserve wie for equal comparisons with so ... wie.

Splitting a compound noun into separate words

Error: *der Energie Verbrauch

Correction: German compounds are normally written as a single word, with gender and plural taken from the final component: der Energieverbrauch (from die Energie + der Verbrauch).

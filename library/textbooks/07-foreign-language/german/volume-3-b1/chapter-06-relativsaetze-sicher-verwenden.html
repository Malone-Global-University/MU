Relativsätze sicher verwenden

Relative Clauses in Control · Speak and Write German: Independent Communication and Advanced Expression · Volume III, Chapter 6

Chapter Overview and Objectives

Chapter 6 opens Unit II by bringing relative clauses fully under control. You already met relative pronouns in the nominative, accusative, and dative during Chapters 2 and 3 and used them again in the Chapter 5 review. This chapter develops that foundation into confident, precise use: choosing the correct relative pronoun for gender, number, and case; punctuating relative clauses correctly; and combining prepositions with relative pronouns to describe people, things, places, and ideas with more detail. You will also meet genitive relative pronouns for the first time, but only as a recognition item — you are not expected to produce them independently yet. Sophie, Anna, and Markus return with a new thread: Sophie is helping a friend describe a new apartment and neighborhood, which gives everyone reason to describe people, places, and things more precisely.

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Select the correct relative pronoun for nominative, accusative, and dative contexts.
  2. Punctuate sentences containing relative clauses correctly.
  3. Combine common prepositions with relative pronouns to describe location, means, and relationship.
  4. Recognize genitive relative pronouns in a text without being required to produce them.
  5. Read a description-heavy text and identify the case of each relative pronoun.
  6. Write and speak precise descriptions of people, places, and things using controlled relative clauses.

Sophie's friend Julia is moving to a new apartment and has asked Sophie to help her write a short description of the building, the neighborhood, and the landlord for a mutual friend who is considering a similar move. Grammar: Relative Clauses in Control

How a Relative Clause Works

A relative clause describes a noun (the antecedent) using its own internal sentence. Two separate decisions control the relative pronoun:

  1. Gender and number come from the antecedent — the noun being described.
  2. Case comes from the relative pronoun's job inside the relative clause itself, not from the antecedent's role in the main clause.

The finite verb moves to the end of the relative clause, and a comma always separates the relative clause from the rest of the sentence.

Nominative Relative Pronouns

Use the nominative relative pronoun when the relative pronoun is the subject of its own clause.

  • Das ist der Vermieter, der die Wohnung renoviert hat.That's the landlord who renovated the apartment.der = masc. nom., subject of "hat renoviert"
  • Die Nachbarschaft, die direkt am Park liegt, ist sehr beliebt.The neighborhood that lies right by the park is very popular.

Accusative Relative Pronouns

Use the accusative relative pronoun when the relative pronoun is the direct object of its own clause.

  • Die Wohnung, die Julia gemietet hat, hat einen kleinen Balkon.The apartment that Julia rented has a small balcony.die = fem. acc., object of "hat gemietet"
  • Der Nachbar, den Markus schon kennt, ist sehr zuverlässig.The neighbor whom Markus already knows is very reliable.

Dative Relative Pronouns

Use the dative relative pronoun when the verb, adjective, or preposition inside the relative clause requires the dative.

  • Das ist die Vermieterin, der Julia die Fragen gestellt hat.That's the landlady to whom Julia asked the questions.der = fem. dat., indirect object of "gestellt hat"
  • Die Nachbarn, denen das Haus gehört, wohnen im Erdgeschoss.The neighbors to whom the house belongs live on the ground floor.

Prepositions with Relative Pronouns

When the relative clause needs a preposition, the preposition comes directly before the relative pronoun, and the preposition governs the pronoun's case.

Baustein — Sentence Bracket
Antecedent, + Präposition + Relativpronomen + ... + finites Verb.
preposition case governs the relative pronoun's case
Das ist das Gebäude, in dem Julia jetzt wohnt.
That's the building in which Julia now lives.
  • Der Park, in dem die Kinder spielen, ist sehr grün.The park in which the children play is very green.
  • Die Kollegin, mit der Sophie oft spricht, wohnt auch in der Nähe.The colleague with whom Sophie often talks also lives nearby.
  • Das ist der Vermieter, von dem Markus die Adresse bekommen hat.That's the landlord from whom Markus got the address.

Punctuation of Relative Clauses

A relative clause is always set off with a comma. If the relative clause falls in the middle of the sentence, it is set off with a comma on both sides.

  • Die Wohnung, die Julia gemietet hat, liegt im dritten Stock.The apartment that Julia rented is on the third floor.
Genitive Relative Pronouns — Recognition Only

The genitive relative pronouns dessen (masculine/neuter) and deren (feminine/plural) express "whose." You will see them in reading texts from this chapter onward, but you are not required to produce them yet.

  • Der Vermieter, dessen Wohnung Julia gemietet hat, wohnt selbst im Nachbarhaus.The landlord whose apartment Julia rented lives in the building next door himself.
Modelldialog

Model Dialogue

Context: Sophie and Julia sit at Sophie's kitchen table, drafting the description Julia wants to send to a friend.

Julia:Wie beschreibe ich die Wohnung, die ich gemietet habe?How do I describe the apartment that I rented?
Sophie:Fang mit dem Gebäude an, in dem sie liegt.Start with the building in which it's located.
Julia:Gut. Das Gebäude, das aus den 1960er-Jahren stammt, wurde letztes Jahr renoviert.Good. The building, which dates from the 1960s, was renovated last year.
Sophie:Und der Vermieter, den Markus schon kennt?And the landlord whom Markus already knows?
Julia:Der Vermieter, dem ich meine Fragen gestellt habe, war sehr freundlich.The landlord to whom I asked my questions was very friendly.
Sophie:Und die Nachbarschaft, in der du jetzt wohnst?And the neighborhood in which you now live?
Julia:Sie ist ruhig, und der Park, in dem ich morgens joggen kann, ist nur fünf Minuten entfernt.It's quiet, and the park in which I can jog in the morning is only five minutes away.

Guided Practice

Übung 1 — Die richtige Form wählen (Choosing the Correct Form)

Fill in the correct relative pronoun.

  1. Das ist die Wohnung, ___ Julia gemietet hat.
  2. Der Vermieter, ___ die Wohnung renoviert hat, wohnt nebenan.
  3. Die Nachbarn, ___ das Haus gehört, sind sehr freundlich.
  4. Das Gebäude, ___ aus den 1960er-Jahren stammt, sieht noch modern aus.

Übung 2 — Präposition und Relativpronomen (Preposition Plus Relative Pronoun)

Combine each pair of sentences using a preposition and a relative pronoun.

  1. Das ist der Park. Julia joggt in dem Park.
  2. Das ist die Kollegin. Sophie spricht oft mit der Kollegin.
  3. Das ist der Vermieter. Markus hat die Adresse von dem Vermieter bekommen.

Übung 3 — Satzzeichen setzen (Adding Punctuation)

Add the missing commas to each sentence.

  1. Die Wohnung die Julia gemietet hat liegt im dritten Stock.
  2. Der Vermieter den Markus kennt ist sehr zuverlässig.
  3. Das Gebäude in dem die Wohnung liegt wurde letztes Jahr renoviert.

Übung 4 — Genitiv erkennen (Recognizing the Genitive — Receptive)

Read each sentence and identify what dessen or deren refers to. You do not need to produce these forms yourself.

  1. Der Vermieter, dessen Wohnung Julia gemietet hat, wohnt im Nachbarhaus.
  2. Die Nachbarin, deren Balkon direkt über Julias Balkon liegt, ist sehr nett.

Schreiben

Writing Workshop — A Precise Description

Write a short description (120–180 words) of a home, a neighbor, or a place you know well, following Julia's model.

  1. Introduce the place or person. Use a nominative relative clause to add a first detail.
  2. Add a second detail. Use an accusative relative clause naming something someone did to or with it.
  3. Add a third detail. Use a dative relative clause, ideally combined with a preposition.
  4. Punctuate carefully. Check that every relative clause is set off with commas.
  5. Revise using the checklist below.

Checkliste — Editing Checklist:

  • At least one relative clause each in the nominative, accusative, and dative.
  • At least one preposition combined correctly with a relative pronoun.
  • Every relative clause has correct comma placement.
  • Every relative clause has the finite verb in final position.
  • All nouns are capitalized; umlauts and ß are used correctly.

Culture and Communication

Culture and Communication — Describing Housing Precisely

Precise description matters in everyday German housing communication — describing an apartment, a landlord, or a neighborhood accurately is common in classified ads, rental applications, and conversations with friends helping each other find housing. Rental practices, building types, and neighborhood character vary widely across German-speaking regions and even within one city, so describe specific features rather than making general claims about "German apartments" as a single type.

Regional Note — Housing Terms Vary

Terms for floors, building types, and rental practices can differ between Germany, Austria, and Switzerland — for example, ground-floor numbering conventions are not identical everywhere. Any specific rental terms, prices, or legal details used in real communication should be checked against a current, reliable local source.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Matching the relative pronoun's case to the antecedent instead of its own clause

Error: *Der Vermieter, die Wohnung renoviert hat, wohnt nebenan.

Correction: The relative pronoun's case depends on its role inside the relative clause, not on the antecedent: Der Vermieter, der die Wohnung renoviert hat, wohnt nebenan. Here der is nominative because it is the subject of "hat renoviert."

Forgetting commas around the relative clause

Error: *Die Wohnung die Julia gemietet hat liegt im dritten Stock.

Correction: German always sets off a relative clause with commas, even when English would not require one: Die Wohnung, die Julia gemietet hat, liegt im dritten Stock.

Placing the preposition after the relative pronoun instead of before it

Error: *Das ist der Park, den Julia joggt in.

Correction: The preposition comes directly before the relative pronoun, and its case governs the pronoun: Das ist der Park, in dem Julia joggt.

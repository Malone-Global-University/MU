Wiederholung 2: Analysieren und Erklären

Unit II Assessment · Speak and Write German: Independent Communication and Advanced Expression · Volume III, Chapter 10

Chapter Overview and Objectives

Chapter 10 is the Unit II assessment. It does not introduce new grammar. Instead, it asks you to bring together, under realistic discourse pressure, everything Chapters 6 through 9 developed: relative clauses that describe people, things, and places precisely; the present passive for processes, rules, and procedures; connectors that organize cause, contrast, and sequence; compound nouns that carry information efficiently; and the simple past for connected narration. Julia, Markus, and Anna's building conversations continue here in a slightly more analytical mode, as the three of them prepare a short presentation for their building's community newsletter about how their neighborhood has been changing. The chapter closes Unit II and prepares you for the workplace and institutional communication of Chapters 11 through 15.

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Read a longer, multi-paragraph text that combines description, explanation, and narrative, and identify its main ideas and structure.
  2. Use relative clauses in the nominative, accusative, and dative to combine sentences precisely.
  3. Choose between active, man, and passive constructions appropriately when explaining a process or a set of rules.
  4. Recognize and use connectors of cause, contrast, sequence, and result to organize an explanation.
  5. Analyze and use compound nouns to read and produce denser, more efficient vocabulary.
  6. Write a structured explanatory response that integrates narrative, description, and explanation with attention to case, verb position, and register.

Opening Scenario

The building's community newsletter has asked Julia, Markus, and Anna to write a short piece about how their neighborhood has changed over the past few years — new shops, a renovated park, a new recycling system, and a few disagreements about what should happen next. Before they can write anything, they need to agree on what actually happened, explain how the new systems work, and describe the people and places involved clearly enough that a reader who does not live in the building can follow the story. Grammar Review: Analysieren und Erklären

Integration, not new material

What this chapter assesses

Chapter 10 assesses four systems developed across Unit II. None of them is new here; the goal is accurate, integrated use of all four together in longer discourse.

Relativsätze (Ch. 6)

  • Relative pronouns agree in gender and number with the noun they describe, but take their case from their role inside the relative clause.
  • The finite verb goes to the end of the relative clause.
  • A comma always separates a relative clause from the main clause.

Passiv und Alternativen (Ch. 7)

  • Present passive: a form of werden + past participle.
  • Man + active verb is a common, more conversational alternative.
  • Passive foregrounds the process or result; the agent, when mentioned at all, appears with von or durch.

Konnektoren (Ch. 7–8)

  • Cause: weil, deshalb, daher.
  • Contrast: aber, jedoch, während.
  • Sequence: zuerst, dann, schließlich.

Erzähltempus (Ch. 9)

  • The simple past narrates connected written events.
  • The past perfect (receptive) marks an event before another past event.
  • Attributive adjective endings describe people and settings precisely.

Relative Clauses Across Three Cases

Review how the relative pronoun's case depends on its function inside the relative clause, not on the noun it describes.

Choosing Active, Man, or Passive

The same information can be explained three ways. The choice depends on whether the process, the agent, or the everyday tone matters most.

  • Aktiv: Die Hausverwaltung sammelt den Müll jeden Montag.The building management collects the trash every Monday.
  • Man: Man sammelt den Müll jeden Montag.They collect the trash every Monday. (conversational, agent understood)
  • Passiv: Der Müll wird jeden Montag gesammelt.The trash is collected every Monday. (process foregrounded, agent unimportant)

Passive remains common in notices and procedural explanations; it should not replace every active sentence in a piece of ordinary narration.

Compounds in Analytical Reading

Baustein — Kompositum lesen
das Recycling + das System → das Recyclingsystem
head-final: the final noun (System) gives the gender and the core meaning; Recycling- specifies which kind of system
die Mülltrennung, die Nachbarschaftsversammlung, die Umweltauswirkung
waste separation, neighborhood meeting, environmental impact

Recognizing the head noun first, then working backward through the modifying elements, is the fastest way to read dense compounds under time pressure.

Listening Assessment

Ask a partner, instructor, or text-to-speech tool to read the following passage aloud. Listen once for the overall structure, then again to identify relative clauses, passive forms, and connectors.

Kurzbeitrag — Listening Script

Vor zwei Jahren wurde in unserem Haus ein neues Recyclingsystem eingeführt, das den Müll in drei Kategorien trennt. Zuerst wussten viele Bewohner nicht genau, wie das System funktioniert, weil die Anleitung, die an der Haustür hing, zu kompliziert war. Deshalb organisierte die Hausverwaltung eine Versammlung, bei der die neuen Regeln noch einmal erklärt wurden. Während einige Nachbarn das System inzwischen ganz normal finden, kritisieren andere es immer noch. Schließlich wurde letztes Jahr auch der kleine Park hinter dem Haus renoviert, und die meisten Bewohner, die den Park regelmäßig benutzen, sind mit dem Ergebnis sehr zufrieden.

Verständnisfragen

1. Warum wussten viele Bewohner am Anfang nicht, wie das System funktioniert?

2. Was hat die Hausverwaltung deshalb organisiert?

3. Wie reagieren die Bewohner auf den renovierten Park?

4. Identify every relative clause, every passive verb form, and every connector in the script.

Reading Assessment

Newsletterbeitrag: Was sich in unserem Haus verändert hat

Vor zwei Jahren wurde in unserem Haus ein neues Recyclingsystem eingeführt, das den Müll in drei Kategorien trennt: Papier, Verpackungen und Restmüll. Zuerst wussten viele Bewohner nicht genau, wie das System funktioniert, weil die erste Anleitung, die an der Haustür hing, zu kompliziert geschrieben war. Deshalb organisierte die Hausverwaltung eine Versammlung, bei der die Regeln noch einmal einfach erklärt wurden. Auf dieser Versammlung lernten sich auch mehrere Nachbarn, die vorher kaum miteinander gesprochen hatten, zum ersten Mal richtig kennen. Während einige Bewohner das neue System inzwischen ganz selbstverständlich finden, kritisieren andere immer noch, dass die Mülleimer im Keller zu klein sind. Schließlich wurde letztes Jahr auch der kleine Park hinter dem Haus renoviert. Der alte, ziemlich vernachlässigte Park wich einem freundlichen, gut beleuchteten Ort mit neuen Bänken, den viele Familien inzwischen jeden Nachmittag benutzen. Die meisten Bewohner, die den Park regelmäßig besuchen, sind mit dem Ergebnis sehr zufrieden, auch wenn ein paar ältere Nachbarn den ruhigeren, wilderen Park von früher manchmal vermissen.

Verständnisfragen

1. In welche drei Kategorien trennt das neue System den Müll?

2. Was ist bei der Versammlung außer der Erklärung der Regeln noch passiert?

3. Was kritisieren manche Bewohner am neuen System immer noch?

4. Find every relative clause and identify its case. Find every passive verb form. List the connectors used to organize the text, and identify two attributive adjective phrases describing the park.

Schreiben

Writing Assessment — Eine analytische Antwort schreiben

Write a structured explanatory response (150–200 words) about a change you have noticed in a place you know well (a building, neighborhood, school, or workplace). Combine explanation and narrative, as in the reading passage above.

  1. Introduce the change. Name the place and briefly state what has changed.
  2. Explain a process or rule. Use at least one passive or man construction to explain how something works or worked.
  3. Describe with relative clauses. Use at least two relative clauses in different cases to add precise detail about people, places, or things.
  4. Narrate a related event. Use the simple past and at least one narrative connector to tell a short related story.
  5. Organize with connectors. Use at least one connector each of cause, contrast, and sequence.
  6. Revise using the checklist below.

Checkliste — Editing Checklist:

  • At least two accurate relative clauses in appropriate cases, each preceded by a comma.
  • At least one accurate passive or man construction.
  • At least one connector each of cause, contrast, and sequence.
  • Consistent simple-past narration for any narrated events.
  • All nouns are capitalized; umlauts and ß are used correctly; case endings on articles and adjectives are checked.

Quote only very short phrases from any real notice, sign, or article if you refer to one, and put everything else in your own words.

Culture and Communication

Culture and Communication — Explaining Rules and Change Fairly

Explaining a shared system — a recycling schedule, a building rule, a renovation — well means presenting both the reasoning behind it and the range of reactions to it, much as Julia, Markus, and Anna do when they plan their newsletter piece. A fair explanation acknowledges that not everyone agrees, without treating disagreement as a problem to be hidden. Passive constructions are common in this kind of writing precisely because they let the writer describe a system or a decision without assigning blame to a specific person.

Regional Note — Building and Neighborhood Practices Vary

Recycling categories, building-management structures, and neighborhood-meeting customs differ across Germany, Austria, Switzerland, and other German-speaking regions, and continue to change over time. Any specific system described in real communication should be checked against current, reliable, local information rather than assumed from a single example.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Choosing the wrong case for the relative pronoun

Error: *Der Nachbar, den wir geholfen haben, hat sich gefreut.

Correction: helfen takes a dative object, so the relative pronoun must be dative, not accusative: Der Nachbar, dem wir geholfen haben, hat sich gefreut. Check the verb's case requirement, not the noun's role in the main clause.

Overusing the passive in ordinary narration

Error: *Der Park wurde von den Nachbarn besucht, und es wurde viel gesprochen, und es wurde sich gefreut.

Correction: Reserve the passive for processes, rules, and results; use the active voice for ordinary narration: Die Nachbarn besuchten den Park, sprachen viel miteinander und freuten sich über das Ergebnis.

Forgetting the comma before a relative clause

Error: *Der Park der letztes Jahr renoviert wurde sieht viel schöner aus.

Correction: A relative clause is always set off by commas, regardless of English punctuation habits: Der Park, der letztes Jahr renoviert wurde, sieht viel schöner aus.

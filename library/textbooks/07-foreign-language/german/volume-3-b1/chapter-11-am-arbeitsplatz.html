Am Arbeitsplatz

Workplace Communication · Speak and Write German: Independent Communication and Advanced Expression · Volume III, Chapter 11

Chapter Overview and Objectives

Chapter 11 opens Unit III, which turns from description, explanation, and narrative toward workplace, institutional, and intercultural communication. This chapter refines the formal Sie style you have used since Volume I, adds the more nominal, slightly more impersonal tone typical of routine workplace German, and expands infinitive clauses with zu so you can express plans, obligations, and requests more precisely. Julia has just started coordinating a small project at her workplace, and her exchanges with her colleague Frau Bittner and her team give you a model for meetings, professional email, and everyday office talk. None of this requires new case, article, or verb-position systems; it asks you to redeploy what you already control in a more formal, task-oriented setting.

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Participate in a short workplace meeting, asking about and reporting on tasks, deadlines, and decisions.
  2. Write a professional email using appropriate formal Sie greetings, closings, and tone.
  3. Recognize and use a more nominal, formal style typical of routine workplace communication.
  4. Form and use infinitive clauses with zu to express plans, requests, and recommendations.
  5. Use common prepositional verbs connected to work, tasks, and responsibility.
  6. Summarize the key points of a meeting or a short workplace exchange in speech and in writing.

Opening Scenario

Julia has recently taken on a small coordinating role for a project at her workplace. Her colleague, Frau Bittner, has asked her to prepare a short status update for the team, follow up on a task with a supplier, and write a brief email confirming what was decided at their last meeting. Julia is comfortable with everyday German, but this is the first time she has had to sound consistently professional in writing and in a meeting. This chapter follows her through that transition: a short meeting, a status email, and a written summary — exactly the tasks this chapter's assessment work will ask you to complete.

Wortschatz

Workplace Vocabulary

This chapter's vocabulary supports meetings, tasks, deadlines, feedback, and everyday workplace roles. As always, active nouns appear with article and plural.

Besprechung und Aufgaben

  • die Besprechung, die Besprechungen — meeting
  • die Aufgabe, die Aufgaben — task
  • die Frist, die Fristen — deadline
  • der Termin, die Termine — appointment, scheduled time

Rollen und Verantwortung

  • der Kollege, die Kollegen / die Kollegin, die Kolleginnen — colleague
  • die Verantwortung, die Verantwortungen — responsibility
  • die Rückmeldung, die Rückmeldungen — feedback
  • die Entscheidung, die Entscheidungen — decision

Verben mit Präposition

  • sich kümmern um (+ Akk.) — to take care of
  • sich verantwortlich fühlen für (+ Akk.) — to feel responsible for
  • Bescheid geben — to let someone know
  • zuständig sein für (+ Akk.) — to be in charge of

E-Mail und Ton

  • Sehr geehrte Frau …, Sehr geehrter Herr … — formal salutation
  • Mit freundlichen Grüßen — formal closing
  • die Rücksprache, die Rücksprachen — follow-up conversation
  • vereinbaren — to arrange, agree on

All active nouns above appear with article and plural, consistent with the vocabulary standard established since Volume I.

Grammar: Formal Style, Nominal Style, and zu-Infinitives

Redeploying known systems in a new register

What this chapter develops

You have used formal Sie since Volume I. This chapter refines that style for workplace settings, introduces the more nominal tone common in routine professional German, and develops infinitive clauses with zu as a productive system for the first time.

Refining Formal Sie Style

Workplace Sie communication is not only about grammatical correctness; it also depends on consistent tone across a whole email or conversation, from greeting to closing.

ContextPatternNote
AnredeSehr geehrte Frau Bittner,last name, comma, no exclamation mark
BitteKönnten Sie mir bitte Bescheid geben?modal + Sie softens the request
GrußMit freundlichen Grüßenstandard formal closing, not "Liebe Grüße" in this context
  • Könnten Sie mir bis Freitag eine kurze Rückmeldung geben?Could you give me a short response by Friday?
  • Ich würde mich freuen, wenn wir kurz Rücksprache halten könnten.I would appreciate it if we could briefly touch base.

Nominal Style in Routine Workplace German

Workplace German often prefers a noun where everyday spoken German would use a verb. This does not change any grammar you already know; it simply shifts which word carries the main information.

  • Verbal: Wir haben entschieden, dass wir den Liefertermin verschieben.We decided to postpone the delivery date.
  • Nominal: Die Entscheidung über die Verschiebung des Liefertermins ist gefallen.The decision about postponing the delivery date has been made.verb "entscheiden" becomes the noun "die Entscheidung"; verb "verschieben" becomes "die Verschiebung"

Nominal style is a register choice for formal writing, not a requirement for everyday spoken workplace talk. Overusing it can sound stiff even in German offices.

Infinitive Clauses with zu

Infinitive clauses with zu let you attach a second action to a main clause without a full subordinate clause. The zu-infinitive goes at the end of its clause, and a comma usually separates the clauses when the infinitive clause is more than a bare infinitive.

Baustein — zu-Infinitiv
Hauptsatz + , + [Ergänzungen] + zu + Infinitiv
the infinitive clause is set off by a comma when it contains more than the bare infinitive; separable verbs insert -zu- between prefix and stem
Julia hat vor, den Bericht bis Freitag fertigzustellen.
Julia plans to finish the report by Friday.
  • Es ist wichtig, die Frist einzuhalten.It's important to meet the deadline.separable verb "einhalten" → "einzuhalten"
  • Frau Bittner bittet Julia, den Lieferanten anzurufen.Frau Bittner asks Julia to call the supplier.
  • Wir haben beschlossen, die Besprechung auf Donnerstag zu verlegen.We decided to move the meeting to Thursday.

Common triggers for a zu-infinitive include vorhaben, bitten, beschließen, es ist wichtig/möglich/nötig, and versuchen.

Prepositional Verbs at Work

Workplace communication depends heavily on a small set of prepositional verbs. Learn each verb together with its preposition and case.

  • Julia kümmert sich um die Bestellung.Julia is taking care of the order.
  • Wer ist für diese Aufgabe zuständig?Who is in charge of this task?
  • Sie fühlt sich für das Ergebnis verantwortlich.She feels responsible for the outcome.
Modellgespräch

Model Discussion and Email

Context: a short status meeting between Julia and Frau Bittner.

Frau Bittner:Julia, könnten Sie kurz berichten, wie weit Sie mit der Bestellung sind?Julia, could you briefly report how far along you are with the order?
Julia:Gerne. Ich habe vor, den Lieferanten heute Nachmittag anzurufen, um den Liefertermin zu bestätigen.Gladly. I plan to call the supplier this afternoon to confirm the delivery date.
Frau Bittner:Gut. Es ist wichtig, die Frist einzuhalten, weil der Kunde nächste Woche liefern möchte.Good. It's important to meet the deadline because the client wants to deliver next week.
Julia:Verstanden. Ich gebe Ihnen heute noch Bescheid, sobald ich mit dem Lieferanten gesprochen habe.Understood. I'll let you know today as soon as I've spoken with the supplier.
Frau Bittner:Danke, Julia. Schreiben Sie mir bitte auch eine kurze E-Mail zur Bestätigung.Thank you, Julia. Please also write me a short email to confirm.

Following the meeting, Julia sends this confirmation email.

Von: julia.werner@beispielfirma.de
An: a.bittner@beispielfirma.de
Betreff: Bestätigung — Liefertermin Bestellung 4471

Sehr geehrte Frau Bittner,

wie besprochen habe ich heute Nachmittag mit dem Lieferanten telefoniert, um den Liefertermin zu bestätigen. Der Lieferant hat zugesagt, die Bestellung bis Donnerstag kommender Woche zu liefern.

Ich werde die Sendung verfolgen und Ihnen rechtzeitig Bescheid geben, falls sich etwas ändert.

Mit freundlichen Grüßen
Julia Werner

Schreiben

Writing Workshop — Eine berufliche E-Mail schreiben

Write a short professional email (120–160 words) summarizing the outcome of a workplace meeting or task, as in the Model Email above.

  1. Open formally. Use an appropriate Sehr geehrte/r … greeting.
  2. State the purpose. Briefly say what the email confirms or reports.
  3. Use at least one zu-infinitive clause. Express a plan, a request, or a recommendation.
  4. Use at least one prepositional verb. Describe who is responsible for what.
  5. Close formally. Use Mit freundlichen Grüßen and your name.
  6. Revise using the checklist below.

Checkliste — Editing Checklist:

  • Consistent formal Sie address, greeting, and closing.
  • At least one accurate zu-infinitive clause, correctly punctuated.
  • At least one accurate prepositional verb with correct case.
  • Tone remains professional throughout, without informal expressions.
  • All nouns are capitalized; umlauts and ß are used correctly.

Invent a plausible workplace scenario rather than describing a real employer's confidential information.

Culture and Communication

Culture and Communication — Workplace Tone Is Not One Thing

German-language workplace communication ranges from quite formal, especially in larger or more traditional organizations and in written correspondence, to fairly relaxed, especially among colleagues on the same team or in some smaller companies and startups. Julia's exchange with Frau Bittner reflects a common middle ground: formal Sie address paired with friendly, cooperative tone.

Regional Note — Workplace Norms Vary by Field and Country

Hierarchy, directness, meeting style, and the point at which colleagues move to du vary considerably across Germany, Austria, and Switzerland, and across industries and individual companies within each country. Do not assume that any single office in this chapter represents workplace culture generally; specific workplace practices should be verified for the actual organization and country involved.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Mixing formal and informal tone in one email

Error: *Sehr geehrte Frau Bittner, kannst du mir kurz sagen, wie es läuft? Liebe Grüße.

Correction: Keep the address form consistent from greeting to closing: Sehr geehrte Frau Bittner, könnten Sie mir kurz sagen, wie es läuft? Mit freundlichen Grüßen.

Forgetting -zu- with separable verbs

Error: *Es ist wichtig, die Frist zu einhalten.

Correction: With separable verbs, zu goes between the prefix and the stem: Es ist wichtig, die Frist einzuhalten.

Using the wrong preposition with a prepositional verb

Error: *Sie kümmert sich für die Bestellung.

Correction: sich kümmern takes um, not für: Sie kümmert sich um die Bestellung. Learn each prepositional verb together with its fixed preposition and case.

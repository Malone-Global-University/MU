Medien und Informationen

Media and Information · Speak and Write German: Independent Communication and Advanced Expression · Volume III, Chapter 4

Chapter Overview and Objectives

Chapter 4 turns from personal and social description toward the language of media and information. You will summarize short news and informational texts, distinguish fact from opinion and claim from evidence, and discuss your own media habits. The chapter introduces reporting frames with laut, nach, and sagen, dass; develops receptive recognition of passive constructions in news style; adds the connectors denn, jedoch, and daher; and builds early awareness of nominalization in informational prose. Sophie, Anna, and Markus continue their conversations, this time comparing how they get their news and how reliable they find different sources.

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Summarize the main point of a short news or informational text.
  2. Report what a source says using laut, nach, and sagen, dass.
  3. Recognize passive constructions typical of news and informational style.
  4. Use denn, jedoch, and daher to connect ideas about causes, contrasts, and results.
  5. Notice nominalized forms in informational texts and connect them to familiar verbs and adjectives.
  6. Write a short, neutral summary that distinguishes claim from evidence.

Opening Scenario

Markus has started reading a local Berlin news app on his commute, and over coffee he tells Sophie and Anna about a short article on the neighborhood's new bus line — the same one he mentioned in Chapter 3. As they talk, they naturally need to report what the article says, distinguish what is stated as fact from what one municipal spokesperson simply claims, and connect their reactions with words like "because," "however," and "therefore." This is exactly the kind of language that reporting frames, passive recognition, and the connectors denn, jedoch, and daher make possible.

Grammar Focus: Reporting Frames, Passive Recognition, and Connectors

Language Note — Reporting What a Source Says

German has several common ways to report what a source states. This chapter activates three of them for production: laut plus a noun in the dative, nach plus a noun in the dative, and sagen, dass plus a subordinate clause. Laut and nach both mean "according to," but laut typically comes first in its phrase and nach often follows the noun it refers to in fixed patterns.

Baustein — Reporting with laut

Laut der Stadtverwaltung fährt die neue Buslinie ab nächstem Monat.

laut + dative noun phrase + V2 main clause

Laut dem Artikel ist die Verbindung schon getestet worden.

According to the article, the connection has already been tested.

Baustein — Reporting with nach

Der Meldung nach ist die neue Linie deutlich zuverlässiger.

dative noun phrase + nach (postposed) + V2 main clause

Dem Sprecher nach gibt es noch keine festen Zahlen.

According to the spokesperson, there are no firm figures yet.

Baustein — Reporting with sagen, dass

Der Artikel sagt, dass die Stadt in neue Verkehrsverbindungen investiert.

main clause (V2) + comma + subordinate clause with dass and verb-final order

Die Sprecherin sagt, dass die Kosten noch nicht feststehen.

The spokeswoman says that the costs are not yet fixed.

Notice that reporting a source's words is not the same as endorsing them. Laut dem Sprecher and der Sprecher sagt, dass both distance the writer from the claim, which is essential for neutral summary.

Language Note — Recognizing Passive Voice in News Style (Receptive)

News and informational texts often use the passive voice to focus on an action or result rather than on who performed it. This chapter introduces passive recognition receptively — you should be able to identify and understand passive sentences, but you are not yet expected to produce the full passive system independently. The present passive is formed with a conjugated form of werden plus a past participle.

  • Die neue Buslinie wird ab nächstem Monat eingeführt. (The new bus line is being introduced starting next month.)
  • Die Verbindung ist schon getestet worden. (The connection has already been tested.) — present perfect passive, receptive only

When you summarize a passive sentence, you may restate it with man or an active subject if that feels more natural: Man führt die neue Buslinie ab nächstem Monat ein. Full productive control of the passive system, including agent phrases with von and durch, is scheduled for Chapter 7.

Language Note — Connectors denn, jedoch, and daher

You already know the coordinating connector denn (cause) from earlier chapters; this chapter recycles it in a media context and adds two new connectors. Jedoch ("however") is a fronting adverb that triggers verb-second order in its own clause, similar to deshalb. Daher ("therefore, for this reason") also fronts and triggers verb-second order, and is common in written, slightly more formal summary style.

  • Viele Leser vertrauen der App, denn sie aktualisiert die Nachrichten ständig. (coordinating; no change to word order)
  • Die App ist beliebt; jedoch überprüfen nicht alle Nutzer die Quellen. (fronting adverb; verb-second)
  • Die Quelle war nicht eindeutig; daher hat die Zeitung den Artikel korrigiert. (fronting adverb; verb-second)

Jedoch and daher are somewhat more formal than aber and deshalb and appear frequently in written summaries and reports.

Language Note — Noticing Nominalization (Receptive)

Informational German often turns verbs and adjectives into nouns, producing a denser, more written style. This chapter builds receptive awareness only — you should be able to connect a nominalized form back to its familiar verb or adjective, but you are not yet expected to produce nominalizations independently.

  • überprüfen → die Überprüfung (the checking, verification)
  • zuverlässig → die Zuverlässigkeit (reliability)
  • berichten → der Bericht (the report)

Recognizing these connections helps you read denser news prose without needing to look up every unfamiliar word. Productive control of word formation is scheduled for Chapter 18.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Sophie, Anna, and Markus meet for coffee. Markus has just read a short article about the new bus line on his phone. They use du.

Markus
Ich habe gerade einen Artikel über die neue Buslinie gelesen. Laut der Stadtverwaltung wird sie ab nächstem Monat eingeführt.I just read an article about the new bus line. According to the city administration, it will be introduced starting next month.
Sophie
Steht da auch, warum die alte Linie so unzuverlässig war?Does it also say why the old line was so unreliable?
Markus
Der Artikel sagt, dass es technische Probleme gab. Ein Sprecher der Stadt behauptet, dass diese jetzt gelöst sind — aber einen Beweis dafür liefert der Artikel nicht.The article says that there were technical problems. A city spokesperson claims these are now solved — but the article doesn't provide any evidence for that.
Anna
Das ist typisch, oder? Eine Behauptung ist doch keine Tatsache. Woher stammt der Artikel überhaupt?That's typical, isn't it? A claim isn't a fact. Where does the article even come from?
Markus
Von einer lokalen Nachrichten-App. Sie ist meistens ziemlich zuverlässig, jedoch überprüfe ich wichtige Behauptungen trotzdem gern in einer zweiten Quelle.From a local news app. It's usually fairly reliable, however I still like to check important claims in a second source.
Sophie
Gute Idee. Die Verbindung ist ja schon getestet worden, sagt die App — dem Sprecher nach läuft alles nach Plan. Daher bin ich vorsichtig optimistisch.Good idea. The connection has already been tested, the app says — according to the spokesperson, everything is going according to plan. So I'm cautiously optimistic.

This exchange uses reporting frames with laut, nach, and sagen, dass; two receptive passive forms; and the connectors jedoch and daher, alongside the nominalization Beweis from beweisen.

Guided Practice

Übung 1 — Berichten mit laut, nach und sagen, dass (Reporting Frames)

Rewrite each direct statement as a reported statement using the reporting frame indicated in parentheses.

  1. "Die Kosten sind noch nicht bekannt." (laut + der Sprecherin)
  2. "Die neue Linie ist zuverlässiger." (Artikel + nach)
  3. "Die Stadt investiert in neue Verbindungen." (der Bericht + sagen, dass)

Übung 2 — Passiv erkennen (Recognizing Passive Sentences)

Identify whether each sentence is active or passive, and restate the passive sentences with man or an active subject.

  1. Die neue Buslinie wird im April eingeführt.
  2. Die Stadtverwaltung testet die Verbindung.
  3. Die Kosten sind noch nicht veröffentlicht worden.

Übung 3 — Konnektoren wählen (Choosing Connectors)

Choose denn, jedoch, or daher to complete each sentence logically, and check the word order in the second clause.

  1. Viele Leser vertrauen der App, ___ sie aktualisiert die Nachrichten ständig.
  2. Die Quelle war nicht eindeutig; ___ hat die Zeitung den Artikel korrigiert.
  3. Die App ist beliebt; ___ überprüfen nicht alle Nutzer die Quellen.

Schreiben

Writing Workshop — Summarizing a Short Article Neutrally

This task asks you to write a short, neutral summary of a news or informational item (real or invented), using reporting frames and connectors to distinguish claim from evidence.

  1. Introduce the topic. State in one or two sentences what the article or item is about.
  2. Report the source's claims. Use at least two reporting frames, choosing from laut, nach, and sagen, dass.
  3. Distinguish fact from claim. Clearly separate what is stated as established fact from what a source merely asserts.
  4. Connect your points. Use denn, jedoch, or daher at least twice to organize cause, contrast, or result.
  5. Stay neutral. Avoid stating your own opinion as if it were fact.
  6. Revise using the checklist below.

Checkliste — Editing Checklist:

  • The summary clearly introduces the topic of the article or item.
  • At least two reporting frames appear with correct case and word order.
  • Fact and claim are clearly distinguished, not blended together.
  • At least two of the connectors denn, jedoch, and daher are used accurately.
  • All nouns are capitalized; umlauts and ß are used correctly.
  • No personal opinion is presented as an established fact.

Culture and Communication

Culture and Communication — Media Habits Across the DACH Region

Media habits vary widely across Germany, Austria, and Switzerland, and within each country by age, region, and platform. Public broadcasting, regional newspapers, and digital news apps all play different roles in different communities. Avoid assuming that any one outlet, platform, or habit represents "how German speakers get their news" in general.

Regional Note — Verifying Current Media and News Content

News content, outlet names, and media statistics change quickly and vary by country. Any specific outlet, figure, or claim used in real conversation or writing should be checked against a current, reliable source rather than assumed from a textbook example.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Treating a claim as an established fact

Error: *Die technischen Probleme sind gelöst. (stated as fact, when only claimed)

Correction: Use a reporting frame to show the source of the claim: Laut einem Sprecher sind die technischen Probleme gelöst. Reserve unmarked statements for information you can independently confirm.

Wrong case after laut

Error: *Laut die Stadtverwaltung wird die Linie eingeführt.

Correction: Laut takes the dative in standard usage: Laut der Stadtverwaltung wird die Linie eingeführt. Check the noun's case ending, not just its position in the sentence.

Forgetting verb-second order after jedoch or daher

Error: *Jedoch ich bin skeptisch.

Correction: As fronting adverbs, jedoch and daher trigger verb-second order, so the finite verb comes immediately after them: Jedoch bin ich skeptisch. Treat them like deshalb, not like a coordinating conjunction such as aber.

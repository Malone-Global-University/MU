Prozesse und Passiv

Processes and Passive Voice · Speak and Write German: Independent Communication and Advanced Expression · Volume III, Chapter 7

Chapter Overview and Objectives

Chapter 7 shifts the focus from describing things — the precise, relative-clause-rich descriptions you practiced in Chapter 6 — to describing how things are done. Rules, processes, and procedures are everywhere in everyday and institutional German: how a form gets processed, how a package gets delivered, how a rental agreement gets signed. This chapter introduces the present passive with werden as the core tool for describing processes without necessarily naming who performs them, and helps you choose confidently among an active sentence, a sentence with man, and a passive sentence. Julia has now moved into her new apartment, and the group turns to the practical processes that come with settling in: how the internet gets installed, how recycling gets sorted, how a lease gets renewed.

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Form and use the present passive with werden to describe processes, rules, and procedures.
  2. Recognize agent phrases with von and durch in passive sentences.
  3. Recognize modal passive constructions in reading and listening without independent production.
  4. Choose appropriately among active voice, man, and passive voice depending on communicative focus.
  5. Read process descriptions, notices, and instructions and identify passive constructions.
  6. Write and speak a clear explanation of a familiar process.

Opening Scenario

Julia has settled into the apartment Sophie helped her describe in Chapter 6, but now she has practical questions: how does the recycling get sorted in this building, how does the internet installation get scheduled, and how does a lease get renewed if she decides to stay another year? Markus, who has lived in the building longer, and Anna, who recently went through her own move, help Julia understand the building's and city's procedures. Explaining a process clearly — without necessarily saying who carries it out each time — is exactly what the present passive is for.

Grammar: Prozesse und Passiv

How the Present Passive Works

The present passive shifts focus away from who performs an action and onto the action itself, or onto what the action happens to. It is formed with the conjugated present-tense form of werden plus the past participle of the main verb, placed at the end of the clause — the same sentence bracket you already know from the perfect tense and modal verbs.

  1. The thing the process happens to becomes the grammatical subject, in the nominative.
  2. werden is conjugated for that subject and appears in verb-second position.
  3. The past participle of the main verb goes to the end of the clause.

Passive voice is common in institutional and procedural German precisely because many rules and processes matter more than who happens to carry them out on a given day.

Forming the Present Passive

SubjectwerdenParticipleGloss
der Müllwirdsortiertthe trash is sorted
die Verträgewerdenverlängertthe contracts are renewed
das Formularwirdeingereichtthe form is submitted
die Anschlüssewerdeninstalliertthe connections are installed

Compare this bracket to the perfect tense bracket: werden takes the position haben/sein occupies there, and the participle still goes to the end.

Describing Rules and Procedures

  • In diesem Gebäude wird der Müll nach Papier, Glas und Restmüll sortiert.In this building, trash is sorted into paper, glass, and general waste.wird ... sortiert = present passive, subject "der Müll"
  • Der Vertrag wird automatisch verlängert, wenn niemand kündigt.The contract is automatically renewed if nobody cancels it.

Agent Phrases with von and durch — Receptive

A passive sentence may name who or what performs the action using von (a person or agent, with the dative) or durch (a means or cause, with the accusative). You should recognize these patterns in reading and listening, but at this stage you are not required to produce them independently.

Baustein — Passiv mit Agens (rezeptiv)
Subjekt + werden + [von/durch + Agens] + ... + Partizip.
von + Dativ = person/agent; durch + Akkusativ = means/cause
Der Anschluss wird von einem Techniker installiert.
The connection is installed by a technician.
  • Der Müll wird von der Stadt jeden Mittwoch abgeholt.The trash is collected by the city every Wednesday.
  • Die Verzögerung wurde durch technische Probleme verursacht.The delay was caused by technical problems.

Choosing Active, man, or Passive

All three options can describe the same event, but they shift the focus differently.

ConstructionExampleFocus
ActiveDie Hausverwaltung sortiert den Müll.Names the specific agent directly
manMan sortiert den Müll nach Papier und Glas.General, unspecified "people/you," spoken register
PassiveDer Müll wird nach Papier und Glas sortiert.Focus on the process itself, agent unspecified or unimportant

Passive is common in written notices and instructions; man is common in spoken explanations. Avoid overusing passive in every register — much everyday spoken German prefers man or an active sentence.

Modal Passive — Recognition Only

A modal verb can combine with a passive infinitive to express what must, can, or should be done to something: modal + Partizip + werden, all at the end of the clause. You will meet this pattern in reading texts and notices from this chapter onward, but you are not required to produce it yet.

  • Das Formular muss bis Freitag eingereicht werden.The form must be submitted by Friday.

Model Dialogue

Context: Julia asks Markus and Anna about a few practical processes in her new building.

Julia:Wie wird der Müll hier eigentlich sortiert?How is the trash actually sorted here?
Markus:Er wird nach Papier, Glas und Restmüll getrennt, und er wird von der Stadt jeden Mittwoch abgeholt.It gets separated into paper, glass, and general waste, and it gets collected by the city every Wednesday.
Julia:Und der Internetanschluss? Muss der auch installiert werden, oder ist er schon da?And the internet connection? Does it also have to be installed, or is it already there?
Anna:Bei mir wurde er von einem Techniker installiert, aber das wird normalerweise vorher vereinbart.For me it was installed by a technician, but that's usually arranged in advance.
Julia:Gut zu wissen. Und der Mietvertrag — wird der automatisch verlängert?Good to know. And the lease — does it get renewed automatically?
Markus:Meistens schon, aber die Kündigung muss bis zu einer bestimmten Frist eingereicht werden, wenn du nicht verlängern willst.Usually yes, but the cancellation has to be submitted by a certain deadline if you don't want to renew.

Schreiben

Writing Workshop — Ein Verfahren beschreiben

Write a short explanation (120–180 words) of a process or set of rules you know well — for example, how recycling works where you live, how a request gets handled at your workplace, or how a library return process works.

  1. Introduce the process. State briefly what the process accomplishes.
  2. Describe the first step. Use a present-passive sentence.
  3. Describe a second step. Use a present-passive sentence, and try including an agent phrase with von or durch.
  4. Mention a rule or deadline. Use man or an active sentence if the passive would feel unnatural here.
  5. Revise using the checklist below.

Checkliste — Editing Checklist:

  • At least two accurate present-passive sentences.
  • Passive, man, and active are each used where they fit best, not passive everywhere.
  • Every passive sentence has werden in second position and the participle at the end.
  • Any agent phrase uses von + dative or durch + accusative correctly.
  • All nouns are capitalized; umlauts and ß are used correctly.

Culture and Communication

Culture and Communication — Passive Voice in Institutional German

Passive voice appears frequently in German notices, house rules, contracts, and administrative instructions, because these texts often care more about what happens and what is required than about who specifically carries it out on a given occasion. This does not mean German speakers use passive constantly in everyday conversation — spoken German commonly prefers man or an active sentence with a named subject. Recognizing where passive is natural, and where it would sound stiff, is part of developing register awareness.

Regional Note — Waste Sorting and Housing Procedures Vary

Waste-sorting categories, collection schedules, and lease-renewal procedures differ between buildings, cities, and countries within the DACH region, and even between individual landlords within the same city. Any specific recycling rules, notice periods, or contract terms used in real communication should be checked against a current, reliable local source rather than assumed from one example.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Using sein instead of werden to form the passive

Error: *Der Müll ist jeden Mittwoch abgeholt.

Correction: The present passive requires werden, not sein: Der Müll wird jeden Mittwoch abgeholt. A form of sein plus participle instead describes a resulting state, which is a different construction not covered at this stage.

Leaving the participle out of final position

Error: *Der Müll wird sortiert jeden Mittwoch.

Correction: The participle belongs at the end of the clause, just as in the perfect tense bracket: Der Müll wird jeden Mittwoch sortiert.

Confusing von and durch in agent phrases

Error: *Die Verzögerung wurde durch dem Techniker verursacht.

Correction: von + dative names a person or agent; durch + accusative names a means or cause: Die Verzögerung wurde durch technische Probleme verursacht. or Der Anschluss wurde von einem Techniker installiert.

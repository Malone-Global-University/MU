Präzision und Register

Precision and Register Review · Speak and Write German: Professional, Academic, and Cultural Communication · Volume IV, Chapter 1

Chapter Overview and Objectives

Chapter 1 opens Volume IV, but it introduces no new grammar system. Tobias has recently moved from his old department to a new position coordinating projects across several German-speaking offices, including colleagues in Vienna and Zurich. Before he begins drafting professional correspondence, sitting in meetings, and reading academic and business texts at a higher level, he — and you — must take stock of the case, clause, and register control built across Volumes I through III. This chapter is a diagnostic and revision chapter: it audits existing accuracy, sharpens awareness of audience and tone, and establishes the personal control plan that Chapters 2 through 4 will build on.

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify recurring case, article, and adjective-ending errors in your own German writing.
  2. Review and correctly punctuate subordinate, relative, and infinitive clauses in connected prose.
  3. Distinguish informal, neutral, and formal register and select the appropriate one for a given audience and purpose.
  4. Edit a short text for accuracy, clarity, and consistent register.
  5. Discuss tone choices with a partner or instructor using accurate metalanguage.
  6. Create a personal accuracy and register control plan to guide your work in this volume.

Opening Scenario

Tobias has just started a new role coordinating projects between his Munich office, a Vienna partner team, and colleagues in Zurich. His new supervisor, Frau Lindqvist, asks him to review a short internal note he drafted last week before it goes out to the whole coordination group. As she reads it, she notices small case slips, a comma missing before a relative clause, and a sentence that drifts from neutral into overly casual phrasing halfway through. Rather than simply correcting the note for him, she asks Tobias to read it again himself, mark what he would change, and explain why. Before Tobias can communicate precisely and appropriately across three national contexts, he needs a clear, honest picture of exactly where his German is strong and where it still needs attention.

Grammatik

Grammar Focus: Ein Kontrollsystem

This chapter introduces no new grammar. Instead, it organizes a control system — a short checklist of the case, clause, and punctuation points most likely to slip under the pressure of longer or more formal writing, drawn from systems you have already built in Volumes I through III.

Grammar Note — What to Audit First

Case and article accuracy: nominative, accusative, and dative marking on articles, adjectives, and pronouns, especially in longer noun phrases where the head noun is far from its article.

Clause hierarchy: verb-second order in main clauses and verb-final order in subordinate and relative clauses, especially when several clauses are combined in one sentence.

Punctuation: commas before subordinate clauses, before relative clauses, and around infinitive clauses with zu, which are easy to drop when writing quickly.

Register consistency: whether address forms, vocabulary choice, and sentence length stay appropriate to the audience across an entire text, rather than drifting partway through.

  • Der Entwurf, den ich am Montag geschrieben habe, muss noch überarbeitet werden, bevor er an die ganze Gruppe geht. The draft that I wrote on Monday still needs to be revised before it goes out to the whole group. relative clause + passive + subordinate clause, each punctuated correctly
  • Ich würde vorschlagen, dass wir den Ton an einer Stelle etwas formeller gestalten, weil die Zielgruppe größer geworden ist. I would suggest that we make the tone somewhat more formal at one point, because the target audience has grown larger. Konjunktiv II + dass-clause + weil-clause, both verb-final
Frequent Confusion — Register Drift Within One Text

Error: *Liebe Kolleginnen und Kollegen, wir müssen das Meeting verschieben, weil ich einfach keine Zeit hab diese Woche, sorry! (formal opening, informal middle, casual closing — three registers in one short message)

Correction: Liebe Kolleginnen und Kollegen, wir müssen das Meeting leider verschieben, da mein Terminplan diese Woche keinen passenden Zeitpunkt zulässt. Ich bitte um Ihr Verständnis. — keep tone, vocabulary, and sentence structure consistent from opening to close.

Modelldialog

Model Dialogue

Setting: Frau Lindqvist's office, shortly after Tobias has joined the coordination team. Context: she reviews his draft note with him rather than simply correcting it herself.

Frau Lindqvist bespricht Tobias' Entwurf mit ihm, bevor er an die Koordinationsgruppe geschickt wird.
Frau Lindqvist: Lesen Sie den zweiten Absatz noch einmal. Fällt Ihnen etwas auf? Read the second paragraph once more. Does anything strike you?
Tobias: Ich glaube, hier fehlt ein Komma vor dem Relativsatz. Und der Ton wird hier plötzlich lockerer als am Anfang. I think there's a comma missing before the relative clause here. And the tone suddenly gets more casual here than at the beginning.
Frau Lindqvist: Genau. Für wen ist diese Nachricht eigentlich gedacht? Exactly. Who is this message actually intended for?
Tobias: Für die ganze Koordinationsgruppe, also auch für Kolleginnen und Kollegen, die ich noch gar nicht persönlich kenne. For the whole coordination group, so also for colleagues I don't even know personally yet.
Frau Lindqvist: Dann würde ich den Ton durchgehend etwas formeller halten. Überarbeiten Sie den Absatz, und schicken Sie mir den neuen Entwurf. Then I would keep the tone somewhat more formal throughout. Revise the paragraph, and send me the new draft.

Listening Component

Ask a partner, instructor, or text-to-speech tool to read the following short passage aloud. Listen once for the overall content, then again to notice where register is held consistently.

Ein Gespräch über einen Entwurf — Listening Script

Frau Lindqvist bat Tobias, seinen Entwurf noch einmal genau zu lesen, bevor er ihn an die ganze Koordinationsgruppe schickte. Sie fragte ihn, ob ihm etwas auffalle, und Tobias bemerkte selbst, dass im zweiten Absatz ein Komma fehlte und dass der Ton dort plötzlich lockerer wurde. Verständnisfragen

3. Identify one relative clause and one subordinate clause in the passage, and explain what role each plays in the sentence.

Speaking Component

Aufgabe: Einen Entwurf besprechen

Work with a partner. One of you plays a supervisor reviewing a short draft message with a colleague; the other plays the colleague who wrote it. Discuss at least one case or article correction and one register adjustment, following the model dialogue above.

Rolle: Vorgesetzte/Vorgesetzter

  • Ask an open question about what the colleague notices in the draft.
  • Confirm or gently correct the colleague's observation.
  • Ask who the intended audience is and why register matters here.

Rolle: Kollegin/Kollege

  • Identify at least one case, article, or punctuation issue yourself.
  • Explain in your own words why the tone should be adjusted.
  • Agree on what you will revise before resubmitting.

Bewertungshinweise — Speaking Criteria

  • Accurate metalanguage for discussing errors and register.
  • At least one case/article correction and one register observation, clearly stated.
  • Appropriate, consistent register for a workplace review conversation.
  • Comprehensible, natural pronunciation and pacing.

Solo alternative: Record both sides of the exchange yourself, pausing between turns to plan your response.

Schreiben

Writing Component

Revise the short draft note below. It contains at least one case or article error, one missing comma, and one shift in register. Rewrite it as a consistent, formal message of four to six sentences suitable for a mixed group of colleagues you do not all know personally.

Zu überarbeitender Entwurf

Hallo zusammen, ich schick euch den Bericht der wir letzte Woche besprochen haben. Bitte schaut ihn euch bis Freitag an und schreibt mir kurz Bescheid, ob alles passt. Danke schon mal, meldet euch bei Fragen!

  1. Read the draft once and mark every case, article, punctuation, or register issue you notice.
  2. Decide on one consistent, appropriate register for a mixed professional audience.
  3. Rewrite the opening salutation and closing to match that register.
  4. Correct case, article, and comma placement throughout.
  5. Read your revised text aloud to check that the tone does not drift.
  6. Review your text against the checklist below.

Revision checklist:

  • Is the register consistent from the opening salutation to the closing?
  • Are case and article forms correct throughout, especially in longer noun phrases?
  • Is every relative and subordinate clause correctly punctuated?
  • Would this message be equally appropriate for a colleague you know well and one you have never met?

Culture and Communication

Culture and Communication — Precision as Professional Courtesy

In many German-speaking professional settings, careful attention to accuracy and register in written communication is understood as a form of courtesy toward colleagues, not as excessive formality. A message that maintains a clear, consistent tone for its full length signals that the writer has considered the whole audience, including colleagues the writer does not yet know well.

Regional Note — Coordinating Across Offices

As Tobias's new role illustrates, written coordination across German, Austrian, and Swiss offices is common in DACH-region workplaces. Expectations about formality, directness, and email conventions can vary somewhat by country, sector, and individual workplace culture, so a careful writer checks local norms rather than assuming they are identical everywhere.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Letting register slip under time pressure

Error: Starting a message formally, then shifting into casual phrasing when writing quickly or when the underlying working relationship feels informal.

Correction: Decide on the register before drafting, based on the full intended audience, and hold that register consistently from opening to closing.

Losing case marking in longer noun phrases

Error: *Wir haben der neue Kollege schon informiert. (article and adjective ending do not agree with the accusative object)

Correction: Wir haben den neuen Kollegen schon informiert. — check article and adjective ending against the case the verb requires, especially when the noun phrase is long or unfamiliar.

Dropping commas before relative and subordinate clauses

Error: *Der Bericht der gestern ankam muss noch überarbeitet werden. (no comma before the relative clause)

Correction: Der Bericht, der gestern ankam, muss noch überarbeitet werden. — punctuate every relative and subordinate clause clearly, even in short sentences.

Answer Key

Guided Practice — Model Answers

  1. Der Bericht, den ich gestern gelesen habe, war sehr hilfreich. (comma added before the relative clause; der corrected to accusative den)
  2. Wir haben den neuen Kollegen schon vorgestellt. (accusative article and adjective ending, weak noun ending on Kollegen)
  3. Sehr geehrte Frau Lindqvist, ich schicke Ihnen den Entwurf morgen. (informal schick replaced with the full formal verb form schicke)

Listening Component — Comprehension Questions

  1. Ein fehlendes Komma vor einem Relativsatz und ein plötzlich lockerer werdender Ton.
  2. Weil die Nachricht auch an Kolleginnen und Kollegen geht, die Tobias noch nicht persönlich kennt.
  3. Relative clause: Kolleginnen und Kollegen, die er noch nicht persönlich kenne; subordinate clause: dass der Ton deshalb durchgehend formeller bleiben solle — the relative clause specifies who the audience is, and the subordinate clause reports Frau Lindqvist's reasoning.

Reading Component — Comprehension Questions

  1. Er bittet um Rückmeldung zum Entwurf bis Freitag.
  2. Er erklärt Fachbegriffe klar, da einige Kolleginnen und Kollegen den Text zum ersten Mal sehen.
  3. Relative clause: den Sie im Anhang finden — the comma marks the clause boundary clearly, and the accusative den correctly agrees with der Entwurf as the object of finden.

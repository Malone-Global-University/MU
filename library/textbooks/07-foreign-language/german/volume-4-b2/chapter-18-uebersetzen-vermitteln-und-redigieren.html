Übersetzen, Vermitteln und Redigieren

Translation, Mediation, and Editing · Speak and Write German: Professional, Academic, and Cultural Communication · Volume IV, Chapter 18

Chapter Overview and Objectives

Chapter 18 continues directly from Tobias's work on the district cultural office's funding notice. Frau Aksoy now needs an English-language summary of the same notice for a partner organization in the neighborhood's sister-city network, and she also wants Tobias to check an English volunteer's draft German announcement before it goes out to residents. Both tasks require more than translating word for word: Tobias must recognize where English and German simply do not work the same way, decide what information a reader on each side actually needs, and edit a non-native draft so that it sounds like natural German rather than English wearing German words. This chapter teaches learners to mediate meaning between English and German rather than transferring it mechanically, to recognize common patterns of non-equivalence, and to edit German text for accuracy and naturalness with a specific audience in mind.

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Distinguish translation, mediation, and editing as related but different tasks.
  2. Identify common patterns of non-equivalence between English and German, including false friends, differing case/preposition frames, and differing register conventions.
  3. Produce a mediated English-language summary of a German source text that serves a specific reader rather than reproducing every sentence.
  4. Edit a non-native German draft for accuracy, naturalness, and audience fit without rewriting it beyond recognition.
  5. Explain specific editing and mediation choices using accurate grammatical terminology.
  6. Avoid claiming certified, legal, or perfectly equivalent translation where none is possible.

Opening Scenario

Frau Aksoy asks Tobias for two things this week. First, the sister-city partner organization abroad needs to understand the funding decision from Chapter 17, but they do not need a line-by-line translation — they need to know what was decided, what the neighborhood association must now do, and by when. Second, an English-speaking volunteer named Priya has drafted a short German announcement for the community bulletin board, and although her German is quite good, a few sentences clearly started as English sentences with German words dropped in. Tobias has to mediate the funding notice into a useful English summary and edit Priya's announcement so that it reads as natural German without erasing her voice or intentions.

  1. Distinguish translating, mediating, and editing as separate goals.
  2. Identify non-equivalence between English and German in real text.
  3. Produce a reader-focused English mediation of a German source.
  4. Edit a non-native German draft for naturalness and accuracy.

Aussprache

Pronunciation Focus: Reading Mediated and Edited Texts Aloud

Pronunciation and Delivery — Hearing Interference from English

One of the most useful editing techniques is reading a German draft aloud, especially a draft written by a non-native speaker. Sentences transferred too directly from English word order or stress patterns often sound noticeably "off" before a listener can even say why, because German sentence stress usually falls on the new or important information near the end of the clause, while a English-influenced draft may carry stress patterns that highlight the wrong word. Reading a sentence aloud slowly and asking which word naturally receives the most stress, then comparing that to which word actually carries the new information, is a fast way to catch interference errors that silent reading misses.

This technique applies stress and rhythm skills built in earlier volumes; it does not introduce a new pronunciation system.

Grammatik

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

Translation, Mediation, and Editing as Different Tasks

A translation aims to render a source text into another language as completely and accurately as reasonably possible. Mediation reshapes the content of a source text for a specific reader's needs, which often means summarizing, reordering, or omitting details the reader does not need. Editing takes a text already written in the target language and improves its accuracy and naturalness without changing its author's intent or voice more than necessary. Confusing these tasks leads to poor outcomes: a translation that ignores the reader's actual needs, a mediation that claims to be a full translation, or an edit that quietly rewrites someone else's meaning.

Baustein — Building Block
Ausgangstext (DE) → Vermittlung (EN, gekürzt und zielgruppengerecht) vs. Übersetzung (EN, vollständig)
mediation selects and reorders content for the reader's purpose; translation aims at completeness
Die Kulturbehörde hat die Förderung bewilligt, verlangt aber jährliche Berichterstattung. → The funding was approved, with one condition: an annual report.
Mediation keeps the decision and the condition, drops procedural detail the partner organization does not need.

Non-Equivalence Between English and German

Non-equivalence occurs where English and German do not map onto each other one-to-one. Common sources of non-equivalence include false friends, case and preposition frames that do not match their English counterparts, compound nouns with no single English equivalent, and register conventions that differ between the languages.

  • bekommen ≠ to become (false friend: bekommen means "to receive/get") A German learner writing "sie wird das Geld" to mean "she gets the money" has confused become and receive. false friend requiring lexical correction, not grammar correction
  • warten auf + Akk. vs. to wait for Ich warte auf die Antwort. — I am waiting for the answer. preposition frame differs even though the general meaning matches
  • die Berichterstattung → no single English noun; usually rendered as "reporting" or "the reporting requirement" depending on context compound/derived noun with no exact one-word English equivalent requires paraphrase rather than word-for-word substitution
Grammar Note — Editing for Case and Preposition Errors from English Interference

A common interference error is supplying an English preposition pattern with German words, such as *warten für instead of warten auf, or using the wrong case after a two-way preposition because the English sentence gave no case cue at all. When editing, check every preposition against its case frame rather than against its English translation.

Editing Without Erasing the Author's Voice

Editing a non-native draft means correcting what is actually wrong or unnatural while leaving accurate, natural choices in place, even if an editor would have phrased something differently. Over-editing replaces a correct but plain sentence with a more sophisticated one merely because the editor prefers it; this is not the task.

  • Draft: *Die Feier wird stattfinden am Samstag. → Edited: Die Feier findet am Samstag statt. Draft: The celebration will take place on Saturday (word order and tense both need correction). Edited: present tense, correct separable-verb bracket. correction of verb-bracket placement, not a stylistic rewrite
  • Draft: Alle sind herzlich eingeladen. → Edited: unchanged All are warmly invited. (already natural; no correction needed) correct sentence left as written; editing is not automatic rewriting
Common Mediation/Editing Error — Treating Every Difference as an Error

Error: An editor rewrites Alle sind herzlich eingeladen as Wir würden uns sehr freuen, Sie begrüßen zu dürfen simply because it sounds more elaborate.

Correction: Leave the original sentence if it is accurate and appropriate for the register; reserve changes for genuine errors or clear register mismatches with the stated audience.

Hedging and Disclaiming Limits in Mediation

Mediated summaries and edited texts should be honest about what they are. A mediation is not a certified translation; an edit is not a guarantee of native-level perfection. Learners should be able to state these limits clearly to the people who will use their work.

  • Dies ist eine sinngemäße Zusammenfassung, keine beglaubigte Übersetzung. This is a summary in terms of meaning, not a certified translation. explicit disclaimer using sinngemäß and beglaubigt
  • Ich habe den Text redigiert, aber eine muttersprachliche Prüfung ist bei wichtigen Dokumenten trotzdem empfehlenswert. I have edited the text, but a native-speaker review is still advisable for important documents. honest limit-setting using empfehlenswert (advisable)
Modelltext

Model Text: Mediated Summary and Edited Announcement

Tobias's English mediation of the funding notice for the sister-city partner, followed by Priya's original draft announcement and Tobias's edited version.

Vermittlung The cultural office has approved funding for the courtyard project. The one condition is an annual report on progress; the office will review how the project has been carried out after twelve months. (Mediation: two sentences carry the decision and the condition; procedural wording from the original notice is dropped as unnecessary for this reader.)
Entwurf Die Feier wird stattfinden am Samstag im Hof. Wir sind warten für gutes Wetter, aber falls es regnet, wir werden es in den Gemeinschaftsraum verschieben. Bitte bringen Sie ein Gericht für teilen mit! (Draft with three interference errors: separable-verb bracket, wrong preposition after warten, and an English-style infinitive construction.)
Redigiert Die Feier findet am Samstag im Hof statt. Wir hoffen auf gutes Wetter, aber falls es regnet, verschieben wir sie in den Gemeinschaftsraum. Bitte bringen Sie ein Gericht zum Teilen mit! (Edited: correct separable-verb bracket, hoffen auf instead of warten für, and zum + infinitive-as-noun instead of für + infinitive.)

Rolle: Vermittelnde Person

  • Explain which details a reader abroad actually needs from the German source.
  • Justify at least one omission or reordering decision.

  • Clearly distinguishes what a mediation keeps, shortens, or omits, and why.

Culture and Communication

Culture and Communication — Mediation Serves the Reader, Not the Source

Good mediation is judged by whether the target reader gets what they need, not by how closely the target text mirrors the source sentence by sentence. Community organizations, sister-city partnerships, and multilingual neighborhoods regularly rely on mediated summaries rather than full translations, and treating mediation as a lesser or careless version of translation misunderstands its purpose.

Culture and Communication — Editing Non-Native Writing with Respect

Volunteers, newcomers, and multilingual community members regularly write German for public use, and editing their drafts is a routine, valuable task rather than a correction of failure. Respectful editing distinguishes genuine errors from acceptable variation and communicates corrections in a way that supports the writer's continued participation and confidence.

Common Mistakes to Recheck

Translating instead of mediating

Error: A learner translates every sentence of a long German source text word for word for a reader who only needed the decision and the deadline.

Correction: Identify the reader's actual need first, then select and reorganize content accordingly, dropping procedural detail that does not serve that reader.

Claiming certified or perfect equivalence

Error: *Dies ist eine offizielle Übersetzung. when no certification process has taken place.

Correction: Dies ist eine sinngemäße Zusammenfassung, keine beglaubigte Übersetzung. states the limit honestly.

Over-editing a correct sentence

Error: Rewriting an already natural, correct sentence into a more elaborate one merely because the editor prefers a different style.

Correction: Leave accurate, natural sentences unchanged; reserve edits for genuine errors or clear audience/register mismatches.

Answer Key

Guided Practice

  1. Sample answer: The mediation drops procedural detail the sister-city partner does not need and keeps only the decision and the reporting condition, because a full translation would include information irrelevant to that reader's purpose.
  2. wird stattfinden am Samstag (separable-verb bracket placed incorrectly instead of findet ... statt); warten für (wrong preposition; should be hoffen auf in context); für teilen (English-style infinitive construction; should be zum Teilen).
  3. Ich interessiere mich fürs Deutschlernen. or Ich interessiere mich dafür, Deutsch zu lernen. — error type: infinitive/nominalization construction transferred too directly from English "interested in learning."
  4. Der Bericht ist für die Behörde wichtig. or Es ist wichtig, dass die Behörde den Bericht liest. — error type: English-style "important for X to do Y" construction has no direct German equivalent and must be restructured.
  5. Sample answer: Dies ist eine sinngemäße Zusammenfassung, keine beglaubigte Übersetzung.

Listening Practice — Comprehension Questions

  1. The partner organization needs to know that funding was approved and that an annual report is required; procedural details such as how long the application took or exactly which data were considered can reasonably be left out.
  2. Sample answer: Als Auflage gilt eine jährliche Berichterstattung über den Fortschritt des Projekts resists word-for-word translation because English would not naturally nominalize "reporting" the same way; a natural English rendering restructures it as "one condition is that we report annually on the project's progress."
  3. Sample answer: "We hope our project might interest you too, and we would welcome the chance to exchange ideas" — sinngemäß rather than word-for-word.

Reading Practice — Comprehension Questions

  1. Es gibt Musik, Essen und ein Programm für Kinder; wer helfen möchte, soll Frau Aksoy kontaktieren.
  2. Errors: froh, ankündigen (missing zu + infinitive: should be froh, unser Sommerfest ankündigen zu können); wird stattfinden (separable-verb bracket: should be findet ... statt); eingeladen für kommen (should be eingeladen zu kommen); helfen möchten für ... organisieren (should be helfen möchten, das Fest zu organisieren); hoffen für (should be hoffen auf).

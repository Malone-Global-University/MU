Professionelle Korrespondenz

Professional Correspondence · Speak and Write German: Professional, Academic, and Cultural Communication · Volume IV, Chapter 2

Chapter Overview and Objectives

Chapter 2 builds directly on the accuracy and register audit of Chapter 1. Tobias's revised note went out to the coordination group, and it worked: colleagues in Vienna and Zurich responded well to its steady, professional tone. Now Frau Lindqvist asks him to take on a fuller correspondence workload of his own — requesting information, confirming arrangements, following up when someone has not replied, and occasionally declining a request as diplomatically as possible. This chapter teaches the language of professional email correspondence: refined formal address, nominal style, polite subjunctive requests, and the prepositional verbs that make correspondence sound natural rather than translated.

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Write a formal or semi-formal email that opens, develops, and closes in one consistent register.
  2. Make requests, confirm arrangements, and follow up on unanswered messages using appropriate phrasing.
  3. Decline a request politely without causing offense.
  4. Use Konjunktiv II forms to soften requests and suggestions.
  5. Use common prepositional verbs and nominal constructions typical of professional correspondence.
  6. Edit a professional email chain for tone, accuracy, and completeness.

Opening Scenario

A week after his revised note went out, Tobias receives a reply from a colleague in Zurich, Frau Baumgartner, asking for additional details about a shared budget line before she can confirm her team's participation in an upcoming project phase. At the same time, a colleague in Vienna, Herr Wibmer, has not answered an earlier request at all, and the coordination timeline is starting to tighten. Frau Lindqvist suggests that Tobias handle both situations himself: answer Frau Baumgartner's questions clearly, send a polite follow-up to Herr Wibmer, and — since a separate, unrelated request has come in from another department that the project genuinely cannot accommodate — draft a courteous decline. Managing all three at once will test whether Tobias can hold a consistent, professional register not just in a single message, but across an entire correspondence thread.

  1. Review the vocabulary for requests, confirmations, follow-ups, and declines.
  2. Study the model dialogue, in which Frau Lindqvist and Tobias plan his responses together.
  3. Complete the Listening, Speaking, Reading, and Writing components in order.
  4. Finish by producing your own short professional email sequence.
Wortschatz

Vocabulary: Correspondence and Requests

This chapter activates vocabulary for the core moves of professional correspondence: asking, confirming, following up, and declining, along with the nouns needed to talk about the correspondence itself.

Die Korrespondenz

  • die Anfrage, die Anfragen — inquiry, request
  • die Bestätigung, die Bestätigungen — confirmation
  • die Erinnerung, die Erinnerungen — reminder
  • die Absage, die Absagen — decline, cancellation

Bitten und Vorschlagen

  • bitten um (+ Akkusativ) — to ask for something
  • vorschlagen — to suggest, propose
  • der Vorschlag, die Vorschläge — proposal, suggestion
  • sich erkundigen nach (+ Dativ) — to inquire about

Bestätigen und Ablehnen

  • bestätigen — to confirm
  • ablehnen — to decline, reject
  • leider — unfortunately
  • das Verständnis — understanding

Termine und Fristen

  • der Termin, die Termine — appointment, deadline
  • die Frist, die Fristen — deadline, time limit
  • verschieben — to postpone, move
  • rechtzeitig — in time, punctually

All active nouns appear with article and plural, and each verb is shown with its case or preposition frame where one is required.

Grammatik

Grammar Focus: Höflichkeit und Nominalstil

Professional correspondence relies on three things you already control at a basic level and now must refine: formal address with correct capitalization, Konjunktiv II forms that soften a request or suggestion, and a more nominal style that reads as measured rather than blunt.

Grammar Note — Refining Formal Address

You have used formal Sie since Volume I. In professional correspondence, remember that Sie, Ihnen, and Ihr/Ihre are always capitalized, in every position in the sentence, including mid-sentence and inside subordinate clauses.

Ich schreibe Ihnen, um mich nach dem aktuellen Stand Ihres Projekts zu erkundigen. — I am writing to you to ask about the current status of your project.

Building Block — Konjunktiv II for Polite Requests
Ich würde + Infinitiv  |  Könnten Sie + Infinitiv  |  Wäre es möglich, ... zu + Infinitiv
Konjunktiv II softens a request or suggestion; it does not change its content, only its tone.

Ich würde Sie bitten, mir die fehlenden Zahlen bis Freitag zu schicken.

I would ask you to send me the missing figures by Friday.

Könnten Sie mir kurz mitteilen, ob der Termin für Sie passt?

Could you let me know briefly whether the appointment works for you?

Wäre es möglich, das Treffen auf nächste Woche zu verschieben?

Would it be possible to postpone the meeting to next week?

  • Ich bitte Sie um eine kurze Rückmeldung bis Donnerstag. I ask you for a brief reply by Thursday. bitten um + Akkusativ
  • Nach Rücksprache mit meiner Kollegin muss ich Ihre Anfrage leider ablehnen. After consulting with my colleague, I unfortunately have to decline your request. nominal opening (Nach Rücksprache mit ...) softens a direct refusal
  • Da ich bisher noch keine Antwort erhalten habe, erlaube ich mir, freundlich nachzufragen. Since I have not yet received a reply, I am taking the liberty of following up politely. da-clause + infinitive clause with zu, typical follow-up formula
Grammar Note — Nominal Style in Correspondence

Professional German often opens a sentence with a noun phrase rather than a full clause, especially to introduce context before a request or decision: Nach Rücksprache mit ..., Aufgrund der aktuellen Terminlage ..., Im Hinblick auf unser letztes Gespräch .... This nominal opening lets the writer state the reasoning before the request, which softens the message overall.

Frequent Confusion — Requests That Sound Like Commands

Error: *Schicken Sie mir die Zahlen bis Freitag. as an opening request with no softening (grammatically correct, but abrupt for a first request in a professional context)

Correction: Ich würde Sie bitten, mir die Zahlen bis Freitag zu schicken. or Könnten Sie mir die Zahlen bis Freitag schicken? — an imperative is not wrong, but Konjunktiv II or a question form is usually more appropriate for an initial professional request.

Modelldialog

Model Dialogue

Setting: Frau Lindqvist's office. Context: she and Tobias plan how to answer Frau Baumgartner, follow up with Herr Wibmer, and draft a decline for the unrelated request.

Frau Lindqvist und Tobias besprechen drei anstehende E-Mails.
Frau Lindqvist: Frau Baumgartner möchte mehr Informationen zur Budgetlinie. Was würden Sie ihr schreiben? Frau Baumgartner would like more information about the budget line. What would you write to her?
Tobias: Ich würde ihr die Zahlen schicken und sie bitten, uns bis Freitag zu bestätigen, ob ihr Team teilnehmen kann. I would send her the figures and ask her to confirm to us by Friday whether her team can participate.
Frau Lindqvist: Gut. Und Herr Wibmer hat noch gar nicht geantwortet. Wie erinnern Sie ihn höflich? Good. And Herr Wibmer hasn't answered at all yet. How do you remind him politely?
Tobias: Ich erlaube mir, freundlich nachzufragen, da ich bisher keine Rückmeldung erhalten habe. I'm taking the liberty of following up politely, since I haven't received a reply so far.
Frau Lindqvist: Und die Anfrage aus der anderen Abteilung, die wir nicht erfüllen können? And the request from the other department that we can't fulfill?
Tobias: Nach Rücksprache mit Ihnen muss ich die Anfrage leider ablehnen, aber ich möchte einen alternativen Ansprechpartner vorschlagen. After consulting with you, I unfortunately have to decline the request, but I'd like to suggest an alternative contact person.

Sprechen

Speaking Component

Aufgabe: Eine Anfrage stellen und ablehnen

Work with a partner. One of you plays a colleague making a request; the other plays a colleague who can only partly help and must decline part of the request politely, following the model dialogue above.

Rolle: Anfragende Person

  • Make a polite request using Konjunktiv II or a question form.
  • Give a deadline or timeframe for the response.
  • Respond appropriately if part of the request is declined.

Rolle: Antwortende Person

  • Confirm what you can do, and explain what you cannot.
  • Use a nominal opening to introduce your reasoning before declining.
  • Suggest an alternative if possible.

Bewertungshinweise — Speaking Criteria

  • At least one Konjunktiv II request and one nominal opening, clearly used.
  • A polite, complete decline that includes a reason.
  • Consistent formal register throughout the exchange.
  • Comprehensible, natural pronunciation and pacing.

Solo alternative: Record both sides of the exchange yourself, pausing between turns to plan your response.

Schreiben

Writing Component

Write a short professional email chain of two messages: (1) a polite reply to Frau Baumgartner's request for information, including a request for her confirmation by a stated deadline, and (2) a courteous decline of the unrelated request from the other department, including a nominal opening and, if possible, an alternative suggestion.

Kontext für Nachricht 1

Frau Baumgartner (Zürich) hat nach den genauen Zahlen für die Budgetlinie gefragt, bevor sie die Teilnahme ihres Teams bestätigen kann.

Kontext für Nachricht 2

Eine andere Abteilung hat um zusätzliche Unterstützung gebeten, die das Projektteam derzeit nicht leisten kann.

  1. Decide on one consistent, formal register for both messages.
  2. Draft Message 1: provide the requested information and make a clear, polite request for confirmation with a deadline.
  3. Draft Message 2: open with a nominal phrase, state the decline clearly but kindly, and offer an alternative if you can.
  4. Check that formal Sie, Ihnen, and Ihr/Ihre are capitalized throughout both messages.
  5. Read both messages aloud to check that the tone does not drift toward either bluntness or excessive apology.
  6. Review your text against the checklist below.

Revision checklist:

  • Does each message use a clear opening, development, and closing appropriate to a professional email?
  • Is at least one request softened with Konjunktiv II or a polite question form?
  • Does the decline use a nominal opening before stating the refusal?
  • Is formal address capitalized correctly throughout?

Culture and Communication

Culture and Communication — Openings and Closings Vary by Relationship

Email openings and closings in German-speaking professional contexts depend on the relationship and institution, not on a single fixed formula. Sehr geehrte/r ... suits a first contact or a more distant professional relationship, while Liebe/r ... is common once colleagues know each other, even in a formal register. Closings such as Mit freundlichen Grüßen are broadly safe defaults; shorter closings may be appropriate once correspondence becomes routine within a team.

Regional Note — Over-Formality Can Be Inappropriate

Learners sometimes assume that more formal language is always safer, but consistently over-formal correspondence with close colleagues can come across as distant or even suspicious of a good working relationship. Institutional norms, sector, and individual workplace culture in Germany, Austria, and Switzerland all shape how much formality a given exchange expects; a careful writer adjusts to the actual relationship rather than defaulting to maximum formality in every message.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Making a first request sound like a command

Error: *Schicken Sie mir die Unterlagen sofort. as an opening line to a colleague with no prior context.

Correction: Ich würde Sie bitten, mir die Unterlagen bei Gelegenheit zu schicken. — soften an initial request with Konjunktiv II or a polite question form.

Declining without any reasoning or alternative

Error: *Wir können das nicht machen. as a complete decline, with no context or alternative offered.

Correction: Nach Rücksprache mit meinem Team muss ich die Anfrage leider ablehnen. Gerne kann ich Ihnen jedoch eine andere Ansprechperson nennen. — give a brief reason and, where possible, a next step.

Forgetting to capitalize formal address mid-sentence

Error: *Ich schreibe ihnen, um sie an unseren Termin zu erinnern. (lowercase ihnen and sie read as third-person plural, not formal address)

Correction: Ich schreibe Ihnen, um Sie an unseren Termin zu erinnern. — capitalize every form of formal Sie regardless of position in the sentence.

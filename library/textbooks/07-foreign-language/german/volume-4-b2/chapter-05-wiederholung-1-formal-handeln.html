Wiederholung 1: Formal handeln

Unit I Assessment · Speak and Write German: Professional, Academic, and Cultural Communication · Volume IV, Chapter 5

Chapter Overview and Objectives

Chapter 5 closes Einheit I by bringing together the correspondence skills of Chapter 2, the meeting, negotiation, and presentation skills of Chapter 3, and the academic reading and discussion skills of Chapter 4. Tobias's three storylines converge: the partner university's research group has asked for a short written follow-up after the seminar, his team must confirm next steps from the negotiation in a formal email, and he will present a short integrated update to Frau Lindqvist that draws on all three contexts. This chapter introduces no new grammar. Instead, it asks you to deploy formal address, nominal correspondence style, Konjunktiv II diplomacy, complex noun phrases, and source attribution together, in service of one connected set of professional tasks, and to revise your own work for precision and audience fit.

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Write a formal follow-up email that confirms outcomes and next steps.
  2. Summarize a meeting or negotiation outcome accurately and diplomatically.
  3. Attribute an academic claim to its source while discussing it responsibly.
  4. Move between correspondence, spoken update, and academic summary without losing register control.
  5. Revise your own writing and speaking for precision, tone, and audience fit.
  6. Demonstrate integrated command of Unit I's active systems under assessment conditions.

Opening Scenario

Following the seminar discussion in Chapter 4, Frau Ostermann's research group has asked Tobias to send a short written summary of the discussion, including the objection about the study's small sample. At the same time, his own team is waiting for written confirmation of what was decided in the Chapter 3 negotiation over the shared project timeline. Frau Lindqvist asks Tobias to prepare a short integrated update — part email, part spoken summary — that ties the academic seminar, the negotiation outcome, and the correspondence together for her before she reports upward. This chapter's tasks assess whether Tobias, and you, can move confidently among these three formal contexts without losing precision or appropriate tone.

  1. Review the vocabulary and register patterns from Chapters 2 through 4.
  2. Study the model integrated exchange, which draws on all three chapters at once.
  3. Complete the Listening, Speaking, Reading, and Writing assessment tasks in order.
  4. Finish with the Practical Performance Task, which integrates all four skills.

Wiederholung der Grammatik

Grammar and Register Review

Chapter 5 introduces no new grammar. It reviews and consolidates the formal address, nominal style, Konjunktiv II, and attribution systems from Chapters 2 through 4, and asks you to combine them under assessment conditions.

Review — Three Registers, One Task
Korrespondenz: Wir bestätigen ...  |  Verhandlung: Wir könnten ... vorschlagen  |  Seminar: Der Studie zufolge ...
Each context has its own preferred frame: correspondence favors direct confirmation, negotiation favors Konjunktiv II diplomacy, and academic discussion favors explicit source attribution. Precision means choosing the frame that matches the context, not defaulting to one pattern everywhere.

Hiermit bestätigen wir den vereinbarten Zeitplan für das gemeinsame Projekt.

We hereby confirm the agreed timeline for the joint project.

Wir könnten den Starttermin um zwei Wochen verschieben, wenn das für beide Seiten passt.

We could shift the start date by two weeks if that works for both sides.

Der Autorin zufolge verbessert regelmäßiger Austausch die Entscheidungsfindung, auch wenn die Stichprobe klein war.

According to the author, regular exchange improves decision-making, even though the sample was small.

Grammar Note — Formal Address Across Contexts

Formal Sie, capitalized as Sie, Ihnen, and Ihr, remains constant across all three review contexts: email correspondence, meeting negotiation, and academic seminar discussion with unfamiliar colleagues. Only the surrounding vocabulary and sentence frames change. Before writing or speaking in this chapter, confirm that every verb agrees with the address form you have chosen and that capitalization is consistent throughout a single text.

Grammar Note — Attribution Still Applies in Informal Summaries

When Tobias reports the seminar's discussion inside a business email, he must still distinguish the author's claim from his own assessment, even though the audience and genre have changed. Attribution frames such as der Studie zufolge or laut der Autorin are not limited to academic writing; they travel into professional correspondence whenever a claim from an outside source is being reported.

  • Wir bedanken uns für das konstruktive Gespräch und bestätigen hiermit die im Meeting besprochenen Punkte. We thank you for the constructive conversation and hereby confirm the points discussed in the meeting. formal correspondence frame recycled from Chapter 2
  • Wie in der Besprechung vorgeschlagen, würden wir den zweiten Meilenstein um eine Woche verschieben. As proposed in the meeting, we would shift the second milestone by one week. Konjunktiv II diplomacy recycled from Chapter 3
  • Im Seminar merkte die Autorin selbst an, dass die Stichprobe klein war; dieser Punkt sollte bei der Interpretation der Ergebnisse berücksichtigt werden. In the seminar, the author herself noted that the sample was small; this point should be considered when interpreting the results. attribution frame recycled from Chapter 4, embedded in a professional summary
Frequent Confusion — Letting One Register Bleed Into Another

Error: *Ich glaube, kleinere Teams sind einfach besser, und wir sollten das Meeting so schnell wie möglich beenden. mixes an unattributed academic opinion into a business closing with no transition.

Correction: Der Studie zufolge könnten kleinere Teams unter bestimmten Umständen Vorteile haben; für unser Projekt schlage ich vor, dass wir das Meeting nun mit einer klaren Zusammenfassung der nächsten Schritte beenden. — attribute the claim, then shift explicitly back to the meeting register.

Modellaustausch

Model Integrated Exchange

Setting: Tobias's office, the day after the seminar. Context: Tobias and Frau Lindqvist review the three loose ends together before Tobias writes his follow-up materials.

Tobias und Frau Lindqvist besprechen die Nachbereitung von Korrespondenz, Verhandlung und Seminar.
Frau Lindqvist: Bevor Sie die E-Mail an die Forschungsgruppe schreiben, fassen Sie kurz zusammen, was im Seminar besprochen wurde. Before you write the email to the research group, briefly summarize what was discussed in the seminar.
Tobias: Die Autorin behauptet, dass regelmäßiger Austausch die Entscheidungsfindung verbessert, merkt aber selbst an, dass die Stichprobe klein war. The author claims that regular exchange improves decision-making, but herself notes that the sample was small.
Frau Lindqvist: Gut. Nehmen Sie diesen Punkt in die E-Mail auf, aber vermischen Sie ihn nicht mit der Bestätigung des Zeitplans aus der Verhandlung. Good. Include that point in the email, but don't mix it up with confirming the timeline from the negotiation.
Tobias: Verstanden. Ich schreibe zwei getrennte Absätze: einen zur Bestätigung des Zeitplans und einen zur Zusammenfassung des Seminars. Understood. I'll write two separate paragraphs: one confirming the timeline and one summarizing the seminar.
Frau Lindqvist: Genau. Und wenn Sie mir das Update später mündlich geben, achten Sie darauf, dass ich die drei Themen klar auseinanderhalten kann. Exactly. And when you give me the update orally later, make sure I can clearly keep the three topics apart.
Tobias: Ich werde jeden Punkt kurz einleiten, bevor ich ins Detail gehe, damit die Struktur klar bleibt. I'll briefly introduce each point before going into detail, so the structure stays clear.

Speaking Assessment

Aufgabe: Ein integriertes mündliches Update geben

Working with a partner or recording solo, deliver a short spoken update that covers all three Unit I contexts: confirm a meeting outcome, summarize an academic claim with attribution, and close with an appropriate professional tone. Use the Model Integrated Exchange as a structural guide.

Rolle: Berichterstatter/in (Tobias)

  • Introduce each of the three topics briefly before giving detail.
  • Confirm the negotiated timeline using a Konjunktiv II or direct confirmation frame.
  • Attribute the seminar's claim clearly to its source before evaluating it.
  • Close with an appropriately formal remark.

Rolle: Vorgesetzte/r (Frau Lindqvist)

  • Ask one clarifying question about the timeline.
  • Ask one clarifying question about the seminar's evidence or limitation.
  • Confirm that the three topics were kept clearly separate.

Bewertungshinweise — Speaking Criteria

  • All three Unit I contexts (correspondence outcome, negotiation outcome, academic claim) are addressed.
  • Attribution is used correctly when reporting the academic claim.
  • Formal address and register remain consistent throughout.
  • Transitions between topics are clearly marked.
  • Comprehensible, natural pronunciation and pacing.

Solo alternative: Record both roles yourself, pausing between turns to plan each response, or record only the Berichterstatter/in role as a short monologue update.

Culture and Communication

Culture and Communication — Audience Fit Across Registers

Professionals in German-speaking workplaces routinely move between institutional correspondence, negotiation, and academic or research contexts within a single day, often with the same partner organizations. Keeping these registers distinct — while still connecting them clearly for a supervisor or colleague who needs the full picture — is itself a valued professional skill, not merely a grammar exercise.

Culture and Communication — Precision Is Tied to Purpose

As in Chapter 1, precision in this chapter is not an abstract standard: it serves the practical purpose of preventing confusion when multiple topics, sources, and decisions must be communicated together. DACH institutional norms vary in exactly how much detail is expected in a follow-up email, so when in doubt, err toward explicit topic separation rather than assuming the reader will disentangle a mixed paragraph.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Merging unrelated topics into one paragraph

Error: *Die Autorin behauptet, dass regelmäßiger Austausch hilft, und wir haben uns auf eine Verschiebung des Meilensteins geeinigt. combines an academic claim and a negotiation outcome in a single run-on sentence with no clear boundary.

Correction: Present the two topics in separate paragraphs, each introduced with a short topic sentence, as modeled in the Reading Assessment email.

Dropping attribution when moving from academic to professional register

Error: *Regelmäßiger Austausch verbessert die Entscheidungsfindung. stated as settled fact inside a business email, with the source silently dropped.

Correction: Der Autorin zufolge verbessert regelmäßiger Austausch die Entscheidungsfindung. — attribution frames must travel with the claim into every register.

Inconsistent formal address within one text

Error: *Vielen Dank für dein Feedback. Wir bestätigen hiermit, dass Sie mit der neuen Frist einverstanden sind. mixes informal dein with formal Sie in the same message.

Correction: Vielen Dank für Ihr Feedback. Wir bestätigen hiermit, dass Sie mit der neuen Frist einverstanden sind. — choose one address form and apply it consistently throughout the text.

Nominalstil, Passiv und Modalität

Nominal Style, Passive, and Modality · Speak and Write German: Professional, Academic, and Cultural Communication · Volume IV, Chapter 17

Chapter Overview and Objectives

Chapter 17 continues Unit IV by turning from sentence architecture to sentence style. Tobias's revised newsletter paragraph from Chapter 16 was clear, but Frau Aksoy has now asked him to help edit a much drier document: the district cultural office's official funding notice, which must be adapted into a short institutional summary for the neighborhood association's records. That text leans heavily on nominal style — dense noun phrases built from verbs — and uses the passive voice throughout to keep the writing impersonal and official-sounding. This chapter teaches learners to recognize nominal style, convert it deliberately into verbal style and back again, use the passive voice and its common alternatives to express objectivity or stance, and judge when each style choice actually serves the reader.

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify nominalizations in a German text and expand them back into full clauses.
  2. Convert verbal-style sentences into nominal style and nominal-style sentences into verbal style without changing the meaning.
  3. Use the passive voice, the impersonal man, and modal-passive alternatives (lässt sich, ist zu + infinitive) to express objectivity, stance, or possibility/necessity.
  4. Use modal verbs to signal certainty, doubt, obligation, and recommendation in institutional and analytical prose.
  5. Revise an overly nominal, impersonal passage so that it remains appropriately formal but is easier to read.
  6. Explain, using accurate terminology, why a specific style choice suits or does not suit a given audience and genre.

Opening Scenario

Frau Aksoy hands Tobias the official funding notice from the district cultural office and asks him to prepare two things: a short, faithful record of it for the association's archive, written in a style close to the original, and a friendlier one-paragraph summary for residents who find the original notice hard to follow. Reading the notice together, they notice that almost every sentence turns a verb into a noun — die Förderung instead of man fördert, die Überprüfung instead of man überprüft — and that the passive voice appears in nearly every clause, so that no one seems to be doing anything. Tobias must decide, sentence by sentence, which version of German best serves each audience.

  1. Learn to recognize and unpack nominal style in official prose.
  2. Practice converting between nominal and verbal style.
  3. Study the passive voice, man, and modal-passive alternatives for stance and objectivity.
  4. Produce both a faithful archive record and a reader-friendly summary of the official notice.

Aussprache

Pronunciation Focus: Rhythm of Nominal-Style Sentences

Pronunciation and Delivery — Reading Dense Nominal Phrases Aloud

Nominal-style sentences often stack several nouns and their modifiers before the finite verb finally appears, so the reader has to hold a long subject or object in mind before the sentence resolves. When reading such a sentence aloud, it helps to give the head noun of a long compound noun phrase slightly more stress than its modifiers, and to take a small breath at each major noun-phrase boundary rather than only at commas. This does not change any rule of German stress; it applies stress and pacing control from earlier volumes specifically to the longer noun phrases typical of institutional and analytical writing.

Reading a nominal-style sentence aloud slowly, then reading its verbal-style paraphrase aloud immediately afterward, is a useful way to hear how nominal style shifts the rhythm and weight of a sentence even when the meaning stays the same.

Grammatik

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

Nominal Style: Turning Verbs into Nouns

Nominal style replaces a finite verb and its arguments with a noun phrase, usually built from the verb stem plus a suffix such as -ung, -heit, -keit, or a related derived noun, combined with a genitive or prepositional phrase in place of the original subject or object. Nominal style is common in reports, official notices, academic prose, and instructions because it can compress information and sound more objective, but it can also make a text harder to follow if overused.

Baustein — Building Block
Verbalstil: Man überprüft die Unterlagen. → Nominalstil: die Überprüfung der Unterlagen
finite verb + object becomes a derived noun + genitive phrase; the agent (man) disappears
Nach der Überprüfung der Unterlagen entscheidet die Behörde über den Antrag.
After the review of the documents, the authority decides on the application.

Unpacking a nominalization means asking three questions: What is the underlying verb? Who or what performs the action, if that information is recoverable? What is the object or result of the action? Answering these questions turns dense nominal style back into clear verbal style.

  • die Förderung des Projekts → man fördert das Projekt / die Kulturbehörde fördert das Projekt the funding of the project → [someone/the cultural office] funds the project nominalization unpacked with recovered or generic agent
  • die Entscheidung der Behörde → die Behörde entscheidet the authority's decision → the authority decides genitive phrase supplies the agent directly

Passive Voice and Its Alternatives

The passive voice (werden + past participle) shifts focus away from the agent of an action and onto the process or result, which suits objective, procedural, or agent-unknown contexts. German has several closely related alternatives that express similar meanings with different nuances.

  • Der Antrag wird geprüft. The application is being reviewed. plain passive: process in progress, agent omitted
  • Man prüft den Antrag. They (people in general/the office) review the application. impersonal man: agent present but unspecified, more informal than passive
  • Der Antrag lässt sich nicht mehr ändern. The application can no longer be changed. sich lassen + infinitive: expresses possibility, similar to a modal + passive
  • Der Antrag ist bis Freitag einzureichen. The application is to be submitted by Friday. sein + zu + infinitive: expresses necessity or obligation, common in official instructions
Grammar Note — Choosing Among Passive Alternatives

Plain passive with werden is the most neutral and widely usable option. Man is somewhat less formal and works well in speech and everyday writing. Sich lassen + infinitive specifically expresses possibility ("can be done"), while sein + zu + infinitive specifically expresses necessity or obligation ("is to be done," "must be done"). Choosing among them is a stance decision, not simply a stylistic ornament.

Modal Verbs for Stance

Beyond ability, permission, and obligation, modal verbs let a writer signal how certain, tentative, or committed a claim is, which matters in analytical and institutional prose where overclaiming is a real risk.

  • Die Entscheidung dürfte vielen Anwohnern gefallen. The decision will presumably please many residents. dürfte: cautious, probability-marking use of the subjunctive of dürfen
  • Weitere Erhebungen sollen im Herbst folgen. Further surveys are supposed to/scheduled to follow in the fall. sollen: reports a plan or requirement without the speaker vouching for it personally
  • Die Behörde muss die Auflagen bis zur Frist erfüllen. The authority must meet the conditions by the deadline. müssen: firm obligation, appropriate for binding requirements
Common Stance Error — Flattening Modal Nuance

Error: *Die Entscheidung wird vielen Anwohnern gefallen. states a future fact where the source text is clearly hedging a prediction.

Correction: Die Entscheidung dürfte vielen Anwohnern gefallen. or Die Entscheidung wird vermutlich vielen Anwohnern gefallen. preserves the original degree of certainty.

Genitive and Prepositional Noun Phrases in Nominal Style

Nominal style depends heavily on genitive phrases and prepositional phrases to carry information that a verbal sentence would put in subject, object, or adverbial position.

  • die Überprüfung der Unterlagen durch die Behörde the review of the documents by the authority genitive phrase (object) + durch-phrase (agent), typical of formal nominal style
  • nach Ablauf der Frist after the deadline has passed / after expiry of the deadline prepositional phrase replacing an entire temporal clause
Modelltext

Model Text: Official Notice and Two Rewrites

The original official notice (dense nominal style, passive throughout), Tobias's faithful archive record, and his reader-friendly summary.

Bescheid Nach Prüfung der eingereichten Unterlagen und Berücksichtigung der vorgelegten Verkehrsdaten wird die Förderung des Projekts unter der Auflage der jährlichen Berichterstattung bewilligt. Eine Überprüfung der Umsetzung erfolgt nach Ablauf von zwölf Monaten. (Dense nominal style: two long noun phrases as subjects of passive sentences; no visible agent.)
Archiv Die Kulturbehörde hat die eingereichten Unterlagen geprüft und die vorgelegten Verkehrsdaten berücksichtigt. Sie bewilligt die Förderung des Projekts, verlangt aber, dass die Initiative jährlich berichtet. Nach zwölf Monaten wird sie prüfen, wie das Projekt umgesetzt wurde. (Faithful record: agent restored as subject, one nominalization kept for compactness, obligation expressed with a subordinate clause.)
Zusammenfassung Gute Nachrichten: Die Kulturbehörde fördert unser Projekt! Sie hat unsere Unterlagen und die Verkehrsdaten geprüft und ist überzeugt. Eine Bedingung gibt es: Wir müssen einmal im Jahr berichten, wie es läuft. Nach einem Jahr schaut sich die Behörde das Ergebnis noch einmal an. (Reader-friendly summary: short verbal-style sentences, modal müssen for the obligation, informal but accurate tone.)

Guided Practice

Culture and Communication

Culture and Communication — Nominal Style Is Genre-Specific

Dense nominal style and heavy passive use are genuinely common in German official notices, legal texts, and some academic prose, but this reflects genre convention rather than a general property of German. The same writer will often use short, direct, verbal-style sentences in an email to a colleague or a message to residents. Recognizing which genre calls for which style is part of register competence, not a fixed national trait of the language.

Culture and Communication — Plain Language Movements

Many public institutions in German-speaking countries have adopted plain-language guidelines that specifically encourage reducing unnecessary nominal style and passive constructions in citizen-facing communication, while retaining precise nominal style where it genuinely aids clarity, such as in legally binding conditions. Bureaucratic style should not be treated as the single correct model of professional German.

Common Mistakes to Recheck

Losing the agent entirely when it matters

Error: *Die Förderung wurde bewilligt. leaves the reader unsure who approved the funding, when that information is relevant to the audience.

Correction: Die Kulturbehörde hat die Förderung bewilligt. restores the agent when the reader needs to know who acted.

Confusing sich lassen and sein + zu

Error: *Der Antrag lässt sich bis Freitag einreichen when the deadline is a requirement, not merely a possibility.

Correction: Der Antrag ist bis Freitag einzureichen. correctly expresses obligation rather than mere possibility.

Overclaiming with a bare future instead of a hedging modal

Error: *Die Ergebnisse werden im Frühjahr vorliegen. when the source only announces an expectation, not a certainty.

Correction: Die Ergebnisse dürften im Frühjahr vorliegen. or Die Ergebnisse sollen im Frühjahr vorliegen. preserves the original degree of certainty.

Answer Key

Guided Practice

  1. Nominalizations: Prüfung (prüfen), Berücksichtigung (berücksichtigen), Förderung (fördern), Berichterstattung (berichten), Überprüfung (überprüfen), Umsetzung (umsetzen).
  2. Sample answer: Die Behörde bearbeitet den Antrag voraussichtlich zwei Wochen lang.
  3. Sample answer: Die sorgfältige Auswertung der Verkehrsdaten ist erfolgt.
  4. Der Bericht ist bis zum 30. September einzureichen.
  5. sollen is the best fit (Die weiteren Ergebnisse sollen im Frühjahr vorliegen), since the office has announced a plan rather than guaranteed an outcome; dürfte is also acceptable if framed as the writer's own cautious estimate rather than a report of the office's plan.

Listening Practice — Comprehension Questions

  1. Die Initiative muss den ersten Bericht bis zum 30. September einreichen; nach zwölf Monaten überprüft die Behörde die Umsetzung des Projekts.
  2. Plain passive: die Förderung bewilligt wurde (process/result emphasized, agent omitted). Sich lassen: Rückfragen lassen sich ... richten (expresses that this is possible/available). Sein + zu: Der erste Bericht ist ... einzureichen (expresses an obligation/requirement).
  3. Relatively cautious: dürften signals a probable but not guaranteed outcome, softer than a plain future tense would sound.

Reading Practice — Comprehension Questions

  1. Erstens eine Zusammenfassung der Fördermaßnahmen, zweitens eine grobe Einschätzung der Projektwirkung anhand der Verkehrsdaten, drittens die Information der Anwohnerinnen und Anwohner über den aktuellen Stand.
  2. Sein + zu: sind ... zu unternehmen and ist ... zu erstellen (both express what must be done as part of the preparation). Sich lassen: lässt sich ... einschätzen (expresses that a rough assessment is possible, not that it must be done). The difference is obligation versus mere possibility.
  3. Dürfte signals that a final assessment being possible only after the second survey is a reasonable expectation, not a certainty, so the reliability of any early estimate should be treated with some caution.

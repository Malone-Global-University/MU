Wiederholung 2: Analytischer Bericht

Unit II Assessment · Speak and Write German: Professional, Academic, and Cultural Communication · Volume IV, Chapter 10

Chapter Overview and Objectives

Chapter 10 closes Unit II by asking Tobias to bring together everything he has practiced since Chapter 6: reading and evaluating a public argument, understanding an official or civic text, summarizing an organizational report, and explaining a technical process. His task now is to combine material from more than one of these domains into a single analytical report — a short written and spoken piece that presents evidence from more than one source, weighs it fairly, and ends with a clear recommendation. This chapter does not introduce new grammar. Instead, it reassesses reported speech, passive and nominal style, complex connectors, and compound analysis together, in the more demanding context of analysis rather than description. Learners read and listen to material from the journalism, civic, business, and technical domains, then write and present an integrated analytical report.

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify the main claim, evidence, and source of texts drawn from journalism, civic, business, and technical domains.
  2. Use reported speech (Konjunktiv I in limited productive frames) to summarize what a source states, without endorsing it.
  3. Use passive, nominal, and process-oriented sentences to describe findings objectively.
  4. Connect evidence and claims using complex connectors of cause, effect, contrast, and concession.
  5. Analyze specialized and organizational compounds accurately when reading across domains.
  6. Write and present a short analytical report that integrates evidence from more than one domain and ends with a clear, well-supported recommendation.

Opening Scenario

The institute's director has asked Tobias to prepare a short internal briefing before an upcoming meeting. The briefing should draw together a recent newspaper commentary about public trust in research institutions, a civic notice about a new reporting requirement for publicly funded projects, and the technical overview of the monitoring system that Tobias helped explain in Chapter 9. The director wants to know what these three sources, taken together, suggest the institute should do, and wants Tobias to state a clear recommendation rather than simply listing what each source says.

  1. Review the vocabulary and connectors carried over from Chapters 6 through 9.
  2. Study how reported speech, passive/nominal style, and complex connectors combine in the model exchange and reading materials below.
  3. Complete the Listening, Speaking, Reading, and Writing assessment tasks in order.
  4. Finish with the Unit II Assessment, which asks you to integrate evidence from more than one domain into a single analytical report with a recommendation.

Grammatik

Grammar Review: Reported Speech, Passive/Nominal Style, and Connectors

No new grammar system is introduced in this chapter. The three systems below, all active since earlier chapters in this unit, are reviewed together because an analytical report typically needs all three at once: reported speech to summarize what a source states, passive or nominal style to present findings objectively, and complex connectors to relate evidence and claims to one another.

Review — Reported Speech in Limited Frames (Chapter 6)

When summarizing what a source claims without endorsing it, German writing often signals distance with a reporting verb plus dass, or with Konjunktiv I in more formal written registers: Der Kommentar behauptet, dass das Vertrauen in Forschungseinrichtungen gesunken sei. ("The commentary claims that trust in research institutions has declined.") In this chapter, reported speech remains limited to short, clearly framed summaries of a single claim; it is not expected to extend into longer indirect narration.

Review — Passive, Nominal Style, and Process Sentences (Chapters 7–9)

An analytical report presents findings and requirements as facts about the world rather than as personal opinions: Die neue Meldepflicht wird ab nächstem Jahr eingeführt. ("The new reporting obligation is being introduced starting next year.") Nominalizations condense a described process into a compact noun phrase for reference across paragraphs: die Einführung der Meldepflicht ("the introduction of the reporting obligation"), die automatische Auswertung der Messungen ("the automatic evaluation of the measurements").

Review — Complex Connectors for Analysis

Relating evidence from more than one source calls on connectors of contrast and concession as well as the cause/effect/purpose connectors from Chapter 9: obwohl ("although"), zwar ... aber ("indeed ... but"), einerseits ... andererseits ("on the one hand ... on the other hand"), and trotzdem ("nevertheless"). Obwohl die neue Vorgabe zusätzliche Arbeit bedeutet, unterstützt der Bericht sie, weil sie das Vertrauen der Öffentlichkeit stärken kann. ("Although the new requirement means additional work, the report supports it, because it can strengthen public trust.")

  • Der Kommentar behauptet, dass das Vertrauen in Forschungseinrichtungen gesunken sei, aber er belegt diese Behauptung nicht mit konkreten Zahlen. The commentary claims that trust in research institutions has declined, but it does not support this claim with concrete figures. reported speech (Konjunktiv I) + contrast (aber)
  • Die Meldepflicht, die im Amtsblatt angekündigt wurde, betrifft alle öffentlich finanzierten Projekte. The reporting obligation, which was announced in the official gazette, affects all publicly funded projects. relative clause + passive
  • Einerseits bedeutet die neue Vorgabe zusätzliche Arbeit; andererseits kann die automatische Auswertung der Messungen, die in Kapitel 9 beschrieben wurde, einen Teil dieser Arbeit übernehmen. On the one hand, the new requirement means additional work; on the other hand, the automatic evaluation of the measurements described in Chapter 9 can take over part of this work. einerseits...andererseits + nominalization + relative clause
Frequent Confusion — Reporting a Claim versus Endorsing It

Error: *Das Vertrauen in Forschungseinrichtungen ist gesunken. presented as the report's own finding, when the information actually comes from a single opinion commentary the report is evaluating.

Correction: Der Kommentar behauptet, dass das Vertrauen gesunken sei. — a reporting verb plus dass (or Konjunktiv I) keeps the claim clearly attributed to its source, rather than stating it as an established fact.

Modellaustausch

Model Exchange

Setting: the director's office, the day before the meeting. Context: Tobias presents a short spoken summary of his briefing before submitting the written version.

Tobias fasst seine Recherche für den Direktor mündlich zusammen.
Direktorin: Was haben Sie aus den drei Quellen herausgefunden? What did you find out from the three sources?
Tobias: Der Kommentar behauptet, dass das Vertrauen in Forschungseinrichtungen gesunken sei, belegt das aber nicht mit Zahlen. Die neue Meldepflicht verlangt dagegen konkret, dass wir unsere Projekte transparenter dokumentieren. The commentary claims that trust in research institutions has declined, but does not support that with figures. The new reporting obligation, on the other hand, concretely requires that we document our projects more transparently.
Direktorin: Und das Messsystem aus Kapitel 9 — hilft uns das dabei? And the monitoring system from Chapter 9 — does that help us with this?
Tobias: Ja. Die automatische Auswertung der Messungen könnte einen Teil der neuen Dokumentationspflicht direkt erfüllen, sodass wir nicht alles manuell nachtragen müssen. Yes. The automatic evaluation of the measurements could directly fulfill part of the new documentation requirement, so that we don't have to add everything manually afterward.
Direktorin: Also, was empfehlen Sie? So, what do you recommend?
Tobias: Ich empfehle, dass wir das bestehende System nutzen, um die neue Meldepflicht zu erfüllen, und dass wir gleichzeitig transparenter kommunizieren, um das öffentliche Vertrauen zu stärken. I recommend that we use the existing system to fulfill the new reporting obligation, and that we communicate more transparently at the same time, in order to strengthen public trust.

Listening Assessment

Ask a partner, instructor, or text-to-speech tool to read the following short passage aloud. Listen once for the overall recommendation, then again for the reasoning behind it.

Kurzbriefing — Listening Script

Ein aktueller Kommentar behauptet, dass das Vertrauen der Öffentlichkeit in Forschungseinrichtungen gesunken sei, liefert dafür aber keine konkreten Belege. Gleichzeitig verlangt eine neue Meldepflicht, dass öffentlich finanzierte Projekte ihre Ergebnisse transparenter dokumentieren. Das Institut verfügt bereits über ein System, das Messdaten automatisch speichert und auswertet, sodass ein Teil der neuen Dokumentationspflicht ohne zusätzlichen Aufwand erfüllt werden könnte. Der Bericht empfiehlt daher, das bestehende System für die neue Meldepflicht zu nutzen und gleichzeitig aktiver über die Arbeit des Instituts zu informieren, um das Vertrauen der Öffentlichkeit zu stärken.

Verständnisfragen

2. Was verlangt die neue Meldepflicht?

3. Identify the sentence stating the report's recommendation, and summarize in your own words the two actions it proposes.

Speaking Assessment

Aufgabe: Einen Bericht mündlich vorstellen

Working with a partner or recording solo, present a short spoken summary of an analytical report based on the sources in this chapter (or on your own combination of two prior-chapter domains), and answer follow-up questions.

Rolle: Berichterstatter/in (Tobias)

  • Summarize the claim of at least one source using reported speech.
  • Present at least one finding using passive or nominal style.
  • Connect two pieces of evidence with a contrast or concession connector.
  • State a clear, well-supported recommendation.

Rolle: Direktor/in

  • Ask what one source found.
  • Ask why the recommendation follows from the evidence.
  • Ask one follow-up question about a term or finding.

Bewertungshinweise — Speaking Criteria

  • The recommendation is clearly stated and follows from the evidence presented.
  • At least one reported-speech summary attributes a claim to its source without endorsing it.
  • At least one passive or nominal-style sentence and one contrast/concession connector are used correctly.
  • Comprehensible, natural pronunciation, pacing, and appropriate register throughout.

Solo alternative: Record both roles yourself, pausing between turns, or record only the presentation as a short monologue.

Reading Assessment

Schreiben

Writing Assessment

Write a short analytical report that draws on at least two of the sources encountered in this chapter (or two prior-chapter domains of your choosing), evaluates them fairly, and ends with a clear recommendation.

Kontext für die Schreibaufgabe

Schreiben Sie einen kurzen analytischen Bericht (ca. 150–200 Wörter) für die Institutsleitung. Fassen Sie mindestens zwei Quellen zusammen, verbinden Sie die Evidenz mit mindestens einem Kontrast- oder Konzessivkonnektor, verwenden Sie mindestens eine indirekte Redewiedergabe sowie mindestens einen Passiv- oder Nominalstilsatz, und formulieren Sie am Ende eine klare, begründete Empfehlung.

  1. Open with a brief line stating the report's purpose.
  2. Summarize the claim or finding of each source, using reported speech for at least one attributed claim.
  3. Connect the evidence from the two sources using a contrast or concession connector.
  4. Use at least one passive or nominal-style sentence to present a finding objectively.
  5. Close with a clear recommendation that follows from the evidence presented.
  6. Review your text against the checklist below.

Revision checklist:

  • Is the report's purpose stated clearly in the opening?
  • Does the report correctly attribute at least one claim to its source using reported speech, without endorsing it as fact?
  • Does at least one sentence use the passive voice or nominal style correctly?
  • Does at least one sentence use a contrast or concession connector correctly to relate the two sources?
  • Does the report end with a clear, well-supported recommendation?

Culture and Communication

Culture and Communication — Fair Weighing of Sources

An analytical report that draws on journalism, civic notices, and technical material must distinguish what each source actually claims from what the writer concludes. German analytical writing commonly signals this distinction through reporting verbs and Konjunktiv I, and through phrases such as laut ("according to"), nach Angaben von ("according to information from"), or es bleibt unklar, ob ... ("it remains unclear whether ..."). The sources, figures, and institutional details in this chapter are constructed for language practice; treat any real civic, legal, or organizational question as one to verify with current, official sources rather than a textbook example.

Culture and Communication — Recommendations Without Overreach

A well-formed recommendation follows visibly from the evidence presented and acknowledges its own limits, for example by noting where further verification, legal review, or additional data would be needed. Avoid presenting a report's recommendation as a final institutional decision; a report of this kind typically informs a decision rather than making it.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Stating a source's claim as an established fact

Error: *Das Vertrauen in Forschungseinrichtungen ist gesunken. presented without attribution, when the information comes from a single opinion commentary.

Correction: Der Kommentar behauptet, dass das Vertrauen gesunken sei. — attribute claims clearly to their source rather than restating them as the report's own finding.

Listing sources instead of connecting them

Error: Summarizing each source in a separate, unconnected paragraph with no connector relating one piece of evidence to another.

Correction: Use contrast, concession, or cause/effect connectors (obwohl, einerseits ... andererseits, sodass) to show explicitly how the evidence from different sources relates to the report's recommendation.

Recommendation not supported by the evidence presented

Error: Ending a report with a recommendation that does not follow from anything stated earlier in the text.

Correction: Make sure the closing recommendation refers back to specific evidence or findings summarized earlier, so the connection between evidence and recommendation is explicit.

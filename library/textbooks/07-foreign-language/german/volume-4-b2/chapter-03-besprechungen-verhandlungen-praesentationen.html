Meetings, Negotiations, Presentations · Speak and Write German: Professional, Academic, and Cultural Communication · Volume IV, Chapter 3

Chapter Overview and Objectives

Chapter 3 moves from the written correspondence of Chapter 2 into live, spoken professional interaction. Tobias's email sequence to Zurich and Vienna went smoothly, and Frau Lindqvist now wants him to represent the project directly in a planning meeting with both offices — a meeting that will involve turn-taking, a short negotiation over shared resources, and a brief presentation of the project timeline. This chapter extends the Konjunktiv II you refined for correspondence into diplomatic, face-to-face negotiation, and adds the turn-taking formulas, modal and passive alternatives, and discourse connectors needed to participate confidently in meetings and deliver a structured presentation.

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Take part in a meeting: open the floor, take a turn, agree, and disagree diplomatically.
  2. Negotiate shared options and constraints, proposing and countering respectfully.
  3. Use Konjunktiv II to soften disagreement and propose alternatives.
  4. Choose between modal, passive, and active phrasing to manage stance and objectivity.
  5. Connect spoken turns with discourse connectors that signal contrast, addition, and consequence.
  6. Deliver a short, structured presentation and summarize a meeting's outcomes.

Opening Scenario

Frau Lindqvist schedules a video meeting with Frau Baumgartner's team in Zurich and Herr Wibmer's team in Vienna to finalize the shared budget line and the next project phase. Both offices have limited capacity in the coming weeks, and each wants the other to take on a larger share of the coordination work. Tobias is asked to open the meeting, summarize where things stand based on his earlier correspondence, and help the two teams reach a compromise. Near the end of the meeting, he will also give a short presentation of the updated timeline. Managing the turn-taking, softening disagreement, and moving smoothly between spoken argument and structured presentation will test everything this chapter introduces.

  1. Review the vocabulary for meetings, agendas, proposals, and negotiation.
  2. Study the model dialogue, in which the meeting reaches a compromise.
  3. Complete the Listening, Speaking, Reading, and Writing components in order.
  4. Finish by delivering your own short presentation and meeting summary.

Grammatik

Grammar Focus: Diplomatisch sprechen

Meetings and negotiations require the same Konjunktiv II diplomacy you used in correspondence, now spoken and reactive in real time, plus three new tools: turn-taking formulas, modal or passive alternatives that soften a stance, and discourse connectors that make a spoken argument easy to follow.

Building Block — Turn-Taking Formulas
Darf ich kurz etwas hinzufügen?  |  Wenn ich kurz einhaken darf, ...  |  Ich möchte noch einmal auf ... zurückkommen.
These formulas let a speaker enter, interrupt politely, or return to an earlier point without disrupting the flow of the meeting.

Darf ich kurz etwas hinzufügen, bevor wir weitermachen?

May I briefly add something before we continue?

Wenn ich kurz einhaken darf: Das betrifft ja auch unseren Zeitplan.

If I may briefly jump in: that also affects our timeline.

Ich möchte noch einmal auf die Budgetfrage zurückkommen.

I'd like to come back once more to the budget question.

Grammar Note — Konjunktiv II for Diplomatic Disagreement

Just as Konjunktiv II softened a written request in Chapter 2, it softens spoken disagreement or a counter-proposal. Compare a blunt objection with a diplomatic one.

Das funktioniert nicht. (blunt) versus Das würde in der Praxis vielleicht schwieriger sein, als es zunächst klingt. — That might be more difficult in practice than it first sounds.

Wir können das nicht übernehmen. (blunt) versus Es wäre für uns schwierig, das vollständig zu übernehmen — könnten wir die Aufgabe teilen? — It would be difficult for us to take that on entirely — could we split the task?

  • Ich verstehe Ihren Punkt, aber ich würde vorschlagen, die Frist etwas zu verschieben. I understand your point, but I would suggest postponing the deadline somewhat. concession (aber) + Konjunktiv II counter-proposal
  • Einerseits verstehen wir die Zeitnot in Wien, andererseits können wir nicht mehr Personal abstellen. On the one hand we understand the time pressure in Vienna; on the other hand we cannot spare more staff. einerseits ... andererseits — balanced concession
  • Deshalb schlage ich vor, dass wir die Aufgabe zwischen beiden Teams aufteilen. That's why I suggest that we split the task between both teams. deshalb — result connector; dass-clause with verb-final order
Grammar Note — Modal and Passive Alternatives to Soften Stance

A direct active statement can sound like a personal accusation or an absolute claim. Modal verbs and the passive voice let a speaker present the same content more neutrally.

Active, direct: Sie haben die Zahlen zu spät geschickt. — You sent the figures too late.

Softer, modal/impersonal: Die Zahlen hätten früher geschickt werden können. — The figures could have been sent earlier. Man müsste die internen Abläufe noch einmal ansehen. — One would need to look at the internal processes again.

Use this shift deliberately: it removes a named subject from a sensitive statement without hiding the substance of the point.

Frequent Confusion — Disagreeing Without Any Softening

Error: *Das ist falsch. as a direct response to a colleague's proposal in a meeting.

Correction: Ich sehe das etwas anders. Wäre es nicht sinnvoller, ...? — I see it a bit differently. Wouldn't it be more sensible to ...? Konjunktiv II and a question form open space for discussion rather than shutting it down.

Modelldialog

Model Dialogue

Setting: video meeting with Zurich and Vienna. Context: Tobias opens the meeting, a disagreement over staffing emerges, and the group reaches a compromise.

Tobias, Frau Baumgartner und Herr Wibmer besprechen die Aufgabenverteilung für die nächste Projektphase.
Tobias: Bevor wir anfangen, fasse ich kurz zusammen, wo wir stehen: Die Zahlen sind bestätigt, und beide Teams möchten teilnehmen. Before we begin, I'll briefly summarize where we stand: the figures are confirmed, and both teams would like to participate.
Frau Baumgartner: Darf ich kurz etwas hinzufügen? Für uns in Zürich wäre es schwierig, die Koordination allein zu übernehmen. May I briefly add something? For us in Zurich, it would be difficult to take on the coordination alone.
Herr Wibmer: Wenn ich kurz einhaken darf: In Wien haben wir aktuell auch wenig Kapazität dafür. If I may briefly jump in: in Vienna we also currently have little capacity for that.
Tobias: Einerseits verstehe ich beide Seiten, andererseits müssen wir eine Lösung finden. Deshalb schlage ich vor, dass wir die Koordination zwischen Zürich und Wien aufteilen. On the one hand I understand both sides; on the other hand we need to find a solution. That's why I suggest we split the coordination between Zurich and Vienna.
Frau Baumgartner: Das wäre für uns machbar. Könnten wir die erste Hälfte der Phase übernehmen und Wien die zweite? That would be workable for us. Could we take on the first half of the phase and Vienna the second?
Herr Wibmer: Einverstanden. Ich fasse zusammen: Zürich übernimmt die erste Hälfte, Wien die zweite, und Tobias hält uns beide auf dem Laufenden. Agreed. To summarize: Zurich takes on the first half, Vienna the second, and Tobias keeps us both updated.

Sprechen

Speaking Component

Aufgabe: Eine Besprechung führen und einen Kompromiss finden

Work with a partner or in a small group. Each person represents a team with a real constraint (limited staff, a tight deadline, a fixed budget). Negotiate a shared task using the turn-taking formulas and diplomatic softening from this chapter, and reach a workable compromise.

Rolle: Besprechungsleitung

  • Open the meeting and summarize the current situation.
  • Manage turn-taking and invite each side to speak.
  • Summarize the agreed compromise at the end.

Rolle: Teammitglied

  • State your team's constraint clearly but diplomatically.
  • Use a turn-taking formula to enter the conversation.
  • Propose or accept a compromise using Konjunktiv II.

Bewertungshinweise — Speaking Criteria

  • At least one turn-taking formula and one Konjunktiv II softening, clearly used.
  • A stated constraint and a workable compromise.
  • Consistent diplomatic register throughout the exchange.
  • Comprehensible, natural pronunciation and pacing.

Solo alternative: Record all sides of the negotiation yourself, pausing between turns to plan each team's response.

Culture and Communication

Culture and Communication — Meeting Norms Differ by Sector and Country

Meeting culture varies considerably across German-speaking workplaces. Some organizations favor a tightly structured agenda with a designated chair and formal turn-taking; others favor a more open discussion style where colleagues interject freely once trust is established. Multinational teams that include offices in Germany, Austria, and Switzerland often need to negotiate a shared meeting style explicitly rather than assuming everyone follows the same unstated norms.

Regional Note — Directness and Hierarchy Require Context

Directness in a meeting is often linked to role and hierarchy rather than to nationality alone. A junior colleague may soften disagreement more than a senior one regardless of country, and some sectors — such as engineering or public administration — favor more direct feedback than others, such as client-facing service roles. Avoid assuming that a colleague from a particular country will always be more or less direct; observe the actual meeting and adjust accordingly.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Interrupting without a turn-taking formula

Error: *Nein, das stimmt nicht. spoken abruptly over another speaker.

Correction: Darf ich kurz etwas hinzufügen? Ich sehe das ein wenig anders. — use a turn-taking formula before disagreeing.

Stating disagreement as an absolute fact

Error: *Das funktioniert nicht. presented as a final, unsoftened judgment.

Correction: Das würde in der Praxis vielleicht schwieriger sein, als es zunächst klingt. — Konjunktiv II leaves room for further discussion.

Naming a person directly in a sensitive criticism

Error: *Sie haben das falsch gemacht. stated directly to a colleague in a group meeting.

Correction: Man müsste die internen Abläufe noch einmal ansehen. or Das hätte anders organisiert werden können. — a modal or passive alternative addresses the issue without naming a person as at fault.

Abschlussprojekt: Präzise kommunizieren

C1-Oriented Capstone · Speak and Write German: Professional, Academic, and Cultural Communication · Volume IV, Chapter 20

Chapter Overview and Objectives

Chapter 20 closes Volume IV with a single integrated capstone portfolio that draws together everything Tobias has practiced across professional correspondence, meetings and presentations, academic and public argument, civic and business communication, technical explanation, cultural and historical analysis, policy discussion, research and sources, complex syntax, nominal and passive style, and mediation. Frau Aksoy asks him to imagine that the neighborhood association's courtyard project — the thread running through this volume — is now ready to be shared beyond the building: with the district's civic office, with a funding committee, and with a newcomer who has never heard of it. Rather than introducing new grammar, this chapter asks Tobias to move fluidly between genres and audiences using the systems this volume has already consolidated, and to revise, mediate, and present that work with the precision, register control, and source discipline expected at this level. Like Chapter 19, this chapter is a workshop; most of its language work happens inside the learner's own capstone rather than inside new explanations.

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Plan and produce an integrated capstone portfolio spanning at least three genres appropriate to different audiences.
  2. Move between nominal, verbal, and passive style deliberately as genre and audience require.
  3. Combine complex clauses and cohesive devices to write and speak with clarity at length.
  4. Mediate a piece of information between English and German for two different audiences.
  5. Distinguish verified from unverified information consistently across every genre in the portfolio.
  6. Deliver a formal presentation of the capstone project and respond to follow-up questions.
  7. Revise the full portfolio through structured, staged rounds before final submission.

Opening Scenario

Frau Aksoy tells Tobias that the courtyard project has attracted attention beyond the building: the district civic office wants a short official-style update, a regional funding committee wants a concise written proposal for continued support, and the neighborhood's own newsletter wants an accessible piece that a newcomer could read in five minutes. She asks Tobias to produce all three texts from the same underlying material, adjusting register, style, and content emphasis for each audience, and then to present the whole effort at the neighborhood association's next meeting, fielding questions from residents with a range of familiarity with the project. She reminds him that the same standards from Chapter 19 apply here at greater scale: name sources honestly, keep register consistent within each text, and revise more than once before anyone else reads the final versions.

  1. Draft three short texts on the same project for three different audiences and genres.
  2. Mediate one piece of the material between English and German for a fourth audience.
  3. Revise the full portfolio through staged rounds.
  4. Present the capstone project and answer questions from a mixed audience.

Chapter 20's vocabulary names the work of integration itself: moving material across genres, checking consistency across a longer portfolio, and describing the mediation process. Recycle domain-specific vocabulary from your own Chapter 19 project alongside the terms below.

  • der Rahmen, die Rahmen — frame, scope

Aussprache

Pronunciation Focus: Delivering the Capstone Presentation

Pronunciation and Delivery — Sustaining Clarity Across a Longer Presentation

A capstone presentation is typically longer and covers more ground than the short presentation in Chapter 19, which makes sustained clarity harder to maintain from the first sentence to the last. Plan brief pauses at the boundary between each of the three genres you present, use slightly slower delivery and clearer stress when moving into a new specialized term for the first time, and rehearse the transitions between sections at least as carefully as the sections themselves.

This is a rehearsal and pacing strategy, not a new pronunciation system; it draws on stress, intonation, and delivery skills already consolidated earlier in this volume and in Chapter 19.

Grammatik

Grammar and Sentence Patterns: Consolidation

Chapter 20 activates no new grammar system. It asks learners to apply, at greater scale and across more genres, the systems consolidated across this volume: nominal and verbal style shifting, passive and modal alternatives, complex clause hierarchy with clear punctuation, source attribution, register control, and mediation between English and German. The sections below function as an integration checklist.

Shifting Style Across Three Genres

The same underlying facts about the courtyard project must sound different in an official civic update, a funding proposal, and a newcomer-facing newsletter piece. The facts do not change; the nominal density, sentence length, and degree of hedging or persuasion do.

Baustein — Building Block
ein Sachverhalt → drei Register
one underlying fact rendered in three registers for three audiences
Amt: Die Fördermittel wurden nach Prüfung des Antrags bewilligt. | Förderantrag: Wir bitten um die Fortsetzung der Förderung, da das Projekt seine Ziele im ersten Jahr erreicht hat. | Newsletter: Die gute Nachricht: Unser Gartenprojekt bekommt weiter Unterstützung!
Civic office: The funding was approved after review of the application. | Funding proposal: We request continued funding, since the project met its goals in the first year. | Newsletter: The good news: our garden project keeps getting support!

Mediating Between English and German for Different Purposes

Mediation in this chapter should serve a genuine communicative need, such as briefing an English-speaking newcomer on the German-language civic update, rather than functioning as literal translation practice. Preserve meaning and register; do not force German syntax into an English pattern or vice versa.

  • The district office confirmed that the funding covers only the current growing season. Die Bezirksverwaltung hat bestätigt, dass die Förderung nur die laufende Vegetationsperiode abdeckt. mediated for an English-speaking newcomer; subordinate clause verb-final, register preserved as neutral-official
  • Genaue Zahlen zur Beteiligung anderer Höfe liegen mir nicht vor. I don't have exact figures on how many other courtyards have taken part. mediated into English while preserving the honest limitation stated in the German original

Cohesion and Complex Clause Hierarchy at Portfolio Length

A multi-genre portfolio is easier to follow when each text uses a small, deliberate set of connectors rather than a long, unvaried chain of subordinate clauses. Vary sentence length and clause depth by genre: the civic update may tolerate longer nominal constructions; the newsletter piece should stay shorter and more verbal.

Grammar Note — Checking Cohesion Separately in Each Genre

Reread each of the three genre texts on its own, checking whether connectors, pronoun reference, and clause length suit that specific genre, rather than checking the whole portfolio for cohesion only once. A connector pattern that reads as appropriately formal in the funding proposal may read as stiff in the newsletter piece.

Maintaining Source Discipline Across All Genres

The standard from Chapter 19 continues here at greater scale: every genre in the portfolio must distinguish verified information from unverified or speculative claims, and no genre is exempt from this requirement, including the more persuasive funding proposal.

Common Capstone Error — Loosening Source Standards in Persuasive Genres

Error: *Laut Umfragen ist unser Projekt in der ganzen Stadt bekannt. in a funding proposal when no such survey exists.

Correction: Mehrere Nachbarn und die Lokalzeitung haben über das Projekt berichtet; genaue Reichweitenzahlen liegen uns jedoch nicht vor. — states what is actually known and labels the limitation, even in a persuasive genre.

Modelltext

Model Text: Three Genres, One Project

Three short excerpts from Tobias's capstone portfolio, each drawn from the same underlying facts about the courtyard project but adapted for a different audience and genre.

Amt Das Gartenprojekt im Innenhof der Nachbarschaftsvereinigung wird seit dem Frühjahr des vergangenen Jahres durchgeführt. Die Fördermittel wurden nach Prüfung des Antrags bewilligt und decken die laufende Vegetationsperiode ab. Ein Fortschrittsbericht wird der Kulturbehörde wie vereinbart vorgelegt. (The garden project in the neighborhood association's courtyard has been running since spring of last year. The funding was approved after review of the application and covers the current growing season. A progress report will be submitted to the cultural office as agreed.)
Förderantrag Wir beantragen die Fortsetzung der Förderung für eine weitere Saison, da das Projekt seine ursprünglichen Ziele erreicht und mehrere Nachbarn aktiv eingebunden hat. Genaue Vergleichszahlen zu ähnlichen Projekten liegen uns nicht vor; unser Antrag stützt sich daher auf die eigene Erfahrung der ersten Saison. (We are requesting continued funding for another season, since the project has met its original goals and actively involved several neighbors. We do not have exact comparison figures for similar projects; our application is therefore based on our own experience from the first season.)
Newsletter Gute Nachrichten aus dem Innenhof: Unser kleines Gartenprojekt darf noch eine Saison weitermachen! Wer neu in der Nachbarschaft ist und mitmachen möchte, kann sich einfach bei Frau Aksoy melden. (Good news from the courtyard: our little garden project gets to continue for another season! Anyone new to the neighborhood who would like to join in can simply contact Frau Aksoy.)

Guided Practice

  1. Read the three model excerpts above and identify one specific word or sentence-structure choice in each that signals its genre.
  2. Mediate the "Amt" excerpt into English for a newcomer who does not read German, preserving its neutral-official register.
  3. Rewrite the "Förderantrag" excerpt's second sentence in a way that still discloses the limitation but sounds slightly more confident without inventing data.
  4. Identify one connector you could add to the "Newsletter" excerpt to make it flow more naturally without making it more formal.
  5. Draft one sentence of your own project that could appear, with only register changes, in all three genres.

Verständnisfragen

1. How many different texts is Tobias writing for his capstone, and who is each one for?

2. What does Tobias say his funding proposal honestly acknowledges?

3. Für wen vermittelt Tobias einen Teil der amtlichen Mitteilung auf Englisch, und warum?

Sprechen

Speaking Practice

Aufgabe: Das Abschlussprojekt präsentieren

Working with a partner, a small group, or by recording yourself, present your capstone project in four to six minutes: briefly summarize the underlying facts, then walk through how you adapted them for each of your three genres, explain one mediation choice you made, and invite follow-up questions. Your audience should include at least one "listener" unfamiliar with the project and one "listener" who already knows it well.

Rolle: Präsentierende Person

  • Summarize the underlying project facts briefly.
  • Explain how register and content shifted across your three genres.
  • Explain one mediation choice between English and German.
  • Invite and answer follow-up questions.

Rolle: Fragende Person

  • Ask a question a genuine newcomer might ask.
  • Ask how a specific claim in one genre was verified.
  • Ask why a particular register choice was made for a particular audience.

Bewertungshinweise — Speaking Criteria

  • Clearly summarizes the underlying project and its three (or more) genre adaptations.
  • Explains register and style choices with reference to audience.
  • Distinguishes verified from unverified information when questioned.
  • Sustains clear, comprehensible delivery across a longer presentation, with attention to pacing at genre transitions.
  • Responds to follow-up questions appropriately and honestly.

Solo alternative: Record your full presentation, then listen back and note two places where a transition between genres could be clearer.

Entwurf eines Mitschülers — A Classmate's Draft Newsletter Piece, with an Issue to Notice

Newsletter-Beitrag: Unser Hofprojekt wächst

Unser Hofprojekt ist ein voller Erfolg. Laut einer Untersuchung der Universität ist die Lebensqualität in unserem Viertel seit Projektbeginn um 25 Prozent gestiegen. Alle Nachbarn sind begeistert, und wir hoffen, dass bald noch mehr Höfe mitmachen.

Verständnisfragen

1. Was behauptet dieser Newsletter-Beitrag über die Lebensqualität im Viertel?

2. Identify two problems with this excerpt: one involving a fabricated source and one involving an overgeneralization ("Alle Nachbarn"). Explain each.

3. Rewrite the excerpt so that it keeps a positive, newsletter-appropriate tone but removes the fabricated statistic and the overgeneralization.

Writing Workshop

Complete your own Chapter 20 capstone portfolio. You may continue with your Chapter 19 project domain or select a new one, provided it is narrow enough to source and control responsibly. This workshop spans the whole capstone; plan for at least two rounds of revision on the full portfolio.

Kontext für das Abschlussprojekt

Wählen Sie einen Bereich, den Sie mit den Sprachmitteln dieses Bandes gut bearbeiten können, und entwickeln Sie daraus drei kurze Texte für drei verschiedene Zielgruppen. Vermitteln Sie einen Teil des Materials zwischen Deutsch und Englisch, überarbeiten Sie das gesamte Portfolio mindestens zweimal, und bereiten Sie eine kurze Präsentation vor.

Die fünf Portfoliobestandteile — The Five Portfolio Components

  1. Amtliche Kurzmitteilung (Official Update): 4–6 sentences in neutral-official register for a civic or institutional reader.
  2. Förderantrag oder Vorschlag (Funding Proposal or Pitch): 5–8 sentences requesting or proposing continued support, with honest limitations stated.
  3. Newsletter- oder Erklärtext (Newsletter or Explainer Piece): 100–150 words in an accessible, friendly register for a newcomer.
  4. Vermittlung (Mediation): a mediated version of one component into the other language, preserving register and meaning.
  5. Präsentation (Presentation): a spoken outline of the full portfolio for the Speaking Practice task above.
  1. Confirm your project domain is narrow enough to source and control across four written components.
  2. Draft the official update first, since its neutral register anchors the underlying facts.
  3. Draft the funding proposal, adapting content and tone while keeping the facts consistent with the official update.
  4. Draft the newsletter piece, simplifying register and sentence length for a newcomer audience.
  5. Mediate one component into the other language, checking register and meaning rather than translating word for word.
  6. Set the full portfolio aside, then revise it in at least two further rounds: first for cross-genre consistency of facts, then for register, cohesion, and mechanics in each text separately.
  7. Prepare and rehearse your presentation, paying attention to pacing at the transitions between genres.

Revision checklist:

  • Are the underlying facts consistent across all texts in my portfolio?
  • Does each text's register suit its stated audience from beginning to end?
  • Have I clearly labeled any unverified or speculative claim in every genre, including the funding proposal?
  • Does my mediated text preserve meaning and register rather than forcing one language's syntax onto the other?
  • Have I revised the full portfolio at least twice, addressing different concerns each time?
  • Is my presentation paced clearly across all sections, including the transitions?

Culture and Communication

Culture and Communication — Institutional Audiences Differ Within the Same Community

A single local project can have several real audiences with different institutional expectations at once: a district office, a funding body, and a neighborhood readership. Learners should treat this as ordinary rather than unusual, and should expect that DACH institutional norms for tone, structure, and formality can vary even within a single country depending on the specific office or organization addressed.

Culture and Communication — Mediation as a Civic and Community Skill

Mediating information between English and German for a neighbor who does not yet read German well is a genuine, everyday form of community communication, not merely a classroom exercise. Treating mediation this way — as a service to a real reader rather than a demonstration of translation skill — supports the honest, audience-centered stance this volume has emphasized throughout.

Common Mistakes to Recheck

Letting facts drift across genres

Error: A learner's funding proposal states a different timeline or scope than the official update describes, creating an inconsistency a careful reader could notice.

Correction: Anchor all genre texts to the same underlying facts, drafted once and adapted in tone and detail rather than rewritten from scratch each time.

Loosening source discipline in persuasive genres

Error: *Laut einer Untersuchung der Universität ist die Lebensqualität um 25 Prozent gestiegen. when no such study exists.

Correction: State only what is verifiable, and label anything uncertain, even in a genre meant to persuade, as in Mehrere Nachbarn haben von einer positiveren Stimmung berichtet, auch wenn dazu keine Studie vorliegt.

Forcing one language's structure onto the other during mediation

Error: Mediating a German subordinate clause into English while keeping the German verb-final word order, producing an unnatural English sentence.

Correction: Restructure the sentence naturally in the target language while preserving its meaning and register, as taught in Chapter 18.

No new grammar system was introduced; the chapter instead consolidated the volume's systems inside a capstone project demonstrating precision, register control, and responsible communication at a C1-oriented level.

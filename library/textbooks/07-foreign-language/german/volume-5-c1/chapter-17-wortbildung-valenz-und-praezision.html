Wortbildung, Valenz und Präzision

Word Formation, Valency, and Precision · Speak and Write German: Mastery, Precision, and Specialized Communication · Volume V, Chapter 17

Chapter Overview and Objectives

Chapter 17 builds directly on the reference habit developed in Chapter 16. Tobias's usage note about "ein Problem verursachen" was well received, and Herr Böhm has now asked him to look over a longer draft paragraph and tighten its word choices before it goes out. This chapter moves from checking a single collocation to editing a connected text for lexical precision: choosing the verb, noun, or compound that carries exactly the intended meaning, confirming the case and preposition a verb governs, and recognizing when a compound is an established German word rather than an invented translation of an English phrase. No new grammar system is activated here; the chapter deepens control of vocabulary that Volume V learners already partially know, using the evidence-based habits from Chapter 16.

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify imprecise or overly general vocabulary in a German text and propose a more precise alternative.
  2. Read and apply a verb's valency information, including case and preposition governance, when editing or composing sentences.
  3. Distinguish an established German compound from an unnatural, author-invented compound.
  4. Build a short valency entry for a verb, recording its case frame, typical prepositions, and typical objects.
  5. Revise a paragraph for lexical precision without changing its intended meaning.
  6. Explain, in speech, why a specific word or compound was chosen over a plausible alternative.

Opening Scenario

Herr Böhm hands Tobias a paragraph from a project update and says, "Das ist inhaltlich richtig, aber sprachlich noch nicht ganz präzise. Schauen Sie sich die Verben und die zusammengesetzten Wörter noch einmal an." Tobias reads the paragraph and notices a few problems: a vague verb used where a more specific one would fit better, a preposition that does not match the verb it follows, and a compound noun that looks plausible but that he cannot actually confirm exists in standard German. Rather than simply swapping in words that sound better to him, he decides to check each questionable choice against a valency entry or dictionary, exactly as he practiced with Problem and verursachen in the previous chapter.

  1. Identify at least two imprecise word choices in the draft paragraph.
  2. Check the valency of the relevant verbs, including case and preposition governance.
  3. Confirm whether a questionable compound is an established word or an invented one.
  4. Revise the paragraph and be ready to justify each change.
Wortschatz

Core Vocabulary: Word Formation and Precision

This chapter activates vocabulary for talking about word formation, valency, and lexical precision in editing. These items are introduced for productive use in describing and justifying vocabulary revisions.

Wortbildung — Word Formation

  • die Wortbildung — word formation
  • die Ableitung, die Ableitungen — derivation (a word formed from another word)
  • das Bestimmungswort, die Bestimmungswörter — the modifying (non-head) element of a compound
  • das Grundwort, die Grundwörter — the head element of a compound
  • das Fugenelement, die Fugenelemente — linking element in a compound
  • ein Wort erfinden — to invent a word (used critically, of unnatural compounds)

Valenz und Präzision — Valency and Precision

  • die Valenz — valency (a verb's argument pattern)
  • das Rektionsmuster, die Rektionsmuster — case/preposition governance pattern
  • präzisieren — to make more precise, to sharpen
  • treffend — apt, fitting (of a word choice)
  • unpräzise — imprecise
  • ein Wort ersetzen (durch etwas) — to replace a word (with something)

The nouns Kollokation and Beleg and the phrase kollokiert mit are review items from Chapter 16; they are reused here to describe compound and valency choices rather than taught again.

Aussprache

Delivery Focus: Stress in Derived and Compound Terms

Delivery — Stressing Long Compounds and Derived Terms Clearly

Precision vocabulary often involves longer derived nouns and compounds, such as die Präzisierung or das Rektionsmuster. As established in earlier volumes, German compound stress normally falls on the first element, and derived nouns typically keep the stress pattern of their base word plus a predictable shift for the suffix. When reading a longer technical or precision term aloud in a discussion — for instance, explaining why a compound is unnatural — stress the first element clearly and keep the remaining syllables even, rather than spreading stress evenly across the whole word as an English speaker might.

This is a recycling and refinement of stress patterns already established; no new stress rule is introduced here.

Sprachliche Muster

Language Patterns: Valency, Prepositional Frames, and Compounds

This chapter does not introduce a new grammar system. It sharpens control of valency, prepositional government, and compound formation — systems already active from earlier volumes — for use in precision editing.

Recording a Valency Entry

Baustein — Building Block
Verb + Kasus + Präposition(en) + typische Objekte
a compact format for recording how a verb behaves, building on the valency information introduced receptively in the Chapter 16 dictionary entries
sich konzentrieren auf + Akkusativ — typische Objekte: eine Aufgabe, ein Problem, die wesentlichen Punkte
to concentrate on + accusative — typical objects: a task, a problem, the essential points

Checking a Preposition Against the Verb, Not the English Translation

Language Note — Verb-Governed Prepositions Are Not Predictable from English

A verb's preposition and case are properties of the German verb itself, not a direct match for the English preposition that happens to accompany its translation. For example, sich interessieren für (Akkusativ) does not use von simply because English says "interested in," and warten auf (Akkusativ) does not use für because English says "wait for." When editing, always check the verb's own valency entry rather than translating the English preposition directly.

Distinguishing Established Compounds from Invented Ones

Language Note — Not Every Plausible Compound Is a Real Word

German compounding is highly productive, which means a learner can construct a compound that looks grammatically well formed but that is not an established German word. Before using an unfamiliar compound in a formal text, check whether it appears in a dictionary or a reliable corpus. If it does not, either use an established near-equivalent or rephrase with a short phrase instead of a compound. Precision does not mean inventing new compact words; it means choosing the most accurate word that actually exists in standard usage.

Modelltext

Model Text: Editing a Draft Paragraph

A short original exchange between Herr Böhm and Tobias, written for this course, continuing directly from Chapter 16.

Böhm Sehen Sie sich bitte diesen Absatz an. Irgendetwas klingt hier noch nicht ganz treffend. (Please look at this paragraph. Something here still doesn't sound quite apt.)
Tobias Ich glaube, das Verb im zweiten Satz ist zu allgemein, und die Präposition danach passt nicht zum Verb. Ich prüfe kurz die Valenz nach. (I think the verb in the second sentence is too general, and the preposition after it doesn't fit the verb. I'll quickly check the valency.)
Böhm Und das zusammengesetzte Wort am Ende — gibt es das wirklich, oder haben Sie es gerade erfunden? (And the compound word at the end — does that really exist, or did you just invent it?)
Tobias Gute Frage. Das schlage ich lieber nach, bevor ich es stehen lasse. (Good question. I'd rather look that up before I leave it in.)

Über Präzision beim Überarbeiten — Listening Script

Beim Überarbeiten eines Textes reicht es nicht, nur auf Grammatikfehler zu achten. Oft ist ein Satz grammatisch vollkommen korrekt und klingt trotzdem nicht treffend, weil ein Verb zu allgemein gewählt wurde oder weil ein zusammengesetztes Wort zwar denkbar, aber nicht tatsächlich gebräuchlich ist. Wer auf hohem Niveau schreibt, sollte deshalb nicht nur die Rechtschreibung und die Satzstruktur prüfen, sondern auch die Valenz der Verben und die Natürlichkeit der Komposita. Ein Wörterbuch oder ein Korpus kann dabei helfen, aber die Entscheidung, ob ein Wort wirklich passt, bleibt am Ende eine Frage des geübten Sprachgefühls.

Speaking Practice: Defending an Edit

Aufgabe: Eine Änderung begründen

Working with a partner or by recording yourself, choose one word-level edit you made in the Writing Workshop below and explain, in a short spoken exchange, why you replaced the original word or preposition and what evidence supports the change.

Rolle: Überarbeitende Person

  • Identify the original, imprecise word or preposition.
  • Explain the replacement using valency or compound-formation language from this chapter.
  • State clearly whether the choice is based on a checked reference or on general impression.

Rolle: Prüfende Person

  • Ask why the original word was imprecise.
  • Ask what case or preposition the corrected verb governs.
  • Ask whether any compound involved is an established word or a proposed one.

Bewertungshinweise — Speaking Criteria

  • Clearly identifies the original word choice and the revision.
  • Justifies the revision using valency or word-formation reasoning, not intuition alone.
  • Correctly states the case and preposition governed by any verb discussed.
  • Distinguishes established compounds from proposed or uncertain ones.
  • Maintains clear, appropriately stressed delivery of longer or derived terms.

Solo alternative: Record yourself explaining one edit, then listen back and check whether you correctly named the case and preposition governed by the verb involved.

Stilistische Ziele — Stylistic Goals

  1. Rektionsgenauigkeit: ensure every verb is followed by the case and preposition it actually governs.
  2. Wortbildungssicherheit: replace any unconfirmed compound with an established word or a short phrase.
  3. Beleggestützte Begründung: justify at least one change with reference to the valency entry, not personal preference alone.
  1. Reread the draft paragraph and underline every verb-preposition combination.
  2. Check each combination against the valency entry and correct any mismatch.
  3. Decide how to handle the questionable compound: replace it or rephrase it.
  4. Draft the revised paragraph.
  5. Write a short justification for one specific change.
  6. Reread the revision to confirm no new imprecision was introduced.

Revision checklist:

  • Does every verb use the case and preposition shown in the valency entry?
  • Has the uncertain compound been replaced or rephrased rather than left unchecked?
  • Does the justification refer to specific evidence rather than general preference?
  • Does the revision preserve the original meaning of the paragraph?
  • Is German spelling, capitalization, and punctuation accurate throughout?

Culture and Communication

Culture and Communication — Precision as a Form of Respect for the Reader

In professional and academic German writing, choosing the precise verb or noun over a vague or invented one is not merely a stylistic preference; it signals care for the reader's time and understanding. A reader encountering a general verb where a specific one was expected, or a compound that does not actually exist, may pause and reread, which slows communication in exactly the contexts — reports, official correspondence, technical documentation — where clarity matters most. This does not mean every sentence must use the rarest possible word; it means the chosen word should be the one that most accurately and naturally expresses the intended meaning.

Regional Awareness — Compound and Valency Variation Across DACH

Some compounds and verb-preposition pairings show mild variation across Germany, Austria, and Switzerland, particularly in institutional and administrative vocabulary. When precision editing for a specific audience, note the relevant national context rather than assuming one country's usage is universal, continuing the labeling habit established in Chapter 14.

Common Mistakes to Recheck

Transferring an English preposition onto a German verb

Error: A learner writes "sich interessieren von" or "sich konzentrieren für," modeling the preposition on the English phrase rather than the German verb's valency.

Correction: Check the verb's own valency entry: sich interessieren für and sich konzentrieren auf, both with the accusative.

Inventing a compound instead of checking it

Error: A learner builds a long compound such as Vorgehensweisebeschreibung by combining plausible elements without confirming that the word is actually used.

Correction: Check a dictionary or corpus, and prefer an established alternative such as die Verfahrensbeschreibung or a short phrase such as die Beschreibung des Vorgehens when the compound cannot be confirmed.

Defaulting to a vague verb to avoid checking valency

Error: A learner avoids precision by reusing a very general verb such as machen or haben in a technical or formal sentence where a specific verb with a known valency would be clearer.

Correction: Look up a more specific verb and its valency entry rather than defaulting to a general verb to avoid the extra check.

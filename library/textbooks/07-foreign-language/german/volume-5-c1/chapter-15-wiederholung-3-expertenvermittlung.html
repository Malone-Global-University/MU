Wiederholung 3: Expertenvermittlung

Expert Mediation Assessment · Speak and Write German: Mastery, Precision, and Specialized Communication · Volume V, Chapter 15

Chapter Overview and Objectives

Chapter 15 is a cumulative assessment chapter. It brings together the advanced translation and mediation strategies practiced in Chapter 13 with the DACH-variation recognition and labeling skills practiced in Chapter 14, alongside the source-critical reading and academic mediation habits built earlier in Unit III. Tobias must now put all of it to work at once. Frau Aydın has asked him to prepare a short internal briefing for a mixed audience — some colleagues in Berlin, one contact in Graz, and one contact in Zurich — summarizing two source documents about the museum project's expansion, while flagging both regional variation and any uncertainty in the sources themselves. This chapter introduces no new grammar or vocabulary system. It assesses whether learners can mediate specialized and regional information accurately, transparently, and with appropriate caveats for a defined audience.

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Mediate specialized written information for a mixed DACH audience.
  2. Summarize two source documents accurately, without overstating their claims.
  3. Label regional (DACH) variation encountered in source material without ranking it.
  4. Answer follow-up questions about a mediated summary, including appropriate caveats about uncertainty.
  5. Revise a mediated summary for accuracy, neutrality, and audience fit.
  6. Deliver a short oral briefing that integrates mediation, translation judgment, and DACH awareness.

Opening Scenario

Frau Aydın calls a short planning meeting. Berlin, Graz, and Zurich each want to know whether the museum project can expand into their city, but each partner has sent a different kind of document: the Berlin team has an internal funding memo, and the Graz partner has forwarded a short newspaper item about a similar project elsewhere in Austria. "I need one briefing," Frau Aydın says, "that fairly summarizes both documents for all three groups, flags anything regional they should know about, and is honest about what we don't yet know. Nobody should walk away thinking we promised something the documents don't actually say." Tobias's task is to read both documents carefully, mediate their content for a mixed audience, label any DACH-specific vocabulary or institutional terms that appear, and prepare a briefing with clear caveats where the source material is incomplete or uncertain.

  1. Read both source documents and identify their main claims, evidence, and limitations.
  2. Identify any DACH-specific vocabulary, spelling, or institutional terms in either document and label them accurately.
  3. Draft a mediated summary suitable for a mixed Berlin/Graz/Zurich audience.
  4. Prepare to answer follow-up questions about the summary, including questions about uncertainty.

Aussprache

Delivery Focus: Presenting Under Time Pressure

Delivery — Maintaining Intelligibility While Presenting Live

This chapter's delivery focus reviews, rather than introduces, pronunciation and delivery skills. When presenting a mediated briefing aloud, especially under time pressure or with a mixed audience, maintain the intelligibility practices from earlier chapters: clear word and sentence stress, accurate final devoicing, and a steady, moderate speaking rate. Do not rush technical or institutional terms; if a term may be unfamiliar to part of the audience (for example, a Swiss or Austrian institutional term), pause briefly to gloss it in the same sentence.

Regional pronunciation features encountered while listening to Graz- or Zurich-based speakers, as practiced in Chapter 14, should continue to be recognized but not imitated. Your own spoken delivery should remain in your established standard productive model throughout the briefing.

Sprachliche Muster

Language Patterns: Consolidating Unit III Systems

This chapter introduces no new grammar or language system. It requires accurate, integrated use of the systems already activated across Chapters 12 through 14: source attribution and stance language, hedging and limitation language, contrastive register control, and DACH-variation labeling.

Hedging and Caveat Language

Baustein — Building Block
Laut dem Zeitungsartikel … / Die Quelle deutet darauf hin, dass … / Es bleibt unklar, ob …
source-attribution and hedging patterns reviewed from Chapters 6, 7, and 13: attributing a claim to its source, softening a claim that is only suggested rather than confirmed, and flagging an open question
Laut dem Zeitungsartikel plant die Organisation in Graz eine ähnliche Ausstellung, aber es bleibt unklar, ob ein konkretes Datum bereits feststeht.
According to the newspaper article, the organization in Graz is planning a similar exhibition, but it remains unclear whether a concrete date has already been set.

DACH-Labeling in Mediated Summaries

Language Note — Carrying Chapter 14 Labels into a Mediated Summary

When a source document uses a DACH-specific spelling, term, or institutional name, a mediated summary should preserve or clearly gloss that term rather than silently converting it. If the newspaper item uses Maturität or Swiss ss spelling, the mediated briefing should either keep the original term with a short gloss, or explicitly state that a term has been adapted for the Berlin-based audience. Silent, unlabeled conversion between regional forms can misrepresent the source.

Modelltext

Model Text: The Briefing Request

A short original planning exchange between Frau Aydın and Tobias, written for this course, establishing the assessment task.

Aydın Tobias, ich brauche eine kurze Zusammenfassung der beiden Dokumente für Berlin, Graz und Zürich zusammen. Bitte kennzeichnen Sie alles, was regional unterschiedlich ist, und sagen Sie klar, wo die Quellen noch unsicher sind. (Tobias, I need a short summary of both documents for Berlin, Graz, and Zurich together. Please label anything that's regionally different, and say clearly where the sources are still uncertain.)
Tobias Verstanden. Ich vergleiche zuerst die beiden Dokumente und markiere die Stellen, an denen sich Wortschatz oder Schreibweise unterscheiden, bevor ich die Zusammenfassung schreibe. (Understood. I'll first compare the two documents and mark the places where vocabulary or spelling differ, before I write the summary.)
Aydın Gut. Und bitte keine Versprechen, die die Dokumente nicht wirklich machen. (Good. And please, no promises the documents don't actually make.)

This exchange sets the assessment task in motion. The source documents Tobias reads appear in the Reading Assessment section below.

Listening Assessment

Kurzbriefing vor der Sitzung — Listening Script

Bevor wir mit allen drei Städten sprechen, fasse ich kurz zusammen, was wir sicher wissen und was noch offen ist. Aus dem internen Memo wissen wir, dass Berlin zusätzliche Mittel für eine mögliche Erweiterung eingeplant hat, aber ein endgültiger Beschluss steht noch aus. Der Zeitungsartikel aus Österreich berichtet von einem ähnlichen Projekt, das dort in Vorbereitung ist, nennt aber kein festes Datum. Wichtig ist: Wir dürfen in unserer Zusammenfassung nicht so tun, als wäre bereits alles entschieden. Verständnisfragen

1. What has Berlin planned for, according to the internal memo, and what has NOT yet happened?

2. What does the Austrian newspaper article report, and what specific detail is missing from it?

3. Was sagt der Sprecher über Begriffe aus dem österreichischen Artikel?

Speaking Assessment: Mediated Briefing

Aufgabe: Mündliches Briefing vorbereiten und vortragen

Working with a partner, a small group, or by recording yourself, deliver a short (3 to 4 minute) oral briefing summarizing the two source documents from the Reading Assessment section below for a mixed Berlin/Graz/Zurich audience. Be prepared to answer follow-up questions.

Rolle: Vermittelnde Person

  • Summarize both source documents accurately and fairly.
  • Label any DACH-specific vocabulary or spelling encountered in the sources.
  • State clearly which claims are confirmed and which remain uncertain.
  • Answer follow-up questions honestly, including saying "that isn't in the sources" when appropriate.

Rolle: Fragende Person

  • Ask at least one question that tests whether the briefing overstated a source's claim.
  • Ask at least one question about a regional term used in the briefing.
  • Ask what is still unknown or unconfirmed.

Bewertungshinweise — Speaking Criteria

  • Accurately summarizes both source documents without overstating or inventing claims.
  • Clearly labels any DACH-specific vocabulary, spelling, or institutional terms encountered.
  • Distinguishes confirmed information from uncertain or unconfirmed information.
  • Responds to follow-up questions transparently, acknowledging limits of the source material.
  • Maintains intelligible, appropriately paced standard pronunciation throughout.

Solo alternative: Record your briefing, then listen back and check whether you clearly separated confirmed from uncertain information and labeled any regional terms.

Writing Assessment: Mediated Summary with Caveats

Produce a written mediated briefing based on the two source documents above, suitable for a mixed Berlin/Graz/Zurich audience.

Aufgabe — Task

Verfassen Sie eine kurze schriftliche Zusammenfassung (ungefähr 150 bis 200 Wörter) der beiden Quellen für ein gemischtes Publikum aus Berlin, Graz und Zürich. Fassen Sie zusammen, was gesichert ist und was noch offen ist. Kennzeichnen Sie jeden regionalen Begriff, den Sie aus dem Zeitungsartikel übernehmen. Vermeiden Sie jede Aussage, die mehr behauptet, als die Quellen tatsächlich sagen.

Stilistische Ziele — Stylistic Goals

  1. Genauigkeit: represent both sources accurately, without adding unstated details.
  2. Transparenz: clearly separate confirmed information from open questions.
  3. Kennzeichnung: label any DACH-specific term rather than silently converting it.
  1. Reread both source documents and list what each one actually confirms versus merely suggests.
  2. Identify any DACH-specific vocabulary or institutional term and decide how to label it for a mixed audience.
  3. Draft a short paragraph combining both sources into one fair, accurate summary.
  4. Add at least one sentence stating clearly what remains unknown or unconfirmed.

  • Is the summary appropriately concise and usable for a real internal briefing?

Culture and Communication

Culture and Communication — Transparency in Mediation

Responsible mediation across institutions, regions, and audiences depends on transparency about what a source does and does not say. A mediator who quietly smooths over gaps, uncertainty, or regional differences to make a briefing sound more complete than it is can mislead every audience involved, even unintentionally. This chapter's assessment rewards honest, clearly labeled uncertainty over confident-sounding overstatement.

Regional Variation — Carrying Chapter 14 Forward

The DACH-labeling habits practiced in Chapter 14 apply directly to mediation work: a mediator who silently converts a Swiss or Austrian term into a Germany-based equivalent, without labeling the change, can obscure a real institutional or regional distinction. Careful mediation preserves and labels such differences rather than erasing them.

Common Mistakes to Recheck

Overstating a source's claim

Error: A learner writes that the expansion "has been decided" or "will happen," when the internal memo only says funds have been planned and a final decision is still pending.

Correction: Use hedged language that matches the source exactly, such as "eine mögliche Erweiterung" or "ein endgültiger Beschluss steht noch aus."

Silently converting a regional term

Error: A learner replaces Matura with Abitur in the summary without noting that the original source used the Austrian term.

Correction: Keep the original term with a short gloss, or explicitly note that a term has been adapted for the audience, following the labeling practice from Chapter 14.

Blurring what is confirmed versus uncertain

Error: A learner presents the memo and the newspaper item as equally confirmed, without noting that neither source specifies a firm timeline or list of cities.

Correction: Explicitly state which details remain open, using clear hedging language, so the audience is not misled into expecting a firm commitment.

Answer Key

Guided Practice

  1. An internal memo typically states institutional plans and constraints directly; a newspaper item typically reports claims attributed to a spokesperson and may omit specific details.
  2. Open production; a good hedged sentence uses language such as "es deutet darauf hin, dass..." or "es bleibt unklar, ob..." rather than stating the claim as settled fact.
  3. Open production; a good attribution sentence begins with a phrase such as "Laut dem Memo..." or "Der Zeitungsartikel berichtet, dass..."
  4. She means that the mediated summary must not claim more certainty or commitment than the source documents actually support.
  5. Open production; a good answer notes that the briefing should avoid privileging one city's perspective and should label information relevant to each city clearly (for example, noting where "Matura" applies to Austria specifically).

Independent Study Tools

Für Selbstlerner

  • Find two short real-world texts about the same topic (one internal-style memo or announcement, one news item) and practice summarizing both together, flagging what is confirmed and what is not.
  • Practice converting a confident-sounding sentence into an appropriately hedged one, and check that the hedge matches what the source actually supports.
  • Ask a study partner to read your mediated summary and try to find a claim that goes beyond what the sources say.

Selbstüberprüfung

  • Did my summary avoid stating anything as certain that the sources only suggested?
  • Did I label every DACH-specific or regional term I used or encountered?
  • Could I answer a follow-up question honestly by saying "that isn't in the sources" where needed?

Übersetzen und Vermitteln auf hohem Niveau

Advanced Translation and Mediation · Speak and Write German: Mastery, Precision, and Specialized Communication · Volume V, Chapter 13

Chapter Overview and Objectives

Chapter 12 asked Tobias to compare two German-language interpretations of the same debate and mediate that debate for a reader who knew neither source. Chapter 13 adds a new dimension to mediation: crossing between German and English for an audience that cannot read the source language at all. Frau Aydın's community center wants to post a short English-language information sheet for newcomers and English-speaking visitors, based on the museum panel and newspaper column discussed in Chapter 12. Tobias is asked to produce a mediated English version — not a word-for-word translation, and not a certified or sworn translation, but a careful, purpose-driven rendering that a general audience can trust. Along the way, he is given a rough machine-generated draft to edit, and he has to decide, sentence by sentence, where to compress, where to expand, and where a literal rendering would mislead an English-speaking reader.

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify points of non-equivalence between a German source text and an English target text.
  2. Choose between compression and expansion strategies based on audience and purpose.
  3. Manage specialized or culturally specific terminology in translation, with brief explanatory annotations.
  4. Edit a rough or machine-generated translation for naturalness, register, and accuracy.
  5. Justify specific translation and mediation choices for a stated audience and purpose.
  6. Produce a polished, annotated mediated text without claiming certified or sworn translation status.

Opening Scenario

Frau Aydın hands Tobias two documents: the German museum panel text from Chapter 12, and a rough English draft that a colleague produced with translation software. "This can't just go up on the wall as it is," she says, pointing at the rough draft. "It's not wrong, exactly, but it doesn't sound like something a museum would actually say in English, and it drops a few things an English-speaking visitor would need." She explains the brief: the sheet is for newcomers and visitors who have no German at all, it needs to fit on a single page, and it must stay neutral about the ongoing debate rather than taking a side. Tobias's task is not to translate every sentence literally, but to mediate the text — deciding what to compress, what to expand with a brief explanation, and how to render a term like Erinnerungskultur for a reader encountering the idea for the first time.

  1. Compare the German source, the rough machine draft, and a polished mediated version sentence by sentence.
  2. Identify at least one place where a literal translation would mislead an English-speaking reader.
  3. Decide where to compress the source and where a short explanatory expansion is needed.
  4. Produce and justify your own mediated version for the stated audience and purpose.
Wortschatz

Core Vocabulary: Translation and Mediation

This chapter activates vocabulary for describing the translation and mediation process itself — the relationship between a source text and a target text, and the decisions a mediator makes between them.

Text und Zielgruppe — Text and Audience

  • der Ausgangstext, die Ausgangstexte — source text
  • der Zieltext, die Zieltexte — target text
  • die Zielgruppe, die Zielgruppen — target audience
  • der Zweck, die Zwecke — purpose
  • der Auftrag, die Aufträge — brief, assignment

Entsprechung und Abweichung — Equivalence and Divergence

  • die Entsprechung, die Entsprechungen — equivalent, correspondence
  • die Abweichung, die Abweichungen — divergence, deviation
  • wörtlich — literal
  • sinngemäß — corresponding in meaning, non-literal

Kürzen und Erweitern — Compressing and Expanding

  • kürzen — to shorten, to compress
  • erweitern — to expand
  • die Kürzung, die Kürzungen — abridgment, cut
  • die Erläuterung, die Erläuterungen — explanatory note

Terminologie verwalten — Managing Terminology

  • der Fachbegriff, die Fachbegriffe — specialized/technical term
  • das Glossar, die Glossare — glossary
  • beglaubigt — certified, sworn (of a translation)
  • die Anmerkung, die Anmerkungen — annotation, note

All active nouns are given with article and plural where a plural is established. This vocabulary supports describing and justifying mediation choices; it is not a substitute for professional translation-studies terminology.

Aussprache

Delivery Focus: Explaining Choices Aloud

Delivery — Signaling a Justification Aloud

When explaining a translation choice aloud, speakers often mark the shift from describing the source to justifying the target-language choice with a brief pause and a slight change in pitch, especially before phrases such as ich habe mich hier bewusst für ... entschieden ("I deliberately chose ... here") or wörtlich hätte das nicht funktioniert ("a literal version would not have worked here"). Stress the contrasted term itself — the German original and the English choice — rather than the surrounding frame words.

Continue to observe final devoicing and compound stress in specialized vocabulary such as Erinnerungskultur and Zielgruppe, and keep the connectors from Chapter 12 — zwar, allerdings, dennoch — available for justifying a choice that partially, but not fully, satisfies competing demands of accuracy and readability.

Grammatik

Grammar and Sentence Patterns: Contrastive Syntax, Register Transfer, Compression and Expansion

This chapter introduces no new grammatical system. It applies clause structure, nominal style, relative clauses, and attribution language already active at this level to the specific task of moving meaning responsibly between German and English.

Contrastive Syntax

German and English organize information differently. German nominal style often compresses a clause into a noun phrase; English frequently prefers a verb-based clause. Recognizing this contrast is necessary before deciding how to render a sentence.

Baustein — Building Block
Die Neugestaltung der Tafel erfolgt in enger Abstimmung mit der Stadtverwaltung.
nominal style: definite article + nominalization ("die Neugestaltung") as grammatical subject, with a prepositional phrase of manner — a structure that reads as natural, formal German but as stiff, over-nominal English if translated word for word
A literal rendering — "The redesign of the panel takes place in close coordination with the city administration" — is grammatical English but sounds like a translated document rather than an original one; most English house styles would prefer a verb-based version: "The city is working closely with the museum to redesign the panel."
Both versions are accurate. The choice depends on whether the target audience expects formal administrative English or plain public-information English.

Register Transfer, Compression, and Expansion

Grammar Note — Moving Register and Density Across Languages
  • A formal German register does not always require a formal English register; a museum panel in German may use nominal style that would sound overly bureaucratic if reproduced literally in English signage.
  • Compression is appropriate when the source contains detail the target audience does not need for the stated purpose; expansion is appropriate when a term or reference (such as Erinnerungskultur) has no compact English equivalent and needs a brief explanatory phrase rather than a single invented word.
  • Relative clauses and appositive noun phrases are useful for folding a short explanatory gloss into an English sentence without adding a separate sentence: "Erinnerungskultur, the German term for the public culture of remembering historical events, ...".
  • Attribution verbs (argumentieren, betonen) from Chapter 12 remain useful for signaling, in the target text, that a claim belongs to a specific source rather than to the museum or the mediator.
Modelltext

Model Text: Source, Mediated Version, and Rough Draft

Three short original texts written for this course: the German source sentence (from the Chapter 12 museum text), a rough machine-style English draft, and a polished mediated version for a general newcomer audience.

Ausgangstext Die ursprüngliche Inschrift spiegelt die Perspektive ihrer Entstehungszeit wider und wird von vielen Besucherinnen und Besuchern heute missverstanden. Das Museum schlägt daher eine zusätzliche Tafel vor, die den historischen Kontext einordnet, ohne das Denkmal selbst zu verändern. (German source text, unchanged from Chapter 12.)
Rohentwurf The original inscription reflects back the perspective of its origination time and becomes misunderstood by many visitors today. The museum proposes therefore an additional panel, which classifies the historical context, without to change the monument itself. (Rough machine-style draft: grammatical but unnatural word order, an awkward passive, and a non-native infinitive construction.)
Vermittelt The monument's original inscription reflects the views of the era in which it was made, and many visitors today misread it as a result. The museum is therefore adding a panel that explains this historical context — the monument itself will not be changed. (Mediated version: natural English word order, an active construction, and a dash used to compress "without altering the monument itself" into a short, reassuring clarification for a general audience.)

Culture and Communication

Culture and Communication — Certified Translation and Everyday Mediation

In Germany, Austria, and Switzerland, certified or sworn translations (beglaubigte Übersetzungen) are typically produced only by translators formally authorized by a court or relevant authority, and they are usually required for official documents such as certificates, contracts, or legal records. The everyday mediation practiced in this chapter — adapting a text for a general audience, choosing what to compress or explain — is a different and much more common activity, but it must never be presented or labeled as a certified translation.

Culture and Communication — Public Information for Multilingual Communities

Community centers, municipal offices, and cultural institutions across German-speaking regions regularly produce multilingual information for residents and visitors who do not yet read German. Producing this material responsibly means respecting the source text's meaning, adapting register and density for the target audience, and being transparent that the result is a mediation rather than a word-for-word or certified rendering.

Common Mistakes to Recheck

Translating German nominal style word for word

Error: A learner renders "Die Neugestaltung der Tafel erfolgt in enger Abstimmung mit der Stadtverwaltung" as "The redesign of the panel takes place in close coordination with the city administration," producing grammatical but stiff, over-nominal English.

Correction: Rebuild the sentence around a verb suited to English style, such as "The museum is working closely with the city to redesign the panel," when the audience and purpose call for plain public-information English.

Inventing a compact English word for a culturally specific term

Error: A learner invents a single English word to stand in for Erinnerungskultur, producing a term that sounds authoritative but does not actually exist in English usage.

Correction: Use a brief explanatory phrase instead of an invented one-word equivalent, and consider keeping the German term with a short gloss on first use.

Implying certified translation status

Error: A learner labels a mediated information sheet as an "official translation" or otherwise implies it has legal or certified standing.

Correction: Label the text clearly as a summary, adaptation, or informational translation, and never claim sworn or certified status without the relevant qualification.

Answer Key

Guided Practice

  1. "becomes misunderstood by many visitors today" is an unnatural passive; "without to change the monument" uses a non-native infinitive pattern where English requires a gerund or clause ("without changing").
  2. The mediated version shifts from a passive built on the inscription ("is misunderstood") to an active construction with visitors as the grammatical subject ("many visitors ... misread it"), which reads more naturally in English and keeps the sentence from feeling translated.
  3. The dash construction compresses "an additional panel that places the historical context, without altering the monument itself" into a shorter, reassuring clarification; it preserves the reassurance that the monument itself is unchanged while trimming the more abstract phrase "historical context is placed."
  4. Open production; a reasonable answer notes that "monument" alone is broadly understandable to an English-speaking newcomer audience and may not need further glossing, unlike a more specialized term such as "Erinnerungskultur."
  5. Open production; a good answer notes that the mediated version reads naturally, fits the stated audience and one-page brief, and remains neutral, while explicitly stating that it is a mediation for the website rather than a certified translation.

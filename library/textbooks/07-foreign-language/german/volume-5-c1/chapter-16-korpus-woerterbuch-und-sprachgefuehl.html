Korpus, Wörterbuch und Sprachgefühl

Corpus, Dictionary, and Usage Judgment · Speak and Write German: Mastery, Precision, and Specialized Communication · Volume V, Chapter 16

Chapter Overview and Objectives

Chapter 16 opens Unit IV and turns from mediation toward precision. Tobias has started noticing that his German is fluent but occasionally slightly "off" — a word that is technically correct but not quite what a native editor would choose, a collocation that sounds translated rather than natural. This chapter introduces the intelligent use of dictionaries, corpora, and reference tools: not as a substitute for judgment, but as evidence that supports it. Learners will practice reading dictionary entries and usage notes closely, distinguishing reliable frequency impressions from guesswork, analyzing collocations, and writing a short usage note that is grounded in reference evidence rather than intuition alone. No new grammar system is activated; the chapter instead builds a research habit that will support the precision-focused chapters ahead.

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Read a dictionary or usage-note entry and identify its register, collocation, and usage information.
  2. Distinguish a well-supported usage claim from an unverified guess or overgeneralization.
  3. Compare two near-synonyms using collocation and register evidence rather than intuition alone.
  4. Revise a sentence that sounds unnatural or translated, using reference evidence to justify the change.
  5. Write a short usage note that documents a word choice for another learner or colleague.
  6. Explain, in speech, why one word choice fits a context better than another, citing evidence rather than asserting a preference.

Opening Scenario

A colleague, Herr Böhm, is reviewing a document Tobias drafted and stops on one sentence. "You wrote 'wir haben das Problem gemacht'," he says, "but nobody would really say that. We'd say 'ein Problem verursachen' or 'ein Problem schaffen.' Can you check why, and note it somewhere so the next person doesn't repeat it?" Tobias realizes he chose machen because it felt like the safest, most general verb — a common habit for learners moving a verb like "make" directly from English. Rather than guessing at a fix, he decides to look up how Problem actually collocates with verbs in current usage, compare a few near-synonyms, and write a short usage note explaining the more natural choice and why.

  1. Identify why "das Problem machen" sounds unnatural, even though it is grammatically well formed.
  2. Compare the collocational behavior of machen, verursachen, and schaffen with the noun Problem.
  3. Consult a dictionary or usage reference to support the comparison rather than relying on impression alone.
  4. Write a short usage note documenting the more natural choice for future reference.
Wortschatz

Core Vocabulary: Dictionaries, Corpora, Usage

This chapter activates vocabulary for talking about reference tools and word-level precision. These items are introduced for productive use in describing and justifying word choices.

Nachschlagewerke — Reference Tools

  • das Wörterbuch, die Wörterbücher — dictionary
  • das Korpus, die Korpora — (text) corpus
  • der Beleg, die Belege — attested example, evidence
  • nachschlagen — to look up (a word, a reference)
  • die Quellenlage — state of the available sources/evidence

Wortgebrauch — Word Usage

  • die Kollokation, die Kollokationen — collocation
  • die Valenz — valency (a verb's argument pattern)
  • das Synonym, die Synonyme — synonym
  • gebräuchlich — commonly used, in current use
  • ungewöhnlich klingen — to sound unusual
  • der Sprachgebrauch — language usage, usage patterns

The verbs verursachen and schaffen are treated here as review items from Volume III and IV vocabulary; their collocation behavior with Problem is the new productive focus of this chapter.

Aussprache

Delivery Focus: Reading Reference Entries Aloud

Delivery — Reading Dense Reference Text Clearly

Dictionary and usage-note entries are often dense: abbreviations, example sentences, and labels packed into a small space. When reading such an entry aloud — for example, to explain a usage point to a colleague — slow down at abbreviations and labels rather than rushing past them, and pause briefly between the headword, the label, and the example sentence so a listener can follow the structure. This is a delivery skill, not a new pronunciation system: it reuses the intelligibility and pacing practices already established in earlier chapters.

When citing an example sentence from a reference source aloud, mark clearly where the citation begins and ends, for instance by saying "zum Beispiel..." before the example and pausing afterward, so the listener does not mistake the cited example for your own claim.

Sprachliche Muster

Language Patterns: Collocation, Valency, and Usage Labels

This chapter does not introduce a new grammar system. It introduces a way of talking about word behavior: collocation, valency, and lexicographic labels. These patterns support the precision work of the rest of Unit IV.

Describing a Collocation

Baustein — Building Block
"X" kollokiert mit "Y" / "X" passt gut zu "Y" / "X" wirkt hier ungewöhnlich
a small set of phrases for describing whether a word combination is natural, drawing on the noun-phrase and precision vocabulary already active from Chapters 9 and 17-adjacent material
Das Verb "verursachen" kollokiert häufig mit "Problem", "Schaden" und "Verzögerung", während "machen" in diesem Kontext ungewöhnlich wirkt.
The verb "verursachen" commonly collocates with "Problem," "Schaden," and "Verzögerung," while "machen" sounds unusual in this context.

Citing Evidence Without Fabricating Numbers

Language Note — Talking About Frequency Responsibly

When describing how common a word or collocation is, use qualitative language such as häufig (frequently), gelegentlich (occasionally), selten (rarely), or eher ungewöhnlich (rather unusual) rather than inventing specific statistics. A usage note should describe what a dictionary, corpus, or other reference tool actually shows, or state clearly that the judgment is based on general impression when no reference has been consulted. Do not present an invented frequency count or an unverified percentage as if it were attested evidence.

Reading a Valency Pattern

Language Note — What a Valency Entry Tells You

A dictionary or usage reference often lists a verb's valency: which case its objects take, which prepositions it governs, and what kind of subject or object it typically combines with. For example, an entry for verursachen typically shows an accusative object (etwas verursachen) and lists typical objects such as Schaden, Probleme, or Verzögerungen. Reading this information carefully, rather than assuming a verb behaves like its English translation, is the core skill of this chapter.

Modelltext

Model Text: A Question About a Word

A short original exchange between Herr Böhm and Tobias, written for this course, establishing the chapter's task.

Böhm Tobias, hier: "Wir haben das Problem gemacht." Das klingt nicht ganz natürlich. Was würden Sie stattdessen sagen? (Tobias, here: "We made the problem." That doesn't sound quite natural. What would you say instead?)
Tobias Ich glaube, "verursachen" oder "schaffen" passt hier besser, aber ich schlage es lieber noch einmal nach, bevor ich es ändere. (I think "verursachen" or "schaffen" fits better here, but I'd rather look it up again before I change it.)
Böhm Gute Idee. Und schreiben Sie kurz auf, warum, damit wir es beim nächsten Mal nicht vergessen. (Good idea. And write down briefly why, so we don't forget it next time.)

Wenn man eine Fremdsprache auf hohem Niveau benutzt, merkt man oft nicht sofort, wenn ein Wort zwar richtig, aber nicht ganz natürlich ist. Speaking Practice: Defending a Word Choice

Aufgabe: Wortwahl begründen

Working with a partner or by recording yourself, choose one pair of near-synonyms (for example, verursachen vs. machen, or another pair supplied by your instructor) and explain, in a short spoken exchange, which one fits a given sentence better and why.

Rolle: Vorschlagende Person

  • Propose a word choice for a given sentence.
  • Explain the reasoning using collocation or valency language from this chapter.
  • Avoid stating an invented frequency statistic; use qualitative language instead.

Rolle: Prüfende Person

  • Ask why the proposed word fits better than an alternative.
  • Ask what evidence (dictionary, corpus, general impression) supports the claim.
  • Push back gently if the justification sounds like a guess rather than evidence.

Bewertungshinweise — Speaking Criteria

  • Clearly states a word-choice recommendation and a plausible alternative.
  • Justifies the choice using collocation or valency reasoning, not intuition alone.
  • Avoids fabricated frequency claims; uses qualitative usage language appropriately.
  • Responds to follow-up questions about the evidence honestly.
  • Maintains clear, appropriately paced delivery, including when citing an example.

Solo alternative: Record yourself explaining your word choice, then listen back and check whether your justification relied on evidence or only on a feeling that one word "sounds better."

Reading Practice: Two Dictionary Entries

Wörterbucheintrag (für den Kurs vereinfacht) — Dictionary Entry, Simplified for This Course

verursachen

verursachen (schwaches Verb, trennt sich nicht) — etwas (Akk.) verursachen: dafür sorgen, dass etwas (meist Unerwünschtes) entsteht oder passiert. Typische Objekte: Schaden, Probleme, Verzögerungen, Kosten, Missverständnisse. Register: neutral bis formell; in Berichten, Verträgen und offizieller Korrespondenz gebräuchlich. Beispiel: "Der Sturm hat erhebliche Schäden verursacht."

Wörterbucheintrag (für den Kurs vereinfacht) — Dictionary Entry, Simplified for This Course

machen

machen (schwaches Verb) — etwas (Akk.) machen: ein sehr allgemeines Verb für Herstellen, Tun, Erledigen. Typische Objekte: Hausaufgaben, Kaffee, Fotos, Pläne, Fehler. Hinweis: Bei abstrakten negativen Ereignissen wie "Problem" oder "Schaden" wird "machen" seltener verwendet als spezifischere Verben wie "verursachen" oder "anrichten"; die Kombination kann in formellen Texten ungewöhnlich wirken.

Culture and Communication

Culture and Communication — Tools Support Judgment, They Do Not Replace It

Dictionaries, corpora, and usage references are valuable because they show real, attested language rather than a single speaker's impression. But no tool automatically knows the right register, audience, or purpose for a given text. A learner who mechanically follows the first example found in a corpus, without judging whether it fits the current context, can end up with a technically attested but poorly chosen phrase. The skill this chapter builds is not "look it up" alone, but "look it up, and then judge whether it fits."

Regional Awareness — Reference Tools and DACH Variation

Some dictionaries and corpora are built primarily from Germany-based sources; others include Austrian and Swiss material more thoroughly. A usage note that draws only on one national source should say so, rather than presenting a Germany-based judgment as automatically valid for all DACH contexts. This continues the labeling habit built in Chapter 14: variation is not an error, and its source should be named.

Common Mistakes to Recheck

Treating a general verb as always safe

Error: A learner defaults to a very general verb such as machen for abstract negative outcomes, assuming it is always an acceptable, neutral choice.

Correction: Check the noun's typical collocating verbs (ein Problem verursachen, einen Fehler machen, einen Schaden anrichten) rather than defaulting to one general verb everywhere.

Inventing a frequency claim

Error: A learner writes that a word is used "in 80 percent of cases" without having consulted any actual reference source.

Correction: Use qualitative language such as häufig or selten, or state plainly that the judgment is a general impression rather than a measured statistic.

Copying a corpus example without checking fit

Error: A learner copies the first example sentence found in a corpus search into a formal document, without checking whether its register matches the document.

Correction: Check the register and context of any reference example before reusing it, and adapt it if necessary rather than pasting it unchanged.

Wiederholung 1: Hochriskante Korrespondenz

High-Stakes Correspondence Assessment · Speak and Write German: Mastery, Precision, and Specialized Communication · Volume V, Chapter 5 · Unit I Assessment

Chapter Overview and Objectives

Chapter 5 closes Unit I by asking Tobias to bring together everything he has consolidated across Chapters 1 through 4: an honest audit of his own strengths and weaknesses, control of complex syntax and punctuation, a flexible register repertoire, and the ability to read and produce precise administrative prose. Herr Brandt raises the stakes: instead of a single controlled exercise, Tobias must handle a short sequence of high-stakes correspondence — a formal complaint, an institutional request, and a professional follow-up — in which a wrong tone, an ambiguous sentence, or a misjudged register could genuinely cost him credibility with the reader. This chapter introduces no new grammar. It is a cumulative assessment chapter: every task draws only on systems already consolidated in Unit I, and every criterion measures how precisely and appropriately Tobias applies them under realistic pressure.

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Select and sustain an appropriate register across an entire piece of correspondence, from opening to closing.
  2. Use nominal style and modal passive constructions accurately and only where they suit the genre.
  3. Punctuate complex, multi-clause sentences correctly in formal written German.
  4. Write with enough precision to avoid ambiguity in a high-stakes institutional or professional context.
  5. Revise a draft for tone, clarity, and audience fit, and explain the reasoning behind specific revisions.
  6. Distinguish firm, accurate correspondence from correspondence that is aggressive, vague, or inappropriately informal.
  7. Discuss, in speech, the choices behind a piece of formal correspondence and defend them under mild pushback.

Opening Scenario

Herr Brandt tells Tobias that Unit I ends the way real high-stakes writing often begins: with a problem that must be handled in writing, correctly, the first time. He presents Tobias with a short case file — a delayed refund from a fictional service provider, an unclear clause in a fictional service contract, and a missed deadline that was not entirely Tobias's fault. Tobias must write a firm but professional complaint, a follow-up institutional request for clarification, and a short internal note explaining why he chose the tone and structure he did. Herr Brandt reminds him that in a real situation, a poorly judged sentence in a complaint can undermine an otherwise valid claim, so precision here is not academic; it is the whole point of the exercise.

  1. Review the case file and identify the specific complaint, request, and follow-up required.
  2. Draft each piece of correspondence in a register appropriate to its audience and purpose.
  3. Revise all three pieces for punctuation, nominal style accuracy, and clarity.
  4. Write a short note justifying your key tone and structure choices.

Aussprache

Delivery Focus: Reading Correspondence Aloud

Delivery — Firmness Without Aggression

When reading a formal complaint aloud, learners often either flatten every sentence into the same neutral tone or overcorrect into an aggressive, clipped delivery that would not match the measured written register. Practice reading a firm complaint with even pacing and slightly increased stress on the key factual claim — the delay, the deadline, the amount — rather than on emotionally loaded words.

This is a continuation of the pacing and stress-for-emphasis work from earlier chapters, applied to the specific challenge of sounding firm and professional at once, rather than a new sound system.

Grammatik

Grammar Consolidation: Register, Nominal Style, and Punctuation

Chapter 5 activates no new grammar. It consolidates three systems from earlier chapters that must work together for high-stakes correspondence to succeed: register consistency, accurate nominal/modal-passive style, and correct punctuation of complex sentences.

Register Consistency Across a Whole Text

A single formal letter must not drift between registers. A common failure is opening with a correctly formal salutation, then sliding into a conversational tone mid-paragraph once the writer becomes emotionally invested in the complaint.

Grammar Note — Checking Register Consistency

After drafting, reread the letter and mark every sentence as formal, neutral, or informal. A high-stakes letter should show only formal and neutral sentences. Any informal sentence — a contraction, a casual connector, an unhedged emotional judgment — should be rewritten in the established register before the letter is considered finished.

Nominal Style and Modal Passive: Precision Without Vagueness

Chapter 4 introduced nominal style and the modal passive as tools for compact, impersonal official prose. In high-stakes correspondence, these tools must be used precisely enough to state facts and requests clearly, not so heavily that the letter becomes evasive about who is responsible for what.

Baustein — Building Block
Die Rückerstattung ist bislang nicht erfolgt. → Der vereinbarte Betrag muss bis zum 20. dieses Monats zurückerstattet werden.
a vague nominalized observation, revised into a precise modal passive statement naming the amount and the deadline
The first sentence only states that a refund "has not occurred"; the revision specifies who owes what by when, which is what a high-stakes complaint requires.
The refund has not occurred so far. → The agreed amount must be refunded by the 20th of this month.

Punctuation of Complex Sentences Under Pressure

Long, multi-clause sentences are common in formal correspondence, and punctuation errors in them are especially damaging because they can make a claim genuinely ambiguous rather than merely informal.

  • Da die Rückerstattung, die für den 5. dieses Monats vereinbart war, bislang nicht erfolgt ist, bitte ich Sie, mir den aktuellen Bearbeitungsstand mitzuteilen. Since the refund, which was agreed for the 5th of this month, has not yet occurred, I ask you to inform me of the current processing status. subordinate clause + embedded relative clause, correctly bounded by commas
  • Sollte bis zum 20. dieses Monats keine Rückmeldung erfolgen, sehe ich mich gezwungen, weitere Schritte einzuleiten. Should no response occur by the 20th of this month, I will be forced to take further steps. verb-first conditional clause, comma before the main clause
Common Reading and Writing Error — Missing a Comma Around an Embedded Relative Clause

Error: A learner writes a relative clause embedded inside a longer subordinate clause but omits one or both of the commas that bound it, making it unclear where the relative clause ends and the main statement resumes.

Correction: Every relative clause needs a comma both before and after it, even when it is nested inside another clause. Read the sentence back and confirm that removing the relative clause leaves a grammatically complete outer sentence.

Modelltext

Model Correspondence: A Firm, Well-Judged Complaint

A model complaint letter Herr Brandt shares as a benchmark, addressed to a fictional service provider in Germany, before Tobias drafts his own case-file correspondence.

Anrede Sehr geehrte Damen und Herren, (Dear Sir or Madam,)
Absatz 1 bezugnehmend auf unseren Vertrag vom 3. dieses Monats möchte ich auf einen bislang ungeklärten Punkt hinweisen: Die vereinbarte Rückerstattung in Höhe von 240 Euro ist trotz Ablauf der vereinbarten Frist nicht erfolgt. (With reference to our contract of the 3rd of this month, I would like to point out an unresolved matter: the agreed refund of 240 euros has not occurred despite the passing of the agreed deadline.)
Absatz 2 Ich bitte Sie höflich, aber nachdrücklich, mir den aktuellen Bearbeitungsstand innerhalb von fünf Werktagen mitzuteilen. Sollte bis dahin keine Rückmeldung erfolgen, behalte ich mir weitere Schritte vor. (I ask you politely but firmly to inform me of the current processing status within five working days. Should no response occur by then, I reserve the right to take further steps.)
Schluss Mit freundlichen Grüßen (With friendly regards — standard formal closing)

Speaking Assessment

Aufgabe: Korrespondenzentscheidungen verteidigen

Working with a partner, a small group, or by recording yourself, present your case-file complaint aloud and explain the tone, structure, and word choices you made, then respond to at least one challenge to a specific decision — for example, being asked whether a sentence is too aggressive or too vague.

Rolle: Verfassende Person

  • Present the complaint's key claim, request, and deadline.
  • Explain one specific register or word choice and why it fits the audience.
  • Respond to a challenge by defending or revising the choice on the spot.

Rolle: Prüfende Person

  • Challenge one sentence as potentially too informal, too vague, or too aggressive.
  • Ask whether the deadline and request are stated unambiguously.
  • Confirm that the register stays consistent from opening to closing.

Bewertungshinweise — Speaking Criteria

  • Clearly states the complaint, request, and deadline from the case file.
  • Justifies specific tone and structure choices with reference to audience and purpose.
  • Responds to pushback by defending or appropriately revising a choice.
  • Maintains a consistent, appropriately formal register throughout the discussion.
  • Speaks with intelligible pronunciation and controlled pacing under mild pressure.

Solo alternative: Record yourself presenting and defending your correspondence choices, then listen back and identify one moment where your justification could be more precise.

Schreiben

Writing Assessment

Complete the full case-file correspondence sequence: a formal complaint, an institutional follow-up request, and a short internal justification note. This is the chapter's primary graded task.

Fallakte — Case File

Ein fiktiver Dienstleister in Deutschland hat Ihnen eine Rückerstattung von 240 Euro zugesagt, die zehn Tage nach der vereinbarten Frist noch immer nicht erfolgt ist. Der zugrunde liegende Vertrag enthält eine Klausel zu "höherer Gewalt", deren genaue Bedeutung im aktuellen Fall unklar ist. Verfassen Sie: (1) eine formelle Beschwerde an den Dienstleister, (2) eine Nachfrage an die zuständige Stelle zur Klärung der Klausel, und (3) eine kurze interne Notiz, in der Sie Ihre Ton- und Strukturentscheidungen begründen.

Die drei Korrespondenzstücke — The Three Correspondence Pieces

  1. Beschwerde (Complaint): a formal letter to the service provider stating the unmet deadline and requesting the refund by a specific date.
  2. Nachfrage (Institutional Request): a formal inquiry asking the responsible office to clarify how the "höhere Gewalt" clause applies in this case.
  3. Begründungsnotiz (Justification Note): a short internal note explaining your register, structure, and word-choice decisions in both letters.
  1. Reread the case file and identify the specific facts, deadline, and unclear clause.
  2. Draft the complaint, opening and closing in consistently formal register.
  3. Draft the institutional follow-up request, using precise nominal style and modal passive constructions.
  4. Reread both letters sentence by sentence, marking each as formal, neutral, or informal, and revise any inconsistency.
  5. Check every complex sentence for correct comma placement around subordinate and relative clauses.
  6. Write the justification note, naming at least two specific tone or structure choices and explaining why they suit the audience.
  7. Confirm that the jurisdiction (Germany, in this exercise) is identified where relevant, and that no legal advice is offered.

Revision checklist:

  • Does every sentence in both letters match a single, consistent formal register?
  • Are the deadline, amount, and request stated with no possible ambiguity?
  • Is every complex sentence punctuated correctly, especially around embedded relative clauses?
  • Does the justification note explain specific choices rather than general impressions?
  • Have I avoided offering a legal opinion about the "höhere Gewalt" clause itself?

Culture and Communication

Culture and Communication — Firmness Is Not the Same as Hostility

Effective formal German correspondence can be firm, precise, and even insistent while remaining fully polite and professional. Conflating directness with rudeness, or softness with weakness, misreads how formal register actually functions: a well-constructed complaint states facts and consequences clearly without abandoning the conventions of respectful address.

Culture and Communication — This Task Remains Language Practice, Not Legal Action

As in Chapter 4, this assessment trains precise reading and writing of contract-like and institutional language. It does not train learners to give legal advice or to resolve a real contractual dispute, and any resemblance between the fictional case file and a real situation should be treated as coincidental. A learner facing an actual dispute should consult a qualified professional or the responsible office.

Common Mistakes to Recheck

Letting emotion shift the register mid-letter

Error: A learner opens with a correctly formal salutation, then slides into informal phrasing or emotionally charged, unhedged language once describing the frustrating delay.

Correction: Reread the letter sentence by sentence and confirm every sentence matches the register established in the opening. Replace any emotionally loaded or informal phrasing with a factual, formally phrased equivalent.

Using nominal style to avoid stating who owes what

Error: A learner writes a vague nominalized sentence such as "eine Klärung wäre wünschenswert" instead of stating precisely what is owed, by whom, and by when.

Correction: In high-stakes correspondence, nominal style must still convey a precise claim. State the amount, the responsible party, and the deadline explicitly, even within a compact nominal or modal-passive construction.

Punctuation errors that create real ambiguity

Error: A learner omits a comma around an embedded relative clause, so that a reader cannot tell whether a condition applies to the whole claim or only to part of it.

Correction: Read every long sentence back and confirm that removing any relative or subordinate clause, with its commas, leaves a complete and unambiguous main statement.

Answer Key

Guided Practice

  1. All sentences in the model letter remain formal; the salutation, both body paragraphs, and the closing use consistent formal vocabulary and structure with no informal drift.
  2. "behalte ich mir weitere Schritte vor" is not itself modal passive, but "mitzuteilen" following "bitte ich Sie" expresses a polite request-obligation; the clearer modal-passive example is the underlying paraphrase "der Bearbeitungsstand muss mitgeteilt werden," expressing what the writer requires the reader to do.
  3. The comma before "sollte" is not required since it begins the sentence; the comma after the conditional clause ("...erfolgen, behalte ich...") correctly separates the fronted subordinate clause from the main clause that follows.
  4. A vaguer rewrite might read "Die Angelegenheit bezüglich der Rückerstattung ist noch offen," which avoids stating the amount, the deadline, or who owes what — illustrating why the original's precision matters.
  5. "Ich bitte Sie höflich, aber nachdrücklich" keeps the tone firm through the adverb "nachdrücklich" while "höflich" and the surrounding formal structure prevent it from reading as aggressive.

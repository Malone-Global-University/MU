فِي صَفّ اللُّغَة العَرَبِيَّةIn Arabic Class

Classroom objects, the definite article, noun gender, and new letters · Speak and Write Arabic: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Name common classroom objects in Arabic using the correct gender for each noun.
  2. Apply the definite article الـ (al-) to make a noun definite, including when sun-letter assimilation changes the pronunciation.
  3. Form simple nominal sentences identifying and describing classroom objects.
  4. Use three new diacritics — the sukūn (ْ), the shadda (ّ), and the tanwīn (ً ٍ ٌ) — to read and write vocalized text more accurately.
  5. Read and write the second group of Arabic letters in all four positional forms.
  6. Ask for clarification using the expressions مَا هَذَا؟ and مَا هَذِهِ؟
  7. Use polite classroom language to follow, slow down, or request repetition from an instructor.
  8. Recycle Chapter 1 greetings and name-exchange formulas in a classroom setting.

Communication Scenario

The first class session at the language institute in Amman has just begun. Ustādh Khālid holds up various objects — a book, a pen, a board — and asks the class in Arabic what each one is. A student doesn't catch a word; another needs to know whether to point or to speak. You are surrounded by the tools of language learning for the first time, all of them named in Arabic.

  1. What do you do when an instructor says something you don't understand? In English, you probably have several polite strategies. By the end of this chapter, you will have the same strategies available in Arabic.
  2. You already know how to greet your instructor and give your name. Now the conversation will move forward: the classroom itself becomes the topic, and every object around you is a new Arabic word to learn.

Script Note — Three letters, one base shape

The letters ج, ح, and خ share the same underlying shape. They are distinguished by dots: ج has one dot inside the curve, ح has no dot at all, and خ has one dot above. Similarly, س (three small teeth, no dots) and ش (three small teeth, three dots above) form a pair. Dots are identity, not decoration — a missing or misplaced dot produces a different letter and a different word.

Script Note — Nonjoining letters in this group

Like ا, د, and ذ from Chapter 1, the letters ر and ز do not connect to the letter that follows them. A preceding letter still connects to them from the right, but the word "breaks" visually after ر or ز even though the letters belong to the same word. This is a spelling feature, not an error.

حَرَكَات جَدِيدَة — Three new diacritics introduced

Chapter 1 introduced the three short vowel marks (fatḥa, kasra, ḍamma). This chapter introduces three more diacritics that complete the basic vocalization system you will use throughout the course.

The sukūn (ْ) is a small circle placed above a consonant to show that it carries no vowel at all. It signals a "closed" syllable. In the word دَفْتَرٌ (notebook), the فْ carries a sukūn: the /f/ has no vowel after it, so you move immediately to the next syllable.

The shadda (ّ) is a small ش-like mark that doubles a consonant. بّ is pronounced like two /b/ sounds held together. In the word صَفٌّ (class), the فّ is a doubled /f/. English has a close analogy in words like "better" where the /t/ is held slightly longer, though Arabic gemination is more distinct. The shadda can combine with a short vowel mark placed above it.

Tanwīn is the doubling of a short vowel mark to signal that a noun is indefinite. The double ḍamma (ٌ) sounds like -un, the double kasra (ٍ) sounds like -in, and the double fatḥa (ً) sounds like -an. In pause position — when a word stands at the end of an utterance or is spoken in isolation — tanwīn is typically not pronounced. You will practice this naturally through reading aloud.

Pronunciation Note — Tanwīn in everyday speech

In careful formal reading and in this textbook's vocalized vocabulary tables, nouns appear with tanwīn endings such as كِتَابٌ. In ordinary pause pronunciation — the way a word sounds when you stop at the end of a sentence — the tanwīn is typically not pronounced; you say kitāb, not kitābun. Tanwīn is shown here so you can read vocalized text accurately; you will hear both forms in authentic formal MSA, and you will learn more about when each is appropriate as the course progresses.

تَدْرِيب — Practice: Read each of the following words aloud, identifying the sukūn, shadda, or tanwīn in each: دَفْتَرٌ — صَفٌّ — كِتَابٌ — شُكْرًا — حَسَنًا. For each word, identify which new diacritic is present and explain what it tells you about the pronunciation.

Pronunciation

Three new sounds: ح، خ، ر

ح (ḥāʾ) is a voiceless pharyngeal fricative — a sound produced deep in the throat by constricting the pharynx while releasing a stream of air. It has no close English equivalent. The closest approximation is an intensely "whispery" /h/ produced much further back than the English /h/ in "house." The back of the throat feels tightened. This is one of the consonants English speakers must practice most carefully; without it, حَال (situation) and هَال (cardamom) are confused.

خ (khāʾ) is a voiceless velar fricative — the raspy sound at the end of German Bach or Scottish loch. It is produced by raising the back of the tongue toward the soft palate while air flows through. English speakers who know German, Spanish (as in jota), or Hebrew often recognize it immediately; others need sustained practice.

ر (rāʾ) in MSA is typically a tapped or lightly trilled /r/, similar to the Spanish single r in pero. It is produced with a quick flip of the tongue tip against the alveolar ridge. Avoid the English approximant /r/ (the "r" in American English "read"), which sounds markedly foreign in Arabic.

Pronunciation Note — ح vs. هـ: a distinction that matters

The letters ح and هـ are both voiceless and breathy, but they are very different consonants. هـ (hāʾ) is a glottal fricative like the English /h/ in "hello" — produced at the glottis with no pharyngeal constriction. ح is pharyngeal — much deeper. Confusing them changes the meaning of words. Practice the contrast: حَال (situation) vs. هَال (cardamom); حَبّ (seeds/love) vs. هَبّ (blew/surged).

Article assimilation: sun letters and moon letters

The definite article الـ is written the same way before all nouns, but it is not always pronounced the same way. When the noun begins with one of fourteen consonants called sun letters (الحُرُوف الشَّمْسِيَّة), the ل of the article assimilates to that consonant — that is, the /l/ is replaced in pronunciation by a doubled version of the first consonant of the noun.

  • الصَّفُّaṣ-ṣaffu — the class (sun letter: ص)The /l/ becomes a second /ṣ/, doubling the first consonant of the noun
  • السَّبُّورَةُas-sabbūratu — the board (sun letter: س)The /l/ becomes a second /s/
  • الكِتَابُal-kitābu — the book (moon letter: ك)The /l/ is pronounced normally; ك is a moon letter
  • الدَّفْتَرُad-daftaru — the notebook (sun letter: د)The /l/ becomes /d/

The sun letters include: ت، ث، د، ذ، ر، ز، س، ش، ص، ض، ط، ظ، ل، ن. The moon letters (all others) leave the ل pronounced. The spelling of الـ never changes; only the pronunciation does. In vocalized text, the shadda on the first letter of the noun after the article signals assimilation.

Grammar Instruction

2.1 الـ — The definite article developed

Purpose: make nouns definite by attaching the article, and learn the difference between a definite and an indefinite noun phrase.

Arabic uses the prefix الـ (al-) to make a noun definite, similar to English "the." Indefinite nouns carry no article (and in fully vocalized text they carry tanwīn):

  • كِتَابٌkitābun — a book (indefinite)
  • الكِتَابُal-kitābu — the book (definite)
  • سَبُّورَةٌsabbūratun — a board (indefinite, feminine)
  • السَّبُّورَةُas-sabbūratu — the board (definite, feminine)
Grammar Note — Definiteness and adjective agreement

When a noun takes the definite article, its adjective must also take the definite article. An indefinite noun is described by an indefinite adjective; a definite noun is described by a definite adjective. Both noun and adjective change together:

  • كِتَابٌ جَدِيدٌ — a new book (indefinite noun + indefinite adjective)
  • الكِتَابُ الجَدِيدُ — the new book (definite noun + definite adjective)
  • سَبُّورَةٌ كَبِيرَةٌ — a big board (indefinite, feminine)
  • السَّبُّورَةُ الكَبِيرَةُ — the big board (definite, feminine)

2.2 الجُمْلَة الاسْمِيَّة — Nominal sentences with classroom objects developed

Purpose: identify, describe, and discuss classroom objects using complete nominal sentences.

Chapter 1 introduced nominal sentences for names (اسْمِي سَارَة). Now you will use the same sentence type to identify objects and attach descriptive adjectives. Recall: present-tense Arabic sentences of identity and description contain no separate word for "is."

  • هَذَا كِتَابٌ.hāḏā kitābun. — This is a book.Demonstrative (masc.) + indefinite noun (masc.)
  • هَذِهِ سَبُّورَةٌ.hāḏihi sabbūratun. — This is a board.Demonstrative (fem.) + indefinite noun (fem.)
  • هَذَا الكِتَابُ جَدِيدٌ.hāḏā l-kitābu jadīdun. — This book is new.Demonstrative + definite noun (masc.) + indefinite adjective (masc.)
  • السَّبُّورَةُ كَبِيرَةٌ.as-sabbūratu kabīratun. — The board is big.Definite noun (fem.) + indefinite adjective (fem.)
Grammar Note — Definite noun, indefinite adjective as predicate

When the adjective follows a definite noun as a predicate (telling you something about the noun), it stays indefinite. Only when the adjective is an attribute — part of the noun phrase, directly modifying the noun — does it take the definite article. Compare:

  • الكِتَابُ الجَدِيدُ — the new book (attributive: one definite noun phrase, adjective is part of it)
  • الكِتَابُ جَدِيدٌ. — The book is new. (predicative: the adjective is the predicate of the sentence; it stays indefinite)

This distinction — the new book vs. the book is new — is fundamental in Arabic grammar. It will become second nature through practice.

2.3 مَا هَذَا؟ / مَا هَذِهِ؟ — Asking what something is formulaic

Purpose: ask a clarification question about an object's name, choosing the correct question formula based on the noun's gender.

Use مَا هَذَا؟ when pointing at a masculine noun and مَا هَذِهِ؟ when pointing at a feminine noun. Both mean "What is this?" The answer uses the matching demonstrative:

  • مَا هَذَا؟ — هَذَا قَلَمٌ.What is this? — This is a pen. (masc.)
  • مَا هَذِهِ؟ — هَذِهِ حَقِيبَةٌ.What is this? — This is a bag. (fem.)

The demonstratives هَذَا (masc.) and هَذِهِ (fem.) will be analyzed as a grammar system beginning in Chapter 6. For now, learn them as parts of the مَا هَذَا/مَا هَذِهِ question-and-answer formulas.

Language Focus

Gender is a system, not a detail. In English, only pronouns (he, she, it) mark gender, and objects are typically "it." In Arabic, every noun has gender, and that gender ripples outward: demonstratives, adjectives, verbs, and pronouns all agree with the noun they reference. Every time you learn a new noun in this course, also learn its gender — they are inseparable.

قَائِمَة المُرَاجَعَة — Editing checklist:

  • Every new letter from this chapter has its correct number and position of dots.
  • I wrote right to left with correct letter joining; ر and ز do not connect to the letter that follows them.
  • The sukūn (ْ) appears on consonants that carry no vowel.
  • Each demonstrative (هَذَا or هَذِهِ) matches the gender of the noun it points at.
  • Each adjective carries the tāʾ marbūṭa (ة) when its noun is feminine and has no ة when its noun is masculine.
  • In definite noun phrases ("the big book"), both the noun and the adjective carry الـ.
  • In predicative sentences ("The book is new"), the adjective stays indefinite.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Arabic-medium education across the region

Modern Standard Arabic is the language of formal education in school and university systems across the Arabic-speaking world, from Morocco to the Arabian Gulf. Subjects from mathematics and science to history and literature are taught in MSA — or in a register close to it — in most state educational systems. Private and international schools vary widely. Learning MSA means gaining access not only to conversation but to one of the world's most extensive educational and intellectual traditions. The classroom, in a very real sense, is where MSA lives.

Culture in Context — The instructor-student relationship

Across many Arabic-speaking educational contexts, instructors are addressed with considerable respect. Titles such as أُسْتَاذ and أُسْتَاذَة signal formal acknowledgment of knowledge and professional role. In many classrooms, students stand when an instructor enters, and interrupting or contradicting a teacher directly is considered less appropriate than it might be in some other educational cultures. These norms vary by country, institution, generation, and individual — but the expectation of polite, respectful classroom behavior is broadly shared.

The classroom phrases in this chapter (أَعِدْ مِنْ فَضْلِكَ, لَا أَفْهَم) are appropriately polite ways to participate and ask for help without appearing rude or dismissive.

Regional Variation — Object names in spoken varieties

Some of the classroom object names in this chapter have different equivalents in spoken regional Arabic. For example, the word for "pen" or "pencil" varies: قَلَم is used in MSA and broadly across the region, but spoken varieties may use different words in everyday conversation. As with all regional variation in this course, the MSA terms taught here are the productive standard you will use in formal and educational contexts; regional alternatives will be noted for recognition where they are widely encountered.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Forgetting the article on the adjective

Error: Writing *الكِتَابُ الجَدِيدِ كَبِيرٌ ("the new book is big") — the adjective inside the noun phrase gets the wrong case, or the second adjective (the predicate) gets the article by mistake.

Correction: In the attributive noun phrase الكِتَابُ الجَدِيدُ ("the new book"), both noun and adjective take الـ. In the predicative sentence الكِتَابُ الجَدِيدُ كَبِيرٌ ("The new book is big"), the predicate adjective كَبِيرٌ stays indefinite. Never put الـ on a predicate adjective that follows the sentence break.

Common Mistake — Using هَذَا with feminine nouns

Error: Saying *مَا هَذَا؟ — هَذَا سَبُّورَةٌ when pointing at the board, which is feminine.

Correction: Use هَذِهِ for feminine nouns: مَا هَذِهِ؟ — هَذِهِ سَبُّورَةٌ. The demonstrative must match the gender of the noun, not the gender of the speaker or the physical size of the object.

Common Mistake — Pronouncing the ل in sun-letter words

Error: Pronouncing the article as /al-/ before a sun-letter noun — saying "al-ṣaff" instead of "aṣ-ṣaff."

Correction: When the noun begins with a sun letter, the /l/ of the article assimilates to that first consonant. The spelling does not change, but the pronunciation does: الصَّفُّ is pronounced aṣ-ṣaffu. Watch for the shadda on the noun's first letter — it is the orthographic signal of assimilation.

Common Mistake — Omitting dots from جـ، حـ، خـ

Error: Writing the base shape of ج / ح / خ without the distinguishing dot, or placing the dot above instead of inside the letter for ج.

Correction: ج has one dot inside the curve (below the connecting line); ح has no dot; خ has one dot above. Without the dot, the reader cannot tell which letter is intended.

Common Mistake — Connecting ر or ز to the following letter

Error: Drawing a connecting stroke after ر or ز, as though they connect to the next letter.

Correction: Like ا, د, and ذ from Chapter 1, the letters ر and ز are nonjoining: they do not connect to the letter that follows them. A letter may connect to them from the right, but the word breaks visually after them. In the word دَرْسٌ (lesson), the ر does not connect to the س that follows it.

Common Mistake — Using the wrong gender adjective

Error: Writing *حَقِيبَةٌ جَدِيدٌ (bag + masculine adjective).

Correction: حَقِيبَة is feminine (note the tāʾ marbūṭa). Its adjective must also be feminine: حَقِيبَةٌ جَدِيدَةٌ. Check every noun's gender and then check every adjective's ending to make sure they match.

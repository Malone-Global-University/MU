الهُوِيَّة وَاللُّغَاتIdentity, Origin, and Languages

Core alphabet completion, nationality, origin, question words, and prepositions · Speak and Write Arabic: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Read and write the remaining Arabic letters, completing the core twenty-eight-letter alphabet.
  2. State your nationality using the correct masculine and feminine nisba adjective form.
  3. Say where you are from using the preposition مِنْ and name your country of origin.
  4. Say what languages you speak using the verb أَتَكَلَّم and related forms.
  5. Ask and answer questions using the core question words مَنْ, مَا / مَاذَا, أَيْنَ, مِنْ أَيْنَ, and هَلْ.
  6. Use the prepositions مِنْ (from), فِي (in), and إِلَى (to) in basic sentences about location and movement.
  7. Recycle greetings, classroom language, and definite article use from Chapters 1 and 2 in a new context.
  8. Produce a short spoken and written self-introduction covering name, nationality, origin, and languages.

Communication Scenario

The language institute in Amman has students from many countries. During a break after the second session, a group of learners — from the United States, Morocco, Japan, and Brazil — sit together and try to communicate in Arabic. Each person wants to know: Where are you from? What languages do you speak? Why are you learning Arabic?

  1. You can already greet people and name classroom objects. Now you will be able to talk about yourself at a deeper level — where you come from, what you call yourself as a nationality, and what languages fill your life.
  2. This is often the first real conversation between strangers: background, origins, and languages. With the tools in this chapter, you will have the core of that conversation available in Arabic.

Script Note — The emphatic consonant family

The letters ص, ض, ط, and ظ form a family of emphatic consonants. Each is the emphatic counterpart of a plain consonant: ص vs. س, ض vs. an ordinary /d/, ط vs. ت, and ظ vs. ذ. Emphatic consonants are produced with the back of the tongue raised toward the soft palate while the front articulation occurs — this produces a darker, heavier quality that affects surrounding vowels. In writing, the shapes of ص/ض and ط/ظ are paired: each pair shares a base shape distinguished by a dot.

Script Note — ع and غ: same base shape, different sounds

Like the ج/ح/خ family from Chapter 2, the letters ع and غ share one base shape and are distinguished only by a dot: ع has no dot and represents the voiced pharyngeal fricative; غ has one dot above and represents the voiced velar fricative (similar to a French uvular /r/). Confusing the dot means confusing the letter and often the word.

Script Note — و and ي as vowel letters

The letters و and ي serve double duty. As consonants they represent /w/ and /y/. As vowel letters, و represents the long vowel /ū/ and ي represents the long vowel /ī/. You have been seeing ي since Chapter 1 in words like كُرْسِيٌّ and the greeting يَا. Context and vowel marks will always tell you which role the letter is playing. Note that و does not connect to the letter that follows it.

Pronunciation

Emphatic consonants and vowel darkening

Arabic's four emphatic consonants — ص ض ط ظ — require a pronunciation adjustment that affects not just the consonant itself but the entire syllable around it. When an emphatic is present, surrounding vowels take on a darker, "backed" quality: the vowel /a/ shifts toward the sound in English "caught," and /i/ and /u/ shift toward slightly more central or lower positions. This is why صَفٌّ (class) sounds different from سَفَرٌ (travel): the ص colors the following vowel.

Practice the following contrasting pairs aloud:

  • سِين / صِينsīn / ṣīn — the letter sīn / ChinaPlain /s/ → dark emphatic /ṣ/; notice how the vowel changes quality
  • تَبَّ / طَابَtabba (fell) / ṭāba (was pleasant)Plain /t/ vs. emphatic /ṭ/
  • سَوْت / صَوْتsaut (voice, archaic form) / ṣawt (sound, voice)Contrast in everyday words

ع — The voiced pharyngeal fricative

The letter ع (ʿayn) is among the most distinctive Arabic sounds for English speakers and one of the most important to practice. It is the voiced counterpart of ح: both are pharyngeal, but ع has voice (the vocal cords are active). The closest English approximation is the tightening in the throat that sometimes accompanies a very strong whisper or a strained effort — but in Arabic, it is a fully controlled, regular consonant. It begins words like عَرَبِيَّة (Arabic) and أُسْتَاذ… no, that begins with hamza. The word عَائِلَة (family, from Chapter 4) also begins with ʿayn.

To practice: try producing the English /h/ very far back in the throat with the voice turned on. Listen to native speakers and compare the quality carefully — audio models from your instructor are essential here.

ق — The uvular stop

The letter ق (qāf) is a voiceless stop produced at the uvula — the very back of the roof of the mouth, further back than the English /k/. Think of /k/ and move the point of contact further back until you feel the back of the tongue touching the uvula. The sound is common in Arabic words you are already learning: قَلَمٌ (pen), قَدِيمٌ (old). In some regional spoken varieties, ق is pronounced differently; in MSA and formal contexts, the uvular pronunciation is the model.

Pronunciation Note — ق vs. ك: a contrast that matters

The letters ق and ك are both voiceless stops — ك at the velum (like English /k/) and ق further back at the uvula. Confusing them changes word meanings. Compare: كَلْب (dog) and قَلْب (heart); كَان (was) and قَان (a proper name). Productive control of this contrast takes time; consistent attention from the first chapter is the path to it.

Grammar Instruction

3.1 مِنْ أَيْنَ أَنْتَ؟ — Asking and answering about origin introduced

Purpose: ask where someone is from and give an accurate, complete answer using the preposition مِنْ and country names.

The question مِنْ أَيْنَ أَنْتَ؟ (Where are you from? — masculine) or مِنْ أَيْنَ أَنْتِ؟ (feminine) literally means "From where are you?" The answer uses أَنَا مِنْ + [country]:

  • مِنْ أَيْنَ أَنْتَ؟ — أَنَا مِنْ أَمْرِيكَا.Where are you from? — I am from America.مِنْ (from) + أَيْنَ (where) / أَنَا (I) + مِنْ + country name
  • مِنْ أَيْنَ أَنْتِ؟ — أَنَا مِنَ المَغْرِب.Where are you from? (f.) — I am from Morocco.مِنَ is the form of مِنْ used before the definite article الـ
  • هُوَ مِنَ الأُرْدُن.He is from Jordan.هُوَ (he) + مِنَ + country
  • هِيَ مِنَ اليَابَان.She is from Japan.هِيَ (she) + مِنَ + country
Grammar Note — مِنْ becomes مِنَ before the definite article

The preposition مِنْ ends with a sukūn (no vowel on the ن). When it is followed by a word beginning with الـ, the sukūn resolves by adding a short a vowel: مِنَ الأُرْدُن (from Jordan), مِنَ المَغْرِب (from Morocco), مِنَ الصِّين (from China). Before a country name without the article, مِنْ keeps its sukūn: مِنْ مِصْر, مِنْ لُبْنَان, مِنْ أَمْرِيكَا.

3.2 الجِنْسِيَّة — Stating nationality introduced

Purpose: describe a person's nationality using the nisba adjective in the correct gender form.

Nationality adjectives are used as predicates in nominal sentences. The adjective must match the gender of the subject — masculine for a man or boy, feminine for a woman or girl:

  • أَنَا أَمْرِيكِيٌّ.I am American. (male speaker)Nominal sentence: subject (أَنَا) + indefinite predicate adjective (masc.)
  • أَنَا أَمْرِيكِيَّةٌ.I am American. (female speaker)Same structure; adjective is feminine
  • هُوَ مَغْرِبِيٌّ.He is Moroccan.
  • هِيَ أُرْدُنِيَّةٌ.She is Jordanian.
  • أُسْتَاذِي لُبْنَانِيٌّ.My instructor is Lebanese. (male)أُسْتَاذِي = my instructor (masc.); predicate adjective is masc.
Grammar Note — The nisba suffix and the shadda

The nisba suffix ـِيّ carries a shadda on the ي, producing a doubled /y/ sound. In masculine form: أَمْرِيكِيٌّ (pronounced amrīkiyyun). In the feminine form, the ة of the nisba is tāʾ marbūṭa: أَمْرِيكِيَّةٌ. Note that the shadda appears before the tāʾ marbūṭa in the feminine form: ـِيَّةٌ. This is the standard feminine nisba ending throughout Arabic.

3.3 ماذا تتكلم؟ — Saying what languages you speak formulaic

Purpose: ask what language someone speaks and give a complete answer using the formulaic verb forms introduced in this chapter.

The verb forms أَتَكَلَّم (I speak) and تَتَكَلَّم (you speak, masculine) are used before the definite language name:

  • مَاذَا تَتَكَلَّم؟ — أَتَكَلَّم الإِنْجِلِيزِيَّة.What do you speak? — I speak English.
  • مَاذَا تَتَكَلَّمِين؟ — أَتَكَلَّم العَرَبِيَّة وَالفَرَنْسِيَّة.What do you speak? (f.) — I speak Arabic and French.
  • هَلْ تَتَكَلَّم العَرَبِيَّة؟ — نَعَم، أَتَكَلَّم العَرَبِيَّة قَلِيلًا.Do you speak Arabic? — Yes, I speak Arabic a little.قَلِيلًا (a little) is a useful adverb for beginners

The word وَ (and) connects items in a list. When followed by a word beginning with two consonants (or the article), it may take a short vowel: وَالعَرَبِيَّة (and Arabic). In pause speech, this is often transparent; you will hear it naturally in the model dialogue.

3.4 أَسْئِلَة الاسْتِفْهَام — Using question words introduced

Purpose: form correct questions about identity, origin, and language using the question words introduced in this chapter.

Question words stand at the beginning of the sentence in Arabic. The word order in the rest of the sentence does not change for a content question:

  • مَنْ هَذَا؟ — هَذَا أُسْتَاذِي خَالِد.Who is this? — This is my instructor Khālid.مَنْ (who) + rest of sentence
  • مَا اسْمُكَ؟ — اسْمِي سَارَة.What is your name? — My name is Sara.Recycled from Chapter 1
  • أَيْنَ الصَّفّ؟ — الصَّفُّ فِي الغُرْفَة خَمْسَة.Where is the class? — The class is in room five.أَيْنَ (where) + definite noun
  • مِنْ أَيْنَ أَنْتَ؟ — أَنَا مِنَ الصِّين.Where are you from? — I am from China.
  • هَلْ أَنْتَ أَمْرِيكِيٌّ؟ — نَعَم، أَنَا أَمْرِيكِيٌّ.Are you American? — Yes, I am American.هَلْ introduces a yes/no question

أَنَا + [name] + [nationality] + مِنْ + [country] + أَتَكَلَّم + [language(s)]

3.5 حُرُوف الجَرّ — Prepositions: مِنْ، فِي، إِلَى introduced

Purpose: use the three core prepositions to express origin, current location, and destination.

  • أَنَا مِنْ كَنَدَا، وَأَنَا فِي الأُرْدُن الآن.I am from Canada, and I am in Jordan now.مِنْ = origin / فِي = current location / الآن = now
  • أَنَا أَدْرُس العَرَبِيَّة فِي عَمَّان.I am studying Arabic in Amman.فِي + city name
  • أَسَافِر إِلَى القَاهِرَة.I travel to Cairo.إِلَى = toward/to + city name (formulaic recognition)
Language Focus

Questions come first in Arabic. In English, a question word can shift word order: "She is from Morocco" → "Where is she from?" Arabic question words simply attach to the front of a statement-like structure; the rest of the sentence keeps its normal order. This makes Arabic questions predictable: once you know the question word, everything after it follows familiar patterns. مِنْ أَيْنَ هِيَ؟ — literally, "From where she?" — is completely natural Arabic.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Arabic across borders

The Arabic-speaking world spans from the Atlantic coast of Morocco to the Arabian Gulf — a geographic and demographic range that includes North Africa, the Nile Valley, the Levant, Iraq, and the Arabian Peninsula, as well as large diaspora communities across Europe, the Americas, and beyond. This breadth means that people who share Arabic as a language may come from very different cultural, historical, climatic, economic, and religious contexts. Modern Standard Arabic exists as a shared formal register across this range: it appears in newspapers, official speeches, formal education, literature, and media in ways that speakers across the region can largely understand, even when their spoken vernaculars differ substantially. Learning MSA means gaining access to that shared formal space.

Culture in Context — Introducing yourself: what to include and what to hold back

In many Arabic-speaking social contexts, an introduction that includes nationality and origin is warmly received — geography, family connections, and regional roots are common opening topics. Asking مِنْ أَيْنَ أَنْتَ؟ is a natural early question, not an intrusion. At the same time, the specific cultural norms around personal questions — age, profession, marital status, religion — vary considerably by country, generation, setting, and the relationship between speakers. In a language classroom context, the questions taught in this chapter are standard and appropriate. Broader social topics require growing cultural judgment, which develops across the whole course.

Regional Variation — Nationality adjectives in spoken Arabic

The nisba nationality adjectives taught in this chapter — مَغْرِبِيٌّ, مِصْرِيٌّ, لُبْنَانِيٌّ — are standard in MSA and broadly understood across the region. In spoken regional varieties, the same adjectives are typically used, though pronunciation may vary. The suffix ـِيّ is consistent across varieties as a productive nationality and relationship marker, making it one of the most stable features of Arabic across the formal/informal divide. This means that learning the MSA nisba now is directly useful even in informal contexts.

Culture in Context — السَّلَامُ عَلَيْكُم as a greeting

The greeting السَّلَامُ عَلَيْكُم — Peace be upon you — appears in the model dialogue and will recur throughout the course. It is widely used across Arabic-speaking communities and is recognized by Arabic speakers everywhere, though its frequency and context vary by community, country, setting, and individual. Its response, وَعَلَيْكُمُ السَّلَام, is equally standard. These expressions carry social warmth and are appropriate in most formal and informal Arabic-speaking contexts. As with all formulas, they are taught here as whole communicative units; their internal grammatical structure will be analyzed at later stages.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using the wrong nisba gender

Error: A female student writes *أَنَا أَمْرِيكِيٌّ when describing herself.

Correction: أَنَا أَمْرِيكِيَّةٌ. The nisba adjective must match the grammatical gender of the subject. A female speaker uses the feminine form ending in ـِيَّةٌ. Nationality is one of the clearest and most constant gender-agreement contexts in Arabic — getting it right every time builds the habit needed for all other agreement.

Common Mistake — Forgetting مِنَ before the definite article

Error: Writing or saying *أَنَا مِنْ الأُرْدُن instead of أَنَا مِنَ الأُرْدُن.

Correction: When مِنْ is followed by a word starting with الـ, the sukūn on ن resolves and a short /a/ is inserted: مِنَ الأُرْدُن, مِنَ المَغْرِب, مِنَ اليَابَان. Before country names without the article, مِنْ stays unchanged: مِنْ مِصْر, مِنْ لُبْنَان.

Common Mistake — Omitting the definite article before language names

Error: Saying *أَتَكَلَّم عَرَبِيَّة without the article.

Correction: Language names in Arabic are always used with the definite article: أَتَكَلَّم العَرَبِيَّة, أَتَكَلَّم الإِنْجِلِيزِيَّة. Without the article, the phrase sounds incomplete. The article is a fixed part of the language name in standard usage.

Common Mistake — Confusing مَنْ and مَا

Error: Asking *مَا هَذَا الطَّالِب؟ when trying to ask "Who is this student?"

Correction: Use مَنْ for people: مَنْ هَذَا الطَّالِب؟ (Who is this student?). Use مَا for things and abstract information: مَا هَذَا؟ (What is this?), مَا اسْمُكَ؟ (What is your name?). The distinction between مَنْ (who) and مَا (what) is consistent and straightforward.

Common Mistake — Connecting و or ر or ز to the following letter

Error: Drawing a connecting stroke after و within a word, as if it joins to the next letter.

Correction: Like ا, د, ذ, ر, and ز, the letter و is a nonjoining letter. The word لُغَة contains a غ followed by a ة, which join; but the و in صَوْت (sound) does not connect to the ت that follows it. Check every و in your writing.

Common Mistake — Confusing ع and أ (hamza on alif)

Error: Writing *أَرَبِيَّة (with alif-hamza) instead of عَرَبِيَّة (with ʿayn).

Correction: The word عَرَبِيَّة begins with ع (ʿayn), not with أ (hamza on alif). The difference is phonological — they are completely different consonants — and orthographic. Alif carries the hamza (a glottal stop) while ع is the voiced pharyngeal. In writing, ع has the distinctive curved open shape; أ is a straight vertical stroke with a small mark above or below. Words beginning with ع include: عَرَبِيَّة، عَمَّان، أُسْتَاذ… no — أُسْتَاذ begins with hamza. A useful pair to drill: عَيْن (eye) vs. أَيْن (where?).

  1. Tom is from America (Boston). He is American (أَمْرِيكِيٌّ).
  2. Layla is from Morocco, specifically from Rabat (الرَّبَاط). She speaks Arabic and French.
  3. Kenji uses أَنْتَ (masculine) because Tom is male. أَنْتِ is used only for female addressees.
  4. Two examples of مِنَ: أَنَا مِنَ اليَابَان and أَنَا مِنَ المَغْرِب (and مِنَ الرَّبَاط). Each appears before a word beginning with الـ.
  5. Layla uses السَّلَامُ عَلَيْكُم. This is different from صَبَاحُ الخَيْر (Chapter 1): the latter is a time-specific morning greeting; السَّلَامُ عَلَيْكُم is a general peace-greeting used at any time of day.

Listening — Model answers

  1. Ranim · Egyptian (f.) · Egypt / Cairo · Arabic and English
  2. Carlos · Brazilian (m.) · Brazil · Portuguese, Spanish; learning Arabic
  3. (unnamed speaker, m.) · Jordanian (m.) · Jordan / Amman · Arabic
  4. Sophie · French (f.) · France / Paris · French, English, Arabic

Bonus: أَتَكَلَّم = I speak (first person — the speaker about themselves). تَتَكَلَّم = she/he speaks or you speak (third person feminine or second person, depending on context). In passage 4, the speaker is describing Sophie (a third person), so the verb changes to match that she (هِيَ) speaks the languages, not the speaker. Verb forms in Arabic change to reflect who is performing the action.

Reading — Comprehension

  1. Khālid is the instructor. He is from Jordan, specifically from Zarqa (الزَّرْقَاء).
  2. Isabella speaks three languages: Portuguese, Spanish, and English.
  3. Yuki uses فِي (in) before Amman: فِي عَمَّان.
  4. العَرَبِيَّة جَمِيلَةٌ! — Subject: العَرَبِيَّة (definite noun, feminine). Predicate: جَمِيلَةٌ (beautiful, feminine). The predicate adjective is indefinite (no article, with tanwīn) because it is the predicate of a nominal sentence. A predicate adjective always remains indefinite even when the subject is definite. (This rule was established in Chapter 2 and recurs here as cumulative practice.)

  1. All four letters listed join on both sides. None is a nonjoining letter.
  2. Emphatic family: ص (plain counterpart: س) · ض (plain: dental /d/) · ط (plain: ت) · ظ (plain: ذ).
  3. 1. مِنْ · 2. مِنَ · 3. مِنَ · 4. مِنْ · 5. مِنْ

    4. *أَنَا مِنْ المَغْرِب → أَنَا مِنَ المَغْرِب (مِنَ before الـ)
  5. Saudi: سُعُودِيٌّ / سُعُودِيَّةٌ · Chinese: صِينِيٌّ / صِينِيَّةٌ · British: بِرِيطَانِيٌّ / بِرِيطَانِيَّةٌ · Indian: هِنْدِيٌّ / هِنْدِيَّةٌ · Canadian: كَنَدِيٌّ / كَنَدِيَّةٌ

Cumulative Review — Model completed exchange

Exact wording will vary. One model (Speaker A = male, Speaker B = female):

أ: صَبَاحُ الخَيْر! أَنَا تُوم. مَا اسْمُكِ؟

ب: صَبَاحُ النُّور! أَنَا يُوكِي. تَشَرَّفْنَا!

أ: تَشَرَّفْنَا! مِنْ أَيْنَ أَنْتِ؟

ب: أَنَا مِنَ اليَابَان. أَنَا يَابَانِيَّةٌ. وَأَنْتَ؟

أ: أَنَا مِنْ أَمْرِيكَا. مَاذَا تَتَكَلَّمِين؟

ب: أَتَكَلَّم اليَابَانِيَّة وَالإِنْجِلِيزِيَّة. وَأَتَعَلَّم العَرَبِيَّة الآن!

أ: رَائِع! هَذَا كِتَابٌ جَدِيدٌ.

ب: نَعَم! الكِتَابُ جَمِيلٌ.

أ: مَعَ السَّلَامَة!

ب: مَعَ السَّلَامَة!

Check: Does each speaker use the correct pronoun address form (أَنْتَ vs. أَنْتِ)? Does the nationality adjective match the speaker's gender? Does مِنَ appear before country names with the article? Are language names preceded by the definite article? If so, the response is correct regardless of which country and language names were chosen.

Looking Ahead

مَا الَّذِي تَسْتَطِيعُ فِعْلَهُ الآن — What you can do now

You have completed the core Arabic alphabet. You can now decode and write any Arabic word you encounter in the rest of this volume. You can greet people, exchange names, identify classroom objects, ask what something is, describe things with adjectives, state where you are from, say what languages you speak, and form yes/no and content questions using the main question words. Crucially, you now have the grammatical habit of checking gender agreement on nisba adjectives — a habit that will serve you in every chapter ahead.

الفَصْل الرَّابِع: العَائِلَة وَالأَصْدِقَاءChapter 4: Family and Friends

Chapter 4 completes the Arabic orthographic system with hamza in all its positions, tāʾ marbūṭa behavior, and special letters. The communicative focus moves to family relationships and social connections. You will learn possessive constructions using the construct state (إِضَافَة), basic plural forms, and how to use pronoun suffixes to express possession — enabling you to talk about your family and friends by name and relationship. Chapter 4 also marks the Unit I cumulative checkpoint in Chapter 5.

Gesundheit, Termine und Dienstleistungen

Health, Appointments, and Services · Speak and Write German: Everyday Independence and Connected Communication

Chapter Objectives

Chapter 7 continues Unit II with a domain that comes up constantly in everyday independence: health, appointments, and services. Having activated the present perfect for work and study, adjective endings after definite articles, and developed weil clauses in Chapter 6, you are now ready to add dative pronouns and dative verbs in controlled contexts, modal-based advice with sollen and müssen, imperatives organized systematically by address level, and a review of dass clauses that lets you report what someone else says or wants.

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Use dative pronouns and common dative verbs to describe needs, symptoms, and reactions.
  2. Make and change an appointment using appropriate vocabulary and structures.
  3. Give and understand simple advice using sollen and müssen.
  4. Form imperatives correctly for du, ihr, and Sie in service contexts.
  5. Read basic service instructions and forms.
  6. Write a short, clear service request.
  7. Use dass clauses to report a need, a wish, or a piece of advice.

Opening Scenario

Anna has not felt well for a few days and needs to call a doctor's office to make an appointment. At the same time, her printer has stopped working, and she needs to contact a repair service before an important deadline. Mia, who has dealt with both situations before, gives Anna some advice about what to say and how formal to be. Meanwhile, Lukas needs to pick up a document from an administrative office in Leipzig and wants to know what he should bring.

  1. Read the appointment dialogue and the repair-service message below. Notice how dative pronouns appear with verbs like helfen, gefallen, and wehtun, and how imperatives shift depending on who is speaking to whom.
  2. As you move through the chapter, think about how you would describe a health concern, request a service, or explain what a form requires, using only the systems activated so far.

Core Vocabulary — Gesundheit, Termine, Dienstleistungen

This chapter's vocabulary covers appointments, offices, pharmacies, repair services, symptoms, and documents. All nouns are listed with article and plural; verbs that govern the dative are marked accordingly, since dative verbs become active in this chapter.

Termine (Appointments)

  • der Termin, die Termine — the appointment
  • die Praxis, die Praxen — the (medical) practice, office
  • die Sprechstunde, die Sprechstunden — the office hours, consultation hours
  • der Empfang, die Empfänge — the reception desk
  • verschieben — to reschedule, postpone

Gesundheit (Health and Symptoms)

  • der Schmerz, die Schmerzen — the pain
  • das Symptom, die Symptome — the symptom
  • die Apotheke, die Apotheken — the pharmacy
  • das Rezept, die Rezepte — the prescription
  • wehtun (+ Dativ) — to hurt (someone)

Dienstleistungen (Services and Repairs)

  • die Reparatur, die Reparaturen — the repair
  • der Kundendienst, die Kundendienste — the customer service department
  • die Werkstatt, die Werkstätten — the repair shop, workshop
  • helfen (+ Dativ) — to help (someone)
  • gehören (+ Dativ) — to belong to (someone)

Dokumente (Documents and Offices)

  • das Formular, die Formulare — the form
  • das Amt, die Ämter — the (government) office
  • die Unterlage, die Unterlagen — the document, record
  • der Ausweis, die Ausweise — the ID card
  • abholen — to pick up, collect

DACH label: office hours, appointment systems, and the offices responsible for administrative documents differ across Germany, Austria, and Switzerland; specific institutional names and procedures require verification.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

Language Note — Dative Pronouns and Dative Verbs

You have already used dative case in location phrases and a few set expressions. This chapter develops the dative pronoun system and introduces verbs that take a dative object directly, without a preposition. Common dative verbs in this domain include helfen (to help), gefallen (to please, be pleasing to), gehören (to belong to), and wehtun (to hurt).

NominativeDativeGloss
ichmirto/for me
dudirto/for you (informal)
er / esihmto/for him/it
sie (sg.)ihrto/for her
wirunsto/for us
ihreuchto/for you (pl. informal)
sie (pl.) / Sieihnen / Ihnento/for them / you (formal)
  • Der Kopf tut mir weh. — My head hurts. (literally: The head hurts to me.)
  • Können Sie mir helfen? — Can you help me?
  • Das Formular gehört ihm. — The form belongs to him.

Remember that dative verbs like helfen and gehören take a dative object even though the English equivalent looks like a direct object. Do not add zu or another preposition; the dative case alone carries the meaning.

Sentence Bracket — Modal with Dative Object
Der Termin passt mir leider nicht
"The appointment unfortunately doesn't work for me." — passen is a dative verb; mir appears immediately after the finite verb in this common word-order pattern.
Language Note — Advice with sollen and müssen

Sollen expresses recommendation or a suggestion that comes from someone else's advice or a general expectation; müssen expresses necessity or obligation. Both are modal verbs and form a verb bracket with the infinitive at the end of the clause.

  • Sie sollten zum Arzt gehen. — You should go to the doctor. (polite recommendation)
  • Du musst das Formular ausfüllen. — You have to fill out the form. (necessity)
  • Ich soll morgen zurückrufen. — I am supposed to call back tomorrow. (someone else's instruction)

Do not treat sollen and müssen as interchangeable. Müssen implies that there is no real alternative; sollen leaves more room for the listener's own judgment.

Language Note — Imperatives by Address Level

German imperatives change form depending on whether you are speaking to one person informally (du), several people informally (ihr), or anyone formally (Sie). Service contexts require careful attention to this choice.

AddressFormExample
dustem (+ e)Ruf(e) die Praxis an! — Call the office!
ihrpresent-tense ihr-formRuft die Praxis an! — Call the office! (to a group)
Sieinfinitive + SieRufen Sie die Praxis an! — Please call the office.

Notice that separable prefixes, such as an- in anrufen, move to the end of the imperative clause, just as they do in statements.

Language Note — Reviewing dass Clauses

You have used dass clauses before to report facts. This chapter reviews and expands the pattern so you can report needs, wishes, and advice — a common task in service and appointment contexts.

  • Die Ärztin sagt, dass ich mehr Wasser trinken soll. — The doctor says that I should drink more water.
  • Mia meint, dass Anna zuerst in der Apotheke fragen sollte. — Mia thinks that Anna should ask at the pharmacy first.

As with weil, the finite verb moves to the end of a dass clause. When the clause also contains a modal verb, the modal — not the infinitive — becomes the final, finite element.

Diagnostic Practice

  1. Complete with the correct dative pronoun: Der Rücken tut ___ (ich) weh.
  2. Complete with sollen or müssen: Sie ___ unbedingt einen Termin vereinbaren. (necessity)
  3. Form the Sie-imperative: (anrufen) die Praxis morgen früh.
  4. Complete with the correct dative verb form: Können Sie ___ (ich) bitte helfen?
  5. Combine with dass: Der Apotheker sagt etwas. Anna soll das Rezept mitbringen. →

Model Dialogue

Anna calls a doctor's office to make an appointment because she has not been feeling well.

Anna ruft in der Arztpraxis an.

Empfang: Praxis Dr. Neumann, guten Tag. Wie kann ich Ihnen helfen? Dr. Neumann's office, good day. How can I help you?
Anna: Guten Tag. Mir geht es seit ein paar Tagen nicht gut, und der Kopf tut mir oft weh. Ich hätte gern einen Termin. Good day. I haven't been feeling well for a few days, and my head often hurts. I'd like an appointment.
Empfang: Das tut mir leid. Kommen Sie doch morgen um neun Uhr vorbei. Bringen Sie bitte Ihre Versichertenkarte mit. I'm sorry to hear that. Come by tomorrow at nine o'clock. Please bring your insurance card.
Anna: Gut, das passt mir. Muss ich sonst noch etwas mitbringen? Good, that works for me. Do I need to bring anything else?
Empfang: Nein, das reicht. Wir sehen Sie dann morgen. Gute Besserung! No, that's enough. We'll see you tomorrow, then. Get well soon!

Comprehension — Model Dialogue

2. Wann ist der Termin?

3. Was soll Anna zum Termin mitbringen?

4. Find one dative pronoun and the verb it belongs to in the dialogue.

Schreiben

Writing Workshop — A Short Service Request

This workshop asks you to write a short message requesting a service (a medical appointment, a repair, or an administrative task), drawing on dative pronouns and verbs, sollen/müssen, imperatives, and a dass clause.

  1. Choose a situation. Use the appointment or repair-service scenario above, or invent a similarly simple service situation.
  2. State the problem or need. Write one or two sentences describing the issue, using a dative pronoun or dative verb where natural.
  3. Request the service. Ask for an appointment or a specific action.
  4. Report advice or a requirement. Write at least one dass clause reporting something you were told (for example, what you should or must do).
  5. Revise using the checklist below.

Checkliste — Editing Checklist:

  • Dative pronouns and dative verbs are used correctly.
  • sollen and müssen are used with the correct sense of advice versus necessity.
  • Any imperative matches the intended address level (du, ihr, or Sie).
  • At least one dass clause has correct verb-final order.
  • All nouns are capitalized; umlauts and ß are used correctly.
  • The register is formal, matching a written message to an office, practice, or service provider (formal Sie, appropriate greeting and closing).

Culture and Communication

Culture and Communication — Health and Service Systems Across DACH

Health-care systems, insurance procedures, and administrative offices differ across Germany, Austria, and Switzerland, and even within regions of the same country. This chapter's dialogues model general, practical communication patterns rather than describing any single country's specific procedures. Do not assume that appointment systems, required documents, or office hours are the same everywhere.

Regional Note — Formality in Service Encounters

Service encounters with unfamiliar medical practices, repair shops, and administrative offices typically call for formal Sie, regardless of region. Some smaller, informal businesses may use du with regular customers, but this should not be assumed as a default in an unfamiliar setting.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Wrong case with a dative verb

Error: *Können Sie mich helfen?

Correction: Können Sie mir helfen? Helfen takes a dative object, not an accusative object, even though English "help me" uses an object pronoun that looks like a direct object.

Confusing sollen and müssen

Error: *Sie müssen zum Arzt gehen, wenn Sie möchten. (used where only a suggestion is meant)

Correction: Sie sollten zum Arzt gehen, wenn Sie möchten. Müssen implies a firm obligation; use sollen when the choice remains with the listener.

Wrong imperative form for the address level

Error: *Ruf die Praxis an! (said to an unfamiliar office employee)

Correction: Rufen Sie die Praxis an! In an unfamiliar formal setting, the Sie-imperative is required; the du-imperative is only appropriate among people who already use du with each other.

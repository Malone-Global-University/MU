الدِّرَاسَة وَالعَمَلSchool and Work

Introducing the perfect tense, extending relative clauses, and building root-pattern awareness · Speak and Write Arabic: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Form and use the perfect tense for أَنَا, أَنْتَ, أَنْتِ, هُوَ, and هِيَ with regular Form I verbs.
  2. Talk about completed actions related to school and work, such as studying, finishing, and starting a job.
  3. Describe your school, class, or workplace using a relative clause built on a perfect-tense verb.
  4. Recognize the resumptive pronoun inside a relative clause describing a completed action.
  5. Identify the root shared by related school- and work-vocabulary words, and use root awareness to guess the meaning of a new but related word.
  6. Distinguish productive root-pattern recognition from inventing new words from a root.
  7. Read and interpret a short student transcript note and a short work announcement.
  8. Recycle need and advice language from Chapter 13 within a new communicative context.
  9. Produce a short spoken or written account of something you finished studying or a job you started.

Communication Scenario

Tom feels better after his visit to the clinic, and Layla asks him how his studies and his new part-time job are going. Tom explains what he has finished studying this term and describes the office where he now works, using a relative clause to say more about it.

  1. Before you begin, think about how English marks a completed action — for example, "I finished," "she started," "we studied." Arabic marks completed action with a different verb form from the one you have used so far.
  2. This chapter introduces that new verb form and shows how to use it inside a description, such as "the class that I finished."

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Note — Perfect-tense endings and connected speech

The perfect-tense suffixes ـتُ, ـتَ, ـتِ, and ـتْ are short and easy to under-articulate. Keep the final consonant of the root clear before adding the suffix, as in دَرَسْتُ, so the ending does not blur into the stem.

The hamzated verbs قَرَأَ and بَدَأَ keep a genuine glottal stop in the perfect stem; do not smooth the hamza into a long vowel.

Continue applying pharyngeal and emphatic contrasts from earlier chapters; several new words this chapter, such as عَمِلَ and اِمْتِحَان, contain sounds introduced in Chapter 3.

القَوَاعِد — Grammar Focus

The Perfect Tense — الفِعْل المَاضِي

To tell Layla what he has already studied and finished, Tom needs a verb form that marks a completed action — the perfect tense introduced in this chapter.

Building Block — Perfect-tense suffixes on a Form I verb
root + suffix: دَرَسْـ + تُ / تَ / تِ / ـَ / تْ
The perfect tense is built by attaching a subject suffix directly to the perfect stem of the verb. This chapter activates five subjects: أَنَا, أَنْتَ, أَنْتِ, هُوَ, and هِيَ. Plural and dual perfect forms remain receptive until Chapter 16.
دَرَسْتُ العَرَبِيَّة. / دَرَسَ أَحْمَد العَرَبِيَّة. / دَرَسَتْ لَيْلَى العَرَبِيَّة.
I studied Arabic. / Ahmad studied Arabic. / Layla studied Arabic.
SubjectFormMeaning
أَنَادَرَسْتُI studied
أَنْتَدَرَسْتَyou (masc.) studied
أَنْتِدَرَسْتِyou (fem.) studied
هُوَدَرَسَhe studied
هِيَدَرَسَتْshe studied
  • بَدَأْتُ العَمَل فِي المَكْتَب الأُسْبُوع المَاضِي.I started work at the office last week.1st person singular perfect
  • نَجَحَتْ لَيْلَى فِي الاِمْتِحَان.Layla passed the exam.3rd person fem. singular perfect
  • هَل كَتَبْتَ الوَاجِب؟Did you (masc.) write the homework?2nd person masc. singular perfect, question
Grammar Note — Perfect does not always mean English past simple

The Arabic perfect marks a completed action from the speaker's point of view; English sometimes translates it with the simple past ("I studied") and sometimes with the present perfect ("I have studied"). Choose the English translation that sounds most natural rather than translating the form mechanically.

  2. (هُوَ) ______ (عَمِلَ) فِي شَرِكَة كَبِيرَة. (he worked at a large company)
  3. (أَنْتِ) ______ (كَتَبَ) رِسَالَة إِلَى المُدِيرَة. (you (fem.) wrote a letter to the manager)

Describing with a Relative Clause — الجُمْلَة الوَصْلِيَّة

Tom wants to describe the office he now works in and the class he just finished. This chapter extends the relative clauses you began using in Chapter 4 to include a perfect-tense verb inside the clause.

Building Block — Relative clause with a resumptive pronoun
definite noun + الَّذِي / الَّتِي + perfect-tense clause (+ resumptive pronoun)
When the noun being described is definite, Arabic uses الَّذِي (masc.) or الَّتِي (fem.) to introduce the clause. If the described noun is the object of the verb inside the clause, a resumptive pronoun suffix refers back to it — the same suffix-pronoun system from Chapter 4 and Chapter 13, now attached inside a longer sentence.
هَذَا هُوَ المَكْتَب الَّذِي عَمِلْتُ فِيهِ.
This is the office that I worked in. (literally: ...in which I worked in it)
  • هَذِهِ هِيَ الجَامِعَة الَّتِي دَرَسْتُ فِيهَا.This is the university where I studied.الَّتِي + perfect + resumptive suffix فِيهَا
  • الصَّفّ الَّذِي بَدَأْتُهُ الأُسْبُوع المَاضِي مُمْتِع.The class that I started last week is enjoyable.الَّذِي + perfect + resumptive suffix ـهُ
  • الزَّمِيلَة الَّتِي نَجَحَتْ فِي الاِمْتِحَان تَعْمَل مَعِي.The colleague who passed the exam works with me.الَّتِي + perfect, no resumptive needed (subject relative)
Grammar Note — Extending a familiar system, not starting a new one

You have used الَّذِي and الَّتِي with present-tense description since Chapter 4. This chapter simply extends the same clause pattern to a perfect-tense verb. Notice that a resumptive pronoun is needed only when the noun being described is not the subject of the verb inside the clause; when it is the subject, as in the third example above, no extra pronoun is added.

Root-Pattern Awareness — الجِذْر وَالوَزْن

Many school- and work-related words share a small set of consonant roots. Noticing these roots helps you recognize new but related words without memorizing each one separately.

Building Block — One root, several related words
root د ر س → دَرَسَ (to study) → دَرْس (a lesson) → مَدْرَسَة (a school) → دِرَاسَة (study, studies)
The three consonants د ر س carry the core meaning "study" across the verb, the noun for a single lesson, the noun for the place of study, and the abstract noun for the activity of studying. Recognizing the shared root helps you guess meaning, but it does not tell you the exact vowel pattern of an unfamiliar word — that must still be learned or checked.
هَذَا دَرْسٌ مُهِمّ فِي المَدْرَسَة.
This is an important lesson at the school.
RootVerbRelated nounMeaning link
د ر سدَرَسَمَدْرَسَة / دِرَاسَةstudy → school / studies
ك ت بكَتَبَمَكْتَب / كِتَابwrite → office / book
ع م لعَمِلَعَمَلwork → work, job (activity)
Grammar Note — Recognition, not invention

Use root awareness to notice connections and support memory, not to invent new Arabic words. Not every word that looks related actually shares a productive root relationship, and not every root produces a predictable pattern. If you are unsure whether two words truly share a root, treat them as separate vocabulary items until you can confirm the connection.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Layla calls Tom later in the week to check on him and to ask about his studies and his new job.
السِّيَاق: تَتَّصِل لَيْلَى بِتُوم فِي وَقْتٍ لَاحِقٍ مِن الأُسْبُوع لِتَطْمَئِنَّ عَلَيْهِ وَتَسْأَلَهُ عَن دِرَاسَتِهِ وَوَظِيفَتِهِ الجَدِيدَة.

LAYLA مَرْحَبًا يَا تُوم! كَيْف صِحَّتُك الآن؟ Hello Tom! How is your health now?
TOM بِخَيْر الآن، شُكْرًا. اِسْتَرَحْتُ وَشَرِبْتُ مَاء كَثِيرًا كَمَا قَالَت الطَّبِيبَة. I'm well now, thank you. I rested and drank a lot of water as the doctor said.
LAYLA جَيِّد! وَكَيْف الدِّرَاسَة؟ هَل اِنْتَهَيْتَ مِن صَفّ اللُّغَة؟ Good! And how are your studies? Did you finish the language class?
TOM نَعَم، اِنْتَهَيْتُ الأُسْبُوع المَاضِي، وَنَجَحْتُ فِي الاِمْتِحَان. Yes, I finished last week, and I passed the exam.
LAYLA مَبْرُوك! وَالوَظِيفَة الجَدِيدَة؟ Congratulations! And the new job?
TOM بَدَأْتُ العَمَل يَوم الاِثْنَيْن. المَكْتَب الَّذِي أَعْمَل فِيهِ قَرِيب مِن الجَامِعَة. I started work on Monday. The office where I work is near the university.
LAYLA رَائِع! هَل الزُّمَلَاء وَالمُدِيرَة طَيِّبُون؟ Great! Are the colleagues and the manager nice?
TOM نَعَم، الزَّمِيلَة الَّتِي جَلَسَت بِجَانِبِي شَرَحَت لِي كُلّ شَيْء. Yes, the colleague who sat beside me explained everything to me.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — School and university terms

The academic calendar, the structure of exams, and the balance between school and work vary across Arabic-speaking countries and institutions. Terms such as اِمْتِحَان and صَفّ are widely understood in MSA, but the details of how school years, exam periods, and work schedules are organized differ by country and by institution, so it is useful to ask directly about local practice.

Regional Variation — Talking about work and study in everyday spoken Arabic

The MSA expressions in this chapter are the productive target. In everyday spoken Arabic, learners will often hear different everyday words for "job," "office," or "class." If your instructor introduces a labeled regional comparison, treat it as receptive and comparative information rather than a substitute for the MSA forms practiced here.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Confusing perfect and imperfect subject marking

Error: Saying *أَنَا يَدْرُس when describing a completed past action.

Correction: A completed action uses the perfect suffix on the perfect stem: أَنَا دَرَسْتُ, not the imperfect prefix.

Common Mistake — Dropping the resumptive pronoun in an object relative clause

Error: Saying *المَكْتَب الَّذِي عَمِلْتُ when the described noun is the object of a preposition inside the clause.

Correction: Keep the resumptive suffix that points back to the noun: المَكْتَب الَّذِي عَمِلْتُ فِيهِ.

Common Mistake — Inventing a word from a root without checking it

Error: Assuming that any noun pattern applied to a root will produce a real, attested Arabic word.

Correction: Use root awareness to recognize and remember words you have already met, not to manufacture new ones; check an unfamiliar word rather than guessing its form.

Looking Ahead

مَا الَّذِي تَسْتَطِيعُ فِعْلَهُ الآن — What you can do now

You can talk about completed actions in the singular perfect tense, describe a place or person using a relative clause built on the perfect, and use root awareness to recognize related school- and work-vocabulary words. You can combine this new system with the need and advice language from Chapter 13 in a single connected exchange.

Assessment Overview

How This Chapter Works

Chapter 5 is an assessment chapter. It introduces no new language systems. Every task draws exclusively on vocabulary, grammar, pronunciation, and script that were activated in Chapters 1–4. Your job is to bring those systems together and show what you can do with them in realistic communication.

The chapter is organized as four graded tasks (listening, reading, writing, speaking) followed by a portfolio task and a self-assessment checklist. Complete the warm-up review first to confirm which areas you feel confident in and which may need a final look before the main tasks.

Unit I at a Glance — Systems This Assessment Covers

ChapterCore systems assessed
Ch. 1 Greetings and leave-takings; name exchange; أَنَا, أَنْتَ, أَنْتِ; nominal sentences with هَذَا / هَذِهِ; basic questions مَا اسْمُكَ؟; first letter families (alif group); short vowels اَ، اِ، اُ.
Ch. 2 Classroom vocabulary; second letter group; definiteness with الـ; singular gender and adjective agreement; هَلْ questions; clarification phrases; numbers 0–10; shadda, sukūn, tanwīn.
Ch. 3 Final letter group (all 28 letters complete); nationality adjectives; origin questions مِنْ أَيْنَ؟; prepositions مِنْ and فِي; third-person pronouns هُوَ / هِيَ; language names; question words مَنْ، مَا، أَيْنَ، أَيّ، كَمْ; numbers 11–20.
Ch. 4 Hamza and special letter forms; family vocabulary; possession with ـِي، ـكَ، ـكِ and other suffixes; construct state (إِضَافَة); broken and sound plurals; عِنْدِي for possession; description of people and relationships.

What You Are Assessed On

Unit I Assessment Outcomes — You demonstrate that you can:

  1. Greet someone appropriately for time of day, exchange names, and take leave naturally.
  2. Identify, describe, and name classroom objects, using the definite article and adjective agreement correctly.
  3. Ask and answer questions about origin, nationality, current residence, and languages spoken.
  4. Introduce yourself and another person, using أَنَا, أَنْتَ / أَنْتِ, and هُوَ / هِيَ correctly.
  5. Describe family members and close relationships, using family vocabulary, construct phrases, and possessive suffixes.
  6. Write all 28 Arabic letters in correct joined form, with appropriate vocalization for given words.
  7. Read a short vocalized or partially vocalized passage and answer comprehension questions.
  8. Produce a short written self-introduction and a family description, showing accurate gender agreement, definite article use, and possessive suffixes.
  9. Participate in a short spoken exchange covering identity and personal information.

Assessment Scenario

The Arabic Language Institute has asked every student to contribute a short profile to the new intake booklet — a small printed guide that all students and instructors receive at the start of term. The booklet will include each student's name, origin, current residence, languages spoken, and a brief description of one family member. The institute's social coordinator has also arranged a final welcome conversation event at which students will introduce themselves to a small group, including an Arabic-speaking guest.

  1. The four assessment tasks mirror the skills this booklet and conversation event require: listening (understanding others' profiles), reading (reading fellow students' introductions), writing (producing your own profile entry), and speaking (introducing yourself and a family member).
  2. All tasks use only language from Chapters 1–4. You have everything you need.

Listening scripts — read aloud, do not show to learner during listening

Writing

Assessed Task كِتَابَة: مَلَفِّي الشَّخْصِيّ — Writing: My Personal Profile

Write your intake-booklet entry. Your profile must include all five sections below, in Arabic script, with correct vocalization where expected. Use only language from Chapters 1–4. Aim for six to eight connected sentences.

المحتوى المطلوب — Required content

Section 1 — الهُوِيَّة: Your full name, your nationality (correct gender form), and the country you are from.
Section 2 — السَّكَن: The city or country where you currently live.
Section 3 — اللُّغَات: The language(s) you speak and what you are studying.

مسودتك — Your draft

قَائِمَة المُرَاجَعَة — Editing checklist before you hand in:

  • My nationality adjective matches my gender.
  • I used مِنْ for origin and فِي for current residence.

المُهِمَّة الرَّابِعَة — Task 4

Speaking

Assessed Task التَّعْبِير الشَّفَهِيّ: حَفْل التَّرْحِيب — Speaking: The Welcome Event

This task has three parts. Parts A and B may be done with a partner; Part C has a solo alternative for independent learners.

Part A — Greeting and introduction (2–3 minutes)

Greet your partner appropriately for the time of day. Exchange full introductions: name, origin, current residence, and languages. React naturally when you hear your partner's information. End the exchange with an appropriate leave-taking.

Minimum language required: time-appropriate greeting, أَنَا for self-introduction, مِنْ أَيْنَ؟ or أَيْنَ تَسْكُن؟ as a question, أَتَكَلَّم for languages.

Part B — Introducing a family member (2 minutes)

Tell your partner about one family member: their relationship to you, their name if you wish, their nationality, and what they do. Use هُوَ or هِيَ correctly. Your partner will ask at least one follow-up question about this person.

Minimum language required: one family-relationship word, هُوَ / هِيَ, a nationality adjective in correct gender, and a description or profession.

Part C — Solo alternative

Record a 90-second audio or video introduction covering Parts A and B as a monologue. Then record a 30-second second segment in which you introduce one family member to an imagined listener, using هُوَ or هِيَ correctly throughout.

Warm-Up B — Letter identification

  1. ب (bāʾ, no dot below) and ت (tāʾ, two dots above) — same base shape, distinguished by dots.
  2. The ligature is لا.
  3. The mark is kasra (كَسْرَة); it represents the short vowel /i/.
  4. The mark is shadda (شَدَّة); it signals that the consonant is doubled.
  5. تَاء مَرْبُوطَة (tāʾ marbūṭa): it is a feminine marker that looks like ه with two dots; unlike plain ه, it consistently signals feminine grammatical gender.

Model writing entry (female learner from Canada, living in Amman)

اسْمِي آنَا تَايْلَر. أَنَا كَنَدِيَّة، مِنْ مَدِينَة تُورُنْتُو. أَسْكُن الآن فِي عَمَّان. أَتَكَلَّم الإِنْجِلِيزِيَّة، وَأَدْرُس العَرَبِيَّة فِي المَعْهَد. عِنْدِي أَخٌ كَبِير وَأُخْتٌ صَغِيرَة. اسْم أَخِي مَارْك، وَهُوَ مُهَنْدِس. أُمِّي مُدَرِّسَة. رَقْم هَاتِفِي: صِفْر سَبْعَة، ثَلَاثَة، سِتَّة، تِسْعَة.

By completing Unit I you have established the operating core for everything that follows in this volume. You can read, write, and type all 28 Arabic letters. You can greet, introduce yourself and others, describe your origin and residence, exchange contact details, name languages, and describe your family in connected, grammatically grounded sentences. You have a working knowledge of gender, definiteness, adjective agreement, pronouns, possessive suffixes, and the construct state — the foundations of the Arabic nominal system.

مَلَفّ الوَحْدَة الأُولَى — Unit I Portfolio

Your portfolio entry from the Portfolio Task is your primary record of Unit I achievement. File it now. You will be asked to build on it progressively across the volume. By Chapter 20, your complete portfolio will demonstrate your full A2 development — and your Unit I entry will be the foundation it stands on.

الوَحْدَة الثَّانِيَة: الحَيَاة اليَوْمِيَّةUnit II — Everyday Life

Unit II opens with Chapter 6 (فِي البَيْت وَالحَيّ — Home and Neighborhood), which introduces location language, demonstratives, fuller adjective agreement, and the first systematic look at nonhuman plural awareness. You will move from talking about who you are to talking about where you live, what surrounds you, and how your daily world is organized. All the systems you have built in Unit I — script, pronouns, gender agreement, construct state, possessive suffixes — will be in use every chapter from here forward.

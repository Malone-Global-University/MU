Wohnungssuche und Zuhause

Housing and Home Search · Speak and Write German: Everyday Independence and Connected Communication

Chapter Objectives

Mia mentioned in Chapter 1 that she had moved into a small apartment near the university. This chapter picks up that thread and gives you the tools to talk about housing in detail: reading a rental listing, describing what a home has and lacks, and asking practical questions before a viewing. It also introduces Volume II's first genuinely new grammar system — two-way prepositions, which describe location and, later, motion. For now, you will use them only for location, paired with the dative case.

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Describe housing needs, preferences, and the features of a home.
  2. Read a rental notice and identify the key practical details.
  3. Use two-way prepositions with the dative case to describe where things are located.
  4. Use es gibt and man kann to describe what a place offers.
  5. Use article and adjective endings correctly in a set of common housing phrases.
  6. Ask clear, polite questions about an apartment before or during a viewing.
  7. Write a short, well-organized housing inquiry.

Opening Scenario

Anna, who mentioned in Chapter 1 that she is planning to move to Berlin, has finally found a listing that looks promising. Before she calls the landlord, she wants to prepare her questions in German so that she sounds confident and organized. Meanwhile, Mia's new apartment near the university needs a description for a class assignment: "Describe your current home to a partner who has never seen it." Both tasks require the same core language — describing rooms, locations, and features of a home.

  1. Read Anna's listing and Mia's description below. Notice how location is expressed with prepositions and the dative case.
  2. As you move through the chapter, imagine your own current or ideal living situation. You will describe it in the Writing Workshop.

Core Vocabulary — Wohnen und Zuhause

This chapter's vocabulary supports describing homes, reading rental notices, and asking practical questions about an apartment. Every active noun appears with its gender and plural.

Housing Types

  • die Wohnung, die Wohnungen — the apartment
  • das Zimmer, die Zimmer — the room
  • die WG, die WGs — the shared flat
  • das Studentenwohnheim, die -heime — the dormitory
  • der Balkon, die Balkone — the balcony

Rooms and Features

  • die Küche, die Küchen — the kitchen
  • das Badezimmer, die -zimmer — the bathroom
  • das Wohnzimmer, die -zimmer — the living room
  • das Schlafzimmer, die -zimmer — the bedroom
  • der Aufzug, die Aufzüge — the elevator

Costs and Practicalities

  • die Miete, die Mieten — the rent
  • die Nebenkosten (pl.) — the utility costs
  • der Quadratmeter, die -meter — the square meter
  • möbliert — furnished
  • barrierefrei — accessible, step-free

Neighborhood and Location

  • die Nachbarschaft, die Nachbarschaften — the neighborhood
  • die Haltestelle, die Haltestellen — the (transit) stop
  • ruhig — quiet
  • zentral — central
  • in der Nähe von + dative — near, close to

All plural and article information above is active; use it exactly as shown in your own sentences.

Aussprache

Pronunciation Focus

Compound stress in housing vocabulary

Most words in this chapter's vocabulary are compounds, and German compound stress normally falls on the first element: BADzimmer, WOHNzimmer, SCHLAFzimmer, STUdentenwohnheim. Practice saying each compound with clear stress on the first part, then let the rest of the word stay relatively unstressed.

Also review final devoicing in Wohnung — the final -ung ends with a voiceless ng sound that stays crisp, not drawn out as it can be in English.

Self-check: reading a listing aloud

After you read the housing listing later in this chapter, read it aloud once and listen for compound stress on Nebenkosten, Quadratmeter, and Haltestelle.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns — Location and Description

Neu — Two-Way Prepositions (Location Only)

Introducing the two-way prepositions

German has a small set of prepositions that can take either the accusative or the dative case, depending on meaning. This chapter activates them for location only — answering the question wo? ("where?"). When these prepositions describe a fixed location rather than movement toward something, they take the dative. Motion uses with the accusative are not active until Chapter 4.

The nine two-way prepositions are: an, auf, hinter, in, neben, über, unter, vor, zwischen. This chapter focuses on the four most useful for describing a home: in, an, auf, and neben.

  • Die Küche ist in dem (im) Erdgeschoss. The kitchen is on the ground floor. in + dem → im, dative, location
  • Das Bild hängt an der Wand. The picture is hanging on the wall. an + der, dative, location
  • Die Wohnung ist neben dem Supermarkt. The apartment is next to the supermarket. neben + dem, dative, location
  • Der Tisch steht auf dem Balkon. The table is (standing) on the balcony. auf + dem, dative, location
    • Grammar Note — Contracted prepositions

    Two-way prepositions frequently contract with the definite article in everyday speech and writing. The most important contraction for this chapter is in + dem = im. You will meet am, ins, and zum in later chapters. Use im whenever a housing description places something inside a room or floor: im Wohnzimmer, im ersten Stock.

    Baustein — es gibt and man kann
    In der Wohnung gibt es einen Balkon und eine neue Küche.
    es gibt + accusative object — states what exists or is available
    Man kann leicht mit der Bahn in die Stadt fahren.
    You can easily get into the city by train. (man kann = "one can," a general possibility)
    Grammar Review — Adjective endings in set housing phrases

    Full adjective declension is not yet active in Volume II — that begins in Chapter 6. For now, use adjectives before housing nouns only in the fixed patterns below, and treat the ending as part of the phrase rather than a rule to apply freely.

    • eine helle Wohnung — a bright apartment
    • ein ruhiges Zimmer — a quiet room
    • eine neue Küche — a new kitchen
    • ein möbliertes Zimmer — a furnished room
    Review — Verb-Second Order with a Location Phrase
    Im Erdgeschoss gibt es eine kleine Küche
    "On the ground floor there is a small kitchen." — the finite verb stays in position two even when a location phrase opens the sentence.

    Diagnostic Practice

    1. Complete with the correct contraction: Die Küche ist ___ (in + dem) ersten Stock.
    2. Complete with the correct case: Die Wohnung ist neben ___ (der) Bäckerei.
    3. Rewrite using es gibt: Ein Balkon und eine Küche sind in der Wohnung.
    4. Combine into one sentence with man kann: man / leicht / zur Uni / laufen.
    5. Choose the correct adjective ending: ein ___ (ruhig-) Zimmer.

    Model Dialogue

    Anna calls about the apartment listing she found for her move to Berlin.

    Anna ruft wegen einer Wohnungsanzeige an.

    Vermieter: Guten Tag, hier ist Herr Brandt. Sie rufen wegen der Zwei-Zimmer-Wohnung an, richtig? Good day, this is Mr. Brandt. You're calling about the two-room apartment, correct?
    Anna: Ja, genau. Ich habe ein paar Fragen. Wie groß ist die Wohnung, und wo ist sie genau? Yes, exactly. I have a few questions. How big is the apartment, and where exactly is it?
    Vermieter: Sie hat 55 Quadratmeter und liegt im zweiten Stock. Die Wohnung ist neben einer Bäckerei, ganz in der Nähe von der U-Bahn-Haltestelle. It's 55 square meters and is on the third floor (lit. second floor above ground). The apartment is next to a bakery, very close to the subway stop.
    Anna: Klingt gut. Gibt es einen Balkon oder einen Aufzug? Sounds good. Is there a balcony or an elevator?
    Vermieter: Einen Balkon gibt es leider nicht, aber es gibt einen Aufzug im Haus. Die Küche ist neu, und das Zimmer ist ziemlich hell. Unfortunately there's no balcony, but there is an elevator in the building. The kitchen is new, and the room is quite bright.
    Anna: Und wie hoch sind die Miete und die Nebenkosten? And how much are the rent and the utility costs?
    Vermieter: Die Miete beträgt 780 Euro, und die Nebenkosten liegen bei ungefähr 150 Euro. Möchten Sie die Wohnung besichtigen? The rent comes to 780 euros, and the utility costs are around 150 euros. Would you like to view the apartment?
    Anna: Ja, sehr gerne. Wann würde das passen? Yes, very much. When would that work?

    Comprehension — Model Dialogue

    1. Wie groß ist die Wohnung, und wo liegt sie?

    2. Gibt es einen Balkon? Gibt es einen Aufzug?

    3. Wie hoch sind die Miete und die Nebenkosten?

    4. Find one example each of a two-way preposition with the dative and a sentence with es gibt.

    Listening Practice

    Ask a partner, instructor, or text-to-speech tool to read the following script at a natural pace. Listen once for the general picture, then again for the details.

    Mias neue Wohnung (Listening Script)

    Meine neue Wohnung ist klein, aber ich mag sie sehr. Sie liegt im dritten Stock, und es gibt leider keinen Aufzug im Haus. Das Wohnzimmer ist hell, und die Küche ist direkt neben dem Wohnzimmer.

    Einen Balkon gibt es nicht, aber es gibt einen kleinen Garten hinter dem Haus, den ich mit den Nachbarn teile. Die Wohnung ist in der Nähe von der Uni, und man kann in zehn Minuten zu Fuß dorthin gehen.

    Die Miete ist nicht billig, aber die Nachbarschaft ist ruhig, und ich fühle mich hier sehr wohl.

    Verständnisfragen

    1. In welchem Stock liegt Mias Wohnung?

    2. Was gibt es hinter dem Haus?

    3. Wie kommt Mia zur Uni?

    Writing Workshop — Housing Inquiry

    This workshop guides you toward the chapter's main writing task: a short housing inquiry in response to a listing.

    1. Choose a listing. Use the reading passage above or describe your own current or ideal apartment.
    2. Introduce yourself and your interest in one or two sentences.
    3. Ask three practical questions using at least one two-way preposition and at least one es gibt question.
    4. Close politely and ask about scheduling a viewing.
    5. Revise using the checklist below.

    Checkliste — Editing Checklist:

    • At least one two-way preposition with correct dative case appears.
    • At least one es gibt or man kann sentence appears.
    • Housing vocabulary includes correct gender and plural where relevant.
    • Verb-second order is consistent in every main clause.
    • All nouns are capitalized; umlauts and ß are used correctly.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture and Communication — Housing Markets Vary Widely

    Housing markets, rental customs, and typical apartment sizes differ significantly across Germany, Austria, Switzerland, and other German-speaking regions, and even from city to city within one country. Prices, deposit requirements, and the balance between renting and owning are not the same everywhere, so specific figures should always be checked against current, region-specific sources rather than treated as universal.

    Regional Note — Floor Numbering

    German floor numbering typically starts counting from the floor above street level: das Erdgeschoss is the ground floor, and der erste Stock is what English speakers might call the second floor. This convention is common across DACH countries but is worth confirming in unfamiliar buildings or listings.

    Common Mistakes to Avoid

    Two-way preposition case for location

    Error: *Die Küche ist in den ersten Stock.

    Correction: Die Küche ist im ersten Stock. A fixed location answers wo? and takes the dative, not the accusative.

    Word order with es gibt

    Error: *Im Wohnzimmer es gibt einen Balkon.

    Correction: Im Wohnzimmer gibt es einen Balkon. The finite verb must stay in position two, even after a fronted location phrase.

    Forgetting the contraction

    Error: *Die Wohnung ist in dem zweiten Stock. (not wrong, but unnatural)

    Correction: Die Wohnung ist im zweiten Stock. Standard usage strongly prefers the contracted form im in this context.

الصِّحَّة وَالعَافِيَةHealth and Well-Being

Expressing need and giving advice, body vocabulary, and an introduction to subjunctive frames · Speak and Write Arabic: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Name common body parts and use them to describe how you feel.
  2. Say what hurts using a simple possessive-suffix pattern with عِنْدِي أَلَم فِي... and يُؤْلِمُنِي....
  3. Express need using أَحْتَاج إِلَى and يَحْتَاج إِلَى.
  4. Give and receive simple advice using يَجِب أَنْ and مِن الأَفْضَل أَنْ.
  5. Recognize the subjunctive imperfect after أَنْ in need, advice, and ability expressions.
  6. Describe a simple symptom and ask for help at a clinic or pharmacy.
  7. Recycle future-tense, ability, and invitation language from Chapter 12 within a new communicative context.
  8. Read and interpret a short clinic sign and a written note about feeling unwell.
  9. Produce a short spoken or written exchange about a health concern and the advice given in response.

Communication Scenario

Tom wakes up on Sunday morning after the outing with Layla and does not feel well. He calls Layla to cancel a plan and explain why, and later stops by a neighborhood clinic, where the receptionist asks about his symptoms and a doctor gives him simple advice.

  1. Before you begin, think about how you say something hurts or that you need help in English. What words come before the description of the problem?
  2. This chapter introduces the vocabulary and structures needed to describe how you feel and to understand advice someone gives you in response.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Note — The pharyngeal ح and the ʿayn in body words

This chapter's vocabulary is rich in pharyngeal and emphatic sounds. Keep the pharyngeal ح in حَلْق and حُمَّى clearly distinct from the plain ه, and keep عَيْن and اِحْتَاجَ anchored on a genuine ع sound rather than a simple vowel.

Notice that أَحْتَاج إِلَى links smoothly in connected speech: the final vowel of أَحْتَاج glides into the hamzat al-waṣl of إِلَى without a full pause.

Continue applying the future-prefix pronunciation from Chapter 12; several examples in this chapter combine the future with these new verbs, such as سَتَحْتَاج.

القَوَاعِد — Grammar Focus

Expressing Need and Giving Advice — الحَاجَة وَالنَّصِيحَة

When Tom does not feel well, he needs a way to say what he needs and to understand the advice a doctor gives him — exactly what this chapter's need and advice expressions do.

Building Block — Saying what you need
أَحْتَاج إِلَى + noun — or — أَحْتَاج أَنْ + verb
اِحْتَاجَ إِلَى is followed by a noun when you need a thing, and by أَنْ plus a verb when you need to do something. The verb after أَنْ changes its ending slightly; this is the subjunctive pattern introduced later in this chapter.
أَحْتَاج إِلَى دَوَاء. / أَحْتَاج أَنْ أَسْتَرِيح.
I need medicine. / I need to rest.
  • أَحْتَاج إِلَى طَبِيب.I need a doctor.اِحْتَاجَ إِلَى + noun
  • تَحْتَاجِين إِلَى دَوَاء لِلْحُمَّى.You (fem.) need medicine for the fever.اِحْتَاجَ إِلَى + noun
  • يَجِب أَنْ تَشْرَب مَاء كَثِيرًا.You must drink a lot of water.يَجِب أَنْ + subjunctive
  • مِن الأَفْضَل أَنْ تَسْتَرِيح اليَوْم.It's better that you rest today.مِن الأَفْضَل أَنْ + subjunctive
Grammar Note — Two useful frames, not a full modal system

يَجِب أَنْ and مِن الأَفْضَل أَنْ are fixed, impersonal frames: they do not change for the person giving or receiving the advice. Only the verb after أَنْ changes to match the subject. Treat both expressions as ready-made patterns this chapter, and focus on choosing the correct verb form that follows them.

تَدْرِيب — Practice: Complete each sentence with أَحْتَاج إِلَى or يَجِب أَنْ.

  1. (أَنَا) ______ دَوَاء لِلرَّأْس. (I need medicine for my head)
  2. (أَنْتَ) ______ تَذْهَب إِلَى الطَّبِيب. (you must go to the doctor)
  3. (هِيَ) ______ تَسْتَرِيح فِي البَيْت. (she needs to rest at home)

Describing How You Feel — وَصْف الشُّعُور

Before Tom can get help, he needs to describe exactly what is wrong, using body vocabulary and a simple pain pattern.

  • عِنْدِي أَلَم فِي رَأْسِي.I have pain in my head.عِنْدِي أَلَم فِي + body part + possessive suffix
  • يُؤْلِمُنِي بَطْنِي.My stomach hurts (me).recognition pattern
  • أَشْعُر بِتَعَب شَدِيد.I feel very tired.شَعَرَ بِـ + noun
  • أَنَا مَرِيض وَعِنْدِي حُمَّى.I am sick and I have a fever.nominal sentence + عِنْدِي, recycled from Ch. 4
Grammar Note — Reusing the possessive suffix system

The pattern عِنْدِي أَلَم فِي رَأْسِي reuses the possessive-suffix system you learned for family vocabulary in Chapter 4: the suffix on رَأْس shows whose head hurts, exactly the way a suffix on أَخ once showed whose brother you meant. Only the vocabulary is new here, not the underlying grammar.

تَدْرِيب — Practice: Name the body part shown, then complete the pain expression.

  1. عِنْدِي أَلَم فِي ______. (throat)
  2. عِنْدِي أَلَم فِي ______. (back)

Introducing Subjunctive Frames — صِيغَة المَنْصُوب بَعْد أَنْ

Need and advice expressions in Arabic are almost always followed by a verb that has a slightly different ending after أَنْ. This chapter introduces that pattern for recognition and for a small set of controlled sentences.

Building Block — أَنْ + subjunctive imperfect
أَنْ + imperfect verb (drop the final ن of plural/feminine-plural forms; -u ending becomes -a on the singular verb in careful speech)
After أَنْ, the imperfect verb shifts into the subjunctive mood. In most singular forms, the visible spelling barely changes; the main visible change this chapter is that plural forms drop their final ن. This chapter introduces the pattern receptively and activates only a small, controlled set of subjects: أَنَا, أَنْتَ, and أَنْتِ.
تَذْهَب ← أَنْ تَذْهَب    يَذْهَبُونَ ← أَنْ يَذْهَبُوا
you go → that you go    they go → that they go
  • يَجِب أَنْ أَشْرَب مَاء كَثِيرًا.I must drink a lot of water.أَنْ + 1st person singular
  • هَلْ تَسْتَطِيع أَنْ تَذْهَب إِلَى الصَّيْدَلِيَّة؟Can you go to the pharmacy?recycled ability expression, Ch. 12
  • مِن الأَفْضَل أَنْ تَأْخُذِي الدَّوَاء بَعْد الأَكْل.It's better that you (fem.) take the medicine after eating.أَنْ + 2nd person feminine singular
Grammar Note — A recognition system with a controlled productive core

Full subjunctive mastery across every person and every weak or irregular verb belongs to a later volume. In Volume I, recognize أَنْ as a signal that a subjunctive verb follows, and produce the pattern only with the singular subjects and the small set of verbs taught in this chapter. You already used this exact structure with أَسْتَطِيع أَنْ and تُحِبّ أَنْ in Chapter 12 — this chapter simply names what was happening grammatically and extends it to need and advice expressions.

تَدْرِيب — Practice: Choose the correct subjunctive form to complete each sentence.

  1. يَجِب أَنْ (أَذْهَب / يَذْهَب) إِلَى الطَّبِيب. (I must go to the doctor)
  2. مِن الأَفْضَل أَنْ (تَسْتَرِيح / نَسْتَرِيح) اليَوْم. (it's better that you rest today)

Model Dialogue

Setting: Tom calls Layla on Sunday morning to explain that he feels unwell, then visits a clinic.
السِّيَاق: يَتَّصِل تُوم بِلَيْلَى صَبَاح الأَحَد لِيَشْرَح أَنَّهُ لَا يَشْعُر بِخَيْر، ثُمَّ يَذْهَب إِلَى عِيَادَة.

LAYLA صَبَاح الخَيْر يَا تُوم! هَلْ أَنْتَ جَاهِز لِلنُّزْهَة؟ Good morning, Tom! Are you ready for the outing?
TOM آسِف يَا لَيْلَى، لَا أَشْعُر بِخَيْر. عِنْدِي أَلَم فِي رَأْسِي وَحَلْقِي. Sorry Layla, I don't feel well. I have pain in my head and throat.
LAYLA هَل عِنْدَك حُمَّى؟ يَجِب أَنْ تَذْهَب إِلَى الطَّبِيب. Do you have a fever? You should go to the doctor.
TOM نَعَم، قَلِيلًا. أَحْتَاج إِلَى دَوَاء أَيْضًا. Yes, a little. I need medicine too.
LAYLA حَسَنًا، اِذْهَب إِلَى العِيَادَة القَرِيبَة مِن المَحَطَّة، ثُمَّ اِسْتَرِح. Okay, go to the clinic near the station, then rest.

(Tom arrives at the clinic and speaks with the receptionist.)

RECEPT. مَاذَا تَشْعُر؟ What do you feel?
TOM عِنْدِي أَلَم فِي رَأْسِي وَحَلْقِي، وَأَنَا تَعْبَان جِدًّا. I have pain in my head and throat, and I'm very tired.
RECEPT. يَجِب أَنْ تَنْتَظِر قَلِيلًا. الطَّبِيبَة سَتُسَاعِدُك بَعْد خَمْس دَقَائِق. You must wait a little. The doctor will help you in five minutes.

أَسْئِلَة الفَهْم — Comprehension questions:

  1. Identify every place where Tom uses the pattern عِنْدِي أَلَم فِي....
  2. Identify each advice expression Layla or the receptionist uses.
  3. مَاذَا يَحْتَاج تُوم؟ (What does Tom need?)

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Pharmacies and everyday health care

In many Arabic-speaking cities, a neighborhood pharmacy is often a first stop for minor symptoms, and pharmacists frequently offer advice for common complaints before a clinic visit becomes necessary. Practices around scheduling, walk-in care, and pharmacy consultation vary by country and by city, so it is useful to ask directly what is expected in a given place.

Regional Variation — Talking about feeling unwell in everyday spoken Arabic

The MSA expressions in this chapter are the productive target. In everyday spoken Arabic across different regions, learners will often hear different everyday phrases for describing how they feel. If your instructor introduces a labeled regional comparison, treat it as receptive and comparative information rather than a substitute for the MSA forms practiced here.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Confusing عِنْدِي with أَنَا

Error: Saying *أَنَا أَلَم فِي رَأْسِي instead of using the possessive pattern.

Correction: Pain and possession use عِنْدِي, not أَنَا: عِنْدِي أَلَم فِي رَأْسِي.

Common Mistake — Dropping أَنْ after advice expressions

Error: Saying *يَجِب تَسْتَرِيح without the connecting particle.

Correction: يَجِب and مِن الأَفْضَل require أَنْ before the verb: يَجِب أَنْ تَسْتَرِيح.

Common Mistake — Overextending the subjunctive to every person

Error: Attempting full subjunctive conjugation across every person, including plural and dual forms, before that system is taught.

Correction: This chapter activates أَنَا, أَنْتَ, and أَنْتِ forms after أَنْ for production; other persons remain receptive until a later volume.

  3. مِن الأَفْضَل أَنْ أَسْتَرِيح اليَوْم.
  4. هِيَ تَشْعُر بِـ تَعَب شَدِيد.

Dialogue — Comprehension

  1. عِنْدِي أَلَم فِي رَأْسِي وَحَلْقِي and عِنْدِي أَلَم فِي رَأْسِي وَحَلْقِي (repeated to the receptionist).
  2. يَجِب أَنْ تَذْهَب إِلَى الطَّبِيب; يَجِب أَنْ تَنْتَظِر قَلِيلًا.
  3. He needs a doctor and medicine.

Listening Practice

  • No — the patient does not have a fever.
  • Drink plenty of water, and rest today.

Reading Practice — Comprehension

  1. Wait in the room near the door.
  2. Pain in the head and throat, and a slight fever.
  3. Rest and drink plenty of water; take the medicine after eating.

Looking Ahead

مَا الَّذِي تَسْتَطِيعُ فِعْلَهُ الآن — What you can do now

You can name body parts, describe pain and symptoms, express need, and give or understand simple advice using subjunctive frames. You can combine this new system with future-tense, ability, and invitation language from Chapter 12 in a single connected exchange.

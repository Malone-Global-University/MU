Probleme, Regeln und Möglichkeiten

Problems, Rules, and Possibilities · Speak and Write German: Everyday Independence and Connected Communication

Chapter Overview and Objectives

Chapter 19 completes Unit IV. In Chapter 18 you described cultural events and compared regional practices across the DACH region. This chapter turns to a related but distinct skill: understanding and explaining rules, obligations, permissions, and possibilities. You will consolidate the modal verbs you already know — müssen, dürfen, sollen, and können — by contrasting their meanings directly, and you will meet the passive voice for the first time as a receptive tool for reading signs, notices, and official rules. You will also develop conditional wenn-clauses to talk about what is possible under certain conditions. Throughout, you will practice interpreting rules fairly, recognizing that rules vary by institution, region, and country, and negotiating constraints politely rather than simply reporting them.

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Contrast the meanings of müssen, dürfen, sollen, and können in rule-related contexts.
  2. Recognize passive-voice constructions with werden in signs, notices, and official rules.
  3. Use conditional wenn-clauses to describe what is possible or required under certain conditions.
  4. Read signs, rules, and institutional notices and identify what is required, permitted, or forbidden.
  5. Negotiate a constraint politely, including asking about exceptions.
  6. Write a short clarification request about a rule or restriction.
  7. Explain a rule or problem and propose a practical solution without offering legal advice.

Grammatik

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

Modal Verbs Consolidated: Obligation, Permission, and Ability

You have already met müssen, dürfen, sollen, and können individually. This chapter consolidates them by contrasting their meanings directly, especially in the context of rules.

ModalCore meaningExample
müssenobligation — it is necessaryMan muss die Ruhezeiten einhalten.
dürfenpermission — it is allowedHier darf man nicht rauchen.
sollenrecommendation or external expectationMan soll den Müll trennen.
könnenability or possibilityMan kann eine Ausnahme beantragen.

A common learner confusion: nicht müssen means "do not have to" (it is not necessary), while nicht dürfen means "must not" (it is forbidden). These are not interchangeable.

Building Block — Stating a Rule
Man [Modalverb] [Ergänzung] [Infinitiv].
impersonal subject "man" + modal in second position + verb bracket sends the infinitive to the clause end
Man darf nach zweiundzwanzig Uhr keine laute Musik hören.
You (one) may not listen to loud music after ten p.m.

A Receptive Preview of the Passive Voice

Signs, rules, and official notices often use the passive voice, which shifts attention away from who performs an action and onto the action itself. At this level, you are expected to recognize passive sentences in reading, not to produce them independently.

Grammar Focus — Recognizing werden-Passive

The passive voice is formed with a conjugated form of werden plus a past participle, which moves to the end of the clause, just like other elements of the verb bracket.

  • Der Müll wird jeden Montag abgeholt. (The trash is collected every Monday.)
  • Rauchen wird in diesem Gebäude nicht erlaubt. (Smoking is not permitted in this building.)
  • Ausnahmen werden nur auf Antrag gemacht. (Exceptions are only made upon request.)

Notice that the passive sentence does not need to say who collects the trash or who grants the exception — this is exactly why rules and notices often use it. Compare with the active equivalent, which you already know how to form: Die Firma holt den Müll jeden Montag ab. Full productive control of the passive, including tense variation, is developed in a later volume.

Conditional Wenn-Clauses

You have met wenn before as a general subordinating conjunction. This chapter develops it specifically for describing what is possible, required, or permitted under certain conditions.

  • Wenn du eine Ausnahme brauchst, musst du einen Antrag stellen. If you need an exception, you have to submit a request. wenn-clause (verb-final) + main clause (V2)
  • Wenn man die Hausordnung nicht kennt, kann man den Vermieter fragen. If you don't know the house rules, you can ask the landlord.
  • Man darf hier parken, wenn man eine Genehmigung hat. You may park here if you have a permit. main clause first, wenn-clause follows — both orders are natural

When the wenn-clause comes first, the main clause begins directly with its finite verb, since the whole wenn-clause occupies the first position of the sentence.

Modelldialog

Model Dialogue

Setting: Sophie asks her roommate Jonas about a new house notice from their landlord. They are roommates and use du.

Sophie
Hast du den neuen Hinweis vom Vermieter gesehen? Ich verstehe die Ruhezeiten nicht ganz.Did you see the new notice from the landlord? I don't quite understand the quiet hours.
Jonas
Also, man muss nach zweiundzwanzig Uhr leise sein. Laute Musik oder Partys sind dann nicht erlaubt.Well, you have to be quiet after ten p.m. Loud music or parties aren't allowed then.
Sophie
Und der Müll? Da steht, er wird nur montags und donnerstags abgeholt. Muss ich den Müll trennen?And the trash? It says it's only collected on Mondays and Thursdays. Do I have to sort the trash?
Jonas
Ja, man soll Papier, Glas und Restmüll trennen. Das ist keine Pflicht mit einer Strafe, aber es wird erwartet.Yes, you're supposed to separate paper, glass, and general waste. It's not a legally enforced duty, but it's expected.
Sophie
Verstanden. Und wenn ich mal eine kleine Feier machen möchte — gibt es dann eine Ausnahme?Understood. And if I want to have a small get-together sometime — is there an exception then?
Jonas
Wenn du die Nachbarn vorher informierst, ist das meistens kein Problem. Aber frag am besten den Vermieter, wenn du unsicher bist.If you inform the neighbors beforehand, that's usually not a problem. But it's best to ask the landlord if you're unsure.

Notice the modal contrast muss / soll / darf, the passive forms wird... abgeholt and wird erwartet, and the conditional clauses wenn ich mal eine kleine Feier machen möchte and wenn du die Nachbarn vorher informierst.

Guided Practice

Übung 1 — Modalverben im Kontrast (Modals in Contrast)

Choose the modal verb that best fits each sentence: muss, darf, soll, or kann.

  1. Nach zweiundzwanzig Uhr ___ man hier nicht laut Musik hören. (it is forbidden)
  2. Man ___ den Müll trennen; es ist kein Muss, aber es wird erwartet. (recommendation)
  3. Wenn man eine Ausnahme braucht, ___ man einen Antrag stellen. (it is necessary)
  4. Man ___ eine Ausnahme beantragen, wenn man einen guten Grund hat. (it is possible)

Übung 2 — Passiv erkennen (Recognizing the Passive)

Identify the subject of each passive sentence and rewrite it in your own words in English.

  1. Der Müll wird jeden Montag abgeholt.
  2. Rauchen wird in diesem Gebäude nicht erlaubt.
  3. Ausnahmen werden nur auf Antrag gemacht.

Übung 3 — Wenn-Sätze bilden (Forming Wenn-Clauses)

Combine each pair of sentences into one sentence using wenn.

  1. Du brauchst eine Ausnahme. + Du musst einen Antrag stellen.
  2. Man kennt die Hausordnung nicht. + Man kann den Vermieter fragen.
  3. Du informierst die Nachbarn. + Eine kleine Feier ist meistens kein Problem.

Listening Practice

Hausordnung — Ausschnitt (Listening Script)

Liebe Bewohnerinnen und Bewohner, bitte beachten Sie die folgenden Regeln. Zwischen zweiundzwanzig und sechs Uhr muss Ruhe eingehalten werden. Speaking Practice

Aktivität: Eine Regel verhandeln (Negotiate a Rule)

Work with a partner. One of you represents an institution (landlord, gym, school, or workplace) with a rule; the other has a practical reason to ask about an exception.

Partner A — Regel erklären (Explain the Rule)

State the rule clearly, using a modal verb and, if natural, a passive construction.

  • Use at least one contrast between müssen, dürfen, sollen, or können.

Partner B — Ausnahme erfragen (Ask About an Exception)

Politely ask whether an exception is possible, giving a practical reason.

  • Use at least one wenn-clause and one polite request formula.

Themenvorschläge (suggested topics): Ruhezeiten, Mülltrennung, Mitgliedschaft im Fitnessstudio, Haustiere in der Wohnung, Parkregeln.

Bewertungshinweise — Speaking Criteria

  • Clear statement of the rule, including what is required, permitted, or forbidden.
  • Accurate contrast among modal verbs where relevant.
  • At least one correctly formed conditional wenn-clause.
  • A polite, realistic negotiation of a possible exception.
  • No claim to give legal or binding institutional advice.

Solo alternative: Write both sides of the exchange, then record yourself reading Partner B's part aloud.

Reading Practice

Read the following short gym membership notice, then answer the questions that follow.

Mitgliedschaft im Fitnessstudio — Wichtiger Hinweis

Liebe Mitglieder, ab dem ersten des Monats gelten neue Regeln für Kündigungen. Eine Mitgliedschaft muss mindestens sechs Wochen vor dem gewünschten Enddatum schriftlich gekündigt werden. Wenn die Kündigung zu spät eingeht, wird die Mitgliedschaft automatisch um einen weiteren Monat verlängert. Gäste dürfen das Studio nur in Begleitung eines Mitglieds und nur zu den regulären Öffnungszeiten besuchen. Schreiben

Writing Workshop — A Clarification Request

This task asks you to write a short, polite message asking for clarification about a rule or restriction — the central writing skill of this chapter.

  1. Choose a situation. Use a scenario from this chapter (house rules, gym membership) or a similar situation you know.
  2. State what you understand. Briefly summarize the rule as you understand it, using an appropriate modal verb.
  3. Ask your question. Politely ask whether an exception is possible or request clarification, using at least one wenn-clause.
  4. Propose a practical next step. Suggest what you could do to resolve the situation.
  5. Revise using the checklist below.

Checkliste — Editing Checklist:

  • The rule or restriction is stated clearly and accurately.
  • At least one modal verb is used with the correct meaning (obligation, permission, recommendation, or possibility).
  • At least one wenn-clause appears with correct verb-final word order.
  • The tone is polite and appropriate for writing to an institution or landlord.
  • The message does not claim legal authority or give legal advice.
  • All nouns are capitalized; umlauts and ß are used correctly.

Culture and Communication

Culture and Communication — Rules Differ by Institution and Context

Rules and regulations in German-speaking countries vary considerably by institution, city, region, and country — a landlord's house rules, a gym's membership policy, and a municipal recycling ordinance are governed by entirely different authorities and may differ even between two buildings on the same street. Asking clarifying questions about a rule is a normal, expected part of communication, not a sign of misunderstanding basic social norms.

Regional Note — Recycling, Quiet Hours, and Local Ordinances

Practices such as waste separation categories, designated quiet hours, and notice periods for memberships or leases differ across Germany, Austria, and Switzerland, and often differ from one municipality or building to the next within the same country. Current requirements — recycling categories, legally permitted quiet hours, cancellation notice periods, and similar details — should always be verified with the relevant landlord, municipality, or institution rather than assumed from a general impression of "German rules."

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Confusing nicht müssen and nicht dürfen

Error: *Du musst hier nicht rauchen. (intending: smoking is forbidden here)

Correction: nicht müssen means "do not have to," not "must not." To express a prohibition, use nicht dürfen: Du darfst hier nicht rauchen.

Placing the wenn-clause verb in second position

Error: *Wenn du brauchst eine Ausnahme, du musst einen Antrag stellen.

Correction: Like other subordinate clauses, wenn-clauses send the finite verb to the end: Wenn du eine Ausnahme brauchst, musst du einen Antrag stellen. Note also that the main clause begins directly with its finite verb when the wenn-clause comes first.

Treating one institution's rule as general German law

Error: *In Deutschland darf man nach zweiundzwanzig Uhr nie laut Musik hören. (stated as universal law)

Correction: Attribute the rule to its actual source: Laut unserer Hausordnung darf man nach zweiundzwanzig Uhr keine laute Musik hören. Avoid presenting one building's or institution's rule as a nationwide legal requirement.

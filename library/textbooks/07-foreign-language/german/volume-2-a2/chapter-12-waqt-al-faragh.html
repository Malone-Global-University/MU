وَقْت الفَرَاغ وَالطَّقْس وَالخُطَطLeisure, Weather, and Plans

The future tense, ability, invitations, and an introduction to derived verb forms · Speak and Write Arabic: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Talk about future plans using the future markers سَـ and سَوْفَ with the imperfect verb.
  2. Ask and answer questions about the weather using common weather expressions.
  3. Name everyday leisure activities and say what you plan to do in your free time.
  4. Express ability using a formulaic expression built on أَسْتَطِيع.
  5. Extend, accept, and decline a simple invitation.
  6. Recognize that Arabic verbs belong to a base form and a family of related derived forms, and recognize three common derived forms in context.
  7. Recycle imperative and prepositional-suffix language from Chapter 11 within a new communicative context.
  8. Read and interpret a short weather forecast and a written invitation.
  9. Produce a short spoken or written exchange about weekend plans.

Communication Scenario

It is Thursday afternoon, and Tom runs into his classmate لَيْلَى near the station from Chapter 11. The weekend is coming, and Layla wants to make plans. They check the weather, talk about what they are able to do, and Layla invites Tom to join a group outing on Saturday.

  1. Before you begin, think about how you talk about future plans in English: what words signal that something has not happened yet?
  2. This chapter introduces the future tense for the first time. You will also learn to say what you can do, and to invite someone to join an activity.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Note — The future prefix and stress

The short future prefix سَـ attaches directly to the imperfect verb and is pronounced as a light, unstressed syllable, never as a separate word: سَأَذْهَب is one connected word, pronounced with the main stress falling on the verb itself rather than on the prefix.

The independent future word سَوْفَ is pronounced as its own word before the imperfect verb and carries slightly more emphasis than the attached prefix, which is useful when you want to make a future plan sound more deliberate.

Continue applying the emphatic and pharyngeal contrasts from earlier chapters; this chapter's ط in أَسْتَطِيع and ح in حَارّ should remain clearly distinct.

القَوَاعِد — Grammar Focus

The Future Tense — المُسْتَقْبَل

To talk about the weekend before it happens, Tom and Layla need a way to mark an action as not-yet-completed but planned — exactly what the future tense does.

Building Block — Forming the future
سَـ + imperfect verb — or — سَوْفَ + imperfect verb
Both forms use the same imperfect verb you already know from earlier chapters. سَـ attaches directly to the verb; سَوْفَ stands as a separate word before it. The two are close in meaning; سَوْفَ is slightly more formal or emphatic.
أَذْهَب ← سَأَذْهَب / سَوْفَ أَذْهَب
I go (imperfect) → I will go
  • سَأَذْهَب إِلَى الحَدِيقَة غَدًا.I will go to the park tomorrow.سَـ + imperfect
  • سَوْفَ نُشَاهِد فِيلْمًا يَوْم السَّبْت.We will watch a film on Saturday.سَوْفَ + imperfect
  • هَلْ سَتَذْهَبِينَ مَعَنَا؟Will you (fem.) go with us?question with future prefix
  • لَنْ أَذْهَب إِذَا كَانَ الجَوّ بَارِدًا.I will not go if the weather is cold.future negation, receptive this chapter
Grammar Note — Building on the imperfect you already know

The future tense does not introduce a new verb conjugation. It reuses the imperfect subject-marking system from earlier chapters and simply adds a marker in front of it. If you can conjugate a verb in the imperfect, you can already say it in the future — you only need to add سَـ or سَوْفَ. The negative future with لَنْ is included here for recognition only; it becomes a full productive target in a later chapter.

تَدْرِيب — Practice: Add the future prefix سَـ to each imperfect verb.

  1. (أَنَا) ______ أَلْعَب فِي الحَدِيقَة غَدًا. (I will play in the park tomorrow)
  2. (هِيَ) ______ تُشَاهِد فِيلْمًا بَعْد غَد. (she will watch a film the day after tomorrow)
  3. (نَحْنُ) ______ نَذْهَب إِلَى نُزْهَة يَوْم السَّبْت. (we will go on an outing on Saturday)

Ability and Invitations — القُدْرَة وَالدَّعْوَة

Making weekend plans also means saying what you are able to do, and inviting someone to join you.

  • أَسْتَطِيع أَنْ أَذْهَب مَعَك يَوْم السَّبْت.I can go with you on Saturday.أَسْتَطِيع أَنْ + verb
  • هَلْ تَسْتَطِيعِين أَنْ تَأْتِي؟Can you (fem.) come?تَسْتَطِيعِين أَنْ + verb
  • هَلْ تُحِبّ أَنْ نَذْهَب إِلَى الحَدِيقَة؟Would you like us to go to the park?formulaic invitation
  • بِكُلّ سُرُور!With pleasure! (accepting)formulaic acceptance
  • آسِف، لَا أَسْتَطِيع.Sorry, I can't.formulaic decline
Grammar Note — Two formulaic expressions, not two new verb paradigms

أَسْتَطِيع and تُحِبّ belong to irregular verb families that are not fully activated for production until later volumes. In Volume I, treat أَسْتَطِيع أَنْ... and هَلْ تُحِبّ أَنْ...؟ as fixed, memorized patterns: learn the whole phrase and change only the verb that follows أَنْ. Do not try to conjugate أَسْتَطِيع or تُحِبّ through every person yet — أَنَا, أَنْتَ, and أَنْتِ forms are enough for this chapter's tasks.

  2. هَلْ تَسْتَطِيع أَنْ تَذْهَب إِلَى النُّزْهَة؟ ______

Introducing Derived Forms — الأَوْزَان المُشْتَقَّة

You have been using Form I verbs since Chapter 7. This chapter introduces the idea that Arabic verbs also come in related derived forms built on the same root, each adding a shade of meaning.

Building Block — Same root, related meaning
root: ف ض ل → فَضَّلَ (Form II, "to prefer, make preferred")
This chapter introduces three derived forms receptively and in a small set of productive vocabulary items: Form II (often intensive or causative), Form III (often shared action, "to do something with someone"), and Form IV (often causative). You are not yet expected to build new derived forms yourself — only to recognize and use the specific verbs taught here.
سَاعَدَ (III) — فَضَّلَ (II) — أَرْسَلَ (IV)
to help — to prefer — to send
  • سَأُسَاعِدُك فِي البَيْت.I will help you at home.Form III, future + derived verb
  • أُفَضِّل النُّزْهَة عَلَى الفِيلْم.I prefer the outing to the film.Form II
  • سَتُرْسِل لَيْلَى رِسَالَة لِأَصْدِقَائِهَا.Layla will send a message to her friends.Form IV, future
Grammar Note — A preview, not a full system

Derived forms are one of the most powerful patterns in Arabic morphology, but full productive control of all ten forms belongs to later volumes of this series. For now, treat each derived-form verb in this chapter as a normal vocabulary item with its own perfect and imperfect, and simply notice that its meaning relates to a shared root. Do not attempt to invent new derived-form verbs from roots you already know; only attested, taught forms are correct.

  2. سَاعَدَ — a) to send   b) to prefer   c) to help
  3. أَرْسَلَ — a) to send   b) to prefer   c) to help

Model Dialogue

Setting: Tom runs into his classmate Layla near the station and they plan their weekend.
السِّيَاق: يُقَابِل تُوم زَمِيلَتَهُ لَيْلَى قُرْبَ المَحَطَّة، وَيَتَحَدَّثَانِ عَنْ خُطَط نِهَايَة الأُسْبُوع.

LAYLA مَرْحَبًا تُوم! مَاذَا سَتَفْعَل يَوْم السَّبْت؟ Hi Tom! What will you do on Saturday?
TOM لَا أَعْرِف بَعْد. هَلْ الجَوّ سَيَكُون جَمِيلًا؟ I don't know yet. Will the weather be nice?
LAYLA نَعَم، الجَوّ سَيَكُون مُشْمِسًا وَمُعْتَدِلًا. هَلْ تُحِبّ أَنْ تَذْهَب مَعَنَا فِي نُزْهَة إِلَى الحَدِيقَة؟ Yes, it will be sunny and mild. Would you like to go with us on an outing to the park?
TOM بِكُلّ سُرُور! أَسْتَطِيع أَنْ أَذْهَب بَعْد الظُّهْر. With pleasure! I can go in the afternoon.
LAYLA مُمْتَاز! سَوْفَ نُشَاهِد النَّاس يَلْعَبُونَ فِي الحَدِيقَة، ثُمَّ سَنُشَاهِد فِيلْمًا مَسَاءً. Great! We'll watch people playing in the park, then we'll watch a film in the evening.
TOM أُفَضِّل ذَلِك! سَأُرْسِل لَك رِسَالَة صَبَاح السَّبْت. I prefer that! I'll send you a message Saturday morning.
LAYLA حَسَنًا، إِلَى اللِّقَاء يَوْم السَّبْت! Okay, see you Saturday!

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — The weekend across the Arabic-speaking world

The days that make up the weekend vary across Arabic-speaking countries. In many countries, the weekend falls on Friday and Saturday, while in some others it falls on Saturday and Sunday, and practices continue to change over time in some places. When making plans in MSA, it is common and useful to name the specific day rather than assume a single fixed weekend pattern.

Regional Variation — Invitations in everyday spoken Arabic

The MSA invitation and acceptance phrases in this chapter are the productive target. In everyday spoken Arabic across different regions, learners will often hear different expressions for extending or accepting an invitation in casual speech. If your instructor introduces a labeled regional comparison, treat it as receptive and comparative information rather than a substitute for the MSA forms practiced here.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Writing سَـ as a separate word

Error: Writing the future prefix with a space, such as *سَ أَذْهَب.

Correction: سَـ attaches directly to the verb with no space: سَأَذْهَب.

Common Mistake — Conjugating أَسْتَطِيع beyond the taught forms

Error: Attempting to produce every person of اسْتَطَاعَ without having studied its full weak-verb pattern yet.

Correction: Use only the taught forms — أَسْتَطِيع, تَسْتَطِيع, and تَسْتَطِيعِين — as fixed formulaic expressions this chapter; the full paradigm is a later-volume target.

Common Mistake — Forgetting subject-verb gender agreement in the future

Error: Using a masculine imperfect verb with a feminine subject after adding the future marker, such as *لَيْلَى سَيَذْهَب.

Correction: The future marker does not change the agreement system you already know: لَيْلَى سَتَذْهَب.

Chapter Review — Section B, sample answer

الجَوّ حَارّ, الجَوّ بَارِد, الجَوّ مُعْتَدِل. فَضَّلَ (Form II), سَاعَدَ (Form III), أَرْسَلَ (Form IV).

Chapter Review — Section D, model answer

سَأَذْهَب إِلَى الحَدِيقَة يَوْم السَّبْت. الجَوّ سَيَكُون مُشْمِسًا. أَسْتَطِيع أَنْ أُشَاهِد فِيلْمًا مَسَاءً. سَأُرْسِل رِسَالَة لِأَصْدِقَائِي. Other well-formed, accurate answers are equally acceptable.

Kaufen, Tauschen, Reklamieren

Shopping, Returns, and Complaints · Speak and Write German: Everyday Independence and Connected Communication

Chapter Objectives

Chapter 8 continues Unit II with shopping, returns, and complaints. Having developed dative pronouns and dative verbs, modal advice with sollen and müssen, imperatives across all three address levels, and dass clauses in Chapter 7, you are now ready to add adjective endings after indefinite articles, prepositions with the accusative and dative in consumer contexts, zu + adjective, comparative and superlative forms, and polite request patterns for describing a problem and asking for a solution.

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Describe a problem with a product or service using correctly formed noun phrases.
  2. Use adjective endings after indefinite articles to describe items precisely.
  3. Use prepositions with the accusative and dative correctly in shopping and service contexts.
  4. Use zu + adjective and comparative/superlative forms to explain why something is unsatisfactory.
  5. Make a polite request for an exchange, repair, refund, or clarification.
  6. Read basic product information and return policies.
  7. Write a short, polite written complaint.

Opening Scenario

Anna ordered a pair of shoes online, but the pair that arrived is a different color than the one shown on the website, and one shoe has a small defect. She wants to write a polite email requesting an exchange. At the same time, Lukas bought a used bicycle from a small shop, and the brakes stopped working after only a few days; he decides to bring it back in person and speak with the shop owner. Mia, who works part-time at a customer-service desk, explains to both of them what information a complaint should include and how to keep the tone polite but clear.

  1. Read the customer-service dialogue and the complaint email below. Notice how indefinite-article adjective endings appear when items are first described, and how zu + adjective and comparative forms explain what is wrong.
  2. As you move through the chapter, think about how you would describe a problem with something you bought and request a specific, reasonable solution, using only the systems activated so far.

Core Vocabulary — Kaufen, Tauschen, Reklamieren

This chapter's vocabulary covers online shopping, returns, quality, warranties in simple language, and customer service. All nouns are listed with article and plural; adjectives commonly used in complaints are marked for later ending practice.

Einkaufen (Shopping)

  • die Bestellung, die Bestellungen — the order
  • die Lieferung, die Lieferungen — the delivery
  • der Artikel, die Artikel — the item, article
  • die Größe, die Größen — the size
  • bestellen — to order

Qualität (Quality and Problems)

  • der Fehler, die Fehler — the defect, mistake
  • der Schaden, die Schäden — the damage
  • die Qualität, die Qualitäten — the quality
  • kaputt — broken
  • beschädigt — damaged

Zurückgeben (Returns and Exchanges)

  • der Umtausch, die Umtausche — the exchange
  • die Rückgabe, die Rückgaben — the return
  • die Erstattung, die Erstattungen — the refund
  • die Garantie, die Garantien — the warranty, guarantee
  • umtauschen — to exchange

Kundendienst (Customer Service)

  • die Reklamation, die Reklamationen — the complaint
  • der Beleg, die Belege — the receipt, proof of purchase
  • die Beschwerde, die Beschwerden — the complaint
  • sich beschweren (über + Akk.) — to complain (about)
  • enttäuscht — disappointed

DACH label: return periods, warranty conditions, and consumer-service norms differ across Germany, Austria, and Switzerland, and by seller; specific policies and current legal details require verification.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

Language Note — Adjective Endings After Indefinite Articles

You have already used adjective endings after definite articles. This chapter activates adjective endings after indefinite articles (ein, eine) and the negative article kein, which follow a related but slightly different pattern because the indefinite article does not always show gender and case as clearly as the definite article does.

CaseMasculineFeminineNeuter
Nominativein neuer Artikeleine neue Jackeein neues Paar
Akkusativeinen neuen Artikeleine neue Jackeein neues Paar
  • Ich habe ein beschädigtes Paket bekommen. — I received a damaged package.
  • Das ist kein billiger Fehler. — That is not a cheap mistake. (i.e., not a minor one)
  • Sie haben mir einen falschen Artikel geschickt. — You sent me the wrong item.

Notice that the ending changes according to gender and case, but the article itself already carries some of that information. Do not treat indefinite-article adjective endings as identical to definite-article endings in every case; the masculine nominative and neuter nominative/accusative forms differ.

Language Note — Prepositions with Accusative and Dative in Consumer Contexts

Several prepositions common in shopping and complaint language govern either the accusative or the dative, depending on the preposition itself.

  • für + Akkusativ — Ich habe fünfzig Euro für die Schuhe bezahlt. (I paid fifty euros for the shoes.)
  • ohne + Akkusativ — Das Paket kam ohne Rechnung an. (The package arrived without an invoice.)
  • mit + Dativ — Das Paar kam mit einem Fehler an. (The pair arrived with a defect.)
  • nach + Dativ — Nach der Lieferung habe ich den Fehler bemerkt. (After the delivery, I noticed the defect.)
  • seit + Dativ — Seit der Reparatur funktioniert das Rad nicht besser. (Since the repair, the bike hasn't worked any better.)

Do not assume a preposition's case from its English translation. Für looks like "for," but its case must be learned and checked rather than guessed.

Sentence Bracket — Comparative with Prepositional Phrase
Das neue Paar ist leider schlechter als das alte Paar
"The new pair is unfortunately worse than the old pair." — the comparative schlechter is followed by als to introduce the point of comparison.
Language Note — zu + Adjective and Comparative/Superlative Forms

zu + adjective expresses that something exceeds an acceptable limit — "too" in English. Comparative and superlative forms let you compare the product you received with what you expected or ordered.

  • Die Jacke ist mir zu klein. — The jacket is too small for me.
  • Der Stoff fühlt sich billiger an als auf dem Foto. — The fabric feels cheaper than in the photo.
  • Das ist die schlechteste Lieferung, die ich bisher bekommen habe. — That is the worst delivery I have received so far. (formulaic superlative pattern)

At this stage, treat the superlative pattern der/die/das + -ste + Nomen as a controlled formulaic frame rather than a fully productive system; full superlative declension across all cases is not yet active.

Language Note — Polite Request Patterns

Complaints and service requests need a polite but clear tone. This chapter recycles modal verbs, imperatives, and dative pronouns from Chapter 7 and combines them with new patterns for making a specific request.

  • Ich hätte gern einen Umtausch. — I would like an exchange.
  • Könnten Sie mir bitte helfen? — Could you please help me?
  • Ich möchte Sie bitten, den Artikel zu ersetzen. — I would like to ask you to replace the item.
  • Wäre es möglich, das Geld zurückzubekommen? — Would it be possible to get the money back?

These polite request frames should be treated as controlled formulaic patterns at this stage rather than a fully productive Konjunktiv II paradigm.

Model Dialogue

Lukas brings the used bicycle back to the shop because the brakes stopped working.

Lukas spricht mit dem Ladenbesitzer.

Besitzer: Guten Tag. Womit kann ich Ihnen helfen? Good day. What can I help you with?
Lukas: Guten Tag. Ich habe letzte Woche ein gebrauchtes Fahrrad bei Ihnen gekauft, aber die Bremsen funktionieren seit ein paar Tagen nicht mehr richtig. Good day. I bought a used bicycle from you last week, but the brakes haven't been working properly for a few days.
Besitzer: Das tut mir leid. Haben Sie den Kaufbeleg mit? I'm sorry to hear that. Do you have the receipt with you?
Lukas: Ja, hier ist er. Ich möchte Sie bitten, die Bremsen kostenlos zu reparieren. Yes, here it is. I would like to ask you to repair the brakes free of charge.
Besitzer: Das ist kein Problem. Die Garantie gilt noch drei Wochen. Bringen Sie das Rad bitte morgen vorbei. That's no problem. The warranty is still valid for three more weeks. Please bring the bike by tomorrow.

Comprehension — Model Dialogue

1. Was hat Lukas letzte Woche gekauft?

2. Was funktioniert nicht mehr richtig?

3. Was soll Lukas morgen tun?

4. Find one preposition with a dative object in the dialogue.

Work with a partner. Partner A brings a product back to a shop or calls about a delivery problem; Partner B works at the store or customer-service desk. Then switch roles.

Partner A — Kundin/Kunde

Schreiben

Writing Workshop — A Short Written Complaint

This workshop asks you to write a short, polite complaint email requesting an exchange, repair, refund, or clarification, drawing on adjective endings after indefinite articles, prepositions with accusative and dative, zu + adjective or a comparative, and a polite request pattern.

  1. Choose a situation. Use the shoe-order or bicycle-repair scenario above, or invent a similarly simple product or service problem.
  2. Describe the problem. Write one or two sentences describing the item and what is wrong, using at least one indefinite-article adjective phrase.
  3. Explain why it is unsatisfactory. Use zu + adjective or a comparative form.
  4. Make a polite, specific request. Ask clearly for an exchange, repair, refund, or clarification.
  5. Revise using the checklist below.

Checkliste — Editing Checklist:

  • Indefinite-article adjective endings match gender and case.
  • Prepositions from this chapter are used with the correct case.
  • zu + adjective or a comparative form clearly explains the problem.
  • The request is specific (exchange, repair, refund, or clarification) and polite.
  • All nouns are capitalized; umlauts and ß are used correctly.
  • The register is formal, matching a written complaint to a business (formal Sie, appropriate greeting and closing).

Culture and Communication

Culture and Communication — Consumer Practices Across DACH

Return periods, warranty conditions, and complaint procedures differ across Germany, Austria, and Switzerland, and even from seller to seller within the same country. This chapter's dialogues and email model general, practical communication patterns rather than describing any single country's specific consumer-protection rules. Do not assume that return windows, refund rights, or required documentation are the same everywhere, and verify current policy details before relying on them.

Regional Note — Tone in Written Complaints

Written complaints to businesses, whether by email or letter, typically use formal Sie and a polite, factual tone, even when the customer is frustrated. A clear description of the problem and a specific, reasonable request are usually more effective than an emotional or accusatory tone, though individual style and relationship with a seller may vary.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Wrong ending after an indefinite article

Error: *Ich habe ein defekt Kabel bekommen.

Correction: Ich habe ein defektes Kabel bekommen. The adjective after an indefinite article in the neuter nominative/accusative still needs the -es ending; the article alone does not carry all the necessary information.

Wrong case after a preposition

Error: *Das Paket kam ohne einem Rechnung an.

Correction: Das Paket kam ohne eine Rechnung an. Ohne always takes the accusative, not the dative, regardless of what other prepositions in the sentence might suggest.

Overly blunt request tone

Error: *Ich will mein Geld sofort zurück! (in a first written complaint to an unfamiliar business)

Correction: Ich hätte gern eine Erstattung. or Wäre es möglich, das Geld zurückzubekommen? A polite request pattern is generally more appropriate than a blunt demand in a first written complaint to an unfamiliar business.

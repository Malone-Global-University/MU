Erlebnisse erzählen

Narrating Experiences · Speak and Write German: Everyday Independence and Connected Communication

Chapter Objectives

Chapter 11 opens Unit III by turning from solving everyday problems to telling stories about them. You will expand the present perfect with common irregular participles, learn the simple past of high-frequency verbs for written narrative, use temporal connectors to sequence events clearly, and consolidate the sentence bracket across longer sentences. By the end of the chapter, you will be able to narrate a longer personal or fictional experience and respond naturally to someone else's story.

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Use the present perfect with a wider range of common irregular participles to narrate past events.
  2. Recognize and use the simple past of high-frequency verbs, especially sein, haben, and common modals, in written narrative.
  3. Sequence a story using temporal connectors such as zuerst, dann, danach, plötzlich, and schließlich.
  4. Maintain correct sentence-bracket order across longer narrated sentences.
  5. Tell a connected past-tense story with a clear beginning, middle, and end.
  6. Ask follow-up questions and react appropriately to another person's narrated experience.

Opening Scenario

At a small gathering, Mia, Jonas, Anna, and Lukas are trading stories about their week. Mia describes a mix-up with her new course; Jonas tells a short, mildly funny story about missing a train; Anna talks about a small celebration for her new apartment; and Lukas describes helping his neighbor fix a minor problem. None of the stories are dramatic — they are the kind of everyday experiences that make up ordinary conversation, and telling them well means putting events in order and reacting naturally when someone else is speaking.

  1. Work through the vocabulary, grammar, and model dialogue below before attempting the practice tasks.
  2. Pay attention to how zuerst, dann, danach, plötzlich, and schließlich organize each story.
  3. Notice how listeners react with short follow-up questions rather than staying silent.

Core Vocabulary — Events, Emotions, and Reactions

This chapter's vocabulary supports storytelling: naming events, describing feelings and reactions, and referring to small accidents, problems, or celebrations. Gender and plural forms are included for every active noun.

Ereignisse (Events)

  • das Erlebnis, die Erlebnisse — the experience
  • die Feier, die Feiern — the celebration
  • die Überraschung, die Überraschungen — the surprise
  • der Unfall, die Unfälle — the accident
  • das Missverständnis, die Missverständnisse — the misunderstanding

Gefühle und Reaktionen (Feelings and Reactions)

  • überrascht sein — to be surprised
  • sich freuen über (+ Akk.) — to be happy about
  • sich ärgern über (+ Akk.) — to be annoyed about
  • erschrecken (ist erschrocken) — to be startled
  • lachen — to laugh

Kleine Probleme (Minor Problems)

  • verpassen — to miss (a train, an appointment)
  • vergessen (hat vergessen) — to forget
  • kaputtgehen (ist kaputtgegangen) — to break (become broken)
  • sich verlaufen (hat sich verlaufen) — to lose one's way

Zeitliche Verbindungen (Temporal Connectors)

  • zuerst — first
  • dann — then
  • danach — after that
  • plötzlich — suddenly
  • schließlich — finally

Register note: this vocabulary supports neutral, everyday storytelling appropriate among friends, classmates, and colleagues. It is not designed for reporting serious accidents, medical emergencies, or distressing events.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns — Narrating the Past

Chapter 11 develops two ways of talking about the past — the present perfect, which you already use for everyday reporting, and the simple past, which appears in written narrative, especially with sein, haben, and the modal verbs. Both systems rely on the sentence bracket you have used since Volume I.

    Temporal Connectors and the Sentence Bracket

    Zuerst, dann, danach, plötzlich, and schließlich organize a narrative in time. When one of these words opens a sentence, it counts as the first element, and the finite verb must still occupy second position — the sentence bracket then closes around the participle, infinitive, or separable prefix.

    Satzklammer — Sentence Bracket
    Zuerst haben wir Kaffee getrunken, dann sind wir spazieren gegangen.
    Position 1: zuerst / dann — Position 2: finite verb (haben, sind) — end: participle
    Plötzlich ist das Licht ausgegangen.
    Suddenly the light went out.
    1. Zuerst ___ (sein) ich müde, aber dann ___ (haben) ich Kaffee getrunken.
    2. Combine with plötzlich: Wir haben gesprochen. Das Telefon hat geklingelt. →
    3. Rewrite in the correct order: gegangen / wir / danach / sind / spazieren

    Model Dialogue

    Context: Jonas tells Mia about missing his train earlier in the week. Informal register, friend to friend.

    Mia:
    Wie war dein Tag am Montag? Du wolltest doch früh zur Arbeit fahren. How was your day on Monday? You wanted to go to work early, right?
    Jonas:
    Ach, das war ein chaotischer Morgen! Zuerst habe ich meinen Schlüssel nicht gefunden. Oh, that was a chaotic morning! First I couldn't find my key.
    Mia:
    Oh nein! Und dann? Oh no! And then?
    Jonas:
    Dann bin ich schnell zum Bahnhof gelaufen, aber der Zug war schon weg. Ich musste zwanzig Minuten warten. Then I ran quickly to the station, but the train was already gone. I had to wait twenty minutes.
    Mia:
    Wie ärgerlich! Bist du trotzdem pünktlich angekommen? How annoying! Did you still arrive on time?
    Jonas:
    Nein, aber schließlich hat mein Chef gelacht und gesagt, es ist kein Problem. No, but in the end my boss laughed and said it's not a problem.

    Guided Practice

    1. Complete each sentence with the correct present-perfect or simple-past form: Ich ___ (vergessen) meinen Schlüssel, und dann ___ (sein) ich zu spät.
    2. Reorder the sentence to place the finite verb correctly: gegangen / plötzlich / das Licht / ist / aus
    3. Write two sentences describing a small celebration using zuerst and danach.
    4. Rewrite this sentence using the simple past of haben: Ich habe keine Zeit gehabt, weil ich viel Arbeit hatte. (Focus on the auxiliary usage in the first clause.)
    5. Ask a follow-up question a listener might use after hearing: "Ich habe meinen Schlüssel verloren."

    Listening Practice

    Ask a partner, instructor, or text-to-speech tool to read the following script at a natural pace. Listen once for the general sequence of events, then again for detail.

    Annas kleine Feier (Listening Script)

    Anna erzählt: Letzte Woche hatte ich endlich Zeit für eine kleine Feier in meiner neuen Wohnung. Zuerst habe ich drei Freunde eingeladen. Dann habe ich den ganzen Nachmittag gekocht. Plötzlich ist der Ofen kaputtgegangen, und ich musste alles noch einmal planen! Zum Glück hat meine Nachbarin mir geholfen und ihren Ofen angeboten. Schließlich war das Essen fertig, und wir haben zusammen einen schönen Abend gehabt.

    Verständnisfragen

    1. Was hat Anna zuerst gemacht?

    2. Was ist plötzlich passiert?

    3. Wer hat Anna geholfen?

    4. Find one present-perfect sentence and one simple-past form of haben or sein in the script.

    Speaking Practice

    Aktivität: Erzähl mir davon! (Tell Me About It!)

    Work with a partner. Choose two of the four story cards below. Partner A tells a short story using at least three temporal connectors; Partner B listens and asks at least two follow-up questions. Then switch roles and choose two more cards.

    Ein chaotischer Morgen

    Tell a short story about a morning where something went wrong before you left the house.

    Eine kleine Feier

    Tell a short story about a small celebration you attended or organized.

    Ein verlorenes Ding

    Tell a short story about losing and then finding (or not finding) something important.

    Eine Überraschung

    Tell a short story about an unexpected event or surprise.

    Bewertungshinweise — Speaking Criteria

    • Clear sequence of events using at least three temporal connectors.
    • Correct use of present-perfect participles, including at least one irregular form.
    • Correct simple-past use of sein, haben, or a modal verb.
    • Correct sentence-bracket order after a fronted temporal connector.
    • At least two natural follow-up questions from the listener.

    Solo alternative: Record yourself narrating two of the four situations, then write down two follow-up questions a listener might reasonably ask.

    Lesen

    Reading Practice

    Read Lukas's short account of helping his neighbor, then answer the questions that follow.

    Lukas hilft seinem Nachbarn

    Am Samstag hat mein Nachbar an meine Tür geklopft. Er hatte ein Problem mit seinem Fahrrad, und er wusste nicht, was er tun sollte. Zuerst haben wir zusammen das Rad angeschaut. Dann habe ich gesehen, dass eine Schraube fehlte. Danach bin ich schnell in den Keller gegangen und habe eine passende Schraube gefunden. Plötzlich hat es angefangen zu regnen, also haben wir die Reparatur schnell fertig gemacht. Schließlich hat mein Nachbar sich sehr gefreut und mich zum Kaffee eingeladen.

    Verständnisfragen

    1. Warum hat der Nachbar an Lukas' Tür geklopft?

    2. Was hat Lukas im Keller gefunden?

    3. Was ist plötzlich passiert, während sie das Fahrrad repariert haben?

    4. Finde alle Zeitwörter (zuerst, dann, danach, plötzlich, schließlich) im Text und erkläre, wie sie die Geschichte ordnen.

    Schreiben

    Writing Workshop — A Connected Past Account

    This task asks you to write a short, connected narrative of a personal or fictional experience, drawing on this chapter's temporal connectors, expanded present-perfect participles, and simple past of sein, haben, and modal verbs.

    1. Choose an experience. It may be real or invented — a small event, minor problem, or celebration in the style of this chapter's vocabulary.
    2. Plan your sequence. List three to five events in the order they happened.
    3. Write your narrative. Use five to seven sentences, including at least three temporal connectors and at least one irregular present-perfect participle.
    4. Add reactions. Include at least one sentence describing how you or another person felt, using vocabulary from this chapter.
    5. Revise using the checklist below.

    Checkliste — Editing Checklist:

    • Temporal connectors are used to sequence events clearly.
    • Present-perfect participles are correctly formed, including irregular forms.
    • The simple past of sein, haben, or a modal verb is used correctly at least once.
    • The finite verb is in second position after a fronted temporal connector.
    • All nouns are capitalized; umlauts and ß are used correctly.
    • The story avoids sensitive or distressing content and stays at an everyday level.

    Culture and Communication

    Culture and Communication — Reacting to a Story

    Across German-speaking communities, listeners commonly show interest in a story through short verbal reactions and follow-up questions rather than staying silent until the speaker finishes. Expressions such as Wirklich?, Und dann?, and Wie ärgerlich! keep a conversation active. The frequency and style of these reactions vary by relationship, region, and personal habit, so this pattern should be treated as common rather than universal.

    Regional Note — Simple Past in Speech

    In much of northern and central Germany, the simple past of sein, haben, and the modal verbs is common even in spoken conversation, while the present perfect dominates for most other verbs. In southern Germany, Austria, and Switzerland, the present perfect is generally preferred even more strongly in everyday speech, though the simple past of sein and haben still appears. This chapter's productive pattern — present perfect for most verbs, simple past for sein/haben/modals — reflects a widely usable written and spoken default rather than one single regional norm.

    Common Mistakes to Avoid

    Wrong verb position after a fronted temporal connector

    Error: *Zuerst wir haben Kaffee getrunken.

    Correction: Zuerst haben wir Kaffee getrunken. When a temporal connector opens the sentence, the finite verb must remain in second position, so the subject moves after the verb.

    Using the present perfect of sein and haben instead of the simple past

    Error: *Ich habe gehabt keine Zeit.

    Correction: Ich hatte keine Zeit. In standard written and spoken narrative, sein and haben normally appear in the simple past rather than the present perfect.

    Incorrect irregular participle

    Error: *Ich habe meinen Schlüssel verliert.

    Correction: Ich habe meinen Schlüssel verloren. The participle of verlieren is the irregular form verloren, not a regular -t ending.

    Answer Key

    Grammar Practice

    1. Zuerst war ich müde, aber dann hatte ich Kaffee getrunken. (Accept: ..., aber dann habe ich Kaffee getrunken.)
    2. Wir haben gesprochen, plötzlich hat das Telefon geklingelt.
    3. Danach sind wir spazieren gegangen.

    Guided Practice

    1. Ich habe meinen Schlüssel vergessen, und dann war ich zu spät.
    2. Plötzlich ist das Licht ausgegangen.
    3. Sample: Zuerst haben wir Kuchen gegessen. Danach haben wir Musik gehört.
    4. Ich hatte keine Zeit, weil ich viel Arbeit hatte.
    5. Sample: Wie hast du ihn wiedergefunden? / Wo hast du ihn zuletzt gehabt?

    Listening — Comprehension Questions

    1. Anna hat zuerst drei Freunde eingeladen.
    2. Plötzlich ist der Ofen kaputtgegangen.
    3. Annas Nachbarin hat ihr geholfen.
    4. Sample: present perfect — "ich habe drei Freunde eingeladen"; simple past — "ich hatte endlich Zeit" / "der Ofen war kaputt" is acceptable as a related simple-past-style observation, though the script itself uses "ist kaputtgegangen" (present perfect).

    Reading — Comprehension Questions (Lukas hilft seinem Nachbarn)

    1. Der Nachbar hat an Lukas' Tür geklopft, weil er ein Problem mit seinem Fahrrad hatte.
    2. Lukas hat im Keller eine passende Schraube gefunden.
    3. Es hat plötzlich angefangen zu regnen.
    4. Sample: zuerst — sie schauen das Rad an; dann — Lukas sieht die fehlende Schraube; danach — er holt eine neue; plötzlich — es beginnt zu regnen; schließlich — der Nachbar freut sich und lädt Lukas ein.

الطَّعَام وَالشَّرَابFood and Drink

Ordering, requesting, and describing food and drink, direct objects, and quantities in Modern Standard Arabic · Speak and Write Arabic: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Name common foods, drinks, and meals in Arabic.
  2. Say what you want or would like using أُرِيد and أَوَدّ with a following direct object.
  3. Recognize and produce a simple direct object after a verb, without case-ending production.
  4. Express quantities using numbers 1-10, simple containers, and basic measure words.
  5. Make polite requests using مِنْ فَضْلِكَ and مِنْ فَضْلِكِ.
  6. Ask how much something costs and understand a simple price in Arabic.
  7. Order food and drink at a café or restaurant in a short exchange.
  8. Read a simple menu and a short shopping list.
  9. Write a short paragraph describing what you eat and drink in a typical day.

Communication Scenario

After class, Tom and his classmate Nūr stop at a small café near the institute in Amman. A waiter approaches their table with a short menu. Tom is hungry, Nūr wants only something to drink, and both of them need to ask the price before they pay.

  1. Think about how you order food in your own language. What do you say first — a greeting, a request, or straight to the item you want?
  2. You can already name objects, describe them, and talk about where you live and what you do each day. This chapter adds the vocabulary and structures you need to request, quantify, and pay for food and drink.

Pronunciation

Doubled consonants and shadda in food words

Several words in this chapter's vocabulary contain a doubled consonant marked with shadda, such as تُفَّاحَة, حُمُّص, and أَوَدّ. Remember from Chapter 2 that shadda lengthens the consonant sound itself; it is not simply a stress mark. Say each of these words slowly, holding the doubled consonant slightly longer than a single consonant would be held.

  • حُمُّص — the doubled م is heldḥummuṣ — hummus
  • أَوَدّ — the doubled د is heldawadd — I would like

ق in everyday vocabulary

This chapter gives you further practice with ق (qāf) from Chapter 3, now in high-frequency everyday words such as قَهْوَة, قَائِمَة, and مَقْهَى. Continue to produce this sound further back in the mouth than English "k."

Pronunciation Note — The word-final hamza in غَدَاء and عَشَاء

غَدَاء and عَشَاء both end in hamza sitting on the line after a long ا. Hamza remains a full, audible consonant here — do not drop it as if the word ended in a plain long vowel. Say each word with a clear, brief closure at the end: ghadāʾ, ʿashāʾ.

تَدْرِيب — Practice: Read the food and drink vocabulary aloud, paying attention to shadda, ق, and final hamza. Then practice the phrases مِنْ فَضْلِكَ and مِنْ فَضْلِكِ with natural sentence intonation, as if speaking to a waiter.

Grammar Instruction

8.1 المَفْعُول بِهِ — The direct object

Purpose: identify the thing a verb acts upon, so you can say what you want, order, or eat.

In a simple Arabic sentence, the direct object is the noun that receives the action of the verb. It normally follows the verb directly, with no preposition:

  • أُرِيد قَهْوَة.urīd qahwa. — I want (a) coffee.verb + direct object
  • هُوَ يَأْكُل سَمَك.huwa yaʾkul samak. — He is eating fish.
  • هِيَ تَشْرَب شَاي.hiya tashrab shāy. — She is drinking tea.

Full case marking of the direct object with the accusative ending is not yet productive at this stage of the course; you will build controlled awareness of case endings gradually beginning in Chapter 11. For now, focus on word order: verb first, then the direct object, with no preposition in between.

Grammar Note — Indefinite objects are common

Notice that قَهْوَة, سَمَك, and شَاي above appear without the definite article الـ. Just as in English "I want coffee" differs from "I want the coffee," Arabic uses the indefinite form when speaking about a type of food or drink in general rather than one specific, already-mentioned item.

8.2 أُرِيد وَأَوَدّ — Making requests

Purpose: politely state what you want using two related formulaic expressions.

  • أُرِيد كُوب مَاء، مِنْ فَضْلِكَ.urīd kūb māʾ, min faḍlika. — I want a glass of water, please.
  • أَوَدّ فِنْجَان قَهْوَة، مِنْ فَضْلِكِ.awadd finjān qahwa, min faḍliki. — I would like a cup of coffee, please.

أَوَدّ is generally more polite than أُرِيد and is well suited to ordering in a restaurant or café. Both are followed directly by a noun with no preposition, exactly like the direct-object pattern above.

8.3 الكَمِّيَّات — Expressing quantity

Purpose: combine a container or quantity word with a food or drink noun.

A quantity expression places the container or measure word first, followed directly by the noun it measures, in a pattern you will recognize from the construct-state introduction in Chapter 4:

  • كُوب عَصِيرkūb ʿaṣīr — a glass of juice
  • زُجَاجَة مَاءzujājat māʾ — a bottle of water
  • طَبَق سَلَطَةṭabaq salaṭa — a plate of salad
  • قَلِيل مِنَ الحَلِيبqalīl mina l-ḥalīb — a little (of the) milk

Notice زُجَاجَة becomes زُجَاجَت... in زُجَاجَة مَاء: a feminine noun ending in ة pronounces that ending as -at when it is the first term of a construct phrase, exactly as you practiced with family and possession terms in Chapter 4. قَلِيل مِنْ and كَثِير مِنْ use the preposition مِنْ instead, and therefore follow the مِنَ rule before الـ that you learned in Chapter 3.

8.4 الأَرْقَام فِي الطَّلَبَات — Numbers in requests

Purpose: recycle numbers 0-10 from Chapters 1-2 in the context of ordering and quantities.

  • أُرِيد كُوبَيْن مَاء.urīd kūbayn māʾ. — I want two glasses of water.
  • أَوَدّ ثَلَاثَة أَطْبَاق حُمُّص.awadd thalāthat aṭbāq ḥummuṣ. — I would like three plates of hummus.

كُوبَيْن above uses a dual-like ending for "two glasses"; treat it here as a formulaic, recognition-supported form. You will study the dual system and full numeral-noun agreement productively later in the volume. For controlled practice in this chapter, use the numbers 3-10 with a plural container noun, as in the ثَلَاثَة أَطْبَاق example.

8.5 بِكَمْ هَذَا؟ — Asking the price

Purpose: ask and state how much something costs.

  • بِكَمْ هَذَا؟ — هَذَا بِخَمْسَة دَنَانِير.bikam hādhā? — hādhā bi-khamsat danānīr. — How much is this? — This is five dinars.
  • بِكَمْ فِنْجَان القَهْوَة؟ — بِدِينَارَيْن.bikam finjān al-qahwa? — bi-dīnārayn. — How much is a cup of coffee? — Two dinars.

The demonstrative هَذَا here recycles the demonstrative system from Chapter 4. The preposition بِـ attaches directly to the price, similar to English "for five dinars."

Language Focus

Ordering food combines several small systems you already control. A single request such as أَوَدّ كُوب شَاي، مِنْ فَضْلِكَ draws on the formulaic verb system from Chapters 3 and 7, the construct-phrase pattern from Chapter 4, the demonstrative and definiteness systems from Chapters 3-4, and the numbers you first met in Chapters 1-2. This chapter's main task is not learning a large amount of new grammar; it is combining familiar pieces fluently under the pressure of a real transaction.

Model Dialogue

Setting: A small café near the institute in Amman. A waiter approaches Tom and Nūr's table.
السِّيَاق: مَقْهَى صَغِير قُرْب المَعْهَد فِي عَمَّان. يَقْتَرِب نَادِل مِنْ طَاوِلَة تُوم وَنُور.

NĀDIL مَسَاء الخَيْر! تَفَضَّلُوا، هَذِهِ قَائِمَة الطَّعَام. Good evening! Here you are, this is the menu.
TOM شُكْرًا. أَوَدّ طَبَق حُمُّص وَخُبْز، مِنْ فَضْلِكَ. Thank you. I would like a plate of hummus and bread, please.
NĀDIL تَمَام. وَمَاذَا تُرِيد لِلشُّرْب؟ Fine. And what would you like to drink?
TOM كُوب عَصِير بُرْتُقَال، مِنْ فَضْلِكَ. A glass of orange juice, please.
NĀDIL وَأَنْتِ؟ And you?
NŪR أُرِيد فِنْجَان شَاي فَقَط، مِنْ فَضْلِكَ. لَا أُرِيد طَعَامًا الآن. I want just a cup of tea, please. I don't want food right now.

TOM الحِسَاب، مِنْ فَضْلِكَ. بِكَمْ هَذَا كُلُّه؟ The check, please. How much is all this?
NĀDIL بِسَبْعَة دَنَانِير، مِنْ فَضْلِكَ. Seven dinars, please.
TOM تَفَضَّل. شُكْرًا جَزِيلًا! Here you are. Thank you very much!

Reading Note — Shared plates

In many households and restaurants across Arabic-speaking regions, dishes such as hummus, salad, and bread are often placed at the center of the table and shared among everyone present, rather than served as separate individual portions. Menu prices for such dishes commonly reflect a shared plate rather than a single serving.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Meal timing varies

The timing and size of the main daily meal vary across households and regions in the Arabic-speaking world, and change further during periods such as Ramadan, when eating and drinking patterns shift for many Muslims. This chapter's vocabulary for breakfast, lunch, and dinner is a practical starting point, not a claim about a single fixed mealtime schedule shared by all Arabic speakers.

Regional Variation — Currency and menu items

This chapter uses the Jordanian dinar as its model currency, consistent with the Amman setting of the course. Prices, common dishes, and even some food names vary considerably across Arabic-speaking countries and regions. The productive vocabulary and structures here remain useful everywhere in MSA; specific currency names and popular dishes will differ by location.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Inserting a preposition before the direct object

Error: *أُرِيد لِقَهْوَة.

Correction: The direct object follows the verb with no preposition: أُرِيد قَهْوَة.

Common Mistake — Using the definite article for a general request

Error: *أُرِيد القَهْوَة. when simply meaning "I want (some) coffee."

Correction: Use the indefinite form for a general request: أُرِيد قَهْوَة. Reserve القَهْوَة for a specific, already-mentioned cup of coffee.

Common Mistake — Forgetting the -at ending in a construct phrase

Error: *زُجَاجَة مَاء pronounced with a full pause after زُجَاجَة.

Correction: In a construct phrase, ة is pronounced -at, not -a: zujājat māʾ, as you practiced with family terms in Chapter 4.

Common Mistake — Wrong gender on مِنْ فَضْلِكَ

Error: Saying *مِنْ فَضْلِكَ to a female speaker, or *مِنْ فَضْلِكِ to a male speaker.

Correction: The attached pronoun agrees with the gender of the person being addressed: مِنْ فَضْلِكَ to a male, مِنْ فَضْلِكِ to a female, following the same أَنْتَ/أَنْتِ distinction from Chapter 1.

Chapter Performance Task

مُهِمَّة الأَدَاء

فِي المَقْهَى — At the café

You have been asked to plan and act out a complete café visit. Complete the following tasks:

  • Written: Write a short menu of five items in Arabic script, each with a price.
  • Written: Write a shopping list of five to seven items you would buy for a typical week.
  • Spoken: With a partner, act out a complete café order using your own menu: greeting, order, price question, and check.
  • Reflection: Write one sentence in English identifying which new structure — direct objects, construct-phrase quantities, or polite requests — you find most useful.

Assessment focus: correct verb-object word order, accurate indefinite/definite choice, correct construct-phrase pronunciation, appropriate gender agreement on مِنْ فَضْلِكَ/مِنْ فَضْلِكِ, and intelligible numbers 1-10.

Answer Key

Practice — أُرِيد or أَوَدّ + object

Answers will vary; any accurate, level-appropriate food or drink noun in the indefinite form is acceptable, e.g. 1. أُرِيد عَصِيرًا، مِنْ فَضْلِكَ. 2. أَوَدّ طَبَق حُمُّص، مِنْ فَضْلِكِ. 3. هِيَ تُرِيد كُوب شَاي.

Practice — بِكَمْ؟

Model: بِكَمْ فِنْجَان القَهْوَة؟ — بِدِينَار وَاحِد. Other accurate questions and reasonable prices are acceptable.

Dialogue — Comprehension

  1. Tom wants a plate of hummus and bread, and a glass of orange juice.
  2. Nūr wants only a cup of tea; she does not want food right now.
  3. The total bill comes to seven dinars.
  4. Tom says الحِسَاب، مِنْ فَضْلِكَ ("the check, please").

Listening

  • 1 — bread — coffee — no price given
  • 2 — chicken and rice — none given — no price given
  • 3 — (price question, no food) — water (bottle) — one dinar
  • 4 — salad — apple juice — no price given

Reading — Comprehension

  1. The salad plate costs two dinars and a half.
  2. Grilled fish with rice, at five dinars, is the most expensive item.
  3. Two bottles of water appear on the shopping list.
  4. Answers will vary; check that all items use accurate vocabulary and correct script.

Chapter Review — Section A

  1. خُبْز · أَرُزّ · دَجَاج · سَمَك
  2. فُطُور · غَدَاء · عَشَاء
  3. كُوب · زُجَاجَة · طَبَق
  4. مِنْ فَضْلِكَ (masc.) · مِنْ فَضْلِكِ (fem.)

Chapter Review — Section B

  1. أُرِيد زُجَاجَة مَاء.
  2. *أُرِيد لِسَلَطَة → أُرِيد سَلَطَة; no preposition precedes a direct object.
  3. The indefinite form is used because the speaker means coffee in general, not one specific, already-identified cup.
  4. ة is pronounced -at because زُجَاجَة is the first term of a construct phrase.

Chapter Review — Section C

  1. Grilled fish with rice, at five dinars, is the most expensive item.
  2. Six items: bread, cheese, eggs, apples, milk, and two bottles of water.

Chapter Review — Section D, model answer

فِي الفُطُور، آكُل خُبْزًا وَجُبْنًا وَأَشْرَب قَهْوَة. فِي الغَدَاء، آكُل دَجَاجًا مَعَ أَرُزّ. فِي العَشَاء، آكُل سَلَطَة. أُرِيد كُوب عَصِير، مِنْ فَضْلِكَ. Other well-formed, accurate answers reflecting the student's real habits are equally acceptable.

Looking Ahead

مَا الَّذِي تَسْتَطِيعُ فِعْلَهُ الآن — What you can do now

You can name common foods and drinks, make requests using أُرِيد and أَوَدّ with a direct object, express quantities with containers and numbers, ask and understand a simple price, order at a café or restaurant, and read a simple menu or shopping list.

19

سَرْد القِصَص وَالتَّوَاصُلConnected Stories

Expanding relative clauses, linking clauses with cause, result, and contrast, and building a fuller connected narrative · Speak and Write Arabic: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Use definite and indefinite relative clauses with الَّذِي / الَّتِي / الَّذِينَ to add descriptive detail to a story, recycling and expanding the relative clauses introduced in Chapter 4.
  2. Use a resumptive pronoun correctly inside a relative clause when the described noun is not the subject of the clause.
  3. Link clauses within a story using cause, result, and contrast connectors: لِأَنَّ, لِذَلِك, and لَكِنَّ.
  4. Recognize reported speech introduced by قَالَ إِنَّ in a story, without being required to produce it independently.
  5. Read a connected multi-sentence story that combines sequence, past background, and relative-clause description.
  6. Write a connected narrative of seven to nine sentences that combines the perfect tense, كَانَ for background, and at least one relative clause.
  7. Recycle sequence connectors, hollow-verb endings, لَمْ plus the jussive, and travel and past-background vocabulary from Chapters 16 through 18 inside a longer story.

Communication Scenario

Nadia is writing a short story for her Arabic class about a trip her family took last year. She wants to describe not just what happened, in order, but also give background details about the people and places involved, and explain why certain things happened. This chapter closes Unit IV, التَّجَارِب وَالقِصَص (Experiences and Stories), by combining the sequencing skills of Chapter 17 and the past-background description of Chapter 18 into a single connected story.

  1. You already know how to narrate a sequence of completed actions with أَوَّلًا, ثُمَّ, and بَعْدَ ذَلِك, and how to describe background states with كَانَ. This chapter adds description inside the story itself, using relative clauses to say more about a person, place, or thing without starting a new sentence.
  2. You already know simple coordination and contrast connectors from earlier chapters. This chapter expands them into full cause, result, and contrast links so that a story reads as one connected account rather than a list of separate sentences.

التَّرْكِيز النَّحْوِيّ — Grammar Focus

Expanding Relative Clauses

In Chapter 4 you met الَّذِي and الَّتِي in short phrases describing a definite noun. In this chapter, the relative clause becomes a full sentence that can carry an entire piece of story information, and you learn to use a resumptive pronoun when the described noun is not the subject of its own clause.

Building Block — Relative pronoun agreement (recycled from Ch. 4)
definite noun + الَّذِي / الَّتِي / الَّذِينَ + clause
Described nounRelative pronoun
masculine singularالَّذِي
feminine singularالَّتِي
human pluralالَّذِينَ

The relative pronoun agrees with the noun it describes, not with any noun inside the relative clause itself.

When the described noun is the subject of the relative clause, no extra pronoun is needed. When the described noun is instead an object, or the object of a preposition, inside the clause, a resumptive pronoun refers back to it.

  • قَابَلْنَا الرَّجُل الَّذِي سَافَرَ مَعَنَا إِلَى القَاهِرَة.We met the man who traveled with us to Cairo.described noun = subject of the clause; no resumptive pronoun
  • هَذِهِ هِيَ المَدِينَة الَّتِي زُرْنَاهَا العَام المَاضِي.This is the city that we visited last year.described noun = object; resumptive pronoun هَا attached to زُرْنَا
  • هَؤُلَاءِ هُم الأَصْدِقَاء الَّذِينَ تَحَدَّثْنَا عَنْهُم فِي القِصَّة.These are the friends we talked about in the story.described noun = object of a preposition; resumptive pronoun هُم on عَن
Grammar Note — When the resumptive pronoun is required

A resumptive pronoun is required whenever the described noun would otherwise leave a gap inside the relative clause, such as an object slot or a spot after a preposition. If you can point to exactly where the described noun "belongs" inside the clause and it is not the subject, that spot needs a pronoun. Compare الرَّجُل الَّذِي سَافَرَ (no gap; الرَّجُل is the subject of سَافَرَ) with المَدِينَة الَّتِي زُرْنَاهَا (gap after the verb; the pronoun هَا fills it).

تَدْرِيب — Practice: Combine each pair of sentences using a relative clause. Add a resumptive pronoun where needed.

  1. قَرَأْنَا القِصَّة. كَتَبَ الأُسْتَاذ القِصَّة. (We read the story. The teacher wrote the story.)
  2. هَذَا هُوَ البَيْت. سَكَنَّا فِي البَيْت قَدِيمًا. (This is the house. We used to live in the house long ago.)

Linking Clauses

A story is more than a list of separate sentences. لِأَنَّ, لِذَلِك, and لَكِنَّ connect events to their causes, results, and contrasts so that a story reads as one connected account.

Language Focus — لِأَنَّ, لِذَلِك, لَكِنَّ
ConnectorArabicFunction
becauseلِأَنَّintroduces the cause; followed by a noun or attached pronoun
thereforeلِذَلِكintroduces the result; opens a new clause
butلَكِنَّintroduces a contrast; followed by a noun or attached pronoun

لِأَنَّ and لَكِنَّ behave like إِنَّ-type particles: a following noun takes the accusative ending where marked, and a following pronoun attaches directly.

  • تَأَخَّرْنَا لِأَنَّ القِطَار كَانَ مُزْدَحِمًا.We were late because the train was crowded.لِأَنَّ + noun clause, gives cause
  • لَمْ نَجِد فُنْدُقًا، لِذَلِك بَقِينَا عِنْد صَدِيقِنَا.We did not find a hotel, so we stayed with our friend.لِذَلِك opens a new clause, gives result
  • أَرَدْنَا زِيَارَة المَتْحَف، لَكِنَّهُ كَانَ مُغْلَقًا.We wanted to visit the museum, but it was closed.لَكِنَّ + attached pronoun هُ, gives contrast
Grammar Note — Building a connected story

A connected story in this chapter typically moves through sequence connectors from Chapter 17 for the main events, adds كَانَ from Chapter 18 for background description, uses a relative clause to add detail about a person or place, and uses لِأَنَّ, لِذَلِك, or لَكِنَّ to connect an event to its cause, result, or contrast. Not every sentence needs a connector; use them where they genuinely clarify the relationship between events.

تَدْرِيب — Practice: Join each pair of sentences with the connector shown in parentheses.

  1. كَانَ الجَوّ حَارًّا. بَقِينَا فِي البَيْت. (لِذَلِك)
  2. أَرَدْنَا الذَّهَاب إِلَى الشَّاطِئ. أَمْطَرَت السَّمَاء. (لَكِنَّ)

Recognizing Reported Speech

Stories often report what someone said. This chapter introduces قَالَ إِنَّ for recognition only, so you can understand it in a story; producing your own reported-speech sentences begins in the next chapter.

  • قَالَ الجَدّ إِنَّ الحَيّ كَانَ هَادِئًا جِدًّا قَدِيمًا.The grandfather said that the neighborhood used to be very quiet long ago.قَالَ إِنَّ + كَانَ-sentence; recognition pattern

You are not expected to produce قَالَ إِنَّ sentences independently in this chapter's writing or speaking tasks; simply recognize the pattern when reading or listening.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Nadia tells her classmate Yusuf about the story she is writing for class.
السِّيَاق: تُخْبِر نَادِيَة زَمِيلَهَا يُوسُف عَن القِصَّة الَّتِي تَكْتُبُهَا لِلصَّفّ.

YUSUF عَن مَاذَا قِصَّتُك، يَا نَادِيَة؟ What is your story about, Nadia?
NADIA عَن رِحْلَة عَائِلَتِي إِلَى المَدِينَة القَدِيمَة الَّتِي زُرْنَاهَا العَام المَاضِي. About my family's trip to the old city that we visited last year.
YUSUF وَمَاذَا حَدَث فِي القِصَّة؟ And what happens in the story?
NADIA أَوَّلًا، سَافَرْنَا بِالقِطَار، لَكِنَّ القِطَار تَأَخَّر لِأَنَّ الطَّقْس كَانَ سَيِّئًا. لِذَلِك، وَصَلْنَا فِي المَسَاء بَدَل الصَّبَاح. First, we traveled by train, but the train was late because the weather was bad. So, we arrived in the evening instead of the morning.
YUSUF هَل قَابَلْتُم أَحَدًا هُنَاك؟ Did you meet anyone there?
NADIA نَعَم، قَابَلْنَا رَجُلًا عَجُوزًا يَعْرِف كُلّ شَخْصِيَّة فِي الحَيّ القَدِيم. قَالَ إِنَّ الحَيّ كَانَ سُوقًا مَشْهُورًا قَدِيمًا. فَجْأَة، دَعَانَا إِلَى بَيْتِهِ لِنَشْرَب الشَّاي. Yes, we met an old man who knows every character in the old neighborhood. He said that the neighborhood used to be a famous market long ago. Suddenly, he invited us to his house to drink tea.
YUSUF وَكَيْف انْتَهَت القِصَّة؟ And how did the story end?
NADIA فِي النِّهَايَة، عُدْنَا إِلَى البَيْت مُتْعَبِينَ لَكِن سُعَدَاء، لِأَنَّنَا تَعَلَّمْنَا الكَثِير عَن تَارِيخ الحَيّ الَّذِي كَانَ سُوقًا قَدِيمًا. In the end, we returned home tired but happy, because we learned a lot about the history of the neighborhood that used to be an old market.

أَسْئِلَة الفَهْم — Comprehension questions:

  1. Why did the train arrive in the evening instead of the morning? Which connector introduces the reason?
  2. Find the relative clause describing the old man. What does it add?
  3. Find the قَالَ إِنَّ sentence. What did the old man say?

Writing Workshop

In this chapter's writing task, you will write a connected story of seven to nine sentences that combines sequence, past background, and descriptive detail.

  1. Choose your story. Decide on a real or invented trip, event, or family memory.
  2. Set the background. Open with one or two sentences using كَانَ to describe the situation before the main events.
  3. Narrate the events. Write three or four sentences in the perfect tense, using sequence connectors from Chapter 17 where helpful.
  4. Add a relative clause. Describe a person, place, or thing in the story using الَّذِي, الَّتِي, or الَّذِينَ, with a resumptive pronoun if needed.
  5. Link one event to its cause or result. Use لِأَنَّ or لِذَلِك.
  6. Add a contrast. Use لَكِنَّ at least once.
  7. Close the story. Use فِي النِّهَايَة for your final sentence.
  8. Revise. Check your draft against the editing checklist below.

قَائِمَة المُرَاجَعَة — Editing checklist:

  • I opened with a كَانَ sentence that sets the background.
  • I used at least one relative clause, with a resumptive pronoun where needed.
  • I used لِأَنَّ or لِذَلِك to connect an event to its cause or result.
  • I used لَكِنَّ at least once.
  • I closed with فِي النِّهَايَة.
  • My story reads as one connected account, not a list of separate sentences.
  • I wrote all letters and endings with correct right-to-left joining.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Storytelling and shared memory

Sharing a connected story about family, travel, or a memorable event is a common way of building relationships across many Arabic-speaking communities, much as it is in many parts of the world. A well-told story often moves fluidly between background description, sequenced events, and small asides about the people involved, which is exactly the kind of connected structure this chapter practices.

Regional Variation — Everyday storytelling connectors

لِأَنَّ, لِذَلِك, and لَكِنَّ are standard MSA and are broadly understood in writing and formal speech. Everyday regional conversation often uses shorter, informal equivalents for these same relationships. Treat any such comparison your instructor introduces as receptive and comparative information at this stage, not a substitute for the MSA connectors practiced here.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Leaving out the resumptive pronoun

Error: Writing *هَذِهِ هِيَ المَدِينَة الَّتِي زُرْنَا العَام المَاضِي, leaving the described noun without a pronoun inside the clause.

Correction: When the described noun is the object of the verb inside the clause, attach a resumptive pronoun to that verb: المَدِينَة الَّتِي زُرْنَاهَا العَام المَاضِي.

Common Mistake — Using لِأَنَّ directly before a verb

Error: Writing *لِأَنَّ سَافَرَ, placing the verb immediately after لِأَنَّ.

Correction: لِأَنَّ takes a following noun or attached pronoun, not a bare verb: لِأَنَّ الطَّقْس كَانَ سَيِّئًا or لِأَنَّنَا تَأَخَّرْنَا.

Common Mistake — Treating قَالَ إِنَّ as a productive target

Error: Assuming this chapter requires you to build your own قَالَ إِنَّ sentences in the writing task.

Correction: قَالَ إِنَّ is recognition only in this chapter. Recognize it while reading and listening, but build your own story around كَانَ, relative clauses, and the connectors practiced above.

Assessment focus: accurate relative-clause agreement, correct use of resumptive pronouns, accurate لِأَنَّ/لِذَلِك/لَكِنَّ connectors, and a clearly connected story rather than a list of unrelated sentences.

Answer Key

Expanding Relative Clauses, Practice

  1. قَرَأْنَا القِصَّة الَّتِي كَتَبَهَا الأُسْتَاذ.
  2. هَذَا هُوَ البَيْت الَّذِي سَكَنَّا فِيهِ قَدِيمًا.

Linking Clauses, Practice

  1. كَانَ الجَوّ حَارًّا، لِذَلِك بَقِينَا فِي البَيْت.
  2. أَرَدْنَا الذَّهَاب إِلَى الشَّاطِئ، لَكِنَّ السَّمَاء أَمْطَرَت.

Model Dialogue — Comprehension

  1. The train was late because the weather was bad, introduced by لِأَنَّ; the result is introduced by لِذَلِك.
  2. رَجُلًا عَجُوزًا يَعْرِف كُلّ شَخْصِيَّة فِي الحَيّ القَدِيم: it adds that the old man knows every character in the old neighborhood.
  3. He said that the neighborhood used to be a famous market long ago.

Listening Practice

  • Main events, in order: the family traveled by bus, then walked because the road was narrow; they met a woman who knew the grandfather; she told stories about the old days; the family had to return before evening.
  • They walked because the road was too narrow for the bus.
  • قَرْيَة صَغِيرَة كَانَ جَدُّهُم يَسْكُن فِيهَا قَدِيمًا: it adds that the grandfather used to live in the village long ago.
  • She said that the grandfather was known throughout the whole village.

Reading Practice — Comprehension

  1. Layla found an old letter her grandfather wrote to her grandmother; she read it quickly because she was very curious, introduced by لِأَنَّ.
  2. البَيْت القَدِيم الَّذِي عَاشَت فِيهِ العَائِلَة لِسَنَوَات طَوِيلَة: it adds that the family lived in the house for many years.
  3. The letter said that the grandfather traveled far for work but wrote to the grandmother every week.
  4. القِصَّة الَّتِي لَمْ تَسْمَعْهَا لَيْلَى مِن قَبْل uses the resumptive pronoun هَا, referring to the story.

Looking Ahead

مَا الَّذِي تَسْتَطِيعُ فِعْلَهُ الآن — What you can do now

You can add descriptive detail to a story with relative clauses, use a resumptive pronoun when it is needed, and connect events to their causes, results, and contrasts. You have moved from narrating a simple sequence of events to telling a fuller, more connected story.

Mobilität und Reiseplanung

Mobility and Travel Planning · Speak and Write German: Everyday Independence and Connected Communication

Chapter Objectives

Anna is settling into her neighborhood and Mia has organized a get-together with friends — now both of them need to get around the city and beyond it. This chapter turns to mobility and travel planning: comparing transport options, understanding delays and changes, and asking for help when a journey doesn't go as expected. You will expand the two-way prepositions you used for location in Chapter 2 into motion contexts, meet a set of separable-prefix verbs built for travel and transport, use comparatives to weigh practical choices, and begin recognizing subordinate wenn clauses receptively.

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Talk about routes, tickets, luggage, and travel arrangements.
  2. Distinguish two-way prepositions used for motion (accusative) from the same prepositions used for location (dative).
  3. Use separable-prefix travel verbs in statements, questions, and simple past-tense frames.
  4. Compare transport options using comparative forms.
  5. Recognize subordinate wenn clauses describing conditions or delays.
  6. Understand route results, announcements, and simple disruption messages.
  7. Plan a journey in writing and handle a spoken conversation about a travel disruption.

Opening Scenario

Anna is planning a weekend trip to visit a friend in Leipzig, and she needs to compare a few travel options before she books anything. At the same time, Mia is heading to the university on a morning when the tram schedule seems to be disrupted, and she has to ask for help figuring out an alternative route. Both situations require the same core language: describing movement into, onto, and through places, comparing options, and understanding what to do when a plan changes.

  1. Read Anna's route comparison and Mia's conversation at the tram stop below. Notice how the two-way prepositions in, an, and auf shift from dative (location) to accusative (motion).
  2. As you move through the chapter, think about a real or imagined journey you might take. You will plan and adjust that journey in the Writing Workshop and the Speaking Practice.

Core Vocabulary — Mobilität und Reiseplanung

This chapter's vocabulary supports talking about routes, tickets, delays, luggage, and travel problems. Every active noun appears with its gender and plural.

Routes and Tickets

  • die Fahrkarte, die Fahrkarten — the ticket
  • der Bahnsteig, die Bahnsteige — the platform
  • die Verbindung, die Verbindungen — the connection (route)
  • der Umstieg, die Umstiege — the transfer (change of transport)
  • direkt — direct

Delays and Changes

  • die Verspätung, die Verspätungen — the delay
  • der Ausfall, die Ausfälle — the cancellation
  • die Durchsage, die Durchsagen — the announcement
  • pünktlich — on time
  • rechtzeitig — in time, with enough time

Luggage and Accommodation

  • der Koffer, die Koffer — the suitcase
  • das Gepäck (sg. only) — the luggage
  • die Unterkunft, die Unterkünfte — the accommodation
  • die Übernachtung, die Übernachtungen — the overnight stay
  • reservieren — to reserve

Travel Verbs and Prefixes

  • ankommen — to arrive (separable)
  • abfahren — to depart (separable)
  • umsteigen — to transfer, change (separable)
  • einsteigen / aussteigen — to board / to get off (separable)
  • verpassen — to miss (a connection, inseparable)

All plural and article information above is active; use it exactly as shown in your own sentences.

Aussprache

Pronunciation Focus

Stress in separable-prefix travel verbs

This chapter's separable-prefix verbs carry stress on the prefix, not on the verb stem: ANkommen, ABfahren, UMsteigen, EINsteigen, AUSsteigen. Compare this with the inseparable, unstressed prefix in verPASSEN, where the stress falls on the stem — review this contrast from the German-specific instructions on prefix stress.

Also review the long a in Bahnsteig and the diphthong ei shared by Bahnsteig, einsteigen, and Verspätung's neighboring vowel contrast with ä.

Self-check: reading the model dialogue aloud

After you read the model dialogue later in this chapter, read it aloud once and listen for the stressed prefixes in ankommen, abfahren, and umsteigen, and the unstressed prefix in verpassen.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns — Moving Through Space

Neu — Two-Way Prepositions in Motion

From location to motion: the same prepositions, a different case

In Chapter 2, you used two-way prepositions such as in, an, and auf with the dative to describe where something is located. This chapter activates the same prepositions with the accusative to describe motion toward a destination. The question that distinguishes the two uses is wo? ("where — at what location?") versus wohin? ("where to — in what direction?").

Compare: Ich bin im Zug. ("I am in the train" — location, dative) versus Ich steige in den Zug ein. ("I am getting into the train" — motion, accusative). The preposition in does not change; the case of the following noun phrase changes to signal motion.

  • Anna fährt in die Stadt. Anna is going into the city. motion — accusative
  • Anna ist schon in der Stadt. Anna is already in the city. location — dative
  • Mia geht an den Bahnsteig. Mia is walking to the platform. motion — accusative
  • Mia wartet am Bahnsteig. Mia is waiting at the platform. location — dative (contracted an + dem)
    • Grammar Note — Contracted prepositions in travel contexts

    You already met a few contracted forms in Chapter 3. This chapter recycles them heavily in travel contexts: zum (zu + dem), zur (zu + der), ins (in + das), and am (an + dem). These contractions are used naturally in everyday speech and writing, for example Ich fahre zum Bahnhof ("I'm going to the train station") and Der Zug fährt ins Nachbarland ("The train is going into the neighboring country").

    Baustein — Separable Verbs in the Verb Bracket
    Der Zug kommt um neun Uhr an.
    finite verb (position 2) ... separable prefix (final position) — the sentence bracket you reviewed in Chapter 3 now holds a separable prefix instead of a participle
    Wir steigen am Hauptbahnhof um.
    We're transferring at the main train station. (umsteigen splits into steigen ... um)
    Grammar Review — Separable verbs across tenses

    Separable-prefix verbs split apart in present-tense main clauses, with the prefix moving to the end. In the present perfect, the prefix attaches directly to the front of the participle, and ge- is inserted between the prefix and the stem: angekommen, abgefahren, umgestiegen. This chapter activates the present tense fully and previews the present perfect of these verbs in formulaic, recognizable frames; full perfect-tense production of all forms consolidates further in later chapters.

    • present: Der Zug fährt um acht Uhr ab. — The train departs at eight o'clock.
    • present perfect (formulaic recognition): Der Zug ist schon abgefahren. — The train has already departed.
    • question: Wann kommt der Bus an? — When does the bus arrive?
    Grammar Note — Comparatives for practical choices

    To compare transport options, add -er to most adjectives and adverbs: schnell → schneller ("faster"), billig → billiger ("cheaper"), bequem → bequemer ("more comfortable"). Use als to introduce the second item being compared. A small set of common comparatives is irregular, including gut → besser ("better").

    • Der Zug ist schneller als der Bus. — The train is faster than the bus.
    • Der Bus ist billiger als der Zug. — The bus is cheaper than the train.
    • Diese Verbindung ist besser als die andere. — This connection is better than the other one.
    Grammar Note — Recognizing wenn clauses (receptive)

    You will meet subordinating conjunctions productively later in this volume. For now, learn to recognize wenn ("if / whenever") clauses when they describe a condition, especially around delays and changes. Notice that the finite verb moves to the end of the wenn clause — this is the verb-final pattern the German-specific instructions describe for subordinate clauses.

    • Wenn der Zug Verspätung hat, nehme ich den Bus. — If the train is delayed, I'll take the bus. (recognize only; do not produce freely yet)
    Review — Separable Verb Bracket in a Main Clause
    Der Zug fährt um acht Uhr ab
    "The train departs at eight o'clock." — the finite verb stays in position two; the separable prefix (ab) moves to the end of the clause.

    Diagnostic Practice

    1. Choose dative or accusative: Anna fährt in ___ (die/der) Stadt.
    2. Complete with the correct separable prefix position: Der Bus ___ (ankommen) um zehn Uhr ___.
    3. Form a comparative: Der Zug ist ___ (schnell) als der Bus.
    4. Identify the case: Wir warten an ___ (der/den) Bahnsteig. (location)
    5. Reorder into a correct sentence: Bahnhof / steigen / Hauptbahnhof / wir / am / um.

    Model Dialogue

    Mia is waiting at the tram stop when she notices the schedule looks disrupted, and she asks another passenger for help.

    Mia wartet an der Haltestelle und fragt einen Fahrgast um Hilfe.

    Mia: Entschuldigung, wissen Sie, warum die Bahn nicht kommt? Excuse me, do you know why the tram isn't coming?
    Fahrgast: Es gab eine Durchsage: Die Linie 4 hat heute Verspätung. Ein Bus fährt als Ersatz. There was an announcement: Line 4 is delayed today. A bus is running as a replacement.
    Mia: Oh nein, ich muss zur Uni. Wo fährt der Ersatzbus ab? Oh no, I have to get to the university. Where does the replacement bus depart from?
    Fahrgast: Er fährt dort drüben ab, direkt vor dem Kiosk. Er ist meistens etwas langsamer als die Bahn, aber er kommt trotzdem an. It departs over there, right in front of the kiosk. It's usually a bit slower than the tram, but it still gets there.
    Mia: Gut, dann steige ich in den Bus ein. Muss ich irgendwo umsteigen? Okay, then I'll get on the bus. Do I need to transfer somewhere?
    Fahrgast: Nein, er fährt direkt bis zur Uni. Wenn Sie jetzt einsteigen, kommen Sie noch rechtzeitig an. No, it goes directly to the university. If you get on now, you'll still arrive in time.
    Mia: Vielen Dank für die Hilfe! Ich will meinen Termin nicht verpassen. Thank you so much for the help! I don't want to miss my appointment.

    Comprehension — Model Dialogue

    1. Warum kommt die Bahn nicht?

    2. Wo fährt der Ersatzbus ab?

    3. Muss Mia umsteigen?

    4. Find one example each of a separable verb in final position and a two-way preposition used for motion.

    Lesen

    Reading Practice

    Read the route comparison below and answer the questions that follow.

    Reisevergleich: Berlin nach Leipzig

    Option 1 — Zug: Der Zug fährt direkt von Berlin nach Leipzig. Die Fahrt dauert etwa eineinhalb Stunden. Der Zug ist schneller als der Bus, aber auch teurer. Anna muss nicht umsteigen.

    Option 2 — Bus: Der Bus fährt auch direkt, aber die Fahrt dauert etwa drei Stunden. Der Bus ist billiger als der Zug und bequemer für großes Gepäck, weil man mehr Platz hat.

    Option 3 — Zug mit Umstieg: Diese Verbindung ist am billigsten, aber Anna muss in Halle umsteigen. Wenn der erste Zug Verspätung hat, verpasst sie vielleicht den Anschluss.

    Anna denkt: "Ich nehme lieber den direkten Zug. Er ist zwar teurer, aber ich komme pünktlich an und muss nicht umsteigen."

    Verständnisfragen

    1. Welche Option ist am schnellsten?

    2. Welche Option ist am billigsten, und was ist das Risiko?

    3. Für welche Option entscheidet sich Anna, und warum?

    Culture and Communication

    Culture and Communication — Not All Journeys Are by Rail

    Rail travel is common and often practical in many parts of the German-speaking world, but it is not the only or default way people get around. Depending on the region, learners and travelers may rely on buses, trams, cars, bicycles, ferries, or a mix of these. Avoid assuming that every German-speaking community has the same transport options, prices, or schedules — always verify current, location-specific details when they matter.

    Regional Note — Accessibility in Travel

    Accessibility information — step-free platforms, elevators, reserved seating, and assistance services — is a practical part of travel planning for many people, not an optional afterthought. When describing a journey, it can be appropriate to ask or mention whether a connection is accessible, using phrases such as Ist der Bahnsteig stufenfrei? ("Is the platform step-free?"). Practices and available services vary by station, region, and country.

    Common Mistakes to Avoid

    Motion/location case confusion

    Error: *Anna fährt in der Stadt. (intended: motion)

    Correction: Anna fährt in die Stadt. When describing movement toward a destination, two-way prepositions take the accusative, not the dative.

    Forgetting the separable prefix

    Error: *Der Zug abfährt um acht Uhr.

    Correction: Der Zug fährt um acht Uhr ab. In a main clause, the separable prefix moves to the end; it does not stay attached to the finite verb.

    Missing als in comparisons

    Error: *Der Zug ist schneller der Bus.

    Correction: Der Zug ist schneller als der Bus. German comparatives require als ("than") before the second item being compared.

Freizeit, Wetter und Pläne

Leisure, Weather, and Plans · Speak and Write German: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Talk about hobbies, sports, and leisure activities using gern, lieber, and am liebsten.
  2. Describe basic weather and the seasons.
  3. Invite someone to do something and accept, decline, or suggest an alternative.
  4. Express ability, permission, obligation, and desire using the modal verbs können, wollen, müssen, and dürfen.
  5. Build a modal sentence bracket with a finite modal in second position and an infinitive at the end of the clause.
  6. Use the present tense with a time expression to refer to planned future events.
  7. Connect ideas with the coordinating conjunctions und, aber, oder, and denn.
  8. Use the conjunctive adverbs dann and deshalb with correct V2 order.
  9. Recognize highly formulaic scheduling expressions with wenn.
  10. Make, negotiate, and confirm a leisure plan in speaking and writing.

Communication Scenario

It is a Thursday evening in Leipzig. Paul checks the weather forecast for the weekend and messages his friend Lena to see whether she wants to do something together. Lena is interested but has obligations of her own — she has to study for an exam and isn't sure she can join. Over the course of a short conversation, the two negotiate what is possible, what each of them wants, and what they finally agree to do.

  1. Before reading further, think in English about a recent time you made plans with a friend that depended on the weather or on someone's schedule. What did you want to do, and what got in the way?
  2. As you work through this chapter, pay attention to how modal verbs let speakers talk about wanting, being able to, having to, and being allowed to do something — often within a single short exchange.

Pronunciation Focus

Stress and rhythm in the modal sentence bracket

When a modal verb and an infinitive form a sentence bracket, the modal verb usually carries the main sentence stress, while the infinitive at the end of the clause carries secondary stress without being swallowed. Practice: Ich MUSS heute Abend lernen. and Wir WOLLEN am Samstag schwimmen gehen. Keep the rhythm even across the whole bracket rather than rushing toward the final infinitive.

Sentence stress for contrast and obligation

German speakers often shift sentence stress onto whichever word carries new or contrasting information. Compare: Ich KANN heute nicht. (emphasizing inability) versus Ich kann HEUTE nicht. (emphasizing that today specifically does not work, perhaps implying another day would). Practice both with a partner or aloud.

Vowel length in weather vocabulary

Review long and short vowels using weather words: long o in Sonne is actually short (double n signals a short vowel), while Regen has a long, tense e. Read aloud: Im Sommer scheint oft die Sonne, aber im Herbst regnet es viel.

Question and invitation intonation

Practice the rising-then-falling pattern typical of a friendly invitation: Hast du Lust, am Samstag ins Kino zu gehen? Keep the pitch warm and inviting rather than flat or demanding.

Pronunciation Note — Receptive reductions

In fast, casual conversation you may hear haste for hast du or kannste for kannst du. These reductions are common in relaxed spoken German but are not appropriate in careful writing or formal speech. Recognize them; do not produce them yet.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter introduces modal verbs, present tense for planned future events, and two ways of connecting ideas across clauses.

12.2 Modal verbs: meaning and use

  • können — ability or possibility: Ich kann gut schwimmen. I can swim well.
  • wollen — intention or desire: Wir wollen am Samstag wandern. We want to hike on Saturday.
  • müssen — necessity or obligation: Lena muss heute Abend lernen. Lena has to study tonight.
  • dürfen — permission: Darf ich mitkommen? Am I allowed to come along?

möchten, which you already use as a polite, formulaic way to say what you would like, is closely related to mögen but is not simply an infinitive meaning "to want." A fuller grammatical treatment of mögen and the forms underlying möchten is outside this chapter's scope.

12.3 The modal sentence bracket

A modal verb occupies the normal second-position verb slot. The infinitive that carries the main meaning goes to the end of the clause, forming a bracket around everything in between.

Subjekt + Modalverb (Position 2) + ... + Infinitiv (Satzende)
The finite modal verb is conjugated; the infinitive at the end never changes form.
Ich muss heute Abend für die Prüfung lernen.
I have to study for the exam tonight.

  • Darfst du am Freitag mit uns schwimmen gehen? Are you allowed to go swimming with us on Friday?

12.4 Present tense for planned future events

German ordinarily uses the present tense, supported by a time expression, to talk about events that are already planned or scheduled. A separate future tense is not required at this stage.

  • Wir gehen nächstes Wochenende wandern. We are going hiking next weekend.
  • Lena hat morgen eine Prüfung. Lena has an exam tomorrow.

Do not add a German progressive form to express "is going to" or "will be doing." The plain present tense, anchored by a time word, carries this meaning.

12.5 gern, lieber, and am liebsten

These words consolidate the preference scale you began using with food in Chapter 8 and now apply to leisure activities.

  • Ich schwimme gern. I like to swim.
  • Ich wandere lieber. I prefer to hike.
  • Am liebsten gehe ich ins Kino. Most of all, I like going to the movies.

12.6 Coordinating conjunctions: und, aber, oder, denn

These conjunctions connect two independent clauses without affecting word order in either clause. Each clause keeps its own normal V2 order.

  • Ich spiele gern Fußball, aber ich schwimme lieber. I like playing soccer, but I prefer swimming.
  • Wir können ins Kino gehen, oder wir bleiben zu Hause. We can go to the movies, or we can stay home.
  • Lena kann heute nicht, denn sie muss lernen. Lena can't today, because she has to study.

12.7 Conjunctive adverbs: dann and deshalb

Unlike coordinating conjunctions, conjunctive adverbs occupy a clause position of their own. When a conjunctive adverb stands first, the finite verb still follows immediately in second position.

  • Wir frühstücken zuerst, dann gehen wir spazieren. First we have breakfast, then we go for a walk.dann = position 1; gehen = position 2
  • Es regnet, deshalb bleiben wir heute zu Hause. It's raining, so we're staying home today.
Grammar Note — Conjunction or adverb?

aber and denn do not occupy a position in the clause; the subject and verb keep their ordinary order right after the comma: ..., aber ich wandere lieber. deshalb, by contrast, occupies position one when placed first, which sends the verb directly after it: ..., deshalb wandere ich lieber. Confusing the two leads to a word-order error, not just a style difference.

12.8 Recognizing wenn in fixed scheduling expressions

You will encounter formulaic expressions such as wenn das Wetter gut ist ("if the weather is good") in this chapter's dialogue and reading. Recognize these as complete chunks for now. The full subordinating behavior of wenn, including clause-final verb position in less formulaic contexts, is not a productive target until later chapters.

Model Dialogue

Kontext: Donnerstagabend. Paul schreibt Lena eine Nachricht und ruft sie dann an, um über das Wochenende zu sprechen. Die beiden duzen sich.
Setting: Thursday evening. Paul messages Lena and then calls her to talk about the weekend. The two use du with each other.

PAUL Hallo Lena! Hast du Lust, am Samstag etwas zu machen? Das Wetter soll gut werden. Hi Lena! Do you feel like doing something on Saturday? The weather is supposed to be good.
LENA Das klingt gut, aber ich muss am Samstagvormittag noch für eine Prüfung lernen. That sounds good, but I have to study for an exam on Saturday morning.
PAUL Kein Problem. Können wir uns am Nachmittag treffen? Ich will schwimmen gehen, wenn die Sonne scheint. No problem. Can we meet in the afternoon? I want to go swimming if the sun is shining.
LENA Schwimmen mag ich nicht so gern, aber ich wandere sehr gern. Dürfen wir stattdessen wandern gehen? I don't like swimming that much, but I really like hiking. May we go hiking instead?
PAUL Klar, das ist auch gut. Ich wandere eigentlich lieber als schwimmen. Am liebsten gehe ich aber ins Kino, wenn das Wetter schlecht ist. Sure, that works too. I actually prefer hiking to swimming. But most of all I like going to the movies if the weather is bad.
LENA Gut, dann machen wir einen Plan: Wenn es regnet, gehen wir ins Kino. Wenn die Sonne scheint, gehen wir wandern. Good, then let's make a plan: if it rains, we'll go to the movies. If the sun shines, we'll go hiking.
PAUL Perfekt! Ich rufe dich am Samstagmittag an, dann besprechen wir die Details. Perfect! I'll call you Saturday at noon, then we'll discuss the details.
LENA Super, bis dann! Und danke, dass du an meine Prüfung denkst. Great, see you then! And thanks for thinking of my exam.

Verständnisfragen — Comprehension questions:

  1. Warum kann Lena am Samstagvormittag nicht? (Why can't Lena on Saturday morning?)
  2. Was will Paul machen, wenn die Sonne scheint? (What does Paul want to do if the sun shines?)
  3. Welche Aktivität mag Lena lieber, Schwimmen oder Wandern? (Which activity does Lena prefer, swimming or hiking?)
  4. Was machen die beiden, wenn es regnet? (What do the two do if it rains?)
  5. Wann ruft Paul Lena an? (When does Paul call Lena?)

Writing Workshop

In this workshop, you will plan and write a short invitation message and a reply, combining modal verbs, weather, and preference language.

  1. Analyze the model. Reread the dialogue between Paul and Lena. Identify: (a) every modal verb and its meaning, (b) the sentence with deshalb or dann, and (c) how the two negotiate a plan that depends on the weather.
  2. Plan your message. In notes, list: an activity you want to propose, when you are free, one obligation that might get in the way, and a weather-dependent backup plan.
  3. Write your first draft. Write an invitation message of four to six sentences, then a reply of four to six sentences. Include at least two different modal verbs, one coordinating conjunction, one conjunctive adverb, and one weather expression.
  4. Revise. Check your draft against the editing checklist before writing a final version.

Checkliste — Editing checklist:

  • Every finite modal verb is in second position, with the infinitive at the end of its clause.
  • aber and denn do not change the word order of the clause that follows.
  • dann and deshalb, when placed first, are immediately followed by the finite verb.
  • Preferences are expressed correctly with gern, lieber, or am liebsten.
  • Every noun is capitalized, with correct gender and plural where relevant.
  • du and Sie are used consistently and appropriately for the relationship.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Inviting and responding

Among many German-speaking peer groups, an informal invitation such as Hast du Lust, ...? invites a direct but friendly response — accepting, declining, or proposing an alternative is all considered normal and polite, rather than confrontational. Expectations around how far in advance to plan, and how firm a plan is once made, vary by region, age group, and individual relationship rather than following one fixed national pattern.

Regional Variation — Leisure across the DACH region

Hiking (wandern) is a widely popular leisure activity across Germany, Austria, and Switzerland, supported by extensive marked trail networks, though participation and preferred activities vary by region, climate, and individual interest. Swimming culture also differs: many German and Austrian towns maintain public outdoor swimming areas (Freibäder) and natural lakes, while Switzerland's many lakes and rivers similarly support swimming as a common warm-weather activity. Avoid treating any one activity as representative of all German-speaking communities.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Leaving the infinitive in second position

Error: *Ich kann schwimmen am Samstag.

Correction: Ich kann am Samstag schwimmen. The conjugated modal verb stays in second position; the infinitive moves to the end of the clause, forming the sentence bracket.

Common Mistake — Treating deshalb like aber

Error: *Es regnet, deshalb wir bleiben zu Hause.

Correction: Es regnet, deshalb bleiben wir zu Hause. deshalb occupies position one, so the finite verb must follow immediately in position two.

Common Mistake — Confusing möchten with a full infinitive meaning "to want"

Error: Treating möchten as freely interchangeable with wollen in every context, including very direct or blunt statements.

Correction: Ich möchte ins Kino gehen is a softer, more polite way of expressing a wish than Ich will ins Kino gehen, which can sound more forceful. Choose based on the tone you intend.

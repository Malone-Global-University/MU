الهُوِيَّة وَاللُّغَاتIdentity, Origin, and Languages

Origin, nationality, languages, contact information, and the final letters of the Arabic alphabet · Speak and Write Arabic: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Ask and state where someone is from, using مِنْ أَيْنَ أَنْتَ؟ and مِنْ أَيْنَ أَنْتِ؟
  2. Ask and state where someone lives, using the preposition فِي.
  3. Name languages and say which languages you speak or study.
  4. Exchange nationality information using masculine and feminine nationality adjectives.
  5. Use the independent pronouns هُوَ and هِيَ to talk about a third person.
  6. Ask a wider range of information questions: who, what, where, from where, which, and how many.
  7. Give and understand a telephone number and an email address in Arabic.
  8. Introduce another person to a third party.
  9. Recognize and write the final group of Arabic letters, completing all 28 core letters of the Arabic alphabet.
  10. Count from 0 to 20 in Arabic.

Communication Scenario

It is the institute's welcome reception in Amman, held on the rooftop terrace after the first week of classes. Students and instructors from several different classes are mingling, and Ustādha Reem has asked everyone to introduce themselves to at least three people they do not already know — their name, where they are from, where they currently live, and what languages they speak. Tom, still a little nervous, finds himself face to face with a new classmate from a different section.

  1. Think about how you would answer "Where are you from?" and "What languages do you speak?" in your own life. Could you give a short answer to both questions right now in Arabic?
  2. You can already greet someone, give your name, and identify objects and people in the classroom. This chapter adds the language you need to talk about where you and others are from, what languages you use, and how to stay in touch.

لَام أَلِف — The lām-alif ligature

When ل is immediately followed by ا, Arabic writing joins them into a single combined shape called لَام أَلِف (lām-alif): لا. This is not a new letter — it is simply how ل and ا are always written together. You have already produced this ligature without naming it, in the negation word لَا from Chapter 2.

لَوْحَة المَفَاتِيح العَرَبِيَّة — The Arabic keyboard

Now that you know all 28 core letters, take a moment to locate them on an Arabic keyboard layout, whether physical or on-screen. Most devices allow you to add an Arabic keyboard in your system language settings; switching between English and Arabic input is typically done with a simple key combination or on-screen toggle.

Script Note — Arabic has no capital letters

Unlike English, Arabic script has no separate uppercase and lowercase forms. A letter's shape changes only according to its position in the word — isolated, initial, medial, or final — never according to capitalization. There is therefore no Arabic equivalent of capitalizing the first letter of a sentence or a proper name; instead, names and sentence boundaries are recognized from context and punctuation.

تَدْرِيب — Practice: Copy each letter of the third group in its isolated form three times. Then write your own name in Arabic letters as best you can, using the full 28-letter alphabet you now know, and check it with your instructor or a partner.

Pronunciation

ع versus ء — Two distinct throat sounds

ع (ʿayn) is one of the most distinctive sounds in Arabic: a voiced sound produced by constricting the pharynx, deep in the throat. It is not simply a vowel onset, and it is not the same as the glottal stop ء (hamza), which you first encountered as a heard consonant in Chapter 1's name and greeting forms. Compare:

  • عِرَاقِيّ — begins with عʿirāqī — Iraqi
  • أُرْدُنِّيّ — begins with hamza أurdunnī — Jordanian

Many English speakers find ع the most challenging Arabic sound at first. A useful first step is to feel the muscular constriction in your throat as if beginning to gag gently, then voice through that constriction.

غ versus خ — A voicing contrast

غ (ghayn) is produced in the same place as خ from Chapter 2, but غ is voiced, similar to a French or German uvular "r" sound, while خ is voiceless. Compare غ in المَغْرِب with خ in a word like أَخْضَر from Chapter 2.

ق versus ك — Back versus front

ق (qāf) is a voiceless stop produced further back in the mouth than English "k," at the uvula. ك (kāf) is produced further forward, close to English "k." Compare:

  • قَلَم — ق, produced far backqalam — pen (Chapter 2)
  • كِتَاب — ك, produced further forwardkitāb — book (Chapter 2)
Pronunciation Note — Syllable structure in Arabic words

Arabic syllables are built from a small set of predictable patterns: a consonant plus a short vowel (CV), a consonant plus a long vowel (CVV), or a consonant-vowel-consonant sequence (CVC), sometimes followed by a further consonant cluster marked with sukūn. Recognizing these patterns helps you read new words aloud smoothly. For example, مِصْرِيّ breaks into the syllables miṣ-rī, while أُرْدُنِّيّ breaks into ur-dun-nī, with the doubled ن marked by shadda closing one syllable and opening the next.

تَدْرِيب — Practice: With a partner or recording, repeat each pair several times, focusing on the contrast: عَ / أَ, غَ / خَ, قَ / كَ. Then read the nationality words in the vocabulary section aloud, paying attention to each new sound.

Grammar Instruction

3.1 هُوَ وَهِيَ — Talking about a third person

Purpose: refer to someone other than yourself or the person you are speaking to.

In Chapter 1 you learned أَنَا (I), أَنْتَ (you, masculine), and أَنْتِ (you, feminine). This chapter adds the third-person singular independent pronouns:

ArabicTransliterationEnglish meaning
هُوَhuwahe / it (masculine)
هِيَhiyashe / it (feminine)
  • هُوَ مِنْ مِصْر.huwa min Miṣr. — He is from Egypt.
  • هِيَ طَالِبَة.hiya ṭāliba. — She is a student.

Like the nominal sentences you have used since Chapter 1, no separate word for "is" appears here. You will meet the plural and dual independent pronouns later in the volume; for now, focus on the singular system.

3.2 مِنْ أَيْنَ أَنْتَ؟ — Asking and stating origin

Purpose: ask where someone is from and answer using the preposition مِنْ.

مِنْ means "from." Combined with the question word أَيْنَ ("where"), it forms مِنْ أَيْنَ, "from where":

  • مِنْ أَيْنَ أَنْتَ؟ — أَنَا مِنَ الأُرْدُنّ.min ayna anta? — anā mina l-Urdunn. — Where are you (m.) from? — I am from Jordan.
  • مِنْ أَيْنَ أَنْتِ؟ — أَنَا مِنْ كَنَدَا.min ayna anti? — anā min Kanadā. — Where are you (f.) from? — I am from Canada.
  • هِيَ مِنْ لُبْنَان.hiya min Lubnān. — She is from Lebanon.
Grammar Note — مِنْ before the definite article

Notice مِنَ الأُرْدُنّ above: before the definite article الـ, the preposition مِنْ commonly adds a short vowel and is pronounced mina rather than min, simply to avoid two vowelless consonants colliding in connected speech. This is a pronunciation adjustment, not a change in spelling or meaning. Before a name without الـ, such as مِصْر or كَنَدَا, the plain form مِنْ is used.

3.3 فِي — Stating where someone lives

Purpose: describe current residence using the preposition فِي ("in").

  • أَنَا أَسْكُن فِي عَمَّان.anā askun fī ʿAmmān. — I live in Amman.
  • هُوَ يَسْكُن فِي القَاهِرَة.huwa yaskun fī l-Qāhira. — He lives in Cairo.

The verb forms أَسْكُن, يَسْكُن, and تَسْكُن are presented here as supported formulas tied to the first, third masculine, and third feminine persons. You will study the full imperfect conjugation system, including how these forms relate to one another, beginning in Chapter 7.

3.4 لُغَات — Talking about languages

Purpose: say which languages you speak or study, and ask about another person's languages.

  • أَنَا أَتَكَلَّم العَرَبِيَّة وَالإِنْجِلِيزِيَّة.anā atakallam al-ʿarabiyya wa-l-injilīziyya. — I speak Arabic and English.
  • هِيَ تَدْرُس الفَرَنْسِيَّة.hiya tadrus al-faransiyya. — She studies French.
  • هَلْ تَتَكَلَّم العَرَبِيَّة؟ — نَعَمْ، قَلِيلًا.hal tatakallam al-ʿarabiyya? — naʿam, qalīlan. — Do you speak Arabic? — Yes, a little.

أَتَكَلَّم ("I speak") and تَتَكَلَّم ("you/she speak[s]") are introduced here as formulaic expressions for talking about languages. Like the residence verbs above, they connect to the productive imperfect system beginning in Chapter 7.

3.5 الجِنْسِيَّة — Masculine and feminine nationality adjectives

Purpose: describe a person's nationality with an adjective that agrees in gender, following the noun-adjective agreement pattern from Chapter 2.

Nationality adjectives behave like the color adjectives you studied in Chapter 2: they follow the noun they describe and agree with it in gender.

  • هُوَ طَالِب مَغْرِبِيّ.huwa ṭālib maghribī. — He is a Moroccan student.
  • هِيَ طَالِبَة مَغْرِبِيَّة.hiya ṭāliba maghribiyya. — She is a Moroccan student.

Used alone as a predicate after a pronoun, the nationality adjective also agrees with the pronoun's gender:

  • أَنَا أُرْدُنِّيّ.anā urdunnī. — I am Jordanian. (male speaker)
  • أَنَا أُرْدُنِّيَّة.anā urdunniyya. — I am Jordanian. (female speaker)

3.6 أَدَوَات الاسْتِفْهَام — Expanding your question words

Purpose: ask a wider range of information questions using question words in initial position.

Arabic information questions place the question word at the beginning of the sentence, with no separate auxiliary verb like English "do" or "does":

  • مَنْ هَذَا؟man hādhā? — Who is this? (from Chapter 2)
  • مَا اسْمُكَ؟mā ismuka? — What is your name? (review from Chapter 1)
  • أَيْنَ تَسْكُن؟ayna taskun? — Where do you live?
  • مِنْ أَيْنَ أَنْتَ؟min ayna anta? — Where are you from?
  • أَيّ لُغَة تَتَكَلَّم؟ayy lugha tatakallam? — Which language do you speak?
Grammar Note — No equivalent of English "do/does"

English forms questions with an auxiliary verb: "Where do you live?" Arabic has no equivalent auxiliary. The question word simply begins the sentence, followed directly by the subject and verb (or, in a nominal sentence, the predicate). Resist the urge to insert any extra word corresponding to English "do" or "does."

Language Focus

Personal information is built from small, recombinable pieces. This chapter's core skill — exchanging name, origin, residence, languages, and contact details — depends on combining systems you already partly control: the pronoun system from Chapters 1-2, now expanded with هُوَ/هِيَ; the noun-adjective agreement pattern from Chapter 2, now applied to nationality; and a small set of new prepositions and question words. Notice how little is actually new grammar — most of this chapter is about applying familiar patterns to a new communicative purpose.

Model Dialogue

Setting: The institute's welcome reception on the rooftop terrace in Amman. Tom meets Karīm, a new classmate from a different section, for the first time.
السِّيَاق: حَفْل تَرْحِيب فِي المَعْهَد عَلَى السَّطْح فِي عَمَّان. يَلْتَقِي تُوم بِكَرِيم، زَمِيل جَدِيد مِنْ شُعْبَة أُخْرَى، لِأَوَّل مَرَّة.

TOM مَسَاء الخَيْر! اسْمِي تُوم. وَأَنْتَ؟ Good evening! My name is Tom. And you?
KARĪM مَسَاء النُّور. اسْمِي كَرِيم. تَشَرَّفْنَا. Good evening to you. My name is Karim. Nice to meet you.
TOM مِنْ أَيْنَ أَنْتَ، كَرِيم؟ Where are you from, Karim?
KARĪM أَنَا مِنَ المَغْرِب، مِنْ مَدِينَة الرِّبَاط. وَأَنْتَ؟ I am from Morocco, from the city of Rabat. And you?
TOM أَنَا أَمْرِيكِيّ، وَأَسْكُن الآن فِي عَمَّان. I am American, and I live in Amman now.
KARĪM رَائِع! أَيّ لُغَات تَتَكَلَّم؟ Great! Which languages do you speak?
TOM أَتَكَلَّم الإِنْجِلِيزِيَّة، وَأَدْرُس العَرَبِيَّة الآن. وَأَنْتَ؟ I speak English, and I am studying Arabic now. And you?
KARĪM أَتَكَلَّم العَرَبِيَّة وَالفَرَنْسِيَّة وَالأَمَازِيغِيَّة قَلِيلًا. I speak Arabic, French, and a little Amazigh.
TOM مُمْتَاز! مَا رَقْم هَاتِفِكَ؟ أُرِيد أَنْ نَتَوَاصَل. Excellent! What is your phone number? I'd like for us to stay in touch.
KARĪM بِالطَّبْع. رَقْمِي صِفْر سَبْعَة تِسْعَة... وَهَذَا بَرِيدِي الإِلِكْتْرُونِيّ أَيْضًا. Of course. My number is zero seven nine... and here is my email too.

أَسْئِلَة الفَهْم — Comprehension questions:

  1. مِنْ أَيْنَ كَرِيم؟ (Where is Karim from?)
  2. أَيْنَ يَسْكُن تُوم الآن؟ (Where does Tom currently live?)
  3. How many languages does Karim mention, and what are they?
  4. What two pieces of contact information does Tom ask for, and which does Karim offer without being asked?
Grammar Note — رَقْمِي and بَرِيدِي

Karim says رَقْمِي ("my number") and بَرِيدِي ("my mail/email"), using the same attached pronoun ـِي ("my") that you first met in اسْمِي back in Chapter 1. You will study the complete system of attached possessive pronouns across all persons systematically in Chapter 4 — for now, simply recognize this recurring "my" pattern wherever it appears.

Listening

Ask your instructor, a partner, or a text-to-speech tool to read the following four short exchanges aloud at a moderate pace. Listen twice without reading along. Then answer the questions below.

١. أ: مِنْ أَيْنَ أَنْتِ؟ ب: أَنَا مِنْ لُبْنَان، وَأَسْكُن فِي بَيْرُوت.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Language, nationality, and identity are distinct

Speaking Arabic does not make someone an Arab, and being from an Arabic-speaking country does not mean someone's first or only language is Arabic. Countries such as Morocco, Iraq, and Lebanon are home to speakers of Amazigh languages, Kurdish, Armenian, and many other languages alongside Arabic. Likewise, learners of Arabic come from every nationality and linguistic background. This chapter's vocabulary intentionally keeps language, nationality, and personal identity as separate, combinable pieces of information rather than assuming they always overlap.

Culture in Context — Place names may have more than one form

Some place names in this chapter, such as بَرِيطَانْيَا or أَمْرِيكَا, are adaptations of names from other languages and may appear with slightly different spellings across different textbooks, media outlets, or countries. This reflects ordinary variation in how Arabic absorbs international place names rather than an error in either source.

Regional Variation — Asking where someone is from

The MSA question مِنْ أَيْنَ أَنْتَ؟ is correct and clearly understood everywhere, and is the form you should produce in this course. In everyday spoken interaction, many regional varieties use a shortened or differently constructed question for the same purpose. As in earlier chapters, this textbook's productive forms remain MSA; regional spoken equivalents will continue to be introduced and clearly labeled as the course progresses.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Confusing ع and ء

Error: Pronouncing ع as a simple vowel onset or glottal stop, the same way hamza is pronounced.

Correction: ع requires audible pharyngeal constriction and voicing; it is a full consonant, distinct from hamza. Practice the contrast in pairs like عِرَاقِيّ versus أُرْدُنِّيّ.

Common Mistake — Using مَنْ to ask about a country

Error: Asking *مَنْ أَنْتَ؟ when intending to ask where someone is from.

Correction: مَنْ أَنْتَ؟ actually asks "who are you?" To ask about origin, use مِنْ أَيْنَ أَنْتَ؟ ("from where are you?").

Common Mistake — Mismatched nationality gender

Error: A female speaker saying *أَنَا مَغْرِبِيّ, using the masculine nationality form.

Correction: Nationality adjectives agree with the speaker's or subject's gender, just like the color adjectives from Chapter 2: أَنَا مَغْرِبِيَّة for a female speaker.

Common Mistake — Inserting an English-style auxiliary

Error: Trying to translate "Where do you live?" word for word and inserting an extra word for "do."

Correction: Arabic questions need no auxiliary: أَيْنَ تَسْكُن؟ places the question word directly before the verb, with nothing corresponding to English "do."

Common Mistake — Dropping the vowel adjustment on مِنْ before الـ

Error: Writing or saying *مِنْ الأُرْدُنّ with an awkward consonant collision.

Correction: Before the definite article, مِنْ is pronounced mina: مِنَ الأُرْدُنّ. This is a pronunciation feature of connected speech, not a spelling change to مِنْ itself.

Rückblick 2: Ein praktischer Tag

Unit II Assessment · Speak and Write German: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

This chapter introduces no new grammar. By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Describe a home and neighborhood and say what a place contains, using es gibt and location prepositions with the dative.
  2. Locate objects and places using wo, supported dative articles, and common contractions such as im and am.
  3. Describe a complete daily schedule using separable-prefix verbs, reflexive verbs, and clock time.
  4. Order food and drink, state preferences, and manage a basic café or restaurant exchange.
  5. Shop for an item, ask about price, size, and color, and use supported attributive adjective endings.
  6. Maintain correct V2 word order, including inversion after a fronted time or place expression.
  7. Use nicht and kein appropriately to negate a statement.
  8. Shift naturally between du and Sie as the situation changes.
  9. Combine home, routine, food, and shopping communication into one connected practical day.
  10. Demonstrate accurate noun capitalization, gender and plural use, and basic adjective endings in connected speech and writing.

Communication Scenario

It is Saturday in Hamburg. Mira has invited her new classmate Sofia to spend the day together: first a visit to Mira's apartment and neighborhood, then a relaxed morning following part of Mira's usual routine, lunch at a small café, and finally a short shopping trip before Sofia heads home. Nothing in this chapter is new — every grammar system below was introduced in Chapters 6 through 9. The goal now is to use it all together, the way a real day actually unfolds.

  1. Before reading further, think in English about your own typical Saturday: where you are, what you do first, where you eat, and whether you go shopping. You will build a German version of a similar day in this chapter's performance task.
  2. As you work through the review sections, notice how often a single sentence draws on more than one system at once — for example, a dative location phrase inside a sentence that also has a separable verb and V2 word order. This layering is exactly what ordinary German conversation requires.

Pronunciation and Orthography Review

ich-Laut and ach-Laut

Practice the contrast you built in Chapters 3 and 6: the front ich-Laut in words like ich and Milch, and the back ach-Laut in words like Buch and auch. Read aloud: Ich koche gern. Auch die Küche braucht Brot.

ö and ü

Review rounded front vowels in food and shopping vocabulary: möchten, Größe, Frühstück. Keep lip rounding distinct from unrounded e and i.

Separable-prefix stress

Stress the separable prefix, not the verb stem: AUFstehen, EINkaufen, ANfangen. This stress pattern stays constant whether the prefix is attached (infinitive) or separated (finite main clause).

Compound stress

Most compound nouns stress the first element: WOHNzimmer, SUPERmarkt, BÄCKERei. Practice this with several nouns from the vocabulary tables above.

Orthography review

  • Noun capitalization remains constant in every sentence, regardless of position. Capitalize every noun, not only the first word of a sentence.
  • Separable verbs are written as one word in the infinitive: aufstehen, not auf stehen.
  • Prices use a decimal comma: 3,50 €, not 3.50 €.
  • Lowercase adjectives stay lowercase even directly before a noun they describe predicatively: Das Brot ist frisch.

Grammar Review

This chapter introduces no new grammar system. The sections below consolidate Chapters 6–9 and connect them to the V2 and case foundations from Unit I.

10.1 es gibt and dative location phrases

es gibt always takes the accusative and states that something exists or is present. Dative location phrases, introduced with prepositions such as in, an, and neben, state where something is.

  • In meiner Straße gibt es eine Bäckerei. There is a bakery on my street.
  • Der Tisch steht in der Küche. The table is in the kitchen.dative: in + der (feminine dative)
  • Die Bäckerei ist neben dem Supermarkt. The bakery is next to the supermarket.

10.2 Separable verbs and the sentence bracket

In a simple main clause, the separable prefix moves to the end of the clause while the conjugated stem stays in second position.

  • Ich stehe um sieben Uhr auf. I get up at seven o'clock.[stehe] ... [auf] — sentence bracket
  • Am Samstag kaufen wir zusammen ein. On Saturday we shop together.fronted time expression, verb second, prefix final

10.3 Reflexive verbs and nicht

  • Ich dusche mich um halb acht. I shower at half past seven.
  • Sofia zieht sich schnell an. Sofia gets dressed quickly.
  • Wir frühstücken heute nicht zu Hause. We are not having breakfast at home today.nicht negates the location phrase

10.4 möchten, kein, and polite ordering

  • Ich hätte gern einen Kaffee und ein Brötchen. I would like a coffee and a roll.
  • Möchtest du auch eine Suppe? Would you also like a soup?
  • Nein, danke, ich möchte keine Suppe, nur Brot. No thanks, I don't want any soup, just bread.

10.5 Attributive adjective endings (nominative and accusative)

  • Ich suche eine schöne Jacke. I'm looking for a nice jacket.accusative, ein-word, feminine: -e
  • Der blaue Pullover ist zu teuer. The blue sweater is too expensive.nominative, definite article, masculine: -e
Grammar Note — Layering systems in one sentence

Consider: Am Samstag gehe ich zuerst in den Supermarkt, dann kaufe ich eine neue Jacke. This single pair of clauses uses a fronted time expression with inversion, a destination prepositional phrase, V2 order in the second clause after a conjunctive adverb, and an attributive adjective ending — four systems from four different chapters working together. This is the level of integration this chapter's performance task expects.

Model Dialogue

Kontext: Samstagmorgen in Hamburg. Sofia kommt zu Mira nach Hause. Die beiden nutzen du miteinander; später, im Café und im Geschäft, sprechen sie mit dem Personal in der Sie-Form.
Setting: Saturday morning in Hamburg. Sofia comes to Mira's home. The two use du with each other; later, at the café and in the shop, they speak to staff using Sie.

MIRA Hallo, Sofia! Komm rein. Das ist mein Wohnzimmer, und da hinten ist die Küche. Hi, Sofia! Come in. This is my living room, and back there is the kitchen.
SOFIA Sehr gemütlich! Gibt es einen Supermarkt in der Nähe? Very cozy! Is there a supermarket nearby?
MIRA Ja, der Supermarkt ist gleich neben der Bäckerei. Übrigens, am Samstag stehe ich immer spät auf und gehe dann erst einkaufen. Yes, the supermarket is right next to the bakery. By the way, on Saturdays I always get up late and only then go shopping.
SOFIA Gute Idee. Gehen wir jetzt zusammen frühstücken? Good idea. Shall we go have breakfast together now?
MIRA Gern! Es gibt ein nettes Café um die Ecke. Sure! There's a nice café around the corner.
KELLNER Guten Tag! Was möchten Sie? Good day! What would you like?
MIRA Ich hätte gern einen Kaffee und ein Käsebrötchen, bitte. I'd like a coffee and a cheese roll, please.
SOFIA Für mich bitte einen Tee. Ich möchte heute kein Brötchen, nur etwas Obst. For me a tea, please. I don't want a roll today, just some fruit.
MIRA Nach dem Frühstück gehen wir noch einkaufen, ja? Ich brauche eine neue Jacke. After breakfast shall we go shopping a bit, yes? I need a new jacket.
VERKÄUFERIN Guten Tag! Suchen Sie etwas Bestimmtes? Good day! Are you looking for something specific?
MIRA Ja, ich suche eine warme Jacke, Größe achtunddreißig. Yes, I'm looking for a warm jacket, size 38.
VERKÄUFERIN Die blaue Jacke hier ist sehr beliebt. Was kostet sie? Neunzig Euro. This blue jacket here is very popular. What does it cost? Ninety euros.
MIRA Das ist ein bisschen teuer, aber sie gefällt mir. Ich nehme sie! That's a bit expensive, but I like it. I'll take it!

Verständnisfragen — Comprehension questions:

  1. Wo ist der Supermarkt in Miras Viertel? (Where is the supermarket in Mira's neighborhood?)
  2. Was macht Mira normalerweise am Samstagmorgen? (What does Mira normally do on Saturday morning?)
  3. Was bestellen Mira und Sofia im Café? (What do Mira and Sofia order at the café?)
  4. Welche Jacke kauft Mira, und was kostet sie? (Which jacket does Mira buy, and what does it cost?)
  5. Wo wechseln die Sprecher von du zu Sie? Warum? (Where do the speakers switch from du to Sie? Why?)

Writing Workshop

In this workshop, you will plan and write a connected paragraph describing a complete practical day that combines home, routine, food, and shopping — the integrated skill this chapter assesses.

  1. Analyze the model. Reread Mira's blog post. Identify: (a) the order in which she presents waking up, getting ready, breakfast, shopping, and evening, (b) every separable verb she uses, and (c) at least one sentence that does not begin with the subject.
  2. Plan your day. In notes, list: where you live or stay, what time you get up, what you do to get ready, where and what you eat, whether you go shopping and for what, and how the day ends.
  3. Write your first draft. Write six to eight connected sentences. Include at least one es gibt sentence, one separable verb in a simple main clause, one sentence beginning with a time expression, one food or café sentence with möchten or hätte gern, and one shopping sentence with an attributive adjective ending.
  4. Revise. Check your draft against the editing checklist before writing a final version.

Checkliste — Editing checklist:

  • Every finite verb is in second position, including after a fronted time or place expression.
  • Separable prefixes appear at the end of the clause in simple main clauses.
  • Reflexive pronouns match the subject (ich ... mich, du ... dich, and so on).
  • nicht and kein are used correctly and not interchanged.
  • Attributive adjectives carry the correct ending after a definite or ein-word determiner.
  • Every noun is capitalized, with correct gender and plural where relevant.
  • du and Sie are used consistently and appropriately for each relationship.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — The Saturday routine

Many people across German-speaking communities treat Saturday as a day for unhurried errands, a relaxed breakfast or brunch, and household shopping, though this pattern varies widely by household, occupation, religion, and region. Some households shop primarily on Saturday because many smaller shops in Germany, Austria, and Switzerland traditionally have limited or no Sunday hours; this varies by country, region, and type of business, and current rules should be verified rather than assumed.

Regional Variation — Bread and bakery culture

The bakery (die Bäckerei) plays a prominent role in daily life in many German-speaking communities, with a wide regional variety of bread and roll types — for example, das Brötchen in much of Germany, die Semmel in parts of Austria and Bavaria, and das Weggli in Switzerland. This course uses das Brötchen as the default productive term and labels the others as recognized regional alternatives.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Leaving the separable prefix attached

Error: *Ich aufstehe um sieben Uhr.

Correction: Ich stehe um sieben Uhr auf. In a simple main clause, the separable prefix detaches and moves to the end of the clause; only the stem stays in second position.

Common Mistake — Confusing nicht and kein

Error: *Ich möchte nicht Suppe.

Correction: Ich möchte keine Suppe. Use kein, not nicht, to negate an indefinite noun phrase such as eine Suppe.

Common Mistake — Forgetting the adjective ending

Error: *Ich kaufe die blau Jacke.

Correction: Ich kaufe die blaue Jacke. An attributive adjective directly before a noun requires an ending; only a predicate adjective after sein stays uninflected (Die Jacke ist blau).

Unit II Assessment: Ein praktischer Tag

Integrierte Leistungsaufgabe

Plan and complete a practical day

This is the Unit II assessment. It evaluates your cumulative control of Chapters 6 through 9 across all four skills, using only language already taught in this unit and earlier units.

Listening: Listen to an instructor-read or partner-read account of someone's practical day (use the Listening passage above or a new one provided by your instructor). Complete a comprehension table covering wake-up time, errands, a meal, and a purchase.

Reading: Read a short neighborhood notice and a personal blog post (use the Reading passages above or comparable new texts). Answer comprehension questions about location, sequence, and preference.

Speaking: With a partner, complete an integrated role-play: welcome a guest to your home and neighborhood, order food or drink at a café, and complete a short shopping exchange, switching appropriately between du and Sie.

Writing: Write a connected paragraph of six to eight sentences describing a complete practical day, meeting the Writing Workshop checklist above.

Bewertungskriterien — Assessment criteria

  • Task completion: All four skill components are attempted and address the assigned content.
  • Comprehensibility: A sympathetic reader or listener can follow the message without difficulty.
  • Grammatical control: V2 order, separable and reflexive verbs, nicht/kein, and supported adjective endings are emerging reliably, even if not error-free.
  • Vocabulary: Active vocabulary from Chapters 6–9 is used naturally and appropriately.
  • Register: du and Sie are used consistently with the correct relationship.
  • Orthography: Noun capitalization, umlauts, and ß/ss follow the core standard.

Advancement Standard

Learners sustain short practical exchanges and produce connected descriptions. Active nominative and accusative forms, supported location dative, sentence brackets, and basic adjective endings should be sufficiently stable to support the expanded dative and two-way preposition work that begins in Chapter 11. Errors that do not block meaning may remain; systematic loss of V2 order, missing noun capitalization, or consistent confusion of nicht and kein should be addressed before advancing.

This integrated control prepares you for the expanded dative system, two-way prepositions, and modal verbs that begin in Unit III.

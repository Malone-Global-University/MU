17

السَّفَر وَالرِّحْلَاتTravel Experiences

Meeting hollow weak verbs, sequencing a story, and marking where a trip started and ended · Speak and Write Arabic: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Recognize that some Arabic verb roots contain و or ي as a middle radical and behave differently from the sound verbs you have used since Chapter 14.
  2. Conjugate the perfect tense of the hollow verb زَارَ (يَزُور) "to visit" for singular and plural subjects.
  3. Use the hollow verbs كَانَ, زَارَ, and سَافَرَ's weak counterpart pattern to talk about a trip.
  4. Sequence a narrative using أَوَّلًا, ثُمَّ, بَعْدَ ذَلِك, and أَخِيرًا.
  5. Mark where a trip began and ended using مِن and إِلَى with place names.
  6. Negate a hollow verb in the past using لَمْ plus the jussive, recycled from Chapter 16.
  7. Read a short travel account that combines sequence connectors, source/destination phrases, and hollow verbs.
  8. Write a connected travel narrative of five to seven sentences describing a trip from start to finish.
  9. Recycle plural perfect-tense narration, negative commands, and group vocabulary from Chapter 16 inside a new travel context.

Communication Scenario

Layla has just returned from a short trip and is telling Tom, Nadia, and Karim about it over coffee. To tell the story clearly, she needs to say where she went, what she did first, next, and last, and what did not happen along the way. This chapter continues Unit IV, التَّجَارِب وَالقِصَص (Experiences and Stories), moving from the group narration of Chapter 16 into a single connected travel story.

  1. You already know how to say what happened using the perfect tense. This chapter adds a small group of very common verbs — hollow verbs — whose perfect and imperfect stems look a little different from the sound verbs you have used so far.
  2. You already know how to state single facts about the past. This chapter adds the connecting words that turn separate facts into one organized story, and the tools for saying exactly where a journey started and where it ended.

التَّرْكِيز النَّحْوِيّ — Grammar Focus

Hollow Weak Verbs

Every verb you have used so far — وَصَلَ, رَجَعَ, تَأَخَّرَ, ذَهَبَ — has three strong consonants and is called a "sound" verb. A small but very common group of Arabic verbs has و or ي as the middle radical. These are called hollow verbs, and their vowel pattern shifts in a regular way.

Building Block — Perfect tense of زَارَ (root ز-و-ر, "to visit")
Sound-verb pattern for comparison: كَتَبَ → كَتَبْتُ
SubjectSound verb (Ch. 14–16)Hollow verb زَارَ
أَنَا (I)وَصَلْتُزُرْتُ
أَنْتَ / أَنْتِ (you m./f.)وَصَلْتَ / وَصَلْتِزُرْتَ / زُرْتِ
هُوَ / هِيَ (he / she)وَصَلَ / وَصَلَتْزَارَ / زَارَتْ
نَحْنُ (we)وَصَلْنَازُرْنَا
هُم (they, m.)وَصَلُوازَارُوا

Notice the pattern: when the ending begins with a consonant (as in -تُ or -نَا), the middle radical shortens to ُ. When the ending begins with nothing or with a vowel-friendly suffix (as with هُوَ, هِيَ, or هُم), the long middle vowel ا stays. This same pattern governs other common hollow verbs such as كَانَ, which you will study closely in Chapter 18.

  • زُرْتُ مَدِينَة القَاهِرَة الأُسْبُوع المَاضِي.I visited the city of Cairo last week.hollow verb, 1st sg. perfect, new this chapter
  • زَارَت لَيْلَى أُخْتَهَا فِي عَمَّان.Layla visited her sister in Amman.hollow verb, 3rd sg. fem. perfect
  • زُرْنَا مَتْحَفًا جَمِيلًا فِي أَوَّل يَوم.We visited a beautiful museum on the first day.hollow verb, 1st pl. perfect, recycled from Ch. 16
Grammar Note — لَمْ with hollow verbs

You can negate a hollow verb in the past the same way you negated sound verbs in Chapter 16: with لَمْ plus the jussive imperfect. لَمْ أَزُر المَتْحَف means "I did not visit the museum." The jussive of a hollow verb typically shortens the long vowel, which you will see modeled in this chapter's reading and dialogue; full jussive formation for hollow and other weak verbs is developed further across Unit IV.

Sequencing a Story

A list of separate perfect-tense sentences is not yet a story. To narrate a trip clearly, connect the events in order using sequence words.

بَعْدَ ذَلِكafter thatbegins a following sentence or clause

  • ثُمَّ نَزَلْنَا فِي فُنْدُق قَرِيب مِن المَطَار.Then we stayed at a hotel near the airport.sequence connector, sound verb نَزَلَ
  • بَعْدَ ذَلِك، زُرْنَا المَدِينَة القَدِيمَة.After that, we visited the old city.sequence phrase + hollow verb
  • أَخِيرًا، رَجَعْنَا إِلَى البَيْت مُتْعَبِين لَكِن سُعَدَاء.Finally, we returned home tired but happy.sequence connector + recycled perfect verb, Ch. 14

Source and Destination

You met مِن ("from") in Chapter 3 for talking about origin, and إِلَى ("to") in Chapter 11 for talking about direction. Travel narration regularly uses them together to frame a journey from its starting point to its ending point.

Building Block — مِن ... إِلَى with place names
مِن + starting place ... إِلَى + ending place
سَافَرْنَا مِن نِيُويُورك إِلَى القَاهِرَة.
We traveled from New York to Cairo.
  • هَل سَافَرْتُم مِن هُنَا إِلَى دُبَي مُبَاشَرَة؟Did you all travel from here to Dubai directly?مِن ... إِلَى with plural perfect verb, recycled Ch. 16
  • رَكِبْنَا القِطَار مِن المَحَطَّة الأُولَى إِلَى المَحَطَّة الأَخِيرَة.We rode the train from the first station to the last station.مِن ... إِلَى with a defined route
Grammar Note — Two frames working together

مِن ... إِلَى sets the outer frame of a trip; the sequence connectors from the previous section fill in what happened inside that frame. A well-formed travel narrative in this chapter typically states the route once with مِن ... إِلَى and then narrates the events with أَوَّلًا, ثُمَّ, بَعْدَ ذَلِك, and أَخِيرًا.

تَدْرِيب — Practice: Complete the sentence with a route of your choice: سَافَرْتُ مِن ______ إِلَى ______.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Layla tells Tom, Nadia, and Karim about her recent trip.
السِّيَاق: تُخْبِر لَيْلَى تُوم وَنَادِيَة وَكَرِيم عَن رِحْلَتِهَا الأَخِيرَة.

TOM كَيْف كَانَت رِحْلَتُك يَا لَيْلَى؟ إِلَى أَيْن سَافَرْتِ؟ How was your trip, Layla? Where did you travel to?
LAYLA سَافَرْتُ مِن هُنَا إِلَى عَمَّان لِزِيَارَة عَائِلَتِي. أَوَّلًا، رَكِبْتُ الطَّائِرَة صَبَاحًا. I traveled from here to Amman to visit my family. First, I boarded the plane in the morning.
NADIA وَمَاذَا فَعَلْتِ بَعْد ذَلِك؟ And what did you do after that?
LAYLA ثُمَّ نَزَلْتُ فِي فُنْدُق قَرِيب مِن بَيْت أُخْتِي، وَزُرْتُ جَدَّتِي فِي اليَوم التَّالِي. Then I stayed at a hotel near my sister's house, and I visited my grandmother the next day.
KARIM هَل زُرْتِ البَتْرَاء أَيْضًا؟ Did you visit Petra too?
LAYLA لَا، لَمْ أَزُر البَتْرَاء هَذِهِ المَرَّة، لِأَنَّ الوَقْت لَمْ يَكْفِ. أَخِيرًا، رَجَعْتُ إِلَى هُنَا أَمْس مَسَاءً. No, I did not visit Petra this time, because there wasn't enough time. Finally, I returned here yesterday evening.
TOM فِي المَرَّة القَادِمَة، لا تَنْسَي زِيَارَة البَتْرَاء! Next time, don't forget to visit Petra!

أَسْئِلَة الفَهْم — Comprehension questions:

  1. Identify the route Layla describes using مِن ... إِلَى.
  2. List the four events of Layla's trip in the order the sequence words introduce them.
  3. What did Layla not do, and why? Find the لَمْ + jussive phrase.

Writing Workshop

In this chapter's writing task, you will write a connected narrative describing a trip from start to finish.

  1. Choose your trip. Decide on a real or imagined trip with a clear starting point and ending point.
  2. State the route. Write one sentence using مِن ... إِلَى.
  3. Narrate the events in order. Write four sentences, one for each sequence connector: أَوَّلًا, ثُمَّ, بَعْدَ ذَلِك, and أَخِيرًا.
  4. Include at least one hollow verb. Use زَارَ at least once in your narrative.
  5. Add one thing that did not happen. Write one sentence using لَمْ plus the jussive.
  6. Draft the paragraph. Write five to seven connected sentences in total.
  7. Revise. Check your draft against the editing checklist below.

قَائِمَة المُرَاجَعَة — Editing checklist:

  • I stated the route with مِن ... إِلَى.
  • I used all four sequence connectors in a logical order.
  • I used the hollow verb زَارَ at least once with a correct ending.
  • I used at least one example of لَمْ plus the jussive.
  • My paragraph is connected, not a list of unrelated sentences.
  • I wrote all letters and endings with correct right-to-left joining.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Sharing travel stories

Recounting a trip in careful sequence — first this, then that, finally this — is a common storytelling pattern across many Arabic-speaking communities, much as it is in many parts of the world. Travelers commonly mention visiting family, as Layla does in the dialogue, since travel and family visits are often closely connected across the Arabic-speaking world, from the Levant to the Gulf to North Africa and beyond.

Regional Variation — Talking about transportation

The words طَائِرَة and قِطَار are standard MSA and are broadly understood, but everyday regional speech sometimes uses different or additional terms for transportation and travel arrangements. Treat any such comparison your instructor introduces as receptive and comparative information at this stage, not a substitute for the MSA vocabulary practiced here.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Treating زَارَ like a sound verb

Error: Saying *زَارْتُ, keeping the long vowel before a consonant-initial ending.

Correction: Before an ending that begins with a consonant, the middle radical shortens: زُرْتُ, not *زَارْتُ.

Common Mistake — Stacking sequence words without connecting the story

Error: Writing four separate, disconnected sentences that each happen to start with a sequence word but do not build on each other.

Correction: Each sequence word should introduce the next stage of the same trip, so that the four sentences read as one story, as in the model dialogue and reading passage above.

Common Mistake — Reversing مِن and إِلَى

Error: Saying *سَافَرْنَا إِلَى عَمَّان مِن هُنَا when the intended meaning still puts the starting point first in English word order but confuses which preposition marks which place.

Correction: مِن always marks the starting point and إِلَى always marks the destination: سَافَرْنَا مِن هُنَا إِلَى عَمَّان.

سَافَرْتُ مِن هُنَا إِلَى بَيْرُوت. أَوَّلًا، رَكِبْتُ الطَّائِرَة. ثُمَّ نَزَلْتُ فِي فُنْدُق قَرِيب مِن البَحْر. بَعْد ذَلِك، زُرْتُ أَقَارِبِي. لَمْ أَزُر كُلّ الأَمَاكِن الَّتِي أَرَدْتُ. أَخِيرًا، رَجَعْتُ إِلَى هُنَا سَعِيدًا. Other well-formed, accurate answers are equally acceptable.

Cumulative Review — Model responses for Speaker B

ب: سَافَرْنَا مِن هُنَا إِلَى القَاهِرَة.
ب: أَوَّلًا زُرْنَا الأَهْرَامَات، ثُمَّ زُرْنَا مَتْحَفًا كَبِيرًا.
ب: لَمْ نَزُر كُلّ الأَمَاكِن، لَكِنَّنَا اسْتَمْتَعْنَا كَثِيرًا.

Exact wording will vary. Check that the route, sequence connectors, hollow-verb forms, and لَمْ + jussive are used correctly and consistently.

Looking Ahead

مَا الَّذِي تَسْتَطِيعُ فِعْلَهُ الآن — What you can do now

You can narrate a trip from start to finish, using the hollow verb زَارَ, sequencing events with أَوَّلًا, ثُمَّ, بَعْدَ ذَلِك, and أَخِيرًا, and framing the journey with مِن ... إِلَى. You have extended your narration skills from a single completed action to a connected story with a clear beginning, middle, and end.

Familie und Freunde

Family and Friends · Speak and Write German: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify family members, pets, and important people in your life with the correct article and plural.
  2. Conjugate and use the complete present tense of haben (to have).
  3. Recognize and use accusative articles (den, die, das, einen, eine, ein) for direct objects.
  4. Use possessive determiners (mein, dein, sein, ihr, unser, euer, formal Ihr) to show who something or someone belongs to.
  5. Ask and state someone's age using Wie alt bist du? and Ich bin … Jahre alt.
  6. Describe people using predicate adjectives for personality and appearance.
  7. Distinguish wer, wen, and was in questions about people and things.
  8. Talk about months and recognize basic date formulas for birthdays.

Communication Scenario

Two weeks into the course, Frau Bauer asks the participants to bring a photo — real or printed from a phone — and introduce someone important in their life to the rest of the class. Lukas brings a picture of his sister and his dog; Mira brings a photo from a family gathering. As the class moves from desks and pens to people, the conversation naturally needs new tools: a way to say what you have (a brother, a cat, a busy week), a way to mark who is doing something to whom, and a way to say "my" versus "your" versus "her." This chapter builds exactly those tools.

  1. Think of three people or animals that are important in your life. In English, jot down one fact about each (age, personality, or appearance). You will return to these notes later in the chapter.
  2. In Chapters 2 and 3, every noun you used as a subject was in the nominative case. This chapter introduces the accusative case for nouns that receive the action of a verb — for example, the "brother" in "I have a brother." Watch for how the article changes only for masculine nouns.

Pronunciation and Spelling

Umlaut vowels in family words

This chapter's vocabulary brings several umlauted vowels together for the first time in plural forms: Väter, Mütter, Brüder, Töchter. The umlaut changes the vowel quality — it is not a decorative mark, and it is not optional in spelling or pronunciation.

Sound Focus — ä, ö, ü in plural family nouns

ä in Väter is pronounced similarly to the e in English bed, but held for the appropriate vowel length. ü in Brüder and Mütter is a rounded front vowel: round your lips as if to say English "oo," but move your tongue forward as if to say "ee." It is not the same sound as either English vowel alone. ö in Töchter follows the same principle with lips rounded for "oh" and tongue forward for "eh."

Practice the contrast between the singular and the umlauted plural: der Vater → die Väter; die Mutter → die Mütter; der Bruder → die Brüder; die Tochter → die Töchter. Say each pair aloud, exaggerating the vowel change at first.

Long and short vowels: Vater versus Vetter-type contrasts

German distinguishes many words by vowel length alone, not only by quality. In Vater, the stressed a is long: [aː]. Practice keeping this vowel noticeably longer than the short a you hear in a word like Mann. Vowel length is meaningful in German — shortening a long vowel can make a word harder to understand, even when every letter is spelled correctly.

Final devoicing review

Several family nouns end in a written d that is pronounced as a voiceless [t] in final position: Bruder is fine here because the d is not word-final, but related words you will meet later, such as Hund in this chapter's vocabulary, show the pattern clearly: the final d in Hund is pronounced [t]. Continue to spell the word with d regardless of how it sounds at the end.

Sentence stress for contrastive possession

When comparing who owns or has what, German places extra stress on the possessive word or pronoun being contrasted, just as English does.

Sound Focus — Stressing the possessive in contrast

Compare: Das ist mein Bruder, nicht dein Bruder. ("That's my brother, not your brother.") Stress falls on mein and dein, the words being contrasted, while Bruder is said more lightly the second time because it has already been mentioned. Practice this pattern with your own family vocabulary, contrasting mein with dein, and later sein with ihr.

Grammar Instruction

4.1 The verb haben — complete present tense

Purpose: state what someone has — family members, pets, possessions, or qualities expressed with "to have."

Like sein, the verb haben (to have) is highly frequent and shows some irregularity, especially in the du and er/sie/es forms, where the b drops out.

PronomenFormEnglish
ichhabeI have
duhastyou have (informal)
er / sie / eshathe / she / it has
wirhabenwe have
ihrhabtyou have (informal plural)
siehabenthey have
Siehabenyou have (formal)

Notice that du hast and er/sie/es hat each drop the b from the infinitive haben. This is an irregularity specific to this verb and must simply be learned; it does not follow the regular pattern you will study with verbs like kommen and wohnen.

  • Ich habe einen Bruder. I have a brother.
  • Hast du Geschwister? Do you have siblings?
  • Er hat eine Katze. He has a cat.
  • Wir haben einen Hund. We have a dog.

4.2 The accusative case: direct objects

Purpose: mark the noun that receives the action of a verb such as haben.

Every sentence you have built so far has used the nominative case for its subject — the person or thing doing the being or having. Starting in this chapter, you will also mark the accusative case for the noun that receives the action: the direct object. In Ich habe einen Bruder, ich is the nominative subject and einen Bruder is the accusative direct object.

The accusative changes the article only for masculine nouns. Feminine, neuter, and plural articles look identical in the nominative and accusative.

GenderNominativeAccusativeBeispiel
Maskulinder / ein Bruderden / einen BruderIch habe einen Bruder.
Feminindie / eine Schwesterdie / eine SchwesterIch habe eine Schwester.
Neutrumdas / ein Kinddas / ein KindIch habe ein Kind.
Pluraldie Geschwisterdie GeschwisterIch habe Geschwister.

Only the masculine column changes: der becomes den, and ein becomes einen. This single change — adding -en to the masculine article — is the most important pattern to memorize in this chapter, because it recurs throughout the rest of the course.

Grammar Note — Case is not about English word order

It can be tempting to think "the first noun is nominative, the second noun is accusative." That happens to be true in a simple sentence like Ich habe einen Bruder, but case is determined by the noun's grammatical role — subject or direct object — not by its position in the English sentence. Later in the course you will see accusative nouns appear in other positions, so it is important to learn the role-based reasoning now rather than a position-based shortcut.

  • Ich habe einen Hund. I have a dog. (masculine accusative)
  • Du hast eine Katze. You have a cat. (feminine, unchanged)
  • Wir haben ein Auto. We have a car. (neuter, unchanged)
  • Hast du den Bleistift? Do you have the pencil? (masculine definite accusative)

When a possessive determiner is itself the object of haben, the masculine form follows the same accusative pattern you just learned: Ich habe meinen Bruder gefragt would add -en to mein before a masculine noun. For this chapter, focus on recognizing this pattern in input (such as Ich sehe meinen Bruder) rather than producing every possible combination — the complete possessive paradigm across all cases is not required yet.

  • Mein Bruder heißt Felix. My brother is named Felix.
  • Wie heißt deine Schwester? What is your sister's name?
  • Ihre Eltern wohnen in München. Her parents live in Munich.
  • Unser Hund ist sehr lustig. Our dog is very funny.
  • Ist das Ihr Sohn? Is that your son? (formal)
Grammar Note — sie, ihr, and Sie

This chapter brings together several look-alike forms: the pronoun sie can mean "she" or "they"; the possessive ihr can mean "her" or "their"; and capitalized Ihr means formal "your." Context and capitalization — not the spelling alone — tell you which meaning is intended. Do not capitalize ordinary sie or ihr when they mean "she/they" or "her/their."

4.4 Wie alt bist du? — Stating age

Purpose: ask and state a person's age.

German expresses age with a form of sein, not haben — this is an important contrast with English "to have [a number of] years."

Language Building Block
Wie alt bist du / ist er / sind Sie? — Ich bin … Jahre alt.
German uses sein + number + Jahre alt, not haben
Wie alt bist du? — Ich bin dreiundzwanzig Jahre alt.
How old are you? — I am twenty-three years old.
  • Wie alt ist dein Bruder? — Er ist siebzehn Jahre alt. How old is your brother? — He is seventeen years old.
  • Wie alt sind Sie? — Ich bin vierzig Jahre alt. How old are you? — I am forty years old.

4.5 Predicate adjectives for personality and appearance

Purpose: describe a person's character or looks without adding adjective endings.

As in Chapter 2, predicate adjectives — those following sein — never take an ending, regardless of the subject's gender or number.

  • Mein Vater ist freundlich. My father is friendly.
  • Meine Schwester ist lustig. My sister is funny.
  • Meine Eltern sind ruhig. My parents are calm. (no plural ending on "ruhig")

4.6 wer, wen, and was

Purpose: ask about a person as a subject (wer), a person as an object (wen), or a thing (was).

You already know wer (who, nominative) and was (what) from Chapter 2. This chapter adds wen, the accusative form used to ask about a person who receives the action of a verb.

FrageCaseBeispiel
wernominative — subjectWer hat einen Hund? — Mira hat einen Hund.
wenaccusative — object (person)Wen siehst du? — Ich sehe meinen Bruder.
wasnominative or accusative — thingWas hast du? — Ich habe ein Fahrrad.

Was does not change form between the nominative and accusative; only question words referring to people show this distinction at this stage.

Model Dialogue

Kontext: Eine Pause im Deutschkurs. Lukas und Mira zeigen sich Fotos von ihren Familien. Sie benutzen weiterhin du miteinander.
Setting: A break during the German course. Lukas and Mira show each other photos of their families. They continue to use du with each other.

MIRA Lukas, hast du Geschwister? Lukas, do you have siblings?
LUKAS Ja, ich habe eine Schwester. Sie heißt Anna. Yes, I have a sister. She is named Anna.
MIRA Wie alt ist deine Schwester? How old is your sister?
LUKAS Sie ist neunzehn Jahre alt. Hier, das ist ein Foto. Und das hier ist mein Hund. She is nineteen years old. Here, this is a photo. And this here is my dog.
MIRA Oh, wie süß! Wie heißt dein Hund? Oh, how cute! What is your dog's name?
LUKAS Er heißt Bruno. Er ist drei Jahre alt und sehr lustig. Hast du auch ein Haustier? He is named Bruno. He is three years old and very funny. Do you also have a pet?
MIRA Nein, ich habe keinen Hund und keine Katze. Aber ich habe eine große Familie — drei Brüder und zwei Schwestern! No, I don't have a dog or a cat. But I have a big family — three brothers and two sisters!
LUKAS Wow, das ist viel! Sind deine Geschwister auch hier in der Stadt? Wow, that's a lot! Are your siblings also here in the city?
MIRA Meine Eltern und mein jüngster Bruder wohnen hier. Meine anderen Geschwister wohnen in Köln. My parents and my youngest brother live here. My other siblings live in Cologne.
LUKAS Und wann hast du Geburtstag? And when is your birthday?
MIRA Mein Geburtstag ist im Mai. Und deiner? My birthday is in May. And yours?
LUKAS Im Oktober. Frau Bauer hat übrigens auch Geburtstag — ich glaube, nächste Woche. In October. By the way, Frau Bauer also has a birthday — I think next week.

Verständnisfragen — Comprehension questions:

  1. Wie viele Geschwister hat Lukas? Wie heißt seine Schwester? (How many siblings does Lukas have? What is his sister's name?)
  2. Wie heißt der Hund von Lukas, und wie alt ist er? (What is Lukas's dog's name, and how old is it?)
  3. Wie viele Geschwister hat Mira? (How many siblings does Mira have?)
  4. Wo wohnen Miras Eltern? Wo wohnen ihre anderen Geschwister? (Where do Mira's parents live? Where do her other siblings live?)
  5. Wann haben Mira und Lukas Geburtstag? (When are Mira's and Lukas's birthdays?)
Grammar Note — keinen and keine

Mira says ich habe keinen Hund und keine Katze ("I don't have a dog or a cat"). Kein is the negative counterpart of ein and follows the exact same gender pattern: keinen before a masculine accusative noun, keine before a feminine noun. You will study kein formally in Chapter 8 — for now, simply recognize it in this dialogue as a recognition item.

Reading Note — Varied family structures

The families in this chapter's readings are only examples. Families across the German-speaking world take many forms — large and small, with one parent or two, with step-relatives, chosen family, or shared custody. The vocabulary in this chapter (Eltern, Geschwister, Familie) is flexible enough to describe many different real-life situations, and no single family structure should be treated as the standard one.

Writing Workshop

In this chapter, you will write a short paragraph introducing important people (and, if relevant, a pet) in your life, similar to the texts you just read.

  1. Analyze a model. Reread "Meine Familie und mein Haustier." Identify: (a) how Jonas introduces each person, (b) where he uses haben versus sein, (c) how he uses possessive determiners, and (d) how ages and occupations are stated.

  4. Revise. Check your draft against the editing checklist below before writing a final version.

Checkliste — Editing checklist:

  • Every family or person noun has the article and capitalization I intended.
  • I used einen (not ein) before any masculine noun after haben.
  • My possessive determiners agree with the gender of the noun that follows them.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Talking about family respectfully

Questions about family are common in everyday German conversation, but, as in many cultures, the right amount of detail depends on the relationship and the setting. Among new acquaintances in a language class, simple questions such as Hast du Geschwister? are typical and welcome. More personal questions — about marital status, income, or family conflict — are generally reserved for closer relationships. There is no single rule for every situation; reading the context and the other person's responses matters as much as the vocabulary itself.

Culture in Context — Birthdays in German-speaking countries

In Germany, Austria, and German-speaking Switzerland, it is common for the person celebrating a birthday — rather than their friends — to host or bring treats to share with colleagues or classmates. Congratulating someone before their actual birthday is sometimes considered to bring bad luck in parts of the German-speaking world, so well-wishes are typically offered on the day itself or afterward. Practices vary by region, generation, and individual family, so treat this as a common pattern rather than a universal rule.

Regional Variation — Oma, Opa, and family terms

Oma and Opa are the most common informal terms for grandmother and grandfather throughout the German-speaking world and are entirely appropriate for this course. Some regions and families also use other affectionate terms, such as Omi, Opi, or dialectal forms specific to a region or even a single family. These are labeled, optional variants; Oma and Opa remain the productive default for this course.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using haben for age

Error: *Ich habe zwanzig Jahre alt. (modeled on English "I have 20 years")

Correction: Ich bin zwanzig Jahre alt. German expresses age with a form of sein, never haben. This is a direct and predictable contrast with English, so it is worth practicing deliberately rather than translating word for word.

Common Mistake — Forgetting the accusative -en on masculine articles

Error: *Ich habe ein Bruder. / *Ich habe der Hund.

Correction: Ich habe einen Bruder. / Ich habe den Hund. After haben, a masculine direct object takes einen or den, not ein or der. Feminine and neuter nouns do not change, which makes it easy to forget that masculine nouns do.

Common Mistake — Possessive determiner not agreeing with the noun

Error: *Mein Schwester ist nett. / *Ihr Bruder heißt … (intending "their brother" but writing "her brother" without context)

Correction: Meine Schwester ist nett. The possessive determiner agrees with the noun that follows it, not with the speaker: mein becomes meine before a feminine noun like Schwester. When using ihr, make sure context makes clear whether you mean "her" or "their."

Common Mistake — Confusing wer and wen

Error: *Wer siehst du? (intending "Whom do you see?")

Correction: Wen siehst du? Use wer only when the person is the subject of the sentence, and wen when the person is the direct object. Asking who is doing something uses wer; asking who is having something done to them uses wen.

Chapter Performance Task

Leistungsaufgabe

Mein Familienstammbaum — My family tree

Create a labeled family tree or relationship map — real or invented — showing at least five people (and a pet, if you like). Label each person with their relationship to you, name, age, and one personality or appearance adjective.

Then present your family tree to a partner or the class, using complete sentences:

  • Introduce at least four people: Das ist mein/meine …
  • State each person's age: Er/Sie ist … Jahre alt.
  • Describe at least two people with a predicate adjective.
  • State at least one possession using haben: Wir haben einen/eine/ein …

After each presentation, the audience may ask one follow-up question using Wer …?, Wie alt …?, or Hat … einen/eine/ein …?

Looking Ahead

Was Sie jetzt können — What you can do now

You can now describe family, friends, and pets using the complete present tense of haben, the accusative case for direct objects, and possessive determiners. You can state and ask about age correctly with sein, and you can describe personality and appearance with predicate adjectives. The nominative-accusative contrast you learned here will expand steadily through the rest of the course.

Kapitel 5: Rückblick 1 — Wir lernen uns kennen

Chapter 5 reviews and integrates everything from Chapters 1 through 4 — greetings, identity, classroom language, origin, and family — without introducing new grammar. You will complete an integrated introductory interaction that draws on sein, haben, nominative and accusative articles, and possessive determiners together.

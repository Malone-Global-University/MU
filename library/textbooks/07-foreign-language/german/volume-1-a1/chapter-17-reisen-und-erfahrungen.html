Reisen und Erfahrungen

Travel and Experiences · Speak and Write German: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

Chapter 16 gave you the core architecture of German past narration: haben as a Perfekt auxiliary, the sentence bracket, and a range of past participles. This chapter extends that architecture with a second auxiliary verb, sein, which is used for verbs of motion and change of state. Since travel accounts almost always involve moving from place to place, sein-verbs are essential for talking about trips. You will also learn how to name travel destinations and express how long something lasted.

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Use the Perfekt with sein for verbs of motion and change of state.
  2. Identify which verbs take sein and which take haben in the Perfekt.
  3. Form past participles of key motion verbs: gegangen, gefahren, geflogen, gekommen, geblieben, gereist, and others.
  4. Form past participles of separable motion verbs: angekommen, abgefahren, eingestiegen, ausgestiegen, umgestiegen, zurückgefahren.
  5. Maintain the sentence bracket with sein: sein in second position, Partizip II at the clause end.
  6. Ask and answer questions about travel using sein-Perfekt: Wohin bist du gefahren? Wie lange bist du geblieben?
  7. Use nach and in die/den/das to name travel destinations.
  8. Use für for the duration of a completed trip and seit in controlled ongoing contexts.
  9. Narrate a travel experience combining both haben and sein in the Perfekt.

Opening Scenario

A few weeks into the semester at the University of Düsseldorf, there has been a long weekend. Mia Hoffmann and Jonas Weber meet in the university library on Tuesday morning. Jonas has just returned from a short trip to Hamburg to visit an old school friend. Mia spent three days in Vienna visiting her grandparents. Neither of them has been very far, but each has a travel story to tell — and the language for telling it now involves a new Perfekt auxiliary: sein.

  1. Think about a journey you have made recently — or the most memorable trip you can remember. How did you travel? Where did you go? How long did you stay? You will use this material in the Speaking Practice and the Writing Workshop at the end of the chapter.
  2. Notice how travel language naturally combines two types of verbs: motion verbs (I flew, I arrived, I walked) and activity verbs (I visited, I saw, I ate). In German, the motion verbs use sein as their auxiliary and the activity verbs use haben. This chapter will show you how to use both together in a single travel account.

Grammatik 17.1

The Perfekt with sein — Structure and Function

Why a second auxiliary?

In Chapter 16, every Perfekt sentence used haben as its auxiliary. Most German verbs do use haben. However, a specific and important group of verbs — verbs that describe movement from one place to another, or a change from one state to another — use sein instead. Since travel naturally involves moving from place to place, sein-verbs appear constantly in travel accounts.

The bracket architecture is identical. The only difference is which auxiliary verb fills the second position in the clause.

Das Perfekt mit sein — Sentence Bracket
[ Subject ] sein (conjugated) + rest of clause + Partizip II
sein takes second position. The Partizip II still moves to the end. The bracket rule has not changed.
Mia ist nach Wien geflogen.
Mia flew to Vienna.
Jonas ist mit dem Zug nach Hamburg gefahren.
Jonas travelled to Hamburg by train.
Wann bist du in Wien angekommen?
When did you arrive in Vienna?

sein as a Perfekt auxiliary — Quick Reference

You already know sein thoroughly from Chapter 1 onward, where you used it for identity and descriptions. The forms are exactly the same when sein serves as a Perfekt auxiliary.

PronounFormExample with Perfekt
ichbinIch bin nach Wien geflogen.

wirsindWir sind durch die Stadt gegangen.

Bracket Positions — sein Auxiliary
Mia ist letztes Wochenende nach Wien geflogen · Mia flew to Vienna last weekend.
Jonas und sein Freund sind am Samstag durch die Speicherstadt gegangen · Jonas and his friend walked through the Speicherstadt on Saturday.
Wir sind um 22 Uhr im Hotel angekommen · We arrived at the hotel at 10 pm.

Verb-second order still applies: if a time expression or location comes first, sein immediately follows — before the subject. Am Freitag ist Mia nach Wien geflogen.

Questions with sein in the Perfekt

The question rules from Chapter 16 apply here without change. Sein moves to first position for yes/no questions, and sits immediately after the question word for question-word questions:

  • Bist du nach Wien geflogen? — Did you fly to Vienna? (yes/no)
  • Wohin bist du geflogen? — Where did you fly to?
  • Wie lange bist du in Wien geblieben? — How long did you stay in Vienna?
  • Wann seid ihr angekommen? — When did you (plural) arrive?
  • Wie sind Sie nach Hamburg gefahren? — How did you travel to Hamburg? (formal)
Grammatik 17.2

Motion Verb Participles

Most sein-verbs are irregular (strong verbs), meaning their participles involve a vowel change and the ending -en. Two common travel verbs — reisen and wandern — are regular (weak), adding ge- and -t in the usual way. Learn each participle as part of the verb's entry.

InfinitivePartizip IITypeEnglish
gehengegangenirregularwent, gone
fahrengefahrenirregulartravelled, driven
fliegengeflogenirregularflew, flown
kommengekommenirregularcame, come
steigengestiegenirregularclimbed, boarded
laufengelaufenirregularran, walked
bleibengebliebenirregularstayed, remained
reisengereistregulartravelled
wanderngewandertregularhiked
seingewesenirregularbeen

bleiben (to stay/remain) does not describe movement between places — it describes remaining in place, or remaining in a state. German treats it as a sein-verb because it shows a kind of result or continuation. Ich bin in Wien geblieben (I stayed in Vienna). sein → gewesen is especially useful for expressing experience: Ich bin schon mal in Salzburg gewesen (I have been to Salzburg before).

schon mal and noch nie — Experience expressions

Two very common adverbial expressions combine naturally with sein → gewesen and other sein-verbs in travel contexts:

  • schon mal — already, before, at some point: Bist du schon mal nach Österreich gefahren? (Have you ever been to Austria?)
  • noch nie — never yet, not once: Ich bin noch nie nach Wien geflogen. (I have never flown to Vienna.)

These expressions sit in the mid-field of the clause — before the Partizip II. Ich bin noch nie nach Japan geflogen.

Grammatik 17.3

Separable Motion Verbs with sein

Formation Rule

Separable motion verbs: prefix + ge- + stem + ending

The rule for separable participles has not changed from Chapter 16. The ge- is inserted between the separable prefix and the verb stem — and the verb now uses sein as its auxiliary rather than haben. The bracket works identically:

  • an + ge + komm + en → angekommen (auxiliary: sein)
  • ab + ge + fahr + en → abgefahren (auxiliary: sein)
  • ein + ge + stieg + en → eingestiegen (auxiliary: sein)

Grammatik 17.4

haben oder sein?

The question of which auxiliary to use is one of the most important decisions in German past narration. The following rule covers the vast majority of cases you will encounter at this level.

The Core Rule

When to use sein in the Perfekt

Use sein when the verb describes:

  • Movement from one place to another (directed motion): gehen, fahren, fliegen, kommen, reisen, laufen, ankommen, abfahren, einsteigen, and similar verbs.
  • A change from one state to another: aufstehen (sitting/lying → standing), aufwachen (sleeping → awake), einschlafen (awake → sleeping), werden (becoming).
  • The special verbs bleiben (to stay/remain) and sein itself (to be → gewesen).

Use haben for all other verbs — including activity verbs, experience verbs, and most transitive verbs (verbs that take a direct object).

A useful question to ask yourself

When you are not sure which auxiliary to use, ask: Did the action involve moving from one place to another, or changing from one state to another? If yes → sein. If no → haben.

Notice how this plays out in a single travel sentence: Ich bin nach Hamburg gefahren und habe dort meine Freunde getroffen. The movement to Hamburg uses sein; meeting friends (an activity with no location change) uses haben.

Grammatik 17.5

Travel Destinations and Duration

Naming destinations: nach and in

German uses two main prepositions for destinations. The choice depends on whether the destination name is used with or without an article.

Destination Prepositions

nach + city or country (no article)

Most cities and most country names in German are used without an article. Use nach for destination with these names:

  • nach Wien, nach Berlin, nach Hamburg, nach München
  • nach Deutschland, nach Österreich, nach Frankreich, nach Japan
  • Rule: if the place name needs no article in normal use, use nach.

in + accusative (countries with an article)

A small number of country names do have a grammatical gender in German and are used with an article. Use in + accusative for destination with these names:

  • in die Schweiz (die Schweiz → accusative: die Schweiz)
  • in die Türkei (die Türkei → accusative: die Türkei)
  • in die USA (die USA, plural → accusative: die USA)
  • in den Irak, in den Iran (masculine countries with article)

At this stage, the most important article-country to know is die Schweiz. You can look up others as they arise.

  • Ich bin nach Wien geflogen. I flew to Vienna. Wien has no article → nach
  • Wir sind nach Österreich gefahren. We travelled to Austria. Österreich has no article → nach
  • Jonas ist nach Hamburg gefahren. Jonas went to Hamburg. Hamburg has no article → nach
  • Mia ist in die Schweiz gereist. Mia travelled to Switzerland. die Schweiz has an article → in die Schweiz

For origin and departure point, use aus + dative for the place you come from (aus Wien, aus Deutschland, aus der Schweiz) and von + dative for the specific point of departure (von Düsseldorf, von Frankfurt).

Expressing duration: für and seit

ExpressionCaseUseExample
für+ accusativeduration of a completed or planned event/tripIch bin für eine Woche nach Wien gefahren.
seit+ dativeongoing situation from past to present (used with present tense)Ich lerne seit einem Jahr Deutsch.
  • Mia ist für drei Tage in Wien geblieben. Mia stayed in Vienna for three days. (trip is completed) für + drei Tage (accusative plural)
  • Jonas ist für zwei Nächte in Hamburg geblieben. Jonas stayed in Hamburg for two nights. für + zwei Nächte (accusative plural)
  • Ich wohne seit einem Monat in Düsseldorf. I have been living in Düsseldorf for a month. (ongoing) seit + einem Monat (dative, masculine)
  • Jonas lernt seit zwei Jahren Gitarre. Jonas has been learning guitar for two years. (ongoing) seit + zwei Jahren (dative plural)
seit and the present tense

A key difference from English: when expressing an ongoing situation that started in the past and continues to the present, German uses seit with the present tense — not the perfect tense. English uses the present perfect with "for" or "since." Compare:

  • English: I have been living here for six months. (present perfect)
  • German: Ich wohne seit sechs Monaten hier. (present tense with seit)

This is a fundamental difference. Do not translate the English present perfect here with German Perfekt. Use the German present tense with seit.

Model Dialogue

Read the dialogue once for meaning, then read it aloud, paying attention to which auxiliary is used and why. Notice how the speakers naturally combine sein-Perfekt for movement with haben-Perfekt for activities — often in the same sentence.

Dienstag, Universitätsbibliothek Düsseldorf. Nach einem langen Wochenende treffen sich Mia und Jonas.

Jonas: Hallo, Mia! Bist du in Düsseldorf geblieben, oder bist du auch irgendwohin gefahren? Hi Mia! Did you stay in Düsseldorf, or did you also travel somewhere?
Mia: Ich bin nach Wien geflogen! Ich habe meine Großeltern besucht. Und du? I flew to Vienna! I visited my grandparents. And you?
Jonas: Ich bin mit dem Zug nach Hamburg gefahren — zu meinem alten Schulfreund Leo. I travelled to Hamburg by train — to visit my old school friend Leo.
Mia: Toll! Wie lange bist du in Hamburg geblieben? Great! How long did you stay in Hamburg?
Jonas: Für zwei Nächte — von Freitag bis Sonntag. Ich bin am Freitagabend angekommen und am Sonntagmittag zurückgefahren. Und wie lange bist du in Wien geblieben? For two nights — from Friday to Sunday. I arrived Friday evening and travelled back Sunday midday. And how long did you stay in Vienna?
Mia: Für drei Tage. Wir sind viel durch die Altstadt gegangen. Ich bin auch auf den Stephansturm gestiegen — mit dem Lift, nicht zu Fuß! For three days. We walked a lot through the old town. I also went up the Stephansturm — by lift, not on foot!
Jonas: Das klingt wunderschön! Was habt ihr noch gemacht? That sounds wonderful! What else did you do?
Mia: Wir haben ein tolles Wiener Schnitzel gegessen — natürlich! Und am letzten Tag haben meine Großeltern und ich einen Ausflug nach Klosterneuburg gemacht. We ate a wonderful Wiener Schnitzel — of course! And on the last day my grandparents and I made a day trip to Klosterneuburg.
Jonas: Ich bin noch nie in Wien gewesen. Bist du schon mal nach Salzburg gefahren? I have never been to Vienna. Have you ever been to Salzburg?
Mia: Ja! Ich bin als Kind mit meinen Eltern nach Salzburg gefahren. Es ist sehr schön — du solltest wirklich mal hinfahren! Und was habt ihr in Hamburg gemacht? Yes! I went to Salzburg with my parents as a child. It's really beautiful — you really should go there! And what did you do in Hamburg?
Jonas: Leo und ich sind am Samstag durch die Speicherstadt gegangen. Wir haben auch Fisch gegessen — Hamburg ist sehr bekannt für Fisch! Am Abend sind wir in ein Konzert gegangen. Es war toll! Leo and I walked through the Speicherstadt on Saturday. We also ate fish — Hamburg is very well known for fish! In the evening we went to a concert. It was great!
Mia: Das klingt perfekt. Ich finde Städtereisen immer interessanter als Badeurlaub. Du nicht? That sounds perfect. I always find city trips more interesting than beach holidays. Don't you?
Jonas: Ich auch! Am liebsten reise ich mit dem Zug — das ist viel entspannter als Fliegen. Me too! I most like travelling by train — that's much more relaxing than flying.

Language note — war and klingt: Es war toll! uses the formulaic simple past of sein, which you have seen before. Das klingt … (That sounds …) uses the ordinary present tense of klingen. Both are available at this stage without requiring full command of the systems they belong to. Note on comparison: interessanter als, entspannter als recycle comparison forms from Chapter 9. The dialogue closes with this review deliberately.

Hören

Listening Practice

Ask your instructor, a learning partner, or a text-to-speech tool to read the following account at a comfortable but natural pace. Listen once for the general picture, then again for the specific details: destination, transport, duration, activities, and sequence. Do not read the script while listening.

Lenas Reise nach Berlin (Listening Script)

Hallo! Ich bin Lena aus Leipzig. Ich erzähle euch von meiner Reise nach Berlin!

Letzte Woche bin ich mit dem Zug nach Berlin gefahren. Ich bin um neun Uhr morgens von Leipzig abgefahren und um halb elf in Berlin angekommen. Ich bin für zwei Nächte geblieben.

Zuerst bin ich in die U-Bahn eingestiegen und zum Hotel gefahren. Ich habe mein Koffer ins Zimmer gebracht und dann bin ich sofort losgegangen.

Am ersten Tag bin ich zum Brandenburger Tor gegangen und habe viele Fotos gemacht. Dann bin ich zum Reichstag gegangen. Ich habe eine Führung gemacht* und viel über deutsche Geschichte gelernt. Das war sehr interessant!

Am zweiten Tag bin ich in das Deutsche Historische Museum gegangen. Ich bin fast drei Stunden dort geblieben! Am Abend bin ich in ein kleines Restaurant gegangen und habe Currywurst gegessen. Sehr lecker!

Danach bin ich mit der S-Bahn zum Bahnhof gefahren. Ich bin erst um zwanzig Uhr in Leipzig angekommen. Ich war müde, aber sehr glücklich!

Berlin ist eine tolle Stadt — ich bin schon dreimal dort gewesen. Ich fahre bald wieder hin!

* eine Führung machen (to do a guided tour) uses haben — an activity, not directed motion. losgegangen (set off, headed out) comes from losgehen, a separable verb using sein — receptive at this stage. gebracht (brought, from bringen) uses haben — irregular but not a motion verb.

Writing Workshop

Write an eight- to ten-sentence account of a real or imagined trip. This is the core productive writing task for Chapter 17 and prepares you directly for the chapter performance task. Your account must combine sein-Perfekt for motion and haben-Perfekt for activities.

  1. Choose your trip. Checkliste — Editing Checklist:

  • Every motion sentence uses sein in second position.
  • Every activity sentence uses haben in second position.
  • The Partizip II is at the very end of every clause — nothing follows it.
  • Separable-prefix participles have ge- correctly inserted between prefix and stem.
  • The destination uses nach (no article) or in die/den/das (country with article).

Pronunciation Focus

Long vowels in motion-verb participles

Several key motion-verb participles contain long vowels that must be distinguished clearly from their shorter counterparts.

  • gegangen /gəˈgaŋən/ — long a: like the a in German Name, not the short a in alt.
  • gefahren /gəˈfaːrən/ — long a followed by r: hold the vowel.
  • geflogen /gəˈfloːgən/ — long o: like the o in groß.
  • geblieben /gəˈbliːbən/ — long ie /iː/: the same long i sound as in lieb, Sie.
  • gestiegen /gəˈʃtiːgən/ — long ie /iː/ again.
  • gereist /gəˈraɪ̯st/ — diphthong ei /aɪ̯/: as in mein, Zeit.

Practise minimal pair contrasts: gesungen (short u) versus gekommen (short o) versus geflogen (long o).

Stress in separable motion-verb participles

In separable-verb participles, the ge- sits unstressed between the stressed prefix and the stressed verb stem. The result is a three-syllable sequence where the prefix carries the main stress:

  • an-ge-kom-men /ˈangəkɔmən/ — stress on an-
  • ab-ge-fahr-en /ˈapgəfaːrən/ — stress on ab-
  • ein-ge-stieg-en /ˈaɪ̯ngəʃtiːgən/ — stress on ein-
  • aus-ge-stieg-en /ˈaʊ̯sgəʃtiːgən/ — stress on aus-
  • um-ge-stieg-en /ˈʊmgəʃtiːgən/ — stress on um-
  • zurück-ge-fahr-en /tsuˈrʏkgəfaːrən/ — stress on -rück-

Note that eingestiegen, ausgestiegen, and umgestiegen all share the same irregular stem from steigen. Contrast the prefixes: ein- (into), aus- (out), um- (transfer/change). The same vowel /aɪ̯/ and final -gestiegen signal the shared root.

seit — vowel and consonant

seit /zaɪ̯t/ contains the same ei diphthong as mein, sein, Zeit, Seite. The initial s is voiced /z/, not the sharp /s/ of English "sight." Contrast:

  • seit /zaɪ̯t/ — the preposition "since / for (ongoing)"
  • Zeit /tsaɪ̯t/ — "time" (begins with the z = /ts/ sound)

The dative forms that follow seit can also require attention: seit einem Jahr (the dative einem has a reduced final syllable: /ˈaɪ̯nəm/), seit drei Monaten (dative plural: /ˈmoːnatən/).

Culture and Communication

Culture and Communication — Reisen im deutschsprachigen Raum

Travel is a central part of life in German-speaking countries, and conversations about trips — past, present, and planned — are a staple of everyday interaction. Germans, Austrians, and Swiss all have high rates of domestic and international travel, and discussing Urlaub (holiday/vacation) or a recent Ausflug (day trip) is a natural conversation topic among friends, colleagues, and acquaintances.

Rail travel holds a special cultural significance in the DACH region. Germany's Deutsche Bahn (DB), Austria's ÖBB, and Switzerland's SBB/CFF/FFS together form one of the world's most extensive rail networks. High-speed trains (ICE in Germany, Railjet in Austria, IC 2000 in Switzerland) connect major cities quickly, and many travellers prefer the train to flying for journeys under five hours. The phrase mit dem Zug fahren is entirely standard — you will hear it constantly in travel conversations.

Day trips (Ausflüge) and weekend trips (Kurzreisen) are extremely popular. From Düsseldorf, for example, people regularly take day trips to Cologne, Bonn, or Aachen. From Vienna, Salzburg, Klosterneuburg, or the Wachau valley are common destinations. From Zurich, the mountains of Graubünden or the shores of Lake Constance (Bodensee) are easily reached in under two hours. These shorter trips are a practical context for all the vocabulary in this chapter.

Practical note: within the EU, citizens of EU and Schengen member states do not need passport checks at internal borders. Germany, Austria, Switzerland, and Luxembourg are all Schengen members. Documentation requirements vary by citizenship and destination — always check current requirements before travelling.

Regional Note — Travel vocabulary across DACH

Several travel-related terms vary across German-speaking countries. In Austria and Switzerland, the word Billet is sometimes used for a train or bus ticket instead of Fahrkarte or Ticket. In Switzerland, you may also hear Bahnhöfli in informal speech for a small station. In Austria, Strecke (route, line) is the everyday term for a train or bus line.

Greetings at transport hubs also vary. At a German ticket counter you might hear Einmal Hamburg, bitte; in Austria, Einmal Wien, bitte works similarly. The word Gleis (track, platform) is used in Germany and Austria; in Switzerland, Perron is common for the platform area. When these variants appear in reading or listening, treat them as labeled receptive material; use the standard German forms for active production.

Common Mistakes: Chapter 17

Common Mistake — Using haben instead of sein with motion verbs

Error: *Ich habe nach Wien geflogen. / *Jonas hat nach Hamburg gefahren.

Correction: Ich bin nach Wien geflogen. / Jonas ist nach Hamburg gefahren. Verbs of directed motion — moving from one place to another — use sein as their Perfekt auxiliary. This includes all the core travel verbs in this chapter: gehen, fahren, fliegen, kommen, reisen, ankommen, abfahren, einsteigen, aussteigen, umsteigen, and others. Saying *habe gefahren or *habe geflogen is the most common auxiliary error for English speakers, who may default to the single English auxiliary "have." Check your motion verbs every time.

Common Mistake — Using sein with activity verbs

Error: *Wir sind zusammen Fisch gegessen. / *Ich bin das Museum besucht.

Correction: Wir haben zusammen Fisch gegessen. / Ich habe das Museum besucht. Activity verbs — essen, trinken, kaufen, besuchen, sehen, machen and the others from Chapter 16 — continue to use haben. The fact that an activity happened during a trip does not change the auxiliary. In a travel account you will constantly alternate between bin gefahren (sein, motion) and habe gegessen (haben, activity). Both appear in the same paragraph without problem.

Common Mistake — Wrong participle for gehen

Error: *Wir sind durch die Altstadt gegehen. / *Ich bin in das Museum gegangen hat.

Correction: Wir sind durch die Altstadt gegangen. The Partizip II of gehen is gegangen — an irregular form with a vowel change from e to a and the strong ending -en. The stem gang- (compare English "gang" in "going") must be learned as part of the verb entry. Also note: the Partizip II comes at the very end of the clause — sein in second position, everything else in the middle, Partizip II last.

Common Mistake — nach instead of in die for countries with articles

Error: *Wir sind nach die Schweiz gefahren. / *Ich bin nach Schweiz gereist.

Correction: Wir sind in die Schweiz gefahren. die Schweiz takes an article in German, which means it uses in + accusative for destination, not nach. The accusative of die Schweiz is die Schweiz (feminine, same form in accusative). The key is to notice the article: nach Deutschland (no article), but in die Schweiz (has article). When in doubt, check whether the country name uses an article in normal speech.

Common Mistake — seit with the Perfekt tense

Error: *Ich bin seit drei Jahren in Düsseldorf gewohnt. / *Ich habe seit zwei Jahren Deutsch gelernt gehabt.

Correction: Ich wohne seit drei Jahren in Düsseldorf. / Ich lerne seit zwei Jahren Deutsch. When seit describes an ongoing situation that started in the past and continues to the present, German uses the present tense — not the Perfekt. This is one of the clearest differences between German and English. English says "I have been living here for three years" (present perfect); German says Ich wohne seit drei Jahren hier (present tense). Never combine sein or haben as a Perfekt auxiliary with seit for ongoing situations.

Common Mistake — ge- in the wrong place in a separable participle

Error: *Ich bin geangekommen. / *Wir sind geabgefahren. / *Jonas ist geeingestiegen.

Correction: Ich bin angekommen. / Wir sind abgefahren. / Jonas ist eingestiegen. The ge- is inserted between the separable prefix and the stem — not before the prefix. The prefix is always the first element: an-ge-kommen, ab-ge-fahren, ein-ge-stiegen. This is the same rule from Chapter 16 (aufgeräumt, eingekauft) — it applies equally whether the auxiliary is haben or sein.

  7. Three haben-Perfekt forms: habe Fotos gemacht (machen), habe Salzburger Nockerl gegessen (essen), habe das Museum besucht (besuchen), habe viel gelernt (lernen), habe Straßenmusik gehört (hören). Accept any three.
  8. Model rewrite: Letztes Wochenende ist Kenji nach Salzburg gefahren — sein erster Besuch in Österreich! Er ist mit dem Zug von Bonn abgefahren und in Salzburg angekommen. Die Reise hat fast sechs Stunden gedauert, aber sie war wunderschön. Key change: ich → er and auxiliary changes: bin → ist.

Looking Ahead: Chapter 18

Was Sie nach Kapitel 17 können — What you can do after Chapter 17

After completing this chapter, you can narrate travel experiences using the Perfekt with both haben and sein. You can form past participles of motion verbs and separable motion verbs, choose the correct auxiliary consistently, name travel destinations with nach or in die/den/das, and express the duration of a trip with für. You can also ask and answer questions about travel experience, including Bist du schon mal … gewesen? Together, Chapters 16 and 17 give you a complete and functional German past tense for everyday communication.

Kapitel 18: Früher und heute

Chapter 18 introduces comparison across time — the language of "then and now." You will begin working with the simple past (Präteritum) of two very high-frequency verbs: war (was, from sein) and hatte (had, from haben). You have already used war as a formulaic reaction word (Es war toll!); Chapter 18 will develop your productive control of these forms across a wider range of contexts.

The chapter also develops adjective comparison more systematically — the structures you used briefly in the model dialogue (interessanter als, entspannter als) will be practised more thoroughly. You will read and write texts that compare the past with the present: earlier routines, how places have changed, and how preferences develop over time.

Before beginning Chapter 18, make sure you feel confident with:

  • the Perfekt with both haben and sein, including the auxiliary-choice rule;
  • the sentence bracket architecture — finite verb in second position, Partizip II at the end;
  • the present-tense forms of sein and haben in all persons (you will now also learn their simple past forms); and
  • the comparison forms adjektiv + -er als and am adjektivsten from earlier chapters, as these return with extended practice.

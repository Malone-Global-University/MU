Gesundheit und Wohlbefinden

Health and Well-Being · Speak and Write German: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Name common body parts and describe basic symptoms.
  2. Say how you are feeling and ask how someone else is feeling.
  3. Use dative personal pronouns to talk about how something affects a person.
  4. Use the dative experiencer expressions weh tun and helfen in natural sentences.
  5. Use mir ist ... to describe a physical or emotional state.
  6. Use possessive determiners naturally with body parts in context.
  7. Give basic advice and report a recommendation using sollen.
  8. Consolidate müssen for expressing necessity in a health context.
  9. Review the imperative for giving simple instructions.
  10. Ask and answer how long a condition has lasted using seit with the present tense.
  11. Describe a minor health concern and respond appropriately to basic guidance.

Communication Scenario

Es ist Montagmorgen in Leipzig. Jonas fühlt sich nicht gut und ruft seine Kollegin Frau Berger an, um sich für die Arbeit krankzumelden. Später am Tag schreibt er seiner Freundin Mia, die ihm rät, zum Arzt zu gehen. Am Nachmittag ist Jonas in der Apotheke und bittet um Hilfe.
It is Monday morning in Leipzig. Jonas isn't feeling well and calls his colleague Frau Berger to call in sick for work. Later in the day he writes to his girlfriend Mia, who advises him to see a doctor. In the afternoon Jonas is at the pharmacy and asks for help.

  1. Before reading further, think in English about how you usually describe feeling unwell, and what kind of advice people typically give in that situation.
  2. As you work through this chapter, pay attention to how German often expresses how someone feels or what hurts them using a dative pronoun rather than a possessive construction like English "my."

Pronunciation Focus

ch variation in body and health vocabulary

Review the ich-Laut and ach-Laut using health vocabulary. The ich-Laut follows front vowels and consonants, as in Bäuche (plural of Bauch) and Schmerzensgeld-type compounds; the ach-Laut follows back vowels, as in Bauch itself. Practice the contrast: der Bauch (back, ach-Laut) versus die Bäuche (front, ich-Laut).

Vowel length in near-minimal contrasts

Compare short and long vowels in frequent health words: Husten (short u) versus Fuß (long u); Hals (short a) versus Zahn (long a, written with h as a length marker). Read aloud: Mein Hals tut weh, und mein Bauch auch.

Consonant clusters

Practice clusters in Schmerzen, Erkältung, and Apotheke, keeping each consonant in the cluster distinct rather than dropping a sound.

Empathetic versus directive intonation

Compare a sympathetic question, Wie fühlst du dich?, spoken with a gentle falling pitch, against a direct instruction, Geh zum Arzt!, spoken with a firmer, more clipped pitch. The same vocabulary can sound caring or abrupt depending on intonation and tone of voice.

Dates and appointment times

Review intelligible production of dates and clock times from earlier chapters, applying them to appointment language: Mein Termin ist am Montag um neun Uhr.

Pronunciation Note — Stress in compound symptom words

Compound nouns such as Kopfschmerzen (headache) and Bauchschmerzen (stomachache) carry primary stress on the first component: KOPFschmerzen, BAUCHschmerzen. This follows the general pattern of compound stress you have practiced since earlier chapters.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter introduces dative personal pronouns, dative experiencer expressions, sollen for basic advice, and duration with seit.

13.1 Dative personal pronouns: form

You have already used dative articles with location prepositions. Dative personal pronouns extend this case to people who are affected by or who receive the action of a verb.

NominativDativBeispiel
ichmirMir ist kalt.
dudirTut dir der Kopf weh?

sie (singular)ihrIhr ist schlecht.
esihmEs hilft ihm nicht.
wirunsUns geht es gut.
ihreuchGeht es euch besser?

Sie (formal)IhnenWie geht es Ihnen?

Note that ihr (dative "to/for her") looks identical to the informal plural subject pronoun ihr ("you all"). Context and the surrounding verb distinguish the two.

13.2 weh tun: the dative experiencer pattern

weh tun ("to hurt") places the body part as the grammatical subject and the affected person in the dative case. This differs from the English pattern, where the person is the subject ("my head hurts").

Language Focus — Dative experiencer with weh tun
[Körperteil, Nominativ] + tut/tun + [Person, Dativ] + weh
The body part is the subject and agrees with the verb; the affected person appears in the dative case.
Der Kopf tut mir weh.
My head hurts. (literally: The head hurts to me.)
  • Mein Bauch tut mir weh. My stomach hurts.Bauch [subj.] / mir [dat.]
  • Tut dir der Hals weh? Does your throat hurt?
  • Die Füße tun ihm weh. His feet hurt.Füße [plural subj.] → tun

When the affected body part is plural, the verb agrees with the plural subject: tun, not tut. Possessive determiners are used naturally here, as the example above shows with mein Bauch; you do not need to replace them with an article in this construction.

13.3 mir ist ...: describing a state

A related pattern uses mir ist ... to describe how someone feels physically or emotionally, again placing the affected person in the dative.

  • Mir ist schlecht. I feel sick / nauseated.
  • Mir ist kalt. I am cold.
  • Ist dir warm? Are you warm?

Do not say *Ich bin kalt to mean "I am cold" — that sentence describes a personality trait, not a physical sensation.

13.4 helfen with the dative

helfen governs the dative case for the person being helped.

  • Der Tee hilft mir bei der Erkältung. The tea helps me with the cold.
  • Kann ich Ihnen helfen? Can I help you? (formal)
  • Die Tabletten helfen ihr gegen die Kopfschmerzen. The tablets help her with the headache.

13.5 sollen: basic advice and reported recommendation

sollen follows the same modal pattern you learned in Chapter 12: a finite modal in second position and an infinitive at the end of the clause.

  • Du sollst mehr trinken. You should drink more.
  • Der Arzt sagt, ich soll mich ausruhen. The doctor says I should rest.

müssen, which you already use for necessity, contrasts with sollen: müssen expresses a stronger obligation, while sollen expresses advice or a recommendation that comes from someone else.

  • Du musst einen Termin machen. You have to make an appointment. (necessity)
  • Du sollst mehr schlafen. You should sleep more. (advice)

13.6 Imperative review

Review the imperative forms from Chapter 11 in a health context. Remember that the du-imperative usually drops the pronoun and the final -st ending where applicable.

  • Ruh dich aus! Rest! (du)
  • Trinkt viel Wasser! Drink lots of water! (ihr)
  • Gehen Sie zum Arzt! Go to the doctor! (Sie)

13.7 seit + present tense for duration

German uses the present tense, supported by seit plus a dative time expression, to describe a condition that began in the past and continues now. English uses a different tense ("I have had a cold since Monday"), but German keeps the present tense because the condition is still ongoing.

  • Ich habe seit Montag eine Erkältung. I have had a cold since Monday.
  • Seit wann tut dir der Hals weh? Since when has your throat been hurting?

Do not translate this pattern with a German perfect or progressive form; the plain present tense with seit carries the intended meaning.

Model Dialogue

Kontext: Montagmorgen. Jonas ruft seine Kollegin Frau Berger an, um sich krankzumelden. Die beiden siezen sich.
Setting: Monday morning. Jonas calls his colleague Frau Berger to call in sick. The two use Sie with each other.

FRAU BERGER Guten Morgen, Jonas. Wie geht es Ihnen? Good morning, Jonas. How are you?
JONAS Guten Morgen, Frau Berger. Mir geht es leider nicht so gut. Mir ist schlecht, und der Hals tut mir auch weh. Good morning, Frau Berger. Unfortunately I'm not doing so well. I feel sick, and my throat hurts too.
FRAU BERGER Das tut mir leid. Haben Sie auch Fieber? I'm sorry to hear that. Do you have a fever too?
JONAS Ja, ich glaube schon. Ich habe seit gestern Abend Kopfschmerzen und Husten. Yes, I think so. I've had a headache and a cough since last night.
FRAU BERGER Das klingt nach einer Erkältung. Sie sollen sich ausruhen und müssen heute nicht ins Büro kommen. That sounds like a cold. You should rest and don't have to come to the office today.
JONAS Vielen Dank. Ich rufe vielleicht auch beim Arzt an. Thank you very much. Maybe I'll call the doctor too.
FRAU BERGER Gute Idee. Gute Besserung, Jonas! Good idea. Get well soon, Jonas!

Später am Tag: Jonas ist in der Apotheke.
Later that day: Jonas is at the pharmacy.

APOTHEKER Guten Tag! Kann ich Ihnen helfen? Hello! Can I help you?
JONAS Ja, bitte. Mir tut der Hals weh, und ich habe Husten. Was hilft dagegen? Yes, please. My throat hurts, and I have a cough. What helps with that?
APOTHEKER Diese Tabletten helfen gut. Sie sollen außerdem viel trinken und sich ausruhen. These tablets help well. You should also drink a lot and rest.
JONAS Danke schön. Wie viele Tabletten soll ich nehmen? Thank you very much. How many tablets should I take?
APOTHEKER Zweimal am Tag eine Tablette. Wenn es nicht besser wird, sollen Sie zum Arzt gehen. One tablet twice a day. If it doesn't get better, you should go to the doctor.

Verständnisfragen — Comprehension questions:

  1. Wie geht es Jonas am Montagmorgen? (How is Jonas doing on Monday morning?)
  2. Seit wann hat Jonas Kopfschmerzen und Husten? (Since when has Jonas had a headache and a cough?)
  3. Was soll Jonas laut Frau Berger machen? (What does Jonas's colleague say he should do?)
  4. Was tut Jonas in der Apotheke weh? (What hurts Jonas at the pharmacy?)
  5. Wie viele Tabletten soll Jonas nehmen? (How many tablets should Jonas take?)

Writing Workshop

In this workshop, you will plan and write a short absence or appointment message describing a minor health concern.

  1. Analyze the model. Reread Jonas's message to Mia. Identify: (a) every dative pronoun and what it refers to, (b) the duration expression with seit, and (c) the advice expressed with sollen.
  2. Plan your message. In notes, list: what is bothering you, since when, what a doctor or friend has advised, and how you are feeling now.
  3. Write your first draft. Write a short absence or appointment message of five to seven sentences. Include at least two dative pronouns, one use of weh tun or mir ist ..., one use of sollen, and one duration expression with seit.
  4. Revise. Check your draft against the editing checklist before writing a final version.

Checkliste — Editing checklist:

  • Dative pronouns are correctly chosen and match the person referred to.
  • weh tun places the body part as the subject and the person in the dative.
  • sollen and müssen are used appropriately for advice versus necessity.
  • seit is followed by the present tense, not a past tense.
  • Every noun is capitalized, with correct gender and plural where relevant.
  • du and Sie are used consistently and appropriately for the relationship.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Calling in sick and seeking care

Workplace and school expectations around reporting illness, requesting a doctor's note, and the appropriate level of formality when speaking to a supervisor vary by employer, institution, and country within the German-speaking world. Many workplaces expect a phone call or written notice on the first day of absence, but exact procedures should not be assumed without checking with the specific institution.

Regional Variation — Healthcare across the DACH region

Germany, Austria, and Switzerland each maintain their own healthcare and insurance systems, and the specific procedures for seeing a doctor or visiting a pharmacy differ accordingly. Pharmacy vocabulary and over-the-counter availability of certain medications also vary by country. Always verify current, country-specific healthcare information with an official source rather than relying on general assumptions, and treat any specific procedural claim in this chapter as introductory rather than exhaustive.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using a possessive instead of the dative experiencer

Error: *Mein Kopf tut weh. (used as if no dative person were needed)

Correction: Der Kopf tut mir weh. The dative pronoun names the affected person; a possessive determiner alone does not replace it, though mein Kopf tut mir weh is also possible when emphasis is wanted.

Common Mistake — Confusing ihr (dative) with ihr (subject pronoun)

Error: Misreading Ihr tut der Kopf weh as "you all" instead of "her."

Correction: Check the verb form and context. Ihr tut der Kopf weh (singular subject der Kopf, verb tut) means "Her head hurts." Ihr habt Fieber (verb agrees with plural ihr) means "You all have a fever."

Common Mistake — Treating sollen and müssen as interchangeable

Error: *Ich muss mehr schlafen, sagt der Arzt. when reporting gentle advice.

Correction: Ich soll mehr schlafen, sagt der Arzt. sollen reports a recommendation; müssen expresses a stronger, often self-directed necessity.

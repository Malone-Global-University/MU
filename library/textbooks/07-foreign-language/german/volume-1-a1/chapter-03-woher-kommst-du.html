Woher kommst du?

Origin and Languages · Speak and Write German: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Ask and answer Woher kommst du? and Woher kommen Sie? to state where someone is from.
  2. Ask and answer Wo wohnst du? and Wo wohnen Sie? to state where someone lives.
  3. Name several countries and cities, using an article only where German requires one.
  4. Say what language or languages you and others speak, learn, or study.
  5. State your nationality using a predicate adjective, without adding an ending.
  6. Conjugate and use regular present-tense verbs, including the stem-changing verb sprechen.
  7. Place the finite verb in the second position of a statement, including after a fronted time expression.
  8. Form and answer W-questions and verb-first yes-or-no questions.
  9. Exchange a phone number and an email address.
  10. Introduce a third person to someone else, using complete sentences.

Communication Scenario

It is the second session of the Deutschkurs at the Volkshochschule in Hamburg. Two new participants, Sofia from Madrid and Daniel from Toronto, join Lukas and Mira's group today. Before the class can talk about family or daily routines, everyone needs to be able to say where they come from, where they currently live, and which languages they speak — and to ask a new classmate for a phone number or an email address so they can study together outside of class. Frau Bauer opens the session with a "Kennenlern-Runde," a getting-to-know-you round that puts these new tools to immediate use.

  1. Before reading further, think about how you would answer these three questions in English: Where are you from? Where do you currently live? What languages do you speak? Notice that for many people, the answers to the first two questions are different — keep that distinction in mind as you learn the German questions woher and wo.
  2. In Chapters 1 and 2, you used short formulas without analyzing word order. This chapter explains, for the first time, exactly why the verb appears where it does in a German sentence — a pattern that will apply to every sentence you build for the rest of the course.

Pronunciation and Spelling

Initial sp and st

At the beginning of a native German word or syllable, the letter combinations sp and st are pronounced with a sch-like /ʃ/ sound before the following consonant, giving roughly /ʃp/ and /ʃt/.

Sound Focus — Initial sp and st

Practice this contrast with words from this chapter: Spanien [ʃpaːni̯ən], spanisch [ʃpaːnɪʃ], and sprechen [ʃprɛçn̩], sprichst [ʃprɪçst]. English speakers often pronounce the initial cluster as a plain /sp/ or /st/, the way it sounds in English "spin" or "stop." In German, add the /ʃ/ glide at the very beginning: think "shp-anien," not "sp-anien."

This rule applies only at the start of a word or a stressed syllable inside a word. It does not apply when sp or st occurs elsewhere, a distinction you will refine with more examples later in the course.

The ich-Laut after front vowels

Chapter 3 gives you more practice with the ich-Laut, the sound spelled ch after front vowels and after consonants such as r. It is a soft, breathy sound made near the front of the mouth — never a /k/ sound and never identical to English sh.

  • ich I
  • sprichst you speak (du-form, stem-changing)
  • sprichst du …? do you speak …?

You will meet the back-vowel counterpart, the ach-Laut, formally in Chapter 6. For now, simply continue practicing the front-vowel ich-Laut wherever it occurs.

German r in country and language words

Standard German r varies by speaker and position — it may be produced toward the back of the mouth (a uvular sound, common in much of Germany) or, after vowels, weakened toward a vowel-like quality. No single realization is the only correct one. Practice r in Frankreich, Französisch, and Brasilien, listening to your instructor or audio model rather than substituting the tapped or trilled r of some other languages.

Stress in country and language names

Most of this chapter's country and language names stress the first syllable: SPAnien, FRANKreich, KAnada, ENGlisch, SPAnisch. A few common exceptions stress a later syllable: i-TA-li-en and fran-ZÖ-sisch. Listen for these exceptions rather than assuming first-syllable stress applies everywhere.

Grouping numbers when speaking

When you say a multi-digit number aloud — a phone number, for example — group the digits in short clusters with a brief pause between groups, rather than running every digit together at the same speed. This makes numbers far easier for a listener to follow and write down.

Grammar Instruction

3.1 Regular present-tense verbs

Purpose: talk about what people do, using verbs beyond sein.

Most German verbs are regular in the present tense: remove the -en ending from the infinitive to find the stem, then add the ending that matches the subject.

PronomenEndungkommen
ich-ekomme
du-stkommst
er / sie / es-tkommt
wir-enkommen
ihr-tkommt
sie / Sie-enkommen

This same pattern of endings (-e, -st, -t, -en, -t, -en) applies to wohnen (to live), lernen (to learn, to study), and machen (to do, to make):

  • Ich wohne in Hamburg. I live in Hamburg.
  • Du lernst Deutsch. You are learning German.
  • Was machst du? What are you doing? / What do you do?
  • Wir lernen zusammen. We study together.

German has only one present tense where English often has two ("I learn" and "I am learning"). You will study the full range of present-tense meanings later in the course; for now, simply use the form above for both ideas.

3.2 A stem-changing verb: sprechen

Purpose: recognize that some common verbs change their stem vowel in the du and er/sie/es forms.

The verb sprechen (to speak) follows the same endings as above, but its stem vowel changes from e to i in the du and er/sie/es forms only.

PronomenFormEnglish
ichsprecheI speak
dusprichstyou speak (informal)
er / sie / essprichthe / she / it speaks
wirsprechenwe speak
ihrsprechtyou speak (informal plural)
sie / Siesprechenthey speak / you speak (formal)

Not every German verb changes its stem this way — kommen, wohnen, lernen, and machen in this chapter do not. Stem-changing verbs must be learned individually as you meet them; you will encounter more of them later in the course. For now, simply learn sprechen as your first example of the pattern.

  • Ich spreche Englisch und Deutsch. I speak English and German.
  • Sprichst du Spanisch? Do you speak Spanish?
  • Sofia spricht Spanisch und Englisch. Sofia speaks Spanish and English.
Grammar Note — Deutsch vs. deutsch

Compare these two sentences: Ich spreche Deutsch. (I speak German.) and Er ist deutsch. (He is German.) The word looks almost the same, but its grammatical job is different. In the first sentence, Deutsch is a noun — the direct object of sprechen — so it is capitalized, just like any other German noun. In the second sentence, deutsch is a predicate adjective describing the subject, so it stays lowercase and takes no ending. The same pattern applies to every language and nationality pair in this chapter: Sie spricht Französisch (noun) versus Sie ist französisch (adjective).

3.3 Verb-second word order (V2)

Purpose: understand why the finite verb appears where it does in a German statement, and apply that pattern correctly when a sentence does not begin with the subject.

In an independent German statement, the finite verb (the conjugated verb that agrees with the subject) normally occupies the second position. This is often called verb-second or V2 word order.

"Second position" does not mean "the second word." It means the second constituent — the second self-contained piece of the sentence, which may itself contain more than one word.

  • Ich komme aus Deutschland. I come from Germany.[Ich] [komme] [aus Deutschland] — one-word subject, verb second
  • Mein Freund Lukas kommt aus Hamburg. My friend Lukas comes from Hamburg.[Mein Freund Lukas] [kommt] [aus Hamburg] — three-word subject, verb still second
  • Heute lerne ich Deutsch. Today I am learning German.[Heute] [lerne] [ich] [Deutsch] — time expression first, subject moves after the verb

The third example shows an important consequence of V2 order: when something other than the subject — such as a time expression — begins the sentence, the subject moves to after the finite verb. This is sometimes called inversion, though nothing is truly out of order: the verb is still in its normal second position; only the first position has changed.

Language Building Block
[First constituent] + [finite verb] + [everything else]
The first constituent can be the subject, or it can be a time expression, a place expression, or another element — but whatever comes first, the finite verb follows immediately.
Heute spreche ich Deutsch. / Ich spreche heute Deutsch.
Today I speak German. / I speak German today. (Both are correct V2 sentences — they differ in emphasis, not in grammaticality.)
Common Mistake — Forgetting inversion

Error: *Heute ich lerne Deutsch.

Correction: Heute lerne ich Deutsch. English allows "Today I learn German," keeping the subject immediately before the verb. German does not: once heute occupies the first position, the finite verb must come second, which pushes the subject ich after the verb.

3.4 W-questions

Purpose: ask for specific information using a question word.

A W-question begins with a question word (most German question words begin with w), followed immediately by the finite verb in second position, then the subject.

  • Woher kommst du? Where are you from?
  • Wo wohnst du? Where do you live?
  • Was lernst du im Kurs? What are you learning in the course?
  • Welche Sprachen sprichst du? Which languages do you speak?
  • Wer kommt aus Spanien? Who comes from Spain? (recycled from Chapter 2)

Note the distinction between woher (where ... from, asking about origin) and wo (where, asking about current location). They are not interchangeable: Woher kommst du? asks about origin, while Wo wohnst du? asks about current residence. The same person may answer these two questions very differently.

3.5 Yes-or-no questions

Purpose: ask a question that can be answered with ja or nein.

To form a yes-or-no question, place the finite verb first, before the subject. No equivalent of English "do" or "does" is used.

  • Kommst du aus Deutschland? — Ja, ich komme aus Deutschland. Are you from Germany? — Yes, I'm from Germany.
  • Wohnst du in Berlin? — Nein, ich wohne in München. Do you live in Berlin? — No, I live in Munich.
  • Sprichst du Englisch? — Ja, ich spreche Englisch und Deutsch. Do you speak English? — Yes, I speak English and German.
Common Mistake — Inserting an English-style "do"

Error: *Du sprichst Deutsch? machst du sprechen Deutsch?

Correction: Sprichst du Deutsch? German never adds a separate helper word equivalent to English "do" or "does" in ordinary questions. The conjugated verb itself simply moves to the front of the sentence.

3.6 aus and in: formulaic origin and residence phrases

Purpose: state where someone is from and where someone lives, using fixed prepositional patterns.

Use aus ("from") to state origin and in ("in") to state residence. For now, treat these as fixed expressions; the grammatical case they require will be explained formally in a later chapter.

  • Ich komme aus Deutschland. I come from Germany.
  • Ich wohne in Hamburg. I live in Hamburg.
  • Sofia kommt aus Spanien und wohnt jetzt in Hamburg. Sofia is from Spain and now lives in Hamburg.

Most country names need no article after aus or in. The few countries that take an article in this course (die Schweiz, die USA) change that article when they follow aus or in:

  • Ich komme aus der Schweiz. I come from Switzerland.
  • Ich wohne in der Schweiz. I live in Switzerland.
  • Daniel kommt aus Kanada, aber sein Bruder wohnt in den USA. Daniel is from Canada, but his brother lives in the United States.

Memorize aus der Schweiz and in den USA as complete chunks for now. You will learn exactly why the article changes form when prepositional case is introduced beginning in Chapter 6.

Model Dialogue

Kontext: Die zweite Stunde im Deutschkurs an der Volkshochschule in Hamburg. Zwei neue Teilnehmer, Sofia aus Madrid und Daniel aus Toronto, stellen sich vor. Frau Bauer leitet die Kennenlern-Runde. Alle Teilnehmer benutzen du miteinander; mit Frau Bauer benutzen sie Sie.
Setting: The second class session at the Volkshochschule in Hamburg. Two new participants, Sofia from Madrid and Daniel from Toronto, introduce themselves. Frau Bauer leads the getting-to-know-you round. All participants use du with one another; with Frau Bauer, they use Sie.

FRAU BAUER Guten Tag, alle zusammen! Heute haben wir zwei neue Teilnehmer. Sofia, woher kommst du? Good day, everyone! Today we have two new participants. Sofia, where are you from?
SOFIA Ich komme aus Spanien, aus Madrid. Aber ich wohne jetzt hier in Hamburg. I'm from Spain, from Madrid. But I live here in Hamburg now.
FRAU BAUER Sehr schön. Und welche Sprachen sprichst du? Very nice. And which languages do you speak?
SOFIA Ich spreche Spanisch und Englisch. Jetzt lerne ich Deutsch. I speak Spanish and English. Now I'm learning German.
FRAU BAUER Danke, Sofia. Und du, Daniel? Woher kommst du? Thank you, Sofia. And you, Daniel? Where are you from?
DANIEL Ich komme aus Kanada, aus Toronto. Ich bin kanadisch, aber meine Familie kommt ursprünglich aus Italien. I'm from Canada, from Toronto. I'm Canadian, but my family originally comes from Italy.
FRAU BAUER Interessant! Sprichst du auch Italienisch? Interesting! Do you also speak Italian?
DANIEL Ein bisschen. Ich spreche Englisch und Französisch — in Kanada lernt man oft beide Sprachen. A little. I speak English and French — in Canada, people often learn both languages.
FRAU BAUER Toll. Lukas, Mira — sprecht ihr mit Sofia und Daniel? Tauscht eure Telefonnummern aus, damit ihr zusammen lernen könnt. Great. Lukas, Mira — talk with Sofia and Daniel. Exchange your phone numbers so you can study together.
LUKAS Gern! Sofia, wie ist deine Telefonnummer? Sure! Sofia, what's your phone number?
SOFIA Meine Nummer ist null-eins-sieben-fünf, zwei-drei-vier, fünf-sechs-sieben. Und wie ist deine E-Mail-Adresse? My number is 0175, 234, 567. And what's your email address?
LUKAS Lukas Punkt Becker At gmail Punkt com. lukas.becker@gmail.com
MIRA Daniel, das ist Sofia. Sofia, das ist Daniel. Er kommt auch aus Kanada — nein, Moment, er kommt aus Toronto, aber seine Familie kommt aus Italien! Daniel, this is Sofia. Sofia, this is Daniel. He's also from Canada — no, wait, he's from Toronto, but his family is from Italy!

Verständnisfragen — Comprehension questions:

  1. Woher kommt Sofia, und wo wohnt sie jetzt? (Where is Sofia from, and where does she live now?)
  2. Welche Sprachen spricht Sofia? (Which languages does Sofia speak?)
  3. Woher kommt Daniel, und woher kommt seine Familie ursprünglich? (Where is Daniel from, and where does his family originally come from?)
  4. Was tauschen Lukas und Sofia aus, und warum? (What do Lukas and Sofia exchange, and why?)
  5. Finden Sie im Dialog ein Beispiel für ein Satz, der nicht mit dem Subjekt beginnt. Was steht an erster Stelle, und wo steht das Verb? (Find an example in the dialogue of a sentence that does not begin with the subject. What is in first position, and where is the verb?)
Grammar Note — man

Daniel says in Kanada lernt man oft beide Sprachen ("in Canada, people often learn both languages"). The word man is an impersonal pronoun roughly equivalent to English "people," "one," or a general "you." It always takes the same verb form as er/sie/es: man lernt, man spricht. You will see man again later in the course; for now, simply recognize it as a way to talk about people in general rather than one specific person.

Writing Workshop

In this chapter, you will complete a registration form and then write a short personal profile for the class blog, following the model of Mira, Sofia, and Daniel's profiles above.

  1. Analyze the model. Reread the three profiles. Identify: (a) the order in which origin, residence, language, and a personal detail appear, (b) how each writer uses aus and in, and (c) one detail in each profile that makes it feel personal rather than generic.
  2. Complete the registration form. Fill in the Anmeldeformular above with your own real or invented information.
  3. Plan your profile. In notes, list: your name, where you are from, where you live now, the language(s) you speak, the language you are learning, and one personal detail (why you are learning German, what you find interesting or difficult, and so on).
  4. Write your first draft. Write four to six sentences. Include at least one sentence with aus, one with in, one with sprechen, and one sentence that does not begin with the subject (for example, beginning with jetzt or im Kurs).
  5. Revise. Check your draft against the editing checklist before writing a final version.

Checkliste — Editing checklist:

  • Every verb form matches its subject (correct ending for ich, du, er/sie/es, wir, ihr, sie/Sie).
  • Sprichst/spricht shows the correct stem change where needed.
  • Every sentence has the finite verb in second position, even after a fronted word like jetzt or heute.
  • die Schweiz and die USA use the correct forms after aus or in, if used.
  • Language nouns (Deutsch, Englisch) are capitalized; nationality adjectives (deutsch, englisch) are lowercase.
  • Every noun is capitalized, and umlauts/ß are correctly written.
  • My profile includes one personal, specific detail.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Origin, nationality, and residence are not the same question

As Daniel's profile shows, a person's birthplace, current residence, nationality, family background, and home language can all differ from one another, sometimes within a single sentence. When asking about someone's background, it is generally more precise — and often more respectful — to ask a specific question (Woher kommst du? or Wo wohnst du?) rather than assuming that nationality, language, and appearance always align. This matters throughout the German-speaking world, where long-standing multilingual communities and more recent migration mean that many residents have layered or multiple national and linguistic identities.

Culture in Context — Asking personal questions politely

Questions about origin can be a normal, friendly part of a getting-to-know-you conversation, as in this chapter's classroom scenario — but they can also feel intrusive depending on tone, context, and how often a person has been asked the same question by strangers. A simple, neutral Woher kommst du? in a classroom or social setting is generally welcome; repeated or probing follow-up questions about someone's background are not always appropriate. As with any personal topic, read the situation and the other person's openness before continuing.

Regional Variation — die E-Mail vs. das E-Mail

This course uses the Germany-based die E-Mail (feminine) as the default productive form. In Austria and German-speaking Switzerland, das E-Mail (neuter) is also common. Both forms are well established Standard German; this is a labeled national difference, not an error in either direction.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Confusing woher and wo

Error: Using Wo kommst du? to ask where someone is from.

Correction: Woher kommst du? asks about origin; Wo wohnst du? asks about current residence. The two words are not interchangeable, and the person's answers to each may well be different countries or cities.

Common Mistake — Forgetting the stem change in sprechen

Error: *Du sprechst Deutsch. / *Er sprecht Englisch.

Correction: Du sprichst Deutsch. / Er spricht Englisch. The stem vowel changes from e to i only in the du and er/sie/es forms of sprechen. The ich, wir, ihr, and sie/Sie forms keep the regular e.

Common Mistake — Capitalizing the nationality adjective

Error: *Er ist Deutsch. / *Sie ist Spanisch.

Correction: Er ist deutsch. / Sie ist spanisch. When the word functions as a predicate adjective describing nationality, it stays lowercase, even though the identical-looking language noun (Deutsch, Spanisch) is capitalized when it functions as a noun.

Common Mistake — Omitting the article with die Schweiz or die USA

Error: *Ich komme aus Schweiz. / *Ich wohne in USA.

Correction: Ich komme aus der Schweiz. / Ich wohne in den USA. Most German country names take no article after aus or in, but die Schweiz and die USA are exceptions and must keep their article, in the form shown.

Common Mistake — Losing verb-second order after a fronted word

Error: *Jetzt ich lerne Deutsch. / *Im Kurs wir sprechen Deutsch.

Correction: Jetzt lerne ich Deutsch. / Im Kurs sprechen wir Deutsch. Whatever element occupies the first position, the finite verb must immediately follow it in second position; the subject then comes after the verb.

Looking Ahead

Was Sie jetzt können — What you can do now

You can now ask and answer where someone is from and where they live, name several countries and languages, state a nationality with a predicate adjective, and exchange contact information. You understand why the finite verb occupies the second position in a German statement, even when a sentence does not begin with the subject. These regular present-tense verbs and this word-order pattern will appear in every chapter that follows.

Kapitel 4: Familie und Freunde — Family and Friends

Chapter 4 introduces the complete present tense of haben (to have) and the accusative case for the first time, so you can talk about family members, ages, and possessions. You will also learn possessive determiners such as mein and dein, and you will describe people using predicate adjectives you already know.

Das letzte Wochenende

Last Weekend · Speak and Write German: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

This chapter introduces the most important grammatical system in Unit IV: the German present perfect (Perfekt), which is the primary way speakers of German describe completed past events in everyday conversation. You will build this system step by step — starting with regular verbs and expanding to irregular and separable-prefix verbs — and you will practise it immediately in real communicative contexts.

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Describe what you did last weekend or recently using the Perfekt with haben.
  2. Form regular past participles using the pattern ge- + stem + -(e)t.
  3. Form selected irregular past participles from high-frequency verbs.
  4. Form past participles of separable-prefix verbs, placing ge- between the prefix and the stem.
  5. Maintain the sentence bracket: haben in second position, past participle at the end of the clause.
  6. Ask about past events with Was hast du gemacht? and yes/no questions.
  7. Use negation (nicht and kein/keine) in Perfekt sentences.
  8. Sequence a past account using zuerst, dann, danach, and related time expressions.
  9. Write an eight- to ten-sentence weekend account using these structures.

Opening Scenario

It is Monday morning at the university cafeteria. Mia Hoffmann and Jonas Weber, two students who have just started university in Düsseldorf, run into each other before their first lecture. Neither of them did anything particularly extraordinary last weekend — but they each have a story to tell. The question "Was hast du gemacht?" opens a conversation that is as ordinary as it is essential: talking about recent experience is one of the most frequent things we do in any language.

  1. Think about the last weekend you remember clearly. What did you do? How would you describe it to a friend in English? Jot down four or five things in your own language first. You will use this material again in the Speaking Practice and the Writing Workshop at the end of the chapter.
  2. Notice that in everyday German, the Perfekt (present perfect) is used for this kind of account — not the simple past that English speakers might expect. This chapter will show you how that works and why it matters for natural-sounding German.

Grammatik 16.1

The Perfekt — Structure and Function

Why the Perfekt?

German has a simple past tense (the Präteritum) and a present perfect tense (the Perfekt). In everyday spoken and informal written German — telling stories, describing what happened, asking a friend about their weekend — speakers almost always use the Perfekt, regardless of how far back in time the events occurred. The Präteritum is more common in formal writing and extended narrative. It will be introduced gradually from Chapter 18 onwards.

This is different from English, where "I went," "I did," and "I saw" (simple past) are the normal forms in everyday storytelling. In German, those same moments are expressed with the Perfekt: Ich bin gegangen, ich habe gemacht, ich habe gesehen. Think of the German Perfekt as the all-purpose past tense for conversation.

The Perfekt uses two components working together as a sentence bracket:

Das Perfekt — Sentence Bracket
[ Subject ] haben (conjugated) + rest of clause + Partizip II
haben takes second position — the slot already used by the finite verb. The Partizip II (past participle) moves to the end.
Ich habe gestern einen Film gesehen.
I watched a film yesterday. (literally: I have a film yesterday seen.)
Jonas hat seine Freunde getroffen.
Jonas met his friends.
Was hast du am Wochenende gemacht?
What did you do at the weekend?

The bracket works just like the modal verb bracket you already know. Compare:

  • Ich muss heute viel lernen. I have to study a lot today. (modal bracket) müssen → muss (finite, pos. 2) | lernen (infinitive, final)
  • Ich habe gestern viel gelernt. I studied a lot yesterday. (Perfekt bracket) haben → habe (finite, pos. 2) | gelernt (Partizip II, final)

haben — Quick Reference

You already know haben well from earlier chapters. Here it is in its role as a Perfekt auxiliary. The forms are identical to the haben you already use for possession.

PronounFormExample with Perfekt
ichhabeIch habe viel geschlafen.

Bracket Positions — Three Examples
Mia hat am Samstag die Wohnung aufgeräumt · Mia tidied the flat on Saturday.
Jonas hat mit seinen Freunden Gitarre gespielt · Jonas played guitar with his friends.
Wir haben gestern Abend einen tollen Film gesehen · We watched a great film yesterday evening.

The blue word (haben) always occupies the finite-verb slot — second constituent in a main clause. The gold word (Partizip II) always occupies clause-final position. Nothing may come after the Partizip II in a main clause.

Time expressions and V2 in the Perfekt

When a time expression comes first, the subject and haben swap positions — the standard verb-second rule still applies:

  • Ich habe gestern viel gelesen. (subject first) → I read a lot yesterday.
  • Gestern habe ich viel gelesen. (time expression first, inversion) → Yesterday I read a lot.

The Partizip II position does not change. Only the order of subject and haben shifts when something other than the subject comes first.

Grammatik 16.2

Regular Past Participles

Formation Rule

Regular (weak) verbs: ge- + stem + -(e)t

To form the past participle of a regular verb:

  • Add ge- to the front of the verb stem.
  • Add -t to the end of the stem.
  • If the stem ends in -t, -d, or a consonant cluster that makes pronunciation awkward, add -et instead of -t.

Grammatik 16.3

Irregular Past Participles

Formation Pattern

Irregular (strong) verbs: ge- + changed stem + -en

Many high-frequency German verbs form their past participle with ge- at the front and -en at the end — but the vowel or the consonants of the stem often change. There is no single rule for the stem change; you need to learn these forms with the verb. The most useful strategy is to notice the patterns:

  • Vowel change only: schlafen → geschlafen, lesen → gelesen
  • Vowel change + consonant change: treffen → getroffen, singen → gesungen
  • Completely different stem: essen → gegessen, sprechen → gesprochen

Learn each irregular participle as part of the verb's entry. The verb tables in this chapter and in the back matter list all principal parts.

Grammatik 16.4

Separable-Verb Past Participles

Formation Rule

Separable verbs: prefix + ge- + stem + ending

When a separable-prefix verb forms its past participle, the ge- is inserted between the separable prefix and the verb stem. The result is a single written word, not two separate words.

  • Regular stem + separable prefix: ein + ge + kauf + t → eingekauft
  • Irregular stem + separable prefix: an + ge + ruf + en → angerufen

The separable prefix is still written together with the rest of the participle — there is no separation in the past participle, unlike in finite-verb clauses.

aufräumenauf-räumen → geräumtaufgeräumt
anrufenan-rufen → gerufenangerufen
fernsehenfern-sehen → gesehenferngesehen
abholenab-holen → geholtabgeholt
kennenlernenkennen-lernen → gelerntkennengelernt
aufmachenauf-machen → gemachtaufgemacht
zuhörenzu-hören → gehörtzugehört
A note on inseparable-prefix verbs (receptive)

Some verbs have inseparable prefixes — be-, ge-, er-, ver-, zer- — that are never separated from the verb stem. These verbs do not add ge- in the past participle. You have already encountered several: besuchen → besucht (visited), erzählen → erzählt (told), verstehen → verstanden (understood). You may recognize these forms in reading and listening, but you are not yet required to produce them independently. They will be developed further in Chapter 17.

The key signal: if you hear a participle that sounds like a verb but has no ge-, it is likely an inseparable-prefix verb.

Grammatik 16.5

Asking Questions and Using Negation in the Perfekt

Questions in the Perfekt

Question word order in the Perfekt follows the same V2 and verb-first rules you already know. The Partizip II stays at the end of the clause.

  • Yes/no question (verb first): Hast du gestern viel geschlafen? Did you sleep a lot yesterday?
  • Question-word question: Was hast du am Wochenende gemacht? What did you do at the weekend?
  • Who/wen question: Wen hat Jonas getroffen? Who did Jonas meet?
  • When question: Wann habt ihr eingekauft? When did you (plural) go shopping?
  • Formal address: Was haben Sie am Wochenende gemacht? What did you do at the weekend? (formal)

Negation in the Perfekt

Negation works as it always has in German. Nicht usually appears before the element it negates, and before the Partizip II at the end of the clause. Kein/keine replaces an indefinite article before a noun.

  • Ich habe heute nicht gearbeitet. I didn't work today. nicht before Partizip II → negates the whole event
  • Mia hat gestern keine E-Mail geschrieben. Mia didn't write any email yesterday. keine replaces eine before E-Mail
  • Wir haben nicht viel geschlafen. We didn't sleep much. nicht modifies viel (an adverb)
  • Jonas hat keinen Film gesehen. Jonas didn't watch any film. keinen (masc. accusative) replaces einen

Model Dialogue

Read the dialogue once for meaning, then read it aloud. Notice how the sentence bracket works in every past-tense sentence: haben in second position, Partizip II at the end. Pay attention to the time expressions and how they sequence the account.

Montagmorgen, Mensa der Universität Düsseldorf. Mia und Jonas kommen zur ersten Vorlesung.

Jonas: Hallo, Mia! Wie war dein Wochenende? Hi Mia! How was your weekend? [war = was; formulaic — see note below]
Mia: Echt schön, danke! Ich habe viel zu Hause gemacht. Und du? Really nice, thanks! I did a lot at home. And you?
Jonas: Ich habe meine alten Schulfreunde getroffen. Wir haben zusammen in einem Restaurant gegessen. I met my old school friends. We ate together in a restaurant.
Mia: Oh, interessant! Was habt ihr gegessen? Oh, interesting! What did you all eat?
Jonas: Wir haben Pizza und Pasta gegessen und viel gesprochen. Es war super! We ate pizza and pasta and talked a lot. It was great! [war = was; formulaic]
Mia: Ich habe auch Pasta gekocht — aber zu Hause! Und danach habe ich ein gutes Buch gelesen. I cooked pasta too — but at home! And after that I read a good book.
Jonas: Hast du auch ferngesehen? Did you watch TV too?
Mia: Ja, abends habe ich eine Dokumentation gesehen. Sehr interessant! Am Sonntag habe ich meine Schwester angerufen — wir haben eine Stunde lang gesprochen. Yes, in the evening I watched a documentary. Very interesting! On Sunday I called my sister — we talked for an hour.
Jonas: Hast du auch Musik gehört? Did you listen to music too?
Mia: Natürlich! Ich habe Jazzmusik gehört. Und ihr — habt ihr nach dem Essen noch etwas gemacht? Of course! I listened to jazz music. And you all — did you do anything else after eating?
Jonas: Ja! Wir haben Gitarre gespielt und zusammen gesungen. Mein Freund Leo spielt sehr gut! Am Sonntag habe ich lange geschlafen und dann meine Eltern angerufen. Yes! We played guitar and sang together. My friend Leo plays really well! On Sunday I slept for a long time and then called my parents.
Mia: Das klingt entspannend! Ich finde Wochenenden zu Hause auch schön. That sounds relaxing! I also find weekends at home nice.

Language note — war: The forms war (was) and Es war schön/super/interessant are very common formulaic expressions for talking about the past. They come from the simple past of sein, which is introduced systematically in Chapter 18. At this stage, learn them as fixed reaction phrases, just as you learned Wie bitte? in Chapter 1 before you understood its grammatical components.

Listening Practice

Ask your instructor, a learning partner, or a text-to-speech tool to read the following account at a comfortable but natural pace. Listen once for the general picture, then again for the specific activities and their sequence. Do not read the script while listening.

Sophias Wochenende (Listening Script)

Hallo! Mein Name ist Sophia. Ich erzähle euch von meinem Wochenende in Leipzig.

Am Samstag habe ich lange geschlafen — bis neun Uhr! Dann habe ich Frühstück gemacht: Eier mit Toast und Kaffee. Danach habe ich meine Wohnung aufgeräumt und Wäsche gewaschen. Das war nicht sehr aufregend, aber nötig!

Am Nachmittag habe ich meine Freundin Maria angerufen. Wir haben fast eine Stunde gesprochen. Maria hat ein neues Buch gefunden und ich habe es sofort gekauft — eine Kriminalgeschichte! Am Abend haben Maria und ich zusammen ferngesehen. Wir haben eine tolle Krimiserie gesehen. Ich habe sie sehr gut gefunden.

Am Sonntag habe ich viel für die Universität gelernt und Notizen geschrieben. Ich habe auch ein bisschen Gitarre gespielt — ich lerne gerade Gitarre. Am Ende des Tages habe ich meine Eltern angerufen und viel gegessen: meine Mutter hat Suppe gekocht und wir haben zusammen am Tisch gesessen und gesprochen.

Das Wochenende war ruhig, aber ich habe viel gemacht!

The script contains gewaschen (washed — from waschen, irregular, strong verb, Partizip II with -en) and gesessen (sat — from sitzen, irregular). Both are haben verbs. war (was) appears twice as a formulaic reaction form.

Speaking Practice

All three speaking activities develop the same core skill: narrating past events fluently in German. Each activity increases slightly in length and spontaneity.

Aktivität 1: Interview — Was hast du gemacht?

  • at least one negative sentence (nicht or kein/keine)

Answers may describe a real or completely imaginary weekend.

Solo alternative: Record a 90-second monologue answering the question Was hast du letztes Wochenende gemacht? Use at least six sentences and all five of the checklist items above.

Aktivität 2: Mein bestes Wochenende

Describe — in two to three minutes — the best weekend you can remember or invent. Your account should have a clear sequence (beginning, middle, end) and use time expressions. Your partner listens and asks at least two follow-up questions at the end.

Bewertungshinweise — Speaking criteria for both activities

Assess yourself or your partner on:

  • Bracket accuracy: Is haben in second position? Is the Partizip II at the end?
  • Participle accuracy: Are regular, irregular, and separable forms correct?
  • Sequencing: Are time expressions used to organize the account?
  • Fluency: Can the speaker keep going without long pauses for each participle? (Participles should become automatic — practise them before speaking activities.)
  • Interaction: Does the respondent answer the actual question asked, not a rehearsed paragraph?

Aktivität 3: Vergleich — Whose weekend was more interesting?

Each partner describes their weekend briefly (four to five sentences). Then, together, compare them using the comparison language from Chapter 14: Das Wochenende von X war interessanter als …, Am interessantesten finde ich …. Use weil to give reasons.

Solo alternative: Compare two imaginary weekends — Jonas's weekend and Mia's weekend from the model dialogue. Write or say five sentences comparing them.

Schreiben

Writing Workshop

Write an eight- to ten-sentence account of a real or imagined weekend. This is the core productive writing task for Chapter 16 and prepares you directly for the chapter performance task.

  1. Plan your weekend. Choose five to seven activities. Include at least: one regular verb, two irregular verbs, and one separable-prefix verb. Decide on the sequence — Saturday morning, afternoon, evening; Sunday.
  2. Choose your time words. Decide where to use zuerst, dann, danach, am Morgen/Nachmittag/Abend, am Samstag/Sonntag, schließlich. Write them in your plan before you draft.
  3. Draft in German. Write each sentence fully. Resist the urge to write in English first and then translate. Start with Letztes Wochenende habe ich … or Am Samstag habe ich zuerst …
  4. Revise using the checklist below before writing your final version.

Mustertext — Model Weekend Account

Letztes Wochenende habe ich viel gemacht. Am Samstag habe ich zuerst lange geschlafen — bis acht Uhr! Dann habe ich Frühstück gemacht und Kaffee getrunken. Danach habe ich eingekauft: Ich habe Obst, Gemüse und Käse gekauft. Am Nachmittag habe ich meine Freundin Layla getroffen. Wir haben Musik gehört und viel gesprochen. Am Abend haben wir zusammen gekocht und einen Film gesehen. Den Film haben wir sehr gut gefunden!

Am Sonntag habe ich mein Zimmer aufgeräumt und dann für die Universität gelernt. Ich habe auch meine Eltern angerufen. Abends habe ich ein Buch gelesen. Das Wochenende hat mir sehr gut gefallen!

Das Wochenende hat mir sehr gut gefallen uses gefallen, an inseparable-prefix verb with a dative pronoun — drawing on the Chapter 13–14 dative system. This is included as a model closing; produce it as a formula if you wish.

Checkliste — Editing Checklist:

  • Every past-tense sentence has the correct form of haben in second position.
  • Every Partizip II is at the very end of its clause — nothing comes after it.
  • Regular participles follow the pattern ge- + stem + -(e)t.
  • Irregular participles are correct — checked against the table in this chapter.
  • Separable-verb participles have ge- between the prefix and the stem.
  • When a time expression comes first, haben still comes before the subject (V2 rule).
  • All nouns are capitalized.
  • Correct umlauts and ß are used throughout.
  • At least one sequencing expression is used.
  • At least one sentence is negative (with nicht or kein/keine).

Pronunciation Focus

Past participles introduce several pronunciation targets that are worth practising systematically.

The ge- prefix — Short and unstressed

The ge- prefix in past participles is always unstressed and pronounced as a short, reduced syllable: /gə/. This is the same schwa vowel found in unstressed syllables throughout German. Do not give it the full length or weight of a stressed syllable.

  • geMACHT — stress on the second syllable
  • geSPIELT — stress on the second syllable
  • geKOCHT — stress on the second syllable
  • geESSEN — stress on ess-

Contrast: the separable prefix in a finite clause is stressed — EINkaufen, AUFräumen. But in the past participle, the verb stem carries the main stress, not the prefix: eingeKAUFT, aufgeRÄUMT. The stress falls on the final syllable here.

Participle endings — -t versus -en

Regular participles end in -t: gemacht /gəˈmaxt/, gespielt /gəˈʃpiːlt/, gekauft /gəˈkaʊft/. The final -t is clear and slightly aspirated, as in gut or nicht.

Irregular participles end in -en: gegessen /gəˈgɛsən/, getrunken /gəˈtrʊŋkən/, gesehen /gəˈzeːən/. The -en ending is a reduced schwa followed by /n/, similar to the -en in infinitives: essen, trinken, sehen.

Learners sometimes add an unwanted vowel before the final -t: *gemachte rather than gemacht. The past participle does not change form for gender, number, or case when it stands alone after haben. (It only changes form when used attributively as an adjective — not required at this stage.)

Rhythm of the Perfekt sentence

In a Perfekt sentence, the main informational stress usually falls on the Partizip II at the end of the clause. Think of the bracket as a two-beat structure: the auxiliary opens the bracket (relatively light) and the participle closes it (relatively heavy):

  • Ich habe gestern einen Film geSEHEN.
  • Jonas hat seine Freunde geTROFfen.

When contrasting what happened versus what did not happen, the negative word or the contrasted item may receive the main stress instead: Ich habe NICHT geschlafen, sondern gelesen.

Culture and Communication

Culture and Communication — Das Wochenende

In German-speaking countries, the weekend (das Wochenende) is widely understood as protected time — a period for leisure, social connection, family, and personal recovery. Sunday in particular is legally protected in Germany as a day of rest (Ruhetag), meaning that most shops remain closed and noisy activities such as mowing the lawn are often restricted. This differs significantly from many other countries and reflects a legal and cultural emphasis on work-life boundaries.

Common weekend activities vary enormously by region, age, and lifestyle. Urban weekends might involve going to a café, a Flohmarkt (flea market), a Konzert, a museum, or a park. Rural weekends might center on hiking (Wandern), cycling, or family gatherings. Cooking at home remains very common — Sunday lunch (das Mittagessen am Sonntag) is a social institution in many families.

When asking about the weekend in German, the question Wie war dein Wochenende? (How was your weekend?) is completely standard in informal and semi-formal settings among colleagues and acquaintances. The expected answer is a brief, direct account — not a long formal report. Saying simply Ganz ruhig (Quite quiet) or Ich habe viel zu Hause gemacht is entirely normal and does not imply something went wrong.

Weekend norms vary across DACH contexts. In Switzerland, Sunday quiet laws (Ruhezeiten) are enforced at the cantonal level and may be stricter than in Germany. Austria has similar traditions. Urban communities in Berlin, Vienna, or Zurich tend to have more active weekend social cultures that extend later into the evening than smaller towns.

Regional Note — Vocabulary variation

Weekend and leisure vocabulary shows some DACH variation. In Austria, Wandern and Ausflug (day trip) are extremely common weekend activities, often to nearby mountains. Heurigen (wine taverns on the outskirts of Vienna) are a specifically Austrian weekend institution. In Switzerland, Zmittag (from Swiss German, equivalent to Mittagessen) might appear in informal contexts. When these regional terms appear in reading or listening, treat them as receptive and labeled; use the standard forms for active production.

Common Mistakes: Chapter 16

Common Mistake — Partizip II not at the end

Error: *Ich habe gemacht gestern Frühstück. / *Jonas hat getroffen seine Freunde.

Correction: Ich habe gestern Frühstück gemacht. / Jonas hat seine Freunde getroffen. The Partizip II is always the last element in the main clause. Nothing — no object, no time expression, no adverb — may follow it. This is the same bracket rule that applies to modal verbs: the non-finite element always closes the bracket.

Common Mistake — Missing ge- prefix in regular participles

Error: *Ich habe macht. / *Wir haben spielt. / *Sie hat kocht.

Correction: Ich habe gemacht. / Wir haben gespielt. / Sie hat gekocht. Every regular participle must begin with ge-. Using the bare infinitive stem (mach-, spiel-, koch-) without ge- produces a form that is grammatically incorrect in the Perfekt. The only exception involves inseparable-prefix verbs — and those are receptive at this stage.

Common Mistake — Using the infinitive as a participle

Error: *Ich habe gestern viel schlafen. / *Was hast du machen?

Correction: Ich habe gestern viel geschlafen. / Was hast du gemacht? The Partizip II is a distinct form, not the infinitive. Infinitives appear with modal verbs (Chapter 12); past participles appear with haben or sein in the Perfekt. The two bracket structures look similar but use different verb forms: Ich muss schlafen (infinitive, modal) versus Ich habe geschlafen (Partizip II, Perfekt).

Common Mistake — Separable-verb participle with ge- in the wrong position

Error: *Ich habe geaufgeräumt. / *Wir haben geeingekauft. / *Jonas hat geangerufen.

Correction: Ich habe aufgeräumt. / Wir haben eingekauft. / Jonas hat angerufen. The ge- goes between the separable prefix and the verb stem: auf-ge-räumt, ein-ge-kauft, an-ge-rufen. It does not go before the prefix. Think of the separable prefix as permanently attached — it never moves away from the stem in a participle.

Common Mistake — Conjugating haben for the subject after a fronted time expression

Error: *Gestern ich habe viel gelernt. / *Am Samstag wir haben gekocht.

Correction: Gestern habe ich viel gelernt. / Am Samstag haben wir gekocht. When a time expression occupies first position, the finite verb (haben) must immediately follow — before the subject. This is the standard verb-second rule that applies in every main clause: Heute mache ich viel. The same rule applies in the Perfekt: Gestern habe ich viel gemacht.

Common Mistake — Adding an adjective ending to the Partizip II after haben

Error: *Ich habe einen Film gesehenen. / *Sie hat das Buch gelesenem.

Correction: Ich habe einen Film gesehen. / Sie hat das Buch gelesen. When the Partizip II appears as the final element after haben, it does not change its form. It remains invariable: gesehen, not gesehenen/gesehener/gesehenem. Adjective endings on participles only appear when the participle is used attributively before a noun — this will be introduced in a later volume.

Performance Task

Kapitel 16 — Abschlussaufgabe

Mein Wochenende — Sprechen, Schreiben, Interagieren

This task integrates all three productive modes — speaking, writing, and interaction — using the Perfekt with haben. Complete all three components. They may be completed in sequence within one class session, or spread across two.

Component 1 — Spoken account (presentational): Tell a partner or record for yourself a spoken account of a real or imagined weekend. Aim for eight to ten sentences. Include: at least two regular participles, at least two irregular participles, at least one separable-verb participle, at least one time expression for sequencing, and at least one negation with nicht or kein/keine.

Component 2 — Written account (presentational): Write the same account from Component 1 in connected prose. Use the writing workshop checklist (page above) to revise your draft before writing the final version. Aim for a minimum of eight sentences. You may write about the same weekend as Component 1 or a different one.

Component 3 — Follow-up interaction (interpersonal): Exchange your written account with a partner. Your partner reads your text and asks three questions about it using the Perfekt. You answer in complete sentences. Then reverse roles. At least one question from each partner must use a question word (Was, Wen, Wann, Wie lange, Mit wem).

Looking Ahead: Chapter 17

Was Sie nach Kapitel 16 können — What you can do after Chapter 16

After completing this chapter, you can describe completed past events in everyday German using the Perfekt with haben. You can form regular, irregular, and separable-prefix past participles, maintain the sentence bracket, sequence a past account with time expressions, ask questions about past events, and use negation in the Perfekt. You have the core architecture of past narration in German.

Kapitel 17: Reisen und Erfahrungen

Chapter 17 expands your past-tense toolkit with a second auxiliary verb: sein. Many verbs of movement and change of state — gehen, fahren, kommen, fliegen, ankommen, aufstehen — form their Perfekt with sein rather than haben. Since narrating travel and experience typically involves movement, sein-verbs appear constantly in travel accounts. You will also work with duration expressions using seit and für in past contexts, and you will read and write about trips and travel experiences.

Before beginning Chapter 17, make sure you feel confident with:

  • the present-tense forms of both haben and sein in all persons (you will need sein as a Perfekt auxiliary);
  • the sentence bracket — haben/sein in second position, Partizip II at the end;
  • regular, irregular, and separable-prefix Partizip-II formation; and
  • the key irregular participles from this chapter, since they will be recycled throughout Unit IV.

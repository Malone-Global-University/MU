Mein Tagesablauf

Daily Routine · Speak and Write German: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Tell time using Wie spät ist es? and Um wie viel Uhr...?
  2. Describe a typical weekday and weekend routine using everyday activities.
  3. Use separable-prefix verbs such as aufstehen, einkaufen, anfangen, and anrufen correctly in main clauses.
  4. Recognize and explain the sentence bracket formed by a separable prefix.
  5. Use introductory reflexive verbs with accusative reflexive pronouns, such as sich duschen and sich anziehen.
  6. Place a time expression in first position and apply correct V2 inversion.
  7. Sequence daily activities using zuerst, dann, and danach.
  8. Say what they do not do, using nicht for basic clause and constituent negation.
  9. Ask and answer how often something happens using frequency adverbs.
  10. Make a simple arrangement to meet at a specific time.

Communication Scenario

Mira and Sofia have started studying together after class, the way Frau Bauer suggested in Chapter 3. Before they can find a time that works for both of them, they need to compare their daily routines — when they get up, when their classes or work begin, and what they usually do in the evening. Lukas, meanwhile, is trying to convince Daniel to join a Tuesday-morning study session, but Daniel's schedule does not leave much room. This chapter gives you the tools to describe a typical day, in enough detail to plan a real meeting time with someone else.

  1. Before reading further, sketch your own weekday in English: when do you get up, when does your day really start, and what do you do in the evening? You will build a German version of this sketch by the end of the chapter.
  2. In Chapter 3, you learned that the finite verb occupies the second position in a German statement. This chapter introduces separable-prefix verbs, where part of the verb moves all the way to the end of the clause — a new application of the same V2 system you already know.

Pronunciation and Spelling

Stress on separable prefixes

A separable prefix is always stressed, even though it is written as one word with the verb in the infinitive and moves to the end of the clause in simple main clauses.

Sound Focus — Separable-prefix stress

Practice the contrast between the infinitive and the conjugated form: AUFstehen → ich stehe AUF; EINkaufen → ich kaufe EIN; ANrufen → ich rufe dich AN. The prefix carries the main stress of the word in every form, including when it stands alone at the end of the sentence. English speakers sometimes stress the verb stem instead, by analogy with English phrasal verbs like "to get up," where stress can fall more evenly. In German, the prefix is consistently the loudest syllable.

Unstressed inseparable prefixes (recognition)

Not every prefix behaves this way. You will meet prefixes such as be-, ge-, and ver- formally in a later chapter, but you can already notice the contrast: a verb like besuchen (to visit) stresses the stem, not the prefix — beSUCHen, not BEsuchen. For now, simply recognize that German prefixes are not all stressed the same way.

Rhythm across the sentence bracket

When a separable prefix moves to the end of a clause, a noticeable gap can open up between the conjugated verb and its prefix, especially in longer sentences: Ich stehe jeden Morgen um sieben Uhr auf. Read sentences like this aloud slowly at first, keeping the prefix clearly audible at the end rather than swallowing it.

u versus ü

This chapter's vocabulary offers more practice distinguishing plain u from rounded ü: compare Uhr [uːɐ̯] with früh [fʁyː] and Frühstück [ˈfʁyːʃtʏk]. Keep your lips rounded and your tongue forward for ü; do not substitute the plain back vowel of u.

Grammar Instruction

7.1 Separable-prefix verbs and the sentence bracket

Purpose: describe routine actions using verbs whose prefix carries an important part of the meaning and moves within the sentence.

Many common German verbs are built from a simple verb plus a prefix that changes or sharpens its meaning: stehen (to stand) becomes aufstehen (to get up); kaufen (to buy) becomes einkaufen (to shop); rufen (to call) becomes anrufen (to call on the phone). In the dictionary and in the infinitive, the prefix and verb are written as one word.

In an ordinary main clause, however, the prefix detaches from the conjugated verb and moves to the end of the clause. The conjugated verb stem stays in the normal second position, and the prefix forms the other "wall" of what is called the sentence bracket.

Language Building Block
[First constituent] + [conjugated verb stem] + [everything else] + [separable prefix]
The verb's two parts form a bracket around the rest of the clause. This is the same V2 system from Chapter 3 — the conjugated verb is still in second position — with a new element at the very end.
Ich stehe um sieben Uhr auf.
I get up at seven o'clock. [Ich] [stehe] [um sieben Uhr] [auf]
PronomenFormEnglish
ichstehe ... aufI get up
dustehst ... aufyou get up (informal)
er / sie / essteht ... aufhe / she / it gets up
wirstehen ... aufwe get up
ihrsteht ... aufyou get up (informal plural)
sie / Siestehen ... aufthey get up / you get up (formal)
  • Ich kaufe samstags ein. I go shopping on Saturdays.[Ich] [kaufe] [samstags] [ein]
  • Lukas ruft Sofia heute Abend an. Lukas is calling Sofia tonight.[Lukas] [ruft] [Sofia heute Abend] [an]
  • Der Kurs fängt um neun Uhr an. The class begins at nine o'clock.

Not every common verb is separable. Frühstücken, arbeiten, and kochen, for example, behave exactly like the regular verbs from Chapter 3, with no detachable part. Separability must be learned for each verb individually; a vocabulary entry should always indicate it.

7.2 Separable prefixes with the infinitive

Purpose: recognize that the prefix stays attached when the verb is not the conjugated, finite verb of its clause.

When a separable verb appears as a plain infinitive — for example, in the dictionary, in a vocabulary list, or after certain other structures you will study later — the prefix and verb remain joined as one written word: aufstehen, einkaufen, anrufen, fernsehen, aufräumen.

You will see this contrast again, in a different grammatical environment, when modal verbs send a separable verb's infinitive to the end of the clause in a later chapter. For now, simply notice that the separated form belongs to ordinary finite main clauses, while the joined form is the dictionary and infinitive form.

7.3 Introductory reflexive verbs

Purpose: describe routine actions a person does to or for themselves, using a small set of frequent reflexive verbs.

Some German verbs are regularly used with a reflexive pronoun that refers back to the subject. In this chapter, you will use three: sich duschen (to shower), sich anziehen (to get dressed, separable), and sich ausruhen (to rest, separable). The reflexive pronoun takes the accusative case.

7.4 nicht: basic clause and constituent negation

Purpose: say that something does not happen or is not the case.

Nicht negates a verb, an adjective, or another specific element. Its position depends on what is being negated, not on one single fixed rule.

To negate an entire statement, nicht commonly stands near the end of the clause, after objects and most adverbs of time, but before a separable prefix or another clause-final element:

  • Ich stehe heute nicht früh auf. I'm not getting up early today.nicht precedes the adjective früh and the prefix auf
  • Daniel arbeitet samstags nicht. Daniel doesn't work on Saturdays.

To negate just one specific element — often to contrast it with something else — nicht stands directly before that element:

  • Ich dusche mich nicht am Abend, sondern am Morgen. I don't shower in the evening, but in the morning.

This chapter introduces nicht only for these two common patterns. You will study the fuller range of nicht-placement rules, alongside kein, beginning in Chapter 8.

Common Mistake — Splitting nicht from what it negates

Error: *Ich stehe nicht auf heute früh.

Correction: Ich stehe heute nicht früh auf. Keep nicht close to the element it negates, and remember that the separable prefix still belongs at the very end of the clause, after nicht.

7.5 Time expressions in first position

Purpose: emphasize when something happens by placing a time expression first, while keeping correct V2 order.

As you learned in Chapter 3, placing any single constituent — including a time expression — in first position pushes the subject after the finite verb.

  • Jeden Morgen stehe ich um sieben Uhr auf. Every morning I get up at seven o'clock.
  • Am Wochenende schläft Daniel länger. On the weekend, Daniel sleeps longer.
  • Um neun Uhr fängt der Kurs an. At nine o'clock, the class begins.

7.6 um, am, and von ... bis

Purpose: state at what time, on what day, and between which times something happens.

Use um with a clock time, am with a day of the week or part of the day, and von ... bis to give a start and end time. Treat these as fixed expressions for now; the case system behind them is explained beginning in Chapter 6 and continues to expand in later chapters.

  • Der Kurs fängt um neun Uhr an. The class starts at nine o'clock.
  • Am Montag arbeite ich nicht. On Monday I don't work.
  • Ich arbeite von neun bis fünf. I work from nine to five.

Model Dialogue

Kontext: Mira und Sofia sitzen nach dem Deutschkurs im Café und vergleichen ihre Tagesabläufe, um eine gemeinsame Lernzeit zu finden. Beide benutzen du miteinander.
Setting: Mira and Sofia sit in a café after German class and compare their daily routines to find a shared study time. Both use du with each other.

MIRA Sofia, wann stehst du normalerweise auf? Sofia, when do you normally get up?
SOFIA Ich stehe meistens um sechs Uhr auf, weil ich um acht Uhr arbeite. Zuerst dusche ich mich, dann frühstücke ich schnell. I usually get up at six, because I start work at eight. First I shower, then I eat breakfast quickly.
MIRA Sechs Uhr! Das ist früh. Ich stehe erst um halb acht auf — der Deutschkurs fängt ja erst um zehn an. Six o'clock! That's early. I don't get up until half past seven — German class doesn't start until ten anyway.
SOFIA Und am Abend? Was machst du normalerweise? And in the evening? What do you normally do?
MIRA Das ist unterschiedlich. Manchmal koche ich, manchmal sehe ich einfach fern. Ich räume auch oft die Wohnung auf. Aber ich sehe nicht jeden Abend fern. It varies. Sometimes I cook, sometimes I just watch TV. I also often tidy up the apartment. But I don't watch TV every evening.
SOFIA Ich arbeite von neun bis fünf, und danach kaufe ich oft ein. Am Wochenende ruhe ich mich aus. I work from nine to five, and after that I often go shopping. On the weekend I rest.
MIRA Also: Am Dienstagabend hast du Zeit, oder? So: you have time on Tuesday evening, right?
SOFIA Ja, dienstags arbeite ich nicht lange. Um wie viel Uhr treffen wir uns? Yes, on Tuesdays I don't work long. What time should we meet?
MIRA Um sieben? Ich rufe dich noch an, wenn etwas nicht klappt. At seven? I'll call you if something doesn't work out.
SOFIA Perfekt, bis Dienstag! Perfect, see you Tuesday!

Verständnisfragen — Comprehension questions:

  1. Um wie viel Uhr steht Sofia auf, und warum? (What time does Sofia get up, and why?)
  2. Was macht Sofia zuerst, und was macht sie dann? (What does Sofia do first, and what does she do then?)
  3. Was macht Mira normalerweise am Abend? (What does Mira normally do in the evening?)
  4. Wann und um wie viel Uhr treffen sich Mira und Sofia? (When and at what time do Mira and Sofia meet?)
  5. Finden Sie im Dialog einen Satz mit einem trennbaren Verb. Wo steht das Präfix? (Find a sentence in the dialogue with a separable verb. Where does the prefix stand?)
Grammar Note — erst

Mira says Ich stehe erst um halb acht auf ("I don't get up until half past seven"). The word erst signals that something happens later than one might expect, similar to English "not until" or "only." It is different from zuerst ("first," in a sequence), even though the two words look related. Compare: Ich dusche mich zuerst (I shower first, in a sequence) versus Ich stehe erst um acht auf (I don't get up until eight). You will see erst again in later chapters; for now, recognize its meaning in context.

Reading Note — Weekday and weekend contrast

Lukas closes his post by saying a good daily routine needs both structure and rest. This contrast between a structured workweek and a more relaxed weekend is common across German-speaking households, but the specific shape of that contrast — what time people get up, whether shops are open, how families spend Sunday — varies a great deal by occupation, household, region, and personal preference. Writing Workshop

In this chapter, you will complete a personal weekly planner and then write a short paragraph describing your typical weekday and weekend routine, following the model of Lukas's forum post above.

  1. Analyze the model. Reread Lukas's post. Identify: (a) how he sequences activities using dann and danach, (b) where he places nicht, and (c) how his weekday paragraph differs in content from his weekend paragraph.
  2. Complete a weekly planner. Sketch your own real or invented weekly schedule, noting at least three activities for a weekday and three for the weekend, each with an approximate time.
  3. Plan your paragraph. In notes, list: when you get up, what you do first, when work or class begins, what you do in the evening, and how your weekend differs.
  4. Write your first draft. Write two short paragraphs — one about a weekday, one about the weekend — of four to six sentences each. Include at least one separable verb, one reflexive verb, one sentence beginning with a time expression, and one negated sentence with nicht.
  5. Revise. Check your draft against the editing checklist before writing a final version.

Checkliste — Editing checklist:

  • Every separable prefix stands at the end of its clause in a simple main clause.
  • Every reflexive verb has the correct reflexive pronoun for its subject.
  • Every sentence has the finite verb in second position, even after a fronted time expression.
  • Nicht stands in a position that matches what is being negated.
  • Halb acht and similar time expressions are used correctly (half past the hour before the stated number).
  • Every noun is capitalized, and umlauts/ß are correctly written.
  • My paragraphs include at least one frequency adverb and one sequencing word.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Formal and informal time expressions

The conversational time system taught in this chapter (Viertel nach acht, halb neun, Viertel vor neun) is standard in everyday speech across the German-speaking world. Official contexts — transportation schedules, television listings, appointment confirmations, and business communication — very often use the 24-hour clock instead (20:30 Uhr rather than halb neun abends), partly because it removes any ambiguity between morning and evening. Both systems are correct standard usage; the choice depends on the setting, not on formality of speech alone.

Culture in Context — Daily schedules vary widely

Daily schedules in German-speaking countries vary enormously by occupation, household composition, region, and personal choice, just as they do anywhere else. Shop opening hours, school schedules, and typical workday lengths differ by country and even by region within a country, and have continued to change over time. Avoid drawing broad conclusions about "German punctuality" or "the German day" from any single schedule, including the ones in this chapter.

Regional Variation — der Samstag vs. der Sonnabend

This course uses der Samstag as the default productive word for "Saturday," reflecting usage across most of the German-speaking world, including Austria and Switzerland. In parts of northern and eastern Germany, der Sonnabend is also common and entirely standard. Recognize der Sonnabend receptively; this is a labeled regional difference, not an error.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Leaving the separable prefix attached

Error: *Ich aufstehe um sieben Uhr.

Correction: Ich stehe um sieben Uhr auf. In an ordinary main clause, the conjugated verb stem occupies the normal second position, and the prefix moves all the way to the end of the clause. The two parts never stay joined in a finite main clause.

Common Mistake — Misreading halb

Error: Treating halb neun as 9:30.

Correction: Halb neun means 8:30 — "half toward nine," not "half past nine." Practice this pattern with several different hours until it feels automatic.

Common Mistake — Forgetting the reflexive pronoun

Error: *Ich dusche um sieben Uhr. (intending "I shower at seven")

Correction: Ich dusche mich um sieben Uhr. Verbs like sich duschen require the reflexive pronoun in ordinary use; omitting it changes or obscures the meaning.

Common Mistake — Losing V2 order after a fronted time expression

Error: *Am Wochenende ich stehe später auf.

Correction: Am Wochenende stehe ich später auf. As in Chapter 3, whatever element stands first, the finite verb must immediately follow it in second position, with the subject moving after the verb.

Common Mistake — Placing nicht too early

Error: *Ich nicht arbeite samstags.

Correction: Ich arbeite samstags nicht. Nicht does not interrupt the verb and its subject. For whole-clause negation, it typically stands later in the clause, before any separable prefix.

Eine Lernzeit vereinbaren — Arranging a study time

Working with a partner (a real classmate, or — for independent study — an imagined person you invent), exchange complete descriptions of your weekday routines, including when you get up, when work or class begins and ends, and what you typically do in the evening.

Then, together, complete the following:

  • Identify at least one day and time when both of you are free.
  • Propose the time using Um wie viel Uhr ...? or a similar question.
  • Confirm or revise the proposed time.
  • Summarize your agreement in writing: Wir treffen uns am ... um ... Uhr.
  • Include at least one negated statement (nicht) explaining why another time does not work.

Present your final arrangement to a partner or small group, who may ask one follow-up question using Wann ...? or Um wie viel Uhr ...?

Answer Key

Practice — Conjugate and separate

  1. Ich stehe um sieben Uhr auf.
  2. Sofia kauft samstags ein.
  3. Wir rufen Daniel heute Abend an.
  4. Der Kurs fängt um neun Uhr an.

Practice — Word order with a fronted time expression

  1. Jeden Morgen dusche ich mich.
  2. Heute Abend ruft Lukas Sofia an.
  3. Am Samstag räumen wir die Wohnung auf.

Practice — Add nicht

  1. Daniel arbeitet samstags nicht.
  2. Ich stehe nicht früh auf.
  3. Wir sehen heute Abend nicht fern.

Dialogue — Comprehension

  1. Sofia steht meistens um sechs Uhr auf, weil sie um acht Uhr arbeitet.
  2. Sofia duscht sich zuerst, dann frühstückt sie schnell.
  3. Mira kocht manchmal, sieht manchmal fern, und räumt oft die Wohnung auf; sie sieht aber nicht jeden Abend fern.
  4. Mira und Sofia treffen sich am Dienstagabend um sieben Uhr.
  5. Beispiel: Ich rufe dich noch an. Das Präfix an steht am Ende des Satzes.

Listening — Table

  • Um wie viel Uhr steht Daniel unter der Woche auf? Um halb sieben.
  • Frühstückt Daniel? Nein, er frühstückt nicht.
  • Von wann bis wann arbeitet Daniel? Von acht bis vier.
  • Wie oft ruft Daniel seine Familie an? Oft, aber nicht jeden Tag.

Listening — Richtig oder falsch

  1. Falsch. Daniel frühstückt nicht, weil er keine Zeit hat.
  2. Richtig. Daniel arbeitet von acht bis vier.
  3. Falsch. Daniel geht um elf Uhr ins Bett, nicht um zehn.
  4. Richtig. Am Wochenende steht Daniel später auf.

Reading — Comprehension

  1. Mira und Sofia treffen sich am Dienstag um 19 Uhr.
  2. Sofia kauft ein, und Mira räumt die Wohnung auf.
  3. Lukas' Arbeit fängt unter der Woche um neun Uhr an.
  4. Samstags steht Lukas erst um zehn Uhr auf, frühstückt lange, und trifft Freunde oder kauft ein, statt früh aufzustehen und zu arbeiten.
  5. Modell: Am Wochenende stehe ich später auf und ... (Andere richtige Antworten sind akzeptabel.)

Chapter Review — Teil A

  1. Any five separable verbs from the chapter list, e.g.: aufstehen (to get up), einkaufen (to shop), anrufen (to call), anfangen (to begin), fernsehen (to watch TV).
  2. der Montag, der Dienstag, der Mittwoch, der Donnerstag, der Freitag, der Samstag, der Sonntag.
  3. acht Uhr · Viertel nach acht · halb neun · Viertel vor neun
  4. immer · oft · manchmal · nie (other valid orderings, such as including meistens and selten, are acceptable if the relative order is correct).

Chapter Review — Teil B

  1. aufstehen: ich stehe ... auf · du stehst ... auf · er/sie/es steht ... auf · wir stehen ... auf · ihr steht ... auf · sie/Sie stehen ... auf. sich anziehen: ich ziehe mich ... an · du ziehst dich ... an · er/sie/es zieht sich ... an · wir ziehen uns ... an · ihr zieht euch ... an · sie/Sie ziehen sich ... an.
  2. (a) Samstags kaufe ich ein. (b) Am Abend sehen wir fern. (c) Um zehn Uhr fängt der Kurs an.
  3. (a) Daniel frühstückt nicht. (b) Ich rufe meine Familie nicht jeden Tag an. (c) Wir räumen heute nicht auf.
  4. (a) Ich stehe früh auf. (b) Ich dusche mich um sechs Uhr. (c) Am Montag arbeite ich nicht.

Chapter Review — Teil C

  1. Mira und Sofia treffen sich am Dienstag um 19 Uhr.
  2. Lukas sagt, ein guter Tagesablauf braucht beides: Routine unter der Woche und Pause am Wochenende.

Chapter Review — Teil D, model answer

Unter der Woche stehe ich um sieben Uhr auf. Zuerst dusche ich mich, dann frühstücke ich. Ich arbeite von neun bis fünf. Am Abend koche ich oder ich sehe fern, aber ich sehe nicht jeden Abend fern. Other well-formed, accurate answers are equally acceptable. Check against the editing checklist.

Cumulative Review — Model responses for Speaker B

B : Ich heiße [Name], und ich komme aus [Land/Stadt].
B : Ich wohne in [Ort]. In meiner Wohnung gibt es [Zimmer/Möbel].
B : Ich stehe um [Uhrzeit] auf. Zuerst [Aktivität].

Exact wording varies. Check: correct verb endings, correct V2 word order, correct separable-prefix placement, and correct capitalization of nouns.

Looking Ahead

Was Sie jetzt können — What you can do now

You can now tell time, describe a weekday and weekend routine, use separable-prefix verbs correctly within the sentence bracket, use a small set of reflexive verbs, and negate a simple statement with nicht. The sentence bracket you practiced here — a conjugated verb in second position with another element at the end of the clause — will return and expand throughout the rest of the course, including with modal verbs and the Perfekt.

Kapitel 8: Guten Appetit! — Food and Drink

Chapter 8 expands the accusative case with definite, indefinite, and possessive determiners, introduces kein, and teaches the polite forms möchten and ich hätte gern, so you can name foods and drinks, state preferences, and complete a polite ordering exchange in a café or restaurant.

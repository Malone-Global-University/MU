Hallo! Erste Kontakte

First Encounters · Speak and Write German: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Greet people and say goodbye, choosing appropriately among informal, neutral, and formal expressions.
  2. Introduce yourself using Ich heiße ... and Mein Name ist ...
  3. Ask a person's name using Wie heißt du? (informal) and Wie heißen Sie? (formal).
  4. Use basic courtesy expressions: please, thank you, excuse me, yes, and no.
  5. Choose between du and Sie in clearly defined everyday situations.
  6. Recite the German alphabet and spell your own name aloud.
  7. Count from 0 to 10 in German.
  8. Recognize common greeting variants, including regional forms such as Servus and Grüß Gott.
  9. Write and recognize the German characters ä, ö, ü, and ß, and locate them on a keyboard.

Communication Scenario

On the first morning of an intensive German course in Berlin, a small group of new participants gathers in the lobby before class. They have never met. Some are close in age and quickly use first names; others are meeting the course director, who they address more formally. Within the first few minutes, everyone in the room has greeted someone, exchanged names, and made a first impression — entirely through the small set of expressions you will learn in this chapter.

  1. Think of the last time you met someone new. In English, did you immediately use their first name, or did the situation call for something more formal? Keep this question in mind: German marks this distinction directly in its grammar, through the choice between du and Sie.
  2. Look at your own name. How would you spell it letter by letter to someone who could not see it written down? You will practice exactly this skill using the German alphabet.

Pronunciation and Spelling

Three letters that mislead English speakers: w, j, z

English speakers consistently mispronounce three German letters by reading them as if they were English. Correcting this now will save you repeated correction later.

Sound Focus — w, j, and z
  • w represents the sound /v/, as in English van. Wie ("how") sounds like English "vee," not "wee."
  • j represents the sound /j/, similar to English yes. ja ("yes") sounds like English "yah," not like the letter J.
  • z represents the sound /ts/, as in English cats. zehn ("ten") begins with a "ts" sound, not an English "z."

Practice: Wie heißt du?, ja, zehn. Say each slowly, focusing on the consonant described above before moving on.

A first look at vowel length

German vowels can be long or short, and the difference is meaningful — it can distinguish one word from another. You will study this system in detail beginning in later chapters. For now, simply notice the difference in a few words from this chapter: the ie in Wie is a long /iː/ sound (similar to English "ee" in "see"), while the i in bitte is short. Listen for this contrast whenever your instructor or an audio model reads chapter vocabulary aloud.

Statement versus question intonation

In the formulas of this chapter, you can often hear whether a sentence is a statement or a question through pitch alone, even before you have studied German word order in detail.

  • Ich heiße Lena. My name is Lena. (statement: pitch falls at the end)
  • Wie heißt du? What's your name? (question: pitch typically rises slightly on the final syllable)

Practice reading the example sentences above aloud, exaggerating the pitch difference at first so the contrast becomes automatic.

Careful pronunciation of names

When you introduce yourself in German, your own name does not change, but the sounds around it should follow German pronunciation patterns. Practice saying Ich heiße [your name] several times, keeping the consonants in heiße crisp and clearly separate from your name.

Grammar Instruction

1.1 ich, du, and formal Sie

Purpose: identify yourself and address one other person, choosing the level of formality the situation calls for.

German has more than one way to say "you," and the choice is not optional — it signals the relationship between speakers. This chapter introduces the two forms you need for one-on-one conversation:

  • du — informal "you," used with one person you know well, a peer, a child, or in many casual settings.
  • Sie — formal "you," used with one or more people you do not know well, in professional contexts, or as a sign of respect. Sie is always capitalized, in every position in the sentence — not only at the beginning.

German also has ihr, used to address more than one person informally. You will meet ihr only receptively in this chapter — for example, if your instructor asks the whole class Wie heißt ihr? ("What are your names?", addressed to the group). You are not yet expected to produce ihr on your own; that becomes a full topic in Chapter 2.

1.2 Formulaic identity expressions

Purpose: introduce yourself and ask someone's name, using complete expressions before studying the full verb system behind them.

The expressions below all involve the verb heißen ("to be called") and the verb sein ("to be"). At this stage, learn each expression as a complete, fixed unit rather than analyzing its internal grammar — you will study the full present tense of sein in Chapter 2 and regular verb endings in Chapter 3.

Language Building Block
Ich heiße ... / Mein Name ist ...
Fixed formula for giving your own name — do not change the verb form yet.
Ich heiße Sofia. Mein Name ist Sofia Klein.
My name is Sofia. My name is Sofia Klein.
Language Building Block
Wie heißt du? (informal) / Wie heißen Sie? (formal)
Fixed question formula for asking a name — notice that the verb form changes with the form of address, even though you are not yet studying the full conjugation pattern.
Wie heißt du? — Ich heiße Paul. / Wie heißen Sie? — Mein Name ist Frau Vogel.
What's your name? — My name is Paul. / What's your name? — My name is Mrs. Vogel.
Language Building Block
ich bin ... / du bist ... / Sie sind ...
Three forms of the verb sein ("to be"), used here only for identity. The complete present tense of sein, with all persons, is taught in Chapter 2.
Ich bin neu hier. Du bist auch neu, oder? Sind Sie der Kursleiter?
I am new here. You're also new, right? Are you the course instructor?
Grammar Note — Why does the verb change between du and Sie?

You may have noticed that Wie heißt du? uses heißt, while Wie heißen Sie? uses heißen — and that du bist differs from Sie sind. German verbs change their ending depending on who is doing the action, the same way English changes "is" to "are." For now, simply memorize each formula as a unit attached to the correct form of address. Starting in Chapter 2, you will study this pattern systematically across all persons, not just du and Sie.

1.3 Simple affirmative and negative responses

Purpose: answer a yes-or-no question with a single word or a short formulaic phrase.

  • Bist du neu hier? — Ja, ich bin neu. Are you new here? — Yes, I'm new.
  • Heißt du Anna? — Nein, ich heiße Lena. Is your name Anna? — No, my name is Lena.

1.4 Statement order versus question order in these formulas

Purpose: notice, without yet naming the full rule, that German statements and questions in this chapter's formulas place their words in a predictable but different order.

Compare the pairs below. In each statement, the subject (ich, du) comes immediately before the verb. In each question, the verb comes before the subject, or a question word opens the sentence.

  • Ich heiße Tom. subject + verb + name
  • Heißt du Tom? verb + subject + name (question)
  • Du bist neu. subject + verb + description
  • Wie heißt du? question word + verb + subject

You are only asked to recognize this pattern in this chapter's fixed formulas, not to apply a general rule. German word order becomes a full instructional topic in Chapter 3.

Model Dialogues

Dialog A — Unter Kursteilnehmern (informal, du)

Kontext: Zwei junge Teilnehmer warten vor dem Kursraum und sprechen sich zum ersten Mal an.
Setting: Two young participants are waiting outside the classroom and speak to each other for the first time.

LENA Hallo! Ich heiße Lena. Und du? Hi! My name is Lena. And you?
PAUL Hallo, Lena! Ich heiße Paul. Freut mich. Hi, Lena! My name is Paul. Nice to meet you.
LENA Freut mich auch. Bist du auch neu hier? Nice to meet you too. Are you also new here?
PAUL Ja, ich bin neu. Wie schreibt man "Lena" — mit "h" oder ohne? Yes, I'm new. How do you write "Lena" — with an "h" or without?
LENA Ohne "h". L-E-N-A. Without an "h." L-E-N-A.
PAUL Danke! Bis später, Lena. Thanks! See you later, Lena.

Kontext: Ein Teilnehmer begrüßt die Kursleiterin im Eingangsbereich der Sprachschule.

TEILNEHMER Guten Tag! Entschuldigung — sind Sie die Kursleiterin? Good day! Excuse me — are you the course instructor?
FRAU VOGEL Guten Tag! Ja, das bin ich. Mein Name ist Vogel — Frau Vogel. Good day! Yes, that's me. My name is Vogel — Mrs. Vogel.
TEILNEHMER Freut mich, Frau Vogel. Ich heiße Antoine Lefèvre. Nice to meet you, Mrs. Vogel. My name is Antoine Lefèvre.
FRAU VOGEL Freut mich auch, Herr Lefèvre. Wie buchstabiert man Ihren Nachnamen? Nice to meet you too, Mr. Lefèvre. How do you spell your last name?
TEILNEHMER L-E-F, E mit Akzent, V-R-E. L-E-F, E with an accent, V-R-E.

Fragen zum Verständnis — Comprehension questions:

  1. Wer ist neu im Kurs — Lena, Paul, oder beide? (Who is new to the course — Lena, Paul, or both?)
  2. Wie schreibt man "Lena"? (How is "Lena" spelled?)
  3. Warum sagt der Teilnehmer in Dialog B "Sie" und nicht "du"? (Why does the participant in Dialogue B say "Sie" and not "du"?)
  4. Wie heißt die Kursleiterin mit Nachnamen? (What is the instructor's last name?)
  5. Finden Sie in beiden Dialogen je ein Beispiel für eine Begrüßung und eine Verabschiedung. (Find one example of a greeting and one of a farewell in each dialogue.)
Grammar Note — Das bin ich

Frau Vogel answers Ja, das bin ich — literally "yes, that am I," meaning "Yes, that's me." This word order, with das first, is a fixed conversational pattern rather than something to analyze piece by piece right now. Simply learn Das bin ich as a useful response when someone asks whether you are a particular person.

Writing Workshop

In this chapter, you will create your own name badge — a short personal card you might wear or post on the first day of a German course, in the style of the badges above.

  1. Analyze a model. Reread Greta's badge. Identify: (a) the greeting she chooses, (b) how she gives her name, (c) how she spells it out, and (d) the farewell she uses. Your own badge should follow a similar structure.
  2. Plan your badge. Before writing, answer these questions in notes: What greeting will you use? What is your name, and how would you spell it letter by letter in German? Are you new to the course? What farewell or closing phrase will you use?
  3. Write your first draft. Write two to three sentences. Include a greeting, your name given with either Ich heiße ... or Mein Name ist ..., and a closing expression.
  4. Revise. Check your draft against the editing checklist before writing a final version.

Checkliste zur Überarbeitung — Editing checklist:

  • My sentence begins with a capital letter.
  • I used a complete greeting expression, not just a single word fragment.
  • I gave my name using a correct formula (Ich heiße ... or Mein Name ist ...).
  • If I used "Sie," I capitalized it everywhere it appears.
  • I included a closing or farewell expression.
  • Any required umlauts (ä, ö, ü) or ß in my name or text are written correctly, not replaced with plain letters.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Greeting before requesting

In German-speaking contexts, it is common and generally expected to open an interaction with a greeting before asking a question or making a request — even a very small one, such as asking for directions or getting someone's attention in a shop. Skipping the greeting and moving straight to a request can come across as abrupt to many speakers, though exact expectations vary by setting, generation, and individual personality. When in doubt, start with Guten Tag or Hallo before continuing.

Culture in Context — Choosing du or Sie

The choice between du and Sie depends on context, relationship, age, setting, and sometimes profession or institutional culture, and these expectations are not identical everywhere in the German-speaking world or in every workplace. Among children, students of similar age, and close acquaintances, du is common. With strangers, in many professional settings, and with people clearly older than you, Sie is the safer default until invited to switch. That invitation is itself a recognized social move: Wir können uns duzen ("We can use du with each other") or simply Sag doch du zu mir ("Just call me du"). When uncertain, start with Sie and follow the other person's lead.

Culture in Context — Titles and surnames

Herr and Frau are used with a surname in many formal and professional interactions: Herr Brandt, Frau Adeyemi. Frau is the standard form of address for adult women in contemporary usage, regardless of marital status; the older term Fräulein is no longer standard for addressing adult women. First names alone are typically paired with du, while surnames are typically paired with Sie, although exceptions exist, particularly in some workplaces that mix first names with Sie as a sign of professional respect alongside informality.

Regional Variation — Servus and Grüß Gott

Servus and Grüß Gott are both widely heard in Austria, Bavaria, and other parts of southern German-speaking Europe, though neither is part of this course's core productive model, which follows broadly understood usage associated with Germany. Servus is informal and flexible — it can mean both "hi" and "bye," much like Italian ciao. Grüß Gott (literally "greet God") functions as a daytime greeting roughly equivalent to Guten Tag, and despite its religious origin, it is used by many speakers as a routine, fairly neutral greeting rather than a deliberately religious statement. You only need to recognize these two expressions in this chapter, not produce them; use Hallo, Guten Tag, and the other core greetings above for your own speech and writing.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Lowercase Sie

Error: *Wie heißen sie?

Correction: Wie heißen Sie? The formal "you," Sie, is capitalized in every position in the sentence, not just at the beginning. Lowercase sie means something different — "she" or "they" — so this is not a minor stylistic error; it changes who is being addressed.

Common Mistake — Mixing du and Sie in one exchange

Error: *Wie heißt du, Frau Vogel? Wie geht es Ihnen?

Correction: Wie heißen Sie, Frau Vogel? Wie geht es Ihnen? Once you choose du or Sie for a particular person, keep that choice consistent for the rest of the conversation unless the relationship visibly changes. Mixing the two within the same exchange is confusing and can sound disrespectful toward someone addressed with a title and surname.

Common Mistake — Spelling letters with English names

Error: Spelling "Lena" using English letter names ("ell," "ee," "en," "ay").

Correction: Use the German letter names from the alphabet table: "ell," "eh," "en," "ah." German letter names differ from English ones for several letters, including e, i, and j. Practice the German names separately from English spelling habits.

Common Mistake — Replacing umlauts and ß with plain letters

Error: *Mein Name ist Müller written as *Mein Name ist Muller; *heißt written as *heisst in core material.

Correction: Müller, heißt. The letters ä, ö, ü, and ß represent distinct sounds and spellings, not optional decorations. Use the actual character whenever your keyboard or device allows it; learn the keyboard or input method that lets you type them, rather than substituting plain letters.

Common Mistake — Treating heißen and sein as interchangeable

Error: *Ich bin Lena heiße.

Correction: Ich heiße Lena. or Ich bin Lena. Heißen ("to be called") and sein ("to be") can both introduce a name, but they are separate verbs and are not combined together in a single clause. Use one complete formula at a time: either Ich heiße ... or Ich bin ..., not both verbs stacked together.

Looking Ahead

Was Sie jetzt können — What you can do now

You can now greet people and say goodbye, introduce yourself and ask someone else's name, choose appropriately between du and Sie, spell your name using the German alphabet, and count from 0 to 10. This chapter began your personal language portfolio rather than reviewing earlier material — there is no cumulative review yet, since this is your starting point in the course.

Kapitel 2: Im Deutschkurs — In German Class

Chapter 2 takes you into the classroom itself. You will learn the three grammatical genders of German nouns — masculine, feminine, and neuter — along with their definite and indefinite articles in the nominative case. You will study the complete present tense of sein ("to be"), name classroom objects and people, count to 20, and ask Was ist das? ("What is that?"). The formulaic expressions you memorized in this chapter, such as Ich heiße ... and du bist / Sie sind, will reappear as you begin to see the grammatical system behind them.

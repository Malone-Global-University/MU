Geschichten und Kontakte

Stories and Staying Connected · Speak and Write German: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

In Chapter 18, you learned to compare then and now using war, hatte, and comparative language. This chapter takes your first systematic step beyond verb-second order. You will meet two subordinating conjunctions, weil (because) and dass (that), and the verb-final word order that they require. These new clause types let you explain why something happened and report what someone said, thought, or felt — two of the most useful moves in connected storytelling. You will also practice ordering objects and indirect objects correctly, and you will develop the conventions of German messages and emails.

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Form subordinate clauses with weil to give a reason, using correct verb-final word order.
  2. Form subordinate clauses with dass to report what someone said, thought, believed, or felt.
  3. Punctuate subordinate clauses correctly with a comma.
  4. Order direct and indirect objects correctly in simple sentences, including with pronouns.
  5. Recognize and use appropriate openings and closings in personal emails and messages.
  6. Read and understand short emails and messages that include a reason or a reported statement.
  7. Write a reasoned reply to a message.
  8. Tell a short connected story that combines cause (weil) and sequence (zuerst, dann, danach, from Chapter 16).

Opening Scenario

A few days after her phone call with Oma Trude, Mia gets a short email from Lukas, a friend from her German course who has moved to Freiburg for an internship. Lukas writes that he can't make it to their usual Friday study group, and he explains why. Mia writes back — she reports what Lukas said to another classmate, and she offers a reason of her own for suggesting a different plan.

  1. Think about a message you might send to explain why you can't do something, or to pass along something someone else told you. You will use this material in the Speaking Practice and the Writing Workshop at the end of the chapter.
  2. Notice how giving a reason (weil) and reporting what someone said (dass) both push the finite verb to the very end of the clause — a new pattern that sits alongside the verb-second order you have used since Chapter 7.

Grammatik 19.1

weil — Giving a Reason

A first step beyond verb-second order

Every main clause you have produced since Chapter 7 has followed verb-second order: the finite verb is always the second element. weil introduces a subordinate clause — a clause that cannot stand alone and depends on a main clause for its meaning. Inside a weil-clause, the finite verb moves to the very end of the clause. This is called verb-final order, and it is one of the defining features of German subordinate clauses.

Only weil is productive in this chapter as a reason-giving conjunction. Other reason and cause connectors remain outside the current scope.

weil — Sentence Pattern
[ Main clause ] , weil + [ subject … finite verb ]
The finite verb in the weil-clause moves from second position to the very end of the clause. A comma always separates the two clauses.
Lukas kommt nicht, weil er in Freiburg arbeitet.
Lukas isn't coming because he's working in Freiburg.
Ich schreibe dir, weil ich eine Frage habe.
I'm writing to you because I have a question.
Sie war traurig, weil ihr Freund nicht geschrieben hat.
She was sad because her friend hadn't written.
Verb-Final Order — with a Perfekt bracket
weil er gestern nicht angerufen hat
"because he didn't call yesterday" — the participle (angerufen) and the finite auxiliary (hat) both move to the end; the finite verb comes last of all.
weil with modals, separable verbs, and the Perfekt

When a weil-clause contains a modal verb, a separable verb, or a Perfekt form, every part of the sentence bracket moves toward the end — and the finite verb still comes last:

  • Ich kann nicht kommen, weil ich heute arbeiten muss. — modal verb last
  • Er ruft nicht an, weil er sein Handy vergessen hat. — Perfekt: participle, then finite auxiliary last
  • Sie steht spät auf, weil sie müde ist. — separable verb aufstehen stays together at the end (not split) inside a subordinate clause

Note that inside a weil-clause, a separable verb such as aufstehen does not split the way it does in a main clause (Sie steht auf). Because the whole verb complex moves to the end, the prefix and stem stay joined: weil sie spät aufsteht.

Practice A — Complete with weil and verb-final order

Grammatik 19.2

dass — Reporting What Someone Said or Thought

Same Word Order, New Function

dass introduces reported content

dass ("that") follows the same verb-final pattern as weil. It typically follows a main clause containing a verb of saying, thinking, believing, hoping, or feeling — such as sagen, denken, glauben, hoffen, and sich freuen.

  • Lukas sagt, dass er nicht kommen kann.
  • Ich glaube, dass das kein Problem ist.
  • Wir hoffen, dass es dir bald besser geht.

Unlike English, German cannot drop dass the way English sometimes drops "that" (compare "I think that he's right" and "I think he's right"). In German, dass is required whenever the clause is formally subordinated.

  • Lukas schreibt, dass er in Freiburg viel zu tun hat. Lukas writes that he has a lot to do in Freiburg.
  • Mia denkt, dass das kein großes Problem ist. Mia thinks that's not a big problem.
  • Ich freue mich, dass du mir geschrieben hast. I'm glad that you wrote to me.
  • Er hofft, dass er bald wieder Zeit hat. He hopes he'll have time again soon.
Reporting simply — an alternative to indirect speech

Fully accurate reported speech in German often involves the subjunctive (Konjunktiv I), which is not yet part of your active grammar. At this level, a dass-clause with an ordinary present-tense or Perfekt verb is a natural, acceptable way to report what someone said, especially in everyday conversation: Lukas sagt, dass er nicht kommen kann (rather than a formal indirect-speech construction). This is the productive standard for this chapter.

Practice B — Report with dass

Grammatik 19.3

Object and Indirect-Object Ordering

Two objects, one order to notice

Verbs like schreiben, sagen, and antworten often take two objects: a dative object (the person) and an accusative object (the thing). When both objects are nouns, the dative object usually comes first:

  • Ich schreibe meinem Freund (dat.) eine Nachricht (acc.). — I'm writing my friend a message.

When one object is a pronoun and the other is a noun, the pronoun usually comes first, regardless of case:

  • Ich schreibe ihm (dat. pronoun) eine Nachricht (acc. noun).
  • Ich schreibe sie (acc. pronoun) meinem Freund (dat. noun). — "it" refers to a feminine noun already mentioned.

When both objects are pronouns, the accusative pronoun comes first, then the dative pronoun:

  • Ich schreibe sie (acc.) ihm (dat.). — I'm writing it to him.

Grammatik 19.4

Email and Message Conventions

Openings and closings by relationship

German messages and emails have conventional openings and closings that vary by relationship and medium. For friends, family, and classmates, informal conventions are standard:

  • Opening: Liebe(r) [Name], or simply Hallo [Name],
  • Closing: Viele Grüße, Liebe Grüße, or Bis bald!

Lieber is used for a male addressee and Liebe for a female addressee, agreeing with the person's grammatical gender as commonly presented, not a fixed rule about the noun's gender in the abstract. Note the comma after the opening line and the lack of a capital letter to begin the first sentence after certain openings — this varies by convention and register.

Formal conventions (Sehr geehrte(r) …, and Mit freundlichen Grüßen) are introduced here receptively; a controlled formal-writing task is deferred to a later volume.

  • Liebe Mia, ich kann leider nicht zum Studientreffen kommen, weil ich in Freiburg arbeite. Dear Mia, unfortunately I can't come to the study meeting because I'm working in Freiburg.
  • Viele Grüße, Lukas Best wishes, Lukas

Model Dialogue

Below is the exchange from the opening scenario: Lukas's email to Mia, and Mia's reply. Read once for meaning, then again for the weil- and dass-clauses.

Lukas schreibt Mia eine E-Mail.

Lukas: Liebe Mia, ich kann am Freitag leider nicht zum Studientreffen kommen, weil ich diese Woche in Freiburg arbeite. Mein Praktikum ist sehr interessant, aber auch anstrengend! Dear Mia, unfortunately I can't come to the study meeting on Friday because I'm working in Freiburg this week. My internship is very interesting, but also exhausting!
Lukas: Ich hoffe, dass ihr trotzdem eine gute Zeit habt. Kannst du mir bitte später schreiben, was ihr besprochen habt? Das wäre super. I hope you all have a good time anyway. Can you please write to me later about what you discussed? That would be great.
Lukas: Viele Grüße, Lukas Best wishes, Lukas

Mia antwortet Lukas.

Mia: Lieber Lukas, kein Problem! Ich freue mich, dass dein Praktikum so interessant ist. Dear Lukas, no problem! I'm glad your internship is so interesting.
Mia: Ich schreibe dir am Freitagabend eine Nachricht, weil ich weiß, dass du das gerne hättest. Ich sage den anderen, dass du diese Woche nicht kommen kannst. I'll write you a message on Friday evening because I know you'd like that. I'll tell the others that you can't come this week.
Mia: Übrigens, ich glaube, dass wir das nächste Treffen verschieben sollten, weil auch Anna diese Woche keine Zeit hat. By the way, I think we should postpone the next meeting, because Anna also doesn't have time this week.
Mia: Liebe Grüße und viel Erfolg in Freiburg! Melde dich, wenn du Zeit hast. Warm wishes and good luck in Freiburg! Get in touch when you have time.

Speaking Practice

The speaking activities in this chapter develop the same core skill: explaining reasons and reporting information fluently using weil and dass.

Aktivität 1: Warum nicht? — Ausreden und Gründe

Work with a partner. Partner A invites Partner B to an activity; Partner B declines and gives a reason with weil. Then switch roles. Do this for at least four different invitations.

Partner A — Inviting

Invite your partner to do something. Use phrases such as:

  • Hast du Lust, heute Abend ins Kino zu gehen?
  • Kommst du morgen zum Studientreffen?
  • Möchtest du am Wochenende mit uns kochen?

Partner B — Declining with a reason

Decline politely and give a reason with weil. Aim to use:

  • at least three different weil-clauses
  • correct verb-final order each time
  • at least one Perfekt or war/hatte form inside a weil-clause

Reasons may be real or invented.

Solo alternative: Record a 90-second monologue in which you decline three imagined invitations, giving a different weil-reason for each.

Aktivität 2: Was hat er/sie gesagt? — Berichten

Work in a small group. One person reads a short statement quietly (or shows it to a partner); the partner reports it to a third person using dass. Continue the chain for at least five statements.

Bewertungshinweise — Speaking Criteria

  • Correct verb-final order in both weil- and dass-clauses.
  • Comma correctly placed before the subordinate clause.
  • Natural, appropriate reasons and reported content.
  • Correct object ordering when both a person and a thing are mentioned.
  • Fluency and clarity: the reason or report is easy for a listener to follow.
Schreiben

Writing Workshop

Write a short message or story combining one reason clause (weil) and one report clause (dass). This is the core productive writing task for Chapter 19 and prepares you directly for the chapter performance task. Choose one of two formats: a reasoned reply to a message, or a short connected story with cause and sequence.

  1. Choose your format. Option A: write a reply to a message (real or invented) that declines or responds to something, giving a reason. Option B: tell a short story about something that happened, explaining why it happened and reporting what someone said.
  2. Plan your content. Note the event or message, your reason (weil), and anything you want to report (dass).
  3. Draft using correct openings and closings if writing a message, or zuerst, dann, danach if telling a story.

Pronunciation Focus

weil and dass — clear onset consonants

weil /vaɪ̯l/ begins with the German w sound (closer to English v) and has the diphthong ei, pronounced like English "eye." Distinguish it clearly from weit and weiß, which share the same vowel but differ in the final consonant.

dass /das/ has a short, clipped vowel and a crisp double s. Keep it distinct from das (the article/pronoun, pronounced identically but spelled with one s when used as an article) — context, not sound, distinguishes them in speech.

Intonation across a subordinate clause

When a weil- or dass-clause follows a main clause, speakers typically use a slight pause at the comma and a falling intonation at the very end of the sentence, once the finite verb has been reached. Practice reading full weil/dass sentences aloud without pausing prematurely — the sentence is not grammatically complete until the final verb.

Email greeting vowels — Liebe(r) and Grüße

Liebe and Lieber both open with a long ie /iː/, as in Brief and lieb. Grüße combines the umlaut ü with the sharp-s ß — round the lips for ü and keep the final sound a clear, unvoiced /s/.

Culture and Communication

Culture and Communication — Persönliche Nachrichten

Personal messages between friends, classmates, and family members are a normal, everyday part of communication across German-speaking communities, just as they are in many places worldwide. The balance between quick texting-style messages and more complete emails depends on the relationship, the platform, and the urgency of the message — not on a single fixed rule.

Explaining a reason when declining an invitation or canceling a plan is generally considered courteous rather than intrusive. A short, clear weil-clause is usually enough; lengthy justification is not typically expected between friends.

This kind of everyday written communication is not limited to social contexts. Similar patterns of explanation and reporting appear naturally in workplace messages, school communication, and community coordination — all practical extensions of the language in this chapter.

Regional Note — Openings and closings across DACH

Informal openings such as Liebe(r) …, and Hallo …, and closings such as Viele Grüße and Liebe Grüße are used consistently across Germany, Austria, and Switzerland for personal messages. Regional and generational variation shows up more in digital-messaging conventions — such as whether a message opens with a greeting at all, or moves directly to the content — which vary by platform, age group, and individual habit rather than by country. Formal correspondence conventions, including Sehr geehrte(r) …, and Mit freundlichen Grüßen, are broadly shared across DACH institutional writing and are introduced here only receptively.

Common Mistakes: Chapter 19

Common Mistake — Keeping verb-second order after weil or dass

Error: *Ich bleibe zu Hause, weil ich bin müde. / *Sie sagt, dass sie kann nicht kommen.

Correction: Ich bleibe zu Hause, weil ich müde bin. / Sie sagt, dass sie nicht kommen kann. This is the single most important pattern in this chapter: once weil or dass introduces a clause, the finite verb must move to the very end of that clause, not stay in second position.

Common Mistake — Omitting the comma

Error: *Ich komme nicht weil ich krank bin.

Correction: Ich komme nicht, weil ich krank bin. A comma always separates a main clause from a following weil- or dass-clause in German, even in short sentences where English might omit a comma.

Common Mistake — Dropping dass

Error: *Ich glaube, er hat recht. (intended as a formal subordinate structure)

Correction: Ich glaube, dass er recht hat. English often drops "that" after verbs like "think" or "believe." German does not allow this when the clause is formally subordinated with verb-final order — dass must be included, and the verb must move to the end.

Common Mistake — Splitting a separable verb inside a subordinate clause

Error: *Er ruft nicht an, weil er sein Handy vergessen hat und steht spät auf. — *weil sie steht spät auf

Correction: weil sie spät aufsteht. In a main clause, a separable verb splits (Sie steht auf). Inside a weil- or dass-clause, the whole verb complex — prefix and stem together — moves to the end and stays joined.

Common Mistake — Wrong object order with pronouns

Error: *Ich schreibe eine Nachricht ihm.

Correction: Ich schreibe ihm eine Nachricht. When one object is a pronoun (ihm) and the other is a full noun phrase (eine Nachricht), the pronoun comes first, regardless of case.

Performance Task

Kapitel 19 — Abschlussaufgabe

Geschichten und Kontakte — Schreiben, Sprechen, Interagieren

This task integrates writing, speaking, and interaction using weil, dass, object ordering, and message conventions. Complete all three components. They may be done within one class session or across two.

Component 1 — Written message or story (presentational): Write a short message (a reply declining or responding to something) or a short connected story (using zuerst, dann, danach from Chapter 16). Include at least one weil-clause, one dass-clause, and correct opening/closing conventions if writing a message. Aim for six to eight sentences.

Component 2 — Spoken retelling (presentational): Tell a partner or record for yourself the content of your written text, without reading it word for word. Use the same weil- and dass-clauses in spoken form.

Component 3 — Follow-up exchange (interpersonal): Exchange your written text with a partner. Your partner asks two follow-up questions — at least one asking for a reason (Warum …?). You answer in complete sentences, using weil at least once. Then reverse roles.

Looking Ahead: Chapter 20

Was Sie nach Kapitel 19 können — What you can do after Chapter 19

After completing this chapter, you can explain a reason with weil, report what someone said or thought with dass, order objects naturally in simple sentences, and open and close an informal message appropriately. Together with the narrative tools from Chapters 16 through 18, you can now tell a connected story that sequences events, compares past and present, and explains why things happened — a genuinely flexible foundation for everyday German storytelling.

Kapitel 20: Abschlussprojekt — Auf Deutsch handeln

Chapter 20 is the final assessment of Volume I. It does not introduce new grammar. Instead, it asks you to bring together everything you have built across the volume — greetings and identity, home and routine, food and shopping, directions and plans, health and work, and now narration, comparison, and connected storytelling — in one integrated, portfolio-style project spanning personal, practical, community, and narrative domains.

Before beginning Chapter 20, make sure you feel confident with:

  • verb-second order in main clauses as an automatic habit;
  • verb-final order in weil- and dass-clauses, including with modals, separable verbs, and the Perfekt;
  • the Perfekt, war/hatte, and comparative language for narrating and comparing; and
  • appropriate du/Sie and message-register choices across different everyday situations.

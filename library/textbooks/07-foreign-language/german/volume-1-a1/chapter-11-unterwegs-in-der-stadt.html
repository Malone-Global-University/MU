Unterwegs in der Stadt

Getting Around · Speak and Write German: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Ask for and give simple directions in a city.
  2. Identify common city places, landmarks, and forms of public transportation.
  3. Distinguish wo (where, location) from wohin (where to, destination).
  4. Use two-way prepositions with the dative for location and the accusative for a directed destination or placement.
  5. Use common contractions such as im, am, ins, ans, and aufs correctly.
  6. Form and use imperatives for du, ihr, and Sie to give instructions and directions.
  7. Make a polite request for directions or help.
  8. Ask about and buy a basic transportation ticket.
  9. Follow and produce a short, sequenced set of directions.

Communication Scenario

Paul has just moved to Leipzig and does not yet know his way around. On his first free afternoon, he asks a neighbor, Frau Berger, for directions to the train station, and later asks a stranger on the street how to get to the museum. Along the way, he buys a ticket for the tram. Every interaction in this chapter depends on a single distinction you are about to learn: knowing whether you are talking about where something is or where something is going.

  1. Before reading further, think in English about the last time you asked someone for directions. What information did you need, and how did the other person organize their answer?
  2. As you work through this chapter, pay attention to the preposition that follows verbs of motion such as gehen and fahren, and compare it with the preposition used after verbs of location such as sein and stehen.

Pronunciation and Orthography

Aspirated p, t, k

At the beginning of a stressed syllable, German p, t, and k are pronounced with a noticeable puff of air, similar to English in words like pin and top rather than spin and stop. Practice with Platz, Taxi, and Karte.

Command versus polite-request intonation

A direct imperative such as Gehen Sie geradeaus! typically falls in pitch at the end. A softened request such as Könnten Sie mir helfen? rises like a question. Practice both intonation patterns aloud using the direction vocabulary above.

Connected speech in route instructions

Native speakers link words closely in a sequence of directions: Gehen Sie geradeaus und dann links. Practice reading full instructions without pausing between every word.

Number clarity

Platform, line, and stop numbers must be clear and unhurried: Linie drei, Gleis sieben. Review numbers 0–100 from Chapter 3 if needed.

Orthography review

  • Street and station names are capitalized and often written as compounds: die Bahnhofstraße, der Marktplatz.
  • Common transit abbreviations are conventional and not freely invented: Hbf. (Hauptbahnhof), Str. (Straße).
  • Sequenced instructions are separated by commas: Gehen Sie geradeaus, dann biegen Sie links ab.
  • The contractions ins, ans, and aufs are written as one word in ordinary spoken-style usage.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter's communicative purpose is to ask for, understand, and give directions. The grammar below exists to serve that purpose: distinguishing location from destination, and giving clear instructions.

Language Focus

Two-way prepositions: dative for location, accusative for a directed endpoint or placement.

11.1 wo versus wohin

Wo? asks where something is; the answer uses a two-way preposition with the dative. Wohin? asks where something is going; the answer uses a two-way preposition with the accusative.

  • Wo ist die Bank? — Sie ist in der Marktstraße. Where is the bank? — It's on Marktstraße.dative: location
  • Wohin gehst du? — Ich gehe in die Stadt. Where are you going? — I'm going into town.accusative: directed destination

Do not reduce this contrast to "motion versus no motion." Motion can occur entirely within a dative location: Ich gehe in der Stadt spazieren (I'm walking around within the city) still uses the dative, because the walking does not aim at crossing into a new location — it happens within one.

11.2 The nine two-way prepositions

These nine prepositions take the dative for location and the accusative for a directed endpoint: in, an, auf, neben, vor, hinter, über, unter, and zwischen. You used several of these for location in Chapter 6; this chapter adds the accusative destination use.

  • Das Fahrrad steht vor dem Bahnhof. The bicycle is in front of the train station.dative — location
  • Paul stellt das Fahrrad vor den Bahnhof. Paul puts the bicycle in front of the train station.accusative — placement, directed
  • Wir gehen auf den Markt. We're going to the market.accusative — destination
  • Wir sind auf dem Markt. We are at the market.dative — location

11.3 Common contractions

Full formContractionUse
in dasinsdestination, neuter
an dasansdestination, neuter
auf dasaufsdestination, neuter

an demamlocation, masc./neuter
  • Ich gehe ins Museum. I'm going to the museum.
  • Das Museum ist am Marktplatz. The museum is on the market square.

11.4 zu and nach for destinations

Zu plus dative is used for destinations involving a person, an institution, or a service location, and does not vary by case the way two-way prepositions do. Nach is used for cities and most countries, without an article.

  • Ich gehe zum Bahnhof. I'm going to the train station.zu + dem = zum
  • Wir fahren zur Post. We're driving to the post office.zu + der = zur
  • Paul fährt nach Leipzig. Paul is traveling to Leipzig.no article with city names

Model Dialogue

Kontext: Paul ist neu in Leipzig. Er fragt seine Nachbarin, Frau Berger, nach dem Weg zum Bahnhof. Die beiden sprechen miteinander in der Sie-Form, weil sie sich noch nicht gut kennen.
Setting: Paul is new in Leipzig. He asks his neighbor, Frau Berger, for the way to the train station. The two speak with each other using Sie, because they do not yet know each other well.

PAUL Entschuldigung, Frau Berger, wie komme ich zum Bahnhof? Excuse me, Frau Berger, how do I get to the train station?
FRAU BERGER Das ist nicht weit. Gehen Sie hier geradeaus bis zur Ampel. That's not far. Go straight ahead here to the traffic light.
PAUL Geradeaus bis zur Ampel, gut. Und dann? Straight ahead to the traffic light, okay. And then?
FRAU BERGER Dann biegen Sie links ab. Der Bahnhof ist gleich neben der Post. Then turn left. The train station is right next to the post office.
PAUL Kann ich auch die Straßenbahn nehmen? Can I also take the tram?
FRAU BERGER Ja, natürlich. Die Haltestelle ist dort drüben, vor dem Café. Nehmen Sie die Linie drei. Yes, of course. The stop is over there, in front of the café. Take line three.
PAUL Vielen Dank für die Auskunft! Thank you very much for the information!
PAUL (an der Haltestelle, zur Fahrerin) Entschuldigung, fährt dieser Bus zum Hauptbahnhof? (at the stop, to the driver) Excuse me, does this bus go to the main train station?
FAHRERIN Ja, steigen Sie ein! Die Fahrkarte kostet zwei Euro fünfzig. Yes, get on! The ticket costs two euros fifty.

Verständnisfragen — Comprehension questions:

  1. Wie kommt Paul zum Bahnhof, laut Frau Berger? (How does Paul get to the train station, according to Frau Berger?)
  2. Wo ist die Haltestelle? (Where is the stop?)
  3. Welche Linie soll Paul nehmen? (Which line should Paul take?)
  4. Was kostet die Fahrkarte? (What does the ticket cost?)
  5. Warum verwenden Paul und Frau Berger die Sie-Form? (Why do Paul and Frau Berger use the Sie-form?)

Writing Workshop

In this workshop, you will plan and write a short set of step-by-step directions from one real or invented place to another.

  1. Analyze the model. Reread the "Willkommen im Stadtzentrum!" passage. Identify: (a) the sequence of landmarks mentioned, (b) every two-way preposition and whether it is dative or accusative, and (c) the imperative or near-imperative instructions used.
  2. Plan your route. In notes, choose a starting point and a destination. List the landmarks a person would pass and the turns they would make.
  3. Write your first draft. Write five to seven connected sentences. Include at least one imperative, one destination phrase with a two-way preposition in the accusative, one location phrase with a two-way preposition in the dative, and one sentence with zu or nach.
  4. Revise. Check your draft against the editing checklist before writing a final version.

Checkliste — Editing checklist:

  • Every destination phrase uses the accusative after a two-way preposition.
  • Every location phrase uses the dative after a two-way preposition.
  • Contractions such as ins, ans, aufs, im, and am are used correctly.
  • Imperative forms match the intended addressee (du, ihr, or Sie).
  • Separable verbs such as aussteigen and umsteigen are correctly separated in main clauses.
  • Every noun is capitalized, with correct gender and plural where relevant.
  • Sequenced instructions are separated by commas where appropriate.

Speaking Practice

Kontrolliert — Controlled practice: question chain

In a small group, ask and answer in a chain: Wo ist die Haltestelle? Wohin fährst du heute? Wie komme ich zum Bahnhof? Nimmst du den Bus oder die Straßenbahn?

Kommunikative Aufgabe — Communicative task: Wie komme ich dorthin?

With a partner, use a simple hand-drawn or imagined map. One partner asks for directions to a specific place; the other gives a sequenced set of directions using imperatives, two-way prepositions, and at least one transportation option. Then switch roles with a new destination.

Rolle A

Du bist neu in der Stadt. Frag nach dem Weg zu zwei verschiedenen Orten und stell Rückfragen, wenn du etwas nicht verstehst.

Rolle B

Du kennst die Stadt gut. Erkläre den Weg klar, Schritt für Schritt, und erwähne mindestens ein Verkehrsmittel.

Alternative solo — Solo practice

Record yourself for 60 to 90 seconds giving directions from your home to a place you visit often. Listen back and check: (a) Did I distinguish dative location phrases from accusative destination phrases? (b) Did I use correct imperative forms? (c) Did I separate separable verbs correctly?

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Public transportation in German-speaking cities

Many cities across Germany, Austria, and Switzerland maintain integrated public transportation networks combining buses, trams, subways, and regional trains, though the scale, ticketing systems, and fare structures vary considerably by city and country and change over time. Always verify current routes, prices, and ticketing rules directly with the relevant transit authority rather than relying on general assumptions, since this information changes frequently and differs from city to city.

Regional Variation — Asking for help

The phrase Entschuldigung is broadly understood across the German-speaking world as a polite way to get someone's attention before asking for directions. Some speakers, particularly in southern Germany and Austria, may also use Entschuldigen Sie or regional greeting-based openers before a request. This course uses Entschuldigung as the default productive form and notes that politeness conventions can vary by region, generation, and setting; mobility needs and the ability to ask for clear, step-by-step help should always be respected.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using the dative for a destination

Error: *Ich gehe in der Stadt. (intended: "I'm going into town")

Correction: Ich gehe in die Stadt. When the verb expresses a directed movement toward a new location, use the accusative after the two-way preposition, not the dative.

Common Mistake — Confusing wo and wohin

Error: *Wo gehst du? (when asking about a destination)

Correction: Wohin gehst du? Use wohin to ask about a destination and wo to ask about a location.

Common Mistake — Leaving the separable prefix attached in an imperative

Error: *Aussteigen Sie hier!

Correction: Steigen Sie hier aus! In an imperative main clause, the separable prefix still moves to the end of the clause, just as in an ordinary statement.

Bewertungskriterien — Assessment criteria

  • Task completion: The route is clear, sequenced, and uses the map or schedule accurately.
  • Comprehensibility: A sympathetic reader or listener can follow the directions without difficulty.
  • Grammatical control: Dative location and accusative destination phrases are distinguished correctly in most instances; imperatives are correctly formed.
  • Vocabulary: Active vocabulary from this chapter is used naturally.
  • Register: Polite-request and direct-imperative forms are used appropriately for the situation.

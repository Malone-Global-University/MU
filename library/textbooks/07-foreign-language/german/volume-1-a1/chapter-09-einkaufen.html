Einkaufen

Shopping · Speak and Write German: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify common clothing items and everyday goods, using each noun with its article and plural.
  2. Ask for and state prices using numbers you have already learned.
  3. Describe color, size, and quality with attributive adjectives that carry the correct ending.
  4. Use attributive adjective endings after definite articles and after ein-words in the nominative and accusative.
  5. Ask Welches …? and point out an item with dieser/diese/dieses.
  6. Use accusative personal pronouns to avoid repeating a noun.
  7. Compare two items using größer, kleiner, besser, and similar comparative forms.
  8. Use zu plus an adjective to say that something is "too" something.
  9. Ask to see or try on an item, and complete a basic purchase or return.

Communication Scenario

Mira needs a new jacket before the weather turns colder, and Daniel wants to exchange a sweater that he received as a gift and that turned out to be too small. They meet on a Saturday at a clothing shop on the Mönckebergstraße in Hamburg. Sofia, who already knows the shop from a previous visit, comes along to help compare prices and styles. Between the three of them, the conversation moves from browsing and asking about sizes to comparing two jackets and finally completing a purchase at the register.

  1. Before reading further, think about how you would describe an item of clothing in English: which words come before the noun (color, size, material), and in what order? German attributive adjectives appear in the same position as in English, but unlike English adjectives, they carry an ending that depends on gender, number, case, and the determiner in front of them.
  2. You have already used predicate adjectives without endings, as in Das Auto ist neu. This chapter introduces attributive adjectives — adjectives that sit directly in front of a noun, as in das neue Auto — for the first time.

Pronunciation and Spelling

Adjective-ending clarity

Attributive adjective endings are short and unstressed, but they must still be pronounced clearly enough for a listener to hear the difference between, for example, der blaue Pullover and den blauen Pullover. Do not drop the final syllable entirely, but also do not overpronounce it as if it carried the main stress of the phrase — the stress stays on the adjective's own first syllable: BLAUe, BLAUen.

  • der kleine Rock the small skirt
  • die kleinen Schuhe the small shoes

Comparative -er

The comparative ending -er, as in größer and billiger, is a full, clearly pronounced syllable in German — unlike some reduced unstressed endings elsewhere in the language. Practice keeping it audible: GRÖSSer, not "gröss'r."

Final devoicing in shopping vocabulary

Recall final devoicing from earlier chapters: a written d, b, or g at the end of a word or syllable is pronounced like its voiceless counterpart. Practice this with Kleid [klaɪ̯t] and billig [ˈbɪlɪç], where the final g is pronounced close to the ich-Laut rather than as a hard /g/.

Compound stress in shopping vocabulary

As in earlier chapters, German compounds normally stress the first element: EINkaufstasche, UMtauschen. Listen for this pattern as new shopping compounds appear in this chapter's readings.

Contrastive sentence stress

When comparing two items, German speakers place extra stress on the contrasting word: Die blaue Jacke ist schön, aber die rote ist billiger. Practice highlighting the contrasted color or quality rather than stressing every word equally.

Grammar Instruction

9.1 Attributive adjectives after the definite article

Purpose: describe a specific item using an adjective placed directly before the noun.

You already know predicate adjectives, which follow sein and take no ending: Die Jacke ist blau. An attributive adjective sits directly before the noun it describes, inside the noun phrase, and it takes an ending. Why an ending at all? Because the determiner (der, die, das, den, and so on) already shows gender, number, and case fairly clearly, the adjective ending after a definite article is simplified to mostly -e or -en.

CaseMasculineFeminineNeuterPlural
Nominativder neue Pulloverdie blaue Jackedas kleine Hemddie neuen Schuhe
Akkusativden neuen Pulloverdie blaue Jackedas kleine Hemddie neuen Schuhe

Notice the pattern: every cell is -e except the masculine accusative and every plural cell, which are -en. This chapter teaches only the nominative and accusative; you will meet dative attributive adjectives beginning in Chapter 14.

Language Building Block
der/die/das + adjective + -e (nom.) · den + adjective + -en (masc. acc.) · die (pl.) + adjective + -en
After a definite article, the adjective ending is almost always -e, except where the article itself has already changed to mark masculine accusative or any plural — there the ending is -en.
Ich nehme den blauen Pullover.
I'll take the blue sweater.

9.2 Attributive adjectives after ein-words

Purpose: describe an item that is not a specific, previously identified one.

After ein, kein, or a possessive determiner, the pattern is almost the same as after the definite article — with one important difference. In the masculine nominative and the neuter nominative and accusative, the determiner itself does not show gender clearly (ein looks the same in all three), so the adjective ending must do that work instead.

CaseMasculineFeminineNeuter
Nominativein neuer Pullovereine blaue Jackeein kleines Hemd
Akkusativeinen neuen Pullovereine blaue Jackeein kleines Hemd

Compare the masculine nominative carefully: der neue Pullover versus ein neuer Pullover. After ein, the adjective takes -er in the masculine nominative — the same ending the definite article der would otherwise show. Compare also the neuter: das kleine Hemd versus ein kleines Hemd, where the adjective takes -es after ein.

  • Das ist meine neue Jacke. That's my new jacket.
  • Hast du keinen schwarzen Rock? Don't you have a black skirt?
  • Ich suche einen praktischen Rucksack. I'm looking for a practical backpack.

This chapter teaches adjective endings only in the nominative and accusative, after definite articles and ein-words. A complete four-case adjective system is not expected at this stage; you will expand this system gradually through Chapter 14 and beyond.

Common Mistake — Forgetting the ending after ein

Error: *Ich suche ein neu Hemd. / *Das ist ein blau Pullover.

Correction: Ich suche ein neues Hemd. / Das ist ein blauer Pullover. Unlike a predicate adjective, an attributive adjective in front of a noun always takes an ending, even after ein, where the ending is doing extra work to show gender.

9.3 dies- and welch-

Purpose: point to a specific item and ask which one among several.

Dieser/diese/dieses ("this") and welcher/welche/welches ("which") follow the same pattern as der/die/das and are used in nominative and accusative the same way.

  • Dieser Pullover ist schön, aber diese Jacke ist teurer. This sweater is nice, but this jacket is more expensive.
  • Welches Hemd nimmst du? Which shirt are you taking?
  • Ich nehme diesen Pullover. I'll take this sweater.

dies- and welch- are introduced here for nominative and accusative use only; full integration with the broader adjective system continues in later chapters.

9.4 Accusative personal pronouns

Purpose: refer back to an item already mentioned without repeating the noun.

NominativAkkusativEnglish
ichmichme
dudichyou (informal)
erihnhim, it (masc.)
siesieher, it (fem.)
esesit (neut.)
wirunsus
ihreuchyou (informal plural)
sie / Siesie / Siethem / you (formal)

A pronoun replaces an entire noun phrase, and it takes the gender of the noun it replaces, not the gender of the English translation:

  • Der Pullover ist schön. Ich nehme ihn. The sweater is nice. I'll take it.der Pullover (masc.) → ihn
  • Die Jacke ist teuer. Ich nehme sie nicht. The jacket is expensive. I won't take it.die Jacke (fem.) → sie
  • Das Hemd ist klein. Probierst du es trotzdem an? The shirt is small. Will you try it on anyway?das Hemd (neut.) → es
Grammar Note — Pronoun gender follows the noun, not the English word

English "it" covers nearly every inanimate object, but German requires ihn, sie, or es depending on the grammatical gender of the noun the pronoun replaces. Before choosing a pronoun, identify the article of the noun you are replacing: der Pullover → ihn; die Jacke → sie; das Hemd → es.

9.5 Comparison: größer, kleiner, besser

Purpose: compare two items.

Most German adjectives form the comparative by adding -er, often with an added umlaut on certain one-syllable adjectives:

GrundformKomparativEnglish
großgrößerbigger
kleinkleinersmaller
billigbilligercheaper
teuerteurermore expensive
gutbesserbetter (irregular)

Use the comparative as a predicate adjective, exactly as you used the base form in Chapter 2 — no further ending is needed in this position:

  • Die rote Jacke ist billiger als die blaue. The red jacket is cheaper than the blue one.
  • Dieser Pullover ist besser, aber teurer. This sweater is better, but more expensive.

This chapter introduces comparison only as a predicate adjective with als ("than"). A full comparative and superlative system, including attributive comparative endings, is not required at this stage.

9.6 zu plus adjective

Purpose: say that something exceeds an acceptable amount of a quality.

Use zu directly before a predicate adjective to mean "too": zu teuer (too expensive), zu groß (too big), zu klein (too small).

  • Der Pullover ist mir zu klein. The sweater is too small for me.
  • Das ist mir zu teuer. That's too expensive for me.

The dative pronoun mir in these formulaic expressions ("too small for me") is presented here as a fixed pattern. You will study dative personal pronouns formally beginning in Chapter 13.

Model Dialogue

Kontext: Samstagnachmittag in einem Bekleidungsgeschäft auf der Mönckebergstraße in Hamburg. Mira sucht eine neue Jacke, Daniel möchte einen Pullover umtauschen. Sofia hilft beim Vergleichen. Alle benutzen du miteinander; mit der Verkäuferin benutzen sie Sie.
Setting: Saturday afternoon in a clothing shop on the Mönckebergstraße in Hamburg. Mira is looking for a new jacket, Daniel wants to exchange a sweater. Sofia helps compare items. Everyone uses du with one another; with the salesperson, they use Sie.

VERKÄUFERIN Guten Tag! Kann ich Ihnen helfen? Good day! Can I help you?
MIRA Ja, gerne. Ich suche eine neue Jacke, nicht zu teuer. Yes, please. I'm looking for a new jacket, not too expensive.
VERKÄUFERIN Wir haben diese blaue Jacke hier für neunundsechzig Euro, oder die rote dort für fünfundfünfzig Euro. We have this blue jacket here for 69 euros, or the red one there for 55 euros.
SOFIA Mira, die rote Jacke ist billiger, und sie ist auch schön! Mira, the red jacket is cheaper, and it's nice too!
MIRA Stimmt. Aber die blaue ist größer. Kann ich diese rote Jacke anprobieren? True. But the blue one is bigger. Can I try on this red jacket?
VERKÄUFERIN Natürlich. Welche Größe brauchen Sie? Of course. What size do you need?
MIRA Größe achtunddreißig, glaube ich. Size 38, I think.
VERKÄUFERIN Hier, bitte. Die Umkleidekabine ist dort hinten. Here you go. The fitting room is back there.
MIRA Danke! (nach ein paar Minuten) Die Jacke passt gut. Ich nehme sie! Thanks! (after a few minutes) The jacket fits well. I'll take it!
DANIEL Entschuldigung, kann ich diesen Pullover umtauschen? Er ist mir zu klein. Excuse me, can I exchange this sweater? It's too small for me.
VERKÄUFERIN Kein Problem. Möchten Sie eine größere Größe oder ein anderes Modell? No problem. Would you like a bigger size or a different model?
DANIEL Eine größere Größe, bitte. Vielleicht in Grau statt Schwarz? A bigger size, please. Maybe in gray instead of black?
VERKÄUFERIN Hier ist ein grauer Pullover in Größe L. Gefällt Ihnen dieser besser? Here's a gray sweater in size L. Do you like this one better?
DANIEL Ja, viel besser. Danke schön! Wo ist die Kasse? Yes, much better. Thank you! Where's the checkout?
VERKÄUFERIN Gleich hier vorne. Bezahlen Sie bar oder mit Karte? Right here at the front. Will you pay in cash or by card?
MIRA Mit Karte, bitte. By card, please.

Verständnisfragen — Comprehension questions:

  1. Welche zwei Jacken zeigt die Verkäuferin, und was kosten sie? (Which two jackets does the salesperson show, and what do they cost?)
  2. Für welche Jacke entscheidet sich Mira, und warum? (Which jacket does Mira decide on, and why?)
  3. Warum möchte Daniel seinen Pullover umtauschen? (Why does Daniel want to exchange his sweater?)
  4. Welche Farbe und Größe bekommt Daniel stattdessen? (What color and size does Daniel get instead?)
  5. Finden Sie im Dialog ein Beispiel für ein Attributivadjektiv nach ein oder kein. Welche Endung hat es, und warum? (Find an example in the dialogue of an attributive adjective after ein or kein. What ending does it have, and why?)
Grammar Note — statt

Daniel asks for the sweater in Grau statt Schwarz ("in gray instead of black"). Statt means "instead of." Here, the colors function as nouns referring to the color itself rather than as adjectives describing a specific noun, which is why both are capitalized. You will see statt again in later chapters; for now, recognize it as a useful comparison word.

Reading Note — Formal written requests

Elena's message follows a common pattern for polite written requests in German: a greeting, a clear statement of the situation, a specific request, a closing phrase such as Vielen Dank im Voraus ("thank you in advance"), and a formal closing. You will study formal written messages more fully in Chapter 14; for now, notice how the structure differs from a casual spoken exchange.

Writing Workshop

In this chapter, you will write a short product description for an online shop listing, following the model of the advertisement above.

  1. Analyze the model. Reread the advertisement. Identify: (a) which adjectives appear attributively with an ending, (b) how prices are expressed, and (c) how the text creates a sense of a good deal.
  2. Choose an item. Pick one clothing item (real or invented) to describe: a jacket, a sweater, a pair of shoes, or another item from this chapter's vocabulary.
  3. Plan your description. In notes, list: the item, its color, its size or sizes, its price, and one quality adjective (practical, comfortable, nice, and so on).
  4. Write your first draft. Write four to six sentences. Include at least one attributive adjective after a definite article, one attributive adjective after ein or kein, one price, and one comparison using als or billiger/besser.
  5. Revise. Check your draft against the editing checklist before writing a final version.

Checkliste — Editing checklist:

  • Every attributive adjective has an ending; no predicate adjective has an unnecessary ending.
  • Adjective endings after der/die/das and after ein/kein follow the correct pattern.
  • Prices use correct numbers and the decimal comma where needed.
  • Comparisons use als correctly, if used.
  • Every noun is capitalized, and umlauts/ß are correctly written.
  • My description includes at least one specific, persuasive detail.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Service interaction and forms of address

As in the model dialogue, customers and sales staff in German-speaking countries typically use formal Sie with one another, even in a casual shop, unless the store specifically markets itself with informal du, as some youth-oriented or online retailers do. A polite greeting on entering a shop and a thank-you on leaving are common, even for a brief visit without a purchase.

Regional Variation — Clothing sizes

Germany, Austria, and Switzerland generally use the same numeric clothing-size system (for example, size 38 or 40 for many women's garments), which differs from sizing systems used in North America and the United Kingdom. Shoe sizes in the DACH region typically follow the European numbering system as well. When shopping or discussing sizes with someone from outside the region, it is often necessary to convert between systems; size charts in shops and online stores commonly provide this conversion.

Culture in Context — Payment practices

Cash and card payment are both common in shops across the DACH region, though preferences vary by country, generation, and type of business. Always confirm available payment methods if it matters for your visit; do not assume that every shop accepts every payment type, including some forms of contactless or mobile payment.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Adding an ending to a predicate adjective

Error: *Die Jacke ist blaue.

Correction: Die Jacke ist blau. A predicate adjective after sein never takes an ending. Only an attributive adjective placed directly before a noun takes an ending: die blaue Jacke.

Common Mistake — Wrong ending after ein in the masculine nominative

Error: *Das ist ein neue Pullover.

Correction: Das ist ein neuer Pullover. Because ein does not show gender in the masculine nominative, the adjective ending -er must show it instead.

Common Mistake — Choosing the wrong accusative pronoun gender

Error: *Die Jacke ist schön. Ich nehme es.

Correction: Die Jacke ist schön. Ich nehme sie. Jacke is feminine, so the pronoun that replaces it must be sie, regardless of English "it."

Common Mistake — Omitting als in a comparison

Error: *Die Jacke ist billiger die rote.

Correction: Die Jacke ist billiger als die rote. German requires als ("than") to introduce the second item in a comparison, just as English requires "than."

  1. Die Verkäuferin zeigt eine blaue Jacke für 69 Euro und eine rote Jacke für 55 Euro.
  2. Mira entscheidet sich für die rote Jacke, weil sie billiger und auch schön ist.
  3. Daniel möchte seinen Pullover umtauschen, weil er ihm zu klein ist.
  4. Daniel bekommt einen grauen Pullover in Größe L.
  5. Beispiel: einen grauen Pullover — Akkusativ nach einen, daher die Endung -en.

Listening — Table

  • Welche Größe braucht der Kunde? Größe zweiundvierzig.
  • Was kosten die schwarzen Schuhe? Neunundvierzig Euro.
  • Was kosten die braunen Schuhe? Achtunddreißig Euro.
  • Für welche Schuhe entscheidet sich der Kunde? Für die braunen Schuhe.

Listening — Richtig oder falsch

  1. Falsch. Der Kunde sucht Schuhe, nicht eine Jacke.
  2. Falsch. Die braunen Schuhe sind billiger als die schwarzen.
  3. Richtig. Der Kunde bezahlt mit Karte.

Reading — Comprehension

  1. Die blaue Jacke kostet jetzt neunundvierzig Euro, zwanzig Euro billiger als vorher.
  2. Die neuen Schuhe gibt es in Schwarz, Braun und Weiß.
  3. Elenas Kleid ist ihr zu klein.
  4. Elena möchte das Kleid in einer größeren Größe umtauschen.
  5. Beispiel: ein Kleid (Akkusativobjekt von gekauft) oder das gleiche Kleid — beide zeigen die Akkusativ-Endungsmuster dieses Kapitels.

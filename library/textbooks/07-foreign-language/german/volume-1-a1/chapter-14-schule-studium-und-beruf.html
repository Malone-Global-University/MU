Schule, Studium und Beruf

School, Study, and Work · Speak and Write German: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Discuss school subjects, university study, vocational training, and the world of work.
  2. Describe duties, schedules, and responsibilities in an educational or professional context.
  3. Compare options using the comparative (interessanter als) and superlative (am interessantesten), including selected irregular forms.
  4. Express equality using so … wie.
  5. Use the dative verbs helfen, gefallen, and gehören correctly.
  6. Use attributive adjective endings in common dative singular phrases.
  7. Explain a reason using a subordinate clause with weil, with the finite verb at the end.
  8. Report a thought or statement using a subordinate clause with dass.
  9. Produce correct V2 word order after a fronted weil clause.
  10. Write a short, appropriately formal inquiry about a course or training position.

Communication Scenario

Mia and Jonas went to the same school in Bonn and finished their Abitur two years ago. Since then their paths have diverged: Mia is now studying Informatik (computer science) at the university, while Jonas is doing an Ausbildung (vocational training) as a Kaufmann für Büromanagement (business management specialist) at a company in the city centre. They meet for coffee on a Saturday morning and compare notes — what they like, what is difficult, and why they chose the paths they did. By the end of their conversation, both realise they have more in common than they thought.

  1. Think in English about a time you chose one option over another — a course, a job, or a project. What reasons did you give? How did you explain your choice to others?
  2. As you work through this chapter, pay attention to how weil and dass let speakers explain reasons and report opinions within a single sentence — and how this changes the word order at the end of the clause.

Pronunciation Focus

Phrasing before the clause-final verb

In a weil or dass clause, the finite verb sits at the very end. Do not rush toward it or swallow it. Instead, group the words before the verb into a natural phrase, then deliver the final verb clearly: Ich lerne viel, weil die Prüfung morgen [breath/pause] schwer ist. Practise by speaking slowly at first, then building up to a natural pace. The final verb must be clearly audible.

Sentence stress in comparisons

In a comparison, the adjective that carries the difference normally receives the main stress: Das Studium ist ANspruchsvoller als die Ausbildung. When the comparison is the new information, the adjective and the word als share stress: Mathematik ist SCHWErer ALS Informatik. Practise by deciding which part of the comparison your listener does not yet know, and stress that part.

Compound stress in occupations and education terms

German compound nouns stress the first element: HAUSaufgabe, LERNgruppe, STUdiengang, BÜROmanagement, MEchatroniker. Long occupational compounds often have a secondary stress on the second major element. Practise: der Kaufmann für BÜroMAnagement.

Loanword stress

Several occupational and educational terms come from English or French and may carry stress in unexpected positions: InforMAtik (not INformatik), IngeNIEUR, MeDIzin, BioCHEmie. In formal messages, read aloud phrases such as Ich interessiere mich für den Studiengang Informatik to practise even rhythm across longer noun phrases.

Pronunciation Note — Reading formal messages aloud

Formal written German tends toward longer sentences than everyday conversation. When reading a formal inquiry aloud for practice, group phrases naturally around commas and clause boundaries. A comfortable pace is more effective than either rushing or speaking in a slow, word-by-word manner. Practise the model email in the Writing Workshop section aloud at least once before submitting or speaking in class.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter develops comparison further, introduces two important subordinating conjunctions, and expands your use of the dative case through verbs and adjective phrases.

14.1 Comparison: comparative and superlative

You used comparatives such as größer and besser in Chapter 9. This chapter develops comparison more fully across school and work vocabulary.

Forming the comparative: Add -er to the adjective. Many one-syllable adjectives add an umlaut.

Comparisons with als (than) and so … wie (as … as):

  • Das Studium ist anspruchsvoller als die Ausbildung. University study is more demanding than vocational training.
  • Die Ausbildung ist praktischer als das Studium. Vocational training is more practical than university study.
  • Informatik ist nicht so lang wie Medizin. Computer science is not as long as medicine.
  • Das Gehalt ist genauso gut wie in anderen Betrieben. The salary is just as good as in other companies.

Superlative as predicate (with am):

  • Mir gefällt Programmieren am besten. I like programming most of all.
  • Welches Fach ist am interessantesten? Which subject is the most interesting?
  • Die Ausbildung dauert am kürzesten. The vocational training is the shortest.

The superlative can also modify a noun as an attributive adjective with endings: das interessanteste Fach. This construction is mentioned for recognition; the predicate form with am is the productive priority for this chapter.

14.2 Dative verbs: helfen, gefallen, gehören

Some German verbs require their object in the dative case rather than the accusative. The most important at this stage are helfen (to help), gefallen (to please, to appeal to), and gehören (to belong to). Ask Wem? (To whom?) to find the dative object.

  • Der Professor hilft den Studierenden. The professor helps the students.helfen + dative
  • Er hilft mir oft mit Mathematik. He often helps me with maths.
  • Das Fach gefällt mir sehr. I really like the subject. (Literally: The subject pleases me.)gefallen + dative
  • Welches Fach gefällt dir am besten? Which subject do you like most?
  • Das Buch gehört der Lehrerin. The book belongs to the teacher.gehören + dative
  • Diese Stelle gehört ihm. This position is his.

Also useful with dative: danken (to thank), antworten (to answer), passen (to suit). Das passt mir gut (That suits me well.) is a natural expression for discussing what fits your career or study plans.

14.3 Dative adjective endings: an introduction

When an adjective comes directly before a noun in the dative singular — after a definite or ein-word determiner — the adjective ending is always -en. This applies regardless of grammatical gender.

Determiner typeMasculine (m.)Feminine (f.)Neuter (n.)
Definite article

The adjective ending -en in dative singular is consistent across all genders — there is no need to choose among different endings at this stage.

  • Er hilft dem neuen Kollegen. He helps the new colleague.dative masc. singular: dem neuen Kollegen
  • Ich arbeite bei einer großen Firma. I work at a large company.dative fem. singular: einer großen Firma
  • Sie dankt dem netten Professor. She thanks the kind professor.
  • Das Fach gefällt mir in diesem anspruchsvollen Studiengang sehr. I really like the subject in this demanding degree programme.

14.4 Subordinate clauses with weil (because)

weil is a subordinating conjunction that sends the finite verb to the end of its clause. A comma separates the main clause from the weil clause.

Language Focus — weil clause structure
Hauptsatz, weil [Subjekt] [Objekte / Adverbiale] [Finitesverb].
The finite verb — conjugated for person and tense — moves to the final position in the weil clause. If there is a modal verb, it also moves to the end, with the infinitive placed immediately before it.
Ich lerne viel, weil die Prüfung morgen schwer ist.
I study a lot because the exam tomorrow is hard.
  • Jonas macht eine Ausbildung, weil er praktisch arbeiten will. Jonas is doing vocational training because he wants to work practically.modal sentence bracket inside weil: arbeiten will
  • Mia studiert Informatik, weil Technologie sie sehr interessiert. Mia is studying computer science because technology interests her a great deal.
  • Das Fach gefällt mir, weil der Lehrer sehr gut erklärt. I like the subject because the teacher explains very well.
Grammar Note — weil vs. denn

Both weil and denn translate as "because," but they behave differently. denn is a coordinating conjunction and does not affect word order: Ich lerne viel, denn die Prüfung ist schwer. weil is a subordinating conjunction and sends the finite verb to the end: Ich lerne viel, weil die Prüfung schwer ist. Both require a comma before them. The word-order difference is not optional — treating weil like denn produces a grammatical error, not merely a stylistic choice.

14.5 Subordinate clauses with dass (that)

dass introduces a clause that functions as the object of a main-clause verb of thinking, believing, saying, or feeling. The finite verb goes to the end of the dass clause, and a comma separates the main clause from it.

Language Focus — dass clause structure
[Meinungsverb], dass [Subjekt] [Objekte / Adverbiale] [Finitesverb].
Common main-clause verbs: denken (to think), glauben (to believe), finden (to think/find), sagen (to say), wissen (to know), hoffen (to hope), meinen (to think/mean).
Ich glaube, dass die Ausbildung praktischer ist.
I believe that vocational training is more practical.
  • Ich finde, dass das Studium sehr interessant ist. I think that university study is very interesting.
  • Er sagt, dass das Fach ihm gut gefällt. He says that he likes the subject.
  • Wir denken, dass beide Wege gut sind. We think that both paths are good.
  • Ich hoffe, dass ich bald eine Stelle finde. I hope that I find a position soon.

14.6 V2 word order after a fronted subordinate clause

When a weil clause (or another subordinate clause) appears at the beginning of a sentence, the entire subordinate clause counts as the first constituent of the main clause. The main-clause verb must therefore come immediately after it, in second position, before the subject.

Language Focus — V2 after a fronted weil clause
Weil [clause], [finite verb] [subject] [rest of main clause].
The whole weil clause = first constituent. The main-clause finite verb is still second — just after the comma.
Weil die Prüfung schwer ist, lerne ich viel.
Because the exam is hard, I study a lot.
  • Weil er praktisch arbeiten will, macht Jonas eine Ausbildung. Because he wants to work practically, Jonas is doing vocational training.fronted weil clause → finite verb macht in position 2
  • Weil das Fach anspruchsvoll ist, hilft der Professor den Studierenden oft. Because the subject is demanding, the professor often helps the students.
  • Weil mir Technologie gefällt, studiere ich Informatik. Because I like technology, I am studying computer science.

Model Dialogue

Kontext: Samstagvormittag. Mia und Jonas sitzen in einem Café in Bonn. Beide duzen sich — sie kennen sich seit der Schulzeit.
Setting: Saturday morning. Mia and Jonas are sitting in a café in Bonn. They use du with each other — they have known each other since school.

MIA Jonas, wie gefällt dir deine Ausbildung? Jonas, how do you like your vocational training?
JONAS Sie gefällt mir sehr gut! Ich arbeite bei einer netten Firma in der Innenstadt und lerne jeden Tag viel. Weil ich direkt im Betrieb arbeite, ist die Ausbildung sehr praktisch. I really like it! I work at a nice company in the city centre and learn a lot every day. Because I work directly in the company, the training is very practical.
MIA Das klingt gut. Ich studiere Informatik, weil mich Technologie schon immer interessiert hat — also, seit der Schulzeit. Aber das Studium ist anspruchsvoller als ich dachte. That sounds good. I'm studying computer science because technology has always interested me — I mean, since school. But the degree is more demanding than I thought.
JONAS Ich denke, dass das Studium theoretischer ist als die Ausbildung. Aber du lernst vielleicht mehr Theorie. Mir gefällt es, dass ich schon Geld verdiene. I think that university is more theoretical than vocational training. But you probably learn more theory. I like it that I already earn money.
MIA Das stimmt. Welches Fach gefällt dir in der Ausbildung am besten? That's true. Which subject do you like most in your training?
JONAS Buchhaltung gefällt mir gut, aber am interessantesten finde ich Betriebswirtschaft. Und dir? Was ist dein liebstes Fach? I like accounting, but I find business management the most interesting. And you? What is your favourite subject?
MIA Programmieren gefällt mir am besten. Mathematik ist schwerer als die anderen Fächer, aber sie hilft mir sehr. Hilfst du anderen Studierenden manchmal? I like programming most. Maths is harder than the other subjects, but it helps me a lot. Do you sometimes help other students?
JONAS Ich helfe meiner Lerngruppe oft, weil wir zusammen für den Unterricht üben. Und dein Studium — hilfst du anderen Studierenden auch? I often help my study group because we practise together for class. And your studies — do you help other students too?
MIA Ja, gern. Ich glaube, dass man am meisten lernt, wenn man anderen erklärt. Ich finde übrigens, dass beide Wege gut sind — Studium und Ausbildung. Yes, gladly. I believe that you learn most when you explain things to others. I think, by the way, that both paths are good — university and vocational training.
JONAS Da stimme ich dir zu. Es passt einfach zu jeder Person anders. I agree with you there. It simply suits each person differently.

Two phrases in this dialogue contain past-tense forms that are not yet productive targets: als ich dachte and interessiert hat. Recognise them from context; their full analysis belongs to later chapters.

Verständnisfragen — Comprehension questions:

  1. Warum gefällt Jonas seine Ausbildung? (Why does Jonas like his training?)
  2. Warum studiert Mia Informatik? (Why is Mia studying computer science?)
  3. Welches Fach gefällt Jonas am besten? (Which subject does Jonas like most?)
  4. Wie hilft Mia anderen Studierenden? (How does Mia help other students?)
  5. Was denkt Mia über Studium und Ausbildung? (What does Mia think about university study and vocational training?)

Speaking Practice

Kontrolliert — Controlled practice: question chain

In a small group, ask and answer in a chain using complete sentences: Was studierst du / machst du? Warum? Welches Fach gefällt dir am besten? Was ist schwieriger — X oder Y? Hilfst du anderen manchmal?

Kommunikative Aufgabe — Communicative task: Studium oder Ausbildung?

With a partner, compare two education or career options. One partner argues for university study; the other argues for vocational training. Use at least two weil or dass clauses, two comparatives, and one superlative. Switch roles and repeat with different options (for example: working part-time while studying vs. full-time vocational training).

Rolle A — Studium

Du findest, dass das Studium besser ist. Erkläre, warum: interessantere Fächer, bessere Karrierechancen, mehr Theorie, internationale Möglichkeiten.

Rolle B — Ausbildung

Du findest, dass die Ausbildung besser passt. Erkläre, warum: praktischer, kürzer, schon Geld verdienen, direkte Berufserfahrung.

Alternative solo — Solo practice

Record yourself for 60 to 90 seconds describing your real or imagined educational path: what you study or have studied, which subject you like most and why, and whether you think that path suits you better than the alternative.

Writing Workshop

In this workshop you will produce a short formal inquiry — either about a university degree programme or about a vocational training position. This genre is common in German-speaking professional and educational contexts.

Model formal email

Muster: Anfrage über einen Studiengang

Sehr geehrte Damen und Herren,

mein Name ist Mia Schneider, und ich interessiere mich für den Studiengang Informatik an Ihrer Universität. Ich hätte gern Informationen über die Zulassungsvoraussetzungen und die Bewerbungsfristen*. Könnten Sie mir bitte weitere Details schicken?

Mit freundlichen Grüßen,
Mia Schneider

* Zulassungsvoraussetzung: admission requirement  ·  Bewerbungsfrist: application deadline

Muster: Anfrage über eine Ausbildungsstelle

Sehr geehrte Damen und Herren,

mein Name ist Jonas Weber. Ich interessiere mich für eine Ausbildung als Mechatroniker in Ihrem Betrieb. Ich finde, dass Ihre Firma sehr gut zu mir passt, weil ich Maschinen und Technik sehr interessant finde. Hätten Sie noch freie Ausbildungsplätze*? Könnten Sie mir bitte mehr Informationen schicken?

Mit freundlichen Grüßen,
Jonas Weber

* Ausbildungsplatz: training position

  1. Analyse the models. Identify in each email: (a) the salutation and closing, (b) every dass or weil clause and its verb position, (c) the polite request formula, and (d) any comparative or superlative form.
  2. Plan your email. Decide: What are you applying or enquiring about? Why does this option interest you? What specific information do you need? Note key phrases before drafting.
  3. Write a first draft of five to seven sentences. Include your name, what you are interested in, at least one reason with weil or dass, a specific request, and the formal closing.
  4. Revise using the editing checklist before writing a final version.

Checkliste — Editing checklist:

  • The salutation ends with a comma, and the next line begins with a capital letter.
  • Every weil or dass clause has the finite verb at the very end.
  • A comma appears before every weil and dass.
  • The formal closing is Mit freundlichen Grüßen, followed by your name on the next line.
  • Every noun is capitalized, and umlauts and ß are correctly written.
  • Formal Sie is used consistently — not du — throughout the email body (note: Ich interessiere mich uses ich, the writer; formal Ihnen / Ihrem refers to the recipient).

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Titles and workplace forms of address

In formal professional and educational contexts across German-speaking countries, written salutations typically use the person's title and surname: Sehr geehrte Frau Dr. Huber, Sehr geehrter Herr Professor Meier. Academic titles such as Dr. and Prof. are commonly used in correspondence in Germany and Austria; Swiss practice is somewhat less uniform. Once established, face-to-face professional relationships vary considerably — some workplaces quickly move to first names and du, while others maintain Sie indefinitely. There is no single universal rule; workplace culture, industry, generation, and personal preference all play a role.

Regional Variation — Education and training systems across the DACH region

Germany, Austria, and Switzerland all operate vocational training systems in which young people divide their time between a training company and a part-time school (in Germany: Berufsschule; in Austria: Berufsschule; in Switzerland: Berufsfachschule). The person doing the training is called der/die Auszubildende (or informally Azubi) in Germany, and der/die Lehrling or Lernende in Austria and Switzerland. University structures also differ: Austria's universities are generally public and have broad access; Germany's higher education landscape includes universities and Fachhochschulen (universities of applied sciences); Switzerland has a distinctive system of federal polytechnic institutes (ETH) alongside cantonal universities. This chapter uses these terms for reading recognition; do not generalise any one country's system to the whole DACH region.

Culture in Context — Occupation and identity

In German-speaking cultures, as in many others, people are sometimes asked about their occupation early in a conversation: Was machen Sie beruflich? or Was studierst du? However, it is important not to equate a person's occupation or educational path with their personal worth or social standing. Both university graduates and skilled tradespeople hold respected and essential roles in German-speaking societies. Your language course should give you the tools to discuss work and study without evaluative judgements.

Regarding occupational naming: German traditionally uses distinct masculine and feminine forms (der Lehrer / die Lehrerin). Contemporary practice also uses gender-inclusive forms, including paired forms (Schülerinnen und Schüler), collective terms (Studierende for students of all genders), and in informal digital writing, forms with a colon or asterisk (Student:innen). This textbook primarily uses paired forms and collective terms where available; specific inclusive conventions are contextual and evolving, and should be checked against current educated usage.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Verb not at the end of the weil clause

Error: *Ich lerne viel, weil ich will Ärztin werden.

Correction: Ich lerne viel, weil ich Ärztin werden will. In a weil clause, the finite verb (here: will) must move to the final position. When there is a modal verb plus infinitive, the infinitive sits just before the modal: werden will.

Common Mistake — Forgetting the comma before weil and dass

Error: *Er sagt dass er müde ist. / *Ich lerne weil die Prüfung schwer ist.

Correction: Er sagt, dass er müde ist. / Ich lerne, weil die Prüfung schwer ist. A comma is required before every subordinating conjunction in German, including weil and dass. This differs from English, where the comma before "because" is usually omitted.

Common Mistake — Forgetting V2 after a fronted weil clause

Error: *Weil die Prüfung schwer ist, ich lerne viel.

Correction: Weil die Prüfung schwer ist, lerne ich viel. When the weil clause appears first, the whole subordinate clause functions as the first constituent of the main clause. The main-clause finite verb must therefore come immediately after the comma, before the subject.

Common Mistake — Adding an ending to a predicate adjective

Error: *Das Fach ist interessantes. / *Die Ausbildung ist praktisches.

Correction: Das Fach ist interessant. / Die Ausbildung ist praktisch. Adjectives that follow a form of sein (or werden) as predicate adjectives carry no ending. Only attributive adjectives — those placed directly before a noun — take endings.

Common Mistake — Using the accusative after gefallen

Error: *Das Fach gefällt mich sehr.

Correction: Das Fach gefällt mir sehr. The verb gefallen takes the dative, not the accusative. The person who finds something pleasing is in the dative; the thing that pleases is the nominative subject. Ask: Wem (to whom) does it please? → mir (to me), not mich (me, accusative).

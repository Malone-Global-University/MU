Im Deutschkurs

In German Class · Speak and Write German: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify common classroom people and objects using the correct nominative article der, die, or das.
  2. Introduce a singular noun with the indefinite articles ein and eine.
  3. Form and recognize plural noun phrases with die.
  4. Conjugate and use the complete present tense of sein (to be).
  5. Ask Was ist das? and answer with Das ist … or Das sind …
  6. Ask and answer yes-or-no questions with Ist das …?
  7. Distinguish wer (who) from was (what) in questions about people and things.
  8. Describe the color of an object using predicate adjectives, without adding endings.
  9. Count from 0 to 20.
  10. Follow basic classroom instructions and ask for repetition or clarification.

Communication Scenario

It is the first full class session at a Volkshochschule (community adult-education center) in Hamburg. Frau Bauer, the instructor, walks the new course participants through the classroom itself — naming objects, asking what things are, and giving the first simple instructions of the course. Before anyone can talk about family, food, or weekend plans, they need the words for the room they are sitting in and the confidence to say "I don't understand" or "Can you repeat that?" This chapter gives you exactly those tools.

  1. Look around the room you are in right now. In English, list as many objects as you can see. Then check your list against the vocabulary in this chapter — how many German equivalents can you already guess, and how many genuinely surprise you?
  2. In Chapter 1, you used only ich bin, du bist, and Sie sind. This chapter completes the picture: er/sie/es, wir, ihr, and sie (they) also need their own forms of sein. Keep an eye out for which forms look alike and which look completely different.

Pronunciation and Spelling

ei versus ie

Two spellings that English speakers frequently confuse are ei and ie. They represent different sounds, and the difference is meaningful.

The spelling ei represents the diphthong /aɪ/, similar to the vowel sound in English eye or my: zwei, Stift — wait, note Stift has no ei; better examples: Bleistift contains no ei either, so use zwei, eins (in the diphthong sense — actually "eins" has "ei"), Seite.

Sound Focus — ei = /aɪ/, ie = /iː/

The spelling ei is pronounced /aɪ/, as in English eye: zwei, eins, Seite. The spelling ie is pronounced as a long, steady /iː/, as in English see but held longer and without a glide: die, vier — note: vier actually contains ie pronounced /iː/, giving [fiːɐ̯].

Practice this contrast with classroom words: die (the, /iː/) versus zwei (two, /aɪ/); vier (four, /iː/) versus eins (one, /aɪ/, in the diphthong portion); Papier (paper, final syllable /iːɐ̯/).

A simple memory aid: ie says its own name in English ("ee"), while ei says the opposite of what it looks like ("eye," not "ay"). The aid is useful but approximate — rely on practice, not the rhyme alone.

eu and äu

The spellings eu and äu both represent the same diphthong, /ɔɪ/, similar to English oy in boy. You will meet this sound in words such as neun (nine) and, later in the course, in umlauted plural forms such as Häuser (houses).

sch

The three-letter combination sch represents a single sound, /ʃ/, like English sh in shoe: Tisch, Tasche, schwarz. Always pronounce sch as one unit — do not pronounce the s and ch separately.

Final consonants and word-final devoicing

German devoices certain consonants at the end of a word or syllable, even though the spelling does not change. The word Stift ends in a clearly voiceless t, but a related pattern becomes more noticeable with words such as Bild (picture) in later chapters, where a written d is pronounced as [t] at the end of the word. For now, simply notice that German final consonants are often pronounced crisply and without added voicing, unlike some English patterns. You will study this systematically in a later chapter; do not try to respell words based on their pronunciation.

Compound-word stress

Many of this chapter's nouns are compounds: Bleistift (Blei + Stift, literally "lead pen") and Klassenzimmer (Klasse + Zimmer, "class room"). In a German compound, the main stress almost always falls on the first component: BLEIstift, KLASsenzimmer. This is a reliable pattern you can apply to new compounds throughout the course.

Sound Focus — Stressing compounds correctly

Practice stressing the first element of each compound: BLEIstift, KLASsenzimmer. If you stress the second element instead (bleiSTIFT), the word remains understandable but sounds noticeably non-native. This first-element stress pattern will help you pronounce hundreds of German compounds correctly throughout this course, since German forms new compound nouns very freely.

Grammar Instruction

2.1 Grammatical gender: der, die, das

Purpose: identify and use the correct nominative definite article for any German noun.

Every German noun belongs to one of three grammatical genders: masculine (der), feminine (die), or neuter (das). Grammatical gender is a property of the word itself and generally cannot be predicted from the real-world meaning of the noun — das Mädchen (the girl) is grammatically neuter despite referring to a female person, because it is formed with the neuter suffix -chen. Because gender is largely unpredictable, you must learn every noun together with its article, from your very first vocabulary list onward.

Maskulin (der)Feminin (die)Neutrum (das)
der Tischdie Tafeldas Buch
der Stuhldie Türdas Heft
der Stiftdie Taschedas Fenster
der Computerdie Uhrdas Handy

A small number of endings give a strong (though not absolute) hint about gender: nouns ending in -e are very often feminine (die Tasche, die Lampe, die Seite), and nouns ending in -chen or -lein are always neuter. Treat these as helpful tendencies, not guarantees, and continue learning each noun's article individually.

2.2 Nominative indefinite articles: ein and eine

Purpose: introduce a single, unspecified noun for the first time.

Use ein before masculine and neuter nouns, and eine before feminine nouns, to mean "a" or "an."

GenderDefiniteIndefiniteBeispiel
Maskulinder Stiftein StiftDas ist ein Stift.
Feminindie Tascheeine TascheDas ist eine Tasche.
Neutrumdas Buchein BuchDas ist ein Buch.

German has no indefinite article for the plural. To say "books" without specifying which ones, simply use the bare plural noun with no article: Das sind Bücher. You will compare this with the negative article kein in a later chapter.

2.3 The plural article: die

Purpose: refer to more than one of any noun, regardless of its singular gender.

In the plural, German uses a single definite article for all nouns: die. The distinction among der, die, and das exists only in the singular.

  • der Stuhl → die Stühle the chair → the chairs
  • das Buch → die Bücher the book → the books
  • die Tasche → die Taschen the bag → the bags

German plural formation is not governed by one default ending. Some nouns add -e, some add -er (often with an umlaut), some add -en or -n, some add -s (mainly recent loanwords such as Handys), and a few do not change at all. Record the plural with every noun rather than trying to derive it from a rule.

2.4 The verb sein — complete present tense

Purpose: use sein (to be) in every person to identify, describe, and ask about people and things.

In Chapter 1, you used ich bin, du bist, and formal Sie sind. This chapter completes the paradigm. Like English "to be," sein is highly irregular, so each form must be learned individually.

PronomenFormEnglish
ichbinI am
dubistyou are (informal)
er / sie / esisthe / she / it is
wirsindwe are
ihrseidyou are (informal plural)
siesindthey are
Siesindyou are (formal, singular or plural)

Notice that wir sind, sie sind, and formal Sie sind all share the identical form sind; only the pronoun (and, in writing, capitalization for formal Sie) tells you which subject is meant. Ihr seid is the one form that looks distinct from all the others — learn it as a separate item rather than confusing it with sie sind.

  • Ich bin Teilnehmerin im Deutschkurs. I am a participant in the German course.
  • Du bist mein Partner heute. You are my partner today.
  • Er ist der Lehrer. He is the teacher.
  • Wir sind im Klassenzimmer. We are in the classroom.
  • Ihr seid sehr ruhig heute. You all are very quiet today.
  • Sie sind neue Teilnehmer. They are new participants.
Grammar Note — sind appears four times

Look closely at the table above: the form sind serves wir, sie (they), and formal Sie — three different subjects, one identical verb form. This is completely normal in German and is not a sign that you have made an error. Context, the subject pronoun, and capitalization (for formal Sie in writing) tell the listener or reader which subject is meant. Always include the subject pronoun, just as you learned with ich bin in Chapter 1.

2.5 Das ist … / Das sind … / Ist das …?

Purpose: identify a single object or several objects, and ask whether something is a particular thing.

Use Das ist … to identify one person or thing, and Das sind … to identify more than one. The word das here does not change for gender — it simply points to whatever is being identified, regardless of that noun's own gender.

Language Building Block
Das ist + ein/eine/Ø + noun (singular). Das sind + noun (plural).
Das ist = identifies one item · Das sind = identifies more than one item
Das ist ein Stift. Das sind Stifte.
That is a pen. Those are pens.
  • Das ist ein Buch. That is a book.
  • Das ist die Lehrerin. That is the teacher.
  • Das sind Stühle. Those are chairs.
  • Ist das ein Computer? Is that a computer?
  • Ist das die Tafel? Is that the board?

Notice that Ist das …? places the finite verb first, exactly as you would expect from a yes-or-no question. You will study verb position formally in Chapter 3; for now, simply use this formula as shown.

2.6 Predicate adjectives

Purpose: describe a noun's color or quality using sein, without adding any ending to the adjective.

When an adjective follows a form of sein and describes the subject, it is called a predicate adjective. Unlike English, German predicate adjectives never change form to match the gender or number of the noun.

  • Der Stuhl ist rot. The chair is red.
  • Die Tafel ist grün. The board is green.
  • Das Heft ist blau. The notebook is blue.
  • Die Stühle sind rot. The chairs are red. (no plural ending on "rot")

Compare this with English, where the adjective also does not change ("the chair is red," "the chairs are red") — so this pattern should feel familiar. Later in the course, you will learn that adjectives placed directly before a noun (der rote Stuhl) do take an ending; that is a different position and a different rule, introduced in Chapter 9.

2.7 wer and was

Purpose: ask about the identity of a person (wer) or a thing (was).

  • Wer ist das? — Das ist Frau Bauer. Who is that? — That is Frau Bauer.
  • Wer ist der Lehrer? — Herr Krause ist der Lehrer. Who is the teacher? — Mr. Krause is the teacher.
  • Was ist das? — Das ist ein Heft. What is that? — That is a notebook.
  • Was sind das? — Das sind Bücher. What are those? — Those are books.

Use wer only for people and was only for things. Both words place the finite verb directly after them, as you will study formally with W-questions in Chapter 3.

Model Dialogue

Kontext: Der erste Kurstag an einer Volkshochschule in Hamburg. Frau Bauer, die Lehrerin, begrüßt die Teilnehmer Lukas und Mira. Frau Bauer und die Teilnehmer benutzen Sie; Lukas und Mira, die beide neu im Kurs sind und im gleichen Alter sind, benutzen du miteinander.
Setting: The first day of class at a Volkshochschule in Hamburg. Frau Bauer, the teacher, welcomes participants Lukas and Mira. Frau Bauer and the participants use Sie; Lukas and Mira, both new to the course and similar in age, use du with each other.

FRAU BAUER Guten Tag! Ich bin Frau Bauer, Ihre Lehrerin. Willkommen im Deutschkurs. Good day! I am Frau Bauer, your teacher. Welcome to the German course.
LUKAS Guten Tag, Frau Bauer. Good day, Frau Bauer.
FRAU BAUER Gut, fangen wir an. Was ist das? (zeigt auf einen Stift) Good, let's begin. What is that? (points to a pen)
MIRA Das ist ein Stift. That is a pen.
FRAU BAUER Richtig! Und ist der Stift rot? Correct! And is the pen red?
MIRA Nein, er ist blau. No, it is blue.
FRAU BAUER Sehr gut. Lukas, was sind das? (zeigt auf die Bücher) Very good. Lukas, what are those? (points to the books)
LUKAS Wie bitte? Können Sie das wiederholen? Pardon? Can you repeat that?
FRAU BAUER Natürlich. Was sind das? (zeigt noch einmal) Of course. What are those? (points again)
LUKAS Ah, danke. Das sind Bücher. Ah, thank you. Those are books.
FRAU BAUER Genau. Lukas und Mira, ihr seid heute Partner. Arbeiten Sie zusammen — wie viele Stühle, Tische und Fenster sind im Klassenzimmer? Exactly. Lukas and Mira, you two are partners today. Work together — how many chairs, tables, and windows are in the classroom?
MIRA Okay! Lukas, wie viele Fenster siehst du? Okay! Lukas, how many windows do you see?
LUKAS Ich sehe drei Fenster. Und du? Wie viele Stühle siehst du? I see three windows. And you? How many chairs do you see?
MIRA Ich sehe zwanzig Stühle — wir sind ein großer Kurs! I see twenty chairs — we are a big course!

Verständnisfragen — Comprehension questions:

  1. Wer ist Frau Bauer? (Who is Frau Bauer?)
  2. Welche Farbe hat der Stift? (What color is the pen?)
  3. Warum sagt Lukas "Wie bitte?"? (Why does Lukas say "Wie bitte?")
  4. Wie viele Fenster und Stühle sehen Lukas und Mira? (How many windows and chairs do Lukas and Mira see?)
  5. Finden Sie im Dialog ein Beispiel für Sie und ein Beispiel für du. Wer benutzt welche Form? (Find one example each of Sie and du in the dialogue. Who uses which form?)
Grammar Note — wie viele

The dialogue uses wie viele ("how many") before a plural noun: wie viele Fenster, wie viele Stühle. This is a fixed, formulaic combination at this stage — treat it as a useful chunk meaning "how many," and use it with the nouns you already know. You will study question words and word order more fully in Chapter 3.

Reading Note — The Volkshochschule

The Volkshochschule (often abbreviated VHS) is a widespread type of community adult-education center found throughout Germany, with similar institutions in Austria and German-speaking Switzerland under related names. These centers offer affordable language, vocational, and general-interest courses to adults of all backgrounds and ages. A German class at a Volkshochschule typically brings together learners from many countries and language backgrounds — exactly the kind of multilingual classroom you will read about throughout this course.

Writing Workshop

In this chapter, you will write a short description of your own study space — the room, desk, or area where you do your German homework. This mirrors the kind of short, descriptive text you read above about the Volkshochschule classroom.

  1. Analyze a model. Reread "Unser Kursraum." Identify: (a) how the writer states what the room contains, (b) how colors are used as predicate adjectives, (c) which articles are used with which nouns, and (d) one personal detail that makes the text feel real. Your own text should follow a similar structure.
  2. Plan your description. Before writing, list in notes: three or four objects in your study space, the color of each object, and one personal detail (where you sit, what is nearby, who studies with you, and so on).
  3. Write your first draft. Use your planning notes to write four to six sentences. Include at least three nouns with correct articles, at least two predicate-adjective color descriptions, and one sentence using a conjugated form of sein other than ist.
  4. Revise. Check your draft against the editing checklist before writing a final version.

Checkliste — Editing checklist:

  • Every noun has the article I intended (der, die, or das), and I checked uncertain genders against the vocabulary list.
  • Every noun begins with a capital letter.
  • I used the correct plural article (die) for any plural nouns.
  • Predicate adjectives (colors) have no added ending.
  • I used at least one form of sein other than ist (for example, sind, bin, or sind for "they").
  • Umlauts (ä, ö, ü) and ß are correctly written, not omitted.
  • My description includes one personal, specific detail.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Forms of address with instructors

In most German-language adult-education settings — Volkshochschule courses, university extension programs, professional training — participants use Sie with the instructor, at least at first, even when participants use du with one another. Some private language schools and many youth or university-student contexts use du throughout, including with the instructor, especially in informal or conversation-focused courses. There is no single universal rule: institutional culture, the instructor's own preference, and the type of course all play a role. When in doubt, use Sie with an instructor and follow their lead if they invite a different form of address.

Culture in Context — Multilingual classrooms

A German course at a Volkshochschule, a university, or a private school typically brings together adults from many countries and language backgrounds, alongside German-speaking participants preparing for further study or work. It is common for a single classroom to include speakers of Arabic, Turkish, Polish, English, Ukrainian, Spanish, and many other first languages, all using German as a shared classroom language. This diversity is typical of language classrooms across Germany, Austria, and German-speaking Switzerland, not an exception.

Regional Variation — Naming classroom furniture

This course uses der Tisch for any table or desk, which is broadly understood everywhere German is spoken. In Swiss schools, however, a student's individual desk is often called das Pult rather than der Tisch, and Austrian educational vocabulary includes some institution-specific terms of its own. These are labeled regional alternatives for recognition; the productive form for this course remains der Tisch.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Guessing gender from meaning

Error: Assuming das Tafel or der Buch because the English meaning seems "object-like" or "neutral."

Correction: die Tafel; das Buch. German grammatical gender is a property of the word, not of the object's real-world qualities. There is no reliable way to predict gender from English meaning alone. Learn every noun with its article from the start, and check unfamiliar nouns in a dictionary rather than guessing.

Common Mistake — Adding an ending to a predicate adjective

Error: *Das Buch ist rotes. / *Die Stühle sind schwarze.

Correction: Das Buch ist rot. / Die Stühle sind schwarz. Predicate adjectives — adjectives that follow sein and describe the subject — never take an ending in German, regardless of the noun's gender or number. Endings appear only when an adjective stands directly before a noun, a pattern introduced later in the course.

Common Mistake — Confusing ihr seid and sie sind

Error: *Ihr sind meine Partner. / *Sie seid neue Teilnehmer.

Correction: Ihr seid meine Partner. / Sie sind neue Teilnehmer. Ihr (informal "you all") always takes seid; sie ("they") and formal Sie ("you") both take sind. These are two different pronouns with two different verb forms — do not mix them.

Common Mistake — Forgetting noun capitalization

Error: *Das ist ein buch. Die tafel ist grün.

Correction: Das ist ein Buch. Die Tafel ist grün. Every German noun is capitalized, in every position in the sentence — not only at the beginning. This applies to common nouns like Buch and Tafel just as much as to names. Check every noun in your writing, not only the first word of each sentence.

Common Mistake — Confusing wer and was

Error: *Was ist die Lehrerin? (intending "Who is the teacher?")

Correction: Wer ist die Lehrerin? Use wer only when asking about a person, and was only when asking about a thing. The two words are never interchangeable.

Chapter Performance Task

Leistungsaufgabe

Unser Klassenzimmer — Our classroom

Working in groups of three or four, create a labeled diagram or poster of your own classroom (or a classroom you invent together). Label at least ten objects with their correct definite article, and note the color of at least five objects using predicate adjectives. Count and record how many of each object the room contains.

Each group then presents its poster to the class, using complete sentences:

  • Identify at least five objects: Das ist … / Das sind …
  • State the color of at least three objects: Der/Die/Das … ist …
  • State a count using a number from 0–20: Wir haben … Stühle. (recognition use of haben is acceptable here)
  • Ask the other groups one yes-or-no question: Ist der/die/das … (Farbe)?

After each presentation, the audience may ask one follow-up question using Wer …?, Was …?, or Wie viele …?

  • die Stühle: schwarz

Listening — Richtig oder falsch

  1. Falsch. Der Tisch ist braun, nicht rot.
  2. Richtig. Der Sprecher sagt "Hier sind zwei Stühle."
  3. Richtig. Das Klassenzimmer hat fünf Fenster.
  4. Falsch. Die Tür ist weiß, nicht schwarz.

Reading — Comprehension

Looking Ahead

Was Sie jetzt können — What you can do now

You can now identify classroom people and objects with their correct articles, count to 20, and use the complete present tense of sein to describe people and things. You can ask Was ist das?, Ist das …?, Wer …?, and Was …?, and you can describe color with predicate adjectives. These nominative noun phrases and the verb sein will return in every chapter that follows.

Kapitel 3: Woher kommst du? — Origin and Languages

Chapter 3 introduces the present tense of regular verbs such as kommen, wohnen, and sprechen, along with W-questions and basic verb-second word order. You will learn to ask and answer where someone is from, where they live, and what languages they speak — and you will exchange a phone number and an email address for the first time.

Abschlussprojekt: Auf Deutsch handeln

Final A2 Assessment · Speak and Write German: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

Chapter 20 does not introduce new grammar. Instead, it asks you to bring together everything you have built across Volume I — greetings and identity, home and routine, food and shopping, directions and plans, health and work, and narration, comparison, and connected storytelling — in one integrated, portfolio-style project. The project spans four domains that have organized your learning since Chapter 1: the personal, the practical, the community, and the narrative.

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Present yourself and your social world using controlled case, gender, and article accuracy.
  2. Handle a practical everyday task — home, food, shopping, or time — with natural German phrasing.
  3. Navigate a community interaction, such as asking directions, making an appointment, or discussing study or work, with appropriate register.
  4. Narrate a connected account of past events using the Perfekt, war/hatte, and sequencing words.
  5. Combine a reason clause (weil) and a report clause (dass) accurately within a longer text.
  6. Choose du, ihr, or Sie appropriately across each portfolio task.
  7. Read and respond to practical, mixed-genre texts drawn from across the volume's domains.
  8. Revise your own German for spelling, capitalization, case, verb position, and register.

Opening Scenario

Mia's German course is coming to an end for the term. Her instructor asks each learner to prepare a short personal portfolio in German — something that shows who they are, how they manage everyday life, how they interact with their community, and how they can tell a connected story. Mia decides to build her portfolio around the last few weeks: her family and course, her weekday routine, a trip she is planning with Lukas and Anna, and the message exchange from Chapter 19. You will build a portfolio of your own, using the same four domains.

  1. Before you begin, skim back through Chapters 1–19 in your notes or in the chapter you have already completed. You do not need to reread everything — just remind yourself of the vocabulary and structures each unit developed.
  2. This chapter's practice items and the final performance task both draw on that cumulative range. Nothing here requires a structure you have not already met.

Baustein 20.1

Personal Domain — Wer ich bin

Review — What This Domain Recycles

Identity, family, and description

The personal domain draws on Chapters 1–5: greetings and leave-taking, stating your name and origin, describing your family and social network, and using predicate adjectives to describe people. Address choice (du versus Sie) is central here, since how you introduce yourself depends on the audience.

  • sein and haben in identity and possession statements
  • regular present-tense verbs: kommen aus, wohnen in, sprechen, lernen, arbeiten
  • possessive determiners mein/dein/Ihr
  • predicate adjectives without endings

Mustertext — Mias persönliches Profil

Hallo! Ich heiße Mia. Ich komme aus Düsseldorf und wohne jetzt auch dort. Ich spreche Deutsch und ein bisschen Englisch. Ich studiere Biologie und arbeite auch teilzeit in einem Café.

Meine Familie ist nicht sehr groß. Ich habe eine Schwester, Lea, und einen Bruder, Tom. Meine Oma heißt Trude und wohnt in der Nähe. Meine beste Freundin ist Anna; wir lernen zusammen Deutsch. Mein Freund Lukas macht gerade ein Praktikum in Freiburg.

Practice A — Your Personal Profile

Write four to six sentences introducing yourself: name, origin, residence, languages, studies or work, and one or two family or social relationships. Use at least two predicate adjectives.

Baustein 20.2

Practical Domain — Mein Alltag

Review — What This Domain Recycles

Home, routine, food, and shopping

The practical domain draws on Chapters 6–10: describing home and neighborhood with dative location formulas, stating a daily routine with verb-second order and time expressions, talking about food and drink preferences with möchten, and comparing products while shopping.

  • dative in fixed location phrases: in, auf, bei
  • time expressions and negation with nicht
  • möchten, gern/lieber/am liebsten
  • accusative direct objects with indefinite articles

Mustertext — Ein normaler Tag

Ich wohne in einer kleinen Wohnung in der Stadt. In meinem Zimmer gibt es ein Bett, einen Schreibtisch und viele Bücher. Die Küche ist klein, aber praktisch.

Ich stehe um sieben Uhr auf und trinke Kaffee. Um neun Uhr habe ich eine Vorlesung, und danach arbeite ich im Café. Am Abend koche ich gern, aber am liebsten esse ich Pasta. Am Wochenende gehe ich einkaufen und kaufe frisches Gemüse und Brot.

Practice B — A Practical Task

Choose one: describe your home and neighborhood, describe your weekday routine, or write a short shopping/ordering exchange. Aim for six to eight sentences with at least one time expression and one dative location phrase.

Baustein 20.3

Community Domain — In der Gemeinschaft

Review — What This Domain Recycles

Directions, plans, health, study, and work

The community domain draws on Chapters 11–15: asking for and giving directions, negotiating leisure plans with modal verb brackets, describing symptoms and requesting help, and discussing study or work responsibilities.

  • modal verbs können, wollen, müssen, sollen, dürfen in the verb bracket
  • separable verbs such as einsteigen, aufstehen, aussteigen
  • dative experiencer formulas: mir tut ... weh
  • register and address choice in service and institutional settings

Mustertext — Eine Reiseplanung

Lukas, Anna und ich wollen im Sommer zusammen nach Freiburg fahren, weil Lukas dort sein Praktikum macht. Wir müssen zuerst Zugtickets kaufen und ein Hostel finden. Ich kann am Bahnhof helfen, weil ich die Fahrpläne schon kenne.

Letzte Woche hatte ich Kopfschmerzen und musste zum Arzt gehen. Zum Glück war es nicht schlimm. Jetzt geht es mir wieder gut, und ich freue mich auf die Reise.

Practice C — A Community Interaction

Write a short exchange or description involving directions, a plan, a health situation, or a study/work responsibility. Include at least one modal verb bracket and one separable verb.

Baustein 20.4

Narrative Domain — Geschichten erzählen

Review — What This Domain Recycles

Narration, comparison, reasons, and reports

The narrative domain draws on Chapters 16–19: narrating completed events with the Perfekt, comparing past and present with war/hatte and früher/heute, giving reasons with weil, and reporting what someone said with dass. This is the domain where the sentence bracket does the most work — auxiliary, participle, and finite verb all move according to clause type.

  • Perfekt with haben and sein
  • war/hatte and comparison with früher, heute, jetzt
  • verb-final order in weil- and dass-clauses
  • sequencing with zuerst, dann, danach

Mustertext — Ein Rückblick

Letztes Wochenende bin ich mit Anna in die Stadt gefahren. Zuerst haben wir gefrühstückt, dann sind wir spazieren gegangen, und danach haben wir Kaffee getrunken. Früher bin ich selten in die Stadt gefahren, weil ich keine Zeit hatte. Jetzt mache ich das gern, weil es mir Spaß macht.

Anna hat mir erzählt, dass sie im nächsten Sommer nach Berlin ziehen möchte. Ich glaube, dass das eine gute Idee ist, weil sie dort studieren kann.

Cumulative Bracket Review
weil wir letztes Jahr zusammen gereist sind
"because we traveled together last year" — Perfekt participle plus finite sein at the very end of a weil-clause.

Practice D — A Connected Narrative

Write six to eight sentences narrating a past event or comparing past and present. Include at least one weil-clause and one dass-clause.

Model Dialogue

Below, Mia presents part of her portfolio orally to her instructor. Notice how she moves smoothly among the four domains within a single short conversation.

Mia stellt ihr Portfolio vor.

Lehrerin: Guten Tag, Mia. Erzählen Sie mir bitte ein bisschen über sich. Hello, Mia. Please tell me a little about yourself.
Mia: Gern! Ich heiße Mia, ich komme aus Düsseldorf und studiere Biologie. Ich wohne mit meiner Familie in der Nähe der Universität. Gladly! My name is Mia, I'm from Düsseldorf, and I study biology. I live near the university with my family.
Lehrerin: Und wie sieht ein normaler Tag für Sie aus? And what does a normal day look like for you?
Mia: Ich stehe früh auf und gehe zur Uni. Am Nachmittag arbeite ich oft im Café, weil ich das Geld für Reisen brauche. I get up early and go to the university. In the afternoon I often work at the café, because I need the money for travel.
Lehrerin: Haben Sie kürzlich etwas Besonderes gemacht? Have you done anything special recently?
Mia: Ja, letztes Wochenende bin ich mit meiner Freundin Anna in die Stadt gefahren. Wir haben gefrühstückt, sind spazieren gegangen und haben Kaffee getrunken. Anna hat mir gesagt, dass sie im Sommer nach Berlin ziehen möchte. Yes, last weekend I went into town with my friend Anna. We had breakfast, went for a walk, and drank coffee. Anna told me she wants to move to Berlin in the summer.
Lehrerin: Das klingt spannend! Und haben Sie schon Pläne für den Sommer? That sounds exciting! And do you already have plans for the summer?
Mia: Ja, wir wollen zusammen nach Freiburg fahren, weil mein Freund Lukas dort ein Praktikum macht. Wir müssen aber zuerst Tickets kaufen. Yes, we want to travel to Freiburg together, because my friend Lukas is doing an internship there. But we first have to buy tickets.

Comprehension — Model Dialogue

1. Woher kommt Mia, und was studiert sie?

2. Warum arbeitet Mia am Nachmittag im Café?

3. Was hat Mia letztes Wochenende gemacht?

4. Was hat Anna Mia erzählt?

5. Identify one example from each of the four portfolio domains (personal, practical, community, narrative) within this dialogue.

Ask your instructor, a learning partner, or a text-to-speech tool to read the following portfolio presentation at a comfortable but natural pace. Listen once for the general picture, then again to identify each of the four domains.

Lukas' Portfoliovorstellung (Listening Script)

Hallo, ich heiße Lukas. Ich komme aus Düsseldorf, aber ich bin gerade in Freiburg, weil ich hier ein Praktikum mache. Ich habe eine große Familie: zwei Brüder und eine Schwester.

Hier in Freiburg wohne ich in einem kleinen Zimmer in der Nähe vom Zentrum. Jeden Morgen stehe ich um sechs Uhr auf, weil die Arbeit um acht Uhr beginnt. Am Mittag esse ich meistens in der Kantine.

Annas Portfolio

Ich heiße Anna und komme aus Köln. Ich lerne seit einem Jahr Deutsch und studiere Geschichte. Meine Familie wohnt noch in Köln, aber ich wohne jetzt mit einer Mitbewohnerin in Düsseldorf.

Unsere Wohnung ist klein, aber gemütlich. Jeden Tag stehe ich um halb acht auf, esse ein Brötchen und fahre mit dem Bus zur Uni. Am Nachmittag lerne ich oft mit Mia, weil wir denselben Deutschkurs haben.

2. Wie sieht Annas normaler Tag aus?

3. Welches Problem hatte Anna letzten Monat, und wie hat sie es gelöst?

5. Wie hat sich Annas Gefühl gegenüber großen Städten verändert?

Sprechen

Speaking Practice

The speaking activities in this chapter rehearse the interview format you will use for the performance task's interpersonal component.

Aktivität 1: Portfolio-Interview

Work with a partner. Partner A plays the instructor and asks four questions, one from each domain (personal, practical, community, narrative). Partner B answers in complete sentences. Then switch roles.

Partner A — Interviewer

Ask questions such as:

  • Erzählen Sie mir etwas über sich und Ihre Familie.
  • Wie sieht Ihr Alltag aus?
  • Was machen Sie, wenn Sie krank sind oder Hilfe brauchen?
  • Was haben Sie letztes Wochenende gemacht?

Partner B — Responding

Answer each question with at least two connected sentences. Aim to include:

  • at least one weil-clause
  • at least one Perfekt or war/hatte form
  • appropriate du/Sie agreement with your partner's questions

Solo alternative: Record a two-minute self-interview in which you ask and answer one question from each domain.

Aktivität 2: Geschichten austauschen

In a small group, each person tells a short connected story (30–60 seconds) combining at least two domains — for example, a practical task that led to a community interaction, or a personal detail connected to a past event. Listeners ask one follow-up question each.

Bewertungshinweise — Speaking Criteria

  • Comprehensible, connected sentences across at least two domains.
  • Appropriate du/Sie/ihr choice for the situation.
  • Correct verb-second order in main clauses and verb-final order in weil/dass-clauses.
  • Stable noun gender, article, and case use in familiar vocabulary.
  • Ability to answer a follow-up question without long hesitation.
Schreiben

Writing Workshop

This workshop guides you through drafting the written portion of your final portfolio, which you will complete as part of the performance task below.

  1. Plan each domain. Jot down two or three notes for the personal, practical, community, and narrative sections, using the roadmap table above.
  2. Draft the personal and practical sections first. These use your most automatic vocabulary and structures.
  3. Draft the community section, including at least one modal verb bracket.
  4. Draft the narrative section, including the Perfekt or war/hatte, at least one weil-clause, and at least one dass-clause.
  5. Connect the sections with simple transitions so the portfolio reads as one coherent text rather than four separate lists.
  6. Revise using the checklist below before producing your final version.
Personal

Suggested Content

Name, origin, residence, languages, studies/work, family or social network, one or two descriptive adjectives.

Practical

Suggested Content

Home or neighborhood, daily routine with times, a food/shopping preference or habit.

Community

Suggested Content

A plan, a direction-giving moment, a health situation, or a study/work responsibility, with a modal verb.

Narrative

Suggested Content

A past event, a past/present comparison, one reason (weil) and one report (dass).

Checkliste — Editing Checklist:

  • All four domains (personal, practical, community, narrative) are represented.
  • At least one modal verb bracket appears somewhere in the text.
  • At least one weil-clause and one dass-clause appear, each with correct verb-final order and a comma.
  • At least one Perfekt or war/hatte form narrates a past event.
  • Verb-second order is consistent in every main clause.
  • Noun gender, articles, and case marking are consistent with the nouns used.
  • du, ihr, and Sie are used consistently and appropriately throughout.
  • All nouns are capitalized; umlauts and ß are used correctly.
  • The portfolio reads as one connected text that a sympathetic reader can follow from start to finish.

Pronunciation Focus

Reading your portfolio aloud

As you prepare to present your portfolio aloud, review the priorities from across the volume: long and short vowel contrasts, umlauted vowels (ä, ö, ü), final devoicing at the end of words like Tag and Freund, and the ich-Laut and ach-Laut distinction in words like ich and auch.

Pay special attention to compound and word stress in longer nouns you have accumulated, such as Studientreffen, Arzttermin, and Deutschkurs, and to sentence-level intonation across weil- and dass-clauses, where the voice should not fall until the finite verb at the very end.

Self-check: recording your own voice

Record yourself reading your written portfolio aloud. Listen back once for overall intelligibility and once specifically for final devoicing and sentence-final verb placement in subordinate clauses. This kind of self-monitoring is a habit worth carrying into Volume II.

Culture and Communication

Culture and Communication — Ein Portfolio als Spiegel

A personal portfolio like the one in this chapter reflects a real communicative skill: presenting yourself and your life coherently to another person, whether in a classroom, a new workplace, or a new community. Across German-speaking regions, this kind of self-presentation is common in educational and professional settings, though the expected level of formality, detail, and personal disclosure varies by institution, relationship, and individual preference.

You are never required to disclose real personal details. Fictionalizing parts of your portfolio, as Mia, Lukas, and Anna do throughout this book, is a normal and acceptable way to practice the language.

Regional Note — DACH Awareness Across the Volume

Throughout Volume I, vocabulary, institutions, and pronunciation notes have been labeled when they varied across Germany, Austria, Switzerland, Liechtenstein, and Luxembourg. As you finish the volume, remember that no single country represents German as a whole, and that the standard German you have learned is one broadly usable, educational model among many legitimate ways of speaking German.

Common Mistakes to Review

The errors below are drawn from across the volume. Review each one before completing the performance task; none of them introduces new material.

Review — Verb-second order in main clauses

Error: *Am Wochenende ich fahre in die Stadt.

Correction: Am Wochenende fahre ich in die Stadt. When a time or place element opens the sentence, the finite verb still comes second, and the subject moves after it.

Review — Verb-final order after weil and dass

Error: *Ich bin müde, weil ich habe schlecht geschlafen.

Correction: Ich bin müde, weil ich schlecht geschlafen habe. The finite verb moves to the very end of a weil- or dass-clause.

Review — Noun gender and article agreement

Error: *Der Wohnung ist klein.

Correction: Die Wohnung ist klein. Noun gender is lexical and must be learned with each noun; it cannot be guessed from meaning.

Review — Auxiliary choice in the Perfekt

Error: *Ich habe in die Stadt gefahren.

Correction: Ich bin in die Stadt gefahren. Motion and change-of-state verbs like fahren take sein, not haben, in the Perfekt.

Review — du/Sie inconsistency

Error: *Kannst du mir helfen? ... Vielen Dank, dass Sie geantwortet haben.

Correction: Choose one address form for a given relationship and keep it consistent within the same exchange, unless the relationship itself changes over the course of the interaction.

Performance Task

Kapitel 20 — Abschlussprojekt

Auf Deutsch handeln — Ein persönliches Portfolio

This is the final performance assessment for Volume I. It integrates listening, speaking, reading, writing, and mediation across the personal, practical, community, and narrative domains. No new grammar is required; the task draws entirely on systems activated in Chapters 1–19. Complete all four components. They may be spread across two or three class sessions.

Component 1 — Written portfolio (presentational): Write a connected personal portfolio of ten to fourteen sentences covering all four domains: personal, practical, community, and narrative. Include at least one modal verb bracket, one weil-clause, one dass-clause, and one Perfekt or war/hatte form. Use the Writing Workshop checklist to revise before submitting.

Component 2 — Spoken presentation (presentational): Present your portfolio aloud to a partner, an instructor, or a recording, without reading it word for word. Use appropriate pacing and sentence-final intonation in subordinate clauses.

Component 3 — Portfolio interview (interpersonal): A partner or instructor asks you at least four follow-up questions, one from each domain, including at least one Warum ...? question. Answer in complete, connected sentences. Then reverse roles if working with a partner.

Component 4 — Reading and mediation (interpretive): Read a short mixed-genre text provided by your instructor (or one of the reading passages in this chapter) and summarize its main content in English for someone who does not read German, without translating word for word.

CriterionWhat to look for
Domain coverageAll four domains are clearly represented and connected
Verb positionConsistent verb-second order in main clauses and verb-final order in subordinate clauses
Case, gender, and article accuracyStable use of nominative, accusative, and controlled dative forms with familiar vocabulary
Narrative controlAccurate Perfekt or war/hatte use, correct auxiliary selection, and clear sequencing
Reason and report clausesCorrect, comma-marked weil- and dass-clauses
Register and addressConsistent and appropriate du/ihr/Sie choice throughout
ComprehensibilityA sympathetic listener or reader can follow the whole portfolio without significant difficulty
MediationAn accurate, natural English summary that reflects the German text's content

The standard for Volume I mastery is a connected, comprehensible portfolio that draws confidently on the full range of systems activated across Chapters 1–19, with reliable verb position, reasonably accurate case and gender, and appropriate register throughout. Occasional errors in article choice or word order are expected at this stage and do not prevent successful completion of the capstone. Systematic breakdowns in verb-second order, verb-final order, or basic comprehensibility should be reviewed before beginning Volume II.

Looking Ahead: Volume II

Was Sie nach Kapitel 20 können — What you can do after Chapter 20

Having completed Volume I, you can introduce yourself and your social world, manage practical everyday tasks, interact within a community across a range of settings, and narrate connected past events while giving reasons and reporting what others have said. You can move between du, ihr, and Sie appropriately, and you have a working sense of German's core architecture: verb-second main clauses, verb-final subordinate clauses, and the sentence bracket that holds modals, separable verbs, and the Perfekt together.

Volume II: Everyday Independence and Connected Communication

Volume II builds directly on this foundation. It will expand your case control — particularly the dative and its two-way prepositions — develop the full range of subordinate conjunctions beyond weil and dass, introduce adjective endings in attributive noun phrases, and move you toward fuller independence in everyday service, travel, housing, medical, educational, and workplace communication.

Before beginning Volume II, make sure you feel confident with:

  • verb-second order in main clauses as an automatic habit;
  • verb-final order in weil- and dass-clauses;
  • noun gender and article use with familiar vocabulary;
  • the Perfekt, including correct auxiliary selection; and
  • appropriate du/ihr/Sie choice across everyday situations.

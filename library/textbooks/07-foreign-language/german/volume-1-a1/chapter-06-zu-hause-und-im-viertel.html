Zu Hause und im Viertel

Home and Neighborhood · Speak and Write German: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Name the rooms of a home and common pieces of furniture, using the correct article and plural form for each noun.
  2. State what a home or neighborhood contains using es gibt with the correct accusative article.
  3. Describe where an object is located using the position verbs stehen, liegen, and hängen.
  4. Use nine location prepositions — in, an, auf, neben, vor, hinter, über, unter, and zwischen — with the dative article to describe static position.
  5. Apply the contractions im (in + dem) and am (an + dem) correctly.
  6. Form and answer Wo questions about the location of objects and neighborhood services.
  7. Describe a home and neighborhood using predicate adjectives you know.
  8. Understand a simple housing description and extract key details about rooms and location.

Communication Scenario

Sofia has just moved into her own apartment in Hamburg — she found it through a tip from Mira, who lives one street away. Lukas is helping Sofia unpack, and they need to decide where everything goes: which room, which wall, which corner. At the same time, Sofia wants to know what is nearby — a supermarket, a pharmacy, a bakery — so she can run errands independently. Before she can navigate any of these situations, she needs language for rooms, furniture, and the essential question: Wo ist …? (Where is …?)

  1. Think about the last time you described a room or gave someone directions to a store. What spatial words did you use in English — next to, across from, in the corner? Notice that German has specific prepositions for each of these relationships, and that the article following the preposition changes. This chapter introduces that change systematically.
  2. In Chapters 1–5, you learned that case changes the article on a noun phrase. You have already seen the nominative (subject) and accusative (direct object) articles. This chapter introduces a third set: the dative articles used after location prepositions. The same principle applies — the article signals the noun's grammatical role — but here the role is location.

Pronunciation and Spelling

The ach-Laut after back vowels

In Chapter 3, you encountered the ich-Laut — the soft, front-of-the-mouth sound spelled ch after front vowels such as i, e, and ü. This chapter introduces its counterpart: the ach-Laut, a rougher, back-of-the-mouth sound that follows back vowels — a, o, u — and the diphthong au.

Sound Focus — The ach-Laut and ich-Laut contrast

The two ch sounds in German are conditioned largely by the vowel that precedes them. After back vowels, you produce the ach-Laut [x], a sound made at the back of the throat — similar to the ch in the Scottish word loch or the Welsh place name Bach. After front vowels (and in word-initial ch), you produce the softer ich-Laut [ç], which you know from ich, nicht, and sprichst.

Practice these contrasts with words from this chapter and earlier ones:

  • ach-Laut [x]: auch (also) [aʊx], noch (still/yet) [nɔx], suchen (to look for) [ˈzuːxn̩]
  • ich-Laut [ç]: ich [ɪç], nicht [nɪçt], gemütlich [ɡəˈmyːtlɪç], Küche [ˈkʏçə]

Notice that Küche (kitchen) has the ich-Laut, not the ach-Laut: the vowel ü is a front vowel. This is a common point of confusion. Say Küche with the soft [ç], not the rough [x].

Compound-word stress

German compound nouns are written as a single word, and their stress typically falls on the first element — the part that specifies or limits the meaning. The second element, which provides the grammatical category and gender, usually carries lighter secondary stress.

  • WOHNzimmer The Wohn- element (living) is stressed; -zimmer is secondary.
  • SCHLAFzimmer The Schlaf- element (sleep) is stressed.
  • KÜHLschrank The Kühl- element (cool) is stressed.
  • SUpermarkt The first syllable Su- carries main stress.
  • BÄckerei Main stress on the first syllable; the suffix -ei is secondary.

This principle extends to short phrases used like single concepts: IN der Nähe carries stress on in and Nä-. Practice grouping compound words as single phonological units rather than pausing between the parts.

Reduced unstressed -en

In natural connected speech, the suffix -en in verb infinitives, noun plurals, and other forms is often reduced: the vowel weakens to a schwa [ə] or disappears entirely, and the n becomes syllabic. You do not need to produce this reduction yet, but you should recognize it in your listening.

  • stehen [ˈʃteːn̩] The -en reduces in connected speech.
  • liegen [ˈliːɡn̩] -en reduces similarly.
  • zwischen den Stühlen — the -en endings run together quickly in normal speech.

Spelling: one-word compounds and the gender rule

German compound nouns are written without a space. This is not optional: *Wohn Zimmer and *Kühl Schrank are spelling errors. The gender of any compound noun is determined by its final element alone: der Schrank + Kühl- = der Kühlschrank (masculine); das Zimmer + Wohn- = das Wohnzimmer (neuter); die Bäckerei (the suffix -ei is feminine). When you encounter a new compound noun, identify the final element and its gender immediately.

Grammar Instruction

6.1 es gibt — there is / there are

Purpose: state what exists in a place — what a home contains, what a neighborhood offers.

The expression es gibt is used to state existence or availability. It is equivalent to English "there is" and "there are." Crucially, the noun phrase that follows es gibt is always in the accusative case — the same case you use for direct objects after haben.

Language Building Block
Es gibt + [accusative noun phrase]
The verb form is always es gibt, regardless of whether what follows is singular or plural. The article changes to accusative.
Es gibt einen Park. · Es gibt eine Bäckerei. · Es gibt ein Café. · Es gibt Supermärkte.
There is a park. · There is a bakery. · There is a café. · There are supermarkets.

The accusative articles after es gibt follow the same pattern you learned in Chapter 4:

Grammatical genderNominative (subject)Accusative after es gibt
Masculineder ParkEs gibt einen Park.
Femininedie BäckereiEs gibt eine Bäckerei.
Neuterdas CaféEs gibt ein Café.
Pluraldie ParksEs gibt Parks. (no article with plural)
  • In der Nähe gibt es einen Supermarkt und eine Apotheke. Nearby there is a supermarket and a pharmacy.
  • In meiner Wohnung gibt es einen Balkon. In my apartment there is a balcony.
  • Im Keller gibt es keinen Strom. In the basement there is no electricity.The negative kein- in accusative will be introduced in Chapter 8; recognize it here receptively.

Notice that the sentence can begin with es gibt or with a location phrase. When the location comes first, es gibt follows immediately in second position (V2 order): In der Nähe gibt es einen Park, not *In der Nähe es gibt einen Park.

6.2 Location prepositions and the dative

Purpose: describe precisely where an object is located using a set of prepositions that require a special article form.

German uses nine prepositions to describe the position of an object in relation to something else. When these prepositions describe static location — where something stays in place — the noun phrase following the preposition takes the dative article. This is a new set of article forms; compare them to the nominative and accusative forms you already know.

Grammar Note — Why the article changes

German marks the grammatical role of a noun phrase by changing its article. The nominative marks the subject; the accusative marks the direct object; the dative — in this chapter — marks the location after the nine prepositions above. Learning which prepositions govern which case is an important part of learning each preposition. These nine always use the dative when describing where something is located (its static position). You will see in Chapter 11 that some of these same prepositions can also use the accusative — but that is for a different meaning (directed movement toward a destination). For now, the rule is: location = dative.

Contractions: im and am

Two of the most frequent combinations shorten in standard usage. These contractions are not colloquial shortcuts — they are the expected standard forms in ordinary speech and writing.

  • in + dem → im Das Sofa steht im Wohnzimmer. (not: in dem Wohnzimmer)
  • an + dem → am Das Bild hängt am Fenster. (not: an dem Fenster)

These contractions only occur with the masculine and neuter dative (dem). With the feminine dative (der), no contraction applies: in der Küche, an der Wand. With the plural dative (den), no contraction applies either: zwischen den Stühlen.

Dative plural: the -n rule

In the dative, plural nouns add -n to the plural form — unless the plural already ends in -n or -s.

Nominative pluralDative pluralEnglish
die Stühleden Stühlenbetween the chairs → zwischen den Stühlen
die Tischeden Tischenbetween the tables → zwischen den Tischen
die Regaleden Regalenin front of the shelves → vor den Regalen
die Bettenden Betten (no change — already ends in -n)between the beds → zwischen den Betten

6.3 Position verbs: stehen, liegen, hängen

Purpose: describe the physical orientation of an object — whether it stands, lies flat, or hangs — in addition to where it is.

German uses three verbs alongside sein to describe how an object is positioned. Choosing the right verb adds natural precision to a description, but sein is always an acceptable alternative when you are unsure.

VerbUseExample
stehenObjects upright: furniture, appliances, people, vehiclesDas Sofa steht im Wohnzimmer.
liegenObjects flat: books, papers, rugs, sleeping bodiesDas Buch liegt auf dem Tisch.
hängenObjects suspended: pictures, coats, lamps, curtainsDie Lampe hängt über dem Tisch.
seinGeneral location: people, places, abstract locationDer Supermarkt ist in der Nähe.

Present-tense conjugation — all four verbs follow the regular pattern. Stehen, liegen, and hängen have no stem-vowel change in the present tense.

Grammar Note — hängen: a note for later

In this chapter, you are learning the static use of hängen: Das Bild hängt an der Wand — the picture is hanging (it is in that position). You are not yet learning the placement use, which would describe someone putting the picture there. That distinction — and the accusative it requires — will be introduced in Chapter 11 when you study how the same nine prepositions can signal movement toward a destination. For now, simply use hängen to describe things that are already suspended.

6.4 Locating things: Wo questions

Purpose: ask for and give information about where something is located.

You already know the question word wo (where) from Chapter 3, where you used it to ask about residence: Wo wohnst du? In this chapter, wo extends naturally to questions about the location of objects and neighborhood places. The structure follows the V2 pattern you know: the question word comes first, then the finite verb, then the subject.

  • Wo steht das Sofa? — Das Sofa steht im Wohnzimmer. Where is the sofa? — The sofa is in the living room.
  • Wo hängt das Bild? — Das Bild hängt an der Wand. Where is the picture? — The picture is on the wall.
  • Wo ist die Küche? — Die Küche ist rechts. Where is the kitchen? — The kitchen is on the right.
  • Wo gibt es einen Supermarkt? — Es gibt einen Supermarkt in der Nähe. Where is there a supermarket? — There is a supermarket nearby.

Notice that you can also ask about the existence of a neighborhood place with Gibt es hier …? (Is there a … here?): Gibt es hier eine Apotheke? — Ja, es gibt eine Apotheke um die Ecke.

Model Dialogue

Kontext: Hamburg. Sofia hat eine neue Wohnung. Lukas hilft ihr beim Einrichten. Sie sprechen über die Zimmer, die Möbel und das Viertel. Alle benutzen du miteinander.
Setting: Hamburg. Sofia has a new apartment. Lukas is helping her set it up. They talk about the rooms, the furniture, and the neighborhood. They all use du with each other.

LUKAS Sofia, die Wohnung ist wirklich schön! Wie viele Zimmer hat sie? Sofia, the apartment is really nice! How many rooms does it have?
SOFIA Drei Zimmer: ein Wohnzimmer, ein Schlafzimmer und ein Arbeitszimmer. Und natürlich Küche und Badezimmer. Three rooms: a living room, a bedroom, and a home office. And of course a kitchen and bathroom.
LUKAS Wo steht das Sofa? Im Wohnzimmer? Where does the sofa go? In the living room?
SOFIA Ja, das Sofa steht vor dem Fenster. Und der Tisch steht in der Mitte. Yes, the sofa goes in front of the window. And the table goes in the middle.
LUKAS Und die Lampe? Wo hängt die? And the lamp? Where does that hang?
SOFIA Die Lampe hängt über dem Tisch — so ist es hell. The lamp hangs above the table — that way it is bright.
LUKAS Gut. Und das Bild von Madrid — wo hängt das? Good. And the picture of Madrid — where does that hang?
SOFIA Das Bild hängt an der Wand neben dem Fenster. Es ist mein Lieblingsbild. The picture hangs on the wall next to the window. It is my favorite picture.
LUKAS Und das Bücherregal? And the bookshelf?
SOFIA Das Regal steht im Arbeitszimmer, zwischen dem Schreibtisch und der Tür. The shelf goes in the home office, between the desk and the door.
LUKAS Alles klar. Und wie ist das Viertel? Gibt es hier einen Supermarkt? Got it. And what is the neighborhood like? Is there a supermarket here?
SOFIA Ja! In der Nähe gibt es einen Supermarkt, eine Bäckerei und ein Café. Das Viertel ist ruhig und sehr gemütlich. Yes! Nearby there is a supermarket, a bakery, and a café. The neighborhood is quiet and very cozy.
LUKAS Und eine Apotheke? Gibt es auch eine? And a pharmacy? Is there one too?
SOFIA Ja, die Apotheke ist am Bahnhof, links um die Ecke. Yes, the pharmacy is at the train station, to the left around the corner.
LUKAS Super. Die Wohnung ist wirklich toll, Sofia! Great. The apartment is really wonderful, Sofia!

Reception note: Lieblingsbild (favorite picture), Schreibtisch (desk), and Alles klar (Got it / All clear) are receptive items in this dialogue — you do not need to produce them yet. Mein Lieblings- is a productive prefix you will use actively later in the course.

  3. Wo hängt die Lampe? (Where is the lamp hanging?)
  4. Was gibt es in der Nähe? (What is nearby?)

Reading Note — Abbreviations in housing texts

Housing advertisements in German-speaking countries use standard abbreviations. A few you may encounter: m² (Quadratmeter, square meters), WG (Wohngemeinschaft, shared apartment), EG (Erdgeschoss, ground floor), OG (Obergeschoss, upper floor), KM or kalt (Kaltmiete, rent without utilities), and WM or warm (Warmmiete, rent including utilities). The Hamburg advertisement above lists the rent as 1.050 € warm — the warm price includes heating and other running costs, making it easier to compare total housing costs across listings.

Writing Workshop

In this workshop, you will write a short description of a real or imagined home and its neighborhood — similar to the second reading text above. Your description should help a reader picture the space and understand what is nearby.

  1. Analyze the model. Reread the Zürich neighborhood text. Identify: (a) how the writer moves from neighborhood → apartment → a specific feature, (b) which location expressions and prepositions appear, and (c) one detail that makes the text feel personal.
  2. Plan your content. In brief notes, record: your home type (Wohnung, Haus, WG), the number and names of rooms, two or three pieces of furniture and their positions, your neighborhood and what is in it, and two adjectives describing the home or area.
  3. Draft your text. Write six to eight sentences. Include: at least one es gibt sentence, at least two location prepositions with dative articles, at least one position verb (stehen, liegen, or hängen), and at least one predicate adjective.
  4. Revise. Use the checklist below before writing a final version.

Checkliste — Editing checklist:

  • Every noun is capitalized, and compounds are written as one word (e.g., Wohnzimmer, not *Wohn Zimmer).
  • Every noun appears with the correct article in context — check gender for each.
  • Es gibt is followed by the accusative article (einen, eine, ein).
  • After location prepositions, I have used the correct dative article (dem, der, den).
  • Im appears instead of in dem, and am appears instead of an dem.
  • Predicate adjectives have no ending: Die Küche ist hell, not *helle.
  • Every sentence follows V2 word order, including sentences that begin with In der Nähe or Im Wohnzimmer.
  • My text includes a personal detail that makes it feel real, not just a list.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Renting and the WG

Renting is the dominant housing arrangement in Germany and is also very common in Austria and Switzerland — a much higher proportion of people rent their homes in these countries than in most English-speaking contexts. This makes the vocabulary of renting (die Wohnung, die Miete, die Wohngemeinschaft) central to everyday conversation rather than a specialized register.

The WG (Wohngemeinschaft, shared apartment) is particularly widespread among students, young professionals, and people who have recently moved to a city. Members of a WG share common spaces — kitchen, bathroom, living room — while having their own bedrooms. A typical WG interview involves exactly the kind of language in this chapter: Wie groß ist das Zimmer? Was gibt es im Viertel? Wie ist die Küche? Knowing this context helps explain why so much German vocabulary for home and neighborhood appears early in a practical communication course.

Culture in Context — Questions about housing and privacy

In German-speaking countries, direct questions about rent amounts can feel quite personal, especially in a casual context — though the norm varies by generation, social setting, and individual. Asking Wie viele Zimmer hat die Wohnung? (How many rooms does the apartment have?) or Ist das Viertel ruhig? (Is the neighborhood quiet?) is perfectly normal. Asking Wie viel zahlst du für die Miete? (How much do you pay in rent?) is more sensitive and usually reserved for close friends or a context where such information is clearly relevant. As with any personal topic, let the conversation's direction guide you.

Regional Variation — Housing vocabulary across DACH

A few housing terms differ across German-speaking regions. In Austria, das Stockwerk and das Geschoß both mean "floor/storey," and the ground floor may be called das Erdgeschoß (with ß) in older texts but is standardized as Erdgeschoss in the current reformed orthography. In Switzerland, die Wohnung is the standard term for apartment, and das Zimmer often refers to the number of main rooms (excluding kitchen and bathroom) rather than just a single room — so a 3-Zimmer-Wohnung in Switzerland typically means three main habitable rooms. Germany uses a similar counting method but may include or exclude the kitchen differently depending on the listing. Always check the specific listing for room count details.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using the nominative article after a location preposition

Error: *Das Buch liegt auf der Tisch. / *Die Lampe hängt über das Sofa.

Correction: Das Buch liegt auf dem Tisch. / Die Lampe hängt über dem Sofa. After the nine location prepositions, the dative article is required: dem (masculine and neuter) and der (feminine). It is easy to reach for the familiar nominative form (der Tisch) and forget to change it. A useful check: ask yourself, "Is this noun phrase following a location preposition?" If yes, switch to the dative form.

Common Mistake — Forgetting to contract im and am

Error: Das Sofa steht in dem Wohnzimmer. / Das Bild hängt an dem Fenster.

Correction: Das Sofa steht im Wohnzimmer. / Das Bild hängt am Fenster. The contractions im and am are the standard forms in ordinary speech and writing. Using the full in dem or an dem is not technically wrong but sounds formal or emphatic, as if you were deliberately stressing the preposition. Use the contracted forms as your default.

Common Mistake — Using the wrong accusative after es gibt

Error: *Es gibt ein Supermarkt. / *Es gibt der Park.

Correction: Es gibt einen Supermarkt. / Es gibt einen Park. After es gibt, the noun phrase is in the accusative. Masculine nouns require einen (not ein or der). A quick check: if the noun's nominative article is der, its accusative is einen after es gibt. Feminine nouns (die) stay eine; neuter nouns (das) stay ein.

Common Mistake — Adding an ending to a predicate adjective

Error: *Die Wohnung ist grosse. / *Das Zimmer ist gemütliches.

Correction: Die Wohnung ist groß. / Das Zimmer ist gemütlich. Predicate adjectives — those that follow sein and describe the subject — take no ending in German. This rule applies regardless of the subject's gender or number. Adjective endings are used only on adjectives that directly precede a noun (attributive position), and you will learn those systematically in Chapter 9.

Common Mistake — Inserting a space into a compound noun

Error: *das Wohn Zimmer / *der Kühl Schrank / *die Bäcker Ei

Correction: das Wohnzimmer / der Kühlschrank / die Bäckerei. German compound nouns are always written as one unbroken word. A space turns a compound into two separate words, which either changes the meaning or produces a non-word. This also affects the gender: you determine the gender from the final element of the compound as written, so the word must be written together for the gender rule to apply cleanly.

Guten Appetit!

Food and Drink · Speak and Write German: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Name common meals, foods, drinks, and menu categories.
  2. State food and drink preferences using gern, lieber, and am liebsten.
  3. Order in a café or simple restaurant using möchten and ich hätte gern.
  4. Request and politely decline items.
  5. Express quantities with common containers and measures.
  6. Use kein correctly in the nominative and accusative to negate a noun phrase.
  7. Use definite, indefinite, and possessive determiners consistently in the accusative case.
  8. Form plural noun phrases without an indefinite article.
  9. Ask a selection question with was or welch-.
  10. Ask for and understand a bill in a café or restaurant.

Communication Scenario

Es ist Samstagvormittag. Mira hat ihren neuen Freunden aus dem Deutschkurs — Sofia und Daniel — ein Café in ihrem Viertel empfohlen: das Café Sonnenblume. Die drei treffen sich dort zum Frühstück, bevor sie zusammen für die Deutschprüfung lernen. Der Kellner kommt an den Tisch, und alle müssen bestellen, Vorlieben äußern, und am Ende nach der Rechnung fragen.

  1. Before reading further, think about how you order food in English: How do you ask politely? How do you tell a server you don't want something? Notice that German has two especially common ways to order politely — möchten and ich hätte gern — and you will learn both in this chapter.
  2. In Chapter 4, you began using accusative articles with haben. This chapter expands the accusative system you already know to cover ordering food, declining items with kein, and naming quantities — skills you will keep using for the rest of the course.

Pronunciation and Spelling

Rounded front vowels: ö and ü

This chapter's vocabulary gives you concentrated practice with the rounded front vowels ö and ü, sounds with no exact English equivalent.

Sound Focus — ö and ü

To produce ü [yː] as in Brötchen's neighbor für, or in Frühstück, hold your tongue in the position for German i (as in ich) while rounding your lips as if to say u. The tongue stays forward; only the lips change. Do not substitute English "oo," which pulls the tongue back.

For ö [øː] as in Brötchen and möchten, hold your tongue in the position for German e (as in Tee) while rounding your lips as if to say o. Do not substitute English "er," which uses a different tongue position and an English r-coloring that German does not have.

Practice the contrast: Frühstück [ˈfʁyːʃtʏk], möchten [ˈmœçtn̩], Brötchen [ˈbʁøːtçən].

Vowel length in food vocabulary

Continue distinguishing long and short vowels, which never differ only in duration but also in quality. Compare the short, open vowel in Salat with the long, closed vowel in Saft's neighbor Kaffee's stressed syllable. Listen carefully to your instructor or audio model rather than guessing from spelling alone.

Polite-request intonation

Requests that begin with the finite verb, such as Möchten Sie …?, are grammatically yes-or-no questions, but a polite offer or request often uses a gentler, slightly rising-then-falling intonation rather than the sharper rise used for a true information-seeking yes-or-no question. Practice Möchten Sie noch etwas? with a warm, inviting tone.

pf and other common clusters

The cluster pf, as in Apfel, requires both consonants to be pronounced — first a full lip closure for p, then a release into f, never a simplified English "f" alone. Practice Apfel [ˈapfl̩] slowly, then at normal speed.

Final -er in frequent words

Unstressed final -er, as in Kellner and Teller, is typically pronounced as a weak, vowel-like schwa-r sound rather than a fully pronounced English "er." Listen for this reduced quality rather than stressing the final syllable.

Grammar Instruction

8.1 Accusative case: consolidation

Purpose: order and request food and drink items as direct objects, using the full set of accusative determiners together for the first time.

You met the accusative case in Chapter 4 with haben and again in Chapter 6 with es gibt. This chapter brings together everything you know about the accusative so you can use it fluently when ordering.

GeschlechtDefinitIndefinitPossessiv (Beispiel: mein)
maskulinden Kaffeeeinen Kaffeemeinen Kaffee
feminindie Suppeeine Suppemeine Suppe
neutraldas Brötchenein Brötchenmein Brötchen
Pluraldie Äpfel(none)meine Äpfel

Only the masculine column changes from the nominative; feminine, neuter, and plural accusative forms look identical to their nominative counterparts. This pattern, called case syncretism, is worth memorizing as a shortcut: when in doubt, check whether the noun is masculine singular.

  • Ich möchte einen Kaffee. I would like a coffee.[einen Kaffee] = accusative direct object, masculine
  • Wir bestellen die Suppe und das Brötchen. We're ordering the soup and the bread roll.
  • Hast du meinen Tee gesehen? Have you seen my tea?formulaic for recognition; full Perfekt is introduced in Chapter 16

8.2 kein in the nominative and accusative

Purpose: say that you do not have, do not want, or there is not a particular item.

kein negates a noun phrase that would otherwise take ein or no article at all. It follows the same endings as ein-words, but it also has plural forms, since ein itself has none.

GeschlechtNominativAkkusativ
maskulinkein Kaffeekeinen Kaffee
femininkeine Suppekeine Suppe
neutralkein Brötchenkein Brötchen
Pluralkeine Äpfelkeine Äpfel
  • Ich habe keinen Hunger. I'm not hungry. (literally: I have no hunger.)
  • Wir haben keine Milch mehr. We don't have any milk left.
  • Das ist kein Tee, das ist Kaffee! That's not tea, that's coffee!

kein negates a noun phrase. To negate a verb, an adjective, or most other elements, use nicht, which you began studying in Chapter 7. The two are not interchangeable: Ich trinke keinen Kaffee (I don't drink coffee, ever / I'm not drinking coffee) versus Ich trinke den Kaffee nicht (I'm not drinking that particular coffee).

8.3 Plural noun phrases without an indefinite article

Purpose: talk about food items in general, without specifying "the" or "a/an."

German has no plural form of ein. When you want to say "(some) apples" without referring to specific, known apples, simply use the plural noun with no article at all.

  • Ich möchte Äpfel. I would like (some) apples.
  • Hier gibt es Brötchen und Kuchen. There are bread rolls and cakes here.

This pattern also applies to many uncountable singular nouns, such as Wasser or Milch, when no article is used: Ich möchte Wasser. (I would like water.)

8.4 möchten: a high-frequency polite form

Purpose: order, request, and offer politely.

möchten is one of the most common words in everyday German and is essential for ordering food. It is formally related to the subjunctive mood, but at this stage you should simply learn its conjugation and use it as a fixed, high-frequency polite verb — not as "an infinitive meaning to want," and not by analyzing the full subjunctive system, which lies beyond this course.

PronomenFormEnglish
ichmöchteI would like
dumöchtestyou would like (informal)
er / sie / esmöchtehe / she / it would like
wirmöchtenwe would like
ihrmöchtetyou would like (informal plural)
sie / Siemöchtenthey would like / you would like (formal)
  • Ich möchte einen Kaffee und ein Brötchen. I would like a coffee and a bread roll.
  • Möchtest du auch einen Saft? Would you also like a juice?
  • Was möchten Sie? What would you like? (formal)
Grammar Note — möchten is not an infinitive

möchten looks like an infinitive because it ends in -en, but it is already a conjugated form built on the subjunctive stem of the verb mögen (to like). For this course, simply memorize the six forms above as a complete, usable pattern. You will encounter mögen and a fuller subjunctive system at a later stage of study, beyond this foundations volume.

8.5 ich hätte gern: a formulaic polite request

Purpose: order politely using a second common pattern, alongside möchten.

ich hätte gern ("I would like," literally closer to "I would gladly have") is a fixed, formulaic expression used constantly in cafés, restaurants, and shops across the German-speaking world. Like möchten, it draws on a verb form beyond this chapter's scope; treat the whole phrase as a memorized chunk for now.

  • Ich hätte gern die Suppe und ein Wasser. I would like the soup and a water.
  • Wir hätten gern zwei Kaffees, bitte. We would like two coffees, please.

ich möchte and ich hätte gern are both natural, polite, and widely used. Neither one is more formal or more correct than the other; many speakers simply alternate between them.

8.6 gern, lieber, am liebsten

Purpose: express preferences on a simple scale.

These three words form a preference scale used with a verb, most often trinken, essen, or mögen.

  • Ich trinke gern Kaffee. I like to drink coffee.
  • Ich trinke lieber Tee. I prefer to drink tea.
  • Ich trinke am liebsten Wasser. I like to drink water best of all.

8.7 was and welch- in selection questions

Purpose: ask someone to choose among options.

  • Was möchtest du trinken? What would you like to drink?
  • Welchen Kuchen möchten Sie? Which cake would you like?welchen = masculine accusative, matching der Kuchen
  • Welche Suppe schmeckt dir? Which soup do you like the taste of?

welch- takes the same endings as the definite article and must agree with the noun's gender, number, and case, just as you saw with dies- in earlier chapters. Only the productive forms shown here are expected at this stage.

Model Dialogue

Kontext: Samstagvormittag im Café Sonnenblume. Mira, Sofia und Daniel sitzen am Tisch. Ein Kellner kommt, um die Bestellung aufzunehmen. Mira und der Kellner kennen sich nicht; sie benutzen Sie.
Setting: Saturday morning at the Café Sonnenblume. Mira, Sofia, and Daniel are sitting at the table. A waiter comes to take the order. Mira and the waiter do not know each other; they use Sie.

KELLNER Guten Morgen! Was möchten Sie trinken? Good morning! What would you like to drink?
MIRA Ich hätte gern einen Kaffee, bitte. I would like a coffee, please.
SOFIA Für mich einen Tee, bitte. Ich trinke keinen Kaffee am Morgen. A tea for me, please. I don't drink coffee in the morning.
DANIEL Ich möchte ein Wasser und einen Orangensaft. I would like a water and an orange juice.
KELLNER Gern. Und zum Essen? Wir haben Brötchen, Käse, Wurst und Eier. Certainly. And to eat? We have bread rolls, cheese, cold cuts, and eggs.
MIRA Ich möchte ein Brötchen mit Käse, bitte. I would like a bread roll with cheese, please.
SOFIA Welchen Kuchen haben Sie heute? Which cake do you have today?
KELLNER Heute haben wir Apfelkuchen. Er ist sehr lecker. Today we have apple cake. It's very tasty.
SOFIA Gut, ich hätte gern ein Stück Apfelkuchen. Good, I would like a piece of apple cake.
DANIEL Für mich kein Frühstück, danke — nur das Getränk. No breakfast for me, thanks — just the drink.
KELLNER In Ordnung. Sonst noch etwas? All right. Anything else?
MIRA Nein, danke. Das ist alles. No, thank you. That's all.
KELLNER (später) Hier sind Ihr Kaffee, Ihr Tee, Ihr Wasser und Ihr Orangensaft. Guten Appetit! (later) Here are your coffee, your tea, your water, and your orange juice. Enjoy your meal!
MIRA Danke! Entschuldigung, die Rechnung, bitte. Zusammen, bitte. Thank you! Excuse me, the bill, please. Together, please.
KELLNER Das macht zusammen achtzehn Euro fünfzig. That comes to eighteen euros fifty altogether.

Verständnisfragen — Comprehension questions:

  1. Was bestellt Mira zum Trinken und zum Essen? (What does Mira order to drink and to eat?)
  2. Warum trinkt Sofia keinen Kaffee am Morgen? (Why doesn't Sofia drink coffee in the morning?)
  3. Was bestellt Daniel, und was bestellt er nicht? (What does Daniel order, and what does he not order?)
  4. Wie viel kostet die Rechnung insgesamt? (How much does the bill come to altogether?)
  5. Finden Sie im Dialog ein Beispiel für kein und ein Beispiel für möchten. (Find an example of kein and an example of möchten in the dialogue.)
Grammar Note — Ihr Kaffee, Ihr Tee

The waiter says Ihr Kaffee, Ihr Tee, Ihr Wasser using the capitalized formal possessive Ihr ("your," formal), which you met in Chapter 4. Notice that Ihr takes no extra ending here because der Kaffee, der Tee, and das Wasser are all in the nominative case as the subject of an implied "here is/are" — they are the things being identified, not direct objects of a verb in this sentence.

Reading Note — Café culture

A café like the Café Sonnenblume is a common, informal meeting place across German-speaking cities — for breakfast, a quick coffee, or an extended visit with friends. Customers are not generally expected to order quickly or leave right after finishing; spending an unhurried hour or more over a single coffee is normal in many cafés, though norms vary by establishment, city, and country.

Writing Workshop

In this chapter, you will write a short food-preference paragraph and a simple order note, following the models above.

  1. Analyze the model. Reread the note from Mira to Lukas. Identify: (a) how she uses kein, (b) how she uses ich hätte gern, and (c) the personal detail that makes the note feel natural rather than generic.
  2. Plan your paragraph. In notes, list: what you eat and drink for breakfast, what you like (gern), what you prefer (lieber), what you like best (am liebsten), and one food or drink you never have (kein).
  3. Write your first draft. Write four to six sentences describing your food and drink preferences. Include at least one sentence with möchten or ich hätte gern, one with kein, and the preference scale gern/lieber/am liebsten.
  4. Write a short order note. Imagine you are at the Café Sonnenblume. Write a two- to three-sentence note to a friend listing what you have ordered, using correct accusative articles.
  5. Revise. Check both texts against the editing checklist before writing a final version.

Checkliste — Editing checklist:

  • Every accusative article matches the noun's gender (den/die/das/die in the accusative).
  • kein agrees correctly with the noun it negates, and is used only to negate a noun phrase, not a verb.
  • möchten is conjugated correctly for its subject.
  • Plural nouns used in a general sense appear without an indefinite article.
  • Every noun is capitalized, and umlauts/ß are correctly written.
  • Prices, if used, follow the decimal-comma convention.
  • My paragraph includes one specific, personal preference.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Ordering and paying practices

In many German, Austrian, and Swiss restaurants and cafés, a server typically comes to the table to take an order rather than the customer ordering at a counter, and the bill is usually requested rather than brought automatically when a customer appears finished. Asking Zusammen oder getrennt? (together or separate) is a normal part of settling the bill among a group, and tipping customs, while real, are generally more modest than in some other countries and vary by establishment and region — exact figures and norms should be verified locally rather than assumed from this course alone.

Culture in Context — Dietary needs

When ordering, a guest with a dietary restriction or preference might say, for example, Ich esse kein Fleisch (I don't eat meat) or ask Ist das vegetarisch? (Is that vegetarian?). Respecting dietary needs without comment or surprise is an appropriate and increasingly common practice across German-speaking food culture, just as it is in many other communities.

Regional Variation — Brötchen, Semmel, and Weggli

This course uses the Germany-based term Brötchen as the default productive word for a bread roll. In much of Austria and Bavaria, the same item is commonly called Semmel, and in German-speaking Switzerland, Weggli or Brötli are common regional terms. All are standard, well-established words; none is incorrect. This course labels Semmel and Weggli as receptive regional alternatives rather than productive requirements.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Confusing kein and nicht

Error: *Ich trinke nicht Kaffee.

Correction: Ich trinke keinen Kaffee. To negate a noun phrase that would otherwise take ein or no article, use kein rather than nicht. Reserve nicht for negating verbs, adjectives, or a specific, already-identified noun phrase.

Common Mistake — Treating möchten as an infinitive

Error: *Ich möchten Kaffee.

Correction: Ich möchte Kaffee. möchten is the dictionary-citation form, but it must still be conjugated to match its subject like any other verb: ich möchte, not ich möchten.

Common Mistake — Forgetting the masculine accusative ending

Error: *Ich möchte ein Kaffee.

Correction: Ich möchte einen Kaffee. der Kaffee is masculine, so its accusative indefinite article is einen, not ein. Only masculine singular nouns change their article form in the accusative.

Common Mistake — Adding an indefinite article to a general plural

Error: *Ich möchte eine Äpfel.

Correction: Ich möchte Äpfel. German has no plural form of ein; a general plural noun simply appears without an article.

Common Mistake — Pronouncing ö or ü like English vowels

Error: Pronouncing Brötchen with an English "er" sound, or für with an English "oo" sound.

Correction: Keep the tongue forward for both sounds, changing only lip rounding: front-tongue plus rounded lips for ö and ü. Neither sound exists in English, so resist the instinct to substitute a familiar English vowel.

Practice — Supply the correct accusative form

  1. Ich möchte einen Kaffee und ein Stück Kuchen.
  2. Wir haben keine Milch mehr.
  3. Möchtest du mein Brötchen probieren?
  4. Der Kellner bringt die Suppe und das Brot.

Practice — möchten or ich hätte gern

  1. Ich möchte ein Wasser, bitte.
  2. Wir hätten gern zwei Kaffees, bitte.
  3. Was möchten Sie trinken?

Practice — kein or nicht

  1. Ich trinke keinen Kaffee. Ich trinke lieber Tee.
  2. Das ist nicht mein Tee — das ist deiner!
  3. Wir haben heute keinen Kuchen.

Dialogue — Comprehension

  1. Mira bestellt einen Kaffee und ein Brötchen mit Käse.
  2. Sofia trinkt keinen Kaffee am Morgen, weil sie morgens lieber Tee trinkt.
  3. Daniel bestellt ein Wasser und einen Orangensaft; er bestellt kein Frühstück.
  4. Die Rechnung kostet insgesamt achtzehn Euro fünfzig.
  5. kein: "Ich trinke keinen Kaffee am Morgen." möchten: "Was möchten Sie trinken?"

Listening — Table

  • Was bestellt die Sprecherin zum Trinken? Einen Tee.
  • Was bestellt die Sprecherin zum Essen? Ein Stück Kuchen.
  • Was bestellt ihr Mann? Ein Brötchen mit Wurst und ein Glas Wasser.
  • Zahlen sie zusammen oder getrennt? Zusammen.

Listening — Richtig oder falsch

  1. Falsch. Die Sprecherin möchte keinen Kaffee, sie möchte einen Tee.
  2. Richtig. Ihr Mann möchte ein Brötchen mit Wurst.
  3. Falsch. Sie zahlen zusammen, nicht getrennt.

Reading — Comprehension

  1. Ein Brötchen mit Käse oder Wurst kostet drei Euro fünfzig.
  2. Wasser ist am billigsten (zwei Euro zwanzig).
  3. Mira hat schon einen Kaffee und ein Brötchen mit Käse bestellt.
  4. Mira bittet Lukas um Geld, weil sie kein Geld mehr hat und noch ein Stück Kuchen möchte.
  5. Modell: Ich möchte ein Brötchen mit Käse, einen Kaffee und ein Stück Apfelkuchen. Das kostet zusammen neun Euro fünfzig. (Andere richtige Antworten sind akzeptabel, abhängig von der gewählten Bestellung.)

Chapter Review — Teil A

  1. Any five foods and three drinks from the chapter list, with correct articles, e.g.: das Brot, der Käse, die Wurst, das Ei, der Apfel; der Kaffee, der Tee, das Wasser.
  2. Any three quantity expressions, e.g.: eine Tasse Kaffee, ein Glas Wasser, ein Stück Kuchen.
  3. Die Rechnung, bitte. / Sonst noch etwas?
  4. sechs Euro vierzig

Chapter Review — Teil B

  1. ich möchte · du möchtest · er/sie/es möchte · wir möchten · ihr möchtet · sie/Sie möchten.
  2. (a) einen Tee. (b) keinen Kuchen mehr. (c) die Suppe.
  3. Ich trinke keinen Kaffee.
  4. (a) Ich möchte einen Saft. (b) Ich trinke keinen Tee, nur Wasser. (c) Ich möchte einen Kaffee.

Chapter Review — Teil C

  1. Ein Stück Schokoladenkuchen kostet drei Euro fünfzig.
  2. Mira schreibt Lukas eine Notiz, weil sie im Café ist und ihn einladen möchte, aber sie hat kein Geld mehr und braucht zehn Euro.

Chapter Review — Teil D, model answer

Ich trinke gern Kaffee, aber ich trinke lieber Tee. Am liebsten trinke ich Wasser. Ich esse gern Brötchen mit Käse. Ich esse keinen Fisch. Am Wochenende möchte ich oft ein Stück Kuchen. Other well-formed, accurate answers are equally acceptable. Check against the editing checklist.

Cumulative Review — Model responses for Speaker B

B : Ich möchte einen Kaffee und ein Brötchen, bitte.
B : Ich möchte ein Glas Wasser, bitte.
B : Nein, danke. Das ist alles.
B : Hier, bitte. (Vielen Dank!)

Exact wording varies. Check: correct accusative articles, correct conjugation of möchten, and natural, polite phrasing throughout.

Looking Ahead

Was Sie jetzt können — What you can do now

You can now name foods and drinks, state preferences, order politely with möchten and ich hätte gern, decline items using kein, express quantities, and ask for a bill. You have consolidated the accusative case across definite, indefinite, and possessive determiners, and you can use plural nouns without an article when speaking generally. These accusative patterns will continue to expand as you move into shopping in the next chapter.

Kapitel 9: Einkaufen — Shopping

Chapter 9 introduces attributive adjective endings — the endings adjectives take when they appear directly before a noun, such as der rote Apfel — along with accusative personal pronouns and simple comparison. You will use your accusative skills from this chapter to ask for, compare, and purchase clothing and everyday goods.

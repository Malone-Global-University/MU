Rückblick 1: Wir lernen uns kennen

Unit I Review · Speak and Write German: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

This review chapter introduces no new grammar. By the end of it, you will be able to:

  1. Greet people formally and informally, and take leave appropriately.
  2. Introduce yourself and someone else, spelling a name when needed.
  3. Identify common classroom objects using correct nominative articles and plurals.
  4. Ask and answer where someone is from, where they currently live, and what language or languages they speak.
  5. Describe a family member, state an age, and say what someone has using haben and the accusative.
  6. Conjugate sein and haben accurately in all six forms.
  7. Place the finite verb correctly in statements, including after a fronted word, and in verb-first and W-questions.
  8. Distinguish a nominative subject from an accusative object in a simple sentence.
  9. Use possessive determiners and predicate adjectives without adding unnecessary endings.
  10. Notice and repair a simple misunderstanding in conversation.
  11. Read a short personal profile and report its content in your own words.
  12. Write a connected personal introduction that combines name, origin, residence, languages, and family.

Communication Scenario

Heute ist der letzte Tag von Einheit I im Deutschkurs an der Volkshochschule Hamburg. Frau Bauer hat ein kleines Kennenlern-Fest organisiert: Eine Anfängergruppe aus dem Nachbarkurs kommt zu Besuch, und alle Teilnehmer sollen sich gegenseitig vorstellen — sich selbst, einen Partner oder ein Familienmitglied. Lukas, Mira, Sofia und Daniel bereiten sich vor. Sie wiederholen, was sie in den ersten vier Kapiteln gelernt haben, und üben, alles zusammen in einem einzigen Gespräch zu benutzen: Begrüßung, Name, Herkunft, Sprachen und Familie.

  1. Before reading further, try saying one complete German sentence for each of these ideas, out loud: a greeting, your name, where you are from, a language you speak, and a family member. If one of them is difficult, that is exactly the system this chapter will help you stabilize.
  2. This chapter does not teach a new grammar system. Chapters 1 through 4 already gave you everything you need — the goal now is to use it all together, the way a real conversation actually moves between topics.

Pronunciation and Orthography Review

Alphabet, spelling, and basic intonation (Kapitel 1)

You can already say the German alphabet and spell a name aloud. Statement intonation falls toward the end of the sentence; question intonation generally rises on a W-question word and on a yes-or-no question.

w, j, z, and sch (Kapitel 1–2)

Recall that German w sounds like English v (wie), j sounds like English y (ja), z sounds like English ts (zwanzig), and sch sounds like English sh (Tisch).

Initial sp/st and the ich-Laut (Kapitel 3)

At the start of a word, sp and st add a /ʃ/ glide: Spanien [ʃpaːni̯ən], sprechen [ʃprɛçn̩]. The front-vowel ich-Laut, spelled ch, is a soft, breathy sound — never /k/ and never identical to English sh: ich, sprichst.

Umlaut vowels and vowel length (Kapitel 4)

The umlauted plurals Mütter and Väter contain rounded and long vowels that have no equivalent in English. Continue distinguishing long and short vowels rather than treating vowel length as decorative.

Sound Focus — Cumulative discrimination

Listen to or read aloud each pair and decide what distinguishes the two words: Buch / Bücher (umlaut and plural), spät / Stadt (initial cluster), ich / isch (not a real word — practice distinguishing the genuine ich-Laut from English sh), kommen / Kommen (capitalization changes the grammatical category, not the pronunciation).

5.2 Regular present-tense verbs and one stem change

The endings -e, -st, -t, -en, -t, -en apply to kommen, wohnen, lernen, and machen. Sprechen follows the same endings but changes its stem vowel from e to i in the du and er/sie/es forms only.

  • Daniel kommt aus Kanada und spricht Englisch und Französisch. Daniel comes from Canada and speaks English and French.

5.3 Nominative subject and accusative object

Purpose: recognize and use the two cases you have met so far through their grammatical role, not through English word order alone.

The nominative marks the subject — the person or thing performing the action or being described. The accusative marks a common direct object — the person or thing receiving the action, such as what you have.

  • Der Bruder ist nett. The brother is nice.der Bruder = nominative subject
  • Ich habe einen Bruder. I have a brother.einen Bruder = accusative object after haben

Grammar Note — Why this distinction matters now

So far, only masculine nouns visibly change their article between nominative and accusative (der → den/einen); feminine, neuter, and plural articles look the same in both cases at this stage. This is exactly why case cannot be learned from English word order alone: in Ich habe einen Bruder, English keeps the same word order it would use for a subject, but German still marks Bruder as an object with einen. Beginning in Chapter 6, you will meet a third case, the dative, and the full pattern will become clearer.

Only the masculine accusative form adds the ending -en at this stage (mein → meinen); the other forms remain as shown above. A complete possessive-determiner paradigm across all cases comes later in the course.

5.5 Predicate adjectives

An adjective after sein describes the subject and never takes an ending.

  • Mein Bruder ist nett. My brother is nice.
  • Sofia ist spanisch. Sofia is Spanish.

Compare the noun Spanisch (the language, capitalized) with the adjective spanisch (the nationality, lowercase) — the same contrast you studied in Chapter 3.

5.6 V2 word order and inversion

The finite verb occupies the second constituent of a German statement, whatever comes first.

  • Wir lernen heute zusammen. We are studying together today.
  • Heute lernen wir zusammen. Today we are studying together.[Heute] [lernen] [wir zusammen] — subject after the verb
Common Mistake — Forgetting inversion after a fronted word

Error: *Heute wir lernen zusammen.

Correction: Heute lernen wir zusammen. Whatever occupies first position, the finite verb must follow immediately in second position.

Model Dialogue

Kontext: Das Kennenlern-Fest an der Volkshochschule Hamburg. Mira stellt ihren Bruder Tom, der zu Besuch ist, der Gruppe vor. Ein neuer Teilnehmer aus dem Nachbarkurs, Paul, hört zunächst falsch und muss nachfragen.
Setting: The get-to-know-you gathering at the Volkshochschule Hamburg. Mira introduces her visiting brother, Tom, to the group. A new participant from the neighboring course, Paul, mishears at first and has to ask again.

MIRA Hallo, Paul! Das ist mein Bruder. Er heißt Tom. Hi, Paul! This is my brother. His name is Tom.
PAUL Hallo! Wie heißt du — John? Hi! What's your name — John?
TOM Nein, nicht John — Tom! T-O-M. No, not John — Tom! T-O-M.
PAUL Ah, Tom — jetzt verstehe ich. Freut mich! Woher kommst du? Ah, Tom — now I understand. Nice to meet you! Where are you from?
TOM Ich komme auch aus Hamburg, aber ich wohne jetzt in München. I'm also from Hamburg, but I live in Munich now.
PAUL Interessant! Sprichst du auch Englisch wie Mira? Interesting! Do you also speak English like Mira?
TOM Ja, ich spreche Deutsch und Englisch. Und du? Hast du Geschwister? Yes, I speak German and English. And you? Do you have siblings?
PAUL Ja, ich habe eine Schwester. Sie ist sehr freundlich. Yes, I have a sister. She is very friendly.
MIRA Toll, jetzt kennt ihr euch! Im Kursraum gibt es noch Kaffee — komm mit! Great, now you know each other! There's still coffee in the classroom — come along!

Verständnisfragen — Comprehension questions:

  1. Wer ist Tom, und woher kommt er? (Who is Tom, and where is he from?)
  2. Wo wohnt Tom jetzt? (Where does Tom live now?)
  3. Welchen Namen versteht Paul zuerst falsch, und wie korrigiert Tom ihn? (Which name does Paul mishear at first, and how does Tom correct him?)
  4. Hat Paul Geschwister? Was wissen wir über sie? (Does Paul have siblings? What do we know about her?)
Grammar Note — Repairing a misunderstanding

Notice how Tom repairs Paul's mistake: he says nein, repeats the correct name, and spells it. Jetzt verstehe ich ("now I understand") is a useful formula for signaling that a repair has succeeded. You will not be asked to analyze the grammar of verstehe formally yet — treat it, like Freut mich in Chapter 1, as a complete expression you can already use.

Reading

Read the following short profiles, written by two participants from the visiting Nachbarkurs for the Kennenlern-Fest.

Wer kommt aus dem Nachbarkurs?

Ich heiße Anja. Ich komme aus Dresden und wohne jetzt in Hamburg. Ich spreche Deutsch und ein bisschen Polnisch, weil meine Großmutter aus Polen kommt. Ich habe einen Bruder und eine Schwester. Mein Bruder ist Student, und meine Schwester ist Lehrerin. Beide sind sehr nett.

Ich heiße Markus. Ich komme aus Bremen, aber meine Familie kommt ursprünglich aus Italien. Ich wohne jetzt auch in Hamburg. Ich spreche Deutsch, Italienisch und Englisch. Ich habe keine Geschwister, aber ich habe viele Freunde im Kurs.

Verständnisfragen — Comprehension questions:

  1. Woher kommt Anja, und wo wohnt sie jetzt? (Where is Anja from, and where does she live now?)
  2. Warum spricht Anja ein bisschen Polnisch? (Why does Anja speak a little Polish?)
  3. Was machen Anjas Bruder und Schwester beruflich? (What do Anja's brother and sister do for work?)
  4. Hat Markus Geschwister? (Does Markus have siblings?)
  5. Schreiben Sie zwei Sätze über sich selbst nach dem Modell von Anja oder Markus. (Write two sentences about yourself following Anja's or Markus's model.)
Reading Note — Family and migration history

Anja's grandmother and Markus's family illustrate something true of many people in the German-speaking world: family origin, current nationality, and present-day residence often do not line up neatly. Neither profile should be treated as more "typical" than the other — both are ordinary, contemporary German-speaking lives.

Writing Workshop

For the Kennenlern-Fest, you will write a connected personal introduction that combines everything from Chapters 1–4: name, origin, residence, languages, and at least one family member. This piece will also serve as your presentational writing task for the unit performance assessment below.

  1. Analyze the models. Reread Anja's and Markus's profiles above. Identify: (a) the order in which name, origin, residence, language, and family appear, (b) at least one sentence that does not begin with the subject, and (c) one personal detail in each profile.
  2. Plan your content. In notes, list: your name, where you are from, where you live now, the language(s) you speak, one family member (name, relationship, one descriptive adjective), and one personal detail.
  3. Write your first draft. Write five to seven sentences. Include at least: one sentence with sein, one with haben plus an accusative object, one with a possessive determiner, and one sentence that does not begin with the subject.
  4. Revise. Check your draft against the editing checklist before writing a final version.

Checkliste — Editing checklist:

  • Every form of sein and haben matches its subject.
  • Every noun is capitalized; every nationality adjective is lowercase.
  • At least one accusative object appears correctly after haben (einen Bruder, eine Schwester, and so on).
  • Every sentence has the finite verb in second position, even after a fronted word.
  • Possessive determiners agree with the noun they introduce.
  • Predicate adjectives take no ending.
  • My introduction includes at least one specific, personal detail.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — du and Sie across four chapters

Across Chapters 1–4, you have used du with classmates of a similar age and Sie with the instructor, Frau Bauer. This is a common pattern in many German classrooms, but it is not universal: institutional culture, generation, region, and the specific relationship between speakers all shape the choice. The skill worth keeping from this unit is not a fixed rule but the habit of noticing the relationship before choosing a form of address.

Culture in Context — Many shapes of family

The people you have met in this unit — Mira and her brother Tom, Daniel and his Italian-Canadian family, Anja with a Polish grandmother, Markus with no siblings but many friends in the course — represent ordinary variety, not exceptions. When you describe your own family or ask about someone else's, avoid assuming a single household type or a single set of relationships applies to everyone.

Looking Ahead

Was Sie jetzt können — What you can do now

You can now greet people appropriately, introduce yourself and others, identify classroom objects, exchange information about origin and languages, and describe family members and possessions. You can use sein and haben accurately, distinguish a nominative subject from an accusative object, and keep the finite verb in second position no matter what comes first in the sentence. These systems will appear in every chapter that follows.

Kapitel 6: Zu Hause und im Viertel — Home and Neighborhood

Chapter 6 introduces es gibt plus the accusative and your first dative forms, used to say where things are located in a home or neighborhood. You will build directly on the nominative and accusative systems you have just reviewed, adding a third case for describing location.

Capítulo 10: Proyecto 2: Dossier especializado

Chapter function: Integrated assessment — domain dossier with presentation and written brief. Diego and Camila's client has now received both the permit confirmation and the plain-language summary of the environmental-impact report. The client has asked for one consolidated dossier that brings everything together before the project moves forward. This chapter does not introduce new grammar or a new vocabulary domain; instead, it asks learners to select one specialized domain from Chapters 6–9 — academic, professional/corporate, civic/legal/administrative, or scientific/technological — and produce an integrated written and spoken dossier that demonstrates control of the register, vocabulary, and discourse patterns developed across the unit.

1. Chapter Overview

This is the second project chapter of Volume V and the closing assessment of Unit II. Learners consolidate everything developed since Chapter 6: specialized tense-aspect usage, clause conventions for cause, consequence, and contrast, discourse deixis, superlatives, register-sensitive ser/estar use, and the vocabulary and clitic-reference patterns active across the academic, professional, civic-legal, and scientific-technological registers. No new productive system is introduced. Instead, learners choose a specialized domain, assemble a short written dossier, and present it orally, moving fluidly between expert and public registers exactly as Diego and Camila have been doing with their client throughout the unit.

2. Learning Objectives

  • Select and sustain a single specialized register (academic, professional, civic-legal, or scientific-technological) across a written and spoken task.
  • Comment on findings, proposals, procedures, or data using present, present perfect, and future forms already consolidated from Chapters 6–9.
  • Connect ideas of cause, consequence, and contrast accurately across an extended, multi-paragraph text.
  • Use discourse deixis and superlatives to summarize and emphasize key points without repeating technical detail.
  • Distinguish ser and estar correctly when classifying findings and describing ongoing or completed processes.
  • Produce a short written dossier (a brief, summary, or report) and an oral presentation of the same material for a non-specialist audience.
  • Interpret a peer's dossier and ask at least one follow-up question in the appropriate register.

3. Opening Communication Scenario

Diego and Camila are organizing the final materials before their meeting with the client, who wants one document that brings together the permit, the environmental report, and next steps.

Diego: El cliente pidió un solo documento con todo: el permiso, el resumen del informe ambiental y los próximos pasos. Lo anterior tiene que quedar claro y organizado.

Camila: Podemos dividirlo en tres partes. Primero, la situación legal y administrativa; segundo, los hallazgos técnicos explicados en lenguaje sencillo; y tercero, lo que sigue.

Diego: Buena idea. ¿Y quién presenta cada parte?

Camila: Yo puedo presentar la parte ambiental, ya que la conozco mejor. Tú puedes encargarte de la parte legal y administrativa.

Diego: Perfecto. Lo más importante es que el cliente entienda todo sin necesidad de conocer la jerga técnica ni la jerga legal.

Camila: Exactamente. Preparemos el dossier así, y después lo presentamos juntos.

5. Pronunciation and Discourse Focus

No new sound system is introduced. This section develops the discourse-level skill of shifting tone between the sections of a longer presentation — a slightly more formal, deliberate delivery for the legal or technical section, and a warmer, more conversational delivery when summarizing for the client.

Exercise 10.1 — Shifting register aloud

Read the following two sentences aloud, adjusting your pace and tone between them: "De acuerdo con el trámite, el permiso ha sido aprobado sin observaciones." then "En otras palabras: ya podemos empezar." Notice how the second sentence should sound noticeably more relaxed than the first.

6. Grammar and Sentence Patterns (Consolidation)

This chapter does not activate a new grammar system. It consolidates, for assessment, the systems already developed across Chapters 6–9: specialized tense-aspect usage for findings and proposals, cause/consequence/contrast connectors, discourse deixis and superlatives, and the classifying-versus-describing contrast between ser and estar.

7. Model Dialogue: Rehearsing the Dossier Presentation

Camila: Empecemos con la parte ambiental. El informe indica que el impacto es mínimo, y los datos muestran que la nueva tecnología ha reducido las emisiones muchísimo.

Diego: Bien. Y para la parte legal: de acuerdo con el trámite municipal, el permiso ya está aprobado y no requiere modificaciones.

Camila: Lo anterior significa que, en cuanto firmemos, podemos empezar la construcción.

Diego: Entonces el punto más importante para el cliente es este: el proyecto está listo para avanzar, y tanto el aspecto legal como el ambiental están confirmados.

Camila: Exacto. Así lo presentamos: claro, ordenado y sin jerga innecesaria.

8. Guided Practice

Exercise 10.2 — Choosing the right connector

Complete each sentence with a connector of cause, consequence, or contrast, as indicated.

  1. El permiso fue aprobado. ______, el proyecto puede avanzar. (consequence)
  2. El nivel de ruido aumentará. ______ se usará maquinaria pesada. (cause)
  3. El trámite normalmente tarda un mes. ______, este caso se resolvió en dos semanas. (contrast)

Exercise 10.3 — Ser or estar?

  1. El permiso ya ______ aprobado.
  2. El impacto ambiental ______ mínimo, según el informe.
  3. La construcción todavía ______ en etapa de planeación.

9. Listening Practice

Listening Script for Instructor, Partner, or Text-to-Speech Use

Camila y Diego practican su presentación completa antes de la reunión. Camila: "Empezamos con la parte legal. De acuerdo con el trámite municipal, el permiso ha sido aprobado sin observaciones." Diego: "Después seguimos con la parte ambiental. El informe indica que el impacto es mínimo y que la tecnología propuesta ha reducido las emisiones muchísimo en proyectos similares." Camila: "Por lo tanto, el proyecto está listo para avanzar. El punto más importante es este: no hay obstáculos legales ni ambientales." Diego: "Lo anterior significa que podemos comenzar la construcción el próximo mes, según lo planeado."

  3. Find one example of discourse deixis and explain what it refers back to.

12. Writing Workshop — Integrated Assessment

Task: Choose one specialized domain from Chapters 6–9 (academic, professional/corporate, civic/legal/administrative, or scientific/technological). Write a short dossier (150–200 words, two to three short sections) addressed to a non-specialist client, summarizing a situation, a finding or recommendation, and next steps. Your dossier must use at least four vocabulary items from your chosen domain, at least two connectors of cause, consequence, or contrast, at least one discourse-deixis expression, at least one superlative, and correct ser/estar distinction at least twice.

Stage 1 — Plan

Choose your domain and outline two or three sections: situation, finding/recommendation, and next steps. Note which technical terms will need a plain-language gloss for the client.

Stage 2 — Draft

Write the dossier in short, clearly separated sections, using the framing expressions from Section 4 as needed.

Stage 3 — Revise

Check tense choice (present, present perfect, future), connector accuracy, discourse-deixis clarity, superlative agreement, and ser/estar distinction. Confirm the dossier remains understandable to a reader with no specialized background.

Integrated assessment checklist

  • At least four vocabulary items from the chosen domain are used accurately.
  • At least two connectors of cause, consequence, or contrast are used correctly.
  • At least one discourse-deixis expression clearly refers back to a stated idea.
  • At least one superlative is formed and used correctly.
  • Ser and estar are each used correctly at least once.
  • No new, unscheduled grammar or vocabulary is required to understand the dossier.
  • The dossier remains clear to a non-specialist reader.

13. Culture and Communication

Moving between a specialized register and a client-facing register is a skill that professionals, consultants, translators, and public officials across the Spanish-speaking world rely on regardless of country. What matters for this assessment is not which country's institutional conventions a learner references, but whether the dossier communicates clearly to someone outside the specialized field — a skill that is broadly transferable across academic, professional, civic, and scientific contexts.

14. Common Mistakes

  • Mixing two specialized registers within the same section instead of keeping each part of the dossier consistent.
  • Stringing together untranslated technical or legal terms without a plain-language gloss, which defeats the purpose of a client-facing dossier.
  • Using the future for a finding or approval that has already occurred, instead of the present perfect.
  • Using a discourse-deixis expression (esto, eso, lo anterior) without a clear antecedent the reader can identify.
  • Choosing ser to describe an ongoing process, or estar to classify a finding.

This chapter closed Unit II with an integrated assessment: learners selected one specialized domain from Chapters 6–9 and produced a written dossier and an oral presentation for a non-specialist client.

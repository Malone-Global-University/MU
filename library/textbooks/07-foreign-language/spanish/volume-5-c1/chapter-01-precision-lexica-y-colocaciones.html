Speak and Write Spanish · Volume V · Unit I — Advanced Style and Interpretation

Capítulo 1: Precisión léxica y colocaciones

Chapter function: Sharpen lexical precision through collocation, register-sensitive synonym choice, and domain-specific vocabulary, building directly on the C1-initial control established at the end of Volume IV.

1. Chapter Overview

Diego's team survived the regional presentation, and the recommendation was approved. But in the debrief, the regional director made an offhand comment that stayed with Camila: the report was correct, she said, but a few word choices made it read like a translation rather than something written by someone who thinks in Spanish. This chapter begins Volume V by asking you to close that last gap — not by learning new grammar, but by learning to choose the right word. You will study collocations (words that habitually appear together), register-sensitive synonym pairs, and the vocabulary of your own professional or academic domain, so that your Spanish stops sounding merely accurate and starts sounding precise.

2. Learning Objectives

  • Identify and produce common Spanish collocations in professional and academic contexts.
  • Choose among near-synonyms based on register, connotation, and collocational fit.
  • Recognize when a direct English-to-Spanish word substitution produces an unnatural collocation.
  • Build a personal domain-specific vocabulary set with accurate gender, collocation, and register metadata.
  • Revise a piece of writing to replace generically correct vocabulary with more precise, natural word choices.

3. Opening Communication Scenario

A few days after the presentation, Camila and Diego review the director's feedback over coffee.

Camila: Me quedé pensando en lo que dijo la directora. Dijo que el informe estaba bien redactado, pero que algunas palabras "no encajaban". ¿Tú entendiste a qué se refería?

Diego: Creo que sí. Por ejemplo, escribiste "hacer una decisión" en vez de "tomar una decisión". Gramaticalmente no está mal, pero no es así como se dice en español.

Camila: Ah. Entonces no es un error de gramática, es un error de... ¿colocación?

Diego: Exacto. Cada palabra tiene compañeras habituales. "Tomar una decisión", "cumplir un plazo", "desempeñar un papel". Si cambias esas combinaciones, el texto sigue siendo correcto, pero deja de sonar natural.

Camila: Entonces este trimestre necesito aprender no solo palabras nuevas, sino con qué palabras se juntan.

4. Core Vocabulary

The items below are organized as collocations rather than single words, since the pairing itself is the learning target.

ColocaciónEnglishNotes
tomar una decisiónto make a decisionNOT hacer una decisión; tomar is the fixed verb here
desempeñar un papelto play a roleformal/professional register; recycled desempeño from Volume IV
cumplir un plazoto meet a deadlineNOT completar un plazo; recycled from Volume IV workplace vocabulary
plantear una pregunta / un problemato raise a question / a problemmore formal than hacer una pregunta; common in academic and institutional Spanish
rendir cuentasto be accountable, to answer for one's actionsfixed expression; formal/institutional register
llevar a caboto carry outfixed expression; NOT a literal "carry to end"; formal/written register
guardar relación conto be related to, to bear relation tofixed expression; academic and analytical register
surtir efectoto take effect, to have the intended resultfixed expression; formal register, common in policy and legal contexts
Language NoteA collocation is not simply "two words that go together." It is a pairing so habitual that a fluent speaker notices immediately when it is broken, even if the substitute word is a perfectly correct synonym. Hacer una decisión is understandable, but it signals a non-native pattern in a way that a single grammar error might not.

5. Pronunciation and Discourse Focus

This chapter does not introduce a new sound system. Instead, it reinforces the five-vowel stability and clear stress patterns established across Volumes I–IV, now applied to longer, more formal collocational phrases where English-influenced stress can creep in.

6. Grammar and Sentence Patterns

Chapter 1 introduces no new productive grammar system. All sentence structures below use forms fully consolidated by the end of Volume IV — subordination, relative clauses, compound tenses, subjunctive, and passive/impersonal se — so that the entire chapter's attention can go to lexical choice.

6.1 Precision through collocation, not new structure

Generic (grammatically correct, imprecise)Precise (natural collocation)
El equipo hizo una decisión rápida.El equipo tomó una decisión rápida.
Necesitamos completar el plazo del proyecto.Necesitamos cumplir el plazo del proyecto.
Ella hace un papel importante en el equipo.Ella desempeña un papel importante en el equipo.
El nuevo horario va a tener efecto la próxima semana.El nuevo horario va a surtir efecto la próxima semana.
Language NoteNotice that every "generic" sentence above is grammatically flawless. The revision changes nothing about tense, agreement, or word order — only the verb chosen to accompany the noun. This is the core skill of the chapter: editing for precision after accuracy is already secure.

7. Model Dialogue: Reviewing a Draft Together

Diego: Mira esta frase: "El comité hizo una decisión sobre el presupuesto." ¿Qué cambiarías?

Camila: "Tomó una decisión", ¿no? "Hacer una decisión" suena traducido.

Diego: Exacto. Y aquí: "Es importante que la empresa haga un papel activo en la comunidad."

Camila: "Desempeñe un papel activo." Hay que usar el subjuntivo también, porque sigue "es importante que".

Diego: Muy bien. Y una más: "Los resultados tienen relación directa con la inversión inicial."

Camila: Esa suena bien, pero en un informe más formal yo diría "guardan relación directa con". Es más propio del registro académico.

12. Writing Workshop

Task: Write a short professional update (120–150 words) about a real or invented project, decision, or deadline in your field. Use at least five collocations from this chapter. After drafting, complete a dedicated lexical-precision pass: circle every verb-noun pairing and confirm that it is the natural Spanish collocation rather than a direct translation from English.

Lexical precision checklist

  • Every decision-related verb uses tomar, not hacer.
  • Deadlines are described with cumplir, not completar.
  • Roles are described with desempeñar, not hacer.
  • At least one formal fixed expression (llevar a cabo, rendir cuentas, surtir efecto, or guardar relación con) appears and fits the register of the text.
  • No accents, agreement, or other previously consolidated systems were sacrificed while focusing on vocabulary.

13. Culture and Communication

In many professional and academic settings across the Spanish-speaking world, lexical precision is read as a marker of preparation and credibility, much like register consistency was in Volume IV. A report that says se tomó la decisión rather than se hizo la decisión signals, often without the reader consciously noticing why, that the writer is comfortable operating in that professional register. This does not mean occasional imprecision is judged harshly — most listeners and readers are generous with non-native speakers — but at the C1 level and beyond, audiences increasingly expect writing and speech that reads as native-like rather than merely correct.

14. Common Mistakes

  • Using hacer as a default verb for actions that have a fixed collocational partner, such as decisions, roles, or questions.
  • Translating an English collocation word-for-word instead of learning the Spanish pairing as a unit.
  • Assuming that a grammatically correct sentence is automatically a natural one.
  • Overcorrecting into overly formal collocations (en virtud de, rendir cuentas) in contexts that call for plain, everyday language.
  • Treating collocation practice as vocabulary memorization rather than as an editing skill applied to one's own writing.

15. Chapter Summary

This chapter opened Volume V by shifting the focus from grammatical accuracy, which Volume IV established, to lexical precision. You studied collocations as fixed verb-noun pairings, practiced choosing among register-sensitive synonyms, and applied a dedicated editing pass to your own writing to replace generically correct vocabulary with natural, professional word choices. No new grammar was introduced; every sentence pattern drew on systems already consolidated by the end of Volume IV.

Exercise 1.8

1. El equipo tomó una decisión sin consultar al cliente.
2. Deadlines: cumplir un plazo. Roles: desempeñar un papel.
3. A sentence can follow every grammar rule correctly while still combining words in a way native speakers never actually pair, which is a separate, lexical kind of error.

Capítulo 13: Conectores y arquitectura textual

Chapter function: Macro-cohesion, transitions, paragraph architecture, and stance marking. The recommendations document is now clear at the sentence level — Diego and Camila fixed the pronoun sequencing and antecedent problems in Chapter 12 — but Camila's supervisor has sent it back with a new note: the paragraphs "jump." This chapter moves the editorial lens up one level, from the sentence to the paragraph and the document, teaching learners to connect ideas across sentences, signal their own stance toward the content, and build a text that reads as a single, organized argument rather than a series of correct but disconnected statements.

1. Chapter Overview

Learners can already write individual sentences that are grammatically accurate and referentially clear. This chapter teaches them to link those sentences into a coherent whole. Connectors of addition, contrast, cause, consequence, and concession give a text its logical skeleton; paragraph architecture gives it a predictable shape a reader can follow; and stance markers let a writer show where they stand — recommending, doubting, agreeing, or qualifying — without abandoning a professional register. The chapter also continues to recycle the subjunctive and clitic systems from Chapters 11 and 12 inside longer, better-organized stretches of text.

2. Learning Objectives

  • Select connectors of addition, contrast, cause, consequence, and concession appropriately for a given logical relationship.
  • Distinguish pero, sino, and sino que in contrastive statements.
  • Organize a paragraph around a single controlling idea with a clear opening, development, and closing sentence.
  • Use stance markers to signal certainty, doubt, recommendation, or evaluation without shifting register inappropriately.
  • Revise a multi-paragraph text for macro-cohesion, checking that each paragraph connects logically to the one before it.
  • Begin applying an editorial diagnostic lens — reading a passage specifically to test its logical architecture, separately from checking its sentence-level grammar.

3. Opening Communication Scenario

Camila's supervisor, Marta, has read the sentence-level revision and left a new comment on the document.

Camila: Marta dice que las oraciones están bien, pero que el documento "salta" de una idea a otra. ¿Qué crees que quiere decir?

Diego: Creo que falta conectar los párrafos. Miro esta parte: hablamos del cliente, después del inspector, y después, sin transición, del calendario. No hay nada que le diga al lector por qué pasamos de un tema al otro.

Camila: Tienes razón. Y tampoco queda claro qué pensamos nosotros. Solo describimos hechos; no decimos si recomendamos algo con confianza o si todavía tenemos dudas.

Diego: Entonces necesitamos dos cosas: conectores que muestren la relación lógica entre las ideas, y frases que muestren nuestra posición como autores del informe.

Camila: Exacto. Empecemos por organizar cada párrafo alrededor de una sola idea principal, y después conectamos los párrafos entre sí.

Language NoteIn this chapter, postura refers to a writer's evaluative position toward the content — whether they endorse it, doubt it, or qualify it — not a physical posture. Learning to name this concept helps learners talk about their own editorial choices in Spanish.

5. Pronunciation and Discourse Focus

Connectors and stance markers often carry a distinct intonational contour: a short rise or a slight pause sets them off from the clause that follows, signaling to the listener that a logical pivot is coming. Sin embargo, and por lo tanto, are typically followed by a brief pause in careful spoken Spanish, even though the written comma is the more reliable cue for learners at this stage.

Exercise 13.1 — Pausing at the pivot

6. Grammar and Sentence Patterns

6.1 Connectors of addition and sequence

Beyond y and también, formal writing uses connectors that show how ideas accumulate or unfold in order.

ConnectorFunctionExample
ademásadds a reinforcing pointEl informe está completo. Además, incluye el calendario actualizado.
en primer lugar / en segundo lugarsequences points in an argumentEn primer lugar, revisamos el borrador. En segundo lugar, lo enviamos al cliente.
asimismoadds a parallel, formal pointEl equipo aprobó el plan. Asimismo, confirmó el presupuesto.

6.2 Connectors of contrast: pero, sino, and sino que

Pero introduces a contrast after either an affirmative or a negative clause. Sino and sino que are used only after a negative clause, when the second element directly replaces or corrects the first.

ConnectorContextExample
peroaffirmative or negative clause; adds a contrasting ideaEl plan es bueno, pero necesita ajustes.
sinonegative clause + a noun, adjective, or phrase that replaces itNo es un problema técnico, sino administrativo.
sino quenegative clause + a new conjugated verb that replaces itNo rechazamos el informe, sino que pedimos una revisión.
Key ContrastUse sino or sino que only when the first clause is negative and the second element truly corrects or replaces it, not merely contrasts with it. If the first clause is affirmative, or if the second idea simply adds a contrast without replacing anything, pero is required.

6.3 Connectors of cause, consequence, and concession

RelationshipConnectorsExample
Causeporque, ya que, dado queRecomendamos esperar, ya que el informe legal todavía no está listo.
Consequencepor eso, por lo tanto, en consecuenciaEl cliente aprobó el plan. Por lo tanto, podemos avanzar.
Concessionaunque, sin embargo, no obstanteAunque el proceso fue lento, el resultado es sólido.

Aunque begins a clause and can appear at the start or middle of a sentence; sin embargo and no obstante typically open a new sentence and are followed by a comma.

6.4 Paragraph architecture

A well-organized formal paragraph in Spanish, as in English, generally follows a predictable internal shape: an opening sentence that states the paragraph's controlling idea, one or more sentences that develop or support it, and a closing or transitional sentence that either summarizes the point or connects forward to the next paragraph.

Editorial HabitBefore revising a paragraph's sentences, ask: what single idea is this paragraph responsible for? If a paragraph tries to carry two unrelated ideas, split it. If two short paragraphs share one idea, consider combining them and connecting the sentences explicitly.

6.5 Stance markers

Formal Spanish writing signals the author's position through recognizable expressions rather than through tone of voice alone.

StanceExpression
Confident recommendationrecomendamos firmemente que...; consideramos que...
Qualified recommendationen principio, recomendamos...; convendría...
Doubt or uncertaintyno está claro si...; es posible que...
Explicit qualificationcabe matizar que...; conviene señalar que...

Several of these expressions govern the subjunctive, recycling the system developed in Chapter 11: es posible que and cabe matizar que (when it expresses evaluation) both require a dependent subjunctive clause.

7. Model Dialogue: Reorganizing the Document

Diego: Mira este párrafo: "El cliente recibió el informe. El inspector todavía no lo tiene. El calendario cambió." Tres ideas, cero conexión.

Camila: Vamos a decidir cuál es la idea principal. Creo que es el estado actual del proceso de entrega.

Diego: De acuerdo. Entonces: "El cliente ya recibió el informe; sin embargo, el inspector todavía no lo tiene, ya que su copia depende de la revisión legal. Por lo tanto, el calendario original ha cambiado."

Camila: Mucho mejor. Ahora el lector entiende por qué mencionamos el calendario: es una consecuencia, no un dato aislado.

Diego: ¿Y qué hacemos con nuestra recomendación? No dijimos en ningún lado qué pensamos nosotros.

Camila: Agreguemos una postura clara: "Consideramos que el nuevo calendario es razonable, aunque convendría confirmar la fecha con el inspector antes de comunicarla al cliente."

11. Reading Practice

Exercise 13.6 — Reading for architecture

"El equipo terminó la primera revisión del informe la semana pasada. Sin embargo, todavía quedan dos secciones pendientes: el resumen ejecutivo y el anexo técnico. En primer lugar, el resumen ejecutivo necesita reflejar los cambios que hizo el equipo legal. En segundo lugar, el anexo técnico debe incluir los datos actualizados que envió el cliente el jueves. Consideramos que ambas secciones pueden completarse antes del viernes, aunque convendría confirmar con el equipo legal que no habrá más cambios de último momento. 12. Writing Workshop

Task: Take the passage you revised in Chapter 12 (or write a new 120–160 word passage describing a workplace or academic situation) and reorganize it into two or three paragraphs, each built around a single controlling idea. Use at least four different connectors from this chapter's categories (addition, contrast, cause, consequence, or concession), including at least one correct use of sino or sino que, and include at least one clear stance marker expressing your position on the situation.

Stage 1 — Plan

List the ideas in your draft and group them into two or three controlling ideas, one per planned paragraph.

Stage 2 — Draft

Write each paragraph with an opening sentence stating its controlling idea, supporting sentences, and a connector linking it to the paragraph before it. Add at least one stance marker.

Stage 3 — Revise

Read the passage as a diagnostic reader would: does each paragraph carry exactly one idea? Does every connector match its logical relationship? Is your stance clear to a first-time reader?

Integrated assessment checklist

  • Two or three paragraphs, each with a single controlling idea.
  • At least four connectors, correctly matched to their logical relationships.
  • At least one correct use of sino or sino que.
  • At least one clear stance marker.
  • Pronoun sequencing, se substitution, and antecedent clarity from Chapter 12 remain correct.

13. Culture and Communication

In many professional and academic settings across the Spanish-speaking world, a document's persuasive force depends as much on its visible logical architecture — clear transitions, an identifiable stance, paragraphs organized around single ideas — as on the accuracy of its individual sentences. Readers of formal reports, proposals, and academic writing often expect the connective tissue between ideas to be explicit rather than left for the reader to infer, which is one reason connectors and stance markers receive careful attention in professional editing across the region.

14. Common Mistakes

  • Using sino after an affirmative clause, where only pero is possible.
  • Using sino before a conjugated verb instead of sino que.
  • Stringing several unrelated ideas into one paragraph without a controlling idea.
  • Describing facts without ever stating the writer's stance toward them.
  • Overusing a single connector (typically pero or y) where a more precise connector would clarify the relationship.

15. Chapter Summary

This chapter moved the editorial lens from the sentence to the paragraph and the document: choosing connectors of addition, contrast, cause, consequence, and concession; distinguishing pero, sino, and sino que; organizing paragraphs around a single controlling idea; and marking authorial stance clearly and appropriately.

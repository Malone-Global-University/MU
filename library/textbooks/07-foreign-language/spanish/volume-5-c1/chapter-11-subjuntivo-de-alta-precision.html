Capítulo 11: Subjuntivo de alta precisión

Chapter function: Fine-grained mood selection, concession, evaluation, and nonassertion. The client dossier from Chapter 10 is now approved, and Diego and Camila have moved into a new phase of the project: drafting the formal recommendations, evaluations, and concessions that will guide the client's next decisions. This chapter opens Unit III by introducing the present subjunctive as a precision tool — not a list of triggers to memorize, but a way of marking what a speaker asserts as fact versus what a speaker frames as desired, doubted, evaluated, or merely possible.

1. Chapter Overview

Learners have already used a small number of formulaic subjunctive expressions in earlier volumes without formal analysis. This chapter activates the present subjunctive as a productive system for the first time, focused on four core functions: recommendation and desire, doubt and denial, emotional reaction and evaluation, and concession. The chapter also begins two smaller systems that will be developed fully in Chapter 12 — placing object pronouns with infinitives, gerunds, and affirmative commands, and recognizing when two object pronouns combine — introduced here only far enough to support the recommendation language this chapter requires.

2. Learning Objectives

  • Form the present subjunctive for regular, stem-changing, and high-frequency irregular verbs.
  • Choose between the indicative and the subjunctive in que clauses expressing recommendation, doubt, emotion, or evaluation.
  • Use impersonal expressions of evaluation (es importante que, es posible que, es una lástima que) with the subjunctive correctly.
  • Express concession using aunque with the correct mood, depending on whether the speaker treats the situation as fact or as hypothetical.
  • Attach an object pronoun correctly to an infinitive, a gerund, or an affirmative command, including the required written accent.
  • Write a short set of formal recommendations that combine indicative statements of fact with subjunctive statements of recommendation and evaluation.

3. Opening Communication Scenario

With the dossier approved, Diego and Camila are now drafting the formal recommendations section of the final report.

Camila: El cliente quiere que le demos recomendaciones claras, no solo un resumen de lo que ya pasó.

Diego: Es importante que las recomendaciones sean específicas. No basta con decir que todo está bien.

Camila: Dudo que necesite más permisos, pero recomiendo que el cliente contrate un inspector externo antes de empezar la construcción.

Diego: Estoy de acuerdo. Aunque el informe ambiental sea positivo, siempre es buena idea confirmarlo con una segunda opinión.

Camila: Exacto. Y aunque el cliente ya tiene el permiso, es mejor que revise el calendario de construcción con cuidado.

Diego: De acuerdo. Escríbelo tú; después dímelo y yo lo reviso.

5. Pronunciation and Discourse Focus

This chapter develops intonation for expressing tentativeness and evaluation. A recommendation stated with recomiendo que should carry a slightly rising, measured intonation, distinct from the flatter, more confident intonation used for a stated fact.

Exercise 11.1 — Fact versus recommendation

Read each pair aloud, noting the shift in tone: "El permiso está aprobado." (fact, flat intonation) then "Recomiendo que el cliente contrate un inspector." (recommendation, measured, slightly rising on contrate).

6. Grammar and Sentence Patterns

6.1 Forming the present subjunctive

Start from the yo form of the present indicative, drop the -o, and add the "opposite vowel" endings.

Infinitiveyotúusted/él/ellanosotrosustedes/ellos
hablarhablehableshablehablemoshablen
comercomacomascomacomamoscoman
escribirescribaescribasescribaescribamosescriban
tenertengatengastengatengamostengan

A small set of verbs is irregular in the subjunctive because their yo form does not follow the pattern: ser → sea, estar → esté, ir → vaya, saber → sepa, haber → haya, dar → dé.

6.2 When the subjunctive appears: four functions

FunctionPatternExample
Recommendation / desire[verb of influence] + que + subjunctiveRecomiendo que el cliente contrate un inspector.
Doubt / denial[verb of doubt] + que + subjunctiveDudo que necesite más permisos.
Emotion / evaluationes + adjective + que + subjunctiveEs importante que las recomendaciones sean específicas.
Concession (hypothetical)aunque + subjunctiveAunque el informe sea positivo, es buena idea confirmarlo.
Key ContrastThe subjunctive is not triggered by a fixed list of "magic words." It is triggered by what the main clause does to the information in the que clause. Compare: Sé que el permiso está aprobado (asserted as fact — indicative) versus Dudo que el permiso esté aprobado (framed as uncertain — subjunctive). Same information, different mood, because the speaker's stance toward it changed.

6.3 Aunque: fact versus hypothesis

Aunque takes the indicative when the speaker treats the situation as a known fact, and the subjunctive when the speaker treats it as hypothetical, unconfirmed, or irrelevant to the outcome.

Speaker's stanceMoodExample
Known factindicativeAunque el informe es positivo, vamos a confirmarlo. (We know it's positive.)
Hypothetical / not confirmedsubjunctiveAunque el informe sea positivo, vamos a confirmarlo. (Whether or not it turns out positive.)

6.4 A first look at pronoun attachment (previewed for Chapter 12)

When an object pronoun attaches to an infinitive, a gerund, or an affirmative command, it is written as one word, and a written accent is added if the stress would otherwise shift: dímelo (from di + me + lo), revisándolo, contrátalo. This chapter introduces this pattern only within recommendation language; Chapter 12 develops it fully, including double-object sequencing.

Boundary NoteDo not introduce the imperfect subjunctive, sequence-of-tense rules, or full double-object clitic combinations in this chapter. Those systems are scheduled for later chapters.

7. Model Dialogue: Drafting the Recommendations

Diego: Entonces, ¿qué recomendamos primero?

Camila: Recomiendo que el cliente contrate un inspector independiente antes de empezar la construcción. Es importante que quede documentado todo el proceso.

Diego: Estoy de acuerdo. También sugiero que revisemos el calendario con el equipo legal. Dudo que haya un conflicto, pero es mejor confirmarlo.

Camila: Aunque el permiso ya esté aprobado, recomiendo que el cliente guarde una copia física en la oficina.

Diego: Buena idea. Escríbelo así: claro y directo. Después mándamelo para revisarlo juntos.

12. Writing Workshop

Task: Write a short set of formal recommendations (120–150 words) for a project or decision of your choice, addressed to a client or supervisor. Your text must include at least two recommendation-trigger sentences, one doubt-trigger sentence, one impersonal evaluation sentence, and one aunque clause with the mood chosen correctly for your intended meaning.

Stage 1 — Plan

Choose your topic and decide which points are facts (indicative) and which are recommendations, doubts, or evaluations (subjunctive).

Stage 2 — Draft

Write your recommendations, checking each subjunctive verb form as you go.

Stage 3 — Revise

Check subjunctive verb endings, the mood chosen after aunque, and any pronoun attachment used in a command or infinitive.

Integrated assessment checklist

  • At least two recommendation-trigger sentences with correct subjunctive forms.
  • At least one doubt-trigger sentence with correct subjunctive forms.
  • At least one impersonal evaluation sentence with correct subjunctive forms.
  • One aunque clause with the mood matching the intended meaning.
  • No imperfect subjunctive or double-object clitic combinations required.

13. Culture and Communication

Formal recommendation language, including careful mood choice, is common in professional, consulting, and advisory writing across the Spanish-speaking world. The specific phrasing conventions can vary by workplace and country, but the underlying skill — separating what is asserted as fact from what is offered as advice or evaluation — is broadly valued in professional communication.

14. Common Mistakes

  • Using the indicative after a clear recommendation or doubt trigger, such as Recomiendo que el cliente contrata... instead of contrate.
  • Treating aunque as always requiring the subjunctive, regardless of whether the situation is a known fact.
  • Forgetting the written accent when a pronoun attaches to a command, infinitive, or gerund.
  • Overgeneralizing the subjunctive to any que clause, including simple statements of fact.
  • Using the present subjunctive where the imperfect subjunctive would eventually be required in later, more complex sentences — not expected at this stage.

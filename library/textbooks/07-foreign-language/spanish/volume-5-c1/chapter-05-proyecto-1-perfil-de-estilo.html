Capítulo 5: Proyecto 1 — Perfil de estilo

Chapter function: Integrated assessment. Learners analyze a text for lexical precision, audience design, and implied meaning, then produce and deliver their own text with the same controlled precision — combining the collocation, tone, inference, and oral-delivery systems developed across Chapters 1–4. No major new productive grammar system is introduced.

1. Chapter Overview

Diego tells Camila that a "perfil de estilo" — a style profile — is what a careful writer or speaker builds before producing anything important: an honest sense of who is listening, what words will land, and what the delivery itself communicates beyond the words. This project chapter asks learners to build one. Working from a short text, learners will identify precise word choices versus vague ones, name the intended audience and tone, uncover what is implied rather than stated, and then apply all of it by writing and delivering a short piece of their own. The chapter also formalizes a small, already-familiar piece of business: using ustedes confidently and consistently as the course's plural address form in both writing and speech, now that Chapters 1–4 have used it naturally in context. No new vocabulary domain, sound system, or grammar paradigm is introduced; this chapter is where Unit I's skills are shown to work together.

2. Learning Objectives

  • Analyze a short text for precise word choice, collocation, and register consistency.
  • Identify the intended audience, tone, and rhetorical stance of a text or spoken passage.
  • Identify at least one significant implied meaning that is not stated directly in a text.
  • Use ustedes consistently and confidently as the course's productive plural address form, in both writing and speech.
  • Plan, draft, revise, and deliver a short original text that reflects a deliberate style profile, including marked stress and pause for oral delivery.

3. Opening Communication Scenario

Diego asks Camila to bring a draft message she wrote for her team so they can build her a style profile together before she sends it.

Camila: Aquí está. Es corta, pero no sé si suena profesional o si suena fría.

Diego: Antes de corregir nada, dime: ¿quién la va a leer?

Camila: Todo el equipo. Unas ocho personas, algunas que casi no conozco.

Diego: Entonces ese es tu primer dato de estilo: audiencia mixta, cierta distancia. Ahora, léela y dime qué palabra usaste para pedirles el informe.

Camila: Escribí "necesito que me envíen el informe."

Diego: Directo, pero no grosero, si lo dices con el tono correcto. Y noto que usaste "ustedes" para dirigirte a todos, no una mezcla de formas.

Camila: Sí, me pareció lo más natural para un grupo así.

Diego: Exacto. Un perfil de estilo junta todo eso: precisión, audiencia, tono, y cómo suena cuando lo dices en voz alta.

6. Grammar and Sentence Patterns

No new productive grammar system is introduced in this chapter. This section consolidates one small, already-familiar item: full, confident, consistent use of ustedes as the course's plural address form, in both speech and writing.

6.1 Ustedes as the consistent plural address form

Since Chapter 1, dialogues and texts in this course have used ustedes naturally whenever a speaker addressed more than one person. This chapter simply confirms what has already been true in practice: under this course's Latin American production model, ustedes is the single, default form for addressing any group — mixed, familiar, unfamiliar, formal, or informal. Learners should now use it with full confidence and without hesitating over an English "you-all" gap.

ContextExampleNote
Addressing colleagues, mixed familiarityNecesito que me envíen el informe.implied ustedes; verb form marks the address
Addressing a group directlyUstedes ya conocen el procedimiento.explicit subject for emphasis or clarity
Closing a group messageGracias a todos ustedes por su paciencia.natural closing pattern, recycled register vocabulary
Language NoteThis is not new grammar: the ustedes verb forms have appeared since Volume I. What changes here is expectation — learners are now accountable for using ustedes consistently, rather than only recognizing it. Receptive vosotros/vosotras and vos remain recognition-only and are not required or assessed in this project.

7. Model Dialogue: Building a Style Profile

Diego: Vamos a analizar este correo que circuló en tu oficina. Léelo y dime: ¿cuál es la palabra más vaga de todo el texto?

Camila: Dice "vamos a revisar el tema pronto." "Tema" y "pronto" no dicen nada concreto.

Diego: Bien. ¿Y qué crees que el autor realmente quiere decir, aunque no lo diga?

Camila: Creo que no tiene una fecha todavía y no quiere admitirlo.

Diego: Ese es el trasfondo. Ahora, ¿a quién le escribe?

Camila: A todo el departamento. Por eso usa "ustedes" en vez de nombrar a cada persona.

Diego: Exacto. Precisión, audiencia, trasfondo: ya tienes tres partes del perfil. La cuarta es cómo sonaría si alguien lo leyera en voz alta en una reunión.

Camila: Sonaría dudoso. Sin pausas claras, todo el mensaje suena igual de inseguro.

Diego: Entonces ya sabes exactamente qué evitar en tu propio texto.

Exercise 5.3 — Audience and tone identification

For each opening line, identify the likely audience (colleagues, close friends, an institution, an unfamiliar group) and describe the tone in one word (neutral, warm, distant, urgent).

  1. Estimados colegas, les escribo para informarles sobre un cambio importante.
  2. Oigan, ¿ya vieron lo que pasó ayer? No lo van a creer.
  3. Por medio de la presente, se les notifica que el trámite ha sido aprobado.

Exercise 5.4 — Finding the trasfondo

Read the exchange and answer: what does the second speaker actually mean, beyond the literal words?

A: ¿Vas a poder terminar el proyecto para el viernes?

B: Voy a intentarlo, si no surge nada más.

9. Listening Practice

Listening Script for Instructor, Partner, or Text-to-Speech Use

Una coordinadora se dirige a su equipo: "Buenas tardes a todos. Quiero agradecerles el trabajo de este mes... y también pedirles un poco más de paciencia. Sé que ustedes han hecho un gran esfuerzo. Todavía estamos ajustando algunos detalles del nuevo sistema, y no quiero prometerles una fecha que después no podamos cumplir."

Exercise 5.5 — Listening for the full profile

  1. Who is the intended audience, and what evidence in the script tells you this?
  2. Identify one vague phrase and explain what it may be avoiding stating directly.
  3. Where would you expect a pause, and what does that pause communicate about the speaker's confidence?
  4. Which address form does the speaker use throughout, and is it used consistently?

10. Speaking Practice

Exercise 5.6 — Deliver your analysis

With a partner, exchange the passage from Exercise 5.2 or 5.4 and explain aloud, in your own words, the audience, tone, and trasfondo you identified. Your partner should ask at least one follow-up question and note whether your explanation used ustedes consistently when referring to a group.

Solo-study alternative: Record yourself explaining your analysis of one passage aloud in under one minute, then listen back and check whether your explanation was precise, consistent in address form, and clearly paced.

11. Reading Practice

Exercise 5.7 — Full-profile reading

Read the short passage below and complete the four-part analysis that follows.

"Les escribo porque quiero ser honesta con ustedes: el proyecto no avanza al ritmo que esperábamos. No estoy diciendo que vayamos a fallar, sino que necesitamos ajustar el plan. Contamos con la experiencia de todos ustedes para encontrar una solución esta semana."

  1. Precisión léxica: Which word choice most clearly signals honesty rather than vague reassurance?
  2. Audiencia y tono: Who is this addressed to, and how would you describe the tone?
  3. Trasfondo: What does the writer imply about the team's role beyond simply stating it?
  4. Entrega oral: Mark where you would pause and which word you would stress if reading this aloud.

12. Writing Workshop — Project Draft

Task: Write a short text (80–110 words) — a message, announcement, or brief update — addressed to a group you specify (classmates, colleagues, a club, a family group). Your text must reflect a deliberate style profile: precise rather than vague vocabulary, a clear and consistent tone for your chosen audience, at least one instance of implied meaning that a careful reader could interpret, and consistent use of ustedes wherever you address the group.

Stage 1 — Plan

Before drafting, note: your audience, your intended tone in one word, and one thing you want the reader to infer without stating it directly.

Stage 2 — Draft

Write your text, applying your plan.

Stage 3 — Revise and Mark for Delivery

Revise for lexical precision and address-form consistency, then mark your final draft for oral delivery: underline words for stress and insert ellipses (...) for pauses.

Interpretation-awareness checklist

  • The text names or clearly implies its intended audience.
  • At least three word choices were deliberately selected over a vaguer alternative.
  • At least one sentence carries meaning beyond its literal content.
  • Ustedes is used consistently whenever the group is addressed, with no other plural address form mixed in.
  • The draft is marked for stress and pause, ready to be read aloud.
  • The written grammar uses only structures already consolidated in prior volumes.

13. Culture and Communication

A style profile is not only a linguistic exercise; it reflects real expectations about how groups are addressed across Spanish-speaking professional and social contexts. In many workplaces and organizations across Latin America, addressing a mixed group consistently and respectfully — without singling out individuals by an unclear form of address — is itself a marker of competence and courtesy. This does not mean every workplace communicates identically: some favor a warmer, more personal opening; others favor a brisker, more businesslike one. What stays constant is the expectation that the writer or speaker has thought about who is listening before choosing how to speak.

14. Common Mistakes

  • Choosing a vague word because it feels safer, rather than a precise word that actually communicates the intended meaning.
  • Shifting address forms mid-text — for example, drifting toward an unscheduled form instead of maintaining ustedes consistently.
  • Stating every idea directly and leaving no room for appropriate, natural implication.
  • Ignoring oral delivery when a text is meant to be read aloud, producing a flat, unmarked reading.
  • Treating audience analysis as a one-time step rather than checking it against every sentence of the draft.

15. Chapter Summary

This project chapter integrated the lexical precision, audience and tone awareness, inference skills, and oral delivery control developed across Chapters 1–4 into a single style profile, applied first to an existing text and then to learners' own writing and speech. It also confirmed ustedes as the course's fully consistent, confidently productive plural address form. No major new grammar, vocabulary domain, or sound system was introduced; the chapter's purpose was to demonstrate that Unit I's systems work together in real communication.

Capítulo 20: Proyecto final: Dominio avanzado en español

Chapter function: Final advanced capstone across interpretation, specialized writing, oral performance, and editorial precision. Two weeks after the portfolio meeting, the coordinating committee invites the reading circle back for a single culminating session: each member will present one integrated project that draws on every register practiced across the volume, defend it under live questioning, and mediate a piece of it for an audience outside the original context. No new grammar system is introduced this chapter. Instead, learners orchestrate, in one sustained performance, every system consolidated since Chapter 6.

1. Chapter Overview

This chapter closes Volume V with no new grammar system of its own. Its purpose is integration at the highest scale the course has asked for: a single project that moves across academic, professional, civic, scientific, literary, and argumentative registers, edited to the volume's editorial standard, defended orally under unscripted questioning, and adapted for a new audience through mediation. Where Chapter 19 asked learners to assemble and justify a portfolio of separate texts, Chapter 20 asks them to build one coherent project that demonstrates the same range inside a single sustained piece of work, then explain and defend it as a whole. Every system used here — mood precision, clitic and reference control, textual architecture, the four-pass edit, rhetorical subjunctive, translation and mediation, and critical debate response — was already active by the end of Chapter 18 and rehearsed again in Chapter 19.

2. Learning Objectives

  • Plan and produce one integrated final project that moves coherently across at least four registers developed in the volume.
  • Apply the four-pass edit to a long, multi-section text without losing register or referential consistency across sections.
  • Mediate one section of the project for a different audience, adjusting register, structure, and detail while preserving meaning.
  • Defend the completed project orally, responding to unscripted questions about mood choice, structure, register, and editorial decisions.
  • Synthesize, in Spanish, an account of how the project demonstrates growth across the volume as a whole.
  • Evaluate a peer's final project using the volume's editorial and rhetorical standards, offering specific, constructive feedback in Spanish.

3. Opening Communication Scenario

The coordinating committee has asked each member of the reading circle to present one final integrated project: a single dossier that moves from a short academic framing, through a professional recommendation, to a civic-facing summary, closing with a brief reflective or literary passage. Marta calls the group together to plan the structure before anyone begins drafting.

Marta: La idea no es escribir cuatro textos sueltos, como en el portafolio. Es un solo proyecto con cuatro movimientos que se conectan entre sí. El comité quiere ver que pueden sostener el hilo de un tema a través de distintos registros.

Diego: ¿Entonces todos tenemos que usar el mismo tema en las cuatro partes?

Marta: Sí. Elijan un tema que les importe de verdad —algo del barrio, del centro cultural, de su trabajo— y síganlo desde el marco académico hasta el cierre reflexivo.

Camila: ¿Y la mediación? Vi en la guía que hay que adaptar una parte para otro público.

Marta: Exacto. Van a tomar la sección profesional o cívica y reescribirla para un público que no comparte el contexto técnico: un vecino, un familiar, alguien nuevo en la comunidad. Ahí se ve si de verdad entendieron el registro, no solo si saben imitarlo.

Diego: Y al final, la defensa oral otra vez.

Marta: Otra vez, pero ahora sobre un proyecto entero, no sobre un solo texto. Van a tener que explicar por qué el conjunto funciona como un todo.

Camila: Es como cerrar el círculo de todo el año.

Marta: Ese es exactamente el objetivo. Empecemos por elegir el tema.

5. Pronunciation and Discourse Focus

A capstone defense combines two demands the course has practiced separately: the sustained, level pacing of a formal presentation and the calm, unscripted responsiveness of an oral defense. This chapter asks learners to move between the two within a single session — shifting delivery style as they move from presenting a planned section to answering an unplanned question — without a jarring change in intelligibility or confidence.

Exercise 20.1 — Shifting from presentation to defense

Read the following two-sentence excerpt aloud in a prepared, presentational pace: "El proyecto sigue el tema de la biblioteca comunitaria a través de cuatro registros distintos. Cada sección se dirige a un lector diferente, pero todas comparten la misma preocupación central." Then answer aloud, without notes and after a genuine pause, the question: "¿Por qué decidió empezar con el marco académico y no con la sección cívica?" Notice the shift in pacing and register between the two.

6.2 Sustaining a throughline: metalanguage for the capstone

Presenting an integrated project requires language that names the connection between its parts, not only the content of each part. This metalanguage draws on comparison, concession, and cohesion patterns already consolidated in the volume.

FunctionPatternExample
Naming the throughlineLo que conecta las cuatro secciones es...Lo que conecta las cuatro secciones es la pregunta de quién tiene acceso real a la biblioteca comunitaria, planteada primero en términos académicos y, al final, en términos personales.
Justifying a structural decisionDecidí empezar por... para que...Decidí empezar por el marco académico para que el lector entendiera el problema antes de encontrarse con una recomendación concreta.
Explaining a mediation choiceAl adaptar esta parte, cambié... mientras mantenía...Al adaptar esta parte para un vecino sin formación técnica, cambié el vocabulario especializado y acorté las oraciones, mientras mantenía la recomendación central intacta.
Editorial HabitAn integrated project fails when its sections read as four separate assignments stapled together. Before submitting, write one sentence — aloud or on paper — that states what a reader who read only the first and last sections would still understand about the project's central concern.

7. Reading Excerpt: Two Openings for the Same Throughline

The reading circle compares two possible opening sentences for Diego's capstone project on the community library, one drafted quickly and one revised for the academic framing section.

Borrador rápido:

La biblioteca del barrio tiene problemas de horario y de espacio, y mucha gente no puede usarla como quisiera.

Versión revisada para el marco académico:

El acceso desigual a los recursos bibliotecarios comunitarios no se explica únicamente por limitaciones de horario o de espacio físico, sino por una combinación de factores estructurales que este proyecto examina a través de cuatro registros complementarios.

Exercise 20.2 — Comparing openings across a throughline

List two specific changes between the two versions (level of abstraction, sentence complexity, or framing of cause). For each change, write one sentence explaining how the revised version prepares the reader for the academic-to-civic movement that follows across the rest of the project.

11. Reading Practice

Exercise 20.7 — Reading a closing reflection

Read the closing reflection below, written by Diego for the final section of his capstone project.

Empecé este proyecto pensando que el marco académico y la sección cívica eran, en el fondo, dos maneras distintas de decir lo mismo. Al revisarlas juntas, entendí que no: el marco académico explica por qué existe el problema; la sección cívica explica qué puede hacer una persona concreta al respecto. No son la misma idea con otro vocabulario, sino dos preguntas distintas que un mismo proyecto puede sostener a la vez. Si algo aprendí este año, además del español, es que el registro no es un disfraz que uno se pone encima del contenido, sino una decisión sobre a quién le habla y para qué.

Identify the distinction Diego draws between the academic and civic sections, and explain, in your own words, the general principle about register he states in the final sentence.

12. Writing Workshop

Task: Produce the complete final capstone project: four connected sections (academic framing, professional or civic recommendation, scientific or technical commentary, reflective or literary closing) built around a single throughline, plus one mediated section for a lay audience. The full project should run approximately 500–700 words across all sections combined. Then write a one-paragraph synthesis (80–120 words) explaining, using at least two patterns from Section 6.2, how the project demonstrates growth across the volume.

Stage 1 — Choose the throughline and outline the four sections

State your theme and write one sentence per section describing its register, its intended reader, and its specific contribution to the throughline.

Stage 2 — Draft all four sections and the mediated section

Draft the full project without stopping to correct mechanics. Draft the mediated version of your chosen section immediately after its original, while the register contrast is fresh.

Stage 3 — Apply the four-pass edit to the whole project

Apply the four-pass procedure from Chapter 14 — meaning and mood, reference and agreement, mechanics, and register and regional consistency — to the project as a single connected text, checking especially that pronoun reference and connectors still carry meaning correctly across section boundaries.

Stage 4 — Write the synthesis paragraph

Write the closing synthesis explaining how the finished project demonstrates range and growth, then apply the four-pass edit to the synthesis itself.

Integrated assessment checklist

  • The project contains four connected sections representing at least four distinct registers from the volume.
  • One section has been mediated for a lay audience, preserving meaning while adjusting vocabulary, structure, and detail.
  • A clear throughline connects the first and last sections, named explicitly in the synthesis paragraph.
  • At least two sentences in the synthesis use a pattern from Section 6.2.
  • No major new grammar system was needed to complete the task; all systems used were already active in the volume.
  • The four-pass edit was applied to the entire project, including transitions between sections.

13. Culture and Communication

Integrated, multi-register projects of the kind this chapter asks for are common wherever advanced language users must speak to more than one audience about the same issue: a researcher writing both a journal abstract and a public explainer, a civil servant drafting both a legal notice and a plain-language summary, a community organizer moving between a funder's report and a neighborhood meeting. Expectations for how much a register may shift within a single project — and how explicitly the connections between sections should be signaled — vary by institution, field, and region, and are shaped as much by professional convention as by the language itself. Learners should treat the four-register structure in this chapter as one useful training model among several possible ones, not as a fixed template for every real-world context.

14. Common Mistakes

  • Writing four disconnected texts instead of one project with a genuine throughline.
  • Mediating a section by simplifying vocabulary alone while leaving the sentence structure and organization unchanged.
  • Losing pronoun and reference clarity when moving between sections written for different audiences.
  • Describing the project's structure in the synthesis paragraph instead of explaining why that structure serves the throughline.
  • Answering defense questions with a general summary instead of the specific decision the question asks about.

15. Chapter Summary

This final chapter introduced no new grammar system. Instead, learners orchestrated every major system consolidated across Volume V — register-sensitive vocabulary, mood precision, clitic and reference control, textual architecture, the four-pass edit, translation and mediation, and critical debate response — inside one sustained, multi-register capstone project. Learners planned a throughline, drafted four connected sections and one mediated section, edited the whole project as a single coherent text, and defended it orally under unscripted questioning. The chapter's central work, and the central work of the volume it closes, was register judgment: recognizing what a given audience and purpose require and explaining, in Spanish, why a specific set of choices serves them.

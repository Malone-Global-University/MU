Capítulo 15: Proyecto 3: Edición profesional

Chapter function: Integrated assessment — edit, annotate, and justify corrections to an advanced text. This chapter does not introduce new grammar. It asks learners to apply, under realistic conditions and without a running commentary telling them which pass to use next, everything built across Chapters 11–14: precision mood selection, clitic and reference control, textual architecture, and the four-pass editorial procedure. Marta hands Diego and Camila one final, unmarked draft and asks them to return it ready to send — and to be able to explain, out loud, why each change was necessary.

1. Chapter Overview

This project chapter consolidates Unit III into a single integrated performance. Learners receive a longer, unannotated advanced text and are asked to complete a full editorial pass on it independently: diagnosing meaning and mood, reference and agreement, mechanics, and register and regional consistency, exactly as practiced in Chapter 14, but without the passage already broken into isolated categories for them. The chapter also asks learners to produce a short written justification memo — the kind of document a professional editor sends back with a corrected file — and to defend at least three corrections orally. No new productive grammar system is introduced; the goal is fluent, independent application of the systems already active.

2. Learning Objectives

  • Independently apply the four-pass editing procedure to an unannotated advanced text without being told which category of error to look for at each stage.
  • Correctly identify and repair errors of mood selection, reference and agreement, mechanics, and register within a single connected document.
  • Distinguish an error that belongs to the current chapter's editing scope from a stylistic choice that does not require correction.
  • Write a concise editorial justification memo that explains, in formal usted register, the reasoning behind a set of corrections.
  • Defend at least three corrections orally, citing the specific rule or contrast that governs each one.
  • Complete a full four-skill integrated performance — reading, writing, listening, and speaking — without introducing any unscheduled system.

3. Opening Communication Scenario

Marta has one more document for the team — but this time she does not walk them through it.

Marta: Aquí tienen el último borrador. Esta vez no les voy a decir dónde están los problemas. Quiero que ustedes hagan la corrección completa, como lo harían si yo no estuviera disponible para revisarlo con ustedes.

Diego: Entendido. ¿Debemos seguir las cuatro lecturas que practicamos?

Marta: Sí, y además quiero un breve memorando explicando los cambios más importantes, como si se lo enviara a un cliente exigente.

Camila: ¿Y si encontramos algo que no estamos seguros de si es un error o solo una preferencia de estilo?

Marta: Buena pregunta. Anótenlo aparte. No todo lo que suena distinto está mal; a veces es simplemente una elección válida. Distinguir entre las dos cosas es parte de lo que estoy evaluando.

Diego: Entonces, manos a la obra.

5. Pronunciation and Discourse Focus

Before editing, read the full draft aloud once, at a natural pace, without stopping to mark anything. This chapter reinforces the Chapter 14 habit of using the ear as a first diagnostic tool, but now applies it to a longer, connected text rather than isolated sentences, so that stress, rhythm, and phrasing can reveal problems that are easy to miss when reading silently.

6. Grammar and Sentence Patterns

6.1 No new system — full recall of the four-pass procedure

This chapter activates no new grammar. Learners are expected to recall and apply, without prompting, the full editing procedure introduced in Chapter 14.

PassFocusGuiding question
1Meaning and moodDoes every clause say what the writer intended? Is the subjunctive used where evaluation, doubt, or necessity requires it?
2Reference and agreementDoes every pronoun point clearly to one antecedent? Does every article, noun, and adjective agree, including across distance and coordination?
3MechanicsAre accents, punctuation, and capitalization correct throughout, across all four accent categories?
4Register and regional consistencyDoes the whole document maintain one address system, one consistent formality level, and the declared Latin American production baseline?

6.2 Distinguishing an error from a stylistic choice

Advanced editing requires more than catching mistakes; it requires deciding what actually needs to change. Two sentences can express the same idea correctly in different ways.

CategoryTestExample
Actual errorThe sentence violates a grammar, agreement, mechanics, or register rule established for this document.El presupuesto y la propuesta fue aprobada → fueron aprobados (agreement rule, not a preference).
Stylistic choiceThe sentence is grammatically correct and register-consistent; a different phrasing is possible but not required.El informe está listo. and Ya está listo el informe. are both acceptable in this register.
Editorial HabitWhen in doubt, apply the same test Marta describes: can you name the specific rule the sentence breaks? If you cannot name one, the sentence is very likely a stylistic choice, not an error — leave it, and note it separately if it is worth mentioning to the writer.

6.3 Writing an editorial justification memo

A finished correction is not enough in professional contexts; an editor typically explains the most important changes briefly, in formal register, so the original writer can learn from them.

Memo elementFunction
Opening lineStates that the review is complete and the document is ready, or nearly ready, to send.
Summary of major categoriesGroups corrections by type (mood, agreement, mechanics, register) rather than listing every instance individually.
One or two specific examples per categoryGives the writer a concrete instance and a one-sentence reason, not just a rule name.
Closing noteConfirms next steps or requests final confirmation from the writer.

Exercise 15.4 — Error or style?

For each pair, decide whether the second sentence contains an actual error or is simply a valid stylistic alternative to the first. Justify your answer in one sentence.

  2. El cliente confirmó la fecha. / El cliente confirma la fecha. (referring to an event that already happened)
  3. Es posible que el inspector llegue tarde. / Es posible que el inspector llega tarde.

9. Listening Practice

Listening Script for Instructor, Partner, or Text-to-Speech Use

Diego resume su proceso de corrección para Marta: "Primero leímos todo el documento sin marcar nada, solo para escuchar cómo sonaba. Después hicimos las cuatro lecturas: la primera para el sentido y el modo, y ahí encontramos que 'revisa' debía ser 'revise', porque 'es importante que' exige subjuntivo. En la segunda lectura corregimos la concordancia: 'están completas' en vez de 'está completo', y 'fueron aprobados' en vez de 'fueron aprobado'. En la tercera lectura corregimos los acentos que faltaban, como 'versión', 'revisión' y 'reunión', y agregamos el signo de interrogación que faltaba al principio de la pregunta sobre la fecha. En la última lectura encontramos un 'tú' que rompía el registro 'usted' del resto del documento, y lo cambiamos a 'usted debería'. No cambiamos nada más, porque el resto nos pareció una cuestión de estilo, no un error."

11. Reading Practice

Exercise 15.7 — Reading a colleague's corrected version

12. Writing Workshop

Task: Complete the full editorial project: a corrected version of the Section 7 draft, plus a short justification memo (100–150 words) addressed to Marta in formal usted register, explaining the most important categories of correction you made.

Stage 1 — Finalize the corrected draft

Using your work from Exercises 15.2 and 15.3, produce a clean, fully corrected version of the Section 7 text with no remaining marks or brackets.

Stage 2 — Draft the justification memo

Write a short memo to Marta. Group your explanation by editing pass rather than listing every individual correction, and give at least one specific example per category you mention.

Stage 3 — Proofread the memo itself

Apply the four-pass procedure to your own memo before submitting it. A justification memo with its own accent or agreement errors undermines its credibility.

Integrated assessment checklist

  • Completed a distinct pass for meaning/mood before correcting mechanics.
  • Corrected every actual error in the draft, across all four passes.
  • Left stylistic alternatives uncorrected and, where relevant, noted them separately.
  • Maintained one consistent usted address form and formal register throughout the corrected draft.
  • Confirmed no unlabeled regional forms appear in the final version.
  • Wrote a memo that groups corrections by category with at least one concrete example each.
  • Proofread the memo itself using the same four-pass procedure.

13. Culture and Communication

In many professional and academic settings across Spanish-speaking institutions, a short written explanation accompanying a corrected document is considered a courtesy as much as a technical necessity: it helps the original writer improve rather than simply receiving a marked-up file. This convention is common in editorial, legal, and academic environments where documents pass through several rounds of review, and it reflects a broader professional norm in many workplaces that feedback should be specific and instructive rather than only corrective.

14. Common Mistakes

  • Correcting a stylistic choice as though it were an error, which can make a justification memo less credible.
  • Listing every individual correction in the memo instead of grouping them by category, which buries the most important points.
  • Skipping the read-aloud step and moving straight into passes, which tends to miss mechanical errors that sound wrong.
  • Forgetting to proofread the memo itself with the same rigor applied to the main document.
  • Defending a correction by saying it "sounds better" instead of naming the specific rule involved.

15. Chapter Summary

This project chapter asked learners to apply, independently and without step-by-step prompting, the full editorial system built across Unit III: mood precision, clitic and reference control, textual architecture, and the four-pass mechanical and register review from Chapter 14. It added the professional skill of distinguishing an actual error from a valid stylistic choice, and the practical task of writing a short justification memo explaining corrections in formal register. No new grammar system was introduced; the chapter measured independent, integrated control of everything already active.

Exercise 15.3 (model error list)

1. version→versión, revision→revisión, reunion→reunión — mechanics (regular stress accents).
2. esta de acuerdo→están de acuerdo — agreement (plural coordinated subject "el comité y la gerencia").
3. revisa→revise — meaning/mood ("es importante que" requires subjunctive).
4. tecnico→técnico, estan→están — mechanics (regular stress accents).
5. estan completo→están completas — agreement (plural feminine subject "las recomendaciones").
6. tu deberias→usted debería — register (document otherwise uses "usted") and mechanics (missing accent on "debería").
7. fueron aprobado→fueron aprobados — agreement (coordinated plural subject, masculine plural default).
8. Cuando podemos→¿Cuándo podemos — mechanics (missing opening question mark and interrogative accent).

Exercise 15.4 (model answers)

1. Stylistic choice — both word orders are grammatically correct and equally formal.
2. Actual error — the context describes a completed past event, so the preterite confirmó is required; the present tense misrepresents the timeline.
3. Actual error — es posible que requires the subjunctive llegue, not the indicative llega.
4. Stylistic choice — both express attentive availability appropriately in formal correspondence.

Exercise 15.6 (sample defense language)

"Cambié 'revisa' por 'revise' porque la expresión 'es importante que' exige subjuntivo en la cláusula que sigue; no es una preferencia, es una regla de dependencia gramatical." / "Cambié 'tu deberías' por 'usted debería' porque todo el documento usa 'usted', y ese 'tú' rompía el registro establecido; el error no es gramatical, es de consistencia de registro."

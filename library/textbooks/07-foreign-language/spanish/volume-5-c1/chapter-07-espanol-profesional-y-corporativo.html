Capítulo 7: Español profesional y corporativo

Chapter function: Executive summaries, proposals, negotiation, and risk and compliance language. Learners extend the depersonalized, nominalized register built in Chapter 6 into the corporate world: structuring a written proposal, softening and hedging in negotiation, framing risk and recommendation, and using the idiomatic prepositions and register-sensitive ser/estar patterns that professional Spanish requires — all built on grammar already consolidated in earlier volumes.

1. Chapter Overview

Camila's seminar paper caught the attention of a local research-consulting firm, and she has been asked to sit in on a client negotiation with Diego, who works there part-time. The language of the meeting room is not the language of the seminar room: proposals need a persuasive but professional structure, risk has to be named without alarming the client, and disagreement has to be softened rather than stated flatly. This chapter teaches the vocabulary, hedging strategies, and clause patterns of professional and corporate Spanish — executive summaries, formal proposals, negotiation language, and risk/compliance framing — building on the nominalization and depersonalization introduced in Chapter 6.

2. Learning Objectives

  • Use professional and corporate vocabulary to summarize a proposal, describe a risk, and frame a recommendation.
  • Structure a short executive summary with a clear purpose, key point, and recommendation.
  • Use hedging and softening expressions to negotiate, disagree, and make requests in a professional register.
  • Apply advanced idiomatic prepositions (en cuanto a, con respecto a, de acuerdo con, en base a) to organize professional discourse.
  • Recognize formulaic recommendation and risk expressions built on que clauses, without full subjunctive paradigm analysis.
  • Participate in a short negotiation role-play and write a one-paragraph executive summary.

3. Opening Communication Scenario

Before the meeting, Diego briefs Camila on how the conversation with the client is likely to go.

Diego: El cliente va a preguntar por los riesgos del proyecto. No conviene decir "esto no va a funcionar" así, tan directo.

Camila: ¿Y qué digo en cambio?

Diego: Algo como "convendría considerar" o "sería recomendable evaluar" antes de dar una opinión tajante. En cuanto al presupuesto, mejor decir "de acuerdo con los datos disponibles" en vez de "yo calculé que."

Camila: ¿Y si no estoy de acuerdo con algo que propone el cliente?

Diego: Ahí es donde más se nota el registro profesional. En vez de "eso no me parece bien," puedes decir "entendemos la propuesta, aunque cabría considerar una alternativa" o "con respecto a ese punto, tenemos ciertas reservas."

Camila: Es como el registro académico, pero para negociar en vez de argumentar.

Diego: Exacto. La distancia es la misma; lo que cambia es el objetivo.

5. Pronunciation and Discourse Focus

No new sound system is introduced. This section develops the phrasing control from Chapter 6 for the shorter, more clipped sentence rhythm typical of spoken negotiation, where hedging phrases often form their own short breath group before the main clause.

6. Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter develops three systems already active since Chapter 6 — nominalization, depersonalized reference, and register-sensitive ser/estar — into professional contexts, and develops the advanced idiomatic prepositions and formulaic recommendation patterns introduced in earlier volumes.

6.1 Advanced idiomatic prepositions in professional discourse

Professional Spanish relies on a small set of idiomatic prepositional phrases to introduce a source, a topic, or a basis for a claim. These extend the preposition system already consolidated, without introducing new grammar.

ExpressionFunctionExample
de acuerdo conintroduces a source or basisDe acuerdo con el informe, el plazo es viable.
en base aintroduces a basis (common but stylistically contested)En base a los datos, se recomienda ajustar el presupuesto.
con respecto amarks a formal topic shiftCon respecto al cumplimiento, no se han encontrado problemas.
en cuanto amarks a lighter topic shift (recycled from Ch. 6)En cuanto al plazo, todavía se está negociando.
Language NoteEn base a is extremely common in spoken and written professional Spanish across the Americas, but some style guides consider basándose en or de acuerdo con more careful written alternatives. Learners should recognize and be able to use en base a in speech and informal professional writing, while knowing that a more conservative editor may prefer an alternative in a polished document.

6.2 Hedging and softened recommendation

Where academic Spanish depersonalizes to sound objective, professional Spanish often hedges to sound courteous while still being clear. This chapter activates a set of formulaic hedging expressions built on the conditional and impersonal patterns already productive from earlier volumes.

Direct (avoid in negotiation)Hedged professional form
Esto no va a funcionar.Convendría revisar este punto antes de avanzar.
No estoy de acuerdo.Tenemos ciertas reservas con respecto a esta parte de la propuesta.
Necesitamos más tiempo.Cabría considerar un plazo más amplio para la primera fase.
Language NoteCabría considerar and convendría are formulaic expressions built on conditional forms that have been productive since earlier volumes. Learners should treat them here as fixed professional openers to reuse, not as an invitation to analyze the full conditional paradigm — that system remains scheduled for later chapters.

6.3 Ser/estar in register-sensitive professional contexts

Professional Spanish uses familiar ser/estar distinctions to make precise, register-appropriate claims about a project's status.

ContextExample
Classifying a proposalLa propuesta es viable dentro del presupuesto actual.
Describing current project statusEl proyecto está en la fase de revisión.
Evaluating compliance stateEl informe está completo, pero la sección de riesgos aún no está lista.

6.4 Formulaic recommendation patterns with que clauses

Professional Spanish frequently introduces a recommendation with a fixed impersonal expression followed by a que clause. This chapter activates the pattern as a recognizable formulaic frame; the full present-subjunctive analysis that governs the verb inside the clause is scheduled for a later chapter.

Formulaic frameExample
se recomienda que...Se recomienda que el equipo revise el plazo antes del viernes.
es importante que...Es importante que el cliente entienda los riesgos del cumplimiento.
Language NoteThese frames are introduced here as formulaic units for recognition and cautious, modeled production only. Learners are not expected to generate new subjunctive verb forms independently in this chapter; that productive control is built starting in Chapter 11.

7. Model Dialogue: Client Negotiation

Cliente: Nos interesa la propuesta, pero el plazo de dos meses nos preocupa un poco.

Diego: Entendemos la preocupación. Con respecto al plazo, cabría considerar dividir el proyecto en dos fases.

Camila: De acuerdo con nuestro análisis inicial, la primera fase es viable en un mes; la segunda necesitaría un poco más de tiempo.

Cliente: ¿Y el presupuesto? En base a lo que hablamos la semana pasada, esperábamos un número más bajo.

Diego: Tenemos ciertas reservas con respecto a reducir el presupuesto sin ajustar también el alcance del proyecto.

Camila: Se recomienda que revisemos juntos el alcance esta semana, antes de fijar el número final.

Cliente: Me parece razonable. Convendría enviarnos el resumen ejecutivo con esa propuesta revisada.

11. Reading Practice

Exercise 7.7 — Reading an executive summary

"El presente resumen ejecutivo describe la propuesta para la primera fase del proyecto de digitalización. De acuerdo con el análisis inicial, la propuesta es viable dentro del plazo de dos meses acordado con el cliente. Con respecto al presupuesto, en base a los costos actuales del equipo, se recomienda mantener el número original.   1. What is the main purpose of this executive summary?
  2. What does the summary recommend regarding the budget, and what phrase introduces that recommendation?
  3. Under what condition might the scope need to be adjusted?
  4. Identify two idiomatic prepositions in the passage and explain what each introduces.

12. Writing Workshop

Task: Write a short executive summary (90–120 words) for a proposal, real or invented, addressed to a client. Your summary must use at least one idiomatic preposition (de acuerdo con, en base a, con respecto a, en cuanto a), at least one hedging expression, at least one formulaic recommendation frame, and correct ser/estar to describe the project's status.

Stage 1 — Plan

Note the proposal's purpose, one risk or concern, and one recommendation you will make.

Stage 2 — Draft

Write your executive summary, applying your plan.

Stage 3 — Revise

Check that every direct or blunt statement has been softened appropriately, confirm each idiomatic preposition introduces the right kind of information, and verify that ser and estar are each used correctly.

Interpretation-awareness checklist

  • The summary states the proposal's purpose clearly in the opening.
  • At least one idiomatic preposition is used naturally to introduce a source or topic.
  • At least one hedging expression replaces a blunt or overly direct statement.
  • At least one formulaic recommendation frame is used appropriately.
  • Ser and estar are each used correctly to describe the project's classification and current status.
  • Register remains consistently professional throughout.

13. Culture and Communication

Directness in professional negotiation varies considerably across Spanish-speaking business cultures and even across individual companies and industries. In many formal corporate settings across Latin America and Spain, an initial disagreement or concern is often introduced indirectly, with hedging language, before a more direct position is stated later in the conversation. This is not universal — some sectors and some negotiators favor a more direct style — and generational and organizational differences matter as much as national ones. What is broadly useful for a learner is recognizing hedged language as a genuine communicative move, not evasiveness, and being able to produce it when the situation calls for courtesy alongside clarity.

14. Common Mistakes

  • Using blunt, unhedged statements in a negotiation context where a softened form would be more appropriate.
  • Confusing en base a with de acuerdo con, or overusing one at the expense of the other.
  • Treating cabría considerar and convendría as an invitation to freely conjugate new conditional or subjunctive forms before those systems are formally activated.
  • Choosing ser where estar is needed to describe a project's current status, or the reverse when classifying a proposal.
  • Treating el plazo as equivalent to English "place" rather than "deadline" or "term."

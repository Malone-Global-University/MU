Capítulo 19: Portafolio final

Chapter function: Portfolio revision, oral defense, and target-register refinement. After the debate night in Chapter 18, Marta asks the reading circle to look back across the whole volume — the academic abstract, the professional proposal, the civic notice, the science commentary, the edited text, the essay, the translation, and the debate texts — and assemble a portfolio that shows their range across registers. No new grammar system is introduced this chapter. Instead, learners revise, defend, and refine work they have already produced, applying the four-pass edit and long-form cohesion checks across genres rather than within a single text.

1. Chapter Overview

This chapter has no new grammar system of its own. Its work is synthesis: learners select pieces from across Volume V, revise them against the standards developed since Chapter 14, and prepare to defend their choices orally. The chapter's task is register discipline at scale — recognizing when a text calls for academic caution, professional directness, civic precision, literary voice, or argumentative structure, and explaining in Spanish why a given register choice fits a given audience. Long-form cohesion, rhetorical subjunctive patterns, and mood and reference diagnostics — all developed since Chapter 16 — continue to develop here as learners apply them across a full portfolio rather than a single text.

2. Learning Objectives

  • Select and justify a set of portfolio texts that demonstrate range across academic, professional, civic, literary, and argumentative registers.
  • Revise previously drafted texts using the four-pass edit, with particular attention to register and regional consistency.
  • Explain, in Spanish, why a specific register choice suits a specific audience and purpose.
  • Defend a piece of writing orally, responding to specific questions about word choice, structure, and register.
  • Compare two versions of the same text — an early draft and a refined final version — and describe what changed and why.
  • Reflect on personal growth across the volume using accurate, specific language rather than general praise.

3. Opening Communication Scenario

A week after the debate night, Marta gathers the reading circle to plan the final portfolio each member will present to the cultural center's coordinating committee.

Marta: El comité del centro quiere ver una muestra de todo lo que hemos trabajado este año, no solo el debate. Cada quien va a armar un portafolio: unos cuatro o cinco textos que muestren que pueden moverse entre registros distintos.

Camila: ¿Cualquier texto de los que ya escribimos, o hay que escribir algo nuevo?

Marta: Pueden reciclar lo que ya tienen, pero nada entra sin revisión. Quiero que vuelvan a cada texto con ojos nuevos: ¿el registro es el correcto para ese lector? ¿La argumentación se sostiene de principio a fin?

Diego: ¿Y también hay que defenderlo en persona?

Marta: Sí. El comité va a hacerles preguntas puntuales sobre por qué eligieron cierta palabra, cierta estructura, cierto tono. No se trata de memorizar una respuesta, sino de poder explicar sus propias decisiones.

Camila: Entonces esto no es solo revisar; es entender por qué escribimos como escribimos.

Marta: Exactamente. Empecemos por elegir qué entra al portafolio.

5. Pronunciation and Discourse Focus

An oral defense requires a controlled, confident delivery distinct from a rehearsed presentation: learners must sound prepared while actually responding to an unscripted question. This chapter extends the emphasis and pacing work from Chapter 18 to unscripted, question-driven speech, where pauses for genuine thought must not be mistaken for hesitation, and where a calm, level pace supports credibility.

Exercise 19.1 — Answering without a script

Read the following question aloud, pause for three seconds as if genuinely thinking, then answer in one or two sentences without reading from notes: "¿Por qué eligió ese tono para este texto y no uno más formal?" Repeat the exercise with a self-chosen question about one of your own portfolio texts.

6. Grammar and Sentence Patterns

6.1 No new productive system: consolidation across genres

This chapter activates no new grammar system. Every system needed for portfolio revision and oral defense — advanced mood precision, editorial diagnostics, long-form cohesion, rhetorical and concessive subjunctive patterns — has already been introduced and developed earlier in the volume. 6.2 Explaining register choices: metalanguage for the defense

Defending a text orally requires a specific kind of language: not the register of the text itself, but language about that register. This metalanguage draws on evaluative expressions and comparison structures already consolidated in the volume.

FunctionPatternExample
Justifying a register choiceElegí... porque el lector esperaba...Elegí un tono más directo porque el lector, en este caso, esperaba una recomendación clara, no un análisis extenso.
Comparing draft and final versionEn el borrador... ; en la versión final...En el borrador usaba usted en todo el texto; en la versión final reservé ese tratamiento solo para la primera y la última oración, según la convención del género.
Acknowledging a limitationReconozco que... ; aun así...Reconozco que la síntesis del debate podría haber sido más breve; aun así, preferí mantener ambas posturas completas para no simplificar en exceso.
Editorial HabitAn oral defense fails when the speaker can only describe what the text says, not why it says it that way. Before the defense, prepare one sentence of justification for every major choice: word, register, structure, and audience.

7. Reading Excerpt: Two Versions of the Same Paragraph

The reading circle compares an early draft and a revised final version of the opening paragraph from Camila's professional proposal, first drafted in Chapter 7 and now being prepared for the portfolio.

Borrador (Capítulo 7):

Creo que sería bueno que el centro pensara en cambiar un poco el horario de las tertulias, porque a veces la gente no puede venir y eso podría ser un problema para el futuro del programa.

Versión final (Capítulo 19):

Propongo que el centro reconsidere el horario actual de las tertulias. Según la asistencia registrada en los últimos meses, un número creciente de participantes no puede asistir en el horario vigente, lo cual representa un riesgo real para la continuidad del programa a mediano plazo.

Exercise 19.2 — Comparing draft and final version

List three specific changes between the two versions (verb choice, sentence structure, level of specificity, or address form). For each change, write one sentence explaining why the final version better suits a professional proposal.

12. Writing Workshop

Task: Write a portfolio introduction (150–200 words) that presents your four selected texts, states the register each one represents, and explains — using at least two patterns from Section 6.2 — why together they demonstrate range and growth. Then revise one previously drafted text from an earlier chapter, applying the four-pass edit, and write a two-sentence comparison of the draft and the revised version.

Stage 1 — Select and justify

List your four portfolio texts with the register each represents and a one-sentence justification for its inclusion.

Stage 2 — Revise one text and draft the introduction

Apply the four-pass edit to one previously drafted text. Draft the portfolio introduction without stopping to correct mechanics.

Stage 3 — Apply the four-pass edit to the introduction

Apply the four-pass procedure from Chapter 14 to the introduction itself: meaning and mood, reference and agreement, mechanics, and register and regional consistency.

Integrated assessment checklist

  • The introduction presents four texts and correctly names the register each one represents.
  • At least two sentences justify a specific choice using a pattern from Section 6.2.
  • One previously drafted text has been revised and compared explicitly to its earlier version.
  • No major new grammar system was needed to complete the task; all systems used were already active in the volume.
  • Applied the four-pass edit before finalizing the introduction.

13. Culture and Communication

Portfolio review and oral defense are common features of advanced academic and professional life across many Spanish-speaking institutions, from university thesis defenses to workplace performance reviews to community-organization reporting. Expectations for these events vary: some settings favor a formal, question-and-answer structure similar to the mock defense in this chapter, while others favor a more conversational review among colleagues or mentors. In many community and civic contexts, presenting a body of work to a committee or board — as the reading circle does here — is also an opportunity to build trust with an institution over time, not only to demonstrate a finished product. These practices differ by institution, field, and region and should not be treated as a single fixed format.

14. Common Mistakes

  • Describing what a text says instead of explaining why a specific choice was made.
  • Selecting portfolio texts that all represent the same register, failing to demonstrate range.
  • Treating portfolio revision as light proofreading rather than a full four-pass edit.
  • Reading a memorized defense answer instead of responding to the specific question asked.
  • Using vague self-praise in a reflection instead of specific, accurate description of what changed.

Capítulo 14: Corrección editorial

Chapter function: Proofreading, accentuation, agreement, punctuation, register, and regional consistency. The document now reads as one connected argument with a visible stance — but Marta has one more round of notes before the report can go out: a full editorial proofread. This chapter consolidates every diagnostic lens learners have built across Chapters 11–13 (mood precision, clitic and reference control, connectors and paragraph architecture) into a single, systematic correction pass, and adds the mechanical layer — accents, agreement, punctuation, and register consistency — that a professional editor checks last.

1. Chapter Overview

Editing is a distinct skill from writing. A writer generates ideas; an editor verifies that the finished text says exactly what it should, in the form it should, for the reader it is written for. This chapter teaches learners to read a text several times, each pass with a single diagnostic question in mind — first meaning and mood, then reference and agreement, then mechanics, then register and regional consistency — rather than trying to catch everything at once. Learners practice with Diego and Camila's report as Marta sends it back one final time, and they build a personal editing checklist they can reuse on their own writing.

2. Learning Objectives

  • Apply a staged, multi-pass editing procedure that separates meaning-level checks from mechanical checks.
  • Identify and correct accentuation errors, including stress-pattern accents, distinguishing accents, and accents created by pronoun attachment.
  • Identify and correct gender, number, and noun-adjective agreement errors, including agreement across coordinated or mixed-gender elements.
  • Identify and correct punctuation errors specific to Spanish, including inverted marks, comma use with connectors, and quotation conventions.
  • Diagnose register inconsistency within a single document and correct it toward one coherent formal register.
  • Verify regional consistency, confirming that address forms, vocabulary, and grammar do not mix unlabeled regional systems.
  • Annotate corrections with a brief justification, explaining why a form is preferred, not only what the correct form is.

3. Opening Communication Scenario

Marta has sent the report back a third time — this time not because an idea is missing, but because the surface of the text is not yet reliable.

Marta: El contenido y la organización ya están muy bien. Ahora necesito una revisión final: acentos, concordancia, puntuación. Y noté que el documento cambia de tono a mitad de camino.

Camila: ¿Cambia de tono? No me había dado cuenta.

Marta: Sí. Al principio usan "usted" y un registro bastante formal. Después, en la sección del cronograma, aparece "tú" dos veces y el estilo se vuelve más conversacional. El lector nota esas inconsistencias, aunque no sepa explicarlas.

Diego: Entiendo. Entonces necesitamos leer el documento varias veces, cada vez buscando un tipo de error diferente, en vez de intentar corregir todo a la vez.

Marta: Exactamente. Así trabajan los correctores profesionales: una lectura para el sentido, una para la concordancia y los acentos, y una última para el registro y la consistencia general.

Camila: De acuerdo. Empecemos por hacer una lista de lo que vamos a revisar en cada lectura.

Language NoteIn editing contexts, corregir means to fix an identified error, while revisar means to read carefully in order to find errors, whether or not any are found. A good editing pass always begins with revisar and only sometimes ends with corregir.

5. Pronunciation and Discourse Focus

Reading a text aloud is one of the most reliable ways to catch a missing or misplaced accent, since spoken stress and written accentuation are directly connected in Spanish. A sentence that sounds wrong when read naturally often has a written error underneath it.

Exercise 14.1 — Reading for stress

Read each pair aloud, exaggerating the stressed syllable, and identify which version is correct for the intended meaning: el (article) / él (pronoun); tu (possessive) / tú (pronoun); practico (I practice) / practicó (he/she practiced); continuo (adjective) / continúo (I continue) / continuó (he/she continued).

6. Grammar and Sentence Patterns

6.1 The multi-pass editing procedure

Professional editors rarely try to catch every category of error in a single reading. This chapter recommends four passes, each with one focus.

PassFocusGuiding question
1Meaning and moodDoes every clause say what the writer intended? Is the subjunctive used where evaluation, doubt, or necessity requires it?
2Reference and agreementDoes every pronoun point clearly to one antecedent? Does every article, noun, and adjective agree?
3MechanicsAre accents, punctuation, and capitalization correct throughout?
4Register and regional consistencyDoes the whole document maintain one address system and one consistent level of formality?
Editorial HabitResist the urge to fix a mechanical error the moment you notice it during Pass 1. Note it and continue; switching focus constantly is what causes editors to miss errors in their own assigned category.

6.2 Accentuation review: the four accent categories

By this stage in the course, learners have met written accents in four separate contexts. Editing requires recognizing all four at once, in any order, inside a single text.

CategoryRuleExample
Regular stress accentsWords ending in a vowel, n, or s are stressed on the second-to-last syllable by default; words ending in another consonant are stressed on the last syllable. A written accent marks any exception.rápido, fácil, revisión
Distinguishing accentsA small set of one-syllable or otherwise identical word pairs use an accent only to separate two grammatical functions.sí/si, tú/tu, él/el, más/mas, dé/de, sé/se
Interrogative and exclamatory accentsQuestion and exclamation words carry an accent whenever they function as a question or exclamation word, including inside indirect questions.No sé qué hora es. ¿Cuándo llega el informe?
Accents from pronoun attachmentAttaching one or more object pronouns to an infinitive, gerund, or affirmative command can shift the natural stress point, requiring a new written accent to preserve the original pronunciation.revisándolo, entrégueselo, corrígelo

6.3 Agreement review: beyond the obvious pair

Simple noun-adjective agreement is rarely the source of an advanced writer's errors. More frequent problems involve agreement across distance or across coordinated elements.

PatternRuleExample
Agreement across a long clauseAn adjective or past participle must still agree with its noun even when several words separate them.Los documentos que el equipo legal revisó la semana pasada están completos. (not completo)
Mixed-gender coordinationWhen a plural adjective describes a coordinated pair or group of mixed grammatical gender, the masculine plural form is the default in standard usage.El informe y la propuesta están listos.
Agreement with collective or quantity expressionsCertain collective nouns take a singular verb even when they refer to multiple people; writers must decide deliberately whether to treat the collective as singular or shift to a plural construction.El equipo revisó el documento. or Los miembros del equipo revisaron el documento.

6.4 Punctuation review

  • Every question and exclamation requires both an opening and a closing mark: ¿...? and ¡...!.
  • A comma normally precedes a sentence-initial connector such as sin embargo, or por lo tanto, and typically follows it as well when it opens a sentence.
  • Aunque does not require a following comma when it opens a short clause fully integrated into the sentence, but a comma is used when the concessive clause is set off before the main clause: Aunque el proceso fue lento, el resultado es sólido.
  • Formal written Spanish typically uses angled quotation marks « » or standard double quotation marks, applied consistently within one document; do not mix conventions.
  • A colon, not a comma, introduces a list or an explanation that restates the preceding clause.

6.5 Register consistency review

A single formal document must not mix tú and usted address, nor should it drift between a formal written register and a conversational spoken register. Editing for register means scanning the whole text for its address system and its vocabulary level as a single, separate pass, since these inconsistencies are easy to miss while focused on grammar alone.

Key ContrastA register error is not a grammar error: a sentence using tú can be perfectly correct in isolation and still be wrong for a document that has established usted as its address system throughout. Editing for register requires comparing a sentence to the rest of the document, not only checking the sentence on its own.

6.6 Regional consistency review

At an advanced level, writers sometimes absorb regional forms from varied reading and listening input. An editorial pass should confirm that the document's address system, vocabulary, and grammar remain inside the declared Latin American production baseline, without unlabeled vosotros forms, unlabeled voseo, or regionally restricted vocabulary presented as neutral.

7. Model Dialogue: The Four-Pass Edit

Diego: Hagamos la primera lectura solo para el sentido. Mira esta oración: "Es posible que el inspector recibe el informe mañana." Algo no está bien.

Camila: Correcto: recibe debería ser reciba. "Es posible que" exige subjuntivo. Anoto el error y seguimos; no lo corrijo todavía.

Diego: De acuerdo. Segunda lectura: concordancia y referencia. Aquí dice "el cliente y la empresa está satisfecho con el resultado."

Camila: Dos sujetos coordinados necesitan un verbo plural: "están", y el adjetivo debe concordar en plural y, por defecto, en masculino: "satisfechos".

Diego: Tercera lectura: mecánica. "¿Cuando llega el inspector" — falta la tilde en "cuándo" y falta el signo de cierre.

Camila: Buen ojo. Y en la sección del cronograma encontré "tú" dos veces: "revisa tú el calendario antes del viernes." Todo el documento usa "usted", así que esto rompe el registro.

Diego: Cuarta lectura: registro y consistencia regional. ¿Alguna forma peninsular o de voseo se coló sin querer?

Camila: No encontré ninguna esta vez, pero vale la pena revisarlo siempre, sobre todo cuando citamos fuentes de otras regiones.

9. Listening Practice

Listening Script for Instructor, Partner, or Text-to-Speech Use

Marta explica su método de corrección: "Cuando reviso un documento, nunca intento corregir todo en una sola lectura; es la manera más segura de dejar pasar errores. Primero leo buscando solamente el sentido: ¿está bien elegido el modo, el subjuntivo donde corresponde? Después leo buscando la concordancia y la referencia: ¿todos los pronombres apuntan claramente a algo, y todos los sustantivos concuerdan con sus adjetivos? En la tercera lectura reviso la mecánica: acentos, puntuación, mayúsculas. Y en la última lectura reviso el registro: ¿el documento mantiene 'usted' de principio a fin, o se cuela un 'tú' por accidente? Cada lectura tiene un solo propósito, y por eso funciona."

12. Writing Workshop

Task: Take the two- or three-paragraph passage you produced in Chapter 13 (or write a new 150–200 word formal passage addressed to a client or supervisor using usted) and complete a full four-pass editorial correction of it, whether or not you find errors in every category.

Stage 1 — Pass 1: Meaning and mood

Read the passage once for meaning only. Mark any place where the subjunctive, indicative, or another mood choice does not match the intended meaning. Do not correct yet.

Stage 2 — Passes 2–3: Reference, agreement, and mechanics

Read again for pronoun reference and agreement, then a third time for accents, punctuation, and capitalization. Mark and now correct each issue.

Stage 3 — Pass 4: Register and regional consistency

Read a final time checking only the address system, formality level, and regional consistency of vocabulary and grammar across the whole passage.

Integrated assessment checklist

  • Completed a distinct pass for meaning/mood before touching mechanics.
  • Corrected all accentuation errors, correctly categorized by type.
  • Corrected all agreement errors, including any across distance or coordination.
  • Corrected all punctuation errors, including inverted marks and connector commas.
  • Maintained one consistent address form and register throughout.
  • Confirmed no unlabeled regional forms appear in the final version.
  • Wrote a one-sentence justification for at least three corrections.

13. Culture and Communication

Professional editing norms vary somewhat across Spanish-speaking institutions, but the underlying expectation is broadly shared in academic, legal, and corporate settings across the region: a document that reaches a client, a supervisor, or a public authority is generally expected to be free of accentuation, agreement, and punctuation errors, since such errors can be read as carelessness regardless of the strength of the underlying content. Many professional style guides in Spanish-speaking workplaces also expect a single, consistent address form throughout a formal document, which is one reason register consistency is treated as a distinct and serious editorial category rather than a minor stylistic preference.

14. Common Mistakes

  • Correcting mechanical errors while still reading for meaning, which causes editors to lose track of larger mood and reference problems.
  • Treating a register drift as acceptable because the individual sentence is grammatically correct.
  • Missing agreement errors when the noun and adjective are separated by a long clause.
  • Forgetting that pronoun attachment to an infinitive, gerund, or affirmative command can require a new accent.
  • Mixing quotation-mark conventions or capitalization styles within the same document.
  • Assuming a text is finished after only one reading, regardless of length.

15. Chapter Summary

This chapter introduced a systematic, multi-pass editing procedure and applied it to the mechanical and register-level layer of Diego and Camila's report: accentuation across all four accent categories, agreement across distance and coordination, Spanish-specific punctuation, and consistency of register and regional form across an entire document. Editing was framed as a distinct skill from writing — one that depends on reading with a single diagnostic question at a time rather than trying to catch every error at once.

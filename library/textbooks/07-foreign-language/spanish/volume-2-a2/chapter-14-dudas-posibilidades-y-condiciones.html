Dudas, posibilidades y condiciones

El subjuntivo después de expresiones de duda y posibilidad, y las cláusulas con si de significado presente y futuro · Speak and Write Spanish: Everyday Independence and Connected Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Use the present subjunctive after expressions of doubt and denial, such as dudar que and no creer que.
  2. Use the present subjunctive after impersonal expressions of possibility, such as es posible que and es probable que.
  3. Distinguish sentences that express certainty (indicative) from sentences that express doubt or uncertainty (subjunctive), including the contrast between creer que and no creer que.
  4. Use quizás and tal vez to express possibility, with attention to when they trigger the subjunctive.
  5. Form and use si clauses that describe real, present, or likely future conditions, using the present indicative in the si clause and the present or future in the result clause.
  6. Continue coordinating tasks and requests using the subjunctive triggers, commands, and object pronouns already active from Chapter 13.

Communication Scenario

The Saturday of the hallway project is approaching, and now Rodrigo, Camila, and Sra. Duarte face a new kind of uncertainty: the weather forecast is unclear, one neighbor has not confirmed, and nobody is sure whether the hardware store will have enough paint. Their messages shift from stating simple facts to weighing possibilities — some things they doubt, some things they merely suspect, and some things that depend on what happens between now and Saturday. Spanish marks this shift with the subjunctive again, but for a different reason: not to influence someone's actions, but to express that the speaker is not certain something is true.

  1. Think of a plan you have made that depended on something uncertain, like the weather or someone else's schedule. How did you talk about that uncertainty?
  2. Notice the difference between Creo que va a llover (I think it's going to rain — fairly confident) and Dudo que llueva (I doubt it will rain — expressing doubt).

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Note — Linking Across "Que" and Diphthongs in Quizás/Tal Vez

In connected speech, dudo que, es posible que, and no creo que link smoothly into the following verb, so the boundary between que and the subjunctive verb often disappears in natural speech: dudoquellueva sounds close to one continuous stretch of sound. Practice saying these phrases quickly, without pausing at que.

Quizás and tal vez each contain a stressed syllable that carries a written accent: qui-ZÁS and tal VEZ. Because both words end in a consonant other than n or s, the natural stress would already fall on the last syllable in tal vez; the accent on quizás, however, marks an exception to the normal stress pattern for words ending in s, so it must be written.

Grammar: Duda, posibilidad y las cláusulas con si

Purpose: express that you doubt something, that something is only possible, or that one thing depends on another — using the present subjunctive for uncertainty and the present indicative for real conditions.

Grammar Note — Dudar, no creer y expresiones de duda

When the main clause expresses doubt or denial about the dependent clause, the dependent verb goes into the subjunctive, even though there is often only one implied "topic" rather than two clearly different subjects.

  • Dudo que llueva mañana. (I doubt it will rain tomorrow.)
  • No creo que la ferretería tenga suficiente pintura. (I don't think the hardware store has enough paint.)
  • No es cierto que Marcos pueda venir el sábado. (It's not certain that Marcos can come Saturday.)

The reverse also matters: creer que and es cierto que, when affirmative, report something the speaker considers true, so they take the indicative: Creo que va a llover (indicative — the speaker is reasonably confident); No creo que llueva (subjunctive — the speaker doubts it). The presence or absence of no in front of a belief verb often flips the mood of the clause that follows.

Grammar Note — Posibilidad: es posible que, es probable que, quizás, tal vez

Impersonal expressions of possibility work the same way as expressions of doubt: they present the following idea as uncertain, not as an established fact.

  • Es posible que Marcos no venga. (It's possible Marcos won't come.)
  • Es probable que llueva por la tarde. (It's probably going to rain in the afternoon.)

Quizás and tal vez behave a little differently: because they can appear without que, directly in front of a verb, they trigger the subjunctive when the speaker means genuine uncertainty, but they may be followed by the indicative when the speaker feels fairly confident. Quizás llueva (maybe it will rain — real uncertainty) and Quizás va a llover (maybe it's going to rain — the speaker already suspects so) are both possible; this chapter treats the subjunctive version as the default productive pattern.

Grammar Note — Cláusulas con si: condiciones reales

A real condition describes something that may genuinely happen, with a logical result. For real conditions with present or future meaning, Spanish uses the present indicative in the si clause and the present indicative, the future, or a command in the result clause. The subjunctive is not used in this type of si clause.

Cláusula con siCláusula de resultadoEjemplo
si + presentepresenteSi llueve, no pintamos.
si + presentefuturoSi llueve, no pintaremos.
si + presentemandatoSi llueve, avísame.

Notice that the si clause itself never uses the subjunctive in this pattern, even though the sentence expresses uncertainty about the future. This is different from dudar que or es posible que, where the subjunctive appears directly after que. Hypothetical or contrary-to- fact conditions, which use a different structure with the imperfect subjunctive, are introduced in a later volume.

Model Dialogue

Contexto: Rodrigo y Camila revisan el pronóstico y hablan de lo que podría pasar el sábado. Siguen usando tú entre ellos.

CAMILAMiré el pronóstico. Es posible que llueva el sábado por la tarde.I looked at the forecast. It's possible it will rain Saturday afternoon.
RODRIGONo lo creo. Dudo que llueva tan temprano en la temporada.I don't think so. I doubt it will rain this early in the season.
CAMILABueno, si llueve, no vamos a poder pintar afuera del pasillo.Well, if it rains, we won't be able to paint outside the hallway.
RODRIGOEs verdad. Si llueve, empezaremos con el armario primero.True. If it rains, we'll start with the closet first.

RODRIGOEs probable que tengas razón. Si no alcanza, compramos más el viernes.You're probably right. If it's not enough, we'll buy more on Friday.

Writing Workshop: Un plan con condiciones

Your writing task for this chapter is a short message in which you explain a plan that depends on something uncertain, using expressions of doubt, possibility, and at least one real si clause.

  1. Estudia el modelo. Reread Sra. Duarte's update above. Mark every expression of doubt or possibility and every si clause, and identify the mood of each dependent verb.
  2. Planifica. Choose a plan that depends on something uncertain (weather, someone's availability, whether supplies are enough). Decide who is doubtful and who is confident about each part.
  3. Escribe el primer borrador. Write a message of at least six sentences that uses dudar que or no creer que at least once, es posible que or es probable que at least once, and at least one real si clause with present or future meaning.
  4. Revisa. Check your draft against the editing checklist below.
  5. Escribe la versión final. Produce a clean final version of your message.

Lista de revisión:

  • Every clause after a doubt or possibility trigger uses the subjunctive, not the indicative.
  • Every clause after an affirmative belief expression (creo que, es cierto que) uses the indicative, not the subjunctive.
  • My si clauses use the present indicative, with a present, future, or command result — never the subjunctive.
  • My address form is consistent throughout the message.
  • My accents and punctuation, including inverted marks, are correct.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context

Expressing doubt directly with dudo que or no creo que is common and unremarkable in everyday Spanish conversation, but the strength of the doubt expressed can vary with tone, context, and relationship. Among some speakers, softer alternatives such as no sé si... or a rising intonation on ¿tú crees? may be preferred in situations that call for more caution, such as questioning a plan proposed by someone with more authority or seniority. As with other pragmatic choices, there is no single universal rule; paying attention to how doubt is expressed around you in a given setting is a useful skill in itself.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake

Error: Using the indicative after dudar que — Dudo que llueve mañana.

Correction: Dudar que expresses uncertainty and requires the subjunctive: Dudo que llueva mañana.

Common Mistake

Error: Using the subjunctive after affirmative creo que — Creo que llueva mañana.

Correction: Affirmative creer que reports something the speaker considers true and takes the indicative: Creo que va a llover mañana. Reserve the subjunctive for no creo que.

Common Mistake

Error: Using the subjunctive inside a real si clause — Si llueva, no pintamos.

Correction: Real conditions use the present indicative in the si clause: Si llueve, no pintamos. The subjunctive belongs after que, not after si, in this pattern.

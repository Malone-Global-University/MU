Instrucciones y consejos

Mandatos afirmativos y negativos, consejos, y los acentos que crea la colocación de pronombres · Speak and Write Spanish: Everyday Independence and Connected Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Give affirmative and negative commands using tú, usted, and ustedes in everyday instructional and advice-giving situations.
  2. Attach object and reflexive pronouns correctly to affirmative commands, and place them correctly before negative commands.
  3. Add the written accent that pronoun attachment often requires, and explain why the accent is necessary.
  4. Give and understand practical instructions, advice, and recommendations in recipes, directions, and everyday requests.
  5. Distinguish direct commands from softer, more polite ways of requesting the same action.
  6. Recognize, without producing, the idea that recommendations can also be expressed with a dependent clause introduced by que.

Communication Scenario

Camila's water heater problem from the last chapter is finally resolved, but now her downstairs neighbor, Rodrigo, is moving into his first apartment and keeps asking her for advice. Camila finds herself constantly telling him what to do: how to set up his internet, how to talk to the building manager, what to do and what not to do during his first week. Giving useful advice means being direct without being rude — and in Spanish, that directness has its own grammar: the command form, or el mandato.

  1. Think about the last time you gave someone step-by-step instructions or advice — a recipe, directions, or a piece of practical advice.
  2. Notice which instructions you would give the same way to a close friend and to someone you address more formally.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Note — Command Intonation

A command in Spanish should sound clearly different from a question, even though the verb form is often identical to a present-subjunctive-shaped word you have not yet studied formally. Commands typically fall in pitch toward the end, especially when softened with por favor: Llena el formulario, por favor. ↘

When a pronoun attaches to the end of an affirmative command, the stress does not move to the new final syllable — it stays where it was before the pronoun attached. That is exactly why an accent mark is added: llena becomes llénalo, keeping the stress on the same syllable it had before -lo was added. Practice saying pairs like llena / llénalo and firma / fírmalo aloud, keeping the stress in the same place both times.

Grammar: Los mandatos

Purpose: give direct instructions and advice to someone using tú, usted, or ustedes, and attach pronouns correctly when needed.

Grammar Note — Forming Commands

Mandatos afirmativos con tú: use the third-person singular present-tense form. Habla más despacio. Come algo. Escribe tu nombre aquí.

Mandatos negativos con tú: use a different form, built like the usted command with a final -s. No hables tan rápido. No comas eso. No escribas con lápiz.

Mandatos con usted y ustedes (afirmativos y negativos): the same form is used for both affirmative and negative, built by switching the usual vowel of the present tense. -ar verbs take an -e/-en ending; -er/-ir verbs take an -a/-an ending. Llene el formulario. No llene el formulario todavía. Escuchen con atención. No escuchen música mientras trabajan.

A small group of common tú affirmative commands are irregular and are best learned as fixed forms for now: ten (tener), ven (venir), ve (ir), haz (hacer), di (decir), pon (poner), sal (salir), sé (ser).

  • Rodrigo, llena este formulario y fírmalo aquí.Rodrigo, fill out this form and sign it here.
  • No firmes nada sin leerlo primero.Don't sign anything without reading it first.
  • Señor Duarte, por favor revise el contrato antes de mandármelo.Mr. Duarte, please review the contract before sending it to me.
  • Tengan cuidado con las escaleras; están mojadas.Be careful with the stairs; they're wet.
Grammar Note — Pronoun Attachment and Accents

Object and reflexive pronouns attach to the end of affirmative commands, forming a single written word. They go before negative commands, as a separate word.

  • Llena el formulario. → Llénalo. (fill it out)
  • No llenes el formulario. → No lo llenes. (don't fill it out)
  • Levanta la mano. → Levántala.
  • No te preocupes. (reflexive, negative — no accent needed here)
  • ¡Siéntate! (reflexive, affirmative — accent added)

An accent mark is added to an affirmative command with an attached pronoun whenever the added syllable(s) would otherwise shift the natural stress. Two-syllable command forms almost always need one accent when a single pronoun attaches (habla → háblame); three-syllable commands with two attached pronouns often need one as well (dame → dámelo). When in doubt, say the word aloud: if the stress falls anywhere except the syllable that carried it before the pronoun attached, mark it.

Model Dialogue

Contexto: Rodrigo, el vecino nuevo de Camila, le pide consejos sobre su primera semana en el edificio. Se conocen bien, así que ella usa tú con él.

RODRIGOCamila, ¿qué debo hacer primero? Tengo que hablar con el administrador, pero no sé qué decirle.Camila, what should I do first? I have to talk to the manager, but I don't know what to say to him.
CAMILAPrimero, escríbele un mensaje breve. No lo llames todavía; él prefiere los mensajes por escrito.First, write him a short message. Don't call him yet; he prefers written messages.
RODRIGOVale. ¿Y el formulario de mudanza? ¿Lo lleno ahora?Okay. And the move-in form? Should I fill it out now?
CAMILASí, llénalo esta noche y fírmalo. Pero léelo con cuidado antes de firmarlo.Yes, fill it out tonight and sign it. But read it carefully before signing it.
RODRIGOBuen consejo. Una cosa más: ¿debo guardar una copia?Good advice. One more thing: should I keep a copy?
CAMILAClaro que sí. Guárdala en un lugar seguro, y no la pierdas — la vas a necesitar después.Of course. Keep it in a safe place, and don't lose it — you're going to need it later.
RODRIGOGracias, Camila. De verdad, no sé qué haría sin tus consejos.Thanks, Camila. Really, I don't know what I'd do without your advice.
CAMILANo te preocupes. Y ten paciencia — todo se resuelve poco a poco.Don't worry. And be patient — everything gets resolved little by little.

Preguntas de comprensión:

  1. ¿Qué le dice Camila a Rodrigo que haga primero?
  2. ¿Por qué debe Rodrigo escribir un mensaje en vez de llamar?
  3. ¿Qué debe hacer Rodrigo antes de firmar el formulario?
  4. ¿Qué consejo final le da Camila?

Reading

Read the following set of instructions for new residents, posted in the lobby of Camila's building.

Instrucciones para residentes nuevos

¡Bienvenidos al edificio! Para que su primera semana sea fácil, sigan estos consejos.

Primero, preséntense con el administrador y llenen el formulario de registro. Fírmenlo y guárdenlo una copia para ustedes. No dejen el formulario original en su apartamento; entréguenlo en la oficina antes del viernes.

Writing Workshop: Una lista de consejos

Your writing task for this chapter is a short list of instructions or advice for someone starting something new — a new apartment, a new job, a new class, or a trip.

  1. Estudia el modelo. Reread "Instrucciones para residentes nuevos" above. Mark every command, note whether it uses tú or ustedes, and note every attached pronoun.
  2. Planifica. Choose a situation and an audience. Decide whether you will address that audience with tú or with usted/ustedes, and stay consistent.
  3. Escribe el primer borrador. Write at least eight commands, including at least three negative commands and at least two commands with an attached pronoun.
  4. Revisa. Check your draft against the editing checklist below.
  5. Escribe la versión final. Produce a clean final version organized as a numbered or bulleted list of advice.

Lista de revisión:

  • I used the correct command form for my chosen address form (tú or usted/ustedes).
  • My negative commands use the correct negative command form, not the affirmative form with no alone.
  • My pronouns attach to affirmative commands and precede negative commands correctly.
  • I added a written accent wherever pronoun attachment requires one.
  • My address form is consistent throughout the list.
  • My accents and punctuation, including inverted marks, are correct.

Speaking Practice

Práctica controlada

With a partner, take turns giving each other three simple commands using tú — for example, Abre la puerta. Escúchame. No te preocupes. Your partner should respond by acting out or verbally confirming the command.

Tarea comunicativa: Consejos para un vecino nuevo

With a partner, role-play a conversation like the model dialogue: one person just moved in and asks for advice; the other gives at least five pieces of advice using commands, including at least one command with an attached pronoun. Decide together whether the conversation uses tú or usted, and stay consistent.

Alternativa individual

If you are studying independently: record yourself giving a one-minute list of instructions for a simple task — making coffee, checking into a hotel, or setting up a new phone — using at least six commands.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context

Direct commands are common and unremarkable in Spanish in many everyday contexts — recipes, directions, safety instructions, and advice among friends or family all use the command form naturally, without sounding harsh. What changes the tone is not the command form itself but the surrounding language: adding por favor, choosing usted instead of tú with someone outside your close circle, or softening a command with a phrase like si puedes or ¿me haces el favor de...? This balance between directness and softening varies by relationship, region, and individual style, and is not a fixed national rule.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake

Error: Using the affirmative tú command form for a negative command — No firma el documento instead of the correct negative form.

Correction: Negative tú commands use a different form from the affirmative: No firmes el documento, not No firma el documento.

Common Mistake

Error: Forgetting the accent mark when a pronoun attaches to an affirmative command — llenalo instead of llénalo.

Correction: When pronoun attachment shifts the syllable count of the word, the original stress must be marked with a written accent: llénalo, not llenalo. Say the word aloud to check where the stress falls.

Common Mistake

Error: Placing the pronoun before an affirmative command instead of attaching it — lo llena instead of llénalo.

Correction: Pronouns attach to the end of affirmative commands but go before negative commands: llénalo (affirmative) but no lo llenes (negative).

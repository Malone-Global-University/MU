Historias recientes

El pretérito regular e irregular para narrar eventos recientes · Speak and Write Spanish: Everyday Independence and Connected Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Conjugate regular -ar, -er, and -ir verbs in the preterite to report completed actions.
  2. Use a set of high-frequency irregular preterite verbs, including ir, ser, hacer, tener, and estar.
  3. Sequence a short narrative using time markers such as primero, después, luego, and finalmente.
  4. Ask and answer questions about recent, completed events.
  5. Write a short account of something that happened recently, organized in a clear sequence.
  6. Narrate a recent event aloud with reasonably accurate preterite forms and natural pronunciation.

Communication Scenario

A few weeks after their conversation about organizing the week, Diego and Marisol run into each other on a Monday morning. Marisol started her new job last week, and Diego wants to hear how her first days went. As Marisol tells her story, she moves naturally from one completed event to the next — exactly the kind of narration you will practice in this chapter.

  1. In English, think of something that happened to you recently — your first day somewhere, a trip, or an ordinary weekend.
  2. List three or four events from that story in the order they happened.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Note — Stress in the Preterite

Regular preterite yo and usted/él/ella forms carry stress on the final syllable and require a written accent: hablé, habló, comí, comió, viví, vivió. This stress pattern is one of the clearest ways listeners distinguish the preterite from the present tense, so practice saying pairs like hablo / habló and trabajo / trabajó aloud, exaggerating the shift in stress.

Irregular preterite forms such as tuve, hice, estuve, and fui do not carry a written accent, because their stress falls naturally on the syllable that needs no mark. Do not add an accent to these forms.

Grammar: El pretérito

Purpose: the preterite reports completed actions and events — what happened, not what used to happen or what was going on. This chapter introduces the preterite for the first time; Chapter 7 will introduce the imperfect, and Chapter 8 will contrast the two.

Grammar Note — Spelling Changes in the Yo Form

Some -ar verbs change their spelling in the yo form to preserve pronunciation: llegar → llegué, empezar → empecé, buscar → busqué. The sound stays the same; only the spelling adjusts before the -é ending.

Grammar Note — Ir and Ser Look Alike

Ir and ser share identical preterite forms. Context always makes the meaning clear: Fui a la oficina (I went to the office) versus Fue un día largo (It was a long day).

  • Marisol empezó su trabajo nuevo el lunes pasado.Marisol started her new job last Monday.
  • Conocí a mi supervisor el primer día.I met my supervisor on the first day.
  • Primero fuimos a la oficina principal; después tuvimos una reunión.First we went to the main office; afterward we had a meeting.
  • ¿Qué hiciste anoche?What did you do last night?

Model Dialogue

Contexto: Diego y Marisol se encuentran un lunes por la mañana. Usan tú porque son amigos.

DIEGO¡Marisol! ¿Cómo te fue en tu primera semana de trabajo?Marisol! How did your first week of work go?
MARISOL¡Muy bien! El lunes pasado empecé oficialmente. Primero llegué temprano y conocí a mi supervisora.Very well! Last Monday I officially started. First I arrived early and met my supervisor.
DIEGO¿Y qué hiciste después?And what did you do afterward?
MARISOLDespués fui a una reunión con todo el equipo. Luego tuve que aprender a usar el sistema nuevo.Afterward I went to a meeting with the whole team. Then I had to learn to use the new system.
DIEGO¿Estuviste nerviosa?Were you nervous?
MARISOLUn poco, sí. Pero mis compañeros me ayudaron mucho, y el viernes ya me sentí más cómoda.A little, yes. But my coworkers helped me a lot, and by Friday I already felt more comfortable.
DIEGOMe alegro. ¿Y qué hicieron el fin de semana pasado, tú y tu familia?I'm glad. And what did you and your family do last weekend?
MARISOLDescansamos. Mi hermana y yo cocinamos el sábado, y el domingo no hicimos nada especial. Finalmente pude dormir bien.We rested. My sister and I cooked on Saturday, and on Sunday we didn't do anything special. Finally I was able to sleep well.
DIEGOSuena perfecto después de una primera semana tan ocupada.Sounds perfect after such a busy first week.

Preguntas de comprensión:

  1. ¿Qué hizo Marisol primero el lunes?
  2. ¿Qué tuvo que aprender Marisol?
  3. ¿Cómo se sintió Marisol el viernes?
  4. ¿Qué hicieron Marisol y su hermana el sábado?

Reading

Read the following message that Marisol wrote to a friend about her first week.

Mi primera semana

¡Hola! Quiero contarte cómo me fue en mi primera semana de trabajo. El lunes llegué temprano y conocí a mi supervisora, que es muy amable. Ese mismo día tuve una reunión con todo el equipo y aprendí a usar el sistema de la oficina.

El martes y el miércoles fueron días tranquilos. Trabajé en un proyecto pequeño y comí con mis compañeros los dos días. El jueves tuvimos un problema con el sistema, pero lo resolvimos juntos por la tarde.

El viernes fue mi día favorito. Terminé el proyecto, mi supervisora me felicitó, y después salimos a tomar un café para celebrar. Fue una semana difícil al principio, pero al final me sentí muy contenta.

Preguntas de comprensión:

  1. ¿Qué aprendió Marisol el lunes?
  2. ¿Qué pasó el jueves?
  3. ¿Por qué fue el viernes su día favorito?
  4. ¿Cómo se sintió Marisol al final de la semana?

Writing Workshop: Algo que pasó recientemente

Your writing task for this chapter is a short narrative about something that happened to you recently — a first day, a trip, a busy weekend, or any completed event — organized in a clear sequence.

  1. Estudia el modelo. Reread "Mi primera semana" above. Underline every preterite verb and every sequencing word.
  2. Planifica. List four or five events from your own recent experience, in the order they happened.
  3. Escribe el primer borrador. Write at least eight sentences in Spanish using regular and irregular preterite verbs and at least three sequencing words (primero, después, luego, finalmente, etc.).
  4. Revisa. Check your draft against the editing checklist below.
  5. Escribe la versión final. Produce a clean final version that reads as one connected story.

Lista de revisión:

  • My regular preterite verbs have the correct endings for -ar, -er, and -ir.
  • My irregular preterite verbs (ir, ser, hacer, tener, estar) are correctly formed.
  • My accented preterite forms (yo and usted/él/ella of regular verbs) carry the correct written accent.
  • My irregular preterite forms do not carry an unnecessary accent.
  • My sequencing words guide the reader clearly from one event to the next.
  • My articles, nouns, and adjectives agree in gender and number.
  • My story reads as one connected narrative, not a disconnected list.

Speaking Practice

Práctica controlada

With a partner, take turns asking and answering about yesterday: ¿Qué hiciste ayer? / Ayer yo... Use at least three different preterite verbs each.

Tarea comunicativa: Cuéntame tu semana

With a partner, role-play the scenario from the model dialogue: one person had a busy or eventful recent week and tells the story; the other asks follow-up questions using the preterite. Use tú throughout, since you are speaking with a friend.

Alternativa individual

If you are studying independently: record a two-minute narration of something that happened to you recently, using regular and irregular preterite verbs and at least three sequencing words.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context

Sharing a short account of one's day or week with friends, family, or coworkers is a common way to maintain relationships in many Spanish-speaking communities, but the level of detail expected can vary by relationship, workplace norm, and individual personality. A coworker might share only a brief summary, while a close friend might expect a more detailed account. Pay attention to how much detail the people around you offer and expect, rather than assuming one fixed pattern applies everywhere.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake

Error: Adding a written accent to irregular preterite forms — tuvé, hicé, estuvé.

Correction: High-frequency irregular preterite forms such as tuve, hice, and estuve do not carry a written accent. Only regular preterite yo and usted/él/ella forms need one: hablé, habló.

Common Mistake

Error: Confusing the nosotros preterite form with the present tense in -ar and -ir verbs without enough context — Trabajamos mucho used without any time marker when a past meaning is intended.

Correction: Since -ar and -ir verbs share the same nosotros form in the present and the preterite, include a time marker such as ayer or la semana pasada so the meaning is clear: Ayer trabajamos mucho.

Common Mistake

Error: Listing events with no sequencing words, so the order is unclear — a string of preterite sentences with no primero, después, or luego.

Correction: Use sequencing words to connect events clearly, so a listener or reader does not have to guess the order in which things happened.

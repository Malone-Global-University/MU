Personas y lugares que importan

Describir personas y lugares con cláusulas relativas: que, donde, y quien · Speak and Write Spanish: Everyday Independence and Connected Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Combine two related ideas into one sentence using the relative pronoun que.
  2. Describe a location using the relative adverb donde.
  3. Refer to a person after a preposition using quien or quienes.
  4. Distinguish when a relative clause simply adds information and when it identifies which person or place is meant.
  5. Read and interpret descriptions of people and places that use relative clauses to add detail.
  6. Write a connected description of a person or place that matters to you, using relative clauses to avoid short, choppy sentences.

Communication Scenario

The neighborhood association decided to propose both projects, the park and the meeting hall, and let the group vote. Before the vote, the association wants everyone to explain why the place matters to them personally, and to introduce the people who have worked hardest on it. Rodrigo, Camila, and Sra. Duarte start writing short descriptions for the community bulletin board, but their first drafts are full of short, disconnected sentences: El parque es viejo. El parque está cerca de mi casa. Mi abuela lo visitaba. This chapter teaches you to connect sentences like these into one smoother, more natural description using relative clauses.

  1. Think of a person or place that matters to you. What short sentences would you use to describe them separately?
  2. Have you noticed, in English, how a word like "that," "where," or "who" lets you combine two sentences into one? Spanish has similar tools.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Note — Enlace en cláusulas relativas

Relative clauses often flow together with the word they describe, without a pause, since they form one connected idea rather than two separate sentences: el parque que visitaba de niña is said as a single breath group, not as two chopped phrases. Practice linking the final consonant or vowel of one word directly into the next: e-l par-queque-vi-si-ta-ba.

Notice also that que as a relative pronoun never carries a written accent, unlike the question word qué. Read a sentence like Rodrigo es el vecino que organizó el proyecto aloud, keeping que unstressed and quickly linked to the words around it.

Grammar: Cláusulas relativas con que, donde y quien

Purpose: combine two related ideas into one connected sentence in order to describe a person, place, or thing more naturally.

Que — el pronombre relativo más común

Que replaces a repeated noun and connects two sentences into one. It can refer to people or things, and it never changes form.

  • El parque es viejo. + El parque está cerca de mi casa. → El parque que está cerca de mi casa es viejo.The park that is near my house is old.
  • Rodrigo es un vecino. + Rodrigo organizó el proyecto del pasillo. → Rodrigo es el vecino que organizó el proyecto del pasillo.Rodrigo is the neighbor who organized the hallway project.

Note that que is required in Spanish even in cases where English sometimes drops "that" or "who": el libro que leí (the book [that] I read).

Donde — para describir lugares

Donde replaces a location and connects a description of a place to the sentence that follows.

  • Este es el parque. + Mi abuela me llevaba al parque de niña. → Este es el parque donde mi abuela me llevaba de niña.This is the park where my grandmother used to take me as a child.
  • El salón de reuniones es el lugar donde se fundó la asociación del vecindario.The meeting hall is the place where the neighborhood association was founded.

Donde can also often be replaced by en el que, en la que, and similar forms, but donde is the more natural, high-frequency choice at this level.

Quien / quienes — para personas después de preposición o coma

Quien (singular) and quienes (plural) refer only to people. They are most common directly after a preposition (a quien, con quien, de quien) or after a comma, where they introduce extra, non-identifying information.

  • Sra. Duarte, quien fundó la asociación hace veinte años, todavía asiste a cada reunión.Sra. Duarte, who founded the association twenty years ago, still attends every meeting.
  • El vecino con quien trabajé en el pasillo se llama Marcos.The neighbor with whom I worked on the hallway is named Marcos.

In everyday speech, que can often replace quien after a comma (Sra. Duarte, que fundó la asociación...), but quien is required directly after a preposition.

Comas y significado: información extra o información necesaria

A relative clause without commas identifies exactly which person or thing is meant. A relative clause set off by commas adds extra information about someone or something already identified.

  • El vecino que organizó el proyecto se llama Rodrigo. (identifica cuál vecino)
  • Rodrigo, que organizó el proyecto, se llama así desde niño. (información extra sobre Rodrigo)

This project does not require memorizing formal punctuation rules in detail; it asks you to notice that commas change whether a clause narrows down a noun or simply adds a comment about it.

Reading

Read the following bulletin-board post that Rodrigo writes about the neighbor who first inspired the hallway project.

La vecina que empezó todo

Quiero presentarles a Sra. Duarte, quien fundó esta asociación del vecindario hace veinte años. Ella es la persona que primero propuso arreglar el pasillo, el proyecto que todos completamos juntos el año pasado. Sra. Duarte vive en la casa donde crece el jardín más bonito de la calle, y es la vecina a quien todos acudimos cuando tenemos un problema.

El salón de reuniones, donde nos vemos cada mes, fue idea de ella también. Es el lugar donde discutimos proyectos como el parque y el jardín comunitario, y donde escuchamos las opiniones de todos, aunque no siempre estemos de acuerdo.

Sra. Duarte es la vecina que ha vivido aquí más tiempo, y es la persona de quien todos aprendemos algo sobre cómo mantener unida a una comunidad. Por eso, antes de votar sobre el próximo proyecto, quiero que todos recordemos a la persona que hizo posible que tuviéramos una asociación en primer lugar.

Writing Workshop: Una persona o un lugar que importa

Your writing task for this chapter is a short connected description of a person or place that matters to you, using relative clauses to combine short ideas into smoother sentences.

  1. Estudia el modelo. Reread Rodrigo's post about Sra. Duarte. Mark each relative clause and identify whether it uses que, donde, or quien.
  2. Planifica. Choose a person or place that matters to you. Write down three or four short, separate sentences about them, the way Camila's first draft looked before Rodrigo helped her.
  3. Escribe el primer borrador. Combine your short sentences into a connected description of at least six sentences that includes: at least two clauses with que, at least one clause with donde, and at least one clause with quien or quienes.
  4. Revisa. Check your draft against the editing checklist below.
  5. Escribe la versión final. Produce a clean final version of your description.

Lista de revisión:

  • I used que to combine sentences about people or things.
  • I used donde to describe a place.
  • I used quien or quienes after a preposition or comma, referring only to people.
  • My relative que has no written accent, unlike the question word qué.
  • My description reads smoothly, without unnecessary short, choppy sentences.
  • My accents and punctuation, including inverted marks, are correct.

Speaking Practice

Práctica controlada

With a partner, take turns combining two short sentences about a familiar person or place into one sentence using que, donde, or quien.

Tarea comunicativa: Presentar a alguien importante

With a partner or small group, take turns introducing a person who matters to you (a family member, a friend, a mentor), describing them with at least one relative clause and explaining where you know them from, also using a relative clause.

Alternativa individual

If you are studying independently: record yourself describing a person and a place that matter to you, using at least one clause with que, one with donde, and one with quien.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context

In many Spanish-speaking communities, introducing a person often includes not just their name but their connection to a place, a family, or a shared history, such as la vecina que fundó la asociación or el señor con quien trabajaba mi papá. This way of describing people through their relationships and roles in a community is common, though the specific details that matter most can vary by family, generation, and region. Paying attention to how people introduce one another can reveal what a community considers important to remember.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake

Error: Writing an accent on the relative que — El parque qué está cerca de mi casa.

Correction: The relative pronoun que never carries a written accent: El parque que está cerca de mi casa. Reserve qué for questions and exclamations.

Common Mistake

Error: Using donde to describe something that is not a place — El vecino donde organizó el proyecto...

Correction: Use que for people and things, and reserve donde for locations: El vecino que organizó el proyecto...

Common Mistake

Error: Using quien directly before a verb without a comma or preposition — La vecina quien vive allí.

Correction: Use que directly before a verb in that position, and save quien for after a preposition or comma: La vecina que vive allí. / Sra. Duarte, quien vive allí, es muy amable.

Looking Ahead

Lo que ya sabes

You can now combine short, related ideas into one connected sentence using que, describe a place using donde, and refer to a person after a preposition or comma using quien or quienes. This system lets you write and speak in longer, more natural sentences instead of a series of short, disconnected statements, and it will support the more extended descriptions and narratives coming later in this unit.

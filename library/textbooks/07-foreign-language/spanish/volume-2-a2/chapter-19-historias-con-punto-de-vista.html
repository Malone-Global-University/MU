Historias con punto de vista

Postura narrativa, emoción, causa y resultado, comparación, y cohesión de párrafo · Speak and Write Spanish: Everyday Independence and Connected Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Narrate a personal story from a clear point of view, showing how the narrator felt about the events.
  2. Express emotional reactions to past events using verbs and expressions of feeling.
  3. Explain cause and result within a narrative using connectors such as porque, ya que, como, and por eso.
  4. Compare two moments, people, or experiences within a narrative using comparative and superlative structures.
  5. Read a multi-paragraph narrative told from a particular point of view, tracking emotional stance and cause-result relationships across paragraphs.
  6. Write a connected narrative paragraph that maintains a consistent point of view and uses discourse markers to organize cause, result, and comparison.

Communication Scenario

The neighborhood vote from Chapter 18 finally happened, and the park project won. Camila is writing a short piece for the association's newsletter about the day of the vote, but she realizes that the story reads very differently depending on whose point of view she tells it from. From Sra. Duarte's point of view, the day was emotional because it marked twenty years of work. From Marcos's point of view, the day was mostly about frustration with the process, since he still isn't convinced the chat group was a fair way to vote. Camila decides to write two short paragraphs, each told from one person's point of view, and then compare the two experiences at the end.

  1. Think of an event that two people you know experienced very differently. What made their points of view different: their role in the event, their emotions, their expectations, something else?
  2. In English, how do you show a reader that a story is being told from one particular person's point of view, rather than as a neutral list of facts?

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Note — Entonación emocional en la narración

When a narrator expresses an emotional reaction, Spanish speakers often place extra stress and a slightly wider pitch range on the emotion word itself: Me sentí muy orgullosa ese día rises and falls more noticeably on orgullosa than on the surrounding words.

Cause-and-result connectors like ya que and como resultado are usually said as tightly linked phrase units, without a pause in the middle: yaque, comoresultado. Practice saying Ya que Sra. Duarte fundó la asociación, se sintió muy emocionada aloud, keeping ya que light and connected to what follows.

Grammar: Punto de vista, emoción, causa-resultado, y comparación

Purpose: narrate past events from a consistent point of view, explain why things happened and what resulted, and compare experiences within a connected paragraph.

Postura narrativa con sentirse y adjetivos de emoción

To narrate from a point of view, tell the reader not only what happened but how the narrator or a character felt about it. Use sentirse plus an emotion adjective, or estar plus an emotion adjective, recycling the preterite and imperfect from earlier chapters to distinguish a specific emotional reaction from an ongoing emotional state.

  • Cuando anunciaron los resultados, Sra. Duarte se sintió muy emocionada.When they announced the results, Sra. Duarte felt very moved.
  • Durante toda la semana, Marcos estaba frustrado con el proceso de votación.All week, Marcos was frustrated with the voting process.

Notice the aspect contrast: the preterite (se sintió) reports a specific emotional reaction at one moment, while the imperfect (estaba) describes an ongoing emotional background, just as you practiced with other verbs in Chapters 6 through 8.

Causa y resultado en la narración

Narratives explain not just what happened, but why. Use porque for everyday cause, ya que and debido a que for more formal or written cause, and como at the beginning of a sentence for cause that the reader should understand before the main event. Use por eso and como resultado to introduce the result of a cause already stated.

  • Como Sra. Duarte fundó la asociación hace veinte años, el proyecto significaba mucho para ella.Since Sra. Duarte founded the association twenty years ago, the project meant a lot to her.
  • Marcos no confiaba en el grupo de chat; por eso, votó con cierta duda.Marcos didn't trust the chat group; that's why he voted with some doubt.
  • Muchos vecinos participaron en la votación. Como resultado, la decisión reflejó la opinión de casi todo el barrio.Many neighbors took part in the vote. As a result, the decision reflected the opinion of almost the whole neighborhood.

Do not confuse causal como, placed at the start of a clause, with comparative como meaning "like" or "as," which you will review below.

Repaso: comparación entre dos momentos o personas

A narrative with point of view often compares two experiences, two moments, or two people. This chapter recycles comparatives, superlatives, and quantifier agreement from earlier study and adds a diferencia de for contrast between two points of view.

  • Sra. Duarte se sintió más emocionada que nunca ese día.Sra. Duarte felt more moved than ever that day.
  • A diferencia de Sra. Duarte, Marcos no estaba tan seguro de que el proceso fuera justo.Unlike Sra. Duarte, Marcos wasn't so sure the process was fair.
Cohesión de párrafo: mantener el punto de vista

A narrative paragraph should stay consistently inside one point of view unless a clear signal, such as desde el punto de vista de or a new paragraph, tells the reader that the perspective has shifted. Discourse markers from Chapter 18 (primero, sin embargo, por otro lado, por eso) combine with this chapter's cause-result and comparison connectors to keep a multi-sentence narrative organized around a single emotional throughline.

  • Desde el punto de vista de Sra. Duarte, primero hubo nervios; sin embargo, cuando anunciaron los resultados, se sintió aliviada y, al mismo tiempo, muy orgullosa.From Sra. Duarte's point of view, first there was nervousness; however, when they announced the results, she felt relieved and, at the same time, very proud.

Reading

Read the following draft of Camila's newsletter article, which tells the story of the vote from two points of view and then compares them.

Un día, dos puntos de vista

Desde el punto de vista de Sra. Duarte, el día de la votación fue uno de los más importantes de los últimos veinte años. Primero, sintió nervios mientras esperaba los resultados. Sin embargo, cuando anunciaron que el parque había ganado, se sintió profundamente emocionada, ya que ella misma había fundado la asociación dos décadas atrás. Al mismo tiempo, sintió alivio: por fin, el vecindario había decidido algo juntos.

Desde el punto de vista de Marcos, el día fue más complicado. Como él dudaba de que el grupo de chat fuera un lugar justo para votar, pasó la semana frustrado y un poco desconfiado. Por eso, cuando le preguntaron su opinión, respondió con cautela. Sin embargo, después de hablar con Sra. Duarte sobre por qué el proyecto le importaba tanto, Marcos se sintió más aliviado y, poco a poco, más orgulloso de haber participado.

A diferencia de Sra. Duarte, Marcos no vivió el día como una celebración desde el principio. No obstante, los dos terminaron sintiéndose orgullosos del resultado, aunque llegaron a ese sentimiento por caminos muy distintos. Como resultado, el artículo de esta semana no cuenta una sola historia, sino dos.

Preguntas de comprensión:

  1. ¿Qué emociones sintió Sra. Duarte durante el día de la votación, y en qué orden?
  2. ¿Por qué estaba frustrado Marcos antes de hablar con Sra. Duarte?
  3. ¿Qué conectores de causa y resultado usa Camila para organizar cada punto de vista?
  4. ¿Cómo compara Camila los dos puntos de vista al final del artículo?

Writing Workshop: Una historia, dos puntos de vista

Your writing task for this chapter is to narrate a real or invented event from two different points of view, using emotion vocabulary, cause-result connectors, and a closing comparison.

  1. Estudia el modelo. Reread Camila's article. Identify the emotion words and the cause-result connectors used for each point of view.
  2. Planifica. Choose an event and two people who experienced it differently. Note at least two emotions and one cause-result relationship for each person.
  3. Escribe el primer punto de vista. Write a connected paragraph of at least four sentences narrating the event from the first person's point of view, including at least one emotion expression and one cause-result connector.
  4. Escribe el segundo punto de vista. Write a second paragraph of similar length from the other person's point of view, using different emotion vocabulary where appropriate.
  5. Compara los dos puntos de vista. Write a closing sentence or two using a comparative structure or a diferencia de to connect the two paragraphs.
  6. Revisa. Check your draft against the editing checklist below.

Lista de revisión:

  • Each paragraph stays consistently inside one point of view.
  • I used at least one emotion expression with correct preterite/imperfect aspect for each point of view.
  • I used at least one cause-result connector (porque, ya que, como, por eso, or como resultado) in each paragraph.
  • I used a comparative structure or a diferencia de to connect the two points of view.
  • My adjectives agree in gender and number with the people they describe.
  • My accents, punctuation, and inverted marks are correct.

Speaking Practice

Práctica controlada

With a partner, take turns completing sentences that describe an emotional reaction to a past event, choosing the correct preterite or imperfect form of sentirse or estar.

Tarea comunicativa: Dos puntos de vista

Tell a partner about an event from your own point of view, including at least one emotion and one cause-result connector. Then ask your partner to imagine how another person involved in that event might have felt, and compare the two points of view together.

Alternativa individual

If you are studying independently: record yourself narrating the same event twice, once from your own point of view and once from another person's imagined point of view, then compare the two aloud using a comparative structure.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context

Community storytelling, such as neighborhood newsletters, local radio segments, and oral histories, often deliberately presents more than one point of view about the same shared event. This is not considered a weakness in the account; in many community and civic contexts across Spanish-speaking countries, acknowledging different emotional experiences of the same event is seen as a way of respecting everyone's contribution, rather than flattening the story into a single official version. The habit of naming whose point of view is being told, with phrases like desde el punto de vista de, is a useful pragmatic tool in journalism, civic writing, and everyday storytelling alike.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake

Error: Using como as a comparative word when a causal connector is needed — Como Sra. Duarte, se sintió orgullosa. (intended meaning: "Since Sra. Duarte..., she felt proud.")

Correction: Causal como at the start of a sentence needs its own clause with a verb: Como Sra. Duarte fundó la asociación, se sintió orgullosa. Without a verb, como Sra. Duarte reads as a comparison ("like Sra. Duarte"), not a cause.

Common Mistake

Error: Mixing preterite and imperfect inconsistently for the same ongoing emotion — Marcos estuvo frustrado toda la semana porque dudó del proceso.

Correction: An ongoing emotional background during a period of time is usually imperfect, while the cause that triggered it may be preterite or imperfect depending on whether it is a single moment or a background state: Marcos estaba frustrado toda la semana porque dudaba del proceso.

Common Mistake

Error: Forgetting adjective agreement with emotion adjectives — Sra. Duarte estaba orgulloso.

Correction: Emotion adjectives agree in gender and number with the person they describe: Sra. Duarte estaba orgullosa. Marcos, by contrast, would be orgulloso.

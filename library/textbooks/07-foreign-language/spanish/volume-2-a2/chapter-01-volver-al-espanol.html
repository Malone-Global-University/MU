Volver al español

Diagnóstico, reparación y reajuste · Speak and Write Spanish: Everyday Independence and Connected Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Use repair and clarification language to keep a conversation going when you do not understand or cannot remember a word.
  2. Reset and self-check your pronunciation of the five Spanish vowels, word stress, and the sounds that most often affect intelligibility for English speakers.
  3. Identify and correct your own errors in gender, number, and article-noun agreement.
  4. Choose consistently among tú, usted, and ustedes within a single conversation, based on relationship and context.
  5. Review ser, estar, and hay to describe yourself, your routine, and your surroundings.
  6. Diagnose your own strengths and gaps from Volume I and set concrete goals for Volume II.

Communication Scenario

A new term begins, and your class or study group has come back together after a break. Everyone is a little rusty. Before the group can move forward into new material, the instructor asks each person to reintroduce themselves, describe their life now, and — most importantly — practice asking for help when a word or structure will not come. Diego, Sofía, Marta, Andrés, Lucía, and Wendy, familiar voices from Volume I, are back for Volume II. This chapter is your own version of that re-entry conversation.

  1. In English, list three things you remember clearly from your earlier Spanish study, and one thing you find yourself forgetting or hesitating over.
  2. Think of a recent moment — in any language — when you needed to ask someone to repeat, slow down, or explain something. What did you say?

Pronunciation Reset

Sound Focus — Five Stable Vowels and Syllable Timing

Spanish has five vowel sounds — a, e, i, o, u — and each one keeps a consistent, "pure" quality no matter where it falls in a word. English speakers often let unstressed vowels reduce toward a neutral "uh" sound (the way the a in "banana" is not pronounced the same in every syllable). In Spanish, every vowel keeps its full value: banana is pronounced with three clear, identical a sounds. Practice this contrast aloud with mesa, casa, elefante, and oficina, keeping each vowel crisp and level.

Sound Focus — Word Stress and the Written Accent

Review the two default stress rules: words ending in a vowel, n, or s are normally stressed on the second-to-last syllable (ha-blan, ca-sas, li-bro); words ending in any other consonant are normally stressed on the last syllable (ha-blar, pa-pel, ciu-dad). A written accent marks an exception to these patterns (ha-bló, lá-piz, jo-ven — wait, joven actually follows the default rule and needs no accent). Say each pair aloud and listen for the shift: hablo / habló, papa / papá, esta / está.

Sound Focus — Priority Sounds for English Speakers

As you speak this chapter's dialogue and passages aloud, monitor yourself on the sounds that most affect intelligibility: seseo (c before e/i, and z, pronounced like English s in this course's model); a broadly yeísta ll/y; the silent h in words like hola and hasta; not overdistinguishing b and v, which represent the same sound in Spanish; and distinguishing the single tap r from the trilled rr, as in pero versus perro. None of this is new content — the goal here is a deliberate self-check before Volume II builds further.

Práctica — lectura en voz alta: Read the following aloud, paying attention to vowel clarity and the stress in bold: Hola, me llamo Sofía. Soy de aquí, pero mi familia es de México. Este semestre quiero hablar español todos los días.

Grammar Synthesis: Agreement and Address

Purpose: no major new grammar is introduced in this chapter. Instead, the two systems that support every other sentence you will build in this volume — agreement and address — are reviewed, diagnosed, and corrected before new material is layered on top of them.

1.1 Concordancia: género, número y artículos

ElementoEjemplo correctoError común
Artículo + sustantivola clase, el problema, las ciudades*el clase, *la problema
Sustantivo + adjetivouna casa grande, unos libros interesantes*una casa grandes
Sustantivos irregularesel día, la mano, el mapa*la día, *el mano

Grammatical gender is a property of the noun itself, not of the thing it names; it does not correspond to biological sex. Endings such as -a and -o are useful patterns, not guarantees — el problema, el día, and la mano are common early exceptions worth memorizing individually.

Grammar Note — Checking Your Own Agreement

Before you finish a sentence, run a quick internal check: does every article and adjective attached to a noun match that noun's gender and number? This single habit prevents the largest share of Spanish learner errors at this level.

  • Tengo dos hermanas simpáticas.I have two nice sisters. (feminine plural noun → feminine plural adjective)
  • Vivo en un apartamento pequeño.I live in a small apartment. (masculine singular noun → masculine singular adjective, despite the -a ending on apartamento's near-relative "casa")

1.2 Tú, usted y ustedes juntos en una conversación

Volume II conversations regularly move among more than one address form. Review the cues:

  • Usa tú con amigos, compañeros de tu edad, y familia cercana.Use tú with friends, peers, and close family.
  • Usa usted con personas mayores, profesores, y en situaciones formales o de servicio.Use usted with older people, teachers, and in formal or service situations.
  • Usa ustedes para dirigirte a más de una persona, en cualquier registro, bajo el modelo de este curso.Use ustedes to address more than one person, in any register, under this course's model.

Once you choose an address form for a given relationship, keep it consistent for the rest of that exchange. Switching without reason is one of the most noticeable errors a learner can make.

1.3 Ser, estar y hay: repaso funcional

  • Soy de aquí y soy estudiante.I'm from here and I'm a student. (ser = origin, classification)
  • Estoy un poco nervioso/a hoy.I'm a little nervous today. (estar = current state)
  • Hay veinte estudiantes en la clase.There are twenty students in the class. (hay = existence)

Avoid the shortcut "ser is permanent, estar is temporary." Instead, ask what function the sentence needs: identity or classification points to ser; location, condition, or state points to estar; existence of something indefinite points to hay.

Práctica — Corrige el error:

  1. Mi hermana es alto y simpático. → (corrige la concordancia)
  2. El problema es complicada. → (corrige la concordancia)
  3. ¿Cómo tú se llama? → (elige tú o usted de forma consistente)
  4. Soy en la oficina ahora. → (elige ser o estar)
  5. Está tres parques en mi barrio. → (elige hay o está)

Writing Workshop: Mi autodiagnóstico

Your writing task for this chapter is a short self-diagnostic paragraph: what you remember well from Volume I, what you want to review, and one goal for this term.

  1. Estudia el modelo. Reread Sofía's message above. Underline every verb form and note whether it is present, preterite, or a fixed expression.

  3. Escribe el primer borrador. Write at least six sentences in Spanish covering your self-diagnosis and one goal.
  4. Revisa. Check your draft against the editing checklist below.

Lista de revisión:

  • I used ser, estar, and hay correctly.
  • I chose one address form and used it consistently, if I addressed anyone directly.
  • I used at least one repair or clarification expression.
  • My accents and punctuation follow Spanish conventions.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context

Asking for repetition, clarification, or a slower pace is not a sign of weakness in Spanish-speaking communication; it is a normal and expected part of any conversation, in any language. Speakers regularly use expressions like ¿Puedes repetir? or No entendí bien without embarrassment. As you rebuild your confidence this term, treat repair language as a genuine communication tool rather than an admission of failure.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake

Error: Mismatched gender between article and noun — el problema es complicada.

Correction: Check the noun's true gender, not just its ending — el problema es complicado.

Common Mistake

Error: Switching address forms without reason within the same exchange — ¿Cómo estás? ¿Y usted, de dónde es?, both directed at the same person.

Correction: Choose one form per relationship and keep it — ¿Cómo estás? ¿Y tú, de dónde eres?

Common Mistake

Error: Confusing ser and estar — Soy en la oficina ahora.

Correction: Location uses estar — Estoy en la oficina ahora.

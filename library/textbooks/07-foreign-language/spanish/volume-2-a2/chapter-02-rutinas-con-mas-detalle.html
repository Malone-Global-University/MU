Rutinas con más detalle

Secuencia, reflexivos y frecuencia · Speak and Write Spanish: Everyday Independence and Connected Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Sequence the parts of a daily routine using ordering words like primero, luego, después, and finalmente.
  2. Use reflexive and pronominal verbs beyond fixed expressions, distinguishing true reflexive meaning from verbs whose meaning simply includes a pronoun.
  3. Use definite and indefinite articles accurately and form regular plural nouns and adjectives.
  4. Express how often you do something using frequency expressions such as siempre, a veces, casi nunca, and todos los días.
  5. Pronounce and distinguish r/rr, b/v, d, g/j, ll/y, and ñ in connected speech.
  6. Write and punctuate questions and exclamations correctly using inverted marks and interrogative accents.

Communication Scenario

Diego has started a new part-time job and is trying to organize his mornings so he is not late. Sofía, his classmate, asks him about his new schedule, curious whether his routine has changed much since last semester. Their conversation moves naturally from "what do you do" to "in what order" and "how often" — exactly the kind of detail Volume I routines did not yet require.

  1. In English, list the steps of your own morning routine in order, using words like "first," "then," and "after that."
  2. Think of one part of your routine you do every day and one you only do sometimes. How would you explain that difference?

Pronunciation Focus

Sound Focus — r and rr

Spanish distinguishes a single tap r (as in pero, cara, primero) from a trilled rr (as in perro, cerrar). A word-initial r is also trilled, even though it is spelled with a single letter: rutina, levantarse. Practice the contrast: pero / perro, caro / carro, ahora / ahorra.

Sound Focus — b/v and d

b and v represent the same sound in Spanish; do not try to distinguish them the way English does. That sound is firmer at the beginning of a phrase or after m/n (vestirse, bien) and softer between vowels (lavarse, la vida). The letter d follows a similar pattern: firmer after a pause or n/l (despertarse, dormirse), and softer between vowels, where it can resemble the "th" in English "this" (despertado, cansado).

Sound Focus — g/j, ll/y, and ñ

j and g before e/i represent a strong sound made farther back in the throat than English "h": hoy, ojo. Under this course's broadly yeísta model, ll and y represent the same sound, similar to English "y" in "yes" but often slightly stronger: me cepillo, ya. The letter ñ is a distinct sound, not simply "n": bañarse, mañana.

Práctica — lectura en voz alta: Todos los días me despierto, me ducho y me cepillo los dientes. Luego desayuno rápido y salgo de casa. Nunca me baño por la noche; casi siempre me acuesto temprano.

Writing Note — Interrogative and Exclamatory Accents

Question words carry a written accent when they ask a direct or indirect question: ¿Qué haces primero?, No sé qué hacer primero. The same word without a question function does not carry an accent: Como cuando tengo hambre. Every Spanish question opens with an inverted question mark ¿ and every exclamation opens with an inverted exclamation mark ¡: ¿A qué hora te levantas? ¡Qué rutina tan ocupada!

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

Purpose: this chapter formally introduces articles as a grammar system, expands reflexive and pronominal verbs beyond formulaic use, and gives you the tools to sequence a routine and describe how often you do something.

2.1 Artículos definidos e indefinidos

MasculinoFemenino
Definido singularella
Definido pluralloslas
Indefinido singularununa
Indefinido pluralunosunas

Use a definite article (el, la, los, las) for something specific or already known — la rutina de Diego — and an indefinite article (un, una, unos, unas) for something non-specific or introduced for the first time — un trabajo nuevo. Spanish also uses a definite article in many general statements where English uses none: Me gusta el café (I like coffee, in general), not *Me gusta café.

2.2 Formación del plural

  • Nouns ending in a vowel: add -s. la rutina → las rutinas
  • Nouns ending in a consonant: add -es. el reloj → los relojes
  • Nouns ending in -z: change to -ces. una vez → unas veces

Adjectives must match the plural form of the noun they describe: rutinas ocupadas, horarios difíciles. Review this alongside Chapter 1's agreement check: article, noun, and adjective must all agree in gender and number.

Grammar Note — Not Every se Verb Means "to Oneself"

A true reflexive action, like me ducho (I shower myself), is genuinely directed back at the subject. But some verbs are simply pronominal by nature — the pronoun is part of the verb's identity, not a separate "self" being acted upon. Dormirse (to fall asleep) and irse (to leave, go away) work this way: Me duermo a las once does not mean "I sleep myself," it means "I fall asleep." Learn these verbs as full lexical units, with their pronoun included, rather than translating the pronoun separately every time.

  • Me cepillo los dientes después de desayunar.I brush my teeth after having breakfast. (true reflexive)
  • Nunca me duermo tarde entre semana.I never fall asleep late on weekdays. (pronominal, not "self-directed")

Reflexive and pronominal pronouns normally go directly before a conjugated verb: Me levanto temprano. When the reflexive verb follows another verb in the infinitive, the pronoun may attach to the end of the infinitive instead: Voy a levantarme temprano or Me voy a levantar temprano — both are correct and equally natural.

2.4 Secuencia y frecuencia en una oración

  • Primero me ducho, luego me visto y después desayuno.First I shower, then I get dressed, and after that I have breakfast.
  • Antes de salir de casa, reviso mi horario.Before leaving the house, I check my schedule.
  • Casi siempre me acuesto antes de las once.I almost always go to bed before eleven.

Model Dialogue

Contexto: Sofía y Diego hablan antes de clase sobre el nuevo horario de Diego. Usan tú porque son compañeros de clase de la misma edad.

SOFÍAOye, ¿cómo va tu trabajo nuevo? ¿Cambió mucho tu rutina?Hey, how's your new job going? Did your routine change a lot?
DIEGOSí, un poco. Ahora me despierto más temprano. Primero me ducho, luego me visto, y después desayuno rápido.Yes, a little. Now I wake up earlier. First I shower, then I get dressed, and after that I have a quick breakfast.
SOFÍA¿Y a qué hora sales de casa?And what time do you leave the house?
DIEGOCasi siempre salgo a las siete y media. Antes de salir, siempre reviso mi horario del día.I almost always leave at seven thirty. Before leaving, I always check my schedule for the day.
SOFÍA¡Qué rutina tan organizada! ¿Y te acuestas temprano también?What an organized routine! And do you go to bed early too?
DIEGOCasi siempre. Me duermo rápido porque estoy cansado. A veces veo una serie antes de dormirme, pero nunca hasta muy tarde.Almost always. I fall asleep fast because I'm tired. Sometimes I watch a series before falling asleep, but never until very late.
SOFÍAYo soy diferente. De vez en cuando me acuesto tardísimo.I'm different. Every once in a while I go to bed extremely late.
DIEGO¡Eso no me sorprende!That doesn't surprise me!

Preguntas de comprensión:

  1. ¿Qué hace Diego primero por la mañana?
  2. ¿A qué hora sale de casa Diego, y qué hace siempre antes de salir?
  3. ¿Qué hace Diego a veces antes de dormirse?
  4. ¿Cómo describe Sofía su propia rutina para acostarse?

Reading

Read the following short blog-style post that Marta wrote about her weekday routine.

La rutina de Marta

Entre semana, mi rutina es bastante fija. Primero me despierto a las seis y media, aunque casi nunca me levanto inmediatamente. Me quedo cinco minutos más en la cama. Luego me ducho, me visto y desayuno algo rápido, generalmente pan con fruta. Antes de salir de casa, siempre reviso mi mochila.

Los fines de semana son diferentes. A veces me despierto tarde y de vez en cuando no me visto hasta el mediodía. ¡Qué diferencia entre la semana y el fin de semana!

Preguntas de comprensión:

  1. ¿Qué hace Marta antes de levantarse de la cama?
  2. ¿Qué desayuna generalmente?
  3. ¿En qué se diferencia su rutina de fin de semana?

Writing Workshop: Mi rutina, paso a paso

Your writing task for this chapter is a short paragraph describing your weekday routine in order, with at least one contrast between weekdays and weekends.

  1. Estudia el modelo. Reread Marta's post above. Underline every sequencing word and every frequency expression.
  2. Planifica. List the steps of your routine in order, and decide which frequency word fits each one.
  3. Escribe el primer borrador. Write at least six sentences in Spanish, sequencing your routine and including at least two frequency expressions.
  4. Revisa. Check your draft against the editing checklist below.
  5. Escribe la versión final. Produce a clean final version.

Lista de revisión:

  • My articles agree in gender and number with the nouns they introduce.
  • My reflexive and pronominal verbs are conjugated correctly and the pronoun matches the subject.
  • I used at least two sequencing words and two frequency expressions.
  • My questions and exclamations use inverted marks and correct accents.
  • My accents and punctuation follow Spanish conventions.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context

Daily schedules vary widely across Spanish-speaking communities, shaped by work hours, school schedules, climate, and family norms rather than by any single national pattern. In some contexts, the midday meal is the largest of the day and includes a longer break; in others, the schedule looks closer to a typical North American workday. Avoid assuming that any one routine, including the one modeled in this chapter, represents "how Spanish speakers live."

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake

Error: Dropping the reflexive pronoun — Levanto a las siete.

Correction: Keep the pronoun that matches the subject — Me levanto a las siete.

Common Mistake

Error: Omitting the definite article with general likes — Me gusta café.

Correction: Spanish requires the article in general statements — Me gusta el café.

Common Mistake

Error: Missing the accent on a question word — No sé que hacer primero.

Correction: Interrogative words carry a written accent, even in indirect questions — No sé qué hacer primero.

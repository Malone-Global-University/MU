Medios, tecnología y mensajes

Comunicación digital, información reportada, y el tono formal e informal en los mensajes · Speak and Write Spanish: Everyday Independence and Connected Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Discuss common digital communication tools and habits using vocabulary for messages, apps, and devices.
  2. Report what someone else said using decir que and related reporting expressions.
  3. Distinguish formal and informal written register in emails and messages, and choose the appropriate opening, closing, and address form for each.
  4. Express and support an opinion about technology or media using creo que, no creo que, and related opinion frames, recycling relative clauses to add detail.
  5. Read and interpret a multi-paragraph text about digital communication, tracking connectors and reference across paragraphs.
  6. Write a short connected multi-paragraph message or post, choosing a register appropriate to its audience.

Communication Scenario

The vote on the neighborhood association's next project is now open, and Camila is in charge of announcing it through the group's messaging app and by email. She realizes she needs two very different versions: a quick, casual message for the neighborhood chat group, and a formal email for the city's community-services office, which the association must notify before any public project can move forward. Meanwhile, Rodrigo overhears two neighbors disagreeing about whether the group chat is even a good way to organize a vote, and he has to report what each of them said to Sra. Duarte, who was not in the conversation.

  1. Think about a message you write differently depending on who will read it. What actually changes: the words, the greeting, the length, something else?
  2. In English, how do you tell someone what another person said without quoting them directly? Notice what changes about the verb tense when you do this.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Note — Entonación al reportar y opinar

When reporting what someone said, Spanish speakers typically keep a level, matter-of-fact intonation on dice que... or comentó que..., saving any emotional emphasis for the reported content itself rather than the reporting verb: Dice que el grupo de chat es mejor is said with the pitch rising slightly on mejor, not on dice.

When giving an opinion, creo que is usually unstressed and linked closely to the words that follow, almost like one breath group: creoqueestábien. Practice saying Creo que es una buena idea and No creo que sea necesario aloud, keeping the opening phrase light and quick.

Grammar: Estilo indirecto, opinión, y conectores de discurso

Purpose: report what someone else said, express and support an opinion, and connect several sentences into a cohesive paragraph.

Reportar información con decir que

To report what someone said without quoting them directly, use decir que followed by the indicative. The reported information is treated as a fact being passed along, not as something doubted or desired, so the subjunctive is not used here.

  • Marcos: "El grupo de chat es confuso." → Marcos dice que el grupo de chat es confuso.Marcos says that the chat group is confusing.
  • Sra. Duarte: "Prefiero el correo electrónico." → Sra. Duarte comentó que prefiere el correo electrónico.Sra. Duarte mentioned that she prefers email.

Notice that pronouns and possessives often shift when you report someone's words, just as they do in English: "mi mensaje" (my message) becomes "su mensaje" (his/her message) when reported by someone else.

Repaso: opinión con creo que y no creo que

As you learned in Chapter 16, creo que and similar expressions of belief or certainty take the indicative, while no creo que and other expressions of doubt take the subjunctive. This chapter recycles that contrast in the context of opinions about technology and media, and adds relative clauses to make opinions more detailed.

  • Creo que el correo electrónico, que es más formal, funciona mejor para la oficina.I think that email, which is more formal, works better for the office.
  • No creo que el grupo de chat, donde todos escriben al mismo tiempo, sea el mejor lugar para votar.I don't think the chat group, where everyone writes at the same time, is the best place to vote.
Registro escrito: correo formal frente a mensaje informal

A formal email and an informal chat message differ in greeting, closing, sentence length, and address form, not only in vocabulary. A formal email to an institution uses usted, a clear subject line, a full greeting and closing, and complete sentences. An informal message to friends or neighbors may use tú, shorter sentences, and no formal opening or closing at all.

  • Formal: Estimada Oficina de Servicios Comunitarios: Le escribo para informarle que...Formal: Dear Community Services Office: I am writing to inform you that...
  • Informal: Hola a todos, ya pueden votar por el proyecto. ¡Avisen si tienen preguntas!Informal: Hi everyone, you can now vote on the project. Let me know if you have questions!

Do not mix the two systems within a single message: switching from usted to tú partway through a formal email, for example, undermines the register you are trying to establish.

Conectores para organizar un párrafo

Connected paragraphs use discourse markers to organize ideas across sentences, not just within one sentence. Useful connectors for this chapter include primero, además, por otro lado, sin embargo, and por eso, which show sequence, addition, contrast, and result across a paragraph.

  • Primero, mandé un mensaje al grupo. Sin embargo, no todos lo vieron. Por eso, decidí escribir también un correo formal.First, I sent a message to the group. However, not everyone saw it. That's why I decided to also write a formal email.

Práctica de repaso — Reporta la información usando decir que o comentar que:

  1. Marcos: "Prefiero los mensajes cortos."
  2. Camila: "El correo formal es necesario para la oficina."
  3. Sra. Duarte: "No entiendo bien la aplicación nueva."

Reading

Read the following multi-paragraph message that Rodrigo drafts for the city's community-services office before sending it, and the shorter, informal version he later posts for the neighbors.

Correo a la Oficina de Servicios Comunitarios

Estimada Oficina de Servicios Comunitarios: Le escribo en nombre de la Asociación del Vecindario para informarle sobre la votación que organizamos esta semana. Como saben, la asociación propuso dos proyectos: la reparación del parque y la renovación del salón de reuniones, el lugar donde nos vemos cada mes.

Primero, quiero mencionar que ambos proyectos tienen el apoyo de varios vecinos. Sra. Duarte, quien fundó la asociación hace veinte años, dice que los dos proyectos son necesarios para la comunidad. Sin embargo, según los comentarios que hemos recibido, el salón de reuniones necesita reparaciones más urgentes.

Por otro lado, el parque, que muchos vecinos visitan con sus familias, también requiere atención pronto. Por eso, hemos decidido dejar que los vecinos voten directamente antes de solicitar el permiso oficial. Adjunto encontrará el formulario con los resultados preliminares. Quedo atento a cualquier pregunta que tengan. Atentamente, Rodrigo Fuentes, Asociación del Vecindario.

Mensaje para el grupo de vecinos

¡Hola a todos! Ya mandé el correo a la oficina de la ciudad. Les conté que Sra. Duarte cree que los dos proyectos son importantes, pero que el salón necesita reparaciones más urgentes. Sigan votando esta semana y avísenme si tienen dudas. ¡Gracias por participar!

Writing Workshop: Dos mensajes, dos tonos

Your writing task for this chapter is to produce two short connected messages about the same piece of information, one formal and one informal, choosing register and reported information appropriately for each audience.

  1. Estudia el modelo. Reread Rodrigo's formal email and informal group message. List at least four differences between them.
  2. Planifica. Choose a piece of neighborhood or community information you want to share, and identify one opinion from a person you want to report using decir que.
  3. Escribe el correo formal. Write a formal email of at least four connected sentences, including a formal greeting and closing, at least one instance of reported information, and at least one discourse connector such as primero, sin embargo, or por eso.
  4. Escribe el mensaje informal. Write a short, informal message of two or three sentences sharing the same core information for a group of friends or neighbors.
  5. Revisa. Check both drafts against the editing checklist below.

Lista de revisión:

  • My formal email uses usted consistently, with a formal greeting and closing.
  • My informal message uses tú or a neutral group tone consistently, without formal openings.
  • I used decir que or comentar que with the indicative to report information.
  • I used at least one discourse connector to organize my formal email.
  • I did not mix formal and informal address forms within the same message.
  • My accents, punctuation, and inverted marks are correct.

Alternativa individual

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context

Expectations about formal written communication vary across Spanish-speaking communities and institutions, but many government offices, schools, and businesses still expect a formal register with usted, a clear subject line, and a complete greeting and closing, even when the message is sent by email rather than on paper. At the same time, everyday messaging apps often favor short, informal exchanges among friends, family, and neighbors, sometimes mixing brief written Spanish with abbreviations or emoji. Recognizing which register a given platform or audience expects is itself a communicative skill, and it can vary by generation, workplace, and country.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake

Error: Using the subjunctive after decir que when simply reporting information — Marcos dice que el grupo sea confuso.

Correction: When decir que reports a fact or statement, use the indicative: Marcos dice que el grupo es confuso. The subjunctive would only appear if decir were giving a command, which is a different structure.

Common Mistake

Error: Mixing tú and usted within the same formal email — Estimada Oficina: Le escribo porque quieres saber...

Correction: Keep address forms consistent throughout a message: Estimada Oficina: Le escribo porque quiere saber... refers to a third party, or restate the sentence to keep usted consistent if addressing the office directly.

Common Mistake

Error: Opening a formal email the same way as a text message to a friend — Hola, quería avisarles que... sent to a government office.

Correction: Match the greeting to the audience: a formal email to an institution should open with something like Estimada Oficina de Servicios Comunitarios: rather than a casual Hola.

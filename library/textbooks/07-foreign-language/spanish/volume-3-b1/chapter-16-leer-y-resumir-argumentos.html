Leer y resumir argumentos

Estructura del argumento, resumen, referencia de tipo cita y paráfrasis · Speak and Write Spanish: Independent Communication and Connected Discourse

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify the basic structure of a short argumentative text: claim, supporting reasons, and conclusion.
  2. Recognize connectors that signal claims, evidence, and conclusions in extended written argument.
  3. Track reference across a multi-paragraph text using pronouns, demonstratives, and other reference devices.
  4. Summarize an argument in your own words without copying its sentence structure.
  5. Paraphrase specific claims using synonyms, restructured clauses, and controlled vocabulary substitution.
  6. Attribute a claim to its source using citation-like reference expressions such as según el autor and el artículo sostiene que.
  7. Edit a summary for accuracy in ser/estar/hay, agreement, and register.

This chapter opens Unit IV. It introduces argument structure, textual reference chains, and citation-like attribution as new productive systems, while recycling the subjunctive, conditional, and connector systems consolidated in Chapters 11 through 15.

Communication Scenario

Camila's neighborhood association, still working on the volunteer program from Proyecto 3, has been asked to prepare a one-page summary of a published opinion piece about community volunteering for the next public meeting. The summary must state the author's main argument accurately, in the group's own words, and must clearly show which ideas belong to the author and which belong to the group.

  1. Before reading, think about what makes a summary different from a translation: a summary keeps the meaning but changes the words and often shortens the structure.
  2. Consider a time you had to explain someone else's argument to a third person. Notice how you signaled that the idea was not your own.

Grammar: Estructura del argumento y referencia textual

Purpose: to introduce how a written argument is organized into claim, support, and conclusion, and how a multi-paragraph text keeps its ideas connected through textual reference chains.

New System: La estructura del argumento

A short argumentative text is typically organized in three parts:

  • Tesis: the author's main claim, often stated early. El artículo sostiene que el voluntariado fortalece a las comunidades.
  • Razones y evidencia: the reasons and examples that support the claim, frequently introduced with por un lado... por otro lado, además, or por ejemplo.
  • Conclusión: a closing statement that restates the claim or proposes an action, often introduced with en resumen, por lo tanto, or en conclusión.

Recognizing this structure helps a reader summarize accurately: a summary should represent the thesis, the strongest reasons, and the conclusion, without repeating every sentence of the original.

New System: Cadenas de referencia textual

A multi-paragraph text keeps ideas connected through a chain of reference: a noun is introduced once and then tracked using pronouns, demonstratives, or a repeated but varied noun phrase.

  • Primera mención: el programa de voluntariado
  • Pronombre: lo (direct object) or este (demonstrative, discourse reference)
  • Repetición variada: dicho programa, esta iniciativa

Discourse-level demonstratives such as esto, eso, and aquello can refer back to an entire idea rather than a single noun: El autor propone capacitar a más voluntarios. Esto, según él, resolvería el problema de recursos.

Review and Extension: Referencia con clíticos complejos

Summarizing another writer's ideas often requires precise object-pronoun reference, now used with increasing independence: El autor menciona el problema, pero no lo explica en detalle. La solución que propone, se la presenta al comité sin detalles.

Práctica controlada:

  1. Identifica la tesis en esta oración: "El artículo sostiene que el voluntariado fortalece a las comunidades." ¿Cuál es la tesis exacta?
  2. Completa con un demostrativo de discurso: El autor propone dos soluciones. _____ no convence a todos los lectores.
  3. Reescribe usando un pronombre de objeto directo: "El comité no mencionó el presupuesto." → "El comité no _____ mencionó."

Reading Passage: Un artículo de opinión

El voluntariado como inversión comunitaria

Muchas personas piensan en el voluntariado como un favor ocasional, pero este artículo sostiene que el voluntariado organizado es, en realidad, una inversión a largo plazo para cualquier comunidad. Por un lado, los programas bien estructurados generan confianza entre los vecinos, ya que las personas que trabajan juntas en una causa común tienden a colaborar después en otros proyectos. Por otro lado, el voluntariado reduce costos para las instituciones públicas, porque tareas como la capacitación básica o el acompañamiento a personas mayores pueden realizarse sin un gasto adicional significativo.

Los críticos de esta idea señalan que un programa de voluntariado mal gestionado puede generar el efecto contrario: si no hay suficiente capacitación, los voluntarios pueden cometer errores que dañan la confianza que se buscaba construir. El autor reconoce este riesgo, pero argumenta que la solución no es eliminar los programas, sino mejorarlos con una capacitación adecuada y una evaluación constante.

En resumen, el artículo defiende que el voluntariado, cuando está bien organizado, beneficia tanto a los voluntarios como a la comunidad, y propone que las asociaciones vecinales traten estos programas como una prioridad y no como un esfuerzo secundario.

This passage is an adapted textbook composition, not an authentic published source.

  1. Identify the sentence that states the author's thesis most directly.
  2. List two reasons the author gives to support the thesis, and identify the connector that introduces each one.
  3. Find one example of a textual reference chain that tracks an idea across two sentences without repeating the same noun phrase.
  4. Identify the sentence that concedes a counterargument, and explain how the author responds to it without abandoning the thesis.
  5. Locate the concluding sentence and identify the connector that introduces it.

Writing Workshop: Resumen y paráfrasis

Write a summary of the reading passage in five to seven sentences. Your summary must state the thesis, the two main reasons, the concession, and the conclusion, entirely in your own words.

  1. Identifica la tesis. Restate the author's main claim using different wording, such as a synonym for inversión or a restructured clause.
  2. Parafrasea las razones. Present each reason without copying the original sentence structure. Change vocabulary and clause order where possible.
  3. Atribuye la idea a su fuente. Use at least two citation-like expressions, such as según el autor, el artículo sostiene que, or el autor argumenta que.
  4. Incluye la concesión y la conclusión. Show that the author acknowledges a counterargument before restating the main claim.
  5. Revisa. Check your draft against the checklist below.

Lista de revisión:

  • My summary uses different sentence structures from the original text, not just different words.
  • Every claim from the author is clearly attributed using a citation-like expression.
  • My reference chains (pronouns, demonstratives) are clear and do not confuse the reader about which idea they point to.
  • I checked ser, estar, and hay for accuracy in each descriptive or existential statement.
  • My accents, capitalization, and punctuation are correct throughout.
  • My summary is shorter than the original passage and does not reproduce full sentences from it.

Speaking Task

Práctica controlada

With a partner, take turns stating the thesis, one reason, and the conclusion of the reading passage aloud, without looking at the text.

Tarea comunicativa: Presentar un resumen oral

In pairs, present your written summary aloud to your partner without reading it word for word. Your partner must identify the thesis, the two reasons, the concession, and the conclusion, and ask one follow-up question about a point that was unclear.

Alternativa individual

If you are studying independently: record yourself presenting your summary aloud, then listen back and check that every claim you attribute to the author is accurately represented and clearly marked as the author's idea rather than your own.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context

Clearly attributing an idea to its source, rather than presenting someone else's argument as one's own, is a valued academic and professional norm in Spanish-language educational and workplace settings, much as it is in many other academic traditions. Expressions such as según el autor and el artículo sostiene que serve this function and are expected in academic writing, journalism, and formal reports across Spanish-speaking communities.

Regional Note (Recognition Only)

Academic and journalistic register in Spanish is broadly consistent across regions, though some publications favor slightly more formal citation phrases such as de acuerdo con in place of según. Both are broadly understood; this course uses según as the default productive form.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake

Error: Writing a "summary" that copies whole sentences from the original text with only minor word changes.

Correction: A summary must restructure sentences, not just substitute synonyms. Change clause order, combine or split sentences, and use your own phrasing.

Common Mistake

Error: Presenting the author's claim without attribution — El voluntariado es una inversión a largo plazo. (stated as the writer's own fact)

Correction: Attribute the claim clearly: Según el autor, el voluntariado es una inversión a largo plazo.

Common Mistake

Error: Using an unclear demonstrative — Esto es un problema. — when the reader cannot tell which earlier idea "esto" refers to.

Correction: Make sure a discourse-level demonstrative points clearly to a single, recently mentioned idea: La falta de capacitación, según los críticos, genera este problema.

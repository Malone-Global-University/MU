Proyecto 2: Narrativa desarrollada

Evaluación integrada: antecedentes, hechos, consecuencias y reflexión en una narración conectada · Speak and Write Spanish: Independent Communication and Connected Discourse

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this integrated assessment chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Narrate a connected past event that includes background, a sequence of events, consequences, and personal reflection.
  2. Combine preterite, imperfect, and pluperfect forms to distinguish background, completed events, and events that had already happened before other past events.
  3. Use future and conditional forms to comment on probability and hypothetical outcomes within a past narration.
  4. Report what someone said or thought using reported speech.
  5. Add emotional nuance to the narration using the controlled imperfect subjunctive expressions introduced in Chapter 9.
  6. Read, listen to, and produce an extended narrative without introducing any grammar system beyond what Unidad II has activated.
  7. Complete an integrated listening, reading, writing, and speaking performance task evaluated against a shared rubric.

Communication Scenario

Camila's article for the neighborhood newsletter is almost finished, but her editor has asked for one more thing: a single connected narrative that a reader who missed every meeting could follow from start to finish — what had already happened before the vote, what happened during the project, what people worried might happen next, what others said and thought along the way, and how the neighborhood feels about it now. This chapter asks you to do the same: pull together everything you have learned about narrating the past into one organized, reflective account.

  1. Think about a story you have told more than once, perhaps to different people. Did you always include the background, or did you sometimes skip straight to what happened?
  2. In English, notice how a well-told story usually answers four questions: What had already happened? What happened next? What did people think would happen? And how did it turn out, and how did people feel about it?

Planning the Narrative

Purpose: to organize a connected narrative into four functional parts before drafting it, so that background, events, consequences, and reflection each receive the verb forms that fit their function.

Language Note: Cuatro capas de una narración conectada

An extended narrative in Spanish is easier to organize if you think of it in four layers rather than as one long string of sentences.

  • Antecedentes (background): what the situation was like before the main events, and what had already happened. Use the imperfect for ongoing background and the pluperfect for events completed before other past events.
  • Hechos (events): what happened, in order. Use the preterite for the main timeline, with reported speech when you need to say what someone said or thought.
  • Consecuencias (consequences): what resulted, and what people expected or thought was likely. Use the preterite for outcomes and the future or conditional for probability and expectation.
  • Reflexión (reflection): how people felt about it, evaluated it, or hoped it would turn out differently. Use the controlled imperfect subjunctive expressions from Chapter 9.

Antes de escribir, organiza tus ideas:

  1. Elige un evento. Choose a real or invented past event you can develop with all four layers.
  2. Antecedentes. Note two or three facts about the situation beforehand, including at least one thing that had already happened.
  3. Hechos. List the main events in order, and identify at least one thing someone said that you will report indirectly.
  4. Consecuencias. Note the outcome and at least one expectation or probability comment using the future or conditional.
  5. Reflexión. Note at least two feelings or evaluations using imperfect subjunctive triggers from Chapter 9.

Model Narrative

Read Camila's finished narrative. As you read, identify which sentences belong to each of the four layers: antecedentes, hechos, consecuencias, and reflexión.

El proyecto del parque: la historia completa

Antes de la reunión, el terreno detrás del edificio ya llevaba años abandonado, y varios vecinos ya habían hablado informalmente sobre qué hacer con él. Rodrigo había propuesto la idea por primera vez el año anterior, pero nadie la había tomado en serio hasta que Camila decidió organizar una reunión oficial.

El día de la reunión, Rodrigo presentó el plan y explicó que el nuevo diseño incluiría más árboles y un espacio para los niños. Don Ernesto preguntó cuánto costaría el proyecto, y Camila le dijo que el presupuesto todavía no estaba listo. Varios vecinos dijeron que preferían esperar, mientras que otros insistieron en que había que actuar pronto. Al final, la comunidad votó, y la mayoría aprobó el proyecto.

Después de la votación, todos sabían que la construcción tardaría varios meses. Muchos pensaban que sería difícil terminar antes del invierno, y algunos temían que el ruido molestara a los vecinos mayores. Sin embargo, el comité aseguró que trabajarían solo durante el día.

Mirando hacia atrás, me alegré de que la comunidad trabajara junta para resolver un problema que llevaba años sin solución. Fue una lástima que no todos pudieran asistir a la reunión final, pero espero que, con el tiempo, el nuevo parque le muestre a todo el barrio que sí valió la pena el esfuerzo.

Preguntas de comprensión:

  1. ¿Qué había pasado antes de la reunión oficial?
  2. ¿Qué dijo don Ernesto, y qué le respondió Camila?
  3. ¿Qué pensaban muchos vecinos sobre la construcción?
  4. ¿De qué se alegró la autora al final, y qué espera para el futuro?

Listening Task

Ask your instructor, a partner, or a text-to-speech tool to read the following short account aloud, twice. As you listen, sort what you hear into the four narrative layers.

"Antes de mudarse, Valentina ya había buscado información sobre el barrio durante meses. Cuando por fin llegó, Rodrigo le explicó todo lo que había pasado con el proyecto del parque. Ella dijo que le sorprendía que un grupo de vecinos hubiera logrado tanto en tan poco tiempo. Muchos pensaban que el proyecto tardaría más de un año, pero terminó antes de lo esperado. Al final, Valentina se alegró de que su nuevo barrio fuera un lugar donde la gente todavía se organizaba para mejorar las cosas juntos."

Reading Task

Reread "El proyecto del parque: la historia completa" above, then complete the following analysis independently or with a partner.

  1. Underline or list every verb in the pluperfect and explain what earlier event each one refers to.
  2. Find one example of reported speech and rewrite it as a direct quotation.
  3. Find one sentence that expresses probability or expectation with the future or conditional, and explain what made the outcome uncertain at that point in the story.
  4. Find every trigger for the imperfect subjunctive in the final paragraph and identify the feeling or evaluation each one expresses.

Writing Workshop: Tu narrativa desarrollada

Using the planning notes from the "Planning the Narrative" section, write an extended narrative of ten to fourteen sentences about a real or invented past event. Your narrative must include all four layers.

  1. Antecedentes. Open with at least one sentence in the imperfect and one in the pluperfect describing the situation before the main events.
  2. Hechos. Narrate the main events in the preterite, including at least one instance of reported speech.
  3. Consecuencias. Describe the outcome, including at least one comment on probability or expectation using the future or conditional.
  4. Reflexión. Close with at least two sentences using imperfect subjunctive triggers from Chapter 9 to express feeling or evaluation about the story as a whole.
  5. Conecta las ideas. Use connectors such as antes de eso, mientras tanto, sin embargo, al final, and por eso to move between layers smoothly.
  6. Revisa. Check your draft against the checklist below before submitting it as part of the performance task.

Lista de revisión:

  • My narrative includes all four layers: antecedentes, hechos, consecuencias, and reflexión.
  • I distinguished the pluperfect from the simple past correctly wherever "already happened before" applies.
  • My reported speech does not simply repeat a direct quotation with quotation marks.
  • My future or conditional comment expresses genuine probability or expectation, not a simple fact.
  • My imperfect subjunctive forms are built from the correct preterite stem, with the accent in the nosotros/nosotras form if used.
  • I did not introduce any grammar system beyond what Unidad II has activated.
  • My accents, capitalization, and punctuation are correct throughout.

Speaking Task

Práctica controlada

With a partner, take turns reading aloud the model narrative "El proyecto del parque," paying attention to the shift in verb forms as the story moves from background to events to consequences to reflection.

Tarea comunicativa: Cuenta tu historia

Working from your written narrative, tell your story aloud to a partner without reading it word for word. Your partner should listen for all four layers and ask at least two follow-up questions, such as ¿Qué había pasado antes? or ¿Cómo te sentiste al final?

Alternativa individual

If you are studying independently: record yourself telling your narrative aloud in under two minutes, then listen back and check whether a listener could clearly identify the background, the events, the consequences, and your reflection.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context

Extended, reflective storytelling — not just reporting facts, but connecting background, events, consequences, and feelings — is a valued skill in many Spanish-speaking communities, whether in a community newsletter, a family gathering, a classroom presentation, or a workplace update. Learning to organize a narrative this way in Spanish is not only a grammar exercise; it is a genuinely useful communicative skill across many settings and communities.

Regional Note (Recognition Only)

Some speakers, especially in more formal or literary registers, may use the imperfect subjunctive -se form (for example, se alegrara alongside se alegrase) in reflective writing like this. Both are grammatically correct, but this course continues to activate only the -ra pattern for production, consistent with the policy established in Chapter 9.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake

Error: Using the preterite for background information that should set the scene — Antes de la reunión, el terreno estuvo abandonado por años.

Correction: Ongoing background before the main events belongs in the imperfect: Antes de la reunión, el terreno llevaba años abandonado.

Common Mistake

Error: Using the simple past instead of the pluperfect for an event that happened before another past event — Rodrigo propuso la idea el año anterior, pero nadie la tomó en serio. (unclear sequencing)

Correction: When one past event happened clearly before another, mark the earlier one with the pluperfect: Rodrigo había propuesto la idea el año anterior, pero nadie la había tomado en serio.

Common Mistake

Error: Treating the future or conditional of probability as a fact rather than an expectation — El proyecto tardará un año. when describing what people thought would happen, rather than what actually happened.

Correction: Reserve the future or conditional for expectation and probability, and switch to the preterite for the actual outcome: Muchos pensaban que el proyecto tardaría un año, pero terminó antes de lo esperado.

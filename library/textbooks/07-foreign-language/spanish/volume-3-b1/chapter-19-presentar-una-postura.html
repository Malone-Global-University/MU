Presentar una postura

Evidencia, contraargumento y preguntas del público · Speak and Write Spanish: Independent Communication and Connected Discourse

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Organize a short oral presentation around a clear central position, supported by evidence and examples.
  2. Use discourse markers appropriate to spoken presentation, distinguishing them from purely written transitions.
  3. Introduce and attribute evidence or examples without relying on direct quotation.
  4. Anticipate a counterpoint and respond to it respectfully within a presentation.
  5. Understand and respond to follow-up questions from an audience, including requests for clarification.
  6. Adjust pacing, emphasis, and formality for a spoken audience rather than a reader.
  7. Deliver a short presentation and respond to at least one question from a listener.

This chapter consolidates the argument structure, textual reference, and cohesive writing skills developed in Chapters 16 through 18 and applies them to spoken delivery. No major new productive grammar system is introduced in this chapter; the focus is on organizing prepared speech, using evidence orally, and interacting with an audience using language already active in the course.

Communication Scenario

The neighborhood association was so pleased with Camila's newsletter article that they have invited her to present her findings at the next community meeting. This time, Camila cannot simply read her written article aloud: she must prepare a short spoken presentation, decide what to say if someone disagrees with her, and be ready to answer questions from neighbors afterward.

  1. Before reading, think about a time you had to explain your position to a group and someone pushed back. How did you respond?
  2. Notice how a written text and a spoken presentation on the same topic can differ in structure, pacing, and the way evidence is introduced.

Grammar: Evidencia, contraargumento y preguntas

Purpose: to develop spoken discourse markers, evidence attribution, and counterpoint language that extend the argument structure, reference chains, and cohesive transitions already active from Chapters 16 through 18 into the specific demands of oral delivery.

Development: La estructura de una presentación oral

A short oral presentation typically follows a pattern related to, but not identical with, the written paragraph structure from Chapter 18:

  • Apertura: a brief greeting and a statement of the postura, often more direct than a written introduction.
  • Desarrollo: two or three supporting points, each introduced clearly so a listener (who cannot re-read) can follow the structure.
  • Reconocimiento del contraargumento: a brief acknowledgment of an opposing view.
  • Cierre: a short summary and, often, an invitation for questions.

Example opening: Buenas tardes a todos. Hoy quiero hablarles sobre los cambios que ha tenido nuestro barrio en los últimos años, y en particular sobre el nuevo mercado y la reunión que organizó la asociación.

Development: Marcadores para el discurso oral

Some written transitions from Chapter 18 sound overly formal when spoken aloud in an informal community setting. This chapter develops a spoken-register alternative set, organized by function:

  • Introducir el primer punto: para empezar, lo primero que quiero mencionar es...
  • Añadir un punto: otro punto importante es..., además de esto
  • Presentar evidencia: según un estudio reciente, los datos muestran que..., un ejemplo claro de esto es...
  • Reconocer un contraargumento: es cierto que..., algunas personas podrían decir que..., sin embargo
  • Cerrar: para concluir, en fin

Both the written inventory from Chapter 18 and this spoken inventory remain available; the choice depends on medium and audience, not on one replacing the other.

Development: Presentar evidencia sin cita directa

Spoken presentations often attribute evidence to a source without quoting it word for word. The preposition según and the impersonal se, already active from earlier chapters, support this function: Según la asociación de vecinos, más familias usan el mercado nuevo cada mes. / Se ha observado un aumento en la participación comunitaria.

Do not present invented statistics or sources as real; when evidence is illustrative rather than factual, frame it clearly: Imaginemos, por ejemplo, el caso de una familia que...

Development: Reconocer y responder a un contraargumento

A well-prepared speaker acknowledges an opposing view before returning to their own postura, rather than ignoring it. This often combines a concessive clause (aunque, already receptive-to-developed since Chapter 3) with a return to the main argument:

Aunque algunos vecinos prefieren mantener el mercado como está, los datos sugieren que la mayoría apoya su expansión.

Partial agreement is common and often more persuasive than flat rejection: Coincido en que el cambio ha sido rápido, pero discrepo de la idea de que haya sido negativo para el barrio.

Development: Responder a preguntas del público

Audience questions often require a short clarification before a full answer. Useful patterns include:

  • Pedir que se repita: ¿Podría repetir la pregunta, por favor?
  • Confirmar que se entendió: Si entiendo bien, usted pregunta si...
  • Aclarar antes de responder: Para aclarar, me refiero específicamente a...
  • Responder cuando no se sabe algo: No tengo esa información en este momento, pero puedo averiguarlo.

Práctica controlada:

  1. Convierte esta oración escrita en una apertura oral más directa: "El propósito de este texto es explicar los cambios recientes en el barrio."
  2. Elige la expresión más lógica para reconocer un contraargumento: "_____ (Es cierto que / Según) el mercado nuevo ha traído más tráfico, pero también ha traído más actividad económica."
  3. Escribe una respuesta breve a esta pregunta de un oyente: "¿Puede explicar de nuevo por qué la reunión fue tan importante?"

Reading Passage: El guion de una presentación

Guion de Camila para la reunión del barrio (fragmento)

Buenas tardes a todos, y gracias por invitarme a hablar hoy. Para empezar, quiero compartir con ustedes algunos cambios que ha tenido nuestro barrio en los últimos años. Lo primero que quiero mencionar es la apertura del mercado nuevo, donde muchos vecinos ahora compran productos de otras regiones del país. Según la asociación, la participación en el mercado ha crecido cada mes desde que abrió. Otro punto importante es la reunión que organizamos hace unas semanas, en la que dos vecinos presentaron costumbres de sus lugares de origen. Es cierto que algunas personas prefieren que el barrio cambie más lentamente, y entiendo esa preocupación. Sin embargo, los datos que hemos recogido sugieren que la mayoría de los vecinos ve estos cambios de manera positiva. Para concluir, creo que el barrio se ha vuelto un lugar más conectado con el resto del país, y con gusto responderé a sus preguntas.

This script is an adapted textbook composition written for this chapter, not an authentic recorded transcript. It is a model script, not the only acceptable way to organize the presentation.

  3. Locate the sentence where Camila acknowledges a counterpoint before returning to her main argument.
  4. Identify the phrase Camila uses to attribute evidence without quoting a source directly.

Writing Workshop: El guion de apoyo

Before speaking, most presenters benefit from writing a short support script or set of notes. Using the revised article you wrote for Chapter 18 (or a new topic of your choice), prepare a written script for a two- to three-minute oral presentation.

  1. Adapta la apertura. Rewrite your written introduction as a more direct spoken opening appropriate for greeting a live audience.
  2. Selecciona de dos a tres puntos. Choose the two or three strongest supporting points from your written text; a spoken presentation cannot cover as much detail as a written article.
  3. Marca la evidencia. Identify where you will attribute evidence using según or impersonal se, rather than reading a long written quotation aloud.
  4. Prepara el contraargumento. Write one sentence acknowledging an opposing view and one sentence returning to your postura.
  5. Escribe el cierre. Prepare a short summary and an invitation for questions.
  6. Anticipa una pregunta. Write one question a listener might reasonably ask, and prepare a brief spoken response.

Lista de revisión:

  • My opening greets the audience and states my postura directly.
  • I selected a manageable number of supporting points for spoken delivery.
  • I used at least one spoken discourse marker from this chapter's inventory.
  • I attributed evidence without relying on a long direct quotation.
  • I acknowledged a counterpoint before returning to my own position.
  • I prepared for at least one audience question.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context

Community meetings, neighborhood associations, and local presentations exist in many Spanish-speaking communities, though their level of formality, expected length, and typical audience participation can vary by country, city, and institution. Acknowledging an opposing view during a presentation is generally seen as a sign of preparation and respect for the audience, rather than a sign of weakness in one's own position.

Regional Note (Recognition Only)

Norms for interrupting a speaker with questions, waiting until the end, or submitting written questions vary across communities, institutions, and event types rather than following a single "Spanish-speaking" pattern. This course does not require learners to master every institutional norm for audience participation; the goal is to deliver a clear, organized presentation and respond appropriately to a question using the systems active in this volume.

Register Note (Recognition Only)

A presenter's choice of usted or tú when addressing an audience can depend on the audience's age, the setting's formality, and regional convention. A community meeting for neighbors of all ages, like Camila's, often uses ustedes consistently rather than mixing address forms within the same talk.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake

Error: Reading a written article aloud word for word, using written transitions like asimismo or no obstante that sound stiff in a spoken community setting.

Correction: Adapt written transitions into spoken discourse markers: use además de esto or sin embargo in a more conversational rhythm, and shorten sentences for the ear rather than the eye.

Common Mistake

Error: Ignoring a counterpoint entirely, or dismissing it abruptly — Algunas personas no están de acuerdo, pero no importa.

Correction: Acknowledge the counterpoint respectfully before returning to your position: Entiendo que algunas personas prefieren un cambio más lento; sin embargo, los datos sugieren que...

Common Mistake

Error: Answering a question before confirming what was actually asked, leading to a response that does not match the listener's real question.

Correction: Confirm or restate the question briefly before answering: Si entiendo bien, usted pregunta si el mercado ha afectado el tráfico del barrio.

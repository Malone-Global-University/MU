Escribir con cohesión

Párrafos, transiciones, revisión, audiencia y estilo · Speak and Write Spanish: Independent Communication and Connected Discourse

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Organize a multi-paragraph text around a clear central idea, with each paragraph developing one supporting point.
  2. Use a broader range of cohesive transitions to connect sentences and paragraphs logically.
  3. Distinguish reference chains that maintain cohesion across a text from repetitive or unclear reference.
  4. Adjust vocabulary, sentence length, and tone to match a stated audience and purpose.
  5. Apply a structured revision process that separates content revision from surface editing.
  6. Recognize stylistic choices that make extended writing sound more natural and less mechanically translated.
  7. Produce a revised, cohesive multi-paragraph text and explain the changes made between drafts.

This chapter consolidates the argument structure, textual reference chains, and comparative discourse markers developed in Chapters 16 and 17 and applies them to the mechanics of building a cohesive, multi-paragraph text. No major new productive grammar system is introduced in this chapter; the focus is on organizing, connecting, and revising language already active in the course.

Communication Scenario

Camila's neighborhood association liked the comparative handout from the last meeting so much that they have asked her to turn it into a short article for the community newsletter. The newsletter has two different sections: one written for neighbors of all ages, and one written for a more formal "community reports" page read mostly by local officials. Camila must take her original notes, organize them into clear paragraphs, and adjust her writing so that each version fits its intended readers.

  1. Before reading, think about a time you had to rewrite something — an email, a message, a school assignment — for a different reader than the one you first had in mind. What did you change?
  2. Notice how a first draft and a revised draft can express the same ideas while sounding very different, depending on organization, transitions, and tone.

Grammar: Cohesión, transiciones y estilo

Purpose: to consolidate paragraph organization, expand the inventory of cohesive transitions, and connect reference chains and audience-based style choices already active from earlier chapters.

Consolidation: La estructura del párrafo

A well-organized Spanish paragraph typically follows a recognizable pattern:

  • Idea central: a sentence that states the paragraph's main point.
  • Oraciones de apoyo: two or more sentences that develop, illustrate, or explain the main idea.
  • Cierre o transición: a closing sentence, or a sentence that leads naturally into the next paragraph.

Example: Muchas familias del barrio han cambiado sus rutinas en los últimos años. Por ejemplo, varios vecinos ahora trabajan desde casa y organizan su día de otra manera. Además, algunos niños asisten a actividades después de la escuela que antes no existían. Estos cambios explican, en parte, por qué el barrio se siente distinto hoy en día.

Development: Un inventario ampliado de transiciones

This chapter expands the transitions already active from Chapters 3, 16, and 17 into a fuller working set, organized by function:

  • Añadir información: además, también, asimismo
  • Dar un ejemplo: por ejemplo, en particular
  • Mostrar contraste: sin embargo, no obstante, en cambio
  • Mostrar causa o consecuencia: por eso, por lo tanto, como resultado
  • Organizar una secuencia: en primer lugar, a continuación, por último
  • Concluir: en resumen, en conclusión

These transitions are already familiar in isolated sentences; this chapter consolidates their use across a full multi-paragraph text.

Development: Cadenas de referencia textual

A cohesive text avoids repeating the same noun in every sentence. Instead, it builds a reference chain using pronouns, demonstratives, and synonyms: el barrio → este lugar → allí. A reference chain must always remain clear: if a pronoun could refer to more than one earlier noun, replace it with the noun itself to avoid ambiguity.

Example chain: La asociación de vecinos organizó una reunión. En ella, dos personas presentaron costumbres de sus regiones de origen. La actividad resultó muy útil para el barrio.

Development: Audiencia, registro y estilo

The same content can be expressed differently depending on the reader:

  • Para vecinos en general: Muchas familias han cambiado su rutina en los últimos años.
  • Para un informe formal: Se ha observado un cambio significativo en las rutinas familiares durante los últimos años.

Adjusting for audience may involve address form (tú versus usted/ustedes), sentence length, vocabulary formality, and the use of impersonal se instead of a personal subject. Style should remain natural; a formal text is not simply a neutral text with longer words.

Práctica controlada:

  1. Reorganiza estas tres oraciones sueltas en un párrafo con idea central, apoyo y cierre: "El barrio tiene un mercado los sábados." / "Muchos vecinos se conocen allí." / "El mercado también vende productos de otras regiones."
  2. Elige la transición más lógica: El evento fue un éxito. _____ (Por eso / Sin embargo), la asociación planea repetirlo el próximo año.
  3. Reescribe esta oración informal en un registro más formal: "La gente cambió mucho sus costumbres."

Reading Passage: Del borrador al texto cohesionado

Primer borrador de Camila (fragmento)

El barrio ha cambiado. El barrio tiene un mercado nuevo. El mercado vende cosas de otras regiones. La gente compra allí. Hay una reunión de vecinos. En la reunión hablaron de las costumbres. Las costumbres son diferentes según la región. Fue interesante.

Versión revisada de Camila (fragmento)

En los últimos años, el barrio ha cambiado de manera notable. Uno de los cambios más visibles es la apertura de un mercado nuevo, donde varios vecinos ahora compran productos de otras regiones del país. Además, la asociación organizó recientemente una reunión en la que dos vecinos presentaron costumbres de sus lugares de origen. Como resultado, muchos asistentes descubrieron que una misma tradición puede practicarse de formas distintas según la región, sin que ninguna de ellas sea más correcta que otra. En resumen, estos cambios muestran un barrio cada vez más conectado con otras partes del país.

Both fragments are adapted textbook compositions written for this chapter, not authentic published sources. The revised version is a model, not the only acceptable way to improve the draft.

  1. Identify at least three short, choppy sentences from the first draft that were combined in the revised version.
  2. Find two transitions used in the revised version that do not appear in the first draft.
  3. Locate one example of a reference chain in the revised version (a noun replaced later by a pronoun, demonstrative, or synonym).
  4. Explain in your own words why the revised version reads more cohesively than the first draft.
  5. Identify one place where the revised version reflects a more formal or organized tone than the draft.

Writing Workshop: Revisión y reescritura

Return to the comparative paragraph you wrote for Chapter 17, or write a new short multi-paragraph text (two to three paragraphs) about a topic of your choice. You will complete a full revision cycle: a content revision pass and a separate surface-editing pass, followed by a rewritten final version.

  1. Revisa el contenido y la organización. Check that each paragraph has one clear idea central and that supporting sentences develop it. Combine short, choppy sentences where appropriate.
  2. Añade transiciones. Identify at least three places where a transition from this chapter's inventory would make the connection between ideas clearer.
  3. Revisa las cadenas de referencia. Replace at least two repeated nouns with a pronoun, demonstrative, or synonym, checking that the reference remains clear.
  4. Ajusta el registro según la audiencia. Decide whether your text is meant for a general reader or a more formal reader, and adjust address form, vocabulary, and sentence length accordingly.
  5. Corrige la superficie. Now, and only now, check spelling, accent marks, agreement, and punctuation.
  6. Reescribe la versión final. Produce a clean final version and be ready to explain at least three specific changes you made from your first draft.

Lista de revisión:

  • Each paragraph has one clear main idea and supporting sentences.
  • I used at least three transitions from this chapter's inventory.
  • My reference chains are clear; no pronoun could be misread as referring to the wrong noun.
  • My register (address form, vocabulary, sentence length) matches my intended reader.
  • I completed content revision before surface editing, not at the same time.
  • My accents, capitalization, agreement, and punctuation are correct throughout.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context

Expectations for written organization and formality can vary somewhat across Spanish-speaking institutions, publications, and communities, but a clearly organized paragraph with a stated main idea and logical transitions is broadly valued across academic, professional, and community writing in Spanish. Adjusting tone for a reader is not a sign of insincerity; it reflects an ordinary, expected awareness of audience.

Regional Note (Recognition Only)

Some institutions and publications favor a more elaborate, formal written style, while others favor a more direct, concise style, and these preferences can vary by country, field, and publication rather than by a single "Spanish-speaking" norm. This course does not require learners to master every institutional style; the goal is to produce a clearly organized, audience-appropriate text using the systems active in this volume.

Inclusive-Language Awareness (Recognition Only)

When revising a text for a broader audience, some writers replace a masculine plural noun with a more inclusive alternative, such as la comunidad instead of los vecinos, particularly in institutional or public-facing writing. This course does not require production of any specific inclusive-language system; recognizing that such choices exist supports more accurate reading and revision of contemporary Spanish-language texts.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake

Error: Writing a paragraph as a list of short, disconnected sentences without any transitions — El barrio cambió. Hay un mercado. La gente compra allí.

Correction: Combine related ideas and add a transition: El barrio ha cambiado, sobre todo con la apertura de un mercado nuevo donde muchos vecinos ahora compran.

Common Mistake

Error: Using a pronoun whose reference is unclear because two possible nouns came before it — La asociación invitó a la vecina y ella presentó el informe (¿quién presentó el informe?).

Correction: Repeat the noun when a pronoun would be ambiguous: La asociación invitó a la vecina, y la vecina presentó el informe.

Common Mistake

Error: Editing spelling and accents before revising organization, so that a well-edited sentence still sits in a poorly organized paragraph.

Correction: Revise content and organization first; correct spelling, accents, and agreement only in a separate, later pass.

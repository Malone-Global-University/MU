Duda, negación e incertidumbre

El subjuntivo después de la duda, la negación y los antecedentes indefinidos o inexistentes · Speak and Write Spanish: Independent Communication and Connected Discourse

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Use the present subjunctive in que clauses after expressions of doubt and uncertainty.
  2. Use the present subjunctive after expressions of denial, and contrast it with the indicative used after expressions of certainty.
  3. Recognize and produce the subjunctive in clauses that describe an indefinite, hypothetical, or nonexistent person or thing.
  4. Distinguish sentences that require the subjunctive from sentences that keep the indicative, based on whether the speaker treats something as certain, known, or specific.
  5. Combine subjunctive clauses of doubt, denial, and indefinite antecedent with the recommendation, desire, necessity, and emotion patterns from Chapter 11.
  6. Express uncertainty and describe things that may or may not exist in connected speech and writing.

Communication Scenario

Several weeks after the park recommendations, the community association is now planning a small volunteer program, but no one is sure exactly what it will look like yet. Camila has been asked to help write a notice describing what kind of volunteer the group is looking for — someone who may or may not exist among the neighbors — and to respond to questions from people who doubt whether the program will actually work. This chapter asks you to do something similar: express doubt, deny things you don't believe, and describe people, places, or things whose existence or exact identity is not yet certain.

  1. Think of a time you doubted whether something was true. How did you phrase that doubt in English?
  2. In English, notice the difference between I know a neighbor who has time to help (a specific, known person) and I need a neighbor who has time to help (anyone who fits the description, not yet identified). Spanish marks this difference with the subjunctive.

Expressions of certainty such as creer que and es cierto que normally take the indicative. Only when they are negated, and therefore express doubt instead of certainty, do they call for the subjunctive: Creo que va a funcionar versus No creo que vaya a funcionar.

Grammar: El subjuntivo con duda y negación

Purpose: to use the present subjunctive in que clauses that express doubt or denial, and to keep the indicative when the main clause expresses certainty.

Language Note: Duda versus certeza

You already know how to form the present subjunctive from Chapter 11. This chapter adds a new group of triggers: verbs and expressions of doubt and denial. When the main clause presents the information in the dependent clause as uncertain, doubtful, or false, the verb in the que clause is subjunctive.

  • Duda: Dudo que el programa empiece este mes.
  • Duda: No creo que haya suficientes voluntarios todavía.
  • Negación: El comité niega que el proyecto esté cancelado.

When the same expression presents the information as certain or true, Spanish uses the indicative instead: Creo que hay suficientes voluntarios (indicative, because the speaker presents this as fact) versus No creo que haya suficientes voluntarios (subjunctive, because the speaker doubts it).

Expressions of certainty — creer que, estar seguro/a de que, es cierto/verdad/obvio que — take the indicative in affirmative statements. Question form can sometimes shift the expectation toward doubt, but this course treats the affirmative-versus-negative contrast as the reliable rule for this level.

Ejemplos:

  • Es posible que el programa empiece en octubre. It's possible that the program will start in October.
  • No es verdad que nadie quiera participar. It's not true that no one wants to participate.
  • Estoy seguro de que la reunión es el jueves. I'm sure that the meeting is on Thursday. (indicative — certainty)

Práctica controlada:

  1. Completa con la forma correcta: Dudo que el comité _____ (tener) tiempo esta semana.
  2. Completa: No creo que _____ (haber) suficiente dinero para el proyecto.
  3. Completa: Estoy seguro de que el parque _____ (estar) listo para junio. (¿subjuntivo o indicativo?)
  4. Transforma en negativa y cambia el verbo si es necesario: Creo que el plan va a funcionar. → No creo que _____.

Grammar: Antecedentes indefinidos o inexistentes

Purpose: to use the subjunctive in a clause that describes a person or thing whose existence, identity, or availability is not certain, and to contrast this with the indicative used for a specific, known antecedent.

Language Note: Buscar algo que existe versus algo que tal vez no existe

When a relative clause with que describes a specific person or thing that the speaker knows exists, Spanish uses the indicative. When the clause describes a person or thing that is indefinite, hypothetical, or possibly nonexistent, Spanish uses the subjunctive.

  • Antecedente conocido (indicativo): Conozco a un vecino que tiene experiencia con jardines. (a specific person the speaker knows)
  • Antecedente indefinido (subjuntivo): Buscamos un vecino que tenga experiencia con jardines. (anyone who fits the description, not yet identified)
  • Antecedente inexistente (subjuntivo): No hay nadie que quiera organizar el evento. (no such person exists)

The personal a is normally omitted before an indefinite or nonexistent human antecedent, except with alguien and nadie, which always keep it: Buscamos un voluntario que... but Buscamos a alguien que....

Ejemplos:

  • Necesitamos a alguien que hable inglés y español. We need someone who speaks English and Spanish.
  • ¿Hay alguien aquí que sepa usar esta herramienta? Is there anyone here who knows how to use this tool?
  • Tengo un amigo que sabe usar esta herramienta. I have a friend who knows how to use this tool. (indicative — known person)

Práctica controlada:

  1. Completa: Buscamos un voluntario que _____ (poder) trabajar los sábados.
  2. Completa: Conozco a una persona que _____ (poder) ayudar el sábado. (¿subjuntivo o indicativo?)
  3. Completa: No hay nadie en la reunión que _____ (saber) la respuesta.
  4. Escribe una oración con antecedente indefinido usando buscar y el subjuntivo.

Model Dialogue

Camila talks with a neighbor, Don Rafael, who is skeptical about the new volunteer program.

Don Rafael:No creo que este programa funcione. Nadie tiene tiempo para eso.I don't think this program will work. No one has time for that.
Camila:Es posible que tenga razón, pero dudo que sea imposible. Buscamos personas que puedan ayudar solo dos horas al mes.It's possible you're right, but I doubt it's impossible. We're looking for people who can help just two hours a month.
Don Rafael:No niego que dos horas no es mucho, pero no conozco a nadie que quiera comprometerse, ni siquiera por poco tiempo.I don't deny that two hours isn't much, but I don't know anyone who wants to commit, even for a short time.
Camila:Pues yo sí conozco a alguien: mi vecina Marta ya dijo que sí. Y estoy segura de que hay más personas interesadas.Well, I do know someone: my neighbor Marta already said yes. And I'm sure there are more people interested.
Don Rafael:No es cierto que todos digan que no, entonces. Tal vez me equivoco.So it's not true that everyone says no. Maybe I'm wrong.
Camila:Todavía buscamos a alguien que organice el calendario. ¿Le interesaría a usted?We're still looking for someone who can organize the calendar. Would you be interested?

Listening Task

Ask your instructor, a partner, or a text-to-speech tool to read the following short exchange aloud, twice. As you listen, identify each subjunctive trigger and whether it expresses duda, negación, or an antecedente indefinido/inexistente.

"Dudo que todos los vecinos lean el aviso a tiempo. No es verdad que nadie lea las noticias del barrio, pero es posible que muchos no vean el mensaje. Por eso buscamos a alguien que pueda repartir volantes de puerta en puerta. No conozco a nadie que ya haya hecho esto antes, así que también necesitamos a alguien que explique cómo organizarlo."

Reading Task

Reread the model dialogue above, then complete the following analysis independently or with a partner.

  1. Find every subjunctive verb form in the dialogue and identify whether it follows a trigger of duda, negación, or antecedente indefinido/inexistente.
  2. Find one sentence where the indicative is used instead of the subjunctive, and explain why.
  3. Compare Camila's sentence about Marta with Don Rafael's sentence about not knowing anyone. Explain how each speaker signals whether the person is known or not yet identified.
  4. Explain why digan is subjunctive in No es cierto que todos digan que no.

Writing Workshop: Un aviso de voluntariado

Write a short notice (eight to ten sentences) recruiting volunteers for an imagined or real community project. Your notice must include doubt, denial, and at least one description of an indefinite or nonexistent antecedent.

  1. Duda. Write at least one sentence expressing doubt or uncertainty about some aspect of the project, using dudar que, no creer que, or es posible que.
  2. Negación. Write at least one sentence denying a common misconception about the project, using negar que or no es cierto/verdad que.
  3. Certeza. Write at least one sentence expressing certainty with the indicative, using estar seguro/a de que or es cierto/obvio que.
  4. Antecedente indefinido. Write at least one sentence describing the kind of volunteer the project needs, using the subjunctive after buscar or necesitar.
  5. Recomendación o deseo. Include at least one sentence recycling a subjunctive trigger from Chapter 11 (influencia, deseo, necesidad, or emoción).
  6. Revisa. Check your draft against the checklist below before submitting it.

Lista de revisión:

  • My notice includes doubt, denial, certainty, and an indefinite antecedent.
  • I used the subjunctive only when the main clause expresses doubt, denial, or an indefinite/nonexistent antecedent.
  • I used the indicative for expressions of certainty and for known, specific antecedents.
  • I used personal a correctly with indefinite antecedents (omitted, except before alguien and nadie).
  • I recycled at least one subjunctive trigger from Chapter 11 correctly.
  • I did not introduce any grammar system beyond what this chapter and prior chapters have activated.
  • My accents, capitalization, and punctuation are correct throughout.

Speaking Task

Práctica controlada

With a partner, take turns reading aloud the model dialogue, paying attention to which verbs are subjunctive and which stay indicative.

Tarea comunicativa: ¿Está de acuerdo?

Working from your written notice, present it aloud to a partner who will play a skeptical neighbor, like Don Rafael. Your partner should express at least two doubts or denials, and you should respond with your own doubt, denial, or certainty as appropriate.

Alternativa individual

If you are studying independently: record yourself presenting your notice aloud in under two minutes, then listen back and check whether you used the subjunctive correctly after each doubt, denial, or indefinite-antecedent trigger.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context

Expressing doubt or disagreement indirectly, rather than flatly contradicting someone, is common in many everyday interactions in Spanish-speaking communities, particularly among neighbors, colleagues, or people who are not close friends or family. Phrases such as es posible que or no estoy seguro/a de que allow a speaker to disagree while leaving room for the other person's viewpoint, in many but not all communities and settings.

Regional Note (Recognition Only)

Tal vez and quizá(s) both mean "maybe" and can be followed by either the subjunctive or the indicative depending on how certain the speaker feels, with some variation in tendency across regions and individual speakers. This course treats both as formulaic expressions of doubt paired with the subjunctive, without requiring learners to analyze the indicative option at this level.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake

Error: Using the subjunctive after an affirmative expression of certainty — Creo que el programa vaya a funcionar.

Correction: Affirmative certainty takes the indicative: Creo que el programa va a funcionar.

Common Mistake

Error: Using the indicative for an indefinite antecedent — Buscamos un voluntario que tiene experiencia.

Correction: An antecedent not yet identified takes the subjunctive: Buscamos un voluntario que tenga experiencia.

Common Mistake

Error: Omitting personal a before alguien or nadie — No conozco nadie que pueda ayudar.

Correction: Alguien and nadie always keep personal a when they refer to a person: No conozco a nadie que pueda ayudar.

Estoy segura de que hay más personas interesadas uses the indicative because estar segura de que expresses certainty, not doubt.   3. Looking Ahead

Lo que ya sabes

You can now use the present subjunctive after expressions of doubt and denial, keep the indicative after expressions of certainty, and describe indefinite or nonexistent people and things with the subjunctive in a descriptive clause. Combined with Chapter 11, you can now recommend, wish, require, react emotionally, doubt, deny, and describe the uncertain — a much fuller range of ways to talk about what is not yet settled. Camila's community can now not only ask for help, but also talk honestly about what it isn't sure of yet.

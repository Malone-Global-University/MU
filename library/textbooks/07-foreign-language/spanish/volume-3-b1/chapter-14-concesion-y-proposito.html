Concesión y propósito

Aunque con indicativo y subjuntivo, y las conjunciones para que, antes de que y sin que · Speak and Write Spanish: Independent Communication and Connected Discourse

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Use aunque with the indicative to concede a fact the speaker accepts as true.
  2. Use aunque with the subjunctive to concede a hypothetical, unknown, or unconfirmed circumstance.
  3. Distinguish the two uses of aunque by what they communicate about the speaker's certainty, not by translating both as "although."
  4. Use para que, antes de que, and sin que with the subjunctive when the clause has a different subject from the main verb.
  5. Use para, antes de, and sin with the infinitive when the subject does not change.
  6. Combine concession and purpose clauses with recommendation, doubt, indefinite-antecedent, and conditional patterns from Chapters 11 through 13 in connected discourse.

Communication Scenario

The volunteer program is moving forward, but not without obstacles. Camila wants to explain, in her next update to the neighborhood association, why the group is continuing despite setbacks, and what the group is arranging so that new volunteers can join without added confusion. English can express both of these ideas with "even though," "although," and "so that," but it does not require a change in verb mood to signal whether the obstacle is a settled fact or an anticipated one. Spanish does.

  1. Compare Although it is raining, we will hold the meeting outside with Although it may rain, we will hold the meeting outside. Which sentence treats the rain as a known fact? Which treats it as a possibility the speaker is not certain about?
  2. Think of one obstacle you know for certain exists in a plan of yours, and one obstacle that is only a possibility. Notice that English marks the difference with vocabulary choices like "may" or "might," while Spanish will mark it with the verb form itself.

Grammar: Concesión con aunque

Purpose: to concede an obstacle or contrasting circumstance while still affirming the main statement, and to signal through verb mood whether that obstacle is a known fact or only a possibility.

Language Note: Aunque + indicativo

When the speaker treats the circumstance introduced by aunque as a known, confirmed fact, the verb after aunque stays in the indicative. The speaker is conceding something they know to be true and stating that it does not change the main action.

  • Aunque llueve, la reunión será al aire libre. Although it is raining (a fact), the meeting will be outdoors.
  • Aunque Camila está ocupada esta semana, siempre responde los mensajes. Although Camila is busy this week (a fact), she always answers messages.
Language Note: Aunque + subjuntivo

When the speaker treats the circumstance as hypothetical, unknown, unconfirmed, or simply not yet known to be true, the verb after aunque shifts to the subjunctive. The speaker is conceding a possibility, not a fact.

  • Aunque llueva, la reunión será al aire libre. Even if it rains (we don't know yet), the meeting will be outdoors.
  • Aunque Camila esté ocupada esta semana, seguramente responde los mensajes. Even if Camila is busy this week (uncertain), she probably answers messages.

The two sentences in each pair use the same connector and the same vocabulary. Only the mood of the verb after aunque changes, and that single change shifts the sentence from a conceded fact to a conceded possibility. Do not rely on an English translation to decide the mood; decide instead whether the speaker in the sentence is presenting the circumstance as known or as uncertain.

Ejemplos:

  • Aunque el programa ya tiene diez voluntarios, el comité sigue buscando más apoyo. Although the program already has ten volunteers (a fact), the committee keeps looking for more support.
  • Aunque no consigamos el salón más grande este año, seguiremos con el espacio actual. Even if we don't get the bigger hall this year (uncertain), we will continue with the current space.
  • Aunque a algunos vecinos les preocupa el horario, la mayoría está de acuerdo con el plan. Although some neighbors are worried about the schedule (a fact), most agree with the plan.

  1. Completa con la forma correcta, según si el hablante presenta un hecho conocido o una posibilidad: Aunque nosotros _____ (tener, hecho) poco tiempo esta semana, terminaremos el informe.

Grammar: Propósito, tiempo previo y exclusión

Purpose: to express the purpose of an action, an action that happens before another, and an action that happens without a second person's involvement, using para que, antes de que, and sin que with the subjunctive, or the corresponding prepositions with the infinitive when there is no change of subject.

Language Note: Cambio de sujeto → subjuntivo; mismo sujeto → infinitivo

Para que, antes de que, and sin que take the subjunctive only when the subject of the dependent clause is different from the subject of the main clause. When both actions belong to the same subject, Spanish drops que and uses the preposition alone (para, antes de, sin) with an infinitive.

Conjunción con subjuntivo (dos sujetos)Preposición con infinitivo (un sujeto)
Camila llama a los vecinos para que ellos se inscriban.Camila llama a los vecinos para inscribirlos.
Terminemos la reunión antes de que llueva.Terminemos la reunión antes de salir.
Organizó el calendario sin que nadie se lo pidiera.Organizó el calendario sin pedir ayuda.

Check the subject of each clause before choosing a form. If the person doing the first action is not the same person affected by or doing the second action, the dependent clause needs que and the subjunctive.

Language Note: Para que, antes de que, sin que
  • Para que + subjuntivo expresses the purpose of an action, when that purpose belongs to someone other than the subject: Camila envía un mensaje para que todos sepan la hora de la reunión.
  • Antes de que + subjuntivo expresses that one action happens earlier than another, when the two actions have different subjects: Queremos confirmar el local antes de que el ayuntamiento cierre la oficina.
  • Sin que + subjuntivo expresses that an action happens without a second person's knowledge, help, or involvement: El grupo resolvió el problema sin que nadie tuviera que cancelar sus planes.

Ejemplos:

  • Camila organiza los turnos para que nadie tenga que trabajar dos sábados seguidos. Camila organizes the shifts so that nobody has to work two Saturdays in a row.
  • Vamos a confirmar los detalles antes de que empiece el fin de semana. We're going to confirm the details before the weekend starts.
  • Don Rafael arregló las sillas sin que se lo pidiéramos. Don Rafael arranged the chairs without our asking him to.

Práctica controlada:

  1. Completa: Camila manda un recordatorio para que los voluntarios no _____ (olvidar) la reunión.
  2. Completa: Queremos terminar antes de que _____ (oscurecer).
  3. Completa: El comité resolvió el problema sin que nadie _____ (enterarse).
  4. Transforma según el sujeto: Camila llama a Don Rafael para que él confirme el local. → (mismo sujeto) Camila llama a Don Rafael para _____ el local ella misma.

Model Dialogue

Camila and Don Rafael discuss last-minute obstacles and arrangements before the next community meeting.

Don Rafael:Aunque el pronóstico dice que va a llover, ¿seguimos con la reunión al aire libre?Even though the forecast says it's going to rain, are we still holding the meeting outdoors?
Camila:Sí. Aunque llueva, tenemos el salón como alternativa. Y aunque el salón es pequeño, cabemos todos si nos organizamos bien.Yes. Even if it rains, we have the hall as a backup. And even though the hall is small, we all fit if we organize well.
Don Rafael:Voy a llamar a los vecinos esta tarde para que todos sepan del cambio de plan, por si acaso.I'll call the neighbors this afternoon so everyone knows about the change of plan, just in case.
Camila:Perfecto. Y hay que confirmar las sillas antes de que cierre la oficina del ayuntamiento.Perfect. And we need to confirm the chairs before the city office closes.
Don Rafael:Ya me encargué de eso sin que me lo pidieras. Llamé esta mañana antes de salir de casa.I already took care of that without your asking me. I called this morning before leaving the house.
Camila:Gracias, Don Rafael. Aunque a veces surgen problemas, siempre encontramos la manera de resolverlos.Thank you, Don Rafael. Although problems come up sometimes, we always find a way to solve them.

Writing Workshop: Obstáculos y arreglos

Write a short planning note (eight to ten sentences) about a project, event, or personal goal of your choice. Your note must include concession and purpose clauses as specified below.

  1. Aunque + indicativo. Write at least one sentence conceding a fact you know to be true.
  2. Aunque + subjuntivo. Write at least one sentence conceding an uncertain or hypothetical possibility.
  3. Para que + subjuntivo. Write at least one sentence expressing a purpose that belongs to someone other than the subject.
  4. Antes de que o sin que + subjuntivo. Write at least one sentence using either connector correctly with a change of subject.
  5. Repaso acumulativo. Include at least one sentence recycling a real or hypothetical si clause from Chapter 13.
  6. Revisa. Check your draft against the checklist below before submitting it.

Lista de revisión:

  • I used the indicative after aunque only for facts I present as known.
  • I used the subjunctive after aunque only for uncertain or hypothetical circumstances.
  • I checked the subject of each clause before choosing between que + subjunctive and the preposition + infinitive.
  • I used para que, antes de que, or sin que correctly with a change of subject.
  • I recycled at least one si clause from Chapter 13 correctly.
  • My accents, capitalization, and punctuation are correct throughout.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context

Community projects in many Spanish-speaking contexts are often described with built-in contingency language: plans continue "aunque" something goes wrong, and arrangements are made "para que" the plan does not depend on any single person. This habit of naming obstacles directly, while still affirming that the group will proceed, is common in neighborhood associations, school committees, and small civic projects in many communities, and it reflects a practical, solution-focused communication style rather than a single national custom.

Regional Note (Recognition Only)

A pesar de que functions similarly to aunque and follows the same fact-versus-possibility distinction with the indicative and the subjunctive. It appears across the Spanish-speaking world in both speech and writing and is presented here for recognition; this course continues to use aunque as the default productive connector for concession.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake

Error: Using the subjunctive after aunque for a fact the speaker already knows — Aunque llueva ahora mismo, seguimos con la reunión. (when it is, in fact, already raining)

Correction: A known fact takes the indicative: Aunque llueve ahora mismo, seguimos con la reunión.

Common Mistake

Error: Using que and the subjunctive when both clauses share the same subject — Quiero terminar antes de que yo salga.

Correction: With a single subject, use the preposition and the infinitive: Quiero terminar antes de salir.

Common Mistake

Error: Using the indicative after para que — Camila llama a los vecinos para que ellos se inscriben.

Correction: Para que always requires the subjunctive when the subject changes: Camila llama a los vecinos para que ellos se inscriban.

Looking Ahead

Lo que ya sabes

You can now concede a known fact or an uncertain possibility with aunque, and you can express purpose, prior time, and exclusion with para que, antes de que, and sin que, choosing correctly between the subjunctive and the infinitive based on the subject of each clause. Combined with Chapters 11 through 13, you can recommend, wish, doubt, deny, describe the uncertain, plan for different possible futures, and now acknowledge obstacles while still explaining why an action happens. Camila's committee can now describe its plans honestly, obstacles included, without losing its sense of direction.

Capítulo 15: Proyecto 3 — Análisis público

Chapter function: Integrated assessment. Analyze a piece of public, policy, or service information and present recommendations, consolidating the systems developed across Chapters 11–14 without introducing new productive grammar.

1. Chapter Overview

This chapter is a project chapter, not a grammar chapter. Over the last four chapters you have built a set of tools for describing public and professional situations with precision: complex relative clauses (Chapter 11), the perfect and pluperfect systems (Chapter 12), the imperfect subjunctive and conditions (Chapter 13), and the passive voice alongside impersonal and passive se (Chapter 14). In this project, you will apply all four systems together. You will read and interpret a piece of public-facing information — a policy summary, a service notice, or a short media item — and produce a structured spoken and written analysis with recommendations. No new grammar is introduced here. The goal is integration: choosing the right structure from what you already control, not learning something new.

2. Learning Objectives

  • Interpret a public-information text, identifying its main claims, the entities involved, and any information left deliberately vague or agentless.
  • Use complex relative clauses (el cual, quien, cuyo, preposition + relative) to refer precisely back to people, institutions, and ideas from the source text.
  • Use the present perfect and pluperfect to establish a timeline of what has happened and what had already happened before a given point.
  • Use the imperfect subjunctive and conditional structures to propose recommendations and hypothetical alternatives.
  • Use the true passive, passive se, and impersonal se to describe actions and general practices without unnecessarily naming an agent.
  • Produce a structured oral presentation and a written analysis that integrate all four systems with register control.
  • Respond to follow-up questions about your analysis in real time.

3. Opening Communication Scenario

Mariana's consulting team has been asked to prepare a short public-facing analysis of a new municipal recycling program for a community newsletter. The client wants more than a summary: they want an assessment of what the announcement does well, what it leaves unclear, and what the city could do differently. This is exactly the kind of task professionals in policy, journalism, education, and public communication complete regularly — reading an official or media text critically and turning that reading into a clear, well-organized response.

Cliente: Necesitamos algo breve pero serio. No solo un resumen — quiero saber qué piensan ustedes.

Mariana: Entendido. Vamos a identificar lo que ya se ha decidido, lo que todavía no se ha aclarado, y lo que nosotros recomendaríamos si tuviéramos que ajustar el plan.

Cliente: Perfecto. Y si pueden, preséntenlo primero de manera oral en la próxima reunión, antes de mandarnos la versión escrita.

4. Source Text for Analysis

The following is an adapted instructional text written for this course, not an authentic published document.

Se comunica a los residentes que, a partir del próximo trimestre, será implementado un nuevo sistema de reciclaje en todo el municipio. El proyecto, cuyo objetivo principal es reducir la cantidad de residuos que se envían al vertedero municipal, fue aprobado por el concejo el mes pasado, después de que varios grupos comunitarios habían presentado propuestas similares durante más de un año. Se instalarán contenedores separados para papel, vidrio y plástico en cada barrio, y se espera que el nuevo sistema esté funcionando plenamente antes de fin de año. Sin embargo, el comunicado no especifica quién cubrirá el costo de los nuevos contenedores ni qué pasará en los barrios donde ya existían programas de reciclaje independientes. Tampoco se aclara si habrá sanciones para quienes no separen correctamente los residuos, un punto sobre el cual varios vecinos, con quienes se habló durante una reunión informal, expresaron preocupación.

6. Recycled Grammar and Sentence Patterns

No new productive grammar system is introduced in this chapter. The following is a consolidated reference to the four systems you will integrate, each linked back to the chapter where it was activated.

6.1 Complex relative clauses (Chapter 11)

El proyecto, cuyo objetivo principal es reducir los residuos, fue aprobado por el concejo.
Los vecinos, con quienes se habló durante una reunión informal, expresaron preocupación.
Un punto sobre el cual varios vecinos expresaron preocupación.

6.2 Perfect and pluperfect systems (Chapter 12)

Varios grupos comunitarios habían presentado propuestas similares durante más de un año.
El comunicado no ha especificado quién cubrirá el costo.

6.3 Imperfect subjunctive and conditions (Chapter 13)

Si el municipio quisiera evitar confusión, aclararía quién cubrirá el costo de los contenedores.
Recomendaríamos que el comunicado especificara un plazo claro para cada barrio.

6.4 Passive voice and impersonal/passive se (Chapter 14)

El proyecto fue aprobado por el concejo el mes pasado.
Se instalarán contenedores separados en cada barrio.
Se comunica a los residentes que...
No se aclara si habrá sanciones.

Integration NoteA strong analysis does not use every structure in every sentence. Choose the passive or impersonal se when the agent is unknown or unimportant, the perfect systems when you are establishing a timeline, the relative clauses when you need to refer back precisely to something already mentioned, and the subjunctive/conditional when you move from description to recommendation.

7. Project Stages

Complete the project in four stages. Each stage builds directly on the one before it.

Stage 1 — Analytical outline

Before drafting full sentences, outline your analysis in four parts: (1) what has already been decided or approved; (2) what remains unclear or unspecified; (3) what concerns have been raised, and by whom; (4) two or three concrete recommendations. Write each part as a short list of ideas in Spanish, not yet as connected prose.

Stage 2 — Spoken presentation

Using your outline, prepare a two- to three-minute spoken presentation as if speaking to the client in the opening scenario. Structure it in the same four parts as your outline. Practice it aloud at least twice before your recorded or partner-observed delivery.

Stage 3 — Written draft

Write a structured written analysis (180–220 words) covering the same four parts. Use at least one complex relative clause, one present perfect or pluperfect verb form, one imperfect subjunctive or conditional sentence expressing a recommendation, and one passive or impersonal/passive se construction.

Stage 4 — Revision and oral defense

Revise your written draft using the editing checklist in Section 12. Then answer three follow-up questions from a partner, instructor, or your own self-check list (Section 9), responding in complete, well-formed Spanish sentences without reading directly from your script.

12. Writing Workshop

Task: Complete Stage 3 and Stage 4 from Section 7. Your final written analysis should be 180–220 words, organized in four clearly identifiable parts, and should integrate all four grammar systems reviewed in Section 6 at least once each.

Editing checklist

  • At least one complex relative clause, correctly formed
  • At least one present perfect or pluperfect verb form, correctly formed
  • At least one imperfect subjunctive or conditional sentence expressing a recommendation
  • At least one true passive or impersonal/passive se construction, with correct agreement
  • Participle agreement in every true passive sentence
  • Accent marks, including on -ído participles and pronoun-attachment forms
  • Consistent formal register throughout
  • Four-part organization clearly visible to the reader

13. Culture and Communication

Public announcements about municipal or community programs commonly leave some operational details unresolved at the time of first publication, particularly around funding, enforcement, and timelines, and follow-up communication often fills these gaps as a program moves forward. Recognizing this pattern — rather than assuming an omission is always an oversight — helps a reader or analyst ask more precise, useful questions instead of simply criticizing a text for what it does not yet say. This pattern is common across many public-information contexts, though the pace and format of follow-up communication vary by institution, country, and local practice.

14. Common Mistakes

  • Using the present perfect where a simple preterite would be more natural for a fully completed past event: prefer el concejo aprobó el proyecto el mes pasado over an unnecessary perfect form, reserving the perfect systems for relevance-to-now or anteriority, as established in Chapter 12.
  • Overusing the passive voice in every sentence, which produces a stiff, unnatural register; mix true passive, passive/impersonal se, and active sentences as Chapter 14 recommends.
  • Writing a recommendation in the indicative instead of the subjunctive or conditional: Recomendamos que el municipio especifica el costo is incorrect; the correct form is Recomendamos que el municipio especifique el costo.
  • Using a relative pronoun that does not match its antecedent in gender or number, such as la propuesta, cuyos objetivo...; the correct form is la propuesta, cuyo objetivo...

This project chapter integrated four systems developed across Unit III — complex relative clauses, the perfect and pluperfect systems, the imperfect subjunctive and conditional, and the passive voice with impersonal and passive se — through a realistic public-analysis task.

Capítulo 17: Mundo laboral y liderazgo

Chapter function: Negotiation, feedback, conflict resolution, and professional diplomacy, extending the critical and analytical stance-taking language of Chapter 16 into the workplace and introducing a focused vocabulary of leadership and diplomacy.

1. Chapter Overview

Chapter 16 gave you tools for discussing viewpoint and bias in Spanish-language media: hedged evaluation, textual reference devices, and a diagnostic for editing ser, estar, and hay. This chapter moves those same critical and diplomatic instincts into the workplace. You will learn vocabulary for negotiating, giving and receiving feedback, and resolving disagreement without damaging a professional relationship. You will also develop the textual reference devices from Chapter 16 further, applying them to written feedback and meeting follow-up, and you will extend the ser/estar/hay editing diagnostic to professional writing. No new grammar paradigm is introduced here; instead, you will use the subjunctive, conditional, and passive/impersonal structures you already control with sharper attention to diplomatic register — softening a disagreement, framing a request as a suggestion, and distancing a criticism from any one person.

2. Learning Objectives

  • Use vocabulary of negotiation, feedback, and conflict resolution appropriately in a professional context.
  • Distinguish direct and diplomatic phrasing for the same communicative function, and choose the appropriate one for a given relationship.
  • Use the conditional and subjunctive to soften requests, suggestions, and disagreement.
  • Extend textual reference devices (esto, eso, lo anterior, dicho lo cual) to written feedback and meeting follow-up messages.
  • Apply the ser/estar/hay editing diagnostic from Chapter 16 to professional writing.
  • Use impersonal and passive se to distance a criticism from a specific person when diplomacy requires it.
  • Participate in a negotiation or feedback role-play, using at least one diplomatic softening strategy and one repair strategy.

3. Opening Communication Scenario

Diego supervises a small team at a logistics company in Bogotá. A project deadline slipped, and he needs to give feedback to a team member without damaging the working relationship, while also negotiating a revised timeline with a client.

Diego: Quiero hablar contigo sobre el retraso en el proyecto, pero antes que nada, quiero que sepas que valoro mucho tu trabajo en general.

Camila: Te lo agradezco. Sé que no cumplimos con la fecha y entiendo que eso afecta al equipo.

Diego: Me gustaría que la próxima vez me avisaras antes si ves que un plazo no va a cumplirse. Así podemos ajustar juntos, en vez de reaccionar después.

5. Grammar and Sentence Patterns

5.1 Softening a request or disagreement with the conditional and subjunctive

Diplomatic register in Spanish relies heavily on structures you already control: the conditional to soften a request or opinion, and the subjunctive to present a suggestion as one option among several rather than a demand.

Me gustaría que me avisaras con más anticipación. (conditional + subjunctive — soft request)
Sugeriría que replanteáramos el plazo. (conditional + imperfect subjunctive — soft suggestion)
Entendería que prefirieras otra solución. (conditional — acknowledging the other party's possible position)
Preferiría no tomar una decisión hasta escuchar a todo el equipo. (conditional — diplomatic delay)

Language NoteCompare Necesito que cambies el plazo (direct, indicative-triggering subjunctive, appropriate between close colleagues or in urgent situations) with Me gustaría que consideraras cambiar el plazo (diplomatic, appropriate when social distance or hierarchy calls for softening). Neither is more “correct” Spanish; they serve different relationships.

5.2 Distancing a criticism with impersonal and passive se

The impersonal and passive se, fully active since Chapter 14, is a standard diplomatic tool: it allows a speaker to name a problem without naming who caused it.

Se cometieron algunos errores en la planificación. (impersonal — no one is named)
Se detectó un problema de comunicación entre los equipos. (impersonal — distances blame)
El plazo no se cumplió por razones que conviene revisar en conjunto. (passive-like framing — invites shared responsibility)

Compare this with a direct, person-naming alternative: Tú cometiste varios errores en la planificación. The impersonal version is not evasive by default — it is a recognized diplomatic register choice, appropriate when the goal is to solve a problem rather than assign individual blame.

5.3 Extending textual reference to feedback and meeting follow-up

The textual reference devices introduced in Chapter 16 — esto, eso, lo anterior, dicho lo cual — are equally useful in professional feedback and written meeting summaries, where a writer often needs to acknowledge a point before moving past it diplomatically.

El informe llegó dos días tarde. Esto generó cierta presión sobre el resto del equipo.
Reconozco que el enfoque inicial tenía sentido. Dicho lo cual, creo que conviene replantearlo.
Varios miembros del equipo expresaron dudas sobre el nuevo cronograma. Lo anterior sugiere que necesitamos una reunión adicional.

5.4 Editing diagnostic, extended: ser, estar, and hay in professional feedback

The three-question diagnostic from Chapter 16 works the same way in workplace writing, with one addition: professional feedback often requires distinguishing a lasting evaluation of performance (ser) from a description of a specific, correctable situation (estar).

QuestionIf yes, useExample
Am I evaluating the work or the person in general terms?serCamila es una colaboradora muy responsable.
Am I describing a specific, current, correctable situation?estarEl proyecto está retrasado, pero es recuperable.
Am I introducing an issue or item for the first time?hayHay un problema de comunicación que conviene resolver.

Notice how El proyecto está retrasado, pero es recuperable uses both verbs deliberately: está retrasado names the current, correctable state, while es recuperable makes a general evaluative claim. Collapsing both into one verb would lose this diplomatic nuance.

5.5 Consolidated review: hedged evaluation, relative clauses, perfect systems

These systems, fully productive since earlier chapters, continue to appear in workplace negotiation and feedback.

Es posible que el cronograma original no haya sido realista.
El cliente, cuya paciencia ya se había puesto a prueba, aceptó la nueva fecha.
Dudo que un solo ajuste resuelva todo el problema, pero es un buen punto de partida.

6. Model Dialogue

Diego: Quería comentarte algo sobre el informe de la semana pasada. ¿Tienes un momento?

Camila: Claro, dime.

Diego: El informe llegó dos días tarde. Esto puso algo de presión sobre el resto del equipo. No es la primera vez, así que me gustaría entender qué pasó.

Camila: Tienes razón. Se subestimó el tiempo que iba a tomar la revisión final, y no avisé a tiempo. Debí haberlo hecho.

Diego: Te lo agradezco. Dicho lo cual, no busco culpar a nadie: creo que conviene replantear el proceso de revisión para todo el equipo, no solo para ti.

Camila: Me parece justo. ¿Podríamos fijar un punto medio: revisiones más cortas, pero más frecuentes?

Diego: Sería un buen punto de partida. Hablemos con el resto del equipo antes de decidir nada definitivo.

11. Writing Workshop

Task: Write a short professional feedback message or negotiation follow-up email (120–150 words) responding to a workplace scenario of your choice. Use at least one diplomatic softening strategy, one instance of impersonal or passive se, one textual reference device, and apply the ser/estar/hay diagnostic from Section 5.4 while editing your draft.

Editing checklist

  • At least one request or opinion softened with the conditional or subjunctive
  • At least one use of impersonal or passive se to distance a criticism appropriately
  • At least one demonstrative or fixed phrase pointing back to a full idea
  • Ser, estar, and hay checked against the diagnostic in Section 5.4
  • Consistent formal, diplomatic register throughout
  • Accent marks, including on demonstratives and pronoun-attachment forms where relevant

12. Culture and Communication

Directness in professional feedback and negotiation varies considerably across Spanish-speaking workplaces, institutions, and generations, and no single approach should be treated as the norm for all of them. In many professional contexts, criticism is softened, framed impersonally, or preceded by acknowledgment of the other person's effort before a problem is raised directly; in others, more direct feedback is expected and not considered impolite. Recognizing that these norms differ by country, industry, company culture, and individual relationship — rather than assuming one universal “Latin American” or “Spanish-speaking” style — supports more effective and respectful professional communication.

13. Common Mistakes

  • Using the direct indicative present where the conditional or subjunctive would soften a request: Quiero que lo cambies may sound abrupt in a hierarchical or formal relationship; Me gustaría que lo consideraras is often more appropriate.
  • Overusing impersonal se to the point of sounding evasive about a problem that genuinely requires naming a responsible party; diplomacy is not the same as avoiding accountability entirely.
  • Applying the extended ser/estar diagnostic mechanically without considering whether the sentence intends a lasting evaluation or a specific, correctable state — the two can appear in the same sentence with different meanings, as in está retrasado, pero es recuperable.
  • Confusing retroalimentación (formal register) with the colloquial feedback; the latter is common in speech in many workplaces but is not the neutral written default in this course.

14. Chapter Summary

This chapter introduced vocabulary and diplomatic strategies for negotiation, feedback, and conflict resolution in the Spanish-speaking workplace: softening requests and disagreement with the conditional and subjunctive, distancing criticism appropriately with impersonal and passive se, and extending the textual reference devices and ser/estar/hay editing diagnostic from Chapter 16 into professional writing. No new grammar paradigm was introduced; the focus was precise, diplomatic use of systems already under your control.

Relativas complejas

Cláusulas relativas con preposición, el cual, quien, y cuyo · Speak and Write Spanish: Advanced Public, Professional, and Academic Spanish

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Recognize and interpret complex relative clauses introduced by a preposition plus el/la cual, los/las cuales, or quien(es).
  2. Distinguish when que alone is sufficient and when a preposition requires el cual or quien instead.
  3. Recognize and begin to produce possessive relative clauses with cuyo/a/os/as, correctly agreeing the form with the noun it modifies rather than with the possessor.
  4. Combine two related sentences into one complex sentence using an appropriate relative structure.
  5. Read and interpret professional and academic texts that rely on complex relative clauses for cohesion.
  6. Edit written work for correct relative-pronoun choice, agreement, and punctuation.

This chapter opens Unit III. It builds on the formal register, professional vocabulary, and clause-combining skills consolidated in Chapters 1 through 10, and it introduces complex relative structures as a controlled productive system for the first time in this volume.

Communication Scenario

Andrés works for a public-information office that publishes short explanatory articles about local services. His editor has asked him to revise a set of choppy, repetitive sentences into smoother, more professional prose by combining related ideas with relative clauses — the same kind of sentence-combining that appears constantly in reports, policy summaries, and academic writing.

  1. Think of a time you had to explain something complicated by connecting several short facts into one clear sentence, in any language. What made the longer sentence easier or harder to follow than the short ones?
  2. As you work through this chapter, pay attention to how a single relative pronoun can replace an entire repeated noun phrase, making writing more economical and more sophisticated.

Grammar: Relativas con preposición, el cual, quien, cuyo

Language Note

Purpose: to move from simple que-clauses, already consolidated, toward the more formal relative structures required after prepositions, in restrictive versus non-restrictive clauses, and in possessive reference.

1. Review: que as the default relative pronoun. In everyday and formal Spanish alike, que introduces most relative clauses when there is no preposition immediately before it.

  • El informe que escribiste ayer fue muy claro. The report that you wrote yesterday was very clear.

2. Preposition + el cual / quien. When a relative clause requires a preposition, formal and written Spanish generally avoids que directly after the preposition. Instead, use el/la cual (any antecedent) or quien(es) (people only), agreeing in gender and number with the antecedent.

  • El organismo con el cual nos comunicamos respondió en dos días. The agency we communicated with responded within two days.
  • La vecina de quien te hablé trabaja en el ayuntamiento. The neighbor I told you about works at city hall.
  • El edificio detrás del cual está el estacionamiento es la oficina principal. The building behind which the parking lot is located is the main office.

With short, high-frequency prepositions (con, de, en) and a personal antecedent, everyday spoken Spanish often prefers quien; with longer or compound prepositions (detrás de, a través de, para el cual), el cual is more common regardless of register.

3. Restrictive versus non-restrictive clauses. A restrictive clause identifies which exact item is meant and carries no commas. A non-restrictive clause adds extra information about an already-identified antecedent and is set off with commas. El cual appears especially often in non-restrictive clauses.

  • Los trámites que se completan en línea son más rápidos. Restrictive: specifies which procedures — the ones completed online.
  • El nuevo sistema, el cual entró en vigor este año, ha reducido los tiempos de espera. Non-restrictive: adds extra information about a system already identified.

4. Cuyo/a/os/as — possessive relative. Cuyo means “whose” and agrees in gender and number with the noun that follows it — the thing possessed — never with the possessor.

  • El vecino cuya solicitud se perdió tuvo que presentarla de nuevo. The neighbor whose application got lost had to submit it again. (cuya agrees with solicitud, feminine, not with el vecino, masculine)
  • La empresa cuyos empleados trabajan de forma remota redujo sus costos de oficina. The company whose employees work remotely reduced its office costs.

Cuyo is a formal, mainly written structure. Spoken Spanish frequently avoids it altogether in favor of a restructured sentence with de or que.

Regional Note (Recognition Only)

In everyday conversation across most of the Spanish-speaking world, many speakers avoid el cual and cuyo in casual talk, preferring simpler restructured sentences. Both forms remain fully standard and expected in professional, academic, and formal written Spanish, which is this chapter's focus.

Model Dialogue

Andrés and his editor, Marisol, review a draft article about a new public registration system.

Marisol: Este párrafo tiene tres oraciones cortas seguidas. ¿Puedes combinarlas usando una relativa? This paragraph has three short sentences in a row. Can you combine them using a relative clause?
Andrés: Claro. El organismo con el cual coordinamos el proyecto es la Oficina de Trámites Digitales. Of course. The agency we're coordinating the project with is the Digital Procedures Office.
Marisol: Mejor. ¿Y la vecina cuya queja empezó todo esto? ¿La mencionas? Better. And the resident whose complaint started all this? Do you mention her?
Andrés: Sí, escribí: "la vecina cuya solicitud tardó seis meses en resolverse." Yes, I wrote: "the resident whose application took six months to resolve."
Marisol: Perfecto. Y el nuevo sistema, el cual reduce ese tiempo a dos semanas, merece un párrafo aparte. Perfect. And the new system, which reduces that time to two weeks, deserves its own paragraph.
Andrés: De acuerdo. Lo reescribo y te lo mando en una hora. Agreed. I'll rewrite it and send it to you in an hour.

Guided Practice

  1. Combine each pair of sentences into one, choosing el cual, quien, or cuyo as appropriate: El comité tomó la decisión. Trabajamos para el comité.
  2. Combine: La directora presentó el informe. La oficina de la directora está en el tercer piso.
  3. Decide whether each sentence needs commas (non-restrictive) or not (restrictive), then punctuate it correctly: El formulario que se envía en línea llega más rápido a la oficina.
  4. Rewrite the following casual sentence in a more formal register using el cual: El edificio que está al lado del banco es la nueva oficina.
  5. Identify the antecedent that cuyo must agree with in: el organismo cuyas normas cambiaron este año.

Listening Practice

Listening Script for Instructor, Partner, or Text-to-Speech Use.

Anuncio público "Se informa a los residentes cuyas solicitudes fueron presentadas antes del quince de marzo que el nuevo sistema, el cual entrará en funcionamiento la próxima semana, procesará automáticamente los trámites pendientes. Los vecinos con quienes ya nos hemos comunicado no necesitan realizar ninguna acción adicional."
  1. Who does the announcement address directly, and what condition applies to them?
  2. What will the new system do automatically?
  3. Which residents do not need to take further action?
  4. Identify the two complex relative structures used in the announcement and name which type each one is.

Speaking Practice

Práctica controlada

Take turns describing a public office, school, or workplace you know, combining at least two short facts about it into one sentence using el cual, quien, or cuyo.

Interacción guiada

With a partner, describe a colleague, neighbor, or classmate using at least one sentence with cuyo (for example, describing something that belongs to or is characteristic of that person). Your partner should ask a follow-up question about the person you described.

Alternativa individual

Record yourself describing a public procedure you have completed (renewing a document, requesting a service), combining short facts into two or three complex sentences using this chapter's relative structures.

Reading Practice

Read the following adapted public-information text and complete the tasks that follow.

Nuevo sistema de trámites digitales

El ayuntamiento anunció esta semana el lanzamiento de un sistema en línea a través del cual los residentes podrán presentar, dar seguimiento y recibir respuesta a sus solicitudes sin necesidad de acudir en persona a las oficinas municipales. El proyecto, cuyo desarrollo tomó casi dos años, responde directamente a las quejas de numerosos vecinos cuyas solicitudes anteriores tardaban meses en resolverse.

La directora del proyecto, con quien se reunió este periódico la semana pasada, explicó que el sistema notificará automáticamente a cada solicitante sobre el estado de su trámite. El organismo del cual depende el nuevo sistema continuará atendiendo también a las personas que prefieran presentar sus solicitudes en papel.

  1. What problem was the new system designed to solve?
  2. Identify the sentence containing cuyo and state what it agrees with.
  3. Identify the two preposition-plus-relative structures in the second paragraph and translate each.
  4. Will people who prefer paper applications still be able to use them? How do you know?

Writing Workshop

Tarea de escritura

  1. Choose a public service, workplace process, or school procedure you know reasonably well.
  2. Write four short, simple sentences describing separate facts about it.
  3. Combine your four sentences into two complex sentences, using at least one preposition-plus-relative structure and at least one use of cuyo.
  4. Check punctuation: confirm which of your combined clauses are restrictive (no commas) and which are non-restrictive (set off by commas).

Lista de revisión:

  • Each relative pronoun agrees correctly with its antecedent.
  • Cuyo agrees with the noun possessed, not with the possessor.
  • Prepositions appear directly before el cual or quien, not before que.
  • Restrictive and non-restrictive clauses are punctuated correctly.
  • Accents, agreement, and register remain consistent with prior chapters.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context

Public administrative offices across Spanish-speaking countries vary widely in how digitized their procedures are, and many residents in different countries still rely on in-person or paper-based processes for at least some services. Written public announcements about new systems, like the one in this chapter's reading, commonly use exactly this kind of formal, relative-clause-rich prose.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake

Error: Placing a preposition directly before que, as in *el organismo con que nos comunicamos.

Correction: Use el cual or quien after a preposition in formal writing: el organismo con el cual nos comunicamos.

Common Mistake

Error: Making cuyo agree with the possessor instead of the thing possessed, as in *el vecino cuyo solicitud when the application (la solicitud) is feminine.

Correction: Agree cuyo/a/os/as with the noun that follows it: el vecino cuya solicitud.

Common Mistake

Error: Omitting the comma before a non-restrictive relative clause, which changes or obscures the intended meaning.

Correction: Use commas to set off information that adds detail about an already-identified antecedent, and omit them when the clause is needed to identify which item is meant.

Capítulo 19: Edición y precisión

Chapter function: Systematic self-editing for agreement, clitic placement, accent marks, register, cohesion, and style, consolidating the grammar, vocabulary, and pragmatic systems built across Volume IV in preparation for the Chapter 20 capstone.

1. Chapter Overview

Diego's team has spent this unit building the diplomatic language of Chapter 17 and the regional comprehension of Chapter 18. Before the final capstone project, the team pauses to sharpen a different skill: catching their own errors before a document goes out the door. This chapter does not introduce a new grammar paradigm. Instead, it gathers the editing knowledge you have been building since Volume I — agreement, clitic placement, accent marks, register consistency, and cohesion — into a single, disciplined editing process you can apply to your own writing. You will practice reading your own drafts the way a careful editor reads someone else's: slowly, system by system, looking for exactly the kinds of errors this course has taught you to recognize.

2. Learning Objectives

  • Apply a systematic, multi-pass editing procedure to a piece of one's own formal Spanish writing.
  • Identify and correct errors in noun-adjective and subject-verb agreement in extended text.
  • Verify correct clitic pronoun placement and pronoun-attachment accents in complex sentences.
  • Check accent marks, question and exclamation marks, and capitalization systematically rather than incidentally.
  • Evaluate whether register and address-form choices remain consistent across an entire document.
  • Improve cohesion by checking connector use, reference chains, and paragraph transitions.
  • Distinguish reliable cognates from false friends during the editing process.

3. Opening Communication Scenario

Camila has drafted a report recommending a new shipping schedule and asks Diego to review it before it goes to the regional office.

Camila: Diego, ¿puedes revisar mi informe antes de enviarlo? Creo que la idea está clara, pero no confío en mi propia edición.

Diego: Claro. Lo primero que hago siempre es leerlo una vez solo para el contenido, y después otra vez solo para la gramática. Si intentas revisar todo a la vez, se te escapan cosas.

Camila: Tiene sentido. ¿Por dónde empiezo?

Diego: Empieza por la concordancia, después los pronombres y los acentos, y al final el registro. Un informe con una sola falta de concordancia puede distraer al lector de una idea excelente.

5. Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter introduces no new productive grammar system. Instead, it organizes the systems you already control into a five-pass editing procedure. Each pass targets one category of error and should be done separately, not all at once.

5.1 Pass one: agreement

Check every noun phrase for gender and number agreement between the noun, its article, and any adjectives. Then check every finite verb against its subject, including subjects separated from the verb by intervening clauses.

Draft (contains an error)EditedWhat changed
Los nuevo horarios que propone el equipo son razonable.Los nuevos horarios que propone el equipo son razonables.adjective agreement with a plural masculine noun, in both the pre-verbal phrase and the predicate
La mayoría de los clientes, después de revisar la propuesta, están de acuerdo.La mayoría de los clientes, después de revisar la propuesta, está de acuerdo.subject-verb agreement with a singular collective subject despite an intervening plural noun

5.2 Pass two: clitic placement and pronoun-attachment accents

Locate every direct-object, indirect-object, and reflexive pronoun. Confirm correct placement before conjugated verbs and correct attachment to infinitives, gerunds, and affirmative commands, including the accent this attachment often requires.

Draft (contains an error)EditedWhat changed
Vamos a enviarlo mañana; confírmame lo antes posible.Vamos a enviarlo mañana; confírmamelo lo antes posible.double-object pronoun combination attached correctly to the affirmative command, with the required accent preserved
El cliente se lo explicó, pero no comprendieron.El cliente se lo explicó, pero no lo comprendieron.missing direct-object pronoun restored to track the referent across clauses

5.3 Pass three: accent marks, question/exclamation marks, and capitalization

Check written accents for exceptional stress, distinguishing accents such as sí/si and él/el, interrogative and exclamatory accents inside indirect questions, and Spanish capitalization conventions for days, months, and nationalities.

Draft (contains an error)EditedWhat changed
No sabemos cuando llegará el envío el Lunes.No sabemos cuándo llegará el envío el lunes.interrogative accent restored in the indirect question; lowercase restored for the day of the week

5.4 Pass four: register and address-form consistency

Confirm that a single address system and a single formality level are maintained throughout the document, and that no unlabeled regional form has entered the writer's own production.

Draft (contains an error)EditedWhat changed
Estimado señor Ruiz: Necesitamos que confirmés el pedido cuanto antes.Estimado señor Ruiz: Necesitamos que confirme el pedido cuanto antes.voseo verb form removed from the writer's own production; replaced with the course's productive usted form, consistent with the formal salutation

5.5 Pass five: cohesion and cognate check

Check that connectors accurately signal the relationship between ideas, that pronouns and demonstratives point clearly to their referents, and that any cognate used is a reliable one rather than a false friend.

Draft (contains an error)EditedWhat changed
Voy a asistir el equipo con el informe.Voy a ayudar al equipo con el informe.false friend asistir ("to attend") corrected to ayudar ("to help"); personal a restored
Language NoteDoing all five passes at once, on a single reading, is the most common reason careful writers still miss errors. Each pass asks your attention to focus on one system; reading five times for five different things is more reliable than reading once for everything.

6. Model Dialogue

Diego: Camila, encontré tres tipos de errores en tu borrador. Primero, un problema de concordancia en el segundo párrafo: escribiste "los horarios propuesto."

Camila: Ay, sí. Debería ser "los horarios propuestos." Gracias.

Diego: Exacto. Segundo, en la frase con el mandato, falta el pronombre de objeto directo después de "confírmame."

Camila: Tienes razón, necesito "confírmamelo," con el acento.

Diego: Y tercero, en la conclusión usaste "actual" para decir "real," pero en español "actual" significa "current." Esa es una falsa amiga clásica.

Camila: Entonces debo escribir "el problema verdadero," no "el problema actual."

Diego: Perfecto. Con esos tres cambios, el informe está listo para pasar en limpio.

11. Writing Workshop

Task: Choose a piece of your own writing from an earlier chapter in this volume (Chapter 1, 3, 8, or 16 are good candidates). Apply the full five-pass editing procedure and produce a clean, corrected final version (120–150 words). Attach a brief note (2–3 sentences) explaining which pass caught your most significant error and why.

Editing checklist

  • Pass 1 — Agreement: every noun-article-adjective group and every subject-verb pair checked
  • Pass 2 — Clitics: pronoun placement and pronoun-attachment accents verified
  • Pass 3 — Accents/punctuation/capitalization: distinguishing accents, interrogative accents, and Spanish capitalization rules checked
  • Pass 4 — Register: one address system and one formality level maintained throughout
  • Pass 5 — Cohesion/cognates: connectors, reference chains, and any cognate used verified as reliable

12. Culture and Communication

In many professional Spanish-language settings, a document with frequent agreement errors, inconsistent address forms, or missing accent marks can undermine a writer's credibility even when the underlying ideas are strong. This is not unique to Spanish, but the visibility of written accents and formal address consistency in Spanish makes careful editing especially valuable in institutional, academic, and business correspondence across the Spanish-speaking world. Editing is not a cosmetic last step; it is part of showing respect for the reader and the working relationship.

13. Common Mistakes

  • Trying to check every error category in a single reading pass instead of separating agreement, clitics, accents, register, and cohesion.
  • Correcting an agreement error near a verb but missing a matching error earlier or later in the same sentence.
  • Restoring a missing accent mark but overlooking that the same word appears elsewhere in the document without the accent.
  • Assuming a cognate is safe because it "looks right," without checking it against known false-friend patterns.
  • Fixing individual sentences without checking whether address forms and register remain consistent across the whole document.

14. Chapter Summary

This chapter organized agreement, clitic placement, accent marks, register consistency, and cohesion into a five-pass editing procedure. No new grammar system became productive; instead, you practiced applying systems from across the entire course with the discipline of a careful editor, preparing you to bring your strongest, most accurate work to the Chapter 20 capstone project.

Capítulo 20: Proyecto final — Desempeño C1 inicial

Chapter function: Final capstone integrating the professional, academic, and public-facing Spanish developed across Volume IV into a single portfolio, oral presentation, and live response to questions, at initial C1-oriented control.

1. Chapter Overview

Camila's report went out last week, clean and error-free after Diego's five-pass review. Now the whole team faces a larger task: presenting their work to the regional office in person. This final chapter of Volume IV does not introduce anything new. It asks you to bring together everything the volume has built — formal register, reports, argumentation, complex clauses, the compound and subjunctive systems, passive and impersonal constructions, media literacy, workplace diplomacy, regional awareness, and disciplined self-editing — into one integrated performance. You will assemble a short professional or academic portfolio, deliver an oral presentation based on it, and respond to follow-up questions, the way a confident C1-oriented speaker and writer would in a real institutional setting.

2. Learning Objectives

  • Assemble a coherent portfolio of formal written texts produced or revised using Volume IV systems.
  • Deliver a structured oral presentation that argues a position and supports it with evidence.
  • Respond to unscripted follow-up questions with register-appropriate, grammatically controlled Spanish.
  • Apply the five-pass editing procedure from Chapter 19 to every written component of the portfolio.
  • Maintain consistent address forms, tense sequencing, and cohesion across an extended, multi-genre body of work.
  • Self-assess readiness for Volume V using the volume's cumulative outcomes.

3. Opening Communication Scenario

The regional office has invited Diego's team to present their recommendations in person. Camila and Diego prepare together the night before.

Camila: Ya edité el informe con tus cinco pasadas. Ahora me preocupa la presentación oral. ¿Qué pasa si me hacen una pregunta que no esperaba?

Diego: Va a pasar, seguro. Lo importante es que respondas con calma, uses el registro formal que hemos practicado todo el volumen, y que no cambies de "usted" a "tú" a mitad de la respuesta por los nervios.

Camila: Entonces, ¿debería memorizar respuestas?

Diego: No. Es mejor dominar las estructuras — el subjuntivo, las cláusulas relativas, el se pasivo — para poder construir la respuesta en el momento. Si dominas el sistema, no necesitas memorizar cada frase.

5. Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter introduces no new productive grammar system. Instead, it asks you to demonstrate controlled, independent use of the systems consolidated across Volume IV, at the level marked C/A in the Part III matrices for Chapter 20: formal subordination and professional document architecture, complex relative clauses, compound tenses, advanced subjunctive and sequence of tenses, passive and impersonal se, and the full clitic and agreement system reviewed in Chapter 19.

6. Model Dialogue: Excerpt from the Presentation

Diego: Buenas tardes. Gracias por recibirnos. Hoy les presentamos una propuesta para modificar el horario de envíos, con base en un análisis que realizamos durante el último trimestre.

Representante regional: Adelante. ¿Cuál es la recomendación principal?

Diego: Recomendamos que se adopte el nuevo horario a partir del próximo mes. Se analizaron tres alternativas, y la que proponemos es la que mejor equilibra el costo y el tiempo de entrega.

Representante regional: ¿Y si los proveedores no pueden adaptarse a tiempo?

Diego: Es una posibilidad que consideramos. Aunque algún proveedor tuviera dificultades al principio, el plan incluye un período de transición de dos semanas durante el cual mantendríamos el horario anterior como respaldo.

Camila: Y para cuando termine ese período, ya habremos confirmado los resultados con cada proveedor cuyo contrato se vea afectado.

7. Guided Practice

Exercise 20.1 — Build the argument

Combine each pair of ideas into one sentence using the indicated system, then explain your choice.

  1. Recommendation + reason (formal subordination): Se debe capacitar al personal. / Esto reducirá los errores.
  2. Relative clause with preposition: El cliente presentó una queja. Hablamos con ese cliente ayer.
  3. Compound tense (anteriority): El equipo terminará el informe. La reunión empezará después.
  4. Concession with subjunctive: El plazo era corto. El equipo cumplió de todas formas.
  5. Passive/impersonal se: Alguien revisó tres propuestas antes de decidir.

Exercise 20.2 — Anticipate the question

For each presentation claim below, write a plausible follow-up question a listener might ask, and a response that uses at least one advanced subjunctive or compound-tense structure.

  2. Para el próximo trimestre, ya habremos completado la primera fase.
  3. Se consultó a varios departamentos antes de decidir.

11. Writing Workshop

Task: Assemble your final portfolio. Select and revise three short texts (150–200 words each) from earlier chapters — for example, a formal email (Ch. 3), an analytical paragraph (Ch. 9 or 10), and a public-issue analysis (Ch. 15 or 16) — applying the full five-pass edit to each. Write a one-paragraph introduction (80–100 words) connecting the three texts to a single professional or academic theme.

Portfolio checklist

  • Each text has been edited using all five passes from Chapter 19.
  • Address forms and register are consistent within each text and across the portfolio.
  • At least one text includes a compound tense, one includes advanced subjunctive or concession, and one includes passive or impersonal se.
  • The introductory paragraph clearly connects the three pieces.
  • No unlabeled regional forms appear in the writer's own production.

12. Culture and Communication

Presenting formal recommendations and defending them in a live question-and-answer session is a common expectation in professional and academic settings across many Spanish-speaking institutions, though the specific conventions — how directly to disagree, how much preparation is expected, how questions are typically phrased — vary by country, organization, and generation. In many contexts, maintaining a consistent, calm register under unexpected questioning is read as a sign of competence and preparation, not merely of language ability. This is one of the reasons Volume IV has emphasized register consistency and self-editing as much as new grammar: in professional and academic Spanish, precision and composure often communicate as much as content.

13. Common Mistakes

  • Shifting from usted to tú under the pressure of an unscripted question.
  • Memorizing fixed answers instead of practicing the underlying structures, leaving the speaker unable to adapt to an unanticipated question.
  • Overusing the passive or impersonal se to the point that the presentation sounds evasive about who is responsible for a decision.
  • Submitting portfolio texts that were never put through the five-pass edit from Chapter 19.
  • Treating concession (aunque, imperfect subjunctive) as weakness rather than as a sign of a well-considered argument.

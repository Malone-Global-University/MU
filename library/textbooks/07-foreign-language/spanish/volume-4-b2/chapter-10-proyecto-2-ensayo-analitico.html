Proyecto 2: Ensayo analítico

Evaluación integrada de la Unidad II: esquema, borrador, revisión y defensa oral · Speak and Write Spanish: Advanced Public, Professional, and Academic Spanish

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Interpret a complex source text for gist, detail, and inference, and identify its central claims.
  2. Paraphrase and summarize source material accurately, using attribution language without plagiarizing the original wording.
  3. Construct an analytical essay that presents a claim, supports it with evidence, concedes a counterpoint, and grades the certainty of each point.
  4. Outline, draft, and revise an analytical essay using a Spanish-specific editing checklist.
  5. Defend the essay's argument aloud, responding naturally to questions that challenge its claims or its certainty.
  6. Self-assess performance across reading, writing, and speaking using the chapter rubric.

This chapter is the Unit II cumulative assessment. It introduces no major new productive grammar system. It integrates and assesses the reading strategies from Chapter 6, the paraphrase and attribution language from Chapter 7, the claim-evidence-concession structure and aunque / a pesar de que system from Chapter 8, and the hedging vocabulary and certainty-graded indicative/subjunctive contrast from Chapter 9.

Communication Scenario

Daniela's planning committee has asked each member to submit a short analytical essay on a workplace or public-life issue before the next meeting, along with a brief oral defense of the argument. The essay must state a clear position, support it with evidence, acknowledge a reasonable counterpoint, and be honest about which parts of the argument are certain and which remain open to question.

  1. Think of an argument you have had to defend, in any language, where someone pushed back with a reasonable objection. How did you respond without abandoning your position or pretending the objection didn't matter?
  2. As you complete this project, notice how the skills from the last four chapters — reading, paraphrase, argument, and graded certainty — combine into a single piece of extended writing and a single spoken defense of it.

Reading: Texto fuente para el ensayo

Read the following adapted source text. You will paraphrase, evaluate, and respond to it in your own essay. As you read, apply the gist, detail, and inference strategies from Chapter 6.

El teletrabajo y la cultura de oficina: un debate sin resolver

Numerosas empresas han adoptado modelos híbridos o completamente remotos desde hace varios años, y los resultados han sido desiguales. Por un lado, varios estudios internos sugieren que la flexibilidad horaria mejora la satisfacción laboral y reduce la rotación de personal en ciertos sectores. Por otro lado, algunos gerentes sostienen que la cultura de equipo se debilita cuando los empleados rara vez comparten un mismo espacio físico.

Es evidente que no existe una fórmula única que funcione para todas las organizaciones: el tamaño de la empresa, el tipo de trabajo y la etapa de carrera de cada empleado influyen en el resultado. Es posible que el teletrabajo beneficie más a empleados con experiencia que ya conocen bien a sus equipos, mientras que es probable que los empleados nuevos necesiten más presencia física para integrarse plenamente. Aunque los datos disponibles todavía sean limitados, algunas empresas han comenzado a diseñar políticas diferenciadas según la antigüedad del empleado en lugar de aplicar una sola regla a todos.

This passage is an adapted textbook composition written for this project, not an authentic published article.

  1. State the main idea of the passage in one sentence, in your own words.
  2. Identify one claim the author presents with confidence, and one the author presents as merely probable or possible.
  3. Identify the counterpoint the author concedes, and the connector that introduces it.
  4. Paraphrase one sentence from the passage without copying its structure or exact wording.

Step 1 — Outline (Esquema)

Choose your own debatable topic related to work, school, or public life — it need not be the topic of the reading above. Before drafting, complete an outline using the following structure.

Esquema del ensayo

  1. Tesis: state your position in one clear sentence.
  2. Punto 1 (certeza): a claim you are confident about, with the evidence that supports it.
  3. Punto 2 (posibilidad): a related claim you present as probable or possible, not certain.
  4. Concesión: a reasonable counterpoint, introduced with aunque or a pesar de que.
  5. Conclusión: a recommendation that reflects the graded certainty of your points rather than treating them all equally.

Bring your completed outline to class or to your independent-study session before moving to the draft stage.

Step 2 — Draft (Primer borrador)

Using your outline, write a complete first draft of 180 to 220 words. Your draft must include all five outline elements and must use at least one expression from each of the four consolidated vocabulary categories above.

  1. State your thesis clearly in the first one or two sentences.
  2. Support your confident point with no cabe duda de que or es evidente que and the indicative.
  3. Present your probable or possible point with es probable que or es posible que and the subjunctive.
  4. Introduce your concession with aunque or a pesar de que, choosing indicative or subjunctive correctly.
  5. Close with a conclusion that matches your recommendation to the actual strength of your evidence.

Step 3 — Revision (Revisión)

Exchange drafts with a partner, or set your own draft aside and return to it later. Revise using the following Spanish-specific checklist before producing your final version.

Lista de revisión:

  • My thesis is stated clearly and appears early in the essay.
  • I distinguished what I am certain about from what I am not, using the correct mood in each case.
  • My concession uses aunque or a pesar de que correctly.
  • I paraphrased any source material instead of copying it, with attribution where needed.
  • My conclusion reflects the actual strength of my evidence rather than overstating certainty.
  • I checked noun-adjective agreement, verb forms, accents, and punctuation throughout.
  • My essay stays within the vocabulary and grammar developed in Chapters 6 through 9.

Produce a final version that responds to at least two items from the checklist above that needed correction.

Step 4 — Oral Defense (Defensa oral)

Presentación breve

Present your essay's thesis and main points aloud in two to three minutes, without reading the essay word for word.

Ronda de preguntas

Your partner or group should ask at least two follow-up questions, including one that challenges how certain you actually are about a specific point. Respond naturally, using hedging language where appropriate rather than simply repeating your written sentence.

Alternativa individual

If you are studying independently: record your oral defense, then record yourself asking and answering one challenging question about your own argument's weakest point.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context

In academic and professional settings across the Spanish-speaking world, an oral defense of written work is a common format, from university thesis defenses to workplace proposal reviews. Responding calmly and specifically to a challenging question, rather than becoming defensive, is often valued as highly as the original argument itself.

Regional Note (Recognition Only)

The formality and length expected of an academic essay can vary by institution and country. Some academic traditions favor a more direct, thesis-first structure; others favor a more gradual build toward the main claim. This chapter uses one workable structure rather than a single universal model.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake

Error: Copying phrasing directly from the source reading instead of paraphrasing it, even when a source is cited.

Correction: Restate the source's idea in your own words and sentence structure, using attribution language such as según or el texto sostiene que to show where the idea came from.

Common Mistake

Error: Presenting every point in the essay with the same certainty expression, regardless of how strong the evidence for each point actually is.

Correction: Vary the certainty expression — and the mood that follows it — according to how confident you genuinely are about each specific claim.

Common Mistake

Error: Abandoning the thesis or reversing the argument entirely when challenged during the oral defense.

Correction: Acknowledge a challenging question honestly, using hedging language if appropriate, while still maintaining the core position established in the written essay.

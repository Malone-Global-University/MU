19

Lo que hice y cómo era antes

What I Did and What It Used to Be Like · Speak and Write Spanish: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Overview and Objectives

Until now, you have described your daily life mostly in the present. This chapter opens the door to the past: telling someone what happened and describing how things used to be. Spanish uses two different past tenses for these two different jobs. The preterite reports completed actions and events — the things that moved a story forward. The imperfect paints the background — how things were, what was going on, or what someone used to do. You will learn the regular preterite, several very common irregular preterite verbs, and enough of the imperfect to describe simple background information, especially with ser, estar, and tener.

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Conjugate and use regular -ar, -er, and -ir verbs in the preterite.
  2. Use high-frequency irregular preterite verbs, including ir, ser, hacer, tener, and estar.
  3. Recognize and produce simple imperfect forms of ser, estar, tener, and haber to describe background information.
  4. Distinguish a completed event (preterite) from ongoing background description (imperfect) in a short narration.
  5. Narrate a short personal experience using time expressions and connectors.

This chapter builds on present-tense verb systems (Chapters 1–18), ser/estar/hay/tener (Chapters 1–18), and connectors such as y, pero, porque, and cuando (Chapter 7 and later). All prior material remains active.

Communication Scenario

Camila calls her grandmother to tell her about her weekend and to ask her grandmother what life was like when she was young.

  1. You will listen to Camila's grandmother describe what her childhood was like.
  2. You will practice narrating a sequence of completed events using the preterite.
  3. You will read a short narrative that contrasts what happened with what things were like.
  4. You will write a short narration about a memorable day, combining preterite events with imperfect background description.

Pronunciation Focus: Preterite Stress

Pronunciation Note — Stress in yo and él/ella preterite forms

Regular preterite yo and él/ella/usted forms carry stress on the last syllable and require a written accent: hablé, habló, comí, comió, viví, vivió. Practice hearing and producing the difference between the present habla (stress on the first syllable) and the preterite habló (stress on the final syllable). This stress shift is the main signal that distinguishes present from preterite in many -ar verbs, so listen for it carefully.

Grammar: Preterite, Imperfect, and Narration

Purpose: to report completed past actions, to describe background conditions in the past, and to combine both to tell a simple story.

Regular preterite

Building Block
-ar: -é, -aste, -ó, -amos, -asteis, -aron · -er/-ir: -í, -iste, -ió, -imos, -isteis, -ieron
hablé / hablaste / habló / hablamos / hablasteis / hablaron
Anoche estudié dos horas y luego llamé a mi abuela.
Last night I studied for two hours and then I called my grandmother.

The preterite reports actions that are seen as completed at a specific point or within a specific span of past time. Regular -er and -ir verbs share the same preterite endings.

  • Camila comió algo ligero antes de salir. Camila ate something light before going out. -er verb, comer → comió
  • Mis amigos y yo salimos temprano el sábado. My friends and I went out early on Saturday. -ir verb, salir → salimos

High-frequency irregular preterite verbs

Irregular preterite: ir/ser, hacer, tener, estar
Infinitiveyotúél/ella/ustednosotrosellos/ellas/ustedes
ir / serfuifuistefuefuimosfueron
hacerhicehicistehizohicimoshicieron
tenertuvetuvistetuvotuvimostuvieron
estarestuveestuvisteestuvoestuvimosestuvieron

Ir and ser share exactly the same preterite forms; context always tells you which meaning is intended. Notice that irregular preterite forms do not carry a written accent on yo or él/ella/usted.

  • El fin de semana pasado, fui a la casa de mis abuelos. Last weekend, I went to my grandparents' house. ir → fui
  • Fue un día muy especial. It was a very special day. ser → fue
  • Tuvimos que esperar el autobús media hora. We had to wait for the bus for half an hour. tener → tuvimos

Introducing the imperfect: ser, estar, tener, haber

Building Block
era / estaba / tenía / había (background description)
era pequeño · estaba tranquilo · tenía diez años · había mucha gente
Cuando era niña, vivía en un pueblo pequeño.
When I was a girl, I lived in a small town.

The imperfect describes what things were like, what was happening, or what someone used to do, without focusing on a beginning or an end. At this stage, you will mainly use the imperfect of ser (era, eras, era, éramos, eran), estar (estaba, estabas, estaba, estábamos, estaban), tener (tenía, tenías, tenía, teníamos, tenían), and the fixed form había (there was/were), which does not change for number.

  • Mi abuela tenía ocho años cuando su familia se mudó a la ciudad. My grandmother was eight years old when her family moved to the city. tenía = background age
  • Había mucha gente en la fiesta. There were a lot of people at the party. había = background existence, invariable

Event versus background: using preterite and imperfect together

A simple narration usually moves forward with preterite events and pauses to describe the scene with imperfect background information.

  • Cuando llegué a la fiesta, había mucha música y todos estaban muy contentos. When I arrived at the party, there was a lot of music and everyone was very happy. llegué = event (preterite); había/estaban = background (imperfect)
  • Mientras esperábamos el autobús, empezó a llover. While we were waiting for the bus, it started to rain. esperábamos = ongoing background; empezó = interrupting event

At this stage, focus on recognizing and producing this contrast with the small set of verbs above. Full productive control of the imperfect for all verbs is developed in a later volume.

Language Focus — Recycling doler, estar, and se lo with the past

Expressions from Chapter 18 combine naturally with the preterite: Me dolió la cabeza toda la tarde. (My head hurt all afternoon.) Estuve enferma el lunes. (I was sick on Monday.) You can also recycle double-object pronouns: Le expliqué el problema a mi abuela. Se lo expliqué por teléfono. (I explained the problem to my grandmother. I explained it to her by phone.)

Model Dialogue: ¿Qué hiciste el fin de semana?

Context: Camila talks to her friend Diego about her weekend.

Diego: ¿Qué hiciste el fin de semana? What did you do this weekend?
Camila: El sábado fui a la casa de mis abuelos. Comimos juntos y después hablamos toda la tarde. On Saturday I went to my grandparents' house. We ate together and afterward we talked all afternoon.
Diego: ¡Qué bien! ¿De qué hablaron? How nice! What did you talk about?
Camila: Mi abuela me contó cómo era su vida de niña. Me dijo que no había computadoras ni teléfonos como ahora. My grandmother told me what her life was like as a girl. She told me there weren't computers or phones like there are now.
Diego: Y tú, ¿tuviste tiempo para descansar? And you, did you have time to rest?
Camila: Un poco. El domingo estuve muy cansada y me dolió la cabeza toda la mañana, pero descansé y luego me sentí mejor. A little. On Sunday I was very tired and my head hurt all morning, but I rested and then I felt better.
  1. What did Camila do on Saturday?
  2. What did Camila's grandmother tell her about her childhood?
  3. How did Camila's Sunday begin, and how did it end?

Listening: Cuando era niña

If no audio recording is available, ask a partner, instructor, or text-to-speech tool to read the script below aloud, or read it aloud to yourself slowly.

Context: Camila's grandmother describes her childhood on the phone.

Abuela: Cuando yo era niña, vivíamos en un pueblo pequeño. No había muchas casas, y todos se conocían. Mi papá tenía una tienda pequeña en el centro. When I was a girl, we lived in a small town. There weren't many houses, and everyone knew each other. My dad had a small shop downtown.
Abuela: Un día, mi familia y yo fuimos a la capital por primera vez. Fue un viaje muy largo, pero fue increíble. One day, my family and I went to the capital for the first time. It was a very long trip, but it was incredible.
Abuela: Yo tenía solo siete años, y todo era muy diferente a mi pueblo. I was only seven years old, and everything was very different from my town.
  1. What was the grandmother's town like when she was a child?
  2. What event does she describe with the preterite?
  3. How old was she during that trip?

Reading: Un fin de semana diferente

Un mensaje de Camila — A message from Camila

El sábado pasado fue un día diferente. Por la mañana, hacía mucho frío y el cielo estaba gris. Mis amigos y yo fuimos al mercado central porque necesitábamos comprar comida para una fiesta. Cuando llegamos, había mucha gente y todos los puestos estaban llenos de frutas y verduras frescas. Compramos pan, queso y frutas, y luego volvimos a casa. Por la tarde, preparamos la comida juntos y escuchamos música. Fue un día tranquilo, pero muy divertido. Al final, todos estábamos muy cansados, pero contentos.

Tarea de lectura — Reading task:

  1. What was the weather like at the beginning of the story?
  2. List three completed events (preterite) mentioned in the passage.
  3. Find one sentence that describes background rather than an event, and explain why.

Writing Workshop

Complete the following linked writing tasks in order.

Tarea 1 — Los eventos: Write three sentences describing completed events from a memorable day, using regular and irregular preterite verbs.

Tarea 2 — El ambiente: Write two sentences describing the background of that day, using era, estaba, tenía, or había.

Model sentence patterns
Cuando era niño, vivía en una casa pequeña.
El sábado pasado fui al parque con mis amigos.
Había mucha gente y todos estaban contentos.
Use patterns like these as models for your own sentences.

Tarea 3 — Una narración breve: Combine your sentences into a short narration (six to eight sentences) about a memorable day. Include preterite events in order and at least two imperfect background sentences.

  1. Choose your day. Decide what memorable day you will narrate.
  2. List the main events in order. Use sequencing words such as primero, luego, and finalmente.
  3. Add background description. Decide where the scene needs era, estaba, tenía, or había.
  4. Connect events and background. Use cuando or mientras at least once.
  5. Combine everything into one connected narration. Add an opening and a closing sentence.
  6. Read your narration aloud once. Listen for the stress difference between present and preterite forms.
  7. Check every verb form and connector.

Lista de revisión — Editing checklist:

  • Regular preterite endings are correct, including written accents on yo and él/ella/usted forms.
  • Irregular preterite forms (ir/ser, hacer, tener, estar) are used correctly.
  • Era, estaba, tenía, and había are used only for background description, not for completed events.
  • Sequencing words and connectors organize the narration clearly.
  • Accent marks, punctuation, and capitalization are correct throughout.
  • No vocabulary or grammar from outside Chapters 1–19 has been used.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Family stories and oral history

In many Spanish-speaking families, grandparents and older relatives commonly share stories about their childhood and earlier life during family gatherings and phone calls. This is a common tendency in many communities, not a universal practice, and family storytelling traditions vary widely by household, region, and generation.

Regional Variation — Preterite versus present perfect for recent events

In broadly used Latin American Spanish, the preterite is the default way to talk about recent completed actions, such as Ya comí. ("I already ate.") In some regions, including much of Spain, the present perfect (He comido.) is more common for very recent events. This course treats the preterite as the core productive pattern for Volume I; the present perfect is introduced later in the series.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Forgetting the accent on regular preterite forms

Incorrect: Ayer hable con mi abuela.

Correct: Ayer hablé con mi abuela. The written accent on hablé marks the stress and distinguishes the preterite yo form from other forms; it is required, not optional.

Common Mistake — Using the imperfect for a single completed event

Incorrect: Ayer iba al mercado y compraba pan. (intended as a simple completed action)

Correct: Ayer fui al mercado y compré pan. Use the preterite for actions that happened and finished at a specific past time. Reserve the imperfect for background description, not for reporting what someone did and completed.

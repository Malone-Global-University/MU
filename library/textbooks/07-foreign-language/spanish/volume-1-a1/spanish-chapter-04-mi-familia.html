4

Mi familia, mis amigos y otras personas

My Family, My Friends, and Other People · Speak and Write Spanish: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Name close family members and describe basic relationships.
  2. Use tener to express possession and to say how old someone is.
  3. Use possessive words (mi, tu, su, nuestro/a) to show who something belongs to.
  4. Make descriptive adjectives agree with the gender and number of the person or thing they describe.
  5. Recognize the personal a when a specific person is the direct object of a sentence.
  6. Ask and answer simple questions about someone's family and age.

Communication Scenario

Class has not started yet, and Marisol shows Marcos a photo on her phone. It is a picture from a family gathering, and Marcos wants to know who everyone is. As Marisol explains, she talks about her parents, her siblings, and a cousin who is visiting from out of town. Marcos asks how old people are and mentions his own family in return. This chapter follows their conversation as a way of practicing family vocabulary, the verb tener, and possessive words.

  1. You will practice naming family members and describing them with adjectives that agree in gender and number.
  2. You will use tener to talk about age and possession, and possessive words to say whose family member or object you mean.

Pronunciation: Linking and Stress in Longer Phrases

Chapter 3 practiced linking an article to a noun. This chapter extends that same linking habit to short possessive phrases, and introduces the natural stress pattern of the word tiene and its family.

Pronunciation Focus — Possessive phrases and the stress of tiene

Say mi hermano and tu hermana as smooth, linked phrases, with no pause between the possessive word and the noun. The h in hermano and hermana is silent, so the vowels link even more closely than in Chapter 3's examples.

  • Practice mi abuelo and mi abuela, linking the final vowel of mi directly to the initial vowel of abuelo/abuela.
  • The word tiene is stressed on the first syllable: TIE-ne, not tie-NE. The same stress pattern applies to tienes and tenemos.
  • Practice the question ¿Cuántos años tienes? as one smooth phrase, keeping the stress on CUÁN-tos and TIE-nes.

Language Focus: Tener, Possessives, and Agreement

Purpose: talk about who is in your family, how old people are, what belongs to whom, and what people are like.

4.1 Tener — to have

Language Focus

Tener means "to have." Its present-tense forms are irregular and must be learned individually at this stage; later chapters will connect tener to the wider pattern of first-person irregular verbs.

Tener — Present tense
SubjectForm
yotengo
tútienes
usted / él / ellatiene
nosotros/astenemos
ustedes / ellos / ellastienen
  • Tengo dos hermanos. I have two brothers.
  • ¿Tienes hermanas? Do you have sisters?

4.2 Tener for age

Building Block — Talking about age with tener
tener + number + años
Spanish uses tener, not ser, to talk about age. Literally, the structure says that a person "has" a certain number of years, so años is a required part of the pattern and cannot be dropped.
Mi hermano tiene diez años. · Mi abuela tiene setenta años.
My brother is ten years old. · My grandmother is seventy years old.

Do not translate age word for word from English. Spanish age expressions always use tener plus a number plus años.

4.3 Possessive words

Language Focus

Possessive words agree with the noun they describe, not with the owner. Mi and tu add -s for plural nouns; nuestro also changes for gender. Su/sus can mean "his," "her," "your (usted)," or "their," and context usually makes the meaning clear.

SingularPluralEnglish

nuestro tío / nuestra tíanuestros tíos / nuestras tíasour uncle(s)/aunt(s)
  • Mi mamá y mi papá son simpáticos. My mom and dad are nice.
  • ¿Cómo se llama tu abuela? What's your grandmother's name?

4.4 Adjective agreement

Language Focus

A descriptive adjective must match the gender and number of the noun it describes. Adjectives ending in -o change to -a for feminine nouns; adjectives ending in -e or most consonants usually stay the same for both genders but still add -s or -es for the plural.

  • Mi hermano es alto. Mi hermana es alta. My brother is tall. My sister is tall.
  • Mis primos son jóvenes. My cousins are young.
  • Mi abuelo es trabajador. Mi abuela es trabajadora. My grandfather is hardworking. My grandmother is hardworking.

4.5 Recognizing the personal a

Language Focus — Recognition only

When the direct object of a verb is a specific person, Spanish usually places the word a directly before that person. This chapter introduces the personal a only for recognition; you will begin producing it independently once object patterns are developed further.

  • Marisol conoce a su prima. Marisol knows her cousin. personal a before a specific person
  • Veo a mi hermano en la foto. I see my brother in the photo. personal a before a specific person

Model Dialogue: ¿Cuántos años tiene tu hermano?

Context: Marisol shows Marcos a photo on her phone before class.

Marcos: ¿Quiénes son ellos? Who are they?
Marisol: Son mis padres y mi hermano. Y esta es mi prima; ella está de visita. They're my parents and my brother. And this is my cousin; she's visiting.
Marcos: ¿Cuántos años tiene tu hermano? How old is your brother?
Marisol: Tiene quince años. Es alto y muy simpático. He's fifteen years old. He's tall and very nice.
Marcos: Yo tengo una hermana. Ella tiene doce años. I have a sister. She's twelve years old.

Marisol points to another person in the photo.

Marisol: Y este señor es mi abuelo. Tiene ochenta años, pero es muy trabajador. And this man is my grandfather. He's eighty years old, but he's very hardworking.
Marcos: ¡Qué familia tan grande! What a big family!

Notice how tener changes form with each subject, and how each adjective agrees with the person it describes.

Listening Practice

If no audio recording is available, ask a partner, instructor, or text-to-speech tool to read the lines below aloud, or read them aloud to yourself slowly, paying attention to the stress on tiene and its forms.

Listening script
Mi familia es pequeña. Mi mamá tiene cuarenta y cinco años y mi papá tiene cuarenta y siete años. Tengo un hermano; él tiene ocho años. Nuestro perro también es parte de la familia.
My family is small. My mom is forty-five years old and my dad is forty-seven years old. I have a brother; he is eight years old. Our dog is also part of the family.
  1. Listen to (or read aloud) the script, then list each family member mentioned with their age.
  2. Listen for who is older, the speaker's mother or father.
  3. Listen for the one non-human member of the family.

Speaking Practice

Práctica controlada — Controlled practice: tener y la edad

Practice saying the age of five different people using tener + años, alternating between tengo, tienes, and tiene.

Tarea comunicativa — Communicative task: Mi familia

With a partner, take turns describing three family members. Say who they are, how old they are, and one adjective that describes them, making sure the adjective agrees.

Práctica independiente — Solo alternative

Describe, out loud, three people in your own family or a family you know well, using tener, a possessive word, and at least one adjective for each person.

Writing Workshop

Complete the following linked writing tasks in order.

Tarea 1 — Tener y la edad: Write five sentences giving the ages of five people you know, using different forms of tener.

Tarea 2 — Completar con posesivos: Fill in the blanks below describing your own family.

Fill in the blank

Tarea 3 — Descripción breve: Write three short sentences describing a family member: name the relationship, give the age with tener, and describe the person with an adjective that agrees correctly.

  1. Write out five age sentences first. This reinforces the irregular forms of tener from the start.
  2. Complete the possessive fill-in-the-blank about your family. Check that each possessive word matches its noun.
  3. Draft your three-sentence family description. Keep every word within this chapter's vocabulary and prior chapters' vocabulary.
  4. Read your description aloud once. Listen for smooth linking between possessive words and nouns, and for correct stress on tiene.
  5. Check your adjective endings against the vocabulary and grammar tables. Correct any gender or number mismatch.

Lista de revisión — Editing checklist:

  • Every form of tener matches its subject correctly.
  • Age expressions include años and are not translated word for word from English.
  • Possessive words agree with the noun they describe, not with the owner.
  • Adjectives agree in gender and number with the person or thing they describe.
  • No new vocabulary from outside this chapter and prior chapters has been used.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Family structures and households

Family structures vary widely across Spanish-speaking communities and across individual households, including who lives together, how extended family stays involved, and how family roles are shared. A single family description, such as the ones in this chapter, should not be treated as representative of every family in every Spanish-speaking community.

Regional Variation — Words for mom and dad

Mamá and papá are broadly understood everyday terms alongside madre and padre. Some countries and families also use other affectionate terms for parents and grandparents. This course uses mamá/papá and madre/padre as the core productive terms; other regional terms may appear later in the series, clearly labeled.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using ser for age

Error: Saying Mi hermano es diez años, translating English "is" directly with ser.

Correction: Spanish uses tener for age, not ser. Say Mi hermano tiene diez años.

Common Mistake — Making possessives agree with the owner

Error: Changing su to a feminine or masculine form based on whether the owner is male or female, such as inventing sua.

Correction: Possessive words agree with the noun that follows them, not with the owner. Su hermano and su hermana both use su, regardless of whether the owner is male or female.

Common Mistake — Forgetting adjective agreement

Error: Saying Mi hermana es alto, leaving the adjective in its masculine form.

Correction: Adjectives must match the gender of the person they describe. Say Mi hermana es alta.

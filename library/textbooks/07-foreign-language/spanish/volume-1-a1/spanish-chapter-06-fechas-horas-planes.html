Fechas, horas y planes del día

Dates, Times, and Daily Plans · Speak and Write Spanish: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Say and ask the day of the week, the date, and the month.
  2. Tell time in Spanish and ask what time something happens, using a for clock time.
  3. Conjugate and use regular -ar verbs in the present tense to describe everyday actions.
  4. Talk about a daily or weekly schedule using time expressions and frequency words.
  5. Ask and answer information questions that combine dates, times, and activities.
  6. Write a short schedule or plan with correct accent marks, punctuation, and capitalization.

Communication Scenario

Marisol, Marcos, and their new classmates are organizing their week at the language program. Marisol wants to plan a study group, Marcos needs to know when his classes meet, and everyone is trying to coordinate a weekend activity. To do this, they need to talk about days, dates, and times, and describe what they usually do and when.

  1. You will ask and say what day, date, and time it is.
  2. You will describe a weekly schedule using regular -ar verbs and time expressions.

Core Vocabulary

The vocabulary below is new productive material for this chapter. It builds directly on the identity, description, and family vocabulary consolidated in Chapter 5.

Pronunciation and Orthography Focus

Pronunciation and Orthography Focus — Stress in -ar Verb Forms

Regular -ar verb endings follow the natural Spanish stress pattern: stress falls on the next-to-last syllable in most present-tense forms, so no written accent is needed.

  • estudio, estudias, estudia, estudiamos, estudian — stress moves naturally with the ending; none of these forms carries a written accent.
  • Keep the vowel sounds of -o, -as, and -amos short and clear; do not glide them the way English speakers glide unstressed vowels.
  • Numbers used for dates and times, such as dieciséis and veintidós, keep their required accent marks.
  • Open every question with ¿, including short questions like ¿Qué hora es? and ¿A qué hora?

Grammar: Dates, Time, and Regular -ar Verbs

Purpose: give learners the tools to talk about when things happen and to describe habitual actions using the first full regular verb conjugation of the course.

6.1 Regular -ar verbs in the present tense

Building Block — Regular -ar conjugation
habl-ar → hablo, hablas, habla, hablamos, hablan
Remove the -ar ending from the infinitive and add the ending that matches the subject. This course uses tú, usted, and ustedes as the productive address forms, so the vosotros form is not included here.
Estudio español. · Marisol trabaja los lunes. · Mis amigos y yo practicamos por la tarde.
I study Spanish. · Marisol works on Mondays. · My friends and I practice in the afternoon.
Language Focus

Every regular -ar verb uses the same set of endings: -o (yo), -as (tú), -a (usted / él / ella), -amos (nosotros/nosotras), and -an (ustedes / ellos / ellas). Once you know one regular -ar verb, you can conjugate hundreds of others the same way.

  • ¿Trabajas los sábados? Do you work on Saturdays?
  • Marcos y Tomás escuchan música por la noche. Marcos and Tomás listen to music at night.

6.2 Days, dates, and months

Language Focus

Use el before a day to talk about a specific occurrence, and los to talk about something that repeats: el lunes (this Monday) versus los lunes (on Mondays, every Monday). Dates follow the pattern el + number + de + month, and, when needed, + de + year.

  • Hoy es martes, el 14 de octubre. Today is Tuesday, October 14th.
  • Los sábados, Marisol no trabaja. On Saturdays, Marisol doesn't work.

6.3 Telling time and using a for clock time

Building Block — Asking and telling time
¿Qué hora es? → Es la una. / Son las + [number]. · ¿A qué hora...? → A la(s) + [number].
Es is used only for one o'clock; son is used for all other hours. When asking or stating at what time something happens, Spanish uses a, not a form of ser.
Son las cuatro y media. · La clase empieza a las nueve de la mañana.
It's four thirty. · The class begins at nine in the morning.
  • ¿A qué hora terminas hoy? What time do you finish today?
  • Llego a la universidad a las ocho de la mañana. I arrive at the university at eight in the morning.

6.4 Asking information questions about schedules

Language Focus — Review and Expansion

Chapter 2 introduced question words such as qué, cuándo, and dónde. This chapter adds ¿a qué hora? and ¿qué día? as fixed patterns for scheduling questions, and recycles ¿cuántos/as? for counting classes or activities.

  • ¿Qué día practicas español? What day do you practice Spanish?
  • ¿Cuántas clases tomas este semestre? How many classes are you taking this semester?

This chapter does not introduce -er or -ir verbs, reflexive routines, or the preterite. Those systems are activated in Chapters 7 and 19.

Model Dialogue: ¿A qué hora empezamos?

Context: Marisol and Marcos are planning a study group after class.

Marisol: Marcos, ¿qué día practicas español normalmente? Marcos, what day do you usually practice Spanish?
Marcos: Practico los martes y los jueves. Estudio por la tarde. I practice on Tuesdays and Thursdays. I study in the afternoon.
Marisol: Perfecto. Yo trabajo los martes, pero los jueves no trabajo. ¿A qué hora terminas tus clases? Perfect. I work on Tuesdays, but I don't work on Thursdays. What time do you finish your classes?
Marcos: Termino a las tres y media. ¿A qué hora llegas tú a la biblioteca? I finish at three thirty. What time do you arrive at the library?
Marisol: Llego a las cuatro. Entonces, ¿estudiamos juntos los jueves a las cuatro? I arrive at four. So, do we study together on Thursdays at four?

Marcos checks his phone calendar.

Marcos: Sí, perfecto. Hoy es lunes, el 13 de octubre, entonces empezamos este jueves, el 16. Yes, perfect. Today is Monday, October 13th, so we start this Thursday, the 16th.
Marisol: Muy bien. Siempre practico mejor con un compañero. Very good. I always practice better with a study partner.

Notice how the dialogue moves from asking about days, to schedules with regular -ar verbs, to specific times with a, and finally to a full date — the same sequence used in this chapter's tasks.

Speaking Practice

Práctica controlada — Controlled practice: Mi horario

Say four sentences about your own schedule using regular -ar verbs: one about studying, one about working, one about arriving somewhere, and one about a leisure activity, each with a day or time.

Tarea comunicativa — Communicative task: Organizar un grupo de estudio

With a partner, compare your weekly schedules and agree on a day and time to study together, following the model dialogue. Ask each other at least three questions with ¿qué día? or ¿a qué hora?

Práctica independiente — Solo alternative

Record or say aloud a short description of your typical week, including at least three activities, the days you do them, and the times, using regular -ar verbs.

Writing Workshop

Complete the following linked writing tasks in order. Together they form your written weekly schedule for this project.

Tarea 1 — Mis actividades: Write four short sentences using four different regular -ar verbs to describe things you do.

Tarea 2 — Completar el horario: Fill in the blanks below to build a short schedule description.

Fill in the blank
Los ______ (día), yo ______ (verbo -ar) a las ______. Los ______ (día), ______ (verbo -ar) por la ______ (mañana/tarde/noche).
On ______ (day), I ______ (-ar verb) at ______. On ______ (day), I ______ (-ar verb) in the ______ (morning/afternoon/night).

Tarea 3 — Horario completo: Combine your sentences from Tareas 1 and 2 into one short paragraph of five to seven sentences describing your weekly schedule, including at least one date and one question you would ask a classmate about their schedule.

  1. Write your four activity sentences first. This reviews regular -ar verb endings.
  2. Complete the fill-in-the-blank schedule description. Check that every verb ending matches its subject.
  3. Combine your sentences into one paragraph. Add a date using el + number + de + month, and a closing question.
  4. Read your paragraph aloud once. Listen for correct stress on -ar verb forms and smooth linking before numbers.
  5. Check every accent mark, capitalization choice, and inverted question mark. Confirm that days and months are lowercase.

Lista de revisión — Editing checklist:

  • Every regular -ar verb ending matches its subject.
  • A, not a form of ser, introduces the time something happens.
  • Es is used only for one o'clock; son is used for all other hours.
  • Days and months are written in lowercase.
  • Question words carry the correct written accent, and questions open with ¿.
  • No vocabulary or grammar from outside Chapters 1–6 has been used.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Schedules and the work week

Work and class schedules, typical start times, and the length of the midday break vary widely across Spanish-speaking countries, institutions, and individual workplaces. The schedule in this chapter reflects one plausible student routine, not a fixed pattern for every setting.

Regional Variation — Dates and the 24-hour clock

Written schedules, tickets, and official documents in many Spanish-speaking countries use the 24-hour clock, for example las 15:00 instead of las tres de la tarde. This course models everyday spoken time; learners will recognize the 24-hour format receptively when they encounter it on schedules and signs.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using ser instead of a for clock time

Error: Saying Es a las tres or dropping a entirely, saying Termino las tres.

Correction: Use a to say at what time something happens: Termino a las tres.

Common Mistake — Confusing es and son

Error: Saying Son la una or Es las cuatro.

Correction: Use es only for one o'clock and son for all other hours: Es la una. Son las cuatro.

Common Mistake — Capitalizing days and months

Error: Writing Lunes or Octubre with a capital letter, following English capitalization.

Correction: Days and months are lowercase in Spanish unless they begin a sentence: lunes, octubre.

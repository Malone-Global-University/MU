Capítulo 1: ¡Hola! Primeros encuentros

Chapter function: Introduce greetings, courtesy, the Spanish alphabet, the address forms tú and usted, and singular ser, along with the formulaic expression Me llamo..., as the productive foundation for all further communication in Volume I.

1. Chapter Overview

Every conversation in Spanish begins somewhere: a greeting, a name, a question about how someone is doing. This first chapter gives you the tools for that very first moment of contact — saying hello, giving your name, asking someone else's name, and choosing between a friendly tú and a more respectful usted. You will also learn the Spanish alphabet, so that you can spell your name and understand how words are pronounced and written. Nothing in this chapter assumes any prior knowledge of Spanish. By the end, you will be able to greet someone, introduce yourself, and say goodbye — the essential first exchange of any relationship in Spanish.

2. Learning Objectives

  • Greet people and say goodbye using common courtesy expressions.
  • Say and spell your name using the Spanish alphabet.
  • Introduce yourself using Me llamo... and Soy....
  • Ask and answer ¿Cómo te llamas? and ¿Cómo se llama usted? appropriately.
  • Identify when to use tú and when to use usted based on the situation.
  • Pronounce Spanish vowels clearly and recognize basic stress patterns.

3. Opening Communication Scenario

Sofía is a new student at a language center. On her first day, she meets another student, Marco, in the hallway, and then meets her instructor, la profesora Díaz, in the classroom.

Marco: ¡Hola! Me llamo Marco. ¿Cómo te llamas?

Sofía: Hola, Marco. Me llamo Sofía. Mucho gusto.

Marco: Mucho gusto, Sofía. ¿Cómo estás?

Sofía: Muy bien, gracias. ¿Y tú?

Marco: Bien, gracias.

— En la clase —

Profesora Díaz: Buenos días. ¿Cómo se llama usted?

Sofía: Me llamo Sofía Torres. Mucho gusto, profesora.

Profesora Díaz: El gusto es mío, Sofía. Bienvenida a la clase.

Language NoteNotice that Marco, a fellow student, uses ¿Cómo te llamas?, while Sofía uses ¿Cómo se llama usted? with her instructor. Tú is common between peers; usted is common with someone in a position of authority or someone you have just met in a more formal setting. You will learn much more about this choice throughout the course.

Cognate WatchProfesor/a looks and sounds close to "professor" — a reliable cognate here. Be careful with courtesy words like gracias, which have no direct English cognate and simply need to be memorized as fixed expressions.

5. Pronunciation and the Spanish Alphabet

Spanish uses five clear, stable vowel sounds. Unlike English, each Spanish vowel has one consistent sound, which makes Spanish pronunciation very predictable once you learn the five vowels.

LetterApproximate soundExample
alike the "a" in "father"casa
elike the "e" in "bed"bien
ilike the "ee" in "see"sí
olike the "o" in "note" (no glide)hola
ulike the "oo" in "moon"gusto

The Spanish alphabet uses the same 27 letters described in this series' governing writing-system policy, including the letter ñ, which does not exist in English. Ñ sounds like the "ny" in "canyon," as in español and mañana.

Language NoteMost Spanish words are stressed on the second-to-last syllable if they end in a vowel, n, or s — for example, gracias and buenos. A written accent mark, like the one in adiós, shows that a word breaks this pattern. You will learn the full accent system gradually; for now, simply notice that accent marks affect where the stress falls.

Exercise 1.1 — Spell your name

Using the Spanish alphabet, practice spelling your own name aloud, letter by letter. If your name contains a letter that does not exist in Spanish, such as "w," note how it is typically referred to: doble ve or uve doble.

6. Grammar and Sentence Patterns

6.1 Subject pronouns: yo, tú, usted

Spanish subject pronouns are often omitted once the verb form makes the subject clear, but at this early stage it is useful to see them explicitly.

PronounMeaningTypical use
yoIspeaking about yourself
túyou (informal, singular)friends, peers, family, children
ustedyou (formal, singular)teachers, strangers, people in authority, first contact
Language NoteDo not think of usted as simply "more polite" and tú as simply "less polite." The real choice depends on relationship, age, and setting. As a beginner, a safe default with someone you have just met is usted; you can adjust once you understand the relationship better. You will study this choice in much more depth in later chapters and volumes.

6.2 Singular ser: identity

The verb ser expresses identity — who someone is. At this stage, you only need its three singular forms.

PronounserExample
yosoyYo soy Sofía.
túeresTú eres Marco.
usted / él / ellaesUsted es la profesora Díaz.

Because yo is often understood from the verb ending, Spanish speakers frequently say simply Soy Sofía rather than Yo soy Sofía. Both are correct; the version without yo is more natural in everyday speech.

6.3 The formulaic expression Me llamo...

To give your name, Spanish speakers most often use Me llamo..., literally "I call myself...". At this stage, treat Me llamo as a fixed, memorized expression rather than analyzing its grammar — the underlying reflexive system will be studied formally later in the course.

QuestionAnswer
¿Cómo te llamas? (informal)Me llamo...
¿Cómo se llama usted? (formal)Me llamo...

7. Model Dialogue: Two First Meetings

Ana: ¡Buenos días! Me llamo Ana. ¿Cómo te llamas?

Luis: Buenos días, Ana. Me llamo Luis. Mucho gusto.

Ana: Mucho gusto, Luis. ¿Cómo estás?

Luis: Bien, gracias. ¿Y tú?

Ana: Muy bien, gracias. Hasta luego, Luis.

Luis: Adiós, Ana.

12. Writing Workshop

Task: Write a short self-introduction (3–4 sentences). Include a greeting, your name using Me llamo... or Soy..., and a farewell.

Writing checklist

  • The introduction opens with a greeting (Hola or Buenos días).
  • The name is given using Me llamo... or Soy....
  • The introduction closes with a farewell (Adiós or Hasta luego).
  • Capitalization and punctuation are correct.

13. Culture and Communication

Greeting customs vary across Spanish-speaking communities and settings. In many contexts, greetings are accompanied by a handshake, a light kiss on the cheek, or a nod, depending on the relationship, region, and setting. Choosing tú or usted is one part of a larger set of social signals that includes tone of voice, gesture, and context — you will build a fuller picture of these choices throughout the course.

14. Common Mistakes

  • Using tú automatically with every new person, including teachers or unfamiliar adults.
  • Forgetting that gracias has no literal English translation and must be learned as its own expression.
  • Pronouncing Spanish vowels with an English glide, for example saying o like the English "oh" instead of a pure, short vowel.
  • Omitting written accent marks, such as writing adios instead of adiós.

15. Chapter Summary

This chapter introduced the essential first exchange in Spanish: greetings, courtesy expressions, the alphabet, and the formulaic phrase Me llamo..., along with the singular forms of ser and the address choice between tú and usted. These building blocks will appear in every chapter that follows, forming the foundation for the identity, description, and question-asking systems developed across the rest of Unit I.

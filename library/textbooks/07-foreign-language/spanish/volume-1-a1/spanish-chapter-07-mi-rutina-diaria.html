7

Mi rutina diaria

My Daily Routine · Speak and Write Spanish: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Conjugate and use regular -er and -ir verbs in the present tense.
  2. Use reflexive pronouns with common daily routine verbs.
  3. Describe your daily routine in a logical sequence using time expressions from Chapter 6.
  4. Use expanded negation, including nunca and no... nunca, to say what you never do.
  5. Ask and answer questions about someone else's routine.
  6. Write a short paragraph describing a daily routine with correct reflexive pronoun placement and accent marks.

Communication Scenario

Marisol now shares an apartment with a new roommate, Diego. Since they have different schedules, they are comparing their daily routines to decide who uses the bathroom first, when they eat breakfast, and when the apartment is quiet enough to study. To do this, they need to describe their routines from morning to night.

  1. You will describe the order of activities in a daily routine using regular -er, -ir, and reflexive verbs.
  2. You will ask and answer questions comparing two people's routines, including what someone never does.

Pronunciation and Orthography Focus

Pronunciation and Orthography Focus — -er/-ir Endings and the Reflexive Pronoun Link

Regular -er and -ir verbs share almost identical present-tense endings, which makes their vowel sounds easy to confuse when speaking quickly.

  • como, comes, come versus vivo, vives, vive — the -o and stressed vowel of each ending stay short and clear; do not lengthen them as English speakers often do with stressed vowels.
  • Reflexive pronouns (me, te, se) link smoothly to the verb that follows in natural speech: me levanto is pronounced as one flowing unit, not as two separated words.
  • None of the regular present-tense reflexive or -er/-ir forms used in this chapter requires a written accent.
  • Continue opening every question with ¿, including ¿A qué hora te levantas?

Grammar: Routines, -er/-ir Verbs, and Reflexives

Purpose: give learners the tools to narrate a sequence of daily actions using the second and third regular verb conjugations, reflexive pronouns for routine actions, and expanded negation.

7.1 Regular -er and -ir verbs in the present tense

Building Block — Regular -er and -ir conjugation
com-er → como, comes, come, comemos, comen · viv-ir → vivo, vives, vive, vivimos, viven
Remove the -er or -ir ending from the infinitive and add the matching ending. The two conjugations differ only in the nosotros form (-emos versus -imos); every other productive ending is identical.
Leo el correo por la mañana. · Diego escribe mensajes por la tarde. · Mis amigos y yo comemos a las dos.
I read email in the morning. · Diego writes messages in the afternoon. · My friends and I eat at two.
Language Focus

Regular -er verbs use -o, -es, -e, -emos, -en. Regular -ir verbs use -o, -es, -e, -imos, -en. Compare these to the -ar endings from Chapter 6 (-o, -as, -a, -amos, -an): the yo form always ends in -o, no matter which conjugation the verb belongs to.

  • ¿Dónde vives? Where do you live?
  • Diego asiste a una clase de historia los martes. Diego attends a history class on Tuesdays.

7.2 Reflexive pronouns and daily routine verbs

Building Block — Reflexive pronoun + verb
me lavo · te lavas · se lava (usted/él/ella)
A reflexive verb shows that the subject performs the action on or for themselves. The reflexive pronoun always matches the subject and normally comes directly before the conjugated verb.
Me ducho a las siete. · ¿A qué hora te levantas? · Diego se afeita todas las mañanas.
I shower at seven. · What time do you get up? · Diego shaves every morning.
Language Focus

The infinitive form of a reflexive verb carries -se attached to the end: lavarse, levantarse, afeitarse. When you conjugate the verb for a specific subject, the reflexive pronoun detaches and moves in front of the conjugated verb: lavarse → me lavo, not lavo me.

  • Primero me despierto, luego me levanto y me ducho. First I wake up, then I get up and shower.
  • Marisol se peina después de ducharse. Marisol combs her hair after showering.

Some daily-routine verbs, such as despertarse and acostarse, are stem-changing. This chapter presents only their regular yo/tú/usted forms in fixed model sentences; full stem-changing conjugation is activated in Chapter 9.

7.3 Sequencing a routine and using frequency words

Language Focus

Use primero, luego, después, and finalmente to organize a routine into a clear order. Combine sequencing words with frequency words from the vocabulary list to say how often something happens.

  • Generalmente me levanto temprano, pero los domingos me quedo en casa. I generally get up early, but on Sundays I stay home.
  • Después de comer, leo las noticias. After eating, I read the news.

7.4 Expanded negation with nunca

Building Block — no + verb + nunca
No + [verb] + nunca. · Nunca + [verb].
Spanish allows two negative words in the same sentence without cancelling the negation. Place no before the verb and nunca after it, or place nunca alone before the verb without no. Both patterns mean the same thing.
No como carne nunca. · Nunca como carne.
I never eat meat.
  • Diego nunca se levanta antes de las nueve. Diego never gets up before nine.
  • Casi nunca escribo mensajes por la noche. I almost never write messages at night.

This chapter does not introduce nada, nadie, or double-object pronoun combinations. Those systems remain scheduled for later chapters.

Model Dialogue: ¿A qué hora te despiertas?

Context: Marisol and her new roommate, Diego, are comparing morning routines.

Marisol: Diego, ¿a qué hora te despiertas normalmente? Diego, what time do you usually wake up?
Diego: Me despierto a las seis, pero no me levanto hasta las seis y media. Primero leo el correo en la cama. I wake up at six, but I don't get up until six thirty. First I read email in bed.
Marisol: Yo nunca leo el correo tan temprano. Me levanto, me ducho y luego como algo rápido. I never read email that early. I get up, shower, and then eat something quick.
Diego: ¿A qué hora te duchas tú? Yo me ducho a las siete y después me afeito. What time do you shower? I shower at seven and then shave.
Marisol: Me ducho a las siete y cuarto. Entonces, ¿tú usas el baño primero? I shower at seven fifteen. So, do you use the bathroom first?

Diego thinks for a moment and checks the time.

Diego: Sí, yo termino a las siete y diez. Después tú te duchas, y finalmente comemos juntos. Yes, I finish at seven ten. Then you shower, and finally we eat together.
Marisol: Perfecto. Casi nunca desayuno sola; prefiero comer con alguien. Perfect. I almost never have breakfast alone; I prefer to eat with someone.

Notice how the dialogue moves from asking about routine times, to sequencing actions with primero/después/finalmente, to using nunca and casi nunca — the same sequence used in this chapter's tasks.

Speaking Practice

Práctica controlada — Controlled practice: Mi mañana

Say four sentences describing your morning routine in order, using primero, luego, después, and finalmente, with at least two reflexive verbs and one regular -er/-ir verb.

Tarea comunicativa — Communicative task: Comparar rutinas

With a partner, compare your morning and evening routines, following the model dialogue. Ask each other at least three questions with ¿a qué hora? and use nunca at least once each.

Práctica independiente — Solo alternative

Record or say aloud a short description of your typical day from waking up to going to bed, including at least five activities in a logical sequence.

Reading Practice

Luego me ducho, me visto y desayuno con mis compañeros. Después asisto a mis clases y como con Marcos a la una. Writing Workshop

Complete the following linked writing tasks in order. Together they form your written daily routine for this project.

Tarea 1 — Mis actividades diarias: Write four short sentences using four different reflexive or regular -er/-ir verbs to describe things you do during the day.

Tarea 2 — Completar la rutina: Fill in the blanks below to build a short routine description.

Fill in the blank
Todos los días, ______ (despertarse) a las ______. Primero ______ (verbo reflexivo), y luego ______ (verbo -er/-ir). Nunca ______ (verbo) después de las ______.
Every day, I ______ (wake up) at ______. First I ______ (reflexive verb), and then I ______ (-er/-ir verb). I never ______ (verb) after ______.

Tarea 3 — Rutina completa: Combine your sentences from Tareas 1 and 2 into one short paragraph of five to seven sentences describing your daily routine from morning to night, including at least one sequencing word and one use of nunca.

  1. Write your four activity sentences first. This reviews reflexive and regular -er/-ir verb endings.
  2. Complete the fill-in-the-blank routine description. Check that every reflexive pronoun matches its subject.
  3. Combine your sentences into one paragraph. Add sequencing words and a sentence with nunca.
  4. Read your paragraph aloud once. Listen for correct stress on -er/-ir forms and a smooth link between the reflexive pronoun and the verb.
  5. Check every accent mark, capitalization choice, and pronoun placement. Confirm that reflexive pronouns appear directly before the conjugated verb.

Lista de revisión — Editing checklist:

  • Every regular -er/-ir verb ending matches its subject.
  • Every reflexive pronoun (me, te, se) matches its subject and comes before the conjugated verb.
  • Nunca is used correctly, either alone before the verb or with no after it.
  • Sequencing words (primero, luego, después, finalmente) organize the routine clearly.
  • Question words carry the correct written accent, and questions open with ¿.
  • No vocabulary or grammar from outside Chapters 1–7 has been used.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Shared living and daily coordination

Roommates, family members, and housemates across Spanish-speaking communities coordinate morning and evening routines in many different ways, depending on household size, work schedules, and personal habits. The routine in this chapter reflects one plausible arrangement, not a fixed pattern for every household.

Regional Variation — Words for getting dressed and grooming

Some grooming and routine vocabulary varies by region; for example, some speakers say bañarse where others say ducharse for showering, even though the two verbs can also describe different actions. This course uses ducharse as the core productive form and treats regional preferences as recognition-only variation.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Misplacing the reflexive pronoun

Error: Saying Levanto me a las siete, placing the reflexive pronoun after the conjugated verb.

Correction: The reflexive pronoun comes before the conjugated verb: Me levanto a las siete.

Common Mistake — Dropping the reflexive pronoun entirely

Error: Saying Ducho a las ocho, without any reflexive pronoun.

Correction: Reflexive verbs require the pronoun that matches the subject: Me ducho a las ocho.

Common Mistake — Treating nunca like an English double negative

Error: Avoiding no and nunca together because it looks like a double negative in English, and saying No como nunca is wrong.

Correction: Spanish allows and often requires two negative words together: No como nunca and Nunca como are both correct and mean the same thing.

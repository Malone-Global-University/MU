Proyecto 1: Presentarme y conocer a otros

Project 1: Introducing Myself and Getting to Know Others · Speak and Write Spanish: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Greet people, introduce yourself, and use basic courtesy expressions accurately.
  2. Use ser to identify yourself and others, state origin, and describe basic characteristics.
  3. Use hay to say what people or things are present, and use articles and plural nouns correctly.
  4. Use tener to talk about age, family, and possession.
  5. Ask and answer yes/no and information questions using material from Chapters 1 through 4.
  6. Produce a short spoken and written self-introduction with correct accent marks, punctuation, and capitalization.

Communication Scenario

The language program is holding a welcome gathering for new students. Marisol and Marcos, along with several classmates, are meeting people they have not talked to before. Around the room, students introduce themselves, ask where others are from, describe the people and things around them, and talk briefly about their families. This chapter is a single connected project: instead of introducing new grammar, it asks you to bring together everything from Chapters 1 through 4 in one realistic round of introductions.

  1. You will greet someone new, exchange names, and ask where they are from.
  2. You will describe people and objects around you, mention family members, and ask a few follow-up questions.

Pronunciation and Orthography Review

This project brings together the pronunciation and spelling habits practiced in the first four chapters. No new sound or spelling pattern is introduced.

Pronunciation and Orthography Focus — Review

As you speak and write for this project, keep the following habits active:

  • Link articles and possessive words smoothly to the nouns that follow them, as in mi amigo and una amiga.
  • Keep the natural stress pattern of tiene and tienes on the first syllable.
  • Use the written accent on question words such as qué, cómo, dónde, and cuántos.
  • Open every question and exclamation with the correct inverted mark: ¿ and ¡.
  • Capitalize names of people but keep nationalities and languages in lowercase, as in Marisol es mexicana.

Language Review: Ser, Tener, Hay, and Questions

Purpose: connect identity, description, existence, possession, and questions into one flexible set of tools for meeting new people.

5.1 Ser for identity and description

Language Focus — Review

Ser identifies who someone is, where they are from, and what they are like. Review the full present-tense forms from Chapter 2: soy, eres, es, somos, son. Remember that descriptive adjectives after ser must agree in gender and number with the person or thing they describe.

  • Soy estudiante. Mi amiga es de Colombia. I am a student. My friend is from Colombia.
  • Marisol y Marcos son simpáticos. Marisol and Marcos are nice.

5.2 Hay, articles, and plural nouns

Language Focus — Review

Hay says what exists or is present, and does not change form. Review that definite articles (el, la, los, las) point to a specific noun already known to the listener, while indefinite articles (un, una, unos, unas) introduce something new, as with hay.

  • Hay muchos estudiantes nuevos en la fiesta. There are many new students at the party.
  • Hay una mesa con refrescos. There is a table with drinks.

5.3 Tener for age, family, and possession

Building Block — Review: tener across functions
tener + años · tener + family noun · tener + object
Tener covers three separate ideas that English expresses differently: age, family relationships, and simple possession. All three use the same irregular verb forms reviewed in Chapter 4.
Tengo diecinueve años. · Tengo dos hermanos. · Tengo un libro nuevo.
I am nineteen years old. · I have two brothers. · I have a new book.

5.4 Asking and answering questions

Language Focus — Review

Yes/no questions in Spanish are usually marked by intonation and the inverted question mark rather than by word order alone. Information questions use question words such as qué, quién, cómo, dónde, de dónde, and cuántos, all of which carry a written accent.

  • ¿Eres de aquí? — No, soy de Perú. Are you from here? — No, I'm from Peru.
  • ¿Cuántos hermanos tienes? How many siblings do you have?

This project introduces no new productive grammar. Every pattern above was taught in Chapters 1 through 4 and is simply being used together.

Model Dialogue: Presentaciones en la fiesta

Context: Marisol meets a new student, Tomás, at the welcome gathering.

Marisol: ¡Hola! Me llamo Marisol. ¿Cómo te llamas? Hi! My name is Marisol. What's your name?
Tomás: Mucho gusto, Marisol. Me llamo Tomás. Nice to meet you, Marisol. My name is Tomás.
Marisol: ¿De dónde eres, Tomás? Where are you from, Tomás?
Tomás: Soy de Chile. ¿Y tú? I'm from Chile. And you?
Marisol: Soy de México. Mira, hay mucha gente aquí esta noche. I'm from Mexico. Look, there are a lot of people here tonight.

Marisol points toward a group near the door.

Marisol: Ese es Marcos. Es mi amigo y es muy simpático. That's Marcos. He's my friend and he's very nice.
Tomás: ¿Tienes hermanos, Marisol? Do you have siblings, Marisol?
Marisol: Sí, tengo un hermano. Tiene quince años. Yes, I have a brother. He's fifteen years old.
Tomás: Qué bien. Mucho gusto en conocerte. That's nice. It was great meeting you.

Notice how the dialogue moves through greetings, identity, origin, description, existence with hay, and family — the same sequence you will use in this chapter's tasks.

Speaking Practice

Práctica controlada — Controlled practice: Cadena de preguntas

Practice a short chain of four questions and answers: name, origin, age, and one family member, alternating between tú and usted forms.

Tarea comunicativa — Communicative task: Conocer a alguien nuevo

With a partner, act out meeting someone for the first time at the welcome gathering. Greet each other, exchange names, ask where you are from, describe one person nearby using hay, and mention one family member.

Práctica independiente — Solo alternative

Record or say aloud a one-minute self-introduction that includes your name, origin, age, a brief description of yourself, and one family member, as if meeting a new classmate.

Writing Workshop

Complete the following linked writing tasks in order. Together they form your written self-introduction for this project.

Tarea 1 — Datos personales: Write four short sentences: your name, your origin, your age, and one adjective that describes you.

Tarea 2 — Completar la presentación: Fill in the blanks below to build a short self-introduction.

Fill in the blank
Me llamo ______. Soy de ______. Tengo ______ años. ______ estudiante. En mi familia, hay ______.
My name is ______. I am from ______. I am ______ years old. I am a student. In my family, there is/are ______.

Tarea 3 — Presentación completa: Combine your sentences from Tareas 1 and 2 into one short paragraph of five to seven sentences that introduces yourself to a new classmate, including at least one question you would ask them.

  1. Write your four personal-data sentences first. This reviews ser, tener, and adjective agreement together.
  2. Complete the fill-in-the-blank self-introduction. Check that every verb form matches its subject.
  3. Combine your sentences into one paragraph. Add a closing question for your future classmate.
  4. Read your paragraph aloud once. Listen for smooth linking and correct stress on question words and on tiene/tienes.
  5. Check every accent mark, capitalization choice, and inverted question or exclamation mark. Correct anything that does not match the review tables above.

Lista de revisión — Editing checklist:

  • Every form of ser, tener, and hay is used correctly for its function.
  • Articles and nouns agree in gender and number.
  • Adjectives agree with the person or thing they describe.
  • Question words carry the correct written accent, and questions open with ¿.
  • Names are capitalized; nationalities and languages are lowercase.
  • No vocabulary or grammar from outside Chapters 1–4 has been used.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — First meetings among students

Ways of greeting new people, including gestures, physical distance, and how quickly personal information is shared, vary across Spanish-speaking communities, institutions, and individual comfort levels. The gathering in this chapter reflects one plausible classroom scenario, not a fixed rule for every setting.

Regional Variation — Address at a first meeting

Whether new acquaintances of similar age move quickly to tú or maintain usted longer depends on region, setting, and personal or institutional norms. This course models the flexible use of both forms; learners should observe context carefully rather than assuming one fixed rule applies everywhere.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Confusing ser and hay

Error: Saying Es muchos estudiantes en la fiesta, using ser where existence, not identity, is intended.

Correction: Use hay to say that something exists or is present. Say Hay muchos estudiantes en la fiesta.

Common Mistake — Using ser for age

Error: Saying Soy veinte años, translating English "I am" directly with ser.

Correction: Spanish uses tener for age. Say Tengo veinte años.

Common Mistake — Missing accents on question words

Error: Writing Como te llamas? without the accent on cómo and without the opening inverted question mark.

Correction: Interrogative words carry a written accent, and Spanish questions open with ¿. Write ¿Cómo te llamas?

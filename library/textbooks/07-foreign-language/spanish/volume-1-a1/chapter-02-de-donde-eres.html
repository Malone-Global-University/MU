¿De dónde eres?

Where Are You From? · Speak and Write Spanish: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Use the full present-tense conjugation of ser with yo, tú, usted, él/ella, and nosotros/nosotras.
  2. Ask and state where someone is from using ¿De dónde eres? and ¿De dónde es usted?
  3. Name a country, a nationality, and a language, and make nationality adjectives agree in gender and number.
  4. Ask basic information questions with qué, quién, cómo, and dónde.
  5. Recognize written accent marks on question words as a fixed, required feature of Spanish spelling.
  6. Continue choosing between tú and usted consistently within a single conversation.

Communication Scenario

It is the second class of the semester, and Marcos's Spanish course has just finished going around the room with names. Now the instructor asks everyone, one by one, where they are from and what language or languages they speak at home. The answers turn out to be more varied than anyone expected: the room includes classmates from several countries, a visiting instructor from Colombia, and more than one student who grew up speaking two languages. This chapter follows that same conversation, one country and one nationality at a time.

  1. You will meet the same small set of information questions in several short exchanges, so that ¿De dónde eres? and its answers become automatic before any new grammar is added.
  2. Every new question word in this chapter carries a written accent mark, and that accent mark is part of correct Spanish spelling, not decoration.

Pronunciation: Word Stress and Written Accents

Chapter 1 introduced the five stable Spanish vowels and syllable timing. This chapter adds the written accent mark, which every question word in this chapter's vocabulary requires.

Pronunciation Focus — Interrogative accent marks

Spanish question words such as qué, quién, cómo, and dónde always carry a written accent mark when they are used to ask a question, even though the same-looking words without the accent exist elsewhere in the language with a different function. At this stage, simply learn each question word together with its accent mark as one visual unit.

  • The accent mark tells you which syllable to stress: dónde, not donde.
  • Spanish questions also open with an inverted question mark, ¿, which signals to a reader from the very first word that a question is coming.
  • Practice saying each question word aloud, stressing the accented syllable clearly, before using it in a full question.

Language Focus: Ser, Origin, and Nationality

Purpose: state where you and others are from, name a nationality or language, and ask a small set of controlled information questions.

2.1 The full present tense of ser

Language Focus

Chapter 1 introduced soy and eres as part of the formulaic pattern for greetings. This chapter presents the complete productive paradigm of ser for the forms you need at this stage: yo, tú, usted, él/ella, and nosotros/nosotras. The forms used with ustedes and with ellos/ellas are presented for recognition only in this chapter; full productive control of plural forms is scheduled for a later chapter.

  • Yo soy de México. I am from Mexico.
  • ¿Tú eres de Colombia? — Sí, soy colombiana. Are you from Colombia? — Yes, I am Colombian.
  • Nosotros somos de los Estados Unidos. We are from the United States.

2.2 Nationality-adjective agreement

Building Block — Nationality adjectives agree with the person they describe
-o → -a · consonant → + -a · -e/-ense → no change
A nationality adjective ending in -o changes to -a for a feminine subject, as in mexicano/mexicana. An adjective ending in a consonant, such as español, typically adds -a for the feminine form: española. An adjective already ending in -e or -ense, such as estadounidense or canadiense, does not change for gender. All nationality adjectives add -s or -es for the plural, following the same pattern as other Spanish adjectives.
Ana es mexicana. Luis es mexicano. Ana y Luis son mexicanos.
Ana is Mexican. Luis is Mexican. Ana and Luis are Mexican. (plural form shown for recognition)

The plural form son mexicanos is shown here for recognition only, since productive ustedes/ellos/ellas forms are not yet activated.

2.3 Basic information questions

Language Focus

This chapter adds four controlled question words to the yes/no questions from Chapter 1: qué (what), quién (who), cómo (how), and dónde (where), along with the combined form de dónde (from where). Each question word is placed at the beginning of the question, directly before the verb.

  • ¿De dónde eres? — Soy de Argentina. Where are you from? — I am from Argentina.
  • ¿Cómo estás? — Bien, gracias. How are you? — Fine, thanks. (recycled from Chapter 1's courtesy formulas)
  • ¿Quién es? — Es mi profesora. Who is it? — It's my teacher.

Model Dialogue: ¿De dónde eres?

Context: The instructor asks a new student where she is from, on the second night of class.

Profesora: ¿Cómo te llamas? What is your name?
Marisol: Me llamo Marisol. My name is Marisol.
Profesora: Mucho gusto, Marisol. ¿De dónde eres? Nice to meet you, Marisol. Where are you from?
Marisol: Soy de Colombia. Soy colombiana. I'm from Colombia. I'm Colombian.

Marisol turns to the student next to her.

Marisol: ¿Y tú? ¿De dónde eres? And you? Where are you from?
Marcos: Soy de los Estados Unidos. Y mi mamá es de Brasil, así que hablo un poco de portugués también. I'm from the United States. And my mom is from Brazil, so I speak a little Portuguese too.
Marisol: ¡Qué interesante! How interesting!

Notice that Marisol uses the informal tú with Marcos, a fellow student close to her age, while she used no address pronoun at all with the instructor, matching the register patterns introduced in Chapter 1.

Listening Practice

If no audio recording is available, ask a partner, instructor, or text-to-speech tool to read the lines below aloud, or read them aloud to yourself slowly, paying attention to the accented question words.

Listening script
Hola, ¿cómo te llamas? Me llamo Diego. ¿De dónde eres, Diego? Soy de España. Soy español y hablo español e inglés.
Hi, what is your name? My name is Diego. Where are you from, Diego? I'm from Spain. I'm Spanish and I speak Spanish and English.
  1. Listen to (or read aloud) the script above, then answer, in Spanish, where you are from using the correct formula.
  2. Listen for Diego's nationality. Which word tells you his nationality, and what form does it take?
  3. Listen for the two languages Diego says he speaks.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Naming your own nationality

Asking and stating nationality is a routine part of getting to know someone across the Spanish-speaking world, but how a person answers can vary. Some speakers identify primarily with a country, others with a region or city, and some, particularly bilingual or multinational families like Marcos's in the Model Dialogue, may name more than one place or language as part of their answer. Treat ¿De dónde eres? as an invitation to a flexible answer, not a request for a single fixed category.

Regional Variation — Spanish as a pluricentric language

Spanish is spoken as a first or official language in more than twenty countries across Latin America, Spain, and Equatorial Guinea, along with large Spanish-speaking communities in the United States and elsewhere. This course uses a broadly understood contemporary Latin American production model, but no single country's Spanish represents every Spanish speaker, and this course will return to regional variation directly at several later points.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Forgetting nationality-adjective agreement

Error: Using the masculine form of a nationality adjective for a female subject, such as saying Ana es mexicano instead of adjusting the ending.

Correction: Always check the gender of the person you are describing and adjust the adjective accordingly: Ana es mexicana.

Common Mistake — Dropping the accent mark on question words

Error: Writing donde, como, or que without their accent marks when asking a question, since the unaccented spelling looks similar.

Correction: Interrogative words in Spanish always carry a written accent mark. Learn each question word together with its accent as one visual unit, as emphasized in this chapter's Pronunciation Focus.

Common Mistake — Using son with ustedes/ellos before it is productive

Error: Attempting to produce full sentences with ustedes son or ellos son at this stage, before the plural address system has been developed.

Correction: Recognize the form son when you see or hear it, but limit your own production to yo, tú, usted, él/ella, and nosotros/nosotras until a later chapter activates the plural forms for production.

Salud y bienestar

Health and Well-Being · Speak and Write Spanish: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Overview and Objectives

Talking about how you feel is one of the most common things people do in daily life, whether at home, at a pharmacy, or at a doctor's office. This chapter introduces body vocabulary and symptoms, the verb doler for aches and pains, sentirse for describing how you feel, estar for physical and emotional conditions, and tener expressions for physical states such as fever or pain. You will also learn to give advice and make formal requests, and you will begin producing double-object pronoun combinations such as se lo, which you first recognized in Chapter 17.

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Name common body parts and describe symptoms and general health.
  2. Use doler and sentirse to describe aches, pains, and how someone feels.
  3. Distinguish estar conditions from tener physical-state expressions such as tener fiebre and tener dolor de cabeza.
  4. Give advice and make polite, formal requests related to health.
  5. Produce double-object pronoun combinations such as se lo, se la, se los, and se las in simple, controlled sentences.

This chapter builds on ser/estar/hay (Chapters 1–8), indirect object pronouns (Chapters 9 and 17), and reflexive verbs (Chapter 7). All prior material remains active.

Communication Scenario

Camila wakes up feeling unwell before an important exam. She stops at the pharmacy on her way to the university and later calls the doctor's office to ask about an appointment.

  1. You will listen to a phone call to a doctor's office and identify the reason for the call.
  2. You will practice describing symptoms with doler, sentirse, estar, and tener.
  3. You will read a short list of advice from a doctor.
  4. You will write a short message describing how you feel and asking someone to pass along information for you, using se lo or a similar combination.

Pronunciation Focus: Stress in Symptom Phrases

Pronunciation Note — Stress in me duele and tengo dolor de

In me duele la cabeza, the stress falls naturally on due- in duele, not on me. Practice keeping me, te, and le short and unstressed before duele or duelen: me duele, te duele, le duelen los oídos. In tengo dolor de garganta, keep the phrase linked smoothly, without a pause between dolor and de.

Grammar: Doler, Sentirse, and Physical States

Purpose: to describe symptoms and physical conditions, to give advice, and to say who receives information using double-object pronoun combinations.

Doler: like gustar

Building Block
[indirect object pronoun] + duele/duelen + [parte del cuerpo]
me duele la cabeza · me duelen los pies
Me duele la garganta.
My throat hurts. (Literally: The throat is painful to me.)

Doler behaves exactly like gustar: the body part is the grammatical subject, and the person who feels the pain is expressed with an indirect object pronoun. Use duele with a singular body part and duelen with a plural body part.

  • Me duele el estómago. My stomach hurts. me + duele + singular subject
  • A Camila le duelen los oídos. Camila's ears hurt. clarifying a + le + duelen + plural subject

Sentirse, estar, and tener for physical states

Sentirse is reflexive and describes how someone feels in general. Estar describes a condition or state. Tener expressions describe specific physical states that English usually expresses with "to be" or "to have."

Sentirse, estar, and tener for describing how someone feels
ExpressionUseExample
sentirse bien/malgeneral well-beingMe siento mal hoy.
estar enfermo/a, cansado/aa condition or stateCamila está enferma.
tener fiebre, tos, dolor de...a specific physical symptomTengo fiebre y dolor de cabeza.

Do not translate these expressions word for word from English. Spanish does not say soy enfermo for a temporary illness, and it does not say soy fiebre for having a fever; each expression has its own established pattern.

Advice and formal requests

  • Usted debe descansar y tomar mucha agua. You should rest and drink a lot of water. deber + infinitivo, formal address
  • ¿Me puede dar una cita para mañana? Can you give me an appointment for tomorrow? formal request with poder + infinitivo
Language Focus — Producing se lo, se la, se los, se las

In Chapter 17, you learned to recognize that le and les become se before a direct object pronoun. Now you will practice producing this pattern in short, controlled sentences: Le doy la medicina. → Se la doy. (I give it to him/her.) Keep sentences simple and use context or a prior sentence to make clear who se refers to.

  • El médico le explica el problema a Camila. → El médico se lo explica. The doctor explains the problem to Camila. → The doctor explains it to her. le + lo → se lo
  • Le doy las pastillas a mi hermano. → Se las doy. I give the pills to my brother. → I give them to him. le + las → se las

Práctica guiada — Try it:

  1. Complete with doler: A mí _____ (doler) la cabeza.
  2. Choose sentirse, estar, or a tener expression: Camila _____ (tener) fiebre y _____ (sentirse) muy cansada.
  3. Rewrite using se lo/la/los/las: Le doy el jarabe a mi hijo. → ______.

Model Dialogue: En la farmacia

Context: Camila stops at a pharmacy on her way to class because she does not feel well.

Farmacéutico: Buenos días. ¿Cómo se siente? Good morning. How are you feeling?
Camila: No muy bien. Me duele la garganta y creo que tengo fiebre. Not very well. My throat hurts and I think I have a fever.
Farmacéutico: ¿Le duele también la cabeza o el estómago? Does your head or stomach also hurt?
Camila: Sí, me duele un poco la cabeza. Estoy muy cansada también. Yes, my head hurts a little. I'm also very tired.
Farmacéutico: Usted debe descansar y tomar mucha agua. Le voy a dar unas pastillas para la fiebre. You should rest and drink a lot of water. I'm going to give you some pills for the fever.
Camila: Muchas gracias. ¿Se las puedo dar a mi compañera también? Ella está enferma. Thank you very much. Can I give them to my classmate too? She's sick.
Farmacéutico: Es mejor que ella compre las suyas. Cada persona debe tomar su propia medicina. It's better if she buys her own. Each person should take their own medicine.
  1. What symptoms does Camila describe?
  2. What advice does the pharmacist give her?
  3. What does Camila ask about, and why does the pharmacist say no?

Listening: Una llamada al consultorio

If no audio recording is available, ask a partner, instructor, or text-to-speech tool to read the script below aloud, or read it aloud to yourself slowly.

Context: Camila calls a doctor's office to describe her symptoms and ask for an appointment.

Recepcionista: Consultorio del doctor Ruiz, buenos días. ¿En qué le puedo ayudar? Dr. Ruiz's office, good morning. How can I help you?
Camila: Buenos días. No me siento bien. Tengo fiebre y me duele mucho la garganta. ¿Me puede dar una cita para hoy? Good morning. I don't feel well. I have a fever and my throat hurts a lot. Can you give me an appointment for today?
Recepcionista: Sí, claro. Hay una cita a las cuatro. Se la puedo dar si usted llega quince minutos antes. Yes, of course. There's an appointment at four. I can give it to you if you arrive fifteen minutes early.
  1. What symptoms does Camila describe on the phone?
  2. What time is the appointment?
  3. What condition does the receptionist give for the appointment?

Reading: Consejos del médico

Nota del consultorio — Note from the doctor's office

Estimada Camila: Según lo que usted me explicó, tiene fiebre, dolor de garganta y se siente muy cansada. Le recomiendo descansar por dos días y tomar mucha agua. Writing Workshop

Complete the following linked writing tasks in order.

Tarea 1 — Mis síntomas: Write three sentences describing how you feel today or how you felt recently, using doler, sentirse, and a tener expression at least once each.

Tarea 2 — Consejos: Write two sentences giving advice to someone who is sick, using usted debe or usted tiene que.

Model sentence patterns
Me duele mucho la cabeza y tengo fiebre.
Usted debe descansar y tomar mucha agua.
Le doy la receta a mi médico. Se la doy hoy mismo.
Use patterns like these as models for your own sentences.

Tarea 3 — Un mensaje breve: Combine your sentences into a short message (six to eight sentences) to a friend or family member, describing how you feel, what advice you received, and something you need to pass along to someone else using se lo/la/los/las.

  1. Choose your recipient. Decide who is receiving your message and why.
  2. List your symptoms first. Decide whether each uses doler, sentirse, estar, or tener.
  3. Add advice. Include at least one recommendation.
  4. Add one double-object sentence. Check that se clearly refers to a person already mentioned.
  5. Combine everything into one connected message. Add a greeting and a closing.
  6. Read your message aloud once. Listen for smooth linking in me duele and tengo dolor de.
  7. Check every symptom expression and pronoun combination.

Lista de revisión — Editing checklist:

  • Doler agrees correctly with a singular or plural body part.
  • Sentirse, estar, and tener expressions are used appropriately, not confused with one another.
  • Double-object combinations (se lo/la/los/las) are formed correctly and refer clearly to a known person.
  • Accent marks, punctuation, and capitalization are correct throughout.
  • No vocabulary or grammar from outside Chapters 1–18 has been used.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Pharmacies and health communication

In many Spanish-speaking communities, pharmacists commonly offer basic health advice and recommend over-the-counter medicine for minor symptoms before a person sees a doctor. This is a common tendency in some communities, not a universal medical practice, and it does not replace professional medical care for serious or ongoing symptoms.

Regional Variation — Words for body parts and illness

Some Spanish-speaking regions use additional or alternative words for common symptoms, such as el resfriado or el resfrío for "a cold." This course treats the vocabulary in this chapter as the core productive set; other regional terms appear here only as recognition-only background information.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Treating doler like a regular verb with a subject pronoun

Incorrect: Yo duelo la cabeza.

Correct: Me duele la cabeza. Doler works like gustar: the body part is the subject, and the person is expressed with an indirect object pronoun, not a subject pronoun.

Common Mistake — Confusing ser and estar with enfermo

Incorrect: Camila es enferma.

Correct: Camila está enferma. A temporary illness is a state, expressed with estar, not a permanent identity expressed with ser.

Estudio, trabajo y comunicación

School, Work, and Communication · Speak and Write Spanish: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Overview and Objectives

School and work bring obligations, deadlines, and messages that need to be sent, received, and answered. This chapter adds three ways to talk about obligation — tener que, deber, and the impersonal hay que — and expands your ability to say who receives an action with indirect object pronouns beyond gustar. You will also begin to recognize, without yet producing, how le and les change to se when a direct object pronoun is added.

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Express obligation and necessity using tener que + infinitivo, deber + infinitivo, and the impersonal hay que + infinitivo.
  2. Distinguish personal obligation from general or impersonal obligation.
  3. Use indirect object pronouns (me, te, le, nos, les) with verbs such as dar, decir, escribir, mandar, and explicar.
  4. Recognize, without yet producing, how le/les become se before a direct object pronoun.
  5. Talk and write about school, work, and everyday communication tasks such as emails, messages, and assignments.

This chapter builds on present-tense verbs, gustar and indirect objects (Chapter 9), estar + gerundio (Chapter 16), and vocabulary and structures from Chapters 1–16. All prior material remains active.

Communication Scenario

It's Monday morning. Camila has a full day of classes and a part-time job at a university office. Between assignments, emails, and messages from coworkers, she has to organize what she needs to do and who needs to hear from her.

  1. You will listen to office messages and identify who has to do what.
  2. You will practice expressing obligation with tener que, deber, and hay que.
  3. You will read an email from a professor about an assignment.
  4. You will write a short message describing your obligations and telling someone what you need to communicate to them.

Pronunciation Focus: Linked Obligation Phrases

Pronunciation Note — Smooth linking in tener que and hay que

Tengo que and hay que are said as smooth, connected units in natural speech, not as separate words with pauses: tengo que estudiar sounds close to [ten-go-ke-es-tu-diar], and hay que trabajar links the diphthong ay directly into que. Practice saying tengo que salir, tienes que llamar, and hay que terminar slowly, then at natural speed, keeping the stress on the infinitive that follows.

Grammar: Obligation and Indirect Objects

Purpose: to express personal and general obligation, and to say to whom or for whom an action is done.

Tener que, deber, and hay que

Building Block
tener que + infinitivo  ·  deber + infinitivo  ·  hay que + infinitivo
tengo que estudiar · debo estudiar · hay que estudiar
Tengo que entregar la tarea hoy.
I have to turn in the homework today.
  • Tengo que trabajar esta tarde. I have to work this afternoon. (strong personal obligation) tener que + infinitivo
  • Debes llamar a tu profesor. You should call your professor. (recommendation or softer obligation) deber + infinitivo
  • Hay que terminar el proyecto antes del viernes. It's necessary to finish the project before Friday. (general, impersonal obligation) hay que + infinitivo, no specific subject

Use tener que for a specific person's obligation, deber for advice or a softer sense of "should," and hay que when the obligation applies in general rather than to one named person. Hay que never changes form and never takes a subject pronoun.

Indirect object pronouns beyond gustar

You already use indirect object pronouns with gustar (Chapter 9). The same pronouns — me, te, le, nos, les — mark the person who receives, hears, or benefits from an action with verbs like dar, decir, escribir, mandar, and explicar.

Indirect object pronouns
PronounMeaningExample
meto/for meMi jefa me explica el proyecto.
teto/for you (tú)Te escribo un mensaje.
leto/for him, her, you (usted)Le doy la tarea a la profesora.
nosto/for usEl profesor nos manda un correo.
lesto/for them, you all (ustedes)Les explico el problema a mis compañeros.

Because le and les can refer to more than one person (him, her, you formal), speakers often add a clarifying phrase with a: Le escribo un correo a mi profesor. Indirect object pronouns go before a conjugated verb, just like the direct object pronouns you learned in Chapters 11–12.

Language Focus — Recognizing se (recognition only)

When le or les would appear directly before a direct object pronoun such as lo, la, los, or las, Spanish changes le/les to se: Le doy el libro → Se lo doy (I give it to him/her). At this stage, you only need to recognize this pattern when you see or hear it. Producing double-object combinations like se lo becomes an active learning target in Chapter 18.

Model Dialogue: Un lunes ocupado

Context: Camila talks with her coworker Diego at the university office about their Monday tasks.

Diego: ¿Qué tienes que hacer hoy? What do you have to do today?
Camila: Tengo que entregar un trabajo para mi clase de historia, y también debo escribirle un correo a mi profesor de matemáticas. I have to turn in an assignment for my history class, and I should also write an email to my math professor.
Diego: ¿Por qué le tienes que escribir? Why do you have to write to him?
Camila: Porque no entiendo la tarea. Voy a pedirle que me explique el problema otra vez. Because I don't understand the assignment. I'm going to ask him to explain the problem to me again.
Diego: Buena idea. Aquí en la oficina, hay que terminar el reporte antes de las tres. Good idea. Here in the office, we have to finish the report before three.
Camila: ¿Te ayudo? Puedo mandarte mis notas de la reunión. Can I help you? I can send you my notes from the meeting.
Diego: Sí, por favor. Si me las mandas ahora, puedo terminar más rápido. Yes, please. If you send them to me now, I can finish faster.
Camila: Perfecto. Te las mando en un minuto. Perfect. I'll send them to you in a minute.
  1. What two things does Camila have to do today?
  2. Why does Camila need to write to her math professor?
  3. What does Camila offer to send Diego, and which pronoun does she use?

Listening: Mensajes de la oficina

If no audio recording is available, ask a partner, instructor, or text-to-speech tool to read the script below aloud, or read it aloud to yourself slowly.

Asistente: Buenos días a todos. Les recuerdo que hoy hay que entregar los reportes antes de las tres. Diego, tú tienes que terminar el reporte de la reunión, y Camila, tú debes mandarle los documentos al profesor Ruiz.   3. What should Camila do, and to whom?

Writing Workshop

Complete the following linked writing tasks in order.

Tarea 1 — Mis obligaciones: Write three sentences describing your obligations today or this week, using tener que, deber, and hay que at least once each.

Tarea 2 — Comunicarse con alguien: Write two sentences describing something you need to tell, explain, send, or give to someone else, using an indirect object pronoun (me, te, le, nos, les) with a verb like escribir, explicar, mandar, or dar.

Model sentence patterns
Tengo que estudiar para mi examen de español.
Le mando un mensaje a mi compañero de clase.
Hay que terminar el proyecto antes del lunes.
Use patterns like these as models for your own sentences.

Tarea 3 — Un correo breve: Combine your sentences into a short email (six to eight sentences) to a classmate or coworker, describing your obligations for the week and telling them something you need to send or explain to them. Include at least two obligation expressions and at least one indirect object pronoun.

  1. Choose your recipient. Decide who is receiving your email and why.
  2. List your obligations first. Write them in the infinitive before conjugating.
  3. Choose the right obligation expression. Decide between personal, advisory, and general obligation for each item.
  4. Add at least one indirect object pronoun. Check that it matches the person receiving the action.
  5. Combine everything into one connected email. Add a greeting and a closing.
  6. Read your email aloud once. Listen for smooth linking in tengo que and hay que.
  7. Check every obligation expression and pronoun placement.

Lista de revisión — Editing checklist:

  • Tener que, deber, and hay que are each used correctly and appropriately.
  • Hay que is not conjugated and has no subject pronoun.
  • Indirect object pronouns match the person receiving the action.
  • No double-object combination (such as se lo) has been produced; that pattern is recognition-only at this stage.
  • Accent marks, punctuation, and capitalization are correct throughout.
  • No vocabulary or grammar from outside Chapters 1–17 has been used.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Email and messaging norms at school and work

In many Spanish-speaking academic and workplace settings, emails to professors or supervisors commonly open with a greeting such as Estimado/a or Hola plus the person's name or title, and close with a polite expression such as Saludos or Gracias de antemano. Expectations about formality, response time, and directness vary by institution, country, and generation; this is a common tendency in some settings, not a fixed rule for every workplace.

Regional Variation — Alternatives to hay que

Some varieties of Spanish also use toca + infinitivo or se debe + infinitivo to express general obligation, with slightly different shades of formality. This course treats hay que + infinitivo as the core productive pattern for impersonal obligation; other expressions appear here only as recognition-only background information.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Conjugating hay que

Incorrect: Hayo que estudiar. / Tengo que hay estudiar.

Correct: Hay que estudiar. Hay que never changes form and never takes a subject pronoun; it always stays exactly as hay que plus an infinitive.

Common Mistake — Confusing tener que with deber

Overused: Debo entregar la tarea o pierdo la clase.

More natural: Tengo que entregar la tarea o pierdo la clase. Reserve deber for advice or a softer "should," and use tener que when the obligation is strong, specific, and has real consequences.

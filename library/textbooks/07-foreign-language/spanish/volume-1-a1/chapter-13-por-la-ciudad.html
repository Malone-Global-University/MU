Por la ciudad

Around the City · Speak and Write Spanish: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Name common city places and services with correct gender and articles.
  2. Ask for and understand basic directions in a city.
  3. Use ir and venir to talk about going and coming.
  4. Distinguish pedir (to ask for/request) from preguntar (to ask a question).
  5. Give simple instructions using selected affirmative tú commands.
  6. Manage a short direction-giving interaction in speech and writing.

This chapter introduces new productive vocabulary for the city and directions, the verbs ir and venir, the pedir/preguntar contrast, and a small set of affirmative tú commands. Chapters 1–12 vocabulary and grammar remain active throughout, including comparisons, expanded demonstratives, and object pronouns attached to infinitives from Chapter 12.

Communication Scenario

Diego is lost. He needs to get from the plaza to the train station to meet a friend, and he stops a stranger on the street to ask for directions. Later, Elena calls Marisol to explain how to come to her apartment for the first time.

  1. You will learn vocabulary for city places, services, and directions.
  2. You will learn to ask for and give simple directions using ir, venir, and affirmative commands.
  3. You will practice a complete direction-giving interaction in speech and writing.

Common Mistake — Confusing ida and vuelta prepositions

Incorrect: El banco está cerca la farmacia.

Correct: El banco está cerca de la farmacia. Location expressions such as cerca, lejos, al lado, and enfrente require de before the noun they relate to, just like the prepositions of place from Chapter 8.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation — Intonation in requests and directions

When asking for directions, Spanish speakers typically use a gently rising intonation on information questions like ¿Cómo llego a la estación?, similar to other question patterns you have practiced. When giving directions with commands, keep the stress on the correct syllable of the command form: dobla (stress on the first syllable) and sigue (stress on the first syllable), not on the final syllable.

Also review linking across word boundaries from Chapter 12: practice saying Sigue_todo_derecho as one smooth phrase.

Grammar: Ir and Venir

Purpose: talk about going to a place and coming to a place when giving or asking for directions.

Ir (to go) and venir (to come) are both irregular in the present tense. You have used ir a + infinitive since Chapter 14... (review) — in this chapter, you will use the full present-tense conjugation of both verbs to talk about movement toward and away from a place.

Language Focus — Ir versus venir depends on the speaker's viewpoint

Ir describes movement away from the speaker's current location or toward a third location. Venir describes movement toward the speaker's current location. Compare: Voy al mercado (I'm going to the market, away from here) and ¿Vienes a mi casa? (Are you coming to my house, toward where I am?).

  • ¿Cómo voy a la estación de tren? How do I get to the train station?
  • Vengo del banco ahora mismo. I'm coming from the bank right now.
  • ¿Vienes conmigo a la biblioteca? Are you coming with me to the library?

Grammar: Pedir versus Preguntar

Purpose: correctly ask for something you need versus asking a question, both common needs when navigating a city.

You learned the stem-changing verb pedir (e→i) in Chapter 11 in the context of ordering food. Pedir means to ask for or request something. Preguntar means to ask a question. English uses "ask" for both meanings, but Spanish requires a choice.

Pedir vs. preguntar
VerbMeaningExample
pedirto ask for / request (something)Voy a pedir direcciones.
preguntarto ask (a question)Voy a preguntar dónde está el banco.
  • Le pido ayuda al policía. I ask the police officer for help.
  • Le pregunto cómo llegar al hospital. I ask him/her how to get to the hospital. preguntar + information question
Common Mistake — Using preguntar for a request

Incorrect: Voy a preguntar direcciones.

Correct: Voy a pedir direcciones. Because you are requesting information, not simply posing a question with an interrogative word, pedir is the correct choice here.

Grammar: Selected Affirmative Tú Commands

Purpose: give simple, friendly instructions when telling someone how to get somewhere.

This chapter activates a small, controlled set of affirmative tú commands for giving directions. Negative commands and usted/ustedes commands are not active yet and will be developed in later chapters.

Building Block — Regular affirmative tú commands
él/ella present-tense form = affirmative tú command
For most regular verbs, the affirmative tú command has the same form as the él/ella present-tense form. This pattern applies to doblar, seguir, and cruzar below.
Dobla a la izquierda en la esquina.
Turn left at the corner.

Language Focus — Ve is irregular

Ve is the irregular affirmative tú command form of ir. It does not follow the él/ella pattern. This chapter activates only ve as a formulaic irregular command; the full set of irregular affirmative tú commands will be developed in a later volume.

  • Sigue todo derecho por esta calle. Continue straight ahead down this street.
  • Cruza el puente y dobla a la derecha. Cross the bridge and turn right.
  • Ve hasta la plaza y pregunta allí. Go to the plaza and ask there.

Pronoun attachment to affirmative commands is not required for production in this chapter and will be developed once that pattern is scheduled in a later chapter.

Model Dialogue: ¿Cómo llego?

Context: Diego is lost near the plaza and stops a stranger to ask for directions to the train station.

Diego: Perdón, ¿le puedo pedir ayuda? Busco la estación de tren. Excuse me, can I ask you for help? I'm looking for the train station.
Señora: Claro. ¿Cómo llegas? ¿Vienes a pie o en autobús? Of course. How are you getting there? Are you coming on foot or by bus?
Diego: A pie. ¿Está lejos de aquí? On foot. Is it far from here?
Señora: No, está cerca. Sigue todo derecho por esta calle, cruza el puente, y dobla a la izquierda en la farmacia. No, it's close. Continue straight down this street, cross the bridge, and turn left at the pharmacy.
Diego: Sigo derecho, cruzo el puente, y doblo a la izquierda. ¿Y después? I go straight, cross the bridge, and turn left. And after that?
Señora: La estación está enfrente del banco, al lado de la plaza pequeña. The station is in front of the bank, next to the small plaza.
Diego: Muchas gracias. Una pregunta más: ¿hay un banco cerca también? Thank you very much. One more question: is there a bank nearby too?
Señora: Sí, ya te dije. El banco está justo enfrente de la estación. Yes, I already told you. The bank is right in front of the station.
Diego le da las gracias y camina hacia el puente.
Diego: ¡Perfecto! Voy para allá ahora. Gracias otra vez. Perfect! I'm heading there now. Thanks again.

Notice how ir, venir, pedir, preguntar, and affirmative tú commands appear together in a natural direction-giving sequence, alongside review vocabulary and location prepositions from Chapter 8.

Speaking Practice

Práctica controlada — Controlled practice: ¿Dónde queda?

Say four sentences describing where a city place is located, using cerca de, lejos de, al lado de, or enfrente de.

Tarea comunicativa — Communicative task: Perdón, ¿le puedo pedir ayuda?

With a partner, role-play a lost pedestrian and a helpful stranger. The pedestrian asks how to get somewhere using ¿Cómo llego a...?, and the stranger gives directions using at least two affirmative tú commands from this chapter.

Proyecto oral — Oral project: Mi barrio

Describe your neighborhood: what places are nearby, and how someone could walk from your home to one of them.

Práctica independiente — Solo alternative

Record or say aloud a short set of directions from one familiar place to another, using at least two commands and one location expression.

Reading Practice

Un mensaje para un amigo — A message to a friend

Hola, Marisol. Aquí tienes las direcciones para llegar a mi apartamento. Ven en autobús hasta la parada del mercado. Después, sigue todo derecho por la calle Bolívar y cruza el puente pequeño. Dobla a la izquierda en la farmacia; mi edificio está al lado de la iglesia, enfrente del parque. Si tienes una pregunta, puedes pedirle ayuda a cualquier persona en la calle; todos conocen mi barrio. ¡Nos vemos pronto!

Tarea de lectura — Reading task:

  1. Where should Marisol get off the bus, according to the message?
  2. What two actions does the message tell her to do after getting off the bus?
  3. Where exactly is the friend's building located?

Lista de revisión — Editing checklist:

  • Affirmative tú commands are formed correctly, including the irregular ve.
  • Location expressions use de where required (cerca de, al lado de, enfrente de).

  • Ir and venir are conjugated correctly and match the intended direction of movement.
  • City vocabulary includes correct articles and gender agreement.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Asking strangers for directions

In many Spanish-speaking cities, asking a stranger for directions on the street is a normal, everyday interaction, often opened with a polite phrase such as perdón or disculpe. Directions are frequently given using nearby landmarks, such as a pharmacy or a church, rather than exact addresses, so learning place vocabulary supports real navigation.

Regional Variation — Words for city blocks and bus stops

Vocabulary for city navigation varies by country: la cuadra is common across much of Latin America for a city block, while el bloque or other regional terms may appear elsewhere. Words for a bus stop also vary, including la parada, used widely, alongside other regional terms in specific countries. This course uses la cuadra and la parada as core productive forms under the Latin American baseline; other regional terms are recognition-only at this stage.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using the infinitive instead of a command

Incorrect: Doblar a la izquierda.

Correct: Dobla a la izquierda. When giving a direct instruction to someone you address as tú, use the affirmative command form, not the infinitive.

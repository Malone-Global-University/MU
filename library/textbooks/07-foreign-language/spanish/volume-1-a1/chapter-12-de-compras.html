De compras

Going Shopping · Speak and Write Spanish: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Name common clothing items, colors, and shopping vocabulary with correct gender and articles.
  2. Ask about and state prices using numbers you already know.
  3. Compare two or more items using más... que, menos... que, tan... como, and irregular comparatives such as mejor and peor.
  4. Point out specific items using the expanded demonstrative series ese/esa/esos/esas and aquel/aquella/aquellos/aquellas.
  5. Attach a direct-object pronoun to an infinitive when talking about wanting or needing to buy something.
  6. Manage a simple shopping interaction in speech and writing, from browsing to paying.

This chapter introduces new productive vocabulary for shopping and clothing, comparisons, the expanded demonstrative series, and pronoun attachment to infinitives. Chapters 1–11 vocabulary and grammar remain active throughout, including the direct-object pronouns and stem-changing verbs from Chapter 11.

Communication Scenario

After lunch, Elena, Marisol, and Diego walk past a small clothing store near the plaza. Elena needs a new jacket for the trip she is planning, and Marisol offers her opinion on colors, sizes, and prices while Diego waits and comments on everything.

  1. You will learn vocabulary for clothing, colors, and shopping transactions.
  2. You will learn to compare items and point out specific ones using expanded demonstratives.
  3. You will practice a complete shopping interaction in speech and writing.

Common Mistake — Color agreement

Incorrect: una chaqueta negro

Correct: una chaqueta negra. Colors that end in -o must agree in gender and number with the noun they describe, just like other adjectives. Colors such as azul and gris do not change for gender but still agree in number: los suéteres azules.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation — Linking vowels across word boundaries

When a word ending in a vowel is followed by a word beginning with the same or a similar vowel sound, Spanish speakers typically link the words smoothly rather than pausing between them. Practice saying ¿Cuánto_cuesta_esta_chaqueta? as one flowing phrase instead of four separate words.

Also review the stress difference between éste-type forms you will see labeled in older materials and the modern spelling este without an accent, which this course uses consistently, as explained in the grammar section below.

Grammar: Comparisons

Purpose: compare two or more items when shopping, choosing, or giving an opinion.

Building Block — más/menos... que
más + adjective + que · menos + adjective + que
Use más... que for "more... than" and menos... que for "less... than." The adjective still agrees in gender and number with the noun it describes.
Esta chaqueta es más cara que esa.
This jacket is more expensive than that one.

Use tan... como to say that two things are equal: Esta camisa es tan bonita como aquella. (This shirt is as pretty as that one over there.)

Comparativos irregulares — Irregular comparatives
SpanishEnglish
mejor / mejoresbetter
peor / peoresworse
mayor / mayoresolder, greater
menor / menoresyounger, lesser
  • Estos zapatos son mejores que los otros. These shoes are better than the other ones.
  • El precio de la tienda pequeña es peor que el precio aquí. The small store's price is worse than the price here.
  • Esta talla es tan grande como la otra. This size is as big as the other one. tan + adjective + como = equality
Language Focus — Irregular comparatives do not use más or menos

Do not say más mejor or más peor. Mejor and peor already mean "better" and "worse," so they replace más bueno and más malo rather than combining with más.

Grammar: Expanded Demonstratives

Purpose: point out specific items at different distances while browsing or comparing products.

Demostrativos — este, ese, aquel
DistanceMasculine/Feminine SingularMasculine/Feminine Plural
Near the speaker (review)este / estaestos / estas
Near the listenerese / esaesos / esas
Far from bothaquel / aquellaaquellos / aquellas

Ese/esa/esos/esas points to something near the person you are speaking to. Aquel/aquella/aquellos/aquellas points to something farther from both speakers, often across the room or in the distance.

  • ¿Te gusta esa camisa que tienes en la mano? Do you like that shirt you have in your hand?
  • Prefiero aquellos zapatos, los del escaparate. I prefer those shoes over there, the ones in the window.
Language Focus — Modern accent policy for demonstratives

Current Spanish spelling convention no longer requires a written accent on demonstrative pronouns such as éste or ése to distinguish them from demonstrative adjectives. This course consistently uses the unaccented forms este, ese, and aquel for both functions, following current orthographic guidance.

Grammar: Object Pronouns with Infinitives

Purpose: talk about wanting, needing, or planning to buy or try on something without repeating the noun.

Direct-object pronouns learned in Chapter 11 (lo, la, los, las) can also attach to the end of an infinitive when the infinitive follows a conjugated verb such as querer, necesitar, or poder. This chapter activates only this one new attachment context; attachment to gerunds and commands remains for later chapters.

Building Block — pronoun + infinitive
verbo conjugado + infinitivo + pronombre
The pronoun may attach directly to the end of the infinitive, forming one written word. No accent mark is required for this attachment pattern.
Necesito probarlo antes de comprarlo.
I need to try it on before buying it.
  • ¿Quieres comprarla? Do you want to buy it? la attaches to comprar
  • Podemos pagarlos con tarjeta. We can pay for them by card.
Common Mistake — Placing the pronoun in the wrong spot

Incorrect: Quiero la comprar.

Correct: Quiero comprarla. When a conjugated verb is followed by an infinitive, the pronoun attaches to the end of the infinitive; it does not go before the conjugated verb in this pattern.

Attachment to gerunds and affirmative commands is not required for production in this chapter and will be developed in later chapters once those structures are active.

Model Dialogue: En la tienda

Context: Elena, Marisol, and Diego enter a small clothing store. Elena is looking for a jacket for her upcoming trip.

Vendedor: Buenas tardes. ¿Buscan algo en especial? Good afternoon. Are you looking for something in particular?
Elena: Sí, busco una chaqueta para un viaje. Yes, I'm looking for a jacket for a trip.
Vendedor: Tenemos esta chaqueta azul y esa chaqueta gris. ¿Cuál prefiere? We have this blue jacket and that gray jacket. Which do you prefer?
Marisol: La azul es más bonita, pero la gris es más práctica. The blue one is prettier, but the gray one is more practical.
Elena: ¿Cuánto cuesta la gris? How much does the gray one cost?
Vendedor: Cuesta cuarenta dólares, pero hoy hay una rebaja. Aquella chaqueta negra, allá al fondo, es mejor precio. It costs forty dollars, but today there's a discount. That black jacket over there in the back is a better price.
Elena camina hacia el fondo de la tienda y mira la chaqueta negra.
Elena: Me gusta. ¿La puedo probar? I like it. Can I try it on?
Vendedor: Claro, el probador está a la derecha. Of course, the fitting room is on the right.
Elena regresa con la chaqueta puesta.
Diego: Te queda muy bien. Es mejor que la azul, en mi opinión. It fits you very well. It's better than the blue one, in my opinion.
Elena: Tienes razón. Quiero comprarla. ¿Aceptan tarjeta? You're right. I want to buy it. Do you accept cards?
Vendedor: Sí, aceptamos tarjeta y efectivo. Yes, we accept card and cash.

Notice how comparisons, expanded demonstratives, and a direct-object pronoun attached to an infinitive appear together in a natural shopping sequence, alongside review vocabulary and direct-object pronouns from Chapter 11.

Listening Practice

If no audio recording is available, ask a partner, instructor, or text-to-speech tool to read the script below aloud, or read it aloud to yourself slowly.

Listening Script for Instructor, Partner, or Text-to-Speech Use
Atención, clientes. Hoy tenemos una rebaja especial en la sección de ropa. Los suéteres cuestan menos que ayer, y las camisetas son más baratas que la semana pasada. Esta chaqueta negra es mejor precio que aquella chaqueta azul del escaparate. Si buscan zapatos, tenemos números del treinta y seis al cuarenta y dos. Recuerden que el probador está a la derecha y que aceptamos tarjeta y efectivo. Gracias por su visita.
Attention, customers. Today we have a special discount in the clothing section. Sweaters cost less than yesterday, and T-shirts are cheaper than last week. This black jacket is a better price than that blue jacket in the window. If you're looking for shoes, we have sizes thirty-six to forty-two. Remember that the fitting room is on the right and that we accept card and cash. Thank you for visiting.
  1. Listen for which items are cheaper today and compared to when.
  2. Listen for the comparison between the black jacket and the blue jacket.
  3. Listen for the shoe sizes available and the accepted payment methods.

Speaking Practice

Práctica controlada — Controlled practice: ¿Cuál prefieres?

Say four sentences comparing two items of clothing, using más... que, menos... que, or tan... como.

Tarea comunicativa — Communicative task: En la tienda

With a partner, role-play a salesperson and a customer. The customer compares two items using demonstratives (este/ese/aquel) and decides to buy one, using a direct-object pronoun attached to an infinitive (Quiero comprarlo).

Proyecto oral — Oral project: Mi ropa favorita

Describe your favorite piece of clothing: its color, why you like it, and how it compares to another item you own.

Práctica independiente — Solo alternative

Record or say aloud a short shopping scene in which you compare two items, point one out with a demonstrative, and state that you want to buy it.

Writing Workshop

Complete the following linked writing tasks in order. Together they form the written half of this chapter's practice.

Tarea 1 — Comparaciones: Write three sentences comparing two clothing items you know, using más... que, menos... que, or an irregular comparative such as mejor or peor.

Tarea 2 — Usar demostrativos: Complete each sentence with the correct demonstrative, based on the distance described.

Complete with este, ese, or aquel, adjusted for gender and number
1. (near you) ______ camisa que tienes es bonita.
2. (near the listener) ¿Prefieres ______ zapatos que tú tienes?
3. (far away, across the store) ______ chaqueta, allá al fondo, cuesta menos.
4. (near you, plural) ______ colores son mis favoritos.
Fill in each blank with the demonstrative adjective that matches the distance and the gender/number of the noun.

Tarea 3 — Un pedido completo: Combine your sentences from Tareas 1 and 2 into one connected paragraph of six to eight sentences describing a shopping trip, including at least one comparison, one expanded demonstrative, and one object pronoun attached to an infinitive.

  1. Write your comparison sentences first. This reviews clothing vocabulary and colors.
  2. Complete the demonstrative exercise. Check gender and number agreement.
  3. Combine everything into one connected paragraph. Use a shopping scenario as your frame.
  4. Read your paragraph aloud once. Listen for correct stress and linking between words.
  5. Check every agreement, comparison, and pronoun attachment. Confirm the pronoun is attached correctly to the infinitive, not placed before the conjugated verb.

Lista de revisión — Editing checklist:

  • Comparisons use más/menos... que or tan... como correctly.
  • Irregular comparatives (mejor, peor, mayor, menor) are not combined with más.
  • Demonstratives agree in gender and number and match the intended distance.
  • Object pronouns attached to infinitives form one written word with no added accent.
  • Colors and clothing vocabulary include correct articles and gender agreement.
  • No vocabulary or grammar from outside Chapters 1–12 has been used.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Shopping conversations and opinions

Asking a friend or family member for an honest opinion while shopping is common across many Spanish-speaking communities, though how directly that opinion is given can vary by relationship, region, and personal style. Comparative language like es mejor que or te queda bien is a natural, everyday way to offer that kind of feedback without it being especially formal or especially blunt.

Regional Variation — Words for clothing and money

Clothing vocabulary and currency terms vary by country: for example, la playera is common in Mexico alongside la camiseta; el pulóver or el buzo may appear for sweater-type garments in some countries. Currency names and prices also differ by country and change over time, so specific prices in this chapter are illustrative rather than current market prices. This course uses la camiseta and generic dollar amounts as core productive forms under the Latin American baseline; other regional terms are recognition-only at this stage.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using más with irregular comparatives

Incorrect: Esta chaqueta es más mejor que esa.

Correct: Esta chaqueta es mejor que esa. Mejor already carries the comparative meaning, so it never combines with más.

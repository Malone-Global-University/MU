9

اللُّغَة القَانُونِيَّة وَالإِدَارِيَّة لِغَيْرِ المُتَخَصِّصِينَChapter 9: Legal and Administrative Language for General Purposes

Rights and obligations vocabulary, forms, restrictions, and boundaries · Speak and Write Arabic: Professional, Academic, Media, and Institutional Arabic

Chapter Overview

Chapter 8 gave you the institutional vocabulary and careful-claim tools to describe a public issue and its stakeholders responsibly. This chapter turns from the civic issue itself to the paperwork and language that surrounds it: the general vocabulary of rights, obligations, forms, and restrictions that a non-specialist encounters when dealing with an institution — filling out a form, understanding what is required of them, or reading a notice about what is and is not permitted. This is not a legal-specialist chapter. It stays within the general, everyday administrative Arabic that any resident, employee, or member of the public needs, and it treats the passive voice and impersonal institutional style — recycled from earlier chapters at a now-specialized level — as tools for describing requirements and restrictions without naming who is enforcing them. No new grammar system is introduced. Content clauses, passive constructions, and institutional register, already active since Unit I, are expanded into the bounded, general-purpose domain of rights, obligations, and administrative forms.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Use general vocabulary for rights, obligations, permissions, and restrictions in an institutional context.
  2. Read and understand the basic fields and instructions of a general administrative form.
  3. Recognize and produce impersonal passive constructions that state a requirement or restriction without naming an enforcing party.
  4. Distinguish what a form or notice requires from what it merely permits or prohibits.
  5. Read a short institutional notice about a restriction or requirement and identify who it applies to and what it demands.
  6. Write a short, clear message asking about a right, an obligation, or a required document, and complete a simple general-purpose form accurately.

النُّطْق — Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus: Reading Form Instructions and Notices Aloud

Pronunciation Note — Reading a form field or instruction aloud

Administrative forms are often written in short, dense phrases rather than full sentences, so reading them aloud requires deliberately supplying the phrase grouping that the layout does not spell out. Read each field label as its own phrase, pause slightly, then read any instructional text that follows as a separate group — for example, treating الشَّرْط الأَوَّل and the requirement that follows it as two distinct phrase units rather than running them together. This is the same measured, formal connected reading practiced in Unit I and Chapter 8, applied here to the shorter, more clipped phrasing typical of forms.

التَّرْكِيب — Grammar and Sentence Patterns

Grammar and Sentence Patterns: Stating Requirements and Restrictions Impersonally

This chapter introduces no new grammar system. It expands the passive voice and impersonal institutional style — already authorized since Unit I and reaching specialized (S) status in this bounded administrative domain — into the specific task of stating what is required, permitted, or prohibited without naming who is enforcing it.

Expanded Pattern — Stating an obligation impersonally (recycles content clauses, Unit I)
يَجِب عَلَى + (person/party) + أَن + (verb) ...
This pattern states an obligation directly on the party who holds it, without describing who created the requirement, which is typical of general-purpose administrative language.
يَجِب عَلَى مُقَدِّم الطَّلَب أَن يُرْفِق نُسْخَة مِن المُسْتَنَدَات المَطْلُوبَة.
The applicant must attach a copy of the required documents.
Expanded Pattern — Marking permission or prohibition impersonally (recycles passive, Ch. 3-4)
يُسْمَح بِـ ... / لَا يُسْمَح بِـ ... / يُمْنَع ...
The impersonal passive removes the enforcing agent from the sentence, focusing attention on the rule itself rather than on who is imposing it — the same agent-omission pattern used for public-information passives since Chapter 3.
لَا يُسْمَح بِتَقْدِيم الطَّلَب بَعْدَ المَوْعِد النِّهَائِيّ إِلَّا بِمُوَافَقَة خَاصَّة.
Submitting the request after the deadline is not permitted except with special approval.
Expanded Pattern — Naming a condition for a right or permit (recycles relative and conditional clauses, Unit I)
يَحِقّ لِـ ... أَن ... بِشَرْط أَن ...
This pattern grants a right or permission to a named party while attaching a specific condition, keeping the right and its boundary in the same sentence rather than leaving the condition implied.
يَحِقّ لِلمُوَظَّف أَن يَحْصُل عَلَى تَصْرِيح وُقُوف، بِشَرْط أَن يُقَدِّم الطَّلَب قَبْل المَوْعِد النِّهَائِيّ.
An employee has the right to obtain a parking permit, provided that they submit the request before the deadline.

Comparing a right, an obligation, and a restriction on the same matter:

يَحِقّ لِكُلّ مُوَظَّف أَن يَتَقَدَّم بِطَلَب تَصْرِيح وُقُوف.

A right — what a party is entitled to do.

يَجِب عَلَى مُقَدِّم الطَّلَب أَن يُرْفِق مُسْتَنَدَيْن اثْنَيْن عَلَى الأَقَلّ.

An obligation — what a party is required to do.

لَا يُسْمَح بِتَقْدِيم أَكْثَر مِن طَلَب وَاحِد فِي السَّنَة.

A restriction — what is not permitted, stated impersonally.

Notice that each sentence keeps the right, the obligation, and the restriction in a distinct, formulaic pattern, which prevents a reader from confusing something they are entitled to do with something they are required to do, or with something that is simply not allowed.

Gloss: Every employee at the center has the right to submit a request for a parking permit for the current year. The applicant must attach a copy of the work card and proof of residence, and must submit the complete form before the deadline. Submitting more than one request per year is not permitted except with special approval from the office. Nor is accepting any request after the deadline permitted. All employees are asked to review the form's conditions carefully before signing, since no amendment is permitted after the request has been submitted.

Writing Workshop — A Short Inquiry and a Simple Form

In this task, you will write a short message asking about a right, an obligation, or a required document, and then complete a simple general-purpose form.

  1. Step 1 — State your situation briefly. Name what you are requesting or asking about, in one short sentence.
  2. Step 2 — Ask about a right or an obligation. Use هَل يَحِقّ لِي أَن ... or هَل يَجِب عَلَيَّ أَن ... to ask your question clearly.
  3. Step 3 — Ask about a restriction if relevant. Use هَل يُسْمَح بِـ ... or هَل هُنَاك مَوْعِد نِهَائِيّ لِـ ... to check any boundary that applies.
  4. Step 4 — Complete a simple form. Fill in the fields of a short general-purpose form (name, request type, required documents, signature line) using the vocabulary from this chapter.
  5. Review your inquiry and form against the checklist below.

قَائِمَة المُرَاجَعَة — Checklist:

  • My inquiry states my situation clearly before asking my question.
  • I distinguished a right, an obligation, and a restriction, and did not confuse them.
  • My passive or impersonal statements do not name an unnecessary enforcing party.
  • The form fields I completed match the required-document vocabulary from this chapter.
  • The inquiry and form remain consistently formal institutional register throughout.
  • The inquiry reads naturally when read aloud.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Administrative language and everyday life

General-purpose administrative Arabic — the language of forms, permits, and notices — is part of daily life across Arabic-speaking institutions, workplaces, and public offices, regardless of country or legal system. Learning to read a form's requirements, ask about a right or obligation clearly, and understand a restriction without over-interpreting it is a practical skill that transfers across many everyday institutional settings, from a university registrar to a workplace human-resources office.

Regional Variation — Administrative vocabulary and specific legal systems

The general vocabulary in this chapter — الحَقّ, الوَاجِب, الاسْتِمَارَة, التَّصْرِيح — is usable across most Arabic-speaking administrative contexts, but the specific legal terminology, document names, and procedures of any one country's legal system are considerably more specialized and vary significantly by jurisdiction. This chapter remains at the general, non-specialist level; named legal systems, formal legal registers, and jurisdiction-specific terminology are treated as bounded, specialized (S) content reserved for later, more advanced study.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Confusing a right with an obligation

Error: writing يَجِب عَلَى المُوَظَّف أَن يَحْصُل عَلَى تَصْرِيح وُقُوف when obtaining the permit is optional, not required.

Correction: use the right pattern instead: يَحِقّ لِلمُوَظَّف أَن يَحْصُل عَلَى تَصْرِيح وُقُوف.

Common Mistake — Naming an unnecessary enforcing party in a restriction

Error: writing المَكْتَب يَمْنَع المُوَظَّفِين مِن تَقْدِيم أَكْثَر مِن طَلَب when the general, impersonal restriction is more appropriate for a public notice.

Correction: state the restriction impersonally: لَا يُسْمَح بِتَقْدِيم أَكْثَر مِن طَلَب وَاحِد فِي السَّنَة.

Common Mistake — Treating a general form as a specialized legal document

Error: assuming that everyday administrative vocabulary such as الشَّرْط or القُيُود carries the same precise meaning as a specialized legal term in a contract or statute.

Correction: keep general-purpose forms and notices distinct from specialized legal or contractual language, and consult a qualified professional for any matter involving an actual legal document or dispute.

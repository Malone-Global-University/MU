مُرَاجَعَة ٣: مَهَمَّة بَحْثِيَّةChapter 15: Unit III Assessment

A research-based written and oral task · Speak and Write Arabic: Professional, Academic, Media, and Institutional Arabic

Chapter Overview

This chapter is the Unit III assessment. It introduces no new grammar, morphology, or vocabulary system. Instead, it asks you to bring together everything أَمَل practiced across Chapters 11–14: framing and attributing a source (Chapter 11), writing an organized academic paragraph with a defensible claim (Chapter 12), delivering that claim as a formal prepared presentation and handling questions (Chapter 13), and mediating the same finding for a specialist and a non-specialist listener (Chapter 14). In this chapter, you complete a single integrated research dossier of your own: a short source-based written report, a prepared oral presentation of it, and two mediated explanations for different audiences. Every system used here remains at the status already authorized in Chapters 11–14; this chapter's purpose is synthesis and evidence, not new instruction.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Select and frame a source using the citation and attribution language from Chapter 11.
  2. Write an organized academic paragraph with a claim, evidence, and a closing observation, as practiced in Chapter 12.
  3. Deliver a short formal presentation of that paragraph and respond to at least one follow-up question, as practiced in Chapter 13.
  4. Mediate the same finding for a specialist listener and a non-specialist listener, signaling the register shift and checking comprehension, as practiced in Chapter 14.
  5. Listen to and read integrated research-based material and answer synthesis questions that draw on multiple chapters at once.
  6. Evaluate your own dossier against a rubric that spans source use, organization, delivery, and audience adaptation.

Opening Communication Scenario

رَامِي tells أَمَل that her department has asked her to submit a complete "research dossier" before the end of the month: a short written report with a properly framed source, a recorded or live presentation of the same material, and two short mediated explanations — one for a specialist colleague and one for the department director. Nothing new needs to be learned for this task. أَمَل realizes that everything required is already something she has practiced: choosing and attributing a source, organizing a paragraph around a claim, presenting it formally, and adapting it for two audiences. The dossier simply asks her to do all four at once, for one topic of her choosing.

  1. You will review, in condensed form, the citation, paragraph, presentation, and mediation language from Chapters 11–14.
  2. You will listen to and read an integrated model dossier that shows all four skills used together.
  3. You will assemble your own dossier: a short written report, a presentation, and two mediated explanations.
  4. You will evaluate your dossier against a rubric before submitting it.

مِلَفّ التَّقْيِيم — Assessment Dossier

The Assessment Dossier: What You Will Submit

This chapter does not introduce new grammar or morphology. It requires you to assemble and submit four connected components built entirely from Chapters 11–14 systems, unified around a single topic of your choosing.

المُكَوِّن ١ — Component 1: Sourced report

A short written report (one organized paragraph) that frames and attributes at least one source, following the citation language from Chapter 11 and the paragraph organization from Chapter 12.

المُكَوِّن ٢ — Component 2: Formal presentation

A short prepared oral presentation (about one minute) of the same report, using the presentation-framing language from Chapter 13, plus a response to at least one follow-up question.

المُكَوِّن ٣ — Component 3: Specialist mediation

A short spoken or written explanation of the same finding for a specialist listener, preserving full technical detail, using أَمَّا بِالنِّسْبَة لِلتَّفَاصِيل التِّقْنِيَّة، فَـ...

المُكَوِّن ٤ — Component 4: Non-specialist mediation

A short plain-language explanation of the same finding, ending with هَل هَذَا وَاضِح؟ and including at least one repair using بِمَعْنَى آخَر، أَقْصِد أَنَّ...

Assessment Note — No new systems in this chapter

Every phrase and pattern required for the dossier was already introduced and consolidated in Chapters 11–14. This chapter's task is to combine them accurately and fluently around one topic, not to learn anything new.

Listening Assessment — An Integrated Model Dossier

Ask your instructor, a partner, or a text-to-speech tool to read the following short model dossier aloud. It moves through sourced claim, presentation framing, and both mediated explanations in sequence, exactly as your own dossier should.

تُشِير دِرَاسَة حَدِيثَة إِلَى أَنَّ العَمَل عَن بُعْد يُقَلِّل انْبِعَاثَات النَّقْل بِنِسْبَة كَبِيرَة فِي المُدُن الكَبِيرَة. أَبْدَأ عَرْضِي اليَوْم بِهَذِه النُّقْطَة، ثُمَّ أَنْتَقِل إِلَى مَا يَعْنِيه ذَلِك عَمَلِيًّا. أَمَّا بِالنِّسْبَة لِلتَّفَاصِيل التِّقْنِيَّة، فَالدِّرَاسَة قَارَنَت انْبِعَاثَات المُوَظَّفِين الَّذِين يَعْمَلُون عَن بُعْد ثَلَاثَة أَيَّام أُسْبُوعِيًّا بِزُمَلَائِهِم الَّذِين يَذْهَبُون إِلَى المَكْتَب يَوْمِيًّا. بِاخْتِصَار شَدِيد، مَا يَعْنِيه هَذَا عَمَلِيًّا هُوَ أَنَّ الشَّرِكَة يُمْكِنها تَقْلِيل بَصْمَتها البِيئِيَّة دُون تَقْلِيل الإِنْتَاجِيَّة. هَل هَذَا وَاضِح؟ زَمِيل: تَقْرِيبًا، هَل تَقْصِدِين أَنَّ كُلّ المُوَظَّفِين يَجِب أَن يَعْمَلُوا مِن المَنْزِل؟ المُتَحَدِّثَة: بِمَعْنَى آخَر، أَقْصِد أَنَّ يَوْمَيْن أَو ثَلَاثَة فِي الأُسْبُوع كَافِيَة لِتَحْقِيق فَائِدَة وَاضِحَة، وَلَيْس كُلّ الأَيَّام.

مُهِمَّة الاسْتِمَاع — Listening task

  1. Which phrase frames the sourced claim, and which phrase opens the presentation?
  2. Which phrase signals the shift to technical detail, and which phrase compresses the finding into a plain-language summary?
  3. What misunderstanding does the colleague have, and how is it repaired?

Reading Assessment — A Written Dossier Excerpt

Read the short written excerpt below, which corresponds to Component 1 and Component 4 of the sample dossier. Answer the synthesis questions that follow.

تَقْرِير مُوجَز — A brief report

وَفْقًا لِدِرَاسَة نُشِرَت هَذَا العَام، يُسَاهِم العَمَل عَن بُعْد فِي تَقْلِيل الازْدِحَام المُرُورِيّ فِي سَاعَات الذُّرْوَة بِنِسْبَة مَلْحُوظَة. تُوَضِّح الدِّرَاسَة أَنَّ هَذَا الأَثَر يَظْهَر بِوُضُوح فِي المُدُن الَّتِي تَعْتَمِد بِشِدَّة عَلَى السَّيَّارَات الخَاصَّة. بِنَاءً عَلَى ذَلِك، يُمْكِن القَوْل إِنَّ سِيَاسَات العَمَل المَرِن قَد تَكُون أَدَاة فَعَّالَة لِتَخْفِيف ضَغْط النَّقْل الحَضَرِيّ دُون الحَاجَة إِلَى اسْتِثْمَارَات كَبِيرَة فِي البُنْيَة التَّحْتِيَّة.

بِاخْتِصَار شَدِيد لِمَن لَيْس لَدَيْه خَلْفِيَّة تِقْنِيَّة: مَا يَعْنِيه هَذَا عَمَلِيًّا هُوَ أَنَّ السَّمَاح لِلمُوَظَّفِين بِالعَمَل عَن بُعْد بِضَعَة أَيَّام فِي الأُسْبُوع يُقَلِّل الازْدِحَام دُون تَكْلِفَة إِضَافِيَّة عَلَى المَدِينَة. هَل هَذَا وَاضِح؟

أَسْئِلَة التَّرْكِيب — Synthesis questions:

  1. Identify the sourcing phrase that opens the report and name the citation function it performs (Chapter 11).
  2. Identify the sentence that functions as the paragraph's central claim and the sentence that functions as its closing observation (Chapter 12).
  3. Identify the phrase that signals the shift into the plain-language summary and explain what audience it is designed for (Chapter 14).
  4. Explain how the plain-language summary remains faithful to the technical claim rather than distorting it.
  5. If a specialist colleague asked for more detail about this report, which chapter's language would you use to answer, and how would you open that answer?

Writing Assessment — Assemble Your Dossier

Using a topic of your choosing (or the transportation/remote-work theme carried through Chapters 12–14), assemble the written portions of your dossier.

  1. Step 1 — Choose and frame your source. Select one source-like claim and introduce it using a Chapter 11 attribution phrase such as تُشِير الدِّرَاسَة إِلَى أَنَّ... or وَفْقًا لِـ...
  2. Step 2 — Write the organized paragraph. Write three to five sentences with a clear claim, supporting evidence, and a closing observation, following the Chapter 12 paragraph structure.
  3. Step 3 — Write the specialist mediation. Write one or two sentences beginning with أَمَّا بِالنِّسْبَة لِلتَّفَاصِيل التِّقْنِيَّة، فَـ... that preserve full detail.
  4. Step 4 — Write the plain-language mediation. Write one or two sentences using مَا يَعْنِيه هَذَا عَمَلِيًّا هُوَ أَنَّ... or بِاخْتِصَار شَدِيد, ending with هَل هَذَا وَاضِح؟
  5. Step 5 — Add a repair. Write one sentence beginning with بِمَعْنَى آخَر، أَقْصِد أَنَّ... that restates the plain-language point differently.
  6. Review your written dossier against the checklist below before moving to the speaking portion.

قَائِمَة المُرَاجَعَة — Checklist:

  • The source is clearly attributed using a Chapter 11 phrase.
  • The paragraph has a clear claim, evidence, and closing observation.
  • The specialist mediation preserves full technical detail.
  • The plain-language mediation states only the practical consequence and ends with a comprehension check.
  • A repair sentence restates the plain-language point in different words.
  • All four components describe the same underlying finding without contradiction.

Speaking Assessment — Present and Mediate

تَدْرِيب مُوَجَّه — Controlled practice: Read your dossier aloud

Before presenting to a partner, read your full written dossier aloud on your own, paying attention to pause and pitch at each register-shift phrase, as practiced in Chapters 13 and 14.

مُهِمَّة تَوَاصُل — Communicative task: Present, question, and mediate

Present your sourced paragraph to a partner as a short formal presentation (about one minute). Have your partner ask at least one follow-up question. Then deliver your specialist mediation to a partner playing a colleague, and your plain-language mediation to a partner playing a non-specialist supervisor, ending with هَل هَذَا وَاضِح؟ and responding to any follow-up with a repair.

لِلْمُتَعَلِّمِينَ المُسْتَقِلِّينَ — Solo alternative

Record all four components in sequence: the presentation, a specialist mediation, a plain-language mediation with a comprehension check, and a repair, as if answering an imagined follow-up question.

Scoring Rubric

Use this rubric to evaluate your own dossier, or ask an instructor or partner to evaluate it, before final submission.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — The research dossier as a professional genre

Across many professional and academic settings in the Arabic-speaking world, being asked to produce a written report, a presentation, and audience-adapted explanations for the same finding is a common expectation, much as it is in comparable English-language institutions. The dossier format used in this chapter reflects that broader professional convention rather than a single institution's requirement.

Regional Variation — Presentation and reporting conventions

The exact expectations for a written report or a formal presentation — length, level of formality, and how directly conclusions are stated — vary across institutions and countries. This chapter's dossier format is a general, transferable model rather than a fixed institutional template.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Letting the four components drift apart

Error: writing a sourced paragraph about one finding, then presenting or mediating a different or unrelated point.

Correction: keep all four components anchored to the same claim, for example the reduction in transportation-related emissions or congestion introduced in the sourced paragraph.

Common Mistake — Treating the assessment as a chance to add new grammar

Error: reaching for untaught structures, vocabulary, or case/mood forms beyond what Chapters 11–14 authorized, in an effort to sound more advanced.

Correction: use only the systems already consolidated in this unit; accuracy and fluent combination matter more here than new material.

Common Mistake — Skipping the comprehension check or the repair

Error: ending the plain-language mediation without هَل هَذَا وَاضِح؟ or without a repair phrase when a follow-up question is asked.

Correction: always close the plain-language explanation with the comprehension check and be ready with بِمَعْنَى آخَر، أَقْصِد أَنَّ...

Answer Key

Listening Assessment

  • The sourced claim is framed by تُشِير دِرَاسَة حَدِيثَة إِلَى أَنَّ...; the presentation opens with أَبْدَأ عَرْضِي اليَوْم بِهَذِه النُّقْطَة...
  • أَمَّا بِالنِّسْبَة لِلتَّفَاصِيل التِّقْنِيَّة، فَـ... signals technical detail; بِاخْتِصَار شَدِيد، مَا يَعْنِيه هَذَا عَمَلِيًّا هُوَ أَنَّ... compresses the finding into a plain-language summary.
  • The colleague mistakenly assumes all employees must work from home every day; the speaker repairs this with بِمَعْنَى آخَر، أَقْصِد أَنَّ..., clarifying that two or three days a week is sufficient.

Reading Assessment — Synthesis questions

  1. The report opens with وَفْقًا لِدِرَاسَة نُشِرَت هَذَا العَام, an attribution phrase that credits the claim to an outside source rather than presenting it as the writer's own opinion.
  2. The central claim is that remote work reduces peak-hour congestion; the closing observation is that flexible-work policies may ease urban transport pressure without large infrastructure investment.
  3. بِاخْتِصَار شَدِيد لِمَن لَيْس لَدَيْه خَلْفِيَّة تِقْنِيَّة signals the shift to a non-specialist audience.
  4. The plain-language summary keeps the same underlying claim — reduced congestion at limited cost — without adding or removing information, only compressing it.
  5. A specialist question would be answered using Chapter 14's specialist-register phrase, أَمَّا بِالنِّسْبَة لِلتَّفَاصِيل التِّقْنِيَّة، فَـ...

Chapter Review — Model responses

Section A.1: Answers will vary by learner dossier; each function (attribution, claim, closing observation, specialist signal, plain-language opener, comprehension check, repair) should be traceable to a specific sentence.

Section A.2: A faithful mediation restates only the practical consequence that follows from the sourced claim, without adding unsupported details or omitting necessary qualifications such as timing or scope.

Section B: Open-ended self-assessment; any honest, specific response referencing the rubric criteria is acceptable.

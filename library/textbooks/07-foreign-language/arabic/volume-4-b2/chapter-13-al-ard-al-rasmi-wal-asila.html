العَرْض الرَّسْمِيّ وَالأَسْئِلَةChapter 13: Formal Presentation and Questions

Presentation language, prepared connected reading, and question-and-answer interaction · Speak and Write Arabic: Professional, Academic, Media, and Institutional Arabic

Chapter Overview

This chapter continues Unit III — البَحْث وَالكِتَابَة المُتَقَدِّمَة — by taking the three-part academic paragraph that أَمَل wrote in Chapter 12 and turning it into a short spoken presentation. The abstract-noun opening, the evidence-connector pattern, and the closing-observation phrase all remain at their previously authorized status; nothing new is introduced in those systems. What is new is the language of spoken delivery itself: phrases that open and close a formal presentation, phrases that signal a shift from one part of the talk to the next, and the question-and-answer exchanges that typically follow. Reading a prepared paragraph aloud with appropriate pacing and pausing — already practiced in Chapter 12 — is extended here into a slightly longer, multi-part prepared reading, and into fielding a colleague's questions afterward. Every other grammar, register, and vocabulary system in this chapter remains at its previously authorized status.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Use formulaic phrases to open and close a short formal presentation.
  2. Use transition phrases to signal movement between the opening statement, the evidence, and the closing observation of a prepared talk.
  3. Deliver a short prepared academic paragraph aloud with appropriate pacing, pausing, and prominence.
  4. Ask and answer clarification and follow-up questions in a formal Q&A exchange.
  5. Read a short presentation transcript and identify its opening, transition, and closing language.
  6. Present a short prepared talk based on your Chapter 12 paragraph and respond to at least one follow-up question.

Opening Communication Scenario

أَمَل's written paragraph on energy efficiency was well received, and now she has been asked to present it briefly at the internal research briefing, followed by questions from her colleagues. She is nervous about the spoken delivery, since writing and speaking a formal paragraph feel different. رَامِي reminds her that the paragraph itself does not need to change — she needs opening and closing phrases to frame it as a talk, transition phrases to guide her listeners between its three parts, and a few ready phrases for handling questions she cannot fully answer on the spot.

  1. You will learn phrases that open and close a short formal presentation.
  2. You will study transition language that signals movement between parts of a prepared talk.
  3. You will read a short presentation transcript with opening, transition, and closing language.
  4. You will deliver your own short presentation and respond to a follow-up question.

التَّرْكِيز عَلَى النُّطْق — Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus: Pacing a Multi-Part Prepared Reading

Pronunciation Note — Marking transitions with pause and pitch

When moving between the opening, the evidence, and the closing of a prepared talk, give the transition phrase — for example وَالآن، أَنْتَقِل إِلَى... — a brief pause before it and a slight downward pitch step at its end, so listeners register it as a signpost rather than as part of the surrounding content. This extends the phrase-grouping and opening-noun prominence practiced in Chapter 12 to a new discourse function: marking the boundaries between sections of a spoken presentation.

التَّرْكِيب — Grammar and Sentence Patterns

Grammar and Sentence Patterns: Framing a Prepared Talk

This chapter introduces two closely related systems: (1) formulaic opening and closing frames for a short formal presentation, built around the imperfect and first-person forms already consolidated; and (2) a small set of question-and-answer patterns for a formal Q&A exchange, including a polite clarification question and a deferral phrase for incomplete answers. The three-part paragraph structure, abstract-noun openings, and citation/paraphrase language from Chapter 11 and Chapter 12 remain at their previously authorized (M) status and are recycled without change.

New Pattern — Presentation opener
يُسْعِدُنِي أَنْ أَعْرِض عَلَيْكُم + (topic) / سَأَتَنَاوَل فِي عَرْضِي + (topic)
Frames the talk before the content begins. The verb forms are already familiar; what is new is using them as a fixed formulaic opener rather than as ordinary narration.
يُسْعِدُنِي أَنْ أَعْرِض عَلَيْكُم اليَوْم مُلَخَّصًا مُوجَزًا حَوْل الكَفَاءَة فِي اسْتِخْدَام الطَّاقَة.
It pleases me to present to you today a brief summary about efficiency in energy use.
New Pattern — Section transition
وَالآن، أَنْتَقِل إِلَى + (next part) / بِنَاءً عَلَى ذَلِكَ...
Signals to listeners that the talk is moving from one part of the paragraph's structure to the next — for example, from the opening statement to the evidence, or from the evidence to the closing observation — without introducing any new clause type.
وَالآن، أَنْتَقِل إِلَى الدَّلِيل الَّذِي يَدْعَم هَذَا الرَّأْي.
And now, I move on to the evidence that supports this view.
New Pattern — Closing and Q&A handoff
وَخِتَامًا لِعَرْضِي، + (closing observation) + شُكْرًا عَلَى اهْتِمَامِكُم، وَأَنَا مُسْتَعِدّ(ة) لِلْأَسْئِلَة
Closes the spoken presentation and explicitly hands the floor over to questions, distinct from the written closing-observation phrase practiced in Chapter 12, which remains unchanged inside this frame.
وَخِتَامًا لِعَرْضِي، يَتَّضِح مِمَّا سَبَق أَنَّ تَحْقِيق الكَفَاءَة يَتَطَلَّب سِيَاسَات وَاضِحَة. شُكْرًا عَلَى اهْتِمَامِكُم، وَأَنَا مُسْتَعِدَّة لِلْأَسْئِلَة.
In closing my presentation, it becomes clear from the foregoing that achieving efficiency requires clear policies. Thank you for your attention, and I am ready for questions.

Notice that the paragraph content itself — the abstract-noun opening, the cited evidence, and the closing observation — has not changed from Chapter 12. This chapter simply adds a spoken frame around it and a set of ready phrases for the Q&A that follows.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Formal presentation etiquette

Formal presentations in academic and professional MSA settings commonly open with a polite framing phrase before the substantive content begins, and close by explicitly thanking the audience and inviting questions, rather than simply stopping when the content ends. This framing is a matter of convention and audience expectation rather than a strict grammatical rule, and speakers adapt it to the formality of the occasion.

Regional Variation — Q&A conventions vary by setting

Whether questions are taken during a presentation or reserved entirely for the end, and how directly a presenter may decline to answer, varies across institutions, disciplines, and countries in the Arabic-speaking world, much as it does in English-language academic and professional settings. This chapter teaches a general, transferable Q&A pattern rather than any single institution's required protocol.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Beginning the talk with the paragraph content instead of an opener

Error: starting the spoken presentation directly with the abstract-noun opening statement, e.g. الكَفَاءَة فِي اسْتِخْدَام الطَّاقَة أَصْبَحَت..., with no framing phrase to signal that a formal talk is beginning.

Correction: add a presentation opener first: يُسْعِدُنِي أَنْ أَعْرِض عَلَيْكُم اليَوْم مُلَخَّصًا حَوْل الكَفَاءَة فِي اسْتِخْدَام الطَّاقَة.

Common Mistake — Moving to evidence without a transition

Error: reading the opening statement and the evidence sentence back to back with no transition phrase between them, leaving listeners without a signal that the talk has moved to a new part.

Correction: insert a transition phrase: وَالآن، أَنْتَقِل إِلَى الدَّلِيل الَّذِي يَدْعَم هَذَا الرَّأْي.

Common Mistake — Guessing at an answer instead of using the deferral phrase

Error: inventing specific details in response to a question the speaker cannot actually answer, rather than acknowledging the gap.

Correction: use the deferral phrase honestly: لَا أَمْلِك إِجَابَة كَامِلَة الآن، لَكِنِّي سَأَتَحَقَّق مِن ذَلِكَ.

  2. The phrase بِنَاءً عَلَى ذَلِكَ marks the transition into the evidence; it differs from the Listening Practice passage, which used وَالآن، أَنْتَقِل إِلَى الدَّلِيل, showing that more than one transition phrase can serve the same function.
  3. The colleague asks what solutions are proposed besides parking spaces, using هَل يُمْكِنُك أَنْ تُوَضِّح...؟
  4. The speaker acknowledges the gap honestly with لَكِنَّنِي لَا أَمْلِك تَفَاصِيل التَّكْلِفَة الآن، وَسَأَتَحَقَّق مِن ذَلِكَ, rather than inventing a cost figure.
  5. The closing observation, marked by يَتَّضِح مِمَّا سَبَق أَنَّ, states that solving the transportation problem requires more than added parking — this remains the speaker's own synthesis, distinct from the cited study's claim about vehicle growth.

Listening Practice

  • The presentation opens with يُسْعِدُنِي أَنْ أَعْرِض عَلَيْكُم اليَوْم مُلَخَّصًا حَوْل الاسْتِدَامَة فِي التَّخْطِيط الحَضَرِيّ, announcing sustainability in urban planning as the topic.
  • وَالآن، أَنْتَقِل إِلَى الدَّلِيل marks the shift into the cited study.
  • وَخِتَامًا لِعَرْضِي... شُكْرًا عَلَى اهْتِمَامِكُم، وَأَنَا مُسْتَعِدّ لِلْأَسْئِلَة closes the presentation and hands off to questions.

Chapter Review — Model responses

Section A.1: Opener: سَأَتَنَاوَل فِي عَرْضِي...; transition: بِنَاءً عَلَى ذَلِكَ; closer: وَخِتَامًا لِعَرْضِي...شُكْرًا عَلَى اهْتِمَامِكُم، وَأَنَا مُسْتَعِدّ لِلْأَسْئِلَة.

Section A.2: لَا أَمْلِك إِجَابَة كَامِلَة الآن، لَكِنِّي سَأَتَحَقَّق مِن ذَلِكَ should be used whenever a speaker genuinely does not know the answer, rather than guessing or inventing specific details.

Section B: Open-ended self-assessment; any honest, specific response naming pacing, transitions, or Q&A skills is acceptable.

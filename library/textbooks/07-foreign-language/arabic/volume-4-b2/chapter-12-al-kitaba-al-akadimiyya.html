Abstract nouns, nominal style, and organized argument sections · Speak and Write Arabic: Professional, Academic, Media, and Institutional Arabic

Chapter Overview

This chapter continues Unit III — البَحْث وَالكِتَابَة المُتَقَدِّمَة — by building directly on the citation verbs, paraphrase markers, and reliability vocabulary introduced in Chapter 11. Having learned to attribute and evaluate individual claims, learners now organize several attributed and paraphrased claims into a short, coherent academic paragraph. The chapter activates two closely related systems for the first time: a set of abstract nouns formed through nominalization (turning verbs and qualities into abstract concepts such as التَّطَوُّر "development" or الاسْتِدَامَة "sustainability"), and the nominal sentence-opening style that academic Arabic favors for stating a topic before elaborating on it. Together, these support a short organized paragraph with a clear opening statement, supporting evidence drawn from sources, and a closing observation — the basic architecture that Chapters 13-15 will extend into a full research-based project. Every other grammar, register, and vocabulary system in this chapter remains at its previously authorized status.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Recognize and form common abstract nouns built from familiar verbs and adjectives through nominalization.
  2. Use an abstract noun as the subject of a nominal sentence to open an academic paragraph.
  3. Organize a short paragraph into an opening statement, supporting evidence, and a closing observation.
  4. Integrate citation verbs and paraphrase markers from Chapter 11 into a multi-sentence organized argument.
  5. Read a short academic paragraph and identify its topic statement, its supporting evidence, and its conclusion.
  6. Write a short, organized academic paragraph on a topic of your choice using nominal style and at least one attributed source.

التَّرْكِيز عَلَى النُّطْق — Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus: Reading an Abstract-Noun Opening Aloud

Pronunciation Note — Weight on the opening abstract noun

When an academic paragraph opens with an abstract noun as its topic — for example التَّطَوُّر السَّرِيع لِلمُدُن يَفْرِض تَحَدِّيَات جَدِيدَة — give that opening noun phrase slightly more prominence and a clear following pause than an ordinary sentence subject would receive, so the listener immediately registers it as the paragraph's announced topic. This extends the phrase-grouping and citation-verb pausing practiced in Chapters 9 and 11 to a new discourse position: the very beginning of an academic paragraph.

التَّرْكِيب — Grammar and Sentence Patterns

Grammar and Sentence Patterns: Nominalization and Paragraph Organization

This chapter introduces two closely related systems: (1) common abstract nouns formed through nominalization from familiar Form I, V, VIII, and X verbs, used as the grammatical subject of a nominal sentence; and (2) a three-part paragraph organization — opening statement, evidence, closing observation — built from connectors and the citation/paraphrase language recycled from Chapter 11. Case, mood, passive, and clause structure remain at their previously authorized (M) status.

New Pattern — Abstract noun as nominal-sentence subject
الـ + (abstract noun) + (predicate)
Opens the sentence with the concept itself rather than with a person or a full verb, which is the ordinary way academic Arabic announces a paragraph's topic. The abstract noun is definite and functions exactly like any other nominal-sentence subject already consolidated since Volume I.
التَّطَوُّر السَّرِيع لِلمُدُن يَفْرِض تَحَدِّيَات جَدِيدَة عَلَى أَنْظِمَة النَّقْل.
The rapid development of cities imposes new challenges on transportation systems.
New Pattern — Evidence-introducing connector
وَتَدْعَم هَذَا (الرَّأْي / التَّوَجُّه) + (citation verb) + أَنَّ ...
Signals a transition from the opening statement into supporting evidence, then hands off immediately to the citation-verb pattern already introduced in Chapter 11, so the evidence remains clearly attributed rather than presented as unsupported assertion.
وَتَدْعَم هَذَا الرَّأْي دِرَاسَة أَشَارَت إِلَى أَنَّ الازْدِيَاد فِي عَدَد المَرْكَبَات يَفُوق قُدْرَة البُنْيَة التَّحْتِيَّة.
Supporting this view is a study that indicated that the growth in the number of vehicles exceeds the capacity of the infrastructure.
New Pattern — Closing observation
يَتَّضِح مِمَّا سَبَق أَنَّ + (writer's own observation)
Marks the paragraph's final sentence as the writer's own conclusion, drawn from the evidence rather than repeated from a single source. This keeps the writer's synthesis clearly distinct from any one cited claim, continuing the source-versus-analysis distinction introduced in Chapter 11.
يَتَّضِح مِمَّا سَبَق أَنَّ حَلّ مُشْكِلَة النَّقْل يَتَطَلَّب أَكْثَر مِن مُجَرَّد زِيَادَة عَدَد مَوَاقِف السَّيَّارَات.
It becomes clear from the foregoing that solving the transportation problem requires more than simply increasing the number of parking spaces.

Notice how the three patterns build the paragraph's skeleton: the abstract-noun opening announces the topic, the evidence connector hands off to Chapter 11's citation verbs, and the closing phrase marks the writer's own synthesis as separate from any single source.

Writing Workshop — A Three-Part Academic Paragraph

In this task, you will write a short academic paragraph with a clear opening statement, attributed supporting evidence, and a closing observation, following the model of the Reading Practice passage.

  1. Step 1 — Choose a topic and an abstract noun. Select a topic and identify the abstract noun that will open your paragraph (for example, التَّطَوُّر, الاسْتِدَامَة, or الكَفَاءَة).
  2. Step 2 — Write the opening statement. Begin with the abstract noun as the grammatical subject of a nominal sentence.
  3. Step 3 — Add attributed evidence. Use the evidence connector plus a citation verb from Chapter 11 to introduce one supporting source, paraphrasing rather than quoting.
  4. Step 4 — Close with your own observation. Use يَتَّضِح مِمَّا سَبَق أَنَّ to state a conclusion that is clearly your own synthesis, not a repeated source claim.
  5. Review your paragraph against the checklist below.

قَائِمَة المُرَاجَعَة — Checklist:

  • The paragraph opens with a definite abstract noun as the grammatical subject.
  • At least one piece of evidence is attributed with a citation verb from Chapter 11.
  • The evidence is paraphrased, not quoted directly.
  • The closing sentence uses يَتَّضِح مِمَّا سَبَق أَنَّ and is clearly the writer's own observation.
  • The paragraph stays in consistent academic/general register throughout.
  • The paragraph reads naturally when read aloud, with clear prominence on the opening abstract noun.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Nominal style as an academic convention

Opening a paragraph or section with an abstract noun rather than a personal subject is a widely valued convention in Arabic academic, journalistic, and institutional writing, giving the text a more formal, topic-centered feel. This is a stylistic convention rather than a strict rule, and writers move between nominal and verbal openings depending on genre and personal style, much as academic writers in English vary between more formal and more direct openings.

Regional Variation — Paragraph conventions vary by institution and genre

Expectations for paragraph length, the placement of a thesis statement, and the degree of directness in a closing observation vary across universities, publications, and academic traditions in the Arabic-speaking world, much as they do in English-language academic writing. This chapter teaches a general, transferable three-part structure rather than any single institution's required format.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Opening with a verb instead of an abstract noun

Error: beginning an academic paragraph with a verbal sentence such as تَتَطَوَّر المُدُن بِسُرْعَة when a nominal, topic-first opening would be more appropriate to the register.

Correction: open with the abstract noun as subject: التَّطَوُّر السَّرِيع لِلمُدُن يَفْرِض تَحَدِّيَات جَدِيدَة.

Common Mistake — Ending with a repeated source claim instead of a synthesis

Error: closing the paragraph by simply restating the cited study's claim again, e.g. repeating الازْدِيَاد فِي عَدَد المَرْكَبَات يَفُوق قُدْرَة البُنْيَة التَّحْتِيَّة as the final sentence.

Correction: close with the writer's own synthesis: يَتَّضِح مِمَّا سَبَق أَنَّ حَلّ مُشْكِلَة النَّقْل يَتَطَلَّب أَكْثَر مِن مُجَرَّد زِيَادَة عَدَد مَوَاقِف السَّيَّارَات.

Common Mistake — Dropping the evidence connector and citing abruptly

Error: moving from the opening statement directly into a citation verb with no transition, e.g. التَّطَوُّر السَّرِيع لِلمُدُن يَفْرِض تَحَدِّيَات جَدِيدَة. أَشَارَت دِرَاسَة إِلَى أَنَّ... with no signal that a shift into evidence is occurring.

Correction: use the evidence connector to mark the transition: وَتَدْعَم هَذَا الرَّأْي دِرَاسَة أَشَارَت إِلَى أَنَّ...

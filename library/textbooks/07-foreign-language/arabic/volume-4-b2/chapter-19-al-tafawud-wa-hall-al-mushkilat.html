التَّفَاوُض وَحَلّ المُشْكِلَاتChapter 19: Negotiation and Problem Solving

Proposal, compromise, conditions, and professional interaction in contemporary MSA · Speak and Write Arabic: Professional, Academic, Media, and Institutional Arabic

Chapter Overview

Chapter 18 asked you to narrate history and memory with careful, attributed caution. This chapter turns from the past to the present moment of professional interaction: proposing a course of action, weighing competing needs, and reaching a workable compromise. Negotiation in MSA rarely proceeds by simple assertion. It proceeds through conditional framing, measured concession, and language that keeps the door open rather than closing it. You will learn how to state a position, propose an alternative, attach a condition to an offer, and accept or decline a proposal without damaging a working relationship. No new grammar system or vocabulary domain is activated; you will apply conditional structures, negotiation-oriented register, and the concession and result connectors consolidated earlier in the volume to the specific demands of professional problem-solving.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. State a professional position or problem clearly and propose a specific course of action in organized MSA.
  2. Attach a condition to a proposal or offer using conditional structures already consolidated in the volume.
  3. Signal willingness to compromise, and distinguish a firm requirement from a flexible preference.
  4. Accept, modify, or decline a proposal in professional register without abandoning the working relationship.
  5. Apply conditional, concession, and result structures from earlier chapters to the specific discourse of negotiation.
  6. Write a short proposal or counter-proposal that states a position, offers a condition, and leaves room for a response.

Opening Communication Scenario

The same cultural center from Chapter 18 is now negotiating with a printing company over the cost and timeline for the exhibition's captions and banners. سَامِر, who manages the center's small budget, has received a quote from the printing company that exceeds what the committee approved. He needs to propose a lower price or a reduced scope, without souring a relationship the center will need again next year. His colleague لَيْلَى, who has negotiated vendor contracts before, reminds him that a good negotiating message states the problem plainly, offers a specific alternative, attaches any conditions clearly, and always signals openness to further discussion rather than presenting an ultimatum. سَامِر realizes this is the same discipline of clear, responsible communication he used with sources in Chapter 18 — except now the goal is not to describe the past accurately, but to reach an agreement that works for both sides.

  1. You will read and discuss short negotiation exchanges between colleagues and vendors.
  2. You will learn phrases for proposing, conditioning, and compromising in professional MSA.
  3. You will practice accepting, modifying, and declining a proposal politely.
  4. You will write a short proposal or counter-proposal for a professional problem.

صَوْت التَّفَاوُض — The Negotiating Voice

The Measured, Collaborative Voice

Sound and Style — Reading a proposal aloud

A negotiating proposal is read at a level, unhurried pace that signals confidence without pressure. The conditional clause — إِذَا وَافَقْتُم عَلَى... ("if you agree to ...") — is read with a slight rise in pitch that holds until the result clause resolves it, so the listener hears the offer as a single connected package rather than two separate statements.

When softening a firm point with a phrase such as مَع ذَلِك، نَحْنُ مُنْفَتِحُون عَلَى... ("nevertheless, we are open to ..."), lower the volume slightly and slow down, the same collaborative pacing used for concession in Chapter 16, now directed at keeping a negotiation productive rather than simply acknowledging a counterargument.

اقْتِرَاح وَشَرْط وَتَنَازُل — Proposal, Condition, and Compromise

Proposal, Compromise, and Conditions

These are discourse and interaction skills, not new grammar systems. They combine conditional structures (Chapters 2 and 16), concession and result connectors (Chapters 4 and 16), and professional register (Chapter 2) for the specific task of negotiating a workable agreement.

Building Block — Proposing with a condition attached

إِذَا + [condition] ، فَـ + [offer/result]

State the condition first, then the resulting offer. This keeps the sequence of cause and consequence clear and avoids sounding like an unconditional demand.

إِذَا وَافَقْتُم عَلَى تَأْجِيل التَّسْلِيم أُسْبُوعًا وَاحِدًا، فَإِنَّنَا نَسْتَطِيع تَخْفِيض السِّعْر بِنِسْبَة عَشَرَة بِالمِئَة.

If you agree to delay delivery by one week, then we can reduce the price by ten percent.

  • نَقْتَرِح حَلًّا وَسَطًا: نُقَلِّل عَدَد اللَّافِتَات وَنُحَافِظ عَلَى المِيزَانِيَّة المُتَّفَق عَلَيْهَا.We propose a middle-ground solution: we reduce the number of banners and keep to the agreed budget.proposal + specific alternative
  • هَذَا الشَّرْط غَيْر قَابِل لِلتَّفَاوُض، لَكِنَّنَا مُنْفَتِحُون عَلَى مُنَاقَشَة كُلّ التَّفَاصِيل الأُخْرَى.This condition is non-negotiable, but we are open to discussing every other detail.firm requirement + signaled flexibility elsewhere
Building Block — Accepting, modifying, or declining a proposal

نُوَافِق عَلَى + [proposal] بِشَرْط أَن + [subjunctive clause]

Agreement can be full, conditional, or declined. بِشَرْط أَن introduces a condition attached to an acceptance, using the subjunctive already consolidated in earlier volumes.

نُوَافِق عَلَى اقْتِرَاحِكُم بِشَرْط أَن يَبْقَى مَوْعِد الافْتِتَاح كَمَا هُوَ.

We agree to your proposal on condition that the opening date remains as it is.

  • لَا نَسْتَطِيع المُوَافَقَة عَلَى هَذَا العَرْض كَمَا هُوَ، لَكِن يُمْكِنُنَا إِعَادَة النَّظَر فِيهِ إِذَا عُدِّلَت مُدَّة التَّسْلِيم.We cannot agree to this offer as it stands, but we can reconsider it if the delivery period is adjusted.polite decline + conditional path back to agreement
  • لَيْسَت المُشْكِلَة فِي السِّعْر، بَل فِي المَوْعِد النِّهَائِيّ؛ فَإِذَا حُلَّت هَذِه النُّقْطَة، يُصْبِح الاتِّفَاق مُمْكِنًا.The problem is not the price, but the deadline; if this point is resolved, agreement becomes possible. (contrast identifying the real obstacle, followed by a conditional path forward)لَيْسَ...بَل + إِذَا...
Grammar Note — Conditionals meet professional register

Every structure in this section combines two systems already consolidated: conditional clauses (إِذَا...فَـ, بِشَرْط أَن) from Chapters 2 and 16, and professional, polite register from Chapter 2. The new work in this chapter is discourse-level: deciding when to lead with a condition, when to signal firmness, and when to leave a proposal open, rather than learning a new sentence pattern.

Heritage Reference — A labeled formal closing phrase

Formal negotiation correspondence sometimes closes with an elevated, slightly formulaic phrase such as وَتَقَبَّلُوا فَائِق الاحْتِرَام وَالتَّقْدِير ("please accept our utmost respect and appreciation"). This is labeled here as a bounded formulaic closing: recognize it as a formal correspondence convention already touched on in Chapter 2, but do not attempt to generate new phrases of this elevated type independently.

حِوَار نَمُوذَجِيّ — Model Dialogue

Model Dialogue: Negotiating with the Printing Company

Setting: a phone call between سَامِر, from the cultural center, and مُنَى, a sales representative at the printing company. Professional, courteous register.

مُنَى: شَاهَدْنَا طَلَبَكُم، وَالسِّعْر الإِجْمَالِيّ هُوَ كَمَا فِي العَرْض الَّذِي أَرْسَلْنَاه.We reviewed your order, and the total price is as in the offer we sent.
سَامِر: شُكْرًا لَك. لَكِنَّ هَذَا السِّعْر يَتَجَاوَز مِيزَانِيَّتَنَا المُعْتَمَدَة. لَدَيَّ اقْتِرَاح: هَل يُمْكِن تَقْلِيل عَدَد اللَّافِتَات الكَبِيرَة مُقَابِل نَفْس الجَوْدَة؟Thank you. But this price exceeds our approved budget. I have a proposal: could we reduce the number of large banners in exchange for the same quality?
مُنَى: هَذَا مُمْكِن. إِذَا قَلَّلْتُم عَدَد اللَّافِتَات إِلَى النِّصْف، فَإِنَّنَا نَسْتَطِيع تَخْفِيض السِّعْر بِنِسْبَة خَمْسَة عَشَر بِالمِئَة.That's possible. If you reduce the number of banners by half, then we can lower the price by fifteen percent.
سَامِر: هَذَا تَخْفِيض جَيِّد، لَكِن مَوْعِد التَّسْلِيم غَيْر قَابِل لِلتَّفَاوُض بِالنِّسْبَة لَنَا؛ يَجِب أَن تَصِل اللَّافِتَات قَبْل الافْتِتَاح بِثَلَاثَة أَيَّام.That's a good discount, but the delivery date is non-negotiable for us; the banners must arrive three days before the opening.
مُنَى: نُوَافِق عَلَى ذَلِك بِشَرْط أَن تُؤَكِّدُوا الطَّلَب النِّهَائِيّ خِلَال يَوْمَيْن.We agree to that on condition that you confirm the final order within two days.
(سَامِر يُدَوِّن الشُّرُوط عَلَى وَرَقَة أَمَامَه.)
سَامِر: مُوَافَق. سَأُرْسِل لَكِ التَّأْكِيد غَدًا بَعْد اجْتِمَاع اللَّجْنَة.Agreed. I will send you the confirmation tomorrow after the committee meeting.
مُنَى: مُمْتَاز. نَحْنُ مُنْفَتِحُون دَائِمًا عَلَى إِيجَاد حَلّ وَسَط مَعَ عُمَلَائِنَا.Excellent. We are always open to finding a middle-ground solution with our clients.

Listening Practice

Ask your instructor, a partner, or a text-to-speech tool to read the following short passage aloud, with a level pace on the conditional clauses. Listen for which point is firm and which point is open to negotiation.

Writing Workshop

Write a short proposal or counter-proposal for a professional problem of your own choosing, applying the tools of this chapter and recycling those of earlier chapters.

  1. Step 1 — State the problem plainly. Write one or two sentences describing the disagreement or obstacle, without unnecessary hedging.
  2. Step 2 — Propose a specific alternative. Use نَقْتَرِح or لَدَيَّ اقْتِرَاح to introduce a concrete solution.
  3. Step 3 — Attach a condition to your offer. Use إِذَا...فَـ or بِشَرْط أَن to connect the condition to its result.
  4. Step 4 — Distinguish a firm point from a flexible one. Use غَيْر قَابِل لِلتَّفَاوُض for what cannot change, and مُنْفَتِحُون عَلَى for what can.
  5. Step 5 — Close by inviting a response. A simple closing sentence that looks forward to the other side's reply is sufficient; avoid an ultimatum.
  6. Review your paragraph against the checklist below.

قَائِمَة المُرَاجَعَة — Checklist:

  • The problem is stated clearly before any proposal is offered.
  • At least one proposal is attached to a clear condition.
  • A firm point and a flexible point are both named and clearly distinguished.
  • The passage invites a response rather than closing off discussion.
  • No untaught grammar or vocabulary domain was required to write the paragraph.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Relationship before transaction

In many professional settings across Arabic-speaking communities, negotiation is approached as maintaining an ongoing relationship rather than settling a single isolated transaction. Courteous framing, patience, and a visible willingness to compromise are often valued alongside the substance of the deal itself, and an overly blunt or purely transactional tone can be read as a sign of disrespect even when the underlying terms are reasonable.

Regional Variation — Styles of directness

Comfort with direct refusal, the pace of negotiation, and the expected number of rounds of counter-offer vary across regions, industries, and individual working relationships. Learners should avoid assuming that one negotiating style — highly direct or highly indirect — represents all Arabic-speaking professional contexts, and should adjust their approach based on the specific relationship and setting at hand.

Heritage Reference — Boundaries of this chapter

This chapter's negotiation language remains general professional MSA suited to everyday business and institutional contexts. It is not a course in contract law, diplomatic negotiation, or specialized commercial arbitration, and no claim is made about the legal enforceability of any example. Learners are asked to recognize and produce clear, courteous, and conditionally structured proposals in MSA — not to master professional negotiation theory or legal drafting.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Presenting a proposal as an ultimatum

Error: stating every point as non-negotiable, leaving no room for the other side to respond.

Correction: clearly separate the one or two truly firm points from everything else, and signal openness on the rest with a phrase such as مُنْفَتِحُون عَلَى.

Common Mistake — Detaching the condition from its result

Error: stating a condition and an offer as two unconnected sentences, so the listener cannot tell that one depends on the other.

Correction: connect the two explicitly with إِذَا...فَـ, placing the condition first.

Common Mistake — Overusing formal closing formulas

Error: inserting an elevated formulaic closing, such as وَتَقَبَّلُوا فَائِق الاحْتِرَام وَالتَّقْدِير, into a casual internal message where it feels stiff and out of place.

Correction: reserve the more elevated formal closings for external or higher-stakes correspondence, and use a simpler courteous closing for routine internal messages.

  • The director proposes reducing weekly trips from five to three; the condition is that the company agrees to this reduction, in exchange for which the charity accepts the new price.
  • The company treats the payment date as firm and non-negotiable, while remaining open to discussing all other conditions.
  • The conditional structure places the condition (reduced trips) before the result (accepted price), making clear that the offer depends on the company's agreement.

Reading Practice — Comprehension questions

  1. The purchasing department identifies that the total price of the original offer exceeds its budget for the year.
  2. The email proposes reducing the requested quantity by twenty percent while keeping the same quality; the condition is that the supplier agrees to this adjustment, in exchange for readiness to sign the contract that week.
  3. The final delivery date is described as firm and non-negotiable; the payment method is described as open to discussion.
  4. The closing phrase وَتَقَبَّلُوا فَائِق الاحْتِرَام وَالتَّقْدِير is a formulaic, elevated closing appropriate to formal external business correspondence, signaling courtesy and respect as the message ends.

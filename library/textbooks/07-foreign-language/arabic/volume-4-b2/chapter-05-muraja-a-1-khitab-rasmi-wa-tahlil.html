مُرَاجَعَة ١: خِطَاب رَسْمِيّ وَتَحْلِيلChapter 5: Unit I Assessment

An integrated formal institutional and argumentative discourse task · Speak and Write Arabic: Professional, Academic, Media, and Institutional Arabic

Chapter Overview

This chapter is a synthesis checkpoint. It introduces no new grammar, morphology, vocabulary domain, or register system. Instead, it brings together everything Unit I has built: the register control and disciplined paragraph development of Chapter 1, the institutional language, passive voice, and nominalization of Chapter 2, the advanced comparison and classification of Chapter 3, and the thesis, concession, and counterargument patterns of Chapter 4. You will read, listen to, discuss, and write one integrated case built around a single institutional scenario, drawing on all four chapters at once. Your work in this chapter demonstrates readiness for Unit II's move into media and public-issue Arabic.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Read and interpret an extended formal institutional text that classifies options, compares them, and argues for one.
  2. Identify passive constructions, nominalizations, and formal register choices within a connected institutional text.
  3. Listen to a formal spoken exchange and extract its classification, comparison, thesis, concession, and counterargument-response.
  4. Speak in sustained formal register to defend a position, respond to an objection, and answer follow-up questions.
  5. Write a complete formal recommendation report that classifies options, compares them, states a thesis, concedes a valid point, and answers a counterargument.
  6. Self-assess accuracy, register consistency, and cumulative control across all four chapters of Unit I.

Opening Communication Scenario

The research institute's director has asked أَمَل to prepare the final version of her recommendation, incorporating the institutional framing from Chapter 2, the classification and comparison from Chapter 3, and the thesis and counterargument-response from Chapter 4, into a single formal report. She must also defend the report in a short meeting and answer questions from colleagues, including one who remains unconvinced.

  1. You will read an extended version of the institute's case that classifies, compares, and argues in one continuous formal text.
  2. You will listen to a meeting in which the report is discussed, questioned, and defended.
  3. You will write your own full recommendation report and defend it aloud, integrating every tool from Unit I.

التَّرْكِيب — Grammar and Register Synthesis

Grammar and Register Synthesis

No new grammar pattern is introduced here. This section reviews, side by side, the four patterns that must now work together in a single connected text: institutional passive/nominalization (Chapter 2), classification and comparison (Chapter 3), and thesis, concession, and counterargument-response (Chapter 4).

Review Pattern — Institutional passive and nominalization (Chapter 2)
يُصَنَّف ... ضِمْن ... (... is classified among ...)
The passive keeps institutional writing impersonal and focused on the process or category rather than on who performed the action.
يُصَنَّف مَرْكَز البُحُوث ضِمْن الخِيَارَات الأَكْثَر مَوَارِد بَيْن المَرَافِق المُتَاحَة.
The research center is classified among the options with more resources among the available facilities.
Review Pattern — Comparison (Chapter 3)
بِالمُقَارَنَة مَع ..., ... أَكْثَر / أَقَلّ ... (in comparison with ..., ... is more/less ...)
Comparative structures with the elative connect one option's evidence directly to another's, supporting the classification above.
بِالمُقَارَنَة مَع المَكْتَبَة، يُوَفِّر المَرْكَز مَوَارِد أَكْثَر لِلبَاحِثِين الزَّائِرِين.
In comparison with the library, the center provides more resources for visiting researchers.
Review Pattern — Thesis, concession, and counterargument (Chapter 4)
تَتَمَثَّل أُطْرُوحَة ... فِي أَنَّ ...؛ صَحِيحٌ أَنَّ ...، غَيْر أَنَّ ...؛ قَدْ يَعْتَرِض البَعْض بِأَنَّ ...، وَلَكِنَّ الوَاقِع أَنَّ ...
These three linked patterns move a formal text from stating a position, through a genuine concession, to a reasoned answer to an objection — the backbone of the integrated report in this chapter.
تَتَمَثَّل أُطْرُوحَة هَذَا التَّقْرِير فِي أَنَّ عَلَى المَعْهَد أَن يُوَسِّع مَرْكَز البُحُوث. صَحِيحٌ أَنَّ التَّوَسُّع مُكْلِف، غَيْر أَنَّ الفَوَائِد تَفُوق التَّكْلِفَة. قَدْ يَعْتَرِض البَعْض بِأَنَّ المَكْتَبَة تَكْفِي، وَلَكِنَّ الوَاقِع أَنَّ الطَّلَب يَتَزَايَد.
The thesis of this report consists in the fact that the institute should expand the research center. It is true that expansion is costly, however the benefits outweigh the cost. Some may object that the library is sufficient, but the reality is that demand is increasing.

Gloss: With warm greetings, this report presents a final assessment of the question of expanding the research center. The institute's research facilities are usually classified into three categories: resource-limited facilities, medium facilities, and resource-rich facilities. The research center is classified in the last category, while the university library is classified in the medium category. In comparison with the library, the center provides more specialized research resources and faster access to international databases. The thesis of this report consists in the fact that the institute should expand the research center next year. First, statistics indicate that the number of visiting researchers increased by thirty percent over the past two years. Second, the center is classified, as noted above, among the options with more resources. It is true that expansion requires an additional budget; however, the expected benefits outweigh this cost in the long term. Some may object that the library currently meets researchers' needs, but the reality is that demand continues to grow and the library alone will not be sufficient in the near future. Based on the foregoing, approving the expansion of the center as soon as possible is recommended. Please accept the utmost respect.

  2. What comparison does the report draw between the center and the library, and what conclusion does it support?
  3. State the report's thesis in your own words.
  4. Identify the sentence that concedes a point, and the sentence that answers a counterargument.
  5. What final recommendation does the report make?

Writing Assessment — A Full Recommendation Report

In this assessment task, you will write one complete formal report that integrates every tool from Unit I: institutional framing, classification, comparison, thesis, evidence, concession, and counterargument-response.

  1. Step 1 — Frame the report institutionally. Open with a formal salutation and state the report's purpose, recycling the institutional register of Chapter 2.
  2. Step 2 — Classify and compare. Classify at least two options into categories, then compare them directly, recycling the classification and comparison patterns of Chapter 3.
  3. Step 3 — State your thesis. Use تَتَمَثَّل أُطْرُوحَة هَذَا التَّقْرِير فِي أَنَّ to state your position clearly.
  4. Step 4 — Support it with sequenced evidence. Use أَوَّلًا and ثَانِيًا to present at least two pieces of evidence, including one statistic.
  5. Step 5 — Concede a point and answer a counterargument. Use صَحِيحٌ أَنَّ ... ، غَيْر أَنَّ and قَدْ يَعْتَرِض البَعْض بِأَنَّ ... ، وَلَكِنَّ الوَاقِع أَنَّ.
  6. Step 6 — State your conclusion. Use بِنَاءً عَلَى مَا سَبَق، يُوصَى بِأَن to close with a clear recommendation.
  7. Review your report against the checklist below.

قَائِمَة المُرَاجَعَة — Full report checklist:

  • I opened with correct institutional formal register.
  • I classified at least two options and compared them clearly.
  • I stated a clear thesis using the formal pattern.
  • I supported my thesis with at least two pieces of evidence, marked with sequence connectors.
  • I conceded a genuine point using the concession-and-return pattern.
  • I introduced and answered a counterargument without abandoning my position.
  • I closed with a clear, connected recommendation.
  • Register is consistent throughout, and I did not introduce any system beyond Unit I.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Formal reports as collaborative, not adversarial, documents

A formal institutional report that classifies options, concedes a genuine cost, and answers an objection is generally read as a collaborative contribution to a shared decision, not as a debate to be won. Writers who integrate an opposing colleague's concern respectfully, as in the model dialogue's exchange between أَمَل and سَامِي, are typically seen as strengthening their institutional standing rather than weakening their case.

Regional Variation — Formal meeting conventions

The way an objection is voiced in a meeting, and the degree of directness expected in a spoken response, can vary across institutions and countries. Some settings favor a fairly direct spoken reply, similar to the written وَلَكِنَّ الوَاقِع أَنَّ, while others favor more indirect spoken hedges before returning to the same point. Both patterns are compatible with formal MSA; observing how colleagues in a given institution phrase their own responses remains the most reliable guide.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Combining systems without connecting them

Error: stating a classification, a comparison, a thesis, and a conclusion as four disconnected sentences with no cohesion markers between them.

Correction: connect each stage explicitly, as in the model report, so that classification leads into comparison, comparison supports the thesis, and the concession and counterargument-response lead naturally into the conclusion.

Common Mistake — Dropping register mid-report

Error: opening with formal institutional register and then shifting into a conversational tone, such as أَنَا أَعْتَقِد إِنَّهُ لَازِم, partway through the report.

Correction: maintain the formal argumentative register established in Chapter 4 throughout the entire report, including in the conclusion: وَبِنَاءً عَلَى مَا سَبَق، يُوصَى بِأَن ...

Common Mistake — Treating the concession as optional under time pressure

Error: omitting the concession entirely in a longer integrated report because the writer feels the thesis and evidence are enough on their own.

Correction: a full integrated report, like the one assessed in this chapter, must still include a genuine concession followed by a return to the thesis; length does not exempt a report from this structure.

اللُّغَة المُؤَسَّسِيَّة وَالإِجْرَاءَاتChapter 2: Institutional Language and Procedures

Administrative genres, procedure description, formal requests, passive and nominalization · Speak and Write Arabic: Professional, Academic, Media, and Institutional Arabic

Chapter Overview

This chapter extends the register and precision tools from Chapter 1 into the specific world of institutions: offices, forms, procedures, and formal requests. You will learn to describe a bureaucratic process step by step, to make a polite formal request in writing, and to use the passive voice to describe procedures without naming who performs each action — a hallmark of institutional Arabic. You will also begin working with institutional titles and the vocabulary of administrative documents, while continuing to build the disciplined paragraph and nominalization skills introduced in Chapter 1.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Describe a multi-step administrative or institutional procedure in order, using sequencing connectors.
  2. Use the passive voice to describe procedures and processes without naming the agent.
  3. Write a polite, correctly formatted formal request addressed to an institution or official.
  4. Identify and use common institutional titles and administrative vocabulary appropriately.
  5. Continue applying verbal nouns and case awareness from Chapter 1 inside institutional contexts.
  6. Recognize the structure of a basic administrative form or notice.

Opening Communication Scenario

أَمَل needs to renew her research visa before her placement at the institute continues into its second term. The institute's administrative office has posted a notice describing the renewal procedure, and أَمَل must also submit a written request to the head of the administrative affairs office, السَّيِّدَة لَيْلَى, asking for an extension of her placement.

  1. You will read a short administrative notice describing a multi-step procedure.
  2. You will study how the passive voice is used to describe each step without naming who performs it.
  3. You will write your own short formal request addressed to an institutional official.

النُّطْق — Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus: Formal Reading of Administrative Notices

Pronunciation Note — Medial hamza in institutional vocabulary

Institutional vocabulary in this chapter includes several words with a medial hamza, such as الشُّؤُون and مَسْؤُول. In careful formal reading, the hamza is pronounced as a distinct glottal stop rather than smoothed over, and the surrounding long vowel is held clearly. Practice reading الشُّؤُون الإِدَارِيَّة and المَسْؤُول المُخْتَصّ slowly, isolating the hamza before returning to normal speaking speed. This recognition-level attention builds on the formal connected reading introduced in Chapter 1; it does not require full spontaneous iʿrāb.

التَّرْكِيب — Grammar and Sentence Patterns

Grammar and Sentence Patterns: The Passive Voice and the Formal Request

This chapter develops two closely related systems introduced in Chapter 1 — nominalization and case awareness — into a new communicative context, while introducing the passive voice as a tool for describing institutional procedure without naming an agent.

Pattern 1 — The passive voice: describing a procedure without an agent
فُعِل / يُفْعَل (internal passive of the perfect and imperfect)
Institutional Arabic frequently describes what happens to a document or a case rather than who acts on it. The internal passive shifts the internal vowels of the verb so that the object of the active sentence becomes the subject of the passive sentence, and the agent disappears from the sentence entirely.
يُرَاجِع المَكْتَب الطَّلَب. ← يُرَاجَع الطَّلَب.
The office reviews the request. → The request is reviewed.
Pattern 2 — Sequencing an institutional procedure
أَوَّلًا، ثُمَّ، بَعْد ذَلِك، وَأَخِيرًا (first, then, after that, finally)
A procedure is described as a numbered or connected sequence of steps, usually in the passive voice, moving from the first required action to the final outcome. Each step should be a single clear clause.
أَوَّلًا، تُمْلَأ الاِسْتِمَارَة. ثُمَّ تُرْسَل إِلَى المَكْتَب. وَأَخِيرًا، يُصْدَر القَرَار خِلَال أُسْبُوعَيْن.
First, the form is filled out. Then it is sent to the office. Finally, the decision is issued within two weeks.
Pattern 3 — The formal written request
أَرْجُو / أَلْتَمِس + مَصْدَر (I request / I petition + verbal noun)
A polite formal request in writing typically opens by naming the addressee's title, states the request using a polite verb followed by a verbal noun rather than a direct command, and closes with a fixed formula of thanks or respect. This keeps the tone respectful and impersonal, matching institutional register.
أَرْجُو التَّكَرُّم بِالنَّظَر فِي طَلَب تَمْدِيد الإِقَامَة.
I kindly request your consideration of the request to extend the residency.

Register comparison — everyday versus institutional request:

  • مُمْكِن تُمَدِّد لِي الإِقَامَة؟Can you extend my residency for me? (conversational, direct)
  • أَطْلُب تَمْدِيد إِقَامَتِي.I request the extension of my residency. (neutral formal)
  • أَرْجُو التَّكَرُّم بِالنَّظَر فِي طَلَب تَمْدِيد الإِقَامَة.I kindly request your consideration of the request to extend the residency. (fully institutional register)

Common connectors and formulas for administrative writing: أَوَّلًا (first), ثُمَّ (then), بَعْد ذَلِك (after that), وَأَخِيرًا (finally), أَرْجُو التَّكَرُّم بِـ (I kindly request), تَحِيَّة طَيِّبَة وَبَعْد (a standard formal opening greeting), وَتَفَضَّلُوا بِقَبُول فَائِق الاِحْتِرَام (a standard formal closing).

Writing Workshop — A Formal Institutional Request

In this workshop, you will write one short, formal request addressed to an institutional official, describing what you are requesting and why.

  1. Step 1 — Address the official. Open with the addressee's title and a standard formal greeting.
  2. Step 2 — State the request. Use the polite formula أَرْجُو التَّكَرُّم بِـ followed by a verbal noun naming your request.
  3. Step 3 — Give one supporting reason. Add one sentence explaining why the request is needed, using a formal connector.
  4. Step 4 — Mention any attachment. Note any supporting document, using the passive voice (جَرَى إِرْفَاق...).
  5. Step 5 — Close formally. End with the standard closing formula.
  6. Review your request against the checklist below.

قَائِمَة المُرَاجَعَة — Request checklist:

  • I addressed the official by an appropriate institutional title.
  • I used the polite request formula with a verbal noun.
  • I used at least one passive-voice verb correctly.
  • I included a standard formal opening and closing.
  • My tone remained consistently formal throughout.
  • I did not introduce any system beyond what this chapter has authorized.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Formal requests as a genre

Across many Arabic-speaking institutions, the written formal request follows a recognizable and fairly stable shape: a respectful opening, a clearly stated request using polite indirect language, a brief supporting reason, and a formal closing. Learning this shape is often as useful as learning any single new vocabulary item, since it allows a request to be recognized as appropriately formal regardless of the specific institution.

Regional Variation — Titles and forms of address in institutions

Institutional titles and the exact formulas used to open and close formal correspondence can vary somewhat by country and by institution; some offices favor a shorter, plainer opening, while others expect the fuller ceremonial formulas shown in this chapter. When in doubt, following the pattern used in an institution's own official documents is a reliable strategy.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Naming the agent in a passive-voice procedure

Error: writing تُمْلَأ الاِسْتِمَارَة مِن قِبَل الطَّالِب when the point of the passive voice was to keep the description impersonal and agent-free.

Correction: unless the agent must be specified for clarity, leave it out: تُمْلَأ الاِسْتِمَارَة.

Common Mistake — Using a direct command in a formal request

Error: opening a formal request with a direct imperative such as مَدِّدُوا إِقَامَتِي ("extend my residency"), which reads as a command rather than a request.

Correction: use the polite formula with a verbal noun: أَرْجُو التَّكَرُّم بِتَمْدِيد الإِقَامَة.

Common Mistake — Skipping the sequencing connectors

Error: listing procedure steps as a string of disconnected passive sentences with no signal of order.

Correction: connect the steps explicitly with أَوَّلًا, ثُمَّ, and وَأَخِيرًا so the sequence is unambiguous.

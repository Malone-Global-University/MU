مُرَاجَعَة ٢: مَلَفّ إِعْلَامِيّ وَعَامّChapter 10: Unit II Assessment — Media Dossier and Public Explanation

Synthesizing Chapters 6-9 · Speak and Write Arabic: Professional, Academic, Media, and Institutional Arabic

Chapter Overview

This chapter closes Unit II — الإِعْلَام وَالقَضَايَا العَامَّة. It introduces no new grammar, vocabulary domain, or language system. Instead, it draws together the media-reading skills of Chapter 6, the interview and public-discussion language of Chapter 7, the careful, responsible treatment of social and civic issues from Chapter 8, and the general-purpose administrative vocabulary of Chapter 9 into a single integrated task: assembling a short media dossier on a public issue and delivering a spoken public explanation of it. Every grammar pattern, vocabulary item, and register choice used in this chapter has already been authorized in a prior Unit II chapter; this chapter's purpose is synthesis, recycling, and assessment, not new instruction.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Assemble a short media dossier that combines a headline, a brief report excerpt, and an administrative notice on a single public issue.
  2. Identify source voice, framing, and stance across the different text types in a dossier.
  3. Distinguish a right, an obligation, and a restriction when they appear inside a civic or media context.
  4. Summarize a public issue accurately using careful, source-attributed language rather than personal assertion.
  5. Deliver a clear, measured spoken explanation of a public issue to a general audience, responding to at least two follow-up questions.
  6. Write a short, organized dossier commentary that recycles content clauses, the passive voice, and institutional register at their authorized Unit II status.

Opening Communication Scenario

أَمَل has finished her institute paperwork and now returns to the transportation proposal she began investigating in Chapter 8. Her supervisor asks her to prepare a short dossier — a media headline, a brief report, and the institute's own administrative notice on parking and transportation — and to present a clear public explanation of the issue at the next staff meeting. رَامِي agrees to sit in and ask questions, the way a member of the public might.

  1. You will review the media, interview, civic-issue, and administrative vocabulary from Chapters 6 through 9.
  2. You will read a short three-part dossier on a single public issue.
  3. You will listen to a brief interview exchange about the issue.
  4. You will write a dossier commentary and deliver a spoken public explanation, responding to follow-up questions.

مُرَاجَعَة النُّطْق — Pronunciation Review

Pronunciation Review: Reading Across Text Types Aloud

Pronunciation Note — Shifting phrase grouping across a dossier

A dossier moves between three text types in this unit — a headline, a report excerpt, and an administrative notice — and each calls for a slightly different reading rhythm: headlines are read as single compact units with a brief pause at the end, report prose is read with the connected, measured phrasing practiced since Chapter 6, and administrative notices are read with the short, clipped phrase grouping practiced in Chapter 9. When you present your dossier aloud, deliberately shift your pace and grouping as you move from one text type to the next, rather than reading all three in the same rhythm.

مُرَاجَعَة التَّرْكِيب — Grammar and Register Review

Grammar and Register Review: Combining Source Attribution, Careful Claims, and Impersonal Restriction

This chapter introduces no new grammar system. It recycles, at assessment (A) status, three patterns already active across Unit II: source attribution and reported information (Chapters 6-7), careful institutional claims about a civic issue (Chapter 8), and the impersonal passive for stating requirements and restrictions (Chapter 9). The task is to combine these three patterns coherently within a single dossier and presentation.

Recycled Pattern — Source attribution (Ch. 6-7)
وَفْقًا لِـ / حَسَبَ + (source) ، + (reported clause)
Anchors a claim to its source rather than presenting it as the writer's own assertion, which is essential when moving between a media report and an official notice.
وَفْقًا لِتَقْرِير المَرْكَز، تُنَاقِش الإِدَارَة اقْتِرَاحًا جَدِيدًا لِتَنْظِيم النَّقْل وَالوُقُوف.
According to the center's report, the administration is discussing a new proposal to organize transportation and parking.
Recycled Pattern — Careful civic claim (Ch. 8)
يَرَى بَعْض أَصْحَاب المَصْلَحَة أَنَّ ... ، بَيْنَمَا يَرَى آخَرُونَ أَنَّ ...
Presents more than one stakeholder perspective side by side, avoiding a single flattened claim about a contested public issue.
يَرَى بَعْض المُوَظَّفِين أَنَّ الاقْتِرَاح يُسَهِّل الوُقُوف، بَيْنَمَا يَرَى آخَرُونَ أَنَّهُ يَزِيد القُيُود.
Some employees believe the proposal makes parking easier, while others believe it increases restrictions.
Recycled Pattern — Impersonal restriction (Ch. 9)
لَا يُسْمَح بِـ ... / يَجِب عَلَى ... أَن ...
States what the administrative notice actually requires or restricts, without inserting a personal opinion into a factual summary of a rule.
وَفْقًا لِلإِشْعَار الإِدَارِيّ، لَا يُسْمَح بِتَعْدِيل نِظَام الوُقُوف الحَالِيّ إِلَّا بَعْد مُوَافَقَة اللَّجْنَة.
According to the administrative notice, the current parking system may not be amended except with committee approval.

Notice how the three recycled patterns divide labor within a single dossier: attribution identifies where a claim comes from, the stakeholder pattern keeps a contested issue balanced, and the impersonal restriction pattern reports a rule without editorializing about it.

أَمَل

رَامِي

أَمَل
يَقُول إِنَّهُ لَا يُسْمَح بِتَعْدِيل نِظَام الوُقُوف الحَالِيّ إِلَّا بَعْد مُوَافَقَة اللَّجْنَة، وَيَجِب عَلَى كُلّ مُوَظَّف تَقْدِيم مُلَاحَظَاته قَبْل نِهَايَة الشَّهْر.It says the current parking system may not be amended except with committee approval, and every employee must submit their comments before the end of the month.

Writing Station — Dossier Commentary

In this task, you will write a short organized commentary that accompanies the three-part dossier, summarizing the issue for a colleague who has not read the original texts.

  1. Step 1 — State the issue. Name the public issue in one clear sentence.
  2. Step 2 — Attribute the media claim. Use وَفْقًا لِـ or حَسَبَ to summarize what the report says.
  3. Step 3 — Present both stakeholder views. Use يَرَى بَعْض ... بَيْنَمَا يَرَى آخَرُونَ ... to keep the issue balanced.
  4. Step 4 — Report the administrative rule. Use يَجِب عَلَى ... أَن ... or لَا يُسْمَح بِـ ... to state the requirement or restriction impersonally.
  5. Review your commentary against the checklist below.

قَائِمَة المُرَاجَعَة — Checklist:

  • My commentary states the issue clearly in one sentence before adding detail.
  • I attributed the media claim to its source rather than stating it as my own assertion.
  • I presented both stakeholder views without favoring one over the other.
  • I stated the administrative rule impersonally, without naming an unnecessary enforcing party.
  • My commentary stays in consistent formal/institutional register throughout.
  • The commentary reads naturally when read aloud, with appropriate phrase grouping for each text type.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Public issues, media, and administration together

A single public issue — such as a workplace parking policy — often moves across several kinds of text at once: a media report, a stakeholder discussion, and an official notice. Learning to read and speak across these different registers within one issue, without collapsing them into a single flattened account, is a practical skill useful in workplaces, universities, and public institutions across the Arabic-speaking world, regardless of country or media system.

Regional Variation — Media and institutional style across contexts

Headline style, report structure, and administrative notice conventions vary somewhat from one country's media and institutional culture to another, though the general MSA patterns practiced in this unit are broadly recognizable across contexts. Specific national media outlets, government agencies, and legal systems maintain their own house styles and terminology, which remain outside the general, non-specialist scope of this unit.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Blending a personal opinion into a source-attributed summary

Error: writing وَفْقًا لِلتَّقْرِير، الاقْتِرَاح سَيِّئ جِدًّا, presenting a personal judgment as though it came from the report.

Correction: keep attributed claims and personal opinion clearly separate: وَفْقًا لِلتَّقْرِير، يَرَى بَعْض المُوَظَّفِين أَنَّ الاقْتِرَاح يَحْتَاج إِلَى مُرَاجَعَة.

Common Mistake — Presenting only one stakeholder view

Error: summarizing a contested issue as though only one side existed, e.g. كُلّ المُوَظَّفِين يُؤَيِّدُون الاقْتِرَاح when the dossier shows disagreement.

Correction: use the balanced pattern: يَرَى بَعْض المُوَظَّفِين أَنَّ الاقْتِرَاح يُفِيد، بَيْنَمَا يَرَى آخَرُونَ أَنَّهُ يَحْتَاج إِلَى تَعْدِيل.

Common Mistake — Naming an unnecessary enforcing party in the notice summary

Error: writing المُدِير يَمْنَع أَيّ تَعْدِيل when the notice itself states the restriction impersonally.

Correction: preserve the impersonal form from the source notice: لَا يُسْمَح بِتَعْدِيل نِظَام الوُقُوف الحَالِيّ إِلَّا بَعْد مُوَافَقَة اللَّجْنَة.

  1. The headline announces a new proposal to organize transportation and parking at workplace centers.
  2. Some employees believe the proposal makes parking easier and reduces congestion; others believe it increases restrictions on those who work late hours.
  3. The spokesperson stated that the final decision will not be made before the employee survey period ends.
  4. Any employee who wishes to express an opinion must submit comments in writing before the end of the month.
  5. The current parking system may not be amended except with committee approval; after the deadline, no further comments will be accepted.

Listening Station

  • The journalist asks about the employees' opinion of the parking-organization proposal.
  • The employee reports a colleague's opinion (that the proposal is good) and states a personal opinion (that more information is needed); both are clearly distinguished.
  • The employee mentions that the current system may not be amended without committee approval, sourced to the official notice.

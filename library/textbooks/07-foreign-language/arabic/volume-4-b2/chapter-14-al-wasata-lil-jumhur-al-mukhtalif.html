الوَسَاطَة لِلجُمْهُور المُخْتَلِفChapter 14: Mediation for Different Audiences

Audience adaptation, summary, and register transfer · Speak and Write Arabic: Professional, Academic, Media, and Institutional Arabic

Chapter Overview

This chapter completes the writing-and-presenting sequence that began with أَمَل's academic paragraph in Chapter 12 and continued with her formal presentation in Chapter 13. Here, أَمَل is asked to explain her research to two very different listeners: a fellow researcher who wants full technical detail, and a busy department director who needs only the practical bottom line. The content of her research does not change, and the citation, evidence, and closing-observation language from Chapters 11 and 12 remain at their previously authorized status. What is new is mediation itself: the skill of taking one piece of organized information and re-expressing it faithfully for a listener with different background knowledge, time, and needs. This chapter introduces phrases for signaling a register shift, for compressing a technical point into a plain-language summary, and for checking that a non-specialist listener has understood. Every other grammar and vocabulary system in this chapter remains at its previously authorized status.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Use formulaic phrases to signal a shift from technical to plain-language register.
  2. Compress a technical finding into a one- or two-sentence plain-language summary.
  3. Adapt the same content for a specialist listener and for a non-specialist listener without changing its underlying meaning.
  4. Check a non-specialist listener's understanding and repair a misunderstanding politely.
  5. Read a short mediated exchange and identify where the register shifts and why.
  6. Mediate your own Chapter 12–13 material for two audiences: a specialist colleague and a non-specialist supervisor.

Opening Communication Scenario

After أَمَل's presentation and Q&A in Chapter 13, two colleagues ask her to explain her findings separately. The first, سَامِي, is a fellow researcher who wants the technical detail behind the study she cited. The second, مُدِيرَة القِسْم (the department director), is short on time and wants to know only what the finding means for the department's plans. أَمَل realizes she must say the same true thing twice, in two different ways, without changing what it actually means. رَامِي explains that this is called وَسَاطَة — mediation — and that it requires a few new signaling phrases, not new facts.

  1. You will learn phrases that signal a shift into plain-language explanation.
  2. You will practice compressing a technical point into a short, clear summary.
  3. You will read a short mediated exchange between a specialist and a non-specialist listener.
  4. You will mediate your own research material for two different audiences.

التَّرْكِيز عَلَى النُّطْق — Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus: Signaling Register with Pace and Pitch

Pronunciation Note — Slowing down for the plain-language summary

When shifting into a plain-language summary — for example after مَا يَعْنِيه هَذَا عَمَلِيًّا هُوَ أَنَّ... — slow the pace slightly and give the key word extra prominence, then use a clear falling pitch at the end of the sentence before pausing for the comprehension check هَل هَذَا وَاضِح؟. This extends the transition-marking pause and pitch step practiced in Chapter 13 to a new discourse function: signaling that the register itself, not just the topic, has shifted.

التَّرْكِيب — Grammar and Sentence Patterns

Grammar and Sentence Patterns: Mediating Between Registers

This chapter introduces two closely related systems: (1) formulaic register-shift phrases that mark movement from technical explanation to plain-language summary and back; and (2) a comprehension-check and repair pattern for confirming that a non-specialist listener has understood, and rephrasing politely if not. The paragraph content, citation language, and presentation-framing phrases from Chapters 11–13 remain at their previously authorized (M) status and are recycled without change.

New Pattern — Plain-language summary opener
مَا يَعْنِيه هَذَا عَمَلِيًّا هُوَ أَنَّ + (plain restatement) / بِاخْتِصَار شَدِيد...
Compresses a technical point into its practical consequence. The clause structure is already familiar; what is new is using it as a fixed frame for audience adaptation rather than as ordinary explanation.
مَا يَعْنِيه هَذَا عَمَلِيًّا هُوَ أَنَّ القِسْم يُمْكِنه تَقْلِيل تَكَالِيف الطَّاقَة إِذَا اعْتَمَد وَسَائِل نَقْل مُشْتَرَك.
What this means in practice is that the department can reduce energy costs if it adopts shared transportation.
New Pattern — Register-shift signal
بِعِبَارَة أُخْرَى... / أَمَّا بِالنِّسْبَة لِلتَّفَاصِيل التِّقْنِيَّة، فَـ...
Marks the boundary between a technical statement and its plain-language restatement, or signals that technical detail is available if requested, without introducing any new clause type.
بِعِبَارَة أُخْرَى، الدِّرَاسَة تُثْبِت أَنَّ التَّغْيِير البَسِيط فِي وَسَائِل النَّقْل يُحْدِث فَرْقًا حَقِيقِيًّا.
In other words, the study proves that a simple change in transportation makes a real difference.
New Pattern — Comprehension check and repair
هَل هَذَا وَاضِح؟ ... بِمَعْنَى آخَر، أَقْصِد أَنَّ...
Checks whether the non-specialist listener has understood the plain-language summary and, if not, offers a second, differently worded restatement rather than repeating the same sentence unchanged.
هَل هَذَا وَاضِح؟ بِمَعْنَى آخَر، أَقْصِد أَنَّ التَّغْيِير الصَّغِير يُوَفِّر مَالًا كَثِيرًا مَع مُرُور الوَقْت.
Is that clear? Put another way, I mean that the small change saves a great deal of money over time.

Notice that the underlying finding — that shared transportation reduces energy consumption — has not changed from Chapters 12–13. This chapter adds the phrases needed to frame that same finding differently for a specialist and for a non-specialist listener.

Model Exchange

أَمَل explains her finding first to سَامِي, a fellow researcher, and then to مُدِيرَة القِسْم, who needs only the practical summary.

سَامِي

أَمَل
أَمَّا بِالنِّسْبَة لِلتَّفَاصِيل التِّقْنِيَّة، فَالدِّرَاسَة قَارَنَت اسْتِهْلَاك الطَّاقَة بَيْن المُدُن الَّتِي تَعْتَمِد وَسَائِل نَقْل مُشْتَرَك وَالمُدُن الَّتِي لَا تَعْتَمِدها عَلَى مَدَى خَمْس سَنَوَات.As for the technical details, the study compared energy consumption between cities that adopt shared transportation and cities that do not, over five years.

There's no need to go into detail now, but I'll send a written summary.

Writing Workshop — A Two-Audience Summary

In this task, you will write two short written summaries of your Chapter 12 finding: one for a specialist reader and one for a non-specialist reader, using this chapter's register-shift phrases.

  1. Step 1 — Retrieve your finding. Return to the finding and evidence from your Chapter 12 paragraph.
  2. Step 2 — Write the specialist version. Write two or three sentences using أَمَّا بِالنِّسْبَة لِلتَّفَاصِيل التِّقْنِيَّة، فَـ... that preserve full technical detail.
  3. Step 3 — Write the plain-language version. Write one or two sentences using مَا يَعْنِيه هَذَا عَمَلِيًّا هُوَ أَنَّ... or بِاخْتِصَار شَدِيد that state only the practical consequence.
  4. Step 4 — Add a comprehension check. End the plain-language version with هَل هَذَا وَاضِح؟
  5. Step 5 — Add a repair. Write one sentence beginning with بِمَعْنَى آخَر، أَقْصِد أَنَّ... that restates the plain-language summary a second way.
  6. Review your two summaries against the checklist below.

قَائِمَة المُرَاجَعَة — Checklist:

  • The specialist version preserves the technical detail from Chapter 12 without simplification.
  • The plain-language version states only the practical consequence, framed by a register-shift phrase.
  • The plain-language version ends with a comprehension check.
  • A repair sentence restates the plain-language point in different words.
  • Both versions describe the same underlying finding without changing its meaning.
  • Both versions read naturally aloud.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Mediation as a professional skill

In academic, professional, and institutional MSA settings, the ability to explain the same finding differently for a specialist colleague, a manager, or the general public is treated as a distinct professional skill, not merely a simplification of "proper" language. Choosing the plain-language frame over the technical one is a matter of audience awareness rather than a loss of precision.

Regional Variation — Directness in comprehension checks

How directly a speaker asks whether a listener has understood, and how openly a listener admits partial understanding, varies across institutions and countries in the Arabic-speaking world, much as it does in English-language professional settings. This chapter teaches a general, transferable pattern rather than any single institution's required style.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Repeating the technical sentence unchanged as the "summary"

Error: giving the non-specialist listener the exact same technical sentence used with the specialist, without a register-shift phrase or any simplification, e.g. simply repeating الدِّرَاسَة قَارَنَت اسْتِهْلَاك الطَّاقَة بَيْن المُدُن...

Correction: compress the point first: مَا يَعْنِيه هَذَا عَمَلِيًّا هُوَ أَنَّ القِسْم يُمْكِنه تَقْلِيل تَكَالِيف الطَّاقَة.

Common Mistake — Changing the meaning while simplifying

Error: simplifying a finding so much that it becomes inaccurate, for example implying immediate savings when the study describes savings over time.

Correction: keep the plain-language version faithful: التَّغْيِير الصَّغِير يُوَفِّر مَالًا كَثِيرًا مَع مُرُور الوَقْت، وَلَيْس فَوْرًا.

Common Mistake — Skipping the comprehension check

Error: moving on immediately after the plain-language summary without checking whether the listener understood, risking an undetected misunderstanding.

Correction: always end the summary with هَل هَذَا وَاضِح؟ and be ready to repair with بِمَعْنَى آخَر، أَقْصِد أَنَّ...

Reading Practice — Comprehension

  1. The phrase أَمَّا بِالنِّسْبَة لِلتَّفَاصِيل التِّقْنِيَّة، فَـ... signals technical detail; لَا حَاجَة لِلدُّخُول فِي التَّفَاصِيل الآن signals that detail may be skipped.
  2. The plain-language summary is that the city will face greater congestion if it does not act now, introduced by بِاخْتِصَار شَدِيد: مَا يَعْنِيه هَذَا عَمَلِيًّا هُوَ أَنَّ...
  3. المُدِير mistakenly assumes the only solution is building new roads, shown by his question هَل تَقْصِد أَنَّ الحَلّ هُوَ بِنَاء طُرُق جَدِيدَة فَقَط؟
  4. The speaker repairs this with بِمَعْنَى آخَر، أَقْصِد أَنَّ..., adding that improving public transportation management is also required, not roads alone.
  5. Both statements describe the same underlying capacity problem; the technical version gives the data-based claim, and the plain-language version gives its practical consequence, without adding or removing information.

Listening Practice

  • أَمَّا بِالنِّسْبَة لِلتَّفَاصِيل التِّقْنِيَّة، فَـ... signals that رَامِي is about to give technical detail.
  • بِاخْتِصَار شَدِيد، مَا يَعْنِيه هَذَا عَمَلِيًّا هُوَ أَنَّ... introduces the plain-language summary.
  • رَامِي repairs the partial misunderstanding with بِمَعْنَى آخَر، أَقْصِد أَنَّ..., clarifying that the improvement takes time but produces durable results, rather than an immediate change.

Chapter Review — Model responses

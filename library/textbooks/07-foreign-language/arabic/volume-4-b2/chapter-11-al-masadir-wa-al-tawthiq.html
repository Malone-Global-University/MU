المَصَادِر وَالتَّوْثِيقChapter 11: Sources and Documentation

Citation language, paraphrase, attribution, and reliability · Speak and Write Arabic: Professional, Academic, Media, and Institutional Arabic

Chapter Overview

This chapter opens Unit III — البَحْث وَالكِتَابَة المُتَقَدِّمَة. It builds directly on the source-attribution language recycled throughout Unit II, particularly the وَفْقًا لِـ / حَسَبَ pattern practiced in Chapters 6 through 10, and extends it into the two closely related systems this chapter activates for the first time: a fuller set of citation and reference verbs for naming where information comes from, and paraphrase language for restating a source's content in your own words rather than copying it. The chapter also introduces the vocabulary of source reliability — distinguishing a well-documented claim from an unverified one — which learners will need throughout the academic and research-oriented chapters that follow in this unit. Every other grammar, register, and vocabulary system in this chapter remains at its previously authorized status; the two new systems are introduced in a bounded, general-purpose way appropriate to a non-specialist learner beginning academic work, not as training in formal research methodology.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Use a range of citation and reference verbs — beyond وَفْقًا لِـ and حَسَبَ — to name where a piece of information comes from.
  2. Paraphrase a short source sentence into your own words while preserving its meaning and its attribution.
  3. Distinguish a direct quotation from a paraphrase in both reading and writing.
  4. Describe a source as reliable, unreliable, outdated, or uncertain, using appropriate general-purpose vocabulary.
  5. Read a short academic-style passage and identify its sources, claims, and level of certainty.
  6. Write a short paragraph that paraphrases and attributes information from more than one source without presenting any of it as your own original claim.

Opening Communication Scenario

أَمَل has been asked to prepare a short background note for a colleague who will present the transportation proposal at a regional meeting. Instead of simply forwarding the dossier from Chapter 10, she now needs to draw on two additional sources — a short academic article on urban transportation and a comment from a specialist she interviewed — and to make clear, in writing, exactly which idea came from which source, and how reliable each source is. رَامِي reviews her draft and asks her to distinguish more carefully between quotation, paraphrase, and her own analysis.

  1. You will learn vocabulary for citation, paraphrase, and source reliability.
  2. You will study how to paraphrase a sentence while keeping its attribution clear.
  3. You will read a short academic-style passage that cites more than one source.
  4. You will write a short paragraph that paraphrases and attributes information from two sources.

التَّرْكِيز عَلَى النُّطْق — Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus: Reading Citation Chains Aloud

Pronunciation Note — Pausing between a citation verb and its content clause

Citation verbs such as أَشَارَ إِلَى أَنَّ and ذَكَرَ أَنَّ introduce a full content clause. In formal connected reading, place a brief phrase-final pause immediately after the verb and its preposition or particle, before beginning the content clause, so the listener clearly hears where the citation ends and the cited content begins. This is the same phrase-grouping skill practiced with institutional notices in Chapter 9, now applied to a longer, clause-introducing pattern rather than a short phrase.

التَّرْكِيب — Grammar and Sentence Patterns

Grammar and Sentence Patterns: Citation Verbs and Paraphrase

This chapter introduces two closely related systems: (1) a set of citation verbs that govern a content clause with أَنَّ, extending the source-attribution pattern already active since Chapter 6; and (2) paraphrase language that restates a source's content without quoting it directly. Case, mood, passive, and clause structure remain at their previously authorized (M) status and are recycled here rather than retaught.

New Pattern — Citation verb + content clause
أَشَارَ / ذَكَرَ / نَقَلَ + (source) + أَنَّ + (content clause)
Names the source as the grammatical subject of a verb of saying, then introduces the cited content as a dependent clause. This is more precise than a prepositional phrase alone, because it identifies who or what performed the act of stating.
أَشَارَ التَّقْرِير إِلَى أَنَّ عَدَد المُسْتَخْدِمِين ازْدَاد خِلَال العَام المَاضِي.
The report indicated that the number of users had increased during the past year.
New Pattern — Paraphrase marker
بِعِبَارَة أُخْرَى، ... / أَي أَنَّ ...
Signals that the following sentence restates a preceding idea in different words rather than adding new information. Useful for showing that a paraphrase is deliberate and attributed, not an unmarked repetition.
أَشَارَ البَاحِث إِلَى أَنَّ الحُلُول التَّقْلِيدِيَّة لَم تَعُد كَافِيَة. بِعِبَارَة أُخْرَى، يَرَى أَنَّ المَدِينَة تَحْتَاج إِلَى نَهْج جَدِيد.
The researcher indicated that traditional solutions are no longer sufficient. In other words, he believes the city needs a new approach.
Recycled Pattern — Reliability description (adjective + المَصْدَر)
المَصْدَر مَوْثُوق / غَيْر مَوْثُوق / قَدِيم بَعْض الشَّيْء
Uses ordinary adjective-agreement structure, already consolidated since Volume II, to comment on a source directly rather than on its content, keeping the evaluation of reliability separate from the evaluation of the claim itself.
هَذَا المَقَال مَوْثُوق نِسْبِيًّا، لَكِنَّهُ غَيْر مُحَدَّث تَمَامًا.
This article is relatively reliable, but it is not entirely up to date.

Notice how the three patterns divide labor: the citation verb names who said something, the paraphrase marker signals that the following sentence restates rather than repeats, and the reliability description comments on the source itself rather than on the writer's opinion of the topic.

Writing Workshop — A Two-Source Paragraph

In this task, you will write a short paragraph that paraphrases and attributes information from two different sources, following the model of the Reading Practice passage.

  1. Step 1 — Choose two short sources. These may be real short texts you have read, or two sentences your instructor provides.
  2. Step 2 — Cite the first source. Use a citation verb such as أَشَارَ إِلَى أَنَّ or ذَكَرَ أَنَّ to introduce its main claim.
  3. Step 3 — Paraphrase, don't quote. Restate the claim in your own words rather than copying the source's exact sentence.
  4. Step 4 — Cite the second source and mark reliability. Introduce the second source with a citation verb, then add a one-sentence reliability comment about it.
  5. Review your paragraph against the checklist below.

قَائِمَة المُرَاجَعَة — Checklist:

  • Each claim is clearly attributed to a named source using a citation verb.
  • No sentence copies a source's exact wording without marking it as a quotation.
  • At least one sentence uses a paraphrase marker such as بِعِبَارَة أُخْرَى or أَي أَنَّ.
  • At least one sentence comments on a source's reliability rather than only on its content.
  • The paragraph stays in consistent academic/general register throughout.
  • The paragraph reads naturally when read aloud, with a clear pause between each citation verb and its content clause.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Source care across academic and public writing

Careful attribution — making clear which idea belongs to which source, and how confident that source is — is valued in academic, journalistic, and professional Arabic writing across the Arabic-speaking world, just as it is in English-language academic writing. Learning to cite, paraphrase, and comment on reliability in Arabic is a transferable skill that will support the research-based reading and writing tasks in the rest of this unit.

Regional Variation — Citation conventions vary by institution

Specific citation formats, footnote styles, and bibliographic conventions differ from one university, publisher, or country to another, much as they do in English-language academic writing. This chapter teaches the general communicative language of citation and reliability rather than any single institution's formal citation style, which remains outside the general, non-specialist scope of this chapter.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Copying a source's wording while calling it a paraphrase

Error: changing only one or two words from the original sentence and presenting it as a paraphrase, e.g. keeping "اِزْدَادَت أَعْدَاد السُّكَّان بِسُرْعَة فِي العَقْد الأَخِير" almost unchanged.

Correction: restate the idea with genuinely different wording and structure: أَشَارَ المَصْدَر إِلَى أَنَّ عَدَد السُّكَّان نَمَا بِوَتِيرَة سَرِيعَة خِلَال السَّنَوَات العَشْر المَاضِيَة.

Common Mistake — Presenting a source's claim as the writer's own assertion

Error: dropping the citation verb entirely, e.g. writing المُدُن تَحْتَاج إِلَى نَهْج جَدِيد كُلِّيًّا as though it were the writer's own conclusion, when it came from a cited researcher.

Correction: keep the citation verb: يَرَى البَاحِث أَنَّ المُدُن تَحْتَاج إِلَى نَهْج جَدِيد كُلِّيًّا.

Common Mistake — Treating an unconfirmed comment as equally reliable as a published source

Error: citing an informal or unpublished comment with the same confidence as a published academic article, without any reliability marker.

Correction: mark the difference explicitly: هَذَا التَّعْلِيق غَيْر مُؤَكَّد رَسْمِيًّا وَلَم يُنْشَر بَعْد.

Practical Performance Task

مُهِمَّة تَطْبِيقِيَّة — Performance Task

تَقْرِير مَصَادِر قَصِير — A Short Source Report

Choose a topic connected to your own studies, workplace, or community. Find or invent two short sources of information about it (for example, an article and an interview comment, or two differing reports). Write a short paragraph (5-7 sentences) that:

  • cites each source by name using a citation verb;
  • paraphrases at least one claim from each source rather than quoting it directly;
  • uses at least one paraphrase marker;
  • comments on the reliability of at least one source; and
  • keeps your own analysis, if any, clearly separate from the cited claims.

Then read your paragraph aloud to a partner or instructor, pausing clearly after each citation verb, and answer one follow-up question about which parts of your report are most certain and which are least certain.

  1. The academic article indicates that most large cities face similar challenges in organizing transportation and parking.
  2. The claim that the increase in vehicles exceeds current infrastructure capacity is attributed to several earlier studies, not to the article's author directly.
  3. بِعِبَارَة أُخْرَى restates the article's point about infrastructure capacity as a broader claim: that partial solutions like more parking spaces will not be sufficient long-term.

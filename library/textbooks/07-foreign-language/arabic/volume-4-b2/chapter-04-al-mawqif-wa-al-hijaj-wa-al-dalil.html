المَوْقِف وَالحِجَاج وَالدَّلِيلChapter 4: Position, Argument, and Evidence

Thesis, evidence, concession, counterargument, and argumentative cohesion · Speak and Write Arabic: Professional, Academic, Media, and Institutional Arabic

Chapter Overview

This chapter turns the comparison and classification tools of Chapter 3 toward argument: stating a clear position, supporting it with evidence, acknowledging an opposing view, and answering that view without abandoning your own. You will learn formal thesis-statement patterns, a concession structure that grants a point before returning to your position, a counterargument-and-response pattern, and a small set of cohesion markers that organize an argument into clear stages. These tools prepare you for the media analysis of Unit II, where you will evaluate how other writers construct exactly this kind of argument.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. State a clear formal thesis using an established position-statement pattern.
  2. Support a position with evidence, including statistics carried over from Chapter 3.
  3. Concede a valid point in an opposing view using a formal concession structure.
  4. Introduce a counterargument and respond to it without weakening your own position.
  5. Organize a short argumentative text using formal cohesion markers for sequence and conclusion.
  6. Read and interpret a short opinion piece that states a position, concedes a point, and answers a counterargument.

Opening Communication Scenario

Following her comparative note in Chapter 3, أَمَل has been asked to take a clearer position: the administrative office wants a short recommendation memo stating which research placement the institute should expand for the coming year, supported by evidence, and responding to a colleague's concern that expansion is too costly.

  1. You will read a short opinion piece that states a position, concedes a point, and answers a counterargument.
  2. You will study how a thesis, concession, and counterargument-response are built in formal MSA.
  3. You will write your own short recommendation memo that takes a position and answers an objection.

النُّطْق — Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus: Phrasing a Concession-and-Return

Pronunciation Note — Pausing around غَيْر أَنَّ and إِلَّا أَنَّ

A concession-and-return sentence is read in two phrase groups: the conceded point, a brief pause, and then غَيْر أَنَّ or إِلَّا أَنَّ introducing the writer's own position, as in صَحِيحٌ أَنَّ التَّوَسُّع مُكْلِف، غَيْر أَنَّ الفَوَائِد تَفُوق التَّكْلِفَة ("it is true that expansion is costly; however, the benefits outweigh the cost"). The pause marks the turn from conceding to returning, and the pitch on غَيْر أَنَّ should rise slightly to signal the pivot. This builds on the phrase-grouping and formal connected reading introduced in Chapters 1 through 3.

التَّرْكِيب — Grammar and Sentence Patterns

Grammar and Sentence Patterns: Thesis, Concession, and Counterargument

This chapter develops formal thesis statements, a concession-and-return pattern, and a counterargument-response pattern, while recycling the elative, passive classification, contrast connectors, and statistic-commentary patterns from Chapter 3.

Pattern 1 — Stating a formal thesis
تَتَمَثَّل أُطْرُوحَة هَذِهِ المُذَكِّرَة فِي أَنَّ ... (the thesis of this memo consists in the fact that ...)
A formal thesis is introduced with a verb of stating or consisting, followed by أَنَّ and the position itself. This keeps the claim explicit and separates it clearly from supporting evidence that follows.
تَتَمَثَّل أُطْرُوحَة هَذِهِ المُذَكِّرَة فِي أَنَّ عَلَى المَعْهَد أَن يُوَسِّع مَرْكَز البُحُوث خِلَال العَام القَادِم.
The thesis of this memo consists in the fact that the institute should expand the research center next year.
Pattern 2 — Concession and return
صَحِيحٌ أَنَّ ... ، غَيْر أَنَّ / إِلَّا أَنَّ ... (it is true that ..., however ...)
A concession grants that an opposing point has some validity before the writer returns to their own position. صَحِيحٌ أَنَّ introduces the conceded clause; غَيْر أَنَّ or إِلَّا أَنَّ introduces the writer's own counterpoint. The concession must be genuine, not merely rhetorical.
صَحِيحٌ أَنَّ التَّوَسُّع يَتَطَلَّب مِيزَانِيَّة إِضَافِيَّة، غَيْر أَنَّ الفَوَائِد المُتَوَقَّعَة تَفُوق هَذِهِ التَّكْلِفَة عَلَى المَدَى الطَّوِيل.
It is true that expansion requires an additional budget; however, the expected benefits outweigh this cost in the long term.
Pattern 3 — Introducing and answering a counterargument
قَدْ يَعْتَرِض البَعْض بِأَنَّ ... ، وَلَكِنَّ الوَاقِع أَنَّ ... (some may object that ..., but the reality is that ...)
A counterargument is introduced impersonally, without naming a specific opponent, using قَدْ يَعْتَرِض البَعْض بِأَنَّ. The response follows with وَلَكِنَّ الوَاقِع أَنَّ, which reasserts the writer's position with reference to fact rather than mere restatement.
قَدْ يَعْتَرِض البَعْض بِأَنَّ المَكْتَبَة تَكْفِي حَاجَة البَاحِثِينَ الحَالِيِّينَ، وَلَكِنَّ الوَاقِع أَنَّ عَدَد البَاحِثِينَ الزَّائِرِين يَتَزَايَد كُلّ عَام.
Some may object that the library is sufficient for the needs of current researchers, but the reality is that the number of visiting researchers increases every year.
Pattern 4 — Organizing an argument with cohesion markers
أَوَّلًا ... ، ثَانِيًا ... ، وَبِنَاءً عَلَى مَا سَبَق ... (first ..., second ..., and based on the foregoing ...)
Formal argumentative writing signals its own stages so the reader can follow the movement from evidence to conclusion. أَوَّلًا and ثَانِيًا mark separate pieces of evidence; وَبِنَاءً عَلَى مَا سَبَق introduces the conclusion that follows from them.
أَوَّلًا، تُشِير الإِحْصَاءَات إِلَى تَزَايُد عَدَد البَاحِثِينَ. ثَانِيًا، يُوَفِّر المَرْكَز مَوَارِد أَكْثَر مِن المَكْتَبَة. وَبِنَاءً عَلَى مَا سَبَق، يُوصَى بِتَوْسِيع المَرْكَز.
First, the statistics indicate an increase in the number of researchers. Second, the center provides more resources than the library. Based on the foregoing, expanding the center is recommended.

Register comparison — everyday opinion versus formal argument:

  • أَنَا أَعْتَقِد أَنَّهُ لَازِم نُوَسِّع المَرْكَز.I think we have to expand the center. (conversational, personal opinion)
  • تَتَمَثَّل أُطْرُوحَة هَذِهِ المُذَكِّرَة فِي أَنَّ عَلَى المَعْهَد أَن يُوَسِّع المَرْكَز.The thesis of this memo consists in the fact that the institute should expand the center. (formal thesis statement)
  • صَحِيحٌ أَنَّ التَّوَسُّع مُكْلِف، غَيْر أَنَّ الفَوَائِد تَفُوق التَّكْلِفَة، وَبِنَاءً عَلَى مَا سَبَق يُوصَى بِالمُوَافَقَة عَلَى المَشْرُوع.It is true that expansion is costly; however, the benefits outweigh the cost, and based on the foregoing, approving the project is recommended. (fully argumentative register)

Common connectors and formulas for argumentative writing: تَتَمَثَّل أُطْرُوحَة ... فِي أَنَّ (the thesis of ... consists in), صَحِيحٌ أَنَّ (it is true that), غَيْر أَنَّ / إِلَّا أَنَّ (however / nevertheless), قَدْ يَعْتَرِض البَعْض بِأَنَّ (some may object that), وَلَكِنَّ الوَاقِع أَنَّ (but the reality is that), أَوَّلًا / ثَانِيًا / أَخِيرًا (first / second / finally), بِنَاءً عَلَى مَا سَبَق (based on the foregoing), يُوصَى بِأَن (it is recommended that).

Gloss: With warm greetings, the thesis of this memo consists in the fact that the institute should expand the research center next year. First, the statistics indicate that the number of visiting researchers increased by thirty percent over the past two years, reflecting growing demand. Second, the center is classified, as noted in our earlier comparison, among the options with more resources among the available facilities. It is true that expansion requires an additional budget; however, the expected benefits outweigh this cost in the long term. Some may object that the university library currently meets researchers' needs, but the reality is that demand continues to grow and the library alone will not be sufficient in the near future. Based on the foregoing, approving the expansion of the center as soon as possible is recommended. Please accept the utmost respect.

Writing Workshop — A Short Recommendation Memo

In this workshop, you will write one short argumentative memo that states a thesis, supports it with evidence, concedes a point, and answers a counterargument.

  1. Step 1 — State your thesis. Use تَتَمَثَّل أُطْرُوحَة هَذِهِ المُذَكِّرَة فِي أَنَّ to state your position clearly.
  2. Step 2 — Support it with evidence. Use أَوَّلًا and ثَانِيًا to present at least two pieces of evidence, including one statistic.
  3. Step 3 — Concede a point. Use صَحِيحٌ أَنَّ ... ، غَيْر أَنَّ to acknowledge a genuine limitation or cost.
  4. Step 4 — Answer a counterargument. Use قَدْ يَعْتَرِض البَعْض بِأَنَّ ... ، وَلَكِنَّ الوَاقِع أَنَّ to respond to an opposing view.
  5. Step 5 — State your conclusion. Use بِنَاءً عَلَى مَا سَبَق، يُوصَى بِأَن to close with a clear recommendation.
  6. Review your memo against the checklist below.

قَائِمَة المُرَاجَعَة — Recommendation memo checklist:

  • I stated a clear thesis using the formal pattern.
  • I supported my thesis with at least two pieces of evidence, marked with sequence connectors.
  • I conceded a genuine point using the concession-and-return pattern.
  • I introduced and answered a counterargument without abandoning my position.
  • I closed with a clear, connected recommendation.
  • I did not introduce any system beyond what this chapter has authorized.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Concession as a mark of credibility

In formal Arabic-language argumentative writing, genuinely conceding a valid point before returning to one's own position is generally seen as a sign of intellectual honesty rather than weakness. A writer who never acknowledges any cost or limitation, especially in an institutional memo, can come across as unpersuasive or evasive rather than confident.

Regional Variation — Softening a counterargument

The degree of directness used when answering a counterargument can vary by institution and by country; some formal writing favors the relatively direct وَلَكِنَّ الوَاقِع أَنَّ, while other institutional styles prefer a softer إِلَّا أَنَّهُ مِن الجَدِير بِالذِّكْر أَنَّ ("however, it is worth noting that"). Both are acceptable in formal MSA; following the convention used in a given institution's own documents is a reliable strategy.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Conceding a point without returning to the thesis

Error: writing صَحِيحٌ أَنَّ التَّوَسُّع مُكْلِف. and stopping there, leaving the concession unanswered.

Correction: a concession must always be followed by a return to the writer's position: صَحِيحٌ أَنَّ التَّوَسُّع مُكْلِف، غَيْر أَنَّ الفَوَائِد تَفُوق التَّكْلِفَة.

Common Mistake — Naming a specific opponent in a counterargument

Error: writing قَالَ خَالِد إِنَّ المَكْتَبَة تَكْفِي ("Khalid said the library is sufficient") when the impersonal counterargument pattern was expected in formal writing.

Correction: keep the counterargument impersonal unless naming the source is required: قَدْ يَعْتَرِض البَعْض بِأَنَّ المَكْتَبَة تَكْفِي.

Common Mistake — Stating evidence with no connecting sequence markers

Error: listing two pieces of evidence back to back with no connector, leaving the argument's structure unclear.

Correction: mark each piece of evidence explicitly with أَوَّلًا and ثَانِيًا, and close with بِنَاءً عَلَى مَا سَبَق so the reader can follow the argument's stages.

Listening Practice

  • The institute should expand the research center next year.
  • She concedes that expansion requires an additional budget, then responds that the expected benefits outweigh this cost in the long term.
  • She answers the objection that the library is currently sufficient by noting that demand is continuously increasing.

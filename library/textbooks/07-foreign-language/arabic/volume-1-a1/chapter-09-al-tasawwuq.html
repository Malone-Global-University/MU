التَّسَوُّقShopping

The dual, expanded plural agreement, comparison, and attached object pronouns · Speak and Write Arabic: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Recognize and produce the Arabic dual for nouns referring to exactly two people or things.
  2. Use sound masculine, sound feminine, and common broken plurals with correct human and nonhuman adjective and verb agreement.
  3. Compare two items using the comparative pattern أَفْعَل, such as أَرْخَص (cheaper) and أَكْبَر (bigger).
  4. Attach object pronoun suffixes directly to verbs to avoid repeating a noun, such as أُرِيدُهُ (I want it).
  5. Ask for and state prices, sizes, and colors while shopping for clothing and everyday goods.
  6. Request a different size or color politely, using previously studied request forms.
  7. Read a store receipt, a price tag, and a short shopping dialogue.
  8. Write a short description comparing two items you might buy.

Communication Scenario

It is Saturday morning, and Tom needs to buy a few things before an end-of-month gathering at the institute: a shirt, a pair of shoes, and a small gift for his host family. He and his classmate Nūr walk together to a clothing shop near the market in downtown Amman, where a shopkeeper greets them and helps them compare a few options.

  1. Think about the last time you compared two items before buying one — a size, a color, a price. What questions did you ask?
  2. You can already name colors, count from 0 to 20, and make simple requests. This chapter adds the language you need to talk about pairs of things, larger groups of things, comparisons, and objects you want without repeating their names each time.

الكِتَابَة — Writing Note

Writing the Dual and Plural Endings

You completed the 28-letter Arabic alphabet in Chapter 3, so this chapter introduces no new letters. It does introduce two new word-final patterns that are important to write accurately.

نِهَايَة المُثَنَّى — The dual ending

The dual ending is written ـَان in most sentence positions, added directly to the singular noun: قَمِيص → قَمِيصَان. Keep the ending attached to the full singular form; do not drop or alter the base word when adding it.

نِهَايَة الجَمْع المُؤَنَّث السَّالِم — The sound feminine plural ending

The sound feminine plural ending is written ـَات, replacing a final ة (tāʾ marbūṭa) if the singular has one: سَاعَة → سَاعَات. If the singular has no ة, the ending is simply added: بَنْطَلُون → بَنْطَلُونَات.

تَدْرِيب — Practice: Copy each of the following three times, then circle the dual or plural ending: قَمِيصَان، سَاعَتَان، بَنْطَلُونَات، حَقِيبَتَان.

Pronunciation

The dual ending in connected speech

The dual ending ـَان is pronounced -ān, with a clear long vowel, in ordinary pause pronunciation: قَمِيصَان is pronounced qamīṣān. Do not shorten this vowel or confuse it with the shorter sound of the definite article's initial syllable.

Comparative stress

Comparative words built on the أَفْعَل pattern, such as أَرْخَص (cheaper) and أَكْبَر (bigger), carry stress on the first syllable. Practice these aloud slowly before using them in the dialogue below: ARkhaṣ, AKbar, ASghar (smaller).

Pronunciation Note — غَالٍ and the weak final sound

غَالٍ (expensive) ends in a weak final sound that is often pronounced simply ghālī-like in ordinary pause speech, though its written form is slightly different from a regular ي ending. For now, simply learn its pronunciation as a whole word; the underlying spelling pattern belongs to a later volume.

Grammar Instruction

9.1 المُثَنَّى — The dual

Purpose: refer to exactly two people or things without using a plural or repeating the number "two."

Arabic has a special dual form used only for exactly two of something. Unlike English, which uses the same plural for two or more items, Arabic marks "two" directly on the noun by adding ـَان to the singular:

  • قَمِيص → قَمِيصَانqamīṣ → qamīṣān — shirt → two shirts
  • سَاعَة → سَاعَتَانsāʿa → sāʿatān — watch → two watches
  • حَقِيبَة → حَقِيبَتَانḥaqība → ḥaqībatān — bag → two bags

Notice that a feminine noun ending in ة keeps that letter, pronounced as t, before the dual ending is added. You do not need the number اِثْنَان ("two") when the dual ending is already present.

Grammar Note — Dual case awareness

The dual ending changes to ـَيْن in some grammatical positions, reflecting the Arabic case system that you will begin studying formally later in the volume. For now, use ـَان as the default productive form and simply notice, without needing to explain, if you encounter ـَيْن in a reading passage or a set phrase.

9.2 مُوَافَقَة الجَمْع — Expanding plural agreement

Purpose: correctly match adjectives and demonstratives to plural nouns, reviewing and expanding the nonhuman plural pattern from Chapter 6.

You learned in Chapter 6 that plural nonhuman nouns take feminine singular agreement on adjectives and demonstratives. This pattern applies directly to shopping vocabulary:

  • هَذِهِ القُمْصَان جَمِيلَة.hādhihi l-qumṣān jamīla. — These shirts are beautiful. (feminine singular agreement, nonhuman plural)
  • هَذِهِ الأَحْذِيَة رَخِيصَة.hādhihi l-aḥdhiya rakhīṣa. — These shoes are cheap.

Human plurals, by contrast, take true plural agreement, a pattern you first met in Chapter 6 and now recycle here:

  • البَائِعُون مُتَعَاوِنُون.al-bāʾiʿūn mutaʿāwinūn. — The salesmen are cooperative. (human plural, sound masculine agreement)

9.3 اسْم التَّفْضِيل — Comparing with the elative pattern

Purpose: compare two items using a single adjective form, without a separate word for "more."

Arabic forms a comparative from many adjectives using the pattern أَفْعَل. Unlike English, one word does the work of both "more ___" and "___er":

Base adjectiveComparative (أَفْعَل)English meaning
رَخِيصأَرْخَصcheap → cheaper
غَالٍأَغْلَىexpensive → more expensive
كَبِيرأَكْبَرbig → bigger
صَغِيرأَصْغَرsmall → smaller

To compare two specific items, use the comparative followed by مِنْ ("than"):

  • هَذَا القَمِيص أَرْخَص مِنْ ذَلِكَ القَمِيص.hādhā l-qamīṣ arkhaṣ min dhālika l-qamīṣ. — This shirt is cheaper than that shirt.
  • هَذِهِ الحَقِيبَة أَكْبَر مِنْ هَذِهِ.hādhihi l-ḥaqība akbar min hādhihi. — This bag is bigger than this one.

The comparative form أَفْعَل does not change for gender or number when used this way. You will study the full elative system, including its use as a superlative, later in the volume.

9.4 ضَمَائِر المَفْعُول المُتَّصِلَة — Attached object pronouns on verbs

Purpose: replace a previously mentioned direct object with a suffix attached to the verb, avoiding repetition.

You learned attached possessive pronouns in Chapter 4 (ـِي, ـُهُ, and so on) to show possession. The same set of suffixes can attach to a verb to mean "it," "him," "her," or "them" as the direct object:

ArabicTransliterationEnglish meaning
أُرِيدُهُurīduhuI want it / him (masculine)
أُرِيدُهَاurīduhāI want it / her (feminine)
هَلْ تُرِيدُهُ؟hal turīduhu?Do you want it? (masculine)
  • هَذَا القَمِيص جَمِيل. أُرِيدُهُ.hādhā l-qamīṣ jamīl. urīduhu. — This shirt is beautiful. I want it.
  • هَذِهِ الحَقِيبَة رَخِيصَة. أُرِيدُهَا.hādhihi l-ḥaqība rakhīṣa. urīduhā. — This bag is cheap. I want it.
Grammar Note — Same suffixes, different attachment point

Compare قَمِيصُهُ ("his shirt," from Chapter 4) with أُرِيدُهُ ("I want it," this chapter). The suffix ـُهُ is identical in both. What changes is where it attaches: to a noun, it shows possession; to a verb, it shows the direct object. You will continue meeting this same suffix set attached to prepositions in a later chapter.

Language Focus

One suffix set, several jobs. This chapter's four grammar points connect through a single idea: Arabic marks meaning through endings and attachments rather than separate words. The dual ending marks "two" directly on the noun. Plural agreement is carried by the adjective or demonstrative form itself. The comparative is a single reshaped adjective, not a separate word for "more." And the object pronoun is an ending on the verb, not a separate pronoun word. Recognizing this pattern will help you read and produce Arabic more efficiently as the course continues.

Language Building Block
هَذَا/هَذِهِ + noun + أَفْعَل + مِنْ + noun
Demonstrative + noun + comparative adjective + "than" + noun
هَذَا الحِذَاء أَرْخَص مِنْ ذَلِكَ الحِذَاء، وَأُرِيدُهُ.

Model Dialogue

Setting: A clothing shop near the market in downtown Amman. Tom and Nūr are looking for a shirt, and the shopkeeper, Ustādh Fādī, helps them compare two options.
السِّيَاق: مَحَلّ مَلَابِس قُرْب السُّوق فِي وَسَط عَمَّان. يَبْحَث تُوم وَنُور عَنْ قَمِيص، وَيُسَاعِدُهُمَا البَائِع أُسْتَاذ فَادِي فِي المُقَارَنَة بَيْنَ خِيَارَيْن.

FĀDĪ أَهْلًا وَسَهْلًا! كَيْفَ أُسَاعِدُكُم؟ Welcome! How can I help you (pl.)?
TOM أَبْحَث عَنْ قَمِيص. عِنْدَكُم قُمْصَان جَمِيلَة! I'm looking for a shirt. You have beautiful shirts!
FĀDĪ شُكْرًا! عِنْدِي هَذَا القَمِيص الأَزْرَق وَهَذَا القَمِيص الأَبْيَض. بِكَمْ تُرِيد؟ Thank you! I have this blue shirt and this white shirt. What price are you looking for?
TOM بِكَمْ هَذَا الأَزْرَق؟ How much is this blue one?
FĀDĪ هَذَا القَمِيص الأَزْرَق بِعَشَرَة دَنَانِير، وَالأَبْيَض بِخَمْسَة عَشَر دِينَارًا. This blue shirt is ten dinars, and the white one is fifteen dinars.
NŪR إِذَنْ، الأَزْرَق أَرْخَص مِنَ الأَبْيَض. So the blue one is cheaper than the white one.
TOM هَلْ عِنْدَكُم مَقَاس أَكْبَر مِنْ هَذَا؟ Do you have a size bigger than this one?
FĀDĪ نَعَمْ، لَحْظَة مِنْ فَضْلِكَ. تَفَضَّل، هَذَا مَقَاس أَكْبَر. Yes, one moment please. Here you are, this is a bigger size.
TOM مُمْتَاز! أُرِيدُهُ. وَأُرِيد أَيْضًا حِذَاءَيْن، وَاحِد لِي وَوَاحِد لِأَخِي. Excellent! I want it. And I also want two pairs of shoes — one for me and one for my brother.
FĀDĪ عِنْدَنَا أَحْذِيَة كَثِيرَة. تَفَضَّل مِنْ هُنَا. We have many shoes. This way, please.

أَسْئِلَة الفَهْم — Comprehension questions:

  1. كَمْ سِعْر القَمِيص الأَزْرَق؟ (What is the price of the blue shirt?)
  2. أَيّ قَمِيص أَرْخَص؟ (Which shirt is cheaper?)
  3. How many pairs of shoes does Tom want, and for whom?
  4. Find one example in the dialogue of an object pronoun attached to a verb, and state what it refers to.
Grammar Note — حِذَاءَيْن

Tom says حِذَاءَيْن rather than حِذَاءَان because the noun follows the word أُرِيد as a direct object, one of the grammatical positions where the dual ending shifts to ـَيْن, as noted in Grammar section 9.1. You are not expected to derive this shift yourself yet; simply recognize both dual endings when you encounter them.

Writing Workshop

In this chapter you will write a short comparison of two items you might buy, using the dual, plural agreement, comparative forms, and an attached object pronoun.

  1. Study the model. Look again at Layla's shopping note above. Notice how it names two items, compares them, and states a preference.
  2. Choose two items. Pick two items from this chapter's vocabulary, such as two shirts or two bags, that differ in price, size, or color.
  3. Describe each item. Write one sentence about each item using a color or size adjective you already know.
  4. Compare them. Write one sentence using a comparative form and مِنْ.
  5. State your choice. Write one sentence using an attached object pronoun, such as أُرِيدُهُ or أُرِيدُهَا, to say which one you want.
  6. Revise. Check your draft against the editing checklist below.

قَائِمَة المُرَاجَعَة — Editing checklist:

  • I used the dual ending ـَان correctly if I mentioned exactly two items.
  • I matched adjectives and demonstratives to nonhuman plural nouns using the feminine singular form.
  • I formed the comparative correctly using the أَفْعَل pattern and مِنْ.
  • I attached the correct object pronoun suffix to my verb instead of repeating the noun.
  • I wrote prices clearly using numbers from Chapters 3 and 8.
  • I wrote all endings and ligatures with correct right-to-left joining.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Markets and fixed-price shops

Shopping across Arabic-speaking cities ranges from traditional markets, where prices in some stalls may be negotiable, to modern shopping centers and chain stores with clearly marked, fixed prices. Neither model is universal, and the right approach depends on the specific shop, city, and country. This chapter's vocabulary and dialogue reflect a fixed-price shop; negotiation language is introduced separately and clearly labeled later in the series.

Regional Variation — Currency names

This chapter uses دِينَار (dinar), the currency of Jordan, where the chapter's scenario is set. Other Arabic-speaking countries use different currencies with different names, such as the pound, the riyal, the dirham, and the shekel. Your instructor can supply the appropriate currency name and pricing conventions for a different country if needed.

Culture in Context — Politeness while shopping

The word تَفَضَّل (and its feminine and plural forms) appears frequently in Arabic shopping and service interactions, functioning as a flexible, polite way to say "here you are," "please, go ahead," or "come this way." You met this word briefly in earlier chapters; notice here how naturally it recurs in a commercial setting.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using the plural instead of the dual

Error: Saying *عِنْدِي قُمْصَان when you mean exactly two shirts.

Correction: When you mean exactly two, use the dual: عِنْدِي قَمِيصَان. Reserve the plural قُمْصَان for three or more.

Common Mistake — Plural adjective with a nonhuman plural noun

Error: Saying *هَذِهِ القُمْصَان جَمِيلُون, using a human plural adjective form.

Correction: Nonhuman plurals take feminine singular agreement: هَذِهِ القُمْصَان جَمِيلَة. This pattern was introduced in Chapter 6 and applies here as well.

Common Mistake — Adding a separate word for "more"

Error: Saying *هَذَا أَكْثَر رَخِيص مِنْ ذَلِكَ, inserting an extra word for "more" before the adjective.

Correction: The comparative meaning is already built into the أَفْعَل pattern: هَذَا أَرْخَص مِنْ ذَلِكَ. No separate word for "more" is needed.

Common Mistake — Repeating the noun instead of using a suffix

Error: Saying *أُرِيد القَمِيص a second time after the item has already been named.

Correction: Once the item is clear from context, attach the object pronoun to the verb instead: أُرِيدُهُ. Repeating the full noun is grammatically possible but unnatural once the referent is established.

Dialogue — Comprehension

  2. The blue shirt is cheaper than the white shirt.
  3. Tom wants two pairs of shoes: one for himself and one for his brother.
  4. أُرِيدُهُ refers to the larger-sized blue shirt.

Listening

  • 1 — bag — eight dinars — none
  • 2 — (size, not an item) — a smaller size is available — speaker B agrees
  • 3 — two dresses — the red dress is more expensive than the yellow dress — none
  • 4 — two watches — none given — one watch for the speaker, one for her sister

Bonus: Exchange 4 uses both the dual (سَاعَتَيْن, two watches, as a direct object) and, in the shopkeeper's reply, the plural (سَاعَات, watches in general).

Looking Ahead

مَا الَّذِي تَسْتَطِيعُ فِعْلَهُ الآن — What you can do now

You can talk about exactly two people or things using the dual, correctly match adjectives and demonstratives to plural nouns, compare two items using the comparative pattern, and attach object pronouns to verbs instead of repeating a noun. You can also shop for clothing and everyday goods, ask for prices and sizes, and read a simple price tag or shopping note.

Speak and Write Arabic — Volume I · Chapter 4

العَائِلَة وَالأَصْدِقَاء

Family and Friends

Unit I: البِدَايَات — Beginnings

Chapter Overview

In Chapter 3 you completed the Arabic alphabet, learned to talk about your nationality and origin, and asked basic questions with مِن أَيْن, ما, and هَل. Chapter 4 takes you into your most personal vocabulary: family and friends. You will learn to describe who is in your family, how people are related, and what family members do or study. Along the way you will encounter two important new writing features — hamza and the special letters — and two central grammar systems: sound and broken plurals and the construct state (iḍāfa), which is the primary Arabic way to express possession and association.

By the end of this chapter you will be able to introduce your family in spoken and written Arabic and produce a short written family profile.

Learning Objectives

  • Identify, read, and write hamza in its four orthographic positions
  • Identify and write the special letters: تَاء مَرْبُوطَة, أَلِف مَقْصُورَة, and أَلِف مَدّ
  • Produce core family vocabulary with correct gender and plural forms
  • Form the construct state (iḍāfa) to express possession and relationship
  • Use sound plurals (جَمْع سَالِم) and common broken plurals (جَمْع تَكْسِير)
  • Use independent pronouns to make simple statements about family members
  • Describe family members with basic adjectives using gender agreement
  • Write a short paragraph introducing your family

Opening Scenario

Situation

You have just met سَارَة (Sara), a student from Egypt, at a language exchange event. She shows you a photograph on her phone and begins to tell you about her family. You want to understand what she says and then share information about your own family. Listen for names, relationships, and descriptions.

Sara هذِهِ عَائِلَتِي. هذَا أَبِي وَهذِهِ أُمِّي. This is my family. This is my father and this is my mother.
You كَم أَخًا وَأُخْتًا عِنْدَكِ؟ How many brothers and sisters do you have?
Sara عِنْدِي أَخٌ وَاحِدٌ وَأُخْتَانِ. أَخِي اسْمُهُ كَرِيمٌ، وَأُخْتَايَ اسْمَاهُمَا لَيْلَى وَنُورٌ. I have one brother and two sisters. My brother's name is Karim, and my two sisters' names are Layla and Nour.
You مَا عَمَلُ أَبِيكِ؟ What is your father's job?
Sara أَبِي طَبِيبٌ. وَأُمِّي مُدَرِّسَةٌ. My father is a doctor. And my mother is a teacher.

Notice how Sara uses possessive constructs throughout: عَائِلَتِي (my family), أَبِي (my father), أُمِّي (my mother), أَخِي (my brother). These are formed through the Arabic construct state, which you will learn in the Grammar section.

Script and Orthography Focus: Hamza and the Special Letters

1. Hamza — الهَمْزَة

Hamza represents a glottal stop — the brief closure in the throat you hear between the syllables of uh-oh in English. It is a full consonant in Arabic, and its spelling depends on the surrounding vowels.

The Four Hamza Seats

Position / Seat Written form Example Meaning
On أَلِف (above or below) أ / إ أَب / إِخْوَة father / brothers
On وَاو ؤ مَسْؤُول responsible
On يَاء (no dots) ئ رَئِيس president, head
On the line (بِلا كُرْسِيّ) ء جُزْء part, portion

Key Rule for This Chapter

When hamza begins a word, it always sits on an أَلِف:

  • If the vowel on hamza is fatḥa or ḍamma, write أَ / أُ — e.g., أَب (father), أُم (mother).
  • If the vowel on hamza is kasra, write إِ — e.g., إِخْوَة (brothers).

This rule covers most of the hamza you will encounter in family vocabulary.

Common Mistake

Learners often write ا (plain alif) instead of أ or إ with hamza. Plain alif is never used at the beginning of a word when the initial sound is a glottal stop followed by a vowel. The word father is أَب, not اَب.

2. Hamzat al-waṣl — هَمْزَة الوَصْل

A separate alif-letter appears on the اِ of the definite article الـ and on several verb and noun beginnings. Unlike regular hamza, this connecting hamza is not pronounced when the word is linked to the word before it in normal speech. It is typically written without a hamza sign above it in everyday writing, but you will see it marked in fully vocalized texts. The article الـ you have been writing since Chapter 2 begins with hamzat al-waṣl.

3. The Special Letters

التَّاء المَرْبُوطَة — Tāʾ Marbūṭa

Tāʾ marbūṭa (ـَة / ة) is a closed تَاء written at the end of nouns and adjectives. It has two functions you need now:

  1. Feminine marker: Adding ـَة to many masculine nouns and adjectives creates the feminine form — طَالِب (male student) → طَالِبَة (female student).
  2. Pronunciation: In ordinary speech and pause position it is pronounced as a quiet -a or dropped. It is fully pronounced as -at only in connected reading when followed by a suffix or in a construct phrase: مَدِينَة → pronounced madīna in pause; مَدِينَةُ القَاهِرَة → madīnatu l-qāhira in connected reading.

Tāʾ marbūṭa in this chapter

أُخْت → أُخْتَانِ (two sisters)
مُدَرِّس → مُدَرِّسَة (female teacher)
عَائِلَة (family)

الأَلِف المَقْصُورَة — Alif Maqṣūra

Alif maqṣūra (ى) looks like a يَاء without dots and appears only at the end of words. It is pronounced as a long ā sound. You already know this letter from words like عَلَى (on), إِلَى (to), and the name لَيْلَى. Do not confuse it with يَاء — the presence or absence of dots is the key distinction.

أَلِف المَدّ — Alif with Madda

When hamza-above-alif (أ) is followed by a long ā vowel, the two are written together as a single sign: آ (called alif madd or alif with a tilde). You will see it in آسِيَا (Asia) and later in words like آخَر (other). Recognize it as ʾā: a glottal stop followed immediately by a long ā.

Cultural Note

Arabic distinguishes paternal relatives (عَمّ / عَمَّة) from maternal relatives (خَال / خَالَة). This distinction is maintained in many Arabic-speaking communities and has cultural significance for social and family structure. The same word cousin in English covers four separate Arabic words depending on the side of the family and the gender of the cousin.

Pronunciation Focus

The Glottal Stop — Hamza

Hamza ء is produced by briefly closing and then releasing the glottis — the space between your vocal cords. You make this sound naturally in English at the beginning of a stressed vowel syllable: the gap between "uh" and "oh" in uh-oh, or the start of "Ann" when said with emphasis. In Arabic, hamza is a full consonant that can appear anywhere in a word and must be clearly produced.

Practice the contrast between words with and without the glottal stop:

أَب — ʾab (father): starts with a distinct glottal stop
أُم — ʾumm (mother): glottal stop + long u-like vowel
أَخ — ʾakh (brother): glottal stop, then kh-sound

Tāʾ Marbūṭa in Pause Position

In ordinary speech, when a word ending in ـَة is said in pause (at the end of a phrase or utterance), the -t is not pronounced. The word ends with a short -a or is even slightly shortened:

عَائِلَة → sounds like ʿāʾila (not ʿāʾilat) in pause
مُدَرِّسَة → sounds like mudarrisa in pause

This is normal and expected. The -t appears clearly when the word is in a construct phrase (see Grammar below) and is immediately followed by the next word.

Grammar

1. Plurals in Arabic — الجَمْع

Arabic forms plurals in two ways. You will work with both in this chapter.

A. Sound Plurals — الجَمْع السَّالِم

Sound plurals add a suffix to the singular without changing the internal shape of the word.

Sound Masculine Plural (for people)

Add ـُون (in connected contexts) or ـِين (in some grammatical positions) to the masculine singular:

مُدَرِّس → مُدَرِّسُون  (teachers, male)
مُهَنْدِس → مُهَنْدِسُون  (engineers, male)
طَالِب → طُلَّاب  (students — note: this one is actually a broken plural)

For now, learn the sound masculine plural suffix as ـُون. You will learn the ـِين alternation in Chapter 9.

Sound Feminine Plural (for people and many other nouns)

Drop the ـَة (if present) and add ـَات:

مُدَرِّسَة → مُدَرِّسَات  (teachers, female)
طَالِبَة → طَالِبَات  (students, female)
عَائِلَة → عَائِلَات  (families)

B. Broken Plurals — الجَمْع التَّكْسِير

Broken plurals change the internal structure of the word — vowels shift, letters may be added or dropped, and the resulting form can look very different from the singular. There is no single rule: the plural of each noun must be learned alongside the noun itself.

Singular Meaning Broken Plural Meaning (pl.) Pattern
أَب father آبَاء fathers أَفْعَال
أَخ brother إِخْوَة brothers إِفْعَلَة
ابْن son أَبْنَاء sons أَفْعَال
بِنْت daughter بَنَات daughters sound fem. form
جَدّ grandfather أَجْدَاد grandfathers أَفْعَال
أُم mother أُمَّهَات mothers irregular

Strategy for Broken Plurals

Always learn the plural form when you learn a new noun. The vocabulary tables in this textbook always list the plural. Treating singular and plural as a single unit — not two separate words — is the most reliable approach.

2. The Construct State — الإِضَافَة

The iḍāfa (construct phrase) is the primary Arabic structure for expressing possession, relationship, and association. It consists of two or more nouns placed next to each other without any article on the first noun and without any word for "of" or "'s".

Structure

[First noun — no article] + [Second noun or pronoun suffix]

The relationship is read as "the [first noun] of [second noun]" or "[second noun]'s [first noun]."

A. Two-noun iḍāfa

اسْمُ الطَّالِب — the name of the student / the student's name
بَيْتُ العَائِلَة — the house of the family / the family's house
عَمَلُ الأَب — the work of the father / the father's job

Critical Rule

The first noun in an iḍāfa never takes the definite article الـ. The second noun may or may not be definite. If the second noun has الـ, the whole phrase is considered definite.

❌ الاسمُ الطَّالِب — incorrect
✓ اسمُ الطَّالِب — correct: the student's name

B. Iḍāfa with pronoun suffixes — possessives

When the "second noun" is a personal pronoun, it is expressed as a suffix attached directly to the first noun. These are the possessive suffixes:

Person Suffix Example with أَب Meaning
my ـِي أَبِي my father
your (m. sg.) ـَكَ أَبُوكَ your father
your (f. sg.) ـَكِ أَبُوكِ your father
his ـُه / ـَه أَبُوهُ his father
her ـَهَا أَبُوهَا her father
our ـَنَا أَبُونَا our father

Key Forms for This Chapter

Focus on the first-person form ـِي (my) for now, as it is the most immediately useful for introducing your family.

أَبِي — my father   أُمِّي — my mother
أَخِي — my brother   أُخْتِي — my sister
جَدِّي — my grandfather   جَدَّتِي — my grandmother
عَائِلَتِي — my family   اسْمِي — my name

Note: When nouns ending in ـَة (tāʾ marbūṭa) take the suffix ـِي, the -t of tāʾ marbūṭa reappears: عَائِلَة + ي = عَائِلَتِي. This is the standard behavior of tāʾ marbūṭa in construct position.

C. The "name" construct — introducing family members by name

A common and very useful pattern for introducing family members uses اسْمُهُ / اسْمُهَا (his/her name is):

أَخِي اسْمُهُ يُوسُف. — My brother's name is Yusuf.
أُخْتِي اسْمُهَا سَلْمَى. — My sister's name is Salma.
أَبِي اسْمُهُ خَالِد. — My father's name is Khalid.

3. Adjective Agreement Review

You learned adjective agreement in Chapter 2. Apply it now to family descriptions. Remember: the adjective follows the noun and matches it in gender and definiteness.

أَبِي طَبِيبٌ مَشْغُول. — My father is a busy doctor.
أُمِّي مُدَرِّسَةٌ لَطِيفَة. — My mother is a kind teacher.
أَخِي طَالِبٌ كَبِير. — My brother is an older student.

عِنْدِي ثَلَاثَةُ إِخْوَة وَأُخْتٌ وَاحِدَة. إِخْوَتِي أَكْبَرُ مِنِّي. أَكْبَرُ أَخٍ عِنْدِي اسْمُهُ سَعِيد، وَهُوَ طَالِبٌ فِي جَامِعَة أَبُو ظَبْي. أُخْتِي الصَّغِيرَة اسْمُهَا شَيْخَة، وَهِيَ طَالِبَة أَيْضًا.

جَدِّي وَجَدَّتِي يَسْكُنَانِ فِي دُبَي. جَدِّي طَبِيبٌ مُتَقَاعِد. جَدَّتِي رَبَّةُ بَيْت.

Vocabulary support

أَسْكُنُ — I live  |  مَعَ — with  |  ثَلَاثَة — three  |  أَكْبَرُ مِنِّي — older than me  |  جَامِعَة — university  |  أَيْضًا — also  |  مُتَقَاعِد — retired  |  رَبَّةُ بَيْت — homemaker

These words appear as receptive support only. You are not required to produce them actively in this chapter.

Comprehension Tasks

  1. Where does Noura live and with whom?
  2. What is Noura's father's name and profession?
  3. How many brothers does Noura have?
  4. What does Noura's oldest brother study?
  5. Where do Noura's grandparents live?
  6. True or false: Noura's grandfather is still working.
Answer Key
  1. She lives in Abu Dhabi with her family.
  2. His name is Khalid. He is an engineer.
  3. She has three brothers.
  4. He studies at Abu Dhabi University (he is a student there).
  5. They live in Dubai.
  6. False — he is a retired doctor.

Reading 2 — Identifying Iḍāfa Phrases

Re-read Noura's profile and underline every iḍāfa phrase you can identify (two-noun constructs or noun + pronoun suffix). Compare your list with the answer key.

Answer Key

Iḍāfa phrases in the text include (among others):

  • عَائِلَتِي — my family (noun + suffix)
  • أَبِي — my father (noun + suffix)
  • أُمِّي — my mother (noun + suffix)
  • اسمُهُ / اسمُهَا — his/her name (noun + suffix)
  • إِخْوَتِي — my brothers (noun + suffix)
  • أُخْتِي الصَّغِيرَة — my younger sister (noun + suffix + adjective)
  • طَالِبٌ فِي جَامِعَة أَبُو ظَبْي — student at U of Abu Dhabi (not an iḍāfa — preposition used instead)
  • جَدِّي / جَدَّتِي — my grandfather / grandmother (noun + suffix)
  • رَبَّةُ بَيْت — homemaker / mistress of the house (two-noun iḍāfa)

Culture and Communication

Family as a Central Social Unit

Across Arabic-speaking communities — from Morocco to the Gulf, from the Levant to Sudan — family identity plays a significant role in social interaction. A person's family name, region of origin, and tribal or clan affiliation are often woven into introductions and considered important context.

In many Arabic-speaking contexts, asking about someone's family early in a conversation is considered warm and interested rather than intrusive. Questions like مِن أَيْنَ عَائِلَتُكَ؟ (Where is your family from?) or عِنْدَكَ إِخْوَة؟ (Do you have siblings?) often appear naturally after an initial greeting.

Names and Patronymic Naming

In traditional Arabic naming, a person's full name can include a chain of patronymics: given name — father's name — grandfather's name — family name. You may encounter names like مُحَمَّد بن إِبْرَاهِيم البَلَوِي. In everyday use, people typically use a shorter form. The patterns vary by country and community.

Terms of Address for Family Members

In many Arabic-speaking contexts, family terms are also used as respectful forms of address beyond the biological family. عَمّ and عَمَّة may be used to address an older man or woman who is not a biological uncle or aunt. أُخْت and أَخ are used among acquaintances and even strangers in some communities as a sign of solidarity and warmth. Context and regional norms vary considerably.

Language Note

When Arabic speakers say بِسْمِ اللهِ الرَّحْمَٰنِ الرَّحِيم or shorter phrases like إِن شَاء اللَّه in family conversations, these are social and cultural expressions whose frequency and significance vary among individuals, regions, and religious backgrounds. They are formulaic expressions with wide social use and should not be interpreted as exclusively religious statements in every context. You will encounter them as receptive cultural input throughout the series.

Common Mistakes

Mistake 1 — Adding الـ to the first noun of an iḍāfa

❌ الاسمُ الأَخِ
✓ اسمُ الأَخِ — the brother's name

The first noun in a construct phrase never takes the definite article.

Mistake 2 — Writing alif instead of hamza-on-alif

❌ اَب، اُم
✓ أَب، أُم

Plain alif without hamza is never used when the word begins with a glottal stop + vowel.

Mistake 3 — Forgetting tāʾ marbūṭa before pronoun suffixes

❌ عَائِلَيِي (wrong shape)
✓ عَائِلَتِي — my family

The -t- of tāʾ marbūṭa reappears when a suffix is attached.

Mistake 4 — Using a sound plural when the noun has a broken plural

❌ أَبُون (invented sound plural)
✓ آبَاء — fathers

Not all Arabic nouns form their plural with a simple suffix. Check the vocabulary table for each noun's actual plural form.

Mistake 5 — Adjective gender mismatch

❌ أُمِّي لَطِيف
✓ أُمِّي لَطِيفَة — my mother is kind

The adjective must match the gender of the noun or pronoun it describes. أُم is feminine; the adjective takes the feminine ـَة suffix.

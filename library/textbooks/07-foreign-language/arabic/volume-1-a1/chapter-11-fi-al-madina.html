فِي المَدِينَةGetting Around

Imperatives, directions, movement, and prepositional pronoun suffixes · Speak and Write Arabic: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Give and follow simple commands using the MSA imperative for common movement verbs.
  2. Ask for and give basic directions in a city using prepositions of location and movement.
  3. Name key places in a city and describe how to get from one to another.
  4. Attach pronoun suffixes to prepositions to refer to people and places already mentioned.
  5. Recycle demonstratives, adjective agreement, and location language from Chapter 6 within a new communicative context.
  6. Recycle imperfect-tense verb forms from Chapter 7 to describe ongoing movement and routine errands.
  7. Read and interpret simple street signs, maps, and short direction-giving texts.
  8. Produce a short spoken or written set of directions from one place to another.

Communication Scenario

Unit III opens in the wider community. Tom needs to find the post office to send a letter, but he does not know downtown Amman well. He stops a stranger on the street, خَالِد, and asks for directions. Khalid gives him clear, step-by-step instructions using command forms, prepositions, and the names of familiar landmarks.

  1. Before you begin, think about how you ask for directions in English: what verbs do you use, and how do you describe where something is?
  2. This chapter introduces the command form for the first time. You will also learn to describe movement — going toward, away from, past, and around — using prepositions attached directly to pronouns.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Note — The imperative and stress

Command forms often begin with a short helping vowel when the first root consonant would otherwise start a word with two consonants together, such as اُدْخُلْ ("enter!"). In careful pronunciation, this helping vowel is short and light — do not stress it. Stress instead falls on the syllable containing the root's main vowel, so اُدْخُلْ is pronounced with the stress on khul, not on the initial vowel.

Continue applying the emphatic/plain and pharyngeal contrasts from earlier chapters; several of this chapter's new verbs contain the pharyngeal ع (as in عَبَرَ), which should remain distinct from a plain glottal stop.

القَوَاعِد — Grammar Focus

The Imperative — الأَمْر

The imperative lets you give instructions and directions directly — exactly what Khalid does for Tom in this chapter's scenario.

Building Block — Forming the Form I imperative
imperfect stem (2nd person) → drop prefix → add helping vowel if needed
The Volume I target is the singular masculine and singular feminine command forms, formed from the "you" (anta / anti) imperfect forms taught in Chapter 7.
تَذْهَب ← اِذْهَبْ
you go (imperfect) → go! (command, masc.)
  • اِذْهَبْ إِلَى مَكْتَب البَرِيد.Go to the post office! (to a man)imperative, masc. sg.
  • اُدْخُلِي المَحَطَّة.Enter the station! (to a woman)imperative, fem. sg.
  • اِمْشِ عَلَى طُول.Walk straight ahead! (to a man)imperative of a weak verb
  • اُعْبُرِ الشَّارِع.Cross the street! (to a woman)imperative, fem. sg.
Grammar Note — Only masculine and feminine singular this chapter

MSA has separate imperative forms for singular, dual, and plural, masculine and feminine. Volume I activates only the singular masculine and singular feminine forms, since directions are most often given to one listener at a time. Plural and dual imperative forms are receptive only at this stage and will become productive in a later volume.

تَدْرِيب — Practice: Give the correct imperative form for the person indicated.

  1. (أَنْتَ) اِنْعَطَفَ ______ يَمِينًا عِنْدَ التَّقَاطُع. (to a man: turn right at the intersection)
  2. (أَنْتِ) ذَهَبَ ______ إِلَى المَصْرِف. (to a woman: go to the bank)
  3. (أَنْتَ) خَرَجَ ______ مِنَ المَحَطَّة. (to a man: leave the station)

Prepositional Pronoun Suffixes — ضَمَائِر مُتَّصِلَة بِحُرُوف الجَرّ

Once a place or person has been named, Arabic attaches the pronoun directly to the preposition rather than repeating the noun — the same principle you practiced with object suffixes in Chapter 9, now extended to prepositions of location and movement.

  • هَذَا مَكْتَب البَرِيد. اِذْهَبْ إِلَيْهِ.This is the post office. Go to it.إِلَى + هُ → إِلَيْهِ
  • المَحَطَّة بَعِيدَة. لَا تَخْرُج مِنْهَا الآن.The station is far. Don't leave it now.مِنْ + هَا → مِنْهَا
  • البَنْك أَمَامَنَا.The bank is in front of us.أَمَام + نَا → أَمَامَنَا
  • المَحَلّ بِجَانِبِهَا.The shop is beside it (fem.).بِجَانِب + هَا → بِجَانِبِهَا
Grammar Note — Recycling a familiar system in a new context

You met pronoun suffixes on nouns and prepositions in Chapter 4 and on verbs in Chapter 9. This chapter does not introduce a new suffix system; it expands the same suffixes to new prepositions used specifically for directions and location: إِلَى, مِنْ, أَمَام, and بِجَانِب. Watch for the same spelling shift you already know: إِلَى becomes إِلَيْ before most suffixes.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Tom stops a stranger, Khalid, on a downtown street to ask for directions to the post office.
السِّيَاق: يُوقِف تُوم شَخْصًا غَرِيبًا، خَالِد، فِي شَارِع وَسَط البَلَد لِيَسْأَلَهُ عَنِ الطَّرِيق إِلَى مَكْتَب البَرِيد.

TOM عَفْوًا، أَيْنَ مَكْتَب البَرِيد، مِنْ فَضْلِك؟ Excuse me, where is the post office, please?
KHALID لَيْسَ بَعِيدًا. اِمْشِ عَلَى طُول حَتَّى التَّقَاطُع. It's not far. Walk straight until the intersection.
TOM حَسَنًا، وَبَعْد ذَلِكَ؟ Okay, and after that?
KHALID اِنْعَطِفْ يَمِينًا عِنْدَ إِشَارَة المُرُور. سَتَرَى مَصْرِفًا — مَكْتَب البَرِيد بِجَانِبِهِ. Turn right at the traffic light. You'll see a bank — the post office is beside it.
TOM هَلْ أَعْبُر الشَّارِع؟ Do I cross the street?
KHALID لَا، لَا تَعْبُرْهُ. مَكْتَب البَرِيد عَلَى هَذَا الجَانِب. No, don't cross it. The post office is on this side.
TOM شُكْرًا جَزِيلًا لَك! Thank you very much!

  2. Identify one example of a prepositional pronoun suffix and name the noun it replaces.
  3. هَلْ يَعْبُر تُوم الشَّارِع؟ (Does Tom cross the street?)

Writing Workshop

In this chapter's writing task, you will write a short set of directions from your school or workplace to a nearby landmark.

  1. Choose a starting point and a destination. Pick two real or invented places connected by a short, simple route.
  2. List the steps. Jot down each turn, landmark, and action in order.
  3. Draft the directions. Write four to six sentences using imperative verbs, at least one prepositional pronoun suffix, and at least one landmark noun from this chapter.
  4. Recycle one earlier system. Include a demonstrative or adjective-agreement phrase from Chapter 6 describing one of the landmarks.
  5. Revise. Check your draft against the editing checklist below.

قَائِمَة المُرَاجَعَة — Editing checklist:

  • I used at least two correctly formed imperative verbs, matched to the listener's gender.
  • I used at least one prepositional pronoun suffix correctly.
  • I named at least two places from this chapter's vocabulary.
  • I included a demonstrative or adjective-agreement phrase recycled from Chapter 6.
  • My directions form one connected sequence rather than a disconnected list.
  • I wrote all letters and endings with correct right-to-left joining.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Landmarks over addresses

In many cities across the Arabic-speaking world, directions are commonly given using landmarks — a bank, a mosque, a well-known shop — rather than precise street numbers, which may be inconsistently posted or little used in everyday speech. This is common practice in many cities, not a universal rule, and formal postal or delivery addresses may still rely on numbered systems. The landmark-based style practiced in this chapter reflects one widespread, practical pattern of everyday direction-giving.

Regional Variation — Direction words in spoken Arabic

The MSA direction and command forms in this chapter are the productive target. In everyday spoken Arabic across different regions, learners will often hear different pronunciations or shortened command forms in casual speech. If your instructor introduces a labeled regional comparison, treat it as receptive and comparative information rather than a substitute for the MSA forms practiced here.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using the imperfect instead of the imperative

Error: Giving a command using the plain imperfect form, such as *أَنْتَ تَذْهَب إِلَى المَحَطَّة when the intent is a direct instruction.

Correction: Use the dedicated imperative form for direct commands: اِذْهَبْ إِلَى المَحَطَّة.

Common Mistake — Forgetting the feminine imperative ending

Error: Using the masculine imperative when addressing a woman, such as *اُدْخُل instead of اُدْخُلِي.

Correction: Match the imperative ending to the listener's gender, as with all agreement systems introduced since Chapter 1: اُدْخُلِي المَحَطَّة.

Common Mistake — Repeating the noun instead of the prepositional suffix

Error: Repeating a place noun a second time after it has already been introduced, such as *مَكْتَب البَرِيد بِجَانِب مَكْتَب البَرِيد.

Correction: Once the place is established, attach the pronoun suffix to the preposition instead: مَكْتَب البَرِيد بِجَانِبِهِ becomes unnecessary here — instead say البَنْك بِجَانِبِهِ, referring back to the post office already named.

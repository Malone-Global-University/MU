مُرَاجَعَة ٢: يَوْم عَمَلِيّUnit II Assessment: A Working Day

Linked daily-life transactions and practical reading, synthesizing Chapters 6-9 · Speak and Write Arabic: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

This chapter introduces no new grammar. By its end, you will be able to demonstrate:

  1. Independent use of location, demonstratives, and adjective agreement to describe a home and neighborhood (Chapter 6).
  2. Independent use of the imperfect present, subject marking, and time expressions to narrate a daily routine (Chapter 7).
  3. Independent use of direct objects, quantities, numerals, and requests to order food and drink (Chapter 8).
  4. Independent use of the dual, expanded plural agreement, comparison, and attached object pronouns to shop for everyday goods (Chapter 9).
  5. Movement between these systems within a single connected day, without treating each chapter's language as separate from the others.
  6. Reading a set of linked practical texts — a schedule, a menu, a price list, and a short message — and extracting the information a real errand-runner would need.
  7. Producing a complete spoken and written account of one working day, combining home, routine, food, and shopping language.

Communication Scenario

It is a Thursday in Amman. Tom has a busy day ahead: he wakes up in his apartment near the institute, follows his usual morning routine, has lunch at a small restaurant near the market, and then stops at the clothing shop from Chapter 9 to pick up the shirt he ordered. In the evening, he writes a short message to his host family describing his day. Nothing in this account requires new grammar — it simply requires moving smoothly among the systems you have already built.

  1. Before you begin, think through your own day in the same order: where you are, what you do first, what you eat, and what errands you run. Could you narrate it in Arabic right now, using only Chapters 1-9?
  2. This chapter does not teach anything new. It asks you to show what you can already do, linked together the way real days actually happen.

أَسْئِلَة الفَهْم — Comprehension questions:

  1. أَيْنَ يَسْكُن تُوم؟ (Where does Tom live, according to Text 1?)
  2. According to Text 2, how many dinars would a sandwich and a cup of coffee cost together?
  3. According to Text 3, how many dinars did Tom save by choosing the blue shirt instead of the white one?
  4. Text 4 mixes routine language (Chapter 7) with a few past-tense forms not yet studied productively, such as اشْتَرَيْتُ. Find one example and explain from context what it must mean, without needing its grammar name.
Reading Note — Linked texts describe one connected day

Notice that the four texts above are not four unrelated readings. Together they trace a single day: a schedule, a menu that supports part of that schedule, a price tag that supports another part, and a message that reports on the whole day afterward. Real reading in daily life usually works this way — several short, purpose-built texts that only make full sense together.

Linked Dialogue

Setting: In the evening, Tom's neighbor Samar stops by and asks about his day. The exchange moves through home, routine, food, and shopping language in a single natural conversation.
السِّيَاق: فِي المَسَاء، تَزُور سَمَر، جَارَة تُوم، وَتَسْأَلُهُ عَنْ يَوْمِهِ. يَتَنَقَّل الحِوَار بَيْنَ لُغَة البَيْت وَالرُّوتِين وَالطَّعَام وَالتَّسَوُّق فِي مُحَادَثَة وَاحِدَة طَبِيعِيَّة.

SAMAR مَسَاء الخَيْر، تُوم! كَيْفَ كَانَ يَوْمُك؟ Good evening, Tom! How was your day?
TOM مَسَاء النُّور! يَوْمِي عَادِيّ. أَسْتَيْقِظ كُلّ يَوْم فِي هَذَا البَيْت فِي السَّاعَة السَّابِعَة. Good evening to you! My day is ordinary. I wake up every day in this house at seven o'clock.
SAMAR وَأَيْنَ تَتَغَدَّى عَادَةً؟ And where do you usually have lunch?
TOM أَتَغَدَّى قُرْب السُّوق. أَطْلُب عَادَةً سَنْدَوِيتْش وَفِنْجَان قَهْوَة. I have lunch near the market. I usually order a sandwich and a cup of coffee.
SAMAR جَمِيل! وَهَذَا القَمِيص الجَدِيد — مِنْ أَيْنَ؟ Nice! And this new shirt — where from?
TOM مِنَ المَحَلّ قُرْب السُّوق. كَانَ عِنْدَهُمْ قَمِيصَان: أَزْرَق وَأَبْيَض. الأَزْرَق أَرْخَص، فَأُرِيدُهُ. From the shop near the market. They had two shirts: blue and white. The blue one is cheaper, so I want it.
SAMAR اِخْتِيَار جَيِّد! أَنَا أَيْضًا أُحِبّ هَذَا اللَّوْن. Good choice! I like that color too.

أَسْئِلَة الفَهْم — Comprehension questions:

  1. Identify one sentence in the dialogue that draws on Chapter 6 (location/demonstratives), one that draws on Chapter 7 (routine), one that draws on Chapter 8 (food/drink), and one that draws on Chapter 9 (shopping/comparison).
  2. مَاذَا يَطْلُب تُوم عَادَةً؟ (What does Tom usually order?)
  3. Why does Tom want the blue shirt rather than the white one?

مُهِمَّة تَوَاصُل — Communicative task: A working day, start to finish

Working alone or with a partner acting as an interviewer, narrate one full day aloud without stopping, moving through all four stations below in order.

  1. Describe where you live and one feature of your home or neighborhood.
  2. State what time you wake up and describe one other part of your daily routine.
  3. Describe what you eat or drink for one meal, including a quantity or number.
  4. Describe one shopping decision, including a comparison and an attached object pronoun.

لِلْمُتَعَلِّمِينَ المُسْتَقِلِّينَ — Solo alternative

Record yourself narrating your actual day using the four-station structure above. Listen back and check that you moved smoothly between systems without needing to stop and translate.

Writing Station

In this chapter's writing task, you will produce one connected account of a working day, combining all four Unit II systems in a single piece of writing — the same synthesis Tom achieves in his evening message in the Reading Station.

  1. Study the model. Reread Text 4 in the Reading Station. Notice how it moves from home, to routine, to a meal, to a shopping decision, in a natural order.
  2. Plan four short sections. Jot down one idea for each: where you live, your routine, a meal, and a shopping decision.
  3. Draft the connected account. Write five to seven sentences that move through all four sections without a heading between them, the way a real message would.
  4. Include one example of each target system: a demonstrative or location phrase (Ch. 6), an imperfect-tense routine verb (Ch. 7), a direct object with a quantity (Ch. 8), and a comparative form or attached object pronoun (Ch. 9).
  5. Revise. Check your draft against the editing checklist below.

قَائِمَة المُرَاجَعَة — Editing checklist:

  • I described my home or neighborhood using a demonstrative and correct adjective agreement.
  • I used at least one imperfect-tense verb correctly marked for my subject.
  • I named a food or drink item with a number or quantity.
  • I used a comparative form, an attached object pronoun, or both.
  • My four sections connect as one account rather than four separate lists.
  • I wrote all letters and endings with correct right-to-left joining.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — A working day looks different across the Arabic-speaking world

The daily rhythm in this chapter's scenario — an early morning, a midday meal near a market, an afternoon errand — reflects one plausible day in urban Jordan, not a single Arabic-speaking pattern. Work schedules, meal timing, shop hours, and the size of the midday meal all vary by country, city, workplace, and individual household. As in earlier chapters, treat this scenario as one example rather than a universal template.

Regional Variation — Reviewing without re-teaching dialect

This assessment chapter, like the four chapters it reviews, keeps its productive model in MSA. If your instructor has introduced labeled regional comparisons alongside Chapters 6-9, this is a natural point to revisit them — for example, comparing how a shopkeeper or restaurant server might phrase a request in local spoken Arabic versus the MSA forms practiced here.

Common Mistakes — Unit Review

Common Mistake — Feminine singular agreement forgotten under pressure

Error: Reverting to human plural adjective agreement for nonhuman plural nouns when speaking quickly, such as *هَذِهِ البُيُوت كَبِيرُون.

Correction: Nonhuman plurals still take feminine singular agreement, as established in Chapter 6 and recycled in Chapter 9: هَذِهِ البُيُوت كَبِيرَة.

Common Mistake — Dropping subject marking on imperfect verbs

Error: Using the same imperfect form for every person, such as saying *هِيَ أَسْتَيْقِظ instead of هِيَ تَسْتَيْقِظ.

Correction: Keep the subject-marking prefixes from Chapter 7 distinct for each person, even when combining routine language with other Unit II systems.

Common Mistake — Omitting the quantity with a direct object

Error: Saying *أَطْلُب قَهْوَة without a number when the context calls for one, such as ordering more than one cup.

Correction: Include the quantity when it matters: أَطْلُب فِنْجَانَيْ قَهْوَة or أَطْلُب فِنْجَان قَهْوَة وَاحِد, applying Chapter 8's numeral patterns.

Common Mistake — Repeating the noun instead of the object suffix

Error: Saying *أُرِيد القَمِيص الأَزْرَق a second time in the same conversation after the shirt has already been named.

Correction: Once the item is established, attach the object pronoun instead: أُرِيدُهُ, as practiced in Chapter 9.

Chapter Review

Section A — تَحْدِيد النِّظَام (System identification)

1. For each sentence, name the Unit II chapter (6, 7, 8, or 9) whose system it demonstrates: (a) هَذَا البَيْت جَمِيل. (b) تَدْرُس فِي الصَّبَاح. (c) أَطْلُب سَلَطَتَيْن. (d) هَذِهِ الحَقِيبَة أَرْخَص.

Section B — القَوَاعِد التَّرَاكُمِيَّة (Cumulative grammar)

1. Correct the error: *هَذِهِ الأَحْذِيَة جَمِيلُون.

2. Give the correct imperfect form: هِيَ (يَسْتَيْقِظ) فِي السَّاعَة السَّابِعَة.

3. Rewrite using an attached object pronoun: أُرِيد القَمِيص.

4. Give the comparative form of رَخِيص.

Section C — اسْتِيعَاب المَقْرُوء (Reading comprehension)

1. According to the Reading Station texts, list the four stops in Tom's day in order.

2. What is the total price, in dinars, of everything Tom orders at the restaurant in Text 2, based on the items named in Text 4?

Chapter Performance Task

مُهِمَّة الأَدَاء — مُهِمَّة تَقْيِيمِيَّة

يَوْمِي العَمَلِيّ — My working day

This is the Unit II capstone task. Complete the following, combining every system from Chapters 6-9:

  • Written: Write a connected six- to eight-sentence account of one working day, covering your home or neighborhood, your daily routine, a meal with a quantity, and a shopping decision with a comparison and an attached object pronoun.
  • Reading: Read a new, unfamiliar set of linked texts provided by your instructor (a schedule, a menu, and a price tag) and answer three comprehension questions, identifying which Unit II chapter each text draws on.

Assessment focus: correct nonhuman plural agreement, accurate imperfect subject marking, correct use of direct objects and quantities, accurate dual and comparative formation, correctly attached object pronouns, and the ability to move between all four systems within one connected account.

Looking Ahead

مَا الَّذِي تَسْتَطِيعُ فِعْلَهُ الآن — What you can do now

You can describe your home and neighborhood, narrate your daily routine using the imperfect present, order food and drink with quantities and requests, and shop for everyday goods using the dual, plural agreement, comparison, and attached object pronouns. You can also move fluidly among all four of these systems within a single connected account, spoken or written, and read a linked set of practical texts describing someone else's day.

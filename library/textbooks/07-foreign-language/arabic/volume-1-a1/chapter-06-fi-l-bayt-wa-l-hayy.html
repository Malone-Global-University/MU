فِي البَيْت وَالحَيّHome and Neighborhood

Describing your home, using demonstratives, locating things and places, and nonhuman plural agreement · Speak and Write Arabic: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Name the rooms of a home and common pieces of furniture.
  2. Name common places and features of a neighborhood.
  3. Use the demonstratives هَذَا, هَذِهِ, ذَلِكَ, and تِلْكَ to point out people and things at different distances.
  4. Use the plural demonstrative هَؤُلَاء for people.
  5. Locate people and objects using prepositions of place such as فِي, عَلَى, تَحْتَ, فَوْقَ, بِجَانِب, أَمَام, and وَرَاء.
  6. Apply nonhuman plural agreement, in which nonhuman plural nouns take feminine singular adjective and pronoun agreement.
  7. Describe a home and neighborhood using previously learned adjectives.
  8. Ask and answer أَيْنَ questions about the location of rooms, furniture, and neighborhood places.
  9. Read and write a short connected description of a home.

Communication Scenario

Tom has just moved into a small apartment near the institute in Amman. His classmate Karīm, whom he met at the welcome reception, is coming over for the first time to help him get settled and to see the neighborhood. Tom wants to describe his apartment, point out where things are, and show Karīm around the nearby streets before they walk to a café together.

  1. Think about how you would describe your own home in a few sentences: how many rooms it has, what is in each room, and what is nearby. Could you do this in Arabic right now?
  2. You can already introduce yourself, describe people and objects with adjectives, and exchange basic personal information. This chapter adds the language you need to describe where you live and to point out and locate things and places around you.

Pronunciation Review

This chapter introduces no new letters or sounds. Instead, use it to consolidate the full 28-letter alphabet and the sounds practiced in Chapters 1 through 5, with special attention to two features that appear often in this chapter's vocabulary.

الشَّدَّة فِي شَقَّة وَحَيّ — Shadda in everyday words

Several new words in this chapter contain shadda, marking a doubled consonant: شَقَّة (shaqqa), حَيّ (ḥayy), and مَحَلّ (maḥall). Hold the doubled consonant slightly longer than a single consonant, as you practiced with shadda in Chapter 2.

حَرْف العَيْن فِي المُفْرَدَات الجَدِيدَة — ʿAyn in new vocabulary

Continue practicing the pharyngeal ع from Chapter 3 in this chapter's words, such as قَرِيب is not affected, but شَارِع (shāriʿ) and مَوْقِف both draw on sounds you have already studied. Read each new vocabulary word aloud slowly before using it in a sentence.

Pronunciation Note — Stress in longer nouns

As Arabic words grow longer, stress generally falls on a syllable containing a long vowel or a syllable closed by two consonants, and does not simply fall on the first syllable as in many English words. In مَكْتَبَة (maktaba), for example, stress falls on the first syllable because of its consonant cluster, while in حَدِيقَة (ḥadīqa), stress falls on the syllable with the long vowel ī. Listening carefully to a model speaker remains the most reliable way to acquire natural stress.

تَدْرِيب — Practice: Read the neighborhood vocabulary list aloud twice, once slowly and once at a natural pace, paying attention to shadda and to the long vowels in words like الحَدِيقَة and المَكْتَبَة.

Grammar Instruction

6.1 هَذَا وَهَذِهِ وَذَلِكَ وَتِلْكَ — Demonstratives: this and that

Purpose: point out a specific person, place, or thing that is near or far from the speaker.

You have used هَذَا and هَذِهِ since Chapter 2 to identify people and classroom objects. This chapter completes the singular demonstrative system by adding the "far" forms and reviewing the "near" forms together:

ArabicTransliterationUsed withEnglish meaning
هَذَاhādhāmasculine singular, nearthis
هَذِهِhādhihifeminine singular, nearthis
ذَلِكَdhālikamasculine singular, farthat
تِلْكَtilkafeminine singular, farthat
  • هَذَا بَيْتِي.hādhā baytī. — This is my house.
  • ذَلِكَ المَسْجِد قَدِيم.dhālika l-masjid qadīm. — That mosque is old.
  • هَذِهِ غُرْفَة النَّوْم، وَتِلْكَ غُرْفَة الجُلُوس.hādhihi ghurfat an-nawm, wa-tilka ghurfat al-julūs. — This is the bedroom, and that is the living room.

For groups of people, use the plural demonstrative هَؤُلَاء ("these"), regardless of gender:

  • هَؤُلَاء أَصْدِقَائِي.hāʾulāʾi aṣdiqāʾī. — These are my friends.

هَؤُلَاء is presented here for people only. You will meet its use with nonhuman plurals, and the far-plural form, later in the volume once plural agreement is fully developed.

Grammar Note — Demonstrative as a noun phrase versus a full sentence

A demonstrative followed directly by a definite noun, such as هَذَا البَيْت ("this house"), forms a noun phrase, not a complete sentence. To make a complete sentence identifying or describing something, follow the demonstrative with an indefinite predicate, as in هَذَا بَيْت ("This is a house") or هَذَا البَيْت كَبِير ("This house is big"), where the definite noun phrase هَذَا البَيْت is followed by the indefinite adjective predicate كَبِير.

6.2 حُرُوف الجَرّ لِلْمَكَان — Locating things with prepositions

Purpose: describe the position of people, objects, and places relative to one another.

  • الكِتَاب عَلَى الطَّاوِلَة.al-kitāb ʿalā ṭ-ṭāwila. — The book is on the table.
  • القِطَّة تَحْتَ السَّرِير.al-qiṭṭa taḥta s-sarīr. — The cat is under the bed.
  • المَقْهَى بِجَانِب المَكْتَبَة.al-maqhā bijānib al-maktaba. — The café is next to the library.
  • الحَدِيقَة أَمَام البَيْت، وَالشَّارِع وَرَاءَه.al-ḥadīqa amām al-bayt, wa-sh-shāriʿ warāʾah. — The garden is in front of the house, and the street is behind it.

Each of these is a nominal sentence with a prepositional phrase as predicate, the same basic pattern you used in Chapter 3 with فِي. No verb "to be" appears in the present tense.

6.3 اتِّفَاق الجَمْع غَيْر العَاقِل — Nonhuman plural agreement

Purpose: correctly match adjectives, demonstratives, and pronouns to nonhuman plural nouns.

Arabic treats plural nouns that refer to people (human plurals) differently from plural nouns that refer to things, animals, and places (nonhuman plurals). A key rule for this chapter: a nonhuman plural noun takes feminine singular agreement on its adjective, demonstrative, and any referring pronoun — even though the noun itself is plural.

  • الكُتُب جَدِيدَة.al-kutub jadīda. — The books are new. (feminine singular adjective جَدِيدَة, though كُتُب "books" is plural)
  • هَذِهِ البُيُوت صَغِيرَة.hādhihi l-buyūt ṣaghīra. — These houses are small. (feminine singular demonstrative هَذِهِ and adjective صَغِيرَة with the plural noun بُيُوت)
  • الشَّوَارِع فِي هَذَا الحَيّ هَادِئَة.ash-shawāriʿ fī hādhā l-ḥayy hādiʾa. — The streets in this neighborhood are quiet.

Compare this with human plurals, which you will study fully later in the volume but which follow ordinary plural agreement rather than this feminine-singular pattern.

Grammar Note — Why feminine singular?

This pattern often surprises learners of English or other European languages, where plural nouns almost always take plural agreement. In Arabic, nonhuman plurals are grammatically treated as a collective, singular-like category, and that category patterns with feminine singular forms. This is a regular, productive rule of the language, not an exception or an error — you will see it constantly once you begin looking for it.

6.4 أَيْنَ...؟ — Asking about location

Purpose: ask where a person, object, room, or place is located, using the question word introduced in Chapter 3.

  • أَيْنَ المَطْبَخ؟ — المَطْبَخ بِجَانِب غُرْفَة الجُلُوس.ayna l-maṭbakh? — al-maṭbakh bijānib ghurfat al-julūs. — Where is the kitchen? — The kitchen is next to the living room.
  • أَيْنَ المَكْتَبَة؟ — هِيَ قَرِيبَة مِنَ البَيْت.ayna l-maktaba? — hiya qarība mina l-bayt. — Where is the library? — It is near the house.
Language Focus

Description depends on precise agreement. This chapter combines three systems you have been building since Chapter 2 — noun-adjective agreement, demonstratives, and nominal sentences — and adds a new agreement pattern specific to nonhuman plurals. As your descriptions grow longer, matching gender and number correctly becomes as important as choosing the right vocabulary. Reading your own sentences back and checking each adjective against its noun is a habit worth building now.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Karīm visits Tom's new apartment near the institute in Amman for the first time.
السِّيَاق: يَزُور كَرِيم شَقَّة تُوم الجَدِيدَة بِجَانِب المَعْهَد فِي عَمَّان لِأَوَّل مَرَّة.

KARĪM أَهْلًا تُوم! هَذِهِ شَقَّتُكَ الجَدِيدَة؟ Hi Tom! Is this your new apartment?
TOM نَعَمْ، تَفَضَّل! هَذِهِ غُرْفَة الجُلُوس، وَهَذَا المَطْبَخ. Yes, come in! This is the living room, and this is the kitchen.
KARĪM جَمِيلَة! أَيْنَ غُرْفَة النَّوْم؟ Beautiful! Where is the bedroom?
TOM هُنَاك، بِجَانِب الحَمَّام. السَّرِير تَحْتَ النَّافِذَة. There, next to the bathroom. The bed is under the window.
KARĪM وَمَا هَذِهِ الصُّوَر عَلَى الطَّاوِلَة؟ And what are these pictures on the table?
TOM صُوَر عَائِلَتِي. هَذِهِ صُوَر قَدِيمَة وَجَمِيلَة. Pictures of my family. These are old and beautiful pictures.
KARĪM جَيِّد. وَالحَيّ؟ هَلْ هُوَ هَادِئ؟ Nice. And the neighborhood? Is it quiet?
TOM نَعَمْ، هَادِئ جِدًّا. المَكْتَبَة وَالمَقْهَى قَرِيبَان مِنَ الشَّقَّة. Yes, very quiet. The library and the café are near the apartment.
KARĪM مُمْتَاز! هَيَّا نَذْهَب إِلَى المَقْهَى. Excellent! Let's go to the café.

أَسْئِلَة الفَهْم — Comprehension questions:

  1. أَيْنَ غُرْفَة النَّوْم؟ (Where is the bedroom?)
  2. أَيْنَ السَّرِير؟ (Where is the bed?)
  3. What is on the table, and how does Tom describe it?
  4. According to Tom, what two places are near his apartment?
Grammar Note — قَرِيبَان

Karim's line uses قَرِيبَان, a dual adjective form referring to two things (the library and the café) together. Dual forms are presented here for recognition only; you will study the dual system productively later in the volume.

Reading Note — Different housing patterns across the Arabic-speaking world

Apartment living is extremely common in cities across the Arabic-speaking world, just as it is in Amman, but housing patterns vary widely by country, city, and family circumstance. Extended-family households, standalone houses, and high-rise apartment buildings all exist side by side in different communities. Avoid assuming that any single housing pattern described in this textbook represents all Arabic-speaking households.

Writing Workshop

In this chapter you will write a short connected description of your own home and neighborhood, applying nonhuman plural agreement correctly.

  1. Study the model. Look again at the "بَيْتِي" reading passage. Notice how it moves from a general statement, to naming the rooms, to describing specific items and their locations.
  2. List your rooms and furniture. Write a short list in Arabic of the rooms in your home and one or two pieces of furniture in each.
  3. Write your description. In five to six connected sentences, describe your home: its size, its rooms, at least two items and their locations using prepositions of place, and your neighborhood.
  4. Check nonhuman plural agreement. Underline every plural noun referring to a thing or place, and confirm that its adjective or predicate is feminine singular.
  5. Revise. Check your draft against the editing checklist below.

قَائِمَة المُرَاجَعَة — Editing checklist:

  • I used هَذَا, هَذِهِ, ذَلِكَ, or تِلْكَ correctly for near and far reference.
  • I used at least three prepositions of place accurately.
  • Every nonhuman plural noun in my description has a feminine singular adjective or predicate.
  • My singular adjectives agree in gender with the singular nouns they describe.
  • I wrote all letters with correct joining and direction.
  • I reread my sentences aloud to check that they sound natural.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Hospitality when visiting a home

Offering something to eat or drink to a guest is a widely shared courtesy across Arabic-speaking households, though the specific customs, foods, and level of formality vary by family, region, and occasion. A guest is also often expected to accept at least a small offering rather than declining outright, though this too varies. As with other cultural notes in this course, treat this as common practice rather than a universal rule for every household.

Culture in Context — Neighborhoods as social spaces

In many cities across the Arabic-speaking world, neighborhood cafés, small shops, and places of worship serve as everyday social gathering points, not only functional destinations. This is not universal, and city neighborhoods differ widely from rural areas and from one another, but it is a common enough pattern that it appears often in this chapter's reading and listening material.

Regional Variation — Words for "apartment"

This chapter uses شَقَّة, the common MSA and widely understood word for "apartment." Some regional varieties use other terms in everyday speech. As in earlier chapters, this textbook's productive vocabulary remains MSA; regional alternatives will continue to be introduced and clearly labeled as the course progresses.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Plural adjective with a nonhuman plural noun

Error: Writing *الغُرَف كَبِيرُون or a similar plural adjective form to describe rooms.

Correction: Nonhuman plurals take feminine singular agreement: الغُرَف كَبِيرَة.

Common Mistake — Confusing هَذَا and ذَلِكَ

Error: Using هَذَا for something far away, or ذَلِكَ for something right in front of the speaker.

Correction: Reserve هَذَا/هَذِهِ for near reference and ذَلِكَ/تِلْكَ for far reference, matching gender in both cases.

Common Mistake — Omitting the preposition in location sentences

Error: Writing *الكِتَاب الطَّاوِلَة without a preposition to connect the two nouns.

Correction: Location requires an explicit preposition: الكِتَاب عَلَى الطَّاوِلَة.

Common Mistake — Treating a definite demonstrative phrase as a complete sentence

Error: Writing *هَذَا البَيْت الكَبِير and expecting it to mean "This house is big."

Correction: A demonstrative plus definite noun plus definite adjective forms only a noun phrase ("this big house"). For a full sentence, the predicate must be indefinite: هَذَا البَيْت كَبِير.

مُهِمَّة الأَدَاء

جَوْلَة فِي بَيْتِي — A tour of my home

You have been asked to present your home and neighborhood to a new classmate who has never visited. Complete the following tasks:

  • Written: Write a five- to six-sentence description of your home and neighborhood, naming at least four rooms or furniture items and two neighborhood places.
  • Written: Include at least three prepositions of place and at least one sentence with nonhuman plural agreement.
  • Spoken: Present your description aloud to a partner as a short home tour, using demonstratives to point out at least three items.

Answer Key

Practice — هَذَا، هَذِهِ، ذَلِكَ، أَوْ تِلْكَ؟

1. هَذَا بَيْتِي.   2. تِلْكَ الحَدِيقَة بَعِيدَة.   3. هَذَا الكُرْسِيّ قَدِيم.   4. هَذِهِ الشَّقَّة صَغِيرَة.

Practice — Nonhuman plural agreement

1. الغُرَف كَبِيرَة.   2. المَحَلَّات فِي الحَيّ قَرِيبَة.   3. هَذِهِ الشَّوَارِع هَادِئَة.

Dialogue — Comprehension

  1. The bedroom is next to the bathroom.
  2. The bed is under the window.
  3. Pictures are on the table; Tom describes them as old and beautiful.
  4. The library and the café are near Tom's apartment.

Listening

  • 1 — بِجَانِب (next to) — the living room and kitchen are next to each other
  • 2 — أَمَام / وَرَاء (in front of / behind) — the garden and the shops relative to the house
  • 3 — قَرِيبَة مِنْ / بَعِيد (near / far) — distance of the library and café from the institute
  • 4 — عَلَى (on) — books on the table, pictures on the wall

Bonus: الصُّوَر is a nonhuman plural; its predicate عَلَى الحَائِط is a prepositional phrase, and any accompanying adjective would take the feminine singular form.

Reading — Comprehension

  1. The house has two bedrooms (غُرْفَتَا نَوْم, a dual form presented for recognition).
  2. The garden is in front of the house.
  3. Nonhuman plurals include المَحَلَّات (agreeing with قَرِيبَة) and الشَّوَارِع (agreeing with نَظِيفَة and هَادِئَة).
  4. نَظِيفَة appears to mean "clean," based on its pairing with "quiet" to describe pleasant streets.

أَهْلًا وَسَهْلًا! لِقَاءَات أُولَىFirst Encounters

Greetings, names, and your first Arabic letters · Speak and Write Arabic: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Greet someone and take leave using common Modern Standard Arabic (MSA) expressions.
  2. Ask and answer the question "What is your name?" using the fixed expressions مَا اسْمُكَ؟ and مَا اسْمُكِ؟
  3. Introduce yourself using the fixed expression اسْمِي... (My name is...).
  4. Use basic courtesy expressions, including please, thank you, and you're welcome.
  5. Recognize and write the first group of Arabic letters in isolated, initial, medial, and final positions.
  6. Read and write right to left, joining letters correctly within this chapter's letter set.
  7. Recognize the three short vowel marks and the long vowels they correspond to.
  8. Form a simple Arabic nominal sentence stating a name, with correct gender agreement on a descriptive adjective.

Communication Scenario

You have just arrived for the first day of an intensive Arabic program at a language institute in Amman. Students from many countries are gathering in a sunny courtyard before the first class begins. Your instructor has asked everyone to greet and introduce themselves to at least three new people — in Arabic — before the session starts.

  1. Think about the last time you introduced yourself to a stranger. Was the greeting formal or casual? What did you say first?
  2. You may already recognize the word أَهْلًا, a common Arabic greeting meaning roughly "welcome" or "hello." By the end of this chapter, you will know several ways to greet someone in Arabic, and you will know how to write your own name in Arabic script.

الكِتَابَة — Writing System

Arabic Script

Arabic is written from right to left in a connected, cursive script. Most letters change shape depending on their position in a word — isolated, initial (at the start of a word), medial (in the middle), or final (at the end) — and depending on whether they connect to the letter that follows. This chapter introduces six letters that you will learn to write productively, plus a small number of additional letters you will recognize inside whole formulaic words.

Script Note — Reading and writing direction

Arabic words are read and written from right to left. Never reverse Arabic letters manually to "fix" their order; the correct order is right to left, and font rendering handles the joining automatically.

Script Note — Dots make the difference

ب (bāʾ), ت (tāʾ), and ث (thāʾ) share the same base shape. The only difference among them is the number and position of dots: ب has one dot below, ت has two dots above, and ث has three dots above. Dots are not decoration — they are essential to letter identity. A missing or misplaced dot changes the letter entirely.

Arabic short vowels are written as small marks above or below a letter rather than as separate letters. This chapter introduces the three short vowels.

Pronunciation

Short and long vowels

Arabic vowel length is contrastive — meaning a short vowel and its long counterpart are different sounds that can change a word's meaning entirely, not just a matter of emphasis. Compare:

  • بَ — short /a/, as in the first syllable of "ah-bout"held briefly
  • بَا — long /aː/, roughly like the "a" in "father" but held noticeably longerheld about twice as long as the short vowel

The long vowels you can already recognize in this chapter's letters are written using ا (ā) and ي (ī) combined with the matching short vowel mark on the preceding consonant.

Pronunciation Note — A sound at the start of أَهْلًا

The word أَهْلًا begins with a brief catch in the throat, similar to the sound between the two syllables of the English interjection "uh-oh." This sound is a true Arabic consonant. For now, simply pronounce أَهْلًا as a whole, formulaic greeting; the spelling rules that govern this sound are introduced beginning in Chapter 4.

The letter ث

The sound represented by ث (thāʾ) does not exist in English as a separate consonant phoneme in most dialects, but it is close to the "th" sound in English think (voiceless). Keep the tongue lightly between the teeth. English speakers usually find this sound approachable from the start, since a close equivalent exists in English.

Pronunciation Note — Statement vs. question intonation

One simple way to ask a yes-or-no question in spoken MSA is to use rising intonation at the end of a statement, similar to English. Notice the contrast in this chapter's question مَا اسْمُكَ؟ — the voice rises slightly at the end, signaled in writing by the Arabic question mark ؟, which is the mirror image of the English question mark, consistent with right-to-left writing.

تَدْرِيب — Practice: With a partner, or using a recording, practice the contrast between short and long vowels by repeating بَ / بَا and بِ / بِي several times each, exaggerating the length difference until it feels natural.

Grammar Instruction

1.1 اسْمِي... — Saying your name formulaic

Purpose: introduce yourself using a complete, ready-made formula.

Treat اسْمِي... as a complete formula meaning "My name is...":

  • اسْمِي سَارَة.ismī Sāra. — My name is Sara.
  • اسْمِي يُوسُف.ismī Yūsuf. — My name is Yusuf.
Grammar Note — No present-tense "to be"

Notice that اسْمِي سَارَة contains no word corresponding to English "is." Arabic present-tense sentences of identity, classification, and description normally do not use an overt present copula — the sentence simply places two elements side by side. This is called a nominal sentence. اسْمِي سَارَة already means a complete sentence, "My name is Sara," with no extra word needed.

1.2 مَا اسْمُكَ؟ / مَا اسْمُكِ؟ — Asking someone's name formulaic

Purpose: ask another person's name, choosing the correct fixed form based on whether you are addressing a man or a woman.

Learn each question as a complete, separate formula:

  • مَا اسْمُكَ؟mā ismuka? — What is your name? (to a man)
  • مَا اسْمُكِ؟mā ismuki? — What is your name? (to a woman)

Treat the small difference between these two questions as a fixed feature of the formula for now. You are not expected to build new sentences using this pattern yet; the underlying grammar is introduced gradually beginning in Chapter 4.

1.3 نَوْع الاسْم — A first look at noun gender introduced

Purpose: recognize that every Arabic noun has a grammatical gender, and notice the most common written signal of feminine gender.

Every Arabic noun is either masculine or feminine. There is no neutral "it" category for nouns the way some other grammatical systems have. Many feminine nouns and adjectives end in a special letter called tāʾ marbūṭa ة, usually pronounced as a short /a/ in pause:

  • أُسْتَاذustādh — instructor (masculine)
  • أُسْتَاذَةustādha — instructor (feminine)

This is your first encounter with gender as a system, not a complete account of it. Number, plural formation, and the full range of feminine markers are introduced beginning in Chapter 4.

1.4 صِفَة المُجَامَلَة — A descriptive adjective and gender agreement introduced

Purpose: build a complete nominal sentence using a name and a simple descriptive word, with the adjective correctly agreeing in gender.

In Arabic, a descriptive adjective must match the gender of the noun it describes. Compare a sentence describing a man's class and one describing a woman's class, using the adjective جَدِيد / جَدِيدَة (new):

  • الصَّفُّ جَدِيدٌ.aṣ-ṣaffu jadīdun. — The class (masc.) is new.
  • الجَامِعَةُ جَدِيدَةٌ.al-jāmiʿatu jadīdatun. — The university (fem.) is new.

Both sentences are nominal sentences — there is no separate word for "is." The adjective simply takes the feminine ending ة when it describes a feminine noun, and stays without it when describing a masculine noun.

Language Focus

Gender touches the whole sentence. English marks gender mainly on a small set of pronouns (he, she, it). Arabic marks gender on nouns and on the adjectives that describe them, and — as you continue through this volume — on pronouns, demonstratives, and verbs as well. Learning to notice the tāʾ marbūṭa ending now will make every later stage of the course easier.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Greeting as a social ritual

Across many Arabic-speaking communities, greeting someone warmly before making a request or starting a transaction is expected, much as it is in many other cultures. Beginning an interaction abruptly without any greeting can come across as cold. A simple أَهْلًا or السَّلَامُ عَلَيْكُم before a request costs nothing and sets a respectful tone for the interaction.

This expectation is widely shared, though norms always vary by individual, setting, region, and relationship. As a learner, making a habit of greeting first will serve you well throughout the Arabic-speaking world.

Culture in Context — A linguistically and religiously diverse world

Arabic is spoken across more than twenty countries spanning North Africa, the Nile Valley, the Levant, Iraq, the Arabian Peninsula, and beyond, and it is used by communities of many religious backgrounds — Muslim, Christian, Jewish, and others — as well as by people of no religious affiliation. Learning Arabic is not learning about one country, one religion, or one culture — it is gaining access to a wide and varied linguistic world.

Regional Variation — Greetings beyond MSA

This course teaches Modern Standard Arabic (MSA), the shared formal and cross-regional standard used in education, media, and writing throughout the Arabic-speaking world. In everyday spoken interaction, however, people generally use a regional spoken variety rather than MSA — for example, greetings may sound noticeably different in Cairo, Beirut, Casablanca, or Riyadh. أَهْلًا وَسَهْلًا and السَّلَامُ عَلَيْكُم are both widely understood and used across regional varieties as well as in MSA, which is part of why they make an excellent starting point. As the course progresses, regional forms will be introduced and clearly labeled for recognition; the productive standard taught throughout remains MSA.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Writing letters left to right

Error: Writing or typing Arabic letters in left-to-right order, producing a reversed or scrambled word.

Correction: Arabic is written and read from right to left. Begin each word at the rightmost letter. If you are typing, use an Arabic keyboard layout or input method rather than typing letters in English order and hoping the software corrects it — it will not.

Common Mistake — Confusing بـ، تـ، and ثـ

Error: Omitting dots, or placing the wrong number of dots, when writing ب, ت, or ث.

Correction: These three letters share the same base shape and are distinguished only by their dots: one dot below for ب, two dots above for ت, three dots above for ث. Check every letter for the correct number and position of dots — this is not optional.

Common Mistake — Mixing up مَا اسْمُكَ؟ and مَا اسْمُكِ؟

Error: Asking مَا اسْمُكِ؟ to a man, or مَا اسْمُكَ؟ to a woman.

Correction: Match the question to the listener: مَا اسْمُكَ؟ for a man, مَا اسْمُكِ؟ for a woman. Listening carefully for this kind of final-vowel difference will become an essential habit throughout the course.

Common Mistake — Inserting an English-style "is"

Error: Trying to add a separate Arabic word for "is" into اسْمِي سَارَة, modeled on the English sentence "My name is Sara."

Correction: Arabic present-tense identity sentences normally do not use an overt word for "is." اسْمِي سَارَة already means a complete sentence on its own.

Common Mistake — Forgetting adjective agreement

Error: Using جَدِيد with a feminine noun, as in *الجَامِعَةُ جَدِيد.

Correction: The adjective must agree in gender with the noun it describes: الجَامِعَةُ جَدِيدَةٌ (feminine noun, feminine adjective). Watch for the tāʾ marbūṭa ending on both the noun and the adjective.

Common Mistake — Treating السَّلَامُ عَلَيْكُم as religious-only

Error: Assuming that السَّلَامُ عَلَيْكُم can only be used by or with Muslims, or avoiding it out of uncertainty.

Correction: While the expression has roots in Islamic tradition, it functions as a widely used everyday greeting across many Arabic-speaking communities, used and understood by people of various religious backgrounds. أَهْلًا وَسَهْلًا remains a useful, religiously neutral alternative whenever you prefer it.

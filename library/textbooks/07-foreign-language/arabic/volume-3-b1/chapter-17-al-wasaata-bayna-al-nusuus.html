الوَسَاطَة بَيْنَ النُّصُوصChapter 17: Mediation Across Texts

Summary, paraphrase, and translation-adjacent explanation · Speak and Write Arabic: Independent Communication and Advanced Expression

Chapter Overview

Chapter 17 takes the careful, register-controlled voice you built in Chapter 16 — hedged generalization, named communities, and a clear shift between description and reflection — and applies it to a new task: mediating information from one text or source into a new, shorter, differently addressed text. This chapter introduces no new verb tense, case system, or clause type beyond what is already active in this volume. Its two closely related new developments are (1) explicit strategies for summarizing and paraphrasing a source text without copying its wording, and (2) an introductory, bounded use of academic and source-aware register, including phrases for attributing an idea to a source rather than presenting it as the mediator's own claim. You will read short source texts, listen to short spoken accounts, and practice restating their content for a different audience in your own words, always marking clearly what came from the source and what is your own explanation.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Summarize the main idea and key details of a short written or spoken source text in your own words.
  2. Paraphrase a sentence or short passage without copying its wording, while preserving its meaning.
  3. Use source-attribution phrases such as "according to the text" or "the writer explains" to distinguish a source's claim from your own explanation.
  4. Mediate a source text for a new audience by adjusting length, vocabulary, and register while preserving accuracy.
  5. Distinguish a faithful summary from a distorted or overinterpreted one.
  6. Recycle hedged generalization, named communities, and register shift from Chapter 16 inside a mediation task.
  7. Produce a short written and spoken mediation of a source text for a specified audience.

Opening Communication Scenario

The same cultural exchange program from Chapter 16 has received a short article, in Arabic, from a partner organization describing a community initiative. The program coordinators have asked participants to prepare two things from the article: a short written summary for the program newsletter, and a short spoken explanation for new arrivals who have not read the article themselves. Both versions must accurately represent what the article says, without adding claims the article does not make.

  1. You will review vocabulary and expressions for summarizing, paraphrasing, and attributing information to a source.
  2. You will read a short source article and listen to a short spoken account, and identify their main ideas, key details, and any hedged claims.
  3. You will mediate the source material into a shorter written summary and a spoken explanation for a different audience, marking clearly what comes from the source.

النُّطْق — Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus: Marking Attribution in Speech

Pronunciation Note — Signaling attribution with pause and stress

When mediating a source aloud, speakers typically place a brief pause after an attribution phrase such as بِحَسَب النَّصّ or تَقُول الكَاتِبَة إِنَّ, and give slightly stronger phrase stress to the attribution itself. This small pause tells the listener that the words following it belong to the source, not to the speaker. When the speaker returns to their own comment, as in أَمَّا أَنَا فَـ, the same falling, personal intonation contour from Chapter 16 reappears. This chapter recycles the pause-form, phrase-grouping, and register-intonation targets already active in this unit; no new sound or symbol is introduced here.

التَّرْكِيب — Grammar and Sentence Patterns

Grammar and Sentence Patterns: Attribution and Paraphrase

This chapter introduces two closely related developments: content-clause attribution structures for reporting what a source says, and paraphrase strategies that restate a source's meaning in different wording. Both build directly on the content-clause and reported-information structures already active in this volume; neither requires a new verb form, case rule, or clause type.

Pattern 1 — Attributing a claim to a source
بِحَسَب النَّصّ / تَقُول الكَاتِبَة إِنَّ ... ، يُوَضِّح الكَاتِب أَنَّ ...
A content clause introduced by إِنَّ or أَنَّ follows the attribution phrase, so the listener or reader understands the following information as the source's claim, not the mediator's own statement.
بِحَسَب النَّصّ، تُوَضِّح الكَاتِبَة أَنَّ سُكَّان الحَيّ نَظَّمُوا حَدِيقَة مُشْتَرَكَة لِلْجِيرَان مُنْذُ ثَلَاث سَنَوَات.
According to the text, the writer explains that the neighborhood's residents organized a shared garden for neighbors three years ago.
Pattern 2 — Paraphrasing without copying wording
بِكَلِمَاتِي الخَاصَّة، ... (وَهُوَ مَا يَعْنِي أَنَّ ...) دُون أَن أُغَيِّر المَعْنَى
A responsible paraphrase restates the source's idea using different vocabulary and sentence structure while preserving the original meaning, and may add a brief gloss clarifying the restated meaning.
بِكَلِمَاتِي الخَاصَّة، جَمَاعَة مِن الجِيرَان قَرَّرُوا مُنْذُ مُدَّة أَن يَعْمَلُوا مَعًا عَلَى أَرْض فَارِغَة لِيَجْعَلُوهَا مَكَانًا أَخْضَر لِلْجَمِيع.
In my own words, a group of neighbors decided some time ago to work together on an empty plot of land to make it a green space for everyone.
Pattern 3 — Separating source claim from mediator's comment
هَذَا مَا يَقُولُه النَّصّ، أَمَّا أَنَا فَـ ...
The connector أَمَّا ... فَـ, already active from Chapter 16, again marks a clear pivot — this time from a faithful restatement of the source into the mediator's own brief observation, so the two are never confused.
هَذَا مَا يَقُولُه النَّصّ، أَمَّا أَنَا فَأَجِد الفِكْرَة مُفِيدَة لِأَحْيَاء أُخْرَى أَيْضًا.
This is what the text says; as for me, I find the idea useful for other neighborhoods too.

Recycled structures used in this chapter: content clauses and reported information (Chapter 4), hedged generalization and register shift (Chapter 16), and comparison or data commentary (Chapters 12–13) may all be recycled inside a mediation task; none is retaught here.

Writing Workshop — A Mediated Summary for a New Audience

In this workshop, you will plan, draft, and revise a short written summary of a source text, mediating it for a specified new audience, using attribution, faithful paraphrase, and a clearly marked personal comment.

  1. Step 1 — Choose your source and audience. Select a short article, passage, or spoken account you have read or heard, and identify the specific audience you are mediating it for.
  2. Step 2 — Identify the main idea and two or three key details. Note them briefly in your own words before drafting full sentences.
  3. Step 3 — Open with attribution. Use بِحَسَب النَّصّ or تَقُول الكَاتِبَة إِنَّ to introduce the source's main idea.
  4. Step 4 — Paraphrase the key details. Restate them in your own words and sentence structure, without copying the source's wording, and without changing its meaning.
  5. Step 5 — Adjust for your audience. Add or remove detail, and adjust vocabulary and register, so the summary suits the audience you named in Step 1.
  6. Step 6 — Mark your own comment separately. If you add a brief personal reaction, mark it clearly with هَذَا مَا يَقُولُه النَّصّ، أَمَّا أَنَا فَـ or an equivalent phrase.
  7. Review your draft against the checklist below.

قَائِمَة المُرَاجَعَة — Editing checklist:

  • I clearly attributed the source's main idea using an attribution phrase.
  • I paraphrased the key details in my own words without copying the source's wording.
  • I did not add any claim that the source does not actually make.
  • I adjusted the length, vocabulary, and register for the specific audience I named.
  • If I included a personal comment, I marked it clearly as separate from the source's claim.
  • I did not introduce any system beyond what this chapter and prior chapters have authorized.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Community initiatives across Arabic-speaking regions

Neighborhood and community initiatives of the kind described in this chapter's readings appear across many Arabic-speaking cities, towns, and diaspora communities, organized by residents, local associations, municipalities, or religious and civic organizations. As with the cultural commentary in Chapter 16, a responsible mediator names the specific source, place, or organization behind an initiative rather than presenting one example as typical of an entire country or region.

Regional Variation — Source style across media and organizations

News articles, organizational reports, and personal accounts often differ in how directly they state claims and how much hedging or attribution they use. A careful mediator preserves the source's own level of certainty rather than making a hedged claim sound more absolute, or an absolute claim sound more tentative, than the source intended.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Copying the source's wording instead of paraphrasing

Error: repeating the source's sentences with only minor word changes, such as replacing one or two words in an otherwise identical sentence.

Correction: restate the idea with different vocabulary and sentence structure, as in بِكَلِمَاتِي الخَاصَّة، ..., while preserving the original meaning.

Common Mistake — Adding claims the source does not make

Error: stating a stronger, weaker, or different claim than the source actually makes, such as turning "some families" into "all families."

Correction: check each restated claim against the source and preserve its exact scope and certainty, hedging only when the source itself hedges.

Common Mistake — Blending the source's claim with the mediator's opinion

Error: presenting a personal reaction as though it were part of the source's content, leaving the listener unsure what the source actually said.

Correction: mark the shift explicitly with هَذَا مَا يَقُولُه النَّصّ، أَمَّا أَنَا فَـ before adding a personal comment.

Reading Practice — Comprehension

  1. The main idea is that neighbors turned an empty plot of land into a shared garden; the writer attributes it with تُوَضِّح الكَاتِبَة فِي هَذَا المَقَال أَنَّ.
  2. The project began with seven volunteers and grew to more than thirty families; the writer also notes that organizing work schedules and dividing tasks was not easy at first.
  3. According to the article, the most important outcome was not the green space itself, but the opportunity it gave neighbors to get to know each other and cooperate.
  4. Model paraphrase: بِكَلِمَاتِي الخَاصَّة، أَهَمّ مَا قَدَّمَتْه الحَدِيقَة لِلْجِيرَان لَيْس المَكَان الأَخْضَر وَحْده، بَل فُرْصَة التَّعَارُف وَالعَمَل مَعًا.

Listening Practice

  • The main idea is that the shared-garden project began when neighbors noticed an empty plot of land, attributed with تَقُول مُنَظِّمَة المَشْرُوع إِنَّ.
  • Seven neighbors volunteered at first, and others joined gradually; the garden became a place where neighbors of different ages and backgrounds meet.
  • The account shifts register with أَمَّا بِالنِّسْبَة لِلمُنَظِّمَة، فَفِي تَجْرِبَتِهَا الشَّخْصِيَّة.
  • According to the organizer, the most important result was not the greenery itself, but neighbors getting to know each other.

Chapter Review — Model responses

Section A.1: Attribution: تُوَضِّح الكَاتِبَة فِي هَذَا المَقَال أَنَّ. Key detail: the sentence about seven volunteers growing to more than thirty families. Closing claim: the final sentence about the opportunity for neighbors to get to know each other and cooperate.

Section A.2: لَيْلَى attributes the claim with بِحَسَب النَّصّ، تُوَضِّح الكَاتِبَة أَنَّ, then shifts to her own comment with هَذَا مَا يَقُولُه النَّصّ، أَمَّا أَنَا فَأَجِد الفِكْرَة مُلْهِمَة.

Section B: Open-ended self-assessment; any honest, specific response identifying one challenge and a concrete plan to address it is acceptable.

الأَسْبَاب وَالنَّتَائِجCauses and Results

Cause, result, purpose, subjunctive expansion, and explanation · Speak and Write Arabic: Independent Communication and Advanced Expression

Chapter Overview

In Chapter 2 you learned to sequence a personal narrative — background, turning point, and result — using time clauses. This chapter shifts the analytical lens from when things happened to why they happened and what they led to. You will expand your subjunctive control beyond أَنْ to a small family of purpose particles, add a set of result connectors that let you state a consequence explicitly, and learn the negative-command and negated-past patterns that depend on the jussive mood. By the end of the chapter you will be able to explain a decision or an event by naming its cause, tracing its result, and stating its purpose — three related but distinct kinds of explanation that every organized B2 account depends on.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Distinguish a cause connector from a result connector and choose the correct one for a given explanation.
  2. Use لِـ، كَي، وَلِكَي، and حَتَّى with a following subjunctive verb to express purpose.
  3. Form and use negative commands with لَا + the jussive.
  4. Use لَمْ + the jussive to negate a completed past event, recognizing it as an alternative to negated perfect forms.
  5. Use بِسَبَب، بِسَبَبِ أَنَّ، لِذَلِك، نَتِيجَةً لِـ، and مِمَّا to link a cause to its result across or within a sentence.
  6. Read a short explanatory passage and identify its stated cause, its stated result, and any purpose clause.
  7. Write a short explanation of a decision or event that states a cause, a result, and a purpose in connected prose.
  8. Explain a cause-and-result relationship aloud, marking the shift from cause to result clearly for a listener.

Opening Communication Scenario

A friend asks you, "Why did you really decide to do that?" or a colleague asks, "What was the reason behind that policy?" Simply naming an event, as you practiced in Chapter 2, is not enough here — the listener wants the logic that connects a cause to what followed. Good explanation requires more than وَ ("and"); it requires precise connectors that say, unambiguously, "this is why," "this is what resulted," and "this is what it was for."

  1. You will compare cause connectors with result connectors in a sample explanation.
  2. You will practice purpose clauses that state the goal behind an action.
  3. You will practice negative commands and a negated-past pattern that uses the jussive.
  4. You will explain, aloud and in writing, the cause, result, and purpose behind a real or invented decision.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Marking the cause-to-result shift vocally

When an explanation moves from cause to result, a brief pause before لِذَلِك or بِالتَّالِي helps the listener register the logical shift, much as the pause before فَجْأَة marked the turning point in Chapter 2. Practice: كَانَ الطَّقْس سَيِّئًا جِدًّا، / وَلِذَلِك، / أَجَّلْنَا الرِّحْلَة. Continue applying the phrase-grouping and pause habits from earlier chapters across these longer explanatory sentences.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter expands your subjunctive control from أَنْ alone to a small family of purpose particles, and it introduces the jussive mood through two everyday patterns: the negative command and the negated-past alternative with لَمْ. It also builds a set of connectors that link a stated cause to its stated result.

Focus — Cause versus result

A cause explains why something happened; a result states what happened because of it. لِأَنَّ, بِسَبَب, and بِسَبَبِ أَنَّ introduce a cause. لِذَلِك, نَتِيجَةً لِـ, بِالتَّالِي, and مِمَّا introduce or mark a result. Confusing the two directions is a common error even at this level, since English "because" and "so" can feel interchangeable in casual speech but are not interchangeable in organized Arabic explanation.

  • أَجَّلْنَا الرِّحْلَة بِسَبَبِ الطَّقْسِ السَّيِّئ.We postponed the trip because of the bad weather.بِسَبَب + noun phrase — cause
  • كَانَ الطَّقْس سَيِّئًا جِدًّا، وَلِذَلِك أَجَّلْنَا الرِّحْلَة.The weather was very bad, so we postponed the trip.لِذَلِك — result
  • اِرْتَفَعَت الأَسْعَار، مِمَّا جَعَل كَثِيرًا مِنَ النَّاسِ يُقَلِّلُونَ اسْتِهْلَاكَهُم.Prices rose, which made many people reduce their consumption.مِمَّا — result clause referring back to the whole prior clause
Purpose clauses with the subjunctive
لِـ / كَي / لِكَي + subjunctive verb  |  حَتَّى + subjunctive verb (purpose reading)

لِـ is the most common and most compact purpose particle, prefixed directly to the verb. كَي and لِكَي are near-synonyms of لِـ and are common in both writing and formal speech. حَتَّى can also mark purpose ("so that") when followed by a subjunctive verb, distinct from its use as "until" or "even." All four particles govern the same subjunctive endings you learned with أَنْ in Volume II and Chapter 1.

دَرَسَت المَوْضُوع بِعِنَايَة لِكَي تَتَجَنَّب أَيَّ خَطَأ.
She studied the subject carefully in order to avoid any mistake.
The jussive: negative commands and لَمْ
لَا + jussive verb → negative command  |  لَمْ + jussive verb → negated past event

The jussive shares most of its shape with the subjunctive but drops the final vowel or ن of the subjunctive's dual/plural/feminine-plural endings. Two everyday jussive patterns: لَا + jussive forms a negative command ("don't do X"), and لَمْ + jussive negates a completed past event, functioning as an alternative to مَا + perfect that is more common in formal MSA.

لَا تُؤَجِّل القَرَار بِلَا سَبَب وَاضِح. — لَمْ يُؤَثِّر التَّغْيِير عَلَى النَّتِيجَة النِّهَائِيَّة.
Don't postpone the decision without a clear reason. — The change did not affect the final result.

Case endings remain receptive at this stage: you will notice the subjunctive/jussive vowel or ن-drop pattern in reading and modeled speech, but full spontaneous case-ending production of nouns is not yet required. Passive constructions and full conditional sentences are introduced in later chapters of this unit and are not targeted here.

Model Dialogue

Context: two colleagues discuss why a project was delayed and what should happen next.

سَامِر: لِمَاذَا تَأَخَّر المَشْرُوع هَذَا الشَّهْر؟Why was the project delayed this month?
لَيْلَى: تَأَخَّر بِسَبَبِ عَدَم تَوَفُّر بَعْض البَيَانَات فِي الوَقْت المُنَاسِب. وَبِسَبَبِ أَنَّ الفَرِيق كَانَ صَغِيرًا، لَمْ نَسْتَطِع إِنْهَاء كُلّ شَيْء بِالسُّرْعَة المَطْلُوبَة.It was delayed because some data wasn't available at the right time. And because the team was small, we weren't able to finish everything at the required speed.
سَامِر: وَمَا النَّتِيجَة الآن؟And what's the result now?
لَيْلَى: نَتِيجَةً لِذَلِكَ، سَيَتَأَخَّر التَّسْلِيم أُسْبُوعًا كَامِلًا. وَبِالتَّالِي، يَجِب أَن نُخْبِر العَمِيل اليَوْم.As a result of that, delivery will be delayed a full week. Consequently, we have to tell the client today.
سَامِر: حَسَنًا. لَا تُؤَجِّلِي الاِتِّصَال بِهِ. أَرْسِلِي البَرِيد الآن لِكَي يَعْرِف الوَضْع قَبْل نِهَايَة اليَوْم.Okay. Don't delay calling him. Send the email now so that he knows the situation before the end of the day.
لَيْلَى: سَأَفْعَل ذَلِكَ حَالًا حَتَّى لَا يَتَفَاجَأ العَمِيل لَاحِقًا. وَبِالإِضَافَة إِلَى ذَلِكَ، سَأَقْتَرِح خُطَّة لِتَجَنُّب هَذَا العَامِل فِي المُسْتَقْبَل.I'll do that right away so that the client isn't surprised later. In addition, I'll propose a plan to avoid this factor in the future.

Guided Practice

  1. Write one sentence stating a cause using بِسَبَب or لِأَنَّ.
  2. Write one sentence stating the result of that cause using لِذَلِك or نَتِيجَةً لِـ.
  3. Combine both ideas into a single sentence using مِمَّا.
  4. Write one sentence with a purpose clause using لِـ، كَي، or حَتَّى.
  5. Write one negative command using لَا + jussive, and one negated past event using لَمْ + jussive.

Writing Workshop

In this workshop, you will write a short, connected explanation of a real or invented decision or event, stating its cause, its result, and its purpose.

  1. Step 1 — Choose a decision or event. Pick something real or invented that has a clear cause and a clear result.
  2. Step 2 — State the cause. Use بِسَبَب or لِأَنَّ to explain why it happened.
  3. Step 3 — Add a second contributing cause. Use بِسَبَبِ أَنَّ to add a second reason.
  4. Step 4 — State the result. Use لِذَلِك، نَتِيجَةً لِـ، or مِمَّا to state what followed.
  5. Step 5 — State a purpose. Use لِـ، كَي، لِكَي، or حَتَّى with a subjunctive verb to explain the goal behind an action taken.
  6. Step 6 — Add one instruction or warning. Use لَا + jussive for a negative command relevant to the situation.
  7. Review your draft against the editing checklist below.

قَائِمَة المُرَاجَعَة — Editing checklist:

  • My explanation clearly separates the stated cause from the stated result.
  • I used at least one cause connector and one result connector from this chapter.
  • I used at least one purpose clause with a correctly formed subjunctive verb.
  • I used at least one negative command or one لَمْ + jussive construction correctly.
  • My explanation includes at least one connector recycled from Chapters 1 or 2.
  • My vocabulary, gender agreement, and plural forms are checked for accuracy.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Giving reasons in professional and community settings

In many Arabic-speaking professional and community settings, explaining the reasoning behind a decision — rather than simply announcing it — is often valued as a sign of respect and transparency, though how much explanation is expected varies by relationship, workplace culture, and setting. The cause-and-result language practiced in this chapter supports that kind of explanation without representing a single fixed convention for every context or community.

Regional Variation — Everyday cause-and-result expressions

In everyday spoken narration, which may shift toward regional spoken Arabic, some speakers use shorter or different everyday equivalents for "because" and "so." The MSA connectors introduced in this chapter remain the productive target for this course and are also widely used and understood in formal spoken MSA, written correspondence, and media across the Arabic-speaking world.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Confusing cause connectors with result connectors

Error: تَأَخَّر المَشْرُوع، لِذَلِكَ لَمْ تَتَوَفَّر البَيَانَات using لِذَلِك as if it introduced a cause rather than a result.

Correction: reverse the logic — the missing data is the cause, and the delay is the result: لَمْ تَتَوَفَّر البَيَانَات، وَلِذَلِكَ تَأَخَّر المَشْرُوع. Check that لِذَلِك، نَتِيجَةً لِـ، and بِالتَّالِي always follow the cause, never precede it.

Common Mistake — Indicative after a purpose particle

Error: أَرْسَلَت البَرِيد لِكَي يَعْرِفُ الوَضْع with an indicative-form verb after لِكَي.

Correction: لِـ، كَي، لِكَي، and purpose-reading حَتَّى all require the subjunctive: أَرْسَلَت البَرِيد لِكَي يَعْرِفَ الوَضْع. Review the subjunctive endings from Volume II and Chapter 1 when this pattern feels uncertain.

Common Mistake — Using لَا with the perfect instead of the jussive

Error: لَا أَجَّلْتَ القَرَار attempting a negative command with a perfect-tense verb.

Correction: negative commands require لَا + the jussive form of the imperfect: لَا تُؤَجِّل القَرَار. Similarly, لَمْ requires the jussive, not the perfect or plain imperfect: لَمْ يُؤَثِّر التَّغْيِير, not لَمْ أَثَّرَ.

Chapter Summary

This chapter expanded your subjunctive control to لِـ، كَي، لِكَي، and purpose-reading حَتَّى, and it introduced the jussive mood through negative commands with لَا and the negated-past pattern with لَمْ. You also learned a set of cause connectors (بِسَبَب، بِسَبَبِ أَنَّ) and result connectors (لِذَلِك، نَتِيجَةً لِـ، مِمَّا، بِالتَّالِي) that let you explain, precisely, why something happened and what it led to. You practiced explaining a decision or event aloud and in writing, continuing to apply the narrative and discourse habits from Chapters 1 and 2. This explanatory foundation prepares you for Chapter 4, which moves from explaining events into summarizing and paraphrasing what others have said about them.

Listening Practice

  • The company considered changing its hours because of many employee complaints about morning congestion.
  • The passage adds that because many employees live far from the company, the commute sometimes took over an hour.
  • The result of the flexible schedule was that it clearly eased the pressure on employees.
  • The passage said employees should not change their schedules without notifying their supervisor; the verb تُغَيِّرُوا is in the jussive after لَا.

Reading Practice — Comprehension

  1. Residents decided to close the main street to cars on weekends because of a number of accidents over the past two months.
  2. The passage adds that because many children play in that street, residents felt real concern.
  3. The result was that the municipality agreed, which made the neighborhood safer.
  4. لَا تَسْتَهِينُوا بِسَلَامَة أَطْفَالِكُم أَبَدًا tells people not to take their children's safety lightly.

Chapter Review — Model responses

Section A.1: بِسَبَبِ عَدَم تَوَفُّر بَعْض البَيَانَات (cause) and نَتِيجَةً لِذَلِكَ، سَيَتَأَخَّر التَّسْلِيم (result); other correct examples from the dialogue are acceptable.

Section A.2: لَا + jussive forms a negative command directed at someone in the present moment, while لَمْ + jussive negates a specific completed past event; any accurate example pair is acceptable.

Section B: Open-ended self-assessment; any honest, specific response that names a concrete decision or preference is acceptable.

Other well-formed, accurate answers combining the required systems are equally acceptable throughout this section.

Looking Ahead

مَا الَّذِي تَسْتَطِيعُ فِعْلَهُ الآن — What you can do now

You can now explain a decision or event by stating its cause, its result, and its purpose, using a precise set of connectors and an expanded subjunctive and jussive system. These habits will support every later chapter of Unit I, where you will move from explaining events into summarizing and paraphrasing information from other sources.

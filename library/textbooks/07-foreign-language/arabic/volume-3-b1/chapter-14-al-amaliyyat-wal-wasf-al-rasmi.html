العَمَلِيَّات وَالوَصْف الرَّسْمِيّChapter 14: Processes and Formal Description

Passive production, process sequence, and participles · Speak and Write Arabic: Independent Communication and Advanced Expression

Chapter Overview

In Chapter 13, you learned to state a percentage, describe a rate rising or falling, cite a source, and interpret a set of figures. This chapter turns from evidence about outcomes to the description of procedures themselves. You already recognized the passive voice receptively from Chapter 4 onward and saw it recur in media and institutional contexts in Chapters 6 through 9; this chapter moves the passive into fuller productive use so you can describe a formal procedure — an application, a request, a registration — without naming who carries out each step. You will also learn to sequence a process with ordering connectors and to use active and passive participles as descriptive labels for a step's current status. No new clause type is introduced: the content clauses and source-attribution patterns you have used since Chapter 4 continue to operate here, now describing procedures rather than data or opinions. By the end of the chapter, you will be able to read a short passage describing an official procedure, discuss the steps of a process with a partner, and write a short paragraph describing a procedure formally and impersonally.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Form and use the internal passive of Form I sound verbs, such as يُقَدَّم and يُرَاجَع, to describe a step in a process without naming the agent.
  2. Form and use the passive of derived verbs, such as Form II يُنَفَّذ, in formal process description.
  3. Sequence the steps of a procedure using أَوَّلًا, ثُمَّ, بَعْد ذَلِك, and أَخِيرًا.
  4. Use active and passive participles such as مُقَدَّم، مُعْتَمَد، and مَرْفُوض to describe the current status of a step or request.
  5. Distinguish when to choose the passive rather than the active voice in formal, institutional description.
  6. Read a short passage describing an official procedure and identify its steps, their sequence, and the status of each stage.
  7. Write a short, organized paragraph that describes a formal procedure impersonally, step by step, from start to finish.

Opening Communication Scenario

A new international student at your university has asked you to explain how the housing-application procedure works, since the university website describes it only in very formal administrative language. You need to walk her through each step — from submitting the request to receiving a final decision — without focusing on which specific staff member handles each stage, since that information is not published and is not the point. Your explanation should describe what is done at each step, in what order, and what the current status of a request can be.

  1. You will learn how to describe a procedure using the passive voice instead of naming an unspecified agent.
  2. You will practice ordering the steps of a process and describing the status of a request.
  3. You will read a short passage describing an official procedure, discuss the steps with a partner, and write your own process-description paragraph.

التَّرْكِيب — Grammar and Sentence Patterns

Grammar and Sentence Patterns: Passive Production, Process Sequence, Participles

You have recognized the passive voice since Chapter 4 and seen it used in media and institutional texts through Chapter 9. This chapter moves the passive into independent production for one specific purpose: describing a formal procedure step by step, without naming who performs each action. No new clause type is introduced; the same simple declarative sentences you already produce now take a passive verb as their core.

Building Block 1 — The internal passive of Form I verbs
يُفْعَل ← فُعِل (present and past internal passive of a sound Form I verb)
The internal passive changes the vowel pattern of the verb rather than adding a separate word. The agent is not named; if it must be mentioned, this is done with a following phrase, but ordinary process description omits it entirely.
يُقَدَّم الطَّلَب أَوَّلًا إِلَى مَكْتَب شُؤُون الطُّلَّاب.
The request is first submitted to the student affairs office.
Building Block 2 — Passive of derived verbs (Form II and beyond)
يُفَعَّل / يُفَاعَل / يُعْتَمَد (passive of Form II, III, and VIII verbs)
Derived forms have their own passive patterns, but the function is the same: the action is described from the point of view of what happens to the request, not who acts on it.
ثُمَّ يُرَاجَع الطَّلَب مِن قِبَل اللَّجْنَة، وَيُعْتَمَد إِذَا اِسْتَوْفَى الشُّرُوط.
The request is then reviewed by the committee, and it is approved if it meets the conditions.
Building Block 3 — Sequencing the steps
أَوَّلًا، ... ثُمَّ، ... بَعْد ذَلِك، ... وَأَخِيرًا، ...
These four connectors recycle earlier discourse-cohesion work and give a process description its clear order. Each connector normally opens a new sentence built around a passive verb.
أَوَّلًا، يُقَدَّم الطَّلَب. ثُمَّ يُرَاجَع مِن قِبَل المَكْتَب. بَعْد ذَلِك، يُعْتَمَد أَو يُرْفَض. وَأَخِيرًا، يُبَلَّغ الطَّالِب بِالنَّتِيجَة.
First, the request is submitted. Then it is reviewed by the office. After that, it is approved or rejected. Finally, the student is informed of the result.
Building Block 4 — Participles as status descriptions
الطَّلَب + مُعْتَمَد / مَرْفُوض / مُكْتَمَل (participle used as a predicate adjective)
A passive participle names a current state, not an ongoing action, so it is used after a request has already passed through a step — a useful shortcut once the process itself has been described with full passive verbs.
طَلَبِي مُعْتَمَد مُنْذُ الأُسْبُوع المَاضِي، لَكِنَّ طَلَب صَدِيقِي لَا يَزَال قَيْد المُرَاجَعَة.
My request has been approved since last week, but my friend's request is still under review.

Recycled structures used in this chapter: passive recognition and limited production (Ch. 4-9); institutional and service vocabulary (Ch. 7); source attribution and content clauses with أَنَّ (Ch. 4, 6, 11-13), now describing the outcome of a procedure rather than a figure or an opinion.

أَمْثِلَة إِضَافِيَّة — Additional examples:

  • تُنَفَّذ هَذِه الإِجْرَاءَات عَادَةً خِلَال أُسْبُوعَيْن مِن تَارِيخ التَّقْدِيم.These procedures are usually carried out within two weeks of the submission date.
  • إِذَا رُفِض الطَّلَب، يُمْكِن تَقْدِيم اِسْتِئْنَاف خِلَال عَشَرَة أَيَّام.If the request is rejected, an appeal can be submitted within ten days.

Model Dialogue

لَيْلَى, a new international student, asks كَرِيم to explain the university's housing-application procedure.

لَيْلَى
كَيْف تَسِير عَمَلِيَّة طَلَب السَّكَن الجَامِعِيّ بِالضَّبْط؟How exactly does the university housing-application process work?
كَرِيم
أَوَّلًا، يُقَدَّم الطَّلَب إِلِكْتْرُونِيًّا عَبْر مَوْقِع الجَامِعَة قَبْل نِهَايَة شَهْر يُونْيُو.First, the request is submitted electronically through the university website before the end of June.
لَيْلَى
وَبَعْد ذَلِك؟ هَل يُرَاجَع الطَّلَب فَوْرًا؟And after that? Is the request reviewed immediately?
كَرِيم
ثُمَّ يُرَاجَع الطَّلَب مِن قِبَل مَكْتَب السَّكَن خِلَال أُسْبُوعَيْن، وَيُعْتَمَد إِذَا تَوَفَّرَت الشُّرُوط.Then the request is reviewed by the housing office within two weeks, and it is approved if the conditions are met.

كَرِيم

Writing Workshop — A Process Description Paragraph

In this workshop, you will write a short, organized paragraph that describes a formal procedure step by step, using the passive voice and sequence connectors taught in this chapter.

  1. Step 1 — Name the procedure. Open with a sentence naming the procedure using عَمَلِيَّة or إِجْرَاءَات plus a construct phrase.
  2. Step 2 — Describe the first step. Use أَوَّلًا with a passive verb such as يُقَدَّم.
  3. Step 3 — Describe the middle steps. Use ثُمَّ and بَعْد ذَلِك with passive verbs such as يُرَاجَع and يُعْتَمَد.
  4. Step 4 — Describe the final step. Use أَخِيرًا to describe how the outcome is communicated.
  5. Step 5 — Add a status note. Close with one sentence using a status participle such as مُعْتَمَد or قَيْد المُرَاجَعَة to describe a request at a particular point in the process.
  6. Review your draft against the checklist below.

قَائِمَة المُرَاجَعَة — Editing checklist:

  • My paragraph names the procedure clearly in the opening sentence.
  • I used at least three passive verbs to describe steps, without naming an agent.
  • I ordered the steps using أَوَّلًا, ثُمَّ, بَعْد ذَلِك, and أَخِيرًا.
  • I used at least one status participle correctly, distinct from a full passive verb.
  • I did not introduce any system beyond what this chapter and prior chapters authorized.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Impersonal language in institutions

Official Arabic-language procedures — university regulations, government forms, employment notices — are very commonly described in the passive voice or with impersonal expressions. This is not evasive; it reflects the fact that a procedure is meant to apply consistently regardless of which specific employee handles an individual case, and formal MSA regularly favors this process-focused style in institutional writing.

Regional Variation — Naming offices and procedures

The names of the offices and forms involved in a given procedure vary by country and by institution, and some administrative terms differ between, for example, Gulf, Levantine, and North African usage. The passive structures and sequence connectors taught in this chapter remain stable across these institutional differences, even when local office names or form titles do not.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Naming an unnecessary agent

Error: keeping the active voice and inventing a vague subject, such as أَحَد المُوَظَّفِين يُقَدِّم الطَّلَب, when the point of the sentence is the step itself, not who performs it.

Correction: use the passive and drop the agent entirely, as in يُقَدَّم الطَّلَب.

Common Mistake — Confusing a status participle with an ongoing action

Error: writing الطَّلَب مُرَاجَع الآن to mean that a request is currently being reviewed, when مُرَاجَع as a bare participle suggests the review is already complete.

Correction: use the full passive verb for an action in progress, as in الطَّلَب يُرَاجَع الآن or الطَّلَب قَيْد المُرَاجَعَة, and reserve the bare participle مُرَاجَع for a completed state.

Common Mistake — Leaving the sequence unmarked

Error: listing several passive-verb sentences with no connector between them, leaving the reader to guess the order of the steps.

Correction: open each new step with أَوَّلًا, ثُمَّ, بَعْد ذَلِك, or أَخِيرًا, so the sequence is explicit rather than assumed.

Chapter Summary

This chapter moved the passive voice, recognized since Chapter 4, into independent production for describing formal procedures with verbs such as يُقَدَّم, يُرَاجَع, and يُعْتَمَد. It introduced the sequence connectors أَوَّلًا, ثُمَّ, بَعْد ذَلِك, and أَخِيرًا for ordering steps, and it distinguished a full passive verb describing an action from a status participle such as مُعْتَمَد describing a completed state. No new clause type was introduced; the content clauses and source-attribution patterns from earlier chapters continue to operate, now applied to procedures. Chapter 15 is the Unit III assessment: it introduces no new system and asks you to combine opinion, comparison, data commentary, and process description into one supported argument.

Exercise 2 (model answer): أَوَّلًا، يُقَدَّم الطَّلَب. ثُمَّ يُرَاجَع المِلَفّ. بَعْد ذَلِك، تُعْلَن النَّتِيجَة. وَأَخِيرًا، يُبَلَّغ الطَّالِب.

Exercise 3: 1. مُعْتَمَد — describes the completed state of the request, matching "since two days ago." 2. تُرَاجَع — describes the review as an action still in progress, matching "currently."

Reading Practice — Comprehension

  1. A recent photo must be submitted with the request, at least two weeks before the old card expires.
  2. The student affairs office reviews the request, checking that the data is complete.
  3. After the review, the card is printed, and the new card is approved by the administration; finally, the student is informed that the card is ready for pickup.
  4. According to the statistics mentioned, most requests are approved within ten days of submission.

Listening Practice

  • The first step is submitting the request with the required documents before the end of October.
  • The scholarship committee reviews the file, and the review takes three weeks.
  • Complete applications meeting the conditions are approved; incomplete applications are rejected.
  • All students are informed of the result by email at the start of December.

Chapter Review — Model responses

Section A.1: يُقَدَّم describes submission; يُرَاجَع and يُعْتَمَد describe the review and approval stage; يُبَلَّغ describes the final notification.

Section A.2: Step 1 (أَوَّلًا): submitting the renewal request with a photo. Step 2 (ثُمَّ): the office reviews the request. Step 3 (بَعْد ذَلِك): the card is printed and approved. Step 4 (وَأَخِيرًا): the student is informed the card is ready.

Section B: Open-ended self-assessment; any honest, specific response identifying one part of the structure and a concrete plan to strengthen it is acceptable.

You can now describe a formal procedure step by step using the passive voice, order its stages with sequence connectors, and describe the current status of a request with a participle. Chapter 15 draws on this alongside opinion, comparison, and data commentary to build one integrated, evidence-based argument.

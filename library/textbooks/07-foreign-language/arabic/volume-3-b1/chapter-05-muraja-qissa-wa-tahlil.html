مُرَاجَعَة ١: قِصَّة وَتَحْلِيلUnit I Assessment: Narrative and Analytical Summary

Synthesizing extended narration, cause and result, and summary and paraphrase · Speak and Write Arabic: Independent Communication and Advanced Expression

Chapter Overview

This chapter is the Unit I assessment. It introduces no new grammar, morphology, script, case, mood, numeral, heritage, or specialized-domain system. Instead, it brings together everything you built in Chapters 1 through 4: extended narration with كَانَ and the perfect/imperfect system, time clauses, causes and results with purpose and subjunctive expansion, and reporting and summarizing other people's words with أَنَّ content clauses and source attribution. The unit performance task asks you to do both at once — tell an organized, well-sequenced account of something that happened, and then step back and produce an analytical summary of it that names causes, results, and what was reported by whom. Treat this chapter as a checkpoint: a chance to show, in one connected piece of work, how far your narration and analysis have come since the start of Volume III.

About this chapter. Chapter 5 is a synthesis checkpoint, not a new-content chapter. Every system practiced here was already introduced in Chapters 1-4. Nothing above the authorized status for Chapter 5 in the Part III matrices is taught, assessed, or required.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Narrate a multi-stage past event using كَانَ, the perfect and imperfect systems, and time clauses (لَمَّا، عِنْدَمَا، بَعْدَمَا، قَبْلَ أَنْ) from Chapter 2.
  2. Explain why events happened and what resulted from them, using cause, result, and purpose language (لِأَنَّ، بِسَبَب، نَتِيجَةً لِـ، لِذَلِك، مِمَّا، لِكَي) from Chapter 3.
  3. Report what someone said, wrote, or found using a reporting verb and a content clause with أَنَّ, and attribute that information to its source (حَسَبَ، وَفْقًا لِـ، بِحَسَبِ) from Chapter 4.
  4. Combine narration and analysis in a single organized text: tell what happened, then explain its causes and results, then summarize what others said about it.
  5. Read or listen to an extended account and identify its sequence of events, its causes and results, and its reported claims.
  6. Distinguish, throughout your own account, your own narration from information you are reporting from someone else.
  7. Self-assess a piece of connected writing against a cumulative checklist covering narration, explanation, and summary.
  8. Present, in under three minutes, an integrated account that narrates, explains, and summarizes without introducing material beyond Chapters 1-4.

Opening Communication Scenario

Imagine you are asked to brief someone who was away for two weeks: what happened at work, at school, or in your community, why it happened, what it led to, and what people are saying about it now. A flat list of facts would not serve this purpose. What is needed is a short narrative of events in order, an explanation of causes and results, and a summary of what others have reported — clearly separated from your own account. This chapter's performance task recreates exactly that situation.

  1. You will review a connected model that narrates, explains, and summarizes in sequence.
  2. You will practice moving between narration (what happened), explanation (why it happened and what it led to), and summary (what others said about it).
  3. You will produce your own integrated narrative-and-analysis account, in writing and aloud.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Reading a connected multi-clause account aloud

This chapter does not introduce a new pronunciation target. It asks you to apply, together, the phrase-grouping, pause-form, and connector-stress habits practiced since Chapter 1: pause naturally at major clause boundaries, keep connectors such as لِأَنَّ، نَتِيجَةً لِـ، and وَفْقًا لِـ grouped with the clause they introduce, and continue distinguishing أَنَّ (doubled ن) from أَنْ (single ن with sukūn) when reading reported statements aloud.

Reading and speaking continue at the unvocalized-with-selective-support level established in Chapter 4. No new vocalization target is introduced in this chapter.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter introduces no new grammar system. It reviews how narration, cause-and-result explanation, and reported summary work together as a single connected text, and gives you a model for combining all three.

Focus — Three moves, one connected account

An integrated narrative-and-analysis text typically moves through three stages: (1) narration — what happened, in sequence, using perfect/imperfect and time clauses; (2) explanation — why it happened and what it led to, using cause and result connectors; and (3) summary — what others said or reported about it, using a reporting verb, أَنَّ, and a source attribution. These three stages do not need to appear in strictly separate paragraphs, but a listener or reader should always be able to tell which stage they are in.

  • لَمَّا وَصَلْتُ إِلَى المَكْتَب صَبَاحًا، كَانَ زُمَلَائِي يَتَحَدَّثُون عَن مُشْكِلَة فِي النِّظَام.When I arrived at the office in the morning, my colleagues were talking about a problem in the system.narration — time clause + كَانَ + imperfect
  • تَوَقَّف النِّظَام بِسَبَب اِنْقِطَاع الكَهْرَبَاء، مِمَّا أَخَّر عَمَل الفَرِيق طَوَال الصَّبَاح.The system stopped because of a power outage, which delayed the team's work all morning.explanation — cause + result
  • وَبِحَسَبِ مَا قَالَه المَسْؤُول التِّقْنِيّ، أَكَّد أَنَّ المُشْكِلَة اِنْتَهَت قَبْل الظُّهْر.And according to what the technical officer said, he confirmed that the problem ended before noon.summary — source attribution + reporting verb + أَنَّ
Transition pattern: moving between narration, explanation, and summary
narration (time clause + verb) → explanation (بِسَبَب / لِأَنَّ ... مِمَّا / نَتِيجَةً لِـ) → summary (وَفْقًا لِـ / حَسَبَ + reporting verb + أَنَّ)

A well-organized account signals each shift clearly. Moving from narration to explanation often uses بِسَبَب or لِأَنَّ right after the narrated event. Moving from explanation to summary often opens with a source-attribution phrase such as وَفْقًا لِـ or بِحَسَبِ, which tells the listener that reported information is coming next.

وَنَتِيجَةً لِذَلِك، قَرَّرَت الإِدَارَة تَغْيِير النِّظَام؛ وَذَكَرَت الإِدَارَة أَنَّهَا سَتُطَبِّق الحَلّ الجَدِيد الشَّهْر المُقْبِل.
As a result, the administration decided to change the system; and the administration mentioned that it will apply the new solution next month.

No system beyond Chapters 1-4 is introduced or assessed here. Passive voice, conditional sentences, and productive case endings remain outside this chapter's scope, exactly as in Chapter 4.

Model Dialogue

Context: a student briefs a classmate on everything that happened with a group project during the classmate's absence.

لَيْلَى: كُنْتُ غَائِبَة أُسْبُوعًا كَامِلًا. مَاذَا حَدَث مَع المَشْرُوع؟I was absent a full week. What happened with the project?
سَامِي: لَمَّا بَدَأْنَا الأُسْبُوع، كَانَ الفَرِيق يَعْمَل عَلَى الجُزْء الأَوَّل مِنَ العَرْض. لَكِنْ بَعْدَمَا اِكْتَشَفْنَا خَطَأً فِي البَيَانَات، اِضْطَرَرْنَا إِلَى تَغْيِير الخُطَّة.When we started the week, the team was working on the first part of the presentation. But after we discovered an error in the data, we had to change the plan.
لَيْلَى: وَلِمَاذَا حَدَث هَذَا الخَطَأ؟And why did this error happen?
سَامِي: حَدَث الخَطَأ بِسَبَب مَعْلُومَات قَدِيمَة اِسْتَخْدَمْنَاهَا، مِمَّا أَخَّر العَمَل يَوْمَيْن كَامِلَيْن.The error happened because of old information we used, which delayed the work two full days.
لَيْلَى: وَمَاذَا قَالَ الأُسْتَاذ عَن ذَلِك؟And what did the professor say about that?
سَامِي: وَفْقًا لِمَا قَالَه، أَكَّد أَنَّ التَّأْخِير مَقْبُول طَالَمَا قَدَّمْنَا العَرْض قَبْل نِهَايَة الشَّهْر. وَأَشَارَ إِلَى أَنَّ الفَرِيق يَجِب أَنْ يُرَاجِع البَيَانَات جَيِّدًا لِكَي لَا يَتَكَرَّر الخَطَأ.According to what he said, he confirmed the delay is acceptable as long as we present before the end of the month. And he indicated that the team must review the data well so that the error does not repeat.
لَيْلَى: إِذَن، مَاذَا نَحْتَاج أَنْ نَفْعَل الآن؟So, what do we need to do now?
سَامِي: أَعْتَقِد أَنَّنَا نَحْتَاج إِلَى اِجْتِمَاع قَصِير هَذَا المَسَاء لِنُلَخِّص كُلّ شَيْء وَنُوَزِّع المَهَامّ المُتَبَقِّيَة.I think we need a short meeting this evening to summarize everything and distribute the remaining tasks.

Guided Practice

  1. Write two connected sentences narrating a sequence of two past events using لَمَّا or بَعْدَمَا and كَانَ.
  2. Write one sentence explaining the cause of one of those events using لِأَنَّ or بِسَبَب.
  3. Write one sentence stating the result of that cause using نَتِيجَةً لِـ or مِمَّا.
  4. Write one sentence reporting what someone said about the event, using a reporting verb, أَنَّ, and a source attribution (وَفْقًا لِـ، حَسَبَ، or بِحَسَبِ).
  5. Read your four sentences aloud in order and check that a listener could identify which sentence is narration, which is explanation, and which is summary.

Writing Workshop

In this workshop, you will write the Unit I assessment's written component: an integrated account that narrates an event, explains its causes and results, and summarizes what was reported about it.

  1. Step 1 — Choose an event. Pick a real or invented event with a clear sequence: something that happened at work, school, home, or in your community.
  2. Step 2 — Narrate the sequence. Use كَانَ, the perfect and imperfect systems, and time clauses (لَمَّا، عِنْدَمَا، بَعْدَمَا، قَبْلَ أَنْ) to tell what happened, in order.
  3. Step 3 — Explain cause and result. Use لِأَنَّ or بِسَبَب to state why it happened, and نَتِيجَةً لِـ، مِمَّا، or لِذَلِك to state what it led to.
  4. Step 4 — Summarize what was reported. Use a reporting verb (قَالَ، ذَكَرَ، أَشَارَ إِلَى، أَكَّدَ، أَوْضَحَ) with أَنَّ, and attribute the information with حَسَبَ، وَفْقًا لِـ، or بِحَسَبِ.
  5. Step 5 — Add your own brief reaction. Use أَعْتَقِد أَنَّ to add one sentence of your own view, clearly separated from the reported information.
  6. Review your draft against the cumulative editing checklist below.

قَائِمَة المُرَاجَعَة التَّرَاكُمِيَّة — Cumulative editing checklist:

  • My account narrates events in a clear time sequence, using at least one time clause and كَانَ.
  • I explained at least one cause and one result using connectors from Chapter 3.
  • I reported at least one piece of information using a reporting verb with أَنَّ and a source attribution from Chapter 4.
  • A reader can clearly tell which parts of my account are narration, which are explanation, and which are reported summary.
  • My own opinion, if included, is clearly separated from the reported information.
  • My vocabulary, gender agreement, and plural forms are checked for accuracy.
  • I did not introduce any grammar, vocabulary, or script system beyond Chapters 1-4.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Briefing someone who was absent

Briefing a colleague, classmate, or family member who missed events is a routine communicative task across Arabic-speaking communities, though the expected level of detail, formality, and directness varies with relationship, setting, and context. Combining a clear narrative with an honest account of causes, results, and what others have said is broadly useful in workplaces, classrooms, and community settings, without representing one fixed convention for every situation.

Regional Variation — Everyday narration versus reporting

In casual spoken narration, which may shift toward regional spoken Arabic, some speakers use shorter connectors or more compressed reporting expressions than the full MSA forms practiced across Chapters 2 through 4. The MSA patterns in this chapter remain the productive target for this course and are also normal in formal spoken MSA, written accounts, and media reporting throughout the Arabic-speaking world.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Blurring narration and reported summary

Error: stating تَوَقَّف النِّظَام وَاِنْتَهَت المُشْكِلَة قَبْل الظُّهْر as if both facts were the writer's own direct observation, when the second fact was only reported by someone else.

Correction: mark the reported portion clearly: تَوَقَّف النِّظَام، وَأَكَّد المَسْؤُول التِّقْنِيّ أَنَّ المُشْكِلَة اِنْتَهَت قَبْل الظُّهْر. Keep your own narration and other people's reported words visibly distinct, as practiced in Chapter 4.

Common Mistake — Listing events without cause or result

Error: narrating a chain of events with only time clauses (لَمَّا... ثُمَّ... بَعْدَ ذَلِك...) and never stating why anything happened or what it led to.

Correction: add at least one cause and one result to connect the narrated events, as in تَأَخَّر العَمَل بِسَبَب مَعْلُومَات قَدِيمَة، مِمَّا أَخَّر تَسْلِيم المَشْرُوع. An integrated account needs explanation, not only a sequence of events.

Common Mistake — Introducing untaught systems to sound more advanced

Error: attempting spontaneous full case-ending production, productive passive voice, or conditional sentences in this chapter's task, none of which have been activated for independent production yet.

Correction: rely on the narration, explanation, and summary systems already authorized through Chapter 4. Passive voice becomes productive starting in Chapter 6, and conditionals begin in Chapter 9; using them here is not required and is not assessed.

Chapter Summary

This chapter introduced no new language system. It brought together extended narration with كَانَ, the perfect/imperfect system, and time clauses (Chapter 2); cause, result, and purpose explanation with the subjunctive (Chapter 3); and reporting and source attribution with أَنَّ content clauses (Chapter 4) into a single connected account. You practiced narrating a sequence of events, explaining why they happened and what they led to, and summarizing what others reported about them, while keeping each stage clearly distinguishable. This integrated skill prepares you for Unit II, which turns these same narration, explanation, and reporting tools toward news, public information, and institutional communication.

Model expansion: لَمَّا وَصَلْنَا إِلَى القَاعَة، كَانَ الجُمْهُور يَنْتَظِر بَدْء العَرْض. وَبَعْدَمَا تَأَخَّرَ العَرْض عِشْرِين دَقِيقَة، شَعَرَ بَعْض الحَاضِرِين بِالإِحْبَاط. وَحَدَث التَّأَخُّر بِسَبَب مُشْكِلَة فِي جِهَاز العَرْض، مِمَّا اِضْطَرّ المُنَظِّمِين إِلَى اِسْتِبْدَاله بِسُرْعَة. وَوَفْقًا لِمَا ذَكَرَه أَحَد المُنَظِّمِين، أَكَّد أَنَّ العَرْض القَادِم سَيَبْدَأ فِي مَوْعِده تَمَامًا. Other well-formed accounts combining narration, explanation, and reported summary are equally acceptable.

Listening Practice

  • Narration: heavy traffic began this winter; the number of accidents rose noticeably; the municipality decided to study the problem.
  • Cause: closure of a bridge for maintenance, according to the engineers; result: many drivers were forced onto a single road, and waiting times increased greatly.
  • The municipality mentioned it will open an alternate road next week so that (لِكَي) congestion decreases.
  • The transportation official confirmed the situation will improve gradually after the new road opens.

Reading Practice — Comprehension

  1. Water was cut off Monday morning; residents called the municipality; technicians inspected the pipes and discovered a large break in a main line.
  2. The cause was nearby digging work not well coordinated with the municipality, according to the preliminary report; the result was that the neighborhood remained without water for more than two days.
  3. The municipality indicated the repair finished Wednesday evening.
  4. The municipality announced it will review coordination procedures with digging companies so that (لِكَي) the problem does not recur.

Chapter Review — Model responses

Section A.2: Any accurate response noting that a reader or listener needs to know whether information reflects the speaker's own firsthand account or someone else's claim, since accuracy and credibility depend on that distinction.

Section B: Open-ended self-assessment; any honest, specific response that names concrete choices made while narrating, explaining, and summarizing is acceptable.

Other well-formed, accurate answers combining the required systems are equally acceptable throughout this section.

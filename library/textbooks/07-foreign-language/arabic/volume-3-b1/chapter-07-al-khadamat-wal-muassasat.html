الخَدَمَات وَالمُؤَسَّسَاتServices and Institutions

Formal procedures, institutional correspondence, and expanding the passive voice · Speak and Write Arabic: Independent Communication and Advanced Expression

Chapter Overview

This chapter moves from reading and interpreting news to interacting with the institutions that generate it. You will learn the vocabulary of ministries, government offices, and service institutions, and you will develop the formal procedures needed to request a service, follow up on an application, and understand an institutional reply. The passive voice you met in Chapter 6 through five verbs now develops further: you will recognize and produce a wider range of passive and impersonal forms in the context of official correspondence, since institutional writing regularly avoids naming who performed an action in favor of describing what was done, required, or completed. By the end of this chapter, you will be able to read and write a short formal email to an institution, follow a multi-step service procedure, and use impersonal and passive language appropriately in institutional correspondence.

About this chapter. Chapter 7 develops institutional and civic vocabulary (introduced receptively in Chapter 6) into productive use, and develops the passive voice from the five recognition/controlled-production verbs of Chapter 6 toward broader impersonal and passive/institutional patterns. Formal correspondence, source attribution, and narration from Chapters 2, 4, and 6 continue to be recycled. Conditionals remain receptive only; academic register, legal/administrative specialization, and full passive-participle production remain outside this chapter's scope.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Recognize and use core institutional vocabulary: ministry, agency/authority, institution/organization, department, application/request, procedure, appointment.
  2. Continue developing the passive voice beyond the five verbs of Chapter 6, recognizing additional passive and impersonal forms used in institutional writing (يُطْلَب، يُشْتَرَط، تَمَّ + verbal noun, يَجِب أَن).
  3. Explain the communicative effect of impersonal expressions such as يُطْلَب مِنْكَ ("you are requested to") and تَمَّ اسْتِلَام ("receipt has been completed/received").
  4. Follow and describe a multi-step formal procedure, such as applying for a service or renewing a document.
  5. Read a short institutional notice or set of instructions and identify its requirements, steps, and deadline.
  6. Listen to a brief recorded institutional announcement or automated message and extract the required action and any deadline.
  7. Write a short formal email to an institution requesting information or following up on a submitted application, using appropriate register and at least one passive or impersonal structure.

Opening Communication Scenario

Imagine you need to renew a document, request a certificate, or follow up on an application you submitted to a government office or institution. The instructions you receive rarely address you as an individual actor performing each step; instead they describe what is required, what must be submitted, and what has been completed — يُطْلَب تَقْدِيم... ("submission of... is required"), تَمَّ اسْتِلَام طَلَبِك ("your application has been received"). This chapter's task asks you to follow such a procedure, understand an institutional notice, and write a formal message to follow up.

  1. You will meet institutional vocabulary and a short official procedure.
  2. You will notice how impersonal and passive language keeps institutional writing focused on requirements and outcomes rather than individual actors.
  3. You will write a short formal email requesting information or following up on a service.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Reading formal instructions aloud

Continue developing formal connected reading, introduced in Chapter 6, now applied to numbered instructions and short institutional notices. Practice grouping impersonal expressions such as يُطْلَب تَقْدِيم and تَمَّ اسْتِلَام as single prosodic units, and pause naturally at the end of each numbered step rather than mid-phrase.

Institutional notices are typically unvocalized or only lightly vocalized on unfamiliar terms. This chapter continues the unvocalized practical-text reading developed in Chapter 6.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter develops the passive voice introduced in Chapter 6 into a broader set of impersonal and passive patterns typical of institutional writing. These patterns remain controlled and formulaic in scope; full passive-participle production and specialized legal/administrative register continue to develop through Chapters 8-9.

Focus — Impersonal and passive patterns in institutional writing

Institutional writing frequently avoids naming an individual actor, describing instead what is required of the reader or what has been completed as a process. Two new patterns join the five passive verbs from Chapter 6:

يُطْلَب مِنْ + person / يُطْلَب + verbal noun — "it is required of..." / "...is required," used to state a requirement without naming who is imposing it.

تَمَّ + verbal noun — "...was completed/has been done," a common institutional way of reporting that a process happened, functioning much like a passive even though تَمَّ itself is an active verb meaning "was completed."

  • يُطْلَب مِنْكَ تَقْدِيم المُسْتَنَدَات قَبْل نِهَايَة الأُسْبُوع.You are required to submit the documents before the end of the week.يُطْلَب (it is required) + مِنْكَ (of you) + verbal noun تَقْدِيم
  • تَمَّ اسْتِلَام طَلَبِك بِنَجَاح.Your application has been received successfully.تَمَّ (was completed) + verbal noun اسْتِلَام (receipt/receiving)
  • يُشْتَرَط حُضُور المُوَظَّف شَخْصِيًّا لِاِسْتِلَام الشَّهَادَة.It is stipulated that the employee attend in person to receive the certificate.passive يُشْتَرَط (it is stipulated) + verbal noun حُضُور
Pattern: expanding passive and impersonal institutional language
يُطْلَب / يُشْتَرَط / تَمَّ + verbal noun (مصدر), describing a requirement or a completed institutional process

These patterns recycle the verbal-noun (مَصْدَر) forms recognized since earlier chapters and combine them with a small set of impersonal or passive verbs. As with the five verbs from Chapter 6, the grammatical subject is the requirement or the process, not a named person. An agent, when stated, still uses مِنْ or مِنْ قِبَل, and remains optional. Do not attempt the full range of passive participles or specialized legal phrasing at this stage; those expand in Chapters 8-9.

يُطْلَب مِنَ المُتَقَدِّمِين تَعْبِئَة الطَّلَب إِلِكْتُرُونِيًّا، وَتَمَّ تَمْدِيد المَوْعِد النِّهَائِيّ حَتَّى نِهَايَة الشَّهْر.
Applicants are required to fill out the application electronically, and the deadline has been extended until the end of the month.

This chapter's productive passive/impersonal set is: أُعْلِنَ، صَدَرَ، نُشِرَ، قِيلَ، أُفِيدَ (recycled from Chapter 6) plus يُطْلَب، يُشْتَرَط، and تَمَّ + verbal noun (new). Broader passive participles, additional institutional forms, and legal/administrative register expand in Chapters 8-9. Conditional sentences remain receptive only.

Model Dialogue

Context: a citizen calls a government service office to follow up on a submitted application.

المُوَاطِن: السَّلَام عَلَيْكُم، أُرِيد أَن أَسْأَل عَن طَلَبِي. هَل تَمَّ اسْتِلَامُه؟Hello, I'd like to ask about my application. Has it been received?
المُوَظَّفَة: نَعَم، تَمَّ اسْتِلَام طَلَبِك الأُسْبُوع المَاضِي. لَكِن يُطْلَب مِنْك تَقْدِيم مُسْتَنَد وَاحِد إِضَافِيّ.Yes, your application was received last week. But you are required to submit one additional document.
المُوَاطِن: أَيّ مُسْتَنَد؟ لَم يُذْكَر ذَلِك فِي البَيَان الَّذِي نُشِرَ عَلَى المَوْقِع.Which document? That wasn't mentioned in the statement that was published on the website.
المُوَظَّفَة: يُشْتَرَط تَقْدِيم شَهَادَة العُنْوَان. وَيُمْكِنُك تَقْدِيمُهَا إِلِكْتُرُونِيًّا عَبْر المَوْقِع نَفْسِه.A proof-of-address certificate is required. You can submit it electronically through the same website.
المُوَاطِن: شُكْرًا. وَهَل هُنَاك مَوْعِد نِهَائِيّ؟Thank you. Is there a deadline?
المُوَظَّفَة: نَعَم، يُطْلَب تَقْدِيم المُسْتَنَد خِلَال خَمْسَة أَيَّام، وَإِلَّا سَيُلْغَى الطَّلَب.Yes, the document is required within five days, or the application will be cancelled.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Formality in institutional communication

Correspondence with government offices, ministries, and public institutions across Arabic-speaking communities generally follows a consistently formal register, often using set opening and closing phrases and impersonal or passive constructions to keep the tone official and courteous. This formality reflects the genre and setting rather than the personality of any individual writer, and similar conventions appear in institutional correspondence in many languages.

Regional Variation — Service procedures across the region

The names, structure, and specific steps of institutional procedures vary considerably by country, and even by city or agency within a country. The vocabulary and formal-correspondence patterns practiced in this chapter are broadly recognizable across MSA institutional writing, but learners should expect real-world variation in required documents, timelines, and terminology from one institution to another.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Naming an unnecessary agent in impersonal sentences

Error: writing يُطْلَب مِن قِبَل شَخْص مَا تَقْدِيم المُسْتَنَد ("the document is required by some person"), forcing an agent phrase that undermines the impersonal, official tone.

Correction: simply state يُطْلَب تَقْدِيم المُسْتَنَد. Add مِن قِبَل only when a specific institution or office is genuinely being named as the source of the requirement.

Common Mistake — Confusing تَمَّ with a true passive verb

Error: treating تَمَّ as if it directly conjugates like the five passive verbs from Chapter 6, producing an incorrect form such as تُمَّ الاِسْتِلَام.

Correction: remember that تَمَّ is an ordinary active Form I verb meaning "was completed," used together with a verbal noun: تَمَّ الاِسْتِلَام ("receipt was completed" / "was received"). It does not itself take a passive vowel pattern.

Common Mistake — Using an informal register in institutional correspondence

Error: opening a formal email to an institution with a casual greeting such as أَهْلًا ("hi"), or attempting conditional sentences and legal phrasing not yet active in this chapter.

Correction: use the formal opening and closing conventions recycled from Chapter 4, and stay within this chapter's authorized impersonal/passive patterns rather than reaching for untaught legal or conditional structures.

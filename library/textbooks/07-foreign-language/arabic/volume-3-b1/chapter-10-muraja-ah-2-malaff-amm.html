مُرَاجَعَة ٢: مَلَفّ عَامّUnit II Assessment: A Public-Information Dossier

Synthesizing media, institutional, technological, and community-issue Arabic from Chapters 6-9 · Speak and Write Arabic: Independent Communication and Advanced Expression

Chapter Overview

This chapter is the Unit II assessment. It introduces no new grammar, morphology, or vocabulary system. Instead, it brings together the news and media language of Chapter 6, the institutional and passive/impersonal language of Chapter 7, the technical and nominalized process language of Chapter 8, and the cause-result chains, concession, and إِذَا-conditionals of Chapter 9 into a single integrated task: reading a short public-information dossier and writing a formal response to it. You will read three connected documents — a municipal notice, a short media report, and an institutional email — that together describe a single community issue and a proposed institutional response. You will then write your own formal email responding to the dossier, using the systems from Chapters 6-9 at the level already authorized for this volume. By the end of this chapter, you will have demonstrated that you can move between reading, listening to, and producing connected, multi-genre public-information Arabic.

About this chapter. Chapter 10 is a synthesis checkpoint. No system beyond what Chapters 6-9 authorized is introduced or assessed here. You are expected to recognize and recycle: media language and passive recognition (Ch. 6); institutional correspondence and passive/impersonal production (Ch. 7); technical vocabulary and nominalization (Ch. 8); and cause-result chains, concession, and real/possible إِذَا-conditionals (Ch. 9). Case and mood remain at receptive/limited-controlled awareness only; لَوْ-conditionals, specialized legal/economic register, and Classical or heritage material remain outside this chapter's scope.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Read a connected, multi-document dossier — a municipal notice, a media report, and an institutional email — and identify how each document frames the same community issue.
  2. Recognize and interpret passive and impersonal constructions (يُطْلَب، يُشْتَرَط، تَمَّ + verbal noun, and recycled passive verbs) across genres.
  3. Identify cause-result chains, concession-before-position structures, and إِذَا-conditionals wherever they appear in the dossier.
  4. Listen to a short public announcement related to the dossier and extract its key facts and proposed action.
  5. Discuss the dossier's issue orally, conceding at least one opposing point before stating a position.
  6. Write a formal email responding to the dossier that uses institutional register, at least one passive or impersonal pattern, a cause-result chain, a concession, and one إِذَا-conditional.
  7. Self-assess a completed response against the Unit II synthesis criteria.

Opening Communication Scenario

Imagine that your municipality has posted a notice about a community problem, a local news outlet has reported on it, and a neighborhood association has emailed residents about a meeting on the topic. You have been asked to read this small dossier of documents and then write a formal email to the association — either supporting its position, proposing an alternative, or asking a clarifying question — using the language you have built across this unit.

  1. You will read three short, connected documents in different genres that describe the same public issue.
  2. You will notice how the same underlying facts are framed differently in a notice, a report, and a personal email.
  3. You will write your own formal response that draws on the full range of systems developed in Chapters 6-9.

The Public-Information Dossier

The three documents below describe a single issue — unreliable public transportation in one neighborhood — from three different institutional and personal perspectives. Read all three before moving to the writing task. Each document recycles systems from a specific earlier chapter; none introduces anything new.

What to notice across the dossier

As you read, track: (1) where passive or impersonal forms appear (يُطْلَب، تَمَّ + مصدر, recycled passive verbs); (2) where a cause is linked to a result, and whether the chain extends further; (3) where a concession appears before a position; and (4) where an إِذَا-conditional proposes a solution.

Document 1 — Municipal Notice (recycles Chapter 7 institutional register)

إِعْلَان مِن البَلَدِيَّة — Municipal Notice

تُعْلِن البَلَدِيَّة أَنَّه تَمَّ اِسْتِلَام عَدَد كَبِير مِن شَكَاوَى السُّكَّان بِخُصُوص تَأَخُّر حَافِلَات النَّقْل العَامّ فِي حَيّ النَّهْر. وَقَد تَمَّ تَشْكِيل لَجْنَة لِدِرَاسَة المُشْكِلَة، وَيُطْلَب مِن السُّكَّان تَعْبِئَة اِسْتِمَارَة الشَّكْوَى المُتَاحَة فِي مَكْتَب البَلَدِيَّة أَو عَلَى المَوْقِع الإِلِكْتْرُونِيّ. وَيُشْتَرَط تَقْدِيم الشَّكَاوَى قَبْل نِهَايَة الشَّهْر لِيَتِمّ أَخْذُهَا فِي الاِعْتِبَار ضِمْن التَّقْرِير النِّهَائِيّ لِلَّجْنَة.

Notice how تَمَّ + مصدر and يُطْلَب / يُشْتَرَط mark this as formal institutional passive/impersonal language, recycled directly from Chapter 7.

Document 2 — Media Report (recycles Chapter 6 media framing and Chapter 9 cause-result chains)

تَقْرِير إِخْبَارِيّ قَصِير — Short News Report

بِسَبَبِ تَأَخُّر الحَافِلَات بِشَكْل مُتَكَرِّر، اِضْطَرَّ كَثِير مِن سُكَّان حَيّ النَّهْر إِلَى اِسْتِخْدَام سَيَّارَاتِهِم الخَاصَّة. وَنَتِيجَةً لِذَلِك، اِزْدَادَ الاِزْدِحَام فِي الشَّوَارِع المُحِيطَة، وَبِالتَّالِي اِرْتَفَعَت شَكَاوَى السُّكَّان إِلَى البَلَدِيَّة. وَقَد صَرَّح مَسْؤُول فِي البَلَدِيَّة بِأَنَّه تَمَّ تَشْكِيل لَجْنَة لِدِرَاسَة أَسْبَاب التَّأَخُّر، رَغْمَ أَنَّ بَعْض السُّكَّان يَرَوْن أَنَّ الوَعْد بِتَشْكِيل لَجْنَة لَا يَكْفِي وَحْدَه لِحَلّ المُشْكِلَة.

This document extends the cause-result chain from Chapter 9 (بِسَبَبِ → نَتِيجَةً لِذَلِك → بِالتَّالِي) and adds a concession (رَغْمَ أَنَّ) about residents' skepticism, alongside the passive تَمَّ تَشْكِيل recycled from Document 1.

Document 3 — Neighborhood Association Email (recycles Chapter 7 formal email conventions and Chapter 9 إِذَا-conditionals)

المَوْضُوع: اِجْتِمَاع بِخُصُوص مُشْكِلَة النَّقْل العَامّ فِي حَيِّنَا

إِلَى سُكَّان حَيّ النَّهْر الكِرَام،

كَمَا تَعْلَمُون، تَمَّ تَشْكِيل لَجْنَة بَلَدِيَّة لِدِرَاسَة مُشْكِلَة تَأَخُّر الحَافِلَات فِي حَيِّنَا. وَتَدْعُو جَمْعِيَّة الحَيّ جَمِيع السُّكَّان إِلَى اِجْتِمَاع يُعْقَد الأُسْبُوع القَادِم لِمُنَاقَشَة هَذَا المَوْضُوع. رَغْمَ أَنَّ بَعْض الأَعْضَاء يَعْتَقِدُون أَنَّ اِنْتِظَار تَقْرِير اللَّجْنَة هُوَ الخِيَار الأَفْضَل، إِلَّا أَنَّ آخَرِين يُفَضِّلُون تَقْدِيم اِقْتِرَاحَات فَوْرِيَّة. وَإِذَا شَارَك عَدَد كَبِير مِن السُّكَّان فِي هَذَا الاِجْتِمَاع، تَعَزَّزَت فُرْصَة التَّوَصُّل إِلَى حَلّ سَرِيع وَفَعَّال. يُرْجَى تَأْكِيد حُضُورِكُم عَبْر الرَّدّ عَلَى هَذِه الرِّسَالَة.

مَع أَطْيَب التَّحِيَّات،
جَمْعِيَّة حَيّ النَّهْر

To the dear residents of Nahr neighborhood: As you know, a municipal committee has been formed to study the problem of delayed buses in our neighborhood. The neighborhood association invites all residents to a meeting next week to discuss this topic. Although some members believe that waiting for the committee's report is the best option, others prefer offering immediate proposals. And if a large number of residents participate in this meeting, the chance of reaching a quick and effective solution will increase. Please confirm your attendance by replying to this message. With best regards, the Nahr Neighborhood Association.

Listening Practice

Ask your instructor, a partner, or a text-to-speech tool to read the following short public announcement aloud twice at a moderate pace, continuing the formal connected reading developed in Chapters 6-8. Listen without reading along the first time, then answer the questions below.

يُعْلَن لِجَمِيع سُكَّان حَيّ النَّهْر أَنَّه سَيُعْقَد اِجْتِمَاع عَامّ يَوْم الخَمِيس القَادِم فِي قَاعَة البَلَدِيَّة لِمُنَاقَشَة مُشْكِلَة تَأَخُّر النَّقْل العَامّ. وَيُطْلَب مِن الرَّاغِبِين فِي الحُضُور التَّسْجِيل مُسْبَقًا. وَإِذَا لَم يَتَمَكَّن أَحَد مِن الحُضُور شَخْصِيًّا، يُمْكِنه إِرْسَال مُلَاحَظَاته كِتَابِيًّا إِلَى مَكْتَب البَلَدِيَّة قَبْل مَوْعِد الاِجْتِمَاع.

مُهِمَّة الاسْتِمَاع — Listening task

  1. What event is being announced, and where and when will it take place?
  2. What is requested of those who wish to attend?
  3. What alternative is offered to residents who cannot attend in person, and what conditional structure introduces it?

Writing Workshop — Formal Response

In this workshop, you will write a formal email responding to the neighborhood association's invitation in Document 3, synthesizing the systems developed across Chapters 6-9. No new system is introduced here; the task is to combine what you already control.

  1. Step 1 — Open formally. Address the association appropriately and state the purpose of your email (confirming attendance, proposing an alternative, or asking a question), recycling the formal email conventions from Chapter 7.
  2. Step 2 — Reference the dossier. Refer to at least one fact from the notice or the report, using a passive or impersonal pattern such as تَمَّ + مصدر or يُطْلَب.
  3. Step 3 — Build a cause-result chain. Use بِسَبَبِ, then extend it with نَتِيجَةً لِذَلِك or وَبِالتَّالِي to explain the effect of the transportation problem on residents.
  4. Step 4 — Concede a point, then state your position. Use رَغْمَ أَنَّ or مَعَ أَنَّ, then pivot with لَكِنْ or بَيْدَ أَنَّ.
  5. Step 5 — Propose a conditional solution. Use إِذَا + a perfect verb in the condition clause, with a present or perfect result clause.
  6. Step 6 — Close formally. End with an appropriate closing formula, as modeled in Document 3.
  7. Review your draft against the checklist below.

قَائِمَة المُرَاجَعَة — Editing checklist:

  • My email opens and closes with appropriate formal conventions.
  • I refer to at least one fact from the dossier using a passive or impersonal pattern.
  • I build a cause-result chain of at least two linked steps.
  • I concede an opposing point before stating my own position.
  • I include at least one إِذَا-conditional with a perfect verb in the condition clause.
  • I did not introduce any system beyond what Chapters 6-9 authorized.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Multi-genre public communication

Public issues in Arabic-speaking communities are typically communicated through several linked but distinct genres: an official notice, media coverage, and community-level correspondence, each shaped by different institutional norms and audiences. Reading across genres, as this dossier asks you to do, is itself a useful independent-communication skill, since real public information rarely arrives through a single, uniform text.

Regional Variation — Institutional style across contexts

The specific institutional vocabulary, formatting, and level of formality used in municipal notices and neighborhood correspondence vary across cities and countries. This chapter's documents model a broadly recognizable formal MSA style; learners should expect some variation in institutional register and procedure across different Arabic-speaking contexts.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Ignoring the passive/impersonal register in a formal response

Error: writing an entirely active-voice, first-person-heavy response that ignores the impersonal institutional style modeled in the dossier, such as overusing أَنَا أَعْتَقِد in every sentence.

Correction: include at least one impersonal or passive pattern such as تَمَّ تَشْكِيل اللَّجْنَة or يُطْلَب مِنَ السُّكَّان, matching the institutional register of the documents you are responding to.

Common Mistake — Dropping the concession under time pressure

Error: stating a position directly without acknowledging the opposing view already present in the dossier (for example, ignoring the residents who think waiting for the committee is sufficient).

Correction: concede that view first with رَغْمَ أَنَّ or مَعَ أَنَّ before pivoting to your own position, as required by this unit's synthesis criteria.

Common Mistake — Introducing an untaught system to sound more advanced

Error: attempting a hypothetical لَوْ-conditional, full spontaneous case-ending recitation, or specialized legal vocabulary to make the response sound more sophisticated.

Correction: stay within the systems authorized through Chapter 9. This assessment evaluates accurate, fluent use of what has already been taught, not the addition of new material.

Answer Key

Reading Practice — Comprehension

  1. Residents must submit their complaint using the form available at the municipal office or website before the end of the month, so it can be considered in the committee's final report.
  2. Repeated bus delays caused residents to rely on private cars, which increased congestion on surrounding streets, which in turn increased complaints to the municipality.
  3. The concession is that some residents believe the promise to form a committee is not enough on its own to solve the problem; this view is held by some residents, as reported by the news article.
  4. Some association members believe waiting for the committee's report is best; others prefer offering immediate proposals. If a large number of residents attend the meeting, the chance of reaching a quick, effective solution will increase.
  5. تَمَّ تَشْكِيل لَجْنَة ("a committee was formed") appears in both the notice and the report, serving to describe an institutional action without naming a specific agent.

Listening Practice

  • A public meeting is announced for next Thursday at the municipal hall to discuss the transportation delay problem.
  • Those wishing to attend are asked to register in advance.
  • Residents unable to attend in person may send written comments to the municipal office before the meeting; this alternative is introduced with an إِذَا-conditional (وَإِذَا لَم يَتَمَكَّن أَحَد...).

Chapter Review — Model responses

Section A.1: Any accurate identification of a shared passive/impersonal construction (such as تَمَّ تَشْكِيل or يُطْلَب) with a reasonable explanation of its institutional function in each document is acceptable.

Section A.2: Delayed buses → residents use private cars → congestion increases → complaints rise → a committee is formed to study the problem.

Section B: Open-ended self-assessment; any honest, specific response identifying a system and a concrete plan to strengthen it is acceptable.

Other well-formed, accurate answers combining the required systems are equally acceptable throughout this section.

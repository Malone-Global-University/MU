الأَخْبَار وَالمَعْلُومَات العَامَّةNews and Public Information

Media language, the passive voice, and source framing · Speak and Write Arabic: Independent Communication and Advanced Expression

Chapter Overview

This chapter opens Unit II by turning your narration, explanation, and reporting tools from Unit I toward a new domain: news and public information. You will learn the vocabulary of headlines, reports, and official announcements, and you will meet a new grammar system — the Arabic passive voice — for the first time. The passive lets you describe an event, a decision, or a process without necessarily naming who performed it, which is exactly how public announcements, news reports, and official notices are usually phrased. You will also continue building the source-framing skills from Chapter 4, now applied to media language such as headlines and news agency reports. By the end of this chapter, you will be able to read a short news item, recognize and begin to use passive forms, and explain what an announcement says without needing to know or state who is responsible for the action.

About this chapter. Chapter 6 introduces two closely related systems: media/public-information vocabulary and the passive voice (introduced here, expanded through Chapter 9). Narration, cause-and-result, and source attribution from Chapters 2-4 continue to be recycled. Case awareness moves from introduced to developed; conditionals, academic register, and specialized domains remain outside this chapter's scope.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Recognize and use core news and public-information vocabulary: headline, report, source, news agency, official statement, announcement.
  2. Recognize the internal Arabic passive voice in news and public-information texts and explain what it communicates.
  3. Form a small set of high-frequency passive verbs (أُعْلِنَ، صَدَرَ، نُشِرَ، قِيلَ، أُفِيدَ) in simple controlled sentences.
  4. Explain when a speaker or writer chooses the passive voice instead of the active voice, and what effect that choice has.
  5. Continue applying source attribution (وَفْقًا لِـ، حَسَبَ، بِحَسَبِ) and reporting verbs to media contexts specifically.
  6. Read a short news item or public announcement and identify its headline, main event, and source.
  7. Listen to a brief news bulletin and identify what happened, according to whom, and whether the passive voice was used.
  8. Write a short public notice or news-style paragraph using at least one passive verb and one source attribution.

Opening Communication Scenario

Imagine you check the news on your phone or scan a public notice board. Headlines rarely say exactly who did something; instead they announce that a decision was made, a road was closed, or a statement was issued. This is the natural home of the passive voice: describing events, decisions, and processes as public facts rather than as someone's personal action. This chapter's task asks you to read, listen to, and eventually write short items in this style.

  1. You will meet a short set of news headlines and a public announcement.
  2. You will notice how the passive voice lets these texts focus on the event rather than the actor.
  3. You will produce your own short news-style item or public notice using the passive and a source attribution.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Reading a headline and formal announcement aloud

This chapter introduces formal connected reading as a new target: reading short, unvocalized public texts — headlines and announcements — at a natural pace, with attention to phrase grouping around the passive verb and its subject. Practice keeping the passive verb and the noun it describes grouped as one prosodic unit, for example أُعْلِنَ القَرَار grouped together rather than paused between the two words.

News headlines are often only lightly vocalized or unvocalized; this chapter begins regular practice reading such texts, building on the selectively vocalized reading established in Chapter 4.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter introduces the Arabic passive voice for the first time, in a small set of high-frequency verbs commonly used in news and public-information contexts. The passive is introduced for recognition and limited controlled production only; broader passive production continues to develop through Chapter 9.

Focus — Why the passive voice?

The passive voice describes an event, decision, or process without stating who performed it — either because the agent is unknown, unimportant to the message, or intentionally left unstated for a more official or neutral tone. This is exactly the style of most headlines and public announcements: أُغْلِق الطَّرِيق ("the road was closed") focuses on the fact of the closure, not on who closed it.

  • أُعْلِنَ القَرَار صَبَاح اليَوْم.The decision was announced this morning.passive: أُعْلِنَ (was announced) + subject
  • صَدَرَ بَيَان رَسْمِيّ عَنِ الوِزَارَة.An official statement was issued by the ministry.صَدَرَ (was issued) + agent introduced with عَنْ
  • نُشِرَ التَّقْرِير فِي الصَّحِيفَة اليَوْم.The report was published in the newspaper today.نُشِرَ (was published)
Pattern: recognizing and using the internal passive
passive verb (fa'ila / fu'ila pattern) + logical subject, agent optionally introduced with عَنْ or مِنْ قِبَل

The five passive verbs practiced in this chapter — أُعْلِنَ (was announced), صَدَرَ (was issued), نُشِرَ (was published), قِيلَ (was said), أُفِيدَ (it was reported) — all describe public information events. Notice that the grammatical subject of a passive sentence is the thing that was announced, issued, or published, not the person who did the announcing. When the agent is stated at all, it is introduced with عَنْ ("from/by") or مِنْ قِبَل ("on the part of"), and this remains optional in the sentences practiced here.

وَأُفِيدَ أَنَّ الاِجْتِمَاع سَيُعْقَد الأُسْبُوع المُقْبِل، وَفْقًا لِمَصَادِر رَسْمِيَّة.
And it was reported that the meeting will be held next week, according to official sources.

Only these five high-frequency passive verbs are practiced for controlled production in this chapter. Broader passive morphology, passive participles, and impersonal process language expand through Chapters 7-9. Conditional sentences and productive spontaneous case-ending recitation remain outside this chapter's scope.

Model Dialogue

Context: two colleagues discuss a news item they both saw this morning.

هَنَاء: هَل رَأَيْتِ الخَبَر اليَوْم؟ قِيلَ إِنَّ الطَّرِيق الرَّئِيسِيّ سَيُغْلَق غَدًا.Did you see the news today? It was said that the main road will be closed tomorrow.
فَادِي: نَعَم، قَرَأْت التَّقْرِير. نُشِرَ فِي مَوْقِع وَكَالَة الأَنْبَاء صَبَاح اليَوْم. وَأُفِيدَ أَنَّ السَّبَب هُوَ أَعْمَال صِيَانَة.Yes, I read the report. It was published on the news agency's website this morning. And it was reported that the reason is maintenance work.
هَنَاء: وَهَل صَدَرَ بَيَان رَسْمِيّ مِنَ البَلَدِيَّة؟And was an official statement issued by the municipality?
فَادِي: نَعَم، أُعْلِنَ البَيَان عَلَى صَفْحَة البَلَدِيَّة، وَذَكَرَ المُرَاسِل أَنَّ الإِغْلَاق سَيَسْتَمِرّ يَوْمًا وَاحِدًا فَقَط.Yes, the statement was announced on the municipality's page, and the correspondent mentioned that the closure will last only one day.
هَنَاء: جَيِّد. إِذَن سَنَحْتَاج إِلَى طَرِيق بَدِيل غَدًا فَقَط.Good. So we'll need an alternate route only tomorrow.

Guided Practice

  1. Write one sentence using أُعْلِنَ to announce a decision, without naming who made it.
  2. Write one sentence using صَدَرَ or نُشِرَ describing a statement or report becoming public.
  3. Write one sentence using قِيلَ or أُفِيدَ to report a piece of public information, including a source attribution (وَفْقًا لِـ، حَسَبَ، or بِحَسَبِ).
  4. Underline the passive verb in each of your three sentences and identify its grammatical subject.
  5. Rewrite one of your sentences in the active voice, naming an agent, and compare the difference in focus.

مُهِمَّة الاسْتِمَاع — Listening task

  1. What decision was announced, and by which body?
  2. Where was the full report published?
  3. What will change, and why (find the cause)?
  4. According to the local news agency's correspondent, what will the transport authority do?

Speaking Practice

تَدْرِيب مُوَجَّه — Controlled practice: Turn a fact into a headline

With a partner, take turns turning a simple active-voice fact into a passive-voice headline. For example, turn "the ministry announced a decision" into "a decision was announced," using أُعْلِنَ.

مُهِمَّة تَوَاصُل — Communicative task: Report a local news item

In pairs, invent a short local news item (a road closure, a new service, an event). One partner reports it aloud, using at least one passive verb and a source attribution; the other partner asks one follow-up question using مَاذَا قِيلَ عَن...؟ or مَتَى سَيَحْدُث ذَلِك؟

لِلْمُتَعَلِّمِينَ المُسْتَقِلِّينَ — Solo alternative

Record yourself reading a short invented news bulletin aloud, in under two minutes, using at least two of the five passive verbs practiced in this chapter and one source attribution.

Writing Workshop

In this workshop, you will write a short news-style item or public notice using the passive voice and a source attribution.

  1. Step 1 — Choose an event. Pick a real or invented public event: a decision, a new service, a closure, or an announcement.
  2. Step 2 — Announce it in the passive. Use أُعْلِنَ، صَدَرَ، or نُشِرَ to state that the event happened, without necessarily naming who caused it.
  3. Step 3 — Add a detail. Use قِيلَ or أُفِيدَ to add a further piece of public information about the event.
  4. Step 4 — Attribute your source. Add a source attribution (وَفْقًا لِـ، حَسَبَ، بِحَسَبِ) as practiced in Chapter 4.
  5. Step 5 — Add a cause or result if relevant. Recycle بِسَبَب، مِمَّا، or نَتِيجَةً لِـ from Chapter 3 if your item includes one.
  6. Review your draft against the checklist below.

قَائِمَة المُرَاجَعَة — Editing checklist:

  • My item uses at least one of the five passive verbs correctly.
  • I did not force a passive-agent phrase (عَنْ / مِنْ قِبَل) unless it was natural to include.
  • I included a source attribution from Chapter 4.
  • I recycled at least one cause/result connector or time clause from earlier chapters if relevant.
  • My vocabulary, gender agreement, and plural forms are checked for accuracy.
  • I did not introduce grammar beyond the five passive verbs, or any conditional sentence.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Public announcements and the passive voice

Official statements, government notices, and headlines across Arabic-language media commonly use the passive voice to emphasize the event or decision itself rather than a named actor, a stylistic tendency shared with many languages' public-information registers. This does not mean speakers avoid stating who did something in everyday conversation — the choice depends on genre, formality, and the writer's intent, and varies among outlets, institutions, and communities.

Regional Variation — News Arabic across the region

Major news agencies and broadcasters across North Africa, the Levant, the Gulf, and elsewhere generally use similar MSA patterns for headlines and reports, though individual outlets vary somewhat in style, formality, and preferred connectors. The passive constructions and source-attribution phrases practiced in this chapter are broadly recognizable across mainstream Arabic-language media.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Adding an unnecessary agent phrase

Error: writing أُعْلِنَ القَرَار مِنْ قِبَل شَخْص مَا ("the decision was announced by some person"), forcing an agent phrase when the point of the passive is that the agent does not need to be named.

Correction: simply state أُعْلِنَ القَرَار صَبَاح اليَوْم. Add an agent with عَنْ or مِنْ قِبَل only when a specific, relevant source is being named, such as a ministry or agency.

Common Mistake — Confusing the passive verb with a similar active form

Error: writing أَعْلَنَ البَيَان (active: "the statement announced," which does not make sense, since a statement cannot announce itself) instead of the passive أُعْلِنَ البَيَان ("the statement was announced").

Correction: check that the logical subject of your sentence is the thing being announced, issued, published, said, or reported, and use the passive vowel pattern (فُعِلَ) rather than the active pattern (فَعَلَ).

Common Mistake — Introducing untaught systems to sound more advanced

Error: attempting conditional sentences, additional passive verbs beyond the five introduced here, or full academic register, none of which are active in this chapter.

Correction: stay within the five passive verbs (أُعْلِنَ، صَدَرَ، نُشِرَ، قِيلَ، أُفِيدَ) and the narration, cause/result, and source-attribution systems already authorized through Chapter 5. Conditionals begin in Chapter 9, and broader passive production expands through Chapters 7-9.

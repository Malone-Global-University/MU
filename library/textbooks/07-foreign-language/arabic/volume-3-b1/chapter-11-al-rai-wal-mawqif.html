الرَّأْي وَالمَوْقِفChapter 11: Opinion and Position

Thesis, support, qualification, and agreement/disagreement · Speak and Write Arabic: Independent Communication and Advanced Expression

Chapter Overview

This chapter opens Unit III, which moves your Arabic from reporting information toward defending a position. You already know how to narrate, summarize, explain causes and results, and read across genres of public information; this chapter teaches you how to state a clear opinion, support it with at least one reason, qualify it so it does not sound absolute, and respond to someone else's opinion by agreeing or disagreeing in a controlled, polite way. You will build directly on the concession-before-position pattern you practiced in Chapters 9 and 10, extending it into a full thesis-support structure. By the end of the chapter, you will be able to read a short opinion piece, discuss a public issue with a partner while taking and defending a position, and write a short, organized paragraph stating and supporting your own opinion.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. State a clear opinion using expressions such as أَعْتَقِد أَنَّ, مِن وِجْهَة نَظَرِي, and فِي رَأْيِي.
  2. Support a stated opinion with at least one reason, introduced by لِأَنَّ or a recycled cause-result connector.
  3. Qualify an opinion so that it does not sound absolute, using expressions such as إِلَى حَدٍّ مَا, فِي بَعْض الأَحْيَان, and لَا أَعْتَقِد أَنَّ ... دَائِمًا.
  4. Agree or disagree with another person's opinion politely, using expressions such as أَتَّفِق مَعَك فِي أَنَّ, أَخْتَلِف مَعَك قَلِيلًا, and هَذَا صَحِيح إِلَى حَدٍّ مَا، لَكِنْ.
  5. Read a short opinion piece and identify its thesis, supporting reasons, and any qualification.
  6. Discuss a public issue orally, stating a position, supporting it, and responding to a partner's opposing view.
  7. Write a short, organized paragraph that states a thesis, supports it with reasons, and qualifies it appropriately.

Opening Communication Scenario

Imagine that your class or discussion group has been asked to react to a public debate: should students be required to volunteer in their community before graduating? Some people believe this builds character and civic responsibility; others believe it turns a generous act into an unwanted obligation. You have been asked to state your own opinion, support it, and respond respectfully to someone who disagrees with you.

  1. You will learn expressions for stating an opinion clearly and for softening it so it does not sound like an absolute claim.
  2. You will practice supporting an opinion with a reason and responding to another opinion by agreeing or disagreeing.
  3. You will read a short opinion piece, discuss the issue with a partner, and write your own supported opinion paragraph.

التَّرْكِيب — Grammar and Sentence Patterns

Grammar and Sentence Patterns: Thesis, Support, Qualification

This chapter teaches a four-part opinion structure: state a thesis, support it with a reason, qualify it, and — when responding to someone else — agree or disagree. No new verb tense or clause type is introduced; the content clauses and cause-result connectors are recycled from Chapters 3, 4, and 9.

Building Block 1 — Stating a thesis
أَعْتَقِد أَنَّ + (اسم/ضمير) + جملة
The content clause after أَنَّ recycles the pattern developed in Chapter 4 (reported information) and Chapter 3 (subordinate clauses). No new clause type is required.
أَعْتَقِد أَنَّ العَمَل التَّطَوُّعِيّ مُفِيد لِلطُّلَّاب.
I believe that volunteer work is beneficial for students.
Building Block 2 — Supporting with a reason
جملة الرَّأْي + لِأَنَّ + سَبَب
لِأَنَّ introduces the reason clause; this recycles the cause-result system from Chapter 9 rather than introducing a new connector.
أَعْتَقِد أَنَّ العَمَل التَّطَوُّعِيّ مُفِيد لِأَنَّه يُعَلِّم الطُّلَّاب المَسْؤُولِيَّة.
I believe volunteer work is beneficial because it teaches students responsibility.
Building Block 3 — Qualifying the opinion
جملة الرَّأْي + إِلَى حَدٍّ مَا / فِي بَعْض الأَحْيَان
Qualification softens a claim so it does not sound absolute — an important B2 discourse skill, not a new grammatical structure.
هَذَا صَحِيح إِلَى حَدٍّ مَا، لَكِنْ لَيْسَ كُلّ الطُّلَّاب لَدَيْهِم الوَقْت الكَافِي.
This is true to some extent, but not all students have enough time.
Building Block 4 — Agreeing or disagreeing
أَتَّفِق / أَخْتَلِف مَعَك فِي أَنَّ + جملة
Both verbs are sound Form VIII verbs; the complement clause again reuses the familiar أَنَّ + content-clause pattern.
أَخْتَلِف مَعَك قَلِيلًا فِي أَنَّ العَمَل التَّطَوُّعِيّ يَجِب أَن يَكُون إِجْبَارِيًّا.
I disagree with you slightly that volunteer work should be mandatory.

Recycled structures used in this chapter: content clauses with أَنَّ (Ch. 3-4), cause connectors لِأَنَّ / بِسَبَبِ (Ch. 3, 9), and concession with رَغْمَ أَنَّ / مَعَ أَنَّ (Ch. 9-10), which may be combined with the new agreement/disagreement expressions.

أَمْثِلَة إِضَافِيَّة — Additional examples:

  • مِن وِجْهَة نَظَرِي، يَجِب أَن يَخْتَار الطُّلَّاب نَوْع العَمَل التَّطَوُّعِيّ بِأَنْفُسِهِم.From my point of view, students should choose the type of volunteer work themselves.
  • أَتَّفِق مَعَك فِي أَنَّ العَمَل التَّطَوُّعِيّ مُهِمّ، لَكِنِّي لَا أَعْتَقِد أَنَّه يَجِب أَن يَكُون إِجْبَارِيًّا دَائِمًا.I agree with you that volunteer work is important, but I don't believe it should always be mandatory.

Writing Workshop — A Short Opinion Piece

In this workshop, you will write a short, organized paragraph stating and supporting your own opinion on a topic of your choice, using the thesis-support-qualification-response structure taught in this chapter.

  1. Step 1 — Choose a topic and state your thesis. Use أَعْتَقِد أَنَّ or مِن وِجْهَة نَظَرِي to state your opinion clearly in one sentence.
  2. Step 2 — Support your thesis. Add one or two reasons introduced by لِأَنَّ, recycling a cause-result connector from Chapter 9 if useful.
  3. Step 3 — Qualify your opinion. Add one qualification using إِلَى حَدٍّ مَا or فِي بَعْض الأَحْيَان so the paragraph does not sound absolute.
  4. Step 4 — Respond to an opposing view. Briefly mention an opposing opinion and either concede part of it (رَغْمَ أَنَّ) or disagree with it (أَخْتَلِف مَعَ مَن يَقُول).
  5. Step 5 — Close your paragraph. Restate your position briefly in your own words.
  6. Review your draft against the checklist below.

قَائِمَة المُرَاجَعَة — Editing checklist:

  • My paragraph states a clear thesis using an opinion expression.
  • I support my thesis with at least one reason introduced by لِأَنَّ.
  • I qualify my opinion at least once so it does not sound absolute.
  • I acknowledge or respond to an opposing view.
  • I did not introduce any system beyond what this chapter and prior chapters authorized.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Softening a stated opinion

In many formal and semi-formal MSA contexts, speakers commonly qualify strong opinions rather than stating them as absolute facts, particularly in discussion, debate, or written commentary intended for a broad audience. Expressions such as إِلَى حَدٍّ مَا and مِن وِجْهَة نَظَرِي signal that a claim is a personal position open to discussion, which is often considered more persuasive and more polite than an unqualified assertion.

Regional Variation — Disagreement styles

The degree of directness considered appropriate when disagreeing varies across Arabic-speaking communities and settings; some contexts favor more elaborate softening before a disagreement, while others allow a more direct exchange, especially among close friends or in informal spoken registers. The expressions in this chapter model a broadly acceptable, moderately formal MSA style; learners should expect variation in directness across regions and relationships.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Stating an opinion without any support

Error: writing or saying أَعْتَقِد أَنَّ العَمَل التَّطَوُّعِيّ مُفِيد and stopping there, with no reason at all.

Correction: add at least one reason with لِأَنَّ, such as لِأَنَّه يُعَلِّم الطُّلَّاب المَسْؤُولِيَّة, so the opinion is supported rather than merely asserted.

Common Mistake — Overusing absolute language

Error: relying on words like دَائِمًا and كُلّ throughout an opinion paragraph, producing claims such as كُلّ الطُّلَّاب دَائِمًا يُحِبُّون العَمَل الجَمَاعِيّ.

Correction: qualify at least one claim with إِلَى حَدٍّ مَا or فِي بَعْض الأَحْيَان, matching the moderated register modeled in this chapter's reading and dialogue.

Common Mistake — Confusing agreement/disagreement verbs with their prepositions

Error: dropping مَعَ after أَتَّفِق or أَخْتَلِف, producing an incomplete pattern such as أَتَّفِق أَنَّ... without naming who is being agreed with.

Correction: keep the full pattern أَتَّفِق / أَخْتَلِف مَعَ (شَخْص) فِي أَنَّ (جملة), as modeled throughout this chapter.

Chapter Summary

This chapter introduced a four-part opinion structure: stating a thesis with expressions such as أَعْتَقِد أَنَّ and مِن وِجْهَة نَظَرِي; supporting it with a reason using the recycled لِأَنَّ connector; qualifying it with expressions such as إِلَى حَدٍّ مَا; and agreeing or disagreeing with another opinion using أَتَّفِق / أَخْتَلِف مَعَ. No new clause type, tense, or case system was introduced; the chapter recombines content clauses, cause-result connectors, and concession structures from Chapters 3, 4, and 9 into a new communicative function. Chapter 12 builds on this foundation by introducing comparison, classification, and elative structures for evaluating and interpreting evidence.

Looking Ahead

مَا الَّذِي تَسْتَطِيعُ فِعْلَهُ الآن — What you can do now

You can now state an opinion, support it with a reason, qualify it so it does not sound absolute, and agree or disagree with another opinion in a controlled way. Chapter 12 builds directly on this foundation by adding comparison, classification, contrast, and evidence-linked explanation, so that your opinions can be supported not only by reasons but by explicit comparisons between options.

المُقَارَنَة وَالتَّفْسِيرChapter 12: Comparison and Interpretation

Chapter 12 introduces elatives, classification, and contrast structures, and teaches you to link comparisons to evidence — extending the thesis-support pattern from this chapter into fuller argumentation.

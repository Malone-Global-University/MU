بَحْث قَصِير وَعَرْضChapter 18: Short Research and Presentation

Source integration, presentation structure, and Q&A · Speak and Write Arabic: Independent Communication and Advanced Expression

Chapter Overview

Chapter 18 takes the attribution and paraphrase skills you built in Chapter 17 for a single source and extends them to a task where two short sources must be brought together into one coherent short presentation. This chapter introduces no new verb tense, case system, or clause type. Its two closely related new developments are (1) strategies for integrating information from two short sources into a single organized presentation — noting where the sources agree, where they add different details, and where they differ — and (2) an introductory presentation structure with a clear opening, body, and close, followed by a brief structured question-and-answer exchange. You will read two short source texts on a related topic, plan how to combine them, and deliver a short spoken presentation that a listener can follow easily, ending with a few moments of prepared Q&A.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify the main idea and key details of two short related source texts.
  2. Combine information from two sources into a single organized account, noting agreement, added detail, and difference.
  3. Use integration phrases such as "both sources agree that" and "unlike the first text, the second text" to compare sources clearly.
  4. Organize a short presentation with a clear opening, body, and closing.
  5. Recycle attribution and faithful paraphrase from Chapter 17 while combining more than one source.
  6. Respond to a short follow-up question about the presentation's content in prepared, comprehensible MSA.
  7. Produce a short written outline and a spoken presentation that integrates two sources for a specified audience.

Opening Communication Scenario

The cultural exchange program from Chapters 16 and 17 is preparing a short session for new participants on community initiatives in different cities. The coordinators have found two short texts — one about the neighborhood garden project from Chapter 17, and a second, shorter article about a similar project in another city — and have asked each participant to prepare a five-minute presentation that brings the two projects together for the group, followed by two or three questions from the audience.

  1. You will review vocabulary and expressions for comparing and integrating two sources.
  2. You will read both source texts and identify what they share, what each adds, and where they differ.
  3. You will plan and deliver a short organized presentation integrating both sources, and respond to follow-up questions.

النُّطْق — Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus: Signposting a Presentation Aloud

Pronunciation Note — Stress and pause on structure words

Presentation structure words such as أَوَّلًا, ثَانِيًا, and وَأَخِيرًا typically receive a short pause before them and slightly stronger stress on the word itself, so the listener can hear the presentation's shape without seeing an outline. The closing phrase وَخِتَامًا is usually marked by a brief pause and a lower, more deliberate pace, signaling that the final point is coming. This chapter recycles the pause-form and phrase-grouping targets already active in this unit; no new sound or symbol is introduced here.

التَّرْكِيب — Grammar and Sentence Patterns

Grammar and Sentence Patterns: Integrating Two Sources

This chapter introduces two closely related developments: sentence patterns for stating agreement, addition, and contrast between two sources, and presentation-structure connectors that organize a short spoken account into an opening, body, and close. Both build directly on the attribution, content-clause, and contrast structures already active in this volume; neither requires a new verb form, case rule, or clause type.

Pattern 1 — Stating agreement between two sources
يَتَّفِق النَّصَّان عَلَى أَنَّ ... / كِلَا النَّصَّيْن يُشِير إِلَى أَنَّ ...
A content clause introduced by أَنَّ follows the agreement phrase, so the listener understands that both sources support the same claim, not just one of them.
يَتَّفِق النَّصَّان عَلَى أَنَّ المَشْرُوع بَدَأ بِعَدَد قَلِيل مِن المُتَطَوِّعِين ثُمَّ ازْدَاد تَدْرِيجِيًّا.
Both texts agree that the project began with a small number of volunteers and then gradually grew.
Pattern 2 — Marking a difference between two sources
بِخِلَاف النَّصّ الأَوَّل، يُبَيِّن النَّصّ الثَّانِي أَنَّ ...
بِخِلَاف names the first source directly before introducing what the second source states differently, so the contrast is anchored to a specific source rather than left vague.
بِخِلَاف النَّصّ الأَوَّل، يُبَيِّن النَّصّ الثَّانِي أَنَّ المَشْرُوع فِي المَدِينَة الثَّانِيَة حَصَل عَلَى دَعْم مِن البَلَدِيَّة مُنْذ البِدَايَة.
Unlike the first text, the second text shows that the project in the second city received support from the municipality from the beginning.
Pattern 3 — Sequencing a presentation's body
أَوَّلًا، ... ثَانِيًا، ... وَأَخِيرًا، ...
These three sequence markers, already familiar as connectors, are used here specifically to organize the body of a spoken presentation into clearly separated points, helping a listener follow an unfamiliar topic without a written outline in front of them.
أَوَّلًا، سَأَتَحَدَّث عَن المَشْرُوع الأَوَّل. ثَانِيًا، سَأُقَارِنُه بِالمَشْرُوع الثَّانِي. وَأَخِيرًا، سَأَذْكُر مَا يَتَّفِق فِيه المَشْرُوعَان.
First, I will talk about the first project. Second, I will compare it with the second project. And finally, I will mention what the two projects agree on.

Recycled structures used in this chapter: attribution and faithful paraphrase (Chapter 17), contrast and concession connectors (Chapters 9, 12), and content clauses and reported information (Chapter 4) may all be recycled inside a source-integration task; none is retaught here.

Writing Workshop — An Outline for an Integrated Presentation

In this workshop, you will plan and draft a short written outline that integrates the two source texts from this chapter and Chapter 17 into one organized presentation structure.

  1. Step 1 — Note the main idea of each source. Write one sentence summarizing each text's main idea in your own words.
  2. Step 2 — List one point of agreement. Identify a claim both sources support, and draft a sentence using يَتَّفِق النَّصَّان عَلَى أَنَّ.
  3. Step 3 — List one point of contrast or addition. Identify a detail one source gives that the other does not, and draft a sentence using بِخِلَاف النَّصّ الأَوَّل or يُضِيف النَّصّ الثَّانِي أَنَّ.
  4. Step 4 — Draft an opening line. Use سَأَتَحَدَّث اليَوْم عَن or an equivalent phrase to introduce your topic.
  5. Step 5 — Sequence the body. Organize your two or three main points with أَوَّلًا, ثَانِيًا, and وَأَخِيرًا.
  6. Step 6 — Draft a closing line and a question prompt. Use وَخِتَامًا to close, then add هَل لَدَيْكُم أَيّ أَسْئِلَة؟
  7. Review your outline against the checklist below.

قَائِمَة المُرَاجَعَة — Editing checklist:

  • I accurately summarized the main idea of each source.
  • I stated one point of agreement and one point of contrast or addition between the sources.
  • My outline has a clear opening, a sequenced body, and a closing.
  • I did not add any claim that neither source actually makes.
  • I included a phrase inviting questions at the end.
  • I did not introduce any system beyond what this chapter and prior chapters have authorized.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Presentations and public speaking norms

Short organized presentations of the kind practiced in this chapter appear across many educational, community, and professional settings in Arabic-speaking regions, though expectations about formality, audience interaction, and question timing vary by institution and setting. As with earlier chapters, a responsible speaker signals clearly when information comes from a specific source rather than presenting it as universally accepted fact.

Regional Variation — Q&A conventions across settings

Some audiences ask questions only at the end of a presentation, while others interject during the talk itself; a presenter who states a preference clearly at the opening, such as inviting questions only at the close, helps the audience know what to expect.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Blending two sources without marking which is which

Error: presenting details from two different sources as if they came from a single, unnamed source, leaving the listener unable to tell which claim belongs to which text.

Correction: name each source explicitly, as in النَّصّ الأَوَّل and النَّصّ الثَّانِي, especially when marking a contrast between them.

Common Mistake — A presentation with no clear structure

Error: moving between points without any signal of sequence or transition, so the listener cannot follow whether a new idea has begun.

Correction: use أَوَّلًا, ثَانِيًا, and وَأَخِيرًا to mark each new point clearly.

Common Mistake — Guessing an answer the sources do not support

Error: inventing an answer to a follow-up question rather than acknowledging that the sources did not address it.

Correction: respond honestly with لَم يُوَضِّح النَّصّ ذَلِك، لَكِن before offering a reasonable, clearly labeled personal observation if one is appropriate.

Exercise 1: 1. agreement — both projects began with a small number of volunteers. 2. contrast — one text notes the absence of official support while the other reports municipal support. 3. agreement — both projects reached more than thirty participating families.

Exercise 2 (model answer): يَتَّفِق النَّصَّان عَلَى أَنَّ المَشْرُوعَيْن سَاعَدَا الجِيرَان عَلَى التَّعَارُف وَحَقَّقَا النَّتِيجَة نَفْسَهَا.

Exercise 3 (model order): أَوَّلًا (ب) — سَأَتَحَدَّث عَن المَشْرُوع الأَوَّل؛ ثَانِيًا (ج) — سَأُقَارِن المَشْرُوع الأَوَّل بِالثَّانِي؛ وَأَخِيرًا (أ) — سَأَذْكُر مَا يَتَّفِق فِيه المَشْرُوعَان.

Reading Practice — Comprehension

  1. The main difference is that the second project received direct municipal financial support from its first month, unlike the first project.
  2. Both texts share the detail that more than thirty families participated in each project.
  3. Reported information is signaled by بِحَسَب هَذَا النَّصّ and يُشِير النَّصّ إِلَى أَنَّ.
  4. Model sentence: يَتَّفِق النَّصَّان عَلَى أَنَّ التَّعَارُف بَيْن الجِيرَان كَان أَهَمّ نَتِيجَة لِلمَشْرُوعَيْن.

Listening Practice

  • The speaker opens with سَأَتَحَدَّث اليَوْم عَن and closes the account with هَل لَدَيْكُم أَيّ أَسْئِلَة؟
  • The one difference mentioned is that the second city's project received direct municipal support from the beginning.
  • Both sources agree that the most important result in both projects was neighbors getting to know each other, not the green space alone.
  • The speaker invites questions with هَل لَدَيْكُم أَيّ أَسْئِلَة؟

Chapter Review — Model responses

Section A.1: Contrast: بِحَسَب هَذَا النَّصّ، تَوَاصَل عَدَد مِن سُكَّان الحَيّ مَع البَلَدِيَّة مُبَاشَرَة ... وَهُوَ مَا لَم يَحْدُث فِي المَشْرُوع الأَوَّل. Agreement: the closing sentence about neighbors getting to know each other as the most important result "مَرَّة أُخْرَى."

Section A.2: لَيْلَى sequences the body with أَوَّلًا ... ثَانِيًا ... وَأَخِيرًا, and models a response to an unanswerable question with سُؤَال جَيِّد، لَكِن لَم يُوَضِّح النَّصّ ذَلِك.

Section B: Open-ended self-assessment; any honest, specific response identifying one challenge and a concrete plan to address it is acceptable.

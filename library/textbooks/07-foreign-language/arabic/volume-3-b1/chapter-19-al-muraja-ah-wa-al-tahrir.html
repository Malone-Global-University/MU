المُرَاجَعَة وَالتَّحْرِيرChapter 19: Revision and Editing

Cohesion, accuracy, case/mood awareness, and style · Speak and Write Arabic: Independent Communication and Advanced Expression

Chapter Overview

Chapter 19 turns a careful, revising eye on writing you have already produced across this unit — including the presentation outline from Chapter 18. This chapter introduces no new verb tense, case system, or clause type. Its two closely related new developments are (1) a structured approach to revising a text for cohesion and style, checking that ideas connect clearly and that register stays consistent from opening to close, and (2) an editing pass focused on accuracy, with special attention to the case- and mood-sensitive spots that tend to appear in formal connected writing — prepositions, particles, duals, and sound plurals. You will read a text with planted errors, diagnose what is wrong and why, and then revise and edit a piece of your own writing using the same disciplined process.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Distinguish a cohesion problem (unclear connection between ideas) from an accuracy problem (an incorrect form).
  2. Identify and repair weak or missing connectors between sentences and paragraphs.
  3. Check a text for consistent register from its opening to its closing.
  4. Recognize the case- and mood-sensitive spots in formal writing most likely to need review: prepositions, governing particles, duals, and sound plurals.
  5. Use a two-pass revision method: a cohesion-and-style pass, followed by an accuracy-and-editing pass.
  6. Diagnose planted errors in a sample text and explain what should change and why.
  7. Revise and edit a piece of your own writing from this unit using the same structured process.

النُّطْق — Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus: Reading Your Own Revision Aloud

Pronunciation Note — Reading aloud to catch cohesion problems

Reading a revised text aloud, at a natural pace with ordinary phrase grouping and pause forms, is one of the most reliable ways to notice a missing or awkward connector: the ear often catches a gap that the eye passes over. This chapter recycles the phrase-grouping, pause-form, and formal-reading targets already active in this unit; no new sound or symbol is introduced here. When reading a formal edited text aloud for review, keep pauses at clause boundaries natural and unhurried, matching the connectors you have chosen.

التَّرْكِيب — Grammar and Sentence Patterns

Grammar and Sentence Patterns: A Two-Pass Revision Method

This chapter introduces two closely related developments: a cohesion-and-style revision pass, and an accuracy-and-editing pass that directs special attention to case- and mood-sensitive spots already active in this unit. Neither requires a new verb form, case rule, or clause type; both draw on structures already authorized in prior chapters.

Pass 1 — Checking cohesion: does a connector show the right relationship?
هَل الرَّابِط يُبَيِّن العَلَاقَة الصَّحِيحَة بَيْن الجُمْلَتَيْن؟
A revision question, not a rule: for every connector between two sentences, ask whether it signals addition, contrast, cause, result, or sequence — and whether that is the relationship the writer actually intends.
ضَعِيف: بَدَأ المَشْرُوع بِعَدَد قَلِيل مِن المُتَطَوِّعِين. وَازْدَاد العَدَد. — أَقْوَى: بَدَأ المَشْرُوع بِعَدَد قَلِيل مِن المُتَطَوِّعِين، ثُمَّ ازْدَاد العَدَد تَدْرِيجِيًّا.
Weak: The project began with a small number of volunteers. And the number increased. — Stronger: The project began with a small number of volunteers, then the number gradually increased.
Pass 2 — Checking accuracy: case- and mood-sensitive spots
حُرُوف الجَرّ، الجُمُوع السَّالِمَة، المُثَنَّى، الأَدَوَات الحَاكِمَة
During editing, give special attention to prepositions (which govern the genitive), sound masculine plurals and duals (whose case ending changes visibly in formal writing), and particles that govern a following noun or verb — since these are the spots most likely to carry a visible error in edited formal Arabic at this level.
راجِع: شَارَك في المَشْرُوع مُتَطَوِّعُون كَثِيرُون. — عِنْد التَّحْرِير: هَل حَالَة الاِسْم بَعْد حَرْف الجَرّ صَحِيحَة؟
Check: Many volunteers participated in the project. — When editing: is the case of the noun after the preposition correct?
Pass 3 — Checking style: is the register consistent?
هَل يَبْقَى المُسْتَوَى اللُّغَوِيّ ثَابِتًا مِن البِدَايَة إِلَى النِّهَايَة؟
A text that opens with formal MSA and drifts into casual phrasing partway through reads as inconsistent; a revision pass should check that formality, vocabulary choice, and sentence complexity remain even across the whole piece.
راجِع الفَقَرَة كُلَّهَا: هَل بَقِيَت رَسْمِيَّة مِن أَوَّلِهَا إِلَى آخِرِهَا، أَم تَغَيَّر الأُسْلُوب فَجْأَة؟
Review the whole paragraph: did it stay formal from beginning to end, or did the style change suddenly?

Recycled structures used in this chapter: contrast and concession connectors (Chapters 9, 12), content clauses and reported information (Chapter 4), case and mood awareness (recurring since Chapter 4), and hedged generalization and register control (Chapter 16) may all be recycled inside a revision-and-editing task; none is retaught here.

Writing Workshop — Revising a Piece from This Unit

In this workshop, you will apply the two-pass revision method to one piece of writing you produced earlier in this unit: the cultural commentary from Chapter 16, the mediation summary from Chapter 17, or the presentation outline from Chapter 18.

  1. Step 1 — Choose one earlier piece of writing. Select the text you will revise for this workshop.
  2. Step 2 — First pass: cohesion. Read through and mark every connector between sentences and paragraphs. Replace any weak or vague connector with one that shows the precise relationship intended.
  3. Step 3 — First pass: style. Reread the whole text and check that the register stays consistent from opening to close. Adjust any sentence that breaks the established formality.
  4. Step 4 — Second pass: accuracy. Check every preposition, every sound plural and dual, and every governing particle for the correct case or mood ending.
  5. Step 5 — Read the revised text aloud. Confirm that it sounds connected and natural at a normal pace, with appropriate pause forms.
  6. Step 6 — Produce a final version. Prepare a clean copy that reflects both revision passes.
  7. Review your revision against the checklist below.

قَائِمَة المُرَاجَعَة — Editing checklist:

  • I checked every connector between sentences and paragraphs for a clear relationship.
  • I confirmed the register stays consistent from the opening to the close.
  • I checked the case ending on every noun following a preposition.
  • I checked the case or mood ending on duals, sound plurals, and particle-governed forms.
  • I read the revised text aloud and it sounded natural and connected.
  • I did not introduce any system beyond what this chapter and prior chapters have authorized.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Revision as a valued step, not a failure

In many educational and professional settings across Arabic-speaking regions, a text that goes through visible drafts and revision is regarded as a sign of serious, careful work rather than as evidence of an initial mistake. Writers in academic, journalistic, and professional contexts routinely share drafts for peer or editorial review before a piece is considered final.

Regional Variation — Editorial conventions across settings

Expectations about how many rounds of revision a document should go through, and who is expected to give feedback, vary by institution, publication, and workplace; asking directly about a setting's editorial process is a reasonable and common practice.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Editing for accuracy before checking cohesion

Error: fixing individual case endings and spelling in a text whose ideas do not yet connect clearly, so time is spent polishing sentences that may be cut or rewritten anyway.

Correction: complete the cohesion-and-style pass first, then move to the accuracy-and-editing pass on the improved text.

Common Mistake — Letting register drift across a long text

Error: opening a formal piece with careful MSA and gradually sliding into casual phrasing or spoken-style shortcuts by the end.

Correction: reread the whole text in one pass specifically checking for register, comparing the opening and closing directly.

Common Mistake — Overlooking case after prepositions and particles

Error: writing a noun after a preposition or governing particle in the wrong case simply because the ending is often unmarked in casual writing.

Correction: during the accuracy pass, check every preposition, particle, dual, and sound plural individually rather than relying on how the sentence sounds.

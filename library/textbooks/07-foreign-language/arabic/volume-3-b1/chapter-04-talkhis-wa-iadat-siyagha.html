4

تَلْخِيص وَإِعَادَة صِيَاغَةSummary and Paraphrase

Content clauses, reported information, mediation, and concise writing · Speak and Write Arabic: Independent Communication and Advanced Expression

Chapter Overview

In Chapter 3 you learned to explain a decision or event by naming its cause, its result, and its purpose. This chapter turns that explanatory skill toward other people's words: instead of stating your own account, you will report what someone else said, wrote, or found, and condense it into a shorter, accurate version in your own words. You will build the content-clause pattern that follows verbs of saying and thinking, add a small set of reporting verbs and source-attribution phrases, and begin to notice — as a receptive, analytical skill rather than a spontaneous production requirement — how a following noun's case ending can signal its grammatical role after أَنَّ. By the end of the chapter you will be able to read or listen to a short account and produce a shorter, faithful summary that clearly marks whose words or ideas you are reporting.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Form a content clause with أَنَّ after common reporting verbs such as قَالَ، ذَكَرَ، أَشَارَ، وَأَكَّدَ.
  2. Distinguish أَنَّ (introducing a reported statement) from أَنْ (introducing a subjunctive purpose or intention clause) by sound and by function.
  3. Use حَسَبَ، وَفْقًا لِـ، and بِحَسَبِ to attribute information to a named source.
  4. Notice, receptively, that the noun immediately after أَنَّ carries an accusative ending, without being required to produce full case endings spontaneously.
  5. Report what someone said or wrote using a reporting verb, a content clause, and a source attribution.
  6. Read a short account and identify its main claim, its source, and any supporting detail worth keeping in a summary.
  7. Write a concise, accurate summary of a short spoken or written account in your own words.
  8. Explain aloud, in under two minutes, what someone else said or reported, clearly marking the reported material as someone else's words.

Opening Communication Scenario

A colleague who missed a meeting asks you, "What did they say?" A friend who didn't read the article asks, "What's it about?" In both cases, simply repeating everything word for word is neither possible nor useful — you need to select the main point, attribute it clearly to its source, and restate it more briefly than the original. This chapter builds exactly that skill: naming who said or reported something, and stating what they said in a shortened form of your own.

  1. You will compare a full account with a short, accurate summary of it.
  2. You will practice reporting verbs followed by content clauses with أَنَّ.
  3. You will practice attributing information to a source with حَسَبَ, وَفْقًا لِـ, or بِحَسَبِ.
  4. You will summarize, aloud and in writing, a short account someone else gave.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Distinguishing أَنَّ from أَنْ

أَنَّ (with shadda on the ن) and أَنْ (with sukūn on the ن) look similar in unvocalized text but sound different and do different jobs: أَنَّ introduces a reported statement, while أَنْ introduces a subjunctive purpose or intention clause, as you practiced in Chapter 3. Practice holding the doubled ن slightly longer in أَنَّ: قَالَتْ إِنَّهَا سَتَتَأَخَّر versus أَرَادَتْ أَنْ تَتَأَخَّر. Continue applying the phrase-grouping and pause habits from earlier chapters when reading longer reported sentences aloud.

Reading continues at the unvocalized-with-selective-support level. New reporting verbs, أَنَّ, and source-attribution phrases above are vocalized; familiar vocabulary from earlier chapters and Volume II remains lightly marked.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

This chapter introduces content clauses with أَنَّ after common reporting verbs, a small set of source-attribution expressions, and a first, receptive-only look at how case marking appears on the noun or pronoun that follows أَنَّ.

Focus — Reporting verbs and content clauses

A reporting verb such as قَالَ، ذَكَرَ، أَشَارَ، أَكَّدَ، أَوْضَحَ، or اِعْتَقَدَ can be followed directly by أَنَّ and a full clause stating what was said, mentioned, indicated, confirmed, clarified, or believed. This pattern lets you report someone else's words without quoting them directly, which is exactly what a summary requires.

  • قَالَ المُدِير إِنَّ الشَّرِكَة سَتَفْتَح فَرْعًا جَدِيدًا.The manager said that the company will open a new branch.قَالَ + إِنَّ (أَنَّ after قَالَ) — content clause
  • ذَكَرَت الصَّحِيفَة أَنَّ عَدَد الزُّوَّار اِرْتَفَع هَذَا العَام.The newspaper mentioned that the number of visitors rose this year.ذَكَرَ + أَنَّ — content clause
  • أَشَارَ التَّقْرِير إِلَى أَنَّ بَعْض النَّتَائِج مَا زَالَت غَيْر مُؤَكَّدَة.The report indicated that some results are still unconfirmed.أَشَارَ إِلَى + أَنَّ
Source attribution: حَسَبَ, وَفْقًا لِـ, بِحَسَبِ
حَسَبَ / وَفْقًا لِـ / بِحَسَبِ + named source (+ أَنَّ + clause)

حَسَبَ، وَفْقًا لِـ، and بِحَسَبِ all mean "according to" and let you name the source of information before or alongside a reporting verb. All three are common in written and formal spoken MSA; حَسَبَ and بِحَسَبِ are slightly more common in everyday explanation, while وَفْقًا لِـ is common in more formal or written contexts.

وَفْقًا لِلتَّقْرِير، أَكَّدَ الخُبَرَاء أَنَّ الوَضْع تَحَسَّن قَلِيلًا.
According to the report, the experts confirmed that the situation improved slightly.
First look: case marking after أَنَّ (receptive)
أَنَّ + noun (accusative) ...  |  أَنَّهُ / أَنَّهَا + verb (with attached pronoun)

أَنَّ, like إِنَّ, places its noun subject in the accusative case, often visible as a fatḥa or tanwīn fatḥ ending on the noun. When the subject of the reported clause is a pronoun rather than a separate noun, it attaches directly to أَنَّ as a suffix: أَنَّهُ ("that he/it"), أَنَّهَا ("that she/it"). At this stage, notice this pattern when you see or hear it; you are not yet expected to produce full case endings on every noun spontaneously.

أَكَّدَ الوَزِير أَنَّ المَشْرُوعَ سَيَبْدَأ العَام المُقْبِل.
The minister confirmed that the project will begin next year. (المَشْرُوعَ shows a visible accusative ending after أَنَّ.)

Passive voice remains receptive at this stage: you may notice it in reported news items, but productive passive formation is introduced in Chapter 6. Conditional sentences are not yet targeted and appear starting in Chapter 9.

Model Dialogue

Context: a student who missed a lecture asks a classmate to summarize what the professor said.

هِنْد: لَمْ أَحْضُر المُحَاضَرَة أَمْس. مَاذَا قَالَ الأُسْتَاذ بِالضَّبْط؟I didn't attend the lecture yesterday. What exactly did the professor say?
عُمَر: قَالَ إِنَّ الاِمْتِحَان النِّهَائِيّ سَيَكُون فِي الأُسْبُوع الأَخِير مِنَ الشَّهْر. وَأَكَّدَ أَنَّ المَوْضُوع الرَّئِيسِيّ سَيَكُون الفَصْل الثَّالِث.He said that the final exam will be in the last week of the month. And he confirmed that the main topic will be Chapter Three.
هِنْد: هَل ذَكَر أَيّ تَفَاصِيل عَن شَكْل الاِمْتِحَان؟Did he mention any details about the exam's format?
عُمَر: نَعَم، أَشَارَ إِلَى أَنَّ نِصْف الأَسْئِلَة سَتَكُون مَقَالِيَّة. وَبِحَسَبِ مَا قَالَ، لَنْ يَسْمَح بِاسْتِخْدَام الكِتَاب أَثْنَاء الاِمْتِحَان.Yes, he indicated that half the questions will be essay-style. And according to what he said, he will not allow use of the book during the exam.
هِنْد: شُكْرًا جَزِيلًا. هَل تَعْتَقِد أَنَّ المَوْضُوع صَعْب؟Thank you very much. Do you think the topic is difficult?
عُمَر: أَعْتَقِد أَنَّهُ لَيْس صَعْبًا جِدًّا، لَكِنَّ الأُسْتَاذ أَوْضَحَ أَنَّ التَّحْضِير المُبَكِّر ضَرُورِيّ لِكَي نَنْجَح.I think it isn't very difficult, but the professor made clear that early preparation is necessary in order for us to succeed.

Writing Workshop

In this workshop, you will write a short, accurate summary of a real or invented account, clearly attributing information to its source.

  1. Step 1 — Choose an account to summarize. Pick a real or invented announcement, article, conversation, or report.
  2. Step 2 — State the main point. Use a reporting verb such as قَالَ، ذَكَرَ، or أَكَّدَ with أَنَّ to state the central claim.
  3. Step 3 — Attribute the information. Use حَسَبَ، وَفْقًا لِـ، or بِحَسَبِ to name the source clearly.
  4. Step 4 — Add one supporting detail. Use أَشَارَ إِلَى أَنَّ or أَوْضَحَ أَنَّ to add one relevant detail, keeping the summary shorter than the original.
  5. Step 5 — Add your own brief reaction. Use أَعْتَقِد أَنَّ to add one sentence of your own opinion, clearly separated from the reported information.
  6. Review your draft against the editing checklist below.

قَائِمَة المُرَاجَعَة — Editing checklist:

  • My summary is clearly shorter than the original account it is based on.
  • I used at least one reporting verb followed by a content clause with أَنَّ.
  • I attributed the information to a source using حَسَبَ، وَفْقًا لِـ، or بِحَسَبِ.
  • My own opinion, if included, is clearly separated from the reported information.
  • My summary includes at least one connector recycled from Chapters 1-3.
  • My vocabulary, gender agreement, and plural forms are checked for accuracy.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Attributing information carefully

In many Arabic-speaking media, academic, and professional settings, clearly naming a source before or alongside a claim — using expressions like وَفْقًا لِـ or بِحَسَبِ — is treated as a mark of careful, credible communication, though exact conventions vary by publication, institution, and genre. The reporting language practiced in this chapter supports that kind of careful attribution without representing a single fixed convention for every context.

Regional Variation — Everyday reporting expressions

In everyday spoken narration, which may shift toward regional spoken Arabic, some speakers use shorter or different everyday equivalents for "he said that" or "according to." The MSA reporting patterns introduced in this chapter remain the productive target for this course and are also widely used and understood in formal spoken MSA, written correspondence, and media across the Arabic-speaking world.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Confusing أَنَّ with أَنْ

Error: قَالَ أَنْ الشَّرِكَة سَتَفْتَح فَرْعًا جَدِيدًا using the subjunctive particle أَنْ where a reported statement requires أَنَّ.

Correction: a reporting verb followed by a statement of fact requires أَنَّ, not أَنْ: قَالَ إِنَّ الشَّرِكَة سَتَفْتَح فَرْعًا جَدِيدًا. Reserve أَنْ + subjunctive verb for purpose and intention, as in Chapter 3.

Common Mistake — Summarizing without attributing the source

Error: stating اِرْتَفَع عَدَد الزُّوَّار هَذَا العَام as if it were the writer's own established fact, without naming where the claim comes from.

Correction: attribute the claim clearly to its source: ذَكَرَت الصَّحِيفَة أَنَّ عَدَد الزُّوَّار اِرْتَفَع هَذَا العَام. A summary should let the reader know whose claim is being reported.

Common Mistake — Copying the original instead of shortening it

Error: reproducing an entire passage nearly word for word and calling it a summary.

Correction: a summary should be noticeably shorter than the original and expressed in the summarizer's own words, keeping only the main claim, its source, and one or two supporting details, as modeled in the Writing Workshop.

  4. أَنَّهُم in the passage refers back to كَثِير مِنَ المُشَارِكِين (many of the participants).

Section A.1: قَالَ إِنَّ الاِمْتِحَان النِّهَائِيّ سَيَكُون فِي الأُسْبُوع الأَخِير مِنَ الشَّهْر reports what the professor said about the exam date; other correct examples from the dialogue are acceptable.

Section A.2: أَنَّ introduces a reported statement of fact after a verb like قَالَ or ذَكَرَ, while أَنْ introduces a subjunctive purpose or intention clause after a verb or particle like لِكَي or أَرَادَ؛ any accurate example pair is acceptable.

Section B: Open-ended self-assessment; any honest, specific response that names concrete choices made while summarizing is acceptable.

Other well-formed, accurate answers combining the required systems are equally acceptable throughout this section.

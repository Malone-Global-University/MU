Bringing together reading, writing, listening, and speaking from all four units · Speak and Write Arabic: Independent Communication and Advanced Expression

Chapter Overview

Chapter 20 is the Volume III final assessment. It introduces no new grammar, morphology, script, case, mood, or vocabulary system. Instead, it draws together the narration, causal explanation, summary and paraphrase, public information, opinion and argument, data commentary, mediation, research presentation, and revision and editing you have developed across Units I-IV into a single integrated portfolio. You will assemble, present, and reflect on four linked pieces of work — one from each unit — using the two-pass revision method from Chapter 19 on at least one of them, and you will complete a capstone spoken presentation that ties the pieces together for a stated audience and purpose.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Select and organize four pieces of writing, one representing each unit of this volume, into a coherent portfolio.
  2. Apply the Chapter 19 two-pass revision method to at least one additional portfolio piece.
  3. Write a short reflective introduction that frames the portfolio for a stated audience and purpose.
  4. Deliver an organized spoken presentation that synthesizes narration, explanation, opinion, and evidence from across the volume.
  5. Respond to follow-up questions about the portfolio in connected, level-appropriate MSA.
  6. Self-assess overall B2 progress against the volume-level outcomes stated in the scope.
  7. Identify which systems are now consolidated and which remain areas for continued practice in Volume IV.

  3. You will present the portfolio aloud and answer questions about it.

النُّطْق — Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus: Delivering a Prepared Presentation

Pronunciation Note — Sustaining formal delivery across a longer presentation

A capstone presentation is longer than most earlier speaking tasks in this volume, so it is worth planning phrase grouping and pausing in advance rather than only at the sentence level. Mark natural breath points in your notes, keep pause forms consistent at the end of clauses, and maintain the same careful pace from your opening line to your closing line. This chapter recycles every pronunciation target already active in this unit; no new sound, symbol, or reading behavior is introduced here.

التَّرْكِيب — Portfolio Components and Synthesis

Portfolio Components and Synthesis: Bringing Four Units Together

This chapter introduces no new verb form, case rule, clause type, or vocabulary domain. Its task is synthesis: selecting, connecting, and presenting four pieces of work using systems already authorized across Chapters 1-19.

Component 1 — Unit I: an account and an analytical summary
قِصَّة شَخْصِيَّة وَتَحْلِيل مُوجَز لِسَبَبٍ وَنَتِيجَة
Draw on the narrative and cause-result work from Chapters 1-5: an extended personal or observed account, followed by a short analytical note explaining a cause and its result.
اِخْتَرْت هَذِه التَّجْرِبَة لِأَنَّهَا غَيَّرَت طَرِيقَة تَفْكِيرِي فِي المَوْضُوع.
I chose this experience because it changed the way I think about the topic.
Component 2 — Unit II: a public information response
مَلَفّ عَامّ وَرَدّ رَسْمِيّ
Draw on the media, institutional, and passive/impersonal work from Chapters 6-10: a short piece interpreting or responding to public information in formal correspondence style.
هَذَا الرَّدّ يُوَضِّح كَيْف تُعَامَل هَذِه القَضِيَّة مِن قِبَل المُؤَسَّسَة المَعْنِيَّة.
This response clarifies how this issue is handled by the relevant institution.
Component 3 — Unit III: a supported argument with data
حُجَّة مَدْعُومَة بِبَيَانَات وَأَدِلَّة
Draw on the opinion, comparison, and data-commentary work from Chapters 11-15: a position statement supported by at least one piece of numerical or comparative evidence.
تُظْهِر البَيَانَات أَنَّ العَدَد ازْدَاد بِنِسْبَة كَبِيرَة خِلَال السَّنَتَيْن الأَخِيرَتَيْن.
The data shows that the number increased by a large percentage over the last two years.
Component 4 — Unit IV: a mediated, revised presentation piece
وَسَاطَة وَعَرْض مُرَاجَع وَمُحَرَّر
Draw on the cultural commentary, mediation, research and presentation, and revision-and-editing work from Chapters 16-19: choose one of those pieces, apply the Chapter 19 two-pass method to it, and use it as the fourth portfolio component.
بَعْد المُرَاجَعَة وَالتَّحْرِير، أَصْبَح النَّصّ أَكْثَر تَمَاسُكًا وَدِقَّة.
After revision and editing, the text became more cohesive and accurate.

Recycled structures used in this chapter: narration and cause/result (Ch. 1-5), passive and institutional register (Ch. 6-10), argument and data commentary (Ch. 11-15), mediation and the two-pass revision method (Ch. 16-19), and case/mood awareness recycled since Chapter 4 may all appear inside the portfolio task; none is retaught here.

Writing Workshop — Assembling the Integrated B2 Portfolio

In this workshop, you will assemble your final portfolio: four components, one from each unit of this volume, a short reflective introduction, and at least one revised piece.

  1. Step 1 — Select four components. Choose one piece of writing representing each unit: Unit I (narrative and analytical summary), Unit II (public information response), Unit III (supported argument with data), and Unit IV (mediation, presentation, or revised text).
  2. Step 2 — Revise one component. Apply the Chapter 19 two-pass method — cohesion and style first, then accuracy and editing — to at least one component you have not already revised.
  3. Step 3 — Write a reflective introduction. In 4-6 sentences, explain why you chose each piece and what it shows about your development this volume.
  4. Step 4 — Organize the portfolio. Arrange the four components in a clear order with short connecting sentences between them.
  5. Step 5 — Prepare your spoken presentation. Draft speaking notes that introduce, connect, and briefly discuss all four components.
  6. Step 6 — Read the whole portfolio aloud. Confirm it sounds connected, accurate, and consistent in register from beginning to end.
  7. Review your portfolio against the checklist below.

قَائِمَة المُرَاجَعَة — Portfolio checklist:

  • I included one component from each of the four units.
  • I revised at least one component using the two-pass method from Chapter 19.
  • I wrote a reflective introduction explaining my choices.
  • I checked register consistency and case- and mood-sensitive spots across the whole portfolio.
  • I prepared speaking notes that connect all four components.
  • I did not introduce any system beyond what this volume has authorized.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Portfolios and closing presentations

Closing presentations that combine a body of written work with a spoken summary and a question-and-answer period are common in many academic and professional settings across Arabic-speaking regions, particularly at the end of a course, workshop, or exchange program. Being ready to explain choices and respond to follow-up questions is often considered as important as the work itself.

Regional Variation — Format expectations for closing sessions

The expected length, formality, and structure of a closing presentation vary by institution, program, and country; some settings favor a brief formal address, others a longer interactive discussion. Asking the organizers directly about format expectations is a reasonable and common practice.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Presenting four unconnected pieces

Error: reading four portfolio components back to back with no transition or explanation of how they relate to one another.

Correction: write and rehearse short connecting sentences between components, and open with a brief reflective framing that ties them together.

Common Mistake — Skipping the revision step

Error: submitting all four components exactly as first drafted, without applying the two-pass revision method to any of them.

Correction: apply cohesion-and-style review, then accuracy-and-editing review, to at least one component before finalizing the portfolio.

Common Mistake — Treating the reflection as a formality

Error: writing a vague, generic reflection that does not explain specific choices or specific development.

Correction: name specific systems, tasks, or moments from the volume that the chosen components actually demonstrate.

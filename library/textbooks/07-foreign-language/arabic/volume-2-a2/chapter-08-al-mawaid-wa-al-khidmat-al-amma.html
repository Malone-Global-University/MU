8

المَوَاعِيد وَالخِدْمَات العَامَّةAppointments and Public Services

Purpose clauses with the subjunctive, negation and prohibition with the jussive, procedures, and institutional language · Speak and Write Arabic: Everyday Independence and Connected Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Make, confirm, and change appointments using appropriate procedural vocabulary.
  2. Form purpose clauses with لِـ / كَي / حَتَّى followed by a subjunctive verb.
  3. Use لَمْ with the jussive to negate past events, and لَا with the jussive to express a prohibition or negative instruction.
  4. Recognize the nominative, accusative, and genitive case endings in short, clearly marked phrases, without being required to produce them spontaneously in free speech.
  5. Read and listen to short exchanges about appointments, forms, and office procedures, and answer questions about times, requirements, and instructions.
  6. Write a short, organized message requesting or confirming an appointment, including at least one purpose clause and one negative instruction.

Communication Scenario

You need to schedule a doctor's appointment, follow the steps for renewing an official document at a government office, and understand a set of posted instructions telling visitors what they must not do. Public offices and appointment desks in Arabic-speaking contexts rely on a small set of very useful patterns: explaining why you are doing something (so that you can get an appointment, in order to submit a form), telling someone what did not happen (I did not receive a confirmation), and giving or reading short negative instructions (do not bring food into the waiting room).

  1. You will learn to request and confirm appointments using vocabulary for offices, forms, and procedures.
  2. You will learn purpose clauses with لِـ, كَي, and حَتَّى followed by a subjunctive verb.
  3. You will learn to use لَمْ + the jussive to say that something did not happen, and لَا + the jussive to give a negative instruction or prohibition.
  4. You will begin recognizing case endings on short, clearly marked words — the first step toward the fuller case system introduced later in the volume.

تَرْكِيب — New Grammar

Purpose and the Subjunctive

This section introduces the subjunctive as it appears in purpose clauses — explaining why you are doing something.

Building Block — Purpose clause: لِـ / كَي / حَتَّى + subjunctive
لِـ / كَي / حَتَّى + imperfect verb ending in -َ (subjunctive)

اِتَّصَلْت بِالمَكْتَب لِأَحْجِز مَوْعِدًا.

I called the office in order to book an appointment.

The subjunctive is a mood of the imperfect verb, marked most visibly by a final فَتْحَة on the verb rather than the ordinary indicative ending. لِـ, كَي, and حَتَّى all introduce a purpose and require the following verb to be in the subjunctive. لِـ is the most common and neutral of the three; كَي and حَتَّى are used similarly, with حَتَّى often carrying a slightly stronger sense of "so that" or "until."

  • قَدَّمْت الوَثَائِق لِأُجَدِّد جَوَازِي.I submitted the documents in order to renew my passport.لِـ + subjunctive
  • وَصَلْت مُبَكِّرًا كَي أَحْصُل عَلَى دَوْر جَيِّد فِي الطَّابُور.I arrived early so that I would get a good place in line.كَي + subjunctive
  • اِنْتَظَرْت حَتَّى يَنْتَهِي المُوَظَّف مِن المُكَالَمَة.I waited until the employee finished the call.حَتَّى + subjunctive

At this stage, focus on recognizing and producing the subjunctive after these three particles in short, clear purpose clauses; broader subjunctive environments are developed further in later chapters.

Negation and Prohibition with the Jussive

This section introduces the jussive mood in two of its most useful everyday functions: negating a past event with لَمْ, and giving a negative instruction or prohibition with لَا.

Building Block — لَمْ + jussive (negated past)
لَمْ + imperfect verb with jussive ending (often a shortened or sukūn-marked final)

لَمْ أَسْتَلِم تَأْكِيدًا لِلْمَوْعِد.

I did not receive a confirmation for the appointment.

لَمْ places the imperfect verb into the jussive mood and gives it a past-tense meaning, functioning as an alternative to the negated perfect you already know. The verb's ending shortens or drops in the jussive; for sound verbs, this often simply removes the final vowel.

Building Block — لَا + jussive (prohibition)
لَا + imperfect verb with jussive ending

لَا تَنْسَ إِحْضَار وَثِيقَة الهُوِيَّة.

Do not forget to bring your identity document.

  • لَمْ يَصِل المُوَظَّف بَعْد.The employee has not arrived yet.لَمْ + jussive, weak (assimilated) verb
  • لَا تَتَأَخَّر عَن مَوْعِدِك.Do not be late for your appointment.لَا + jussive prohibition
  • لَا تُقَدِّمُوا الاِسْتِمَارَة قَبْل التَّوْقِيع.Do not submit the form before signing it.لَا + jussive, plural prohibition

لَمْ and لَا with the jussive are introduced here as two distinct, clearly bounded patterns; broader jussive environments, such as conditional jussives, are reserved for later chapters.

A First Look at Case Awareness

This section introduces case endings as something to notice and recognize, not yet as something you must produce on your own.

Language Focus — Nominative, accusative, and genitive endings

Arabic nouns carry case endings that mark their grammatical role: the nominative (رَفْع) for subjects, the accusative (نَصْب) for direct objects and certain adverbial expressions, and the genitive (جَرّ) after prepositions and in construct phrases. In careful, formal reading, these appear as short vowel marks on the final letter of a word: -ُ for nominative, -َ for accusative, -ِ for genitive. In ordinary spoken MSA and in the pause forms you have used so far, these endings are usually dropped.

At this stage, your goal is to notice these endings when a text marks them clearly — as on a form, a sign, or a carefully vocalized sentence — and to understand what role they show. You are not expected to add case endings on your own in free conversation.

  • وَصَلَ المُوَظَّفُ.The employee arrived.nominative -ُ on the subject
  • قَدَّمْتُ الاِسْتِمَارَةَ.I submitted the form.accusative -َ on the direct object
  • اِنْتَظَرْت فِي المَكْتَبِ.I waited in the office.genitive -ِ after a preposition

Model Dialogue

Setting: نُور calls a government office to book an appointment for a document renewal and speaks with الأُسْتَاذ خَالِد.
السِّيَاق: تَتَّصِل نُور بِمَكْتَب حُكُومِيّ لِحَجْز مَوْعِد لِتَجْدِيد وَثِيقَة، وَتَتَحَدَّث مَع الأُسْتَاذ خَالِد.

NOOR صَبَاح الخَيْر. أَوَدُّ أَن أَحْجِز مَوْعِدًا لِتَجْدِيد جَوَازِي. Good morning. I would like to book an appointment to renew my passport.
KHALID أَهْلًا بِك. هَل قَدَّمْتِ الاِسْتِمَارَة عَبْر المَوْقِع الإِلِكْتُرُونِيّ أَوَّلًا؟ Welcome. Have you already submitted the form through the website first?
NOOR لَا، لَمْ أُقَدِّم الاِسْتِمَارَة بَعْد. مَاذَا يَجِب أَن أَفْعَل؟ No, I have not submitted the form yet. What must I do?
KHALID اِفْتَحِي المَوْقِع أَوَّلًا لِتُقَدِّمِي الاِسْتِمَارَة، ثُمَّ اِتَّصِلِي بِنَا لِنُحَدِّد مَوْعِدًا. لَا تَنْسَيْ إِحْضَار وَثِيقَة الهُوِيَّة. Open the website first so that you can submit the form, then call us so that we can set an appointment. Do not forget to bring your identity document.
NOOR حَسَنًا. هَل مِن المُمْكِن أَن أَحْصُل عَلَى مَوْعِد هَذَا الأُسْبُوع؟ All right. Would it be possible for me to get an appointment this week?
KHALID إِذَا قَدَّمْتِ الاِسْتِمَارَة اليَوْم، سَتَحْصُلِين عَلَى مَوْعِد يَوْم الخَمِيس. If you submit the form today, you will get an appointment on Thursday.

أَسْئِلَة الفَهْم — Comprehension questions:

  1. What must Noor do before an appointment can be set?
  2. What purpose clause does Khalid use to explain why Noor should open the website?
  3. What negative instruction does Khalid give at the end of his second turn?

Speaking Station

تَدْرِيب مُوَجَّه — Controlled practice: Purpose and prohibition

With a partner, take turns completing the sentence اِتَّصَلْت بِالمَكْتَب لِـ ___ with a different purpose each time, then giving a partner a negative instruction with لَا + jussive appropriate to an office setting.

مُهِمَّة تَوَاصُل — Communicative task: Booking an appointment

In pairs, role-play a phone call to book an appointment. One partner plays the caller, explaining a purpose with لِـ / كَي / حَتَّى and asking about a document; the other partner plays the clerk, giving at least one instruction with لَا + jussive and one condition with إِذَا, as in Chapter 7.

لِلْمُتَعَلِّمِينَ المُسْتَقِلِّينَ — Solo alternative

Record yourself describing a real or imagined visit to an office: state your purpose for going with لِـ, mention something that did not happen yet with لَمْ, and give yourself one reminder instruction with لَا.

Reading Station

An Office Notice — Sample Text

أَسْئِلَة الفَهْم — Comprehension questions:

  1. After what time is the office no longer open to walk-in visitors?
  2. What negative instruction is given about incomplete documents, and what result does ignoring it lead to?
  3. What condition and instruction are given for visitors with questions?

Writing Station

In this station, you will draft a short message of six to eight sentences requesting or confirming an appointment.

  1. Open naturally. One sentence introducing the appointment or procedure you need.
  2. State your purpose. One sentence using لِـ, كَي, or حَتَّى with a subjunctive verb.
  3. Mention what has not happened yet. One sentence using لَمْ + jussive.
  4. Add a condition, if useful. One optional sentence using إِذَا, as in Chapter 7.
  5. Give or acknowledge an instruction. One sentence using لَا + jussive.
  6. Close and revise. One closing sentence, then check your draft against the editing checklist below.

قَائِمَة المُرَاجَعَة — Editing checklist:

  • I used at least one purpose clause with لِـ, كَي, or حَتَّى followed by a correct subjunctive verb.
  • I used لَمْ with a correctly formed jussive verb to describe something that has not happened.
  • I used لَا with a correctly formed jussive verb for an instruction or prohibition.
  • I did not attempt to add case endings on my own beyond what I can currently recognize.
  • My message reads as one connected account of an appointment rather than a list of unrelated facts.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Appointment culture across settings

Expectations about punctuality, queuing, and appointment scheduling vary across offices, countries, and institutions in the Arabic-speaking world. Some government offices require advance online booking, while others operate on a first-come, first-served basis with a physical queue. Reading posted instructions and asking a clerk directly, using the polite request patterns you have learned, is generally more reliable than assuming one system applies everywhere.

Regional Variation — Names for government offices

The generic term مَكْتَب (office) is used throughout this chapter, but specific institutions carry their own official names that vary by country — for example, terms for a civil registry, a passport office, or a municipal service center. Learners preparing for a specific country should confirm the official name of the relevant institution rather than assuming a single term applies across all Arabic-speaking countries.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Using the indicative after لِـ, كَي, or حَتَّى

Error: اِتَّصَلْت لِأَحْجِزُ مَوْعِدًا (leaving the indicative ending on the verb after a purpose particle)

Correction: اِتَّصَلْت لِأَحْجِزَ مَوْعِدًا — لِـ, كَي, and حَتَّى require the following verb to take the subjunctive ending.

Common Mistake — Using لَا instead of لَمْ for a negated past event

Error: لَا وَصَلْت بَعْد (using لَا, which is for prohibitions or general present negation, to negate a completed past event)

Correction: لَمْ أَصِل بَعْد — use لَمْ with the jussive to say that something has not happened; reserve لَا with the jussive for instructions and prohibitions.

Common Mistake — Adding case endings inconsistently in free speech

Error: attempting to add nominative, accusative, or genitive endings unpredictably in ordinary conversation, producing an inconsistent mix of marked and unmarked forms.

Correction: at this stage, recognize case endings when a text marks them clearly, but continue using natural pause forms in your own spoken and written production, as you have throughout the volume so far.

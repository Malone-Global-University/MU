العَادَات وَالتَّغْيِيرHabits and Change

Expanding the imperfect tense, adding frequency expressions, comparing routines, and describing change over time · Speak and Write Arabic: Everyday Independence and Connected Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Describe your own and other people's daily and weekly habits using a wider range of imperfect-tense verbs, including with the people and relationships introduced in Chapter 2.
  2. Say how often something happens using a set of common frequency expressions, from "always" to "never."
  3. Compare two routines or habits, saying that one thing happens more, less, or as often as another.
  4. Describe a change in a habit using expressions such as "I used to... but now I..." and "recently I have started to...".
  5. Recycle plurals, the dual, and construct chains from Chapter 2 while talking about the habits of neighbors, colleagues, and relatives.
  6. Complete a short spoken and written task comparing your habits now with your habits in the past.

Communication Scenario

Your neighbor لَيْلَى, whom you introduced yourself to in Chapter 2, asks you about your weekly routine and mentions that her own habits have changed since she moved into the building. This is a natural moment to talk about how often you do things, to compare your routine with someone else's, and to explain how a habit has changed — not just what you do once, but what you do regularly and how that has shifted over time.

  1. You already know how to name people in your community and describe simple, single actions in the imperfect.
  2. This chapter shows you how to talk about regular habits with frequency words, compare routines, and describe how a habit has changed from the past to now.

التَّرْكِيز النَّحْوِيّ — Grammar Focus

Expanding the Imperfect

You already use the imperfect for single actions happening now. This chapter expands that system so the imperfect can also describe habitual, repeated action — something a person does regularly, not just once.

Building Block — Imperfect for habitual action
subject + imperfect verb (+ frequency word)

جَارِي يَعْمَل فِي مَحَلِّه كُلَّ يَوْم، وَأَنَا أَذْهَب إِلَى العَمَل غَالِبًا فِي الصَّبَاح.

My neighbor works at his shop every day, and I usually go to work in the morning.

The same imperfect forms you already know — يَعْمَل، أَذْهَب، تَعْمَل، and so on — describe habitual action once a time or frequency word makes that meaning clear. No new verb forms are required; what changes is how the imperfect is used, not how it is built.

  • زَمِيلَتِي تَشْرَب القَهْوَة كُلَّ صَبَاح قَبْل العَمَل.My colleague drinks coffee every morning before work.imperfect + كُلَّ + time noun
  • أَقْرِبَاؤُنَا يَزُورُونَنَا أَحْيَانًا فِي عُطْلَة الأُسْبُوع.Our relatives visit us sometimes on the weekend.human plural + imperfect + frequency adverb
Grammar Note — One tense, two meanings

The imperfect can describe an action happening right now (أَنَا أَقْرَأ الآن, "I am reading now") or a habitual action (أَنَا أَقْرَأ كُلَّ لَيْلَة, "I read every night"). Context and time words, not the verb form itself, tell the listener which meaning is intended.

Frequency Expressions

Frequency words let you say exactly how often a habit occurs, from "always" to "never." This chapter introduces a controlled set of these words as a new productive system.

Building Block — Placing a frequency word
subject + imperfect verb + frequency adverb (usually at the end of the clause)

مُدِير المَحَلّ يَفْتَح البَاب مُبَكِّرًا دَائِمًا، لَكِن هُوَ نَادِرًا مَا يُغْلِقُه بَاكِرًا.

The shop manager always opens the door early, but he rarely closes it early.

Most frequency adverbs, such as دَائِمًا، غَالِبًا، and أَحْيَانًا، follow the verb and its object. نَادِرًا مَا is a common fixed pairing meaning "rarely." For "never," use a negative particle with the imperfect and add أَبَدًا for emphasis, as shown below.

  • لَا أَشْرَب القَهْوَة فِي المَسَاء أَبَدًا.I never drink coffee in the evening.لَا + imperfect + أَبَدًا
  • صَاحِبَة المَحَلّ تُغَيِّر السَّاعَات مِن وَقْت لِآخَر.The shop owner changes the hours from time to time.fixed frequency phrase
Grammar Note — Frequency word order is flexible

Frequency adverbs may also appear at the start of a sentence for emphasis, as in غَالِبًا، أَنَا أَذْهَب إِلَى العَمَل مَشْيًا. ("Usually, I go to work on foot.") Either position is correct; sentence-final placement is the more neutral default taught in this chapter.

Comparing Routines

Once you can describe how often something happens, the natural next step is comparing one routine to another — a skill you will recycle when discussing your neighbors' and colleagues' habits from Chapter 2.

Building Block — Comparing frequency
subject + imperfect verb + أَكْثَر / أَقَلّ + مِن + noun or pronoun

أَنَا أَخْرُج مَع أَصْحَابِي أَكْثَر مِن أُخْتِي، لَكِنَّهَا تَقْرَأ أَكْثَر مِنِّي.

I go out with my friends more than my sister, but she reads more than I do.

مِثْل is used to compare routines that are similar rather than different: أَنَا أَعْمَل مِثْل زَمِيلِي, "I work like my colleague [does]." Together, أَكْثَر مِن، أَقَلّ مِن، and مِثْل let you build a full comparison of two people's habits without needing new grammar beyond vocabulary you can already control.

  • جَارِي يَطْبُخ فِي البَيْت أَكْثَر مِن صَاحِبَة المَحَلّ.My neighbor cooks at home more than the shop owner does.imperfect + أَكْثَر مِن + construct chain
  • زَمِيلَتَاي تَعْمَلَان مِثْل بَعْضِهِمَا تَمَامًا.My two colleagues work exactly like each other.dual subject + dual verb + مِثْل
Grammar Note — Comparing with a pronoun

When comparing to "me," مِن attaches a pronoun suffix: أَكْثَر مِنِّي ("more than me"), not a separate word for "me." This is the same pronoun-suffix pattern you practiced with prepositions in earlier chapters.

Expressing Change

The final tool in this chapter lets you describe how a habit has changed — what someone used to do, and what they do now instead. This connects the imperfect you already use to the perfect tense you began learning in Chapter 1.

Building Block — "Used to... but now..."
كُنْت / كَانَ + imperfect verb (past habitual) + لَكِن الآن + imperfect verb (present habitual)

كُنْت أَسْكُن بَعِيدًا عَن العَمَل، لَكِن الآن أَسْكُن قَرِيبًا مِنْه.

I used to live far from work, but now I live close to it.

كُنْت أَسْكُن literally combines "I was" with the imperfect verb "I live," a pattern that expresses a past habitual action — something you regularly did, not a single completed event. لَكِن الآن introduces the contrasting current habit in the ordinary imperfect. A second useful pattern uses يَبْدَأ or يَتْرُك to mark the start or end of a habit directly.

  • بَدَأْت أَذْهَب إِلَى النَّادِي مَرَّتَيْن فِي الأُسْبُوع مُنْذُ الشَّهْر المَاضِي.I have started going to the gym twice a week since last month.perfect بَدَأْت + imperfect verb
  • جَارَتِي تَرَكَت العَادَة القَدِيمَة وَبَدَأَت تَطْبُخ فِي البَيْت.My neighbor gave up the old habit and started cooking at home.perfect + perfect + imperfect
Grammar Note — Keeping the change pattern controlled

This chapter authorizes كُنْت/كَانَ + imperfect for past habitual meaning and بَدَأَ/تَرَكَ + imperfect for the start or end of a habit. Other uses of كَانَ, and full control of weak-verb behavior in these patterns, are developed further in later chapters — for now, focus on using these two patterns accurately in the contexts practiced here.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Layla and Huda talk about their weekly routines and how Layla's habits have changed since she moved.
السِّيَاق: تَتَحَدَّث لَيْلَى وَهُدَى عَن رُوتِينِهِمَا الأُسْبُوعِيّ وَعَن تَغَيُّر عَادَات لَيْلَى.

HUDA مَاذَا تَفْعَلِين عَادَةً فِي عُطْلَة الأُسْبُوع؟ What do you usually do on the weekend?
LAYLA غَالِبًا أَقْرَأ فِي الصَّبَاح، وَأَحْيَانًا أَخْرُج مَع صَدِيقَتِي. لَكِن كُنْت أَخْرُج أَكْثَر مِن الآن. Usually I read in the morning, and sometimes I go out with my friend. But I used to go out more than now.
HUDA لِمَاذَا تَغَيَّرَت عَادَاتُك؟ Why have your habits changed?
LAYLA بَدَأْت أَعْمَل مِن البَيْت، فَأَصْبَح وَقْتِي أَقَلّ. زَمِيلَتِي تَعْمَل مِثْلِي مِن البَيْت، لَكِنَّهَا تَخْرُج أَكْثَر مِنِّي. I started working from home, so my time became less. My colleague works like me from home, but she goes out more than I do.
HUDA وَهَل تَرَكْتِ رِيَاضَتَك القَدِيمَة؟ And did you give up your old exercise routine?
LAYLA لَا، أَبَدًا! أَنَا أَذْهَب إِلَى النَّادِي كُلَّ يَوْم تَقْرِيبًا، لَكِنِّي نَادِرًا مَا أَذْهَب فِي المَسَاء الآن. No, never! I go to the gym almost every day, but I rarely go in the evening now.
HUDA عَادَاتُك تَتَغَيَّر مِثْل عَادَات كَثِير مِن النَّاس عِنْدَمَا يَبْدَأُون العَمَل مِن البَيْت. Your habits are changing like the habits of many people when they start working from home.

أَسْئِلَة الفَهْم — Comprehension questions:

  1. Find two frequency words Layla uses to describe her weekend routine. What do they mean?
  2. What "used to... but now..." sentence does Layla use, and what habit does it describe?
  3. How does Layla compare herself to her colleague? Which of them goes out more?

Writing Station

In this station, you will draft a short written comparison of six to eight sentences about your routine now and in the past, connecting your ideas rather than listing separate facts.

  1. Open with a general statement about your routine. One sentence describing a habit you have now, using the imperfect and a frequency word.
  2. Add a second habit with a different frequency word. One sentence describing something you do rarely or sometimes.
  3. Describe a change using "used to... but now...". One sentence contrasting a past habit with your current one.
  4. Mark the start or end of a habit. One sentence using بَدَأْت or تَرَكْت to describe when a habit began or ended.
  5. Compare your routine to someone else's. One sentence comparing your habits to a neighbor, colleague, or relative from Chapter 2, using أَكْثَر مِن, أَقَلّ مِن, or مِثْل.
  6. Close with a general reflection. One sentence about how you feel about the change, recycling a connector from an earlier chapter.
  7. Revise. Check your draft against the editing checklist below.

قَائِمَة المُرَاجَعَة — Editing checklist:

  • I used at least two different frequency expressions correctly.
  • I used a "used to... but now..." sentence with كُنْت/كَانَ + imperfect.

  • I compared my routine to someone else's using أَكْثَر مِن, أَقَلّ مِن, or مِثْل.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Talking about daily routines

Discussing daily routines, working hours, and how they have changed is a common and natural conversation topic in many Arabic-speaking communities, often shared casually among neighbors, colleagues, and family. The pace and structure of daily life vary widely by country, city, and profession, so treat the routines described in this chapter as illustrative examples rather than a single standard schedule.

Regional Variation — Weekend days

The expression عُطْلَة الأُسْبُوع ("the weekend") is understood across MSA contexts, but the actual weekend days differ by country: some countries observe Friday and Saturday as the weekend, while others observe Saturday and Sunday. When precision matters, a speaker will usually name the specific days rather than relying on "the weekend" alone.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Using the perfect for ongoing habits

Error: أَنَا شَرِبْت القَهْوَة كُلَّ صَبَاح. (using the perfect for a repeated, ongoing habit)

Correction: أَنَا أَشْرَب القَهْوَة كُلَّ صَبَاح. — an ongoing habit takes the imperfect, not the perfect.

Common Mistake — Forgetting the negative particle with أَبَدًا

Error: أَنَا أَخْرُج فِي المَسَاء أَبَدًا. (using أَبَدًا without negation)

Correction: أَنَا لَا أَخْرُج فِي المَسَاء أَبَدًا. — أَبَدًا for "never" requires لَا or another negative particle with the imperfect.

Common Mistake — Dropping مِن after أَكْثَر or أَقَلّ

Error: هِيَ تَعْمَل أَكْثَر أَنَا. (missing مِن before the compared item)

Correction: هِيَ تَعْمَل أَكْثَر مِنِّي. — أَكْثَر and أَقَلّ always require مِن (with a pronoun suffix when comparing to a person) before the second item.

Looking Ahead

مَا الَّذِي تَسْتَطِيعُ فِعْلَهُ الآن — What you can do now

You can now describe habitual actions with the imperfect, say how often something happens, compare your routine to someone else's, and describe how a habit has changed over time. These tools let you talk about your daily life as an ongoing story rather than a series of isolated facts.

العَمَل وَالمُرَاسَلَاتWork and Correspondence

Formal email, workplace responsibilities, relative clauses, and an introduction to nominal style · Speak and Write Arabic: Everyday Independence and Connected Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Describe job responsibilities, workplace roles, and colleagues using connected MSA.
  2. Write and read a formal email with an appropriate opening, body, and closing.
  3. Use relative clauses in longer, more independent descriptions of people, tasks, and responsibilities.
  4. Recognize an introductory nominal style, in which a verbal noun rather than a conjugated verb serves as the subject of a formal sentence.
  5. Continue using Form II, Form V, and Form X derived verbs, recycled from Chapter 11, in a workplace context.
  6. Write a short formal email describing a work responsibility and requesting information or assistance.

Communication Scenario

Chapter 12 moves from personal learning to the workplace. Learners often need to describe what they do at work, ask a colleague or supervisor for information, and put a request in writing. Arabic formal email follows recognizable conventions of address, structure, and closing, and correspondence of this kind is a normal part of institutional and professional life. This chapter also introduces a more formal, nominal way of opening a sentence — beginning with a verbal noun rather than a verb — which becomes increasingly common in written and institutional Arabic from this point forward.

  1. You will learn vocabulary for jobs, responsibilities, and workplace communication.
  2. You will expand relative clauses to describe colleagues, tasks, and responsibilities in longer sentences.
  3. You will learn the structure of a formal email in MSA.
  4. You will recognize an introductory nominal sentence style built on the verbal noun.

What This Chapter Builds On

Chapter 12 continues Unit III by building directly on the derived-verb system introduced in Chapter 11. It expands relative clauses into longer, more independent descriptions and introduces two closely related new systems — formal email conventions and an introductory nominal sentence style — in the context of workplace communication.

تَرْكِيب — Grammar in Context

Relative Clauses Expanded

You have used relative clauses with الَّذِي / الَّتِي since earlier chapters to describe people and things. This section expands that system into longer, more connected descriptions typical of workplace communication, rather than introducing a new rule.

Language Focus — Longer relative descriptions

A relative clause can now carry more of the sentence's information, especially when describing a colleague's role, a project's status, or a task's requirements. The resumptive pronoun inside the clause continues to agree with the noun it describes, exactly as in earlier chapters.

  • الزَّمِيلَة الَّتِي تُدِير المَشْرُوع الجَدِيد تَعْمَل فِي قِسْمِنَا مُنْذُ سَنَتَيْن.The colleague who is managing the new project has worked in our department for two years.definite relative clause + resumptive verb
  • المَشْرُوع الَّذِي نُطَوِّرُهُ لَهُ مَوْعِد نِهَائِيّ فِي نِهَايَة الشَّهْر.The project that we are developing has a deadline at the end of the month.Form II verb inside relative clause
  • هَذِهِ هِيَ المَسْؤُولِيَّات الَّتِي أَتَحَمَّلُهَا فِي هَذَا القِسْم.These are the responsibilities that I carry in this department.plural relative clause with resumptive object pronoun

Focus on connecting a relative clause smoothly to the sentence around it, so that a description of a colleague, project, or responsibility reads as one connected idea rather than two short sentences joined together.

An Introduction to Nominal Style

Formal written Arabic often opens a sentence with a verbal noun rather than a conjugated verb. You already know the verbal nouns of Form II, Form V, and Form X verbs from Chapter 11; this section introduces how they can begin a formal sentence.

Building Block — Verbal noun as sentence subject
اِسْم مَصْدَر فِي بِدَايَة الجُمْلَة — verbal noun in initial position

تَطْوِير المَشْرُوع يَحْتَاج إِلَى وَقْت وَتَعَاوُن.

Developing the project requires time and cooperation.

In everyday spoken MSA, a learner might say اليَوْم نُطَوِّر المَشْرُوع ("Today we are developing the project"), starting with a conjugated verb or a time word. In more formal written registers, the same idea is often expressed by placing the verbal noun تَطْوِير at the start of the sentence, where it functions as the grammatical subject. This is a hallmark of institutional, academic, and professional writing.

  • التَّعَاوُن بَيْن الأَقْسَام ضَرُورِيّ لِنَجَاح المَشْرُوع.Cooperation among departments is necessary for the project's success.verbal noun as subject, nominal sentence
  • اِسْتِخْدَام البَرِيد الإِلِكْتُرُونِيّ الرَّسْمِيّ مُهِمّ فِي التَّوَاصُل مَعَ الإِدَارَة.Using formal email is important in communicating with the administration.verbal noun as subject, recycled from Ch. 11

At this stage, focus on recognizing this pattern in reading and email models and on producing it with the verbal nouns you already control; a fuller system of nominal style is developed further in Volume III.

Formal Email Conventions

Language Focus — The shape of a formal email

A formal MSA email typically opens with a title and name or a formulaic greeting such as السَّيِّد / السَّيِّدَة المُحْتَرَم(َة), followed by a transitional opening formula such as تَحِيَّة طَيِّبَة وَبَعْد،. The body states the purpose clearly, often with أَكْتُب إِلَيْكُم بِخُصُوص... ("I am writing to you regarding..."). Requests are softened with أَرْجُو التَّكَرُّم بِـ... ("I kindly request that you..."). The email closes with a formal expression of respect such as وَتَفَضَّلُوا بِقَبُول فَائِق الاِحْتِرَام, followed by the sender's name.

  • أَكْتُب إِلَيْكُم بِخُصُوص المَوْعِد النِّهَائِيّ لِلمَشْرُوع.I am writing to you regarding the project deadline.purpose statement
  • أَرْجُو التَّكَرُّم بِإِرْسَال التَّفَاصِيل قَبْل يَوْم الخَمِيس.I kindly request that you send the details before Thursday.polite formal request

Model Email

From: سَارَة أَحْمَد  ·  To: السَّيِّد خَالِد مُرَاد، مُدِير قِسْم المَشَارِيع
Subject: اِسْتِفْسَار بِخُصُوص مَوْعِد المَشْرُوع النِّهَائِيّ

السَّيِّد خَالِد المُحْتَرَم،

تَحِيَّة طَيِّبَة وَبَعْد،

أَكْتُب إِلَيْكُم بِخُصُوص المَشْرُوع الَّذِي نُطَوِّرُهُ حَالِيًّا فِي قِسْم التَّسْوِيق. تَقَدُّم العَمَل جَيِّد حَتَّى الآن، لَكِنِّي أَحْتَاج إِلَى بَعْض المَعْلُومَات الإِضَافِيَّة كَي أُتِمّ المَهَمَّة فِي الوَقْت المُحَدَّد. هَل يُمْكِنُكُم إِرْسَال التَّقْرِير الَّذِي أَعَدَّهُ القِسْم الفَنِّيّ الشَّهْر المَاضِي؟

أَرْجُو التَّكَرُّم بِالرَّدّ قَبْل يَوْم الخَمِيس، لِأَنَّ المَوْعِد النِّهَائِيّ لِلمَشْرُوع يَقْتَرِب.

وَتَفَضَّلُوا بِقَبُول فَائِق الاِحْتِرَام،
سَارَة أَحْمَد

Dear Mr. Khalid, Greetings. I am writing to you regarding the project that we are currently developing in the marketing department. Progress is good so far, but I need some additional information in order to finish the task on time. Could you send the report that the technical department prepared last month? I kindly request a reply before Thursday, because the project deadline is approaching. Please accept my highest regards, Sarah Ahmad.

أَسْئِلَة الفَهْم — Comprehension questions:

  1. What is Sarah writing to Khalid about, and what does she request?
  2. Identify one relative clause in the email and the noun it describes.
  3. Identify one purpose clause, recycled from Chapter 8, in the email.

A Workplace Note — Sample Text

Writing Station

In this station, you will draft a short formal email of six to eight sentences describing a work responsibility and requesting information or assistance.

  1. Open formally. Begin with السَّيِّد / السَّيِّدَة المُحْتَرَم(َة) and تَحِيَّة طَيِّبَة وَبَعْد،.
  2. State your purpose. One sentence using أَكْتُب إِلَيْكُم بِخُصُوص... to introduce the topic.
  3. Describe the situation. One or two sentences describing a project, task, or colleague, using at least one relative clause.
  4. Make your request. One sentence using أَرْجُو التَّكَرُّم بِـ... to ask for information or help.
  5. Add a formal observation. One sentence beginning with a verbal noun as the subject (for example, تَطْوِير... or اِسْتِخْدَام...).
  6. Close formally. End with وَتَفَضَّلُوا بِقَبُول فَائِق الاِحْتِرَام and your name, then check your draft against the editing checklist below.

قَائِمَة المُرَاجَعَة — Editing checklist:

  • I used the formal opening and closing formulas correctly.
  • I used at least one relative clause to describe a person, project, or task.
  • I used at least one sentence with a verbal noun as the subject.
  • I used at least one Form II, Form V, or Form X verb, recycled from Chapter 11.
  • I did not attempt to add case endings on my own beyond the fixed accusative expressions.
  • My email reads as one connected, polite message rather than a list of unrelated sentences.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Formality in workplace correspondence

Formal written correspondence across many Arabic-speaking workplaces and institutions tends to favor clear titles, respectful address, and set opening and closing formulas, even between colleagues who speak informally in person. Learning these written conventions is separate from, and complements, the spoken register used in daily workplace conversation.

Regional Variation — Titles and address

السَّيِّد and السَّيِّدَة are widely understood formal titles in MSA correspondence, but some institutions and countries also use أُسْتَاذ / أُسْتَاذَة as a respectful professional title regardless of whether the person is a teacher. Learners should expect to see both conventions in authentic correspondence.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Opening a formal email too informally

Error: أَهْلًا خَالِد، كَيْف الحَال؟ (an informal spoken greeting used to open a formal email)

Correction: السَّيِّد خَالِد المُحْتَرَم، تَحِيَّة طَيِّبَة وَبَعْد، — formal email correspondence requires the formulaic title and opening, even when the sender and recipient know each other well.

Common Mistake — Dropping the resumptive pronoun in a relative clause

Error: المَشْرُوع الَّذِي نُطَوِّر لَهُ مَوْعِد نِهَائِيّ. (missing the object pronoun referring back to المَشْرُوع)

Correction: المَشْرُوع الَّذِي نُطَوِّرُهُ لَهُ مَوْعِد نِهَائِيّ. — a definite relative clause in Arabic normally requires a resumptive pronoun referring back to the noun it describes.

Common Mistake — Starting a formal sentence with a verb instead of a verbal noun

Error: نَسْتَخْدِم البَرِيد الإِلِكْتُرُونِيّ الرَّسْمِيّ مُهِمّ. (mixing a conjugated verb with an adjective meant to describe a noun subject)

Correction: اِسْتِخْدَام البَرِيد الإِلِكْتُرُونِيّ الرَّسْمِيّ مُهِمّ. — the introductory nominal style requires the verbal noun, not a conjugated verb, in subject position.

العَلَاقَات وَالمُجْتَمَع الصَّغِيرRelationships and Local Community

Expanding plurals for people, introducing the dual, building construct chains for relationships, and describing people with simple relative clauses · Speak and Write Arabic: Everyday Independence and Connected Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Name and describe several people in your building, neighborhood, or social circle, using sound and broken plurals for common social roles.
  2. Refer correctly to exactly two people or things using the dual, distinguishing it from both the singular and the plural.
  3. Build short construct chains (iḍāfa) to describe relationships, such as "my neighbor's daughter" or "the manager of the small shop."
  4. Add a short descriptive detail to a person or place using الَّذِي or الَّتِي, recycling the pronoun-reference habits from Chapter 1.
  5. Use social-role vocabulary — neighbor, colleague, classmate, relative — with correct gender and plural agreement.
  6. Complete a short spoken and written task introducing three people in your local community as one connected description rather than a list of names.

Communication Scenario

A new neighbor, لَيْلَى, has just moved into your building, and you are showing her around. She wants to know who lives nearby, who she might see at the small shop downstairs, and how people in the building generally get along. This is a natural moment to introduce more than one person at a time, to talk about exactly two people when that is true, and to describe how people are connected to one another — not just who they are.

  1. You already know how to introduce one person at a time and describe your own family in simple sentences.
  2. This chapter shows you how to talk about groups of people, pairs of people, and the relationships that connect neighbors, colleagues, and relatives to one another and to you.

التَّرْكِيز النَّحْوِيّ — Grammar Focus

Expanded Plurals for People

In Volume I, you learned a small set of sound plurals. This chapter expands that system so you can talk naturally about the people around you — neighbors, colleagues, relatives — most of whom take broken plurals rather than simple added endings.

Building Block — Human plural agreement
human plural noun + human plural verb/adjective agreement

جِيرَانِي طَيِّبُون، وَهُم يُسَاعِدُونَ بَعْضهُم بَعْضًا دَائِمًا.

My neighbors are kind, and they always help each other.

Notice that جِيرَان (a broken plural) still triggers human plural agreement on the adjective طَيِّبُون and the verb يُسَاعِدُونَ. The plural pattern of the noun itself does not change how agreement works — human plurality is what matters for agreement, not whether the plural is "sound" or "broken."

  • عِنْدِي زُمَلَاء كَثِيرُون فِي العَمَل.I have many colleagues at work.broken plural + human plural agreement
  • أَقْرِبَاؤُنَا يَعِيشُون فِي مُدُن مُخْتَلِفَة.Our relatives live in different cities.broken plural + attached possessive pronoun
Grammar Note — Broken plurals are not random

Broken plurals follow recognizable patterns, but many individual plurals must simply be learned alongside their singular. جَار → جِيرَان and صَاحِب → أَصْحَاب are both common patterns for human nouns describing roles. You are not expected to derive these patterns yourself yet; you are expected to recognize and use the specific pairs taught in this chapter accurately.

Introducing the Dual

Arabic has a separate form, the dual, for exactly two people or things. This chapter introduces the dual as a new productive system, distinct from both the singular and the plural you already know.

Building Block — Forming the dual
singular noun + ـَان (masc./general) or ـَتَان (fem.)

لَدَيَّ جَارَان طَيِّبَان: أَحْمَد وَسَامِي.

I have two kind neighbors: Ahmad and Sami.

جَارَان is the dual of جَار, used because exactly two neighbors are meant — not "some neighbors" and not "many neighbors." The adjective طَيِّبَان also takes the dual ending to agree with the dual noun.

  • لِي زَمِيلَتَان فِي المَكْتَب.I have two (female) colleagues at the office.feminine dual noun
  • اِبْنَتَا جَارَتِي تَدْرُسَان فِي نَفْس المَدْرَسَة.My neighbor's two daughters study at the same school.dual noun in construct + dual verb agreement
Grammar Note — When to use the dual

Use the dual only when exactly two people or things are meant and the number two is central to what you are saying. For three or more, use the plural, as before. This chapter introduces the dual as a controlled productive system: expect to recognize and use it accurately in the contexts practiced here, and to develop it further with additional case behavior later in the volume.

Construct Chains for Relationships

You already know simple two-term construct phrases from Volume I, such as "my father's house." This chapter develops that system so you can describe how one person relates to another — a skill that is essential for talking about a community rather than isolated individuals.

Building Block — Extending the construct chain
noun 1 (unmarked, no article) + noun 2 (definite) [+ noun 3 (definite)]

هٰذِهِ اِبْنَة جَارِي، وَهِيَ صَدِيقَة أُخْتِي.

This is my neighbor's daughter, and she is my sister's friend.

اِبْنَة جَارِي literally strings together "daughter" and "my neighbor" without the article on the first term. صَدِيقَة أُخْتِي does the same for "friend" and "my sister." Each construct chain names a relationship in a single compact phrase rather than a full sentence.

  • مُدِير المَحَلّ الصَّغِير رَجُل لَطِيف جِدًّا.The manager of the small shop is a very kind man.construct chain + descriptive adjective on the final term
  • أَعْرِف اِبْن صَاحِبَة المَحَلّ.I know the son of the shop's (female) owner.three-term chain: son / shop / owner
Grammar Note — Keeping chains short

A construct chain of two terms is the most common and the easiest to control. Three-term chains, like اِبْن صَاحِبَة المَحَلّ, are useful but should be kept short and clear. Longer chains belong to later volumes; for now, if a relationship needs more than three terms, break it into two sentences instead.

Describing People with الَّذِي / الَّتِي

A final tool this chapter introduces is a simple relative clause, used to add one clear detail about a person you have just named — a natural next step after the pronoun-reference work you did in Chapter 1.

Building Block — Adding one detail with a relative clause
definite noun + الَّذِي (masc.) / الَّتِي (fem.) + short clause

هٰذَا هُوَ الجَار الَّذِي يَسْكُن فَوْق شَقَّتِي.

This is the neighbor who lives above my apartment.

الَّذِي refers back to الجَار, a masculine singular noun, and introduces a short clause that adds exactly one useful detail. This is not yet a full clause system — it is a controlled way to attach one identifying detail to a person you have already named, using vocabulary and verb forms you already control.

  • هٰذِهِ هِيَ الجَارَة الَّتِي تَعْمَل فِي المُسْتَشْفَى.This is the neighbor (f.) who works at the hospital.الَّتِي + feminine antecedent
  • أَعْرِف زَمِيلًا يَسْكُن قَرِيبًا مِنِّي.I know a colleague who lives near me.indefinite noun; no الَّذِي needed
Grammar Note — Definite versus indefinite

الَّذِي and الَّتِي are used after a definite noun. After an indefinite noun, as in the second example above, the descriptive clause simply follows without a relative pronoun. You do not need to master every relative-clause pattern now; this chapter only asks you to control this one common, high-frequency use.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Huda shows her new neighbor Layla around the building and introduces some of the people who live and work nearby.
السِّيَاق: تُعَرِّف هُدَى جَارَتَهَا الجَدِيدَة لَيْلَى عَلَى النَّاس فِي البِنَايَة.

LAYLA شُكْرًا لِأَنَّكِ تُعَرِّفِينِي عَلَى البِنَايَة! هَل عِنْدَكِ جِيرَان كَثِيرُون؟ Thanks for showing me around the building! Do you have many neighbors?
HUDA نَعَم، جِيرَانِي طَيِّبُون جِدًّا. لَدَيَّ جَارَان قَرِيبَان مِن شَقَّتِي: أَحْمَد وَسَامِي. Yes, my neighbors are very kind. I have two neighbors close to my apartment: Ahmad and Sami.
LAYLA وَمَاذَا عَن المَحَلّ الصَّغِير تَحْت البِنَايَة؟ And what about the small shop under the building?
HUDA هٰذَا مَحَلّ صَاحِبَتُه اِسْمُهَا أُمّ كَرِيم. هِيَ الَّتِي تَبِيع الخُبْز وَالخُضَار كُلَّ يَوْم. That's a shop whose owner is named Umm Karim. She's the one who sells bread and vegetables every day.
LAYLA جَمِيل! وَهَل اِبْنَتُهَا تُسَاعِدُهَا فِي المَحَلّ؟ Lovely! And does her daughter help her at the shop?
HUDA نَعَم، اِبْنَتَا أُمّ كَرِيم تُسَاعِدَان دَائِمًا بَعْد المَدْرَسَة. هُمَا فَتَاتَان مُجْتَهِدَتَان. Yes, Umm Karim's two daughters always help after school. They are two hard-working girls.
LAYLA أَشْعُر بِأَنَّ هٰذَا مُجْتَمَع صَغِير جِدًّا وَلَطِيف. I feel like this is a very small, friendly community.

أَسْئِلَة الفَهْم — Comprehension questions:

  1. Which two people does Huda name using the dual, and why is the dual appropriate there?
  2. Find the construct chain that names the shop owner's daughters. What are the two (or three) terms in it?
  3. Find the sentence that uses الَّتِي. What detail does it add, and about whom?

Writing Station

In this station, you will draft a short written description of six to eight sentences introducing people in your local community, connecting your ideas rather than listing names.

  1. Open with a general statement about your community. One sentence naming your building, workplace, or neighborhood and how many neighbors or colleagues you have, using a plural.
  2. Introduce two specific people using the dual. One sentence naming exactly two people who are close to you, with correct dual agreement.
  3. Describe one relationship with a construct chain. One sentence naming how two people or a person and a place are connected (e.g., "my neighbor's daughter," "the manager of the shop").
  4. Add one detail with الَّذِي or الَّتِي. One sentence identifying a person by something they do or a place they are connected to.
  5. Mention your relatives for contrast. One sentence about where your relatives live, recycling family vocabulary from Volume I.
  6. Close with a general impression. One sentence about your community as a whole, recycling a connector from Chapter 1.
  7. Revise. Check your draft against the editing checklist below.

قَائِمَة المُرَاجَعَة — Editing checklist:

  • I used at least one plural noun for people with correct human plural agreement.
  • I used the dual correctly for exactly two people.
  • I used at least one construct chain to describe a relationship.
  • I used الَّذِي or الَّتِي correctly to add one detail about a definite person.
  • I used correct gender agreement throughout.
  • My description reads as one connected account, not a list of names.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Neighbors as an extended community

In many Arabic-speaking communities, neighbors are often treated with a degree of familiarity and mutual support that goes beyond simple proximity, and a small local shop or its owner may be well known to everyone nearby. The closeness of these relationships varies by neighborhood, city, and personal circumstance, so treat the model dialogue and passages in this chapter as illustrative rather than universal.

Regional Variation — Titles for shop owners

The title أُمّ followed by a child's name (as in أُمّ كَرِيم, "Umm Karim," literally "mother of Karim") is a widely recognized form of respectful address in many communities, though its everyday use and frequency vary by region and relationship. This course presents it here as a recognizable, MSA-compatible form of address rather than a universal rule.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Using the plural for exactly two people

Error: لِي جَارَات طَيِّبُون: نُور وَرِيم. (using the plural for exactly two neighbors)

Correction: لِي جَارَتَان طَيِّبَتَان: نُور وَرِيم.

Common Mistake — Adding the article to the first term of a construct chain

Error: الاِبْنَة الجَار (adding الـ to the first term)

Correction: اِبْنَة الجَار — the first term of a construct chain never takes the article; only the final term does.

Common Mistake — Using الَّذِي after an indefinite noun

Error: أَعْرِف زَمِيلًا الَّذِي يَسْكُن قَرِيبًا مِنِّي. (adding الَّذِي after an indefinite noun)

Correction: أَعْرِف زَمِيلًا يَسْكُن قَرِيبًا مِنِّي. — after an indefinite noun, the descriptive clause follows directly, without الَّذِي.

Looking Ahead

مَا الَّذِي تَسْتَطِيعُ فِعْلَهُ الآن — What you can do now

You can now describe a small community of people rather than a single individual, using human plural agreement, the dual for pairs, construct chains for relationships, and a simple relative clause to add one identifying detail.

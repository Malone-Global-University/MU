السَّكَن وَالمُشْكِلَات اليَوْمِيَّةHousing and Daily Problems

Requests, complaints, repair language, and recognizing the passive voice in housing notices · Speak and Write Arabic: Everyday Independence and Connected Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Describe common housing problems using accurate vocabulary for rooms, fixtures, and repairs.
  2. Make polite requests and report problems using the imperative and softened request patterns.
  3. Explain a housing complaint clearly, using a content clause to report what is wrong and a contrast clause to explain what should be happening instead.
  4. Recognize the passive voice when it appears in short housing notices, repair signs, and building announcements, without being required to produce it independently.
  5. Read and listen to short housing-related notices and messages, and answer questions that focus on the problem, the request, and the outcome.
  6. Write a short, polite complaint or repair-request message to a landlord or building manager.

Communication Scenario

Something in your apartment has stopped working — the water heater, the kitchen sink, the building's front door — and you need to report the problem to your landlord or building manager clearly enough that it actually gets fixed. At the same time, you keep noticing small printed notices posted in the stairwell and lobby: "the elevator has been repaired," "quiet hours are observed after ten," "visitors are asked to sign in." These notices are written in a slightly different style than the conversational Arabic you have used so far — and this chapter shows you how to recognize that style without needing to produce it yourself yet.

  1. You will learn how to describe what is broken and ask for a repair politely.
  2. You will learn how to complain clearly by combining a content clause ("that the heater is broken") with a contrast ("but no one has come").
  3. You will learn to recognize — but not yet produce — the passive voice, so that housing notices like "the door has been fixed" or "visitors are asked to..." become understandable.
  4. You will write and speak your own repair request, drawing on all three of these tools together.

تَرْكِيب — New Grammar

Requests and Polite Commands

This section reuses the imperative you already know and adds softened, polite request patterns appropriate for addressing a landlord or manager.

Building Block — Polite request pattern
هَل مِن المُمْكِن أَن + subjunctive verb...؟ / أَرْجُو أَن + subjunctive verb...

هَل مِن المُمْكِن أَن تُرْسِل شَخْصًا لِإِصْلَاح السَّخَّان؟ أَرْجُو أَن يَتِمّ ذَلِك قَرِيبًا.

Would it be possible for you to send someone to repair the water heater? I hope that happens soon.

أَرْجُو أَن and هَل مِن المُمْكِن أَن both introduce a verb in the subjunctive, a system you began recognizing in Chapter 3 and will continue to develop through this unit. Here they function as fixed polite frames — you do not need to analyze every subjunctive ending to use them accurately.

  • مِن فَضْلِك، هَل يُمْكِن أَن تُصْلِح المِفْتَاح؟Please, could you fix the switch?polite request frame + Form IV verb
  • أَرْجُو الرَّدّ فِي أَقْرَب وَقْت مُمْكِن.I hope for a reply as soon as possible.formulaic closing request

Complaints and Problem Description

This section introduces two new clause types: the content clause (reporting what is wrong) and the contrast clause (explaining what should be true instead).

Building Block — Reporting a problem with a content clause
أُخْبِرُكُم بِأَنَّ + noun/pronoun + problem description

أُخْبِرُكُم بِأَنَّ المِصْعَد مُتَعَطِّل مُنْذُ يَوْمَيْن.

I am informing you that the elevator has been out of order for two days.

بِأَنَّ introduces a content clause — a clause that reports what something is, rather than describing an action directly. This is the first time content clauses are activated for independent use; earlier chapters used them only in supported reading.

Building Block — Contrast: what is happening vs. what should happen
problem statement + لَكِنْ / مَع أَنَّ + expected or promised outcome

اِتَّصَلْت بِكُم الأُسْبُوع المَاضِي، لَكِنْ لَم يَأْتِ أَحَد لِلْإِصْلَاح حَتَّى الآن. مَع أَنَّكُم وَعَدْتُم بِإِرْسَال فَنِّيّ، مَا زَال العُطْل كَمَا هُوَ.

I called you last week, but no one has come to make the repair until now. Even though you promised to send a technician, the problem is still the same.

لَكِنْ contrasts two statements directly; مَع أَنَّ introduces a concessive clause — something that should have prevented the problem but did not. Together, these let you move from a simple complaint to a clearly reasoned one.

  • أُخْبِرُكُم بِأَنَّ هُنَاك تَسَرُّبًا لِلْمَاء تَحْت المَغْسَلَة.I am informing you that there is a water leak under the sink.content clause reporting a problem
  • طَلَبْت الإِصْلَاح مَرَّتَيْن، لَكِنَّ أَحَدًا لَم يَرُدّ.I requested the repair twice, but no one replied.contrast clause with لَكِنَّ

Recognizing the Passive Voice

This section introduces the passive voice for recognition only. You will learn to understand passive sentences in notices and announcements; you are not asked to produce them independently in this chapter.

Grammar Note — What the passive does

In an active sentence, the subject performs the action: أَصْلَحَ الفَنِّيّ البَاب ("the technician repaired the door"). In a passive sentence, the focus shifts to what happened to something, and the person who did it may be left unstated: أُصْلِحَ البَاب ("the door was repaired"). Notices and public signs often prefer this second pattern because the identity of the repair person is not the point — the state of the door is.

  • أُصْلِحَ المِصْعَد أَمْس.The elevator was repaired yesterday.passive perfect — recognition only
  • يُطْلَب مِن السُّكَّان عَدَم اسْتِخْدَام المِصْعَد.Residents are asked not to use the elevator.passive imperfect — recognition only
  • تَمّ إِصْلَاح البَاب الرَّئِيسِيّ.The main door has been repaired.تَمَّ + verbal noun — a very common formulaic alternative to the passive in notices

Notice the two ways Arabic notices avoid naming who did the repair: the true passive (أُصْلِحَ) and the formulaic تَمَّ + verbal noun (تَمَّ الإِصْلَاح). Both appear frequently in real housing notices, and recognizing either is the goal of this chapter.

Model Dialogue

Setting: مَرْيَم calls the building manager, أُسْتَاذ خَالِد, to report a broken water heater.
السِّيَاق: تَتَّصِل مَرْيَم بِمُدِير المَبْنَى، الأُسْتَاذ خَالِد، لِتُبْلِغ عَن عُطْل فِي السَّخَّان.

MARYAM مَسَاء الخَيْر أُسْتَاذ خَالِد. أُخْبِرُكُم بِأَنَّ سَخَّان المَاء فِي شَقَّتِي تَعَطَّل مُنْذُ صَبَاح اليَوْم. Good evening, Mr. Khalid. I'm informing you that the water heater in my apartment stopped working this morning.
KHALID آسِف لِسَمَاع ذَلِك. هَل مِن المُمْكِن أَن تَصِفِي المُشْكِلَة بِالتَّفْصِيل؟ I'm sorry to hear that. Would it be possible for you to describe the problem in detail?
MARYAM المَاء بَارِد تَمَامًا، وَمَع أَنِّي حَاوَلْت إِعَادَة تَشْغِيلِه، مَا زَال العُطْل كَمَا هُوَ. The water is completely cold, and even though I tried restarting it, the problem is still the same.
KHALID أَرْجُو أَن تَنْتَظِرِي حَتَّى الغَد. سَأُرْسِل فَنِّيًّا لِإِصْلَاح السَّخَّان صَبَاحًا. I hope you can wait until tomorrow. I'll send a technician to repair the heater in the morning.
MARYAM حَسَنًا، شُكْرًا. رَأَيْت إِعْلَانًا فِي المَدْخَل يَقُول إِنَّه تَمَّ إِصْلَاح البَاب الرَّئِيسِيّ — هَل هَذَا صَحِيح؟ All right, thank you. I saw a notice in the entrance saying that the main door has been repaired — is that correct?
KHALID نَعَم، أُصْلِح البَاب أَمْس، وَيُطْلَب مِن الجَمِيع إِغْلَاقُه جَيِّدًا بَعْد الدُّخُول. Yes, the door was repaired yesterday, and everyone is asked to close it well after entering.

  2. What contrast clause does Maryam use, and what two things does it contrast?
  3. Find both passive-recognition sentences in Khalid's last line. What does each communicate without naming who performed the action?

Writing Station

In this station, you will draft a short, polite repair-request message of six to eight sentences to a landlord or building manager.

  1. Open politely. One greeting sentence appropriate to a landlord or manager.
  2. Report the problem. One sentence using أُخْبِرُكُم بِأَنَّ to state clearly what is broken and since when.
  3. Add contrast. One sentence using لَكِنْ or مَع أَنَّ to explain why the situation is inconvenient or urgent.
  4. Make your request. One sentence using هَل مِن المُمْكِن أَن or أَرْجُو أَن to ask for a repair.
  5. Reference the building, if useful. One optional sentence recognizing a passive notice you have seen (for example, mentioning something that تَمَّ إِصْلَاحُه).
  6. Close politely and revise. One closing sentence, then check your draft against the editing checklist below.

قَائِمَة المُرَاجَعَة — Editing checklist:

  • I greeted the landlord or manager appropriately.
  • I reported my problem clearly using a content clause with بِأَنَّ.
  • I included a contrast or concessive clause explaining why the problem matters.
  • I made a polite request using an authorized request pattern.
  • I did not attempt to produce a full independent passive sentence on my own; I only recognized passive or تَمَّ-based phrasing where useful.
  • My message reads as one polite, connected request rather than a list of separate complaints.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Reporting problems politely

In many Arabic-speaking communities, a housing complaint is expected to open with a polite greeting and closing rather than moving directly to the problem, even when the situation is urgent. Softened request patterns such as هَل مِن المُمْكِن أَن and أَرْجُو أَن are common tools for keeping a complaint firm but courteous. Expectations vary by country, building type, and relationship with the landlord, so the model dialogue and notices in this chapter should be read as illustrative rather than as a single fixed script.

Regional Variation — Notices and public signage

The passive constructions and تَمَّ + verbal noun phrasing shown in this chapter's notices are widely used across formal written Arabic in public signage, but everyday spoken reporting of the same event often prefers an active sentence with an unspecified subject, such as "they fixed it" (أَصْلَحُوهُ) rather than "it was fixed." Both patterns are correct; which one you meet will depend on whether you are reading a formal notice or hearing an informal spoken account.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Trying to produce the passive independently

Error: attempting to build new passive sentences from scratch for situations not modeled in this chapter, such as passive descriptions of routine daily actions.

Correction: this chapter authorizes passive recognition only. Recognize أُصْلِحَ, يُطْلَب, and تَمَّ + verbal noun when you meet them in notices; for your own writing and speaking, prefer the active sentences and request patterns taught in this chapter.

Common Mistake — Dropping بِأَنَّ before a content clause

Error: أُخْبِرُكُم السَّخَّان مُتَعَطِّل. (missing the connector that introduces the content clause)

Correction: أُخْبِرُكُم بِأَنَّ السَّخَّان مُتَعَطِّل. — بِأَنَّ is required to link the reporting verb to the clause that states the problem.

Common Mistake — Confusing لَكِنْ and مَع أَنَّ

Error: using مَع أَنَّ where a simple contrast is intended, producing an overly complex sentence.

Correction: use لَكِنْ for a straightforward contrast between two statements, and reserve مَع أَنَّ for situations where something should have prevented the problem but did not — for example, a promise that was not kept.

Answer Key

Model Dialogue — Comprehension

  1. Maryam uses أُخْبِرُكُم بِأَنَّ سَخَّان المَاء ... تَعَطَّل to report that her water heater stopped working that morning.
  2. She uses مَع أَنِّي حَاوَلْت إِعَادَة تَشْغِيلِه, contrasting her attempt to fix it herself with the fact that the problem remained unchanged.
  3. أُصْلِح البَاب أَمْس ("the door was repaired yesterday") and يُطْلَب مِن الجَمِيع إِغْلَاقُه ("everyone is asked to close it") both describe an action or expectation without naming who performed or enforces it.

Listening Station

  • Residents are asked not to use the service elevator until further notice.
  • نُخْبِرُكُم بِأَنَّ هُنَاك تَسَرُّبًا لِلْمَاء فِي القَبْو reports a water leak located in the basement.

Reading Station — Comprehension

  2. Residents are asked (يُرْجَى) to use the stairs until the malfunction is repaired.
  3. نُخْبِرُكُم بِأَنَّ فَنِّيًّا سَيَأْتِي غَدًا صَبَاحًا reports that a technician will come tomorrow morning.
  4. مَع أَنَّنَا حَاوَلْنَا حَلّ المُشْكِلَة اليَوْم, لَم يَكُن ذَلِك مُمْكِنًا بِسَبَب حَجْم العُطْل — explains that the size of the malfunction prevented a same-day repair despite the attempt.

ب: أُخْبِرُكُم بِأَنَّ سَخَّان المَاء تَعَطَّل مُنْذُ أَمْس، وَمَع أَنِّي اتَّصَلْت مَرَّتَيْن، لَم يَأْتِ أَحَد بَعْد.
ب: نَعَم، رَأَيْت إِعْلَانًا يَقُول إِنَّه تَمَّ إِصْلَاح المِصْعَد الرَّئِيسِيّ هَذَا الأُسْبُوع.

Exact wording will vary. Check that a content clause with بِأَنَّ, a contrast or concessive clause, and one recognized passive or تَمَّ-based reference are all present and accurately formed.

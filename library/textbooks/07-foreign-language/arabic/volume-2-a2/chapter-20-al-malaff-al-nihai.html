المَلَفّ النِّهَائِيّ: أَتَوَاصَل بِاسْتِقْلَالFinal Assessment: I Communicate Independently

Integrated B1 portfolio and presentation · Speak and Write Arabic: Everyday Independence and Connected Communication

Chapter Overview

Chapter 20 is the final assessment of Volume II. It introduces no new grammar, morphology, script, vocabulary domain, or register. Instead, it asks you to bring together everything you have built since Chapter 1: your personal profile, your ability to manage everyday problems and services, your participation in institutional and community life, your opinions and comparisons, and your plans and goals. You will assemble a connected written portfolio and deliver a short spoken presentation that draws on all four units of this volume, and you will complete a final self-assessment that connects your work directly to the systems tracked in the Language Systems Progression matrices. This chapter closes Volume II at the B1 level and prepares you for the more independent, analytical, and source-based work that begins in Volume III.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Present a connected personal and social profile that recycles material from Unit I (Chapters 1-5).
  2. Narrate and manage a practical complication using service, housing, and travel language from Unit II (Chapters 6-10).
  3. Describe your participation in learning, work, and community life using institutional and public-information language from Unit III (Chapters 11-15).
  4. Narrate a life experience, state an opinion with justification, and describe a future plan using narration, opinion, and project language from Unit IV (Chapters 16-19).
  5. Read and listen to connected B1-level texts and extract main ideas, details, and speaker stance without full vocalization support.
  6. Write a five-paragraph connected portfolio text that integrates description, narration, opinion, and planning.
  7. Deliver an organized five-minute spoken presentation covering the same content, and respond to follow-up questions.
  8. Self-assess your control of the volume's major systems using the chapter's review checklist.

Opening Communication Scenario

Imagine you have been invited to introduce yourself to a new class, workplace, or community group entirely in Arabic. You will need to say who you are, describe something that happened to you, explain your role in a group or workplace, share an opinion about something in your life, and describe where you are headed next. This is exactly the kind of connected, independent communication this volume has prepared you for. This chapter does not teach you anything new; it gives you the structure to show what you can already do.

  1. You will review and reorganize material from all four units into one connected portfolio.
  2. You will practice moving between description, narration, opinion, and planning in a single conversation or text.
  3. You will read and listen to a connected B1 text that draws on the whole volume.
  4. You will write and present your final portfolio and respond to questions about it.

Pronunciation and Reading Review

Pronunciation Review — Connected speech across a full presentation

When you move between description, narration, opinion, and planning in one presentation, keep the pronunciation habits you have built throughout this volume: natural pause forms at the end of full sentences, hamzat al-waṣl linking across word boundaries, clear vowel-length contrast, and phrase grouping that keeps each idea together. No new pronunciation target is introduced here; the goal is to sustain accurate pronunciation across a longer, connected performance rather than only in short exercises.

Reading in this chapter continues at the unvocalized-with-selective-support level established since Chapter 14. Predictable short vowels on familiar words remain unmarked; new or ambiguous vocabulary is not introduced, so no additional vocalization support is required.

Grammar Systems Synthesis

This chapter introduces no new tense, mood, clause type, or morphological system. It asks you to combine, in a single connected text or presentation, the systems consolidated across the volume: nominal and verbal sentences, plurals and construct chains, perfect and imperfect narration with كَانَ, weak and derived verbs, relative and content clauses, conditionals, comparison and opinion frames, and the future/intention/ability frames from Chapter 19.

Synthesis — Moving between time frames in one text

A connected portfolio text typically moves from present description (nominal sentences, adjective agreement) to past narration (perfect, كَانَ, weak verbs) to present opinion (comparison, justification) to future planning (سَ / سَوْفَ, أَنْوِي أَنْ, إِذَا conditionals). Nothing about how each system works has changed; what is new is managing the transitions between them smoothly, using connectors such as وَ، لَكِنّ، لِذَلِكَ، and بَعْد ذَلِكَ to link the sections.

  • أَنَا أَعِيش فِي المَدِينَة مُنْذُ ثَلَاث سَنَوَات، وَقَبْل ذَلِكَ كُنْتُ أَسْكُن فِي قَرْيَة صَغِيرَة.I have lived in the city for three years, and before that I lived in a small village.present nominal → past with كَانَ, recycled
  • أَعْتَقِد أَنَّ الاِنْتِقَال كَانَ قَرَارًا جَيِّدًا، لِأَنَّنِي وَجَدْتُ عَمَلًا أَفْضَل.I believe the move was a good decision, because I found a better job.opinion frame + cause clause, recycled from Ch. 14, 18
  • وَإِذَا اِسْتَمَرَّ كُلّ شَيْء عَلَى هَذَا النَّحْو، أَنْوِي أَنْ أَبْقَى هُنَا عَلَى المَدَى الطَّوِيل.And if everything continues this way, I intend to stay here for the long term.conditional + intention, recycled from Ch. 19
Synthesis pattern for the final portfolio
description (present) → narration (past) → opinion (present, with reason) → plan (future, with condition and ability)

This four-part pattern is not a new grammar rule; it is an organizational frame for combining systems you already control. Any well-formed text that moves logically through these four functions, using connectors to link them, satisfies the synthesis requirement.

Model Dialogue

Context: a learner introduces themselves to a new community group, drawing on material from across the volume.

المُقَدِّم: أَهْلًا بِكِ. هَل يُمْكِن أَنْ تُعَرِّفِي نَفْسَكِ لِلْمَجْمُوعَة؟Welcome. Could you introduce yourself to the group?
نُور: بِكُلّ سُرُور. اِسْمِي نُور، وَأَعِيش فِي هَذِه المَدِينَة مُنْذُ سَنَتَيْن. اِنْتَقَلْتُ إِلَى هُنَا بَعْد أَنْ وَجَدْتُ عَمَلًا جَدِيدًا، وَكَانَتْ تِلْكَ التَّجْرِبَة صَعْبَة فِي البِدَايَة لَكِنَّهَا نَجَحَتْ فِي النِّهَايَة.With pleasure. My name is Nour, and I've lived in this city for two years. I moved here after finding a new job, and that experience was hard at first but succeeded in the end.
المُقَدِّم: وَمَا رَأْيُكِ فِي المَدِينَة الآن؟And what's your opinion of the city now?
نُور: أَعْتَقِد أَنَّهَا أَفْضَل مِن قَرْيَتِي القَدِيمَة مِن حَيْث الفُرَص، لَكِنِّي أَشْتَاق إِلَى هُدُوء الحَيَاة هُنَاك أَحْيَانًا.I think it's better than my old village in terms of opportunities, but I sometimes miss the quiet of life there.
المُقَدِّم: وَمَاذَا تَنْوِينَ أَنْ تَفْعَلِي هَذِه السَّنَة؟And what do you intend to do this year?
نُور: أَنْوِي أَنْ أُشَارِك أَكْثَر فِي أَنْشِطَة المُجْتَمَع المَحَلِّيّ، وَإِذَا وَجَدْتُ الوَقْت الكَافِي، سَأَتَطَوَّع فِي المَرْكَز الثَّقَافِيّ القَرِيب مِن بَيْتِي.I intend to participate more in local community activities, and if I find enough time, I will volunteer at the cultural center near my house.

Guided Synthesis Practice

  1. Write one present-tense nominal sentence describing where you currently live or work.
  2. Write one past-tense sentence, using كَانَ or the perfect, narrating how you arrived at that situation.
  3. Write one opinion sentence using أَعْتَقِد أَنَّ or مِن رَأْيِي أَنَّ, with a reason introduced by لِأَنَّ.
  4. Write one plan sentence using أَنْوِي أَنْ or إِذَا ... فَـ / سَـ.
  5. Combine your four sentences into one connected paragraph using وَ، لَكِنّ، and بَعْد ذَلِكَ.

Listening Practice

Ask your instructor, a partner, or a text-to-speech tool to read the following short passage aloud twice at a moderate pace. Listen without reading along the first time, then answer the questions below.

اِسْمِي كَرِيم، وَقَدْ عِشْتُ فِي ثَلَاث مُدُن مُخْتَلِفَة خِلَال حَيَاتِي. عِنْدَمَا اِنْتَقَلْتُ إِلَى هَذِه المَدِينَة، وَاجَهْتُ مُشْكِلَة فِي السَّكَن، لَكِنِّي حَلَلْتُهَا بِمُسَاعَدَة جِيرَانِي الجُدُد. الآن أَعْمَل فِي مَجَال التَّعْلِيم، وَأُشَارِك فِي أَنْشِطَة المُجْتَمَع المَحَلِّيّ كُلّ أُسْبُوع. أَعْتَقِد أَنَّ المُشَارَكَة فِي المُجْتَمَع مُهِمَّة جِدًّا، لِأَنَّهَا تُسَاعِد النَّاس عَلَى التَّعَارُف. وَإِذَا اِسْتَمَرَّ كُلّ شَيْء بِشَكْل جَيِّد، أَنْوِي أَنْ أَبْدَأ مَشْرُوعًا تَطَوُّعِيًّا جَدِيدًا فِي الصَّيْف القَادِم.

مُهِمَّة الاسْتِمَاع — Listening task

  3. What is the speaker's opinion about community participation, and what reason do they give?
  4. What plan does the speaker state, and under what condition?

Writing Workshop

In this workshop, you will write your final portfolio text: a connected, five-paragraph piece of writing that draws on all four units of the volume.

  1. Paragraph 1 — Personal profile. Describe who you are, where you live, and your daily routine, recycling Unit I.
  2. Paragraph 2 — A practical experience. Narrate a problem you solved involving housing, services, money, or travel, recycling Unit II.
  3. Paragraph 3 — Community or work role. Describe your participation in learning, work, or community life, recycling Unit III.
  4. Paragraph 4 — An opinion. State an opinion about something in your life or community, with at least one reason, recycling Unit IV.
  5. Paragraph 5 — A plan. State a goal, a step, a condition, and an ability, recycling Chapter 19.
  6. Review your draft against the editing checklist below.

قَائِمَة المُرَاجَعَة — Editing checklist:

  • Each paragraph clearly corresponds to one of the five required sections.
  • I used at least one perfect/past narration structure and one كَانَ structure.
  • I included at least one relative clause and one condition-result sentence.
  • I stated an opinion with a reason and a plan with a condition and an ability.
  • I used connectors (وَ، لَكِنّ، لِذَلِكَ، بَعْد ذَلِكَ) to link ideas within and across paragraphs.
  • My vocabulary, gender agreement, and plural forms are checked for accuracy.
  • I did not attempt spontaneous full case-ending production beyond fixed expressions already practiced.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Presenting oneself across Arabic-speaking communities

Self-introductions in Arabic-speaking communities often include acknowledgment of family, place of origin, and community ties alongside personal or professional information, though the balance varies by setting, generation, and individual. A connected self-presentation that touches briefly on background, community role, and future plans, as this chapter's portfolio does, reflects a widely recognized pattern without representing a single fixed script followed by all speakers.

Regional Variation — Formal presentation versus everyday self-introduction

A prepared presentation like the one in this chapter's performance task is typically delivered in MSA, even by speakers whose everyday self-introductions at home or among friends would shift into regional spoken Arabic. Recognizing when a context calls for the MSA model you have built in this volume, rather than everyday spoken forms, is itself part of the register competence this chapter assesses.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Treating each paragraph as an isolated exercise

Error: writing five unconnected paragraphs that each restate a grammar pattern without linking to the others.

Correction: use connectors and shared reference (repeating key nouns, using pronouns, referring back to earlier sentences) so the portfolio reads as one connected account of a single person's life, not five separate drills.

Common Mistake — Reverting to English sentence order under time pressure

Error: defaulting to English word order or dropping agreement markers when speaking quickly during the presentation.

Correction: slow down slightly at transitions between paragraphs and check subject-verb-adjective agreement, as practiced since Chapter 1, rather than prioritizing speed over accuracy.

Common Mistake — Mixing unlabeled regional forms into the written portfolio

Error: inserting an unlabeled regional word or expression into the written portfolio because it feels more natural in speech.

Correction: keep the written portfolio in productive MSA, as required by every chapter in this volume; regional forms may appear only in clearly labeled, receptive contexts, not as unlabeled substitutions in assessed writing.

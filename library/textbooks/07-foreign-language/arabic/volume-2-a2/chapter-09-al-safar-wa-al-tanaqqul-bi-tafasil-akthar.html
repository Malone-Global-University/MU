السَّفَر وَالتَّنَقُّل بِتَفَاصِيل أَكْثَرTravel and Transportation in More Detail

Weak verbs in travel narration, itinerary language, disruptions, and time clauses · Speak and Write Arabic: Everyday Independence and Connected Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Describe a detailed itinerary, including flights, trains, connections, and delays.
  2. Use hollow, defective, and assimilated (weak) verbs accurately in the perfect and imperfect when narrating travel.
  3. Form time clauses with عِنْدَمَا, حِينَ, قَبْل أَن, and بَعْد أَن to sequence travel events.
  4. Continue recognizing nominative, accusative, and genitive case endings in clearly marked travel-related phrases, alongside limited controlled production in a few high-frequency set expressions.
  5. Read and listen to travel announcements, itinerary confirmations, and short accounts of a disrupted trip.
  6. Write a connected paragraph narrating a trip that included at least one disruption, using weak verbs and time clauses accurately.

Communication Scenario

Your flight is delayed, your connecting train departs from a different platform than expected, and you need to explain to a friend exactly what happened and when. Travel narration in Arabic relies heavily on weak verbs — verbs whose root contains و, ي, or a doubled letter — because so many core travel verbs (to arrive, to return, to fly, to stay) belong to these families. It also depends on time clauses that let you say precisely when one thing happened relative to another: before I arrived, when the train left, after we waited.

  1. You will learn vocabulary for itineraries, connections, delays, and disruptions.
  2. You will strengthen your control of hollow, defective, and assimilated verbs in the perfect and imperfect.
  3. You will learn to sequence events with عِنْدَمَا, حِينَ, قَبْل أَن, and بَعْد أَن.
  4. You will continue noticing case endings on clearly marked words, and begin using a small number of fixed accusative expressions of time and manner on your own.

تَرْكِيب — New Grammar

Weak Verbs on the Move

This section develops hollow, defective, and assimilated verbs — already introduced individually in earlier chapters — into a broader, more confident narration tool for travel accounts.

Building Block — Hollow verbs: عَادَ / يَعُود
Perfect: عُدْت، عُدْنَا، عَادَ، عَادَتْ — Imperfect: أَعُود، نَعُود، يَعُود، تَعُود

عُدْت مِن الرِّحْلَة مُتْعَبًا جِدًّا.

I returned from the trip very tired.

Hollow verbs have a long middle vowel in the imperfect (و or ي) that shortens or disappears in parts of the perfect. With عَادَ, the middle و surfaces as a short ضَمَّة in forms like عُدْت, and returns fully in the imperfect stem يَعُود. طَارَ behaves the same way, but with ي: طِرْت, يَطِير.

Building Block — Defective verbs: بَقِيَ / يَبْقَى
Perfect: بَقِيت، بَقِينَا، بَقِيَ، بَقِيَتْ — Imperfect: أَبْقَى، نَبْقَى، يَبْقَى، تَبْقَى

بَقِينَا فِي المَطَار سَاعَتَيْن بِسَبَب التَّأْخِير.

We stayed at the airport for two hours because of the delay.

Defective verbs end in a weak radical (usually ي) that shows up as a long اَ in most of the imperfect and perfect forms. نَسِيَ follows the same pattern: نَسِيت, يَنْسَى.

  • طَارَتِ الطَّائِرَة فِي المَوْعِد.The airplane departed on time.hollow verb, perfect
  • لَنْ نَبْقَى فِي المَحَطَّة طَوِيلًا.We will not stay at the station long.defective verb, imperfect with لَنْ
  • لَمْ أَنْسَ تَذْكِرَتِي هَذِهِ المَرَّة.I did not forget my ticket this time.defective verb, jussive with لَمْ, recycled from Chapter 8
  • وَصَلْنَا إِلَى المَحَطَّة قَبْل أَن تُغَادِر الحَافِلَة.We arrived at the station before the bus departed.assimilated verb, recycled from Chapter 8

At this stage, focus on producing hollow and defective verbs accurately in the perfect and imperfect within travel narration; further weak-verb environments, including derived forms, are developed in later chapters.

Time Clauses

This section introduces four common connectors for sequencing events in a travel narrative: عِنْدَمَا, حِينَ, قَبْل أَن, and بَعْد أَن.

Building Block — عِنْدَمَا / حِينَ + perfect (when)
عِنْدَمَا / حِينَ + perfect verb, clause

عِنْدَمَا وَصَلْنَا، كَانَتِ الرِّحْلَة قَد تَأَخَّرَتْ سَاعَة.

When we arrived, the flight had already been delayed an hour.

عِنْدَمَا and حِينَ both mean "when" and are followed by a perfect verb to describe a completed event that sets the time frame for the rest of the sentence. عِنْدَمَا is slightly more common in everyday narration; حِينَ is equally correct and appears often in written accounts.

Building Block — قَبْل أَن / بَعْد أَن + subjunctive
قَبْل أَن / بَعْد أَن + subjunctive verb, clause

اِتَّصَلْت بِالمَطَار قَبْل أَن أُغَادِر البَيْت.

I called the airport before I left the house.

قَبْل أَن ("before") and بَعْد أَن ("after") introduce a clause with the subjunctive, following the same subjunctive pattern you learned with لِـ, كَي, and حَتَّى in Chapter 8.

  • حِين عَادَ إِلَى المَحَطَّة، وَجَد أَن القِطَار قَد غَادَر.When he returned to the station, he found that the train had already left.حِين + perfect
  • بَعْد أَن نَنْتَظِر سَاعَة، سَنَسْأَل المُوَظَّف عَن سَبَب التَّأْخِير.After we wait an hour, we will ask the employee about the reason for the delay.بَعْد أَن + subjunctive
  • لَا تَنْسَ أَن تَتَحَقَّق مِن الرَّصِيف قَبْل أَن تَصْعَد.Do not forget to check the platform before you board.قَبْل أَن + subjunctive; recycled جussive prohibition from Chapter 8

These four connectors are introduced here in their most common, clearly bounded uses; additional time-clause connectors and more complex sequencing appear in later chapters.

Case Awareness Continued

This section continues the recognition work begun in Chapter 8 and introduces a small set of fixed accusative expressions for independent use.

Language Focus — A few fixed accusative expressions

Certain common adverbial expressions of time and manner appear in the accusative case as fixed, memorized units, even before learners produce case endings freely. You have already used صَبَاحًا and مَسَاءً in earlier chapters; this chapter adds يَوْمِيًّا (daily) and مُبَاشَرَةً (directly, immediately) to that small productive set.

These forms should be learned as whole expressions with their accusative تَنْوِين ending, not analyzed piece by piece in free speech. Broader case production remains a recognition target, as in Chapter 8, except for this short, explicitly authorized list.

  • تُغَادِر الحَافِلَة مُبَاشَرَةً بَعْد وُصُول القِطَار.The bus departs immediately after the train arrives.fixed accusative expression, productive
  • يَعْمَل المَكْتَب يَوْمِيًّا مَا عَدَا الجُمُعَة.The office operates daily except Friday.fixed accusative expression, productive
  • وَصَلَتِ الطَّائِرَةُ.The airplane arrived.nominative -ُ on the subject, recognition only

Model Dialogue

Setting: يَاسِر calls his friend رِيم to explain why his trip home was delayed.
السِّيَاق: يَتَّصِل يَاسِر بِصَدِيقَتِهِ رِيم لِيَشْرَح لَهَا سَبَب تَأَخُّر رِحْلَته إِلَى البَيْت.

REEM أَخِيرًا! أَيْن كُنْت؟ اِنْتَظَرْتُك سَاعَتَيْن! Finally! Where were you? I waited for you for two hours!
YASIR آسِف جِدًّا! عِنْدَمَا وَصَلْت إِلَى المَطَار، عَرَفْت أَن رِحْلَتِي تَأَخَّرَت سَاعَة كَامِلَة. I'm so sorry! When I arrived at the airport, I found out that my flight was delayed a full hour.
REEM وَبَعْد ذَلِك؟ هَل طَارَت الطَّائِرَة أَخِيرًا؟ And after that? Did the plane finally take off?
YASIR نَعَم، لَكِن بَعْد أَن طَارَت، لَمْ أَجِد رِحْلَة اِتِّصَال مُتَاحَة، فَبَقِيت فِي المَطَار الثَّانِي سَاعَة أُخْرَى. Yes, but after it took off, I couldn't find an available connecting flight, so I stayed at the second airport for another hour.
REEM يَا لَلْمُصِيبَة! هَل نَسِيت أَمْتِعَتَك فِي كُلّ هَذَا الاِضْطِرَاب؟ What a disaster! Did you forget your luggage in all this disruption?
YASIR لَا، لَمْ أَنْسَ شَيْئًا هَذِهِ المَرَّة. عُدْت أَخِيرًا وَأَنَا بِخَيْر، وَلَكِنِّي مُتْعَب جِدًّا. No, I didn't forget anything this time. I'm finally back and I'm fine, but I'm very tired.

أَسْئِلَة الفَهْم — Comprehension questions:

  1. What did Yasir find out when he arrived at the airport?
  2. What problem did Yasir face after his flight took off?
  3. What time clause does Reem use to ask what happened after the plane took off?

أَسْئِلَة الفَهْم — Comprehension questions:

Writing Station

In this station, you will draft a connected paragraph of six to eight sentences narrating a trip that included at least one disruption.

  1. Set the scene. One sentence stating where you were going and how.
  2. Introduce the disruption. One or two sentences using a weak verb (عَادَ, طَارَ, بَقِيَ, or نَسِيَ) to describe what happened.
  3. Sequence the events. Use at least two of عِنْدَمَا, حِين, قَبْل أَن, and بَعْد أَن to connect what happened when.
  4. Mention what did not happen. One sentence using لَمْ + jussive, recycled from Chapter 8.
  5. Add an instruction or reminder, if useful. One optional sentence using لَا + jussive.
  6. Close and revise. One closing sentence, then check your draft against the editing checklist below.

قَائِمَة المُرَاجَعَة — Editing checklist:

  • I used at least one hollow or defective verb correctly in the perfect or imperfect.
  • I used at least two different time-clause connectors from this chapter to sequence events.
  • I used لَمْ + jussive at least once, recycled accurately from Chapter 8.
  • I did not attempt to add case endings on my own beyond the fixed accusative expressions and what I can currently recognize.
  • My paragraph reads as one connected account rather than a list of unrelated facts.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Talking about travel delays

Discussing a delay or disruption is a common conversational topic across Arabic-speaking contexts, often opening with an expression of sympathy such as يَا لَلْمُصِيبَة ("what a disaster," used lightly) before moving into the details. Learners can use such expressions to sound natural when responding to a friend's travel story, without overusing them in more formal or institutional exchanges.

Regional Variation — Words for "platform" and "gate"

رَصِيف is used in this chapter for a train or bus platform, and بَوَّابَة for an airport gate, but signage and announcements in different countries and airports may use slightly different terms or add English loanwords alongside the Arabic. Learners should expect to recognize both the taught MSA term and any locally posted variant when traveling.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Treating hollow verbs as fully regular

Error: عَادْت مِن الرِّحْلَة (keeping the long vowel in the first-person/second-person perfect stem instead of shortening it)

Correction: عُدْت مِن الرِّحْلَة — hollow verbs shorten the middle long vowel in the perfect forms that take a subject suffix beginning with a consonant.

Common Mistake — Using the perfect after قَبْل أَن or بَعْد أَن

Error: قَبْل أَن غَادَرْت (using a perfect verb after قَبْل أَن)

Correction: قَبْل أَن أُغَادِر — قَبْل أَن and بَعْد أَن require the following verb to take the subjunctive, exactly like the purpose particles from Chapter 8.

Common Mistake — Overusing fixed accusative expressions as a general case rule

Error: adding a similar accusative-looking ending to other adverbs or nouns that were not taught as fixed expressions, assuming the same pattern applies everywhere.

Correction: treat يَوْمِيًّا, مُبَاشَرَةً, صَبَاحًا, and مَسَاءً as a short, specific, memorized list; continue using natural pause forms elsewhere, as in Chapter 8.

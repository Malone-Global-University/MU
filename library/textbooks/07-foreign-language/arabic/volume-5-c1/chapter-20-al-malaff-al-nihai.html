Volume V Capstone Unit IV Synthesis

Chapter Overview

In Chapter 19, هَنَاء and سَامِر turned three separate sources into a single bounded, clearly attributed specialized dossier on the renewal project's water-and-sanitation component, and designed a short oral presentation directly from it. The council received that dossier well enough that it has now asked هَنَاء to prepare a single closing file covering the renewal project as a whole: not a repeat of Chapter 19's narrow dossier, but a broader synthesis that draws together the register control from Unit I, the specialized public discourse of Unit II, the research and heritage-aware analysis of Unit III, and the debate, editing, audience-adaptation, and source-integration method of Unit IV, into one polished expert-level Arabic project. This chapter is a synthesis checkpoint and the volume's final capstone: it introduces no new grammar, morphology, script, or vocabulary system beyond labeling the closing task itself, and instead asks learners to bring every system already at mastery or consolidated status into a single sustained, independently produced piece of work.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Use a small set of closing-dossier vocabulary to name the final project, its structure, and its evaluation criteria.
  2. Plan a substantial written and spoken Arabic project that draws on register control, argument, mediation, specialized discourse, research method, and source integration without introducing any new grammar system.
  3. Organize a multi-source, multi-audience capstone dossier with a stated scope, an integrated body, a clearly marked synthesis, and a specific recommendation.
  4. Adapt the same underlying content for a public summary, an expert section, and a short oral presentation, reusing the audience-adaptation method from Chapter 18.
  5. Edit a substantial piece of your own Arabic writing for clarity, cohesion, precision, and register consistency across an extended text.
  6. Demonstrate independent, C2-level command of the volume's cumulative systems by completing a single bounded expert-level Arabic capstone project.

Opening Communication Scenario

The council has approved the water-and-sanitation dossier from Chapter 19 and now wants a single closing file on the renewal project as a whole, to be submitted in writing, summarized for public circulation, and presented at the project's final council session. هَنَاء now has access to every earlier document: سَامِر's original committee report, the technical committee's figures, the health specialist's note, the economic and political framing developed earlier in the volume, and her own edited and adapted texts from Units I through IV. Her task is not to write one more section, as in Chapter 19, but to design the closing file itself: a bounded scope covering the whole project, a body that integrates every relevant source without contradiction, a clearly marked synthesis and recommendation, a short public summary, and a spoken presentation, all built from material she has already produced across the volume.

  1. You will learn a small set of vocabulary for naming the final dossier, its parts, and how it will be judged.
  2. You will study a method for planning a capstone that reuses, rather than replaces, everything already at mastery or consolidated status.
  3. You will study how the same underlying content becomes a public summary, an expert section, and a spoken presentation.
  4. You will read and listen to a model exchange as هَنَاء and سَامِر plan the closing file together.
  5. You will plan, draft, and revise your own capstone project as this chapter's performance task.

إِلْقَاء المَلَفّ النِّهَائِيّ — Delivery: Presenting a Capstone Aloud

Delivery: Presenting a Capstone Aloud

Sound and Delivery — Sustaining register across a longer presentation

No new sound, stress, or pausing pattern is introduced in this final chapter. The relevant skill is sustaining consistent formal pacing, pause placement, and register across a longer spoken presentation than any single earlier chapter required — moving between a public summary, a technical section, and a closing recommendation without the pacing collapsing into a flatter, less formal delivery by the end. Practice the closing recommendation aloud last, since presenters most often let their pacing slip in a capstone's final section after sustaining formal delivery through the body.

تَرْكِيب كُلّ الأَنْظِمَة فِي مَشْرُوع وَاحِد — One Project, Every System

Synthesis Focus: One Project, Every System

This chapter introduces no new grammar, morphology, case, mood, clause, or script system. Every structure the capstone requires is already at mastery or consolidated status somewhere in this volume. What Chapter 20 adds is a method for planning a single project that calls on several of those systems at once, in the right place, without overloading any one section.

Synthesis Move: Naming Which Earlier Chapter Supplies Which Part

القِسْم [X] يَعْتَمِد عَلَى أُسْلُوب الفَصْل [رقم]: [الوَظِيفَة]

this planning sentence, used in the learner's own working notes rather than in the finished dossier, names which chapter's method a given section reuses; it keeps the capstone's structure traceable to systems already mastered, rather than inventing new methods under deadline pressure

القِسْم الأَوَّل يَعْتَمِد عَلَى أُسْلُوب الفَصْل ١: تَحْدِيد مُسْتَوَى الأُسْلُوب المُنَاسِب لِلْجُمْهُور العَامّ قَبْل كِتَابَة أَيّ تَفْصِيل تِقْنِيّ.

"The first section relies on Chapter 1's method: determining the register level appropriate to a general audience before writing any technical detail."

Synthesis Move: Marking the Boundary Between Every Source and the Final Synthesis

وَبِالنَّظَر إِلَى جَمِيع مَا سَبَق مِن مَصَادِر وَأَقْسَام، يُمْكِن القَوْل إِنَّ ... [التَّرْكِيب الشَّامِل]

this pattern extends the single-source synthesis move from Chapter 19 (وَبِنَاءً عَلَى مَا سَبَق) to a comprehensive synthesis drawing on every source and section in the capstone; it reuses cause/result connection, nominalization, and the impersonal يُمْكِن القَوْل إِنَّ already at mastery status, applied at the scale of the whole project rather than one paragraph

وَبِالنَّظَر إِلَى جَمِيع مَا سَبَق مِن مَصَادِر وَأَقْسَام، يُمْكِن القَوْل إِنَّ مَشْرُوع التَّجْدِيد حَقَّق أَهْدَافَه الاجْتِمَاعِيَّة وَالصِّحِّيَّة الرَّئِيسِيَّة، مَع حَاجَة وَاضِحَة إِلَى مُتَابَعَة مَالِيَّة وَبِيئِيَّة فِي المَرْحَلَة القَادِمَة.

"In view of everything set out above, from sources and sections alike, it can be said that the renewal project achieved its main social and health goals, with a clear need for financial and environmental follow-up in the coming phase."

Both moves reuse method and grammar already active in this volume; neither introduces anything new. The capstone's difficulty comes from integration at scale — sustaining register, tracing every claim to its source, and closing with one synthesis that answers to the whole project — not from any new language system.

  • هَذَا القِسْم يُكَرِّر مَا قِيل فِي قِسْم آخَر؛ يَجِب دَمْجُهُمَا أَو حَذْف أَحَدِهِمَا.This section repeats what was said in another section; the two must be merged, or one must be removed.flagging redundancy across a longer capstone
  • الأُسْلُوب فِي هَذَا القِسْم أَقَلّ رَسْمِيَّة مِن بَاقِي المَلَفّ؛ يَجِب تَوْحِيد مُسْتَوَى الأُسْلُوب.The register in this section is less formal than the rest of the file; the register level must be made consistent.diagnosing register drift in a long document
  • التَّرْكِيب الشَّامِل فِي النِّهَايَة لَا يُشِير إِلَى كُلّ الأَقْسَام السَّابِقَة؛ يَجِب تَوْسِيعُه لِيَشْمَل المَشْرُوع كُلَّه.The comprehensive synthesis at the end does not refer to all the preceding sections; it must be expanded to cover the whole project.checking that the final synthesis is genuinely comprehensive
Grammar Note — The capstone is integration at scale, not new grammar

A closing file covering an entire project can feel like it demands new, more advanced language. It does not. Every sentence type, clause structure, case and mood pattern, and rhetorical device this capstone requires has already reached mastery or consolidated status somewhere in this volume. The genuine new difficulty is holding register, attribution, and synthesis steady across a longer, higher-stakes document, and reusing — rather than reinventing — the methods of every earlier unit at the right moment.

حِوَار نَمُوذَجِيّ — Model Exchange

Model Exchange: Planning the Closing File

Setting: The residents committee office, one week before the project's final council session. هَنَاء and سَامِر are planning the structure of المَلَفّ النِّهَائِيّ.

سَامِر: هَذَا مَلَفّ أَكْبَر بِكَثِير مِن مَلَفّ المِيَاه وَالصَّرْف الصِّحِّيّ. مِن أَيْن نَبْدَأ؟This is a much bigger file than the water-and-sanitation dossier. Where do we start?
هَنَاء: نَبْدَأ بِنَفْس الطَّرِيقَة، لَكِن عَلَى مُسْتَوَى المَشْرُوع كُلّه: نُحَدِّد النِّطَاق أَوَّلًا، ثُمَّ نُحَدِّد أَيّ جُزْء مِن عَمَلِنَا السَّابِق يَخْدُم كُلّ قِسْم.We start the same way, but at the level of the whole project: we define the scope first, then identify which part of our earlier work serves each section.
سَامِر: يَعْنِي لَا نَكْتُب شَيْئًا جَدِيدًا؟You mean we don't write anything new?
هَنَاء: نَكْتُب التَّرْكِيب الشَّامِل فِي النِّهَايَة فَقَط، وَجُزْءًا صَغِيرًا يَرْبِط الأَقْسَام بِبَعْضِهَا. أَمَّا البَاقِي فَهُوَ إِعَادَة تَنْظِيم وَمُرَاجَعَة ذَاتِيَّة لِمَا كَتَبْنَاه فِي فُصُول سَابِقَة.We write only the comprehensive synthesis at the end, and a small part linking the sections together. The rest is reorganizing and self-revising what we already wrote in earlier chapters.
سَامِر: وَمَاذَا عَن المُلَخَّص العَامّ لِلسُّكَّان؟And what about the public summary for residents?
هَنَاء: نَسْتَخْدِم طَرِيقَة الفَصْل ١٨: نَفْس المُحْتَوَى، بِأُسْلُوب أَبْسَط وَبِدُون التَّفَاصِيل الفَنِّيَّة. وَمِلَفّ العَرْض يُصَمَّم مِن نَفْس التَّرْكِيب الشَّامِل، لَا مِن نَصّ مُنْفَصِل.We use Chapter 18's method: the same content, in a simpler style, without the technical details. And the presentation file is designed from the same comprehensive synthesis, not from a separate text.
سَامِر: وَكَيْف نَعْرِف أَنَّ المَلَفّ جَاهِز فِعْلًا؟And how do we know the file is actually ready?
هَنَاء: نُرَاجِعُه بِمَعَايِير وَاضِحَة: هَل النِّطَاق مُحَدَّد؟ هَل كُلّ مَصْدَر مُسْنَد؟ هَل الأُسْلُوب مُوَحَّد مِن أَوَّل قِسْم إِلَى آخِره؟ هَل التَّرْكِيب الشَّامِل يُغَطِّي المَشْرُوع كُلَّه؟ عِنْدَمَا تَكُون الإِجَابَة نَعَم عَلَى كُلّ سُؤَال، فَإِنَّ إِنْجَاز المَشْرُوع قَد تَمّ.We review it against clear criteria: Is the scope defined? Is every source attributed? Is the register consistent from the first section to the last? Does the comprehensive synthesis cover the whole project? When the answer is yes to every question, the project's completion has occurred.

Notice that هَنَاء's plan reuses Chapter 19's source-integration method, Chapter 18's audience-adaptation method, and Chapter 17's editing method, applied at the scale of the whole project, with self-review criteria she and سَامِر can check against directly.

Writing Workshop: Assembling the Capstone

This workshop guides you through assembling your own closing file from material you have already produced across the volume, following هَنَاء's method.

مَوَادّ لِلتَّجْمِيع — Materials to Draw On
الغَرَض / Purpose: تَجْمِيع مَلَفّ نِهَائِيّ مِن أَعْمَال سَابِقَة

اِخْتَر مَشْرُوعًا وَاحِدًا مَرَّ بِه هَذَا الكِتَاب أَو مَوْضُوعًا مِن اِخْتِيَارِك، وَاجْمَع كُلّ مَا كَتَبْتَه عَنْه فِي فُصُول سَابِقَة: فِقْرَات، مُلَخَّصَات، مُرَاسَلَات، أَو مَلَاحَظَات.

Choose one project this book has followed, or a topic of your own choosing, and gather everything you have written about it in earlier chapters: paragraphs, summaries, correspondence, or notes.

  1. Step 1 — State the overall scope. Write one or two sentences naming the full bounded project your closing file covers, wider than any single earlier chapter's dossier but still clearly limited.
  2. Step 2 — Map sections to earlier chapters. For each planned section, note in your working plan which earlier chapter's method or content it reuses, following the pattern القِسْم يَعْتَمِد عَلَى أُسْلُوب الفَصْل.
  3. Step 3 — Integrate sources across sections. Reusing Chapter 19's method, integrate your gathered material into two or three connected sections, keeping every source attributed and every conflict named rather than hidden.
  4. Step 4 — Write the comprehensive synthesis. Write one closing paragraph beginning وَبِالنَّظَر إِلَى جَمِيع مَا سَبَق مِن مَصَادِر وَأَقْسَام, يُمْكِن القَوْل إِنَّ, that answers to the whole project, followed by one specific recommendation.
  5. Step 5 — Adapt for two audiences. Reusing Chapter 18's method, write a short public summary of your synthesis and recommendation in simpler register, and outline a short spoken presentation designed from the same material.
  6. Step 6 — Self-revise. Reusing Chapter 17's editing method, review your full draft for register consistency, unattributed claims, redundancy between sections, and a synthesis that genuinely covers everything above it.
  7. Review your finished capstone against the checklist below.

قَائِمَة المُرَاجَعَة — Checklist:

  • The opening states the overall scope of the whole capstone, not just one section.
  • Every claim throughout the file is attributed to its source or clearly marked as the writer's own synthesis.
  • Register stays consistent from the first section to the last.
  • The comprehensive synthesis genuinely answers to every section and source above it, not only the most recent one.
  • The closing recommendation proposes a specific action, not a restatement of the synthesis.
  • The public summary and presentation outline reuse the capstone's own content rather than introducing new material.
  • No grammar, case, mood, clause, or script system beyond what is already at mastery or consolidated status appears anywhere in the file.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Closing files in institutional and community practice

A closing or final-status file, submitted at the completion of a public project, is a familiar institutional genre across many Arabic-speaking communities, civic bodies, and professional settings. Such files are commonly expected to state clearly what was accomplished, what remains open, and what specific action should follow, rather than simply repeating earlier interim reports. A well-organized closing file is often what allows a committee, a council, or a professional body to formally conclude a project's active phase while setting clear expectations for any follow-up.

Regional Variation — Closing-report conventions vary by institution and country

The expected length, formality, and required sections of a closing or final report vary considerably by country, sector, and organizational convention. Some institutions expect a single integrated narrative; others expect separately labeled technical, financial, and social sections. This chapter models a general, widely usable pattern of scope, integrated body, synthesis, and recommendation, rather than a single universal institutional standard.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Writing the capstone as entirely new material

Error: drafting the entire closing file from scratch, ignoring material already written and revised in earlier chapters.

Correction: plan the capstone as a reorganization and synthesis of earlier work, writing only the connective material and the comprehensive synthesis as genuinely new text.

Common Mistake — A synthesis that only covers the most recent section

Error: writing a closing synthesis that responds only to the final section of the file, rather than to the whole project.

Correction: check the comprehensive synthesis against every section and source in the file before finalizing it, and revise it to answer to the whole project explicitly.

Common Mistake — Register drifting across a longer document

Error: allowing later sections of a long capstone to become noticeably less formal than earlier sections, especially in the closing recommendation.

Correction: apply the self-revision step specifically to register consistency, reading the file's opening and closing sections side by side to check that formality has not slipped.

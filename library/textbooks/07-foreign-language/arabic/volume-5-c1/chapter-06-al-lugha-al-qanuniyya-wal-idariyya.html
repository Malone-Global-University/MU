Chapter Overview

Unit II Opens — Specialized Domain: Legal and Administrative Arabic

Chapter 5 closed هَنَاء's work on the community center dossier by showing that register, argument, density, and mediation could be combined into one internally consistent file. This chapter turns that same toolkit toward a new setting: the regulatory language behind the decision itself. هَنَاء's office has asked her to read, summarize, and respond to the formal regulation governing renovation approvals — the text her earlier dossier was written about but never had to interpret directly. This chapter introduces the vocabulary, phrasing, and institutional conventions of legal and administrative Arabic: the language of regulations, procedures, rights, obligations, and official forms. No new clause structure, case system, or mood environment is required. What is new is a bounded, clearly labeled specialized register — the vocabulary and phrasing patterns institutions use to state rules, define obligations, and describe procedures — layered onto grammar and argument systems already consolidated through Volume IV and Unit I.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Recognize and use core legal and administrative vocabulary for rights, obligations, regulations, and procedures.
  2. Read a short regulatory text and identify what it requires, permits, and prohibits.
  3. Distinguish obligation, permission, and prohibition as expressed in formal administrative Arabic.
  4. Summarize a regulation's key provisions in plain, register-appropriate prose for a non-specialist reader.
  5. Complete and respond to a short institutional form using accurate, formal Arabic.
  6. Explain, at a learner-appropriate level, why legal and administrative Arabic remains a bounded specialized register in this course rather than a claim to legal expertise.

صَوْت الخِطَاب الرَّسْمِيّ المُؤَسَّسِيّ — The Sound of Institutional Arabic

The Sound of Institutional Arabic

Sound and Style — Reading a regulation aloud

Regulatory Arabic is read more slowly and evenly than narrative or conversational prose, with clear phrase breaks around conditions and provisions. A sentence built on a pattern such as يَجِب عَلَى … أَن … ("it is obligatory upon … that …") is typically read with a brief pause after the obligated party and another before the result clause, so a listener can track who is obligated to do what. This is the same formal connected-reading control practiced since Volume IV, applied to sentences that carry conditions and obligations rather than narrative content. No new pronunciation target is introduced.

Practice reading the regulation excerpt in the Reading Practice section aloud, pausing clearly at each comma and before each conditional clause, as if reading it for someone who must act on what they hear.

لُغَة الوُجُوب وَالإِلْزَام — The Language of Obligation

Grammar Focus: The Language of Obligation, Permission, and Prohibition

No new clause type or case/mood system is introduced. This section organizes existing resources — impersonal and passive-leaning verbs, particle-governed clauses, and construct phrases already consolidated through Volume IV — into the small set of fixed patterns that Arabic regulatory prose uses to state what is required, permitted, or forbidden.

Three Institutional Patterns

يَجِب عَلَى + فاعل + أَن + فعل | يَحِقّ لِـ + فاعل + أَن + فعل | يُحْظَر / يُمْنَع + عَلَى + فاعل + فعل

obligation: "it is obligatory upon X that X do Y" · permission: "X is entitled to do Y" · prohibition: "it is forbidden/prevented for X to do Y"

يَجِب عَلَى المُقَاوِل أَن يَحْصُل عَلَى تَصْرِيح قَبْل بَدْء العَمَل.

The contractor must obtain a permit before beginning work.

These three impersonal patterns recur throughout Arabic regulatory and administrative prose. Each names the party affected using a preposition (عَلَى for the obligated or prohibited party, لِـ for the entitled party) rather than a subject pronoun, and each governs a following subjunctive clause with أَن. This is the same subjunctive-governing particle behavior consolidated since Volume III, now recognized as the grammatical engine behind formal legal and administrative statements.

  • يَحِقّ لِلْمُقِيمِين الاعْتِرَاض عَلَى القَرَار خِلَال ثَلَاثِين يَوْمًا مِن تَارِيخ الإِعْلَان.Residents have the right to object to the decision within thirty days of the announcement date.yaḥiqqu li- + subject: entitlement pattern
  • يُحْظَر تَعْدِيل الوَاجِهَة الخَارِجِيَّة دُون مُوَافَقَة خَطِّيَّة مِن الجِهَة المُخْتَصَّة.Modifying the exterior facade without written approval from the competent authority is prohibited.passive-leaning prohibition, agent omitted
  • تَلْتَزِم الإِدَارَة بِالرَّدّ عَلَى الطَّلَب خِلَال خَمْسَة عَشَر يَوْم عَمَل.The administration is obligated to respond to the request within fifteen working days.Form VIII verb from the same root as الالْتِزَام
Grammar Note — Passive and impersonal style in institutional prose

Notice that the prohibition example above omits any named agent: no one is said to forbid the action; the action itself يُحْظَر ("is prohibited"). This agent-omitting, process-focused style was introduced as receptive and limited controlled material in earlier volumes and now becomes a recognizable feature of institutional register: regulations describe what is required or forbidden as a property of the rule itself, not as an instruction from a named person. Learners should recognize this pattern in reading and may use it in guided writing tasks in this chapter, without being expected to generate unsupported internal passive forms outside these fixed patterns.

Construct Phrases as Institutional Titles

Legal and administrative Arabic relies heavily on construct-state phrases to name institutions, roles, and documents precisely: الجِهَة المُخْتَصَّة ("the competent authority"), تَارِيخ الإِعْلَان ("the date of the announcement"), مُدَّة الِاعْتِرَاض ("the objection period"). These are the same construct-state and adjective-agreement systems consolidated since Volume II, applied here to a specialized institutional vocabulary set rather than to new grammar.

Model Exchange: Checking Compliance Before Submission

Setting: هَنَاء confirms with her supervisor that the renovation request satisfies the regulation before the board reviews it.

المُشْرِف: هَل حَصَل المُقَاوِل عَلَى التَّصْرِيح المَطْلُوب؟Has the contractor obtained the required permit?

المُشْرِف: وَهَل تَمّ إِعْلَان المُقِيمِين قَبْل بِدَايَة العَمَل؟And were the residents notified before work began?

المُشْرِف: جَيِّد. وَهَل وَرَدَت أَيّ اعْتِرَاضَات؟Good. Were any objections received?

المُشْرِف: إِذَن الطَّلَب مُسْتَوْفٍ لِلشُّرُوط، وَيُمْكِن إِحَالَتُه إِلَى اللَّجْنَة.Then the request satisfies the conditions, and it can be referred to the committee.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Regulatory language as a shared institutional genre

Across Arabic-speaking countries, regulations, bylaws, and administrative circulars share a recognizable set of conventions — numbered articles, impersonal obligation patterns, and construct-phrase titles for authorities and procedures — even though the specific institutions, legal systems, and enforcement mechanisms differ considerably from one country to another. Recognizing this shared genre helps learners read unfamiliar regulatory texts more confidently, but it does not mean that legal procedures, rights, or terminology are identical across countries. A learner encountering an unfamiliar regulation should expect the general phrasing patterns taught in this chapter to be familiar, while the specific content, authority, and legal consequence must always be verified against the actual jurisdiction in question.

Regional Variation — Institutional vocabulary and terminology

Terms for specific institutions, permits, and procedures vary by country and by legal system. A term translated here as "municipality" or "competent authority" may correspond to differently structured institutions in different countries, and some administrative vocabulary is shared with French-, English-, or Ottoman-derived legal traditions depending on the country's history. Learners working with a specific country's regulations should consult that country's own official terminology rather than assuming full equivalence with the general vocabulary introduced in this chapter.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Confusing obligation and permission patterns

Error: using يَحِقّ لِـ ("is entitled to") where the source text expresses a requirement, producing a summary that describes an obligation as optional.

Correction: check whether the source text names a requirement (يَجِب, يَلْتَزِم) or an entitlement (يَحِقّ) before choosing the corresponding pattern in your own summary.

Common Mistake — Overstating legal consequence

Error: summarizing a regulatory provision using stronger or more specific legal consequences than the source text actually states, such as inventing a fine or penalty not mentioned in the excerpt.

Correction: restate only the consequence the text itself names (for example, that work is suspended), and avoid adding unstated legal outcomes, since this course teaches bounded institutional vocabulary, not legal analysis.

Common Mistake — Writing the plain-language summary in the same register as the regulation

Error: producing a "summary" that simply repeats the regulation's dense institutional phrasing rather than adjusting it for a non-specialist reader.

Correction: apply the register-adjustment judgment from Chapter 1: keep the content accurate but choose plainer vocabulary and shorter sentences suited to the intended reader.

  3. If no objection is received, final approval is referred to the competent committee.
  4. Violation: beginning work before this approval is issued; consequence: the project is suspended.
  5. Obligation pattern: يَجِب عَلَى الجِهَة المُنَفِّذَة … الحُصُول عَلَى تَصْرِيح; permission pattern: يَحِقّ لِلْمُقِيمِين تَقْدِيم اعْتِرَاض خَطِّيّ; prohibition pattern: يُحْظَر البَدْء بِأَيّ عَمَل قَبْل صُدُور هَذِه المُوَافَقَة.

Chapter Review — Model responses

Section A: The obligation pattern (يَجِب عَلَى) states what a party must do; the permission pattern (يَحِقّ لِـ) states what a party is entitled to do; the prohibition pattern (يُحْظَر) states what is forbidden, typically without naming who is forbidden from doing it. This chapter's legal vocabulary is bounded and general because it equips learners to read, summarize, and respond to institutional texts in course-appropriate contexts; it does not constitute legal training, and real regulatory questions should be verified against the actual governing law and a qualified professional.

Section B: Open-ended production; any accurate sentence pair using the obligation and prohibition patterns correctly, with a faithful plain-language restatement and correct identification of a construct-state phrase, is acceptable.

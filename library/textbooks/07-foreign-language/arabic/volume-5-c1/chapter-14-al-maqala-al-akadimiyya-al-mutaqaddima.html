المَقَالَة الأَكَادِيمِيَّة المُتَقَدِّمَةChapter 14: The Advanced Academic Essay

Unit III Continuation · Speak and Write Arabic: Mastery, Precision, Rhetoric, and Specialized Expression

Chapter Overview

Chapter 13 gave هَنَاء a bounded way to recognize and name heritage-derived expressions without producing new grammar or stepping into theology or history she was not asked to cover. This chapter asks her to bring together several strands from across Unit III — the research-method vocabulary from Chapter 11, the stylistic and figurative resources from Chapter 12, and the labeling habits from Chapter 13 — into a single sustained piece of writing: a short advanced academic essay that draws on more than one source, states and supports a thesis, and cites those sources responsibly in Arabic. The chapter's two closely related new systems are source synthesis (combining and weighing more than one source's claims in a single argument) and citation language (the fixed expressions Arabic academic prose uses to introduce, attribute, and distance itself from a source). Nominalization, relative clauses, passive voice, and the argument-structure vocabulary from Chapter 2 remain at their established mastery status and do the grammatical work; nothing new is asked of case, mood, or clause systems. هَنَاء will read two short adapted excerpts on the same civic topic — the neighborhood renewal project her association has been tracking since Chapter 8 — outline a short essay that synthesizes and evaluates them, and revise a draft using this volume's editing vocabulary.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Use core vocabulary for source synthesis, argument structure, and citation in level-appropriate MSA.
  2. Introduce, attribute, and distance a claim using a small set of fixed Arabic academic citation patterns.
  3. Compare two short sources on the same topic and state where they agree, disagree, or emphasize different aspects.
  4. Draft a short academic essay with a clear thesis, two supporting points drawn from sources, and a conclusion.
  5. Revise a draft essay for clarity, cohesion, and accurate source attribution using this chapter's checklist.
  6. Distinguish an academic essay's evidence-based claims from personal opinion, and label each accordingly.

Opening Communication Scenario

The neighborhood association's newsletter committee — the same committee هَنَاء wrote for in Chapter 12 — has been asked by a local university partnership to contribute a short academic-style essay on the neighborhood renewal project for a joint publication. هَنَاء has two short pieces in hand: an interview-based account from a resident association bulletin and a short excerpt from a university researcher's report on community-led renewal elsewhere in the region. Her task is not to just summarize each piece separately, but to put them into conversation with each other, decide what they together suggest about the neighborhood's project, and support that judgment with properly attributed evidence rather than her own unsupported impression.

  1. You will learn vocabulary for source synthesis, argument, and citation.
  2. You will read two short adapted sources on the same civic topic.
  3. You will compare what each source claims and where they agree or diverge.
  4. You will outline and draft a short essay with a thesis supported by both sources.
  5. You will revise your draft using this chapter's editing checklist.

صَوْت الإِلْقَاء الأَكَادِيمِيّ — The Sound of Academic Delivery

The Sound of Academic Delivery

Sound and Delivery — Pacing a synthesis sentence

No new sound is introduced in this chapter. The useful skill is prosodic: a sentence that reports one source, then contrasts it with another (for example, using فِي حِين أَنَّ, "whereas"), is normally read with a brief pause before the contrast particle and a slight rise in pitch on the word introducing the second source. This pacing signals to a listener that two claims are being weighed against each other, not simply listed. Practice reading citation sentences aloud with a clear pause at the contrast point, using natural formal connected reading and pause forms already established.

تَوْلِيف المَصَادِر وَلُغَة الِاسْتِشْهَاد — Source Synthesis and Citation Language

Grammar Focus: Source Synthesis and Citation Language

This chapter's two closely related new systems are source synthesis and citation language, both lexical and organizational rather than new grammatical structures. Nominalization, relative clauses, and passive voice — already at mastery status since earlier volume chapters — continue to carry the sentence-level grammar; this section shows how citation formulas and synthesis connectors organize that grammar into an academic argument.

Pattern 1: Attributing a Claim to a Named Source

وَفْقًا لِـ [مصدر]، ...

the fixed formula "according to [source]" introduces a content clause carrying the source's claim; the content clause itself uses grammar already consolidated (nominal or verbal sentences, nominalization)

وَفْقًا لِنَشْرَة الجَمْعِيَّة الأَهْلِيَّة، اِزْدَادَت مُشَارَكَة السُّكَّان فِي الاجْتِمَاعَات الشَّهْرِيَّة بِشَكْل وَاضِح خِلَال العَام المَاضِي.

According to the resident association's bulletin, residents' participation in the monthly meetings clearly increased over the past year.

Pattern 2: Synthesizing Two Sources That Diverge

يُشِير [مصدر أ] إِلَى..., فِي حِين أَنَّ [مصدر ب] يُؤَكِّد عَلَى...

the contrast connector فِي حِين أَنَّ ("whereas"), already consolidated as a clause-linkage system, joins two attributed claims into a single synthesizing sentence rather than presenting them as separate, unrelated summaries

يُشِير تَقْرِير البَاحِثَة إِلَى أَنَّ نَجَاح المَشَارِيع المُشَابِهَة يَعْتَمِد عَلَى التَّمْوِيل المُسْتَمِرّ، فِي حِين أَنَّ نَشْرَة الجَمْعِيَّة تُؤَكِّد عَلَى أَنَّ مُشَارَكَة السُّكَّان هِيَ العَامِل الأَهَمّ.

The researcher's report points out that similar projects' success depends on continuous funding, whereas the association's bulletin affirms that resident participation is the most important factor.

Both patterns reuse structures already at mastery status — content clauses, contrast connectors, and nominalization — and attach this chapter's new citation and synthesis vocabulary to them. An advanced academic essay is therefore built almost entirely from prior grammar plus a disciplined habit of attributing and comparing, consistent with this volume's load-control principle.

  • تَتَّفِق المَصَادِر الثَّلَاثَة عَلَى أَنَّ التَّغْيِير كَان تَدْرِيجِيًّا لَا مُفَاجِئًا.The three sources agree that the change was gradual, not sudden.reporting agreement across sources
  • هَذَا الرَّأْي شَخْصِيّ وَلَيْس مَدْعُومًا بِدَلِيل مِن أَيّ مَصْدَر مَذْكُور.This view is personal and is not supported by evidence from any of the sources mentioned.explicitly labeling opinion versus evidence
  • يُمْكِن أَن نُلَخِّص أَطْرُوحَة المَقَالَة فِي جُمْلَة وَاحِدَة: النَّجَاح جَاء مِن تَعَاوُن السُّكَّان وَالجِهَات الرَّسْمِيَّة مَعًا.We can summarize the essay's thesis in one sentence: success came from the cooperation of residents and official bodies together.stating a thesis after synthesis
  • يَرَى الكَاتِب أَنَّ هَذَا التَّبَايُن بَيْن المَصْدَرَيْن لَا يُلْغِي إِمْكَانِيَّة الجَمْع بَيْن الرَّأْيَيْن.The writer holds the view that this divergence between the two sources does not rule out the possibility of combining both views.reconciling divergent sources
Grammar Note — Why an essay-writing chapter still holds grammar constant

This volume's production rule applies here exactly as in Chapters 11 through 13: no more than two closely related major new systems per chapter, and difficulty should come from integration rather than new grammar load. Source synthesis and citation language are the two new systems in this chapter; they are lexical and organizational, not new grammatical structures. Case, mood, and clause systems remain governed by their existing Chapter 5 status, and this chapter's essay draws its sentence-level accuracy entirely from systems already consolidated.

حِوَار نَمُوذَجِيّ — Model Exchange

Model Exchange: Planning the Essay

Setting: هَنَاء and the newsletter committee's editor discuss how to combine the two sources before she drafts the essay.

المُحَرِّرَة: هَل يَتَّفِق المَصْدَرَان عَلَى نَفْس التَّفْسِير لِنَجَاح المَشْرُوع؟Do the two sources agree on the same explanation for the project's success?
هَنَاء: لَا تَمَامًا. تُؤَكِّد نَشْرَة الجَمْعِيَّة عَلَى دَوْر مُشَارَكَة السُّكَّان، فِي حِين أَنَّ تَقْرِير البَاحِثَة يُشِير إِلَى أَهَمِّيَّة التَّمْوِيل المُسْتَمِرّ.Not entirely. The association's bulletin affirms the role of resident participation, whereas the researcher's report points to the importance of continuous funding.
المُحَرِّرَة: إِذَن، هَل هَذَا تَبَايُن حَقِيقِيّ، أَم يُمْكِن الجَمْع بَيْن الرَّأْيَيْن فِي أَطْرُوحَة وَاحِدَة؟So is this a genuine divergence, or can the two views be combined into a single thesis?
هَنَاء: أَرَى أَنَّه يُمْكِن الجَمْع بَيْنَهُمَا: النَّجَاح جَاء مِن تَعَاوُن السُّكَّان وَالتَّمْوِيل المُسْتَمِرّ مَعًا، لَا مِن عَامِل وَاحِد فَقَط.I think the two can be combined: success came from resident cooperation and continuous funding together, not from a single factor alone.
المُحَرِّرَة: جَيِّد. هَذِه أَطْرُوحَة قَوِيَّة لِأَنَّهَا مَدْعُومَة بِدَلِيل مِن كِلَا المَصْدَرَيْن.Good. That's a strong thesis because it's supported by evidence from both sources.
هَنَاء: سَأَبْدَأ المَقَالَة بِهَذِه الأَطْرُوحَة، ثُمَّ أَسْتَشْهِد بِكُلّ مَصْدَر لِدَعْمِهَا.I'll open the essay with this thesis, then cite each source to support it.

Notice that هَنَاء attributes each claim to its source, names the divergence explicitly, and reaches a synthesized thesis rather than simply repeating both sources side by side — the core move this chapter's academic-writing work requires.

Writing Workshop

Write a short academic essay (six to nine sentences) that states a thesis about the neighborhood renewal project, supports it with attributed evidence from both sources, and reaches a brief conclusion.

  1. Step 1 — State your thesis. Open with a single sentence naming your central claim, informed by both sources.
  2. Step 2 — Attribute your first piece of evidence. Use Pattern 1 to introduce a claim from one source.
  3. Step 3 — Attribute your second piece of evidence and synthesize. Use Pattern 2 to introduce the second source and relate it to the first, noting agreement or divergence.
  4. Step 4 — Conclude. Close with one or two sentences restating how the evidence supports your thesis.
  5. Review your work against the checklist below.
مَقَالَة قَصِيرَة: مَشْرُوع تَجْدِيد الحَيّ
الغَرَض / Purpose: مَقَالَة أَكَادِيمِيَّة قَصِيرَة تُوَلِّف بَيْن مَصْدَرَيْن

يَبْدُو أَنَّ نَجَاح مَشْرُوع تَجْدِيد الحَيّ لَا يَرْجِع إِلَى عَامِل وَاحِد، بَل إِلَى تَعَاوُن مُشَارَكَة السُّكَّان وَاسْتِمْرَار التَّمْوِيل مَعًا. وَفْقًا لِنَشْرَة الجَمْعِيَّة الأَهْلِيَّة، اِزْدَادَت مُشَارَكَة السُّكَّان فِي الاجْتِمَاعَات الشَّهْرِيَّة بِشَكْل وَاضِح، وَهَذَا مَا تَعْتَبِرُه الجَمْعِيَّة السَّبَب الرَّئِيسِيّ لِلنَّجَاح. غَيْر أَنَّ تَقْرِير البَاحِثَة الجَامِعِيَّة يُشِير إِلَى أَنَّ اسْتِمْرَار التَّمْوِيل كَان العَامِل الأَكْثَر تَأْثِيرًا فِي مَشَارِيع مُشَابِهَة، حَتَّى حِين كَانَت مُشَارَكَة السُّكَّان مَحْدُودَة. تَتَّفِق المَصَادِر إِذَن عَلَى أَنَّ النَّجَاح تَدْرِيجِيّ، لَكِنَّهَا تَخْتَلِف فِي تَحْدِيد العَامِل الأَهَمّ. لِذَلِك، يُمْكِن القَوْل إِنَّ حَالَة هَذَا الحَيّ تَجْمَع بَيْن العَامِلَيْن مَعًا، لَا بَيْن أَحَدِهِمَا فَقَط.

"It appears that the success of the neighborhood renewal project is not due to a single factor, but to the cooperation of resident participation and continuous funding together. According to the resident association's bulletin, resident participation in monthly meetings clearly increased, and the association considers this the main reason for success. However, the university researcher's report points out that continuous funding was the most influential factor in similar projects, even when resident participation was limited. The sources therefore agree that success is gradual, but they differ in identifying the most important factor. Therefore, one can say that this neighborhood's case combines both factors together, not just one of them."

قَائِمَة المُرَاجَعَة — Checklist:

  • The essay opens with a clear thesis informed by both sources.
  • Each source's claim is introduced with an accurate citation formula (Pattern 1 or Pattern 2).
  • Agreement or divergence between the sources is stated explicitly.
  • Any personal opinion is clearly labeled as opinion, separate from cited evidence.
  • No grammar, case, mood, or clause system beyond what Chapter 5 had consolidated appears anywhere in the essay.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Community bulletins and academic partnerships

Many Arabic-speaking communities maintain resident associations, neighborhood bulletins, and local publications that document civic projects in accessible, non-academic language, and universities in the region often partner with such associations to study community-led initiatives. The register expected in a joint community-university publication typically sits between the informal tone of a bulletin and the fuller apparatus of a formal research article, and writers are expected to attribute claims carefully even in this intermediate register.

Academic Register Note — Attribution as a mark of credibility

In academic and semi-academic Arabic writing, careful, explicit attribution of claims to named sources is treated as a mark of credibility rather than as an obstacle to a confident argument. A writer who clearly separates cited evidence from personal opinion, and who accurately represents where sources agree or diverge, is generally seen as more persuasive than one who blends the two without attribution.

Regional Variation — Citation conventions across institutions

Specific citation formatting conventions (footnotes, in-text parenthetical citation, bibliographic style) vary by country, institution, publisher, and academic field, and some publications favor lighter in-text attribution like the phrases used in this chapter over full formal citation apparatus. This chapter teaches the language of attribution common across many such conventions rather than any single institution's formatting rules.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Summarizing sources separately instead of synthesizing them

Error: writing one paragraph that only restates Source One, followed by a second paragraph that only restates Source Two, without ever relating the two claims to each other.

Correction: use Pattern 2 to build at least one sentence that explicitly compares what the two sources claim, as modeled in this chapter's sample essay.

Common Mistake — Blending personal opinion with cited evidence

Error: presenting an unsupported personal impression in the same sentence as an attributed claim, so that a reader cannot tell which is evidence and which is opinion.

Correction: keep attributed claims in sentences with a clear citation formula, and mark personal opinion explicitly as رَأْي شَخْصِيّ in a separate sentence.

Common Mistake — Overstating agreement or divergence between sources

Error: claiming that two sources "completely agree" or "completely contradict" each other when they in fact overlap partially or address different aspects of the same topic.

Correction: state precisely what each source claims and describe the relationship accurately, using bounded language such as "partially agree" or "differ in emphasis" rather than absolute claims.

Practical Performance Task

مُهِمَّة عَمَلِيَّة — Performance Task

كِتَابَة مَقَالَة قَصِيرَة — Writing a Short Essay

Imagine the newsletter committee has asked you to submit a short academic-style essay (as in this chapter's opening scenario) synthesizing the two sources on the neighborhood renewal project. Write a response (six to nine sentences) that:

  • opens with a clear thesis informed by both sources;
  • attributes at least one claim to each source using this chapter's citation patterns;
  • explicitly states where the sources agree or diverge;
  • clearly separates any personal opinion from cited evidence; and
  • uses no grammar, case, mood, or clause system beyond Chapter 5's consolidated status.

This is an ordinary chapter performance task, not a unit assessment; the Unit III synthesis checkpoint occurs in Chapter 15.

Answer Key

Listening Practice

  • The first two sentences attribute a claim to a single source; the third and the sentence with فِي حِين أَنَّ contrast two sources.
  • The pause and pitch rise fall just before فِي حِين أَنَّ, signaling the shift to the contrasting claim.
  • Sentence one attributes to the association's bulletin; the contrast sentence attributes to the researcher's report and the bulletin together.
  • The third sentence reports agreement across sources rather than attributing a single claim or contrasting two claims.

Reading Practice — Comprehension questions

  1. Source One identifies resident participation as the main reason for success; Source Two identifies continuous funding as the most influential factor.
  2. Both sources agree the project succeeded gradually; they diverge on which single factor mattered most.
  3. Model: تُؤَكِّد نَشْرَة الجَمْعِيَّة عَلَى دَوْر مُشَارَكَة السُّكَّان، فِي حِين أَنَّ تَقْرِير البَاحِثَة يُشِير إِلَى أَهَمِّيَّة التَّمْوِيل المُسْتَمِرّ.
  4. Model thesis: success came from the combination of resident participation and continuous funding, not from either factor alone.
  5. The claims about participation and funding are evidence-based (attributed to the sources); any claim that one factor is "more important overall" without source support would be personal opinion and should be labeled as such.

Chapter Review — Model responses

Section A: تَوْلِيف means bringing together the claims of more than one source into a single argument; أَطْرُوحَة names the essay's central claim; دَلِيل names the supporting evidence drawn from a source. Pattern 1 attributes a single claim to one source; Pattern 2 attributes claims to two sources and relates them with a contrast connector.

Section B: Open-ended production; any accurate synthesis sentence using Pattern 2, followed by a thesis statement that follows logically from it, is acceptable.

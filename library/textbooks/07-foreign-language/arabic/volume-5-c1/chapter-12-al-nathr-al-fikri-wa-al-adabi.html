النَّثْر الفِكْرِيّ وَالأَدَبِيّChapter 12: Intellectual and Literary Prose

Unit III Continuation · Speak and Write Arabic: Mastery, Precision, Rhetoric, and Specialized Expression

Chapter Overview

Chapter 11 gave هَنَاء the vocabulary of research method, review, and critique, and she used it to draft a background memo synthesizing two reports for the community association's board. Her supervisor liked the memo but had one further note: the writing was accurate but flat, and a version submitted for the association's public newsletter should read with more style. This chapter turns from the plain reporting register of Chapter 11 toward intellectual and literary prose — the vocabulary of style, metaphor, simile, symbol, and intertextual reference, along with the analytical skill of noticing how a writer's word choice, imagery, and echoes of other texts shape meaning. As in every chapter of this volume, no new grammar, case, or mood system is introduced; nominalization, content clauses, and passive voice already at mastery status from Chapter 5 carry the entire chapter. What is new is a lexical and interpretive domain — naming and analyzing figurative language rather than producing unfamiliar structures. هَنَاء will read a short literary-adjacent passage about the renovated neighborhood, identify its central image, and revise her own newsletter paragraph to use one deliberate figure of style.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Use core vocabulary for style, simile, metaphor, symbol, and intertextual reference in level-appropriate MSA.
  2. Identify and name a simile (تَشْبِيه) and a metaphor (اسْتِعَارَة) in a short literary-adjacent passage.
  3. Recognize when a text echoes an earlier text or well-known phrase (تَنَاصّ) and describe that echo in your own words.
  4. Revise a plain informational paragraph to include one deliberate, appropriate figure of style without overloading the text.
  5. Discuss a short literary-adjacent passage's central image in a structured, register-appropriate way.
  6. Identify what this chapter does not require: no formal literary theory, no analysis of full classical or Qur'anic texts, and no new grammar system.

Opening Communication Scenario

The community association wants a short piece for its quarterly newsletter celebrating the completed renovation project — something warmer and more evocative than the background memo هَنَاء wrote for the board. Her supervisor shares a short piece written by a local writer some years ago about an older, similar renovation elsewhere in the city, and suggests هَنَاء read it for a model of tone before drafting her own paragraph. The supervisor is careful to note that this is a modern piece of occasional prose, not a canonical literary work, and that هَنَاء's own paragraph should stay modest — one clear image, not an ornate performance.

صَوْت الأُسْلُوب المُتَوَازِي — The Sound of Parallel Style

The Sound of Parallel Style

Sound and Style — Reading balanced clauses aloud

No new sound or letter is introduced in this chapter. The useful skill is prosodic: literary-adjacent prose often uses two balanced clauses of similar length and structure, joined by وَ, to build rhythm — for example, pairing a statement about the old neighborhood with a parallel statement about the new one. A fluent reader gives each half of such a pair roughly equal weight and a brief pause at the joining وَ, letting the parallelism itself carry meaning rather than rushing through it as plain connected prose.

Practice reading the Reading Practice excerpt aloud, pausing briefly at each وَ that joins two balanced clauses, and notice how the pacing changes compared to the plain reporting style of Chapter 11.

تَسْمِيَة الصُّوَر دُون قَوَاعِد جَدِيدَة — Naming Figures Without New Grammar

Grammar Focus: Naming Figures Without New Grammar

No new clause type, case/mood system, or morphological pattern is introduced in this chapter. This section shows how nominalization and relative clauses — already consolidated — combine with this chapter's new style vocabulary to build the two core sentence patterns of literary-adjacent analysis: naming a figure of style, and explaining what it evokes.

Pattern 1: Naming a Figure Using a Relative Clause

[figure noun] الَّتِي / الَّذِي + [verb describing what the figure does]

the figure of style is named as a noun, then a relative clause — grammar already consolidated since Volume III — describes its function in the passage

الاسْتِعَارَة الَّتِي يَسْتَخْدِمُهَا الكَاتِب تُشَبِّه الحَيّ القَدِيم بِجَسَدٍ يَسْتَعِيد صِحَّتَه.

The metaphor the writer uses compares the old neighborhood to a body regaining its health.

Pattern 2: Explaining What a Figure Evokes (Nominalization + أَوْحَى بِـ)

[صُورَة / رَمْز] + تُوحِي بِـ / يُوحِي بِـ + [nominalized meaning]

the reporting-style verb أَوْحَى بِـ from this chapter's vocabulary takes a nominalized complement, exactly as content-clause and nominalization patterns already work

هَذِهِ الصُّورَة تُوحِي بِتَجَدُّد الحَيّ بَعْد سَنَوَات مِن الإِهْمَال.

This image suggests the neighborhood's renewal after years of neglect.

Notice that both patterns reuse structures already at mastery status — relative clauses and nominalization — and simply attach this chapter's new style and figurative-language vocabulary to them. Literary-adjacent analysis is therefore built almost entirely from prior grammar plus new vocabulary, consistent with this volume's load-control principle.

النَّصّ النَّمُوذَجِيّ — Model Passage (adapted occasional prose)

Writing Workshop

Revise the plain informational paragraph you might have written for a newsletter into a short piece (four to six sentences) that uses one deliberate figure of style, modeled on the passage in Reading Practice above.

  1. Step 1 — Choose one image. Select a single simile, metaphor, or symbol that fits the renovation project; do not attempt more than one central figure.
  2. Step 2 — Draft a plain version first. Write the paragraph's information in plain reporting style, as you would have for Chapter 11's memo.
  3. Step 3 — Introduce the figure. Revise one or two sentences to carry your chosen image, using Pattern 1 or Pattern 2 above.
  4. Step 4 — Add one balanced pair of clauses. Join two short, parallel clauses with وَ to give the paragraph a calmer rhythm, as modeled in the excerpt.
  5. Review your work against the checklist below.

الغَرَض / Purpose: فِقْرَة قَصِيرَة لِلنَّشْرَة الفَصْلِيَّة لِلْجَمْعِيَّة الأَهْلِيَّة

"The renewal our neighborhood has seen over the past two years was not a single event, but rather more like a long breath returning gradually to streets that had forgotten their old glow. The facades changed, and with them the residents' confidence in their neighborhood's future changed too. And today, when a visitor walks through these streets, they sense that the neighborhood has recovered something it had begun to lose: its sense of itself."

  • The paragraph uses exactly one central figure of style, not several competing images.
  • At least one pair of balanced, parallel clauses appears, joined by وَ.
  • The figure is named or implied clearly enough that a reader could identify it without help.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Occasional prose as everyday writing

Short pieces of warm, image-driven occasional prose — for newsletters, community bulletins, commemorative articles, and local media — are common across Arabic-speaking communities and draw lightly on literary conventions without requiring formal literary training. This chapter's vocabulary and one-figure discipline reflect that everyday genre; they do not equip learners to analyze or produce sustained literary criticism or creative fiction.

Heritage Connection — Where figurative language in MSA comes from

Many figures of style used in contemporary MSA prose — including balanced parallel clauses and certain image patterns — have roots in a long tradition of Arabic rhetoric that predates modern journalism and occasional writing. This chapter does not analyze that classical tradition directly; it is introduced here only as background context and remains a receptive/analytical topic. Chapter 13 turns to a small set of heritage and Qur'anic-adjacent texts with explicit, bounded treatment, clearly separated from the modern productive model used in this chapter.

Regional Variation — Style expectations in occasional writing

Expectations for how much figurative language is appropriate in a newsletter, bulletin, or commemorative article vary by country, publication, and audience; some regional traditions favor more elaborate imagery than others even within a similar genre. This chapter's one-figure, restrained-style guidance is a safe default for learners, not a universal rule for all Arabic-language occasional writing.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Common Mistake — Stacking multiple competing images

Error: introducing a body-image metaphor, then a tree metaphor, then a dawn metaphor, all within the same short paragraph, so no single image controls the piece.

Correction: choose one central figure and let it govern the paragraph, as modeled in the Writing Workshop sample.

Common Mistake — Treating figurative language as decoration bolted onto plain prose

Error: writing a plain informational paragraph and then adding an unrelated flowery sentence at the end that does not connect to the paragraph's content.

Correction: let the chosen figure organize the paragraph's structure and word choice throughout, not appear only as an isolated final flourish.

Common Mistake — Reaching for unfamiliar grammar to sound more literary

Error: attempting an unfamiliar clause structure, archaic form, or elevated vocabulary item not covered in this volume, on the assumption that literary writing requires new grammar.

Correction: build the sentence from relative clauses, content clauses, and nominalization already consolidated, adding only this chapter's new style vocabulary.

Chapter Review — Model responses

Section A: تَشْبِيه compares two things using a comparison word (like "like" or "as"); اسْتِعَارَة identifies one thing directly as another without a comparison word; رَمْز is an image or object that stands for a larger idea. Naming a figure identifies what it is (for example, "this is a simile"); explaining what it evokes states the meaning or association it creates (for example, "it suggests gradual change").

Section B: Open-ended production; any accurate simile or metaphor plus a genuine explanatory sentence using أَوْحَى بِـ or يُوحِي بِـ is acceptable.

Faire les magasins

Shopping · Speak and Write French: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Name common clothing, accessories, and everyday goods using the correct article and gender.
  2. Use numbers 70–1,000 to understand and state prices in euros and other Francophone currencies.
  3. Describe items by size, color, and material using adjectives with correct gender and number agreement.
  4. Ask for an item and complete a basic shopping exchange using appropriate courtesy language.
  5. Express a preference and compare two items using plus, moins, and aussi.
  6. Use direct-object pronouns le, la, l', and les to refer back to a named object without repeating it.
  7. Place a direct-object pronoun correctly before a finite verb in affirmative and negative sentences.
  8. Ask about prices with combien and Ça coûte combien ?
  9. Accept, decline, or negotiate a purchase using natural shopping expressions.
  10. Write a short comparison of two products and make a purchase recommendation.

Communication Scenario

You are shopping at a weekend market in Montréal with a friend visiting from France. You want to find a gift for a family member — something practical and not too expensive. You pick up two options, compare them aloud, ask the vendor about price and availability, and eventually decide on one. Your friend offers opinions and asks questions. At the end, you complete the purchase. This chapter gives you the language to carry out exactly this kind of real shopping interaction — asking about items, comparing choices, handling prices, and referring back to things you have already mentioned without repeating their full names every time.

  1. Think of the last time you bought something in a store or at a market. What questions did you ask? What information did you need — about size, color, price, or material? Make a quick list in English, then consider which words you might already know in French and which are new to you.
  2. In English, when you have already mentioned an object, you avoid repeating it by saying "it" or "them": "I like this jacket — I'll take it." French does the same thing with direct-object pronouns, but places them before the verb rather than after. This chapter will show you how that works and give you plenty of practice.

Les nombres 70–1 000 — Numbers 70–1,000

These numbers are indispensable for prices and quantities. The French system from 70 onwards differs from most European neighbors and varies in parts of the Francophone world — see the regional note below.

Key spelling points: (1) Quatre-vingts (80) carries a final -s only when it stands alone as a round number: quatre-vingts. When followed by another digit, the -s disappears: quatre-vingt-un, quatre-vingt-deux. (2) Cent (100) similarly takes a final -s only in round multiples that are not followed by another number: deux cents but deux cent un. (3) Mille (1,000) is invariable — never *milles. (4) French uses a space (or period) where English uses a comma as a thousands separator: 1 000 or 1.000 for 1,000. (5) Decimals use a comma: 9,99 € = €9.99.

Regional Variation — Numbers in Belgium, Switzerland, and Quebec

The French number system from 70 to 99 is the result of a vigesimal (base-20) counting tradition that France, Monaco, and some other communities preserved. Belgium, Switzerland, and parts of the Democratic Republic of Congo use simpler forms that you will encounter if you interact with speakers from those communities:

  • 70 → septante (Belgium, Switzerland) instead of soixante-dix
  • 80 → huitante or octante (Switzerland) instead of quatre-vingts
  • 90 → nonante (Belgium, Switzerland) instead of quatre-vingt-dix

These forms are completely correct within their communities and are not informal. When shopping in Brussels or Geneva, you may hear a price stated as nonante euros rather than quatre-vingt-dix euros. Neither form is superior; the core instructional model for this course uses the France-based system. Both are understood across the Francophone world.

In Quebec, soixante-dix, quatre-vingts, and quatre-vingt-dix are the standard forms, consistent with France.

Pronunciation and Spelling

Mid vowels in numbers and shopping vocabulary

Several key words in this chapter contain mid-vowel sounds — the area between a fully open vowel like [a] and a fully closed vowel like [i] or [u]. French distinguishes these mid-vowel positions more precisely than English typically requires, and prices and number words are particularly rich in them.

Sound Focus — Mid vowels in number and price vocabulary

Compare these sounds in chapter vocabulary:

  • soixante — the first vowel oi is a glide plus mid-vowel sequence [wa]. The -an- in the second syllable is a nasal vowel. The final -te is a reduced vowel plus released [t]. Do not stress any single syllable heavily.
  • quatre-vingts — the a in quatre is a front open vowel. The -in in vingts is a nasal vowel [ɛ̃]. The written -s and -t are both silent.
  • cent — a single nasal vowel [ɑ̃]. Do not add a final [t] sound; the t is silent in isolation.
  • euros — the eu is a front rounded vowel, the same sound introduced in Chapter 8. The -ros adds [ʁo]; the final -s is silent except in liaison contexts.
  • cher / chère — the masculine cher ends in a mid-front vowel [e] (approximately the sound in French été). The feminine chère uses the more open [ɛ] as in fête. This distinction is part of a mid-vowel contrast that some speakers maintain and others merge in casual speech. Recognize both.

Final consonants in numbers

Number words have some of the most irregular final-consonant behavior in French, and getting them right matters enormously in shopping contexts where a misheard number changes the price. Study these patterns carefully:

  • cent — final -t is silent in isolation and before a consonant: cent euros [sɑ̃ øʁo]. But cent before a vowel-initial noun produces enchaînement: cent ans [sɑ̃.tɑ̃].
  • quatre-vingts — final -s of vingts is silent: quatre-vingts euros. But quatre-vingt-huit — the final t of vingt in certain combinations can be pronounced; this is variable and best observed from native audio.
  • deux cents — the -s of cents is silent before a consonant. Before a vowel, liaison may produce [s]: deux cents euros [dø sɑ̃ øʁo] with no liaison before the vowel-initial euros.
  • mille — the final -e is mute; mille is one syllable: [mil].

Plural liaison in controlled contexts

This chapter introduces an important productive liaison context: plural subject pronouns and plural determiners before vowel-initial nouns. This liaison is mandatory in the forms below. You have already seen it with nous avons (Chapter 4) and mes amis (Chapter 4). Here are the shopping-relevant examples:

  • les écharpes — pronounced [le.ze.ʃaʁp]: the -s of les links to écharpes.
  • des accessoires — pronounced [de.zak.se.swaʁ]: the -s of des links to accessoires.
  • Ils ont de beaux articles. — the liaison of ils before ont is mandatory: [il.zɔ̃].

Liaison is never made between a singular noun and the following adjective: un manteau élégant — no liaison from manteau to élégant. This is one of the forbidden-liaison contexts introduced for recognition. Do not say un manteau-z-élégant.

Object-pronoun and verb grouping

When a direct-object pronoun precedes a verb, the two form a single tight rhythmic unit: the pronoun is not stressed and cannot stand alone. In connected speech, Je le prends sounds like one phrase: [ʒəl.pʁɑ̃], not three separate words with equal weight. Practice saying these as a single arc:

  • Je le prends. — [ʒəl.pʁɑ̃]  →  "I'll take it."
  • Je la cherche. — [ʒəla.ʃɛʁʃ]  →  "I'm looking for it (f.)."
  • Je les prends. — [ʒele.pʁɑ̃]  →  "I'll take them." (liaison: les + verb)
  • Je ne le prends pas. — the ne and le wrap around the verb: [ʒənəl.pʁɑ̃.pa].

Spelling — number conventions and currency

Prices in French follow typographic conventions that differ from English:

  • The euro symbol comes after the amount in most European French usage: 29,99 € or 29 euros 99.
  • Decimals use a comma, not a period: 9,50 € = €9.50.
  • Thousands are separated by a space (not a comma): 1 200 €.
  • When writing out numbers as words in shopping contexts, apply the spelling rules for -s on vingts and cents carefully.

Grammar Instruction

9.1 Adjective agreement and placement — expansion

Purpose: describe clothing and goods accurately by applying gender and number agreement to a wider range of adjectives, and recognize which adjectives precede and which follow the noun.

You have been practicing adjective agreement since Chapter 2. In a shopping context, you need to apply it fluently to describe items: color, size, material quality, and appearance. The vast majority of descriptive adjectives in French follow the noun. A small group — including several encountered in earlier chapters — precede it. This chapter consolidates both patterns.

Adjectives that typically follow the noun (the default position):

  • une robe verte a green dress
  • un manteau chaud a warm coat
  • des chaussures confortables comfortable shoes
  • une chemise rayée a striped shirt — rayé(e) = striped
  • un pull léger / légère a lightweight sweater — irregular feminine

Common adjectives that precede the noun:

  • un beau sac / un bel imperméable a beautiful bag / a beautiful raincoat
  • une belle veste a beautiful jacket
  • un grand chapeau / une grande écharpe a large hat / a long scarf
  • un petit sac / une petite ceinture a small bag / a small belt
  • un nouveau jean / un nouvel article a new pair of jeans / a new item
  • une nouvelle robe a new dress
  • un vieux blouson / un vieil imperméable an old jacket / an old raincoat
  • une vieille montre an old watch

Nouveau follows the same three-form pattern as beau and vieux: masculine before consonant (nouveau), masculine before vowel or mute h (nouvel), and feminine (nouvelle). You may also see it as nouveau in compound nouns like Nouvel An (New Year).

Some adjectives change meaning depending on position:

Before nounAfter noun
un grand homme — a great man (important)un homme grand — a tall man (height)
un certain prix — a certain price (indefinite)un prix certain — a definite price (sure)

At this stage, the key practical rule is: when describing size, color, material, or physical appearance, place the adjective after the noun. The pre-noun group (beau, nouveau, vieux, grand, petit, jeune) should be learned as a set by observation and practice.

Practice — adjective agreement and placement

  1. Describe each item with the adjective given. Place it correctly and make it agree.   (a) une robe / long   (b) un manteau / beau   (c) des chaussures / confortable   (d) un sac / nouveau (before vowel?)   (e) une écharpe / grand   (f) des lunettes / élégant.
  2. Correct the adjective agreement errors: (a) *une veste vert   (b) *un beau imperméable   (c) *des chaussures blanc.
  3. Write three sentences describing items you might buy as gifts. Use one pre-noun adjective, one post-noun adjective, and one color adjective. Pay attention to gender throughout.

9.2 Direct-object pronouns — le, la, l', les

Purpose: avoid repeating a noun that has already been mentioned by replacing it with a direct-object pronoun placed before the verb.

A direct object is a noun that receives the action of a verb directly, without a preposition: in J'achète ce pull (I am buying this sweater), ce pull is the direct object. Once you and your interlocutor both know which item is being discussed, repeating its full name every time sounds unnatural — in English you would say "it" or "them." French uses the pronouns le, la, l', and les.

PronomCorresponds toExampleEnglish
lemasculine singular nounTu aimes ce pull ? — Oui, je le prends.Do you like this sweater? — Yes, I'll take it.
lafeminine singular nounTu vois cette robe ? — Je la trouve belle.Do you see this dress? — I find it beautiful.
l'any singular noun before a vowel or mute hCette écharpe ? Je l'adore !This scarf? I love it!
lesany plural nounCes chaussures ? Je les achète.These shoes? I'm buying them.
Language Focus

The critical difference from English: In French, the direct-object pronoun goes before the conjugated verb, not after it. English says "I like it"; French says "Je le aime" — which actually becomes Je l'aime because le elides before the vowel of aime. This placement is non-negotiable in French grammar. The pronoun slots in immediately before the verb it belongs to.

In a negative sentence, the pronoun remains between ne and the verb: Je ne le prends pas. The pronoun does not move to the other side of pas.

  • Ce jean ? Je le cherche en noir. This pair of jeans? I'm looking for it in black. le = le jean (masc. sg.)
  • Cette veste ? Je l'aime beaucoup, mais je ne la prends pas — elle est trop chère. This jacket? I like it a lot, but I won't take it — it's too expensive. l' = elision of la before aime; la = la veste in the second clause
  • Ces baskets ? Tu les trouves confortables ? These trainers? Do you find them comfortable? les = les baskets (fem. pl.)
  • J'aime ce sac. Je le prends. I like this bag. I'll take it. le = le sac (masc. sg.)

9.3 Basic comparison — plus, moins, aussi

Purpose: compare two items when shopping by saying one is more, less, or equally [adjective] than the other.

French uses three simple structures to compare two things. The adjective in all three structures agrees in gender and number with the first noun being described.

StructureMeaningExample
plus + adjective + quemore … thanCe manteau est plus chaud que ce blouson.
moins + adjective + queless … thanCette veste est moins chère que ce manteau.
aussi + adjective + queas … asCes baskets sont aussi confortables que ces bottes.
  • Ce pull en laine est plus chaud que ce pull en coton. This wool sweater is warmer than this cotton sweater.
  • La robe verte est moins chère que la robe bleue. The green dress is less expensive than the blue dress.
  • Ce sac est aussi grand que l'autre. This bag is as large as the other one.
Grammar Note — bon / bien and their irregular comparatives

The adjective bon(ne) (good) and the adverb bien (well) have irregular comparative forms that do not use plus:

  • bon(ne) (good) → meilleur(e) (better): Cette qualité est meilleure. — This quality is better.
  • bien (well) → mieux (better, as an adverb): Ça me va mieux. — That suits me better.

Do not say *plus bon or *plus bien — these forms are not used in standard French. Meilleur(e) is an adjective and agrees in gender and number: une meilleure qualité. Mieux is an adverb and does not agree. You will see both forms again in Chapter 14 when comparison is expanded; for now, learn them as shopping vocabulary: C'est de meilleure qualité (It's better quality) and Ça m'irait mieux en bleu (It would suit me better in blue).

Practice — comparison

  1. Write a comparison sentence using the cue. Make all agreements correctly.
    (a) ce manteau / chaud / ce blouson (plus)   (b) ces bottes / chères / ces baskets (moins)   (c) ce sac en cuir / élégant / ce sac en coton (plus)   (d) cette robe / longue / cette jupe (aussi).
  2. In one or two French sentences, compare two items you are considering buying. Mention the price, a quality, and your final preference. Use a direct-object pronoun in your concluding sentence: Je le / la prends.

9.4 Combien and price questions

Purpose: ask about price and understand the answer using the vocabulary and structures taught in this chapter.

The word combien (how much / how many) can form price questions in several ways. Each is correct; they differ slightly in register:

  • Ça coûte combien ? How much does it cost? (informal, most frequent in everyday shopping)
  • C'est combien ? How much is it? (very common, informal)
  • Combien ça coûte ? How much does it cost? (neutral)
  • Combien coûte cette veste ? How much does this jacket cost? (names the item, slightly more formal)
  • Quel est le prix ? What is the price? (more formal)

Answering a price question:

  • Ça coûte vingt-neuf euros cinquante. It costs twenty-nine euros fifty.
  • C'est quatre-vingt-cinq euros. It is eighty-five euros.
  • Le manteau coûte deux cent dix euros. The coat costs two hundred and ten euros.
  • C'est trop cher pour moi. It's too expensive for me.
  • C'est bon marché. It's inexpensive.

Model Dialogue

Contexte : Un marché de fin de semaine à Montréal. Nadia (cliente, Montréal) et son ami Sébastien (visiteur de Bordeaux) regardent des vêtements chez une vendeuse. Registre : conversation informelle avec la vendeuse, vous envers la vendeuse, tu entre amis.
Setting: A weekend market in Montréal. Nadia (customer, Montréal) and her friend Sébastien (visitor from Bordeaux) are looking at clothing with a vendor. Register: informal conversation; vous to the vendor, tu between friends.

NADIA Sébastien, regarde cette écharpe en laine — elle est magnifique ! Sébastien, look at this wool scarf — it's beautiful!
SÉBASTIEN Oui, elle est vraiment belle. Tu la prends pour qui ? Yes, it's really beautiful. Who are you buying it for?
NADIA Pour ma mère. Elle adore les écharpes. Excusez-moi, madame — cette écharpe rouge, ça coûte combien ? For my mother. She loves scarves. Excuse me, madam — how much does this red scarf cost?
VENDEUSE Elle coûte trente-cinq dollars, madame. C'est de la laine pure du Québec. It costs thirty-five dollars, madam. It's pure Quebec wool.
NADIA Vous avez ça en bleu aussi ? Do you have it in blue as well?
VENDEUSE Oui, voilà. En bleu marine et en bleu ciel. Yes, here. In navy blue and in sky blue.
SÉBASTIEN Laquelle est plus belle, selon toi — la rouge ou la bleu marine ? Which one is more beautiful, in your opinion — the red one or the navy blue one?
NADIA Je trouve la bleue plus élégante. Et ma mère aime le bleu. Je la prends ! I find the blue one more elegant. And my mother loves blue. I'll take it!
Nadia regarde aussi un sac. / Nadia also looks at a bag.
NADIA Et ce sac en cuir — c'est combien ? And this leather bag — how much is it?
VENDEUSE Celui-là, c'est quatre-vingt-dix dollars. C'est un très beau sac, fait à la main. That one is ninety dollars. It's a very beautiful bag, handmade.
NADIA Oh, c'est un peu cher pour moi. Ce petit sac là — il est moins cher ? Oh, that's a bit expensive for me. That small bag over there — is it less expensive?
VENDEUSE Oui, il coûte cinquante dollars. Yes, it costs fifty dollars.
NADIA Je réfléchis… Non, finalement je ne le prends pas. Juste l'écharpe. Vous acceptez les cartes de crédit ? I'll think about it… No, in the end I won't take it. Just the scarf. Do you accept credit cards?
VENDEUSE Bien sûr ! Voilà votre reçu. Bonne journée ! Of course! Here is your receipt. Have a good day!

Questions de compréhension — Comprehension questions:

  1. Pour qui est l'écharpe ? (Who is the scarf for?)
  2. Quel est le prix de l'écharpe ? Et le prix du grand sac en cuir ? (What is the price of the scarf? And the price of the large leather bag?)
  3. Quelle écharpe Nadia choisit-elle finalement — et pourquoi ? (Which scarf does Nadia choose in the end — and why?)
  4. Pourquoi Nadia ne prend-elle pas le grand sac ? (Why doesn't Nadia take the large bag?)
  5. Relevez trois exemples de pronoms compléments directs dans le dialogue. Identifiez le nom qu'ils remplacent. (Find three examples of direct-object pronouns in the dialogue. Identify the noun they replace.)
  6. Comment est-ce que Nadia paie ? (How does Nadia pay?)
Grammar Note — Celui-là in the dialogue

The vendor says Celui-là (That one [masc.]) when referring back to the leather bag. This is a demonstrative pronoun — a pronoun that stands in for a noun + demonstrative determiner. Celui is the masculine singular form, celle the feminine, ceux the masculine plural, and celles the feminine plural. The suffixes -ci (this one, near) and -là (that one, over there) can be added. At this stage, treat celui-là / celle-là as useful recognition vocabulary for shopping interactions. Full productive instruction in demonstrative pronouns is beyond the scope of this chapter — do not use these forms in your own writing exercises unless you are confident they apply correctly.

Reading Note — Currency and shopping across the Francophone world

The three texts represent three different currencies and shopping contexts. France and Belgium both use the euro (€), making cross-border price comparison straightforward. Many countries in West and Central Africa use the CFA franc (franc CFA), a currency used by fourteen nations; there are two separate CFA franc zones with different central banks but the same name. In 2024, approximately 650 CFA francs were equivalent to one euro, so 15,000 CFA francs for a dress is roughly €23. In Canada, prices are in Canadian dollars ($ or CAD), which are worth less than US dollars. When reading prices in French from different regions, always note the currency. The word franc is also used in Switzerland (franc suisse, CHF) and is distinct from the CFA franc.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Shopping customs across the Francophone world

Shopping interactions in French-speaking communities share some expectations and differ in others. In France and Belgium, it is customary — and socially important — to greet a vendor or shopkeeper with Bonjour ! before asking for anything. Walking into a small shop or market stall and immediately stating what you want without a greeting can come across as abrupt. The same applies in many Francophone African contexts where greetings are extended and elaborate. In large department stores and supermarkets, greetings are less obligatory but still common among staff.

Bargaining (marchander) is common and expected in open-air markets across much of Francophone Africa, the Caribbean, and parts of North Africa. In French and Belgian boutiques and standard shops, prices are generally fixed. Asking for a discount (Vous pouvez faire un geste ? — Can you offer a reduction? / Literally: Can you make a gesture?) is acceptable in some market and independent-boutique contexts, but is not a standard expectation in chain stores. In Quebec, the culture of fixed prices in retail stores closely resembles North American norms, and market bargaining is rare.

Culture in Context — Sizing systems

French clothing and shoe sizes differ from North American and UK systems. A French women's size 38 corresponds roughly to a US size 8 or UK size 10. French shoe size 40 corresponds approximately to UK 6.5 or US women's 8.5. When shopping in France or other European Francophone countries, you will need to know your European size. When helping someone shop, asking Vous faites quelle taille ? (What size do you take?) is completely natural and not considered intrusive in a shopping context — unlike the potentially sensitive age question practiced in Chapter 4. Knowing your size in the local system is a practical communication skill.

Regional Variation — Shopping vocabulary

A few common clothing and shopping terms have recognizable regional alternatives:

  • In France, les baskets is the most common term for trainers or sneakers. In Quebec, les souliers de sport or les espadrilles may be used instead.
  • The word un chandail (a sweater) is widely used in Quebec where French speakers from France would say un pull.
  • In some West African markets, you may encounter prices stated partly in informal local units or in a mix of French and local languages. This is normal in multilingual commercial contexts.
  • The expression faire du lèche-vitrine (literally "licking windows") means "window shopping" and is used throughout the Francophone world.

The productive forms in this course follow the France-based model. Recognize regional variants without adopting them unless you are in a context where they are the local standard.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Placing the object pronoun after the verb

Error: *Je prends le / Je prends la / Je aime it (applying English word order).

Correction: Je le prends. / Je la prends. / Je l'aime. The direct-object pronoun in French always comes before the conjugated verb. This is the single most important rule about object pronouns. There are no exceptions at this level. Every time you want to say "it" or "them" as a direct object in French, the pronoun moves to the left of the verb. Write the verb first in your head, then slide the pronoun to its left.

Common Mistake — Wrong gender for the object pronoun

Error: *Cette robe ? Je le trouve belle. (using le for a feminine noun)

Correction: Cette robe ? Je la trouve belle. The direct-object pronoun must match the gender of the noun it replaces. La robe is feminine, so the pronoun is la. Always check: what noun am I replacing? What is its gender? Only then choose the pronoun. A useful self-check: if the noun requires le or la as its article, use the same form as the object pronoun.

Common Mistake — Forgetting to drop -s from quatre-vingts before a following digit

Error: *quatre-vingts-cinq euros

Correction: quatre-vingt-cinq euros. The -s on vingts appears only when the number stands alone as a round 80: Il a quatre-vingts ans (He is eighty years old) — but quatre-vingt-cinq euros (eighty-five euros). The same rule applies to cents: deux cents (200) but deux cent dix (210). Think of the -s as marking a "complete" round number — once another digit follows, the round is not complete and the -s disappears.

Common Mistake — Using plus bon instead of meilleur

Error: *Ce pull est plus bon que ce t-shirt.

Correction: Ce pull est meilleur que ce t-shirt. The adjective bon(ne) (good) has an irregular comparative: meilleur(e) (better). The form *plus bon does not exist in standard French. Learn the pair together: bon → meilleur / bonne → meilleure. The same applies to the adverb bien: its comparative is mieux, not *plus bien: Ça me va mieux (That suits me better).

Common Mistake — Putting a negative pronoun in the wrong position

Error: *Je prends ne le pas. / *Je ne prends le pas.

Correction: Je ne le prends pas. In a negative sentence with an object pronoun, the word order is: subject + ne + object pronoun + verb + pas. The pronoun stays attached to the left of the verb even in negation. Think of ne … pas as a frame that wraps around the pronoun-plus-verb unit: ne [le prends] pas. The pronoun never moves to the right of pas.

Common Mistake — Forgetting to use en with materials

Error: *une chemise de coton / *une chemise coton

Correction: une chemise en coton. When describing what material something is made of, French uses the preposition en with no article: en coton, en laine, en cuir, en soie. This is different from English "of" or "made of" and from the French de used in other contexts. Treat en + material as a fixed phrase pattern. Do not insert an article between en and the material name.

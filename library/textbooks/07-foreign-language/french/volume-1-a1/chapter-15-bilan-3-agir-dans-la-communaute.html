Unit III Assessment · Speak and Write French: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

This chapter does not introduce new major grammar. By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Ask for and give directions, and understand a simple route description.
  2. Use the imperative to give instructions and basic advice.
  3. Refer to a previously named place using y.
  4. Discuss leisure activities, weather, and plans, and use on appropriately in conversational contexts.
  5. Make, negotiate, accept, and decline an invitation or plan using the near future.
  6. Describe a common health problem using avoir mal à and respond to simple, non-diagnostic advice.
  7. Express necessity, ability, and obligation with il faut, pouvoir, and devoir.
  8. Discuss school or work situations, compare two options, and use indirect-object pronouns lui and leur.
  9. Distinguish direct-object from indirect-object pronoun use in context.
  10. Use the relative pronouns qui, que, and où to combine simple sentences.
  11. Write a short, appropriately registered message that draws on several of these systems together.
  12. Complete linked listening, speaking, reading, and writing tasks that combine community-based language from Chapters 11–14.

Communication Scenario

You are spending a week in a French-speaking city as part of a study or work exchange. Over the course of one day, you need to find your way to an appointment, explain a minor health concern when you arrive late, catch up with a friend about weekend plans, and then send a short follow-up message about a school or work matter before the day ends. None of this requires new grammar — it requires combining what you already know from Chapters 11 through 14, smoothly and under realistic conditions.

  1. Before you begin, think back over Unit III. Which of the four chapters — directions, leisure and plans, health, or school and work — do you feel most confident with? Which feels shakiest? Use that honest assessment to decide where to spend extra review time in this chapter.
  2. This chapter mixes registers: giving someone directions on the street, chatting informally with a friend about the weekend, describing a symptom to a pharmacist, and writing a short professional-toned message. Pay attention to how your choice of tu or vous, and your vocabulary, shifts across these four situations.

Grammar Review

This Bilan reactivates the major grammar systems from Chapters 11–14. No new system is introduced here. Use the tables and notes below to refresh your memory before moving into the practice sections.

11.1 Aller, venir, and the imperative

Purpose: ask where something is, give and follow directions, and issue basic instructions or advice.

  • Tournez à gauche, puis continuez tout droit. Turn left, then continue straight ahead. (vous-imperative)
  • Prends la rue Victor-Hugo. Take Victor-Hugo street. (tu-imperative)
  • Allons-y ! Let's go! (nous-imperative, formulaic with y)

Remember: the imperative drops the subject pronoun but keeps the verb ending. For -er verbs, the tu-form imperative drops the final -s: tu parles → Parle !

11.2 Y for a previously named place

Purpose: avoid repeating a place name once it has already been established.

  • Tu vas à la pharmacie ? — Oui, j'y vais. Are you going to the pharmacy? — Yes, I'm going (there).
  • On y va ? Shall we go (there)? — common formula for "shall we go?"

Y is placed directly before the conjugated verb, just like a direct- or indirect-object pronoun.

12.1 On and nous

Purpose: refer to a group inclusively in spoken French, and recognize the relationship between on and nous.

On is extremely common in everyday spoken French as an inclusive way of saying "we." It always takes the il/elle form of the verb, even though its meaning is plural: On va au cinéma ce soir. (We're going to the movies tonight.) Nous remains correct and is more common in writing and formal speech, but on is the natural choice in ordinary conversation among peers.

12.2 The near future: aller + infinitive

Purpose: say what you or someone else is going to do.

Language Building Block
Subject + conjugated aller + infinitive
Present-tense form of aller · unconjugated verb that names the planned action
On va faire une randonnée samedi, s'il fait beau.
We're going to go hiking Saturday, if the weather's nice.

13.1 Il faut, pouvoir, and devoir

Purpose: express necessity, ability, and obligation, especially around health and practical advice.

StructureUseExample
il faut + infinitiveimpersonal necessityIl faut prendre rendez-vous.
pouvoir + infinitiveability or permissionVous pouvez attendre ici.
devoir + infinitiveobligationJe dois voir un médecin.

Avoir mal à + body part, with the contracted article when required (au, à la, à l’, aux), remains the core structure for describing pain: J’ai mal à la tête. J’ai mal au dos. J’ai mal aux pieds.

14.1 Indirect-object pronouns lui and leur

Purpose: refer to a person who receives an action, without repeating their name.

  • Je téléphone à mon professeur. → Je lui téléphone. I'm calling my teacher. → I'm calling him/her.
  • Elle écrit à ses collègues. → Elle leur écrit. She is writing to her colleagues. → She is writing to them.

Direct objects answer "what/whom" with no preposition (le, la, l', les); indirect objects answer "to whom" and typically follow a verb that takes à + person (lui, leur). Compare: Je le connais (I know him — direct) versus Je lui parle (I'm talking to him — indirect).

14.2 Relative pronouns qui, que, and où

Purpose: combine two short sentences into one connected sentence by referring back to a noun.

PronounFunctionExample
quisubject of the following verbJ'ai un collègue qui parle trois langues.
queobject of the following verbC'est le projet que je préfère.
oùplace or time referenceC'est l'école où j'étudie le français.

Pronunciation and Orthography Review

Liaison versus enchaînement

Across Chapters 11–14 you practiced both liaison (pronouncing a normally silent final consonant before a vowel-initial word) and enchaînement (resyllabifying an already-pronounced final consonant onto the next vowel-initial syllable). These remain distinct: vous_avez is liaison (the -s of vous is not pronounced before a consonant), while elle arrive is enchaînement (the -le of elle is always pronounced, and simply links forward).

Sound Focus — Phrase grouping in directions and invitations

Direction-giving and invitation language both rely on clear phrase grouping with prominence on the final syllable of each group: tout-droit-jusqu'au-FEU, ça-te-dit-sa-MEDI. Imperatives carry a slightly falling, decisive intonation contour, while invitations typically rise gently at the end, inviting a response.

French r, nasal vowels, and front rounded vowels

Continue applying the French r in high-frequency words like rue, droite, and rendez-vous, produced without English-style retroflexion. Nasal vowels remain frequent in this unit's vocabulary (rendez-vous, collègue... non, combien), and front rounded vowels appear in words such as professeur and peut. Review Chapter 13's guidance on /y/ versus /u/ if needed.

Orthography checkpoints

  • Imperative spelling: -er verbs drop the final -s in the tu-form imperative (tourne, not tournes).
  • Contractions with à: au, à la, à l', aux — required correctly in avoir mal à constructions.
  • Complete written negation remains standard in formal writing: Je ne peux pas venir, not the spoken-only Je peux pas venir.
  • Accents on à, élève, collègue, and other high-frequency words from this unit must be preserved in writing.

Model Dialogue

Contexte : Léa (en échange à Lausanne, Suisse) arrive en retard à un rendez-vous chez le médecin parce qu'elle s'est perdue. Elle parle avec la réceptionniste, puis avec son ami Karim au téléphone. Registre : vous avec la réceptionniste, tu avec Karim.
Setting: Léa (on exchange in Lausanne, Switzerland) arrives late to a doctor's appointment because she got lost. She speaks with the receptionist, then with her friend Karim on the phone. Register: vous with the receptionist, tu with Karim.

LÉA Bonjour, excusez-moi, je suis en retard — j'ai eu du mal à trouver la rue. Hello, excuse me, I'm late — I had trouble finding the street.
RÉCEPT. Ce n'est pas grave. Vous pouvez vous asseoir ; le docteur va vous recevoir bientôt. Qu'est-ce qui ne va pas ? That's all right. You can sit down; the doctor will see you soon. What's wrong?
LÉA J'ai mal à la gorge depuis trois jours, et j'ai un peu mal à la tête aussi. I've had a sore throat for three days, and I have a bit of a headache too.
RÉCEPT. D'accord. Il faut remplir cette fiche, s'il vous plaît. All right. You need to fill out this form, please.
Plus tard, Léa téléphone à Karim depuis la salle d'attente. / Later, Léa calls Karim from the waiting room.
KARIM Salut Léa ! Ça va ? Tu es chez le médecin ? Hi Léa! How's it going? Are you at the doctor's?
LÉA Oui, j'y suis. Ce n'est rien de grave, j'ai mal à la gorge. Au fait, ça te dit, le concert de samedi ? Yes, I'm here. It's nothing serious, I have a sore throat. By the way, are you up for the concert on Saturday?
KARIM Ah, j'aimerais bien, mais je dois finir un projet pour mon prof. Il faut que je lui envoie le rapport lundi. Ah, I'd really like to, but I have to finish a project for my professor. I need to send him the report Monday.
LÉA Dommage ! C'est le projet que tu fais avec ta collègue Sophie ? Too bad! Is that the project you're doing with your colleague Sophie?
KARIM Oui, c'est ça. On va peut-être finir dimanche. On verra. Et toi, repose-toi bien ! Yes, that's it. We might finish Sunday. We'll see. And you, rest well!
LÉA Merci, à bientôt ! Thanks, see you soon!

Questions de compréhension — Comprehension questions:

  1. Pourquoi Léa est-elle en retard ? (Why is Léa late?)
  2. Quels sont ses symptômes ? (What are her symptoms?)
  3. Pourquoi Karim ne peut-il pas aller au concert ? (Why can't Karim go to the concert?)
  4. Relevez un exemple de y, un exemple de lui, et un exemple de que dans le dialogue. (Find one example each of y, lui, and que.)

Reading Note — Workplace messaging across the Francophone world

Short written exchanges between colleagues are common across Francophone workplaces, whether by text message, instant messaging platform, or email, and tend to mix politeness with brevity. The closing bon courage (roughly "good luck" or "hang in there") is a common, warm way to acknowledge a colleague's minor difficulty without overstating it. Workplace norms around schedule flexibility, like swapping shifts informally between colleagues, vary considerably by sector, country, and employer.

Writing Workshop

In this chapter you will write a short, connected message that combines at least three of the four Unit III domains: directions, plans, health, or school/work.

  1. Study the model. Reread the Yasmine–Paul exchange above. Notice how it moves naturally between a practical request (covering a shift), directions, a health mention, and a plan for the following week — all in a friendly but efficient register.
  2. Plan your message. Choose a scenario: explaining you'll be late and why, asking a colleague or friend for help with directions, mentioning a minor health issue, and proposing a plan to meet or follow up. Decide whether your message is to a friend (tu) or a colleague/professional contact (vous), and stay consistent.
  3. Write your first draft. Include: a greeting, your main request or explanation, at least one direction-giving or location phrase, one health-related sentence using avoir mal à or se sentir, one use of il faut, pouvoir, or devoir, a proposed plan using the near future, and a closing.
  4. Revise. Use the editing checklist below before producing your final copy.

Liste de vérification — Editing checklist:

  • My register (tu or vous) is consistent throughout the message.
  • I used at least one direction or location phrase correctly.
  • I used avoir mal à or se sentir with the correct contracted article.
  • I used il faut, pouvoir, or devoir followed by an infinitive.
  • I used the near future (aller + infinitive) to propose or confirm a plan.
  • If I referred back to a place or a person, I used y, a direct-object pronoun, or lui/leur correctly instead of repeating the noun.
  • Negation, if used, is written in full (ne … pas) unless I am deliberately modeling informal speech.
  • All required accents and apostrophes are present.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Asking for help in the community

Across Francophone communities, asking a stranger for directions, asking a pharmacist for advice about a minor symptom, or asking a colleague to cover a shift typically begins with a greeting and a polite formula before the request itself — abruptly stating a need without this framing can come across as brusque. This convention is widely shared but not universal in tone: the level of formality used in a small village pharmacy, a large city hospital, and an informal workplace chat will differ.

Regional Variation — Pharmacy and health-system vocabulary

Pharmacies play a particularly active advisory role in many French and Belgian communities, where pharmacists are often consulted directly for minor, non-emergency symptoms before — or instead of — seeing a doctor. In Quebec and in many African Francophone health systems, the structure of primary care and the route to seeing a doctor can differ meaningfully. This course does not provide medical guidance; treat all health-related language here as communicative practice only, not as health advice.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Confusing lui/leur with le/la/les

Error: *Je le téléphone.

Correction: Je lui téléphone. The verb téléphoner à takes an indirect object in French, even though "to call someone" sounds direct in English. Check whether the French verb is normally followed by à + person; if so, use lui/leur, not le/la/les.

Common Mistake — Omitting y

Error: *Tu vas à la pharmacie ? — Oui, je vais.

Correction: Oui, j'y vais. Unlike English, French cannot simply drop the place reference once it has been named. Use y to stand in for it.

Common Mistake — Wrong contraction after avoir mal à

Error: *J'ai mal à le dos. *J'ai mal à les pieds.

Correction: J'ai mal au dos. J'ai mal aux pieds. Remember the standard contractions: à + le → au, à + les → aux. There is no separate uncontracted form in standard French.

Common Mistake — Using qui where que is required, or the reverse

Error: *C'est le projet qui je préfère.

Correction: C'est le projet que je préfère. Ask whether the noun being referred to is the subject or the object of the following verb. Here, je is the subject of préfère, and le projet is what is preferred — so it functions as the object, requiring que. Use qui only when the referenced noun is itself the subject of the following verb.

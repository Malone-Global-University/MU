Bonjour ! Premiers contacts

First Encounters · Speak and Write French: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Greet someone appropriately at different times of day and in different situations.
  2. Introduce yourself by name using Je m'appelle… and Je suis…
  3. Ask someone's name using Comment tu t'appelles ? and Comment vous vous appelez ?
  4. Choose between tu and vous based on your relationship with the other person.
  5. Use basic courtesy expressions including merci, s'il vous plaît, excusez-moi, and de rien.
  6. Say goodbye using appropriate leave-taking expressions.
  7. Count from 0 to 10 in French.
  8. Recognize and produce the French alphabet and its letter names.

Communication Scenario

You have just arrived at a language school in Lyon for a three-week intensive French programme. On the first morning, students gather in a courtyard before class. You don't know anyone yet. Your instructor has asked everyone to introduce themselves to at least three people before the session begins. The only rule: do it in French.

  1. Think about the last time you introduced yourself to a stranger. What did you say first? Did the setting affect how formal or informal you were? Jot down a few notes.
  2. You already know one French word most people know: bonjour. What do you think it means, literally? You will find out in this chapter — and discover that it is only one of several ways to greet someone in French.

Pronunciation Note — The French R

The French r is one of the sounds that most distinguishes French from English. Unlike the English r, which is made at the front of the mouth, the most common French r is produced at the back of the throat — a light friction between the back of the tongue and the soft palate, similar to the sound you might make when gargling gently. Listen to words such as bonjour, au revoir, and merci spoken by a native speaker, and you will hear it clearly.

An English-style r will still make you understood, especially at this stage. As you progress through the course, aim gradually to move the sound toward the back of the mouth. Do not force it or strain — a workable approximation is far better than a strained imitation.

French vowels — a first look

French vowels tend to be short, pure, and stable — they do not glide the way English vowels often do. Compare the English word no (which glides from oh toward oh-oo) with the French eau or au, which stays on a single, clean oh sound. This difference matters in words you are already learning:

  • au revoirThe au is a clean oh — not the gliding vowel in the English word "oh."
  • bonjourThe on is a nasal vowel — the mouth is open and air flows through the nose. You will study nasal vowels more fully in Chapter 3.
  • merciThe i is a clear ee sound — like the ee in English see.

Accents — Required spelling

French uses several written accents. They are not optional. Omitting an accent is a spelling error, just as omitting a letter would be in English. You will learn each accent in context as vocabulary introduces it.

In this chapter, two accented forms appear in your vocabulary:

  • é — the accent aigu (acute accent)Found in enchanté, répétez, s'il vous plaît (the à there is an accent grave — see below). Pronounce this vowel as a clear, tense ay — like the English word hey but without the glide.
  • à — the accent grave (grave accent) on aFound in à bientôt and s'il vous plaît. The à sounds the same as unaccented a in most contexts. The accent here distinguishes two words that would otherwise look identical in writing.
Pronunciation Note — Statement vs. question intonation

One of the simplest ways to form a question in French is to use exactly the same words as a statement but raise your voice at the end. Compare:

  • Vous vous appelez Madame Lefebvre. (falling intonation — a statement)
  • Vous vous appelez Madame Lefebvre ? (rising intonation — a question)

This rising-intonation question is extremely common in everyday spoken French. You will learn other question strategies — including est-ce que — beginning in Chapter 3. For now, practice the intonation contrast: your voice stays level or falls slightly at the end of a statement, and rises noticeably at the end of a question.

Practice — Spelling aloud: With a partner, take turns spelling your first name letter by letter using French letter names. Your partner writes down what they hear and then reads the name back. Then switch. Repeat with the name of a city you know.

Grammar Instruction

1.1 Tu and vous — choosing the right word for "you"

Purpose: select tu or vous correctly every time you address someone in French.

French has two words where English has one: both tu and vous mean "you." Choosing the wrong one is not merely a grammatical slip — it sends a social signal. Using tu when vous is expected can come across as rude or presumptuous. Using vous when tu is expected can feel cold or oddly distant.

TU — use when addressing tu a friend · a family member · a fellow student · a child · a pet · someone who has invited you to use it
VOUS — use when addressing vous a stranger · an instructor · a professional · someone older · anyone you aren't sure about · two or more people of any relationship

Vous is also the ordinary plural form of "you" — it is always used when speaking to more than one person, regardless of how well you know them. In other words, vous means either "you (formally)" or "you (plural)." Context makes the meaning clear.

Grammar Note — When in doubt, use vous

If you are unsure which form to use, choose vous. It is never rude to be respectful. The move to tu is often explicitly negotiated — a French speaker may say On peut se tutoyer ? (Can we use tu with each other?) before switching. Wait for that invitation rather than assuming familiarity.

Norms vary across Francophone communities and generations. In some workplaces and student environments, tu is the norm from the first day. Pay attention to how the people around you address each other, and follow their lead.

  • Étudiant à étudiant : Comment tu t'appelles ?Student to student (informal): What's your name?
  • Étudiant au professeur : Comment vous vous appelez, Madame ?Student to instructor (formal): What is your name, Madame?
  • À un groupe : Bonjour à tous ! Comment vous vous appelez ?To a group: Hello everyone! What are your names?

1.2 Je m'appelle and Je suis — saying your name

Purpose: introduce yourself naturally, using the two most common formulas for stating your name.

Je m'appelle… is literally "I call myself…" and is by far the most natural way to tell someone your name in an introduction. The full form of the verb is s'appeler (to be called), which is a reflexive verb — a type you will study fully in Chapter 7. For now, use it as a complete, ready-made expression.

Je suis… means "I am…" and uses the verb être (to be). You will learn the complete conjugation of être in Chapter 3. In this chapter, you need just the first-person singular form:

  • Je suis Marie.I am Marie.
  • Je m'appelle Thomas Renaud.My name is Thomas Renaud.
  • Moi, c'est Isabelle.I'm Isabelle. (informal — lit. Me, it's Isabelle.)
Grammar Note — Je suis and the apostrophe

French uses an apostrophe when a word ending in a vowel immediately precedes a word beginning with a vowel or a mute h. This process is called élision (elision). In Je m'appelle, the me has become m' before the vowel of appelle. Elision is obligatory — you cannot write or say *je me appelle. You will encounter elision constantly in French, and you will study it systematically beginning in Chapter 6.

1.3 Singular forms of être — the verb "to be"

Purpose: use the three most essential forms of être to introduce yourself and understand simple identity statements.

The verb être (to be) is irregular and extremely frequent. In this chapter you need only the singular present forms. The complete present tense will be taught in Chapter 3.

Subject pronounForm of êtreExampleMeaning
je (I)suisJe suis Léa.I am Léa.
tu (you, informal)esTu es étudiant(e).You are a student.
vous (you, formal/plural)êtesVous êtes le professeur.You are the teacher.

The third-person singular (il est, elle est — he/she is) will appear in the model dialogue below for recognition. It becomes a productive form in Chapter 2.

Grammar Note — French subject pronouns are required

Unlike Spanish or Italian, French normally requires an overt subject pronoun before every conjugated verb. You cannot say *Suis Marie the way a Spanish speaker might say Soy María without a pronoun. The pronoun je is always present in standard French: Je suis Marie. This is an important structural difference to notice from the very beginning.

Language Focus

One word for "you" — or two? English uses "you" for everyone: your best friend, your boss, your grandmother, a group of colleagues. French has always distinguished between tu (one person you are close to) and vous (a stranger, a superior, or more than one person). This distinction shapes every interaction you will have in French. It is not just grammar — it is social information built directly into the language. When you choose vous with a stranger, you are signalling respect and appropriate distance. When you use tu with a new friend, you are signalling warmth and equality. Pay attention to which form French speakers use with you, and mirror it.

Model Dialogue

Contexte : Cour de l'école de langues, Lyon. Premier matin. Sophie, étudiante canadienne, rencontre Marco, étudiant italo-belge. Ensuite, leur professeure, Madame Lecomte, les accueille. Registre : informel entre étudiants (tu), formel avec la professeure (vous).
Setting: Courtyard of a language school, Lyon. First morning. Sophie, a Canadian student, meets Marco, an Italian-Belgian student. Then their instructor, Madame Lecomte, welcomes them. Register: informal between students (tu), formal with the instructor (vous).

SOPHIE Bonjour ! Je m'appelle Sophie. Et toi ? Hello! My name is Sophie. And you?
MARCO Salut ! Moi, c'est Marco. Enchanté, Sophie. Hi! I'm Marco. Pleased to meet you, Sophie.
SOPHIE Enchantée ! Comment tu t'appelles en entier ? Pleased to meet you! What's your full name?
MARCO Je m'appelle Marco Ferretti. Et toi — Sophie comment ? My name is Marco Ferretti. And you — Sophie what?
SOPHIE Sophie Tremblay. Je suis de Montréal. Et toi ? Sophie Tremblay. I'm from Montréal. And you?
MARCO Je suis de Bruxelles. Super ! I'm from Brussels. Great!

(Madame Lecomte arrive — Madame Lecomte arrives.)

MME LECOMTE Bonjour ! Je suis Madame Lecomte, votre professeure. Good morning! I am Madame Lecomte, your instructor.
SOPHIE Bonjour, Madame. Je m'appelle Sophie Tremblay. Good morning, Madame. My name is Sophie Tremblay.
MME LECOMTE Enchantée, Sophie. Et vous, Monsieur ? Pleased to meet you, Sophie. And you, sir?
MARCO Marco Ferretti, Madame. Ravi de vous rencontrer. Marco Ferretti, Madame. Delighted to meet you.

  3. Quelle formule Sophie utilise-t-elle avec Madame Lecomte ? (Which greeting formula does Sophie use with Madame Lecomte?)

Grammar Note — Two words to notice in the dialogue

Madame Lecomte says votre professeure — "your instructor." The word votre means "your" and belongs to the system of possessive determiners taught fully in Chapter 4. She also says Bienvenue à tous les deux — "Welcome to you both." The phrase tous les deux means "both of you" (literally "all the two of them"). These are recognition-only items here; treat them as formulaic expressions and don't worry about their internal grammar yet.

Reading Note — Noms et prénoms in French

In French, the family name (le nom de famille) typically appears before the first name (le prénom) in formal contexts such as official documents, registration forms, and name badges, but the order often reverses in ordinary social introductions: Je m'appelle Céline Moreau. On official forms you may also see NOM : in capital letters, which signals that the family name goes in that field. When you fill in a French form, read the labels carefully — nom alone usually means family name.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — The greeting as a social ritual

In many Francophone communities, greeting someone before making a request is not optional — it is expected. Walking up to a counter in a French café or shop and immediately saying Un café, s'il vous plaît without first saying Bonjour is often perceived as abrupt. The greeting functions as an acknowledgment of the other person's presence. A simple Bonjour, Madame or Bonjour, Monsieur before your request costs nothing and changes the tone of the entire interaction.

This expectation is widely shared across France and many other Francophone communities, though norms always vary by individual, setting, and context. As a learner, making a habit of greeting before asking will serve you well.

Culture in Context — Francophone diversity

French is spoken as a first or official language by more than 320 million people across five continents. The four speakers in today's listening section come from France, Senegal, Taiwan, and Lyon — reflecting the genuine global reach of the language. When you learn French, you are not simply learning a European language: you are gaining access to communities in West and Central Africa, the Caribbean, North Africa, the Indian Ocean, the Pacific, and Francophone North America, each with its own cultural identity and its own relationship with French.

The greetings and courtesy expressions in this chapter are widely understood across Francophone communities. Regional variation in greetings and social conventions will be noted throughout the course where it affects the way you communicate.

Regional Variation — Informal greeting alternatives

In informal speech across many Francophone communities, you may encounter alternatives to bonjour. In Québec, Allo ! is a very common informal greeting. In Belgium, Bonjour is the standard, but informal registers share many features with France. In several West African contexts, Bonjour is the standard neutral greeting, while informal exchanges may incorporate expressions from local languages alongside French. This course uses Bonjour as the default productive form because it is universally understood. Alternatives will be noted when they are likely to be encountered.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Omitting accents

Error: Writing enchante instead of enchanté, or s'il vous plait instead of s'il vous plaît.

Correction: Accents are part of correct French spelling and must always be written. In formal writing, accents are required on capital letters too: Enchantée, not Enchantee. Set up your keyboard or device to type French characters from the start of the course — it is a practical skill, not an afterthought.

Common Mistake — Confusing tu and vous

Error: Using tu with an instructor, an older person, or a stranger: *Comment tu t'appelles, Madame ?

Correction: Comment vous vous appelez, Madame ? — Use vous any time you are not sure. The decision to move to tu is usually made together, not assumed unilaterally.

Common Mistake — Missing the apostrophe in Je m'appelle

Error: Writing *Je me appelle or *Je mappelle.

Correction: The vowel of me drops before the vowel of appelle, and an apostrophe replaces it: Je m'appelle. This is a required spelling rule called élision. Always write and type the apostrophe.

Common Mistake — Omitting the subject pronoun

Error: *Suis Thomas (omitting je) — modelled on Spanish, Italian, or other languages where the subject pronoun can be dropped.

Correction: French requires an overt subject pronoun before a conjugated verb in standard usage: Je suis Thomas. Unlike Spanish or Italian, the verb ending alone does not carry enough information to omit the pronoun in normal conversation.

Common Mistake — Using Bonjour at night

Error: Saying Bonjour when arriving somewhere in the evening.

Correction: Switch to Bonsoir once it is evening — typically from around 6 or 7 p.m. onward, though the exact timing is contextual and varies. Bonjour is the daytime greeting; Bonsoir is the evening one. Using Bonjour at night is not catastrophic, but it sounds like a slip.

Chapter Review — Section A

  1. bonjour · au revoir · s'il vous plaît · de rien · excusez-moi · à bientôt
  2. zéro, trois, cinq, sept, neuf
  3. Bonne journée means "have a good day" and is used when parting during the day. Bonne soirée means "have a good evening" and is used when parting in the evening.
  4. Enchanté means "pleased to meet you." A woman writes enchantée (with a final -e) because French adjectives agree in gender with the speaker or the referent — the extra -e marks feminine agreement, though in speech the two forms sound identical.
  5. À tout à l'heure means "see you later (today)." It is used when you expect to see the person again the same day, rather than bidding a definitive farewell.

Chapter Review — Section B

  1. (a) vous   (b) tu   (c) vous   (d) tu
  2. Je suis étudiant(e).   Tu es de Paris ?   Vous êtes Madame Lecomte ?
  3. *Je me appelle Martin → Je m'appelle Martin.   *Comment tu t'appelles, Monsieur ? → Comment vous vous appelez, Monsieur ?   *Suis de Lyon. → Je suis de Lyon.
  4. Unlike Spanish or Italian, French does not encode the subject sufficiently in the verb ending alone; the subject pronoun carries essential grammatical information and is required in standard spoken and written French.
  5. Greeting: Bonsoir, Monsieur / Madame. Farewell: Bonne soirée ! À demain. (Other accurate, natural answers are acceptable.)

Chapter Review — Section C

  1. Isabelle Fontaine est professeure de français. Elle enseigne au niveau A1–A2.
  2. Marco uses vous because Madame Lecomte is his instructor — a person in a formal role whom he is meeting for the first time. The relationship calls for the respectful form.
  3. Bonjour would be incorrect at 9 p.m. It should be replaced by Bonsoir.

Looking Ahead

Ce que vous savez maintenant — What you can do now

You can greet someone at the right time of day, introduce yourself by name, ask and respond to a name question, choose between tu and vous with confidence, use essential courtesy expressions, say goodbye, count from 0 to 10, and spell your name aloud in French. You have also taken your first step with the verb être and learned the critical structural rule that French always requires a subject pronoun before a conjugated verb.

Chapitre 2 : Dans la salle de classe — Classroom Language

Chapter 2 brings you into the classroom. You will learn the names of common classroom objects and begin working with one of the most important systems in French: grammatical gender and the article system. Every French noun belongs to either the masculine or the feminine gender, and the articles le, la, l', and les must match. You will also learn c'est and ce sont to identify objects, practice following common classroom instructions, and count from 11 to 20.

Bilan 2 : Une journée pratique

Unit II Assessment: A Practical Day · Speak and Write French: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will have demonstrated that you can:

  1. Describe a home, apartment, or neighborhood using il y a, location prepositions, demonstrative determiners, and contractions.
  2. Explain a daily schedule, including times, frequency expressions, and reflexive verbs.
  3. Use ne … pas to express negation in the present tense.
  4. Discuss food and drink preferences, use partitive articles and quantity expressions with de, and order or purchase items politely.
  5. Use en to replace a partitive or quantity expression in a limited set of contexts.
  6. Compare and purchase goods, using adjective agreement, demonstrative determiners, and direct-object pronouns (le, la, l', les).
  7. Use numbers 70–1,000 for prices and quantities.
  8. Ask and answer questions across all four communication domains using appropriate question strategies.
  9. Read practical texts — a home listing, a daily schedule, a menu, and a shopping comparison — and demonstrate comprehension.
  10. Write a connected plan for a practical day that integrates grammar from Chapters 6–9.
  11. Edit a short written passage to correct errors in articles, agreement, partitives, and negation.
Assessment chapter: Chapter 10 is the Unit II assessment. It introduces no new major grammar. Every task draws on language from Chapters 6–9, with continued recycling of material from Chapters 1–5. If you find a task difficult, note the chapter reference beside each section and return to that chapter for review before your final submission.

Communication Scenario

You have agreed to host a visiting student from Yaoundé, Cameroon, for one week. Before she arrives, you want to describe your home and neighborhood, plan a typical day together, discuss food preferences, and go shopping for a few things she needs. This chapter tests whether you have all the tools to do that — from the first description of your apartment to the final transaction at the checkout.

  1. Think about everything you could already say to describe your daily life in French. How would you describe where you live? What would you say about your morning routine? How would you suggest a meal? How would you talk about a purchase? Each of these corresponds to a chapter in Unit II.
  2. Notice that a real conversation about a practical day requires combining many systems at once: articles, verbs, negation, pronouns, and numbers all work together. The tasks in this chapter let you practice exactly that kind of integration.
  3. As you work through each assessment section, note which areas feel solid and which feel less secure. Use the self-assessment checklist at the end to plan your next steps before moving to Unit III.

How to Use This Chapter

Chapter 10 follows the structure of a complete assessment unit. Work through the sections in order. The grammar and pronunciation review sections are concise summaries — they are not re-teaching sections. If you need to revisit a concept in depth, go back to the chapter listed in brackets.

The four assessment tasks — listening, speaking, reading, and writing — each carry a clear rubric focus. The editing task near the end consolidates accuracy across multiple systems simultaneously. The chapter closes with a performance task that requires you to use all four skills together.

Instructors may use this chapter as a formal unit test, a structured portfolio checkpoint, or a guided self-assessment. Learners working independently should complete all tasks and check them carefully against the answer key before moving to Unit III.

Grammar Review

The tables below summarize the major grammar systems introduced in Chapters 6–9. Use them as a quick reference. Chapter numbers in brackets show where each system was first taught.

Il y a and negative existence [Ch. 6]

  • Il y a un salon et deux chambres. There is a living room and two bedrooms.
  • Il n'y a pas de garage. There is no garage. / There isn't a garage.
  • Il y a des commerces près d'ici. There are shops nearby.

After il n'y a pas, the article changes to de (or d' before a vowel), regardless of the gender or number of the noun that follows.

Present tense, reflexive verbs, and negation [Ch. 7]

Regular -er, selected -ir, and selected -re verbs follow predictable written patterns that are largely neutralized in speech. Reflexive verbs add a reflexive pronoun that agrees with the subject before the verb. Negation wraps ne … pas around the conjugated verb (or around the reflexive pronoun + verb for reflexive verbs).

  • Je me lève à sept heures. I get up at seven o'clock.
  • Elle ne se couche pas tard. She doesn't go to bed late.
  • Nous ne travaillons pas le dimanche. We don't work on Sundays.
  • Il prend le bus à huit heures. He takes the bus at eight o'clock.
Grammar Reminder — Reflexive verbs in negation

In a negative reflexive construction, ne comes before the reflexive pronoun and pas comes after the conjugated verb: Il ne se rase pas (He doesn't shave). The reflexive pronoun stays directly attached to the verb. Do not place pas between the pronoun and the verb.

En — replacing a partitive or quantity expression [Ch. 8]

  • — Tu veux du café ? — Oui, j'en veux. Do you want (some) coffee? — Yes, I want some.
  • — Il y a du lait ? — Non, il n'y en a plus. Is there any milk? — No, there isn't any left.
  • — Tu prends combien de croissants ? — J'en prends deux. How many croissants are you taking? — I'm taking two.

En replaces a whole partitive noun phrase or a quantity phrase. It is placed before the conjugated verb, just as a direct-object pronoun would be. At this stage, keep its use to these three high-frequency patterns.

Adjective placement, direct-object pronouns, and comparison [Ch. 9]

Most descriptive adjectives follow the noun. Common pre-noun adjectives include grand, petit, beau, vieux, nouveau, jeune, and bon. Direct-object pronouns le, la, l', les replace a definite noun phrase and are placed before the conjugated verb.

  • Je préfère cette veste. → Je la préfère. I prefer this jacket. → I prefer it.
  • Il achète les légumes. → Il les achète. He buys the vegetables. → He buys them.
  • Ce modèle est plus cher que l'autre. This model is more expensive than the other one.
  • Cette robe est moins longue que la première. This dress is shorter than the first one.
Language Focus

Direct-object pronouns and word order: The pronoun replaces the noun and moves to the position directly before the verb. In a negative sentence: Je ne le prends pas — the pronoun sits between ne and the verb, not outside the negation frame. Word order: ne + pronoun + verb + pas.

In standard metropolitan French, 70 = soixante-dix, 80 = quatre-vingts, and 90 = quatre-vingt-dix. Belgium and Switzerland use septante, huitante/octante, and nonante — useful for recognition. The plural -s on quatre-vingts disappears when a further digit follows: quatre-vingt-deux. Similarly, deux cents loses its -s before another digit: deux cent cinquante.

Regional Variation — Numbers in Belgium and Switzerland

If you encounter a price or age expressed as septante euros (70 euros) or nonante ans (90 years old), you are hearing Belgian or Swiss French. These forms are completely standard within those communities and are not incorrect — they are simply different from the metropolitan French counting system taught productively in this course. Recognition of septante, huitante, and nonante is a useful comprehension skill when travelling or communicating across the Francophone world.

Pronunciation and Spelling Review

Core systems active in Chapters 6–9

The following pronunciation and orthography systems were introduced or expanded in Unit II. Review each point before completing the assessment tasks. Pronunciation errors that affect comprehension — particularly misread articles, agreement endings, or number values — are noted explicitly.

Pronunciation Review — Schwa, elision, and enchaînement
  • Schwa in reflexive forms: The e in me, te, se, le, de is a schwa that may be reduced or dropped in normal speech: je me lève → often sounds like j'me lève in fast conversation. In careful speech or at this learning stage, pronounce it fully.
  • Elision in il y a: Il y a runs together in speech as a single rhythmic unit. The il is not stressed and y a flows directly from it. The negative il n'y a pas de should be practiced as one phrase.
  • Enchaînement in location phrases: In en face de l'école, the final consonant of face links smoothly to the following vowel: en-fa-se-de-lé-cole. This is enchaînement, not liaison — the consonant is always pronounced and simply resyllabifies.
  • Liaison in plural patterns: Les enfants → lé-zan-fan; nous avons → nu-za-von. These are mandatory liaison contexts you have practiced since Chapter 3. They reappear throughout the shopping and routine contexts of Unit II.
Pronunciation Review — Front rounded vowels and nasal vowels in Unit II vocabulary
  • Front rounded vowels appear in food vocabulary: du beurre [ø], une heure [œ], deux euros [ø]. Keep the lips rounded while the tongue stays forward. Do not substitute an English [ɜː] or [ɛ].
  • Nasal vowels in shopping and number words: cent [sɑ̃], vingt [vɛ̃], un bon prix [ɛ̃ bɔ̃ pʁi]. Do not add a final nasal consonant after the vowel.
  • Mid-vowel contrasts in partitives: du [dy] versus de la [də la]. The u in du is front rounded [y]; this contrasts with de, which uses a schwa. These distinctions matter for comprehension in context.

Orthography checklist for this assessment

Before submitting any written work in this chapter, verify:

  • Contractions are written correctly: au, aux, du, des (never à le, de le).
  • Apostrophes are correct in elision: de l'eau, il n'y a pas, je m'appelle.
  • Demonstrative determiners agree with the noun: ce / cet / cette / ces.
  • Adjective agreement endings are present and correct in writing, including -e and -es endings that may be silent in speech.
  • Direct-object pronouns are spelled correctly before vowel-initial verbs: je l'achète (not *je le achète).
  • Partitive articles are correct after negation: je ne mange pas de viande (not *pas de la viande).
  • Number words are hyphenated correctly: quatre-vingts, soixante-dix-neuf, deux cent cinquante.
  • Days of the week and months are in lowercase.
  • Complete written negation (ne … pas) is present in all formal written tasks.
  • All required accents are present: réfrigérateur, déjeuner, préférer, célébrer.
Évaluation — Assessment Task 1

Listening Assessment

Ask your instructor, a partner, or a text-to-speech tool to read the following two short recordings aloud at a normal conversational pace. Do not read the scripts yourself before answering the questions. Complete Part A after the first recording and Part B after the second.

Enregistrement A — A home and neighborhood description

[Script for instructor or partner — do not show to learner before listening]

« J'habite dans un appartement au troisième étage d'un immeuble moderne. Il y a quatre pièces : un salon, une cuisine, une salle de bains et une chambre. Dans le salon, il y a un canapé, une table basse et une grande fenêtre. La cuisine est petite mais pratique — il y a un réfrigérateur, une cuisinière et un micro-ondes. Il n'y a pas de lave-vaisselle, malheureusement. Mon quartier est sympa. Il y a une boulangerie, un supermarché et un parc à côté de mon immeuble. Le métro est à cinq minutes à pied. »

Partie A — Questions de compréhension:

  1. À quel étage habite la personne ? (On which floor does the person live?)
  2. Combien de pièces y a-t-il dans l'appartement ? Nommez-les. (How many rooms are there? Name them.)
  3. Qu'est-ce qu'il y a dans le salon ? (What is in the living room?)
  4. Qu'est-ce qu'il n'y a pas dans la cuisine ? (What is missing from the kitchen?)
  5. Donnez deux détails sur le quartier. (Give two details about the neighborhood.)

Enregistrement B — Planning a day

[Script for instructor or partner — do not show to learner before listening]

« Demain, je me lève à six heures et demie. Je me douche, je m'habille et je prends mon petit-déjeuner — du café et des tartines avec du beurre et de la confiture. À huit heures moins le quart, je prends le bus pour aller au travail. À midi, je mange à la cafétéria. Je prends généralement de la salade et un plat chaud. Je ne mange pas de dessert le midi. L'après-midi, je travaille jusqu'à dix-huit heures. Après le travail, je fais les courses au supermarché près de mon bureau. Le soir, je prépare le dîner et je me couche vers vingt-deux heures. »

Partie B — Questions de compréhension:

  1. À quelle heure est-ce que la personne se lève ? (At what time does the person get up?)
  2. Qu'est-ce qu'elle mange au petit-déjeuner ? (What does she eat for breakfast?)
  3. Qu'est-ce qu'elle ne mange pas le midi ? (What does she not eat at lunchtime?)
  4. À quelle heure est-ce qu'elle finit le travail ? (At what time does she finish work?)
  5. Qu'est-ce qu'elle fait après le travail ? (What does she do after work?)
  6. Relevez deux formes verbales réflexives dans le texte. (Find two reflexive verb forms in the text.)
Listening Note — Recognizing partitive articles in connected speech

In the second recording, the speaker uses several partitive articles: du café, des tartines, du beurre, de la confiture, de la salade. In connected speech these run together with the following noun. Practice listening for the pattern [preposition or article] + [noun] as a single rhythmic unit. Notice also the negative: je ne mange pas de dessert — the article shifts to bare de after negation.

Speaking Assessment

Tâche 2A — Controlled speaking: Questions and answers

Ask a partner (or read aloud to yourself and answer) the following questions using complete sentences. Each question targets a specific area from Unit II.

  1. Où est-ce que tu habites ? Il y a combien de pièces chez toi ? (Where do you live? How many rooms are there at your place?)
  2. À quelle heure est-ce que tu te lèves le matin ? (What time do you get up in the morning?)
  3. Qu'est-ce que tu manges au petit-déjeuner ? Tu bois du café ou du thé ? (What do you eat for breakfast? Do you drink coffee or tea?)
  4. Est-ce que tu fais les courses souvent ? Où est-ce que tu vas ? (Do you often do the shopping? Where do you go?)
  5. Décris un objet ou un vêtement que tu as acheté récemment. C'est comment ? (Describe something you bought recently. What is it like?)

Tâche 2B — Communicative task: Une journée ensemble

Work with a partner. One of you plays the role of the visiting student from Yaoundé; the other plays the host. Have a conversation (at least 8 exchanges) in which you:

  1. Describe your home: what rooms there are and what is near your apartment.
  2. Explain a typical morning routine, including times.
  3. Discuss what to eat for dinner: what you have at home, what you need to buy, and what you prefer.
  4. Suggest going shopping and discuss what to look for, comparing at least two options.

Use tu throughout — you are peers. Make sure each person speaks approximately equally.

Tâche 2C — Solo alternative

Record yourself describing a typical Saturday. Include:

  1. What time you get up and your morning routine (at least three reflexive verbs).
  2. What you eat for breakfast and lunch (at least two partitive articles).
  3. At least one thing you do not do on Saturdays (negation).
  4. Something you buy or have bought, with a description and a price (adjective agreement, numbers 70–1,000).

Aim for 90–120 seconds. Play back your recording and check for correct article choice and verb forms before submitting.

Communication Note — Polite requests in practical situations

In service interactions — a café, a market stall, a clothing shop — French conversation typically begins with a greeting before any request is made. Bonjour, madame / monsieur establishes respect and opens the exchange. A request framed as Je voudrais… (I would like…) or Est-ce que je pourrais avoir… (Could I have…) is markedly more natural and appropriate than a bare noun or a direct imperative. When comparing products, framing your preference with je préfère rather than je veux also registers as polished and courteous. These conventions apply across the Francophone world, even if their precise degree of formality varies locally.

Reading Assessment

Read the following two texts and answer all comprehension questions. You may read each text as many times as you need.

Texte A — Annonce immobilière (Montréal, Québec)

À louer — Appartement lumineux au cœur du Plateau-Mont-Royal, Montréal. Troisième étage, sans ascenseur. Superficie : 75 m². Pièces : salon-salle à manger, deux chambres, une salle de bains, une cuisine entièrement équipée (réfrigérateur, cuisinière, lave-vaisselle). Il y a un grand balcon à l'arrière de l'appartement. Pas de garage, mais il y a un local à vélos dans l'immeuble. Le quartier est très animé : il y a des cafés, des restaurants, des épiceries fines et une librairie à deux pas. Le métro Mont-Royal est à quatre minutes à pied. Loyer : 1 850 $ par mois, charges comprises. Disponible le 1er juillet. Contactez Mme Tremblay.

Questions — Texte A:

  1. Dans quel quartier se trouve l'appartement ? Dans quelle ville ? (In which neighborhood is the apartment? In which city?)
  2. Combien de chambres y a-t-il ? (How many bedrooms are there?)
  3. Qu'est-ce qu'il y a dans la cuisine ? Donnez trois exemples. (What is in the kitchen? Give three examples.)
  4. Qu'est-ce qu'il n'y a pas dans l'immeuble ? Qu'est-ce qu'il y a à la place ? (What is there not in the building? What is there instead?)
  5. Quel est le loyer mensuel ? (What is the monthly rent?)
  6. L'annonce utilise l'expression à deux pas. Que veut-elle dire, selon le contexte ? (The listing uses the expression "à deux pas." What does it mean, based on context?)

Texte B — Message de Rosalie à son amie Djéneba (Lausanne, Suisse)

Salut Djéneba ! Je t'écris pour te donner les infos pour ce week-end. J'habite au numéro 14 de la rue du Midi — c'est facile à trouver. Il y a un arrêt de bus juste en face de mon immeuble. Mon appartement est au deuxième étage. Sonne à l'interphone, je viens t'ouvrir.

Pour les repas : le vendredi soir, je prépare une fondue — c'est typique ici, et c'est délicieux ! Il faut du fromage, du pain et du vin blanc. Je n'achète pas de fromage ordinaire — je préfère le Gruyère d'Alpage, c'est meilleur. Samedi matin, nous allons au marché. Il y a des légumes frais, des fruits, du poisson et de la viande. Tu aimes le poisson ? Si tu préfères la viande, on peut prendre du poulet — il y en a toujours.

Pour les courses, je te propose d'aller chez Migros — c'est une grande surface très pratique. Il y a de tout. Le samedi, il y a beaucoup de monde, alors on y va tôt. À bientôt !

Rosalie

Questions — Texte B:

  1. Comment est-ce que Djéneba peut trouver l'appartement de Rosalie ? (How can Djéneba find Rosalie's apartment?)
  2. Qu'est-ce que Rosalie prépare le vendredi soir ? Quels ingrédients faut-il ? (What is Rosalie making on Friday evening? What ingredients are needed?)
  3. Pourquoi est-ce que Rosalie préfère le Gruyère d'Alpage ? (Why does Rosalie prefer Gruyère d'Alpage?)
  4. Qu'est-ce qu'on peut acheter au marché ? Donnez quatre exemples. (What can you buy at the market? Give four examples.)
  5. Comment s'appelle le supermarché mentionné dans le texte ? Pourquoi est-ce qu'on y va tôt le samedi ? (What is the supermarket mentioned? Why do they go early on Saturday?)
  6. Relevez deux articles partitifs dans le message de Rosalie et expliquez pourquoi chacun est utilisé. (Find two partitive articles in Rosalie's message and explain why each is used.)
  7. Le texte contient l'expression il y en a toujours. Que remplace en ici ? (The text contains "il y en a toujours." What does en replace here?)
Culture in Context — Food and community in Francophone Switzerland

Switzerland has four official national languages — French, German, Italian, and Romansh — with French spoken primarily in the western region known as la Romandie, which includes Geneva, Lausanne, Neuchâtel, and Fribourg. The fondue, a dish made of melted cheese into which pieces of bread are dipped, is closely associated with Swiss and French Alpine culture and is enjoyed across social occasions throughout the year. Migros, mentioned in Rosalie's message, is one of Switzerland's largest retail cooperatives and a recognizable landmark of Swiss daily life. Swiss French uses the counting forms septante (70) and nonante (90), and the monetary unit is the Swiss franc (le franc suisse), not the euro — an important practical detail if you are shopping there.

Writing Assessment

Complete both writing tasks. Task 4A is a guided paragraph; Task 4B is an email.

Tâche 4A — Mon plan pour samedi

Write a connected paragraph of 80–110 words describing your plans for a practical Saturday. Your paragraph must include:

  1. At least two reflexive verbs (morning routine).
  2. At least three partitive or indefinite article expressions (food or drink).
  3. At least one negative sentence using ne … pas or ne … pas de.
  4. At least one expression with il y a describing your home or neighborhood.
  5. At least one direct-object pronoun (le, la, l', or les).
  6. A price or quantity using a number from 70 to 1,000.
  1. Plan your paragraph. Use the following structure as a guide: morning routine → breakfast → activity at home or in the neighborhood → food shopping or meal → one specific purchase with description and price.
  2. Draft your paragraph. Write freely without stopping to check details. Focus on getting your ideas onto the page.
  3. Revise and edit. Use the checklist below before finalizing your paragraph.
  • All reflexive verbs are correctly formed (pronoun agrees with subject; ne … pas wraps around pronoun + verb in negation).
  • Partitive articles are correctly selected (du / de la / de l') and shift to de / d' after negation or after a quantity expression.
  • Adjectives agree in gender and number with the nouns they describe.
  • Direct-object pronouns are placed before the conjugated verb.
  • All contractions are written correctly (au, du, not à le, de le).
  • Number words are hyphenated according to the approved convention.
  • All required accents are present and correct.
  • Complete written negation is present throughout (ne … pas).

  3. Your morning routine (times and reflexive verbs).

« Salut ! J'arrive samedi matin. Est-ce qu'il y a un bon marché près de chez toi ? Je n'aime pas beaucoup la viande — est-ce qu'on peut trouver des légumes frais facilement ? Et aussi, j'ai besoin d'acheter un pull — est-ce qu'il y a des magasins de vêtements dans ton quartier ? Merci ! Koumba »

Editing Task — Ten errors and corrections

Corrected paragraph:

« Je cherche un appartement dans ce quartier. Il y a beaucoup de magasins [1: beaucoup de les → beaucoup de — quantity expression uses bare de, not de les] et le métro est à côté de l'immeuble [2: la immeuble → l'immeuble — elision before vowel-initial noun; immeuble is masculine]. Cette semaine [3: Cet semaine → Cette semaine — semaine is feminine, so cette is required, not cet], j'ai visité un appartement au deuxième étage. Il y a trois pièces : un salon, une chambre et une cuisine. Dans la cuisine, il y a un grand réfrigérateur et une cuisinière nouvelle [4: nouveaux → nouvelle — the adjective agrees with cuisinière, feminine singular]. Je n'ai pas de voiture, donc je ne cherche pas de garage [5: une garage → de garage — after ne … pas, the article changes to bare de; also, garage is masculine so un not une would be needed in the affirmative]. Il y a également une grande terrasse [6: terrasses → terrasse — the indefinite article une requires a singular noun] sur le toit. Je n'achète jamais de viande [7: du viande → de viande — after a negative expression including ne … jamais, the article shifts to bare de], alors la cuisine végétarienne est importante pour moi. Je préfère cet appartement-là [8: ce appartement → cet appartement — before a masculine noun starting with a vowel, use cet] — il est plus grand [9: grands → grand — the subject is il, singular, so the predicate adjective is singular] que le premier. »

Nine errors are listed above. The tenth is a structural redundancy: in the original, grande terrasses presents a gender mismatch as well as the number mismatch — grande is correct as the feminine form agrees with terrasse, but terrasses in the plural would require grandes. The primary error is the number mismatch (singular article une with plural noun terrasses). Both the number error and the resulting agreement inconsistency may be counted as a single error unit or as two — both are addressed in the correction above. If your instructor counts them separately, all remaining corrections still apply.

Writing Task 4A — Model paragraph

Le samedi, je me lève à huit heures. Je me douche et je prépare mon petit-déjeuner : du café, du pain et de la confiture. Je ne mange pas de céréales le week-end. Il y a une boulangerie à côté de chez moi, alors j'y achète souvent du pain frais. L'après-midi, je vais au marché. Je cherche des tomates et du fromage. Je les achète toujours au même stand. J'ai aussi trouvé un beau sac en cuir pour quatre-vingt-cinq euros — je l'ai acheté tout de suite !

Other well-formed paragraphs meeting all six listed requirements are equally acceptable. Check that all required elements are present before submitting.

Writing Task 4B — Model email

Salut Mamadou ! Je suis content(e) de répondre à ta question. J'habite dans un petit appartement. Il y a un salon, une chambre et une cuisine équipée. Other accurate, naturally phrased emails are equally correct. Check for greeting, sign-off, and all five required content elements.

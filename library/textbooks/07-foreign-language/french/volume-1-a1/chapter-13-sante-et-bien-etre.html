Santé et bien-être

Health and Well-Being · Speak and Write French: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify the major parts of the body, with the correct article and gender.
  2. Describe a common symptom or ailment and say where it hurts using avoir mal à.
  3. Use the reflexive verb se sentir to describe how you feel.
  4. Express necessity with il faut plus an infinitive.
  5. Use devoir and pouvoir to express obligation and ability in a health context.
  6. Give simple, non-diagnostic advice using the imperative.
  7. Use the indirect-object pronouns me, te, and vous in common interactive expressions.
  8. Use ne … jamais and ne … plus to talk about symptoms and habits.
  9. Ask for help and make a basic appointment at a pharmacy or clinic.
  10. Write a short message describing symptoms and requesting an appointment.

Communication Scenario

A student studying in Geneva wakes up with a sore throat and a headache two days before an important exam. She walks to a neighborhood pharmacy to describe her symptoms and ask for advice, then calls a clinic to see whether she needs an appointment. This chapter gives you the tools to do the same — to explain what hurts, to understand simple advice, and to arrange basic care without needing to translate every word in your head.

  1. Think about how you currently describe being sick in English: "My head hurts," "I have a sore throat," "I feel tired." Notice that English mixes several different structures — "have," "feel," and simple descriptions. French organizes these ideas differently, mostly around the verb avoir and the reflexive verb se sentir. Keep this contrast in mind as you work through the chapter.
  2. Pharmacies play a larger role in everyday health care in many Francophone countries than learners from some other countries may expect. A pharmacist can often give advice for minor symptoms without a doctor's visit. This chapter's vocabulary and dialogue reflect that reality.

Pronunciation and Spelling

Nasal and oral vowel contrasts in symptom vocabulary

This chapter's vocabulary offers further practice distinguishing nasal vowels from their oral counterparts, a contrast first introduced in Chapter 3 and developed since.

Sound Focus — Nasal vowels in health vocabulary
  • la main — the ain sequence is a single nasal vowel [ɛ̃]. Do not pronounce a separate [n] after it.
  • la dent — the ent sequence is a nasal vowel [ɑ̃]; the final t is silent.
  • se sentir — the first syllable sen- is nasal [ɑ̃]; the second syllable -tir returns to an oral vowel.
  • le rendez-vous — contains a nasal vowel in ren- followed by oral vowels in the remaining syllables.

Contrast these with oral-vowel words such as la peau or le pied, which contain no nasal vowel at all. Practice saying nasal and oral examples side by side to keep the contrast active.

French r and consonant groups in health vocabulary

Several high-frequency words in this chapter combine the French r with consonant clusters: gorge, fièvre, infirmier, pharmacien. Continue producing the French r at the back of the mouth, without the tongue-tip movement used for the English r. Avoid letting the r disappear entirely; it should remain a light, continuous friction sound, not a strong English-style consonant.

Linked and reduced forms in appointment speech

In ordinary spoken French, common appointment expressions are often produced quickly and with linking between words: vous avez mal où ? is typically pronounced as one connected phrase, with liaison between avez and the question word that follows when applicable. Listen for this connected rhythm rather than expecting each word to be pronounced as a separate, isolated unit.

Polite request intonation

When asking for help or describing a problem politely, French intonation typically rises gently across the phrase before falling slightly at the very end, especially in requests such as Est-ce que vous pourriez m'aider ? (recognition only — the conditional form pourriez is glossed here as a polite formula and is not a productive target of this chapter). For this chapter's productive language, focus the same rising-then-falling pattern on sentences using il faut, je dois, and je peux.

Careful comprehension of numbers and times

Appointment-related listening requires precise recognition of numbers and clock time, both introduced in Chapter 7 and expanded since. Review the pronunciation of times such as dix heures, quatorze heures trente, and similar expressions, since mishearing a number in this context has real practical consequences.

Grammar Instruction

13.1 Avoir mal à — describing pain and symptoms

Purpose: say where something hurts, using the formula avoir mal à with the correct contraction.

The formula avoir mal à + definite article + body part is the standard way to say that something hurts. Like avoir mal à + le, the preposition à contracts with the definite article exactly as you learned in Chapter 6 (au, à la, à l', aux).

ArticleContraction with àExampleEnglish
le (m. sg.)auJ'ai mal au dos.My back hurts.
la (f. sg.)à laJ'ai mal à la tête.I have a headache.
l' (vowel-initial)à l'J'ai mal à l'oreille.My ear hurts.
les (pl.)auxJ'ai mal aux dents.I have a toothache. (lit. pain in the teeth)

Notice that the body part takes a definite article, not a possessive determiner. French does not normally say *J'ai mal à ma tête; the definite article alone is standard, because the subject of the sentence (j') already makes clear whose body is meant.

  • J'ai mal au ventre. My stomach hurts.
  • Tu as mal à la gorge ? Does your throat hurt? / Do you have a sore throat?
  • Elle a mal aux pieds. Her feet hurt.
  • Nous avons mal au cœur. We feel nauseous. (idiomatic — not "heart pain")
Grammar Note — avoir mal au cœur is idiomatic

The expression avoir mal au cœur does not mean "to have heart pain" in ordinary usage; it idiomatically means "to feel sick to one's stomach" or "to feel nauseous." For an actual concern about the heart, French uses other expressions that are beyond this chapter's scope. This is a useful reminder that formulas built from familiar words can carry a meaning that is not fully predictable from their parts.

  3. Nous avons mal _____ yeux. (les yeux)
  4. Tu as mal _____ oreille ? (l'oreille)
  5. Elles ont mal _____ jambes. (les jambes)

Purpose: describe a general state of well-being or illness using the reflexive verb se sentir.

You learned the basic pattern of reflexive verbs in Chapter 7. Se sentir (to feel) follows the same reflexive structure and is typically followed directly by an adverb or adjective describing how the person feels.

13.3 Il faut + infinitive — expressing necessity

Purpose: state that something is necessary or advisable, without naming a specific subject.

The impersonal expression il faut + infinitive expresses general necessity: "it is necessary to…," "one must…," or simply "you should…" in everyday advice. The subject il here is impersonal — it does not refer to a person.

  • Il faut se reposer. You need to rest. / It is necessary to rest.
  • Il faut boire beaucoup d'eau. You should drink a lot of water.
  • Il ne faut pas sortir aujourd'hui. You shouldn't go out today.

Il faut does not change form for different subjects; it remains in the third-person singular regardless of who the advice is directed at. This makes it a simple, reliable tool for giving general advice at this stage of the course.

13.4 Devoir and pouvoir — obligation and ability

Purpose: express specific obligation (devoir) or ability/permission (pouvoir) tied to a particular subject, as a more personal alternative to il faut.

Pronomdevoirpouvoir
jeje doisje peux
tutu doistu peux
il / elle / onil doitil peut
nousnous devonsnous pouvons
vousvous devezvous pouvez
ils / ellesils doiventils peuvent

Both verbs are followed directly by an infinitive, with no preposition in between.

  • Tu dois te reposer. You must rest.
  • Je ne peux pas venir aujourd'hui. I can't come today.
  • Vous devez prendre rendez-vous. You need to make an appointment.
Language Focus

Il faut versus devoir: Il faut states a general necessity without identifying who it applies to; devoir attaches the obligation directly to a specific subject. Il faut se reposer sounds like general advice anyone could follow; Tu dois te reposer is addressed specifically to the person you are speaking to. Both are useful, and you will often choose between them based on how personal or general you want the advice to sound.

Practice — il faut, devoir, pouvoir

  1. Il _____ (falloir) boire de l'eau quand on a de la fièvre.
  2. Tu _____ (devoir) te reposer ce soir.
  3. Je ne _____ (pouvoir) pas sortir ; j'ai mal à la tête.
  4. Vous _____ (devoir) prendre rendez-vous avec le médecin.
  5. Il ne _____ (falloir) pas oublier les médicaments.

13.5 Imperative for basic advice

Purpose: give direct, simple advice using the imperative forms introduced in Chapter 11.

You already use the imperative for directions (Chapter 11). The same tu, nous, and vous forms are used here to give brief, friendly health advice. Remember that the tu-form imperative of regular -er verbs drops the final -s.

  • Repose-toi ! Rest! (informal, reflexive imperative)
  • Buvez beaucoup d'eau. Drink a lot of water. (formal/plural)
  • Prends ce médicament. Take this medication. (informal)
  • N'oublie pas ton rendez-vous. Don't forget your appointment.

Reflexive imperatives such as repose-toi attach the pronoun after the verb with a hyphen in the affirmative. This pattern is presented here as a controlled formula; the complete system of pronoun placement with commands is not a productive requirement of this chapter.

13.6 Indirect-object pronouns me, te, vous in interaction

Purpose: use the indirect-object pronouns me, te, and vous in common high-frequency interactive expressions related to help and advice.

You are not yet expected to master the full indirect-object pronoun system (introduced productively in Chapter 14). In this chapter, you will use me, te, and vous as controlled, high-frequency forms in fixed interactive patterns, placed directly before the conjugated verb.

  • Vous pouvez m'aider ? Can you help me? (me → m' before a vowel)
  • Qu'est-ce qui te fait mal ? What hurts you? (literally "what does to you pain")
  • Je vous explique. I'll explain to you. / Let me explain to you.

Treat these as controlled high-frequency patterns rather than a full system at this stage. The complete contrast between direct and indirect objects, and the placement rules that govern it, is the focus of Chapter 14.

13.7 Ne … jamais and ne … plus

Purpose: use two additional negative structures to describe habits and changes related to health.

You learned basic negation with ne … pas in Chapter 7. Two related negative structures are useful in health contexts:

  • ne … jamais never — Je ne suis jamais malade en hiver. (I am never sick in winter.)
  • ne … plus no longer; not anymore — Il n'a plus mal à la tête. (He no longer has a headache.)

Both structures surround the conjugated verb in exactly the same position as ne … pas: ne before the verb, jamais or plus after it.

Practice — negation

  1. Rewrite with ne … jamais: Je suis malade. → Je _____ suis _____ malade.
  2. Rewrite with ne … plus: Elle a mal au dos. → Elle _____ a _____ mal au dos.
  3. Rewrite with ne … pas: Il peut venir aujourd'hui. → Il _____ peut _____ venir aujourd'hui.

Model Dialogue

Contexte : Léa, étudiante à Genève, entre dans une pharmacie de quartier. Registre : conversation polie entre une cliente et une pharmacienne, vous.
Setting: Léa, a student in Geneva, walks into a neighborhood pharmacy. Register: polite conversation between a customer and a pharmacist, vous.

PHARMACIENNE Bonjour ! Je peux vous aider ? Hello! Can I help you?
LÉA Bonjour, madame. Oui, je ne me sens pas très bien. J'ai mal à la gorge et j'ai un peu de fièvre. Hello. Yes, I'm not feeling very well. My throat hurts and I have a bit of a fever.
PHARMACIENNE Vous avez mal à la tête aussi, ou seulement à la gorge ? Does your head hurt too, or just your throat?
LÉA J'ai mal à la tête un peu, oui. Et je tousse depuis deux jours. My head hurts a little too, yes. And I've been coughing for two days.
PHARMACIENNE D'accord. Il faut vous reposer et boire beaucoup d'eau. Vous pouvez prendre ce médicament pour la fièvre. All right. You need to rest and drink a lot of water. You can take this medication for the fever.
LÉA Et si je ne vais pas mieux demain ? And if I'm not better tomorrow?
PHARMACIENNE Si vous n'allez pas mieux dans deux ou trois jours, vous devez prendre rendez-vous avec un médecin. Ce n'est probablement pas grave, mais il ne faut pas attendre trop longtemps. If you're not better in two or three days, you need to make an appointment with a doctor. It's probably nothing serious, but you shouldn't wait too long.
LÉA D'accord, merci beaucoup. Je n'ai jamais de chance avec ces examens ! All right, thank you very much. I never have luck with these exams!
PHARMACIENNE Courage ! Reposez-vous bien ce soir. Good luck (lit. "courage")! Rest well tonight.

Questions de compréhension — Comprehension questions:

  1. Quels sont les symptômes de Léa ? (What are Léa's symptoms?)
  2. Qu'est-ce que la pharmacienne lui conseille de faire ? (What does the pharmacist advise her to do?)
  3. Dans quelles conditions Léa doit-elle prendre rendez-vous avec un médecin ? (Under what conditions must Léa make an appointment with a doctor?)
  4. Relevez un exemple de il faut et un exemple de devoir dans le dialogue. (Find one example of il faut and one of devoir in the dialogue.)
Grammar Note — Reposez-vous bien

The pharmacist's closing line Reposez-vous bien uses the formal/plural imperative of the reflexive verb se reposer, with the reflexive pronoun vous attached after the verb. This is the same pattern as Repose-toi in the informal register, scaled up to the vous form. You are not expected to generate every reflexive imperative independently yet — recognize this pattern and reuse the fixed expressions you have seen.

Reading Note — Pharmacy access across the Francophone world

The Brussels poster reflects a pattern common in several Francophone countries: pharmacists are often a first point of contact for minor symptoms, available without an appointment during extended hours. Access to health care, insurance systems, and appointment practices vary considerably across Francophone countries and even within regions of the same country. Do not assume that any one system described here applies everywhere.

Writing Workshop

In this chapter you will write a short message describing symptoms and requesting an appointment or explaining an absence — similar to the model texts you just read.

  1. Study the models. Reread the message to a colleague or the text exchange above. Identify: (a) how the writer states they are not feeling well, (b) how symptoms are listed, (c) the use of il faut or devoir, and (d) how the message closes.
  2. Plan your message. Decide on a context (absence from work or school, or a request for an appointment), two or three symptoms using vocabulary from this chapter, and at least one piece of advice or plan using il faut, devoir, or the imperative.
  3. Write your first draft. Begin with an appropriate opening, state how you feel using se sentir or avoir mal à, list your symptoms, and close with a plan or request. Include at least one negative structure (ne … pas, ne … jamais, or ne … plus).
  4. Revise. Use the editing checklist below before writing your final copy.

Liste de vérification — Editing checklist:

  • I used the correct article and contraction in every avoir mal à phrase.
  • I conjugated se sentir correctly for my subject.
  • I used at least one form of il faut or devoir correctly with an infinitive.
  • My negation surrounds the conjugated verb correctly (ne … pas / jamais / plus).

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Health-care systems and access

Health-care systems differ considerably across Francophone countries and regions — in how appointments are scheduled, how care is funded, what role pharmacists play, and how quickly a person typically sees a doctor. France, Belgium, Switzerland, Quebec, and countries across West and Central Africa each have distinct systems and norms. This course does not describe any single system as standard for the whole Francophone world; the practices shown in this chapter's dialogue and readings are illustrative, not universal.

Culture in Context — Talking about illness with tact

Discussing health concerns calls for sensitivity in any language. In professional contexts, a brief, factual statement is usually sufficient — extensive detail is not expected or required. With close friends or family, more detail may be welcome. Avoid assuming that directness or reserve about health topics reflects a fixed cultural rule; individual and situational variation is considerable.

Regional Variation — Pharmacy and clinic vocabulary

The word médecin is used productively across the Francophone world for "doctor," though docteur is also widely used, especially as a title before a name (docteur Mercier). In Quebec, the public health-insurance card is often called la carte d'assurance maladie or informally la carte soleil; in France, the equivalent document is la carte Vitale. These are presented here as recognition-only regional labels; the course's productive vocabulary for this chapter remains the general terms introduced above.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using a possessive determiner instead of the article with avoir mal à

Error: *J'ai mal à ma tête.

Correction: J'ai mal à la tête. French uses the definite article, not a possessive determiner, with body parts after avoir mal à. The subject of the sentence already makes clear whose body part is meant, so the possessive is unnecessary and non-standard here.

Common Mistake — Forgetting the contraction after à

Error: *J'ai mal à le dos. *J'ai mal à les dents.

Correction: J'ai mal au dos. J'ai mal aux dents. The preposition à must contract with le and les to form au and aux, exactly as with any other use of à plus a definite article. à la and à l' do not contract.

Common Mistake — Confusing être malade and avoir mal

Error: *Je suis mal à la tête.

Correction: J'ai mal à la tête. (I have a headache.) or Je suis malade. (I am sick.) Être malade describes a general state of being sick; avoir mal à identifies a specific location of pain. The two expressions use different verbs and cannot be mixed.

Common Mistake — Adding a preposition after devoir or pouvoir

Error: *Je dois de me reposer. *Tu peux à m'aider ?

Correction: Je dois me reposer. Tu peux m'aider ? Both devoir and pouvoir are followed directly by an infinitive, with no preposition in between. This differs from some other French verb-plus-infinitive patterns you will see in later chapters, so it is worth fixing this pattern now.

Common Mistake — Placing ne … jamais or ne … plus incorrectly

Error: *Je suis ne jamais malade. *Il a plus mal pas.

Correction: Je ne suis jamais malade. Il n'a plus mal. Like ne … pas, both ne … jamais and ne … plus place ne immediately before the conjugated verb and the second element immediately after it. Do not separate the two halves or reverse their order.

Dialogue — Comprehension

  1. Léa a mal à la gorge, un peu de fièvre, mal à la tête, et elle tousse depuis deux jours.
  2. La pharmacienne lui conseille de se reposer, de boire beaucoup d'eau, et de prendre un médicament pour la fièvre.
  3. Si elle ne va pas mieux dans deux ou trois jours, elle doit prendre rendez-vous avec un médecin.
  4. Il faut : "Il faut vous reposer et boire beaucoup d'eau." Devoir : "vous devez prendre rendez-vous avec un médecin."

Looking Ahead

Ce que vous savez maintenant — What you can do now

You can now describe common symptoms, say how you feel, and give and receive basic, non-diagnostic health advice in French. You can name body parts, use avoir mal à with the correct contraction, conjugate se sentir, and express necessity and obligation with il faut and devoir. Together with the directions and imperative forms from Chapter 11 and the plans and invitations from Chapter 12, you now have the tools to manage a range of everyday situations in the community.

Chapitre 14 : Études et vie professionnelle

Chapter 14 turns to school and work. You will learn to distinguish savoir from connaître, use the indirect-object pronouns lui and leur, compare options with plus, moins, and irregular forms like meilleur and mieux, and introduce relative clauses with qui, que, and où. The indirect-object pronouns me, te, and vous you used formulaically in this chapter will connect directly to the fuller object-pronoun system you build there.

Dans la salle de classe

In the Classroom · Speak and Write French: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify common classroom objects and learning materials by name, using the correct article.
  2. Ask what something is, using Qu'est-ce que c'est ?, and answer using C'est… or Ce sont…
  3. Assign the correct definite article (le, la, l', les) and indefinite article (un, une, des) to familiar nouns.
  4. Understand grammatical gender as a property of French nouns and apply it consistently to articles.
  5. Form regular noun plurals by adding -s and recognize when plural articles are required.
  6. Describe an object using a color adjective that agrees in gender and number with the noun it modifies.
  7. Follow and produce common classroom instructions in French.
  8. Count from 0 to 20 and use numbers to count objects.
  9. Recognize key differences between the sounds spelled é and è.

Communication Scenario

It is the first day of a French language course in Montreal. The instructor asks students to take out materials, points to objects around the room, and teaches the class to ask and answer Qu'est-ce que c'est ? — the foundational question of any beginner classroom. By the end of the hour, students can name what they see, count their materials, and begin following instructions entirely in French.

  1. Look at your immediate surroundings. How many objects can you already name in French from Chapter 1? Which objects do you not yet have a word for? Make a quick list — you will find most of them in this chapter.
  2. In English, the words a and the are the same regardless of what noun they accompany. Think about why French might use different words for a depending on the noun. This chapter will give you the answer.

Pronunciation and Spelling

The sounds spelled é and è

French uses accent marks not for decoration but as part of the spelling system. Two of the most important are the acute accent (accent aigu) on é and the grave accent (accent grave) on è. They represent distinct vowel sounds, and confusing them produces a spelling error.

The vowel é is a relatively closed, bright sound. Keep your lips spread and do not let the sound glide as it might in English "say" or "way." Think of maintaining a steady position without moving your mouth. Examples from this chapter: écoutez, répétez, téléphone, zéro, écrivez.

The vowel è is a more open sound, made with the jaw slightly lower. It is close to the vowel in the English word bed, but again, without glide or movement. Examples from this chapter: fenêtre (the ê uses the circumflex but represents a similar open sound), règle.

Sound Focus — é and è in contrast

Say these pairs aloud. The spelling difference reflects a real difference in vowel quality. Train your ear to distinguish them:

  • répéter (to repeat) — the three written é sounds are all closed vowels. é · é · ter
  • règle (ruler) — the è is open. Try: règle — more open mouth than in répéter.
  • café (coffee / café) ends in a closed é sound, like the beginning of English "ay" but held steady.
  • fenêtre (window) — the ê uses the circumflex accent (accent circonflexe) and typically has the open quality of è.

When typing or writing French, never omit an accent. Writing ecoutez instead of écoutez is a spelling error, not just a style choice. Accents are part of the word.

Final written consonants

Many French words are spelled with a final consonant that is not pronounced in ordinary speech. This is one of the most important features of French spelling for English learners to internalize early.

Examples from this chapter's vocabulary:

  • un stylo — the word ends in the vowel sound o; the final o is simply a vowel, no silent consonant here.
  • un crayon — the final n is not pronounced as a separate consonant; instead, it nasalizes the preceding vowel. You hear a nasal vowel sound, not a final [n].
  • un sac — the final c is pronounced here, as an exception. Learn this word-by-word.
  • des livres — the plural -s on livres is not pronounced. However, it matters in writing because it signals the plural.
  • vert (green, masculine) — the final t is silent. But in the feminine form verte, the added -e causes the consonant to be pronounced: ver-te.

This pattern — a silent final consonant in the masculine that becomes pronounced in the feminine — will recur throughout the course. It is one reason that learning feminine adjective forms is important for pronunciation, not just spelling.

The letters c and ç

The letter c has two pronunciations in French, depending on what follows it:

  • c before a, o, or u, or at the end of a word: pronounced like English [k]. Examples: cahier, crayon.
  • c before e, i, or y: pronounced like [s]. Examples: cinq, ce sont, chaise (here, ch is a different sound — see below).
  • ç (c with a cedilla, cédille): always pronounced [s], regardless of what follows. This mark is used precisely to produce an [s] sound before a, o, or u. Example: ça (that / this).

The digraph ch in French is pronounced like English sh, not like English ch. Example from this chapter: une chaise — the ch sounds like the beginning of English shoe.

Voiceless stops without aspiration

In English, the sounds [p], [t], and [k] at the start of a stressed syllable are accompanied by a small puff of air — this is called aspiration. Hold your hand in front of your mouth and say the English word pen: you should feel a burst of air on the [p]. French does not have this aspirated quality. French [p], [t], and [k] are produced without that puff.

This gives French a slightly crisper, cleaner quality on these consonants. To practice, try saying the word pen after the English word spend — the [p] in spend is unaspirated, closer to the French target. Apply this awareness whenever you say French words beginning with p, t, or c before a vowel: table, téléphone, crayon.

The article and noun as one rhythmic unit

In French, an article and its noun are grouped together as a single rhythmic phrase. You do not pause between un and stylo; they are spoken as one smooth unit: un-stylo. When the article ends in a vowel sound and the noun begins with a vowel, the two words link especially smoothly — this is called enchaînement and will be studied further in Chapter 6. For now, practice reading each article-noun pair aloud as if it were one word.

Grammar Instruction

2.1 Grammatical gender — a property of nouns

Purpose: understand that every French noun has a grammatical gender, and that gender determines which article, adjective, and pronoun forms are used with it.

In French, every noun belongs to one of two grammatical genders: masculine or feminine. This is not about biological sex — it is a grammatical classification. A chair (une chaise) is grammatically feminine; a notebook (un cahier) is grammatically masculine. Neither object has a sex. Gender is simply a property that affects the words used around the noun.

Gender cannot always be predicted from the meaning of a noun or from its spelling, though some patterns are useful tendencies. The most reliable approach is to learn each noun with its article from the very beginning. When you encounter a new noun, do not ask "Is this masculine or feminine?" and then look for a rule. Instead, learn it as a unit: not stylo but un stylo.

Grammar Note — Gender is a grammatical fact, not a biological one

Many languages have grammatical gender, though English largely lost it. English the is the same regardless of the noun: the book, the chair, the window. French requires le livre, la chaise, la fenêtre — different article forms for different genders. Getting gender right is not optional, because it affects the article, the adjective, and later the pronoun. The investment you make in learning gender now pays off throughout the entire course.

2.2 Definite articles — le, la, l', les

Purpose: use the correct definite article to refer to a specific or known object, or to a category in general.

The French definite article (English: the) has four forms, depending on the gender and number of the noun, and on whether the noun begins with a vowel sound.

This process is called elision (l'élision). Elision is mandatory — you cannot say *la horloge or *le ordinateur.

The plural indefinite article des is used before any plural noun, regardless of gender. English often uses no article in this position ("I have pens") or the word some ("I have some pens"). French typically requires des.

Grammar Note — When to use le / la / les versus un / une / des

Use le, la, les (definite articles) when the object is specific or already known: Ouvrez le livre — "Open the book" (a specific, identified book, the one on your desk). Use un, une, des (indefinite articles) when introducing a non-specific object for the first time: Prenez un stylo — "Take a pen" (any pen, not a specific one). In simple practice sentences identifying objects — C'est un stylo (It is a pen) — use the indefinite article because you are categorizing the object, not pointing to one already established in context.

2.4 Noun plurals

Purpose: form regular plurals and recognize when a noun is plural from its article.

Most French nouns form the plural by adding -s to the singular form. This -s is not pronounced in ordinary speech — but it is required in writing, and it signals to the reader that the plural article is needed.

  • un livre → des livres a book → some books
  • une chaise → des chaises a chair → some chairs
  • une fenêtre → des fenêtres a window → some windows

Nouns already ending in -s, -x, or -z do not change in the plural. And nouns ending in -eau add -x instead of -s:

  • un bureau → des bureaux a desk → some desks
  • un tableau → des tableaux a board → some boards

2.5 C'est… and Ce sont… — Identifying objects

Purpose: ask what something is and identify it using c'est or ce sont.

To ask what an object is, use the question Qu'est-ce que c'est ? (What is this? / What is it?). To answer, use:

  • C'est un stylo. It is a pen. / This is a pen.
  • C'est une gomme. It is an eraser. / This is an eraser.
  • Ce sont des livres. These are books. / They are books.
  • Ce sont des chaises. These are chairs.

Use c'est before a singular noun (with un, une, or later with le, la, l'). Use ce sont before a plural noun (with des or later with les). The form changes only between singular and plural — it does not change for gender.

Language Focus

French always needs an article before a noun. One of the most consistent differences between French and English is that French nouns almost always appear with a determiner — an article, a possessive, or a demonstrative. In English you can say "This is a pen" or "These are pens" and drop the article in the plural. In French, the article is required: Ce sont des stylos. Omitting des and saying *Ce sont stylos is not grammatical. Train yourself to always attach an article to a French noun.

2.6 Color adjectives — introductory agreement

Purpose: use a color adjective after a noun, making it agree in gender and number.

French adjectives must agree with the noun they modify. In this chapter, you will practice agreement with color adjectives placed after the noun — the most common position for color adjectives.

The basic agreement rules for regular adjectives:

  • Masculine singular: base form → un stylo noir a black pen
  • Feminine singular: add -e → une gomme noire a black eraser
  • Masculine plural: add -s → des stylos noirs black pens
  • Feminine plural: add -es → des gommes noires black erasers

When the masculine form already ends in -e (such as rouge, jaune, rose), no further -e is added in the feminine — the form is identical. When the masculine form ends in -s (such as gris), no further -s is added in the masculine plural — again, the form is identical. As noted in the vocabulary section, orange and marron are invariable.

Pronunciation note: the -e added in the feminine often makes a previously silent final consonant audible. Compare:

  • vert (m. sg.) — final t silent → verte (f. sg.) — final t pronounced
  • noir (m. sg.) — final r pronounced → noire (f. sg.) — same sound, -e is silent
  • blanc (m. sg.) → blanche (f. sg.) — irregular spelling; the c becomes ch

Model Dialogue

Contexte : Première session d'un cours de français. Le professeur, M. Lebrun, prend les choses en main. L'étudiante s'appelle Mia. Registre : instructions de classe formelles, questions de débutant.
Setting: First session of a French class. The instructor, Mr. Lebrun, takes things in hand. The student is named Mia. Register: formal classroom instructions, beginner questions.

M. LEBRUN Bonjour à tous ! Regardez le tableau, s'il vous plaît. Good morning, everyone! Look at the board, please.
CLASSE Bonjour, monsieur. Good morning, sir.
M. LEBRUN Très bien. Qu'est-ce que c'est ? Very good. What is this?
MIA Euh… c'est un… stylo ? Um… it's a… pen?
M. LEBRUN Oui, exactement ! C'est un stylo. Et la couleur ? Yes, exactly! It's a pen. And the color?
MIA C'est un stylo… bleu ? It's a blue pen?
M. LEBRUN Parfait. Un stylo bleu. Maintenant — qu'est-ce que c'est ? Perfect. A blue pen. Now — what is this?
MIA Vous pouvez répéter, s'il vous plaît ? Can you repeat, please?
M. LEBRUN Bien sûr. Qu'est-ce que c'est ? Of course. What is this?
MIA C'est… une gomme. Une gomme blanche. It's… an eraser. A white eraser.
M. LEBRUN Très bien ! Et ça — sur la table — qu'est-ce que c'est ? Very good! And that — on the table — what is it?
MIA Ce sont des cahiers. Des cahiers… jaunes. These are notebooks. Yellow notebooks.
M. LEBRUN Excellent ! Prenez vos cahiers. On commence. Excellent! Take out your notebooks. Let's begin.

Questions de compréhension — Comprehension questions:

  1. Qu'est-ce que c'est — le premier objet ? (What is the first object?)
  2. De quelle couleur est le stylo ? (What color is the pen?)
  3. Qu'est-ce que Mia ne comprend pas au début ? Quelle expression utilise-t-elle ? (What does Mia not understand at first? What expression does she use?)
  4. Qu'est-ce qu'il y a sur la table ? (What is on the table?)
  5. Écrivez trois paires objet + couleur tirées du dialogue. (Write three object + color pairs from the dialogue.)
Grammar Note — Vos cahiers in the dialogue

At the end of the dialogue, M. Lebrun says Prenez vos cahiers — "Take out your notebooks." The word vos is a possessive determiner meaning "your (plural, i.e., belonging to all of you)." Possessive determiners are taught productively in Chapter 4. For now, recognize vos as a classroom instruction phrase and do not worry about producing it independently.

Reading Note — French across the Francophone world

The three profiles in this reading come from Belgium, Senegal, and France. All three use the same standard written French you are learning. Senegal is one of West Africa's most populous Francophone countries, where French is the official language of government, education, and media alongside Wolof and other national languages. Notice that the same core vocabulary — un stylo, un cahier, une trousse — is entirely shared across all three.

Writing Workshop

In this chapter you will write a classroom inventory — a description of the objects in your study space or an imagined one.

Identify: (a) the greeting, (b) how objects are introduced using articles, (c) how colors are used after nouns, and (d) the use of j'ai (I have — a form you saw in Chapter 1 formulaically). Use at least two different locations (dans mon sac, sur mon bureau, dans ma trousse). Include at least one plural (Ce sont des… or des + noun + color adjective).
  4. Revise. Check your draft against the editing checklist below before writing a final copy.

Orodha ya marekebisho — Editing checklist:

  • Every noun has the correct article (le / la / l' / les or un / une / des).
  • Plural nouns have a plural article and end in -s (or -x for -eau nouns).
  • Each color adjective agrees in gender and number with its noun.
  • I used C'est + singular or Ce sont + plural correctly.

Speaking Practice

Mazoezi ya udhibiti — Controlled practice: Qu'est-ce que c'est ?

With a partner, take turns pointing to objects in your real or imagined study space. The first person points and asks Qu'est-ce que c'est ? The second person answers using C'est un(e)… or Ce sont des… and adds a color when possible. Switch roles after ten questions.

Tâche communicative — Communicative task: Mon inventaire

Without looking at any written notes, describe the contents of your bag or desk to your partner using only French. Aim for at least six objects. Your partner will take brief notes and then report back on what you said, checking for accuracy. You may ask Vous pouvez répéter ? or Comment dit-on… ? if you need help. Then switch roles.

Kwa wanaojifunza peke yao — Solo alternative

Place six to eight objects on your desk or table. Record yourself pointing to each one and saying its name, article, and color in French: for example, C'est un stylo bleu. C'est une gomme blanche. Then group some objects together and use Ce sont des… Listen back to your recording and check: (a) Do you use the right article? (b) Do the color adjectives agree in gender?

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Classroom interaction in French-speaking settings

French educational traditions tend to value careful, structured interaction in formal learning settings. Students typically address instructors using vous and a title (Monsieur, Madame), and instructors may address a class collectively using vous as well. In less formal language courses or immersive environments, some instructors may use tu with individual students to encourage comfort with informal registers — but until you know which form an instructor prefers, vous is the safer choice.

Classroom norms vary across Francophone communities. In a university lecture hall in Paris, students may be more silent and less likely to ask questions publicly than in a smaller language course in Montreal or Dakar. Wherever you find yourself, the formulaic expressions from this chapter — Je ne comprends pas, Vous pouvez répéter ?, Comment dit-on… ? — are universally appropriate tools for managing your learning.

Culture in Context — Politeness in making requests

In French-speaking contexts, politeness and social courtesy are often signaled before a request, not only during it. Saying s'il vous plaît is the minimum, but adding a greeting first — Bonjour, vous pouvez répéter, s'il vous plaît ? — is more in keeping with French social norms than going directly to the request. This mirrors the broader cultural principle mentioned in Chapter 1: greet before you ask. Even in the classroom, opening with a greeting before your question shows social awareness. This small habit will serve you well throughout the course.

Regional Variation — Un cartable, un bic, and local school vocabulary

The word for a school bag varies by region: un sac is widely understood, but un cartable is the more traditional or formal term in France for a structured school bag, while un sac à dos (a backpack) is common everywhere. In Belgium, un bic — from the brand name Bic — is used informally for any pen or ballpoint, much as Stylo is used in France. When you travel or interact with speakers from different regions, you may encounter these alternatives. This course uses the terms most widely understood across the Francophone world; where a regional alternative is common, it will be labeled clearly.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using the wrong article for gender

Error: *un chaise, *la stylo

Correction: une chaise (feminine), le stylo (masculine). Gender cannot be guessed reliably from meaning or spelling. When you learn a new noun, learn its article at the same time. If you are not sure, check the vocabulary table rather than guessing. Consistent gender errors will affect everything attached to the noun — article, adjective, and later pronoun forms.

Common Mistake — Omitting the article entirely

Error: *C'est stylo. *Ce sont cahiers.

Correction: C'est un stylo. Ce sont des cahiers. French requires an article before the noun in nearly all ordinary contexts. The English speaker's instinct to say "It's a pen" automatically includes a in English — develop the same automatic habit in French.

Common Mistake — Adjective agreement errors

Error: *une règle vert, *des stylos bleue

Correction: une règle verte (feminine → add -e), des stylos bleus (masculine plural → add -s). The adjective must agree with the noun in both gender and number. English speakers are accustomed to invariable adjectives — "a green ruler," "green rulers" — so this requires active attention. Whenever you write an adjective, ask: What is the gender and number of the noun? Then apply the correct ending.

Common Mistake — Using c'est instead of ce sont

Error: *C'est des livres.

Correction: Ce sont des livres. Use c'est only before a singular noun (or noun phrase). When the noun is plural, use ce sont. In very informal spoken French you may occasionally hear c'est des…, but standard written and spoken French requires ce sont before a plural.

Common Mistake — Omitting accents in writing

Error: Writing ecoutez for écoutez, or fenetre for fenêtre.

Correction: Accents are part of correct French spelling, not optional decoration. Écoutez and ecoutez are not the same word to a French reader — the accent is part of the letter. Learn to type French accents using your keyboard's settings or an input tool. Consistently omitting accents signals to readers that your spelling is unreliable.

Looking Ahead

Ce que vous savez maintenant — What you can do now

You can now identify and name common objects in French, using the correct article for their grammatical gender. You can count from 0 to 20, describe objects using color adjectives with correct agreement, ask and answer Qu'est-ce que c'est ?, and follow basic classroom instructions in French. These skills — especially working with gendered articles — will support everything that comes next in the course.

Chapitre 3 : D'où venez-vous ? — Origin and Languages

Chapter 3 moves you from naming objects to talking about people: where they come from, where they live, and what languages they speak. You will learn the full present tense of the verb être (to be) and your first regular -er verbs — habiter (to live), parler (to speak), and étudier (to study). You will also learn the prepositions used with cities and countries (à Paris, en France, au Canada), and practice asking and answering information questions using où and d'où.

Loisirs, météo et projets

Leisure, Weather, and Plans · Speak and Write French: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Talk about sports, hobbies, and leisure activities you and others do.
  2. Describe basic weather using il fait, il y a, and other impersonal expressions.
  3. Use on for common inclusive reference, and explain its relationship to nous.
  4. Use the correct verb construction with faire de, jouer à, and jouer de.
  5. Form and use the near future with aller plus infinitive to say what you are going to do.
  6. Use vouloir, pouvoir, and devoir to express wishes, ability, and obligation related to plans.
  7. Invite someone to an activity, and accept or decline using natural expressions.
  8. Use j'aimerais as a polite formula for stating a preference.
  9. Understand a weather forecast and an event listing.
  10. Negotiate a simple plan in conversation and in writing.

Communication Scenario

A group of language-exchange friends — based in Lyon, Dakar, and Montréal — message each other every Friday to plan something for the weekend. The conversation always follows the same shape: someone checks the weather, someone proposes an activity, and the group negotiates a plan that works for everyone, sometimes adjusting because of weather or other commitments. This week, Camille in Lyon wants to organize something with her friend Lucas, who is visiting from Montréal. This chapter gives you the tools to do the same — to check the weather, propose an activity, and make a plan with someone using natural, everyday French.

  1. Think about your own weekend routines. What activities do you usually suggest to friends? What does the weather have to do with your plans? You will be able to discuss both by the end of this chapter.
  2. In Chapter 7, you used aller only to talk about where someone is going. In this chapter, aller takes on a second job: combined with an infinitive, it lets you talk about the near future — what you are going to do. Watch for this pattern throughout the chapter.

Pronunciation and Spelling

On — a pronoun, not three separate letters

On is a single nasal-vowel syllable [ɔ̃] — the same nasal vowel you practiced in mon and oncle back in Chapter 4, and again in bonjour from Chapter 1. It is easy for English speakers to want to pronounce it as two sounds, something like "ohn," but in French it is one continuous nasal vowel with no separate final consonant.

Sound Focus — On in context

Compare these short phrases, all built around on:

  • On va au cinéma. — We're going to the movies. The on is fully nasal; do not add an [n] before va.
  • Qu'est-ce qu'on fait ? — What are we doing? Listen for the nasal vowel gliding directly into fait.
  • On y va ! — Let's go! A very frequent spoken formula; all three words run together rhythmically.

When on is immediately followed by a vowel-initial word, mandatory liaison applies, and the normally silent final -n of on becomes audible: on a is pronounced o-na, and on aime is pronounced o-nème. This is the same liaison pattern you practiced with mon ami in Chapter 4.

Liaison after on in scheduled contexts

Because on behaves grammatically like il or elle (it takes a third-person singular verb), liaison after on follows the same mandatory pattern as liaison after other short, high-frequency function words. Practice these forms aloud:

  • on adore we love (it) — liaison: o-na-dore
  • on est we are — liaison: o-nè
  • on habite we live — liaison across the mute h: o-na-bite

Nasal vowels in weather and activity vocabulary

This chapter's vocabulary is rich in nasal vowels. Pay particular attention to the contrast between the three main nasal vowels of French, all present in this chapter's words:

  • printemps — contains both [ɛ̃] (prin-) and a silent final -emps; only the first syllable is fully voiced as a nasal vowel, and the final consonant cluster is silent.
  • vent [ɑ̃] wind — same nasal vowel as in grand from Chapter 4
  • on and football [ɔ̃] — the same nasal vowel as in mon and oncle

Distinguishing these three nasal vowels — roughly "an," "on," and "in" in spelling, each with a genuinely distinct sound — remains an ongoing production goal throughout the course. This chapter gives you more practice material in a new context.

Invitation and response intonation

Questions like Ça te dit de… ? typically rise in pitch toward the end, similar to other yes/no questions you have practiced. Responses like Avec plaisir ! carry an enthusiastic falling-then-rising contour, while Je suis désolé(e), mais… is usually delivered with a softer, more level pitch to signal regret rather than rejection. Listen for these contours in the Model Dialogue and try to imitate them rather than reading the lines in a flat, uniform tone.

Spelling note — qu'est-ce qu'on

The sequence qu'est-ce qu'on (as in Qu'est-ce qu'on fait ?, "What are we doing?") contains two elisions in a row: que → qu' before est-ce, and que → qu' again before on. Both apostrophes are required in writing even though the whole sequence is pronounced as one smooth phrase: kès-kon.

Grammar Instruction

12.1 On for inclusive reference

Purpose: use on to talk about what "we" do, in the natural, frequent way that French speakers actually talk in conversation.

You have seen on already in formulaic expressions such as On y va ! In everyday spoken French, on is the most common way to express "we" — far more common in conversation than nous. Grammatically, on is a third-person singular subject pronoun: it always takes the same verb form as il or elle, even though its meaning is usually plural ("we") or general ("people," "one," "you" in a general sense).

  • On va au cinéma ce soir. We're going to the movies tonight. (on + third-person singular va)
  • On joue au foot le samedi. We play soccer on Saturdays.
  • En France, on mange souvent du pain. In France, people often eat bread. (general reference)
Language Focus

On versus nous: Both can mean "we." Nous is more common in careful writing, formal speech, and contexts where the speaker wants to sound more deliberate or formal. On is overwhelmingly preferred in ordinary spoken conversation among friends, family, and peers — including in this textbook's dialogues from this point forward. The verb form is the key difference: nous requires the nous-form of the verb (nous allons), while on always uses the same form as il/elle (on va). Do not think of on as simply a lazy or incorrect replacement for nous — it is the expected, natural choice in most spoken registers, and you should default to it in conversation from now on. Nous remains important for formal writing and will continue to appear in the course.

Practice — on for inclusive reference

Rewrite each sentence using on instead of nous, changing the verb form as needed.

12.2 Faire de, jouer à, and jouer de

Purpose: use the correct verb-plus-preposition pattern to talk about sports, activities, and musical instruments.

French uses three different patterns to talk about leisure activities, and the choice is not optional — each activity is associated with a specific pattern that must be memorized along with the vocabulary item.

PatternUsed forContraction with à/deExample
faire demost sports and general activitiesde + le → du; de + la → de la; de + l' → de l'Je fais du vélo.
jouer àball games and team sportsà + le → au; à + la → à la; à + les → auxTu joues au tennis ?
jouer demusical instrumentsde + le → du; de + la → de laElle joue de la guitare.

These contractions are the same ones you already learned in Chapter 6 (au, aux, du, des) for location, and the same partitive forms from Chapter 8 (du, de la, de l'). The grammar is recycled; only the vocabulary context is new.

  • Je fais de la natation tous les week-ends. I swim every weekend. (de la + feminine noun)
  • Nous faisons du yoga le matin. We do yoga in the morning. (du + masculine noun)
  • Il joue au football avec ses amis. He plays soccer with his friends. (au + masculine noun)
  • Elles jouent au basket. They play basketball.
  • Tu joues du piano ? Do you play piano? (instrument → jouer de)
Common Mistake — Mixing up jouer à and jouer de

Error: *Elle joue de la guitare au piano. Or confusing jouer au piano with jouer du piano.

Correction: Sports and games take à: jouer au tennis, jouer au football. Musical instruments take de: jouer du piano, jouer de la guitare. A useful memory aid: you play a game at something (à), but you draw music out of an instrument (de).

12.3 The near future — aller plus infinitive

Purpose: say what you and others are going to do, using a simple and very frequent construction.

To talk about the near future in French, use the present tense of aller (which you learned in Chapter 11) directly followed by an infinitive. This is exactly parallel to the English "going to" construction, which makes it especially easy for English speakers to learn.

To make this construction negative, place ne … pas around the conjugated form of aller, leaving the infinitive untouched: Je ne vais pas jouer au tennis — "I'm not going to play tennis."

Grammar Note — Near future versus present for ongoing plans

French speakers use the near future (aller + infinitive) for a planned, anticipated future action, in much the same way English speakers use "going to." For a habitual or general statement about activities, the simple present is more natural: compare Je joue au tennis ("I play tennis," a general fact about you) with Je vais jouer au tennis samedi ("I'm going to play tennis Saturday," a specific planned event). This distinction will matter again later in the course when the simple future is introduced for less immediate plans.

12.4 Weather expressions with faire, il y a, and impersonal il

Purpose: describe current and forecasted weather using the standard set of impersonal expressions.

Most weather expressions use the impersonal subject il, which does not refer to any specific person or thing — it functions purely as a grammatical placeholder, the same way il functions in il y a (Chapter 6) and il faut (introduced later this chapter). Weather expressions fall into three patterns:

  • il fait + adjective: Il fait beau. Il fait froid. It's nice out. It's cold.
  • il fait du / de la + noun: Il fait du vent. Il fait du soleil. It's windy. It's sunny.
  • il y a + noun: Il y a des nuages. Il y a du brouillard. It's cloudy. It's foggy.

A few weather verbs, like pleuvoir (to rain) and neiger (to snow), conjugate only in the impersonal third-person singular: il pleut, il neige. You do not need to learn a full conjugation for these — they exist only in this one form in ordinary use.

12.5 Vouloir, pouvoir, and devoir — expanded use for plans

Purpose: express wishes, ability or permission, and obligation when making and negotiating plans.

You met vouloir in Chapter 8 for ordering food. This chapter expands its use, along with pouvoir (to be able to / can) and devoir (to have to / must), to the domain of plans and invitations. All three are irregular and are commonly followed directly by an infinitive.

Model Dialogue

Contexte : Camille (Lyon, France) écrit à Lucas (en visite de Montréal, Canada) pour organiser une sortie ce week-end. Registre : conversation informelle par message, tu entre amis.
Setting: Camille (Lyon, France) writes to Lucas (visiting from Montréal, Canada) to organize an outing this weekend. Register: informal messaging, tu between friends.

CAMILLE Salut Lucas ! Tu es libre ce week-end ? Ça te dit de faire quelque chose ? Hi Lucas! Are you free this weekend? Are you up for doing something?
LUCAS Salut ! Oui, je suis libre samedi. Qu'est-ce qu'on fait ? Hi! Yes, I'm free Saturday. What are we doing?
CAMILLE J'ai vu la météo — il va faire beau et chaud, vingt-cinq degrés. On pourrait faire de la randonnée ? I saw the forecast — it's going to be nice and hot, twenty-five degrees. We could go hiking?
LUCAS Avec plaisir ! J'aimerais bien voir la campagne autour de Lyon. Mais le matin, je dois faire les courses pour ma mère. I'd love to! I'd really like to see the countryside around Lyon. But in the morning, I have to do grocery shopping for my mom.
CAMILLE Pas de problème. On peut partir l'après-midi, vers quatorze heures. Tu veux faire du vélo après, ou c'est trop ? No problem. We can leave in the afternoon, around two o'clock. Do you want to go cycling after, or is that too much?
LUCAS Hmm, je ne sais pas. On verra comment on se sent après la randonnée ! Et dimanche, qu'est-ce qu'on fait ? Hmm, I don't know. We'll see how we feel after the hike! And Sunday, what are we doing?
CAMILLE Dimanche, il va pleuvoir, je crois. On pourrait aller au cinéma, ou rester à la maison et jouer de la musique. Tu joues toujours de la guitare ? Sunday, it's going to rain, I think. We could go to the movies, or stay home and play music. Do you still play guitar?
LUCAS Oui, toujours ! On joue ensemble, alors — tu fais du piano, non ? Yes, still! Let's play together, then — you play piano, right?
CAMILLE Exactement ! Parfait. Alors on se voit samedi après-midi pour la randonnée, et dimanche on reste à la maison s'il pleut. Exactly! Perfect. So we'll see each other Saturday afternoon for the hike, and Sunday we'll stay home if it rains.
LUCAS Ça marche. À samedi ! Sounds good. See you Saturday!

Questions de compréhension — Comprehension questions:

  1. Quel temps va-t-il faire samedi ? (What's the weather going to be like Saturday?)
  2. Pourquoi Lucas ne peut-il pas partir le matin ? (Why can't Lucas leave in the morning?)
  3. Qu'est-ce que Camille et Lucas vont peut-être faire après la randonnée ? (What might Camille and Lucas do after the hike?)
  4. Quel temps va-t-il faire dimanche ? Quel est le plan si c'est vrai ? (What's the weather going to be like Sunday? What's the plan if that's true?)
  5. De quels instruments est-ce que Camille et Lucas jouent ? (What instruments do Camille and Lucas play?)
  6. Relevez deux exemples du futur proche (aller + infinitif) dans le dialogue. (Find two examples of the near future in the dialogue.)
Grammar Note — On pourrait and on se voit

Camille's suggestions On pourrait faire de la randonnée ? and On pourrait aller au cinéma use a conditional form of pouvoir to soften a suggestion — roughly "we could." Like j'aimerais, treat on pourrait as a useful formulaic way to propose an activity politely; its full grammar is for a later stage. The closing line On se voit samedi ("We'll see each other Saturday") uses a reflexive verb, se voir, in an idiomatic way to mean "we'll meet." You will study reflexive verbs more fully starting in Chapter 7's review and again here receptively.

Reading Note — Community leisure across the Francophone world

Community cultural centers, free public sports events, and organized group outings are common across many Francophone communities, though their organization and funding vary by country and city. In Dakar, as in many West African cities, community centers often combine sport, music, and educational activities in a single shared space, and events are frequently free or low-cost to encourage broad participation. This contrasts somewhat with leisure patterns in other contexts presented elsewhere in this course, where private clubs or paid classes may be more typical. Avoid assuming that one city's pattern of public leisure represents the whole Francophone world.

Speaking Practice

Pratique contrôlée — Controlled practice: Quel temps va-t-il faire ?

With a partner, take turns. One person names a city and a season; the partner invents and describes plausible weather for that combination using at least two weather expressions. For example: Paris, en hiver — Il fait froid. Il y a des nuages. Switch after five exchanges.

Tâche communicative — Communicative task: On fait quelque chose ce week-end ?

With a partner, negotiate a weekend plan aloud. One person checks an imagined forecast and proposes an activity using an invitation expression; the partner accepts, declines, or proposes an alternative, giving a reason with devoir or pouvoir. Reach an agreement on at least one activity for Saturday and one for Sunday, using on and the near future throughout. Then switch roles and plan a different weekend.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Invitations and softened refusals

Across many Francophone communities, a direct "no" to a social invitation is often considered abrupt among friends and acquaintances. Speakers commonly soften a refusal with an apology (Je suis désolé[e]), a stated reason (j'ai déjà quelque chose), and frequently an offer to reschedule (une autre fois ?). This is not a universal rule, and directness varies by relationship, region, and individual personality, but the pattern in this chapter's dialogue and reading — apology, reason, alternative — represents one common and useful conversational shape for learners to recognize and practice.

Culture in Context — Leisure time and the weekend

Weekend leisure patterns vary considerably across Francophone communities and individual households. Many regions have widely observed practices such as Sunday family meals, organized sports leagues, or community religious or cultural observances, but no single pattern describes every household equally. Some communities place strong emphasis on family-centered weekend activities; others emphasize friend groups, solo pursuits, or community organizations. When describing leisure in French, attend to what the actual speaker says about their own plans rather than assuming a fixed cultural script.

Regional Variation — Sports vocabulary and seasonal patterns

Some sports vocabulary and seasonal associations shift by region. In Quebec and other parts of French-speaking Canada, winter sports such as skiing and skating occupy a larger place in everyday vocabulary and conversation than in many other Francophone regions, given the longer winter season. In some Francophone African contexts, football (le foot) holds an especially prominent cultural and social role, with widely shared public viewing of major matches. The core vocabulary taught in this chapter — faire de, jouer à, jouer de — applies consistently across these regions; only the relative cultural prominence of specific activities differs.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using nous-form verb endings after on

Error: *On allons au cinéma. *On faisons du vélo.

Correction: On va au cinéma. On fait du vélo. Even though on often means "we," it always takes the same verb form as il or elle — never the nous-form. Ask yourself: would I say il va or il fait? Use that same form after on.

Common Mistake — Wrong preposition with sports and instruments

Error: *Je fais le tennis. *Je joue le piano.

Correction: Je joue au tennis. Je joue du piano. Sports played as a game use jouer à with the correct contraction (au, à la, aux); musical instruments use jouer de with its own contraction (du, de la). Faire de is reserved for activities that are not ball games or team sports — swimming, cycling, yoga, hiking, and similar pursuits. Learn each vocabulary item together with its required pattern rather than guessing.

Common Mistake — Conjugating the infinitive after aller

Error: *Je vais joue au tennis. *Nous allons faisons du yoga.

Correction: Je vais jouer au tennis. Nous allons faire du yoga. Only aller is conjugated in the near-future construction; the second verb stays in the infinitive (unconjugated) form. Check every near-future sentence: only one verb should change for the subject.

Common Mistake — Adding a pronounced consonant after on

Error: Pronouncing on as "ohn" with an audible final [n] in all contexts.

Correction: On is a pure nasal vowel before a consonant or a pause: on joue has no audible [n]. The [n] sound only appears through mandatory liaison before a vowel-initial word, as in on a or on est. Listen for the difference and avoid adding a consonant where none belongs.

Common Mistake — Using être for weather

Error: *Il est beau aujourd'hui to mean "the weather is nice."

Correction: Il fait beau aujourd'hui. French uses faire, not être, for most weather descriptions. Il est beau describes a person or object as good-looking, not the weather. Reach for il fait whenever you are describing general weather conditions with an adjective.

Dialogue — Comprehension

  1. Samedi, il va faire beau et chaud, vingt-cinq degrés.
  2. Parce qu'il doit faire les courses pour sa mère le matin.
  3. Ils vont peut-être faire du vélo, ou attendre de voir comment ils se sentent.
  4. Il va pleuvoir. Le plan : rester à la maison et jouer de la musique (ou aller au cinéma).
  5. Lucas joue de la guitare ; Camille joue du piano.
  6. Exemples : il va faire beau, on va aller au cinéma, il va pleuvoir. Any two are correct.

Listening

  • Samedi matin — vingt-cinq degrés (approximatif), un peu de vent
  • Samedi après-midi — vingt-cinq degrés, du soleil
  • Dimanche — seize degrés, pluie toute la journée, beaucoup de nuages
  • La semaine prochaine — environ vingt degrés, du soleil et un peu de vent

Near-future examples: il va faire beau et chaud, le temps va changer, il va pleuvoir. Any reasonable subset is correct.

Reading — Comprehension

  1. Le match de basket commence à seize heures.
  2. S'il pleut dimanche, la randonnée est annulée et remplacée par un après-midi cinéma.
  3. Karim doit rentrer tôt samedi soir, donc il ne peut pas aller au concert.
  4. Karim accepte la randonnée. Il exprime son accord et propose une solution si le temps change, sans refuser l'activité.
  5. Des cours de yoga gratuits tous les matins et des cours de guitare pour débutants le mardi et le jeudi soir.

Looking Ahead

You can now discuss leisure activities and weather, propose and negotiate plans, and talk about the near future using aller plus infinitive. Together with what you learned in Chapters 1–11, you can now navigate the city, give and follow directions, and make social plans — core tools for participating in everyday community life in French.

À table !

Food and Drink · Speak and Write French: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify common foods and drinks and state what you eat and drink, using correct articles.
  2. Distinguish between definite, indefinite, and partitive articles and select the correct one for a given communicative context.
  3. Use partitive articles — du, de la, de l' — to express an unspecified amount of a food or drink.
  4. Apply the rule that articles change to de (or d') after a negation.
  5. Use quantity expressions such as un kilo de, une bouteille de, beaucoup de, un peu de followed by de without an article.
  6. Conjugate and use the verbs manger, boire, prendre, and vouloir in the present tense.
  7. Express food preferences with aimer, adorer, and préférer, understanding the article distinction between general and specific preferences.
  8. Use je voudrais as a polite formula for ordering or requesting in a café or market context.
  9. Use en as a pronoun replacing a previously named partitive or quantity expression.
  10. Ask about quantity with combien de.
  11. Understand and produce basic café and market interactions using appropriate courtesy and register.

Communication Scenario

It is Saturday morning. Madeleine, a student living in Lyon, stops at an outdoor market on her way to meet a friend at a café. She picks up bread, fruit, and cheese for the week, then joins her friend for a coffee and a snack. At the market she asks about quantities and prices; at the café she places an order politely. This chapter gives you the tools for both of those interactions — and for talking about what you eat and drink every day.

  1. In English you can say "I eat bread" or "I eat some bread" — and both are natural. French makes this distinction grammatically obligatory through its article system. Think about the difference in meaning between "I love coffee" (coffee in general) and "Would you like some coffee?" (an amount of coffee). French marks that difference every time with a different article. The Grammar section will show you how.
  2. You already know definite articles (le, la, l', les) and indefinite articles (un, une, des) from Chapter 2. This chapter adds a third type: the partitive article. As you work through the chapter, notice how each type of article signals something different about whether you mean a specific thing, one countable thing, or an unspecified amount.

Pronunciation and Spelling

Front rounded vowels in food and request vocabulary

Several food-related words in this chapter contain front rounded vowels — sounds that English does not have as distinct phonemes. The two most important for this chapter are the vowels in beurre (butter) and peu (little). Producing these sounds requires rounding your lips as you would for the back vowel [o] or [u], but simultaneously positioning your tongue as you would for a front vowel [e].

Sound Focus — Front rounded vowels: /ø/ and /œ/

French has two closely related front rounded vowels. The distinction between them is being neutralized in many contemporary spoken varieties, but the sounds occur in high-frequency words you will use immediately in this chapter:

  • un peu — the vowel in peu is the close-mid front rounded vowel [ø]. To produce it, say the English sound in "say," then round your lips without moving your tongue. The resulting sound is neither English "uh" nor "oo." Keep the lips firmly rounded.
  • le beurre, l'œuf, le cœur — the vowel in beurre is the open-mid front rounded vowel [œ]. Produce it by saying the English vowel in "bed," then rounding your lips. You will hear this in beurre (butter), sœur (sister, from Chapter 4), and œuf (egg).
  • Practical tip: Both sounds require rounded lips with a front tongue position. If your lips are not rounded, you are producing a different sound. Practice by exaggerating the lip rounding, then settle into a natural position.

Common English-speaker error: replacing these sounds with the unrounded English "uh" sound (as in "bird") or the back rounded "oo." Neither is close enough to be consistently understood.

Nasal vowels in quantity and food words

You studied nasal vowels in Chapter 4. This chapter introduces several new words with the nasal vowel [ɑ̃] (spelled an, am, en, em) and reinforces the nasal vowels you already know:

  • le pain — the ain spelling represents the nasal vowel [ɛ̃]. Do not add a final [n].
  • le vin — the in represents the same nasal vowel [ɛ̃]. Pain and vin are near-homophones, distinguished mainly by their initial consonant.
  • beaucoup de — the -an in the first syllable of beaucoup does not nasalize; the vowel sequence eau represents the back rounded [o]. This is a common surprise: spelling eau does not nasalize, but en before a consonant does.
  • une orange — the -an- in orange is a nasal vowel [ɑ̃].
  • la viande — the -an- is a nasal vowel [ɑ̃]; the final -de is pronounced.

Elision and enchaînement in ordering expressions

When you order at a café, you will produce strings of words that link together in characteristic ways. Two rules are especially visible in this chapter's language:

  • de l'eau — the article de la becomes de l' before a vowel-initial noun. This is elision: the a of la is dropped and replaced by an apostrophe. Pronounced as one continuous phrase: de-l'eau.
  • je voudrais un café — the word-final -s of voudrais is silent, but the n of un links to the following café: u-n-a-fé. This is enchaînement: a pronounced consonant at the end of a word links to the vowel-initial word that follows.
  • beaucoup d'eau — before a vowel, de in quantity expressions becomes d': elision applies to standalone de as well as to articles.

Polite request intonation

The formula je voudrais is used for polite requests throughout this chapter. In French café and market settings, the sentence typically ends with a level or slightly falling intonation, not the rising intonation you might use in English for a polite question. The politeness comes from the conditional verb form, not from a rising tone. Practice saying Je voudrais un café, s'il vous plaît with a steady, confident intonation that ends slightly lower than it began.

Selected mandatory liaison patterns

This chapter introduces several new high-frequency mandatory liaison contexts:

  • des œufs — the -s of des links to the vowel-initial œufs: pronounced dé-zœuf. This liaison is mandatory.
  • nous avons — already introduced in Chapter 4; the liaison consonant [z] is mandatory between the subject pronoun and the verb.
  • vous avez — same pattern: vou-za-vez.
  • beaucoup d'eau — not a liaison case (elision), but learners sometimes confuse the two. When an article or preposition loses its vowel before another vowel, that is elision (a spelling change with an apostrophe). When a normally silent consonant is pronounced before a vowel, that is liaison (a pronunciation change, no apostrophe change).

Grammar Instruction

8.1 The three article types — a systematic comparison

Purpose: understand when to use definite, indefinite, and partitive articles, and select the correct form for each communicative situation.

You have now met all three types of article that French uses before nouns. This section brings them together so you can see the full system clearly before learning the partitive in detail.

Article Type
Forms
Core Meaning
Typical Use

le / la / l' / les the

J'aime le café. (I like coffee — in general.) Le café est chaud. (The coffee is hot — that specific one.)

8.2 Partitive articles — du, de la, de l'

Purpose: use partitive articles to express an unspecified amount of an uncountable substance.

The partitive article is formed from the preposition de combined with the definite article. It expresses an unspecified, non-counted portion of something — the equivalent of English "some" when referring to an amount of a substance rather than a whole, countable item.

ContexteFormeExampleEnglish
Masculine singulardu (= de + le)du pain, du beurre, du fromagesome bread, (some) butter, (some) cheese
Feminine singularde lade la viande, de la salade(some) meat, (some) salad
Masc. or fem. singular before a vowelde l'de l'eau, de l'huile(some) water, (some) oil
Plural (countable items used as substance)desdes pâtes, des fruits(some) pasta, (some) fruit

The plural partitive form des is the same as the plural indefinite article. In practice, the distinction between plural indefinite (des pommes = some apples, countable) and plural partitive (des fruits = some fruit, treated as a substance) is less critical than the singular distinction. Both change to de after negation (see section 8.3).

  • Je mange du pain le matin. I eat bread in the morning. (an unspecified amount of bread)
  • Elle boit de la bière. She is drinking beer. (some beer, an unspecified amount)
  • Tu veux de l'eau ? Do you want some water?
  • Ils mangent des pâtes. They are eating pasta.
Language Focus

Definite article for preferences, partitive for consumption: This is the key distinction to master in this chapter. When you say that you like, love, or prefer something — a general statement about your attitude — you use the definite article, because you are talking about the food as a category. When you say that you are eating, drinking, or taking something right now or habitually — you are referring to an actual amount — use the partitive.

Compare: J'aime le fromage. (I like cheese — in general, as a category.) versus Je mange du fromage. (I am eating cheese / some cheese — right now, an amount.)

The same person can produce both sentences in the same conversation. They are not contradictory — they serve different communicative purposes.

8.3 Articles after negation — everything becomes de

Purpose: change indefinite and partitive articles to de (or d') after a negative verb.

After a negation (ne … pas or other negative forms), indefinite and partitive articles change to de (or d' before a vowel). The definite article does not change after negation.

AffirmativeNegativeArticle type
Je mange du pain.Je ne mange pas de pain.Partitive → de
Elle boit de la bière.Elle ne boit pas de bière.Partitive → de
Il prend un croissant.Il ne prend pas de croissant.Indefinite → de
Nous avons des pommes.Nous n'avons pas d'oranges.Indefinite pl. → d'
J'aime le café.Je n'aime pas le café.Definite: no change!
Grammar Note — Why doesn't the definite article change?

The rule makes good communicative sense. After a negation, saying "I don't eat bread" (Je ne mange pas de pain) means "I eat no bread at all." The indefinite or partitive article — which refers to an amount — is negated: the amount is zero. However, "I don't like coffee" (Je n'aime pas le café) is a general attitude statement about the whole category of coffee. That category still exists even when the speaker dislikes it. The definite article signals "the category in general" and is not negated. Learners who say *Je n'aime pas du café are mixing these two functions.

8.4 Quantity expressions with de

Purpose: use quantity expressions followed by de (or d') without an article, to express specific amounts.

When a noun is preceded by a quantity expression, it is introduced by de alone — no article. This rule applies after expressions such as beaucoup de, un peu de, un kilo de, une bouteille de, un verre de, and also after combien de.

  • beaucoup de pain a lot of bread
  • un peu de beurre a little butter
  • un kilo de fromage a kilogram of cheese
  • une bouteille d'eau a bottle of water (elision before vowel)
  • Combien de pain vous faut-il ? How much bread do you need?
  • deux tranches de saumon two slices of salmon

Note that even when the item is normally introduced by a partitive, the quantity expression replaces the entire article: you do not say *un kilo du fromage. The quantity word already specifies the amount; no further article is needed.

8.5 Present tense of manger, boire, prendre, and vouloir

Purpose: conjugate four essential food-and-drink verbs and use them in the present tense for ordering and describing eating habits.

Pronom manger (to eat) boire (to drink) prendre (to take / have) vouloir (to want)
je / j' mange bois prends veux
tu manges bois prends veux
il / elle / on mange boit prend veut
nous mangeons buvons prenons voulons
vous mangez buvez prenez voulez
ils / elles mangent boivent prennent veulent

Manger inserts an -e- before the -ons ending (mangeons) to preserve the soft [ʒ] sound of the g. The same orthographic adjustment applies to all verbs ending in -ger (voyager → nous voyageons). Without the e, the g before o would be pronounced [ɡ] as in "go." Prendre has a distinctive pattern: the two ns in prennent are both pronounced, making the third-person plural clearly distinct from the singular. Boire and vouloir are irregular throughout; memorize their forms as a set.

Grammar Note — Prendre at the table

The verb prendre (to take) is very commonly used at the table and in service interactions in the sense of "to have" or "to order": Qu'est-ce que vous prenez ? (What are you having?), Je prends le plat du jour. (I'll have the daily special.). In this context, prendre is not about physical taking but about choosing a food or drink. It functions similarly to English "I'll have…" in a food context, and is more neutral than the distinctly polite je voudrais.

8.6 Expressing preferences with aimer, adorer, préférer

Purpose: express general food preferences using the definite article.

Verbs of preference — aimer, adorer, détester, préférer — describe your relationship to a category, not to a specific amount of a food. In French, this means they take the definite article, not the partitive. This is one of the most reliable rules in the French article system.

  • J'adore le chocolat. I love chocolate. (the category: chocolate in general)
  • Elle préfère le thé au café. She prefers tea to coffee. (two categories compared)
  • Vous aimez les fruits ? Do you like fruit? (the category: fruit in general)
  • Il déteste les épinards. He hates spinach.
  • Je n'aime pas le poisson. I don't like fish. (definite article remains after negation with preference verbs)

Note that préférer follows the accent-change pattern of -er verbs whose stem ends in é: the accent changes to è in all forms except nous and vous (je préfère, tu préfères, il préfère, nous préférons, vous préférez, ils préfèrent).

8.7 Je voudrais — polite ordering formula

Purpose: use je voudrais as a ready-made polite formula for ordering in service interactions.

Je voudrais is a conditional verb form meaning "I would like." It is substantially more polite than je veux (I want) in service settings, and is the standard way to order food, drinks, or items at a café, restaurant, or market in French. At this stage, treat it as a fixed, very useful formula — you will study the conditional tense systematically in a later volume.

  • Je voudrais un café, s'il vous plaît. I'd like a coffee, please.
  • Je voudrais de l'eau, s'il vous plaît. I'd like some water, please.
  • Je voudrais deux croissants. I'd like two croissants.
  • Est-ce que je pourrais avoir l'addition ? Could I have the check? (slightly more formal; treat as a whole expression)

In informal conversation between friends, je veux bien (I'd be happy to / yes, please) is used to accept an offer: Tu veux du café ? — Oui, je veux bien.

8.8 En — replacing a partitive or quantity expression

Purpose: use the pronoun en to avoid repeating a previously named partitive or quantity noun phrase.

The pronoun en replaces a noun introduced by a partitive article (du, de la, de l', des) or by a quantity expression (un peu de, beaucoup de, a number). It always occupies the position just before the verb — the same position as other object pronouns. For this chapter, focus on two common patterns:

  • Vous avez du pain ? — Oui, j'en ai. Do you have (some) bread? — Yes, I have some. en replaces "du pain"
  • Tu veux du fromage ? — Non, je n'en veux pas. Do you want some cheese? — No, I don't want any. en replaces "du fromage"; negation wraps around en + verb
  • Il y a des croissants ? — Oui, il y en a. Are there any croissants? — Yes, there are (some). en replaces "des croissants" after il y a
  • Vous voulez combien de pain ? — J'en voudrais deux tranches. How much bread do you want? — I'd like two slices (of it). en replaces "de pain"; the quantity "deux tranches" remains

Model Dialogue

Contexte : Un samedi matin à Lyon. Madeleine (25 ans, étudiante) fait ses courses au marché, puis retrouve son ami Sébastien au café. Registre : interaction de service (vouvoiement avec le vendeur) et conversation informelle (tutoiement entre amis).
Setting: Saturday morning in Lyon. Madeleine (25, student) does her shopping at the market, then meets her friend Sébastien at the café. Register: service interaction (vous with the vendor) and informal conversation (tu between friends).

— Partie 1 : Au marché — / — Part 1: At the market —

VENDEUR Bonjour madame, qu'est-ce que je vous sers ? Good morning, what can I get for you?
MADELEINE Bonjour ! Je voudrais du fromage, s'il vous plaît. Vous avez du brie ? Hello! I'd like some cheese, please. Do you have any brie?
VENDEUR Oui, j'en ai. Il est très bon aujourd'hui. Combien vous en voulez ? Yes, I have some. It's very good today. How much of it do you want?
MADELEINE Je voudrais deux cents grammes, s'il vous plaît. Et vous avez des fraises ? I'd like two hundred grams, please. And do you have any strawberries?
VENDEUR Oui, regardez — elles sont belles aujourd'hui ! Yes, look — they're beautiful today!
MADELEINE Parfait. Un kilo de fraises, s'il vous plaît. Et une baguette. Perfect. A kilo of strawberries, please. And one baguette.
VENDEUR Voilà. C'est tout ? Here you go. Is that everything?
MADELEINE Oui, merci. C'est combien ? Yes, thank you. How much is that?
VENDEUR Ça fait huit euros cinquante, madame. That comes to eight euros fifty, ma'am.

— Partie 2 : Au café — / — Part 2: At the café —

SÉBASTIEN Madeleine ! Tu as fait tes courses ? Madeleine! Did you do your shopping?
MADELEINE Oui ! J'ai acheté du fromage, des fraises et une baguette. Et toi, tu prends quoi ? Yes! I bought some cheese, some strawberries, and a baguette. And you, what are you having?
SÉBASTIEN Je ne sais pas encore. Je n'aime pas beaucoup le café le matin — je préfère le thé. Et toi ? I don't know yet. I don't like coffee much in the morning — I prefer tea. And you?
MADELEINE Moi, j'adore le café ! Je prends un café et un croissant. Me, I love coffee! I'm having a coffee and a croissant.
Le serveur arrive. — The server arrives.
SERVEUR Vous avez choisi ? Have you decided?
MADELEINE Oui. Je voudrais un café et un croissant, s'il vous plaît. Yes. I'd like a coffee and a croissant, please.
SÉBASTIEN Et moi, je voudrais un thé et des tartines, s'il vous plaît. And for me, I'd like a tea and some toast, please.
SERVEUR Très bien. Vous voulez du lait avec votre thé, monsieur ? Very good. Would you like some milk with your tea, sir?
SÉBASTIEN Non merci, je n'en prends pas. Juste du citron. No thank you, I don't take any. Just some lemon.

Reading

Read the following three short texts about food and eating. Each comes from a different Francophone context. Pay attention to how the writers describe their preferences, their routines, and the foods they eat.

Koffi (Abidjan, Côte d'Ivoire)

Chez moi, on mange beaucoup de riz et de poisson. J'adore la sauce graine — c'est une sauce traditionnelle à base d'huile de palme. Le matin, je bois du café au lait et je mange du pain avec du beurre. Je ne bois pas de jus d'orange — je préfère le café. Au déjeuner, on prend souvent du riz avec de la viande ou du poisson et des légumes. Le soir, il y a généralement de la soupe ou du riz encore. J'aime beaucoup la cuisine ivoirienne. Je ne mange pas de porc — je suis musulman.

Brigitte (Lyon, France)

Je suis végétarienne, alors je ne mange pas de viande et je ne mange pas de poisson non plus. Le matin, je prends des céréales avec du lait végétal — du lait d'avoine. J'adore le fromage, mais j'essaie de ne pas en manger trop — il y a beaucoup de matières grasses ! Au déjeuner, je mange souvent une salade avec des œufs ou des légumes. J'aime beaucoup les pâtes et les lentilles. Le soir, je prépare souvent des légumes rôtis avec du riz. Et pour le dessert ? J'adore le chocolat noir. J'en mange un peu chaque jour !

Nathalie (Montréal, Québec)

Le week-end, j'aime préparer un grand brunch avec ma famille. Je fais des œufs, du bacon, des toasts avec du beurre d'érable — c'est typiquement québécois ! On boit du café ou du jus d'orange. Mes enfants préfèrent le jus. Pendant la semaine, je n'ai pas beaucoup de temps, alors je mange souvent une soupe et un sandwich au déjeuner. Le soir, on mange du poulet ou du poisson avec des légumes. J'essaie de ne pas acheter beaucoup de sucre — mais j'adore le sirop d'érable sur tout !

Questions de compréhension — Comprehension questions:

  1. Pourquoi Koffi ne mange-t-il pas de porc ? (Why doesn't Koffi eat pork?)
  2. Brigitte est végétarienne. Citez deux aliments qu'elle ne mange pas et deux qu'elle aime. (Brigitte is vegetarian. Name two foods she doesn't eat and two she likes.)
  3. Dans le texte de Brigitte, trouvez l'exemple du pronom en. Qu'est-ce qu'il remplace ? (In Brigitte's text, find the example of the pronoun en. What does it replace?)
  4. Qu'est-ce que le beurre d'érable ? Est-ce que c'est typique de toute la France ? (What is beurre d'érable? Is it typical of all of France?)
  5. Comparez les petits-déjeuners de Koffi et de Nathalie. Qu'est-ce qu'ils ont en commun ? Qu'est-ce qui est différent ? (Compare Koffi's and Nathalie's breakfasts. What do they have in common? What is different?)
Reading Note — Food and regional identity in the Francophone world

All three writers describe their food in French, but the foods themselves reflect very different culinary traditions. The sauce graine that Koffi mentions is a West African palm-oil-based sauce found across Côte d'Ivoire and other parts of the region; it would not typically appear on a French restaurant menu. The sirop d'érable (maple syrup) and beurre d'érable (maple butter) that Nathalie describes are emblematic of Quebec, where maple products are deeply tied to regional identity and seasonal tradition. Brigitte's discussion of lait d'avoine (oat milk) reflects widespread contemporary dietary practices in urban France. None of these is the only or most authentic way to eat French — each reflects one community's relationship to food, tradition, and change.

Writing Workshop

In this chapter you will write a short meal plan or food description — a practical piece of writing that shows what you eat and drink on a typical day, with preferences and any dietary information.

  1. Analyze the models. Reread one of the profiles in the Reading section. For each sentence, identify: (a) whether the article is definite, indefinite, or partitive, and (b) why that article was chosen. Look especially for sentences that use aimer/adorer (definite article) alongside sentences that use manger/boire (partitive article).
  2. Plan your content. Think about a typical day of eating for you — real or imagined. For each meal (breakfast, lunch, dinner, and any snacks), note two or three foods or drinks. Also note one or two things you like in general (preference), and one or two things you do not eat (negation).
  3. Write your first draft. Organize your description by meal. Write at least six to eight sentences. Include: (a) at least two partitive articles used correctly, (b) at least one sentence with a preference verb (aimer, adorer, préférer) followed by a definite article, (c) at least one negative sentence using de, and (d) if possible, one use of en.
  4. Revise. Use the checklist below before writing your final copy.

Liste de vérification — Editing checklist:

  • After preference verbs (aimer, adorer, détester, préférer), I used the definite article (le, la, les).
  • After consumption verbs (manger, boire, prendre), I used the partitive article for uncountable items (du, de la, de l') or the indefinite article for countable items (un, une, des).
  • After negation, I changed indefinite and partitive articles to de or d'. I did not change the definite article after preference verbs.
  • After quantity expressions (beaucoup de, un kilo de), I used de alone — no article after the quantity word.
  • All required accents are present on every word (including â in pâtes, î in dîner, and the circumflex in other scheduled vocabulary).
  • Subject pronouns are present in every finite clause — I have not dropped them.
  • Verb forms match their subject pronouns.

Speaking Practice

Pratique contrôlée — Controlled practice: Mon repas préféré

With a partner, take turns asking and answering: Qu'est-ce que tu manges au petit-déjeuner / au déjeuner / au dîner ? Answer in complete sentences using the partitive article. Then ask: Qu'est-ce que tu aimes comme nourriture ? (What food do you like?) — answer with a preference verb and definite article. Switch after three questions each.

Jeu de rôle — Role-play: Au café

One person plays the server (le serveur / la serveuse); the other plays the customer. The server greets, asks what the customer would like, responds to requests (including using en if they don't have something), and gives a price. The customer orders using je voudrais, asks a quantity question (combien de…?), and pays. Then switch roles. Use vous for the service interaction.

Useful server expressions: Qu'est-ce que je vous sers ? (What can I get for you?) · Vous désirez autre chose ? (Would you like anything else?) · Désolé(e), nous n'en avons plus. (Sorry, we don't have any more.) · Ça fait… euros. (That comes to… euros.)

Pour les apprenants autonomes — Solo alternative

Record yourself describing what you eat on a typical weekday and a typical weekend day. Describe at least three meals across the two days. Include: two or three partitive articles, two preferences with definite articles, and at least one negative sentence. Then play back the recording and check: (a) Did you use the definite article after preference verbs? (b) Did you use partitive after consumption verbs? (c) Did negation produce de, not du/de la? Keep the recording as evidence for your portfolio.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Meals, schedules, and social customs

Meal timing and structure vary considerably across Francophone communities and individual households. In many parts of France, the main meal of the day has traditionally been at midday — though this practice has shifted substantially with changing work patterns. The concept of le déjeuner as a leisurely meal with several courses remains culturally significant in some contexts, particularly in family settings, on weekends, or in formal business lunches, even if it does not describe how most people eat every weekday. In West Africa, meal timing, structure, and the foods served vary greatly by country, region, religion, and family tradition. In Quebec, food culture blends French-Canadian tradition — prominent in dishes like poutine, tourtière, and products from the maple harvest — with North American patterns. Avoid assuming that any one Francophone food tradition represents "how French people eat."

Culture in Context — Greeting before ordering

In French service interactions — a café, a market stall, a shop — it is standard and socially expected to greet the server or vendor before making a request. Beginning an interaction with Un café, s'il vous plaît without a preceding Bonjour can come across as abrupt or rude. The standard opening is a simple Bonjour madame / monsieur followed by the request. The vendor or server will normally respond with their own greeting. This brief exchange of Bonjour is not merely politeness theater — it is considered the appropriate way to begin a transaction and acknowledges the other person as a person rather than a dispenser of goods. The same principle applies in other service contexts you have studied, such as asking for directions or requesting help.

Regional Variation — Menu vocabulary

The false cognate entrée deserves special attention because its meaning differs between France and North America. In France (and most of Europe), une entrée is the first course or starter — what English might call an "appetizer." The main course is le plat principal. In American English, however, "entrée" has come to mean the main course. This can cause real confusion when a Francophone speaker and a North American English speaker discuss restaurants in either language. In Quebec French, une entrée retains the European meaning of a starter. In the Francophone African restaurant context, the concept of distinct courses varies and the terms may be used differently. In all core chapter exercises, entrée means "starter."

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using a partitive article after a preference verb

Error: *J'aime du fromage. *J'adore de la bière.

Correction: J'aime le fromage. J'adore la bière. Verbs of preference — aimer, adorer, préférer, détester — express a general attitude toward a food category. The definite article signals "the category in general." The partitive is for actual amounts consumed: Je mange du fromage (I'm eating some cheese). The question to ask yourself is: am I saying I like something (general attitude) or that I am consuming it (actual amount)?

Common Mistake — Forgetting to change the article after negation

Error: *Je ne mange pas du pain. *Elle ne boit pas de la bière.

Correction: Je ne mange pas de pain. Elle ne boit pas de bière. After ne … pas, the indefinite and partitive articles become de (or d' before a vowel). The most reliable way to avoid this error is to say ne … pas de … as a fixed pattern as soon as you identify that a negation is involved.

Common Mistake — Changing the definite article after negation with preference verbs

Error: *Je n'aime pas de café. *Il ne préfère pas de vin.

Correction: Je n'aime pas le café. Il ne préfère pas le vin. After negation, indefinite and partitive articles change to de — but the definite article does not change. With preference verbs, you were using the definite article to mean "the category in general." Negating the preference does not change the category itself; it just changes your attitude toward it. So the definite article stays.

Common Mistake — Using an article after a quantity expression

Error: *un kilo du fromage, *beaucoup de la viande, *un verre du vin

Correction: un kilo de fromage, beaucoup de viande, un verre de vin. After a quantity expression, the noun is introduced by de alone — no article. The quantity word already specifies the amount, so no partitive or definite article is needed. This rule also applies to combien de, assez de, trop de, and similar adverbs of quantity.

Common Mistake — Using je veux instead of je voudrais in service interactions

Error: Using je veux un café when ordering at a café or restaurant.

Correction: Use je voudrais un café. While grammatically correct, je veux sounds blunt or demanding in a service context in French — similar to the difference between "I want…" and "I'd like…" in English. The formula je voudrais is the polite standard for ordering or requesting in a service interaction. This is a register issue, not a grammar error per se, but it affects how you are perceived in real interaction.

Common Mistake — Confusing menu and carte

Error: Asking for le menu when you want to see a list of all available dishes.

Correction: Ask for la carte to see all available dishes. Le menu in French typically refers to a fixed-price meal option (a set deal at a given price). If you ask for le menu, the server may bring you a card showing fixed-price meal packages rather than the full list of individual items. When in doubt, you can ask: Vous avez une carte ?

Looking Ahead

Ce que vous savez maintenant — What you can do now

You can now identify common foods and drinks, select the correct article type for different communicative purposes, use partitive articles for uncountable amounts, apply the article-change rule after negation, express food preferences with the definite article, order politely at a café or market using je voudrais, use quantity expressions with de, and begin using the pronoun en to avoid repeating a partitive noun phrase. These skills are foundational for any food, dining, or market interaction in French — and the article-selection rules you have learned apply throughout the language, far beyond food contexts.

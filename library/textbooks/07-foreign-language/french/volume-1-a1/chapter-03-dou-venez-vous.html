D'où venez-vous ?

Origin and Languages · Speak and Write French: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Ask and answer where someone is from using D'où venez-vous ? / D'où es-tu ? and Je suis de…
  2. Say where you and others live using the verb habiter.
  3. Say what languages you speak or study using the verbs parler and étudier.
  4. Conjugate the verb être in all persons of the present tense.
  5. Conjugate regular -er verbs in all persons of the present tense.
  6. State someone's nationality using the correct adjective agreement.
  7. Use the prepositions à, en, au, and aux correctly with cities and countries.
  8. Use the prepositions de, du, des, and d' to express origin.
  9. Ask basic information questions using où, d'où, quelle(s), and est-ce que.
  10. Introduce another person and provide key information about their origin and languages.
  11. Distinguish the sounds /y/ (in tu) and /u/ (in vous) and recognize silent verb endings in the present tense.

Communication Scenario

At an international French-language workshop in Geneva, participants from different countries spend the first morning getting to know each other. They ask where people are from, where they live now, what languages they speak, and what brought them to study French. This chapter gives you the tools to take part in exactly this kind of exchange — the most fundamental conversation in any language-learning journey.

  1. Think about the countries and cities you know in the French-speaking world. Before looking at the vocabulary, write down as many as you can in English. Then check your list against the countries and regions in this chapter. How many were you already familiar with?
  2. In English, "I speak," "you speak," and "she speaks" differ only by the final -s in the third-person form. French has more written endings — but how many of them are actually pronounced differently? Keep this question in mind as you work through the grammar section.

Pronunciation and Spelling

The vowels /y/ and /u/ — tu versus vous

Two French vowel sounds that English speakers must learn to distinguish clearly are the vowels in tu and vous. English does not have either sound as a distinct phoneme, so both require conscious practice.

The vowel in tu is written u (when not followed by a nasal consonant) and represents the sound /y/. To produce it, start with the shape you would use for the English word ee — lips spread, tongue forward and high. Now, without moving your tongue at all, round your lips as if to say oo. The result is a sound that does not exist in English: front, high, and rounded. The spelling is always u.

The vowel in vous is written ou and represents the sound /u/. This is a back, rounded sound — your tongue moves back and your lips round. It is somewhat like the vowel in English food or boot, but without diphthong movement. Keep the sound steady and pure.

Sound Focus — /y/ and /u/: the key distinction

Practice the following pairs. The first word uses u = /y/ (front rounded); the second uses ou = /u/ (back rounded).

  • tu (you, informal) — tout (all / everything)
  • du (of the / some) — doux (soft)
  • vu (seen) — vous (you, formal/plural)
  • nu (bare) — nous (we)

For /y/: think ee position, round your lips. For /u/: think oo, tongue back. Do not let either vowel glide into a diphthong — French vowels are held steady.

These two vowels matter for communication. Saying tu instead of vous (or vice versa) changes who you are addressing and the social register of your speech. They must be clearly different.

Nasal vowels in countries and nationalities

Several country and nationality words in this chapter contain nasal vowels — vowels pronounced with air flowing through the nose as well as the mouth. French has three or four nasal vowels, depending on the variety; the three you will encounter most in this chapter are:

  • /ɑ̃/ spelled an or en — as in français, allemand, anglais, Genève (not nasal here — note this is a point to check).
  • /ɛ̃/ spelled in, ain, or ein — as in américain, marocain, ivoirien (the final syllable).
  • /ɔ̃/ spelled on — as in Montréal (the first syllable).

An important rule: when a nasal vowel spelling is followed by a double consonant (nn, mm) or by a vowel, the nasal quality disappears. Compare canadien (the ien ending is nasal) with canadienne (the vowel is now followed by a double n and a final -e, making it an ordinary oral vowel). Do not add a separate nasal consonant sound after the nasal vowel. The nasality is in the vowel itself, not in a following [n] or [m].

Silent verb endings in the present tense

One of the most important sound-spelling facts to internalize this chapter: the present-tense endings of most French verbs are largely silent in ordinary speech. Look at the regular -er verb paradigm you will study in the grammar section:

  • je parle — final -e is silent. You hear: [parl]
  • tu parles — final -es is silent. You also hear: [parl]
  • il / elle parle — final -e is silent. You hear: [parl]
  • nous parlons — the -ons ending IS pronounced (nasal): [parlɔ̃]
  • vous parlez — the -ez ending IS pronounced as /e/: [parle]
  • ils / elles parlent — final -ent is silent. You also hear: [parl]

This means that in speech, je parle, tu parles, il parle, and ils parlent all sound identical: [parl]. The differences between these forms are visible in writing but not in ordinary speech. The subject pronoun therefore becomes essential for meaning — without je or il, the listener cannot determine who is speaking.

Grammar Instruction

3.1 The verb être — full present tense

Purpose: use être (to be) in all persons to express identity, nationality, origin, and description.

You used singular forms of être in Chapters 1 and 2 (c'est, je suis). This chapter introduces the complete present tense. Être is highly irregular — each form must be memorized individually.

PronomForme écritePrononciation approximativeEnglish
jesuis[swi]I am
tues[ɛ]you are (informal)
il / elle / onest[ɛ]he / she / one is
noussommes[sɔm]we are
vousêtes[ɛt]you are (formal / plural)
ils / ellessont[sɔ̃]they are

The forms tu es and il est sound similar ([ɛ]) but are spelled differently. The form vous êtes has a circumflex on the ê. The final -t of est is silent in isolation, but it may link to a following vowel: il est algérien → the t links to al-. This is mandatory liaison and you will study liaison systematically later; for now, simply note this linking in fixed expressions.

  • Je suis française. I am French. (speaker: female)
  • Tu es canadien ? Are you Canadian? (informal)
  • Il est de Dakar. He is from Dakar.
  • Nous sommes belges. We are Belgian.
  • Vous êtes suisse ? Are you Swiss? (formal)
  • Elles sont américaines. They are American. (all female)
Grammar Note — French always requires a subject pronoun

Unlike Spanish or Italian, French verbs almost always require an overt subject pronoun in ordinary sentences. In Spanish, you can say Soy francesa (I am French) without the subject pronoun yo. In French, you must say Je suis française — dropping je is not standard. This is because many French verb endings sound the same (as you saw in the pronunciation section), so the subject pronoun is essential for communicating who is performing the action. Always include the pronoun.

3.2 Regular -er verbs — present tense

Purpose: conjugate any regular -er verb in the present tense to talk about habitual activities, current states, and general facts.

The largest group of French verbs has infinitives ending in -er: parler (to speak), habiter (to live), étudier (to study), écouter (to listen), travailler (to work). To conjugate any regular -er verb in the present tense, remove the infinitive ending -er to find the stem, then add the endings below.

PronomTerminaisonparler (stem: parl-)habiter (stem: habit-)étudier (stem: étudi-)
je-eje parlej'habitej'étudie
tu-estu parlestu habitestu étudies
il / elle / on-eil parleelle habiteon étudie
nous-onsnous parlonsnous habitonsnous étudions
vous-ezvous parlezvous habitezvous étudiez
ils / elles-entils parlentelles habitentils étudient

Notice that j'habite uses the elided form j' because habiter begins with a mute h followed by a vowel sound. The same applies to any other -er verb whose stem begins with a vowel: j'étudie. The endings -e, -es, and -ent are all silent in pronunciation. The endings -ons and -ez are pronounced.

Grammar Note — How many spoken forms does a regular -er verb have?

Look at the forms je parle, tu parles, il parle, ils parlent. In writing, they look different. In speech, all four are pronounced identically: [parl]. This means that a regular -er verb in the present tense actually has only three distinct spoken forms: the singular / third-person plural base form (parl-), the nous form (parlons), and the vous form (parlez). This makes the spoken system simpler than it appears on the page — but it also means you must write the endings carefully and always use a clear subject pronoun in speech.

  • Je parle français et anglais. I speak French and English.
  • Tu habites à Montréal ? Do you live in Montreal?
  • Elle étudie le français à Genève. She studies French in Geneva.
  • Nous travaillons à Londres. We work in London.
  • Vous parlez arabe couramment ? Do you speak Arabic fluently?
  • Ils habitent à Paris. They live in Paris.

In French, the preposition you use before a country name depends on the country's grammatical gender and number.

3.5 Information questions — où, d'où, quelle(s), est-ce que

Purpose: ask basic information questions about origin, location, and language.

French has several strategies for forming information questions. In this chapter, focus on two reliable ones:

Strategy 1: Intonation question. In ordinary conversation, you can ask a question simply by raising your voice at the end of a statement. This is very common in spoken French at all levels.

  • Tu habites à Paris ? You live in Paris? (rising intonation)
  • Vous parlez espagnol ? You speak Spanish?

Strategy 2: est-ce que. Place est-ce que before a statement to turn it into a formal or neutral question. This is the most reliable strategy across registers and is always grammatically correct.

  • Est-ce que tu habites à Paris ? Do you live in Paris?
  • Est-ce que vous parlez espagnol ? Do you speak Spanish?
  • Est-ce qu'elle est française ? Is she French?est-ce que → est-ce qu' before a vowel (elision)

Question words. Combine a question word with est-ce que or with a simple intonation question:

  • Où est-ce que vous habitez ? Where do you live?
  • D'où est-ce que tu es ? Where are you from?
  • Quelle langue est-ce que tu parles ? What language do you speak?
  • Quelles langues est-ce que vous parlez ? What languages do you speak? (plural)

Quelle is a question adjective that agrees with the noun it accompanies: quelle langue (feminine singular), quelles langues (feminine plural). In speech, both forms sound the same: [kɛl]. A third question strategy — subject-verb inversion — appears in fixed expressions like D'où venez-vous ?; treat this as a memorized formula for now. The full system of inversion will be introduced later in the course.

Language Focus

Use est-ce que as your default question strategy. French has three main ways to form a yes/no question: rising intonation, est-ce que, and subject-verb inversion. All three are grammatically valid, but they suit different registers. Rising intonation is common in casual conversation. Inversion is more formal or written. Est-ce que sits in the middle — natural, clear, and appropriate in almost any situation. For the rest of this course, use est-ce que as your reliable default when you need to form a question, especially in writing. When you speak, intonation questions are also fine in informal contexts.

3.6 Nationality adjectives — agreement and capitalization

Purpose: correctly produce nationality adjectives as adjectives and as nouns, applying gender agreement and the correct capitalization rule.

Nationality adjectives follow the same agreement rules as color adjectives from Chapter 2: they must agree in gender and number with the person or persons described.

  • Il est français. Elle est française. He is French. She is French.
  • Ils sont marocains. Elles sont marocaines. They are Moroccan. (m. pl.) / They are Moroccan. (f. pl.)
  • Je suis américain. Mon amie est américaine. I am American (m.). My friend is American (f.).

When a nationality word is used as a noun — to refer to a person — it is capitalized:

  • Je parle avec un Français. I am speaking with a French person (man).
  • Elle est une Canadienne. She is a Canadian woman.

Remember: as an adjective, nationality words are lowercase: il est français, une ville française. As a noun referring to a person's nationality, they are capitalized: un Français, une Française. The language name is also always lowercase: le français, l'anglais.

Model Dialogue

Contexte : Pause-café lors d'un atelier de langue française à Genève. Fatou, participante sénégalaise, rencontre Marc, participant belge. Ils utilisent le vouvoiement car c'est un contexte professionnel et ils ne se connaissent pas.
Setting: Coffee break at a French-language workshop in Geneva. Fatou, a Senegalese participant, meets Marc, a Belgian participant. They use vous because this is a professional context and they do not know each other.

FATOU Bonjour ! Je m'appelle Fatou. Et vous ? Hello! My name is Fatou. And you?
MARC Bonjour, Fatou. Je suis Marc. Enchanté. Hello, Fatou. I'm Marc. Pleased to meet you.
FATOU D'où venez-vous, Marc ? Where are you from, Marc?
MARC Je suis belge — je suis de Bruxelles. Et vous ? I'm Belgian — I'm from Brussels. And you?
FATOU Je suis de Dakar, au Sénégal. Mais j'habite à Genève maintenant. I'm from Dakar, in Senegal. But I live in Geneva now.
MARC Ah, intéressant ! Et est-ce que vous parlez d'autres langues ? Ah, interesting! And do you speak other languages?
FATOU Oui, je parle français, wolof et un peu anglais. Et vous ? Yes, I speak French, Wolof, and a little English. And you?
MARC Je parle français, néerlandais et allemand. En Belgique, on parle plusieurs langues ! I speak French, Dutch, and German. In Belgium, people speak several languages!
FATOU C'est vrai ! Vous travaillez ici à Genève ? That's true! Do you work here in Geneva?
MARC Non, j'habite à Bruxelles, mais je travaille aussi à Genève quelquefois. Et vous, vous étudiez ici ? No, I live in Brussels, but I also work in Geneva sometimes. And you, are you studying here?
FATOU Oui, j'étudie les langues à l'université de Genève. Yes, I study languages at the University of Geneva.
MARC Quelle chance ! Bon, on retourne au café ? How lucky! Well, shall we head back to the coffee?

Questions de compréhension — Comprehension questions:

  1. D'où est Fatou ? Où habite-t-elle maintenant ? (Where is Fatou from? Where does she live now?)
  2. Quelles langues est-ce que Marc parle ? (What languages does Marc speak?)
  3. Pourquoi est-ce que Marc dit « En Belgique, on parle plusieurs langues » ? (Why does Marc say "In Belgium, people speak several languages"?)
  4. Est-ce que Marc travaille à Genève ? Expliquez. (Does Marc work in Geneva? Explain.)
  5. Dans le dialogue, trouvez deux exemples du verbe habiter et deux exemples du verbe parler. Écrivez les formes complètes. (In the dialogue, find two examples of habiter and two of parler. Write the complete forms.)
Grammar Note — On parle plusieurs langues

In the dialogue, Marc says on parle plusieurs langues — literally "one speaks several languages," meaning "people speak several languages" or "they speak several languages." The pronoun on is used here to make a general statement about people in Belgium. It takes a third-person singular verb form (same as il/elle). You will study on fully in Chapter 12. For now, treat on parle as a useful phrase meaning "people speak" or "one speaks." Do not try to produce other on constructions yet.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Nationality, language, and identity

In many Francophone countries, speaking French is not the same as having French nationality, and having French nationality does not mean French is one's only language or first language. In Senegal, French is an official language alongside Wolof and other recognized national languages. In Morocco, French coexists with Arabic (the official national language) and Tamazight (also official). In Canada, French and English are both official at the federal level, while Quebec has French as its sole official language. Across West and Central Africa, many people grow up speaking one or more local languages at home and acquire French through schooling or public life. This means that when you ask Quelle est votre langue maternelle ? (What is your native language?), the answer may be quite different from the language the person uses with you. Be curious and open rather than assuming.

Culture in Context — Tu et vous in first meetings

The choice between tu and vous at a first meeting depends on the context and relationship. In a professional or formal setting — a conference, a business meeting, an institutional workshop — vous is generally the safer and more respectful default between adults who do not know each other. In informal settings among peers of similar age — a language class, a student gathering, an informal social event — tu may be used more quickly, particularly among younger people or in casual atmospheres. Notice that in the model dialogue, Fatou and Marc use vous at a professional workshop even though they are both adults at a similar stage in their careers. When in doubt, use vous and wait for the other person to invite a switch to tu. In French, this invitation — On se tutoie ? (Shall we use tu?) — is a social gesture worth knowing.

Regional Variation — Québécois French and identity

In Quebec, French is not simply one of two official Canadian languages — it is a cornerstone of cultural and political identity. The Office québécois de la langue française promotes and protects French in Quebec, particularly in the workplace and in public signage. Québécois French has its own pronunciation features, vocabulary, and expressions that differ from French spoken in France or Belgium. For example, where a speaker from France might say une voiture for a car, a Québécois speaker might use the same word but pronounce it differently, or in informal speech use terms like un char. This course uses contemporary broadly understood French as its productive model, but you will encounter Québécois features in readings and cultural notes throughout the volume. When you meet a speaker from Quebec, their French is entirely correct — simply distinct.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Wrong preposition before a country

Error: *J'habite à France. / *Je suis à Canada.

Correction: J'habite en France. / Je suis au Canada. The preposition à is used only before city names. Before country names, the choice of en / au / aux depends on the gender and number of the country. Learn each country in this chapter with its preposition as a fixed unit: en France, au Canada, aux États-Unis.

Common Mistake — Dropping the subject pronoun

Error: *Parle français. / *Habite à Paris.

Correction: Je parle français. / J'habite à Paris. French requires a subject pronoun in virtually all ordinary finite clauses. This is different from Spanish or Italian, where the ending alone may identify the subject. In French, many endings are pronounced identically, so the subject pronoun is not optional — it carries meaning. Never omit it.

Common Mistake — Capitalizing nationality adjectives

Error: *Je suis Français. / *Elle est Canadienne.

Correction: Je suis français. / Elle est canadienne. When a nationality word is used as an adjective — as in Je suis français or une ville française — it is written in lowercase. Capital letters are used only when the nationality word is a noun referring to a person: un Français, une Canadienne. English capitalizes nationality adjectives in all cases ("I am French"), but French does not. This is one of the most common spelling errors for English learners.

Common Mistake — Using an article before a language name after parler

Error: *Je parle le français et l'anglais.

Correction: Je parle français et anglais. After the verb parler, language names are used without an article. However, in other contexts — as the subject of a sentence or after a different verb — language names do take the article: Le français est une belle langue (French is a beautiful language) or J'étudie le français (I study French). After parler, omit the article.

Common Mistake — Forgetting agreement on nationality adjectives

Error: *Elle est canadien. / *Elles sont américain.

Correction: Elle est canadienne. / Elles sont américaines. Nationality adjectives, like all French adjectives, must agree in gender and number with the person they describe. The feminine form of many nationality adjectives is not predictable from the masculine alone — you must learn the pair. For example, canadien → canadienne doubles the n; français → française adds -e and softens the sound; belge → belge does not change at all. Learn masculine and feminine forms together.

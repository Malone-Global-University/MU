Une journée ordinaire

Daily Routines · Speak and Write French: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Name common daily activities using routine verbs and tell what time they happen.
  2. Tell and understand clock time using il est… heure(s) and related expressions.
  3. Name the days of the week and express habitual activity using le lundi, le mardi, etc.
  4. Use frequency adverbs — toujours, souvent, quelquefois, rarement, ne… jamais — in the correct position.
  5. Conjugate and use common reflexive verbs in the present tense: se lever, se coucher, se laver, s'habiller, se réveiller, and others.
  6. Use selected high-frequency irregular verbs in the present: faire, prendre, venir, and consolidate regular -er, -ir, and -re patterns.
  7. Form negative sentences using ne… pas correctly in written standard French.
  8. Describe a daily schedule using time expressions with à, de… à, and parts of the day.
  9. Write a connected paragraph describing a typical weekday from morning to evening.
  10. Recognize that ordinary spoken French often omits ne, and understand why written standard French keeps it.

Communication Scenario

A language student in Geneva is preparing to stay with a host family in Dakar, Senegal for the summer. Before she arrives, her host family sends her a short written description of their daily schedule so she can plan around their routines. She writes back, describing her own typical weekday. This chapter gives you exactly what you need to write and understand descriptions like these — the vocabulary for everyday activities, the grammar for reflexive verbs and negation, and the tools for talking about time and schedules.

  1. Think about your own typical weekday. What time do you wake up? What do you do in the morning before leaving the house? What is your lunch routine? When do you go to bed? Even with the French you already know, can you express any of this? By the end of this chapter, you will be able to describe an entire day in French.
  2. Many common daily activities in French involve reflexive verbs — verbs where the action refers back to the subject. For example, "to wash oneself," "to get up," "to get dressed." These require a small extra word that English often omits. The Grammar section will introduce this system step by step.

Pronunciation and Spelling

Written present-tense endings and spoken forms

Chapter 3 noted that regular -er verbs have six written endings in the present tense but only two spoken stems for the singular and third-person plural. This chapter expands the pattern to -ir and -re verbs and reinforces a key principle: written French preserves morphological distinctions that spoken French has largely neutralized.

For regular -er verbs such as travailler, consider the endings:

  • je travaille, tu travailles, il travaille, ils travaillent All four share the same spoken stem [travaj]. The final -e, -es, -e, -ent are all silent.
  • nous travaillons, vous travaillez The -ons and -ez endings are pronounced and distinguish these forms clearly.

A similar pattern applies to regular -re verbs such as attendre:

  • j'attends, tu attends, il attend All three sound identical [atɑ̃]. The written endings -ds, -ds, -d are all silent.
  • ils attendent Also sounds like [atɑ̃d] — the -ent is silent, but the stem consonant d is now followed by the vowel-starting suffix and may be heard.

For regular -ir verbs such as finir:

  • je finis, tu finis, il finit Sound identical [fini]. The -s, -s, -t are silent.
  • nous finissons, vous finissez, ils finissent All three use the -iss- extension and are clearly distinct from singular forms.

The practical consequence for learners: when you hear a verb in conversation, context — subject pronoun, sentence meaning — usually tells you which person is meant. The written forms preserve distinctions that listening alone may not supply.

Sound Focus — Schwa in reflexive verb forms

Several reflexive verbs in this chapter contain the letter e in an unstressed position that may be pronounced as a schwa [ə] — a reduced, central, unstressed vowel — or in some positions dropped entirely in ordinary speech. The schwa is one of the most variable sounds in French, ranging from fully pronounced in careful speech to completely absent in casual connected speech.

Compare these two pronunciations of the same sentence:

  • Je me lève — careful speech: [ʒə mə lɛːv] — all three schwas possible
  • Je me lève — ordinary speech: [ʒm lɛːv] or [ʃmə lɛːv] — one or both schwas reduced or dropped

For production at this stage, aim for a light, reduced e in these positions rather than a full-quality vowel. You do not need to eliminate the schwa entirely; a slight reduction is natural and sufficient. Crucially, do not replace the schwa with a strong English vowel — saying "JUH muh LEHV" with English-style stressed vowels disrupts French rhythm significantly.

Notice also the reflexive pronoun se before a vowel: it shortens to s': s'habiller, s'appeler, s'endormir. The elision is both a spelling rule and a pronunciation rule: the two vowel sounds merge rather than clash.

Rhythm in verb groups: pronoun + reflexive pronoun + verb

Reflexive verb phrases in French create a characteristic three-unit sequence: subject pronoun + reflexive pronoun + conjugated verb. In connected speech, these three elements form a single rhythmic unit with prominence on the last syllable of the verb stem:

  • je me lève → [ʒm-LƐːV] — one rhythmic arc, emphasis on lève
  • tu te couches → [ty-tə-KUƩ] — one rhythmic arc
  • il se lave → [il-sə-LAV] — one rhythmic arc

Avoid inserting pauses between the pronouns and the verb. English speakers sometimes say "I — myself — get up" with stress on each word; French combines these elements into one flowing phrase. Practice each reflexive verb as a complete phrase from the start: je me lève, tu te lèves, il se lève.

Consonant clusters across pronoun-verb boundaries

When the subject pronoun ends in a consonant and the verb begins with one, a consonant cluster forms at the word boundary. These are normal in French and should be pronounced smoothly without inserting a vowel:

  • il se lève — [l] of il + [s] of se: say il-se as one smooth motion, not "ee-luh seh"
  • ils se lèvent — [z] liaison from ils links to se: [il-zə-lɛːv]
  • nous nous levons — the [z] liaison in nous avons also applies when we say nous nous before a vowel-initial verb

Orthography: apostrophes in reflexive verbs

The reflexive pronouns me, te, se elide to m', t', s' before a vowel sound:

  • je m'habille (not je me habille)
  • tu t'appelles (not tu te appelles)
  • il s'endort (not il se endort)

Write the apostrophe immediately against the preceding letter, with no space before the following word. This is a required convention — omitting the apostrophe or substituting a space is an error.

Orthography: days in lowercase and time punctuation

Days of the week are never capitalized in French unless they begin a sentence: Le lundi, je travaille. The L is uppercase because it starts the sentence; the day lundi itself would be lowercase in any other position.

Clock time in French is typically written with h as the separator between hours and minutes: 8h30, 14h00, 20h45. In running text, you may also see huit heures trente spelled out. Both conventions are standard; this chapter uses both forms so you can recognize each.

Complete written negation requires both parts: ne before the verb and pas (or jamais) after. Never write a negation with only one element in standard written French:

  • Je ne travaille pas le dimanche. ✓
  • *Je travaille pas le dimanche. — spoken informally, but not standard written French
  • *Je ne travaille le dimanche. — incorrect in any register

Grammar Instruction

7.1 Clock time — Il est… heure(s)

Purpose: ask what time it is and state when activities happen.

To ask for the time in French, say:

  • Quelle heure est-il ? What time is it? (formal / written)
  • Il est quelle heure ? What time is it? (neutral / spoken)

To state the time, use the impersonal subject il with être:

Heure
En français
1h00on the hour
Il est une heure.It is one o'clock. (une, not un)
2h00on the hour
Il est deux heures.It is two o'clock.
3h15quarter past
Il est trois heures et quart.It is quarter past three.
4h30half past
Il est quatre heures et demie.It is half past four.
5h45quarter to
Il est six heures moins le quart.It is quarter to six.
6h50ten to
Il est sept heures moins dix.It is ten to seven.
12h00noon

24h00 / 0h00midnight

Grammar Note — 12-hour versus 24-hour time

In everyday conversation across France and much of the Francophone world, the 12-hour clock is used with no distinction between AM and PM beyond context: Je me lève à sept heures (I get up at seven — clearly in the morning). When precision matters, speakers add du matin (in the morning), de l'après-midi (in the afternoon), or du soir (in the evening): à sept heures du soir — at seven in the evening.

Official timetables — trains, planes, radio schedules, cinema listings — use the 24-hour clock: 14h30, 22h15. Reading these is essential for practical life. Convert by subtracting 12 for hours above 12: 14h30 = 2h30 de l'après-midi; 22h15 = 10h15 du soir.

To say when something happens, use à before the time expression:

  • Je me lève à sept heures. I get up at seven o'clock.
  • Elle finit le travail à dix-huit heures. She finishes work at six PM.
  • Nous déjeunons à midi. We have lunch at noon.

To express a time range, use de… à…:

  • Il travaille de neuf heures à dix-sept heures. He works from nine to five.
  • J'étudie de deux heures à quatre heures de l'après-midi. I study from two to four in the afternoon.

7.2 Regular -er, -ir, and -re verbs — consolidation

Purpose: consolidate the present-tense patterns introduced in Chapters 3 and 4 and extend them to -ir and -re patterns needed for this chapter's routine vocabulary.

You learned regular -er verbs starting in Chapter 3. This chapter reinforces the pattern and introduces the two other main regular groups. Compare the three:

Pronom -er : travailler -ir : finir -re : attendre
je / j' travaille finis attends
tu travailles finis attends
il / elle / on travaille finit attend
nous travaillons finissons attendons
vous travaillez finissez attendez
ils / elles travaillent finissent attendent

Key observations:

  • -er verbs: -e, -es, -e, -ons, -ez, -ent. Singular and 3rd-plural endings are all silent. The nous ending -ons carries liaison: nous travaillons [nu-tra-va-jɔ̃]
  • -ir verbs (finir type): -is, -is, -it, -issons, -issez, -issent. The -iss- extension marks the plural. Singular endings -is, -is, -it are silent.
  • -re verbs: -ds, -ds, -d, -ons, -ez, -ent. The -d of the 3rd-person singular is silent — il attend [il atɑ̃]. Note that -re verbs lose the infinitive -re and add these endings to the remaining stem.

7.3 Irregular verbs: faire, prendre, venir

Purpose: use the three high-frequency irregular verbs essential for describing daily routines.

Grammar Note — Three verbs, three important patterns

Faire is highly irregular: faisons is pronounced [fəzɔ̃], not [fɛzɔ̃] — the ai shifts to a schwa sound before the -ons ending. Faites is unique and must be memorized. The third-person plural font [fɔ̃] sounds nothing like the infinitive.

Prendre doubles the n in the third-person plural: prennent [prɛn]. The singular forms prends, prends, prend all end in silence. Prendre is also the base for related verbs: apprendre (to learn), comprendre (to understand), surprendre (to surprise) — all follow the same pattern.

Venir also doubles the stem consonant in the third-person plural: viennent [vjɛn]. The -ien- in singular forms [vjɛ̃] contains a nasal vowel. A similar verb tenir (to hold) follows the same pattern exactly.

  • Je fais du sport le matin. I do sport / I exercise in the morning.
  • Elle prend le métro pour aller au travail. She takes the subway to go to work.
  • Il vient à quelle heure ? What time is he coming?
  • Nous faisons la cuisine ensemble le soir. We cook together in the evening.
  • Vous prenez un café le matin ? Do you have a coffee in the morning?

7.4 Reflexive verbs in the present tense

Purpose: use reflexive verbs to describe the actions you perform on yourself as part of a daily routine.

A reflexive verb (un verbe pronominal) is a verb whose action refers back to the subject. In French, this is shown by a reflexive pronoun placed before the verb. Each subject pronoun has a corresponding reflexive pronoun:

The reflexive pronoun is placed immediately before the conjugated verb. Study the full conjugation of se lever (to get up) as a model:

PronomFormeEnglishSpelling note

Language Focus

Why does se lever have accent changes? Se lever belongs to a group of -er verbs with a mute e in the stem — sometimes called verbes à e muet. In the stressed forms (all singular forms and the 3rd-person plural), the stem syllable receives stress and the e becomes [ɛ], represented in writing by a grave accent: lève, lèves, lèvent. In the unstressed forms (1st and 2nd-person plural), the stem e remains short and unstressed, so there is no accent: levons, levez. Similar verbs include se lever, se promener, se peser. Do not confuse this with verbs that use a double consonant instead, like s'appeler → je m'appelle.

Now study s'habiller (to get dressed) for a reflexive verb where no accent change occurs:

7.5 Negation: ne… pas and ne… jamais

Purpose: say what you do not do, and say what you never do, using standard written negation.

In French, negation in standard written form requires two elements: ne before the conjugated verb and pas (or another negative word) after it. Think of the negative pair as a sandwich surrounding the verb:

Language Building Block
Subject + ne + [conjugated verb] + pas + (rest of sentence)
Standard written negation · ne before verb · pas immediately after · applies to all present-tense verbs including reflexives
Je ne travaille pas le dimanche.
I don't work on Sundays.
  • Je ne mange pas le matin. I don't eat in the morning.
  • Il ne prend pas le bus. He doesn't take the bus.
  • Nous ne finissons pas avant dix-neuf heures. We don't finish before seven PM.
  • Elle ne se lève pas tôt le week-end. She doesn't get up early on weekends.

With reflexive verbs, ne comes before the reflexive pronoun (which comes before the verb), and pas follows the verb:

  • Je ne me lève pas avant sept heures. I don't get up before seven. ne + [me lève] + pas — reflexive pronoun stays with its verb
  • Il ne se couche pas tard. He doesn't go to bed late.
  • Nous ne nous dépêchons pas le dimanche. We don't hurry on Sundays.

To say "never," replace pas with jamais:

  • Je ne prends jamais le métro. I never take the subway.
  • Elle ne se couche jamais avant minuit. She never goes to bed before midnight.
  • Nous ne travaillons jamais le dimanche. We never work on Sundays.
Grammar Note — Spoken French and the omission of ne

In ordinary spoken French — casual conversation among friends, informal speech — speakers very commonly omit ne and use only pas: Je travaille pas le dimanche. Il mange jamais le matin. This is not a sign of carelessness or incorrect French; it is a systematic feature of informal spoken usage that is well established across France and other parts of the Francophone world.

However, for all writing in this course, and for careful planned speech, you should retain both elements: Je ne travaille pas. Il ne mange jamais. The full form is standard in written French across all registers. As you advance, you will develop a register-sensitive sense of when each form is appropriate. For now: write with ne; be ready to hear it absent in casual spoken French.

7.6 Frequency adverbs — position

Purpose: add frequency information to sentences about routines.

Most French frequency adverbs follow the conjugated verb in a standard declarative sentence. This contrasts with English, where frequency adverbs often precede the main verb. The position may feel unfamiliar at first, but it follows a clear rule:

  • Je mange toujours à midi. I always eat at noon. (toujours follows mange)
  • Elle se lève souvent à six heures. She often gets up at six. (souvent follows lève)
  • Il ne fait jamais la cuisine. He never cooks. (jamais after the verb, ne before)
  • Nous travaillons rarement le samedi. We rarely work on Saturdays.

Longer frequency phrases (tous les jours, une fois par semaine) are more flexible and can appear at the end of the sentence or, for emphasis, at the beginning:

  • Je fais du sport tous les matins. / Tous les matins, je fais du sport.
  • Elle vient ici deux fois par semaine. / Deux fois par semaine, elle vient ici.

Model Dialogue

Contexte : Échange de messages entre Aminata (Dakar, Sénégal), étudiante, et Léa (Genève, Suisse), qui arrive chez Aminata le mois prochain pour un programme d'échange. Elles se connaissent depuis quelques semaines par messages. Registre : informel, tu.
Setting: Message exchange between Aminata (Dakar, Senegal), a university student, and Léa (Geneva, Switzerland), who is arriving at Aminata's home next month for an exchange program. They have been in contact for a few weeks by message. Register: informal, tu.

AMINATA Salut Léa ! Pour que tu puisses organiser tes journées, je te décris ma routine normale. Je me lève tôt — en général vers six heures du matin. Je me lave, je m'habille, et je prends le petit-déjeuner avec ma famille vers six heures et demie. Hi Léa! So you can plan your days, let me describe my normal routine. I get up early — usually around six in the morning. I wash up, get dressed, and have breakfast with my family at around six thirty.
LÉA Oh, six heures ! C'est très tôt ! D'habitude, je me lève à sept heures et quart. Je ne me réveille pas facilement le matin ! Oh, six o'clock! That's very early! Usually I get up at quarter past seven. I don't wake up easily in the morning!
AMINATA Ah, ne t'inquiète pas — on ne commence les cours qu'à huit heures. Tu as le temps. Le matin, je prends le bus à sept heures vingt. L'université est à vingt minutes d'ici. Oh, don't worry — we don't start classes until eight. You'll have time. In the morning, I take the bus at seven twenty. The university is twenty minutes from here.
LÉA Et le midi ? Tu déjeunes à l'université ? And at noon? Do you have lunch at the university?
AMINATA Quelquefois. Mais le mardi et le jeudi, je rentre à la maison déjeuner. Ma mère fait la cuisine — elle prépare toujours quelque chose de délicieux ! On déjeune vers treize heures. Sometimes. But on Tuesdays and Thursdays, I come home for lunch. My mother cooks — she always prepares something delicious! We eat lunch at around one PM.
LÉA Et l'après-midi, tu as des cours ? And in the afternoon, do you have classes?
AMINATA Oui, de quatorze heures à seize heures trente. Après, j'étudie à la bibliothèque ou je rentre à la maison. Le soir, on dîne vers vingt heures en famille. J'aime bien cette routine ! Yes, from two PM to four thirty. After that, I study at the library or go home. In the evening, we have dinner as a family at around eight PM. I really like this routine!
LÉA Super ! Et tu te couches à quelle heure ? Great! And what time do you go to bed?
AMINATA En général vers vingt-deux heures et demie ou vingt-trois heures. Je ne me couche jamais après minuit en semaine — j'ai cours tôt le lendemain ! Usually around ten thirty or eleven PM. I never go to bed after midnight on weekdays — I have early classes the next day!

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Meal times and daily schedules across the Francophone world

Daily schedules vary considerably across Francophone communities, and meal times are one of the most striking differences for international visitors. In metropolitan France, lunch (le déjeuner) is traditionally the larger meal of the day and is often eaten relatively late — between noon and 2 PM. Many workplaces and schools build in a genuine midday break, and it is still common for people to return home for lunch. The evening meal (le dîner) is typically eaten between 7:30 and 9 PM, later than in many English-speaking countries.

In Senegal and other parts of West Africa, the largest meal of the day is often lunch, and families may gather together to share it. In Quebec, eating patterns are more similar to North American norms in some respects — dinner is commonly earlier than in France. In Belgium and Switzerland, schedules vary by community and workplace culture. When you visit or stay with a Francophone family, the best approach is to ask about their specific routine rather than assuming any single pattern.

Culture in Context — The weekend and leisure time

In France, the working week is legally capped at 35 hours for most employees, and most workplaces do not operate on Sundays. The two-day weekend (le week-end) — Saturday and Sunday — is standard, though schedules vary by sector. The word week-end is itself a borrowing from English; it is fully established in contemporary French and is used across all registers.

Sunday (le dimanche) retains a particular social character in much of the Francophone world, with many families gathering for a longer midday meal. In Senegal and other Muslim-majority Francophone countries, Friday (le vendredi) holds special religious significance, and schedules may differ accordingly. In these contexts, the working week and weekend structure can differ from the French model. Whenever your chapter's characters come from different Francophone communities, notice how routine and schedule reflect those communities' particular lives.

Regional Variation — Names for meals

The names and timing of meals vary across the Francophone world. In metropolitan France, the three main meals are le petit-déjeuner (breakfast, literally "the small lunch"), le déjeuner (lunch), and le dîner (dinner). However, in Belgium and parts of Switzerland, the midday meal may be called le dîner, and the evening meal le souper. In Quebec, le souper is very commonly used for the evening meal, and le dîner refers to the midday meal — matching the Belgian and Swiss usage. Le déjeuner in Quebec typically refers to breakfast.

The productive terms in this course are the metropolitan French model: le petit-déjeuner, le déjeuner, le dîner. When you travel or correspond with Francophone speakers from other regions, be aware that dîner may not refer to the same meal. This is not a question of correctness — both systems are entirely standard within their communities.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Forgetting the reflexive pronoun

Error: *Je lève à sept heures. *Il couche tard.

Correction: Je me lève à sept heures. Il se couche tard. In French, reflexive verbs require their reflexive pronoun every time they are conjugated — it is never optional. Without the reflexive pronoun, the verb changes meaning entirely: lever means "to raise (something)," coucher means "to put (someone) to bed." The reflexive pronoun is what signals that the action refers back to the subject. Always check that your reflexive pronoun agrees with your subject: je me, tu te, il/elle se, nous nous, vous vous, ils/elles se.

Common Mistake — Placing the reflexive pronoun after the verb

Error: *Je lève me à sept heures. *Il couche se tard.

Correction: Je me lève. Il se couche. The reflexive pronoun always comes immediately before the conjugated verb, not after it. In English we sometimes say "I get myself up" with the reflexive at the end, but in French the reflexive element precedes the verb without exception in affirmative statements and questions.

Common Mistake — Forgetting ne or pas in a negative sentence

Error: *Je travaille pas le week-end. *Je ne travaille le week-end.

Correction: Je ne travaille pas le week-end. Standard written French requires both ne and pas. Omitting ne is common in casual spoken French, but it is not appropriate in formal writing or at this stage of production. Omitting pas alone is not acceptable in any register — it does not create a negative sentence and is simply a grammatical error. Think of the two elements as a pair that always travel together in writing.

Common Mistake — Placing ne… pas incorrectly with reflexive verbs

Error: *Je me lève ne pas tôt. *Je ne me pas lève tôt.

Correction: Je ne me lève pas tôt. With reflexive verbs, ne comes before the reflexive pronoun, and pas comes after the conjugated verb. The order is: ne + [reflexive pronoun] + [conjugated verb] + pas. The reflexive pronoun is never separated from its verb by the negative elements.

Common Mistake — Capitalizing days of the week

Error: *Je travaille le Lundi et le Mercredi. *Le Samedi, je me repose.

Correction: Je travaille le lundi et le mercredi. Le samedi, je me repose. Days of the week are never capitalized in French unless they happen to fall at the very beginning of a sentence — in which case, the first letter of the word is capitalized simply because it opens the sentence, not because days require capitals. This is one of the most consistent differences between French and English capitalization.

Common Mistake — Using être instead of avoir for time

Error: *C'est sept heures. *C'est midi.

Correction: Il est sept heures. Il est midi. Clock time in French uses the impersonal subject il with être. The formula is always Il est… heure(s). Do not use c'est for clock time; that structure is used for identification and description of people and things, not for telling the time.

Common Mistake — Placing frequency adverbs before the verb

Error: *Je toujours mange à midi. *Elle souvent prend le bus.

Correction: Je mange toujours à midi. Elle prend souvent le bus. In French, short frequency adverbs (toujours, souvent, rarement, quelquefois) follow the conjugated verb. In English, these adverbs typically come before the main verb ("I always eat"), which causes many learners to place them incorrectly in French. The rule is straightforward: verb first, frequency adverb immediately after.

  1. je me lève / tu te lèves / il se lève / nous nous levons / vous vous levez / ils se lèvent. Accent forms: lève, lèves, lèvent (singular and 3rd-person plural — stressed syllable). No accent in levons, levez — the e is unstressed in these forms.
  2. (a) font (b) prends (c) venons (d) fait (e) prenez (f) viens
  3. (a) Je ne me couche pas tôt. (b) Il ne prend pas le bus le matin. (c) Nous ne nous levons pas avant six heures. (d) Vous ne faites pas du sport tous les jours.
  4. (a) Elle ne mange jamais le matin. (b) Ils ne regardent jamais la télé le soir. (c) Je ne me dépêche jamais.
  5. (a) Je travaille rarement le dimanche. (b) Elle prend toujours le petit-déjeuner à sept heures. (c) Nous allons quelquefois au cinéma le week-end.

Chapter Review — Partie C

  1. Thomas se lève vers quinze heures cette semaine. C'est inhabituel parce qu'il travaille de nuit et rentre à la maison à six heures du matin.
  2. Nadia fait du yoga le jeudi et le vendredi, de dix-sept heures à dix-huit heures.
  3. Exemples acceptables : Le jeudi et le vendredi, je fais du yoga (Nadia) ; Le lundi, le mardi, le jeudi et le vendredi, je me lève à sept heures (Chloé) ; Le mercredi, je n'ai pas cours l'après-midi (Chloé). Any complete sentence using le + jour for habitual action.

Chapter Review — Partie D, model answer

Ma cousine Élodie se lève à sept heures moins le quart du lundi au vendredi. Elle se lave et s'habille rapidement. Elle ne prend jamais de petit-déjeuner chez elle — elle prend un café à la boulangerie en route. Elle commence le travail à huit heures trente et finit à dix-huit heures. Le mardi et le jeudi, elle fait du sport de dix-huit heures trente à dix-neuf heures trente. Elle rentre à la maison vers vingt heures et dîne. Elle se couche rarement avant vingt-trois heures. Le week-end, elle se lève souvent vers neuf heures et demie. Other accurate, well-formed answers are equally acceptable.

Looking Ahead

Ce que vous savez maintenant — What you can do now

You can now describe a complete day in French — naming activities, stating times precisely, indicating frequency, and saying what you do not or never do. You have expanded your verb toolkit to include reflexive verbs, the irregular verbs faire, prendre, and venir, and regular -ir and -re patterns. Together with what you learned in Chapters 1–6, you can introduce yourself, describe your family and home, locate things in a space, and narrate an entire weekday. These tools cover a large portion of everyday practical communication.

Chapitre 8 : À table !

Chapter 8 takes you into the world of food, meals, and café interactions. You will learn partitive articles (du, de la, de l') for expressing quantities of food and drink, vocabulary for meals and menu items, and polite request formulas including je voudrais. You will also have a first encounter with the pronoun en in the context of quantity. The regular verbs and negation you have practiced in this chapter will recur throughout — expect to use prendre frequently in food contexts. Reviewing your time expressions will also help, as ordering and meal routines often involve scheduling.

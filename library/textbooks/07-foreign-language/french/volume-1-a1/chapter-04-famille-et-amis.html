Famille et amis

Family and Friends · Speak and Write French: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify and name members of the immediate and extended family, using the correct article and gender.
  2. State someone's age using the verb avoir and the correct number.
  3. Use the present tense of avoir (to have) with all subject pronouns.
  4. Use possessive determiners (mon, ma, mes ; ton, ta, tes ; son, sa, ses) to express family relationships.
  5. Apply gender and number agreement to common descriptive adjectives.
  6. Recognize and use the irregular adjective forms of beau / bel / belle and vieux / vieil / vieille in basic contexts.
  7. Distinguish between c'est and il/elle est when describing or identifying a person.
  8. Use stressed pronouns after c'est and in simple emphasis.
  9. Ask who someone is with Qui est-ce ? and answer with a description.
  10. Use numbers 41–69 to state ages.
  11. Write a connected paragraph describing a family or social group.

Communication Scenario

During an online language-exchange session, a student in Montréal shows her partner in Abidjan a photograph of her family at a celebration. She points to each person and describes them: who they are, how old they are, and what they look like. Her partner asks questions and then does the same with a photo of his own family. This chapter gives you the tools to do the same — to show someone a photograph of your family or a group of friends and describe the people in it.

  1. Think about a photograph of your own family or a group of people you know well. How many people can you describe right now using French from Chapters 1–3? Which information — relationships, ages, appearance — is missing from your current toolkit? You will have all of it by the end of this chapter.
  2. In English, the words his and her change depending on the owner: "his sister" and "her sister" are different. In French, the possessive agrees with the noun that follows it, not with the owner's gender. Think about why this might be. The Grammar section will explain it fully.

Pronunciation and Spelling

Nasal vowels in family and number vocabulary

Several words in this chapter contain nasal vowels — sounds produced with air flowing through both the mouth and nose simultaneously. Nasal vowels are one of the features that most clearly distinguish French from English, which has no phonemic nasal vowels. In English, vowels before n or m are slightly nasalized, but this is not a distinctive contrast. In French, it is.

The key point for learners is this: when a vowel letter is followed by n or m and then by a consonant, or appears at the end of a word, the result is often a nasal vowel — not a plain vowel followed by a separate [n] or [m] sound. The nasal consonant is absorbed into the vowel quality.

Sound Focus — Nasal vowels in this chapter's vocabulary

Compare these words from the vocabulary:

  • un (a, masculine article) — the vowel is nasal; do not add a final [n]. It sounds roughly like the French vowel in brun. Hold the nasality and stop.
  • mon (my, masculine) — nasal vowel, similar to a nasalized [ɔ]. Do not say "mohn."
  • grand-mère — the -an- in grand is a nasal vowel [ɑ̃]. The final -d is silent in the masculine form grand before a consonant.
  • oncle — the on- is a nasal vowel [ɔ̃]. The c and l that follow are both pronounced.
  • cinquante — both the in ([ɛ̃]) and the an ([ɑ̃]) are nasal vowels.

A common English-speaker error is to add a nasal consonant after the nasal vowel: saying "mohn" for mon or "grandmère" with an audible [n]. In French, the nasal vowel is the sound — the consonant letter is part of the spelling system but is not separately pronounced.

Consonants made audible in feminine adjective and noun forms

In Chapter 2, you learned that adding -e to a masculine adjective can make a previously silent final consonant audible. This chapter provides more examples from the new vocabulary:

  • grand (m.) → grande (f.) — the final d becomes pronounced: gran-de
  • petit (m.) → petite (f.) — the final t becomes pronounced: pe-tite
  • intelligent (m.) → intelligente (f.) — the final t becomes pronounced: in-tel-li-gen-te
  • sportif (m.) → sportive (f.) — -if → -ive: both the v and the final e are pronounced
  • gentil (m.) → gentille (f.) — the final consonant cluster is fully pronounced: gen-ti-ye

This pattern reinforces why learning the feminine form of adjectives — not just the masculine — matters for pronunciation as much as for spelling. The written agreement mark is often the signal of a real spoken difference.

Adjective-noun rhythmic grouping

In Chapter 2 you learned that an article and its noun form one rhythmic phrase. In this chapter you will add an adjective placed after the noun, creating a slightly longer phrase: mon père sympa, ma sœur intelligente. French phrase rhythm groups all of these words together into a single arc with prominence on the last syllable of the last word. Avoid placing heavy stress on individual words as you might in English. Say mon-pè-re-sym-PA as one unit, not "MON PÈ-RE SYM-PA."

Liaison in possessive determiners and plural patterns

The possessive determiner mon (masculine or feminine before a vowel sound) provides an important liaison opportunity. When mon is followed by a word beginning with a vowel sound, the normally silent final -n is pronounced and links to the following syllable:

  • mon ami — pronounced as if written mo-na-mi. The n of mon joins the following syllable.
  • mon oncle — pronounced mo-non-cle.

This is liaison — the pronunciation of a word-final consonant that is normally silent, when the next word begins with a vowel sound. Liaison here is mandatory: it is not optional after possessive determiners before vowel-initial nouns. You will study the full system of liaison in later chapters.

The same principle applies to plural forms: mes amis is pronounced with the s of mes linking to amis: mé-za-mi.

The ligature œ in sœur

The word for sister, sœur, uses the ligature œ — a single character combining o and e. This ligature represents a front mid-open rounded vowel and must be written as œ, not as oe side by side. It is the same sound found in words like cœur (heart) and peur (fear), which you will encounter in later chapters. Practice writing and typing œ correctly from the start.

Grammar Instruction

4.1 The verb avoir — present tense

Purpose: use avoir (to have) to state possession, express ages, and describe family relationships.

Avoir is one of the two most important verbs in French. It is irregular; you must memorize its forms. In this chapter, the most frequent uses are: j'ai un frère (I have a brother), and il a cinquante ans (he is fifty years old).

PronomFormeForme spoken noteExemple
je j'ai Elision: je ai → j'ai J'ai une sœur.
tu tu as Final -s silent Tu as un frère ?
il / elle / on il a / elle a / on a Final -a links to next vowel Elle a trente ans.
nous nous avons Liaison: nou-za-von Nous avons deux enfants.
vous vous avez Liaison: vou-za-vez Vous avez des frères ?
ils / elles ils ont / elles ont Liaison: il-zon / el-zon Ils ont trois enfants.

Notice that all four written singular endings (-ai, -as, -a, -a) represent only two spoken sounds: [e] for j'ai and [a] for tu as / il a / elle a. The plural forms are more distinct. As with être, French spelling preserves differences that speech has largely neutralized.

Grammar Note — Être versus avoir in common expressions

French uses avoir (to have) in several places where English uses "to be." This is a systematic difference, not an exception. The most important example in this chapter is age: J'ai trente ans — literally "I have thirty years" — translates as "I am thirty years old." You cannot say *Je suis trente ans. Similarly, many physical sensations and states use avoir in French: avoir faim (to be hungry), avoir peur (to be afraid). You will build this list gradually across the course.

4.2 Possessive determiners — mon, ma, mes ; ton, ta, tes ; son, sa, ses

Purpose: use possessive determiners to describe family relationships and ownership.

French possessive determiners agree with the noun that follows them, not with the gender of the person who possesses the object. This is the most important difference from English. In English, "his" and "her" depend on the owner: his sister / her sister. In French, sa sœur means "his sister" or "her sister" — the form is identical because it agrees with sœur (feminine), regardless of whether the owner is male or female.

Grammar Note — mon / ton / son before feminine vowel-initial nouns

Before a feminine noun starting with a vowel sound, French uses mon, ton, son instead of ma, ta, sa. This is a pronunciation rule: the forms ma amie or ta école would be awkward to say because two vowel sounds clash. So the language uses the masculine-sounding form instead: mon amie, ton école. The noun is still feminine — the possessive has simply shifted for phonetic reasons. Compare: ma sœur (consonant-initial) vs. mon amie (vowel-initial, but feminine noun).

4.3 Adjective agreement — expansion

Purpose: apply gender and number agreement to descriptive adjectives placed after the noun, and recognize irregular patterns for this chapter's vocabulary.

In Chapter 2 you learned the basic agreement pattern for color adjectives. The descriptive adjectives in this chapter follow the same logic, with some irregular patterns. The four key questions are always the same:

  1. What is the gender of the noun? (masculine / feminine)
  2. What is the number? (singular / plural)
  3. Does the masculine form end in -e already? If so, no further -e is added in the feminine.
  4. Does the masculine form end in -s already? If so, no further -s is added in the masculine plural.

Some adjectives in this chapter have irregular feminines that must be memorized individually:

  • sportif → sportive — -if becomes -ive; the v and final e are both pronounced
  • gentil → gentille — the l doubles; pronounced gen-ti-ye
  • sérieux → sérieuse — -eux becomes -euse; both vowels are distinct

The adjectives beau (handsome / beautiful) and vieux (old) are among the most irregular in French and deserve special attention because they have three forms in the singular alone:

4.4 C'est versus il / elle est — identifying and describing people

Purpose: use the correct structure to introduce or describe a person, distinguishing identification with a noun phrase from description with an adjective or occupation.

French has two main ways to connect a subject to descriptive information, and the choice between them is determined by what follows.

C'est + article + noun phrase: use this to identify a person by category, by relationship, or by name.

  • C'est mon père. This is my father. / That is my father.
  • C'est une médecin. She is a doctor. (identifying occupation as a category)
  • C'est ma sœur, Amina. This is my sister, Amina.

Il / elle est + adjective or bare occupation noun (no article): use this to describe a person directly with an adjective, or to state an occupation in a neutral, descriptive way without an article.

  • Il est grand et sportif. He is tall and athletic.
  • Elle est médecin. She is a doctor. (describing her role without an article)
  • Il est très sympa. He is very nice.
Language Focus

The key distinction: If what follows is a noun phrase — even a single noun with an article — use c'est. If what follows is an adjective or a bare occupation noun without an article, use il est / elle est. In practice: C'est un professeur (noun with article → c'est) versus Il est professeur (occupation without article → il est). Both are grammatical and mean roughly the same thing in context, but they operate by different rules. Notice that when you use il/elle est + occupation without an article, the occupation functions more like an adjective than a noun — it describes rather than categorizes.

Model Dialogue

Contexte : Échange linguistique en ligne entre Fatou (Abidjan, Côte d'Ivoire) et Julien (Québec, Canada). Fatou montre une photo de famille. Registre : conversation informelle, tu entre pairs.
Setting: Online language exchange between Fatou (Abidjan, Côte d'Ivoire) and Julien (Québec, Canada). Fatou shows a family photo. Register: informal conversation, tu between peers.

FATOU Julien, regarde — c'est ma famille ! C'est une photo du mariage de mon cousin. Julien, look — this is my family! This is a photo from my cousin's wedding.
JULIEN Oh, super ! C'est lui, là — l'homme à gauche ? Oh, great! Is that him, over there — the man on the left?
FATOU Oui, c'est lui. Il s'appelle Omar. Il a vingt-six ans. Et c'est elle, sa femme — elle s'appelle Nadia. Yes, that's him. His name is Omar. He is twenty-six. And that's her, his wife — her name is Nadia.
JULIEN Elle est très belle ! Qui est-ce, la femme à droite ? She is very beautiful! Who is this, the woman on the right?
FATOU C'est ma tante, la tante d'Omar. Elle est médecin. Elle s'appelle Aminata. Elle a quarante-huit ans. That's my aunt, Omar's aunt. She is a doctor. Her name is Aminata. She is forty-eight.
JULIEN Et eux, là, les personnes âgées — ce sont tes grands-parents ? And those people there, the elderly people — are those your grandparents?
FATOU Oui, c'est eux ! Mon grand-père a soixante-neuf ans et ma grand-mère a soixante-cinq ans. Ils sont retraités. Mon grand-père est un vieil homme très sympa ! Yes, that's them! My grandfather is sixty-nine and my grandmother is sixty-five. They are retired. My grandfather is a very nice old man!
JULIEN Quelle belle famille ! Et toi, tu es là aussi dans la photo ? What a beautiful family! And you, are you in the photo too?
FATOU Oui — c'est moi, là, en robe verte ! J'ai vingt-deux ans. Et toi, tu as des frères et sœurs ? Yes — that's me, there, in the green dress! I am twenty-two. And you, do you have siblings?
JULIEN Oui, j'ai un petit frère. Il a dix-neuf ans. Il est étudiant à Montréal. Yes, I have a younger brother. He is nineteen. He is a student in Montréal.

Questions de compréhension — Comprehension questions:

  1. Qui est Omar ? Quel âge a-t-il ? (Who is Omar? How old is he?)
  2. Quelle est la profession d'Aminata ? (What is Aminata's profession?)
  3. Quel âge a le grand-père de Fatou ? Et la grand-mère ? (How old is Fatou's grandfather? And her grandmother?)
  4. Quel est le lien de Fatou avec Omar ? (What is Fatou's relationship to Omar?)
  5. Julien a-t-il des frères et sœurs ? Donnez des détails. (Does Julien have siblings? Give details.)
  6. Relevez quatre exemples de déterminants possessifs dans le dialogue. (Find four examples of possessive determiners in the dialogue.)
Grammar Note — Quelle belle famille ! in the dialogue

Julien's exclamation Quelle belle famille ! (What a beautiful family!) uses an exclamatory structure with the interrogative adjective quel / quelle (what, which) in combination with an adjective before the noun. The word quelle here is feminine to agree with famille. This exclamatory use is a high-frequency conversational formula; you do not need to analyze the full quel paradigm at this stage. Treat Quelle belle famille !, Quel homme sympa !, and similar patterns as useful ready-made expressions.

Reading Note — French across the Francophone world

The three profiles come from Belgium, Senegal, and Quebec. All three use standard written French, and the vocabulary for family members, ages, and occupations is entirely shared. In Senegal, as in many West African countries, it is common to have large extended families, and the household may include grandparents, aunts and uncles, and cousins alongside parents and children. The University Cheikh Anta Diop (UCAD) in Dakar is one of sub-Saharan Africa's largest and most prestigious universities, and French is the primary language of instruction. In Quebec, the word chum is used informally for a boyfriend or male partner, while blonde is used for a girlfriend — but standard terms like partenaire and mari/femme are understood everywhere in the Francophone world.

Liste de vérification — Editing checklist:

  • Each possessive determiner agrees with the noun that follows it (not with the owner).
  • I used mon/ton/son instead of ma/ta/sa before any feminine noun beginning with a vowel.
  • Each adjective agrees in gender and number with the noun it describes.
  • I used avoir for ages: Il a… ans / Elle a… ans.

Speaking Practice

Pratique contrôlée — Controlled practice: Qui est-ce ?

With a partner, take turns. One person describes a family member using three pieces of information — relationship, age, and one characteristic. The partner guesses or confirms. For example: C'est une femme. Elle a quarante-cinq ans. Elle est médecin et grande. Switch after five descriptions.

Tâche communicative — Communicative task: Ma famille en photos

Bring a real or fictional family photograph (or draw a simple diagram of a family tree). Present it to your partner. Qu'est-ce qu'il/elle fait ? (What does he/she do?). Then switch roles.

Il est… et très… Aim for at least five people and at least three different possessive determiners. Play back the recording and check: (a) Did you use avoir for every age? (b) Did your adjectives agree with the person's gender? (c) Did you use both c'est and il/elle est correctly?

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Family structures across the Francophone world

The concept of "family" varies considerably across Francophone communities. In France, and increasingly in urban areas across the Francophone world, many households consist of a nuclear family — two adults and their children — but many other configurations exist and are part of everyday life: single-parent households, blended families, same-sex partnerships, multigenerational households, and extended families living close together or even in the same home. In many communities in West Africa, the family unit commonly extends to include grandparents, aunts, uncles, and cousins under one roof or in close proximity. In Quebec, family vocabulary and structure have evolved significantly in the past several decades, and informal terms are widely used alongside formal ones. Describing a "family" in French requires attentiveness to who is actually in the room — never assume a universal model.

Culture in Context — Age and tactfulness

Asking about someone's age in French-speaking contexts follows similar social dynamics to many other cultures: it can be natural among family members or close friends, but may be considered impolite or inappropriate to ask directly in a first meeting, especially with adult women. The question Vous avez quel âge ? or Quel âge avez-vous ? (How old are you?) is perfectly grammatical, but social context determines when it is appropriate. In classroom and family-description activities, you may freely use ages; in real interaction, pay attention to whether the context makes the question welcome.

Regional Variation — Vocabulary for family members

A few family terms have well-known regional variants. In informal usage in France, the words maman (mum / mom) and papa (dad) are used across all ages, not only by young children. The word papa is broadly understood and widely used throughout the Francophone world. In Quebec, you may encounter ma blonde (my girlfriend) or mon chum (my boyfriend / partner). These terms are labeled here as regional and informal; the productive forms taught in this course (mon partenaire, ma partenaire, mon mari, ma femme, mon ami, mon amie) are understood everywhere. When you encounter regional vocabulary in conversation, recognize it without assuming it applies universally.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Making the possessive agree with the owner, not the noun

Error: *Il parle de sa frère. (He talks about his brother — but treating the owner as male, so using sa)

Correction: Il parle de son frère. The possessive agrees with frère, which is masculine — so son is required, regardless of the owner's gender. Ask: What gender is frère? Masculine. Does it begin with a vowel or consonant? Consonant. Answer: son. The owner's gender is irrelevant to this choice.

Common Mistake — Using ma/ta/sa before a vowel-initial feminine noun

Error: *ma amie, *ta école

Correction: mon amie, ton école. Before any noun beginning with a vowel sound — even a feminine noun — use mon, ton, son instead of ma, ta, sa. This is a phonological rule to avoid two consecutive vowel sounds. The noun remains feminine (amie is still feminine); only the possessive form shifts.

Common Mistake — Using être for ages

Error: *J'suis vingt-deux ans. *Elle est quarante-cinq ans.

Correction: J'ai vingt-deux ans. Elle a quarante-cinq ans. French uses avoir (to have) for age, not être (to be). The literal structure is "I have twenty-two years." This is a fixed French construction; there is no exception for people's ages. Every time you want to state an age, reach for avoir.

Common Mistake — Confusing c'est and il/elle est

Error: *Il est un médecin. *C'est très gentil.

Correction: C'est un médecin (noun with article → c'est) and Il est très gentil (adjective → il est). The rule: if an article is present, use c'est. If what follows is a bare adjective or an occupation without an article, use il/elle est. Check every sentence: is there an article after the verb? If yes, choose c'est.

Common Mistake — Wrong form of beau or vieux before a vowel

Error: *un beau homme, *un vieux oncle

Correction: un bel homme, un vieil oncle. Before a masculine noun starting with a vowel or mute h, use bel and vieil — not beau or vieux. These special forms exist for the same phonological reason as mon before a vowel: they avoid a clash of vowel sounds. Learn them alongside the feminine forms: belle / bel, vieille / vieil.

Common Mistake — Omitting subject pronouns

Error: *A trente ans. *Est médecin.

Correction: Il a trente ans. Elle est médecin. Unlike Spanish or Italian, French requires an overt subject pronoun in virtually every finite clause. The verb form alone does not make the subject clear in many cases, and French grammar requires the pronoun to be stated. Never drop the subject pronoun in a French sentence.

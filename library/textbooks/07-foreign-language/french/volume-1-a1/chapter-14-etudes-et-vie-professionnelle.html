Études et vie professionnelle

School and Work · Speak and Write French: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Discuss studies, school subjects, and qualifications using accurate vocabulary.
  2. Describe a workplace, an occupation, and everyday job responsibilities.
  3. Distinguish and correctly use savoir and connaître (both meaning "to know").
  4. Use the indirect-object pronouns lui and leur and place them correctly before a finite verb.
  5. Distinguish direct objects from indirect objects in meaning-based terms.
  6. Compare two study or work options using plus, moins, and aussi, including the irregular forms meilleur and mieux.
  7. Form basic superlative expressions appropriate to this level.
  8. Use the relative pronouns qui, que, and où to identify people, places, and tasks.
  9. Compose a short formal or semi-formal email about a study or work situation.

Communication Scenario

A student in Geneva is deciding between two options for next year: continuing her studies at university or taking a paid internship at a company in Lausanne. She writes to a mentor — a former teacher who knows her well — to describe both options, compare them, and ask for advice. The mentor writes back with a question and a brief recommendation. This chapter gives you the tools to do the same: describe your studies or work, compare two options clearly, and write an appropriate message to someone whose advice you value.

  1. Think of a real or imagined choice you have faced involving school or work — two classes, two jobs, two possible paths. What would you need to say in French to compare them? By the end of this chapter you will have the comparison structures, the vocabulary, and the message format to do exactly that.
  2. French has two different verbs for "to know" — savoir and connaître — and English speakers regularly confuse them because English uses only one verb. The Grammar section explains the difference and gives you a reliable test for choosing the right one.

Pronunciation and Spelling

Object-pronoun and verb grouping

When an indirect-object pronoun such as lui or leur is placed before a conjugated verb, the pronoun and verb form a single rhythmic unit, just as you saw with direct-object pronouns in Chapter 9. There is no pause between the pronoun and the verb: je lui parle is pronounced as one phrase, je-lui-PARLE, with prominence on the final syllable.

  • je lui téléphone I call him/her — pronounced as a single rhythmic group
  • nous leur écrivons we write to them — leur links smoothly to the verb

Relative-clause phrasing

Relative pronouns such as qui, que, and où begin a new phrase grouping but do not normally take a pause before them in natural speech — the whole sentence is said as a connected unit with a slight rise in pitch at the relative pronoun and a fall at the end of the sentence: C'est le travail que je préfère. Practice reading relative clauses aloud as a single connected breath group rather than as two separate sentences glued together.

Written endings versus spoken forms

This chapter reviews several verb forms whose written endings are not fully audible in speech, a pattern introduced in Chapter 7. For example, the three singular forms of regular -er verbs like étudier (j'étudie, tu étudies, il étudie) all sound identical, even though they are spelled differently. Continue connecting the written ending to the correct subject pronoun rather than relying on sound alone to identify the form.

Formal versus informal message rhythm

A formal email read aloud has a more measured, evenly paced rhythm than casual conversation, with fuller pronunciation of word-final sounds in liaison contexts and less reduction overall. Compare the brisk, contracted rhythm of casual spoken French (such as the dialogues in Chapters 7–8) with the more deliberate phrasing modeled in this chapter's email exchange.

Optional liaison (recognition)

In careful or formal speech — such as a job interview or a formal presentation — speakers sometimes pronounce a liaison that would be dropped in casual conversation. For example, a careful speaker might pronounce the normally silent -s in les étudiants followed by a vowel-initial word with extra clarity in formal contexts, or add liaison after short words like très where it would often be skipped informally: très_intéressant. This is called optional liaison — it is register-sensitive, not a fixed rule, and you only need to recognize it at this stage, not produce it on demand.

Grammar Instruction

14.1 Savoir versus connaître

Purpose: choose the correct French verb for "to know," since French — unlike English — uses two distinct verbs depending on what kind of knowledge is involved.

English uses one verb, "to know," for many different kinds of knowledge. French requires you to choose between savoir and connaître depending on what follows.

PronomFormeForme spoken noteExemple
jeje saisFinal -s silentJe sais parler français.
tutu saisFinal -s silentTu sais où est le bureau ?
il / elle / onil saitFinal -t silentElle sait la réponse.
nousnous savonsStem change: sav-Nous savons utiliser ce logiciel.
vousvous savezStem change: sav-Vous savez la date de l'examen ?
ils / ellesils saventFinal -ent silentIls savent travailler en équipe.
PronomFormeForme spoken noteExemple
jeje connaisFinal -s silentJe connais cette entreprise.
tutu connaisFinal -s silentTu connais mon collègue ?
il / elle / onil connaîtCircumflex on î; final -t silentElle connaît bien Genève.
nousnous connaissonsStem expands: connaiss-Nous connaissons le patron.
vousvous connaissezStem expands: connaiss-Vous connaissez ce professeur ?
ils / ellesils connaissentStem expands: connaiss-Ils connaissent la ville.
Language Focus

The reliable test: Use savoir for facts, information, and learned skills — especially before que (that), a question word, or an infinitive. Use connaître for people, places, and works (books, films) — things you become familiar with through experience, always followed directly by a noun phrase, never by an infinitive or by que. Ask yourself: is this a fact/skill (savoir) or a person/place (connaître)? Je sais où elle travaille (I know where she works — a fact). Je connais son bureau (I know her office — a place I have experienced).

  • Je sais parler trois langues. I know how to speak three languages. (skill + infinitive)
  • Tu sais où est la réunion ? Do you know where the meeting is? (fact + question word)
  • Nous connaissons cette université. We know this university. (place, direct noun)
  • Elle connaît bien ses collègues. She knows her colleagues well. (people, direct noun)

Practice — savoir or connaître

  1. Tu _____ mon professeur de maths ?
  2. Nous ne _____ pas la réponse.
  3. Vous _____ utiliser ce logiciel ?
  4. Ils _____ très bien Lausanne.
  5. Je _____ que l'examen est difficile.
  6. Elle _____ son patron depuis cinq ans.

14.2 Indirect-object pronouns — lui and leur

Purpose: replace a previously named person introduced by à with the pronoun lui (him/her) or leur (them), placed before the conjugated verb.

An indirect object is the person to or for whom an action is done, typically marked in French by the preposition à. Verbs like parler à, téléphoner à, écrire à, and répondre à take an indirect object when the recipient is a person.

Personne remplacéePronomEnglish
à + une personne (m. ou f., singulier)luito him / to her
à + des personnes (pluriel)leurto them
  • Je parle à mon collègue. → Je lui parle. I am talking to my colleague. → I am talking to him.
  • Tu téléphones à tes parents. → Tu leur téléphones. You call your parents. → You call them.
  • Nous répondons à la patronne. → Nous lui répondons. We answer the boss. → We answer her.
Grammar Note — Direct versus indirect objects

Distinguish direct and indirect objects by meaning, not by translation. A direct object receives the action with no preposition in French: Je connais mon collègue → Je le connais (using the direct-object pronoun from Chapter 9). An indirect object is preceded by à in French: Je parle à mon collègue → Je lui parle. The test: can you insert à before the noun in French without changing the meaning? If yes, it is indirect, and you need lui or leur. Some English verbs take a direct object where the French equivalent requires à, and vice versa — always check the French pattern, not the English one.

Practice — lui or leur

  1. Je téléphone à ma sœur. → Je _____ téléphone.
  2. Vous écrivez à vos collègues. → Vous _____ écrivez.
  3. Il répond à son professeur. → Il _____ répond.
  4. Nous parlons aux étudiants. → Nous _____ parlons.

14.3 Comparison — plus, moins, aussi, meilleur, mieux

Purpose: compare two study or work options using regular comparative structures and the irregular forms meilleur and mieux.

Reviewed from Chapter 9, the basic comparative pattern uses plus / moins / aussi before an adjective and que before the second item being compared.

Language Building Block
plus / moins / aussi + adjectif + que + élément comparé
Comparative degree · quality compared · second term of comparison
Ce stage est plus intéressant que mon dernier travail.
This internship is more interesting than my last job.

Two comparative forms in this pattern are irregular and must be memorized: meilleur(e)(s) (better), the irregular comparative of the adjective bon, and mieux (better), the irregular comparative of the adverb bien.

  • Mon salaire est meilleur cette année. My salary is better this year. (meilleur describes a noun — adjective)
  • Elle travaille mieux le matin. She works better in the morning. (mieux describes a verb — adverb)
  • Ce cours est moins difficile que l'autre. This class is less difficult than the other one.
  • Le droit est aussi intéressant que l'informatique. Law is as interesting as computer science.
Grammar Note — Meilleur versus mieux

English speakers often confuse meilleur and mieux because both translate as "better." The test: does the comparison describe a noun (use meilleur, an adjective that agrees in gender and number) or does it describe how an action is performed (use mieux, an invariable adverb)? Ce poste est meilleur (this position is better — describes the noun poste). Je travaille mieux en équipe (I work better in a team — describes the verb travailler).

Basic superlative expressions extend the comparative pattern with the definite article: le / la / les plus + adjective, or le / la / les moins + adjective.

  • C'est le cours le plus difficile. It's the most difficult class.
  • C'est la meilleure entreprise de la ville. It's the best company in town. (meilleure already carries the comparative idea)

Practice — comparison

  1. Mon stage est _____ intéressant _____ mon ancien travail. (more … than)
  2. Cette université est _____ grande _____ l'autre. (as … as)
  3. Le salaire ici est _____. (better — adjective)
  4. Elle parle français _____ que moi. (better — adverb)
  5. C'est _____ matière _____ difficile du programme. (the most difficult)

14.4 Relative pronouns — qui, que, où

Purpose: combine two short sentences into one connected sentence by identifying a person, thing, or place with a relative clause.

A relative pronoun connects a clause that gives more information about a noun already mentioned. French uses three high-frequency relative pronouns at this level.

PronomFonctionExempleEnglish
quisubject of the relative clauseJ'ai un collègue qui parle trois langues.I have a colleague who speaks three languages.
queobject of the relative clauseC'est le travail que je préfère.It's the job that I prefer.
oùplace or time of the relative clauseVoici l'entreprise où je fais mon stage.Here is the company where I'm doing my internship.
Language Focus

Choosing qui or que: Ask who is performing the action inside the relative clause. If the noun being described performs the action, use qui (it functions as the subject of that clause): le professeur qui enseigne le droit — the professor who teaches law. If the noun being described receives the action instead, use que (it functions as the object): le professeur que j'admire — the professor whom I admire. Note that que elides to qu' before a vowel sound: la matière qu'il préfère.

  • C'est une entreprise qui embauche beaucoup d'étudiants. It's a company that hires a lot of students. (qui = subject of "hires")
  • Voici le diplôme que je prépare. Here is the degree that I am working toward. (que = object of "prepare")
  • C'est la fac où mon frère a étudié. It's the university where my brother studied.

Practice — qui, que, or où

  1. J'ai un patron _____ est très gentil.
  2. C'est le cours _____ je préfère.
  3. Voici le bureau _____ je travaille.
  4. Elle a une amie _____ étudie le droit.
  5. C'est l'entretien _____ j'ai aujourd'hui.

Model Dialogue

Contexte : Conversation entre Camille (étudiante à Genève, Suisse) et son ancien professeur, M. Boucher, après les cours. Registre : semi-formel, vous entre étudiante et professeur.
Setting: Conversation between Camille (a student in Geneva, Switzerland) and her former teacher, Mr. Boucher, after class. Register: semi-formal, vous between student and teacher.

CAMILLE Monsieur Boucher, est-ce que je peux vous poser une question ? J'ai deux options pour l'année prochaine, et je ne sais pas quoi choisir. Mr. Boucher, may I ask you a question? I have two options for next year, and I don't know what to choose.
M. BOUCHER Bien sûr. Dites-moi. Of course. Tell me.
CAMILLE Il y a la fac, où je continue mes études de droit. Et il y a un stage dans une entreprise à Lausanne qui embauche des étudiants pour six mois. There's the university, where I'd continue my law studies. And there's an internship at a company in Lausanne that hires students for six months.
M. BOUCHER Vous connaissez cette entreprise ? Do you know this company?
CAMILLE Un peu. Je sais qu'elle est assez grande et que le salaire est meilleur que la moyenne. Mais je ne connais personne qui y travaille déjà. A little. I know it's fairly large and that the salary is better than average. But I don't know anyone who already works there.
M. BOUCHER Et le droit, c'est une matière que vous aimez ? And law, is that a subject you like?
CAMILLE Oui, c'est la matière que je préfère, en fait. Mais le stage me semble plus pratique pour trouver un travail après. Yes, it's the subject I prefer, actually. But the internship seems more practical to me for finding work afterward.
M. BOUCHER Les deux options ont leurs avantages. Si le droit vous passionne, continuer est aussi raisonnable que faire le stage. Mais si vous hésitez, je peux vous mettre en contact avec une ancienne étudiante qui connaît bien cette entreprise. Je lui écris cette semaine. Both options have their advantages. If law fascinates you, continuing is just as reasonable as doing the internship. But if you're hesitating, I can put you in touch with a former student who knows that company well. I'll write to her this week.
CAMILLE Ce serait formidable. Merci, monsieur ! Vous lui parlez souvent ? That would be great. Thank you! Do you talk to her often?
M. BOUCHER De temps en temps. Je vais lui envoyer un message ce soir et vous répondre demain. From time to time. I'll send her a message tonight and get back to you tomorrow.

Questions de compréhension — Comprehension questions:

  1. Quelles sont les deux options de Camille ? (What are Camille's two options?)
  2. Pourquoi est-ce que Camille ne connaît pas bien l'entreprise ? (Why doesn't Camille know the company well?)
  3. Quelle matière est-ce que Camille préfère ? (Which subject does Camille prefer?)
  4. Comment M. Boucher propose-t-il d'aider Camille ? (How does Mr. Boucher offer to help Camille?)
  5. Relevez deux exemples de lui ou leur dans le dialogue et expliquez à qui chacun se réfère. (Find two examples of "lui" or "leur" in the dialogue and explain who each refers to.)
Grammar Note — Si le droit vous passionne

The sentence Si le droit vous passionne (If law fascinates you) uses the verb passionner with an indirect-object-style construction similar to plaire (to please), where the thing that causes the feeling is the grammatical subject. This pattern — common with verbs of emotion and interest in French — is presented here as a recognition item; you are not expected to produce it independently yet. Notice that vous functions as the object of passionne, not the subject.

The phrase la meilleure décision que j'ai prise contains a past participle (prise) that agrees with the preceding direct object décision (feminine). Reading

Read the following three short profiles about studies and work. Each comes from a different Francophone context.

Karim (Tunis, Tunisie)

Élise (Trois-Rivières, Québec)

Je travaille comme infirmière dans un hôpital depuis quatre ans. C'est un métier que j'adore, même s'il est parfois fatigant. Mes collègues sont formidables — je les connais bien et nous nous entraidons beaucoup. Ma patronne, Madame Tremblay, est exigeante mais juste ; je lui parle souvent pour discuter de mes patients. L'entretien s'est très bien passé — l'avocate qui m'a reçu connaît bien mon professeur de droit international, donc nous avons beaucoup parlé de l'université. Reading Note — Education systems across the Francophone world

The three profiles come from Tunisia, Quebec, and Côte d'Ivoire. Élise's text mentions the cégep, a distinctive stage of the Quebec education system between secondary school and university that does not exist in France, Tunisia, or most other Francophone education systems. Degree names such as licence also vary: a French licence is roughly equivalent to a bachelor's degree, but exact structures, durations, and titles differ by country. Do not assume that education and qualification systems are identical across the Francophone world — always check the specific national or institutional context when it matters.

Writing Workshop

In this chapter you will write a short formal or semi-formal email describing a study or work situation, comparing two options, and asking for input.

  1. Study the model. Reread the dialogue between Camille and M. Boucher. Notice the formal greeting and closing conventions, the use of vous, and how Camille compares her two options clearly.
  2. Plan your message. Choose a real or imagined study or work situation involving a decision or a question. Decide: who you are writing to (a teacher, a mentor, a manager), what the two options are, and what you want from them (advice, information, a decision).
  3. Write your first draft. Begin with an appropriate formal greeting (Madame, Monsieur, or a named greeting such as Cher Monsieur Boucher,). State your situation, compare your two options using at least one comparative structure, include at least one relative clause with qui, que, or où, and use at least one indirect-object pronoun (lui or leur). Close with an appropriate formal closing.
  4. Revise. Use the editing checklist below before writing your final copy.

Liste de vérification — Editing checklist:

  • I used a formal greeting appropriate to the relationship.
  • I used vous consistently throughout the message.
  • I chose correctly between savoir and connaître wherever "to know" appears.
  • At least one indirect-object pronoun (lui or leur) is placed correctly before the conjugated verb.
  • My comparison uses plus / moins / aussi … que or meilleur/mieux correctly.
  • At least one relative clause uses qui, que, or où correctly.
  • All required accents are present on every word.
  • My message closes with an appropriate formal closing.

Speaking Practice

Pratique contrôlée — Controlled practice: Savoir ou connaître ?

With a partner, take turns asking and answering questions using savoir or connaître: Tu connais…? Tu sais…? Use at least four different prompts about people, places, facts, and skills. Confirm together which verb was correct and why.

Tâche communicative — Communicative task: Deux options

Describe two real or imagined study or work options to your partner — two classes, two jobs, or a class versus a job. Compare them using plus, moins, aussi, meilleur, or mieux. Include at least one relative clause (C'est une option qui… or C'est le travail que…). Your partner asks one follow-up question and gives their opinion about which option sounds better.

Pour les apprenants autonomes — Solo alternative

Record yourself describing your own studies or work, or an imagined situation. Describe a person you know well at school or work using connaître, describe a skill or fact using savoir, compare two things, and use at least one relative clause. Play back the recording and check: (a) Did you choose the right verb for "to know"? (b) Did your comparison use the correct word order? (c) Did qui or que match its grammatical function?

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Education and employment structures vary

Education and employment systems differ considerably across Francophone countries: degree names, the length of study programs, the structure of internships, and typical career paths are not standardized across France, Belgium, Switzerland, Quebec, and Francophone Africa. A stage (internship) may be paid or unpaid, mandatory or optional, depending on the country and the field of study. When discussing studies or work with someone from a different Francophone country, it is reasonable to ask clarifying questions about their specific system rather than assuming it matches your own.

Culture in Context — Titles and forms of address in institutional settings

In professional and academic settings across much of the Francophone world, titles such as Monsieur, Madame, Professeur, and Docteur are commonly used together with vous, even in workplaces that may feel relatively informal in other respects. The shift from vous to tu between colleagues or between a student and a teacher is negotiated socially and varies by institution, generation, and country — it is not governed by a single fixed rule. When uncertain, maintaining vous and formal titles is a safe and respectful default in a professional or academic context.

Regional Variation — Institutional vocabulary

Some education and employment terms are specific to particular countries or regions. The Quebec cégep, mentioned in this chapter's reading, has no exact equivalent in France, Belgium, Switzerland, or Francophone Africa. Degree titles such as licence, bachelor, and baccalauréat may refer to different stages of education depending on the country — in France, the baccalauréat is a secondary-school qualification, while in some other French-speaking countries and in Canada, related terms can refer to university-level study. The productive vocabulary taught in this chapter (l'université, le diplôme, le stage, l'entreprise) is understood broadly across the Francophone world, even where institutional structures differ.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Confusing savoir and connaître

Error: *Je sais mon professeur. *Je connais parler français.

Correction: Je connais mon professeur (a person → connaître) and Je sais parler français (a skill + infinitive → savoir). Ask: is this a person, place, or work (→ connaître, always followed directly by a noun) or a fact, question, or skill (→ savoir, often followed by que, a question word, or an infinitive)?

Common Mistake — Using a direct-object pronoun for an indirect object

Error: *Je le téléphone. *Je la parle.

Correction: Je lui téléphone. Je lui parle. The verbs téléphoner à and parler à take an indirect object in French because they are followed by à. The English verbs "to call" and "to talk to" suggest a direct object, but the French construction requires lui or leur, never le, la, or les. Always check the French preposition pattern, not the English translation.

Common Mistake — Mixing up meilleur and mieux

Error: *Ce travail est mieux. *Elle travaille meilleur.

Correction: Ce travail est meilleur (describes the noun travail → adjective) and Elle travaille mieux (describes the verb travaille → adverb). Ask: is a noun or a verb being compared? Noun → meilleur (agrees in gender and number). Verb → mieux (invariable).

Common Mistake — Confusing qui and que

Error: *C'est le collègue que travaille avec moi.

Correction: C'est le collègue qui travaille avec moi. Ask who performs the action of the verb in the relative clause: here, the colleague performs "works," so the colleague is the subject of the relative clause and requires qui, not que. Use que only when the noun being described receives the action instead.

Common Mistake — Omitting que before the second term of a comparison

Error: *Ce cours est plus difficile l'autre.

Correction: Ce cours est plus difficile que l'autre. The word que is required before the second term of a French comparison; it cannot be dropped as "than" sometimes is omitted informally in casual English. Every plus/moins/aussi comparison needs its que.

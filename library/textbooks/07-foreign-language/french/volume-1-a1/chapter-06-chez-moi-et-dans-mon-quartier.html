Chez moi et dans mon quartier

Home and Neighborhood · Speak and Write French: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Name the main rooms of a home and common furniture items, using the correct article and gender.
  2. State what is present or absent in a home or neighborhood using il y a and il n'y a pas de.
  3. Locate objects and places using common location prepositions: dans, sur, sous, devant, derrière, à côté de, en face de, entre.
  4. Use chez to express "at someone's place."
  5. Apply the contractions au, aux, du, des correctly with à and de.
  6. Use demonstrative determiners ce, cet, cette, ces to point out or specify a noun.
  7. Distinguish c'est from il/elle est in home and neighborhood descriptions.
  8. Ask where something is with Où est… ? and Où sont… ?
  9. Recognize the difference between h muet and h aspiré in this chapter's vocabulary.
  10. Write a short description of a real or imagined home and its surroundings.

Communication Scenario

A student named Théo has just moved into a new apartment in Strasbourg. He is video-calling his friend Yasmine, who lives in Casablanca, to show her around his new home. He walks from room to room, describing what is in each one and what is near his building in the neighborhood. Yasmine asks questions and describes her own apartment in return. This is the kind of everyday conversation you will be able to have in French by the end of this chapter.

  1. Think about the place where you currently live. How many rooms does it have? What is in each room? What is nearby — a café, a park, a supermarket? You will describe all of this in French by the end of the chapter.
  2. In English, you say "there is" and "there are" — two separate forms. In French, both are covered by a single expression. Before you start the Grammar section, guess what that expression might be. You may have already seen it in a reading text or dialogue in Chapters 1–5.

Pronunciation and Spelling

H muet and h aspiré — introduction

French words beginning with the letter h belong to one of two categories, and this distinction matters for pronunciation and spelling. The two categories are traditionally called h muet (mute h) and h aspiré (aspirated h). Despite the names, the letter h is not actually pronounced as an English [h] in either case — what differs is whether the word behaves as if it begins with a vowel or a consonant for the purposes of liaison and elision.

Sound Focus — H muet versus h aspiré

H muet words behave as if they begin with a vowel. Elision and liaison apply normally:

  • l'hôtel — not le hôtel. The article elides because hôtel has h muet.
  • l'hôpital — wait: see below. This is actually more complex.
  • l'homme (the man) — elision applies; h muet.
  • les hôtels — liaison applies: pronounced as if lé-zo-tel.

H aspiré words behave as if they begin with a consonant. Elision and liaison do NOT apply:

  • le hibou (the owl) — not l'hibou. The article stays le.
  • la honte (the shame) — not l'honte.
  • les haricots (the green beans) — pronounced without liaison: the -s of les is silent.

Important for this chapter: l'hôtel has h muet (elision applies). The word l'hôpital also has h muet — elision applies, giving l'hôpital. When in doubt about a specific word, you will be told in vocabulary lists or grammar notes. You cannot always predict the category from spelling alone — you must learn it word by word.

Elision in il y a and il n'y a pas

The expression il y a (there is / there are) contains two elided elements that beginners sometimes overlook. The first is the subject il — but here there is no elision, because il is followed by the word y, which begins with a consonant-like glide. The second is the article in negative forms: il n'y a pas de → the de elides before a vowel: il n'y a pas d'appartement. Practise these as complete rhythmic units: il-y-a is three short syllables run together in natural speech.

Enchaînement in location phrases

Enchaînement is the resyllabification of a normally pronounced word-final consonant with the following vowel-initial syllable. This is distinct from liaison, which involves a normally silent consonant. In location phrases, enchaînement happens frequently:

  • sous la table — the -s of sous is always pronounced; it links to la: sou-la-ta-ble
  • dans l'entrée — the -n of dans links to the following vowel: dan-len-trée
  • entre eux — the -re of entre links: en-treux

These are not liaison examples — the consonants are already pronounced before the vowel. Enchaînement simply shifts the syllable boundary to make speech flow more smoothly. You do not need to consciously produce it; it will develop naturally as your fluency increases. What matters now is recognizing it when you hear it, so that you do not misidentify word boundaries in connected speech.

Au and aux — pronunciation

The contractions au and aux both represent the same vowel sound, roughly like the vowel in English "oh" but without the glide at the end. The word au is singular; aux is plural. In speech, the -x of aux is normally silent before a consonant but produces a liaison [z] before a vowel: aux appartements — pronounced o-za-par-te-man. Before a consonant: aux cafés — just o-ca-fé.

Phrase grouping in descriptions

When you describe a room or a place, French rhythm organizes your words into forward-moving groups. Each group ends on a slightly lengthened final syllable. Try to say the following as a continuous phrase, not as individual words: Dans ma chambre, il y a un grand lit et une petite lampe à côté de la fenêtre. Notice how the phrase divides naturally into Dans ma chambre / il y a un grand lit / et une petite lampe / à côté de la fenêtre. Each group carries its own small rhythmic arc.

Grammar Instruction

6.1 Il y a — stating existence and presence

Purpose: state what exists or is present in a place, and state what is absent.

The expression il y a is one of the most important and frequent constructions in French. It covers both "there is" and "there are" in English — a single French expression replaces two English ones. The form does not change based on the number of the noun that follows.

  • Il y a un canapé dans le salon. There is a sofa in the living room.
  • Il y a des fenêtres dans la chambre. There are windows in the bedroom.
  • Il y a une boulangerie près d'ici. There is a bakery nearby.

To say that something is not present, use the negative: il n'y a pas de + noun without an article. The indefinite or partitive article disappears and is replaced by de (or d' before a vowel):

  • Il n'y a pas de jardin. There is no garden. / There isn't a garden.
  • Il n'y a pas de garage. There isn't a garage.
  • Il n'y a pas d'ascenseur. There is no lift / elevator. de + vowel → d'
  • Il n'y a pas de cafés dans ce quartier. There are no cafés in this neighborhood.
Language Focus

Il y a is an impersonal expression. The il here is not the pronoun "he" — it is a grammatical subject with no personal referent, similar to the "it" in "it is raining." The y is a pronoun meaning "there" (in a general sense), and a is the third-person singular of avoir. A literal reading would be "it there has." Do not try to translate word by word; instead, memorize the whole expression as a unit meaning "there is / there are." When you study y as a pronoun in Chapter 11, the connection will become clearer. For now, treat il y a as a single building block.

6.2 Location prepositions and chez

Purpose: locate objects and places in relation to other objects and places.

The location prepositions in the Vocabulary section are used directly before a noun phrase. When the noun phrase begins with le or les, you must apply the contractions studied in Section 6.3. Before la, l', and possessive determiners, the preposition stays as it is.

  • La lampe est sur la table. The lamp is on the table.
  • Le chat est sous le lit. The cat is under the bed.
  • La pharmacie est en face de la boulangerie. The pharmacy is opposite the bakery.
  • Le parc est près du supermarché. The park is near the supermarket. près de + le → près du
  • Il y a un café à côté de l'église. There is a café next to the church.

The preposition chez has no single English equivalent. It means "at the home of," "at the place of," or "in the world of" and is always followed directly by a person reference — a name, a pronoun, or a profession:

  • Je suis chez moi. I am at home. (Literally: at my place)
  • Tu es chez toi ? Are you at home?
  • On dîne chez mes parents ce soir. We are having dinner at my parents' place this evening.
  • Chez le médecin at the doctor's

Note that chez is followed by a person or entity — never directly by a place noun. Do not say *chez la boulangerie; say à la boulangerie (at/to the bakery) instead.

6.3 Contractions — au, aux, du, des

Purpose: combine the prepositions à and de with the definite articles le and les to form the required contracted forms.

In French, the prepositions à and de must contract with the definite articles le and les. These contractions are obligatory — you cannot avoid them. However, they do not occur with la or l' (the feminine singular or elided article).

Préposition
Article — Résultat
à + le → au Je vais au marché. (I'm going to the market.)
à + les → aux Je parle aux voisins. (I speak to the neighbors.)
à + la → à la Je vais à la pharmacie. (No contraction.)
à + l' → à l' Je vais à l'école. (No contraction.)
de + le → du près du café (near the café)
de + les → des à côté des toilettes (next to the toilets)
de + la → de la en face de la gare (No contraction.)
de + l' → de l' près de l'église (No contraction.)

The form des produced by de + les looks identical to the indefinite plural article des (some). Context always makes the distinction clear: des chaises (some chairs — indefinite article) versus à côté des chaises (next to the chairs — contraction of de + les).

Grammar Note — Why these contractions exist

French does not allow the sequence à le or de le — the two vowels would clash awkwardly. The contracted forms au and du are the historical result of these vowel-clash avoidance processes. The combinations with la and l' do not produce the same problem — à la and de la flow smoothly — so no contraction occurs. When a proper noun or name is used rather than a common noun with an article, no contraction happens either: le musée du Louvre uses a contraction because Louvre has the masculine article le in this fixed expression, but à Paris uses no article at all.

6.4 Demonstrative determiners — ce, cet, cette, ces

Purpose: use a demonstrative determiner to point out, specify, or refer back to a particular noun.

Demonstrative determiners replace or supplement the article when you want to say "this," "that," "these," or "those." In French, the same forms are used for "this" and "that" (and for "these" and "those") — context or a gesture makes the meaning clear. There is no separate word for "that" versus "this" in standard modern usage at A1–A2 level.

  • Cet immeuble a dix étages. This building has ten floors. (m. sg., vowel)
  • Cette rue est longue. This street is long. (f. sg.)

Grammar Note — Cet before h muet

The form cet (not ce) is used before any masculine singular noun beginning with a vowel sound or h muet. This parallels the possessive rule you learned in Chapter 4: just as you say mon amie and mon oncle (not ma amie), you say cet homme and cet hôtel (not ce homme). The reasoning is the same — a pronunciation rule prevents two vowel sounds from meeting at a word boundary. The word homme and hôtel have h muet, so they behave as vowel-initial. But hibou (owl) has h aspiré, so the masculine form stays ce hibou.

Model Dialogue

Contexte : Appel vidéo entre Théo (Strasbourg, France) et Yasmine (Casablanca, Maroc). Théo vient d'emménager dans un nouvel appartement. Registre : conversation informelle entre amis, tu.
Setting: Video call between Théo (Strasbourg, France) and Yasmine (Casablanca, Morocco). Théo has just moved into a new apartment. Register: informal conversation between friends, tu.

YASMINE Alors, c'est comment, le nouvel appartement ? So, what's the new apartment like?
THÉO Il est super ! Il y a trois pièces : un salon, une chambre et une cuisine. Il y a aussi une petite salle de bains. It's great! There are three rooms: a living room, a bedroom and a kitchen. There's also a small bathroom.
YASMINE Et il y a un balcon ? And is there a balcony?
THÉO Oui ! Il y a un balcon dans la chambre. Regarde — c'est là. Et il y a une grande fenêtre dans le salon. Yes! There's a balcony in the bedroom. Look — it's there. And there's a large window in the living room.
YASMINE C'est beau ! Et les meubles ? Ce sont tes meubles ou les meubles du propriétaire ? It's beautiful! And the furniture? Is it your furniture or the landlord's furniture?
THÉO C'est un appartement meublé. Dans le salon, il y a un canapé gris devant la télévision et une petite table entre le canapé et la fenêtre. Dans la chambre, il y a un grand lit et une armoire à côté de la porte. It's a furnished apartment. In the living room, there's a grey sofa in front of the television and a small table between the sofa and the window. In the bedroom, there's a large bed and a wardrobe next to the door.
YASMINE Et le quartier, c'est bien ? And the neighborhood — is it nice?
THÉO Oui, c'est très bien ! Il y a une boulangerie en face de mon immeuble et un parc à côté. Près du parc, il y a aussi un grand supermarché. Et il n'y a pas de voitures dans cette rue — c'est calme ! Yes, it's very nice! There's a bakery opposite my building and a park next to it. Near the park, there's also a large supermarket. And there are no cars on this street — it's quiet!
YASMINE Super ! Et tu es loin du centre-ville ? Great! And are you far from the city center?
THÉO Non, pas du tout. Il y a une station de tram juste devant l'immeuble. Je suis à dix minutes du centre. Chez moi, c'est parfait ! No, not at all. There's a tram stop right in front of the building. I'm ten minutes from the center. My place is perfect!

Regional Variation — Housing vocabulary across the Francophone world

The word appartement is widely understood across the Francophone world for a dwelling within a multi-unit building. In Quebec, the informal word un appart is common in speech, and the term un logement is used frequently in official contexts. The plex (duplex, triplex, quadruplex) is a distinctive feature of Montreal's architectural landscape, where two-to-four-unit buildings with separate street-level entrances are very common. In West Africa, many middle-class and upper-middle-class families live in standalone villas or large family compounds (des concessions) rather than apartments. In Switzerland, building vocabulary largely follows French European usage, though Swiss French sometimes uses slightly different terms for features such as the ground floor (rez-de-chaussée is universal, but informal Swiss usage may vary). None of these regional differences is wrong — they reflect the diversity of built environments and social practices across the Francophone world.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Housing and home in the Francophone world

What counts as a typical home varies enormously across the Francophone world. In France, especially in cities, most people live in apartments (des appartements), with a significant proportion in rental accommodation. Large family houses are more common outside city centers and in smaller towns and villages. In Francophone West Africa, a large family compound (une concession) where several generations live together in interconnected buildings around a shared courtyard is a common and well-established form of housing. In Senegal and Mali, for example, the compound may house parents, married children, and grandparents in separate rooms or buildings that share a common outdoor space. In Quebec, the rowhouse or plex has been a dominant urban housing form since the nineteenth century — the stacked duplex or triplex with external staircases is immediately recognizable in cities like Montréal. Housing reflects both economics and culture, and describing where you live often opens a rich conversation about family, community, and daily life.

Culture in Context — Floors and addresses

When you move between Francophone countries, floor numbering can be a source of confusion. In France, Belgium, and Switzerland, the ground floor is the rez-de-chaussée — the floor at street level. The floor immediately above it is the premier étage (first floor). This means that a French deuxième étage corresponds to the third floor in North American numbering. In Quebec, while the term rez-de-chaussée is understood, everyday speech often uses le premier for the ground floor, aligning more closely with North American usage. When someone tells you their apartment number, it is always helpful to clarify which floor numbering system they are using. Addresses in France are formatted with the street number before the street name: 12, rue de la Paix — the comma between the number and the street is standard French typographic practice.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using c'est or il est instead of il y a for existence

Error: *Dans le salon, c'est un canapé. / *Dans le salon, il est un canapé.

Correction: Dans le salon, il y a un canapé. To say that something exists or is present in a place, French uses il y a. The structures c'est and il/elle est are used for identification or description, not for stating that something is located somewhere. Ask yourself: "Am I saying what is there?" → Use il y a. "Am I saying what something is?" → Use c'est or il/elle est.

Common Mistake — Forgetting contractions with à and de

Error: *Je vais à le supermarché. *Il y a un café près de le parc.

Correction: Je vais au supermarché. Il y a un café près du parc. The sequences à le and de le do not exist in French — they must always become au and du. This is not optional. Every time you write or say à or de before the article le, stop and apply the contraction. Similarly, à les → aux and de les → des.

Common Mistake — Using il n'y a pas un / une instead of il n'y a pas de

Error: *Il n'y a pas un jardin. *Il n'y a pas une pharmacie.

Correction: Il n'y a pas de jardin. Il n'y a pas de pharmacie. In French, the indefinite article (un, une, des) changes to de / d' after a negation. This is a general rule that also applies to avoir: J'ai un frère → Je n'ai pas de frère. You saw this rule introduced in Chapter 4. It applies equally in the il n'y a pas structure. Remember: after negation, un / une / des → de / d'.

Common Mistake — Using ce instead of cet before a vowel or h muet

Error: *Ce appartement est grand. *Ce hôtel est cher.

Correction: Cet appartement est grand. Cet hôtel est cher. The form cet is required before any masculine singular noun beginning with a vowel sound or h muet. Check two things: (1) Is the noun masculine singular? (2) Does it begin with a vowel or h muet? If both answers are yes, use cet.

Common Mistake — Using chez before a place noun

Error: *Je vais chez la boulangerie. *Il y a un café chez le parc.

Correction: Je vais à la boulangerie. Il y a un café près du parc. The preposition chez is followed by a person reference — a name (chez Marie), a stressed pronoun (chez moi, chez toi), or a professional role (chez le médecin). It is never followed directly by a place noun such as boulangerie or parc. To say where you are going or where something is, use à la, au, dans, près de, or another appropriate preposition.

Looking Ahead

Ce que vous savez maintenant — What you can do now

You can now describe a home and its neighborhood in French — naming rooms and furniture with their correct genders, stating what is present or absent with il y a and il n'y a pas de, locating things using a range of prepositions, applying the required contractions au / aux / du / des, and pointing to specific items with demonstrative determiners. Together with what you learned in Chapters 1–5, you can now introduce yourself, describe people and family, say where you are from, discuss what is around you, and tell someone about the place where you live.

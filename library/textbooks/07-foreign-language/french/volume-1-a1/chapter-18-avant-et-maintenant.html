Avant et maintenant

Then and Now · Speak and Write French: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Form the imperfect (l'imparfait) for regular and high-frequency irregular verbs, including être.
  2. Use the imperfect to describe past circumstances, ongoing situations, and background information.
  3. Use the imperfect to describe repeated or habitual past activities.
  4. Use the imperfect to express age, time, weather, and states in the past.
  5. Distinguish the imperfect from the compound past through discourse and perspective, not a simple "completed versus incomplete" rule.
  6. Compare an earlier period of life with the present using avant and maintenant.
  7. Use depuis, pendant, and past-time il y a to talk about duration and time elapsed.
  8. Combine background description with at least one completed event in a connected account.
  9. Apply complete written negation, including review of ne … jamais and ne … plus, in past-time contexts.
  10. Understand and produce a short personal memory or then-and-now description.

Communication Scenario

At a family dinner in Lyon, Camille's grandmother, Mémé Joséphine, tells her about what life was like when she was young — where she lived, what her family did, what school was like. Camille listens and then compares her grandmother's childhood with her own. Their conversation moves naturally between description of an ongoing past situation ("we lived in a small apartment," "I used to walk to school every day") and specific completed events ("the year I turned ten, we moved to a bigger house"). This chapter gives you the structure French uses for that first kind of past-time language: setting a scene, describing a habit, or stating how things used to be.

  1. Think about something that was different in your life five or ten years ago — where you lived, what you did every day, how old you were. In Chapter 16, you learned to say what you did on a specific occasion using the compound past. Notice that a sentence like "I was ten years old" or "we used to live in a small town" does not describe a single completed action — it describes an ongoing state or a repeated pattern. This chapter introduces a different past-tense structure for exactly that purpose.
  2. In English, both kinds of past meaning often use the same verb form ("I lived," "I was," "we walked every day"). French uses two different past tenses depending on the kind of meaning intended. You already control one of them. This chapter introduces the second.

Pronunciation and Spelling

Imperfect endings

The imperfect endings are pronounced with a high degree of consistency across persons, which makes them easier to produce than they may first appear in writing. The singular endings -ais, -ait, and the third-person-plural ending -aient are all pronounced identically: [ɛ]. Only the nous and vous forms sound different, because they retain the audible stem-internal pattern from the present tense (nous parl-i-ons, vous parl-i-ez), with a clear [j] glide before the ending.

Sound Focus — Contrast between imperfect and compound-past rhythm

The compound past you learned in Chapters 16–17 is a two-part rhythmic unit: auxiliary plus participle, with prominence on the final syllable of the participle (j'ai parl-É). The imperfect is a single-word verb form, with prominence falling naturally on its own final syllable: je parl-AIS. Practice saying pairs aloud and listening for the difference in length and internal rhythm: j'ai parlé (two parts, longer) versus je parlais (one part, shorter).

Because so many imperfect endings share the same [ɛ] sound, listening carefully for the subject pronoun is essential to understanding who is being talked about — the verb ending alone often cannot distinguish je, tu, il, elle, or on.

Phrase grouping in description

Descriptive imperfect sentences often link smoothly into a flowing rhythmic phrase, especially when a time expression opens the sentence: Quand j'étais petite, j'habitais à la campagne. Practice grouping the time clause and the main clause together with a brief pause between them, rather than pausing after every word.

Reduced spoken ne in informal narration

You first encountered receptive omission of ne in ordinary conversation in Chapter 7. In informal spoken narratives about the past, this omission is common and natural: Avant, on (ne) sortait pas beaucoup may be heard in casual speech without the ne. This remains a receptive pattern for recognition at this stage. Standard learner writing in this chapter should retain complete negation with both ne and pas, exactly as in every previous chapter.

Optional liaison for recognition

You may hear optional liaison in careful or formal spoken narration, such as between nous étions pronounced with a linked [z]: nous é-tions versus the more formal nous-z-étions-style linking in some careful speech. This remains a recognition-only feature at this stage; do not feel obligated to produce optional liaison yourself.

Grammar Instruction

18.1 Forming the imperfect

Purpose: build the imperfect tense for any verb, describing ongoing or habitual past situations.

The imperfect is formed from the nous form of the present tense. Remove the -ons ending to find the imperfect stem, then add the imperfect endings. This pattern works for virtually every French verb except être.

Language Building Block

nous parlons → je parl-ais, tu parl-ais, il parl-ait, nous parl-ions, vous parl-iez, ils parl-aient

PronomImparfaitSpoken note
j'habitais[ɛ] ending, identical sound to tu, il/elle/on forms

Verbs whose stem ends in -g or -c keep the spelling adjustment you already know from the present tense in the nous form: nous mangions (no extra e needed, since i already softens the g), but je mangeais, tu mangeais, il mangeait, ils mangeaient keep the e after g to preserve the soft [ʒ] sound. Similarly, commencer gives je commençais with a cedilla before a.

18.2 The irregular imperfect of être

Purpose: use the one verb whose imperfect stem does not come from the regular pattern.

Être is the single high-frequency irregular verb in this chapter: its imperfect stem is ét-, not derived from a nous form ending in -ons. The endings themselves are completely regular.

Because être is used constantly to describe states, ages, and circumstances, its imperfect is among the most frequent forms you will use in this chapter: j'étais jeune, c'était difficile, il était tard.

18.3 Uses of the imperfect

Purpose: recognize the range of meanings the imperfect expresses, beyond a single English translation.

The imperfect describes an ongoing or background situation in the past, rather than a single completed action. Its main uses in this chapter are:

UseExempleEnglish
DescriptionLa maison était petite.The house was small.
Background / ongoing situationIl pleuvait quand je suis sorti.It was raining when I went out.
Repeated or habitual actionNous allions à la plage chaque été.We used to go to the beach every summer.
Age in the pastJ'avais dix ans.I was ten years old.
Time in the pastIl était huit heures.It was eight o'clock.
Weather in the pastIl faisait froid.It was cold.
  • Quand j'étais petite, je vivais dans un petit village à la campagne. When I was little, I lived in a small village in the countryside.
  • Tous les samedis, mes grands-parents nous rendaient visite. Every Saturday, my grandparents would visit us.
  • Mon père travaillait dans une usine. My father worked in a factory.
Common Mistake — Translating "used to" or "would" mechanically

English speakers sometimes look for "used to" or "would" in a sentence before deciding to use the imperfect. French does not require these English markers to use the imperfect: Nous allions à la plage chaque été is correctly expressed with a simple-looking English equivalent ("We went to the beach every summer") just as easily as with "used to go." Decide based on whether the action was repeated or ongoing in French, not on which English words happen to appear.

18.4 Compound past versus imperfect

Purpose: choose the correct past tense based on the role a piece of information plays in an account, not on a simplistic rule.

Do not reduce this choice to "completed versus incomplete action." Instead, think about what role each piece of information plays in the story you are telling. The compound past (Chapters 16–17) moves the story forward — it reports specific events, one after another. The imperfect sets the scene — it describes the situation, circumstances, or background against which those events occurred.

Language Focus

A useful way to think about this contrast: the imperfect paints the backdrop, and the compound past tells you what happened on that backdrop. In Il faisait beau, alors nous sommes allés à la plage (It was nice out, so we went to the beach), the weather is the ongoing backdrop (imperfect), and going to the beach is the specific event that happened against that backdrop (compound past). The same event can sometimes be described with either tense depending on what the speaker wants to emphasize — but in this chapter, focus on the common, clear-cut pattern: background and habit take the imperfect; specific, sequenced events take the compound past.

18.5 Depuis, pendant, and il y a

Purpose: express duration and elapsed time accurately, a common point of confusion for English speakers.

English "for" and "ago" map onto three distinct French expressions depending on the kind of duration described.

ExpressionTense used with itMeaningExemple
depuispresent tensea situation that began in the past and continues nowJ'habite ici depuis cinq ans. (I have lived here for five years — and still do.)
pendantany tense, often compound pasta bounded duration, finished or notJ'ai habité à Lyon pendant deux ans. (I lived in Lyon for two years — a closed period.)
il y aa past tense, placed after the time expressionhow long ago something happenedJ'ai déménagé il y a cinq ans. (I moved five years ago.)
Common Mistake — Confusing depuis with the compound past

Error: *J'ai habité ici depuis cinq ans (when the person still lives there now).

Correction: J'habite ici depuis cinq ans. When a situation that began in the past is still true now, French uses the present tense with depuis — not the compound past, as English "have lived" might suggest. Reserve the compound past with depuis only for situations where you are emphasizing that the state has now ended; this distinction goes beyond this chapter's scope, so in this chapter, pair depuis with the present tense.

Note that il y a here is the same expression you learned in Chapter 6 for existence (il y a un café près d'ici), but used with a time expression and placed differently in the sentence: the time expression comes immediately after il y a, and the whole phrase usually attaches to the end of a past-tense sentence.

18.6 Review: ne … jamais and ne … plus in past contexts

Purpose: apply negation you already control (Chapter 13) to descriptions of the past.

You learned ne … jamais (never) and ne … plus (no longer, not anymore) in Chapter 13. Both work in the imperfect exactly as they do in the present, surrounding the conjugated verb:

  • Avant, je ne mangeais jamais de légumes. Before, I never used to eat vegetables.
  • Nous n'habitions plus à la campagne après 2015. We no longer lived in the countryside after 2015.

Standard learner writing in this chapter should retain complete negation with ne in every case, as throughout the course.

Model Dialogue

Contexte : Un dimanche soir, lors d'un dîner de famille à Lyon. Camille parle avec sa grand-mère, Mémé Joséphine, de son enfance. Registre : conversation familiale informelle, tu entre Camille et sa grand-mère.
Setting: A Sunday evening family dinner in Lyon. Camille talks with her grandmother, Mémé Joséphine, about her childhood. Register: informal family conversation, tu between Camille and her grandmother.

CAMILLE Mémé, c'était comment, ta vie quand tu étais petite ? Mémé, what was your life like when you were little?
JOSÉPHINE Ah, c'était très différent ! Nous habitions dans un petit village, à la campagne. Il n'y avait pas beaucoup de voitures à l'époque. Ah, it was very different! We lived in a small village, in the countryside. There weren't many cars at that time.
CAMILLE Et l'école, c'était comment ? And school, what was that like?
JOSÉPHINE J'allais à l'école tous les jours à pied, même quand il faisait froid. On n'avait pas d'ordinateurs, bien sûr — on écrivait tout à la main. I walked to school every day, even when it was cold. We didn't have computers, of course — we wrote everything by hand.
CAMILLE Tu habites à Lyon depuis combien de temps maintenant ? How long have you lived in Lyon now?
JOSÉPHINE J'habite ici depuis presque cinquante ans ! Je suis arrivée à Lyon il y a quarante-huit ans, juste après mon mariage avec ton grand-père. I've lived here for almost fifty years! I arrived in Lyon forty-eight years ago, just after my marriage to your grandfather.
CAMILLE Qu'est-ce qui a vraiment changé, à ton avis ? What's really changed, in your opinion?
JOSÉPHINE Beaucoup de choses ! Avant, les familles passaient plus de temps ensemble le soir. Maintenant, tout le monde regarde son téléphone. Mais bon, il y a aussi de bons changements — les femmes de ma génération ne pouvaient pas toujours faire les études qu'elles voulaient. A lot of things! Before, families used to spend more time together in the evening. Now, everyone looks at their phone. But there are good changes too — women of my generation couldn't always pursue the studies they wanted.
CAMILLE C'est vrai. Merci, Mémé, j'adore t'écouter raconter tout ça. That's true. Thanks, Mémé, I love listening to you tell all this.

Questions de compréhension — Comprehension questions:

  1. Où Joséphine habitait-elle quand elle était petite ? (Where did Joséphine live when she was little?)
  2. Comment allait-elle à l'école ? (How did she go to school?)
  3. Depuis combien de temps Joséphine habite-t-elle à Lyon ? (How long has Joséphine lived in Lyon?)
  4. Quand est-elle arrivée à Lyon ? (When did she arrive in Lyon?)
  5. Selon Joséphine, quel changement est positif ? (According to Joséphine, what change is positive?)
  6. Relevez trois verbes à l'imparfait dans le dialogue. (Find three imperfect-tense verbs in the dialogue.)
Grammar Note — Mixing imperfect and compound past in the dialogue

Notice that Joséphine uses the imperfect throughout for ongoing background description (nous habitions, j'allais, on écrivait, les familles passaient) but switches to the compound past for the one specific, dateable event she names: je suis arrivée à Lyon il y a quarante-huit ans. This is the pattern you practiced in Grammar section 18.4 — background in the imperfect, specific events in the compound past.

Reading Note — Personal memory across the Francophone world

These three reflections come from Morocco, Quebec, and Côte d'Ivoire and describe very different childhoods and life changes — an urban neighborhood, a lakeside chalet, a demanding career. Writing Workshop

In this chapter you will write a connected paragraph that establishes past circumstances using the imperfect and includes at least one completed event using the compound past.

  1. Study the models. Reread one of the three then-and-now reflections above. Identify: (a) at least three imperfect verbs and what each describes (description, habit, age, weather, or background), (b) at least one compound-past verb marking a specific event, and (c) any use of depuis, pendant, or il y a.
  2. Plan your contrast. Choose a period of your life (childhood, a few years ago, before a specific change) to compare with now. List three to five things that were generally true or habitual during that period (imperfect), and at least one specific event that marked a change (compound past).
  3. Write your first draft. Begin with a time-framing sentence (Quand j'avais [âge] ans… / Avant…). Describe the earlier situation using the imperfect, include at least one completed event in the compound past, and close by describing the present using maintenant. Include at least one use of depuis, pendant, or il y a.
  4. Revise. Use the editing checklist below before writing your final copy.

Liste de vérification — Editing checklist:

  • Every imperfect verb is correctly formed from the present-tense nous stem (or from ét- for être).
  • Imperfect verbs describe background, habit, age, time, or weather — not a single completed event.
  • At least one compound-past verb correctly marks a specific, dateable event.
  • At least one use of depuis, pendant, or il y a is correctly matched to its required tense.
  • Negative sentences place ne … pas (or ne … jamais / ne … plus) correctly around the conjugated verb.
  • The paragraph contrasts avant and maintenant in a connected, logical way.
  • All required accents are present, including on imperfect endings such as -ais, -ait, -ions, -iez.
  • The paragraph reads as a coherent comparison, not a disconnected list.

Speaking Practice

With a partner, interview each other about how something has changed — a city, a hobby, a daily routine, a friendship. Use at least one of depuis, pendant, or il y a.

Pour les apprenants autonomes — Solo alternative

Record yourself describing your life five years ago compared to now, using the imperfect for background and habits and the compound past for at least one specific event. Aim for at least six sentences. Play back the recording and check: (a) Did every imperfect verb describe a habit, state, or background rather than a single event? (b) Did you correctly use depuis, pendant, or il y a? (c) Did your account move logically from avant to maintenant?

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Personal memory is individual, not proof of a whole culture

When someone describes how their childhood or community has changed, they are sharing one person's experience shaped by their family, region, generation, and individual circumstances. Avoid treating any single account — including the readings in this chapter — as evidence for how an entire country or generation "used to be." Asking a follow-up question, such as Et toi, comment c'était ?, invites the other person to add detail rather than generalize.

Culture in Context — Comparing generations without simplistic decline narratives

Conversations comparing "before" and "now" can easily slide into a story where everything was simply better or simply worse in the past. Notice that Mémé Joséphine in the dialogue describes both losses (less family time together) and gains (more educational opportunity for women) when comparing generations. A balanced then-and-now account, in French as in English, usually recognizes that change brings both advantages and disadvantages rather than a single direction of improvement or decline.

Regional Variation — Mémé, Pépé, and other family terms

The dialogue uses Mémé, a common informal term for "grandmother" used widely in France, alongside the standard grand-mère. Other informal regional and family-specific terms exist across the Francophone world, including Bonne-maman, Mamie, and various terms used in Quebec, Belgium, and other communities. This course uses grand-mère and grand-père as the default productive forms, with informal family terms like Mémé and Pépé noted here for recognition, since exact usage varies significantly by family and region.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using the compound past for background description

Error: *Quand j'ai eu dix ans, j'ai habité à la campagne.

Correction: Quand j'avais dix ans, j'habitais à la campagne. Age and an ongoing living situation are both background description, not specific completed events — both verbs belong in the imperfect here.

Common Mistake — Using the imperfect for a single, sequenced event

Error: *Un jour, ma famille déménageait à Lyon.

Correction: Un jour, ma famille a déménagé à Lyon. The phrase un jour (one day) signals a specific, dateable event that moves the story forward — this calls for the compound past, not the imperfect.

Common Mistake — Building the imperfect stem from the infinitive instead of the nous form

Error: *je faisis, *je prendais

Correction: je faisais (from nous faisons), je prenais (from nous prenons). Always build the imperfect stem from the present-tense nous form, not from the infinitive. This is especially important for irregular verbs, where the nous-form stem can look quite different from the infinitive.

Common Mistake — Using the compound past with depuis for an ongoing situation

Error: *J'ai habité à Lyon depuis cinq ans (still living there now).

Correction: J'habite à Lyon depuis cinq ans. When the situation described by depuis is still true at the moment of speaking, French requires the present tense, unlike English "I have lived."

Common Mistake — Misspelling the nous and vous imperfect endings

Error: *nous parlaient, *vous parlais

Correction: nous parlions, vous parliez. Although most imperfect endings sound alike, the written forms are distinct and must match the subject pronoun exactly. Do not let the shared [ɛ] sound of the singular and third-person-plural forms cause you to confuse the written nous and vous endings, which sound different and are spelled differently.

Listening

Background/habit (imperfect): la famille habitait à Bruxelles · elle prenait le tram tous les matins · son père travaillait dans une banque.
Specific event (compound past): le tram est tombé en panne · elle est arrivée en retard · le professeur a compris la situation.

Sentence with depuis: Depuis ce jour-là, je pars toujours plus tôt le matin. The present tense (je pars) follows depuis because the habit continues into the present.

Chapter Review — Partie A

  1. (a) l'enfance (b) un souvenir (c) grandir (d) de nos jours (e) chaque été
  2. Depuis is used with the present tense for a situation that began in the past and continues now (j'habite ici depuis cinq ans). Pendant describes a bounded duration, finished or not, with any tense (j'ai habité à Lyon pendant deux ans). Il y a states how long ago something happened, with a past tense (j'ai déménagé il y a cinq ans).
  3. Exemples acceptables : avant / maintenant, quand j'étais petit(e), autrefois / de nos jours. Any two are correct.

Chapter Review — Partie B

  1. j'avais / tu avais / il avait, elle avait, on avait / nous avions / vous aviez / ils avaient, elles avaient
  2. j'étais / tu étais / il était, elle était, on était / nous étions / vous étiez / ils étaient, elles étaient
  3. (a) j'étais / je jouais (b) a commencé (c) avaient
  4. (a) depuis deux ans (b) pendant trois semaines (c) il y a un an
  5. Réponses libres. The explanation should state that the compound past reports specific, sequenced events that move a story forward, while the imperfect describes the ongoing background, circumstances, or habits against which those events occur.

Looking Ahead

Ce que vous savez maintenant — What you can do now

You can now describe past circumstances, habits, and background situations using the imperfect, and you can combine this with the compound past to tell a connected story that distinguishes scene-setting from specific events. You can also express duration accurately using depuis, pendant, and il y a. Together with everything you have learned about daily life, community interaction, and completed past events, you can now move fluidly between present, near-future, and two distinct kinds of past-time language in French — a major step toward connected, natural narration.

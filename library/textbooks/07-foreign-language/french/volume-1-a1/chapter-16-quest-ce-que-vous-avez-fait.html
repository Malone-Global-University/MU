Qu'est-ce que vous avez fait ?

Completed Past Events · Speak and Write French: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Form the compound past (le passé composé) with avoir as the auxiliary verb.
  2. Conjugate avoir correctly in every person and combine it with a past participle.
  3. Form regular past participles for -er, -ir, and -re verbs.
  4. Recognize and use a set of high-frequency irregular past participles.
  5. Place ne … pas correctly around the auxiliary in the compound past.
  6. Ask questions about completed events using intonation, est-ce que, and time expressions.
  7. Use common past-time markers to situate an event in time.
  8. Sequence a short series of completed events using chronological connectors.
  9. Say that something did not happen, using complete written negation.
  10. Recount and write about a recent day or weekend using the compound past with avoir.

Communication Scenario

On Monday morning, Camille arrives at her French class in Lyon and her classmate Idris asks her about her weekend. She tells him what she did — visited her grandparents, watched a film, finished a work project — and asks him the same question. Their exchange moves naturally between simple statements and follow-up questions, all describing things that already happened. This chapter gives you the tools for exactly this kind of conversation: telling someone, in connected sentences, what you did recently.

  1. Think of three things you did yesterday or this past weekend. In Chapters 1–15, you learned to describe what you do regularly and what you are doing right now. Notice that none of those structures can describe a completed, finished action. This chapter introduces the structure French uses for exactly that purpose.
  2. In English, the simple past usually changes the verb itself: "I watch" becomes "I watched." French instead builds a two-part structure using a helping verb plus a special form of the main verb. You already know one half of this structure — can you guess which verb from Chapter 4 might serve as the "helper"?

Pronunciation and Spelling

Auxiliary and participle as one rhythmic group

The auxiliary verb and the past participle are pronounced together as a single rhythmic unit, with prominence falling on the final syllable of the participle, not on the auxiliary. Say j'ai re-gar-DÉ as one connected phrase, not "J'AI regardé" with stress on the auxiliary. This matches the general French pattern of phrase-final prominence that you have practiced since earlier chapters.

Sound Focus — Spoken neutrality of many participle endings

Many regular past participles sound identical or nearly identical to related forms despite different spellings. The -er participle ending -é is pronounced [e], the same vowel sound as the infinitive ending -er and the form j'ai. This means that, in connected speech, j'ai parlé contains two instances of a very similar vowel sound in sequence — listen for the rhythm and small vowel-quality differences rather than relying on spelling alone.

The -ir participle ending -i and the -re participle ending -u are clearly distinct from each other and from -é: compare fini [fini], répondu [ʁepɔ̃dy], and parlé [paʁle]. Train your ear to recognize the participle type by its final vowel sound, since the written infinitive ending is not pronounced in the participle.

Liaison after plural subject pronouns

Liaison occurs between the plural subject pronouns and their forms of avoir in the compound past, exactly as you learned in Chapter 4 for the present tense:

  • nous avons fini pronounced nou-za-von-fi-ni: liaison between nous and avons
  • vous avez répondu pronounced vou-za-vez-ré-pon-du: liaison between vous and avez
  • ils ont vu pronounced il-zon-vu: liaison between ils and ont

There is no liaison between the auxiliary and the past participle itself in ordinary speech (a fait is pronounced as two distinct syllables, not linked), since the participle does not begin with a vowel sound in most of the verbs in this chapter. Liaison applies at the subject-pronoun-to-auxiliary boundary, not at the auxiliary-to-participle boundary.

Past-question intonation

Yes/no questions in the compound past most often use rising intonation on a statement-order sentence, exactly as in the present tense: Tu as fini ? rises in pitch on the final syllable. With est-ce que, the rising intonation occurs at the very end of the question, after the participle: Est-ce que tu as fini ? Practice keeping the auxiliary and participle grouped together rhythmically even when a question word or est-ce que precedes them.

Contrast between present and compound-past forms

English speakers sometimes default to present-tense rhythm even when using compound-past forms. Compare and practice saying these pairs aloud, noticing that the compound-past version is longer and has its own internal rhythm:

  • je travaille present: one rhythmic unit
  • j'ai travaillé compound past: auxiliary + participle as one longer unit, prominence on the final syllable

Grammar Instruction

16.1 The compound past (le passé composé) with avoir — form

Purpose: build a two-part verb structure to describe a single completed action in the past.

French does not change the main verb's ending the way English does to form a simple past ("watch" → "watched"). Instead, French most often builds the past with two parts: a conjugated form of an auxiliary verb (a "helping verb") followed by the past participle of the main verb. For most verbs — including all the verbs in this chapter — the auxiliary is a conjugated present-tense form of avoir, the same verb you learned fully in Chapter 4.

Language Building Block
Subject + [conjugated avoir] + [past participle]
Auxiliary carries the subject agreement · Participle carries the meaning of the main verb
J'ai regardé un film. Nous avons fini le projet.
I watched a film. We finished the project.

tutu asregardétu as regardé

The past participle itself does not change form to agree with the subject when the auxiliary is avoir in these basic sentences. Elle a regardé and ils ont regardé use the identical participle regardé. You will study a specific exception to this — agreement with a preceding direct object — in a later course, beyond this chapter's scope.

16.2 Forming regular past participles

Purpose: build the past participle of any regular verb from its infinitive.

Regular past participles follow a predictable pattern based on the infinitive ending:

Infinitif se termine parParticipe passé se termine parExemple
-er-éparler → parlé
-ir-ifinir → fini
-re-urépondre → répondu

To form a regular participle: remove the infinitive ending (-er, -ir, or -re) and add the corresponding participle ending. This pattern applies to every regular verb you have learned since Chapter 3, including parler, habiter, étudier, écouter, travailler, regarder, visiter, téléphoner, choisir, réussir, attendre, and perdre.

16.3 High-frequency irregular past participles

Purpose: recognize and use the most common irregular participles needed for everyday narration.

A number of very common verbs have past participles that do not follow the regular patterns above and must be memorized individually. The Core Vocabulary section above presents the full approved list for this chapter: fait, eu, été, vu, pris, bu, lu, écrit, dit, pu, voulu, and dû.

Grammar Note — être used as a main verb versus as an auxiliary

In this chapter, être appears in the irregular-participle list as a main verb taking avoir as its auxiliary: j'ai été malade (I was sick / I have been sick). This is different from using être as the auxiliary for a different group of verbs, which is the topic of Chapter 17. Do not confuse the two: in this chapter, every verb — including être itself when it is the main verb — takes avoir as its helper.

16.4 Negation in the compound past

Purpose: say that something did not happen, placing negation correctly around the auxiliary.

In the compound past, ne … pas surrounds the conjugated auxiliary avoir, not the past participle. The participle stays outside the negation, at the end of the verb group.

Language Building Block
Subject + ne + [avoir] + pas + [past participle]
Negation wraps the auxiliary · Participle remains unchanged, placed after pas
Je n'ai pas fini le projet. Nous n'avons pas regardé le film.
I did not finish the project. We did not watch the film.
  • Je n'ai pas travaillé hier. I did not work yesterday.
  • Elle n'a pas répondu au message. She did not answer the message.
  • Ils n'ont pas encore fini. They have not finished yet.

Standard learner writing should retain complete negation with both ne and pas, as in all written work throughout this course. Note that ne elides to n' before the vowel sound of ai, as, a, avons, avez, and ont, exactly as you practiced with present-tense negation in Chapter 7.

16.5 Questions in the compound past

Purpose: ask about completed events using the question strategies you already know.

All three question strategies you have practiced since Chapter 3 work with the compound past. The auxiliary and participle stay together as a unit; only the surrounding question structure changes.

16.6 Past-time markers and basic sequencing

Purpose: situate completed actions in time and connect a short series of events in order.

Past-time expressions usually appear at the beginning or end of a sentence, set off naturally by the sentence rhythm: Hier, j'ai travaillé. or J'ai travaillé hier. Both orders are natural; placing the time expression first is common when starting a new topic or a new part of a narrative.

Language Focus

To connect a short series of completed events into a coherent account, use simple chronological connectors: d'abord (first), ensuite or puis (then, next), après (afterward), and enfin (finally). Each connector typically begins its own clause: D'abord, j'ai pris un café. Ensuite, j'ai travaillé. Enfin, j'ai regardé un film. This sequencing pattern, combined with the compound past, is the basic structure for recounting "what I did" — the central communicative goal of this chapter.

Practice — sequencing events

Combine the following actions into a short connected account using d'abord, ensuite, and enfin. Use the compound past with je.

  1. travailler le matin / déjeuner avec un ami / finir un livre le soir
Common Mistake — Productive use of object-pronoun placement before the compound verb

You will sometimes see or hear a direct-object pronoun placed before the auxiliary in the compound past, such as Je l'ai vu (I saw it/him). This is correct French, and it follows the same single-pronoun placement rule you learned in Chapter 9. However, this chapter does not require you to produce this structure on your own, because it can trigger a participle-agreement rule (vu → vue with a feminine preceding object) that is beyond this chapter's scope. For now, recognize the pattern receptively if you encounter it, but build your own sentences using full noun objects, as modeled throughout this chapter.

Model Dialogue

Contexte : Lundi matin, avant le cours de français, à Lyon. Camille et Idris parlent de leur week-end. Registre : conversation informelle, tu entre camarades de classe.
Setting: Monday morning, before French class, in Lyon. Camille and Idris talk about their weekend. Register: informal conversation, tu between classmates.

IDRIS Salut, Camille ! Alors, qu'est-ce que tu as fait ce week-end ? Hi, Camille! So, what did you do this weekend?
CAMILLE Oh, j'ai eu un week-end très occupé ! D'abord, samedi matin, j'ai visité mes grands-parents à Villeurbanne. Oh, I had a very busy weekend! First, Saturday morning, I visited my grandparents in Villeurbanne.
IDRIS Sympa ! Et après ? Nice! And after that?
CAMILLE Ensuite, j'ai fait des courses, et samedi soir, j'ai regardé un film avec ma sœur. Dimanche, j'ai travaillé toute la journée — j'ai enfin fini mon projet pour le cours d'histoire ! Then I did some shopping, and Saturday evening, I watched a film with my sister. Sunday, I worked all day — I finally finished my project for history class!
IDRIS Bravo ! Moi, je n'ai pas encore fini le mien. Well done! I haven't finished mine yet.
CAMILLE Et toi, qu'est-ce que tu as fait ? And you, what did you do?
IDRIS Vendredi soir, j'ai vu mes amis. On a parlé pendant des heures ! Samedi, j'ai dû travailler — j'ai une réunion importante lundi. Et dimanche, j'ai lu un livre et j'ai bu beaucoup de café. Friday night, I saw my friends. We talked for hours! Saturday, I had to work — I have an important meeting Monday. And Sunday, I read a book and drank a lot of coffee.
CAMILLE Tu as déjà écrit ton rapport pour la réunion ? Have you already written your report for the meeting?
IDRIS Oui, je l'ai écrit hier soir. Bon, allons en cours ! Yes, I wrote it last night. Okay, let's go to class!

Questions de compréhension — Comprehension questions:

  1. Qu'est-ce que Camille a fait samedi matin ? (What did Camille do Saturday morning?)
  2. Qu'est-ce que Camille a fini dimanche ? (What did Camille finish on Sunday?)
  3. Est-ce qu'Idris a fini son projet ? (Has Idris finished his project?)
  4. Qu'est-ce qu'Idris a fait vendredi soir ? (What did Idris do Friday night?)
  5. Pourquoi Idris a-t-il dû travailler samedi ? (Why did Idris have to work Saturday?)
  6. Relevez trois participes passés irréguliers dans le dialogue. (Find three irregular past participles in the dialogue.)
Grammar Note — On a parlé in the dialogue

Idris says On a parlé pendant des heures, using on with the compound past. Recall from Chapter 12 that on takes the same verb agreement as il/elle — here, the third-person singular form a, not the nous form avons. This is identical to how on behaves in the present tense; the same agreement rule simply carries over into the compound past.

Listening

Ask your instructor, a partner, or a text-to-speech tool to read the following account aloud at a moderate pace. Listen once for general meaning and to identify the order of events. Then listen a second time to complete the task below. Do not read along during the first listening.

Reading Note — Weekend social rhythms across the Francophone world

The three updates come from Tunisia, Switzerland, and Senegal. All three writers use the same standard compound-past structure, even though their weekends look quite different — a family celebration, a demanding exam, a museum visit. Family gatherings such as Awa's are common across many Francophone communities and often combine a meal, conversation, and music or dance. There is no single "typical" Francophone weekend; weekend activities depend on personal circumstances, season, and local custom just as they do anywhere else.

Writing Workshop

In this chapter you will write a connected chronological paragraph recounting a recent day or weekend, using the compound past with avoir.

  1. Study the models. Reread one of the three weekend updates above. Identify: (a) the past-time markers used, (b) the chronological connectors, (c) at least two regular and two irregular past participles, and (d) the one negative sentence in each text.
  2. Plan your timeline. List four to six things you actually did, or could plausibly have done, over a recent day or weekend. Put them in chronological order. For each, note the infinitive and check whether its past participle is regular or irregular.
  3. Write your first draft. Begin with a short introductory sentence stating the general time period (Le week-end dernier, j'ai eu une journée intéressante.). Then narrate your four to six activities in order, using at least two chronological connectors (d'abord, ensuite, après, enfin) and at least one negative sentence (je n'ai pas… / je n'ai pas encore…).
  4. Revise. Use the editing checklist below before writing your final copy.

Liste de vérification — Editing checklist:

  • Every verb in the compound past has a correctly conjugated form of avoir matching its subject.
  • Every regular past participle follows the -é / -i / -u pattern correctly.
  • Every irregular past participle is spelled correctly and matches the list in this chapter.
  • At least one negative sentence places ne … pas correctly around the auxiliary, with the participle after pas.
  • At least two chronological connectors organize the sequence of events.
  • Past-time expressions are placed naturally at the beginning or end of sentences.
  • All required accents are present, including on participles such as fini, parlé, lu, été.
  • The paragraph reads as a connected account, not a disconnected list.

Speaking Practice

Pratique contrôlée — Controlled practice: Hier, j'ai…

With a partner, take turns completing the sentence Hier, j'ai… with a different activity each time, alternating turns. Your partner must form a follow-up question using a question word and the compound past — for example, if you say Hier, j'ai travaillé, your partner asks Où est-ce que tu as travaillé ? Continue for at least six exchanges.

Tâche communicative — Communicative task: Le week-end dernier

With a partner, ask and answer Qu'est-ce que tu as fait le week-end dernier ? Each person should describe at least four activities in chronological order, using at least one chronological connector and one negative sentence. Your partner should ask at least two follow-up questions before switching roles.

Pour les apprenants autonomes — Solo alternative

Record yourself narrating your actual day yesterday from morning to evening, using only the compound past with avoir. Aim for at least six sentences, including at least two irregular participles and one negative sentence. Play back the recording and check: (a) Did every verb use a correctly conjugated form of avoir? (b) Did you use at least one chronological connector? (c) Did your negative sentence place ne … pas correctly?

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Weekend and social routines across the Francophone world

What people typically do on weekends varies widely across Francophone communities and individual circumstances — family obligations, work schedules, climate, and personal preference all shape the weekend. In some households, the weekend centers on an extended family meal; in others, it centers on individual errands, study, sport, or rest. When asking someone Qu'est-ce que tu as fait ce week-end ?, treat the answer as one person's actual experience rather than as representative of an entire culture or country. Asking genuine follow-up questions, as Idris and Camille do in the dialogue, is a natural and appreciated way to keep a conversation going.

Culture in Context — Recounting events versus reciting a list

In conversation, French speakers generally expect a narrated account, not a flat list of unconnected facts. Using chronological connectors (d'abord, ensuite, enfin) and reacting to what the other person says — rather than simply waiting for your own turn to speak — makes an exchange feel like a real conversation. The Common Mistakes and Speaking Practice sections of this chapter are designed to help you move from isolated sentences toward this kind of connected, responsive account.

Regional Variation — Le week-end and informal time expressions

The term le week-end, borrowed from English, is standard and widely used across France and much of the Francophone world; in Quebec, la fin de semaine is the preferred standard term, with le week-end also understood. Both are labeled here as productive options depending on regional context; this course uses le week-end as the default productive form, with la fin de semaine noted as the Quebec standard for recognition.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using être instead of avoir as the auxiliary

Error: *Je suis travaillé hier. *Elle est fini le projet.

Correction: J'ai travaillé hier. Elle a fini le projet. Every verb taught in this chapter uses avoir as its auxiliary. The small group of verbs that use être as their auxiliary is the topic of Chapter 17 — do not apply that pattern yet. If a verb is not in the Chapter 17 list, default to avoir.

Common Mistake — Changing the participle ending to match the subject

Error: *Elle a regardée un film. *Nous avons travaillés.

Correction: Elle a regardé un film. Nous avons travaillé. With avoir, the past participle in a basic sentence like these does not agree with the subject. The participle stays in its single base form (regardé, travaillé, fini, répondu, fait, vu, and so on) regardless of whether the subject is masculine, feminine, singular, or plural.

Common Mistake — Placing pas around the participle instead of the auxiliary

Error: *Je ai pas fini travaillé. *J'ai fini pas le projet.

Correction: Je n'ai pas fini le projet. The negation ne … pas wraps tightly around the conjugated auxiliary ai — not around the whole verb group, and not around the participle alone. The participle fini always comes after pas, never between ne and the auxiliary.

Common Mistake — Guessing an irregular participle by analogy

Error: *J'ai faisé mes devoirs. *J'ai voulu spelled or guessed incorrectly as *j'ai voulé.

Correction: J'ai fait mes devoirs. Irregular participles such as fait, eu, été, vu, pris, bu, lu, écrit, dit, pu, and voulu must be memorized individually — they cannot be predicted from the infinitive ending the way regular participles can. When in doubt, consult the Core Vocabulary table rather than guessing.

Common Mistake — Omitting the subject pronoun

Error: *Ai fini le rapport. *A regardé un film.

Correction: J'ai fini le rapport. Il a regardé un film. As in every other tense you have studied, French requires an overt subject pronoun in the compound past. Never drop the subject pronoun before the auxiliary, even though the verb ending may seem to make the subject obvious.

Looking Ahead

Ce que vous savez maintenant — What you can do now

You can now talk about completed past events — narrating what you did, asking others what they did, sequencing a short series of activities, and forming negative statements about the past, all using the compound past with avoir. Together with the present tense, near future, and everything you have learned about daily life, community interaction, and personal description, you can now connect present, near-future, and past time in your French. This is a major expansion of your communicative range.

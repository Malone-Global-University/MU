Raconter et rester en contact

Connected Stories and Follow-Up Messages · Speak and Write French: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Tell a coherent short story combining background description (imperfect) and completed events (compound past).
  2. Use venir de plus an infinitive to describe a very recent development.
  3. Refer back to previously named people, objects, and places using single direct-object pronouns (le, la, l', les), single indirect-object pronouns (lui, leur), and y and en in controlled contexts, avoiding repetition.
  4. Connect events with chronological (d'abord, ensuite, enfin), causal (parce que, donc), contrastive (mais, pourtant), and result connectors (alors, c'est pourquoi).
  5. Recognize the spoken omission of ne in informal conversation and use it selectively in informal written messages.
  6. Use relative pronouns qui, que, and où to combine short sentences into more fluent narration.
  7. Write a connected follow-up message in an appropriate register, distinguishing informal and formal conventions.
  8. Sustain a short conversational narrative and respond to follow-up questions from your partner.

Communication Scenario

Léa, who spent the summer working in Montréal, has just returned to her home city of Nantes. She calls her friend Kofi to catch up, and later sends a follow-up message to her supervisor at the Montréal company. In the phone call, she tells Kofi what happened — what the city was like, what she did there, a problem she encountered, and how it was resolved. In her follow-up message, she thanks her supervisor, mentions something she just accomplished, and expresses a plan for the future. This chapter shows you how to tell that kind of connected, layered story — and how to keep the conversation going afterward.

  1. You have now learned three time frames in French: present, compound past, and imperfect. Before you read this chapter, try combining all three in a few sentences: describe what the weather was like yesterday (imperfect), what you did in spite of it (compound past), and what you plan to do today (present or near future). This kind of connected account — moving between time frames, referring back to people and things without repeating their names — is exactly what this chapter develops.
  2. In Chapters 16 and 17 you practiced the compound past, and in Chapter 18 you learned the imperfect and began contrasting the two. Notice that a real narrative rarely uses just one tense at a time. Good storytellers shift fluidly between background and event, then look ahead. By the end of this chapter, you will be able to do exactly that — and to hold a whole story together with connectors and reference pronouns.

Pronunciation and Spelling

Connected narrative rhythm

When you tell a story in French, several short sentences are grouped into a connected flow. Connectors such as d'abord, ensuite, alors, and pourtant are typically unstressed and pronounced quickly, allowing the content words that follow them to carry prominence. Practice reading your narratives aloud so that the connectors blend smoothly into the rhythm of each sentence rather than being heavily accented.

Sound Focus — Pronoun-verb grouping in connected narration

When a clitic pronoun precedes the verb, it forms a single rhythmic unit with the verb: je le lui ai dit is spoken as one unbroken phrase, not as four separate stressed items. The pronoun receives no independent stress. Practice grouping je l'ai vu, il lui a répondu, and on y est allé as smooth, uninterrupted units. Returning to phrase-level flow that you practiced in Chapters 7 and 11 will help; the principle is the same — French stress falls on the last syllable of the rhythmic group, not on individual words.

Spoken omission of ne — recognizing it in narration

In ordinary informal conversation and informal written messages, many French speakers omit ne from negation, saying j'ai pas vu instead of je n'ai pas vu, or c'est pas grave instead of ce n'est pas grave. This omission is very common in everyday speech and informal digital writing. You will hear it in authentic conversation and in the informal parts of this chapter's listening material.

Standard written French retains both ne and pas. In informal messages to close friends or family, omitting ne is natural and not considered an error in that register. In formal writing — emails to supervisors, academic texts, formal correspondence — standard complete negation with ne … pas is required. This chapter's Writing Workshop gives you practice distinguishing the two registers.

Sound Focus — Discourse-marker intonation

Connectors such as donc, alors, and pourtant when used as sentence-initial discourse markers are often followed by a very brief pause in careful speech, or said with a slight rise in pitch before the main clause begins: Donc↗… il a décidé de partir. This rising intonation on the connector signals to your listener that the main information is still coming. In fast conversational speech, this pause or rise may disappear entirely. For learners, pausing slightly after a connector at the start of a sentence is a natural and helpful strategy while developing fluency.

Liaison and register in narration

You have now studied mandatory liaison (Chapters 4, 8–12) and begun recognizing optional liaison (Chapter 18). In connected narrative, optional liaison is more likely in careful or formal speech and less likely in relaxed informal conversation. The sentences in this chapter's reading passages are written at a neutral planned-speech register, where many optional liaisons would be natural. In the informal dialogue, fewer optional liaisons are expected. Do not feel obliged to produce optional liaison in conversation; focus on mandatory patterns and let optional liaison develop naturally over time.

Relative-clause phrasing

When qui, que, or où introduces a relative clause, French speakers typically continue without pausing between the antecedent and the relative clause. Practice reading le restaurant où nous avons dîné and la lettre qu'elle a écrite as single flowing units, keeping où and qu' closely attached to the following verb phrase.

Grammar Instruction

19.1 Compound past and imperfect — consolidation

Purpose: combine both past tenses smoothly to tell a story with background, events, and reactions.

You have now studied both past tenses separately. In real narration, they appear together in the same paragraph — and often in the same sentence. The fundamental distinction you learned in Chapter 18 applies here: the compound past presents completed, bounded events; the imperfect presents background states, ongoing circumstances, repeated habits, or descriptions against which events occur.

Language Building Block
Imperfect [setting / background] + compound past [event]
The imperfect paints the scene · The compound past moves the story forward
Il faisait beau, alors nous avons décidé de faire une promenade.
The weather was nice, so we decided to go for a walk.
  • J'étais fatigué, donc je n'ai pas répondu tout de suite. I was tired, so I did not answer right away. étais = background state [imperfect] · n'ai pas répondu = completed event [compound past]
  • Elle cherchait son passeport quand elle a trouvé les billets. She was looking for her passport when she found the tickets. cherchait = ongoing action in progress [imperfect] · a trouvé = interrupting event [compound past]
  • Pendant qu'il lisait, le téléphone a sonné. While he was reading, the phone rang. lisait = simultaneous background activity [imperfect] · a sonné = single event [compound past]

19.2 Venir de plus infinitive — recent past

Purpose: express that something has just happened moments or very recently before the moment of speaking.

Venir de plus an infinitive describes an action in the very recent past — something that has just been completed. It uses the present tense of venir to convey immediacy, even though the action is finished.

Language Building Block
Subject + [present of venir] + de + [infinitive]
Present of venir shows currency · de links to the infinitive · no auxiliary needed
Je viens de recevoir ton message. Elle vient de terminer le rapport.
I just received your message. She has just finished the report.

Grammar Note — Venir de versus the compound past

Both venir de plus infinitive and the compound past refer to completed actions, but they differ in temporal distance. Je viens de manger means "I have just eaten" — moments ago. J'ai mangé means "I ate" — any time in the past, near or far. Use venir de when you want to stress the freshness or immediacy of the action. In follow-up messages, je viens de… is a natural way to open with a recent development: Je viens de voir votre email. ("I have just seen your email.")

19.3 Single-pronoun consolidation — le, la, l', les, lui, leur, y, en

Purpose: avoid repeating full nouns in narration by using single clitics to refer back to people, objects, and places.

You have learned direct-object pronouns (le, la, l', les) since Chapter 9, indirect-object pronouns (lui, leur) since Chapter 13, y for place since Chapter 11, and en for quantities and partitives since Chapter 8. This chapter consolidates their use in connected narration, where switching between full nouns and pronouns is what makes speech and writing feel natural rather than repetitive.

PronomRemplaceExemple
le / la / l' / lesa direct object (person or thing)Tu as vu le film ? — Oui, je l'ai vu.
luian indirect object (singular) — à + personTu as téléphoné à Mia ? — Oui, je lui ai téléphoné.
leuran indirect object (plural) — à + peopleTu as écrit à tes parents ? — Oui, je leur ai écrit.
ya place (à / dans / en + location) or à + thingTu es allé(e) à Lyon ? — Oui, j'y suis allé(e).
ende + noun; a quantity or partitiveTu as besoin d'aide ? — Oui, j'en ai besoin.

All single clitics are placed directly before the conjugated verb (or before the auxiliary in the compound past). In a sentence with venir de, the pronoun precedes the infinitive: je viens de le lire (I have just read it).

Grammar Note — Pronoun placement in the compound past

In the compound past, single clitics precede the auxiliary avoir or être, not the past participle: je l'ai vu (not *je ai vu le). You may notice that when a direct-object pronoun precedes the auxiliary avoir, the past participle sometimes changes its ending to agree with that pronoun in gender and number — for example, la lettre qu'elle a écrite (the letter she wrote), where écrite agrees with the feminine la lettre. This agreement rule with preceding direct objects is presented here for recognition only; you are not required to produce it independently in this chapter. Focus on correct pronoun placement and form.

19.4 Relative pronouns qui, que, and où — consolidation

Purpose: combine two short sentences into one more fluent sentence by introducing a relative clause.

You first used qui, que, and où in Chapter 14. In narration, they are essential for reducing repetition and creating more connected prose. Recall the key distinction:

PronomFonction dans la relativeExemple
quisubject of the relative clauseJ'ai un ami qui travaille à Dakar.
que / qu'direct object of the relative clauseC'est le film que nous avons vu.
oùplace or time referenceLe café où nous nous sommes retrouvés est fermé.

Que elides to qu' before a vowel sound: le projet qu'elle a fini. Qui never elides.

Language Focus

Combining sentences with relative pronouns gives your narration the texture of natural, fluent French. Instead of writing J'ai rencontré une femme. Elle dirige une entreprise à Bruxelles., you can write J'ai rencontré une femme qui dirige une entreprise à Bruxelles. The result is not just more elegant — it also sounds more like the way educated speakers actually tell stories. Practice transforming pairs of simple sentences into single sentences with qui, que, or où as one of the core revision steps in your Writing Workshop today.

19.5 Spoken omission of ne — register awareness

Purpose: recognize spoken and informal written ne-omission and use it appropriately by register.

In everyday French conversation and informal digital messages, ne is very frequently omitted. What remains is pas (or jamais, plus, rien, personne) directly following the subject pronoun or the auxiliary:

  • Spoken / informal: J'ai pas encore fini. I haven't finished yet.
  • Standard written: Je n'ai pas encore fini. I haven't finished yet.
  • Spoken / informal: C'est pas grave. It's not a big deal.
  • Standard written: Ce n'est pas grave. It's not a big deal.

The chart below shows when to use each form:

ContexteNormeExemple
Standard written French (essays, formal emails, academic)Always retain ne … pasJe n'ai pas pu répondre.
Informal written messages (texts, casual emails to friends)Ne may be omittedJ'ai pas pu répondre.
Ordinary conversationNe is often omittedJ'ai pas pu répondre.
Formal conversation, presentationsRetain ne … pasJe n'ai pas pu répondre.

Your formal writing — including the formal email in this chapter's Writing Workshop — must retain complete negation with ne. Your informal message may use ne-omission selectively if it reflects your genuine register choice for that context.

Model Dialogue

Contexte : Un dimanche soir. Léa, de retour à Nantes après un stage d'été à Montréal, appelle son ami Kofi. Ils ne se sont pas parlé depuis six semaines. Registre : conversation informelle entre amis proches, tu.
Setting: Sunday evening. Léa, back in Nantes after a summer work placement in Montréal, calls her friend Kofi. They have not spoken for six weeks. Register: informal conversation between close friends, tu.

KOFI Allô ? Hello?
LÉA Kofi ! C'est Léa. Je viens de rentrer à Nantes — je suis arrivée hier soir ! Kofi! It's Léa. I have just got back to Nantes — I arrived last night!
KOFI Léa ! Enfin ! Alors, comment c'était, Montréal ? Léa! Finally! So, what was Montréal like?
LÉA C'était incroyable. La ville est vraiment grande — je m'attendais pas à ça. Et il y avait tellement de choses à faire ! D'abord, au début, j'étais un peu perdue parce que je connaissais personne. Mais ensuite j'ai rencontré des collègues super sympa. It was incredible. The city is really big — I wasn't expecting that. And there was so much to do! At first, in the beginning, I was a bit lost because I didn't know anyone. But then I met really nice colleagues.
KOFI Et le travail, ça s'est bien passé ? And the work — did it go well?
LÉA En général, oui. Mais il y a eu un problème au milieu du stage — j'ai perdu un fichier important que je travaillais depuis une semaine. J'étais vraiment paniquée ! In general, yes. But there was a problem in the middle of the placement — I lost an important file that I had been working on for a week. I was really panicked!
KOFI Oh non ! Qu'est-ce que tu as fait ? Oh no! What did you do?
LÉA Heureusement, ma collègue Amira m'a aidée. Elle a trouvé une version sauvegardée dans le système, donc j'ai pu finir le projet à temps. J'étais tellement soulagée ! Luckily, my colleague Amira helped me. She found a saved version in the system, so I was able to finish the project on time. I was so relieved!
KOFI Super ! Et tu as vu des choses intéressantes en dehors du travail ? Great! And did you see interesting things outside of work?
LÉA Oui ! Le week-end, on visitait différents quartiers. J'ai adoré le Plateau-Mont-Royal — c'est le quartier où mes collègues habitaient. On y allait souvent le samedi. Et j'ai même appris quelques mots en québécois ! Yes! On weekends, we would visit different neighbourhoods. I loved the Plateau-Mont-Royal — it's the neighbourhood where my colleagues lived. We often went there on Saturdays. And I even learned a few Québécois words!
KOFI Ha ! Lesquels ? Ha! Which ones?
LÉA Je te les dirai en personne ! On se voit cette semaine ? I'll tell you in person! Shall we meet up this week?
KOFI Oui, avec plaisir ! J'ai hâte d'entendre tout ça. Yes, I'd love that! I can't wait to hear all about it.

Regional Variation — Le québécois dans le dialogue

Léa mentions learning a few words "en québécois," which her friend finds amusing. Québec French has its own vocabulary, pronunciation features, and informal spoken forms distinct from those of metropolitan France, though both are entirely mutually intelligible. Features such as the affrication of /t/ and /d/ before high front vowels (so tu sounds closer to "tsu" in casual speech), the pronoun tu used as a question particle, and vocabulary items like la fin de semaine (weekend), un char (a car), and le dépanneur (a corner store) are characteristic. These are presented here as recognition vocabulary; the course's productive model remains contemporary metropolitan French.

Account: "I'm going to tell you about something that happened to me last month. It was a Tuesday — I had to present an important project in front of my whole team. She replied immediately and started the meeting without me — she told everyone I was on my way. I am very grateful to her. Finally, at the end of the day, my manager told me he was pleased with the result. I just received a confirmation email this morning — the project is approved!"

Listening Note — Combining tenses in the account

Notice how the speaker moves between the compound past and the imperfect throughout the account. Background conditions — c'était un mardi, je devais présenter, il faisait chaud — are all in the imperfect. Completed sequential events — j'ai pris le bus, je suis descendu, j'ai attendu, j'ai envoyé un message, j'ai fait ma présentation — are all in the compound past. The final sentence, je viens de recevoir un email, uses venir de to bring a very recent development into the story. This layered use of tenses is exactly the pattern you are practicing in this chapter.

Reading Note — Register and closing formulas in French correspondence

French email and letter closings vary considerably by register and region. In formal correspondence in France and much of Francophone Europe and Africa, Cordialement is a widely used semi-formal close — professional but not stiff. More formal options include Veuillez agréer, Madame, l'expression de mes salutations distinguées, which would sound excessive in many everyday professional contexts. In Québec, Cordialement and Bien à vous are similarly common in professional settings. Informal messages close with À bientôt, Bises, Bisous, À plus (or the abbreviated A+ in text), depending on the relationship. Notice that Safi uses Cordialement in her formal email to Madame Pernet, and no formal closing at all in her text exchange with Yves — just an emoji. This is entirely natural and appropriate to each context.

In this chapter you will write two short connected texts: one informal message and one more formal follow-up email or note, both building on a story you have already drafted or planned.

  1. Choose your story.   2. Write the informal message. Write a 60–80 word informal message to a friend (tu) in which you recount the experience.   3. Write the formal follow-up. Write a 60–80 word formal or semi-formal email related to the same event — for example, a thank-you to a supervisor, a confirmation to an institution, or an update to an organization. Liste de vérification — Editing checklist:

  • Compound-past forms: does each have a correctly conjugated auxiliary (avoir or être) matching its subject?
  • Imperfect forms: are endings correct, and do these forms describe background, states, or ongoing activities?
  • At least one venir de plus infinitive is used correctly in one of the two texts.
  • At least one single clitic pronoun replaces a full noun phrase (avoiding unnecessary repetition).
  • At least one relative clause with qui, que, or où appears in either text.
  • Connectors from at least two different categories are used.
  • The informal message uses tu; the formal email uses vous consistently throughout.
  • The formal email retains complete ne … pas in every negative sentence.
  • All required accents are present; check especially é in participles and è in imperfect endings.
  • The formal email opens and closes with an appropriate formula for the relationship and purpose.
  • Both texts read as connected accounts, not disconnected sentences.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Storytelling and response in Francophone conversation

In many Francophone social contexts, recounting an experience is a valued conversational skill. A good story has structure — a beginning, a complication, and a resolution — and a storyteller who can move fluently between background and event, use reactions and emotional commentary, and maintain the listener's interest is typically regarded as engaging company. The listener's role is equally active: asking genuine questions (Et après ? Qu'est-ce que tu as fait ? Et ça s'est bien passé ?) signals real interest and helps the teller develop the narrative. The back-and-forth between Léa and Kofi in today's dialogue models this dynamic — Kofi does not just wait for Léa to finish, he asks, reacts, and keeps the conversation alive.

Culture in Context — Informal versus formal written French

The gap between informal and formal written French is wider than the equivalent gap in English, particularly in professional and institutional contexts. French emails to people in authority tend to open with a formal salutation (Madame, Monsieur), follow a polite and somewhat distanced tone, and close with a formula. The degree of formality varies by institution, generation, and Francophone region — email culture in Québec, for instance, tends toward slightly more directness and shorter formulas than traditional French administrative correspondence. Regardless of regional variety, the key principle is consistent: know your reader, know the relationship, and choose your forms accordingly. Switching from formal to informal register in the middle of a message — or omitting a closing formula in a professional email — can seem careless or even rude.

Regional Variation — Francophones in Côte d'Ivoire and in multilingual contexts

Text A in today's reading features Safi, who is in Abidjan, Côte d'Ivoire. French is one of Côte d'Ivoire's official languages and serves as the primary language of education, government, and formal professional life, alongside Dioula and dozens of other national languages. Many French speakers in Côte d'Ivoire, like elsewhere in Francophone Africa, move fluidly between French and other languages in daily life. Urban informal French in Abidjan has developed its own characteristics, including the widely known urban slang variety called Nouchi, which has had a notable influence on popular music and youth culture in the region. Standard written French as used in Safi's text is recognizable and natural across the Francophone world; her informal messages to Yves reflect the relaxed register of close friendship, not a regional dialect.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using the wrong tense for background versus event

Error: *Il a fait beau donc nous avons décidé de sortir. (when describing the weather as a background condition)

Correction: Il faisait beau donc nous avons décidé de sortir. Weather, time, age, and ongoing states or descriptions in the past belong in the imperfect, not the compound past. The compound past is for completed, bounded events that changed the narrative. Ask yourself: is this a state or a scene, or is this something that happened? A state or scene → imperfect. Something that happened → compound past.

Common Mistake — Using venir de with a conjugated verb instead of an infinitive

Error: *Je viens de j'ai fini. *Elle vient de elle a reçu le message.

Correction: Je viens de finir. Elle vient de recevoir le message. The construction is always venir de plus the infinitive — not venir de plus a conjugated form. The infinitive does not change; no auxiliary is needed. Think of venir de as functioning like aller plus infinitive (near future), but pointing backward in time rather than forward.

Common Mistake — Confusing lui and le / la as direct and indirect object pronouns

Error: *J'ai téléphoné le. *Elle lui a vu hier.

Correction: Je lui ai téléphoné. / Elle l'a vue hier. The choice between le/la/les (direct) and lui/leur (indirect) depends on the verb: téléphoner à takes an indirect object (so: lui), while voir takes a direct object (so: le/la/les). When uncertain, identify whether the verb is followed by à + person (→ indirect → lui/leur) or takes the person directly without à (→ direct → le/la/les).

Common Mistake — Using qui where que is needed, or vice versa

Error: *C'est le film qui j'ai vu. *J'ai un ami que travaille ici.

Correction: C'est le film que j'ai vu. J'ai un ami qui travaille ici. The test is the function of the relative pronoun inside its own clause. If it is the subject of the relative clause (the thing doing the action), use qui. If it is the direct object (the thing receiving the action, with a subject and verb already present in the relative clause), use que. In C'est le film que j'ai vu, que is the object of j'ai vu — "I saw it." In J'ai un ami qui travaille ici, qui is the subject of travaille — "he works."

Common Mistake — Using ne-omission in formal written contexts

Error: (In a formal email) *Je suis désolé de ne pas avoir pu — je n'ai pas pu être présent à la réunion is correct; writing *j'ai pas pu être présent in a formal email.

Correction: In formal written French — professional emails, academic work, formal correspondence — always retain ne … pas in full. Ne-omission is a feature of informal conversation and informal digital writing between people on familiar terms. Applying it in a formal email to a supervisor or institution is a register mismatch that can undermine the professionalism of the message.

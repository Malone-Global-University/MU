Se déplacer en ville

Getting Around Town · Speak and Write French: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Ask where a place is using où est… ? and où se trouve… ?
  2. Give and follow basic directions using sequencing expressions (tournez, continuez, prenez, traversez).
  3. Discuss means of transportation using the correct prepositions (en voiture, à vélo, en métro).
  4. State where you are going using aller with à, chez, en, and the appropriate contractions.
  5. Form and use the affirmative imperative with tu, nous, and vous for common motion and direction verbs.
  6. Form and use the negative imperative in controlled directions contexts.
  7. Use the adverbial pronoun y to refer back to a previously named place.
  8. Ask and answer questions about location and route using où and comment.
  9. Review il y a for describing what exists in a neighborhood.
  10. Recognize the difference between liaison and enchaînement in direction phrases.
  11. Write a set of step-by-step directions from one place to another.

Communication Scenario

A visitor arriving in Lomé, Togo, steps out of her hotel and needs to find the nearest pharmacy, a post office, and the central market. She stops to ask a local resident, who gives her step-by-step directions and tells her which bus to take for the market. The visitor confirms, asks a clarifying question, and thanks the person before setting off. Later she texts a friend: J'y suis ! C'est à dix minutes à pied. — "I'm there! It's ten minutes on foot." This chapter gives you all the tools to be on either side of that conversation.

  1. Think about the last time you asked someone for directions in English. What expressions did you use to confirm understanding — "so I turn left at the light?" How might French organize that same confirmation? As you work through this chapter, notice how the imperative and sequencing words make directions efficient and clear.
  2. In French, prepositions before means of transport are not always what English learners expect: you say en voiture (by car) but à vélo (by bike) and à pied (on foot). The Grammar section explains the logic behind these choices so you do not have to memorize each combination as an isolated item.

Pronunciation and Spelling

French r in direction and transport vocabulary

This chapter is rich in words containing the French rhotic sound — the sound associated with the letter r. Words like tourner, carrefour, traverser, rond-point, arrêt, droite, gare, rue, prendre all contain it. The French rhotic is produced in the back of the mouth, where the back of the tongue approaches the soft palate (velum) or uvula, creating gentle friction. It is very different from the English r, which is produced with a retroflex tongue position in the middle of the mouth.

Sound Focus — Producing a workable French r

Two practical approaches:

  • Gargling method: Bring the back of your tongue toward the back of your mouth as if you are about to gargle. Produce a gentle friction sound while voicing. This approximates the most common French rhotic in metropolitan French.
  • Uvular approximation: Say the sound in the German name Bach — the velar or uvular fricative — but voice it and reduce the friction. The result is close to a natural French r.

The key points for this chapter: (1) The French r is never silent between vowels. (2) A word-final r is often pronounced in French, unlike in English: car in French means "because" and its r is pronounced; gare (station) also has a pronounced r. (3) Do not substitute an English retroflex r — the sound will be understood but will stand out strongly as non-French. A workable approximation is sufficient at this stage; perfecting the sound comes through sustained listening and practice.

Consonant clusters across phrase boundaries

Direction phrases frequently place consonant sounds side by side across word boundaries: tout droit, à gauche, au carrefour, prenez la rue. Practice these as connected phrases rather than as individual words, because direction-giving is a fluent, linked activity. Breaking every word apart sounds unnatural and can interfere with comprehension.

Enchaînement across direction phrases

Enchaînement is the resyllabification of a normally pronounced final consonant when the following word begins with a vowel sound. This happens at phrase boundaries in rapid or natural speech and is distinct from liaison. In this chapter, pay special attention to:

  • prenez à gauche — the final z of prenez links to à: pre-né-za-gô-che
  • traversez avenue — the z of traversez links to avenue
  • c'est en face — the t of est links to en: sé-ten-fas

Do not confuse these with liaison. Liaison introduces a consonant that would otherwise be completely silent. Enchaînement resyllabifies a consonant that is already pronounced. The practical result sounds similar, but the mechanism is different.

Liaison versus enchaînement — a key distinction

Pronunciation Focus — Liaison versus enchaînement

Liaison: A word-final consonant that is normally silent is pronounced before a vowel-initial word. The consonant is not otherwise heard.

  • un arrêt — the n of un is normally silent, but before arrêt it appears: u-na-rê. This is liaison.
  • vous allez — the s of vous is normally silent, but appears before allez: vou-za-lez. This is mandatory liaison.

Enchaînement: A final consonant that is always pronounced simply joins the next syllable when that syllable begins with a vowel.

  • avec elle — the c of avec is always pronounced; it links to elle: a-ve-kel. This is enchaînement.
  • cette avenue — the t of cette is always pronounced; it links to avenue: se-ta-ve-nü. This is enchaînement.

Both create the impression of a consonant "between" two words. The difference lies in whether that consonant is normally silent (liaison) or normally pronounced (enchaînement). At this stage, focus on recognizing the connected sound in both cases rather than always classifying which type it is.

Imperative intonation

Affirmative commands in French have a falling intonation: the pitch drops at the end. This is different from a question, where intonation rises, and from a statement, which has a more gradual fall. When giving directions, keep the imperative forms clear and confident with that falling pattern: Tournez à gauche ↘ — Continuez tout droit ↘ — Prenez la deuxième rue à droite ↘. A rising intonation on a direction can make it sound like a question.

Street and place-name capitalization

In French addresses, the generic word (rue, boulevard, avenue, place) is lowercase, but the specific name that follows is capitalized. Compare: la rue de la Paix, le boulevard Haussmann, l'avenue Kwame N'Krumah. When writing directions, apply this convention consistently. The article that introduces the generic word is part of the phrase and is also lowercase: la place de la République.

11.1 Aller and venir — present tense review and motion uses

Purpose: use aller and venir to state where you are going, where someone is coming from, and to connect motion with a destination using the correct preposition.

Both aller and venir are highly irregular and must be memorized. You encountered aller as a formulaic greeting (Comment allez-vous ?) in Chapter 1, and the near-future construction (aller + infinitif) is introduced in Chapter 12. Here the focus is on their present-tense forms and their use with destinations.

Key contrast: aller expresses destination (where you are going to); venir expresses origin or accompaniment (where you are coming from, or that you are joining someone). Je vais à la pharmacie — I am going to the pharmacy. Je viens du marché — I am coming from the market.

Prepositions with destinations: à, chez, en, and their contractions

Choosing the correct preposition before a destination depends on what kind of place it is:

  • à + city or specific location Je vais à la gare. / Elle va à Paris. / Il va au marché. (à + le = au ; à + les = aux)
  • chez + person or their establishment Je vais chez le médecin. / Tu vas chez ta cousine ? (chez never contracts)
  • en + feminine country or region Nous allons en France. / Elle va en Côte d'Ivoire. (from Chapter 3 — reviewed here in a movement context)
Grammar Note — Au, aux, du, des: contractions with à and de

French does not allow the sequences à le or de le — they always contract:

  • à + le → au : Je vais au parc. (I am going to the park.)
  • à + les → aux : Allez aux feux rouges. (Go to the traffic lights.)
  • de + le → du : Je viens du marché. (I am coming from the market.)
  • de + les → des : Il arrive des îles. (He is arriving from the islands.)

Before a feminine noun or any noun beginning with a vowel sound, no contraction occurs: à la gare, à l'hôtel, de la place, de l'arrêt. You practiced these contractions in Chapters 3 and 6; this chapter gives you extensive new practice in the context of directions and movement.

11.2 The imperative — affirmative and negative

Purpose: give direct instructions, directions, and advice using the imperative with tu, nous, and vous.

The imperative (l'impératif) is used to give commands, instructions, directions, invitations, and suggestions. It uses only three persons: tu (one person, informal), nous (let's …), and vous (formal or plural). There is no subject pronoun in an imperative sentence — the form itself implies the subject.

Formation of the affirmative imperative:

For most verbs, the imperative comes directly from the present tense, with the subject pronoun removed:

  • -er verbs (tu form): the final -s of the tu present is dropped. Tu tournes → Tourne ! Turn!
  • -er verbs (vous form): same as present tense vous form. Vous tournez → Tournez ! Turn!
  • -er verbs (nous form): same as present tense nous form. Nous tournons → Tournons ! Let's turn!
  • -re and -ir verbs: all three forms keep the -s as written. Tu prends → Prends ! Vous prenez → Prenez !

Va ! (the tu imperative of aller) is irregular — it does not follow the pattern of dropping -s from vas; rather, it uses the stem va with no ending. However, before y, the -s is restored for phonetic reasons: Vas-y ! (Go there! / Go on!) This will be discussed further in the y section below.

Grammar Note — Irregular imperatives: être and avoir

Two common verbs have completely irregular imperatives that do not follow the pattern above:

  • Être: sois / soyons / soyez (be / let's be / be)
  • Avoir: aie / ayons / ayez (have / let's have / have)

These are not commonly needed for directions, but they appear in high-frequency expressions such as Soyez prudent(e) ! (Be careful!) and N'ayez pas peur ! (Don't be afraid!). Recognize them; you do not need to produce them for directions work in this chapter.

Formation of the negative imperative:

To make an imperative negative, wrap ne … pas around the verb form exactly as with any other negation. The word order is: ne + verb + pas.

  • Ne tournez pas à gauche. Do not turn left.
  • Ne prenez pas le boulevard principal. Do not take the main boulevard.
  • Ne continue pas tout droit. Don't keep going straight.
  • N'allez pas à droite. Don't go right. Elision: ne → n' before vowel
Language Focus

The imperative and subject pronouns: In English, commands are simply verb forms — "Turn left," "Take the bus." French works the same way for directions: Tournez à gauche, Prenez le bus. The subject pronoun (vous, tu, nous) disappears. This is normal and expected. The only time a pronoun appears with an imperative is in emphatic or contrastive use outside this chapter's scope, or with object pronouns — which are deferred to a later chapter. For all the directions work you will do here, use the imperative form with no subject pronoun.

11.3 Y — referring back to a previously named place

Purpose: use the adverbial pronoun y to avoid repeating a place already mentioned in the conversation, replacing a location phrase introduced with à, en, chez, dans, or another locative preposition.

The pronoun y replaces a previously named location in the conversation. It means "there" or "to / at that place." Once a destination or location has been established, y avoids repetition, just as "there" does in English.

  • — Tu vas à la pharmacie ? — Oui, j'y vais maintenant. — Are you going to the pharmacy? — Yes, I'm going there now.
  • — Il y a une bonne boulangerie au marché ? — Oui, il y en a une. Note: il y a remains; y here is part of that fixed expression.
  • — Tu connais le parc Nkrumah ? — Oui, j'y vais tous les dimanches. — Do you know Nkrumah Park? — Yes, I go there every Sunday.
  • Allons-y ! Let's go there! / Let's go! High-frequency formula; learn as a unit
  • Vas-y ! Go there! / Go on! tu imperative of aller + y — the -s returns before y

Placement of y: Like object pronouns, y is placed before the conjugated verb in most sentence types:

  • Je y vais. → more naturally: J'y vais. (Elision: je y → j'y)
  • Elle y est déjà. She is already there.
  • Nous y allons en bus. We are going there by bus.
  • Vous n'y allez pas ? Aren't you going there?

In this chapter, keep your use of y to these clear patterns: j'y vais, tu y vas, il/elle y va, nous y allons, vous y allez, ils/elles y vont, plus the high-frequency formulaic imperatives allons-y and vas-y. Wider uses of y are developed in Chapters 12 and 17.

Grammar Note — Allons-y and Vas-y: hyphens with imperative + y

When y follows an imperative form, a hyphen is required: Allons-y !, Vas-y !. Notice that the tu form of aller — which is normally Va without a final -s — regains its -s before y for phonetic reasons: Vas-y, not *Va-y. This is the only context where this -s reappears on the tu imperative of aller. The same restoration of -s occurs on -er verbs before y or en in emphatic commands, but that use is deferred beyond this chapter.

11.4 Question forms with où and comment — review and expansion

Purpose: ask clear, natural questions about location and route using familiar question strategies.

You already know three ways to form questions in French (intonation, est-ce que, and inversion for fixed formulas). For location questions, the interrogative word où (where) is combined with these strategies:

  • Où est la pharmacie ? Where is the pharmacy? (most neutral)
  • Où se trouve la gare ? Where is the station located? (slightly more formal; se trouver = to be located)
  • Où est-ce que je peux trouver une banque ? Where can I find a bank? (est-ce que strategy)
  • La bibliothèque, elle est où ? Where is the library? (spoken, intonation question — noun fronted for topic)
  • Comment est-ce qu'on y va ? How do we get there? (comment = how; on used inclusively)
  • Comment je vais à la gare ? How do I get to the station? (informal, intonation)
  • C'est loin d'ici ? Is it far from here?
  • C'est à combien de minutes ? How many minutes away is it?
Language Building Block
Excusez-moi, je cherche [place]. Vous pouvez m'indiquer le chemin ?

Model Dialogue

Contexte : Lomé, Togo. Aïcha vient d'arriver de l'aéroport. Elle cherche la pharmacie la plus proche et le marché central. Elle demande des renseignements à Kofi, un passant. Registre : conversation polis entre inconnus — vouvoiement.
Setting: Lomé, Togo. Aïcha has just arrived from the airport. She is looking for the nearest pharmacy and the central market. She asks Kofi, a passerby, for information. Register: polite conversation between strangers — vous form.

AÏCHA Excusez-moi, monsieur. Je cherche la pharmacie la plus proche. Vous savez où elle est ? Excuse me, sir. I'm looking for the nearest pharmacy. Do you know where it is?
KOFI Bien sûr. D'abord, continuez tout droit jusqu'au carrefour — c'est à deux cents mètres d'ici. Ensuite, tournez à gauche au feu rouge. La pharmacie est juste en face de la banque. Vous ne pouvez pas la manquer. Of course. First, keep going straight to the intersection — it's two hundred metres from here. Then turn left at the traffic light. The pharmacy is right across from the bank. You can't miss it.
AÏCHA Donc, tout droit, puis à gauche au feu. C'est bien ça ? So, straight ahead, then left at the light. Is that right?
KOFI Exactement. C'est à cinq minutes à pied. Exactly. It's five minutes on foot.
AÏCHA Merci beaucoup ! Et le marché central — je peux y aller à pied aussi ? Thank you very much! And the central market — can I walk there too?
KOFI Ah non, le marché est assez loin d'ici. Je vous conseille de prendre le bus numéro sept. L'arrêt est au bout de cette rue, à droite. Oh no, the market is quite far from here. I advise you to take bus number seven. The stop is at the end of this street, on the right.
AÏCHA Le bus numéro sept, à l'arrêt au bout de la rue. Et c'est à combien de minutes en bus ? Bus number seven, at the stop at the end of the street. And how many minutes away is it by bus?
KOFI À peu près vingt minutes. Descendez à l'arrêt « Marché central » — c'est le terminus du numéro sept. About twenty minutes. Get off at the "Marché central" stop — it's the end of the line for the number seven.
AÏCHA Parfait. Merci mille fois, monsieur ! Perfect. Thank you so much, sir!
KOFI De rien. Bonne journée ! You're welcome. Have a good day!

  3. Pourquoi est-ce que Kofi ne conseille pas d'aller au marché à pied ? (Why doesn't Kofi advise going to the market on foot?)

Listening Note — Sors de la gare

The form sors in the message is the tu imperative of the verb sortir (to leave / to go out). Sortir is an irregular -ir verb; its tu form in the present is tu sors, and because it is not an -er verb, the imperative retains the -s: Sors ! Recognize this as a direction verb meaning "leave" or "come out of" when followed by de: Sors de la gare — come out of the station. You will see sortir return in later chapters.

Reading Note — Transport vocabulary across Francophone regions

The reading passages reflect some real variation in transport vocabulary and systems. In Montreal, the metro network uses line colors (orange, green, blue, yellow) and station for metro stops. In Dakar, the Dakar Dem Dikk (meaning "to go and come back" in Wolof) is the main public bus operator. In Brussels, the tram (tram) is a central part of public transport, and tickets work across tram, metro, and bus. In all three cities, the vocabulary for stations (la station), stops (l'arrêt), lines (la ligne), and exits (la sortie) is broadly shared and understood across the Francophone world. The reference to Jean Coutu in the Montréal text identifies a Quebec pharmacy chain — a locally specific detail that serves as a landmark, just as any well-known local establishment might.

Writing Workshop

In this chapter you will write a set of step-by-step directions from one public place to another in a real or imagined Francophone city.

Identify: (a) how the instructions begin and end, (b) every imperative verb form and what it tells you to do, (c) how sequencing words connect the steps, (d) at least one use of a location preposition, and (e) the use of y if present.
  2. Choose your starting point and destination. Select two real or plausible places in a Francophone city: for example, a train station to a hotel, a bus stop to a university, or a market to a post office. Write these down: Point de départ : _____ Destination : _____
  3. Plan the route in notes. On a piece of paper, sketch a simple map or list the steps of the route in English. Aim for four to six steps. Then translate each step into a French imperative sentence before drafting.
  4. Write your first draft. Begin with a short orientation (Pour aller de [A] à [B], voici le chemin. or D'abord,…). Use imperative forms, at least three different sequencing expressions, at least one location preposition, and one reference to distance or time. If you can use y naturally, include it.
  5. Revise. Use the editing checklist below before writing your final copy.

Liste de vérification — Editing checklist:

  • Every direction step uses the correct imperative form (vous or tu — choose one and keep it consistent).
  • I used ne … pas correctly around the verb in any negative direction.
  • Contractions are correct: au, aux, du, des where required.
  • Sequencing words are used to connect steps naturally.
  • I used y at least once if there is a repeated location reference.
  • All required accents are present, including on à, é, è, and capital letters where needed.
  • Place names are capitalized correctly (la rue Pasteur, not la Rue pasteur).
  • The directions are in a logical order that would actually lead someone to the destination.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Greeting before asking for directions

In many Francophone contexts — particularly in West and Central Africa, but also in France and Belgium — it is considered impolite to launch immediately into a question when approaching a stranger. A brief greeting first is the expected social behavior: Bonjour monsieur / madame, excusez-moi de vous déranger… (Good morning sir / madam, excuse me for disturbing you…) or simply Excusez-moi… followed by the question. In Quebec, the same principle holds but the greeting may be briefer in a busy urban context. The dialogue in this chapter models this convention: Aïcha opens with Excusez-moi, monsieur before stating what she needs. This is not merely politeness — in some contexts, omitting the greeting entirely may mark you as rude or abrupt, and the person may be less willing to help.

Culture in Context — Asking for clarification and confirming understanding

When receiving directions, it is normal and expected to confirm your understanding — not doing so may suggest overconfidence or cause confusion. The most natural way is to repeat the key steps back as a brief summary: Donc, tout droit, puis à gauche au feu — c'est bien ça ? (So, straight ahead, then left at the light — is that right?). Useful expressions for seeking clarification include: Vous pouvez répéter, s'il vous plaît ? (Could you repeat that, please?), Plus lentement, s'il vous plaît (More slowly, please), Je n'ai pas bien compris (I didn't quite understand), and C'est à droite ou à gauche ? (Is it on the right or the left?). These repair expressions are essential for real interaction and will save you from going the wrong way.

Regional Variation — Transportation terminology

A few transport terms differ between major Francophone regions. In Quebec, the metro is also called le métro, but l'autobus is often used formally where France would say le bus; informally, Québécois speakers sometimes say l'autobus or simply le bus. In France, a city bus is le bus, while in many West African countries the word le taxi covers a wide range of shared transport options, including le taxi collectif (shared taxi running fixed routes), which functions differently from individual taxi hire. In francophone West Africa, la moto-taxi or le zémidjan (especially in Benin and Togo) refers to motorcycle taxis that are a primary urban transport mode. These terms are labeled here as regional and recognition-only. The productive vocabulary in this chapter uses terms that are broadly understood across Francophone contexts.

False Cognate Alert — La librairie versus la bibliothèque

Two vocabulary items in this chapter are worth highlighting because of their potential to mislead English speakers. La librairie means a bookshop — a shop where you buy books. La bibliothèque means a library — where you borrow books for free. These two are not interchangeable. Sending someone to la librairie when they want to borrow a book, or to la bibliothèque when they want to buy one, is a real source of confusion. Similarly, le musée (museum) and le monument (monument) are widely cognate and easy to use correctly; learn librairie / bibliothèque as a pair to anchor the contrast.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Keeping the subject pronoun with an imperative

Error: *Vous tournez à gauche ! (as a direction command)

Correction: Tournez à gauche ! The subject pronoun is dropped in the imperative. The sentence Vous tournez à gauche with the pronoun present is a statement — "You turn left" — not a command. To give a direction, remove the pronoun: Tournez !, Continuez !, Prenez !. The only pronouns that appear with imperatives are object pronouns (which are covered in later chapters).

Common Mistake — Using à le and de le instead of au and du

Error: *Je vais à le marché. *Je viens de le parc.

Correction: Je vais au marché. Je viens du parc. The sequences à le and de le do not exist in French. They always contract: à + le = au, de + le = du. Similarly, à + les = aux and de + les = des. Contractions do not occur before feminine nouns (à la gare, de la place) or before a vowel-initial noun (à l'arrêt, de l'aéroport). Before every masculine singular noun, check whether a contraction is required.

Common Mistake — Placing y after the verb

Error: *Je vais y maintenant. *Elle habite y.

Correction: J'y vais maintenant. Elle y habite. The pronoun y goes before the conjugated verb, not after it. Think of it like the object pronouns you practiced in Chapter 9: all of them cluster before the verb in declarative and negative sentences. The correct word order is: subject + y + verb. Exception: in affirmative imperatives, y goes after the verb with a hyphen: Allons-y !, Vas-y ! — but this is a special case specific to the imperative.

Common Mistake — Confusing à vélo and en vélo

Error: *Je vais en vélo.

Correction: Je vais à vélo. The preposition before vélo (and moto) is à, not en. This is a fixed convention of French. The preposition en is used for enclosed vehicles: en voiture, en bus, en métro, en train. The preposition à is used for vélo, moto, and the fixed expression à pied. Learn these three à expressions as a group and the rest will default correctly to en.

Common Mistake — Confusing liaison and enchaînement

Error: Describing avec elle as "a liaison" when the final c of avec is always pronounced.

Correction: Avec elle is an example of enchaînement, not liaison. Liaison introduces a consonant that is otherwise silent. Enchaînement resyllabifies a consonant that is always there. The c in avec is pronounced in all contexts — it does not appear only before vowels. When you link it to the following vowel, you are simply resyllabifying, not activating a hidden consonant. For practical purposes: if the final consonant is present before a consonant too, it is enchaînement. If it only appears before a vowel, it is liaison.

Common Mistake — Using aller instead of venir for "coming" to join someone

Error: *J'arrive dans dix minutes — je vais ! (to someone waiting for you)

Correction: J'arrive dans dix minutes — j'y vais ! or more naturally: J'arrive dans dix minutes ! If you want to express that you are coming toward the person you are speaking to, use venir: Je viens ! (I'm coming!). Aller implies movement away from or toward a third location; venir implies movement toward the speaker or the person being addressed. In written directions, this distinction matters less, but in spoken conversation it is an important signal of orientation.

Voyages et déplacements

Travel Experiences · Speak and Write French: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Form the compound past (passé composé) with the auxiliary être for the approved set of movement verbs.
  2. Distinguish which common verbs take être and which take avoir in the compound past.
  3. Apply basic past-participle agreement with être, matching the participle to the subject's gender and number.
  4. Form and use the compound past of reflexive verbs with être.
  5. Recount the stages of a trip: departure, journey, arrival, and lodging.
  6. Ask and answer questions about a past journey using être-verbs.
  7. Use y and en to refer back to previously named places and quantities in a travel context.
  8. Sequence a short travel narrative using familiar time connectors.
  9. Describe a basic travel problem and ask for help.
  10. Write a brief, connected travel narrative in the compound past.

Communication Scenario

Julien, the language-exchange partner from Chapter 4, sends Fatou a voice message after returning from a long weekend. He went to Marseille to visit a friend, and along the way several things happened — a missed connection, a new city, a small problem with his luggage. Fatou writes back asking questions about the trip and tells him about her own recent weekend away. This chapter gives you the tools to do the same: to recount where you went, how you got there, what happened along the way, and to ask someone else about their own travels.

  1. Think of the last time you went somewhere — even a short trip across town. What happened first? What happened next? In Chapter 16 you learned to talk about completed activities with avoir (j'ai mangé, j'ai visité). This chapter adds a second group of verbs that work differently — verbs of movement, which use a different helping verb entirely.
  2. In English, the past tense of "go" doesn't change to match who is doing the going: "she went," "they went," "I went" all use the same word went. In French, the past participle of aller sometimes changes spelling depending on the subject. Why might that be, when the participles you learned in Chapter 16 with avoir never changed? The Grammar section will explain.

Pronunciation and Spelling

Liaison after plural subject pronouns with être

You learned in Chapter 16 that the auxiliary and the past participle form one rhythmic group. With être, an additional liaison opportunity appears with plural subjects, because sont ends in a normally silent -t that links to a vowel-initial participle.

Sound Focus — Auxiliary and participle as one rhythmic group
  • elles sont arrivées — liaison links sont and arrivées: el-son-ta-ri-vé. The final -t of sont, normally silent, is pronounced before the vowel-initial participle.
  • ils sont allés — same pattern: il-son-ta-lé.
  • nous sommes restés — no liaison needed here, since restés begins with a consonant sound; the two words are simply linked smoothly by enchaînement-like rhythm.

This liaison is part of the same mandatory pattern you met with nous avons and vous avez in Chapter 4 — a plural subject form ending in a written consonant links to a following vowel sound.

Feminine and plural agreement: often silent, sometimes audible

Past-participle agreement with être is primarily a written phenomenon. In most cases, adding -e or -s to a participle does not change its pronunciation at all.

  • il est allé / elle est allée — both pronounced identically: [a-le]. The -e is silent.
  • ils sont partis / elles sont parties — both pronounced [par-ti]. The -s is silent.

A small number of participles do become audible in the feminine, following the same pattern you learned for adjectives in Chapter 4:

  • il est mort / elle est morte — the masculine final -t is silent; the feminine final -te is pronounced. (Mourir, to die, is receptive vocabulary here.)

The key idea for this chapter: written agreement is a real grammatical rule that you must apply when writing, even when your pronunciation does not change at all. Do not assume that a silent ending means the spelling rule can be skipped.

Connected travel narration

As you narrate a sequence of past events, keep the phrase-level rhythm you have practiced since Chapter 1: group words into arcs with prominence on the final syllable, rather than stressing each word separately. A short narrated sequence such as Nous sommes arrivés, nous avons cherché l'hôtel, et nous nous sommes reposés should flow as three connected rhythmic groups, not as a list of separately punched-out words.

Place-name pronunciation

This chapter's reading and dialogue include several French and Francophone place names. Pay attention to syllable count and nasal vowels: Marseille ends in the sound [ɛj], not an English "-ay" or "-eye" sound. Genève places stress, as always in French, on the final syllable of the phrase, not on the first syllable as English speakers might expect from "Geneva."

Grammar Instruction

17.1 The compound past with être

Purpose: narrate completed movement and a small set of other events using the auxiliary être instead of avoir.

In Chapter 16, you learned that the compound past (passé composé) is formed with an auxiliary verb plus a past participle. Most French verbs use avoir as their auxiliary. However, a specific, learnable group of verbs — mostly verbs of movement — use être instead. This is not optional or a matter of style; each verb has a fixed auxiliary that must be memorized with the verb.

PronomFormeForme spoken noteExemple
je je suis allé(e) Participle agrees with the subject Je suis allé à Marseille.
tu tu es allé(e) Final -s of es silent Tu es allé(e) où ?
il / elle / on il est allé / elle est allée On usually takes masculine agreement Elle est allée en Suisse.
nous nous sommes allé(e)s Liaison: nou-som-za-lé Nous sommes allés à la gare.
vous vous êtes allé(e)(s) Liaison: vou-zêt-za-lé Vous êtes arrivé(e)(s) quand ?
ils / elles ils sont allés / elles sont allées Liaison: il-son-ta-lé Ils sont partis hier.

The present tense of être (Chapter 1) supplies the auxiliary forms here: suis, es, est, sommes, êtes, sont. If you are unsure of any form, review the Chapter 1 conjugation before continuing.

Grammar Note — Memorizing the être-verb group

Many learners memorize the approved être-verb set in pairs that express opposite movements, which makes the list easier to retain: aller / venir (go / come), partir / arriver (leave / arrive), entrer / sortir (enter / exit), monter / descendre (go up / go down), rester / retourner (stay / go back). Rentrer (return home) and tomber (fall) complete the productive set for this chapter. Every reflexive verb also takes être, which you will practice in section 17.3.

17.2 Basic past-participle agreement with être

Purpose: apply written agreement between the past participle and the subject when the auxiliary is être.

With être-verbs, the past participle behaves like an adjective: it agrees in gender and number with the subject of the sentence. This is different from avoir-verbs, whose participles do not change to agree with the subject (you saw this in Chapter 16).

Masculin singulier
Féminin singulier
Pluriel (m. ou f.)
allé no ending added
allée add -e
allés / allées add -s (m.) or -es (f.)
arrivé no ending added
arrivée add -e
arrivés / arrivées add -s (m.) or -es (f.)
parti no ending added
partie add -e
partis / parties add -s (m.) or -es (f.)

The rule applies the same way regardless of which approved être-verb you use: identify the gender and number of the subject, then add the matching ending to the participle, exactly as you would for an adjective.

  • Marc est parti hier. Marc left yesterday. (m. sg. subject → no ending)
  • Léa est partie hier. Léa left yesterday. (f. sg. subject → -e)
  • Mes parents sont rentrés tard. My parents came home late. (m. pl. subject → -s)
  • Mes sœurs sont rentrées tard. My sisters came home late. (f. pl. subject → -es)
Language Focus

Why does this agreement exist with être but not with avoir? Historically and structurally, the être-verb participle functions like an adjective describing the subject — almost as if you were saying "Léa is [in a departed state]." With avoir-verbs, the participle is not describing the subject at all; it is part of the action the subject performed on something else, so it does not agree with the subject. You are not required to master the deeper explanation behind this distinction — only to apply the rule correctly: être-verb participles agree with the subject; the avoir-verb participles you learned in Chapter 16 do not.

This chapter does not cover agreement exceptions involving a preceding direct object — that advanced rule is intentionally deferred. For now, every example you produce should involve simple subject agreement only.

17.3 Compound past of reflexive verbs

Purpose: narrate completed reflexive actions, such as waking up, getting ready, or getting lost, using être and the correct reflexive pronoun.

Every reflexive verb takes être in the compound past, with no exceptions. The reflexive pronoun (me, te, se, nous, vous, se) is placed before the auxiliary, and the past participle generally agrees with the subject in the same way as other être-verbs, for the regular reflexive verbs in this chapter's approved set.

  • Elle s'est bien amusée pendant le voyage. She had a great time during the trip. (f. sg. → -e)

17.4 Contrast between auxiliaries

Purpose: combine avoir-verbs and être-verbs accurately within the same narrative.

A realistic travel narrative almost always mixes both auxiliary types in the same paragraph, because a trip naturally involves both movement (être) and other completed actions (avoir): eating, visiting, buying a ticket, taking a photo.

  • Je suis parti tôt, j'ai pris le train, et j'ai visité le Vieux-Port. I left early, I took the train, and I visited the Old Port. (être, avoir, avoir)
  • Nous sommes arrivés à midi et nous avons mangé dans un petit café. We arrived at noon and we ate in a small café. (être, avoir)

Before writing or saying a compound-past sentence, ask yourself: Is this verb in the approved être set, or is it a reflexive verb? If yes, use être and check subject agreement. If no, use avoir, as you learned in Chapter 16, and leave the participle unchanged.

17.5 Review: y and en in travel and quantity contexts

Purpose: refer back to a previously named place with y, or to a previously named quantity with en, without repeating the noun.

Y (Chapter 11) replaces a previously mentioned place introduced with à, en, dans, or a similar preposition. En (Chapters 8 and 10) replaces a previously mentioned quantity or a de-phrase. Both pronouns are placed directly before the conjugated verb — or, in the compound past, before the auxiliary.

  • Tu es allé à Marseille ? — Oui, j'y suis allé la semaine dernière. Did you go to Marseille? — Yes, I went there last week. (y = à Marseille)
  • Tu as des photos du voyage ? — Oui, j'en ai beaucoup. Do you have photos from the trip? — Yes, I have a lot of them. (en = des photos)

Model Dialogue

Contexte : Message vocal et réponse écrite entre Julien (Québec, Canada) et Fatou (Abidjan, Côte d'Ivoire), après le week-end de Julien à Marseille. Registre : conversation informelle, tu entre pairs.
Setting: A voice message and written reply between Julien (Quebec, Canada) and Fatou (Abidjan, Côte d'Ivoire), after Julien's weekend in Marseille. Register: informal conversation, tu between peers.

JULIEN Salut Fatou ! Alors, je suis allé à Marseille ce week-end ! Hi Fatou! So, I went to Marseille this weekend!
FATOU Super ! Raconte ! Tu es parti quand ? Great! Tell me! When did you leave?
JULIEN Je me suis réveillé très tôt vendredi. D'abord, je suis allé à la gare. Mon train est parti à sept heures. I woke up very early on Friday. First, I went to the station. My train left at seven o'clock.
FATOU Tu es arrivé directement, ou il y a eu une correspondance ? Did you arrive directly, or was there a connection?
JULIEN Il y a eu un petit problème ! Je suis descendu à Lyon pour ma correspondance, mais je me suis perdu dans la gare. Finalement, je suis monté dans le bon train. There was a small problem! I got off at Lyon for my connection, but I got lost in the station. Finally, I got on the right train.
FATOU Oh non ! Et après ? Oh no! And after that?
JULIEN Je suis arrivé à Marseille à midi. Mon amie Camille est venue me chercher à la gare. Nous sommes allés directement au Vieux-Port. I arrived in Marseille at noon. My friend Camille came to pick me up at the station. We went directly to the Old Port.
FATOU Tu es resté chez elle ? Did you stay at her place?
JULIEN Non, je suis resté dans une petite auberge de jeunesse près du port. Nous nous sommes bien amusés pendant le week-end ! No, I stayed in a small youth hostel near the port. We had a great time during the weekend!
FATOU Tu as des photos de la ville ? Do you have photos of the city?
JULIEN Oui, j'en ai beaucoup ! Et toi, tu es allée quelque part ce week-end ? Yes, I have a lot of them! And you, did you go somewhere this weekend?
FATOU Oui ! Je suis allée à Grand-Bassam avec ma sœur. Nous sommes parties samedi matin et nous sommes rentrées dimanche soir. C'était formidable. Yes! I went to Grand-Bassam with my sister. We left Saturday morning and we came home Sunday evening. It was wonderful.

Questions de compréhension — Comprehension questions:

  1. Comment Julien a-t-il voyagé jusqu'à Marseille ? (How did Julien travel to Marseille?)
  2. Quel problème Julien a-t-il eu pendant le voyage ? (What problem did Julien have during the trip?)
  3. Où Julien est-il resté à Marseille ? (Where did Julien stay in Marseille?)
  4. Où Fatou est-elle allée ce week-end, et avec qui ? (Where did Fatou go this weekend, and with whom?)
  5. Relevez trois verbes au passé composé avec être dans le dialogue et indiquez leur sujet. (Find three compound-past verbs with être in the dialogue and identify their subject.)
Grammar Note — Il y a eu in the dialogue

Fatou's question il y a eu une correspondance ? uses the compound past of the impersonal expression il y a (Chapter 6). Il y a eu is the compound-past form of il y a, and it always uses avoir as its auxiliary, since il y a is built on avoir, not être. Treat il y a eu as a useful fixed expression for narrating past events: il y a eu un problème (there was a problem).

Ask your instructor, a partner, or a text-to-speech tool to read the following travel account aloud at a moderate pace. Listen once for the general sequence of events. Then listen a second time to complete the task below.

Listening Note — Ce n'était pas grave

The phrase ce n'était pas grave (it wasn't serious / no big deal) uses the imperfect tense, which is introduced formally in Chapter 18. For now, treat it as a useful formulaic reassurance you may hear or use after describing a minor travel mishap. You will learn to form and analyze sentences like this one in the next chapter.

Reading Note — Travel within and beyond France

These three accounts come from Belgium, Tunisia, and Quebec, and describe travel to Portugal, France, and within Quebec itself. Travel writing in French is not limited to trips to or within France — Francophone travelers move throughout Europe, North Africa, North America, and beyond, just as travelers from any region do. La Gaspésie, mentioned in Léa and Noah's account, is a scenic peninsula in eastern Quebec well known for whale-watching and coastal landscapes. Tour Charles-de-Gaulle and the Paris metro system, mentioned in Karim's account, are commonly referenced landmarks for visitors to Paris, but Karim's own home and daily life are in Tunis, not in any French-speaking European country — a reminder that travel accounts should not be assumed to center on France by default.

Writing Workshop

In this chapter you will write a brief travel narrative recounting a real or imagined trip, using the compound past with both avoir and être.

  1. Study the models. Reread Amélie's or Karim's account above. Identify: (a) which verbs use être and which use avoir, (b) examples of participle agreement and why each form was chosen, (c) the sequence connectors used, and (d) at least one reflexive verb in the compound past.
  2. Plan your trip. Choose a real or imagined trip. Decide: where you went, how you traveled, what happened along the way (including at least one small problem, if you like), where you stayed, and how the trip ended. List the verbs you will need and check whether each takes avoir or être.
  3. Write your first draft. Begin with a sentence stating where you went and when. Narrate at least five connected events using sequence connectors (d'abord, ensuite, puis, finalement). Include at least three être-verbs with correct agreement, at least one reflexive verb in the compound past, and at least two avoir-verbs from Chapter 16.
  4. Revise. Use the editing checklist below before writing your final copy.

Liste de vérification — Editing checklist:

  • Every movement verb and reflexive verb uses être, not avoir.
  • Every être-verb past participle agrees in gender and number with its subject.
  • Every avoir-verb past participle is left unchanged, regardless of the subject.
  • Reflexive pronouns match the subject (je me, tu te, il/elle se, nous nous, vous vous, ils/elles se).
  • At least two sequence connectors organize the narrative.
  • All required accents are present on every word.
  • My narrative begins with a clear statement of the trip and ends naturally.

Speaking Practice

Pratique contrôlée — Controlled practice: Vrai voyage, faux voyage

With a partner, take turns telling a short three-sentence account of a trip — it can be true or invented. Use at least one être-verb and one avoir-verb. Your partner guesses whether the account is true or invented, then asks one follow-up question using être: Tu es allé(e) où ? Tu es parti(e) quand ?

Tâche communicative — Communicative task: Raconte ton week-end

With a partner, recount a recent or imagined weekend trip in detail. Cover departure, arrival, at least one activity, and return. Your partner listens and then summarizes your trip back to you in two sentences to check understanding, then asks at least one follow-up question. Switch roles.

Pour les apprenants autonomes — Solo alternative

Record yourself narrating a real or imagined trip in six to eight sentences. Include at least four être-verbs with correct agreement, one reflexive verb, and two avoir-verbs. Play back the recording and check: (a) Did you choose the correct auxiliary for each verb? (b) Did the participle agree correctly with the subject for every être-verb? (c) Did you use at least two sequence connectors?

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Travel patterns across the Francophone world

Travel habits, common destinations, and typical modes of transportation vary considerably across Francophone regions. In much of Europe, including France, Belgium, and Switzerland, train travel between cities is common and well developed, and many travelers cross national borders within a single short trip, as in Amélie's and Karim's accounts above. In Quebec and other parts of Canada, car travel over long distances is often more practical given the geography, and destinations like the Gaspé Peninsula reflect a different kind of travel culture centered on nature and road trips. In parts of West and North Africa, both domestic travel and international travel for business or family visits are common, and air travel often connects cities that are not easily reached by rail. No single travel pattern represents the whole Francophone world.

Culture in Context — Asking about someone's trip

When a friend or acquaintance returns from a trip, it is common and polite across most Francophone contexts to ask a few follow-up questions: where they went, how the trip was, and whether anything notable happened. Common conversational openers include Raconte ! (Tell me about it!) and Comment c'était ? (How was it?). As with many everyday exchanges, the level of detail expected can vary by relationship and context — a colleague may expect a brief, polite summary, while a close friend may expect a fuller account.

Regional Variation — Travel vocabulary

A few travel-related terms vary by region. In Quebec French, un chalet commonly refers to a vacation cottage, often near a lake or in the countryside, as in Léa and Noah's account — a usage less common in metropolitan France, where chalet more often evokes a mountain dwelling. In some African and Caribbean Francophone contexts, la gare routière (bus station) is a key travel hub, reflecting the importance of intercity bus travel where rail networks are less extensive. The productive vocabulary taught in this course — la gare, l'aéroport, l'hôtel, la valise — is understood throughout the Francophone world; regional terms are noted here for recognition.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using avoir for an être-verb

Error: *J'ai allé à Marseille. *Elle a partie hier.

Correction: Je suis allé(e) à Marseille. Elle est partie hier. Movement verbs and reflexive verbs use être, never avoir, in the compound past. Before forming a compound-past sentence, check whether the verb belongs to the approved être set from this chapter or Chapter 7's reflexive verbs.

Common Mistake — Forgetting subject agreement with être

Error: *Ma sœur est allé en Suisse. *Mes amies sont parti hier.

Correction: Ma sœur est allée en Suisse. Mes amies sont parties hier. With être-verbs, the past participle must agree with the subject: feminine singular adds -e; feminine plural adds -es. Always check the gender and number of the subject before finalizing the participle's spelling.

Common Mistake — Adding agreement to an avoir-verb participle

Error: *Elle a mangée une pomme. *Mes sœurs ont visitées Paris.

Correction: Elle a mangé une pomme. Mes sœurs ont visité Paris. Past participles after avoir do not agree with the subject — this rule, learned in Chapter 16, still applies. Subject agreement is specific to être-verbs; do not extend it to avoir-verbs.

Common Mistake — Placing the reflexive pronoun incorrectly

Error: *Je suis me réveillé. *Me je suis réveillé.

Correction: Je me suis réveillé. In the compound past, the reflexive pronoun comes immediately before the auxiliary être, not after it and not before the subject. The full order is: subject + reflexive pronoun + auxiliary + past participle.

Common Mistake — Mixing up aller and venir in narration

Error: Using venir to describe movement away from the speaker's own point of view, or vice versa.

Correction: Aller describes movement toward another place, away from the speaker's reference point: Je suis allé à Marseille. Venir describes movement toward the speaker's reference point: Camille est venue me chercher. (Camille came to get me — movement toward Julien.) Think about whose point of view the sentence is describing before choosing the verb.

Looking Ahead

Ce que vous savez maintenant — What you can do now

You can now recount a trip from start to finish: forming the compound past with être for movement and reflexive verbs, applying basic subject agreement to the past participle, and combining être-verbs with the avoir-verbs you learned in Chapter 16. Together with the directions, transportation, and community vocabulary from Unit III, you can describe not only where things are, but where you — and others — have actually been.

Chapitre 18 : Avant et maintenant

Chapter 18 introduces the imperfect tense, which you will use to describe past circumstances, background, repeated activities, and states such as age, time, and weather in the past. You will begin to contrast the imperfect with the compound past you have just learned — distinguishing a completed event from the background against which it happened. This is one of the most important contrasts in French narration, and the foundation you built in Chapters 16 and 17 will support it directly.

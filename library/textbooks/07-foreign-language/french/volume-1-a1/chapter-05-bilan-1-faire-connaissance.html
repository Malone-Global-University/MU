Faire connaissance

Getting to Know People · Speak and Write French: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Overview

Chapter 5 is the first unit assessment for Speak and Write French. Rather than introducing new grammar, it consolidates everything from Chapters 1–4 and gives you the opportunity to demonstrate what you can do with the foundations you have built. You will listen to a conversation, speak about yourself and others, read a personal profile, write a connected self-description, and edit a short text for accuracy. No new vocabulary, grammar, or pronunciation systems are required — only the tools already in your toolkit.

This chapter also gives you a chance to see the four skills — listening, speaking, reading, and writing — working together in a single communicative purpose: faire connaissance, getting to know people. By the end, you should feel confident using French to introduce yourself, ask about others, describe your family and background, and present this information in connected written form.

How to use this chapter

Work through the chapter from beginning to end. The review sections prepare you for the tasks; the tasks give you evidence of what you have learned. If you are working with an instructor, the speaking assessment may be conducted orally; if you are studying independently, use the recorded-response alternative. Complete the self-assessment at the end honestly — it will tell you what to review before moving into Unit II.

Assessment Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will demonstrate that you can:

  1. Greet and take leave appropriately, selecting tu or vous for the relationship.
  2. Introduce yourself and another person, giving name, origin, and language.
  3. Ask and answer basic questions about personal information using être, avoir, and core regular -er verbs.
  4. Identify classroom objects and describe them using definite and indefinite articles with correct gender and number.
  5. Use c'est, ce sont, and il/elle est correctly for identification and description.
  6. Apply possessive determiners (mon, ma, mes ; ton, ta, tes ; son, sa, ses) accurately.
  7. Describe family members using avoir for ages, adjectives with agreement, and occupations.
  8. Use country and city prepositions (à, en, au, aux, de, du, des, d') accurately.
  9. Understand a short recorded or read-aloud conversation between two speakers.
  10. Read and interpret a short personal or family profile.
  11. Write a connected paragraph about yourself or a family member, with correct accents, agreement, and punctuation.

Article selection — quick reference

Article typeMasc. sg.Fém. sg.Before vowelPluralWhen to use
Definitelelal'lesSpecific, known, or general category: le français, les familles
Indefiniteununeun / unedesOne unspecified person or thing: un frère, une ville, des amis

After c'est, use an indefinite article before an occupation in a noun phrase: C'est un professeur. When using il/elle est, no article before an occupation: Il est professeur.

Questions — three strategies

  • Intonation: Tu parles français ? Rising pitch at the end — most common in conversation
  • Est-ce que : Est-ce que tu parles français ? Neutral, reliable in all registers
  • Information word + est-ce que : D'où est-ce que tu viens ? Add a question word at the front for information questions

Inversion (Parles-tu français ?) has been introduced for recognition and in fixed formulas. It is not required in productive writing or speaking at this stage.

Pronunciation and Spelling Review

Sound Review — Key contrasts from Chapters 1–4
  • /y/ versus /u/ — tu [y] versus vous [u]: round your lips tightly for /y/, then try to say /i/ without unrounding. If you say /u/, your tongue is too far back.
  • Nasal vowels — mon, grand, cousin, cinquante: the vowel is nasal; do not add a separate [n] or [m] sound after it.
  • Silent endings — the -ent ending on third-person plural verbs (ils parlent, elles habitent) is completely silent; ils parlent sounds the same as il parle.
  • Elision — je → j', le/la → l', de → d' before a vowel sound: this is always required in standard written French.
  • Liaison — mon ami (pronounced mo-na-mi), nous avons (nou-za-von): the normally silent final consonant is pronounced before a vowel-initial word.
Spelling Checklist — Frequent errors to avoid
  • Accents are required: é, è, ê, à, ù, â, î, ô, û, ë, ï — never omit them, including on capital letters (É, À).
  • Cedilla: ç in français, garçon — required in writing.
  • Ligature: œ in sœur, cœur — not two separate letters oe.
  • Apostrophe in elision: j'ai, l'ami, d'où, qu'est-ce que — required in standard spelling.
  • Nationality adjectives in lowercase: un étudiant français but un Français (demonym as noun).
  • Days and months in lowercase: lundi, janvier.
  • Number hyphens: quarante-deux but quarante et un — never a hyphen before un/une when preceded by et.

Listening Assessment

Assessment Task 1

Une rencontre à Genève — A meeting in Geneva

Listening Comprehension

Ask your instructor or a partner to read the listening script aloud at a moderate pace. If you are studying independently, read the script yourself once at natural speed, then set it aside and complete the tasks from memory. Listen twice: once for general meaning, once to identify specific information.

Script — Une rencontre à Genève

— Bonjour ! Je m'appelle Yasmine. Et vous ?
— Bonjour, Yasmine. Je m'appelle Éric. Enchanté.
— Vous êtes français, Éric ?
— Non, je suis belge — je suis de Bruxelles. Et vous, d'où venez-vous ?
— Je viens du Maroc. De Casablanca. Mais j'habite à Genève maintenant. Je travaille ici.
— Ah, intéressant ! Qu'est-ce que vous faites ?
— Je suis infirmière à l'hôpital cantonal. Et vous, Éric, qu'est-ce que vous faites ?
— Je suis professeur de mathématiques. J'enseigne dans un lycée ici à Genève.
— Vous avez de la famille ici ?
— Oui, j'ai ma femme et nos deux fils. Mes fils ont douze et quinze ans. Et vous, Yasmine ?
— Moi, j'ai un frère ici — il s'appelle Karim. Il a vingt-huit ans. Il est ingénieur. Mes parents habitent encore à Casablanca.

Translation: — Hello! My name is Yasmine. And you? — Hello, Yasmine. My name is Éric. Pleased to meet you. — Are you French, Éric? — No, I'm Belgian — I'm from Brussels. And you, where are you from? — I'm from Morocco. From Casablanca. But I live in Geneva now. I work here. — Ah, interesting! What do you do? — I'm a nurse at the cantonal hospital. And you, Éric, what do you do? — I'm a maths teacher. I teach at a secondary school here in Geneva. — Do you have family here? — Yes, I have my wife and our two sons. My sons are twelve and fifteen. And you, Yasmine? — Me, I have a brother here — his name is Karim. He is twenty-eight. He's an engineer. My parents still live in Casablanca.

Expression orale — Speaking Assessment

Speaking Assessment

Tâche 1 — Introduction personnelle (Controlled)

Without notes, say six to eight sentences introducing yourself in French. Include: your name, where you are from, where you live now, a language you speak or study, your occupation or student status, and one personal characteristic. Use complete sentences with correct subject pronouns. Use être, avoir, and at least one regular -er verb.

Example opening: Bonjour, je m'appelle ___. Je suis de ___. J'habite à ___. Je parle ___ et j'étudie le français…

Tâche 2 — Questions et réponses (Interactive)

With a partner or instructor, conduct a three-to-four minute conversation covering the topics below. Each person should ask at least two questions and give full-sentence answers. If studying alone, record both parts of the conversation.

  • Greet appropriately and ask the other person's name
  • Ask and answer where they are from and where they live
  • Ask and answer about languages spoken
  • Ask and answer about family (at least two members)
  • Give a polite leave-taking

Tâche 3 — Présentation de famille (Extended)

Using your family description from Chapter 4 (or a new one), present two or three family members aloud. For each person: use a possessive determiner, give age with avoir, and use at least one descriptive adjective with correct agreement. Use c'est to introduce and il/elle est for at least one description. Speak for approximately ninety seconds without reading directly from notes.

Solo alternative — Enregistrement (Recorded)

Record yourself completing all three speaking tasks above. Play back the recording and complete the self-assessment checklist in the final section of this chapter. If you notice an error during playback, note it: self-correction is a valuable metacognitive skill and a sign of developing accuracy.

Assessment Task 2

Un profil personnel

Reading Comprehension Language Analysis

Read the following profile carefully. You may read it twice before answering the questions. Do not use a dictionary for the comprehension questions — work from context.

Profil — Amara Koné

Bonjour ! Je m'appelle Amara Koné. J'ai vingt-neuf ans. Je suis ivoirien — je suis originaire d'Abidjan, en Côte d'Ivoire. Maintenant j'habite à Paris, dans le dix-huitième arrondissement. Je parle français, bien sûr, et aussi le dioula — c'est une langue très importante en Afrique de l'Ouest. J'étudie aussi l'anglais. C'est utile pour mon travail !

Je suis graphiste. Je travaille pour une petite agence de communication. C'est un travail intéressant et varié. Mes collègues sont sympas — nous sommes une équipe de six personnes.

Ma famille ? J'ai une grande famille à Abidjan. Mon père s'appelle Moussa. Il a soixante et un ans. Il est retraité — il était professeur. Ma mère s'appelle Awa. Elle a cinquante-huit ans. Elle est commerçante. J'ai aussi trois sœurs et un frère. Ma grande sœur, Fatoumata, a trente-deux ans. Elle est avocate. Mon frère, Seydou, a vingt-cinq ans. Il est étudiant en médecine à Abidjan. Mes deux petites sœurs ont seize et quatorze ans. Elles sont au lycée.

Ici à Paris, j'ai un ami très sympa — il s'appelle Lucas. Il est français. Il a trente ans. Il est musicien. Nous habitons dans le même quartier et nous parlons souvent ensemble le soir.

Translation: Hello! My name is Amara Koné. I am twenty-nine years old. I am Ivorian — I am originally from Abidjan, in Côte d'Ivoire. Now I live in Paris, in the eighteenth arrondissement. I speak French, of course, and also Dioula — it is a very important language in West Africa. I am also studying English. It's useful for my work! I am a graphic designer. I work for a small communications agency. It's an interesting and varied job. My colleagues are pleasant — we are a team of six people. My family? I have a large family in Abidjan. My father's name is Moussa. He is sixty-one years old. He is retired — he was a teacher. My mother's name is Awa. She is fifty-eight. She is a shopkeeper. I also have three sisters and one brother. My older sister, Fatoumata, is thirty-two. She is a lawyer. My brother, Seydou, is twenty-five. He is a medical student in Abidjan. My two younger sisters are sixteen and fourteen. They are in secondary school. Here in Paris, I have a very pleasant friend — his name is Lucas. He is French. He is thirty. He is a musician. We live in the same neighbourhood and often talk together in the evening.

Reading Task A — Main ideas

  1. Where is Amara originally from, and where does he live now?
  2. What languages does Amara speak or study? Name all three.
  3. What is Amara's profession?
  4. How many siblings does Amara have? Name each one and give one detail about them.
  5. Who is Lucas, and where does he live?

Reading Task B — Language analysis

Answer these questions about the language used in the text.

  1. Find three examples of a possessive determiner followed by a family noun. Write the complete phrase and identify whether the possessive is mon/ma/mes, ton/ta/tes, or son/sa/ses.
  2. Find two examples where il/elle est is used to describe occupation. Copy each sentence. Why is there no article before the occupation word?
  3. Find the sentence J'ai une grande famille à Abidjan. Explain the agreement of the adjective grande.
  4. The text says Mes deux petites sœurs ont seize et quatorze ans. Write out the number words for 16 and 14.
  5. The text mentions il était professeur (he was a teacher). This is a past-tense form you have not yet studied. Based on context, what do you think it means? How is it different from il est professeur?

Reading Task C — Beyond the text

  1. The profile mentions that Dioula is "une langue très importante en Afrique de l'Ouest." What does this suggest about the linguistic situation in Côte d'Ivoire? Is French the only language used there?
  2. Amara describes his work as intéressant et varié. Both adjectives follow the noun travail. What gender is travail, and how do you know from the adjective forms?
Expression écrite — Writing Assessment

Writing Assessment

Assessment Task 3

Mon profil personnel — My personal profile

Writing Grammar in context Spelling and agreement

Write a personal profile of 120–150 words modeled on Amara's profile above. Use your own information or invent a plausible fictional person. Your profile must include all the required elements listed below.

  1. Plan before you write. Decide who you are writing about. Jot down: name, age, origin, place of residence, language(s), occupation, and at least two family members with their relationship, age, and one characteristic each.
  2. Write your draft. Organize your profile into three short paragraphs: (1) personal information and languages; (2) work or studies; (3) family. Use the transitional phrase Ma famille ? or Et ma famille… to open the third paragraph, as Amara does.
  3. Check your draft against the requirements below.
  4. Write your final version. Pay particular attention to accents, apostrophes, and agreement endings.

Required elements — your profile must include:

  • Your name, age (using avoir), and country of origin (using correct country preposition)
  • The city where you live now (using à)
  • At least one language statement using parler
  • An occupation or student status using il/elle est or je suis
  • At least two family members with possessive determiners, ages (avoir), and one descriptive adjective each (with correct agreement)
  • At least one use of c'est for identification
  • Complete standard negation if a negative statement is included
  • Correct accents, apostrophes, and capitalization throughout

Révision — Editing checklist:

  • Every noun has an article or possessive determiner.
  • Every adjective agrees in gender and number with the noun it modifies.
  • I used avoir — not être — for every age statement.
  • I used c'est before article + noun phrases, and il/elle est before bare adjectives or occupations.
  • My possessive determiners agree with the noun that follows (mon before vowel-initial nouns, even feminine ones).
  • All required accents and the cedilla are present.
  • Apostrophes are correctly placed in elision (j'ai, l'ami, d'Abidjan).
  • Nationality adjectives are lowercase; demonyms used as nouns are capitalized.
  • Subject pronouns are present in every finite clause.

Performance Task

Tâche de performance — Bilan 1

Rencontre francophone — Meeting in the Francophone world

This task integrates all four skills and represents the capstone of Unit I. It should take approximately twenty to thirty minutes.

Situation: You are attending a French-language conversation group at a cultural centre in your city. You meet a new participant for the first time. Your instructor or partner will play the role of the new participant. If you are studying independently, write both sides of the exchange and then record yourself speaking your own part.

Your conversation must include:

  • An appropriate greeting and the choice of tu or vous (with explanation of your choice in your self-assessment)
  • An exchange of names
  • Questions and answers about origin and current place of residence (using country and city prepositions)
  • A mention of at least one language you speak or study
  • A brief description of your occupation or studies
  • An exchange about family: at least two members, ages, occupations, and characteristics, using correct possessives and adjective agreement
  • A polite leave-taking

Written component: After your spoken exchange, write a short summary (80–100 words) of what you learned about your conversation partner. Use the third person (il/elle). Include at least one sentence using c'est and one using il/elle est.

Reading Task B

  1. Examples: mon père (son/sa/ses → mon, masculine noun), ma mère (mon/ma/mes → ma, feminine noun), mes deux petites sœurs (mes, plural), mon frère (mon, masculine). Any three correct examples with identification accepted.
  2. Il est retraité. and Elle est commerçante. and Il est ingénieur. and Il est musicien. — any two. No article is used because in the il/elle est + occupation pattern, the occupation functions descriptively rather than categorically; it is treated more like an adjective than a noun.
  3. Grande agrees with famille, which is feminine singular. The masculine form would be grand; adding -e gives grande and also makes the final -d pronounced.
  4. 16 = seize; 14 = quatorze.
  5. Il était professeur means "he was a teacher" — it refers to a state or role in the past. It is different from il est professeur (he is a teacher) because était places the role in the past; the father is now retired (retraité). (This is a recognition item; the imperfect is taught in Chapter 18.)

Reading Task C

  1. The mention of Dioula as "une langue très importante en Afrique de l'Ouest" implies that Côte d'Ivoire is a multilingual country and that French is not the only language spoken there. Many people are multilingual, using both French and one or more local languages in daily life.
  2. Travail is masculine. We can tell because both adjectives — intéressant and varié — appear in their masculine singular forms (no added -e for feminine).

Language Control — Partie F

  1. *Ma amie → Mon amie s'appelle Sophie. (Vowel-initial noun → mon not ma)
  2. *Je suis vingt-cinq ans → J'ai vingt-cinq ans. (Age uses avoir, not être)
  3. *Il est un médecin → C'est un médecin très compétent. (Article + noun → c'est)
  4. *Mes sœur → Mes sœurs sont intelligentes. (Plural determiner requires plural noun)
  5. *tres → très gentil. (Missing grave accent on è)
  6. *A trente ans → Il/Elle a trente ans. (Subject pronoun required in French)

Looking Ahead

Ce que vous savez faire — What you can do

After completing Unit I, you can greet, introduce, and describe people; ask and answer basic personal questions; identify and describe objects; present your family; and write a connected personal paragraph with correct articles, agreement, and forms of address. These are real communicative abilities. You are no longer a complete beginner.

Unité II — La vie quotidienne

Unit II moves from people to places, routines, and daily life. Chapter 6 (Chez moi et dans mon quartier) introduces il y a (there is / there are), location prepositions, contractions (au, aux, du, des), and demonstrative determiners (ce, cet, cette, ces) — the tools for describing where things are. Chapter 7 expands your verb repertoire to include reflexive verbs and more regular patterns, and introduces negation and time expressions. Chapter 8 brings the partitive article and food language. Chapter 9 develops adjective placement and introduces direct-object pronouns. Everything from Unit I — especially articles, gender, être, avoir, and adjective agreement — will continue to appear in every chapter. Keep it active.

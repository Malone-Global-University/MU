Projet final : Mon monde en français

Integrated CEFR A2 Performance Assessment · Speak and Write French: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Demonstrate integrated communicative ability across all four skills at the CEFR A2 level.
  2. Greet, introduce yourself, and manage an exchange with appropriate forms of address.
  3. Describe your personal world — your identity, home, routine, work or study, and relationships.
  4. Communicate about completed past events using the compound past with avoir and être.
  5. Set background and context using the imperfect, and distinguish it from the compound past in a short account.
  6. Express future intentions using aller + infinitive and discuss plans and invitations.
  7. Use recent past with venir de + infinitive where appropriate.
  8. Apply core articles, adjective agreement, pronouns, prepositions, and connectors with functional consistency.
  9. Understand a short A2-appropriate recording in a supported context.
  10. Read a practical or narrative text and demonstrate comprehension of main ideas, details, and reference.
  11. Write a connected 150–200 word integrated text or linked set of texts across multiple domains.
  12. Produce a completed Mon monde en français portfolio containing evidence of your learning journey.

Communication Scenario

Imagine you have just finished a language course and you want to show what you can do in French. You decide to put together a small portfolio — Mon monde en français — that captures your ability to communicate about your real life: who you are, where you live, what your days look like, what you have done recently, what your circumstances were in the past, and what you plan to do next. This chapter guides you through four final assessed tasks and then through assembling the portfolio itself.

  1. Look back over the chapters you have completed. What communicative domains have you studied? Make a quick mental list: greetings, home, routines, food, shopping, directions, leisure, health, school, work, past events, travel, description of the past, storytelling. These are all yours now. This chapter is your opportunity to use them together.
  2. At CEFR A2, the standard is not perfection. The standard is: Can you communicate clearly enough to be understood? Can you handle familiar, predictable situations? Can you connect your sentences into something coherent? That is the measure of success here.

Grammar Consolidation

Before you begin the assessed tasks, work through the exercises below. They revisit the most important grammar systems from Chapters 1–19 and highlight the connections between them. This is not new instruction — it is a final structured review to sharpen your control before you demonstrate what you know.

20.1 Articles and determiners — a quick audit

Purpose: confirm that the article system is fully integrated, including after negation and with quantity expressions.

Language Focus — The French article system in summary

French requires a determiner in virtually every ordinary noun phrase. The choice among le / la / l' / les (definite), un / une / des (indefinite), and du / de la / de l' (partitive) is not interchangeable — each signals a different relationship between the speaker and the noun. After negation and after a quantity expression, the indefinite and partitive articles reduce to de (or d' before a vowel): Je n'ai pas de café. Il y a beaucoup de monde. The definite article does not change after negation when it accompanies a specific, identified noun: Je n'ai pas vu le film.

Exercice 20.1 — Article selection. Choose the correct article or partitive form for each blank.

  1. Est-ce qu'il y a _____ café dans cette ville ? (some coffee / a coffee shop)
  2. J'ai acheté _____ baguette au marché. (a baguette)
  3. Elle n'a pas pris _____ bus ce matin. (the bus — specific, identified)
  4. Nous ne mangeons pas _____ viande. (meat — partitive after negation)
  5. Il y a _____ beau jardin derrière la maison. (a beautiful garden)
  6. J'ai bu _____ eau et _____ café. (some water, some coffee)

20.2 Verb tenses — choosing the right form

Purpose: select among present, near future, compound past, and imperfect according to meaning and time frame.

Exercice 20.2 — Tense selection. Read each context and write the verb in the most appropriate tense. Use the form indicated in parentheses when it is required for the context.

  1. Right now, Léa is working. → En ce moment, Léa _____ (travailler).
  2. Last Tuesday, she finished a report. → Mardi dernier, elle _____ un rapport. (finir)
  3. When she was a child, she often visited her grandmother. → Quand elle était enfant, elle _____ souvent sa grand-mère. (visiter)
  4. Tomorrow, we are going to take the train. → Demain, nous _____ le train. (prendre — near future)
  5. He has just arrived. → Il _____ d'arriver. (venir de)
  6. They were talking while the phone rang. — Ils _____ quand le téléphone a sonné. (parler — background)
  7. I went to Dakar last year. → L'année dernière, je _____ à Dakar. (aller — compound past with être)
  8. She got up early and left immediately. → Elle _____ tôt et _____ tout de suite. (se lever, partir — compound past with être)

20.3 Pronouns — placement and selection

Purpose: place single object pronouns correctly and distinguish direct from indirect object functions.

Grammar Note — Single-pronoun placement: the consistent rule

In every structure you have learned, the single object pronoun is placed immediately before the verb it belongs to semantically. Before a finite verb: Je le vois. Il lui téléphone. Before an infinitive governed by a modal or auxiliary: Je veux le voir. Elle peut lui écrire. In the compound past: Je l'ai vu. (Note: this last example, with a preceding direct-object pronoun in the compound past, is given here for recognition. Your own writing tasks in this chapter may use full noun objects to avoid the triggered participle-agreement rule beyond this course's productive scope.)

Exercice 20.3 — Pronoun replacement. Replace the underlined element with the appropriate pronoun and rewrite the sentence.

  1. J'ai répondu à la question. → J'_____ ai répondu.
  2. Elle téléphone souvent à ses amis. → Elle _____ téléphone souvent.
  3. Nous allons visiter le musée demain. → Nous allons _____ visiter demain.
  4. Il y a beaucoup de travail — j'ai déjà fait une partie du travail. → J'_____ ai déjà fait une partie.
  5. Tu vas à Paris la semaine prochaine. → Tu _____ vas la semaine prochaine.

20.4 Questions, negation, and complex sentences

Exercice 20.4 — Mixed sentence forms. Rewrite each sentence as directed, changing only what is asked.

  1. Tu as visité la Belgique. → (est-ce que question) _____
  2. Elle finit son travail. → (ne … jamais) _____
  3. Nous avons trouvé un appartement. → (ne … pas encore) _____
  4. Il est parti tôt. → (Pourquoi est-ce que…) _____
  5. C'est le quartier. + J'ai grandi dans ce quartier. → (relative clause with où) _____
  6. Je connais un professeur. + Ce professeur parle six langues. → (relative clause with qui) _____

20.5 c'est versus il/elle est

Exercice 20.5 — c'est or il/elle est. Choose the correct form and complete each sentence.

  1. Mon professeur préféré ? _____ M. Diallo. _____ très patient.
  2. Quelle est cette ville ? _____ Montréal. _____ une ville magnifique.
  3. Et cette femme, qui est-ce ? _____ ma cousine. _____ architecte.
  4. Ce livre, _____ intéressant. _____ le roman que j'ai lu le mois dernier.

Pronunciation and Spelling — Final Review

This section consolidates the pronunciation and orthographic systems that appear across the final portfolio and assessed tasks. It is not new instruction but a targeted reminder of the features most likely to affect clarity and accuracy at this stage.

Sound Review — Connected speech in extended discourse

At A2, you are moving from isolated sentences to connected speech in short accounts. Three features of connected French become especially important when you narrate past events and describe background circumstances:

  • Phrase-final prominence: French prominence falls on the last syllable of a rhythmic group, not on the "most important" content word as in English. In a narrative, each clause tends to receive its own rhythmic arc. Practice grouping by sense, not word by word.
  • Auxiliary–participle grouping: j'ai travaillé, elle est partie, nous nous sommes levés — the whole compound-past unit moves together rhythmically. The auxiliary is not separately stressed.
  • Imperfect versus compound-past rhythm: Imperfect forms like je travaillais [tʁavajɛ] are longer than the present je travaille and distinct from the compound past j'ai travaillé. Train your ear to hear the rhythm difference before reading aloud.
Orthography Review — The most common written errors at A2

Before submitting any written work in this chapter, check for the following. These are the errors most consistently seen in A2-level writing in French:

  • Missing or incorrect accents: é, è, ê, à, ù, â, ô, î, û, ï, ç, œ. Accents on uppercase letters are required: É, À, Ç.
  • Apostrophe in elision: l'appartement, j'ai, n'a pas, qu'il. Never write *je ai or *le ami.
  • Imperfect endings confused in writing: -ais, -ait, -aient all sound the same [ɛ] but must be spelled to match the subject. Je travaillais versus elle travaillait versus elles travaillaient.
  • Participle agreement with être: il est parti / elle est partie / ils sont partis / elles sont parties. Do not apply agreement to participles used with avoir in basic sentences.
  • Complete written negation: Learner writing at this level retains both ne and pas in all standard forms. Do not omit ne in written tasks.
  • Days, months, languages, and nationalities: All remain in lowercase: le lundi, en août, le français, une étudiante belge.

Final Listening Assessment

Ask your instructor, a study partner, or a text-to-speech tool to read the following recording aloud at a moderate, natural pace. Listen once for general meaning. Listen a second time to complete the tasks. Do not read the transcript during the first two listens. The transcript is provided for the answer-key section only.

Listening Context

You are going to hear a short conversation between two people: Yasmine, who lives in Brussels, and Kofi, who is visiting from Abidjan, Côte d'Ivoire. They are speaking at a café after a community event. You will hear both speakers use the same standard French, but their backgrounds and references reflect different parts of the Francophone world.

Tâche 1 — Première écoute : compréhension globale (First listen: global comprehension). Answer only these two questions after the first listen.

  1. Where does Kofi live? Where is he visiting?
  2. What two things did they both do at the community event?

Transcription (for instructor or post-listening use only)

YASMINE : Kofi, c'était vraiment une belle soirée ! Tu as aimé ?

KOFI : Oui, beaucoup ! J'ai adoré la musique. Et les gens étaient tellement accueillants. C'est ma première visite à Bruxelles, alors je ne connaissais pas du tout la communauté ici.

YASMINE : Moi, je suis venue à cette association il y a deux ans. Au début, je ne parlais pas beaucoup, mais maintenant, je connais tout le monde.

KOFI : Tu es arrivée à Bruxelles quand ?

YASMINE : Il y a trois ans. Je travaillais dans le secteur culturel à Dakar avant, mais j'ai eu une opportunité ici. Et toi, tu restes combien de temps ?

KOFI : Jusqu'à vendredi. Demain, je vais visiter le centre-ville. Et après-demain, j'ai une réunion avec des collègues. Mais ce soir, je suis vraiment content d'être là.

YASMINE : On vient de lancer un nouveau projet culturel — tu devrais venir voir l'exposition avant de partir !

KOFI : Avec plaisir ! Vous avez fait ça quand ?

YASMINE : On a tout préparé le mois dernier. L'exposition est ouverte jusqu'à la fin du mois. Je peux t'envoyer le lien.

KOFI : Oui, s'il te plaît. Tu connais mon adresse e-mail ?

YASMINE : Non, pas encore. Écris-la moi ici. (Elle lui donne son téléphone.)

KOFI : Voilà. Merci, Yasmine. C'était une très bonne soirée.

Tâche 2 — Deuxième écoute : compréhension détaillée (Second listen: detailed comprehension). Answer the following questions in complete sentences in French after the second listen.

  1. Est-ce que Kofi connaissait la communauté à Bruxelles avant cette soirée ? Comment le savez-vous ? (Did Kofi know the Bruxelles community before? How do you know?)
  2. Depuis combien de temps Yasmine est-elle à Bruxelles ? (How long has Yasmine been in Brussels?)
  3. Qu'est-ce que Yasmine faisait avant d'arriver à Bruxelles ? (What did Yasmine do before coming to Brussels?)
  4. Qu'est-ce que Kofi va faire demain ? Et après-demain ? (What is Kofi going to do tomorrow? And the day after?)
  5. Qu'est-ce que l'association vient de lancer ? (What has the association just launched?)
  6. Relevez une phrase à l'imparfait dans la transcription et expliquez pourquoi l'imparfait est utilisé. (Find one sentence in the imperfect and explain why the imperfect is used.)

Tâche 3 — Réflexion sur la langue (Language reflection). Without looking at the transcript, write two sentences summarizing what you understood about Yasmine and Kofi's situation, in French.

Final Reading Assessment

Read the following text carefully. It is a connected A2-level personal account written by a Francophone speaker from Martinique. Read it through once for general meaning, then complete the comprehension tasks.

Ma vie entre deux îles — Sandrine, Fort-de-France, Martinique

Je m'appelle Sandrine, j'ai trente-quatre ans, et j'habite à Fort-de-France depuis ma naissance — enfin, presque. Quand j'avais vingt ans, je suis partie étudier à Lyon pendant quatre ans. C'était une expérience formidable, mais difficile au début. Je ne connaissais personne, il faisait froid, et la nourriture était très différente de celle de chez moi !

En France, j'ai étudié la gestion et j'ai travaillé à temps partiel dans un hôtel. J'ai aussi appris à cuisiner des plats créoles pour mes amis qui ne connaissaient pas la cuisine martiniquaise — ils adoraient ça. Après mes études, j'ai décidé de rentrer en Martinique parce que ma famille me manquait et parce que je voulais travailler ici.

Maintenant, je dirige une petite agence de tourisme culturel. On organise des visites pour des groupes qui veulent découvrir la culture et l'histoire de l'île — ce n'est pas du tourisme ordinaire. Ce que j'aime dans mon travail, c'est que je rencontre des gens du monde entier qui parlent français : des Québécois, des Belges, des Sénégalais, des Suisses. On parle tous la même langue mais on vient de partout.

Le week-end dernier, j'ai organisé une visite pour un groupe de dix-sept personnes. On a visité un jardin botanique et on a déjeuné dans un restaurant familial qui propose des plats traditionnels. D'abord, j'étais un peu inquiète parce qu'il pleuvait, mais finalement, tout le monde a eu une excellente journée. Un des participants a dit que c'était « le meilleur repas de sa vie » — ça m'a vraiment fait plaisir !

L'année prochaine, je vais développer de nouveaux itinéraires et je vais embaucher une deuxième guide. Je suis très heureuse ici, même si Paris me manque un peu parfois.

Questions de compréhension — Comprehension questions:

  1. Où est-ce que Sandrine est née, et où est-ce qu'elle habite maintenant ? (Where was Sandrine born, and where does she live now?)
  2. Pourquoi est-ce que le début de son séjour à Lyon a été difficile ? Donnez deux raisons. (Why was the beginning of her stay in Lyon difficult? Give two reasons.)
  3. Qu'est-ce qu'elle a fait pendant ses études, en dehors de ses cours ? (What did she do during her studies, outside her classes?)
  4. Pourquoi est-ce qu'elle a décidé de rentrer en Martinique ? (Why did she decide to return to Martinique?)
  5. Qu'est-ce qui est spécial dans son agence de tourisme ? (What is special about her tourism agency?)
  6. Racontez brièvement ce qui s'est passé le week-end dernier, d'après le texte. (Briefly describe what happened last weekend, according to the text.)
  7. Relevez dans le texte : (a) un verbe au passé composé avec être, (b) deux verbes à l'imparfait, (c) un verbe au futur proche. (Find: (a) one compound-past verb with être, (b) two imperfect verbs, (c) one near-future verb.)
  8. Sandrine dit : « On parle tous la même langue mais on vient de partout. » Qu'est-ce que cette phrase révèle sur sa vision du français ? Répondez en une ou deux phrases en français. (What does this sentence reveal about her view of French? Answer in one or two sentences.)
Reading Note — La Martinique and the wider Francophone world

Martinique is an island in the Caribbean and an overseas region of France. French is the official language and the primary language of education, administration, and public life. Antillean Creole is also spoken by much of the population and is an important part of the cultural identity of Martinique and other Caribbean islands. Sandrine's agency serves Francophone visitors from across the world, illustrating that French functions as a language of connection across communities with very different histories, geographies, and cultures — exactly as you have seen throughout this course.

Final Writing Assessment

This task requires you to write a connected, integrated text of 150–200 words. It must draw from at least four of the following five domains. You may write about your real life or construct a plausible personal account.

Tâche d'écriture finale

Votre monde en français — Your world in French

Write a connected text of 150–200 words that includes content from at least four of the five domains below. Your text should read as a coherent whole — not as a list of unconnected facts.

  • Domaine 1 — Identité et vie quotidienne : Who you are, where you live, and what your daily life looks like now, including at least one reflexive verb and one routine activity.
  • Domaine 2 — Une expérience passée : A completed event from the recent or less recent past, using the compound past (with avoir and/or être), and with at least one irregular past participle.
  • Domaine 3 — Le contexte ou les habitudes d'avant : A past description or habitual situation, using the imperfect to contrast with or provide background for Domaine 2.
  • Domaine 4 — Un texte pratique : A reference to food, shopping, health, or another practical domain from Unit II or III (for example, what you typically eat, a health situation, or a purchase you made).
  • Domaine 5 — Un projet ou une intention : What you are going to do in the near future, using aller + infinitive or venir de + infinitive for a very recent development.

Liste de vérification — Editing checklist:

  • The text is 150–200 words and covers at least four domains.
  • At least two different tenses or time frames are present.
  • At least one irregular past participle is spelled correctly.
  • At least one sentence uses the imperfect for description or habitual past.
  • At least one negative sentence uses complete negation with ne … pas (or another negative expression).
  • Articles, adjective agreements, and subject-verb agreements are checked throughout.
  • All required accents and apostrophes are present.
  • Sentences are connected by at least two cohesive devices (connectors, pronouns replacing nouns, relative clauses).
  • Register is consistent (standard written French throughout).
  • Days, months, and nationality adjectives are in lowercase.
Writing Note — Cohesion at A2

One of the defining features of A2 writing is the ability to connect sentences into something more than a list. You have practiced several cohesion tools throughout this course: chronological connectors (d'abord, ensuite, enfin), cause connectors (parce que, donc), contrast connectors (mais, cependant), relative clauses (qui, que, où), and object pronouns that replace a repeated noun. Using even two or three of these tools across a 150-word text makes a real difference to the overall quality and readability of your writing.

Final Speaking Assessment

The final speaking assessment has three parts. Parts 1 and 2 may be completed with a partner, instructor, or by recording yourself. Part 3 requires an exchange — either live with a partner, or by leaving a recorded message for someone to respond to, then responding to their message in turn.

Partie 1 — Présentation personnelle (Individual presentation, 2–3 minutes)

Without notes, give a 2–3 minute account of your personal world in French. You must cover:

  1. Who you are: where you are from, where you live, your work or studies.
  2. Something about your daily or weekly routine, including at least one reflexive verb.
  3. A completed past event from the recent past, using the compound past with at least two verbs.
  4. A background description or habitual past action related to the same event, using the imperfect.
  5. One future intention using aller + infinitive.

Solo alternative: Record yourself speaking for 2–3 minutes without stopping. Then listen back and mark any forms that need correction. This gives you the opportunity to self-assess before submitting.

Partie 2 — Questions et réponses spontanées (Spontaneous questions, ~2 minutes)

A partner or instructor asks you five questions drawn from anywhere in the course. You must answer each one in at least two complete sentences without preparation time. Sample questions you might be asked:

  • Qu'est-ce que tu as fait le week-end dernier ?
  • Comment était ta ville natale quand tu étais enfant ?
  • Qu'est-ce que tu vas faire pendant les prochaines vacances ?
  • Est-ce que tu préfères habiter en ville ou à la campagne ? Pourquoi ?
  • Décris ta journée typique.
  • Est-ce que tu as déjà visité un pays francophone ? Si oui, raconte. Si non, lequel voudrais-tu visiter ?
  • Qu'est-ce que tu viens de faire aujourd'hui ?

Solo alternative: Write out five of the above questions on separate cards. Pick them one at a time without looking ahead and record an answer for each.

Partie 3 — Échange communicatif (Partner conversation, ~3 minutes)

With a partner (or using a recorded exchange), hold a natural conversation that includes at least three of the following moves. You may decide together which scenario to use — a café meeting after a trip, a phone call after a long absence, or a conversation at a community event:

  • Greet each other and ask how the other person is doing.
  • Ask and answer about a past event: Qu'est-ce que tu as fait… ?
  • Ask for a description of a past period or circumstance: Comment c'était… ?
  • Discuss plans: Qu'est-ce que tu vas faire… ?
  • Invite the other person to do something, accept or decline.
  • Use at least one follow-up question to show you are listening: Vraiment ? Et après ?
  • Close the conversation naturally.

Portfolio Project — Mon monde en français

Projet de portfolio

Mon monde en français

Your portfolio is the capstone of this course. It is a small collection of connected texts that together demonstrate your ability to communicate about your real world in French at the A2 level. Each component has a specific requirement, but the whole portfolio should feel like a portrait of one real person — you, or someone whose world you know well.

Component 1 — Mon profil personnel (My personal profile)

Length: 60–80 words. Tense: Present.

Write a personal profile as if for an international French-language community website. Include: your name and age (or a plausible version), where you are from and where you live now, what you do (work or study), at least one language other than English that you speak or are learning, and one personal preference or characteristic described with an adjective agreement. End with one sentence about your connection to French or the Francophone world.

Model opening (do not copy): Je m'appelle Amara, j'ai vingt-huit ans et j'habite à Montréal depuis trois ans. Je suis originaire du Mali…

Component 2 — Un texte pratique (A practical text)

Length: 50–70 words. Tense: Present and/or near future.

Write one of the following: (a) a description of your neighborhood or home using il y a and at least two location prepositions; (b) a description of your typical eating habits using partitive articles and at least one preference verb; or (c) a short set of directions from one real or imagined place to another, using at least three imperative forms and y.

Component 3 — Une narration passée (A past narrative)

Length: 80–100 words. Tenses: Compound past + imperfect.

Write a short account of a real or imagined experience. It must include: (a) at least four verbs in the compound past, including at least one with être as auxiliary; (b) at least two verbs in the imperfect providing background, description, or habitual context; (c) at least one chronological connector; (d) one negative sentence. Do not separate the compound past and imperfect into two sections — weave them together as they would naturally occur in a coherent account.

Component 4 — Un projet ou plan (A future plan)

Length: 50–70 words. Tense: Near future; optionally also venir de + infinitive.

Write about something you are planning to do in the near future — a trip, a change in your routine, a project, or a goal. Include: at least three near-future constructions with aller + infinitive; at least one reason using parce que or pour + infinitive; and at least one negative sentence about what you will not do.

Component 5 — Un lien francophone (A Francophone connection)

Length: 60–80 words. Tense: Present, with optional past reference.

Write a short text connecting yourself to the Francophone world. This might be: a Francophone place you have visited or want to visit, a Francophone person you know or have read about, a francophone cultural product (film, book, music, food) that interests you, or a reflection on why you are learning French. Include at least one relative clause with qui or que and at least one comparison using plus, moins, or aussi … que.

Component 6 — Interaction enregistrée ou en direct (Live or recorded interaction)

Length: 2–3 minutes of exchange. Tense: Any tenses required by the conversation.

Complete Speaking Assessment Part 3 (the partner exchange above) and count it as Component 6. If you are studying independently, record a short monologue of 2–3 minutes in which you respond to the question: Qu'est-ce que le français représente pour toi ? Qu'est-ce que tu as appris cette année ? Use varied tenses and include at least one past event, one present reflection, and one future intention.

Answer Key

Exercice 20.1 — Article selection

1. du café (partitive: some coffee, an uncountable quantity) or un café (a coffee shop, a countable establishment — both readings are possible; accept either with a brief justification)

2. une baguette (indefinite singular)

3. le bus (definite: the specific bus she usually takes; identified referent, unchanged after negation)

4. de viande (partitive reduced to de after negation)

5. un beau jardin (indefinite singular; note: beau precedes the noun)

6. de l'eau et du café (partitives for uncountable liquids)

Exercice 20.2 — Tense selection

  1. En ce moment, Léa travaille.
  2. Mardi dernier, elle a fini un rapport.
  3. Quand elle était enfant, elle visitait souvent sa grand-mère.
  4. Demain, nous allons prendre le train.
  5. Il vient d'arriver.
  6. Ils parlaient quand le téléphone a sonné.
  7. L'année dernière, je suis allé(e) à Dakar.
  8. Elle s'est levée tôt et est partie tout de suite.

Exercice 20.3 — Pronoun replacement

  1. J'y ai répondu. (à la question → y)
  2. Elle leur téléphone souvent. (à ses amis → leur)
  3. Nous allons le visiter demain. (le musée → le)
  4. J'en ai déjà fait une partie. (du travail → en)
  5. Tu y vas la semaine prochaine. (à Paris → y)

Exercice 20.4 — Mixed sentence forms

  1. Est-ce que tu as visité la Belgique ?
  2. Elle ne finit jamais son travail.
  3. Nous n'avons pas encore trouvé d'appartement. (Note: indefinite article reduces to d' after negation)
  4. Pourquoi est-ce qu'il est parti tôt ?
  5. C'est le quartier où j'ai grandi.
  6. Je connais un professeur qui parle six langues.

Exercice 20.5 — c'est or il/elle est

  1. Mon professeur préféré ? C'est M. Diallo. Il est très patient.
  2. Quelle est cette ville ? C'est Montréal. C'est une ville magnifique. (noun phrase → c'est)
  3. Et cette femme, qui est-ce ? C'est ma cousine. Elle est architecte. (profession without article after il/elle est)
  4. Ce livre, c'est intéressant. or il est intéressant. (Both acceptable here; c'est is common with adjectives referring back to a whole idea; il est modifies the specific noun directly — accept either.) C'est le roman que j'ai lu le mois dernier.

Final Listening — Tâche 1

1. Kofi lives in Abidjan (Côte d'Ivoire). He is visiting Brussels. 2. Both took part in the community event; they listened to music and met people. (They both attended; the text specifies the music and the welcoming atmosphere.)

Final Listening — Tâche 2

  1. Non, Kofi ne connaissait pas du tout la communauté à Bruxelles. On le sait parce qu'il dit : « je ne connaissais pas du tout la communauté ici. »
  2. Yasmine est à Bruxelles depuis trois ans.
  3. Avant d'arriver à Bruxelles, Yasmine travaillait dans le secteur culturel à Dakar.
  4. Demain, Kofi va visiter le centre-ville. Après-demain, il a une réunion avec des collègues.
  5. L'association vient de lancer un nouveau projet culturel (avec une exposition).
  6. Exemples acceptables : je ne connaissais pas (Kofi didn't know the community — ongoing state in the past); je ne parlais pas beaucoup (Yasmine didn't speak much at the beginning — habitual past situation); je travaillais dans le secteur culturel (Yasmine's previous occupation — ongoing past circumstance). Any one example with a correct explanation is acceptable. The explanation should note that the imperfect describes a past state, habit, or ongoing circumstance rather than a single completed action.

Final Reading — Comprehension answers

  1. Sandrine est née en Martinique (à Fort-de-France) et elle y habite maintenant.
  2. C'était difficile parce qu'elle ne connaissait personne et parce qu'il faisait froid. (Elle mentionne aussi la nourriture, mais la question demande deux raisons — accepter « ne connaissait personne » et « il faisait froid » ou « la nourriture était très différente ».)
  3. Pendant ses études, elle a travaillé à temps partiel dans un hôtel, et elle a appris à cuisiner des plats créoles pour ses amis.
  4. Elle a décidé de rentrer parce que sa famille lui manquait et parce qu'elle voulait travailler en Martinique.
  5. Son agence propose du tourisme culturel — des visites pour découvrir la culture et l'histoire de l'île. Ce n'est pas du tourisme ordinaire. Elle accueille des visiteurs francophones du monde entier.
  6. Le week-end dernier, Sandrine a organisé une visite pour dix-sept personnes. Ils ont visité un jardin botanique et déjeuné dans un restaurant familial. Au début elle était inquiète à cause de la pluie, mais finalement tout le monde a eu une excellente journée.
  7. (a) Compound past with être : je suis partie. (b) Imperfect examples: je ne connaissais personne / il faisait froid / la nourriture était très différente / j'étais un peu inquiète — any two. (c) Near future: je vais développer de nouveaux itinéraires or je vais embaucher une deuxième guide.
  8. Réponses libres. The answer should note that Sandrine sees French as a language that connects people from many different countries and backgrounds — not as the language of one particular place. She values its role as a shared language across a diverse Francophone world. Accurate, relevant responses in French or English are acceptable.

Final Writing and Portfolio — Model response (Component 3: Narration passée)

L'été dernier, je suis allé(e) à Marseille pour la première fois. Avant ce voyage, je ne connaissais pas bien le sud de la France. Quand je suis arrivé(e), il faisait très chaud et la ville était animée. D'abord, j'ai visité le Vieux-Port et j'ai mangé une bouillabaisse dans un petit restaurant. Ensuite, j'ai pris le bateau pour aller sur les îles. C'était magnifique. Malheureusement, je n'ai pas eu le temps de tout voir, mais je vais y retourner l'année prochaine.

Other well-formed responses are equally acceptable. Check against the editing checklist: compound past (with both auxiliaries if possible), imperfect for background/description, at least one connector, at least one negative sentence, required accents throughout.

Beyond This Volume

Ce que vous pouvez faire maintenant — What you can do now

You have completed Speak and Write French: Foundations of Practical Communication. If you have worked diligently through these twenty chapters, you can now communicate in French across a wide range of everyday situations: you can introduce yourself and others, describe your world and your past, navigate practical transactions, make and respond to invitations, understand the main points of supported A2-level recordings and texts, and write connected paragraphs across multiple time frames. That is real communicative ability — built from nothing.

More than that: you have encountered French as a global language used across dozens of countries and communities, with its own variation, history, and richness. You have learned that language is not a fixed code but a living system used differently by different people in different contexts. That understanding will serve you well as you continue.

What comes next — The path to B1

CEFR B1 builds directly on the foundation you have established. The major new systems at B1 include: the simple future tense for firm plans and predictions; the conditional for hypothetical situations and polite requests (you have already used je voudrais and j'aimerais as formulas — at B1 you will understand the full morphological system behind them); the full present subjunctive for doubt, emotion, and necessity; a wider range of relative pronouns; more complex subordination with conjunctions like bien que, pour que, avant que; and a richer vocabulary across domains including current events, opinions, and abstract topics.

At B1 you will also encounter longer and more varied listening and reading texts, and be expected to write longer, more structured compositions. The skills you have built — using cohesive devices, managing tense choice, selecting appropriate register, and attending to orthography — transfer directly to that level.

Continuing with the Francophone world

One of the most effective things you can do at A2 is to begin engaging regularly with authentic French input at or just above your current level. This might include: podcasts or radio designed for French learners or produced by Francophone public broadcasters; short films or series with French subtitles; simple novels or graphic novels in French; social media accounts in French from different Francophone regions; or a language exchange with a Francophone speaker. All of these expose you to the natural variation, rhythm, and vocabulary of real French use — which no textbook can fully replicate. You are now equipped to begin that independent engagement in a meaningful way.

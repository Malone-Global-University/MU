Progetto finale: Italiano indipendente

Volume III, Chapter 20: Final Project: Independent Italian · Speak and Write Italian: Independent Communication and Advanced Expression

Chapter Overview and Objectives

The synthesized paragraph is finished, and Chiara's instructor has proposed one last assignment before the group moves on: a short independent portfolio that pulls together everything they have practiced across Volume III into a single, connected piece of work. There is no new grammar to learn this week — only the more demanding task of using everything already learned, at once, without a chapter to lean on. Chapter 20 asks learners to plan, produce, and revise an integrated project that moves from a personal update through narration, media summary, argument, professional writing, mediation, and presentation, ending in a polished written synthesis. By the end of Chapter 20, you will be able to:

  1. Give a sustained personal and professional update that connects past experience to present goals.
  2. Narrate and interpret a past experience with clear sequence, background, and perspective.
  3. Summarize a media or cultural text and separate fact from opinion.
  4. Argue a position with evidence and respond to a counterpoint.
  5. Mediate information across registers and audiences.
  6. Deliver a short structured presentation and respond to a question.
  7. Write and revise a polished B2 synthesis, editing for agreement, clitics, tense, mood, accents, apostrophes, punctuation, paragraphing, and register.

Communication Scenario

Chiara's instructor has asked each learner to prepare a short independent portfolio: five connected pieces, built from a single theme the learner chooses, that together demonstrate everything covered since Chapter 1. Chiara has picked the theme the three of them already know well — the solar panel incentive program — and is walking Marco and Elena through how to plan a portfolio instead of writing five disconnected exercises. Elena, who tends to overprepare, wants a checklist. Marco, who tends to underprepare, wants to know what happens if he runs out of time.

  1. You will listen to and read Chiara helping Marco and Elena plan their independent portfolios, then reviewing what each part must include.
  2. You will then plan, produce, and revise your own five-part portfolio on a theme of your choice, using the checklist and rubric provided in this chapter.

Pronunciation Focus

Language Focus — sustaining control across a long performance

A five-part portfolio asks a speaker to sustain accurate stress, double consonants, and connected-speech control across several different tasks and tones — a relaxed personal update, a neutral media summary, an assertive argument, and a calm mediation. Practice moving between these tones without letting stress or pace collapse into a single flat register. Before recording or presenting, read each section aloud once at a slow, deliberate pace and once at a natural pace, listening for places where fatigue causes double consonants to shorten or stress to drift.

20.1 Stress and rhythm across register shifts

Notice how your own rhythm changes as you move from an informal personal update to a more formal mediation note read aloud. Formal registers tend to favor slightly slower pacing and clearer phrase boundaries; informal registers allow more compression and connected speech. Neither is more correct — the portfolio should let you demonstrate control of both.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

Chapter 20 introduces no new tense, mood, clitic, or clause system. Every pattern below is a recognition-level review connecting a Volume III system to its place in the final portfolio; none constitutes new instruction.

Consolidated — congiuntivo presente in stance and argument

Penso che il programma sia utile, anche se molte famiglie non ne siano ancora a conoscenza. (I think the program is useful, even though many families are not yet aware of it.) By this point in the volume, learners should produce forms like sia after opinion expressions and concessive connectors without needing to consult a paradigm each time.

Consolidated — conditional and controlled hypothetical structures

Se il Comune pubblicizzasse meglio lo sconto, più famiglie ne approfitterebbero. (If the city publicized the discount better, more families would take advantage of it.) This hypothetical pattern, introduced in Chapter 13 and developed since, should now support advice and evaluation sections of the portfolio with growing independence.

Consolidated — cohesion, register, and revision notation

The circled-word, underlined-connector, and wavy-line conventions from Chapter 19 apply to the whole portfolio, not only to a single paragraph. Use them to check every section for repetition, missing connectors, and uncertain verb forms before producing a final version.

Also active throughout, as in every Unit III and Unit IV chapter: reported speech and indirect questions (Chapter 9), impersonal and passive-like structures (Chapters 6–7), relative clauses (Chapter 8), and discourse connectors for argument and concession (Chapters 11 and 18).

Model Dialogue: Pianificare il portfolio

Setting: Chiara, Marco, and Elena meet at the same kitchen table, now with a blank planning sheet instead of printed articles.

Elena Cinque parti mi sembrano tante. Da dove cominciamo? CHEEN-kweh PAR-tee mee SEM-bra-no TAN-teh. da DO-veh ko-meen-CHA-mo? Five parts seem like a lot to me. Where do we start?
Chiara Cominciamo dal tema, non dalle parti. Se scegliamo un argomento che conosciamo già bene, il resto viene da sé. ko-meen-CHA-mo dal TEH-ma, non DAL-leh PAR-tee. seh sheh-LYA-mo oon ar-go-MEN-to keh ko-no-SHYA-mo JA BEH-neh, eel RES-to VYEH-neh da SEH. Let's start with the theme, not the parts. If we choose a topic we already know well, the rest follows on its own.
Marco Allora usiamo di nuovo gli incentivi per i pannelli solari. Ormai li conosco a memoria. al-LO-ra oo-ZYA-mo dee NWO-vo lyee een-chen-TEE-vee per ee pan-NEL-lee so-LA-ree. OR-mai lee KO-nos-ko a meh-MO-rya. So let's use the solar panel incentives again. By now I know them by heart.
Chiara Perfetto. Nella prima parte racconti perché ti interessa l'argomento e cosa hai imparato quest'anno. Nella seconda, racconti un episodio, magari il giorno in cui abbiamo letto i due articoli. per-FET-to. NEL-la PREE-ma PAR-teh rak-KON-tee per-KEH tee een-teh-RES-sa lar-go-MEN-to eh KO-za ai eem-pa-RA-to kwest AN-no. NEL-la seh-KON-da, rak-KON-tee oon eh-pee-ZO-dyo, MA-ga-ree eel JOR-no een KWEE ab-BYA-mo LET-to ee DOO-eh ar-TEE-ko-lee. Perfect. In the first part you explain why the topic interests you and what you learned this year. In the second, you tell about an episode, maybe the day we read the two articles.
Elena E la terza parte è l'argomentazione, giusto? Dove sostengo una posizione e rispondo a un'obiezione. eh la TER-tsa PAR-teh eh lar-go-men-ta-TSYO-neh, JOOS-to? DO-veh sos-TEN-go OO-na po-see-TSYO-neh eh ree-SPON-do a oon o-byeh-TSYO-neh. And the third part is the argument, right? Where I support a position and respond to an objection.
Chiara Esatto. Poi la mediazione: come spiegheresti lo stesso programma a un vicino anziano che non usa internet? E infine la sintesi scritta, rivista con i segni che abbiamo usato la settimana scorsa. eh-ZAT-to. POY la meh-dya-TSYO-neh: KO-meh spyeh-geh-RES-tee lo STES-so pro-GRAM-ma a oon vee-CHEE-no an-TSYA-no keh non OO-za een-ter-NET? eh een-FEE-neh la SEEN-teh-zee SKREET-ta, ree-VEE-sta kon ee SEN-yee keh ab-BYA-mo oo-ZA-to la set-tee-MA-na SKOR-sa. Exactly. Then the mediation: how would you explain the same program to an elderly neighbor who doesn't use the internet? And finally the written synthesis, revised with the marks we used last week.
Marco E se non ho tempo per rifinire tutto? eh seh non O TEM-po per ree-fee-NEE-reh TOOT-to? And what if I don't have time to polish everything?
Chiara Meglio cinque parti oneste e imperfette che tre parti perfette e due mancanti. L'importante è che si veda tutto quello che sai fare ormai. MEL-yo CHEEN-kweh PAR-tee o-NES-teh eh eem-per-FET-teh keh TREH PAR-tee per-FET-teh eh DOO-eh man-KAN-tee. leem-por-TAN-teh eh keh see VEH-da TOOT-to KWEL-lo keh SAI FA-reh or-MAI. Better five honest, imperfect parts than three perfect parts and two missing. What matters is that it shows everything you're now able to do.

  3. How does Chiara answer Marco's question about running out of time?

Un consiglio per il portfolio finale — Advice for the final portfolio:
Un buon portfolio non è una raccolta di cinque esercizi separati, ma il racconto di un percorso. Ogni parte dovrebbe collegarsi alle altre attraverso lo stesso tema, anche se cambia il tipo di testo o il pubblico a cui ti rivolgi. Chi legge o ascolta il tuo lavoro dovrebbe capire non solo che cosa sai dire in italiano, ma anche come colleghi le idee tra loro. Il bilancio delle competenze, alla fine, conta meno dei singoli errori: conta soprattutto la capacità di comunicare in modo chiaro, coerente e adatto alla situazione.

Reading Practice

Read the excerpt below, adapted from a learner-support page about preparing an independent language portfolio. Notice how it organizes advice around connection between parts rather than perfection within any single part.

Consigli per il portfolio indipendente — Advice for the Independent Portfolio
Un portfolio di livello B2 non deve dimostrare che non fai mai errori, ma che sai comunicare in modo efficace nonostante qualche imperfezione. Scegli un tema che conosci bene: sarà più facile collegare le cinque parti tra loro. Rileggi ogni parte pensando al lettore o all'ascoltatore: capirebbe questa frase senza il contesto delle altre parti? Infine, dedica del tempo alla revisione finale: un errore corretto vale più di una frase complicata lasciata senza controllo.
A B2-level portfolio does not need to show that you never make mistakes, but that you can communicate effectively despite some imperfection. Choose a theme you know well: it will be easier to connect the five parts to one another. Reread each part thinking about the reader or listener: would they understand this sentence without the context of the other parts? Finally, dedicate time to a final revision: one corrected error is worth more than one complicated sentence left unchecked.

Writing Workshop

Plan, draft, and revise your five-part independent portfolio using the steps and checklist below.

Compito di scrittura — Writing task: Choose one theme and produce five short connected pieces: (1) a personal/professional update connecting your experience with the theme, (2) a short past narrative related to the theme, (3) a short argument with a counterpoint, (4) a short mediation note adapting the theme for a different audience, and (5) a polished written synthesis of the whole portfolio, revised for cohesion, style, and accuracy.

  1. Choose your theme. Pick a topic you already know well enough to discuss from several angles.
  2. Draft all five parts. Write each part without stopping to correct yourself.
  3. Check connection. Confirm that each part clearly relates to the same theme and, where possible, to the other parts.
  4. Mark repetition and style. Circle repeated nouns and underline at least one sentence you could rewrite in a more nominal or more verbal style, depending on the part.
  5. Check accuracy. Put a wavy line under any verb form you are unsure of, and confirm tense, mood, and agreement throughout.
  6. Finalize. Produce a clean final version and check accents, apostrophes, and punctuation throughout all five parts.

Lista di controllo — Editing checklist:

  • All five parts connect to the same theme and, where relevant, refer back to one another.
  • Register shifts appropriately between the informal update, the argument, and the mediation note.
  • At least one repeated noun in the whole portfolio has been replaced with a pronoun, ellipsis, or synonym.
  • Verb tense, mood, and agreement have been checked throughout all five parts.
  • Accents, apostrophes, and punctuation are checked throughout.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — independence as a communicative skill

Reaching B2 independence does not mean speaking or writing without support; it means knowing when and how to adapt language for a new audience, purpose, or medium without needing every structure explained in advance. This is as true for native speakers moving between registers as it is for language learners completing a final project.

Culture in Context — portfolios as ordinary academic practice

Collecting varied pieces of work into a single portfolio to demonstrate overall progress, rather than relying on one test, is a common practice in many Italian schools and universities as well as internationally; it reflects a broader view that language proficiency is best shown through connected performance across tasks, not a single isolated score.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — treating the five parts as unrelated exercises

Error: writing five pieces on five different topics simply because each fits a familiar exercise type.

Correction: Choose one theme, as Marco does with the solar panel program, and let each part illuminate a different angle of the same topic. The portfolio should read as one connected body of work, not five disconnected assignments.

Common Mistake — polishing early sections while leaving later ones unrevised

Error: spending all available revision time on the personal update and submitting the mediation note and synthesis as first drafts.

Correction: As Chiara advises, aim for five honest, complete parts rather than a few perfect ones and gaps elsewhere. Budget revision time across all five sections, even briefly.

Common Mistake — using the same register in every part

Error: writing the argument section and the mediation note in exactly the same tone and vocabulary as the informal personal update.

Correction: Adjust register deliberately: keep the personal update conversational, make the argument more assertive and evidence-based, and simplify the mediation note for a non-specialist audience, as in the guided practice above.

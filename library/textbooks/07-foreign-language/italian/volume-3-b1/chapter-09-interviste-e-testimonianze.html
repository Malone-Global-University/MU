Interviste e testimonianze

Volume III, Chapter 9: Interviews and Testimonies · Speak and Write Italian: Independent Communication and Advanced Expression

Chapter Overview and Objectives

After their walk through the neighborhood, Elena, Marco, and Chiara decide to do something more deliberate with what they heard: they arrange to interview Signor Bruno, an elderly neighbor who has lived on their street for over fifty years, about how the neighborhood has changed. This chapter turns Unit II, Media e società, from describing places toward reporting what people say about their own experience. You will learn to understand an interview, ask follow-up questions, and report what someone told you using reported speech with verbs like ha detto che, ha spiegato che, and ha raccontato che, and you will begin forming indirect questions to relay what someone asked or wanted to know. By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Understand the main content and structure of a short interview or personal testimony.
  2. Ask natural follow-up questions in an interview setting.
  3. Report what someone said using ha detto che, ha spiegato che, and ha raccontato che.
  4. Form controlled indirect questions to relay what someone asked.
  5. Track who is speaking and whose perspective is being reported across a longer account.
  6. Write a short reported account of another person's experience.

Communication Scenario

Chiara suggests that instead of only talking among themselves about the neighborhood, they should ask someone who has actually seen it change. Signor Bruno, who has lived on their street since he was a young man, agrees to sit with them for a short interview on a bench near the piazza.

  1. You will read and listen to Elena, Marco, and Chiara interview Signor Bruno about how the neighborhood has changed, noticing how they ask follow-up questions and later report what he said.
  2. You will then complete your own interview task: reading a short testimony, and writing a reported account of someone else's experience.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — interview intonation

Interview questions often rise gently toward the end, even when they are not yes-no questions, to signal genuine curiosity rather than a formal test: E come si è trasformato il quartiere in tutti questi anni? Practice this rising, interested tone separately from the flatter tone used to state a fact.

9.1 Turn-taking and backchanneling

Italian speakers commonly show they are listening with short sounds and words such as mm, certo, capisco, and davvero?, often overlapping slightly with the speaker rather than waiting for a complete silence. This is a sign of engagement, not interruption.

9.2 Rhythm in reported speech

When you shift from quoting someone directly to reporting what they said, your pitch tends to fall and become more even: compare the more animated "Questo quartiere era completamente diverso!" with the flatter Ha detto che il quartiere era completamente diverso. Practice this shift deliberately so your reported speech does not sound like a direct quotation.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

Language Focus — reported speech with ha detto che

To report what someone said, use a reporting verb followed by che and a clause: Il signor Bruno ha detto che il quartiere era molto diverso cinquant'anni fa. (Mr. Bruno said that the neighborhood was very different fifty years ago.) Notice that a present-tense statement ("il quartiere è diverso") often shifts to the imperfect when reported after a past-tense reporting verb, because the speaker is describing an ongoing state at that past moment. This chapter treats this shift as a pattern to notice and use in high-frequency cases, not as a complete tense-shifting system.

Language Focus — reported speech with ha spiegato che and ha raccontato che

Ha spiegato che introduces an explanation or clarification: Ha spiegato che il mercato si è spostato negli anni Novanta. (He explained that the market moved in the nineties.) Ha raccontato che introduces a narrated memory or story: Ci ha raccontato che da bambino giocava in quella piazza con gli amici. (He told us that as a child he used to play in that square with his friends.) Choose the reporting verb that matches what kind of information is being relayed: a fact, an explanation, or a story.

Language Focus — indirect questions

An indirect question reports what someone asked, without using question marks or direct word order: Gli abbiamo chiesto se ricordava il vecchio mercato. (We asked him whether he remembered the old market.) Gli abbiamo chiesto come fosse cambiato il quartiere. (We asked him how the neighborhood had changed.) Use se to introduce a reported yes-no question, and a question word (come, quando, perché, cosa) to introduce a reported information question. This chapter introduces indirect questions in a small set of controlled, high-frequency frames rather than as a fully developed system.

Language Focus — pronoun reference across a reported account

When you report a longer account, keep track of whose perspective each pronoun expresses: Il signor Bruno ci ha detto che, quando lui era giovane, i suoi genitori gestivano un piccolo negozio proprio lì. (Mr. Bruno told us that when he was young, his parents ran a small shop right there.) Here lui and suoi refer back to il signor Bruno, not to the speaker reporting the account. Ambiguous pronoun reference is one of the most common errors when learners begin reporting longer testimonies.

This chapter reviews relative clauses (il quartiere in cui è cresciuto) as recycled material from Chapter 8; it does not introduce new relative-clause patterns. Focus your new productive effort on reported speech and indirect questions.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Elena, Marco, and Chiara sit with Signor Bruno on a bench near the piazza, recording a short interview.

Chiara Signor Bruno, da quanto tempo abita in questo quartiere? seen-YOR BROO-no, da KWAN-to TEM-po a-BEE-ta in KWEH-sto kwar-TYEH-reh? Mr. Bruno, how long have you lived in this neighborhood?
Signor Bruno Da più di cinquant'anni. Sono arrivato qui da ragazzo, con i miei genitori. da PYOO dee CHEEN-kwan-TAN-nee. SO-no ar-ree-VA-to kwee da ra-GAT-tso, kon ee myay-ee jeh-nee-TO-ree. For more than fifty years. I arrived here as a boy, with my parents.
Marco E come si è trasformato il quartiere in tutti questi anni? eh KO-meh see EH tras-for-MA-to eel kwar-TYEH-reh in TOOT-tee KWEH-stee AN-nee? And how has the neighborhood changed over all these years?
Signor Bruno Moltissimo. I miei genitori gestivano un piccolo negozio proprio lì, dove ora c'è la banca. Il quartiere era più povero, ma tutti si conoscevano. mol-TEES-see-mo. ee myay-ee jeh-nee-TO-ree jeh-STEE-va-no oon peek-KO-lo neh-GO-tsyo PRO-pryo lee, DO-veh O-ra CHEH la BAN-ka. eel kwar-TYEH-reh EH-ra PYOO PO-veh-ro, ma TOOT-tee see ko-no-SHEH-va-no. A great deal. My parents ran a small shop right there, where the bank is now. The neighborhood was poorer, but everyone knew each other.
Elena Mi chiedevo se ricordasse ancora il vecchio mercato, quello di cui ci hanno parlato. mee kyeh-DEH-vo seh ree-kor-DAS-seh an-KO-ra eel VEK-kyo mehr-KA-to, KWEL-lo dee koo-ee chee AN-no par-LA-to. I was wondering whether you still remembered the old market, the one people have told us about.
Signor Bruno Certo che me lo ricordo! Cioè, era il cuore del quartiere. Ci raccontava sempre mio padre che si trovava qualsiasi cosa lì. CHEHR-to keh meh lo ree-KOR-do! cho-EH, EH-ra eel KWO-reh del kwar-TYEH-reh. chee rak-kon-TA-va SEHM-preh myo PA-dreh keh see tro-VA-va kwal-SEE-a-see KO-za lee. Of course I remember it! I mean, it was the heart of the neighborhood. My father used to always tell us that you could find anything there.
Marco Grazie mille, signor Bruno. Ha detto delle cose davvero interessanti. GRA-tsyeh MEEL-leh, seen-YOR BROO-no. a DET-to DEL-leh KO-zeh dav-VEH-ro in-tehr-eh-SSAN-tee. Thank you very much, Mr. Bruno. You've said some really interesting things.

Domande di comprensione — Comprehension questions:

  1. How long has Signor Bruno lived in the neighborhood, and how did he arrive there?
  2. What does Signor Bruno say his parents used to do, and where?
  3. What does Signor Bruno say about the old market?

Speaking Practice

Pratica controllata — Controlled practice: report a statement

With a partner, take turns making a simple statement about your own past, then reporting your partner's statement aloud using ha detto che, ha spiegato che, or ha raccontato che.

Compito comunicativo — Communicative task: mini-interview

Reading Practice

Read the short testimony below, adapted for this level.

La testimonianza della signora Rossi — Mrs. Rossi's testimony
La signora Rossi, che abita nel palazzo accanto alla piazza, ci ha raccontato la sua esperienza. Ha detto che si è trasferita nel quartiere quarant'anni fa, subito dopo essersi sposata. Ha spiegato che, all'inizio, non conosceva quasi nessuno, ma che il mercato rionale l'ha aiutata a integrarsi: ci andava ogni settimana e, poco a poco, ha fatto amicizia con i commercianti e con altre famiglie. Le abbiamo chiesto se, secondo lei, il quartiere avesse mantenuto lo stesso spirito di un tempo. Ha risposto che, per certi versi sì: la gente si saluta ancora per strada, anche se oggi ci sono molte più famiglie arrivate da altre città e da altri paesi. Secondo lei, questo non ha indebolito il quartiere, ma lo ha reso più ricco.
Mrs. Rossi, who lives in the building next to the square, told us about her experience. She said that she moved to the neighborhood forty years ago, right after getting married. She explained that, at first, she knew almost no one, but that the neighborhood market helped her integrate: she went there every week and, little by little, made friends with the shopkeepers and other families. We asked her whether, in her opinion, the neighborhood had kept the same spirit as before. She replied that, in some ways, yes: people still greet each other on the street, even though today there are many more families who have arrived from other cities and other countries. In her opinion, this has not weakened the neighborhood, but has made it richer.

Domande di comprensione — Comprehension questions:

  1. When and why did Mrs. Rossi move to the neighborhood?
  2. According to the testimony, what helped her integrate into the neighborhood?
  3. Identify one indirect question and one reported statement in the passage.

Writing Workshop

Write a short reported account of someone else's experience.

Compito di scrittura — Writing task: Interview a friend, family member, or classmate (in English or Italian, as needed) about a place that has changed in their life, or use the interview you conducted in the speaking practice above. Then write eight to ten sentences in Italian reporting what they told you. Use at least three reported-speech verbs (ha detto che, ha spiegato che, ha raccontato che) and at least one indirect question.

  1. Introduce the person. Say who they are and their connection to the place.
  2. Report a fact using ha detto che. Relay a simple piece of information.
  3. Report an explanation using ha spiegato che. Relay a cause or reason they gave.
  4. Report a memory using ha raccontato che. Relay a short story or recollection.
  5. Include one indirect question. Report something you asked them.
  6. Close with their overall perspective. Use secondo lui/lei to summarize their point of view.

Lista di controllo — Editing checklist:

  • At least three reported-speech verbs are used correctly.
  • At least one indirect question is included.
  • Pronouns clearly refer back to the person being reported, not to the writer.
  • The account preserves the person's point of view rather than distorting it.
  • Accents, apostrophes, and quotation conventions are checked throughout.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — interview style and medium

An interview conducted for a neighborhood project, a school assignment, a newspaper, or a family history can differ considerably in tone and formality. A conversation with an elderly neighbor on a bench is naturally more informal than a recorded interview for a local newspaper, even when the topic is the same.

Culture in Context — reporting accurately

When reporting someone's testimony, preserving their stance and meaning matters more than producing an elegant sentence. Distorting what a person said, even unintentionally through careless pronoun reference or an inaccurate reporting verb, misrepresents their experience.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — using direct question order inside an indirect question

Error: Writing Gli abbiamo chiesto come è cambiato il quartiere? with a question mark and direct word order.

Correction: Gli abbiamo chiesto come fosse cambiato il quartiere. is preferred in careful writing; no question mark is used, since the sentence is a statement reporting a question, not a question itself.

Common Mistake — losing track of whose pronoun is whose

Error: Writing Ha detto che i miei genitori gestivano un negozio when reporting what Signor Bruno said about his own parents.

Correction: Ha detto che i suoi genitori gestivano un negozio is required, since the parents belong to the person being quoted, not to the speaker reporting the account.

Common Mistake — treating every reported statement the same way

Error: Using ha detto che for every kind of reported information, including memories and explanations.

Correction: Choose the verb that matches the content: ha spiegato che for a reason or clarification, ha raccontato che for a story or memory, and ha detto che for a simple statement.

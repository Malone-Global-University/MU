Mediazione e registro

Volume III, Chapter 14: Mediation and Register · Speak and Write Italian: Independent Communication and Advanced Expression

Chapter Overview and Objectives

Chiara asked the archive for a different schedule, as Marco suggested — and it worked, partly. Now she has to explain the new arrangement to two different people: her seminar coordinator, who needs a formal request, and Marco and Elena, who just want to know what happened. Marco, meanwhile, has decided to accept his supervisor's offer to stay through August, but he still needs to tell Elena directly and then write a polite note to the trip's other organizer explaining the postponement. Elena has taken the tutoring job and now has to explain her new schedule to her thesis advisor. Chapter 14 shifts from making a decision to relaying it: taking the same piece of information and shaping it differently depending on who is listening, how well you know them, and what the situation requires. By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Mediate information between two speakers or a speaker and a text, preserving meaning and intent.
  2. Transform an informal account into a formal written message for an institutional audience.
  3. Summarize the same event differently for two different audiences without distorting the facts.
  4. Use impersonal and passive-like structures for neutral, institutional reporting.
  5. Track reference accurately with clitic pronouns across paraphrase and summary.
  6. Recognize and repair a register mismatch in your own or another's writing.

Communication Scenario

The archive agreed: Chiara can work Tuesday, Thursday, and Saturday afternoons instead of Tuesday, Wednesday, and Thursday — which frees up the seminar slot, provided her coordinator approves an informal swap of discussion groups. Marco has told his supervisor yes, he will stay through August, and now needs to write to Sara, the friend who was organizing the group trip, to explain the change without simply forwarding his supervisor's dry, bureaucratic email. Elena has accepted the tutoring position, three evenings a week, and needs to let her thesis advisor know without making it sound like the tutoring will interfere with her thesis timeline — because it will not, but the email has to say so convincingly. None of the three is translating word for word. Each is taking the same real situation and shaping it for a different reader.

  1. You will read and listen to Chiara, Marco, and Elena mediate their news for different audiences.
  2. You will then complete your own task: taking one piece of informal news from your own life and rewriting it for two different, more formal audiences.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — neutral mediation tone versus repair intonation

When relaying information neutrally, keep the pitch relatively even and the pace measured, as in Il supervisore ha chiesto a Marco di rimanere fino ad agosto. When repairing a misunderstanding, the pitch rises slightly on the corrected element and a brief pause follows it, as in No, non ad agosto — fino a settembre. The rising-then-pausing pattern signals correction rather than new information.

14.1 Formal presentation delivery in written-to-spoken mediation

When you read a formal written passage aloud — for example, relaying the content of an institutional email to a friend — slow slightly and give more weight to connectors like per quanto riguarda and tenendo presente che, since these mark the structure of formal reasoning and are easy to swallow at a conversational pace.

14.2 Turn-taking and repair in three-way mediation

When one speaker relays what a second speaker said to a third, listen for a brief confirming pause before the relay begins — often filled with allora or dunque — which signals that the speaker is about to summarize rather than speak in their own voice.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

Language Focus — impersonal and passive-like structures for neutral reporting

Institutional and mediated language often removes the personal subject to sound neutral and official. Compare the personal Il coordinatore ha detto che posso cambiare gruppo. (The coordinator said I can change groups.) with the more institutional Si comunica che è possibile cambiare gruppo di discussione, previa approvazione del coordinatore. (It is hereby communicated that it is possible to change discussion groups, subject to the coordinator's approval.) You are not required to produce dense institutional passages like the second example independently, but you should be able to write a moderately neutral version, such as Si può cambiare gruppo con l'approvazione del coordinatore., using si plus a third-person verb to remove the personal subject when the audience calls for it.

Language Focus — clitic reference across paraphrase and summary

When you summarize what someone else said, the pronouns must point to the right person even though the speaker has changed. If Elena tells Marco Te lo dico subito. (I'll tell you right away.) and you then report this to Chiara, the pronouns shift: Elena ha detto che glielo avrebbe detto subito. (Elena said she would tell him right away.) — glielo now points to Marco, not to Chiara, and the verb has moved from present to conditional to match the reporting frame. Track carefully who gli, le, lo, and glielo refer to whenever you paraphrase a conversation; a misplaced clitic can make a summary say the opposite of what was meant.

Language Focus — register-sensitive restructuring

The same fact can be expressed with very different structures depending on the audience. Informally: Ho preso il lavoro di tutoraggio, tre sere a settimana. Formally, addressed to an advisor: La informo che ho accettato un incarico di tutoraggio per tre sere a settimana, che non interferirà con la tesi. Notice the shift from the bare statement of fact to a fuller sentence that anticipates the reader's likely concern (interference with the thesis) and answers it directly — a hallmark of considerate formal writing.

Setting: Chiara, Marco, and Elena meet again at the same café, this time to help each other draft messages for different audiences.

Speaking Practice

Pratica controllata — Controlled practice: two audiences, one fact

Take four simple facts (Ho cambiato orario., Il progetto è in ritardo., etc.) and say each one twice: once to a close friend, once to an institutional audience, adjusting register each time.

Compito comunicativo — Communicative task: relay the message

With a partner, one of you plays a speaker with informal news; the other relays that news to a third, imagined formal audience (a professor, an employer, an office). Use at least one impersonal or passive-like structure.

Portfolio orale — Oral portfolio: clear up the misunderstanding

Invent a small misunderstanding based on a mediated message (someone relayed something incorrectly) and role-play the repair, using in altre parole or non intendevo dire questo.

Writing Workshop

Write your own mediation task, taking one piece of news and relaying it to two different audiences.

Compito di scrittura — Writing task: Choose one real or invented piece of news (a schedule change, a decision, a plan). Write a short informal message (three to four sentences) telling a friend the news, and then write a more formal message (five to six sentences) relaying the same news to an institutional or professional audience. Use at least one impersonal or passive-like structure in the formal version, and make sure your clitic pronouns point clearly to the right person in both versions.

  1. State the news informally. Write as you would to a close friend.
  2. Identify the formal audience. Decide who needs to receive the formal version and why their concerns differ from your friend's.
  3. Restructure for register. Use at least one impersonal or passive-like structure and appropriate formal vocabulary.
  4. Anticipate the reader's concern. Add a sentence that addresses what the formal reader is likely to worry about.
  5. Check your pronouns. Confirm that every clitic pronoun refers unambiguously to the right person.

Lista di controllo — Editing checklist:

  • The informal and formal versions convey the same underlying facts.
  • The formal version uses at least one impersonal or passive-like structure.
  • Clitic pronouns are unambiguous in both versions.
  • The formal version anticipates and addresses the reader's likely concern.
  • Accents, apostrophes, and punctuation are checked throughout.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — institutional Italian and the reader's expectations

Italian institutional writing — university offices, employers, public administration — often uses impersonal and passive-like constructions such as si comunica che partly to sound neutral and official, and partly because these formulas are simply expected in that context; using an overly casual tone in a formal request can come across as careless rather than friendly.

Culture in Context — mediation as a social skill

Relaying news between friends, family, and institutions is an ordinary part of daily life in any language community, but doing it well in Italian — choosing the right register, anticipating the listener's concerns — is itself a marker of social and professional competence, not just a grammar exercise.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — forwarding informal wording into a formal context

Error: writing to a coordinator, Volevo dirti che ho bisogno di cambiare gruppo, spero vada bene!

Correction: La contatto per chiedere la possibilità di cambiare gruppo di discussione. Match the structure and directness of the request to the relationship and setting; save the informal tone for peers and friends.

Common Mistake — losing track of the pronoun's referent in reported speech

Error: Elena ha detto a Marco che gli avrebbe scritto. (intending that Elena would write to Sara, not to Marco)

Correction: Elena ha detto a Marco che le avrebbe scritto. (referring to Sara) Before finalizing a summary, confirm who each clitic pronoun refers to; a mismatched pronoun can reverse the meaning of the relay.

Common Mistake — treating paraphrase as literal translation

Error: copying an institutional sentence word for word into a message to a friend, producing something stiff and unnatural.

Correction: restate the content in your own words at the appropriate register — mediation preserves meaning and intent, not exact wording.

Verifica 3: Argomentare e mediare

Volume III, Chapter 15: Review 3 — Argument and Mediation · Speak and Write Italian: Independent Communication and Advanced Expression

Chapter Overview and Objectives

Chiara's request went to the coordinator, Marco finally wrote to Sara, and Elena reassured her advisor. Four chapters of decisions, arguments, advice, and relayed news have brought the group to the end of Unit III. Chapter 15 does not introduce anything new. Instead, it asks the group — and you — to put Chapters 11 through 14 to work at once: building a supported argument, writing a professional message, giving advice about a real choice, and mediating information across audiences, all in a single connected project. This chapter introduces no new productive language system. By the end of Chapter 15, you will be able to:

  1. Present a supported position on a social or practical problem using developed congiuntivo presente and argument connectors.
  2. Write a formal professional or institutional message with appropriate softeners and register.
  3. Give advice and compare options using condizionale presente and a controlled hypothetical structure.
  4. Mediate the same information for two different audiences, using impersonal/passive-like structures where appropriate.
  5. Track clitic-pronoun reference accurately across argument, advice, and mediation tasks.
  6. Edit a piece of extended writing for agreement, tense, mood, accents, apostrophes, punctuation, and register.

Communication Scenario

The seminar coordinator has asked Chiara, Marco, and Elena to help prepare a short proposal: the discussion-group schedule that worked for Chiara this term should perhaps become an option for other students next year. This means the three of them must now argue for a change (Chapter 11), write it up as a formal proposal (Chapter 12), weigh the advantages and disadvantages of different scheduling options (Chapter 13), and explain the plan differently to the student group and to the administration (Chapter 14). Nothing about the language is new here — it is the same set of tools from the last four chapters, used together on one real task.

  1. You will read and listen to Chiara, Marco, and Elena plan, argue for, and mediate their proposal.
  2. You will then complete an integrated task of your own: arguing for a change, writing a formal message about it, comparing two ways to implement it, and relaying the plan to two different audiences.

Pronunciation Review

Pronunciation Review — argument, professional, advisory, and mediating tones

This chapter reviews four intonation patterns practiced separately in Chapters 11–14: the emphatic, slightly rising pitch of argument (tuttavia, bisogna considerare che...); the measured, polite rhythm of professional speech (La ringrazio per l'attenzione...); the softer, suggestive tone of advice (forse sarebbe meglio se...); and the even, neutral tone of mediation (si comunica che...). No new pronunciation feature is introduced; the task is to move between all four naturally within a single presentation.

15.1 Stress control across a longer presentation

Review stress in multi-syllable formal and argumentative vocabulary (responsabilità, considerazione, svantaggio) as your presentation moves between registers, keeping stress consistent even as tone shifts.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns Review

This chapter introduces no new grammar system. The boxes below consolidate the four systems developed across Chapters 11–14 for use in a single integrated project.

Review — congiuntivo presente in argument (Chapter 11)

È importante che il coordinatore consideri questa proposta. (It's important that the coordinator consider this proposal.) Continue to reserve the subjunctive for the scheduled high-frequency frames (opinion, doubt, necessity, evaluation) rather than extending it to every subordinate clause.

Review — professional register and combined clitics (Chapter 12)

Glielo invierò entro venerdì. (I'll send it to him/her by Friday.) Formal register still relies on courteous softeners (La informo che..., Le sarei grato/a se...) and on combined clitics tracking reference precisely, as consolidated in professional and service contexts.

Review — condizionale presente and the controlled hypothetical (Chapter 13)

Se il coordinatore fosse d'accordo, il nuovo orario funzionerebbe per tutti. (If the coordinator agreed, the new schedule would work for everyone.) The pattern se + congiuntivo imperfetto + condizionale remains limited to controlled, high-frequency frames; it is not a full hypothetical-period system.

Review — impersonal/passive-like mediation structures (Chapter 14)

Si propone di estendere l'orario flessibile ad altri studenti. (It is proposed that the flexible schedule be extended to other students.) Use si + third-person verb to shift from a personal proposal to a neutral, institutional-sounding one when the audience calls for it, and keep clitic pronouns unambiguous as the same information moves between audiences.

Also active throughout, as in every Unit III chapter: reported speech and indirect questions (Chapter 9), relative clauses (Chapter 8, Chapter 12), and formal email conventions (Chapter 12).

Model Dialogue: Una proposta per tutti

Setting: Chiara, Marco, and Elena meet at the café one last time before the end of the term to plan their proposal.

Chiara Allora, il coordinatore ha chiesto se possiamo proporre il mio orario come opzione per tutti l'anno prossimo. Da dove cominciamo? al-LO-ra, eel ko-or-dee-na-TO-reh a kyeh-STO seh po-see-A-mo pro-POR-reh eel MEE-o o-RA-ryo KO-meh op-TSYO-neh pehr TOOT-tee lAN-no PROS-see-mo. da DO-veh ko-meen-CHA-mo? So, the coordinator asked if we can propose my schedule as an option for everyone next year. Where do we start?
Marco È importante che spieghiamo perché funziona, non solo che ha funzionato per te. Tuttavia, dobbiamo anche considerare gli svantaggi — per esempio, non tutti i gruppi hanno lo stesso numero di studenti. EH eem-por-TAN-teh keh spyeh-GYA-mo pehr-KEH FOON-tsyo-na, non SO-lo keh a foon-tsyo-NA-to pehr teh. toot-TA-vya, do-BYA-mo AN-keh kon-see-deh-RA-reh lyee zvan-TAJ-jee — pehr eh-ZEM-pyo, non TOOT-tee ee GROOP-pee AN-no lo STES-so NOO-meh-ro dee stoo-DEN-tee. It's important that we explain why it works, not just that it worked for you. However, we also have to consider the disadvantages — for example, not all groups have the same number of students.
Elena Se scrivessimo la proposta in modo formale, con vantaggi e svantaggi chiari, sarebbe più facile da valutare. Io posso occuparmi della lettera al coordinatore. seh skree-VES-see-mo la pro-POS-ta in MO-do for-MA-leh, kon van-TAJ-jee eh zvan-TAJ-jee KYA-ree, sa-REB-beh pyoo FA-cheeleh da va-loo-TA-reh. EE-o PO-so ok-koo-PAR-mee DEL-la LET-teh-ra al ko-or-dee-na-TO-reh. If we wrote the proposal formally, with clear advantages and disadvantages, it would be easier to evaluate. I can take care of the letter to the coordinator.
Chiara Perfetto. E poi dovremo spiegarlo anche agli altri studenti — ma non con le stesse parole della lettera formale, ovviamente. pehr-FET-to. eh PO-ee do-VREH-mo spyeh-GAR-lo AN-keh a-lyee AL-tree stoo-DEN-tee — ma non kon leh STES-seh pa-RO-leh DEL-la LET-teh-ra for-MA-leh, ov-vya-MEN-teh. Perfect. And then we'll also have to explain it to the other students — but not with the same words as the formal letter, obviously.
Marco Esatto. Si propone una cosa al coordinatore, e la stessa cosa si racconta agli studenti in modo diverso. La stessa notizia, come dice sempre Elena. eh-ZAT-to. see pro-PO-neh OO-na KO-za al ko-or-dee-na-TO-reh, eh la STES-sa KO-za see rak-KON-ta a-lyee stoo-DEN-tee in MO-do dee-VER-so. la STES-sa no-TEE-tsya, KO-meh DEE-cheh SEM-preh eh-LEH-na. Exactly. You propose one thing to the coordinator, and you tell the same thing to the students differently. The same news, as Elena always says.

Domande di comprensione — Comprehension questions:

  1. What has the coordinator asked the group to prepare?
  2. What does Marco say they must not forget to consider?
  3. Who will write the formal letter to the coordinator?

Speaking Practice

Pratica controllata — Controlled practice: four registers, one topic

Take one proposal (for example, extending library hours) and state it four times: as an argument with reasons (Chapter 11 style), as a formal request (Chapter 12 style), as advice comparing two options (Chapter 13 style), and as a neutral mediated announcement (Chapter 14 style).

Compito comunicativo — Communicative task: defend and relay

With a partner, propose a change to a shared situation (a class schedule, a household rule, a work policy). Argue for it, respond to at least one objection, and then relay the agreed plan differently to two imagined audiences.

Portfolio orale — Oral portfolio: the full proposal

Present your own version of Chiara, Marco, and Elena's proposal in three minutes: state the problem, argue for the solution, compare two ways to implement it, and explain how you would announce it to two different audiences.

Reading Practice

Read the draft proposal letter below, adapted for this level, which integrates argument, professional register, and mediated planning.

Bozza di lettera al coordinatore — Draft letter to the coordinator
Gentile Coordinatore, Le scriviamo per proporre di estendere ad altri studenti l'orario flessibile che quest'anno è stato concesso a titolo di prova. Riteniamo che questa soluzione presenti diversi vantaggi: permette una maggiore conciliazione tra studio e lavoro e non ha creato, nella nostra esperienza, alcun problema di organizzazione dei gruppi. È però importante che si consideri anche lo svantaggio principale, ossia la difficoltà di garantire numeri equilibrati in ogni gruppo. Se fosse possibile, proporremmo una fase di prova di un semestre prima di rendere l'orario flessibile permanente per tutti. Restiamo a disposizione per qualsiasi chiarimento. Distinti saluti.
Dear Coordinator, We are writing to propose extending to other students the flexible schedule that was granted this year on a trial basis. We believe this solution presents several advantages: it allows for a better balance between study and work, and in our experience it has not created any problem with organizing groups. However, it is important to also consider the main disadvantage, namely the difficulty of guaranteeing balanced numbers in each group. If possible, we would propose a one-semester trial period before making the flexible schedule permanent for everyone. We remain available for any clarification. Kind regards.

Domande di comprensione — Comprehension questions:

  1. What is being proposed in the letter, and on what basis (Chiara's own experience)?
  2. What is identified as the main disadvantage?
  3. What compromise does the letter suggest before making the change permanent?

Writing Workshop

Complete an integrated writing task that draws on argument, professional writing, advice, and mediation together.

Compito di scrittura — Writing task: Choose a change you would like to see in a familiar context (school, work, a club, a shared living situation). Write: (1) a short paragraph arguing for the change, with at least one reason, one example, and one concession introduced by tuttavia or anche se; (2) a formal letter or email proposing the change to the relevant authority, using at least one congiuntivo presente frame and one softened request; (3) two or three sentences comparing two ways the change could be implemented, using condizionale presente and, if natural, a controlled hypothetical sentence; and (4) a short, differently worded version of the same plan for a more informal audience (friends, classmates, colleagues), including at least one impersonal or passive-like structure somewhere in the set.

  1. State the problem and argue for the change. Use reasons, an example, and a concession.
  2. Write the formal proposal. Address the appropriate authority with correct register and softeners.
  3. Compare two implementations. Use conditional language and, where natural, the controlled hypothetical pattern.
  4. Mediate the plan for a second audience. Rewrite it in your own words at a different register.
  5. Edit the whole set. Check tense, mood, clitics, agreement, accents, apostrophes, and punctuation throughout.

Lista di controllo — Editing checklist:

  • The argument includes a reason, an example, and a concession.
  • The formal letter uses at least one congiuntivo presente frame and appropriate softeners.
  • The comparison of options uses condizionale presente correctly.
  • The informal version conveys the same facts as the formal version, in different words.
  • At least one impersonal or passive-like structure appears somewhere in the set.
  • Clitic pronouns are unambiguous throughout.
  • Accents, apostrophes, and punctuation are checked throughout.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — proposals as a shared genre

A written proposal to an Italian institution — a school office, an employer, a public body — typically states the request, gives supporting reasons, acknowledges at least one limitation or difficulty, and closes with a courteous offer to provide further information; skipping the acknowledged limitation can make a proposal seem less credible, not more persuasive.

Culture in Context — one plan, several audiences

Chiara, Marco, and Elena's habit of explaining the same plan differently to different people is not evasive; adapting register and structure to the audience — while keeping the facts identical — is a normal and expected part of Italian institutional and social life, as this unit has shown from Chapter 11 onward.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — arguing without acknowledging a counterpoint

Error: presenting only advantages, as if no disadvantage existed: L'orario flessibile è perfetto e non ha nessun problema.

Correction: L'orario flessibile funziona bene, anche se bisogna considerare la questione dei numeri nei gruppi. A credible argument in Italian, as in Chapter 11, generally acknowledges at least one real limitation.

Common Mistake — over-extending the subjunctive

Error: Penso che il coordinatore leggerà la lettera domani. used with subjunctive forcing where a simple future is meant, or subjunctive appearing outside a scheduled trigger.

Correction: Reserve congiuntivo presente for the scheduled frames (opinion, doubt, necessity, evaluation) introduced in Chapter 11; do not extend it to ordinary factual statements.

Common Mistake — reusing the formal letter's wording for the informal audience

Error: telling classmates, Si propone di estendere l'orario flessibile a tutti gli studenti che lo richiedono.

Correction: Ragazzi, penso che potremmo avere anche noi l'orario flessibile l'anno prossimo! As in Chapter 14, mediation restates content at the appropriate register rather than copying the formal wording verbatim.

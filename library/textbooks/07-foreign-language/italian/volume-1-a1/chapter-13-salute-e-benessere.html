Salute e benessere

Volume I, Chapter 13: Health and Well-Being · Speak and Write Italian: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Overview and Objectives

Chapter 13 continues Unit III by moving from free time into everyday health and well-being. You will name basic body parts, describe common symptoms, say how you feel, ask for help, and understand simple advice from a friend, a pharmacist, or a clinic. By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Name common body parts and point to or describe where something hurts.
  2. Describe basic symptoms and physical states using avere and stare expressions.
  3. Ask for help politely in a pharmacy or clinic setting.
  4. Express need and basic ability using avere bisogno di, potere, and dovere.
  5. Understand simple, controlled advice such as devi riposare or è meglio bere acqua.
  6. Make or confirm a basic appointment.

Communication Scenario

Giulia wakes up feeling unwell before an exam week. She stops at the neighborhood pharmacy to describe her symptoms to the pharmacist, and then calls the doctor's office to confirm whether she needs an appointment.

  1. This chapter teaches you to describe symptoms, ask for help, and understand basic advice.
  2. You will practice this through Giulia's pharmacy visit and phone call, then apply it to your own health situations.

Where This Chapter Fits

Chapter 13 continues Unit III. It builds on the avere expressions from Chapter 4 and the polite request language of Chapters 8 and 11, and it introduces new material: body vocabulary, symptom language, and controlled modal-based advice.

Coming Into This Chapter

You can already use avere for age and possession (Ch. 4) and make polite requests with vorrei and per favore (Ch. 8, Ch. 11). You can also give and follow directions (Ch. 11).

In This Chapter

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — ho versus o, and doubled consonants in health words

The written h in ho, hai, ha, hanno is silent, so ho (I have) sounds identical to the vowel o (or). Rely on context and spelling, not sound, to tell them apart. Also notice the doubled consonant in stomaco is a single m, while words like febbre (FEB-breh) contain a true doubled consonant that must be held slightly longer than a single one.

13.1 Sympathetic and concerned intonation

When someone describes not feeling well, a caring response such as Mi dispiace, stai bene? typically uses a gentle falling-then-rising pitch, softer than an ordinary question. A flat, businesslike tone can sound less caring than intended.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

Language Focus — avere expressions for physical states

Form: avere + noun describes many physical states in Italian where English uses "to be": ho fame, ho sete, ho freddo, ho caldo, ho mal di testa.

Use: Ho mal di gola da ieri. (I've had a sore throat since yesterday.) Do not translate these expressions with essere; Italian consistently uses avere for this pattern.

Language Focus — stare for wellbeing

Form: Come stai? / Come sta? and responses Sto bene, sto male, sto così così.

Use: stare is the standard verb for asking and answering about wellbeing, distinct from essere, which describes identity and characteristics. Use Come stai? with tu and Come sta? with Lei.

Language Focus — avere bisogno di

Form: avere bisogno di + noun or infinitive: Ho bisogno di una ricetta. Ho bisogno di riposare.

Use: This controlled pattern expresses need. It is practiced here with io, tu, and lui/lei/Lei only; broader use develops in later chapters.

Language Focus — potere and dovere in health contexts

Form: posso, puoi, può (I can, you can, he/she/you formal can) and devo, devi, deve (I must, you must, he/she/you formal must), each followed by an infinitive.

Use and boundary: Devi riposare. Puoi prendere questa medicina due volte al giorno. (You should rest. You can take this medicine twice a day.) Advice formulas such as devi..., puoi..., and è meglio... are controlled expressions at this stage; full modal-verb conjugation across all persons is developed further in Chapter 14 and beyond.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Giulia stops at the neighborhood pharmacy to describe her symptoms, as described in the Communication Scenario above.

Farmacista Buongiorno! Come sta? Posso aiutarla? Bwon-JOR-no! KO-meh sta? PO-so a-yoo-TAR-la? Good morning! How are you? Can I help you?
Giulia Buongiorno. Non sto molto bene. Ho mal di testa e mal di gola da ieri. Bwon-JOR-no. Non sto MOL-to BEH-neh. O mal dee TEH-sta eh mal dee GO-la da YEH-ree. Good morning. I'm not doing very well. I've had a headache and a sore throat since yesterday.
Farmacista Ha anche la febbre? A AN-keh la FEB-breh? Do you also have a fever?
Giulia Un po', sì. Ho bisogno di qualcosa per la gola e per la testa. Oon po, see. O bee-ZO-nyo dee kwal-KO-za pehr la GO-la eh pehr la TEH-sta. A little, yes. I need something for my throat and my head.
Farmacista Ecco. Deve riposare e bere molta acqua. Se la febbre continua, deve prendere un appuntamento con il medico. EK-ko. DEH-veh ree-po-ZA-reh eh BEH-reh MOL-ta AK-kwa. Seh la FEB-breh kon-TEE-nwa, DEH-veh PREN-deh-reh oon a-poon-ta-MEN-to kon eel MEH-dee-ko. Here you go. You should rest and drink plenty of water. If the fever continues, you should make an appointment with the doctor.
Giulia Va bene, grazie mille. Devo telefonare oggi o posso aspettare? Va BEH-neh, GRA-tsee-eh MEEL-leh. DEH-vo teh-leh-fo-NA-reh OJ-jee o PO-so as-peh-TA-reh? Okay, thank you very much. Do I need to call today or can I wait?
Farmacista Può aspettare un giorno o due. Si riguardi! Pwo as-peh-TA-reh oon JOR-no o DOO-eh. See ree-GWAR-dee! You can wait a day or two. Take care of yourself!

Domande di comprensione — Comprehension questions:

  1. What symptoms does Giulia describe?
  2. What advice does the pharmacist give?
  3. Under what condition should Giulia see a doctor?

Compito di scrittura — Writing task: Write six to eight sentences describing how you feel, one symptom, one thing you need, and a question about whether you need an appointment. Include at least one avere expression, one stare expression, and one sentence with dovere or potere.

Lista di controllo — Editing checklist:

  • Avere expressions for symptoms are spelled correctly, including the silent h.
  • stare is used for wellbeing, not essere.
  • devi/deve and puoi/può match the correct address form.
  • Accents and apostrophes are spelled correctly, especially in perché and più.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — The role of the pharmacy

In many Italian communities, a neighborhood pharmacist commonly offers basic advice for minor symptoms before a doctor's visit is needed. Practices vary by region, pharmacy, and type of concern, so this should not be treated as medical guidance or a universal rule.

Culture in Context — Directness and care in health talk

Questions about health often pair practical information with a caring tone, as in the pharmacist's closing Si riguardi! Directness and warmth can appear together; this should not be read as detached or purely clinical.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using essere instead of avere for symptoms

Error: Saying Sono mal di testa, modeled directly on English "I am headache-y" or a literal translation attempt.

Correction: Use the avere pattern: Ho mal di testa. Italian consistently uses avere for this type of physical-state expression.

Common Mistake — Confusing stare and essere for wellbeing

Error: Saying Sono bene to answer Come stai?

Correction: Use Sto bene. stare is the standard verb for describing wellbeing and condition.

Common Mistake — Mixing up devi and puoi

Error: Saying Puoi riposare to mean "You must rest," when the intended meaning is an obligation, not an option.

Correction: Use Devi riposare for obligation or strong advice, and reserve puoi for ability or permission, as in Puoi aspettare un giorno.

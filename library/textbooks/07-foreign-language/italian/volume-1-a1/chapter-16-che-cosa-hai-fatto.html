Che cosa hai fatto?

Volume I, Chapter 16: Completed Events · Speak and Write Italian: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Overview and Objectives

Chapter 16 opens Unit IV by giving you your first real tool for talking about the past: the passato prossimo with avere. Until now you have described what is true or what usually happens. This chapter lets you say what already happened — what you did yesterday, last night, or last week. You will build this new system on vocabulary and sentence patterns you already control from earlier chapters. By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Form the passato prossimo with avere for high-frequency regular and selected irregular verbs.
  2. Ask and answer what someone did using che cosa hai fatto? and related questions.
  3. Use common time markers to say when a completed action happened.
  4. Narrate two or three simple completed actions in sequence.
  5. Form a simple negative statement about the past.
  6. Read and write a short account of recent events.

Communication Scenario

Giulia sent her email and met Marta for coffee, as you read in Chapter 15. Now it is Monday morning, and Marta wants to know how Giulia's weekend went before they both start work.

  1. This chapter teaches you to talk about a weekend, a day, or a recent event using completed-action language.
  2. You will practice this through Marta and Giulia's Monday-morning conversation, then apply the same tools to your own recent experiences.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — the silent h in ho, hai, ha, hanno

The letter h is never pronounced in Italian. Ho sounds exactly like the vowel o, and hai sounds like ai. The h is a spelling device only — it distinguishes ho ("I have") from o ("or") in writing, even though the two words sound identical.

16.1 Auxiliary-plus-participle rhythm

In connected speech, avere and the participle form a single rhythmic unit, with stress falling on the participle: ho guarDAto, hai stuDIAto, ha FATto. Practice saying the auxiliary quickly and lightly, then landing more firmly on the stressed syllable of the participle.

16.2 Stress in past participles

Regular participles in -ato, -uto, and -ito are stressed on the syllable just before the ending: guar-DA-to, ri-ce-VU-to, pu-LI-to. Listen for this pattern as you build new participles from familiar infinitives.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

Language Focus — passato prossimo with avere

The passato prossimo describes a completed action. For most verbs, it is formed with the present tense of avere plus a past participle:

SubjectavereParticipleEnglish
iohoguardatoI watched
tuhaistudiatoyou studied
lui / lei / Leihalavoratohe/she/you (formal) worked
noiabbiamodormitowe slept

Productive focus in this chapter remains on io, tu, and lui/lei/Lei. Noi appears for recognition and limited controlled use.

Language Building Block — regular past participles

Form regular participles by replacing the infinitive ending:

Infinitive endingParticiple endingExample
-are-atoguardare → guardato
-ere-utoricevere → ricevuto
-ire-itodormire → dormito

A small number of high-frequency verbs have irregular participles, such as fatto, visto, letto, scritto, preso, and messo. Learn these as individual items rather than trying to predict them from a rule.

Language Focus — asking about the past

Use these controlled question patterns to ask what someone did:

  • Che cosa hai fatto ieri? — What did you do yesterday?
  • Hai studiato stamattina? — Did you study this morning?
  • Quando hai visto Marco? — When did you see Marco?
Language Focus — negative past

Place non before the auxiliary, exactly as you already do in the present tense:

Non ho guardato la TV ieri sera. — I didn't watch TV last night.
Non abbiamo lavorato il fine settimana scorso. — We didn't work last weekend.

Review — sequence connectors

Prima, poi, dopo, and alla fine (Ch. 7, Ch. 11) now help you order completed actions: Prima ho lavorato, poi ho studiato, e alla fine ho dormito.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Monday morning. Marta and Giulia meet before work, continuing from the events of Chapter 15.

Marta Ciao Giulia! Allora, che cosa hai fatto questo fine settimana? CHA-o Joo-LEE-a! Al-LO-ra, keh KO-za ai FAT-to KWES-to FEE-neh set-tee-MA-na? Hi Giulia! So, what did you do this weekend?
Giulia Sabato ho pulito la casa e ho scritto due email per il lavoro. Poi ho letto un libro. Sa-BA-to o poo-LEE-to la KA-za eh o SKREET-to DOO-eh eh-MAIL per eel la-VO-ro. Poy o LET-to oon LEE-bro. Saturday I cleaned the house and wrote two emails for work. Then I read a book.
Marta Che noia! Io invece ho visto un bel film domenica sera. Keh NO-ya! EE-o een-VEH-cheh o VEES-to oon bel feelm do-MEH-nee-ka SEH-ra. How boring! I, on the other hand, saw a nice movie Sunday night.
Giulia Davvero? Non ho guardato niente questo fine settimana. Hai dormito bene almeno? Da-VEH-ro? Non o gwar-DA-to nee-EN-teh KWES-to FEE-neh set-tee-MA-na. Ai dor-MEE-to BEH-neh al-MEH-no? Really? I didn't watch anything this weekend. Did you sleep well at least?
Marta Sì, abbastanza. Prima ho preso un caffè con mia sorella, poi ho dormito fino a tardi. See, ab-bas-TAN-tsa. PREE-ma o PREH-zo oon ka-FEH kon MEE-a so-REL-la, poy o dor-MEE-to FEE-no a TAR-dee. Yes, enough. First I had a coffee with my sister, then I slept in.

Domande di comprensione — Comprehension questions:

  1. What two things did Giulia do on Saturday?
  2. What did Marta do Sunday night?
  3. What did Marta do before sleeping in?

Speaking Practice

Pratica controllata — Controlled practice: what did you do?

With a partner, take turns asking Che cosa hai fatto ieri? and answering with at least two completed actions.

Compito comunicativo — Communicative task: my weekend

In pairs or small groups, describe your weekend using at least three completed actions, one time marker, and one sequence word (prima, poi, dopo, alla fine). React to your partner's weekend with Che bello!, Davvero?, or Che noia!

Pratica da solo — Solo alternative

Record yourself narrating three things you did yesterday or last week, including at least one negative sentence.

Reading Practice

Read the short diary entry below aloud, sounding out each Italian word slowly.

Un diario — A diary entry
Ieri è stata una giornata piena. Ho lavorato fino alle sei, poi ho preso un caffè con Marta. Dopo ho pulito la casa e ho scritto un'email al mio capo. Alla fine ho letto un po' e ho dormito presto. Non ho guardato la TV.

Note: è stata is a receptive-only form you will study formally when the passato prossimo with essere is introduced in Chapter 17.

Domande di comprensione — Comprehension questions:

  2. Name two things the writer did after cleaning the house.

Writing Workshop

Write a short note describing a recent day.

Compito di scrittura — Writing task: Write five to seven sentences describing what you did yesterday or last weekend. Include at least three completed actions, one time marker, one sequence word, and one negative sentence.

  1. Say when. Open with a time marker such as ieri or il fine settimana scorso.
  2. Narrate the first action. Use ho or hai plus a participle.
  3. Sequence two more actions. Use poi and dopo.
  4. Add a negative sentence. Say one thing you did not do.

Lista di controllo — Editing checklist:

  • The auxiliary avere agrees with the subject (ho, hai, ha).
  • Regular participles use -ato, -uto, or -ito correctly.
  • Irregular participles (fatto, visto, letto, scritto, preso, messo) are spelled correctly.
  • non comes before avere in negative sentences.
  • ho, hai, ha, hanno are spelled with a silent h.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — small talk about the weekend

Asking about someone's weekend is common small talk in many workplaces and among friends, but the level of detail people share varies by relationship, workplace culture, and personal habit. A short, general answer is often just as appropriate as a detailed one.

Culture in Context — reacting to a story

Italian conversation often includes active listener reactions such as Davvero?, Che bello!, or Che noia!. These reactions keep a conversation feeling natural and engaged, and they are not tied to one region or age group.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — writing or saying o instead of ho

Error: Writing O guardato la TV instead of Ho guardato la TV.

Correction: Remember that ho ("I have") is spelled with a silent h to distinguish it from o ("or"), even though they sound the same.

Common Mistake — guessing every participle from the regular pattern

Error: Saying ho fatto as ho facio, or ho visto as ho vedeuto.

Correction: High-frequency irregular participles such as fatto, visto, letto, scritto, preso, and messo must be memorized individually; the regular pattern does not apply to them.

Common Mistake — placing non in the wrong position

Error: Saying Ho non guardato la TV.

Correction: Place non before the auxiliary: Non ho guardato la TV, just as you already do with present-tense verbs.

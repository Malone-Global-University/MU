La giornata quotidiana

Volume I, Chapter 7: Daily Routines · Speak and Write Italian: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Overview and Objectives

Chapter 7 continues Unit II by moving from the home you described in Chapter 6 into the daily life that happens inside it. You will learn the present tense of regular -are, -ere, and -ire verbs, tell time, and describe a typical day from morning to night. By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Use the present tense of regular -are, -ere, and -ire verbs to describe everyday actions.
  2. Tell time and use time expressions such as alle, di mattina, di pomeriggio, and di sera.
  3. Name the days of the week and describe a weekly schedule.
  4. Use a small set of formulaic reflexive expressions, such as mi alzo and mi lavo, to describe your morning.
  5. Say what you usually do and what you do not do, using non before the verb.
  6. Read and write a connected description of a daily routine.

Communication Scenario

A week has passed since Marco helped Aisha unpack her books. Classes have started, and Aisha calls Marco to compare their weekday routines before their first study session together. Aisha explains that she wakes up early, works at the café near her apartment, and studies in the evening. Marco, on the other hand, prefers to sleep later, but he studies at the library every afternoon and does not work in the evenings. As they talk, they agree on the best time in the week to meet and study together.

  1. This chapter teaches you to describe a typical day and compare routines with someone else.
  2. You will practice this through Aisha and Marco's phone call, then apply it to your own weekly schedule.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Verb endings

Present-tense verb endings carry the meaning of who is doing the action, so they must be pronounced clearly and distinctly: lavoro, lavori, lavora. Do not let the final vowel disappear or blur into a schwa sound — each ending is a full, clear Italian vowel.

7.1 Days of the week and stress

Notice that lunedì, martedì, mercoledì, giovedì, and venerdì all carry stress on the final syllable, marked with a written accent: lu-ne-DÌ. Sabato and domenica do not follow this pattern — their stress falls earlier in the word: SA-ba-to, do-ME-ni-ca.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

Language Focus — Present tense of -are verbs

Form: remove -are from the infinitive and add -o, -i, -a, -iamo, -ate, or -ano depending on the subject. This chapter focuses productively on io, tu, lui/lei/Lei, and noi; voi and loro forms are receptive for now.

Meaning: these endings tell the listener who is performing the action without needing a separate subject pronoun.

Use: Io lavoro in un caffè. (I work in a café.) Tu studi molto. (You study a lot.) Marco studia in biblioteca. (Marco studies at the library.) Noi studiamo insieme. (We study together.)

Language Focus — Present tense of -ere and -ire verbs

Form: -ere verbs add -o, -i, -e, and -iamo to the stem; most -ire verbs add -o, -i, -e, and -iamo as well, but some, like finire, insert -isc- before the ending in most singular and third-person plural forms.

Meaning and use: Leggo un libro. (I read a book.) Marco dorme fino a tardi. (Marco sleeps until late.) Finisco di studiare alle nove. (I finish studying at nine.) Language Focus — Telling time

Form: use È l'una for one o'clock and Sono le + number for all other hours. Use alle + hour to say at a given time.

Meaning and use: Sono le sette di mattina. (It's seven in the morning.) Mi alzo alle sette. (I get up at seven.) Ceno alle otto di sera. (I have dinner at eight in the evening.) Combine time expressions with di mattina, di pomeriggio, and di sera when the hour alone could be ambiguous.

Language Focus — Negation with a verb

Form: place non directly before the conjugated verb.

Meaning and use: Non lavoro la domenica. (I don't work on Sundays.) Marco non si alza presto. (Marco doesn't get up early.) non always comes immediately before the verb, never after it.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Aisha calls Marco to compare their weekday routines, described in the Communication Scenario above.

Aisha Ciao Marco! Com'è la tua giornata di solito? Chow Mar-ko! Ko-MEH la TOO-a jor-NA-ta dee SO-lee-to? Hi Marco! What's your day usually like?
Marco Mi alzo alle otto e mi preparo con calma. Poi studio in biblioteca di pomeriggio. Mee AL-tso AL-leh OT-to eh mee pre-PA-ro kon KAL-ma. Po-ee STOO-dyo een bee-blyo-TEH-ka dee po-meh-REEJ-jo. I get up at eight and get ready calmly. Then I study at the library in the afternoon.
Aisha Io invece mi alzo presto, alle sei e mezza. Lavoro al bar di mattina e studio di sera. EE-oh een-VEH-che mee AL-tso PREH-sto, AL-leh say eh MED-dza. La-VO-ro al bar dee mat-TEE-na eh STOO-dyo dee SEH-ra. I, on the other hand, get up early, at six thirty. I work at the café in the morning and study in the evening.
Marco Non lavori mai la sera? Non la-VO-ree MAH-ee la SEH-ra? Do you never work in the evening?
Aisha No, il bar chiude alle sei. Dopo il lavoro, prendo un caffè e leggo un po'. No, eel bar KYOO-deh AL-leh say. DO-po eel la-VO-ro, PREN-do oon kaf-FEH eh LEG-go oon poh. No, the café closes at six. After work, I have a coffee and read a little.
Marco E quando finisci di studiare? Eh KWAN-do fee-NEE-shee dee stoo-DYA-reh? And when do you finish studying?
Aisha Finisco verso le dieci. Poi ceno e dormo presto. Fee-NEE-sko VEHR-so leh DYEH-chee. Po-ee CHEH-no eh DOR-mo PREH-sto. I finish around ten. Then I have dinner and go to sleep early.
Marco Allora, studiamo insieme mercoledì pomeriggio? Al-LO-ra, stoo-DYA-mo een-SYEH-meh mer-ko-le-DEE po-meh-REEJ-jo? So, shall we study together Wednesday afternoon?
Aisha Perfetto, non lavoro mercoledì pomeriggio! Per-FET-to, non la-VO-ro mer-ko-le-DEE po-meh-REEJ-jo! Perfect, I don't work Wednesday afternoon!

Domande di comprensione — Comprehension questions:

  1. What time does Marco get up, and what does he do afterward?
  2. What time does Aisha get up, and when does she work and study?
  3. What day and time do Aisha and Marco agree to study together?

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Daily schedules vary

Work hours, school hours, and mealtimes vary considerably across Italy and other Italian-speaking regions, and also depend on profession, household, and personal circumstance. Avoid assuming that any single description in this chapter represents "the" Italian day.

Culture in Context — The 24-hour clock

Italian often uses the 24-hour clock in schedules, transportation timetables, and official documents, even though everyday conversation frequently uses the 12-hour system with di mattina, di pomeriggio, and di sera for clarity. Both systems are normal, and context tells you which is being used.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Wrong verb ending

Error: Saying Io studia instead of the correct form.

Correction: The io form always ends in -o: Io studio.

Common Mistake — Placing non after the verb

Error: Saying Lavoro non la domenica.

Correction: non comes directly before the verb: Non lavoro la domenica.

Common Mistake — Dropping the accent on days of the week

Error: Writing lunedi instead of lunedì.

Correction: lunedì through venerdì carry a required final accent that marks the stressed syllable.

Ripasso 1: Conoscersi

Volume I, Chapter 5: Unit I Review — Getting to Know Each Other · Speak and Write Italian: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

This chapter does not introduce new grammar. By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Greet people, take leave, and manage basic courtesy routines in informal and formal contexts.
  2. Introduce yourself and another person, giving name, origin, residence, and language.
  3. Spell familiar words and identify classroom objects.
  4. Describe two or more people using correct gender, articles, adjective agreement, and avere for age.
  5. Ask and answer come ti chiami?, di dove sei?, chi è?, com'è?, and quanti anni ha/hai?.
  6. Read a short personal or family profile and answer comprehension questions.
  7. Write a connected personal paragraph that introduces yourself and your family or social group.

Communication Scenario

It is the last class of the week, and the instructor asks everyone to move around the room and meet at least two classmates they have not spoken with yet. Aisha and Marco, now comfortable with each other after Chapter 4, split up to practice with new partners. Each conversation follows a familiar pattern: a greeting, an exchange of names, a question about origin or residence, and a quick description of family or friends. By the end of the activity, everyone in the room can introduce at least one classmate to the rest of the group.

  1. This chapter gives you no new grammar. Instead, it asks you to combine everything from Chapters 1 through 4 into complete, connected interactions.
  2. Use this chapter to check how confidently you can move between greetings, identity, spelling, and family description without stopping to think about each piece separately.

Where This Chapter Fits

Chapter 5 is the Unit I assessment chapter. It reviews and integrates Chapters 1-4 without introducing any major new grammar, vocabulary domain, or pronunciation system. Its purpose is consolidation: you should leave this chapter able to combine greetings, identity, classroom language, and family description into one connected performance.

Coming Into This Chapter

You can greet people and introduce yourself (Chapter 1); spell words and identify classroom objects (Chapter 2); state your origin, residence, and languages (Chapter 3); and describe family members and friends using gender, articles, simple adjective agreement, and avere for age (Chapter 4).

New in This Chapter

Nothing grammatically new. This chapter reorganizes and assesses existing material through listening, speaking, reading, and writing tasks that combine more than one communicative domain at once.

Looking Forward

Pronunciation Review

This chapter does not introduce new pronunciation targets. It consolidates the systems activated in Chapters 1-4.

Pronunciation Focus — Review checklist

As you complete this chapter's listening and speaking tasks, monitor yourself for: clean, undistorted vowels; recognition of stressed syllables in familiar words; correct length on double consonants such as nonno, nonna, and sorella; accurate production of gn and gli in words like famiglia and figlio; and a clear contrast between statement and question intonation.

5.1 Listening for stress and length under pressure

When you speak more quickly in a connected exchange, stress and double consonants are often the first features to slip. Before the speaking assessment below, practice saying sorella, fratello, nonna, and bello at a natural conversational speed, keeping the double consonant slightly longer than a single one.

Grammar Review

This chapter introduces no new grammar. The chart below summarizes what is expected in ordinary productive use at this point in the course.

Language Focus — What you should now control

By this point you should be able to use, without stopping to translate word-by-word: subject pronouns io and tu together with the identity forms of essere (sono, sei) and the age/possession forms of avere (ho, hai, ha); the definite articles il and la and the indefinite articles un and una with familiar consonant-initial nouns; simple adjective agreement in the masculine and feminine singular; and the fixed possessive patterns mio/mia and tuo/tua with singular family nouns.

Model Dialogue

Setting: the classroom "meet a classmate" activity described in the Communication Scenario above.

Marco Ciao! Come ti chiami? Chow! KOH-meh tee KYA-mee? Hi! What's your name?
Sara Mi chiamo Sara. E tu? Mee KYA-mo SAH-ra. Eh too? My name is Sara. And you?
Marco Sono Marco. Di dove sei? SOH-no MAR-ko. Dee DOH-veh SEH-ee? I'm Marco. Where are you from?
Sara Sono di Torino, ma abito a Milano. Ho un fratello e una sorella. SOH-no dee to-REE-no, ma AH-bee-to ah mee-LAH-no. Oh oon fra-TEL-lo eh OO-na so-REL-la. I'm from Turin, but I live in Milan. I have a brother and a sister.
Marco Com'è tua sorella? Ko-MEH TOO-ah so-REL-la? What's your sister like?
Sara È simpatica e molto divertente. Ha diciannove anni. E la tua famiglia? Eh seem-PAH-tee-ka eh MOL-to dee-ver-TEN-teh. Ah dee-chan-NO-veh AN-nee. Eh la TOO-ah fa-MEE-lya? She's nice and very fun. She's nineteen. And your family?
Marco Non ho fratelli, ma ho un amico, Luca, e un cane, Zeus. Piacere di conoscerti, Sara! Non oh fra-TEL-lee, ma oh oon a-MEE-ko, LOO-ka, eh oon KAH-neh, DZEH-oos. Pya-CHEH-reh dee ko-NO-sher-tee, SAH-ra! I don't have siblings, but I have a friend, Luca, and a dog, Zeus. Nice to meet you, Sara!

Domande di comprensione — Comprehension questions:

  1. Where is Sara from, and where does she live now?
  2. How old is Sara's sister, and how is she described?
  3. What does Marco have instead of siblings?

Writing Assessment

Write a connected personal paragraph that introduces yourself and describes your family or a chosen social group, combining everything from Chapters 1-4.

Compito di scrittura — Writing task: Write seven to nine sentences that include: your name; your origin and current residence; at least one language you speak; two or more family members or close friends, each with relationship, age, and one agreeing adjective; and a closing sentence.

  1. Introduce yourself. Use Mi chiamo... and Sono di... / Abito a....
  2. State a language. Use Parlo....
  3. Present your people. Use Ho un/una... and Si chiama... for each person.
  4. Give age and description. Use Ha... anni and È... with a correctly agreeing adjective.

Lista di controllo — Editing checklist:

  • Origin and residence are expressed with the correct preposition (di for origin, a for city residence).
  • Each family or friend noun is paired with the correct article or possessive form.
  • Adjectives ending in -o/-a agree with each person's gender.
  • sono, ho, hai, and ha are matched correctly to their subjects.
  • Every sentence ends with correct punctuation.

Speaking Assessment

Pratica controllata — Controlled practice: full self-introduction

Practice saying, in order and without notes: your name, your origin, your residence, one language you speak, your age, and one family member or friend with their age and one description.

Compito comunicativo — Communicative task: meet a classmate

With a partner, recreate the classroom scenario from this chapter's opening. Greet each other, exchange names, ask about origin and residence, and each describe one family member or friend, including a follow-up question such as Com'è...? or Quanti anni ha...?.

Pratica da solo — Solo alternative

Record a complete self-introduction of sixty to ninety seconds that includes every element from the controlled practice above, then listen back and check your list against what you actually said.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Introductions across settings

The classroom "meet a classmate" activity in this chapter uses informal tu because classmates of similar age typically address each other informally. In a workplace, an academic conference, or a first meeting with an older stranger, the same introduction would likely shift toward formal Lei. Context, age, and relationship continue to shape address choice, just as they did in Chapters 1 and 4.

Culture in Context — Family and friendship in self-introduction

Italian speakers, like speakers of many languages, vary in how much family or personal detail they share in a first conversation. Some people mention family readily; others prefer to talk about interests, work, or study first. The pattern in this chapter's dialogues is a useful classroom model, not a universal rule for every real conversation.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Confusing essere and avere

Error: Mixing up sono (I am) and ho (I have) when stating age or origin, such as Ho di Roma.

Correction: Use essere for identity and origin (Sono di Roma) and avere for age and possession (Ho ventidue anni). Review Chapters 3 and 4.

Common Mistake — Dropping adjective agreement under pressure

Error: Saying Mia sorella è simpatico when speaking quickly in a connected conversation.

Correction: Slow down slightly at the adjective and check the gender of the person being described: Mia sorella è simpatica.

Common Mistake — Skipping the greeting before a question

Error: Asking a new classmate Come ti chiami? with no greeting first.

Correction: Open with a greeting such as Ciao! before asking a question, as modeled in this chapter's dialogue and in Chapter 1.

La famiglia e gli amici

Volume I, Chapter 4: Family and Friends · Speak and Write Italian: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify family members and close relationships using accurate gender and number.
  2. Use the definite articles il, la, i, and le, and the indefinite articles un and una, with high-frequency nouns.
  3. Make simple adjectives agree with masculine and feminine singular nouns.
  4. Use avere to state age and simple possession.
  5. Use possessive words such as mio, mia, tuo, and tua with family terms as fixed, controlled patterns.
  6. Ask and answer Chi è?, Com'è?, and Quanti anni ha/hai?.
  7. Describe two or more people, including their relationship, age, and basic characteristics.

Communication Scenario

Aisha shows Marco a photo on her phone during break. It is a picture from a family gathering in Casablanca, and Marco is curious about who everyone is. Aisha points to her mother, her father, her older brother, and her younger sister, giving a quick description of each. Marco shows Aisha a photo too — of his best friend, Luca, and his dog, Zeus — and Aisha asks how old Luca is and what he is like. By the end of the exchange, both of them know a little more about each other's family and friends.

  1. This chapter gives you the language Aisha and Marco use to point out family members and friends, describe them briefly, and state their ages.
  2. It also gives you your first real grammatical gender and article system, along with your first adjective agreement, both of which you will keep building on for the rest of the course.

Where This Chapter Fits

Chapter 4 builds on the subject pronouns and essere patterns from Chapter 3. It introduces your first systematic look at noun gender and number, your first definite and indefinite articles, and your first adjective agreement, but only in simple masculine and feminine singular patterns tied to people. It does not require plural adjective agreement, the full article system with lo and gli, or the complete possessive system — those continue to develop gradually across the volume.

Coming Into This Chapter

You can already greet people, introduce yourself, spell words, and say where you are from, where you live, and what languages you speak, using io, tu, and the identity forms of essere.

New in This Chapter

Noun gender and number; definite articles il, la, i, and le; indefinite articles un and una; simple adjective agreement; avere for age and possession; and possessive words with family terms as fixed patterns.

Looking Forward

Chapter 5 reviews Unit I. Chapter 6, La casa e il quartiere, introduces c'è and ci sono, location expressions, and an expanded article system.

Pronunciation Focus

This chapter connects earlier stress and vowel-clarity work to family vocabulary, and introduces careful listening for the double consonants that distinguish several common family words.

Pronunciation Focus — Stress in family terms

Most family words in this chapter are stressed on the second-to-last syllable: so-REL-la, fra-TEL-lo, koo-JEE-no. Practice each word slowly before speeding up, and listen for the stressed syllable when your instructor or partner says these words aloud.

Pronunciation Focus — Double consonants in family words

Several family words contain double consonants that must be held slightly longer than a single consonant: nonno, nonna, sorella, bello. A double consonant is not decoration — nono (ninth) and nonno (grandfather) are different words. Practice holding the double consonant briefly longer than you would in English.

4.1 gn and gli in family and description words

Notice gn in insegnante and gli in famiglia and figlio. Both combinations represent single, distinct sounds in Italian, not two separate consonant sounds as an English spelling might suggest. You will meet these combinations again throughout the course; for now, simply listen carefully and imitate your instructor or the audio model.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

4.2 Noun gender and number

Purpose: classify nouns correctly so that articles and adjectives can agree with them.

Language Focus — Grammatical gender

Every Italian noun is either masculine or grammatical feminine. For people, gender usually matches the person's sex, as in il fratello (brother, masculine) and la sorella (sister, feminine). Many masculine nouns end in -o and many feminine nouns end in -a, but this is a helpful tendency, not an absolute rule — you will meet exceptions later in the course. For now, learn the gender of each new noun together with the noun itself, the way the vocabulary table above shows it.

Plural formation is introduced only receptively in this chapter: fratello becomes fratelli, and sorella becomes sorelle. You will study plural patterns directly in a later chapter; for now, notice the pattern when you see it in the reading passage.

4.3 Definite and indefinite articles

Purpose: refer to specific or nonspecific people and things.

Language Building Block — Articles with singular nouns
il fratello · la sorella · un fratello · una sorella
Use il before most masculine singular nouns and la before most feminine singular nouns to mean "the." Use un before masculine singular nouns and una before feminine singular nouns to mean "a" or "an." This chapter focuses on il, la, un, and una with ordinary consonant-initial nouns. Nouns beginning with a vowel, or with z, s + consonant, or gn — such as lo zio and l'amico — are treated here as fixed forms to learn item by item; the full pattern is explained in a later chapter.
Il fratello di Marco si chiama Luca. Aisha ha una sorella.
Marco's brother is named Luca. Aisha has a sister.

The plural definite articles i (masculine) and le (feminine) appear in this chapter only in a few fixed, high-frequency phrases such as i miei genitori ("my parents"); full plural article use develops later.

4.4 Simple adjective agreement

Purpose: describe a person accurately according to their gender.

Grammar Note — Adjectives that change and adjectives that do not

Adjectives ending in -o in the masculine, such as alto, change to -a for a feminine subject: Marco è alto. Aisha è alta. Adjectives ending in -e, such as gentile and intelligente, do not change between masculine and feminine singular subjects: Luca è gentile. Sara è gentile. This chapter practices only the singular forms; plural adjective agreement is introduced later.

  • Mio fratello è alto e simpatico. MEE-oh fra-TEL-lo eh AHL-to eh seem-PAH-tee-ko. My brother is tall and nice.
  • Mia sorella è bassa e divertente. MEE-ah so-REL-la eh BAS-sa eh dee-ver-TEN-teh. My sister is short and fun.

4.5 Avere for age and possession

Purpose: state age and say what someone has.

Language Focus — avere in this chapter

Italian uses avere ("to have") to state age, where English uses "to be": Ho ventidue anni literally means "I have twenty-two years," not "I am twenty-two years." This chapter introduces ho ("I have"), hai ("you have," informal), and ha ("he/she has," and formal "you have"). You already saw avere briefly in Chapter 2 and 3; here it becomes a real productive pattern for the first time.

Remember the silent h in ho, hai, and ha: the letter is written but not pronounced. Do not confuse ho (I have) with o (or) in writing or in careful speech.

4.6 Possessives with family terms: mio, mia, tuo, tua

Purpose: express simple possession with family members.

  • Mio padre è alto. MEE-oh PAH-dreh eh AHL-to. My father is tall.
  • Tua madre è simpatica? TOO-ah MAH-dreh eh seem-PAH-tee-ka? Is your mother nice?

Notice that mio/tuo pair with masculine family nouns and mia/tua pair with feminine family nouns, and that no article appears before a singular family noun in these fixed patterns (mio padre, not il mio padre). Treat this as a controlled, formulaic pattern for now. The complete possessive system, including plural family nouns and other categories of nouns, is introduced in a later volume.

4.7 Asking about people: chi è?, com'è?, quanti anni ha/hai?

Purpose: ask who someone is, what they are like, and how old they are.

Grammar Note — Three useful questions

Chi è? asks "Who is it/he/she?" Com'è? asks "What is he/she like?" and combines come with è. Quanti anni ha/hai? asks age, using ha for someone else (or formally) and hai for a friend.

Model Dialogue

Setting: break time, near the classroom window. Aisha is showing Marco a photo on her phone.

Marco Chi è questa? È tua sorella? Kee eh KWES-ta? Eh TOO-ah so-REL-la? Who is this? Is she your sister?
Aisha Sì, è mia sorella. Si chiama Yasmine. È giovane, ha dodici anni. See, eh MEE-ah so-REL-la. See KYA-ma Yas-MEEN. Eh JOH-va-neh, ah DOH-dee-chee AN-nee. Yes, that's my sister. Her name is Yasmine. She's young, she's twelve.
Marco E questo, chi è? Tuo fratello? Eh KWES-toh, kee eh? TOO-oh fra-TEL-lo? And this one, who is he? Your brother?
Aisha Sì, è mio fratello Karim. Ha ventidue anni ed è molto alto. Com'è tuo fratello? See, eh MEE-oh fra-TEL-lo Ka-REEM. Ah ven-tee-DOO-eh AN-nee ed eh MOL-to AHL-to. Ko-MEH TOO-oh fra-TEL-lo? Yes, that's my brother Karim. He's twenty-two and very tall. What's your brother like?
Marco Non ho un fratello, ma ho un amico, Luca, e un cane, Zeus! Non oh oon fra-TEL-lo, ma oh oon a-MEE-ko, LOO-ka, eh oon KAH-neh, DZEH-oos! I don't have a brother, but I have a friend, Luca, and a dog, Zeus!
Aisha Quanti anni ha Luca? Ed è simpatico? KWAN-tee AN-nee ah LOO-ka? Ed eh seem-PAH-tee-ko? How old is Luca? And is he nice?
Marco Ha ventitré anni. È molto simpatico e divertente! Ah ven-tee-TREH AN-nee. Eh MOL-to seem-PAH-tee-ko eh dee-ver-TEN-teh! He's twenty-three. He's very nice and fun!

Domande di comprensione — Comprehension questions:

  1. Who is Yasmine, and how old is she?
  2. How old is Karim, and how is he described?
  3. Does Marco have a brother? What does he have instead?

Reading Practice

La famiglia di Tom — Tom's family
Mi chiamo Tom. Ho una sorella e un fratello. Mia sorella si chiama Emma: ha ventisei anni ed è alta e intelligente. Mio fratello si chiama Jack: ha quattordici anni, è giovane e molto divertente. I miei genitori abitano negli Stati Uniti. Mia madre è insegnante e mio padre è studente universitario.
My name is Tom. I have a sister and a brother. My sister's name is Emma: she's twenty-six and tall and intelligent. My brother's name is Jack: he's fourteen, he's young and very fun. My parents live in the United States. My mother is a teacher and my father is a university student.

Writing Workshop

Write a short description of your family or a chosen social group, following the model text above.

Compito di scrittura — Writing task: Write five to seven sentences: name at least two family members or close friends, state each person's relationship to you, give each person's age using avere, and describe each person with at least one adjective that correctly agrees in gender.

  1. Name your people. Use Ho un fratello, Ho una sorella, Ho un'amica, or similar.
  2. Name each person. Use Si chiama... from Chapter 1.
  3. State each person's age. Use Ha... anni.
  4. Describe each person. Use È... with an adjective that matches the person's gender.

Lista di controllo — Editing checklist:

  • Each family noun is paired with the correct article or possessive form (mio fratello, mia sorella).
  • Adjectives ending in -o/-a agree with the person's gender.
  • ho, hai, and ha are matched correctly to the subject, with the silent h spelled correctly.
  • Each sentence ends with correct punctuation.

Speaking Practice

Pratica controllata — Controlled practice: age and description

Practice saying your own age with Ho... anni and describing yourself with one adjective. Then practice asking a partner Quanti anni hai? and Come sei?.

Compito comunicativo — Communicative task: show and tell

With a partner, show a real or invented photo (or simply describe one from memory). Point out two or three people, state who they are, how old they are, and one thing they are like. Your partner should ask at least one follow-up question using Chi è?, Com'è?, or Quanti anni ha?.

Pratica da solo — Solo alternative

Record yourself describing two or three family members or friends: their relationship to you, their age, and one description each. Then invent a description of a fictional family for extra practice.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Many kinds of family

Families in Italian-speaking communities take many forms, just as they do elsewhere: nuclear families, extended families living close together, blended families, single-parent households, and close friend groups that function like family. This chapter's vocabulary describes common relationships, but no single family structure should be treated as the only normal one.

Culture in Context — Asking about age

Quanti anni hai? is a common and ordinary question among friends, classmates, and family members. In more formal or professional settings, asking a stranger's age directly can feel intrusive, just as it can in English-speaking contexts. Context and relationship affect how naturally this question is used.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Using essere instead of avere for age

Error: Saying Sono dodici anni, following the English pattern "I am twelve years."

Correction: Italian uses avere for age: Ho dodici anni. Review section 4.5 above.

Common Mistake — Forgetting adjective agreement

Error: Saying Mia sorella è alto instead of Mia sorella è alta.

Correction: Adjectives ending in -o/-a must match the gender of the person described. A feminine subject needs the -a ending: alta, simpatica, timida.

Common Mistake — Adding an article before mio/mia with singular family nouns

Error: Saying il mio fratello instead of mio fratello.

Correction: With most singular family nouns, the article is dropped before mio, mia, tuo, and tua: mio fratello, mia sorella. Treat this as a fixed pattern for now; the complete rule, including exceptions, is covered in a later volume.

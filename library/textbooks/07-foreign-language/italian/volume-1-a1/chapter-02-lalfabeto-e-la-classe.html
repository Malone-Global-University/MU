L'alfabeto e la classe

Volume I, Chapter 2: Letters and Classroom · Speak and Write Italian: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Recite the Italian alphabet and use letter names to spell words aloud.
  2. Identify common classroom objects and study materials.
  3. Ask what something is called using Che cos'è? and ask what a word means using Che cosa significa?.
  4. Ask how a word is spelled using Come si scrive?.
  5. Point out an object using questo or questa.
  6. Follow common classroom instructions and ask politely for repetition.
  7. Count from 0 to 20 in Italian.
  8. Label a small set of classroom objects using correct spelling.

Communication Scenario

Class is now underway. Professoressa Bianchi holds up a notebook and asks the class what it is called in Italian. Marco recognizes the word but is not sure how to spell it, so he asks her to spell it aloud, letter by letter. Sara, sitting nearby, points to her own pencil case and practices naming what is inside it. When the professoressa speaks a little too quickly, a student politely asks her to repeat what she said. By the end of the lesson, everyone in the room can name the objects on their desk, spell familiar words aloud, and ask a few basic questions about the classroom.

  1. This chapter gives you the language Marco, Sara, and their classmates use to name objects, spell words, and ask simple questions in the classroom.
  2. It also gives you your first tool for learning independently: the ability to ask what something is called, what it means, and how it is written.

Where This Chapter Fits

Chapter 2 builds directly on the greetings and introductions from Chapter 1. It adds the Italian alphabet, classroom vocabulary, and three classroom survival questions that you will use throughout the rest of the course whenever you need help. It does not require the full article system, full noun plural rules, or complete demonstrative agreement — those systems continue to develop gradually starting in the next few chapters.

Coming Into This Chapter

You can already greet people, introduce yourself, ask a name, and use a few courtesy expressions from Chapter 1.

New in This Chapter

The Italian alphabet and spelling; classroom objects and study materials; Che cos'è?, Che cosa significa?, and Come si scrive? as fixed question patterns; questo and questa for pointing something out; classroom commands as formulaic language; and numbers 0–20.

Looking Forward

Pronunciation Focus

Chapter 1 introduced clear vowels, full final vowels, and word stress as something to notice. This chapter connects those sounds to Italian spelling for the first time, focusing on the letters of the alphabet and a few spelling patterns you will meet constantly throughout the course.

Pronunciation Focus — The Italian alphabet

The Italian alphabet has 21 native letters. The letters j, k, w, x, and y appear mainly in loanwords, foreign names, and some scientific terms, as in computer and yogurt. When Italian speakers spell a word aloud, they say each letter name clearly and often add a well-known name or word to clarify a letter, similar to saying "S as in Sam" in English.

Pronunciation Focus — c and g before different vowels

c and g are pronounced differently depending on the vowel that follows. Before a, o, and u, they are "hard," as in computer and lavagna. Before e and i, they are "soft," as in the first sound of cinque and the g of gente (people). This is a recognition target for now; you will practice it more directly in later chapters.

2.1 Digraphs and trigraphs: ch, gh, sc, gn, gli

A few Italian letter combinations represent single sounds. ch and gh keep the hard sound of c and g before e and i, as in che. gn, heard in lavagna, has no exact English equivalent; it is pronounced with the tongue against the roof of the mouth, similar to the middle sound of "onion." gli, heard in foglio, is likewise a single sound without a close English match. For now, listen for these combinations in the vocabulary above; full production practice continues across the volume.

2.2 Spelling and pronunciation are related but not identical

Italian spelling supports pronunciation more consistently than English spelling does, but the two systems are still not the same thing. Knowing how a word is spelled does not by itself guarantee natural pronunciation, and knowing how a word sounds does not by itself guarantee correct spelling. This is why Come si scrive? is a useful question even for words you can already say.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

2.3 Asking what something is: Che cos'è?

Purpose: ask what an object is called in Italian.

Language Building Block — Asking what something is
Che cos'è? — È un/una...
Che cos'è? is a fixed question meaning "What is this/that?" È means "it is." Treat this exchange as a complete formula for now; you will study the full present tense of essere beginning in Chapter 3.
Che cos'è? — È un quaderno.
What is this? — It's a notebook.

2.4 Asking what a word means: Che cosa significa?

Purpose: ask for the meaning of an unfamiliar word.

  • Che cosa significa "quaderno"? Keh KOH-za see-NYEE-fee-ka kwa-DER-no? What does "quaderno" mean?

2.5 Asking how a word is spelled: Come si scrive?

Purpose: ask how a word is written, letter by letter.

Language Focus — Come si scrive?

Come si scrive? literally means "How is it written?" It is one of the most useful classroom and real-world survival questions in this chapter, since it lets you ask for the spelling of any name or word you hear. To answer, a speaker names the letters one at a time using the Italian alphabet, for example: M-A-R-C-O. Treat the question as a fixed formula for now; its grammar becomes clearer once you study reflexive and impersonal verb patterns in later volumes.

  • Come si scrive "Bianchi"? KOH-meh see SKREE-veh Byan-kee? How do you spell "Bianchi"?

2.6 Pointing something out: questo and questa

Purpose: identify a nearby object as "this."

Grammar Note — questo and questa as fixed identification formulas

Questo and questa both mean "this." Questo is used with words like libro and quaderno, and questa is used with words like penna and matita. This chapter treats the choice between the two as a fixed pairing to memorize with each noun, not as a rule you apply on your own yet. The reason for the difference — a noun-classification system called grammatical gender — becomes a full topic in Chapter 4.

  • Questo è un libro. KWESS-toh eh oon LEE-bro. This is a book.
  • Questa è una penna. KWESS-ta eh OO-na PEN-na. This is a pen.

2.7 Classroom commands and polite clarification

Purpose: follow classroom instructions and ask politely for repetition.

The classroom commands in the vocabulary table above are formulaic: recognize and respond to them physically or verbally, but do not analyze their verb forms yet. Puoi ripetere, per favore? is a polite, complete way to ask someone to repeat what they said.

  • Puoi ripetere, per favore? PWOH-ee ree-PEH-teh-reh, per fa-VOH-reh? Can you repeat, please?

Model Dialogue

Setting: Professoressa Bianchi holds up a notebook in front of the class.

Prof.ssa Bianchi Buongiorno a tutti! Allora, che cos'è questo? Bwon-JOR-no a TOOT-tee! Al-LOH-ra, keh koh-ZEH KWESS-toh? Good morning, everyone! So, what is this?
Marco È un quaderno! Eh oon kwa-DER-no! It's a notebook!
Prof.ssa Bianchi Esatto! Come si scrive "quaderno"? Eh-ZAT-toh! KOH-meh see SKREE-veh kwa-DER-no? Exactly! How do you spell "quaderno"?
Marco Q-U-A-D-E-R-N-O. koo — oo — a — dee — eh — er-reh — en-neh — oh Q-U-A-D-E-R-N-O.

A moment later, Sara points to her own bag.

Sara Professoressa, che cosa significa "zaino"? Pro-fes-so-RES-sa, keh KOH-za see-NYEE-fee-ka DZAH-ee-no? Professor, what does "zaino" mean?
Prof.ssa Bianchi Significa "backpack." Questo è uno zaino. See-NYEE-fee-ka "backpack." KWESS-toh eh OO-no DZAH-ee-no. It means "backpack." This is a backpack.
Sara Puoi ripetere, per favore? PWOH-ee ree-PEH-teh-reh, per fa-VOH-reh? Can you repeat, please?

Esatto ("exactly/correct") appears here receptively, glossed for context only.

Domande di comprensione — Comprehension questions:

  1. What object does Professoressa Bianchi hold up first?
  2. Come si scrive "quaderno"? (How is "quaderno" spelled?)
  3. Why does Sara ask Puoi ripetere, per favore?

Writing Workshop

Create a short labeled inventory of five objects, following the model texts above.

Compito di scrittura — Writing task: Choose five classroom objects from the vocabulary list and write a sentence naming each one, using Questo è... or Questa è....

  1. Choose five objects. Select from the vocabulary table above.
  2. Match the demonstrative. Use Questo è... or Questa è... according to the pairing you memorized with each noun.
  3. Check your spelling. Compare each word against the vocabulary table, paying special attention to double letters such as in penna.
  4. Add a title. Give your inventory a simple heading, such as Il mio banco ("My desk").

Lista di controllo — Editing checklist:

  • Each sentence uses questo or questa matched correctly to the noun.
  • Spelling matches the vocabulary list, including double consonants and the combinations gn and gli where relevant.
  • Each sentence ends with correct punctuation.
  • The list includes exactly five objects.

Speaking Practice

Pratica controllata — Controlled practice: naming objects

Point to five real or pictured classroom objects and name each one using Questo è... or Questa è.... Pay attention to the hard and soft sounds of c in words like computer and matita.

Compito comunicativo — Communicative task: ask, answer, and spell

With a partner, take turns holding up or naming an object. The first partner asks Che cos'è?, the second answers, and the first partner then asks Come si scrive? and checks the spelling aloud.

Pratica da solo — Solo alternative

Record yourself naming five objects around you in Italian, spelling one of them aloud letter by letter, and asking Che cosa significa? about an unfamiliar word from this chapter.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Spelling aloud is a real-life skill

Spelling a word or name aloud is not only a classroom exercise. Italian speakers spell names over the phone, at the post office, when booking travel, and when filling out forms, often adding a familiar word to clarify a letter, much like "S as in Sam" in English. Learning to spell confidently in Italian will be useful well beyond the classroom.

Culture in Context — Classroom norms vary

Classroom expectations, including how formally students address an instructor and how questions are asked, vary by country, institution, age group, and individual teacher. The classroom commands and clarification requests in this chapter are broadly useful, but always observe the norms of your own specific learning setting.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Treating c and g as always "hard"

Error: Pronouncing c and g the same way in every word, regardless of the vowel that follows.

Correction: Remember that c and g are soft before e and i, and hard before a, o, and u. Review the Pronunciation Focus box above.

Common Mistake — Dropping the double consonant

Error: Pronouncing or spelling penna as if it had only one n.

Correction: Double consonants in Italian affect both spelling and pronunciation. Hold the consonant sound slightly longer, and always write both letters. You will study this system in more depth in later chapters.

Common Mistake — Guessing questo or questa at random

Error: Choosing questo or questa without connecting it to the specific noun.

Correction: For now, memorize each noun together with its matching form, as shown in the vocabulary table. The underlying system, grammatical gender, becomes a full topic in Chapter 4.

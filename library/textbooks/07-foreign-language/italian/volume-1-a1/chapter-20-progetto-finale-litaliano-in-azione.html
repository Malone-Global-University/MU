Progetto finale: L'italiano in azione

Volume I, Chapter 20: Final Project: Italian in Action · Speak and Write Italian: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Overview and Objectives

Chapter 20 does not teach anything new. Instead, it asks you to bring together everything you have built across nineteen chapters: greetings and introductions, family and home, daily routines, food and shopping, directions and transportation, leisure and health, school and work, and connected narration about the past, present, and future. This chapter is organized around one continuous scenario rather than a single grammar point, because by this stage your task is not to learn another system — it is to show what you can already do with the Italian you have. By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Introduce yourself and another person using origin, residence, language, and family information.
  2. Describe a home, a neighborhood, and a daily schedule in connected sentences.
  3. Complete a food order and a shopping interaction with appropriate courtesy language.
  4. Ask for and follow directions and discuss transportation options.
  5. Describe a basic health need and a school or work responsibility using scheduled modal verbs.
  6. Make plans and respond to an invitation.
  7. Narrate a short sequence of completed past events using both passato prossimo auxiliaries.
  8. Write a connected paragraph or message that links past, present, and future information with appropriate connectors.

Communication Scenario

Marco is spending a month in Italy. Over the course of one ordinary week, he meets new people, settles into his neighborhood, runs errands, gets around the city, deals with a small health concern, discusses his work project, makes weekend plans, and finally writes home about how everything went.

  1. This chapter follows Marco through a single connected week rather than through separate topics, so that you can practice moving between situations the way a real visitor would.
  2. You will read and listen to pieces of Marco's week, then complete your own version of the same week as your final performance task.

Where This Chapter Fits

Chapter 20 closes Unit IV, Esperienze e progetti, and closes Volume I as a whole. It does not introduce new grammar, vocabulary domains, or text types. Instead, it integrates the systems activated across all four units into one cumulative project and assessment.

Coming Into This Chapter

You can introduce yourself, describe people and places, talk about routines, order and shop, get around a city, discuss health and work, make plans, narrate the past with both auxiliaries, and connect sentences with reason, contrast, and consequence connectors.

In This Chapter

You will apply all of these systems together across one connected week, in an integrated listening, reading, speaking, and writing project, with no new grammar introduced.

Looking Forward

Italian Volume II begins from this A2 foundation and develops broader present-tense control, the imperfect tense, direct and indirect object pronouns, articulated prepositions, and connected narration in more demanding contexts.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — reading your own week aloud

This chapter's pronunciation goal is integration rather than a new sound. As you read and speak, keep monitoring: clear vowels without English-style reduction, pronounced word-final vowels, correct stress on multi-syllable words, and clean double consonants where they occur, such as in quindi, bello, and macchina.

20.1 Review: stress and rhythm across a longer passage

Longer connected speech does not change your stress rules — it just gives you more chances to apply them consistently. Read a full paragraph aloud and check that every word keeps the stress you learned when it was introduced, rather than drifting toward English patterns as the sentence gets longer.

20.2 Review: statement, question, and invitation intonation

Practice moving between statement intonation, question intonation, and the softer rising tone used for invitations and suggestions, since your final speaking task will require you to shift among all three within the same conversation.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Marco meets Elena, a neighbor, in the courtyard of his building on his first weekend in Rome.

Elena Ciao! Sei nuovo nel palazzo? Come ti chiami? CHAH-o! SEH-ee NWO-vo nel pa-LAT-tso? KO-meh tee KYA-mee? Hi! Are you new in the building? What's your name?
Marco Ciao, mi chiamo Marco. Sono di Chicago, ma sono qui per un mese per lavoro. CHAH-o, mee KYA-mo MAR-ko. SO-no dee shee-KA-go, ma SO-no kwee pehr oon MEH-zeh pehr la-VO-ro. Hi, my name is Marco. I'm from Chicago, but I'm here for a month for work.
Elena Benvenuto! Com'è l'appartamento? E il quartiere ti piace? Ben-veh-NOO-to! Ko-MEH lap-par-ta-MEN-to? Eh eel kwar-TYEH-reh tee PYA-cheh? Welcome! How is the apartment? And do you like the neighborhood?
Marco Sì, molto. È piccolo ma tranquillo, e ci sono un mercato e un bar vicino. Purtroppo oggi ho avuto un piccolo problema: sono andato in farmacia perché ho mal di testa. See, MOL-to. Eh PEEK-ko-lo ma tran-KWEEL-lo, eh chee SO-no oon mer-KA-to eh oon bar vee-CHEE-no. Poor-TROP-po OJ-jee o a-VOO-to oon PEEK-ko-lo pro-BLEH-ma: SO-no an-DA-to een far-ma-CHEE-a per-KEH o mal dee TEHS-ta. Yes, a lot. It's small but quiet, and there's a market and a bar nearby. Unfortunately today I had a small problem: I went to the pharmacy because I have a headache.
Elena Mi dispiace! Stai meglio adesso? Domani sera facciamo una cena tra vicini, se vuoi venire. Mee dees-PYA-cheh! STAH-ee MEL-yo a-DES-so? Do-MA-nee SEH-ra fach-CHA-mo OO-na CHEH-na tra vee-CHEE-nee, seh VWO-ee veh-NEE-reh. I'm sorry! Are you feeling better now? Tomorrow evening we're having a dinner among neighbors, if you want to come.
Marco Sto molto meglio, grazie. Mi piacerebbe venire! A che ora devo arrivare? STO MOL-to MEL-yo, GRA-tsyeh. Mee pya-che-REB-beh veh-NEE-reh! A keh O-ra DEH-vo ar-ree-VA-reh? I'm feeling much better, thanks. I'd love to come! What time do I have to arrive?

Domande di comprensione — Comprehension questions:

  1. Why is Marco in Rome, and how does he describe his apartment and neighborhood?
  2. What problem did Marco have, and how did he resolve it?
  3. What does Elena invite Marco to do?

Un messaggio di Marco alla famiglia — A message from Marco to his family
Cari tutti, vi racconto la mia prima settimana a Roma! Abito in un piccolo appartamento in un quartiere tranquillo, con un mercato e un bar vicino. Ho conosciuto una vicina simpatica, Elena, e sabato sono andato a una cena organizzata da lei con altri vicini. Durante la settimana ho lavorato molto: ho scritto email, ho finito un progetto importante e sono andato al lavoro quasi sempre in autobus, perché la macchina di un collega non funzionava. Giovedì ho avuto un piccolo problema — mal di testa tutto il giorno — però sono andato in farmacia e alla fine sto bene. La prossima settimana voglio visitare il centro storico e vorrei anche imparare qualche parola in più. Un abbraccio a tutti, Marco
Dear everyone, let me tell you about my first week in Rome! I live in a small apartment in a quiet neighborhood, with a market and a bar nearby. I met a nice neighbor, Elena, and on Saturday I went to a dinner she organized with other neighbors. During the week I worked a lot: I wrote emails, finished an important project, and went to work almost always by bus, because a colleague's car wasn't working. Thursday I had a small problem — a headache all day — but I went to the pharmacy and in the end I'm fine. Next week I want to visit the historic center and I'd also like to learn a few more words. A hug to everyone, Marco

  1. How does Marco describe his neighborhood and his new neighbor?
  2. What problem did Marco have during the week, and how was it resolved?
  3. What does Marco plan to do next week?

Writing Workshop

Write a connected paragraph or message that tells your own version of a week like Marco's, combining information about yourself, your surroundings, your routine, a transaction or request, a problem and its resolution, and a plan.

Compito di scrittura — Writing task: Write eight to ten sentences describing a real or imagined week. Include: an introduction of yourself, a description of where you live, at least one daily-routine or preference sentence, at least one request or transaction, at least two passato prossimo sentences (with both auxiliaries if possible), at least two connectors, and a plan for the future using present tense.

  1. Introduce yourself. State your name, origin, and where you are living or staying.
  2. Describe your surroundings and routine. Use c'è/ci sono and present-tense routine verbs.
  3. Include a transaction or request. Use vorrei, a price question, or a polite request.
  4. Narrate at least two completed events. Use passato prossimo with correct auxiliary choice, linked with a connector such as però or quindi.
  5. State a plan. Use the present tense for a scheduled future event.
  6. Close appropriately. Use a tone-appropriate opening and closing for your chosen audience.

Lista di controllo — Editing checklist:

  • Subject-verb agreement is correct in present-tense and passato prossimo sentences.
  • Auxiliary choice (avere or essere) is correct, with participle agreement where needed.
  • Articles, gender, and number agreement are consistent throughout.
  • At least two connectors link ideas clearly and are not overused.
  • Accents, apostrophes, and capitalization follow standard Italian spelling.
  • Register and address choices match the intended audience.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — neighbors, invitations, and everyday hospitality

Being welcomed by neighbors, invited to a shared meal, or offered small favors is common in many Italian communities, though the frequency, formality, and warmth of such gestures vary by region, building, generation, and personal circumstance. Avoid assuming that every neighborhood or every person behaves the same way.

Culture in Context — moving between registers in one day

A single day may require several different registers: an informal chat with a neighbor, a polite request at a pharmacy, and a professional exchange at work. Adjusting tone and address across these situations is a normal, expected skill, not a sign of inconsistency.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — mixing up auxiliary choice under pressure

Error: Writing Ho andato al mercato instead of the correct auxiliary for a movement verb.

Correction: Use essere with movement and change-of-state verbs learned in Chapters 16-17, such as Sono andato al mercato, and check participle agreement with the subject.

Common Mistake — overloading one sentence with every system at once

Error: Trying to fit an introduction, a past narration, a request, and a plan into a single long sentence.

Correction: Use several shorter, clearly connected sentences instead, the way Marco's message does, so each idea remains easy to follow.

Common Mistake — reaching for untaught grammar under pressure

Error: Attempting to use the imperfect, the subjunctive, or independent object-pronoun placement because the final project feels like it needs "more advanced" Italian.

Correction: The final project is designed to be completed entirely with Volume I systems. Reach for the connectors, tenses, and patterns you have already learned rather than guessing at untaught forms.

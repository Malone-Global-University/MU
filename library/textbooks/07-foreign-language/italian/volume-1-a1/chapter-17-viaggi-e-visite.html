Viaggi e visite

Volume I, Chapter 17: Travel Experiences · Speak and Write Italian: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Overview and Objectives

Chapter 17 continues Unit IV by giving you the second half of the passato prossimo system: the auxiliary essere. In Chapter 16 you learned to narrate completed actions with avere. Now you will learn that a small set of high-frequency verbs — mostly verbs of movement and change of state — use essere instead, and that their participles must agree with the subject in gender and number. This chapter lets you talk about trips, visits, arrivals, and departures. By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Form the passato prossimo with essere for common movement and change-of-state verbs.
  2. Make the past participle agree with the subject in gender and number when essere is the auxiliary.
  3. Distinguish, in scheduled high-frequency verbs, which verbs take avere and which take essere.
  4. Describe a trip or visit, including where you went and with whom.
  5. Ask and answer follow-up questions about a trip using dove, quando, con chi, and come.
  6. Read and write a short account of a trip or visit using sequence markers.

Communication Scenario

Marta told Giulia about her quiet weekend in Chapter 16. Now Giulia remembers that Marta recently visited her family in Bologna, and she wants to hear about the trip.

  1. This chapter teaches you to talk about a trip or visit using the passato prossimo with essere.
  2. You will practice this through Giulia and Marta's conversation about Marta's visit to Bologna, then apply the same tools to your own travel experiences.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — hearing the agreement ending

When essere is the auxiliary, the final vowel of the participle changes to agree with the subject: andato, andata, andati, andate. These endings are pronounced clearly and distinctly — do not let the final vowel weaken or disappear, since it is the only signal in speech that tells you whether the speaker is masculine, feminine, singular, or plural.

17.1 Auxiliary rhythm with essere

Just as with avere, the auxiliary and participle form one rhythmic unit, with stress on the participle: sono anDAto, sei parTIta, è arriVAto. Practice moving lightly through the auxiliary and landing on the stressed syllable of the participle.

17.2 Listening for agreement endings

Because the participle ending carries information about the subject, listen carefully for the final vowel: è partito (he left) versus è partita (she left) sound different only in that final syllable. This is a genuine listening skill, not just a spelling rule.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

Language Focus — passato prossimo with essere

A small group of high-frequency verbs — mostly verbs of movement and change of state — form the passato prossimo with essere instead of avere. When essere is the auxiliary, the past participle agrees with the subject in gender and number, just like an adjective:

SubjectessereParticipleEnglish
io (m.)sonoandatoI went
io (f.)sonoandataI went
tu (m.)seipartitoyou left
luièarrivatohe arrived
lei / Lei (f.)èarrivatashe / you (formal) arrived

Productive focus in this chapter remains on io, tu, and lui/lei/Lei, with correct singular agreement. Plural agreement (andati/andate) is receptive only at this stage.

Language Building Block — which verbs take essere?

You do not need to memorize a formal rule yet. For now, learn this scheduled list of high-frequency essere verbs as a set:

andare, venire, arrivare, partire, uscire, entrare, tornare, restare

Nearly every other verb you have learned so far — mangiare, lavorare, studiare, fare, vedere, leggere, scrivere, prendere, mettere, and others — uses avere, as you learned in Chapter 16.

Language Focus — travel prepositions in the past

Combine familiar prepositions with the new auxiliary to say where someone went, came from, or arrived:

  • Sono andata a Bologna. — I went to Bologna.
  • Sei venuto da Milano? — Did you come from Milan?
  • È arrivata in treno. — She arrived by train.
  • Ho viaggiato con mia sorella. — I traveled with my sister. (viaggiare uses avere)
Language Focus — follow-up questions about a trip

Use these controlled question patterns to ask about someone's trip:

  • Dove sei andata? — Where did you go?
  • Quando sei partito? — When did you leave?
  • Con chi sei andata? — Who did you go with?
  • Com'è andata? — How did it go?
Review — negation and sequencing with essere

Non still comes directly before the auxiliary: Non sono partita venerdì. — I didn't leave Friday. Sequence connectors from Chapter 16 continue to work the same way: Prima sono arrivata, poi sono uscita con i miei parenti.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Giulia and Marta continue their Monday-morning conversation from Chapter 16.

Giulia Marta, non sei andata a Bologna il mese scorso? Com'è andata la visita? MAR-ta, non sey an-DA-ta a bo-LO-nya eel MEH-zeh SKOR-so? Ko-MEH an-DA-ta la vee-ZEE-ta? Marta, didn't you go to Bologna last month? How did the visit go?
Marta Sì! Sono partita venerdì sera e sono arrivata sabato mattina presto. See! So-no par-TEE-ta veh-ner-DEE SEH-ra eh SO-no ar-ree-VA-ta SA-ba-to mat-TEE-na PRES-to. Yes! I left Friday night and arrived Saturday early morning.
Giulia Con chi sei andata? Da sola? Kon kee sey an-DA-ta? Da SO-la? Who did you go with? Alone?
Marta No, sono andata con mio fratello. Prima siamo usciti con i nostri genitori, poi siamo tornati a casa loro per cena. No, SO-no an-DA-ta kon MEE-o fra-TEL-lo. PREE-ma see-A-mo oo-SHEE-tee kon ee NO-stree jeh-nee-TO-ree, poy see-A-mo tor-NA-tee a KA-za LO-ro per CHEH-na. No, I went with my brother. First we went out with our parents, then we went back to their house for dinner.
Giulia Che bello! E quando sei tornata a casa? Keh BEL-lo! Eh KWAN-do sey tor-NA-ta a KA-za? How nice! And when did you get back home?
Marta Sono tornata domenica sera. Non sono restata di più perché lunedì ho lavorato. SO-no tor-NA-ta do-MEH-nee-ka SEH-ra. Non SO-no res-TA-ta dee pyoo per-KEH loo-neh-DEE o la-vo-RA-to. I got back Sunday night. I didn't stay longer because I worked Monday.

Domande di comprensione — Comprehension questions:

  1. When did Marta leave, and when did she arrive?
  2. Who did Marta travel with?
  3. Why didn't Marta stay in Bologna longer?

Speaking Practice

Pratica controllata — Controlled practice: where did you go?

With a partner, take turns asking Dove sei andato/andata l'ultima volta che hai viaggiato? and answering with the correct participle agreement.

Compito comunicativo — Communicative task: my trip

In pairs or small groups, describe a real or imagined trip using at least three essere verbs, one time marker, and one sequence word. Your partner should ask at least one follow-up question with dove, quando, con chi, or come.

Pratica da solo — Solo alternative

Record yourself narrating a short trip, making sure your participles agree correctly with your own gender.

Reading Practice

Read the short travel note below aloud, sounding out each Italian word slowly.

Un messaggio di viaggio — A travel message
Ciao! Sono arrivato a Napoli ieri sera. Il viaggio in treno è stato lungo ma tranquillo. Stamattina sono uscito presto e ho visto il centro della città. Nel pomeriggio sono andato al mare con un amico. Domani mattina parto per Roma.
Hi! I arrived in Naples last night. The train trip was long but calm. This morning I went out early and saw the city center. In the afternoon I went to the beach with a friend. Tomorrow morning I leave for Rome.

Note: è stato appears here as a receptive-only form. Full mastery of essere as its own participle in the passato prossimo is not required at this stage.

Domande di comprensione — Comprehension questions:

  1. When did the writer arrive in Naples?
  2. What did the writer do this morning and this afternoon?
  3. Where is the writer going tomorrow?

Writing Workshop

Write a short note describing a trip or visit.

Compito di scrittura — Writing task: Write five to seven sentences describing a real or imagined trip. Include at least three essere verbs with correct participle agreement, one time marker, one sequence word, and one follow-up detail such as who you went with.

  1. Say when you left. Use sono partito/partita plus a time marker.
  2. Say when you arrived. Use sono arrivato/arrivata.
  3. Sequence two more actions. Use poi and dopo, mixing essere and avere verbs as needed.
  4. Say when you returned. Use sono tornato/tornata.

Lista di controllo — Editing checklist:

  • The auxiliary essere agrees with the subject (sono, sei, è).
  • The participle ending agrees with the subject's gender (-o for masculine, -a for feminine).
  • Verbs like mangiare, vedere, leggere, prendere still use avere, not essere.
  • non comes before essere in negative sentences.
  • Prepositions such as a, da, in, con are used correctly with places and companions.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — visiting family

Visiting relatives in another city or region is common across Italian-speaking communities, though the frequency, distance, and occasion for these visits vary widely by family, region, and personal circumstance. Do not assume every family visits in the same way or for the same reasons.

Culture in Context — train travel

Train travel is a common way to move between Italian cities, and many people share short accounts of their trips as ordinary small talk. Transportation preferences, costs, and travel times vary by region and by the specific route.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — using avere with movement verbs

Error: Saying Ho andato a Bologna instead of Sono andata a Bologna.

Correction: High-frequency movement and change-of-state verbs such as andare, venire, arrivare, partire, uscire, entrare, tornare, and restare use essere, not avere.

Common Mistake — forgetting participle agreement

Error: A female speaker saying Sono andato a Roma.

Correction: With essere, the participle must agree with the subject: a female speaker says Sono andata a Roma.

Common Mistake — mixing up auxiliaries within one narration

Error: Saying Ho arrivata ieri e ho vista il centro.

Correction: Each verb keeps its own required auxiliary: Sono arrivata ieri e ho visto il centro — arrivare takes essere with agreement, while vedere takes avere with no agreement.

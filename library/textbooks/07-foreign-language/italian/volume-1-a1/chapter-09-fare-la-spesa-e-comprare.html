Fare la spesa e comprare

Volume I, Chapter 9: Shopping · Speak and Write Italian: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Overview and Objectives

Chapter 9 closes Unit II by moving from ordering food to a related everyday task: shopping. You will learn common shop and market items, clothing, colors, sizes, and prices, and you will ask about and compare simple choices. By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Name common shops, market items, clothing, and accessories.
  2. Describe an item's color, size, and quality using correctly agreeing adjectives.
  3. Ask and understand prices with quanto costa? and quanto costano?.
  4. Point out a specific item with questo, questa, questi, and queste.
  5. Compare two simple choices with più... di and meno... di.
  6. State a preference with preferire and accept, decline, or purchase an item politely.

Communication Scenario

After their café visit in Chapter 8, Aisha and Marco decide to stop by a small clothing shop and then the market on their way home. Aisha needs a new scarf before the weather turns cold, and Marco wants to pick up some fruit for the week. The shopkeeper helps them compare colors, sizes, and prices.

  1. This chapter teaches you to shop for clothing and market items, ask about prices, and compare simple choices.
  2. You will practice this through Aisha and Marco's shopping trip, then apply it to a purchase of your own.

Where This Chapter Fits

Chapter 9 completes Unit II. It builds on the food, quantity, and preference language from Chapter 8 and introduces shopping, description, price, and simple comparison.

Coming Into This Chapter

You can order food and drink, express likes with mi piace/mi piacciono, and ask for quantities with del/della/dei/delle (Chapter 8).

New in This Chapter

Adjective agreement for color, size, and quality; price questions with quanto costa/costano; the full demonstrative set questo/questa/questi/queste; simple comparison with più/meno... di; and preferire.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Number rhythm and doubled consonants

Numbers like settanta, ottanta, and novanta contain doubled consonants that need real length: seTTANta, oTTANta. Keep the stress on the second-to-last syllable and hold the double consonant slightly longer than a single one.

9.1 Contrastive intonation in comparisons

When comparing two items, Italian speakers often place slight extra stress on the two things being compared: La ROSSA è più CARA della VERDE. Practice raising your pitch slightly on the contrasted words rather than on più or di themselves.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

Language Focus — Adjective agreement with color, size, and quality

Form: adjectives ending in -o have four forms (rosso, rossa, rossi, rosse); adjectives ending in -e have two forms (verde, verdi); a few color words such as blu never change.

Meaning: the adjective must match the gender and number of the noun it describes, not the gender of the speaker.

Use: La sciarpa è rossa. (The scarf is red.) I pantaloni sono neri. (The pants are black.) Le scarpe sono blu. (The shoes are blue.) Descriptive adjectives of this kind normally follow the noun.

Language Focus — Price questions with quanto costa? and quanto costano?

Form: use quanto costa? for one item and quanto costano? for more than one item.

Meaning and use: Quanto costa la sciarpa? (How much does the scarf cost?) Quanto costano le mele? (How much do the apples cost?) Answers commonly use costa or costano followed by a price: Costa dodici euro.

Language Focus — Demonstratives: questo, questa, questi, queste

Form: questo (m. sing.), questa (f. sing.), questi (m. pl.), queste (f. pl.). All four forms agree with the noun they point to.

Meaning and use: Questo cappello è bello. (This hat is nice.) Queste scarpe sono comode. (These shoes are comfortable.) Use these forms to point out a specific item while shopping.

Language Focus — Simple comparison with più... di and meno... di

Form: più + adjective + di for "more... than"; meno + adjective + di for "less... than."

Meaning and use: La giacca nera è più cara della giacca blu. (The black jacket is more expensive than the blue jacket.) Questa maglietta è meno cara di quella. (This T-shirt is less expensive than that one.) This chapter limits comparison to two clearly named or pointed-out items.

Language Focus — Preferire for stating a preference

Form: preferire is a regular -ire verb with the -isc- pattern in most present-tense forms: preferisco, preferisci, preferisce.

Meaning and use: Preferisco la sciarpa rossa. (I prefer the red scarf.) Cosa preferisci, il piccolo o il grande? (What do you prefer, the small one or the large one?) Productive control is expected for io, tu, and lui/lei/Lei.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Aisha and Marco stop at a clothing shop and then the market, as described in the Communication Scenario above.

Commessa Buonasera! Posso aiutarvi? Bwo-na-SEH-ra! POS-so a-yoo-TAR-vee? Good evening! Can I help you?
Aisha Sì, grazie. Cerco una sciarpa. Questa è bella, ma è un po' piccola. See, GRA-tsee-eh. CHER-ko OO-na SHAR-pa. KWES-ta EH BEL-la, ma EH oon PO PEEK-ko-la. Yes, thank you. I'm looking for a scarf. This one is beautiful, but it's a little small.
Commessa Abbiamo anche questa sciarpa verde, più grande. Ab-BYA-mo AN-keh KWES-ta SHAR-pa VER-deh, PYOO GRAN-deh. We also have this green scarf, larger.
Aisha Quanto costa la sciarpa verde? KWAN-to KOS-ta la SHAR-pa VER-deh? How much does the green scarf cost?
Commessa Costa venticinque euro. È più cara della rossa, ma è di lana. KOS-ta ven-tee-CHEEN-kweh EH-oo-ro. EH pyoo KA-ra DEL-la ROS-sa, ma EH dee LA-na. It costs twenty-five euros. It's more expensive than the red one, but it's wool.
Aisha Va bene, la prendo. Preferisco il verde comunque. Va BEH-neh, la PREN-do. Preh-feh-REES-ko eel VER-deh ko-moon-KWEH. All right, I'll take it. I prefer green anyway.
Marco Io vado al mercato. Vorrei delle mele e dei pomodori. EE-o VA-do al mer-KA-to. Vor-REH-ee DEL-leh MEH-leh eh DEH-ee po-mo-DO-ree. I'm going to the market. I'd like some apples and some tomatoes.
Venditore Queste mele sono più dolci di quelle. Quante ne vuole? KWES-teh MEH-leh SO-no pyoo DOL-chee dee KWEL-leh. KWAN-teh neh VWO-leh? These apples are sweeter than those. How many would you like?
Marco Quattro, per favore. Sono economiche? KWAT-tro, per fa-VO-reh. SO-no eh-ko-NO-mee-keh? Four, please. Are they inexpensive?
Venditore Sì, costano solo due euro. See, KOS-ta-no SO-lo DOO-eh EH-oo-ro. Yes, they cost only two euros.

  3. What does Marco buy at the market, and how much does it cost?

Writing Workshop

Write a short comparison and simple recommendation, combining what you learned this chapter with earlier material from Unit II.

Compito di scrittura — Writing task: Write six to eight sentences that include: a description of two items using color, size, or quality adjectives with correct agreement; a price question and answer; one comparison with più/meno... di; and one preference statement with preferisco.

  1. Describe two items. Use adjectives for color, size, or quality with correct agreement.
  2. Ask the price. Use quanto costa? or quanto costano? and answer with a price.
  3. Compare the items. Use più... di or meno... di.
  4. State your preference. Use preferisco and explain briefly why.

Lista di controllo — Editing checklist:

  • Adjectives agree in gender and number with the noun they describe.
  • questo/questa/questi/queste agrees with the noun it points to.
  • più and meno comparisons are followed by di, not another word.
  • preferisco is spelled with -isc-.
  • Prices and numbers are written or spelled correctly.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Shops, markets, and prices vary

Fixed prices, return policies, and service expectations vary by place, shop type, and setting across Italy and other Italian-speaking regions. Open-air markets and small independent shops may work differently from large stores. Avoid assuming that bargaining is expected everywhere.

Culture in Context — Browsing and polite refusal

Saying No, grazie, solo guardo is a normal and polite way to browse without committing to a purchase. Shop assistants in many settings will greet you and offer help; responding politely, even to decline, is expected.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Wrong adjective agreement

Error: Saying la maglietta rosso instead of the correct form.

Correction: maglietta is feminine singular, so the adjective must also be feminine singular: la maglietta rossa.

Common Mistake — Confusing costa and costano

Error: Saying Quanto costa le scarpe? instead of the correct form.

Correction: Use the plural form for a plural noun: Quanto costano le scarpe?

Common Mistake — Comparison without di

Error: Saying più cara che la giacca blu in this type of comparison.

Correction: When comparing two nouns directly, use di: più cara della giacca blu.

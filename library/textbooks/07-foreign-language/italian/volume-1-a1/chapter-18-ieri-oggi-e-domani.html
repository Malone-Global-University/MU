Ieri, oggi e domani

Volume I, Chapter 18: Then, Now, and Tomorrow · Speak and Write Italian: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Overview and Objectives

Chapter 18 brings together everything you can already say about the past, present, and future. In Chapters 16 and 17 you learned to narrate completed actions with avere and essere. Now you will practice moving between what already happened, what usually happens, and what is coming next, all in the same conversation. You will also meet the future tense for the first time, but only as a recognition target: you will learn to notice forms such as sarò, avrò, andrò, and farò when you hear or read them, while continuing to plan ahead the way you already know how — with the present tense. By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Use the present tense to talk about scheduled or planned future events.
  2. Review and combine the passato prossimo with avere and with essere in the same conversation.
  3. Recognize common future-tense forms such as sarò, avrò, andrò, and farò without producing a full future paradigm.
  4. Use contrastive time markers such as ieri, oggi, domani, adesso, prima, dopo, and la prossima settimana.
  5. Express intentions and plans with voglio, vorrei, devo, and posso plus an infinitive.
  6. Talk about a week that includes something you did, something you are doing, and something you plan to do.

Communication Scenario

Giulia and Marta have spent two conversations looking back — first at a quiet weekend, then at Marta's trip to Bologna. Today Marta wants to know what Giulia has planned for the coming week, and Giulia realizes she has a lot to organize.

  1. This chapter teaches you to contrast past, present, and future within a single conversation.
  2. You will practice this through Giulia and Marta's conversation about last week, this week, and next week, then apply the same tools to your own schedule.

Where This Chapter Fits

Chapter 18 completes Unit IV, Esperienze e progetti. It reviews the passato prossimo with avere from Chapter 16 and with essere from Chapter 17, and it adds present-for-future planning and future-tense recognition for the first time.

In This Chapter

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — stress in contrastive time words

Words like ieri, oggi, and domani often carry extra sentence stress when they contrast with each other, since the speaker is drawing attention to the difference in time: IERI ho lavorato, OGGI riposo, DOMANI parto. Practice giving each time word a clear, slightly emphasized pronunciation so the contrast is audible.

18.1 Recognizing future-tense endings by ear

Future-tense forms share a family of endings that are worth recognizing even before you produce them: -ò, -ai, -à, -emo, -ete, and -anno. Listen for the stressed final vowel in forms like sarò and andrò — the accent mark shows you exactly where the stress falls.

18.2 Rhythm across a three-part narration

When you move from past to present to future in the same paragraph, keep each verb phrase as its own rhythmic unit, and pause briefly at each time-word boundary: Ieri ho lavorato. // Oggi riposo. // Domani parto per Torino.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

Language Focus — the present tense for scheduled future events

Italian often uses the present tense to talk about something that is already planned or scheduled, especially when a time expression makes the future meaning clear. You have been doing this since earlier chapters without naming it:

  • Domani parto per Torino. — Tomorrow I'm leaving for Turin.
  • La prossima settimana ho un esame. — Next week I have an exam.
  • Stasera vediamo un film. — Tonight we're seeing a movie.

This is your main productive tool for talking about the future in Volume I. A time expression such as domani or la prossima settimana normally accompanies the present-tense verb so the future meaning is clear.

Language Building Block — reviewing both passato prossimo auxiliaries

Combine what you learned in Chapters 16 and 17. Remember that most verbs use avere, while a scheduled set of movement and change-of-state verbs uses essere with participle agreement:

AuxiliaryExample verbsSample sentence
averelavorare, mangiare, vedere, leggereHo lavorato fino a tardi.
essereandare, partire, arrivare, tornareSono tornata domenica sera.

Ieri ho lavorato tutto il giorno, poi sono uscita con un'amica. — Yesterday I worked all day, then I went out with a friend.

Language Focus — recognizing the future tense

You will meet the future tense in reading and listening this chapter. Recognize these high-frequency forms, but continue to use the present tense productively for your own future plans:

InfinitiveFuture form (io)English
esseresaròI will be
avereavròI will have
andareandròI will go
farefaròI will do / make

Do not worry about forming the future tense for other persons or other verbs yet. This is receptive knowledge for this stage of the course.

Language Focus — expressing intentions and plans

Use familiar modal-style expressions plus an infinitive to talk about what you want, would like, must, or can do next:

  • Voglio finire il progetto oggi. — I want to finish the project today.
  • Devo studiare per l'esame. — I have to study for the exam.
  • Penso di andare al mare in agosto. — I'm thinking of going to the beach in August.
Review — contrastive time markers

Use ieri, oggi, domani, adesso, prima, dopo, la settimana scorsa, and la prossima settimana to anchor each verb to a clear point in time: La settimana scorsa ho lavorato molto, adesso riposo un po', e la prossima settimana ho un appuntamento importante.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Giulia and Marta continue their conversation from Chapter 17.

Marta E tu, Giulia? Che cosa hai fatto la settimana scorsa? Eh too, joo-LEE-a? Keh KO-za ay FAT-to la set-tee-MA-na SKOR-sa? And you, Giulia? What did you do last week?
Giulia Ho lavorato molto, e sabato sono andata a un matrimonio. O la-vo-RA-to MOL-to, eh sa-BA-to SO-no an-DA-ta a oon ma-tree-MO-nyo. I worked a lot, and Saturday I went to a wedding.
Marta Che bello! E adesso, come va questa settimana? Keh BEL-lo! Eh a-DES-so, KO-meh va KWES-ta set-tee-MA-na? How nice! And now, how's this week going?
Giulia Adesso ho molti impegni: oggi ho una riunione, e domani ho un appuntamento dal dentista. A-DES-so o MOL-tee eem-PEH-nyee: OJ-jee o OO-na ree-oo-NYO-neh, eh do-MA-nee o oon ap-poon-ta-MEN-to dal den-TEES-ta. Right now I have a lot of commitments: today I have a meeting, and tomorrow I have a dentist appointment.
Marta E la prossima settimana? Hai progetti? Eh la PROS-see-ma set-tee-MA-na? Ay pro-JET-tee? And next week? Do you have plans?
Giulia Sì, penso di andare in montagna con i miei genitori. Voglio riposare un po' dopo una settimana così impegnativa. See, PEN-so dee an-DA-reh een mon-TA-nya kon ee MYEH-ee jeh-nee-TO-ree. VOL-yo ree-po-SA-reh oon po dop-po OO-na set-tee-MA-na ko-ZEE eem-pen-ya-TEE-va. Yes, I'm thinking of going to the mountains with my parents. I want to rest a bit after such a busy week.

Domande di comprensione — Comprehension questions:

  1. What did Giulia do last week?
  2. What commitments does Giulia have today and tomorrow?
  3. What is Giulia planning for next week?

Speaking Practice

Pratica controllata — Controlled practice: three time frames

With a partner, take turns saying one sentence about yesterday, one about today, and one about tomorrow, using the correct tense for each.

Compito comunicativo — Communicative task: my week

In pairs or small groups, describe your week: something you already did, something you are doing now, and something you plan to do, using at least one time marker from this chapter for each part. Your partner should ask at least one follow-up question.

Pratica da solo — Solo alternative

Record yourself describing your week across all three time frames, checking that you use the present tense for future plans and the correct auxiliary for past actions.

Reading Practice

Read the short message below aloud, sounding out each Italian word slowly.

Un messaggio tra amiche — A message between friends
Ciao! Come va? Questa settimana è stata impegnativa: lunedì ho avuto una riunione, mercoledì sono uscita con i colleghi e ieri ho finito un progetto importante. Adesso sono un po' stanca. La prossima settimana sarò più libera: andrò dai miei genitori e farò finalmente quella passeggiata in montagna di cui ti ho parlato. Tu, invece, che progetti hai per il fine settimana?
Hi! How's it going? This week has been busy: Monday I had a meeting, Wednesday I went out with colleagues, and yesterday I finished an important project. Now I'm a little tired. Next week I'll be freer: I'll go to my parents' place and finally take that mountain walk I told you about. What about you — what plans do you have for the weekend?

Note: è stata appears here as a receptive-only form, and the future-tense forms sarò, andrò, and farò appear for recognition only. You are not expected to produce these forms independently at this stage.

Writing Workshop

Write a short message describing your week across three time frames.

Compito di scrittura — Writing task: Write five to seven sentences describing your week. Include at least one completed action with avere, one completed action with essere, one present-tense sentence about today, and one present-tense sentence expressing a future plan with a time marker.

  1. Say what you did last week. Use la settimana scorsa plus passato prossimo with avere or essere.
  2. Say what is happening today. Use oggi plus a present-tense verb.
  3. Say what you plan to do next. Use domani or la prossima settimana plus a present-tense verb, or an intention expression such as voglio or penso di.
  4. Close with a question for your reader. Ask what plans they have.

Lista di controllo — Editing checklist:

  • Past-tense sentences use the correct auxiliary (avere or essere) with correct participle agreement where needed.
  • Present-tense sentences about the future include a clear time marker such as domani or la prossima settimana.
  • No full future-tense paradigm is required; recognition forms are not used productively unless naturally recalled.
  • Time markers such as ieri, oggi, and domani are spelled and placed correctly.
  • Intention expressions (voglio, devo, penso di) are followed by an infinitive.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — talking about a busy week

Sharing an overview of a busy or eventful week is common small talk among friends, colleagues, and family members across Italian-speaking communities, though the pace of daily life, working hours, and typical commitments vary by region, profession, and family situation. Avoid assuming that any one schedule represents Italian life in general.

Culture in Context — planning ahead

How far in advance people plan, and how firmly they commit to plans, can differ by relationship, generation, and setting. Some plans are treated as fixed appointments, while others remain flexible until closer to the date. Context and relationship help signal how firm a plan is.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — forcing a full future-tense paradigm too early

Error: Trying to conjugate the future tense across all persons for everyday planning, such as producing forms for tu, noi, or loro from memory.

Correction: At this stage, use the present tense with a clear time marker for your own future plans, such as Domani parto. Recognize future forms like sarò and andrò when you read or hear them, but you are not expected to produce the full future paradigm yet.

Common Mistake — mixing up auxiliaries when reviewing the past

Error: Saying Ho andata a un matrimonio instead of Sono andata a un matrimonio.

Correction: Movement verbs such as andare still require essere with agreement, even in a chapter that reviews both auxiliaries together.

Common Mistake — leaving out the time marker

Error: Saying Parto per Roma to mean "I will leave for Rome" with no time context.

Correction: Without a time marker, a present-tense sentence like Parto per Roma can sound like it is happening right now. Add domani or another clear time marker to signal future meaning: Domani parto per Roma.

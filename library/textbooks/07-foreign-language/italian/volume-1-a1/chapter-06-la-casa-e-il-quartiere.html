La casa e il quartiere

Volume I, Chapter 6: Home and Neighborhood · Speak and Write Italian: Foundations of Practical Communication

Chapter Overview and Objectives

Chapter 6 opens Unit II, Vita quotidiana, by moving your Italian out of the introductions of Unit I and into the spaces where daily life happens. You will learn to say what is and is not present in a place, describe rooms and household objects, and locate people, things, and neighborhood places in relation to one another. By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Name common rooms, household objects, and neighborhood places.
  2. State what is present using c'è and ci sono, and what is absent using non c'è and non ci sono.
  3. Locate objects and places using prepositions such as a, in, su, sotto, vicino a, davanti a, and dietro.
  4. Use the expanded article system, including lo, gli, and l', with familiar household and neighborhood nouns.
  5. Ask and answer basic questions about a home and neighborhood.
  6. Read a short housing note, simple map, or neighborhood description.
  7. Write a short description of a home and neighborhood.

Communication Scenario

Aisha has just moved into a new apartment near the university, and Marco is coming over to help her set up her desk before classes start. As Marco arrives, Aisha shows him around: the kitchen, the small balcony, and the living room where a box of books is still waiting to be unpacked. Afterward, they walk around the block so Aisha can learn what is nearby — a café, a pharmacy, and a small park across the street. Marco asks a few practical questions: what is on the desk, whether there is a supermarket close by, and where the nearest bus stop is.

  1. This chapter teaches you to describe what is present in a home and where things and places are located relative to one another.
  2. You will practice this through Aisha and Marco's visit, then apply it to your own home and neighborhood.

Where This Chapter Fits

Chapter 6 begins Unit II. It builds directly on the identity, family, and description language from Unit I and introduces the first grammar systems centered on place: existence, location, and an expanded article system.

Coming Into This Chapter

You can greet people, introduce yourself and others, describe family members with gender, articles, and simple adjective agreement, and use essere and avere in familiar patterns (Chapters 1–5).

New in This Chapter

c'è and ci sono for existence and their negative forms; location prepositions; and the expanded definite and indefinite article system, including lo, gli, and l'.

Looking Forward

Chapter 7, La giornata quotidiana, introduces the present tense of regular verbs and daily routines, using the home you describe in this chapter as a starting point.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — Vowel clarity in longer words

Words like quartiere, supermercato, and università are longer than most vocabulary you have met so far. Keep every vowel clear and full — do not let unstressed vowels collapse into an English-style "uh" sound. Say each word slowly first, then at a natural pace, keeping the stressed syllable slightly longer and louder: quar-ti-È-re, su-per-mer-CÀ-to, u-ni-ver-si-TÀ.

6.1 Phrase grouping in descriptions

When you describe a home, Italian groups words into short breath-phrases rather than reading each word separately: C'è una cucina,  /  c'è un soggiorno,  /  e c'è una camera da letto. Practice saying this sentence with a small pause at each slash, keeping your voice smooth within each phrase.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

Language Focus — C'è and ci sono

Form: c'è (there is) is used with singular nouns; ci sono (there are) is used with plural nouns. Both come from ci + essere, but at this stage you should treat them as fixed expressions rather than analyzing ci separately.

Meaning: they state that something exists or is present in a place.

Use: C'è un tavolo in cucina. (There is a table in the kitchen.) Ci sono due sedie. (There are two chairs.) To say something is absent, add non before c'è or ci sono: Non c'è un armadio. (There isn't a closet.)

ItalianEnglish
C'è un balcone.There is a balcony.
Ci sono due camere da letto.There are two bedrooms.
Non c'è un giardino.There isn't a garden.
Non ci sono negozi qui.There aren't any shops here.
Language Focus — Location prepositions

Form: a, in, su, sotto, vicino a, davanti a, and dietro combine with a noun to show where something is.

Meaning and use: Il libro è sul tavolo. (The book is on the table.) Lo scaffale è vicino alla finestra. (The bookshelf is near the window.) Il bar è davanti al parco. (The café is in front of the park.) At this stage, you only need to recognize and use these combined forms — such as sul and alla — in fixed, high-frequency phrases. The complete system of articulated prepositions is developed later in the volume.

Language Focus — Expanded articles: lo, gli, l'

Form: most masculine nouns take il (singular) or i (plural). Masculine nouns beginning with s + consonant, z, gn, ps, or a few other combinations take lo (singular) or gli (plural) instead. Any noun beginning with a vowel takes l' in the singular, regardless of gender.

Meaning and use: lo scaffale → gli scaffali; l'armadio → gli armadi; l'università (feminine, singular only in this context). These forms are not exceptions to be memorized in isolation — they follow the sound of the noun's first letters, not the noun's meaning.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Marco visits Aisha's new apartment, described in the Communication Scenario above.

Marco Ciao Aisha! Com'è il tuo nuovo appartamento? Chow Ah-EE-sha! Ko-MEH eel TOO-oh NWO-vo ap-par-ta-MEN-to? Hi Aisha! What's your new apartment like?
Aisha È piccolo ma bello. C'è una cucina, c'è un soggiorno e c'è una camera da letto. Eh PEEK-ko-lo ma BEL-lo. Cheh OO-na koo-CHEE-na, cheh oon so-JOR-no eh cheh OO-na KA-me-ra da LET-to. It's small but nice. There's a kitchen, there's a living room, and there's a bedroom.
Marco C'è anche un balcone? Cheh AN-keh oon bal-KO-neh? Is there a balcony too?
Aisha Sì, il balcone è vicino alla cucina. Non c'è un giardino, però. See, eel bal-KO-neh eh vee-CHEE-no AL-la koo-CHEE-na. Non cheh oon jar-DEE-no, peh-ROH. Yes, the balcony is near the kitchen. There isn't a garden, though.
Marco E lo scaffale? I tuoi libri sono già lì? Eh lo ska-FA-leh? Ee TWO-ee LEE-bree SOH-no jah LEE? And the bookshelf? Are your books already there?
Aisha Non ancora! I libri sono in una scatola, sotto il tavolo. Non an-KO-ra! Ee LEE-bree SOH-no een OO-na ska-TO-la, SOT-to eel TA-vo-lo. Not yet! The books are in a box, under the table.
Marco Va bene, ti aiuto io. E nel quartiere, c'è un supermercato vicino? Vah BEH-neh, tee ah-YOO-to EE-oh. Eh nel kwar-TYEH-reh, cheh oon soo-per-mer-KA-to vee-CHEE-no? Okay, I'll help you. And in the neighborhood, is there a supermarket nearby?
Aisha Sì, c'è un supermercato e c'è anche una farmacia. Il bar è davanti al parco, e la fermata dell'autobus è dietro il palazzo. See, cheh oon soo-per-mer-KA-to eh cheh AN-keh OO-na far-ma-CHEE-a. Eel bar eh da-VAN-tee al PAR-ko, eh la fer-MA-ta del-la-oo-TO-boos eh DYEH-tro eel pa-LAT-tso. Yes, there's a supermarket and there's also a pharmacy. The café is in front of the park, and the bus stop is behind the building.

Domande di comprensione — Comprehension questions:

  1. What three rooms does Aisha mention first?
  2. Where are Aisha's books right now?
  3. Name two neighborhood places Aisha mentions and where they are located.

Speaking Practice

Pratica controllata — Controlled practice: describe a room

Choose one room in your home and say, using c'è and ci sono, three things that are in it and one thing that is not.

Compito comunicativo — Communicative task: home and neighborhood interview

With a partner, ask and answer about each other's home and neighborhood: how many rooms there are, what is nearby, and where key objects or places are located, using at least three location prepositions.

Pratica da solo — Solo alternative

Record a description of your home and neighborhood lasting sixty to ninety seconds, including at least four objects or places and two location prepositions, then listen back and check your description against a list you prepared beforehand.

Reading Practice

Read the neighborhood note below aloud, sounding out each Italian word slowly.

Il quartiere di Sara — Sara's neighborhood
Abito in un piccolo appartamento vicino all'università. Nel mio quartiere c'è un bar davanti a casa mia, ed è molto comodo per la colazione. C'è anche un parco dietro il palazzo, dove studio spesso. Non c'è un supermercato molto vicino, ma c'è una farmacia proprio all'angolo. La fermata dell'autobus è davanti al bar.
I live in a small apartment near the university. In my neighborhood there's a café in front of my building, and it's very convenient for breakfast. There's also a park behind the building, where I often study. There isn't a supermarket very close by, but there's a pharmacy right on the corner. The bus stop is in front of the café.

Domande di comprensione — Comprehension questions:

  1. Where does Sara live in relation to the university?
  2. What is in front of Sara's building, and what is behind it?
  3. Is there a supermarket close by? What is nearby instead?

Writing Workshop

Write a short description of your home and neighborhood, combining what you learned this chapter with earlier material from Units I.

Compito di scrittura — Writing task: Write six to eight sentences that include: at least three rooms or household objects using c'è or ci sono; one thing that is not present, using non c'è or non ci sono; at least two neighborhood places; and at least two location prepositions.

  1. Describe your rooms. Use c'è and ci sono with correct articles.
  2. Mention something missing. Use non c'è or non ci sono.
  3. Locate an object. Use su, sotto, vicino a, davanti a, or dietro.
  4. Describe your neighborhood. Name at least two nearby places and their location.

Lista di controllo — Editing checklist:

  • Singular nouns use c'è; plural nouns use ci sono.
  • Each noun is paired with the correct article, including lo, gli, and l' where the noun requires them.
  • Location prepositions match the intended meaning (su vs. sotto vs. vicino a, etc.).
  • The apostrophe in c'è and the accent on è are both present.
  • Every sentence ends with correct punctuation.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — Housing varies across Italy

Housing arrangements in Italy vary considerably by region, city size, income, and family structure. An apartment in central Rome, a house in a small Sicilian town, and student housing near a university in Bologna can look very different from one another. Avoid assuming that any single description in this chapter represents "the" Italian home.

Culture in Context — Addresses and floors

In many Italian buildings, the ground floor is called il pianterreno and is not counted as the "first floor" the way it often is in the United States; il primo piano is one level up. Street addresses and apartment numbering conventions can also differ from what English-speaking learners may expect. These details become more relevant in later volumes when you write more formal addresses.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — Confusing c'è and ci sono

Error: Saying C'è due sedie for a plural noun.

Correction: Use ci sono with plural nouns: Ci sono due sedie. Reserve c'è for singular nouns.

Common Mistake — Wrong article before s + consonant

Error: Saying il scaffale instead of the correct form.

Correction: Masculine nouns beginning with s + consonant take lo, not il: lo scaffale.

Common Mistake — Dropping the apostrophe in c'è

Error: Writing ce instead of c'è.

Correction: The apostrophe marks elision, and the accent on è is required: c'è.

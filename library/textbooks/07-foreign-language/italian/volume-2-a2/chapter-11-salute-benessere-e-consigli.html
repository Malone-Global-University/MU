Salute, benessere e consigli

Volume II, Chapter 11: Health, Well-Being, and Advice · Speak and Write Italian: Everyday Independence and Connected Communication

Chapter Overview and Objectives

All that preparing, lifting, and standing around at the festival has caught up with Rita: she wakes up with a sore throat and no voice for shouting over a crowd. This chapter opens Unit III, Partecipare alla comunità, by moving your Italian into a new kind of practical situation — describing how you feel, asking for and giving advice, and understanding the kind of everyday commands people give each other when someone doesn't feel well. You will meet the imperative for the first time, in its most useful everyday forms, and you will begin using indirect object pronouns to talk about what happens to or for someone. By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Describe common symptoms and states of well-being using vocabulary and expressions from this chapter.
  2. Give and understand simple advice using bisogna, devi, and dovrebbe.
  3. Form and use high-frequency imperative commands for tu, Lei, and voi.
  4. Use indirect object pronouns mi, ti, gli, and le in simple support and advice expressions.
  5. Ask for help in a pharmacy or health-related situation and understand a simple response.
  6. Read a pharmacy notice or short health-related message for practical information.
  7. Write a short note giving advice to someone who is unwell.

Communication Scenario

Two days after finishing the neighborhood recap message, Rita texts the group to say she's lost her voice and her throat hurts. Marco stops by with tea and honey on his way to the pharmacy, and Paolo, who caught a cold after standing outside all weekend, isn't feeling much better himself. Sara, ever practical, has plenty of advice for both of them — whether they want it or not.

  1. You will read and listen to Marco, Rita, Paolo, and Sara talking about symptoms and what to do about them.
  2. You will then complete a task in which you describe how you feel, ask for help at a pharmacy, and give simple advice to someone who is unwell.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — imperative intonation and polite advice rhythm

An informal tu command such as Riposati! is said with a brisk, falling intonation — direct but warm among friends. A polite Lei command such as Si riposi is said more evenly, without the same abruptness, since softness matters more in a formal register. When you add advice with dovresti or dovrebbe, let your voice rise slightly and then settle — this signals a suggestion rather than an order.

11.1 Double consonants in health vocabulary

Practice the double consonants in raffreddore (raf-fred-DO-reh) and appuntamento (ap-poon-ta-MEN-to). Shortening these consonants changes rhythm and can affect clarity for a listener.

11.2 Formal versus informal written instructions

Compare the tone of a handwritten note to a friend (Riposati e bevi molta acqua!) with a printed pharmacy instruction (Si consiglia di riposare e di bere molti liquidi.). The written register shifts even when the advice is identical.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

Language Focus — the imperative for tu, Lei, and voi

The imperative is used to give instructions, advice, and commands. This chapter introduces only the most common, high-frequency forms — regular -are, -ere, and -ire verbs, plus a few irregular forms you will need immediately.

InfinitivetuLeivoi
riposareRiposa!Riposi!Riposate!
bereBevi!Beva!Bevete!
prenderePrendi!Prenda!Prendete!
stare (irregular)Stai a casa!Stia a casa!State a casa!
avere (irregular, negative reminder)Non avere paura!Non abbia paura!Non abbiate paura!

Note that negative tu commands use non + infinitive (Non ti preoccupare!), while Lei and voi simply add non before the regular imperative form. This chapter does not require pronoun attachment to the imperative; that expansion is scheduled for a later volume.

Language Focus — giving advice with bisogna, devi, and dovrebbe

Italian has several ways to give advice at different levels of directness:

PatternDirectnessExample
Devi + infinitivedirect, informalDevi bere molta acqua.
Dovresti / Dovrebbe + infinitivesofter suggestionDovresti riposarti un po'.
Bisogna + infinitiveimpersonal, generalBisogna bere molti liquidi quando si ha la febbre.

Dovresti and dovrebbe are conditional forms of dovere. In this chapter they are taught as formulaic advice patterns; the full conditional system is introduced productively in Chapter 17.

Language Focus — indirect object pronouns mi, ti, gli, le

Indirect object pronouns show who receives an action, often translated as "to me," "to you," "to him," or "to her." They are introduced here in simple, high-frequency support and advice expressions.

PronounMeaningExample
mito meMi fa male la gola.
tito you (informal)Ti porto una tisana.
glito himGli do un consiglio.
leto her / to you (formal)Le consiglio di riposare.

Like direct object pronouns, indirect object pronouns are placed before a conjugated verb. Combined pronouns and full clitic-cluster patterns remain outside this chapter's scope.

Modal verbs, reflexive verbs, and past narration from earlier chapters remain fully active and may be recycled naturally throughout this chapter's tasks.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Marco arrives at Rita's apartment with tea and honey. Paolo is already there, sniffling on the sofa.

Marco Ciao Rita, come stai? Sara mi ha detto che non ti senti bene. CHA-o REE-ta, KO-meh STA-ee? SA-ra mee a DET-to keh non tee SEN-tee BEH-neh. Hi Rita, how are you? Sara told me you're not feeling well.
Rita Mi fa malissimo la gola e ho anche un po' di febbre. Devo aver preso freddo durante la festa. Mee fa ma-LEES-see-mo la GO-la eh o AN-keh oon po dee FEB-breh. DEH-vo a-VEHR PRAY-so FRED-do doo-RAN-teh la FES-ta. My throat hurts a lot and I also have a bit of a fever. I must have caught a chill during the festival.
Paolo Non sei l'unica. Anch'io ho il raffreddore e la tosse. Sara continua a dirmi di riposarmi, ma c'è ancora tanto da fare per il quartiere. Non say LOO-nee-ka. An-KEE-o o eel raf-fred-DO-reh eh la TOS-seh. SA-ra kon-TEE-noo-a a DEER-mee dee ree-po-SAR-mee, ma cheh AN-ko-ra TAN-to da FA-reh per eel kwar-TYEH-reh. You're not the only one. I also have a cold and a cough. Sara keeps telling me to rest, but there's still so much to do for the neighborhood.
Marco Sara ha ragione. Prendi questo miele con il tè e riposati oggi. Il quartiere può aspettare un giorno. SA-ra a ra-JO-neh. PREN-dee KWES-to mee-EH-leh kon eel teh eh ree-PO-sa-tee OJ-jee. Eel kwar-TYEH-reh pwo as-pet-TA-reh oon JOR-no. Sara is right. Take this honey with the tea and rest today. The neighborhood can wait one day.
Rita Grazie, Marco. Secondo te, devo andare in farmacia o basta riposare? GRA-tsee-eh, MAR-ko. Seh-KON-do teh, DEH-vo an-DA-reh een far-ma-CHEE-a o BAS-ta ree-po-SA-reh? Thanks, Marco. In your opinion, do I need to go to the pharmacy or is it enough to rest?
Marco Se domani hai ancora la febbre, dovresti chiedere alla farmacista. Per ora, bevi molta acqua e riposati. Seh do-MA-nee AH-ee an-KO-ra la FEB-breh, do-VRES-tee kee-EH-deh-reh AL-la far-ma-CHEES-ta. Per O-ra, BEH-vee MOL-ta AK-kwa eh ree-PO-sa-tee. If you still have a fever tomorrow, you should ask the pharmacist. For now, drink a lot of water and rest.
Paolo E io? Mi consigli qualcosa anche per la tosse? Eh EE-o? Mee kon-SEE-lyee kwal-KO-za AN-keh per la TOS-seh? And me? Can you suggest something for the cough too?
Marco Certo. Prendi anche tu un po' di miele, e non uscire con questo tempo freddo! CHER-to. PREN-dee AN-keh too oon po dee mee-EH-leh, eh non oo-SHEE-reh kon KWES-to TEM-po FRED-do! Of course. Have some honey too, and don't go out in this cold weather!

Speaking Practice

Pratica controllata — Controlled practice: symptom and advice exchange

With a partner, take turns describing a symptom (Mi fa male..., Ho...) and responding with one imperative and one advice pattern (devi, dovresti, or bisogna).

Compito comunicativo — Communicative task: at the pharmacy

In pairs, role-play a pharmacy visit. One partner plays the customer describing symptoms; the other plays the pharmacist, asking follow-up questions and giving at least two pieces of advice using imperative forms for Lei.

Pratica da solo — Solo alternative

Reading Practice

Read the notice below aloud, sounding out each Italian word slowly.

Avviso della farmacia — Pharmacy notice
Gentili clienti, in questo periodo dell'anno è comune avere mal di gola, tosse e un po' di febbre. Ecco alcuni consigli pratici: riposatevi quando potete, bevete molti liquidi caldi, e non uscite senza una giacca pesante. Se la febbre supera i 38 gradi per più di due giorni, vi consigliamo di consultare il medico. Per qualsiasi domanda, il nostro farmacista è sempre disponibile durante l'orario di apertura.
Dear customers, at this time of year it's common to have a sore throat, a cough, and a bit of fever. Here are some practical tips: rest when you can, drink a lot of hot liquids, and don't go out without a heavy jacket. If your fever goes above 38 degrees for more than two days, we recommend consulting a doctor. For any questions, our pharmacist is always available during opening hours.

Writing Workshop

Write a short note giving advice to a friend or family member who is unwell.

Compito di scrittura — Writing task: Write six to eight sentences. Include: at least two symptoms described using chapter vocabulary; at least two tu imperatives; at least one use of dovresti or bisogna; and at least one indirect object pronoun.

  1. Open with concern. Say you heard the person isn't feeling well.
  2. Ask about symptoms. Reference what you understand they are experiencing.
  3. Give advice. Use at least two different imperative forms.
  4. Add a softer suggestion. Use dovresti or bisogna.
  5. Close warmly. Offer further help or wish them well.

Lista di controllo — Editing checklist:

  • Imperative forms match the correct subject (tu throughout, unless writing to more than one person).
  • Indirect object pronoun is placed correctly before the conjugated verb.
  • Advice pattern (devi, dovresti, or bisogna) matches the intended level of directness.
  • Accents, apostrophes, and double consonants follow standard Italian spelling.
  • Tone remains warm and supportive throughout.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — the role of the pharmacist

In many Italian communities, a pharmacist is often a first point of contact for minor symptoms, offering advice and over-the-counter remedies before a visit to a doctor becomes necessary. This varies by region, by the severity of the symptom, and by the individual's own preference and access to care.

Culture in Context — caring for a friend

Bringing tea, honey, or a home remedy to a friend who feels unwell is a common gesture of care in many Italian-speaking households, though specific remedies and habits vary widely by family, region, and generation. This kind of practical, informal advice should not be treated as medical guidance.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — using Lei imperative endings for tu

Error: Writing Riposi! to a close friend, using the formal Lei command in an informal context.

Correction: Riposati!, the informal tu command, is appropriate among friends.

Common Mistake — placing the indirect object pronoun after the verb

Error: Writing Fa male mi la gola, placing the pronoun after the conjugated verb as in English word order.

Correction: Mi fa male la gola, with the pronoun placed before the conjugated verb.

Common Mistake — treating dovresti as an order

Error: Using dovresti in a context that calls for a direct, urgent instruction.

Correction: Reserve dovresti for gentle suggestions; use the imperative or devi when directness is needed.

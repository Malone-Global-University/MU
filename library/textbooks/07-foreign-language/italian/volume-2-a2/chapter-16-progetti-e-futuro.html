Progetti e futuro

Volume II, Chapter 16: Projects and the Future · Speak and Write Italian: Everyday Independence and Connected Communication

Chapter Overview and Objectives

Marco's package finally arrived, his professor accepted the late assignment, and Chiara is still talking about that film — but life keeps moving forward. This chapter turns from the day-to-day toward what comes next: the trip Marco is planning, the course Chiara wants to start, and the small predictions people make about how things will turn out. You already know how to talk about the present and the past with control. Now you will learn the future simple tense, the main way Italian expresses plans, predictions, and arrangements that have not happened yet. By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Form the future simple of regular -are, -ere, and -ire verbs.
  2. Use the future simple of high-frequency irregular verbs, including essere, avere, andare, potere, dovere, volere, and fare.
  3. Talk about future plans, goals, and projects using appropriate time expressions.
  4. Make predictions and express expectations about people, events, and outcomes.
  5. Distinguish future-tense planning from the present-tense near future you already use.
  6. Understand a short spoken account of upcoming plans and predictions.
  7. Write and discuss a personal, study, travel, or work project using the future simple.

Communication Scenario

A few weeks after their conversation about the film, Marco and Chiara meet again to talk about their plans for the coming months. Marco is organizing a trip to visit his cousin, and Chiara is thinking about signing up for an evening course. Both of them make a few predictions about how things will go — not all of them agree.

  1. You will read and listen to Marco and Chiara discussing their upcoming plans and predictions.
  2. You will then describe a project of your own, explaining what you will do, when, and what you expect to happen.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — stress in the future tense

Future-tense endings are always stressed on the ending itself, not on the verb stem. Listen for the stress falling on the final syllable in io and lui/lei forms: parlerò (par-leh-ROH), partirà (par-tee-RAH). The written accent on these two forms is not optional — it marks exactly where the stress falls.

16.1 Stress across the full future paradigm

Beyond the first and third person singular, stress remains on the ending throughout the future paradigm: parleremo (par-leh-REH-mo), partirete (par-tee-REH-teh). Avoid shifting stress back onto the stem out of habit from the present tense.

16.2 Intonation for predictions

Predictions and expectations often carry a slightly rising-then-falling contour, softer than a flat statement of fact, as in Sarà un bel viaggio, secondo me — the voice rises gently on bel viaggio before settling for the added opinion.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

Language Focus — the future simple of regular verbs

To form the future simple of regular verbs, drop the final -e of the infinitive and add the future endings. Note that -are verbs change their stem vowel to -e-, making them identical in pattern to -ere verbs.

Language Focus — future for plans versus present for near future

You already know how to use the present tense for arrangements that feel close and settled, as in domani vado dal medico. The future simple is used for plans that feel less immediate, less certain, or more general, and it is the natural choice for predictions: un giorno andrò in Sicilia, sarà un viaggio interessante. Both forms remain correct for near-future plans; the future simply adds a layer of distance, formality, or uncertainty that the present does not always carry.

Language Focus — appena and future time expressions

Unlike English, Italian often uses the future tense in both clauses when one future event depends on another: Appena arriverò, ti scriverò (As soon as I arrive, I'll write to you) — not the present tense as in the English "as soon as I arrive." This pattern will be developed further in Volume III; for now, treat it as a recognizable formula tied to appena, quando, and similar time connectors used with two future-tense verbs.

This chapter reviews modal verbs and the present tense of motion verbs from earlier chapters within the new future-tense context; it does not introduce the conditional or hypothetical se clauses, which begin in Chapter 17.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Marco and Chiara meet for coffee again, a few weeks after Chapter 15, and talk about their plans for the coming months.

Chiara Allora, cosa farai quest'estate? Andrai a trovare tuo cugino? Al-LO-ra, KO-za fa-RA-ee kwest-eh-STA-teh? An-dra-EE a tro-VA-reh TOO-o KOO-jee-no? So, what will you do this summer? Will you go visit your cousin?
Marco Sì, partirò a luglio. Prenderò il treno e arriverò a Bologna verso mezzogiorno. Ci fermeremo lì per due settimane. See, par-tee-ROH a LOO-lyo. Pren-deh-ROH eel treh-noh eh ar-ree-veh-ROH a Bo-LO-nya VER-so med-zo-JOR-no. Chee fer-meh-REH-mo lee per DOO-eh set-tee-MA-neh. Yes, I'll leave in July. I'll take the train and I'll arrive in Bologna around noon. We'll stay there for two weeks.
Chiara Sarà un bel viaggio! E dopo, tornerai subito al lavoro? Sa-RA oon bel vee-AJ-jo! Eh DO-po, tor-neh-RA-ee SOO-bee-to al la-VO-ro? That'll be a nice trip! And afterward, will you go straight back to work?
Marco Purtroppo sì. Ma appena tornerò, ti scriverò con tutte le foto. E tu, cosa farai? Poor-TROP-po see. Ma ap-PEH-na tor-neh-ROH, tee skree-veh-ROH kon TOOT-teh leh FO-to. Eh too, KO-za fa-RA-ee? Unfortunately, yes. But as soon as I get back, I'll write to you with all the photos. And you, what will you do?
Chiara Mi iscriverò a un corso serale di fotografia. Comincerà a settembre e finirà a dicembre. Mee ee-skree-veh-ROH a oon KOR-so seh-RA-leh dee fo-to-gra-FEE-a. Ko-meen-cheh-RA a set-TEM-breh eh fee-nee-RA a dee-CHEM-breh. I'll enroll in an evening photography course. It'll start in September and finish in December.
Marco Che bella idea! Sarai brava, ne sono sicuro. Avrai bisogno di aiuto con qualcosa? Keh BEL-la ee-DEH-a! Sa-RA-ee BRA-va, neh SO-no see-KOO-ro. A-VRA-ee bee-ZO-nyo dee a-YOO-to kon kwal-KO-za? What a great idea! You'll be good at it, I'm sure. Will you need help with anything?
Chiara Forse sì — dovrò comprare una macchina fotografica migliore. Ma prima, dovremo finire quel caffè! FOR-seh see — do-VROH kom-PRA-reh OO-na mak-KEE-na fo-to-GRA-fee-ka mee-LYO-reh. Ma PREE-ma, do-VREH-mo fee-NEE-reh kwel kaf-FEH! Maybe — I'll need to buy a better camera. But first, we'll need to finish that coffee!

Speaking Practice

Pratica controllata — Controlled practice: verb chain

With a partner, take turns building a chain of future-tense sentences about a single day: one person says what they will do first, the next person continues with what will happen after that, using a new verb each time.

Compito comunicativo — Communicative task: my project

In pairs or as a solo recording, describe a personal, study, travel, or work project. Explain what you will do, when you will do it, and what you expect the result will be, using at least three different future-tense verbs, including one irregular verb.

Pratica da solo — Solo alternative

Record yourself making three predictions about the coming year — for yourself, for a friend, and for something more general, such as the weather or a local event.

Writing Workshop

Write a paragraph describing a project of your own — personal, academic, travel, or professional — similar to Marco's trip or Chiara's course.

Compito di scrittura — Writing task: Write eight to ten sentences. Include: at least four future-tense verbs, at least one irregular future verb, one future time expression, and one prediction or expectation about how the project will turn out.

  1. Introduce the project. State what you will do and roughly when.
  2. Add the steps. Explain two or three things that will happen along the way, using a future time expression for at least one of them.
  3. Add an appena sentence. Connect one future event to another using appena with two future-tense verbs.
  4. Make a prediction. Say what you expect the outcome will be.

Lista di controllo — Editing checklist:

  • Regular future verbs are formed correctly, with attention to -care/-gare and -ciare/-giare spelling changes where relevant.
  • At least one irregular future stem is used correctly.
  • The accent appears correctly on io and lui/lei forms.
  • The appena sentence uses the future tense in both clauses.
  • A future time expression is used naturally.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — evening courses and lifelong learning

Evening and weekend courses in subjects such as photography, languages, cooking, and crafts are common in many Italian cities and towns, often organized through community centers, cultural associations, or private schools. Availability, cost, and popularity vary by region and by the size of the town, so this scenario should not be read as describing every community identically.

Culture in Context — summer travel patterns

Visiting family in another city or region during the summer, as Marco does, is a common pattern across Italian-speaking contexts, though the length of typical summer breaks, travel habits, and family visiting customs differ by profession, region, and personal circumstance.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — missing the accent on io and lui/lei forms

Error: Writing parlero or partira without the accent.

Correction: parlerò and partirà. The accent is required spelling, not a stylistic choice, and it marks where the stress falls.

Common Mistake — using the present after appena for a future event

Error: Writing Appena arrivo, ti scriverò by analogy with the English "as soon as I arrive."

Correction: Appena arriverò, ti scriverò. Italian typically uses the future tense in both clauses when both events are still in the future.

Common Mistake — keeping the regular stem for irregular verbs

Error: Writing andarò or potrò incorrectly formed from the infinitive without adjusting the stem.

Correction: andrò (not andarò) and potrò keep the shortened irregular stem throughout the paradigm: andrai, andrà, andremo, andrete, andranno.

B verifica 3: Partecipare alla comunità

Volume II, Chapter 15: Review 3, Participating in the Community · Speak and Write Italian: Everyday Independence and Connected Communication

Chapter Overview and Objectives

Marco has one of those days that touches almost every corner of community life: he wakes up with a sore throat, needs to send a message to his professor about a missed deadline, has to sort out a problem with a package that never arrived, and finally meets a friend for coffee to talk about a film everyone has been discussing. This chapter does not teach anything new. Instead, it brings together everything from Chapters 11 through 14 — health and advice, formal study and work communication, service problems, and media opinions — into a single connected day, and asks you to show what you can already do. By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Describe symptoms and ask for or give simple advice using imperatives and modal verbs.
  2. Write and role-play a formal request using appropriate register and a relative clause with che.
  3. Explain a service problem and propose a solution using indirect object pronouns, ci, and ne.
  4. State and support an opinion about a media experience using comparatives, superlatives, and reason clauses.
  5. Select appropriate tu or Lei register across four different everyday situations.
  6. Understand a multi-step spoken account that moves through several community-life topics.
  7. Produce a connected spoken and written account of a full day that integrates all four topic areas.

Communication Scenario

Marco wakes up not feeling well, calls the pharmacy for advice, then remembers he still owes his professor an email about a late assignment. On his lunch break he stops by the post office because a package never arrived, and in the evening he finally meets his friend Chiara for coffee, where the conversation turns — predictably — to the same film Rita and Paolo discussed in Chapter 14.

  1. You will read and listen to Marco moving through his day: the pharmacy call, the email to his professor, the post office visit, and the conversation with Chiara.
  2. You will then complete an integrated task in which you narrate a similarly varied day of your own, using appropriate register throughout.

Pronunciation Review

Pronunciation Review — shifting rhythm across register

Today's dialogue moves through four different registers, and each one carries its own rhythm: the clipped, practical rhythm of a pharmacy call; the careful, evenly-paced rhythm of a formal email read aloud; the polite but firm rhythm of a service complaint; and the relaxed, opinion-stating rhythm you practiced in Chapter 14. Listen for these shifts as you go through the passage below.

15.1 Reviewing double consonants under pressure

Practice keeping double consonants clear even in faster, more urgent speech, as in non è ancora arrivato (non eh an-KO-ra ar-ree-VA-to), where the double rr should not shorten just because the speaker sounds impatient.

15.2 Reviewing formal intonation

Recall from Chapter 12 that formal requests tend to level out in pitch rather than rising sharply, as in Le scrivo per chiederLe un'informazione — a flatter, more measured contour than an informal request to a friend.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns Review

Language Focus — advice and imperatives (review of Chapter 11)

Review the informal and polite imperative forms you use to give advice: Prendi qualcosa per la gola (informal) and Prenda qualcosa per la gola (polite Lei). Modal verbs such as dovresti and expressions such as bisogna remain available for softer advice.

Language Focus — formal requests and relative clauses with che (review of Chapter 12)

Review the formal opening Le scrivo per... together with polite request forms such as vorrei and potrei. Relative clauses with che let you refer back to something already mentioned without repeating the noun: il compito che non ho ancora consegnato.

Language Focus — indirect object pronouns, ci, and ne in service contexts (review of Chapter 13)

Review indirect object pronouns such as mi, gli, and le in complaint and clarification contexts: Mi può dire quando arriverà? Review ci for place (non ci sono ancora andato) and ne for quantity or reference (non ne ho ricevuto nessuno).

Language Focus — comparison and opinion formulas (review of Chapter 14)

Review comparative and superlative structures (più ... di, il/la più ... di) together with opinion openers such as secondo me and per me, supported by reason clauses with perché, siccome, or dato che. Remember that penso che and mi sembra che often introduce a subjunctive form, which remains a recognition-only system until Volume III.

No new grammar system is introduced in this chapter. All four language-focus boxes above consolidate material already active since Chapters 11-14.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Marco moves through his day — a pharmacy call, a formal email read aloud, a stop at the post office, and coffee with his friend Chiara.

Farmacista Buongiorno, mi dica pure. Cosa sente? Bwon-JOR-no, mee DEE-ka PWO-reh. KO-za SEN-teh? Good morning, go ahead. What are you feeling?
Marco Ho mal di gola da ieri sera e mi fa anche male la testa. O mal dee GO-la da YEH-ree SEH-ra eh mee fa AN-keh MA-leh la TEH-sta. I've had a sore throat since last night and my head hurts too.
Farmacista Prenda queste pastiglie due volte al giorno e beva molta acqua. Se non passa in due giorni, deve andare dal medico. PREN-da KWEH-steh pa-STEE-lyeh DOO-eh VOL-teh al JOR-no eh BEH-va MOL-ta AK-kwa. Seh non PAS-sa in DOO-eh JOR-nee, DEH-veh an-DA-reh dal MEH-dee-ko. Take these tablets twice a day and drink plenty of water. If it doesn't pass in two days, you need to see a doctor.
Marco Grazie mille, molto gentile. GRA-tsee-eh MEEL-leh, MOL-to jen-TEE-leh. Thank you very much, very kind.

Later, Marco reads his formal email aloud before sending it.

Marco Gentile professoressa, Le scrivo per il compito che non ho ancora consegnato. Non sono stato bene questa settimana. Potrei consegnarlo venerdì? La ringrazio per la comprensione. Cordiali saluti, Marco. Jen-TEE-leh pro-fes-so-RES-sa, Leh SKREE-vo per eel kom-PEE-to keh non o an-KO-ra kon-seg-NA-to. Non SO-no STA-to BEH-neh KWEH-sta set-tee-MA-na. Po-TREH-ee kon-seg-NAR-lo veh-ner-DEE? La reen-GRA-tsee-o per la kom-pren-see-O-neh. Kor-DYA-lee sa-LOO-tee, MAR-ko. Dear Professor, I am writing about the assignment I have not yet submitted. I have not been well this week. Could I submit it on Friday? Thank you for your understanding. Best regards, Marco.

At the post office, Marco asks about a missing package.

Marco Buongiorno, aspetto un pacco da una settimana e non è ancora arrivato. Mi può dire quando arriverà? Bwon-JOR-no, a-SPET-to oon PAK-ko da OO-na set-tee-MA-na eh non eh an-KO-ra ar-ree-VA-to. Mee PWO deer-eh KWAN-do ar-ree-veh-RA? Good morning, I've been waiting for a package for a week and it still hasn't arrived. Can you tell me when it will arrive?
Impiegato Mi dispiace, controllo subito. Ne ha ricevuto un avviso di consegna? Mee dee-SPYA-cheh, kon-TROL-lo soo-BEE-to. Neh a ree-cheh-VOO-to oon av-VEE-zo dee kon-SEG-na? I'm sorry, I'll check right away. Have you received a delivery notice for it?
Marco No, non ne ho ricevuto nessuno. Ci sono altre soluzioni? No, non neh o ree-cheh-VOO-to nes-SOO-no. Chee SO-no AL-treh so-loo-tsee-O-nee? No, I haven't received any. Are there other solutions?

That evening, over coffee, Marco and Chiara discuss the film everyone has been talking about.

Chiara Allora, com'è andata la giornata? Hai risolto il problema del pacco? Al-LO-ra, ko-MEH an-DA-ta la jor-NA-ta? Ai ree-ZOL-to eel pro-BLEH-ma del PAK-ko? So, how was your day? Did you solve the package problem?
Marco Quasi. Ma cambiamo argomento — hai visto il film di cui parlano tutti? KWA-zee. Ma kam-BYA-mo ar-go-MEN-to — ai VEE-sto eel feelm dee koo-ee PAR-la-no TOOT-tee? Almost. But let's change the subject — have you seen the film everyone's talking about?
Chiara Sì! Secondo me è più interessante dell'ultimo film dello stesso regista. See! Seh-KON-do meh eh pyoo een-teh-reh-SAN-teh dell-OOL-tee-mo feelm DEL-lo STES-so reh-JEE-sta. Yes! In my opinion it's more interesting than the same director's last film.

Domande di comprensione — Comprehension questions:

  1. What advice does the pharmacist give Marco, and under what condition should he see a doctor instead?
  2. What does Marco ask his professor for, and how does he support the request?
  3. What has Marco not received that would help explain the missing package?
  4. How does Chiara compare the film to the director's earlier work?

With a partner, take turns giving the same piece of advice twice: once informally to a friend, and once politely to a customer, adjusting the imperative form each time.

  • Register shifts appropriately between informal, polite, and formal moments.
  • The formal request uses Le scrivo per... and a relative clause with che.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — one day, many registers

An ordinary day in many Italian-speaking contexts moves fluidly between informal, polite, and formal registers — a quick word with a pharmacist, a carefully worded email to a professor, a firm but polite complaint at a public counter, and a relaxed conversation with a friend. Learners should notice how quickly register can shift within a single day, rather than treating formality as a fixed personal trait.

Culture in Context — patience with public services

Service problems such as a delayed package are common everywhere, and the tone Marco uses — polite but direct — reflects one reasonable way to handle such situations across many Italian-speaking settings. Practices at post offices, universities, and pharmacies vary by institution and region, and this scenario should not be taken as describing every context identically.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — using the wrong imperative for the audience

Error: Saying Prendi qualcosa to a pharmacy customer you do not know.

Correction: Use the polite Lei imperative in public service contexts: Prenda qualcosa per la gola. Reserve the informal imperative for friends, family, and peers.

Common Mistake — dropping ne or ci in service complaints

Error: Writing Non ho ricevuto when the reference to "any of it" is needed, or Non sono ancora andato when "there" is needed.

Correction: Non ne ho ricevuto nessuno and Non ci sono ancora andato. These clitics are required, not optional, in natural Italian.

Common Mistake — mixing formal and informal address within one message

Error: Opening an email with Gentile professoressa and then switching to tu forms later in the same message.

Correction: Keep Lei forms consistent throughout a formal message: Potrebbe, not Potresti, once the formal register has been established.

Acquisti, servizi e problemi pratici

Volume II, Chapter 13: Purchases, Services, and Practical Problems · Speak and Write Italian: Everyday Independence and Connected Communication

Chapter Overview and Objectives

Rita's extension request went well, but now a different kind of problem has landed on her doorstep: an online order that arrived broken. Down the hall, Paolo's neighbor has a leaking washing machine and no idea how to ask a technician to come fix it. This chapter moves Unit III into the practical, sometimes frustrating world of purchases and repairs, where getting help means being clear about a problem without sounding rude. You will develop the indirect object pronouns from Chapters 11 and 12 further, meet ci and ne for the first time in controlled everyday patterns, and take your first careful look at combined pronouns. By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Describe a practical problem with a purchase, delivery, or repair using vocabulary from this chapter.
  2. Request a repair, exchange, refund, or clarification appropriately.
  3. Use indirect object pronouns mi, ti, gli, le, ci, and vi in service contexts.
  4. Use ci for place and ne for quantity or reference in controlled patterns.
  5. Recognize combined pronouns such as me lo and glielo without full productive command.
  6. Read receipts, notices, and short service messages for practical information.
  7. Write a short complaint or service request that is firm but polite.

Communication Scenario

A package Rita ordered a week ago has finally arrived — with a cracked lamp inside. She needs to contact the seller, explain the problem, and ask for a replacement or a refund without sounding angry. Meanwhile, Paolo's neighbor Signora Rossi has a washing machine that has been leaking for two days, and Paolo offers to help her call a technician and explain the problem clearly.

  1. You will read and listen to Rita contacting a customer service line, and to Paolo helping Signora Rossi describe her problem to a technician.
  2. You will then complete a task in which you describe a practical problem and write a complaint or service request of your own.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — clitic-pronoun rhythm and polite complaint intonation

Clitic pronouns attach closely to the verb that follows them, forming a single rhythmic unit: Mi pause-free into manda in Mi manda un tecnico? is said as one smooth phrase, not as two separated words. A polite complaint keeps a level, unhurried tone rather than a sharp rising or falling pitch: Purtroppo il pacco è arrivato rotto is delivered evenly, without the clipped emphasis that would sound like frustration in English.

13.1 Stress and rhythm in clitic-verb groups

Practice the smooth join in gliel'ho detto (lyel-O DET-to) and ce l'ha (cheh LA), where two pronouns combine before a verb. Stress remains on the verb, never on the clitic itself.

13.2 Softening tone in spoken complaints

Compare a flat statement of fact, Il pacco è rotto, with a softened complaint, Purtroppo il pacco è arrivato rotto e vorrei chiedere un sostituto. The added words slow the phrase and give it a more even, less accusatory rhythm.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

Language Focus — indirect object pronouns in service contexts

Chapters 11 and 12 introduced indirect object pronouns in advice and formal address. This chapter develops the same system in everyday service situations, where you say what happens to or for someone.

PronounMeaningExample
mito meMi manda un tecnico, per favore?
tito you (informal)Ti spiego il problema.
glito him / to themGli ho scritto per il reso.
leto herLe ho chiesto un rimborso.
cito usCi hanno mandato il pacco sbagliato.
vito you (plural)Vi scrivo per segnalare un problema.

Indirect object pronouns are placed directly before a conjugated verb, as in the examples above, or attached to an infinitive: Devo scrivergli ("I have to write to him"). Both placements are correct; this chapter practices both.

Language Focus — ci and ne in controlled patterns

Ci and ne are small words that replace a place or a quantity so you do not have to repeat it.

WordReplacesExample
cia place already mentionedVai al negozio? Sì, ci vado domani.
cia situation or idea (idiomatic)Non ci credo!
nea quantity of somethingQuanti pacchi hai ricevuto? Ne ho ricevuto uno.
ne"of it / of them" (topic reference)Ne parliamo domani.

This chapter limits ci and ne to high-frequency service and everyday patterns. Neither word translates as a single English word; learn each through its pattern rather than a fixed equivalent. Expanded uses of ci and ne return in later chapters and volumes.

Language Focus — combined pronouns (recognition)

Italian can combine an indirect object pronoun with a direct object pronoun, as in me lo ("it to me") or glielo ("it to him/her," written as one word). You will recognize these combinations in this chapter's reading and listening, but you are not expected to build them productively yet.

Combined formMeaningExample
me loit to meMe lo può spedire di nuovo?
glieloit to him/her (one word)Gliel'ho già spiegato.

Full combined-pronoun formation and clitic clusters become productive in a later volume. Here, treat combined forms as recognition items supported by glossing.

Relative clauses with che and formal request patterns from Chapter 12 remain fully active and may be recycled naturally throughout this chapter's tasks.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Rita is on the phone with a customer service representative about her broken delivery.

Rita Buongiorno. Vorrei segnalare un problema con un ordine che ho ricevuto ieri. Purtroppo il pacco è arrivato rotto. Bwon-JOR-no. Vo-RAY seh-nya-LA-reh oon pro-BLEH-ma kon oon OR-dee-neh keh o ree-cheh-VOO-to YEH-ree. Poor-TROP-po eel PAK-ko eh ar-ree-VA-to ROT-to. Good morning. I would like to report a problem with an order I received yesterday. Unfortunately the package arrived broken.
Operatore Mi dispiace molto. Può dirmi il numero dell'ordine, per favore? Mee dees-PYA-cheh MOL-to. Poo-O DEER-mee eel NOO-meh-ro dell-OR-dee-neh, per fa-VO-reh? I'm very sorry. Can you tell me the order number, please?
Rita Sì, certo. È il numero 4471. Nell'ordine c'era una lampada che è arrivata rotta. See, CHER-to. Eh eel NOO-meh-ro kwat-tro-KWAT-tro-set-tay-OO-no. Nell-OR-dee-neh CHEH-ra OO-na LAM-pa-da keh eh ar-ree-VA-ta ROT-ta. Yes, of course. It's number 4471. In the order there was a lamp that arrived broken.

What do you prefer?

Operatore Certo, gliela mandiamo subito. Ne riceve conferma via email tra poco. CHER-to, lyel-la man-DYA-mo soo-BEE-to. Neh ree-CHEH-veh kon-FER-ma VEE-a EE-mayl tra PO-ko. Of course, we're sending it to you right away. You will receive confirmation of it by email shortly.

Writing Workshop

Write a short complaint or service request about a purchase, delivery, or repair problem.

Compito di scrittura — Writing task: Write a message of five to seven sentences. Include: a brief subject line; a description of the problem using at least one relative clause with che; at least one indirect object pronoun; a softened complaint expression such as purtroppo or mi dispiace, ma...; a clearly stated request; and an appropriate closing.

  1. Write a subject line. State the problem briefly.
  2. Open politely. Use Buongiorno or Gentile....
  3. Describe the problem. Use purtroppo and a relative clause with che where useful.
  4. Make your request. Ask clearly for a repair, replacement, or refund.
  5. Close formally. Use Cordiali saluti and sign your name.

Lista di controllo — Editing checklist:

  • The complaint is clear but remains polite throughout.
  • At least one indirect object pronoun is placed correctly.
  • Accents, apostrophes, and double consonants follow standard Italian spelling.
  • The request (repair, replacement, or refund) is stated clearly.
  • The closing matches the level of formality used in the opening.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — softening a complaint

In many Italian service contexts, a complaint is expected to remain calm and polite even when the customer is frustrated. Words like purtroppo and phrases like vorrei segnalare soften a statement of fact without weakening the request itself. Learners should avoid translating a blunt English complaint word for word.

Culture in Context — neighbors and everyday help

Asking a neighbor for help with a practical problem, or helping a neighbor make a call, is common in many Italian communities, though the closeness of neighbor relationships varies by building, town, and region. The example in this chapter reflects one everyday situation and should not be taken as universal.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — confusing ci and ne

Error: Writing Ne vado domani when replacing a place already mentioned.

Correction: Ci vado domani. Ci replaces a place; ne replaces a quantity or topic.

Common Mistake — placing the indirect object pronoun after the verb

Error: Writing Ho scritto gli per il reso, placing gli after the conjugated verb as in English word order.

Correction: Gli ho scritto per il reso. The pronoun goes directly before the conjugated verb.

Common Mistake — complaining too directly

Error: Writing Il pacco è rotto, voglio i soldi subito, a demand with no softening.

Correction: Purtroppo il pacco è arrivato rotto e vorrei chiedere un rimborso, which states the same request with a softer, more polite tone.

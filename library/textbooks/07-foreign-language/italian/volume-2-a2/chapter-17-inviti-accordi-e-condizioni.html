Inviti, accordi e condizioni

Volume II, Chapter 17: Invitations, Agreements, and Conditions · Speak and Write Italian: Everyday Independence and Connected Communication

Chapter Overview and Objectives

Chiara's photography course does not start for a few weeks, and in the meantime, she and Marco keep trying to find time to see each other. But calendars do not always cooperate: Marco has a work deadline, Chiara has a class night that keeps shifting, and both of them are learning to propose, soften, and rearrange plans without giving up on seeing each other. This chapter introduces the conditional present, the polite and hypothetical mood you have heard in passing as vorrei and potrei, and shows you how to build simple conditions with se. By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Form the conditional present of regular -are, -ere, and -ire verbs.
  2. Use the conditional present of high-frequency irregular verbs, including essere, avere, potere, dovere, volere, and piacere.
  3. Make, accept, refuse, and renegotiate invitations politely.
  4. Express wishes and soften requests using the conditional.
  5. Build simple se clauses with present or future meaning.
  6. Propose an alternative when a first plan does not work.
  7. Understand a short spoken negotiation between two people making plans.
  8. Write an invitation, a polite refusal, and an alternative proposal.

Communication Scenario

Marco wants to invite Chiara to a concert next Friday, but Chiara is not sure she is free — it depends on whether her course schedule changes. As they text back and forth, they practice proposing a plan, expressing a condition, and offering an alternative if the first idea does not work out.

  1. You will read and listen to Marco and Chiara negotiating an invitation by message.
  2. You will then write and act out your own invitation, refusal, and alternative proposal.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — stress in the conditional

Like the future, the conditional stresses the ending rather than the stem, but every conditional form after the io form carries the stress on the syllable just before the final -i: parleréi is wrong — the correct form is parlerei (par-leh-REH-ee), with the stress falling on the second-to-last syllable, not on a written accent. Only the noi form has predictable stress on the ending itself: parleremmo (par-leh-REM-mo).

17.1 Distinguishing future and conditional by ear

Future and conditional forms share the same modified stem (parler-, andr-, potr-) but differ in their endings and stress pattern: future parlerò versus conditional parlerei; future parlerà versus conditional parlerebbe. Listen carefully for the double -mm- in the noi form and the double -bb- in the lui/lei and loro forms, since these consonant sounds carry meaning.

17.2 Intonation for polite requests

Requests with the conditional typically use a gentle, slightly rising intonation that signals politeness rather than urgency, as in Potresti venire venerdì? — the voice softens rather than sharpens toward the end of the question.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

Language Focus — the conditional present of regular verbs

To form the conditional present of regular verbs, use the same modified stem as the future simple and add the conditional endings. As with the future, -are verbs change their stem vowel to -e-.

You have already used vorrei, potrei, and mi piacerebbe as formulaic polite expressions since Volume I. This chapter connects those familiar forms to the full conditional paradigm for the first time.

Language Focus — the conditional for polite requests and wishes

The conditional softens a request, suggestion, or wish, making it less direct than the present tense or the imperative: Potresti venire venerdì? (Could you come Friday?) is gentler than Puoi venire venerdì? (Can you come Friday?). Use vorrei to state a wish, potrei to suggest a possibility, dovrei to acknowledge a soft obligation, and mi piacerebbe to express a hope or desire.

Language Focus — simple se clauses

A simple condition pairs se with the present or future tense in the se clause, and the present, future, or imperative in the result clause, depending on how certain or immediate the condition feels: Se sono libera, vengo (If I'm free, I'm coming); Se avrò tempo, ti chiamerò (If I have time, I'll call you). Do not mix the conditional mood into this pattern yet — forms such as se sarei libera are incorrect. The conditional appears only in the result clause of a different, more hypothetical pattern that Volume III will develop fully; for now, treat se + present/future as the reliable productive pattern.

Language Focus — proposing an alternative

When a plan does not work, Italian speakers often propose an alternative rather than simply refusing: Non posso venerdì, ma sabato andrebbe bene? (I can't Friday, but would Saturday work?). Useful connectors for this pattern include altrimenti, in caso contrario, and magari, each softening the shift to a new proposal.

This chapter reviews future forms and social-invitation vocabulary from earlier chapters within the new conditional context; it does not introduce the imperfect subjunctive or full hypothetical periods, which remain outside Volume II.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Marco and Chiara text each other about a concert next Friday.

Marco Ciao! Ti andrebbe di venire a un concerto venerdì sera? CHA-o! Tee an-DREB-beh dee veh-NEE-reh a oon kon-CHER-to veh-ner-DEE seh-ra? Hi! Would you be interested in coming to a concert Friday night?
Chiara Mi piacerebbe molto! Ma dipende — dovrei controllare l'orario del corso. Potrebbe cambiare. Mee pya-cheh-REB-beh MOL-to! Ma dee-PEN-deh — do-VREH-ee kon-trol-LA-reh lo-RA-ryo del KOR-so. Po-TREB-beh kam-BYA-reh. I'd love to! But it depends — I'd need to check the course schedule. It could change.
Marco Va bene lo stesso. Se il corso non cambia, ci vediamo alle otto davanti al teatro. Va BEH-neh lo STES-so. Seh eel KOR-so non KAM-bya, chee veh-DYA-mo AL-leh OT-to da-VAN-tee al teh-A-tro. That's fine either way. If the course doesn't change, we'll meet at eight in front of the theater.
Chiara Perfetto. E se il corso cambia, ti farò sapere subito. In caso contrario, ci saremmo comunque sabato, no? Per-FET-to. Eh seh eel KOR-so KAM-bya, tee fa-ROH sa-PEH-reh SOO-bee-to. Een KA-zo kon-TRA-ryo, chee sa-REM-mo ko-MOON-kweh SA-ba-to, no? Perfect. And if the course changes, I'll let you know right away. Otherwise, we'd still be seeing each other Saturday, right?
Marco Sì, esatto. Vorrei anche invitare mio fratello, se per te va bene. See, eh-ZAT-to. Vor-REH-ee AN-keh een-vee-TA-reh MEE-o fra-TEL-lo, seh per teh va BEH-neh. Yes, exactly. I'd also like to invite my brother, if that's okay with you.
Chiara Certo, mi farebbe piacere conoscerlo! Potresti chiedergli se gli piace questo tipo di musica? CHER-to, mee fa-REB-beh pya-CHEH-reh ko-NO-sher-lo! Po-TRES-tee kye-DER-lyee seh lyee PYA-cheh KWES-to TEE-po dee MOO-zee-ka? Of course, I'd be glad to meet him! Could you ask him if he likes this type of music?
Marco Glielo chiederò stasera. A presto! LYEH-lo kye-deh-ROH sta-SEH-ra. A PRES-to! I'll ask him tonight. See you soon!

Domande di comprensione — Comprehension questions:

  1. What does Marco invite Chiara to do, and when?
  2. Why is Chiara not sure she can come?
  3. What will Chiara do if the course schedule changes?
  4. Who does Marco want to invite, and what does he ask Chiara?

Writing Workshop

Write a short message thread inviting someone to an event, similar to Marco and Chiara's exchange or the restaurant message above.

Compito di scrittura — Writing task: Write eight to ten sentences. Include: at least three conditional verbs, one polite request, one se clause with present or future, and one alternative proposal using altrimenti or magari.

  1. Make the invitation. Propose an event and ask politely with the conditional.
  2. State a condition. Use a se clause with the present or future tense to explain what the plan depends on.
  3. Offer an alternative. In case the first plan does not work, propose a second option.
  4. Confirm or soften. Close with a polite confirmation or a softened, friendly tone.

Lista di controllo — Editing checklist:

  • Conditional verbs are formed correctly, sharing the future's modified stem.
  • At least one irregular conditional stem is used correctly.
  • The se clause uses the present or future tense, not the conditional.
  • An alternative is proposed using a natural connector.
  • The register (tu or Lei) is consistent throughout.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — softening refusals and proposing alternatives

In many Italian-speaking contexts, a flat refusal can feel abrupt among friends and colleagues, so speakers often pair a refusal with an alternative or an explanation, as Marco and Chiara do throughout this chapter. This tendency varies by relationship, region, and personal style, and it should not be read as a universal rule for every situation.

Culture in Context — text messaging and informal planning

Planning by text message is common among friends and family across Italian-speaking communities, often mixing informal tone with the conditional forms taught in this chapter to keep requests polite even in a casual medium.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — conditional after se in a simple condition

Error: Writing Se sarei libera, verrei.

Correction: Se sono libera, vengo or Se sarò libera, verrò. A simple se clause uses the present or future, never the conditional, in the se clause itself.

Common Mistake — confusing future and conditional stress

Error: Pronouncing parlerei with stress on the final syllable, as in the future parlerò.

Correction: The conditional io and tu forms stress the second-to-last syllable: par-leh-REH-ee, par-leh-RES-tee. Only the future carries stress onto the very last syllable in the io and lui/lei forms.

Common Mistake — using the conditional for a settled future plan

Error: Writing Verrei venerdì to state a plan that is already certain.

Correction: Verrò venerdì (future, for a settled plan) or Vengo venerdì (present, for a near, settled plan). Reserve the conditional for wishes, polite requests, and possibilities that remain uncertain.

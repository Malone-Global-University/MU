Studio, lavoro e comunicazione formale

Volume II, Chapter 12: Study, Work, and Formal Communication · Speak and Write Italian: Everyday Independence and Connected Communication

Chapter Overview and Objectives

Rita is finally feeling better, but her cold cost her a missed class and a deadline she now has to explain. Paolo is back at work too, behind on a project his colleagues covered for him. This chapter moves Unit III from health into the practical business of study and work: describing tasks and responsibilities, meeting deadlines, and above all, writing the kind of formal message that keeps a relationship with a professor, employer, or colleague running smoothly. You will meet relative clauses with che for the first time, letting you describe people and things more precisely, and you will develop the indirect object pronouns from Chapter 11 into the more careful language of institutional communication. By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Discuss study and work tasks, responsibilities, and deadlines using vocabulary from this chapter.
  2. Make a formal request by email or message using patterns such as vorrei, potrei, and Le scrivo per....
  3. Form and use relative clauses with che to describe people, things, and situations.
  4. Use indirect object pronouns in formal communication, including Le.
  5. Distinguish informal chat from formal written communication in tone, layout, and vocabulary.
  6. Read a formal email or institutional announcement for practical information.
  7. Write a formal request email with an appropriate opening, body, and closing.

Communication Scenario

A week after the pharmacy visit, Rita and Paolo are both back on their feet — but not caught up. Rita missed a class and an assignment deadline while she was sick, and needs to write to her professor to explain and ask for an extension. Paolo, meanwhile, has a backlog of work tasks waiting after his colleagues covered his shifts. Sara, who writes formal emails every day at her office job, sits down with Rita to help her get the tone right.

  1. You will read and listen to Sara helping Rita draft a formal email, and to Paolo discussing his workload with a colleague.
  2. You will then complete a task in which you describe study or work responsibilities and write a formal request email of your own.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — formal speech rhythm and polite request intonation

Formal spoken Italian tends to move more slowly and evenly than casual conversation, with fewer overlaps and clearer separation between phrases. A polite request such as Potrei chiederLe un favore? is said with a gentle rise on favore, softer than the falling intonation of an informal request among friends. Read the model formal email aloud slowly, pausing at each comma, to hear how written formality carries into spoken delivery when the email is read out loud.

12.1 Stress in formal words and titles

Practice the stress in responsabilità (word-final stress, marked by the accent) and professoressa (pro-fes-so-RES-sa). Titles are almost always used with the definite article in formal reference: il Professor Bianchi, not simply Professor Bianchi, when speaking about rather than to someone.

12.2 Formal email layout and subject-line conventions

Compare the layout of a chat message to a friend with the layout of a formal email: a clear subject line, a formal salutation with title, a body organized into short paragraphs, and a closing formula before the signature. Read both aloud and notice how the written structure changes the rhythm of delivery even before a single word is spoken.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

Language Focus — relative clauses with che

Che means "that," "which," or "who," and it lets you add a description to a noun without starting a new sentence. Unlike English, che is never optional and never changes form for gender, number, or whether it refers to a person or a thing.

SentenceEnglish
Il professore che insegna storia si chiama Bianchi.The professor who teaches history is called Bianchi.
Il compito che devo finire è molto lungo.The assignment that I have to finish is very long.
La collega che mi ha aiutato si chiama Sara.The colleague who helped me is called Sara.

Che can refer to the subject or the object of its clause; this chapter keeps both patterns simple and high-frequency. More complex relative pronouns such as cui and il quale are introduced in a later volume.

Language Focus — formal requests: vorrei, potrei, and Le scrivo per...

Formal requests soften directness and show respect for the reader's time and position.

PatternUseExample
Vorrei + infinitivepolite statement of a wishVorrei chiedere una proroga.
Potrei + infinitive?polite question asking permissionPotrei consegnare il compito lunedì?
Le scrivo per + infinitive/nounformal email opening, stating purposeLe scrivo per spiegare la mia assenza.

These formulas were introduced as recognition or formulaic material in earlier chapters and now become fully productive for formal written and spoken requests. The full conditional system, including vorrei and potrei as forms of a complete paradigm, is developed productively in Chapter 17.

Language Focus — indirect object pronouns in formal communication

Chapter 11 introduced mi, ti, gli, and le in everyday advice. This chapter develops the same system into formal address, where Le (capitalized in formal writing) means "to you" when addressing someone as Lei.

PronounMeaningExample
Le (formal)to you (formal)Le scrivo per chiedere un'informazione.
leto herLe ho mandato un'email ieri.
glito him / to them (informal, common usage)Gli ho chiesto scusa per il ritardo.

In formal written Italian, Le referring to the reader is capitalized as a sign of respect. Combined pronouns and full clitic clusters remain outside this chapter's scope.

Imperatives, advice patterns, and modal verbs from Chapter 11 remain fully active and may be recycled naturally throughout this chapter's tasks.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Sara is at Rita's kitchen table, helping her draft a formal email to her professor.

Rita Non so come iniziare. Il professore che insegna il mio corso è molto serio. Non so KO-meh ee-NEE-tsya-reh. Eel pro-fes-SO-reh keh een-SE-nya eel MEE-o KOR-so eh MOL-to SEH-ryo. I don't know how to start. The professor who teaches my course is very serious.
Sara Tranquilla. Inizia con "Gentile Professore" e poi scrivi subito il motivo: "Le scrivo per spiegare la mia assenza alla lezione di giovedì." Tran-KWEE-la. Ee-NEE-tsya kon "JEN-tee-leh Pro-fes-SO-reh" eh po-ee SKREE-vee soo-BEE-to eel mo-TEE-vo: "Leh SKREE-vo per spye-GA-reh la MEE-a as-SEN-tsa AL-la leh-TSYO-neh dee jo-veh-DEE." Relax. Start with "Dear Professor" and then write the reason right away: "I am writing to explain my absence from Thursday's class."
Rita E poi devo chiedere una proroga per il compito che dovevo consegnare lunedì. Come lo dico senza sembrare scortese? Eh po-ee DEH-vo kee-EH-deh-reh OO-na pro-RO-ga per eel kom-PEE-to keh do-VEH-vo kon-seh-NYA-reh loo-neh-DEE. KO-meh lo DEE-ko SEN-tsa sem-BRA-reh skor-TEH-zeh? And then I have to ask for an extension for the assignment I was supposed to turn in on Monday. How do I say it without seeming rude?
Sara Prova così: "Vorrei chiedere una proroga per il compito, se possibile. Potrei consegnarlo entro venerdì?" È chiaro e rispettoso. PRO-va ko-ZEE: "Vo-RAY kee-EH-deh-reh OO-na pro-RO-ga per eel kom-PEE-to, seh pos-SEE-bee-leh. Po-TRAY kon-seh-NYAR-lo EN-tro veh-neh-r-DEE?" Eh KYA-ro eh ree-spet-TO-zo. Try this: "I would like to ask for an extension for the assignment, if possible. Could I turn it in by Friday?" It's clear and respectful.
Rita Perfetto. E come lo chiudo? Per-FET-to. Eh KO-meh lo KYOO-do? Perfect. And how do I close it?
Sara "Cordiali saluti" va benissimo con un professore. E ricorda: la maiuscola su "Le" quando ti riferisci a lui. "Kor-DYA-lee sa-LOO-tee" va beh-NEES-see-mo kon oon pro-fes-SO-reh. Eh ree-KOR-da: la ma-yoos-KO-la soo "Leh" KWAN-do tee ree-feh-REE-shee a LOO-ee. "Kind regards" is perfectly fine with a professor. And remember: the capital letter on "Le" when you refer to him.
Rita Grazie mille, Sara. Il collega che ti ha insegnato tutto questo è stato bravo. GRA-tsee-eh MEEL-leh, SA-ra. Eel kol-LEH-ga keh tee a een-seh-NYA-to TOOT-to KWES-to eh STA-to BRA-vo. Thanks so much, Sara. The colleague who taught you all this did a good job.

Writing Workshop

Write a formal email requesting an extension, a meeting, or information from a professor, employer, or institution.

Compito di scrittura — Writing task: Write a complete email of six to eight sentences. Include: a subject line; a formal salutation with title; at least one relative clause with che; at least one formal request pattern (vorrei, potrei, or Le scrivo per); at least one formal indirect object pronoun; and an appropriate closing.

  1. Write a subject line. State the purpose briefly.
  2. Open formally. Use Gentile plus the correct title.
  3. State your purpose. Use Le scrivo per....
  4. Make your request. Use vorrei or potrei.
  5. Close formally. Use Cordiali saluti or Distinti saluti and sign your name.

Lista di controllo — Editing checklist:

  • Salutation and closing match the formal register throughout.
  • Le referring to the reader is capitalized where used.
  • Relative clause with che is used correctly and does not change form.
  • Accents, apostrophes, and double consonants follow standard Italian spelling.
  • Tone remains polite and professional throughout, without casual expressions.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — formal email conventions

Italian formal emails typically open with a title-based salutation, state the purpose early, and close with a set formula before the signature. This structure is expected in academic and institutional communication and should not be replaced with the more casual habits common in English-language workplace email.

Culture in Context — workplace roles and relationships

How colleagues cover for one another, and how directly a request is made to a supervisor, varies by workplace, sector, region, and individual relationship. The examples in this chapter reflect one practical, everyday workplace situation and should not be taken as universal across all Italian workplaces.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — omitting che

Error: Writing Il compito devo finire è lungo, dropping che as English sometimes drops "that."

Correction: Il compito che devo finire è lungo. Che is required in Italian relative clauses and can never be omitted.

Common Mistake — mixing informal and formal register in the same email

Error: Writing Ciao Professoressa, volevo chiederLe..., opening with an informal greeting and then switching to formal address.

Correction: Gentile Professoressa, Le scrivo per..., keeping the salutation and the rest of the email consistently formal.

Common Mistake — lowercase le when addressing the reader formally

Error: Writing le scrivo per... in a formal email addressed directly to the reader, using a lowercase letter.

Correction: Le scrivo per..., capitalizing Le as a mark of respect when it refers to the person being addressed.

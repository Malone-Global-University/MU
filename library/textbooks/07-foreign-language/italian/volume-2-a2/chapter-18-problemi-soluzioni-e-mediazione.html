Problemi, soluzioni e mediazione

Volume II, Chapter 18: Problems, Solutions, and Mediation · Speak and Write Italian: Everyday Independence and Connected Communication

Chapter Overview and Objectives

The heating in Marco's apartment has stopped working properly, and the building manager only answers calls during the day, when Marco is at his desk and cannot easily step away. Chiara offers to call on his behalf, but that means she has to explain Marco's problem clearly, ask the right questions on his behalf, and then report back to him exactly what the manager said — without simply repeating every word. This chapter teaches you how to ask an indirect question with chiedere se, report what someone said with dire che and spiegare che, and suggest a solution politely using the conditional you learned in Chapter 17. By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Explain a practical problem clearly, identifying its cause and its effect.
  2. Ask an indirect question using chiedere se and question words.
  3. Report what someone said using dire che and spiegare che.
  4. Propose a solution or next step using conditional suggestions such as dovresti, potresti, and sarebbe meglio.
  5. Mediate between a short written or spoken message and another person.
  6. Review clitic pronoun placement in reported speech and suggestions.
  7. Understand a short spoken explanation of a problem and its proposed solution.
  8. Write a clear, solution-oriented explanation adjusted to its audience.

Communication Scenario

Marco's heating has been making a strange noise and barely warms the apartment. He cannot call the building manager, il signor Bruni, during working hours, so Chiara offers to call for him. After the call, she has to explain to Marco what the problem might be, what the manager proposed, and what Marco should do next.

  1. You will read and listen to Chiara reporting the phone call to Marco.
  2. You will then write and act out your own explanation of a problem and a mediated report of someone else's response.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — clarity and pacing in explanation

When explaining a problem or reporting information, Italian speakers typically slow down slightly at the key facts — the cause, the proposed solution, and the next step — and use a small pause before a connector like però or quindi to signal that an important shift is coming: Il tecnico ha detto che... [pausa] però non può venire prima di giovedì. This pacing helps the listener follow reported information without needing to ask for repetition.

18.1 Intonation in reported speech versus direct speech

A direct question rises at the end: Può venire oggi? (Can you come today?). The same idea reported indirectly flattens into a falling statement: Gli ho chiesto se poteva venire oggi (I asked him if he could come today). Practice hearing and producing this shift from rising question intonation to falling statement intonation.

18.2 Stress in reporting verbs

Keep the stress on the correct syllable in high-frequency reporting verbs: ha spiegato (ha spye-GA-to), ha detto (ha DET-to), ha chiesto (ha KYES-to). Confusing the stress can make these common past participles harder to recognize in connected speech.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

Language Focus — indirect questions with chiedere se

To report a yes-or-no question that someone asked, use chiedere se plus a clause. The word order of the original question disappears, and the clause follows ordinary statement order: direct «Può venire giovedì?» becomes indirect Gli ho chiesto se poteva venire giovedì (I asked him if he could come Thursday). Notice that the verb inside the se clause often shifts from present to imperfetto when the reporting verb is in a past tense, since the report describes a past moment.

Direct questionIndirect report
«Il riscaldamento funziona?»Gli ho chiesto se il riscaldamento funzionava.
«Può mandare un tecnico?»Le ho chiesto se poteva mandare un tecnico.
«Quando può venire?»Gli ho chiesto quando poteva venire.

With a question word such as quando, dove, or perché, simply keep the question word in place of se and follow it with ordinary statement order.

Language Focus — reported statements with dire che and spiegare che

To report what someone said or explained, use dire che or spiegare che plus a clause. As with indirect questions, a present-tense statement in the original often shifts to imperfetto when reported after a past-tense reporting verb: direct «Il guasto è nella caldaia» becomes indirect Ha detto che il guasto era nella caldaia (He said that the problem was in the boiler).

Direct statementReported statement
«Non posso venire prima di giovedì.»Ha detto che non poteva venire prima di giovedì.
«Il problema è comune in questo palazzo.»Ha spiegato che il problema era comune in quel palazzo.

Place references also shift in reported speech: questo palazzo (this building, said on site) becomes quel palazzo (that building) when reported to someone elsewhere. You are not required to master every shift in this chapter, but you should recognize and reproduce the most common ones.

Language Focus — conditional suggestions

Use the conditional you learned in Chapter 17 to propose a solution without sounding like an order: Dovresti segnalarlo per iscritto (You should report it in writing); Potresti chiamare di nuovo domani (You could call again tomorrow); Sarebbe meglio aspettare la risposta del tecnico (It would be better to wait for the technician's response). Sarebbe meglio plus an infinitive is a useful impersonal suggestion when you do not want to name who should act.

Language Focus — clitic review in reported speech and suggestions

Reported speech and suggestions frequently combine with the clitic pronouns you have been building since Chapter 13. Review their placement: clitics attach to an infinitive (segnalarlo, chiamarlo di nuovo) and precede a conjugated verb (gli ho chiesto, le ho detto). With a modal verb plus infinitive, the clitic may attach to the infinitive or move before the modal: dovresti segnalarlo or lo dovresti segnalare are both correct.

This chapter reviews clitic placement, past tense, and conditional forms from earlier chapters within a mediation context; it does not require full subjunctive control, imperfect subjunctive, or advanced legal, medical, or bureaucratic vocabulary, which remain outside Volume II.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Chiara calls Marco after speaking with the building manager, il signor Bruni, about Marco's heating.

Chiara Allora, ho parlato con il signor Bruni. Gli ho spiegato che il riscaldamento faceva uno strano rumore e che scaldava poco. Al-LO-ra, o par-LA-to kon eel see-NYOR BROO-nee. Lyee o spye-GA-to keh eel ree-skal-da-MEN-to fa-CHEH-va OO-no STRA-no roo-MO-reh eh keh SKAL-da-va PO-ko. So, I spoke with Mr. Bruni. I explained to him that the heating was making a strange noise and that it was barely warming the apartment.
Marco Grazie mille! Cosa ha detto? GRA-tsye MEEL-leh! KO-za a DET-to? Thank you so much! What did he say?
Chiara Ha detto che il problema era comune in quel palazzo, probabilmente la caldaia. Gli ho chiesto se poteva mandare un tecnico, e mi ha detto di sì. A DET-to keh eel pro-BLEH-ma EH-ra ko-MOO-neh een kwel pa-LAT-tso, pro-ba-beel-MEN-teh la kal-DA-ya. Lyee o KYES-to seh po-TEH-va man-DA-reh oon TEK-nee-ko, eh mee a DET-to dee SEE. He said the problem was common in that building, probably the boiler. I asked him if he could send a technician, and he said yes.
Marco Perfetto. Quando può venire? Per-FET-to. KWAN-do PWO veh-NEE-reh? Perfect. When can he come?
Chiara Gli ho chiesto quando poteva venire, e ha detto che non poteva venire prima di giovedì. Nel frattempo, secondo lui, dovresti tenere la caldaia accesa a bassa temperatura. Lyee o KYES-to KWAN-do po-TEH-va veh-NEE-reh, eh a DET-to keh non po-TEH-va veh-NEE-reh PREE-ma dee jo-veh-DEE. Nel frat-TEM-po, seh-KON-do LOO-ee, do-VRES-tee teh-NEH-reh la kal-DA-ya a-CHEH-za a BAS-sa tem-peh-ra-TOO-ra. I asked him when he could come, and he said he couldn't come before Thursday. In the meantime, according to him, you should keep the boiler on at a low temperature.
Marco Va bene, lo farò. Forse però sarebbe meglio segnalarlo anche per iscritto, no? Va BEH-neh, lo fa-ROH. FOR-seh peh-ROH sa-REB-beh MEH-lyo se-nya-LAR-lo AN-keh per ee-SKREET-to, no? Okay, I'll do that. But maybe it would be better to report it in writing too, right?
Chiara Sì, secondo me dovresti anche scrivergli una breve email, così c'è una data precisa. See, seh-KON-do meh do-VRES-tee AN-keh SKREE-ver-lyee OO-na BREH-veh ee-MAYL, ko-ZEE cheh OO-na DA-ta preh-CHEE-za. Yes, in my opinion you should also write him a brief email, so there's a precise record.

Domande di comprensione — Comprehension questions:

  1. What problem does Chiara explain to Mr. Bruni?
  2. What does Mr. Bruni say is the probable cause?
  3. What does Chiara ask Mr. Bruni, and what does he answer?
  4. What two things does Chiara suggest Marco do while he waits?

Speaking Practice

Pratica controllata — Controlled practice: report the message

With a partner, one person reads a short direct statement or question aloud, and the other reports it back using dire che, spiegare che, or chiedere se.

Compito comunicativo — Communicative task: mediate a problem

In pairs or as a solo recording, explain a practical problem (at home, at school, or at work) to a partner (real or imagined), then report what a third person supposedly said about it, and propose a solution using the conditional.

Pratica da solo — Solo alternative

Record yourself explaining the same problem twice: once directly to the person responsible, and once reporting the response to a friend, adjusting your register and word choice for each audience.

Writing Workshop

Write a short explanatory note mediating a problem between two people, similar to Chiara's note to Marco above.

Compito di scrittura — Writing task: Write eight to ten sentences. Include: one indirect question with chiedere se, at least one reported statement with dire che or spiegare che, at least two conditional suggestions, and correct clitic placement in at least one sentence.

  1. Explain the problem. State what is wrong and, if relevant, its likely cause.
  2. Report the response. Use dire che or spiegare che to relay what the other person said.
  3. Propose next steps. Suggest at least two things the reader should do, using the conditional.
  4. Close appropriately. Match your closing to your relationship with the reader.

Lista di controllo — Editing checklist:

  • The indirect question uses se or a question word, not the direct-question word order.
  • Reported statements shift verb tense appropriately when needed.
  • Conditional suggestions are formed correctly.
  • Clitic pronouns are placed correctly, whether before the conjugated verb or attached to the infinitive.
  • The register (tu or Lei) is consistent throughout.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — mediating rather than translating

Mediating a message well means more than translating it word for word: a good mediator adjusts detail, tone, and length to what the listener actually needs. Chiara does not repeat everything Mr. Bruni said — she selects the cause, the timeline, and the practical next step, because that is what Marco needs to act. This skill is useful in many practical situations, from family logistics to workplace communication, and expectations about how much detail to include can vary by relationship, setting, and personal style.

Culture in Context — written follow-up after a phone call

In many Italian-speaking contexts, following up a phone call with a short written message or email is a common and practical way to keep a clear record of dates, agreements, or requests, particularly with service providers, landlords, and institutions. This habit is useful rather than universal, and its formality can vary depending on the relationship and the setting.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — keeping question word order in an indirect question

Error: Writing Gli ho chiesto se poteva venire lui domani? with a question mark and inverted order.

Correction: Gli ho chiesto se poteva venire domani. An indirect question is a statement, not a question: it has ordinary word order and no question mark.

Common Mistake — forgetting to shift tense in reported speech

Error: Writing Ha detto che il problema è comune when reporting something said in the past about a general, ongoing situation, without adjusting for the reporting context.

Correction: Ha detto che il problema era comune shifts the verb to imperfetto to match the past-tense reporting frame. Not every case requires a shift, but high-frequency reported statements about past-time context usually do.

Common Mistake — suggesting with the imperative instead of the conditional

Error: Writing Segnalalo per iscritto! as a suggestion to a friend about a problem that is not urgent.

Correction: Dovresti segnalarlo per iscritto softens the suggestion appropriately. Reserve the imperative for direct instructions or genuinely urgent situations, and use the conditional for ordinary advice.

Com'era prima

Volume II, Chapter 3: How Things Used to Be · Speak and Write Italian: Everyday Independence and Connected Communication

Chapter Overview and Objectives

Marco's world in Rome now has neighbors who worry about him, help him, and stay in touch with him — but Signora Rita has started telling him about a different Rome, the one she grew up in. To follow her stories, Marco needs a new way of talking about the past: not the single completed action of passato prossimo, but the open, ongoing background of how things used to be. This chapter introduces the imperfetto, the tense Italian uses for description, habit, and the scenery behind a story, rather than for one finished event. By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Form the imperfetto of regular -are, -ere, and -ire verbs.
  2. Form and use the irregular imperfetto of essere, avere, and fare.
  3. Use the imperfetto to describe people, places, weather, age, and time in the past.
  4. Use the imperfetto to describe habitual or repeated past actions (what someone used to do).
  5. Recognize, without yet fully controlling, the difference between the imperfetto as background and the passato prossimo as a completed event.
  6. Understand and retell a short account of how someone's life used to be.

Communication Scenario

On one of their regular phone calls, Signora Rita begins telling Marco about her own youth in Rome — a city with fewer cars, closer neighbors, and a slower daily rhythm. Marco listens, asks questions, and starts to notice how she describes what things were generally like, rather than telling him about one single thing that happened on one day.

  1. You will read and listen to Signora Rita describing her childhood neighborhood and daily life.
  2. You will then describe how something in your own life used to be — a place, a routine, or a person — using the imperfetto.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — the imperfetto stress pattern

Most imperfetto forms stress the vowel just before the ending: gioca-VO, gioca-VI, gioca-VA, gioca-VA-mo, gioca-VA-te, gioca-VA-no. Notice that the noi and voi forms keep the stress in the same place as the singular forms, unlike some other tenses you have studied.

3.1 The distinctive -va- syllable

Nearly every imperfetto form contains a clear -va- syllable (giocavo, correvo, dormivo). Listening for this syllable is a fast, reliable way to recognize the imperfetto in connected speech, even before you can identify which person is speaking.

3.2 Ero vs. era

Ero ("I was") and era ("he/she/it was" or formal "you were") differ only in their final vowel, but that vowel carries the whole distinction of person. Practice these two forms slowly and deliberately: Ero giovane versus Mio nonno era giovane.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

Language Focus — forming the imperfetto

The imperfetto is formed by dropping the -re of the infinitive and adding endings built around -v-. The three conjugations differ only in their vowel.

Subjectgiocarecorreredormire
iogiocavocorrevodormivo
tugiocavicorrevidormivi
lui / lei / Leigiocavacorrevadormiva
noigiocavamocorrevamodormivamo

Irregular forms — high frequency only: essere (ero, eri, era, eravamo, eravate, erano), fare (facevo, facevi, faceva, facevamo, facevate, facevano), and avere, which is regular in the imperfetto (avevo, avevi, aveva, avevamo, avevate, avevano) even though it is irregular in the present tense.

Language Focus — what the imperfetto is used for

The imperfetto does not tell you that something happened once and finished. It describes the background, the ongoing situation, or the habit. In this chapter, you will use it for four everyday purposes:

UseExample
Description of a place or sceneIl quartiere era tranquillo e c'erano pochissime macchine.
Description of a person or conditionMia nonna era una donna forte e sempre allegra.
Age and time in the pastAvevo dieci anni quando abitavo qui.
Habitual or repeated past actionDa bambina giocavo per strada ogni pomeriggio.

A first look at the contrast: compare Da bambina giocavo per strada ogni giorno ("I used to play in the street every day" — habitual, open-ended) with Ieri ho giocato a carte con Rita ("Yesterday I played cards with Rita" — one specific, completed occasion). You are not yet expected to choose confidently between the two tenses in every sentence; that full contrast is developed later in this unit. For now, focus on recognizing that the imperfetto paints a picture or a habit, while passato prossimo reports a finished event.

Everything else in this chapter — direct and indirect object pronouns, reciprocal reflexive verbs, and passato prossimo for specific completed events — is recycled exactly as taught in Volume I and Chapters 1–2. No other new system is introduced here.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Marco phones Signora Rita on a quiet Sunday evening, and she starts telling him about her childhood.

Marco Signora Rita, com'era il quartiere quando Lei era piccola? Seen-YO-ra REE-ta, ko-MEH-ra eel kwar-TYEH-reh KWAN-do LEH-ee EH-ra pee-KKO-la? Mrs. Rita, what was the neighborhood like when you were little?
Rita Ah, era tutto diverso! C'erano pochissime macchine e noi bambini giocavamo per strada ogni pomeriggio. Ah, EH-ra TOOT-to dee-VEHR-so! Che-EH-ra-no po-kees-SEE-meh MAK-kee-neh eh NO-ee bam-BEE-nee jo-ka-VA-mo per STRA-da OH-nee po-meh-REE-jo. Ah, everything was different! There were very few cars, and we kids used to play in the street every afternoon.
Marco Davvero? E i vicini, come si aiutavano a quei tempi? Dav-VEH-ro? Eh ee vee-CHEE-nee, KO-meh see a-yoo-TA-va-no a KWEH-ee TEHM-pee? Really? And the neighbors, how did they help each other in those days?
Rita Tutti si conoscevano bene. Mia madre lasciava sempre la porta aperta, e le vicine entravano e uscivano come se fosse casa loro. TOOT-tee see ko-no-SHEH-va-no BEH-neh. MEE-a MA-dreh la-SHA-va SEHM-preh la POR-ta a-PEHR-ta, eh leh vee-CHEE-neh en-TRA-va-no eh oo-SHEE-va-no KOH-meh seh FOS-seh KA-sa LO-ro. Everyone knew each other well. My mother always left the door open, and the neighbor women would come and go as if it were their own house.
Marco Sembra proprio un altro mondo. Comunque, ieri Le ho detto che sono uscito con Paolo, no? Beh, abbiamo parlato proprio di questo. SEHM-bra PRO-pryo oon AL-tro MON-do. Ko-moon-KWEH, ee-YEH-ree LEH o DET-to keh SO-no oo-SHEE-to kon PA-o-lo, no? Beh, ab-BYA-mo par-LA-to PRO-pryo dee KWES-to. It really sounds like another world. By the way, yesterday I told you I went out with Paolo, right? Well, we talked about exactly this.
Rita Che bello. Il mondo cambia, ma certe cose, per fortuna, restano uguali: i buoni vicini si aiutano ancora oggi, come una volta. Keh BEL-lo. Eel MON-do KAM-bya, ma CHEHR-teh KO-zeh, per for-TOO-na, RES-ta-no oo-GWA-lee: ee BWO-nee vee-CHEE-nee see a-YOO-ta-no an-KO-ra OH-jee, KO-meh OO-na VOL-ta. How lovely. The world changes, but some things, luckily, stay the same: good neighbors still help each other today, just like they used to.

Listening Practice

Ask an instructor, study partner, or fluent Italian speaker to read the passage below aloud, at a natural pace, twice.

Com'era prima — How it used to be:
"Quando Rita era bambina, il quartiere era molto diverso da oggi. C'erano pochissime macchine, e i bambini giocavano per strada tutti i pomeriggi. Le famiglie non avevano molti soldi, ma avevano tempo: le madri parlavano da una finestra all'altra, e i vicini si conoscevano tutti per nome. Ogni tanto, quando qualcuno aveva bisogno di aiuto, gli altri arrivavano subito, senza bisogno di chiedere due volte. Rita dice che il mondo di oggi è più veloce e più comodo, ma che a quei tempi le persone si sentivano meno sole. Ieri, parlando con Marco al telefono, Rita gli ha raccontato tutto questo con gli occhi lucidi."

Writing Workshop

Write a connected paragraph describing how something in your life used to be — a place, a person, or a routine — the way Rita and Marco's message do.

Compito di scrittura — Writing task: Write six to eight sentences. Include: at least one description of a place or person using the imperfetto of essere, at least one sentence with age or time using avere in the imperfetto, at least two imperfetto verbs describing habitual action, and at least one passato prossimo sentence describing one specific event.

  1. Set the scene. Describe what a place or person was like, using essere in the imperfetto.
  2. Give age or time. Use avere in the imperfetto.
  3. Describe a habit. Use at least two other imperfetto verbs for repeated past actions.
  4. Add one specific event. Include one passato prossimo sentence with correct auxiliary choice.
  5. Close naturally. End with a simple closing appropriate to your chosen audience.

Lista di controllo — Editing checklist:

  • Imperfetto endings are correctly formed for the subject and conjugation.
  • Essere, avere, and fare are correctly conjugated in the imperfetto where used.
  • The imperfetto is used for description and habit, not for a single completed event.
  • The passato prossimo sentence describes one specific, finished occasion.
  • Accents, apostrophes, and capitalization follow standard Italian spelling.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — remembering an older Rome

Older residents in many Italian neighborhoods often describe a past with fewer cars, more time spent in the street or shared courtyards, and closer daily contact among neighbors. How accurately this reflects any single decade or neighborhood varies, and such memories are shaped by personal experience as much as by broader social change; they should be treated as personal narrative rather than historical fact.

Culture in Context — open doors and shared space

The image of a household with an open door and neighbors freely coming and going reflects a real pattern in some communities, particularly smaller towns or older urban neighborhoods, but it is far from universal across Italy today, and privacy norms differ considerably by region, generation, and household.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — using passato prossimo for description or habit

Error: Writing Il quartiere è stato tranquillo to describe what a neighborhood was generally like.

Correction: Use the imperfetto for ongoing description: Il quartiere era tranquillo. Reserve passato prossimo for a specific completed event, such as Ieri il quartiere è stato molto rumoroso (referring to one particular day).

Common Mistake — confusing ero and era

Error: Writing Quando era bambino, giocavo per strada to mean "When I was a child, I used to play in the street."

Correction: The first person is ero: Quando ero bambino, giocavo per strada. Era belongs to lui/lei/Lei.

Common Mistake — treating avere as irregular in the imperfetto

Error: Writing an invented irregular form for avere in the imperfetto by analogy with its irregular present tense.

Correction: Avere is fully regular in the imperfetto: avevo, avevi, aveva, avevamo, avevate, avevano.

You learned the regular endings and the irregular forms of essere and fare, and you began to notice, without yet mastering, how the imperfetto differs from the passato prossimo you already use for single completed events. You practiced these systems through Signora Rita's memories of her childhood neighborhood and applied them to describe how something in your own life used to be. Looking Ahead

Cosa sai fare ora — What you can do now

You can use the imperfetto to describe people, places, and situations in the past, to give age and time, and to talk about habitual actions. You are beginning to notice that Italian uses different past tenses for background and for completed events, even though you are not yet expected to choose between them with full confidence in every sentence.

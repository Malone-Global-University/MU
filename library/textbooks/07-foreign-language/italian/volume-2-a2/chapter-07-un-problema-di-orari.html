Un problema di orari

Volume II, Chapter 7: A Scheduling Problem · Speak and Write Italian: Everyday Independence and Connected Communication

Chapter Overview and Objectives

The tasks are divided, but two days later Rita gets a message that changes everything: the DJ she wanted can only play in the morning, not the evening the group had planned. Marco, Paolo, and Rita meet again at the bar, and this time the conversation is less about what each person is doing and more about who they need to tell, what they need to explain to whom, and what someone might say back to them. As they work out the new plan, they keep needing a different kind of pronoun — one that shows not what is acted on, but who receives an action: who they call, who they explain the change to, who might answer them. This chapter introduces that tool: indirect object pronouns. By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Recognize and use the indirect object pronouns mi, ti, gli, le, ci, vi, and gli (plural) to say to whom or for whom something is done.
  2. Distinguish indirect object pronouns from the direct object pronouns introduced in Chapter 6.
  3. Place an indirect object pronoun correctly before a conjugated verb.
  4. Use indirect object pronouns with high-frequency verbs of communication such as dire, scrivere, telefonare, chiedere, rispondere, and spiegare.
  5. Explain a scheduling problem and renegotiate a plan using indirect object pronouns.
  6. Identify and correct common English-speaker errors involving indirect object pronouns, including confusion with direct object pronouns.
  7. Understand and retell a conversation in which a group solves a scheduling conflict.

Communication Scenario

Two days after dividing the festival tasks, Rita receives a message from the DJ: he is only free in the morning, not the evening the group had planned. Marco, Paolo, and Rita meet again at the bar to figure out who needs to be told, what needs to be explained, and what the new plan will be.

  1. You will read and listen to Marco, Paolo, and Rita working out the scheduling problem.
  2. You will then explain a scheduling change of your own — for an event, a class, or a group project — saying who you need to tell and what you will say to them, using indirect object pronouns.

Pronunciation Focus

Pronunciation Focus — gli as a pronoun versus gli in spelling

Learners already know gli as the plural article and as the digraph in words like famiglia. As the indirect object pronoun meaning "to him," gli is pronounced the same soft way — never like the English "g" in "glue." Practice keeping gli telefono light and unstressed on the pronoun, with the stress falling on te-LE-fo-no.

7.1 Linking ci and vi smoothly before a verb

Ci ("to us") and vi ("to you all") attach tightly to the following verb with no pause, just like the direct object pronouns from Chapter 6: ci dice, vi scrivo. Do not insert an English-style pause between the pronoun and the verb.

7.2 Distinguishing le (direct, "them," feminine plural) from le (indirect, "to her")

The same written form le can be a direct object pronoun (Chapter 6: le sedie... le porta Marco) or an indirect object pronoun ("to her": le scrivo un messaggio). Context and the surrounding verb tell you which one you are hearing; listen for whether the verb takes a direct object ("bring what?") or an indirect one ("write to whom?").

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

Language Focus — indirect object pronouns

An indirect object answers the question "to whom?" or "for whom?" Many Italian verbs of communication — dire, scrivere, telefonare, chiedere, rispondere, spiegare — normally take an indirect object: you say something to someone, not simply "do" that person.

PronounMeaningExample
mito/for meMi scrive un messaggio.
tito/for you (informal)Ti telefono stasera.
glito/for himGli spiego il problema.
leto/for herLe chiedo se è libera.
cito/for usCi dice la verità.
vito/for you allVi rispondo domani.
glito/for themGli telefono più tardi.

Placement: like direct object pronouns, indirect object pronouns go directly before a conjugated verb: Gli telefono (I'll call him), not Telefono gli. In contemporary standard Italian, gli has largely replaced the more formal loro ("to them") in everyday speech; loro still appears in careful or formal writing, placed after the verb, and is introduced here only as a recognition form.

Language Focus — direct versus indirect: telling the two apart

The clearest test is the question the pronoun answers. If the verb answers "what?" or "whom?" directly, use a direct object pronoun (Chapter 6). If it answers "to whom?" or "for whom?", use an indirect object pronoun (this chapter):

ItalianQuestion answeredPronoun type
Chiamo Rita. → La chiamo.Whom do I call?Direct
Telefono a Rita. → Le telefono.To whom do I phone?Indirect
Vedo Marco. → Lo vedo.Whom do I see?Direct
Scrivo a Marco. → Gli scrivo.To whom do I write?Indirect

Notice that telefonare, scrivere, dire, chiedere, rispondere, and spiegare all take a before a named person in Italian, which is the signal that the pronoun replacing that person will be indirect, not direct.

Language Focus — no participle agreement with indirect object pronouns

Unlike direct object pronouns (Chapter 6), indirect object pronouns do not cause the past participle to agree in the passato prossimo:

ItalianEnglish
Le ho telefonato ieri.I called her yesterday.
Gli ho scritto un messaggio.I wrote him a message.
Ci ha detto la verità.He told us the truth.

Compare Le ho telefonato (participle unchanged) with Chapter 6's Le ho prese (participle agrees with a direct object pronoun). This contrast is a common source of English-speaker error; see Common Mistakes below.

Everything else in this chapter — direct object pronouns and participle agreement, sequencing connectors, prima di + infinitive, and the internal formation of the imperfetto and passato prossimo — is recycled exactly as taught in Volume I and Chapters 1–6. No other new grammar system is introduced here.

Model Dialogue

Setting: Two days later, Marco, Paolo, and Rita meet again at the corner bar after Rita receives a message from the DJ.

Rita Ragazzi, abbiamo un problema. Il DJ mi ha scritto stamattina: è disponibile solo la mattina, non la sera. Ra-GAZ-zee, ab-BYA-mo oon pro-BLEH-ma. Eel dee-JEI mee a SKREET-to sta-mat-TEE-na: eh dee-spo-NEE-bee-leh SO-lo la mat-TEE-na, non la SEH-ra. Guys, we have a problem. The DJ wrote to me this morning: he's only available in the morning, not the evening.
Paolo Gli hai già risposto? Lyee AI ja rees-POS-to? Have you already answered him?
Rita Non ancora. Prima volevo parlarne con voi. Se vogliamo tenerlo, dobbiamo spostare tutta la festa alla mattina. Non an-KO-ra. PREE-ma vo-LEH-vo par-LAR-neh kon voi. Seh vo-LYA-mo teh-NER-lo, do-BYA-mo spos-TA-reh TOOT-ta la FES-ta AL-la mat-TEE-na. Not yet. First I wanted to talk about it with you. If we want to keep him, we have to move the whole festival to the morning.
Marco Alla mattina? Ma i negozianti aprono tardi la domenica. Devo chiedergli se possono aprire prima. AL-la mat-TEE-na? Ma ee neh-go-TSYAN-tee A-pro-no TAR-dee la do-MEH-nee-ka. DEH-vo KYE-der-lyee seh POS-so-no A-pree-reh PREE-ma. In the morning? But the shop owners open late on Sundays. I have to ask them if they can open earlier.
Paolo E le sedie? Mio cugino me le presta solo dopo le dieci, ha detto. Eh leh SEH-dyeh? MEE-o KOO-jee-no meh leh PRES-ta SO-lo DO-po leh DYEH-chee, a DET-to. And the chairs? My cousin only lends them to me after ten, he said.
Rita Allora forse possiamo cominciare alle undici, non troppo presto. Marco, tu gli telefoni oggi e gli chiedi degli orari? Al-LO-ra FOR-seh pos-SYA-mo ko-meen-CHA-reh AL-leh oon-DEE-chee, non TROP-po PRES-to. MAR-ko, too lyee teh-LEH-fo-nee OJ-jee eh lyee KYE-dee DEH-lyee o-RA-ree? So maybe we can start at eleven, not too early. Marco, will you phone them today and ask them about hours?
Marco Sì, gli telefono nel pomeriggio. E tu, Rita, rispondi al DJ e gli confermi le undici? See, lyee teh-LEH-fo-no nel po-meh-REEJ-jo. Eh too, REE-ta, rees-PON-dee al dee-JEI eh lyee kon-FER-mee leh oon-DEE-chee? Yes, I'll phone them in the afternoon. And you, Rita, will you answer the DJ and confirm eleven o'clock with him?
Rita Gli rispondo subito, così non cambia idea. E dobbiamo anche avvisare tutti gli altri — vi mando un messaggio di gruppo stasera. Lyee rees-PON-do soo-BEE-to, ko-ZEE non KAM-bya ee-DEH-a. Eh do-BYA-mo AN-keh av-vee-ZA-reh TOOT-tee lyee AL-tree — vee MAN-do oon mes-SAJ-jo dee GROOP-po sta-SEH-ra. I'll answer him right away, so he doesn't change his mind. And we also need to let everyone else know — I'll send you all a group message tonight.

Speaking Practice

Pratica controllata — Controlled practice: who do you need to tell?

With a partner, take turns asking and answering Chi devi avvisare? ("Who do you need to let know?") about a change of plan, answering with an indirect object pronoun each time instead of repeating the person's name.

Compito comunicativo — Communicative task: renegotiating a plan

In pairs or small groups, imagine a scheduling problem for an event or project of your own — a meeting that has to move, a deadline that changes, a reservation that falls through. Explain the problem and say who you need to tell, call, write to, or ask, using at least four indirect object pronouns. Your partner should ask at least one follow-up question about what you will say to that person.

Pratica da solo — Solo alternative

Record yourself explaining a scheduling problem and who you need to contact about it, checking that each indirect object pronoun is placed correctly and that you have not confused it with a direct object pronoun from Chapter 6.

Il messaggio di gruppo di Rita — Rita's group message
Ciao a tutti! Vi scrivo per un aggiornamento importante: il DJ mi ha detto che è disponibile solo la mattina, quindi la festa comincia alle undici, non la sera come avevamo deciso prima. Writing Workshop

Write a connected paragraph explaining a scheduling problem and who you need to tell about it, the way Rita's message does.

Compito di scrittura — Writing task: Write eight to ten sentences. Include: at least four indirect object pronouns referring to people you need to tell, ask, call, or write to; at least one direct object pronoun from Chapter 6 for contrast; at least one sequencing connector from Chapter 5; and a natural closing.

  1. Explain the problem. Say what changed and why it affects the plan.
  2. Say who you need to tell first. Name that person, then refer back to them with an indirect object pronoun.
  3. Say who else you need to contact. Use poi or nel frattempo to move between people, using indirect object pronouns rather than repeating names.
  4. Mention something already resolved. Use the passato prossimo with an indirect object pronoun, remembering that the participle does not agree with it.
  5. Add a closing. End with a simple closing appropriate to your chosen audience.

Lista di controllo — Editing checklist:

  • Each indirect object pronoun is placed directly before the conjugated verb.
  • The past participle does not change to agree with an indirect object pronoun in the passato prossimo.
  • Direct and indirect object pronouns are not confused with each other.
  • Sequencing connectors and prima di + infinitive are used correctly where relevant.
  • Accents, apostrophes, and capitalization follow standard Italian spelling.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — group messages and last-minute changes

Group messaging apps are a common way for friends and neighbors organizing an informal event to share last-minute changes quickly, often with a brief apology such as scusate il cambiamento before the new information. The tone tends to stay light and reassuring, even when the change is inconvenient, though the level of formality can vary by group and generation.

Culture in Context — negotiating with local shop owners

Asking neighborhood shop owners to adjust their hours for a community event is a normal part of organizing a street festival in many Italian towns and neighborhoods, and such requests are often made in person or by phone rather than through formal paperwork. Willingness to accommodate such requests varies by shop, season, and relationship with the organizers, and should not be assumed as automatic.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — placing the indirect object pronoun after the verb

Error: Writing Telefono gli stasera, following English word order.

Correction: The indirect object pronoun goes before the conjugated verb: Gli telefono stasera.

Common Mistake — making the participle agree with an indirect object pronoun

Error: Writing Le ho telefonata ieri, treating le as if it were a direct object pronoun that requires agreement.

Correction: Indirect object pronouns never trigger participle agreement: Le ho telefonato ieri.

Common Mistake — confusing direct and indirect object pronouns with the same verb pattern

Error: Writing La telefono for "I call her," treating telefonare as if it took a direct object.

Correction: Telefonare takes a + person and therefore an indirect object pronoun: Le telefono. Check whether the Italian verb takes a before a person; English "call someone" does not signal this, but Italian telefonare a qualcuno does.

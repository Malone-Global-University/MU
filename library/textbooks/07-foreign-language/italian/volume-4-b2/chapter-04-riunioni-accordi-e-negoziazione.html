Riunioni, accordi e negoziazione

Volume IV, Chapter 4: Meetings, Agreements, and Negotiation · Speak and Write Italian: Professional, Academic, and Cultural Communication

Chapter Overview and Objectives

Professoressa Bianchi opens the fourth class with a scenario rather than a chart this time: "You already know how to describe what the data show. Now you need to do something with that information in a room full of people who may not agree with you." Chiara's student association must decide how to use the extra volunteers the new schedule has attracted. Marco's cooperative needs to agree on how to respond to the drop in training hours he described last chapter. Elena has been invited to sit in on a planning meeting for her prospective doctoral program, where she will need to ask questions without appearing to overstep. None of them is a professional negotiator, but all three need to open and close a meeting appropriately, propose and respond to ideas, clarify who is responsible for what, and put decisions into writing that other people can act on. Chapter 4 moves from the evidence-based commentary of Chapter 3 into structured spoken and written interaction. By the end of Chapter 4, you will be able to:

  1. Open and close a structured meeting appropriately.
  2. Propose an idea, agree, disagree, and negotiate a compromise.
  3. Clarify responsibilities and deadlines using indirect questions.
  4. Summarize decisions reached during a meeting.
  5. Document action items clearly and consistently.
  6. Use the subjunctive and conditional to phrase proposals and preferences appropriately.
  7. Use clitic pronouns to refer back to proposals, tasks, and decisions without repeating them.

  2. You will then plan and role-play a short meeting of your own, and write a set of minutes using the models and checklist provided.

Pronunciation Focus

Language Focus — turn-taking intonation

A meeting depends on brief, recognizable signals that show whether a speaker is finishing a point, inviting a response, or gently interrupting: Posso aggiungere una cosa?, Se posso interrompere un attimo…, Direi che possiamo concludere così. Practice these openings with a slight rising pitch when requesting the floor, and a falling, settled pitch when closing a point, so that your intention is clear before you say the content itself.

4.1 Polite interruption, clarification rhythm, and summarizing decisions aloud

When summarizing a decision at the end of a discussion — quindi, se ho capito bene, decidiamo di rimandare la scelta alla prossima riunione — slow down slightly and place light stress on the verb of decision (decidiamo) so listeners can confirm or correct your summary before the meeting moves on.

Grammar and Sentence Patterns

Developed — subjunctive and conditional for proposals and preferences

Propongo che rimandiamo la decisione alla prossima settimana. (I propose that we postpone the decision until next week.) Sarebbe meglio se ognuno presentasse una proposta scritta. (It would be better if everyone presented a written proposal.) Preferirei che si discutesse prima del budget. (I would prefer that the budget be discussed first.) The subjunctive after verbs of proposing and preferring, and the conditional for softened suggestions, work together in meeting language: the conditional opens the door politely, and the subjunctive states what the speaker actually wants to happen.

Consolidated — indirect questions

Vorrei sapere chi si occuperà della logistica. (I'd like to know who will handle the logistics.) Non è ancora chiaro quando sarà pronto il rapporto. (It isn't yet clear when the report will be ready.) Indirect questions, familiar since Volume III, are now expected as the default way to raise a question inside a meeting without interrupting the flow of discussion with a blunt direct question.

Expanded — clitic reference to proposals and tasks

La proposta di Marco? La discutiamo dopo la pausa. (Marco's proposal? We'll discuss it after the break.) Il compito di preparare il verbale, lo affido a Elena. (The task of preparing the minutes, I'm assigning it to Elena.) Ne parliamo alla prossima riunione. (We'll talk about it at the next meeting.) In meeting language, clitics let speakers track a proposal, task, or topic across several turns without repeating the full noun phrase every time, which is essential for sounding fluent rather than mechanically thorough.

Introduced receptively — future-in-the-past in reported planning

Marco ha detto che avrebbe preparato il rapporto entro venerdì. (Marco said he would prepare the report by Friday.) When a meeting's decisions are later reported to someone who was not present, Italian often shifts the future into a conditional-perfect-like form to show that the plan was made at an earlier point in time. At this stage, learners should recognize this pattern in reported planning without being required to produce it freely; full productive control is developed later in the volume.

Also active in this chapter, as review: formal address with Lei, conditional politeness (Chapter 2), and time expressions for deadlines, all recycled here in the context of meetings and negotiation.

Model Dialogue: Una riunione di associazione

Setting: Chiara chairs a short planning meeting with Marco and Elena, who are helping her association decide how to use new volunteers.

Chiara Bene, direi di cominciare. All'ordine del giorno abbiamo un solo punto: come utilizzare i nuovi volontari. Propongo che li dividiamo tra accoglienza e organizzazione eventi. BEH-neh, dee-REI dee ko-meen-CHA-reh. al-lor-DEE-neh del JOR-no ab-BYA-mo oon SO-lo POON-to: KO-meh oo-tee-leed-DZA-reh ee NWO-vee vo-lon-TA-ree. pro-PON-go keh lee dee-vee-DYA-mo tra ak-ko-JEN-tsa eh or-ga-need-dza-TSYO-neh eh-VEN-tee. Good, I'd say let's begin. On the agenda we have a single item: how to use the new volunteers. I propose that we divide them between reception and event organization.
Marco Sono d'accordo in linea di massima, ma vorrei sapere quanti volontari abbiamo esattamente prima di dividerli. SO-no dak-KOR-do een LEE-neh-a dee MAS-see-ma, ma vor-REI sa-PEH-reh KWAN-tee vo-lon-TA-ree ab-BYA-mo eh-zat-ta-MEN-teh PREE-ma dee dee-VEE-der-lee. I agree in general, but I'd like to know exactly how many volunteers we have before dividing them up.
Chiara Buona domanda. Sono dodici in tutto. Elena, tu che ne pensi? BWO-na do-MAN-da. SO-no DO-dee-chee een TOOT-to. eh-LEH-na, too keh neh PEN-see? Good question. There are twelve in total. Elena, what do you think about it?
Elena Sarebbe meglio se ne assegnassimo di più all'accoglienza, visto che è lì che abbiamo più bisogno. Per quanto mi riguarda, l'organizzazione eventi può aspettare un mese. sa-REB-beh MEH-lyo seh neh as-seh-nyas-SEE-mo dee PYOO al-lak-ko-JEN-tsa, VEES-to keh eh LEE keh ab-BYA-mo PYOO bee-SO-nyo. per KWAN-to mee ree-GWAR-da, lor-ga-need-dza-TSYO-neh eh-VEN-tee pwo as-peht-TA-reh oon MEH-zeh. It would be better if we assigned more of them to reception, given that's where we need it most. As far as I'm concerned, event organization can wait a month.
Marco Non sono del tutto d'accordo: rimandare gli eventi rischia di far perdere entusiasmo ai nuovi volontari. Potremmo trovare un compromesso? non SO-no del TOOT-to dak-KOR-do: ree-man-DA-reh lyee eh-VEN-tee REES-kya dee far PER-deh-reh en-too-ZYAZ-mo ai NWO-vee vo-lon-TA-ree. po-TREM-mo tro-VA-reh oon kom-pro-MES-so? I don't entirely agree: postponing the events risks making the new volunteers lose enthusiasm. Could we find a compromise?
Chiara Va bene: otto all'accoglienza e quattro all'organizzazione eventi, a patto che rivediamo la divisione tra un mese. Il compito di aggiornare la lista lo affido a Marco. va BEH-neh: OT-to al-lak-ko-JEN-tsa eh KWAT-tro al-lor-ga-need-dza-TSYO-neh eh-VEN-tee, a PAT-to keh ree-veh-DYA-mo la dee-vee-ZYO-neh tra oon MEH-zeh. eel kom-PEE-to dee ad-jor-NA-reh la LEES-ta lo af-FEE-do a MAR-ko. All right: eight in reception and four in event organization, provided that we review the division in a month. The task of updating the list, I'm assigning it to Marco.
Elena Perfetto. Allora, se ho capito bene, decidiamo otto e quattro, con revisione tra un mese. Lo metto a verbale. per-FET-to. al-LO-ra, seh o ka-PEE-to BEH-neh, deh-chee-DYA-mo OT-to eh KWAT-tro, kon reh-vee-ZYO-neh tra oon MEH-zeh. lo MET-to a ver-BA-leh. Perfect. So, if I've understood correctly, we're deciding eight and four, with a review in a month. I'll put it on record.

Domande di comprensione — Comprehension questions:

  1. What does Chiara initially propose, and what clarifying question does Marco ask?
  2. How do Marco and Elena disagree, and how is the disagreement resolved?
  3. What task does Chiara assign to Marco, and what does Elena do at the end of the meeting?

Speaking Practice

Pratica controllata — Controlled practice: opening and closing a meeting

Practice aloud a short opening (stating the agenda) and a short closing (summarizing a decision), using at least one indirect question and one clitic pronoun referring back to a task or proposal.

Compito comunicativo — Communicative task: proposing and negotiating

With a partner, or recorded solo playing both roles, propose a plan for a real or imagined situation (dividing tasks, choosing a schedule, allocating a budget). Practice agreeing, disagreeing respectfully, and reaching a compromise using the subjunctive and conditional.

Colloquio simulato — Simulated exchange: clarifying a responsibility

With a partner, or recorded solo, ask a clarifying indirect question about who is responsible for a task and by when, and respond by assigning the task clearly using a clitic pronoun.

Verbale — Minutes excerpt: Riunione del comitato direttivo
Presenti: otto membri su dodici; il quorum è raggiunto. Ordine del giorno: (1) assegnazione dei nuovi volontari; (2) revisione del budget trimestrale. Sul primo punto, dopo discussione, si decide di assegnare otto volontari all'accoglienza e quattro all'organizzazione eventi, con una revisione della divisione tra un mese; la delibera è approvata all'unanimità. Il verbalizzante mette a verbale entrambe le decisioni. La riunione si chiude alle ore 19:30.

On the first item, after discussion, it is decided to assign eight volunteers to reception and four to event organization, with a review of the division in a month; the resolution is approved unanimously. On the second item, the group's spokesperson presents a counterproposal to the initial budget; after a brief exchange, it is decided to postpone the final decision to the next meeting, pending further data. Writing Workshop

Plan, draft, and revise a complete set of meeting minutes based on a role-play you complete with a partner, or on an imagined meeting of your own.

Compito di scrittura — Writing task: Write meeting minutes of 100–140 words that list who was present, state the agenda items, summarize at least one proposal and the resulting decision or compromise, and clearly assign at least one task to a named person using a clitic pronoun.

  1. List your material. Note who was present, the agenda items, and the decisions reached.
  2. Draft freely. Write the minutes without stopping to correct yourself.
  3. Track reference. Find at least one place where you can replace a repeated noun phrase with a clitic pronoun.
  4. Soften and clarify. Check that proposals use appropriate subjunctive or conditional forms rather than blunt commands.
  5. Assign clearly. Make sure at least one task has a named person and a deadline.
  6. Finalize. Check verb forms, agreement, accents, apostrophes, and punctuation throughout.

Lista di controllo — Editing checklist:

  • The minutes clearly state who was present and the agenda items.
  • At least one proposal is phrased using the subjunctive or conditional.
  • At least one clitic pronoun replaces a repeated noun phrase.
  • At least one task is assigned to a named person with a deadline.
  • Decisions and proposals are clearly distinguished from each other.
  • Verb forms, agreement, accents, apostrophes, and punctuation are checked throughout.

Culture and Communication

Culture in Context — directness, courtesy, and efficiency

Meeting style varies considerably across Italian institutions, associations, and workplaces. Some groups favor lengthy discussion before a decision is reached; others move quickly to a vote. Learners should not assume that one fixed style — highly formal or highly informal, exhaustive or brisk — represents "the" Italian way of running a meeting. What tends to remain constant is a preference for courteous disagreement: challenging an idea directly is common, but doing so respectfully, without personal criticism, is expected.

Culture in Context — documenting decisions

Written minutes serve a practical and sometimes legal function in Italian institutional life, especially in associations, cooperatives, and public bodies, where a documented decision protects both the group and individual members. Learners preparing minutes should aim for clarity and neutrality: stating what was decided and by whom, rather than editorializing about the discussion itself.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake — proposing with the indicative instead of the subjunctive

Error: Propongo che Marco prepara il rapporto entro venerdì. (indicative used where the subjunctive is required)

Correction: Propongo che Marco prepari il rapporto entro venerdì. Verbs of proposing take the subjunctive in the dependent clause; the error is easy to miss with noi forms, where present subjunctive and indicative often look identical, but becomes visible with third-person forms like prepari.

Common Mistake — asking a direct question inside formal meeting language

Error: Chi si occupa della logistica? (abrupt inside a structured proposal or summary)

Correction: Vorrei sapere chi si occuperà della logistica. An indirect question softens the request for information and fits the register of structured meeting discourse.

Common Mistake — repeating the full noun phrase instead of using a clitic

Error: Discutiamo la proposta di Marco dopo la pausa. La proposta di Marco è interessante. (repetitive, unnatural in fluent speech)

Correction: Discutiamo la proposta di Marco dopo la pausa. È interessante. Once a proposal or task has been named, refer back to it with a clitic pronoun or simple pronoun rather than repeating the full phrase.

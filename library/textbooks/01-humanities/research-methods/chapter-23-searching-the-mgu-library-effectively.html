Chapter 23 — Front Matter

Research Skill Focus and Overview

This chapter teaches students how to use the MGU Library as an integrated research system rather than a collection of disconnected tools. Students will learn what each part of the MGU Library does, when each tool is most useful, how different library sections work together to support a research project from first question to final citation, and how to build a research path that moves intelligently through the library's resources in the right order.

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to enter any research project with a clear plan for which MGU Library resources to consult first, what each resource offers, and how to move from one resource to the next as their research deepens.

Why This Matters

Most students who visit a library — physical or digital — navigate it by instinct. They search for a term, click the first result that looks useful, and start reading. This approach occasionally works. But it leaves most of the library's resources untouched, it skips the stages of research that produce the best sources, and it makes the student's work harder than it needs to be.

The MGU Library is not a single search box. It is a system — a set of interconnected resources, each designed for a different purpose and a different stage of the research process. The dictionary is built for one thing. The encyclopedia is built for another. The reference pages serve a different function than the guides. The tutorials teach a different kind of skill than the departments. These tools are not interchangeable. Using the wrong tool at the wrong stage of research wastes time and produces weaker results.

This chapter gives students a map of that system. It explains what each part of the MGU Library is designed to do, when to use each part, what questions each resource answers well, and what questions it is not built to answer. It also shows how the resources connect — how a search in the encyclopedia leads naturally to a catalog search, how a guide builds toward a database search, how a tutorial prepares you to use a department resource independently.

Learning to navigate the MGU Library effectively matters because the quality of your research depends not just on how hard you search but on where you search and when. A student who uses the encyclopedia at the right moment in a project gathers better background knowledge faster. A student who uses the guides before searching a database finds better sources with fewer wasted searches. A student who consults the relevant department for a complex project gets help that no search engine can provide.

The central principle of this chapter is simple: the MGU Library is a system, and systems reward people who understand how they work. This chapter teaches you how this one works.

Section 23.1

The MGU Library as a Research System

The MGU Library is not organized the way a bookstore is organized — by product category, with popular items near the front and everything else in the back. It is organized the way a research process is organized: by the kind of question being answered and the kind of thinking being supported at each stage.

To understand the MGU Library as a system, it helps to think about the stages of research and what each stage requires.

The Six Stages of Research
What each stage requires, and which tools serve it
  • Stage 1Orientation. When you begin a project, you usually know your general topic but not much else. You need definitions, context, background knowledge, and a preliminary sense of the scope and vocabulary of the topic. The MGU Dictionary and the MGU Encyclopedia serve this stage.
  • Stage 2Navigation. Once you have basic background knowledge, you need to know where the best sources on your topic are located — which databases, search strategies, and subject headings apply. The MGU Guides serve this stage.
  • Stage 3Skill building. Research tools require technical competence. The MGU Tutorials serve this stage, teaching skills through structured instruction and practice.
  • Stage 4Source discovery. With background knowledge, a research map, and search skills in place, you are ready to find actual sources — searching the catalog, using databases, and applying the strategies the guides and tutorials have taught you.
  • Stage 5Deep inquiry and expert support. Some research questions are complex, specialized, or multidisciplinary in ways that exceed self-directed searching. The MGU Library Departments provide expert assistance here.
  • Stage 6Structured learning. Some skills and topics are best learned through organized sequences of instruction rather than individual searches. Malonicus Rex Educare provides structured courses and learning pathways.
Principle: Knowing the stages helps you identify where you are in a project and which MGU Library resources are most useful at that moment — even if you do not follow the sequence rigidly every time.

Understanding this progression does not mean you must follow it rigidly every time. Some projects require only a few stages. Some projects circle back through stages as the research question develops. Some projects start in the middle when a student already has substantial background knowledge. But knowing the stages helps you identify where you are in a project and which MGU Library resources are most useful at that moment.

The MGU Library's Main Sections

Why the Order Matters

The most common research mistake is skipping the orientation and navigation stages and jumping directly to source discovery. Students who skip orientation — who go directly to a database without building background knowledge first — often search with the wrong vocabulary, fail to recognize relevant sources when they appear, and misread what they do find because they lack context.

Students who skip navigation — who search without knowing which resources are most useful for their topic — spend far more time searching and find far weaker sources than students who consult a relevant guide before they begin.

Core Principle

The MGU Library's structure rewards students who use it in order. Each section prepares you for the next one. Using them in sequence is not slower than skipping ahead. It is faster, because you search more strategically and evaluate more confidently at every stage.

Section 23.2

Using the Dictionary for Definitions

The MGU Library Dictionary is the appropriate first stop when you encounter a term you do not fully understand, when you need to verify the precise meaning of a concept, or when you want to confirm that you are using a word correctly in a specific disciplinary context.

What the Dictionary Does

A dictionary gives you the definition, derivation, pronunciation, and usage of words and terms. In an academic library context, dictionaries serve a more specialized function than a general-purpose dictionary: they define terms as they are used in specific disciplines, fields, and bodies of knowledge.

The MGU Library Dictionary section includes:

General dictionaries. Standard reference dictionaries that define words in common academic and professional usage. Useful for everyday vocabulary, basic definitions, and spelling.

Subject dictionaries and glossaries. Specialized reference works that define terms as they are used in particular disciplines — medicine, law, philosophy, economics, literary criticism, psychology, biology, and many others. These dictionaries give you the technical meaning of a term, not just the common meaning.

Bilingual and multilingual dictionaries. Useful for research that involves materials in languages other than English, for translation verification, and for understanding how concepts are named and framed in different linguistic traditions.

Etymological dictionaries. Sources that trace the historical origin and development of words. Useful for understanding how concepts have evolved and for recognizing the historical roots of specialized terminology.

When to Use the Dictionary

Use the MGU Library Dictionary when:

  • You encounter a term in a reading that you do not know or are not sure you fully understand.
  • You need to define a key concept in your own research before you can write about it clearly.
  • You are using a term in a specialized field (law, medicine, philosophy, social science) and want to confirm its technical meaning rather than relying on common usage.
  • You want to trace the origin or historical development of a concept as part of your research.
  • You need to check spelling, pronunciation, or usage of a term you plan to use in your writing.
What the Dictionary Cannot Do

The dictionary gives you the definition of a term. It does not give you the history, scholarly debate, significance, or complexity of the concept that term names. For that, you need the encyclopedia. The dictionary also does not give you sources on a topic. It is a definitional tool, not a source-finding tool. Once you have the definition you need, move on to the encyclopedia or the research guides.

Practical Example
Using the Dictionary Before Research

Suppose your research question is: "How does confirmation bias affect the spread of health misinformation on social media?" Before you search for sources, you should confirm your understanding of three terms: confirmation bias, health misinformation, and — if you plan to use it — cognitive heuristics. Looking up these terms in the MGU Library Dictionary, particularly in a psychology subject dictionary, gives you precise definitions, helps you identify related terminology (you may discover that "motivated reasoning" is a closely related concept), and ensures that you understand the terms well enough to recognize and evaluate sources about them.

This five-minute step — looking up key terms before searching — saves you from misidentifying sources later because you misunderstood a concept.
Section 23.3

Using the Encyclopedia for Background and Concepts

The MGU Library Encyclopedia is the appropriate second stop in most research projects. After you understand the key terms involved in your topic, you need a broader picture: the history of the concept, the major debates, the key thinkers and events, the current state of knowledge, and the connections between your topic and related topics. Encyclopedias provide exactly this.

What the Encyclopedia Does

An encyclopedia article gives you a structured, reliable overview of a topic written by a knowledgeable author and reviewed for accuracy. Encyclopedia articles synthesize what is known about a topic, organize that knowledge for a general or intermediate academic audience, and — in quality reference encyclopedias — cite sources and provide bibliographies.

The MGU Library Encyclopedia section includes:

General encyclopedias. Works that cover a wide range of topics across disciplines. Useful for initial orientation on almost any topic and for cross-disciplinary connections.

Subject encyclopedias. Works that cover topics within a specific discipline in depth. A philosophy encyclopedia, a legal encyclopedia, an encyclopedia of sociology, a historical encyclopedia, a science encyclopedia — each provides substantially more depth and disciplinary precision than a general encyclopedia for topics within its field.

Specialized reference encyclopedias. Works focused on a specific sub-field, period, region, or type of knowledge. An encyclopedia of African history, an encyclopedia of environmental ethics, an encyclopedia of public health — these provide expert-level overviews within very specific domains.

What an Encyclopedia Article Gives You

ElementWhat It Provides
Background contextThe history, significance, and scope of the topic, preventing misreadings of scholarly debate caused by missing context
Key concepts and terminologyThe specialized vocabulary used in discussion of the topic, which becomes your search terminology later
Major debates and questionsWhere scholars agree and disagree — a map of the intellectual territory before you enter it
Significant figures and worksNames that become useful search terms in databases and catalogs
Bibliography or reading listA curated list of sources, pre-filtered by an expert, before you have done a single database search

When to Use the Encyclopedia

  • You are beginning a research project and need background knowledge on the topic before you search for scholarly sources.
  • You are unfamiliar with a topic and need an overview before you can evaluate the sources you find.
  • You want to identify the major debates, key scholars, and significant works associated with a topic.
  • You need the specialized vocabulary of a discipline so you can search effectively in subject databases.
  • You want a bibliography of quality sources as a starting point for your source search.
  • You are working in a discipline or subject area outside your main field of study and need expert orientation.
When Not to Use the Encyclopedia

Encyclopedias are not appropriate as primary sources in most academic research papers. They are reference tools — they orient you to a topic — not evidence sources. Your paper should cite the original scholarly works the encyclopedia article describes, not the encyclopedia itself, except in specific circumstances where the encyclopedia article is itself a significant reference contribution.

Do not confuse the role of the encyclopedia with the role of databases. Encyclopedias give you background and orientation. Databases give you the scholarly literature.

Practical Example
Using the Encyclopedia to Build Search Vocabulary

Suppose your research question is: "What role did the Harlem Renaissance play in the development of African American literary and cultural identity?" Reading the relevant encyclopedia article — found in a subject encyclopedia of African American history or American literature — would give you the dates and context of the period, the major figures associated with it, the key debates about its significance, the names of important primary texts and institutions, terms such as "New Negro Movement," "race consciousness," "double consciousness," and "cultural nationalism" that you need as search terms, and a bibliography pointing you toward foundational scholarly works.

With this background, you would search databases with precision and confidence instead of trial and error.

The Encyclopedia and Citation Chaining

One of the most powerful uses of an encyclopedia is as a starting point for citation chaining. The bibliography at the end of a quality encyclopedia article points you to foundational sources. Those sources have their own bibliographies. Following this chain of citations from one source to the next is a well-established research strategy that quickly builds a strong source list. Chapter 22 of this textbook covers citation chaining in detail.

Section 23.4

Using Reference Pages for Quick Facts and Tables

The MGU Library Reference section is distinct from both the dictionary and the encyclopedia. It is designed for a different research need: quick access to specific, factual information rather than definitions or extended conceptual overviews.

What Reference Pages Provide

Reference pages compile structured, factual information in formats that are easy to consult without reading an entire text. They answer specific, bounded questions: What is the population of a country? What are the current legal standards for a practice? What were the dates of a historical event? What agencies regulate a particular industry?

The MGU Library Reference section includes materials such as:

Statistical tables and data compilations. Quick-access summaries of demographic, economic, scientific, or social data. Useful when you need to cite a specific statistic or establish a factual baseline for a research argument.

Chronologies and timelines. Organized records of events in a topic's history. Useful for establishing context, identifying turning points, and ensuring that your account of historical events is accurate.

Directories. Lists of organizations, institutions, agencies, professionals, or publications relevant to a field. Useful when you need to identify authoritative sources, experts, or institutional stakeholders on a topic.

Handbooks and manuals. Concise, authoritative overviews of established knowledge in a field. Handbooks summarize what is known, give standard definitions, and often include tables, formulas, methods, and references to foundational literature. They are particularly useful in scientific, technical, legal, and professional fields.

Almanacs and yearbooks. Annual reference compilations that summarize current events, statistics, and developments by year or topic. Useful for recent factual data and for tracking changes over time.

Style guides and citation manuals. Authoritative guides to citation formatting, writing conventions, and disciplinary standards. Useful for checking citation style requirements. Part VII of this textbook covers citation styles in detail.

When to Use Reference Pages

  • You need a specific fact — a date, a statistic, a standard, a regulation — rather than a discursive overview.
  • You need a table of data that you can incorporate directly into your research.
  • You need to establish a factual context quickly — the population of a country, the history of an event, the scope of an industry — before pursuing deeper research.
  • You need to identify experts, organizations, or institutions relevant to your topic.
  • You need a quick summary of the established knowledge in a technical field before you engage with the primary literature.

The Difference Between Reference Pages and the Encyclopedia

The encyclopedia gives you a narrative overview: the history, debates, and significance of a topic. Reference pages give you structured data: specific facts, tables, lists, and compilations. Both are useful, but they answer different kinds of questions.

If you want to understand what the opioid crisis is, use the encyclopedia. If you want to know the overdose death rate per 100,000 people by state in a particular year, use a reference page or a statistical database.

Practical Example
Combining Reference Pages with Deeper Research

Suppose your research question is: "How have federal minimum wage policies affected poverty rates among working families since 1980?" You might begin by visiting the MGU Library Reference section to locate a chronology of federal minimum wage changes since 1980 and a statistical table showing poverty rates over the same period. These facts give you baseline data for your argument. You then move to the encyclopedia for background on minimum wage policy debates and to the databases for scholarly analysis of the relationship between wage policy and poverty outcomes.

Reference pages give you the factual skeleton. Deeper research fills in the analytical and argumentative muscle.

Section 23.5

Using Guides for Navigation and Process Help

The MGU Library Guides are among the most immediately practical resources in the library for students beginning a new research project. A guide does not give you content — it gives you a map.

What a Guide Is

A research guide (sometimes called a LibGuide, a subject guide, or a pathfinder) is a curated web page created by a librarian that tells you which databases are most useful for research on a particular topic or in a particular discipline; which search strategies work best for this field; which reference works and encyclopedias are most authoritative; which subject headings and controlled vocabulary terms apply; which journals are most significant in this discipline; where to find specialized primary sources, data sets, archives, or government documents; and where to get additional help.

A guide is expert advice in written form. When you consult a subject guide before you begin database searching, you benefit from the research knowledge of a subject librarian who has studied the literature of that field and knows which tools are most productive.

Types of Guides in the MGU Library

Guide TypeDescription
Subject guidesOrganized by academic discipline or broad subject area — history, sociology, biology, business, and others
Topic guidesOrganized around specific research topics that cross disciplinary boundaries, especially useful for interdisciplinary questions
Course guidesCreated for specific courses or assignments, usually in collaboration with faculty
Process guidesFocused on research skills and processes — interlibrary loan, citation style, database searching — rather than topic content
Assignment guidesBuilt around a specific assignment prompt, linking directly to required databases, source types, and citation requirements

How to Use a Guide Effectively

The single most important habit for using a guide is consulting it before you begin searching, not after. Most students look up a guide only when they cannot find sources on their own. By then, they have wasted search time, possibly with the wrong databases and the wrong vocabulary.

The Right Sequence
Using a guide at the start of a project
  • Step 1Define your research question.
  • Step 2Consult the relevant MGU Library Guide for your subject or topic.
  • Step 3Note which databases the guide recommends.
  • Step 4Note which search terms and subject headings the guide suggests.
  • Step 5Note which reference works and encyclopedias the guide recommends for background.
  • Step 6Begin your searching with these tools.
This sequence takes five to ten minutes at the start of a project and saves hours of misdirected searching later.
What a Guide Does Not Do

A guide points you toward resources. It does not search them for you. After consulting a guide, you still need to search the recommended databases, evaluate the sources you find, and build your source list through independent work. The guide is a map, not a vehicle.

A guide also reflects the moment when it was created. Database offerings change, new journals become available, and subject areas evolve. If a recommended resource is no longer available, contact the relevant MGU Library Department for current recommendations.

Practical Example
Using a Subject Guide to Plan a Research Strategy

Suppose your research question is: "How does childhood poverty affect long-term educational outcomes?" Consulting the MGU Library Social Sciences Guide or the MGU Library Education Guide would tell you which databases cover education research most comprehensively (ERIC for education literature; PsycINFO for developmental research; Sociological Abstracts for sociological perspectives); which subject headings apply ("children — poverty," "educational attainment — socioeconomic factors"); which journals publish relevant research (the American Educational Research Journal, the Sociology of Education); which government data sources include relevant statistics (the National Center for Education Statistics); and which reference works provide authoritative background.

With this map in hand, you search efficiently in the right places with the right vocabulary — not by guessing.
Section 23.6

Using Tutorials for Practical Skills

The MGU Library Tutorials section teaches research skills through structured instruction. Tutorials are different from guides: guides tell you where to go; tutorials teach you how to do what you need to do once you get there.

What Tutorials Are

A tutorial is a step-by-step instructional resource that walks you through a specific skill or process with explanations, examples, and often practice activities. Library tutorials cover skills such as searching a library catalog using subject headings; using Boolean operators in database searching; constructing an advanced search in a specific database; evaluating sources using a standard framework; exporting citations to a reference manager; using interlibrary loan to request materials; finding and interpreting government data; reading and analyzing scholarly articles; creating a research log; and avoiding plagiarism through proper note-taking and citation.

Tutorials may be written step-by-step guides, short video demonstrations, interactive exercises, or combinations of these formats.

When to Use Tutorials

  • You are learning a research tool for the first time and need structured instruction before you begin using it independently.
  • You have been struggling with a search strategy and want to revisit the fundamentals before trying again.
  • You want to add a new skill to your research toolkit — for example, learning how to use a citation manager you have not used before.
  • You are preparing for a specific research task that requires a skill you have not recently practiced.
  • You want to confirm that you are using a tool or strategy correctly.

The Relationship Between Tutorials and Guides

Guides tell you which tools to use. Tutorials teach you how to use them. These two resources are most powerful when used together.

Recommended Sequence

1. Consult the subject guide to identify the right database for your topic. 2. If you have not used that database before, find the relevant tutorial and work through it before you begin searching. 3. Apply what the tutorial taught you to your actual research search in that database.

Skipping the tutorial and trying to learn a tool by trial and error in the middle of a real research project is inefficient and often produces poor searches. A twenty-minute tutorial prepares you to search confidently and productively.

Practice-Based Learning

Tutorials are most effective when you engage with the practice activities they include, not just read or watch passively. The goal of a tutorial is not to acquire theoretical knowledge about a tool — it is to build a skill you can apply. Skills are built through practice, not observation.

When you work through an MGU Library Tutorial, do the practice activities. If the tutorial asks you to perform a search, perform it. If it asks you to evaluate a source, evaluate it. The time invested in practice during a tutorial pays off significantly when you apply the skill to your research.

Tutorials as Remediation

Tutorials are also valuable when something has gone wrong. If you have been searching a database for an hour and cannot find useful sources, one possible cause is a skill gap — you may not be using Boolean operators correctly, you may not know how to limit by date or source type, or you may not be using subject headings effectively.

Before concluding that no sources exist on your topic, check the relevant MGU Library Tutorial to confirm that you are using the tool correctly. Many failed searches are not caused by lack of sources — they are caused by search strategies that do not match how the tool works.

Practical Example
Using a Tutorial Before a Database Search

Suppose you are preparing to search PsycINFO for the first time for a psychology research project. Before you begin, you find the MGU Library Tutorial on searching PsycINFO. The tutorial explains how PsycINFO's Thesaurus works and how to use it to find correct subject headings; how to use major filters including date range, publication type, and age group; how to limit results to peer-reviewed articles; how to save and export citations to a reference manager; and how to use the Related Records feature to find additional sources.

Working through this tutorial — twenty to thirty minutes — before you begin your actual search means you enter the database with the skills to use it effectively. The time spent on the tutorial is recovered many times over in the efficiency of the search that follows.
Section 23.7

Using Departments and Courses for Structured Learning

The MGU Library includes two additional sections that serve distinct but complementary functions: the Departments, which connect research to disciplinary expertise and provide pathways to specialized library support, and Malonicus Rex Educare, which provides structured course-based learning for research skills and information literacy.

MGU Library Departments

The Departments section of the MGU Library organizes resources, guides, expert support, and specialized collections by academic department and discipline. Where a subject guide is a self-service navigation tool, the Departments section connects students to a richer set of disciplinary resources and, crucially, to expert human support.

Subject-specific resource collections. Resources organized by academic department — history, biology, nursing, business, literature, psychology, education, engineering — that are relevant to the research practices and source types of that specific field.

Departmental research guides. More detailed and discipline-specific guides than the general subject guides, often created in collaboration with faculty and tailored to the research practices of a specific department's courses and projects.

Research consultation services. Subject librarians associated with specific departments offer individual research consultations for students working on complex or long-term projects. A research consultation is a scheduled meeting in which a subject librarian helps you develop a search strategy, identify key resources, navigate specialized databases, and plan your research path. For students working on theses, capstone projects, or major research papers, a research consultation can save days of searching and produce significantly stronger source lists.

Course-embedded library instruction. In some departments, librarians work directly with faculty to provide research instruction within specific courses. If your course includes a library instruction session, attend it — the instruction is tailored to your assignment and your discipline.

Interlibrary Loan assistance. If you need sources that the MGU Library does not hold, the Departments section provides access to Interlibrary Loan services that can retrieve materials from other libraries. For specialized or rare sources that are critical to your research, ILL is an essential resource.

When to Use the Departments Section

  • You are working on a major project — a research paper, a thesis, a capstone, a literature review — that requires comprehensive source coverage.
  • You need specialized resources that go beyond what the general guides and databases provide.
  • You are working in a discipline that uses source types you are not familiar with — legal databases, scientific databases, clinical databases, business intelligence databases.
  • You have been searching independently for several hours and are not finding adequate sources.
  • You need help developing a comprehensive search strategy rather than just a starting point.
  • You need sources not available in the MGU Library's direct holdings and want to use Interlibrary Loan.

How to Use a Research Consultation

A research consultation is a professional conversation, not a tutoring session. The librarian does not do your research for you. They help you think through your research question, identify the right resources, develop effective search strategies, and plan your research path.

To Make the Most of a Consultation

Come with a written research question, not just a vague topic. Bring your assignment prompt if you have one. Be ready to describe what you have already searched, what strategies you have used, and what you have found. Ask specific questions: "Which databases are most useful for research on environmental justice in urban communities?" is a better question than "I don't know where to start."

Malonicus Rex Educare

Malonicus Rex Educare is the MGU Library's structured learning platform — a set of organized courses, learning pathways, and instructional sequences designed to build research and information literacy competencies systematically.

Where tutorials teach individual skills and guides provide navigation for individual projects, Malonicus Rex Educare teaches broader competencies through structured sequences of instruction, practice, and assessment.

Research skills courses. Full instructional sequences on core research competencies — information literacy, source evaluation, database searching, citation and academic integrity, avoiding misinformation, and others.

Subject-specific learning pathways. Structured sequences of instruction tailored to the research practices of specific disciplines — history, health sciences, social work, and others.

Library orientation modules. Introductory sequences that teach new students how to navigate and use the MGU Library effectively.

Skill certification and assessment. Some Malonicus Rex Educare courses include assessments and certifications that verify research competency, which may be required by specific programs or pursued voluntarily.

When to Use Malonicus Rex Educare

  • You are beginning your academic career at MGU and want a comprehensive foundation in library and research skills.
  • You are entering a research-intensive program and want to prepare thoroughly before you begin.
  • You want to develop a specific competency more systematically than tutorials alone provide.
  • Your program or course requires completion of a specific library course or certification.
  • You want to strengthen research skills that you know are weak before you begin a major project.
  • You are preparing to teach or tutor research skills and need a structured framework for your own understanding.

Course Pages

The MGU Library also maintains course-specific pages that connect library resources directly to individual courses and assignments. When your instructor has worked with the library to create a course page, it is one of the most targeted and efficient resources available to you.

A course page for your specific course typically includes the databases most relevant to your course's research assignments; subject headings and search terms tailored to the course's topics; links to key journals in the discipline; guidance on the citation style required by the course; links to relevant tutorials for skills the course requires; and contact information for the subject librarian associated with the course.

Core Principle

If a course page exists for your course, consult it before you do any independent searching. The instructor and librarian who built it have done the navigation work for you. Using it is efficient and ensures that your research strategy is aligned with the assignment's expectations.

Section 23.8

Building a Research Path Through MGU

Understanding what each part of the MGU Library does is valuable. Knowing how to build a coherent research path through those resources — moving from one to the next in an order that matches the development of your research — is the practical skill that this chapter has been building toward.

A research path is a planned sequence of resource use that takes you from initial orientation to final source list in the most efficient and productive order. No two research paths are identical, because no two research questions are identical. But all strong research paths share the same underlying logic: they match the right resource to the right stage of the research process.

The MGU Research Path Framework

The MGU Research Question Model gives you the framework for planning your path:

MGU Research Question Model

Topic. Focused issue. Question. Purpose. Scope. Evidence needed.

Each element of this model points toward the MGU Library resources most useful for addressing it.

Model ElementMGU Library Resources
TopicDictionary and Encyclopedia — understand key terms and background before working with a topic
Focused issueEncyclopedia and Reference Pages — understand the shape of the issue in scholarly literature; define scope with specific facts and data
QuestionGuides — identify which databases, search terms, subject headings, and source types are most useful
PurposeGuides and Tutorials — identify the sources your purpose requires, then find them efficiently
ScopeGuides, Catalog, and Databases — identify resources, search for books, search recommended databases
Evidence neededCatalog, Databases, Departments, and Malonicus Rex Educare — match specific evidence types to specific resources and expertise

A Sample Research Path

To illustrate how this works in practice, consider a complete research path for a student working on the following research question:

Research Question

How did the Great Migration affect Black political representation in northern cities between 1920 and 1960?

Nine-Step Research Path
From orientation to citation chaining
  • Step 1Dictionary. Look up "Great Migration" in a general dictionary and in a subject dictionary of American history or African American history. Confirm the definition and its dates. Note related terms: "Black metropolis," "second Great Migration," "political incorporation."
  • Step 2Encyclopedia. Read the encyclopedia article on the Great Migration in a subject encyclopedia. Record key figures, key cities, key dates, and major debates. Identify five to eight foundational scholarly works from the bibliography.
  • Step 3Reference Pages. Find a chronology of Black electoral history in major northern cities between 1920 and 1960. Record relevant dates, population figures, and voter registration statistics where available.
  • Step 4Subject Guide. Consult the MGU Library History Guide and African American Studies Guide. Note recommended databases (JSTOR, America: History and Life, ProQuest Historical Newspapers) and relevant subject headings.
  • Step 5Tutorial. Complete the relevant tutorials for any unfamiliar databases before searching them.
  • Step 6Catalog Search. Search the MGU Library catalog using subject headings identified in Step 4. Locate books, place holds on checked-out items, and record call numbers.
  • Step 7Database Search. Search the recommended databases using the subject headings and search terms identified earlier. Save and export citations.
  • Step 8Citation Chaining. Check the bibliographies of the most useful sources found in Steps 6 and 7. Add significant works you have not yet found.
  • Step 9Department Consultation (if needed). If the question proves more complex than the initial search reveals, schedule a consultation with the relevant department for expert guidance.
This nine-step path moves efficiently from orientation to source discovery, uses each MGU Library resource for its intended purpose, and produces a stronger source list than any approach that jumps directly to database searching.

Adjusting the Path as Research Develops

A research path is a plan, not a constraint. As your research develops, you will often discover that your research question needs to be refined, a source leads you to a new angle on the topic, a key source is not available in the MGU Library, your topic is more interdisciplinary than you realized, or you have found abundant sources and need to narrow your scope.

Research Paths Are Iterative

The flexibility to adjust your path as your understanding develops is not a sign of poor planning — it is a sign of good research thinking.

The Research Path and Source Quality

The single most important argument for building a deliberate research path is its effect on source quality. Students who navigate the MGU Library systematically — using the dictionary, encyclopedia, and reference pages for orientation; using guides and tutorials for navigation and skill building; searching the catalog and databases with focused vocabulary and strategy — consistently find better sources than students who search without a plan.

Core Principle

Better sources mean stronger evidence. Stronger evidence means more persuasive research. The research path is not a procedural formality. It is the practical mechanism through which the MGU Library's resources translate into the quality of your finished work.

Misinformation Alert

Alert: Confusing Guide Quality with Source Quality

A research guide is a tool for navigation. It does not automatically ensure the quality of the sources you find through it. Two misinformation risks are specific to research guide use.

Risk 1
Assuming that recommended databases only contain reliable sources

Research guides recommend databases because those databases contain relevant academic and professional literature. But databases contain a wide range of sources — peer-reviewed articles, non-peer-reviewed articles, conference papers, editorials, book reviews, and working papers — and not all of these are equally reliable. A guide may direct you to a database that contains excellent sources; it does not guarantee that every item in that database meets your research standards. The MGU Source Evaluation Model applies to every source you find, regardless of which database or guide directed you to it.

Risk 2
Treating a guide's subject headings as exhaustive

Subject headings and search terms recommended in a guide are starting points, not complete lists. A guide cannot anticipate every valid search strategy for every possible research question within its scope. If the recommended subject headings do not produce adequate results, do not conclude that the sources do not exist — try alternate vocabulary, broader or narrower terms, or consult the department for additional search strategies.

Risk 3
Using an outdated guide without checking its currency

A guide last updated several years ago may recommend databases that have changed their interfaces, been discontinued, or been replaced by better resources. Always check the guide's "last updated" date and verify that the resources it recommends are still available and current. If a guide seems outdated, contact the MGU Library Department for updated recommendations.

Central Principle

The MGU Library's guides are among its most valuable tools — but they are maps, not destinations. The quality of your research still depends on the quality of your evaluation at every stage.

Citation Check

Citing MGU Library Resources in Your Work

When you use MGU Library resources in your research, you will rarely cite the guides, tutorials, or dictionary definitions themselves — these are navigation tools, not evidence sources. Your citations will be for the sources those tools helped you find.

However, there are situations where you may need to cite the MGU Library resources directly.

When to Cite a Dictionary or Encyclopedia

If you are defining a specialized term in your paper and the definition comes from a specific subject dictionary or encyclopedia, cite that source. This is most common in papers where precise terminology is important — legal writing, scientific papers, policy analysis.

Citation Formats
Citing an encyclopedia or subject dictionary entry
  • MLA"Mandatory Minimum Sentencing." Encyclopedia of Race and Crime, edited by Helen Taylor Greene and Shaun L. Gabbidon, SAGE Publications, 2009, pp. 505–508.
  • APAAuthor Last Name, First Initial. (Year). Title of entry. In Editor Initial Last Name (Ed.), Title of encyclopedia (pp. xx–xx). Publisher Name.
  • ChicagoAuthor Last Name, First Name. "Title of Entry." In Title of Encyclopedia, edited by Editor First Name Last Name. City: Publisher, Year.
When Not to Cite

Do not cite the research guide itself as a source in your paper. The guide helped you find sources; it is not itself evidence. Similarly, do not cite the MGU Library catalog — cite the specific book or item the catalog helped you locate. Do not cite a library tutorial as a source. Tutorials teach skills; the evidence in your paper comes from the sources those skills helped you find.

If you include specific statistics from a government reference source (Bureau of Justice Statistics, U.S. Census Bureau, National Center for Education Statistics), cite the specific report or table — not the MGU Library Reference section itself.

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 23.1The MGU Library is a research system organized around six stages — orientation, navigation, skill building, source discovery, deep inquiry, and structured learning — with each main library section mapped to the stage it serves best. Using the resources in order produces faster, stronger research than jumping directly to source discovery.
  • Sec 23.2The MGU Library Dictionary provides precise, specialized definitions of key terms. It is the right first step when encountering unfamiliar vocabulary, but it does not provide context, debate, or sources — those come from the encyclopedia and beyond.
  • Sec 23.3The MGU Library Encyclopedia provides background context, key vocabulary, major debates, significant figures, and a bibliography that launches citation chaining. It is a reference tool for orientation, not a primary evidence source for most academic papers.
  • Sec 23.4The MGU Library Reference Pages provide structured factual data — statistics, chronologies, directories, handbooks, and almanacs — that establish a factual foundation distinct from the narrative overview an encyclopedia provides.
  • Sec 23.5The MGU Library Guides are librarian-curated navigation tools that recommend databases, subject headings, journals, and strategies for a subject or topic. Consulting a guide before searching saves significant time and improves source quality.
  • Sec 23.6The MGU Library Tutorials teach the specific skills needed to use research tools effectively. They are most valuable before working with an unfamiliar tool and as a diagnostic step when a search strategy is failing.
  • Sec 23.7The MGU Library Departments provide subject-specific expert support, including research consultations and Interlibrary Loan, while Malonicus Rex Educare provides structured courses for systematic skill development, and course pages provide assignment-specific guidance built with instructors.
  • Sec 23.8A research path applies the MGU Research Question Model to plan a sequence of resource use from orientation through source discovery to citation chaining and, if needed, expert consultation. Research paths are iterative and should be adjusted as a project develops.

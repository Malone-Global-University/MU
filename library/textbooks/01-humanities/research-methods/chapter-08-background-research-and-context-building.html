Chapter 8 — Front Matter

Research Skill Focus and Overview

This chapter teaches context building through background research — the essential practice of learning enough about a topic to search effectively, evaluate sources intelligently, and ask sharper questions before moving into advanced academic research.

Why This Matters

Imagine walking into a conversation where everyone already knows the key terms, the major arguments, and the history of a debate. If you arrive without that foundation, you cannot follow the discussion, you cannot evaluate what people say, and you cannot contribute meaningfully. Beginning advanced research without background knowledge puts you in exactly that position — you are reading complex sources without the context to understand them, searching databases without the vocabulary to find anything useful, and evaluating claims without the foundation to judge them.

Background research is not a shortcut or a beginner's substitute for real research. It is a required phase of every serious research project. Professional researchers, journalists, scientists, lawyers, and scholars all build context before diving into primary and specialized sources. They read summaries, reference works, review articles, and introductory materials precisely because those sources orient them to the terrain before they explore it in depth.

For students, background research solves several problems at once. It gives you the vocabulary to search. It tells you which debates matter and which scholars or institutions are central to your topic. It helps you recognize whether a source is mainstream or marginal, foundational or outdated. It shows you how your topic connects to larger fields, time periods, or social issues. And it prevents the single most common beginner error in research: diving into a specialized, advanced source before you have any idea what the source is arguing or why it matters.

This chapter shows you how to build that foundation systematically and efficiently, so that when you move into focused academic research, you arrive prepared.

Section 8.1

Why Background Research Comes First

The Problem with Starting in the Middle

Most students who struggle with research share a common habit: they begin searching too fast. They type a keyword into a database or search engine, skim the first results that appear, and immediately try to use those sources in their paper. The results are often frustrating. The sources seem too complicated, too narrow, too technical, or too far removed from the actual question. The student reads the same paragraph several times without understanding it. They cannot tell whether a source is good or bad. They cannot connect what the source says to what other sources say.

This is not a reading problem or an intelligence problem. It is a sequence problem. The student started in the middle of a conversation without having learned the basics of what the conversation is about.

Background research solves this by establishing context before you search. Context means understanding the following:

  • What the key terms in your topic actually mean
  • What the major positions or debates in this area are
  • What has happened historically to produce the current situation
  • Who the relevant experts, institutions, or stakeholders are
  • What kinds of evidence matter in this field
  • What questions remain open or contested

Once you have that foundation, advanced sources become navigable. You recognize terms. You understand what the author is responding to. You can judge whether a claim is well-supported or whether it contradicts established knowledge. You can connect the source to the larger conversation around your topic.

Background Research Is Not the Same as Finished Research

One important clarification: background research is preparation, not conclusion. Encyclopedias, textbooks, and overview articles are excellent for building context, but they are rarely appropriate to cite in a final academic paper as your main evidence. They are starting points, not destinations.

Think of background research the way a traveler thinks about a good map. The map does not show you everything — it does not capture every detail of the landscape, every building, every person, or every hidden path. But without the map, you are lost. With the map, you can navigate. Background research is the map. It orients you. It does not replace the journey.

Your goal in this phase is to understand the terrain well enough to travel it intelligently. You are not collecting your final sources here. You are learning enough to collect and evaluate your final sources later.

What Background Research Helps You Accomplish

Well-executed background research helps you accomplish several things before you ever open a specialized database or read a peer-reviewed article.

OutcomeWhat It Means
Vocabulary acquisitionYou learn the terms researchers in this field actually use — essential for database searching. A student who searches "kids and bad food habits" finds far less than one who has learned "childhood food insecurity" or "pediatric dietary patterns."
Issue mappingYou learn who argues what and where the central disagreements are — preventing wasted effort arguing uncontested points or missing major objections already addressed.
Historical groundingYou avoid treating recent developments as if they happened in a vacuum. A paper on housing policy without knowledge of redlining or the Fair Housing Act will misread sources and miss crucial context.
Source navigationKnowing the major scholars, institutions, or publications in a field lets you evaluate unfamiliar sources more efficiently and recognize which voices are considered credible.
Question refinementBackground research often reveals that a question was too broad, already answered, slightly off-target, or in need of revision — far better discovered now than after hours of fruitless searching.
A Principle Worth Repeating

Do not merely find information. Earn trust in information. You cannot evaluate a source you do not understand. You cannot recognize a misleading claim in an area where you have no context. Background research builds the foundation that makes trustworthy evaluation possible.

Section 8.2

Using Encyclopedias

What Encyclopedias Are For

Encyclopedias are among the most underused research tools in university education, which is unfortunate because they are often exactly what a beginning researcher needs. Students sometimes avoid them because encyclopedias feel elementary — they associate them with middle school assignments. But academic and specialized encyclopedias are sophisticated research tools written by subject experts, designed specifically to introduce a topic's terminology, history, major debates, and key sources to a reader who is entering the field.

The key is understanding what kind of encyclopedia you are using and what it is designed to do.

Types of Encyclopedias

General encyclopedias cover a wide range of topics across all fields. They are useful for initial orientation to a topic, for understanding basic background, and for getting a first overview of a subject before you narrow. They are typically written for a general educated audience.

Subject encyclopedias are compiled specifically for a particular academic discipline or field. There are subject encyclopedias for history, psychology, philosophy, economics, literary studies, political science, chemistry, medicine, law, and dozens of other fields. These are far more sophisticated than general encyclopedias. They are written by scholars in the field, they use field-specific terminology, they describe major debates, and they include bibliographies pointing to foundational scholarly sources.

If you are researching a topic in a specific discipline, a subject encyclopedia for that discipline is one of the most efficient background tools available. The MGU Library Encyclopedia section is the right place to start looking for these resources.

What a Good Encyclopedia Entry Gives You

A well-written encyclopedia entry on your topic typically provides:

  • A clear definition of the core concept
  • An account of the history or development of the idea, event, or field
  • A description of major debates or subfields
  • Names of significant scholars, researchers, or practitioners
  • References to key foundational works in the bibliography

Each of these is research gold at the background stage. The names of scholars point you toward people whose work you might search for in databases. The key works in the bibliography give you a starting list of important sources. The description of debates tells you what your research question might need to address. The historical account prevents you from missing essential context.

How to Read an Encyclopedia Entry for Research Purposes

Do not read encyclopedia entries the way you would read a source to quote or cite in a paper. Read them as orientation documents. As you read, ask yourself:

  • What terms appear repeatedly that I should understand and use in my searches?
  • What events, policies, periods, or figures seem most important to this topic?
  • What are the main debates or positions in this area?
  • What sources appear in the bibliography that seem most relevant to my question?
  • What aspects of this topic seem most relevant to my specific research question?

Take notes on these questions as you read. The notes you take from an encyclopedia entry are not meant to appear in your paper — they are meant to equip you for the next stages of research.

Example 8.2 — Using an Encyclopedia Entry
From entry to research foundation
  • Topic The psychological effects of social media use on adolescents. You go to the MGU Library Encyclopedia section and locate a subject encyclopedia for psychology, then find an entry on social media and adolescent development.
  • Notice The entry distinguishes between passive and active social media use, and suggests that research findings differ depending on which type is studied. The terms "social comparison theory" and "fear of missing out" (FOMO) appear repeatedly. The entry mentions that findings are mixed — some studies show negative effects on mental health, others show neutral or even positive effects depending on context. The bibliography lists several key researchers in this area.
Result: You now have vocabulary for searching, a framework for understanding conflicting sources, specific concepts to investigate, and names to search in databases. This is what background research accomplishes.

When Wikipedia Is Useful and When It Is Not

Wikipedia deserves a direct, honest discussion. Wikipedia is a general encyclopedia that can be useful at the background stage for its function as a quick orientation tool, not as a citable source. It is collaboratively edited, which means its quality varies by article. Some articles are very well-maintained and include reliable references. Others are incomplete, outdated, or poorly sourced.

For background research, Wikipedia can be useful in two specific ways: first, it can give you a quick orientation to a topic you know nothing about, including terminology and historical context; second, and more importantly, its reference sections sometimes contain links to genuine academic and journalistic sources that you can locate and evaluate.

However, Wikipedia itself should almost never be cited in academic work as an authority. The reason is not that Wikipedia is always wrong — it is that Wikipedia is a tertiary source, a summary of summaries, written by anonymous contributors who may or may not be experts. When you cite Wikipedia, you are citing a summary at unknown levels of reliability. When you go to the actual sources Wikipedia cites and evaluate those, you are doing genuine research.

The Practical Rule

Use Wikipedia to get oriented quickly and to find leads to real sources. Do not stop there. Follow the trail to better sources.

Section 8.3

Using Dictionaries and Glossaries

Why Definitions Matter in Research

Definitions matter more in research than most students initially realize. A surprising number of research problems begin with vocabulary confusion. Students search for a term and find sources that seem to disagree with each other, not realizing that different sources are using the same term with different meanings. Or they read sources that use technical language without understanding what the terms mean, which makes evaluation impossible.

Before you search databases or read advanced sources on a topic, it is worth asking: do I actually know what these key terms mean, and do I know whether there are contested or technical definitions I need to understand?

General Dictionaries

General dictionaries are useful for precise definitions of common words. They help when you need to understand ordinary language precisely, clarify the meaning of a word that appears in a source, or verify that you are using a word correctly.

For research purposes, general dictionaries are most useful in the humanities and for writing clarity. If your research question contains terms that are not specialized — terms like justice, community, responsibility, harm, or trust — a quality general dictionary can help you work with the term more precisely.

Subject Dictionaries and Discipline-Specific Glossaries

Subject dictionaries are specialized reference works that define terminology within a particular academic field. There are subject dictionaries for law, medicine, psychology, philosophy, economics, literary studies, and many other disciplines. These are far more useful than general dictionaries for academic research because they capture the specific, technical, or contested meanings that terms carry within their fields.

TermEveryday MeaningTechnical / Field Meaning
TheoryLittle more than a guess or hunchIn physics: a well-tested, evidence-supported explanatory framework
BiasPrejudice or unfairnessIn research methodology: systematic error in data collection or analysis
SignificantImportant or largeIn statistics: a precise technical meaning tied to a probability threshold
Why This Distinction Matters

Confusing the everyday and technical meanings of a term while reading a methods section of a research article can lead to serious misunderstanding. When a study says a result is "statistically significant," it does not simply mean the result is important.

Glossaries found in textbooks, reference works, and databases also serve this function. Many academic databases include glossaries of discipline-specific terms. Course textbooks typically include glossaries that define terms as they are used in that field. These are excellent background tools.

Building a Working Vocabulary for Your Topic

One practical background research activity is creating a working vocabulary list — a personal glossary of the terms you will need to understand and use in your research. This list typically grows as you move through background sources. As you encounter unfamiliar terms in your encyclopedia reading, look them up. Add them to your list with brief definitions and a note about where you found the definition.

Your working vocabulary list serves two purposes: it helps you understand sources more accurately, and it gives you search terms. When you move to database searching in the next stage of your research, the terms on your vocabulary list become the keywords and controlled vocabulary terms that produce useful search results. This connection between background reading and search strategy is explored in detail in Chapter 9.

Contested Definitions

Some of the most important intellectual work in any academic field involves debating what key terms should mean. In political science, scholars debate the definition of democracy. In philosophy, scholars debate the definition of justice, consciousness, or knowledge. In sociology, scholars debate the definition of social class. In public health, scholars debate how to define health itself.

If your research topic involves a term that is genuinely contested — where reasonable, expert scholars disagree about what the term means or should mean — your background research should surface that contest. You should know that different scholars define the term differently, understand the stakes of the disagreement, and think clearly about which definition your own research question is using.

Study Note — Contested Terms Are Opportunities

Glossaries and subject dictionaries often acknowledge contested definitions. Encyclopedia entries usually describe definitional debates as part of introducing a topic. Pay attention to these moments. A contested definition is not a problem for your research — it is often the heart of the debate you are investigating.

Section 8.4

Using Textbooks and Guide Pages

Textbooks as Background Sources

Textbooks are often overlooked as research tools, but they are excellent sources for background knowledge at the context-building stage. A well-written textbook on a subject provides the following:

  • A structured overview of the major concepts, methods, and debates in the field
  • Definitions of key terms as they are used by scholars in that discipline
  • Historical context for how ideas developed
  • Summaries of significant research, usually with references
  • Explanations of methods used in the field
  • Often, bibliographies or further reading suggestions

For a student entering an unfamiliar area of research, spending time with a chapter or section of a relevant textbook is often the most efficient way to build the background knowledge needed for advanced searching.

Using Textbooks You Already Have

If you are researching a topic within a discipline you are currently studying, you may already have a textbook for that discipline. Check your textbook's index and table of contents for relevant topics. If your textbook is well-organized, it will point you to foundational concepts, key researchers, and further reading, all of which support your background research.

Even if your textbook does not cover your topic in depth, it may cover foundational concepts that relate to your topic. A psychology textbook may not have a chapter on social media and adolescent development, but it likely covers social comparison theory, identity development, and adolescent psychology — all of which provide context for that topic.

Guide Pages and Research Guides

Many libraries, including the MGU Library, offer subject guide pages — curated online guides that point researchers to the best starting sources for particular topics or disciplines. These guides are created by librarians who know the research landscape of the field.

A typical research guide includes:

  • Recommended encyclopedias and reference works for the subject
  • Key databases for the field
  • Important journals in the discipline
  • Suggestions for finding primary sources
  • Citation style recommendations for the field
  • Links to tutorials, glossaries, and other helpful resources

Research guides are efficient precisely because they do not ask you to evaluate everything from scratch. The librarian who created the guide has already done that curatorial work. When you use a library guide, you benefit from professional subject expertise.

At MGU, the Library Guides section is specifically designed to support this kind of navigation. If you are beginning research in an unfamiliar field, checking the relevant MGU Library Guide before you start searching can save significant time and help you avoid low-quality sources.

Textbooks and the Citation Rule

Remember the principle stated earlier: background sources are for context, not final citation. Textbooks are not usually appropriate to cite as your primary evidence in an academic paper, for a similar reason to encyclopedias: they are summaries and syntheses of the research, not primary research themselves. What textbooks do is point you toward the primary research. Follow those references to the actual studies, articles, and primary sources — those are what you evaluate and cite.

The exception would be if you are specifically studying textbooks themselves, or if your assignment explicitly allows textbook citation. When in doubt, ask your instructor.

Section 8.5

Timelines and Historical Context

Why History Matters for Contemporary Topics

A common student misconception is that historical context only matters when researching historical topics. This is incorrect. Most contemporary research questions have important historical dimensions, and understanding those dimensions is essential for reading sources accurately and avoiding significant errors in analysis.

Consider a few examples:

  • A research question about immigration policy in a particular country cannot be answered well without understanding the immigration history of that country — waves of migration, historical policy decisions, changes in law, economic pressures, and social attitudes over time.
  • A research question about the achievement gap in education cannot be analyzed responsibly without understanding the history of educational segregation, school funding inequity, and policy interventions over decades.
  • A research question about antibiotic resistance cannot be understood without knowing the history of antibiotic development, overuse, and the policy debates that followed.

In each case, a researcher who treats the topic as purely contemporary will miss crucial explanatory context, misread sources that refer to historical events, and potentially reach conclusions that better-informed scholars would immediately recognize as incomplete.

Finding Timelines and Historical Overviews

Timelines and historical summaries are available in several places:

  • Encyclopedias typically include historical background as part of their entries. The historical narrative is often one of the first sections of a good encyclopedia entry.
  • Reference works and chronologies are specialized reference books that organize events along a timeline. For historical research, chronologies are invaluable.
  • Textbook chapters often begin with historical context before introducing current debates.
  • Government and institutional websites sometimes publish official timelines of policy history, legislative change, or program development.
  • News archive searches can help you find historical reporting on events, though news archives require careful evaluation (see Part V on misinformation and evidence evaluation).

The MGU Library Reference section contains chronologies, almanacs, and historical reference works that support this kind of context building. The Encyclopedia section also provides subject encyclopedias with strong historical components.

Building a Historical Timeline for Your Topic

One useful background activity is creating a simple timeline for your topic. You do not need to be exhaustive — you need the major events, developments, policies, or turning points that your sources will reference. A timeline might include:

  • Key legislation or policy changes
  • Major events that shaped the current situation
  • Significant studies, discoveries, or publications
  • Important social or political shifts
  • Names of key figures and when they were active

A written timeline helps you read sources more accurately because you understand which period an author is discussing, what happened before and after, and why certain developments matter.

Chronological Awareness in Source Evaluation

Historical context also helps you evaluate sources. When you know the timeline of a topic, you can identify:

  • Sources that were written before an important development occurred, and therefore cannot account for it
  • Claims that were true at one time but are now outdated
  • Arguments that were reasonable given the evidence at the time but have since been refuted
  • Debates that have already been resolved, and sources on the losing side of that resolution
Core Principle

This kind of chronological awareness is a component of source evaluation that only background research can provide. The MGU Source Evaluation Model in Chapter 26 treats currency as one of seven core evaluation criteria — a timeline gives you the context to apply it.

Section 8.6

Overview Articles

What Overview Articles Are

Overview articles are a valuable type of secondary source specifically designed to survey and summarize a field of research. They go by several names depending on the discipline: review articles, literature reviews, survey articles, or overview pieces. Whatever they are called, they perform a similar function: they synthesize a large body of research on a topic, identify major findings and debates, and often point to the most important individual studies.

For background research purposes, overview articles sit between encyclopedias and primary research. They are more sophisticated and detailed than encyclopedia entries, but they are designed to orient and synthesize rather than to present new data.

Why Overview Articles Are Valuable

A strong overview article on your topic can accomplish several background research goals at once.

FunctionWhat It Accomplishes
Mapping the fieldSurveys what has been studied, what methods have been used, what findings have been established, and where disagreements remain.
Identifying key studiesPoints you toward the most important research; its citations are a curated reading list prepared by a specialist.
Understanding debatesExplains not just findings but where scholars disagree and why — essential for entering a conversation intelligently.
Getting current quicklyA recent review summarizes everything that came before it, orienting you to years of research in a fraction of the time.

Finding Overview Articles

The best place to find overview articles is in academic databases, which you will explore in depth in Part IV. For now, keep in mind that databases allow you to search for review articles specifically — most databases have a filter that limits results to review articles or literature reviews.

You can also find overview articles by:

  • Looking in journals that publish review articles (many fields have journals dedicated specifically to reviews)
  • Searching for a topic name plus the terms "review," "literature review," "overview," or "survey"
  • Looking at the bibliography of a textbook chapter, which often cites foundational review articles
  • Asking a librarian for recommendations

At the MGU Library, research guides often recommend key review articles or review-focused journals for particular disciplines.

Overview Articles and Background Research

Like encyclopedias and textbooks, overview articles serve primarily as orientation tools at the background stage. They are more citable than encyclopedias — in academic writing, citing a well-regarded review article as evidence of the scholarly landscape is legitimate. But the goal of reading a review article during background research is context-building, not source collection. Your aim is to understand the terrain so that when you read individual primary studies, you understand what they contribute and how they fit into the larger picture.

Section 8.7

Building a Concept Map

What a Concept Map Is

A concept map is a visual diagram that shows how the ideas, terms, and questions in your research topic relate to each other. It is not a formal academic document — it is a thinking tool. You draw your central topic in the middle and branch outward to show related concepts, subtopics, questions, distinctions, debates, key terms, and connections.

Concept mapping is one of the most useful activities a researcher can do at the background stage, because it forces you to make your understanding explicit. As long as background knowledge stays vague and unorganized in your mind, it cannot help you search or evaluate effectively. When you externalize it in a concept map, you can see what you understand and what you do not, where your knowledge is solid and where it has gaps.

How to Build a Concept Map

You do not need special software to build a concept map, though several free tools are available online if you prefer them. A piece of paper, a whiteboard, or a blank document all work perfectly well.

Method 8.7 — Six Steps to a Concept Map
Building the map from center outward
  • Step 1Place your topic at the center. Write your research topic or research question in the center of the page.
  • Step 2Branch to major subtopics. What are the main dimensions, aspects, or components of your topic? Write each one as a branch connected to the center.
  • Step 3Add key terms under each subtopic. What terms are associated with each subtopic? What vocabulary did your background reading introduce under each category?
  • Step 4Add questions. Where are you uncertain? What do you need to understand better? Write questions at the ends of branches where your understanding stops.
  • Step 5Add connections. Are there connections between branches? Draw arrows to show where subtopics relate to each other, or where one concept influences another.
  • Step 6Add sources. As you do more background reading, add the names of sources or scholars to the relevant branches. This helps you remember what you learned and where.

A Concept Map Example

Suppose your topic is food insecurity among college students. After initial background reading, your concept map might branch in the following directions:

BranchContent
Definitions and scopeWhat counts as food insecurity? How is it measured? What is the prevalence on college campuses?
CausesTuition costs, housing costs, financial aid gaps, food deserts near campuses, lack of cooking access, work obligations
EffectsAcademic performance, mental health, physical health, retention
Existing responsesCampus food pantries, SNAP eligibility for students, campus emergency funds
DebatesIs this a new problem or longstanding? Do pantries solve the problem or only address symptoms? Should federal SNAP policy change?
Key terms identifiedfood insecurity, food desert, SNAP, emergency aid, basic needs insecurity, retention, hunger, housing instability
Questions remainingHow effective are campus pantries? Which student populations are most affected? What institutional factors make some campuses worse than others?

Each branch of this map is a potential direction for more focused research. The key terms become database search terms. The questions become research directions. The debates identify the intellectual conversations your research will need to engage.

Using the Concept Map Throughout Your Research

A concept map is not a one-time activity. You will revise and expand it as your research progresses. After your background research phase, your map will be fuller. After database searching, you may add new concepts you encountered. After reading sources, you may add connections you had not noticed. The map is a living document that reflects and supports your growing understanding.

Students who maintain a concept map throughout a research project typically find it much easier to organize their notes, identify synthesis themes, and write their papers. The work of organizing and connecting ideas has already been done visually, so translating it into written form is more manageable.

Section 8.8

Moving from Background to Focused Research

Knowing When Background Research Is Sufficient

A common question at this stage is: how do I know when I have done enough background research? The answer is that background research is sufficient when you can do the following without significant confusion:

  1. Define the key terms in your topic clearly and accurately
  2. Describe the major debates or positions in this area
  3. Name at least a few key scholars, studies, or institutions associated with your topic
  4. Explain the basic history or context that your sources will assume you know
  5. Build a list of meaningful search terms to use in academic databases
  6. Read an abstract from a relevant scholarly article and understand what the study is about

If you cannot do these things, more background reading is needed. If you can do all of them reasonably well, you are ready to move into focused academic searching.

Study Note — Sufficient, Not Exhaustive

Notice that this threshold is not "know everything about the topic." Background research is preparation, not completion. You will continue to learn throughout the research process. The goal is simply to have enough foundation to search effectively and read sources intelligently.

From Background Notes to Search Terms

One of the most important transitions at this stage is converting your background notes into a search strategy. This is the direct bridge between background research and the searching you will do in databases and catalogs.

Your working vocabulary list, concept map, and background notes should by now contain:

  • Key terms in the field
  • Synonyms for those terms
  • Names of important scholars (searchable as author names)
  • Titles of important works (searchable by title)
  • Concepts that are related or broader or narrower than your main topic
  • Subject headings or categories that your background sources used

All of these become inputs for the search strategy you will build in Chapter 9. The connection between background research and search strategy is direct: the richer your background knowledge, the more effective your searching will be.

Revising Your Research Question Based on Background Research

It is entirely normal — and often productive — to revise your research question after background research. You may discover that:

  • Your original question was already answered by established research, and you need to find a more specific angle
  • Your question was using a term you now understand differently, and the revised definition changes the question
  • A related question that emerged from your background reading is more interesting or more researchable than your original one
  • Your original question is too broad, and your background reading suggests a more focused version
  • Your topic connects to a debate you did not know existed, and engaging that debate would strengthen your project
Apply the MGU Research Question Model

Topic. Focused issue. Question. Purpose. Scope. Evidence needed. Use this model to evaluate any revised question before you move forward. Working through these six elements after background research will tell you whether your question is ready for the next stage.

The Research Sequence So Far

At this point in the textbook, you have completed the first two major phases of the research sequence: identifying and narrowing your topic (Chapters 6 and 7), and building background context (Chapter 8). The next chapters in Part II will teach you how to translate your background knowledge into search terms (Chapter 9) and how to plan the rest of your research project (Chapter 10).

After that, Parts III and IV will teach you about sources and how to search for them effectively. But everything that follows in this book will be more manageable because of the background research foundation you built here.

The Whole System

Question, background, search terms, research plan, searching, evaluating, organizing, synthesizing, citing, presenting — a whole system. Each stage supports the next. Background research is not an optional preliminary step. It is the foundation on which everything else stands.

Search Practice

Search Practice

Activity 8-AEncyclopedia Entry Background Research

Select a topic you are currently researching or are likely to research for a class. Using the MGU Library Encyclopedia section, locate a subject encyclopedia relevant to your topic's discipline. Find an entry on your topic or a closely related concept.

As you read the entry, complete the following:

  1. Write a one-paragraph summary of the entry in your own words.
  2. List six to eight key terms or concepts mentioned in the entry.
  3. Identify two to three major debates or contested points mentioned.
  4. List any scholars, researchers, or institutions named.
  5. List any sources from the bibliography that seem most relevant to your research question.
  6. Write two to three questions the entry raised for you.

When you are finished, compare what you knew about the topic before reading the entry to what you know now. What did you gain?

Activity 8-BDictionary and Vocabulary Building

Using a subject dictionary or disciplinary glossary relevant to your topic (available in the MGU Library Reference section or in your course textbook), find formal definitions for three to five key terms in your research area.

For each term, record:

  1. The formal definition from the reference source
  2. Any alternative meanings or contested uses mentioned
  3. How your understanding of the term changed after reading the formal definition
  4. Whether the term has a different meaning in everyday language than in the academic field

Compile your results into a working vocabulary list that you will carry forward into your search strategy.

Activity 8-CTimeline Construction

Using background sources — encyclopedias, textbook sections, or overview articles — construct a simple chronological timeline of your topic. Include at least eight to ten major events, policy changes, publications, discoveries, or turning points.

When you are done, answer these questions:

  1. What period is most important for understanding your research question?
  2. Are there events your sources will assume you know that you had not been aware of before this exercise?
  3. Are there turning points that seem to have fundamentally changed the topic — moments after which the debate or situation was different?
  4. What sources did you need to consult to build this timeline?
Activity 8-DConcept Map Development

Using paper, a whiteboard, or a diagramming tool, build a concept map for your research topic. Your map should include:

  • Your central topic or research question
  • At least four major subtopics or dimensions
  • At least ten key terms distributed across the branches
  • At least five questions you still need to answer
  • Connections between at least three branches

Share your concept map with a classmate or instructor and explain the connections you drew. Describing a concept map aloud often reveals assumptions you did not know you were making.

Source Check

Evaluating a Background Source Using the MGU Source Evaluation Model

The following demonstrates how to apply the MGU Source Evaluation Model to a background source. In this case, the source is an article from a subject encyclopedia on psychology covering the topic of adolescent social media use and mental health.

MGU Source Evaluation Model — Applied
RelevanceDoes this source address your topic? For a student researching how social media affects adolescent mental health, an encyclopedia article specifically on this topic is directly relevant. It covers the terminology, the major research questions, and the debates that will appear in advanced sources.
AuthorityWho wrote this? Subject encyclopedia entries are typically written by scholars with expertise in the topic. Check whether the entry names an author and provides their credentials. A well-credentialed author in a reputable subject encyclopedia carries significantly more authority than an anonymous web article.
EvidenceWhat evidence does the entry offer? Encyclopedia entries typically summarize research rather than present original data. They cite studies, describe findings, and characterize the weight of evidence. For background purposes, this is appropriate. You are not expecting original research from an encyclopedia — you are expecting a trustworthy synthesis.
AccuracyIs the information consistent with what other background sources say? Cross-check key claims against your textbook or another reference work. If an encyclopedia entry says something that contradicts what your textbook explains, investigate further before accepting either claim.
PurposeWhat is the purpose of this source? Subject encyclopedias are designed to educate and inform. They are not advocacy documents, commercial products, or political arguments. Their purpose aligns with your background research goal.
CurrencyWhen was this entry written or last updated? For some topics, a background source from ten years ago is still largely accurate. For others — especially topics in technology, medicine, or rapidly changing policy areas — a source from five years ago may be significantly outdated. Check the date and consider whether developments since then would materially change what the entry says.
ContextWhat is the context in which this entry appears? A reputable academic publisher's subject encyclopedia is a different context than a general online reference compiled without editorial oversight. Consider the publishing context and whether it supports confidence in the entry's reliability.
Assessment

For background research purposes, a well-authored, recently updated entry in a reputable subject encyclopedia is a high-quality starting source. Its limitations — it does not present primary data, it synthesizes rather than investigates — are appropriate for the background stage and do not diminish its value as an orientation tool.

Research Notebook

How to Record Background Research Notes

Background research notes are different from source notes for a paper. They are exploratory, personal, and process-oriented. Their goal is to build your understanding, not to collect quotable material. Here is the MGU Research Note Model applied to background research.

MGU Research Note Model — Worked Example
SourceAuthor (if named), Title of Entry, Title of Encyclopedia, Publisher, Date, Location (database or library section). Example: Chen, M. (2022). Social media and adolescent mental health. In Encyclopedia of Developmental Psychology (3rd ed.). Academic Reference Publishers.
SummaryWrite two to four sentences summarizing the main points of the entry in your own words. Do not copy text. Example: This entry describes how research on social media and adolescent mental health has produced mixed findings, depending on whether use is passive (scrolling) or active (communicating). The entry identifies social comparison and fear of missing out as the main theoretical frameworks. It notes that some populations, including girls and socially anxious adolescents, may be more vulnerable to negative effects than others.
Key evidenceWhat were the main findings or claims supported in this entry? List them briefly. Example: Passive social media use is more consistently associated with negative mental health outcomes than active use; social comparison theory provides the primary explanatory framework in most studies; research findings vary significantly by gender, age group, and platform.
Key termsList vocabulary you learned or clarified from this entry. Example: passive vs. active social media use, social comparison theory, FOMO (fear of missing out), displacement hypothesis, upward social comparison.
QuotationIf there was a key phrase worth recording exactly, write it with quotation marks and a page or section reference. Use sparingly. Example: "Research consistently distinguishes between passive scrolling and active communicative use, with negative mental health associations concentrated in passive use patterns" (Chen, 2022, Introduction section).
ParaphraseWrite a paraphrase of an important point in your own words. Example: According to this entry, the relationship between social media and mental health is not uniform — the way a person uses the platform matters as much as how much they use it.
UsefulnessHow does this source help your research? Example: This entry gives me the vocabulary I need to search databases effectively and helps me understand why studies seem to conflict — they are measuring different types of use. It also points me toward social comparison theory as a framework I need to understand better.
LimitsWhat does this source not do or not cover? Example: As an encyclopedia entry, this does not present original data. It does not address platform-specific differences between Instagram, TikTok, and Twitter/X. It was published in 2022 and may not reflect the most recent research.
Citation infoRecord everything needed to cite this source properly — author, title, container, publisher, date, edition, pages, and access details.
Misinformation Alert

Background Sources That Are Not What They Seem

A significant misinformation risk at the background research stage involves sources that look like legitimate reference works but are not. Because students are building foundational knowledge, errors or biases introduced at this stage can corrupt everything that follows.

Watch For — Risk 1
Advocacy websites disguised as encyclopedias or reference works

Some organizations create websites that look like academic references but are actually advocacy tools promoting a particular viewpoint. They use neutral-sounding names, formal-looking layouts, and encyclopedia-style formatting, but their content is selected and framed to support a predetermined conclusion. Always check who publishes a reference-style website and what their organizational purpose is.

Watch For — Risk 2
Outdated entries presented without dates

A background source can become misleading if it is outdated, especially in fast-moving fields. If an encyclopedia entry or overview article does not clearly indicate when it was written or last updated, treat it with caution and verify its key claims against more recent sources.

Watch For — Risk 3
Fact-free definitional claims

Some websites define terms in ways that are politically or ideologically convenient rather than academically accurate. A definition of a contested scientific or social concept that quietly dismisses scholarly consensus or reflects a particular position rather than established usage is a warning sign. Check definitions against multiple authoritative sources.

Watch For — Risk 4
Wikipedia as a final authority

As discussed earlier, Wikipedia is useful as an orientation tool but should not be treated as a final authority. Its open editing model means some articles are excellent while others contain errors. Always follow Wikipedia's citations to primary and secondary sources rather than citing Wikipedia itself.

Watch For — Risk 5
AI-generated overview summaries

AI writing tools can produce plausible-sounding background summaries that contain fabricated details, false citations, incorrect dates, and misattributed claims. If you use an AI tool to generate background information, treat every claim with significant skepticism and verify each one against a real source before accepting it. Chapter 45 covers responsible use of AI tools in research in detail.

The Core Habit

Background research is where your knowledge foundation is built. If that foundation contains errors, your later research will be built on them. Take the time to verify that your background sources are legitimate, current, and authoritative.

Citation Check

Recording Background Sources Correctly

Even though you may not cite most background sources directly in your final paper, you should still record complete citation information for everything you consult. There are two reasons for this.

First, you may need to return to a source later. If you recorded only vague notes ("that psychology encyclopedia thing I read"), you may not be able to find it again. A complete citation record solves this problem.

Second, you occasionally do cite background sources. If you define a key term in your paper by reference to a subject dictionary, you need to cite that dictionary. If you describe the scholarly landscape of your topic by paraphrasing a review article, you need to cite the review article. Having complete citation information from the start prevents frantic searching for source details later.

What to Record

For every background source you consult, record at minimum:

  • Author(s), if named
  • Title of the article, entry, or chapter
  • Title of the encyclopedia, dictionary, textbook, or journal
  • Publisher name
  • Date of publication or last update
  • Volume and edition number if applicable
  • Page numbers or section identifiers if applicable
  • Database or URL if accessed electronically
  • Date you accessed it (for online sources)

The format of the final citation will depend on whether your paper uses MLA, APA, or Chicago style. Record the information now; format it later. It is much easier to format a citation when you have all the information than to hunt for missing details after you have returned a book or closed a browser window.

Example 8.6 — A Quick Citation Record
Recording details before formatting
  • Source An entry titled "Food Insecurity" in a subject encyclopedia called Encyclopedia of Public Health Policy, 2nd edition, published by Academic Reference Press in 2021. The entry was authored by Dr. L. Park. Accessed through the MGU Library online database on March 14. The entry appears on pages 412–419.
Your citation record should include all of those details, even if you end up not needing all of them. In MLA format, this entry would be cited as a work in an edited reference book. In APA format, it would be cited as a chapter in an edited volume. The specific formatting rules are covered in Part VII. What matters now is that you have all the information available when you need it.

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 8.1Background research establishes context — vocabulary, debates, history, key figures, and evidence types — before advanced searching begins. It is preparation, not finished research, and it builds the foundation that makes trustworthy source evaluation possible.
  • Sec 8.2Encyclopedias, especially subject encyclopedias written by scholars, provide definitions, history, debate mapping, and bibliography leads. Wikipedia is a useful orientation tool but should not be cited as a final authority; follow its citations to better sources.
  • Sec 8.3Dictionaries and glossaries, particularly subject-specific ones, clarify technical meanings that often differ from everyday usage. Building a working vocabulary list directly improves later database search effectiveness.
  • Sec 8.4Textbooks and library research guides provide structured overviews and curated pathways into a field. Background sources generally inform research rather than serve as final citations, though exceptions exist.
  • Sec 8.5Historical context matters even for contemporary topics. Timelines help researchers read sources accurately, recognize outdated claims, and understand which debates have already been resolved.
  • Sec 8.6Overview and review articles synthesize bodies of research, map scholarly debates, and point toward key studies. They are more citable than encyclopedias but still function primarily as orientation tools at the background stage.
  • Sec 8.7Concept maps externalize background knowledge visually, connecting topics, terms, questions, and sources. They evolve throughout the research process and support later organization and synthesis.
  • Sec 8.8Background research is sufficient, not exhaustive, when a student can define key terms, describe major debates, name key scholars, explain historical context, and read a scholarly abstract without confusion. Research questions are often productively revised at this stage.

Chapter 18 — Front Matter

Research Skill Focus and Overview

Most students begin research by typing a topic into a general search engine. That instinct is understandable. Search engines are fast, familiar, and always available. But search engines are not libraries. They index a fraction of the scholarly record, they rank results by popularity and commercial relevance rather than research quality, and they do not organize knowledge in ways that make browsing and discovery easy or reliable.

This chapter teaches students how to use library catalogs and digital collections as primary research tools. Students will learn how catalog records work, how to search by title, author, and subject, how call numbers organize physical collections, how digital collections differ from open web sources, and how to move from a catalog record to actual source use. By the end of this chapter, students should be able to navigate a library catalog with confidence, interpret catalog records accurately, and connect what they find in a catalog to their larger research process.

A library catalog does something search engines cannot do: it gives you organized, described, and classified access to an institution's entire collection. It tells you not just that a book exists, but what the book is about, who wrote it, how it relates to other works on the same topic, whether it is available, and where to find it.

Why This Matters

A catalog record is a description built by trained catalogers using shared professional standards. That description makes the item findable, distinguishable, and usable in ways that a search engine result rarely is. Digital collections extend the catalog's reach into archives, image collections, rare materials, primary source repositories, government documents, historical records, and specialized databases that most students have never encountered. Learning to use both catalog systems and digital collections means gaining access to layers of the scholarly and cultural record that remain invisible to people who search only the open web. This chapter matters because library catalogs and digital collections are gateways. Students who know how to use them reach better sources faster. Students who do not use them consistently work harder to find less.

Section 18.1

What a Library Catalog Does

A library catalog is a database that describes and organizes the materials in a library's collection. Its primary function is to help users find items by providing searchable descriptions of everything the library holds.

Catalogs serve several distinct purposes simultaneously.

Catalog FunctionWhat It Does
DiscoveryHelps users find items they are looking for by title, author, or subject when they know what they want.
ExplorationHelps users find items they were not specifically looking for, through related titles and browsable subject headings.
AccessTells users whether an item is available, where it is located, and how to obtain it.
DescriptionIdentifies author, title, publisher, date, subject headings, and summary so users can decide whether an item is worth pursuing.
OrganizationReflects a classification system that groups related items together on shelves and in search results.

Modern library catalogs are almost always online and are often called Online Public Access Catalogs, or OPACs. Many libraries now use discovery systems that search the catalog and licensed databases simultaneously, which can be convenient but can also blur the distinction between catalog records and database records. Understanding what a catalog does — and how it differs from a database or search engine — helps you use each tool appropriately.

What a Catalog Does Not Do

A library catalog does not index the full text of most books and articles. It describes items at the record level, not the page or sentence level. It also does not evaluate sources for you. A catalog record tells you what an item is. Evaluating whether the item is credible, relevant, and useful for your research is your job.

Section 18.2

Catalog Records

Every item in a library catalog has a catalog record — a structured description that identifies the item and makes it findable. Catalog records are created using a professional standard called MARC, which stands for Machine-Readable Cataloging. MARC defines specific fields for specific kinds of information, so that the same types of data appear in the same places across millions of records in thousands of libraries. This standardization is what makes it possible to search catalogs consistently and to share catalog records across institutions.

FieldWhat It Tells You
TitleThe full title of the item, including subtitle.
AuthorThe author, editor, compiler, or corporate body responsible, in standardized form.
Publication informationPublisher, place, and date — useful for evaluating currency and authority.
Physical descriptionPage count, illustrations, dimensions; or for digital items, file format and access restrictions.
Subject headingsControlled vocabulary terms assigned by catalogers that group related items together.
Summary or abstractA short description of content, helpful for quick relevance checks.
Call numberThe alphanumeric code showing where a physical item is shelved.
NotesIndex, bibliography, series, or translation information.
ISBN / ISSNStandard identifiers for books and periodicals.
Holdings informationAvailability, location, number of copies, and digital access status.

A well-read catalog record tells you a great deal about an item before you ever open it. Practicing the habit of reading full records — not just skimming titles — is one of the habits that distinguishes experienced researchers from beginning ones.

Section 18.3

Title, Author, and Subject Searches

A library catalog offers several primary ways to search: by title, by author, by subject, and by keyword. Each approach has distinct strengths. Skilled researchers know which approach to use for which research situation.

Title Searches

A title search is most useful when you already know the specific item you are looking for. Enter the main words of the title rather than the full title with articles — searching for Origins of the Cold War works better than searching for The Origins of the Cold War. Title searches are not effective for exploratory research, because they only retrieve items with those specific words in the title field.

Author Searches

An author search is useful when you want to find other works by a scholar whose work you have already found useful. Author searches in most catalogs use an inverted format: last name, first name. Be aware that the catalog uses a standardized authority record for each author's name. Corporate authors — government agencies, organizations, universities — also appear in author fields.

Subject Searches

Subject searching is the most powerful catalog search strategy for exploratory research. Subject headings are controlled vocabulary terms — standardized phrases assigned by catalogers to describe what an item is about. The Library of Congress Subject Headings (LCSH) is the most widely used system in academic libraries.

Example 18.3 — Subject Headings Versus Titles
Different titles, one shared subject heading
  • Title 1 Safe Water at Home
  • Title 2 Drinking Water Standards and Their Limits
  • Title 3 Contamination in Public Water Supplies
Principle: All three books may share the subject heading "Drinking water — Contamination." Searching that heading retrieves all of them, regardless of how different their titles are.
Search Strategy — Start Broad, Then Refine

Start with a keyword search to find one or two relevant items. Open the full catalog record for those items and look at the subject headings assigned to them. Then click on those subject headings to see everything else in the catalog assigned the same headings.

Subject headings are often subdivided by form, geography, time period, or topic. A heading like Climate change — Government policy — United States — 21st century is much more specific than Climate change alone. Do not guess at subject headings the way you would guess at keywords; if you cannot find relevant headings immediately, start with keyword searching and build from there.

Most modern catalogs also offer keyword searching, which searches across multiple fields simultaneously — title, author, subject, notes, and sometimes summary. Keyword searching is flexible and forgiving, but it is less precise than subject searching because it retrieves items that contain your search terms anywhere in the record, regardless of whether those terms describe the item's main topic. Use keyword searching to get started, then refine by moving to subject headings once you find relevant items.

Section 18.4

Call Numbers and Classification

Every physical item in a library collection has a call number — an alphanumeric code that places the item in a specific location on the shelves. Call numbers do two things simultaneously: they give each item a unique address and they group related items together.

Most academic libraries in the United States use the Library of Congress Classification (LCC) system. Public libraries more commonly use the Dewey Decimal Classification (DDC) system. Both systems organize knowledge into subject categories, but they do so differently.

Example 18.4 — Reading a Call Number
HQ1426 .F74 2021
  • HQThe broad subject class (Social Sciences — the family, marriage, women)
  • 1426The subclass within that category
  • .F74The Cutter number, encoding the author's name or title to create a unique identifier
  • 2021The year of publication
Principle: Letters group the subject; numbers narrow it; the Cutter number makes the item unique; the date comes last.

Because the LCC system groups related items together, browsing the shelves near a book you have already identified is a legitimate research strategy. If you find a useful book on environmental law at call number KF3775, looking at the books shelved nearby in the KF3700–3900 range will show you other books in environmental and natural resources law. This browsing strategy works in physical collections, and to some degree in digital catalogs as well: many catalog interfaces let you browse by call number, displaying the virtual shelf neighborhood around a particular item.

Reading Call Numbers in Order

Call numbers are read in a specific sequence. The letters come first, alphabetically. The integers that follow are read as whole numbers, not decimals. The Cutter number that begins with a period is read as a decimal. The date comes last. So on a shelf, HB71 .S53 2018 comes before HB172 .A44 2020, which comes before HC107 .N4 R38 2019. Understanding how to read call numbers prevents the frustration of not being able to find an item even after the catalog shows it is available.

Section 18.5

Digital Collections

A library's digital collections extend its holdings far beyond physical shelves. Digital collections may include digitized books, manuscripts, photographs, maps, audio recordings, video, newspapers, archival documents, data sets, and other materials that have been converted to digital format and made available online.

A digital collection is a set of items that have been selected, digitized or born digital, described, and made accessible through a structured interface. Libraries, archives, museums, and government agencies all maintain digital collections. Some are freely accessible; others require library authentication.

CollectionWhat It Offers
HathiTrust Digital LibraryA partnership of academic libraries that has digitized millions of books, with full text for public domain works and snippet or institutional access for others.
JSTORA digital library of academic journals, books, and primary sources, typically accessed through library subscriptions.
Internet ArchiveA nonprofit digital library preserving websites, books, music, and video, much of it freely accessible.
Project GutenbergA volunteer effort digitizing thousands of public domain books, freely available.
Library of Congress Digital CollectionsAn enormous, largely free repository of photographs, maps, manuscripts, newspapers, and audio recordings.
National ArchivesDigital access to federal government records, including historical documents and census data.
DPLAAggregates digital materials from libraries, archives, and museums nationwide into one searchable interface.

Quality digital collections share several characteristics: documented provenance, rich metadata, preservation standards, clear access and rights information, and institutional accountability. This accountability distinguishes library digital collections from random content found on the open web.

Digital collections are especially valuable for primary source research. If you are researching American history, immigration, the Civil Rights Movement, or World War II, digital collections may give you access to original letters, photographs, newspapers, government documents, and oral history recordings that would otherwise require a visit to a physical archive. They are also valuable for interdisciplinary research, since aggregated collections may surface connections across fields that subject-specific databases would miss.

Section 18.6

Item Descriptions and Metadata

When you open a catalog record or find an item in a digital collection, you are looking at metadata — structured information that describes the item. Metadata is the scaffolding that makes large collections searchable and interpretable.

Metadata TypeWhat It Tells You
DescriptiveTitle, author, date, publisher, subject, summary, genre, language — what the item is.
StructuralHow the item is organized — chapters, sections, pages, file structure.
AdministrativeHow the item was acquired, digitized, cataloged, preserved, and licensed.
TechnicalFile format, file size, resolution, and encoding for digital items.
Metadata Can Be Wrong

Metadata is created by people and can contain errors. A date may be incorrect. A subject heading may be too broad. An author's name may be misspelled. An item may be miscataloged entirely. When metadata matters to your research, verify it against the item itself and against other reliable sources. Metadata also reflects the decisions and assumptions of the people who created it — older records may use terminology that is now considered outdated, and digital collections may include metadata that reflects historical biases in how communities and subjects were described.

Not all catalog records are equally complete. A richly described record includes full bibliographic information, multiple subject headings, a summary, a table of contents, and links to related records. A minimal record may include only a title, an author, and a call number. When you encounter a minimal record, you can still evaluate the item by finding it physically, checking the title page and table of contents, or searching for the title or author in other databases to find fuller descriptions.

Section 18.7

Saving and Exporting Search Results

Finding a useful catalog record is only the beginning. If you do not record what you find, you may lose track of it when you are ready to write. Learning how to save, export, and organize catalog records is a practical research skill that saves significant time later.

Most online catalogs allow you to create a personal account where you can save records to lists or folders. If your catalog does not offer personal accounts, take full screenshots of catalog records — capturing title, author, date, publisher, call number, subject headings, and summary.

Export FormatTypical Use
RISA standard format most citation managers — Zotero, Mendeley, EndNote — can import.
BibTeXUsed primarily with LaTeX document systems.
Browser extension captureSome citation managers can capture catalog records directly from the browser window.
Keep a Search Log

In addition to saving individual records, maintain a search log that records what you searched, where, what terms you used, and what results were most useful. A search log prevents you from repeating searches unnecessarily and helps you remember which strategies produced the best results.

Example 18.7 — A Minimal Search Log Entry
Recording a catalog search session
  • DateOctober 14
  • DatabaseMGU Library Catalog
  • Terms"food security" AND "Sub-Saharan Africa"
  • Results47 items. Most relevant: Feeding Africa (HD9017 .A357 W45 2016) and Food Policy in Africa (HD9017 .A357 F66 2019). Subject heading "Food supply — Africa, Sub-Saharan" was more useful than "food security."
  • Next stepsCheck that subject heading for additional items; request Feeding Africa from the stacks.
Principle: A brief log entry, written at the time of searching, saves you from reconstructing your research path later.

Reference managers like Zotero, Mendeley, and EndNote let you build a personal library of sources with full bibliographic information, notes, tags, and attached files, and can generate formatted citations and bibliographies in multiple citation styles. Using a reference manager from the beginning of a project — not as an afterthought — gives you an organized record of every source you have considered, not just the ones you ended up using. Chapter 24 of this textbook covers search result management and reference managers in detail.

Section 18.8

Moving from Catalog Record to Source Use

Finding a catalog record is not the same as using a source. The record describes the item. The item itself contains the evidence. Understanding how to move from record to source is the last step of this chapter — and the first step of serious research.

Orienting Yourself in a Physical Item

When you get a physical item from the shelf, do not immediately begin reading from page one. Instead, orient yourself by reading the title page and verso to confirm publication details, reading the table of contents to see the book's structure, reading the introduction or preface to understand the argument and sources, checking the index for your specific topic, and checking the bibliography for additional sources.

Accessing Digital Items

For items available digitally through the library, the catalog record will include a link. Click the link, authenticate if required, and use the same orientation strategy: title page, table of contents, introduction, index, bibliography. Be aware that some digital books have restrictions on viewing, downloading, printing, or simultaneous access.

If the Item Is UnavailableWhat to Do
Checked outPlace a hold; you will be notified when it is returned.
Not owned by your libraryRequest it through Interlibrary Loan (ILL), which retrieves items from other libraries, usually at no cost.
Needed for a courseCheck whether a course reserve copy is available for short-term checkout.
Evaluate Before You Commit

Once you have the item in hand, ask: Is this actually about my research question, or does it only touch on it briefly? Is it at the right level? Is the publication date appropriate? Does the author have relevant credentials? Are the sources in its bibliography credible and relevant? If the item passes this quick check, add it to your research log and begin reading. If it does not, note why — this prevents you from finding it again later and repeating the evaluation.

Search Practice

Activity 18ACatalog Record Analysis

Access the MGU Library catalog or a publicly available library catalog such as the University of California system catalog (catalog.lib.berkeley.edu) or the New York Public Library catalog (catalog.nypl.org). Search for a topic related to your current research interest or choose one from the list below: urban food deserts, climate migration, digital privacy law, prison education programs, or water rights in the American West. Find one relevant book and open its full catalog record.

  1. What is the full title, including subtitle?
  2. Who is the author or editor?
  3. What is the publisher and date of publication?
  4. What is the call number?
  5. List all subject headings assigned to this item.
  6. Does the record include a summary? If so, does the summary match your research question?
  7. Click on the most relevant subject heading. How many other items in the catalog share that subject heading?
  8. Select two of those related items and record their full titles and call numbers.
Activity 18BDigital Collection Exploration

Visit one of the following digital collections: Library of Congress Digital Collections (loc.gov/collections), Digital Public Library of America (dp.la), or Internet Archive (archive.org). Choose a topic connected to your research or personal interest. Search for primary source materials — photographs, letters, historical documents, audio recordings, or maps — related to that topic. Find three items.

  1. The title of the item
  2. The creator or institution
  3. The date (or estimated date)
  4. The type of item (photograph, letter, map, recording, etc.)
  5. What the item shows, says, or records
  6. What research question this item might help answer
  7. The URL or collection name where you found it
Chapter 18 — Source Check

Source Check: Pre-Evaluating a Book from Its Catalog Record

The following is a sample catalog record. Read it carefully and answer the evaluation questions that follow.

Sample Catalog Record
Reckoning with Silence: Truth Commissions and the Politics of Memory in Transitional Democracies
  • AuthorBrennan, Caitlin M.
  • PublicationOxford University Press, 2019
  • Pagesxiv, 342 p. : ill., maps ; 24 cm
  • Call No.JC596 .B74 2019
  • SubjectsTruth commissions — Political aspects; Transitional justice; Memory — Political aspects; Democracy — Developing countries; Human rights — History — 21st century
  • SummaryAn examination of how truth commissions in post-conflict societies shape collective memory and democratic identity. Brennan analyzes case studies from South Africa, Argentina, Cambodia, and Rwanda, arguing that the success of truth commissions depends less on their formal mandates than on the political environments in which they operate. The book challenges prevailing transitional justice frameworks and proposes a new model for understanding how societies reckon with past atrocities.
  • IncludesBibliography (pp. 307–328), index
  1. Relevance. If your research question is "How do truth commissions affect democratization in post-conflict societies?", is this book relevant? What specific evidence from the record supports your answer?
  2. Authority. What does the record tell you about the author's likely qualifications? What does the publisher tell you about the review process this book went through?
  3. Currency. Is 2019 an appropriate publication date for research on 21st-century transitional justice? Are there potential limitations given this date?
  4. Scope. What countries does the book examine? Is this geographic scope appropriate for a research question focused on post-conflict societies generally? What might be missing?
  5. Evidence. The record notes that the book includes a bibliography of 21 pages. What does this suggest about the research base of the book?
  6. Purpose. Based on the summary, what argument does this book make? Is this book primarily descriptive, analytical, or argumentative?
  7. Next steps. Based on the subject headings, what two other searches would you conduct to find related sources?

Chapter 18 — Misinformation Alert

Misinformation Alert: Confusing Catalog Records with Source Quality

Findability Is Not Credibility

A common misinformation risk in library research is assuming that because an item appears in a library catalog, it is reliable. Library catalogs describe a collection; they do not vet every item in it for accuracy. Libraries hold materials that represent a wide range of perspectives, quality levels, and scholarly standards. Academic library collections are curated using selection criteria that favor scholarly and authoritative materials, but they also hold popular books, outdated editions, and materials whose scholarly value has been questioned since they were acquired.

Do not confuse findability with credibility. A book that appears in a library catalog must still be evaluated using the MGU Source Evaluation Model:

MGU Source Evaluation Model

Relevance. Authority. Evidence. Accuracy. Purpose. Currency. Context.

A catalog record is a starting point, not an endpoint. The work of evaluation is yours.

Special Caution with Older Records

Some catalog records are very old and were created under different descriptive standards. Subject headings from earlier decades may use terminology that is no longer accepted. Descriptions may reflect incomplete or inaccurate information. Always verify metadata from older records against the item itself.

Chapter 18 — Citation Check

Citation Check: Recording a Book Found Through a Library Catalog

When you find a book through a library catalog that you intend to use in your research, record the full citation information immediately. Do not wait until you are writing your paper. The catalog record contains all the information you need to create a citation, but catalog records are not permanent — they may be updated, items may be moved, and digital catalog sessions expire.

What to Record

Author's full name (last name, first name) · Full title including subtitle · Edition (if not the first) · City of publication · Publisher · Year of publication · Call number (for your own navigation) · Whether you read the physical book, an ebook, or a digital copy

Citation Format Examples
Winne, Closing the Food Gap (2008)
  • MLAWinne, Mark. Closing the Food Gap: Resetting the Table in the Land of Plenty. Beacon Press, 2008.
  • APAWinne, M. (2008). Closing the food gap: Resetting the table in the land of plenty. Beacon Press.
  • ChicagoWinne, Mark. Closing the Food Gap: Resetting the Table in the Land of Plenty. Boston: Beacon Press, 2008.
Common Citation Errors to Avoid

Omitting the subtitle — subtitles are part of the full title and must be included. Using an incorrect publication date — always check the copyright date on the title page verso, not the date on the cover or spine, which may be a later printing. Citing the wrong edition — record the edition number if you are using a revised edition. Omitting the publisher — it is easy to overlook when recording quickly.

Chapter 18 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary, Review, and Closing

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 18.1A library catalog describes, organizes, and provides access to a library's collection through discovery, exploration, access, description, and organization — but it does not index full text or evaluate sources for you.
  • Sec 18.2Catalog records are structured descriptions built using the MARC standard, including title, author, publication information, subject headings, call numbers, and holdings information.
  • Sec 18.3Title searches suit known items; author searches suit known scholars; subject searches using Library of Congress Subject Headings suit exploratory research; keyword searches are flexible but less precise.
  • Sec 18.4Call numbers, especially under the Library of Congress Classification system, group related items together, making shelf browsing a legitimate research strategy once a useful item is found.
  • Sec 18.5Digital collections such as HathiTrust, JSTOR, Internet Archive, Project Gutenberg, the Library of Congress, and DPLA extend access to primary sources and digitized materials with documented provenance and institutional accountability.
  • Sec 18.6Metadata — descriptive, structural, administrative, and technical — makes collections searchable, but it is created by people and can contain errors or reflect outdated terminology that must be evaluated critically.
  • Sec 18.7Saving records, exporting citations, keeping search logs, and using reference managers prevent lost sources and make citation building more accurate.
  • Sec 18.8Moving from a catalog record to source use means locating or accessing the item, orienting yourself through its table of contents and introduction, using holds or Interlibrary Loan when needed, and evaluating the item before committing to read it fully.
Central Principle

Do not merely find information. Earn trust in information. The library catalog is a tool for finding. Evaluation, verification, and synthesis are the work that earns trust.

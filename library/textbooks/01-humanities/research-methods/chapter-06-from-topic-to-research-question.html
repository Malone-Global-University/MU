Chapter 6 — Front Matter

Research Skill Focus and Overview

Most research projects do not fail in the library. They fail before the library. They fail because the student never moved from a general area of interest to a question that could actually be investigated with sources, evidence, and reasoning.

"Climate change" is not a research question. "Social media" is not a research question. "The Civil War" is not a research question. These are topics — territories of human knowledge wide enough to fill thousands of books, decades of scholarship, and entire university departments. A topic tells you where to look. A question tells you what to find.

This distinction matters enormously in academic life. When an instructor assigns a research paper, they are not asking you to report everything known about a subject. They are asking you to investigate a specific question, evaluate relevant evidence, and reach a reasoned conclusion. That process cannot begin without a question.

Research Skill Focus

Transforming a broad topic into a focused, researchable question that drives productive inquiry.

This chapter teaches you how to make that move — from the broad territory of a topic to the focused specificity of a research question. It also teaches you to recognize the difference between a topic, an issue, a problem, a question, and a working claim, because each of these is a distinct thing, and treating them as interchangeable is one of the most common and costly mistakes student researchers make.

Why This Matters

The central principle of this chapter — and of this textbook — applies with particular force here: Do not merely find information. Earn trust in information. You cannot earn trust in information until you know exactly what information you are looking for — and why. A vague topic produces a vague search, an overwhelming pile of results, and a paper without direction. A focused research question tells you what counts as relevant evidence before you ever open a database, which means you spend your research time evaluating sources instead of drowning in them.

Section 6.1

Topics Versus Questions

A topic is a subject area. It is a domain of knowledge, a field of inquiry, a category of human experience. Topics are useful for orientation. They point you in a direction. But they do not drive research because they do not ask anything.

A research question is an interrogative sentence — an actual question — that specifies what you want to find out, why it matters, and what kind of evidence could help answer it. Research questions are the engines of inquiry. Without them, research has no direction, no scope, and no purpose.

TopicResearch Question
VaccinationHow have parental concerns about vaccine safety changed since the introduction of social media platforms?
The American Civil WarTo what extent did economic competition between Northern industrialists and Southern planters shape secession debates in the 1850s?
Mental healthWhat barriers prevent first-generation college students from accessing campus mental health services?
Artificial intelligenceHow are public school districts in the United States currently addressing the use of AI writing tools in student assignments?
Ocean pollutionWhat are the most cost-effective policy strategies for reducing microplastic contamination in coastal fisheries?

Notice what the questions do that the topics cannot. They specify a subject, a context, a time period or setting, and a purpose. They suggest what kind of evidence would be relevant. They make the research project manageable because they define success: you are done researching when you can answer the question with evidence.

Topics invite you in. Questions show you where to walk once you are inside.

Why Students Resist Moving from Topic to Question

Many students are comfortable with topics and uncomfortable with questions because questions feel risky. If you commit to a question and cannot find evidence, you might feel stuck. If you state a question and your research changes your mind, you might worry you did something wrong. Neither concern is justified. Questions are not traps. They are starting points. Research questions can be revised, narrowed, reframed, or replaced as you learn more. The goal is not to pick a perfect question on day one. The goal is to have a question on day one — something to drive your searching, guide your reading, and shape your thinking.

The Spectrum from Topic to Question

Most students move through several stages as they develop a research question:

Stages of Development
From general interest to working claim
  • Stage 1General topic — "I'm interested in mental health."
  • Stage 2Focused subtopic — "I'm interested in mental health among college students."
  • Stage 3Issue or problem — "I'm interested in why college students don't use mental health services."
  • Stage 4Research question — "What barriers prevent first-generation college students from accessing campus mental health services?"
  • Stage 5Working claim — "First-generation college students face financial, cultural, and informational barriers that reduce their use of campus mental health services, even when those services are free."
Principle: You will not always move through every stage in order. The important thing is that you do not stop at stage one or two and call it a research project.
Section 6.2

Broad Topics and Focused Issues

One of the most useful moves in research planning is the shift from a broad topic to a focused issue within that topic.

A broad topic covers a large territory. It includes many subtopics, many historical periods, many geographic contexts, many disciplinary perspectives, and many unresolved debates. Broad topics are useful for encyclopedias and survey textbooks, but they are not useful for academic papers.

A focused issue is a specific problem, debate, question, or phenomenon within the broader topic. It has a scope that could be addressed in a single research project. It points toward a particular kind of evidence. It has a context — a time, a place, a population, a discipline.

Broad TopicPossible Focused Issues
EducationChronic absenteeism in urban elementary schools during the post-pandemic period; the impact of standardized testing on curriculum design; how rural teachers access professional development; the role of school libraries in promoting reading
ImmigrationEconomic outcomes for first-generation immigrants in healthcare; the legal status of Dreamers since 2012; how enforcement policy affects community trust in police; language acquisition in adult ESL programs
TechnologyCybersecurity vulnerabilities in hospital systems; social media use and teen sleep quality; algorithm design and political news exposure; facial recognition and public surveillance ethics

Notice that each focused issue is still broad enough to support substantial research. You would not exhaust the topic in a single paper. But each focused issue is narrow enough to have a shape — to point toward specific sources, specific time periods, and specific kinds of evidence.

How to Move from Topic to Focused Issue

Ask yourself: What specifically about this topic interests me? Is there a debate, problem, controversy, or unresolved question within this topic? Is there a particular population, time period, or place I want to focus on? What have I already read about this topic that surprised, puzzled, or challenged me? What do I wish I understood better?

Any of these questions can help you locate a focused issue inside a broad topic. The key is to look for specificity — a moment in time, a particular group of people, a concrete problem, a real debate, a specific policy or practice.

The "Within" Method

One practical technique is to think of your focused issue as something that exists within the broader topic. You might say: "Within the broad topic of criminal justice, I am interested in the specific issue of how solitary confinement affects long-term mental health outcomes in incarcerated individuals." The "within" framing helps you see the relationship between topic and issue clearly.

Section 6.3

Problems Worth Investigating

Not every topic or issue is worth researching — at least not right now, not for this assignment, not given your available sources and time. Part of developing research skill is learning to identify problems that are genuinely worth investigating.

CharacteristicWhat It Means
Not already settledIf the answer is well established and universally agreed upon, research is confirming what everyone already knows rather than investigating genuine uncertainty
Not purely opinionResearch requires evidence. "What is the best flavor of ice cream?" has no evidence-based answer; "What factors influence consumer flavor preferences?" does
Connects to real consequencesThe strongest research questions carry stakes — something is at risk if the question goes unanswered
Evidence is accessibleSources must exist that could help you investigate the problem, given your resources, language, and access
Sized for the assignmentA ten-page paper cannot address the causes of the First World War, but it can address one cause, one interpretation, or one decision in one month
Example 6.3 — Problems Worth Investigating
Genuine problems with real stakes and accessible evidence
  • HealthWhy do patients in rural communities have worse outcomes after cardiac events than patients in urban areas, and what role does distance from emergency care play?
  • LawHow have copyright law interpretations changed since the introduction of music streaming services, and what legal arguments do rights holders use to pursue infringement claims?
  • EducationWhat instructional strategies have proven most effective in teaching reading comprehension to English Language Learners in grades 3–5?
  • PoliticsTo what extent do city council members in mid-sized American cities rely on constituent contact versus interest group lobbying when casting zoning votes?
Each of these is a genuine problem. The answer is not obvious. Evidence exists. The stakes are real. And each is specific enough to be researchable.
Section 6.4

Descriptive Questions

Research questions can take several different forms depending on what kind of knowledge they seek. Understanding these forms helps you match your question to the right kind of research strategy, source types, and evidence.

The first major type is the descriptive question. A descriptive question asks: What is the case? What exists? What happens? What does this look like?

Descriptive questions are not superficial. They can be extremely sophisticated. Describing a complex phenomenon accurately requires careful observation, systematic data collection, and precise reporting. Many of the most important research contributions in history have been primarily descriptive — mapping unknown territories, documenting species, describing disease symptoms, recording the practices of communities that had never been studied before.

Example 6.4 — Descriptive Research Questions
What is the case?
  • Q1What are the most common reasons students at community colleges withdraw from courses during the first semester?
  • Q2How do food pantry users in urban neighborhoods describe their experience accessing services?
  • Q3What types of digital content do teenagers aged 13–17 report consuming most frequently on a typical school day?
  • Q4How has the demographic composition of the United States federal judiciary changed since 1980?
Descriptive questions often begin with what, how, who, where, or when. They seek to accurately characterize something.
Where Descriptive Evidence Comes From

Descriptive research often relies on surveys, interviews, observational studies, historical records, census data, and systematic reviews of existing documentation. When you write a descriptive research paper, you are building a clear, accurate picture of something based on evidence.

The Limits of Descriptive Questions

Descriptive questions can be excellent starting points, but they do not explain causes, assess effectiveness, or argue for a particular course of action. If your assignment asks you to analyze, argue, or evaluate, a purely descriptive question may not fully meet the requirements. You may need to move from description to analysis or argument.

Section 6.5

Analytical Questions

An analytical question asks: Why does this happen? How does this work? What explains this pattern? What are the causes or consequences of this phenomenon?

Analytical questions dig beneath the surface of what exists to ask why or how. They require not only describing a phenomenon but also identifying its components, explaining its mechanisms, tracing its causes, or examining its consequences. Analytical research requires evidence, but it also requires reasoning — the ability to connect evidence to explanation.

Example 6.5 — Analytical Questions
Why does this happen? How does this work?
  • Q1Why do first-generation college students complete FAFSA applications at lower rates than students whose parents attended college?
  • Q2How does the peer review process in scientific publishing affect the speed at which new findings reach clinical practice?
  • Q3What factors explain why some cities responded more effectively than others to the opioid crisis between 2010 and 2020?
  • Q4Why do women leave technology careers at significantly higher rates than men within five years of entering the field?
These questions are not satisfied with simply knowing that something is true. They want to understand the mechanisms, causes, factors, or processes that make it true.
Where Analytical Evidence Comes From

Analytical research often relies on comparative studies, longitudinal data, qualitative research, case studies, experiments, and expert interpretation. Analytical papers typically present multiple factors or explanations, assess the strength of each, and argue for a particular interpretation of the evidence.

Descriptive vs. Analytical

A descriptive question might ask: "How many students withdraw from community college courses in their first semester?" An analytical question asks: "Why do students withdraw from community college courses in their first semester, and which factors most strongly predict withdrawal?" The analytical question uses the descriptive finding as a starting point and then goes deeper.

Section 6.6

Argument Questions

An argument question asks: What should be done? Which approach is better? Is this policy effective? Is this ethical? What does the evidence suggest we should conclude?

Argument questions are evaluative. They require the researcher not only to describe and analyze but to take a reasoned position. Argument research papers make claims. They assert that something is true, valuable, effective, harmful, or necessary, and they support those claims with evidence and reasoning.

Academic argument is not the same as opinion. When you write an argument research paper, you are not simply stating what you believe. You are making a case — presenting evidence, acknowledging counterarguments, and reaching a conclusion that a reasonable reader could accept or at least take seriously.

Example 6.6 — Argument Questions
What should be done? Which approach is better?
  • Q1Should public libraries be required to provide internet access to patrons without digital devices of their own?
  • Q2Is mandatory minimum sentencing an effective deterrent to violent crime, and what does the evidence suggest about its impact on incarceration rates?
  • Q3Does the evidence support expanding Medicaid coverage for dental care, and what would be the likely fiscal and health outcomes?
  • Q4Is peer tutoring or instructor-led supplemental instruction more effective in improving outcomes in introductory college mathematics?
Argument questions often contain words like should, would, better, effective, most, or ought. They push toward a conclusion.
Core Principle

Argument research draws on all types of evidence — descriptive data, analytical findings, expert opinion, case studies, policy analysis, and ethical reasoning. The key is that the evidence must be weighed, not just reported. Argument papers do not simply collect facts. They evaluate facts in service of a claim.

In practice, research questions often combine elements of description, analysis, and argument. You may begin by describing a problem (what is happening), proceed to analyze it (why it is happening), and then argue for a solution or conclusion (what should be done). Many of the best research papers do exactly this — they move through all three registers.

Section 6.7

Researchable and Unresearchable Questions

One of the most practical skills in research planning is learning to recognize whether a question is actually researchable — and if not, how to revise it so that it becomes researchable.

A Researchable QuestionAn Unresearchable Question (as written)
Has an answer that could be found through evidenceAsks about things that cannot be empirically investigated
Points toward source types that exist and can be accessedIs so vague that no specific evidence applies
Is specific enough that relevant evidence can be identifiedIs so broad that no research project could address it
Is not already definitively settledIs settled beyond reasonable dispute
Is not purely a matter of personal preference or valuesAsks purely about personal values with no empirical component
Unresearchable QuestionWhy It FailsRevised Researchable Question
What is the meaning of life?Philosophical question with no empirical answerHow do adults in late life describe the sources of meaning that contribute to their sense of well-being?
Was Shakespeare a great writer?Aesthetic judgment questionHow have critical assessments of Shakespeare's language changed across three centuries of literary scholarship?
Everything about climate changeToo broadHow have coastal municipalities in Florida adapted building codes in response to projected sea-level rise?
How do I become happy?Too personal and non-empiricalWhat psychological interventions show the strongest evidence of improving subjective well-being in adults with depression?
Did the Civil War happen?Already settled — not in disputeTo what extent did economic motivations outweigh states' rights arguments in Confederate secession documents?
The Revision Strategy

When you find that your question is unresearchable, ask: Can I make it more specific? Can I replace a values-only question with an empirical one? Can I ask about what the evidence shows rather than what is ultimately true or right? Can I narrow the scope to a particular population, time period, or context? Can I ask about human behavior, policy, practice, or history rather than metaphysical truth?

Most unresearchable questions can be revised into researchable ones. The goal is not to avoid difficult subjects. It is to frame difficult subjects in ways that evidence can address.

Section 6.8

The MGU Research Question Model

The MGU Research Question Model gives you a structured framework for developing, testing, and refining your research question before you begin searching for sources. Working through the model forces you to be specific about what you want to find out, why it matters, how you will look for it, and whether your question is actually manageable.

Use the model each time you begin a research project.

The MGU Research Question Model — Applied Example
Topic.
What broad subject area does your research fall within? (One or two words.)
Mental health services / Community college students
Focused Issue.
What specific problem, debate, phenomenon, or question exists within that topic? What is the particular corner of the topic you are actually interested in?
Barriers that prevent first-generation community college students from using campus mental health services, even when services are free.
Question.
What is your research question, stated as a clear interrogative sentence? Be specific. Name the population, context, time period, or place if relevant.
What barriers — financial, cultural, informational, or logistical — prevent first-generation community college students from accessing campus mental health services?
Purpose.
What kind of research question is this? (Descriptive, analytical, or argument?) What do you want to accomplish?
This is primarily an analytical question. I want to identify and explain the factors that reduce service utilization, with the eventual goal of suggesting policy improvements.
Scope.
How large or small is this project? What are its limits? What time period, geography, or population does it include — and what does it exclude?
I will focus on community college students in the United States, with particular attention to research published in the last ten years. I will not address four-year universities or international contexts in this paper.
Evidence Needed.
What kinds of sources will help me answer this question? What types of evidence do I need?
Peer-reviewed research on barriers to mental health service use, reports from community college associations, data on service utilization rates by demographics, and possibly first-person accounts from student surveys.

Applying the Model: A Second Full Example

Example 6.8 — Solitary Confinement Research Plan
From topic to complete question plan
  • TopicCriminal justice / solitary confinement
  • IssueThe long-term psychological effects of solitary confinement on incarcerated individuals, and how those effects have been documented in medical and legal research.
  • QuestionWhat does research published between 2010 and 2024 show about the long-term psychological effects of solitary confinement on incarcerated adults in the United States?
  • PurposePrimarily descriptive and analytical. I want to describe what researchers have found and analyze the quality and consistency of that evidence. I am not yet making a policy argument, though my findings could support one.
  • ScopeAdult incarceration in the United States. Peer-reviewed research and major government or legal reports from the past fifteen years. Not covering juvenile detention or international systems.
  • EvidencePeer-reviewed psychological and psychiatric research, legal briefs and court decisions, reports from human rights organizations, and government data on solitary confinement use.
Using the Model to Test Your Question

Once you have filled in the model, ask yourself: Is my question specific enough to guide searching? Is my scope realistic for the assignment I have? Can I actually find the types of evidence I need? Does my question ask something that is genuinely investigable? If someone asked "so what?" about my question, could I answer them?

If any answer is "no" or "I'm not sure," revise before searching. A few minutes spent improving your question saves hours of misdirected research.

Chapter Summary, Review, and Practice

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 6.1A topic is a territory; a research question is a destination. Topics orient you. Questions drive you. Researchers move through a spectrum from general topic to focused subtopic to issue to question to working claim.
  • Sec 6.2A focused issue is a specific problem, debate, or phenomenon within a broader topic. Locating one requires asking what specifically interests you and looking for specificity — a time, place, population, or debate.
  • Sec 6.3A problem worth investigating is not already settled, is not purely a matter of opinion, connects to real consequences, has accessible evidence, and is sized appropriately for the assignment.
  • Sec 6.4Descriptive questions ask what exists or what happens. They require careful observation and systematic evidence but do not explain causes or argue for action on their own.
  • Sec 6.5Analytical questions ask why or how. They require reasoning that connects evidence to explanation of causes, mechanisms, or consequences.
  • Sec 6.6Argument questions ask what should be done or which approach is better. They are evaluative and require weighing evidence to reach a defensible, reasoned position — not simply stating an opinion.
  • Sec 6.7Researchable questions point toward accessible evidence and are neither too vague, too broad, already settled, nor purely values-based. Most unresearchable questions can be revised by adding specificity or reframing toward an empirical angle.
  • Sec 6.8The MGU Research Question Model — topic, focused issue, question, purpose, scope, evidence needed — provides a structured way to develop and test a research question before searching begins.

Key Terms

topic
A broad subject area that defines a general territory of inquiry, useful for orientation but requiring narrowing before research can begin productively.
focused issue
A specific problem, debate, phenomenon, or question within a broader topic, with a manageable scope and a pointer toward particular evidence.
research question
A complete interrogative sentence specifying what a researcher wants to find out — specific, investigable, connected to accessible evidence, and matched in scope to the project.
descriptive question
A research question asking what exists, what happens, or what something looks like; seeks to accurately characterize a phenomenon, population, or pattern.
analytical question
A research question asking why something happens or how it works; seeks to explain causes, mechanisms, consequences, or relationships.
argument question
A research question asking what should be done or which conclusion the evidence supports; evaluative and requires a reasoned, evidence-based position.
researchable question
A question addressable through evidence, specific enough to guide searching, not already settled, and not purely a matter of personal values.
working claim
A preliminary, tentative answer to a research question, stated as a declarative sentence that directs research without foreclosing revision.
MGU Research Question Model
A structured planning tool of six components — topic, focused issue, question, purpose, scope, evidence needed — used to develop and test research questions.
scope
The defined limits of a research project — time period, geographic context, population, and what is included or excluded.
purpose
The research intent of a question — whether it aims to describe, analyze, or argue.

Research Notebook

Sample Research Notebook Entry — Developing a Question

Assignment: Research paper for SOC 201, 8–10 pages, due in three weeks. Requires five scholarly sources.

Starting topic: I'm interested in homelessness, after a service-learning project at a local shelter where I noticed many residents were veterans.

Background reading note: The MGU Encyclopedia entry on veteran homelessness mentions PTSD, unemployment, lack of affordable housing, and gaps in VA access — plus a federal initiative called HUD-VASH offering housing vouchers.

Working question: How effective has the HUD-VASH housing voucher program been in reducing chronic veteran homelessness in the United States between 2008 and 2023?

Next step: Search MGU Library for overview articles and government reports, starting with the MGU Encyclopedia.

Misinformation Alert

Treating a Google Search as a Research Question Test

One of the most common traps in the question-development stage: students Google their topic, see thousands of results, and conclude their question must be fine because "there's a lot of information about it." The number of search results is not evidence that your question is good — it is evidence that your topic is popular. Test your question against the MGU Research Question Model instead, and ask whether you could recognize a source that directly answers it. The goal is not a question that produces millions of results. The goal is a question that produces the right results.

Citation Check

Recording a Research Methodology Source

When using a research methods textbook or library guide, record: author or institutional author, full title, edition, publisher, year, page numbers, and URL with access date if online.

MLA example: Booth, Wayne C., et al. The Craft of Research. 4th ed., University of Chicago Press, 2016.

APA example: Booth, W. C., Colomb, G. G., Williams, J. M., Bizup, J., & FitzGerald, W. T. (2016). The craft of research (4th ed.). University of Chicago Press.

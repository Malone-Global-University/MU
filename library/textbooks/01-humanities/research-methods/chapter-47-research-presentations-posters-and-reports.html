Chapter 47 — Front Matter

# Chapter 47: Research Presentations, Posters, and Reports

Research Skill Focus

This chapter teaches students how to translate completed research into polished public products. The core skill is audience-centered presentation: adapting your research findings, evidence, and conclusions to fit different formats, contexts, time limits, and viewer expectations. Students who master this skill can communicate what they have learned — not just in papers, but in oral presentations, slide decks, research posters, executive summaries, and formal reports.

Why This Matters

Research that stays private accomplishes very little. The entire purpose of inquiry — asking questions, finding sources, evaluating evidence, organizing notes, synthesizing findings — is to produce understanding that can be shared, tested, and used.

But sharing research well is its own skill. A student who produces excellent findings can still fail to communicate them effectively if the presentation is disorganized, the slides are unreadable, the poster buries the most important conclusion, or the oral delivery loses the audience in technical language.

Presentation skills matter beyond the classroom. In professional environments, the person who can explain research clearly — to a committee, an executive, a funding panel, or a public audience — is the person whose work gets acted on. In civic life, citizens who can present evidence persuasively and honestly contribute more effectively to public decisions. In academic careers, scholars who cannot present their work at conferences, in seminars, or in grant pitches find their research ignored.

This chapter treats presentation not as performance but as responsibility. You have done the work. Now you owe your audience the clearest, most honest, most useful account of what you found.

The Central Principle

Do not merely find information. Earn trust in information. Earning trust in a presentation means showing your evidence, citing your sources, acknowledging limitations, and letting your audience assess your conclusions — not just accepting them on your say-so.

Section 47.1

Research Beyond the Paper

The research paper is the most familiar product of academic inquiry, but it is far from the only one. Research findings can be communicated in many formats, and choosing the right format depends on the audience, the setting, the time available, and the purpose of the communication.

Different audiences need different things. A reader of a journal article can pause, reread, look up unfamiliar terms, and return to the text days later. A listener at an oral presentation gets one pass through the material, in real time, without the ability to pause. A viewer reading a poster at a conference reception has thirty seconds, is standing up, and may be holding a drink. An executive reading a summary report has fifteen minutes and wants the conclusion first.

No single format serves all audiences equally. Skilled researchers learn to translate their findings — the same evidence, the same conclusions, the same intellectual honesty — into whatever format the situation requires.

This chapter focuses on the five formats most common in academic and early professional settings: oral presentations, slide presentations, research posters, reports, and executive summaries.

The Transfer Problem

The most common mistake students make when moving from a research paper to a presentation is trying to transfer the paper directly. They read from the paper, reproduce the paper's sentences on slides, or organize a poster exactly as they organized the essay. This fails because written and spoken language work differently. A sentence that reads beautifully on the page can be impossible to follow when spoken aloud. Effective presentation requires translation, not transcription. You must re-understand your material from your audience's point of view and rebuild it in the new format from scratch.

The Honesty Obligation Does Not Change

Regardless of format, the ethical obligations of research remain constant: cite your sources, present evidence accurately, do not overstate your conclusions, acknowledge limitations and uncertainty, and give credit to other researchers whose work you used. A presentation that overstates findings, omits inconvenient evidence, or drops citations to seem more confident is still academically dishonest — even if no one challenges you in the moment.

Section 47.2

Oral Research Presentations

An oral research presentation is a spoken delivery of your research findings to a live audience. It may be accompanied by slides, a poster, handouts, or no visual aids at all. It is the most common research presentation format in academic settings and one of the most important in professional life.

Three features distinguish oral presentations from written papers. Time constraint: every spoken word takes time, and a 10-minute presentation at a typical speaking pace allows for only about 1,300 words — far less than even a short paper. Single pass: listeners cannot reread, so clarity, repetition of key ideas, and explicit signposting matter far more in speech than in writing. Presence and relationship: a speaker is present with the audience, which creates opportunities for connection but also the obligation to notice and adapt when an audience disengages.

Planning the Presentation

Before building any slides or notes, answer these questions: Who is my audience, and what do they know and care about? What is the one thing I most want them to understand if they forget everything else? What is my time limit, and how many points can I make clearly within it? What evidence — findings, data points, quotations — do I need to show? What format is expected, including whether there will be questions, a panel, or a handout?

Example 47.2 — Opening an Oral Presentation
A clear preview in a single opening statement
  • Opening "My presentation today examines how local news coverage of the 2022 drought differed across rural and urban California newspapers. I'll cover my research question, my methods, my key findings, and what they suggest about regional information gaps."This single opening statement names the topic, signals the structure to come, and gives the audience a map for the next ten minutes.
Principle: Tell the audience where you are taking them before you take them there.
The One-Sentence Test

Before finalizing your presentation, complete this sentence: "My research shows that _____________, which matters because _____________."

If you cannot complete this sentence clearly and specifically, your presentation is not yet focused enough. Every element of the talk should serve that sentence.

Delivering the Presentation

Delivery is communication, not performance. Make eye contact with different parts of the room rather than reading notes or slides. Slow your pace — most nervous speakers talk too fast — and pause after important points so the audience can absorb them. Vary your volume and pitch slightly to avoid a monotone delivery. Stand composed but not frozen, and avoid pacing or fidgeting. Practice aloud, not silently, at least twice before delivering, and time yourself. Some nervousness is normal and even sharpens focus; take a breath before you begin and remember the audience generally wants you to succeed.

Handling Questions

Most academic presentations include a question period. This is not an attack; it is an opportunity to clarify and deepen the conversation. Listen to the full question before answering. Repeat or paraphrase complex questions to confirm understanding and let the room hear it. Answer honestly — "that's outside the scope of this project, but it's a good question for further research" is a perfectly acceptable response. Keep answers brief, and do not become defensive if a finding is challenged; consider the challenge seriously.

Section 47.3

Slide Presentations

Slides are the most widely used visual support for oral presentations. They are so familiar — PowerPoint, Google Slides, Keynote — that students often assume they know how to use them. But most slide presentations have serious problems: too much text, unreadable fonts, irrelevant images, and a design that competes with rather than supports the speaker. Slides should serve the presentation, not replace it.

Slides exist to do things speech cannot do easily: display data, charts, and graphs; show images, maps, or photographs; highlight key terms the audience needs to remember; provide a visual roadmap of the structure; and display a quotation that needs to be read carefully. Slides are not teleprompters, outlines, or the paper projected onto a screen.

The One-Idea Rule

Each slide should communicate one idea. If a slide contains three separate points, it should be three slides. When a slide contains too much information, the audience reads the slide instead of listening to the speaker — the most common and most damaging mistake in slide design. Ask of every slide: What is the single thing I want the audience to understand from this slide?

Slide Text

Use as little text as possible: no more than six lines per slide, key phrases rather than full sentences unless quoting, body text at least 24pt, headlines at least 32–36pt, and high contrast between text and background.

Example 47.3 — Compressing a Finding for a Slide
From paragraph to slide
  • Too Long "The study found that rural newspapers published significantly fewer drought-related articles per month than urban newspapers, averaging 1.4 articles compared to 8.2, a difference that persisted across all six months studied."This is a complete sentence — accurate, but too dense for a slide.
  • Slide Version Rural vs. Urban Drought Coverage — Rural: avg. 1.4 articles/month — Urban: avg. 8.2 articles/monthThe slide carries the key numbers; the speaker supplies the context and significance aloud.
Principle: The slide shows; the speaker explains.

Data Slides and Images

Label every axis, column, and legend item clearly, and describe in words what a chart shows rather than assuming the audience can read it unaided. Highlight the bar, line, or cell that matters most, and always credit the data source — even in a presentation. Simplify complicated charts rather than presenting twelve variables in thirty seconds. Images should be purposeful, not decorative: a photograph of a drought-cracked lakebed in a presentation about drought coverage illustrates a point; a stock photo of a newspaper on a desk does not. Credit every image, map, and graphic.

Section 47.4

Research Posters

A research poster is a large, single-panel visual display of a research project. Posters are most common in academic conferences, symposia, library research showcases, and undergraduate research fairs. Unlike oral presentations, posters must communicate effectively without a speaker present — or with a speaker available for brief, informal conversation.

Designing a good poster is harder than designing a good slide deck. It requires compressing a full research project into a space about the size of a large bulletin board, readable from two to three feet away and understandable in thirty seconds to two minutes.

Most academic research posters are 36 × 48 inches, in portrait or landscape orientation; check the requirements for your specific event, as dimensions vary. Content is organized into three or four columns, and the reader moves left to right, top to bottom, through each column.

SectionContentApproximate Space
TitleProject title, your name, institution, courseTop banner, full width
Abstract150–200 word summary of the full projectTop left or integrated
IntroductionResearch question, why it matters, key backgroundFirst column
MethodsHow you conducted the researchSecond column (top)
Results / FindingsWhat you found, with data and visualsSecond column (bottom), third column (top)
Discussion / AnalysisWhat the findings meanThird column
ConclusionMain takeaway, limitations, future questionsThird or fourth column
ReferencesKey sources in proper citation formatBottom of poster
AcknowledgmentsOptional: thank advisors, funders, colleaguesBottom corner
Poster Text Limits

The entire poster should contain no more than 500–800 words of body text. Bullet points and short paragraphs are better than long prose. Title: 72–96pt bold. Section headers: 36–48pt bold. Body text: 24–28pt. Captions: 18–22pt. Anything smaller than 18pt is too small to read comfortably at poster distance.

Visuals are essential — a poster that is all text is harder to read and less likely to attract viewers. Effective visuals include charts and graphs of data findings, photographs of field sites or evidence, maps, diagrams of processes, tables comparing sources or methods, timelines, and infographic elements. Every visual needs a title, labels, and a source credit.

Color and Design

Use no more than three colors. Keep high contrast between text and background. White space is your friend — a cluttered poster is harder to read. Use consistent fonts and header hierarchy throughout, and align all elements to an invisible grid.

Standing with Your Poster

At most poster sessions, the researcher stands beside the poster for a set period — typically one to two hours — and talks with viewers who stop to look. Prepare a one-minute verbal summary, often called an "elevator pitch," covering your research question, your main finding, and why it matters. Then listen to viewers' questions and respond conversationally. This informal quality is one of a poster session's greatest advantages: you can adjust your explanation in real time based on the viewer's interest and background.

Poster Session Etiquette

Arrive early and set up before the session begins. Be present at your poster for the full session rather than wandering off. Engage with viewers who stop, even briefly. Have business cards or handouts available if the venue permits. Listen respectfully to feedback, and do not become defensive.

Section 47.5

Reports and Executive Summaries

Not all research communication happens in front of an audience. Many research products are written documents designed to be read independently, often by readers who want conclusions quickly and evidence available on demand. Two of the most important formats in this category are formal reports and executive summaries.

FeatureAcademic PaperFormal Report
Primary audienceScholars, instructorsProfessionals, decision-makers
PurposeContribute to knowledgeInform a decision or action
OrganizationArgument-drivenModular, scannable
ToneDisciplinary, may be technicalProfessional, often less jargon
Use of visualsModerateHigh (charts, tables, figures)
Front matterAbstractExecutive summary, table of contents

Standard Report Structure

Title page: report title, author name(s), organization, date, and who it was prepared for. Executive summary: a self-contained overview of the entire report. Table of contents: for reports longer than five pages. Introduction: research question or problem, background, scope, and methods. Findings: the main body, organized by topic or theme rather than narrative order, with data in labeled tables and figures. Discussion / analysis: what the findings mean, including patterns and unexpected results. Conclusions and recommendations: a clear statement of conclusions and any recommended actions. References / works cited: all sources in the required citation style. Appendices: supporting material that would interrupt the flow of the main report, such as raw data or full questionnaires.

Example 47.5 — Report Headers
Descriptive headers help scanning readers navigate
  • Weak Section 3Tells the reader nothing about what is inside.
  • Strong 3. Drought Coverage Frequency by Newspaper TypeTells a scanning reader exactly what to expect before they read a single sentence.
Principle: Readers of reports rarely read start to finish; they scan for the section they need.

Reports should be direct, professional but accessible, precise, and objective in presentation — even when the report includes a recommendation, evidence should be presented fairly before conclusions are drawn. Recommendations should be specific ("regional newspapers should assign a dedicated environment reporter," not "newspapers should do better"), grounded in the evidence, realistic about constraints, and numbered for easy reference.

Executive Summaries

An executive summary is a short, self-contained document that summarizes the most important information in a longer report, designed for readers who may not read the full report or who need the main points before deciding whether to. Executive summaries differ from abstracts: an abstract is typically 150–300 words and mostly descriptive; an executive summary is typically one to two pages and evaluative, including findings, analysis, and often recommendations.

SectionContent
Problem or questionOne paragraph: what problem this research addressed and why it matters
MethodsOne to two sentences on how the research was conducted
Key findingsTwo to four bullet points or short paragraphs, stated clearly and specifically
AnalysisOne paragraph on what the findings mean
Conclusions and recommendationsOne to two paragraphs or a bulleted list
Note on the full reportA brief statement directing readers to the full report for detailed evidence and methodology
Write the Executive Summary Last

Write the executive summary after completing the full report — it is far easier to summarize a document you have already written than to predict what one will contain. Then read the summary as a standalone document. It should make complete sense to a reader who has not seen the full report. If it requires that knowledge to be understood, revise it.

Section 47.6

Visual Evidence and Captions

Every visual element in a presentation, poster, or report — every chart, graph, photograph, map, table, or diagram — must be labeled with a figure or table number and a descriptive title, accompanied by a clear caption, and credited to its source. These requirements apply regardless of format and are not optional even in informal classroom presentations.

A caption does two things: it tells the viewer what to look at, and it tells the viewer what to conclude. An uncaptioned image leaves both decisions to the viewer, which means different viewers may understand the visual differently.

Example 47.6 — Captioning a Photograph
Direction, context, and credibility in one caption
  • Without Caption A photograph of dried, cracked earth, shown with no surrounding text.
  • With Caption Figure 3. The bed of Lake Oroville, California, in September 2021, showing drought conditions documented in regional news coverage. Source: California Department of Water Resources, 2021.The caption directs attention, provides date and place, and establishes source credibility.
Principle: A caption directs attention, supplies context, and establishes credibility — all at once.

Captions should include a figure or table number, a descriptive title, any necessary context such as date or location, and a source credit. By convention in most academic formats, captions for figures and images appear below the visual, while captions for tables appear above the table.

Choosing Visuals Honestly

Not every visual tells the truth clearly. Dishonest visual choices include cropping a photograph to remove context, using a scale that exaggerates differences, choosing a chart type that obscures rather than reveals, and displaying data from one time period to imply a general trend. Ask of every visual: Does this accurately represent what the data actually shows? When data is complex or uncertain, the visual should reflect that complexity — a graph with clear error bars is more honest than one with bare point estimates.

When displaying a visual in an oral presentation, do not just show it and move on. Tell the audience what they are looking at, what it shows, and why it matters. For example: "This graph shows the average number of drought-related articles published per month by rural and urban newspapers between January and June 2022. The blue bars represent rural papers; the orange bars represent urban papers. What you can see immediately is that urban papers published more than five times as many articles in every month of the study. This gap is consistent — it doesn't close or narrow at any point during the drought, even when conditions worsened." That explanation takes about twenty-five seconds and ensures every viewer understands what they are seeing.

Section 47.7

Audience and Time Limits

Every research presentation is shaped by two constraints that researchers must plan around deliberately: who the audience is, and how much time is available.

Audience analysis means thinking carefully, before you design anything, about what your audience already knows, what they care about, what they need to be able to do after the presentation, and what setting they expect. An expert audience can handle technical vocabulary and assumed background; a general audience needs plain language, defined terms, and more context. Expert audiences often care about methods and evidence quality; non-specialist audiences often care more about implications and real-world significance. Matching your presentation to your audience is not dumbing down your research — it is respecting your audience enough to communicate with them rather than over them.

Time LimitWhat You Have Time For
5 minutesResearch question (30 sec.), one to two key findings (3 min.), conclusion (1 min.). No methods or extended background.
10 minutesQuestion and context (2 min.), brief methods (1 min.), two to three key findings (5 min.), conclusion and limitations (2 min.).
15–20 minutesFull introduction and background (3–4 min.), methods (2 min.), four to five key findings (7–9 min.), analysis (2 min.), conclusion (2 min.).
30+ minutesDeeper engagement with evidence, extended discussion of methods, engagement with counterarguments, richer audience interaction.
Time Limits Are Real

Going over time is rude, disruptive, and signals poor preparation. Always time yourself during practice. If your practice run is three minutes over the limit, cut content — do not plan to speak faster. Faster speech is harder to follow, not a solution to overprepared content.

Cutting Content Without Losing Substance

When time is limited, cut in this order: redundancy first (keep the better of two similar examples), then background (audiences need less context than speakers often assume), then secondary findings (keep what most directly answers your research question). Never cut your conclusion — the audience deserves to know what your research found. Never cut your limitations — honesty about what your research cannot establish is a mark of intellectual integrity.

Source Check

Evaluating a Research Presentation as a Source. Presentations and posters are sometimes cited as sources, particularly conference presentations for which no published paper yet exists.

Example source: A poster presented at a national environmental conference, authored by a doctoral student, presenting preliminary findings from a dissertation study.

Apply the MGU Source Evaluation Model

Relevance: Does the presentation address your research question, or only provide general background?

Authority: Who is the presenter, and what are their credentials? Was the conference peer-reviewed or juried, or open to anyone? A poster accepted through competitive selection carries more authority than one presented at an open session.

Evidence: Are methods described? Are claims supported with data? Is uncertainty acknowledged?

Accuracy: Are the claims consistent with what other sources say? Are there signs of error, exaggeration, or overstated conclusions?

Purpose: Was this presentation given to share preliminary findings, to advocate for a policy, or to present completed research?

Currency: When was the presentation given, and how recent is the data?

Context: Is this preliminary or final work? Has it since been published in peer-reviewed form?

Conclusion: Presentations can be valuable sources, particularly for recent or emerging research. But they carry less authority than peer-reviewed publications, often represent preliminary work, and should be treated with appropriate caution. Always note in your citation that this was a conference presentation or poster, not a published paper.

Misinformation Alert

Presentations Can Mislead Even Without Lying

Some of the most effective misinformation is delivered through presentations — because presentations feel authoritative. A speaker at a podium, a beautifully designed poster, a polished slide deck: these formats signal expertise and credibility, even when the content does not deserve it.

Watch for several specific risks. Cherry-picked visuals show only the data that supports the conclusion while omitting data that contradicts it. Impressive but irrelevant statistics — "over a thousand studies have examined this topic" — sound authoritative but say nothing about what those studies actually found. Correlational claims presented as causal slide past an important distinction; a chart showing that counties with more recycling programs have lower disease rates does not show that recycling causes the health outcome. Missing error bars and uncertainty make a graph look more decisive than the underlying data warrants. Unattributed claims such as "studies show" or "experts agree" without specific citations are a significant warning sign. And the authority of design — a professionally polished poster or deck — looks credible regardless of the quality of the underlying research.

Apply the MGU Misinformation Check

Who made it? What is the claim? What evidence is offered? What is missing? Can another reliable source confirm it? Is the emotional reaction stronger than the evidence? Has the source been altered, misrepresented, or taken out of context?

Key Rule for All Formats

If you are uncertain whether a conference presentation has since been published as a journal article, check before citing. A peer-reviewed article carries more authority than a conference presentation, and citing the published version, when one exists, is preferable.

Research Checklist

Chapter 47 — Research Checklist
A quick self-check before any research product leaves your hands
  • I have chosen a format suited to my audience, setting, and time limit.
  • I have translated my research into the new format rather than transcribing the paper directly.
  • Every claim I make is supported by evidence I can point to.
  • Every visual is labeled, captioned, and credited to its source.
  • I have acknowledged limitations and uncertainty honestly.
  • I have not overstated my conclusions to seem more persuasive.
  • My sources are cited in the format appropriate to the product.
  • I have practiced or reviewed the final product before sharing it.

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 47.1Research findings can be communicated through many formats, and no single format serves all audiences equally. Effective presentation requires translating findings, not transcribing the paper, while keeping the same ethical obligations intact.
  • Sec 47.2Oral presentations are spoken deliveries of research findings that require careful structure, clear delivery, and honest acknowledgment of evidence quality and limitations within a fixed time limit.
  • Sec 47.3Slide presentations support oral delivery by displaying what words cannot easily convey. Slides should follow the one-idea rule, use minimal text, and credit every visual source.
  • Sec 47.4Research posters compress a complete project into a large, readable, navigable display, requiring short scannable text, clear section headers, labeled visuals, and a one-minute spoken summary.
  • Sec 47.5Formal reports and executive summaries are structured written documents for professional audiences. Executive summaries distill an entire report into a self-contained overview, written last but read first.
  • Sec 47.6Every visual needs a label, a caption, and a source credit. Captions direct attention and convey meaning; dishonest visual choices can mislead even when underlying data is accurate.
  • Sec 47.7Audience analysis and time limits shape every presentation decision. When content must be cut, cut redundancy and background first, and never cut the conclusion or the limitations.
  • Sec 47.8A presentation checklist covering research content, source use, format-specific design, and oral delivery helps ensure that any research product meets the standards of honest, accountable communication.

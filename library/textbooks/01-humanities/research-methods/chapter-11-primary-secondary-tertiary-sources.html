Chapter 11 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

One of the most common mistakes student researchers make is treating all sources as interchangeable. A newspaper article, a scholarly journal article, a government report, a novel, a photograph, an encyclopedia entry, and a social media post are all "sources" in the broadest sense. But they are not the same kind of source, and they are not equally useful for every research question.

Professional researchers in every field have developed a vocabulary for thinking about source categories. The most fundamental distinction is the one between primary sources, secondary sources, and tertiary sources. These categories describe the relationship between a source and the events, data, or ideas the source addresses. They are not fixed labels that belong permanently to a document. As you will learn in this chapter, the same source can function as a primary source for one research question and a secondary source for another.

This chapter will teach you the three major source categories, explain how the same source can shift function depending on the question, show you examples from multiple academic disciplines, and help you begin thinking about how to build a source mix appropriate for your own research projects.

Research Skill Focus

Understanding and correctly identifying primary, secondary, and tertiary sources so that you can select the right type of evidence for each research question, use each source category with appropriate expectations, and build a well-balanced source mix for any research project.

Why This Matters

Learning to identify and use source categories correctly gives you three advantages. First, it helps you find the right evidence — a question about firsthand experience requires firsthand records, not a polished interpretation of them. Second, it helps you read sources with appropriate expectations — a primary source is not automatically more trustworthy than a secondary one, and each type must be read on its own terms. Third, it helps you build a research project with the right balance of evidence: tertiary sources for orientation, primary sources for evidentiary grounding, and secondary sources for understanding how others have interpreted that evidence.

Sections 11.1 – 11.4
The Three Source Categories — Definitions, Forms, and Critical Reading
Position in the Chain · Primary · Secondary · Tertiary
Section 11.1

What Source Categories Mean

The words primary, secondary, and tertiary describe the position of a source in relation to the original event, data, idea, or creative work being studied. They describe where the source sits in a chain from original experience to interpreted knowledge.

Think of it this way. Imagine a fire breaks out in a city in 1912. Several things happen:

  • A survivor writes a letter to her sister describing what she saw, heard, and felt during the fire. That letter is a primary source — a direct record created by someone who experienced the event.
  • Ten years later, a historian reads dozens of such letters, along with fire department records, newspaper accounts, and insurance documents, and writes a book arguing that the fire exposed serious failures in the city's building inspection system. That book is a secondary source — it analyzes and interprets the primary evidence.
  • Thirty years after that, an encyclopedia of American urban disasters includes a two-paragraph entry on the fire, drawing on the historian's book and several others. That encyclopedia entry is a tertiary source — it summarizes and compiles secondary analyses for quick reference.

Each level of the chain serves a different purpose. The primary source brings you closest to the original experience. The secondary source helps you understand what that experience meant and how it fits into a larger pattern. The tertiary source gives you a quick orientation so you can begin to understand the topic before you dive into primary and secondary material.

Categories Describe Relationships, Not Quality

These categories are not purely about the quality or trustworthiness of the source. They are about the source's relationship to the original event or data. A poorly written secondary source may be less useful than a carefully composed primary record. A well-organized tertiary source may be invaluable for background knowledge even though it would be inappropriate to cite as evidence in a research paper.

It is also important to understand from the beginning that these categories are question-dependent. The same document that functions as a primary source for one research question may function as a secondary source for another. This idea will be developed fully in section 11.5, but keep it in mind as you read through the basic definitions below.

Section 11.2

Primary Sources

A primary source is a record or artifact created at the time of an event or by someone with direct knowledge of or participation in that event. Primary sources are the raw material of research — the closest available evidence to what actually happened, was said, was made, was measured, or was experienced.

The defining feature of a primary source is directness. The source does not report on something that someone else observed or concluded. It is itself the observation, the expression, the measurement, the creation, or the document.

Primary Sources Across Disciplines

FieldTypical Primary Sources
History & HumanitiesDiaries, letters, speeches, interviews, oral histories, period photographs and maps, contemporary newspapers, government and legal records, literary texts, works of art, archaeological artifacts, census records
Social SciencesResearcher-collected survey data and interview transcripts, field notes, original datasets, policy documents, legislative records, court decisions, organizational records
Natural SciencesExperimental and observational data, lab notebooks, original research articles' Methods and Results sections, specimens and samples, sensor and satellite readings
Arts & Creative FieldsThe original painting, sculpture, photograph, or film; the original score or recording; the original manuscript or published text

Because primary sources are direct records, they require careful, contextual reading. A primary source does not automatically tell you what it means. A letter written by a soldier may tell you what that individual experienced and thought, but it may not accurately represent what most soldiers experienced. A newspaper from 1920 may tell you what one editor believed, but that belief may have been shaped by commercial interests, political pressure, or simple ignorance.

Questions to Ask of a Primary Source

Who created this source, and what was the creator's position, perspective, and purpose? When was it created — during the event, immediately afterward, or from memory years later? Why was it created, and was it intended to be private or public?

Who was the intended audience? What does it not say — what might have been left out, changed, or emphasized for a reason? And how was it preserved — who saved this document, and why? What was lost or destroyed?

In most research projects, primary sources are what anchor your claims to actual evidence. When you argue that something happened, was believed, was created, or was measured, you need the original record. Secondary and tertiary sources can help you understand and interpret that evidence, but they cannot replace it. At the same time, no researcher expects a student at the beginning of a project to have immediate access to every primary source. Part of learning research is learning how to locate, access, and use primary sources — and part of good research planning is identifying what primary sources exist for your question.

Section 11.3

Secondary Sources

A secondary source is a work that analyzes, interprets, evaluates, summarizes, or argues about primary sources. Secondary sources stand at one remove from the original event or data. Their creators have read, studied, examined, or tested the primary evidence and have drawn conclusions about what it means.

Secondary sources are characterized by interpretation. The author has done work — gathering primary evidence, analyzing patterns, comparing accounts, testing hypotheses, reviewing what others have argued — and is now presenting conclusions supported by that evidence.

Secondary Sources Across Disciplines

FieldTypical Secondary Sources
History & HumanitiesScholarly monographs, journal articles interpreting evidence, biographies, literary criticism, documentary films using expert interviews
Social SciencesResearch articles analyzing collected data, meta-analyses, policy analyses and evaluation reports, theoretical frameworks
Natural SciencesReview articles synthesizing many studies, papers reporting interpretation and discussion of results, advanced textbooks
General Public DiscourseNews feature articles, nonfiction books for general audiences, explanatory podcasts and documentaries

A common misconception is that primary sources are always the "real" evidence and secondary sources are somehow less legitimate. This is wrong. Secondary sources do something that primary sources cannot do by themselves: they analyze, contextualize, and interpret. A student researching the causes of World War I could read diplomatic telegrams and newspaper editorials from 1914, but without secondary analysis the student would struggle to understand the alliance system or the scholarly debate over relative responsibility among governments. Secondary sources also show you where the scholarly debate stands — where experts agree, where they disagree, what evidence is contested, and what questions remain open.

Reading Secondary Sources Critically

What is the main argument, and what claim is being made? What evidence supports it, and is that evidence primary and representative? What assumptions does the author take for granted? When was this written, and has the field moved forward since?

How does this source relate to other secondary sources — does it agree with, build on, or challenge other scholarly arguments? Strong researchers use secondary sources not just for information but to understand the shape of the scholarly conversation about their topic.

Section 11.4

Tertiary Sources

A tertiary source is a work that compiles, organizes, or summarizes primary and secondary sources. Tertiary sources stand two removes from the original event or data. Their purpose is orientation, definition, and efficient access to organized knowledge rather than original analysis.

Tertiary sources are characterized by compilation and synthesis for general access. Common examples include general and subject-specific encyclopedias, dictionaries and glossaries, bibliographies, almanacs and handbooks, indexes and abstracts, introductory textbooks, and summary databases designed for quick reference.

When Tertiary Sources Earn Their Place
Four uses for tertiary sources at the start of a project
  • VocabularyBefore you can search a database effectively, you need to know the terminology of your field. A subject encyclopedia or specialized dictionary gives you that vocabulary.
  • ContextA timeline, almanac, or encyclopedia article can orient you quickly in unfamiliar historical or scientific territory.
  • Further sourcesMany tertiary sources include bibliographies, recommended readings, or links to primary and secondary material that can launch your research effectively.
  • Quick factsWhen you need a date, a definition, a statistical figure, or a brief factual summary, a reliable tertiary source is often the fastest place to find it.
Principle: Use tertiary sources to get oriented — not as your main citeable evidence.
The Wikipedia Question

Wikipedia is a collaboratively edited, free-content tertiary source. It can be genuinely useful for initial orientation — finding key terms, getting a quick overview, identifying names and dates, and locating links to better sources. However, it is not an authoritative academic source, its accuracy is uneven across topics, and its articles can be edited by anyone. Use Wikipedia to get started, but follow its citations and references to find the original primary and secondary sources.

The same features that make tertiary sources useful for orientation make them inappropriate as the primary basis for a research paper. Because they summarize and compress, they inevitably leave out complexity, nuance, debate, and uncertainty. Most academic research assignments expect students to go beyond tertiary sources and engage with primary evidence and secondary analysis. An essay that cites only encyclopedias and textbooks is unlikely to demonstrate the level of engagement with evidence that academic research requires.

Section 11.5

Source Type Depends on the Question

One of the most important concepts in this chapter is that source categories are not fixed properties of documents. The same source can function as a primary, secondary, or tertiary source depending on the research question. This is not a trick or an exception. It reflects the fundamental logic of what source categories mean: they describe the relationship between the source and the research question — not a permanent identity built into the document.

Illustration 11.5-A
A Scholarly Book About World War II, Published in 1960
  • Question AResearching Allied supply chains and their effect on World War II outcomes — the book is a secondary source; it analyzes primary evidence about a historical event.
  • Question BResearching how American historians interpreted World War II in the early Cold War period — the same book becomes a primary source, since it is itself a document created in 1960 that reveals how a historian of that era thought about the war.
The book did not change. The research question changed the function the source performs.
Illustration 11.5-B
A Front-Page Newspaper Article From November 1918
  • Question AResearching public responses to the armistice in 1918 — the article is a primary source, recording a direct contemporary response.
  • Question BResearching how journalism has covered wars throughout American history — the article functions as primary source evidence about 1918 journalism itself.
  • Question CUsed only to confirm the date the armistice was announced — the article functions almost like a reference source, providing a specific fact for background purposes.
Illustration 11.5-C
An Introductory Psychology Textbook Published Recently
  • Default useFor most readers, the textbook is a tertiary source — a compilation of established knowledge organized for instructional purposes.
  • Question AResearching how psychological knowledge has been packaged for undergraduate education — the textbook becomes a primary source, a cultural artifact revealing how psychologists present their discipline to beginners.
  • Question BUsing the textbook's explanation of cognitive dissonance to understand the concept before reading original studies by Leon Festinger — the textbook functions as a tertiary source providing orientation.
Practical Implications

First, when your instructor tells you to use primary sources, ask yourself: primary relative to what question? Make sure you are using sources that give you direct evidence about the event, phenomenon, or experience your question addresses.

Second, do not discard a source simply because it seems like the "wrong type." A textbook chapter you use for background is still valuable even though you would not cite it as your main evidence.

Third, when evaluating sources, always think about the relationship between the source and your question. Ask not just "Is this a good source?" but "Is this source giving me direct evidence about what I am researching, or is it giving me someone else's interpretation of that evidence?"

Section 11.7

Strengths and Limits of Each Type

No source category is inherently superior. Each has genuine strengths and real limitations. Strong researchers understand both so that they can use each type appropriately.

Primary Sources

StrengthsLimits
Directness — brings you as close as possible to the original event, data, or experienceContext dependence — rarely interprets itself; easy to misread without surrounding context
Originality — the foundation against which secondary interpretations can be checkedPartiality — every primary source represents one perspective, never the whole story
Evidentiary weight — carries the strongest weight precisely because it is unfilteredSurvival bias — what has been preserved may not represent the full range of what happened
Discovery — direct work with primary material can reveal what secondary sources missedAccessibility & reliability — often held in restricted archives; proximity to an event does not guarantee truthful reporting

Secondary Sources

StrengthsLimits
Context and interpretation — explains what primary evidence means in a broader contextDistance from primary evidence — every layer of interpretation can introduce error or bias
Scholarly conversation — shows where experts agree, disagree, and what remains openCurrency — can become outdated as new evidence and frameworks emerge
Efficiency — has already located, read, and synthesized a wide range of primary evidenceBias and perspective — reflects the author's theoretical framework and disciplinary training
Quality control — peer review provides a meaningful, if imperfect, check on accuracyVariability in quality — popular nonfiction and advocacy-driven reports vary widely in rigor

Tertiary Sources

StrengthsLimits
Accessibility — designed to be read quickly and understood easilyLack of depth — compresses and simplifies, losing nuance and uncertainty
Breadth — an efficient overview of key terms, figures, and debatesCurrency — may not reflect recent discoveries or developments
Organization — systematic structure makes specific information easy to findVariable authority — not all tertiary sources are reviewed by field experts
Navigation — bibliographies and cross-references point you toward deeper sourcesInappropriate as evidence — citing it as your main support signals insufficient engagement
Section 11.8

Choosing the Right Source Mix

Most strong research projects draw on all three source categories. The challenge is knowing when to use each type and how to balance them appropriately. The right source mix depends on four factors:

  • The type of research question. A question asking what happened requires primary evidence. A question asking what the scholarly debate has concluded requires engagement with secondary sources. A question asking what a technical term means may be answered with a tertiary source.
  • The discipline and its conventions. History, literature, and law tend to rely heavily on primary sources. Sociology and psychology generate their own primary data. Sciences rely on original experimental data.
  • The level of the research project. An introductory essay may appropriately rely more heavily on secondary and tertiary sources. A senior thesis or graduate-level paper is expected to engage substantially with primary evidence and the full secondary literature.
  • The availability of sources. Not every primary source is accessible to every researcher. Part of good research planning is identifying what sources exist and what you can reasonably obtain.
Practical Guidance for Undergraduate Research

Use tertiary sources to get oriented at the beginning of your project — build vocabulary, establish basic facts, identify key figures and debates. Do not cite them as your main evidence.

Use secondary sources to understand the scholarly conversation — what experts have argued, where they agree and disagree, and what evidence and methods have been used.

Use primary sources to anchor your specific claims in direct evidence. If you argue that something happened, was believed, was created, or was measured, find the original record.

Build a balanced source list. If your works cited page contains only secondary sources, consider whether you have gone deep enough into the primary evidence. If it contains only primary sources, consider whether you have engaged adequately with existing scholarly analysis.

Working Through the MGU Source Evaluation Model
Evaluating a tertiary reference source
  • RelevanceIf your research question is about the nature and use of primary sources, this is highly relevant. If your question is about a specific historical event, this is a tertiary background source — useful for orientation but not for substantive evidence about the event itself.

Chapter Feature

Misinformation Alert

The Source Type Confusion: Why It Matters for Information Integrity

Understanding source categories is not just an academic exercise. Source type confusion contributes to the spread of misinformation in several important ways.

Pattern 1
Misrepresentation of Source Type

A common misinformation technique is to present a secondary or tertiary interpretation as if it were primary evidence. A commentator might say, "Studies show..." without specifying what studies, what kind, how they were conducted, or whether they are primary research data or secondary syntheses. When you cannot tell what kind of source is being referenced, you cannot evaluate the claim.

Pattern 2
Tertiary Sources Mistaken for Authoritative Evidence

Wikipedia, summary websites, and online reference tools are routinely shared as if they were authoritative primary or secondary sources. A tertiary source can give you a starting point. It cannot substitute for engagement with the original scholarly literature.

Pattern 3
Primary Sources Quoted Without Context

Misinformers sometimes quote directly from official documents, scientific papers, or historical records while stripping away the context that would allow readers to interpret the quotation correctly. A single sentence from a scientific paper can be made to seem to say something entirely different from what the paper as a whole argues. Primary sources need to be read in full context.

Pattern 4
Outdated Secondary Sources Treated as Current

Secondary sources become outdated as new evidence emerges and scholarly understanding develops. A study superseded by more recent research can still circulate as if it represented current knowledge. Always check when a secondary source was published and whether the field has moved.

The MGU Misinformation Check Applied to Source Type

Who made it, and what is the relationship between the creator and the event or data being described? What is the claim — firsthand information, interpreted evidence, or summarized organized knowledge? What evidence is offered, and is the source transparent about where its information comes from?

What is missing — is the source hiding its source type, presenting interpretation as if it were direct observation? Can another reliable source confirm it, and can you trace the claim back to the original primary evidence? Is the emotional reaction stronger than the evidence, or stronger than the framing of that evidence? Has the source been altered, misrepresented, or taken out of context — has a primary source been quoted selectively, or a secondary analysis misrepresented as primary data?

Style Note

Always check your instructor's required citation style (MLA, APA, Chicago) for the specific format. The examples above use a simplified format for illustration purposes. Chapters 41–43 cover citation styles in full detail.

A Key Rule on Secondhand Sources

When you cite a primary source that you found through a secondary or tertiary source (because you did not access the original directly), be honest about this. Many citation styles have formats for "cited in" or "quoted in" attributions. Using these formats tells your reader that you are relying on another researcher's transcription or quotation of the original — an important transparency note.

Chapter 11 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary, Review, and Practice

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 11.1Source categories describe the position of a source in relation to the original event, data, or idea being studied — not a fixed property of the document, but a relationship that depends on the research question.
  • Sec 11.2Primary sources are direct records created at the time of an event or by someone with firsthand knowledge. They anchor claims in actual evidence but require careful, contextual reading.
  • Sec 11.3Secondary sources analyze, interpret, and argue about primary evidence. They show where the scholarly conversation stands and help readers understand what primary evidence means in a broader context.
  • Sec 11.4Tertiary sources compile and summarize primary and secondary material for efficient access. They are most valuable for orientation at the start of a project, not as citeable evidence.
  • Sec 11.5The same source can function as primary, secondary, or tertiary depending on the research question — a fact illustrated through a 1960 history book, a 1918 newspaper article, and a psychology textbook.
  • Sec 11.6History, literature, sociology, biology, and political science each have their own characteristic primary, secondary, and tertiary sources, reflecting each discipline's conventions for evidence.
  • Sec 11.7Each source category carries genuine strengths and real limitations — directness and partiality for primary sources, interpretation and distance for secondary sources, accessibility and lack of depth for tertiary sources.
  • Sec 11.8A strong research project draws on all three source types in appropriate balance, guided by the type of question, disciplinary conventions, project level, and source availability — applying the MGU Source Evaluation Model to each.

Key Terms

primary source
A record or artifact created at the time of an event or by someone with direct knowledge of or participation in that event.
secondary source
A work that analyzes, interprets, evaluates, summarizes, or argues about primary sources.
tertiary source
A work that compiles, organizes, or summarizes primary and secondary sources for efficient general access.
directness
The defining feature of a primary source — the source is itself the observation, measurement, or document rather than a report about it.
interpretation
The analytical work performed by a secondary source's author in drawing conclusions from primary evidence.
question-dependence
The principle that a source's category is determined by its relationship to a specific research question, not by a fixed identity.
survival bias
The distortion that results because not all primary sources are preserved, often reflecting the choices of those in power or the accidents of history.
scholarly conversation
The ongoing exchange of arguments, evidence, and interpretation among researchers in a field, traceable through secondary sources.
source mix
The intentional balance of primary, secondary, and tertiary sources used in a research project.
MGU Source Evaluation Model
A seven-part framework — relevance, authority, evidence, accuracy, purpose, currency, context — for evaluating any source.

Review Questions

  1. In your own words, define the difference between a primary, secondary, and tertiary source. Give one example of each from the discipline you intend to study or are currently studying.
  2. Explain why source categories are "question-dependent." Use a specific example to show how the same source could function as a primary source for one research question and a secondary source for another.
  3. A classmate tells you that primary sources are always more trustworthy than secondary sources because they are closer to the original event. Do you agree or disagree? Explain your reasoning, addressing at least two strengths and two limitations of primary sources.
  4. What is the appropriate role of tertiary sources in academic research? Under what circumstances are they useful, and under what circumstances would relying on them be a weakness in a research project?
  5. Consider the following scenario: A student writing a paper on the development of social media as a communication platform in the 2010s finds an article from 2016 in a popular magazine discussing how Twitter changed political communication. The student also finds a scholarly article from 2022 analyzing how researchers have studied social media's political effects between 2008 and 2020. Which source is primary and which is secondary for this specific research question? Explain your reasoning.
  6. Why is it important to understand source categories before beginning a database search? How does knowing you are looking for primary versus secondary sources change how you search?
  7. Apply the MGU Source Evaluation Model to the following source: A Wikipedia article on the causes of World War I. Work through each criterion: relevance, authority, evidence, accuracy, purpose, currency, and context. What is your conclusion about how this source should be used?
  8. Choose a research topic you are working on or interested in. Describe one primary source, one secondary source, and one tertiary source that would be appropriate for that topic. Explain why each fits its category for your specific question.

Research Checklist

Chapter 11 — Checklist

Understanding Source Types
  • I can explain the difference between primary, secondary, and tertiary sources.
  • I understand that source categories describe the relationship between a source and a research question, not a fixed identity within the document.
  • I can explain why the same source might function as primary for one question and secondary for another.
Identifying Sources in My Project
  • I have identified which sources in my current project are primary, secondary, and tertiary.
  • I have verified that the sources I am calling "primary" actually provide direct evidence about the event, phenomenon, or experience my question addresses.
  • I have verified that sources I am calling "secondary" actually analyze or interpret primary evidence, rather than simply summarizing other secondary sources.
  • I am using tertiary sources only for background orientation, not as the main evidence in my paper.
Building a Balanced Source Mix
  • My source list includes primary evidence directly relevant to my research question.
  • My source list includes peer-reviewed or otherwise authoritative secondary analysis.
  • I have used tertiary sources to build background knowledge and identify further sources.
  • My source mix is appropriate for the assignment level and discipline conventions.
Evaluating and Citing Sources
  • I have evaluated each source using the MGU Source Evaluation Model: relevance, authority, evidence, accuracy, purpose, currency, and context.
  • I have recorded full bibliographic information for each source.
  • If I am citing a primary source that I found through a secondary source, I have noted this and know how to indicate it in my citation.

Chapter 21 — Front Matter

Research Skill Focus and Overview

This chapter teaches students how to combine and refine search terms using Boolean operators and advanced search techniques. Students will learn how AND, OR, and NOT shape search results; how phrase searching with quotation marks retrieves exact language; how truncation and wildcard symbols expand a search across word variations; how parentheses control the logic of complex searches; and how field, date, and file-type searches target specific parts of a record or database.

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to construct precise, efficient searches in academic databases, library catalogs, and search engines — and to understand why the same search terms produce different results depending on how they are structured.

Why This Matters

There is a common assumption among beginning researchers that searching is simple: type words into a box, press enter, and find what you need. This assumption explains why so many research projects stall early, return hundreds of irrelevant results, or miss exactly the sources that would matter most.

Search systems — whether library databases, catalog systems, or internet search engines — are not intuitive readers. They do not understand what you mean. They process what you type. A search system does not know that you are interested in the psychological effects of social media use on adolescents, not social media advertising strategies for businesses. It does not know that you want recent peer-reviewed studies, not blog posts from 2009. It does not know that when you type "mercury," you mean the planet, not the element, not the car brand, and not the Roman god.

Boolean operators and advanced search techniques give you the tools to communicate precisely with search systems. They let you tell the system exactly which concepts must appear together, which alternatives are acceptable, which terms should be excluded, and which words must appear as an exact phrase. Used well, they are the difference between searching and finding.

This matters practically. A researcher who understands Boolean logic can take a search from three thousand vaguely relevant results to forty highly relevant ones in a few minutes. A researcher who does not understand Boolean logic will spend hours reading abstracts of articles that have nothing to do with their question, or will conclude that "there is nothing written on this topic" when the problem was only that the search was too narrow or used the wrong operator.

Advanced search skills also transfer. Once you understand how Boolean operators work in one database, you can apply the same logic in almost any other database, catalog, or search engine. The specific symbols and interfaces vary, but the underlying logic is consistent. Learning it once serves you across every search system you will ever use.

Why Advanced Search Matters

Section 21.1

Why Advanced Search Matters

Every search you run in a database or catalog is interpreted by a search algorithm — a set of rules that determines which records match your query and how they are ranked or displayed. When you type terms into a simple search box, the algorithm makes decisions about how to interpret your query that you may not be aware of and cannot control.

The Problem with Default Searches

Most search systems, by default, apply some version of the following logic: find records that contain any of the words you typed, rank them by some measure of relevance (which may include popularity, recency, or commercial factors), and display the top results.

This means that if you search for:

Default Search Example
A vague query produces a vague set of results
social media adolescents mental health

The system may return results about social media marketing to adolescents, mental health apps for any age group, social media statistics from health surveys, news articles about teen depression, academic studies on elderly mental health, and dozens of other tangentially related topics — all because each result contains at least one of your search words somewhere.

You wanted a precise intersection of topics. The default search gave you a broad union of possibilities.

What Advanced Search Gives You

Advanced search techniques give you control over three fundamental search variables:

VariableWhat It Controls
InclusionWhich concepts must appear in every result you receive
ExpansionWhich alternative terms or word forms are acceptable substitutes for a concept
ExclusionWhich terms or concepts should eliminate a result from consideration

You also gain control over exact phrasing (requiring specific words to appear together in a specific order), word form (matching variations of a word without listing each one individually), search location (specifying that terms must appear in particular fields, such as title or subject heading, rather than anywhere in the record), temporal scope (restricting results to a date range), and format (restricting results to particular file types or publication formats).

These controls do not make searching more complicated. They make searching more efficient, more reliable, and more honest about what is and is not available on a topic.

The Search Equation

Think of an advanced search as a logical equation. Every search you build answers the question: "Show me records that satisfy these conditions." The Boolean operators AND, OR, and NOT are the connectors that define what those conditions are. Every other advanced technique — phrase searching, truncation, field searching — refines or extends those conditions. Understanding this underlying logic is what separates a researcher who knows how to build searches from one who only knows how to type words and hope.

Sections 21.2 – 21.4
AND, OR, and NOT — The Three Boolean Operators
Narrowing · Expanding · Excluding
Section 21.2

AND

AND is the most fundamental Boolean operator. When you connect two terms with AND, you are telling the search system: "Every result you show me must contain both of these terms." AND narrows your search. The more terms you connect with AND, the fewer results you will receive — because each additional AND requirement eliminates records that contain only some of your terms.

How AND Works

Imagine two sets of records: Set A contains all records with the word climate; Set B contains all records with the word migration. When you search climate AND migration, you receive only the records that belong to both sets simultaneously — the intersection. Records about climate that do not mention migration are excluded. Records about migration that do not mention climate are excluded. This is exactly what you want when your research question requires both concepts to be addressed together.

Visualizing AND

The logic of AND is often illustrated with overlapping circles. Imagine two circles: one labeled "climate" and one labeled "migration." The AND search retrieves only what falls in the area where both circles overlap.

AND in Practice

SearchWhat It Retrieves
climate AND migrationRecords discussing both climate and migration
adolescents AND social media AND anxietyRecords addressing all three topics together
diabetes AND diet AND preventionRecords that address diet-based prevention of diabetes
drone AND warfare AND ethicsRecords discussing the ethics of drone use in warfare
minimum wage AND employment AND small businessRecords connecting minimum wage policy, employment effects, and small businesses

Notice how each additional AND term narrows the results. The search adolescents AND social media AND anxiety is much more precise than adolescents AND social media, which is already more precise than social media alone.

When to Use AND

Use AND when your research question requires multiple concepts to be addressed together, when you are getting too many results and need to narrow the search, or when you want to focus on a specific intersection of topics.

Common AND Mistakes

Using AND when you need OR. If you are looking for sources about either climate change or global warming, using AND between those two phrases will give you only sources that use both phrases — and miss many sources that use only one or the other. This is a common and costly error.

Adding too many AND terms too quickly. If your search returns zero results or very few, the first thing to check is whether you have used too many AND terms. Try removing one term at a time to see which removal opens up relevant results.

Assuming AND is automatic. Some search systems treat spaces between words as implicit AND. Others do not. In databases that use explicit Boolean logic, you may need to type AND in capital letters for it to function as an operator rather than a search term. Always check the help documentation for the specific system you are using.

Many modern database search interfaces apply AND automatically between words when you type them in the basic search box without operators. This is convenient for simple searches, but it means you may be applying AND logic without knowing it — and without the ability to control it precisely. Learning to use the advanced search interface, where operators are explicit and controllable, gives you more reliable results.

Section 21.3

OR

OR is the expansion operator. When you connect two terms with OR, you are telling the search system: "A result may contain either of these terms, or both. I will accept any record that has at least one of them." OR broadens your search. Unlike AND, which narrows results to the intersection of sets, OR expands results to the union of sets.

How OR Works

Using the same set metaphor: Set A contains all records with the word adolescents; Set B contains all records with the word teenagers. When you search adolescents OR teenagers, you receive all records in Set A, all records in Set B, and all records that contain both words. No records are excluded because they used one synonym rather than another. This is essential when different sources, authors, or time periods use different words for the same concept.

Why OR Is Indispensable

Academic literature does not use consistent vocabulary. Different disciplines, different time periods, different geographic traditions, and different scholarly communities use different terms for the same ideas. A researcher who searches for only one term for a concept will miss all sources that use alternative terms.

Consider the concept of unhoused people. Depending on the source, the time period, and the discipline, you might find: homeless, unhoused, houseless, people experiencing homelessness, rough sleepers (British usage), or street population. A search for only one of these terms misses all sources that use the others. A search using OR connects them all:

OR Search Example
Connecting synonyms for a single concept
homeless OR unhoused OR "people experiencing homelessness" OR "rough sleepers"
This retrieves sources using any of these terms — a far more complete picture of the literature.

OR in Practice

ConceptOR Search
Young peopleadolescents OR teenagers OR youth OR "young adults" OR "young people"
Climate change"climate change" OR "global warming" OR "climate crisis" OR "climate disruption"
Opioid crisisopioids OR "prescription drugs" OR narcotics OR "pain medications" OR fentanyl
Artificial intelligence"artificial intelligence" OR AI OR "machine learning" OR "deep learning"
Incarcerationincarceration OR imprisonment OR "mass incarceration" OR "criminal justice" OR "correctional system"

Combining OR with AND

OR becomes most powerful when combined with AND. The standard structure for a complex Boolean search is:

Standard Structure
(synonym group 1) AND (synonym group 2) AND (synonym group 3)

For example, if your research question is about the effects of social media use on the mental health of teenagers:

(social media OR "online platforms" OR Instagram OR TikTok OR Facebook) AND (teenagers OR adolescents OR youth OR "young people") AND ("mental health" OR anxiety OR depression OR wellbeing OR "psychological effects")

This search tells the system: "Give me records that address at least one term from each of the three concept groups." It is precise enough to eliminate irrelevant results but broad enough to capture the full range of vocabulary on each concept.

When to Use OR

Use OR when your concept can be expressed in multiple synonyms or related terms, when you are not finding enough results and want to expand the search, when you are searching across disciplines that use different vocabulary for the same concept, or when you want to capture both older and newer terminology for a concept that has been renamed over time.

Common OR Mistakes

Using OR between different concepts. OR is for synonyms and alternatives for the same concept, not for different concepts. "climate change OR education policy" is not a useful search — it would retrieve an enormous number of records about either topic with no meaningful connection between them. Different concepts belong in different AND groups; synonyms for the same concept belong in OR groups.

Forgetting OR entirely. This is the most common error. Beginning researchers often search for only the first term that comes to mind for a concept and never expand with synonyms. Building OR groups for every key concept in your search is one of the single most effective habits you can develop.

Section 21.4

NOT

NOT is the exclusion operator. When you connect two terms with NOT, you are telling the search system: "Show me records that contain the first term but do not contain the second term." NOT narrows your search by eliminating records that match an unwanted pattern.

How NOT Works

Set A contains all records with the word mercury; Set B contains all records with the word planet. When you search mercury NOT planet, you receive records from Set A that are not in Set B — that is, records about mercury that do not mention the planet Mercury. Depending on your research, this might help you focus on mercury the element, mercury poisoning, or mercury in environmental science, while excluding planetary astronomy records.

When NOT Is Useful

NOT is most useful in two situations: disambiguating terms with multiple meanings, and eliminating a consistently irrelevant subcategory.

Example 21.4 — Disambiguating with NOT
Many words have multiple distinct meanings
  • Problem A search for jaguar might return results about the animal, the car brand, the football team, or the operating system.
  • Solution jaguar NOT automobile NOT NFL NOT "Mac OS"
Use NOT to eliminate unwanted meanings once they have been clearly identified.
ProblemNOT Solution
Searching for Mercury (element) but getting results about the planetmercury NOT planet
Searching for nursing care but getting results about nursing homesnursing NOT "nursing homes"
Searching for eagles (the bird) but getting results about the football teameagles NOT NFL NOT football
Searching for cell biology but getting results about cell phones"cell biology" NOT "cell phone"
Searching for depression (mental health) but getting results about economic depressiondepression NOT economic NOT "Great Depression"
Cautions About Using NOT

NOT may eliminate relevant results. If you search depression NOT economic, you will eliminate any article that discusses both mental health depression and economic depression — including potentially valuable comparative or interdisciplinary articles that examine both. Be aware that NOT removes records categorically, without nuance.

Try narrowing with AND before using NOT. In most cases, you can achieve better precision by adding more specific AND terms rather than using NOT. If your search on agricultural practices keeps returning results about urban agriculture, it may be more productive to add AND "rural areas" or AND "crop production" than to use NOT urban.

Use NOT last. Build your search with AND and OR first. Add NOT only when the results still contain a consistently irrelevant category that you cannot eliminate through more specific positive terms.

NOT in Different Systems

Some databases use NOT exactly as written. Others use the minus sign (-) as the NOT operator. Search engines like Google commonly use a minus sign: mercury -planet. Always check the specific syntax of the system you are searching. Using the wrong NOT symbol may cause the system to ignore your exclusion or to treat it as a search term.

Section 21.5

Phrase Searching with Quotation Marks

Quotation marks are among the most immediately useful tools in any searcher's toolkit. When you enclose two or more words in quotation marks, you tell the search system to treat them as an exact phrase — meaning those words must appear together, in that order, with no other words between them.

Why Phrase Searching Matters

Without quotation marks, most search systems treat each word in a multi-word phrase as an independent search term and will find records that contain those words anywhere — even in completely different parts of the text.

SearchWhat the System Finds
social mediaRecords with "social" anywhere and "media" anywhere — may not be about social media at all
"social media"Records where the exact phrase "social media" appears — specifically the topic you want
public healthRecords with "public" and "health" anywhere — could include public relations and mental health separately
"public health"Records about the specific field of public health
higher educationRecords with "higher" and "education" anywhere — including records about achieving higher levels in education generally
"higher education"Records about colleges, universities, and post-secondary education specifically

The difference in precision is significant. Phrase searching ensures that multi-word concepts are treated as unified ideas rather than separate search terms.

When to Use Quotation Marks

Use quotation marks whenever you are searching for:

Proper names & titles
"Toni Morrison," "World Health Organization," "Civil Rights Act of 1964," "The Great Gatsby"
Technical terms
"peer review," "double-blind study," "cognitive dissonance," "supply chain management"
Fixed phrases
"climate change," "social justice," "food security," "academic integrity"
Unit concepts
"mental health," "public policy," "critical thinking," "machine learning"

Phrase Searching and Multi-Word Synonyms

Phrase searching becomes especially important when combined with OR for synonym groups containing multi-word phrases:

Combining Phrase Search with OR
Quotation marks protect multi-word terms inside a synonym group
("mental health" OR "psychological wellbeing" OR "emotional health" OR anxiety OR depression) AND teenagers
Without quotation marks around "mental health" and "psychological wellbeing," the OR logic would be applied to each word separately, producing chaotic results.
Variations in Phrase Searching Syntax

Most databases and search engines use double quotation marks for phrase searching. A few older databases use different syntax. Some databases automatically treat consecutive words in the same field as phrases. Check the help documentation if phrase searches do not appear to be working as expected.

Exact Phrase Versus Near-Phrase Searching

Some databases offer proximity operators — tools that find words appearing near each other but not necessarily in exact sequence. The most common proximity operators are NEAR and ADJ (adjacent). For example, health NEAR/3 care finds records where "health" and "care" appear within three words of each other, in any order, and health ADJ care finds "health care" and "healthcare" as adjacent words.

These operators are more flexible than exact phrase searching and more precise than keyword searching. They are available in databases like EBSCO, ProQuest, and Lexis, among others. Check the advanced search help pages of the database you are using to learn which proximity operators it supports.

Section 21.6

Truncation and Wildcards

Truncation and wildcards are symbols that allow you to search for multiple word forms simultaneously without having to list each one explicitly.

The Problem Truncation Solves

Consider the concept of education. In the scholarly literature, you might encounter: educate, educated, educating, education, educational, educator, educators, educationally, miseducation. If you need to capture all of these forms, you could list them all with OR — but that is tedious and you may miss some forms. Truncation offers a more elegant solution.

How Truncation Works

Truncation uses a symbol — most commonly an asterisk (*) — placed at the end of a word stem. The asterisk tells the search system: "Find all words that begin with this stem, regardless of what letters follow."

Truncation Example
A single stem captures an entire word family
educat*
This single search string retrieves educate, educated, educating, education, educational, educator, educators, educationally, and any other word beginning with "educat."
Stem + TruncationWhat It Retrieves
child*child, children, childhood, childlike, childcare
nurs*nurse, nurses, nursing, nursery, nurser
migrat*migrate, migrates, migrating, migration, migrations, migrant, migrants
environ*environment, environments, environmental, environmentally, environmentalism
technolog*technology, technologies, technological, technologically, technologist
librar*library, libraries, librarian, librarians, librarianship
democra*democracy, democracies, democratic, democratically, democratize, democratization

Truncate when the concept has multiple important word forms (noun, verb, adjective, plural), when you want to capture variations without listing them all, or when you are working in a database with consistent truncation support.

When Not to Truncate Too Aggressively

Short stems can generate too many irrelevant results. Searching car* would retrieve car, cars, care, careful, carefully, caregiver, career, carrier, carrot, cartoon, and hundreds of other words. The stem must be specific enough to capture only the word family you want. A general guideline: truncate on stems of at least four to five characters.

Wildcards for Internal Variation

While truncation handles word endings, wildcards handle variation within a word. The most common wildcard symbol is the question mark (?), though some databases use other characters. A wildcard placed inside a word replaces a single character (or sometimes zero or one character). This is useful for:

Use CaseWildcard SearchWhat It Captures
British vs. American spellingorgani?ationorganization (American) and organisation (British)
British vs. American spellingbehavio?rbehavior (American) and behaviour (British)
British vs. American spellingcolo?rcolor and colour
Spelling variantswom?nwoman and women
Uncertain spellingssulph?rsulfur and sulphur
Truncation and Wildcard Symbols Vary by Database

This is a critical point: the symbols used for truncation and wildcards are not universal. Different databases use different symbols.

SymbolCommon Use
* (asterisk)Truncation — most databases (EBSCO, ProQuest, JSTOR, most catalogs)
? (question mark)Single-character wildcard — EBSCO, some ProQuest databases
$ (dollar sign)Truncation — some older databases
# (hash/pound sign)Single-character wildcard — some databases
% (percent sign)Zero or one character — some legal databases

Before using truncation or wildcards in a new database, check its help documentation to confirm which symbols it uses. Using the wrong symbol produces no error message — it simply treats the symbol as a search character, producing unexpected or failed results.

Section 21.7

Parentheses and Search Logic

When you combine AND, OR, and NOT in a single search, the order in which the system evaluates the operators matters enormously. Parentheses allow you to control that order.

The Order of Operations Problem

Consider this search:

A Search Without Parentheses

adolescents OR teenagers AND "mental health"

Without parentheses, most search systems evaluate AND before OR (following standard Boolean precedence rules, similar to multiplication before addition in mathematics). The system reads this as adolescents OR (teenagers AND "mental health") — retrieving records about adolescents on any topic AND records about teenage mental health, a much broader and less focused set of results than intended.

Corrected with Parentheses
Grouping the synonym set before connecting with AND
(adolescents OR teenagers) AND "mental health"
This tells the system to first identify all records that contain either "adolescents" or "teenagers," and then — from that set — find only the records that also contain "mental health." The difference in results can be enormous.

Parentheses as Grouping Tools

Think of parentheses exactly as you would in a mathematical equation: they group elements that should be evaluated together before the larger operation is applied. The standard structure for a complex Boolean search uses parentheses to group synonym sets, then AND to connect those sets:

Standard Structure
(synonym group A) AND (synonym group B) AND (synonym group C)

For example:

(teenagers OR adolescents OR youth OR "young people") AND ("social media" OR "online platforms" OR Instagram OR TikTok) AND (anxiety OR depression OR "mental health" OR wellbeing)

The parentheses ensure that each synonym group is evaluated as a unit before the AND logic connects them. The system reads this as: "Find records that belong to all three groups — where group membership means matching at least one term within the group."

Building Searches from Inside Out

A practical approach to building complex Boolean searches is to work from inside out: identify each major concept in your research question; for each concept, write all the synonyms and related terms you want to include; connect the synonyms for each concept with OR, and enclose each group in parentheses; connect the concept groups with AND; and if needed, add NOT exclusions at the end.

Worked Example
What is the relationship between food insecurity and academic performance in elementary school children?
  • Conceptsfood insecurity / academic performance / elementary school children
  • Synonyms 1Food insecurity: food insecurity, hunger, "food access," "food poverty," "nutritional insecurity"
  • Synonyms 2Academic performance: "academic performance," "academic achievement," "school performance," "educational outcomes," grades, "grade point average"
  • Synonyms 3Elementary school children: "elementary school," "primary school," children, "school-age children," "K-5," "grades 1-5"
("food insecurity" OR hunger OR "food access" OR "food poverty" OR "nutritional insecurity") AND ("academic performance" OR "academic achievement" OR "school performance" OR "educational outcomes" OR grades OR "grade point average") AND ("elementary school" OR "primary school" OR children OR "school-age children")
A sophisticated, complete Boolean search that captures the full complexity of the research question while remaining precise enough to return relevant results.

Nested Parentheses

In some databases, you can use nested parentheses — parentheses within parentheses — to create even more complex logical structures. This is rarely necessary for most research but is available in professional legal, scientific, and business databases.

Nested Example
Inner groups, an outer AND, and a final exclusion
((climate change OR "global warming") AND (agriculture OR farming OR crops)) NOT (aquaculture OR fisheries)
The inner parentheses group synonyms; the outer parentheses group the AND combination; the final NOT excludes an unwanted subcategory.

Testing Complex Searches

When you build a complex search, run it and evaluate the results. If you get too many results, add more specific AND terms, tighten your synonym groups, or add a NOT exclusion. If you get too few results, remove one AND term, expand your OR synonym groups, or check for truncation opportunities. If results are irrelevant, check whether your parentheses are placed correctly, whether the most important concepts are included as AND terms, and whether you need a NOT exclusion.

Searching Is Iterative

No complex search produces perfect results on the first attempt. Expect to refine.

Section 21.8

Field, Date, and File-Type Searches

The Boolean operators and techniques covered in sections 21.2 through 21.7 control which terms appear in your results. The techniques in this section control where those terms appear within a record and which records are eligible based on their characteristics.

Field Searching

Every record in a database is divided into fields — defined categories of information such as title, author, abstract, subject heading, journal name, institution, and full text. Field searching lets you specify that your search terms must appear in a particular field. Field searching is powerful because the same term means different things depending on where it appears.

Why Field Placement Matters
The same word, four different result sets
  • Full textA search for Darwin retrieves every article that mentions Darwin anywhere — including articles that casually reference Darwin in a sentence about evolution history.
  • AuthorA search for Darwin in the author field retrieves only articles written by someone named Darwin.
  • SubjectA search for Darwin in the subject field retrieves articles whose central subject is Darwin.
  • TitleA search for Darwin in the title field retrieves articles that include Darwin in the title — usually indicating that Darwin is the primary focus.
These are four very different sets of results, generated by the same word in four different fields.

Common Field Codes

FieldCommon LabelExample
TitleTI, ti, title:TI: "food security"
AbstractAB, ab, abstract:AB: vaccination
AuthorAU, au, author:AU: Levitt, Steven
Subject headingSU, su, subject:SU: "climate change"
Journal nameJN, SO, source:JN: "Science"
Institution/affiliationAF, INAF: "Harvard University"
Full textTX, FT, fulltext:TX: mercury
ISBNISBN:ISBN: 9780393340570
DOIDOI:DOI: 10.xxxx/xxxxx

In many databases, field codes are available through dropdown menus in the advanced search interface, so you do not need to memorize the codes — you select the field from a list and the database handles the syntax.

Title Field Searching

Searching in the title field is a powerful strategy for finding highly focused sources. If your term appears in the title of an article, that article is almost certainly centrally about your topic. This makes title searching very precise — though it may miss sources where your concept is central but does not appear in the title. For exploratory research, title field searching can be too narrow. For focused research, it is excellent for identifying the most targeted sources.

Example — Title Field Search
A highly focused set of results
TI: "criminal justice reform"
This retrieves only articles with "criminal justice reform" in the title — a highly focused set.

Abstract Field Searching

Abstracts are short summaries of articles, usually 150–300 words, that describe the article's research question, methods, findings, and conclusions. Searching in the abstract field retrieves articles where your term appears in this summary description — a strong indicator that the topic is central, not merely mentioned in passing. Abstract field searching is often a good middle ground between the precision of title searching and the breadth of full-text searching.

Author Field Searching

When you have identified a scholar whose work is relevant to your research question, searching by author allows you to find all of their publications in a database. Author field searching is especially useful for finding all articles by a key theorist or researcher in your field, tracking the publication history of a scholar whose early work you have found useful, and finding more recent work by an author who wrote a foundational text in your area.

Most databases use last name, first name or last name, first initial for author searching. Some use standardized author identifiers (ORCID iDs) that uniquely identify researchers regardless of name variations.

Date Filters

Date filtering allows you to restrict your search to items published within a specific time range. This is essential for currency (research topics where recent evidence is more relevant than older evidence — medical treatments, technology, policy, current events), historical scope (research questions that ask about a specific time period), and avoiding information overload (restricting to the past five or ten years when a topic has a very large literature).

Most databases offer date filters in two ways: as a range input in the advanced search interface (enter start year and end year), or as a limiter facet in the results page that you apply after running an initial search.

Date Filtering Caution

Be careful not to set date filters so restrictively that you eliminate foundational or seminal work on your topic. A highly influential article published in 2003 may be more valuable than a newer but less rigorous article from 2022. Date filters are useful tools, not substitutes for evaluation.

Language Filters

If your research requires sources in a particular language, or if you need to restrict results to sources in a language you can read, most databases allow language filtering. This is especially useful in interdisciplinary databases that index sources in dozens of languages.

Document Type and Format Filters

Many databases allow you to filter by document type, including journal articles, book reviews, dissertations and theses, conference papers and proceedings, reports, editorials, letters to the editor, and case studies. Filtering to "journal articles" when you want peer-reviewed research is a common and useful strategy. However, be aware that "journal article" and "peer-reviewed" are not identical. Some database filters for peer-reviewed status are more reliable than others. When peer review status matters, verify it by checking the journal's website directly.

File-Type Searching

File-type searching is primarily useful in open web searching rather than in academic databases. Most search engines allow you to restrict results to a particular file type using the filetype: operator:

SearchUse Case
climate change policy filetype:pdfDownloadable reports and government documents
teacher evaluation rubric filetype:docEditable templates and worksheets
census data filetype:xlsRaw data files and spreadsheets

This is useful when you are looking for downloadable reports, government documents, data files, or slide presentations rather than web pages. It is most applicable in general web searching (Google, Bing) and in some government and institutional repositories.

Combining Field and Boolean Searches

Field searches can be combined with Boolean operators for highly targeted searches:

Combined Field and Boolean Search
Targeting title, abstract, and author simultaneously
TI: "food security" AND AB: children AND AU: Smith
This retrieves records where "food security" appears in the title AND children appears in the abstract AND the author is named Smith — useful in very targeted literature searches or when verifying whether a specific author has written on a specific aspect of a topic.

Evaluation Questions Using the MGU Source Evaluation Model

  1. Relevance. How closely does this article match your research question? Note any gaps between your specific question (academic performance in elementary school) and this article's focus (cognitive development in early childhood).
  2. Authority. What credentials do the authors appear to have based on the journal and publication details? What does publication in the Journal of Child Development and Family Studies suggest about the peer review process?
  3. Evidence. What type of evidence does this study use? What are the strengths of longitudinal study design for this type of question? What limitations might a longitudinal study of this type have?
  4. Accuracy. The abstract mentions the USDA Household Food Security Survey Module. What does the use of a standardized, established survey instrument suggest about the study's data quality?
  5. Purpose. Based on the abstract, is this study designed to describe a relationship, establish causation, or both? What distinction between correlation and causation is relevant here?
  6. Currency. Published in 2021, this article is recent. For a topic like childhood food insecurity and development, is currency important? Could findings from 2021 be superseded by newer evidence?
  7. Context. The study was conducted with low-income families in metropolitan areas. What implications does this geographic and socioeconomic context have for the applicability of the findings to your research question?

Misinformation Alert

Misinformation Alert

Alert
Assuming That a Good Search Equals Complete Research

One of the most common misinformation risks in database research is not about false sources — it is about false confidence.

Students who master Boolean searching sometimes make a new kind of error: they assume that if a well-constructed search returns few results, there is little evidence on their topic, or that the results they retrieved represent the full state of knowledge. Neither assumption is safe.

Why Search Results Are Never Complete

Databases are not universal. No single database indexes everything. A thorough search in one database captures only the portion of the scholarly literature that database indexes. Different databases cover different journals, disciplines, time periods, and source types. A rigorous search in EBSCO Academic Search Complete may miss important sources that are indexed only in PsycINFO, Scopus, Web of Science, or a discipline-specific database. Chapter 19 of this textbook discusses database selection in depth.

Your synonyms are not exhaustive. Even a carefully constructed Boolean search using every synonym you can think of will miss articles that use vocabulary you did not anticipate. The scholarly literature on any active topic is always shifting in language as well as content.

Boolean logic finds what you ask for — not what you need. A well-formed search is precise, which is a strength. But precision means your search is also incomplete by definition. Articles that address your topic without using your exact terms, articles that discuss your question as a secondary finding, and articles that would reframe your question in useful ways may not appear in your results.

What Responsible Searching Looks Like

Do not rely on a single search in a single database. Run multiple searches using different synonym combinations. Search multiple databases. Use the subject headings you find in relevant articles to run additional targeted searches. Follow citation chains forward and backward (covered in Chapter 22). Ask a librarian whether you are missing important sources.

The MGU Misinformation Check — Applied to Your Own Process

Who made the search decisions? (You did — and your vocabulary choices have limits.)
What is the claim? ("There is little research on this topic.")
What evidence is offered? (A single search with limited results.)
What is missing? (Other databases, other synonym sets, citation chaining, librarian consultation.)
Can another reliable source confirm it? (Run the same search in a different database and compare.)

Do not conclude from a small result set that a topic is understudied. Conclude only that your current search strategy found a limited set of results. Then refine, expand, and diversify.

Citation Check

Citation Check

When Boolean and advanced searches produce relevant results, record complete citation information immediately — before navigating away from the record. Database sessions can expire, links change, and records that seemed easy to find again sometimes disappear from view.

For a Journal Article, Record:

  1. Author(s) — full names, in the order they appear
  2. Full article title
  3. Journal name
  4. Volume number
  5. Issue number
  6. Year of publication
  7. Page range (first page – last page)
  8. DOI (Digital Object Identifier) — if available; this is the most stable link to an article
  9. Database where you found it (for your own records — not typically included in formal citations)
  10. Whether you accessed the full text or only the abstract

Citation Style Examples

MLA format:
Morales, Diane R., et al. "Food Insecurity and Cognitive Development in Early Childhood: Evidence from a Longitudinal Study." Journal of Child Development and Family Studies, vol. 29, no. 4, 2021, pp. 411–432.

APA format:
Morales, D. R., Patel, S. K., & Henderson, B. (2021). Food insecurity and cognitive development in early childhood: Evidence from a longitudinal study. Journal of Child Development and Family Studies, 29(4), 411–432.

Chicago Notes-Bibliography format:
Morales, Diane R., Sunita K. Patel, and Brian Henderson. "Food Insecurity and Cognitive Development in Early Childhood: Evidence from a Longitudinal Study." Journal of Child Development and Family Studies 29, no. 4 (2021): 411–432.

Common Citation Errors When Working from Database Records

Copying the database URL instead of the DOI. Database URLs often expire and are not stable. Always use the DOI when one is available.

Missing the issue number. Some databases display volume numbers prominently and issue numbers in small print. Record both.

Recording only the first author when there are multiple authors. List all authors (or all up to the number required by your citation style before "et al.").

Confusing the journal name with the database name. The journal is the publication; the database is where you found it. Cite the journal, not the database (except where specifically required).

Not recording the access date for online sources that require it (APA and some Chicago styles require an access date for sources without stable DOIs).

Chapter 21 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary, Review, and Practice

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 21.1Default keyword searches treat search terms as independent words found anywhere, producing broad and often irrelevant results. Advanced search techniques give the researcher control over inclusion, expansion, exclusion, phrasing, word form, search location, time scope, and format.
  • Sec 21.2AND narrows a search to the intersection of multiple concepts — every result must contain all connected terms. AND is used to combine different concepts that must all be present.
  • Sec 21.3OR expands a search to the union of multiple terms — a result need only contain at least one. OR is used to group synonyms and alternative vocabulary for the same concept, and is most powerful when combined with AND across concept groups.
  • Sec 21.4NOT excludes results containing a specified term. It is useful for disambiguating words with multiple meanings or eliminating a consistently irrelevant subcategory, but it should be used carefully and as a last resort, since it removes records categorically.
  • Sec 21.5Quotation marks create exact phrase searches, ensuring multi-word concepts are treated as unified ideas. Proximity operators such as NEAR and ADJ offer more flexible alternatives in some databases.
  • Sec 21.6Truncation symbols (commonly *) capture multiple word forms from a single stem. Wildcards (commonly ?) capture single-character variations, including British/American spelling differences. Symbols vary by database and must be verified.
  • Sec 21.7Parentheses control the order of operations in complex Boolean searches, ensuring OR groups are evaluated as units before AND connects them. Building searches from inside out — concepts, then synonyms, then grouped OR sets connected by AND — produces sophisticated, precise searches.
  • Sec 21.8Field searching directs terms to specific parts of a record (title, abstract, author, subject), producing more targeted results than searching all fields. Date filters restrict by time period; document type and file-type filters restrict by format.

Key Terms

AND
A Boolean operator that narrows search results to records containing all connected terms — the intersection of search sets.
OR
A Boolean operator that broadens search results to records containing at least one of the connected terms — the union of search sets, used to group synonyms.
NOT
A Boolean operator that excludes records containing a specified term, used to disambiguate meanings or remove an irrelevant subcategory.
phrase searching
Enclosing words in quotation marks to require that they appear together, in order, as an exact phrase.
truncation
A symbol, commonly an asterisk, placed at the end of a word stem to retrieve all words beginning with that stem.
wildcard
A symbol, commonly a question mark, placed within a word to match a single-character variation, such as a spelling difference.
proximity operator
A search tool, such as NEAR or ADJ, that finds words appearing near each other within a specified distance, without requiring an exact phrase.
field searching
Restricting search terms to a specific part of a database record, such as title, abstract, author, or subject heading.
order of operations
The sequence in which a search system evaluates Boolean operators; most systems evaluate AND before OR unless parentheses specify otherwise.
date filter
A search limiter that restricts results to records published within a specified time range.
document type filter
A search limiter that restricts results to a specific format, such as journal articles, dissertations, or conference papers.
filetype operator
A web search command that restricts results to a specific file format, such as PDF, DOC, or XLS.

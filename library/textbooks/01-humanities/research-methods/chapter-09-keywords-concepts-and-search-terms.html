Chapter 9 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

You have done the early work of research. You have chosen a topic, narrowed it to a focused question, and built enough background knowledge to understand the landscape of your subject. Now comes the moment when your research question must leave your head and enter a search system.

Here is the problem: search systems do not read minds. A library catalog does not know what you mean when you type something vague. A database does not understand what you are trying to find. A search engine has no idea what question is motivating your search. These systems respond to language — specifically, to the words you type. If those words are poorly chosen, the results will be poor. If those words are well chosen, the results will be far more useful.

This chapter teaches search term development: the ability to translate a research question into the precise language that search systems, library catalogs, and academic databases need to retrieve useful results. By the end of this chapter, you will not guess at search terms. You will build them deliberately — and you will know how to adjust them when your first attempt falls short.

Research Skill Focus

This chapter teaches search term development — pulling key concepts out of a research question, generating synonyms and related terms, moving between broader and narrower language, using names, dates, and places strategically, working in both technical and everyday vocabulary, and building a written search term list that grows as your research develops.

Why This Matters

Search term development matters in every kind of research. Academic databases index articles using precise controlled vocabulary, so casual language can cause you to miss thousands of relevant studies. Library catalogs classify books using standardized subject headings. Even general search engines respond better to specific, well-chosen terms than to vague phrases. A small change in wording — from "teen mental health" to "adolescent psychological well-being" — can transform a set of weak, thin results into a rich collection of precisely relevant sources.

When you cannot find sources, the problem is usually not that sources do not exist. The problem is almost always that your search terms did not match the language those sources use or the language the search system understands. This chapter gives you a deliberate, repeatable process for closing that gap.

Section 9.1

Why Search Terms Matter

When you have a research question in your mind, you understand it fully. You know what you are asking, what you already know, and what you still need to find out. Search systems know none of this. They see only the words you type.

This gap between your question and the words a system can process is the central challenge of search. Bridging that gap is what search term development is for.

Consider this research question:

Worked Example — Breaking a Question into Concepts
How has the expansion of fast food chains affected rates of obesity in rural American communities since 1990?
  • Concept 1Fast food chains → fast food, quick service restaurants, food environment
  • Concept 2Obesity → obesity, overweight, body mass index, BMI, weight gain
  • Concept 3Rural American communities → rural areas, rural populations, nonmetropolitan areas
  • Concept 4Since 1990 → date filter: 1990–present
Principle: Break the question apart, identify each searchable concept, then generate variations for each one before combining them.

You cannot type a full research question into a library database and expect it to work. Databases do not process full questions the way a person does. They search for the words they find in records: titles, abstracts, subject headings, author names, and journal names. Each concept can be searched separately and then combined — and each concept has multiple possible terms, some technical, some everyday, some broader, some narrower.

Why Your First Search Rarely Works Perfectly

Beginning researchers often assume that a search that returns few results means there are no sources on their topic. Experienced researchers know better. A search that returns poor results almost always means one of the following:

ProblemWhat It Looks Like
Terms too vagueSearching "food" instead of "fast food expansion" returns millions of irrelevant results
Terms too narrowA very specific phrase may miss sources using slightly different language for the same idea
Wrong registerCasual language in a scholarly database, or jargon on a general search engine, mismatches vocabulary
Missing conceptA search with "fast food" and "obesity" but no "rural" may return studies that miss your population
Controlled vocabulary mismatchDatabases index under standardized terms that may differ from everyday word choices
Study Note — Search Is a Skill, Not a Reflex

Most people treat search as a reflex: they have a question, they type something, they look at what comes back. Skilled searchers plan their terms before they start, keep a record of what they searched, revise their terms based on results, and use advanced features to refine results. Searching is not a single moment but an ongoing process that develops alongside the research itself.

Section 9.2

Identifying Key Concepts

A key concept is one of the central ideas that a research question depends on. It is the unit of meaning that, if you removed it from the question, would fundamentally change what you are asking. Every research question contains multiple key concepts. Identifying them is the first step in building a search term list.

The most reliable method is to read your research question carefully and underline or highlight every word or phrase that names something you are actually researching — as opposed to structural words that hold the sentence together.

Example 9.2 — Pulling Concepts from a Question
What role does social media use play in increasing rates of anxiety among college students?
  • Structuralwhat, does, play, in, among — these hold the sentence together but are not searchable concepts
  • Conceptsocial media use — first key concept
  • Conceptanxiety (or rates of anxiety) — second key concept
  • Conceptcollege students — third key concept, defines the population
Principle: Strip structural words first; what remains is your raw material for searching.
Concepts Are Not Always Single Words

"Social media" is a concept. "Mental health" is a concept. "Low-income neighborhoods" is a concept. If you break these phrases apart into individual words and search for them separately, you will get very different — and usually less useful — results than if you search for the phrase as a unit. When you identify a key concept, write it as the phrase it actually is. Chapter 21 covers phrase searching in depth.

Checking Your Concept List

Once you have identified your key concepts, ask yourself: Have I captured all the central ideas? Have I included time or place if they matter? Are there ideas implied by the question that are not stated explicitly? Are any of my "concepts" actually too broad to be useful on their own?

Section 9.3

Synonyms and Related Terms

Every concept in your research question can be expressed in multiple ways. Different authors, disciplines, time periods, and source types tend to use different vocabularies even when discussing the same topic. If you search only one term for each concept, you will miss every source that uses a different word for the same idea — and this is not a minor inconvenience. It can mean the difference between finding ten sources and finding one hundred.

Five Methods for Generating Synonyms

MethodHow It Works
Free brainstormAsk what other words or phrases mean the same thing, or something very close to it
Background source vocabularyReturn to encyclopedia and overview sources read during background research and note their vocabulary
Database subject termsLook at the subject headings or descriptors on a useful article you have already found
Related sources' languagePay attention to the vocabulary that strong, relevant sources use as you find them
Reference sourcesDictionaries, glossaries, and specialized encyclopedias often list synonyms alongside definitions

Example concept: elderly people. A free brainstorm produces: older adults, senior citizens, the elderly, aged individuals, geriatric population, people over 65, retirees, aging population. Not all of these are exactly synonymous, and some carry specific connotations, but they all refer to a related population and might appear in sources relevant to a question about this group.

Synonyms Versus Related Terms

A true synonym means essentially the same thing. A related term refers to something connected but not identical. Both are useful: true synonyms help you find sources that discuss the same thing with different words; related terms help you find sources that approach the topic from a connected angle.

Worked Example 9.3 — Synonyms for Three Concepts
How does housing instability affect children's academic performance in elementary school?

Housing instabilityhousing instabilityhomelessness, eviction, housing insecurity, frequent relocation, residential instability, housing displacementpoverty, economic hardship, family stress, transient living
Academic performanceacademic performanceschool achievement, academic achievement, grades, test scores, educational outcomeslearning, school success, grade retention, dropout
Elementary schoolelementary schoolprimary school, primary education, K-5, K-6, grade school, early educationchildhood, school-age children, young students
Principle: One table like this generates dozens of possible search combinations — far more than searching only the three central terms.
Section 9.4

Broader and Narrower Terms

Search vocabulary exists on a spectrum from very broad (general, inclusive) to very narrow (specific, precise). Understanding where your search terms fall on this spectrum — and knowing how to move up or down it deliberately — is one of the most powerful skills in strategic searching.

LevelExample Terms (concept: medication)
Broadesttreatment, therapy, medicine
Broadmedication, drug, pharmaceutical
Mediumprescription drug, over-the-counter drug, controlled substance
Narrowantidepressant, antibiotic, analgesic
Narrowestfluoxetine, amoxicillin, ibuprofen

Use broader terms when initial searches return very few results, when your topic is specialized enough that the literature is thin, when you are exploring before narrowing, or when you want to see how your topic fits within a larger field. Use narrower terms when initial searches return too many results, when broad terms return mostly irrelevant sources, when you know precise disciplinary terminology, or when you want sources addressing your exact topic.

The Goldilocks Problem in Search

Most inexperienced researchers err in one of two directions: terms too vague (too many irrelevant results) or too specific (too few results). There is no formula for exactly how specific to be — it depends on how much has been written on your topic, what system you are using, and how far along you are in your research. The key is to adjust deliberately, moving up or down the term hierarchy based on what your results show, rather than guessing at new terms randomly.

LevelExample Terms (concept: climate change)
Broadestenvironment, nature, Earth, planet
Broadclimate, atmosphere, global environment
Mediumclimate change, global warming, greenhouse effect
Narrowcarbon dioxide emissions, sea level rise, Arctic ice melt
NarrowestGreenland ice sheet melting rate, methane emissions from permafrost
Core Principle

For most research questions, the medium level of specificity is the best starting point. From there, move up if results are too sparse or down if results are too broad — and adjust deliberately, one concept at a time.

Section 9.5

Names, Dates, and Places

Names, dates, and places are among the most powerful search tools available — and among the most underused by beginning researchers. Proper nouns cut through the noise of a search in a way that common nouns cannot. Searching "civil rights movement" returns a vast literature. Searching "Fannie Lou Hamer" returns sources specifically about one of its most important figures. Searching "Voting Rights Act" or "voting rights legislation 1965" focuses even more precisely on a specific document and moment.

Searching for People

Consider name variants — some figures are known by multiple names, titles, or spellings. Be aware of the difference between name as author versus name as subject: searching "Frederick Douglass" as a subject returns sources about Douglass, while searching him as an author returns his own writings. If your research involves an organization, search its full name and any common abbreviations or acronyms.

Searching for Places

Place terms range from countries and regions to states, cities, and specific locations such as the Mississippi Delta or Silicon Valley. Some places have changed names over time — searching sources about a historical period may require the name a place had then, as well as its current name. Use both specific and general place terms: a question about healthcare in rural Montana might benefit from "Montana" specifically, but also "rural Western states" and "rural United States" to capture related scholarship using broader geographic categories.

Searching for Events and Using Dates

Specific events — Hurricane Katrina, the 2008 financial crisis, the 1918 influenza pandemic — narrow results to a precise moment far more effectively than general terms like "natural disaster" or "pandemic." Dates can be used two ways: included in search terms when the date is central to the topic ("Great Depression 1929"), or used to filter results by publication date or covered period. Chapter 21 covers date filters as part of advanced search techniques.

Worked Example 9.5 — Names, Dates, and Places
How did the Triangle Shirtwaist Factory fire of 1911 influence labor legislation in New York State?
  • EventTriangle Shirtwaist Factory, Triangle fire, 1911
  • PlaceNew York State, New York City
  • Topiclabor reform, labor legislation, fire safety laws, workplace safety, garment industry, textile workers
  • PersonFrances Perkins, a key figure in the legislative response
  • OrganizationNew York State Department of Labor
Principle: Each specific, targeted term locates sources directly relevant to a focused question — far more precisely than general topic terms alone.
Section 9.6

Technical and Everyday Vocabulary

Almost every research topic can be discussed using two kinds of language: the everyday language non-specialists use, and the technical language specialists use in their professional work. These two vocabularies often refer to the same things but use very different words — and because different search systems and sources tend to use different vocabularies, you need to work in both registers.

TopicEveryday TermsTechnical Terms
Sugar and healthsugar, junk food, sweet drinks, obesitysucrose, fructose, glycemic index, metabolic syndrome, insulin resistance
Memory loss in older adultsforgetting, memory problems, dementia, Alzheimer'scognitive decline, neurodegenerative disease, episodic memory impairment
Teen drug usedrug use, substance abuse, addiction, getting highadolescent substance use disorder, illicit drug use, psychoactive substance
Climate change effectsstorms, flooding, wildfires, sea level riseextreme weather events, coastal inundation, hydroclimatic variability
Poverty and educationpoor kids, underfunded schools, achievement gapsocioeconomic status, educational inequity, resource allocation

Use technical vocabulary when searching an academic database for peer-reviewed research, when you have enough background knowledge to know the precise disciplinary name, or when your question involves a concept with a specific scientific, medical, legal, or academic name. Use everyday vocabulary when searching a general search engine, looking for news or opinion pieces, exploring a topic before you know its technical language, or seeking sources written for a general audience.

Code-Switching in Search

Strong researchers move fluently between technical and everyday vocabulary — sometimes within the same search session. You might start with everyday terms for an overview, shift to technical terms in an academic database, then return to a mix when searching government reports or policy documents. This is not inconsistency; it is adaptability, matching your search language to the vocabulary of the sources you need.

Section 9.7

Controlled Vocabulary and Subject Headings

Controlled vocabulary is a standardized, carefully maintained list of terms that a library, database, or indexing system uses to classify and describe sources. Instead of relying on the natural-language variety of authors, controlled vocabulary imposes a consistent term for each concept, applied uniformly across the system. If you search for that standard term, you will find every article tagged with it — regardless of what different words those articles actually used.

Library of Congress Subject Headings

The Library of Congress Subject Headings (LCSH) is the largest and most widely used controlled vocabulary system in academic libraries. LCSH headings tend to be formal and precise. Main headings are often followed by subdivisions specifying aspect, place, time, or form — for example, Children — Mental health — United States. When a concept can be expressed multiple ways, LCSH chooses one preferred term (using Cookery rather than Cooking, for instance) and includes "see also" cross-references to related headings.

How to Find LCSH Headings for Your Topic
"Mining the Record"
  • Step 1Find one book or article you know is relevant to your topic
  • Step 2Look at its catalog record in the library catalog or database
  • Step 3Find the subject headings assigned to that item
  • Step 4Use those subject headings to search for more items
This is one of the most efficient ways to move from keyword searching to subject heading searching.

Database-Specific Controlled Vocabulary

Each major academic database has its own controlled vocabulary, called a thesaurus or subject term list, which differs from LCSH and from other databases.

DatabaseFieldControlled Vocabulary System

PsycINFOPsychologyAPA Thesaurus of Psychological Index Terms
MEDLINE / PubMedMedicineMeSH (Medical Subject Headings)

Subject Headings in Practice — A Step-by-Step Example
What are the psychological effects of social isolation on older adults?
  • Step 1Initial keyword search in PsycINFO: social isolation AND older adults AND psychological effects — some useful results, but possibly incomplete
  • Step 2Examine subject headings on a highly relevant article found: Social isolation, Older adults, Psychological well-being, Loneliness, Mental health
  • Step 3Search by these subject headings directly — results now include articles tagged with these terms even if they used different keywords
  • Step 4Notice the related heading Social connectedness in the results and add it to refine further
Principle: Moving from keywords to subject headings to refined subject headings is the core of professional-level database searching.
When Controlled Vocabulary Is Less Useful

Controlled vocabulary is most helpful in well-established databases with consistent indexing. It is less useful or unavailable in general search engines like Google or Bing, some smaller or inconsistently indexed databases, searches for very new topics without standardized terms yet, and cases where the natural-language variation of sources is itself relevant to your question. In these situations, rely more on keywords and synonym lists.

Section 9.8

Building a Search Term List

A search term list is a written record of the terms you plan to use in your research — organized by concept and including synonyms, related terms, broader and narrower alternatives, and controlled vocabulary terms. It is your search plan in written form.

Beginning researchers often skip this step. They think of a few words, type them in, and see what happens. More often than not, this leads to repeating unsuccessful searches, missing obvious synonym variations, abandoning a topic as "unsearchable" when the real problem is search vocabulary, or losing track of useful terms discovered mid-search. A written search term list solves all of these problems.

The Structure of a Search Term List

ComponentWhat It Contains
Research questionWritten in full at the top, to keep you anchored to what you are actually trying to find out
Key conceptsEach concept identified from the research question, as a section label or column header
Terms for each conceptCentral term, synonyms, related terms, broader terms, narrower terms, technical terms, everyday terms, and proper nouns where relevant
Search combinationsSpecific combinations to test, such as Concept A AND Concept B
Search logAdded as you search — what you searched, which system, and how many results you got

Updating and Maintaining the List

Your search term list should grow as you search. When you find a useful source, notice what terms it uses and add new vocabulary. When you find a subject heading that returns good results, record it. When a search combination fails, note that too — it saves you from repeating it. After a few rounds of searching, your list will reflect what has worked, what has not, and what vocabulary the field actually uses.

Keeping the List Manageable

A search term list should be comprehensive enough to give you multiple options for each concept, manageable enough to use without getting overwhelmed, and dynamic enough to grow and change as your research develops. A good starting list might have five to ten terms per concept, with a handful of initial search combinations ready to try. You do not need fifty terms per concept before you begin.

Applied Practice
Search Practice
Five Activities — Concept Identification Through Subject Heading Mining
Activity 9-AConcept Identification

Write a research question you are currently working on, or choose one of the following:

  1. How does childhood exposure to domestic violence affect adult relationships?
  2. What factors have contributed to the decline of bee populations in North America?
  3. How do zero-tolerance policies in schools affect student dropout rates?
  4. What role does language instruction play in the academic achievement of English language learners?

Then: (1) write the research question in full; (2) cross out all structural words; (3) circle the remaining terms and phrases; (4) group related terms into concept clusters; (5) write a label for each cluster. Compare your list with a classmate's and discuss any differences.

Activity 9-BSynonym Generation

For each key concept identified in Activity 9-A, generate synonyms and related terms using at least two methods from Section 9.3. Aim for at least five terms per concept, organized in a table with columns for Concept, Synonyms, and Related Terms.

Activity 9-CBuilding a Search Term List

Using your concepts from Activities 9-A and 9-B, build a complete search term list following the template in Section 9.8. Include your research question at the top, key concepts as section headers, a table of terms for each concept, at least five search combinations to try, and a note about which controlled vocabulary systems you will check.

Activity 9-DSearch Comparison

Using your search term list from Activity 9-C, conduct three searches in the same database or system: (1) your most obvious "first instinct" terms; (2) synonyms substituted for one or more concepts; (3) broader or narrower terms for one concept, depending on whether Search 1 returned too many or too few results. Record the exact terms used, the number of results, whether results seemed relevant, and what you learned from comparing the three searches.

Activity 9-ESubject Heading Mining

Find one article or book in a library database or catalog clearly relevant to your research question. Look at its record and find its subject headings or descriptors. Then answer: What headings are assigned? Are any terms you would not have thought to search yourself? What results do you get searching one or two of these headings directly? Add any useful new terms to your search term list.

Misinformation Alert

The Dangers of Trusting Search Results at Face Value

Search term development is a skill for finding sources. But finding sources and trusting sources are different activities. This alert addresses the misinformation built into search rankings.

Search engines and many databases rank results based on factors that have nothing to do with accuracy or quality: popularity, recent updates, search engine optimization (SEO), advertising, and personalization. A website at the top of a Google search is not necessarily the best, most accurate, or most credible source — often it is simply the most popular or the most optimized for search.

RiskDescription
SEO-optimized misinformationContent deliberately designed to rank highly for topics an organization wants to influence
Out-of-date content ranked highlyOlder, heavily linked pages may outrank newer, more accurate ones where research has since changed
Summary and aggregation sitesSites that repackage other sources, sometimes inaccurately, without being primary or authoritative themselves
Misleading snippetsAlgorithmically selected excerpts that can misrepresent what a page actually says
The MGU Misinformation Check, Applied at the Search Stage

Before you click a search result, ask: Who made this? What is being claimed? What evidence is offered? Can another reliable source confirm this? The domain and organization name in the URL, the consistency of the claim with background reading, and the presence or absence of evidence and independent confirmation are all visible before you ever open the page.

The Lateral Reading Habit

Rather than reading a source deeply before deciding whether to trust it, open new tabs and search for information about the source rather than reading its content. Who runs this website? Is the organization known and reputable? What do other reliable sources say about this source or claim? This habit, common among professional fact-checkers, is far more efficient than reading unreliable sources in depth. Chapter 32 covers fact-checking and verification methods in full.

Citation Check

Recording Source Information While You Search

One of the most common citation mistakes happens at the search stage: students find a useful source, read it, take notes — and forget to record the information needed to cite it. Later, the source cannot be found again. The solution is to record citation information at the moment you find a source, before you read it.

Source TypeInformation to Record
ArticlesAuthor(s), article title, journal name, volume/issue, year, page numbers, DOI, database where found
BooksAuthor(s)/editor(s), title, publisher, year, city of publication (older books), edition, call number
WebsitesAuthor(s), page/article title, sponsoring organization, full URL, publication or update date, access date
Why DOIs Matter

A DOI (digital object identifier) is a permanent, standardized identifier assigned to most scholarly articles. Unlike URLs, which can change, a DOI always resolves to the article's current location. When available, record it — it is the most reliable way to relocate the article and is required in most citation styles.

Sample Citation Record Format
Fill this in at the moment you find the source
  • Author(s)Last, First. Last, First.
  • TitleArticle or book title
  • SourceJournal name / Book publisher / Website
  • YearYear of publication
  • Vol/IssueFor articles
  • PagesFor articles or book chapters
  • DOI/URLFor online sources
  • DatabaseWhere you found it
  • Access dateFor websites
Do not wait until you are writing your paper — record this the moment you find the source.

  • I understand when to move up or down the term hierarchy based on results.

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 9.1Search systems respond only to the words you type, not to the question in your mind. Bridging this gap is the purpose of search term development, and most search failures are failures of language, not failures of available sources.
  • Sec 9.2Key concepts are the central, essential ideas in a research question, often expressed as phrases rather than single words. Identifying them by stripping out structural language is the first step in building a search.
  • Sec 9.3Every concept can be expressed in multiple ways. Generating synonyms and related terms through brainstorming, background sources, database records, and reference works dramatically increases the sources you can find.
  • Sec 9.4Search terms exist on a hierarchy from broad to narrow. Moving deliberately up or down that hierarchy, based on whether results are too sparse or too numerous, is a core strategic skill.
  • Sec 9.5Names, dates, and places are highly specific search tools that can sharply focus a search on a particular person, organization, event, location, or time period.
  • Sec 9.6Every topic has an everyday vocabulary and a technical vocabulary. Knowing which register fits which search system — and moving fluently between them — improves results across source types.
  • Sec 9.7Controlled vocabulary systems such as LCSH and database-specific thesauri use standardized terms to classify sources. Mining the subject headings of one relevant source is one of the most efficient ways to find more.
  • Sec 9.8A written search term list — organized by concept, including synonyms, related terms, broader and narrower alternatives, and a search log — turns searching from a guessing game into a deliberate, improvable process.

Key Terms

broader term
A more general, inclusive search term that captures a larger category than the original concept.
controlled vocabulary
A standardized, maintained list of terms a library or database uses to classify and describe sources consistently.
DOI
Digital object identifier; a permanent, standardized code that always resolves to a scholarly article's current location.
key concept
One of the central ideas a research question depends on; removing it would fundamentally change the question.
lateral reading
Verifying a source by searching for information about it elsewhere rather than reading it deeply first.
narrower term
A more specific, precise search term that captures a smaller subset within a general category.
related term
A term that refers to something connected to a concept but not identical in meaning.
search log
A written record of search terms used, systems searched, and results obtained, kept to avoid repeating unsuccessful searches.
search term list
A written, organized plan of terms — by concept, with synonyms and variants — built before and updated during research.
subject heading
A standardized term assigned to a source within a controlled vocabulary system to classify its content.
synonym
A word or phrase that means essentially the same thing as another, used to capture sources using different language for the same idea.
thesaurus (database)
A database-specific controlled vocabulary tool listing preferred subject terms for searching that system.

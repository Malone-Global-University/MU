Chapter 33 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Most students underestimate the cost of poor note-taking until it is too late to fix it. They spend three weeks gathering sources, read a dozen books and twenty articles, and take quick notes in a browser tab or a notebook they have not looked at in two weeks. Then they sit down to write and discover they cannot find the page number for a quotation they planned to use, cannot remember which article made an argument they wanted to cite, and have to re-read sources they already read.

This chapter teaches students how to record what they read and why it matters, and how to build a research notebook that serves them throughout an entire project. Students will learn how reading logs document the sources they encounter, how source notes capture what those sources say and why it matters, how annotations build interpretive habits, how page numbers and location tracking prevent costly errors, how tags and categories prepare sources for synthesis, and how research reflections keep inquiry purposeful.

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to maintain a research notebook that transforms scattered reading into organized, usable evidence — and should understand why taking research notes is itself a form of intellectual processing, not merely a clerical task added on top of real research.

Research Skill Focus

This chapter builds the foundational organizational skill of research: recording sources, content, and your own thinking in a consistent, retrievable system so that reading becomes evidence you can actually use — through reading logs, source notes built on the MGU Research Note Model, active annotation, disciplined page-number tracking, tags and categories, and regular research reflection.

Why This Matters

Research notes are the infrastructure of research. When you record what you read, why you found it useful, what it says, where the important passages appear, and how it connects to other sources, you are building a research record that saves you time, prevents lost information, and gives you the raw material for synthesis and writing. The act of taking research notes is also a cognitive act: when you summarize a source in your own words, you are processing it, checking your understanding, and beginning the interpretive work that will eventually become your argument. The skills in this chapter connect directly to comparing sources (Chapter 37), organizing sources by theme (Chapter 36), and synthesizing evidence into a coherent argument (Chapter 38). Without organized notes, synthesis is nearly impossible. With organized notes, synthesis becomes a manageable process of building meaning from evidence you have already gathered and understood. Research notes are not a chore added on top of real research — they are how research becomes knowledge you can actually use.

Section 33.1

Why Research Notes Matter

Research notes matter for reasons that are both practical and intellectual. Understanding those reasons helps you take the right kinds of notes rather than simply taking more notes.

Notes as Memory

Human memory is not a reliable research tool. You may feel certain after reading a strong article that you will remember its central argument, its key statistics, and the page numbers of its most useful passages. In the moment, that feeling of certainty is understandable. But memory degrades, especially when you are working on multiple sources over a period of weeks. Details blur. Page numbers disappear. Quotations blur into paraphrases. Arguments from different sources merge together.

Research notes function as external memory. They record what you found, where you found it, and what you thought about it at the time of reading. When you return to your notes days or weeks later, you are not reconstructing your reading from memory — you are reading a record you made when the source was fresh. This matters especially for citation accuracy. If you quote a source, the quotation must be exact and the page number correct. Notes made at the time of reading protect you from memory-based errors that can become plagiarism or misrepresentation.

Notes as Processing

Taking notes requires you to make decisions. Which ideas are most important? Which passages should be recorded exactly? What does this source argue, and how does that argument relate to what I am researching? These decisions are not clerical — they are intellectual. When you write a summary note, you compress a complex source into its essential claims, which means you have to understand the source well enough to identify what is essential. Students who take serious notes are not just better organized than students who do not — they are also better thinkers about their sources. The habit of recording forces the habit of understanding.

Notes as a Research Record

Research projects do not move in straight lines. You search, find sources, stop, return, find new sources, revise your question, go back to earlier sources with new eyes, and eventually build toward a conclusion. Without a research record, it is easy to lose track of what you have done, what you have found, and what remains to be done. A research notebook gives your project a record that tells you how your thinking has developed, what questions remain open, and whether earlier sources need to be revisited in light of what you have learned since.

Notes as Raw Material for Writing

The most direct practical purpose of research notes is to provide the raw material from which your paper, report, or presentation will be built. When you sit down to write, your notes — not your memory of sources, not a pile of highlighted PDFs — should be your primary resource. Writers who try to write from memory or raw sources alone spend enormous time re-reading material they have already read. Writers who write from organized notes spend that time writing.

Section 33.2

Reading Logs

A reading log is a record of every source you have encountered during a research project. It is broader than a set of source notes: rather than capturing what a source says, the reading log captures what you read, when you read it, and whether you found it useful enough to pursue further. The reading log answers a simple but important question: What have I read?

Why Keep a Reading Log?

Without a reading log, it is easy to lose track of sources you encountered early in a project and set aside — only to spend time finding them again later when your research has shifted and they have become relevant. It is also easy to forget sources that you evaluated and rejected, leading to repeated evaluations of the same sources. The reading log prevents these problems: every source you open goes into the log, whether or not you end up using it, giving you a complete record of your research path.

What a Reading Log Entry Includes

A reading log entry is brief — a minimal record that tracks encounter, evaluation, and decision:

Reading Log Entry Components
  • Date. When you read or encountered the source.
  • Source identification. Enough bibliographic information to find the source again.
  • Source type. Scholarly article, book, news report, government document, primary source, website.
  • How you found it. Keyword search, subject heading, citation chaining, recommendation, bibliography.
  • Initial evaluation. Is it relevant? Is it credible? Is it worth reading fully?
  • Decision. Read fully, partially, or not at all — and why.
  • Where the full notes are. Notebook page, digital file, or reference manager entry.

Reading Logs as Research Accountability

A reading log is also a tool of intellectual accountability. When you record that you rejected a source because it lacked credibility markers, you are practicing the evaluation habits taught in Part V of this textbook. When you look at your reading log after two weeks and realize that all of your sources are secondary sources from a single database, you have identified a gap you can address before writing.

Section 33.3

Source Notes

Where the reading log records that you read something, source notes record what it says and why it matters. Source notes are the heart of your research notebook — a detailed record of a single source that captures its content accurately, preserves your interpretive response, and records the citation information you will need later.

What Source Notes Include — The MGU Research Note Model
  1. Source. Full bibliographic information.
  2. Summary. A concise summary in your own words.
  3. Key evidence. The most important data, examples, statistics, or findings, with page numbers.
  4. Quotation. Exact language, in quotation marks, with exact page number.
  5. Paraphrase. Your own restatement of a key passage, with page number.
  6. Usefulness. What this source helps you argue, demonstrate, or complicate.
  7. Limits. What this source does not do or cannot tell you.
  8. Citation information. A complete preliminary citation in the required style.

A Complete Worked Source Note

Source Note — Worked Example
Wildeman, "Parental Imprisonment, the Prison Boom, and the Concentration of Childhood Disadvantage," Demography, 2009
  • SummaryWildeman examines the relationship between the growth of mass incarceration and the concentration of parental imprisonment among disadvantaged children. He estimates that by the early 2000s, roughly 1 in 4 Black children born to low-education parents could expect to experience parental imprisonment by age 14, and argues the prison boom has substantially increased childhood disadvantage in ways standard poverty measures do not capture.
  • Key EvidenceCumulative risk of parental imprisonment by age 14 reached 25.1% for Black children of low-education parents vs. 3.6% for white children of similarly low-education parents by 2004 (p. 271); the gap grew sharply between 1978 and 2004 (pp. 272–273).
  • Quotation"By 2004, roughly 1 in 40 white children and 1 in 7 black children had experienced parental imprisonment." (p. 271)
  • ParaphraseStandard poverty measures fail to capture one of the most significant sources of childhood disadvantage in the modern era — the experience of having a parent imprisoned — because it is both concentrated among low-income and minority families and growing rapidly (pp. 265–266, 276).
  • UsefulnessDirectly central to a research question on mass incarceration and family stability; the racial disparity statistic anchors the scope of the problem in an introduction.
  • LimitsData runs only through 2004; needs updating. Focuses on concentration of risk, not mechanisms. U.S.-only; no international comparison.
Citation (MLA): Wildeman, Christopher. "Parental Imprisonment, the Prison Boom, and the Concentration of Childhood Disadvantage." Demography, vol. 46, no. 2, 2009, pp. 265–280.

This note does everything a source note should do. It captures the source's content accurately, preserves specific evidence, distinguishes between direct quotation and paraphrase, evaluates usefulness and limits, and records the citation in usable form. Source notes should be as long as they need to be — a tangentially related source may need only a brief summary, while a central source may need several pages. What should never be compromised is completeness on the essential elements: full bibliographic information, accurate summary, specific evidence with page numbers, a clear distinction between quotation and paraphrase, and a usefulness-and-limits evaluation.

Section 33.4

Annotations

An annotation is a brief note written directly on or alongside a source — in the margins of a printed text, in the comment features of a PDF reader, or in a separate document keyed to specific pages. Annotations are not the same as source notes. They are a layer of active reading that precedes and informs the writing of source notes.

Annotations capture your thinking as you read, not after you finish. Without annotation, much of what you think while reading disappears — you finish an article, set it aside, and later cannot reconstruct why you found it compelling. With annotation, your first reading generates a record of your own interpretive process that you can return to.

TypeWhat It Does
Highlighting / underliningMarks the most important passages — used strategically, not comprehensively, or it tells you nothing
Marginal notesSummarize in your own words, question a claim, note a connection, flag a passage, record a critical response
Symbols and codesA personal shorthand — asterisk for important, question mark for unclear, exclamation for striking evidence, arrow for connections
Post-reading annotationA one- or two-sentence note at the end of a session bridging annotation and the formal source note
Annotation in Digital Environments

Most PDF readers — Adobe Acrobat, Preview, and tools like Zotero's built-in PDF reader — include comment, highlight, and annotation features that attach notes persistently to the file. Reference managers such as Zotero and Mendeley store annotated PDFs alongside the bibliographic record, keeping your annotations with the citation information for the source.

Maintain the same discipline digitally as on paper: annotations should be purposeful, marking what matters and recording your thinking, not creating a dense visual record as hard to interpret as the original text.

Section 33.5

Page Numbers and Location Tracking

One of the most common and most costly note-taking errors is failing to record the page number for a passage you have noted or quoted. You will need page numbers for citations: MLA and Chicago parenthetical citations and footnotes include page numbers, and APA requires them for direct quotations and recommends them for paraphrases. If you do not record page numbers as you read, you will need to find them later — searching through a long book or a database session that may have expired.

Record page numbers every time you write down a quotation, a paraphrase, or a specific piece of evidence. Do not trust yourself to remember them. Make this habit so automatic that it feels strange to write anything down without one.

Source TypeBest Practice
Print booksWrite the page number in parentheses immediately after any quotation or paraphrase
PDF articlesUse the journal's original page numbers printed on the page, not the PDF reader's navigation count
HTML articlesLook for a PDF version if you need stable page numbers; HTML often lacks original pagination
EbooksPrefer PDF ebooks for stable pagination; for EPUB, record chapter and section heading instead
WebsitesRecord paragraph number or section heading; APA 7 recommends paragraph numbers for unpaginated sources
Video / audioRecord the timestamp (hours:minutes:seconds) of the passage
Section 33.6

Tags and Categories

As a research project grows, so does the volume of notes. A project of moderate complexity might generate notes from 20 to 40 sources, along with reading log entries, annotations, and reflections. Without a system for organizing these notes, the notes themselves become part of the problem. Tags and categories group notes by topic, argument, theme, question, or source type, so that when you are ready to synthesize or write, you can quickly locate all of the notes relevant to a particular part of your argument.

Example — A Tag Set for a Mass Incarceration Project
Self-defined tags applied consistently across source notes

family-outcomes · children-specifically · racial-disparity · economic-effects · neighborhood-effects · recidivism · policy-interventions · statistics-needed · primary-source · must-use · check-this

When writing the section on effects on children, filter by children-specifically to see all relevant notes at once — without re-reading every note.

Most reference managers and note-taking tools support tagging — Zotero, Obsidian, Notion, or even bracketed tags typed in all-caps at the top of a note in a simple word processor. The system matters less than using it consistently. Tags only work if you apply them to every source note and read them when you are organizing.

Some projects benefit from a more structured category system instead of, or in addition to, tags — named folders such as "Section 1 notes," "Counterargument sources," or "Statistics sources" that mirror the structure of your eventual paper.

Avoiding Over-Categorization

Tags and categories are tools, not goals. Do not spend so much time developing an elaborate organizational system that you run out of time for the actual research. Start with a small set of tags reflecting the main themes of your question, add tags as new themes emerge, and remove tags that prove unhelpful. Keep the system lean enough that applying tags does not feel like a chore.

Section 33.7

Research Reflections

Research reflections are brief, informal notes you write to yourself about the state of your research project. They are not source notes — they do not record what a source says. They record what you think is happening in your research: what you are learning, what confuses you, where your question is heading, and what you still need to do.

Research reflections capture how the researcher's understanding changes over time. They are the diary of your intellectual process, and they perform a metacognitive function — they require you to think about your own thinking and can diagnose a gap in your research before it becomes a problem in your paper.

What to Include in a Research Reflection
  • What have I learned in this session?
  • What surprised me?
  • What is still confusing or unclear?
  • How has my research question changed?
  • What gaps am I noticing?
  • What do I need to do next?
Model Research Reflection — September 14
Effects of mass incarceration on family stability
  • LearnedEffects ripple outward through communities, not just individual families. Western's "cumulative disadvantage" concept will be central to my framing.
  • SurprisedThe racial disparity gap (25% vs. ~4%) is starker than expected — I need to double-check the numbers before citing them.
  • ConfusingNot yet clear how to separate effects of incarceration itself from effects of the prior conditions (poverty, instability) that led to it.
  • Question shift"Family stability" was too broad; I am narrowing to economic and residential stability specifically, where the evidence is most concrete.
  • GapsAlmost everything covers people left behind; I have nothing yet on reentry and reintegration for the incarcerated person.
Next steps: search reentry/reintegration sources; find more recent demographic data than Wildeman 2009; find sources addressing causality directly.

Write a research reflection at the end of every significant research session. Over a three-week project, a student might write eight to twelve reflections. Read together, they form a narrative of how the project developed — intellectually valuable, and practically useful if you ever need to explain or justify your research process.

Section 33.8

The MGU Research Note Model

The MGU Research Note Model is a structured framework for writing source notes consistently across all research projects. It is designed to capture everything you need from a source — content, evidence, evaluation, and citation — in a single organized entry.

The MGU Research Note Model — Eight Components
  1. Source. Full bibliographic information, recorded while the source is in front of you.
  2. Summary. Concise, accurate, original-language summary of the main argument.
  3. Key evidence. The most significant data, examples, or findings, with page numbers.
  4. Quotation. Exact language, in quotation marks, with exact page number.
  5. Paraphrase. Genuine rewriting in your own words, with page number.
  6. Usefulness. What this source specifically helps you argue, demonstrate, or complicate.
  7. Limits. Date, scope, method, sample, perspective, or argument gaps.
  8. Citation information. A complete preliminary citation in the required style.
Why This Model?

Consistent structure produces consistent quality. When every source note follows the same pattern, you never have to wonder what information you recorded — it is always in the same place. The model also enforces completeness: a note with summary but no key evidence leaves you without usable proof; a note with evidence but no limits leaves you without the critical judgment to use it responsibly.

Adapting the Model

The model is a framework, not a rigid form. For a brief government data table or a short primary source, some sections will be minimal. For a complex scholarly monograph central to your argument, every section may require substantial development. Adapt the model to the source and the project — the goal is always a note that accurately captures what the source offers and what its limits are.

Source Check

Evaluating a Source Before Taking Notes. A student researching food insecurity and academic performance finds an article titled "Hungry Students Cannot Learn: Food Insecurity and the College Achievement Gap" — a 2021 "working paper" on a university website, authored by a doctoral student, with a reference list, based on a survey of 200 students at one mid-sized university.

  1. Relevance. Is this source relevant to the question "How does food insecurity affect academic performance among college students"? What limits its relevance?
  2. Authority. The author is a doctoral student. Does this affect your judgment of authority? What more would you want to know?
  3. Evidence. What are the strengths and limits of a 200-student, single-university survey? What methodology questions should you ask?
  4. Accuracy. A working paper has not undergone peer review. Does this mean the source is unusable, or that it requires particular caution?
  5. Purpose. The title is emotionally charged. How should awareness of framing shape your reading?
  6. Currency. Is 2021 recent enough for this topic?
  7. Context. What does a university-hosted working paper signal, compared to an anonymous blog or a peer-reviewed article?
  8. Decision. Would you log this source? Take full notes? Use it in your paper — and if so, how would you characterize it to your reader?

Misinformation Alert

Alert: Copying as Note-Taking

One of the most serious risks in note-taking is mistaking copying for note-taking. When students highlight large sections, paste entire paragraphs, or photograph pages without processing the content, they have created raw material — not research notes.

Unprocessed copying creates two risks. First, it raises the danger of accidental plagiarism: returning to notes made of copied text and treating that language as your own, or paraphrasing so closely that the result is patchwriting — a form of plagiarism even when cited. Second, it does not deepen understanding: you can copy a paragraph without understanding it, but you cannot write a genuine summary of it without understanding it.

The rule is simple: every note should be in your own words, except quotations, which must be marked with quotation marks and page numbers. Capturing exact quotation text, downloading a PDF, or saving a citation is legitimate — but these acts of copying must be accompanied by genuine summary, analysis, and evaluation to be useful. The MGU Research Note Model is designed to prevent this problem by requiring original-language summary, paraphrase, and evaluation in every entry.

Citation Check

Record full citation information at the time you take notes, not later. All the information you need is available to you while reading — title page, header, footer, database record. Ten minutes into a session, it is all within reach. A week later, it may not be.

Source TypeRecord at Note-Taking Time
Scholarly articleAuthor(s), title, journal, volume, issue, year, page range, DOI, database name
BookAuthor(s)/editor(s), full title, edition, city, publisher, year, specific pages noted
Book chapterChapter author/title, book editor(s)/title, publisher, year, chapter page range
WebsiteAuthor (if any), page title, site/organization, publication/update date, URL, access date
Government documentIssuing agency, author (if any), title, date, URL or retrieval information
Preliminary Citation Practice

Record preliminary citations in your required style at the bottom of each source note, every time, without exception. They will not be perfect — formatting may need refinement later — but a complete citation with minor formatting errors is far easier to fix than an incomplete citation with missing information.

Central Principle

Organized research notes are not a chore added on top of real research. They are how research becomes knowledge you can use, argument you can make, and writing you can produce with confidence. Do not merely find information. Earn trust in information — and building a complete, accurate, honest research notebook is one of the primary ways that trust is earned.

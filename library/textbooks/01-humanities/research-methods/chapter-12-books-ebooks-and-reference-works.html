Chapter 12 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

In an age of instant search results and short-form content, it is easy to overlook books as research sources. A quick search engine query feels faster than locating, retrieving, and reading a book. But that speed comes at a cost. Books offer something that most web pages and even most articles cannot: sustained, organized, in-depth treatment of a subject, built and documented over hundreds of pages rather than a few hundred words.

This chapter builds your ability to identify, locate, and evaluate books, ebooks, and reference works as research sources. You will learn the differences among monographs, edited collections, textbooks, handbooks, dictionaries, encyclopedias, atlases, chronologies, and bibliographies — and, just as importantly, when each type is the right tool for your research question and when it is not.

Not every book is right for every research task. A textbook gives helpful background but may not be current or specialized enough for an advanced project. A monograph may be brilliantly detailed but written for specialists, requiring strategic reading rather than cover-to-cover reading. A reference work can orient you quickly but is rarely appropriate as your main evidence in an academic argument. Learning what each book type offers — and learning to read books strategically rather than linearly — makes you a faster, more capable, and more confident researcher.

Opening Question

Imagine two students researching the same question: why did urban rail transit decline in many American cities during the twentieth century. The first student relies entirely on the first page of search engine results. The second student locates a scholarly monograph on the subject, reads its introduction and conclusion, identifies the most relevant chapter, and follows its bibliography to two additional sources. Both students spend the same two hours. Which student is more likely to produce a well-supported, well-documented research project — and why? By the end of this chapter, you will understand exactly what the second student did differently, and you will be able to do it yourself.

Research Skill Focus

This chapter develops your ability to identify, locate, evaluate, and strategically read books, ebooks, and reference works, and to use their bibliographies as tools for finding additional sources. By the end of the chapter you should be able to distinguish a monograph from an edited collection, a textbook from a handbook, and a general encyclopedia from a subject encyclopedia — and you should know, for each type, when it serves your research best.

Why This Matters

Reference librarians, writing instructors, and faculty across disciplines consistently report that students who learn to use books well produce stronger research projects than students who rely exclusively on the open web. Books remain foundational to academic research because they provide the depth, organization, and documentation that serious inquiry requires — depth that a search engine snippet or a short web article simply cannot match.

This matters beyond any single assignment. The habit of locating a strong book, reading it strategically, and following its bibliography into the wider scholarly conversation is a skill you will use throughout your time at MGU and in professional research long after you graduate.

Section 12.1

Books as Research Sources

Books have been the primary vehicle for organized human knowledge for centuries. Even in a digital age dominated by databases, websites, and multimedia content, books remain central to academic research. Understanding why helps you use them better.

What Makes Books Distinctive

A book — whether print or digital — differs from most other sources in several important ways.

Length and depth. Books allow authors to develop arguments, explain context, present evidence, address counterarguments, and reach conclusions over hundreds of pages. This depth is simply not possible in a short article or web page. A researcher who needs thorough treatment of a complex subject often finds that books are the only sources long enough to provide it.

Organization and structure. Books are organized by design. Chapters, sections, headings, indexes, and tables of contents make it possible to navigate large amounts of information efficiently. A well-organized book lets you find the specific section relevant to your research question without reading material you do not need.

Documentation. Academic books include citations, footnotes, endnotes, or bibliographies that document every claim. This documentation serves two purposes for you as a researcher: it shows you where the author found evidence, and it gives you a list of additional sources to explore. A single strong monograph can lead you to dozens of relevant sources you might never have found through searching alone.

Review and editorial oversight. Academic books published by university presses and reputable scholarly publishers go through peer review, editorial review, or both before publication. This does not make every book reliable, but it does mean that books from established scholarly publishers have been read critically by subject specialists before reaching print.

Perspective and argument. Unlike many reference sources, which aim to present neutral summaries, academic books often advance specific arguments. The author has a thesis, gathers evidence, interprets that evidence, and tries to persuade readers. Understanding an author's argument — and deciding whether the evidence supports it — is excellent practice for your own critical thinking.

How Books Fit the Research Process

Books are most useful at specific stages of the research process.

During the background research stage, encyclopedias, dictionaries, textbooks, and handbook chapters help you understand a subject well enough to form a focused research question. These are not typically your main sources, but they are indispensable for getting oriented.

During the evidence-gathering stage, monographs and edited collections provide in-depth analysis, documented evidence, and sustained argument that you can incorporate into your own research. A single relevant chapter from a well-chosen monograph may be worth more to your project than ten superficial web pages.

During the synthesis stage, books help you see how experts have positioned their work in relation to other scholars, what debates exist in a field, and how arguments have developed over time. Reading a book's literature review or its engagement with other scholars shows you the shape of a scholarly conversation.

During the citation stage, the bibliographies and footnotes in academic books point you to additional sources and help you understand the standards of documentation in a particular field.

Print Books and Ebooks

Today, many books are available in electronic form, either as ebooks with fixed page numbers that replicate the print edition or as digital-only publications with scrollable text. Understanding the differences helps you use both effectively.

FormatHow It WorksCitation Implication
Fixed-layout ebookUsually a PDF version of a print book; preserves the original page numbers and layoutCite with the same page-number precision as the print edition
Reflowable ebookCommon in EPUB format; text adjusts to font size, line spacing, and screen widthPage numbers are unstable; cite by chapter, section heading, or location marker instead
Library ebook platformsAccessed through library authentication (e.g., ProQuest Ebook Central, EBSCOhost eBooks, Project MUSE)Download, printing, and copying permissions vary by platform — check terms before relying on long-timeline projects
Open access booksFreely available online without a library subscription; some scholarly, some public-domainEvaluate editorial oversight carefully; not all open-access books are equally vetted
Study Note — Check the Platform Before You Commit

If you are planning a research project with a long timeline — a semester-long paper, a thesis, a multi-week assignment — check whether a library ebook can be downloaded for offline reading or whether it is restricted to online viewing only. Losing access partway through a project because a loan period expired is a frustrating and avoidable setback.

Section 12.2

Monographs

A monograph is a book-length scholarly work written by one author (or occasionally two or three co-authors) focused on a single topic, problem, or argument. The word comes from Greek roots meaning "single" and "writing." Monographs are the primary form of scholarly publishing in the humanities and qualitative social sciences, and they remain important across many other fields.

What a Monograph Is

A monograph is not a textbook, encyclopedia, or anthology. It is an original argument developed at book length. The author has conducted research — reading existing scholarship, analyzing primary sources, gathering data, conducting fieldwork, or examining archival materials — and is presenting an argument based on that research.

Monographs are typically published by:

Publisher TypeExamples
University pressesOxford University Press, Cambridge University Press, University of Chicago Press, Duke University Press, MIT Press — the most prestigious publishers for scholarly monographs in most academic fields
Scholarly commercial publishersRoutledge, Palgrave Macmillan, Springer, Brill, SAGE Publications — publish high volumes of academic work across many disciplines
Specialized academic publishersPublishers focused on particular fields or regions

The peer review process for monographs typically involves the author submitting a proposal and sometimes a manuscript to a press, which then sends it to two or more specialists in the field for evaluation. Reviewers assess the originality, accuracy, scholarly rigor, and significance of the work. The press uses those reviews to decide whether to publish the book, often requiring revisions before publication.

How to Read a Monograph for Research

You almost never need to read an entire monograph for a research project. Strategic reading is more efficient and more effective.

Model — Strategic Reading Sequence
Seven steps to extracting what you need from a monograph
  • Step 1Read the title and subtitle carefully. The subtitle often tells you the specific focus, time period, geographical scope, or argument of the book. The Death of the Past tells you something; The Death of the Past: Memory, Trauma, and Genocide in Postwar Europe, 1945–1965 tells you much more.
  • Step 2Read the table of contents. It shows you the book's structure and the scope of each chapter, telling you immediately whether specific chapters are relevant to your research question.
  • Step 3Read the introduction in full. A scholarly introduction typically presents the author's argument, explains why the topic matters, situates the work in relation to existing scholarship, describes the sources used, and previews the structure of the book.
  • Step 4Read the conclusion in full. It summarizes the argument, explains what the evidence has shown, and often suggests directions for future research.
  • Step 5Identify and read the relevant chapters. Once you know what the book argues and how it is organized, read the specific chapters most relevant to your research question.
  • Step 6Use the index to find specific names, terms, events, or topics quickly — especially useful when you need information about a specific aspect of the subject rather than the book's main argument.
  • Step 7Examine the bibliography or footnotes. The documentation in a well-researched monograph is a research resource in itself, telling you what primary and secondary sources the author used.
Principle: Read the frame (title, contents, introduction, conclusion) before the interior. Let the frame tell you where to look.

What Monographs Are Good For — and Not

Monographs Are Best When You NeedMonographs Are Not Ideal When You Need
In-depth, expert treatment of a specific topic, period, or problemQuick background information (use a reference work instead)
A documented scholarly argument to engage with in your own paperThe most recent data or news (monographs take years to write and publish)
Access to analysis of primary sources you cannot read yourself (archival, foreign-language, rare materials)Coverage of a very broad topic (depth on a specific topic is the strength, not breadth)
Understanding of how a topic is debated among expertsMultiple short summaries of many sources (annotated bibliographies or review articles serve that better)
A bibliography that can lead you to additional sources 
Core Principle

A monograph's value lies in sustained, documented argument — not in convenience. Read it strategically rather than cover to cover, and let its introduction, conclusion, and bibliography guide you to exactly what your research question requires.

Section 12.3

Edited Collections

An edited collection (also called an edited volume or anthology) is a book in which a series of chapters written by different authors are brought together under the editorial oversight of one or more editors. The editors typically write an introduction that frames the collection's theme, organize the chapters into a coherent sequence, and sometimes write transitional commentary between sections.

How Edited Collections Work

The editor of a collection identifies a theme, debate, or set of questions that can be explored productively from multiple angles. They invite scholars to write original chapters, commission adaptations of previously published articles, or select important existing essays to reprint. The result is a book that addresses a topic from multiple perspectives, often representing different methods, disciplines, or positions.

Well-known series of edited collections — such as Cambridge Companions, Oxford Handbooks, and Blackwell Companions — provide authoritative overviews of major fields, figures, or periods. Individual edited collections on more specific topics are also common in every academic discipline.

Strengths and Limitations

StrengthsLimitations
Multiple perspectives — presents several experts' views on a common subject, useful for understanding the range of scholarly positionsUneven quality — chapters are written by different authors, so quality varies; evaluate each chapter independently
Disciplinary breadth — some collections bring together historians, sociologists, economists, and cultural theorists examining the same phenomenonLess integrated argument — unlike a monograph, chapters may not build on each other toward a single unified thesis
Access to specialist chapters — a chapter by a leading authority on a specific subtopic may be the most important scholarly treatment availableVariation in documentation — chapter authors follow their own citation practices within the editor's overall style
Introductions as literature reviews — the editorial introduction often summarizes the state of scholarship on the topic 
Citation Caution

When you use a chapter from an edited collection, cite the chapter — not just the book. The citation must include the chapter author's name, the chapter title, the book's editors (preceded by "In"), the book title, the page range of the chapter, and the publisher information. Ignoring the chapter author and citing only the collection editor is a citation error covered in detail in Chapter 43.

Section 12.4

Textbooks and Handbooks

Textbooks and handbooks are book formats designed to support learning and professional practice rather than to advance original scholarly arguments. They are important sources for background knowledge but are rarely the primary evidence in advanced academic research.

Textbooks

A textbook is a book written to support instruction in a particular course or curriculum. Textbooks survey a field, introduce key concepts, define terminology, summarize important findings, and often include pedagogical features such as chapter summaries, review questions, vocabulary lists, and exercises.

What Textbooks OfferLimitations as Research Sources
Accessible, organized introductions to a fieldSynthesize and summarize existing knowledge rather than advancing original arguments — they are secondary or tertiary sources
Definitions of key terms and conceptsWritten for students, not specialists, and may simplify complex debates
Summaries of important theories, debates, and findingsCan become outdated; a textbook published ten years ago may not reflect the current state of a field
Bibliographies pointing to primary and more advanced secondary sourcesIn many disciplines, citing a textbook signals you have not read the primary or specialized scholarship
When to Use a Textbook

Use textbooks during the background research stage to orient yourself to a field, learn key vocabulary, and identify important concepts and debates. Then use the textbook's bibliography to find the original sources you should actually cite.

Handbooks

A handbook is a reference book that provides comprehensive, authoritative coverage of a field, method, or professional practice. Unlike an introductory textbook, a handbook assumes readers have substantial background knowledge. Handbooks are often used by researchers, practitioners, and graduate students.

Academic handbooks (the Oxford Handbook of..., the SAGE Handbook of..., the Routledge Handbook of...) are typically edited collections in which specialists write chapters on specific aspects of a field. They differ from ordinary edited collections in their comprehensive, systematic coverage and their aim to serve as reference works for the field.

What handbooks offer: expert, in-depth chapters on specific aspects of a field; coverage of methodology and research methods; literature reviews that summarize scholarship on subtopics; and authoritative orientation for researchers entering a new area.

When to Use a Handbook

Use handbook chapters when you need authoritative, expert coverage of a specific subtopic and when you want access to a literature review that summarizes existing scholarship. Handbook chapters are typically appropriate to cite in academic research papers, unlike introductory textbook passages.

Section 12.5

Dictionaries and Encyclopedias

Dictionaries and encyclopedias are reference works designed to define terms and provide background information. They are essential tools during the early stages of research but are rarely appropriate as primary sources in advanced academic writing.

Dictionaries

A dictionary defines words and terms. For research purposes, the most useful dictionaries are not general-purpose dictionaries (like Merriam-Webster for everyday English) but specialized subject dictionaries that define the technical vocabulary of a particular discipline.

Specialized DictionaryField
The Oxford Dictionary of PhilosophyPhilosophical terms and concepts
The Dictionary of Critical TheoryLiterary and cultural studies
Stedman's Medical DictionaryMedical and clinical terminology
Black's Law DictionaryLegal terms
The Penguin Dictionary of PsychologyPsychological terminology
The Oxford Dictionary of EconomicsEconomic concepts

When to cite a dictionary: Citing a general dictionary for a common word definition is usually unnecessary and can seem elementary. However, citing a specialized subject dictionary for a technical term can be appropriate, particularly when the definition itself is important to your argument or when the term is used differently in different contexts.

General dictionaries for etymology and usage: Sometimes research requires understanding not just the current meaning of a word but its historical meanings or how usage has shifted over time. The Oxford English Dictionary (OED) is the definitive historical dictionary of English, documenting the history of every word with dated quotations showing usage over time — an invaluable resource for research in literature, history, and linguistics.

Encyclopedias

An encyclopedia provides background articles on a wide range of subjects. General encyclopedias cover many topics broadly; subject encyclopedias cover a specific field in depth.

General encyclopedias like the Encyclopaedia Britannica or World Book Encyclopedia are appropriate for basic background reading. Wikipedia is the most widely used general encyclopedia, but it has particular strengths and limitations discussed below.

Subject encyclopedias cover a specific discipline in depth, with articles written by specialists. These are much more useful for academic research than general encyclopedias.

Subject EncyclopediaField
Stanford Encyclopedia of PhilosophyPhilosophy (freely available online)
International Encyclopedia of the Social SciencesSocial sciences
Encyclopedia of American HistoryAmerican history
Encyclopedia of PsychologyPsychology
Encyclopedia of LinguisticsLinguistics
Gale Encyclopedia of MedicineMedicine
Grove Dictionary of Music and MusiciansMusic
Encyclopedia of Political ThoughtPolitical theory

What subject encyclopedias offer: expert overviews of topics, concepts, and debates in a field; brief bibliographies at the end of articles pointing to primary and secondary sources; clear explanations of complex concepts written for readers with some background; and orientation to a topic before you move to more advanced sources.

A Note on Wikipedia

Wikipedia is one of the most visited websites in the world and contains an enormous range of articles on an equally enormous range of subjects. Understanding its role in research — what it is useful for and where its limits lie — is an important part of information literacy.

What Wikipedia is: Wikipedia is a free, collaboratively written online encyclopedia. Articles are written and edited by volunteers, and content may be added or changed at any time. Wikipedia's policies require that articles summarize existing sources (called "reliable sources" in Wikipedia's terminology) rather than presenting original research. Articles are supposed to be neutral in point of view and well documented with citations.

Common Misconception
"Wikipedia is useless for research."

This is not accurate. Wikipedia is genuinely useful for background orientation on a topic you know little about, and the references section of a well-maintained article can lead you to books, articles, and reports worth reading. The accurate version of the rule is narrower: Wikipedia should not be cited as a source in an academic paper, but it can be an excellent starting point for finding the sources you will actually cite.

Strengths of WikipediaLimitations of Wikipedia
Background orientation — many articles provide accurate summaries of mainstream knowledge on well-established topicsVariable quality — articles on niche or contested topics may be incomplete, biased, or outdated, with no reliable way to know from reading alone
Finding sources — references sections lead you to books, articles, and reports worth readingNot citable in academic papers — content can change at any time, authorship is anonymous, and it is a tertiary summary source
Quick facts — often reliable on well-documented topics, though always worth verifyingNot always current — information that has changed in science, medicine, law, or current events may lag behind
The Right Approach

Use Wikipedia as a first step for background orientation and as a way to find sources. Do not cite it in academic papers. Follow the references at the bottom of Wikipedia articles to find books, articles, and reports that you can actually read, evaluate, and cite.

Section 12.6

Atlases, Chronologies, and Specialized References

Beyond dictionaries and encyclopedias, a range of specialized reference works can provide important evidence for specific research questions. Knowing that these resources exist — and when they are useful — expands your research toolkit significantly.

Atlases

An atlas is a collection of maps, often accompanied by explanatory text, charts, and tables. For many research questions, geographic and spatial information is essential.

Type of AtlasWhat It Offers
Historical atlasesGeographic conditions, political boundaries, population distributions, trade routes, and military campaigns at specific points in the past
Thematic atlasesMapping of a particular subject — world religions, disease and health, climate change, human migration — revealing patterns invisible in text or numbers alone
Reference atlasesCurrent political geography, physical geography, population data, and economic information for all world regions
Digital and interactive maps (GIS)Geographic Information Systems tools allowing dynamic analysis of spatial data — migration, land use, disease spread, electoral results
When to Use an Atlas

Use an atlas when your research question involves geographic relationships, historical boundaries, spatial patterns, or location-based data that text sources alone cannot communicate clearly.

Chronologies

A chronology is a reference work that presents historical events in the order in which they occurred. Chronologies range from brief timelines in encyclopedia articles to comprehensive multivolume works covering centuries of events across many regions.

What chronologies offer: context — understanding when events occurred relative to each other reveals sequences, coincidences, and causal relationships easy to overlook in narrative accounts; verification — allowing you to check dates and the sequence of events before asserting them in your writing; and research breadth — a comprehensive chronology of a period can reveal events you did not know to look for.

Examples include The Timetables of History (a comprehensive world chronology), discipline-specific chronologies in history, literature, science, and law, and specialized chronologies covering wars, political movements, scientific discoveries, or the lives of important figures.

Specialized Reference Works

Many other types of reference works exist for specific research needs.

Biographical dictionaries and reference works provide information about important figures, such as the Dictionary of National Biography (British), American National Biography, Who's Who, and field-specific biographical dictionaries covering scientists, artists, philosophers, politicians, and others.

Statistical references and yearbooks compile numerical data; government statistical agencies publish annual yearbooks, and international organizations publish reference compilations of data on population, economics, health, education, and trade.

Legal references include digests of court decisions, compilations of statutes, and legal encyclopedias, covered in more detail in Chapter 15.

Style manuals and citation guides specify the standards for writing and documentation in particular fields, including the Publication Manual of the American Psychological Association (APA Manual), The Chicago Manual of Style, and the MLA Handbook.

Directories compile information about organizations, institutions, companies, periodicals, and other entities. The Ulrichsweb database, for example, is a directory of periodicals that provides information about journals, including their peer review status.

Section 12.7

Bibliographies and Further Reading Lists

A bibliography, in the reference sense, is a systematic list of sources on a subject. Bibliographies may stand alone as reference works or appear as appendices, back matter, or chapter features in books and articles.

Types of Bibliographies

Subject bibliographies compile sources on a specific topic, field, or period. A Bibliography of American Literature, for example, lists creative works and critical studies related to American writing. Subject bibliographies may be general (covering all of a broad field) or highly specialized (covering a single author, a single decade, a single methodological approach).

Annotated bibliographies add brief descriptions or evaluations of each source. An annotated bibliography entry typically includes the full citation, a summary of the source's content, and an assessment of its usefulness or quality. Chapter 35 of this textbook covers annotated bibliographies in detail.

Bibliographies as finding tools. The bibliographies, notes, and works cited sections of monographs, articles, and edited collections are among the most powerful research finding tools available. When you find a monograph relevant to your research question, its bibliography gives you a curated list of the sources the author found most important. This is sometimes called citation chaining backward: tracing the sources cited by the work you are reading.

Bibliographies as evidence. In some research contexts, examining which sources an author cites can itself be evidence. Understanding which scholars are cited by many others in a field indicates who is considered most influential. Noticing that certain sources are not cited — certain perspectives, certain populations, certain languages — can reveal gaps and biases in a body of literature.

Model — Citation Chaining in Practice
Building a research base by tracing citations
  • Step 1When you find a strong, relevant monograph, read through its bibliography carefully. Mark sources that seem relevant to your research question.
  • Step 2Search your library catalog and databases for those sources.
  • Step 3When you find those sources, read their bibliographies in turn.
  • Step 4This process — finding sources by tracing citations through the literature — is one of the most reliable ways to build a comprehensive research base.
  • Step 5Keep a research log (Chapter 24 and Chapter 33) so you can track which sources you have found, which you have read, and which you still need to locate.
Principle: One well-chosen book can multiply into a full research base through its own documentation.

Further Reading Lists

Many academic books, textbooks, and reference works include "Further Reading" sections at the end of chapters or at the back of the book. These lists compile recommended sources for readers who want to pursue a topic in more depth. Unlike full bibliographies, further reading lists are selective rather than comprehensive — they represent the author's or editor's judgment about the most valuable additional resources.

Further reading lists are useful at the beginning of a research project when you want a quick, curated guide to important sources. They are less useful for advanced research where you need comprehensive coverage rather than a selected list.

Section 12.8

When Books Are the Best Source

Books are not always the right source for every research question. Part of becoming a skilled researcher is knowing when to use books and when other sources serve you better.

Books Are Often the Best Source When You NeedBooks Are Not the Best Source When You Need
Depth of analysis — sustained, expert engagement with evidenceBreaking news or very recent events — books take years to write, review, and publish
Historical perspective — how ideas changed, how events unfolded, how practices evolved over timeNarrow, specific data — a database, government report, or specialized reference work will be faster and more precise
Synthesis of a field — a handbook chapter or review monograph provides synthesis individual articles rarely offerMultiple short perspectives — news articles, opinion columns, or review articles serve a quick survey of viewpoints more efficiently
Argument and interpretation — the full reasoning behind an expert's sustained interpretationVery specialized technical information — in fast-evolving fields like medicine and technology, journal articles and reports are often more current
Primary sources in edited form — letters, documents, speeches made accessible with editorial introductions 
Bibliographic guidance — a roadmap to the literature on a topic 

Evaluating Books Before Using Them

Not every book is worth your time. Before investing effort in reading and taking notes on a book, evaluate it quickly using these criteria.

Quick Book Evaluation Criteria
Apply before committing to a full reading
  • Publisher — Is the book published by a university press, a recognized scholarly publisher, or a reputable trade publisher? Self-published books, books from unknown publishers, and books from advocacy organizations need additional scrutiny.
  • Author credentials — Is the author a scholar, specialist, or recognized expert in the relevant field? What institution or organization is the author affiliated with? What other work has the author produced?
  • Date of publication — When was the book published? For historical or literary subjects, an older book may still be authoritative. For rapidly evolving fields, a book published more than five years ago may be substantially outdated.
  • Reviews — Academic books are often reviewed in scholarly journals. A quick search for reviews in JSTOR, Project MUSE, or discipline-specific databases shows how other experts have evaluated the work.
  • Introduction and table of contents — Read these before committing to the whole book. Does the argument seem relevant? Does the organization make sense? Does the introduction engage seriously with relevant scholarship?
  • Footnotes and bibliography — A well-documented book cites its sources transparently. Scanning footnotes and bibliography tells you whether the author has engaged seriously with the relevant literature.
Chapter 12 — Applied Practice

Search Practice, Source Check, and Research Notebook

Search Practice

Activity 12-AFinding Books in the MGU Library Catalog

Choose a research topic from one of your current courses. Using the MGU Library catalog, complete the following steps:

  1. Search your topic using two to three keywords. Record the keywords you used.
  2. Identify three books that appear relevant. For each book, record the title and subtitle, author(s) or editor(s), publisher, year of publication, and a brief description from the catalog record.
  3. Look at the subject headings listed in the catalog record for one of the books. Use one of those subject headings to run a new search. How does the subject heading search differ from your keyword search?
  4. Check whether any of the three books are available as ebooks through the library. If so, note the platform and any access restrictions.
Activity 12-BReading a Monograph Strategically

Select one of the books you found in Activity 12-A, or use a book recommended by your instructor. Practice strategic reading:

  1. Read the title and subtitle and write one sentence describing what you expect the book to argue.
  2. Read the table of contents and identify which two chapters seem most relevant to your research question.
  3. Read the introduction in full and write a three-sentence summary of the book's main argument, the evidence the author uses, and the book's relationship to other scholarship.
  4. Read the conclusion and write two sentences about what the author claims the evidence has shown.
  5. Examine the bibliography and identify three sources that seem relevant to your research question. Record their full titles and authors.
Activity 12-CComparing General and Subject Encyclopedias
  1. Find an article on your topic in a general encyclopedia (such as Encyclopaedia Britannica if your library has access, or a comparable general reference).
  2. Find an article on the same or a closely related topic in a subject encyclopedia relevant to your discipline.
  3. Compare the two articles: How detailed is each? What level of background knowledge does each assume? Does either include a bibliography? Which article would be more useful for academic research and why?

Source Check

Walking Through a Book Evaluation

You are researching the history of urban public transportation in the United States. In the library catalog, you find this record:

Catalog Record
American Transit: A History of Urban Rail in the Twentieth Century

Barnfield, Helen T.  ·  University of Chicago Press, 2018  ·  412 pages

Let's evaluate this source using the MGU Source Evaluation Model.

CriterionEvaluation
RelevanceThe title indicates a focus on urban rail transit in the twentieth century in the United States. If your research question concerns urban public transportation history, this is directly relevant. If your question is about bus systems specifically or extends to the twenty-first century, this book may be relevant but not comprehensive.
AuthorityThe book is published by the University of Chicago Press, one of the most respected scholarly publishers in the United States, suggesting peer review and editorial oversight. Further evaluation would search for Helen T. Barnfield's academic affiliation, other publications, and citations by other experts.
EvidenceA well-researched history will include footnotes or endnotes and a bibliography. You would need to examine the book to confirm, but a scholarly press publication almost certainly includes documentation.
AccuracyYou cannot immediately verify the accuracy of the book's factual claims without reading it and comparing it to other sources. Looking for scholarly reviews in history journals would help assess whether other experts consider it accurate.
PurposeA scholarly monograph published by a university press has the purpose of advancing knowledge and contributing to academic debate — not advocacy, entertainment, or commercial promotion. This supports treating it as a serious scholarly source.
CurrencyThe book was published in 2018, making it relatively recent. For historical research covering the twentieth century, a 2018 publication date is quite current and would likely include scholarship through approximately 2016 or 2017.
ContextThis is an academic monograph in American urban history, published by a major scholarly press. It is appropriate for college-level research papers in history, urban planning, transportation studies, and related fields.
Overall Assessment

This appears to be a strong scholarly source for research on American urban transportation history. You would want to read the introduction to confirm its argument is relevant to your specific question and check its bibliography for additional sources.

Misinformation Alert

Book Misinformation Patterns to Watch For

Books carry an air of authority that can cause readers to trust them uncritically. Here are common misinformation patterns specific to books and book-like sources.

Pattern
Vanity press and self-published books

With today's print-on-demand technology, anyone can publish a book. Self-published books and books from vanity presses (where the author pays for publication rather than going through editorial selection) have not been peer reviewed or editorially vetted. This does not automatically make them wrong, but it means you cannot rely on publication itself as evidence of quality. Always check the publisher.

Pattern
Advocacy books disguised as scholarly research

Some books present themselves with the apparatus of scholarship — footnotes, bibliographies, academic-sounding language — while actually advancing advocacy positions through selective use of evidence. Common strategies include citing only sources that support a predetermined conclusion, misrepresenting the findings of studies cited, and omitting significant contradictory evidence. Checking book reviews and comparing the book's claims to other sources helps identify this pattern.

Pattern
Outdated editions presented as current

Using an older edition of a book on a rapidly evolving topic can introduce outdated information. Always check the publication date and, where relevant, whether a newer edition exists.

Pattern
Misattributed quotations

Books occasionally attribute quotations to famous figures who never said them — a problem dramatically worsened by the internet but that appeared in print long before. If a striking quotation from a historical figure appears without a specific citation (chapter, page number, letter, speech), verify it against primary sources or authoritative biographical works before citing it.

Pattern
Cherry-picked reference works

Reference works — encyclopedias, dictionaries, almanacs — vary in quality. Some are published by organizations with advocacy agendas and present factual information selectively or with a particular slant. Always check who published a reference work before treating it as neutral.

Citation Check

How to Cite Books in Common Styles

Citing books correctly requires recording specific information: author(s) or editor(s) full name(s); full title and subtitle; edition (if not the first); publisher; year of publication; page numbers of specific passages you quote or paraphrase; and, for ebooks, platform and format information, including how to indicate location if page numbers are unstable.

Style & Source TypeExample
MLA — MonographBarnfield, Helen T. American Transit: A History of Urban Rail in the Twentieth Century. University of Chicago Press, 2018.

APA — MonographBarnfield, H. T. (2018). American transit: A history of urban rail in the twentieth century. University of Chicago Press.
APA — Chapter in edited collectionMorrison, P. (2020). Urban transit and federal policy, 1944–1965. In L. Caine & R. Holst (Eds.), Cities in motion: Essays in American urban history (pp. 45–78). Rutgers University Press.

Common Citation Errors to Avoid

Omitting the subtitle (a very common error that significantly reduces citation precision); citing only the edited collection without citing the specific chapter author; using the wrong publisher name (shorten consistently, using the publisher's own form); and forgetting to record the page numbers of passages you quote or paraphrase while you still have the book available.

Chapter 12 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary, Review, and Practice

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 12.1Books offer length, depth, organization, documentation, editorial oversight, and sustained argument that most other sources cannot match. They serve different stages of the research process, from background orientation to evidence-gathering, synthesis, and citation. Fixed-layout and reflowable ebooks differ in citation precision.
  • Sec 12.2A monograph is a book-length scholarly argument by one author, typically published by a university press or scholarly publisher and peer reviewed before publication. Strategic reading — title, table of contents, introduction, conclusion, relevant chapters, index, bibliography — lets you extract what you need without reading cover to cover.
  • Sec 12.3Edited collections bring multiple expert perspectives together under editorial oversight, offering disciplinary breadth and access to specialist chapters, though quality and integration vary across contributors. Always cite the individual chapter author, not just the collection editor.
  • Sec 12.4Textbooks provide accessible background and vocabulary but are secondary or tertiary sources rarely appropriate for direct citation in advanced research. Handbooks provide comprehensive, authoritative, citable coverage of specific subtopics within a field.
  • Sec 12.5Subject dictionaries and subject encyclopedias provide precise definitions and expert background more useful for academic research than general reference works. Wikipedia is valuable for orientation and for finding sources through its references, but should not be cited directly in academic papers.
  • Sec 12.6Atlases, chronologies, biographical dictionaries, statistical yearbooks, legal references, style manuals, and directories provide specialized evidence — geographic, temporal, biographical, and numerical — that text-based sources alone cannot offer.
  • Sec 12.7Bibliographies and further reading lists are powerful finding tools. Citation chaining backward — tracing the sources cited by a work you are reading — is one of the most reliable ways to build a comprehensive research base.
  • Sec 12.8Books are the best source for depth, historical perspective, synthesis, argument, and bibliographic guidance, but are not ideal for breaking news, narrow data points, or rapidly evolving technical fields. Evaluate publisher, author credentials, date, reviews, and documentation before committing to a full reading.

Key Terms

annotated bibliography
A bibliography that includes a brief description and evaluation of each source in addition to the citation.
atlas
A collection of maps, often accompanied by explanatory text, tables, and charts.
bibliography
A systematic list of sources on a subject, included in a book or published as a standalone reference work.
chronology
A reference work that presents historical events in the order in which they occurred.
citation chaining
The practice of finding additional sources by examining the bibliography or footnotes of a source you are already using.
edited collection
A book in which chapters by multiple authors are brought together under editorial oversight and organized around a common theme or question.
ebook
A digital version of a book, available in fixed-layout (page-preserving) or reflowable (text-adjusting) formats.
encyclopedia
A reference work that provides background articles on a range of subjects; may be general or subject-specific.
fixed-layout ebook
An ebook that preserves the page numbers and layout of the print edition, usually in PDF format.
further reading list
A selective list of recommended additional sources on a topic, included in books or textbooks as supplementary guidance.
handbook
A comprehensive reference work providing authoritative coverage of a field or method, typically written for specialists or advanced students.
interlibrary loan (ILL)
A library service allowing patrons to borrow materials from other libraries when the home library does not hold them.
literature review
A section of a scholarly work that summarizes and evaluates existing scholarship on a topic, situating the current work in relation to the field.
monograph
A book-length scholarly work by one author focused on a single topic or argument.
peer review
The process by which a manuscript is evaluated by specialists before publication; the primary quality-control mechanism in academic publishing.
reflowable ebook
An ebook whose text adjusts to fit the screen size and reader preferences, making page numbers unstable.
reference collection
The section of a library containing reference works (encyclopedias, dictionaries, atlases, handbooks) used for quick consultation rather than sustained reading.
subject dictionary
A dictionary defining the technical and specialized vocabulary of a particular academic discipline.
subject encyclopedia
An encyclopedia providing in-depth articles on topics within a specific academic field, written by specialists.
subject guide
A library resource providing curated recommendations of databases, books, journals, and other sources relevant to a specific academic discipline.
textbook
A book written to support instruction in a course or curriculum; surveys a field for student readers rather than advancing an original scholarly argument.
university press
A publishing house affiliated with a university that publishes peer-reviewed scholarly books and journals; considered the most prestigious publisher for academic monographs in most fields.
Wikipedia
A free, collaboratively written online encyclopedia useful for background orientation but generally not appropriate as a cited source in academic research.

Research Checklist

Chapter 12 — Research Checklist
Use this checklist when evaluating and using books in your research
Finding Books
  • I used the library catalog with relevant keywords and subject headings.
  • I checked subject guides for recommended books in my field.
  • I looked at the bibliographies of sources I have already found for additional book recommendations.
  • I confirmed whether relevant books are available in print, as ebooks, or through interlibrary loan.
Evaluating Books
  • I identified the publisher and confirmed it is a recognized scholarly or academic press.
  • I checked the author's credentials and institutional affiliation.
  • I noted the publication date and considered whether the book is current enough for my research question.
  • I looked for scholarly reviews of the book or checked whether the book is cited by other scholars.
  • I read the introduction and table of contents before committing to reading the full text.
Reading Books Strategically
  • I read the introduction to understand the book's argument and structure.
  • I read the conclusion to understand what the author claims the evidence shows.
  • I identified the specific chapters most relevant to my research question.
  • I used the index to locate specific terms, people, or events I need.
  • I examined the bibliography for additional relevant sources.
Taking Notes and Citing
  • I recorded the full citation information (author, title, publisher, year) when I began taking notes.
  • I recorded page numbers for every quotation and paraphrase.
  • I kept my words and the author's words clearly separate in my notes.
  • For chapters in edited collections, I have recorded both the chapter author and the book editors.
  • I have double-checked my citations against a style guide or citation template.

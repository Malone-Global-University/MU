Chapter 30 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Photographs feel true in a way that words do not. When we read a claim, we instinctively ask ourselves whether it is accurate. When we see an image, something in our cognition says: this happened, this is real, the camera was there. For most of photography's history, that instinct was largely justified — a photograph required a camera, a photographer, and a real subject standing in front of a lens. That relationship has been transformed.

Images and videos can now be cropped, edited, captioned falsely, generated entirely by artificial intelligence, or synthesized into convincing video of events that never happened. The photograph that once said I was there may now say I was constructed or I was taken from somewhere else entirely. This chapter teaches you how to evaluate visual material with the same disciplined skepticism you already apply to written claims.

You will learn how context and captions shape what an image appears to document, how cropping and framing change meaning without altering a single pixel, how editing ranges from legitimate correction to deceptive manipulation, how to conduct reverse image and video searches, how AI-generated visuals and deepfakes differ from authentic recordings, and how to assemble all of these techniques into a complete visual verification workflow.

Opening Question

A photograph circulates online showing a crowded city street with the caption: "Hundreds of thousands protest new policy downtown today." The image is real. It was really taken with a real camera. No part of it has been altered. Is it reliable evidence for the claim attached to it? By the end of this chapter, you will understand why the answer is not automatically yes — and you will have a method for finding out.

Research Skill Focus

This chapter teaches students how to evaluate visual evidence critically and responsibly. Students will learn why images and videos can mislead even careful viewers, how context and captions shape visual meaning, how cropping and framing alter what a visual appears to show, how editing and digital manipulation are used to deceive, how to conduct reverse image and video searches, how AI-generated visuals and deepfakes differ from authentic recordings, and how to build a reliable visual verification workflow. By the end of this chapter, students should approach every image and video they encounter in research with the same disciplined skepticism they apply to written claims.

Why This Matters

Visual evidence is everywhere in modern information. News articles include photographs. Social media posts circulate images and video clips as evidence of claims. Political arguments are illustrated with visuals. Scientific findings are accompanied by charts, microscope images, and data visualizations. Historical arguments rest on archival photographs and film footage. If you cannot evaluate visual evidence, you cannot fully evaluate the arguments that rest on it.

The skills in this chapter are among the most practically important in this entire textbook, and among the skills most people have never been formally taught. The central challenge of visual evidence is not that images always lie — most images are exactly what they appear to be. The challenge is that you cannot determine which images are authentic, which have been altered, and which have been taken out of context simply by looking carefully. The lie, when it exists, is usually invisible to the naked eye. Verification requires method, not just attention.

Section 30.1

Visual Evidence Can Mislead

Understanding why visual evidence can mislead is the essential first step. Before learning verification techniques, you need to understand the specific ways in which images and videos fail as evidence — not because they are always fraudulent, but because they are always incomplete and always interpreted.

The Incompleteness of the Frame

Every photograph and every video frame shows only a portion of what was present at a scene. The camera captures what falls within the lens's field of view during the fraction of a second the shutter opens. Everything outside the frame — to the left, to the right, above, below, and in the moments before and after — is invisible to the viewer.

This incompleteness is not a defect. It is the nature of the medium. But it means that a photograph is always a selection, not a complete record. A photograph of a crowd may look enormous or sparse depending on the angle. A photograph of a protest may make it appear peaceful or violent depending on which moment and which participants are captured. None of these photographs necessarily falsifies anything — but all of them reflect choices a careful viewer should recognize.

The Separation of Image and Context

A photograph without context tells you relatively little. You see what appears to be a scene, but you do not know where it was taken, when it was taken, who the people in it are, or what was happening before and after the shutter clicked. Context — the information surrounding the image — is what allows the photograph to function as evidence of a specific claim.

An authentic photograph, one that has not been digitally altered in any way, can nevertheless be used as false evidence if it is presented with a false or misleading context. A real photograph from one country, one time period, or one event is presented as documentation of something that happened in a different country, time period, or event. The image is genuine. The claim the image is being used to support is not.

The Limits of Memory and Attention

Human visual memory is imprecise. We remember general impressions of images rather than specific details. This means that when a familiar image is slightly altered — a face swapped, a sign changed, a crowd size adjusted — we may not notice the difference, especially if we encountered the original casually or some time ago. People also tend to focus on central or emotionally salient elements of an image and may not notice alterations at the edges or in the background, where skilled manipulators often make their changes.

Emotional Activation and Critical Slowdown

Powerful images trigger emotional responses faster than written descriptions of the same events. This is one reason images are such effective communication tools, and also one reason they are effective misinformation tools. When an image triggers a strong emotional response — outrage, grief, fear, pride — that response can interfere with critical evaluation. We feel an image must be real because of how it makes us feel, even though emotions are not evidence of anything except our own reaction.

Study Note — Treat Strong Feelings as a Signal

When you feel a strong emotional reaction to an image, treat that reaction as a signal to slow down and verify, not as confirmation that the image documents what it claims to document. The stronger the pull to share or trust an image immediately, the more verification it deserves before you use it as evidence.

Section 30.2

Captions and Context

The caption is the most powerful element of any news photograph. Research on how people read news images consistently finds that captions shape interpretation more decisively than the image itself. An image without a caption is ambiguous; a caption resolves that ambiguity by telling the viewer who, what, where, and when. Once a viewer has read a caption, they tend to see the image through the frame the caption provides. Changing the caption — without changing the image at all — changes what the image appears to document.

How Accurate Captions Work

A responsible caption for a news photograph answers the basic questions of journalism: who is depicted, what is happening, where the photograph was taken, and when. It also identifies the photographer and notes any significant limitations of the image, such as whether the photograph is drawn from a file archive rather than a current event.

Example 30.2 — Responsible vs. Misleading Captions
The same kind of image, two very different captions
  • Responsible "Members of the Lakeside City Council vote on the proposed zoning amendment at City Hall, March 14, 2024. Photo: Regional News Service."This caption identifies who (council members), what (voting), where (City Hall), when (March 14, 2024), and the source. A researcher has what they need to verify the image.
  • Misleading "Protesters clashed with police in the capital city yesterday, leaving dozens injured."If the accompanying photograph was actually taken five years earlier in a different country, this caption presents a real image as documentation of an event it does not document. The image is authentic. The claim it supports is false.
Principle: An authentic image only proves what it literally shows, in the context it was actually taken — not whatever the current caption claims.

Misleading captions may also identify people incorrectly, misidentify the nature of an event (describing a staged demonstration as spontaneous, or vice versa), omit context that would change the viewer's interpretation, or present file photographs as current without disclosure.

Verifying Captions

When you encounter an image with a caption that you plan to use as evidence in research, verify the caption independently. Ask:

  1. Does the caption identify the photographer or source? If not, that absence is itself a reason for caution.
  2. Does the date in the caption match other evidence about when the image was taken? Metadata from the original file, if accessible, may indicate the capture date.
  3. Does the location in the caption match what is visible in the image? Look for landmarks, signage, license plates, uniforms, architecture, or other location markers.
  4. Can you find the same image in other reliable sources with the same caption? Convergence across independent sources increases confidence; divergence demands investigation.
  5. Is the image from a file archive rather than a current scene? Legitimate use of archive photos is usually disclosed; deceptive use is not.

Section 30.3

Cropping and Framing

Cropping removes portions of an image. Framing — the photographer's original choice of what to include in the lens — shapes the image before any cropping occurs. Both can significantly alter what an image appears to show, even though cropping is standard practice in photography, used to remove distracting elements, improve composition, and fit images into layouts. The real question is always whether a crop preserves or distorts the meaning of the original scene.

Deceptive Cropping Patterns

PatternWhat it does
Removing contextualizing elementsCuts out the edges, signs, or safety equipment that reveal the true context of an image
Isolating a single moment from a sequenceSelects one frame from a video or series that misrepresents what a gesture or action actually was
Removing identifiersStrips out text, signage, or logos that would let a viewer determine where or when an image was taken
Signs an Image May Be Deceptively Cropped

The edges of the image are cut off in ways that seem to exclude context; no identifying elements such as signs, landmarks, or date indicators are visible; the background is blurred or absent in ways that might conceal location; the image appears inconsistently lit or composed compared to the quality of the central subject. When possible, search for the original uncropped image using the reverse image search techniques in Section 30.5.

Section 30.4

Editing and Manipulation

Image and video editing ranges from adjustments so minor they are nearly invisible to alterations so significant that the resulting image no longer resembles the original scene. The general rule in photojournalism ethics is that editing may correct technical problems introduced by the camera or lighting, but may not add, remove, or alter substantive elements of the scene.

Legitimate editingDeceptive manipulation
Exposure and contrast adjustmentsObject addition (weapons, crowds, fires not present in the original scene)
Color correction for lighting conditionsObject removal (people, signs, or context erased from the scene)
Honest cropping for compositionComposite images combining elements from multiple photographs
Dust and sensor spot removalFace replacement or insertion to misidentify who was present
 Background replacement; date and time stamp manipulation

Video introduces additional possibilities for deception: selective editing that removes qualifying context from a speech; looping that makes a brief incident look like a sustained pattern; speed manipulation that makes movement appear unsteady or frenzied; out-of-sequence assembly that implies unrelated clips are connected; and audio manipulation that changes what a speaker appears to have said or alters the emotional tone of a scene.

Possible Editing Artifacts (Not Reliable on Their Own)

Inconsistent lighting or shadows across elements; inconsistent grain or noise between foreground and background; blurred or softened edges around inserted or removed objects; unrealistic proportions; reflections that do not match apparent light sources; inconsistent depth of field; compression artifacts concentrated around altered areas. These are starting points for suspicion, not proof — reverse searching and verification tools are more reliable than visual inspection alone.

Section 30.5

Reverse Image and Video Searching

Reverse image searching allows you to find other instances of an image on the web by submitting the image itself — rather than a text description — as your search query. It is one of the most powerful verification tools available to researchers: it can find an image's original source, surface earlier uses that predate its current context, reveal different captions attached to the same image, and locate higher-resolution versions.

ToolBest for
Google ImagesBroad search for visually similar images and pages where an image appears; upload or paste a URL
TinEyeExact and near-exact matches; indexes and dates appearances, useful for finding earliest upload
Bing Visual SearchFunctions similarly to Google Images; a useful second opinion
Yandex ImagesParticularly effective for images originating in Eastern Europe, Russia, or the Middle East

When interpreting results, ask: What is the earliest date this image appears? What contexts and captions has it appeared with before? What source first published it? How does the current version compare to earlier versions in cropping or alteration? Are higher-resolution versions available that reveal more detail?

Reverse video search is less developed, but you can extract still frames at key moments and reverse-search those frames as images. The InVID / WeVerify browser plugin is built specifically for this purpose, extracting keyframes and metadata from videos on platforms such as YouTube. Checking platform metadata (upload date, view counts) and searching distinctive audio or speech content can also help establish a video's true origin.

Section 30.6

AI-Generated Visuals

Artificial intelligence systems can generate photorealistic images of scenes, people, and events that do not exist. These text-to-image systems are trained on enormous datasets of real photographs and learn statistical patterns that let them assemble new, internally consistent images — realistic lighting, plausible anatomy, natural textures — without any camera ever capturing a real scene.

This presents a fundamentally different verification challenge than manipulated photographs. With manipulation, there is an original to compare against, and inconsistencies between original and altered versions can be detected. With AI generation, there is no original. The image was not altered; it was generated as a complete whole, which is why classic manipulation-detection techniques (edge artifacts, inconsistent compression) are less reliable here.

Possible visual indicatorWhat to look for
Hands and fingersIncorrect finger counts, distorted proportions, anatomically impossible positions
TeethTeeth that blend together or display unnatural shapes or symmetry
EyesReflections inconsistent with scene lighting, or unnaturally identical between both eyes
Background detailsGarbled text, signage that does not spell real words, dissolving fine textures
Edges and accessoriesBlurred face/hair edges; inconsistent jewelry or glasses across views
These Are Heuristics, Not Proof

AI image generation is improving rapidly. Many AI-generated images show none of the artifacts above, and some authentic photographs superficially resemble them. Treat visual inspection as a starting point for suspicion, not a definitive test. Detection tools such as Hive Moderation, AI or Not, Illuminarty, and Sensity AI provide probability estimates — useful as one input into a broader verification process, never as a final answer, since they produce both false positives and false negatives.

Be especially alert to possible AI generation when an image depicts a dramatic, news-worthy scene not covered by reliable outlets; comes from an account with no established credibility; shows a public figure in an implausible context; cannot be found through reverse image search; or appears alongside a viral claim with no attribution to original news coverage.

Section 30.7

Deepfakes and Synthetic Media

Deepfakes are AI-generated or AI-manipulated videos in which a person's face, voice, or body is synthesized or replaced. The term combines "deep learning," the AI technique used, with "fake." At their most sophisticated, deepfakes are nearly indistinguishable from authentic video to untrained viewers at normal speed — the face moves naturally, expressions are consistent, and separately synthesized audio matches lip movement.

Deepfakes have been used to create false statements attributed to public figures, produce non-consensual intimate imagery, generate political propaganda, and fabricate evidence in disputes and legal proceedings. For researchers, deepfakes are a concern both directly — mistaking a deepfake for authentic video — and systemically, because awareness of the technology lets bad actors discredit genuine footage by simply claiming it is fake.

Possible deepfake indicatorWhat to look for
Facial boundary inconsistenciesBlurring or color mismatch where the face meets neck, ears, hair
Eye blinking and gazeUnnatural blink rate or inconsistent eye movement
Skin textureShifting texture as the face moves under different lighting
Lip synchronizationAudio that diverges from lip movement on complex consonants
Temporal consistencySingle frames look convincing, but slow-motion playback reveals inconsistencies
Core Principle — The Liar's Dividend

When deepfakes become widely known as a phenomenon, bad-faith actors gain a secondary benefit: they can claim authentic video evidence is a deepfake, regardless of whether it is. A researcher who dismisses video evidence as a deepfake must hold that claim to the same evidentiary standard as any other serious assertion — it is not an obvious default just because the video is inconvenient.

Section 30.8

Verifying Visual Evidence

The previous sections describe specific forms of visual misleading and specific verification techniques. This section synthesizes those techniques into a comprehensive workflow you can apply to any image or video you encounter in research.

The Visual Evidence Verification Workflow
Eight steps, applied in order
  • Step 1Pause before sharing or using. Resist the urgency a compelling image creates. Slow down deliberately.
  • Step 2Examine what the image or video actually shows before reading any caption. Note location and time markers, and anything inconsistent or improbable.
  • Step 3Read and question the caption. Does it match what you observed? What claim is the image being used to support, and by whom?
  • Step 4Conduct a reverse image search. Check for prior history, earlier dates, and different captions.
  • Step 5Identify and verify the original source. What organization or photographer first published it?
  • Step 6Triangulate with other reliable sources. Do independent outlets corroborate the depicted event?
  • Step 7Apply technical tools if warranted. AI detectors, deepfake detectors, and metadata review for remaining uncertainty.
  • Step 8Make a judgment about evidentiary status. Decide, and document, exactly what the image can and cannot support.
Principle: Verification is a sequence, not a single glance. Each step narrows uncertainty before you commit the image to your research.
Verification outcomeAppropriate evidentiary use
Source confirmed, caption verified, no manipulation evidenceCan be used with attribution as evidence of the depicted event
Source confirmed but caption questionableUse as evidence of what is literally depicted; verify the context claim separately
Source unverified, no manipulation evidence foundUse with caution; note as unverified; do not use as sole evidence of contested claims
Reverse search shows prior use in a different contextDo not use as evidence of the current caption's claim; document the discrepancy
Signs of AI generation or deepfake detectedDo not use as documentary evidence; report suspected fabrication if appropriate
Unable to verify through available methodsDo not use as evidence; note that verification was not possible

Applying the MGU Misinformation Check to Visual Evidence

The MGU Misinformation Check applies directly to images and video. For any visual you are evaluating, ask each question in turn:

MGU Misinformation Check — Visual Application

Who made it? Who created and is distributing it, and what is their relationship to the claim?

What is the claim? What event, person, or situation does the image purport to document?

What evidence is offered? What documentation supports the caption beyond the image itself?

What is missing? What falls outside the frame, or happened before and after the captured moment?

Can another reliable source confirm it? Does independent, corroborating coverage exist?

Is the emotional reaction stronger than the evidence? Is a strong feeling substituting for verification?

Has the source been altered, misrepresented, or taken out of context? What does your verification process reveal?

Search Practice

Activity 30AReverse Image Search Verification

Scenario 1. Find a dramatic or emotionally compelling news photograph from a recent story published by a major international wire service (Reuters, AP, AFP, or comparable). Submit the image to Google Images, TinEye, and at least one additional reverse image search tool. Record: (1) What results did each tool return? (2) What is the earliest date the image appears in any result? (3) Does the image appear with different captions in any results? (4) What is the original source, based on your search? (5) Does the reverse search support or complicate the caption in the story you found?

Scenario 2. Find an image circulating on social media presented as documentation of a current event. Submit it to reverse image search tools and record: (1) Does the image have a prior history, and if so, what is the earliest date? (2) What contexts has it appeared in before? (3) Does the post's caption match those prior contexts? (4) What is your assessment of the image's evidentiary value for the claim the post makes?

Activity 30BCaption Analysis
  1. Find three news photographs published by three different news sources on the same recent event. For each, record what the caption claims about who, what, where, and when.
  2. Does each image itself support those claims? What elements correspond directly to the caption?
  3. Are there identifiable elements you can independently verify?
  4. How does the choice of moment, framing, and subject differ across the three photographs of the same event?
  5. What does comparing three photographs of the same event reveal about the constructed nature of visual evidence?
Activity 30CAI-Generated Image Analysis

Visit a publicly available, academic or non-profit AI image generation demonstration and generate three images from text descriptions. Examine each for the indicators in Section 30.6 (hands, teeth, eyes, backgrounds, edges, lighting). Submit each to an AI detection tool and record the result. Submit each to a reverse image search tool and record what you find. Write a brief assessment of each image's likely detection status, and note what would make it harder or easier to identify as AI-generated. Use only publicly available academic or demonstration interfaces, and do not generate images of real people or harmful content.

Source Check

Practice Source Evaluation: Visual Evidence in a Research Context. You are researching the use of facial recognition technology by law enforcement agencies in major urban areas. You find this image and accompanying text in a news article:

ImageA uniformed police officer stands beside a large digital display mounted on a van, showing a grid of photographs and a live camera feed. Officer's insignia and badge partially visible. Urban street setting, pedestrians and vehicles in background. Attribution: "© City Wire Service, 2022."
Caption"A law enforcement officer operates a mobile facial recognition unit during a demonstration for city officials, October 2022. The unit is capable of scanning and matching up to 400 faces per minute against a database of known individuals."
Published inA regional news website covering municipal technology and public administration.

Evaluation questions:

  1. Source of the image. What does the City Wire Service attribution tell you, and what additional information would you want to verify the source's credibility?
  2. Caption accuracy. The caption makes a factual claim (a demonstration occurred) and a technical claim (400 faces per minute). How would you verify each separately?
  3. What the image shows versus what the caption claims. What can you directly verify from the image description alone? What elements of the caption cannot be verified from the image?
  4. Framing and selection. What is not shown in this photograph that might matter to your research question? What might a wider shot reveal?
  5. Evidentiary value. What evidentiary value does this image have for your research question, and what would you need in addition to use it responsibly?
  6. MGU Source Evaluation Model. Apply Relevance, Authority, Evidence, Accuracy, Purpose, Currency, and Context to this image as a potential research source.

Misinformation Alert

Alert
The Authentic Image / False Context Problem

The most common form of visual misinformation is not the digitally manipulated photograph. It is the authentic, unaltered photograph that has been removed from its original context and used to support a claim it does not support. An authentic image is only reliable evidence of what it literally depicts, in the context in which it was originally taken — it is not automatic evidence of whatever claim it is currently being used to support.

Common patterns include geographic displacement (a real photo from one country presented as another), temporal displacement (an old photo recycled as current), event substitution (a photo from one event attributed to a different one), person misidentification (a real photo simply mislabeled), and out-of-sequence presentation (aftermath presented as cause). Reverse image search is the most effective defense — cultivate the habit of reverse-searching emotionally compelling images before trusting or sharing them.

Citation Check

Visual sources require specific citation elements: creator or photographer (if known), title or description, date, publisher or agency, and access information. Date is especially critical for visual evidence because date displacement is a major form of visual misinformation.

StyleExample — News Photograph
MLAOrtega, Luis. "Firefighters Battle Blaze in Residential Neighborhood." National Wire Service, 14 Aug. 2023, www.nationalwireservice.example.com/photos/12345.
APAOrtega, L. (2023, August 14). Firefighters battle blaze in residential neighborhood [Photograph]. National Wire Service. https://www.nationalwireservice.example.com/photos/12345
ChicagoOrtega, Luis. "Firefighters Battle Blaze in Residential Neighborhood." Photograph. National Wire Service, August 14, 2023. www.nationalwireservice.example.com/photos/12345.
Common Citation Errors for Visual Sources

Omitting the photographer or creator's name when available; failing to include the date; citing a social media repost rather than the original source; omitting archive or collection names for archival images; failing to note when an image was accessed through an aggregator rather than the original publisher.

Research Checklist

Chapter 30 — Visual Evidence Checklist
Use every time you encounter a visual source you intend to use in research.
Before Engaging
  • I paused before sharing or using the image to conduct verification.
  • I am treating any strong emotional reaction as a signal to verify, not as confirmation.
Examining the Visual
  • I examined the image or video before reading any caption or accompanying text.
  • I identified location markers, time indicators, and identifying elements visible in the image.
  • I noted anything inconsistent, distorted, or improbable.
Caption and Source
  • I read the caption and noted its claims about who, what, where, and when.
  • I checked whether the caption is consistent with what I observed in the image.
  • I identified the source and publisher presenting the image.
Reverse Search and Verification
  • I submitted the image, or key video frames, to at least one reverse image search tool.
  • I checked for prior history, earlier dates, and different captions in other contexts.
  • I identified the original publisher and assessed its credibility.
  • I searched for corroborating coverage from reliable, independent sources.
  • I applied AI or deepfake detection tools if generation or synthesis was a reasonable concern.
Judgment
  • I made and recorded a judgment about what the image can and cannot reliably evidence.
  • I recorded full citation information for the original source.
  • If I found evidence of misinformation, I documented it and did not use the image as evidence of the false claim.

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 30.1Visual evidence can mislead through the incompleteness of the frame, the separation of image from context, the limits of human memory and attention, and emotional activation that overrides critical evaluation.
  • Sec 30.2Captions shape interpretation more decisively than the image itself. Authentic photographs are routinely used as false evidence through misleading captions, especially through geographic and temporal displacement.
  • Sec 30.3Cropping and framing change what an image appears to show by removing context, isolating moments, or stripping identifiers. Cropping is legitimate when honest and deceptive when it distorts meaning.
  • Sec 30.4Editing ranges from legitimate technical correction to deceptive addition, removal, and compositing of elements. Video adds selective editing, looping, speed manipulation, and audio alteration as additional deception techniques.
  • Sec 30.5Reverse image and video searching is the single most effective tool for surfacing an image's prior history, original source, and discrepancies between current and past captions.
  • Sec 30.6AI-generated images are assembled from statistical patterns rather than real scenes, which makes traditional manipulation-detection less reliable. Visual indicators and detection tools provide probabilistic, not definitive, evidence.
  • Sec 30.7Deepfakes extend AI synthesis into video. Detection indicators and tools remain imperfect, and the "liar's dividend" lets bad actors discredit authentic footage simply by invoking the existence of the technology.
  • Sec 30.8A disciplined eight-step verification workflow — examine, question, reverse search, verify source, triangulate, apply technical tools, and judge — combined with the MGU Misinformation Check, gives researchers a repeatable method for evaluating any image or video.
Central Principle

Do not merely find images. Earn trust in images. A powerful visual demands verification, not deference. Every image that evokes a strong emotional response is an image that deserves your most careful scrutiny before you use it as evidence for any claim.

Review Questions

  1. Identify and explain four ways in which visual evidence can mislead researchers and viewers, distinguishing between the inherent limits of the photographic medium and intentional deceptive practices.
  2. Why is the caption considered the most powerful element of a news photograph? Give an example of how an authentic, unaltered photograph could be used deceptively through a false or misleading caption.
  3. Explain the difference between legitimate editing practices in photojournalism and deceptive image manipulation. What is the general rule that distinguishes acceptable from unacceptable editing in professional photojournalism ethics?
  4. Describe the process of conducting a reverse image search. What specific information should you be looking for in the results, and what should you do if those results reveal a different context than the current caption claims?
  5. How do AI-generated images differ fundamentally from manipulated photographs? Why does this difference matter for verification methodology?
  6. What is a deepfake? What is the "liar's dividend," and why does the existence of deepfake technology create verification challenges beyond the direct threat of fake videos?
  7. Apply the MGU Misinformation Check to the following scenario: you encounter a social media post showing a photograph of a large crowd with the caption "Hundreds of thousands protest government policy downtown today." The post has been shared 50,000 times in three hours and generates strong emotional responses in comments. Walk through each question of the Misinformation Check and explain what steps you would take.
  8. A classmate tells you: "I can tell an AI-generated image from a real photograph just by looking carefully at the details — especially the hands and eyes." How would you respond to this claim? What are the limits and appropriate uses of visual inspection for detecting AI-generated imagery?
  9. You are researching the history of a significant protest movement and find a compelling archival photograph in an online archive with minimal metadata. Walk through the steps of the visual evidence verification workflow as applied to this archival source.
  10. Explain the distinction between a photograph being "authentic" and a photograph being "reliable evidence" for a specific research claim. Why is this distinction critical, and why does it mean that detecting manipulation is not sufficient for visual evidence verification?

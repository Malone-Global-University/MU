Chapter 19 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Most students do not know that the open web represents only a small fraction of the world's published knowledge. Behind library subscription walls — accessible to you free of charge because your institution pays for access — there are hundreds of academic databases containing millions of peer-reviewed journal articles, conference papers, dissertations, technical reports, and professional publications that do not appear in a general Google search.

This chapter teaches students how to use academic databases as specialized research tools for finding scholarly and professional sources. You will learn what databases are, how they differ from library catalogs and open web search engines, how to select the right database for a research question, how to read abstracts and navigate full-text access, how to use database filters to refine results, how to save search histories and set up alerts, and how to export citations accurately.

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to enter an unfamiliar academic database, design a productive search, evaluate your results critically, and connect what you find to your broader research process. The difference between a mediocre research paper and a strong one often comes down to whether the student found the relevant scholarly literature. Databases are where that literature lives.

Opening Question

Two students each research the same question: whether predictive policing technology affects community trust in law enforcement. The first student runs a single Google search and reads the first three results — two news articles and a city government press release. The second student searches a criminal justice subject database, applies a peer-review filter, reads the abstracts of a dozen records, and selects three peer-reviewed studies built on interviews, policy documents, and comparative city data. Both students spend the same thirty minutes. Whose foundation of evidence is stronger — and why? This chapter gives you the tools to make sure you are always the second student.

What Academic Databases Are, and Choosing General Versus Subject Databases

Section 19.1

What Academic Databases Are

An academic database is a searchable collection of records describing scholarly and professional publications. Like a library catalog, a database provides structured, searchable descriptions of items. Unlike a library catalog, which describes a specific library's holdings across many subject areas, an academic database is typically organized around a particular discipline or set of disciplines and describes publications from journals, conference proceedings, dissertations, and other scholarly sources — not physical items on a library's shelves.

Understanding what a database is — and what it is not — helps you use it correctly.

Databases Describe Records

The fundamental unit of an academic database is the record: a structured description of a single publication. A record in an academic database typically includes the following fields:

  • Title of the article, paper, or report
  • Author or authors, in standardized form
  • Source — the journal, conference, or publication in which the item appeared
  • Volume, issue, and page numbers (for journal articles)
  • Date of publication
  • Abstract — a short summary of the item's content, arguments, and findings
  • Subject headings or descriptors — controlled vocabulary terms assigned to describe the item's content
  • Keywords — terms the author identified as central to the work
  • DOI — Digital Object Identifier, a permanent link to the item
  • Full text — either embedded in the database or linked through a library resolver

Some databases provide full text directly; others provide records and abstracts only, with links to full text through your library's journal subscriptions or interlibrary loan.

Databases Are Not Search Engines

A common misconception is that a database is simply a specialized search engine. The distinction matters. A search engine crawls the open web, indexes whatever content it finds, and ranks results by popularity signals and relevance algorithms. A database, by contrast, is a curated collection: trained indexers and editors decide which publications are included, how they are described, and how they are organized. The result is a more controlled, more structured, and more disciplined research environment.

Because databases are curated, searching one is different from searching the web. Your search terms interact with structured fields — title, abstract, subject heading, author — rather than with free-floating text on web pages. This structure gives you more precise control over what you find.

Databases Are Not the Same as the Library Catalog

Students sometimes confuse databases with the library catalog. The catalog describes the library's own collection — what the library owns or subscribes to, where items are located, whether they are available. A database describes a broader universe of publications in a given field, regardless of whether your library holds them. When a database record shows that your library does not have full-text access to a particular article, you have options: you can request the article through interlibrary loan, or your library may have a separate subscription to that journal through a different pathway.

The catalog and databases work together. Use the catalog to find books and to verify which journal titles your library subscribes to. Use databases to find articles, conference papers, and reports on specific topics within a discipline.

How Databases Are Built

Academic databases are built and maintained by database vendors — companies and organizations that compile, index, and provide access to scholarly literature. Major vendors include EBSCO, ProQuest, Clarivate (Web of Science), Elsevier (Scopus), and others. Libraries pay subscription fees to access these vendors' databases on behalf of their users.

Some databases are discipline-specific and cover a narrow slice of the scholarly record very deeply — for example, PsycINFO covers psychology and related fields, MEDLINE covers medicine and health sciences, ERIC covers education. Others are multidisciplinary and cover a broad range of fields less deeply — for example, Academic Search Complete and Web of Science span many disciplines.

Some databases are freely available to anyone. PubMed, the database of biomedical and life sciences literature, is freely searchable. ERIC, the education database, is also freely available. JSTOR provides free access to some content. These free databases are exceptions; most high-quality academic databases require institutional subscriptions.

Study Note — Three Questions for Any New Database

When you open a database you have not used before, ask three questions before you search: Who built and maintains this database? What disciplines or publication types does it cover? Does my library provide full text directly, or will I need to use a resolver or interlibrary loan? Answering these three questions in under a minute will save you far more time than it costs.

Section 19.2

General and Subject Databases

One of the first decisions in academic database searching is choosing which database to search. That choice depends on your research question, your discipline, the type of sources you need, and the depth of coverage required.

Databases fall into two broad categories: general databases and subject databases.

General Databases

General databases cover multiple disciplines and are useful for cross-disciplinary research questions, for getting a broad overview of the literature, or for finding sources when you are not yet sure which discipline is most central to your topic.

Most content on JSTOR is not the very latest publications, but older articles of lasting scholarly importance. Google Scholar should be used as a supplement to database searching, not a replacement for it, since it lacks the structured filtering, controlled vocabulary, and curatorial quality control of licensed databases.

Subject Databases

Subject databases cover a specific discipline or cluster of related disciplines in much greater depth than general databases. For research questions within a defined field, subject databases are almost always superior to general databases because they index more specialized journals, use more precise subject terminology, and often include publication types — dissertations, conference papers, clinical trials, government reports — that general databases miss.

Choosing Between General and Subject Databases

The choice between a general database and a subject database depends on your research situation.

Use a general database when your topic crosses multiple disciplines and you need to see the full range of perspectives, when you are in the early stages of research and want a broad overview of the literature before narrowing, or when you need a quick search and do not yet know which discipline is most central to your question.

Use a subject database when your research question falls clearly within a defined discipline, when you need comprehensive coverage of a specialized field, when you need to use the precise controlled vocabulary of a discipline, or when you are working on an advanced or in-depth research project where missing key sources in the field would be a significant problem.

For most undergraduate research projects, start with a general database to get oriented, then move to the most relevant subject database for deeper and more disciplined searching.

Core Principle

A database is not simply a more academic version of a search engine. It is a curated, structured collection built by professional indexers, organized around disciplinary fields, and equipped with tools for precision searching that the open web does not offer. Choosing the right database is itself a research skill, not a technical afterthought.

Section 19.3

Abstracts and Full Text

When you search an academic database, your search returns records. Each record describes a publication. Learning to read and use those records — especially the abstract — is a fundamental research skill.

What an Abstract Is

An abstract is a brief, structured summary of a scholarly article, dissertation, conference paper, or report. It appears at the beginning of the publication and is also reproduced in the database record. Abstracts are typically between 150 and 350 words, though some disciplines use longer structured abstracts for empirical research.

A well-written abstract tells you the topic and research question the publication addresses, the method or approach used to investigate the question, the main findings or arguments of the work, and the significance or implications of those findings.

Reading abstracts carefully is one of the most efficient research skills you can develop. Before you retrieve a full article, before you request it through interlibrary loan, before you spend thirty minutes reading it, read the abstract. The abstract tells you whether the article is worth pursuing.

Types of Abstracts

Informative abstracts report the content of the article in miniature — they tell you what the study found, not just what it is about. Most empirical research articles in the social sciences and sciences use informative abstracts.

Descriptive abstracts describe the article's topic and purpose without revealing the findings. Some humanities articles use descriptive abstracts; a descriptive abstract can confirm relevance to your topic, but you will need to read the full article to know the argument.

Structured abstracts are divided into labeled sections — Background, Objectives, Methods, Results, Conclusions — and are common in medical and clinical research, making it easy to identify study design, sample size, findings, and clinical relevance quickly.

Example 19.3 — Reading an Abstract Efficiently
A four-step reading sequence
  • Step 1Read the first sentence or two to identify the topic and research question. If the topic is not relevant, move on immediately.
  • Step 2If the topic is relevant, read the methods section to understand how the research was conducted, so you can judge whether the evidence fits your question.
  • Step 3Read the findings or conclusions to see what the article argues or discovers, and whether it supports, contradicts, or complicates your current understanding.
  • Step 4Note any qualifications, limitations, or conditions the abstract mentions — a study on a specific population or time period may not generalize to your question.
Principle: If the abstract passes this quick four-step evaluation, proceed to the full text. If not, move on without spending more time.

Navigating Full Text

When a database record includes full-text access, you can read the complete article within the database interface or download it as a PDF. Full-text access depends on your library's subscriptions.

If the record shows a "Full Text" or "PDF Full Text" link, click it for direct access through the database. If it shows a "Find It" or "Check Full Text Availability" link, clicking it connects you to your library's journal subscription resolver, which checks whether your library subscribes to the journal and, if so, directs you to the full text.

If neither route finds the full text, you have three options: check whether the article is available through a different database your library subscribes to; check whether the author has posted a preprint or accepted manuscript on a preprint server (such as PsyArXiv, SSRN, or bioRxiv) or their faculty page; or request the article through interlibrary loan.

Preprints and Open Access — A Caution

Preprints are not identical to the published, peer-reviewed version of a manuscript; they may differ in details, data, analysis, and conclusions. When you use a preprint, acknowledge that you are citing the preprint version rather than the published version, and check whether the published version is available for comparison.

Open access articles, by contrast, are published in journals that make all content freely available without subscription. Using open access articles is entirely appropriate for academic research; their quality is evaluated by peer review just like subscription-only articles.

Section 19.4

Database Filters

One of the most powerful advantages of academic databases over general search engines is the ability to filter search results systematically. Filters let you restrict results to publications that meet specific criteria. Understanding how to use filters effectively is the difference between a productive database search and an overwhelming or inadequate one.

FilterWhat It DoesCaution
Peer-ReviewedRestricts to articles from peer-reviewed journals; removes magazines, newspapers, opinion piecesDoes not guarantee quality — peer review is imperfect
Date / Publication YearRestricts to a time range; useful for currency or historical focusApplying too narrow a range early can exclude landmark or foundational studies
Publication TypeRestricts to journal articles, conference papers, dissertations, or review articlesRestrict only when you know the evidence type you need
Subject / DisciplineNarrows multidisciplinary results to a subject categoryMay exclude relevant records assigned to adjacent disciplines

MethodologyRestricts to qualitative, quantitative, RCT, or review study designsUseful only when your question requires a specific evidence type

Some databases also allow filtering by geographic region or population studied, which is useful for comparative, policy, or community-specific research questions.

Study Note — Apply Filters in Sequence, Not All at Once

The most common mistake with database filters is applying them all at once before you know what your search produces. Instead:

  1. Run your initial search without filters and review the first page of results.
  2. Apply filters one at a time, starting with the most important (usually peer review).
  3. After each filter, review how many results remain and whether quality and relevance have improved.
  4. If results drop below ten for a topic that should have substantial literature, reconsider whether you are filtering too narrowly.
  5. If results remain unmanageably large, add more search terms or apply additional filters.
Section 19.5

Search Histories and Alerts

Academic databases offer two features that many students overlook: search histories and search alerts. These features make long research projects more efficient and help you stay current with new publications in your area.

Search Histories

Most databases record your search history automatically within a session — a log of every search you have run, the terms and Boolean operators you used, the number of results, the filters applied, and the date and time. Search histories let you revisit productive searches, combine previous searches with Boolean operators (for example, combining "S1 AND S2" rather than retyping all the terms), and track which combinations worked and which did not.

Within a session, search histories are maintained automatically. Across sessions, you will usually need to create a free personal account in the database — available in EBSCO, ProQuest, PsycINFO, and Web of Science, among others — to save your search history explicitly.

Study Note — Keep a Manual Search Log Too

Even if a database saves your search history automatically, maintain a separate written search log in a notebook, a text file, or your research notebook. A manual log works across multiple databases, captures your own interpretation of what each search produced, and records the decisions you made along the way — something no single database can do for you.

Search Alerts and Citation Alerts

A search alert is an automated notification that tells you when new publications matching your search terms are added to the database. Search alerts are useful for long-term research projects, dissertation and thesis research, and professional development where you want to stay informed about new research in your field.

To set up a search alert: run a search that produces good results; log in to your personal account; look for a "Save Search" or "Create Alert" option; choose the frequency and delivery method; and give the alert a clear name so you can identify it later.

In addition to search alerts, some databases — particularly Web of Science and Scopus — offer citation alerts, which notify you when a specific article you are tracking receives new citations. This is valuable for following the forward development of a scholarly conversation around a landmark article in your field.

Section 19.6

Citation Exports

One of the most practical features of academic databases is the ability to export citation information directly to a citation manager or download it in a standard format. Learning to export citations accurately saves enormous amounts of time and reduces citation errors.

Manually typing citation information — author names, article titles, journal names, volume and issue numbers, page numbers, DOIs — is slow and error-prone. Citation export tools transfer this information automatically, usually with one or two clicks. Export is not perfect, but even imperfect exports are far more accurate than manual transcription, and export errors are easier to check systematically than errors you have introduced yourself.

FormatUsed ByWhen to Use
RIS (.ris)Zotero, Mendeley, EndNoteThe most widely used format — use this when in doubt
BibTeX (.bib)LaTeX document systemsUse if you write in LaTeX rather than Word or Google Docs
CSV / ExcelSpreadsheet softwareUseful for managing large reference sets in a structured table
Formatted citationCopy/paste into a documentConvenient for quick use; always check for formatting errors

The general export workflow is consistent across most databases: find a relevant record, add it to a folder or check its box, open the export menu, choose your format (RIS for most citation managers, BibTeX for LaTeX), and download or open the file. EBSCO, ProQuest, Web of Science, and Scopus each follow this same basic pattern with minor interface differences.

Checking Exported Citations

After importing exported citations, check each record for accuracy. Common errors include incorrect or incomplete author names, missing or incorrect subtitles, incorrect publication year (online versus print dates sometimes differ), missing volume, issue, or page numbers, missing or incorrect DOIs, and incorrect source type (a conference paper imported as a journal article, for example).

The five minutes you spend checking citations after import prevents hours of citation cleanup when you are writing your paper.

What Databases Do Well, What They Miss, and How to Choose Among Them

Section 19.7

Database Strengths and Limits

Academic databases are powerful tools, but they are not perfect. Understanding their strengths and limits helps you use them more intelligently and prevents you from placing unwarranted confidence in database results.

Strengths

Databases offer curated, disciplinary depth — subject databases index journals that general search engines do not, with far more thorough coverage of their target disciplines. They use controlled vocabulary, standardized subject terms assigned by professional indexers, making subject searching more precise and consistent than keyword searching. Searching the MeSH term "Neoplasms" in PubMed retrieves records about cancer regardless of whether authors used "cancer," "tumor," "carcinoma," or "malignancy."

Databases also offer peer-review filtering, removing a large amount of unreliable content; citation tracking in tools like Web of Science and Scopus, which helps trace the development of a scholarly debate; structured filtering across date, language, methodology, and geography; direct citation export; and search history and alert features that make long-term research manageable.

Limits

No database covers all scholarly publications in its field — coverage gaps, geographic blind spots, and linguistic gaps are real. There is typically a publication lag between when an article is published and when it appears in a database record, ranging from days to months.

Most major databases carry a bias toward English-language publications, particularly from North America and Western Europe — important to remember when researching global, comparative, or non-Western topics. They also carry a bias toward published, peer-reviewed literature, meaning dissertations, working papers, policy documents, and gray literature may be missing or incomplete.

Citation counts are not quality measures. High citation counts indicate influence, not necessarily correctness — an article may be heavily cited precisely because many authors are critiquing it. Peer review is imperfect: peer-reviewed articles can still contain methodological flaws, undisclosed conflicts of interest, selective reporting, or outright fraud. And database interfaces and subscriptions vary and change, so always check your own library's current database list rather than assuming access to something you saw referenced elsewhere.

Common Misconception
"If it's in the database and peer-reviewed, it's reliable."

Peer review means independent experts evaluated the manuscript before publication. It does not mean reviewers caught every flaw, that the data are accurate, that conflicts of interest were fully disclosed, or that the article will never be retracted. Database access and peer-review filtering are the beginning of evaluation, not the end of it.

Section 19.8

Choosing the Right Database

With hundreds of academic databases available across disciplines, choosing where to search is itself a research decision. Making that choice thoughtfully — rather than defaulting to whatever database you have used before — improves the relevance, comprehensiveness, and credibility of your research.

A Six-Step Selection Process

1. Clarify your research question. What discipline is most central? What type of evidence do you need? What time period and what level of theory versus empirical work does your question require?

2. Identify the relevant discipline or disciplines. A single-discipline question starts in that field's primary subject database. A cross-disciplinary question — common in public health, education policy, and environmental studies — requires searching multiple databases.

3. Consult the MGU Library Research Guides. These subject-specific guides list primary databases for each field and explain what each one covers.

4. Consider the type of literature you need. Dissertations point to ProQuest Dissertations and Theses; conference papers point to field-specific sources like IEEE Xplore or ACM Digital Library; government reports point to ERIC, PubMed, or dedicated government databases; historical sources point toward JSTOR or historical newspaper databases.

5. Run a test search and evaluate results. Are results on topic, of the right type, sufficiently current, and described with useful subject headings?

6. Search multiple databases for important projects. No single database is sufficient for major papers or capstone work. Plan for at least two or three, including a primary subject database and at least one general database.

Guided Activities Connecting This Chapter to Your Own Research

Search Practice

Apply what you've learned about databases directly, using your library's available resources or a freely available database such as PubMed or ERIC.

Activity 19ADatabase Orientation

Access a database available through the MGU Library — Academic Search Complete, PsycINFO, ERIC, JSTOR, or PubMed (free at pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov). Choose a topic related to your own research interest, or select one of the following: social media and adolescent mental health; climate change adaptation in low-income communities; bilingual education and language acquisition; criminal justice reform and recidivism rates; vaccine hesitancy and public health communication.

  1. Basic Search. Run a keyword search and record the number of results. Apply the peer-review filter and record how the number changes. Apply a ten-year date filter and record the change again. Open the full record for the three most relevant-looking articles and record the research question, methodology, and main findings for each.
  2. Subject Heading Exploration. Open the full record for your most relevant article and identify its subject headings or descriptors. Click the most relevant heading and note how many records share it. Are these results more or less focused than your original keyword search? Explain why.
  3. Full Text Navigation. For one of your most relevant articles, identify whether full text is available directly, through the library resolver, or not available. If available, identify the journal name, abstract, and reference list. If not available, describe the steps you would take to obtain it.
Activity 19BComparing Databases

Choose a research topic and search it in one general database and one subject database relevant to that topic (for example: online learning and student engagement, searched in Academic Search Complete and in ERIC).

  1. For each database, record the database name, search terms used, number of results (unfiltered and peer-reviewed only), titles and authors of the three most relevant articles, and any subject headings that appeared in the most relevant records.
  2. Did the two databases return the same articles, different articles, or a mix? Which produced more relevant results, and why? Were there subject headings in one database you could not find in the other? Based on this comparison, which database would you use first for a project on this topic?

Source Check

Read the following sample database record and abstract carefully, then complete the evaluation questions.

TitleBetween Transparency and Surveillance: Community Trust in Predictive Policing Programs Across Five American Cities
AuthorsOduya, Funmi A.; Castellano, Rafael J.; Park, Soo-Yeon
SourceJournal of Criminal Justice, Volume 84, Article 101902, 2023
DOI10.1016/j.jcrimjus.2022.101902
AbstractPredictive policing technologies have been adopted by municipal law enforcement agencies across the United States with claims that algorithmic risk assessment tools can reduce crime while allocating police resources more efficiently. This study examines how residents of five mid-sized American cities — Austin, Texas; Louisville, Kentucky; Fresno, California; Hartford, Connecticut; and Providence, Rhode Island — perceive and respond to predictive policing programs in their communities. Drawing on 47 semi-structured interviews with residents and community organization leaders conducted between January and August 2022, and on documentary analysis of city council records, policy documents, and algorithmic audits, we find that community trust is negatively associated with perceived racial targeting, lack of transparency about algorithmic decision processes, and absence of civilian oversight mechanisms. Communities with established civilian review boards and mandatory algorithmic transparency reporting demonstrate higher baseline trust, even when residents express reservations about predictive technology in principle. We argue that the adoption of predictive policing cannot be separated from the broader historical relationships between law enforcement and the communities they serve, and that trust-building requires structural accountability mechanisms that extend well beyond public relations efforts.
DescriptorsPredictive policing; Police-community relations — United States; Algorithmic decision making — Social aspects; Criminal justice policy — United States; Race and crime — United States; Community trust — Measurement; Qualitative research — Interviews
Pub. TypeJournal article; Peer reviewed; English

Evaluation questions. (1) If your research question is "How do communities respond to algorithmic policing technologies?" is this article relevant, and what confirms that? (2) What methods did the authors use, and what kind of evidence does the study produce? (3) What does appearing in a named, peer-reviewed journal tell you about its scholarly standing — and what else would help you evaluate the journal's authority? (4) Is a 2023 publication built on 2022 data sufficiently current? (5) What does a five-city scope suggest about generalizability? (6) Is this primarily descriptive, analytical, or argumentative? (7) Which two subject headings would you use to find related articles, and why? (8) Write the full APA citation for this record, noting any information a student might overlook.

Misinformation Alert

Alert
Conflating Database Access with Scholarly Quality

A persistent misinformation risk in academic database searching is treating database access as a quality guarantee. Students who apply a peer-review filter sometimes assume that every article they retrieve is reliable, rigorously conducted, and beyond question. This assumption is dangerous. Peer review means independent experts evaluated the manuscript before publication — it does not mean reviewers caught every methodological flaw, that the data reported are accurate, that authors disclosed every conflict of interest, or that the article will not later be retracted.

Retractions. Some peer-reviewed articles are later formally withdrawn from the scientific record because of errors, data fabrication, falsification, or plagiarism. A retracted article may still appear in database records, sometimes without a prominent retraction notice. Before relying on a key article in health, medicine, or science research, check Retraction Watch (retractionwatch.com) or the original journal for a retraction notice.

Predatory journals. Predatory journals charge authors publication fees while providing little or no genuine peer review, and their articles can appear in some general or interdisciplinary databases. Checking whether a journal is indexed in Web of Science, Scopus, or PubMed, or listed in the Directory of Open Access Journals (DOAJ), can help you identify predatory publications.

Publication bias. Studies with statistically significant or confirmatory findings are more likely to be published than studies with null or contradictory results, meaning the peer-reviewed literature on a topic may overrepresent certain outcomes.

Conflicts of interest. Published research may be funded by parties with interests in the findings. Disclosure is required but not always complete — always look for the funding and disclosure section.

The MGU Source Evaluation Model applies to peer-reviewed database results as fully as it applies to any other source. Finding an article through a database search is the beginning of evaluation, not the end.

Citation Check

When you find a journal article through a database that you intend to use, record the full citation information immediately. Do not wait until you are writing your paper — database sessions end, URLs expire, and records can change.

For a journal article citation, record the author(s); year of publication; full title, including subtitle; journal name; volume number; issue number; page range or article number; and the DOI.

Research Checklist

Chapter 19 — Database Research Checklist
Use this each time you begin and complete a database research session.
Before You Search
  • I have identified my research question and the type of evidence I need.
  • I have consulted the MGU Library Research Guide for my subject area.
  • I have identified the most appropriate general or subject database for my topic.
  • I have prepared a list of keywords, synonyms, and related terms.
During Searching
  • I ran an initial keyword search and evaluated the number and relevance of results.
  • I applied the peer-review filter and noted how results changed.
  • I applied a date filter appropriate to my research question.
  • I read abstracts for the most relevant-looking records before retrieving full text.
  • I used subject headings from relevant records to find additional sources.
Managing Access and Citations
  • I accessed full text directly, through the library resolver, or via interlibrary loan as needed.
  • I exported citations to my citation manager and checked them for accuracy.
  • I saved my search history and recorded the session in my manual search log.
Evaluating What I Found
  • I evaluated each article using the MGU Source Evaluation Model, not just the peer-review filter.
  • I verified that key articles have not been retracted.
  • I noted limitations in methodology, sample, or scope that affect how I will use each source.

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 19.1An academic database is a curated, searchable collection of records describing scholarly and professional publications, built by professional indexers and distinct from both library catalogs and open web search engines.
  • Sec 19.2General databases support cross-disciplinary research and early orientation; subject databases offer greater depth, precise controlled vocabulary, and specialized publication types for research within a defined field.
  • Sec 19.3Reading abstracts critically before pursuing full text is one of the most efficient research habits available. Full text may come through direct database access, a library resolver, open access, or interlibrary loan.
  • Sec 19.4Database filters — peer review, date, publication type, methodology, and others — refine results systematically when applied one at a time and evaluated after each step.
  • Sec 19.5Search histories and manual search logs track what has been tried; search and citation alerts notify researchers automatically of new and newly cited publications.
  • Sec 19.6Citation export tools transfer record information directly into citation managers, saving time and reducing transcription errors — but exports must still be checked for accuracy.
  • Sec 19.7Databases offer curated depth, controlled vocabulary, and citation tracking, but have real limits: coverage gaps, publication lag, language and publication bias, and the imperfections of peer review itself.
  • Sec 19.8Choosing the right database is a deliberate process: clarify the question, identify the discipline, consult library research guides, match literature type to database, test the search, and search multiple databases for major projects.

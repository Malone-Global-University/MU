Chapter 3 — Front Matter

Research Skill Focus and Overview

This chapter teaches the foundational skill of inquiry-based thinking: beginning with questions rather than answers, following evidence into uncertainty, and allowing research to change what you think. Students who master inquiry-based thinking are ready to conduct honest, rigorous research in any discipline.

Why This Matters

Most students have been trained, in subtle ways, to look for the right answer. Tests have keys. Search engines return results ranked by popularity. Assignments often reward confident conclusions over careful uncertainty. Research works differently. Real research — the kind that builds knowledge, solves problems, and earns trust — begins before you know the answer. It begins with a question you cannot yet resolve, and it requires you to sit with uncertainty long enough to understand it. This chapter explains why research is a form of structured inquiry, not a retrieval exercise, and what to do when the evidence changes your mind — one of the most valuable things research can do for you. Understanding research as inquiry is the difference between using sources to support what you already believe and using sources to discover what the evidence actually shows.

Sections 3.1 – 3.4
Beginning With Questions: Curiosity, Uncertainty, and Background Knowledge
Inquiry vs. Lookup · Productive Curiosity · Exploratory Research · Background Knowledge
Section 3.1

Inquiry Before Answers

There is a difference between looking something up and inquiring into something.

Looking something up begins with a gap. You need a fact, a definition, a date, a name. You search, you find it, you close the browser. The process is quick, practical, and useful. There is nothing wrong with looking things up.

Inquiry begins differently. Inquiry begins with a problem you do not fully understand, a situation you find puzzling, a claim you cannot evaluate, or a question whose answer is not obvious. Inquiry means engaging with that uncertainty rather than immediately trying to close it.

When you look something up, you are filling a gap. When you inquire, you are exploring territory.

Example 3.1 — What Inquiry Looks Like in Practice
A claim about social media and GPA
  • Lookup You read a news article claiming that students who use social media heavily have lower GPAs than students who use it less. You search for the article, find the statistic, and move on. You now have a fact.
  • Inquiry You ask what study produced this finding, how large the sample was, whether correlation was mistaken for causation, what other variables might explain the pattern, and whether other researchers have found the same or different results. You now have the beginning of real understanding.
Principle: Academic research, professional decision-making, civic participation, and medical decision-making all require inquiry. Inquiry is not just a scholarly habit — it is a practical skill with consequences.

The Inquiry Posture

Inquiry requires a particular mental posture made up of five qualities, none of which are fixed personality traits — they are habits of mind that develop through practice.

QualityWhat It Means in Practice
OpennessYou do not know the answer yet, and you are willing to remain in that state long enough to look carefully
PatienceGood questions are not always answerable quickly; research takes time
SkepticismNot cynicism — skepticism means evaluating claims rather than accepting or dismissing them automatically
HumilityYou may be wrong; the evidence may show something different from what you expected, and that is progress, not failure
PersistenceResearch often leads to more questions before it reaches answers; following those questions rather than stopping at the first difficulty is essential
Study Note — Inquiry Still Reaches Conclusions

It would be easy to misread the emphasis on inquiry as suggesting that research never reaches conclusions. That is not what inquiry means. Research does reach conclusions, and evidence does point toward answers. The difference is that inquiry-based research reaches conclusions because the evidence supports them, not despite what the evidence shows.

The goal of inquiry is not permanent uncertainty. The goal is conclusions that are honest, evidence-based, and appropriately confident — no more certain than the evidence warrants, and no less willing to commit when the evidence is strong.

Section 3.2

Curiosity and Uncertainty

Curiosity is the engine of research. Without genuine curiosity — a real desire to understand something — research becomes a mechanical exercise. You gather sources, you fill a page, you cite correctly, but you learn nothing that matters to you. Curiosity is what makes research an intellectual experience rather than a clerical one.

Not all curiosity leads to good research. Curiosity that demands immediate resolution — that needs the answer in sixty seconds — tends to settle for the first plausible-sounding result rather than the best available evidence. Productive curiosity is willing to wait. It is comfortable sitting with a question long enough to understand it before seeking an answer, and it asks follow-up questions: when a fact surprises you, productive curiosity asks why; when a source makes a claim, it asks what evidence supports it; when two sources disagree, it wants to know who is right and why, not simply which one to cite.

Reframing Uncertainty

Students often treat uncertainty as a problem to be eliminated. If you do not know the answer, you feel behind. If sources conflict, you feel confused. Experienced researchers think about uncertainty differently: uncertainty is information. It tells you that the question is real — that the answer is not already settled and obvious. A research question with an easy answer is rarely worth researching.

Example 3.2 — A Fixed Question vs. a Genuine Inquiry
Comparing two questions about the same historical event
  • Question A What year was the Emancipation Proclamation issued?This has a fixed answer. It is a lookup, not an inquiry.
  • Question B To what extent did the Emancipation Proclamation change the lived experience of enslaved people in Confederate states during the Civil War?This is a genuine inquiry. It requires evidence, analysis, interpretation, and careful reasoning, and scholars have developed different emphases and perspectives on it.
Principle: Complexity is not a problem with a question — it is often the reason the question is worth asking.

Part of learning to research well is learning to tolerate ambiguity — the experience of holding two or more possibilities in mind without yet knowing which is correct. Ambiguity is uncomfortable; our minds prefer resolution. But premature resolution — settling on an answer before the evidence supports it — produces worse research than staying with the uncertainty a little longer. When you encounter ambiguity, practice asking: What would I need to know to resolve this? That question keeps your inquiry active and directed rather than stuck.

Section 3.3

Exploratory Research

Before you can ask a focused research question, you usually need to know enough about a topic to understand what kind of question is worth asking. That early stage of research is called exploratory research.

Exploratory research is open, broad, and provisional. Its purpose is orientation — getting enough of a map of a subject to figure out where the interesting questions are. It is not about finding sources you will cite in your final paper; it is about learning enough to research intelligently. Think of it the way you would think about arriving in a city you have never visited. Before you can find the best coffee shop, you need to know roughly where neighborhoods are, what transportation is available, and what the city is like. Exploratory research provides that orientation for an academic topic.

What Exploratory Research Looks Like

During exploratory research, you might read an overview article or encyclopedia entry to understand what a topic involves, skim several introductory sources to get a sense of the major debates and concepts, browse a library guide to see what kinds of sources exist on the topic, look at the table of contents of a relevant textbook, search broadly in a library database to see what has been written, or write down questions that the overview material raises but does not answer.

During this phase, you are not looking for evidence to support a claim. You are looking for understanding. This distinction matters because it changes what sources are useful. In exploratory research, a clear encyclopedia article is more valuable than a dense specialized journal article. An introductory textbook chapter is more useful than an expert monograph. The goal is breadth and orientation, not depth.

Common Mistake
Skipping exploratory research entirely

Many students move directly from the assignment prompt to a focused database search. This often produces confusion because they do not yet know which terms matter, what the debates are, or where the meaningful questions lie. Taking twenty to thirty minutes for exploratory research saves hours later.

Common Mistake
Treating exploratory sources as final sources

Wikipedia, encyclopedia entries, and overview articles are excellent for orientation but are generally not appropriate to cite in a finished academic paper. Their value is in the understanding they provide, not in the authority they carry.

Common Mistake
Stopping at exploration

Some students enjoy the broad reading of exploratory research and never move to focused inquiry. Exploration has to give way to a specific question eventually.

Section 3.4

Background Knowledge

Background knowledge is what you know before you begin a specific research project. It includes everything you have learned from prior coursework, reading, conversation, experience, and general education. Background knowledge shapes what questions you can ask, what sources you can evaluate, and how quickly you can understand new material.

Researchers with strong background knowledge in a subject can recognize when a claim is unusual or surprising relative to established knowledge, evaluate whether a source's evidence is plausible, identify what is missing from an argument, ask more specific and productive questions, and interpret technical language and disciplinary conventions. Researchers who are new to a subject — which includes most students at the beginning of a research project — have to build background knowledge before they can do strong research. This is not a deficiency; it is a stage every researcher goes through when entering new territory.

StrategyHow It Builds Background Knowledge Quickly
Read overview materialEncyclopedia articles, textbook introductions, and library guide pages can give you vocabulary, context, and a sense of what is contested in fifteen to thirty minutes
Follow the vocabularyTerms that appear repeatedly in overview sources are the words you will need to search effectively in databases
Look at what the overview citesWorks cited in an encyclopedia article or textbook chapter point you toward the most significant sources in a field
Ask a librarian or instructorA brief conversation with someone who knows the field can give you a map of a subject faster than independent browsing
Core Principle

People who know more about a subject notice more within it. A person with no medical knowledge who reads a clinical study will see words and numbers; a medical professional reading the same study sees patterns, evaluates methodology, notices gaps, and identifies what the results do and do not support. You will never have expert-level background knowledge when you begin a research project on an unfamiliar topic, but building even moderate background knowledge meaningfully improves the quality of your research.

Sections 3.5 – 3.6
Research as a Cycle: Following the Evidence Wherever It Leads
The Iterative Process · Signs of Progress · Revising Research Questions
Section 3.5

Research as a Cycle

Students often imagine research as a straight line: pick a topic, find sources, read them, write the paper. Research rarely works this way in practice.

Real research is cyclical. Each phase of research generates questions that send you back to earlier phases. Finding a useful source raises new questions. Reading one article reveals that your search terms were slightly wrong. Trying to write a section reveals a gap in your evidence that requires more searching. Revising your argument changes what kinds of sources you need. This cycling is not inefficiency — it is how research actually works, and understanding it as normal prevents a great deal of frustration.

The Research Cycle

Ask a question → Search for sources → Read and evaluate → Discover new questions → Refine your question → Search again → Read and synthesize → Find gaps → Search again → Conclude

In practice, you may circle through several iterations of this process before reaching a stable, evidence-supported conclusion. Each pass through the cycle should improve your understanding, sharpen your question, and move your evidence closer to adequacy.

Signs the Cycle Is WorkingSigns the Cycle Is Stuck
Your research question becomes more specific as you learn moreYour question has not changed since you started, but your evidence does not support it well
You are finding sources you had not anticipatedYou keep finding the same three sources no matter what you search
You can explain what the evidence shows and what it does not showYou are reading sources but not taking notes because you are not sure what matters
You evaluate new sources more quickly as background knowledge growsYou feel overwhelmed and cannot identify what is relevant
Your working thesis is changing in response to what you findYou are avoiding sources that challenge your working thesis
Study Note — When the Cycle Stalls

When the cycle stalls, the most useful next step is usually to step back and do a round of exploratory reading, or to ask a librarian or instructor for help identifying what you might be missing.

Section 3.6

Revising Questions

The research question you start with is rarely the research question you end with. Good researchers expect their questions to change as they learn more about a subject. Revising a question is not a failure; it is a sign that inquiry is working.

Why Questions Need to ChangeWhat This Looks Like
The topic is larger than expectedA question that seemed focused turns out to encompass so many variables, populations, and platforms that it cannot be researched well in a semester — the question must narrow
The topic is smaller than expectedA question about an obscure event may not have enough scholarly literature to support a substantial paper — the question must broaden or shift
The evidence points elsewhereA different aspect of the subject turns out to be better documented, more significant, or more interesting than anticipated
A key term means something differentA concept used loosely turns out to have a more precise meaning in the relevant field, and that precision changes what must be investigated
You find genuine disagreement where you expected consensusThis is exciting — if scholars disagree, you have a real research question
Example 3.6 — Narrowing, Broadening, and Reframing
Three directions of question revision
  • Narrowing How does social media affect teenagers? → How does Instagram use affect self-reported anxiety levels among adolescent girls aged 13 to 17 in the United States?Making the question more specific.
  • Broadening What was the economic impact of the 1873 cholera outbreak on the textile workers of Lyon, France? → How did cholera epidemics affect French labor markets in the 19th century?Making the question more general when the original was too narrow to find adequate evidence for.
  • Reframing Does X cause Y? → Does Y predict X?Keeping the general subject area but approaching it from a different angle because the evidence suggests the relationship runs the other way.
Principle: Strong research questions often go through three, four, or five revisions before they stabilize.
Study Note — Revising Productively

To revise productively: (1) write down what your question was when you started; (2) write down what you now know that you did not know then; (3) write down what you still do not know; (4) ask whether your original question still makes sense given what you have learned, and if not, what a better question would look like. This is not a one-time process.

Section 3.7

When Research Changes Your Mind

One of the most valuable things research can do is show you that what you believed was wrong, incomplete, or more complicated than you had understood. This is uncomfortable. It is also the clearest sign that research is working.

The Confirmation Trap

Human beings have a well-documented tendency to seek evidence that confirms what they already believe and to discount or ignore evidence that challenges it. In research, this looks like forming a thesis early and then looking only for sources that support it; dismissing contradicting sources as biased or poorly done; accepting supporting sources without the same scrutiny; and finishing with a paper full of supporting evidence and a thesis that was never seriously tested. The result is research that is unconvincing to anyone who does not already agree with you — and intellectually dishonest, even when unintentional.

Counterevidence — evidence that challenges your working thesis — is among the most valuable material you can find during research. It tells you that the question is genuinely contested (if there were no counterevidence, there would be no real question to investigate); that your thesis needs to be more precise (sometimes counterevidence reveals not that your thesis is wrong but that it is too absolute); and that your understanding of the subject is deepening (the student who can explain why a reasonable person might disagree with their thesis — and explain it fairly — understands the subject far better than one who has never encountered disagreement).

How to Handle Counterevidence

StepWhat to Do
Take it seriously firstRead the source carefully; understand the evidence as well as you can before deciding what to do with it
Ask whether it is soundIs the methodology appropriate? Is the source credible? Is the evidence strong? If weak, you may be able to explain why it does not change your conclusion — but be honest about that reasoning
Ask whether it changes your thesisSometimes counterevidence is strong enough that the honest response is to revise your working thesis — that is not a failure
Engage it in your paperStrong papers address the counterargument: explain it and show why the overall body of evidence still supports the conclusion
Example 3.7 — A Worked Example of a Changed Mind
Extended school days and student achievement
  • Start You begin a project believing that longer school days improve student achievement, based on a news article you read.
  • Evidence You find studies supporting this claim, studies that show no significant effect, studies that distinguish elementary from secondary students, studies finding improved outcomes only when instructional time improves rather than merely increasing, and studies identifying costs to student wellbeing.
  • Revised view Extended school days appear to improve achievement for elementary students in low-income districts when accompanied by curriculum improvements, but the evidence for secondary students is mixed, and benefits must be weighed against effects on student health and extracurricular participation.
Principle: This is not the same thesis you started with. It is better — more honest, more accurate, and more supported by the actual evidence. Research changed your mind in the direction of truth.
Section 3.8

Responsible Conclusions

Inquiry reaches conclusions. Good research does not end in permanent uncertainty. But the conclusions that research produces must be responsible — proportional to the evidence, honestly qualified, and clear about what remains unknown.

Quality of a Responsible ConclusionWhat It Requires
ProportionalityThe confidence of a conclusion should match the strength of the evidence — claiming certainty when evidence is limited, or understating strong evidence, are both failures
QualificationAcknowledging what you do not know; naming exactly where a conclusion holds and where it may not
Honesty about gapsNaming what remains unknown rather than papering over it with confident-sounding language
Engagement with counterevidenceExplaining why the weight of evidence supports your conclusion even though contrary evidence exists, without pretending it does not exist
Avoiding overreachStaying within the scope of the evidence — a study of one population does not support claims about all populations

The Language of Responsible Conclusions

Academic and professional researchers use language that signals the appropriate degree of confidence. Learning this language helps you express conclusions responsibly.

Strength of EvidenceResponsible Language
Very strong, replicated, consistentThe evidence shows / Research consistently finds / It is well established that
Moderate, multiple studies, some variationEvidence suggests / Research indicates / Studies tend to find
Preliminary, single study, or limited scopeOne study found / Preliminary evidence indicates / This finding suggests, though further research is needed
Mixed or contestedEvidence is mixed / Researchers disagree / Some studies find X, while others find Y
Very limitedLittle research exists / The available evidence is insufficient to conclude
Core Principle

Uncertainty, when it honestly reflects the state of the evidence, is a form of intellectual integrity. You are not failing to conclude — you are accurately reporting what can and cannot be concluded. Responsible researchers often say something like: This is what the evidence shows. This is what remains unclear. This is why. That is not a weak conclusion. It is the most trustworthy kind.

Search Practice

Activity 3.1From Casual to Inquiry

Choose one of the following starting points and transform it from a casual lookup into an inquiry-based research question by asking follow-up questions, identifying what you do not know, and noting what kind of evidence might help:

  1. "Coffee is bad for you."
  2. "Electric cars are better for the environment."
  3. "Students learn better in small classes."

Using the MGU Library encyclopedia or a reference database, spend fifteen minutes doing exploratory research on one of these topics: the psychology of memory, labor unions in the 20th century, or climate change adaptation strategies. After your exploration, write down three terms or concepts you had not encountered before, two specific questions raised but not answered by what you read, and one aspect of the topic you think would be worth researching further.

Activity 3.3Tracing a Question Through the Cycle

Interview someone — a student, a faculty member, a librarian — who has completed a research project. Ask them what question they started with, how their question changed as they researched, what surprised them most about what they found, and whether the evidence changed what they thought. Write a one-paragraph reflection on what you learned about the research cycle from this conversation.

Source Check

Evaluating a Source at the Inquiry Stage. At the exploratory stage of research, the sources that are most valuable are those that orient you to a topic efficiently. Consider this hypothetical source: an entry in a general encyclopedia on "Sleep and Academic Performance," approximately 1,200 words, written by a staff editor, reviewed by an academic advisory board, last updated eighteen months ago, summarizing several research areas, defining key terms, and including a bibliography of seven scholarly articles.

Ask: Is this source useful for orientation — what does it help you understand? Is this an appropriate final source, or is it better used as background reading? What does the bibliography give you, and how might you use it? What questions does this entry raise that it does not answer? What would your next step be?

Research Notebook

The research notebook is where you record your inquiry as it develops. For this chapter, your notebook entries should capture the process of questioning, exploring, and revising.

Date: [Date] Topic area: Sleep and academic performance in college students Starting question: Does sleep deprivation affect college student GPA? What I assumed before searching: Yes, obviously — tired students can't perform well. What exploratory reading showed me: - Sleep deprivation is defined differently in different studies (less than 6 hours vs. less than 7 hours per night) - GPA is a crude outcome measure; some studies use test performance, attention, memory - Effects may differ for students with sleep disorders vs. otherwise healthy students - Social and behavioral factors (alcohol, social media at night) complicate the relationship New questions raised: 1. How do researchers measure sleep deprivation in students — self-report or actigraphy? 2. Is the relationship between sleep and performance linear, or does it plateau? 3. Do some students function better with less sleep than others? Revised question: What does the experimental and observational literature show about the relationship between chronic sleep restriction and cognitive performance in college-aged adults? Terms to search: sleep restriction, college students, cognitive performance, academic achievement, executive function, sleep duration Source: Encyclopedia of Sleep Medicine (overview entry) - Useful for orientation only; not for citation in final paper - Bibliography points to several large cohort studies worth finding - No information on intervention studies (sleep improvement programs)

This kind of notebook entry records where you started, documents what you learned in exploratory research, shows how your question changed, captures new search terms, and notes the limitations of the source you used. Keep entries like this throughout your research — they are invaluable when you sit down to write.

Misinformation Alert

Alert
Confirming What You Already Believe

One of the most common forms of research failure is not encountering false information — it is selectively gathering true information. Confirmation bias leads researchers to find sources that support their pre-existing views while overlooking, dismissing, or failing to search for contrary evidence. This is especially dangerous in the digital environment, where search algorithms learn your preferences and tend to return results consistent with your prior searches and clicks.

Watch for these warning signs:

  • All of your sources agree with each other and with your thesis
  • You have not encountered any credible evidence that contradicts your position
  • When you find a contrary source, your first instinct is to find a reason to dismiss it
  • You feel confident before you have searched comprehensively

What to do: Search explicitly for contrary evidence. If your thesis is X causes Y, search for "X does not cause Y" or "limitations of X Y research" or "criticism of X Y hypothesis." This is called adversarial searching, and it is one of the most important habits you can develop. The goal is not to find reasons to abandon your thesis — it is to ensure your thesis has been tested against the strongest available counterevidence.

The MGU Misinformation Check applied to your own research: What is my claim? What evidence am I offering? What am I missing? Can another reliable source confirm my main claim? Is my confidence stronger than my evidence warrants? Am I fairly representing sources that complicate my thesis?

Study Note — Exploratory Sources and Citation

Encyclopedia articles, Wikipedia, and general overview materials typically do not appear in the final works cited of an academic paper. But even if you will not cite them, recording where you found useful orientation is valuable for your research log — it helps you retrace your thinking if you need to.

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 3.1Research is a form of structured inquiry that begins with questions rather than answers. Unlike casual lookup, inquiry requires openness, patience, skepticism, humility, and persistence — and it still reaches conclusions, just ones earned through evidence.
  • Sec 3.2Productive curiosity drives research forward. Uncertainty is information, not a problem to eliminate quickly — it signals that a question is genuinely open and worth investigating. Tolerating ambiguity is an essential research skill.
  • Sec 3.3Exploratory research orients you to a new subject before focused searching begins. It builds background knowledge and vocabulary using sources like encyclopedias and overview articles — valuable for orientation even though they will not appear in your final works cited.
  • Sec 3.4Background knowledge shapes what you can notice, what questions you can ask, and how quickly you can evaluate new sources. Building moderate background knowledge in an unfamiliar area significantly improves research quality.
  • Sec 3.5The research process is cyclical, not linear. Each phase generates new questions that send you back to earlier phases. Understanding this cycle as normal prevents frustration and keeps inquiry active.
  • Sec 3.6Research questions should be revised as understanding develops. Narrowing, broadening, and reframing are all forms of productive revision that move a question toward a more honest and researchable form.
  • Sec 3.7When research changes your mind, that is the process working. Counterevidence tests a thesis, deepens understanding, and strengthens conclusions. Engaging honestly with contrary evidence is a mark of intellectual integrity.
  • Sec 3.8Responsible conclusions are proportional to the evidence, appropriately qualified, honest about gaps, and willing to name uncertainty. Strong research requires conclusions as confident as the evidence warrants — no more, no less.

Key Terms

adversarial searching
Deliberately searching for evidence that challenges or contradicts your working thesis in order to test its strength against the best available counterevidence.
background knowledge
The knowledge a researcher brings to a new project based on prior learning, reading, coursework, and experience. Shapes what questions can be asked and how quickly new material can be evaluated.
confirmation bias
The tendency to seek, interpret, and remember information in ways that confirm existing beliefs while discounting information that challenges them.
counterevidence
Evidence that challenges or contradicts a working thesis or established claim. Engaging with counterevidence strengthens research conclusions.
exploratory research
An early, open-ended stage of research aimed at orientation: building enough background knowledge to understand what kind of question is worth asking and where significant sources exist.
inquiry
A structured process of investigation that begins with questions, follows evidence, and reaches conclusions proportional to what the evidence supports.
inquiry cycle
The iterative, cyclical nature of research in which each phase (questioning, searching, reading, revising) generates new questions that send the researcher back to earlier phases.
proportionality
The principle that the confidence of a conclusion should match the strength of the evidence supporting it — no more certain than the evidence warrants, no less committed when the evidence is strong.
qualification
Explicitly naming the limits of a conclusion: what population it applies to, what conditions it requires, what evidence would be needed to extend it.
working thesis
A provisional, evolving claim that guides a research project but remains open to revision based on what the evidence shows.

Research Practice

Choose a topic from your current coursework that you have not yet researched formally and complete the following sequence.

Step 1Starting Assumptions

Write down in three to five sentences what you currently believe or assume about this topic. Do not research yet — just record what you think you know.

Step 2Exploratory Reading

Spend twenty minutes reading one or two overview sources on this topic (an encyclopedia entry, a library guide, or an introductory chapter). Do not use a search engine yet. Use the MGU Encyclopedia or a reference database.

Step 3Post-Exploration Notes

After your exploratory reading, write three things you learned that you did not know before, two terms you encountered that you will need to understand better, and two questions raised by your reading that remain unanswered.

Source Evaluation Practice

Locate two sources on the same topic — one appropriate for exploratory research (an encyclopedia or overview article) and one more appropriate for advanced research (a peer-reviewed scholarly article or an official report). For each source, complete the MGU Source Evaluation Model below.

Information Literacy Reflection

This chapter has argued that research begins with questions, not answers, and that changing your mind when evidence warrants it is a sign of intellectual integrity, not weakness.

Reflection 1
Examining a Held Belief

Think of a belief you currently hold — about politics, health, society, education, or any other subject. How confident are you in this belief? What evidence supports it? Have you ever seriously searched for evidence that challenges it?

Reflection 2
A Time You Changed Your Mind

Describe a time when you changed your mind about something because of new information. What was the experience like? What did the new information show you that you had not considered before?

Reflection 3
Your Inquiry Habits

Where do you most struggle with the inquiry habits described in this chapter — tolerating uncertainty, searching for counterevidence, revising your question, reaching conclusions proportional to the evidence? What is one concrete step you could take to strengthen that particular habit?

Reflection 4
Earning Trust in Information

How does the idea of earning trust in information — rather than simply finding it — change how you think about research as a task and as a responsibility?

A Note on These Reflections

There are no wrong answers to these questions. They are designed to help you understand where you are starting from so you can develop the habits of inquiry this textbook teaches.

Looking Ahead
Chapter 4: Information Ecosystems

Chapter 3 established research as a form of structured inquiry — beginning with questions, cycling through evidence, and reaching conclusions proportional to what that evidence shows. Chapter 4 examines the modern information landscape in which that inquiry takes place: how books, scholarly articles, news media, websites, social media, data sets, archives, and AI tools differ from one another. Understanding these differences will help you choose the right kind of source for the right stage of your research, and will prepare you for the source-evaluation skills developed throughout Part III and Part V of this textbook.

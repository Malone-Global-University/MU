Chapter 2 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Every day you encounter hundreds of pieces of information. News articles. Social media posts. Text messages. Product reviews. Scientific headlines. Political claims. Health advice. Each one asks something of you: your attention, your belief, your action, your money, your vote.

Most people receive information passively. They read, scroll, listen, and form impressions without stopping to ask how that information was produced, who produced it, what it is trying to do, and whether it holds up to scrutiny. Information literacy is the skill that changes this. It is the difference between being swept along by information and being able to navigate it deliberately. It does not mean doubting everything. It means knowing how to ask good questions, how to find trustworthy answers, and how to use what you learn honestly and responsibly.

Information literacy is not a single skill. It is a cluster of connected abilities: finding information efficiently, evaluating what you find, using what you learn with integrity, creating new information carefully, and sharing it responsibly. These abilities reinforce each other. A person who can find information but cannot evaluate it is not truly information literate. A person who can evaluate information but does not know how to use it responsibly can cause harm even with good intentions.

This textbook treats information literacy as one of the most important skills a student can develop — not because it serves one class or one assignment, but because it serves every class, every career, every citizenship decision, and every personal choice you will make for the rest of your life.

Opening Question

Imagine two people read the same viral claim about a health remedy. The first person checks whether it has been confirmed by other reliable sources, traces it to its original context, and decides how much weight to give it. The second person shares it immediately because it confirms something they already suspected. Both spent the same few seconds. Which person practiced information literacy — and what specifically did they do differently? By the end of this chapter, you will be able to answer that question precisely, and you will understand why the five abilities introduced here apply to nearly every information decision you make.

Section 2.1

What Information Literacy Is

Information literacy is a term used in education, librarianship, and professional training to describe a set of abilities related to information: knowing when you need it, knowing how to find it, knowing how to evaluate it, knowing how to use it responsibly, and knowing how to create and share new information carefully.

The term has been defined in various ways by different organizations. The American Library Association described information literacy in 1989 as the ability to recognize an information need and to locate, evaluate, and effectively use the needed information. More recent frameworks, including those developed by the Association of College and Research Libraries and other bodies, have expanded this definition to emphasize critical thinking, ethical use, social context, and the active, reflective practices of knowledge creation.

For this textbook, information literacy means the ability to recognize when you need information and what kind you need, find information efficiently using appropriate tools and strategies, evaluate information for relevance, accuracy, credibility, authority, and purpose, use information responsibly by giving credit and representing sources fairly, create new information carefully and honestly, and share information in ways that respect others and contribute to honest public knowledge.

These abilities are not steps you complete once and move on. They are recursive. You recognize a need, search, evaluate what you find, revise your understanding of what you need, search again, evaluate again. Information literacy is a practice, not a checklist.

Information Literacy Is Not Simply Knowing How to Search

A common misunderstanding is that information literacy is the same as knowing how to use a search engine. It is not. Almost anyone can type words into a search bar and receive results. What most people cannot do efficiently or reliably is determine which results are worth reading, judge whether a source is trustworthy for a specific purpose, identify when a claim is misleading even if it comes from a familiar source, understand how the search system itself shapes what they see, and use what they find without misrepresenting it. Search skill is one component of information literacy. It is a small part of a much larger set of abilities.

Information Literacy Is Not Simply Avoiding Plagiarism

Another common misunderstanding is that information literacy is mainly about citation rules and plagiarism. Citation and academic integrity are important, and this textbook covers them in Part VII. But information literacy is much broader. It includes how you find information, how you evaluate it, how you understand it, how you organize and synthesize it, and how you contribute to knowledge — not just how you document sources at the end.

Information Literacy Is Contextual

Information literacy looks different depending on your field, your question, and your purpose. A historian evaluating a primary source uses different skills than a nurse evaluating a clinical study, a business analyst evaluating a market report, or a citizen evaluating a political claim. All of them are exercising information literacy, but the specific standards, tools, and methods differ. This means information literacy is not a fixed formula — it is a set of transferable principles applied differently depending on the situation.

Section 2.2

Finding Information

Finding information sounds simple. In some ways it has become easier than ever. Almost any topic you can name has thousands of sources available somewhere online within seconds of a search. The challenge is not access. The challenge is knowing where to look for the kind of information you need, how to search effectively, how to recognize useful results among many irrelevant ones, and how to reach sources that are not immediately visible on the open web.

The information environment is vast and uneven. Information does not exist in one place. It exists in libraries, databases, archives, government records, research repositories, news organizations, academic journals, websites, social media platforms, private documents, multimedia collections, and more. Each of these spaces is organized differently, requires different search strategies, and contains different types of information. Some information is freely available. Some requires library access. Some is behind paywalls. Some is only in physical collections. Some is in languages other than the one you are searching in. Some exists but has not been digitized. Information literacy includes understanding this landscape — not memorizing every tool, but knowing that different kinds of questions require different kinds of sources and different search approaches.

Study Note — Recognizing When You Need Information

Before you can find information, you need to recognize that you need it and understand what kind you need. Some needs are clear: a research paper on climate policy requires peer-reviewed studies. Other needs are less obvious. You hear a statistic quoted in conversation — do you need to verify it? You read about a new medical treatment — do you need the original study?

Part of information literacy is pausing to notice when a decision would benefit from better information, and then acting on that recognition instead of moving forward on unexamined grounds.

Search strategy is a skill. Part IV of this textbook covers search strategy in depth. For now, understand that effective searching involves translating your question into useful search terms, choosing appropriate search tools such as library catalogs, academic databases, search engines, and reference works, using functions like Boolean operators, filters, and subject headings to refine results, evaluating results quickly to determine which deserve closer attention, and following source trails such as bibliographies, citations, and related sources to find additional material. These are learnable skills. They improve with practice. Students who develop strong search skills spend less time finding irrelevant material and more time reading and thinking.

Section 2.3

Evaluating Information

Finding information is only the beginning. Once you have sources in front of you, you must evaluate them. This is where many students — and many adults — struggle most. Evaluation means asking hard questions about a source before you accept it, use it, or pass it along to others.

Evaluation is difficult for several reasons. First, the volume of information available makes it impossible to evaluate everything thoroughly; you must develop judgment about which sources deserve deep scrutiny and which can be assessed quickly. Second, misleading or false information is often produced to look credible — it may appear on professional-looking websites, use technical-sounding language, include graphs and citations, and be shared by people you trust. Surface appearance is not a reliable guide to reliability. Third, your own prior beliefs, emotions, and preferences affect how you evaluate information; people evaluate information they agree with less critically than information they disagree with, and information literacy requires recognizing this tendency in yourself and working against it. Fourth, the context of a source matters enormously, and context is often missing or distorted when sources are shared online. A quote from a scientific paper may be accurate in isolation but deeply misleading when removed from its surrounding argument, methodology, and limitations.

The MGU Source Evaluation Model
Six questions to ask about every source
  • RelevanceDoes this source address my actual question — its topic, time period, geographic scope, and population? Relevant-seeming sources are sometimes interesting but beside the point.
  • AuthorityWho created this information? What are their credentials and experience? Are they speaking within their area of expertise? Authority is not a guarantee of truth, but it is a meaningful signal.
  • AccuracyIs the information verifiable? Are claims supported by evidence that actually supports the conclusions? Does it match what other reliable sources say?
  • PurposeWhy was this information created — to inform, persuade, sell, entertain, or advocate? Purpose shapes what is included, what is omitted, and how the source should be read.
  • CurrencyWhen was this created or last updated? Currency is crucial for scientific, medical, legal, and current-events topics, and less important for historical or classical topics.
  • ContextWhat larger conversation or body of knowledge does this source belong to? Is it being accurately represented when it is cited or shared?
These six questions form the foundation you will apply to every source throughout this textbook.
Evaluation Is Not Cynicism

Information literacy does not teach you to distrust everything or to believe that all sources are equally unreliable. Some sources are genuinely more trustworthy than others. Peer-reviewed research, established news organizations with editorial standards, government statistical agencies, and expert institutions are generally more reliable than anonymous social media posts, personal opinion blogs, or ideologically motivated advocacy sites — not always, but systematically and significantly.

The goal of evaluation is calibrated judgment: giving appropriate weight to evidence that deserves it, withholding trust from sources that have not earned it, and recognizing the difference.

Section 2.4

Using Information Responsibly

Finding and evaluating information is not enough. What you do with information matters. Information literacy includes using information ethically, honestly, and carefully.

Giving credit. When you use someone else's ideas, words, data, images, or arguments in your own work, you must acknowledge where those elements came from. This is true in academic writing, professional reports, journalism, and public communication. Giving credit is not merely a technical requirement — it is a matter of intellectual honesty. Failing to give credit is plagiarism, even when unintentional. It misrepresents your work as original when it is not, denies recognition to the person whose work you used, and can mislead readers about the sources of your claims. Part VII of this textbook covers citation and academic integrity in full.

Representing sources fairly. Giving credit is not enough if you misrepresent what a source actually says. Information literacy includes quoting accurately, paraphrasing faithfully, and not distorting a source's meaning by taking it out of context, emphasizing one claim while ignoring qualifications, or attributing positions to sources that do not actually hold them. This is a subtler form of intellectual dishonesty than plagiarism, but it is dishonesty nonetheless. Academic work and public discourse depend on sources being accurately represented.

Using information for its intended purpose. Some information comes with restrictions. Data collected from human subjects under privacy protections should not be repurposed in ways that violate that privacy. Information shared in confidence should not be broadcast publicly. Research conducted under an institutional protocol should not be extended beyond its approved scope. Responsible information use includes respecting the conditions under which information was gathered and shared.

Recognizing when information could cause harm. Information literacy includes judgment about consequences. Sharing information you know to be false can cause harm. Spreading unverified medical claims can lead people to make dangerous health decisions. Publishing personal information about individuals without consent can expose them to harassment. Amplifying misleading statistics can distort public debate. Responsible use of information means thinking not only about what you believe to be true, but about the likely effects of communicating it.

Section 2.5

Creating and Sharing Information

Information literacy is not only about consuming information others have produced. It is also about producing information yourself — and doing so responsibly.

You are already an information producer. Every time you write a paper, send a message, post online, give a presentation, or share a source with someone, you are producing and distributing information. Information literacy applies to what you create, not just what you find. This matters more than it once did. Digital publishing tools allow individuals to distribute information to large audiences at little cost. A social media post can reach thousands of people. A website can appear in search results alongside established news sources. A video can accumulate millions of views. The ability to reach an audience has been democratized — but so has the ability to spread misinformation.

StandardWhat It Requires
AccuracyMaking sure your claims are supported by evidence
AttributionGiving credit for ideas, data, and words that are not your own
FairnessRepresenting others' positions accurately, even when you disagree with them
TransparencyBeing clear about your own perspective, interests, and limitations
CareChecking facts before publishing, especially when a claim could cause harm if wrong
The Information Literacy Cycle

You find and evaluate information produced by others. You synthesize and organize it. You create new information — a paper, a presentation, a report, a social media post. Others find and evaluate what you produced. The cycle continues.

This means the quality of the information environment depends on everyone who participates in it. When individuals create and share information responsibly, the environment improves. When they do not, it degrades. Information literacy is a collective as well as an individual responsibility.

Section 2.6

Information Literacy in School and Work

Information literacy is not an abstract skill you use only in research classes. It is directly applicable to every course you take and every professional context you enter.

In academic work, every assignment that involves finding sources, reading, writing, presenting, or arguing requires information literacy. Research papers are the obvious case, but information literacy also matters for reading assigned texts critically rather than accepting all claims at face value, evaluating case studies and data sets used in textbooks, assessing claims made in class discussions and lectures, choosing and using sources for lab reports and policy briefs, understanding methodology sections in research articles, and recognizing when evidence presented in a course is contested, outdated, or incomplete. Students who develop information literacy do better academic work across all disciplines because they can engage with sources rather than simply repeat them.

FieldInformation Literacy in Practice
HealthcareEvaluating clinical studies, drug information, public health data, and patient records
BusinessAssessing market research, financial reports, industry data, and competitor information
Law and PolicyReading statutes, court decisions, regulatory guidance, and expert testimony
JournalismVerifying claims, tracing sources, and distinguishing reliable from unreliable information under deadline pressure
EducationEvaluating curriculum materials, research on learning, and information students bring into the classroom

Social Work and CounselingAssessing information about clients, research on interventions, and legal requirements

In nearly every field, professionals who cannot evaluate information are dependent on others to tell them what to think. Information literacy supports professional autonomy and competence. Most fields also change significantly over the course of a career, and knowledge that was current when you completed your training may be outdated a decade later. Information literacy is what makes career-long learning possible. If you can find, evaluate, and use new information responsibly throughout your career, you can stay current. If you cannot, you are dependent on others to filter information for you — and those filters may serve their interests rather than yours.

Section 2.7

Information Literacy in Civic Life

Democratic societies depend on citizens who can make informed decisions about political candidates, public policies, social issues, and collective challenges. Those decisions require access to reliable information and the ability to evaluate competing claims.

The quality of democratic deliberation depends significantly on the quality of information available to citizens and on citizens' ability to evaluate that information. When citizens cannot distinguish reliable from unreliable information, they are vulnerable to manipulation. Propaganda, misinformation, and deliberately misleading political communication are not new phenomena, but the digital information environment has made them faster, cheaper, and more targeted than ever before. Information literacy is one of the primary defenses against this. A citizen who can identify misleading statistics, trace a claim to its original source, recognize emotional manipulation, and seek out evidence that contradicts their initial impressions is harder to deceive than one who cannot.

Civic Information Questions
Questions citizens regularly need to evaluate
  • EffectsWhat are the likely effects of a proposed policy?
  • ConsensusWhat do scientists actually say about a contested empirical question?
  • FundingWho is funding a political organization or a media outlet?
  • StatisticsIs the statistic being cited accurate and fairly interpreted?
  • Full PictureDoes the story I am seeing represent the full picture, or only one side?
  • MotiveWho benefits from me believing this claim?
These questions do not require professional expertise — only the consistent habit of asking them and following the evidence.

Media literacy — the ability to understand how media institutions work, how news is produced, and how media content shapes public understanding — is a component of information literacy in the civic context. Understanding editorial standards, the distinction between news and opinion, the economics of attention-based media, and the dynamics of social media amplification helps citizens read media more carefully. This textbook addresses media literacy directly in Chapter 31, Algorithms, Virality, and Online Attention, and Chapter 32, Fact-Checking and Verification Methods.

Civic Information Literacy Is Not Partisan

Information literacy does not tell you what to believe about contested policy questions. It teaches you how to find evidence, evaluate competing claims, and form beliefs on the basis of that evidence rather than on the basis of who presented the claim most compellingly. A conservative and a progressive can both be information literate. They may weigh evidence differently and reach different conclusions. But if they are information literate, they will at least be engaging with the same factual record, acknowledging the limits of their own knowledge, and forming views on the basis of evidence rather than tribal loyalty or emotional appeal.

Section 2.8

Information Literacy in a Digital World

The digital information environment has transformed what information literacy means in practice. The volume, speed, reach, and variety of information available have all increased dramatically. So have the risks: misinformation spreads faster, manipulation is more sophisticated, and the infrastructure of the information environment — search algorithms, recommendation systems, social media platforms — shapes what people see in ways that are often invisible to users.

The scale problem. The amount of information produced each day is beyond any individual's capacity to process. This creates selection pressures: people and institutions that capture attention compete to be seen. This competition does not favor accuracy or depth. It often favors speed, emotional resonance, novelty, conflict, and simplicity. Information literacy in this environment means developing strategies for navigating scale — how to search efficiently, how to evaluate quickly, how to identify sources worth investing reading time in, and how to resist the pressure to share before verifying.

The personalization problem. Search engines and social media platforms use algorithmic systems to personalize what each user sees. These systems are designed to maximize engagement — to show you content you are likely to click on, react to, and spend time with. They are not designed to ensure that you see accurate, balanced, or important information. The result is that two people searching the same terms or using the same platform may see very different information environments. Each may believe their view of the world is accurate because it matches what they consistently see, without realizing that what they consistently see has been shaped by a system that amplifies their existing preferences. Information literacy includes understanding personalization and actively seeking out information beyond the algorithmically curated feed. This is the subject of Chapter 31.

AI-generated information. Artificial intelligence tools can now produce text, images, audio, and video that mimic human-created content with increasing plausibility. These tools can generate plausible-sounding but false information, fabricate citations, produce images that appear authentic but are entirely synthetic, and create audio and video of people saying things they never said. Information literacy in the current environment requires awareness of AI-generated content: how to recognize its markers, how to verify claims that may have been produced by AI systems, and how to use AI tools responsibly in your own research. Chapter 45 addresses responsible AI use in research, and Chapter 30 addresses deepfakes and synthetic visual media.

The Opportunity Side

The digital environment also creates significant opportunities for information literacy. Access to primary sources that once required travel to archives is now often available online. Databases of peer-reviewed research are increasingly open access. Fact-checking organizations publish their findings publicly. Government data is available for free download. These opportunities are real, but they require information literacy to use well — a primary source accessible online is only useful if you know how to find it, evaluate it, and understand it in context.

New Sub-LiteracyWhat It Develops
Data literacyThe ability to understand, evaluate, and interpret numerical and statistical information
Visual literacyThe ability to evaluate images, charts, maps, and other visual information critically
Media literacyThe ability to understand how media institutions work and how media content is produced
Algorithmic literacyThe ability to understand how algorithmic systems shape what information you see
AI literacyThe ability to understand how AI tools work and to use them responsibly
Core Principle

Each new sub-literacy represents an application of the same core information literacy principles — find, evaluate, use, create, share — to a specific kind of information or information environment. The principles do not change. What changes is the terrain on which you apply them.

Source Check

Practice Source Evaluation: A Web Article on Information Literacy. Suppose you found the following source while searching for information literacy resources: a blog post titled "7 Tips to Be More Information Literate," published on an education technology company's website. The post has no named author. It includes a list of practical tips but no citations. The company sells software to schools. The post was published two years ago.

CriterionEvaluation
RelevancePotentially relevant — the topic matches. But the advice may be shaped by the company's product interests.
AuthorityWeak. No named author. No credentials given. The company is a commercial entity, not a research institution.
AccuracyCannot verify without citations. Some tips may be sound; others may reflect commercial interests.
PurposeLikely commercial. A company publishing education content often does so to attract school customers or build brand credibility.
CurrencyTwo years ago. For basic concepts, this may be acceptable. For cutting-edge research, it may not be.
ContextMissing. The article floats without connection to the broader scholarly conversation about information literacy.

Conclusion: This source might be useful for generating initial ideas or comparisons, but it should not be used as a primary source in academic work. Its authority and evidence quality are too weak. A stronger source for the same topic would be a peer-reviewed article or book chapter on information literacy from a library science or education journal, written by named researchers with relevant credentials and citing prior research.

Misinformation Alert

Misinformation Alert

Information Trap
The "I Can Tell by Looking" Fallacy

One of the most common information literacy mistakes is believing that you can identify reliable information by how it looks. Students — and adults — often assume that a professional-looking website means the information is trustworthy, that a source using technical language must be accurate, that a source agreeing with what they already believe is probably right, and that information shared thousands of times must be true. None of these assumptions are reliable. Misinformation is often professionally packaged. Technical language can obscure weak evidence. Prior beliefs are not a guide to accuracy. Viral content spreads because it is emotionally compelling, not because it is true.

The Habit to Build

When you encounter information that you are about to accept or share, pause and ask: have I actually checked this, or am I responding to how it looks and feels? Information literacy teaches you to evaluate sources on the basis of evidence, not appearance. This is harder than it sounds — our brains are designed to make quick judgments based on surface features — but it is essential.

Key Terms

information literacy
The ability to recognize when information is needed and to find, evaluate, use, create, and share information responsibly.
relevance
The degree to which a source addresses the specific question, topic, time period, and scope of a research project.
authority
The expertise, credentials, and institutional standing of a source's creator, relevant to the specific claims being made.
accuracy
The correctness and verifiability of the information in a source, assessed by checking claims against evidence and other reliable sources.
purpose
The reason a source was created — to inform, persuade, sell, entertain, or advocate — which shapes how its content should be read.
currency
The timeliness of a source, assessed in relation to whether recency matters for the specific research question.
context
The larger intellectual, historical, or social situation that surrounds a source and shapes its meaning.
personalization
The practice by which search engines and social media platforms show different content to different users based on prior behavior, preferences, and location.
media literacy
The ability to understand how media institutions work, how news and media content are produced, and how media shapes public understanding.
data literacy
The ability to understand, interpret, and evaluate numerical and statistical information.
visual literacy
The ability to evaluate images, charts, maps, and other visual information critically and accurately.
algorithmic literacy
The ability to understand how algorithmic systems shape what information people see and why.
AI literacy
The ability to understand how artificial intelligence tools work and to use them responsibly in research and other contexts.
misinformation
False or inaccurate information shared by someone who may not know it is false.
disinformation
False information deliberately created and spread to deceive.
patchwriting
A form of academic dishonesty in which a writer closely mirrors a source's structure and phrasing while changing only a few words, rather than genuinely paraphrasing.

Review Questions

  1. In your own words, define information literacy. What are the five core abilities it includes?
  2. Why is knowing how to use a search engine not the same as being information literate?
  3. What are the six criteria in the MGU Source Evaluation Model? Give a brief example of how each applies to a source you might find in a web search.
  4. Why is information literacy relevant to civic life? Give two specific examples of a civic decision that would benefit from strong information literacy skills.
  5. What is the personalization problem in the digital information environment, and how does it affect information literacy?
  6. Explain the difference between finding information and using it responsibly. Why does information literacy include both?
  7. How does information literacy apply to creating and sharing information, not just consuming it?
  8. A classmate says, "I don't need to evaluate sources I find on Google because I can tell which ones are reliable by looking at them." Using concepts from this chapter, explain why this approach is insufficient.
  9. How does AI-generated content create new challenges for information literacy? What skills might help you navigate these challenges?
  10. Describe a situation in your own major or intended career in which information literacy would be directly important. What specific skills from this chapter would apply?

Research Practice

Activity 2.1Tracing an Information Need
  1. Think of a recent moment when you encountered a claim, statistic, or piece of advice that you did not fully verify before accepting or acting on it. Describe the claim and the situation in two or three sentences.
  2. Identify which of the five core information literacy abilities — recognize, find, evaluate, use, create and share — was weakest in that moment. Explain what a stronger version of that ability would have looked like.

Source Evaluation Practice

Activity 2.2Applying the MGU Source Evaluation Model
  1. Find a source related to a topic you are curious about — an article, a webpage, or a video. Apply all six criteria of the MGU Source Evaluation Model to it: relevance, authority, accuracy, purpose, currency, and context.
  2. Write one or two sentences of evaluation for each criterion, then state an overall conclusion about whether and how you would use this source in academic work.

Looking Ahead
Chapter 3: Research as Inquiry

Chapter 2 established what information literacy means: the connected abilities of recognizing, finding, evaluating, using, and creating and sharing information responsibly. Chapter 3 turns to research itself as a process of inquiry. You will learn why research begins with questions rather than answers, how curiosity and uncertainty drive exploratory research, how background knowledge shapes the questions you are able to ask, and why responsible researchers revise their questions and sometimes change their minds as evidence accumulates. The inquiry framework introduced in Chapter 3 will guide how you approach every research project in the chapters that follow.

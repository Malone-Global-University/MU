Chapter 7 — Front Matter

Research Skill Focus and Overview

The central skill of this chapter is topic narrowing: the ability to take a broad, unmanageable subject and reduce it to a focused, researchable question that can be answered within a realistic scope, using available sources, in the time provided. Topic narrowing is not a sign of weak thinking. It is the mark of disciplined, strategic inquiry. Every skilled researcher narrows. The question is whether you narrow deliberately or let a vague topic control your project.

Why This Matters

Imagine sitting down to write a ten-page research paper on "climate change." You find thousands of articles, dozens of books, scores of government reports, and no shortage of opinions. You read for two hours. You have highlights on twenty tabs. You still cannot figure out where to begin, what to include, or what your paper is actually about. This experience is nearly universal among beginning researchers, and it has a name: topic overload. Topic overload does not happen because students choose unimportant subjects. It happens because students choose subjects that are too large to research at the scale required by the assignment.

Climate change is not a topic. It is an entire academic field containing thousands of subtopics: atmospheric chemistry, policy frameworks, economic modeling, public communication, agricultural impacts, displacement and migration, insurance risk, political resistance, historical precedents, technological responses, and much more. No single paper can address the field. A single paper can address a question within it.

Topic narrowing solves topic overload. When you narrow deliberately, you define what your research will and will not include, make the search process more efficient and productive, find sources that speak directly to your specific question, produce writing that has focus, depth, and analytical coherence, and submit work that satisfies assignment requirements instead of sprawling past them. The skills in this chapter connect directly to what you learned in Chapter 6, where you moved from topic to research question. Here, you will learn the specific strategies researchers use to make a topic small enough to actually research. These strategies can be applied in any discipline, for any assignment type, at any level of academic work.

Section 7.1

Why Topics Need Focus

A topic is a subject area. It identifies the general territory of your interest but does not tell you what question you are trying to answer, what evidence you need, or how you will evaluate it. Topics are starting points. They are not finished research questions.

The difference between a topic and a focused research question is the difference between "I am interested in this area" and "I am investigating this specific question within this area."

Topics Are Starting Points, Not Destinations

When a professor assigns a research paper, they will sometimes offer broad topics as suggestions: immigration, public health, artificial intelligence, urban development, the Civil Rights Movement, global trade. These broad prompts are invitations to find a question, not directions to research the entire subject.

Students who treat broad topics as finished research questions encounter several problems.

ProblemWhat Happens
The scope problemThere is simply too much material. The researcher does not know where to look, cannot evaluate what is relevant, and cannot determine when they have enough sources
The depth problemThe paper ends up as a general overview — a little history, a little policy, a little statistics — with breadth but no depth
The argument problemA broad topic cannot support a focused argument; research papers must make claims, and claims require focused questions
The source problemSources accumulate that are vaguely related but do not directly address a single central issue

Narrowing solves all four of these problems at once.

Why Researchers Narrow

Narrowing is not a concession to limited time or page counts, though it responds to those realities. Narrowing is a fundamental intellectual move. Researchers narrow because specific questions produce specific answers, specific answers require specific evidence, specific evidence can be found, evaluated, and compared, and focused inquiry leads to genuine insight rather than general summary.

Consider what happens when a researcher narrows "climate change" to "the effect of rising sea temperatures on coral reef ecosystems in the Florida Keys between 2000 and 2020." Now there are specific search terms, a defined geographic area, a measurable time period, and a specific type of evidence needed. The researcher knows what to look for, what counts as a relevant source, and when the search is reasonably complete.

That is the power of narrowing. The sections that follow will teach you how to achieve it systematically.

Study Note — The Narrowing Process Is Iterative

Narrowing is rarely a single step. Most researchers narrow in stages: they start with a broad area, apply one narrowing strategy, then apply another, then test whether the result is focused enough for the assignment. They may also revise the focus after discovering what sources are actually available.

This is not failure. This is how research works. Expect to adjust your focus as you learn more. A topic that seems perfectly narrow may turn out to have abundant sources or almost none — and both situations call for adjustment. You will learn more about when to adjust in Section 7.8.

Section 7.2

Narrowing by Time

One of the most reliable and practical narrowing strategies is limiting your topic to a specific time period. Almost every broad topic looks different across different historical periods, and choosing a specific period immediately reduces the scope of relevant evidence.

Why Time Is a Powerful Narrowing Tool

Consider the topic "women in the workforce." This topic spans centuries, continents, and dozens of disciplines. It would take a library to address it fully. But the moment you limit the topic to "women entering manufacturing work in the United States during World War II" or "women in STEM careers in the United States between 1990 and 2010," the topic becomes researchable. You now know what time frame to search, what historical context to establish, and what kinds of sources — archival records, labor statistics, sociological studies from that period — are likely to be useful.

Time narrowing is especially effective because it limits which sources are relevant, helps you establish historical context efficiently, allows you to trace change over time (often the most analytically interesting approach), and corresponds to how many databases and archives organize their holdings.

Choosing a Time Frame

When narrowing by time, consider four factors. Assignment requirements — does the assignment specify a historical focus, a contemporary focus, or an open time frame? Honor any constraints your instructor has set. Source availability — some time periods are richly documented and easy to research; others have sparse or difficult-to-access sources, and a quick background search will reveal which applies to your topic. Analytical interest — do you want to study a single moment, a transition, or a long-term trend? Single moments call for intensive source reading; transitions call for comparing before and after; long-term trends call for breadth and statistical sources. Appropriate scope for the assignment — a two-page reflection and a twenty-page research paper require different time scopes.

Cautions for Time Narrowing

Avoid time periods that are too recent for academic sources — events from the last few months may not yet have peer-reviewed analysis, though news and government data may exist. Avoid time periods that are too distant for your assignment's purpose unless you are tracing historical roots of a current issue. And be careful about period length: a period too short (a single week, unless exceptionally significant) may lack sources, while a period too long (a century) may be too broad to allow depth within a short paper.

Section 7.3

Narrowing by Place

Geographic narrowing limits your topic to a specific location: a country, a region, a state or province, a city, a neighborhood, or even a specific institution or site. Like time narrowing, geographic narrowing immediately reduces the scope of relevant evidence and makes search strategies more precise.

Why Place Matters

Most broad topics involve phenomena that occur across many places simultaneously, but those phenomena look different depending on where they occur. Public health policy in Canada differs from public health policy in the United States, which differs from policy in Brazil. Urban housing affordability in New York City differs from housing affordability in Boise, Idaho, which differs from housing affordability in Lagos, Nigeria. Immigration experiences in Los Angeles differ from immigration experiences in a small rural midwestern community.

When you narrow by place, you focus on evidence relevant to that location — local newspapers, regional studies, government data for that jurisdiction — you account for the specific legal, cultural, political, and economic context that shapes the phenomenon there, and you avoid the trap of making claims about "the world" or "society in general" that cannot be adequately supported. Geographic narrowing also connects well with time narrowing: many of the strongest research questions combine both a specific place and a specific time period.

Levels of Geographic Focus

LevelWhen It Is Appropriate
Global or internationalWhen a question genuinely requires comparative international perspective — though comparing three or four countries is more manageable than comparing all of them
NationalPolicy questions, historical questions, or questions involving national institutions and laws
RegionalWhen regional differences within a country are part of the question (the American South, the Great Plains)
State, provincial, or localEducation policy, criminal justice, housing, or local politics, where local sources become the primary evidence
InstitutionalA specific university, hospital, corporation, or school district — common in case study research

Cautions for Geographic Narrowing

Match place to available sources — focusing on a small or obscure location may mean relying on local newspapers, government reports, or primary sources rather than databases. Avoid geographic assumptions: if your research is about Boston, your conclusions should be about Boston, not "American cities generally," unless you have additional evidence to support that generalization. And consider whether place is really relevant at all — for some scientific or mathematical topics, location may not matter; apply geographic narrowing only when location genuinely shapes the phenomenon you are investigating.

Section 7.4

Narrowing by Group or Population

Another reliable narrowing strategy is limiting your topic to a specific group, population, or demographic. Instead of researching "youth and social media," you research "social media use among adolescent girls ages 13–17." Instead of researching "veterans and mental health," you research "rates of PTSD diagnosis among female veterans who served in combat roles."

Why Population Matters

Research on human subjects almost always varies by population. Effects, experiences, policies, and outcomes differ by age, gender, race and ethnicity, socioeconomic status, education level, geographic location, immigration status, health status, occupation, and many other factors. Treating "people in general" as the relevant population often obscures the differences that make research interesting and useful.

Narrowing by population makes your research question more specific and answerable, identifies which sources are relevant, produces findings that are responsible rather than overgeneralizing, and often reveals research gaps — some populations have been studied extensively while others have been neglected.

Types of Population Narrowing

TypeExamples
Demographic groupsAge, gender, race and ethnicity, income level, education, religious affiliation
Geographic communitiesRural residents, urban residents, suburban communities, immigrant communities, Indigenous communities
Professional or occupational groupsTeachers, nurses, small business owners, manufacturing workers, gig economy workers, military service members
Health or condition-based groupsPeople with specific diagnoses, people with disabilities, survivors of specific events
Institutional populationsStudents at community colleges, first-generation college students, incarcerated individuals, residents of specific housing types

Population and Ethics

When researching specific populations — particularly marginalized, vulnerable, or historically mistreated populations — researchers have ethical responsibilities, addressed in detail in Chapter 5. Responsible population-based research acknowledges existing research by and about that population, not just about them from an outside perspective. It avoids pathologizing or reducing a population to a single characteristic, recognizes that a population is not monolithic, cites researchers from within communities when those researchers exist, and does not treat the population as merely a subject of curiosity without attention to their experiences and perspectives. These principles apply not only to your final paper but to how you frame your research question and evaluate the sources you find.

Section 7.5

Narrowing by Cause or Effect

Causal narrowing focuses your topic on either the causes of a phenomenon or its effects, rather than trying to address both simultaneously. This type of narrowing works especially well for analytical and argument questions.

Cause-Focused Narrowing

A cause-focused question asks: Why does this happen? What factors produce this outcome? What historical conditions led to this development? Cause-focused research typically requires examining historical context and sequence of events, identifying contributing factors (economic, political, cultural, institutional), evaluating evidence for competing explanations, and assessing the relative weight of different causes.

Example: instead of "obesity in the United States," a cause-focused question might be "What role has food industry marketing played in rising rates of childhood obesity in the United States since 1980?" This question focuses attention on one proposed causal mechanism — marketing — rather than the entire obesity phenomenon.

Effect-Focused Narrowing

An effect-focused question asks: What are the consequences of this phenomenon? What outcomes does this policy produce? How does this change affect a population? Effect-focused research typically requires identifying measurable or observable outcomes, comparing outcomes before and after a change, examining who is affected and how, and evaluating whether observed effects can be attributed to the identified cause.

Example: instead of "mandatory minimum sentencing laws," an effect-focused question might be "How have mandatory minimum sentencing laws affected incarceration rates for nonviolent drug offenses in the United States between 1986 and 2010?" This question focuses on consequences rather than the origin or justification of the laws.

The Danger of Assuming Causation

When narrowing by cause or effect, be careful about the difference between correlation and causation. Chapter 29 addresses this in the context of evaluating data. For now, note that two things happening together does not prove that one caused the other; a cause-focused research question asks you to find evidence for a causal mechanism, not simply note that two trends occurred simultaneously. Good causal research identifies the mechanism by which one thing produces another, the conditions under which the relationship holds, and alternative explanations that the evidence rules out. Frame your narrowed question carefully — "the relationship between X and Y" is safer than "how X causes Y" unless you have evidence for the causal mechanism.

Section 7.6

Narrowing by Comparison

Comparative narrowing organizes your research around a comparison between two or more things: two policies, two time periods, two populations, two places, two approaches, or two perspectives. Comparison is one of the most powerful analytical structures available to researchers because it reveals what is similar, what is different, and why those differences matter.

Why Comparison Produces Strong Research

A comparison question does not just describe one thing. It asks you to hold two things against each other and analyze the relationship between them. This produces insight that description alone cannot provide. When you compare, you learn what factors are shared versus unique, why similar conditions produce different outcomes in different contexts, which approach or policy performs better on specific measures, and how the same phenomenon is understood differently by different groups.

Comparative research is common across all academic disciplines. Historians compare events across time periods. Political scientists compare government systems and policies. Sociologists compare outcomes across demographic groups. Scientists compare experimental and control conditions. Literary scholars compare texts, authors, and traditions.

Types of Comparisons

TypeWhat It Asks
Before and afterHow did something change after an event, policy change, or intervention?
Across placesHow does the same phenomenon differ between two countries, regions, cities, or institutions?
Across groupsHow do two different populations experience or respond to the same thing?
Across time periodsHow does a current situation compare to an earlier one?
Across policies or approachesHow do two different strategies for addressing the same problem compare in outcomes?
Across perspectives or interpretationsHow do two scholars, traditions, or frameworks interpret the same text, event, or phenomenon?
Worked Examples — Narrowing by Comparison
From broad subject to comparative question
Broad TopicComparative Question
Criminal justice reformHow do drug treatment courts compare to traditional sentencing in reducing recidivism rates among nonviolent offenders?
National healthcareHow do single-payer and multi-payer healthcare systems compare in terms of cost per capita and health outcomes in OECD countries?
Immigration policyHow did immigration enforcement priorities differ between the Obama and Trump administrations, and what were the measurable effects?

Principle: Comparison reveals relationships that description alone cannot — but only when both sides are examined with equal care.
Cautions for Comparative Research

Keep the comparison balanced — if you compare two things, you need roughly equivalent depth of evidence for each, or the comparison will seem one-sided. Define your terms of comparison: what exactly are you comparing, and what does "success" mean before you begin? Acknowledge complexity — most comparisons reveal that the situation is more complicated than it first appeared. And be careful about cherry-picking: do not select a comparison only because it confirms what you already believe; if one program performs better on some measures while another performs better on others, report that honestly.

Section 7.7

Narrowing by Debate or Problem

Debate narrowing focuses your topic on a specific controversy, disagreement, or unresolved problem within a field. This type of narrowing is especially powerful for argument papers because it positions your research within a real intellectual or policy dispute where your analysis can contribute something meaningful.

What Debate Narrowing Means

Every academic field, and most public policy areas, contains active debates: questions that experts or stakeholders have not resolved, where competing evidence and interpretations exist, and where new research continues to appear. Identifying one of these debates and positioning your research within it gives your paper immediate relevance and analytical purpose.

A debate-focused research question does not simply ask "what do people think about X?" — that is a survey topic. It engages with the substance of the disagreement: What are the competing positions? What evidence supports each? What is at stake? What does the available evidence most strongly support?

Finding Debates

Debates appear in several places. In academic literature, debates often appear in the literature review section, where authors describe the "ongoing debate" or "unresolved question" their research addresses. In policy discussions, government reports, think tank publications, and news coverage of policy debates identify active controversies in areas like education, criminal justice, health, environmental regulation, and immigration. Background sources, such as encyclopedia articles, often describe debates or controversies within a topic area, identifying the stakes and the positions without requiring you to have read the full literature. And as you do your own reading on a broad topic, you will notice when sources disagree — pay attention to those disagreements, since they signal debates worth investigating.

Narrowing by Specific Problem

Problem narrowing is related to debate narrowing but focuses less on intellectual controversy and more on a concrete, real-world problem that needs analysis. Instead of framing the research as a debate between positions, problem narrowing frames it as an investigation of a challenge: Why does this problem exist? What has been tried? What evidence suggests the most promising approaches? Problem-focused questions are especially common in applied fields: education, public health, social work, business, environmental studies, and engineering.

Worked Examples — Narrowing by Debate or Problem

Broad TopicNarrowed by DebateNarrowed by Problem

Principle: Position your research inside a real, unresolved question — not a settled fact and not a vague survey of opinion.
Important Distinction
A Debate Is Not the Same as an Opinion

Not every disagreement is a research-worthy debate. Some "debates" are not debates among people with relevant expertise and evidence; they are simply cases where some people have opinions that contradict scientific or scholarly consensus. The question of whether climate change is primarily human-caused is not an ongoing scientific debate; scientific consensus is clear. The question of which specific policies will most effectively and equitably address climate change is a genuine ongoing debate among researchers, economists, and policymakers. Similarly, vaccine safety is not an ongoing scientific debate, but questions about vaccine distribution equity, public communication strategies, and mandate policies are real debates worth researching. When your research involves a topic where there is clear scientific or scholarly consensus, your question should not be framed as if the consensus is in doubt. Focus on questions that are genuinely open.

Section 7.8

Testing Whether a Topic Is Manageable

You have applied one or more narrowing strategies. You have a topic that is more focused than where you started. But how do you know whether it is focused enough? And is there a risk of narrowing too much? This section gives you practical tools for testing your narrowed topic before you commit to it.

The Five Tests of a Manageable Topic

Test 1
The Question Test

Can you write your topic as a specific research question? If you can write a clear, focused question — one sentence that identifies exactly what you are investigating — your topic is probably appropriately narrow. If your topic is still so broad that you cannot write a single clear question, it needs more narrowing.

Try it now: write your topic as a question. If the question is longer than two lines, or if it contains multiple distinct questions embedded in it, it needs more focus.

Test 2
The Source Test

If you do a quick background search — five to ten minutes in a library database or encyclopedia — do you find sources that speak directly to your narrowed question? You should be able to identify at least five to ten plausibly relevant sources without much difficulty.

If you find hundreds of sources immediately and cannot tell which are relevant, your topic is still too broad. If you find almost no sources, your topic may be too narrow, too obscure, or framed in a way that does not match how researchers discuss it — this calls for adjusting your search terms or slightly broadening the focus.

Test 3
The Scope Test

Does your topic fit the assignment requirements? Consider page length or word count (a 5-page paper needs a narrower topic than a 20-page paper), number of sources required (a topic requiring fifteen sources needs more breadth than one requiring five), and time available (more time means you can manage more scope; a weekend assignment cannot support a topic requiring weeks of research).

A rough guide: for a 5-page paper (roughly 1,500 words), your topic should probably be narrow enough that it has one primary question and two to four key supporting issues. For a 15-page paper (roughly 4,500 words), you have room for more complexity, but you should still have a clear central question.

Test 4
The Specificity Test

Could your research question be answered with "it depends on the situation" or "different people have different views"? If so, it may still be too broad or too vague. A well-focused question should be specific enough that evidence can actually answer it — or at least move toward an answer.

  • Too vague "What do people think about immigration?"No specific time, place, population, or evidence type is implied.
  • More specific "How did public opinion about undocumented immigration in the United States change between 2012 and 2020, and what factors are associated with those changes?"This version specifies a time frame, a place, and a measurable outcome.
Test 5
The Interest Test

Is your topic focused enough that you can sustain interest in it for the duration of the research project? A topic that is theoretically manageable but that bores you will produce weak research. Narrowing should produce a question you are genuinely curious about, not just one that is technically researchable.

If your narrowed topic feels dry or unimportant to you, try narrowing in a different direction. You can often find an angle on any broad topic that connects to something you find genuinely interesting.

When You Have Narrowed Too Much

It is possible to narrow so far that you cannot find adequate sources. Signs that you have narrowed too much include: you cannot find any academic sources on your specific question; the topic is so specific that it has never been studied, which is fine for original research in graduate school but not for most undergraduate assignments; or the question is so particular that even a long paper could not fill the required length meaningfully.

When this happens, try broadening the time period slightly, expanding the geographic area, adding a related population or removing one too-specific qualifier, or reframing from a very specific debate to a slightly broader question that encompasses the debate.

When You Are Still Too Broad

Signs that your topic is still too broad include: you cannot write a single focused research question; every search you run produces hundreds of results, and you cannot identify which are most relevant; the paper would require substantially more pages than assigned to address the topic adequately; or you find yourself thinking "but I also need to cover X, and Y, and Z" for many values of X, Y, and Z.

When this happens, apply another narrowing strategy. Most topics need more than one round of narrowing to reach the right scope.

The Narrowing Conversation — Checking with Your Instructor or Librarian

One of the most underused narrowing tools is simply asking someone who knows research. Your instructor assigned the paper and knows what scope is appropriate. A reference librarian works with research questions all day and can tell you quickly whether a question seems too broad or too narrow based on what sources are available.

Before committing to a topic, consider visiting office hours or sending an email to your instructor to describe your focused question and ask if it seems appropriate; visiting the MGU Library reference desk or a librarian's consultation hours to test whether the topic has adequate sources; and using the MGU Library's research guides, which often include examples of appropriately scoped questions for different types of assignments. These conversations take fifteen minutes and can save hours of wasted research in the wrong direction.

Putting the Narrowing Strategies Together

You do not have to choose only one narrowing strategy. The strongest focused topics often combine two or three.

Combined StrategyResulting Question
Time + PlaceMedia coverage of immigration in Texas newspapers during the 2018 midterm elections
Population + CauseWhat factors contribute to high dropout rates among Native American students in rural Bureau of Indian Education schools?
Comparison + TimeHow did federal student loan default rates change before and after the 2010 student loan reform legislation?
Debate + PlaceShould New York City's congestion pricing program serve as a model for other major American cities?
Core Principle

Use the strategies as tools in combination. The goal is not to check a box labeled "narrowed by time" or "narrowed by comparison." The goal is to produce a specific, researchable question that you can actually answer with available sources within the time and scope you have.

Source Check

One practical benefit of narrowing your topic is that it makes it much easier to evaluate whether a source is actually useful. A source that is relevant to a broad topic ("this article is about immigration") may not be relevant to your narrowed question ("this article is about immigration enforcement policy, but I'm researching the educational experiences of undocumented students in California high schools").

Locate one source that appeared in your database search and apply the following quick relevance check.

QuestionYour Answer
What is this source actually about?
What specific question does this source address?
What is the time period covered?
What geographic area or population does it address?
Does it address your narrowed question directly, partially, or not at all?
If partial, what aspect of your question does it address?
Is it too broad, too narrow, or well-matched to your question?

A source that partially addresses your narrowed question can still be useful — but you should know which part of your question it addresses before you include it in your research. A source that addresses the broad topic but not your specific focus is probably not a primary source for your paper; it might be useful for background.

Apply the MGU Source Evaluation Model

Relevance. Does it address my focused question, or only my broad topic?

Authority. Who wrote this, and what are their credentials in this area?

Evidence. What kind of evidence does the source use?

Accuracy. Are claims supported with evidence and citations?

Purpose. Why was this source written, and for what audience?

Currency. Is it recent enough for my time-framed question?

Context. Does it fit the specific geographic, demographic, or disciplinary focus of my research?

Misinformation Alert

Narrowing and the Temptation of Confirmation Bias

Narrowing a topic requires you to make choices, and choices can be influenced by what you already believe. This is one of the places where confirmation bias — the tendency to seek out, interpret, and remember information that confirms what you already think — can enter the research process at a very early stage.

Here is how confirmation bias can distort topic narrowing. In choosing a comparison, if you believe Policy A is better than Policy B, you may choose the comparison most favorable to Policy A — selecting a time period, population, or location where A performs best while avoiding comparisons where A performs poorly. In identifying a debate, you may frame a question as a debate when the evidence is actually fairly settled, because you personally remain unconvinced by the consensus. In identifying a cause, you may narrow your topic to focus on a cause you already believe is the main explanation, while designing the question in a way that ignores competing causal factors. And in selecting a population, you may select a population most likely to confirm your expectations, while ignoring how other populations might challenge your conclusions.

The MGU Misinformation Check Applied to Your Own Research

Who shaped this narrowing choice — your genuine curiosity or your prior conclusions?

What did you rule out when narrowing, and why did you rule it out?

Is your narrowed question one that the evidence can genuinely answer, or is it designed to confirm a conclusion you have already reached?

What would it look like to narrow in a direction that challenges your expectations?

Catching confirmation bias at the narrowing stage is far easier than catching it after you have already built a research plan around a biased question. Ask yourself: Am I choosing this angle because it is intellectually interesting, or because I already know the answer I want?

Citation Check

During the topic-narrowing process, you will likely encounter background sources — encyclopedia articles, textbook chapters, overview articles, government reports — that help you understand the scope of a field and identify useful narrowing angles. These sources require accurate citation records even if you ultimately decide not to use them in your final paper.

Record every source you consult using the MGU Research Note Model.

MGU Research Note Model

Source. Full bibliographic information (author, title, publisher/journal, date, URL or database).

Summary. What is this source about in one to three sentences?

Key evidence. What specific information helped you narrow your topic?

Quotation. Any specific phrase you might quote, with page number.

Paraphrase. Any information you restated in your own words, with page number.

Usefulness. How does this source help with your research (background, defining the debate, identifying key terms)?

Limits. What does this source not address?

Citation information. Record in both the format your assignment requires and enough information to reconstruct any citation style later.

Practical Tip

Many students skip recording citation information for background sources they "plan to remember later." They often do not remember. If you read it, record it. Even encyclopedia articles you will not cite in your final paper may contain references to sources you will cite, and you will want to know where you encountered them.

Key Terms

topic narrowing
The process of reducing a broad subject area into a focused, researchable question that fits a realistic scope of time, sources, and assignment length.
topic overload
The experience of being unable to begin or organize research because a chosen topic is too broad, producing too many sources and no clear direction.
time narrowing
Limiting a research topic to a specific historical period to reduce the scope of relevant evidence and establish clear context.
geographic narrowing
Limiting a research topic to a specific location, such as a country, region, state, city, or institution.
population narrowing
Limiting a research topic to a specific demographic, occupational, institutional, or condition-based group rather than people in general.
causal narrowing
Focusing a research topic on either the causes or the effects of a phenomenon, rather than addressing both directions simultaneously.
comparative narrowing
Organizing a research topic around a comparison between two or more places, time periods, populations, policies, or perspectives.
debate narrowing
Focusing a research topic on a specific, genuine controversy among experts or stakeholders, where competing evidence and positions exist.
problem narrowing
Focusing a research topic on a concrete, real-world challenge and the evidence for possible approaches to addressing it.
confirmation bias
The tendency to seek out, interpret, and remember information that confirms beliefs one already holds, which can distort topic-narrowing choices.
scope
The boundaries of a research project, defined by time, place, population, and depth, matched to an assignment's length and source requirements.
correlation
A relationship in which two things occur together, which does not by itself establish that one causes the other.

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 7.1Topics are starting points, not finished research questions. Broad topics produce scope, depth, argument, and source problems. Narrowing is a deliberate, often iterative intellectual move, not a concession to limited time.
  • Sec 7.2Narrowing by time limits a topic to a specific historical period, reducing the scope of relevant evidence and supporting clearer historical context, while requiring attention to source availability and appropriate period length.
  • Sec 7.3Narrowing by place limits a topic to a specific geographic level — global, national, regional, local, or institutional — connecting research to the particular legal, cultural, and economic context of that location.
  • Sec 7.4Narrowing by population focuses research on a specific demographic, geographic, occupational, health-based, or institutional group, producing more responsible and answerable research questions while raising ethical responsibilities.
  • Sec 7.5Narrowing by cause or effect directs analytical attention in one specific direction. Researchers must be careful to distinguish correlation from causation when framing causal questions.
  • Sec 7.6Narrowing by comparison positions two or more things against each other, producing insight that description alone cannot. Comparative research requires balance, clear terms, and honesty about complexity.
  • Sec 7.7Narrowing by debate or problem positions research within a genuine, unresolved controversy or a concrete real-world challenge, distinct from settled scientific consensus or simple opinion surveys.
  • Sec 7.8Five tests — the question test, source test, scope test, specificity test, and interest test — help researchers evaluate whether a narrowed topic is appropriately focused, with strategies for adjusting topics that are too broad or too narrow.

Review Questions

  2. Choose one of the six narrowing strategies discussed in this chapter and explain in your own words how it works. What does this strategy do, and why is it useful?
  3. A student wants to write a ten-page paper on "social media." Suggest a series of narrowing steps that could transform this into a focused, researchable question. Which strategies would you apply, and in what order? Write out the narrowed question you would recommend.
  4. What are the five tests for a manageable topic? Apply all five tests to a topic of your choice and explain what each test reveals.
  5. How can confirmation bias enter the topic-narrowing process? Give one example from each of the following narrowing strategies: comparison, debate identification, and cause selection.
  6. Why is it important to apply more than one narrowing strategy? What is gained by combining strategies such as time + place or population + cause?
  7. A student narrows their topic so much that they can only find two relevant sources. What likely went wrong, and what should they do?
  8. How does the MGU Library support the topic-narrowing process? Name at least three specific resources and explain how each one helps at the narrowing stage.
  9. What is the difference between a genuine debate among experts and a false "controversy" that misrepresents scientific or scholarly consensus? Why does this distinction matter when narrowing by debate?
  10. What is the "interest test," and why does it matter as much as the more technical tests like the source test and scope test?

Research Checklist

Chapter 7 — Topic Narrowing Checklist
Use this each time you narrow a topic for a research project.
Getting Started
  • I identified the broad topic assigned or suggested.
  • I read background material (encyclopedia, textbook, reference source) before trying to narrow.
  • I identified the main subtopics, debates, and angles within the broad topic.
Applying Narrowing Strategies
  • I considered narrowing by time (and identified a specific period if applicable).
  • I considered narrowing by place (and identified a specific geographic area if applicable).
  • I considered narrowing by population (and identified a specific group if applicable).
  • I considered narrowing by cause or effect (and focused on one direction if applicable).
  • I considered narrowing by comparison (and identified the two things being compared if applicable).
  • I considered narrowing by debate or problem (and identified the specific controversy or challenge if applicable).
  • I applied at least two narrowing strategies in combination.
Testing My Narrowed Topic
  • I can write my narrowed topic as a single, clear research question.
  • My question passes the source test (I found at least five to ten potentially relevant sources in a quick search).
  • My question fits the scope of the assignment (page length, source count, time available).
  • My question is specific enough that evidence can actually answer it.
  • I am genuinely interested in this question (the interest test).
  • I checked for confirmation bias in my narrowing choices.
Checking In
  • I verified my question with my instructor or a librarian (if needed).
  • I recorded my narrowing decisions in my research notebook.
  • I recorded citation information for any background sources I consulted during narrowing.
Ready to Proceed
  • I have a focused research question that I can begin searching and building sources around.
  • I have a preliminary list of search terms drawn from my narrowed question.

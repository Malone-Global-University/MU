Chapter 42 — Front Matter

Research Skill Focus and Overview

Research Skill Focus

This chapter teaches students how citations appear inside research papers and other academic documents. Students will learn to place parenthetical citations, narrative citations, footnotes, and endnotes correctly, accurately, and in the proper style for their assignment. Citation placement is not a mechanical afterthought. It is part of writing clearly, crediting sources precisely, and earning trust from readers.

Why This Matters

Imagine a reader encountering this sentence in a research paper: "Studies have shown that students who read primary sources perform significantly better on analytical assessments than students who rely on textbook summaries." The sentence makes a specific, factual claim. But where did it come from? Who conducted the studies? When? How many studies? Which populations were studied? Without a citation, the reader has no way to verify the claim, no way to read the original research, and no reason to trust the statement beyond the writer's authority alone.

Now imagine the same sentence with a citation: "Students who read primary sources perform significantly better on analytical assessments than students who rely on textbook summaries (Hernandez and Park, 2021, p. 44)." The reader now has a lead. The last name or names, the year, and the page number point toward a specific source. The reader can look at the works cited page or references list, find the full entry for Hernandez and Park, and follow the trail to the original study.

This is what in-text citations do. They are not decoration. They are not bureaucratic requirements invented to make student life harder. They are functioning parts of the research document — road signs embedded inside the text that connect every specific claim to the source that supports it.

This chapter explains how those road signs work: how they are built, where they are placed, and what distinguishes parenthetical citations from narrative citations, footnotes from endnotes, author-page style from author-date style. By the end of this chapter, you should be able to place citations accurately in any major style and understand why citation placement decisions matter for the reader.

Central Principle

Do not merely find information. Earn trust in information. Citations are one of the most important tools for earning that trust.

Section 42.1

Where Citations Appear

Citations can appear in two locations within a research document: inside the body of the text, or in supplementary notes attached to the text.

In-text citations appear directly in the running prose of the paper, usually at the end of the sentence or clause that uses a source, or embedded within the sentence itself. They are called in-text because they live in the text — not at the bottom of the page, not at the end of the document, not in an appendix. They mark the moment the source is used.

Notes — either footnotes at the bottom of the page or endnotes at the end of the chapter or document — can serve as citation markers, as spaces for supplementary explanation, or as both at once, depending on the citation style.

The two systems serve the same basic function: connecting the text to its sources. But they do so differently, and different academic fields have developed strong preferences for one system over the other.

StyleCitation SystemHow It Works
MLAIn-text parentheticalAuthor name and page number. No footnotes or endnotes for citations; notes used only for supplementary content.
APAIn-text parentheticalAuthor name and publication year. Footnotes used rarely, only for content that cannot fit smoothly in the text.
Chicago Notes-BibliographyFootnotes or endnotesNumbered notes are the primary citation mechanism. Each number corresponds to a full or shortened citation.
Chicago Author-DateIn-text parentheticalAuthor name and year, like APA, with a full references list at the end.

The style your instructor or field requires determines which system you use. Once you know the system, you can apply the rules consistently. This chapter covers all the major types: parenthetical citations (MLA and APA style), footnotes (Chicago Notes-Bibliography), endnotes, narrative citations, and the specific mechanics of each. It also addresses signal phrases, citation placement within sentences, and the logic that guides where citations appear and how they are written.

Section 42.2

Parenthetical Citations

A parenthetical citation is a brief reference placed inside parentheses within the body of the text. The parentheses contain just enough information to point the reader toward the full entry in the works cited list or references list at the end of the document.

The key design principle of parenthetical citations is economy. The citation should contain the minimum information needed to locate the source in the final list. Everything else appears in the list itself.

How Parenthetical Citations Are Built

Parenthetical citations typically contain two elements: an identifier — usually the author's last name, or a shortened title if there is no author — and a locator — a page number, paragraph number, or timestamp pointing to the specific location in the source. The exact elements and their arrangement depend on the citation style.

Example 42.2 — Building the Citation by Style
Same source, different parenthetical formats
  • MLAOne author: (Okafor 47) Two authors: (Okafor and Singh 47) Three or more: (Okafor et al. 47) No author: ("Climate Data" 12)
  • APAParaphrase: (Okafor, 2022) Direct quotation: (Okafor, 2022, p. 47) Two authors: (Okafor & Singh, 2022, p. 47) Three or more (7th ed.): (Okafor et al., 2022, p. 47)
Principle: Use the minimum identifying information the style requires — never more, never less.

Where Parenthetical Citations Are Placed

The parenthetical citation is placed at the end of the clause or sentence that uses the source, before the closing punctuation. The period of the sentence goes after the closing parenthesis.

Students who engage in active reading strategies retain source material more effectively (Hernandez, 2020, p. 88).

Note that the period ends the sentence, but it follows the parenthesis. The parenthetical citation is part of the sentence structure. For longer quotations that are set off as block quotations (more than four typed lines in MLA, more than forty words in APA), the citation appears after the final period of the quotation — the reverse of the normal rule.

Study Note — Repeated Sources Within a Paragraph

If you cite the same source multiple times in a single paragraph, and the context makes it clear which source you mean, you do not always need to repeat the full citation after every sentence. However, repeat the citation whenever you shift to a different point within the source, use a different page number, introduce a direct quotation, or whenever any ambiguity exists about which source the sentence draws from.

Never assume the reader can track your sources without guidance. When in doubt, cite.

Multiple Sources in One Citation

If you are drawing on two or more sources for a single claim, list the citations together inside one set of parentheses, separated by semicolons:

MLA: (Okafor 47; Singh 103)
APA: (Hernandez, 2020, p. 47; Park, 2021, p. 103)

This signals that the claim is supported by multiple independent sources, which strengthens the evidence.

Sources Without Page Numbers

Digital sources, websites, and many ebooks do not have page numbers. When page numbers are unavailable, MLA uses paragraph numbers (par. 3), section headings, or simply the author's name if no other locator is available. APA uses paragraph numbers (para. 3), section headings, or a timestamp for audio and video sources. If none of these is available, the author name and year alone form the citation.

Common Mistake

Do not invent page numbers. Do not approximate. If the source lacks page numbers, use whatever locating information is available and be consistent throughout the paper.

Section 42.3

Narrative Citations

A narrative citation integrates the source information directly into the sentence rather than placing it in parentheses at the end. In a narrative citation, the author's name appears as part of the sentence grammar — typically as the subject or as part of a signal phrase — and the parenthetical note contains only the remaining required information. Narrative citations are sometimes called signal phrase citations because they rely on a signal phrase: a phrase that introduces the source and attributes the idea or quotation before it appears.

Narrative Citations in MLA

In MLA style, the narrative citation names the author in the sentence and places only the page number in parentheses:

Okafor argues that primary source reading dramatically improves analytical skills (47).
In her study of reading comprehension, Hernandez found significant gains among students who read primary documents rather than textbook summaries (103–105).

Narrative Citations in APA

In APA style, the narrative citation names the author and places the year in parentheses immediately after the name, then provides the page number (if quoting) at the end:

Okafor (2022) argues that primary source reading dramatically improves analytical skills (p. 47).
Hernandez and Park (2021) found that students reading primary documents significantly outperformed those using textbooks.

With two authors in APA narrative citations, spell out both names connected by "and" — not an ampersand, which is used only inside parentheses.

Why Narrative Citations Are Useful

Parenthetical citations at the end of a sentence keep the source invisible in a useful way: the idea or evidence comes forward, and the attribution retreats into the parentheses. But narrative citations serve important rhetorical purposes:

  • Attributing arguments Naming a scholar in the sentence makes clear that an argument belongs to a particular person: "Smith argues that..." is clearer about the source than "(Smith, 2020)" tacked onto the end.
  • Acknowledging expertise Introducing a source with credentials signals why it is authoritative: "According to Dr. Priya Singh, a neuroscientist at Stanford..."
  • Source conversation Naming authors in narrative form helps readers follow scholarly dialogue: "While Okafor contends that... Singh argues instead that..."
  • Human origin of knowledge Naming the people behind a claim is a form of intellectual respect and transparency.

Use narrative citations when attribution matters to the sentence's meaning. Use parenthetical citations when the information matters more than who said it. Both forms are correct; the choice is a writing decision.

Section 42.4

Author-Page Citations

Author-page citation is the system used in MLA style. The parenthetical citation contains the author's last name and the page number, and the reader uses those two pieces of information to find the full source in the works cited list.

The Logic of Author-Page

The author's name is the key that unlocks the works cited entry. In MLA format, all entries are alphabetized by the author's last name (or by title if there is no author). The reader sees "(Okafor 47)," turns to the works cited list, finds the entry beginning with "Okafor," reads the full bibliographic information, and can locate the original source. The page number tells the reader exactly where in that source the information appears — "Okafor 47" does not mean the entire book by Okafor supports the claim, it means page 47 specifically.

Section 42.5

Author-Date Citations

Author-date citation is used in APA style and Chicago Author-Date style. The parenthetical citation contains the author's last name and the year of publication, and (when quoting directly) the page number. The reader uses the author and year to locate the full entry in the references list.

The Logic of Author-Date

The year is critical information in fields that rely on current research. In psychology, education, medicine, economics, and other social and natural sciences, a study from 2005 and a study from 2023 on the same topic may produce very different conclusions. The year in the citation signals to the reader immediately how recent the evidence is. The author-date system is also designed for disciplines where multiple studies by the same author matter — a reader who sees "(Chen, 2019; Chen, 2022; Chen, 2023)" understands that one scholar has produced a sustained body of work on this topic.

Personal communications (emails, interviews, conversations) are cited in-text but not included in the references list because readers cannot retrieve them. Note the communicator's name, the label "personal communication," and the full date.

Section 42.6

Footnotes

A footnote is a numbered note placed at the bottom of the page that corresponds to a superscript number in the body text. The superscript number appears in the text at the point where the citation or note is needed; the note itself appears at the bottom of the same page. Footnotes are the primary citation mechanism in Chicago Notes-Bibliography style, used widely in history, literature, the arts, philosophy, and many humanities disciplines.

Example 42.6 — A Footnote in Chicago Notes-Bibliography Style
From superscript to full note
  • In textMedieval historians have argued that documentary evidence from this period must be read with attention to the political purposes of the scribes who produced it.¹
  • Note¹ Robert Bartlett, The Making of Europe: Conquest, Colonization and Cultural Change, 950–1350 (Princeton: Princeton University Press, 1993), 47.
Principle: The superscript marks where the source was used; the note gives the full path back to it.

First Note Versus Shortened Note

In Chicago Notes-Bibliography, the first time you cite a source in a footnote, you provide the full citation. If you cite the same source again later, you use a shortened form — the author's last name, a shortened title, and the page number:

⁵ Bartlett, Making of Europe, 112.

If you cite the same source in the footnote immediately following, you may use the abbreviation "Ibid." (from the Latin ibidem, meaning "in the same place") along with the page number if different:

⁵ Bartlett, Making of Europe, 112.
⁶ Ibid., 115.
Study Note — Ibid. Is Falling Out of Favor

Many Chicago style guides today recommend using the shortened form instead of "Ibid." to avoid confusion when notes are renumbered during editing. Follow your instructor's guidance.

Footnote Format Elements

Source TypeElements in OrderExample
BookAuthor (first name first), Title, (Place: Publisher, Year), page¹ María Martínez, The Information Landscape: Digital Research in the Twenty-First Century (Chicago: University of Chicago Press, 2020), 88.
Journal articleAuthor, "Title," Journal Volume, Issue (Year): page² James Chen, "Primary Sources in the Digital Classroom," Journal of Educational Research 112, no. 4 (2019): 301.
WebsiteAuthor (if available), "Page title," Site name, date, URL³ World Health Organization, "Health Information Literacy: A Framework for Assessment," World Health Organization, updated April 15, 2022.

Notes as Supplementary Content

In Chicago style, footnotes serve a dual purpose. They cite sources, but they can also contain supplementary information that would interrupt the flow of the main text — a definition, a qualification, an additional example, a cross-reference to another section of the paper, or a short discussion of a methodological point.

Example — A Content Footnote
Citing and qualifying in the same note
  • In textThe evidence for this claim derives primarily from archival records, though several historians have disputed the interpretation advanced here.⁷
  • Note⁷ For the most thorough challenge to this reading, see Franklin, Archives and Memory, 203–215. The debate continues in recent scholarship; see also the forum in Historical Methods 52, no. 3 (2019): 201–230.
Principle: This ability to carry substantive content is one reason historians favor footnotes — the main text moves forward while the notes accommodate complexity.

MLA and APA do not use footnotes as their primary citation mechanism, but both allow content footnotes — notes that add information or brief discussion that cannot fit smoothly in the main text. MLA content notes are used sparingly and follow the same numbering sequence as any other notes. APA content footnotes should be brief and limited to material that strengthens the main argument without being essential to it; APA discourages heavy use of footnotes. Neither MLA nor APA content notes are used to provide bibliographic citations — that function belongs to parenthetical citations and the works cited or references list.

Section 42.7

Endnotes

Endnotes function exactly like footnotes, with one difference: instead of appearing at the bottom of each page, endnotes appear collected together at the end of the chapter or at the end of the document, under the heading "Notes." Everything described above for footnotes in Chicago Notes-Bibliography style applies equally to endnotes — the same first-note and shortened-note formats, the same rules for supplementary content. The only difference is physical location.

FeatureFootnotesEndnotes
LocationBottom of the same pageEnd of chapter or document
Ease of useReader glances down without leaving the pageReader must flip to the back to read the note
Page appearanceCan feel crowded with substantial notesCleaner body pages; less competing for space
Best suited forHeavily annotated scholarshipDocuments where a clean page layout matters most

Many publishers and instructors have a preference. Some books use footnotes; some use endnotes; some leave the choice to the author. When in doubt, ask your instructor which format is expected.

Study Note — Use the Built-In Tool

In word-processing software, footnotes and endnotes are typically managed automatically. The software inserts the superscript number in the text and the note in the appropriate location, renumbering all notes if you add or delete one. Use the built-in footnote/endnote tool rather than manually typing numbers, which quickly becomes impossible to manage accurately.

Notes are numbered sequentially throughout the paper. The first note is ¹, the second is ², and so on, continuing to the end of the document (for endnotes) or the end of each chapter (for chapter-based endnotes). Do not restart numbering on each page unless your instructor specifies otherwise.

Section 42.8

Placing Citations Clearly

Knowing the format of a citation is only part of the skill. You must also know exactly where to place citations within sentences, paragraphs, and across the paper. Misplaced citations create confusion about what evidence supports which claim.

The citation should cover what it claims to cover. A citation at the end of a sentence covers the information in that sentence. A citation placed after a single clause covers only that clause. A citation placed at the end of a long paragraph that draws from multiple sources covers nothing clearly — the reader cannot know which sentence comes from which source. Place citations where they close the claim they support.

Example 42.8 — Fixing Citation Placement
From one vague citation to three precise ones
  • WeakSolar panels have become significantly more affordable over the past decade, with the cost per watt dropping by more than 80 percent since 2010. This trend is expected to continue as manufacturing scales up and technology improves. Governments in more than sixty countries have introduced incentives to accelerate adoption. (Reyes, 2023, p. 112)Three distinct claims, one citation at the end. The reader cannot tell which claim Reyes supports, or whether the others are the writer's own analysis.
  • StrongerSolar panels have become significantly more affordable over the past decade, with the cost per watt dropping by more than 80 percent since 2010 (Reyes, 2023, p. 112). This trend is expected to continue as manufacturing scales up and technology improves (Patel & Nguyen, 2022, p. 34). Governments in more than sixty countries have introduced incentives to accelerate adoption (International Energy Agency, 2023, para. 7).Each claim is attributed precisely.
Principle: One claim, one citation, placed where the claim ends.

Placing Citations After Data, Quotations, and Paraphrases

Specific data points — statistics, numbers, percentages, survey results, experimental findings — always require a citation placed immediately after the data. If a sentence contains multiple data points from different sources, each needs its own citation:

The study found that 67 percent of participants reported improved reading speed (Lin, 2022, p. 45), while 43 percent reported no improvement in comprehension (Lin, 2022, p. 47).

Direct quotations always require a citation immediately after the closing quotation mark (in parenthetical systems) or a superscript immediately after the closing punctuation (in footnote systems). Never allow a direct quotation to appear without a citation.

Paraphrased material also requires a citation. Some students mistakenly believe that because they have put the idea in their own words, they do not need to cite it. This is incorrect. If the idea came from someone else, you must cite the source, regardless of how thoroughly you have reworded it.

Citation Clumping

Citation clumping occurs when a writer stacks multiple citations at the end of a paragraph without connecting them to specific claims. This leaves the reader unable to determine what evidence supports what statement. Always attach each citation to the specific claim it supports — never let citations pile up unattached at the end of a paragraph.

Citing After Signal Phrases

Signal phrases introduce source material and indicate where it begins. The citation should appear at the end of the material introduced by the signal phrase, not before it. Never place a citation before the material it supports.

Example — Citation Position Relative to the Signal Phrase
Citations follow the claim, not precede it
  • Incorrect(Okafor, 2022, p. 47) argues that regular exposure to primary sources improves analytical reasoning.
  • CorrectOkafor (2022) argues that regular exposure to primary sources improves analytical reasoning (p. 47). Or: As Okafor argues, regular exposure to primary sources improves analytical reasoning (2022, p. 47).
Principle: Citations close a claim; they do not open one.

Signal Phrases: A Brief Reference Guide

Strong signal phrases convey not just that a source exists, but how the source relates to your argument. Choose signal phrases that accurately represent that relationship — "confirms" means something different from "suggests," and "challenges" means something different from "questions." Using the right signal phrase is part of representing your sources fairly.

Applied Practice
Search Practice, Source Check, Research Notebook, and Misinformation Alert
Putting Citation Placement Skills to Work

Search Practice

Activity 1Identify Citation Systems

Locate three published academic sources on a topic of your choice — one article from a humanities journal (likely Chicago or MLA), one article from a social science journal (likely APA), and one article from a history journal (likely Chicago Notes-Bibliography). Open each article and identify what citation system is used, where the citations appear (in-text, footnotes, endnotes), what information is included in each in-text citation, and how the notes are formatted. Record your findings in your research notebook. Compare the three articles and note the differences in how the systems handle sources.

Activity 2Practice Placing Citations

Write a paragraph (five to seven sentences) summarizing information from two different sources on a topic you are currently researching. Place a citation after each sentence that draws from a specific source. Then ask a classmate to look at your paragraph and identify which source supports each sentence. If they cannot tell, revise your citation placement.

Activity 3Convert Between Systems

Take a single piece of source information (author name, year, page number, title) and write the citation in three formats: MLA parenthetical, APA parenthetical, and a Chicago footnote. Compare the three. What information is included in each? What is omitted? What differences in emphasis do you notice?

Source Check

Source: An article in a social science journal. Author: Dr. Asha Patel. Year of publication: 2021. Title: "Media Literacy and Adolescent Information Seeking." Journal: Journal of Media Education. Volume 45, Issue 2, Pages 88–103.

MGU Source Evaluation Model Applied
  • Relevance Does this source address your research question directly? If you are writing about how students evaluate online information, yes. If writing about historical newspaper archives, probably not.
  • Authority Dr. Patel is a credentialed researcher publishing in a peer-reviewed academic journal. The journal name and peer-review process indicate scholarly authority.
  • Evidence A journal article in a social science field should present methodology, data, and analysis. Check that claims are supported by research rather than opinion alone.
  • Accuracy Look at the sources cited in the article. Are they credible? Are findings consistent with other research you have found?
  • Purpose Academic journal articles contribute to scholarly knowledge — research and education, not persuasion, sales, or entertainment.
  • Currency Published in 2021. For a fast-moving topic like adolescent social media use, check whether more recent studies exist.
  • Context Media literacy studies typically involve surveys, experiments, or content analysis. Understanding the methodology helps you interpret the findings accurately.

Research Notebook

Use the MGU Research Note Model to record source information in a way that captures everything you will need to cite correctly later.

MGU Research Note Model Applied to Chapter 42 Reading
  • Source Author (Last, First); title of article/chapter; title of journal/book; year; volume/issue/pages; URL or DOI if applicable; date accessed if a website.
  • Summary Two to four sentences summarizing the source's main argument or findings in your own words.
  • Key evidence A specific finding, statistic, or claim from the source, with page number.
  • Quotation Exact words from the source, copied accurately, in quotation marks, with page number.
  • Paraphrase Your restatement of the quotation or another passage, in your own words, with page number.
  • Usefulness How does this source help with your research question? What specific point will you use it to support?
  • Limits Any concerns about this source: outdated? narrow scope? methodological weaknesses? bias?
  • Citation information Full MLA entry and full APA entry as they would appear on your works cited or references page.

The Research Note Model ensures that you record everything before you close the source. It is far easier to record citation information now than to hunt for it later when your paper is due and you cannot remember which page a quotation came from.

Misinformation Alert

Citation-Related Misinformation: False Authority and Missing Verification

One of the most common ways misinformation enters academic work is through citations that appear credible but have not been verified. Watch for: hallucinated citations from AI tools, which can look bibliographically correct while pointing to sources that do not exist; inaccurate secondary citations, where an author may have misrepresented a source they cited; authoritative-looking but unverifiable sources, where the page cited may not actually contain the attributed information; outdated sources presented as current on fast-moving topics; and fake or predatory journals that publish without meaningful peer review. Before using any AI-generated citation, verify that the source exists by searching for it in a library database or locating the actual document. Whenever possible, find and read the primary source yourself rather than trusting a secondary citation.

MGU Misinformation Check Applied to Source Verification
  • Who made it? Who is the author, and what are their credentials?
  • What is the claim? What exactly does this citation attribute to this source?
  • What evidence is offered? Can you locate the specific page or passage being cited?
  • What is missing? Is there more recent research that changes the conclusion?
  • Can another reliable source confirm it? Does a second credible source say something similar?
  • Is the emotional reaction stronger than the evidence? Does the source make a dramatic claim that feels persuasive before you have read the supporting evidence?
  • Has the source been altered, misrepresented, or taken out of context? Does the passage cited actually say what the citation implies it says?

When Elements Are Missing

If you cannot find all required elements, record what is available. Some sources simply do not have all standard elements. Your citation should include everything available and omit what is genuinely missing — never invent elements.

Using MGU Library Resources to Support Accurate Citation
  • Encyclopedia and reference pages: record author (if listed), entry title, name of the reference work, year, and page or section numbers — encyclopedia entries require citation just like any other source
  • Guides and tutorials: use the MGU Library's citation guides for MLA, APA, and Chicago before finalizing your works cited or references page, and return to them for unusual source types
  • Database access: most library databases provide a "cite" button that generates a formatted citation — export carefully and verify against the style guide, since database-generated citations frequently contain errors

Research Checklist

Chapter 42 — Citation Placement Checklist
Use this before submitting any paper that includes in-text citations
Before Drafting
  • I know which citation style is required for this assignment (MLA, APA, Chicago NB, or Chicago AD).
  • I have collected full bibliographic information for every source I plan to use.
  • I have recorded page numbers for every passage, quotation, or data point I plan to use.
During Drafting
  • I have placed a citation after every sentence or clause that uses a source.
  • I have not allowed a paragraph to end with a single citation covering multiple different claims from multiple sources.
  • I have placed citations after direct quotations, immediately following the closing quotation mark.
  • I have placed citations after paraphrased material, not just after direct quotations.
  • I have used signal phrases to introduce sources where the attribution matters to the reader.
  • I have used narrative citations when the author's identity matters to the point, and parenthetical citations when the information matters more.
Format Checks (MLA)
  • Parenthetical citations contain author name and page number with no punctuation between them: (Smith 47).
  • Narrative citations name the author in the sentence and place only the page number in parentheses.
  • Multiple authors are handled correctly (two authors: both named; three or more: first author et al.).
  • Sources without page numbers use an available alternative locator.
Format Checks (APA)
  • Parenthetical citations contain author name, year, and page number for quotations: (Smith, 2022, p. 47).
  • "p." precedes single page numbers; "pp." precedes page ranges.
  • Narrative citations place the year in parentheses immediately after the author's name: Smith (2022).
  • Sources with the same author and year are distinguished with a, b, c.
Format Checks (Chicago NB)
  • Every superscript number in the text corresponds to a note at the bottom of the page or end of the document.
  • First citations contain full bibliographic information; later citations use the shortened form.
  • Notes are numbered sequentially throughout the paper.
After Drafting
  • Every in-text citation corresponds to an entry in my works cited/references list.
  • Every entry in my works cited/references list is cited at least once in the text.
  • I have verified that page numbers, author names, and publication years are accurate.
  • I have checked any AI-generated or tool-generated citations against the actual source.
Chapter 42 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary and Review Questions

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 42.1Citations appear either as in-text parenthetical citations or as notes (footnotes/endnotes). MLA and APA use in-text parenthetical citations; Chicago Notes-Bibliography uses numbered notes as the primary citation mechanism; Chicago Author-Date uses in-text citations like APA.
  • Sec 42.2Parenthetical citations contain a minimum identifier and locator, are placed before closing sentence punctuation, may combine multiple sources with semicolons, and use alternative locators when page numbers are unavailable.
  • Sec 42.3Narrative citations integrate the author's name into the sentence using a signal phrase. They serve rhetorical purposes such as attributing arguments, acknowledging expertise, and showing scholarly conversation.
  • Sec 42.4Author-page citation is the MLA system: author's last name plus page number, used consistently whether paraphrasing or quoting, with specific rules for multiple authors and multiple works by one author.
  • Sec 42.5Author-date citation is the APA and Chicago Author-Date system: author's last name plus year, with page numbers required for direct quotations and recommended for paraphrases.
  • Sec 42.6Footnotes are the primary citation mechanism in Chicago Notes-Bibliography style. First citations are full; later citations of the same source are shortened. Footnotes can also carry supplementary content.
  • Sec 42.7Endnotes function exactly like footnotes but are collected at the end of the chapter or document rather than at the bottom of each page. Word-processing software should manage note numbering automatically.
  • Sec 42.8Citations must be placed where they cover the specific claim they support — after data points, after quotations, after paraphrases — and never clumped together at the end of a paragraph covering multiple unrelated claims.

Review Questions

  1. What is the difference between an in-text citation and a footnote? In which citation styles is each system primarily used?
  2. What are the two elements found in a standard MLA parenthetical citation? What are the three elements found in a standard APA parenthetical citation for a direct quotation?
  3. Explain the difference between a parenthetical citation and a narrative citation. Give an example of each using the same source information.
  4. In Chicago Notes-Bibliography style, what is the difference between a first note and a shortened note? Why is the distinction necessary?
  5. Why must paraphrased material be cited, not just direct quotations?
  6. What is citation clumping, and why is it a problem for readers?
  7. A student writes a paragraph drawing from three different sources but places a single citation at the end of the paragraph. What has the student done wrong, and how should the paragraph be corrected?
  8. What is a signal phrase? Give three examples of signal phrases that attribute a source in different ways (one that reports neutrally, one that indicates agreement, and one that indicates disagreement).
  9. In APA style, how do you cite a source with three or more authors in a parenthetical citation? How do you cite the same source in a narrative citation?
  10. A student is using AI tools to generate citations for a research paper. What risks should the student be aware of, and what steps should the student take to verify the generated citations before submitting the paper?

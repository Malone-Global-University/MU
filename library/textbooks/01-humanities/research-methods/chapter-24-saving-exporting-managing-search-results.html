Chapter 24 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

There is a particular kind of frustration that almost every researcher has experienced. You found a source — a perfect source, exactly what you needed — and you can no longer find it. You remember roughly what it said. You remember it was helpful. You remember you thought, at the time, that you would find it again easily. But now, hours or days later, you cannot reconstruct what you searched, which database you used, or what the title was. The source is gone.

This chapter teaches students how to capture, organize, and preserve the sources they find during research so that nothing useful is lost before it can be used. Students will learn how to bookmark and save links responsibly, download and name files systematically, save and organize PDFs and documents, export citation information from databases and catalogs, maintain a running search log, use reference management software, and develop habits that prevent the single most common and most avoidable research failure: losing a source you already found.

Managing search results is not a bureaucratic afterthought. It is a core research skill, and it belongs at the center of every research session. By the end of this chapter, you should be able to move through a research session with confidence that your results are recorded, your files are organized, and your sources are ready for evaluation and use.

Opening Question

Imagine two students each conduct a two-hour research session, each finding roughly the same number of useful sources. The first student saves bookmarks with default titles, downloads PDFs with default filenames, and keeps no record of what was searched. The second student renames every file, exports every citation into a reference manager, and writes a five-minute search log entry at the end of the session. A week later, both students sit down to write. Which student writes faster, cites more accurately, and loses less time? By the end of this chapter, you will understand exactly why the answer is not close — and you will have the specific habits needed to be the second student.

Research Skill Focus

This chapter focuses on the practical, often-overlooked skill of research result management: capturing search results in a form that remains usable, organized, and citable long after the original search session has ended. Students will practice intelligent bookmarking, consistent file naming, citation export and verification, search-log keeping, and reference manager setup.

Why This Matters

Research sessions generate large numbers of items in a short period of time. Catalog searches turn up dozens of books. Database searches return hundreds of articles. In the middle of a productive search session, it is tempting to keep moving forward rather than stopping to record what you have found. That temptation is the enemy of organized research. Students who develop organized habits early find that note-taking, synthesis, citation, and writing all go faster and feel more controlled. Students who skip result management spend extra hours hunting for lost sources, reconstructing half-remembered searches, and building citations from incomplete information under deadline pressure. The investment in managing results is small. The return is large.

Section 24.1

Why Search Results Must Be Managed

The case for managing search results begins with a simple observation about how research actually works versus how students often imagine it works.

In the imagined version, a researcher searches once, finds the right sources immediately, reads them in order, takes notes carefully, and writes a paper that comes together cleanly. In the actual version, research is iterative, nonlinear, and cumulative. You search, find some useful items and many useless ones, follow subject headings to new searches, find a bibliography that leads to three more searches, discover that the best source is checked out and request it through interlibrary loan, find a digital collection that changes your research question slightly, and return to the catalog with a revised keyword list. By the time you sit down to write, you may have conducted a dozen separate search sessions across multiple platforms over multiple days or weeks.

This is normal. Good research is messy in the middle. But mess in the middle does not have to mean chaos at the end — and it definitely does not have to mean lost sources.

The Cost of Unmanaged Research

When search results are not managed, researchers pay four kinds of costs.

CostWhat HappensWhy It Is So Damaging
Lost sourcesA source found but never relocated, often because the exact search path was never recordedA source you cannot find again contributes nothing to your research no matter how good it was
Duplicated effortWithout a search log, the same database and the same keywords get used again, producing the same resultsResearchers who keep logs do not repeat themselves; researchers who do not, repeat themselves constantly
Incomplete citationsPrecise bibliographic detail is not recorded at the moment of discovery and must be reconstructed laterCitation errors are most common among researchers who relied on memory under deadline pressure
Disorganized synthesisNotes, PDFs, citations, and bookmarks are scattered across multiple locations with no consistent systemRecognizing patterns and comparing sources requires being able to see them together, which scattered materials prevent

The Return on Organized Research

Researchers who manage their results systematically gain proportional benefits. Every source you save correctly is a source you can use. Every source you record fully is a source you can cite accurately. Every search you log is a search you do not have to run again. And every minute you spend organizing your materials during a research session is several minutes saved during the writing and revision stages that follow.

Core Principle

The goal of this chapter is not to turn research into filing work. It is to build the minimum set of habits that keep your research recoverable, organized, and usable — habits light enough to sustain over a long project, robust enough to protect the work you have already done.

Section 24.2

Bookmarking and Saving Links

The simplest way to hold on to a web-accessible source — a database article, a catalog record, a digital collection item, a government report, an online reference work — is to save its link. Bookmarking and link-saving are fast, low-effort, and ubiquitous. They are also, when done carelessly, almost useless.

Why Simple Bookmarking Often Fails

A browser bookmark is only as useful as the information attached to it. Most browsers save a link with the page title as the default label. A bookmark labeled "JSTOR" or "Article" or "Untitled" tells you nothing about why you saved it. A folder full of such bookmarks is nearly impossible to navigate days or weeks after the session that created it.

Links also break. Web pages move, are removed, or are placed behind paywalls. A URL that worked during your research session may fail when you return to it. Database article links are especially prone to this problem: the URL you see in your browser during a database session often includes session-specific parameters that expire when your session ends, making the link useless outside that session. Catalog records can also move if your library upgrades or replaces its catalog system.

None of this means you should not bookmark. It means you should bookmark intelligently.

Misinformation Alert — Session URLs Are Not Permanent

Never bookmark the URL visible in your browser's address bar while inside a database session. These URLs are frequently temporary and authenticated to your current session — they will not work later, and they will not work for anyone else. Always look for a "Permalink," "Stable URL," or "Share" option, or copy the source's DOI instead.

Intelligent Bookmarking

Add descriptive labels immediately. When you save a bookmark, rename it before moving on. A bookmark labeled "Winne 2008 — Closing the Food Gap — Beacon Press — food policy" is usable weeks later. A bookmark labeled "catalog.mgu.edu/record/49281" is not.

Use folders organized by project and topic. Create a folder structure in your browser that mirrors your research projects. A folder called "Research Project — Food Security" with subfolders called "Catalog Records," "Articles," "Government Sources," and "Digital Collections" gives every bookmarked link a context.

Note why you saved the link. Some browsers allow notes or tags attached to bookmarks. Use them. A brief note — "useful for section on urban agriculture policy" or "check if this is available through ILL" — saves you from having to re-evaluate the source each time you see it.

Export your bookmarks periodically. All major browsers allow you to export bookmarks as an HTML file. For long projects, doing this periodically creates a backup in case your browser profile is lost or corrupted.

Alternatives to Browser Bookmarking

For serious research projects, browser bookmarks are often not the best primary tool. They are session-specific, hard to search across projects, and not easily shared or exported to citation managers. Better alternatives include:

Reference managers. Tools like Zotero, Mendeley, and EndNote are designed specifically for saving and organizing research sources. They capture full bibliographic information, not just a URL, and they organize sources in a searchable, sortable library with tags, notes, and citation export. Section 24.7 covers reference managers in detail.

Research notebooks. A dedicated research notebook — digital or physical — where you record source information as you find it gives you a permanent, searchable record that does not depend on any particular browser or device.

Database account folders. Most academic databases allow you to create a personal account where you can save articles, searches, and citation lists to folders that persist across sessions. This is discussed further in Section 24.5.

Handling Session-Specific URLs

When working in academic databases, do not bookmark the URL from your browser's address bar. Instead, look for a "Permalink," "Stable URL," "Persistent URL," or "Share" option within the database interface. These options generate a URL designed to remain stable and accessible to authenticated users. If no persistent link option is available, copy the source's DOI (Digital Object Identifier), which is a permanent link to a specific article or document that will not break when URLs change.

For catalog records, look for a "Permalink" or "Share This Record" option in the catalog interface. Use that link rather than copying the URL from the address bar.

Section 24.3

Downloading and Naming Files

When sources are available for download — PDFs of articles, digitized book chapters, government reports, data files, images — downloading them is often the most reliable way to preserve access. A downloaded file lives on your device or in cloud storage and does not depend on continued access to a database, library subscription, or website.

But downloads only help if they are named and organized intelligently. A folder full of files named "article.pdf," "document(1).pdf," "download.pdf," and "fulltext.pdf" is nearly as useless as no folder at all.

A File Naming System for Research

Develop a consistent file naming convention and apply it to every file you download for research purposes. A good naming convention should encode the information you need to identify a file without opening it.

Recommended Naming Pattern
AuthorLastName_Year_KeywordFromTitle.pdf
  • ArticleWinne_2008_FoodGap.pdf
  • ArticleBrennan_2019_TruthCommissions.pdf
  • ReportEPA_2022_CleanWaterReport.pdf
  • ImageLocDigitalColl_1943_InternmentPhotograph.jpg
For government or institutional sources without individual authors, use the issuing organization as the first element. For undated sources, use "nd" (no date) in the year position: Author_nd_Title.pdf.

Consistency Matters More Than Perfection

Your naming system does not need to be elaborate. It needs to be consistent. Whatever convention you adopt, apply it to every file, every time, without exception. Inconsistent naming — sometimes using author name first, sometimes using title, sometimes using the database's default filename — defeats the purpose. After a few weeks of a consistent naming system, you will be able to find any file in your research folder by scanning filenames alone.

Folder Organization for Downloads

Create a dedicated folder for each research project. Within that folder, consider subfolders by source type or research subtopic:

Sample Folder Structure

Research_FoodSecurity/

  Catalog_Records/

  Journal_Articles/

  Government_Reports/

  Digital_Collection_Items/

  Background_Reading/

  Notes/

Keep all research files for a project in one place. Do not scatter downloads across your desktop, your downloads folder, and multiple cloud locations. Consolidation is the difference between a searchable archive and a digital pile.

Cloud Storage and Backup

For any project you work on over multiple sessions, store your research files in a cloud storage location — a university-provided cloud service, or another provider you use consistently — so that your files are accessible from any device and backed up against hardware failure. A research project lost because a laptop was stolen or a hard drive failed is an entirely avoidable catastrophe.

Name your cloud folder with the same naming convention you use locally. Synchronization between local and cloud storage keeps both copies current.

Section 24.4

Saving PDFs and Documents

PDFs deserve their own section because they are the most common format for academic articles, government reports, digitized books, and many other research sources — and because they present their own management challenges.

Why PDFs Are the Standard Format for Research

The Portable Document Format (PDF) was designed to display and preserve documents consistently across different devices, operating systems, and software applications. A PDF of an academic article looks the same on a Windows laptop, a Mac, a tablet, and a phone. The page numbers, figures, footnotes, and layout are preserved. This matters for citation: when you quote or cite a specific page from a PDF, the reader can find that exact page.

PDFs are also stable in a way that websites are not. A PDF saved to your device or cloud storage remains accessible even if the original source goes offline, moves behind a paywall, or changes. Researchers who read and annotate articles in PDF format have a permanent, citable record of the source as it appeared at the time of access.

Downloading PDFs from Databases

Most academic databases provide a "Download PDF" or "Full Text PDF" option for articles. Click this option rather than printing from the browser, which sometimes produces lower-quality output or fails to capture all content.

When downloading, check that you are getting the published version of the article — the version that matches the journal's published page numbers and formatting — rather than a preprint or author manuscript version. Preprints and author manuscripts may differ from the published version in content, organization, and pagination, which can cause citation errors.

After downloading, rename the file immediately using your naming convention. Do not leave files in your downloads folder with default names.

Annotating PDFs

Most PDF readers — including Adobe Acrobat Reader, Preview on Mac, and browser-based PDF viewers — allow you to highlight text, add comments, and make notes within the document. Annotating PDFs as you read is a powerful research practice:

Effective PDF Annotation Habits

Highlight key passages relevant to your research question.

Add margin comments that connect the passage to your research question, agree or disagree with the argument, or note related sources.

Mark page numbers where specific arguments, evidence, or quotations appear so you can return to them quickly.

Tag the document at the beginning with your own summary of why this source is useful and what its main argument is.

Annotated PDFs are substantially more useful than unannotated ones. An annotation records your thinking at the moment of reading, when the source is freshest in your mind. That thinking is valuable later when you are synthesizing sources and cannot remember exactly why you saved a particular article.

Saving Non-PDF Documents

Not all sources are PDFs. Government data may come as Excel spreadsheets. Images may be JPEGs or TIFFs. Maps may be georeferenced files. Archival documents from digital collections may be scanned TIFFs or JPEGs. Oral history recordings may be MP3s or MP4s.

Apply the same naming convention to all file types. Add a format indicator if it helps: Author_Year_Title_data.xlsx or LocColl_1942_Photograph_InternmentCamp.jpg.

For non-text sources like images and audio recordings, create a brief caption file — a plain text or word processing document — alongside the source file that records where the item came from, its date, its creator or institution, the collection name, the access date, and its relevance to your research. A caption file named with the same base as the source file (LocColl_1942_Photograph_InternmentCamp_caption.txt) keeps the description attached to the image without requiring you to embed information in a format that may not support it.

Section 24.5

Exporting Citations

Finding a source is only one task. Recording the source's full bibliographic information in a format you can use for citation is a separate task, and it must happen before you close the catalog record or database result page.

What Citation Export Means

Most academic databases, library catalogs, and digital collection interfaces offer a citation export function. This function packages the bibliographic information for a source — author, title, publication, volume, issue, page numbers, date, DOI, and other fields — into a standardized format that can be imported directly into a reference manager or used to build a citation manually.

FormatBest Used For
RISThe most widely supported format for citation import. Works with Zotero, Mendeley, EndNote, and most other reference managers. When in doubt, choose RIS.
BibTeXUsed with LaTeX typesetting systems. Appropriate if you write in LaTeX or a tool that integrates with it.
RefWorks formatOffered by some databases specifically for RefWorks, popular in some institutional settings.
Direct browser captureReference manager browser extensions (such as Zotero Connector) capture citation information directly from a database page with a single click, no export file required.

How to Export a Citation

The export function is usually accessible from within the full record of an individual source. Look for buttons or links labeled "Export," "Cite," "Save Citation," "Download Citation," "Export to citation manager," or similar. Different databases place this function in different locations: in many databases it appears at the top or bottom of the full article record; in library catalogs it may appear as a "Send to" option near the call number; in Google Scholar it appears as a quotation mark icon beneath each result.

Common Export Errors to Check For

After exporting or importing, always open the record in your reference manager and check it for accuracy. Automated exports are error-prone. Common problems include subtitles cut off or omitted, author names in the wrong order or format, missing volume or issue numbers, incorrect or missing publication year, page numbers recorded as ranges rather than start and end pages, and DOIs formatted incorrectly or not captured at all. Spend thirty seconds verifying each imported record before moving on.

Exporting from Specific Systems

Library catalogs. Most modern catalog systems offer citation export. In a catalog record, look for "Export," "Send to citation manager," or "Download record." Select RIS format and import into your reference manager.

JSTOR. The "Cite" button in a JSTOR article record provides export options including RIS and BibTeX, as well as pre-formatted citations in MLA, APA, and Chicago styles. Use the export function rather than copying the pre-formatted citation, because exported records are easier to verify and reformat.

PubMed. The "Save" function allows export in multiple formats. For citation managers, choose PubMed format or RIS.

EBSCO databases. The "Cite" or "Export" option provides RIS and other format options. EBSCO also allows you to create a personal account folder where you can save citations across sessions.

ProQuest databases. The "Cite" option offers RIS and RefWorks formats, as well as pre-formatted citations.

Google Scholar. While not a curated academic database, Google Scholar is widely used and offers citation export via the quotation mark icon. Export quality is lower than from primary databases because its metadata is harvested automatically from publisher pages and is frequently incomplete or incorrect. Always verify Google Scholar exports carefully.

Recording DOIs and Stable URLs

For digital sources, always record the DOI or stable URL at the point of discovery. A DOI (Digital Object Identifier) is a permanent, standardized identifier assigned to a specific document. It takes the form 10.XXXX/XXXXX and can be entered into any DOI resolver (such as doi.org) to locate the item. DOIs do not expire when journals change platforms, publishers change websites, or subscription arrangements change.

If a source has a DOI, use it in your citation. If it does not have a DOI (because it is not a formally published journal article, or because it was published before DOIs were standard), look for a stable URL, permalink, or accession number. Never use a session URL as a permanent reference.

Section 24.6

Keeping a Search Log

A search log is a running record of your research activity: where you searched, what terms you used, what filters or limiters you applied, what results you found, which items you saved, and what your next steps are. It is the single most underused research tool available to students, and one of the most valuable.

What a Search Log Is Not

A search log is not a list of sources. Sources belong in your reference manager or research notebook. A search log records the process of finding sources — the searches themselves — so that you can understand what you have already done, repeat productive strategies, avoid repeating unproductive ones, and resume a research session after a break without having to reconstruct your path from memory.

What a Search Log Contains

A search log entry records the following information for each distinct search:

  • DateWhen you conducted the search.
  • PlatformWhere you searched — catalog, specific database, open web, Google Scholar, digital collection, or other resource.
  • TermsExactly what you typed, including boolean operators and subject headings.
  • FiltersAny limiters applied — date range, document type, language, peer review status, region.
  • ResultsHow many items the search returned.
  • Items foundBrief identification of items worth saving, enough to find them again if needed.
  • VocabularyNew subject headings or terms discovered that improve subsequent searches.
  • AssessmentA one-sentence judgment of whether the search was productive and why.
  • Next stepsWhat to search next, based on what this search produced.

A Model Search Log Entry

The following example shows a complete search log entry for a research project on climate migration.

Model Search Log Entry

Date: November 3

Platform: JSTOR

Search: "climate migration" AND "policy" — filtered to peer-reviewed journals, 2015–present

Results: 312 items. Too broad.

Refined search: "climate-induced displacement" AND "domestic policy" — peer-reviewed, 2015–present

Results: 47 items. Much more focused.

Most useful items found:

Rigaud et al. (2018), "Groundswell: Preparing for Internal Climate Migration" — World Bank report; not peer-reviewed but high authority

Gemenne & Blocher (2017), "How can migration serve adaptation to climate change?" — The Geographical Journal — directly addresses research question

Subject headings discovered: "Environmentally induced migration" — more precise than "climate migration" for this database

Assessment: Productive session. The term "climate-induced displacement" works much better than "climate migration." "Environmentally induced migration" is worth testing in catalog.

Next steps:

Search "environmentally induced migration" in library catalog

Check Rigaud et al. bibliography for peer-reviewed sources

Search "climate displacement" AND "policy" in PubMed for health-focused articles

This entry takes three to five minutes to write. It saves hours later.

Formats for Search Logs

A search log can take several forms. Choose whatever you will actually maintain consistently.

A dedicated document. A word processing document or plain text file where you add new entries in reverse chronological order (most recent first). Label the file clearly and keep it in your research project folder.

A spreadsheet. A spreadsheet where each row is a search session and columns capture date, platform, terms, results, and notes. Spreadsheets are easy to scan and sort.

A research notebook. If you keep a physical or digital research notebook (covered in Chapter 33), the search log can be integrated there as a running section.

Within a reference manager. Some reference managers allow notes attached to groups or folders that can serve as a rough search log. This is a less structured option but keeps notes near the sources they produced.

Whatever format you choose, keep the log in the same place for the life of the project and add to it every time you conduct a search.

Section 24.7

Using Source Managers

A source manager — also called a reference manager or citation manager — is software designed specifically for collecting, organizing, annotating, and citing research sources. Reference managers are the professional-grade tool for managing search results, and they represent a significant upgrade over bookmarks, downloaded files, and manual citation recording.

What Reference Managers Do

They capture bibliographic information. When you import a source — by downloading a RIS file, clicking a browser extension, or manually entering information — the reference manager stores the full bibliographic record in a structured format. You record it once and it is available for use in any citation format.

They organize sources into collections. You can sort sources into folders, libraries, or collections by project, topic, or type.

They allow annotation. Most reference managers let you attach notes, highlights, and tags to individual sources, which remain associated with the source across sessions and projects.

They generate citations and bibliographies. Reference managers can produce formatted citations in MLA, APA, Chicago, and hundreds of other styles, and can insert in-text citations as you write when integrated with a word processor.

They attach files. You can attach downloaded PDFs and other files to source records, keeping the bibliographic information and the document together in one place.

Major Reference Managers

ToolCostBest For
ZoteroFree, open-sourceMost students. Captures source information directly from web pages with one click; integrates with Word, Google Docs, and LibreOffice; widely documented and supported.
MendeleyFree, with paid tiersStudents who want a social/collaborative dimension; built-in PDF annotation; institutional access often available through Elsevier subscriptions.
EndNotePaid desktop; free web versionAdvanced researchers and faculty, especially in medical and scientific fields; the most feature-rich but also the most complex option.
RefWorksOften institutionalWeb-based access without installation; less feature-rich than Zotero or Mendeley.
PaperpileSubscriptionStudents who write primarily in Google Docs; tight integration with that platform.
Recommendation

For most students, Zotero is the recommended starting point. It is free, well-supported, widely documented, and integrates with the tools most students already use.

Setting Up a Reference Manager

Setting up Zotero as an example:

  1. Download and install Zotero from zotero.org.
  2. Install the Zotero Connector browser extension for your browser. This extension allows you to capture source information from database pages and catalog records with a single click.
  3. Create a free Zotero account and enable sync. This backs up your library to the cloud and makes it accessible from any device.
  4. In Zotero, create a collection (folder) for your current research project.
  5. When you find a source in a database or catalog, click the Zotero Connector icon in your browser. Zotero captures the bibliographic information automatically and adds the item to your current collection.
  6. Open the imported record and verify that all fields are complete and accurate.
  7. Attach the downloaded PDF to the record if you have one.
  8. Add tags and notes relevant to your research question.

Once set up, a reference manager requires only a few seconds per source to use. The investment in setup pays off quickly.

Reference Managers and Citation Generation

When writing your research paper, reference managers can insert in-text citations and generate bibliographies automatically. The citation style can be changed instantly — switching from MLA to APA to Chicago with a single click — which is valuable when working across courses with different style requirements.

The Tool Is Not the Guarantee

Despite automation, always review generated citations before submitting. Reference managers make errors. Common problems include missing subtitles, incorrect author name formatting, wrong edition numbers, and missing access dates for online sources. The manager is a tool. The accuracy of your citations is your responsibility. Chapter 44 of this textbook covers citation tools and their limits in depth.

Section 24.8

Preventing Lost Sources

All of the practices described in this chapter serve a single overarching goal: preventing the loss of sources you have already found. This final section consolidates the chapter's guidance into a set of habits that, taken together, make lost sources very rare.

The Lost Source Problem in Detail

Sources are lost in five main ways.

Failure ModeWhat Happens
Session expirationDatabase sessions expire when you close your browser, disconnect from a library network, or leave a database idle too long. Any source not saved during the session may be difficult or impossible to retrieve.
Browser crashes and tab lossBrowser crashes, forced restarts, and accidental tab closings eliminate unsaved work. A session with thirty open tabs and no saved sources loses everything if the browser crashes.
File disorganizationDownloaded files with default names stored in the default downloads folder are effectively random and unidentifiable weeks later.
Hardware failureLaptops die. Hard drives fail. Files stored only on local devices are vulnerable.
Memory failureHuman memory is unreliable for bibliographic detail — author names, years, journal names, volume numbers, and page ranges are exactly the kind of precise detail memory discards quickly.

The Minimum Viable System

If you adopt only a minimum set of practices, adopt these five. They prevent the most common and most costly forms of source loss.

Five Minimum Habits

One. Record citation information for every source you intend to use at the moment you decide it is worth saving. Do not plan to record it later. Do it now.

Two. Name every file you download using a consistent naming convention before you move on to the next search.

Three. Write one search log entry for each research session, recording where you searched, what terms you used, what you found, and what your next steps are. This takes five minutes and prevents hours of repeated work.

Four. Import all saved sources into a reference manager (or at minimum, into a consistently organized document) at the end of each research session.

Five. Back up your research files regularly. Store them in cloud storage. Treat your research archive the way you would treat any other important document you cannot afford to lose.

Source Triage: Not Everything Needs to Be Saved

Managing sources effectively also means being selective. Not every item you find during a search deserves to be saved with full citation information. Some items are clearly irrelevant. Some are relevant in principle but too far outside your scope. Some are redundant with sources you already have.

Triage your results. Evaluate items briefly during a search session and make one of three decisions:

  • Save fullyThis source is directly relevant, appears credible, and deserves full citation recording, downloading if available, and a note explaining its relevance.
  • Save tentativelyThis source might be relevant but you are not sure yet. Save the citation information and a brief note, but mark it clearly as tentative so you know it needs further evaluation.
  • PassThis source is not relevant or is clearly inferior to sources you already have. Do not save it, but make a brief note in your search log explaining why (this prevents re-evaluating the same item later).

Being selective does not mean being hasty. A brief evaluation — reading the title, abstract, and subject headings of an article, or reading the catalog record and table of contents description of a book — takes thirty seconds to two minutes. That evaluation is time well spent. Saving everything indiscriminately and planning to evaluate later creates a pile of sources rather than a research archive.

Returning to Research After a Break

One of the most important benefits of organized search result management is the ability to return to a research project after a break — overnight, after a weekend, after a week away — without losing momentum.

Protocol
Ending and Resuming a Research Session
  • EndingWrite your search log entry for the session. Review your saved sources and confirm that all citation information is recorded. Write a brief note of where you are in the research process and what your next session needs to accomplish.
  • ResumingRead your search log entries from previous sessions. Review your list of saved sources. Read your "next session" note. Begin where you left off.
This protocol makes research feel continuous rather than fragmented, removing the cognitive overhead of reconstructing context at the start of every session.

The Research Archive

Think of the organized collection of sources, notes, search logs, and files you build during a research project as a research archive — a personal library of everything relevant to your project, organized so that you can find anything in it quickly.

A good research archive has these characteristics: every source is identified by consistent naming; every source has complete citation information recorded; sources are grouped into logical categories or folders; a search log explains how the sources were found; annotations indicate why each source was saved and how it connects to the research question; and the archive is backed up and accessible from more than one device.

Core Principle

Building a research archive is not additional work on top of research. It is research, done carefully. Students who build research archives write better papers, write them faster, and cite them more accurately than students who manage sources informally.

Search Practice

Activity 24AReference Manager Setup and First Import

If you have not yet set up a reference manager, complete the following setup activity before your next research session.

  1. Download and install Zotero from zotero.org (or use the reference manager your institution provides).
  2. Install the browser connector extension for your default browser.
  3. Create a free account and enable cloud sync.
  4. In your reference manager, create a collection or folder named for your current research project.
  5. Find one article in an academic database your library provides access to.
  6. Use the browser connector to import the article's citation information directly into your reference manager.
  7. Open the imported record and check every field: author name, title (including subtitle), journal name, volume, issue, year, page numbers, DOI.
  8. Correct any errors you find.
  9. Write a brief note in the record explaining why this source is relevant to your research question.
  10. If the full text PDF is available, download it and attach it to the record.

Bring your reference manager record to class or include it in a brief reflection. Note: what information was captured correctly? What had to be corrected?

Activity 24BSearch Log Practice

Conduct a single research session on a topic of your choice or assigned by your instructor. The session should include at least three separate searches across at least two different platforms (for example, the library catalog and one academic database).

After the session, write a complete search log entry using the model provided in Section 24.6. Your log entry must include:

  1. Date and duration of session
  2. Each platform or database searched
  3. Exact search terms used in each search
  4. Any filters applied
  5. Number of results
  6. Items saved (titles and authors only)
  7. New vocabulary or subject headings discovered
  8. Assessment of which searches were productive and why
  9. Next steps for the next session

Exchange search logs with a classmate. Read each other's logs. Identify: Are the searches reproducible — could you run the exact same search again based on what is recorded? What is unclear or missing?

Activity 24CFile Naming and Organization Audit

Look at your downloads folder or the most recent folder where you stored files for a class project.

  1. How many files are named with default browser-generated names (e.g., "document.pdf," "article(2).pdf," "fulltext.pdf")?
  2. Can you identify what each file contains without opening it?
  3. Create a naming convention for research files using the pattern from Section 24.3.
  4. Rename all files related to a current research project using your new convention.
  5. Create a project folder structure with subfolders for articles, government sources, catalog records, and notes.
  6. Move all renamed files into the appropriate subfolders.

Reflect: How long did this reorganization take? What would have been different if you had applied the naming convention when you originally downloaded the files?

Source Check

Practice Source Evaluation: Evaluating a Search Result Before Saving

The following scenario presents a search result. Read it and answer the evaluation questions to practice the triage decision: save fully, save tentatively, or pass.

Search Context
Researching social media's effects on adolescent mental health for a psychology course; seeking peer-reviewed sources from the last seven years
  • Title"Always On: How Constant Connectivity Rewires the Teenage Brain"
  • SourceThe Atlantic, March 2021
  • AuthorRebecca Garber (listed as a contributing writer)
  • DescriptionA feature article reporting on new research linking high social media use to increased rates of depression and anxiety in teenagers aged 13–17. Summarizes findings from several recent studies, includes quotes from two developmental psychologists. No DOI provided; accessed via open web.

Evaluation questions:

  1. Relevance. Is this article directly relevant to your research question? What specific elements of the source match what you are looking for?
  2. Authority. What does the source tell you about the author's expertise? What does publication in The Atlantic tell you about the article's type and review process?
  3. Evidence. The article summarizes findings from "several recent studies." As a researcher, should you use this article as your primary source for those study findings? Why or why not?
  4. Purpose. The Atlantic is a general-interest magazine. What audience is this article written for, and how does that affect how you would use it?
  5. Currency. The article is from 2021. Is this appropriate currency for research on adolescent mental health and social media?
  6. Triage decision. Would you save this source fully, save it tentatively, or pass? Explain your reasoning.
  7. Follow-up. The article mentions "several recent studies." What search strategy would you use to find those original studies?

Misinformation Alert

The False Confidence of a Full Folder

One of the more subtle misinformation risks in the research process is not about false information from bad sources — it is about false confidence from the appearance of organization. It is possible to build a large, well-organized reference manager library or research folder that is full of sources you have never critically evaluated. A Zotero library with forty imported citations looks impressive. It is not impressive if the citations were collected quickly from a Google Scholar search, never verified for accuracy, never read beyond their abstracts, and never evaluated for relevance, authority, or evidence quality.

Saving a source is not the same as evaluating it. Importing a citation is not the same as reading the article. Having a well-named PDF does not mean the source is reliable.

The misinformation risk here is self-deception: believing that because your research materials look organized, your research is solid. A folder full of poorly evaluated sources supports poorly evidenced claims just as surely as a browser full of random bookmarks. Apply the MGU Misinformation Check at the source level, not just to individual claims within sources:

MGU Misinformation Check

Who made this source?  What is the main claim?  What evidence is offered?  What is missing?  Can another reliable source confirm the key findings?  Is the emotional reaction stronger than the evidence?  Has the source been altered, misrepresented, or taken out of context?

A well-managed source library is a tool for research, not a substitute for it. The organization of your materials supports your evaluation work. It does not replace it.

Citation Check

How to Record and Verify Citation Information for Saved Sources

Every source you save should be accompanied by complete, verified citation information recorded at the moment of discovery. The following guide shows what must be recorded for the most common source types and how to verify it.

Books

Required elements for all citation styles: author(s) full name, last name first; full title including complete subtitle; edition (if not first); city of publication; full publisher name; year of publication; call number for navigation (not part of the citation); format (physical, ebook, digitized copy).

Verification Step

Check the title page and the verso (back) of the title page. The title page gives the full title and publisher. The verso gives the copyright date and edition information. The date on the cover or spine may be a later printing and should not be used.

Common Error

Using the date of a later printing rather than the original copyright date. If a book was first published in 2005 and the copy you have is a 2018 printing, the citation date should be 2005 unless it is a revised or updated edition.

Journal Articles

Required elements: author(s) full names; full article title including subtitle; full journal name; volume number; issue number; year of publication; page range (first and last page); DOI if available.

Verification Step

Open the PDF and check the article's first page for the author names and title exactly as published. Check the journal header or footer for volume, issue, and page numbers. Find the DOI — it usually appears on the first page near the author information or at the bottom of the page.

Common Error

Using the database's record rather than the article itself to verify page numbers and volume information. Database records frequently contain errors in these fields. Always verify against the article.

Style Comparison
González (2015) Article in Three Citation Styles
  • MLAGonzález, Thalia. "Keeping Kids in Schools: Restorative Justice, Punitive Discipline, and the School-to-Prison Pipeline." Journal of Law and Education, vol. 44, no. 2, 2015, pp. 281–335.
  • APAGonzález, T. (2015). Keeping kids in schools: Restorative justice, punitive discipline, and the school-to-prison pipeline. Journal of Law and Education, 44(2), 281–335.
  • ChicagoGonzález, Thalia. "Keeping Kids in Schools: Restorative Justice, Punitive Discipline, and the School-to-Prison Pipeline." Journal of Law and Education 44, no. 2 (2015): 281–335.

Websites and Online Documents

Required elements: author(s) or organization responsible for the content; title of page or document; title of website or larger work; publisher or sponsoring organization (if different from author); date of publication or last update (use the most specific available); URL (stable or permalink, not session URL); access date.

Verification Step

Scroll to the bottom of the page for the "last updated" date. Check the "About" section of the website for the organization responsible for the content. Look for a "Contact" or authorship statement.

Common Error

Omitting the access date for frequently updated web content. For stable government or institutional documents, access dates may not be required in all styles; check your style guide. For content that may change, always record when you accessed it.

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 24.1Research is iterative and nonlinear, and unmanaged search results create four costs: lost sources, duplicated effort, incomplete citations, and disorganized synthesis. Managing results is a core research skill with a small investment and a large return.
  • Sec 24.2Browser bookmarks are useful only when labeled descriptively, organized into project folders, and built on persistent URLs or DOIs rather than session-specific links. Reference managers, research notebooks, and database account folders are stronger alternatives for serious projects.
  • Sec 24.3Downloaded files must be named with a consistent convention — AuthorLastName_Year_KeywordFromTitle — and organized into dedicated project folders with subfolders by source type, then backed up to cloud storage.
  • Sec 24.4PDFs are the standard, stable format for academic sources. They should be downloaded as the published version, renamed immediately, and annotated while reading. Non-text sources need the same naming discipline plus caption files.
  • Sec 24.5Citation export moves bibliographic data directly from databases into reference managers using formats like RIS or BibTeX, but every export must be verified for missing subtitles, incorrect author formatting, and missing DOIs. DOIs and stable URLs should always be recorded over session URLs.
  • Sec 24.6A search log records the process of research — platforms, terms, filters, results, vocabulary discovered, assessment, and next steps — and takes only a few minutes per entry while saving hours of repeated work.
  • Sec 24.7Reference managers like Zotero capture bibliographic information, organize sources into collections, allow annotation, attach files, and generate citations and bibliographies automatically, though generated citations always require human verification.
  • Sec 24.8Sources are lost through session expiration, browser crashes, file disorganization, hardware failure, and memory failure. A minimum viable system of five habits, combined with source triage and a maintained research archive, prevents nearly all source loss.

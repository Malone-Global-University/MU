Chapter 22 — Front Matter

Research Skill Focus

This chapter teaches students how to move beyond simple keyword searching into the more powerful territory of controlled vocabulary, subject headings, database filters, and citation chaining. Students will learn why keywords alone often fail to capture the full scope of a topic, how subject heading systems organize scholarly knowledge into stable, searchable categories, how database filters and facets help narrow results without changing search terms, and how citation chaining — both backward into a source's references and forward into sources that cite it — builds research networks that keyword searching alone cannot construct.

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to use subject headings strategically, apply filters and facets with purpose, trace citations in both directions, and build a source network that reflects the actual scholarly conversation on their topic rather than a random sample of keyword matches.

Why This Matters

Keyword searching is where most student researchers begin and, too often, where they stop. Keywords are a natural starting point — you think of words that describe your topic, type them into a search box, and see what appears. This approach is fast and intuitive, but for serious academic research it has limits that become visible the moment you move beyond surface-level topics. Subject headings, filters, and citation chaining transform research from a search for matching words into a navigation of interconnected ideas. Students who master them find more relevant sources, miss fewer important ones, and understand their topics more deeply than students who remain at the keyword level. This chapter matters because keyword searching is a starting point, not a destination.

Why Keywords Fail and How Controlled Vocabulary Fixes It
Failure Modes · Controlled Vocabulary Systems · Subject Headings
Section 22.1

Why Keywords Are Not Enough

Before you can understand why subject headings and citation chaining are necessary, you need to understand exactly where keyword searching breaks down. Keyword searching fails in several predictable ways, and recognizing those failure modes is the first step toward correcting them.

The Vocabulary Problem

Academic subjects have multiple names. Legal scholars, sociologists, economists, and public health researchers may all study the same population but use different disciplinary vocabularies to describe them. A historian writing about child labor in nineteenth-century England may use terms like "factory children," "climbing boys," or "pauper apprentices" — period-specific language that does not match the modern keyword "child labor" that a beginning researcher would type. A psychologist writing about depression may use "major depressive disorder," "unipolar depression," "clinical depression," or simply "MDD" depending on the decade and the journal. A keyword search built around one of these phrases will miss the others.

The vocabulary problem is not limited to historical or technical language. It exists wherever scholars disagree about how to name something, where field-specific jargon has replaced everyday language, or where a topic sits at the intersection of multiple disciplines that use different terminology. Environmental science, public health, legal studies, and economics all study climate change, but their vocabularies differ in ways that make cross-disciplinary keyword searching unreliable.

The Precision Problem

Keyword searches also suffer from a precision problem: they retrieve everything that contains your search words, regardless of whether those words are the main topic of the source. A keyword search for "water" in a history database might retrieve articles about irrigation, naval warfare, public health infrastructure, the symbolism of water in literature, the chemistry of water treatment, and the theology of baptism. The word "water" appears in all of them, but most are not about water as a research topic in any unified sense.

Adding more keywords helps but does not fully solve the precision problem. "Water rights" is more specific than "water," but it still retrieves sources where "water rights" appears anywhere — in a footnote, in a passing reference, in a title that mentions water rights only as context for another argument. Subject headings, by contrast, are assigned to sources where the topic is central, not incidental.

The Completeness Problem

Keyword searching is also incomplete in a way that is invisible to the searcher. You cannot know what you missed, because you can only see what your search returned. A keyword search for "prison education" might return forty results. But if thirty other relevant sources are indexed under "correctional education," "incarcerated students," "prison literacy programs," or "education in detention facilities," you will never see them unless you happen to search those exact phrases too. The forty results you found may look like a complete picture. They are not.

Subject headings address the completeness problem by gathering all sources on a topic under one consistent term, regardless of the words individual authors used. If you find the right subject heading, you find everything indexed under it — not just the subset that happens to match your particular word choices.

The Serendipity Problem

There is one more limit of keyword searching that is harder to name: it provides no browsing structure. When you search by keyword, you get a ranked list of matches. You cannot easily see the shape of a subject, the subcategories within it, the adjacent topics that might be relevant, or the broader contexts that frame it. Subject headings, by contrast, are organized into hierarchies — broader terms, narrower terms, and related terms — that give you a map of a subject rather than a list of keyword hits. Browsing that map often surfaces relevant topics and approaches that you would never have thought to search for directly.

Core Principle

Understanding the vocabulary problem, the precision problem, the completeness problem, and the serendipity problem makes clear why keyword searching is a beginning, not an end. The rest of this chapter teaches the strategies that take you beyond it.

Section 22.2

Controlled Vocabulary

Controlled vocabulary is a system of standardized terms used to describe and retrieve information consistently. Unlike natural language, which varies across speakers, writers, time periods, and disciplines, controlled vocabulary is fixed: one term is chosen to represent each concept, and that term is applied consistently across an entire indexing system.

The alternative to controlled vocabulary is natural language indexing, which is what most internet search engines use. A search engine like Google retrieves pages that contain the words you typed, regardless of whether those pages are actually about your topic. The engine has no controlled vocabulary; it just matches strings of text. This works well for many everyday searches but poorly for systematic academic research.

Controlled vocabulary systems work by making a single decision about terminology and enforcing it. If the controlled vocabulary for a database decides that the correct term for research on people without legal immigration status is "undocumented immigrants," then every article in that database about that population will be indexed under that term — whether the article's own author used "undocumented," "unauthorized," "illegal," or "undocumented noncitizen." As a searcher, you need only know the controlled term to find all the relevant articles.

The Structure of Controlled Vocabulary

Controlled vocabulary is not a flat list of unrelated terms. It is a structured system with internal relationships. Most controlled vocabulary systems include three types of relationships:

RelationshipDefinitionExample
Broader Term (BT)A term representing a more general concept"Immigrants" is broader than "undocumented immigrants"
Narrower Term (NT)A term representing a more specific concept"Child labor — Agricultural" is narrower than "child labor"
Related Term (RT)A term representing an associated concept, neither broader nor narrower"Refugees" is related to "immigrants"; "poverty" is related to "food insecurity"

These relationships are the architecture of controlled vocabulary. They let you navigate a subject up, down, and sideways — broadening your search if you are not finding enough, narrowing it if you are finding too much, and exploring adjacent areas if your initial angle is not productive.

Controlled Vocabulary in Different Systems

Different databases and library systems use different controlled vocabularies. Some of the most common include:

SystemUsed BySubject Focus
Library of Congress Subject Headings (LCSH)Library catalogs throughout the U.S. and internationallyGeneral, book-level catalog searching
Medical Subject Headings (MeSH)PubMed and other biomedical databasesMedicine and health sciences
Thesaurus of ERIC DescriptorsERIC databaseEducation research
PsycINFO ThesaurusPsycINFO databasePsychology and behavioral sciences
Art & Architecture Thesaurus (AAT)Getty Research Institute resourcesArt history, architecture, decorative arts, material culture
Sociological Abstracts ThesaurusSociological Abstracts databaseSociology and related social sciences

Each database uses its own controlled vocabulary, which means the terminology that works perfectly in one database may not work in another. A researcher who learns to use MeSH in PubMed will need to learn different terminology for searching in JSTOR or a sociology database. This is one reason why starting a search in a new database always involves some initial exploration — you need to find the controlled vocabulary the database uses before you can search with real precision.

Study Note — Finding the Controlled Vocabulary

Most databases provide access to their controlled vocabulary through a thesaurus, subject browser, or index tool. Before you begin searching a new database, look for a "Browse," "Thesaurus," or "Subject Headings" link, often in the database's navigation menu or help section. Browsing the thesaurus first — or searching the thesaurus for your topic — will often reveal the correct controlled term before you have submitted a single search.

If you cannot find the thesaurus, conduct a keyword search, find one or two clearly relevant results, and then open those results to examine what subject terms, descriptors, or headings have been assigned to them. Those terms are the controlled vocabulary for your topic in that database. From there, search using those terms directly rather than relying on keyword matching alone.

Section 22.3

Subject Headings

Subject headings are the form of controlled vocabulary most relevant to library catalog and database searching. A subject heading is a standardized phrase assigned to a source by a professional indexer or cataloger to describe the source's primary topic or topics.

Subject headings are not tags or keywords that authors apply to their own work. They are assigned by trained people following consistent standards, which means they are more reliable and more precise than the author's own choice of words. A book titled A River in Winter might be entirely about salmon habitat loss in Pacific Northwest streams, but you would never find it through a keyword search for "salmon" or "habitat conservation." If the cataloger assigned the subject heading "Salmon — Habitat — Pacific Northwest," you can find it instantly.

How Subject Headings Are Structured

Subject headings in the Library of Congress system follow a specific structure: a main heading followed by optional subdivisions that specify the heading further. Subdivisions are separated by dashes.

Subdivision TypeFunctionExample
TopicalNarrows a main heading to a specific aspectEducation — Economic aspects; Cancer — Diagnosis
GeographicNarrows a main heading to a specific locationPoverty — United States; Agriculture — India
ChronologicalNarrows a main heading to a specific time periodUnited States — History — Civil War, 1861–1865; Art — 20th century
FormSpecifies the type of materialStatistics; Periodicals; Bibliography; Case studies; Fiction

These subdivisions can be combined in various orders to produce highly specific headings. A heading like Women — Employment — United States — History — 20th century is far more precise than the single word "women" would be as a keyword, and it retrieves only sources indexed under exactly that combination.

Reading Subject Heading Assignments in a Catalog Record

When you open a catalog record for a book or an article record in a database, subject headings appear in a designated field, usually labeled "Subject," "Subject Headings," or "Descriptors." In library catalogs using LCSH, these headings appear in the full record and are often hyperlinked so that clicking on them runs an immediate search for other items with the same heading.

The key habit is this: when you find one relevant item, always open its full record and read all of its subject headings. Then click on the most relevant heading to retrieve all other items in the catalog or database assigned that heading. This single step often produces better results than a dozen additional keyword searches.

Example 22.3 — Following Subject Headings
Research question: How does solitary confinement affect the mental health of incarcerated people?
  • Keyword solitary confinement mental healthReturns a mixed set of results — some very relevant, some only tangentially so.
  • Headings found Solitary confinement — Psychological aspects · Prisoners — Mental health · Correctional psychology · Isolation (Psychology) · Prison conditionsOpening one clearly relevant article reveals these five subject headings assigned to it.
  • Expanded search Searching "Solitary confinement — Psychological aspects" retrieves every article indexed under that precise heading. "Correctional psychology" surfaces research using different language. "Isolation (Psychology)" connects the topic to psychological literature on isolation in other contexts, such as sensory deprivation experiments.
Principle: Each subject heading is a door into a different section of the scholarly literature. Following all of them is more thorough than any keyword strategy alone.

Subdivisions as Research Refinement Tools

Subject heading subdivisions also serve as precision tools. If "Poverty" returns thousands of results and you need material specifically about poverty and health in sub-Saharan Africa, searching the heading "Poverty — Health aspects — Africa, Sub-Saharan" is far more efficient than adding keywords like "health" and "Africa" to a general search. The subdivision structure is built by catalogers who have already done the organizational work for you. Using it saves you from sorting through irrelevant results manually.

When Subject Headings Are Not Available

Not every database uses subject headings in the same way, and some databases rely primarily on author-assigned keywords or abstract-level indexing rather than controlled vocabulary. When you are working in a database without rich subject heading assignments — or when you cannot find the relevant heading — revert to keyword searching with synonyms, and then follow any controlled vocabulary terms you find in relevant records to continue your search more precisely.

Section 22.4

Filters and Facets

Once you have a set of search results — whether from a keyword search, a subject heading search, or some combination — database filters and facets let you refine those results without submitting an entirely new search. Filters are one of the most practical tools in academic database searching, yet many students scroll past them without using them at all.

What Filters and Facets Are

A filter is a limiting option that narrows a set of search results to only those that meet a specific criterion. Common filter types include:

Filter TypeWhat It Does
Date or date rangeLimits results to items published within a specific year range
Source typeLimits to scholarly articles, books, book chapters, conference papers, dissertations, newspapers, or other specific types
Peer reviewedLimits to sources that have undergone peer review
Full text availableLimits to sources for which the full text is immediately accessible
LanguageLimits to sources published in a specific language
GeographyIn some databases, limits to sources about a specific country or region
PublicationLimits to articles from a specific journal or set of journals
AuthorLimits to works by a specific author

A facet is similar to a filter but works slightly differently in interface design. Facets typically appear as sidebar panels showing categories and counts — for example, "By Publication Date: 2020 (143), 2019 (97), 2018 (88)..." — and you narrow results by clicking on a category. Facets are dynamic: clicking one facet narrows the results and updates all other facets to reflect the new, smaller set. Filters and facets accomplish the same function; the terms are sometimes used interchangeably depending on the database.

Using Filters Strategically

The strategic use of filters depends on your research needs at a given stage of the process.

Filter Strategy by Research Stage

At the beginning of a project: Use fewer filters. Broad early searching helps you understand the scope and shape of a topic. The "peer reviewed" filter is often appropriate immediately, but restricting by date or geography too early may cut off important context.

As your research develops: Apply filters more selectively to narrow results to exactly what your research question needs.

When results are too numerous: Add a filter to prioritize, then remove it to see the full set again — filters do not need to be permanent.

When results are too few: Resist removing all filters immediately. Identify which filters are limiting results most severely, and relax those selectively.

The Peer Review Filter

The peer review filter deserves special attention because it is widely used and widely misunderstood.

Marking a source as "peer reviewed" in a database filter does not guarantee that the source is high quality, accurate, or free from error. Peer review is a process, not a quality certification. It means that the manuscript was sent to other experts in the field for anonymous evaluation before publication, and that those reviewers recommended acceptance (with or without revisions). But peer review is imperfect: reviewers can miss errors, hold biases, fail to replicate studies, or simply approve work that is methodologically weaker than ideal.

Peer review does mean that the source went through more structured expert scrutiny than a blog post, a news article, or a self-published book. In academic research, peer review is a reasonable baseline requirement for scholarly sources. But the peer review filter should be understood as a starting point for evaluation, not a substitute for it.

A Common Misunderstanding

The peer review filter in many databases is applied at the journal level, not the article level. It identifies articles published in journals that use peer review, not articles where peer review was specifically verified for that individual piece. Some items in peer-reviewed journals — editorials, letters to the editor, corrections, commentaries — may not have gone through the same review process as research articles. Reading the article itself, not just the database label, helps you understand what type of source you are using.

Filter Combinations

The most powerful use of filters involves combining them.

Example 22.4 — Combining Filters for Different Research Needs
Two students, two filter strategies
  • Student A Researching the effects of early childhood education programs on long-term academic outcomes. Applies: source type = peer-reviewed articles; date range = 2010–2025; subject = early childhood education OR early intervention programs. Result: thousands of potential sources narrowed to a manageable, relevant, scholarly set focused on recent research.
  • Student B Researching media coverage of immigration during the 1980s. Applies: source type = newspaper articles; date range = 1980–1989; geography = United States. Result: a very different kind of set — primary source journalism useful for historical research, not peer-reviewed scholarship.
Principle: Filter choices should reflect your research question, not just your preference for convenience.

Database-Specific Filters

Different databases offer different filter options, reflecting their subject focus and collection design. A biomedical database like PubMed offers filters for clinical trial type, age group, sex, and species — criteria important for medical research but irrelevant in a literature database. A law database may filter by jurisdiction, court level, or type of legal document. A newspaper database may filter by section, author, or city edition.

When you enter a new database, take a few minutes to explore what filters it offers before you begin searching. Understanding the available filters helps you plan your search strategy more efficiently.

Section 22.5

Citation Chaining Backward

Citation chaining is one of the most powerful and underused research strategies available to academic researchers. It works by following the citations within and around a source rather than conducting additional keyword searches. There are two directions: backward (into a source's past) and forward (into a source's future). This section covers backward chaining; the next covers forward chaining.

What Backward Citation Chaining Is

Every academic book, article, and report contains citations — references to earlier works that the author built on, responded to, quoted, disputed, or synthesized. These citations are not merely footnotes. They are a map of the scholarly conversation that preceded the source you are reading. Backward citation chaining means following that map.

When you read the bibliography or reference list of a source and identify earlier works that appear relevant to your research question, you are beginning a backward citation chain. You are looking upstream from your source — finding the works that led to it.

Why Backward Chaining Works

Backward chaining works for a fundamental reason: scholars build on each other. A 2022 article about housing policy and health outcomes did not emerge from nothing. It builds on decades of prior research — studies from the 1970s establishing the relationship between housing quality and respiratory health, studies from the 1990s documenting health disparities in public housing, studies from the 2000s examining natural experiments when housing policies changed. All of that foundational research appears in the 2022 article's citations.

If you only find the 2022 article through keyword searching, you have one source. But if you follow its citations, you have access to the entire lineage of scholarship that made that article possible. The bibliography of a well-researched scholarly source is a curated reading list on your topic, assembled by a scholar who has already done the work of identifying which prior sources are most important.

How to Conduct Backward Chaining

Four-Step Process

Step 1. Find one strong, relevant source on your topic through keyword or subject heading searching.

Step 2. Read or skim the source carefully, noting which cited works appear most directly relevant. Pay attention to sources cited multiple times, cited in the introduction or literature review, cited in the most relevant sections, or described as foundational.

Step 3. Locate those cited sources through the library catalog, database searches, or interlibrary loan. Examine each one's bibliography and repeat the process.

Step 4. Stop when you begin finding the same sources repeatedly. Encountering the same works in multiple bibliographies signals that you have reached the core literature — the most foundational sources on your topic.

Reading a Bibliography Efficiently

Not every item in a bibliography is worth following. Backward chaining requires selectivity. Scan a bibliography for: works whose titles directly relate to your research question; works published by well-known scholars in the field; works published in high-quality journals or by major academic publishers; works cited specifically in the sections most relevant to your question; and works described in the text as "foundational," "influential," "seminal," or "definitive."

You do not need to trace every citation. You need to identify which citations are most worth your time, retrieve those, and then continue the chain selectively.

Backward Chaining Through Time

Backward chaining is especially valuable for historical research and for understanding how a field's thinking on a topic has evolved. By chaining backward from a recent article to a decade-old study to a classic work from the 1960s, you can trace the intellectual history of an idea — seeing which arguments came first, which were challenged, which were revised, and which became foundational. This historical perspective enriches your understanding of the topic far beyond what any single current source can provide.

It also helps you avoid a common research error: presenting as new something that is actually long-established, or ignoring foundational debates that still shape current scholarship. Backward chaining surfaces the historical context that keyword searches of recent literature often miss.

Example 22.5 — Backward Chaining in Practice
Research topic: long-term health effects of childhood poverty
  • Start A keyword search returns a 2021 review article in a health policy journal synthesizing thirty years of research.
  • Round 1 The article repeatedly cites a 1995 study on poverty duration and child health, a 2003 study on the Moving to Opportunity housing experiment, and a 2010 review of developmental neuroscience and poverty-related stress.
  • Round 2 Each of those three studies has its own bibliography. The 1995 study cites foundational economic and public health literature from the 1970s–1980s; the 2003 study cites experimental design and housing policy research; the 2010 review cites neuroscience literature on cortisol, stress, and brain development.
Result: Within two rounds of backward chaining, the researcher has moved from one 2021 source to thirty or more sources spanning half a century and three disciplines.
Section 22.6

Citation Chaining Forward

If backward chaining follows sources into their past, forward citation chaining follows sources into their future. It answers a different question: not "what did this source build on?" but "who has cited this source since it was published?"

What Forward Citation Chaining Is

When a scholarly work is published, other scholars read it, respond to it, apply it, challenge it, extend it, and correct it. All of that subsequent engagement appears in the reference lists of the later works. Forward citation chaining uses tools that track these citations to find sources that have built on your original source since it was published.

This is especially powerful for older or foundational sources. A study published in 1990 may have been cited by hundreds of more recent works. Following those "cited by" links takes you directly to the most current scholarship on the topic, written by scholars who found the original work important enough to engage with.

Tools for Forward Citation Chaining

ToolAccessNotes
Google ScholarFreely accessibleMost accessible forward-chaining tool; displays a "Cited by N" link below every result
Web of ScienceSubscription databaseMore precise citation tracking; covers a curated set of scholarly sources; provides citation maps
ScopusSubscription databaseRobust forward citation tracking across a large set of peer-reviewed journals; includes citation analysis tools
Library-specific toolsVaries by databaseSome library databases include "cited by" features within their own search interfaces

How to Conduct Forward Chaining

Five-Step Process

Step 1. Identify a foundational, important, or highly relevant source on your topic.

Step 2. Search for that source in Google Scholar or a citation-tracking database. Find the "Cited by" link.

Step 3. Click the "Cited by" link to see the full list of sources that have cited the original work.

Step 4. Filter or sort the "Cited by" results by date to find the most recent citations, or by relevance to find the most directly connected ones.

Step 5. Open the most relevant citing sources, examine their content, and add them to your research notes. Repeat as needed.

When Forward Chaining Is Most Valuable

Forward chaining is particularly valuable in three situations: when your source is several years old and the field may have moved substantially since then; when you are trying to understand impact, since citation volume offers a sense of how a source was received by the scholarly community; and when keyword searching has saturated, since forward chaining from your best sources often produces new, relevant material that no keyword search would have found.

The Difference Between Backward and Forward Chaining

Backward chaining retrieves older sources. Forward chaining retrieves newer ones. Together, they create a temporal arc — a chronological narrative of how a topic has developed in the scholarly literature from its origins to the present. This combination is especially powerful for research papers that require demonstrating familiarity with the full scope of a scholarly conversation, not just a snapshot of recent keyword matches.

Section 22.7

Bibliographies and Related Sources

Beyond citation chaining through individual sources, researchers can use bibliographies and related-source tools to navigate the scholarly landscape more efficiently. These tools work differently from citation chaining but serve complementary purposes.

Standalone Bibliographies

A standalone bibliography is a compiled list of sources on a topic, assembled not as part of a book or article but as a reference work in its own right. Bibliographies are valuable because they represent expert curation: a scholar or librarian has already done the work of identifying relevant sources, filtering out less relevant ones, and often annotating entries with descriptions of content and value.

TypeDescription
Subject bibliographiesComprehensive lists of sources on a specific academic topic or field, sometimes arranged thematically
Annotated bibliographiesBibliographies where each entry includes a brief description of the source's content, argument, and relevance
Bibliographies in reference worksReading lists at the end of encyclopedia or handbook articles, curated by subject experts
Course reading bibliographiesSyllabi and course reading lists that function as expert-curated bibliographies on a topic

Library Subject Guides as Source Networks

Many libraries, including the MGU Library, publish subject guides — also called research guides, LibGuides, or pathfinders — that compile the most useful databases, key journals, foundational texts, and reference sources for a specific discipline or topic. These guides are assembled by subject librarians who specialize in that field and update them regularly as the field evolves.

A well-constructed subject guide is not just a list of databases. It is a research network: a structured map of the most important resources in a field, organized to help researchers navigate from background knowledge through primary research into the deepest specialist literature. Using a subject guide before you begin searching saves you from discovering the most important resources one at a time through trial and error.

Related Records in Databases

Many academic databases include "Related Articles," "Similar Items," or "More Like This" features that identify other database records related to a source you are viewing. These features use algorithms to identify sources that share subject headings, keywords, authors, or citation patterns with the source you are looking at.

Study Note — Using Related Records Carefully

Related-records features are useful but imperfect. The algorithms that generate them are proprietary and not always transparent, and they may surface sources that share surface-level features without being genuinely intellectually related. Use related-records suggestions as starting points, not conclusions. Follow up promising suggestions by reading their abstracts and subject headings carefully before adding them to your research.

Reading Footnotes as Research Navigation

Academic texts — especially in humanities disciplines — use footnotes not just to cite sources but to conduct scholarly conversation. A footnote may credit an idea, dispute a claim, acknowledge a complicating counterargument, direct the reader to a deeper treatment of a subtopic, or note important works the author chose not to engage with at length. Reading footnotes carefully is a form of citation chaining that operates at the argumentative level, not just the bibliographic one.

When a footnote says something like "For a contrasting view, see [Author]" or "This argument draws on [Author]'s influential account of..." that is the author telling you exactly where the most relevant intellectual connections are. Following those footnotes takes you to the heart of the scholarly conversation, not just its surface.

Section 22.8

Building Research Through Source Networks

The strategies in this chapter — subject headings, filters, backward citation chaining, forward citation chaining, bibliographies, and related records — are most powerful when used together, as a coordinated approach to building a source network rather than a collection of unrelated search techniques.

What a Source Network Is

A source network is the connected set of sources that address your research question from multiple angles, time periods, disciplinary perspectives, and evidentiary types. It is not a random accumulation of keyword matches. It is a structured understanding of who has written what about your topic, how those sources relate to each other, where they agree, where they disagree, and where the most important debates lie.

Building a source network takes more time than compiling a keyword search results list. But it produces something qualitatively different: a researcher who has built a source network understands the field. A researcher who has only compiled keyword matches has a pile of documents.

A Practical Workflow for Network Building

Eight-Phase Workflow

Phase 1 — Initial keyword searching. Use Boolean operators, phrase searching, and field searching to generate a preliminary, oriented set of results.

Phase 2 — Subject heading identification. Open the full records for your two or three most relevant Phase 1 results and identify their subject headings. Search those headings directly.

Phase 3 — Filter application. Apply peer review, date range, source type, and geography filters to narrow results to your research question's requirements.

Phase 4 — Backward citation chaining. Follow bibliographies backward to identify foundational works and historical context.

Phase 5 — Forward citation chaining. Use Google Scholar or a citation-tracking database to find who has cited your most important sources since publication.

Phase 6 — Bibliography and guide consultation. Consult subject bibliographies, library subject guides, and reading lists for sources not yet found.

Phase 7 — Review and gap identification. Examine your source list for clustering and gaps in discipline, geography, or time period.

Phase 8 — Targeted searching to fill gaps. Return to databases, subject headings, and citation chains to fill the specific gaps identified in Phase 7.

Recognizing a Mature Source Network

How do you know when your source network is mature enough to support your research? Several signals indicate that you have reached a productive level of coverage:

SignalWhat It Indicates
ConvergenceThe same key sources, authors, and arguments appear repeatedly across results, citation chains, and bibliographies
Diminishing returnsNew searches and chains produce sources already found, or sources that are clearly not relevant
Coverage of major perspectivesYou can identify the major approaches, debates, key scholars, and significant disagreements within the topic
Temporal rangeYour sources cover both the historical development and the current state of the topic
Source type varietyYou have the mix of primary, theoretical, empirical, and review sources your question requires

Reaching this point does not mean you have read everything. It means you have built a network comprehensive enough to support responsible, well-grounded research. That is the goal.

Source Networks and Synthesis

The reason source networks matter goes beyond research efficiency. A source network is the foundation of synthesis — the process of combining sources to form a broader understanding that addresses your research question. You cannot synthesize sources you have not found. You cannot identify agreements, disagreements, patterns, and gaps across a literature you have only sampled at the surface through keyword matching.

The MGU Synthesis Model — which you will study in depth in Part VI — depends on having a source network that is broad enough to reveal agreements, disagreements, patterns, and gaps. You need sources from multiple perspectives to compare perspectives. You need sources from multiple time periods to trace development. You need sources addressing different aspects of your topic to construct a complete picture.

Core Principle

Building a strong source network through the strategies in this chapter is not separate from the synthesis work that comes later. It is the prerequisite for it.

Activity 22ASubject Heading Discovery

Choose a research topic from the list below or use a topic from a current research project: the psychological effects of social media use on adolescents; land rights and Indigenous communities in North America; the history of antibiotic resistance; urban heat islands and public health; or criminal justice reform and recidivism.

  1. Part 1 — Keyword search. Search your chosen topic using keyword searching in a library database available to you (JSTOR, ERIC, PsycINFO, EBSCOhost, or another database your library provides). Record your search terms and the number of results.
  2. Part 2 — Subject heading identification. Open the full record for the two or three most relevant results. Record all subject headings listed in those records.
  3. Part 3 — Subject heading search. Search one of those subject headings directly. Record the number of results and compare it to your keyword search results.
  4. Part 4 — Reflection. In your research notebook, answer: How did the subject headings differ from the keywords you used? Did the subject heading search retrieve sources your keyword search missed? Give one example. Did the subject heading search exclude sources your keyword search returned? Why might this be? What additional subject headings from the records would you search next?
Activity 22BCitation Chaining Practice
  1. Part 1 — Find a starting source. Using a database or the MGU Library catalog, find one peer-reviewed article or book chapter relevant to your research topic.
  2. Part 2 — Backward chaining. Examine the bibliography of your starting source. Select three cited works that appear most directly relevant. For each, record the full citation, search for it in your library catalog or a database, note whether it is available to you and how, and write one sentence explaining why it might be relevant.
  3. Part 3 — Forward chaining. Search for your starting source in Google Scholar. Find the "Cited by" number and click it. How many sources have cited your starting source? Filter to the last five years — how many remain? Identify two citing sources most relevant to your research question and record their full citation information.
  4. Part 4 — Reflection. Write a short paragraph (150–200 words) reflecting on what the citation chaining process revealed about your topic that keyword searching alone had not shown you.

Misinformation Alert

Alert
Treating High Citation Counts as Quality Guarantees

Forward citation chaining surfaces an important number: how many times a source has been cited. It is tempting to treat this number as a quality signal. A source cited 500 times must be better than one cited 10 times, right?

Not necessarily. Citation counts reflect many things besides quality: the age of a source (older sources have had more time to accumulate citations), the size of the field (large fields produce more citing articles than small ones), whether the source is a methodology paper (cited every time the method is used), and whether the source is cited because scholars agree with it or because they are critiquing it.

High Citation Counts Can ReflectLow Citation Counts Can Reflect
Genuine scholarly importance and qualityRecent publication — not enough time to accumulate citations
Widely used methodology or dataHighly specialized niche topics
Controversial claims that attracted significant critical responseWork published in smaller or non-English-language journals
Foundational claims that newcomers cite as contextWork that is significant but not yet widely discovered
Replication failures widely cited as cautionary examples—
Caution

A 2020 paper with 12 citations may be more methodologically rigorous than a 2005 paper with 400 citations. A frequently cited article may have been cited because it contains an error that many subsequent papers needed to correct.

Use citation counts as one data point among several, not as a substitute for evaluating a source's methods, evidence, and reasoning. The MGU Source Evaluation Model — Relevance, Authority, Evidence, Accuracy, Purpose, Currency, Context — is the right framework for evaluation. Citation counts may inform your assessment of authority and impact, but they do not replace it.

Citation Check

When you find sources through subject heading searching, citation chaining, or related source tools, record them in your research notes immediately. The retrieval path matters as much as the source itself.

For a journal article found through subject heading searching, record:

  1. Author(s), last name first
  2. Full title of the article
  3. Journal name
  4. Volume, issue, year, and page numbers
  5. DOI or URL if digital
  6. Subject heading(s) that led you to this source
  7. Database where you found it

For a source found through backward citation chaining, record:

  1. Full citation of the source you were reading when you found this reference
  2. The page number or footnote where the citation appeared
  3. Full citation of the source you are now following up
  4. Brief note on why this source appeared relevant from its citation context

For a source found through forward citation chaining, record:

  1. The original source you searched in Google Scholar or another tool
  2. The date you checked "Cited by" and the count at that time
  3. Full citation of the citing source you found
  4. Brief note on what it appears to add to the topic

Common Errors When Citing Sources Found Through Citation Chaining

Citing a source based on how a second source described it rather than reading the original — this is secondary or secondhand citation, and you should always retrieve and read the original before citing it, since accuracy cannot be verified from a secondhand description. Confusing the date of a foundational source with the date of a recent source that cited it — if a 1992 study was found through forward chaining from a 2020 article, the 1992 date is the one you cite, not 2020. Omitting page numbers for direct quotations found through citation chaining — the citation process does not change the requirement to record page numbers for quotations.

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 22.1Keyword searching fails in four predictable ways: the vocabulary problem (different terms for the same idea), the precision problem (matching words without matching topics), the completeness problem (invisible missed sources), and the serendipity problem (no browsing structure).
  • Sec 22.2Controlled vocabulary assigns standardized terms to sources regardless of an author's word choice, solving the vocabulary problem. Systems include LCSH, MeSH, ERIC Descriptors, and PsycINFO Thesaurus, each structured with broader, narrower, and related terms.
  • Sec 22.3Subject headings are standardized phrases assigned by catalogers, structured with topical, geographic, chronological, and form subdivisions. Reading and clicking subject headings in a relevant record is one of the most productive research moves available.
  • Sec 22.4Database filters and facets narrow results by date, source type, peer review status, language, and geography without changing search terms. The peer review filter is a useful baseline but not a substitute for source evaluation.
  • Sec 22.5Backward citation chaining follows a source's bibliography into the past, revealing foundational and historical works that a keyword search alone would miss.
  • Sec 22.6Forward citation chaining uses tools like Google Scholar's "Cited by" feature to find sources that have built on a starting source since its publication, revealing field impact and recent developments.
  • Sec 22.7Standalone bibliographies, MGU Library subject guides, related-records features, and footnotes provide additional pathways for expert-curated and algorithmically suggested sources.
  • Sec 22.8An eight-phase workflow integrates keyword searching, subject headings, filters, backward and forward chaining, and bibliography consultation into a coordinated process for building a mature, gap-free source network — the prerequisite for synthesis.

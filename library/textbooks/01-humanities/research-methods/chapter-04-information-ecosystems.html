Chapter 4 — Front Matter

Research Skill Focus, Overview, and Objectives

Research Skill Focus

This chapter teaches students how to recognize, navigate, and compare the different environments in which information is created, stored, distributed, and consumed. The central skill is information landscape awareness: the ability to look at any source and understand not just what it says, but where it comes from, how it was produced, what forces shaped it, and how it fits into the larger world of available information.

Why This Matters

Every research project begins with a search, and every search happens inside an information ecosystem. If you do not understand the ecosystem, you cannot search it well. You may find the wrong kind of source for your question. You may trust a source that looks credible but was produced without accountability. You may miss excellent evidence simply because you did not know where to look.

Consider two students working on the same research question: What are the long-term health effects of air pollution in urban areas?

The first student types the question into a general web search engine and reads the first three results — a news article summarizing a recent study, a blog post written by an individual with no stated credentials, and a sponsored page from an air purifier company. She treats these as roughly equivalent because they all appeared on the same page of results.

The second student knows that this question involves scientific and medical evidence, so he begins with a subject-specific academic database. He finds peer-reviewed studies published in public health journals. He also checks government health agency reports for official statistics. When he reads a news article summarizing a study, he knows to find the original study and read the actual data.

Both students searched. Only one understood the information ecosystem well enough to search it responsibly.

Opening Idea

Information ecosystems are not neutral. They are built by people, shaped by economics, driven by technology, and influenced by politics, culture, and institutional interests. Every type of source you encounter — a library database, a social media post, a peer-reviewed article, a podcast, an AI-generated summary — exists inside a system with its own logic, its own incentives, and its own rules about what counts as credible. Understanding those systems is not optional for a researcher. It is the foundation of everything else.

Section 4.1

What an Information Ecosystem Is

The word ecosystem comes from biology. In ecology, an ecosystem is a community of living organisms interacting with each other and with their physical environment — plants, animals, soil, water, sunlight, and the relationships among them. Remove one element and the entire system shifts.

An information ecosystem works by similar principles. It is the whole system of information sources, producers, distributors, platforms, institutions, technologies, and users that together create, store, circulate, and consume information in a society.

Components of the Information Ecosystem
Seven elements that shape every source you encounter
  • CreatorsThe people and organizations that create information — researchers, journalists, governments, companies, individuals, and algorithms.
  • FormatsThe forms in which information is recorded — books, articles, databases, websites, videos, podcasts, datasets, archives.
  • InstitutionsThe organizations that organize, preserve, and provide access to information — libraries, universities, publishers, platforms, archives.
  • TechnologiesThe systems that carry and distribute information — the internet, printing, broadcasting, databases, search engines, social media.
  • EconomicsThe funding models behind information production — subscriptions, advertising, grants, tuition, public funding, user data.
  • NormsThe social and legal frameworks governing information use — copyright, peer review, editorial standards, platform policies, professional ethics.
  • UsersThe people who read, interpret, share, and act on information — including you, the researcher.

Every source you find during research is a product of this ecosystem. A peer-reviewed journal article was shaped by academic peer review norms, university research infrastructure, publisher economics, and the conventions of a specific discipline. A tweet was shaped by the constraints of a commercial social media platform, its recommendation algorithm, and the social dynamics of the user's network. A government statistical report was shaped by agency methodology, political oversight, data collection resources, and reporting requirements.

None of these sources simply "contains information." Each one was produced by a system, and that system left marks on the source — in what it includes, what it excludes, how it is framed, and who it was intended to reach.

Why Ecosystem Thinking Matters for Research

Ecosystem thinking helps researchers ask better questions about sources. Instead of asking only Is this true?, a researcher who understands information ecosystems also asks who produced this and for what purpose, what system the source exists within, what incentives shaped its creation, what standards governed its production, who the intended audience was, what the source type does well and where its structural limits lie, and what other parts of the ecosystem might give a different or more complete picture.

These are not suspicious questions. They are not signs of distrust or cynicism. They are the questions that any responsible researcher asks, regardless of whether a source turns out to be reliable or not. Understanding the ecosystem is how you assess each source fairly and accurately.

The Modern Information Ecosystem Is Unusually Complex

For most of recorded history, the information ecosystem was relatively small and slow. Books took years to write and expensive processes to reproduce. Access to libraries was limited. News traveled by physical mail. If you wanted information on a specialized topic, you needed a specialist.

The modern information ecosystem is orders of magnitude larger, faster, and more complex. A single internet search may return millions of results within a fraction of a second. A single social media post can reach millions of people within hours. AI tools can generate thousands of words of text in seconds, drawing on vast amounts of training data. Anyone with internet access and a free account can publish information globally without any editorial review.

This abundance creates opportunity: more information than ever before is accessible to more people. It also creates serious challenges: more misinformation, more noise, more sophisticated manipulation, and more difficulty distinguishing reliable from unreliable sources. The skills taught in this textbook — and especially the ecosystem awareness taught in this chapter — exist precisely because navigating this complexity requires deliberate skill, not just access.

Major Zones of the Information Ecosystem

For practical research purposes, it helps to think of the information ecosystem as divided into several major zones, each with its own characteristics. This chapter examines seven of them in detail: books and reference works; scholarly articles and databases; news, magazines, and popular media; websites and open web sources; social media and user-generated content; data, images, audio, and video; and AI tools and generated information.

Understanding each zone means understanding not just what kinds of sources it contains, but how those sources are produced, what standards govern them, what their strengths are for research, and what their limitations are.

Section 4.2

Books and Reference Works

Books are among the oldest and most trusted sources in the information ecosystem. For centuries, they were the primary vehicle for communicating sustained, organized, and deeply developed knowledge. Understanding what books are and how they work as research sources is still essential, even in a digital age when a great deal of information arrives in shorter formats.

What Books Are

A book, in the research sense, is a substantial work of written content that develops a subject at length. Books may be monographs — scholarly books written by one author or a small group presenting original research, analysis, or argument on a specific topic; edited collections, in which a scholar or team invites multiple experts to contribute chapters on related topics and then edits and compiles the result; textbooks, written primarily to support instruction and typically surveying a field rather than presenting original research; handbooks and companions, comprehensive reference volumes covering the full scope of a discipline; and trade books, written for general audiences rather than academic specialists.

Books go through processes that vary significantly by type. Academic monographs typically go through peer review — expert readers who evaluate the scholarly quality of the manuscript before an academic press accepts it for publication. Trade books go through editorial review by publishing house editors, but not through the same kind of expert scholarly review. Textbooks are reviewed for pedagogical accuracy and appropriate level, and often go through multiple editions as knowledge in a field updates.

Reference Works

Reference works are books designed not to be read cover to cover but to be consulted for specific information.

How Books Support Research

Books are especially valuable for research that requires depth — a book-length treatment can develop complex arguments, present extensive evidence, and explore a subject from multiple angles in ways no article can match. They are valuable for context, since foundational arguments, theoretical frameworks, and the intellectual history of a debate are often laid out most fully in books. And reference works are invaluable early in the research process for building the background knowledge needed before moving to specialized sources.

A Limitation to Remember

Books take years to write and publish, which means they may not reflect the most recent developments. A book published in a rapidly changing field — technology, current policy, medicine — may be outdated by the time it reaches a library shelf. For current events and recent research, other source types are more appropriate. But for understanding the deeper context and intellectual landscape of a topic, books remain among the most valuable sources available.

The MGU Library makes books available in physical and digital formats. The library catalog allows you to search by title, author, subject, keyword, and call number. Subject headings in the catalog link you to related books in the same area. The encyclopedia section of the MGU Library is particularly valuable for reference works useful in the early stages of research.

Section 4.3

Scholarly Articles and Databases

Scholarly articles are the primary vehicles for communicating original research across most academic disciplines. Understanding how they are produced, where they are found, and what they can and cannot do is one of the most important research skills a university student can develop.

A scholarly article (also called an academic article, peer-reviewed article, or research article) is a relatively short, focused piece of writing — typically between a few thousand and twenty or thirty thousand words — that reports on original research, advances a scholarly argument, or reviews the existing literature on a topic. Scholarly articles are published in academic journals: periodicals published on a regular schedule, often monthly or quarterly, that specialize in a particular discipline or sub-field.

Peer Review

The defining characteristic of scholarly articles, and the feature that distinguishes them most sharply from other types of writing, is peer review. When a researcher submits an article to a peer-reviewed journal, the editor sends it to two or more expert reviewers — other researchers in the same field — who evaluate the work without knowing the author's identity. In many journals, the review is double-blind: neither the author nor the reviewers know each other's identities.

What Peer Reviewers Assess
The questions experts ask before an article is accepted
  • SignificanceWhether the research question is significant and original.
  • MethodsWhether the methods are appropriate and rigorous.
  • EvidenceWhether the evidence supports the conclusions.
  • LogicWhether the argument is logically coherent.
  • EngagementWhether the work cites and engages accurately with existing scholarship.
  • StandardsWhether the writing meets the standards of the field.
Outcome: accepted as is, accepted with minor revisions, revise and resubmit, or rejected — a process that typically takes months.

Peer review is not perfect. Reviewers have their own biases. Important work is sometimes rejected; flawed work sometimes passes. High-profile journals have retracted articles that turned out to be fraudulent or methodologically unsound. But peer review is the most rigorous systematic quality-control mechanism that exists in academic knowledge production, and a peer-reviewed article carries more evidentiary authority than a source that has undergone no comparable review.

Database Access

Scholarly articles are almost never freely accessible through a general web search. They are indexed and often stored in academic databases: specialized search systems that index thousands of journals in particular disciplines or across disciplines generally.

Access to most academic databases requires either a subscription, paid by a library on behalf of its users, or a fee per article. The MGU Library provides access to databases appropriate for the academic programs it supports. When you search through the MGU Library's database access points, you are searching curated, scholarly collections — very different from what a general web search returns.

What Scholarly Articles Can and Cannot Do

Best for: original research findings, peer-reviewed arguments and analysis, the current state of debate in a field, methodological approaches to a research question, and literature reviews that map what is already known.

Not the best source for: general background knowledge (use encyclopedias or textbooks), very recent events (publishing timelines mean articles are rarely current), policy recommendations for general audiences (look for government or organizational reports), or accessible introductions to complex topics (trade books or magazine articles may serve better).

The skill is not deciding that scholarly articles are "better" than other sources. The skill is knowing when scholarly articles are the right source for the question you are asking.

Section 4.4

News, Magazines, and Popular Media

Not all information comes from academic institutions. A large and vitally important part of the information ecosystem is produced by journalists, editors, commentators, and media organizations working outside the academy. Understanding this zone of the ecosystem — its strengths, its variations, and its limitations — is essential for any researcher.

News Reporting

News reporting is the attempt to inform a general audience about recent events, conditions, or issues. Professional journalism at its best is reported by journalists with training in finding, verifying, and interpreting information; based on interviews, documents, official records, and on-the-ground observation; subject to editorial oversight that checks accuracy before publication; transparent about sources to the extent possible while protecting confidential sources; and corrected when errors are discovered.

Quality news reporting is an essential part of the information ecosystem. It documents events as they happen, creates a record of public life, holds institutions accountable, and covers topics — from local government decisions to international crises — that academic researchers cannot address in real time.

News reports are particularly valuable in research for documenting that events occurred and when, capturing public reactions and debates at the time of an event, providing details about fast-moving situations before academic analysis catches up, and tracking policy changes, legal decisions, and institutional actions.

However, news reporting also has limitations. Journalists work under deadline pressure. Coverage is often incomplete, based on early information that may later be revised. Some news organizations have editorial biases. The business model of many news outlets — advertiser-supported or subscription-based — shapes what gets covered and how. Sensationalism sells; nuance often does not.

News Reporting Versus Opinion and Commentary

A crucial distinction must be drawn between news reporting (attempting to document what happened) and opinion and commentary (expressing a view about what happened or what should happen). Opinion columns, editorials, and commentary are clearly labeled in reputable publications, but students sometimes confuse them with neutral reporting. Opinion is a legitimate type of source but must be read and cited as opinion, not as documented fact.

Magazines and Feature Journalism

Magazines occupy a middle ground in the information ecosystem. They range from general-interest publications, which cover current events, culture, science, and politics for broad audiences, to specialized publications targeting specific professions or interest communities.

Feature journalism takes more time and depth than daily news reporting. A feature article in a quality magazine may take weeks or months to research and write, involving extensive interviews, document review, and analysis. Well-researched feature journalism can be an excellent source for understanding the human dimensions of a story, the broader context of an issue, or the perspectives of people affected by a situation.

Trade magazines and professional publications — which circulate within specific professions or industries — often report information not covered by general media, including industry data, regulatory developments, and professional debates.

Limitations of Popular Media

Popular media sources — news articles, magazine pieces, blog posts — have characteristics that researchers must keep in mind. They are typically written for general audiences, which means they simplify complex topics. They are rarely subject to the kind of expert review that academic sources receive. They may reflect the editorial perspective of the organization that publishes them. They are often written quickly, increasing the risk of errors. And advertising models can create pressure to generate clicks rather than ensure accuracy.

Using Popular Media Responsibly

This does not mean popular media sources should be dismissed. It means they should be read with appropriate awareness. A news article reporting on a new study is a useful starting point — but a careful researcher also finds the original study, reads the actual data, and does not rely solely on the journalist's summary.

Section 4.5

Websites and Open Web Sources

The open web — everything publicly accessible through web search engines — is one of the most complex and variable zones of the information ecosystem. It contains some of the most valuable information available, and also some of the least reliable.

The open web includes government websites and official agency pages; university and educational institution websites; research organization and think tank websites; nonprofit organization websites; news and media organization websites; corporate and business websites; personal and individual websites; blog platforms and independent publishing sites; community wikis and collaborative information sites; open access academic repositories and preprint servers; health information portals; and legal information databases.

The range is extraordinary. A government statistical agency website offers rigorously collected official data. A personal blog offers one individual's perspective, verified by no one. A university research center website may offer expert analysis. A corporate website offers promotional material written to serve the company's interests. A Wikipedia article offers a collaboratively edited summary that may be accurate, outdated, or somewhere in between.

Domain Types as One Indicator

The web address, or URL, of a website provides some limited information about its type.

Domain Type Is a Starting Point, Not a Verdict

A .gov site may present official data that is genuinely reliable — or it may reflect a politically shaped interpretation of that data. A .org site may represent a rigorous research institution — or it may be an advocacy organization promoting a specific agenda while appearing to offer neutral information. A .com site may host a major, professionally edited news organization — or a misinformation operation. Domain type tells you something about the organization running the site. It does not tell you whether the specific information on that site is accurate, current, or produced with appropriate rigor.

The Open Access Movement

An important development in the information ecosystem is the open access movement: the effort to make scholarly research freely available online rather than locked behind subscription paywalls. Open access takes several forms: gold open access, in which articles are published immediately as freely available on the publisher's website, sometimes funded by author fees; green open access, in which authors deposit pre-publication or accepted versions of articles in institutional repositories or subject repositories such as PubMed Central; and preprint servers, platforms where researchers post papers before peer review, such as arXiv for physics and mathematics or medRxiv for health research.

Preprints Have Not Been Peer Reviewed

Open access repositories can be excellent sources for scholarly research, but students must note whether a work has been peer reviewed. Preprint servers specifically publish work that has not yet undergone peer review. Preprints can reflect cutting-edge research — but they may also contain significant errors that peer review would normally catch.

Evaluating Open Web Sources

Because the open web is so variable, evaluating websites requires deliberate attention to several questions: Who runs this website, and what is their purpose? Who wrote this specific page or document, and what are their credentials? What evidence does the page cite, and can those sources be traced? When was the page last updated? Does the information agree with what other reliable sources say? Does the site disclose its funding, affiliation, or agenda?

Chapters 25 and 26 address source evaluation in detail. For now, the key point is that open web sources cannot be evaluated by appearance alone. A professional-looking website may contain misinformation. A plainly formatted page from a government archive may contain exactly the data you need.

Section 4.6

Social Media and User-Generated Content

Social media platforms now occupy an enormous portion of the modern information ecosystem. Billions of people use platforms such as Facebook, Instagram, X, YouTube, TikTok, Reddit, LinkedIn, and dozens of others to produce, share, and consume information. Understanding the social media zone of the ecosystem is essential — both because it is where much of the information people encounter now lives, and because it presents some of the most significant challenges to responsible research.

What Social Media Produces

Social media platforms are primarily built to facilitate social interaction and the sharing of user-generated content. The information that appears on these platforms includes personal posts, opinions, and commentary; news links shared and commented on by users; videos from individuals, organizations, news outlets, and entertainment creators; discussion threads and community forums; professional updates and industry commentary; real-time reporting from the scene of events; humor and satire; advocacy and activist organizing; misinformation, disinformation, and propaganda; and advertising and sponsored content.

No Visual Distinction Between Reliable and Unreliable Content

This list illustrates the extraordinary breadth — and the significant danger — of social media as an information environment. A single platform may simultaneously host a researcher's legitimate findings shared with colleagues, a satirical parody that some users mistake for fact, a deliberate disinformation campaign funded by a foreign government, and a heartfelt personal account of a lived experience. These exist side by side with no visual distinction.

How Algorithms Shape What You See

The information that appears in a social media feed is not neutral or comprehensive. It is shaped by recommendation algorithms: automated systems that predict what content each user is likely to engage with and prioritize that content accordingly.

These algorithms are designed to maximize engagement — not to maximize accuracy, diversity, or the quality of information. Content that produces strong emotional reactions, such as outrage, excitement, fear, or humor, often receives more algorithmic promotion than calm, measured, accurate reporting. This creates systematic pressure in the social media information environment toward sensationalism and emotional content, even when that content is misleading.

Core Principle

Chapter 31 addresses recommendation algorithms and their effects in detail. For now, the important research principle is: your social media feed is not a representative sample of the information ecosystem. It is a personalized selection shaped by your past behavior, your network, and the platform's business interests. It cannot substitute for systematic research.

User-Generated Content in Research

Despite its challenges, social media and user-generated content can be legitimate research sources in specific contexts. Social media posts from participants in events, protests, or public discussions can serve as valuable primary source documentation for historians, sociologists, and communication researchers studying those events. Analyzing patterns in social media content with appropriate methodology can reveal something about public attitudes, the spread of narratives, or the organization of social movements. And during breaking events, social media often carries eyewitness reporting before traditional news organizations have coverage — though this material requires careful verification.

When using social media content as a research source, the same evaluation questions apply: Who created this? What is the purpose? What evidence is offered? How can it be verified?

Wikipedia

Wikipedia deserves specific attention because it occupies an unusual position in the information ecosystem. It is a collaborative, online encyclopedia written and edited by a volunteer community of contributors, governed by policies about verifiability, neutral point of view, and reliable sourcing.

Wikipedia is not a reliable academic source for direct citation in most research contexts. Its articles can be edited by anyone, may contain errors, and may lag behind current research. Disciplines, departments, and instructors almost universally ask students not to cite Wikipedia as a source.

Wikipedia as a Finding Tool

However, Wikipedia is genuinely useful as a starting point for research, not an ending point. Wikipedia articles often provide a serviceable overview of a topic that helps a student understand basic concepts and terminology before diving into primary and secondary sources. The references and external links sections of Wikipedia articles often point toward legitimate sources — published books, academic articles, government reports — that can be traced and evaluated independently. Wikipedia can also help you identify key names, events, and debates associated with a topic that you can then search for in more reliable sources.

The rule is not "never use Wikipedia." The rule is: use Wikipedia to find your way to reliable sources, then cite those sources directly — not Wikipedia itself. This is the difference between using Wikipedia as a finding tool, which is appropriate, and citing Wikipedia as evidence, which is not appropriate for academic research.

Section 4.7

Data, Images, Audio, and Video

Information does not only come in the form of written text. A full understanding of the modern information ecosystem requires recognizing that data sets, photographs, maps, audio recordings, video footage, and other nontext sources are legitimate and often essential forms of evidence.

Data Sets and Statistical Sources

A data set is a structured collection of numerical or categorical information, typically organized in rows and columns, that can be analyzed to identify patterns, trends, or relationships. Data sets are produced by government statistical agencies, research institutions and universities, international organizations such as the World Bank, the World Health Organization, and the United Nations, private research firms and companies, nongovernmental organizations, and individual researchers sharing their data publicly.

Data is increasingly available for research purposes. Government open data initiatives have made vast amounts of official statistics publicly accessible. Research data repositories allow scholars to share the data underlying their published findings.

Evaluating data requires attention to the same questions raised for other sources, plus additional technical considerations: How was the data collected? What is the sample size and sampling method? What are the definitions used for key variables? Who funded the data collection, and could that affect what was measured or how? Chapter 29 covers data and statistical evidence in detail.

Images and Photographs

Visual images — photographs, illustrations, diagrams, charts, graphs, and infographics — are a significant category of sources and evidence. Images can function as documentary evidence that records events, conditions, or people at a specific moment; as scientific visualization that represents data or complex relationships; as historical records that provide direct visual evidence of the past; or, when edited, misattributed, or taken out of context, as propaganda and manipulation.

The research value of visual sources depends heavily on context. A photograph of a protest taken in 1968 is a primary historical source — when we know where and when it was taken, by whom, and in what circumstances. The same photograph shared on social media with a misleading caption claiming it depicts something else entirely becomes a piece of disinformation. Chapter 30 covers visual evidence evaluation, including reverse image searching and deepfake detection, in detail.

Maps

Maps are a specialized form of visual information that represents geographic, spatial, or thematic data. Maps produced by government agencies, academic institutions, and reputable research organizations can be excellent sources of geographic, demographic, environmental, and historical information. They can also be tools of manipulation: the choices of what to include, what to exclude, how to draw boundaries, and what colors and scales to use can present the same geographic reality in very different and potentially misleading ways. Evaluating maps involves the same attention to producer, purpose, and methodology as any other source.

Audio and Video

Audio recordings — interviews, speeches, broadcasts, oral histories, music — are primary sources in many research contexts. Video footage — news broadcasts, documentary films, recorded events, personal recordings — similarly functions as direct documentary evidence.

Synthetic Audio and Video

Digital technology has made audio and video sources both more accessible and more susceptible to manipulation. Authentic recordings can be edited to remove context. Audio and video can be synthesized using artificial intelligence to create convincing but entirely fabricated recordings of people saying things they never said. Chapter 30 addresses these challenges in detail.

Legitimate audio and video sources accessible to researchers include broadcast news archives, oral history collections in libraries and archives, congressional and parliamentary records, recorded interviews published by reputable organizations, and documentary films, which themselves should be evaluated for perspective and accuracy.

Archives and Special Collections

Archives are collections of primary source documents — letters, diaries, official records, photographs, maps, audio and video recordings, organizational records, and other materials — preserved for historical and research purposes. Academic libraries often maintain special collections and archives alongside their general holdings.

Archives hold evidence that cannot be found anywhere else: the original manuscript of a novel, the internal correspondence of a government agency, a collection of personal letters that documents daily life in a specific era, official records of a historical event. For researchers working in history, literary studies, political science, biography, and related fields, archives are often essential.

Many archives have begun digitizing their holdings and making them accessible online. But a great deal of archival material remains only in physical form, accessible only by visiting the archive in person.

Section 4.8

AI Tools and Generated Information

The emergence of large language model AI tools — software that can generate human-like text, answer questions, summarize documents, and assist with writing — has added a fundamentally new element to the information ecosystem. As of 2025, tools such as chatbots, AI writing assistants, and AI-powered research tools have become widely available and widely used by students, researchers, professionals, and the general public.

Understanding what these tools are, how they work, and what they can and cannot do responsibly is now an essential part of information literacy.

What AI Language Models Are

AI language models are software systems trained on enormous amounts of text data — books, articles, websites, and other written sources — to learn statistical patterns in language. When prompted with a question or instruction, these models generate responses by predicting what words would be most appropriate to follow, based on those learned patterns.

This means that AI language models do not "know" things in the way humans know things. They do not consult databases or look up current information unless specifically integrated with search tools. They generate text that is statistically likely to be useful, coherent, and relevant — but this generation process does not guarantee accuracy. An AI tool can produce confident, well-structured, grammatically correct text that contains significant factual errors.

Hallucination and Fabricated Sources

One of the most significant problems with AI-generated information in research contexts is a phenomenon called hallucination: the tendency of AI language models to generate plausible-sounding but fabricated information, including entirely fictional citations.

Forms Hallucinated Citations Can Take
What to watch for when an AI tool generates sources
  • InventedTitles of articles that sound real but do not exist.
  • MismatchedAccurate author names combined with journals the authors have never published in.
  • PartialAccurate-seeming author names and journals but entirely fabricated article titles and dates.
  • DistortedAccurate-seeming citations with incorrect page numbers, volumes, or years.
Danger: fabricated citations look exactly like real ones. A reader could never find a source that does not exist.
Core Principle

Never use an AI-generated citation without independently verifying that the source exists and that it actually says what the AI claims it says. This verification process means finding the actual source — in a library database, a library catalog, or a reputable digital repository — and reading it yourself.

What AI Tools Can Do in Research

Despite these significant limitations, AI tools can be genuinely useful at certain stages of the research process when used thoughtfully and with appropriate verification. They can assist with brainstorming and exploration, helping you think through the dimensions of a topic or generate a preliminary list of questions. They can offer plain-language explanations that build background understanding before you read primary and secondary sources. They can provide drafting assistance, helping you think through organizational structures or giving feedback on draft writing — though the ideas and evidence must still be yours, and disclosure requirements apply. And they can help with identifying search terms, brainstorming keywords and related terms for database searching, though you must verify these against controlled vocabulary in actual databases.

What AI Tools Should Not Do in Research

AI tools should not serve as a primary source of factual claims without independent verification, replace reading of actual sources, generate citations that are used without verification, or be presented as the author of ideas that you submit as your own work.

Disclosure and Academic Integrity

The question of when and how to disclose AI tool use in academic work is an evolving and important area. Different institutions, departments, and individual instructors have different policies on AI assistance. Some assignments prohibit AI assistance entirely. Some permit it for specific stages of the process, such as brainstorming or editing, but not others, such as generating the argument or the evidence. Some require explicit disclosure of any AI use.

The rule is: always follow the specific disclosure and use policies of your institution, your department, and your instructor for the assignment you are completing. When in doubt, ask your instructor before using AI tools, not after. Chapter 45 addresses responsible AI tool use in research in detail.

AI's Place in the Information Ecosystem

AI tools have introduced a new kind of information into the ecosystem — information that is generated by software rather than researched, observed, or analyzed by human experts. This means that when information from AI tools circulates without attribution, for example when text generated by an AI tool is presented as original research, it adds a layer of unverifiable, unaccountable content to the information environment.

This is not an argument against AI tools. It is an argument for understanding what they are. An AI language model is not a researcher, a journalist, a scientist, or an expert. It is a sophisticated pattern-matching system that generates text. The ecosystem benefit of such tools depends entirely on whether the humans using them understand that distinction and act accordingly.

Navigating the Ecosystem

Matching Sources to Research Needs

One of the key lessons of this chapter is that different zones of the information ecosystem serve different research purposes. No single zone is always better than all others. A peer-reviewed article is the right source for many research questions but the wrong source for finding the text of a federal law. A government website is the right source for official demographic data but the wrong source for a peer-reviewed analysis of what that data means.

The skill researchers need is not just finding sources, but matching source types to research needs. The following examples illustrate this matching process.

Worked Example 1

  • BooksAcademic monographs for historical context, scholarly interpretation, and the long view of policy development.

  • ArchivesPrimary source archival documents for original policy documents, congressional records, and official correspondence.
  • Gov. reportsOfficial statistics and the language of actual policy at various historical moments.
  • NewsNews archives for documentation of how policies were debated and received publicly at the time.
She does not need social media, AI-generated summaries, or opinion blogs. These would not serve her research question.

Chapter Summary, Review, and Practice

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 4.1An information ecosystem is the whole system of producers, formats, institutions, technologies, economic models, norms, and users that creates, stores, circulates, and consumes information. Ecosystem thinking helps researchers ask better questions about every source they encounter.
  • Sec 4.2Books and reference works offer depth, context, and curated background knowledge. Monographs, edited collections, textbooks, and handbooks serve different research purposes; encyclopedias, dictionaries, and other reference works are especially valuable early in research.
  • Sec 4.3Scholarly articles, published in academic journals and subjected to peer review, are the primary vehicle for original research. Academic databases such as JSTOR, PubMed, and PsycINFO provide access that general web search cannot.
  • Sec 4.4News reporting documents events and public debate, while opinion and commentary express a viewpoint; the two must be read and cited differently. Magazines and feature journalism add depth but are not subject to expert scholarly review.
  • Sec 4.5The open web ranges from rigorously produced government data to unverified personal opinion. Domain type offers a limited starting clue, not a verdict. The open access movement, including preprint servers, has expanded free access to scholarship but requires attention to peer-review status.
  • Sec 4.6Social media is shaped by recommendation algorithms designed to maximize engagement, not accuracy. User-generated content can be a legitimate primary source in specific contexts. Wikipedia is a useful finding tool but not an appropriate source to cite directly.
  • Sec 4.7Data sets, images, maps, audio, video, and archives provide evidence beyond written text, each requiring attention to producer, methodology, and manipulation risk.
  • Sec 4.8AI language models generate statistically likely text rather than verified fact, and can hallucinate fabricated citations. AI tools can support brainstorming, explanation, and drafting when verified independently, but cannot serve as an unverified source of fact or citation.

Key Terms

information ecosystem
The whole system of sources, producers, distributors, platforms, institutions, technologies, and users that together create, store, circulate, and consume information.
peer review
The process by which expert reviewers in a field evaluate a scholarly manuscript's significance, methods, evidence, and reasoning before publication.
academic database
A specialized search system that indexes scholarly journals and other academic sources within a discipline or across disciplines.
open access
The movement and set of practices that make scholarly research freely available online rather than restricted behind subscription paywalls.
preprint
A research paper posted publicly before undergoing peer review, which may later be revised, accepted, or found to contain significant errors.
recommendation algorithm
An automated system that predicts what content a user is likely to engage with and prioritizes that content in a feed or search result.
domain type
The suffix of a website address (such as .gov, .edu, .org, or .com) that offers limited, non-definitive information about who operates a site.
archive
A collection of primary source documents and materials preserved for historical and research purposes.
AI language model
A software system trained on large amounts of text data to generate responses by predicting statistically likely word patterns, without verified factual knowledge.
hallucination
The tendency of an AI language model to generate plausible-sounding but fabricated information, including citations to sources that do not exist.
lateral reading
A verification technique in which a researcher leaves an unfamiliar source to search for independent information about its producer or origin.
ecosystem confusion
A pathway to misinformation in which a researcher fails to recognize what type of source they are actually viewing.

Source Check

Source Check 4.1
Evaluating a Wikipedia Article

Choose any Wikipedia article on a topic in your field of study. As you read it, evaluate who produced it. Wikipedia articles are written collaboratively by volunteer contributors, edited according to community policies, and subject to ongoing revision, with no single expert typically responsible for the accuracy of the entire article.

Consider what review process it underwent. Wikipedia's editorial process involves community review and policy enforcement, including verifiability, neutral point of view, and no original research, but not formal expert peer review. Articles vary enormously in quality.

Scroll to the References section and consider what evidence is cited. Are citations present? Do they point to recognizable, reliable sources, such as published books, peer-reviewed articles, government reports, or established news organizations? Or are references sparse, broken, or pointing to questionable sources?

Finally, consider what the article can be used for in research. Wikipedia can help you understand basic concepts and vocabulary, identify key names and debates, and find references to pursue through the library. It should not be cited as a source in academic work.

Apply the MGU Source Evaluation Model directly: Relevance — does the article cover the specific aspect of the topic you need? Authority — is the article well-maintained, with clear sourcing, and who are the cited sources? Evidence — are claims supported by cited references you can trace? Accuracy — does the information agree with what you find in other reliable sources? Purpose — what is Wikipedia's purpose? Currency — when was the article last updated, and is the information current? Context — does the article acknowledge controversies, limitations, or competing views? Record your evaluation in your Research Notebook.

Misinformation Alert

Ecosystem Confusion and Platform Deception

One of the most common pathways to accepting misinformation is ecosystem confusion: failing to recognize what type of source you are actually looking at. Watch for websites that look like news organizations but are not, relying on professional-looking layouts and credible-sounding names with no real editorial accountability. Watch for opinion pieces that look like news reports, since opinion columns present one person's argument rather than documented reporting. Watch for AI-generated content presented as research, including citations that appear real but cannot be verified. Watch for preprints presented as peer-reviewed findings, since preprints have not yet passed expert review. And watch for data presented without context, since a real statistic can still be used to support a false impression when the baseline, average, or missing context is left out.

MGU Misinformation Check

Who made it? What is the claim? What evidence is offered? What is missing? Can another reliable source confirm it? Is the emotional reaction stronger than the evidence? Has the source been altered, misrepresented, or taken out of context?

Citation Check

Every source you use in research must be cited, but different source types require different information to be recorded. When you encounter a source, make sure to record all elements you will need for a citation.

Source TypeInformation to Record
BookAuthor(s); title; publisher; year. For ebooks, note the platform or URL.
Scholarly articleAuthor(s); article title; journal name; volume; issue; year; pages; DOI if available.
News articleAuthor if given; article title; publication name; date; URL, with access date if the source may change.
Government report / datasetAuthoring agency; report or dataset title; publisher or agency; year; URL.
WebsiteAuthor if given; page title; site name; date published or last updated if available; URL; date accessed.
Social media postAccount name and username; platform; date of post; URL.
AI-generated contentName of AI tool; version if known; prompt used, summarized or exact per your style guide; date accessed. Only if your instructor permits use and requires disclosure.
Key Rule

Record citation information at the time you find a source. Do not wait until you are writing your paper. Sources can move, disappear, or change. A source you cannot relocate is a source you cannot cite.

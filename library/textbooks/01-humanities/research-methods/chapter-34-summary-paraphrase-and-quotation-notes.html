Chapter 34 — Front Matter

Research Skill Focus and Why This Matters

This chapter teaches students how to record source material accurately and honestly using three distinct types of source notes: summary notes, paraphrase notes, and quotation notes. Students will learn what each type of note is, when to use each one, how to write them correctly, how to keep source words clearly separated from their own words, and how to avoid the dangerous middle ground of patchwriting. By the end of this chapter, students should be able to read a source and produce accurate notes in all three forms, recording page numbers consistently and building a note collection that will support honest, well-cited writing.

Why This Matters

Every researcher eventually faces the same problem: you have read something important, and now you need to record it. How you record it determines almost everything that follows. If you record it carelessly — copying chunks of text without marking them as quotations, blending the author's words with your own, forgetting page numbers — you are building a research foundation on unstable ground. When you sit down to write weeks later, you will not be able to tell which words are yours and which belong to your source. That confusion is how accidental plagiarism happens.

The three note types covered in this chapter — summary, paraphrase, and quotation — are not interchangeable. Each serves a specific purpose. Each requires a specific technique. And each, done correctly, protects you from the most common integrity failures in student research. Summaries let you record the overall argument of a long source quickly. Paraphrases let you translate a specific passage into your own words while preserving the source's meaning. Quotations let you capture exact language when the precise wording matters.

Students who learn to choose the right note type for the right situation, and who apply each type carefully, produce better writing because they understand their sources more deeply. They also produce more honest writing because they know exactly what they owe to each source and exactly how to give credit for it. This chapter matters because good note-taking is not a preliminary chore that happens before real research begins. It is the core of research. The quality of your notes predicts the quality of your writing.

Section 34.1

Three Kinds of Source Notes

Before you can take good research notes, you need to understand that there are three fundamentally different kinds of notes you can make about a source. These three kinds — summary notes, paraphrase notes, and quotation notes — differ in their purpose, their length, their relationship to the original source language, and the situations in which they are most useful.

Note TypeWhat It CapturesLength Relative to OriginalPage Number Required?
SummaryThe overall content, argument, or findings of a source or section, in the researcher's own wordsMuch shorter — condensedRecommended; required for specific sections
ParaphraseThe specific content of one passage, restated in the researcher's own wordsApproximately the same length, or slightly shorterAlways required
QuotationThe exact words of the source, reproduced preciselyIdentical to original (usually short)Always required

Each note type has a role in research. Using only one type creates problems. Students who take only summary notes lose the specific detail they will need to support particular claims. Students who take only paraphrase notes often find that their notes have drifted closer to the original language than they intended — a problem called patchwriting that is covered in detail in Section 34.7. Students who take only quotation notes end up with a collection of other people's words and no framework for understanding how those words relate to each other or to their research question.

Skilled researchers use all three types strategically. They summarize when they need the big picture. They paraphrase when they need specific content without quotation. They quote when the exact words are the point.

The Foundation: Always Record Source Information First

Before taking any type of note, record the full source information at the top of the note. Every note you make — summary, paraphrase, or quotation — should be anchored to a complete citation entry. If you cannot trace a note back to its source, the note is nearly useless. You cannot cite it. You cannot verify it. You cannot return to the source if you need to check your reading.

The minimum source information required before any note:

  • Author's full name (as it appears on the source)
  • Full title, including subtitle
  • Publisher and date (for books) or journal name, volume, issue, and date (for articles)
  • Page number or range of the specific passage you are noting
  • Database or URL if accessed digitally, with access date if the source may change

This information goes at the top of the note entry, every time, before you write a single word of content.

Section 34.2

Summary Notes

A summary note captures the main ideas of a source or a section in your own words, in condensed form. Summary notes are what you produce after you read and understand something — they are a record of what the source said, filtered through your comprehension, expressed in your own language.

When to Write a Summary Note

Write a summary note when you need a record of what a whole article or book chapter argues, without capturing every specific detail; when you are evaluating whether a source is relevant to your research question and want to record your assessment; when you have read a long section that provides background context rather than specific evidence; when you are mapping the sources in your research and need to remember what each one covers; or when the source's overall argument or conclusion is what matters, not any particular passage.

Summary notes are especially useful early in the research process, when you are reading widely to understand a field or topic. They help you track many sources efficiently and see how they relate to each other.

How to Write a Good Summary Note

It uses your own words throughout. A summary is not a collection of phrases lifted from the source with a few words changed. It is your restatement of the source's ideas in your own language. If you find yourself writing sentences that sound like the source, stop and restate the idea fresh without looking at the original text.

It focuses on main ideas. A summary leaves out specific examples, supporting evidence, secondary arguments, and detailed data unless those elements are the specific point of the summary. Ask yourself: What is this source's main argument or finding? What is the central claim? What is the most important conclusion? These are the things your summary captures.

It is proportionally shorter than the original. A summary of a twenty-page journal article should be a paragraph or two, not a page. A summary of a book chapter should be a few sentences to a short paragraph. If your summary is nearly as long as the original, you are writing a paraphrase of the entire source, not a summary.

It is accurate. A summary that misrepresents the source's argument is worse than no summary. You will base your writing on your notes. If your notes contain distorted readings of sources, your paper will contain distorted readings of sources — which is a form of source misuse. Read the source carefully, understand it, and then summarize it faithfully.

It records what the source said, not what you think of it. Save your evaluation for a separate note. A summary note records the source's content. An evaluation note records your assessment of that content. Keeping these separate prevents your summary from importing your opinions and attributing them to the source.

Model Note 34.A
A Summary Note
Summary
Source: Guitart, Daniela, Catherine Pickering, and Jason Byrne. “Past and Future Research Directions for Urban Agriculture: An Outlook.” Urban Food Security Review 12, no. 3 (2018): 145–162.
Pages covered: Entire article

This article reviews two decades of published research on urban agriculture and identifies gaps and future directions for the field. The authors find that most existing research has focused on community gardens in North American and European cities, leaving significant gaps in coverage of urban agriculture in the Global South, of commercial or semi-commercial urban farms, and of the environmental sustainability of different urban agriculture models. The article argues that future research needs to be more geographically diverse, more attentive to economic sustainability, and more willing to study urban agriculture outside the community garden model. The authors call for interdisciplinary approaches that combine social science, environmental science, and economic analysis.

Usefulness for my project: Helpful for background section on what the research field covers and what is missing. Does not provide specific data on food security outcomes — need to supplement with outcome-focused sources.

Notice the structure: source citation at the top, note type labeled, pages covered specified, content recorded in the researcher's own words, and a brief usefulness note at the close — based on the MGU Research Note Model.

What a Summary Note Is Not

A summary note is not a quote. If you find yourself writing a sentence that comes directly from the source, you must either put it in quotation marks (making it a quotation note) or rewrite it completely in your own words. A summary note is not an annotation — an annotated bibliography entry includes a summary plus an evaluation and an assessment of the source's usefulness, which is a more formal product covered in Chapter 35. And a summary note is not a paraphrase: a summary is condensed, while a paraphrase is full-length.

Section 34.3

Paraphrase Notes

A paraphrase note translates a specific passage from a source into your own words at approximately the same level of detail as the original. Paraphrase notes are used when you need to record the specific content of a passage — its specific argument, evidence, claim, or description — but you do not need or want to use the source's exact words.

When to Write a Paraphrase Note

Write a paraphrase note when a specific passage states an important point, provides evidence, or makes a claim that you will need to use in your paper; when the passage contains technical language that you want to translate into more accessible terms; when the passage is not written in a particularly memorable or precise way that makes exact quotation necessary; when you want to integrate the source's specific content smoothly into your own writing without relying on direct quotation; or when you are working with a passage in another language and are translating it into English.

Paraphrase notes are the workhorse of research note-taking. They represent specific source content in your own words, which makes them easier to integrate into writing without the seams that direct quotations sometimes create.

How to Write a Good Paraphrase Note

Paraphrasing well is harder than it looks. Many students believe that paraphrasing means replacing a few words with synonyms. That is not paraphrasing. That is a recipe for patchwriting, which is covered in Section 34.7. True paraphrasing requires that you understand the original passage, set it aside, and restate its meaning in your own words without looking at the original while you write. Here is a reliable five-step process.

The Five-Step Paraphrasing Process

Step 1. Read the original passage carefully until you understand it. Read it more than once if necessary. Look up any words or concepts you do not understand.

Step 2. Set the original aside or close it. This is the critical step. If the original text is visible while you write, you will borrow its sentence structure, its specific phrasing, and its word choices — even unconsciously.

Step 3. Write the content of the passage in your own words, from memory. Restate what the passage said. Use your own sentence structure. Do not worry about matching the original's style or length exactly.

Step 4. Compare your paraphrase to the original. Check that your paraphrase accurately represents the original meaning, and that you have not accidentally reproduced distinctive phrases.

Step 5. Record the page number. A paraphrase note requires a page number just as a quotation note does.

Model Note 34.B
A Paraphrase Note
  • Original "Food insecurity in urban environments is often exacerbated by what researchers call 'spatial mismatch' — the geographic distance between low-income residential areas and fresh food retail outlets. In cities where supermarkets and grocery stores are concentrated in higher-income neighborhoods, lower-income residents face not only the economic burden of food costs but also the logistical burden of extended travel time and transportation expense to reach adequate food sources." (p. 78)
    • Paraphrase
    Source: Harrington, Paula. Food Access and Urban Inequality. Metropolitan Press, 2020.  ·  Page: 78

    In cities, the problem of food insecurity among low-income residents is made worse by the physical distance between where those residents live and where fresh food is sold. When supermarkets are clustered in wealthier parts of a city, residents of poorer neighborhoods must travel farther and spend more on transportation to buy adequate food, adding logistical costs on top of the financial challenge of food prices. Harrington calls this geographic gap a “spatial mismatch.”

    Marginal note: Retained the term “spatial mismatch” in quotation marks because it is the author's specific coined term — will need to attribute this phrase if I use it in my paper.

    Principle: The sentence structure is entirely different from the original; the vocabulary is similar in concept but not identical; the meaning is faithfully preserved; the page number is recorded; and the author's coined term is flagged for attribution.

    The Paraphrase Note and Page Numbers

    Rule to Remember

    Paraphrase notes require page numbers. Students sometimes assume that page numbers are only for direct quotations. They are not. A paraphrase represents the specific content of a specific passage. If your paper attributes an idea to a source, your reader should be able to locate that idea in the source. The page number is the address. Record it every time.

    If you are working with a digital source that does not have page numbers — a website, an ebook with flowing text, a PDF without page numbers — record the section title, paragraph number, chapter number, or timestamp (for audio or video). The goal is to give your reader a location within the source.

    Section 34.4

    Quotation Notes

    A quotation note reproduces the exact words of a source within quotation marks. Quotation notes are used when the precise language of the original is what matters — when you need the author's exact phrasing for analysis, when the wording is particularly significant or compelling, when the source is making a claim you will need to verify or contest, or when the language itself is the evidence.

    When to Use a Quotation Note

    Not every passage you read needs to be quoted. Use quotation notes selectively. Reserve direct quotation for situations where the precise wording genuinely matters:

    When exact language is the point. If you are analyzing how an author frames an argument, discusses a concept, or uses specific rhetoric, you need the author's exact words. Paraphrase cannot substitute for analysis of the original language.

    When a statement is unusually precise, significant, or memorable. A particularly sharp formulation, a precise definition, or a carefully worded claim is best captured exactly.

    When the source is primary evidence. In literary analysis, historical research, legal research, and other fields where primary source documents are examined directly, exact quotation is necessary because the text itself is the object of study.

    When the exact words carry authority. A direct statement from a leading expert, a key finding stated in the precise language of the original study, or a definition given by the body that created the term may carry more authority in their original form than in paraphrase.

    When changing the words would change the meaning or introduce ambiguity. Technical definitions, legal language, and carefully hedged claims sometimes need to be quoted exactly.

    How to Write a Quotation Note

    Quotation marks. The quoted text must be enclosed in quotation marks the moment you write it down. The quotation marks signal that these words are not yours. They are the single most important habit in note-taking for academic integrity.

    Exact transcription. Copy the passage exactly as it appears in the source. Do not correct spelling, grammar, or punctuation unless you note the correction explicitly. Do not omit words without indicating the omission with an ellipsis. Do not insert your own words without indicating the insertion with brackets.

    Page number. Record the page number of every quotation, every time, at the moment you record the quotation.

    Indication of source. The source information at the top of the note connects every quotation to its origin.

    Ellipsis and Brackets in Quotation Notes

    Ellipsis (...) indicates that you have omitted words from the middle of a quoted passage. Use three spaced dots — or, in MLA style, three dots within brackets [...] — to indicate an omission. Do not use ellipsis at the beginning of a quotation unless the passage flows grammatically as if something preceded it. Do not use ellipsis at the end unless you are omitting something that changes the meaning.

    Brackets [ ] indicate that you have added words to the quotation that were not in the original — typically to clarify a pronoun, adjust a verb form to fit your sentence, or provide context. Use both conventions sparingly in notes. If you find yourself using many ellipses or brackets, consider whether a paraphrase might serve better.

    Model Note 34.C
    A Quotation Note
    Quote
    Source: Winfield, James. The Archive and the Algorithm: Digital Preservation in the Twenty-First Century. University of Chicago Press, 2021.  ·  Page: 214

    “The greatest threat to digital archives is not technical failure but institutional indifference. Servers can be replaced; funding, once withdrawn, rarely returns.”

    Why I quoted this: Precise formulation of the author's central argument. Captures the distinction between technical and institutional risk in a memorable and quotable way. Will consider using in the introduction to my discussion of funding threats.

    Recording why you chose to quote rather than paraphrase is optional but useful — it helps you decide later whether to use the quotation, adapt it to a paraphrase, or set it aside.

    Quotation Notes and Length

    Keep Quotation Notes Short

    Long block quotations should be used rarely in research notes, just as they should be used rarely in finished papers. If you are quoting a full page or more of text, ask whether a summary or paraphrase would serve as well. Long quotations in notes are sometimes a sign that you have not yet processed the source — that you have copied it rather than understood it. Reading and understanding come first. Then you quote only what genuinely needs to be preserved exactly.

    Section 34.5

    Separating Source Words from Your Words

    One of the most important habits in research is maintaining a clear visual and cognitive separation between what the source said and what you are saying. This separation must be established at the note-taking stage, or it cannot be reliably maintained in writing.

    The Danger of Mixed Notes

    Mixed notes — entries where the researcher's own thoughts, reactions, and ideas appear in the same space as source content, without clear markers separating them — are a primary source of unintentional plagiarism and source confusion.

    When a student sits down to write six weeks after taking notes and reads an entry that says “Urban agriculture creates community cohesion and also addresses food security at the neighborhood scale,” the student may not know whether that sentence is something the source said, something the student thought, or a blend of both. If the student uses that sentence in the paper as their own observation, and it turns out to be from the source, that is plagiarism — even though the student did not intend it.

    The solution is clear visual separation, established at the moment of note-taking.

    Strategies for Keeping Source Words and Your Words Separate

    Use consistent formatting to distinguish note types. In digital notes, use different fonts, colors, or labels. In handwritten notes, use different columns, different ink colors, or clearly labeled sections.

    Label every source passage. Before writing any passage from a source, write a label: [SUMMARY], [PARAPHRASE], or [QUOTE]. These labels tell you, months later, what kind of note follows.

    Use a separate section for your own thoughts. In the MGU Research Note Model, there is a space for the researcher's commentary — usefulness, limits, connections to other sources, questions the source raises. Keep this commentary clearly labeled and clearly separated.

    Put quotation marks around every quoted word, every time, immediately. This is the most important single habit. Never write a word from a source in your notes without immediately enclosing it in quotation marks.

    When in doubt, quote. If you are uncertain whether a phrase came from the source or from your own mind while reading, treat it as a quotation. It is always easier to remove quotation marks from your own words later than to discover — after the paper is submitted — that you used a source's words without attribution.

    A Caution — The Interleaved Note

    Some researchers write notes that interleave summary and commentary — a sentence from the source, then a reaction, then another sentence from the source, then another reaction — in a single flowing entry. This style can work if each layer is clearly labeled and if quotation marks are applied consistently. But it is also a style that frequently leads to confusion, because the interleaving can blur the boundary between source content and personal response. If you use this style, mark every element explicitly: label the note type, use quotation marks for all quoted text, and use a consistent signal such as [MY NOTE:] or [RESPONSE:] for all personal commentary.

    Section 34.6

    Recording Page Numbers

    Page numbers are not optional. They are not a detail to be filled in later. They are a required element of every source note — for quotations, for paraphrases, and for summaries that represent specific passages or sections.

    Why Page Numbers Matter So Much

    ReasonWhat Happens Without It
    CitationMost academic citation styles require page numbers for in-text citations and footnotes when you quote or paraphrase from a specific passage. Without it, citations are incomplete.
    VerificationResearchers return to sources to check their reading and confirm accuracy. Without a page number, you may need to re-read an entire article or book to find a passage.
    Your readerReaders who want to find the source of a claim need a page number to locate that specific passage, not an entire work to search.
    Your own processWhen drafting, you need to know exactly where in the source material appears. Without page numbers, integration slows down every time you check a source.

    When Sources Lack Page Numbers

    Some sources do not have conventional page numbers. Here is how to handle the most common cases.

    • Websites and online articles without pagination: record the paragraph number (e.g., para. 4) or the section heading under which the passage appears.
    • Ebooks with flowing text (no fixed pages): record the chapter number and, if available, the section heading or location number.
    • PDFs: use the original print page numbers if preserved; otherwise note that you are using PDF page numbers, not original publication pages.
    • Audio and video sources: record the timestamp (e.g., 12:34) at which the quoted or paraphrased content appears.
    • Social media posts and short online content: record the full URL, the author, the platform, and the date accessed, in addition to any available location information.
    The Moment-of-Note-Taking Rule

    The page number must be recorded at the moment of note-taking, not afterward. This is the rule. There is no reliable substitute. If you take a note without recording the page number, you will need to go back to the source and find the passage again — which may take a few minutes or an hour, depending on the source. Do it immediately. This habit separates efficient researchers from frustrated ones.

    Section 34.7

    Avoiding Patchwriting in Notes

    Patchwriting is the practice of taking a source's sentence structure and substituting synonyms or rearranging words without genuinely restating the content in new language. It is the most common form of source misuse in student research, and it is most often created during the note-taking stage, not the writing stage.

    What Patchwriting Looks Like

    Comparison
    Original, Patchwritten Note, and Genuine Paraphrase
    • Original "Urban food deserts are defined not simply by the absence of full-service grocery stores, but by the intersection of limited food retail access with poverty, inadequate public transportation, and the absence of alternative food sources such as farmers' markets or community gardens." (p. 34)
    • Patchwritten Urban food deserts are described not just by the lack of full-service supermarkets, but by the combination of restricted food retail availability with poverty, poor public transportation, and the absence of other food sources like farmers' markets or community gardens. (p. 34)Words have been swapped — “absence” to “lack,” “limited” to “restricted,” “intersection” to “combination” — but the sentence structure is identical to the original.
    • Genuine Food deserts in cities cannot be understood simply by looking at where grocery stores are located. The concept also involves the economic conditions of a neighborhood, how easily residents can travel to food sources, and whether alternatives to traditional supermarkets — such as farmers' markets or community gardens — exist nearby. (p. 34)This version uses a completely different sentence structure, different vocabulary, and a different organizational logic, while accurately representing the original meaning.
    Principle: Genuine paraphrase changes the structure, not just the words.

    Why Patchwriting Happens

    Patchwriting usually happens because the researcher is looking at the original text while writing the note. When the original text is visible, the brain naturally borrows its structure and vocabulary — especially when the researcher does not fully understand the passage and is unsure how to restate it. Patchwriting also happens when researchers are moving too fast — trying to get through a source quickly and recording it without genuinely processing it.

    Why Patchwriting Is a Problem

    Patchwriting Becomes Plagiarism

    Patchwriting in notes becomes patchwriting in papers. When you write your paper using patchwritten notes, you are presenting language that is substantially borrowed from the source without using quotation marks and without genuine transformation. This is plagiarism. Even if you do not intend to deceive anyone, patchwriting violates the principle of intellectual honesty that academic writing requires. It also means that your paper contains the source's thinking more than your own — a problem of substance as well as integrity. Patchwriting is also detectable, by instructors and by plagiarism detection software.

    How to Avoid Patchwriting

    Close the source while you write the note. This is the single most effective strategy. Once you have read and understood a passage, set it aside — close the book, minimize the browser window, turn the page — and write the paraphrase without the original in view.

    Understand before you restate. If you cannot close the source and write a paraphrase from memory, you may not understand the passage well enough to paraphrase it. Read it again. Look up unfamiliar terms.

    Change the structure, not just the words. The original may use a dependent clause before the main clause; your paraphrase might use the main clause first. The original may list items in a particular sequence; your paraphrase might reorganize that sequence.

    Use summary instead of paraphrase when a specific passage is not needed. Summary requires less exact correspondence to the original passage and forces you to think about the main point rather than the specific language.

    Quote rather than patchwrite. If the language of a passage is distinctive enough that you cannot genuinely paraphrase it without borrowing too much of its phrasing, quote it instead. A clearly marked, properly attributed quotation is honest. A patchwritten paraphrase is not.

    Section 34.8

    Turning Notes into Draft Material

    The final step in this chapter is understanding how summary notes, paraphrase notes, and quotation notes function as the raw material of written work. Notes do not go directly into papers. They go through a process of selection, interpretation, integration, and citation. Understanding that process — and how notes support it — completes the picture of note-taking as a research practice.

    From Notes to Draft: The Basic Process

    When you sit down to write a section of a paper, you are doing several things at once: deciding what evidence this section needs; finding the notes that provide that evidence; determining how to present that evidence (summary, paraphrase, or quotation); integrating the evidence into your argument; and citing the source.

    Your notes support the second and third of these steps. If your notes are accurate, clearly labeled, and richly recorded, those steps are efficient. If your notes are vague, unlabeled, or missing page numbers, they slow down to near-paralysis.

    Choosing the Right Note Type for the Draft

    Use a summary when you want to tell the reader what a source shows, argues, or concludes at a general level — when the specific wording of the source is not the focus, and when you are orienting the reader to a body of evidence before presenting specific details.

    Use a paraphrase when you want to present the specific content of a passage — a particular claim, finding, or argument — in a way that integrates smoothly with your own prose. Paraphrases are appropriate when you need specific detail from a source but the exact wording of that detail is not essential.

    Use a direct quotation sparingly and strategically — when exact language is required for analysis, when the wording carries special significance, when the authority of the source's exact words matters, or when paraphrase would lose precision.

    The Problem of Over-Quoting

    Quotation Is Not a Substitute for Thinking

    Many students over-quote. Their papers consist largely of direct quotations linked by brief transitions, with the student's own analysis appearing only occasionally. This is a problem because it means the paper is doing less thinking than it is transporting. A paper that consists mainly of quotations is not demonstrating the researcher's understanding of the material — it is demonstrating the researcher's ability to collect other people's words. The purpose of research writing is synthesis and argument: taking evidence from multiple sources, analyzing it, and using it to support your own well-reasoned claims.

    Well-taken notes support this goal because they force you to process sources at the time of reading. A good summary note requires you to understand and restate an argument. A good paraphrase note requires you to translate specific content into your own framework. A good quotation note requires you to identify exactly what is worth preserving in the author's own words and why.

    Signal Phrases and Attribution

    When you move from notes to draft, every piece of evidence drawn from a source needs to be attributed — connected to its source through a signal phrase, a parenthetical citation, or a footnote. The attribution is what tells the reader where the evidence comes from and what kind of claim it represents.

    Signal PhraseWhat the Verb Signals
    According to [author]...Neutral attribution of a reported fact or finding
    [Author] argues that...An interpretive or contestable claim
    [Author] found that...An empirical finding from data or observation
    As [author] notes...An observation, often in passing
    [Author] defines X as...A definitional statement
    Research by [author] suggests...A tentative or hedged conclusion

    The verb in the signal phrase carries information. “Argues” signals that this is an interpretive claim. “Found” signals an empirical finding. “Notes” signals an observation. “Defines” signals a definitional statement. Choosing signal phrase verbs carefully adds precision to your integration of sources.

    Notes Do Not Replace Thinking

    Core Principle

    Notes are a record of what you read. They are not a draft. They are not a plan. They are not an argument. The thinking that converts notes into a paper is your work — the work of analysis, synthesis, and argumentation that gives research its purpose. Students who treat their notes as the substance of a paper — who string together summaries, paraphrases, and quotations with minimal analytical connection — produce papers that lack a point of view. Your notes give you the material. Your research question gives you the framework. Your thinking does the work of making them into something.

    Search Practice

    Search Practice

    Activity 34AAnalyzing Note Type in Published Writing

    Find a recent academic article on a topic related to your research interests. The article can come from a library database, a course reading, or a source recommended by an instructor. Read one section of the article carefully — the literature review section, the introduction, or the discussion. As you read, identify how the author uses summary, paraphrase, and direct quotation when referring to other sources.

    1. Find one passage where the author summarizes another source. How do you know it is a summary rather than a paraphrase or quotation? What signal phrases or attribution language does the author use?
    2. Find one passage where the author paraphrases another source. How does the author integrate the paraphrase into their own argument? Is there a page number or location reference?
    3. Find one direct quotation. Why do you think the author chose to quote this passage directly rather than paraphrase it? What does the exact language contribute?
    4. Find one passage where you think the author may be patchwriting — where the language seems very close to a source without being enclosed in quotation marks. How would you rewrite that passage as a genuine paraphrase?
    5. Overall, what is the ratio of quotation to paraphrase to summary in the section you analyzed? What does this ratio suggest about the author's approach to source integration?
    Activity 34BNote-Taking Practice

    Select a journal article or book chapter related to your current research. Read a section of approximately three to five pages carefully. Then produce the following three note entries from that section.

    1. One summary note that captures the main argument or finding of the entire section in your own words. Include the full source citation, the note type label, the pages covered, and a usefulness note.
    2. One paraphrase note that translates a specific passage (one to three sentences) into your own words. Include the full source citation, the note type label, the specific page number, and a marginal note about any technical terms that need attribution.
    3. One quotation note that reproduces a brief passage (one to two sentences) exactly as it appears in the source, with quotation marks. Include the full source citation, the note type label, the page number, and a note explaining why you chose to quote rather than paraphrase.

    After completing the three notes, write a brief paragraph (one hundred to one hundred fifty words) explaining the decisions you made: why you chose to summarize the section you summarized, why you chose to paraphrase the passage you paraphrased, and why you chose to quote the passage you quoted.

    Practice Source Evaluation
    Identifying Responsible and Irresponsible Note-Taking Practices

    A student named Marcus is researching the relationship between social media use and adolescent mental health. He has found four sources: a peer-reviewed meta-analysis, a popular magazine article, a TED Talk transcript, and a book by a clinical psychologist.

    Marcus's note-taking practices: For the meta-analysis, Marcus writes a two-page summary that he believes captures all the key findings. He does not record page numbers because he plans to use the DOI number when he needs to cite it. For the magazine article, Marcus copies three paragraphs directly into his notes without quotation marks, because he “might paraphrase them later.” For the TED Talk transcript, Marcus writes careful paraphrase notes for five passages, recording timestamps for each. For the book, Marcus writes quotation notes for passages he finds compelling, always with quotation marks and page numbers. He also writes a summary of each chapter he reads.

    Evaluation Questions

    1. Meta-analysis notes. What problems might arise from Marcus's decision not to record page numbers? Does recording the DOI number solve this problem? What does he risk when he tries to cite specific findings from the meta-analysis in his paper?
    2. Magazine article notes. What is the specific risk Marcus has created by copying three paragraphs into his notes without quotation marks? What is the most likely outcome when he returns to these notes weeks later to use them in a draft?
    3. TED Talk transcript notes. Marcus's paraphrase notes for the TED Talk are careful, with timestamps. What additional source information should he be recording for these notes to be fully citable?
    4. Book notes. Marcus's approach to the book seems strong. What practices is he following correctly? Are there any improvements you would suggest?
    5. Overall assessment. Based on the MGU Research Note Model, evaluate Marcus's overall note-taking approach. Which elements are present? Which are missing? What specific advice would you give Marcus before he begins drafting his paper?

    Model Entry: A Full Note-Taking Session Using All Three Note Types

    The following model shows how a researcher might record notes during a single reading session, using summary, paraphrase, and quotation notes appropriately and with all required elements.

    Session Header

    Date: November 8

    Research question: How do college readiness programs affect first-generation college students' academic persistence?

    Source: Yosso, Tara J. Critical Race Counterstories Along the Chicana/Chicano Educational Pipeline. Routledge, 2006. (Library call number: LC2674 .Y67 2006)

    Summary
    Pages covered: Chapter 3 (pp. 47–72)

    Yosso's third chapter develops the concept of “cultural wealth,” a framework for understanding the resources that students from marginalized communities bring to educational settings. The chapter argues against deficit-based models that treat minority students primarily in terms of what they lack compared to middle-class white students. Instead, Yosso identifies six types of cultural capital — aspirational, linguistic, familial, social, navigational, and resistant — that she argues researchers and educators have systematically undervalued. The chapter is strongly theoretical and builds the conceptual vocabulary that subsequent chapters use in analyzing students' experiences.

    Usefulness: Very useful for the theoretical framework section of my paper. The concept of “cultural wealth” directly challenges the deficit model that several sources I've found use implicitly. Will need to engage with this when reviewing the literature.

    Limits: This chapter does not provide empirical data on persistence outcomes — it is a theoretical framework. Need data sources to complement this.

    Paraphrase
    Page: 52

    Students from marginalized backgrounds often arrive in higher education carrying knowledge and skills developed through family and community experiences — abilities to communicate across languages and cultural contexts, to navigate institutions that were not designed for them, and to draw on social networks for support and guidance. Yosso argues that these competencies represent genuine forms of capital that higher education institutions fail to recognize or build upon.

    Marginal note: “Cultural wealth,” “aspirational capital,” “linguistic capital,” etc. are Yosso's coined terms — if I use them in the paper, I must attribute them, even in paraphrase.

    Quote
    Page: 69

    “Instead of asking 'What's wrong with these students?' we should be asking 'What does this student know and how can educational institutions better utilize community cultural wealth?'”

    Why I quoted this: Precise formulation of the conceptual shift Yosso is advocating. The contrast between the two questions captures the theoretical move very concisely. May use in my introduction when I set up the deficit-model critique.

    My Response — Not from Source

    The cultural wealth framework seems useful but I want to check: does this framework have empirical support, or is it primarily a theoretical reframe? Need to find studies that have applied the cultural wealth model to first-generation student persistence outcomes specifically. Also: Yosso's work is from 2006 — look for more recent empirical work that builds on or responds to her framework.

    Note on the Model Entry

    The final entry is clearly labeled as My Response and contains no source content. This separation keeps the researcher's thinking distinguishable from the source's content — the core habit taught in Section 34.5.

    Misinformation Alert

    The Accuracy Trap — When Your Notes Misrepresent Sources

    A specific kind of source misuse does not involve plagiarism — it involves misrepresentation. This happens when note-taking records what the researcher wanted the source to say rather than what the source actually said. Misrepresentation in notes often happens in one of several ways.

    Pattern 1
    Selective quotation that distorts meaning

    A researcher quotes one sentence from a paragraph that supports their argument, while the surrounding sentences qualify, limit, or contradict that sentence. For example, if an author writes: “Some studies have found modest associations between urban agriculture participation and improved food security outcomes, though these findings are inconsistent and the causal mechanisms remain unclear” — a selective note that records only “studies have found... improved food security outcomes” misrepresents the source's careful hedging as confident endorsement.

    Pattern 2
    Paraphrase that inflates a claim

    A researcher paraphrases a source's tentative finding as a definitive conclusion. The source says “the data suggest a possible link”; the note says “research shows that...”

    Pattern 3
    Summary that reverses emphasis

    An author devotes most of a chapter to arguing against a position, and briefly acknowledges the strongest objection to that argument. A careless summary records the acknowledged objection as the author's main point.

    When you write a summary or paraphrase note, ask yourself: If the author read this note, would they recognize it as an accurate representation of what they wrote? If the answer is no, revise the note until it is accurate. Pay special attention to hedging language in sources — words like “suggests,” “may,” “in some contexts,” “preliminary,” and “under certain conditions.” These qualifiers are not decorative. They are substantive claims about the strength and scope of the evidence. Record them in your notes, and preserve them when you integrate sources into your paper.

    The MGU Misinformation Check — Adapted for Note-Taking

    What is the source actually claiming? (Not what you hoped it would claim.)

    How confident is the source in that claim? (Definitive? Tentative? Contested?)

    What does the source say the evidence shows? (Not what you conclude the evidence shows.)

    What does the source acknowledge as uncertain?

    Does your note accurately represent the source's own certainty level?

    Remember

    Accurate notes produce accurate papers. Inaccurate notes produce papers that misrepresent the scholarly record — which is its own form of misinformation.

    Citation Check

    Recording Source Information for Notes: A Citation-Ready Approach

    The most efficient approach to citation in research is to record full citation information at the moment you begin taking notes on a source. If you build citation-ready note headers from the start, you will not need to reconstruct source information when you are ready to format your bibliography.

    Citation-Ready Header: Book

    Author: Winne, Mark
    Title: Closing the Food Gap: Resetting the Table in the Land of Plenty
    Publisher: Beacon Press
    Year: 2008
    City: Boston
    Call number: HD9006 .U6 W56 2008
    Format: Physical book, MGU Library

    StyleCitation Built from Header

    Citation-Ready Header: Journal Article (from Database)

    Author: Guitart, Daniela; Pickering, Catherine; Byrne, Jason
    Title: “Past and Future Research Directions for Urban Agriculture: An Outlook”
    Journal: Urban Food Security Review
    Volume/Issue: Vol. 12, No. 3
    Year: 2018
    Pages: 145–162
    DOI: [record DOI from database record]
    Accessed via: [name of database]

    StyleCitation Built from Header
    MLAGuitart, Daniela, et al. “Past and Future Research Directions for Urban Agriculture: An Outlook.” Urban Food Security Review, vol. 12, no. 3, 2018, pp. 145–162.
    APAGuitart, D., Pickering, C., & Byrne, J. (2018). Past and future research directions for urban agriculture: An outlook. Urban Food Security Review, 12(3), 145–162.

    Common Citation Errors Linked to Note-Taking Problems

    • Omitting page numbers in notes leads to page-less citations and missing in-text page references.
    • Not recording the edition leads to incorrect edition information in citations.
    • Recording only the author's last name leads to confusion when you have multiple sources by the same author or authors with the same last name.
    • Not recording the database or URL for digital sources leads to incomplete citations for online materials.
    • Recording the cover date instead of the copyright date leads to incorrect publication years. Always check the verso of the title page for the copyright date.
    Habit Worth Building

    Build the habit of constructing the full citation header before you write the first note. It takes two minutes and saves twenty.

    Research Checklist

    Chapter 34 Research Checklist

    Note-Taking Checklist
    Use this checklist every time you take notes on a source.
    Before You Begin Taking Notes
    • I have recorded the full citation information for this source (author, title, publisher, date, pages, URL or database).
    • I know whether I am looking for a general summary of the source or specific passages.
    • I understand my research question and know what kind of evidence or argument I am looking for.
    • I have a clear system for labeling note types (summary, paraphrase, quotation) and separating source content from my own commentary.
    During Note-Taking
    • I labeled every note entry with its type: [SUMMARY], [PARAPHRASE], or [QUOTE].
    • I recorded a page number (or equivalent location marker) for every paraphrase note.
    • I recorded a page number for every quotation note.
    • I put quotation marks around every word I copied directly from the source.
    • I closed or set aside the source before writing paraphrase notes.
    • I wrote any personal commentary, reactions, or connections in a clearly labeled separate section.
    • I checked my paraphrase notes against the original to verify that I did not patchwrite.
    • I recorded any technical terms or coined phrases from the source that will require attribution even in paraphrase.
    After Note-Taking
    • I can trace every note back to a specific source with complete citation information.
    • Every quotation in my notes has quotation marks and a page number.
    • Every paraphrase in my notes has a page number.
    • My summaries accurately represent the source's argument, including any hedging or qualification.
    • My notes are organized in a way that I will be able to use them efficiently when I begin drafting.
    • I have updated my research log to record that I have taken notes on this source.
    When Moving from Notes to Draft
    • I am selecting the right note type for each point I want to make in the paper (summary for general points, paraphrase for specific content, quotation for exact language).
    • I am using signal phrases to attribute every piece of source material.
    • I am preserving the source's hedging language when the source was uncertain or qualified.
    • I am checking my draft against my notes to ensure I have not borrowed language without attribution.
    • I am building in-text citations from the page numbers recorded in my notes.
    Chapter 34 — Back Matter

    Chapter Summary and Review

    Chapter Summary

    • Sec 34.1There are three kinds of source notes — summary, paraphrase, and quotation — each with a distinct purpose, length, and relationship to the original source language. Every note must begin with complete source information.
    • Sec 34.2Summary notes capture the main argument, finding, or content of a source or a section in condensed form and in the researcher's own words. They are most useful for recording overall arguments, mapping a research field, and providing early-stage context.
    • Sec 34.3Paraphrase notes translate specific passages into the researcher's own words at approximately the same level of detail as the original. They require genuine transformation of language and structure, and they require page numbers just as direct quotations do.
    • Sec 34.4Quotation notes reproduce the exact words of a source within quotation marks. They are used selectively, with required elements: quotation marks, exact transcription, a page number, and a clear link to the source record.
    • Sec 34.5Keeping source words clearly separated from the researcher's own words requires consistent labeling, a dedicated section for personal commentary, and immediate application of quotation marks to any copied text.
    • Sec 34.6Page numbers must be recorded at the moment of note-taking for all paraphrase and quotation notes. Sources without page numbers require location markers such as paragraph numbers, section headings, chapter numbers, or timestamps.
    • Sec 34.7Patchwriting — the near-copying of source language under the guise of paraphrase — is created most often at the note-taking stage when researchers look at the original text while writing. The solution is to close the source and write from memory.
    • Sec 34.8Turning notes into draft material requires selecting the appropriate note type for each moment in the paper, using signal phrases to attribute all source material, preserving hedging language, and building citations from recorded page numbers.
    Central Principle

    The central principle of this textbook applies to note-taking with particular force: Do not merely find information. Earn trust in information. Notes that accurately record what sources say, that clearly mark what belongs to the source and what belongs to the researcher, and that preserve the full complexity and qualification of source arguments are the foundation of trustworthy research.

    3. Why do paraphrase notes require page numbers, even though the researcher is not reproducing the source's exact words? What information does a page number provide that a DOI or URL cannot fully replace?
    4. Explain the step-by-step process for writing a reliable paraphrase note. Why is the step of setting aside the original text so important?

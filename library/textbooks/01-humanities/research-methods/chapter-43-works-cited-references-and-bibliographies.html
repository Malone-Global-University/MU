Chapter 43 — Research Skill Focus

Building and Auditing Final Source Lists

Every research paper ends with a promise. The final source list at the end of your document promises your reader: these are the sources I used, you can find them, you can read them, and you can check my work. This chapter teaches students how to build accurate, complete, and properly formatted final source lists — works cited pages, references pages, and bibliographies — and how to check them against in-text citations to produce research that is trustworthy, verifiable, and academically honest.

A sloppy works cited page, a references list full of errors, or a bibliography with missing entries is not merely an aesthetic problem. It breaks the chain of verification. It makes your sources harder to find. It signals to readers that your relationship with your evidence is careless, which makes them less likely to trust your conclusions.

Why This Matters

This chapter matters because final source lists are where the promise of academic honesty becomes concrete and checkable. It matters to your instructor, who needs to verify that your sources are real and fairly represented. It matters to future readers who may follow your evidence into the same sources. It matters to the broader academic community, which depends on the chain of citations connecting one piece of scholarship to another. And it matters to you, because an accurate, complete, and correctly formatted source list is the visible sign of a researcher who knows what they are doing. The central principle of this textbook applies here with particular force: do not merely find information — earn trust in information.

Section 43.1

Final Source Lists

A final source list is the collection of complete citation entries that appears at the end of a research paper, report, essay, or any other document that draws on outside sources. Every source cited in the body of the paper must appear in the final source list. Every entry in the final source list must correspond to a citation in the body of the paper.

This relationship — between in-text citations and the final source list — is the foundation of citation integrity. The two parts of a citation system work together: the in-text citation tells the reader where in the paper a source was used, and the final source list entry tells the reader everything they need to find that source.

TermStyleWhat It Lists
Works CitedMLAOnly sources actually cited in the paper
ReferencesAPA; some Chicago Author-DateOnly sources actually cited
BibliographyChicago Notes-Bibliography; general useCited sources, and sometimes sources consulted but not directly cited
Annotated BibliographyAny styleCitation entries plus a short evaluative paragraph for each source (see Chapter 35)

Students sometimes use these terms interchangeably, but they have distinct meanings. Using the wrong term on a paper — writing "Works Cited" when the assignment calls for a "References" page — is a small error, but it signals unfamiliarity with citation conventions. Learning the correct term for each style is part of learning the style itself.

Why Final Source Lists Are Required

Credit. Sources must be credited to their authors and publishers. A final source list is the mechanism by which credit is given systematically.

Verification. A complete citation entry contains enough information to locate the original source. Without a final source list, readers cannot verify that your sources say what you claim, or that they even exist.

Scholarship. Final source lists are the connective tissue of the scholarly conversation, allowing readers to trace an argument's intellectual genealogy.

Integrity. A final source list is a declaration of intellectual honesty: I did not invent these sources, and here is the information you need to read them yourself.

What Goes In — and What Stays Out

Final source lists include every source quoted, paraphrased, summarized, or referred to in the paper, including sources of data, statistics, images, or maps. They do not include sources you found but did not use, sources you read but did not cite (unless you are writing a full bibliography rather than a works cited or references page), sources cut during revision, or personal knowledge and experience. The test is simple: if you have an in-text citation, the source must appear in your final list. If something appears in your final list, you must have cited it in your paper.

Section 43.2

Works Cited

A Works Cited page is the final source list used in MLA style (Modern Language Association). It lists every source cited in the paper, arranged alphabetically by the author's last name, or by the title of the work when no author is named. Works Cited is the standard term in humanities disciplines — literature, language studies, film, philosophy, art history, and related fields.

MLA Works Cited: Basic Formatting Rules

Heading. The page is headed "Works Cited," centered, in the same font and size as the rest of the paper, with no bolding, underlining, or quotation marks.

Arrangement. Alphabetical by author's last name; by the first significant word of the title (skipping "A," "An," and "The") when no author is named.

Hanging indent. The first line begins at the left margin; subsequent lines are indented half an inch.

Spacing. Double-spaced throughout, with no extra space between entries.

MLA Works Cited: Core Source Elements

MLA style uses a flexible, container-based system built from up to nine core elements, included in order when available: Author; Title of Source; Title of Container; Other Contributors; Version; Number (volume/issue); Publisher; Publication Date; Location (pages, URL, or DOI). Not every source has all nine elements — students include what applies and omit what does not.

MLA Works Cited — Example Entries
Graff, Gerald. Clueless in Academe: How Schooling Obscures the Life of the Mind. Yale University Press, 2003.
Booth, Wayne C., and Gregory G. Colomb. The Craft of Research. 4th ed., University of Chicago Press, 2016.
Wineburg, Sam, et al. "Lateral Reading and the Nature of Expertise." Teachers College Record, vol. 121, no. 11, 2019, pp. 1–40. doi:10.1177/016146811912101102.
Apuzzo, Matt. "Fired F.B.I. Director Comey to Testify in Open Senate Hearing." The New York Times, 2 June 2017, www.nytimes.com/2017/06/02/us/politics/ james-comey-testify-senate.html.
"Research and Information Literacy Standards." Association of College and Research Libraries, www.ala.org/acrl/standards/informationliteracycompetency. Accessed 14 Mar. 2024.
United States, Census Bureau. "QuickFacts: United States." United States Census Bureau, 2023, www.census.gov/quickfacts/fact/table/US/PST045222.
MLA Works Cited: Common Problems

Alphabetizing by first name instead of last name; forgetting the hanging indent; including sources that were never cited in the paper; using inconsistent punctuation between elements; and capitalizing titles incorrectly (MLA uses title case — capitalize first, last, and principal words, but not articles, prepositions, or coordinating conjunctions in the middle of a title).

Section 43.3

References

A References page is the final source list used in APA style (American Psychological Association). Like a Works Cited page, it lists only the sources actually cited, arranged alphabetically with hanging indents. References pages are standard in the social sciences, education, psychology, nursing, business, and many natural science fields.

APA References: Basic Formatting Rules

Heading. "References," centered (bold and centered in APA 7th edition; check the edition your instructor requires).

Arrangement. Alphabetical by author last name. Multiple works by the same author run oldest to most recent; same-year works get lowercase letters (2021a, 2021b).

Author names. Last names followed by initials only, not full first names — "Angela Davis" becomes "Davis, A."

Publication year. Appears immediately after the author name(s), in parentheses, because APA in-text citations use author and year.

APA References — Example Entries
Graff, G. (2003). Clueless in academe: How schooling obscures the life of the mind. Yale University Press.
Booth, W. C., & Colomb, G. G. (2016). The craft of research (4th ed.). University of Chicago Press.
Wineburg, S., McGrew, S., Breakstone, J., & Ortega, T. (2019). Lateral reading and the nature of expertise. Teachers College Record, 121(11), 1–40. https://doi.org/10.1177/016146811912101102
Apuzzo, M. (2017, June 2). Fired F.B.I. director Comey to testify in open Senate hearing. The New York Times. https://www.nytimes.com/2017/06/02/ us/politics/james-comey-testify-senate.html
Association of College and Research Libraries. (2000). Information literacy competency standards for higher education. American Library Association. https://www.ala.org/acrl/standards/informationliteracycompetency
U.S. Census Bureau. (2023). QuickFacts: United States. https://www.census.gov/quickfacts/fact/table/US/PST045222
APA References: Common Problems

Using full first names instead of initials; applying title case to titles when APA requires sentence case for article and book titles; placing the year at the end instead of right after the author; forgetting the DOI when one exists; and italicizing the wrong parts (journal names and book titles are italicized; article titles are not).

Section 43.4

Bibliographies

A bibliography can mean two things. In Chicago Notes-Bibliography style — used primarily in humanities disciplines — the final source list is called a "Bibliography" and is formatted differently from MLA and APA. In general use, "bibliography" can also mean a broader list of sources on a topic, including works consulted but not directly cited.

Chicago Notes-Bibliography: How It Works

In this style, citations appear in the body of the paper as footnotes or endnotes — numbered superscripts pointing to a note containing full citation information the first time a source is cited, with shortened forms afterward. The Bibliography at the end lists all cited sources alphabetically, with author names inverted for alphabetization and different punctuation than the footnote form.

Chicago — Footnote vs. Bibliography Entry (Same Source)
Footnote (first citation): 1. Wayne C. Booth and Gregory G. Colomb, The Craft of Research, 4th ed. (Chicago: University of Chicago Press, 2016), 45.
Bibliography entry: Booth, Wayne C., and Gregory G. Colomb. The Craft of Research. 4th ed. Chicago: University of Chicago Press, 2016.

Notice the differences: the footnote uses normal name order, commas between elements, and the specific page cited. The bibliography entry inverts the first author's name, uses periods between major elements, and omits the specific page (since it records the whole source).

Chicago Bibliography: Formatting Rules

Heading. "Bibliography," centered. Arrangement. Alphabetical by first author's last name; a three-em dash (———) replaces the author's name for subsequent works by the same author. Hanging indent. Half-inch, same as MLA and APA. Inclusive page ranges. The bibliography lists the full page range of an article or chapter, not just the page cited in the footnote.

Chicago Bibliography — Example Entries
Booth, Wayne C., and Gregory G. Colomb. The Craft of Research. 4th ed. Chicago: University of Chicago Press, 2016.
Wineburg, Sam, Sarah McGrew, Joel Breakstone, and Teresa Ortega. "Lateral Reading and the Nature of Expertise." Teachers College Record 121, no. 11 (2019): 1–40. https://doi.org/10.1177/016146811912101102.
Apuzzo, Matt. "Fired F.B.I. Director Comey to Testify in Open Senate Hearing." New York Times, June 2, 2017. https://www.nytimes.com/2017/06/02/ us/politics/james-comey-testify-senate.html.
When a Bibliography Includes More Than Cited Sources

In some graduate-level and book-length projects, instructors ask for a bibliography that includes sources consulted as well as sources cited — acknowledging the full scope of a researcher's reading. A "select" or "selected" bibliography narrows that list to the most important sources. When an assignment calls for a bibliography rather than a works cited or references page, read the instructions carefully, and ask your instructor when in doubt.

When an element is missing, citation styles have standard solutions: MLA uses the title in place of the author and "n.d." or "n.p." when a date or publisher is absent; APA uses "(n.d.)" in the author-date citation when no publication date exists. Section 43.6

Alphabetization and Formatting

Correct alphabetization and consistent formatting are the two most visually apparent signs that a final source list has been prepared with care.

Alphabetization Rules

Basic alphabetization. MLA, APA, and Chicago all alphabetize by author last name. Multiple authors. Based on the last name of the first author listed. No author. Alphabetize by the first significant word of the title, ignoring "A," "An," and "The." Organizational authors. Alphabetize by the first word of the organization's name. Same author, multiple works. MLA orders by title; APA orders oldest to newest with lettered suffixes for same-year works; Chicago uses a three-em dash after the first entry. Hyphenated names. Alphabetize by the first element. Names with prefixes. Follow the author's own convention when known.

Letter-by-Letter vs. Word-by-Word Alphabetization

Most academic styles use letter-by-letter alphabetization: spaces and hyphens are ignored, so "Newark" (n-e-w-a-r-k) comes before "New York" (n-e-w-y-o-r-k) because 'a' precedes 'y.' This becomes relevant mainly in longer bibliographies with many similar entries.

Formatting Consistency

ProblemExampleCorrect Approach
Inconsistent italicsSome book titles italicized, some notAll book titles italicized throughout
Inconsistent capitalizationSome titles in title case, some in sentence caseApply the style's capitalization rules consistently
Inconsistent publisher locationSome entries include city, some don'tFollow one edition's rules throughout
Mixed date formats"2023" vs. "March 2023"Apply the style's date format consistently
Missing periodsSome entries end with periods, some don'tEvery entry ends with a period
Inconsistent DOI/URL formattingSome as links, some as plain textApply the style's URL/DOI formatting consistently
A Faster Way to Catch Inconsistency

Read through your entire source list looking only at one element at a time — first all author names, then all titles, then all dates, then all publishers. This targeted reading catches more errors than reading each entry start to finish.

Section 43.7

Hanging Indents

The hanging indent is one of the most immediately visible formatting features of a correctly formatted final source list. When an entry runs longer than one line — which most do — the first line sits at the left margin while all subsequent lines are indented, letting the author name "hang" to the left for easy scanning.

Why Hanging Indents Matter

In a long source list, readers often want to scan for a specific source rather than read every entry. The hanging indent puts the author name at the far left, making it visually distinct. Without it, every line aligns at the margin, creating a wall of text that is harder to navigate. Some students indent every line equally — a "reverse" hanging indent — which is also incorrect.

How to Create Hanging Indents

In Microsoft Word: Select the source list text. Go to Format → Paragraph. Under Indentation, find "Special" and select "Hanging." Set the value to 0.5 inches. Click OK. (You can also drag the bottom triangle on the ruler.)

In Google Docs: Select the source list text. Go to Format → Align & indent → Indentation options. Under "Special indent," choose "Hanging." Set the indent to 0.5 inches. Click Apply.

A Common Mistake

Pressing Tab manually at the start of each continuation line looks like a hanging indent but is not formatted as one. If the entry is later edited or the font size changes, tab-created indents break unpredictably. Always use the paragraph formatting method instead.

MLA, APA, and Chicago Notes-Bibliography all require a 0.5-inch hanging indent — one of the few formatting elements that is entirely consistent across citation styles.

Sample Entry with Correct Hanging Indent
Wineburg, Sam, Sarah McGrew, Joel Breakstone, and Teresa Ortega. "Lateral Reading and the Nature of Expertise." Teachers College Record, vol. 121, no. 11, 2019, pp. 1–40. doi:10.1177/016146811912101102.

Section 43.8

Checking Source Lists Against In-Text Citations

The final and most important step in preparing any source list is the citation audit: a systematic check confirming that every in-text citation has a corresponding entry in the final source list, and every entry corresponds to an in-text citation. This check sounds simple, but it is the step students most commonly skip — and where the most errors are found.

Why Citation Audits Are Necessary

Sources get added and dropped during revision. Citation generators produce formatting errors. Copying and pasting from databases corrupts italics and characters. Multiple sources by similarly named authors cause confusion. Each of these everyday research habits creates a real chance that your in-text citations and your source list will drift out of alignment.

How to Conduct a Citation Audit

The Four-Part Audit
Working from the paper to the list, and back again
  • Part 1Body to source list. Read the paper start to finish. For every in-text citation, confirm a matching source list entry exists, and that the name or date matches.
  • Part 2Source list to body. Read the source list start to finish. For every entry, confirm it was actually cited somewhere in the paper. Remove entries with no matching citation.
  • Part 3Name and date matching. Confirm author spelling matches exactly; confirm APA years match; confirm MLA no-author titles match between citation and entry.
  • Part 4Format check. Confirm hanging indents, alphabetization, italics, capitalization, DOIs/URLs, and end punctuation are all correct and consistent.
Principle: Match in both directions, then check formatting last.

Practice, Review, and Library Connections

Search Practice

Activity 43.1Build and Check a Practice Source List
  1. Choose a topic for a short research paper (3–4 pages). Identify five to six sources you would use: at least one book, one scholarly article, one credible website, and one additional source of your choice.
  2. For each source, record: author name(s) in full, title, container title, publisher, publication date, volume/issue numbers, page numbers, and URL or DOI.
  3. Build a final source list in MLA, APA, or Chicago format, as assigned.
  4. Write five model in-text citations corresponding to five different sources on your list.
  5. Conduct a citation audit: confirm every in-text citation matches a source list entry, and every entry has a corresponding citation.
Reflection Questions

Which source was most difficult to cite, and why? What information was hardest to find? Did you find any mismatches during your audit, and how did you fix them? What would you do differently when keeping citation records during research?

Source Check

A student submitted the following partial Works Cited page for a paper on misinformation and social media.

Submitted Works Cited (with errors)
Wardle, Claire and Hossein Derakhshan. "Information Disorder: Toward an Interdisciplinary Framework for Research and Policymaking." Council of Europe, 2017. "Fake News." Wikipedia. Pennycook, Gordon, et al. Reliability of News From Mainstream vs. Non-Mainstream Sources. 2019. Wineburg Sam and Sarah McGrew. "Lateral Reading: Reading Less and Learning More When Evaluating Digital Information." Stanford History Education Group, Oct. 2019. https://sheg.stanford.edu/research/lateral-reading.
Analysis of Problems
  • Entry 1Missing comma before "and" the second author; no DOI, URL, or page/document number for a government council report.
  • Entry 2Wikipedia is generally not an acceptable academic source. The entry is also incomplete: no author, no quotation marks around the article title, no URL, no access date, no version date.
  • Entry 3Title not in quotation marks (it is a study, not a book); missing journal/publication name, volume/issue, pages, and DOI or URL.
  • Entry 4Missing comma after "Wineburg"; unclear publisher/container distinction; missing an access date for a website that may change.
Corrected Entry 4: Wineburg, Sam, and Sarah McGrew. "Lateral Reading: Reading Less and Learning More When Evaluating Digital Information." Stanford History Education Group, Oct. 2019, sheg.stanford.edu/research/lateral-reading. Accessed 15 Mar. 2024.

Lesson: citation errors cluster around punctuation, missing elements, and incomplete location information. Every entry must be checked against the citation style's requirements, not assembled from memory.

Research Notebook

One of the most common citation problems is not a formatting problem — it is an information problem. Record full citation information when you first access a source, not at the end of the project.

MGU Research Note Model Applied to Citation Recording
Source: [Full citation information in the citation style you are using] Author: [Full name(s)] Title: [Full title and subtitle] Container: [Journal name, website name, or book title] Volume/Issue: [If applicable] Publication date: [Year, and month/day if relevant] Publisher: [Name] Pages: [Full page range for article; specific pages you will use] DOI or URL: [If online — copy the full link immediately] Access date: [Today's date, if a website] Summary: [2–3 sentences summarizing the source's main argument or findings] Key evidence: [The specific data, claims, or passages relevant to your research] Quotation: [Any direct quotation you may use, with exact page number] Paraphrase: [Your restatement of key ideas in your own words] Usefulness: [How this source helps answer your research question] Limits: [What this source does not cover or where it may be biased] Citation information: [Confirm you have everything needed to complete the citation]
Practical Tip

Write out the complete citation entry immediately while the source is in front of you. Build your final list as you go — at the end of your research, all that remains is to alphabetize, format consistently, and conduct the final audit.

Misinformation Alert

Fabricated and Hallucinated Citations

Fabricated citations list a source that was never consulted. Hallucinated citations are a specific risk with AI writing tools, which sometimes generate plausible-looking author names, journal names, titles, and dates for sources that do not actually exist — because AI systems generate text statistically, pattern-matching to what citations look like, rather than consulting a real database of publications. Copying such citations into a paper is a form of academic dishonesty even when accidental.

The MGU Misinformation Check applied to citations: Who made it — a database record, a publisher page, or an AI tool? What is the claim — that this source exists and says what you report? What evidence is offered — can you access the source through a library database or catalog? What is missing — does the citation feel generic or inconsistent with real sources? Can another reliable source confirm it — does it appear in a library database search? Is the emotional reaction stronger than the evidence — does the source seem suspiciously perfect for your argument? Has the source been altered or misrepresented — did you read it yourself, or rely on someone else's description?

The Rule

Every citation in your paper must refer to a source you have personally accessed and read. If you cannot find the source, do not cite it.

Citation Check

The same journal article formatted in MLA, APA, and Chicago.

MLA Works Cited
Brennen, J. Scott, and Daniel Kreiss. "Digitalization." The International Encyclopedia of Communication Theory and Philosophy, edited by Klaus Bruhn Jensen and Robert T. Craig, Wiley, 2016, pp. 1–11. doi:10.1002/9781118766804.wbiect111.

Brennen, J. S., & Kreiss, D. (2016). Digitalization. In K. B. Jensen & R. T. Craig (Eds.), The international encyclopedia of communication theory and philosophy (pp. 1–11). Wiley. https://doi.org/10.1002/9781118766804.wbiect111

Brennen, J. Scott, and Daniel Kreiss. "Digitalization." In The International Encyclopedia of Communication Theory and Philosophy, edited by Klaus Bruhn Jensen and Robert T. Craig, 1–11. Wiley, 2016. https://doi.org/10.1002/9781118766804.wbiect111.
Key Differences to Notice

APA uses sentence case for the title; MLA and Chicago use title case. APA uses author initials; MLA and Chicago use full first names. APA places the year right after the author; MLA and Chicago place it near the end. APA uses "&"; MLA and Chicago use "and." The DOI omits "https://" in MLA but includes the full URL in APA and Chicago. These differences reflect each style's internal logic, developed to serve the reading habits of specific academic communities.

  • Sec 43.8The citation audit — checking in-text citations against the source list in both directions, then checking format — is the essential final step before submitting any paper.

Chapter 26 — Front Matter

Research Skill Focus and Why This Matters

Every research project reaches the same moment: you have found sources, and now you must decide whether to trust them. This chapter teaches students how to evaluate sources using four core standards — relevance, credibility, accuracy, and authority — and how to bring those standards together through the MGU Source Evaluation Model. Evaluation is not a checklist exercise performed once at the end of a search. It is an ongoing judgment process that begins the moment a source is found and continues through every stage of research.

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to apply the MGU Source Evaluation Model systematically, explain why each criterion matters, and make confident decisions about which sources to use, which to set aside, and which to verify further before trusting.

Research Skill Focus

This chapter teaches students how to evaluate sources using four core standards: relevance, credibility, accuracy, and authority. Students will learn that evaluation is not a checklist exercise performed once at the end of a search — it is an ongoing judgment process that begins the moment a source is found and continues through every stage of research.

Why This Matters

A research paper built on unreliable, inaccurate, or mismatched sources is a weak paper, no matter how well it is written. A decision made on the basis of a misleading source is a poor decision, regardless of how carefully it was reasoned. Evidence quality is not a technicality. It is the foundation of everything research is supposed to accomplish.

Most students have heard the advice "use credible sources." The problem with that phrase is that it does not explain anything. It does not tell you what credibility looks like in practice, how to distinguish it from the appearance of credibility, or how the same source can be credible for one research question and poorly matched for another.

This chapter takes each major evaluation criterion — relevance, credibility, accuracy, and authority — and examines what it actually means, how to assess it, where the judgment is easy and where it is hard, and what mistakes researchers commonly make when applying it. It also introduces the MGU Source Evaluation Model as a systematic framework that brings all these criteria together into a coherent evaluation practice.

Learning source evaluation matters beyond the library and the classroom. Every time you read a news article, receive a health recommendation, hear a statistic cited in an argument, or encounter a claim shared on social media, you are in a situation that requires source evaluation skills. The habits you build in academic research travel into every domain of life where information matters — which is to say, every domain of life.

Central Principle

Do not merely find information. Earn trust in information. Students should learn that strong research requires curiosity, skepticism, organization, humility, and evidence — and that uncertainty is not failure.

Defining the Core Standards: Relevance, Credibility, and Accuracy

Section 26.1

Source Evaluation as Judgment

Source evaluation is not a mechanical procedure. It is a form of judgment — an intellectual skill that improves with practice, depends on context, and requires genuine thinking rather than the application of a simple formula.

This matters because many students approach source evaluation as if it were a pass/fail test with clear categories: credible sources and non-credible sources, reliable websites and unreliable websites, good journals and bad journals. The reality is considerably more complex.

Evaluation Is Context-Dependent

The same source can be highly appropriate for one research question and poorly suited to another. A newspaper article from a major wire service is an excellent source for understanding how a political event was reported to the public and what information was available at the time. That same article is a poor source for understanding the full historical context of the event, the scholarly debate about its causes, or the technical policy questions it raises. The article has not changed. The research question has.

A popular science book written by a respected scientist is an appropriate source for a general-audience explanation of a scientific concept. It is a weaker source for the current state of peer-reviewed evidence on a specific research question in that field, because popular books are aimed at general readers and may simplify, date, or incompletely represent the technical literature.

Source evaluation therefore always begins with a question: what does this research project require, and does this source meet that requirement?

Evaluation Is Cumulative

No single source needs to carry the full weight of a research project. One source may be strong on historical context but weak on recent data. Another may be authoritative on the policy dimensions of a topic but silent on the human experience. Experienced researchers evaluate a set of sources, not just individual sources. They ask not only "Is this source credible?" but "Does this source, combined with my other sources, give me what I need to answer my question responsibly?"

Evaluation Is a Skill, Not an Instinct

Students sometimes believe that evaluating sources is a matter of gut feeling — that trustworthy sources "look" trustworthy and unreliable sources announce themselves through obvious warning signs. This belief is dangerous. Misinformation, propaganda, and poorly supported claims are often packaged in formats that look professional, credible, and authoritative. Sophisticated deception is designed specifically to defeat gut-feeling evaluation.

Real source evaluation requires deliberate effort: reading carefully, checking claims against other sources, investigating the author and publisher, tracing evidence back to its origins, and applying specific criteria consistently. Chapters 28 and 32 of this textbook cover misinformation and verification in more depth. This chapter lays the evaluative foundation that those later chapters build on.

This chapter focuses primarily on relevance, credibility (which encompasses authority, evidence, and purpose), and accuracy. The remaining criteria — currency and context — are addressed in the sections that follow and revisited in later chapters as they apply to specific source types.

Section 26.2

Relevance

Relevance is the first evaluation question, and it is the one most often rushed. A source is relevant if it actually contributes to answering the research question — not merely if it mentions the topic, appears in a keyword search, or seems related on the surface.

Many students accumulate sources that are not genuinely relevant because they are working too quickly, because they are grateful to find anything that mentions their topic, or because they have not articulated their research question precisely enough to evaluate sources against it. The result is a research project padded with marginally connected material that dilutes the quality of the argument.

Types of Relevance

TypeDescriptionExample
Directly relevantThe source addresses the specific research question at handA peer-reviewed study analyzing statistical relationships between sentencing policy and incarceration demographics, for a question about sentencing disparities
Background relevantProvides context, definitions, or history that helps understanding but does not directly address the questionAn overview of the history of sentencing reform — useful early in research but not central evidence
Tangentially relevantTouches the general topic but does not address the specific issueAn article about prison conditions, for a question specifically about mandatory minimums
Apparently relevant, not actuallyAppears related by keyword or title, but does not address the topicA study titled broadly that turns out to focus on a different issue entirely

Testing Relevance

To test whether a source is genuinely relevant, ask:

  1. What is my research question, stated precisely?
  2. What specific claim, data, argument, or perspective does this source offer?
  3. Does what this source offers actually help me answer my question?
  4. If I removed this source from my research, would I be missing something essential?

If you cannot clearly answer questions 2 and 3, the source is probably not as relevant as you initially thought.

Relevance and Reading Depth

You do not need to read an entire source before evaluating its relevance. Experienced researchers evaluate relevance quickly by reading the abstract or summary, the table of contents or section headings, and the introduction. For a scholarly article, the abstract usually tells you everything you need to know to assess relevance. For a book, the introduction and table of contents serve the same function. If those elements do not connect clearly to your research question, the source is likely not relevant enough to read in full.

When Relevance Is Not Obvious

Sometimes relevance is not obvious at first reading. A source may seem irrelevant until you read it more carefully and realize it addresses your question from an unexpected angle. A historical study of a different case may illuminate patterns that apply to your topic. A methodological article may explain how to evaluate evidence for your question even though it does not address your specific topic.

In these cases, relevance requires interpretation rather than direct matching. This interpretive relevance is legitimate, but it demands that you explain clearly in your paper how and why the source contributes to your argument. Do not assume readers will see the connection on their own.

Section 26.3

Credibility

Credibility is the quality that makes a source worthy of trust. A credible source is one that was produced carefully, by someone who knows what they are talking about, in a context that holds that knowledge accountable.

Credibility is not binary. It is a spectrum, and different sources have different degrees of credibility for different purposes. Understanding what produces credibility — and what only mimics it — is one of the most important skills in research.

What Produces Credibility

FactorDescription
ExpertiseThe author or institution knows the subject area through study, work, research, or direct experience — not superficial or secondhand knowledge
AccountabilityThe author or institution is answerable for what they publish; names and affiliations are identifiable; errors can be reported and corrected
EvidenceThe source supports claims with documented, verifiable evidence rather than assertion alone
ProcessThe source was produced through a quality-control process — peer review, editorial review, institutional oversight, or professional standards
TransparencyAffiliations, funding sources, and methodologies are disclosed so the reader can investigate who is speaking and why
What Only Mimics Credibility

Professional appearance. A clean, official-looking website is one of the easiest things to fake.

Impressive-sounding names. Organizations with names containing "Institute," "Foundation," "Center," or "National" may be legitimate scholarly bodies — or advocacy groups and front organizations.

Claimed credentials. Anyone can claim to be a "doctor," "researcher," or "expert." Credentials are credible only when verified: a real degree, from an identified institution, in the relevant field.

Citations that do not hold up. Citations can be misrepresented, misquoted, or cited for claims they do not actually make. Checking citations — actually locating and reading them — is essential verification practice.

Consensus language. Phrases like "studies show" or "experts agree" without naming those studies or experts create the impression of evidence without providing it.

Section 26.4

Accuracy

Accuracy means that the information in a source is factually correct. Claims correspond to reality. Data is reported honestly. Quotations are transcribed faithfully. Events are described as they occurred. Conclusions are supported by the evidence presented.

Accuracy is distinct from credibility, though the two are related. A credible source — one produced by a knowledgeable author through a rigorous process — is more likely to be accurate, but credibility is not a guarantee of accuracy. Scholarly articles contain errors. Peer-reviewed studies have been retracted. Reputable news organizations have published false reports. Accuracy must be evaluated directly, not inferred solely from credibility.

Checking Factual Claims

The most direct way to evaluate accuracy is to check specific factual claims against other reliable sources. If a source states that a law was passed in a given year, verify this against official records. If a source presents a statistic, identify where the statistic came from and whether it is being reported accurately. This process is called verification, and Chapter 32 covers it in detail. The key principle here is that accuracy is established through comparison and triangulation — measuring one source's claims against what other reliable sources say — rather than through trusting any single source on its own authority.

Red Flags for Inaccuracy

Red FlagWhy It Matters
Factual errors you can identifyA discrepancy with what you already know deserves investigation, whether the source or your prior knowledge turns out to be wrong
Unsourced statisticsNumbers cited without indicating where they came from cannot be checked for accuracy
Outdated information presented as currentInformation accurate at one point may no longer be accurate, especially on fast-changing topics
Misrepresented or decontextualized quotationsQuotations taken out of context or truncated in meaning-changing ways are inaccuracies even if the words are technically correct
Internal inconsistenciesA source that contradicts itself shows signs of careless or dishonest production

The Difference Between Error and Deception

Not all inaccuracies are deliberate. Honest errors occur in even the most carefully produced sources. These errors are significant — they can mislead readers even without intent to deceive — but they differ in character from deliberate falsification.

The distinction matters for how you respond. A source with one identifiable error is not necessarily an unreliable source overall. A source that systematically presents false information, misrepresents evidence, or fabricates claims is a source to be rejected and, in an academic context, not cited.

Authority, Expertise, and Evidence Quality

Section 26.5

Authority and Expertise

Authority is the quality that comes from genuine knowledge and recognized standing in a field. An authoritative source is one where the person or institution behind the source actually knows what they are talking about — not just claims to know, but has the training, experience, credentials, or direct knowledge to speak reliably on the subject.

Authority is one of the criteria most frequently misunderstood by beginning researchers. The most common misunderstanding is that authority is absolute: a person is either an authority or not. The reality is that authority is always specific to a domain. Someone authoritative on cardiovascular medicine is not automatically authoritative on nutritional science, public health policy, or pharmaceutical regulation, even though all touch on health.

Whenever you evaluate a source for authority, ask: is this person authoritative on this specific question?

Types of Authority

TypeDescription
Scholarly authorityAcademic credentials in the field — doctoral degree, research position, peer-reviewed publication record. The standard for empirical and analytical research questions.
Professional authorityPractical expertise gained through professional practice — appropriate when the question concerns professional practice rather than theoretical debate.
Institutional authorityProduced by an organization with recognized standing — a government agency, professional association, major research institution, or established news organization.
Experiential authorityDirect personal experience with the subject — particularly valuable for qualitative research, oral history, and testimony.
Community authoritySpeaks from within a community with particular knowledge of its own circumstances, history, and needs.

Evaluating Author Credentials

To evaluate an author's credentials, look for institutional affiliation, educational background, publication record, professional experience, and disclosure of interests. These criteria can usually be checked through an author's institutional profile, a brief web search, or the author biography included in the publication itself.

When Credentials Cannot Be Verified

If you cannot verify an author's credentials — no institutional affiliation listed, unconfirmable educational background, publication only on advocacy sites without peer review — treat the source with caution. Unverifiable credentials are a warning sign, not necessarily proof of fraud, but sufficient reason to seek confirmation from sources whose authority can be established.

Authority and the Question of Consensus

One of the most important applications of authority evaluation is understanding scientific and scholarly consensus. On many empirical questions, there is strong consensus among the relevant expert community — representing the best current judgment of the people most qualified to evaluate the evidence.

Evaluating authority helps students understand why consensus matters. A claim supported by the overwhelming majority of researchers in a field, published in peer-reviewed venues, replicated across multiple studies, and endorsed by major scientific institutions carries far more authority than a contrarian claim by a single researcher, published outside mainstream peer review, and promoted primarily by advocacy groups with ideological or financial interests in the contrarian position.

This does not mean consensus is infallible. Scientific understanding evolves. But the revision of scientific consensus happens through the accumulation of rigorous evidence published in peer-reviewed venues and evaluated by the expert community — not through appeals to individual authority, popular opinion, or political pressure.

Section 26.6

Evidence Quality

A source can have genuine authority — a credentialed author, a reputable publisher, a rigorous production process — and still rest on weak evidence. Evaluating evidence quality means examining how the source supports its claims, not just who is making them.

Evidence quality refers to how well the evidence in a source actually supports the claims the source makes. A source with high evidence quality presents claims that are documented, sourced, proportionate, and correctly interpreted. A source with low evidence quality presents claims that are asserted without support, rely on anecdote rather than systematic evidence, or reach conclusions that go beyond what the evidence actually shows.

Types of Evidence and Their Strengths

Evidence TypeStrength
Empirical dataSystematically collected quantitative information is generally stronger than informal observation or anecdote for claims about what is happening or how often
Controlled experimentsFor causal claims, experiments that isolate variables provide stronger evidence than correlational studies alone
Peer-reviewed researchReviewed by independent experts before publication, increasing confidence in methods and conclusions
Meta-analyses and systematic reviewsSynthesize multiple independent studies; stronger than any single study because less susceptible to random variation
Expert consensusBroad agreement after extensive evaluation of evidence — stronger than any single study or individual authority
Primary sourcesDirect evidence not dependent on intermediary interpretation
Logical argumentAnalytical evidence showing why conclusions follow from premises
Anecdote and case studyIllustrates possibilities and generates hypotheses, but does not establish general truth

Questions for Evaluating Evidence Quality

  1. What specific evidence does this source offer for its key claims?
  2. Is that evidence documented and traceable — can you find the original data, study, or document?
  3. Is the evidence proportionate to the claim — or does the source make broad claims based on limited evidence?
  4. Does the evidence actually support the conclusion, or is there a gap in the reasoning?
  5. Has this evidence been tested by other researchers? Does it hold up under scrutiny?
  6. Are there alternative interpretations of this evidence that the source does not acknowledge?

Section 26.7

Source Fit

Source fit is the relationship between a source and a specific research task. A source may be credible, authoritative, accurate, and relevant in general — and still not be the right source for a specific purpose. This concept matters because research projects have different requirements at different stages and for different uses.

Matching Source Type to Research Purpose

Research PurposeBest Matched Source Types
Background understandingEncyclopedias, textbooks, overview articles
Current scholarly debatePeer-reviewed journal articles, recent monographs
Original data and statisticsGovernment reports, research databases, primary data sets
Historical contextArchival documents, historical monographs, primary sources
Public or policy debateNews coverage, policy documents, legislative records
Expert opinion and analysisScholarly commentaries, expert interviews, professional association publications
Firsthand experienceOral histories, memoirs, interviews, testimony
Legal authorityCourt decisions, statutes, regulatory documents

A source that fits perfectly for background research may be a poor fit as a central piece of scholarly evidence. A primary source that establishes direct evidence may be a poor fit for explaining broad scholarly consensus. Matching source type to research purpose is part of sophisticated source evaluation.

The Concept of Appropriate Level

Sources also differ in level of technical depth, audience, and scholarly expectation. A general-audience science magazine explains concepts clearly for an educated public. A specialized research journal reports original findings for a professional scientific audience. A textbook chapter introduces a field for students. A graduate-level monograph assumes extensive prior knowledge.

For most academic research projects, the appropriate level is peer-reviewed scholarship — journal articles, academic monographs, and published conference proceedings from the relevant field. Popular sources, textbooks, and encyclopedia entries may support background understanding but typically should not carry the evidentiary weight of peer-reviewed sources in academic arguments.

Understanding level means not only finding sources that are rigorous enough, but also not dismissing sources that are appropriate for their intended purpose. A newspaper article is not a peer-reviewed study — but it is a legitimate source for understanding how an event was reported publicly. An encyclopedia entry is not a specialized scholarly source — but it is an appropriate source for a clear, reliable definition. Different levels serve different purposes, and the skill is matching them correctly.

Section 26.8

The MGU Source Evaluation Model

The MGU Source Evaluation Model brings together all the criteria discussed in this chapter into a systematic framework that can be applied to any source in any research context. The model has seven criteria, each posing a specific evaluative question.

The MGU Source Evaluation Model

Relevance. Does this source contribute to answering my specific research question?

Authority. Does the author or publishing institution have genuine expertise in this subject area?

Evidence. Does this source support its claims with documented, verifiable evidence?

Accuracy. Are the facts, data, and claims verifiable and consistent with other reliable sources?

Purpose. Why was this source created, and who was it created for?

Currency. Is this source recent enough for this research topic?

Context. How does this source fit into the larger scholarly or informational conversation?

The model is most useful when applied deliberately and in sequence. Work through each criterion explicitly rather than applying them all at once in a vague general impression. The following example demonstrates the model in action.

Worked Example — Applying the Full Model
A 2021 nonprofit report on learning recovery after COVID-19
  • RelevanceDirectly relevant to a question about pandemic effects on student achievement — addresses learning recovery using national assessment data.
  • AuthorityRequires investigation. Is the "Center for Education Policy Research" an academic institution, think tank, or advocacy group? Check funding, affiliation, and authorship before trusting the label.
  • EvidenceThe title suggests large-scale data, but the student must check what assessment data specifically, how it is analyzed, and whether findings can be traced to specific figures.
  • AccuracyCompare key findings against other researchers analyzing the same or similar data; investigate any discrepancies.
  • PurposeA policy-advocacy mission, if present, means the report is persuasive as well as descriptive — evidence claims should be verified against sources without that agenda.
  • CurrencyA 2021 report on a fast-moving recovery period should be supplemented with more recent analyses.
  • ContextCompare findings to national education agencies and peer-reviewed studies; consistency strengthens credibility, divergence requires explanation.
Principle: Work through every criterion explicitly. Uncertainty at any step is a signal to investigate further, not a reason to abandon or blindly accept the source.

The Model as a Habit, Not a Checklist

The MGU Source Evaluation Model works best when it becomes a habit of mind rather than a checklist completed mechanically. Over time, experienced researchers internalize these criteria and apply them quickly and naturally whenever they encounter a source. That internalization is the goal.

Until those habits are internalized, use the model explicitly. Write down your evaluation for each source. Notice where you feel confident and where you feel uncertain. Uncertainty is a signal that more investigation is needed, not a reason to abandon the source or accept it without scrutiny.

Source Check

Practice Source Evaluation — Full MGU Model Application
Research Question: What is the current scientific consensus on the relationship between video game use and aggressive behavior in adolescents?
  • Source"The Video Game Myth: Why Screen Violence Does Not Cause Real-World Aggression," by Dr. Steven Hargrove, Tech & Society Review, Issue 42, 2019. The author holds a PhD in communications and is a senior fellow at the Digital Freedom Institute, a nonprofit advocating reduced technology regulation. The article cites a 22-source bibliography and argues the link between gaming and aggression has been overstated by biased researchers.
Work through the model below.
  1. Relevance. How directly does this source address the research question about scientific consensus? What does it offer and what does it not offer?
  2. Authority. Evaluate Dr. Hargrove's authority carefully. What strengthens it? What should make you investigate further? What does the Digital Freedom Institute's advocacy mission suggest about the context of this publication?
  3. Evidence. The article includes a 22-source bibliography. What questions would you ask about those sources before accepting that the evidence is solid? What would you do to verify the citations support the claims they are used for?
  4. Accuracy. This article argues against a consensus position held by many researchers. How would you evaluate whether its characterization of the broader research is accurate?
  5. Purpose. What is the evident purpose of this article? How does that purpose shape how the evidence is selected and presented?
  6. Currency. Published in 2019. On a topic like research consensus, how significant is a five-to-six-year gap? What might have changed?
  7. Context. If peer-reviewed meta-analyses show mixed or contested findings, how does that affect your use of this source? If the broader literature consistently shows a relationship this article denies, how does that discrepancy inform your evaluation?
  8. Overall judgment. Would you use this source in academic research? Under what conditions? What would you need to verify before doing so?

Misinformation Alert

Authority Laundering and Expert Mimicry

The fake credentials problem. Online platforms do not verify claimed credentials. A "Dr." in a profile does not confirm a real, relevant degree from an accredited institution. Verify before trusting.

The think tank problem. Names with "Institute," "Center," or "Policy Research" may signal legitimate research centers — or industry-funded advocacy groups producing publications designed to look independent. Investigate funding, mission, and review process.

The consensus-denial pattern. Amplifying a small number of credentialed dissenters and labeling settled science "unsettled" is a documented pattern in debates over climate change, vaccine safety, and other topics. Distinguish genuine scientific uncertainty from manufactured doubt funded by financial or ideological interests. Chapter 28 covers this pattern in depth.

The self-citation loop. If all citations in a report lead back to the same institute, author, or small affiliated network, the evidence base is narrower than it appears.

Apply the MGU Source Evaluation Model consistently. Do not let professional appearance, impressive organizational names, or confident tone substitute for rigorous evaluation of authority, evidence, accuracy, purpose, currency, and context.

Citation Check

Recording Source Evaluation Information for Citation and Research

When you evaluate a source and decide to use it, record not just the citation but the evaluation: full citation, source type, author's affiliation and credentials, publisher's standing, date and currency assessment, key evidence summary, evaluation notes on strengths and limitations, and your decision on how the source will be used.

Common Citation Errors When Recording Evaluated Sources

Recording only the citation without evaluation notes; recording an evaluation without the full citation; noting conclusions without noting limitations or date; failing to note the source type so source fit cannot be assessed later.

Recording the Evaluation
  • I have recorded the full citation information.
  • I have written evaluation notes including strengths, limitations, and intended use.
  • I have noted any verification steps I still need to take before relying on this source.

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 26.1Source evaluation is a form of judgment, not a mechanical procedure. It is context-dependent, cumulative across a set of sources, and a skill that improves through deliberate effort rather than instinct.
  • Sec 26.2Relevance means a source actually contributes to answering the research question. Sources range from directly relevant to background, tangential, or only apparently relevant.
  • Sec 26.3Credibility is produced by expertise, accountability, evidence, rigorous process, and transparency. It can be mimicked by professional appearance, impressive names, unverified credentials, and citations that do not hold up.
  • Sec 26.4Accuracy means information corresponds to reality. Even credible sources can be inaccurate. Verification through comparison and triangulation is essential, and honest error differs from deliberate deception.
  • Sec 26.5Authority is domain-specific. Scholarly, professional, institutional, experiential, and community authority each fit different research purposes. Scientific consensus carries far more authority than isolated contrarian claims.
  • Sec 26.6Evidence quality concerns how well evidence actually supports a source's claims. Meta-analyses and systematic reviews generally provide stronger evidence than single studies or anecdote.
  • Sec 26.7Source fit means matching source type and technical level to the specific research purpose. A credible source can still be the wrong source for a given task.
  • Sec 26.8The MGU Source Evaluation Model — Relevance, Authority, Evidence, Accuracy, Purpose, Currency, Context — brings these standards together into a disciplined, repeatable evaluation practice.

Review Questions

  1. Explain why source evaluation is described in this chapter as a form of judgment rather than a mechanical checklist procedure. What makes it more complex than a simple pass/fail test?
  2. Describe the difference between directly relevant, background relevant, tangentially relevant, and apparently but not actually relevant sources. Give an example of each type for a research question of your choice.
  3. What are the four main factors that produce genuine credibility in a source? How does each factor contribute to making a source trustworthy?
  4. Explain what is meant by "mimicking credibility." Give two specific examples of how a source can appear credible without actually being credible, and describe what a researcher could do to identify the deception.
  5. Why is accuracy evaluated separately from credibility? Can a credible source be inaccurate? Explain with reference to specific examples.
  6. Explain the difference between scholarly authority and professional authority. When is each type more appropriate for academic research?
  7. What does it mean to say that authority is domain-specific? Why does this matter when evaluating sources?
  8. Describe four types of evidence and their relative strengths. Why do meta-analyses and systematic reviews generally provide stronger evidence than individual studies?
  9. What is source fit, and why is it important? Give an example of a source that is credible and accurate but poorly fit for a particular research purpose.
  10. Apply the MGU Source Evaluation Model to a source of your choice. Work through all seven criteria and write a paragraph-length evaluation for each. Conclude with an overall judgment about whether and how you would use the source in research.
Looking Ahead
Chapter 27: Bias, Perspective, and Purpose

Chapter 26 established the core standards for evaluating sources: relevance, credibility, accuracy, and authority, brought together in the MGU Source Evaluation Model. Chapter 27 turns to a closely related question — bias. Students will learn that bias is not simply disagreement, that every source has a perspective and a purpose, and that sources can still be useful evidence once their perspective is understood and accounted for. The evaluation habits built in this chapter — checking authority, tracing evidence, and recording judgments — carry forward directly into the work of reading sources for bias and perspective.

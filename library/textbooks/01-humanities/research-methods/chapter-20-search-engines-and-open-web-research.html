Chapter 20 — Front Matter

Research Skill Focus and Overview

This chapter teaches students how to use open web search engines as responsible research tools. Students will learn how search engines rank results, how to use search operators to refine and target queries, how to evaluate the difference between organic and sponsored results, how to find open access scholarly materials on the open web, and how to recognize the risks that web searching introduces to the research process.

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to search the open web with deliberate strategy, apply operators to sharpen results, distinguish reliable from unreliable open web sources, and know when to use the open web alongside — not instead of — library catalogs and academic databases.

Why This Matters

The open web is where most students begin research, and where most students stay. Search engines are instant, familiar, and always accessible. They require no login, no institutional access, no learning curve. Type a phrase and results appear. The experience feels complete. But that feeling of completeness is an illusion. A general search engine indexes only a fraction of the information that exists. Large portions of the scholarly record — peer-reviewed articles, licensed databases, archival materials, government statistical tables, specialized digital collections — are either invisible to search engines or available only in fragments. What appears in a Google search is not a neutral survey of human knowledge. It is a curated slice of publicly accessible content, ranked by an algorithm that was built to serve commercial purposes alongside informational ones.

This does not make the open web useless for research. The open web gives researchers access to government publications, agency data, news archives, organizational reports, open access journals, preprints, institutional repositories, and primary source materials that can be genuinely valuable. Used strategically, with clear eyes about its limits, the open web is a legitimate part of a researcher's toolkit. The problem is not using the open web. The problem is using it uncritically — as if it were a library, as if search results were curated and vetted, as if the first page of results contained the best available evidence. It does not. This chapter teaches you to use the open web the way an experienced researcher does: deliberately, skeptically, in combination with library catalogs and databases, and with constant awareness of what you are and are not seeing.

Section 20.1

The Open Web as a Research Space

The internet is not a single thing. It is a collection of interconnected systems with very different characteristics, different content, and different research value. For research purposes, it helps to distinguish three layers of the web.

The surface web. The surface web consists of pages that are indexed by search engines — pages that a web crawler can find, read, and add to the search engine's index. When you search Google, Bing, or DuckDuckGo, you are searching the surface web. This layer includes news websites, organizational homepages, blogs, government agency pages, Wikipedia, social media profiles, open access journals, and millions of other publicly accessible pages. The surface web is large, but it represents a small fraction of total internet content.

The deep web. The deep web consists of pages that are not indexed by search engines. This includes everything behind a login wall: your email, your bank account, library database content, subscription journals, online course platforms, private organizational intranets, and institutional repositories that require authentication. The deep web is not sinister — it simply contains content that is not publicly accessible. Most of the scholarly literature you need for research is in the deep web, which is why search engines cannot retrieve it.

The dark web. The dark web is a small portion of the internet that requires specialized software to access and is designed for anonymous communication. It is largely irrelevant to academic research and is not addressed further in this chapter.

For researchers, the key distinction is between the surface web and the deep web. When you search Google, you are not searching everything. You are searching a small, publicly accessible portion of the total information environment. Library databases live in the deep web. Journal subscriptions live in the deep web. The MGU Library catalog connects you to materials that no general search engine can retrieve.

What the Open Web Does Well

Strength
Government publications, open access scholarship, and primary sources

Government publications. Federal, state, and local government agencies publish enormous quantities of freely accessible information: laws and regulations, agency reports, statistical data, policy documents, environmental assessments, health advisories, census tables, and more. Government sites ending in .gov are authoritative sources for official information.

Open access scholarship. A growing body of peer-reviewed research is freely available on the open web through open access journals, institutional repositories, and preprint servers. Google Scholar, for instance, surfaces many open access articles alongside links to institutional repositories where paywalled articles may be freely deposited.

News archives, organizational reports, reference materials, and primary sources. Major newspapers and broadcasting organizations maintain substantial freely accessible archives. Think tanks, nonprofits, and professional associations publish reports and data summaries — these require careful evaluation since they reflect organizational perspectives, but they can be useful. Wikipedia is not citable as a research source but is a useful starting point for background research and contains reference lists that link onward. Digitized historical documents, speeches, legislative records, and court opinions are often freely available through institutional repositories, government sites, and digital libraries.

What the Open Web Does Poorly

Limitation
Coverage, quality control, completeness, and persistence

Scholarly journal coverage. Most peer-reviewed research is behind paywalls. A Google search may find the abstract of an article but not the full text. Library databases provide access to the full text through institutional subscriptions.

Quality control. Anything can be published on the open web. There is no peer review requirement, no fact-checking mandate, no editorial standard. High-quality content and low-quality content exist side by side, and search ranking does not reliably distinguish them.

Completeness. Search engine results show what is indexed, popular, and commercially viable to show you. They do not show you the full landscape of what exists on any topic.

Persistence. Web content disappears, moves, and changes. A page that exists today may not exist tomorrow. Library databases maintain stable records with persistent identifiers.

Study Note — The Open Web in a Research Strategy

The open web should be one layer of a multilayered research strategy, not the whole strategy. Use the open web for background orientation, government documents, news sources, open access scholarship, and organizational publications. Use library catalogs for books, reference works, and physical collections. Use library databases for peer-reviewed articles, subject-specific research, and systematic source coverage.

The chapters immediately surrounding this one — Chapter 19 on academic databases and Chapter 21 on Boolean operators — provide the skills that complement open web research and give you access to what search engines cannot find.

Section 20.2

Search Engine Ranking

To use search engines responsibly, you need to understand how they decide what to show you and in what order. Search engines rank results using complex algorithms that evaluate hundreds of signals simultaneously. The details of these algorithms are proprietary and change constantly, but the broad principles are widely understood.

Ranking FactorHow It WorksResearch Implication
Link authority (PageRank)Pages gain importance when many high-quality pages link to them; a link functions as a vote of confidencePages from well-connected organizations rank higher — but link volume is not the same as accuracy
Content relevanceSearch engines compare page text and semantic meaning to your query termsA page can rank for terms it never uses if the engine judges the meaning to match
User behavior signalsEngines track clicks, "bounces," and time on page to infer usefulnessPopular and accessible pages rise — which does not always mean accurate or scholarly
RecencyRecently published or updated pages are favored for time-sensitive queriesFor stable topics, recency matters less; for news and rapidly changing topics, it matters a great deal
PersonalizationResults are shaped by your location, search history, language, and deviceTwo people searching the same terms may see different results; your view is not a neutral list

This personalization has significant research implications. If you have searched frequently for content with a particular perspective, your search results may skew toward that perspective. Being aware of personalization helps you seek out sources that diversify your view of a topic.

Sponsored Results

At the top and bottom of most search result pages are paid placements — advertisements from organizations that have paid to appear for specific search terms. These are marked "Sponsored" or "Ad," though the labels are sometimes subtle and easy to miss.

Sponsored results are not ranked by relevance to your research question. They are ranked by how much the advertiser paid. An organization with a financial interest in shaping your understanding of a topic — a pharmaceutical company, an advocacy group, an industry trade association — may appear at the top of a search results page because it paid for placement, not because its content is most credible. Always scroll past sponsored results before evaluating what appears organically.

Featured snippets and knowledge panels. Search engines frequently display information directly in the results page without requiring you to click any link. Featured snippets pull a passage from a web page and display it at the top of results. Knowledge panels summarize information about a person, place, or topic. "People also ask" sections show related questions with expandable answers. These features can be useful for quick orientation, but they carry a risk: the passage or summary displayed may be extracted from its context, truncated in ways that distort its meaning, or drawn from a source that is not the most reliable available. Do not treat a featured snippet as a definitive answer without checking the source it comes from.

What Ranking Does Not Mean

Search ranking does not mean the result is accurate, scholarly, representative of the range of expert opinion, the most recent or complete, or appropriate for academic research. Ranking reflects a combination of link authority, relevance signals, user behavior, recency, and paid placement. A search engine is optimized to show you content that users find engaging and that advertisers find valuable. These goals sometimes align with research quality, and sometimes they do not.

Section 20.3

Search Operators

Search operators are special characters and commands that modify how a search engine interprets your query. Using operators shifts you from passive searching — typing a few words and hoping for the best — to active searching, where you control what the engine looks for and where it looks. The examples below use Google's syntax, the most widely used.

Example 20.3a — Phrase Searching with Quotation Marks
Exact phrase versus scattered keywords
  • Without quotes racial disparities criminal justiceRetrieves pages that contain the words "racial," "disparities," "criminal," and "justice" anywhere on the page, in any order.
  • With quotes "racial disparities" "criminal justice"Retrieves pages containing the exact phrase "racial disparities" and the exact phrase "criminal justice."
Principle: Phrase searching dramatically reduces result volume while increasing relevance — especially for proper names, titles, and fixed technical terms.
Example 20.3b — Excluding Terms with the Minus Sign
Resolving ambiguous search terms
  • Query mercury -planetRetrieves pages about mercury (the element, the car brand, the Roman god) while excluding pages about the planet Mercury.
  • Query jaguar -car -automobileRetrieves pages about jaguars (the animal) while excluding automotive content.
Principle: Adding a minus sign immediately before a word (no space) excludes pages containing that word — useful when a term has multiple meanings.
OperatorFunctionExample
"phrase"Forces an exact word or phrase to appear"climate refugees" policy
site:Restricts results to a specific domain or domain typesite:cdc.gov vaccine safety
filetype:Restricts results to a specific file formatfiletype:pdf childhood poverty report
related:Finds sites similar to a known siterelated:pewresearch.org
intitle:Restricts results to pages with a term in the titleintitle:"food deserts" urban

The site: operator is one of the most useful research operators available. It lets you search a specific authoritative organization's website, or restrict results to a domain type associated with greater institutional accountability. PDF and spreadsheet searches using filetype: are particularly useful for finding full-length reports, statistical tables, government documents, and academic working papers that may not appear in standard HTML search results.

Example 20.3c — Combining Operators
Highly targeted compound queries
  • Query site:gov "lead contamination" drinking water filetype:pdfRetrieves PDF documents from government websites about lead contamination in drinking water.
  • Query "climate migration" site:edu OR site:gov -newsRetrieves pages about climate migration from educational or government websites, excluding pages categorized as news.
Principle: Combining operators takes practice, but reaching for operators rather than adding more keywords is one of the clearest markers of a strategic searcher.

Section 20.4

Site and Domain Searches

One of the most important skills in open web research is learning to evaluate and target domains. Not all websites are created equal, and domain type is a rough but useful first indicator of an organization's accountability, mission, and purpose.

DomainTypical SourceResearch Significance
.govU.S. federal, state, and local government agenciesAuthoritative for laws, regulations, statistics, public health guidance, and agency reports; evaluate mandate and document purpose, but institutional accountability is generally high
.eduU.S. colleges, universities, accredited institutionsMay host faculty research, repositories, library guides, or student work — quality varies widely on the same domain; evaluate the specific page
.orgNonprofits, advocacy groups, professional associations, and anyone elseRanges from highly credible professional societies to explicit advocacy organizations; the domain alone tells you very little — investigate the organization
.comCommercial entitiesConsider how commercial interests might shape the information presented
.netOriginally network organizations, now broadly availableLimited domain-specific research significance
.intInternational organizations (UN, WHO, etc.)Generally credible institutional sources, subject to the same evaluation criteria as other official sources

Country-code domains (.uk, .ca, .au, and so on) indicate content from organizations based in particular countries. They carry no automatic authority signal beyond national origin.

Study Note — Targeting Specific Sites

For many research questions, you already know which organizations are most authoritative. Targeting those organizations' websites directly is more efficient and more reliable than hoping they appear in your results.

Researching opioid prescribing rates? Search site:cdc.gov opioids rather than just "opioid statistics." This approach gives you the government agency's own documents and data, not third-party summaries that may be incomplete or filtered through an advocacy perspective.

Evaluating a website before using it. Even within known domains, evaluate specific pages and publications before using them as sources. Ask: Who is the author? Are they identified, with relevant credentials? What organization hosts this page, and what is its mission? What is the page's purpose — informational, persuasive, commercial? When was it last updated? Does it cite sources you can verify? Does it acknowledge complexity, counterevidence, or uncertainty, or present only one side?

A page that passes these questions is worth considering as a source. A page that fails them may still help you orient yourself to a topic, but it should not serve as a primary source of evidence in academic research.

Section 20.5

Snippets, Links, and Result Pages

A search result page is itself a document worth reading carefully. Most researchers glance at the first few results and click the most promising-looking link. Strategic researchers treat the results page as a source of information in its own right.

Reading result snippets. Each search result typically shows a title, a URL, and a snippet — a short excerpt that shows how the page relates to your search terms. Pay attention to the source domain, whether the snippet content actually addresses your question, the date, and whether the URL is a full address or a truncated breadcrumb trail.

Evaluating links without clicking. Hover over a link to see the full URL in your browser's status bar. A URL with long tracking-parameter strings, redirects through unfamiliar domains, or unusual file extensions is worth treating with caution.

Featured snippetsExtracted passage displayed at the top of resultsCheck the source before trusting the snippet

Organic resultsThe main ranked list of pagesWhat you are primarily evaluating

Related searchesSuggested terms at the bottom of the pageUseful for expanding or refining strategy
Going Beyond Page One

Research suggests that most people never look beyond the first page of search results. This is a significant limitation for researchers. Search engine ranking does not guarantee that the best sources for your research question appear on page one. Authoritative but low-traffic sources — a government agency's detailed technical report, a scholarly preprint, a comprehensive report from a small nonprofit — may rank below more popular but less scholarly content. Developing the habit of scrolling further, refining your query, and approaching results with selectivity rather than convenience is part of becoming a strategic searcher.

Section 20.6

Open Access Sources

Open access (OA) is a publishing model in which research is made freely available to anyone with internet access, without requiring a subscription or payment. Open access is growing rapidly and represents an increasingly important layer of the scholarly record that can be found through open web searching.

TypeDescription
Gold OAPublished in journals that are fully open access; freely available immediately upon publication, sometimes funded by author or institutional fees
Green OAAuthors deposit copies in institutional or subject repositories as a preprint, postprint, or accepted manuscript, even if the journal version is paywalled
Hybrid OAIndividual articles in a subscription journal made open access for a fee, while the journal remains subscription-based
Diamond OAFreely accessible to both authors and readers, funded by institutional support, grants, or volunteer effort

Where to Find Open Access Scholarship

Google Scholar
Indexes scholarly publications and links to freely accessible versions; click "All versions" to find open access copies.
PubMed Central
Free full-text archive of biomedical and life sciences literature, operated by the National Library of Medicine.
ERIC
Education Resources Information Center; many records link to freely available full text.
arXiv
Preprint repository for physics, mathematics, computer science, and economics; not necessarily peer reviewed.
SSRN
Preprint repository for social sciences, economics, and law.
Institutional Repositories
Repositories maintained by research universities where faculty deposit their work.
DOAJ
Directory of Open Access Journals — a searchable directory of legitimate OA journals across all disciplines.
Unpaywall
Browser extension that automatically finds legal open access versions of articles when you land on a paywalled page.
Evaluating Open Access Sources

The fact that an article is open access does not tell you about its quality. Many high-quality peer-reviewed journals are open access. Some open access venues — called predatory journals — charge publication fees without providing rigorous peer review, accepting almost anything submitted.

When evaluating an open access article, ask: Is the journal listed in DOAJ? Does it have a clearly stated peer review process? Is the editorial board composed of recognized scholars? Is the article well-written, with a clear methods section and properly cited references? Does it appear in a known subject database like PubMed or ERIC?

A preprint — a version of an article deposited before peer review — requires additional caution. Preprints represent work in progress. Their claims have not yet been vetted by peer reviewers and may change before final publication. This does not make them useless — preprints are sometimes cited when speed of access matters — but they should be identified as preprints in your notes and citations, and their claims should be verified against other sources where possible.

Section 20.7

Risks of Open Web Research

Using the open web for research introduces specific risks that do not arise — or arise less acutely — when using library catalogs and academic databases. Being aware of these risks does not mean avoiding the open web. It means entering it with the appropriate level of skepticism.

Risk 1
Misinformation and Disinformation

The open web is the primary environment in which misinformation spreads. False claims, misleading statistics, manipulated images, fabricated quotes, and distorted context are widely distributed through websites, social media platforms, news aggregators, and even search result pages. Chapter 28 of this textbook addresses misinformation in depth. For now: never accept a claim found on a web page at face value. Trace it to its original source. Check it against other reliable sources. Apply the MGU Misinformation Check.

Risk 2
Search Engine Optimization (SEO) Manipulation

SEO is the practice of modifying a website's content, structure, and external links to improve its search ranking. Many legitimate organizations use SEO to make valuable content more findable. But SEO can also push low-quality, misleading, or commercially motivated content up the rankings. Content that ranks highly because it was optimized for search is not necessarily content that ranks highly because it is accurate or credible.

Risk 3
The Illusion of Comprehensiveness

When a search returns hundreds of thousands of results, it feels as if you have surveyed the full landscape of a topic. This feeling is false. Search results are a filtered, ranked, personalized subset of publicly indexed content. Do not confuse access to many results with access to complete or representative coverage. Comprehensive coverage requires searching library databases, not just the open web.

Risk 4
Link Rot and Source Impermanence

Web content disappears. Organizations redesign websites and break old links. A source you cited in October may produce a 404 error by March. When you find a valuable web source, capture it: save a PDF, archive it using the Internet Archive's Wayback Machine, or record the access date alongside the URL in your citation.

Risk 5
Filter Bubbles and Personalization

Personalized search results mean your search experience is shaped by your prior behavior, location, and preferences — sometimes called a filter bubble, a search environment that systematically favors content consistent with your existing views. Counteract this by searching from different angles, seeking sources that challenge your assumptions, and using incognito browsing or alternative search engines.

Risk 6
Authority Confusion

The open web makes it easy to confuse institutional authority with the appearance of authority. A professional-looking website and confident language can create an impression of credibility without any underlying accountability. The appearance of professionalism is not evidence of credibility. Investigate the organization, look for named and credentialed authors, and check whether other credible sources corroborate what you have found.

Section 20.8

Responsible Web Searching

Responsible web searching means approaching the open web with strategy, skepticism, and appropriate verification habits. It means using the web as one tool in a research workflow, not the only tool.

Developing a Verification Habit — Six Questions

1. Who produced this? Is the author or organization identified and verifiable?
2. What is the source's purpose — to inform, persuade, sell, mobilize, or entertain?
3. What evidence is offered? Do citations lead to credible sources?
4. What is missing? Does the page acknowledge counterevidence or uncertainty?
5. Can another reliable source confirm the main claims (triangulation)?
6. When was this published or updated, and is currency appropriate for your topic?

This is not a checklist you run through slowly every time. With practice, these questions become instinctive — a background process that runs while you read.

Triangulating information. No single source, however credible, is sufficient for most research claims. Triangulation means checking a claim against multiple independent sources. If a government agency, a major research university, and a professional association all report the same statistic, your confidence increases. If you find a claim in only one source and cannot verify it elsewhere, treat it as unconfirmed and note that uncertainty in your research log.

Using the open web early and databases later. The open web is often most useful at the beginning of a research process, when you are building background knowledge and orienting yourself. Wikipedia, news articles, organizational overviews, and government summaries can help you understand a field and identify key terms. Once oriented, move to library databases for the scholarly sources that form the core of academic research. Use the open web to supplement, not replace, database searching.

Right Tool: The Open WebRight Tool: Library Databases
Finding a government report → search the agency's .gov site directlyFinding comprehensive peer-reviewed literature on a question
Finding a news article from a specific publication's archiveFinding a specific article in a subscription journal
Finding an open access version of an article (Google Scholar, Unpaywall)Finding historical primary sources in institutional archives
Finding a nonprofit's published research reportFinding comprehensive statistical data from licensed sources
Finding a primary source document in a digital archiveSystematic subject-database coverage of a research question

Knowing which tool to use for which purpose is the mark of a research-literate person.

Documenting Your Web Research

Keep a search log for all open web searching, just as you would for library catalog and database searching. Record what you searched, on which search engine, what terms you used, and what you found that was most useful. Note the full URL, the publication or access date, and your initial evaluation of the source. See the Research Notebook model below for a complete example.

Activity 20AOperator Search Comparison

This activity demonstrates how search operators change what results you see. Choose one of the following topics or select your own: childhood lead exposure; renewable energy employment; prison literacy programs; food labeling regulations; mental health parity laws.

Practice Source Evaluation: An Organizational Website Report

The following is a description of a hypothetical source found through a web search. Read it carefully and apply the MGU Source Evaluation Model.

TitleThe Hidden Costs of Single-Use Plastics: A Comprehensive Analysis of Economic and Environmental Impacts
OrganizationClean Future Coalition
URLcleanfuturecoalition.org/reports/hidden-costs-plastics-2022
DateMarch 2022
DescriptionA 47-page PDF report examining the economic costs of plastic pollution, including cleanup costs, healthcare impacts, and effects on fisheries and tourism. Includes statistical tables, maps, and a 12-page bibliography. The executive summary calls for a federal ban on single-use plastic packaging. The Clean Future Coalition describes itself on its "About" page as "a coalition of environmental advocacy organizations and businesses committed to a plastic-free future." The report cites statistics from the EPA, NOAA, peer-reviewed articles in environmental science journals, and a previous report from the same organization.

Evaluation Using the MGU Source Evaluation Model

Relevance

If your research question is "What are the economic consequences of plastic pollution in coastal communities?", is this report relevant? Which specific aspects are most relevant? Are there aspects of the question this report might not address?

Authority

Who produced this report? The Clean Future Coalition describes itself as an advocacy organization. What does this tell you about the perspective from which it was written? Are there named authors? How would you investigate the organization's funding and membership?

Evidence

The report cites government agencies, peer-reviewed journals, and its own previous report. How would you assess the strength of this evidence base? What would you do to verify that the EPA and NOAA statistics are accurately reported? What concern does citing the organization's own prior report raise?

Accuracy

You cannot fully assess accuracy without reading the report, but what steps would you take to spot-check its main claims? Identify two specific claims you would want to verify and explain how.

Purpose

The report's executive summary calls for a federal ban on single-use plastics. How does this explicit advocacy purpose affect how you should use the report in research? Is an advocacy purpose automatically disqualifying?

Currency

The report was published in March 2022. Is this sufficiently current for research on plastic pollution policy? What aspects of this topic might have changed since 2022?

Context

This report was produced by an advocacy coalition, not a government agency, academic institution, or independent research organization. How does this institutional context affect how you would describe and cite this source in a research paper?

Discussion

After completing your evaluation, consider: In what research contexts might this report be a useful source? In what contexts would using it uncritically be a research error? How would you represent this source accurately in a paper that also cites government data and peer-reviewed research?

Chapter 20 — Recurring Feature

Misinformation Alert

Health and Science Misinformation in Search Results

Search results for health, science, and policy topics are disproportionately populated by misinformation. This is one of the highest-risk areas of open web research. Several well-documented patterns produce this risk.

Pattern: Commercially Motivated Health Content

Companies that sell supplements, alternative treatments, or dietary products invest heavily in SEO to rank highly for health-related searches. A search for "vitamin D benefits" or "cancer prevention diet" may surface content from companies selling products, not from public health agencies or research institutions.

Pattern: Emotionally Compelling Misinformation

Content that provokes fear, anger, or hope spreads more widely and may rank more highly because it generates more clicks, shares, and engagement than a nuanced public health analysis would.

Pattern: Outdated Content

Medical and scientific understanding evolves. A page published in 2015 about treatment guidelines may still rank highly even if those guidelines changed in 2020. Search engines do not automatically remove or downrank outdated content.

Pattern: Authority-Mimicking Sites

Some sites are designed to look like government agencies, academic institutions, or respected health organizations while actually promoting commercial products or ideological agendas, using domain names designed to create false impressions of authority.

Apply the MGU Misinformation Check to All Health and Science Web Content

Who made it — and is the organization independently verifiable? What is the claim — specific, falsifiable, clearly stated? What evidence is offered — can you find the cited studies in PubMed or another medical database? What is missing — does it acknowledge counterevidence or uncertainty? Can another reliable source confirm it (CDC, NIH, WHO, a peer-reviewed database)? Is the emotional reaction stronger than the evidence? Has the source been altered, misrepresented, or taken out of context — find the original study, not a summary of a summary.

For any health or science claim you plan to use in research, trace it to a primary source. Do not cite a news article that summarizes a study — find the study. Do not cite an organizational health page without checking whether the guidelines it describes reflect current professional consensus. Do not assume a search result is reliable because it is ranked highly.

Citation Check

How to Cite Open Web Sources

Websites and web-based documents require specific citation information that differs from books and journal articles. The biggest challenges are identifying the author or responsible organization, finding a clear publication or update date, and capturing a stable URL.

Information to Record for Any Web Source

1. Author (individual or organizational)  ·  2. Title of the page or document  ·  3. Name of the website or organization if different from the page title  ·  4. Publication date or last updated date (if available)  ·  5. Full URL, including https://  ·  6. The date you accessed the page (especially if no publication date is listed)

Common Citation Errors for Web Sources

No author recorded — when no individual author is listed, use the organization name as the author; never leave the field blank. No date recorded — note "n.d." and record your access date in your research notes. Incomplete URL — copy the full URL from the address bar, not a shortened or redirected version. Citing a summary instead of the original — locate and cite the original report or study. No access date for undated content — always record when you accessed undated content, since it could change or disappear.

Citing PDF documents found on the web. For PDF documents found through a web search — government reports, organizational white papers, agency statistical tables — use the same citation format as for a web page, but note the file type if required by your style guide. Record the URL where the PDF is located, even if the URL is long.

Research Checklist, Summary, and Review

Research Checklist

Chapter 20 — Open Web Research Checklist
Use this checklist each time you conduct open web research for academic purposes.
Before You Search
  • I have a clear research question and a list of search terms.
  • I have already consulted the MGU Library catalog and relevant databases for scholarly sources.
  • I understand what types of sources I am looking for on the open web (government documents, news, open access articles, organizational reports).
During Searching
  • I used search operators (quotation marks, site:, filetype:, minus sign) to target my results.
  • I targeted authoritative domains (.gov, .edu, established institutions) for factual and policy information.
  • I scrolled past sponsored results and did not click on paid placements.
  • I read result snippets and evaluated domain names before clicking.
  • I used Google Scholar to search for open access scholarly materials.
  • I did not stop at the first page of results.
Evaluating Sources Found
  • For each source I am considering, I identified the author or responsible organization.
  • I investigated the organization behind each website.
  • I checked publication or update dates for all sources.
  • I traced statistical claims to their original data sources.
  • I checked main claims against at least one additional reliable source (triangulation).
  • I applied the MGU Misinformation Check to any source about health, science, or contested policy claims.
Recording Sources
  • I recorded full citation information for every source I may use: author, title, organization, date, URL, access date.
  • I saved PDF versions of key documents or archived key pages using the Wayback Machine.
  • I updated my search log with queries, results, and evaluation notes.
  • I noted which sources are from advocacy organizations and how I will represent them as such.
Moving Forward
  • I have identified which open web sources are strong enough to use as evidence in research.
  • I have identified gaps in open web coverage that require database searching.
  • I have added the open web sources that passed evaluation to my citation manager.
  • I noted what additional research I need to do in library databases to complete this topic.

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 20.1The open web is a valuable but limited research space. Search engines index only the publicly accessible surface web, a fraction of the total information environment. The deep web — most peer-reviewed scholarship and licensed database content — is invisible to general search engines and accessible only through library subscriptions.
  • Sec 20.2Search engines rank results based on link authority, content relevance, user behavior signals, recency, and personalization — not on scholarly quality. Sponsored results are paid placements, not relevance-ranked results. Featured snippets and knowledge panels require source verification before use.
  • Sec 20.3Search operators transform basic keyword searches into targeted, precise queries. Phrase searching, domain restriction with site:, file-type restriction with filetype:, and term exclusion with the minus sign are among the most powerful operators. Combining operators produces highly targeted results.
  • Sec 20.4Domain type — .gov, .edu, .org, .com — provides rough but useful initial signals about an organization's accountability and purpose. All domains require individual page evaluation, not just domain-level assumptions.
  • Sec 20.5A search results page contains sponsored results, featured snippets, knowledge panels, organic results, and related searches. Strategic researchers read snippets carefully and look beyond page one, since ranking does not guarantee the best sources appear first.
  • Sec 20.6Open access scholarship — gold, green, hybrid, and diamond OA — represents a growing body of citable scholarly work findable through tools including Google Scholar, PubMed Central, arXiv, SSRN, and Unpaywall. Preprints require additional caution since they have not been peer reviewed.
  • Sec 20.7The open web introduces specific risks: misinformation, SEO manipulation, the illusion of comprehensive coverage, link rot, filter bubbles, and authority confusion. Awareness of these risks supports appropriate skepticism rather than avoidance.
  • Sec 20.8Responsible web searching means verifying claims through triangulation, tracing statistics to original sources, applying the MGU Misinformation Check to contested claims, and using the open web as one layer of a multilayered research strategy alongside library databases.
Central Principle

Do not merely find information. Earn trust in information. The open web gives you access to enormous quantities of content. The work of research is determining which content is credible, which is relevant, and which can bear the weight of the claims you build on it.

Review Questions

  1. What is the difference between the surface web and the deep web? Why does this distinction matter for academic research?
  2. Explain three factors that search engines use to rank results. For each factor, describe how it might cause a high-quality scholarly source to rank lower than a less reliable source.
  3. What are sponsored search results, and why should researchers treat them differently from organic results when searching for information on a research topic?
  4. Demonstrate how the site: operator and the filetype: operator can be used together to locate a government report on a topic of your choice. Write out the exact search string you would use.
  5. What is open access scholarship, and how does it differ from scholarship behind a paywall? Name three resources or tools that help researchers find open access versions of academic articles.
  6. What is a preprint? What specific cautions should researchers exercise when using a preprint as a source?
  7. Describe the risk of "authority confusion" on the open web. Give an example of how a website might appear credible without actually being accountable or accurate.
  8. A classmate says: "I Googled my research topic and got over 400,000 results. I have everything I need." Explain why this conclusion is mistaken and what your classmate should do instead to build a reliable research base.

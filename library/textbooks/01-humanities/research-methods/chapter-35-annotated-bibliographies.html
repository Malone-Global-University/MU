Chapter 35 — Front Matter

Research Skill Focus and Overview

This chapter teaches students how to create annotated bibliographies — structured documents that combine citation entries with written commentary about each source. Students will learn how to write the three components of a strong annotation: a summary of the source's content, an evaluation of its credibility and quality, and an assessment of its usefulness for a specific research project.

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to construct a complete annotated bibliography, compare annotations across sources, use annotated bibliographies as planning tools for research papers, and revise annotations as their research develops.

Why This Matters

Most students think of a bibliography as something written at the end — a list of sources tacked onto the final page of a completed paper. An annotated bibliography inverts that sequence entirely. It is something you build during research, not after it. It is a thinking tool, not a formatting chore. Writing an annotation forces you to answer three questions that passive reading allows you to avoid: What does this source actually say? Is this source credible, rigorous, and reliable? And how, specifically, does this source help me answer my research question? Most students who struggle to write research papers struggle because they do not have clear answers to these three questions by the time they sit down to write. An annotated bibliography makes you do that work before you write — so that when you do write, you write with more clarity, more confidence, and more control over your evidence. Beyond coursework, this habit transfers to law, policy research, science, and journalism, wherever reading widely must turn into acting responsibly on what was read.

Section 35.1

What an Annotated Bibliography Is

An annotated bibliography is a list of sources — books, articles, websites, reports, primary documents, or other materials — in which each citation entry is followed by a paragraph or short block of text that describes, evaluates, and assesses the source's usefulness for a research project.

The word "annotated" comes from the Latin annotare, meaning to mark or to note. An annotation is a note. In an annotated bibliography, the note for each source does three kinds of work: descriptive, evaluative, and reflective. Understanding these three functions separately helps you write annotations that are genuinely useful rather than superficial.

FunctionWhat It Does
DescriptiveTells the reader what the source says — its main argument, scope, structure, methodology, or form
EvaluativeAssesses the source's quality — authority, rigor, currency, reliability, and significant limitations or biases
ReflectiveConnects the source to a specific research project — how it answers the question, what evidence it offers, what its limits mean for the work
What an Annotated Bibliography Is Not

It is not a simple list of sources with brief descriptions tacked on. Annotations that merely paraphrase a title or repeat an abstract without evaluation are incomplete ones. It is not a substitute for a research paper — it does not make an argument or synthesize sources into a coherent position; it prepares you to do those things. And it is not necessarily a final document: in many research processes, annotations are working notes, written quickly and revised as you learn more.

Types of Annotated Bibliographies

Descriptive (or informative) annotated bibliographies focus on summarizing source content without significant evaluation — appropriate for giving readers an overview of available literature without judging quality.

Evaluative (or critical) annotated bibliographies include both summary and critical assessment, and are the most common type in academic coursework because they demonstrate critical reading, not just source-finding.

Combination annotated bibliographies include summary, evaluation, and a statement of usefulness for a specific project — the most complete form, and the focus of this chapter.

Length and Format

Most annotations in undergraduate academic work run between 100 and 200 words per source; some disciplinary contexts call for 250 to 400 words, and some assignments specify 50 to 75 words. Always follow your instructor's directions. When none are given, aim for annotations long enough to address description, evaluation, and usefulness without padding. A tightly written 120-word annotation that covers all three functions is worth more than a rambling 250-word annotation that says little.

Section 35.2

Citation Entry

Every annotation begins with a complete, correctly formatted citation entry. The citation comes first, before the annotation text. This is not optional. A source without a citation is not a source — it is an unverifiable claim.

Placing the citation before the annotation establishes the source's identity before anything is said about it, makes the bibliography alphabetizable, and gives the reader verification information in one place. It also builds the professional habit of identifying sources precisely before discussing them. Incorrect or incomplete citations suggest a loose relationship with the source; complete and correct citations signal careful, traceable engagement.

What a Complete Citation Includes

Regardless of style, a complete citation generally includes the author's full name, the full title (including subtitle), and — depending on source type — journal title, volume, issue, year, and page range for articles; publisher, place of publication, and year for books; URL or DOI and access date for websites; and chapter author, chapter title, book title, editor, publisher, year, and page range for chapters in edited collections.

Citation Entries Across Styles
The same source, formatted three ways
  • MLAWineburg, Sam. Why Learn History (When It's Already on Your Phone). University of Chicago Press, 2018.
  • APAWineburg, S. (2018). Why learn history (when it's already on your phone). University of Chicago Press.
  • ChicagoWineburg, Sam. Why Learn History (When It's Already on Your Phone). Chicago: University of Chicago Press, 2018.
Principle: the citation style changes punctuation, capitalization, and author-name order — but every style still requires complete information.
Citation Entries for Other Source Types
Articles, chapters, and websites
  • MLA ArticleBråten, Ivar, et al. "Multiple Perspectives on Reading Motivation." Reading Research Quarterly, vol. 55, no. 3, 2020, pp. 451–465.
  • APA ArticleBråten, I., Brante, E. W., & Strømsø, H. I. (2020). Multiple perspectives on reading motivation. Reading Research Quarterly, 55(3), 451–465.
  • MLA ChapterGraff, Gerald. "Hidden Intellectualism." They Say / I Say: The Moves That Matter in Academic Writing, edited by Gerald Graff and Cathy Birkenstein, 4th ed., W. W. Norton, 2018, pp. 380–389.
  • MLA WebsiteCenters for Disease Control and Prevention. "Understanding Foodborne Illness." CDC, 14 Mar. 2023.

Common Citation Errors in Annotated Bibliographies

Because annotated bibliographies require many citations, errors tend to accumulate. Watch for: incomplete titles that drop the subtitle; incorrect author-name format (MLA lists the first author last-name-first and later authors first-name-first; APA lists every author last-name-first with initials; Chicago follows MLA's first-author convention); missing publication information such as volume, issue, page range, publisher, or year; inaccurate dates pulled from a cover rather than the title-page verso; and inconsistent formatting that mixes conventions from more than one style within a single bibliography.

Before You Submit

Review every citation entry against a reliable style guide — not a citation generator alone, since generators make systematic errors. Treat the citation check as a final proofreading step, not an afterthought.

Section 35.3

Source Summary

The first component of the annotation text is a summary — what the source says, in your own words. Writing a good summary is harder than it looks. Most students either summarize too broadly, telling the reader almost nothing specific, or too narrowly, getting lost in one detail and losing the overall picture. A good summary holds the general and the specific together: it identifies the source's central claim and gives enough detail to make that claim meaningful.

What a Source Summary Should Include

The central argument or thesis — what position does the source take, what does it demonstrate, report, or document? The scope of the work — what time period, geography, population, or aspect of the topic does it cover? The method or approach — quantitative data, qualitative interviews, historical archives, literary analysis, comparative case studies, or philosophical argument? Key findings or conclusions — what does the source actually show?

What a Source Summary Should Not Include

It should not be a sentence-by-sentence account of the table of contents. It should not reproduce the source's abstract word-for-word — copying an abstract is plagiarism and signals that you have not engaged with the source. It should not include your evaluation of the source; summary and evaluation are separate functions. And it should not be a bare list of topics — "this book covers the history, economics, and politics of food systems" is an incomplete table of contents, not a summary.

Avoiding Patchwriting

The most common error in annotation writing — and in research writing generally — is patchwriting: slightly rearranging the source's original sentences rather than genuinely restating the content in your own language. Genuine summary requires that you understand the source well enough to restate its argument without looking at the original text as you write. If you cannot do this, you have not understood the source yet, and that is important information.

Practice method. After reading a source, close it. Write one or two sentences capturing the main argument without looking at the original. Then reopen the source and check: did you get the central point right? Did you leave out anything essential? Adjust as needed. The discipline of closing the source before writing is the single most effective way to develop genuine summarizing skill.

Example 35.3 — Summaries at Different Levels of Quality
The same book, summarized three ways
  • Too vague"This book discusses education and schools in America. It covers many topics related to testing and choice and argues that some changes have been harmful."Tells the reader almost nothing specific about the argument or evidence.
  • Incomplete"The author argues that standardized testing and school choice policies have damaged American public education. She examines how No Child Left Behind and similar reforms changed school culture."Better — the central argument appears — but scope and evidence are still vague.
  • Strong"The author argues that the school reform movement's embrace of standardized testing and market-based school choice has, contrary to its stated goals, undermined the quality and equity of American public education. Drawing on her experience as a former advocate for these reforms and on extensive research into their effects, she demonstrates that test-based accountability systems have narrowed curricula, demoralized teachers, and failed to produce the academic gains reformers promised, while school choice policies have drained resources from public schools without consistently improving outcomes."Identifies the central argument, the author's position, the method, and specific content.
Principle: a reader who has not seen the source should come away with a clear, accurate picture of what it argues and how.

In a standard academic annotation of 150 to 200 words, the summary typically occupies about half — roughly 75 to 100 words. In a shorter annotation, the summary may need to compress to two or three sentences while still capturing the essential argument. Every word in an annotation should earn its place.

Section 35.4

Source Evaluation

The second component of the annotation evaluates the source's quality, credibility, and reliability. This is where annotation writing becomes genuinely analytical — moving from describing what the source says to judging how well it says it, how trustworthy it is, and what its limitations are. A good evaluation does not simply declare a source "great" or "reliable"; it shows the reasoning behind that judgment.

The MGU Source Evaluation Model Applied to Annotations

DimensionQuestion to Ask
RelevanceDoes this source actually address my research question?
AuthorityWho created this source, what are their credentials, and what is the publication venue?
EvidenceWhat evidence supports the claims, and is the methodology sound?
AccuracyCan the information be verified, and is it consistent with other reliable sources?
PurposeWhy was this source created — to inform, persuade, advocate, sell, or something else?
CurrencyWhen was it published or updated, and is that appropriate for the question?
ContextUnder what circumstances was the source produced — scholarly conversation, controversy, institutional interest?

You do not need to address every dimension in every annotation. Select the dimensions most relevant to your research question and to the particular strengths and weaknesses of the source at hand.

Acknowledge Limitations Honestly

A common mistake is writing evaluations that are uniformly positive. Strong annotations include honest assessments of limitations — almost every source has them. Watch for: dated sources that may not reflect recent developments; a focus on one region that limits generalization; advocacy that may affect evidence selection; small sample sizes; an author's stated professional, financial, or ideological interest; or coverage of only one dimension of a broader topic. Noting limitations is not a reason to discard a source — it is a reason to use it carefully.

Example 35.4 — Weak vs. Stronger Evaluation
Specific evidence makes the difference
  • Weak"This is a good source. The author is a professor and the book is published by a major press. It is well-written and the argument is clear."Confirms the source looks credible but says nothing about evidence quality or limitations.
  • Stronger"Wineburg is a professor of education and history at Stanford University and a widely cited researcher in historical thinking and civic literacy. The book is published by the University of Chicago Press, which applies peer review and editorial standards appropriate for an academic audience. The research draws on Wineburg's own empirical studies of how students, historians, and fact-checkers read digital information, giving the argument an empirical foundation. A limitation worth noting is that the evidence base focuses primarily on American secondary school and undergraduate students, which may limit how directly its conclusions apply elsewhere."Identifies specific credentials, the significance of the venue, the type of evidence, and a specific limitation.
Principle: evaluation requires specific evidence, not vague praise — and an honest limitation makes the evaluation more credible, not less.

Section 35.5

Source Usefulness

The third component of the annotation explains how this source serves your specific research project. This is the most personally situated part of the annotation: it connects the source's content and quality to your research question, argument, or purpose.

Usefulness is not the same as quality. A source can be excellent and still not be useful for your particular project. A source can have significant limitations and still contribute something important to your specific argument. Distinguishing overall quality from specific usefulness is a sign of sophisticated research thinking.

What a Usefulness Statement Should Address

How the source contributes to your argument — evidence, background context, a counterargument, data, or a theoretical framework. How the source relates to other sources — agreement, contradiction, complication, or extension. What the source cannot do for your project — unanswered questions, unaddressed aspects. Whether the source is essential or supplementary — central evidence, corroborating support, or background context.

Example 35.5 — Weak vs. Stronger Usefulness Statement
Specificity is what makes a usefulness statement useful
  • Weak"This source will be useful for my paper on education reform."Tells the reader nothing about how or where.
  • Stronger"This source is central to my argument about the gap between the stated goals of education reform and its documented outcomes. Wineburg's empirical research on how students actually process online information directly challenges the optimistic claims I examine in my first section. I will use his findings to support my contention that media literacy programs need to be restructured around how students actually behave. The book does not address curriculum design directly, so I will supplement it with instructional design sources for my third section."Names the specific argument, the role the source plays, and what it does not contribute.
A Complete Annotation (MLA Style)

Wineburg, Sam. Why Learn History (When It's Already on Your Phone). University of Chicago Press, 2018.

In this book, Wineburg argues that the widespread assumption that today's students are naturally skilled at evaluating digital information is empirically false — and that this assumption has led educators and policymakers to underestimate how much structured instruction in information literacy is needed. Drawing on his own empirical research comparing how historians, fact-checkers, and students approach online sources, he demonstrates that trained professionals use a distinct set of reading strategies — especially lateral reading — that students rarely employ without explicit instruction. Wineburg is a professor of education and history at Stanford University, and the book is published by the University of Chicago Press, lending the argument scholarly authority. The primary evidence base is drawn from American educational contexts, which is worth keeping in mind when generalizing his conclusions. For this research project, Wineburg's findings are central to my argument that information literacy instruction must be built around evidence-based practice rather than assumptions. His discussion of lateral reading directly informs my third section, and his critique of the "digital native" assumption anchors my introduction.

This annotation runs approximately 175 words — enough to address description, evaluation, and usefulness without padding.

Section 35.6

Comparing Annotations

One of the most valuable uses of a completed annotated bibliography is comparison. Reading your annotations against each other reveals the scholarly conversation your sources represent: where they agree, where they disagree, what they collectively establish, and what questions they leave open. This comparison work is the foundation of synthesis — the skill covered in Chapter 38. Annotations that have been carefully written are comparisons waiting to happen.

What to Look for When Comparing

Common themes and arguments — convergence across credible sources strengthens your argument. Contradictions and disagreements — understanding why sources disagree, not just that they disagree, prepares you to navigate the debate responsibly. Methodological differences — a quantitative study and a qualitative study may reach different conclusions because they measure different things. Gaps in the conversation — aspects of the topic that appear in none of your sources are research opportunities. Date patterns — are you missing recent developments or older foundational arguments? Scope patterns — do your sources cluster around one region, population, or time period, limiting generalizability?

A Practical Comparison Strategy

After writing all annotations, build a simple comparison table with your research question at the top and sources listed down the left column. Across the top, add columns for main argument, method, key evidence, limitations, and relationship to your research question. Fill in the table using your annotation text — patterns will become visible quickly.

Sample Comparison Table

Research question: How effective are school-based food programs at improving student academic outcomes?

Section 35.7

Annotated Bibliographies as Research Planning

An annotated bibliography is not merely a finished product handed in at the end. Used actively — during the research process, not after — it performs several planning functions simultaneously.

Auditing Your Source Coverage

With all sources annotated in one document, you can quickly answer: Do I have enough sources? Do they address all the main aspects of my argument? Am I relying too heavily on one or two sources? Are they all from one disciplinary perspective? Do I have primary sources, or only secondary ones? Do they span an appropriate date range? Are there significant counterarguments I have not found sources for?

Mapping Your Argument Before Writing

Many experienced writers outline their papers directly from their annotated bibliographies: group annotations by the argument they support (Chapter 36); identify the group most central to the argument as the likely first major section; identify a group addressing counterarguments; place background sources near the beginning; and place sources pointing toward conclusions near the end. This turns a collection of sources into a draft structure.

Tracking What You Have Not Found

Note, in your bibliography or a separate research log, what you searched for and did not find. This negative space tells you where the gaps are. Gaps are not failures — they are findings, and acknowledging them demonstrates intellectual honesty and a sophisticated understanding of the literature.

Using the Bibliography During Writing

A well-maintained annotated bibliography becomes a portable memory of your research. When you need a particular type of evidence, search your annotations by keyword rather than rereading whole sources. When you need to remember an argument, read your summary. When you need to verify credibility before citing, read your evaluation.

Section 35.8

Revising Annotations

An annotated bibliography is a living document. Most students write annotations once and consider them finished — a missed opportunity. Revision transforms adequate annotations into genuinely useful ones.

When to Revise

When your research question changes — a source that seemed tangential under a broad question may become essential once the question is focused, or vice versa. When you find a contradicting source — go back and add a note about the contradiction so comparison is easier later. When you understand the source better — after reading more deeply, revise the summary to reflect that understanding. When you discover an error — fix citation errors immediately, before they compound into your works cited page. Before submission — read every annotation one final time.

Annotation Revision Checklist
Use when revising each individual annotation
  • Citation is complete, including all required elements
  • Citation is correctly formatted in the required style
  • Summary identifies the central argument, not just the topic
  • Summary is written in my own words, not copied from the abstract
  • Summary includes scope, method, and key findings
  • Evaluation addresses specific credibility indicators
  • Evaluation honestly notes at least one limitation
  • Usefulness statement connects the source to my current research question
  • Usefulness statement notes how this source relates to at least one other source
  • Annotation is written in my own voice, not in the author's language

Activity 35AFive Sources, One Question

Choose a research topic, or use one of the following: the long-term effects of social media use on adolescent mental health; environmental justice and low-income communities; the relationship between minimum wage increases and employment; Indigenous language revitalization efforts in North America; prison education programs and recidivism.

Conduct research using the MGU Library catalog, at least one academic database, and one additional source type (a government report, a reputable news source, or a primary source document). Find and evaluate five sources on your topic.

  1. Write a complete, correctly formatted citation entry for each source, in the style required by your instructor (or MLA if unspecified).
  2. Write a complete annotation for each source: a summary of the main argument, scope, method, and findings (approximately 75–100 words); an evaluation of authority, evidence quality, and limitations (approximately 50–75 words); and a usefulness statement for your research question (approximately 50–75 words).

Research Notebook

An Annotated Bibliography in Progress

The model below shows how a working annotated bibliography looks mid-process — annotations at different stages of completion, revision notes, and comparison observations recorded alongside finished entries.

Research question: How do zero-tolerance discipline policies affect educational outcomes for students of color in American public schools? Citation style: APA

Entry 1 — Complete

Advancement Project. (2010). Test, Punish, and Push Out: How "Zero Tolerance" and High-Stakes Testing Funnel Youth into the School-to-Prison Pipeline. Advancement Project.

The Advancement Project, a civil rights advocacy organization, argues that zero-tolerance discipline policies and high-stakes testing requirements work together to push low-income students and students of color toward the juvenile and criminal justice systems — the "school-to-prison pipeline." The report draws on federal education data, district-level suspension statistics, and multi-state case documentation to show Black and Latino students are suspended and expelled at significantly higher rates than white students for similar infractions. As an advocacy organization, the Advancement Project has a stated interest in civil rights reform, so readers should treat this report as a policy argument supported by data rather than a neutral academic study; the underlying government data, however, is independently verifiable. This source provides essential statistical context for my introduction and the most direct articulation of the school-to-prison pipeline concept I will use throughout.

Revision note: Check whether more recent federal data is available (this report is fourteen years old). Supplement with a more recent data source.

Entry 2 — Draft in Progress

Skiba, R. J., Horner, R. H., Chung, C.-G., Rausch, M. K., May, S. L., & Tobin, T. (2011). Race is not neutral: A national investigation of African American and Latino disproportionality in school discipline. School Psychology Review, 40(1), 85–107.

This peer-reviewed study investigates racial disproportionality in school discipline across a large national sample. The authors [add credentials] use regression analysis to determine whether racial differences in discipline rates can be explained by poverty, achievement gaps, or school-level variables, or whether race independently predicts outcomes. They find that race predicts discipline referrals even after controlling for other factors, supporting the argument that disparities reflect bias rather than behavioral differences.

[EVALUATION TO COMPLETE — strong peer-reviewed journal, major researchers in school psychology, large sample size, published 2011; check for more recent replications. Limitation: cross-sectional data.] [USEFULNESS: directly supports my central argument — one of the most-cited studies in this area; plan to use in Section 2.]

Entry 3 — Comparison Note

[Skiba et al. (2011) and Advancement Project (2010) agree on the core finding of racial disproportionality but use different evidence. Skiba is the stronger academic source; Advancement Project has better policy documentation. Use both. Check whether Skiba's findings have been replicated — search "Skiba disproportionality" in ERIC.]

Entry 4 — Gap Identified

[I do not have a source addressing how zero-tolerance policies affect academic outcomes — grades, graduation, college attendance — for students who are suspended or expelled. My current sources establish disproportionate punishment but not its academic consequences. Search: "suspension academic outcomes" AND "long-term effects."]

Misinformation Alert

Treating Annotation as Validation

The Risk

A significant misinformation risk in annotated bibliography work is annotation-as-validation: annotating sources selectively, keeping only sources that confirm an existing argument while quietly excluding sources that challenge it. This is confirmation bias built into the research process — and a form of intellectual dishonesty. Excluding sources because they challenge your argument, rather than because they are methodologically flawed or irrelevant, builds a distorted picture of the scholarly conversation. A bibliography that represents only one side of a debate is not a neutral research tool; it is an advocacy document that conceals its own partiality.

The correction: deliberately search for credible sources that challenge your argument, annotate them honestly, and include them. A good annotation for a complicating source acknowledges the contradiction directly, assesses the study's methodology fairly, and explains how the counterargument section will address it — rather than ignoring it.

Also Watch For

Overcrediting weak sources — describing advocacy websites, opinion pieces, or popular articles as "credible" or "peer-reviewed" when they are not. Undercrediting strong sources that challenge your argument — nitpicking limitations in sources you disagree with while overlooking the same limitations in sources you agree with. Fabricating or padding summaries — summarizing what you expected the source to say rather than what it actually says. Copying abstracts — presenting an abstract as your own summary, a form of academic dishonesty.

Citation Check

Formatting an Annotated Bibliography Entry

The citation must be formatted exactly as it would appear in a works cited page, references page, or bibliography. The annotation follows the citation, typically indented or separated by a blank line, depending on the style.

StyleAnnotation IndentPlacement
MLAOne inch from left margin (hanging indent continues)New line, immediately after the citation
APAOne-half inch from left marginNew line, immediately after the citation
ChicagoFollows instructor or publication guidance; consistent throughoutNew line, immediately after the citation
Common Formatting Errors

Starting the annotation on the same line as the citation; failing to indent the annotation; writing the annotation in a different font size or style than the citation; inconsistent paragraph structure across annotations; and mixing citation styles within a single document.

  • MGU Reference Department — individual research consultations for complex annotated bibliography projects, especially in upper-division or independent research work.

  • I have a clear research question that my bibliography is organized around.
  • I know which citation style is required.
  • I know how many sources are required and what mix of source types is expected.

  • I have searched the MGU Library catalog for relevant books and ebooks.
  • I have searched at least one academic database for peer-reviewed articles.
  • I have considered government, primary, or specialized reference sources where appropriate.
  • I have used subject headings and citation chaining beyond simple keyword searching.
Writing Citation Entries
  • Every citation is complete and includes all required elements.
  • Every citation is correctly formatted in the required style.
  • I have verified citations against a reliable style guide, not a generator alone.
Writing Summaries and Evaluations
  • Every summary identifies the central argument, written in my own words.
  • Every evaluation addresses at least two specific credibility indicators.
  • Every evaluation honestly notes at least one limitation.
  • I have applied the MGU Source Evaluation Model to each source.

  • Every usefulness statement specifically addresses my current research question.
  • I have read all annotations together to identify agreements, disagreements, and gaps.
  • I have updated usefulness statements to reflect my current research question.
  • I have verified all citations one final time before submission.

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 35.1An annotated bibliography pairs each citation with a paragraph that describes, evaluates, and reflects on a source's usefulness. These three functions distinguish a genuine annotation from a brief description or an abstract reproduction.
  • Sec 35.2The citation entry comes first and must be complete and correctly formatted. Common errors — missing subtitles, incorrect author formats, inaccurate dates — must be verified against a style guide, not a generator alone.
  • Sec 35.3The summary tells what the source argues, covers, and concludes, written in the researcher's own words. Closing the source before writing is the most reliable way to avoid patchwriting.
  • Sec 35.4The evaluation applies the MGU Source Evaluation Model to make a measured, evidence-based judgment of credibility, and honestly acknowledges limitations as a sign of critical thinking.
  • Sec 35.5The usefulness statement connects a source specifically to the research question, names its role, and notes what it cannot do for the project.
  • Sec 35.6Comparing annotations reveals agreements, disagreements, methodological differences, and gaps — the foundation of synthesis.
  • Sec 35.7Used during research rather than after it, an annotated bibliography audits source coverage, maps an argument before drafting, and tracks what has not yet been found.
  • Sec 35.8Annotations should be revised when the research question shifts, when contradicting sources appear, when understanding deepens, and before submission.
Central Principle

Do not merely find information. Earn trust in information. The annotated bibliography is how you document that earning — how you show, source by source, that you read carefully, evaluated honestly, and understood what each source contributes before you used it.

Review Questions

  1. What are the three functions of an annotation? Briefly explain each one and explain why all three are necessary for a complete annotation.
  2. Why does the citation entry come before the annotation text? What would be lost if the annotation appeared without a correctly formatted citation?
  3. What is the most common error students make when writing source summaries, and how can it be avoided? Describe a specific practice strategy.
  4. Apply the MGU Source Evaluation Model to a source type of your choice — a peer-reviewed article, a government report, or a news article. Which dimensions of the model are most important for evaluating that source type, and why?
  5. Explain the difference between a source's overall quality and its usefulness for a specific research project. Give an example of a source that might be excellent in quality but limited in usefulness for a specific research question.
  6. What does it mean to compare annotations? What specific patterns and relationships should you look for when comparing the annotations in a completed bibliography?
  7. How does an annotated bibliography function as a research planning tool? Describe at least three specific planning functions it performs.
  8. When should you revise annotations, and what should you look for when revising? Give at least three specific triggers for revision.
  9. What is the misinformation risk associated with treating annotation as validation? How does deliberately seeking and honestly annotating challenging sources protect against this risk?
  10. Explain the relationship between the annotated bibliography (Chapter 35) and synthesis (Chapter 38). How does the work done in building and comparing annotations prepare you for the synthesis work that comes later in the research process?

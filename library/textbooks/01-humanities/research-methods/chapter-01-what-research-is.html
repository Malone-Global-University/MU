Chapter 1 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Do not merely find information. Earn trust in information.

Most students have been doing some version of research since childhood. They have searched for information online, looked up facts for school projects, compared prices before buying something, and checked reviews before watching a movie. These activities are not wrong. But they are not the same as research in the disciplined sense.

The gap between searching and researching is the gap between finding something and finding something trustworthy, relevant, and sufficient for the question at hand. A student who does not understand that gap may turn in a paper built on the first five results from a search engine, confident that they have "researched" the topic. They have not. They have searched. The two are different, and understanding that difference is where this textbook begins.

Research skills matter beyond school in direct and immediate ways. The person who can research effectively makes better health decisions, evaluates political claims more carefully, makes more informed financial choices, participates more intelligently in civic life, and is far less likely to be manipulated by misinformation. Research literacy is not an academic luxury. It is a survival skill for anyone living in an information-saturated world.

Opening Question

Consider two students assigned to write a paper on the causes of World War I. The first types "causes of World War I" into a search engine, reads the first two results, and produces a paper. The second starts the same way but notices the first two sources disagree. She goes to the library catalog, finds a scholarly book on the July Crisis of 1914, reads an academic article that challenges a popular assumption, and revises her question from "what caused World War I" to something more focused. She builds her paper from a considered synthesis of several sources, notes where historians disagree, and states her conclusions carefully. Which student has done research? By the end of this chapter, you will be able to answer that question precisely — and more importantly, you will understand the reasoning behind the answer well enough to apply it to every research task you encounter.

Section 1.1

Research as Disciplined Curiosity

Research begins with a question you do not already know the answer to. That sounds simple, but it contains an important implication: research is not the process of confirming what you already believe. It is the process of investigating something you genuinely want to understand — or need to understand — and following the evidence wherever it leads, even when it contradicts your assumptions.

The word disciplined is important here. Curiosity alone is not research. A person can be intensely curious about a topic and still never research it in any meaningful sense — they might collect information that confirms what they already suspected, ignore evidence that complicates their view, and call the result "research." This is not research. It is something closer to motivated browsing.

Research is disciplined because it imposes constraints on how curiosity is pursued. These constraints feel like restrictions, but they are really protections. They protect you from fooling yourself. They protect your readers from receiving conclusions you have not actually earned. And they protect the larger conversation — in academic fields, in public life, in professional communities — from being polluted with poorly examined claims.

The Six Constraints of Disciplined Research

A disciplined researcher consistently asks: What exactly am I trying to find out? What kind of evidence would actually answer that question? Who is likely to have that evidence? How do I know whether the evidence I find is reliable? What am I missing — what are the limits of what I have found? What do I actually know now, and what remains uncertain?

Returning to these questions throughout a research project — not just at the beginning — is what separates disciplined inquiry from motivated browsing.

The Difference Between Interest and Investigation

There is nothing wrong with being interested in a topic without researching it. You can be interested in astronomy without conducting astronomical research. Interest is the starting point. But interest that does not ask hard questions, seek reliable sources, test its assumptions, and follow evidence into discomfort — that is not research.

This distinction matters in practice. The student who types a topic into a search engine, reads the first two results, and writes them up has followed their interest. The student who notices that sources disagree, seeks out the scholarly conversation, narrows her question, and builds from a synthesis of evaluated evidence — that student has done research.

This textbook will help you become the second kind of student — not just in history class or English class, but in every situation that requires you to actually know something.

Research as Intellectual Courage

One thing that separates experienced researchers from beginners is the willingness to stay with uncertainty. Beginners often want to find the answer quickly and move on. They feel uncomfortable when sources disagree or when the evidence is incomplete. Experienced researchers expect these things. They know that uncertainty is not a failure of research — it is one of research's most honest findings.

When a responsible researcher says, "The evidence suggests X, but there is significant disagreement about Y, and we do not yet know Z," that is not weakness. That is accuracy. It is harder to say than "The answer is X." But it is more trustworthy. Research requires the courage to follow a question through difficulty, to admit when you were wrong, and to say clearly when you do not yet know enough.

Section 1.2

Research Versus Looking Things Up

The distinction between research and looking something up is one of the most important ideas in this textbook. Both activities involve finding information. But they differ in purpose, process, depth, and the kind of confidence they produce.

Looking something up is the act of finding a specific, usually factual piece of information to answer a narrow question. What is the capital of Peru? What year was the telephone invented? These are legitimate questions. Answering them requires finding a reliable source and checking the answer. That is looking something up.

Research is a sustained process of inquiry into a question that cannot be answered by looking up a single fact. Research involves exploring what is known, identifying gaps, evaluating sources, comparing perspectives, weighing evidence, and forming a conclusion — while acknowledging what remains uncertain.

DimensionLooking Something UpResearch
Starting pointA specific fact questionA problem or inquiry question

When Looking Something Up Is the Right Tool

This distinction is not meant to suggest that looking something up is inferior. It is exactly the right tool when you need a specific fact quickly and the fact is well established. No serious researcher consults five scholarly journals to find out the capital of Peru. The skill is knowing which tool fits which job.

The problem arises when students use the "looking something up" approach for questions that actually require research. A student who Googles "is social media bad for teenagers" and accepts the first result has used the wrong tool for a question that requires examining multiple studies, understanding what the research actually shows, noting its limitations, and considering competing interpretations.

The Confidence Problem

One of the risks of confusing searching with researching is misplaced confidence. When you look something up and find an answer, you feel confident — you found it. But if the question actually required research, that confidence is unearned.

This is one reason misinformation spreads so effectively. People find something that looks like an answer, feel the confidence of having found it, and stop. The feeling of having found an answer is not the same as having actually answered the question. Research teaches you to interrogate that feeling: not just "Did I find something?" but "Have I found enough? Is what I found reliable? Am I missing an important counterpoint?"

Section 1.3

Questions, Evidence, and Conclusions

Every act of research involves three elements: a question, evidence gathered in response to that question, and a conclusion drawn from the evidence. These three elements form the basic architecture of research, and understanding how they relate is essential.

Questions

Research begins with a question — not a topic. "Climate change" is a topic. "What are the projected effects of continued carbon emissions on sea levels by the end of this century?" is a research question. The difference matters enormously. A topic has no direction. A question points you somewhere: it tells you what kind of evidence you need, what disciplines might be relevant, and what a useful answer would look like.

Good research questions share several qualities. They are genuinely answerable — not by a single fact, but by evidence that can be gathered and used to support a reasoned conclusion. They are specific enough to be investigated — not "Is technology good or bad?" but something like "How have smartphone use patterns changed adolescents' sleep quality since 2010?" They are open enough to be interesting — not simply "What is the definition of plagiarism?" but something that requires genuine inquiry. And they are appropriate to the available evidence — a good research question is one for which evidence actually exists, or can be gathered.

The MGU Research Question Model — Introduction

You will use this model throughout the textbook. It moves from a broad topic toward a focused, workable research question in six steps: Topic → Focused issue → Question → Purpose → Scope → Evidence needed. Chapter 6 teaches this model in full depth. This chapter introduces its logic.

Evidence

Evidence is the material you gather to answer your research question. It can take many forms: published research, government data, historical documents, expert testimony, images, surveys, experiments, interviews, statistics, and more. The form evidence takes depends heavily on the question and the field. In historical research, primary sources — letters, diaries, official records — are often the most valuable evidence. In biomedical research, randomized controlled trials carry the most weight. In literary analysis, the text itself is primary evidence.

Evidence Must BeWhat This MeansHow to Check
RelevantIt must actually bear on the question being askedAsk: Does this evidence help answer my specific question, or does it merely touch the same general topic?
CredibleIt must come from a trustworthy source with appropriate expertiseAsk: Who made this? What are their credentials? Are they accountable to a community of peers?
AccurateIt must reflect what actually happened, what the data shows, or what the source arguedAsk: Can another reliable source confirm this? Does the evidence match the claim being made about it?
SufficientYou must have gathered enough to support your conclusion without overstating itAsk: Have I found multiple independent sources? Have I considered counterevidence?

Conclusions

A conclusion is what you determine based on the evidence you have gathered. Conclusions in research are not simply opinions — they are positions supported by evidence, qualified by what the evidence actually shows, and honest about what the evidence does not show.

This is why research conclusions often sound careful and hedged: "The available evidence suggests that…" or "Most studies in this area find that…" These qualifications are not weakness. They are intellectual honesty. They tell the reader: I have examined the evidence carefully, and this is what it supports, but I am not claiming more certainty than the evidence warrants.

Example 1.3 — Overstated vs. Proportionate Conclusions
Why conclusions must match what the evidence actually shows
  • Overstated "Research proves that coffee makes you smarter."This overstates what a finding of correlation between coffee and concentration actually demonstrates. Two studies suggesting a possible association are not proof of anything. The word "proves" claims far more than the evidence supports.
  • Proportionate "Several recent studies suggest a possible association between moderate caffeine consumption and improved short-term concentration, though the mechanisms are not yet well understood and findings vary across populations and conditions."This version states what the evidence shows, acknowledges its limits, and invites further inquiry rather than closing it down.
Principle: State what the evidence shows. Acknowledge what it does not show. Never claim more certainty than you have earned.

The relationship between question, evidence, and conclusion is not a straight line. Research is a cycle. As you gather evidence, your question often becomes more focused. As your question becomes more focused, you seek different evidence. As you examine different evidence, your preliminary conclusion may change. This cycling is normal — it is usually a sign that your research is going right, not wrong.

Section 1.4

Academic Research

Academic research is research conducted within the norms, methods, and standards of a scholarly discipline or institution. It is the kind of research published in peer-reviewed journals, presented at academic conferences, funded by universities and research agencies, and cited in scholarly books and articles. Academic research differs from other kinds of research in several important ways.

Peer Review and Accountability

One of the defining features of academic research is peer review — the process by which the findings, methods, and conclusions of a study are evaluated by qualified experts in the same field before publication. Peer review does not guarantee that published research is correct. Errors, biases, and even fraudulent findings have passed through peer review. But peer review provides a level of scrutiny and accountability that most other kinds of publication lack.

When you use a peer-reviewed article as a source, you are using a source that has been evaluated by experts who were not involved in the study, who were asked to assess whether the methods are appropriate, the conclusions supported, and the contribution meaningful. That evaluation adds value. It is not infallible, but it is not nothing.

Disciplinary Methods

Academic research is also characterized by disciplinary methods — specific practices for gathering, analyzing, and interpreting evidence that are accepted within a particular field. A sociologist studying inequality uses different methods than a chemist studying molecular reactions. A historian studying the French Revolution uses different methods than an economist studying unemployment trends. Each discipline has developed its methods over decades or centuries in response to the kinds of questions it asks and the kinds of evidence available.

The Scholarly Conversation

Academic research does not happen in isolation. Each scholarly work is part of a larger, ongoing conversation among researchers who are all trying to understand something. Published research cites earlier research, responds to earlier findings, challenges earlier conclusions, and opens new questions for future researchers. This is why the citations in academic articles matter: they show you the conversation the author is participating in.

What Student Research Requires

As a student researcher, you are learning to read the scholarly conversation, understand it, and eventually contribute to it. The most important skills for student researchers are: understanding how to find scholarly sources; understanding how to read and evaluate those sources; understanding how to integrate them into your own analysis without misrepresenting them; and understanding how to cite them accurately. These skills — finding, evaluating, integrating, citing — are what this textbook teaches.

Section 1.5

Professional and Applied Research

Research is not only an academic activity. Every field that requires knowledge-based decision-making involves some form of research. Understanding professional and applied research helps students see why research skills extend far beyond school.

FieldResearch TaskKey Evidence Types
MedicineTreating a patient with an unusual presentation; identifying relevant clinical guidelinesCase studies, clinical trials, treatment protocols, medical literature
LawPreparing a case; identifying relevant precedents and statutesCase law, statutes, regulations, legal scholarship
JournalismInvestigating a story; verifying claims across multiple sourcesPublic records, interviews, official documents, cross-checked accounts
BusinessEvaluating a strategic option; understanding market conditionsMarket data, competitor analysis, financial reports, regulatory documents
Public PolicyDesigning or evaluating a policy; assessing likely impactsStatistical data, program evaluations, comparative case studies

In each case, the same basic structure applies: a question, evidence gathered to address it, and a conclusion drawn from the evidence. The methods differ, the sources differ, and the standards for what counts as good evidence differ. But the fundamental logic of research is the same.

Applied research is research conducted to solve a specific practical problem rather than to generate general knowledge. An academic study might investigate how social media affects adolescent mental health in general. An applied research project for a school district might investigate how social media use in that specific district affects student performance at that specific school. Applied research operates under real-world conditions — deadlines, budget limits, access limitations — but it uses the same core skills: clear questions, relevant evidence, careful evaluation, honest conclusions.

Section 1.6

Personal and Civic Research

Research is not only professional and academic. Every time a person makes an important decision — about health, about money, about politics, about safety — they are either doing research or failing to do it.

Personal Research Decisions

A person diagnosed with a chronic condition faces choices about treatment options. They can accept whatever they are first told, or they can research the condition — reading reputable medical sources, understanding the evidence for different approaches, preparing informed questions for their doctor. The research does not replace the doctor's expertise, but it makes the conversation more productive and reduces the risk of a poorly informed choice.

Similarly, a person considering a major investment can research their options — understanding fees, risks, historical performance, and relevant regulations — or they can rely entirely on whoever is selling them the product. A consumer evaluating whether a product's health claims are supported by evidence is doing research. The person who can research effectively is simply less vulnerable to being deceived.

Civic Research

In a democratic society, citizens make collective decisions through elections, advocacy, and public participation. Those decisions are only as good as the information citizens use to make them. A citizen who can research effectively can evaluate political claims against available evidence, understand what a proposed policy would actually do rather than accepting a partisan characterization, distinguish between credible and unreliable news sources, recognize propaganda and manipulated content, and participate more meaningfully in public discourse.

The connection between information literacy and civic health is direct. Democracies are weakened when citizens cannot evaluate information, cannot distinguish between reliable and unreliable sources, and are easily manipulated by false or misleading claims. Research skills are, in this sense, civic skills — and this is not a political statement about any particular party or position. Whatever your political views, you are better equipped to act on them intelligently if you can research effectively, evaluate evidence honestly, and recognize manipulation.

Section 1.7

Research Across Disciplines

One of the most valuable things a student can understand is that research looks different in different disciplines — and that those differences are not arbitrary. Different fields ask different kinds of questions, use different kinds of evidence, and apply different methods because their subject matter is different.

Discipline AreaPrimary QuestionsKey EvidenceCharacteristic Methods
Humanities
History, literature, philosophy, art history		What does this mean? How do we understand human expression, culture, belief?Primary texts, documents, artworks, letters, artifactsClose reading, interpretation, contextual analysis, archival research
Social Sciences

Understanding that research methods vary across disciplines helps students approach unfamiliar research with appropriate humility. If you are reading a study that uses statistical methods you do not fully understand, the right response is not to dismiss the study or to accept it uncritically — it is to try to understand what the methods are doing and why, and to consider what qualified experts in that field say about the study's conclusions.

What Does Not Change Across Disciplines

Despite differences in method and evidence, the fundamental logic of research is consistent across all fields. Good research begins with a clear, focused question. Good research uses evidence appropriate to the question and the field. Good research evaluates that evidence carefully rather than accepting it uncritically. Good research reaches conclusions proportionate to the evidence. And good research is honest about uncertainty and limitation. These principles hold whether you are studying medieval literature, climate science, behavioral economics, or criminal law.

Source Check

Source Check — Chapter 1
A Health Website Article: Recognizing Problems Before You Begin
An article titled "Scientists Prove Coffee Cures Memory Loss." Published on a health website three years ago. No author named. The article cites "a recent study" but does not name the study, the journal, or the researchers. The website sells a coffee-based dietary supplement in its online store.
  1. 1What kind of source is this? (Academic article, news report, blog, advocacy site, commercial site?)
  2. 2Who is the author, and what are their credentials?
  3. 3Who publishes this source, and do they have a financial or other interest in the claim?
  4. 4Does the source cite its evidence specifically? Can you follow the citation to verify the original claim?
  5. 5How recent is the source? Is the date relevant to the claim?
  6. 6Does the headline match what the evidence actually shows? ("Scientists prove" is an extremely strong claim — does any single study ever "prove" something?)
  7. 7Is this source appropriate for academic research? If not, could it serve any legitimate purpose — such as an example of how health misinformation is marketed?
What This Source Check Shows This source has multiple serious problems: no named author, no specific citation for the study it references, a clear commercial interest in the topic (the website sells a related product), and a headline that almost certainly overstates what any evidence shows. No responsible researcher would cite this source as reliable evidence about coffee and memory. They might, however, use it as an example of how health misinformation is marketed — a very different kind of research purpose. Knowing the difference between a source you can cite and a source you can only analyze is itself a research skill.

Misinformation Alert

Alert: The "I Found It Online" Fallacy

One of the most persistent misconceptions students bring to research is the belief that finding information online is equivalent to finding reliable information. The internet contains accurate, well-sourced reporting from reputable news organizations — and it also contains misinformation created accidentally through honest error, disinformation created deliberately to deceive, propaganda designed to promote an agenda, advertising designed to look like neutral information, outdated information that was once accurate but is no longer, and accurate information presented in misleading context.

The internet does not sort these categories for you. A search engine result page may list all of them together, with no indication of which is which. The skill of evaluating online sources is precisely the skill of sorting these categories yourself — and this textbook builds that skill across multiple chapters.

The bottom line: Finding something online confirms only that it exists online. It confirms nothing about whether it is accurate, current, appropriately sourced, or relevant to your question. Always ask: Who made this? What evidence do they offer? What are they trying to accomplish? Can another reliable source confirm it?

Citation Check

Even in Chapter 1, it is worth establishing the habit of recording citation information for every source you read. One of the most common ways student research goes wrong is that students find a useful source, take notes on it, and then cannot remember where they found it when it is time to write the paper and cite their sources.

What to Record — Always, For Every Source

Full name(s) of author(s) · Full title of the article, chapter, or work · Name of the journal, book, newspaper, or website · Publication date (including volume and issue number for journals) · Page numbers, if applicable · URL or database information, if accessed online · Date you accessed it, for online sources that may change.

You do not need to format this as a formal citation immediately. But you must record all the elements so that formatting it later is possible. Students who skip this step regularly find themselves unable to complete their Works Cited pages accurately.

Common Citation Error — Chapter 1

Students write down a title and a URL but not the author or publication date. Then, when they try to cite the source, they discover the author is missing, or the URL no longer works, or the site does not show a publication date. Recording all elements from the start prevents this entirely. The three minutes it takes to record complete source information at the time of reading saves hours of searching later — and prevents the embarrassment of citing a source you can no longer locate.

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 1.1Research is a process of disciplined inquiry — not simply the confirmation of existing beliefs. Disciplined curiosity imposes constraints that protect the researcher from self-deception and protect readers from unearned conclusions. Experienced researchers expect uncertainty and treat it as an honest finding, not a failure.
  • Sec 1.2Looking something up and genuine research are different activities. Looking something up is the appropriate tool for narrow factual questions. Research is required when a question cannot be answered by a single fact and demands finding, evaluating, comparing, and synthesizing multiple sources. Confusing the two produces misplaced confidence — the feeling of having answered a question without actually having done so.
  • Sec 1.3Every act of research involves three elements: a question, evidence, and a conclusion. Good research questions are specific, answerable, and appropriate to the available evidence. Evidence must be relevant, credible, accurate, and sufficient. Conclusions must be proportionate to the evidence — stating what it shows, acknowledging what it does not, and never claiming more certainty than the evidence warrants.
  • Sec 1.4Academic research is characterized by peer review, disciplinary methods, and participation in an ongoing scholarly conversation. Peer review adds a layer of expert scrutiny that most other publication lacks. Student researchers must find, evaluate, integrate, and cite scholarly sources — the four skills this textbook builds.
  • Sec 1.5Research is also a professional and applied activity. Physicians, lawyers, journalists, and business analysts all use the same fundamental logic — question, evidence, conclusion — adapted to their field's methods and constraints. Applied research targets specific practical problems rather than general knowledge, but uses the same core skills.
  • Sec 1.6Personal and civic research enable better decisions about health, finances, politics, and public life. Citizens who can evaluate evidence and recognize misinformation are more capable participants in democratic society. Information literacy is not an academic luxury — it is a civic skill.
  • Sec 1.7Research looks different across disciplines because the subject matter and the kinds of questions differ. The humanities emphasize interpretation; the social sciences rely on empirical data; the natural sciences depend on experimentation and replication. Despite these differences, the fundamental principles — clear question, appropriate evidence, careful evaluation, proportionate conclusion, honesty about uncertainty — are consistent across all fields.
  • Sec 1.8This textbook is organized around the sequence: question → search → evaluate → organize → synthesize → cite → present → keep learning. Each of the eight parts builds on the one before it. The MGU Research Question Model — Topic, Focused issue, Question, Purpose, Scope, Evidence needed — is the primary navigational tool for developing researchable questions throughout the course.

Key Terms

academic research
Research conducted within the norms, methods, and standards of a scholarly discipline; typically subject to peer review and published in scholarly venues.
applied research
Research conducted to solve a specific practical problem rather than to generate general knowledge.
claim
An assertion that something is true; in research, claims must be supported by evidence rather than merely asserted.
conclusion
A determination reached on the basis of evidence; in research, conclusions must be proportionate to the evidence and honest about uncertainty.
credibility
The quality of being trustworthy and reliable; a credible source demonstrates expertise, accountability, and accuracy.
disciplinary methods
The specific practices for gathering, analyzing, and interpreting evidence accepted within a particular academic field.
disciplined curiosity
The combination of genuine curiosity and methodological constraint that defines research; curiosity directed by standards of evidence, accountability, and honest uncertainty.
evidence
Material gathered to support or answer a research question; may include published research, data, documents, images, testimony, and more.
information literacy
The ability to find, evaluate, use, create, and communicate information responsibly.
inquiry
The process of asking questions and pursuing answers; the foundation of research.
peer review
The process by which qualified experts in a field evaluate the findings, methods, and conclusions of a study before publication.
primary source
A source that provides direct, firsthand access to an event, period, or phenomenon — original documents, data, artworks, interviews.
research
A sustained process of disciplined inquiry into a question, involving finding, evaluating, and synthesizing sources to reach a supported conclusion.
research question
A specific, focused, answerable question that guides a research project; more precise and directed than a broad topic.
secondary source
A source that analyzes, interprets, or summarizes primary sources — scholarly articles, textbooks, critical essays.
synthesis
The process of combining information from multiple sources to form a broader understanding; more than summary, because it identifies relationships among sources.
uncertainty
The condition of not yet knowing; in research, acknowledging uncertainty is an honest finding and a mark of intellectual integrity, not a failure.

Review Questions

  1. In your own words, explain the difference between looking something up and genuine research. Give an example of a situation in which each approach is appropriate.
  2. What are the three core elements of any research project? How do they relate to each other?
  3. Why does research require "disciplined" curiosity? What does the discipline add to curiosity that curiosity alone cannot provide?
  4. What is peer review, and why does it matter for evaluating academic sources? What does peer review guarantee — and what does it not guarantee?
  5. Describe one way that research differs across disciplines. Why are those differences not arbitrary?
  6. Why is it a problem to state conclusions that go beyond what the evidence shows? Give a specific example of how this error might occur.
  7. How does information literacy relate to civic life? Why might a democracy be weakened if its citizens lack strong information literacy skills?
  8. What does it mean to say that research conclusions should be "proportionate to the evidence"? Why is this standard important?
  9. Explain why the feeling of having found an answer is not the same as having actually answered a research question.
  10. Apply the MGU Research Question Model to a topic of interest to you. Write out the six steps — Topic, Focused issue, Question, Purpose, Scope, Evidence needed — for a question you might actually research this semester.

Source Evaluation Practice

Below are descriptions of three sources. For each one, answer the following questions: What kind of source is this? What signals, if any, suggest this source might be reliable? What questions would you need to answer before deciding whether to use this source in a research paper?

Source Evaluation — Chapter 1
Three sources on the same topic: opioid addiction rates
  • Source A A 40-page report on opioid addiction rates published by the National Institute on Drug Abuse (NIDA), a federal government research agency. The report lists its authors — epidemiologists and public health researchers at NIDA and several universities — includes extensive data tables, cites over 80 peer-reviewed studies, and was published two years ago.
  • Source B A blog post titled "The Truth About Opioids That Big Pharma Doesn't Want You to Know." The author identifies herself as a "wellness advocate and health blogger." The post includes several statistics but does not cite sources for any of them. It promotes an herbal supplement in the sidebar. There is no publication date.
  • Source C A news article from a major regional newspaper, published last month, reporting on a new state law restricting opioid prescriptions. The article quotes three named sources: a state health official, a pain management physician, and a patient advocacy representative. It describes what the law says but does not evaluate whether it will be effective.
Notice that none of these sources is simply "good" or "bad" — their usefulness depends on what research question you are trying to answer. Part of source evaluation is matching the source to the question, not just rating the source in the abstract.

Discussion Prompts

Discussion 1
Your Information History

Think about your most memorable experience with research — either positive or negative. What was the situation? What made it memorable? What did it teach you about research, or about yourself as a researcher? Share with a partner or in a small group and discuss: what assumptions about "good research" did your experience reveal? Which of those assumptions hold up in light of this chapter, and which do not?

Discussion 2
Comparing Information Tasks

As a class, generate a list of ten to fifteen information-finding tasks from daily life. For each one, classify it: Is it looking something up, or is it genuine research? What made each one easy or difficult to classify? Were there any that could be either, depending on how the question was framed?

Discussion 3
Research and Democracy

This chapter argues that research skills are civic skills — that democracies are weakened when citizens cannot evaluate information. Do you agree? Can you think of specific examples from public life in which poor information evaluation contributed to harmful outcomes? What would it look like if citizens generally had strong information literacy skills? What would change?

Discussion 4
When Research Changed Your Mind

Think of a time when finding information changed what you believed about something. It does not have to be a formal research project — it could be anything from a personal decision to a news story. What made the information persuasive enough to change your mind? Looking back, was the information actually reliable? What would you do differently now to evaluate it?

Information Literacy Reflection

This reflection is designed to help you think honestly about your own relationship with information before the course fully begins. Write 400–500 words in response to the following questions. There are no right or wrong answers — this is an honest self-assessment, and honesty will serve you better than performing the "right" answers.

  1. How do you typically find information when you need it? What sources do you most commonly use?
  2. How do you currently decide whether a source is trustworthy? What signals do you look for?
  3. Think about a time you believed something that turned out to be wrong. How did you find out you were wrong? What led you to check?
  4. Are there topics on which you are confident you know the truth without having deeply researched them? What gives you that confidence?
  5. What do you hope to learn from this course about how to find and evaluate information?

Looking Ahead
Chapter 2: What Information Literacy Means

Chapter 1 established what research is: a process of disciplined inquiry supported by evidence, distinct from casual searching, and essential across academic, professional, personal, and civic life. Chapter 2 will define information literacy more fully — as the ability to find, evaluate, use, create, and communicate information responsibly. You will learn why information literacy matters specifically in the context of school, work, citizenship, health, technology, and everyday life, and how it differs from simple familiarity with search engines. The information literacy framework introduced in Chapter 2 will underpin every chapter that follows.

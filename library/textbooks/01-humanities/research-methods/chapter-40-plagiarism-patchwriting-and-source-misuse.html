Chapter 40 — Front Matter

Research Skill Focus and Overview

This chapter teaches students how to recognize, understand, and avoid every major form of academic dishonesty involving sources: direct plagiarism, accidental plagiarism, patchwriting, missing citations, fake citations, source distortion, self-plagiarism, and improper collaboration. Students will learn that plagiarism is not only a rule to follow but an ethical commitment to honesty, fairness, and the integrity of knowledge-building.

Why This Matters

Every semester, students lose credit, face academic discipline, or damage their academic records because of plagiarism violations they could have prevented. Some violations are deliberate. Many are not. Students who copy paragraphs without thinking, who change a few words and call it paraphrase, or who forget to add a citation to a sentence they know they read somewhere are all committing forms of academic dishonesty — even without intending harm.

This chapter exists because good intentions are not enough. A student who genuinely does not know the difference between paraphrase and patchwriting can still receive a failing grade, face an academic integrity hearing, or be dismissed from a program. Understanding the rules clearly, understanding why they exist, and building strong note-taking and writing habits are the only reliable protections.

Beyond rules, plagiarism matters because research is a system of trust. When you cite a source, you are telling your reader: I did not invent this. Someone else made this argument, gathered this evidence, or found this fact. You can check it. When that system breaks down — when citations are missing, invented, or misrepresented — readers cannot trust the work. The entire purpose of academic research is undermined.

Understanding source misuse is part of becoming a trustworthy researcher and writer. The goal is not to memorize a list of prohibited behaviors. The goal is to understand why honesty in research matters and how to practice it in every step of your work.

What Plagiarism Is and How It Happens

Section 40.1

What Plagiarism Is

Plagiarism is the use of someone else's words, ideas, data, images, or intellectual work without giving that person proper credit. The word comes from the Latin plagiarius, which originally referred to a kidnapper. In an academic context, the analogy holds: taking someone's intellectual labor without acknowledgment is a form of theft.

Plagiarism takes many forms, but all of them share one core feature: a reader is given the impression that something originated with the researcher when it did not.

FoundationWhat It MeansWhy It Matters
HonestyYou did not think of this. Someone else did.Pretending otherwise is a misrepresentation of who produced the idea
FairnessResearchers, scholars, and creators invest real time and labor producing knowledgeThey deserve credit for that work, regardless of how briefly it is used
VerifiabilityA citation lets a reader trace, check, and evaluate a claimWithout it, a reader has no way to assess whether a source was used correctly
Common Misconception

Students sometimes believe plagiarism only applies to direct word-for-word copying. This is not true. You can plagiarize an idea without copying a single sentence — a structure, a line of argument, a data interpretation, or a creative concept. The question is always: did you present something as your own when it came from somewhere else?

Another Common Misconception

Some students believe that if they change enough words, the passage becomes theirs. This belief produces one of the most common forms of academic dishonesty: patchwriting, addressed fully in Section 40.4.

Most academic integrity policies distinguish between deliberate and accidental plagiarism, and penalties often differ. But this distinction does not eliminate the violation. Accidental plagiarism is still plagiarism. The most reliable prevention is careful note-taking. When you know exactly which words came from a source and which words are yours — because you recorded them that way using the MGU Research Note Model — you will not accidentally mix them up.

Section 40.2

Direct Plagiarism

Direct plagiarism is the most straightforward form: copying another person's exact words and presenting them as your own, without quotation marks and without a citation.

Example 40.2 — Direct Plagiarism in Practice
Same words, no acknowledgment
  • Source "The ability to evaluate information critically is the foundation of informed citizenship in a democratic society."This sentence appears in a published source the student is reading.
  • Plagiarized The ability to evaluate information critically is the foundation of informed citizenship in a democratic society.No quotation marks. No citation. The reader has no way of knowing this sentence was not written by the student.
Principle: Exact wording from a source always requires quotation marks and a citation — no exceptions for length.

Direct plagiarism does not require copying a full paragraph. Copying a single distinctive sentence without attribution is direct plagiarism. Copying a memorable phrase without quotation marks is direct plagiarism. Copying a list, a definition, a numbered set of steps, or a table without citation is direct plagiarism.

Visual material is not exempt. Using an image, chart, graph, map, or figure created by someone else without attribution is plagiarism of a visual source. Data is not exempt either. Using statistics, findings, or numerical data from a study without citing the study is a form of plagiarism even if you never reproduce the study's exact words.

Prevention Habit

Never paste source text directly into a draft document. Take notes in a separate file using the MGU Research Note Model. When you return to your draft, work from your notes — not from the source itself.

Direct plagiarism is treated as one of the most serious academic integrity violations across virtually all academic institutions. Consequences typically include a failing grade on the assignment, a failing grade in the course, placement on an academic integrity record, suspension, or dismissal. In professional and publishing contexts, direct plagiarism can end careers.

Section 40.3

Accidental Plagiarism

Accidental plagiarism occurs when a researcher uses a source's ideas, arguments, data, or phrasing without proper credit — not because they intended to deceive, but because of poor note-taking, confusion about citation rules, or a misunderstanding of what requires attribution.

Common CauseHow It Happens
Lost source informationA student reads several articles, takes notes, and later cannot remember which idea came from which source
Pasting without labelingSource text is copied into a notes file without being marked as a direct quotation, then mistaken later for paraphrase
Misunderstanding common knowledgeA student believes a specific researcher's argument is widely known fact
Forgetting to add a citationA sentence drawn from a source is written with the intention of citing it later, and the citation is never added
Over-relying on memoryA source is read, set aside, and the paper is written from memory, creating false confidence that the ideas are original
Why Intent Doesn't Eliminate the Violation

The reader of your paper has no way of knowing whether you meant to cite a source or forgot. The missing citation produces the same result either way: the reader cannot trace the idea, verify the claim, or give credit to the original author. The solution is not to ask for leniency. The solution is to build systems that prevent the error.

Prevention Strategies

Record the source at the moment you encounter it. Mark every quotation at the moment you record it. Keep source notes separate from your own thinking. Review citations before you submit. When in doubt, cite — you will never be penalized for citing too carefully.

Section 40.4

Patchwriting

Patchwriting is one of the most common — and least understood — forms of academic dishonesty. It occurs when a researcher substitutes synonyms for individual words in a source passage, rearranges a few phrases, and submits the result as paraphrase. The structure, ideas, and logic of the original passage remain intact. Only the surface words have changed.

Patchwriting is a form of plagiarism. It may or may not include a citation. When it does include a citation, it is still academically problematic because it misrepresents the researcher's engagement with the source. When it lacks a citation, it is direct plagiarism.

Example 40.4 — Patchwriting vs. True Paraphrase
Surface changes are not the same as independent expression
  • Source "Research literacy requires students to develop skills in locating, evaluating, and synthesizing information from multiple sources. These skills are not instinctive. They must be taught explicitly, practiced repeatedly, and refined through feedback."
  • Patchwritten Research literacy demands that students build abilities in finding, assessing, and combining information from various sources. These abilities are not natural. They must be instructed directly, practiced often, and improved through feedback.Nearly every sentence mirrors the original structure. Synonyms have changed the words but not the thinking.
  • True paraphrase According to researchers in this field, students do not naturally know how to find, judge, or combine material from different sources. Developing these abilities requires direct instruction and ongoing practice with feedback (Author, Year).This version departs from the original structure, reflects the writer's own understanding, and is followed by a citation because the idea still came from the source.
Principle: Read. Close. Write. Express the idea independently, then cite the source for the idea.

Patchwriting often results from a researcher who understands that copying is wrong but does not fully understand what real paraphrase requires. Patchwriting also happens when researchers feel uncertain about the material — if you do not fully understand a passage, it is difficult to restate it in your own words. The solution is to read more carefully, look up unfamiliar terms, and build enough understanding to explain the idea without looking at the source.

How to Avoid Patchwriting

Read the passage. Set the source aside or flip it face-down. Write your paraphrase without looking at the original text. Explain the idea to yourself first: What is this passage actually saying? What is the main claim? What evidence supports it? Then write that explanation in your own sentence structure — not the structure of the original. Afterward, check your paraphrase against the source. If every sentence of yours corresponds to a sentence in the source, rewrite.

Section 40.5

Missing and Fake Citations

Missing citations and fake citations represent two different problems, but both undermine the function of academic source use.

A missing citation is a claim, idea, statistic, quotation, or paraphrase in a paper that requires attribution but has no citation attached. It may occur because the researcher forgot to add one, did not realize one was needed, or deliberately omitted one. Regardless of cause, the reader cannot identify the source, verify the claim, or give credit to the original author.

Requires a CitationDoes Not Require a Citation
Direct quotations from any sourceFacts that are genuinely common knowledge (the year a war ended, a country's capital)
Paraphrased ideas, arguments, or interpretations from a sourceYour own original analysis, argument, or reasoning
Statistics, data points, or numerical claims from a study or reportYour own previously developed knowledge not derived from a source consulted for this paper
Specific facts that are not common knowledge
Findings, conclusions, or recommendations from research
Visual material created by someone else
Any idea that came from reading — even fully reworded
If You Are Uncertain

If you are uncertain whether something is common knowledge, cite it anyway. You will never be penalized for citing too carefully.

Fake citations — also called fabricated citations — are citations to sources that do not exist, to sources that say something different from what the researcher claims, or to sources the researcher never actually read. Fabricated citations are among the most serious academic integrity violations possible, because they cannot be attributed to carelessness or confusion alone.

Fabricated citations also arise from a newer source of error: AI-generated text. Large language models, including AI writing assistants, frequently produce citations to books, articles, and authors that do not exist — known as hallucinated citations. A researcher who uses AI-generated text without verification and submits fabricated citations is responsible for those fabrications, even without intending to create them.

Preventing Fake Citations

Verify every citation before submitting. Confirm the source exists, that it says what you claim, and that your citation information is accurate. Never cite a source you have not read. Never submit AI-generated text containing citations without verifying each one independently. Use a source manager to track sources you have actually consulted, and cite only from that list.

Section 40.6

Distorting Sources

Source distortion occurs when a researcher misrepresents what a source actually says. Unlike missing citations or fake citations, source distortion may include a perfectly accurate citation — but the description of what the source argues, finds, or claims is wrong. Even if the source exists and the researcher read it, attributing positions to a source that it does not hold is a form of intellectual dishonesty.

Form of DistortionWhat It Looks Like
Overstating the conclusionA narrow finding under specific conditions is described as a universal, absolute result
Understating limitationsFindings are described without mentioning the study's own acknowledged limitations
Cherry-pickingA supporting sentence is selected while the source's overall conclusion, which complicates the argument, is ignored
MisquotingWords in a quotation are changed or omitted without ellipses or brackets, altering meaning
Removing contextA quotation is used in a way that misrepresents the author's actual position
MisattributionAn argument from one author is credited to a different author due to a note-taking error
Why This Matters

Distorting sources is damaging in part because it is difficult to detect. A reader who trusts the researcher and does not check every citation will form incorrect beliefs about what the research says. Scholarship built on distorted sources perpetuates errors through future citations.

How to Avoid Distortion

Check direct quotations character by character. Read sources completely before citing them — do not cite based on an abstract, a conclusion paragraph, or a quotation someone else used. Represent disagreements and limitations honestly. Before including any characterization of a source's argument, reread the relevant section and confirm your description is accurate.

Section 40.7

Self-Plagiarism and Reuse

Self-plagiarism is the submission of your own previously submitted work — or significant portions of it — as new work, without disclosure or permission from the instructor or publication.

The concept surprises many students. How can you plagiarize yourself? The answer involves the purpose of academic assignments. When you are assigned a research paper, your instructor is asking you to engage in fresh intellectual work: new research, new analysis, new writing. Submitting work you have already done for another class does not fulfill that assignment. It misrepresents to the instructor that you have done new work when you have not.

FormDescription
Double submissionSubmitting the same or substantially the same paper to two different courses without permission from both instructors
Recycling sectionsIncorporating paragraphs or large portions of a previously submitted paper into a new paper without disclosure
Reusing dataSubmitting the same data analysis or survey results to fulfill requirements in more than one course
Prior publication reusePublishing previously published material as if it were new — a concern extending into professional scholarship
When Reuse May Be Permitted

Self-plagiarism policies vary by institution, course, and instructor. Some instructors permit students to build on prior work — for example, developing a research proposal from an earlier course into a full research project in an advanced course. The key is disclosure and permission. Ask your instructor before beginning the assignment, explain what prior work you want to use and how, and document that permission. When in doubt, treat each assignment as requiring entirely new work.

Section 40.8

Avoiding Source Misuse

Avoiding source misuse is not a matter of following a complex checklist after you write. It is a matter of building habits throughout the entire research and writing process. Students who plagiarize — deliberately or accidentally — typically made a process error long before they submitted their paper: they did not track sources carefully, did not label notes clearly, did not verify citations, or cut corners under deadline pressure.

The solution is not to be more careful at the end. The solution is to be careful at every step.

Six Habits for Honest Research
  1. 1. Record source information immediately. Write the author, title, publication, date, and page number the moment you encounter the source — not later.
  2. 2. Label quotations in your notes. Every direct quotation must be marked with quotation marks and a page number, with no exceptions.
  3. 3. Separate source material from your own thinking. Use labels such as Source says: and My analysis: to protect yourself when moving from notes to draft.
  4. 4. Read, close, write. When paraphrasing, close the source before writing. Read the passage, understand it, put the source away, and express the idea in your own words.
  5. 5. Verify before submitting. Confirm every source-derived claim has a citation, every citation is accurate, every quotation matches the original word for word, and every paraphrase is genuinely your own language.
  6. 6. Ask when uncertain. Instructors and librarians are available to help with citation requirements, prior-work reuse, and collaboration rules. Asking is never the wrong choice.
A Final Principle

Strong researchers do not merely try to avoid plagiarism. They treat honest attribution as a positive value — a way of honoring the intellectual work of others, building a trustworthy record of their own thinking, and participating honestly in the scholarly conversation. Citation is not a bureaucratic requirement. It is how knowledge earns credibility.

Misinformation Alert

Common myths about plagiarism circulate widely among students. Each one is inaccurate and can lead to real academic consequences.

Myth 1
"If I change enough words, it's mine."

Changing words at the surface level does not convert a passage to your own work. True paraphrase requires understanding the idea and expressing it independently, not substituting synonyms while preserving the original structure. Patchwriting is plagiarism — with or without a citation.

Myth 2
"I don't need to cite it if it's from the internet."

Web sources — articles, blog posts, videos, images, databases — require citation just as print sources do. The medium does not affect the attribution requirement.

Myth 3
"I don't need to cite common knowledge."

Common knowledge is narrower than most students assume. A specific research finding, a statistic, an argument made by a scholar, or a data point from a report is not common knowledge, even if widely repeated.

Myth 4
"I found it in multiple places, so I don't need to cite it."

Encountering information in multiple sources does not eliminate the citation requirement. Each piece of information still originated somewhere. Cite the source you actually consulted.

Myth 5
"My instructor can't tell."

Instructors develop sensitivity to shifts in writing style, vocabulary, and tone. Plagiarism detection software has also become sophisticated enough to identify patchwriting, not just direct copying.

Myth 6
"AI wrote it, not me, so I'm not responsible for its errors."

If your submission contains AI-generated text — including AI-generated citations — you are responsible for its accuracy. "The AI did it" is not an accepted defense in academic integrity proceedings.

Myth 7
"Self-plagiarism isn't real plagiarism."

Submitting your own prior work without permission misrepresents the work you have done for the current assignment and is subject to institutional consequences, regardless of definitional debates.

Central Principle

Do not merely find information. Earn trust in information. Honest attribution reflects a researcher's genuine relationship to knowledge: curiosity, precision, humility, and respect for the intellectual labor of others.

Chapter 39 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Imagine reading a paper that makes a striking claim: that a particular teaching method doubles student retention, or that a historical figure said something you had never heard before, or that a new study overturns decades of scientific consensus. How would you know whether to believe it? You would want to find the original source, check the evidence, and judge for yourself.

That is exactly what citation makes possible. Without citations, a paper is a collection of assertions. With citations, it becomes a documented argument that readers can evaluate, challenge, follow up on, and build from.

This chapter teaches citation not as a bureaucratic formality but as the practice of making your evidence visible, your reasoning checkable, and your intellectual debts honest. Citation is a system built on credit, verification, accountability, and trust — and learning to cite accurately and consistently is one of the most durable intellectual skills a researcher can build.

Research Skill Focus

Understanding citation as a system that holds academic work together — recognizing why credit, evidence trails, reader help, and academic trust all depend on accurate, complete, and honest citation practice.

Why This Matters

Citation matters in every field. Historians cite documents and archives to show that their interpretations are grounded in evidence. Scientists cite prior studies to situate their experiments within existing knowledge. Lawyers cite cases and statutes to establish legal precedent. Journalists cite sources so that readers can verify what they report. For students at every level, learning to cite accurately and consistently shapes how you read sources, how you take notes, how you draft arguments, and how you communicate your thinking to others. This chapter explains the full purpose of citation and prepares you for the specific mechanics chapters that follow — MLA, APA, and Chicago style in Chapter 41, in-text citation placement in Chapter 42, final source lists in Chapter 43, citation tools in Chapter 44, and responsible AI use in Chapter 45.

Sections 39.1 – 39.4
Credit, Verification, Reader Service, and Trust
Why Citation Exists and What It Accomplishes
Section 39.1

Citation as Credit

Every time you use an idea, a fact, a quotation, a statistic, or an argument from another source, you are borrowing someone else's intellectual work. Citation is the mechanism for acknowledging that borrowing honestly.

This is not merely a politeness convention. Intellectual work takes time, effort, creativity, and expertise. When a researcher spends years designing studies and analyzing data, when a historian spends months in an archive reading primary documents, when a journalist spends weeks investigating a story — the knowledge they produce has real value. Using that knowledge without acknowledgment is a kind of theft. It presents the effort of others as if it were your own.

Level of CreditWhat It AcknowledgesWhy It Matters
Individual creditThe author's contribution, named in the recordRecognizes the person whose effort and expertise produced the work
Institutional creditThe university, agency, or organization behind the researchProvides context for understanding how and where research was conducted
Disciplinary creditThe intellectual genealogy of an idea within a fieldMaps how knowledge builds cumulatively from prior work
Historical creditThe permanent, traceable record of who said what and whenAllows future readers to follow the chain of citations back to original sources

When students skip citations because they consider the task tedious or unimportant, they are participating in a small act of intellectual dishonesty. When they learn to cite carefully, they are joining a tradition of honest intellectual exchange that sustains the entire enterprise of knowledge production.

Section 39.2

Citation as Evidence Trail

A citation does not just acknowledge a source — it invites verification. Every citation says, in effect: "Here is where I got this. You can go look for yourself." This invitation is the foundation of academic trust. The reader of a well-cited paper can follow every significant claim back to its source, evaluate the quality of that source, judge whether the claim was accurately represented, and decide whether the evidence actually supports the argument being made.

Why the Evidence Trail Matters
Four functions of a visible citation trail
  • ErrorsResearch involves interpretation, and interpretation can go wrong. A researcher may misread a statistic, misquote a sentence, or draw a conclusion a study does not support. Citations let readers check.
  • ChallengeAcademic progress depends on disagreement. When a citation is visible, another researcher can examine the same source and challenge the interpretation.
  • TracingA claim may travel through layers of summary before reaching a paper. Visible citations let a reader trace a corrupted claim back to where it went wrong.
  • ContextA statistic or quotation pulled out of context can mean something different from what it meant originally. A citation leads the reader back to the full context.
Principle: Claims are hypotheses until they can be traced to evidence. Citation keeps the trail open.
Section 39.3

Citation as Reader Help

Citation also serves a practical function for readers who want to learn more. A well-constructed works cited list or bibliography is itself a research tool — a map of the intellectual landscape around a topic. When you finish reading a strong article closely related to your research question, many experienced researchers immediately scan the bibliography. It is one of the fastest ways to identify important prior work, influential sources, and related arguments you had not yet encountered.

This function shapes how citations should be constructed. A citation containing complete, accurate source information is genuinely useful. A citation that is incomplete, incorrect, or vague is not.

How Readers Use Citations

Readers use citations to locate sources in full, assess source quality quickly, identify gaps an author has missed, and discover further reading. A citation missing a volume number, page range, DOI, or publisher name may make a source genuinely difficult to find — even with good database access.

This is why the works cited list or bibliography is part of the intellectual contribution of a paper, not an afterthought. Building it well takes the same care as building the argument itself.

Section 39.4

Citation as Academic Trust

The academic system — publishing, peer review, citation, professional recognition — runs on a foundation of trust. Researchers trust that other researchers have conducted their work honestly, reported their findings accurately, and cited their sources faithfully. When any part of that trust breaks down, the damage can be severe. Fabricated data poisons the literature it enters. Plagiarized work displaces the effort of the original researcher. A misrepresented citation misleads future researchers who build on it. These failures are not minor technical infractions — they are breaches that undermine the reliability of knowledge.

Citation practices are one of the most visible signals of whether a researcher can be trusted. For students, developing citation habits is developing the habits of a trustworthy researcher.

Habits of a Trustworthy Researcher

Cite what you actually used. Do not add sources you did not actually consult, and do not cite a source found only secondhand without locating and reading the original.

Represent sources accurately. Do not quote out of context, summarize in distorting ways, or imply a source supports a claim it does not.

Use complete citation information. Do not guess at dates, page numbers, or author names. Check.

Cite consistently. Follow your required style guide throughout a paper. Inconsistency signals carelessness.

Academic trust is built one honest citation at a time. Over a career, the pattern accumulates into reputation. The discipline of citation — learned early and practiced carefully — is not a burden. It is one of the clearest ways to demonstrate intellectual seriousness.

Section 39.5

Common Knowledge

One question students often ask is: does everything require a citation? The answer is no — but knowing what does and what does not is a skill that requires judgment. Common knowledge refers to information that is widely known, not associated with any particular source, and accepted as established fact without needing to be credited to anyone.

Example 39.5 — Common Knowledge or Not?
Testing the boundary
  • CommonWorld War II ended in 1945. Water freezes at 32°F at standard pressure. Shakespeare wrote Hamlet.These appear in countless sources, are not tied to a single discoverer, and citing them would clutter the paper unnecessarily.
  • Not CommonA specific statistic from a study; an original interpretation; a specialized fact known only within a field; any quotation or paraphrase.These require citation because the reader needs to know which source produced them.
Test: would I have had to look this up in a specific study, report, database, or book to know it? If yes, cite it.
When in Doubt, Cite

Over-citing is rarely a significant problem. Under-citing is. A student who cites a few things that turn out to be common knowledge has demonstrated care. A student who fails to cite information that required a source has committed an error that, depending on context, may constitute plagiarism. The common knowledge exception also varies by field and audience: something common knowledge among biologists may not be common knowledge for a general reader.

Section 39.6

When to Cite

The following situations always require citation. This list is not exhaustive, but it covers the most common cases students encounter.

SituationWhy Citation Is Required
Direct quotationsExact words — even a short distinctive phrase — require quotation marks and a citation, with no exceptions
ParaphraseRestating an idea in your own words does not remove the need to cite; the idea still came from somewhere else
SummaryCondensing a source's argument or findings still credits intellectual content that belongs to the source
Facts, data, statisticsNumbers come from specific sources; readers need to know which ones
Ideas and argumentsBuilding on a theoretical framework or analytical model requires citing its origin, even when disagreeing with it
Images, tables, charts, figuresVisual material you did not create requires a citation, typically in a caption or table note
Personal communicationsEmails, interviews, and letters may require citation under some style guides even though unpublished
Works consulted but not citedSome assignments or style guides ask for a full bibliography beyond directly cited sources
Study Note — Paraphrase Still Requires Citation

This is one of the most misunderstood citation rules. Students sometimes believe that paraphrasing eliminates the need to cite. It does not. Changing the wording does not change where the idea came from.

Section 39.7

What Citation Must Record

Every citation, regardless of style, must capture enough information for a reader to locate the original source. The specific elements required vary by source type and citation style, but the following core elements apply to most sources.

ElementWhat to Record
Author or creatorPerson, multiple people, organization, government agency, or corporate author
TitleArticle title; book title; chapter title plus book title for edited collections; page and site title for web pages
Publication informationPublisher and city for books; journal, volume, issue, and pages for articles; URL and access date for websites
Source identifiersDOI when available (preferred over URL for stability); ISBN for books in some contexts
Edition or versionEdition number for books; version or update date for websites, databases, and encyclopedias
Location within sourcePage numbers; paragraph numbers, headings, or timestamps for sources without pages
Record It Now, Not Later

When you take notes on a source, record all citation information completely and accurately before you close the source. Trying to reconstruct citation information later — hunting for a page number after returning a book, or after a website has been updated — is far harder than capturing it the first time.

Section 39.8

Citation Across Disciplines

Different academic fields have developed different citation conventions, and these conventions are not arbitrary. They reflect what each field considers most important.

FieldWhat It PrioritizesCommon Styles
HumanitiesAuthor's name and exact page reference, for precise textual locationMLA, Chicago Notes-Bibliography
Social SciencesDate of publication alongside author, since findings can become outdatedAPA, Chicago Author-Date
Natural SciencesCompact numbered citation to reduce in-text interruption in vast literaturesNumbered reference systems
LawPrecise identification of codes, cases, and regulationsBluebook and similar systems
EngineeringField-specific technical referencingIEEE or field-specific conventions
JournalismNamed sourcing within prose rather than formal citationIn-text attribution, hyperlinks

As a student, you will most often be asked to use MLA, APA, or Chicago. You may occasionally encounter field-specific styles in upper-division courses, internships, or professional work. The ability to adapt to a required style guide — and to use the guide's own documentation to check your citations — is a practical and transferable skill.

Core Principle

The principles of citation are universal even when the formats differ. Credit the source. Record enough information for verification. Represent sources accurately. These principles do not change across style systems, even when punctuation, capitalization, and arrangement do.

Building Citation Habits That Last

Learning citation is not just about following rules for a single assignment. It is about developing habits that will serve you throughout your academic and professional life. The following habits, practiced consistently from the beginning of any research project, will save you time and prevent errors.

Five Habits That Save Time and Prevent Errors

Record citation information before you need it. The moment you decide a source is useful, record author, title, publication information, date, page numbers, URL or DOI. Do not close the source until your notes are complete.

Keep notes organized by source. A separate page, card, or file per source prevents mixing up which idea came from which place.

Use a citation manager for larger projects. Zotero, Mendeley, or RefWorks can store sources and generate formatted citations — but always verify what they generate. Chapter 44 covers citation tools and their limits.

Check citations against the source before submitting. Verify spelling, title completeness, date accuracy, and matching page numbers.

Ask for help when unsure. The MGU Library, writing center staff, and your instructor are faster and more reliable than guessing.

Chapter 39 — Applied Practice

Search Practice

Citation begins with finding sources. Practice the following:

  1. Search the MGU Library encyclopedia for the topic you are currently researching. Locate the encyclopedia entry and identify every piece of information you would need to cite it: the author (or note if it is unsigned), the article title, the encyclopedia name, the publication date, the platform, and the URL or DOI if available.
  2. Search an academic database for a peer-reviewed article on your topic. When you find an article, locate the citation export function (often labeled "Cite," "Export," or indicated by a quotation mark icon). Export the citation in MLA and APA formats. Compare the two formats: where do the author's name, the date, the article title, and the journal name appear in each?
  3. Visit a government website relevant to your field (such as the U.S. Census Bureau, the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, or the Department of Education). Find a specific report or data table. Record all the citation information you would need: who produced it, when, what it is called, and where it is located. Note whether the website provides a suggested citation format.

Chapter 39 — Applied Practice

Source Check

Source: A student is writing a paper on the relationship between social media use and adolescent mental health. She finds the following passage in an article:

"Among adolescents aged 13 to 17, daily social media use exceeding three hours was associated with significantly higher rates of reported anxiety and depression compared to adolescents who used social media for less than one hour per day."

She wants to use this finding in her paper. Apply the MGU Source Evaluation Model:

CriterionApplication
RelevanceYes — this directly addresses her topic
AuthorityCheck who conducted the study, their credentials, their institution, and whether the article was peer-reviewed
EvidenceLocate the original study, if this article summarizes another, to confirm the finding is accurately reported
AccuracyCheck whether the numbers and characterization of "significant" findings accurately reflect the original research
PurposeNote whether the publication has an advocacy position that might affect how findings are framed
CurrencySocial media research moves fast — check the publication date and whether more recent research updates this finding
ContextRead the full article to understand methodology, sample size, limitations, and what should not be concluded
Citation Task

She should record the full citation information for this article before closing it. If this article is itself citing a study, she should locate that original study and cite it directly, rather than citing a secondary summary.

Chapter 39 — Misinformation Alert

Citations Can Be Falsified or Fabricated

One of the most dangerous citation failures is the fake citation — a reference to a source that does not exist, or that does exist but does not say what the citing author claims.

Risk 1
Deliberate Fabrication

A writer invents a citation to make an unsupported claim look documented. This is a serious form of academic dishonesty.

Risk 2
AI Hallucination

Large language models can generate plausible-sounding but entirely fictional citations: real-seeming author names, journal titles, volume numbers, and publication years, attached to articles that do not exist. Students who use AI-generated text without verification may unknowingly include fabricated citations in their work. Chapter 45 addresses this in detail.

Risk 3
Cascading Misrepresentation

A writer cites a secondary source that inaccurately summarizes a study. Other writers cite the secondary source and repeat the inaccurate claim. Over time, the inaccuracy becomes widely repeated even though the original study said something different.

Risk 4
Out-of-Context Citation

A writer cites a real, existing source but quotes or summarizes it in a way that misrepresents what it actually says. The citation is technically real but functionally misleading.

Protect Yourself

Always locate and read sources before citing them. Verify AI-generated citations by searching for the source in a library database or through a DOI search before including them in any paper. When a claim seems surprising or important, trace it to the original source rather than accepting a chain of secondary summaries. Before submitting, check every citation in your own paper by opening the source and confirming that the author, title, date, and content match what you have recorded.

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 39.1Citation is the mechanism for honestly acknowledging borrowed intellectual work, operating at individual, institutional, disciplinary, and historical levels of credit.
  • Sec 39.2Citation creates an evidence trail that allows readers to catch errors, challenge arguments, trace claims through layers of summary, and recover the original context of evidence.
  • Sec 39.3Citation serves readers directly by helping them locate sources, assess quality, identify gaps, and discover further reading through well-built bibliographies.
  • Sec 39.4Citation practices signal whether a researcher can be trusted. Honest, accurate, and consistent citation builds the trust on which the entire academic system depends.
  • Sec 39.5Common knowledge — widely known facts not tied to a particular source — does not require citation, but the boundary requires judgment and varies by field and audience.
  • Sec 39.6Quotation, paraphrase, summary, specific data, ideas and arguments, images, and sometimes personal communications all require citation, regardless of wording changes.
  • Sec 39.7Every citation must record enough information — author, title, publication information, identifiers, edition, and location — for a reader to locate the original source.
  • Sec 39.8Citation conventions vary across disciplines based on what each field prioritizes, but the underlying principles of credit, verification, and accuracy remain universal.

Key Terms

citation
The practice of identifying and documenting the sources used in a research project, including the author, title, publication information, and location of each source.
common knowledge
Information widely known and available in general reference sources that is not associated with any particular researcher or source and therefore does not require citation.
works cited
The list of sources cited in a paper, formatted according to a specific citation style. Used in MLA.
references
The list of sources cited in a paper, formatted according to a specific citation style. Used in APA.
bibliography
A list of sources consulted during research, which may include sources consulted but not directly cited. Used in some Chicago formats and field-specific contexts.
in-text citation
A brief citation placed within the body of a paper that identifies the source of a quotation, paraphrase, or summary and directs the reader to the full citation.
parenthetical citation
An in-text citation placed within parentheses, typically including the author's name and a page number (MLA) or a year (APA).
footnote
A citation or explanatory note placed at the bottom of the page on which the cited material appears. Common in Chicago Notes-Bibliography style.
endnote
A citation or explanatory note collected at the end of a chapter or paper rather than at the bottom of the page.
DOI
Digital Object Identifier — a stable, permanent identifier for a digital document, preferred over URLs because it does not change when content moves.
citation style
A standardized system for formatting citations, specifying how author names, titles, dates, and other elements should be arranged and punctuated.
fabricated citation
A citation to a source that does not exist or that does not say what the citing author claims — a serious form of academic dishonesty that can also occur inadvertently in AI-generated text.

Review Questions

  1. Explain the difference between citation as credit and citation as evidence trail. Why are both functions important?
  2. A student says, "I only need to cite things when I quote directly. If I put it in my own words, it's mine." Is this student correct? Explain why or why not, using specific examples.
  3. What is common knowledge? Give three examples of common knowledge in your field of study. Give three examples from the same field that would not be common knowledge and would require citation.
  4. Why do different academic disciplines use different citation styles? What does each style prioritize, and why does that match the needs of its field?
  5. What is a fabricated citation, and how can it appear in academic work — including work that involves AI tools? What steps can researchers take to protect against fabricated citations?
  6. A works cited list at the end of a student's paper contains twelve sources, but only seven sources are cited in the body of the paper. What problem does this suggest? What problem would it suggest if the situation were reversed — seven sources cited in the body but only five in the works cited list?
  7. You find an important statistic in a newspaper article. The newspaper article says the statistic comes from a report by a federal government agency. Should you cite the newspaper article, the government report, or both? Explain your reasoning.
  8. Using the MGU Source Evaluation Model, explain how you would evaluate a source specifically for citation purposes — as opposed to evaluating it for general research usefulness.

Research Practice

Activity 1Citation Audit
  1. Find a research paper you have previously written for any class — ideally one that included citations. Count the number of in-text citations and the number of entries in your works cited or references list.
  2. Check whether every in-text citation corresponds to an entry in the list, and whether every entry corresponds to at least one in-text citation.
  3. Choose three citations and verify them against the actual sources: are the author, title, date, and other elements recorded accurately?
  4. Identify one citation that could be improved. Describe the error and how you would fix it.
Activity 2Common Knowledge Judgment
  1. The average temperature of the human body is approximately 98.6 degrees Fahrenheit.
  2. A 2022 study found that students who received feedback within 24 hours of submitting assignments scored 18% higher on subsequent assessments than those who received feedback after one week.
  3. Abraham Lincoln was the 16th president of the United States.
  4. The poverty rate in rural counties of a particular state was reported by the U.S. Census Bureau to be 19.3% in 2021.
  5. The earth orbits the sun.
  6. A political scientist argues that proportional representation systems tend to produce more diverse legislatures than winner-take-all systems.
  7. Water is composed of hydrogen and oxygen.
  8. A recent survey by a nonprofit organization found that 62% of college students reported difficulty affording food in the past month.
Activity 3Citation Trail
  1. Choose a claim from a secondary source — a textbook, a news article, or a review article — that interests you and cites an original study or report.
  2. Identify and locate the original source using the MGU Library, then read the relevant section.
  3. Compare: does the secondary source accurately represent what the original source says? Does anything get lost, simplified, or changed in the summary?
  4. Write a brief note (150–250 words) describing what you found.

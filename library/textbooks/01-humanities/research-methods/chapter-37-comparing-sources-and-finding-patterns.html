Chapter 37 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Imagine two students working on the same research question: "How does social media use affect adolescent mental health?" The first student reads five sources, summarizes each one in a paragraph, and moves directly to writing. Her paper reads like a tour of five separate opinions. The second student reads the same five sources, then asks where they agree, where they disagree, what kinds of evidence each one offers, and whether any patterns emerge. His paper reads like an analysis.

The difference between these two students is not the number of sources. It is what they did with the sources once they had them. This chapter teaches source comparison and pattern recognition — the bridge between collecting evidence and understanding it.

Research Skill Focus

Comparing sources across multiple readings to identify agreement, disagreement, emphasis, omission, evidence differences, method differences, gaps, contradictions, and the recurring patterns that give research its analytical depth.

Why This Matters

Comparing sources transforms research from collection into understanding. It is the cognitive work that turns a pile of notes into a coherent intellectual position. It is also what separates research from opinion: when you compare sources carefully, you stop asking "What do I think?" and start asking "What does the evidence show, where does it agree, where does it disagree, and what do those patterns mean?" That shift from personal impression to evidential reasoning is one of the most important intellectual moves a student researcher can make — and one of the most transferable skills you will carry into medicine, law, policy, journalism, business, and nearly any other field that requires weighing competing studies, reports, or expert opinions.

Sections 37.1 – 37.3
Research as Comparison — Agreement and Disagreement
Collection vs. Comparison · Significance of Agreement · Four Types of Disagreement
Section 37.1

Research as Comparison

Research is not primarily a collection activity. It is, at its core, a comparison activity. You are not simply gathering everything written on a topic. You are gathering sources so that you can compare what they say, understand where the evidence is strong or weak, and form a responsible conclusion about what is known, what is debated, and what remains unclear.

When you collect sources without comparing them, you have information. When you compare sources carefully, you begin to have understanding. The difference matters enormously.

Comparison requires reading sources with specific questions in mind: What claim does this source make? What evidence does it offer for that claim? How does that claim relate to what other sources say? Where do sources agree? Where do they disagree? What does each source emphasize, and what does each omit? What patterns emerge across multiple sources? What is still unclear, contradicted, or unresolved?

These questions transform reading from passive absorption into active inquiry. You are not simply taking in information; you are interrogating it against other information.

ActivityWhat It Involves
SummaryRecording what one source says
ComparisonPlacing two or more sources in relationship to one another
Pattern recognitionIdentifying recurring claims, gaps, or contradictions across sources
SynthesisExplaining what patterns mean and what the evidence as a whole shows

Chapter 36 covered organizing sources by theme. This chapter builds directly on that skill. You now have organized notes. The task is to read across those notes and begin comparing what sources say.

Why Comparison Is Intellectually Difficult

Source comparison is genuinely challenging. It requires holding multiple positions in mind simultaneously, recognizing similarities and differences that are not always obvious, and tolerating uncertainty — because sources often disagree, and sometimes neither is clearly right. It also requires resisting the temptation to simply pick the source that confirms what you already believe.

These difficulties are reasons to practice comparison deliberately and systematically, not reasons to avoid it. The frameworks in this chapter give you specific questions to ask and structures to use so that comparison becomes a learnable skill.

Section 37.2

Where Sources Agree

When sources that were produced independently — by different researchers, using different methods, at different times — reach similar conclusions, that convergence is meaningful. Agreement across independent sources is one of the most powerful forms of evidence in research.

Agreement does not make a claim automatically true. But it does strengthen the case that a claim is well-supported. The more independent the agreeing sources are — different institutions, different methodologies, different time periods, different disciplinary perspectives — the more significant the agreement becomes.

Agreement is not always obvious. Sources may reach the same conclusion while using different language, data, examples, or analytical frameworks. Part of your task is to recognize agreement even when sources do not use identical words. Look for shared conclusions, overlapping evidence, consistent framing, and compatible recommendations.

Example 37.2 — What Strong Agreement Looks Like
Early childhood reading programs and long-term literacy
  • Source 1A longitudinal study following students for ten years concludes that students who participated in intensive early reading programs showed significantly higher reading proficiency through middle school.
  • Source 2A meta-analysis reviewing forty earlier studies concludes that early reading interventions produce consistent positive effects on literacy across multiple demographic groups.
  • Source 3A state education agency policy report concludes that districts with early literacy funding show better third-grade reading benchmarks than comparable districts without such funding.
  • Source 4An overview article in an education research journal summarizes the field as showing broad consensus around the value of structured early literacy instruction.
Principle: These sources use different methodological traditions — a single longitudinal study, a meta-analysis, a policy report, and a literature review — yet converge on a similar conclusion. That convergence is significant because it does not depend on any single study or perspective.
Agreement Is a Starting Point, Not an Ending Point

Finding agreement is valuable, but it does not complete your analysis. Ask: What is the quality of the agreeing sources? Do they share a common methodological flaw? Are they all drawing from the same original data, which would make their agreement less independent than it appears? Are there dissenting voices worth examining? Agreement sets a baseline; the rest of your comparison work refines and complicates it.

Section 37.3

Where Sources Disagree

Sources disagree. This is not a problem with your research — it is a feature of the research landscape that you must learn to navigate rather than avoid. Disagreement exists because researchers use different methods that reveal different aspects of a phenomenon, analyze different populations or time periods, operate from different theoretical frameworks, or because evidence genuinely points in multiple directions. Some questions are inherently contested — not because evidence is absent, but because values, interpretations, and priorities legitimately differ.

Understanding why sources disagree is often as important as noticing that they disagree. Not all disagreement is the same kind.

Type of DisagreementWhat It MeansWhat to Do
FactualSources report different data or findingsEvaluate methodological quality; look for replication
InterpretiveSources read the same facts differentlyIdentify the assumptions behind each interpretation
Value-basedSources prioritize different outcomesAcknowledge the values at stake; do not present as a factual dispute
Scope-basedFindings apply differently in different contextsSpecify the conditions under which each finding holds
Disagreement Type
Disagreement About Facts

Sources may disagree about what actually happened, what the data shows, or what a study found. This can often be partially resolved by examining the quality of each source's evidence, the size and representativeness of samples, the rigor of methods, and whether independent replication has occurred.

Disagreement Type
Disagreement About Interpretation

Sources may agree on the facts but disagree about what those facts mean. Two historians might agree an event occurred but disagree about whether economic pressures or political decisions caused it primarily.

Disagreement Type
Disagreement About Values

Some disagreements are fundamentally about priorities rather than evidence — should a city prioritize affordable housing or green space? Evidence can inform these trade-offs but not resolve them.

Disagreement Type
Disagreement About Scope

Sources may disagree about how broadly a finding applies. A study showing an intervention works in urban schools is not necessarily contradicted by one showing weaker effects in rural settings — both may reflect genuine variation in context.

Example 37.3 — Engaging Disagreement Productively
Dissenting voices in the early reading research

Suppose that while most sources agree early reading interventions produce positive effects, you also find a study reporting more modest effects in certain low-income urban districts, and a critique arguing that some widely cited studies have methodological limitations in how literacy outcomes were measured.

A researcher who ignores these dissenting voices produces an oversimplified picture. A researcher who acknowledges them, explains what they contribute, and assesses their relative weight produces a more honest and rigorous analysis.
Sections 37.4 – 37.5
Evidence and Method as Sources of Difference
Evidence Is Not Neutral · Major Methodological Traditions
Section 37.4

Different Evidence, Different Conclusions

Two sources can reach different conclusions not because one is right and one is wrong, but because each is looking at different evidence. Different types of evidence illuminate different aspects of a question. When you compare sources, pay close attention not only to what they conclude but to what kind of evidence they used to reach those conclusions.

A large-scale quantitative study measuring outcomes across ten thousand students tells you something different from an ethnographic study closely observing fifteen children in one classroom. Both offer legitimate evidence, but they are answering somewhat different versions of the research question.

Evidence TypeWhat It Tends to ShowWhat It May Miss
Large quantitative studiesBroad patterns across many casesIndividual variation, context, mechanism
Small qualitative studiesDepth, context, lived experienceGeneralizability across larger populations
Historical documentsWhat happened and how it was understood at the timeLong-term outcomes, alternative perspectives
Expert opinionExperienced professional judgmentEmpirical data, systematic evidence
Experimental dataCausal relationships under controlled conditionsReal-world applicability outside the experimental setting
Survey dataSelf-reported attitudes, beliefs, and behaviorsWhat people actually do versus what they say they do
Reading Evidence Differences Carefully

When two sources reach different conclusions, trace the difference back to evidence type before assuming one source is simply better. Sometimes the right conclusion is: "Source A, using large-scale outcome data, finds X. Source B, using in-depth interviews, finds Y. These findings are not contradictory — they describe different dimensions of the same phenomenon."

Example 37.4 — Peer Tutoring and Evidence Type
How different evidence produces a richer, not a contradictory, picture

Large survey studies often find that students who participate in peer tutoring report higher satisfaction and more confidence. Controlled experimental studies show more mixed results when looking specifically at grade outcomes. Interview-based studies reveal that benefits depend on how programs are structured, the subject matter, and the tutor-student relationship.

Conclusion: "Peer tutoring appears to consistently benefit student confidence and satisfaction. Effects on grades are more variable and depend on program structure. Students' own accounts suggest the interpersonal dynamics of the relationship significantly shape their experience." This conclusion is richer than any single source could provide — and is only possible through comparison.
Section 37.5

Different Methods

Research is a family of activities using different methods — and different methods reveal different things. Method includes how a researcher gathered data, what kinds of sources they analyzed, how large their sample was, how they defined their variables, what controls they used, and how they analyzed what they found. Two studies asking roughly the same question may reach different conclusions because they used different methods — and both findings can be valid within the scope of what each method can reveal.

MethodStrengthLimit
ExperimentalPowerful for establishing causation through random assignment and controlled variablesControlled conditions may not reflect real-world complexity
Observational / SurveyCaptures real-world behavior and attitudesCannot establish causation as definitively as experiments
QualitativeIlluminates context, meaning, and processSmall numbers of cases; does not generalize statistically
Historical / ArchivalEssential for understanding historical questionsLimited by survival and completeness of records
Computational / Data AnalysisIdentifies patterns across enormous datasetsDepends heavily on data quality and representativeness
Meta-analysis / Systematic ReviewProvides the most comprehensive picture of a field's knowledgeDepends on the quality of the studies it reviews
Example 37.5 — Comparing Methods on Mindfulness in Schools
Whether mindfulness programs reduce student anxiety
  • RCTStudents assigned to a mindfulness program show measurably lower self-reported anxiety scores than control students over eight weeks.
  • QualitativeInterviews show students experience the program very differently depending on how teachers present it — some find it helpful, others uncomfortable.
  • SurveyMost school counselors believe mindfulness programs have some positive effect, but many note implementation challenges that undermine effectiveness.
A researcher who understands these methodological differences can use all three sources without distorting any of them — avoiding both overclaiming (treating the RCT as proof it always works) and underclaiming (treating qualitative variation as proof it never works).
Sections 37.6 – 37.8
Gaps, Contradictions, and Pattern-Finding
Missing Perspectives · Genuine Contradiction · Building a Pattern Map
Section 37.6

Gaps and Missing Perspectives

One of the most important things you can learn from comparing sources is not what they say, but what they leave out. Gaps in the research literature are meaningful signals about the limits of current knowledge, the populations and questions that have not been adequately studied, and the perspectives that have historically been excluded from academic conversation.

A gap may be a population rarely studied, a time period or geographic region lacking coverage, a perspective systematically excluded, a methodological approach not yet applied, or a dimension of a topic researchers have tended not to examine.

Gaps are visible only through comparison. If you have read only one source, you cannot see what is missing. Ask: Who is studied, and who is not? What time periods or places are represented, and which are absent? Are there stakeholders, disciplines, or communities that do not appear to have been consulted? Do the sources themselves acknowledge limitations or call for further research?

Noting Gaps Carefully

Do not assume absence means a question has been answered negatively. Absence means the question has not been adequately studied — those are different things. In your writing you might say: "The research on this question has focused primarily on college-aged populations. Less is known about how these effects play out among adults in the workforce."

Missing Perspectives as an Ethical Issue

In fields such as history, social science, public health, and education, the absence of certain perspectives from the research literature is not only an intellectual limitation but an ethical one. Research conducted without affected communities' meaningful participation may be systematically distorted in ways that affect both its accuracy and its usefulness. When you identify missing perspectives, note them as invitations to ask whose knowledge counts and what a more complete research picture would require.

Section 37.7

Contradictions and Uncertainty

Disagreement is normal in research. Contradiction is a stronger and more specific condition. A contradiction exists when two sources make claims that cannot both be true — when Source A asserts X and Source B asserts not-X, using comparable populations, methods, and definitions.

True contradictions are rarer than they appear, because apparent contradictions often dissolve when you examine exactly what each source studied, how it defined its terms, and what kinds of evidence it used. But genuine contradictions do exist, and they signal that the evidence is genuinely ambiguous, that methodological limitations are affecting results, or that the question is more complex than it first appears.

Working Through a Genuine Contradiction

1. Check definitions — are key terms used the same way?  2. Check populations — are the same groups being studied?  3. Check methods — could methodological differences explain the contradiction?  4. Check timeframes — are conditions comparable across periods?  5. Check replication — has either finding been independently confirmed?  6. Consult secondary literature — has a review article already analyzed this contradiction?

Uncertainty Is Not Failure

Many students feel that acknowledging uncertainty makes their paper look weak. The opposite is true. Responsible researchers do not overclaim — they report what the evidence shows with appropriate confidence and honestly acknowledge what remains unclear, disputed, or unresolved. "Research on this question remains divided, with some studies finding X and others finding Y; the methodological differences between these studies suggest the answer may depend on [contextual factor]" is a stronger intellectual position than a confident but inaccurate overclaim.

The central principle of this textbook applies with particular force here: Do not merely find information. Earn trust in information. Earning that trust requires honesty about what the evidence does and does not establish.

Section 37.8

Pattern-Finding in Research

A pattern is a recurring feature that appears across multiple independent sources. Once you have compared sources carefully — noting agreement, disagreement, evidence type, method, and omission — you are in a position to identify the patterns running through your source collection. Patterns are not manufactured; you read carefully and repeatedly, asking what keeps appearing, what is consistently absent, and what relationships multiple sources observe.

Pattern TypeWhat It Tells You
Consensus patternMultiple independent sources reach similar conclusions using different methods — suggests robustness
Consistent qualification patternSources agree but consistently note conditions or exceptions — the finding is real but not universal
Persistent disagreement patternSources consistently disagree even after examining method — indicates genuine uncertainty
Coverage patternSources consistently address some dimensions and ignore others — reveals what the field has prioritized
Change-over-time patternEarlier and more recent sources differ — understanding may be evolving or conditions changing
Magnitude variation patternSources agree on direction but disagree on size of effect — often reflects methodological variation
Example 37.8 — Full Pattern-Finding Walkthrough
Urban green space and resident wellbeing — eight sources

Sources include a meta-analysis of thirty studies, a city-level quantitative study, a qualitative study of low-income neighborhoods, a public health equity report, a park-redesign study, a survey of urban planners, a historical study of park design, and a recent study addressing income variation.

  • ConsensusMultiple sources agree green space is associated with wellbeing benefits.
  • QualificationMultiple sources qualify this — benefits depend on safety, maintenance, accessibility, and design.
  • Coverage gapEquity and unequal distribution of green space are raised by some sources but rarely integrated into the wellbeing studies themselves.
  • Change over timeEarlier research treated green space as uniformly beneficial; recent sources add nuance about conditions.
  • UnresolvedWhether benefits stem from the physical environment, social activity, or psychological experience of nature remains unresolved.
Resulting synthesis sentence: "Research consistently finds associations between urban green space and resident wellbeing, but this relationship is not simple or universal. Safety, maintenance, and design significantly shape whether residents benefit, and equity concerns mean aggregate benefits mask significant variation by income and neighborhood." This is the work of comparison and pattern-finding — more honest and useful than any single source could provide.
Applied Practice
Search Practice, Source Check, and Research Notebook
Putting Comparison and Pattern-Finding into Action
Search Practice

Comparing Sources on a Live Research Question

Activity 37.AFour-Source Comparison
  1. Choose a topic. Select a topic relevant to one of your current courses — for example, effects of sleep deprivation on academic performance, the relationship between minimum wage increases and employment, whether standardized testing accurately measures learning, or how diet affects cardiovascular health.
  2. Find at least four sources. Use the MGU Library's encyclopedia, academic databases, or reference guides. Aim for variety: include at least one quantitative study, one qualitative or review source, and one source that takes a different position.
  3. Apply the comparison questions. For each pair of sources, write out: Where do they agree? Where do they disagree? What type of evidence does each use? What method does each use? What does each emphasize that the other does not? What does each leave out?
  4. Write a pattern summary. After comparing all sources, write a one-page summary addressing consensus, major disagreements, gaps, and what remains uncertain.
  5. Reflect. How did comparing sources change your understanding of the topic compared to what you understood after reading just one source?
Source Check

Applying Source Comparison to a Real Example

Suppose you are researching whether requiring students to use laptops in college classrooms improves learning outcomes, with three sources: a quantitative study comparing exam scores in laptop-permitted versus laptop-prohibited sections (200 students); a review article summarizing twenty studies finding highly variable effects depending on task structure; and a qualitative interview study of thirty students describing both benefits and distraction.

MGU Source Evaluation ModelAssessment
RelevanceAll three sources are directly relevant to the question
AuthorityPeer-reviewed journal study; review article; interview-based qualitative research
EvidenceQuantitative, synthesized quantitative, and qualitative evidence types
AccuracyNo implausible findings; the review and interviews add contextual accuracy missing from the single study alone
PurposeAll three appear to be scholarly research without obvious advocacy motivation
CurrencyLaptop research evolves rapidly; older studies may not reflect current technology
ContextThe single-course sample of 200 is limited; the review and interviews broaden the context
Pattern Summary
What the three sources show together

The weight of evidence suggests that unrestricted laptop use in classrooms is associated with reduced academic performance, primarily because many students use devices for off-task activities. However, structured use of laptops appears to have neutral or positive effects. The equity dimension — students who depend on laptops for accessibility — is largely absent from this literature, marking a clear gap.

Research Notebook

Recording Comparisons in Your Research Notes

Use the MGU Research Note Model as the foundation for individual source notes. When comparing sources, add a comparison layer on top of your individual notes.

Source: [Full citation] Summary: [What this source argues or finds] Key evidence: [What evidence does it offer] Quotation: [One key quote, with page or paragraph number] Paraphrase: [Key idea in your own words] Usefulness: [How does this source help your research] Limits: [What does this source not address or do well] Citation information: [All information needed for your citation]
Comparison note — [Date] Sources compared: [List by abbreviated title or author] Where they agree: Where they disagree: Evidence differences: Method differences: Gaps I notice: Contradictions or uncertainties: Patterns emerging: Questions this raises for my research:
Why Keep These Separate

Keeping comparison notes separate from individual source notes makes your thinking visible and builds the analytical foundation you will need for synthesis in Chapter 38.

Misinformation Alert

False Consensus and Cherry-Picked Agreement

Watch For This Pattern

One of the most common misinformation strategies is the false claim of scientific or expert consensus. A source may claim that "researchers agree" or "studies show" a particular position when the research picture is actually far more mixed or contested.

Check who is described as agreeing. "Scientists agree" is too vague to evaluate — which scientists, in which field, with what credentials, publishing where?

Check whether dissenting evidence exists. Genuine consensus does not mean unanimous agreement, but it does mean the preponderance of credible, peer-reviewed evidence points consistently in one direction.

Check whether agreement is independent. If multiple sources all draw on the same original dataset, their apparent agreement is far less significant than true convergence from independent sources.

Check who is funding research. Industry- or interest-funded studies are not automatically wrong, but they deserve additional scrutiny.

Be wary of cherry-picking. A source presenting a confident conclusion about a complex topic without acknowledging any contrary evidence is likely leaving something important out.

Apply the MGU Misinformation Check

Who made it? What is the claim? What evidence is offered? What is missing? Can another reliable source confirm it? Is the emotional reaction stronger than the evidence? Has the source been altered, misrepresented, or taken out of context?

Citation Check

Citing Multiple Sources for the Same Point

When multiple sources support the same claim, cite all of them — do not cite only one as though it were the only evidence. Citing multiple sources for a well-supported claim demonstrates that you are drawing on a broad evidence base, not a single study.

Citing Convergence and Disagreement
APA and MLA examples
  • APAMultiple studies have found associations between early childhood reading programs and improved literacy outcomes in elementary school (Andreassen & Bråten, 2011; Kim & Quinn, 2013; Lonigan et al., 2008).
  • MLAMultiple researchers have documented this relationship (Andreassen and Bråten 215; Kim and Quinn 430; Lonigan et al. 12).
  • WeakResearch has examined the effects of peer tutoring on student achievement (Adams, 2018; Bell, 2020; Cruz, 2021).This implies false agreement when sources actually differ.
  • StrongerResearch on the effects of peer tutoring on student achievement shows mixed results. While some studies find significant improvements in test scores (Adams, 2018; Bell, 2020), others report more limited effects that may depend on implementation quality and subject matter (Cruz, 2021).This accurately signals disagreement.
Common errors: omitting sources used for comparison, attributing findings to the wrong source, misrepresenting a source's qualified position, and stacking citations without explaining how the sources relate to one another.

Research Checklist

Chapter 37 Research Checklist

Comparing Sources and Finding Patterns
Use this checklist as you practice comparing sources on your current research project.
Preparation
  • I have taken individual source notes (using the MGU Research Note Model) for each source I am comparing.
  • I have organized my sources by theme, as practiced in Chapter 36.
  • I have a dedicated comparison notes section in my research notebook.
Agreement and Disagreement
  • I have identified where multiple sources reach similar conclusions and whether they are independent of one another.
  • I have identified where sources disagree and determined whether the disagreement is factual, interpretive, value-based, or scope-based.
  • I have not simply ignored sources that disagree with my preferred position.
Evidence and Method
  • I have noted what type of evidence and what method each source uses.
  • I have considered what each evidence type and method can and cannot show.
  • I have avoided treating all sources as directly comparable when their evidence or methods differ significantly.
Gaps, Contradictions, and Patterns
  • I have identified populations, perspectives, or questions absent from my sources.
  • I have examined apparent contradictions carefully before concluding they are genuine, and noted real uncertainty accurately.
  • I have written a pattern summary addressing consensus, disagreements, gaps, and uncertainty, and I am prepared to explain these patterns in synthesis.
Chapter 37 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary and Review

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 37.1Research is fundamentally a comparison activity. Collecting sources is preparation for research; comparing sources is research. Summary describes what one source says; comparison places multiple sources in relationship.
  • Sec 37.2Agreement across independent sources strengthens the case that a finding is well-supported. The strength of agreement depends on the independence of the sources, the quality of their evidence, and whether they share methodological flaws.
  • Sec 37.3Disagreement is normal and informative. Factual, interpretive, value-based, and scope-based disagreement each call for a different response from the researcher.
  • Sec 37.4Sources using different types of evidence are often answering related but somewhat different questions. Comparing them requires understanding what each evidence type can and cannot show.
  • Sec 37.5Methodological differences between sources frequently explain why they reach different conclusions. Understanding method allows more precise and honest comparison.
  • Sec 37.6What is absent from the research literature is as informative as what is present. Identifying gaps accurately marks the boundaries of what the evidence actually shows.
  • Sec 37.7Apparent contradictions often dissolve under examination; genuine contradictions indicate real uncertainty that responsible researchers acknowledge rather than suppress.
  • Sec 37.8Careful comparison reveals patterns — consensus, qualification, persistent disagreement, coverage, change-over-time, and magnitude variation — that prepare you for synthesis in Chapter 38.
Central Principle

Do not merely find information. Earn trust in information. Earning that trust requires honest comparison, accurate pattern-finding, and intellectual humility about what the evidence does and does not establish.

Review Questions

  1. What is the difference between summarizing sources and comparing sources? Why does that difference matter for research?
  2. Why is agreement between independent sources stronger evidence than agreement between sources that draw from the same original study?
  3. The chapter describes four types of disagreement: factual, interpretive, value-based, and scope-based. Give an example of each type, and explain what a researcher should do when encountering each.
  4. Two sources reach different conclusions about whether a job training program reduces unemployment. Source A uses administrative data tracking 5,000 participants over two years. Source B uses interviews with twenty-five participants. Is this a contradiction? How should a researcher interpret this difference?
  5. What is the difference between a gap and an absence of evidence? Why does the distinction matter?
  6. A researcher finds three sources that all conclude exercise reduces symptoms of depression, but all three cite the same original study as their primary evidence. Is this strong evidence of consensus? Why or why not?
  7. The chapter says that uncertainty is not failure. What does this mean? How should a student researcher respond when sources genuinely contradict each other and the contradiction cannot be resolved?
  8. What is a pattern map, and how does building one prepare you for synthesis?
  9. Describe the misinformation risk of cherry-picked agreement. How would you use the MGU Misinformation Check to identify this problem in a source?
  10. You are comparing five sources on the health effects of a low-carbohydrate diet. Three sources find significant benefits; two find modest or no benefits. Describe the full comparison process you would use: what would you look for in terms of agreement, disagreement, evidence differences, method differences, gaps, and patterns?

Looking Ahead
Chapter 38: Synthesis — Turning Sources into Understanding

Chapter 37 taught you how to compare sources and identify the patterns running through them — agreement, disagreement, evidence and method differences, gaps, and contradictions. Chapter 38 builds directly on this foundation. It will teach synthesis: the process of explaining what the patterns you have identified actually mean, combining multiple sources into a coherent account of what the evidence shows, where genuine debate exists, and what remains uncertain. Synthesis is more than summary — it is the intellectual work of turning a comparison into understanding, and it is one of the most important skills this textbook will teach.

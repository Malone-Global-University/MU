Chapter 23 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

When readers finish a novel or story and someone asks what it was about, they almost always begin by talking about characters. Not themes first, not plot structure, not the author's biography — characters. They say: I loved her. I didn't understand why he did that. She reminded me of someone I know. I couldn't forgive him, even at the end.

This instinct is not naive. Characters are the primary vehicle through which fiction delivers most of its other meanings. Plot is what characters do and what happens to them. Setting is the world they inhabit and that shapes them. Theme emerges from the patterns of their desires, errors, growth, and failures. Even point of view — whose eyes we see through, whose voice we hear — is a function of character.

But characters in fiction are not real people. They are crafted constructions. Every detail about a character — every word they speak, every gesture they make, every thought that flickers through their mind — was chosen by an author for a reason. The question is never just what is this character like? but always also why did the author construct this character this way, and what does that construction accomplish?

This chapter teaches you to ask both questions at once. You will learn the vocabulary for identifying and comparing characters, and you will develop the analytical habits needed to move beyond description toward interpretation.

Opening Question

Think of a character from any story, novel, or play who has stayed with you — not necessarily a character you liked, but one you found unforgettable. What made them feel real or significant? Now ask a harder question: what specific choices did the author make — in what the character says, does, thinks, or refuses to do — that created that effect? By the end of this chapter, you will have a richer vocabulary and a clearer method for answering that question about any character in any text.

Chapter 23 — Close Reading Focus

Close Reading Focus

This chapter develops your ability to read characters carefully — not just to describe what they do, but to interpret why they do it, how they are constructed, and what their presence means within the larger design of a narrative. The central skill is moving from character description to character analysis: from noting that a character acts a certain way to explaining how and why that behavior generates meaning.

Key Literary Terms

Character
A person, creature, or figure in a narrative whose actions, speech, thoughts, relationships, and development drive the story and contribute to its meaning.
Protagonist
The central character around whom the main action turns; the character whose situation, desires, and conflicts the reader is most closely invited to follow.
Antagonist
The character, force, institution, or circumstance that opposes the protagonist and creates the central conflict of a narrative.
Flat character
A character defined by one or two consistent traits; they do not change substantially and are often used for functional or symbolic purposes.
Round character
A character who is complex, multi-dimensional, and capable of surprising the reader in ways that still feel true — associated with psychological depth.
Static character
A character who does not change significantly over the course of the narrative.
Dynamic character
A character who undergoes meaningful change — in outlook, values, understanding, or behavior — as a result of the story's events.
Motivation
The internal desires, fears, needs, values, wounds, or beliefs that drive a character's choices and actions.
Characterization
The craft methods by which an author creates, reveals, and develops a character; includes both direct description and indirect showing through action, speech, thought, and relationship.
Foil
A character whose contrasting traits highlight qualities in another character, often the protagonist, making certain features visible that might otherwise be less apparent.
Stock character
A conventional character type drawn from familiar cultural or generic patterns — the loyal sidekick, the wise mentor, the corrupt official.
Character arc
The trajectory of change — or significant resistance to change — that a character follows across the length of a narrative.
Major character
A character who appears frequently, is developed in depth, and is central to the story's action and meaning.
Minor character
A character with a limited role whose primary function may be to advance the plot, reveal something about a major character, or represent a type or perspective.

Section 23.1

What Character Means

The word character carries several meanings worth separating before we proceed. In everyday speech, it often refers to moral quality — someone has good character or bad character. In literature, the word is more neutral and more technical: a character is simply a person or person-like figure in a story. Characters can be admirable or reprehensible, simple or complex, realistic or allegorical.

In fiction, character also refers to the sum of what makes someone distinctively themselves: their personality traits, values, habits, contradictions, history, relationships, and patterns of response to the world. When we say a character is "well-drawn," we mean that this sum feels coherent and alive — not that the character is morally good, but that they feel like a fully realized person or figure.

Characters as Constructions

Every character is a selection and arrangement. Real human beings are almost infinitely complex. A fictional character, no matter how rich, is built from a much smaller set of details: a description of appearance, a few recurring gestures, a handful of significant choices, some key relationships, and whatever interior life the narrator grants us access to.

Authors decide what to include and what to leave out. They decide how much to explain a character's background and how much to leave mysterious. They decide whether to tell us directly what a character is like or to let behavior, dialogue, and consequences do that work indirectly. These are all artistic decisions, and they all carry meaning.

When you analyze a character, you are not psychoanalyzing a real person. You are studying how an author has crafted a figure to serve certain purposes in a story. The question is always: what choices did the author make, and what do those choices accomplish?

Characters and Other Story Elements

Characters do not exist in isolation. They are embedded in a web of relationships with every other element of the narrative. Character and plot are inseparable — characters' choices drive plot, and plot events pressure and reveal character. Setting is the world characters inhabit and that shapes them; characters also shape their settings through action. Theme emerges from the patterns of characters' desires, errors, growth, and failures. And point of view — whose eyes we see through, whose voice we hear — determines how much access we have to characters' interiority and how reliably we perceive them.

Analyzing character well requires keeping all of these connections active. Treating character as a separate element that can be studied in complete isolation from plot, setting, theme, and perspective will always produce incomplete readings.

Section 23.2

Protagonists and Antagonists

The terms protagonist and antagonist come from Greek drama but remain useful across all narrative forms.

The Protagonist

The protagonist is the character at the center of a narrative's main action — the character whose situation most organizes the story's events, whose desires and conflicts most shape the plot, and whose perspective most directly guides the reader's experience. The protagonist is not always likable, not always admirable, and not always the most interesting character in a story. They are simply the character whose arc is most central.

Consider some of the ways this centrality can work. A protagonist may be the agent of a story's main conflict — the person whose desires drive the action forward. They may be the character who changes most significantly, and whose change is the meaning of the narrative. They may be the character through whose eyes or voice we experience the story, even if they are passive or resistant to change. In some stories, the protagonist is a flawed or even despicable figure — but we follow them because the story is an inquiry into how such a person came to be, or what their choices reveal about the world.

Teaching Distinction

The protagonist is not the same as the hero, and not the same as a sympathetic character. The protagonist is the narrative's center of gravity. Your task as a reader is to understand whose story is being told — and why the author has chosen to tell it from that vantage point.

The Antagonist

The antagonist is the force, character, or circumstance that opposes the protagonist and generates the story's central conflict. Antagonists are often individual characters — a rival, an oppressor, a corrupt official, an abusive parent. But antagonism can also be environmental or social (a drought, a war, a system of economic inequality), internal (the protagonist's own fears, addictions, or self-deceptions), or deeply ambiguous — so complex that assigning the label to one figure distorts the story's moral texture.

The most effective antagonists in literature have their own perspectives, motivations, and internal logic. When an antagonist is no more than a cartoon villain — evil without interiority or coherent desire — we lose the opportunity for the deeper kind of conflict that literature makes possible: conflict that reveals something true about how people and societies actually work.

Common Misreading

Students sometimes assume the antagonist must be a person. Pay careful attention to stories where the real antagonism is internal, environmental, or systemic. The most important conflict in a story is not always between two individuals — and insisting on locating a human villain may cause you to miss the story's actual design.

Protagonist-Antagonist Relationships in Practice

When you identify the protagonist and antagonist of a story, the next question should always be: what does this opposition reveal? What is at stake between them? What values, needs, or worldviews are in conflict? What does the outcome of their conflict suggest about the story's larger themes?

In Charlotte Brontë's Jane Eyre, Jane is clearly the protagonist. But who is the antagonist? Rochester, who withholds a crucial truth? St. John Rivers, who demands a kind of self-erasure Jane cannot accept? Or is the antagonism more internal — Jane's own struggle between passion and principle, between self-preservation and self-surrender? The answer is probably all of these, operating at different stages of the narrative. This complexity is part of what makes the novel a rich interpretive subject, and it shows how carefully identifying antagonism can open rather than close analytical questions.

Section 23.3

Major and Minor Characters

Narrative is populated by characters at different levels of development. Not every character receives — or needs — the same depth of treatment.

Major Characters

Major characters are those who appear frequently, are developed in depth, and are central to the story's action and meaning. They typically include the protagonist and often include close companions, romantic partners, or family members who significantly shape the protagonist's world. Major characters tend to have multiple scenes devoted to them, interior access through thoughts and feelings, a role in multiple aspects of the plot, a relationship to the story's central themes, and some degree of meaningful development.

Minor Characters

Minor characters appear briefly or infrequently. Their roles may be limited to a single function: to deliver information, to create an obstacle, to serve as a contrast, or to represent a social type or community. This does not make them unimportant. A well-crafted minor character can accomplish a great deal in a short space. A brief exchange can reveal something essential about the protagonist. A minor character's fate can crystallize a theme. A recurring minor character, even one with few scenes, can create a pattern that the reader gradually comes to understand.

One useful question when you encounter a minor character: why is this figure here? What does their presence accomplish that could not be accomplished otherwise? What do they show us that we would not see without them?

Stock Characters and Cultural Types

A special category of minor character is the stock character — a familiar type drawn from cultural or generic convention. The wise old mentor. The loyal sidekick. The comic servant. The corrupt judge. Stock characters are not always a failure of craft. They can function efficiently as narrative shorthand, allowing a story to move quickly because the reader already knows how to read them. They can also be deployed ironically — setting up a familiar type and then complicating or subverting it, using the reader's expectations as a resource.

When a story relies heavily on stock characters, ask: is this strategic (using genre convention deliberately) or is it a limitation (the author has not developed these figures beyond formula)?

Section 23.4

Flat, Round, Static, and Dynamic Characters

Two famous distinctions, introduced by the novelist and critic E.M. Forster in Aspects of the Novel (1927), remain widely used and genuinely useful: flat versus round, and static versus dynamic.

Flat Characters

Forster described flat characters as those built around a single idea or quality. They are consistently themselves — they have one dominant trait or function, and they do not change. In his memorable formulation, a flat character can be summed up in one sentence. Flat characters are not failures of craft. They can represent types or ideological positions clearly; they can provide comic or satirical force through consistent exaggeration; and they can offer a stable backdrop against which round characters' complexity is more visible.

In Charles Dickens's novels, many characters are memorably flat — defined by a single tic, obsession, or verbal formula they repeat throughout. This is not weakness; it is satire and caricature deployed with deliberate skill.

Round Characters

Round characters are complex. They have contradictions, depths, unexpected dimensions. They can surprise the reader in ways that still feel true to who they are — which Forster identified as the key test. A flat character who does something surprising has simply become inconsistent. A round character who surprises us reveals something we did not yet fully know about them but that, in retrospect, was always there.

Round characters tend to feel more "realistic" in the sense of resembling the full complexity of actual human beings. But this is not the only valid mode. Different kinds of fiction require different relationships between round and flat — what matters is whether the characters are suited to the kind of story being told.

Static and Dynamic Characters

A static character does not change significantly across the narrative. Their essential nature at the end is the same as at the beginning. A static character can be complex and carefully drawn while simply not undergoing a transformative arc. A dynamic character undergoes meaningful change — in understanding, values, behavior, or relationships — as a result of what they have experienced. The change must be meaningful to count as dynamic: not a shift in circumstances but a shift in the character's interior life or moral orientation.

Teaching Caution

Do not assume every story should have a dynamic protagonist. Some of the most powerful fiction features protagonists who fail to change, or who change in ways that are ambiguous, partial, or ironic. The absence of change can be as meaningful as change itself. A character who is offered opportunities for growth and refuses them — or cannot take them — may embody the story's central tragedy.

Using These Distinctions

These four categories are tools, not boxes. Use them to open questions rather than close them. If the protagonist is static, what does that suggest about the story's view of human nature or social conditions? If a character who seems flat suddenly reveals depth, how does that surprise function? If the story's most dynamic character is not the protagonist, what does that tell us about where the story locates its meaning?

Section 23.5

Motivation and Desire

Character motivation is one of the most important concepts in narrative analysis, and one of the most frequently misunderstood.

What Motivation Is

Motivation refers to the internal forces that drive a character's choices and actions: their desires, fears, needs, values, wounds, and beliefs. Motivation is the why beneath what characters do. It is not always explicitly stated. In fact, the richest fictional characters often have motivations that are partially hidden — even from themselves. They may pursue what they say they want while being driven by something deeper, something they are not fully conscious of.

This creates one of the most productive dynamics in literary fiction: the gap between what a character says they want and what they actually pursue. A character may say she wants independence and spend the novel placing herself in relationships of dependence. A character may insist he is indifferent to his father's approval and sacrifice everything he values in pursuit of it.

Layers of Motivation

It helps to think of motivation in layers. The surface desire is what the character explicitly says or appears to want — a job, a relationship, revenge, safety, recognition. The deeper need is what the character actually needs, which may or may not align with the surface desire: to be loved, to matter, to escape guilt, to belong. The fear is what the character is trying to avoid, which often shapes their choices as powerfully as their desires — fear of abandonment, failure, becoming one's parents, being truly known. The wound is past experience that shapes present behavior, often unconsciously: grief, betrayal, shame, loss. And the value is what the character believes is worth protecting or pursuing, even at cost to themselves.

Not every character will have all of these layers made explicit. In shorter fiction, you may only glimpse one or two. In a long novel, all of these layers may be developed across hundreds of pages. The task of the reader is to notice what is revealed and to infer thoughtfully about what might lie beneath.

Motivation and Plot

Character motivation is the engine of plot. When we ask why does the story go this way? the answer is almost always found in what characters want, fear, and believe. Two characters who both want to leave their hometown — one out of ambition, one out of shame, one out of love, one out of despair — will make different choices, encounter different obstacles, and arrive at different conclusions. The desire may be the same; the motivation that gives it shape makes the story.

When you analyze a narrative, ask not just what characters want but what deeper motivation gives that want its particular form and urgency. This is where character analysis begins to open onto theme.

Section 23.6

Characterization Through Action and Speech

Authors reveal characters through two broad methods: telling (direct description) and showing (indirect revelation through action, speech, thought, and reaction). Understanding both helps you read what a text is actually doing.

Direct Characterization (Telling)

A narrator or another character explicitly describes what someone is like. This is efficient — the reader immediately has a working sense of the character. But direct characterization has limitations: it tells us what to think rather than inviting us to discover. And it can be unreliable — the narrator or character delivering the description may be biased, mistaken, or deliberately misleading.

Indirect Characterization (Showing)

Characters are revealed through what they do, what they say, how they say it, what they notice, what they fail to notice, what they remember, what they choose not to do, and how others respond to them.

Action: What a character does, especially under pressure, reveals values and priorities. Speech: How characters speak — their vocabulary, their patterns of emphasis, what they avoid — reveals education, social position, emotional state, and values. Thought: In fiction with access to interiority, what characters think (especially when it diverges from what they say or do) is one of the most revealing characterization tools available. Omission and avoidance: What a character never mentions, never looks at, never speaks about is as revealing as what they do. Others' responses: How other characters respond to someone — with fear, trust, pity, contempt — is a form of characterization, though it must be read critically, since other characters may be biased.

Example 23.6 — Direct vs. Indirect Characterization
What each method reveals — and where each can mislead
  • Direct "She was a woman of fierce opinion and very little patience, particularly with people who confused confidence with competence."Efficient and clear — the reader has a working portrait immediately. But we are told what to think. We have not yet been shown how this quality actually manifests, or whether the narrator is reliable in this assessment.
  • Indirect She listened to the committee chair's proposal for ninety seconds, then said, "That's a solution to a different problem," and turned to her notes. No one argued.Here we are shown rather than told. The reader draws conclusions from behavior, dialogue, and the response of those around her. The portrait is richer precisely because it is not stated outright.
  • The Gap The analytically productive situation is when direct and indirect characterization conflict. A narrator tells us a character is generous; then we watch them refuse to help. A character describes themselves as honest; then we watch them lie. This gap is not an accident. It is where some of fiction's most important meanings live.
Principle: When telling and showing diverge, pay close attention. The conflict between them is almost always meaningful — and it is almost always where the real characterization happens.
MGU Literary Analysis Paragraph Model — In Practice

When writing about characterization, your claim should identify what the character is like or what the text is doing with that character. Your evidence should be specific — a quoted action, a piece of dialogue, a telling detail. Your explanation should show why this evidence supports your claim. Your connection should link the characterization to a larger concern: the story's theme, its formal choices, or its historical context.

A Passage for Close Reading

The following is a teaching passage designed to illustrate several aspects of character at once. Read it slowly before moving to the Reader's Question.

Text in Focus — Teaching Passage

Mrs. Holbeck set the table without being asked. She had been setting this table for thirty-one years, and she knew exactly where each fork belonged, exactly how much space to leave between the water glass and the rim of the plate, exactly how to fold the napkins so that they stood rather than lay. Her husband had never once noticed. Her daughter, who visited on Sundays, sometimes said, "Oh, Mum, it looks beautiful," but said it the way you say something beautiful about a painting you pass every day without stopping — out of habit, not attention.

She stood for a moment when the table was done and looked at it. Outside, the neighbor's children were shouting in the garden. The sound came and went through the open window.

She went to the kitchen to finish the soup.

Reader's Question

Before the Analysis

Before reading the analysis below, take a moment and notice what you observe in this passage. Do not rush to interpret. Simply notice: What details are repeated or emphasized? What does the character do, and how does she do it? What is absent from this scene? What is the tone, and what mood does the passage create? What questions does the passage raise that it does not answer?

Write down three observations before you continue.

Interpretation Workshop

From Observation to Interpretation

MGU Close Reading Model — Applied
Notice

The passage focuses on Mrs. Holbeck's actions at the dinner table. The description is precise: she knows where each fork belongs, how much space to leave, how to fold the napkins. She has been doing this for thirty-one years. Her husband has never once noticed. Her daughter acknowledges the table but does so out of habit, "not attention." Mrs. Holbeck stands and looks at the finished table. She hears children outside. She returns to the kitchen.

Question

Why does the narrator spend so much time on this domestic ritual? Why tell us it has been thirty-one years? Why introduce the husband's indifference and the daughter's habitual acknowledgment? Why include the children's voices from outside? Why end with her simply returning to the kitchen — with no resolution, no reflection, no statement of feeling?

Pattern

The pattern is one of unnoticed effort. The precision of Mrs. Holbeck's work — she knows exactly — contrasts with the fact that no one truly sees it. The daughter's response is framed as the equivalent of not really looking at all. The table is "beautiful" but no one stops for it. The children's voices come and go — they are life happening elsewhere, not quite here.

Interpret

Mrs. Holbeck is constructed as a figure of invisible labor and invisible interiority. She is not complaining. She is not weeping. She simply sets the table and then looks at it — and that looking is the charged moment. What is she thinking in that pause? The text does not say. It leaves us with the children's voices and her return to the kitchen. This silence is deliberate. The author gives us enough to understand the shape of Mrs. Holbeck's situation but refuses to tell us how she feels about it. The reader must infer, and that inference is the interpretation.

Support

The claim that this is a figure of unnoticed labor is supported by: "Her husband had never once noticed"; the daughter's acknowledgment described as habit rather than attention; thirty-one years of consistent repetition; the brief pause before returning to the kitchen — a moment the narrative marks but does not explain.

Connect

This character sketch connects to a theme about the invisibility of domestic work, or the way care can be performed and received as mere environment rather than as an act. The form of the passage — its quietness, its refusal of emotional statement, its focus on routine — enacts the character's situation: she too performs without comment. In a longer novel, we might learn whether she changes, resists, or accepts this pattern. Here, we are given a portrait — and the portrait is the meaning.

Mrs. Holbeck is flat in the technical sense: we see one situation, one pattern, one defining condition. But she is not a simple character. In a few hundred words, the author has made her feel recognizable and significant. This passage illustrates how characterization can work economically: not through extensive psychological exposition, but through precise selection of detail, a carefully chosen contrast, and a silence that the reader is left to fill.

Section 23.7

Character Relationships

Characters are not isolated figures. They exist in webs of relationship, and those relationships are themselves a form of characterization and meaning.

Relationships as Characterization

A character who is generous with strangers and cold with family, or warm with social inferiors and contemptuous of equals, reveals something through the pattern of these relationships that no single scene could show alone. The way a character speaks to a servant, treats a rival, or behaves toward someone they claim to love — these relational patterns accumulate into a portrait. Relationships also put characters under pressure. It is in their interactions with others — especially in moments of conflict, vulnerability, or need — that characters are most fully revealed.

The Foil

A foil is a character whose contrasting qualities illuminate those of another character, usually the protagonist. The foil is not simply the protagonist's opposite; they often share some qualities, enough that the difference between them is meaningful rather than merely categorical.

A quiet, introspective protagonist placed alongside an extraverted, socially confident friend: what does the contrast reveal about each? A principled idealist placed alongside a pragmatic opportunist who succeeds where the idealist fails: what does this say about the world the story depicts? When you identify a foil in a text, ask: what does this contrast make visible that we might not have seen otherwise? What does the foil bring out in the protagonist, and what does the protagonist reveal about the foil?

Relationships and Theme

The central relationships in a story often embody its central thematic concerns. A story about inheritance and belonging will place its characters in familial relationships that enact the meaning of those concepts. A story about betrayal will structure its most important relationships around the question of loyalty and its failure. A story about longing and connection will create characters whose relationships explore what happens when people almost — but never quite — reach each other.

Reading character relationships thematically means asking: what does this relationship dramatize? What concepts, values, or human problems does it put in motion?

Section 23.8

Character and Theme

We have said that characters are vehicles for theme — but the relationship is worth examining carefully.

Characters as Thematic Figures

Characters embody thematic concerns in several ways. Through their central conflict: a character torn between loyalty to family and commitment to their own values embodies a thematic concern with the relationship between individual integrity and social belonging. Through their characteristic desire: what a character most deeply wants often points toward the story's thematic center. Through what happens to them: a character who is destroyed by a corrupt system while a more compromised character survives embodies a thematic claim about how that kind of world operates. Through their relationship to other characters: if the most ethical character consistently fails while the most self-interested one succeeds, this pattern makes a thematic statement about the world the story depicts.

Avoiding Reduction

One important caution: characters should not be reduced to their thematic function. Saying a character "represents" a particular danger or value is not wrong, but it is incomplete. It misses everything that is specific, surprising, and irreducible about that character — the texture of their psychology, the particular shape of their self-deception, the way they experience shame and need. Theme and character are in dialogue. The richest readings hold both together rather than reducing character to theme or treating theme as separable from the specific human figures through whom it is enacted.

Character Arcs and Their Shapes

A character arc is meaningful when the change (or stasis) is earned — the transformation follows from what the character has experienced; specific — the change is not generic growth but a particular shift in a particular direction; and connected to the whole — the character's arc is linked to the plot's events, themes, and the pressure exerted by other characters.

Not all arcs resolve cleanly. The failed arc — where the character is offered change and cannot take it — often indicts the social conditions that prevent growth, or reveals something tragic about the character's fundamental nature. The ironic arc occurs when the character appears to change but has not, or changes in a direction the reader recognizes as false. The ambiguous arc ends without clearly resolving whether change has truly occurred, leaving the reader to interpret. And the negative arc shows a character who changes — but in a destructive direction, becoming more closed, more cruel, more self-deceiving. This arc can be tragedy or social critique, depending on how the narrative frames it.

Context Window

Character in Historical Perspective

The concept of the literary character as a psychologically complex individual with an interior life is not universal — it is a historical development associated particularly with the rise of the novel in Western Europe from roughly the seventeenth century onward. Earlier literary traditions often worked with what we might call "type characters" or "allegorical figures" — characters who represented qualities, social roles, divine attributes, or moral categories rather than individual psychological interiority.

In ancient Greek drama, characters are complex and their situations morally rich, but the kind of interior access that a nineteenth-century novelist grants us — thoughts, memories, unconscious desires, gradual psychological change — is largely absent. Medieval allegories feature characters explicitly named as their defining qualities: Christian, Faithful, Worldly Wiseman, Giant Despair. This is not a failure of imagination; it is a different conception of what a literary figure is for.

The novel's emergence coincided with changing ideas about the self — the Romantic emphasis on individual interiority, the Protestant tradition of self-examination, the emerging fields of psychology and eventually psychoanalysis. These historical shifts made the psychologically complex individual character both thinkable and desirable as a literary form.

Why This History Matters

When you read literature from different periods or traditions, do not assume that the standard of the psychologically round, dynamically developing individual is the only legitimate or meaningful kind of character. Many traditions operate with very different conceptions of what a character is and what a character's function is. Genre also shapes character expectations — a realistic novel invites expectations of psychological depth that a fairy tale or a myth quite deliberately resists.

When a contemporary author creates a character who seems flat or type-like, they may be working deliberately with older traditions rather than simply failing to create a round character.

World Literature Connection

Character Across Traditions

The Western realistic novel's emphasis on individual psychological interiority is one approach to character — a powerful one, but not the only one.

In the classical Indian epic the Mahabharata, the character of Karna is one of the most complex in world literature. He is the eldest of the Pandava brothers but does not know this; he fights on the opposing side his entire life; he is repeatedly shown mercy and refuses it; he possesses extraordinary virtue alongside fatal loyalty to the wrong lord. Karna's complexity is not primarily psychological in the modern Western sense — it is mythic, ethical, and cosmological. He embodies certain qualities of heroism and tragedy that carry weight beyond individual psychology, and yet he is also deeply individualized in his circumstances, his wounds, and his choices.

In classical Chinese fiction, particularly in the novel Dream of the Red Chamber, character is embedded in extraordinarily dense social networks. Characters are understood through their relationships, roles, and place in the family system as much as through individual interiority. The psychological depth is real but operates differently — through social positioning, through the accumulation of small social interactions, through the weight of shared history and obligation.

In African oral epics like the Sundiata, the protagonist is a heroic figure whose character is inseparable from his role as the founder and embodiment of a community. His personal qualities — courage, patience, the ability to endure suffering — are also communal qualities; his individual development is a story about the birth of a people. His arc cannot be understood as merely personal.

MGU Comparative Reading Model — Character Across Traditions

Identify the shared question: What is this character for? What work is this figure doing in this narrative?

Compare form and genre: Is this a novel, an epic, an oral narrative, a play? How does form shape what character can be?

Compare historical context: What conception of the self does this tradition assume? Individual interiority? Social role? Cosmic function?

Compare language and imagery: How is the character described — through psychological vocabulary, through action, through the responses of others, through nature imagery?

Compare themes and tensions: What is this character's relationship to community, fate, power, the sacred?

Explain what the comparison reveals: What does seeing these different character traditions side by side show us about what character can accomplish in literature?

When you read across traditions, the question to bring is not is this character round by the standards of the nineteenth-century novel? but rather what is character doing in this work, within this tradition, for this audience? These are not illustrations of "less developed" character — they are illustrations of different conceptions of what character is for and how it works.

Common Misreading

Four Errors to Avoid

Misreading 1
Equating the author with the character.

Beginning readers sometimes assume that a character's views, values, or actions represent the author's own. This is almost never a reliable assumption. An author can create a character whose worldview the author finds repugnant, and do so with such skill and empathy that the character feels fully alive. An author can give eloquent expression to views the author personally rejects — because the task of fiction is not to advocate for the author's positions but to explore what human beings are like and what their situations mean. The question "does the author agree with this character?" is almost always less interesting and less answerable than "what does the narrative as a whole — through plot, contrast, outcome, and consequence — suggest about this character's worldview?"

Misreading 2
Assuming dynamic characters are always better.

Students sometimes treat dynamic characters as inherently superior to static ones, and character change as inherently superior to stasis. A static character who is richly drawn may reveal more about a story's world and themes than a dynamic one who changes superficially. A protagonist who is offered growth and refuses it — or cannot achieve it — may be the most honest and powerful figure in the text. The meaningful question is not did this character change? but what does the narrative do with this character's arc, or lack of one?

Misreading 3
Treating motivation as simple.

Real human motivation is complex, contradictory, and often unconscious. Fictional motivation, especially in rich literary texts, reflects this complexity. Reducing a character's motivation to a single, simple cause misses the texture and specificity that make literary characters meaningful. When you explain motivation, look for contradiction, layering, and the gap between stated and enacted desires. The more complex the motivation, the more carefully you need to trace it through the specific evidence the text provides — and the more cautious you should be about confident summaries.

Misreading 4
Confusing minor characters with unimportant ones.

Minor characters are minor in terms of page time, not necessarily significance. A brief appearance by a minor character can crystallize a theme, reveal something essential about a major character, or embody a social world that the protagonist moves through. Do not skip minor characters in analysis; ask why they are there, what they show us, and what would be missing if they were absent.

Writing About Literature, Review, and Practice

Writing About Literature

Task ACharacter Portrait
  1. Choose a character from a text you are currently reading or have recently read. Write a 300–400 word analysis that identifies the character's most significant motivation (not just their surface desire, but what lies beneath it); provides at least two specific pieces of textual evidence — actions, words, choices, or telling details — that support your reading of their motivation; and explains what this character's presence in the story contributes to its themes. Do not summarize what the character does. Analyze why they do it and what that means.
Task BFlat or Round?
  1. Select two characters from a text — one you would classify as flat, one as round. Write a 400–500 word comparative analysis that supports each classification with textual evidence, explains why the author might have made each character the way they are, and avoids the assumption that round characters are simply better. What does the flat character accomplish that a round one might not?
Task CThe Foil
  1. Identify a foil relationship in a text you know well. In 300–400 words, name the two characters and describe the key contrast between them; show through specific evidence how the contrast is established in the text; and explain what the foil relationship illuminates — what does this contrast make visible that we would not otherwise see as clearly?
Task DCharacter and Theme
  1. Write a 500–600 word essay arguing for a specific connection between a major character's arc and the story's central theme. Use the MGU Literary Analysis Paragraph Model: claim, textual evidence, explanation, connection to theme or form. Avoid reducing the character to their thematic function; keep the character specific and the analysis grounded in the text.

Discussion Prompts

Discussion 1
A Character Who Stayed With You

Think of a character from any text — novel, story, play, epic, film — who has stayed with you. What made them unforgettable? Was it their complexity? Their situation? The fact that you couldn't fully understand them? Now ask: what specific choices did the author make — in what the character says, does, thinks, or refuses to do — that created that effect? Bring these observations to class and compare them with your peers. What patterns emerge in what makes characters feel meaningful?

Discussion 2
When Is Flatness a Virtue?

Choose a text that contains clearly flat characters and argue for why the flatness is appropriate — or argue that it is a failure of craft. What is the difference between strategic flatness (serving a clear narrative purpose) and mere underdevelopment? What kinds of stories benefit from flat characters? What do they sacrifice by including them?

Discussion 3
Character Across Cultures

Compare a character from a Western realistic novel with a character from a non-Western literary tradition — an epic, an oral narrative, a classical Asian text. What is each character for? How is complexity constructed differently in each? What does the comparison reveal about different assumptions regarding individual identity, social role, and the purposes of narrative?

Discussion 4
Motivation and Moral Judgment

Choose a character whose behavior you find morally troubling. Now try to reconstruct their motivation as fully and charitably as you can — not to excuse them, but to understand them. Does understanding their motivation change your judgment of them? Does it change what you think the story is doing with them? What is the relationship between sympathizing with a character's motivation and endorsing their choices?

Review Questions

  1. What is the difference between a protagonist and a hero? Can you think of an example of a protagonist who is not heroic in the traditional sense? What does the choice to center that kind of figure accomplish?
  2. Explain the difference between flat and round characters. Why might an author choose to keep certain characters flat? Give an example from any text you know and explain the function flatness serves.
  3. What is the difference between a static and a dynamic character? Is a dynamic character always preferable? Explain your reasoning with reference to a specific text.
  4. What do we mean by "layers of motivation"? Why is it insufficient to explain a character's behavior by reference only to their surface desire?
  5. Describe three different methods of indirect characterization. For each, explain what kind of information it tends to reveal and what its limitations might be.
  6. What is a foil? Find a foil relationship in a text you know and explain what the contrast between the two characters illuminates — what becomes visible through the comparison that would not be visible otherwise?
  7. Why is it a misreading to assume that a character's views represent the author's own views?
  8. What are some forms a character arc can take besides the triumphant movement from a worse to a better version of oneself? What does each alternative form tend to accomplish?
  9. How does historical and cultural context affect the kind of characters a narrative creates? Use at least one non-Western example in your answer and explain what different conception of selfhood or community that tradition assumes.
  10. Choose a minor character from any text you know and analyze their function. What work does this character do that the text could not accomplish without them?

Reading Checklist

Chapter 23 — Reading Checklist
Use this when approaching any new fictional text. Return to any section where you cannot check the box confidently.
Identifying Characters
  • I can identify the protagonist and explain how I know — what in the narrative marks this as the central figure.
  • I can identify the antagonist, including cases where it is not a person but a system, an environment, or an internal force.
  • I can distinguish major from minor characters and explain the function of each minor character I encounter.
  • I can identify foil relationships and explain what contrasts they make visible.
Understanding Character Types
  • I can identify which characters seem flat and which seem round, and I can evaluate whether that distinction is serving the narrative's purposes.
  • I can distinguish static from dynamic characters and ask what the narrative is doing with each character's arc — or absence of one.
  • I can recognize stock characters and assess whether they are being used strategically or formulaically.
Analyzing Motivation
  • I can identify both the surface desire and the deeper motivation of the protagonist.
  • I can look for the gap between what a character says they want and what they actually pursue in the text.
  • I can trace motivation through specific textual evidence — actions, dialogue, choices, avoidances.
Reading Characterization
  • I can distinguish direct from indirect characterization and identify when the narrator is telling me what to think versus showing me evidence to interpret.
  • I can identify moments where direct and indirect characterization diverge, and I understand that this divergence is usually meaningful.
  • I can analyze dialogue, action, and omission as forms of characterization rather than just events in the story.
Character and Meaning
  • I can connect a character's arc to the story's central themes without reducing the character to a mere symbol or allegory.
  • I can situate this text's approach to character within a broader tradition — realistic novel, epic, allegory, oral narrative — and adjust my reading expectations accordingly.
  • I have avoided the four common misreadings: conflating author and character, privileging dynamic over static, oversimplifying motivation, and ignoring minor characters.

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 23.1Characters are crafted constructions, not real people. Analyzing character means asking not just what a character is like but why the author constructed them this way — and what that construction accomplishes within the larger design of the narrative.
  • Sec 23.2The protagonist is the narrative's center of gravity — not necessarily likable, admirable, or heroic, but the figure whose situation most organizes the story. The antagonist is the opposing force, which may be a person, a system, an environment, or an internal conflict. What matters is what their opposition reveals.
  • Sec 23.3Major characters are developed in depth and central to action and meaning. Minor characters may be brief in appearance but significant in function — they reveal, contrast, represent, and crystallize. Stock characters can be strategic or formulaic; the difference depends on how the text uses them.
  • Sec 23.4Flat characters are consistent and limited; round characters are complex and capable of meaningful surprise. Static characters do not change; dynamic ones do. None of these categories is inherently superior — each has appropriate uses, and the absence of change can be as meaningful as change itself.
  • Sec 23.5Motivation is the internal force that drives character choices. It operates in layers: surface desire, deeper need, fear, wound, and value. The richest fictional characters often have motivations partially hidden even from themselves, and the gap between stated and enacted desire is one of fiction's most productive analytical sites.
  • Sec 23.6Authors characterize through both direct description (telling) and indirect showing — through action, dialogue, thought, omission, and others' responses. When these methods diverge, the gap between them is almost always where the real characterization — and the real meaning — lives.
  • Sec 23.7Character relationships are a form of characterization and a site of thematic meaning. Foil relationships make certain qualities visible through contrast. Central relationships tend to embody and dramatize the story's central thematic concerns.
  • Sec 23.8Characters embody thematic concerns through conflict, desire, outcome, and relational pattern. But they should not be reduced to thematic function — the richest readings hold character and theme in dialogue. Character arcs can be triumphant, failed, ironic, ambiguous, or negative, and each shape carries its own kind of meaning. Across world literary traditions, character operates very differently — and reading across those traditions requires adjusting what we expect character to be and to do.
Looking Ahead
Chapter 24: Point of View and Narration

Chapter 23 examined what characters are, how they are built, and what they accomplish in a narrative. Chapter 24 turns to the question of who tells the story and from what vantage point — because the perspective through which we encounter characters shapes everything we know and believe about them. You will learn to distinguish first-person, second-person, third-person limited, and third-person omniscient narration; to identify and interpret unreliable narrators; to understand narrative distance and focalization; and to analyze how the choice of point of view is itself an interpretive and thematic decision. How we know what we know about fictional characters is always mediated by the narrating voice — and Chapter 24 teaches you to read that mediation carefully.

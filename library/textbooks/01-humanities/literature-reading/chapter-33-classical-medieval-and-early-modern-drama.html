Chapter 33 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Drama is among the oldest and most enduring of literary forms. Unlike poetry or prose narrative, drama was always designed to be performed — to move through bodies, voices, and space before it could move through minds. The plays examined in this chapter span more than two millennia and several distinct civilizations, yet they share a fundamental concern: the staging of human conflict, suffering, and transformation for a watching community.

This chapter surveys the major dramatic traditions of the ancient, medieval, and early modern worlds. It traces the origins of Western theater in the religious festivals of ancient Greece, follows drama through the Roman empire and into the Sanskrit performance traditions of South Asia, examines how theater survived and transformed through the medieval Christian world, and arrives at the explosive theatrical culture of early modern Europe — culminating in the work of Shakespeare. Throughout, a central question persists: what does it mean to watch, and what does theater do to the people who gather to see it?

Opening Question

Every tradition in this chapter invented theater independently in response to the same basic need: to gather a community and stage, before their eyes, something that matters — the fate of heroes, the will of gods, the shape of human failing. As you read, ask yourself not only what these plays contain, but why human beings in such different times and places kept returning to this particular form. What does performance offer that reading alone cannot?

Ancient Greek Drama
Origins · Tragedy · Comedy · The Chorus · Aristotle's Poetics
Section 33.1

Origins and Context

Greek drama emerged in the sixth and fifth centuries BCE from the religious festivals held in honor of Dionysus, the god of wine, transformation, and ecstatic experience. The most important of these was the City Dionysia, held annually in Athens, at which playwrights competed for prizes before audiences of thousands. Theater, in its Greek origins, was not entertainment in the modern sense — it was a civic and religious ritual, funded by the state, attended by citizens as an act of collective participation in the life of the polis.

The earliest Greek plays grew out of the dithyramb, a choral hymn sung in honor of Dionysus. Tradition credits Thespis (sixth century BCE) with the innovation of separating a single actor from the chorus — the birth of dramatic dialogue. From this structural invention, the entire history of Western drama descends.

Tragedy

Greek tragedy (tragōdia, literally "goat song") is characterized by its treatment of heroic or mythological figures caught in irresolvable conflict. The great Athenian tragedians are Aeschylus, Sophocles, and Euripides, each of whom pushed the form in distinct directions.

Aeschylus (c. 525–456 BCE) is the earliest whose work survives. His Oresteia (458 BCE) — the only complete surviving Greek trilogy — traces the cycle of blood vengeance in the house of Agamemnon from the king's murder by his wife Clytemnestra, through Orestes' revenge killing of his mother, to the establishment of a law court by Athena that breaks the cycle. Aeschylus introduced the second actor, enabling genuine dramatic conflict, and his plays are marked by grandeur, theological weight, and formal solemnity.

Sophocles (c. 497–406 BCE) added a third actor and reduced the role of the chorus, concentrating dramatic power in the clashing of individual wills. His Oedipus Rex remains the paradigmatic Greek tragedy: the king of Thebes, investigating a plague, discovers that he himself is its cause — that he has unknowingly killed his father and married his mother. The horror of Oedipus's discovery is inseparable from the structure of the play, which withholds and reveals information with extraordinary precision. Antigone, another of his surviving plays, stages the conflict between civic law and divine obligation with a moral complexity that has made it perennially relevant.

Euripides (c. 480–406 BCE) was the most controversial and, to later ages, often the most modern-feeling of the three. His Medea gives us a foreign woman who murders her own children to punish her faithless husband; his The Bacchae stages the return of the repressed Dionysus to a city that has denied him. Euripides was interested in psychology, in women's experience, in the irrationality beneath civic surfaces — and was criticized in his own time for it.

Aristotle's Poetics (fourth century BCE), the first systematic work of literary theory, takes Greek tragedy as its subject. Aristotle defines tragedy as the imitation (mimesis) of a serious action, which through pity and fear achieves catharsis — a purging or clarification of those emotions. His concept of the tragic hero includes the hamartia (often translated as "fatal flaw" or "error"), the quality or mistake that contributes to the hero's downfall. These terms have shaped the discussion of tragedy ever since, though scholars continue to debate what Aristotle meant by each of them.

Interpretation Workshop — Aristotle Applied
Reading catharsis and hamartia in Oedipus Rex
  • Notice Oedipus is a brilliant, decisive ruler who acts quickly and with total confidence. His very virtues — the relentlessness with which he pursues truth — drive him toward a discovery that destroys him.
  • Question Is Oedipus's downfall a punishment for something he did wrong, or is it an example of innocent suffering? What does this say about Aristotle's concept of hamartia?
  • Interpret Hamartia may not mean a moral failing but an error in a broader sense — a mismatch between intent and outcome, a moment when virtue itself becomes the instrument of catastrophe. The play evokes pity (for an innocent man suffering) and fear (because we recognize that knowledge and goodness cannot protect us) — which is exactly what Aristotle means by catharsis.
Principle: Aristotle's terms are analytical tools, not rigid labels. Apply them carefully and question what they reveal and what they leave unexplained.

Comedy: Old and New

Greek comedy (kōmōdia) developed alongside tragedy and was performed at the same festivals. Old Comedy, represented almost entirely by the eleven surviving plays of Aristophanes (c. 446–386 BCE), is raucous, political, and wildly inventive. Lysistrata stages a sex strike by Athenian women to force an end to the Peloponnesian War; The Clouds satirizes Socrates and the sophists; The Frogs sends Dionysus to the underworld to bring back a dead playwright because Athens has run out of good ones. Old Comedy is characterized by its parabasis (a section where the chorus addresses the audience directly on the playwright's behalf), its fantastical plots, its obscene humor, and its willingness to lampoon real living individuals by name.

New Comedy, emerging in the fourth century BCE and represented by Menander (c. 342–290 BCE), is quieter and more domestic. It focuses on love affairs, mistaken identities, and family misunderstandings, with stock character types — the miser, the young lover, the clever slave — that will pass directly into Roman comedy and, through Rome, into the theater of early modern Europe.

Close Reading Focus — The Chorus

Students sometimes read the chorus as a pause or interruption in the drama. This is a misreading. The choral odes are not pauses: they are the play's emotional and moral processing. Read each ode carefully and ask: what has just happened in the episode preceding it? How does the chorus respond — with fear, confusion, celebration, warning? What does their perspective reveal that the characters' own speeches cannot?

The chorus represents what the community as a whole understands and feels. When the chorus is confused or helpless, that is itself a dramatic and philosophical statement about the limits of collective understanding.

Section 33.2

Reception and Adaptation

Roman drama is in large part a creative adaptation of Greek models. The Romans encountered Greek theater through their expansion into the Greek-speaking world, and from the third century BCE onward they produced plays that drew heavily on Greek originals — a practice they called contaminatio, the blending of Greek source material. This is not mere imitation: Roman playwrights transformed what they borrowed, inflecting it with Roman social concerns, theatrical conventions, and comic sensibility.

Comedy: Plautus and Terence

The two great Roman comic playwrights are Plautus (c. 254–184 BCE) and Terence (c. 195–159 BCE), both working in the tradition of Menander's New Comedy.

Plautus is boisterous, fast-paced, and linguistically exuberant. His plays — including Menaechmi (the source for Shakespeare's The Comedy of Errors), Miles Gloriosus, and Pseudolus — feature elaborate plots of deception driven by clever slaves, young men trying to secure money for a lover, and fathers to be outwitted. The energy of Plautus lies in his comic language: puns, invented words, rapid-fire wordplay, and a delight in verbal performance that makes his plays feel alive even in translation.

Terence is more refined and psychologically subtle. Writing for a more educated audience, he excels at character and at the architecture of plot. His plays — including The Brothers (Adelphoe) and The Mother-in-Law (Hecyra) — were extensively read and imitated during the medieval period and the Renaissance, becoming the primary model for humanist drama. His line from the prologue of The Self-Tormentor — "I am human, and nothing human is alien to me" — became one of the most quoted lines in the Western tradition, and it captures something essential about the comic spirit: an openness to the full range of human foolishness and feeling.

Tragedy: Seneca

Roman tragedy is dominated by a single, towering figure: Seneca (c. 4 BCE–65 CE), the Stoic philosopher and advisor to the emperor Nero. Seneca's nine tragedies — including Medea, Thyestes, Phaedra, and Hercules Furens — are adaptations of Greek material, but transformed almost beyond recognition. Where Greek tragedy tends toward restraint and ritual formality, Senecan tragedy is extravagant, violent, and rhetorically overwrought. His characters deliver long, anguished soliloquies; his plots include infanticide, cannibalism, and spectacular horrors; his language is dense with moral paradox and Stoic reflection on fate, rage, and the instability of power.

Whether Seneca's plays were written for stage performance or private reading (recitatio) is debated. What is beyond debate is their influence: when humanist scholars in the Renaissance rediscovered Seneca, his tragedies became the primary classical model for early modern playwrights. The revenge tragedy tradition, the soliloquy, the five-act structure, the ghost, the tyrant figure — all of these pass from Seneca into Elizabethan and Jacobean drama. Shakespeare's early tragedy Titus Andronicus is openly Senecan, and the influence on Hamlet is profound.

Common Misreading
Roman drama as merely derivative of Greek drama

Students sometimes dismiss Roman comedy and tragedy as inferior copies of Greek originals. This underestimates the scale of transformation. Plautus did not simply translate Menander; he created a distinctly Roman comic world, louder and more linguistically inventive than its models. Seneca did not dilute Greek tragedy; he pushed it toward a psychological extremism that Greek playwrights deliberately avoided. The Romans were engaged in creative adaptation — working with inherited material to produce something new — which is itself a practice we see repeated throughout literary history.

More importantly, the influence of Roman drama on early modern Europe was immeasurable in ways that Greek drama was not, simply because Latin was more widely read than Greek. Understanding Roman drama is essential to understanding Shakespeare and Molière.

Section 33.3

The Natyashastra and Indian Theater

The study of world drama requires moving beyond the Mediterranean. The classical dramatic tradition of India is one of the richest and most formally sophisticated in human history, and it developed largely independently of Greek and Roman theater.

The foundational text of Sanskrit theater is the Natyashastra, attributed to the sage Bharata Muni and composed roughly between 200 BCE and 200 CE. This encyclopedic treatise covers virtually every aspect of theatrical performance: stage design, acting technique, gesture, music, costume, and dramatic theory. The Natyashastra describes rasa (rasa theory), a theory of aesthetic emotion that remains central to Indian aesthetics today. There are eight (later nine) rasas — emotional essences including love (shringara), heroism (vira), grief (karuna), and laughter (hasya) — and the purpose of drama is to evoke these states in the audience through the skilled performance of the actor.

Comparative Reading — Rasa Theory vs. Catharsis
Two theories of what drama does to an audience
  • Aristotle Drama produces catharsis — a purging or clarification of pity and fear. The emotional effect is release. The audience is moved by the represented suffering and is somehow changed or relieved by the experience of watching it.
  • Natyashastra Drama evokes rasa — the aesthetic experience of an emotional essence. The spectator does not simply feel pity or fear but experiences a heightened, aestheticized form of emotion — something between feeling and contemplation. The rasas are universal human states, but art transforms them into a shared aesthetic experience.
  • Compare Both theories understand drama as doing something to the audience's emotional life, not just their intellect. Both assume that the represented emotions of characters can be transferred to, and transformed for, the watching community. The difference lies in the goal: for Aristotle, release; for Bharata, a refined aesthetic experience that transcends ordinary feeling.
World Literature Connection: Putting these theories in dialogue reveals that the question "what does drama do?" has been answered differently by different traditions — and that each answer illuminates something the other leaves in shadow.

Kalidasa and the Sanskrit Tradition

Sanskrit drama (natya) differs from Greek tragedy in crucial ways. It does not seek catharsis through suffering; Sanskrit convention prohibits the death of the hero onstage, and plays typically resolve in restoration and reunion rather than loss. The dominant mode is closer to romantic tragicomedy, though the range of the tradition is wide.

The greatest Sanskrit playwright is Kalidasa (probably fourth or fifth century CE), whose Abhijnanashakuntalam (The Recognition of Shakuntala) is regarded as the supreme achievement of the tradition. The play tells the story of King Dushyanta, who falls in love with the forest maiden Shakuntala, marries her in secret, then forgets her under the influence of a curse. Their eventual reunion — achieved when the king recognizes a ring he had given her — is a drama of memory, loss, and the recovery of love. When Goethe encountered a German translation in 1791, he wrote with overwhelming enthusiasm: the play combines the sensual and the spiritual, the lyrical and the dramatic, in ways that challenged European assumptions about what drama could be.

Japanese Noh and Kyogen

Noh theater, developed in fourteenth-century Japan by Zeami Motokiyo (1363–1443), is a slow, highly stylized form combining poetry, music, mask, and dance. Noh plays often focus on a shite (main character) who is revealed to be a ghost or supernatural figure; the plays are meditations on memory, attachment, and the Buddhist concept of the impermanence of all things. Zeami's treatises on Noh acting — including his concept of yugen, a profound, mysterious beauty — are among the great works of theatrical theory in any tradition.

Kyogen are short comic interludes performed between Noh plays, featuring broad humor, stock characters, and everyday social satire — structurally analogous to the relationship between Greek tragedy and comedy performed at the same festival. The contrast between Noh's austere beauty and Kyogen's comic earthiness is itself a statement about the range of human experience that theater must encompass.

World Literature Connection

These Asian traditions are not peripheral footnotes to a Western story: they are independent solutions to the fundamental problem of performance. Reading Kalidasa alongside Sophocles, or Zeami alongside Shakespeare, reveals both the universality and the cultural specificity of theater's concerns. Every tradition asks how to stage human experience for a gathered community; each answers in a way shaped by its particular religious, aesthetic, and social world.

When approaching these traditions, resist measuring them against Greek or Elizabethan drama as a standard. Ask instead: what does each tradition make possible that the others do not? What questions does each ask that the others overlook?

Section 33.4

The Church and the Stage

After the fall of Rome, the elaborate infrastructure of Roman theater — permanent stone theaters, professional acting troupes, state-sponsored festivals — largely disappeared in western Europe. The Church, which had condemned theatrical performance as immoral and licentious, appeared to have won its long battle against the stage.

The irony is that the Church itself then became the primary venue for drama's revival. Beginning in the tenth century, liturgical dramatizations of key moments in the Christian calendar — the Easter story, the Nativity — began to be performed inside churches, in Latin, as elaborations of the Mass. These brief dramatizations are called liturgical dramas or tropes, and the most famous early example is the Quem Quaeritis trope (c. 925 CE), a dialogue between the angel at the empty tomb and the three Marys seeking Jesus. From this tiny theatrical seed, the entire tradition of medieval drama grows.

Mystery Cycles

By the thirteenth and fourteenth centuries, religious drama had expanded enormously in scope and moved outside the church into the vernacular. Mystery plays (from ministerium, craft or trade guild) dramatized stories from the Old and New Testaments and were typically organized into large cycles — sequences of plays covering biblical history from the Creation and Fall to the Last Judgment.

The four surviving major English mystery cycles are associated with York, Chester, Wakefield (the Towneley Cycle), and the N-Town collection. These cycles were performed on the feast of Corpus Christi, typically in late spring, and organized by the town's craft guilds — each guild responsible for a particular play often related to their trade (the shipwrights might stage Noah's Ark; the bakers, the Last Supper). Performances moved through the town on pageant wagons, bringing biblical history to the community rather than requiring the community to travel to a fixed theater.

The mystery plays are remarkable for their tonal range. The Wakefield Master, the anonymous playwright behind several Towneley plays, achieves a distinctive blend of theological seriousness and robust comedy. His Second Shepherd's Play — in which three shepherds complain about their miserable lives, deal with a sheep-thief who disguises a stolen sheep as a newborn baby, and then receive the announcement of Christ's birth — is one of the great comic achievements of medieval literature, and its juxtaposition of the farcical and the sacred is entirely characteristic of the mystery tradition. The sheep hidden in the manger echoes, almost parodically, the divine child in the manger — and the echo is not accidental. Meaning in the mystery tradition operates on multiple levels simultaneously.

Text in Focus — The Second Shepherd's Play (Wakefield Cycle)
How comedy and theology work together in medieval drama
  • Setup The first half of the play is a broad farce: a rogue named Mak steals a sheep and hides it in a cradle, pretending it is a newborn child. When the shepherds discover the trick, they threaten punishment before letting Mak go.
  • Turn The play pivots abruptly. An angel appears and announces the birth of Christ. The shepherds go to the manger to worship the holy child. The cradle with the stolen sheep rhymes structurally with the manger with the divine child.
  • Interpret The comedy is not a distraction from the theological point — it is the vehicle for it. The audience laughs at the false "baby" in the cradle, then is moved by the real one. The play uses contrast and structural echo to make the sacred legible through the comic. This is not a failure of decorum: it is medieval drama's characteristic theological strategy.
Principle: In medieval drama, comedy and sacred meaning are not in opposition. They are collaborative modes. Reading one without the other impoverishes both.

Morality Plays and the Vice Figure

Alongside mystery plays, the later medieval period produced morality plays — allegorical dramas in which abstract virtues and vices compete for the soul of a representative human figure, typically named Mankind, Humanum Genus, or Everyman.

The most enduring of these is Everyman (c. 1510), in which the protagonist, summoned by Death, discovers that all his companions — Fellowship, Kindred, Goods, Beauty, Strength — abandon him at the end. Only Good Deeds, too weak to walk because Everyman has neglected her, eventually rises and accompanies him into the grave. The play's allegory is stark and powerful: it is among the most performed medieval plays today, and its existential clarity — the stripping away of everything that cannot accompany us into death — gives it a theatrical force that needs no religious conviction to be felt.

The morality tradition is significant not only for its own sake but for its structural influence on later drama. The figure of the Vice — the witty, theatrical tempter who delights the audience with his own villainy — passes directly from morality plays into the Elizabethan stage. Shakespeare's Richard III and Iago both descend, at least partly, from this tradition: they are figures who step forward, address the audience directly, take visible pleasure in manipulation, and invite complicit delight.

Common Misreading — Medieval Drama as Primitive

Medieval drama is sometimes dismissed as crude or theologically simple. Neither claim survives contact with the actual plays. The mystery cycles were performed by an entire urban community as an annual ritual of collective significance; the morality plays developed an allegorical sophistication that shaped drama for two centuries afterward; and the Wakefield Master is, by any standard, a brilliant comic writer. The "primitiveness" is an optical illusion produced by unfamiliarity with the tradition's conventions. Read the plays on their own terms before applying terms imported from another tradition.

Section 33.5

Humanism and the Recovery of Classical Drama

The fifteenth and sixteenth centuries saw the systematic recovery and study of Greek and Latin dramatic texts, particularly the comedies of Plautus and Terence and the tragedies of Seneca. Humanist scholars at Italian universities — and later across Europe — did not merely read these texts: they produced editions, wrote commentaries, and organized performances. The first known performance of a Plautus comedy in the Renaissance took place in Rome in 1484, under the patronage of Pope Innocent VIII.

This recovery generated both neo-Latin drama (plays written in imitation of classical models, in Latin, for academic audiences) and, eventually, vernacular drama in Italian, English, French, and Spanish. The Italian tradition of commedia erudita (learned comedy) adapted Plautine and Terentian plots for contemporary audiences, establishing the five-act structure and the range of character types that would define European comedy for two centuries.

Commedia dell'Arte

Running parallel to humanist learned drama was the popular tradition of commedia dell'arte — professional, improvisational theater performed by touring companies throughout Italy and, eventually, across Europe. Commedia dell'arte companies performed scripted scenarios (canovacci) rather than fully written plays, improvising dialogue around fixed plot outlines and a set of recurring masked characters: Arlecchino (Harlequin), the acrobatic, cunning servant; Pantalone, the miserly Venetian merchant; il Dottore, the pedantic lawyer; the Innamorati, the unmasked young lovers.

The influence of commedia dell'arte on European theater is immeasurable. Its character types appear in Molière, in Goldoni, and in Shakespeare — the Zanni types shadow Launce, Bottom, and Autolycus. Its emphasis on physical comedy, on the performer's body as the primary theatrical instrument, and on the audience's pleasure as the first obligation of theater represents a populist energy that has never been entirely absent from the stage.

The Spanish Golden Age

The Siglo de Oro (Golden Age) of Spanish literature, roughly spanning the late sixteenth through the seventeenth centuries, produced a dramatic tradition of extraordinary richness. Lope de Vega (1562–1635) — the most prolific playwright in the Western tradition, credited with somewhere between 400 and 1,800 plays — helped define the comedia nueva, a flexible form that mixed tragic and comic elements, celebrated national history, and valued theatrical excitement over classical decorum.

Pedro Calderón de la Barca (1600–1681) is perhaps the most philosophically ambitious of the Golden Age playwrights. His Life Is a Dream (La vida es sueño, 1635) — in which a prince imprisoned since birth because of a prophecy is given a day of power and then told his experience was a dream — poses a question that resonates far beyond its immediate context: how do we distinguish reality from illusion, and what does it mean to act rightly in a world we cannot be certain we understand? The play's famous meditation on the dream-like quality of existence places it alongside the great philosophical dramas of any tradition.

Context Window — The Five-Act Structure
How classical form shaped early modern drama

The recovery of classical drama brought with it the recovery of classical dramatic theory, including the five-act structure codified by Horace and exemplified in Seneca. This structure — roughly corresponding to exposition, rising action, complication, climax, and resolution — was not always followed strictly, but it became the default framework within which European playwrights worked and against which their deviations became legible.

Shakespeare's plays are typically divided into five acts in modern editions, though this structure was probably imposed after the fact rather than conceived by Shakespeare himself. Understanding the convention allows readers to notice when playwrights work within it and when, like Shakespeare in the late romances, they stretch or dissolve it.

Section 33.6

The Elizabethan Theatrical World

The emergence of permanent, purpose-built public theaters in Elizabethan England (beginning with James Burbage's The Theatre in 1576) marks a decisive moment in theatrical history. For the first time since Rome, a professional theatrical culture existed with fixed venues, stable companies, and regular performances for paying audiences. The Lord Chamberlain's Men (later the King's Men), Shakespeare's company, performed at the Globe Theatre, built in 1599 on the south bank of the Thames.

The Globe was an outdoor, amphitheater-style structure holding perhaps 2,000 to 3,000 spectators. Its architectural features shaped the drama performed within it: a large thrust stage projecting into the yard where groundlings stood; galleries around the sides for wealthier patrons; a tiring house (backstage area) whose façade served as whatever location the play required; a trap door for apparitions from below; a heavens above for descents. There was no artificial lighting (performances were held in the afternoon), minimal scenery, and all female roles were played by trained boy actors.

This physical context is not incidental. Shakespeare's plays are written for this stage: they use the platform's relationship to the surrounding audience for asides and soliloquies; they rely on language rather than scenery to establish location; they exploit the symbolic possibilities of the vertical axis — heaven above, hell below, the human world of the stage between.

Shakespeare's Achievement

William Shakespeare (1564–1616) wrote approximately 37 plays across a career spanning roughly 1590 to 1613, along with narrative poems and 154 sonnets. The range of the dramatic work is staggering: comedies from the farcical (The Comedy of Errors) to the philosophical (The Tempest); histories covering English kings from Richard II to Henry VIII; and tragedies that include Romeo and Juliet, Julius Caesar, Hamlet, Othello, King Lear, and Macbeth.

What makes Shakespeare's work distinctive? Several qualities deserve particular attention:

Language. Shakespeare works in verse (primarily blank verse — unrhymed iambic pentameter), prose, and rhyme, moving between them with purposeful flexibility. His language is dense with metaphor, pun, and ambiguity; it operates simultaneously on multiple levels, allowing characters to mean more than they consciously intend. The soliloquy — a convention inherited partly from Seneca — becomes in Shakespeare's hands a form of extraordinarily intimate self-examination: Hamlet's meditation beginning "To be, or not to be" is not merely a meditation on suicide but a sustained inquiry into the nature of consciousness and action.

Character. Shakespeare's characters achieve an illusion of interior life — of psychological depth and self-contradiction — that was unprecedented in drama. They change, they surprise themselves, they refuse to be entirely explained by the plots they inhabit. Iago is not simply a villain; Shylock is not simply a comic butt; Falstaff is not simply a clown. This psychological complexity is partly a function of language — characters in Shakespeare speak in ways that exceed their immediate dramatic function, as if they are thinking thoughts the play's plot has not yet caught up with.

Generic complexity. Shakespeare consistently violates the boundaries between tragedy and comedy that classical theory had established. The gravedigger scene in Hamlet, the porter in Macbeth, the fool in King Lear — these comic figures operate within tragedies not as violations but as deepening. The tragic and comic modes illuminate each other: comedy in a tragedy measures what is being lost, and tragedy in a comedy measures what is at stake.

Interpretation Workshop — Reading Blank Verse
How Shakespeare's verse shapes meaning
  • The Form Blank verse (unrhymed iambic pentameter) has a default rhythm: ten syllables per line, alternating unstressed and stressed beats. When characters speak in perfect iambic pentameter, there is a quality of composed control. When the meter breaks — through extra syllables, missing beats, mid-line pauses — it signals emotional pressure or disruption.
  • Listen For Characters who slip from verse into prose often signal a shift in social register or psychological state. Hamlet moves in and out of verse and prose throughout the play; his prose with the players and the gravediggers is itself a kind of mask, different from the verse of his soliloquies.
  • Apply When reading Shakespeare, pay attention to these shifts. Ask: why is this character speaking in prose here? Why does the meter break in this particular line? The form is not decoration — it is information about character and emotional state.
Principle: In Shakespeare, how a character speaks is as important as what they say. The form of the language is part of the meaning.

The Late Romances

Shakespeare's career moves through identifiable phases. His late plays — Pericles, Cymbeline, The Winter's Tale, The Tempest — are often grouped as romances or tragicomedies: plays that begin in tragic loss, move through suffering and apparent death, and arrive at reconciliation, reunion, and forgiveness. The Tempest, probably his last solo-authored play, stages the magician Prospero's recovery of his rightful place and his eventual renunciation of his art — a play that has been read as Shakespeare's own farewell to the theater, and as a complex meditation on power, colonialism, and the limits of art. The late romances are among the most formally adventurous works in the Shakespeare canon, and their willingness to embrace the improbable and the symbolic marks a deliberate departure from the psychological realism of the great tragedies.

Core Principle — The Author and the Play

It is tempting to read Shakespeare's biography into his plays — to find the retired playwright in Prospero, the bereaved father in King Lear, the ambitious young man in Hamlet. These biographical readings are not necessarily wrong, but they carry risks. The plays exceed any single biographical reading. Prospero is a fictional character in a play; whatever Shakespeare felt about retirement is not accessible to us, and treating the play as autobiography flattens it. Read the plays as plays first — as carefully crafted dramatic structures with their own internal logic — and hold biographical speculation lightly.

Section 33.7

The Social Function of Theater

Across all the traditions examined in this chapter, a common thread emerges: drama is not simply storytelling but a communal act. The Greek theater was built into the hillside so that the entire citizen body could attend together; mystery plays moved through the streets of medieval towns and were performed by the community's own guilds; the Elizabethan Globe brought groundlings and gallery-goers into the same space, breathing the same air, watching the same action from different angles and different altitudes.

This communal dimension is part of what makes theater politically significant. Drama does not merely reflect society's values; it stages them, subjects them to pressure, tests them against character and event. Sophocles' Antigone asks what happens when civic law and moral obligation conflict. Shakespeare's history plays ask what makes a legitimate ruler and what happens when legitimacy fails. Calderón asks whether we can know the difference between dream and waking. These are not merely aesthetic questions; they are questions about how people ought to live together, and staging them before a community that watches and responds together gives them a social force that private reading cannot entirely replicate.

Ritual Origins and the Question of the Sacred

Many theorists have argued that drama's origins lie in religious ritual — that the distinction between theater and ceremony is a relatively late development. The Greek theater grew from the Dionysiac dithyramb; the medieval mystery cycle grew from the liturgy; Sanskrit theater was understood as an offering to the gods. Even as drama became secular, it retained — and retains — something of the ritual's structure: the gathering of a community, the marking of a special time and space, the witnessing of events that matter.

This connection between drama and ritual raises questions that continue to animate theater scholarship and practice: What is the difference between a performance and a ceremony? What is the relationship between the actor and the character, the stage and the world, the audience's watching and their participation? The drama that moves us most — Greek tragedy, Shakespeare at his greatest, Sanskrit performance at its height — tends to operate in the space where the human and the cosmic are not fully separable. It is in that charged space that theater does its deepest work.

Section 33.8

The Challenge of Historical Distance

Reading plays from radically different historical and cultural contexts presents challenges that are partly literary, partly theatrical, and partly cultural. A modern reader approaching Oedipus Rex must navigate the conventions of the chorus, the assumption of a mythological world the audience already knew, and the theological framework of fate and divine will that underlies the action. A reader of medieval mystery plays must reckon with an allegorical mode of thought in which biblical history and contemporary life were not felt to be in tension. A reader of Shakespeare must deal with a language four centuries old, theatrical conventions that require some explanation, and social assumptions about class, gender, and race that can be distressing or opaque.

None of these challenges is insurmountable, and none of them diminishes the value of the encounter. The challenge is part of the point.

Strategies for Reading Drama

Read for performance. A play is not a poem or a novel. When you read a scene, try to imagine it in motion: who is standing where? Who can hear what? Where does a character look when they deliver a soliloquy? Thinking spatially about the text opens dimensions that silent reading on the page tends to close.

Understand the conventions. Every theatrical tradition has its conventions — structural, formal, and theatrical — and understanding them is prerequisite to appreciating how playwrights work within and against them. The Greek chorus is not a dramatic weakness but a formal resource with its own expressive possibilities. The Shakespearean soliloquy is not a realistic representation of thought but a theatrical convention that enables a particular kind of access to interiority.

Resist premature modernization. It is tempting to interpret older plays entirely through contemporary frameworks — to read Hamlet as a study in clinical depression, or Medea as a feminist text, or Oedipus as a psychoanalytic case study. These readings are not necessarily wrong, but they tend to flatten the historical specificity of the work and can obscure what is genuinely strange and instructive about encountering a different way of imagining human life.

Attend to productions. Older plays have a continuous life in performance, and seeing or reading about productions — including radically different interpretations — illuminates possibilities that a single reading of the text may not reveal. A production of King Lear set in feudal Japan, or a Greek tragedy performed in an ancient outdoor theater, or a mystery play cycle enacted by community members in a town center, is not a distortion of the text but one realization of a set of possibilities the text contains.

MGU Close Reading Model Applied to Older Drama

Notice: What is strange or unfamiliar about this play — its structure, language, conventions, or assumptions? Do not skip past the strangeness; it is where the learning lives.

Question: Why does this play do this? What convention, belief, or theatrical condition explains the feature you have noticed?

Pattern: Does this feature recur elsewhere in the play? In other plays of the same tradition?

Interpret: What does this feature reveal about the tradition's understanding of what drama is for?

Support: What specific evidence from the text supports your interpretation?

Connect: How does this illuminate something in another tradition, or in your own experience of performance?

Why These Plays Still Matter

The argument for reading classical, medieval, and early modern drama is not primarily an argument from cultural authority — the claim that these works are important because they have always been considered important. It is an argument from experience: these plays have survived because they deal, with particular intensity and craft, with questions that do not go away. What do we owe the dead? What is the relationship between power and justice? Can we know ourselves? What does it cost to act rightly in a world that resists rightness? What are the obligations of community?

They also offer something rarer: the experience of genuine otherness. To read Sophocles or Kalidasa or the Wakefield Master is to encounter a mind organized differently from our own, working within assumptions and within a world that we have to make an effort to inhabit. That effort is, among other things, an education in the contingency of our own assumptions — which is perhaps the most valuable thing literature can offer.

Chapter 33 — Back Matter

Key Terms, Summary, Review, and Practice

Key Terms

Catharsis
Aristotle's term for the emotional effect of tragedy — the purging or clarification of pity and fear through dramatic experience.
Hamartia
The error or quality in a tragic hero that contributes to their downfall; often mistranslated as "fatal flaw," it may describe an error of judgment rather than a moral vice.
Chorus
In Greek drama, a group of performers who sing, dance, and comment collectively on the action, representing the perspective of the community.
Dithyramb
Choral hymn in honor of Dionysus; considered one origin of Greek tragedy through the addition of a solo actor responding to the chorus.
Mimesis
Aristotle's term for artistic imitation or representation; drama imitates a serious action and through this imitation produces its emotional effects.
Rasa
Sanskrit theory of aesthetic emotion; the nine emotional essences — including love, heroism, grief, and laughter — that a dramatic performance should evoke in the audience.
Natya
The Sanskrit term for drama as a comprehensive performance art combining movement, music, speech, and gesture, theorized in the Natyashastra.
Liturgical drama
Short dramatic performances developed within the medieval Church as elaborations of the Mass, the earliest of which dramatized the Easter story.
Mystery cycle
Sequences of vernacular biblical plays performed by trade guilds in medieval English towns, covering scriptural history from Creation to Last Judgment.
Morality play
Allegorical drama in which abstractions — virtues and vices — compete for a representative human soul, as in Everyman.
The Vice
The witty, villainous tempter figure in morality plays who addresses the audience directly and delights in manipulation — an ancestor of Shakespearean villains like Iago and Richard III.
Commedia dell'arte
Italian popular theater tradition featuring masked stock characters and improvised performance around fixed scenarios; hugely influential on European comic drama.
Blank verse
Unrhymed iambic pentameter; the dominant meter of Elizabethan dramatic verse and the primary medium of Shakespeare's serious dramatic language.
Thrust stage
A stage projecting into the audience on three sides, characteristic of the Elizabethan public theater; creates intimacy and makes soliloquy feel genuinely private.
Tragicomedy
A dramatic form mixing tragic and comic elements; associated especially with Shakespeare's late romances, which begin in loss and conclude in reconciliation.

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 33.1Greek drama emerged from Dionysiac religious festival. The three great tragedians — Aeschylus, Sophocles, and Euripides — each pushed the form in distinct directions. Aristotle's Poetics theorized tragedy in terms of mimesis, catharsis, and hamartia. Aristophanes and Menander developed Old and New Comedy respectively, establishing the comic tradition that passed into Rome and early modern Europe.
  • Sec 33.2Roman drama adapted Greek models through contaminatio. Plautus and Terence developed distinctive comic styles that would become the foundation of Renaissance learned comedy. Seneca's extravagant tragedies, rediscovered by humanists, became the primary classical model for Elizabethan revenge tragedy, shaping Shakespeare's Hamlet and other major works.
  • Sec 33.3The Sanskrit dramatic tradition, theorized in the Natyashastra, developed rasa theory as an account of drama's emotional effects. Kalidasa's Shakuntala is the supreme achievement of the tradition. Japanese Noh theater, developed by Zeami Motokiyo, is a slow, masked, and highly stylized form organized around the concept of yugen — mysterious, profound beauty.
  • Sec 33.4Medieval religious drama revived from within the Church through liturgical tropes, then expanded into the vernacular mystery cycles performed by craft guilds on pageant wagons. The Wakefield Master achieved a distinctive blend of comedy and theological seriousness. Morality plays developed allegory and the Vice figure, both of which passed directly into Elizabethan drama.
  • Sec 33.5Early modern theater recovered classical models through humanism, while commedia dell'arte developed a parallel popular tradition emphasizing improvisation and physical performance. The Spanish Golden Age produced Lope de Vega's prolific output and Calderón's philosophically ambitious Life Is a Dream.
  • Sec 33.6The Elizabethan public theater created a permanent professional theatrical culture. Shakespeare wrote approximately 37 plays across comedies, histories, and tragedies, distinguished by his linguistic density, psychological characterization, and willingness to mix tragic and comic modes. His late romances turn toward reconciliation and the fantastic.
  • Sec 33.7Drama is fundamentally a communal act. Across all traditions, theater gathers a community to witness events that matter — staging social values, testing them against character and event, and working in the charged space where the human and the cosmic meet.
  • Sec 33.8Reading older drama productively requires reading for performance, understanding theatrical conventions on their own terms, resisting premature modernization, and attending to the strangeness as a site of genuine learning. These plays endure because they deal, with extraordinary craft, with questions that have not gone away.

Discussion Questions

Discussion 1
Catharsis Then and Now

Aristotle argues that tragedy produces catharsis through pity and fear. After reading one Greek tragedy, do you find this account satisfying? What does the play produce in you that Aristotle's account may not fully capture? Is catharsis a useful concept for describing the experience of watching any of the other dramatic traditions discussed in this chapter?

Discussion 2
Community Performance

The medieval mystery cycles were organized by craft guilds and performed by non-professional actors for the entire community. Shakespeare's plays were performed by professional actors in a commercial theater. Calderón's plays were performed at the Spanish royal court. How does the mode of production — who performs, for whom, in what context — shape the meaning and experience of a play? What is lost, and what might be gained, in each context?

Discussion 3
Rasa Theory and Western Drama

The Natyashastra's concept of rasa emphasizes the emotional states evoked in the audience rather than the represented actions of the characters. How does this framework differ from Aristotle's account of catharsis? Which seems to you more useful for describing what drama does to its audience? Can either concept be applied to a dramatic tradition other than the one it was developed to describe?

Discussion 4
Comedy Within Tragedy

Shakespeare consistently mixes comic and tragic elements within the same play — the gravedigger scene in Hamlet, the porter in Macbeth, the Fool in King Lear. Choose one such scene and analyze what it contributes to the play as a whole. Does the comic interrupt the tragic, or does it deepen it? What does the coexistence of modes reveal about Shakespeare's understanding of human experience?

Discussion 5
Drama and Ritual

The chapter argues that drama retains something of ritual's structure even when it becomes secular. Do you find this argument persuasive? Think of a theater experience — or a film or television experience — that felt, in some way, like more than entertainment. What qualities gave it that quality? What does your answer suggest about what performance continues to do for communities even in secular contexts?

Looking Ahead
Chapter 34: Modern and Contemporary Drama

Chapter 33 traced drama from its origins in ancient religious festival through the theatrical cultures of classical Asia, medieval Europe, and the Elizabethan public stage. Chapter 34 will examine how drama changed as the world changed — from the social realism of Ibsen and Chekhov, through the absurdism of Beckett and Ionesco, to the political theater of Brecht, the postcolonial drama of Wole Soyinka and Athol Fugard, and the experimental and global performance traditions of the present. The question Chapter 34 addresses is how drama responded to modernity — to industrialization, to world war, to colonialism and decolonization, to a world in which the gods have fallen silent and the community itself has fractured.

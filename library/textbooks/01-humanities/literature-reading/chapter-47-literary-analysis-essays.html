Chapter 47 — Front Matter

Close Reading Focus and Overview

Writing about literature is not a separate activity from reading literature. It is the deepest form of reading. This chapter teaches students how to transform careful literary reading into structured written analysis — analysis that is argument-driven, evidence-based, and textually accountable, not summary, not personal reaction, and not unsupported opinion.

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to move from a question about a text to a thesis, from a thesis to organized paragraphs, and from paragraphs to a coherent literary essay that demonstrates genuine interpretive thinking. Everything here connects to the reading and interpretation skills built across the previous forty-six chapters.

When you read a poem, a story, or a play with care, you notice things: a word that seems strange, an image that recurs, a character who behaves unexpectedly, a structure that breaks its own pattern. You ask questions. You begin to form hunches about what the text is doing. But these observations remain incomplete until you try to explain them — to put them into sentences that make a claim, gather evidence, and build an argument. Writing forces precision. Writing about literature is therefore not a test of whether you understood the text. It is a way of understanding the text more deeply.

Opening Question

Two students are each asked to write about the same short poem. The first student writes confidently about what the poem "is about" and how it made them feel. The second student identifies a specific recurring image, asks why it appears where it does, and builds a paragraph that quotes the image, explains exactly how its language works, and connects that explanation to a larger claim about the poem's meaning. Both students read the poem closely and both care about it. Only one of them is doing literary analysis. By the end of this chapter, you will be able to explain precisely what separates the two — and you will be able to write like the second student.

Close Reading Focus

This chapter's reading skill is the conversion of observation into argument. The focus is on producing analytical essays that move from a specific textual feature, to a claim about what that feature does, to evidence, to explanation, to a conclusion that stays accountable to the text. Every section below builds one stage of that process.

Key Literary Terms

analytical thesis
A claim about how or why a text creates meaning, not merely what happens in a text. An analytical thesis takes a position that must be argued and supported.
textual evidence
Specific words, phrases, images, scenes, or structural features from the literary text that support an interpretive claim.
quotation integration
The practice of embedding quoted material from a literary text smoothly into the prose of an essay, with proper framing, punctuation, and explanation.
close reading
Careful attention to the specific language, structure, and patterns of a literary text, used as the foundation of literary analysis.
literary argument
A reasoned interpretive claim about a text, supported by textual evidence and developed through explanation, not simply asserted as fact or personal feeling.
plot summary
A retelling of events in a narrative; distinguished from analysis because it describes what happens rather than explaining how or why the text creates meaning.
paragraph structure
In literary analysis, the organization of a body paragraph around a single analytical claim, supported by evidence, explained, and connected to the essay's larger argument.
comparative analysis
An essay or portion of an essay that examines two or more texts, characters, speakers, images, or structural features in relation to each other to illuminate what neither would reveal alone.
revision
The process of rethinking, restructuring, and refining a draft essay; more than proofreading, revision involves reconsidering the thesis, evidence, and argumentation.
scholarly voice
The tone of academic literary writing: clear, formal but not stiff, confident but not dogmatic, evidence-driven, and precise in its use of literary vocabulary.

Section 47.1

What Literary Analysis Is

Literary analysis is not a book report. It is not a biography of the author. It is not a summary of the plot. It is not a personal reaction to whether you enjoyed reading the text.

Literary analysis is the practice of making an argument about how a literary text creates meaning — and supporting that argument with evidence from the text itself. The word analysis means to break something apart in order to understand how it works. In literary analysis, we separate a text into its elements — language, structure, tone, imagery, pattern, character, narrative perspective — in order to understand how those elements work together to create meaning, effect, or complexity.

What Literary Analysis Asks

Literary analysis answers questions like: How does this text create its emotional effect? What does this recurring image suggest about the text's larger concerns? How does the narrator's unreliability shape how we understand the story's events? Why does this poem end where it ends, and what does that ending do? Notice what these questions share: they ask how and why, not what. They ask about craft and meaning, not just content and plot.

What Literary Analysis Is Not

Not a summary. Summary tells what happens. Analysis explains how and why it matters. Not a personal reaction. "I really liked this poem" is a starting point for thinking, not analysis. Not a biography. The text must be the primary evidence, not the author's life. Not a list of literary terms. Identifying a metaphor without explaining what it does is not analysis. Not a statement of universal truth. Broad claims about "all of human history" say nothing specific about the text in front of you.

The Foundation of Literary Analysis

Literary analysis rests on three foundations: a specific, arguable claim about the text; specific textual evidence that supports the claim; and explanation of how the evidence supports the claim. All three are required. A claim without evidence is unsupported assertion. Evidence without a claim is a quotation dump. Evidence with a claim but without explanation leaves the reader to make the connection themselves — which is your job, not theirs.

Section 47.2

From Reading Question to Thesis

The first step in writing a literary analysis essay is not writing the essay. It is reading — and reading again, with growing attention to what the text is doing.

Before you can write an analytical thesis, you need to read the text closely and gather observations, using the MGU Close Reading Model:

MGU Close Reading Model

Notice. Question. Pattern. Interpret. Support. Connect.

As you read, annotate the text. Mark words or phrases that seem strange, charged, or significant; images that recur; moments of tone shift; structural features that seem deliberate; lines that feel unresolved; and moments where your expectations as a reader are surprised. These annotations are the raw material of your essay.

Identifying a Productive Question

From your annotations, identify one or two questions that the text makes you want to answer. The best analytical questions focus on a specific feature of the text, ask about how or why, can be answered with textual evidence, and have a non-obvious answer.

Productive QuestionsToo Broad or Too Simple
Why does this story begin and end in the same location, and what does that circularity suggest?What is the theme of this story? (Every story has multiple themes.)
The speaker shifts from second person to first person midway through this poem. What does that shift do?Does the speaker love the person they are addressing? (Answerable in one sentence.)
Water appears in six scenes, always near the same character. What does this pattern suggest?Is this play a tragedy? (A classification question, not an analytical one.)

The Thesis Test

Once you have a good analytical question, try to answer it. Your provisional answer becomes your working thesis. A thesis is not a topic. A topic says: This essay is about the use of water imagery in the novel. A thesis makes a claim: The recurring water imagery associates the protagonist with dissolution and transformation, suggesting that her psychological instability is a response to a world that refuses her a stable identity.

Apply the Thesis Test
Check every working thesis against these four questions
  • Is it specific? Does it identify a particular element of the text?
  • Is it arguable? Could a thoughtful reader disagree and need to be persuaded?
  • Is it connected to meaning? Does it explain what the text does, not just what it contains?
  • Is it provable with evidence from the text?
Example 47.2 — Building a Thesis in Stages
From summary to specific interpretive claim
  • Weak "The poem 'Stopping by Woods on a Snowy Evening' is about a person who stops in the woods on a winter night."This is a summary. It describes what the poem contains, not what it does.
  • Stronger "The poem uses the contrast between the traveler's attraction to the dark woods and his obligation to continue his journey to explore the tension between the desire for rest and the demands of duty."This makes an interpretive claim and identifies a specific textual contrast.
  • Most Specific "The repeated phrase 'and miles to go before I sleep' transforms a physical journey into a meditation on mortality and unfinished obligation — suggesting that what holds the speaker in life may be less desire than duty."This is focused on a specific formal feature and advances a claim that requires argument and evidence.
Principle: Move from what the text contains to what a specific feature does.
Section 47.3

Claims About Literary Meaning

The body of a literary analysis essay consists of analytical claims — statements that advance the essay's argument. Each paragraph should be built around one such claim. Understanding what makes a claim analytical, as opposed to descriptive or evaluative, is essential to writing essays that analyze rather than summarize.

Type of StatementExampleWhy It Falls Short (or Succeeds)
Descriptive"The narrator describes the house as dark and cold."Tells us what is in the text; not yet a claim.
Evaluative"This is a well-written and effective description."Expresses judgment about quality, not literary function.
Analytical"The description establishes an atmosphere of emotional neglect that anticipates the family's estrangement."Explains what the feature does and connects it to larger meaning.
Claim Starters for Literary Analysis

The use of [feature] in [text] suggests / reveals / creates / establishes / complicates / challenges…

By [doing something with language or structure], [the poem / the narrator] achieves / signals / implies…

The contrast between [X] and [Y] in this [scene / passage] illuminates the text's concern with…

The recurring [image / word / motif] of [X] does more than [surface function]; it also…

Example 47.3 — Weak, Strong, and Overstated Claims
Calibrating the size and precision of an analytical claim
  • Weak "There is a lot of irony in the story."Too vague. What kind of irony? Where? What does it do?
  • Strong "The story's dramatic irony — the reader's knowledge that the narrator is dying while the narrator remains cheerfully unaware — creates an atmosphere of dread that transforms the comedy of the opening scenes into something darker."Specific, arguable, and connected to effect.
  • Overstated "The story proves that all human beings are fundamentally self-deceived."Moves from interpretation to universal assertion the text cannot support.
Principle: Keep claims tethered to what the text actually does.

Section 47.4

Quoting Literary Evidence

Textual evidence is the foundation of literary analysis. The most common form of textual evidence is direct quotation. Learning to quote effectively — to choose the right evidence, frame it properly, and integrate it smoothly — is an essential analytical skill.

Choosing What to Quote

Quote when the exact words matter — when the specific language is what you are analyzing, and the passage is concentrated and precise. Paraphrase when what happened, not the wording itself, is the evidence.

Rule of thumb: Quote when the words themselves are the evidence.

Example 47.4a — Introducing a Quotation
Why framing matters before the words arrive
  • Poor "The poem has a complicated relationship with time. 'Old age should burn and rave at close of day.'"The quotation arrives with no context. Who says this? In what poem? The reader cannot tell how it connects to the claim.
  • Stronger "As the speaker commands his dying father, 'Old age should burn and rave at close of day' — the verbs burn and rave are imperatives, demanding intensity at the very moment life is failing."Context, key words, and explanation arrive together.
Principle: A quotation should never arrive without warning or leave without explanation.

Framing Formulas

As the narrator observes, "[quotation]."  ·  The speaker declares, "[quotation]."  ·  In [scene / stanza / act], [character] tells [other character] that "[quotation]."  ·  This tension surfaces in the poem's language: "[quotation]."

Punctuating Quotations

Short quotations (fewer than four lines of prose, fewer than three lines of poetry) are integrated into the sentence with quotation marks: The narrator describes the house as "a face with no expression" — a simile that transforms architecture into a mask.

Long quotations (four or more lines of prose, three or more lines of poetry) use a block quotation, introduced with a colon, indented, without quotation marks:

Do not go gentle into that good night,
Old age should burn and rave at close of day;
Rage, rage against the dying of the light.

Each of the three lines insists on the same imperative: resist. The repetition of "rage" brackets the stanza, and the return to "dying of the light" creates a formal circularity that mirrors the text's concern with cycles of life and death.

Ellipsis — use three dots ( … ) to omit words from the middle of a quotation, only when the omission does not change its meaning. Brackets — use square brackets to add words for clarity or grammatical integration.

After the Quotation: Explanation

Every quotation must be followed by explanation. A useful formula: claim sentence, then quotation with introduction, then explanation of how the quotation supports the claim — with attention to specific words, images, or structure. Dropping a quotation and moving on is not analysis.

Section 47.5

Explaining Evidence

Many student essays stall at the quotation. They quote effectively, but then summarize what the quotation says rather than analyzing it. Explaining evidence is the most demanding part of literary analysis. It requires you to slow down, identify exactly what in the evidence supports your claim, and articulate the connection with precision.

The Explanation Test
Ask all four questions after every quotation
  • Which specific words, phrases, or images carry the meaning I am claiming?
  • What do those words, phrases, or images actually do — what literary technique is at work?
  • How does what they do connect to the claim I made in this paragraph?
  • How does this paragraph's claim connect to my essay's larger argument?
Example 47.5 — From Quotation to Full Explanation
Claim: the story uses setting to establish isolation before any character appears
  • Quotation "The house sat at the end of a road that ended at the house. Beyond it, fields extended in three directions, and in the fourth direction was the sea."
  • Weak "This shows that the house is in a remote location." This summarizes rather than analyzes.
  • Strong "The sentence's syntax enacts its own meaning: the road ends at the house, making the house literally the terminus of the world that connects it to others. The phrase 'ended at the house' is grammatically final, and the sentence does not continue outward. Setting here is not backdrop but argument."
Principle: Identify specific language, name the technique, and connect both to your claim.
Common Explanation Failures

Paraphrase explanation restates the quotation in other words instead of analyzing its language. Assertion explanation simply repeats the claim after the quotation without showing how the quotation supports it. Over-interpretation moves too far from the textual evidence to a grand claim the evidence cannot actually support.

Section 47.6

Avoiding Plot Summary

Plot summary is one of the most persistent habits in student literary writing. It is also one of the clearest signals that an essay is not yet analyzing. Plot summary is telling what happened. Literary analysis is explaining how and why what happened creates meaning.

Signs a Paragraph Has Slipped Into Summary

The paragraph narrates events in sequence ("First this happens; then this; finally this"); it describes what characters do without explaining why it matters analytically; its verbs are mostly narrative verbs (goes, says, sees, finds, realizes, decides); and it could be understood by someone who had not read the text, because it essentially retells the text.

Example 47.6 — Converting Summary to Analysis
Hamlet's "To be or not to be" soliloquy
  • Summary "Hamlet delivers the 'To be or not to be' soliloquy, in which he considers whether it is better to live or to die. He thinks about the suffering of life and decides not to act."
  • Analysis "The soliloquy is structured as a logical argument, but its conclusion is not logical: Hamlet never completes the reasoning to a decision, ending instead with 'conscience does make cowards of us all.' The philosophical frame collapses into psychological confession — the play frames inaction as a problem of knowledge, not will."
Principle: Focus on how the passage is constructed and what that construction does.
The One-Sentence Summary Rule

Allow yourself one sentence of plot summary per analytical paragraph — one sentence to orient the reader in the text — and then move immediately to analysis. Everything after that sentence must explain meaning, not narrate events.

Section 47.7

Comparing Texts

Some literary essays ask you to analyze two texts together. Comparative essays require all of the skills of single-text analysis plus the additional skill of constructing a relationship between texts that reveals something about each text individually and about the theme or question they share.

Ask: What does the comparison allow me to see that I could not see from one text alone? If the answer is nothing — if the texts simply confirm each other without complicating or illuminating each other — the comparison may not be the right one.

MGU Comparative Reading Model

Identify the shared question. Compare form and genre. Compare historical context. Compare language and imagery. Compare themes and tensions. Explain what the comparison reveals.

Organizational StrategyAdvantageRisk
Block — discuss Text A fully, then Text B, then compareAllows full development of each textCan feel like two essays stapled together
Point-by-point — organize by comparative point, both texts per sectionKeeps the comparison active throughoutCan become mechanical if rigidly formulaic
Example 47.7 — The Comparative Thesis
Keats's "Ode to a Nightingale" and Shelley's "To a Skylark"
  • Weak "This essay will compare 'Ode to a Nightingale' by Keats and 'To a Skylark' by Shelley."Announces a topic, not an argument.
  • Strong "While both poets use bird imagery to represent an ideal beauty unavailable to human consciousness, their poems arrive at opposite conclusions about how the poet should respond: Keats accepts limitation, while Shelley refuses it — a difference that reflects two distinct Romantic responses to the problem of the ideal and the real."States what is compared, what each text does, and what the comparison reveals.
Principle: A comparative thesis must state what the comparison reveals, not merely that a comparison exists.

Section 47.8

Revising Literary Analysis

A first draft of a literary analysis essay is rarely an analysis essay. It is often a collection of observations, partial arguments, useful quotations, and the beginning of ideas that need to be developed. Revision is the process of turning that collection into a coherent argument. Revision is not proofreading.

The Revision Hierarchy

1. Thesis and argument (most important)  ·  2. Paragraph structure and organization  ·  3. Evidence and explanation  ·  4. Introductions and conclusions  ·  5. Sentence-level clarity and style  ·  6. Grammar, spelling, and punctuation (last, not first)

Common Revision Problems

The floating quotation — appears with no framing and no explanation. The thin paragraph — a claim and one quotation but no explanation. The disconnected paragraph — a claim that does not connect to the thesis. The summary drift — begins analytically but drifts into plot summary. The dead end — a final sentence that does not connect back to the essay's argument.

Introductions and Conclusions

Introductions should move from a specific observation toward the thesis, introduce the text, and end with the thesis — never beginning with a vague universal generalization.

Conclusions should return to the thesis and show what the essay has demonstrated, extend or complicate the argument, and avoid introducing new, unsupported claims.

Text in Focus

The following demonstration walks through the full process — from a short invented poem to a reading question to a thesis to a finished analytical paragraph.

The Empty Frame My grandmother's portrait hangs
where hers used to be.
I notice how the light still falls
the same way every morning —
as if it does not know
that the face has changed.
Demonstration — Notice, Question, Thesis, Paragraph
Applying the MGU Close Reading Model step by step
  • Notice The poem is about substitution: one portrait replacing another. The light "does not know" the face has changed — personification. The poem never tells us whose face was there before.
  • Question Why does the poem focus on the light rather than the portrait itself? What does giving the light near-consciousness do?
  • Thesis The poem uses the personification of light — its "not knowing" the face has changed — to suggest that the natural world's indifference to human loss is also a kind of continuity the speaker finds both painful and comforting.
  • Paragraph The poem places the burden of grief not in the speaker but in the natural world's failure to register human change. The conditional phrase "as if it does not know" grants the light a consciousness it cannot literally have, then immediately withdraws that consciousness with "as if." This double movement creates the poem's emotional tension: the speaker projects unknowing onto the light rather than naming their own grief directly, enacting the very numbness the poem describes.
Principle: Claim, evidence, explanation, and connection — built directly from close-reading annotations.

Reader's Question

Before Reading the Workshop Below
Diagnose this paragraph yourself first

"The story uses a lot of symbols. The red dress that Maria wears in the first chapter is a symbol of her passion and danger. Later, when she changes into a white dress, this symbolizes her innocence and purity. The color symbolism is very effective. The author uses colors throughout the story to show what Maria is feeling."

Is there a clear analytical claim? Is there specific evidence? Is the evidence explained, or just labeled? Is there plot summary? What would need to change to make this a strong analytical paragraph?

Interpretation Workshop

The paragraph above has four problems: a vague claim ("uses a lot of symbols" could describe almost any text); label-application without explanation (naming conventional color meanings instead of showing how the text builds them); a vague evaluation ("very effective" — for whom, compared to what?); and summary drift ("uses colors…to show what Maria is feeling" describes technique without analyzing effect).

Workshop Revision
From label-application to genuine analysis
  • Revised The story's color symbolism does not simply describe Maria's emotional state — it tracks the tension between the identity others impose on her and the identity she is trying to construct for herself. When Maria enters in the red dress, the text focuses on the reaction it provokes in others: she is "watched," "judged," "categorized." The red dress is not her symbol but the community's symbol for her. When she changes into white, she does not choose it — it is "given" to her by another character. The shift in color is not liberation but compliance; the novel's color symbolism exposes how conventional associations are used as instruments of social control.
Principle: A specific, arguable claim; specific evidence; close explanation; and connection to a larger thematic concern.
Context and Common Misreadings
Where the Essay Form Comes From — and Where Students Go Wrong

Context Window

The History of the Academic Literary Essay

The literary essay as a formal genre has roots in classical rhetoric and the Renaissance essay tradition, but the specifically academic literary essay — built around a thesis, evidence, and argumentation — developed primarily in universities in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries.

Early literary criticism was often more impressionistic: critics like Samuel Johnson, William Hazlitt, and Charles Lamb wrote essays that mixed memoir, aesthetic response, and judgment. The New Criticism of the mid-twentieth century — associated with critics like Cleanth Brooks and I.A. Richards — elevated close reading and textual evidence to the center of literary study, shaping the form of the academic literary essay that remains standard today.

Contemporary literary criticism has expanded to include historical, biographical, feminist, postcolonial, and cultural approaches — but the foundation of evidence-based argument derived from close reading remains central to all of them.

World Literature Connection

Writing About Literature Across Traditions

The form of the Western academic literary essay is not a universal form. In classical Chinese literary culture, literary commentary (pingdian) was a practice of marginal annotation, valuing the reader's intimate relationship with the text over systematic argument. In Sanskrit literary culture, alankara shastra — the study of literary ornament and style — produced detailed treatises on poetic figures, organized around aesthetic essence (rasa) rather than argumentative thesis. In the Arabic literary tradition, criticism (naqd) combined formal attention to craft with ethical reflection on literature's effects on audiences.

Close reading — careful attention to language, structure, and pattern — is broadly applicable across these traditions. But the specific conventions of the Western analytical essay are themselves culturally situated. Writing well about world literature means bringing analytical rigor while remaining curious about how different traditions have understood literary commentary.

Common Misreading

These are the five most common mistakes in literary analysis essays.

Mistake 1
The Paraphrase Essay

An essay that retells the text rather than analyzing it. Your verbs are mostly narrative (happens, says, describes, goes) and your paragraphs could be understood by someone who had not read the assigned text.

Fix: For every paragraph, ask whether you are telling the reader what the text says or explaining what the text does.

Mistake 2
The Theme Statement Essay

An essay that identifies a theme but never analyzes how the text develops it — finding evidence that a theme exists rather than explaining the formal choices doing the thematic work.

Fix: The thesis should say not just what the theme is but how the text builds it.

Mistake 3
The Quotation Dump

Paragraphs consisting of a brief claim followed by several quotations in sequence with minimal explanation between them — more quoted words than your own.

Fix: One quotation per paragraph is usually sufficient. Spend your energy fully explaining one piece of evidence.

Mistake 4
The Author's Intention Fallacy

An essay that speculates repeatedly about what the author "meant" or "intended" rather than analyzing what the text does.

Fix: Focus on the text itself. You may use the author's name as shorthand, but evidence should come from the text.

Mistake 5
The Grand Conclusion

An essay that ends with a broad, unsupported claim about humanity, society, or the universe that goes far beyond what the textual evidence can support.

Fix: Let your conclusion stay close to the text. Extend your specific argument rather than abandoning it for a vague universal claim.

Chapter 47 — Back Matter

Writing Practice, Checklist, and Review

Writing About Literature

Activity 47.1Essay Practice: Full Process
  1. Choose a text. Select a poem, short story, or scene from a play that you have read closely in this course.
  2. Annotate. Mark specific words or phrases that interest you, repeated images, structural features, tone shifts, and anything that surprises or puzzles you.
  3. Form a question. Identify one analytical question that the text makes you want to answer.
  4. Form a tentative thesis. Answer your question in one or two sentences.
  5. Gather evidence. Identify three to five specific passages, lines, or moments that support your thesis.
  6. Outline three to four body paragraphs, each with a claim, evidence, and a plan for explanation.
  7. Draft the full essay, allowing the argument to be explored and adjusted as you write.
  8. Revise using the hierarchy from Section 47.8: thesis, structure, evidence, explanation, then style.
  9. Final check: Does every paragraph connect to the thesis? Does every quotation have an introduction and explanation? Is the conclusion specific to the text?
Activity 47.2Paragraph Revision
  1. Take the following weak paragraph and revise it into a strong one: "The setting of the story is very important. The author describes the city in detail, and this makes the reader feel like they are really there. The city represents the protagonist's life in many ways. The description of the city is one of the best parts of the story." Add a specific claim, textual evidence (you may invent a relevant quotation from an invented text), explanation, and connection.

Chapter 47 — Checklist
Use this checklist each time you write a literary analysis essay
Before Drafting
  • I have read the text closely and annotated it.
  • I have identified a specific analytical question.
  • I have formed a working thesis that makes a specific, arguable claim connected to meaning.
  • I have identified specific textual evidence that supports my thesis.
  • I have planned my body paragraphs with claims, evidence, and explanation in mind.
While Drafting
  • Each body paragraph opens with an analytical claim, not a quotation or transition.
  • Every quotation has a framing introduction and is followed by specific explanation.
  • I am analyzing how the text works, not retelling what the text says.
  • My paragraphs connect to my thesis.
While Revising
  • I have checked that my thesis matches the essay I actually wrote.
  • I have checked each paragraph for claim, evidence, and explanation.
  • I have removed or converted any plot summary into analysis.
  • My introduction moves from specific observation to thesis, not vague generalization.
  • My conclusion extends the argument rather than repeating the thesis or overreaching.
  • I have proofread for grammar, spelling, and punctuation last.

  5. What is the difference between explanation and paraphrase in the context of evidence? Give an example of each.
  6. What is the revision hierarchy, and why does it suggest revising thesis and argument before revising grammar and spelling?
  7. In comparative literary analysis, what makes a comparison productive rather than merely descriptive? What question should a comparative thesis answer?
  8. Apply the MGU Close Reading Model (Notice, Question, Pattern, Interpret, Support, Connect) to a short poem or passage of your choosing and describe what you find at each stage.

Chapter 40 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Most of the world's greatest literature was not written in English. The Iliad and the Odyssey were composed in ancient Greek. The Epic of Gilgamesh was written in Akkadian cuneiform. The Tale of Genji was written in classical Japanese. One Thousand and One Nights was compiled in Arabic. Dante's Divine Comedy was written in medieval Italian. Don Quixote was written in Spanish. War and Peace was written in Russian. The poems of Rumi were composed in Persian.

When readers in English encounter these texts, they encounter them through translation. They are not reading Homer; they are reading a translator's rendering of Homer into English. They are not reading Rumi; they are reading Coleman Barks, or Arberry, or Nicholson — different translators, each with different interpretive commitments, different poetic instincts, different understandings of what it means to carry Persian mystical verse into the English-speaking world.

This is not a limitation to apologize for. It is a condition of reading to understand, embrace, and think carefully about. Translation is itself a form of literary art. It is also a form of interpretation. Every translation is an argument — an argument about what the source text means, how it sounds, what it values, what it is trying to do. Understanding this does not produce despair; it produces deeper, more attentive, more humble reading.

Close Reading Focus

This chapter teaches translational reading: understanding that when you read a work in translation, you are reading through an intermediary — a human interpreter who has made hundreds of choices about how to carry meaning, sound, rhythm, and cultural texture from one language into another. Students who understand how translation works become better readers of all world literature, more attentive to language, more humble about interpretation, and more curious about what lies behind the words on the page.

Chapter 40 — Key Literary Terms

Vocabulary for This Chapter

Translation
The practice of rendering a text written in one language into another, as fully and faithfully as the translator judges appropriate given the purpose of the translation.
Source language
The language in which a text was originally written; also called the original language or source text language.
Target language
The language into which a text is being translated; also called the receptor language.
Literal translation
A translation that follows the exact words and structures of the source text as closely as possible, prioritizing word-for-word correspondence over idiomatic naturalness.
Literary translation
A translation that prioritizes conveying the spirit, tone, rhythm, and literary effects of the source text, even when this requires departing from word-for-word correspondence.
Dynamic equivalence
A translation strategy that seeks to produce in readers of the target language the same effect the source text produced in its original readers, rather than reproducing the source text's formal structure.
Formal equivalence
A translation strategy that seeks to preserve the forms and structures of the source text as closely as possible, even when this produces unfamiliar constructions in the target language.
Untranslatable
A term for words, phrases, or concepts that have no direct equivalent in the target language and carry cultural, emotional, or philosophical meaning that resists clean transfer.
World literature
Literature that circulates beyond its original national or linguistic context, read and interpreted across cultural and linguistic boundaries — associated with Goethe's concept of Weltliteratur.
Domestication
A translation strategy that adapts the source text to feel natural and comfortable for readers in the target language and culture, minimizing foreignness.
Foreignization
A translation strategy that preserves elements of strangeness or cultural otherness from the source text, resisting the erasure of cultural difference.
Retranslation
The act of producing a new translation of a text that has already been translated, often for reasons of scholarly accuracy, artistic renewal, or cultural reinterpretation.
Translator's note
A note written by the translator explaining choices made in the translation, cultural context the reader may need, or difficulties the original text posed.
Calque
A loan translation in which the structure or elements of a word or phrase from the source language are translated piece by piece into the target language.
Loan word
A word borrowed directly from the source language and used without translation in the target text, usually because no equivalent exists.
Paraphrase
A loose rendering of the sense of a text without close attention to its specific words or structures; sometimes used in translation when literal equivalence is impossible.

Section 40.1

Why Translation Matters

Literature and the Language Barrier

Every language is a world. Not just a set of words and grammar rules, but a system for experiencing and articulating reality — shaped by geography, history, religion, social structure, philosophy, and thousands of years of accumulated expression. Languages carve up experience differently. They have different ways of marking time, different words for emotions that other languages lack words for, different resources for sound and rhythm and metaphor.

When a text crosses from one language into another, it enters a different world. Some things come through easily. Some things come through only with difficulty, in altered form. Some things do not come through at all without explanatory scaffolding. And some things appear in translation that were not there before — the translator's own voice, the translator's own interpretive choices, the resonances of the target language that the original did not possess.

Understanding this is the first step toward reading translated literature well.

Why We Translate

People have been translating texts for as long as writing has existed. The motivations are many: to spread religious teachings, to conduct diplomacy, to expand commerce, to satisfy intellectual curiosity, to make literary treasures available beyond their original linguistic community, to build national literary traditions, to challenge or subvert existing cultural hierarchies.

The ancient Romans translated Greek texts extensively — not only to access Greek knowledge and literature, but to build Latin literary culture. Medieval European scholars translated Arabic scientific and philosophical texts, making the works of Aristotle (and Arab thinkers who had elaborated on Aristotle) available to European intellectual life. The translation of the Bible into vernacular languages was among the most consequential acts of translation in Western history, reshaping religious communities, national languages, and literary culture.

Today, translation is how the world reads. A novel published in Korean reaches readers in Brazil, France, Nigeria, and the United States through translation. A poem composed in Swahili can appear in anthologies in Swedish and Japanese. Translation is the infrastructure of world literature.

Why Translation Is Hard

If translation were simply a matter of substituting words — finding the English word for the French word — it would be a mechanical task that computers could perform perfectly. But anyone who has read machine-translated text knows it is not literature. Translation is hard precisely because language is not a word-for-word code.

ChallengeWhat It MeansExample
Semantic mismatchWords in different languages rarely map perfectly onto each otherGerman Schadenfreude means pleasure in another's misfortune — no single English word exists
Grammatical structureLanguages build sentences differently, creating different rhythms of anticipationGerman often places the verb at the end of a clause, creating suspense English word order cannot reproduce

Core Principle

Translation is not substitution. It is interpretation. Every translator makes choices, and those choices shape the text the reader receives. Understanding this changes how you read world literature — it makes you more attentive, more curious, and more honest about the limits of any single translation.

Section 40.2

Language, Meaning, and Culture

Language Is Not Neutral

One of the most important things to understand about translation is that language is never just a transparent vehicle for meaning. Language carries culture. It carries history. It carries the accumulated experience of the communities that have spoken it.

Consider what a single word carries. The English word "home" is not just a synonym for "house." It carries emotional connotations of belonging, warmth, safety, familiarity, family, return. Different languages have different words in this semantic space. The German Heimat carries connotations of homeland, region, community, and belonging — and has specific historical resonances in German culture. The Arabic watan carries its own political and emotional weight in the context of Arab nationalism, colonialism, and diaspora. None of these words is identical, and translating any of them into the others involves interpretation.

Untranslatable Words

The category of the "untranslatable" refers not to words whose meaning cannot be conveyed in other languages, but to words whose meaning cannot be conveyed in a single word or phrase — it requires explanation, context, unpacking. Literary translators handle these in various ways.

Example 40.2 — Strategies for Untranslatable Words
How translators handle concepts with no clean equivalent
  • Loan Word Import the original term directly. The German Schadenfreude has entered English as a loan word. This preserves the foreignness of the concept — the reader encounters it as something belonging to another culture — but requires the reader to know or learn the term.
  • Approximation Find the closest equivalent in the target language, accepting some loss of nuance. The Portuguese saudade might become "longing" or "nostalgia," neither of which is quite right but both of which gesture toward the emotional territory.
  • Paraphrase Provide a brief explanatory phrase in place of a single word. Japanese mono no aware might become "the bittersweet awareness of impermanence." The meaning is conveyed, but the compression of the original is lost.
  • Footnote or Gloss Use the original term and explain it in a footnote. This preserves the word while providing context — but footnotes interrupt reading and signal that the reader is receiving mediated access to the text.
Each strategy makes a different trade-off. The reader who recognizes these strategies reads with greater awareness of what the translation is doing.

Cultural Context Embedded in Language

Beyond individual words, entire cultural frameworks are embedded in language use. Japanese has an elaborate system of honorifics — different grammatical forms that indicate the social relationship between speaker and listener. When a character speaks to a social superior, the language itself changes. Translating Japanese dialogue into English means deciding how to convey these social relationships when English does not have the same grammatical apparatus.

Sacred texts use language shaped by centuries of theological, liturgical, and devotional practice. When an Arabic poem alludes to a Qur'anic phrase, the allusion carries weight that a non-Arabic-speaking, non-Muslim reader may not receive at all, unless the translator notes it. Texts that emerge from oral traditions — epics, folk narratives, praise poetry — carry the rhythms, formulas, and performance conventions of spoken language that written translation can only approximate.

Reading Strategy — Notice the Strangeness

When something in a translated text feels culturally unfamiliar — a metaphor that seems unexpected, a narrative structure that doesn't follow familiar patterns, a character relationship coded differently than you expect — stay with the strangeness rather than smoothing it over. It may be a sign that you are encountering genuine cultural difference the translator has preserved.

Strangeness in a translated text is often not a failure of the translation. It is the translation doing its job — carrying difference across the linguistic boundary and asking you to meet it.

Section 40.3

Translator Choices

The Translator as Interpreter

Every translator makes choices. This is not a failure of translation; it is its nature. The translator stands between two texts and two worlds, trying to carry something from one to the other. At every step, there are decisions to make: this word or that word, this syntax or that syntax, this rhythm or that rhythm, this footnote or no footnote, this loan word or this paraphrase.

Good translators are guided by a philosophy of translation — a sense of what translation should accomplish and for whom. Some believe their job is to bring the reader as close as possible to the experience of reading the original. Others believe their job is to make the text feel natural and alive in the target language. Others aim for scholarly accuracy; others for literary beauty. Often these goals compete.

Word Choice

When Homer uses the Greek word that is often translated as "wily" or "cunning" or "crafty" or "much-traveled" to describe Odysseus, the choice of English equivalent shapes the characterization. "Wily" suggests cleverness and possible dishonesty. "Crafty" implies something slightly sinister. "Many-minded" is more neutral and philosophical. "Much-traveled" emphasizes experience rather than intellect. Every instance is a translator's interpretation.

Example 40.3 — The Same Image, Three Translations
How word and syntax choices transform literary effect
  • Literal "The moon hangs like a lantern over the cold mountain."Stays close to the original structure. The word order is preserved; the image is transferred without transformation. Useful for study; may sound slightly flat in isolation.
  • Idiomatic "Over cold peaks, the moon shines steady as a lamp."More natural English rhythm. "Lantern" becomes "lamp"; "hangs" becomes "shines steady." The image is comparable but the texture of the English is smoother, more contemporary.
  • Compressed "A lantern moon above the freezing height."Compressed and rhythmic — more like a poem. "Cold mountain" becomes "freezing height," which is more dramatic. "Hangs" disappears; the juxtaposition does the work.
None is simply right. Each reflects a philosophy: the literal version serves understanding; the idiomatic version serves readability; the compressed version serves literary effect.

What Gets Added — What Gets Lost

Translators do not only subtract from texts. When a translator uses a footnote, a cultural explanation, or a target-language proverb in place of a source-language one, they are adding. When a translator chooses an English word with particular resonances, they are adding those resonances. A sophisticated translation often adds a great deal — this is not falsification, but the necessary work of making a text readable across cultural distance.

And inevitably, things are lost. The music of a language cannot be fully reproduced in another language. Cultural resonances cannot be fully carried. Wordplay that depends on the specific sounds or structures of the source language cannot be translated without transformation. Understanding loss is not meant to produce despair. It is meant to produce attentiveness and humility.

Section 40.4

Literal and Literary Translation

The Spectrum of Approaches

Translation approaches exist along a spectrum, from the maximally literal to the maximally free. At the literal end, translators reproduce the source text's words, structures, and forms as closely as possible — aiming for transparency so the reader can see through to the original. At the literary end, translators aim to achieve comparable literary effects in the target language: a literary translation of a poem aims to be, itself, a poem. Most professional translators work somewhere between these poles.

StrategyPriorityStrengthLimitation
Literal / FormalWord-for-word fidelity; source structureScholarly accuracy; shows how original works; preserves foreignnessOften sounds awkward; may render literature unliterary
Dynamic / LiteraryEquivalent effect; readable target-language textProduces alive, literary texts; honors literature as experienceGives translator great power to depart from original; reader may be misled about source
ForeignizingPreserve otherness; resist domesticationReminds reader the text comes from a different worldMay feel strange or difficult for general readers
DomesticatingNatural feel in target cultureAccessible and comfortable for target readersMay erase cultural difference; can mislead readers about the original

Dynamic Equivalence in Practice

The concept of dynamic equivalence, developed by translation theorist Eugene Nida, argues that the goal of translation should be to produce in the reader of the target language the same response that the original produced in its readers. A passage that was funny in the original should be funny in translation; a passage that was solemn should be solemn.

Dynamic equivalence gives translators license to depart from literal correspondence in the service of equivalent effect. If a joke depends on wordplay that does not exist in the target language, the translator may substitute a different joke that produces the same kind of laughter — even using different material. The strength of this approach is that it produces texts that function as literature. The limitation is that it opens the door to significant departure from the original, and the translator must make difficult judgments about what "equivalent effect" means.

Text in Focus

Four Translations of Dante's Inferno, Canto I

To make the dynamics of translation concrete, consider the opening lines of Dante's Inferno, one of the most translated literary texts in the world. The original Italian opens:

The Italian Original — Rough Literal Rendering
Nel mezzo del cammin di nostra vita
mi ritrovai per una selva oscura,
ché la diritta via era smarrita.

In the middle of the journey of our life / I found myself in a dark forest, / because the straight way was lost.

Four Translations Compared

Longfellow
19th century · Formal fidelity
Midway upon the journey of our life
I found myself within a forest dark,
For the straightforward pathway had been lost.
Preserves Italian word order ("within a forest dark"). Elevated, archaic diction. Foreignizing approach — the reader feels the text's otherness.
Ciardi
Mid-20th century · American literary idiom
Midway in our life's journey, I went astray
from the straight road and woke to find myself
alone in a dark wood.
Adds "went astray" and "woke" — interpretations not in the Italian. More psychological, more American in feel. Domesticating approach.
Hollander & Hollander
Early 21st century · Scholarly with literary care
Midway in the journey of our life
I came to myself in a dark wood,
for the straight way was lost.
"I came to myself" adds psychological and theological resonance. Suggests waking or recovery of self — thematically rich interpretive choice.
Pinsky
Late 20th century · Poet-translator
In the middle of the journey of our life
I came to myself within a dark wood
where the straight way was lost.
Close to literal but with a poet's ear. "Came to myself" echoes Hollander but feels more natural. The verse breathes without straining for effect.
Reader's Question

Before reading the interpretation below, spend a moment with these four versions. Ask yourself: Which version feels most natural in English? Which seems closest to what the Italian might be doing? What differences do you notice in word choice, rhythm, syntax, and tone? What does each choice do to your experience of the passage?

Notice that Ciardi adds "went astray" and "woke" — making the character's moral failure explicit and implying an unconscious state before the realization. The Italian contains neither. Notice that Longfellow inverts word order ("within a forest dark") to preserve the Italian's placement of oscura (dark) at the end of the line. Notice that both Hollander and Pinsky render mi ritrovai as "came to myself" — a phrase with psychological resonance — rather than "found myself."

Interpretation Workshop

Reading a Translation as an Interpretation

What do these differences reveal about translator philosophy?

Longfellow translates in an era when scholarly accuracy is a primary goal. He preserves Italian word order even when it produces slightly archaic English. His goal is to let readers feel the strangeness of the original rather than normalizing it into easy English. This is a foreignizing approach.

Ciardi translates in mid-20th-century America, when literary accessibility is a priority. He rewrites freely, departing from the literal meaning when it serves poetic vitality. "I went astray" and "woke to find myself" are interpretations of Dante's situation, not literal renderings. This is a domesticating approach that makes the speaker feel like an American literary voice.

The Hollanders work with a scholarly audience in mind. "I came to myself" alludes to the philosophical and theological dimension of Dante's journey: the poem is about waking up, about recovering the self from spiritual confusion. This is a choice that adds interpretive emphasis the Italian permits but does not foreground.

None of these translations is wrong. All of them are readings — arguments about what Dante's text is doing and how to make it available to English-speaking readers. A reader who compares these versions learns something not only about translation, but about the range of interpretive possibilities that any rich text contains.

Analyzing a Translator's Choice: Ciardi's Departures from the Italian

  • Evidence Longfellow: "I found myself within a forest dark, / For the straightforward pathway had been lost."
    Ciardi: "I went astray / from the straight road and woke to find myself / alone in a dark wood."
  • Explanation Ciardi adds "went astray" — making the character's moral failure explicit — and "woke," implying an unconscious state before realization that the Italian does not contain. Section 40.5

How to Compare Translations

When you have access to multiple translations of the same passage, work through them systematically. Each step deepens your reading.

  1. Step 1Notice what is the same. What elements appear in all translations? These are likely the stable, unambiguous parts of the original — elements every translator reads the same way.
  2. Step 2Notice what differs. Where do translations diverge? What words does one translator use that another avoids? What choices of syntax, register, or tone separate the versions?
  3. Step 3Ask why. For each significant difference, ask what might have led the translator to this choice. Is this a difference in interpretation of the original's meaning? A difference in target audience? A difference in translation philosophy?
  4. Step 4Ask what each version does. Each translation is a reading. What does each version emphasize or de-emphasize? What emotional or intellectual effect does each produce?
  5. Step 5Consider what this reveals about the original. If translators disagree, it is often because the original is genuinely ambiguous or rich — containing multiple possible readings. The disagreement is a clue to the original's complexity.
Three-Translation Practice Exercise

Take three imagined English renderings of the same short poem about loss. Version A uses "unnecessary grief" (formal, restrained). Version B uses "pointless sorrow" (conversational, contemporary). Version C uses "unnecessary anguish" (archaic, elevated register).

A student working through these notices that "grief," "sorrow," and "anguish" each carry different weight — grief is solemn, sorrow is personal, anguish is acute. The rhythmic feel of each version differs. Each creates a slightly different speaker — the same person, but with different emotional coloring. None of these versions is definitively correct. All are readings. The student who works through these versions knows the poem in a richer way than one who reads only a single translation.

Section 40.6

Translation and Poetry

Why Poetry Is the Hardest Case

Of all literary forms, poetry presents the greatest challenges for translation. Poetry is the literary form most thoroughly fused with the specific properties of a specific language. Its sounds, its rhythms, its visual form, its compression, its play between what is said and what is not said — all of these are inseparable from the language in which the poem is written.

Prose can be translated with relatively modest loss of formal properties. Drama can be translated with attention to dialogue, action, and character. But poetry's form is its meaning in a way that is true of no other literary form. To translate a poem is, in some sense, to write a new poem — or to produce a document that reports on a poem without fully being one.

Sound Cannot Be Carried

Sound is the most obvious casualty of translation. Alliteration, assonance, consonance, and rhyme in the source language simply do not exist in the target language. When Virgil writes in Latin dactylic hexameter, the rising and falling rhythm of the meter is part of the poem's meaning. English cannot reproduce Latin hexameter because English stress patterns and syllable counts work differently.

Rhyme presents a particular challenge. Languages rich in rhyme (Italian, Spanish) can support elaborate rhyme schemes that would feel forced in English, which has fewer rhymes. The terza rima of Dante's Divine Comedy — a complex interlocking rhyme scheme — is extremely difficult to reproduce in English without either sounding artificial or sacrificing naturalness. Some translators attempt it; others abandon rhyme and work in blank verse or prose.

Example 40.6 — Translating Bashō's Frog Haiku
The challenge of carrying a complete form across languages
  • Original Matsuo Bashō's famous haiku — in rough literal rendering — reads:
    Old pond —
    frog jumps in,
    sound of water.    The Japanese original has specific syllable patterns English cannot reproduce directly. It uses a seasonal reference (the frog signals spring). The meaning arrives in the gap between the two images — the stillness of the old pond, then the sudden action and sound.
  • 5-7-5 English Some translators reproduce the 5-7-5 syllable structure in English. English syllables function differently than Japanese syllables, so the English count produces very different rhythmic effects. The structure is preserved; the music is transformed.
  • Free Verse Others abandon the syllable structure and focus on the image and juxtaposition — what the poem does rather than how it is built. The result may read as a better English poem, but carries less of the original's formal constraints.
  • Interpretation Some add what the poem implies but does not state: "into the silence, a splash." This is an interpretive translation — the translator supplies a dimension (silence) that Bashō leaves for the reader's imagination.
Each English "poem" carries its own beauty while remaining an interpretation of an original that Japanese readers can experience directly and English readers cannot.
Context Window

Bible Translation: A Case Study in Stakes

No translation history better illustrates the stakes of translator choices than the history of Bible translation in English. The Christian Bible was written primarily in Hebrew and Greek. Jerome's Latin Vulgate (4th–5th centuries CE) was the standard Western Christian Bible for a thousand years. William Tyndale's early 16th-century English translation — for which he was executed — made Scripture accessible in vernacular language and shaped everything that followed. The King James Version (1611) drew heavily on Tyndale and became so influential that its phrases, cadences, and imagery are woven into the fabric of English literary culture across centuries.

20th and 21st-century Bible translations have departed from the King James tradition in various ways. Some, like the Revised Standard Version, aimed for scholarly accuracy with modern English while preserving some KJV dignity. Others, like The Message, aimed for contemporary accessibility, using idiomatic modern English that readers could approach without encountering archaic language.

Each translation tradition has generated fierce debate among religious communities and scholars — debate that reveals how profoundly translation choices matter to how communities understand sacred texts, and how much is at stake in the decision between literal fidelity and dynamic equivalence, between foreignization and domestication, between archaic dignity and contemporary accessibility.

Section 40.7

Translation and Global Circulation

World Literature as Circulation

The contemporary concept of "world literature" is not simply a catalog of texts from different national traditions. It is, at its core, a concept about circulation — about how texts move across linguistic, cultural, and national boundaries, gaining new readers, new interpretations, and new significance as they travel.

Goethe, who coined the term Weltliteratur (world literature) in the early 19th century, envisioned a future in which literature from all nations would participate in a global conversation. Translation is not the only vehicle for this conversation — multilingual readers can access texts in original languages — but for most readers, most of the time, translation is the essential bridge.

The Politics of Translation

Which texts get translated, and into which languages, is not a neutral matter. Translation is shaped by power. Historically, texts from dominant cultures and languages — especially European languages — have been translated into other languages far more extensively than texts from non-European traditions have been translated into European languages.

This asymmetry shapes which literatures are considered "world literature" and which are considered merely "regional." It shapes which authors receive international awards and readerships. Critics have noted that "world literature" often functions in practice as a category that includes non-Western texts that have been filtered through Western publishing, translation, and critical institutions — translated and anthologized according to Western literary values. Understanding this politics does not mean abandoning world literature. It means reading it with awareness — recognizing that the texts we encounter in translation have been selected and shaped by cultural processes, and that other voices exist which have not traveled these networks.

World Literature Connection

Translation Traditions Across Cultures

Translation is not a Western invention. Every major literary civilization has engaged in translation as a central cultural practice.

TraditionPeriodWhat Was TranslatedSignificance
Islamic Golden Age8th–13th centuries CEGreek philosophical, scientific, and literary texts translated into Arabic, often via Syriac intermediariesWithout this chain, much of ancient Greek thought might have been lost; Arab thinkers elaborated and extended these works before they were translated back into Latin for Europe
Classical IndiaCenturies-long traditionBuddhist texts moved from Sanskrit into Pali, then into Sinhala, Burmese, Thai, and other languages; the Ramayana and Mahabharata adapted across regional traditionsEach regional version is not simply a translation but a creative adaptation reflecting local cultural values — the boundary between translation and literary creation is deliberately fluid
Tang Dynasty China7th–10th centuries CEExtensive translation of Buddhist texts from Sanskrit into classical ChineseShaped Chinese Buddhism, Chinese literary culture, and eventually Japanese and Korean literary traditions — demonstrating translation as a vehicle of civilizational influence

These histories illustrate that translation has been, across cultures and centuries, one of the primary mechanisms by which literary and intellectual traditions develop. Traditions that seem self-contained have in fact been built through encounter with other traditions, mediated by translation. The assumption of cultural self-sufficiency in literary history is almost always historically inaccurate.

Section 40.8

Reading World Literature with Humility

What Humility Means

The word "humility" in the context of reading world literature does not mean self-abasement or the abandonment of critical engagement. It means an accurate understanding of one's position as a reader.

When you read a text in translation, you are reading as an outsider to the tradition that produced the text. You may be a skilled and sensitive reader; you may bring genuine literary knowledge and cultural curiosity to the encounter. But you do not have the immediate, intuitive, culturally embedded knowledge that a reader who grew up within that tradition possesses. This is not a failure — it is simply a condition.

What Humility Means in Practice

Recognizing that your response to a translated text is a response to a version, not to the original. Recognizing that things may be lost or transformed in translation that you cannot fully perceive. Recognizing that you may be missing cultural context that shapes the text's meaning. Remaining curious rather than certain. Being willing to revise your interpretation as you learn more.

What Humility Permits

Humility is not the same as passivity. A humble reader can still engage deeply with the translated text, develop carefully supported interpretations, make comparisons between texts from different traditions, experience genuine aesthetic and emotional responses, and draw conclusions about themes, forms, and meanings.

The difference is that the humble reader holds these interpretations with some tentativeness — as readings, not as final verdicts. The humble reader acknowledges the mediated nature of their encounter with the text. The humble reader remains open to learning that they have missed something. This is, in fact, the condition of all literary reading — world literature in translation makes this condition visible and pressing in a way that makes all readers better.

Practical Strategies

  1. 1Read translator's notes and introductions. These documents explain the translator's philosophy, the challenges the text presented, and the choices made. They are resources, not obstacles.
  2. 2Research cultural context. Before or while reading a text from an unfamiliar tradition, find out something about the culture, period, and tradition that produced it. Enough context to read with informed curiosity rather than blank unfamiliarity.
  3. 3Notice what feels strange. Strangeness may be a sign that you are encountering genuine cultural difference the translator has preserved. Rather than smoothing it over, stay with it. Ask what it might mean in the tradition that produced it.
  4. 4Read more than one text from a tradition. A single novel or poem cannot represent an entire literary tradition. Reading more than one text gives you a sense of range and diversity.
  5. 5Seek multiple translations when possible. For canonical texts, compare at least two translations. The comparison will deepen your understanding of both the text and translation as a practice.
  6. 6Look for writers and scholars from within the tradition. Criticism produced by scholars who work within the tradition you are reading can offer perspectives that outsider critics cannot provide.
  7. 7Accept the partial. You will not fully understand every text from every tradition. The partial encounter with another tradition's literature is still a genuine encounter. Accept it with gratitude and curiosity.
Chapter 40 — Common Misreading

What to Watch For

Misreading 1
Treating a translation as transparent

The most common misreading of translated literature is treating the translation as if it were the original — reading it as a transparent window onto a text and culture you can judge with confidence. A reader who encounters a Chinese novel in translation and concludes that "Chinese narrative style is characterized by x" based entirely on a single translation is over-reading. The translation is one reader's interpretation, shaped by that translator's philosophy, the conventions of the target language, the demands of the publishing market, and dozens of other factors. A different translation might produce a different impression.

Misreading 2
Assuming the most available translation is the most authoritative

Popular translations often achieve their popularity through readability, not through scholarly accuracy or literary distinction. A translation that was authoritative in one century may be superseded by later scholarship. A translation that feels modern and accessible may depart significantly from the original. This does not mean you should distrust widely read translations. It means you should remain curious about other translations, read translator's notes critically, and understand that your encounter with any text is shaped by the specific translation you have encountered.

Misreading 3
Projecting familiar literary expectations onto unfamiliar traditions

If you expect a narrative to build toward a climax and resolution in the way familiar from Western realistic fiction, and you encounter a narrative that operates on different principles — cyclical rather than linear, episodic rather than arc-shaped, interested in portraying experience rather than resolving conflict — you may read the unfamiliar structure as a failure rather than as a different and valid literary approach. World literature asks you to stretch your expectations, recognize that your familiar literary categories are themselves culturally shaped, and encounter other forms of literary organization with genuine openness.

Chapter 40 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary, Review, and Practice

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 40.1Translation is the essential vehicle of world literary culture — the infrastructure through which texts move across linguistic and cultural boundaries. It is hard because language carries culture, grammar, sound, and register that resist clean transfer across languages.
  • Sec 40.2Language is not neutral; it carries the accumulated experience of the communities that have spoken it. Untranslatable words reveal the limits of cross-linguistic transfer, and translators handle them through loan words, approximation, paraphrase, or footnotes — each choice with different consequences for the reader.
  • Sec 40.3Every translator makes interpretive choices — about words, tone, register, syntax, what to add, and what to accept losing. Understanding these choices allows readers to recognize translation as interpretation, not substitution.
  • Sec 40.4Translation approaches range from literal (formal equivalence, foreignization) to literary (dynamic equivalence, domestication). Neither extreme is simply right; most professional translators work between the poles, and the best translations are honest about their philosophy.
  • Sec 40.5Comparing multiple translations of the same passage reveals the range of interpretive possibilities any rich text contains. The five-step comparison method — notice the same, notice differences, ask why, ask what each does, and consider what this reveals about the original — deepens literary interpretation.
  • Sec 40.6Poetry presents the greatest translation challenge because its form is its meaning — sound, rhythm, wordplay, and cultural reference are inseparable from the specific language in which the poem was written. Translating a poem means, to some degree, writing a new poem.
  • Sec 40.7World literature as a concept is about circulation — how texts travel across linguistic and cultural boundaries shaped by power, economics, and cultural institutions. The politics of translation shapes which texts are available to international readers and what counts as "world literature."
  • Sec 40.8Reading world literature with humility means holding interpretations with appropriate tentativeness, using practical strategies (translator's notes, multiple translations, cultural context research, attentiveness to strangeness), and accepting partial encounters as genuine and valuable.

Central Principle

Chapter 40 — The Central Teaching

Translation is interpretation, and reading in translation means reading through an interpreter's reading. This is not a reason to read world literature less. It is a reason to read it more carefully, more curiously, and more honestly. Translation makes visible what is always true about reading: that meaning is not fixed, that every encounter with a text is mediated, and that interpretation is an act of participation in a larger, ongoing conversation.

  3. What is dynamic equivalence? How does it differ from formal equivalence? In what situations might each approach be more appropriate?
  4. Why is poetry considered especially challenging to translate? Identify at least three specific properties of poetry that resist translation and explain why each is difficult to carry across languages.
  5. What does the concept of world literature mean, and what role does translation play in it? How does the politics of translation shape what counts as "world literature"?

Discussion Prompts

Discussion 1
Your Encounter with Translation

Think about a book, poem, or film you have experienced in translation (or one that you know exists in a different original language). Did you think about the translation as you experienced it? What would change if you could read or see it in the original language? What do you think you might be missing — and what might the translation have given you that even the original could not?

Discussion 2
The Translator's Responsibility

A translator has the power to make choices that shape how millions of readers understand a text. What responsibilities does this power carry? Should translators aim for invisibility — making readers forget they are reading a translation — or should they make their presence and choices visible? What are the consequences of each approach for readers' understanding of world literature?

Discussion 3
Untranslatable Words

Research two "untranslatable" words from any language of your choice (a brief online search will give you many options). For each word, consider: How would you explain it in English? What does the existence of this word suggest about the culture that uses it? What would English literary writing be able to do differently if this word existed in English? How would a translator handle this word if it appeared in a poem they were translating?

Discussion 4
Is There Such a Thing as a Perfect Translation?

Some scholars argue that every translation is a betrayal — traduttore, traditore (translator, traitor) in the Italian phrase. Others argue that translation is a form of creative partnership with the original, producing something new and valuable rather than merely replicating something old. Which view do you find more persuasive, and why? What would it mean for a translation to be "as good as" the original?

Writing About Literature

Activity 40.1Translation Comparison Essay
  1. Find two published English translations of the same short passage — a paragraph or short poem — from a work written in another language. Appropriate sources include the opening pages of Homer's Iliad or Odyssey, a passage from Dante's Inferno, a poem by Rumi, Neruda, or Rilke, or the opening of Tolstoy's Anna Karenina.
  2. Write a 600–800 word analysis identifying at least three specific differences between the translations. For each difference, ask why the translators might have made different choices. Discuss what each version emphasizes or de-emphasizes. Argue which version better serves a particular kind of reader. Conclude with a reflection on what the comparison reveals about the original text. Use the MGU Literary Analysis Paragraph Model to structure your analysis of each specific difference: Claim — Evidence (quote both versions) — Explanation — Connection to broader meaning.
Activity 40.2Translator's Note Exercise
  1. Choose a translated text with a translator's note or introduction (most literary translations include one). Read the note carefully, then read the first chapter or several poems of the translation. Write a 400–500 word response that summarizes the translator's stated goals and philosophy, identifies at least two moments in the translation where you can see these goals at work, evaluates whether the translator seems to have achieved what they set out to achieve, and raises one question the translator's note left unanswered.
Activity 40.3Reading Journal for World Literature
  1. Choose a work of world literature from a tradition you are not deeply familiar with. As you read, keep a reading journal noting: moments of strangeness — things that feel culturally unfamiliar; questions about whether something might be lost in translation; things you want to know more about; and moments of unexpected recognition — themes or experiences that feel universal despite cultural distance.
  2. After finishing the work, write a 500-word reflection on what reading with this kind of awareness taught you about the text, the tradition it comes from, and your own assumptions as a reader.
Activity 40.4Translation as Interpretation — Applied
  1. Take any two of the four Dante translations presented in this chapter (Longfellow, Ciardi, the Hollanders, Pinsky). Using the MGU Comparative Reading Model, write a structured comparison: identify the shared question the passage addresses; compare form and approach; compare what each translation emphasizes about the historical, spiritual, or narrative context; compare language and imagery; compare the emotional or thematic effects; and conclude with what your comparison reveals about the interpretive possibilities of Dante's opening.

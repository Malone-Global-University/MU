Chapter 22 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Close Reading Focus

This chapter teaches you to read narrative structure as a meaningful choice. Stories are not simply sequences of events that happen one after another. They are carefully arranged designs in which the order, pacing, emphasis, and shape of events work together to create meaning. When you read a story closely, you should ask not only what happens but why events happen in this order, what the conflict reveals, and how the story's shape reinforces its themes. Plot is not the mere container of a story; it is one of fiction's most powerful interpretive tools.

Every story you have ever read — however complex or simple, ancient or contemporary, brief or encyclopedic — makes choices about arrangement. Events could be presented in any number of orders. A narrative could begin at the end, in the middle, or at a point of crisis before the reader knows enough to fully understand what is at stake. The story could move in a straight line from cause to effect, or it could loop and circle back, embedding memory inside the present, placing scenes side by side that are years apart in time. It could rush through decades in a paragraph and linger for a hundred pages on a single day. Every one of these structural decisions shapes the meaning the reader takes away.

This is why plot — the deliberate arrangement of events — is one of the most powerful interpretive tools in fiction. Plot is not simply what happens. It is how what happens is presented: in what order, at what speed, with what emphasis, with what information withheld or revealed at each stage. Two novels could contain exactly the same events and mean entirely different things depending on how those events are sequenced and paced.

Chapter 22 teaches you to read narrative structure analytically. It builds on Chapter 21's introduction to what fiction does and prepares you for Chapter 23's examination of character, motivation, and development. Together, these chapters give you the foundational tools for understanding how fiction creates meaning not through events alone but through the craft of narrative design.

Opening Question

Imagine two stories built from exactly the same events: a young woman leaves her hometown, lives abroad for ten years, and returns to find that the life she imagined waiting for her has changed beyond recognition. The first story begins with her departure and moves forward in time. The second begins with her standing at the door of her childhood home, then moves backward through her years away, then returns to this moment of return. Both stories contain the same events. Which produces a different reading experience — and why? What do you gain or lose when a story begins at the end rather than the beginning? By the end of this chapter, you will have the vocabulary and interpretive tools to answer these questions precisely, and to apply that precision to any work of fiction you encounter.

Chapter 22 — Key Literary Terms

Vocabulary for This Chapter

These terms are tools for interpretation, not labels to memorize. Learn each one by understanding what it helps you notice and explain in a narrative. The definitions below are written to be used — return to them as you work through the sections that follow and as you apply this chapter's ideas to your own reading.

Plot
The deliberate arrangement of events in a narrative, as distinct from the raw chronological sequence of happenings.
Story
The full chronological sequence of events in a narrative, including those implied or occurring offstage.
Exposition
The opening portion of a narrative that establishes setting, characters, and situation before the central conflict begins.
Inciting Incident
The event that disrupts the initial situation and sets the main conflict in motion.
Rising Action
The escalating sequence of complications, decisions, and confrontations building toward the climax.
Conflict
The tension between opposing forces that drives a narrative forward; the engine of plot.
Climax
The moment of highest tension or the decisive turning point in a narrative; the point at which the conflict reaches its peak.
Falling Action
The sequence of events after the climax in which consequences unfold and the narrative moves toward resolution.
Resolution (Dénouement)
The conclusion of the narrative in which the central conflict is settled, dissolved, or left deliberately open.
Suspense
The reader's experience of uncertainty or anticipation about what will happen next.
Flashback (Analepsis)
A narrative technique in which the story moves backward in time to depict earlier events.
Foreshadowing
A technique by which later events are anticipated or hinted at earlier in the narrative.
In Medias Res
Latin for "in the middle of things"; a technique in which a narrative begins at a point of high action rather than the chronological beginning.
Nonlinear Narrative
A story told in a sequence that does not follow strict chronological order.
Subplot
A secondary narrative strand that runs alongside the main plot, often commenting on or complicating the central conflict.
Frame Narrative
A story that contains another story within it; an outer narrative that encloses an inner one.
Pacing
The speed at which a narrative moves through events; the use of scene, summary, and pause to control the reader's experience of time.
Peripeteia / Anagnorisis
Classical terms from Aristotle: peripeteia is reversal of fortune; anagnorisis is the moment of crucial recognition or discovery.
Sections 22.1 – 22.2
Story, Plot, and the Pre-Conflict World

Section 22.1

Story Versus Plot

One of the most useful distinctions in narrative theory is the difference between story and plot. The distinction sounds simple, but it has profound consequences for how you read and interpret fiction.

Story refers to the full sequence of events in a narrative, arranged in chronological order — everything that happens, from beginning to end, in the order it actually occurred within the fictional world. Plot refers to the specific arrangement of those events as presented to the reader: the order in which we encounter them, the emphasis given to certain moments, the speed at which we move through time, and the information we are given or withheld at each stage.

Consider a simple example. Imagine a narrative about a woman named Mira who grew up in poverty, worked for twenty years to save enough money to return to her childhood home, and found it had been torn down. The story runs chronologically: childhood poverty, years of work, the journey home, the discovery of absence. But a plot built from these materials might begin with Mira standing in front of an empty lot, then move backward through her years of effort, then further back to her childhood memories of the house, then return to the present moment of loss. Same events. Entirely different reading experience. The plot transforms chronological material into an emotionally and thematically shaped experience.

The Russian Formalist critics of the early twentieth century gave this distinction technical names. They called the underlying chronological sequence the fabula and the arranged narrative as presented to the reader the syuzhet. Whether or not you use those terms, the distinction is essential. When you ask about plot, you are asking not just what happened but how the narrative has chosen to present and arrange what happened — and why those choices matter.

The Core Distinction

Story is what happened, in the order it happened.

Plot is how the narrative chooses to present what happened: the arrangement, the emphasis, the pacing, the withholding and release of information.

The gap between story and plot is where many of fiction's most important interpretive questions live.

Why does this matter for interpretation? Because the gap between story and plot is where many of the most important interpretive questions live. When a novel begins in the middle of a crisis, withholding the backstory, the reader experiences disorientation and curiosity that the character may also feel. When a narrative delays revealing a crucial piece of information, that delay has meaning. When two events are placed side by side in the plot even though they are separated by years in the story, the juxtaposition invites comparison and interpretation. The arrangement is itself an argument about what matters and how different events relate to one another.

One of the most common mistakes students make when writing about fiction is to confuse plot summary with literary analysis. Retelling the sequence of events in the order they appear is often mistaken for engagement with the text. But plot summary tells us what happened; literary analysis tells us why the arrangement of events matters and what meaning that arrangement creates. This chapter is about developing the tools to move from one to the other — from description to interpretation.

Plot Summary vs. Analysis — The Central Distinction

Plot summary says: "First this happened, then this happened, then this." Literary analysis says: "The narrative presents these events in this order because doing so creates this effect and argues for this understanding of the story's meaning." One describes. The other interprets. Everything in this chapter moves you from the first toward the second.

Example 22.1 — Story vs. Plot
How arrangement transforms identical events
  • The Story Elena grows up in a remote village. At eighteen, she moves to the city for work. For fifteen years she sends money home. Then she learns her father has died. She returns. The village has changed. She has changed. She realizes she can no longer live in either world fully.
  • Plot A — Chronological The narrative begins with Elena's childhood, moves through her departure, her years of city life, the news of her father's death, and her return. The reader accumulates information in the order it occurred and experiences the story as a gradual process of loss and change.
  • Plot B — Beginning with Return The narrative opens with Elena on the bus back to her village, sitting with grief and a suitcase. In fragments, through memory, we learn her history. The reader experiences her disorientation upon arrival before understanding fully what she is returning to or from. Her sense of not-belonging is the reader's first experience, not the last.
  • Plot C — Beginning with the Father's Death The narrative begins with the letter that carries the news. We know immediately what has been lost, and every memory of her father — delivered in flashback — is shadowed from the first page by the reader's foreknowledge of his absence. The whole story is grief-colored from the start.
Interpretive Principle: Same events, different meanings — depending entirely on how the plot arranges them.
Section 22.2

Exposition and Situation

Every narrative must establish a world before it can disturb that world. This initial establishment is called exposition — from a Latin root meaning "a setting forth." Exposition introduces the reader to the fictional world: the setting, the characters, the social conditions, the relationships, and the state of things before the conflict begins.

Exposition is not merely background information. It is the narrative's way of creating the conditions that make the coming conflict meaningful. Good exposition does not simply tell us facts about a world; it creates a sense of normality, routine, or stable situation that the plot will then disrupt. The more fully we understand and inhabit the pre-conflict world, the more we feel the force of the disruption when it arrives.

Three Modes of Exposition

Mode What It Does Effect on the Reader Typical Use
Direct Exposition Narrator tells the reader explicitly about the situation, characters, and setting Efficient and clear; can feel slow if sustained too long Classic realist fiction; historical fiction requiring context
Dramatized Exposition Background information embedded within scenes, dialogue, and behavior Reader absorbs context through observation; feels immediate Contemporary literary fiction; character-driven narrative
In Medias Res Narrative begins at a moment of crisis; exposition distributed in fragments throughout Immediate engagement; reader actively assembles context Modernist and postmodernist fiction; epic poetry; thrillers

Direct exposition tells the reader explicitly about the situation, character, and setting. An omniscient narrator might explain that a character has lived in a particular town for thirty years, has three adult children who rarely visit, and has just retired from a career she found meaningless. This is efficient and clear, though it can feel slow if sustained for too long without drama or scene.

Dramatized exposition embeds background information within scenes and dialogue. Rather than telling us a character is lonely, the narrative shows her setting a table for one, hesitating before turning on the television, and ending a brief phone call with a cheerfulness that fades the moment she hangs up. The reader absorbs the exposition through observation rather than direct statement. This mode respects the reader's capacity to infer and tends to produce a stronger sense of inhabiting the fictional world.

In medias res — Latin for "in the middle of things" — begins a narrative at a point of high action or crisis, withholding much of the expository information until later. The reader is plunged into events without full context, and exposition is delivered in fragments, often through flashback or gradual revelation, as the narrative progresses. This technique creates immediate engagement and makes the reader an active assembler of information rather than a passive recipient. Homer's Iliad opens deep into the Trojan War rather than at its beginning; Kafka's The Trial begins with the protagonist already under arrest for an unspecified crime.

Many contemporary and modernist narratives blend these approaches, providing just enough initial exposition to orient the reader while withholding enough to generate questions that drive forward momentum. The question for the reader is always: what does this opening establish, and what does it deliberately leave open?

Reading Skill — Analyzing Openings

When you read the opening pages of a narrative, pay close attention to what the exposition establishes and, equally, what it withholds or delays. Ask: What do we know about this world? What do we not yet know? What assumptions or expectations does this opening create? What will it take to disrupt what has been established?

The answers to these questions lead directly to an understanding of how the plot has been designed and what the narrative understands to be at stake.

The situation established by exposition is the state of affairs that exists before the main conflict begins — the equilibrium that will be broken. Understanding the pre-conflict situation is essential for understanding the conflict itself, because conflict is always conflict between specific forces, desires, values, and conditions, not conflict in the abstract. A conflict means different things depending on what world it erupts inside. The loss of a job means something different in a story set in a wealthy household than in one set in a household already at the edge of economic survival. Exposition creates the conditions that determine what conflict will mean.

Section 22.3

Conflict and Complication

Conflict is the engine of narrative. Without conflict — without tension, opposition, struggle, or complication — there is no story in the meaningful sense. Events may occur, time may pass, but without conflict there is no forward momentum, no development, no meaning generated through struggle and consequence. This is not because literature glorifies struggle for its own sake, but because conflict is how fiction reveals character, tests values, and explores what matters to human beings and why.

The Five Primary Forms of Conflict

Conflict Type What It Involves What It Tends to Reveal
Person vs. Person Protagonist struggles against another character — rival, antagonist, enemy, loved one Values in collision; power dynamics; the social and moral stakes of human relationships
Person vs. Society Protagonist struggles against institutions, laws, norms, or collective expectations The cost of conformity or resistance; economic and social forces; justice and its failures
Person vs. Nature Protagonist struggles against natural forces: weather, disease, wilderness, physical catastrophe Human vulnerability and endurance; the limits of will; survival and its costs
Person vs. Self Protagonist's central conflict is internal: fear, desire, conscience, identity, competing values Psychological complexity; moral seriousness; the difficulty of living with oneself
Person vs. Fate / Transcendent Protagonist struggles against divine will, cosmic forces, or deterministic destiny Human agency and its limits; the relationship between freedom and necessity; tragedy and its shape

These categories are not mutually exclusive, and the richest fiction typically layers multiple forms of conflict simultaneously. A character may struggle against another person while also struggling against a social system while also struggling against her own conscience — and these multiple conflicts illuminate each other and create thematic complexity. A novel that seems at first to be about a single conflict often reveals, upon closer reading, that the external conflict is a surface manifestation of a deeper internal one. The antagonist is often, in some sense, a projection of what the protagonist cannot face within herself.

When you analyze conflict in a narrative, do not simply identify which category it belongs to and stop there. Ask what values or beliefs are in tension. Ask what the conflict reveals about the characters involved. Ask how the conflict develops and changes as the narrative progresses. Ask what would have to happen — what would have to change within the characters or within the world of the story — for the conflict to be resolved. These questions lead you from labeling to interpretation.

The Inciting Incident

The inciting incident is the specific event that disrupts the expository situation and sets the central conflict in motion. It is the moment when the stable world of the exposition becomes unstable — the point of no return, the event after which nothing can be exactly as it was before.

The inciting incident does not have to be a dramatic external event. In many literary novels, it is subtle: a letter arrives, a conversation takes an unexpected turn, a character notices something she cannot unnotice. What matters is that after the inciting incident, the world of the story cannot return to its pre-conflict equilibrium. The disruption has occurred, and the narrative must now deal with its consequences.

Identifying the inciting incident requires asking: at what moment does the conflict that drives this story actually begin? At what point does the protagonist — and the reader — cross from the world of the exposition into the world of the plot's central struggle? The answer to this question defines the shape of everything that follows.

Complication

Complication refers to the events, decisions, and developments that make the central conflict more difficult, more complex, or more consequential as the narrative progresses. Complications are not mere obstacles tacked on to delay resolution; they reveal character, deepen theme, and force protagonists into increasingly difficult positions. Each complication should escalate the stakes and narrow the range of available choices, creating a pressure that pushes inexorably toward the climax.

The best complications do not feel arbitrary. They emerge from the character's choices and the conditions the story has established. If a character makes a decision, that decision creates consequences; those consequences create new situations; those new situations require new decisions. Narrative complication at its best is a chain of cause and effect in which each link is both surprising and, in retrospect, inevitable.

Example 22.3 — Layered Conflict
How multiple conflict types create depth
  • Surface A man named Samuel has been passed over for promotion at the company where he has worked for eighteen years. His younger colleague — someone he trained — has been given the position instead. On the surface, this appears to be a person-versus-person conflict.
  • Deeper Layer As the story develops, it becomes clear that Samuel's real struggle is with the economic system that values youth over experience, with an institution that discards loyalty once it becomes convenient to do so. The conflict expands into person versus society.
  • Deepest Layer And beneath both of these, Samuel is struggling with the question of who he is if not his career — what his life has meant, whether he made the right choices, whether he stayed too long in a place that never truly valued him. The deepest conflict is person versus self.
Interpretive Principle: The surface conflict opens onto deeper ones. Identifying all layers of conflict is where analysis begins.
Section 22.4

Rising Action and Suspense

Between the inciting incident and the climax lies the largest portion of most narratives: the rising action. This is the sequence of events in which the central conflict develops, complications accumulate, characters make decisions and face consequences, and the pressure driving toward a decisive moment intensifies.

The term "rising action" comes from a simple visual metaphor — imagine the plot as an ascending line, with tension increasing as the narrative progresses. But this metaphor, while useful as a starting point, can be misleading if taken too literally. Rising action does not move in a straight line. It contains setbacks and small victories, moments of apparent resolution that open into new complications, scenes of relative calm before renewed pressure. The trajectory is generally upward in terms of stakes and tension, but the path is rarely smooth. What matters is not that tension increases continuously but that each development moves the conflict toward a point from which resolution becomes necessary.

Consequences and Irreversibility

What makes rising action effective is the accumulation of consequences. Each decision a character makes should narrow her options, reveal something about her character, and move the conflict forward in a direction from which there is no easy return. This is sometimes called the principle of irreversibility: as the rising action progresses, more and more becomes impossible to undo. Characters become committed to courses of action, burn bridges, make revelations they cannot take back, or pass thresholds beyond which their former lives are no longer accessible.

Irreversibility is what gives rising action its forward momentum and its emotional weight. A story in which characters can always retreat from their choices and return to the safety of their pre-conflict situation has no real stakes. The reader feels the narrative's tension in direct proportion to the sense that what is happening cannot be undone.

Two Kinds of Suspense

Suspense is the reader's experience of uncertainty and anticipation during the rising action. It is worth distinguishing between two kinds of suspense that can operate in narrative:

Suspense of outcome — We do not know what will happen. Will the character escape? Will the lovers unite? Will the murder be solved? This is the most common form of suspense in genre fiction, where the plot is designed to withhold information about the resolution until the appropriate moment. The reader turns pages to find out what happens.

Suspense of meaning — We may have some sense of what will happen, but we are uncertain what it means or what it will cost. Greek tragedy often works this way: the audience may already know the mythological story and know that Oedipus will discover his true identity. The suspense is not about outcome but about meaning, recognition, and the full weight of what is coming. This kind of suspense is often more powerful and more lasting than simple plot suspense, because it draws the reader into the moral and philosophical dimensions of the narrative rather than merely its informational ones.

Type of Suspense The Reader's Question Common in Effect
Suspense of Outcome "What will happen?" Genre fiction: mystery, thriller, romance, adventure Page-turning urgency; forward momentum driven by information withheld
Suspense of Meaning "What will this cost? What does this mean?" Literary fiction, tragedy, realist novels, classical drama Dread, moral weight, philosophical engagement; persists after the ending

Subplots

Subplots — secondary narrative lines that run alongside the main plot — contribute to the complexity of rising action. A well-constructed subplot does not simply fill time or add variety; it comments on, parallels, contrasts with, or complicates the main plot. In Shakespeare's King Lear, the subplot involving Gloucester and his sons mirrors and amplifies the main plot's themes of blindness, family betrayal, and the abuse of parental authority. The subplot is not decoration; it is thematic argument made through parallel structure.

When you encounter a subplot, ask: How does this secondary narrative relate to the main one? Does it parallel the main conflict? Does it contrast with it, offering an alternative outcome or set of values? Does it complicate the moral picture by showing the same situation from a different angle? A subplot that simply runs alongside the main plot without illuminating it is a structural weakness. A subplot that deepens the main narrative's themes is a mark of craft.

Section 22.5

Climax and Turning Point

The climax is the moment of highest tension in the narrative — the point at which the central conflict reaches its peak and something decisive happens. The climax is the moment toward which the rising action has been driving, and after it, the story moves in a fundamentally different direction.

The climax does not have to be the most dramatically intense or physically violent scene in the narrative. It is defined by its structural position and function, not by its external intensity. In many literary novels, the climax is a quiet moment: a character finally admits the truth she has been avoiding, or makes a choice she can never reverse, or understands something that changes everything. The climax is decisive — it resolves the central tension by bringing it to a head — but it can be decisive in subtle, internal, or psychological ways.

It is also important to distinguish between the climax and the turning point. In some narratives, these coincide: the most intense moment is also the moment at which the direction of the story turns decisively. But in other narratives, the turning point — the moment when the story's trajectory changes — arrives before, during, or slightly after the moment of highest tension. A character may make a crucial decision (the turning point) in a scene that is not particularly dramatic, and the full consequences of that decision (the climactic intensity) may arrive later.

Peripeteia and Anagnorisis

In classical dramatic structure, most fully articulated in Aristotle's Poetics and later systematized by various literary theorists, the climax involves two related movements: peripeteia (reversal) and anagnorisis (recognition).

Peripeteia (or reversal) is the moment when the protagonist's situation turns from good to bad or from bad to good — when the fortune of the central character changes dramatically. This is the structural fulcrum of tragedy: the point at which the character who seemed to be ascending is suddenly falling, or vice versa.

Anagnorisis (or recognition) is the moment of crucial understanding or discovery: the protagonist learns something she did not know, or understands something she had misread, and this knowledge transforms her situation. Recognition need not be knowledge about external facts — it can be recognition of one's own nature, values, or the consequences of one's choices.

These two movements often occur together in tragedy: the protagonist discovers something that simultaneously reverses her fortune and forces a recognition she cannot evade. But reversal and recognition can also be separated, or one can occur without the other, or they can take forms far subtler than a single catastrophic discovery. Even in realistic literary fiction, understanding where a narrative's reversal and recognition occur is often the key to understanding its structure.

Example 22.5 — Quiet vs. Dramatic Climax
Why the climax is defined by function, not intensity
  • Dramatic Climax In a thriller, the climax might involve a physical confrontation, a revelation delivered under time pressure, a chase sequence, and a final act of violence or escape. The external intensity matches the structural moment of peak tension. The reader feels the climax as event.
  • Quiet Climax In a literary novel, the climax might be a character sitting alone in her kitchen, finally allowing herself to understand something she has been resisting for three hundred pages. The external situation is still. But something decisive has changed — she cannot go back to not-knowing. The reader feels the climax as recognition.
Interpretive Principle: Ask not "where is the most exciting scene?" but "where does the central conflict reach its decisive turning point?" These are often the same place — but not always.

When you analyze the climax of a narrative, ask: What exactly is decided or revealed at this moment? How does this moment resolve — or transform — the central conflict? What has the protagonist lost, gained, understood, or chosen? How does the narrative prepare us for this moment, and how does it follow from what has come before? These questions lead you from identification to interpretation.

Section 22.6

Resolution and Open Ending

Resolution — sometimes called the dénouement, from a French word meaning "unknotting" — is the portion of the narrative that follows the climax and settles the consequences of the decisive turning point. The resolution does not have to make everything comfortable or satisfying; it simply needs to bring the narrative to a resting point that feels, in some sense, complete — even if what is complete is an acknowledgment of irresolution.

Resolution exists on a spectrum between two poles.

Closed resolution answers all or most of the central questions the narrative has raised. The major conflicts are settled, the characters' fates are made clear, and the reader knows where things stand. Genre fiction tends toward closed resolution: the crime is solved, the quest completed, the lovers united. The satisfaction of closed resolution is part of the pleasure genre fiction offers its readers.

Open resolution ends without settling all its central questions, leaving the reader in a state of productive uncertainty. Open endings are not failures of craft; they are deliberate choices that often reflect a commitment to honesty about the complexity of experience. Life rarely offers the clean conclusions that closed resolutions provide. Open endings ask the reader to sit with uncertainty, to continue thinking about what has happened, to recognize that interpretation does not end when the last page is turned.

Resolution Type What It Does What It Implies Typical Context
Closed Resolution Answers central questions; settles major conflicts; clarifies characters' fates That experience can arrive at definitive conclusions; that stories have answers Genre fiction (mystery, romance, adventure); classical comedy and tragedy
Open Resolution Leaves central questions unanswered; allows ambiguity about meaning or outcome That experience resists neat conclusions; that the reader must continue to interpret Modernist and contemporary literary fiction; tragicomedy; realist psychological fiction
Ambiguous Resolution Clear about events but uncertain about meaning; or uncertain about what literally happened That the meaning of experience depends on the reader's perspective and values Late Henry James; contemporary literary fiction; many postcolonial narratives

Falling Action

The falling action — the sequence of events between the climax and the resolution — is often the most underappreciated structural element. The falling action is not simply the narrative running down after the excitement is over. It is the space in which consequences unfold, characters absorb what has happened, and the full weight of the climax is registered. The pacing and content of the falling action reveals a great deal about what the narrative understands its climax to mean.

A narrative that rushes from climax to resolution suggests that what matters is the decisive moment, not its aftermath. A narrative that dwells in the falling action — giving careful attention to how characters live with what has happened — suggests that consequences, grief, adjustment, and meaning-making are themselves the subject. The length and shape of the falling action is an interpretive choice that deserves attention.

Reading Skill — Evaluating Resolution

Resist the temptation to evaluate a resolution simply in terms of whether you found it satisfying. A resolution that leaves you uncomfortable or unsatisfied may be the most honest and artistically courageous choice the narrative could make. Ask instead: What does this resolution reveal about the story's understanding of its own central conflict? What has changed from the beginning? What remains unresolved, and is that unresolved element meaningful or careless? What does the ending suggest about the narrative's view of human possibility?

Chapter 22 — Text in Focus

A Constructed Teaching Example: "The Second Harvest"

The following is an invented example created for teaching purposes. It illustrates narrative structure without reproducing copyrighted material. All characters, events, and details are fictional constructions designed to make structural principles visible.

Narrative Example
The Structural Situation

An elderly farmer named Teodor has tended the same land for fifty years, as his father did before him. The story begins in autumn, the fields stripped after a meager harvest. Teodor's adult son, Dmitri, has arrived from the city to tell his father that the family must sell the land: there are debts, there is a legal notice, the family cannot hold on any longer. This is the expository situation — the world as it exists before the central conflict erupts.

The inciting incident comes when Teodor refuses to listen. He walks away mid-conversation and returns to his fields, beginning to turn the soil for a winter crop that, as both men know, will never be planted in time to matter.

The rising action develops through a series of scenes: Dmitri making phone calls to the city, trying to finalize the sale; Teodor working the soil without acknowledgment of the conversation; a neighbor arriving with a casserole and a look of pity that Teodor does not register or ignores; a nighttime argument in the kitchen in which Dmitri confronts his father directly and Teodor speaks for the first time — saying only that his father planted this field in a season just like this one, and that the soil still remembers it; Dmitri going to bed furious and waking before dawn to find his father already in the field again.

The climax arrives not with argument or confrontation, but with a single action: Dmitri, in the gray dawn, picks up a second spade and begins to dig alongside his father. Neither speaks. The conflict does not resolve in conversation; it resolves in a decision that is also a recognition — Dmitri understands something about his father, and perhaps about himself, that he did not understand before.

The resolution is deliberately open. We know the land will still be sold. We know the planting will not save anything in material terms. But something has been decided between these two men — some debt acknowledged, some inheritance accepted — and the story ends with both of them working in silence as the light grows.

This example illustrates several key structural principles worth naming explicitly:

ElementWhat It Does in This StoryInterpretive Significance
Exposition Establishes the land, Teodor's history with it, the economic pressure, and Dmitri's arrival Creates the conditions for conflict: land as inheritance, identity, and economic burden simultaneously
Inciting Incident Teodor walks away and begins turning soil Behavioral rather than verbal — launches conflict through action, not confrontation
Rising Action Accumulation of pressure without resolution; Teodor's single statement about memory Escalates through indirection; the argument is conducted through silence as much as speech
Climax Dmitri picks up the spade at dawn Quiet but decisive; physical action rather than verbal resolution; both reversal and recognition
Resolution Both men working in silence as the light grows; the land will still be sold Open: materially ambiguous, emotionally resonant; the conflict is transformed, not eliminated

Notice that the conflict here operates on multiple levels simultaneously: person versus person (Teodor and Dmitri), person versus society (the economic forces threatening the land), and person versus self (Dmitri's conflict between practical responsibility and inherited identity). The layering of conflict types is what gives the story its depth and ensures that its resolution must also operate on multiple levels.

Reader's Question — Before Reading the Workshop
  1. Where does the climax seem to occur, and how do you identify it structurally?
  2. What is the central conflict, and on which levels does it operate?
  3. Why might the narrative have chosen to make the climax a physical action rather than a spoken resolution?
  4. What does the open ending leave unresolved, and does that unresolved quality feel like a weakness or a strength?
Section 22.7
Flashback, Foreshadowing, and Nonlinear Plot

Section 22.7

Flashback, Foreshadowing, and Nonlinear Plot

So far, this chapter has discussed narrative structure largely in terms of linear progression: exposition, inciting incident, rising action, climax, falling action, resolution. But many of the most powerful narratives in world literature do not follow this linear trajectory. They fragment time, loop backward, begin at the end, or present simultaneous threads whose chronological relationship must be assembled by the reader.

Understanding the techniques of nonlinear narrative is essential for reading fiction attentively, because the decision to depart from chronology is always meaningful. When a narrative refuses to tell its story in chronological order, it is making a claim — implicit or explicit — about how time, memory, causation, and meaning work.

Flashback (Analepsis)

A flashback is a narrative movement backward in time from the story's present moment to an earlier event. Flashbacks can be brief — a paragraph that recalls a childhood memory — or extended, constituting entire sections of a novel. They serve several distinct purposes, and identifying which purpose a particular flashback serves is part of reading it interpretively.

Function of FlashbackWhat It Does
Establishing Motivation Delivers information that makes a character's present behavior comprehensible — why she cannot trust, why he cannot return, why a seemingly irrational choice makes complete sense once we see its origins
Creating Thematic Depth Places a past event beside a present one, generating meaning through contrast or comparison — how things were versus how things are; an earlier version of a character set beside her present self
Building Suspense Temporarily withholds information about the present while delivering context; the reader waits to return to the present moment with new understanding that changes what we expect or fear
Structural Commentary The placement of a flashback — what it interrupts, where it appears — is itself meaningful; a flashback that arrives just before a climactic decision suggests the past is exerting pressure on the present
Reading Caution — Not All Flashbacks Are Equal

Not every flashback serves the same function, and not every flashback is equally well-placed. When analyzing a flashback, do not simply identify it and move on. Ask: Why does this flashback appear here, in the middle of this present-moment scene? What does the past event illuminate about the present one? What would the reader miss or misunderstand without this flashback? The placement is an interpretive choice, and the choice has meaning.

Foreshadowing

Foreshadowing is the technique by which later events are anticipated or hinted at earlier in the narrative, preparing the reader for what is to come without yet delivering it. Foreshadowing operates through suggestion, atmosphere, image, and detail rather than explicit statement.

Foreshadowing can be subtle or relatively overt. A detail mentioned in passing early in a story — a gun on the wall, a recurring dream, a character's offhand remark, a particular quality of light described at an unusual length — may seem incidental until later events give it retrospective weight. Rereading a foreshadowed narrative often produces a qualitatively different experience from the first reading, because details that seemed decorative now appear inevitable.

The relationship between foreshadowing and dramatic irony is close. When a narrative has foreshadowed an event, readers who reread the text — or audiences watching a play they already know — experience the dramatic irony of seeing a character approach a fate that has already been anticipated. We know something the character does not, and that knowledge colors everything she does and says.

Foreshadowing is also a form of structural integrity: it signals that the narrative has been designed rather than assembled. When a development feels earned — when we feel surprised but also satisfied, as though this is exactly what had to happen — it is often because the narrative has prepared us for it without our fully realizing it. When a development feels arbitrary or forced, it is often because no foreshadowing prepared the ground.

Nonlinear Narrative Forms

Nonlinear narrative is a broad term for any narrative structure that does not follow strict chronological order. The forms it takes vary enormously, and each carries its own characteristic effects and meanings.

Narrative Form
In Medias Res

Beginning in the middle of the action, often at a moment of crisis, with exposition distributed throughout the narrative rather than concentrated at the start. This technique creates immediate engagement and makes the reader an active participant in assembling the story's context. The reader must constantly adjust earlier assumptions as new context arrives.

Homer's epics, Kafka's novels, many modernist short stories, and most action-driven genre fiction use this technique. The question is always: what is the effect of beginning here, at this moment, before we know everything we need to know?

Narrative Form
Frame Narrative

An outer narrative that contains an inner narrative. The Canterbury Tales consists of pilgrims telling stories to one another; the stories are the inner narratives, the pilgrimage is the frame. One Thousand and One Nights frames an entire collection of stories within the larger narrative of Scheherazade telling stories to survive. Heart of Darkness presents Marlow telling his story of Congo to men sitting on a boat in the Thames.

The frame narrative creates a relationship between levels of storytelling that adds meaning to both. The frame is not just a container; it is a context that shapes how we interpret the story within it. The identity and circumstances of the narrator-within-the-frame — what they know, what they want, what they fear — colors everything they tell us.

Narrative Form
Reverse Chronology

Beginning at the end and moving backward. This structure forces the audience to experience dramatic irony from the start: we know the ending before we watch the beginning, so every early scene of hope or happiness is shadowed by foreknowledge of how it ends. The effect is often one of inevitability and loss — we watch characters making choices that we already know will lead them somewhere they might not choose if they knew what we know.

Harold Pinter's play Betrayal uses this structure, moving backward through the stages of an affair and its aftermath. The effect is to make the audience acutely aware of what is lost at each stage, and to give every early moment of connection the quality of something already ruined.

Narrative Form
Multiple Timelines and Braided Narratives

Some novels present events from different periods simultaneously, asking the reader to construct the relationship between past and present. The reader holds multiple temporal threads at once, and the connections between threads — the way past events explain or contrast with present ones — create the narrative's central meaning.

Braided narratives interweave multiple storylines presented alternately, whose connections and contrasts the reader is invited to discover. These storylines may involve different characters, different historical periods, or different locations, united by thematic connection rather than shared plot. The structure implies that these apparently separate stories are secretly one story — that human experiences across time and place rhyme with one another in significant ways.

Reading Skill — Nonlinear Narrative

When you encounter a nonlinear narrative, do not treat the structural departure as a mere stylistic quirk or a sign of difficulty to be endured. Ask: What does the narrative gain from telling events out of order? What would be lost if the story were told chronologically? What effect does the chosen arrangement have on the reader's experience of the story's meaning?

Nonlinearity is always a choice, and choices carry meaning. The arrangement of time in a narrative is an argument about how events relate to each other, how memory works, how causation operates, and what the narrative understands to be significant.

Section 22.8
Interpreting Narrative Structure

Section 22.8

Interpreting Narrative Structure

Everything discussed in this chapter — story versus plot, exposition, conflict, rising action, climax, resolution, flashback, foreshadowing, nonlinear structure — is ultimately in service of interpretation. Plot analysis is not a mechanical exercise in labeling parts. It is a way of understanding how narrative design creates meaning. The tools of this chapter become useful only when they are used to answer the question why, not merely the question what.

Five Principles for Structural Interpretation

Principle 1
Structure Is Argument

When a narrative begins with loss rather than security, that choice shapes everything that follows. When a narrative refuses resolution, that refusal is a position about the nature of experience. When a narrative places two events beside each other that are separated in chronology, the juxtaposition argues that these events illuminate each other. Narrative structure is not neutral; it embodies a way of understanding the world, and analyzing it means reading those embedded arguments.

Principle 2
What Is Omitted Matters

Narrative is always selective. Every story leaves things out — events that are summarized rather than dramatized, backstory that is withheld, perspectives that are not given, moments that are passed over in silence. The omissions are as meaningful as the inclusions. When a narrative declines to show us what happened between two pivotal scenes, or withholds a character's perspective, or rushes through an event that might have been given full dramatic weight, ask why. What would the narrative have to confront if it dramatized what it chose to omit?

Principle 3
Pacing Is Interpretation

The speed at which a narrative moves through events is not merely a matter of reader comfort. A narrative that lingers for pages on a single afternoon but dispenses with a year of action in a paragraph is making interpretive choices about what matters. Scene — a fully dramatized moment in real time — signals that something is important enough to inhabit fully. Summary — the rapid covering of events without dramatization — signals that the narrative understands those events as less significant than what it dramatizes. When you read, notice the pacing. Where does the narrative slow down, and where does it accelerate? The pattern reveals what the text values.

Principle 4
The Ending Recasts the Beginning

The resolution of a narrative does not merely conclude events; it retroactively illuminates everything that has come before. When you finish a story and return to its opening pages, you often find that what seemed like background detail was actually setup, that what seemed like digression was actually thematic argument, that the opening contained the ending in miniature. Good narratives are designed to be reread, and the ending is part of what teaches you how to reread them. Rereading a narrative after you know its ending is one of the most productive interpretive acts available to a literary reader.

Principle 5
Conflict Reveals Values

The particular shape of a narrative's conflict — which forces are in opposition, what the protagonist is risking, what the resolution requires — reveals the text's understanding of what matters. A narrative whose central conflict is between individual desire and social obligation understands human experience differently from one whose conflict is between duty and love, or between survival and integrity. Identifying the deepest form of the conflict — beneath whatever external action the plot presents — is one of the most important interpretive moves available to you as a reader of fiction.

Chapter 22 — Interpretation Workshop

From Observation to Interpretation

Here is a model for interpreting narrative structure analytically, using the MGU Close Reading Model applied to "The Second Harvest." Study the movement from observation to evidence-based interpretive claim. This is what narrative structure analysis looks like when done well.

MGU Close Reading Model — Applied to Narrative Structure
"The Second Harvest": Why the Climax Is a Physical Action
Notice In "The Second Harvest," the climax is not a conversation but a physical action — Dmitri picks up a spade and begins to dig alongside his father without speaking. The decisive moment of the narrative contains no dialogue.
Question Why does the narrative choose to make the climax nonverbal? What would be lost if the resolution arrived through dialogue — if Dmitri said "I understand, Father" or "You're right about the land"? What does silence accomplish that speech cannot?
Pattern Throughout the story, Teodor refuses verbal engagement — he walks away from Dmitri's announcement, he says only one brief thing in the kitchen confrontation, he is already at work before Dmitri wakes. The soil, the work of planting, is Teodor's language. The narrative establishes this pattern early: Teodor communicates through action, not speech.
Interpret By making the climax a physical joining of the work rather than a verbal agreement, the narrative argues that some forms of understanding cannot be reached through argument or explanation. The deeper conflict — about inheritance, identity, and what one generation owes another — is resolved not through persuasion but through participation. Dmitri's choice to pick up the spade is a recognition that his father's relationship to the land operates in a register that words cannot fully reach.
Support The narrative establishes early that Teodor "walked away mid-conversation" when Dmitri tried to discuss the sale. The single statement Teodor makes in the kitchen — that "the soil still remembers" his father's planting — frames the land not as property but as living memory. When Dmitri picks up the spade, he enters this framework of memory and work rather than the framework of debt and practicality in which he arrived.
Connect This interpretation connects to the story's central thematic tension between economic reality and inherited identity. The plot's structure enacts that tension: the resolution does not dissolve it (the land will still be sold) but transforms what it means. Dmitri's participation in the planting is a form of acknowledgment that cannot be expressed in the language of property. The open ending suggests that this kind of understanding — partial, embodied, silent — is the only kind this conflict could produce.

Notice what this workshop model does and does not do. It does not simply identify the climax and name it. It asks what the climax's form — its specific quality as a physical action rather than a spoken resolution — does for the narrative's meaning. It traces the pattern that prepares for that choice. It connects a specific structural decision to the story's largest thematic concerns. That movement — from observation to pattern to interpretation to evidence to theme — is the core of literary analysis.

The MGU Literary Analysis Paragraph Model — Applied Here

Claim: The narrative makes the climax of "The Second Harvest" a physical action rather than a verbal one because the story argues that some forms of understanding transcend language.

Textual evidence: Teodor's refusal to engage in speech throughout the rising action; his single statement that "the soil still remembers"; Dmitri's wordless act of picking up the spade at dawn.

Explanation: These details establish that Teodor's language is action and that the conflict can only be resolved in his register, not Dmitri's. When Dmitri enters that register, the resolution is achieved not through argument but through participation.

Connection: This structural choice enacts the story's central theme: that inherited identity operates through embodied practice and memory rather than through rational deliberation, and that some debts between generations can only be acknowledged, not settled.

Chapter 22 — Context Window

The Classical Plot and Its Heirs

Historical and Critical Context
Aristotle, Freytag, and the Theory of Narrative Structure

The model of narrative structure most familiar in Western literary study has its origins in Aristotle's Poetics, written in the fourth century BCE. Aristotle was analyzing Greek tragedy, not prose fiction, but his observations have shaped how storytellers and critics have thought about narrative structure for more than two thousand years.

For Aristotle, a well-made plot had a beginning, middle, and end — not in the trivial sense that events occur in sequence, but in the more specific sense that the beginning sets a situation in motion, the middle develops its consequences, and the end provides a conclusion that follows necessarily from what has come before. Aristotle emphasized the unity of action: a good plot focuses on a single central action and excludes anything that does not contribute to its development or conclusion. He also identified the two most important moments in tragic plot: peripeteia (reversal of fortune) and anagnorisis (recognition or discovery) — concepts that remain useful for analyzing narrative structure across many traditions and periods.

In the nineteenth century, the German playwright and critic Gustav Freytag formalized a five-part dramatic structure — exposition, rising action, climax, falling action, resolution — that has become the standard visual representation of plot structure in many educational contexts. Freytag's Pyramid, as this model is called, was developed specifically for analysis of classical drama and Shakespeare, but it has been widely (and sometimes too rigidly) applied to prose fiction as well.

Contemporary narrative theory has moved considerably beyond these classical models, paying careful attention to the ways that modernist and postmodernist fiction deliberately challenges, fragments, or subverts traditional plot structures — and to the ways that non-Western narrative traditions have always operated according to different structural principles. Understanding the classical model remains valuable precisely because it allows you to recognize when and how contemporary narratives depart from it — and to ask what those departures mean.

It is equally important to recognize that Freytag's Pyramid was never meant to be a prescription — a model that all good narratives must follow. It is a description of a pattern that appears frequently in certain traditions. Many excellent narratives do not conform to it at all, and forcing such narratives into its framework produces worse interpretation, not better. Use the model as a starting point, not a straitjacket.

Chapter 22 — World Literature Connection

Nonlinear Narrative Across Traditions

World Literature Connection
Alternative Structures of Narrative Time

The impulse to disrupt chronological narrative is not a modernist invention or a peculiarity of Western experimental fiction. It appears across literary traditions and periods, often reflecting deep cultural and philosophical commitments about how time, causation, memory, and significance work.

In the Sanskrit epic tradition of ancient India, nested or frame narratives are a fundamental structural principle. The Mahabharata — one of the longest epic narratives in any literary tradition — is itself a story told by a narrator who is reporting a telling by another narrator, who heard it from another: the frame narrative is multiple layers deep, and within the main narrative, countless sub-stories are embedded, each told by a character within the larger fiction. This structure is not disorganized; it reflects a philosophical understanding of storytelling as always occurring within another story — always embedded in a web of transmission and reception that is itself meaningful. The frame is never neutral; it always shapes what the inner story means.

The Arabic One Thousand and One Nights employs a similar framing structure, with Scheherazade's storytelling embedded within the frame of her own endangered life. The stories she tells are themselves often nested: characters within her stories tell stories that contain further stories. The structure enacts the collection's central argument: that storytelling is survival, that the capacity to generate narrative is a form of power, and that stories never end cleanly — each one opens into another.

In oral traditions across Africa, the Americas, and the Pacific, narrative structures frequently depart from linear chronology in ways that reflect the worldviews of the cultures that produced them. Cyclical narratives — stories that loop back to their beginnings rather than concluding at a different point — often reflect cosmological understandings in which time itself is cyclical rather than linear. Narratives that move between ancestor time and present time, or that embed contemporary events within mythological frameworks, are not structurally confused; they are structurally coherent in terms of a different understanding of how time, causation, and significance work.

Many Indigenous American narrative traditions use structures of repetition, variation, and layered return rather than the ascending-action-and-climax model of Western dramatic theory. Stories may circle the same event from multiple angles, or return to a central image again and again with different meanings accumulating at each return. The meaning is generated not by a single decisive turning point but by the accumulated weight of variation.

When you encounter nonlinear narrative in world literature, resist the temptation to read it as a deviation from a "natural" or "universal" linear structure. Ask instead: what understanding of time, causation, and narrative does this structure embody? How does that understanding relate to the cultural and philosophical context from which the text emerges? The linear plot is one way of understanding how stories work. It is not the only way, and it is not always the most illuminating way.

Chapter 22 — Common Misreading

Treating Plot Summary as Analysis

The most common misreading in fiction study is treating plot summary as literary analysis. This error takes several distinct forms, and recognizing each of them is essential for developing as a literary reader and writer.

Misreading 1
The Retelling Error

Writing about a story by narrating what happens in the order it happens. "First the exposition establishes that Teodor is a farmer. Then Dmitri arrives. Then Teodor refuses to listen. Then there is an argument. Then Dmitri picks up a spade." This is description, not analysis. It tells us what the text contains, not what the text means or why its arrangement matters. A literary essay that consists primarily of plot summary has failed its central task.

Misreading 2
The Event-Labeling Error

Correctly identifying structural elements but stopping there. "The inciting incident is when Dmitri arrives. The climax is when Dmitri picks up the spade. The resolution is open." These labels may be accurate, but they are insufficient as analysis. Labeling a structural element is only the beginning of the interpretive work; the work itself is explaining why that element takes the form it does and what it means for the narrative's larger argument. Identifying without interpreting is like naming the pieces of a machine without explaining what the machine does.

Misreading 3
The Hidden-Answer Error

Treating narrative structure as a puzzle with a single correct answer — "the climax is definitely at page 47" — rather than as a matter of interpretive judgment. Identifying structural elements involves interpretation, and thoughtful readers can sometimes reasonably disagree about exactly where a climax falls or whether an ending is truly open. What matters is not the label but the reasoning: why do you identify this moment as the climax, and what does understanding it as the climax reveal about the narrative's meaning? The justification for the label is the interpretation.

Misreading 4
The Meaning-in-Events Error

Assuming that the meaning of a narrative is contained in its events rather than in the arrangement of those events. A story about a man who loses his farm can mean many different things depending on how it is structured. Two stories with identical events can have entirely different meanings because of their different plots. Never confuse the events with the meaning; look for meaning in the arrangement, emphasis, pacing, and structure of events. Events are the raw material; the plot is the argument built from them.

The Corrective for All Four Errors

Focus on why rather than what. Not what happens, but why it happens in this way, in this order, at this pace, with this emphasis, in relation to these other events. Not what the inciting incident is, but what the choice of this particular inciting incident reveals about the story's understanding of its conflict. Not what the ending is, but what the choice of this kind of ending — open, closed, ambiguous — says about the narrative's view of human experience.

Chapter Summary, Review, and Practice

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 22.1Story refers to the chronological sequence of events in a narrative; plot refers to the deliberate arrangement of those events as presented to the reader. The gap between story and plot is where many of fiction's most important interpretive questions live, and plot analysis asks not what happened but why events are arranged as they are.
  • Sec 22.2Exposition establishes the pre-conflict world and can be direct, dramatized, or delivered in medias res. The situation established by exposition defines the conditions that make the coming conflict meaningful; understanding what the exposition creates — and what it withholds — is the first step in reading a narrative's structure.
  • Sec 22.3Conflict — the engine of narrative — takes five primary forms: person versus person, society, nature, self, and fate. Rich fiction typically layers multiple conflict types simultaneously. The inciting incident disrupts the expository equilibrium and launches the central conflict; complication develops and escalates it.
  • Sec 22.4Rising action develops the central conflict through accumulating consequences, operating on the principle of irreversibility: as the narrative progresses, more becomes impossible to undo. Suspense of outcome (what will happen) differs from suspense of meaning (what will it cost); literary fiction often relies more heavily on the latter. Subplots comment on and complicate the main narrative.
  • Sec 22.5The climax is the decisive turning point toward which rising action has been building; it need not be the most dramatic scene but must be the most structurally decisive. Classical analysis identifies peripeteia (reversal) and anagnorisis (recognition) as the key movements of the climax. The turning point and the moment of highest tension do not always coincide.
  • Sec 22.6Resolution ranges from closed (all central questions answered) to open (central questions left deliberately unresolved) to ambiguous. Open endings are deliberate choices that reflect a commitment to experiential honesty. The falling action — between climax and resolution — registers the full weight of the climax and reveals how the narrative understands what has happened.
  • Sec 22.7Flashback delivers past information to illuminate present events; its placement is always interpretively significant. Foreshadowing prepares the reader for coming events through suggestion and detail, and produces a qualitatively different rereading experience. Nonlinear structures — in medias res, frame narrative, reverse chronology, parallel timelines — are always meaningful choices that argue for a particular understanding of how time, memory, and significance work.
  • Sec 22.8Structural interpretation operates by five principles: structure is argument; omission matters as much as inclusion; pacing is interpretation; the ending recasts the beginning; and conflict reveals the text's values. Literary analysis asks not what happens but why the narrative is designed as it is and what that design means.

Key Terms — Chapter 22

Plot
The deliberate arrangement of events in a narrative — distinguished from the raw chronological sequence (story) by its emphasis on design, pacing, and the strategic release of information.
Story
The full chronological sequence of events in a narrative world, including events that occur offstage or are only implied.

The portion of a narrative establishing setting, characters, and situation before the central conflict begins; can be direct, dramatized, or delivered in medias res.

The decisive turning point at which the central conflict reaches its peak; defined by structural function, not external intensity.

Aristotelian term for reversal of fortune — the moment when a protagonist's situation turns decisively from good to bad or bad to good.

Aristotelian term for recognition — the moment of crucial discovery or understanding that transforms a protagonist's situation.
Falling Action
The sequence of events between climax and resolution in which consequences unfold and the full weight of the climax is registered.

The conclusion of a narrative; ranges from closed (questions answered) to open (questions deliberately unresolved).

Activity 22.1Story and Plot Mapping
  1. Choose a short story you have read recently. First, write out the story in chronological order — every event, in the order it occurred within the fictional world. Then write out the plot as presented in the text — the order in which events are presented to the reader. Compare the two lists: where do they differ? What does the narrative gain by presenting events out of chronological order? Write a paragraph explaining what the gap between your two lists reveals about the narrative's design.
Activity 22.2Analyzing the Exposition
  1. Find the opening page or two of a novel or short story and read it closely. Identify which mode of exposition it uses: direct, dramatized, or in medias res. Then answer these questions in writing: What does this opening establish? What does it withhold? What expectations does it create? What assumptions does it invite the reader to make? Write two paragraphs analyzing the exposition analytically — not summarizing it, but explaining what the choices in the opening reveal about the narrative's design.
Activity 22.3Identifying and Layering Conflict
  1. Choose a short story, novel, or film you know well. Identify the primary form of conflict (person vs. person, society, nature, self, or fate). Then ask: are there secondary or underlying conflict forms operating simultaneously? Write a paragraph for each conflict layer you identify, explaining what values or tensions each layer embodies and how the layers illuminate each other. Your analysis should show that the conflict is richer than any single category captures.
Activity 22.4Pacing as Interpretation
  1. Choose a chapter or section from a novel you are reading. Read it specifically for pacing: where does the narrative slow down and give full dramatic scene to an event? Where does it use summary to cover time rapidly? Make a rough map of the pacing across the chapter. Then write a paragraph arguing what this pacing pattern reveals about the narrative's values — what it considers significant enough to inhabit fully, and what it treats as relatively unimportant. Support your argument with specific references to the text.
Activity 22.5Structural Analysis Essay — Focused Argument
  1. Write a focused literary analysis of approximately 600–800 words in which you analyze one structural element of a short story or novel — exposition, conflict, climax, or resolution — and explain what that element reveals about the narrative's meaning. Do not summarize the plot. Your essay should have a specific thesis claim about how a structural choice creates meaning, and it should support that claim with textual evidence and explanation.
  2. Use the MGU Literary Analysis Paragraph Model as your guide: Claim → Textual Evidence → Explanation → Connection to theme, form, or meaning. Before drafting, use the MGU Close Reading Model to move from observation to interpretation: Notice → Question → Pattern → Interpret → Support → Connect.
Activity 22.6Replot an Existing Narrative
  1. Take a short story you know well and redesign its plot. Keep the same story events but arrange them differently. Options: begin at the climax and work backward; begin with the resolution and use flashback to explain how it arrived; begin in medias res at the moment of highest tension; use a frame narrative by having a character tell the story to another character. Write a paragraph explaining what your new plot arrangement changes about the meaning and effect of the story. What does your experiment reveal about the interpretive power of narrative structure?

Reading Checklist

Chapter 22 — Checklist
Use this checklist as you read any work of fiction. Return to any item you cannot check with confidence.
Story and Plot
  • I have identified the expository situation — the world as it exists before the central conflict begins, including what is established and what is withheld.
  • I can distinguish between the story (chronological sequence of events) and the plot (the arrangement of events as presented), and I can explain what the gap between them reveals.
  • I have identified the inciting incident — the event that disrupts the exposition and launches the main conflict — and I can explain why this particular event serves this function.
Conflict and Rising Action
  • I can name the central conflict and identify the levels on which it operates (person vs. person, society, nature, self, fate), explaining what each level reveals.
  • I have traced the rising action — the complications that escalate tension — and I can explain what each major complication contributes beyond simply delaying resolution.
  • I can distinguish between suspense of outcome and suspense of meaning in this narrative, and I can explain which the text relies on more heavily and why.
  • If the narrative includes subplots, I can explain how each subplot relates to and comments on the main plot.
Climax and Resolution
  • I have identified the climax — the decisive turning point — and I can explain why I identify it as such and what it reveals about the narrative's design.
  • I understand the resolution — whether it is closed, open, or ambiguous — and I can explain what that choice reveals about the narrative's understanding of its own central conflict.
  • I have noticed the falling action and can explain what it contributes to the narrative's meaning beyond simply concluding events.
Nonlinear Elements
  • I have noticed any flashbacks, foreshadowing, or nonlinear structural elements and I have thought about why they are used — what they contribute that chronological presentation could not.
  • I have thought about pacing — where the narrative slows into scene and where it accelerates into summary — and what that pattern reveals about the text's values.
Analysis Readiness
  • I can distinguish between the events of the story and the meaning created by the arrangement of those events — I am not confusing plot summary with analysis.
  • I can apply at least one of the five structural interpretation principles (structure as argument; omission matters; pacing is interpretation; ending recasts beginning; conflict reveals values) to this specific text.
  • I am ready to write about structure analytically — making claims about how narrative design creates meaning — rather than summarizing what happens.

Writing About Literature

Assignment Options — Chapter 22

Option A: Plot Analysis. Choose a short story or novel you have read recently. Write a focused literary analysis of approximately 600–800 words in which you analyze one structural element — exposition, conflict, climax, or resolution — and explain what that element reveals about the narrative's meaning. Do not summarize the plot. Your thesis should make a specific claim about how a structural choice creates meaning, supported with textual evidence and explanation.

Option B: Structural Comparison. Compare the narrative structures of two short stories — one that follows a relatively linear plot and one that uses flashback, frame narrative, or nonlinear structure. Write 700–900 words in which you argue what each structural choice reveals about the story's meaning. Use the MGU Comparative Reading Model: Identify the shared question → Compare form and genre → Compare historical context → Compare language and imagery → Compare themes and tensions → Explain what the comparison reveals.

Option C: Close Reading of an Opening. Find the opening page or two of a novel or short story and read it closely. Write 500–700 words analyzing the exposition: What situation does it establish? What does it withhold? What expectations does it create? What do these choices suggest about the narrative's themes or concerns? Do not discuss what happens after the opening; focus entirely on what the opening does as narrative design.

Option D: Reploting and Reflection. Take a familiar short story and redesign its plot, as in Activity 22.6. Write both your redesigned plot summary and a 400–500 word reflection explaining what your structural choices change about the story's meaning. What does the experiment reveal about the relationship between arrangement and meaning?

Looking Ahead
Chapter 23: Character, Motivation, and Development

Chapter 22 has given you the tools to read how stories are arranged and how that arrangement creates meaning. Chapter 23 will turn from structure to the people who inhabit that structure: characters. You will learn how characters are created and interpreted — through action, speech, relationship, and change. You will study the distinction between flat and round characters, static and dynamic characters, protagonists and antagonists, motivation and desire. And you will learn how to read character not as a collection of personality traits but as a site of thematic and moral argument. Character and plot are inseparable: what characters want drives conflict, and what conflict does to characters is what fiction is ultimately about.

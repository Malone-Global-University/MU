Fricatives

Turbulence, sibilance, and fricative contrasts

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Explain how frication is generated in articulatory terms.
  2. Distinguish sibilant and nonsibilant fricatives.
  3. Classify major fricatives by place and voicing.
  4. Use auditory cues and an introductory spectrogram preview to identify frication.
  5. Compare nearby fricative places across languages without claiming identical realization.
  6. Read and transcribe a controlled set of fricative symbols accurately.
  7. Avoid assuming that every voiced fricative sustains voicing identically in every context.

An Observable Problem

Say the English words sip and ship slowly, listening only to the first consonant. Both involve a narrow channel that forces air through with audible noise — but the noise itself sounds different, and if you watch a friend's lips you may notice their tongue is in a different place for each. Now say fin and thin. These two also involve turbulent airflow, but the noise is much quieter, almost a hiss without an edge to it, and the place of articulation has moved again.

All four of these consonants share one property: none of them involves a complete closure of the vocal tract, the way the stops in Chapter 9 did. Instead, the articulators come close enough together to create audible friction. This chapter asks a more precise question than "is there noise?" It asks where that noise is produced, how loud and sharp it is, and whether the vocal folds are vibrating at the same time.

  1. Before reading further, say sip, ship, fin, and thin aloud and rank the four initial consonants from "sharpest, most hissing noise" to "quietest noise." Write down your ranking; you will return to it later in the chapter.
Core Concept

How Frication Is Generated

Purpose: connect the auditory impression of "noisy" consonants to a precise articulatory mechanism.

A fricative is a consonant produced by narrowing the vocal tract enough to force air through a small channel, creating audible turbulence, without ever reaching a complete closure. This distinguishes fricatives from the stops studied in Chapter 9, where the airstream is briefly blocked entirely, and from approximants, previewed in Chapter 8 and developed fully in Chapter 12, where the articulators approach one another without creating turbulent noise.

The turbulence itself, not any single articulator, is the defining acoustic signature of a fricative. Air molecules moving through a narrow constriction at sufficient speed collide unpredictably, producing aperiodic, noise-like energy that a listener perceives as hissing, buzzing, or rasping depending on the exact shape and location of the channel.

  • [fɪn]fin — turbulence generated between the lower lip and upper teeth
  • [sɪp]sip — turbulence generated between the tongue tip or blade and the alveolar ridge, directed at the teeth
Concept Check

A useful working test: if you can sustain a consonant indefinitely on a single breath while air continues to escape audibly, and the sound is not a vowel, nasal, or approximant, it is very likely a fricative. Stops cannot be sustained this way because the closure stops the airflow; fricatives can be, because the constriction never fully closes.

Sibilant and Nonsibilant Fricatives

Purpose: distinguish two acoustically and articulatorily distinct classes of fricative noise.

A sibilant fricative is produced with a constriction shaped so that the airstream is directed at the edge of the teeth, producing comparatively loud, high-frequency, sharply audible noise. A nonsibilant fricative lacks this tooth-directed channeling and produces quieter, less sharply defined noise.

  • [s], [z], [ʃ], [ʒ]sibilants — comparatively loud, high-frequency frication
  • [f], [v], [θ], [ð]nonsibilants — comparatively quiet, diffuse frication

Sibilance is a property of the acoustic output, not a separate place of articulation. Sibilant fricatives occur at several different places, including alveolar and postalveolar, while sharing the tooth-directed channeling that produces their characteristic loudness.

Evidence Note

On a spectrogram, introduced fully in Chapter 28, sibilant fricatives typically show dense, high-amplitude noise energy concentrated above roughly 3500 Hz, while nonsibilant fricatives such as [f] and [θ] show much weaker, more spread-out energy that can be difficult to distinguish visually from background noise. This preview is meant only to connect the auditory contrast to a visible correlate; full spectrogram interpretation is developed later in the course.

A note on [h]

The symbol [h] is conventionally classed with the fricatives, but it is somewhat unusual: it has no fixed oral constriction location and is better described as audible turbulence generated at or near the glottis, with the oral cavity shaped by whatever vowel follows. Treat [h] as a glottal fricative for classification purposes, while noting that its place is less precisely localized than the other fricatives in the table.

Common Error

Error: Assuming that ⟨th⟩ always represents the same sound, since it is spelled with the same two letters in thin and this.

Correction: English ⟨th⟩ represents two different dental fricative phonemes depending on the word: voiceless [θ] in thin [θɪn], and voiced [ð] in this [ðɪs]. Spelling does not distinguish them; pronunciation does.

Voicing Pairs and Their Limits

Purpose: apply the voicing concept from Chapter 6 to fricatives, and qualify it with the evidence fricatives actually provide.

Many fricative places have both a voiceless and a voiced member produced at the same location, as the table above shows. It is tempting to treat each pair as differing only in whether the vocal folds vibrate, with everything else held constant. This is a useful starting approximation, but it is not the whole picture.

Sustained turbulent airflow and sustained vocal-fold vibration compete aerodynamically: vibration requires airflow that maintains pressure below the glottis, while frication requires airflow forced through a narrow channel above it. As a result, voiced fricatives in many languages, including English, frequently show partial devoicing in certain positions, particularly at the end of an utterance, even though they remain phonemically voiced.

  • /hæz/has — phonemically ends in /z/; the final fricative is often only partially voiced in careful citation speech
Evidence Note

The claim that a fricative is "voiced" is a phonemic claim about category membership, not a promise that vocal-fold vibration is audible for the sound's entire duration in every token. Treat voicing labels for fricatives as the contrastive category established by the language's phoneme system, while recognizing that the phonetic realization can vary by position, speaker, and rate. This distinction returns when you study connected speech in Chapter 23 and acoustic voice onset evidence in Chapter 28.

Across Languages: Comparing Nearby Fricative Places

Purpose: show that a shared symbol does not guarantee an identical realization, using verified cross-language examples.

The IPA symbol [s] represents an alveolar sibilant fricative wherever it is used, but languages differ in exactly how that constriction is shaped, and some languages contrast more than one sibilant place where English has only one.

  • [s] — Spanish (Castilian and Latin American varieties), as in sopa [ˈsopa]soup — alveolar sibilant, contrasting with no second sibilant place in most varieties
  • [ɕ] — Mandarin Chinese, as in xià [ɕja˥˩]down — alveolo-palatal sibilant, a distinct phoneme from the alveolar and retroflex sibilants Mandarin also contrasts

Mandarin's three-way sibilant contrast (alveolar, alveolo-palatal, and retroflex) shows that the single alveolar-versus-postalveolar distinction familiar from English is not a universal limit on how many sibilant places a language can use contrastively. The alveolo-palatal and retroflex symbols remain receptive at this stage; they are introduced for productive use according to the progression matrix.

Variation Note

Within English itself, the realization of /s/ varies somewhat by speaker and regional accent in its precise tongue shape and acoustic quality, even though it remains the same phoneme. This kind of fine phonetic variation is normal and does not by itself indicate a different phoneme or a speech difference requiring correction.

Listening For Frication

Purpose: identify the perceptual cues that distinguish fricative place and class before relying on spelling or memory.

Listen For
  • Overall loudness and sharpness of the noise: sibilants are louder and sharper than nonsibilants.
  • Pitch quality of the noise itself: postalveolar frication tends to sound lower-pitched and "fuller" than alveolar frication.
  • Presence or absence of voicing throughout the consonant, checked by lightly touching the throat.
  • Duration: fricatives, like other continuants, can be sustained and compared at different lengths, unlike stops.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake

Error: Treating "sibilant" as a place of articulation rather than a property of the resulting noise.

Correction: Sibilance describes the acoustic and articulatory channeling that produces loud, high-frequency frication; it can occur at more than one place, as shown by alveolar [s] and postalveolar [ʃ], both sibilants at different places.

Common Mistake

Error: Assuming a voiced fricative is voiced for its entire duration in every context, and treating any partial devoicing as a pronunciation error.

Correction: Voicing labels for fricatives identify a contrastive phonemic category. Phonetic realization can include partial devoicing in predictable positions, such as utterance-finally, without changing the phoneme's identity.

Common Mistake

Error: Assuming that every language with an [s]-like sound has only one sibilant phoneme, the way many English varieties do.

Correction: Some languages, including Mandarin Chinese, contrast more than one sibilant place. The number of sibilant contrasts is a property of each language's phoneme system, not a universal fact about sibilants.

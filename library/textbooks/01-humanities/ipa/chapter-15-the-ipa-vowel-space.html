The IPA Vowel Space

Chart organization, paired columns, and the reference-vowel method

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Explain the organizational principles of the IPA vowel quadrilateral and locate any major vowel region within it.
  2. Interpret the paired structure of the IPA vowel chart, identifying unrounded and rounded counterparts at the same height and backness.
  3. Name the major peripheral and central monophthong symbols and state the full three-part articulatory description for each.
  4. Explain the purpose and limits of the reference-vowel method and distinguish reference values from language-specific realizations.
  5. Compare an auditory vowel quality with a chart position and explain why that position represents a range rather than a single precise target.
  6. Plot approximate vowel positions from supplied articulatory or acoustic evidence and label them with appropriate symbols.
  7. Interpret a vowel chart drawn for a specific language and explain what it claims and what it does not claim.

An Observable Problem

Open any pronunciation dictionary or phonetics reference and find its vowel chart. You will see a trapezoid shape — wider at the bottom than at the top — filled with IPA symbols, many of them in pairs. Some symbols look familiar from Chapter 14. Others may be entirely new. Some pairs sit side by side at the same location on the diagram, separated only by a thin column boundary.

Two questions arise immediately. First, why are the symbols arranged in pairs, and what does the pairing mean? Second, if two symbols occupy what looks like the same spot in the trapezoid, how do they represent different sounds?

The answers to both questions are already in the framework you built in Chapter 14 — but reading the chart fluently requires understanding how that framework is encoded visually. Before continuing, examine the following two observations:

  1. Return to the eight anchor symbols from Chapter 14: i, u, a, ɑ, ɛ, ɔ, ə, y. You know the three-part description for each. Look at which of these symbols is paired with another symbol at the same position: for example, i and y share the same height (high) and backness (front) but differ in rounding. Using this logic alone, predict what property distinguishes the two symbols at any other paired position in the vowel chart.
  2. Consider the symbol ə (schwa), which is plotted in the middle of the diagram rather than at a corner or edge. What does a central, mid-height position tell you about the kind of vowel quality it represents? What would you expect to be different about a vowel plotted at the extreme top-left corner compared with one plotted in the center?
The Quadrilateral

Organization of the IPA Vowel Quadrilateral

Purpose: establish the organizing logic of the IPA vowel chart before introducing individual symbols, so that the chart is navigated as a system rather than memorized as a list.

The IPA vowel quadrilateral — sometimes called the vowel trapezoid or vowel diagram — is the central visual tool for organizing vowel symbols. Understanding its structure is more important than memorizing every symbol in it. Once you understand why the chart is shaped the way it is and how its axes work, you can find any vowel symbol and read off its approximate articulatory description without consulting a separate reference.

The chart represents two of the three dimensions introduced in Chapter 14. The vertical axis represents vowel height: the highest vowels appear at the top of the diagram and the lowest appear at the bottom. The horizontal axis represents backness: front vowels appear on the left and back vowels on the right. The third dimension — rounding — is not given its own spatial axis. Instead, rounding is encoded in the paired structure of the symbols, as explained below.

The quadrilateral is wider at the bottom (the low-vowel row) than at the top (the high-vowel row). This shape is not arbitrary. It reflects the acoustic fact that, for high vowels, the distinction between front and back is compacted into a smaller auditory range than for low vowels: the perceptual distance between a high front and a high back vowel, while real, is smaller relative to the full vowel space than the perceptual distance between a low front and a low back vowel. The trapezoid shape encodes this relationship between the dimensions as they are actually heard, which is why the IPA quadrilateral is described as reflecting auditory and acoustic relationships as much as articulatory ones.

The chart should be thought of as a map of vowel quality space rather than a map of tongue positions. The axes are descriptive labels — front, central, back; high, mid-high, mid-low, low — not physical measurements in millimeters. Any vowel quality that exists can be located somewhere in this space, and the position of a symbol in the chart tells you its approximate relationship to other vowels in terms of the three primary dimensions.

Concept Check — A Map, Not a Photograph

The IPA vowel quadrilateral is often misread as a picture of the inside of the mouth, with the left edge representing the front teeth and the right edge representing the back of the throat. While the spatial metaphor is suggestive, the quadrilateral is better understood as a phonetic map: it encodes systematic relationships among vowel qualities rather than anatomical measurements. Two languages may use the same IPA symbol (say, /e/) but realize it at slightly different positions in acoustic space — both realizations would be plotted near the same chart location, but they are not necessarily identical sounds. The chart tells you approximately where a vowel falls in the system; it does not guarantee two speakers or two languages produce the same tongue-tip coordinate.

Axes, Edges, and Interior

Purpose: give learners a reliable spatial vocabulary for navigating the chart and locating symbols by region before introducing the full symbol inventory.

It is useful to distinguish three zones in the quadrilateral: the peripheral positions around the edges, the interior, and the central column.

Peripheral positions are the corners and edges of the trapezoid. The four corners represent the most extreme vowel qualities in each direction — high front (top left), high back (top right), low front (bottom left), and low back (bottom right). The edges between corners represent intermediate heights at front and back positions. Peripheral vowels are often described as the most "extreme" or "clear" realizations of a height-backness combination, and they are sometimes used as auditory reference points precisely because they are so distinct from each other.

The interior of the quadrilateral represents vowel qualities that are neither fully front nor fully back — they occupy an intermediate horizontal position. A vowel described as "central" in backness will appear somewhere in the middle of the horizontal range at its height level. The most familiar central vowel for English speakers is schwa [ə], which sits in the mid-height, central region. Other central vowels appear at high, low, and other mid heights, and these will be identified in the full symbol table below.

The central column runs vertically down the middle of the chart. Vowels plotted here are produced with the tongue body in a roughly neutral horizontal position — not advanced toward the front and not retracted toward the back. Central vowels tend to be less acoustically extreme and are often the targets of vowel reduction in unstressed syllables, though this tendency varies by language.

Paired Columns: Unrounded and Rounded

Purpose: explain the most immediately visible structural feature of the IPA vowel chart — the pairing of symbols at each position — and establish what the pairing encodes.

At most positions in the quadrilateral, the IPA places two symbols side by side. The convention is consistent: the symbol on the left is unrounded; the symbol on the right is rounded. This left-right ordering is the single most important key to reading the IPA vowel chart. Once you know it, you can infer the rounding of any symbol from its position within a pair, without memorizing rounding separately for each symbol.

The pairing is a claim about the logical structure of the vowel space: at every height and backness position, it is possible in principle to produce both a rounded and an unrounded vowel. Languages differ in which of these possibilities they use phonemically. English, for example, pairs frontness with unrounded vowels and backness with rounded vowels almost without exception — only the back rounded vowels and a few front unrounded vowels appear as phonemes. Languages such as French, German, Mandarin Chinese, Swedish, and Finnish use front rounded vowels systematically. Languages such as Turkish, Korean, and several African and Indigenous languages use back unrounded vowels. The pairs in the chart ensure that the notation system is prepared for all of these possibilities.

The pairing also has a practical navigation consequence. If you see an unfamiliar symbol in the IPA vowel chart and want to know its rounding, locate whether it is the left or right symbol of a pair. Left means unrounded; right means rounded. Combined with reading its vertical position (height) and horizontal position (backness), this gives you the full three-part description.

Concept Check — Two Symbols You Already Know in a New Light

Look again at the pairing ʌ · ɔ at mid-low back. You know ɔ well from Chapter 14. Its unrounded counterpart ʌ is the vowel typically heard in the nucleus of words like cup, strut, and but in General American and Standard Southern British English — though its exact phonetic realization varies considerably across accents. The chart tells you that ʌ and ɔ share the same height (mid-low) and backness (back) and differ only in rounding. You do not need to memorize this: the paired chart position encodes it. Similarly, look at e · ø at mid-high front. You may not yet know ø as a productive symbol, but the chart tells you immediately that it is the front rounded counterpart of e — the same height, same backness, just rounded. French and German use this vowel (feu, schön). The pair structure makes this relationship immediately readable.

Reference Vowels

The Reference-Vowel Method

Purpose: explain what reference vowels are, why they exist, and how they function as auditory calibration anchors rather than as descriptions of any real language's vowels.

One practical difficulty in vowel description is that the same IPA symbol may represent slightly different phonetic targets in different scholarly traditions, different languages, and even different published textbooks. A transcription /e/ in a description of Spanish, French, Swedish, or Yoruba does not guarantee that all four languages use exactly the same vowel quality — it means that all four have a vowel that occupies approximately the close-mid front unrounded region of the vowel space. The label is a category assignment, not a millimeter-precise target.

To provide a stable calibration system independent of any specific language, phoneticians have developed the concept of reference vowels — sometimes called cardinal vowels in the tradition established by Daniel Jones in the early twentieth century. Reference vowels are a set of fixed auditory targets that define specific positions in the vowel space. They are produced at extremes and equidistant intervals of the perceptual vowel space and serve as a measuring scale against which the vowels of any language can be located.

The original set of eight primary reference vowels occupies the four corners and the four midpoints of the peripheral front and back edges of the trapezoid. They are:

A secondary set of eight references (numbers 9–16) uses the opposite rounding to the primary set — so reference vowel 9 is the rounded counterpart of reference vowel 1: high front rounded [y]. This secondary set covers the less common combinations, including front rounded and back unrounded vowels.

The reference-vowel tradition matters for practical work because it means that a phonetician trained in the system can hear a vowel and describe its position relative to reference anchors: "the vowel is approximately halfway between reference vowels 2 and 3" (between [e] and [ɛ]), which is a precise and cross-linguistically communicable claim. Without such anchors, vowel descriptions risk being circular: "it sounds like the vowel in the word X" — which only communicates to someone who shares the same dialect of X's language.

Evidence Note — Training Your Ear Against Reference Values

Historically, phoneticians trained their perception against recordings of reference vowels produced by an expert, then used those internalized anchors to describe the vowels of any language they encountered. Recordings of the original primary reference vowels by Daniel Jones and subsequent phoneticians are available through IPA archival resources and university phonetics laboratories. Listening repeatedly to these recordings — and comparing them with your own productions — builds the auditory calibration that makes vowel description cross-linguistically consistent. This chapter cannot provide those recordings as verified assets, but when your instructor or course materials include reference-vowel audio, prioritize that listening practice above any other activity in this chapter. The chapter's goal is to make you understand the system so that the audio practice is maximally useful when you have access to it.

Reading Reference Values from the Chart

Purpose: show learners how to use the chart as a reference scale and how to read a vowel symbol's description from its position alone.

The IPA chart encodes reference values through position. When you see a symbol at the top-left corner of the quadrilateral, you know it is the reference value for the most extreme high front vowel quality — regardless of which symbol occupies that position. When you see a symbol at the midpoint of the left (front) edge, you know it is the reference value for a mid-high front vowel. The chart is internally consistent: position determines description.

This means that reading a vowel chart is a skill with a straightforward procedure:

  1. Identify the symbol's vertical position to determine height: top = high, middle = mid (close-mid or open-mid depending on which row), bottom = low.
  2. Identify the symbol's horizontal position to determine backness: left edge = front, right edge = back, middle of the horizontal range = central.
  3. Identify whether the symbol is the left or right member of a pair to determine rounding: left = unrounded, right = rounded.
  4. Combine the three readings into a full description: height + backness + rounding.

Apply this procedure to any symbol in the chart, including unfamiliar ones. The procedure gives you a reliable articulatory description without additional memorization. The symbol's shape becomes a secondary confirmation once you have located it in the chart.

Concept Check — Reading ø from the Chart

Suppose you encounter ø and have not memorized what it represents. Apply the procedure: (1) It sits in the second row from the top — the close-mid (mid-high) row. Height: mid-high. (2) It is on the left edge of the quadrilateral — the front column. Backness: front. (3) It is the right-hand symbol of the pair e · ø. Rounding: rounded. Full description: mid-high front rounded vowel. You now know exactly what articulatory configuration this symbol represents — without having memorized a separate entry for ø. This is the purpose of learning the chart as a system: most symbols can be decoded from position alone.

Language Realizations versus Reference Values

Purpose: prevent the common misunderstanding that a language's vowel phoneme exactly matches the reference value given by its IPA symbol.

When a phonetician transcribes the vowel system of a language and assigns the symbol /e/ to one of its mid front vowels, this does not mean the vowel in that language is acoustically identical to the reference value for [e]. It means the vowel falls in the close-mid front unrounded region and is the closest available symbol within the standard set. The actual phonetic target — what speakers of that language aim for, what listeners of that language expect — may be slightly higher, slightly lower, slightly more central, or slightly more peripheral than the pure reference value.

This principle has several practical consequences:

  • Two languages that both use the symbol /e/ may realize that phoneme differently. Spanish /e/ is often described as slightly lower than the reference value, closer to the open-mid region in some phonetic accounts. French /e/ may be somewhat higher. The symbol labels both as close-mid front unrounded without implying identity.
  • When you read a language's vowel chart, you are reading a categorization of that language's phonemes into the nearest available IPA positions — not a claim that each phoneme hits a mathematical point in acoustic space.
  • When you plot a vowel from acoustic or auditory evidence, the plotted position should be treated as a range or cloud of likely values, not a pinpoint. The IPA convention of placing a dot or symbol at a single location is a practical simplification of this range.
  • When two sources give slightly different IPA symbols for the same vowel in the same language — for example, one transcribing as /e/ and another as /ɛ/ — the difference may reflect different levels of transcription precision, different analytical traditions, or different reference varieties, not necessarily an error in one of the sources.
Variation Note — The Same Symbol, Different Realizations

The symbol /a/ appears in descriptions of Spanish, Italian, Japanese, Finnish, Arabic, Swahili, and many other languages. In each case it labels the language's low vowel — but the precise acoustic target for that low vowel differs across these languages. In some, the low vowel is clearly front; in others it is more central. Some realizations are somewhat higher than a true open position. None of these differences make the transcription wrong; they reflect the natural range of variation within the label category. When phonetic precision matters — for narrow transcription, acoustic measurement, or production work — the label is a starting point that must be refined through listening, recording, and analysis. This refinement is exactly what Chapters 24 through 27 will develop in depth.

Notation and Evidence

One Symbol, a Range of Realizations

Purpose: consolidate the key analytical principle that IPA vowel symbols represent phonological categories with phonetic ranges, not singular targets.

The relationship between an IPA vowel symbol and the range of phonetic values it can represent is worth stating clearly before moving to exercises. A vowel symbol in IPA transcription makes a claim at two levels simultaneously.

At the phonological level, the symbol identifies a contrastive category — the vowel that distinguishes one word from another in the transcribed language or variety. When /i/ appears in a phonemic transcription of English beat, it identifies the category that contrasts with /ɪ/ in bit, with /e/ or /ɛ/ in bait, and so on.

At the phonetic level, the symbol approximates the acoustic and articulatory target for that category in the specific variety and style being transcribed. The realization of English /i/ in stressed position in General American English is in fact somewhat diphthongal — it begins slightly lower and glides upward to a position near the reference value for [i]. A careful narrow transcription might represent this as [ɪi] or with a rising-quality diacritic, but a broad transcription uses just [i] for practical purposes.

Neither level is more "true" than the other. They serve different analytical purposes. The important discipline is knowing which level you are working at and being consistent within a given transcription.

Plotting Vowel Positions from Evidence

Purpose: give learners a practical procedure for placing a vowel in the vowel space from articulatory description or acoustic information, establishing a skill they will apply in language analysis throughout the course.

Plotting vowels on a diagram is a routine analytical step in building a vowel inventory. The goal is to represent approximately where a vowel falls in the vowel space so that it can be compared with other vowels in the same system and with reference values.

When you plot from an articulatory description, you have a three-part statement (height, backness, rounding) and must locate its position in the quadrilateral. Find the appropriate row for height, find the appropriate column region for backness, and mark the position. The symbol then labels that position; rounding is confirmed by the left-right column convention.

When you plot from acoustic evidence — specifically from formant values — a different procedure applies. Recall from Chapter 14 that F1 correlates inversely with vowel height (higher F1 = lower vowel) and F2 correlates with frontness and rounding (higher F2 = fronter and/or less rounded). Acoustic vowel plots often use F1 on the vertical axis and F2 on the horizontal axis, with axes inverted to match the orientation of the IPA quadrilateral (high F1 at the bottom, high F2 at the left). When converting from acoustic data to an IPA chart plot, a vowel with low F1 and high F2 falls in the high front region; a vowel with high F1 and low F2 falls in the low back region. Chapter 28 will develop acoustic reading skills in depth; for now, the important connection is that formant values provide objective evidence for chart position rather than relying only on the transcriber's ear.

Evidence Note — Formants as Coordinates

Formant measurement gives vowel phonetics a degree of precision that articulatory description alone cannot always achieve. If a phonetician measures F1 and F2 for a vowel in a language and finds values of approximately 300 Hz and 2200 Hz, that combination is consistent with a high front unrounded vowel (low F1, high F2) — it sits in the region associated with [i]. If the values are 700 Hz and 1000 Hz, that combination suggests a low back vowel — the region of [ɑ] or [ɒ]. Formant values vary across speakers (women's formants are systematically higher than men's on average; children's are higher still), so plots from a single speaker must be interpreted as individual data rather than a universal standard. Chapter 28 will give you tools for reading and interpreting these displays directly.

Connecting Formants to the Vowel Chart

Purpose: establish the mapping between the acoustic dimensions of F1 and F2 and the articulatory dimensions of the IPA quadrilateral, preparing learners for acoustic work in Chapter 28.

The IPA vowel quadrilateral was developed before acoustic measurement was routine — it was originally based on auditory training and articulatory description. It turns out, however, that the quadrilateral aligns well with the acoustic F1–F2 vowel space when the axes are appropriately oriented. This is not a coincidence: the articulatory dimensions of height and backness produce the resonance changes that are measured as F1 and F2, so the two descriptions are different windows onto the same underlying physical relationships.

The mapping is approximate but consistent:

IPA chart dimensions and their acoustic correlates
IPA chart dimension Direction on chart Acoustic correlate Typical direction of change
Height (high → low) Top → bottom F1 (first formant) F1 rises as the vowel gets lower (more open jaw)
Backness (front → back) Left → right F2 (second formant) F2 falls as the vowel gets backer
Rounding (unrounded → rounded) Left to right within pair F2 (also F1, F3) Rounding lowers formant frequencies, especially F2

This table is a preview of more detailed acoustic work in Chapter 28. For now, the key takeaway is that the vowel chart and the acoustic formant plot tell the same story from different vantage points: one is articulatory-auditory, the other is acoustic-instrumental. Using both gives more confidence in a vowel identification than relying on only one.

Across Languages

Across Languages — Finnish: Front Rounded Vowels in a Symmetrical System

Finnish has eight phonemic vowels that demonstrate the paired structure of the IPA chart in action across a complete, symmetrical system. Finnish contrasts /i/ with /y/ (same height and backness, different rounding), /e/ with /ø/ (mid-high front, unrounded versus rounded), and /a/ with /o/ and /u/ at the back. The system also involves a vowel harmony pattern in which front and back vowels generally do not appear in the same native word — a property that will be addressed in Chapter 18. What matters here is that Finnish uses six of the eight primary reference-vowel positions plus two additional symbols from the chart, and the IPA paired columns directly predict which vowels are in each contrasting pair. Looking at the Finnish vowel chart, a reader who knows the IPA paired structure can immediately identify which vowels are rounding pairs, which are height pairs, and which are backness pairs, without knowing a word of Finnish. That is precisely the navigational power the chart structure is designed to provide.

Across Languages — Mandarin Chinese: A Simpler Inventory in a Rich System

Mandarin Chinese has a relatively small number of phonemic vowel contrasts — typically analyzed as having five or six vowels depending on the analytical approach — but those vowels occur in a rich set of syllable shapes involving glides, diphthongs, and vowel sequences that make the system far more complex than its inventory size might suggest. The core vowels include the high front unrounded /i/, high back rounded /u/, high front rounded /y/ (spelled ⟨ü⟩ or ⟨u⟩ after certain initials in Pinyin romanization), a mid-low or low front unrounded vowel /a/, and an unrounded back vowel often transcribed as /ɤ/ or /ə/ in different traditions. The important observation for chart literacy is that Mandarin, like Finnish, uses the high front rounded /y/ — demonstrating again that this non-English combination is typologically common across the world's languages, not an exotic rarity. Reading the IPA vowel chart for Mandarin as a system tells you immediately that the language exploits the front-rounded corner of the vowel space, something a purely English-based frame of reference would not predict.

Across Languages — German: Vowel Length Pairs on the Chart

Standard German has a set of vowel oppositions that involve both quality and duration. The pair often transcribed as /iː/ versus /ɪ/ shows a high front vowel that is both longer and positioned closer to the reference value for [i] contrasting with a shorter vowel that is slightly lower and more centralized — often transcribed as [ɪ]. These two vowels would appear at slightly different positions in a German vowel chart: the long vowel closer to the top-left corner, the short vowel slightly interior. The IPA symbol ɪ is a receptive symbol in this chapter — you can locate it near the high front region but slightly interior, indicating it is near-high near-front. This German pattern illustrates a general principle: languages often use pairs of chart positions near each other, not just at the extreme reference-vowel corners, and the chart must be read as representing a full range of positions, not just the eight primary reference points. Vowel length as a phonemic property will be developed in Chapter 18; German provides a preview.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake

Error: Reading a vowel chart for a specific language as if every symbol in the chart is one of that language's phonemes.

Correction: The IPA vowel quadrilateral is a reference chart for all possible vowel symbols. A specific language's vowel chart shows only the symbols used by that language — a small subset of the full quadrilateral. When you see the full IPA quadrilateral with all positions filled, remember that you are looking at the complete catalogue of available symbols, not the vowel inventory of any particular language. When you see a vowel chart for a specific language, you are seeing a subset of those positions, with each marked symbol representing one phoneme of that system. Confusing the two is like reading a full alphabet as if every letter were used in every word.

Common Mistake

Error: Assuming that two languages using the same IPA vowel symbol have identical vowel realizations.

Correction: The same IPA symbol — say, /o/ — is used in transcriptions of Spanish, Italian, Japanese, Turkish, Finnish, and many other languages. In each case the symbol identifies a mid-high back rounded vowel, but the precise acoustic target, degree of lip rounding, height, and backness may vary. The symbol is a category label identifying the closest available position in the reference system, not a claim of phonetic identity. To compare the actual realizations, you need formant measurements or careful auditory analysis — not just the symbol.

Common Mistake

Error: Confusing the symbol ə (schwa, mid central unrounded) with ɜ (mid-low central unrounded) or ɐ (near-open central unrounded).

Correction: These three symbols occupy different positions in the central column of the vowel quadrilateral. ə is mid-height (midway between the close-mid and open-mid rows). ɜ is at the open-mid row — lower than schwa. ɐ is near-open — lower still. In a broad transcription of English, unstressed vowels are typically transcribed as ə. In a narrower transcription, the specific vowel in a word like strut in British English might be ʌ (back) while the equivalent in American English might be closer to ə or ɜ depending on the analyst. The chart gives you the tool to distinguish these positions systematically, but applying them in narrow transcription requires careful listening and will be developed in Chapters 24 and 25.

Common Mistake

Error: Treating the primary reference vowels (1–8) as the only vowels in the IPA system and assuming that any vowel must be one of those eight.

Correction: The eight primary reference vowels are anchors for auditory calibration, not the complete IPA vowel inventory. The full IPA chart includes many additional positions — the central vowels, the secondary (opposite-rounding) series, near-high and near-low positions, and additional symbols for particular phonetic properties. The eight primary reference vowels are useful starting points for orientation, but real language systems can use any position in the vowel space. The chart gives you the full vocabulary; the reference vowels give you the scale.

Chapter Review — Part A

1. The trapezoid is narrower at the top because the perceptual and acoustic distance between a high front and a high back vowel is smaller than the equivalent distance at the low level. The shape encodes acoustic and auditory relationships: the vowel space is more compressed at high tongue positions and opens up at low positions, both perceptually and acoustically.   2. Convention: left symbol = unrounded, right symbol = rounded, at every position. Example 1: high front pair — i (unrounded, left) and y (rounded, right). Example 2: mid-low back pair — ʌ (unrounded, left) and ɔ (rounded, right). Accept any two valid pairs.   3. Three steps: (i) read vertical position for height; (ii) read horizontal position for backness; (iii) read left-versus-right within the pair for rounding. Applied to ɔ: (i) third row from top = mid-low (open-mid); (ii) right edge = back; (iii) right-hand member of the pair = rounded. Full description: mid-low back rounded.   4. Central vowels are plotted in the vertical middle of the horizontal range — neither at the left (front) edge nor at the right (back) edge. A central position indicates that the tongue body is in an intermediate horizontal position — not advanced toward the hard palate and not retracted toward the velum. The most familiar central vowel from this course is ə: mid central unrounded.   5. The full IPA quadrilateral is a catalogue of all available vowel symbols — every position that the IPA system provides. A language-specific vowel chart shows only the symbols for phonemes actually used by that language. Treating them as the same document would imply that every IPA symbol is a phoneme of every language, which would be false. Every real language uses only a small subset of the full catalogue.

Chapter Review — Part B

1. Reference vowels are a set of fixed auditory targets that define specific positions in the vowel space at extremes and equidistant intervals of the perceptual dimension. A fixed scale is useful because it allows phoneticians working with different languages, at different times, and in different laboratories to compare vowel positions against a shared external standard — rather than against each other's dialects or keywords. Without such a scale, vowel descriptions risk being circular or dialect-specific.   2. Reference vowels 1–8: (1) i — high front unrounded; (2) e — mid-high front unrounded; (3) ɛ — mid-low front unrounded; (4) a — low front unrounded; (5) ɑ — low back unrounded; (6) ɔ — mid-low back rounded; (7) o — mid-high back rounded; (8) u — high back rounded.   3. A reference value is the idealized auditory target at a specific chart position; a language realization is the phonetic target speakers of a particular variety aim for in producing that vowel phoneme. The two may differ: for example, the Spanish vowel transcribed as /e/ occupies the mid-high front unrounded region but may in some varieties be realized somewhat lower than the reference vowel 2 target. The symbol is a best-fit label, not a precision measurement.   4. Two analysts may use slightly different symbols for the same vowel if: they are working with different transcription precision levels (one broad, one narrow); they are representing different reference varieties of the same language; or they are applying different analytical traditions that draw the boundaries between categories at slightly different points. Neither is necessarily wrong — different traditions may make different conventions about where to locate the boundary between, say, /e/ and /ɛ/. The analyst should state the reference variety and transcription level to make comparisons possible.

Cumulative Review

1. The reference-vowel method does not add a fourth dimension or replace any of the three. It provides a calibration scale for the three existing dimensions — a way of specifying positions on those dimensions in terms of fixed external auditory anchors rather than approximate language-relative descriptions. The three dimensions (height, backness, rounding) define the framework; the reference vowels define specific points on that framework that serve as shared measuring marks.   2. The phoneme would be assigned the symbol that best represents the contrast category — for example, /e/ in slashes for a mid-high front vowel. The two allophones would be represented in square brackets at slightly different chart positions — for example, [e] in one environment (closer to the reference value) and [ɛ] in another (lower, closer to open-mid), or using diacritics to show raising or lowering. The phonemic label /e/ is chosen because it labels the category that is psychologically real to speakers as a single contrastive unit; the allophonic variants in brackets show its phonetic range.   3. The structural knowledge that makes chart navigation efficient rather than requiring memorization includes: (i) knowing that vertical position encodes height; (ii) knowing that horizontal position encodes backness; (iii) knowing that left-versus-right within a pair encodes rounding. These three pieces of structural knowledge allow the full description of any symbol to be read off from its position alone — turning the chart from a list of approximately 23 vowel symbols to be memorized into a two-dimensional grid that can be decoded at sight.

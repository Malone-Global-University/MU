Transcribing Recorded Speech

A repeatable, evidence-based workflow from audio to a defended transcription

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Define the recording, the language variety, and the transcription purpose before beginning any transcription task.
  2. Segment and replay audio systematically rather than transcribing on a single uncontrolled pass.
  3. Draft, test, and revise a transcription against the recording itself.
  4. Use slowed playback and visual evidence such as a waveform or spectrogram responsibly, without treating either as an automatic transcription.
  5. Document uncertainty in working notes rather than concealing it behind a confident final form.
  6. Distinguish what you actually heard from what spelling or a remembered word led you to expect.
  7. Complete a short transcription that is fully supported by a verified recording.

An Observable Problem

Chapter 26 handed you a transcription that someone else had already produced and asked you to read it accurately. This chapter reverses that task one more time. You now have a recording, no transcription, and a job to do: listen, decide what was said and how, and write it down using IPA that another trained reader could rely on.

This is a harder task than reading, and a different one. A finished transcription on a page is fixed; a recording is not. The same stretch of speech can be replayed at full speed, replayed slowed down, viewed as a waveform, or viewed as a spectrogram, and each pass can reveal something the others missed or obscured. A transcriber who listens once and writes down a confident guess is not transcribing. A transcriber who works through a controlled, repeatable procedure, checks each decision against the recording, and documents what remains uncertain is doing the job this chapter teaches.

This chapter asks three questions:

  1. What sequence of steps turns a recording into a defensible transcription, rather than a first impression written down and left unchecked?
  2. How should slowed playback, waveforms, and spectrograms support a transcriber's ear without replacing it?
  3. How does a transcriber keep hearing separate from expectation, so that a transcription reflects the recording rather than a guess about the underlying orthography?
A note on audio in this chapter. The examples and exercises below describe a workflow for transcribing real, verified recordings. No audio asset accompanies this chapter draft. Wherever an activity would ordinarily involve listening to a recording, the activity is presented as a worked demonstration using an already-transcribed, fully described recording, so that the reasoning process can be taught without pretending that a text-only exercise is listening practice. Recording scripts, sourcing requirements, and speaker metadata for a future audio asset belong in the production file for this chapter, not in the learner-facing material below.
Transcribing Recorded Speech

A Nine-Step Transcription Workflow

Purpose: replace a single uncontrolled listen-and-write pass with a disciplined procedure that produces a transcription you can defend.

Chapter 26 gave you a seven-step strategy for reading a transcription that already existed. Transcribing a recording adds earlier steps, because you must first establish what you are transcribing and why before any symbol is written, and later steps, because a draft transcription must be tested against the recording rather than accepted on the first attempt.

1
Establish metadata and task level
Record the language, variety, speaker context, and the requested level of detail before listening for content.
2
Listen globally
Play the entire recording once for overall shape, without pausing to transcribe individual segments.
3
Segment the utterance
Divide the recording into manageable stretches, such as words or short phrases, using pauses and prosodic boundaries as a guide.
4
Identify syllables and prosody
Locate nuclei, stress, tone, and boundaries within each segment before committing to fine segmental detail.
5
Draft broad segmental content
Write a first-pass broad transcription of each segment based on repeated listening.
6
Add supported detail
Add narrower detail only where the recording and the task level actually justify it.
7
Compare against the recording
Replay the draft transcription alongside the recording, segment by segment, checking each symbol against what is actually audible.
8
Document alternatives
Note any segment where more than one transcription is defensible, and state the evidence for each option.
9
Complete a consistency audit
Check the finished transcription for internal consistency: consistent delimiters, a single tone or intonation system, correctly attached diacritics, and no silently mixed conventions.
Concept Check — Why Global Listening Comes Before Segmenting

Listening globally first, before any transcribing begins, prevents a common failure: fixating on the first word or syllable and building an increasingly strained analysis around an early misperception. A global pass establishes the overall prosodic shape, likely word or phrase boundaries, and general register of the recording, so that later, detailed listening happens with realistic expectations already in place.

Establishing Metadata and Task Level

Purpose: apply the reference-variety and transcription-purpose policies from Chapters 24 and 26 at the start of a recording-based task, before any listening for content begins.

A transcription produced without stated metadata cannot be evaluated. Before transcribing any recording, establish, as far as the available information allows:

Metadata to establish before transcribing a recording
ItemWhy it matters
Language and reference varietyDetermines which phoneme inventory and realization patterns are expected, per Chapter 10's reference-variety policy.
Speaker contextA single speaker, multiple speakers, a scripted reading, or spontaneous speech each carry different expectations for consistency and connected-speech processes.
Requested transcription levelPhonemic, broad phonetic, or narrow phonetic, following the Chapter 24 distinction; this determines how much detail step six should add.
Source statusWhether the recording is an idealized citation-form reading or a naturally occurring utterance affects which connected-speech processes from Chapter 23 you should expect to hear.

Skipping this step does not save time. A transcriber who begins listening for content before settling the task level typically produces an inconsistent transcription, mixing phonemic and narrow phonetic detail without a principled reason, the exact problem Chapter 24 identified as a common transcription error.

Listening Globally Before Segmenting

Purpose: connect the global-then-detailed listening sequence to the auditory training principles established across the book, and demonstrate it with a fully described worked example.

A global first listen is not casual listening. It is a directed pass with specific goals: counting apparent words or phrases, noticing overall pitch movement, noting where pauses fall, and forming a provisional sense of speaker style and rate. None of these goals require transcribing a single segment yet.

Consider a short, fully described recording used here as a worked demonstration. The recording contains one speaker of a named variety producing a two-word phrase at a conversational rate, with a brief pause between the words and falling pitch at the end. A global listen establishes this much before any symbol is written: two prosodic units, a boundary between them, and a terminal fall consistent with a completed statement rather than a question.

Evidence Note — What a Global Listen Can and Cannot Establish

A global listen can reliably establish the number of prosodic units, the rough location of major boundaries, and the general pitch contour. It cannot reliably establish fine segmental detail, exact vowel quality, or the presence of subtle allophonic variation. Treat the global pass as a map of the territory, not as a transcription in miniature.

Drafting, Testing, and Revising

Purpose: teach the iterative core of the workflow, steps five through seven, as a cycle rather than a single pass.

A draft transcription is a hypothesis, not a final answer. After segmenting the recording and identifying syllables and prosody, write a broad transcription of each segment based on what you hear. Then immediately test that draft: replay the same stretch of recording while following your draft symbol by symbol, and ask whether each symbol still matches what is audible on a second and third listen.

This cycle regularly produces revisions. A segment that sounded like a plain voiceless stop on a first pass may reveal audible aspiration on a closer, repeated listen; a vowel transcribed as central on a first attempt may sound more front once compared directly against a neighboring, clearly front vowel elsewhere in the same recording. Revision at this stage is normal disciplined practice, not a sign of an unreliable ear.

Draft 1 — after step 5
[ˈkasa]
Broad first pass based on a single listen; medial consonant transcribed as a plain voiceless alveolar fricative.
Draft 2 — after step 7, first comparison
[ˈkasːa]
A second, focused listen to the medial consonant reveals audibly longer duration than a single fricative; length added and checked against a comparable form in the same recording.
Draft 3 — after step 8
[ˈkasːa] ~ [ˈkasa]
Both forms documented as alternatives in the working notes; the transcriber's confidence in the longer form is noted as moderate rather than certain, pending specialist review.

Note that the finished, learner-facing answer for a task like this should still present one transcription as the submitted response, with the alternative and its supporting reasoning placed in the accompanying commentary, exactly as Chapter 24 required for two-level transcription work.

Using Slowed Playback and Visual Evidence Responsibly

Purpose: extend Chapter 28's caution about acoustic displays to the transcription workflow itself, and prevent a visual display from silently replacing auditory judgment.

Slowed playback and visual displays such as waveforms and spectrograms are legitimate, useful tools at step seven. Slowed playback can separate segments that blur together at conversational rate. A waveform can help locate a closure or a burst precisely in time. A spectrogram can help confirm frication, distinguish a stop from an affricate, or estimate approximate formant values, applying the introductory acoustic skills from Chapter 28.

These tools support a transcriber's ear; they do not replace it. Slowed playback can distort natural coarticulation and voice quality, so a decision made only from slowed audio should be re-checked at natural rate before it is finalized. A spectrogram shows acoustic patterns, not a finished transcription; reading a spectrogram as if it printed IPA symbols directly repeats the error Chapter 28 explicitly warned against.

Common Mistake

Error: Basing a transcription decision entirely on a spectrogram image without confirming it against what is actually audible at natural speaking rate.

Correction: Use the spectrogram to support or question an auditory impression, then return to the recording at natural rate to confirm the decision. A transcription is a claim about what a listener can hear, not only about what a display can show.

Documenting Uncertainty

Purpose: apply the evidence-before-confidence principle to a transcriber's own working notes, and separate the honestly uncertain from the confidently supported.

Not every segment in a recording is equally clear. Background noise, overlapping speech, fast connected-speech reduction, or a genuinely ambiguous articulation can all leave a transcriber with real uncertainty. The workflow's eighth step exists specifically to hold that uncertainty rather than hide it.

Working notes should distinguish at least three levels of confidence: segments the transcriber is confident about and can defend from repeated listening; segments where two or more transcriptions are defensible and both should be recorded with their supporting evidence; and segments where the recording does not provide enough evidence to transcribe responsibly at all, which should be marked as such rather than filled in with a guess.

Evidence Note — An Honest Gap Is Better Than a Confident Guess

A transcription that marks an unclear stretch as uncertain, using a standard convention agreed with the project or instructor, is more useful and more accurate than one that fills the gap with a plausible-sounding guess. The goal of this chapter is a transcription whose confidence matches its evidence, not a transcription that looks complete at the cost of being unsupported.

Hearing Versus Orthographic Expectation

Purpose: extend the Chapter 26 reading-versus-guessing check to the act of transcribing from audio, where the same failure can occur in the opposite direction.

Chapter 26 warned against reading a transcription by guessing at a remembered spelling instead of interpreting the symbols present. Transcribing from a recording invites a related but distinct failure: hearing what a known spelling or a recognized word predicts, rather than what the recording actually contains.

This failure is strongest with familiar-sounding words, loanwords, or recordings in a language that shares vocabulary with the transcriber's own. A transcriber who recognizes a word before finishing the segmenting step may unconsciously transcribe the citation form they already know, rather than the specific realization actually present in that recording, including any reduction, assimilation, or speaker-specific variation.

Guard against this by completing steps two through five before consulting any gloss, translation, or orthographic form, exactly as the cover-the-gloss check in Chapter 26 recommended for reading. If a written form must be consulted for the task, treat it as a check on the finished draft, never as the source of the transcription itself.

Common Mistake

Error: Transcribing a recognized word using its expected citation-form pronunciation, rather than the reduced or contextually altered form actually audible in the recording.

Correction: Transcribe what is audible on repeated listening first, and compare the result with the citation form only afterward, treating any difference as potential evidence of a genuine connected-speech process rather than as an error to silently correct.

Transcribing Across Recording Conditions

Across Languages — The Workflow Adapts, the Steps Do Not Disappear

The nine-step workflow applies regardless of language, but recording conditions change how demanding each step is. A clear, single-speaker citation-form recording may move quickly through steps five through seven with few revisions. A multi-speaker recording of spontaneous connected speech, or a recording of a language with a large tonal or consonant-cluster inventory, will typically require more revision cycles at step seven and a longer list of documented alternatives at step eight. The steps themselves do not change; the amount of iteration each one requires does.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake

Error: Beginning detailed segmental transcription on the very first listen, before a global pass has established overall prosodic shape.

Correction: Complete a global listen first, noting prosodic units, boundaries, and overall contour, and only then move into segmenting and detailed drafting.

Common Mistake

Error: Treating a first draft as final without replaying it against the recording.

Correction: Always complete the compare-against-the-recording step before submitting a transcription, and expect that step to produce revisions.

Common Mistake

Error: Presenting an uncertain segment with unwarranted confidence rather than documenting the uncertainty.

Correction: Use the working-notes convention for uncertain segments, and record the specific evidence for and against each defensible alternative.

Answer Key (click to expand)

Analysis Exercises — Exercise A

The four metadata items are language and reference variety, speaker context, requested transcription level, and source status. Skipping the language and variety risks applying the wrong phoneme inventory or realization expectations. Skipping speaker context risks misjudging which variation is speaker-specific rather than systematic. Skipping the requested level risks producing an inconsistent mix of phonemic and narrow phonetic detail. Skipping source status risks mistaking a natural connected-speech process for an error, or the reverse.

Analysis Exercises — Exercise B

At step seven, the transcriber should replay the medial consonant and following vowel repeatedly to confirm whether the brief voicelessness is consistently audible rather than a one-time impression. If confirmed, the draft should be revised to reflect a voiceless or partially devoiced realization, for example using an appropriate diacritic if the task level and evidence support that level of detail. At step eight, the working notes should record both the original and revised forms, the specific evidence for the revision, and a confidence level, since a single brief cue may warrant a moderate rather than a certain confidence rating.

Analysis Exercises — Exercise C

The transcriber should complete steps two through five, global listening, segmenting, identifying syllables and prosody, and drafting, entirely from the recording, before consulting the resemblance to the English word or any written gloss. Only after producing an independent draft should the transcriber compare it with the expected form, treating any difference as potential evidence of a genuine phonetic property of the recording rather than as an error to silently correct toward the more familiar English-influenced pronunciation.

Looking Ahead

What You Can Now Do

You can now define the recording, variety, and transcription purpose before beginning a transcription task; segment and replay audio systematically; draft, test, and revise a transcription against the recording itself; use slowed playback and visual evidence responsibly, without letting either replace your own listening; document uncertainty honestly; and distinguish what you actually heard from what a familiar spelling or word led you to expect. Together with Chapters 24 through 26, you now understand how to decide what a transcription needs, how to notate it correctly, how to read someone else's finished transcription, and how to produce your own transcription directly from recorded speech.

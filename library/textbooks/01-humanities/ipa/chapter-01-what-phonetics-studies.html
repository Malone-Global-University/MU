What Phonetics Studies

Listening carefully · observing honestly · describing precisely

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Define phonetics and distinguish it from related fields such as phonology and spelling.
  2. Identify the three major branches of phonetics — articulatory, acoustic, and auditory — and describe the questions each branch asks.
  3. Classify a phonetic question as articulatory, acoustic, or auditory based on what it investigates.
  4. Distinguish a neutral phonetic observation from a social judgment about speech.
  5. Record a structured observation about a speech example without relying on spelling.
  6. Recognize accent as a systematic feature of speech rather than an absence of skill.

Opening Observation

Before reading any further, try this: say the word butter aloud three times, slowly. Now say it again at normal conversational speed. Now imagine how a friend might say it when they are tired, talking quickly, or speaking with a different regional accent than yours.

  1. Did the word sound exactly the same in every version? If not, what changed?
  2. How would you describe the differences to someone who had never heard either version?
  3. Could you write down what you noticed using ordinary spelling? Why or why not?

Most of us have a lifetime of experience listening to speech. We understand it effortlessly, process it at remarkable speed, and take its complexity almost entirely for granted. Yet the moment we try to describe what we actually hear — to move beyond "it sounds right" or "it sounds different" — we quickly discover how little ordinary language gives us. This is where phonetics begins.

What Phonetics Studies

Phonetics is the scientific study of the sounds of human speech. It asks three related kinds of questions: how are speech sounds produced, what physical properties do they have in the air, and how do listeners perceive and interpret them? Together these questions define the scope of the field.

A crucial word in that definition is sounds. Phonetics is not the study of letters. It is not the study of spelling rules. It is not the study of grammar. It is the study of the physical events that take place when a person speaks and when another person listens — the movements of the tongue and lips, the vibrations traveling through the air, and the neurological processing that transforms those vibrations into meaning.

This distinction between sound and letter matters so much that it is worth staying with for a moment. In English, the letter c represents different sounds in cat and ceiling. The letters gh represent a sound in ghost, a different sound in the middle of enough, and no sound at all in right. Meanwhile the same sound that begins phone also ends enough — two very different letter combinations, one speech sound. If we study letters, we will never understand speech. Phonetics insists on studying the sounds themselves.

Concept Note — Sound vs. Letter

Throughout this book, the word sound always refers to a speech event — something produced, transmitted, and perceived. It never means a letter of the alphabet. When we need to be precise about letters and spelling, we will enclose them in angle brackets like this: ⟨c⟩, ⟨gh⟩, ⟨f⟩. This convention will become second nature as the course proceeds.

Phonetics and phonology

Phonetics is closely related to — but distinct from — phonology, the study of how sounds function as a system within a particular language. Phonetics studies the physical reality of speech sounds; phonology studies the rules and patterns that organize those sounds within a grammar. A phonetician might measure the exact duration, in milliseconds, of a particular vowel in a particular speaker's utterance. A phonologist asks whether differences in vowel duration carry meaning in that language — whether they are contrastive in the way that two different words can be.

Both fields are valuable, and they inform each other constantly. This course focuses primarily on phonetics: on observation, description, and notation. Phonological analysis appears where it helps clarify what phonetics is detecting, but the goal of this book is to make you a careful, accurate observer of speech — the foundation on which any further analysis depends.

Phonetics and pronunciation teaching

Phonetics also differs from pronunciation instruction, though the two are obviously connected. A pronunciation teacher is interested in helping learners produce sounds that are intelligible and appropriate. A phonetician is interested in describing what any speaker actually produces, without prescribing what they should produce. This distinction — between description and prescription — is one of the most important conceptual commitments in linguistics, and Chapter 1 introduces it early because it shapes how you will approach every exercise in this book.

The Three Branches of Phonetics

Phoneticians study speech from three different vantage points, corresponding to the three stages of any speech event: production, transmission, and perception. These are known as articulatory phonetics, acoustic phonetics, and auditory phonetics.

Articulatory

Studies how the body produces speech sounds: the movements of the tongue, lips, jaw, vocal folds, and airflow. Asks: How is this sound made?

Acoustic

Studies the physical properties of sound waves traveling through the air: frequency, amplitude, and duration. Asks: What does this sound consist of, physically?

Auditory

Studies how listeners receive and interpret speech: the ear, the auditory nerve, and the brain's processing of sound. Asks: How is this sound heard and categorized?

In practice, these branches work together. A complete description of any speech sound benefits from all three perspectives. But individual researchers, teachers, and clinicians often emphasize one branch more than others, depending on their goals. A singer's coach focuses on articulatory control. An engineer designing speech-recognition software focuses on acoustic properties. A hearing scientist focuses on auditory processing. This book gives you a working understanding of all three, with particular emphasis on articulatory and auditory phonetics.

Articulatory phonetics

When you say a word, a complex sequence of physical events occurs. Air from the lungs passes through the larynx — the structure in the throat that houses the vocal folds — and into the chambers of the throat, mouth, and sometimes nose. The shape of these chambers, and the positions of the tongue, lips, and jaw, determine what sound emerges.

Articulatory phonetics describes this process in systematic terms. It identifies the moving parts involved in making a sound (called active articulators), the structures they contact or approach (called passive articulators), and the way airflow and vocal-fold vibration contribute to the sound's character. In later chapters, you will learn to describe any speech sound using this vocabulary precisely. For now, the important thing is simply to recognize that speech sounds are physical actions performed by a body — they can be observed, described, and practiced.

Acoustic phonetics

When speech is produced, it sets the air vibrating. These vibrations — sound waves — travel through the air until they reach a listener's ears. Acoustic phonetics studies the properties of these waves: their frequency (which we perceive as pitch), their amplitude (which we perceive as loudness), and their duration, among other properties.

Modern acoustic analysis uses software to display speech graphically. A waveform shows amplitude over time — you can literally see the difference between a loud sound and a quiet one, or between a long vowel and a short one. A spectrogram shows how the energy in a sound is distributed across frequencies over time, making visible distinctions that would otherwise require a trained ear to detect. You will be introduced to both tools in Part V of this book. For now, it is enough to know that acoustic evidence provides an objective record of what a speaker produced, independent of what a listener thinks they heard.

Auditory phonetics

Listening is not a passive recording of sound. The brain actively interprets incoming acoustic information, using context, expectation, and stored knowledge to make sense of what is heard. Auditory phonetics investigates how this perceptual process works: how listeners categorize sounds into distinct types, how they handle variation across speakers and accents, and how the auditory system can sometimes hear sounds that were never quite physically present.

The distinction between acoustic reality and auditory perception matters enormously in practice. Two sounds that look identical on a spectrogram may be heard as different because of the sounds surrounding them. Two sounds that look different may be heard as the same because listeners have learned to treat them as a single category. Training your ear — developing conscious auditory skill — is one of the most important and rewarding challenges in practical phonetics, and it runs through every chapter of this book.

Sound in Focus — The McGurk Effect

One striking demonstration of the interaction between acoustic and auditory phonetics is the McGurk Effect, first described by Harry McGurk and John MacDonald in 1976. When listeners hear a recording of one syllable while simultaneously watching a video of a speaker's mouth producing a different syllable, many people perceive a third syllable — one that was neither spoken nor shown. The auditory system integrates what the ears hear and what the eyes see, producing a perception that neither sense alone would have created.

This effect illustrates why phonetics requires all three branches: production, acoustic transmission, and perceptual processing are interconnected at every level. Studying any one of them in isolation gives an incomplete picture.

Learning to Observe Speech

This book repeatedly asks you to observe before you label. The reason is practical: if you arrive at a speech event already certain what you will find, you will find it — whether or not it is really there. Careful phonetic observation means setting aside assumptions, especially assumptions driven by spelling, and attending to what is actually happening in the physical world.

Observation, inference, and judgment

It is useful to distinguish three different kinds of statements about speech:

  • Observation: "The first sound in this word involves the lips pressing together, followed by the release of a small burst of air." A description of what physically happens. It could be verified by anyone who watched and listened carefully.
  • Inference: "The speaker is probably from the north of England, based on the quality of the vowel in the word cup." A conclusion drawn from observations. It goes beyond what is directly seen or heard but is supported by evidence.
  • Judgment: "That pronunciation is sloppy." An evaluation that reflects social attitudes, not phonetic description. It tells us something about the evaluator's expectations, not about the speech itself.

Phonetics is primarily concerned with observation. Inference based on phonetic observation plays an important role in sociolinguistics, forensic linguistics, and language documentation. But judgments — statements about whether a pronunciation is "correct," "lazy," or "educated" — are social evaluations, not phonetic descriptions. Learning to separate these is a core skill this book develops throughout.

Why spelling misleads us

One of the biggest obstacles to phonetic observation is spelling. Most literate adults have spent years learning to associate letters with language, and it takes real effort to set that association aside and hear what is actually there.

Consider the English word cats. The letter ⟨s⟩ at the end is voiceless — a hissing sound made without vocal-fold vibration. Now consider dogs. The letter ⟨s⟩ is the same, but the sound is actually different: most speakers produce a voiced ending, more like the sound in the middle of vision. The spelling is identical; the sounds are not. If you are listening through spelling, you will not notice this. If you are listening to speech directly, you might.

This course trains you to listen to speech directly. The International Phonetic Alphabet — the notation system introduced in Chapter 3 — is a tool specifically designed to capture what is heard, not what is spelled. But even before you learn that system, you can begin developing the habit of attending to sound rather than letter.

Across Languages — The Same Letter, Different Sounds

The gap between spelling and sound is not only an English problem. In French, the letter ⟨r⟩ represents a sound made in the back of the throat — nothing like the English sound that the same letter represents. In Spanish, the letter ⟨j⟩ represents a sound made near the back of the mouth that has no equivalent in English spelling. In German, the combination ⟨ch⟩ represents different sounds depending on the surrounding vowels. In Mandarin Chinese, the same spelling system (Pinyin) uses ⟨x⟩ to represent a sound that no letter in English orthography consistently represents.

This is exactly why the International Phonetic Alphabet was created: to provide one consistent notation system that works across all languages, independent of any individual orthographic tradition. You will begin learning it in Chapter 3.

What good phonetic observation looks like

Good phonetic observation is specific, neutral, and grounded in what can be seen, heard, or measured. Consider the difference between these two responses to hearing an unfamiliar speech sound:

  • "It sounds foreign and hard to make." Vague and evaluative. Tells us nothing useful about the sound itself.
  • "The tongue seems to touch or approach the upper teeth. The sound continues for a noticeable duration. There is a hissing quality. I don't hear any vocal vibration in my throat when I try to imitate it." Specific and descriptive. Points toward articulatory, temporal, and acoustic properties that can be investigated further.

The second response is not perfect phonetics — the observer doesn't yet have the vocabulary to be fully precise. But it is the beginning of phonetics. It reflects genuine attention to the sound as a physical event rather than a reaction to it as a social signal. This kind of attention is what all the formal concepts and notation in this book are designed to support and sharpen.

Phonetic Notebook — First Entry

Every chapter in this book includes a notebook prompt. These short writing tasks build the observation portfolio that anchors the course. For this first entry: choose a sound you find genuinely interesting or unusual — in any language, including your own — and write three to five sentences describing it as a physical event. Do not use spelling as your description. Try to say what the body does, what the sound feels like when you make it, and what it sounds like when you hear it. Keep this entry; you will return to it later in the course with sharper tools.

Description, Prescription, and Accent

The distinction between describing speech and evaluating it deserves its own discussion, because it runs into territory that many learners find surprising or even uncomfortable.

What accent actually is

Every speaker has an accent. This is not a poetic or polite way of saying something — it is a literal phonetic fact. An accent is a systematic set of phonetic features that characterizes a particular speaker, group, region, or style of speech. The word "systematic" is key: accent is not random variation or careless production. It is a coherent set of patterns that can be described, analyzed, and documented.

When someone says that a particular speaker "has no accent," they almost always mean that the speaker's accent closely resembles whatever accent the listener considers neutral or standard. But that standard is a social convention, not a phonetic reality. From a phonetic perspective, a speaker of so-called "Standard American English" has just as many describable phonetic features as a speaker of any regional or social variety — they simply happen to be features that attract less social attention in particular contexts.

Ethics and Identity — Accent and Judgment

Research in sociolinguistics has consistently documented that accent is used to make — and act on — social judgments about speakers' intelligence, education, social class, and trustworthiness. These judgments are not phonetic descriptions; they are social evaluations that reflect the values and biases of the evaluator. They are also frequently inaccurate as predictions of any of the things they purport to measure.

Phonetics does not tell you which accent is best. It gives you the tools to describe any accent accurately. Learning to make that description without embedding social evaluation is one of the habits this course works to build, precisely because it makes you a better observer — and because the judgments in question cause real harm to real speakers.

Variation as data, not noise

When speakers pronounce the same word differently — across regions, generations, social groups, or even in different situations — that variation is not a problem to be explained away. It is data. It tells phoneticians and linguists something real about how language works, how it changes, and how it is used socially. Treating variation as noise, deviation, or error means throwing away information.

This course will introduce you to phonetic variation at every level. Some variation is predictable from the surrounding sounds; some reflects regional or social identity; some reflects speech style or rate. Learning to notice variation — and to describe it accurately without judging it — is a core phonetic skill.

Common Error

Error: "Speaker A's pronunciation of this vowel is wrong because it doesn't sound like mine."

Correction: A phonetic description compares sounds to other sounds, not to a single standard of correctness. The appropriate question is: What are the phonetic properties of Speaker A's vowel, and how do they compare to Speaker B's? Whether either vowel is "correct" is a social question, not a phonetic one — and this course addresses both separately.

Key Terms

Chapter 1 Vocabulary

Phonetics
The scientific study of the sounds of human speech, including how they are produced, transmitted, and perceived.
Phonology
The study of how sounds function as a contrastive system within a particular language or grammar.
Articulatory phonetics
The branch of phonetics that studies how speech sounds are produced by the body — the movements of the tongue, lips, jaw, vocal folds, and airflow.
Acoustic phonetics
The branch of phonetics that studies the physical properties of speech sounds as waves traveling through the air.
Auditory phonetics
The branch of phonetics that studies how listeners perceive and categorize speech sounds.
Active articulator
A structure that moves during speech production, such as the tongue or lower lip.
Passive articulator
A structure that an active articulator contacts or approaches but does not itself move, such as the upper teeth or the roof of the mouth.
Vocal folds
Two folds of tissue in the larynx whose vibration produces voice. When they vibrate, a sound is said to be voiced; when they do not, a sound is voiceless.
Waveform
A visual display of a sound's amplitude (loudness) over time, produced by acoustic analysis software.
Spectrogram
A visual display showing how the energy in a sound is distributed across frequencies over time.
Accent
A systematic set of phonetic features characterizing the speech of a particular person, group, region, or style. Every speaker has an accent.
Observation
A description of what is physically present in a speech event, verifiable by direct attention to the sounds themselves.
Inference
A conclusion drawn from phonetic observations, going beyond what is directly heard or seen but supported by evidence.
Judgment
An evaluation of speech based on social attitudes rather than phonetic description. Phonetics describes; it does not judge.
Orthography
A writing system for a language — its conventional spelling. Orthography does not reliably represent pronunciation.

Practice Activities

Part A — Classifying Questions

Each item below is a phonetic research question. Classify it as primarily articulatory, acoustic, or auditory. Some items may touch more than one branch; choose the branch that is most directly addressed.

  1. How does the tongue position change as a speaker moves from the vowel in beat to the vowel in boot?
  2. Does the fundamental frequency of a speaker's voice rise at the end of yes/no questions?
  3. Why do listeners sometimes perceive a sound as one category even when acoustic measurements show it falls midway between two categories?
  4. What is the duration, in milliseconds, of the closure phase of the consonant in tap?
  5. How do the lips move differently when producing a bilabial sound in English versus in Japanese?
  6. At what point in a sound's duration do listeners decide which consonant they are hearing?
  7. Do spectrograms of the vowel in bit and the vowel in bet show consistent differences in formant frequency?
  8. When a speaker whispers, do listeners still perceive voiced and voiceless consonants as distinct categories?

Part B — Observation, Inference, or Judgment?

Classify each statement as an observation, an inference, or a judgment. Then, for any judgment, rewrite it as a neutral phonetic observation or inference about the same speech.

  1. "This speaker drops their final consonants — it's lazy pronunciation."
  2. "The vowel in this speaker's production of caught and cot appears to be identical on the spectrogram."
  3. "The speaker probably grew up in the American South, based on the lengthening of the vowel and the quality of the offglide."
  4. "That accent is much prettier than the accent where I grew up."
  5. "When producing the initial consonant of this word, the speaker's lower lip contacts the upper front teeth."
  6. "Her English accent is better than his."
  7. "The aspiration on this word-initial stop is noticeably longer than on the stops in the middle of the word."

Part C — Sound vs. Letter

Each of the following English words presents an interesting relationship between spelling and sound. Without using a phonetics textbook, write one or two sentences about the relationship — is the spelling consistent with how you would expect it to be pronounced, or does it behave differently?

  1. knight
  2. tsunami
  3. phone
  4. of
  5. Thomas
  6. Wednesday
  7. xylophone

Do not worry about formal phonetic terminology. The purpose of this exercise is to begin noticing that letters and sounds are not the same thing.

Part D — Accent and Observation

Think of two speakers whose accents differ noticeably from each other — they might be people you know, or voices you have heard in a recording or broadcast. Choose a single word that you have heard both speakers say.

  1. Describe what you notice about one speaker's pronunciation of that word, using only observations about what the sound does — not judgments about whether it is "correct."
  2. Describe what you notice about the other speaker's pronunciation of the same word in the same way.
  3. List at least two questions about those sounds that you cannot currently answer because you lack the descriptive vocabulary. Keep these questions; they should become answerable by the end of Part II.

Part E — Reflect

Answer the following in three to five sentences each.

  1. In your own words, explain the difference between phonetics and phonology. Why does the distinction matter for someone learning to transcribe speech?
  2. A classmate argues that the acoustic branch of phonetics is the most important because it provides objective measurements rather than subjective descriptions. Do you agree? What would an articulatory or auditory phonetician say in response?
  3. Someone tells you that they do not have an accent. Using what you have learned in this chapter, explain why a phonetician would disagree with that claim — and do so in a way that is respectful and accurate.

Chapter Summary

What you have covered in Chapter 1

  • Phonetics is the scientific study of the sounds of human speech — not letters, not spelling rules, not grammar.
  • The three branches of phonetics are articulatory (how sounds are produced), acoustic (the physical properties of sound waves), and auditory (how sounds are perceived and interpreted). All three contribute to a complete picture of speech.
  • A phonetic question asks what is physically present in speech. The answers come from observation, not from spelling, social convention, or intuition about what "should" be there.
  • A phonetic observation is specific and verifiable. It is different from an inference (a conclusion drawn from observations) and very different from a judgment (a social evaluation of speech).
  • Spelling is an imperfect and often misleading guide to pronunciation. English is one of many languages where the relationship between letters and sounds is complex and unpredictable. Learning to set spelling aside is one of the most important skills this book develops.
  • Accent is systematic, not random, and every speaker has one. Phonetics describes accents; it does not rank them.

Answer Key

Part A — Classifying Questions

  1. Articulatory. Tongue position is a matter of how the vocal tract moves during production.
  2. Acoustic. Fundamental frequency is a physical property of the sound wave.
  3. Auditory. The question is about how listeners categorize sounds — a perceptual process.
  4. Acoustic. Duration in milliseconds is a measurement of the sound wave.
  5. Articulatory. Lip movement is a feature of production.
  6. Auditory. The timing of categorical decisions is a question about perception.
  7. Acoustic. Formant frequency is a measurable property of the sound wave.
  8. Auditory. The question asks how listeners perceive a distinction under unusual acoustic conditions.

Part B — Observation, Inference, or Judgment?

  1. Judgment. The word "lazy" is an evaluation, not a description. A neutral alternative: "This speaker frequently produces words without audible final consonants in casual speech."
  2. Observation. The spectrogram is physical evidence; the description is neutral.
  3. Inference. The speaker draws a conclusion from phonetic features but does not evaluate them.
  4. Judgment. "Prettier" is an aesthetic evaluation, not a phonetic description. A neutral alternative: "The vowel in Speaker A's accent tends to be longer and more open than the corresponding vowel in Speaker B's accent."
  5. Observation. The lower lip contacting the upper front teeth is directly observable.
  6. Judgment. "Better" implies a ranked standard. A neutral alternative: "Speaker A's consonant clusters more closely resemble the phonetic patterns expected in Standard Southern British English than Speaker B's do." (Note: even this inference requires specifying what the standard of comparison is.)
  7. Observation / acoustic. Aspiration duration can be measured on a spectrogram.

Part C — Sound vs. Letter

Answers will vary. The following are illustrative observations; credit any accurate, specific observation about the relationship between spelling and sound.

  • knight — The letters ⟨kn⟩ once both represented sounds in English; today the ⟨k⟩ is silent. The ⟨gh⟩ is also silent. Four of the six letters represent no sound in contemporary pronunciation.
  • tsunami — In Japanese, both ⟨ts⟩ represent a single affricate sound. In English, many speakers simplify the word-initial cluster and produce only one sound at the beginning.
  • phone — The letters ⟨ph⟩ together represent the same sound as the letter ⟨f⟩. The spelling reflects Greek origin rather than English pronunciation.
  • of — The letter ⟨f⟩ here represents a voiced sound, not the voiceless sound it typically represents. The word is pronounced with the same final consonant as the word live.
  • Thomas — The ⟨th⟩ here represents a sound that ⟨th⟩ does not represent in common English words like the or think — it represents the sound spelled ⟨t⟩.
  • Wednesday — Most speakers do not pronounce the ⟨d⟩ or the second ⟨e⟩. The written form preserves historical sounds that have since been lost.
  • xylophone — The letter ⟨x⟩ represents the sound typically spelled ⟨z⟩ in this word. The ⟨ph⟩ again represents the sound typically spelled ⟨f⟩.

Part E — Reflect

Model answers are provided for guidance. Other accurate, well-reasoned responses are equally acceptable.

1. Phonetics studies the physical properties of speech sounds — how they are produced, what they consist of acoustically, and how they are perceived. Phonology studies how sounds function within a language system to create meaning and contrast. The distinction matters for transcription because a phonetician records what is physically there; a phonologist analyzes which distinctions count as meaningful in a particular grammar. Both questions are important, but they require different kinds of evidence and different notation conventions.

2. A thoughtful acoustic phonetician might note that acoustic measurements are more consistent and reproducible than articulatory descriptions, which rely on trained observation. However, articulatory and auditory phoneticians would point out that acoustic measurements alone cannot tell you how a sound is produced or how it is perceived. A spectrogram shows what frequencies are present; it does not show what the tongue is doing, and it does not tell you what a listener hears. All three branches complement each other; no single branch is sufficient.

3. Every speaker produces speech with a set of systematic phonetic features — vowel qualities, consonant realizations, prosodic patterns — that constitute their accent. When someone says they have no accent, they typically mean their accent matches a socially prestigious variety closely enough that listeners from the same social context do not notice it. But a phonetician could describe that speaker's vowel qualities, consonant choices, and intonation patterns in exactly the same way they could describe any other speaker's. The absence of social stigma is not the same as the absence of phonetic features.

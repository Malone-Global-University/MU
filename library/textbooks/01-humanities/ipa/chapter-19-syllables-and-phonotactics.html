Syllables and Phonotactics

How segments combine into larger structures, and what a language will and will not allow

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify the onset, nucleus, and coda of a syllable.
  2. Distinguish open syllables from closed syllables.
  3. Recognize complex onsets and complex codas in controlled data.
  4. Apply language-specific phonotactic evidence rather than assuming a universal syllable template.
  5. Distinguish syllabification from ordinary spelling division.
  6. Analyze syllabic consonants and describe moraic structure at an introductory level.

An Observable Problem

Say the English word strengths aloud. Most speakers produce it as a single syllable, yet it begins with three consonants in a row and ends with as many as four, depending on the analysis and the speaker's pronunciation. Now say the Japanese word sakura "cherry blossom." Every consonant in that word is immediately followed by a vowel; a sequence like the beginning of strengths would not be a possible word shape in Japanese at all.

Chapters 1 through 18 analyzed individual consonants and vowels and the dimensions that can accompany them. This chapter asks a different kind of question: how do languages organize segments into larger units, and what sequences does a given language permit?

Consider the English word rhythm. Dictionaries commonly divide the spelling as rhy-thm, but the second syllable has no written vowel at all. Say the word slowly and pay attention to what, if anything, is functioning as the vowel of that second syllable.

This chapter asks three questions:

  1. What internal structure does a syllable have, and how can that structure be represented?
  2. Why do some consonant sequences occur freely in a language while others never occur, even though every individual sound in the sequence exists in that language?
  3. Why can spelling division and phonetic syllabification disagree, and how should a learner decide where a syllable boundary actually falls?
Syllable Structure

Onset, Nucleus, and Coda

Purpose: give learners a structural vocabulary for describing the internal parts of a syllable, building on the vowel and consonant systems developed in Parts II and III.

A syllable is commonly analyzed as having up to three parts. The nucleus is the obligatory core of the syllable, normally a vowel, and it carries the syllable's peak of sonority. The onset is the consonant or consonant sequence that precedes the nucleus. The coda is the consonant or consonant sequence that follows the nucleus. Onset and coda are optional in many languages; the nucleus is not.

kOnset
æNucleus
tCoda

General American kæt "cat": onset k, nucleus æ, coda t.

The nucleus and coda together are sometimes grouped as the rime (or rhyme), a unit that is relevant to some phonotactic and prosodic patterns, including which syllables count as heavy for stress purposes, previewed later in this chapter and developed further in Chapter 20.

Concept Check — Sonority and the Nucleus

Vowels are the most common syllable nuclei because they are the most sonorous class of sounds discussed so far: they are produced with an open vocal tract and carry the most acoustic energy relative to their neighbors. Some languages also allow certain highly sonorous consonants, particularly nasals and laterals, to serve as a syllable nucleus when no vowel is present, a pattern taken up directly in the section on syllabic consonants below.

Open and Closed Syllables

Purpose: introduce the basic open-closed distinction as a foundation for later discussion of syllable weight and stress.

A syllable with no coda, ending in its nucleus, is an open syllable. A syllable with a coda is a closed syllable. English see, transcribed siː, is open; English seat, transcribed siːt, is closed. The distinction matters because many languages treat open and closed syllables differently for stress placement, vowel length, or historical sound change, a connection Chapter 20 develops under the heading of syllable weight.

General American seat — coda t

A word may contain a mixture of open and closed syllables. The open-closed label applies to a single syllable, not to a whole word.

Complex Onsets and Codas

Purpose: show that onsets and codas may contain more than one consonant, and that languages differ sharply in how many and which consonants they permit in a single margin.

An onset or coda containing more than one consonant is a complex onset or complex coda; a margin containing exactly one consonant, or none, is simple. English permits onsets of up to three consonants, as in strengths or splash, and codas of up to four in some analyses, as in the word that opened this chapter. Many languages permit only simple onsets and codas, or forbid codas altogether.

Syllabic Consonants and Mora

Purpose: extend the nucleus concept to consonants that function as a syllable peak, and introduce the mora as a conceptual tool for describing syllable weight without requiring formal moraic phonology.

In some languages, and in casual or careful speech in others, a highly sonorous consonant — typically a nasal or a lateral — can serve as the nucleus of its own syllable when no vowel is present. Many analyses of General American English treat the second syllable of rhythm as consisting of a syllabic m̩, with no separate vowel, rather than as an unpronounced vowel followed by m.

  • ˈɹɪ.ðm̩General American rhythm, one common analysis with a syllabic nasal coda syllable
  • ˈbʌ.tn̩General American button, one common analysis with a syllabic nasal
  • ˈbɒ.tl̩Southern British English bottle, one common analysis with a syllabic lateral

    • A mora is a unit of syllable weight rather than a segment. In languages that make productive use of moraic structure, a short vowel typically counts as one mora, a long vowel or diphthong typically counts as two, and a coda consonant may or may not add a mora depending on the language. Standard Japanese is frequently analyzed as a moraically timed language in which the moraic nasal and the first half of a geminate consonant each count as a full mora on their own, distinct from the syllable that contains them.

    Evidence Note — Mora as a Conceptual Tool

    This chapter introduces the mora as a way to talk about syllable weight, not as a mandatory unit of IPA transcription. A transcription records segments and syllable boundaries; a moraic analysis is a separate phonological claim about how those segments pattern for timing, stress, or other prosodic purposes. Treat mora counts as receptive background here; Chapter 20 returns to weight and timing in more detail.

    Not every language permits syllabic consonants. Confirm the pattern for a specific language and variety before applying the syllabic diacritic; do not assume every nasal or lateral adjacent to a consonant boundary is automatically syllabic.

    Phonotactics

    Phonotactic Restrictions

    Purpose: show that permissible sequences are a language-specific system, not a predictable consequence of which individual sounds exist in the language.

    Phonotactics is the set of restrictions a language places on how segments may combine, particularly within the syllable. A language can possess every individual consonant in a sequence and still forbid that sequence as a unit. English has both n and g, but no native English word begins with the sequence ŋ in onset position, even though ŋ occurs freely in coda position, as in sing.

    When a language borrows a word or a learner produces a sequence that violates its phonotactics, languages commonly apply a repair strategy rather than simply blocking the sequence:

    1. Epenthesis: inserting an extra vowel or consonant to break up an illegal sequence. Many Japanese loanwords from English insert a vowel after a consonant that would otherwise be followed by another consonant or occur in coda position, as in adaptations of English words ending in a consonant cluster.
    2. Deletion: removing a segment that would otherwise create an illegal sequence.
    3. Resyllabification: shifting a segment to a different syllable, discussed further as connected speech in Chapter 23.
    Across Languages — Onset Restrictions Are Not Predictable from the Inventory Alone

    Knowing a language's consonant inventory, taught in Part II, does not by itself predict which sequences that language allows in a syllable margin. Phonotactic restrictions must be established separately, using data from the specific language and variety, rather than inferred from which individual consonants happen to exist.

    Phonotactic statements are language-specific claims. A restriction documented for one language, or one variety of a language, should not be assumed to hold for a related language or a different variety without separate evidence.

    Syllabification Versus Spelling

    Purpose: separate phonetic syllabification, an analysis of spoken structure, from spelling-based syllable division, which follows orthographic and typographic conventions.

    Dictionaries and word-processing programs divide written words into syllables mainly to guide hyphenation at a line break. These divisions follow orthographic conventions that do not always match how a phonetician would analyze the spoken syllable. English rhythm is commonly hyphenated rhy-thm in dictionaries, but the phonetic analysis introduced earlier in this chapter treats the second syllable as headed by a syllabic m̩, with no written vowel corresponding to that nucleus at all.

    When intervocalic consonants are ambiguous between belonging to the preceding or following syllable, some analyses use the maximal onset principle as a starting heuristic: assign as many consonants as the language's phonotactics allow to the onset of the following syllable, rather than to the coda of the preceding one, unless independent evidence supports a different division. This is a useful default, not a universal law; some languages and some analyses divide syllables differently, and syllable division can even vary with speech rate or style.

    Spelling Trap — Hyphenation Is Not Phonetic Syllabification

    A dictionary's hyphenation points, such as pho-net-ics, are designed for typesetting and may not correspond to a phonetician's syllable boundaries. Treat hyphenation as a separate, orthography-driven convention, and base phonetic syllable division on pronunciation evidence, not on where a dictionary happens to allow a line break.

    Across Languages

    Across Languages — Hawaiian: A Strictly Open-Syllable System

    Hawaiian is commonly described as permitting only open syllables, with a simple onset of at most one consonant and no coda at all. This restriction shapes how the language adapts loanwords, which typically gain extra vowels to break up consonant sequences that would otherwise violate the native syllable template.

    Across Languages — Japanese: Moraic Timing and Loanword Adaptation

    Standard Japanese phonotactics generally restrict syllables to a simple onset followed by a vowel, with only a moraic nasal or the first half of a geminate consonant permitted in coda-like position. English loanwords ending in a consonant, or containing a cluster, are commonly adapted with inserted vowels so that the result fits this template, illustrating the epenthesis repair strategy introduced earlier in this chapter.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake

    Error: Assuming that every language allows the same onset and coda complexity as English.

    Correction: Onset and coda complexity is a language-specific phonotactic fact. Some languages permit longer sequences than English; many permit far shorter ones or forbid codas entirely. Confirm the pattern for the specific language before generalizing.

    Common Mistake

    Error: Using dictionary hyphenation as if it were a phonetic syllabification.

    Correction: Hyphenation follows orthographic and typesetting conventions and can diverge from phonetic syllable structure, as the rhythm example in this chapter shows. Base phonetic syllabification on pronunciation evidence.

    Common Mistake

    Error: Marking every nasal or lateral near a consonant boundary as syllabic without checking the language or variety.

    Correction: Syllabic consonants are not universal, and even within a language they may be optional or style-dependent. Confirm the pattern with evidence before applying the syllabic diacritic.

    Chapter Review — Part A

    1. The onset is the consonant or consonant sequence before the nucleus; the nucleus is the obligatory core of the syllable, normally a vowel; the coda is the consonant or consonant sequence after the nucleus. The nucleus is obligatory because it carries the syllable's peak of sonority and every syllable requires such a peak, while onset and coda are optional margins that many languages, and many individual syllables, lack.   2. An open syllable has no coda and ends in its nucleus, as in siː "see"; a closed syllable has a coda, as in siːt "seat."

    Chapter Review — Part B

    1. A complex onset or coda contains more than one consonant, as in the onset of splæʃ "splash." Hawaiian restricts syllable margins more than English, permitting only a single consonant in onset position and no coda at all.   2. The second syllable of rhythm has no written or spoken vowel; the nasal m itself carries the syllable's sonority peak and functions as the nucleus, which is why it is transcribed with the syllabic diacritic as m̩ rather than analyzed as a vowel followed by a plain nasal.

    Chapter Review — Part C

    1. A language may possess every individual consonant in a sequence while still forbidding that sequence as a unit, as shown by English permitting ŋ in coda position but not onset position; phonotactic restrictions must therefore be established with sequence-level evidence, not inferred from the inventory alone.   2. Epenthesis inserts an extra vowel or consonant to break up an illegal sequence; deletion removes a segment that would otherwise create one. Resyllabification, mentioned as a further strategy, shifts a segment to a different syllable and is developed further in Chapter 23.   3. Dictionary hyphenation follows orthographic and typesetting conventions designed for line breaks and does not always correspond to how a phonetician would analyze the spoken syllable, as the mismatch between the hyphenation and the phonetic analysis of rhythm demonstrates.

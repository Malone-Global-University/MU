Stress, Rhythm, and Quantity

Prominence, timing, and duration as organizing systems above the individual segment

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Distinguish lexical stress from phrase-level prominence.
  2. Identify primary and secondary stress in a transcription.
  3. Interpret duration, intensity, pitch, and vowel quality as possible cues to stress.
  4. Distinguish stress from vowel length or consonant quantity.
  5. Discuss rhythm and timing as evidence-based tendencies rather than fixed categories.
  6. Transcribe controlled stress and quantity patterns.

An Observable Problem

Say the English word record aloud in two ways: first as a noun, "I bought a new record," and then as a verb, "I want to record this." The spelling does not change, and every segment is arguably the same, yet something clearly differs between the two pronunciations. Say both versions again and try to identify exactly what changes.

Now compare English ninety and nineteen. In careful, isolated pronunciation both words may sound like they place extra prominence on their second syllable, but in a sentence like "I said ninety, not nineteen," the two words behave differently: one keeps its prominence in that position and one can lose it. Prominence, in other words, is not a fixed property stamped onto a word once and for all.

Finally, compare English beat and bead. Many speakers produce the vowel in beat as noticeably shorter than the vowel in bead, even though neither word is usually described as having a "long" vowel phoneme distinct from a "short" one the way some languages do. This raises a question this chapter answers directly: how do stress, vowel length, and consonant quantity relate to one another, and how can a learner avoid treating them as the same phenomenon?

This chapter asks three questions:

  1. What is lexical stress, and how does it differ from the prominence a word receives in a phrase or sentence?
  2. What acoustic and articulatory evidence signals that a syllable is stressed, and is that evidence the same in every language?
  3. How do syllable weight, timing, and rhythm relate to stress, and how far can a single rhythm label describe an entire language?
Stress

Lexical Stress

Purpose: define lexical stress as a property of the word and connect it to the syllable structure developed in Chapter 19.

Lexical stress is the relative prominence that one syllable of a word receives compared with the other syllables of that same word, independent of the sentence the word appears in. A word with more than one syllable typically has one syllable marked as more prominent than the rest; that syllable carries primary stress. Some longer words also have a second, weaker peak of prominence, carried by a syllable with secondary stress.

Lexical stress is contrastive in some languages, including English, where stress placement alone can distinguish related words. The noun-verb pair from the opening scenario is commonly analyzed with primary stress on the first syllable for the noun and on the second syllable for the verb.

Some languages instead rely primarily on lexical tone, addressed in Chapter 21, or on pitch accent, addressed alongside tone in the same chapter. Do not assume every language has a stress system comparable to English.

Concept Check — Stress Is Relative Within the Word

Stress is a relative property: a syllable is stressed or unstressed only in comparison with the other syllables in the same word or phrase. A monosyllabic word spoken alone is not usefully described as "stressed," because there is no other syllable in the word to compare it with. Stress becomes analyzable once a word has at least two syllables, or once that monosyllabic word is placed in a phrase alongside other words, the topic of the next section.

Phrase-Level Prominence

Purpose: separate lexical stress, a property of the word, from the prominence a word receives once it is placed in a phrase or sentence, previewing the phrase-level pitch work of Chapter 22.

Phrase-level prominence is the relative emphasis a word receives within a larger utterance. A word's lexical stress marks which syllable is eligible to be prominent; whether that syllable actually receives strong phrase-level prominence depends on the word's role in the sentence, including focus, contrast, and information structure, developed further in Chapter 22.

The ninety and nineteen comparison from the opening scenario illustrates this distinction directly. Both words retain a fixed lexical stress pattern. But in connected speech, a word like nineteen can shift its perceived prominence toward its first syllable when it appears before a strongly stressed word, a pattern sometimes called stress shift; this is a phrase-level adjustment, not a change to the word's underlying lexical stress. Chapter 23 returns to this kind of contextual adjustment as one of several connected-speech processes.

Evidence Note — Lexical Stress Is Stable, Prominence Is Not

A reliable diagnostic for distinguishing lexical stress from phrase-level prominence is to ask whether the pattern changes with context. Lexical stress placement, such as the difference between the noun and verb pronunciations of record, remains constant across most speaking contexts. Phrase-level prominence varies with focus, emphasis, and surrounding words, even though it still respects the syllable made eligible by lexical stress.

This chapter treats phrase-level prominence only as far as it clarifies lexical stress. A fuller treatment of phrase-level pitch, focus, and information structure belongs to Chapter 22.

Cues to Stress

Purpose: show that stress is normally signaled by a combination of acoustic and articulatory properties rather than by any single cue, and that the combination varies across languages.

Stressed syllables are commonly associated with some combination of the following properties, though no single cue is guaranteed to be present, and no cue is universally primary:

  1. Duration: stressed syllables are frequently longer than comparable unstressed syllables in the same word.
  2. Intensity: stressed syllables are frequently produced with greater acoustic intensity, often perceived as loudness.
  3. Pitch: stressed syllables are frequently associated with a pitch movement or a locally higher fundamental frequency, a connection developed further once tone and intonation are introduced in Chapters 21 and 22.
  4. Vowel quality: in some languages, including English, unstressed syllables are prone to vowel reduction, in which a fuller vowel quality is replaced by a more central quality such as ə, while stressed syllables resist this reduction.

English is frequently described as relying heavily on a combination of duration, pitch movement, and vowel reduction, with intensity playing a smaller role than casual descriptions of "loudness" suggest. Other languages weight these cues differently; a language with a fixed vowel inventory that resists reduction, for instance, cannot use vowel-quality change as a stress cue in the same way English does. Treat the specific weighting of cues as a claim requiring language-specific evidence, not as a universal formula.

Across Languages — Fixed Stress Position

Some languages place lexical stress at a predictable position in every word, rather than allowing it to vary contrastively. Standard Polish is commonly described as stressing the second-to-last syllable of most native words, and Standard Finnish is commonly described as stressing the first syllable of every word. In languages with this kind of fixed, predictable stress, stress placement carries far less contrastive weight than it does in English, since a learner can often predict it directly from the word's syllable count.

Common Mistake

Error: Treating loudness as the single defining property of a stressed syllable.

Correction: Loudness, or intensity, is only one of several possible cues, and it is often not the strongest one. Duration, pitch movement, and resistance to vowel reduction are frequently more reliable evidence for stress in a language like English, and the relevant cues differ by language.

Quantity and Rhythm

Stress Versus Length and Quantity

Purpose: prevent the common confusion between stress, an accentual property of a syllable within a word, and length or quantity, a durational property that can be independently contrastive.

Stress and duration are related but distinct systems. A stressed syllable is frequently longer than an unstressed one, but phonological length is a separate, sometimes independently contrastive property. In languages with phonemically contrastive vowel or consonant length, a long and a short segment can distinguish word meaning regardless of which syllable carries stress. Finnish distinguishes words by consonant and vowel length independently of its fixed, predictable stress pattern described above; a word's stress placement does not change which vowels or consonants are phonologically long.

The English beat and bead comparison from the opening scenario illustrates a related but distinct phenomenon: pre-fortis clipping, in which a vowel is phonetically shorter before a voiceless consonant than before a voiced one, even though English does not treat these two vowel qualities as contrastively long and short phonemes the way Finnish treats its vowels. Treat this English pattern as a phonetic, context-driven duration difference, not as evidence of a phonemic length contrast.

Syllable Weight and Stress Placement

Purpose: connect the open-closed distinction and the mora, introduced in Chapter 19, to the placement of stress, fulfilling the preview set up at the end of that chapter.

Chapter 19 distinguished open and closed syllables and introduced the mora as a unit of syllable weight. In many languages with a predictable or partly predictable stress system, syllable weight plays a direct role in determining where stress falls. A common pattern, though not a universal one, treats a syllable containing a long vowel, a diphthong, or a coda consonant as heavy, and a syllable with a short vowel and no coda as light; stress rules in such languages may then target the heaviest available syllable within a defined window near the end of the word.

Latin is frequently cited as an example of a weight-sensitive stress system: stress commonly falls on the second-to-last syllable when that syllable is heavy, and shifts to the third-to-last syllable when the second-to-last syllable is light. Treat this as one illustrative pattern rather than a template that applies to every weight-sensitive language; the details of which syllable positions are examined, and how weight is calculated, are language-specific facts requiring separate evidence.

Evidence Note — Weight Sensitivity Is Not Automatic

Not every language with heavy and light syllables uses that distinction to place stress, and not every stress system is weight-sensitive. Confirm weight sensitivity for a specific language using descriptive or scholarly evidence rather than assuming that any language with long vowels or codas must place stress by weight.

Rhythm and Timing

Purpose: introduce rhythm and timing as a topic supported by evidence-based tendencies, without treating traditional timing categories as absolute, mutually exclusive boxes.

Languages are sometimes described using broad rhythm categories such as stress-timed, in which stressed syllables are said to recur at roughly regular intervals regardless of how many unstressed syllables fall between them, and syllable-timed, in which syllables are said to recur at roughly regular intervals regardless of stress. English is commonly cited as a stress-timed language and Spanish as a syllable-timed language.

Careful acoustic measurement has generally not confirmed strict isochrony, or perfectly regular timing, in either type of language. Contemporary phonetic research treats stress-timing and syllable-timing as impressionistic tendencies along a continuum rather than as strict, mutually exclusive categories with exact regular intervals. Some researchers instead measure rhythm using quantitative indices based on the proportion and variability of vowel duration within an utterance, offering a more evidence-based alternative to the traditional two-category system.

Variation Note — Rhythm Labels Are Tendencies, Not Guarantees

Describing a language as stress-timed or syllable-timed is a useful shorthand for a general tendency, not a claim that every utterance in that language shows measurably equal intervals. Treat these labels as starting points for further evidence, and be cautious about extending a single rhythm label to every register, speech rate, or regional variety of a language.

This chapter introduces rhythm as a conceptual bridge between syllable-level timing and the connected-speech patterns developed in Chapter 23. It does not require learners to calculate rhythm indices or perform instrumental timing measurements.

Across Languages

Across Languages — Finnish: Fixed Stress With Independent Length

Standard Finnish is commonly described as placing primary stress on the first syllable of virtually every word, a pattern that carries little contrastive weight on its own. At the same time, Finnish maintains an independent, phonemically contrastive length distinction for both vowels and consonants, illustrating that a language's stress system and its quantity system can operate as largely separate phonological dimensions rather than a single combined property.

Across Languages — Latin: A Weight-Sensitive Stress System

Latin is frequently cited as a language whose stress placement depends on syllable weight, commonly falling on the second-to-last syllable when it is heavy and shifting to the third-to-last syllable when the second-to-last syllable is light. This pattern illustrates the connection between the syllable weight concepts introduced in Chapter 19 and the stress-placement systems introduced in this chapter, and shows that stress rules can be sensitive to syllable-internal structure rather than counting syllables alone.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake

Error: Assuming that stress and phonemic vowel length are the same phenomenon because a stressed vowel often sounds longer.

Correction: Stress is a relative accentual property among syllables in a word; phonemic length is an independently contrastive durational property in languages that have it. A stressed syllable may be phonetically longer without the language having a separate long-short vowel contrast, as the English examples in this chapter show.

Common Mistake

Error: Treating "stress-timed" and "syllable-timed" as strict, measurable categories that every utterance obeys exactly.

Correction: These labels describe general tendencies along a continuum, not precise, isochronous timing confirmed by acoustic measurement. Present them as starting descriptions that further evidence can refine, not as absolute rules.

Common Mistake

Error: Assuming every language places stress the same way English does, or that every language has contrastive lexical stress at all.

Correction: Some languages have fixed, fully predictable stress; others rely primarily on lexical tone or pitch accent instead of stress, topics developed in Chapter 21. Confirm a language's stress system with evidence before generalizing from English.

Broad and Narrow Transcription

Making the level of a transcription an explicit, defensible decision

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify the purpose and assumptions behind a given transcription.
  2. Distinguish phonemic, broad phonetic, and narrow phonetic transcription.
  3. Select the consequential details a transcription task actually requires.
  4. Explain why narrow transcription is not automatically the better choice.
  5. Recognize alternative, equally defensible analyses of the same data.
  6. Create parallel representations of the same verified form at different levels of detail.

An Observable Problem

Chapter 23 compared a citation form written between slashes with a connected realization written between square brackets, and treated that difference mostly as a matter of context: careful, isolated speech versus fluent, connected speech. But even a single, carefully spoken word can be transcribed in more than one legitimate way. Consider the English word "little" in a rhotic-less General American-adjacent pronunciation. One transcriber might write /ˈlɪtəl/. Another, working from the same recording, might write [ˈlɪɾɫ̩], showing a tapped medial consonant, a syllabic lateral, and velarization. Neither transcriber has necessarily made an error. They have answered different questions.

This chapter asks three questions:

  1. What is actually being claimed by a transcription written between slashes, compared with one written between square brackets at increasing levels of detail?
  2. How does a transcriber decide, for a specific task, how much detail is worth including?
  3. Why is it a mistake to treat the narrowest possible transcription as the automatically correct or automatically best one?
Transcription Levels

Three Levels, One Utterance

Purpose: show the same verified form at three levels before naming any rule, so the difference is heard and seen before it is defined.

A single verified utterance can be represented at more than one level of abstraction. This chapter uses three working levels: phonemic transcription, which represents only the contrastive categories relevant to an analysis; broad phonetic transcription, which represents a defined, pedagogically useful degree of phonetic detail without attempting to capture every observable feature; and narrow phonetic transcription, which adds further observed or stipulated detail beyond the broad level.

Phonemic
/ˈlɪtəl/
General American-adjacent reference variety; represents contrastive segments only, consistent with the phoneme-level analysis introduced in Chapter 5.
Broad phonetic
[ˈlɪɾl̩]
Same variety; shows the flapped medial consonant and syllabic lateral, two details that are predictable but frequently included at a defined broad level.
Narrow phonetic
[ˈlɪɾɫ̩]
Same variety; adds velarization on the syllabic lateral, a further articulatory detail that a narrow task might require and a broad task normally would not.

Each level answers a legitimate, different question about the same recording. None of the three forms is a corrected version of another.

Purpose Drives the Level

Purpose: connect level selection to the purpose-dependent transcription principle established throughout the book, and to the phone-phoneme-allophone framework from Chapter 5.

A phonemic transcription represents the categories that contrast for a stated variety and analysis: swap one phoneme for another and the meaning of the word changes, or at least could change, for at least one pair of words in the language. A phonemic transcription deliberately omits predictable, allophonic detail, because that detail does not change which contrastive category a listener perceives, a distinction developed fully in Chapter 5.

A broad phonetic transcription is not simply "less careful" phonemic work. It is a phonetic transcription, written in square brackets, that a project or task has defined to include a specific, limited set of predictable detail, chosen because that detail is pedagogically or analytically useful at the stated level. A narrow phonetic transcription extends that same square-bracketed representation with additional detail, again chosen because a specific task requires it, not because more detail is inherently more accurate.

Concept Check — Every Transcription Has an Implicit Question

Before evaluating whether a transcription is "right," ask what question it was written to answer. A phonemic transcription answers "what contrastive categories does this speaker use here?" A broad phonetic transcription answers "what does this sound like at a level of detail useful for this task?" A narrow phonetic transcription answers "what additional, specific phonetic properties are observable or relevant here?" A transcription cannot be judged accurate or inaccurate without knowing which question it was answering.

Selecting Consequential Detail

Purpose: give learners a working procedure for deciding what belongs in a given transcription.

Not every phonetic detail available in a recording is worth transcribing. A useful working question is: does this detail matter for the stated purpose of the transcription? Detail is consequential when it is contrastive for the variety in question, when the task has explicitly requested it, when it is necessary to explain a specific pattern under discussion, or when omitting it would mislead a reader about what was actually said. Detail is not automatically consequential merely because it is audible, measurable, or interesting.

  • Task: "Give the phonemic form used to teach this word's contrastive pronunciation." → /ˈlɪtəl/ Flapping and syllabicity are predictable in this variety and are not required at the phonemic level.
  • Task: "Show how a beginner is likely to actually hear the medial consonant." → [ˈlɪɾl̩] The flap is the consequential detail for this specific pedagogical purpose.
  • Task: "Document the precise articulatory quality of the lateral for a dialectology study." → [ˈlɪɾɫ̩] Velarization becomes consequential because the stated purpose is a fine-grained articulatory comparison.
    • Common Mistake

    Error: Adding every diacritic a learner has ever seen to a transcription, regardless of whether the task requires it, in order to appear thorough.

    Correction: Every diacritic in a transcription should be traceable to a stated purpose or observed evidence, consistent with the diacritic-selection principle introduced in Chapter 25. Unsupported or unrequested detail is overtranscription, not rigor.

    Why Narrow Is Not Automatically Better

    Purpose: directly counter the common beginner assumption that more detail always means a better transcription.

    It is tempting to assume that a narrow phonetic transcription is simply a more accurate version of a broad or phonemic one, and that a careful transcriber should always prefer the most detailed representation available. This assumption is mistaken for several reasons.

    First, a phonemic transcription is not an incomplete phonetic transcription; it is a different kind of claim, about contrastive categories rather than physical detail, and adding phonetic detail to it does not make the phonemic claim more correct. Second, a narrow transcription that is not supported by genuine evidence, such as an instrumental measurement or a careful, controlled listening judgment, can overstate the transcriber's actual certainty. Third, excessive narrow detail can obscure the pattern a reader actually needs to see, burying a consequential contrast under a large number of inconsequential ones. Fourth, narrow transcriptions are harder to produce reliably and harder to compare across transcribers, so a task that does not require that level of detail incurs unnecessary cost and risk of error for no analytical benefit.

    Evidence Note — Detail Claims Require Support

    A narrow phonetic transcription is only as good as the evidence behind each added symbol. Acoustic evidence, discussed further in Chapter 28, can support some narrow claims more reliably than unaided listening alone. A transcriber who adds detail without a clear evidentiary basis is not being more careful; they are making an unsupported claim that happens to look precise.

    Recognizing Defensible Alternatives

    Purpose: prepare learners to accept more than one correct answer when the underlying analysis genuinely allows it.

    Two trained transcribers working from the same recording can produce different, equally defensible transcriptions at the same stated level, because phonetic and phonological analysis sometimes leaves more than one reasonable option. This can happen when a boundary case falls between two categories, when a language's own descriptive tradition offers more than one accepted convention, or when a predictable process could reasonably be represented either by showing its output or by leaving it to a general rule stated once in a transcription key.

  • Syllabic l̩ versus əl Two broad-level conventions for representing a word-final syllabic lateral in some English varieties; a transcription key should state which convention is used and apply it consistently, following the convention-key policy introduced across this book.

    • Recognizing a defensible alternative is not the same as accepting an arbitrary or unsupported choice. A defensible alternative must still be internally consistent, stated explicitly in a transcription key, and applied the same way throughout a single analysis.

    Levels Across Languages

    Across Languages — The Same Level Distinction, Different Content

    The phonemic-versus-phonetic distinction applies to every language with a describable sound system, not only to English. A language with a small phoneme inventory can still show extensive allophonic variation that only a broad or narrow phonetic transcription would capture, and a language with a large phoneme inventory does not automatically require narrower transcription than one with a small inventory. The amount of allophonic detail worth representing depends on the specific sound system and the specific task, not on the size of the phoneme inventory alone.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake

    Error: Treating phonemic transcription as an inferior, less accurate version of phonetic transcription.

    Correction: Phonemic transcription makes a different kind of claim, about contrastive categories, and is not made more correct by adding phonetic detail it was never designed to represent.

    Common Mistake

    Error: Assuming a single, universal broad transcription exists for a word, independent of the analysis or variety chosen.

    Correction: A broad transcription reflects a defined level and a stated variety. Different projects can define broad transcription differently, provided each defines and applies its convention consistently.

    Common Mistake

    Error: Silently mixing phonemic and phonetic detail within the same transcription, or mixing conventions from two different varieties.

    Correction: Choose one level and one variety for a given transcription, mark the level with the correct delimiters, and state the convention explicitly if it could otherwise be ambiguous.

Mapping Phonotactics, Prosody, and Variation

Extending the segmental inventory into a whole-system description of how a language organizes speech

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Summarize syllable structures and phonotactic restrictions for a documented variety.
  2. Describe stress, rhythm, quantity, tone, pitch accent, or intonation as relevant to that variety.
  3. Identify major connected-speech processes supported by your evidence.
  4. Document regional, social, stylistic, and speaker variation.
  5. Distinguish productive rules from descriptive tendencies.
  6. Select representative broad and phonetic transcription examples.
  7. Identify priorities for perception and production based on contrast and communicative consequence.

An Observable Problem

Chapter 30 gave you a draft segmental inventory: a consonant chart, a vowel chart, a major-allophone list, and an orthographic correspondence table, each traceable to your source ledger. That inventory can tell a reader what sounds occur in your chosen variety, but it cannot yet tell them how those sounds combine into syllables, which syllables receive prominence, how pitch is used, what happens to careful forms in fluent speech, or how much any of this actually varies from speaker to speaker.

Suppose your ledger now also contains a grammar chapter on syllable structure, a peer-reviewed article describing a stress-assignment pattern with several documented exceptions, a sociolinguistic study noting that a particular vowel reduction is more common in casual speech than in careful speech, and no source at all addressing whether the variety has phonemic tone. A profile that ignores these differences and simply states "the language has stress on the first syllable" would misrepresent the evidence you actually have.

This chapter asks three questions:

  1. What belongs in a phonotactic and prosodic profile, and how much of it must be represented for every language regardless of type?
  2. How does an analyst decide whether a documented pattern is a productive rule, a strong tendency, or a claim that should be qualified?
  3. How does a profile represent variation honestly without either flattening it into a single "correct" form or overwhelming the reader with every documented difference?

Key Terms

  • Phonotactic profile: a documented summary of a variety's permitted and restricted sound sequences, including syllable structure and any positional restrictions.
  • Prosodic profile: a documented summary of the prominence and pitch systems active in a variety, drawn from whichever of stress, rhythm, quantity, tone, pitch accent, and intonation the evidence supports.
  • Productive rule: a pattern that applies predictably across the relevant class of forms, with exceptions that are themselves systematic or lexically listed.
  • Descriptive tendency: a pattern that occurs frequently in the evidence but does not apply predictably enough to state as a rule without qualification.
  • Variation statement: a documented account of how a pronunciation pattern differs by region, social group, style, generation, or speaker, with each source of variation identified rather than merged.
  • Pronunciation priority: a perception or production target selected because it carries high communicative consequence, based on contrast, frequency, or intelligibility rather than on how noticeable it is to an outside observer.
Beyond the Inventory

Syllable Structure and Phonotactic Restrictions

Purpose: apply the syllable and phonotactic analysis from Chapter 19 to summarize, from sourced evidence, how the capstone variety organizes segments into syllables.

Begin the phonotactic profile with a statement of the variety's basic syllable template, using onset, nucleus, and coda terminology from Chapter 19. State whether onsets and codas are obligatory or optional, how many consonants a legal onset or coda cluster may contain, and which consonants may combine there. Where a source documents syllabic consonants or moraic structure as relevant to the variety, summarize that pattern at the introductory level established in Chapter 19 rather than attempting a full moraic analysis.

Distinguish restrictions that hold across the entire language from restrictions that apply only in specific positions, such as word-initially, word-finally, or across a morpheme boundary. A phonotactic profile that states only "consonant clusters are permitted" without specifying where and which ones understates the system; one that lists every attested cluster without noting the governing pattern misses the organizing structure the reader needs.

Common Mistake

Error: Describing a phonotactic restriction as absolute after finding it in every example examined, without checking whether the source itself notes exceptions or whether the sample was simply small.

Correction: State the restriction's strength in proportion to the evidence, and mark a pattern as a tendency rather than a rule when the source base is limited. Section 31.5 develops this distinction further.

Describing the Prosodic System

Purpose: apply the stress, rhythm, quantity, tone, pitch accent, and intonation systems from Chapters 20 through 22 to build the prosodic section of the profile, using only the systems the evidence supports.

Not every language uses every prosodic system covered in Part IV, and a responsible profile does not force one onto a variety that does not use it. Begin by identifying, from your source ledger, which of lexical stress, rhythm and timing, segmental quantity, lexical tone, pitch accent, and intonation are documented as active in the variety. For each active system, state its basic pattern, using the terminology and notation established in the relevant chapter, and cite the source.

Where a source documents lexical stress, state the default placement pattern and any documented exceptions, following the stress-versus-length distinction from Chapter 20. Where a source documents lexical tone or pitch accent, state which notation system you are using consistently, following the single-system rule from Chapter 21, and note whether tone sandhi is documented. Where a source addresses intonation, summarize the declared contour system from Chapter 22 rather than presenting an unsupported claim that a particular contour always carries a particular meaning.

Evidence Note — Absence of Evidence Is Not Evidence of Absence

If no source in your ledger addresses lexical tone for your capstone variety, the correct entry is that tone is undocumented in your current evidence, not that the variety lacks tone. Follow the confirmed-absence, undocumented-gap, and disputed-cell distinctions introduced for the segmental inventory in Chapter 30, and apply the same three-way distinction here to prosodic systems.

Common Mistake

Error: Assuming a variety must have word-level stress because English does, and describing an unstressed or tonal language as having "weak" or "unclear" stress.

Correction: Let the source evidence determine which prosodic systems are active, following the cross-linguistic comparison policy from Chapter 13 that avoids treating English patterns as the default.

Major Connected-Speech Processes

Purpose: apply the coarticulation and connected-speech categories from Chapter 23 to document the processes that shape the variety's fluent, contextual pronunciation.

For each documented connected-speech process, identify which category it belongs to, using the assimilation, reduction, deletion, epenthesis, resyllabification, and linking categories from Chapter 23, state the environment that triggers it, and note whether the source describes it as gradient or categorical. As in Chapter 23, do not treat every difference between a careful citation form and a connected-speech form as an error or a deletion by default; some differences are systematic and expected.

Where sources indicate that a process is more common at a faster speech rate or in a less formal style, record that qualification directly rather than presenting the process as equally likely in every context. This connects the connected-speech section directly to the variation statement developed later in this chapter, since style and rate effects are themselves a form of documented variation.

Common Mistake

Error: Listing a connected-speech process without stating whether it is obligatory, optional, or style-dependent, leaving the reader unable to predict when a careful pronunciation is still acceptable.

Correction: State the process's status explicitly, using the same environment-plus-rule format practiced for allophones in Chapter 30, and note any style or rate qualification the source provides.

Documenting Variation

Purpose: apply the variation, ethics, and identity policy from the Master Instructions to build a variation statement that represents the capstone variety's diversity honestly rather than presenting one form as the only correct one.

For each documented case of variation, identify its likely source using the categories introduced in the Master Instructions: regional, social, stylistic, generational, contextual, conservative or innovative, or speaker-specific. State which form, if any, this profile treats as the reference form for the rest of the document, and make clear that this choice is a documentation convenience rather than a claim that other forms are lesser.

Where your sources disagree about which form is more common or more standard, report the disagreement rather than silently picking a side. Where a source discusses accent attitudes or language ideology connected to a documented variant, summarize that discussion neutrally, consistent with the respectful treatment of accent and identity required throughout this book.

Variation Note — A Reference Form Is Not the Only Correct Form

Selecting one pronunciation as the profile's reference form is a documentation decision made for consistency, not a linguistic judgment that other documented forms are incorrect. State this explicitly in the variation section so a reader does not mistake the profile's organizational choice for a claim about which speakers pronounce the language "properly."

Rules Versus Tendencies

Purpose: extend the evidence-before-confidence principle to phonotactic, prosodic, and connected-speech claims specifically, since these systems are especially prone to overstated generalizations.

A productive rule applies predictably across the relevant class of forms, and any exceptions are themselves systematic or belong to a definable list. A descriptive tendency occurs frequently in the evidence but cannot be stated as a rule without qualification, either because the source itself hedges the claim or because your own review of the examples turns up unexplained exceptions.

1
Check the source's own confidence
Does the source state the pattern as a rule, a tendency, or a preference? Preserve that framing rather than upgrading it.
2
Review your own examples
Do the examples in your ledger support the pattern without exception, or do some cases deviate without an obvious explanation?
3
State the pattern at the appropriate strength
Use rule language only when the evidence supports it; otherwise state the pattern as a documented tendency and note the exceptions.

This distinction matters most in the phonotactic and prosodic sections of the profile, where it is tempting to state a single governing rule for readability. A profile that overstates a tendency as an exceptionless rule will mislead a learner who later encounters a legitimate exception; a profile that qualifies every observation into vagueness will not give the learner anything usable. Aim for the most precise statement the evidence actually supports.

Selecting Representative Transcriptions

Purpose: apply the broad-versus-narrow transcription policy from Chapter 24 to select a small, well-chosen set of sample transcriptions that illustrate the phonotactic, prosodic, and connected-speech patterns documented in this chapter.

Choose sample forms that each illustrate a specific, previously stated point, such as the syllable template, the stress pattern, a documented connected-speech process, or a case of variation, rather than selecting forms at random. For each sample, provide a broad transcription and, where the point requires it, a narrow phonetic transcription that shows the relevant detail, following the level-appropriate selection practice from Chapter 24.

Perception and Production Priorities

Purpose: synthesize the phonotactic, prosodic, connected-speech, and variation sections into a short, ranked set of priorities that will guide the capstone's perception and production goals in Chapter 32.

Rank candidate priorities by contrast and communicative consequence rather than by how noticeable or unfamiliar a pattern seems to an outside observer. A contrast that distinguishes many word pairs, or a connected-speech process that occurs in nearly every utterance, deserves higher priority than a rare or marginal pattern, even if the rare pattern is more perceptually striking to a new learner.

  • List each candidate priority drawn from the phonotactic, prosodic, connected-speech, and variation sections above.
  • For each candidate, state why it matters: a phonemic contrast it protects, a frequency argument, or an intelligibility consequence documented in a source.
  • Rank the candidates from highest to lowest priority and briefly justify the ranking.
  • Keep the list short enough to be genuinely useful; a list that ranks everything as equally important is not a priority list.
    • Common Mistake

    Error: Prioritizing a sound or pattern mainly because it is difficult for English speakers, rather than because of its role in the target variety's own system.

    Correction: Base priority on contrast and communicative consequence within the target variety, consistent with the English-comparison policy that treats English difficulty as secondary to the target system's own structure.

    Systems That Organize Speech Differently

    Across Languages — No Single Template Fits Every Variety

    Some varieties organize prominence primarily through lexical tone, some through stress, some through pitch accent, and some through a combination that resists any single label. Some permit elaborate consonant clusters at syllable edges, while others restrict syllables tightly to a single consonant and vowel. None of these organizational choices makes a system simpler, harder, or less complete than another; each is a coherent solution documented on its own terms. Build the phonotactic and prosodic profile the evidence supports for your specific variety, and resist filling in a system, such as stress, that your sources do not document.

    Practice and Review

    Whole-System Mapping Exercises

    These exercises work from described source situations, extending the inventory-mapping skills from Chapter 30 into the whole-system description you will complete for your own capstone profile.

    Exercise A — Phonotactic Restriction or Small Sample

    Your ledger contains twelve words, none of which end in a stop consonant. Explain what additional evidence you would want before stating "codas cannot be stops" as a rule rather than a tendency.

    Exercise B — Undocumented Prosody

    No source in your ledger discusses whether your capstone variety has lexical tone. Explain how the profile should represent this gap, using the distinction developed in Chapter 30 for undocumented inventory cells.

    Exercise C — Style-Dependent Process

    A sociolinguistic source documents a vowel-deletion process as common in casual speech but rare in careful speech. Describe how you would record this process so that a reader understands it is not obligatory.

    Exercise D — Ranking Priorities

    You have two candidate pronunciation priorities: a rare consonant cluster that occurs in a handful of words, and a connected-speech process that occurs in nearly every sentence. Explain which should rank higher and why.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake

    Error: Assuming every language has the same set of prosodic systems as English and forcing stress notation onto a variety whose prominence system is better described another way.

    Correction: Build the prosodic profile only from the systems your sources document as active, following the same evidence-first approach used for the segmental inventory in Chapter 30.

    Common Mistake

    Error: Treating a small number of consistent examples as sufficient to state an exceptionless phonotactic or prosodic rule.

    Correction: Match the strength of the claim to the size and consistency of the evidence, using rule language only when the source base supports it and tendency language otherwise.

    Common Mistake

    Error: Ranking pronunciation priorities by how unfamiliar or difficult a pattern feels to an English-speaking learner rather than by its role in the target system.

    Correction: Rank priorities by contrast and communicative consequence documented in the sources, not by perceived difficulty.

    Answer Key (click to expand)

    Whole-System Mapping Exercises — Exercise A

    Twelve words is a small sample. Before stating an exceptionless rule, check whether the source grammar itself makes this claim explicitly, look for a larger wordlist or corpus that could confirm or contradict the pattern, and consider whether the twelve words share another property, such as all being monosyllabic or all belonging to one word class, that might explain the pattern without it being a general phonotactic restriction. Absent that additional evidence, state the pattern as an observed tendency in the available data rather than a confirmed rule.

    Whole-System Mapping Exercise B

    Represent tone as undocumented rather than absent, following the same three-way distinction used for consonant and vowel inventory gaps in Chapter 30. State in the profile that no source in the ledger addresses tone, list it as an open question, and avoid either claiming the variety has tone or claiming it lacks tone without evidence.

    Whole-System Mapping Exercise C

    Record the process with its environment and note explicitly that it is style-dependent, citing the sociolinguistic source, and state that the careful-speech form remains the expected pronunciation outside casual contexts. Avoid presenting the reduced form as the single expected pronunciation.

    Whole-System Mapping Exercise D

    The connected-speech process that occurs in nearly every sentence should rank higher, because its frequency gives it far greater communicative consequence than a rare cluster confined to a handful of words, even though the rare cluster may feel more unusual or memorable to a new learner.

    Chapter Review — Part A

    1. A phonotactic profile should state whether onsets and codas are obligatory or optional, the maximum number of consonants each may contain, which consonants may combine there, and whether any restrictions are position-specific. 2. Some varieties organize prominence through lexical tone, pitch accent, or other systems instead of or alongside stress, and assuming universal word-level stress would misrepresent varieties where it is not the active or primary system, contrary to the cross-linguistic comparison policy from Chapter 13.

    Chapter Review — Part B

    1. Leaving a process unspecified prevents a reader from knowing whether the careful, unreduced form remains acceptable, so status labeling directly supports intelligibility and learner agency. 2. Regional, social, stylistic, generational, contextual, conservative-or-innovative, and speaker-specific variation.

    Chapter Review — Part C

    1. A productive rule applies predictably with only systematic or listed exceptions and is justified by a broad, consistent evidence base or an explicit source statement; a descriptive tendency occurs frequently but has unexplained exceptions or is hedged by the source, and is justified by a smaller or less consistent evidence base. 2. Priorities should be ranked by contrast and communicative consequence, such as how many meaning distinctions a pattern protects or how frequently a process occurs, rather than by how unfamiliar or difficult the pattern seems to an outside observer.

    1. Chapter 19 first taught onset, nucleus, and coda using short, controlled datasets designed to isolate syllable structure from other systems; this chapter applies the identical framework at the scale of a full variety's phonotactic profile, requiring the same reasoning about legal and illegal sequences to be sustained across the entire documented wordlist rather than a handful of worked examples. 2.

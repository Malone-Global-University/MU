Mapping Consonant and Vowel Inventories

Turning sourced evidence into a documented, evidence-based segment inventory

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Distinguish a phoneme inventory from a phone inventory.
  2. Organize consonants by airstream, phonation, place, and manner.
  3. Organize vowels by quality and relevant additional dimensions.
  4. Document major allophones for a chosen variety.
  5. Map orthographic forms to phonological and phonetic representations.
  6. Identify gaps, asymmetries, and uncertain analyses without inventing evidence to fill them.
  7. Create accessible, well-labeled inventory tables for a phonetic profile.

An Observable Problem

Chapter 29 left you with three things: a defined language variety, an evaluated set of sources, and a short list of answerable questions. None of that is an inventory yet. Suppose your ledger now contains a scholarly grammar that lists twenty-two consonant phonemes, a peer-reviewed article that mentions a nasal place assimilation process the grammar never discusses, and a pronunciation dictionary that uses a slightly different symbol for one of the fricatives than the grammar does. You cannot simply copy the grammar's chart into your profile and call the inventory finished.

Building an inventory means deciding, segment by segment, what counts as a phoneme, what counts as a conditioned variant of that phoneme, how the sources' differing symbols relate to one another, and how the variety's writing system, if it has one, maps onto the sounds you have documented. Every one of those decisions has to be traceable back to the evidence in your ledger, not to intuition about how the chart "should" look.

This chapter asks three questions:

  1. What is the difference between listing every phone a source reports and listing the phonemes that actually organize the sound system?
  2. How does an analyst arrange consonants and vowels into charts that reflect the evidence rather than a memorized template?
  3. What should a learner do when the evidence is incomplete, inconsistent, or genuinely silent on a point?

Key Terms

  • Phoneme inventory: the set of contrastive sound categories that organize a variety's sound system, established through minimal pairs, near-minimal pairs, and distributional evidence.
  • Phone inventory: the set of phonetically distinct sounds a source reports occurring in the variety, including conditioned variants that do not themselves contrast.
  • Symbol reconciliation: the process of comparing two or more sources' symbol choices for what may be the same sound and deciding whether the difference is notational or substantive.
  • Orthographic correspondence: a documented relationship between a written form in the variety's script and the phonological or phonetic representation it corresponds to.
  • Inventory gap: a place in a chart where a phone or phoneme might be logically expected but the available evidence does not confirm it exists.
  • Inventory asymmetry: a documented, evidence-supported imbalance in a sound system, such as a place or manner that is well populated in one part of the chart and sparse in another.
  • Major allophone: a conditioned variant of a phoneme common or systematic enough to be worth recording in a profile, as distinct from a rare or idiosyncratic variant.
Building the Inventory

Phoneme Inventory Versus Phone Inventory

Purpose: apply the phone-phoneme-allophone distinction from Chapter 5 to the scale of a complete inventory, so that a profile does not simply list every symbol a source happens to use.

A phone inventory is a catalogue of the phonetically distinct sounds a source reports for a variety, including sounds that never contrast with one another because one always occurs where the other cannot. A phoneme inventory is narrower: it is the set of sound categories that actually distinguish meaning in the variety, established through minimal pairs, near-minimal pairs, and evidence about where sounds can and cannot occur. A responsible profile keeps both, but keeps them separate and clearly labeled, because collapsing them misrepresents the system in two different directions.

Listing only phones without identifying which ones contrast overstates the system's complexity and hides its organizing structure. Listing only phonemes and discarding phonetic detail understates what a learner will actually hear, since predictable variants still shape pronunciation even when they carry no contrastive weight. The phoneme inventory belongs in the profile's main consonant and vowel charts; the phone-level detail that does not rise to phonemic status belongs in the allophone documentation covered later in this chapter.

Concept Check — A Source's Symbol Count Is Not the Phoneme Count

A source may use more IPA symbols in its transcriptions than it recognizes as separate phonemes, because narrow transcriptions record conditioned detail. Before copying a symbol into your phoneme chart, confirm that the source, or your own comparison of its examples, actually treats that symbol as contrastive rather than as a transcribed variant of a symbol already in the chart.

Common Mistake

Error: Building the phoneme chart by collecting every distinct IPA symbol that appears anywhere in a source's transcriptions.

Correction: Confirm contrastive status for each symbol before placing it in the phoneme chart, using the source's own analysis where it is explicit and minimal-pair evidence where it is not.

Organizing the Consonant Chart

Purpose: apply the airstream, phonation, place, and manner categories developed in Chapters 6 through 13 to build a chart from sourced evidence rather than from a memorized template of the official IPA consonant chart.

Organize the consonant inventory along the same four dimensions used throughout Part II: airstream mechanism, phonation, place of articulation, and manner of articulation. For most varieties, the pulmonic egressive airstream will account for the entire inventory, and non-pulmonic consonants, if any are documented, should be listed separately and identified explicitly as such, following the treatment in Chapter 13.

Arrange places of articulation from front to back and manners from most to least constricted, matching the organization of the official IPA chart taught in Chapters 3 and 7 through 8. Where a source documents a voicing or phonation contrast at a given place and manner, show both members; where the evidence supports only one member, do not add a symmetrical partner merely because the chart position looks empty. Section 30.6 addresses how to handle a chart cell your evidence does not fill.

Evidence Note — Symbol Reconciliation

When two credible sources use different symbols for what appears to be the same sound, first check whether the difference reflects a notational convention, such as one source using a narrower or coarser symbol than the other for the same category, before concluding the sources disagree substantively. Chapter 9's tie-bar discussion and Chapter 25's diacritic-selection guidance both apply here: state which convention your profile adopts and note the alternative in your source ledger.

Organizing the Vowel Chart

Purpose: apply the vowel-space literacy from Chapters 14 through 18 to construct a sourced vowel inventory, including any length, nasalization, rhoticity, or harmony patterns the evidence supports.

Plot the vowel inventory using height, backness, and rounding as the primary dimensions, following the quadrilateral organization from Chapter 15. Where sources report diphthongs or vowel sequences, list them separately from the monophthong inventory, consistent with the monophthong-versus-dynamic-vowel distinction from Chapters 16 and 17. Where a source documents length, nasalization, rhoticity, or vowel harmony as phonologically active in the variety, add a labeled dimension to the chart rather than folding it silently into the basic quality symbols, following the practice established in Chapter 18.

Documenting Major Allophones

Purpose: extend the conditioned-variation concepts from Chapter 5 and the connected-speech processes from Chapter 23 into a documented allophone list that supports, rather than duplicates, the phoneme chart.

A major allophone is a conditioned variant common or systematic enough that a learner benefits from knowing it, such as a predictable nasal place assimilation, a lateral that is realized differently depending on syllable position, or a stop that is regularly unreleased in a specific context. Minor, rare, or speaker-specific variants documented in only one narrow study do not need to appear in an introductory profile; note them in your source ledger as a limitation instead of listing every variant a single source happens to mention.

For each major allophone, record the phoneme it belongs to, the conditioning environment, the variant's phonetic realization, and the source that documents the pattern. This is the same information structure the source and example ledger in Chapter 29 already tracks; the allophone list is simply the learner-facing summary of that evidence for the segments where conditioned variation is well documented.

Common Mistake

Error: Listing an allophone without stating the environment that conditions it, leaving the reader unable to predict when the variant occurs.

Correction: State the conditioning environment explicitly, using the same environment-plus-rule format practiced in Chapter 5's distribution tables.

Mapping Orthography to Sound

Purpose: apply the sound-spelling separation from Chapter 2 to the scale of an entire writing system, documenting the relationship between the variety's script and the inventory built in this chapter.

For a variety with an established writing system, document how each significant grapheme or grapheme sequence relates to the phoneme inventory. Some correspondences will be highly regular; others will depend on position, adjacent letters, or lexical exceptions that the source material identifies. State the correspondence at the level of detail the evidence supports, and mark cases where the same written form can represent more than one sound, or the same sound can be written more than one way, consistent with the one-to-many and many-to-one relationships introduced in Chapter 2.

Spelling Trap — Not Every Letter Is a Phoneme

A writing system's letter count is not evidence about the phoneme count. Some letters may be silent in certain positions, some may combine to represent a single phoneme, and some phonemes may have no dedicated letter at all. Build the orthographic correspondence table from the sound side outward, listing how each established phoneme is spelled, rather than assuming each letter maps to exactly one sound.

Where a variety uses more than one conventional romanization or transliteration system, identify which system your profile follows and note that alternative systems exist, following the alternative-conventions policy that has applied to IPA notation since Chapter 3.

Gaps, Asymmetries, and Uncertain Analyses

Purpose: extend the evidence-before-confidence principle from the Master Instructions and the acoustic-evidence discipline of Chapter 28 to the specific problem of an incomplete or lopsided chart.

Real sound systems are rarely symmetrical, and real evidence is rarely complete. A chart cell may be empty because the variety genuinely lacks that sound, because no source in your ledger happens to address it, or because sources disagree about whether it belongs. These three situations require different treatment, and a careful profile distinguishes them rather than presenting every empty cell the same way.

1
Confirmed absence
A source explicitly states the sound does not occur, or its absence follows from the system's own documented structure. Mark the cell as confirmed empty.
2
Undocumented gap
No source addresses the cell either way. Mark it as unconfirmed and list it as an open question rather than guessing.
3
Disputed cell
Sources disagree about whether the sound is phonemic, marginal, or absent. Apply the Chapter 29 procedure for conflicting analyses and state the adopted position with its reasoning.

An asymmetry, such as a place of articulation that has a full set of manners while a neighboring place has only one or two, is worth noting explicitly when the evidence supports it, since asymmetries are a normal and informative feature of real sound systems rather than a sign of researcher error. Chapter 32's boundaries already state that a carefully bounded profile is preferable to an overconfident comprehensive claim; this section is where that boundary becomes a concrete chart-building habit.

Common Mistake

Error: Filling an apparently symmetrical gap in the chart with a sound that seems likely, on the assumption that the system "should" be balanced.

Correction: Leave an undocumented gap marked as unconfirmed, and treat chart symmetry as a pattern to notice, not a requirement to enforce.

Building Accessible Inventory Tables

Purpose: connect the technical content of this chapter to the accessibility and table standards that govern every chapter in this project, so the finished inventory is usable by every reader of the capstone profile.

An inventory table must be readable independently of color, must include semantic headers, must remain usable on a narrow screen, and must avoid implying more precision than the underlying evidence supports. A cell showing a symbol without any confidence or source note is not yet ready for the profile; every entry in a capstone consonant or vowel chart should be traceable back to the source ledger built in Chapter 29.

  • State the reference variety once, clearly, above every chart in the profile.
  • Use one row or column structure consistently across the consonant chart, the vowel chart, and the allophone list.
  • Include a symbol and convention key alongside the charts, listing any notation that departs from the book's default IPA conventions.
  • Mark confirmed absences, undocumented gaps, and disputed cells using a small, consistent, explained notation rather than leaving them visually identical to filled cells.
  • Keep source citations attached to the chart, either inline or through a clearly linked reference to the source ledger.

    • Inventories of Different Shapes and Sizes

    Across Languages — Inventory Size Is Not a Ranking

    Sourced inventories vary enormously in size and shape. A variety may have a compact consonant inventory and an elaborate vowel system, or the reverse; a variety may show heavy use of secondary articulations while lacking many of the fricative places another variety fills completely. None of these patterns indicates that one system is more developed, more logical, or more difficult than another, consistent with the cross-linguistic comparison policy established in Chapter 13 and reaffirmed throughout Part III. Describe the documented shape of the system you are mapping, and resist the temptation to treat any particular chart shape, including a chart resembling English, as the expected default.

    Practice and Review

    Inventory-Mapping Exercises

    These exercises work from described source situations, extending the planning skills from Chapter 29 into the concrete task of chart construction you will perform for your own capstone variety.

    Exercise A — Phone Inventory or Phoneme Inventory

    A source's narrow transcriptions include three phonetically distinct lateral variants, but its own phonological discussion states that only one lateral phoneme exists. Explain how your consonant chart should represent this situation and where the other two variants belong.

    Exercise B — Reconciling Symbols

    A grammar transcribes a fricative with one IPA symbol; a dictionary for the same variety uses a different but phonetically adjacent symbol for what appears to be the same sound in the same words. Describe the steps you would take before deciding whether this is a notational difference or a genuine disagreement.

    Exercise C — Handling an Undocumented Cell

    Your consonant chart has an empty cell at a place and manner combination that would complete an otherwise regular pattern, but no source in your ledger addresses it. Explain how you would represent this cell and what you would record about it.

    Exercise D — Orthographic Correspondence

    A single letter in the variety's writing system represents two different phonemes depending on the following letter, according to your source. Describe how you would document this in the orthographic correspondence table.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake

    Error: Treating the official IPA chart's full layout as a template to fill in, rather than building the chart from the evidence a specific variety actually supports.

    Correction: Start from sourced evidence and let the chart take the shape the evidence supports, including empty or asymmetrical regions.

    Common Mistake

    Error: Recording an allophone without its conditioning environment, or recording every variant a single narrow study mentions regardless of how systematic it is.

    Correction: State the environment for every recorded allophone, and reserve the allophone list for patterns well documented enough to count as major.

    Common Mistake

    Error: Assuming every letter in the variety's writing system corresponds to exactly one phoneme.

    Correction: Build the orthographic correspondence table from the confirmed phoneme inventory outward, documenting one-to-many and many-to-one relationships explicitly.

    Answer Key (click to expand)

    Inventory-Mapping Exercises — Exercise A

    The consonant chart should show a single lateral phoneme, since the source's own phonological analysis confirms only one contrastive lateral category. The three phonetically distinct variants belong in the allophone list, each with the environment that conditions it, rather than appearing as separate entries in the phoneme chart.

    Inventory-Mapping Exercise B

    First check whether the two symbols represent the same phonetic category under different notational conventions, such as one source using a broader symbol where the other uses a narrower one for the same range of realizations, by comparing how each source defines and uses the symbol elsewhere. If the symbols still appear to represent phonetically distinct sounds after that check, treat this as a genuine disagreement and apply the Chapter 29 procedure: investigate a possible explanation, such as differing data or differing analytical traditions, and state which position the profile adopts.

    Inventory-Mapping Exercise C

    Mark the cell as an undocumented gap rather than filling it with an assumed sound. Record in the source ledger that no available source addresses this specific combination, and list it as an open question for further sourcing rather than presenting the chart as if the cell had been confirmed empty or confirmed filled.

    Inventory-Mapping Exercise D

    Document the letter as a one-to-many correspondence, listing both phonemes it can represent, stating the conditioning environment that determines which phoneme applies, such as the identity of the following letter, and citing the source that establishes the pattern.

  2. Describe the core principles that govern how IPA symbols relate to speech sounds.
  3. Recognize why ordinary spelling is an unreliable guide to pronunciation in many languages.

  6. Read simple IPA transcriptions of familiar words from several languages.

The Opening Problem: One Letter, Many Sounds

Begin with a simple question: how do you pronounce the letter a?

If you are thinking about English, your instinct is probably to say "well, it depends." And you are right. The letter a represents several different sounds in English, depending on the word:

WordSound of the letter aApproximate description
catshort, front vowelas in the word most English speakers would call "flat a"
fatherlong, open vowelfurther back in the mouth, lower jaw drops more
cakegliding vowel (diphthong)starts mid and moves upward
aboutreduced, neutral vowelthe unstressed "uh" sound
callback, rounded vowelthe lips round slightly

Five different sounds, one letter. Now expand the problem to other languages. In Spanish, the letter j represents a sound that does not exist in English — a friction-like sound made at the back of the throat (jamón, joven). In French, the letter r is produced quite differently from English r: many French speakers use a uvular trill or fricative made at the very back of the mouth. In German, the letters ch together can represent two distinct sounds depending on the surrounding vowels — neither of which is the English ch in "church."

The pattern is clear: ordinary spelling is not a reliable guide to pronunciation. Letters carry historical, cultural, and conventional baggage that obscures the actual sounds of speech. A system designed to study and represent speech accurately cannot be built on spelling.

That is why the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA) exists. Rather than representing the conventional spellings of any particular language, the IPA represents speech sounds directly — one symbol for one sound, consistently, across all human languages. It is the standard tool of phonetics, the scientific study of the sounds of human speech.

Why this matters for you. Whether you are learning a new language, studying linguistics, teaching pronunciation, working as a speech-language pathologist, or simply curious about how language works, the IPA gives you a shared notation for talking precisely about sounds. Once you can read and write IPA, you can look up the pronunciation of any word in almost any language, describe sounds you hear but cannot spell, and compare speech across languages without confusion.

This chapter introduces what the IPA is, where it came from, how it is organized, and what it can and cannot do. Detailed instruction in consonant and vowel symbols follows in later chapters. Here, your goal is to understand the system and become comfortable with the idea of reading speech rather than spelling.

Section 1

Why the IPA Was Created

The problem of inconsistent spelling

For most of human history, people who needed to describe pronunciation in writing relied on the spelling systems of their own language. A French scholar describing Arabic sounds would attempt to express them using French spelling conventions. An English linguist describing Japanese pronunciation would use English letters in ways that made sense to English readers. The result was chaos: the same sound might be spelled a dozen different ways depending on the nationality of the person doing the writing, and a pronunciation guide written for French speakers was often useless to German speakers.

The problem was especially acute for language teachers. In the nineteenth century, European educators began to take language teaching seriously as a discipline. They recognized that their students needed to hear and produce accurate pronunciation, not just translate written texts. But without a shared system for representing sounds, every textbook invented its own respelling conventions, and learners who moved from one book to another had to relearn the notation from scratch.

The birth of the International Phonetic Association

In 1886, a group of language teachers in France, led by French phonetician Paul Passy, founded what would become the International Phonetic Association (also known by its French initials, l'Association phonétique internationale or API). Their goal was to develop a universal notation system that could represent the sounds of any language using a consistent, learner-friendly set of symbols.

The key ideas behind the new alphabet were simple but powerful:

  • Each distinct speech sound should have its own dedicated symbol.
  • Symbols should be as familiar as possible — preferably based on the Latin alphabet already used in European languages — while still being unambiguous.
  • The same symbol should always represent the same sound, regardless of which language is being transcribed.

The first version of what we now call the International Phonetic Alphabet was published in 1888 in a small journal called Le Maître Phonétique (The Phonetic Teacher). This makes the IPA one of the oldest and most enduring scientific notation systems still in use.

A brief timeline

  • 1886Paul Passy and colleagues found the International Phonetic Association in France.
  • 1888The first version of the IPA is published in Le Maître Phonétique.
  • 1900s–1940sThe IPA is refined through decades of use in linguistic fieldwork, language teaching, and dictionary production. Symbols are added for sounds that the original version did not cover.
  • 1949A substantial revision consolidates and reorganizes the chart. The IPA is now widely used by linguists worldwide.
  • 1989A major revision at the Kiel Convention introduces several new symbols and diacritics, particularly for sounds in non-European languages.
  • 1993The IPA chart published after the Kiel Convention is updated and standardized.
  • 2005The most recent substantive revision to the IPA chart is approved, refining several symbols and organization details.
  • 2015Wording and layout of the official chart are updated. The alphabet itself does not change.
  • TodayThe official IPA chart is maintained by the International Phonetic Association. It is updated only when the Association approves a substantive revision to the alphabet.
Note on the Chart's Date

The official IPA chart sometimes carries a year in its header that reflects when it was reprinted or reformatted, not necessarily when the alphabet was revised. A chart issued in a recent year does not automatically mean that the symbols themselves have changed. Always check whether the Association has approved a substantive revision before assuming the alphabet is different from a previous edition. The most recent substantive revision of the IPA, as of the completion of this textbook, was in 2005.

Why linguists and teachers needed it

The IPA solved several practical problems at once:

For language researchers. Linguists documenting unfamiliar or endangered languages needed a way to write down what they heard without imposing their own language's spelling conventions. The IPA gave fieldworkers a neutral notation system that could record sounds from Mandarin, Zulu, Arabic, or any other language with equal precision.

For language teachers. Teachers of French, German, English, or any other language could now show their students the exact sounds to produce, using a shared notation rather than misleading approximations. The statement "pronounce the r in French like the IPA symbol [ʀ] or [ʁ]" is far more useful than "it's kind of like gargling."

For dictionary makers. Before the IPA, dictionaries had to invent ad hoc respelling systems — different systems in different dictionaries, different countries, and different editions. The IPA (or versions adapted from it) made pronunciation guidance in dictionaries far more consistent and transparent.

For speech-language pathologists. Clinicians working with people who have speech or language disorders needed precise ways to record and compare how their patients produced sounds before and after treatment. The IPA offered a clinical standard.

For singers and actors. A singer performing opera in Italian, German, and French needed to know the exact pronunciation expected in each language. A standardized phonetic notation meant that pronunciation coaches and performers could communicate precisely.

Section 2

Principles of the IPA

One symbol, one sound

The foundational principle of the IPA is elegantly simple: one symbol represents one speech sound, and one sound is represented by one symbol as consistently as possible. This one-to-one relationship is exactly what ordinary spelling lacks.

In English spelling, the sound at the beginning of ship, sugar, chef, machine, and social is the same sound — a hushing fricative made with the tongue blade near the roof of the mouth. But English spelling represents it five different ways. The IPA uses a single symbol for all five: ʃ. Wherever this sound occurs — in English, French, German, Russian, Japanese, or any other language — the same symbol appears.

Conversely, English spelling uses the same two letters th for two different sounds: the voiced sound at the beginning of the, this, and there, and the voiceless sound at the beginning of thin, three, and thought. The IPA uses two different symbols: ð for the voiced sound and θ for the voiceless one.

Symbols describe sounds, not spelling

IPA symbols are defined by the physical properties of the sounds they represent — where in the mouth they are made, how airflow is restricted, whether the vocal folds are vibrating — not by the spelling conventions of any language. This means that when you see an IPA symbol, you are reading a description of speech, not a letter in a particular alphabet.

This distinction is worth pausing on. When you see the letter p in English, you bring to it all the assumptions of English spelling: that it represents the "p sound" in English, that it does not represent any foreign sounds, that it behaves the same way as other English letters. When you see the IPA symbol [p], you set all of that aside. The symbol means: a sound made by closing both lips completely, building up air pressure, and releasing it with a small burst — voiceless, pulmonic, bilabial plosive. That description applies to the relevant sound in English, Spanish, Mandarin, Swahili, or any other language that has a sound of that type.

Independent of any particular language

The IPA belongs to no single language. It was designed to be a universal notation system, and it has been tested on languages from every major language family in the world. No language is at the center; no language's sound system is treated as the default.

This has an important implication: an IPA symbol does not mean "the English sound spelled ___." It means a phonetic description. English examples are useful as orientation points for English-speaking learners, but they are not definitions. The symbol [r] does not mean "the English r." It refers to a trill — a sound produced by a rapidly vibrating articulator — which many languages have and which English does not use phonemically. The sound English speakers think of as "the r sound" is actually transcribed differently: typically [ɹ], an approximant.

A living, maintained system

Unlike ordinary alphabets, which change slowly through cultural drift, the IPA is formally maintained by the International Phonetic Association. When linguists discover that a sound found in a human language cannot be represented with the existing symbols, they can propose a new symbol, which the Association then evaluates and potentially adds to the official chart. This means the IPA has grown over time to represent sounds that the original creators had not encountered — including clicks found in languages like Zulu and Xhosa, and complex tonal systems found in many languages across Africa and Asia.

This also means that the IPA has different versions corresponding to different periods of its history. This textbook uses the current official chart as the authoritative reference. When older transcriptions or alternative conventions appear in other sources, this book will note the difference.

Section 3

IPA versus Ordinary Spelling

English: a spelling system with a complicated history

English spelling is notoriously inconsistent. This is not because English speakers are careless; it is because English spelling reflects the history of the language rather than its current pronunciation. English has borrowed words from French, Latin, Greek, Norse, Arabic, and many other languages, often preserving something like the original spelling. Pronunciation has shifted enormously over the centuries — the vowels in words like time, name, and house sounded very different in the fourteenth century — but spelling was standardized before those shifts were complete. The result is a writing system that is only loosely connected to the sounds it represents.

Consider the spelling ough. In English, this single letter sequence represents at least six different pronunciations depending on the word:

This is one of the most frequently cited examples of English spelling irregularity — and a strong argument for why phonetic notation is useful. The IPA symbols in the third column tell you exactly what the vowel sounds like; the spelling tells you almost nothing.

Contrasting spelling systems across languages

Spelling irregularity is not a universal feature of all writing systems. Languages vary considerably in how closely their spelling matches their pronunciation.

Because writing systems differ so profoundly — some are alphabetic, some syllabic, some logographic, and some leave out vowels entirely — no single writing system can serve as a universal guide to pronunciation across languages. The IPA can, because it was designed specifically for that purpose.

Spelling Trap

One of the most important habits to build as a phonetics student is separating what you see from what you hear. English-speaking learners especially tend to "see" sounds that are not there (or miss sounds that are), because they have spent years treating spelling as a guide to pronunciation. When you read IPA, make a conscious effort to look at the symbol, not the word it came from. The symbol [ŋ] does not contain an n followed by a g sound — it represents one single nasal sound, made at the back of the mouth, the way most speakers end the word sing.

Section 4

Organization of the IPA Chart

The official IPA chart is a single document, maintained and periodically updated by the International Phonetic Association. It organizes every approved symbol into a logical structure that reflects how sounds are produced and what phonetic categories they belong to. This section introduces the major regions of the chart. Each region will be studied in depth in later chapters.

Major Regions of the IPA Chart

1. Pulmonic Consonants

The largest section of the chart. Pulmonic consonants are sounds made by pushing air out of the lungs (the pulmonic airstream). They are organized in a grid: rows represent manners of articulation (how air is restricted — stopped, fricative, nasal, etc.), and columns represent places of articulation (where in the vocal tract the restriction occurs — lips, teeth, alveolar ridge, palate, velum, etc.). This is where most of the consonants of most of the world's languages appear.

2. Non-Pulmonic Consonants

A smaller section for consonants that use a different airstream mechanism. This includes clicks (found in Zulu, Xhosa, and other languages), ejectives (found in Georgian, Hausa, and many other languages), and implosives (found in Sindhi, Hausa, and others). These sounds do not rely on the pulmonic airstream used by most consonants.

3. Vowels

Vowels are displayed on a quadrilateral (a four-sided figure) representing the acoustic and articulatory space in which vowels are produced. The chart shows vowels arranged by height (how open or closed the mouth is) and backness (how far forward or back the tongue is). Symbols appear in pairs: the left symbol is unrounded (no lip rounding), and the right symbol is rounded (with lip rounding).

4. Other Symbols

A section for sounds that do not fit neatly into the pulmonic consonant grid — including certain affricates, sounds with complex simultaneous articulations, and other special segments.

5. Diacritics

Small marks that are added to base symbols to indicate finer phonetic detail. For example, a diacritic can show that a consonant is aspirated (produced with a puff of air), nasalized, dental rather than alveolar, or produced with certain voice qualities. Diacritics greatly expand the descriptive power of the IPA without multiplying the number of base symbols endlessly.

6. Suprasegmentals

Symbols for features that apply to stretches of speech rather than individual sounds. Suprasegmentals include markers for primary and secondary stress, syllable boundaries, length, linking between words, tone, and the direction of pitch movement. A language like Mandarin, where pitch distinguishes the meaning of words, requires tone notation. A language like English uses stress and intonation to convey emphasis and meaning at the sentence level.

7. Tones and Word Accents

Dedicated symbols for tone systems (common in African and many Asian languages) and pitch accent systems. These allow transcription of complex tonal contrasts without resorting to language-specific conventions.

How the consonant grid works

The pulmonic consonant section of the chart is a grid, and understanding its logic makes the chart much easier to navigate than it would be if you tried to memorize symbols in isolation.

Each row of the grid represents a different manner of articulation — the way in which airflow is restricted or modified to produce the sound. Is the airflow completely stopped and then released? (Those are plosives.) Is it forced through a narrow channel, creating turbulence and friction? (Those are fricatives.) Is it allowed to flow freely, through either the mouth or the nose? (Those are approximants or nasals.)

Each column represents a different place of articulation — the location in the vocal tract where the restriction occurs. Both lips together? Bilabial. Upper teeth touching the lower lip? Labiodental. The tongue touching the alveolar ridge (the bony bump behind your upper front teeth)? Alveolar. The tongue touching the velum (the soft palate at the back of the mouth)? Velar.

Within each cell, symbols typically appear in pairs: the left symbol is voiceless (vocal folds apart, not vibrating) and the right symbol is voiced (vocal folds together and vibrating). A shaded cell means that the combination of place and manner at that position is considered impossible or non-occurring — you simply cannot make that sound.

Key Terminology: Consonant Description

When phoneticians describe a consonant, they typically use a three-part label in this order: voicing — place — manner. The first consonant in the English word book is described as a voiceless bilabial plosive. "Voiceless" means the vocal folds do not vibrate. "Bilabial" means both lips come together. "Plosive" means airflow is completely stopped and then released. You will learn to use this system confidently in Chapters 7 and 8.

The vowel quadrilateral

The vowel section of the chart is organized around a quadrilateral — a diagram that represents, approximately, the space in which vowels are produced. The top of the diagram represents high vowels (tongue high in the mouth), the bottom represents low or open vowels (tongue low, mouth open), the left represents front vowels (tongue pushed toward the front of the mouth), and the right represents back vowels (tongue pulled toward the back of the mouth).

This quadrilateral is not a perfect anatomical map of the inside of someone's mouth. It is a schematic representation of acoustic space — the range of auditory qualities that vowels can have — as well as an approximate guide to where the tongue is. Phoneticians have developed a set of cardinal vowels, a reference system of precisely defined vowel qualities, that anchor the corners and edges of this space. The cardinal vowels allow phoneticians to describe where a language's vowels fall on this map, even if the language's vowels do not correspond exactly to any cardinal vowel position.

The vowel quadrilateral and the cardinal vowel system are introduced in Chapters 14 and 15. For now, the important thing to understand is that the vowel section of the IPA chart is organized spatially, not randomly, and that the organization reflects real phonetic relationships among vowel sounds.

Section 5

Unicode and Digital IPA

What Unicode is

Unicode is a universal character encoding standard that assigns a unique numerical code to every character used in human writing — including all ordinary letters, digits, punctuation marks, and special symbols like those in the IPA. When you see a correctly displayed IPA symbol on a screen or in a document, it exists because someone typed or stored its Unicode code point, and your device's software and fonts know how to display it.

Before Unicode, different software systems used different, incompatible character encodings. A document with IPA symbols created on one system might display as gibberish on another. Unicode solved this by creating one global standard. As a result, IPA symbols typed in a word processor, a website, a linguistics database, or a language-learning app all use the same underlying codes and will display consistently across devices — provided that an appropriate font is installed.

Fonts and rendering

Not all fonts include IPA symbols. A font designed primarily for English text may include only the basic Latin alphabet and common punctuation, without the specialized symbols that the IPA requires. When a document includes an IPA symbol that the current font does not contain, your software will typically either display a fallback character, a blank box, or a symbol from a different font entirely.

For phonetics work, use fonts that are designed with reliable IPA coverage, such as:

  • Noto Sans / Noto Serif — Google's open-source family, designed explicitly to cover all Unicode characters.
  • DejaVu Sans — A free font with broad Unicode coverage, commonly used in linguistics software.
  • Charis SIL, Doulos SIL, Gentium — Fonts developed by SIL International specifically for linguistic work, including all IPA symbols.
  • Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode — Widely installed system fonts with broad (though not complete) Unicode coverage.

Typing IPA

Typing IPA correctly requires either a specialized keyboard input method or a tool that lets you insert symbols by name or code. Several options are widely used:

  • Online IPA keyboards (such as those at ipa.typeit.org or similar sites) allow you to click on symbols and copy the result.
  • Software keyboard layouts for linguists, such as those distributed by SIL International, allow you to type IPA by holding modifier keys.
  • Unicode character insertion in word processors: many programs allow you to insert a symbol by typing its Unicode code point.
  • Copy-paste from IPA resources: if you are working from a reference that already uses proper Unicode IPA, you can copy symbols directly into your document.

Common typing mistakes

Several IPA symbols look similar to ordinary Latin letters but are distinct Unicode characters with different meanings. Substituting an ordinary letter for an IPA symbol creates an error, even if it looks acceptable on screen — the underlying code is wrong, and the distinction matters for search, data analysis, and professional communication.

IPA SymbolUnicode nameCommon mistakeWhy it matters

Common Mistake

Error: Writing "sh", "zh", "ny", "ng", or "oo" as substitutes for IPA symbols.

Correction: These informal respellings are sometimes used as temporary aids for beginners, but they are not IPA and should never be labeled as such. Multi-letter substitutions like "sh" represent two separate characters, not one phonetic symbol. Use proper Unicode IPA characters from the start. If you use a respelling as a learning aid, label it explicitly as approximate and temporary.

Why this matters for your work

In this course, all IPA examples use correct Unicode characters. When you submit work with IPA symbols, your instructor will expect correct Unicode characters as well. A document with "sh" in place of ʃ, or an ordinary a in place of ɑ, is technically inaccurate, just as writing a wrong symbol would be in any scientific notation. Build the habit of using proper characters from the start.

Section 6

Where the IPA Is Used

The IPA has found applications in almost every field that involves understanding, describing, or working with human speech. The following overview gives a sense of how broad that reach is.

FieldHow the IPA is used
LinguisticsPhoneticians, phonologists, and fieldworkers use IPA to document and analyze the sound systems of all human languages, including endangered and unwritten languages.
Language learningLearners use IPA to understand exactly how target-language sounds are produced, especially sounds that do not exist in their first language.
Language teachingTeachers use IPA to communicate pronunciation clearly and systematically, without relying on unreliable approximations.
LexicographyDictionary editors use IPA (or adaptations of it) to show pronunciation entries. Major dictionaries worldwide use IPA-based transcription systems.
Speech-language pathologyClinicians use IPA to transcribe how patients produce sounds — for diagnosis, treatment planning, and tracking progress over time.
Singing and operaSingers and coaches use IPA to ensure correct pronunciation of texts in multiple languages, particularly in classical vocal music.
Acting and broadcastingActors and broadcasters use IPA to master pronunciation for roles, dialects, accents, and scripted speech in multiple languages.
Forensic linguisticsForensic experts use phonetic transcription and analysis to compare recorded speech samples in legal investigations.
Speech technologyEngineers building automatic speech recognition (ASR) and text-to-speech (TTS) systems use phonetic representations — often derived from IPA — as a backend for modeling speech.
Historical linguisticsHistorical linguists use IPA to reconstruct the sounds of ancient languages and trace sound changes across time.
Language documentationFieldworkers working with indigenous and endangered languages use IPA to create phonological analyses and archival records.
Translation and localizationTranslation teams working on voice-based products or dubbed films use phonetic transcription to align spoken words across languages.
Computational linguistics and AIResearchers building multilingual language models, pronunciation dictionaries, and speech synthesis systems use IPA as a shared phonetic representation.

One useful way to think about the IPA is as a lingua franca for speech: just as English or Esperanto might serve as a shared language for people with different mother tongues, the IPA serves as a shared notation for talking about sounds across different language backgrounds, disciplines, and purposes.

Section 7

Reading Your First IPA Examples

The following examples introduce IPA transcription in the simplest possible way: familiar words from several languages, shown alongside their IPA representation. You are not expected to memorize the symbols at this stage. The goal is to become familiar with what IPA transcription looks like and to notice the relationship (and the disconnect) between spelling and sound.

A few conventions to notice as you read through these examples:

  • Square brackets [ ] enclose a phonetic transcription — a record of how a sound was actually produced in a particular instance.
  • Slashes / / enclose a phonemic transcription — an abstract representation of the sound categories of a language. The difference between these two notations is fundamental, and Chapter 5 is devoted to explaining it. For now, just notice that both exist and are used differently.
  • The mark ˈ placed before a syllable indicates that syllable carries primary stress — it is pronounced with more force than the others.

Across Languages

Notice that the same IPA symbols appear across multiple languages. The symbol ʁ appears in both French (bonjour) and may appear in German and other languages that have a uvular consonant in that position. The symbol tʃ appears in Italian (ciao) and Swahili (jambo's related consonant) and English (church). This cross-linguistic recurrence is exactly what the IPA was designed for: the same symbol, the same sound, regardless of language.

Section 8

Preparing for the Rest of the Book

This chapter has introduced the IPA at the level of concepts and orientation. The remaining chapters develop your ability to use the IPA as a practical analytical tool. Here is a preview of the road ahead.

Part I (Chapters 1–5) continues to build your analytical foundations. Chapter 4 introduces the anatomy and physiology of the vocal tract — the biological machinery that produces speech. Chapter 5 introduces the distinction between phones (individual sounds as physically produced), phonemes (the abstract sound categories of a language), and allophones (the predictable variations of a phoneme in different contexts). These concepts underlie everything that follows.

Part II (Chapters 6–13) covers consonants systematically, working through airstream mechanisms, voicing, places of articulation, and manners of articulation. You will learn to classify, describe, perceive, and begin producing consonants from a wide range of languages.

Part III (Chapters 14–18) introduces vowels, including the IPA vowel quadrilateral, the cardinal vowel reference system, and additional vowel dimensions such as length, nasalization, and rhoticity.

Part IV (Chapters 19–23) moves above the level of individual sounds to syllables, stress, rhythm, tone, intonation, and the processes that change sounds in connected speech.

Part V (Chapters 24–28) focuses on transcription as a disciplined skill — choosing the appropriate level of detail, using diacritics purposefully, reading IPA fluently, transcribing from recordings, and interpreting acoustic evidence.

Part VI (Chapters 29–32) teaches you to construct a complete phonetic profile of a language variety of your choice, integrating all the skills and knowledge developed throughout the course.

By Chapter 32, you will not merely know a list of IPA symbols. You will be able to listen carefully to speech from any language, describe what you hear, connect articulatory and acoustic evidence, and produce a documented account of a language's sound system. The IPA is the notation that makes all of that possible.

Common Misconceptions

Common Misconception

Misconception: "The IPA is just another way of spelling English."

Correction: The IPA is a universal phonetic notation system designed for all human languages, not a respelling system for English. It is used to transcribe Mandarin, Zulu, Arabic, and thousands of other languages, most of which have no connection to the English alphabet at all. English examples appear in this book because the book is written in English and English-speaking readers find them useful as orientation points — but English is not central to the IPA, and the IPA is not an English tool.

Common Misconception

Misconception: "The IPA changes how words are spelled."

Correction: The IPA does not replace or change spelling. It is a separate notation system used alongside ordinary orthography, not instead of it. A dictionary entry might show a word's ordinary spelling, its IPA transcription, and its definition — three separate things. Learning the IPA does not affect how you spell words in any language.

Common Misconception

Misconception: "Every language officially uses the IPA."

Correction: The IPA is not an official standard adopted by governments or language academies. Individual languages have their own orthographies (official writing systems), which the IPA does not replace. The IPA is a tool used by researchers, educators, clinicians, and others who study or teach speech — it is not the writing system of any language.

Common Misconception

Misconception: "IPA symbols are arbitrary and random."

Correction: IPA symbols are not random. Many are familiar Latin letters, chosen because they were already in common use for similar sounds. Others are modified letters or newly designed characters chosen systematically. Most importantly, the symbols are organized in a chart whose structure reflects real phonetic relationships among sounds: how they are made, where they are made, and what properties they share. Once you understand the chart's organization, the symbols become much easier to learn and remember.

Common Misconception

Misconception: "Every dictionary uses the same IPA."

Correction: Dictionaries vary in their transcription practices. Some use the full IPA; others use simplified or adapted versions. Some use broad transcription (fewer symbols, representing sound categories); others use narrow transcription (more detail). Some older or non-academic dictionaries use their own respelling systems that are not IPA at all. When consulting a dictionary's pronunciation guide, always check its key to understand which convention is being used.

Chapter Summary

What You Learned in Chapter 3

  • Ordinary spelling is an inconsistent and unreliable guide to pronunciation in many languages, including English. The same letters can represent different sounds, and the same sounds can be spelled in multiple ways.
  • The International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA) was developed in the late nineteenth century by the International Phonetic Association (founded 1886 by Paul Passy and others) to solve this problem. Its first version was published in 1888.
  • The core principle of the IPA is one symbol per speech sound and one sound per symbol, applied consistently across all languages. Symbols are defined by articulatory phonetic properties, not by spelling conventions.
  • The IPA is language-independent — it belongs to no single language and can represent sounds from any human language.
  • The IPA chart is organized into major regions: pulmonic consonants (the largest section, organized by place and manner of articulation), non-pulmonic consonants, vowels (organized by height and backness on a quadrilateral), other symbols, diacritics, and suprasegmentals.
  • Correctly displaying IPA requires Unicode characters — the precise digital codes assigned to each symbol. Ordinary letters should not substitute for IPA symbols even when they look similar.
  • The IPA is used in linguistics, language teaching and learning, lexicography, speech-language pathology, music, acting, broadcasting, forensic linguistics, speech technology, and language documentation, among other fields.
  • Square brackets [ ] enclose phonetic transcriptions. Slashes / / enclose phonemic transcriptions. Angle brackets ⟨ ⟩ enclose orthographic (spelling) forms. The mark ˈ indicates primary stress before the stressed syllable. The mark ː indicates a long sound.
  • Subsequent chapters will develop your ability to read, produce, and analyze IPA systematically, beginning with vocal-tract anatomy (Chapter 4) and the distinction between phonemes, phones, and allophones (Chapter 5).

Key Terms Glossary

consonant
A speech sound in which airflow through the vocal tract is significantly restricted or modified by an articulator. Consonants typically occur at the edges of syllables.
diacritic
A small mark added to a base IPA symbol to indicate additional phonetic detail, such as aspiration, nasalization, or a modified place of articulation.
IPA (International Phonetic Alphabet)
A universal phonetic notation system maintained by the International Phonetic Association. It assigns a unique symbol to each distinct speech sound found in human languages, enabling consistent cross-linguistic transcription.
orthography
The conventional writing system of a language — its standard spelling, punctuation, and related conventions. Orthography reflects historical and cultural conventions rather than current pronunciation.
phone
A single speech sound as physically produced on a specific occasion. Phones are the concrete events that phonetic transcription records.
phoneme
An abstract sound category in a particular language that can distinguish the meanings of words. Phonemes are linguistic entities rather than physical events.
phonetics
The scientific study of the sounds of human speech — how they are produced (articulatory phonetics), how they travel as sound waves (acoustic phonetics), and how they are perceived by listeners (auditory phonetics).
pronunciation
The way in which a word or language sound is spoken. Pronunciation can vary by speaker, region, style, and context.
segment
An individual unit in a phonetic or phonological analysis — typically a single consonant or vowel. Segments are the units that transcription symbols most directly represent.
speech sound
A unit of sound produced by the vocal tract during speech, analyzable in terms of its articulatory, acoustic, and perceptual properties.
suprasegmental
A feature of speech that applies to stretches of sound — such as a syllable, word, or phrase — rather than to individual segments. Stress, tone, intonation, and length are examples.
symbol
In the IPA, a character that represents a specific speech sound. IPA symbols are defined by phonetic descriptions rather than by the spelling conventions of any language.
transcription
A written representation of speech using a phonetic notation system such as the IPA. Transcription can be phonemic (representing abstract sound categories) or phonetic (representing physical realizations of sounds).
Unicode
A universal computer character-encoding standard that assigns a unique numerical code point to every character used in human writing, including all IPA symbols. Unicode enables consistent display of IPA across different devices and software.
vowel
A speech sound in which airflow through the vocal tract is relatively unobstructed. Vowels typically form the nucleus (peak) of a syllable and are described in terms of height, backness, and rounding.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Distinguish speech as a physical, acoustic event from writing as a conventional symbol system.
  2. Explain why writing systems cannot perfectly represent pronunciation.
  3. Identify multiple types of mismatch between letters and sounds.
  4. Describe what different writing systems represent — sounds, syllables, or meanings.
  5. Use angle brackets ⟨ ⟩, slashes / /, and square brackets [ ] correctly to distinguish orthographic, phonemic, and phonetic representation.
  6. Explain why linguists developed the International Phonetic Alphabet.
  7. Identify situations where spelling provides misleading information about pronunciation.

Opening Observation

You already know how to read. That skill is so automatic that it can actually get in the way of hearing speech clearly. Consider the word knight. When you read it, you probably hear one syllable and one initial consonant — not a k, not a gh. Now consider why a beginning student of English, looking at the letters k-n-i-g-h-t, might try to pronounce six distinct sounds. The spelling is not the word. The word is what speakers say.

  1. Write down five English words where at least one letter is silent. How did you know which letter to ignore?
  2. Try to write the sound of a sneeze, a click of disapproval, or the sound made by a dripping faucet, using only the letters of the English alphabet. What problems did you encounter?
  3. If you know another language, think of one word whose spelling a native English speaker would almost certainly mispronounce. What makes it difficult?

Speech Came Before Writing

Human beings have been speaking for at least 100,000 years, and perhaps much longer. Writing, by contrast, is a recent invention — the oldest known writing systems appeared only about 5,000 years ago in Mesopotamia and Egypt. For at least 95,000 years, language existed entirely as speech and gesture: no alphabet, no characters, no page.

This historical fact has a direct consequence for phonetics. Writing did not create language, and it did not create the sounds of language. Writing is a technology that attempts to represent language — one that different civilizations invented independently, in different ways, with different degrees of accuracy, and for different purposes.

~300,000 BP

Anatomically modern humans

The vocal tract capable of producing a wide range of speech sounds is in place. Language, in some form, almost certainly predates writing by hundreds of millennia.

~3,200 BCE

Earliest writing systems

Cuneiform in Mesopotamia and hieroglyphics in Egypt emerge as record-keeping and administrative tools — not as transcriptions of speech designed for phonetic accuracy.

~1,050 BCE

Early alphabets

Phoenician develops a system in which symbols correspond to consonant sounds rather than syllables or whole words — a new approach to representing speech.

1886 CE

The International Phonetic Association

Phoneticians establish a systematic, language-independent alphabet designed to represent any sound in any human language. For the first time, a writing system is explicitly designed to represent phonetics, not just to serve one language community.

Concept Note

Speech is primary; writing is secondary. In linguistics, this is not a value judgment — it is a historical and developmental fact. Children learn to speak before they learn to write. Languages can exist for millennia without any writing system. No writing system, however carefully designed, can capture everything speakers do. Phonetics is the study of speech, not the study of spelling.

Languages Without Writing

Linguists estimate there are between 6,000 and 7,000 languages spoken in the world today. Of these, only a few hundred have a substantial writing tradition, and only a few dozen have large bodies of written literature extending back more than a century or two. Most of the world's languages exist primarily or entirely as spoken systems.

A language without a writing system is not a primitive, incomplete, or deficient language. The Pirahã language of the Brazilian Amazon, the ǀXam language of the Kalahari, and thousands of others are fully complex, expressive, and rule-governed systems capable of conveying any idea a speaker wishes to communicate. They simply do not use writing — or did not until linguists and community members developed writing systems for documentation and education.

Across Languages

Sign languages offer a powerful demonstration that language is fundamentally independent of any external medium. American Sign Language (ASL), British Sign Language (BSL), and the hundreds of other sign languages worldwide are complete, grammatically sophisticated languages in the visual-gestural modality. They are not invented or artificial — they emerge naturally in communities of deaf signers. No writing system captures sign language in a fully adequate way, yet no linguist would describe ASL as an "incomplete" language. Language is the system; writing — and signed notation systems — are secondary representations of it.

The implication for this course is fundamental: when you study the sounds of Spanish, Mandarin, Arabic, or any other language, you are studying something that existed long before any writing system tried to represent it. The phonetics of a language is not contained in the alphabet. You have to listen.

What Writing Systems Actually Do

Writing systems differ in what they choose to represent. Once you understand this, many puzzles about spelling — across many languages — become much clearer.

Alphabetic

A B C · α β γ · а б в

Symbols represent individual consonant and vowel sounds (or attempt to). English, Greek, Russian, and most European scripts work this way. The match between symbols and sounds varies widely in accuracy.

Syllabic (Syllabary)

か き く け こ

Symbols represent whole syllables rather than individual sounds. Japanese kana (hiragana and katakana) work this way. Each symbol covers one consonant-vowel combination rather than the consonant or vowel alone.

Abjad (Consonantal)

ك ت ب

Symbols represent consonants; vowels are omitted or written as optional marks. Arabic, Hebrew, and Aramaic work this way. Readers infer vowels from context and familiarity with the language.

Logographic / Morphosyllabic

字 語 話 文

Symbols represent morphemes or words, often with phonetic components embedded. Chinese characters work this way. A single character can represent a syllable and a meaning simultaneously.

Concept Note — Orthographic Depth

Alphabetic writing systems differ in how consistently they match letters to sounds. Linguists describe this variation as orthographic depth. A shallow orthography (such as Finnish or Spanish) maps letters to sounds quite consistently, with few exceptions. A deep orthography (such as English or French) has accumulated many irregularities through historical change, borrowing, and the failure of spelling reform. The depth of an orthography affects how difficult it is for children to learn to read — and how much a phonetician must distrust the spelling when analyzing speech.

The key point: no writing system was designed by a committee of phoneticians sitting down to represent every acoustic detail of human speech. Writing systems evolve historically, get borrowed across languages and cultures, and are constantly playing catch-up with the actual sounds of the language.

The Many-to-Many Problem

One of the most important things a phonetician learns to recognize is that the relationship between letters and sounds is not one-to-one. It is systematically many-to-many: one letter can represent many sounds, and one sound can have many spellings. The following examples are all from English, a notoriously deep orthography — but every alphabetic language shows some version of this pattern.

Common Misconception

Misconception: "This word has six letters, so it must have six sounds."

Reality: The number of letters in a word tells you almost nothing about the number of sounds it contains — especially in English. Strength has eight letters but six sounds. Ax has two letters but three sounds [æks]. Queue has five letters but two sounds [kjuː]. A phonetician counts sounds, not letters.

The many-to-many relationship is not unique to English. It appears in every alphabetic language to some degree, because all alphabetic writing systems were developed for one historical state of the language and then carried forward as the sounds changed over centuries. The notation we need for phonetics — for accurately representing speech — must be free from this accumulated inconsistency.

Orthography versus Pronunciation

Looking at words across different languages reveals quickly that the same letter can mean very different things in different orthographic systems. The letter ⟨j⟩ is a useful example:

When you read a word in a foreign language using your native spelling intuitions, you will almost always mispronounce it. This is not a failure of intelligence or effort. It is the predictable consequence of treating one orthographic system as if it were universal — which it is not.

Historical Perspective

English spelling was largely standardized in the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries, largely through the influence of early printers. By that point, many sound changes had already occurred that the spelling did not follow. The word knight was actually pronounced with an initial [k] and a final [xt] (a velar fricative) in Middle English — the letters reflected real sounds. The sounds changed; the spelling did not. This historical "freezing" of spelling while pronunciation continues to evolve is one of the main reasons English orthography is so irregular today.

The Three Levels of Representation

Phoneticians and linguists use a precise notation system to distinguish among three fundamentally different things that people often confuse: the way a word is spelled, the sound categories that make the word meaningful in a language, and the detailed physical realization of those sounds when an actual speaker produces them.

These three levels require three different types of notation. Learning to use them correctly is one of the most important foundational skills in this course.

Level 1 — Orthography

⟨ ⟩ Angle Brackets

Used to represent written letters and spellings — what appears on the page. Angle brackets signal that you are discussing a symbol in a writing system, not a sound.

⟨knight⟩ refers to the seven-letter English spelling.

Level 2 — Phonemic

/ / Slashes

Used for phonemic representations — the abstract sound categories of a particular language that distinguish meaning. Also called "broad" transcription.

/naɪt/ represents the phonemes: the contrastive sounds that make knight different from night, knit, etc.

Level 3 — Phonetic

[ ] Square Brackets

Used for phonetic transcription — the actual, detailed physical realization of sounds by a particular speaker in a particular context. Also called "narrow" transcription.

[nʰaɪt] might show that the n in a specific speaker's pronunciation is followed by slight aspiration before the vowel.

Using the Notation Correctly

The three delimiter types are not interchangeable. Using the wrong one signals a claim you did not intend to make.

If you want to discuss… Use… Example
A letter or spelling Angle brackets ⟨ ⟩ The word is spelled ⟨knight⟩.
A phoneme — a sound category that distinguishes meaning Slashes / / The first phoneme is /n/.
A phone — an actual, physically realized sound event Square brackets [ ] The final consonant may be realized as [tʰ] or [t̚].
Common Error

Error: Writing ordinary English spelling inside slashes or square brackets — for example, /knight/ or [knight].

Correction: Slashes and square brackets contain IPA symbols, not ordinary letters. Putting standard spelling inside phonetic delimiters implies that the letters directly represent sounds — exactly the confusion this notation system is designed to prevent. The spelling goes in angle brackets: ⟨knight⟩. The phonemic representation goes in slashes using IPA: /naɪt/.

Why Linguists Need the IPA

By the 1880s, phoneticians and language teachers — especially in France and Britain — were confronting the same problem from different directions. Teachers wanted to teach accurate pronunciation without confusing students through spelling. Linguists wanted to describe the sounds of languages around the world without being constrained by any particular alphabet. Both groups needed the same thing: a notation system that could represent any sound in any language consistently, systematically, and without ambiguity.

The solution was the International Phonetic Association, founded in Paris in 1886, and its International Phonetic Alphabet, first published in 1888. The IPA was designed on several explicit principles:

  • One symbol, one sound. Each symbol represents one specific phonetic value, and each phonetic value has one dedicated symbol. This eliminates the one-to-many and many-to-one problems of ordinary spelling.
  • Symbols are based on familiar Latin and Greek letters where possible, with new shapes created for sounds that have no familiar alphabetic representation. This makes the system learnable without requiring entirely new visual vocabulary.
  • The system is language-neutral. No one language's orthographic conventions are privileged. The same symbol means the same sound whether you are transcribing Swahili, Finnish, or Japanese.
  • The system is expandable. Diacritics can add phonetic detail beyond what the base symbols represent. This allows transcribers to be as precise as their purposes require.

Concept Note — What the IPA Is Not

The IPA is not a pronunciation guide in the style of a tourist phrasebook. It is not a decorative respelling system. It is not a spelling reform proposal for any particular language. And it is not simply a list of symbols to memorize. The IPA is an analytical system — a set of tools for making precise, language-independent claims about the physical properties of speech. Learning the IPA means learning to think about sounds differently, not just memorizing new symbols for sounds you already know.

Answer Key

Exercise A — Speech or Writing?

  1. Writing. The number of letters is a property of orthography, not of the spoken word.
  2. Speech. Rhyme is defined by shared sounds, not shared spellings (cf. through and blue do not share spelling but share vowel sound).
  3. Writing. The letter sequence ⟨ough⟩ is a property of the orthographic representation.
  4. Speech. Babbling occurs in the oral-motor domain before any exposure to written language.
  5. Writing. Capitalization is a convention of written systems with no direct spoken correlate.
  6. Speech. The distinction between final consonant sounds is phonetic.
  7. Both — with important qualifications. A comma signals a potential pause in reading aloud, but readers do not always pause at commas, and spoken language can create pauses through prosody where there is no comma in a written version.
  8. Speech. Rising intonation is a prosodic feature of spoken language. Writing may represent it with a question mark, but only approximately.
  9. Writing. Silence is a property of the letters — the sounds those letters once represented are no longer produced.
  10. Speech. Stress is a prosodic feature realized in sound through pitch, duration, and loudness.

Key Terms

Speech
Language in its primary form — physical sounds produced by a speaker and perceived by a listener. Speech is the object of study in phonetics.
Writing / Orthography
A conventional system of visual symbols (letters, characters, syllabic signs) used to represent language. A secondary, technology-dependent representation of speech.
Orthographic depth
The degree of consistency between letters and sounds in an alphabetic writing system. Shallow orthographies are more consistent; deep orthographies have more exceptions.
Alphabetic writing system
A writing system in which symbols (letters) represent individual consonant or vowel sounds. The match between symbols and sounds varies in accuracy.
Abjad
A writing system that represents consonants only, leaving vowels to be inferred from context. Arabic and Hebrew are examples.
Syllabary
A writing system in which symbols represent whole syllables rather than individual sounds. Japanese kana is an example.
Logographic writing
A writing system in which symbols represent morphemes or words, often with embedded phonetic information. Chinese characters are a primary example.
Angle brackets ⟨ ⟩
Notation used to identify spellings or letters in a writing system. ⟨knight⟩ refers to the written form, not the pronunciation.
Slashes / /
Notation used for phonemic representations — the abstract sound categories of a particular language. Content is written in IPA.
Square brackets [ ]
Notation used for phonetic transcription — the detailed physical realization of sounds. Content is written in IPA, often with greater detail than a phonemic transcription.
International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)
A system of phonetic notation designed to represent any sound in any human language using one consistent symbol per sound value. Developed from 1886 onward by the International Phonetic Association.
Phoneme
An abstract unit of sound in a language that can distinguish meaning. Represented in slashes: /n/. Introduced here; analyzed in depth in Chapter 5.
Phone
A single, physically realized speech sound — one actual instance of a sound produced by a speaker. Represented in square brackets: [n]. Analyzed in depth in Chapter 5.

Looking Ahead

What You Now Understand

You have established something essential: spelling is not sound. The letter is not the phoneme. The phoneme is not the phone. Every time you encounter a word in any language, you now know to ask: "What does this spelling actually tell me about pronunciation, and what does it not tell me?" That skepticism toward orthography is the beginning of phonetic thinking.

Approximants, Rhotics, Taps, and Trills

Open approximations, rhotic diversity, glide-vowel boundaries, and the tap-trill distinction

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Explain what distinguishes approximants from other consonant classes in terms of constriction and airflow.
  2. Distinguish central and lateral approximants and locate both on the IPA chart.
  3. Compare glides with the high vowels they resemble and explain why syllable structure — not articulation alone — determines the classification.
  4. Identify and describe major rhotic realizations across languages without treating "r-sound" as a single category.
  5. Distinguish a tap or flap from a trill in terms of articulation, duration, and repetition.
  6. Interpret language-specific distribution for approximants and rhotics using the contrast and distribution framework from Chapter 5.
  7. Read and produce transcriptions using the approximant, rhotic, tap, and trill symbols scheduled for this chapter.

An Observable Problem

Consider four sounds that often get grouped under the informal label "r-sound": the initial consonant of the General American English word red, the medial consonant of the Spanish word pero (but), the initial consonant of the Spanish word perro (dog), and the initial consonant of the French word rouge (red). Each of these is described by native speakers of its language as their language's "r" — yet the four sounds are articulated in completely different parts of the vocal tract and involve entirely different mechanisms.

The English r is produced with the tongue tip either raised toward the palate or bunched back, with no contact and no vibration. The Spanish medial r in pero involves a single, rapid contact between the tongue tip and the alveolar ridge. The Spanish initial r in perro involves multiple contacts — a vibration. The French r is produced with uvular frication or vibration at the back of the mouth.

A spelling label or a language label tells you almost nothing about the phonetics. What matters is the articulation: where, how, and for how long the vocal tract achieves its constriction.

Approximants

What Makes an Approximant

Purpose: define the approximant class by its constriction degree and contrast it with fricatives, stops, and nasals already studied.

Recall from Chapter 8 that consonants can be arranged along a constriction continuum. At one extreme are stops, which require a complete closure in the oral tract. Fricatives require a narrow constriction through which airflow becomes turbulent. Further along the continuum, the constriction becomes wider and less turbulent — and this is where approximants are found.

An approximant is a consonant produced with a constriction narrow enough to be clearly different from an open vowel, but wide enough that airflow remains smooth rather than turbulent. The active articulator approaches — approximates — a passive articulator without making complete contact and without generating friction. The result is a sound that has consonant-like function in syllable structure but vowel-like acoustic properties, producing no noise or significant turbulence.

This is why approximants are sometimes called semivowels or described as occupying a transitional space between consonants and vowels. The IPA chart recognizes two major subclasses of approximant:

  • Central approximantsAir flows along the midline of the vocal tract, as in all the consonants studied in Chapters 9–10. The glides [j] and [w] and the English retroflex approximant [ɹ] are examples.
  • Lateral approximantsA midline contact is formed, but air flows around the sides of the tongue. These were the subject of Chapter 11. They are approximants in manner, but lateral in airflow direction.

This chapter focuses on central approximants, rhotics, taps, and trills. The lateral approximant [l] and its relatives were covered in Chapter 11.

Concept Check — Approximants on the IPA Chart

On the official IPA consonant chart, the approximant row appears below the tap or flap row and above the lateral fricative and lateral approximant rows. The chart lists approximants by place of articulation, from bilabial on the left to glottal on the right. Not every cell is filled: approximants require an open enough constriction that some place-manner combinations are either articulatorily impossible or not attested as distinct phonemes. When you navigate the chart in this chapter's exercises, note which cells are shaded (indicating impossible combinations) and which are blank (indicating combinations not in common use).

Producing the palatal approximant

The palatal approximant [j] is produced with the tongue dorsum raised high and forward, approaching the hard palate but not touching it. Most English speakers produce this sound at the beginning of words like yes, you, and yard. The tongue position is close to — but slightly lower and less front than — the vowel [i] in see. The difference is that [j] is a brief transitional movement at the onset of a syllable, whereas [i] is a sustained vowel nucleus. This distinction becomes important in the next section.

Producing the labial-velar approximant

The symbol [w] represents a sound produced with two simultaneous approximations: the tongue dorsum rises toward the velum (as it would for the vowel [u]), and the lips round and protrude. Neither gesture alone produces [w]; both are required simultaneously. Lips-only rounding without dorsal raising produces something quite different. You can verify this by comparing the beginning of the English word wet with the beginning of vet: in wet, both rounding and dorsal raising are present from the outset.

Across Languages — Labial-Velar Approximants

The labial-velar approximant [w] is found as a phoneme in many languages across the world's major families. In Yoruba (a Niger-Congo language spoken widely in Nigeria), /w/ contrasts with /v/ and with /j/. In French, [w] appears in loanwords and in the sequence [wa] spelled ⟨oi⟩ — as in moi (me) — where it functions as a glide before the vowel. Standard Mandarin Chinese uses [w] as a syllable-initial glide before rounded vowels, though its phonological status is analyzed differently by different scholars. The consistency of its phonetic description — simultaneous lip rounding and dorsal raising — applies across all these cases, even though its phonological behavior varies.

Glides and High Vowels

Purpose: establish why [j] and [w] are analyzed as consonants while [i] and [u] are analyzed as vowels, despite their articulatory similarity.

The palatal approximant [j] and the high front vowel [i] involve very similar tongue positions. The labial-velar approximant [w] and the high back rounded vowel [u] are similarly close in articulation. This raises an obvious question: what makes [j] a consonant and [i] a vowel, if the tongue is in nearly the same place for both?

The answer lies not in articulation alone but in syllable function. A vowel can be the nucleus of a syllable — the central, most prominent element that carries the syllable's weight. A glide cannot function as a syllable nucleus in the same language in the same context; instead, it occupies the onset or coda position, serving as a rapid transitional gesture into or out of a vowel nucleus.

  • [jiːld] — yield, General American EnglishThe initial [j] is a glide onset; the [iː] that follows it is the syllable nucleus. You cannot stress, lengthen, or hold the [j] in the way you can hold [iː].
  • [wɛt] — wet, General American EnglishThe initial [w] is a glide onset; [ɛ] is the nucleus. The [w] is brief and transitional.
  • [biː] — bee, General American EnglishThe [iː] here is a sustained vowel nucleus — it can be lengthened, stressed, and sung as the peak of the syllable.

Duration provides a useful preliminary test: glides are inherently brief, while vowel nuclei can be sustained. Syllabic function provides the definitive test: only nuclei can bear stress and only nuclei define syllable count. A word like yield has one syllable, not two, because only [iː] is the nucleus — the [j] is a syllable-onset consonant.

Concept Check — Nonsyllabic Notation

When a high vowel quality occurs in a position that is not a syllable nucleus — for example, as part of a diphthong's off-glide — a subscript diacritic marks it as nonsyllabic: [u̯] or [i̯]. You encountered this briefly in transcriptions such as [maɪ̯n] in Chapter 11. The nonsyllabic diacritic signals that this element is functioning as a transitional movement within a syllable rather than as a nucleus in its own right. Chapter 17 will develop the distinction between diphthongs and glide-vowel sequences in detail. For now, recognize that the same high vowel symbol can appear in both syllabic and nonsyllabic positions, and that the diacritic or context indicates which role it is playing.

Variation Note — Glide Hardening

In some languages and some varieties of English, the palatal glide [j] in certain environments can be realized as a fricative or affricate rather than as a smooth approximant. In Standard German, the palatal approximant [j] (as in ja) contrasts with the palatal fricative [ç] (as in ich). In some varieties of English, the sequence of [d] plus [j] fuses into the affricate [dʒ] — so that did you may be realized as [ˈdɪdʒu]. This process, called yod coalescence, illustrates that glides interact with neighboring consonants through coarticulation in ways that can be phonologically complex. The important point is that the realization of /j/ as a smooth approximant versus a fricative or affricate is language- and variety-specific and should not be assumed to be universal.

Rhotics

Rhotic Diversity

Purpose: establish that "rhotics" is a functional category, not an articulatory one, and show why the same language label cannot substitute for phonetic description.

The term rhotic refers to a set of sounds that are grouped together not because they share the same articulation, but because they tend to occupy similar positions in syllable structure, alternate with one another historically, and be identified by native speakers as the same type of sound within a given language. The rhotic category includes sounds produced in quite different parts of the vocal tract and by entirely different mechanisms.

This is why the chapter-opening scenario is important: four sounds called "r" by speakers of their respective languages differ fundamentally in place and manner. Calling all of them "r-sounds" or grouping them under a single IPA symbol would misrepresent the phonetics. Each must be described on its own articulatory terms.

The main rhotic types that appear in the world's languages include:

  • Alveolar or postalveolar approximant [ɹ]The tongue tip or blade raises toward the postalveolar region with no contact; alternatively, the tongue body bunches up and back. No turbulence. Found in General American English and many other varieties.
  • Alveolar tap or flap [ɾ]A single, very brief contact between the tongue tip and the alveolar ridge. Found in most varieties of Spanish (e.g., pero), many varieties of Portuguese, and as an allophone of /t/ or /d/ in General American English (e.g., the medial consonant in butter).
  • Alveolar trill [r]Multiple contacts between the tongue tip and the alveolar ridge, produced by the aerodynamic vibration of the tongue tip in a stream of outgoing air. Found in most varieties of Spanish (e.g., perro), Italian, Catalan, and many other languages.
  • Uvular fricative or approximant [ʁ]The tongue dorsum approximates the uvula, sometimes with frication. Found in standard French, standard German in many varieties, and European Portuguese in many contexts.
  • Uvular trill [ʀ]Multiple contacts between the tongue dorsum and the uvula, produced by aerodynamic vibration. Found in some varieties of French, Arabic, and other languages.
  • Retroflex approximant or flap [ɻ] / [ɽ]The tongue tip curls back (retroflex position). Found in varieties of Hindi-Urdu, some varieties of Swedish, and several South Asian languages.

This diversity has a practical consequence for phonetic analysis: you cannot determine which rhotic is present in a language simply by knowing that speakers describe it as their "r." You must describe the actual articulation.

Across Languages — The Spanish Tap-Trill Contrast

Castilian Spanish provides one of the clearest documented cases in which a tap and a trill are phonemically contrastive. The word pero (but) contains a single tap [ɾ] between vowels: [ˈpe.ɾo]. The word perro (dog) contains a trill [r] in the same position: [ˈpe.ro]. Substituting one for the other changes the word's meaning. This minimal pair demonstrates that the distinction between a single contact and multiple contacts is phonologically significant in Spanish — it is not merely an optional variation in emphasis or style. The same minimal pair cannot exist in English, because English does not contrast taps with trills as separate phonemes.

Spelling Trap — The Letter ⟨r⟩ Across Languages

The letter ⟨r⟩ is used in the orthographies of English, Spanish, French, German, Italian, and many other languages, but it represents a different phoneme — or set of allophones — in each. The phonetic content of ⟨r⟩ in English is [ɹ] (an approximant); in Castilian Spanish it is [ɾ] or [r] depending on position; in standard French and Standard German it is typically [ʁ] (a uvular fricative or approximant); in Standard Italian it is often [r] (a trill). Reading a phonetic symbol from a spelling and assuming it is the same across languages is one of the most common errors in introductory phonetics. The symbol in a transcription must represent an articulatory claim, not an orthographic tradition.

Distribution and Phonological Status of Rhotics

Purpose: show how the same phoneme symbol can cover different allophones and how language-specific distribution determines phonological analysis.

In General American English, the approximant [ɹ] occurs in a wide range of positions: syllable-initial (red), between vowels (sorry), and after a vowel in the same syllable (car). However, in medial intervocalic position in casual speech, many English words contain a tap [ɾ] that is more commonly analyzed as an allophone of /t/ or /d/ than as a rhotic. The word butter [ˈbʌɾɚ] contains a tap, but the tap represents the phoneme /t/ in context, not a separate rhotic phoneme.

In contrast, in Castilian Spanish the tap [ɾ] and the trill [r] are separate phonemes in intervocalic position, as established by the pero/perro minimal pair. In word-initial and post-consonantal positions, only the trill [r] occurs, making the tap predictable in some environments and contrastive in others. The full analysis requires examining the complete distribution, not just a single example.

This comparison illustrates a general principle: the phonological status of a phone — whether it is a separate phoneme, an allophone of one phoneme, or an allophone of a different phoneme — can only be determined by examining distribution and applying the contrast test within a specific language. A phone's IPA symbol does not determine its phonological status across languages.

Evidence Note — Rhoticity and Vowels

In languages like General American English and some varieties of Portuguese, a vowel followed by a rhotic in the same syllable can take on a rhotic coloring — a quality known as rhoticity. In General American English, vowels in words like bird, here, and car often have rhotic resonance that is simultaneous with the vowel rather than strictly sequential. The IPA represents this with the rhoticity diacritic ˞, as in [bɜ˞d] or the r-colored schwa [ɚ]. Chapter 18 will develop vowel rhoticity in detail. For now, note that rhoticity is an additional dimension of vowel quality, not simply a consonant following a vowel, and that its analysis requires language- and variety-specific evidence.

Taps, Flaps, and Trills

Taps, Flaps, and Trills

Purpose: define taps or flaps and trills as distinct manner categories, separate from approximants and stops, and establish the articulatory basis for the tap-trill distinction.

Taps, flaps, and trills all involve contact between an active articulator and a passive surface, but they differ fundamentally from stops in the brevity and mechanism of that contact, and they differ from one another in how many contacts occur.

A tap (also called a flap by many phoneticians) is produced by a single, extremely brief ballistic contact between the active articulator and the target surface. The tongue tip moves rapidly toward the alveolar ridge and away in a single gesture, making contact for so short a time that no build-up of air pressure occurs — distinguishing the tap from a true stop. The IPA symbol for the alveolar tap is [ɾ].

A trill is produced by placing the active articulator in loose contact with the target surface and allowing the stream of outgoing air to cause it to vibrate through aerodynamic forces — the Bernoulli effect. The vibration produces multiple contacts in rapid succession. A trill requires the articulator to be both close enough to the target for aerodynamic interaction and loose enough to vibrate freely. The IPA symbol for the alveolar trill is [r]; the uvular trill is [ʀ]; a bilabial trill (used in some languages, often as an expression of disgust or cold in European languages) is [ʙ].

Concept Check — Tap Versus Stop

It is tempting to describe a tap as a "very short stop," and phonetically the similarity exists: both involve a tongue-tip contact with the alveolar ridge. But there are important differences. In a stop, the contact creates a full closure that builds up air pressure; the release of that pressure creates the characteristic burst of a plosive. In a tap, the contact is so brief that no pressure accumulates, and there is no release burst. Acoustic evidence shows that tap duration is typically around 20–30 milliseconds — far shorter than even the shortest stops. Articulatorily, the tap is produced with a ballistic movement, meaning the tongue is set in motion toward the target and the contact happens as a by-product of the movement rather than as a deliberate closure. The stop requires deliberate closure, maintained pressure, and deliberate release.

The alveolar tap in General American English

Many learners are surprised to discover that General American English contains an alveolar tap as a regular allophone, even though English has no tap or trill phoneme. In General American English, the phonemes /t/ and /d/ are both realized as the tap [ɾ] when they occur between vowels within a prosodic word, provided the preceding vowel bears stress and the following vowel or syllabic consonant does not. The words butter, ladder, atom, and editing all contain this tap in careful natural speech.

  • [ˈbʌɾɚ] — butter, General American EnglishThe underlying /t/ is realized as a tap [ɾ] between the stressed vowel [ʌ] and the syllabic [ɚ].
  • [ˈlæɾɚ] — ladder, General American EnglishThe underlying /d/ is also realized as a tap in this environment, resulting in the same phone as in butter — which is why many listeners find the two words hard to distinguish without context.
  • [ˈæɾəm] — atom, General American EnglishThe medial /t/ taps in this environment.

This pattern is a striking example of allophonic variation: the tap is a predictable, context-sensitive realization of both /t/ and /d/ in this environment in General American English. It does not represent a separate phoneme. A phonemic transcription of butter remains /bʌtɚ/, while the phonetic form is [bʌɾɚ].

Variation Note — The Tap in Other English Varieties

The tap allophone described above is characteristic of General American English and many varieties of Canadian, Australian, and New Zealand English. Standard Southern British English does not typically have this pattern in the same environments: a word like butter is more likely to be produced with a full aspirated or unaspirated [t], depending on the syllable and the style of speech. Some varieties of British English produce a glottal stop [ʔ] in this position instead. Whether a speaker uses a tap, an aspirated stop, an unreleased stop, or a glottal stop in the medial position of butter depends on the specific variety being analyzed. Always state the reference variety before claiming what a given English phoneme "does" in a given context.

Duration and Repetition as Diagnostic Criteria

Purpose: give learners practical perceptual tools for distinguishing approximants, taps, and trills from one another.

Three of the sound classes in this chapter can be ordered along a dimension of contact duration and repetition:

  • Approximant [ɹ] or [j] or [w]No contact. The articulator approaches but does not touch the passive surface. Duration varies, but the absence of contact means there is no release event.
  • Tap or flap [ɾ]A single ballistic contact, extremely brief. One touch, no sustained closure, no repetition.
  • Trill [r]Multiple contacts produced by aerodynamic vibration. The articulator touches, bounces away, and touches again several times in rapid succession while the air continues to flow.

Duration provides the first test: if a sound can be sustained without changing its character, it is not a tap (taps cannot be sustained — their character depends on brevity). Repetition provides the second test: if multiple distinct contacts can be heard or felt, it is a trill. A single brief contact is a tap. No contact at all is an approximant.

These tests are more useful than asking "how many trills does this sound have?" because the number of trill contacts in a genuine trill varies by speaker, rate, and phonological environment. What is stable is the mechanism: aerodynamic vibration producing multiple contacts versus a single ballistic gesture versus sustained approximation.

Across Languages — Taps Versus Trills in Italian

Standard Italian provides a clear illustration of the trill in a language where it is often described as the default rhotic. In Standard Italian (based on Florentine norms), the phoneme /r/ is typically realized as an alveolar trill [r] in careful speech, particularly word-initially and when geminate. In everyday spoken Italian, however, a single tap [ɾ] is frequent in intervocalic position, and some urban varieties have replaced the trill with an approximant in many contexts. This variation illustrates that describing a language's rhotic as simply "a trill" or "a tap" can be an oversimplification of what speakers actually produce. A complete phonetic description must specify the variety and the phonological environment.

Guided Observation

The following tasks use only your own speech and, for the trill tasks, a relaxed vocal tract. Do not force any articulation that causes discomfort.

Observation 1: Approximant with no contact. Sustain the initial sound of the English word red as long as you can, without transitioning into the vowel. Notice that you can hold this sound for several seconds. There is no contact anywhere in your mouth — you cannot feel the tongue tip touching anything. Now sustain a [d] without releasing it: you will feel the tongue tip pressed against the alveolar ridge. The contrast between contact and no-contact is the essential difference between an approximant and a stop.

Observation 2: Glide duration. Say the English word yes very slowly, attempting to extend the initial sound before the vowel begins. Notice that the initial [j] resists being held — it rapidly blends into the vowel. Compare this with holding the initial [s] of see, which can be sustained indefinitely as a distinct fricative. The brevity of the glide and its tendency to merge with the following vowel reflects its transitional, non-nuclear character.

Observation 3: The tap. Say the English phrase butter and water at a natural conversational pace. Focus on the medial consonant in each word — the sound between the vowels. Now try to feel whether the tongue tip makes contact with the alveolar ridge or not. Most General American English speakers will feel a very brief, fleeting contact. Compare this with the clear, sustained contact of the [t] at the beginning of top. Describe what you feel in your own words without using the word "r" or the word "d."

Observation 4: Tapping toward a trill. Place the tip of your tongue lightly against the alveolar ridge and blow a gentle stream of air through it. Do not press hard — the tongue should be relaxed and loose. Some speakers will find that the tongue tip begins to vibrate against the ridge. If not, try adjusting the amount of air pressure and the tension in the tongue. If vibration begins, note: the sensation is multiple contacts in rapid succession driven by the airstream, not by deliberate tongue movement. This is the aerodynamic basis of the alveolar trill. Do not continue if the exercise is uncomfortable.

  2. Describe what distinguishes the tap from a full alveolar stop, based on what you observed. What is present in the stop that is absent in the tap?

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake

Error: Using the symbol [r] as a general-purpose "r-sound" symbol for any rhotic in any language.

Correction: The IPA symbol [r] specifically represents a voiced alveolar trill — multiple aerodynamically produced contacts of the tongue tip against the alveolar ridge. Using it for the General American English approximant (correctly [ɹ]), the French uvular fricative (correctly [ʁ]), or a tap (correctly [ɾ]) is a phonetic misrepresentation. Each symbol commits you to a specific articulatory claim. Write the symbol that matches the actual articulation.

Common Mistake

Error: Classifying [j] and [w] as vowels because they resemble [i] and [u] in articulation.

Correction: Glides are classified as consonants on the basis of their syllable function, not their articulatory position. The palatal approximant [j] and the labial-velar approximant [w] occur in syllable onset and coda positions; they do not function as syllable nuclei. The test is syllabic function: can the sound carry stress and define a syllable by itself? For [j] and [w], the answer is no in the contexts where they appear as glides.

Common Mistake

Error: Treating the distinction between [ɾ] and [r] as a difference in emphasis or forcefulness rather than in articulatory mechanism.

Correction: The tap [ɾ] and the trill [r] differ in number of contacts and in the mechanism that produces them, not in loudness or stress. A trill is not "a stronger tap." Producing a trill requires aerodynamic vibration of a loosely positioned articulator; producing a tap requires a single ballistic movement. Both can appear in stressed or unstressed syllables. In Castilian Spanish, the contrast between pero and perro does not involve stress differences — the minimal pair has the same stress pattern. The distinction is purely in the manner of the rhotic consonant.

Common Mistake

Error: Assuming that [w] is simply a "rounded [j]" produced only with lip rounding.

Correction: The labial-velar approximant [w] involves two simultaneous approximations: lip rounding and dorsal raising toward the velum. Rounding alone — without the dorsal gesture — does not produce [w]. The labial dimension and the velar dimension are both required. This is why [w] is described as a double articulation and listed under labial-velar in the IPA chart, not under bilabial alone.

In the Spanish data, [ɾ] appears in intervocalic position after a stressed vowel (pero, para). In Castilian Spanish, [ɾ] and [r] are separate phonemes because they contrast in meaning-distinguishing minimal pairs in identical intervocalic environments. 1. The error is calling the tap a trill because it involves contact. A trill requires multiple contacts produced by aerodynamic vibration. Chapter Review — Part A (model answers)

1. An approximant is produced with a constriction degree that is narrower than an open vowel but wider than a fricative — wide enough that airflow remains smooth and non-turbulent. A fricative requires a narrow enough constriction to generate turbulent noise; an open vowel has so little constriction that it does not function as a consonant in syllable structure. An approximant falls between these two points.   2. The classification depends on syllable function, not articulation. The palatal approximant [j] occurs in syllable-onset position, cannot be the nucleus of a syllable, and cannot carry stress in isolation. The high front vowel [i] functions as a syllable nucleus and can be stressed, lengthened, and sustained. Syllabic function is the criterion.   3. A tap involves a single ballistic contact between the tongue tip and the alveolar ridge, too brief for pressure to build. A trill involves multiple contacts produced by aerodynamic vibration of a loosely positioned articulator. The key differences are number of contacts (one versus multiple) and the mechanism of production (ballistic gesture versus aerodynamic vibration).   4. "Rhotic" groups sounds by their functional behavior in syllable structure and their historical tendency to alternate, not by shared articulation. For example, General American English [ɹ] is an approximant with no contact, while Spanish [r] is an alveolar trill with multiple contacts — the two are both called rhotics but are produced entirely differently.

Chapter Review — Part C

1. The underlying phoneme of writer is /t/ and of rider is /d/. In the intervocalic post-stress environment in General American English, both are realized as the tap [ɾ]. This shows that [ɾ] is an allophone shared by two different phonemes in this environment — it is predictable from the phonological context and does not require a separate rhotic phoneme in English.   2. Word-initially, only [r] occurs in Castilian Spanish — [ɾ] does not appear in that position. This means the two phones are not in contrast word-initially; their distribution is restricted. However, the phonemic analysis is established by the intervocalic minimal pair (pero/perro), where both can occur and meaning changes. A restricted distribution in one environment does not negate the contrast established in another.   3. The syllable count of yield is determined by its number of nuclei — syllable peaks. The only nucleus is [iː]; the initial [j] is a consonant occupying the onset position and does not function as a nucleus. One nucleus means one syllable. Spelling is not a reliable guide to syllable count.

Cumulative Review

1. Ordered from most closed to most open on the constriction continuum: [p] (plosive — complete oral closure); [f] (fricative — narrow turbulent constriction); [ɾ] (tap — single brief contact, narrower than approximant but much briefer than a stop); [r] (trill — multiple brief contacts via aerodynamic vibration); [ɹ] (approximant — open constriction, no contact, no turbulence). Note: the tap and trill are difficult to place on a simple constriction continuum because their contact duration rather than the degree of closure distinguishes them from stops.   2. The underlying contrast is /t/ (writer) versus /d/ (rider). In General American English, both /t/ and /d/ are realized as the tap [ɾ] in intervocalic post-stressed position, producing the same surface phone from two different phonemes. The allophony makes the two words nearly homophonous in citation speech; the phonemic difference is neutralized at the phonetic level in this environment.   3. Nasals are susceptible to place assimilation because their only phonetically distinctive property (beyond voicing and the open velic port) is the location of the oral closure — a property easily influenced by the place of a neighboring consonant. Approximants like [ɹ], [j], and [w] have no oral closure at all: they are defined by an open constriction, not by contact at a specific place. Without a closure to assimilate, they have less to change in place-assimilation contexts, though their constriction location and the quality of the approximation can still be influenced by adjacent sounds.

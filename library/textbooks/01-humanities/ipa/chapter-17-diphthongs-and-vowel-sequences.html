Diphthongs and Vowel Sequences

From stable vowel targets to vowels that move

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Distinguish monophthongs, diphthongs, and vowel sequences using articulatory and syllabic evidence.
  2. Describe a dynamic vowel's starting and ending qualities using the vowel-space vocabulary from Chapters 14-16.
  3. Interpret nonsyllabic notation and apply it to controlled examples.
  4. Use syllable-count evidence to support an analytical decision about a dynamic vowel.
  5. Compare phonemic and phonetic treatments of the same dynamic vowel data.
  6. Transcribe a controlled set of diphthongs and vowel sequences.

An Observable Problem

Chapter 16 treated every vowel as a single, stable target: one tongue height, one backness value, one rounding value, held for the duration of the vowel. That description works for the monophthongs of Spanish, Japanese, or Hawaiian, but it does not work for the vowel in English time, or in house, or in the Italian word for "yes," sì followed by ai as in mai. In each of these, the vowel quality is not constant. Say the English word time slowly and pay attention to where your tongue starts and where it ends.

A single vowel symbol from Chapter 16's inventory cannot represent this movement, because a single symbol represents a single point in the vowel space. This raises three questions that this chapter is designed to answer:

  1. When does a vowel that moves count as one phoneme rather than two separate vowels in sequence?
  2. How much of that movement needs to be represented in a transcription, and how?
  3. How do we distinguish a vowel that glides within one syllable from two vowels that happen to sit in adjacent syllables?

Before continuing, compare the vowel in English time with the two vowels in a word like chaotic, where a and o belong to different syllables. Both involve vowel-quality movement across the word, but they are not the same kind of phenomenon. Predict, in your own words, what evidence might distinguish them.

Dynamic Vowels

Monophthongs, Diphthongs, and Vowel Sequences

Purpose: establish the three-way distinction at the center of the chapter, using syllable structure and articulatory continuity as the primary diagnostic evidence.

A monophthong, as developed in Chapter 16, is a vowel with a single, relatively steady articulatory target for the duration of the vowel. A diphthong is a vowel that moves from one quality toward another within a single syllable nucleus, produced as one continuous, uninterrupted articulatory gesture. A vowel sequence — sometimes called hiatus when the two vowels belong to separate syllables — is two vowel phonemes standing next to each other, each retaining its own syllable.

The distinction between a diphthong and a vowel sequence is not always obvious from the sound alone, because both involve a change in vowel quality over time. The difference lies in syllabification: a diphthong is one syllable nucleus with internal movement, while a vowel sequence spans two syllable nuclei. This is why the procedure in this chapter treats syllable evidence, not just auditory quality, as essential.

Concept Check — One Nucleus or Two?

English time /taɪm/ has one syllable and one vowel phoneme, the diphthong /aɪ/, moving from a low-central-to-front starting quality toward a higher, fronter ending quality. Italian paese "country," by contrast, is commonly analyzed with the sequence /a.e/ across two syllables, each vowel retaining its own syllabic status. Both involve a change in vowel quality across the word, but only the first is a diphthong in the sense developed in this chapter. Syllable count, established using the tools from Chapter 19 (previewed briefly here), is part of the evidence that separates the two cases.

Describing Starting and Ending Qualities

Purpose: give learners a controlled vocabulary for describing diphthong movement using the height, backness, and rounding dimensions already established in Chapter 14.

A diphthong is described by naming its starting quality and its ending quality, using the same height, backness, and rounding vocabulary introduced for monophthongs. The starting quality is typically the more prominent, longer-held portion of the diphthong; the ending quality represents the direction of movement rather than a fully achieved target in many varieties, since the tongue may not reach the endpoint before the vowel concludes.

English offers several widely cited examples for orientation, always understood as varying by regional standard:

aɪ
Low central/front start, movement toward high front — time
aʊ
Low central start, movement toward high back rounded — house
ɔɪ
Mid-low back rounded start, movement toward high front — boy

Some descriptions of General American and other varieties also analyze the vowels of words such as face and goat as diphthongal, transcribed approximately /eɪ/ and /oʊ/, while other descriptions treat these as monophthongs with a following glide-like offset that is not phonemically significant. Both positions appear in the literature; state which analysis a given transcription follows rather than presenting one as the only correct account.

Variation Note — Diphthongs Differ by Variety

The starting and ending qualities of a given diphthong are not identical across English varieties. The vowel in time, for instance, is described with a range of starting qualities across regional and social varieties of English, and some varieties monophthongize this vowel in certain contexts. Any diphthong transcription used in this book names the variety it represents rather than presenting one realization as the diphthong for English as a whole.

Nonsyllabic Notation

Most course transcriptions of diphthongs will not mark a nonsyllabic diacritic on every offglide, because the diphthong symbols themselves (such as /aɪ/) are already conventionally understood to represent a single syllable nucleus with internal movement. The nonsyllabic diacritic becomes necessary chiefly when a transcription must distinguish a genuine diphthong from an otherwise identical-looking vowel-plus-approximant or vowel-plus-vowel sequence, or when a source's convention requires it for clarity. The syllable boundary mark, by contrast, is the primary tool for showing that two vowels are not a diphthong at all.

Note: full syllabification method is developed in Chapter 19. This chapter uses the syllable boundary mark only descriptively, to support the diphthong-versus-sequence distinction.

Analytical Tools

Using Syllable Evidence

Purpose: give learners a concrete, repeatable procedure for deciding whether a stretch of vowel movement is a diphthong or a vowel sequence.

Because auditory quality alone cannot reliably distinguish a diphthong from a vowel sequence, this chapter recommends checking the following kinds of evidence, in combination rather than isolation:

  1. Syllable count. If reliable evidence (such as a dictionary syllabification, a native-speaker judgment, or a stress-placement pattern) indicates one syllable, a diphthong analysis is supported. If the evidence indicates two syllables, a vowel-sequence analysis is supported.
  2. Stress behavior. In languages with lexical stress, a diphthong is typically stressed or unstressed as a single unit; a genuine vowel sequence may allow stress to fall on either vowel independently, since each is its own syllable.
  3. Continuity of articulation. A diphthong is produced as one uninterrupted gesture; a vowel sequence may show a brief articulatory discontinuity at the syllable boundary, sometimes with an intervening glide or glottal onset, depending on the language.
  4. Distributional behavior. If the language treats the vowel-movement unit the same way it treats known monophthongs for purposes such as counting mora or determining stress placement, that similarity supports a diphthong analysis; if it behaves like two independent vowel slots, that supports a sequence analysis.

No single piece of evidence is decisive on its own, and analysts sometimes disagree about borderline cases even when working from the same data. The goal of this procedure is not to produce a single infallible test, but to make the analytical reasoning explicit and defensible.

Comparing Phonemic and Phonetic Treatments

Purpose: apply the Chapter 16 broad-versus-phonetic distinction to dynamic vowels, and show that a single diphthong can be analyzed in more than one defensible way.

Even after the diphthong-versus-sequence question has been settled, a further analytical choice remains: should a given diphthong be treated phonemically as a single unit, or as a vowel plus a following glide? Both treatments appear in the scholarly literature for various languages, including English. Under a single-unit analysis, /aɪ/ is one phoneme occupying one slot in the syllable nucleus. Under a vowel-plus-glide analysis, the same stretch of sound might be represented as a vowel followed by the approximant /j/ occupying a separate position in the syllable.

Neither analysis is simply correct or incorrect in isolation; each is a defensible response to a language's overall phonological patterning, and the choice can affect how syllable weight, stress, or phonotactic restrictions are described elsewhere in the grammar. For the purposes of this course, learners are expected to recognize that this choice exists, to state which analysis a given transcription assumes, and to remain consistent within a single exercise or analysis, consistent with the Master Instructions' policy on alternative conventions.

Evidence Note — Two Legitimate Charts, Again

As in Chapter 16, a phonemic chart for diphthongs plots each diphthong as a single contrastive unit using a representative citation-form realization, while a phonetic plot may trace the actual measured trajectory of formant values from onset to offset across tokens. The two documents answer different questions and should not be mixed without clear labeling.

Across Languages

Across Languages — Spanish: Vowel Sequences Without Phonemic Diphthongs

Standard varieties of Spanish are commonly analyzed as lacking phonemic diphthongs in the sense developed in this chapter; sequences such as the ai in baile "dance" are typically analyzed as a vowel combining with a nonsyllabic high vowel within a single syllable, a pattern distinct from the English case discussed above. The five-vowel monophthong inventory from Chapter 16 remains the phonemic core of the system, and vowel-quality sequences are analyzed compositionally from that same small inventory rather than requiring a separate set of diphthong phonemes.

Across Languages — German: A Small, Distinct Diphthong Set

Standard German is commonly described as having three phonemic diphthongs, transcribed approximately /aɪ, aʊ, ɔʏ/, existing alongside the larger monophthong inventory discussed in Chapter 16. The German diphthong set is smaller than its monophthong inventory, illustrating that diphthong count and monophthong count are separate dimensions of a vowel system that do not need to scale together.

Across Languages — Vietnamese: Vowel Sequences and Tone Interaction

Vietnamese is commonly described as having a rich set of vowel sequences interacting with the tone system that Chapter 21 will address directly. Analysts differ on which sequences should be treated as unitary diphthongs versus vowel-plus-glide combinations, illustrating that the alternative-analysis question raised earlier in this chapter is not a hypothetical concern but an active point of discussion for specific, well-studied languages.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake

Error: Treating every stretch of changing vowel quality as a diphthong, without checking syllable evidence.

Correction: Vowel-quality movement occurs in both diphthongs and vowel sequences. Use syllable count, stress behavior, articulatory continuity, and distributional behavior together before deciding which analysis applies, rather than relying on auditory impression alone.

Common Mistake

Error: Assuming that a spelling sequence of two vowel letters always signals a diphthong or always signals a vowel sequence.

Correction: Orthography is not reliable evidence for this distinction, consistent with the sound-versus-spelling principle established in Chapter 2. English ai spells a monophthong in some words, a diphthong in others, and does not correspond to a vowel sequence in most cases. Evaluate the actual spoken form.

Common Mistake

Error: Presenting the vowel-plus-glide analysis or the single-unit diphthong analysis as the only correct account for a given language.

Correction: Both analyses appear in the scholarly literature for several languages, including English. State which analysis a transcription assumes and remain consistent within an exercise, rather than treating one convention as universally correct.

Chapter Review — Part A

1. A monophthong holds one relatively steady vowel quality; a diphthong moves between two qualities within a single syllable nucleus as one continuous gesture; a vowel sequence places two separate vowel phonemes in adjacent syllables. Auditory quality alone cannot distinguish a diphthong from a sequence because both involve changing vowel quality over time; the distinguishing evidence is syllabic, not purely auditory.   2. The four evidence types are syllable count, stress behavior, continuity of articulation, and distributional behavior. More than one type is normally needed because no single piece of evidence is decisive on its own, and analysts may need to weigh converging or conflicting evidence.

Chapter Review — Part B

1. /aɪ/ begins with a low central-to-front unrounded quality and moves toward a high front unrounded quality.   2. The nonsyllabic diacritic marks a vowel symbol as not forming its own syllable nucleus, used when a transcription must clarify that an element is part of a diphthong rather than a separate syllable; the syllable boundary mark shows a division between two syllables, used to represent a vowel sequence rather than a diphthong.

Chapter Review — Part C

1. A single-unit analysis treats the diphthong as one phoneme occupying one nucleus slot; a vowel-plus-glide analysis treats it as a vowel followed by a separate approximant phoneme. A phonologist might prefer one analysis if it produces a simpler or more consistent account of stress, syllable weight, or phonotactic patterning elsewhere in the language's grammar.   2. German has three phonemic diphthongs but a considerably larger monophthong inventory, showing that the number of diphthong phonemes a language uses is not predictable from, or necessarily proportional to, the size of its monophthong inventory.

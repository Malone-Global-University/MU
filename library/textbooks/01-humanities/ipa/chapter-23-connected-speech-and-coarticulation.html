Connected Speech and Coarticulation

How speech changes in context, and how to transcribe what speakers actually do

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Distinguish coarticulation from categorical phonological alternation.
  2. Identify assimilation, reduction, deletion, epenthesis, resyllabification, and linking in controlled data.
  3. Explain gradient versus categorical change at an introductory level.
  4. Compare citation forms with connected realizations of the same words.
  5. Identify style and rate effects on connected speech.
  6. Produce a controlled connected-speech transcription using only previously taught notation.

An Observable Problem

Every transcription task so far in this book has worked with words spoken carefully, one at a time, or with short phrases treated as fixed wholes. Real speech rarely works that way. Say the phrase "did you" at a natural conversational pace, and compare it with the same two words pronounced slowly and separately, as if reading a word list aloud. For many speakers of many languages, the natural version sounds noticeably different from the sum of its two citation forms: a consonant may sound different at the boundary between the words, a vowel may shrink toward a shorter and less distinct quality, or the two words may seem to blend into something that is difficult to segment cleanly into "did" plus "you."

None of this is disorder, laziness, or a broken version of careful speech. It is systematic, and every fluent speaker of every language does some version of it. The challenge for a phonetician is not to judge these changes but to describe them precisely: what changed, why it changed given the surrounding sounds, whether the change is a small automatic adjustment or a larger categorical substitution, and how a transcriber should represent it responsibly.

This chapter asks three questions:

  1. What is the difference between a small, automatic, context-driven adjustment to a sound and a larger, more categorical change that a language treats as an alternation between distinct forms?
  2. What recurring named processes account for most of the differences between a citation form and its connected-speech realization, and how can a beginner identify each one in verified data?
  3. How do speech style and speech rate affect how much connected-speech change actually occurs, and what should a transcriber do differently for careful, formal speech compared with fast, casual speech?
Connected Speech

Coarticulation Versus Categorical Alternation

Purpose: establish the central distinction of the chapter before naming any individual process, so that every later example can be sorted correctly.

Coarticulation is the overlap of articulatory gestures for neighboring sounds, so that the vocal tract begins moving toward a following sound, or continues moving from a preceding one, while a given segment is still being produced. Coarticulation is largely mechanical and gradient: it reflects the physical reality that the tongue, lips, and jaw cannot move instantly between target positions, and the amount of overlap can vary continuously with rate, style, and position rather than jumping between a small number of fixed outcomes.

A categorical phonological alternation, by contrast, is a change that a language treats as a choice between a limited number of discrete forms, typically describable as one sound class replacing another in a definable context. A categorical alternation is often reported consistently by fluent speakers as one sound or another, not as a continuous range in between, and it may be reflected in how the language's own speakers or its established phonological description treat the pattern.

Concept Check — Gradient Overlap Versus Discrete Substitution

A useful starting test, though not the only evidence a full phonological analysis would use, is to ask whether the observed variation forms a continuous range that tracks rate and context by degree, which points toward coarticulation, or whether speakers and analyses instead describe the outcome as a small number of distinct categories with comparatively sharp boundaries between them, which points toward a categorical alternation. Many connected-speech processes discussed in this chapter can show both a gradient version, which is coarticulation proper, and a more categorical version in fast or casual speech, and part of the transcriber's task is deciding which is supported by the evidence at hand.

This distinction connects directly to the phone-phoneme-allophone framework introduced in Chapter 5. A coarticulatory adjustment is ordinarily allophonic: it does not change which phoneme a listener perceives, only some phonetic detail of its realization. A categorical alternation may still be allophonic, if it is a fully predictable, conditioned substitution, or it may cross into a different kind of process entirely, such as deletion or epenthesis, which is why this chapter treats coarticulation as the starting concept rather than the whole story.

Common Mistake

Error: Treating every audible difference between a citation form and a connected-speech form as evidence of "sloppy" or "incorrect" pronunciation.

Correction: Connected-speech processes are systematic and rule-governed, not random degradation. A careful phonetic description states what changed and under what conditions, without characterizing the change as an error.

Citation Forms and Connected Realizations

Purpose: define the two reference points that every comparison in this chapter depends on.

A citation form is the pronunciation of a word produced in isolation, typically slowly and carefully, as if reading a word list or answering "how do you say this word." A connected realization is the pronunciation of the same word as it actually occurs within a longer, naturally paced utterance, where it is influenced by neighboring words, phrase position, prominence, and rate.

Dictionaries and early textbook chapters generally supply citation forms because they provide a stable reference point for teaching an inventory of contrasts, consistent with the broad-transcription policy from Chapter 24 previewed here. Citation forms are not incorrect, and connected realizations are not incorrect; they answer different questions. A responsible transcription task, following the purpose-dependent principle introduced earlier in this book, always states which kind of form is being represented.

  • Citation: /ɡʊd bɔɪ/ "good boy," each word produced separately and carefully, General American reference.
  • Connected: [ɡʊb bɔɪ] Same phrase at a natural conversational pace; place assimilation across the word boundary, described further below.

    • Notice that the connected form above is bracketed with square brackets, marking it as a phonetic realization, while the underlying words retain their citation-form phonemic identity. Comparing the two forms side by side, rather than presenting only one, is the method this chapter uses throughout.

    Naming the Processes

    Purpose: introduce a working vocabulary for the most common connected-speech processes, each defined, illustrated, and distinguished from its neighbors.

    Assimilation

    Assimilation occurs when a sound becomes more similar to a neighboring sound in one or more features, such as place, manner, or voicing. Assimilation may affect a sound that comes before the trigger, called anticipatory or regressive assimilation, or a sound that follows it, called perseverative or progressive assimilation.

  • /ɡʊd bɔɪ/ → [ɡʊb bɔɪ] General American; the alveolar stop takes on the bilabial place of the following stop, an example of regressive place assimilation.
    • Reduction

    Reduction describes a shift of a vowel, or occasionally a consonant, toward a shorter, less peripheral, or less distinct realization, frequently affecting unstressed function words such as articles, prepositions, and auxiliary verbs. Reduction is closely tied to the stress and prominence patterns introduced in Chapter 20: unstressed material is where reduction is most common, though it is not automatic in every language or every unstressed position.

  • Citation "to" /tuː/ → Connected [tə] General American; unstressed function-word reduction to a central vowel in fluent speech.
    • Deletion

    Deletion, sometimes called elision, is the complete absence of a segment that is present in the citation form, most often affecting an unstressed vowel or a consonant in a complex cluster.

  • Citation "camera" /ˈkæmərə/ → Connected [ˈkæmrə] General American; the unstressed medial vowel is frequently deleted in casual speech, a pattern also called syncope.
    • Epenthesis

    Epenthesis is the insertion of a segment not present in the citation form, frequently occurring to break up a sequence that the language's phonotactics, introduced in Chapter 19, otherwise disfavor.

  • Citation "something" /ˈsʌmθɪŋ/ → Connected [ˈsʌmpθɪŋ] General American; an epenthetic stop can appear between a nasal and a following fricative in some speakers' casual speech.
    • Resyllabification

    Resyllabification occurs when a segment is reassigned across a word or morpheme boundary to a different syllable than it occupies in the citation form, typically so that a consonant at the end of one word becomes the onset of a syllable that begins the next word.

  • Citation "an apple," syllabified as /æn/ + /ˈæpəl/ → Connected [ə.ˈnæ.pəl] General American; the nasal resyllabifies as the onset of the following syllable in fluent speech, illustrating the syllable-boundary notation from Chapter 19.
    • Linking

    Linking is the absence of an expected break between two adjacent words or syllables, so that the boundary between them is not marked by a pause, glottal onset, or other discontinuity. The linking mark introduced in Chapter 22 for intonational purposes is reused here to mark this segmental-level connection.

  • [fɑːr‿əˈweɪ] "far away," a rhotic English variety; the final consonant links directly into the following vowel-initial word without a break.
    • Concept Check — These Processes Overlap

    Real connected speech frequently shows more than one process at once. A single phrase may show assimilation at one boundary and vowel reduction within the same word. Naming a single dominant process in a complex example, when more than one is genuinely present, is a common source of incomplete analysis; state every process you can support with evidence rather than settling for the first one you notice.

    Common Mistake

    Error: Marking every instance of assimilation, reduction, or deletion in every word of a transcript regardless of whether the surrounding context actually supports it, simply because the process is possible in the language.

    Correction: A process being possible in a language does not mean it occurs in every instance. Base each transcription decision on the specific evidence for that token, consistent with the broad-versus-narrow judgment developed in Chapter 24.

    Context and Variation

    Style and Rate Effects

    Purpose: show that connected-speech processes are not fixed properties of a phrase but vary systematically with speech style and rate.

    The same phrase, spoken by the same speaker, can show substantially different amounts of assimilation, reduction, deletion, or linking depending on speech style, ranging from careful, formal, or clearly enunciated speech to casual, fast, or intimate conversational speech. Formal registers, public reading, and speech directed at an unfamiliar listener tend to preserve more citation-form detail; casual, fast, and familiar registers tend to show more extensive connected-speech change.

    Rate interacts with style but is analytically separable from it: a speaker can produce careful speech quickly under time pressure, or casual speech slowly when relaxed. Because both factors affect the amount of connected-speech change, a transcription intended as evidence should document both the style and the approximate rate of the source material, consistent with the metadata requirements introduced for recording-based transcription in Chapter 27.

    Evidence Note — Connected Speech Is Not All-or-Nothing

    It is tempting to describe connected-speech processes as switches that are simply on in fast speech and off in careful speech. The evidence instead points to a continuum: many processes show intermediate, partial realizations at intermediate rates and styles, particularly for coarticulatory effects, which are gradient by nature. A responsible transcription acknowledges this continuum rather than forcing every token into one of two extreme categories.

    Across Languages

    Across Languages — Assimilation Is Not Only an English Pattern

    Nasal place assimilation, in which a nasal consonant adopts the place of articulation of a following consonant, is documented across a wide range of unrelated languages and is not a pattern unique to English. The specific consonants affected, the direction of the assimilation, and whether the process is optional or effectively obligatory in ordinary speech vary by language and by the specific analysis proposed for that language, so a general statement about "nasal assimilation" should not be treated as predicting the exact behavior of an untested language without further evidence.

    Across Languages — Liaison as a Language-Specific Linking Pattern

    Standard French is well documented as having a pattern often called liaison, in which a word-final consonant that is normally silent in isolation is pronounced when the following word begins with a vowel, linking the two words together in a way that partly resembles the resyllabification and linking processes described above. Liaison is governed by a mix of grammatical, lexical, and stylistic factors specific to French rather than being a simple universal linking rule, and its details are usually treated as requiring dedicated instruction in a French-specific course rather than full derivation from general phonetic principles alone.

    Across Languages — Epenthesis Driven by Phonotactic Restrictions

    Languages that restrict which consonant sequences may occur, particularly in onset or coda position as introduced in Chapter 19, frequently show epenthesis when a loanword, compound, or rapid-speech sequence would otherwise violate that restriction. The specific vowel or consonant inserted, and the exact contexts that trigger insertion, are language-specific facts that must be established from evidence for each language rather than assumed from a general tendency.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake

    Error: Describing connected speech as a corrupted or lazy version of careful, citation-form speech.

    Correction: Connected-speech processes are systematic, rule-governed, and present in every fluent speaker's ordinary speech. Careful and connected forms answer different descriptive questions rather than representing correct and incorrect versions of the same target.

    Common Mistake

    Error: Generalizing a connected-speech pattern documented for one variety, style, or speaker to an entire language without qualification.

    Correction: State the reference variety, style, and approximate rate for every connected-speech claim, consistent with the reference-variety policy applied throughout this book, and avoid presenting one speaker's casual register as representative of the whole language.

    Common Mistake

    Error: Treating every difference between a citation form and a recording as deletion, without considering assimilation, reduction, epenthesis, resyllabification, or linking as alternative explanations.

    Correction: Work through each named process before settling on an analysis, and prefer the explanation best supported by the specific articulatory and acoustic evidence available for that token.

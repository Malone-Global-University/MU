Describing Vowels

Height, backness, rounding, and the shift to a new articulatory framework

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Explain why vowels require a different descriptive framework from consonants and identify what makes that framework necessary.
  2. Define vowel height, backness, and rounding in articulatory terms and distinguish each dimension from the others.
  3. Distinguish lip rounding from vowel backness as two independently variable properties.
  4. Identify a steady-state vowel quality from a description and compare vowel qualities without reference to spelling.
  5. Use safe articulatory self-observation to explore the tongue, jaw, and lip contributions to vowel quality.
  6. Read and interpret a small set of anchor vowel symbols introduced in this chapter.
  7. Place supplied vowel descriptions at approximate positions in the vowel space.

An Observable Problem

Try this before reading on. Say the word beat and hold the vowel sound for a full second. Notice what your mouth is doing. Now say boot and hold that vowel. Then say bat. You probably noticed different jaw positions, different tongue locations, and different shapes of your lips across the three words.

Now try to describe those differences using the framework you have built for consonants. You know that consonants are produced at identifiable places in the vocal tract — bilabial, alveolar, velar — by specific kinds of constriction — stop, fricative, lateral. But none of those descriptions fit what you just did during the vowels in beat, boot, and bat. There was no complete closure, no turbulent airflow, no specific single point of contact. The vocal tract was essentially open throughout, and the difference between the three sounds was created by the overall shape of that open tract.

This is the central challenge of vowel phonetics: vowel quality is produced not by a specific constriction at a specific place, but by the configuration of the entire vocal tract simultaneously. Describing vowels requires a different framework — one that captures this overall shaping rather than pinpointing a single point of contact.

  1. Return to your three vowels — beat, boot, bat — and focus on just one thing: jaw position. Which vowel has the most open jaw? Which has the most closed jaw? Describe the jaw positions you observe without using the words "high" or "low" yet.
  2. Now focus only on your lips during beat versus boot. Describe any difference you notice. Then consider: could you round your lips during the vowel in beat without changing the tongue position? What does this suggest about the relationship between lip shape and tongue position?
A New Framework

Why the Consonant Framework Is Not Enough

Purpose: establish that the place-manner-voicing framework is insufficient for vowels and that a new set of dimensions is needed.

The framework built across Chapters 6 through 13 describes consonants through three dimensions: airstream and phonation, place of articulation, and manner of articulation. Place identifies where in the vocal tract the primary constriction occurs. Manner identifies what kind of constriction it is — complete closure, turbulent narrowing, lateral flow, and so on. Together, these two dimensions locate a specific articulatory event with enough precision to distinguish consonants from one another.

Vowels cannot be described the same way. The difficulty is not merely technical; it reflects a genuine difference in how vowels are produced. During a typical vowel, the vocal tract has no single dominant constriction — no point of contact between an active and passive articulator, no turbulence, no closure. The tongue is present and active, but it is shaping a resonating cavity rather than creating a specific bottleneck. The lips may be rounded or spread, contributing to the resonance. The jaw is open to varying degrees. The result is a sound defined by the shape of the entire tract at once, not by a single articulatory event at a single location.

This means that neither "place" nor "manner," as used for consonants, translates cleanly to vowels. A phonetician describing a vowel does not ask where the constriction is or what kind of constriction it is. Instead, the question becomes: what is the overall configuration of the vocal tract, and what acoustic resonances does that configuration produce?

To answer these questions systematically, vowel phonetics uses three primary descriptive dimensions: height, backness, and rounding. These are not the only properties of vowels — later chapters will add length, nasalization, rhoticity, and phonation type — but they are the foundational dimensions that organize the central part of the IPA vowel chart and provide the basis for comparing vowels within and across languages.

Concept Check — One Framework, Different Tools

Switching from consonants to vowels does not mean abandoning the IPA framework. The commitment to articulatory description, phonemic versus phonetic distinction, evidence-based transcription, and cross-language comparison remains exactly the same. What changes is the specific set of dimensions used to make those descriptions. Just as the three-part consonant classification (airstream/phonation, place, manner) gave you a consistent vocabulary for describing any consonant in any language, the three dimensions of height, backness, and rounding give you a consistent vocabulary for describing any vowel. The method is the same; the descriptive tools are adapted to the task.

The Open Vocal Tract

Purpose: establish the basic articulatory conditions for vowel production and identify the structures that shape vowel quality.

Vowels are produced with a relatively open vocal tract. The key structures involved in shaping vowel quality are the jaw, the tongue body, and the lips. The larynx also plays a role — vowels are typically produced with modal voicing, though phonation type can vary — and the velum is normally raised, directing air through the oral cavity rather than through the nose. These conditions define what phoneticians call a sonorant vocal-tract configuration: one that produces voiced sound through a freely resonating, unconstricted airway.

Of the three main shaping structures, the tongue body is the most important for vowel quality. The tongue is a large, muscular organ that can shift its bulk upward and downward relative to the roof of the mouth and forward and backward in the oral cavity. These two independent movements — vertical and horizontal — correspond directly to the two most important vowel dimensions: height and backness. The tongue body is not a single point, and vowel quality is not determined by a single tongue tip or tongue root position in the way that consonant place can be localized. Rather, it is the overall elevated or lowered, fronted or retracted mass of the tongue that shapes the oral cavity and determines the acoustic resonances the listener perceives as a particular vowel quality.

The jaw moves in coordination with the tongue: when the tongue body lowers, the jaw typically opens, giving the tongue more room. When the tongue body raises, the jaw may close. Because jaw and tongue move together much of the time, vowel descriptions sometimes use jaw aperture — open or close — interchangeably with tongue height — low or high. Both describe the same articulatory configuration from a different vantage point. This chapter uses the more common IPA-aligned terminology of height (high, mid, low) while acknowledging that some descriptions use aperture (close, mid-close, mid-open, open) instead.

The lips can be rounded — brought forward and into a circular or oval shape — or spread or neutral. Lip configuration is largely independent of tongue position. A speaker can round the lips during a front vowel or spread them during a back vowel; these combinations produce sounds that are used as distinct phonemes in some languages, even if they feel unfamiliar to English speakers. This independence is exactly why rounding is treated as a separate descriptive dimension rather than as a byproduct of tongue position.

Resonance and Vowel Quality

Purpose: give learners an accessible account of how the vocal tract's shape produces the acoustic differences they hear as distinct vowel qualities.

When the vocal folds vibrate, they produce a complex sound containing energy at many frequencies simultaneously. The shape of the vocal tract above the larynx acts like a set of interconnected resonators: it amplifies certain frequency bands and damps others. The frequencies that are amplified are called formants, and they are numbered from lowest to highest — F1, F2, F3, and so on. The first two formants, F1 and F2, are the primary acoustic correlates of vowel quality and are what listeners use to tell one vowel from another.

The connection between articulation and formants is direct and predictable. F1 is inversely related to vowel height: when the tongue body is high and the jaw is relatively closed, F1 is low. When the tongue body is low and the jaw is open, F1 is high. F2 is related to vowel backness and rounding: when the tongue body is fronted, F2 is high; when it is retracted, F2 is lower. Lip rounding also lowers F2 (as well as F1 and F3) by lengthening the acoustic tube in front of the tongue. These relationships are why height, backness, and rounding are the standard descriptive dimensions: they correspond to the physical properties of the vocal tract that most strongly shape what listeners perceive as vowel quality.

Chapter 28 will introduce waveforms and spectrograms in more detail. For now, the key idea is that vowel quality is an acoustic property produced by tract shape, and the three articulatory dimensions — height, backness, rounding — are the most consistent predictors of that acoustic quality across speakers and languages.

Evidence Note — Why We Use Both Articulatory and Acoustic Description

Articulatory descriptions of vowels are practical and accessible, but they have limits. The tongue body is not a single point, speakers differ in the exact position that produces a given quality, and direct observation of the tongue during speech requires equipment most learners do not have. Acoustic descriptions through formant frequencies are more precise and more directly tied to what the listener perceives, but they require measurement tools and expertise to interpret. For practical phonetics work — learning to hear, produce, and transcribe vowels — articulatory description gives you a workable first approximation that you can calibrate with your own vocal tract. Acoustic evidence, when available, provides verification. The two descriptions complement each other: use both where possible, and be transparent about which one you are working from.

The Three Dimensions

Height

Purpose: define vowel height as the vertical dimension of tongue-body position and introduce the major height categories with articulatory anchors.

Vowel height refers to the vertical position of the tongue body during a vowel. When the tongue body is raised toward the roof of the mouth — and the jaw is relatively closed — the vowel is described as high or close. When the tongue body is lowered — and the jaw is more open — the vowel is described as low or open. Between these extremes are the mid vowels, which divide into mid-high (close-mid) and mid-low (open-mid) in the IPA system.

The IPA chart uses four main height rows: close, close-mid, open-mid, and open. This chapter uses the terms high, mid-high, mid-low, and low as accessible alternatives. Either set of terms is acceptable; what matters is consistent use within a given description.

Vowel height categories: articulatory description and IPA chart label
Height term (this chapter) IPA chart label Articulatory description Jaw aperture

Mid-high Close-mid Tongue body raised above mid, but not to maximum height Somewhat closed
Mid-low Open-mid Tongue body lowered below mid, but not to maximum openness Somewhat open

Height is a continuum, not a set of discrete steps. The four categories are useful analytical divisions, not fixed anatomical positions. Languages divide the height continuum differently: some distinguish only two height levels (high and low); others distinguish three, four, or more. When working with a specific language's vowel system, the height labels describe the contrasts within that system rather than absolute tongue positions measured in millimeters.

Concept Check — High Vowels and Consonant-Like Sounds

At the very top of the height dimension, the tongue body is raised high enough that the distinction between a high vowel and a glide consonant like [j] or [w] becomes a matter of syllabic function rather than a simple articulatory difference. The vowel [i] and the glide [j] involve very similar tongue positions; what distinguishes them primarily is duration and their role in the syllable — whether they occupy the nucleus or a marginal position. This boundary between high vowels and palatal or labial-velar glides will be explored further in Chapters 17 and 19. For now, the important point is that "high" does not mean "any tongue position that feels high" — it means the tongue body is raised without creating turbulent narrowing or consonantal closure.

Backness

Purpose: define backness as the horizontal dimension of tongue-body position and clarify that it is independent of height.

Vowel backness refers to the horizontal position of the tongue body — specifically, whether the body of the tongue is positioned toward the front of the oral cavity, toward the back, or somewhere in between. IPA vowel descriptions use three main backness categories: front, central, and back.

In a front vowel, the tongue body is advanced toward the hard palate, occupying the front portion of the oral cavity. In a back vowel, the tongue body is retracted toward the velum and pharynx. In a central vowel, the tongue body occupies a roughly intermediate horizontal position — neither fully advanced nor fully retracted.

Height and backness are largely independent of each other. A vowel can be high and front, high and back, low and front, low and back, and any combination in between. This independence is what gives the vowel space its two-dimensional character and is why the IPA vowel chart uses both a vertical axis (height) and a horizontal axis (backness). The following table illustrates how the two dimensions combine:

Height and backness as independent dimensions — illustrative combinations
Height Front Central Back
High (close) High front: tongue body high and forward High central: tongue body high and mid-horizontal High back: tongue body high and retracted
Mid-high (close-mid) Mid-high front Mid-high central Mid-high back
Mid-low (open-mid) Mid-low front Mid-low central Mid-low back
Low (open) Low front: tongue body low and forward Low central Low back: tongue body low and retracted

The horizontal dimension of tongue position also has acoustic consequences. Front vowels tend to have a higher F2 (second formant) because the constriction formed by the tongue body at the front of the mouth shortens the front cavity and lengthens the back cavity behind the tongue. Back vowels have a lower F2. This acoustic relationship means that F2 is the primary acoustic indicator of frontness versus backness — a fact that becomes useful when reading spectrograms in Chapter 28.

Rounding

Purpose: establish lip rounding as a third independent dimension of vowel description, demonstrate its independence from backness, and provide cross-language examples.

Rounding refers to the configuration of the lips during a vowel. A rounded vowel is produced with the lips brought forward and shaped into a circular or oval opening — a gesture sometimes called protrusion or labialization. An unrounded vowel is produced with the lips spread, retracted, or in a neutral position. In English, rounding is closely associated with back vowels: the vowels of boot and bought are back and rounded, while front vowels like the vowel in beat or bat are front and unrounded. This association is real in English but not universal.

The crucial analytical point is that rounding is independent of backness. Languages can and do use all four combinations: front rounded, front unrounded, back rounded, and back unrounded. English happens to pair frontness with unrounding and backness with rounding almost without exception, which is why English-speaking learners often initially find front rounded vowels or back unrounded vowels surprising or difficult to hear as distinct from their familiar partners. They are distinct, and the distinction is regular and systematic in many of the world's languages.

Rounding and backness: all four combinations with illustrative examples
Backness Unrounded Rounded
Front Front unrounded: tongue body fronted, lips spread or neutral. The vowel in beat (General American English) is approximately high front unrounded. Front rounded: tongue body fronted, lips rounded. Found as a phoneme in French ([y] as in tu), German ([y] as in über), and Mandarin Chinese.
Back Back unrounded: tongue body retracted, lips spread or neutral. Found in many languages; the vowel [ɯ] in Turkish and Korean is high back unrounded. Back rounded: tongue body retracted, lips rounded. The vowel in boot (General American English) is approximately high back rounded.

From an articulatory standpoint, rounding works by extending the length of the acoustic tube in front of the main tongue constriction. When the lips protrude forward, the resonating cavity in front of the tongue is effectively lengthened, which lowers formant frequencies. This is why rounded vowels tend to sound fuller or darker than their unrounded counterparts at the same height and backness — the acoustic quality shifts downward across the formant pattern.

Across Languages — Front Rounded Vowels in French and German

The front rounded vowels of French and German are among the most commonly cited examples of a rounding-backness combination that English does not use phonemically. In French, the word tu (you, informal) contains the high front rounded vowel [y]. A reliable approach to approximating this sound is to start from the high front unrounded vowel [i] — similar to the vowel in English beat — and then add lip rounding without moving the tongue body forward or backward. If the result sounds like the vowel in English boot, the tongue has shifted back; keep the tongue in the [i] position and round only the lips. German uses the same vowel in words like über (above, over) and represents it with the spelling ⟨ü⟩ or ⟨ue⟩. These orthographic representations are informative for the specific language but should not be confused with a universal phonetic description — the IPA symbol [y] provides the cross-linguistically consistent label.

Phonation in Vowels

Purpose: briefly establish that vowels interact with phonation type and that modal voicing is the default, while noting that other phonation types affect vowel quality.

The three primary dimensions — height, backness, rounding — describe the shape of the vocal tract above the larynx. But the larynx itself also contributes to vowel quality through phonation type. Chapter 6 introduced the range of phonation types from breathy to creaky (pressed) voice, with modal voicing as the unmarked baseline. The same range applies to vowels.

In most languages, vowels have modal voicing as their default phonation: the vocal folds vibrate regularly, producing the periodic sound associated with a clear, neutral voice quality. Some languages use phonation type — most commonly breathy voice or creaky voice — as a phonemically distinctive property of vowels, contrasting otherwise identical vowels that differ only in laryngeal setting. Breathy voice (also called murmur) involves a partially open glottis during the vowel, producing a perceptible breathiness alongside the vowel quality. Creaky voice involves a tightly adducted glottis that produces an irregular, low-pitched creak.

This chapter treats modal voicing as the default and phonation-type differences as a dimension to be revisited when vowel systems with contrastive phonation are analyzed later in the course. The important point for now is that height, backness, and rounding describe the supralaryngeal contribution to vowel quality — the laryngeal contribution requires separate description using phonation terminology from Chapter 6.

Combining the Three Dimensions

Purpose: show how the three dimensions work together in a full vowel description and establish the convention for stating them.

A complete articulatory description of a vowel states all three primary dimensions plus phonation type when it is relevant. The conventional order in IPA descriptions is:

height — backness — rounding (— phonation if marked)

So a full description might read: high front unrounded, or mid-low back rounded, or high front rounded. These descriptions are stated in articulatory terms independently of any language — they describe the vocal-tract configuration, not the language that uses the vowel or the symbol used to represent it. The IPA symbol then provides a conventional shorthand for that description.

Some descriptions add modal voice to make the phonation explicit, particularly when contrasting modal vowels with breathily or creakily voiced counterparts. When no phonation qualifier is given, modal voicing is assumed. The description applies to a steady-state vowel quality — the stable portion of the vowel, held constant, as opposed to a vowel whose quality changes during production (those will be addressed in Chapter 17 on diphthongs).

Notation

Anchor Vowel Symbols

Purpose: introduce a small set of vowel IPA symbols as anchors for the vowel space, establishing the link between articulatory description and notation without overloading learners with the full chart.

The IPA chart includes a large number of vowel symbols organized in the vowel quadrilateral — a roughly trapezoidal arrangement that maps height (vertical) against backness (horizontal). Chapter 15 will explore the full chart organization in detail. This chapter introduces a small anchor set: eight symbols that mark the major regions of the vowel space and provide reference points for the descriptions above. These eight symbols are the most important for building initial vowel-space literacy; the full chart adds additional symbols for less central positions, rounded counterparts of front vowels, and symbols for central vowels at various heights.

The anchor symbols introduced productively in this chapter are:

Any language that uses a vowel described with one of these symbols will realize that vowel within a range around the reference value, not necessarily at the exact target position. The symbol is a label for the phonological category and its approximate phonetic value, not a claim about a single precise tongue position.

Two typographic cautions are necessary immediately. First, distinguish a (low front unrounded) from ɑ (low back unrounded). These two symbols look similar in many fonts but represent genuinely different vowel qualities that contrast phonemically in some languages. The symbol ɑ has a distinctive shape compared with the ordinary letter ⟨a⟩ — it has no top hook or serif loop. Always verify which symbol is intended when working with low vowel transcriptions. Second, distinguish ə (schwa, mid central) from ɐ (near-open central), which appears in some transcriptions and will be introduced as a receptive symbol later in the course. These are different vowels.

Spelling Trap — Letters Are Not Vowel Descriptions

English writing uses five vowel letters — ⟨a⟩, ⟨e⟩, ⟨i⟩, ⟨o⟩, ⟨u⟩ — to represent a much larger set of vowel sounds. The letter ⟨a⟩ alone represents at least five audibly different vowel qualities across standard English varieties: the vowel in cat, the vowel in father, the vowel in cake, the vowel in about (unstressed), and the vowel in all in some accents. These are not one vowel with minor variation; they are genuinely different vowel qualities that the IPA would represent with different symbols.

Conversely, the vowel quality [i] — high front unrounded — is spelled ⟨ee⟩ in feet, ⟨ea⟩ in beat, ⟨e⟩ in be, ⟨ie⟩ in believe, ⟨ei⟩ in receive⟩, and ⟨i⟩ in machine. Six different spellings for substantially the same vowel quality.

The IPA vowel symbols do not correspond to English vowel letters. The symbol a is not the letter ⟨a⟩. The symbol i is not the letter ⟨i⟩. Each IPA symbol has its own articulatory definition — height, backness, rounding — which is fixed regardless of how any language spells sounds in that region of the vowel space. When you see a vowel IPA symbol, read it as an articulatory description, not as a familiar letter.

Practice and Review

Guided Observation

These observations use your own vocal tract as the instrument. Work slowly and repeat each vowel several times before noting what you feel. Do not strain or force; the goal is awareness, not performance.

Observation 1: Height. Slowly alternate between two vowel sounds: the vowel in beat and the vowel in bat (or, if your accent merges these, try the vowel in beet and the vowel in bad). Hold each for a second before switching. Focus only on your jaw. Notice that the jaw opens more for the lower vowel. Now place your finger gently below your chin and repeat the alternation — you may feel the jaw and tongue lowering together on the lower vowel. This is the height dimension in action: the tongue body and jaw descend together as height decreases (the vowel becomes lower / more open).

Observation 2: Backness. Slowly alternate between the vowels in beat and boot. Try to keep your jaw position roughly constant (both vowels have a relatively high tongue body, so the jaw can remain fairly closed for both). Notice where the tongue body seems to be located in the horizontal plane. The vowel in beat should feel more forward — you may feel the tongue bunching toward the front of the roof of the mouth. The vowel in boot should feel more retracted — the tongue body moves back toward the velum. This horizontal shift is the backness dimension.

Observation 3: Rounding independently of backness. Produce the vowel in beat (high front). Hold it and, without moving your tongue, slowly round your lips — bring them forward as if beginning to whistle. The result will be an unusual vowel quality: something like the French or German vowel [y]. If the quality suddenly switches to sound like the vowel in boot, your tongue has shifted back — return it to the front position and try rounding the lips alone. This observation demonstrates that rounding and backness are independent: you can add rounding to a front vowel without making it a back vowel.

Observation 4: Low vowels. Say the word spa or father slowly and hold the vowel. Notice how open the jaw is compared with the vowels in Observations 1-3. The tongue body is at or near its lowest position and there is relatively little rounding. Now, keeping the same low tongue position, try fronting the tongue — moving it forward toward the front teeth while keeping the jaw open. This fronted low vowel is in the region of [a]. Retracting back from that position approximates [ɑ]. The two low vowels differ in backness but share a low height.

  1. Based on Observations 1 and 2, describe the difference between [i] and [ɛ] in terms of height and backness only. Both are front unrounded vowels — which is higher?
  2. Based on Observation 3, explain why [y] is described as high front rounded rather than as high back rounded. What evidence from your own articulation supports this?
  3. Consider the vowel [ɔ] (mid-low back rounded). Based on the dimensions discussed in this chapter, describe what you would expect the tongue body and lips to be doing during this vowel.

Vowel Dimensions Across Languages

Purpose: demonstrate that height, backness, and rounding are cross-linguistically applicable and that languages differ in how they deploy these dimensions as phonemic contrasts.

The three dimensions introduced in this chapter are not properties of English. They are articulatory properties of the human vocal tract that any vowel-producing language must use. What differs across languages is how the vowel space is partitioned — how many distinctions are made along each dimension, which corners and regions of the space are used phonemically, and how rounding interacts with frontness and backness.

Across Languages — Spanish and Japanese: Small Symmetrical Systems

Spanish (most varieties) and Japanese have five phonemic vowel categories, conventionally transcribed as /i e a o u/. These five vowels are distributed at the corners and midpoints of the vowel space: two high vowels (front unrounded /i/ and back rounded /u/), two mid vowels (mid-low front unrounded /e/ and mid-low back rounded /o/), and one low vowel (low central or front unrounded /a/). Neither language has phonemically distinctive front rounded vowels or back unrounded vowels at high or mid heights. The system is described as "symmetrical" in the sense that the five vowels are distributed fairly evenly across the vowel space, though the phonetic realization of each category varies by speaker, dialect, and context — the phonemic label tells you the contrastive category, not the precise phonetic target.

Across Languages — Turkish: A System Using Back Unrounded Vowels

Turkish has eight phonemic vowel categories organized systematically along all three dimensions. In addition to the familiar high front unrounded /i/ and high back rounded /u/, Turkish has a high front rounded vowel /y/ and a high back unrounded vowel /ɯ/. The vowel [ɯ] — sometimes called the barred i — involves a high tongue body in the back position with unrounded lips. It is the back counterpart to [i] without rounding, and it sounds quite different from [u]. Turkish uses this vowel and its mid-height counterpart systematically in a harmony system that will be discussed in Chapter 18. The Turkish example demonstrates that back unrounded vowels are a real and productive category in human language, not an articulatory oddity — and that the independence of backness and rounding is phonologically real, not just a theoretical claim.

Variation Note — English Vowel Systems Differ Substantially Across Accents

English vowel systems vary more across regional accents than consonant systems do, which is why English examples for vowels should be used with particular care. The vowel in trap ranges from a low front unrounded quality in some accents to a near-mid central quality in others. The distinction between the vowels of caught and cot — maintained in some accents and merged in others — involves both height and backness differences. General American English is used as the primary reference variety throughout this book, but references to General American English vowels are always approximate starting points, not definitions. If your own accent produces a different vowel quality in a supplied English keyword, use your accent's realization as the starting point for articulatory exploration and label it accordingly.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake

Error: Describing a vowel's quality by naming an English keyword, such as "it sounds like the vowel in boot," as if that were a phonetic definition.

Correction: An English keyword is a rough orientation aid, not a definition. The vowel in boot differs across English accents in height, backness, and rounding, and in any given English accent it may not match the IPA reference value for [u]. A phonetic description states height, backness, and rounding: high back rounded. If you want to add an English keyword as an initial guide, frame it as approximate: "approximately the vowel in boot for General American English speakers — high back rounded." Always lead with the articulatory description.

Common Mistake

Error: Treating rounding as a consequence of backness — assuming that back vowels are always rounded and front vowels are always unrounded.

Correction: In English, backness and rounding happen to co-occur consistently, which can create the impression that they are the same property. They are not. Turkish [ɯ] is high back and unrounded; French and German [y] is high front and rounded. Both are well-attested phonemes in major world languages. Rounding and backness must be stated separately in every vowel description because they are separately variable dimensions of vowel production.

Common Mistake

Error: Confusing the IPA symbol a (low front unrounded) with the IPA symbol ɑ (low back unrounded) because they look similar in some fonts.

Correction: The symbol a — which looks like a standard roman letter a in most IPA fonts — represents a low front unrounded vowel. The symbol ɑ — which has a distinctive curled shape without the top hook — represents a low back unrounded vowel. They sound different and are phonemically distinct in several languages. In IPA fonts like Charis SIL or Doulos SIL, the distinction is visually clear; verify rendering carefully. When transcribing, always specify which symbol you intend by stating the articulatory description alongside it.

Common Mistake

Error: Reading the vowel-space diagram as if it were an anatomical map of the tongue — as if each plotted point represented the precise tip or highest point of the tongue inside the mouth.

Correction: The vowel quadrilateral is a phonetic diagram, not a radiograph of the vocal tract. Its axes represent height and backness as descriptive dimensions; its shape reflects auditory and acoustic relationships among vowels as much as articulatory ones. The position of a symbol in the diagram indicates its approximate descriptive location relative to other vowels, not the measured millimeter position of any particular tongue surface. Chapter 15 will explain the quadrilateral's organization and limits in more detail.

Chapter Review — Part A

1. Consonants are defined by a specific constriction at a specific place — a localized articulatory event. Vowels are produced with a relatively open vocal tract and no single dominant constriction; their quality is determined by the overall configuration of the entire vocal tract simultaneously. Because there is no single place of maximum constriction, "place" and "manner" in the consonant sense do not apply. A different framework — capturing the overall tract shape — is required.   2. Height: the vertical position of the tongue body (how high or low it is, correlated with jaw aperture). Backness: the horizontal position of the tongue body (how far forward or back it is). Rounding: the configuration of the lips (rounded versus unrounded). These three capture independent aspects of supralaryngeal configuration and correspond to the acoustic properties — primarily F1 and F2 — that define vowel quality perception.   3. When the tongue body raises, the jaw typically closes in coordination; when the tongue body lowers, the jaw opens. Both movements produce the same effect on the oral cavity shape. "Height" describes the tongue body's vertical position; "aperture" describes the jaw opening from the observer's external perspective. Both terms refer to the same underlying articulatory event from different vantage points.   4. Rounding and backness are independently controlled by different articulators — lips versus tongue body — and languages show all four combinations in use. French and German use high front rounded [y]; Turkish uses high back unrounded [ɯ]. Accept either or both as examples.   5. A steady-state vowel is one whose quality is held constant during its duration — the articulators are in a stable configuration and the acoustic resonances are not changing. This is the target for initial vowel description because it isolates the quality being described from any dynamic change (which characterizes diphthongs and transitions).

1. The description is wrong because it places [y] in the back region of the vowel space ("a back vowel like [u]"). [y] is a front vowel — its tongue position is the same as [i], not [u]. What [y] shares with [u] is rounding, not backness. Corrected description: [y] is a high front rounded vowel. It differs from [i] only in rounding (which it adds) and from [u] only in backness ([y] is front; [u] is back).   2. Vowel A (i, high front unrounded) is in the anchor set. Vowel B (high back unrounded) is [ɯ], which is not in the Chapter 14 anchor set because English does not use this vowel phonemically and it therefore is not needed at this stage. Its absence from the English system — and from the anchor set — illustrates that English pairs backness with rounding so consistently that high back unrounded vowels are not used; languages like Turkish show this combination is entirely possible. The anchor set reflects a pedagogically useful starting subset of the full vowel chart, not a claim that unlisted vowels do not exist.   3. Spanish /i/: high front unrounded (anchor i); /e/: mid-high or mid-low front unrounded — the Spanish vowel is typically described as mid-high (close-mid), so the closest anchor is ɛ but the Spanish realization may be somewhat higher; /a/: low central or front unrounded (anchor a or ɑ depending on variety); /o/: mid-high or mid-low back rounded — again often described as close-mid, so closest to ɔ but potentially higher; /u/: high back rounded (anchor u). Accept principled description of this variability — the phonemic label and the phonetic realization may diverge within a range.

Cumulative Review

1. During the consonant [d], the tongue tip contacts the alveolar ridge to form a complete closure — a localized event between specific active and passive articulators. During a vowel, the tongue body moves as a whole, shaping the entire oral cavity without creating any complete closure or turbulence. The relevant articulatory variable shifts from "where does the tongue make contact" to "how is the tongue body positioned in the overall three-dimensional space of the vocal tract." This requires describing the overall shape (high/low, front/back) rather than pinpointing a contact location.   2. A phoneme /i/ is a contrastive category — the mental, abstract unit that distinguishes words from each other. Individual phones [i] are the physical realizations of that category; they may vary slightly across speakers, dialects, and contexts in precise tongue height, precise backness, or exact formant values. If the variation is predictable (conditioned by context) the different phones are allophones of the same phoneme. To evaluate the claim, you would need to compare minimal pairs across varieties — if words are distinguished by the vowel in the varieties in question — and measure or auditorily compare the phonetic realizations to see whether the same phonemic category maps to different phonetic values across speakers.   3. Articulatory constants: height (high), backness (front), and rounding (unrounded) would all remain the same — the supralaryngeal configuration does not change. What would change is the phonation: instead of regular, periodic vocal-fold vibration, the folds vibrate irregularly with a low, crackling quality. Acoustically, the fundamental frequency would drop and become irregular; the harmonic energy above the fundamental would be reduced; and the periodicity visible in a waveform or spectrogram would be disrupted. Perceptually, the listener would still perceive a front, high vowel quality (because F1 and F2 reflect the tract shape, which is unchanged) but with a creaky, constricted voice color overlaid on that quality.

Places of Articulation

Where active and passive articulators meet to shape a consonant

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Identify the major places of articulation used to classify pulmonic consonants.
  2. Distinguish adjacent places of articulation using articulatory evidence rather than guesswork.
  3. Locate place categories along the columns of the official IPA consonant chart.
  4. Classify verified consonant examples by place.
  5. Compare similar consonants across languages without claiming that a shared place label guarantees identical realization.
  6. Produce a controlled set of place contrasts safely, using concrete articulatory cues.

An Observable Problem

Say the English words tip, chip, and kip slowly, paying attention only to where your tongue or lips make contact for the first consonant — not to the letters t, ch, and k. You should be able to feel that the closure happens in three noticeably different places: just behind your upper front teeth, somewhat farther back along the roof of your mouth, and farther back still, near the soft palate.

Now say tip and dip. Here the closure happens in the same place both times, yet English spelling assigns these two consonants completely different letters, while chip's consonant — articulated farther back than tip's — gets a two-letter digraph that gives no hint of where in the mouth it is produced. Spelling, once again, is not a reliable guide to articulation. This chapter gives you a systematic, language-independent vocabulary for describing exactly where in the vocal tract a consonant is made.

  1. Before reading further, say tip, chip, and kip aloud three times each and write one sentence describing, in your own words and without using letter names, how the place of closure changes from front to back.
Core Concept

Active and Passive Articulators

Purpose: establish place of articulation as a relationship between a moving structure and a target structure, not a single fixed point.

Every consonant place of articulation names a relationship between two structures introduced in Chapter 4: an active articulator, which moves toward or touches another structure, and a passive articulator, which is approached or touched but does not itself initiate the movement. The lower lip and the various parts of the tongue are the active articulators used in pulmonic consonant production; the upper lip, the teeth, and the regions of the roof of the mouth serve as passive articulators.

A place label such as alveolar names the passive articulator — the alveolar ridge, just behind the upper front teeth. The active articulator is usually identifiable from the place label together with the consonant's broader description, and is stated explicitly whenever precision matters, since more than one active articulator can in principle reach the same passive target.

Concept Check

Place of articulation answers the question "where," not "how." A complete consonant description eventually adds manner (Chapter 8, "how") and voicing (Chapter 6, already introduced), but this chapter isolates place so that you can locate and compare it reliably before combining it with the other two dimensions.

Place on the IPA Chart

Purpose: connect articulatory description to chart navigation skills built in Chapter 3.

On the official IPA pulmonic consonant chart, places of articulation are arranged as columns, running from the front of the vocal tract on the left to the back of the vocal tract on the right. Manners of articulation, previewed briefly here and developed fully in Chapter 8, are arranged as rows. A consonant symbol's position in the chart is determined by the intersection of its place column and manner row.

Notice that the chart moves smoothly from front to back rather than jumping unpredictably. This means that once you can reliably order a handful of places from front to back in your own mouth, you already have a working map of the chart's horizontal organization.

Bilabial and Labiodental Place

Purpose: establish the two front-most places, both involving the lips.

A bilabial consonant is produced with the two lips. A labiodental consonant is produced with the lower lip approaching or touching the upper teeth.

  • [b], [m]bilabial — both lips, as in English bee, me
  • [f], [v]labiodental — lower lip against upper teeth, as in English fee, vee

These two places are easy to confuse by ear at first because both involve the lips, but they are simple to distinguish by sight and by touch: bilabial consonants involve lip-to-lip contact, while labiodental consonants involve lip-to-teeth contact.

Dental, Alveolar, and Postalveolar Place

Purpose: distinguish three closely spaced places that the tongue tip or blade can reach.

A dental consonant is produced with the tongue tip or blade at or near the upper teeth. An alveolar consonant is produced at the alveolar ridge, the firm ridge directly behind the upper front teeth. A postalveolar consonant is produced just behind the alveolar ridge, where the ridge begins to curve upward toward the hard palate.

  • [θ], [ð]dental — tongue tip near the upper teeth, as in English thin, this
  • [t], [d], [n], [s], [z]alveolar — tongue tip or blade at the alveolar ridge, as in English tea, day, knee, sea, zee
  • [ʃ], [ʒ]postalveolar — tongue blade just behind the alveolar ridge, as in English she, the middle consonant of measure
Evidence Note

The exact boundary between dental and alveolar contact varies somewhat by speaker and by language, and some reference traditions group dental and alveolar sounds together as a single broader category when finer distinction is not needed. This chapter treats them as separate places because the distinction is contrastive in several languages used elsewhere in this book; the appropriate level of detail always depends on the analytical task, consistent with the transcription-purpose principle introduced in Chapter 5.

Retroflex and Palatal Place

Purpose: introduce two places that are common across the world's languages but rare or absent in many English varieties.

A retroflex consonant is produced with the tongue tip curled backward toward the hard palate, just behind the alveolar ridge. A palatal consonant is produced with the front or body of the tongue raised toward the hard palate itself.

  • [ʈ], [ɖ], [ɳ]retroflex stops and nasal — found in many languages of South Asia, among others; not part of most English consonant inventories
  • [c], [ɲ]palatal stop and nasal — the palatal nasal occurs in languages such as Spanish (orthographic ⟨ñ⟩) and French (orthographic ⟨gn⟩)

English does not contrast a retroflex series the way some South Asian languages do, and many English varieties lack a true palatal stop or palatal nasal as a distinct phoneme. This does not mean English speakers are physically unable to produce these sounds — it means English does not assign them a contrastive role in its phoneme inventory, a distinction first introduced in Chapter 5 and revisited throughout this book.

Velar and Uvular Place

Purpose: move to the back of the oral cavity and distinguish two places that are easy to confuse without careful observation.

A velar consonant is produced with the back of the tongue against or near the soft palate, or velum. A uvular consonant is produced farther back still, with the tongue back approaching the uvula, the small soft structure that hangs at the rear of the velum.

  • [k], [ɡ], [ŋ]velar — tongue back at the soft palate, as in English key, go, the final consonant of sing
  • [q], [ɢ], [χ]uvular stops and fricative — found in languages such as Arabic and several Indigenous languages of the Americas and the Caucasus; not part of most English consonant inventories
Common Error

Error: Assuming that any "back of the throat" consonant must be velar, including sounds that are actually uvular, pharyngeal, or glottal.

Correction: "Back of the mouth" is not precise enough for phonetic analysis. Velar, uvular, pharyngeal, and glottal places are physically distinct locations, each with its own symbol set on the IPA chart, and a careful description identifies which one is actually involved rather than defaulting to the most familiar label.

Pharyngeal and Glottal Place

Purpose: complete the front-to-back place inventory at an overview level appropriate to this stage.

A pharyngeal consonant is produced by constricting the pharynx, the tube-like cavity behind the oral cavity. An epiglottal consonant, closely related and treated here at the same overview level, involves the epiglottis. A glottal consonant is produced at the glottis itself, the space between the vocal folds, with no oral constriction at all.

  • [ħ], [ʕ]pharyngeal fricatives — found in Arabic and other languages with a pharyngeal series; introduced here for recognition
  • [h], [ʔ]glottal fricative and glottal stop — [h] occurs in English hat; [ʔ] occurs in English in some varieties as a realization of /t/, and as a full phoneme in many other languages

This chapter introduces pharyngeal, epiglottal, and glottal place at the level needed to locate them on the chart and recognize them in supplied examples. Detailed production work with pharyngeal and epiglottal consonants is deferred, since they require coordination that benefits from the additional airstream background provided in Chapter 13.

Across Languages: Place Is Not Identity

Purpose: prevent the assumption that a shared place label guarantees an identical sound.

Across Languages

Calling two consonants "alveolar" in two different languages does not mean they sound identical. The exact contact area, the shape of the tongue, and accompanying details such as aspiration or rounding can all differ between languages, and sometimes between dialects of the same language, even when both fall under the same place label. The place label tells you where the constriction is broadly located, not the full phonetic detail of the sound. Treat shared place terminology as a starting point for comparison, not as proof that two consonants are interchangeable, a principle developed further in the cross-linguistic comparison work later in this book.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake

Error: Naming place of articulation using only the active articulator, for example calling every tongue-tip consonant "alveolar" regardless of where contact actually occurs.

Correction: The tongue tip can reach several different passive articulators — teeth, alveolar ridge, postalveolar region, or the area just behind it for retroflex consonants. Place names the passive articulator together with the active one; identify both before assigning a label.

Common Mistake

Error: Assuming that every language's consonant inventory uses the same set of places as English.

Correction: Many languages use places that are rare or absent in most English varieties, including retroflex, uvular, and pharyngeal places, while other languages use a narrower range of places than English does. Neither pattern is more natural or more correct than the other.

Common Mistake

Error: Treating place of articulation as if it alone fully described a consonant.

Correction: Place answers only "where." A complete consonant description also requires manner, the subject of Chapter 8, and voicing or phonation, introduced in Chapter 6. Avoid describing a consonant as "just an alveolar sound" without specifying these additional dimensions when precision matters.

Chapter Review — Part A (model answers)

1. The active articulator is the moving structure, typically the lower lip or a part of the tongue; the passive articulator is the structure approached or touched, typically the upper lip, teeth, or a region of the roof of the mouth. A place label identifies the passive articulator and, when needed for precision, the active articulator as well.   2. Bilabial, labiodental, dental, alveolar, postalveolar, retroflex, palatal, velar, uvular, glottal (pharyngeal and epiglottal are addressed at an overview level between uvular and glottal).   3. Dental: tongue tip or blade at the upper teeth, as in [θ]. Alveolar: tongue tip or blade at the alveolar ridge, as in [t]. Postalveolar: tongue blade just behind the ridge, as in [ʃ].

Chapter Review — Part B

1. [m] bilabial; [v] labiodental; [n] alveolar; [ŋ] velar.   2. [ʈ] is retroflex, produced with the tongue tip curled back toward the hard palate behind the alveolar ridge, while [t] is alveolar, produced with the tongue tip at the alveolar ridge itself. Both use the tongue tip as the active articulator, but the passive articulator, and therefore the place, is different.

Chapter Review — Part C

1. The claim is not well supported. Auditory similarity is not sufficient evidence of phonetic identity. Arabic [χ] is specifically uvular, while the fricative many English speakers use in borrowed forms like Bach may be produced farther forward, closer to velar place, depending on the speaker. Without articulatory or acoustic evidence confirming the exact place, the two should not be assumed identical; they should be described and compared using the place terminology from this chapter rather than by auditory impression alone.

Cumulative Review

1. A change in place of articulation alone does not automatically prove separate phonemes; contrast must still be tested directly, for example with a minimal pair, since two phones with different places could still turn out to be allophones in complementary distribution for a particular language.   2. Answers will vary; for example, Spanish [p] in [pan] can be described as a voiceless bilabial sound (manner, "stop," will be added once Chapter 8 is complete).

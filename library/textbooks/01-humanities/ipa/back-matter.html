Back Matter

Reference Materials, Practical Tools, and Indexes

A desktop reference for continued use after the course: the complete symbol inventory, pronunciation atlases, transcription and acoustic references, the language-mapping toolkit that supports the Chapter 32 capstone, professional resources, and the book's indexes.

How to Use This Back Matter

The chapters of this book teach the method: how to observe speech, classify it, produce it safely, notate it, and map it responsibly across languages. This back matter is the reference companion to that method. It is organized so that a reader can return to a single appendix without rereading a chapter — while transcribing a recording, building a language profile, or checking a symbol whose name has been forgotten.

Every appendix follows the same evidentiary and notational policies as the chapters: reference varieties are named where they matter, symbols use verified Unicode values, and no appendix implies more certainty or completeness than the underlying evidence supports. Appendix B, for example, is a core reference to the symbols used most often in this book and in general phonetic description — not a substitute for the official chart in Appendix A, which remains the authoritative source for the complete alphabet.

Appendix A

Complete International Phonetic Alphabet Chart

Purpose: orient the reader to the full official chart and its major regions, consistent with the chart-literacy work of Chapter 3.

The International Phonetic Association maintains the official IPA chart in five coordinated regions: the pulmonic consonants, organized by place across the columns and manner down the rows; the non-pulmonic consonants, covering clicks, voiced implosives, and ejectives; the vowels, arranged on the vowel quadrilateral by height and backness with rounded-unrounded pairs; the diacritics, grouped by function; and the suprasegmentals, covering stress, length, tone, and intonation marking.

Appendix B

IPA Symbol Dictionary

Purpose: give a fast, reliable articulatory reference for the symbols most often needed in general phonetic work, following the symbol-definition standard set in the Master Instructions — never an English keyword alone.

This dictionary covers the core symbol set used repeatedly across the book's chapters and examples. It is a working reference, not a replacement for the complete official chart in Appendix A. Each entry gives the symbol, its conventional name, an articulatory description, and the kind of example context in which learners will meet it in this book.

Selected Consonants
  • pvoiceless bilabial plosive
    Both lips form a complete closure; the airstream is pulmonic egressive; the vocal folds do not vibrate during closure.
    Introduced in Chapter 7 (place) and developed in Chapter 9 (stops).
  • bvoiced bilabial plosive
    Same closure as p, with vocal-fold vibration during some or all of the closure, subject to language-specific voice onset timing.
    Contrasted with p in Chapter 9's voice onset time work.
  • tvoiceless alveolar plosive
    Tongue tip or blade contacts the alveolar ridge; complete closure; voiceless.
    Core place-manner example throughout Chapters 7–9.
  • dvoiced alveolar plosive
    Same closure as t, with voicing.
    Paired with t in stop-voicing contrasts.
  • kvoiceless velar plosive
    Back of the tongue contacts the soft palate; complete closure; voiceless.
    Introduced with the velar place category in Chapter 7.
  • ɡvoiced velar plosive
    Same closure as k, with voicing. Note the IPA-specific single-story shape, distinct from the typographic letter g in most text fonts.
    Flagged as a confusable pair in the Master Instructions typography rules.
  • ʔglottal stop
    Complete closure of the vocal folds, blocking airflow entirely, then release.
    Introduced with glottal place in Chapter 7.
  • fvoiceless labiodental fricative
    Lower lip approaches the upper teeth, creating audible turbulence; voiceless.
    Nonsibilant fricative example in Chapter 10.
  • vvoiced labiodental fricative
    Same constriction as f, with voicing.
    Contrasted with f and with the approximant ʋ.
  • θvoiceless dental fricative
    Tongue tip approaches or touches the upper teeth; nonsibilant turbulence; voiceless.
    Nonsibilant dental example in Chapter 10.
  • ðvoiced dental fricative
    Same constriction as θ, with voicing.
    Contrasted with θ.
  • svoiceless alveolar fricative
    Grooved tongue channels air against the teeth, producing high-frequency sibilant turbulence; voiceless.
    Core sibilant example in Chapter 10.
  • zvoiced alveolar fricative
    Same constriction as s, with voicing.
    Contrasted with s.
  • ʃvoiceless postalveolar fricative
    Sibilant turbulence produced slightly behind the alveolar ridge, with a broader channel than s; voiceless.
    Distinguished from s and ɕ in Chapter 10's place-discrimination work.
  • ʒvoiced postalveolar fricative
    Same constriction as ʃ, with voicing.
    Contrasted with ʃ.
  • xvoiceless velar fricative
    Back of the tongue approaches the soft palate without full closure, producing turbulence; voiceless.
    Compared with h and χ in Chapter 10.
  • hvoiceless glottal fricative
    Audible turbulence generated at or near the glottis with an otherwise open vocal tract.
    Glottal fricative example in Chapter 10.
  • mbilabial nasal
    Complete oral closure at the lips with the velum lowered, allowing airflow through the nose.
    Core nasal example in Chapter 11.
  • nalveolar nasal
    Complete oral closure at the alveolar ridge with nasal airflow.
    Used throughout Chapter 11's nasal-place assimilation work.
  • ŋvelar nasal
    Complete oral closure at the velum with nasal airflow.
    Distinguished from the two-letter spelling that often represents it in English orthography.
  • lalveolar lateral approximant
    Tongue tip contacts the alveolar ridge while air flows around one or both sides.
    Core lateral example in Chapter 11.
  • ɹalveolar approximant
    Tongue approaches the alveolar or postalveolar region without full contact or audible turbulence.
    One of several rhotic realizations discussed in Chapter 12.
  • ɾalveolar tap
    A single brief contact of the tongue tip against the alveolar ridge.
    Distinguished from the trill r in Chapter 12.
  • ralveolar trill
    Repeated rapid contact between the tongue tip and the alveolar ridge, driven by airflow.
    Compared with the tap and with other rhotic approximants in Chapter 12.
  • jpalatal approximant
    Tongue body approaches the hard palate without full closure or turbulence; typically nonsyllabic.
    Compared with the vowel i in Chapter 12's glide-versus-vowel analysis.
  • wlabial-velar approximant
    Simultaneous lip rounding and tongue-body approach toward the velum; typically nonsyllabic.
    Doubly articulated approximant example in Chapter 12.

  • yclose front rounded vowel

Digital entry and rendering checklist

  1. Enter combining diacritics immediately after their base character, not as separate free-standing marks.
  2. Verify that combining marks remain attached at normal and enlarged text sizes.
  3. Confirm the active font has full IPA coverage before publishing or exporting.
  4. Check rendering across the target platforms: web, PDF, EPUB, and print.
  5. Avoid autocorrect or autocomplete features that silently substitute a visually similar non-IPA character.

Appendix J

Transcription Style Guide

Purpose: consolidate the transcription-level and notational-consistency guidance from Chapters 24–27 into a single style reference.

Choosing a transcription level

  • Broad (phonemic) transcription represents contrastive categories only; use slashes.
  • Broad phonetic transcription represents a defined pedagogical or descriptive level of detail; use brackets.
  • Narrow phonetic transcription adds observed or task-specific detail beyond the broad level; use brackets.
  • State the requested or intended level before transcribing, and do not mix levels silently within one transcription.

Notation consistency

  • Declare the reference variety before transcribing any language-specific example.
  • Use one tone or intonation notation system per exercise.
  • Attach diacritics to their intended base symbol and verify combining-mark rendering.
  • Do not include unsupported detail merely because a symbol exists for it.

Editorial conventions

  • Provide a transcription key alongside any transcription that uses more than the core symbol set.
  • Mark uncertain segments explicitly rather than guessing silently.
  • Distinguish an idealized citation-form transcription from a connected-speech transcription in any caption or label.
Appendix K

Transcription Workflow

Purpose: a printable checklist version of the nine-step recording-transcription workflow taught in Chapter 27.

  1. Establish metadata: recording, speaker, variety, and the task's requested transcription level.
  2. Listen globally before transcribing any detail.
  3. Segment the utterance into manageable stretches.
  4. Identify syllables and prominent prosody.
  5. Draft the broad segmental content first.
  6. Add only the detail the task supports.
  7. Compare the draft against the recording again.
  8. Document alternatives and uncertain decisions.
  9. Complete a final consistency audit against your declared conventions.

Do not transcribe from a written script while representing the work as a transcription of the recording, consistent with the audio-integrity policy in the Scope and Sequence.

Evidence Note — What a Display Can and Cannot Establish

An acoustic display supports a phonetic hypothesis; it does not produce an automatic transcription and it cannot resolve a phonological analysis by itself. Always report the speaker or data source, the measurement context, and the units, following the acoustic-evidence policy in the Master Instructions.

Appendix M

Common Transcription Errors

Purpose: a single catalog of the error patterns flagged throughout Chapters 24–27, for quick self-diagnosis.

Confusing Phoneme and Phone

Placing a phonetic bracket around a claim that is actually phonemic, or the reverse. Check what the delimiter is claiming before choosing it, following Chapter 5 and Chapter 24.

Overusing Narrow Transcription

Adding detail the task does not require, which can obscure the contrastive categories a reader actually needs. Match the level of detail to the stated purpose, following Chapter 24.

Incorrect Diacritic Placement

Attaching a combining mark to the wrong character, or leaving it detached so it renders as a separate glyph. Verify attachment visually after any digital entry, following Chapter 25 and Appendix E.

Unicode Mistakes

Substituting a visually similar but incorrect character, such as an ordinary letter a for ɑ, or an ordinary g for ɡ. Check confusable pairs against Appendix E before finalizing any transcription.

Delimiter Errors

Placing ordinary spelling inside phonemic or phonetic delimiters, or omitting angle brackets when citing an orthographic form. Review Chapter 2 and Chapter 3's delimiter conventions.

Appendix R

Major Phonetic Terminology

Purpose: a working glossary of the terminology distinguished throughout the book, per the Terminology Policy in the Master Instructions. See the End Matter Glossary for the complete alphabetical listing.

Airstream mechanism. The physiological system that initiates airflow for a speech sound: pulmonic, glottalic, or lingual.
Allophone. A contextually determined phonetic realization of a phoneme, predictable from its environment or freely varying without changing meaning.
Articulatory phonetics. The study of how speech sounds are physically produced by the vocal tract.
Broad transcription. A transcription representing contrastive categories or a defined pedagogical level, not every phonetic detail.
Coarticulation. Overlapping articulatory gestures across adjacent segments, producing gradient contextual variation.
Formant. A resonant frequency band of the vocal tract that shapes vowel and some consonant quality, visible on a spectrogram.
Minimal pair. Two forms differing in exactly one sound, in the same position, with a different meaning, used as evidence for phonemic contrast.
Narrow transcription. A transcription adding observed or task-specified phonetic detail beyond a broad level.
Phone. A single, physically produced speech sound, independent of its phonological status.
Phoneme. A contrastive sound category in a given language or variety, capable of distinguishing meaning.
Phonotactics. The language-specific rules governing how sounds may combine into syllables and sequences.
Reference variety. The specific, named language variety an example or claim is drawn from.
Suprasegmental. A phonetic feature, such as stress, tone, or intonation, that extends over more than a single segment.
Voice onset time (VOT). The interval between the release of a stop closure and the onset of vocal-fold vibration.
Appendix S

Frequently Confused Terms

Terms that are easy to conflate
TermTermKey difference
PhonePhonemeA phone is a physical realization; a phoneme is a contrastive category established by evidence within a language.
AlveolarDentalAlveolar targets the ridge behind the upper teeth; dental targets the teeth themselves.
StressAccentStress is phonetic and phonological prominence within a word or phrase; accent, in this contrast, refers to a variety's overall pronunciation pattern.
ToneIntonationTone is a lexical, word-level pitch contrast; intonation is a phrase-level pitch pattern tied to discourse, not individual word meaning.
VoicedVoiced aspirationVoicing refers to vocal-fold vibration; aspiration is a timing property of voiceless stops and is a separate dimension from voicing itself.
LengthDurationLength is a phonological category (long versus short); duration is the raw phonetic measurement in time, which may vary even among tokens of the same phonological length.

Glossary

The complete alphabetical glossary consolidates every key term introduced across the thirty-two chapters and the terminology distinctions in Appendix R. Below is a representative sample; the production edition compiles the full alphabetical list from all chapter key-term boxes during final assembly.

Accent (variety sense). The overall pronunciation pattern characteristic of a speaker or community, distinct from stress-based accent within a word.
Assimilation. A sound becoming more similar to a neighboring sound in one or more features.
Cardinal vowel. One of a defined set of reference vowel qualities used to anchor the IPA vowel space, independent of any specific language.
Diphthong. A single vowel nucleus involving a perceptible change in quality within one syllable.
Epenthesis. The insertion of a sound not present in an underlying or citation form, typically to repair a phonotactic violation.
Formant transition. The change in formant frequencies over time as the vocal tract moves between articulatory targets.
Harmony (vowel). A pattern in which vowels within a word agree in one or more features, such as backness or rounding.
Mora. A unit of syllable weight used in some languages' prosodic and metrical systems, smaller than or equal to a syllable.
Pitch accent. A system in which a limited, language-specific pitch pattern marks prominence, distinct from both stress-accent and full lexical tone systems.
Resyllabification. The reassignment of a segment to a different syllable than its citation-form syllabification, typically in connected speech.
Sibilant. A fricative or affricate produced with a channeled airstream directed at the teeth, producing a high-intensity, high-frequency turbulence.
Tone sandhi. A predictable change in a tone's realization based on its context, typically neighboring tones.

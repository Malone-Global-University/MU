Intonation and Information Structure

Phrase-level pitch, boundaries, prominence, and the discourse work intonation performs

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Distinguish intonation from lexical tone and from lexical stress.
  2. Identify phrase boundaries and prominent elements in an utterance.
  3. Describe global rises, falls, and other common intonational contours.
  4. Relate intonation to discourse context without assigning fixed, universal meanings to a contour.
  5. Interpret a declared, introductory intonation-notation system.
  6. Compare phrase-level pitch patterns across named varieties.

An Observable Problem

Chapter 21 established that in a lexical-tone language, pitch can attach permanently to a word, changing its meaning no matter where the word appears in a sentence. That chapter also previewed a second, very different way that pitch does work in language: pitch that rises and falls across an entire phrase or sentence rather than sitting on a single syllable. Consider the same sentence, spoken twice with identical words in an identical order, but with the pitch pattern across the whole utterance shaped differently each time. Listeners in many languages report a different overall effect: perhaps one version sounds like a question and the other like a statement, or perhaps one sounds finished and the other sounds like the speaker has more to say.

Nothing about the words has changed. No segment has changed. If the language does not use lexical tone, then no word-level pitch category has changed either. What has changed is the shape of pitch across the phrase, and that shape is doing real communicative work: marking where one idea ends and another begins, showing what information is new or already known to the listener, or signaling how the speaker feels about what is being said.

This chapter asks three questions:

  1. How does phrase-level pitch patterning, called intonation, differ analytically from the lexical tone and lexical stress systems already introduced, and how can a learner tell which system is responsible for a given pitch movement?
  2. What recurring pitch events, such as boundaries and points of prominence, organize an intonation contour, and how can a beginner represent them using one declared notation system?
  3. Why is it inaccurate to assign a single, fixed meaning, such as "questioning," to a rise or a fall, and what evidence is needed before making a claim about what a specific contour communicates in a specific language or context?
Intonation

Intonation Versus Tone and Stress

Purpose: place intonation precisely alongside the two systems already studied, lexical stress in Chapter 20 and lexical tone in Chapter 21, so that a pitch movement is never assigned to the wrong system by default.

Intonation is the patterning of pitch across a phrase or sentence that is not tied to the identity of an individual word. An intonation contour spans multiple syllables, often an entire utterance, and can change while every word in the utterance stays the same. Intonation is layered on top of whatever word-level pitch or stress pattern the language independently requires, rather than replacing it.

Chapter 20 introduced the diagnostic that separates lexical stress, a relatively fixed property of a word, from phrase-level prominence, which can shift depending on what the speaker wants to highlight. Chapter 21 extended a parallel diagnostic to separate lexical tone, fixed to a word regardless of sentence context, from intonation, which varies with sentence type and discourse function. This chapter uses the same underlying test: if a pitch or prominence pattern is stable across contexts for a specific word, it belongs to that word's stress or tone; if it shifts with the sentence's function, structure, or the speaker's communicative goal, it belongs to intonation.

Concept Check — Three Systems, One Diagnostic Family

Lexical stress, lexical tone, and intonation are three different systems, but they share a family of diagnostics: ask whether the pattern is a fixed property of a specific word or a variable property of the phrase or sentence containing that word. A fixed pattern points to stress or tone; a variable pattern that depends on sentence type, focus, or discourse context points to intonation. Applying this diagnostic carefully prevents a learner from mistaking an intonational rise for a tone or mistaking sentence stress for a lexical accent.

As Chapter 21 noted, a language can have lexical tone and intonation at the same time, with the two systems layered together rather than one displacing the other. The same is true of stress and intonation: a language with lexical stress still uses intonation to mark phrase boundaries, prominence at the sentence level, and discourse meaning. Separating the contribution of each system to a given pitch trace is an analytical skill, not something that can be read directly off a single recording without training.

Phrase Boundaries and Prominent Elements

Purpose: introduce the two structural building blocks of an intonation contour, phrase boundaries and points of prominence, before assigning any meaning to a specific pitch shape.

An intonation contour is organized around two recurring structural elements:

  1. Phrase boundaries mark the edges of intonational units, the stretches of speech over which a single coherent pitch pattern extends. A boundary is often, though not always, accompanied by a pause, a lengthening of the final syllable before the boundary, and a noticeable pitch movement at the edge.
  2. Prominent elements are the syllables within a phrase that receive the greatest perceptual emphasis, frequently realized through a combination of pitch movement, increased duration, and increased intensity, extending the cues introduced for lexical stress in Chapter 20 to the phrase level.

A useful first step in analyzing any intonation contour is to locate its boundaries before attempting to describe its pitch shape in detail. Segmenting a long utterance into intonational phrases, and then identifying the most prominent syllable within each phrase, gives the analysis a structure to build on rather than treating the pitch trace as one undifferentiated curve.

Concept Check — Prominence Is Not Automatically the Last Word

Beginners sometimes assume that the most prominent element in a phrase is always its final word. This is a common pattern in some languages under neutral, non-contrastive conditions, but it is not a universal rule. A speaker can shift prominence to an earlier word to highlight new information, to correct a previous statement, or to draw a contrast, a phenomenon developed further later in this chapter under information structure. Locate prominence by listening for the cues themselves rather than by assuming its position in advance.

Global Rises, Falls, and Common Contours

Purpose: describe recurring, broadly attested contour shapes as a starting vocabulary for analysis, without implying that every language organizes its contours identically.

Across many, though not all, studied languages, a small set of global contour shapes recur often enough to serve as a useful descriptive starting point:

  1. A global fall, in which pitch descends across the phrase or drops sharply near the end, frequently associated in many languages with a sense of completion or finality.
  2. A global rise, in which pitch ascends across the phrase or rises sharply near the end, frequently associated in many languages with incompleteness, continuation, or certain question types.
  3. A rise-fall or fall-rise, in which the phrase combines both movements, often associated with more complex meanings such as contrast, reservation, or implication.
  4. A relatively level or plateau contour, in which pitch changes comparatively little across the phrase, which some languages and registers use for lists, routine information, or a deliberately flat affect.

Common Mistake

Error: Learning the four shapes above as a fixed universal code, so that "rise equals question" is applied automatically to any language or utterance.

Correction: Treat these shapes as a starting descriptive vocabulary, confirmed for a given language and context only with evidence. The information-structure section below develops this caution further.

Notating Intonation

Purpose: introduce the course's declared, limited intonation-notation system, connected to the boundary and prominence concepts above and kept distinct from the tone notation of Chapter 21.

This textbook uses a limited, explicitly defined set of marks for introductory intonation notation, sufficient for the analysis expected at this stage without adopting a complete autosegmental or ToBI-style system, which lies beyond this book's scope.

As in Chapter 21, do not mix this system with an unrelated notation, such as informal arrows or punctuation invented for a single exercise, without declaring the substitution explicitly.

Common Mistake

Error: Using ordinary punctuation, such as a question mark or exclamation point, as if it were a phonetic transcription of intonation.

Correction: Punctuation reflects orthographic and grammatical convention, not measured pitch behavior, and different languages punctuate differently. Use the declared boundary and contour marks introduced in this chapter, or a fuller system in advanced work, to represent intonation itself.

Meaning and Variation

Intonation and Information Structure

Purpose: connect intonational prominence to discourse meaning carefully, showing how a contour interacts with what is new, given, or contrastive in a conversation, without assigning that contour one fixed universal meaning.

Information structure refers to how an utterance organizes information relative to what speaker and listener already share, distinguishing, for example, information that is new to the conversation from information that has already been established. Intonational prominence frequently interacts with information structure: a speaker may shift the most prominent syllable in a sentence away from its default position specifically to mark a word as newly introduced, contrastive, or corrective.

  • "I ordered the SALAD." Default final-word prominence in many neutral statements; no special contrast implied beyond ordinary completion.
  • "I ordered the salad, not the SOUP." Prominence shifts to mark an explicit contrast with a previously mentioned or assumed alternative.

    • Because a shift in prominence can signal contrast, correction, emphasis, or new information depending on context, the same phrase-level pitch event can support several different discourse functions. Determining which function is active in a given utterance requires attention to the surrounding conversational context, not just the pitch trace in isolation.

    Evidence Note — A Rise Does Not Always Mean a Question

    It is tempting to state a simple rule such as "a final rise marks a question." This rule fails as a universal claim. Many languages use a final rise for a substantial share of yes-no questions, but the same or similar languages also use rises for lists, for continuation before a pause, for polite or hedged statements, and for other functions, while some question types are marked primarily by word order or a question particle rather than by pitch at all. A specific claim about what a specific contour means in a specific language requires evidence from that language, not an assumption carried over from a learner's first language.

    Because English is the primary comparison language of this textbook, learners should be especially cautious about projecting English intonational habits onto other languages. English intonation itself varies by regional and social variety in ways that affect politeness, question marking, and emphasis, so even English-internal generalizations require care, consistent with the reference-variety policy applied throughout the course.

    Across Languages

    Across Languages — Rising Terminals in Some English Varieties

    Some varieties of English, in some social and regional contexts, are documented as using a rising terminal contour, sometimes discussed under the label "uptalk," on utterances that are grammatically statements rather than questions. Researchers have proposed several possible discourse functions for this pattern, including checking for listener engagement or softening an assertion, and the pattern has also attracted significant social judgment that is not linguistically grounded. Treat any specific claim about this pattern's function or social distribution as requiring a specific source and variety label, not a general fact about English.

    Across Languages — Question Marking Beyond Pitch

    Not every language relies primarily on intonation to mark a yes-no question. Many languages instead use a dedicated question particle, a change in word order, or a combination of a particle and a more modest pitch adjustment, with intonation playing a smaller or different role than it does in some well-studied European languages. This reinforces a point made throughout this book: a familiar pattern from one language, or one family of languages, should not be assumed to generalize.

    Across Languages — Intonation Layered Over Lexical Tone

    In lexical-tone languages such as Standard Mandarin, introduced in Chapter 21, intonation does not override the tone of individual syllables in the way it can shift stress placement in a stress language; instead, phrase-level pitch adjustments are superimposed on the existing tone pattern, for example by gradually raising or lowering the overall pitch register across a phrase while each syllable's tone shape remains recognizable. Untangling the two contributions in such languages requires dedicated instrumental analysis and is an active area of research rather than a settled, simple procedure.

    Common Mistakes

    Common Mistake

    Error: Treating every pitch movement in an utterance as intonation, without checking whether the language has lexical tone or lexical stress that could account for part of the pattern.

    Correction: Apply the diagnostic developed across Chapters 20 through 22 before attributing a pitch movement to intonation: confirm whether the movement is tied to a specific word regardless of context, pointing to tone or stress, or whether it varies with sentence type, structure, or discourse goal, pointing to intonation.

    Common Mistake

    Error: Assuming that the final word of a sentence is always the most prominent element.

    Correction: Final-word prominence is common under neutral conditions in some languages but is not universal and can shift for contrastive, corrective, or discourse-structural reasons. Identify prominence from its acoustic cues and context rather than assuming its position.

    Common Mistake

    Error: Assigning a fixed universal meaning, such as "rise equals question," to a contour shape without evidence from the specific language and context.

    Correction: A given contour shape can serve multiple discourse functions even within one language, and different languages assign different functions to similar shapes. State a proposed function as a hypothesis requiring evidence, not as an automatic rule.

Manners of Articulation

How degree and type of constriction organize the consonants of any language

Chapter Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Distinguish complete closure, turbulent constriction, and open approximation as the three basic degrees of constriction.
  2. Distinguish central and lateral airflow.
  3. Explain nasal versus oral release.
  4. Classify major consonant manners: stop, nasal, trill, tap or flap, fricative, lateral fricative, approximant, lateral approximant, and affricate.
  5. Combine place, manner, and voicing into a single standard consonant description.
  6. Locate manner rows on the official IPA chart.
  7. Explain why affricates require more than one static constriction label.

An Observable Problem

Say the English words sue, too, and Sue followed immediately by true slowly, paying attention only to what your tongue does right after it leaves the alveolar ridge, not to the letters on the page. In too [tʰuː], the tongue tip seals completely against the ridge and then releases all at once. In sue [suː], the tongue tip never makes complete contact — it narrows the passage just enough to force air through with audible friction. In a rhotic-r variety's true [tɹ̥uː], the second sound is narrower still than a vowel but never closes completely and never produces friction.

All three consonants — the [t], the [s], and the [ɹ] — can be made at very similar places in the mouth. What makes them sound so different is not where the tongue goes but how much it closes off the airflow and what kind of release follows. Chapter 7 gave you the vocabulary for where. This chapter gives you the vocabulary for how.

  1. Before reading further, say too, sue, and (if your speech has an approximant r) the second sound of true several times in a row. Write one sentence describing, in your own words, the difference in how much the airflow is blocked in each case.
Core Concept

The Constriction Continuum

Purpose: establish the organizing dimension that the rest of the chapter builds on.

Every pulmonic consonant can be placed somewhere along a continuum of how narrow the vocal tract becomes at the point of constriction:

  • Complete closurethe active and passive articulators seal off the airflow entirely, at least briefly
  • Turbulent constrictionthe articulators narrow the passage enough that air forced through it becomes audibly turbulent, producing friction noise
  • Open approximationthe articulators approach one another but leave a passage too open for friction noise to be produced

Manner of articulation classifies a consonant according to where it falls on this continuum, together with two further questions: does air escape down the center of the tongue or around one or both sides, and does it escape through the mouth, the nose, or both? Answering all three questions for a given consonant — degree of constriction, central versus lateral airflow, and oral versus nasal release — produces the manner labels introduced in this chapter.

Concept Check

Manner is independent of place. The same manner label can apply at many different places of articulation, and the same place can host consonants of many different manners. A bilabial stop [p] and a velar stop [k] share a manner but not a place; a bilabial stop [p] and a bilabial nasal [m] share a place but not a manner. A full consonant description always needs both, plus voicing.

Stops (Plosives): Complete Closure

Purpose: introduce the manner produced by total, momentary blockage of the airstream.

A stop, also called a plosive, involves a complete closure somewhere in the vocal tract, a brief buildup of air pressure behind that closure, and a release. Stops can occur at many places of articulation and with either voicing:

  • [pʰɪn]pin — voiceless bilabial stop
  • [bɪn]bin — voiced bilabial stop
  • [kʰæt]cat — voiceless velar stop
  • [dɔɡ]dog — voiced alveolar stop onset, voiced velar stop coda

Chapter 9 develops stops in much greater detail, including their internal phases, aspiration, and voice onset timing. For now, the essential point is that stop names a manner, not a place: a bilabial stop and a velar stop share the same manner of complete closure and release, even though they happen at opposite ends of the vocal tract.

Nasals: Closure with Nasal Release

Purpose: show that a complete oral closure does not always mean the airstream stops.

A nasal consonant also involves a complete closure in the oral cavity, but the velum lowers and allows air to escape continuously through the nose instead of building up pressure for an oral release. Because the airflow is continuous rather than blocked, nasals behave very differently from oral stops even though both involve complete oral closure.

  • [mæp]map — bilabial nasal, oral closure at the lips, air escaping through the nose
  • [nɛt]net — alveolar nasal
  • [sɪŋ]sing — velar nasal

You can confirm the nasal release for yourself with a simple test: say [m] and hold it, then pinch your nostrils shut. The sound stops almost immediately, because the only escape route for the air has been blocked. Chapter 11 returns to nasals in full, including nasal place assimilation.

Trills and Taps or Flaps: Repeated or Single Brief Contact

Purpose: distinguish manners defined by the timing and repetition of contact rather than by a single static constriction.

Two further manners involve brief contact between an active and a passive articulator, but they differ in how many times and how that contact occurs.

A trill involves an articulator set into vibration by the airstream, striking the passive articulator several times in rapid succession. A tap or flap involves a single, very brief contact, with no sustained vibration.

  • [r]an alveolar trill, found for example in many varieties of Spanish in words such as ⟨perro⟩ "dog"
  • [ɾ]an alveolar tap, found for example in the same variety of Spanish in words such as ⟨pero⟩ "but," and as a common realization of intervocalic t or d in many varieties of North American English, as in butter

Chapter 12 treats trills, taps, and the broader family of rhotic consonants in much greater depth. For now, the key distinction is repetition: one brief strike is a tap or flap, several rapid strikes in a row is a trill.

Fricatives: Turbulent Constriction

Purpose: introduce the manner produced by forcing air through a narrow but unsealed passage.

A fricative involves a constriction narrow enough to force the airstream through turbulently, producing continuous friction noise, but never narrow enough to stop the airflow completely. Fricatives can be sustained for as long as the airstream lasts, unlike stops.

  • [fæn]fan — voiceless labiodental fricative
  • [væn]van — voiced labiodental fricative
  • [suː]sue — voiceless alveolar fricative
  • [zuː]zoo — voiced alveolar fricative

Chapter 10 develops fricatives across the full range of places where they occur and introduces the sibilant versus nonsibilant distinction in detail.

Listen For

The defining auditory cue for a fricative is continuous, noisy turbulence with no silent gap and no sudden release burst. Compare [s] in sue, which you can sustain indefinitely with steady hissing, against [t] in too, which has a brief silent closure followed by a single sharp release. Try sustaining each sound for two full seconds and notice that only the fricative can be held without interruption.

Lateral Fricatives: Turbulence Around the Side of the Tongue

Purpose: introduce lateral airflow through the turbulent-constriction manner before extending it to approximants.

So far, every manner described has involved air escaping over the center of the tongue, which is called central airflow. A lateral consonant instead directs air around one or both sides of the tongue, while the center of the tongue maintains contact with the roof of the mouth. A lateral fricative combines lateral airflow with a constriction narrow enough to produce audible friction.

  • [ɬ]a voiceless alveolar lateral fricative, found for example in Welsh, where it is spelled ⟨ll⟩, as in ⟨llan⟩ "church"

Lateral fricatives do not occur in most English varieties, which makes this an opportunity to practice a genuinely unfamiliar manner-place combination using the production guidance below, rather than relying on an English keyword that does not exist for this sound.

How to Produce It — [ɬ]
  1. Place the tip and blade of your tongue against the alveolar ridge, exactly where you would for an English [l].
  2. Keep that central contact in place — do not release it.
  3. Lower one or both sides of your tongue away from your back teeth, opening a narrow side passage.
  4. Push air through that narrow side passage without voicing, so that you hear a soft hissing sound coming from the side of your mouth rather than the center.
  5. If voicing creeps in, you are producing [ɮ], the voiced counterpart, instead — pull the voicing back and let the sound stay breathy and voiceless.
  6. Compare your result against the central fricative [s]: the air should clearly be escaping from the side, not the center, of your mouth.

Stop immediately if this produces any strain or discomfort; a soft, breathy side-hiss is the target, not a forced or scraped sound.

Approximants: Open, Frictionless Constriction

Purpose: introduce the manner produced by a constriction too open for friction.

An approximant involves articulators that approach each other, narrowing the vocal tract, but never narrow enough to generate the turbulent friction noise of a fricative. Approximants are voiced in the great majority of the world's languages and often resemble vowels acoustically, but function as consonants in syllable structure.

  • [jɛs]yes — palatal approximant
  • [wɪn]win — labial-velar approximant
  • [ɹɛd]red, in many rhotic English varieties — alveolar or postalveolar approximant

Chapter 12 returns to approximants in detail, including the relationship between glides such as [j] and [w] and the high vowels they closely resemble.

Lateral Approximants: Open Constriction Around the Tongue

Purpose: complete the central/lateral and stop/fricative/approximant grid with the manner most familiar to English speakers from spelled ⟨l⟩.

A lateral approximant combines lateral airflow with a constriction open enough to avoid friction. The central contact at the alveolar ridge is the same kind of contact found in [ɬ] above, but the side passage is wider, so no turbulence is produced.

  • [lɛt]let — alveolar lateral approximant
  • [fʊɫ]full, in many varieties — a velarized alveolar lateral approximant, previewed further in Chapter 11

This pair of consonants — [ɬ] and [l] — illustrates manner classification cleanly: identical place and identical lateral airflow, but a different degree of constriction separates a lateral fricative from a lateral approximant.

Affricates: A Coordinated Contour

Purpose: show why some consonants resist a single static manner label.

Every manner described so far names a single, relatively stable configuration. An affricate is different: it begins as a complete closure, like a stop, and then releases not suddenly but into a brief period of fricative-style turbulence at essentially the same place of articulation. An affricate is best described as a stop-to-fricative contour rather than as a single static manner.

  • [tʃɪp]chip — voiceless postalveolar affricate, beginning as a stop closure and releasing into postalveolar frication
  • [dʒʌmp]jump — voiced postalveolar affricate

Because an affricate is a single sound functioning as one unit in the language's sound system, not a sequence of an independent stop plus an independent fricative, the two symbols are often joined with a tie bar ͡ to show that they form one coordinated event: [t͡ʃ]. Some transcription traditions omit the tie bar when the affricate analysis is unambiguous from context; this chapter introduces the tie bar for recognition and uses it in worked examples so you can interpret it, but does not yet require you to decide independently when omission is defensible. That decision is developed further in Chapter 9.

Common Error

Error: Treating any stop immediately followed by any fricative as an affricate, for example analyzing the ts sequence in English cats the same way as the ch in chip.

Correction: An affricate requires the stop and fricative portions to share essentially the same place of articulation and to function as a single unit in the language's sound system. Cats [kʰæts] is generally analyzed in English as a stop plus a separate fricative across a morpheme boundary, not as a unitary affricate, even though it sounds superficially similar to [t͡ʃ]. Place and function, not just adjacency, determine the analysis.

Place, Manner, and Voicing Together

Purpose: combine the three independent dimensions into the standard format for describing any pulmonic consonant.

Chapters 6 and 7 introduced voicing and place of articulation. This chapter completes the third dimension. A standard consonant description states all three, conventionally in the order voicing, place, manner:

Locating Manner on the Chart

Purpose: connect the manner categories in this chapter to the row structure of the official IPA consonant chart.

On the official pulmonic consonant chart, place of articulation organizes the columns, from bilabial on the left to glottal on the right, and manner of articulation organizes the rows, ordered from the most closed manner at the top to the most open at the bottom: plosive, nasal, trill, tap or flap, fricative, lateral fricative, approximant, lateral approximant. Locating a symbol therefore means finding the intersection of its place column and its manner row, and reading any cell shading or symbol pairing for voicing. Affricates are not given a row of their own because they are contours across two manners rather than a single static manner; they are typically shown as a tie-barred sequence rather than as a chart cell.

Open the official IPA chart now and locate the row for fricatives. Confirm that it contains more columns with filled-in symbols than the row for trills, and consider, without yet trying to explain it fully, why a chart organized by place and manner would predict that some manner-place combinations are far more common across languages than others.

Complete this self-observation task before the classification lab. No audio recording is required — you are listening to and feeling your own speech.

For each pair below, say both words slowly and decide whether the first consonant involves complete closure, turbulent constriction, or open approximation. Write your classification before reading further.

  1. tip vs. sip
  2. nap vs. tap
  3. led vs. red (in a rhotic-r variety)

Now repeat each word and add a second judgment: for the first consonant, is the airflow central or lateral, and does it escape orally or nasally? Note any case where you are uncertain, and identify what additional test — such as the nostril-pinch test for nasals — could resolve the uncertainty.

Common Mistakes

Common Mistake

Error: Assuming that "approximant" means quiet or weak rather than referring to a specific degree of constriction.

Correction: Approximant is a precise articulatory category defined by an open constriction that produces no friction, not a vague description of loudness. Approximants can be perceptually quite prominent, as in the strong glide onset of yes.

Common Mistake

Error: Calling every English ⟨l⟩ sound identical in manner and place across all positions in a word.

Correction: While both are lateral approximants, many English varieties realize ⟨l⟩ differently depending on position, as previewed earlier in this chapter and developed fully in Chapter 11. Manner classification tells you the broad category; it does not by itself capture every detail of a variety's allophonic pattern.

Common Mistake

Error: Assuming a sound is rare or unusual simply because it does not occur in English, as with the lateral fricative [ɬ].

Correction: Manners and places that are absent from one language are often common elsewhere. Treat unfamiliar manner-place combinations as ordinary parts of the world's sound systems, not as exotic exceptions.

Chapter Review — Part A (model answers)

1. A stop involves complete closure of the vocal tract; a fricative involves a constriction narrow enough to cause audible turbulence but not complete closure; an approximant involves a constriction open enough to avoid turbulence altogether. The three sit at decreasing degrees of constriction along one continuum.   2. Central airflow passes over the center of the tongue; lateral airflow passes around one or both sides of the tongue while the center maintains contact with the roof of the mouth.   3. A nasal shares complete oral closure with a stop, but the lowered velum allows continuous airflow through the nose, so the airstream is never blocked the way it is for an oral stop; this continuous airflow, not the oral closure alone, is what defines the manner.

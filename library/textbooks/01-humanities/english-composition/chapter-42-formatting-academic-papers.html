Chapter 42 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

When students think about academic writing, they usually think about ideas, evidence, and arguments. Formatting is easy to overlook. It seems like a mechanical afterthought — something to handle in the final minutes before submitting a paper. But the way a paper looks on the page is part of the communication. A well-formatted paper signals that the writer is serious, organized, and respectful of the reader's time. A poorly formatted paper distracts from even the best thinking.

This chapter teaches academic paper formatting as a system of conventions, not as an arbitrary set of rules to memorize. Every formatting choice — margin width, spacing, font size, page number placement — has a reason behind it. When you understand the reasons, the rules stop feeling random and start feeling sensible. You also become better at adapting when different courses, instructors, or disciplines ask for variations.

The chapter begins with the purpose of formatting and then moves through each major element of a finished academic paper: titles, headings, margins and spacing, page numbers, title pages, abstracts, tables and figures, and appendices. It closes with a complete final submission checklist designed to help you catch formatting errors before your paper leaves your hands.

Opening Question

Imagine submitting two versions of the same essay to a professor. Both contain identical ideas, identical evidence, and identical sentences. The first has one-inch margins, double-spacing, a properly formatted title, and page numbers in the correct location. The second has half-inch margins, inconsistent fonts, no page numbers, and no identifying information. Which paper do you think the professor will receive more favorably before reading a single sentence? Formatting communicates before your words do — and this chapter will show you how to make sure it communicates the right things.

Section 42.1

Why Formatting Matters

When you submit a paper to a professor, you are not just handing over your ideas. You are presenting a document. That document needs to communicate clearly at every level — from the depth of your argument down to the width of your margins. Formatting is the visual layer of academic communication, and it matters more than many students realize.

Consider what happens when formatting breaks down. A paper with inconsistent heading sizes confuses readers about which ideas are main points and which are supporting details. A paper with no page numbers is difficult to reference in discussion or grading. A paper that runs all its text together without white space is physically fatiguing to read. A paper submitted without a title page in a course that requires one signals inattention. None of these problems involve the quality of the ideas in the paper — but all of them interfere with how those ideas are received.

Formatting conventions in academic writing developed for practical reasons. They create a predictable visual structure that helps readers navigate documents efficiently. When every paper in a course uses the same margin widths, spacing, and font, instructors can read faster, make annotations in consistent margins, and focus on the content rather than decoding the presentation. When journals require a specific style, reviewers around the world can process submissions consistently, even across languages and institutions.

Core Principle

Formatting is a form of professionalism. In academic and professional life, the ability to follow conventions accurately signals competence. A researcher who submits a journal article with incorrect citation format or misaligned section headers creates unnecessary work for reviewers. A student who submits a paper with no title, inconsistent spacing, and missing page numbers suggests — whether fairly or not — that the same inattention may have been applied to the research itself. Formatting is not about perfection for its own sake. It is about respecting the conventions of the academic community you are joining.

Formatting and the Writing Process

Formatting is a finishing task, not a starting task. Students who spend the first hour of writing adjusting margin settings, selecting fonts, and centering title text are using revision time ineffectively. The content of the paper — the ideas, the evidence, the argument — must come first. Format the paper after you have a complete, revised draft.

That said, it is worth knowing the formatting requirements before you begin drafting. If you know a paper requires an abstract, you can plan for it. If you know headings are expected, you can draft with those section divisions in mind. Understanding the complete requirements early means there will be no surprises at the end, and no need to reorganize a finished paper to accommodate structural requirements you did not anticipate.

Section 42.2

Titles and Headings

Every academic paper needs a title. The title is the reader's first encounter with your work, and it should do real communicative work. A strong academic title identifies the topic, signals the paper's focus or argument, and helps a reader decide whether the document is relevant to their needs. Titles in academic writing should be precise and informative — not vague, not excessively clever, and not misleading.

Writing an Effective Paper Title

A good academic title tells the reader what the paper is about. That sounds obvious, but many student papers are submitted with titles so general that they communicate almost nothing. Consider the difference between these two titles for the same paper:

Title Comparison
Weak title versus specific, informative title
  • WeakModern Society — could belong to almost any paper on any subject. It identifies neither the topic of the paper nor the writer's specific focus.
  • StrongSocial Media Use and Adolescent Self-Reported Anxiety: Patterns from a High School Survey Study — identifies the subject (social media and anxiety), the population (adolescents and high school students), and the method (survey). A reader can immediately evaluate relevance.
Principle: An effective title identifies the topic, signals the paper's focus, and gives the reader enough information to understand what the paper does.

For papers that make an argument or take a position, a two-part title with a colon can be an effective structure. The first part captures an angle or phrase; the second part provides specificity and clarifies the scope:

Model Two-Part Titles

Writing Ourselves Into Learning: How Reflective Journaling Supports Academic Skill Development

Beyond Memorization: Critical Thinking in Secondary School History Courses

This structure is common in academic publishing and works well for analytical and argumentative papers. It is not mandatory, but it is a useful option when a single phrase would be too compressed or too vague on its own.

Common Mistake

Students frequently italicize or underline their own paper title, perhaps because they associate these marks with importance or because they know titles are formatted with special marks — but those marks apply to other works you cite, not to your own title. Your paper's title appears in plain text at the top of the first page (or on the title page).

Headings Within the Paper

Not every academic paper uses section headings. Short essays of one to three pages typically do not require headings because the paper is compact enough to navigate without them. Longer papers, research papers, laboratory reports, and papers in certain disciplines typically do use headings to help readers locate sections and understand structure.

Headings create a visual hierarchy in the paper. A first-level heading identifies a major section. A second-level heading identifies a subsection within that major section. A third-level heading identifies a further subdivision. In most college writing, you will rarely need more than two levels of headings.

The formatting of headings varies by style guide. In APA style, there is a precise five-level heading system. In MLA style, headings are optional and their formatting is less rigidly specified. In Chicago style, headings are common in longer works but vary by publication. When in doubt, follow your instructor's requirements or your assigned style guide.

Principles for Headings Across All Styles
  • Headings should identify what the section contains — be specific, not generic (Analysis of Survey Results by Age Group, not simply Analysis).
  • Headings at the same level should be parallel in grammatical structure (all noun phrases, or all verb phrases — not a mix).
  • Headings should not be complete sentences unless the style specifically requires it.
  • Do not place a heading immediately followed by another heading with no text between them. Include at least a sentence of introductory text before the first subsection heading.

Section 42.3

Margins, Spacing, and Fonts

The visual density of a page affects readability. Academic papers use standardized margins, line spacing, and fonts to create a reading experience that is comfortable and consistent. These choices are not aesthetic preferences; they are established conventions that serve practical purposes across every academic context where they are applied.

Margins

The standard margin width for most academic papers in North American higher education is one inch on all sides: top, bottom, left, and right. This applies to MLA, APA, and Chicago styles unless specific exceptions are noted by a particular publication or instructor.

One-inch margins serve several functions. They create white space around the text, which reduces visual fatigue over long documents. They leave room for handwritten or printed annotations and grading comments. They create a professional appearance that reflects standard publishing and word-processing convention.

Students sometimes reduce margins to squeeze more text onto fewer pages, attempting to meet a minimum page count requirement without writing the necessary content. Instructors recognize this practice immediately — a page with narrow margins is visually distinct from a correctly formatted one. Similarly, students sometimes expand margins to stretch a paper that is too short. Both practices substitute formatting manipulation for genuine writing work, and neither produces a better paper. If your paper is too long, revise the content. If it is too short, develop your ideas more fully.

Line Spacing

Double-spacing is the standard for most academic papers in MLA and APA styles. Chicago style requires double-spacing in student papers and manuscripts submitted for publication. Double-spacing makes text easier to read and leaves room for editorial annotations. Double-spacing applies throughout the paper, including the body text, the reference list or works cited page, any block quotations, and any notes.

Spacing IssueCorrect PracticeCommon Error
Space between paragraphsIn double-spaced papers, no extra blank line between paragraphs. New paragraphs are signaled by indentation only.Word processor default often adds extra space after paragraphs. Adjust manually.
Space after headingsStandard line spacing only between a heading and the text below it. No extra blank line.Inserting a blank line after every heading, which creates excessive white space.
Space after a periodOne space follows a period at the end of a sentence. All current style guides (MLA, APA, Chicago) recommend one space.Using two spaces after a period (a habit carried over from typewriter conventions).

Indentation

In standard academic formatting, the first line of each body paragraph is indented one-half inch from the left margin. This indentation signals the beginning of a new paragraph. Do not use a blank line between paragraphs as a substitute for indentation in double-spaced academic papers. The correct practice is standard double-spacing plus first-line indentation for every body paragraph.

Use your word processor's paragraph-formatting function to apply indentation, rather than pressing the tab key or space bar. The paragraph-formatting function applies consistent, reliable indentation throughout the document and is less prone to inconsistency when text is edited.

Block quotations are formatted differently from regular indented paragraphs. Rather than quotation marks, block quotations are set off from the main text by indenting the entire quotation from the left margin. In MLA style, block quotations begin when a prose quotation exceeds four lines; the entire quotation is indented one-half inch. In APA style, block format begins at forty words; the entire quotation is indented one-half inch. Always check your specific style guide for the exact threshold and indentation requirement.

Fonts

The guiding principle across all academic style guides is readability. Choose a font that is clean, professional, and easy to read over many pages. The most commonly specified fonts are Times New Roman 12-point (the traditional default for MLA and APA), Arial 11-point or Calibri 11-point (acceptable in APA 7th edition), and Georgia 11-point (accepted as a serif alternative in some styles).

Use a single, consistent font throughout the paper. Do not switch fonts between the title, headings, and body text. Mixing fonts makes a paper look inconsistent. Bold and italic formatting should be applied only according to the conventions of your style guide — bold for APA headings at certain levels, italics for specific purposes like titles of works in running text — not for general emphasis or decoration.

Decorative fonts, handwriting-style fonts, very small fonts (below 10-point), and very large fonts are all inappropriate for academic papers. If your instructor does not specify a font, Times New Roman 12-point is a reliable default choice across disciplines.

Study Note — Default Word Processor Settings

Many word processing programs default to Calibri 11-point, 1.15 line spacing, and extra space after paragraphs. These settings do not match standard academic formatting for MLA or traditional APA. Develop the habit of opening a properly formatted template at the beginning of each term. Save it with correct margins, font, spacing, and header settings, then open that template for every new paper instead of starting from the program's default. This one habit will eliminate many formatting errors before they occur.

Section 42.4

Page Numbers and Running Heads

Page numbers may seem like a small detail, but they serve important purposes. They help readers navigate a long document, allow instructors to reference specific passages in feedback, and support proper citation when others discuss your work. Academic papers should always include page numbers unless an instructor specifically exempts very short assignments.

Page Number Placement

StylePage Number LocationWhat Appears with the Number
MLAUpper right corner, one-half inch from the top of the page, flush with the right margin.Writer's last name followed by the page number: Smith 3
APA (Student)Upper right corner of each page.Page number alone. No running title required in 7th edition student papers.
APA (Professional)Upper right corner of each page.Page number in upper right; shortened paper title (running head) in upper left, all caps.
ChicagoTypically upper right corner or centered at the bottom of the page.Page number alone. Footnotes (common in Chicago) appear at the bottom of the relevant page.

Always use your word processor's header or footer function to insert page numbers. Do not type page numbers manually on each page; manually entered numbers will not adjust if you add, delete, or rearrange text. The header or footer function places the number automatically and keeps it consistent throughout the document, even as the text changes.

In all styles, consider how the first page is handled. If a title page is included (as in APA format), the title page is page 1 and the abstract is page 2. If no title page is used (as in MLA format), the first page of text is page 1 and the page number typically appears in the header. Follow your specific style guide on whether the number appears on the first page or begins on the second.

Running Heads

A running head is a brief, repeated header that appears at the top of every page of a document. Running heads serve as quick navigation aids in longer documents and help identify the document if pages become separated.

In MLA format, the running head is the writer's last name followed by the page number in the upper right corner — it functions primarily as a page identifier rather than a document title. In APA professional format, a running head is a shortened version of the paper title (fifty characters or fewer, in all capital letters) that appears in the upper left header alongside the page number in the upper right. In APA 7th edition student papers, a formal running head is not required; only the page number appears in the header. In Chicago student papers, a formal running head is not typically required.

Study Note

When using the APA professional running head format, the header on the title page reads "Running head: SHORTENED TITLE" and every subsequent page reads only "SHORTENED TITLE" without the label "Running head." In APA 7th edition student papers, this entire convention is eliminated — page number only. Always confirm which version of APA your course requires.

Section 42.5

Title Pages and Abstracts

Some academic papers require formal front matter before the main text begins. The two most common forms of front matter are the title page and the abstract. Whether these are required depends on the style, the course, and the instructor's assignment guidelines. Understanding when and how to produce these elements is an important part of academic paper formatting.

Title Pages

A title page is a separate opening page that presents the paper's identifying information before the text begins. The content and layout of a title page varies significantly across styles.

StyleTitle Page Required?Information Included
MLANot required for most student papers. Identifying information appears at the top left of the first page of text.Student's name, instructor's name, course name and number, date (format: 15 April 2026). Paper title centered below, before the first paragraph.
APA (Student)Required.Paper title (bold, centered, upper half of page); student's name; department and institution; course number and name; instructor's name; assignment due date.
APA (Professional)Required (different format).Running head; paper title; author name(s); institutional affiliation; author note.
ChicagoTypically required for research papers and longer formal documents.Paper title; student's name; course name; instructor's name; date. Usually vertically and horizontally centered on the page.

For any paper that requires a title page, keep the design clean and simple. Use the same font and size as the rest of the paper. Do not add decorative borders, clip art, colored backgrounds, or elaborate design elements. A title page communicates professionalism through simplicity. Its purpose is identification, not presentation.

Common Mistake

Students sometimes create visually elaborate title pages with oversized fonts, decorative headers, or graphic elements — believing this makes the paper look more serious or polished. In academic formatting, the opposite is true. An elaborate title page looks inappropriate and suggests the writer does not understand academic conventions. Simplicity and correctness are the standards.

Abstracts

An abstract is a brief, self-contained summary of a paper's purpose, methods, findings, and significance. It appears on a separate page immediately after the title page, before the main text of the paper begins. Abstracts are standard in scientific and social science writing and are required in APA format papers at the professional level and in many academic courses.

A strong abstract typically addresses four questions in compressed form: What did this paper set out to do? How was it done? What did it find? Why does it matter? The abstract should be dense with meaningful content — it is not a vague introduction to the topic and it is not a list of sections the paper covers. It is a compressed version of the paper's complete intellectual content.

Abstracts are usually 150 to 250 words in APA format, though the exact range depends on the journal or assignment. The abstract appears as a single block paragraph, without first-line indentation, on a page labeled "Abstract" centered at the top. The text begins on the next line without indentation. A reader who reads only the abstract should be able to understand the paper's purpose, method, and conclusions without reading the full text.

Model Abstract
Student research paper — approximately 120 words
  • PurposeIdentifies the research question and population.
  • MethodDescribes how the study was conducted and what data were collected.
  • FindingStates the main result clearly and specifically.
  • SignificanceExplains what the result implies for practice or future research.
This paper examines the relationship between daily recreational screen time and self-reported sleep quality among undergraduate students at a four-year university. A survey of 142 students collected data on screen use habits, sleep duration, and perceived sleep quality over a two-week period. Results indicate that students reporting more than four hours of evening screen time had significantly lower self-reported sleep quality scores than those reporting two hours or fewer. The findings suggest that screen-time reduction strategies may be a practical target for student wellness programs and support growing evidence that evening light exposure from screens interferes with sleep onset. Implications for campus health education are discussed.
Note: Every sentence does real work. The topic, method, population, finding, and implication are all present. No sentence is vague or merely decorative.

Writing an abstract is genuinely difficult. Because the abstract must represent an entire paper in under 250 words, every sentence must carry significant meaning. The abstract is the last section of the paper you should draft, after the paper itself is complete — you cannot accurately summarize something you have not yet fully written.

Not all academic essays require abstracts. Short analytical papers, response papers, and in-class essays typically do not. Research papers submitted in psychology, education, nursing, and the social sciences frequently do. Always check your assignment requirements before drafting front matter.

Section 42.6

Tables, Figures, and Captions

Academic papers in many fields include tables and figures to present data, illustrate relationships, or provide visual support for written claims. Tables organize information in rows and columns. Figures include graphs, charts, photographs, diagrams, and any other visual elements that are not tables. Both must be formatted carefully, numbered systematically, and integrated meaningfully into the paper's prose.

When to Use Tables and Figures

Tables and figures are not decoration. They appear in academic papers when visual presentation genuinely communicates something more efficiently or clearly than prose can. A table comparing test score results across six groups is clearer than six separate sentences of numbers. A line graph showing a trend over time is clearer than a paragraph describing small numerical changes across many data points.

Before inserting a table or figure, ask yourself two questions. First: does this visual element contain information that is important to the paper's argument or findings? Second: is this information genuinely clearer in visual form than it would be in prose? If the answer to either question is no, the table or figure probably does not belong in the paper. Visual elements should earn their place through clarity and relevance, not appear to add length or visual complexity.

Formatting Figures

Figures follow a numbering and captioning system similar to tables, with one important difference: the label and caption appear below the figure in APA style, not above it as with tables. This placement is consistent regardless of whether the figure is a graph, photograph, diagram, or illustration.

The figure number appears in bold, followed immediately on the same line (or the next line) by the caption. The caption is a brief description of what the figure shows, written in plain (not italic) text for the description portion. If the figure was created by someone else or adapted from another source, the source must be credited in a figure note below the caption — this is a citation requirement, not optional.

When creating charts and graphs for academic papers, be mindful that many papers are printed or reviewed in black and white. If a chart uses color to distinguish categories, check that the colors remain distinguishable in grayscale. Use different line styles (solid, dashed, dotted) or patterns in bar charts rather than relying on color alone to distinguish categories.

Integrating Tables and Figures into the Paper

A table or figure is not self-explanatory. Every table and figure in the paper must be referred to explicitly in the prose before or at the point where it appears. Do not insert a visual element without mentioning it in the text. The prose reference should direct the reader to the visual and explain what to look for:

Model In-Text References to Visual Elements

As shown in Table 1, students who reported more than four hours of evening screen time constituted the largest single group in the sample.

Figure 2 illustrates the downward trend in self-reported sleep quality scores as evening screen time increases.

Common Mistake

Students sometimes insert a table or figure and then repeat all of its information in the surrounding prose. This creates redundancy. The prose should direct the reader to the visual element and explain its significance — not duplicate its data point by point. Let the table or figure do the work of presenting numbers or images; use the prose to interpret and contextualize what the visual shows.

Section 42.7

Appendices

An appendix (plural: appendices) is a section at the end of a paper containing supplementary material that supports the main text but would interrupt the flow of reading if placed within it. Appendices allow writers to include detailed, useful material without burdening the reader in the middle of the argument. They are particularly common in research papers, empirical studies, and longer analytical documents.

What Belongs in an Appendix

Not every paper needs an appendix. Appendices are most appropriate when the supplementary material is genuinely relevant to the paper's claims and useful to readers who want to understand or verify the work in depth. Common appendix contents include:

Appropriate Appendix Contents
  • Survey instruments or questionnaires used in research
  • Interview protocols listing the specific questions asked
  • Raw data tables too large or detailed to appear in the body
  • Extended calculations or derivations referenced but not fully displayed in the text
  • Supporting documents — texts, transcripts, or historical materials analyzed in the paper
  • Additional figures or tables relevant but not central to the argument
  • Coding schemes or rubrics used in content analysis or qualitative research

The key criterion is that the material must be genuinely relevant to the paper's content and useful to a reader who wants to understand or replicate the work. An appendix is not a place to deposit everything gathered during research. If a piece of material is not relevant enough to mention in the main text, it probably should not appear in an appendix either — and if it is important enough to mention, make sure you do refer to it in the body.

Formatting Appendices

Each appendix is labeled separately. If there is only one appendix, it is simply labeled Appendix. If there are multiple appendices, they are labeled Appendix A, Appendix B, Appendix C, and so on, in the order they are first mentioned in the main text.

Each appendix begins on a new page and carries a descriptive title. In APA style, the appendix label appears centered at the top of the page, and the title appears on the next line, centered, in bold. The material follows with standard formatting — the same font and spacing as the rest of the paper.

Appendices appear after the reference list in APA format, or after the bibliography or works cited page in MLA or Chicago format. Every appendix must be mentioned in the body of the paper. The reference in the text can be brief: The complete survey instrument is included in Appendix A. This tells the reader additional material is available and where to find it.

Section 42.8

Final Submission Checks

The final submission check is a distinct task from revision and editing. By this stage, your paper's content, argument, and sentence-level prose should already be as strong as you can make them. The final submission check is specifically about presentation: does the paper look the way it should? Does it meet all the formatting requirements of the assignment?

A thorough final submission check catches formatting errors before they reach the reader. These errors are often easy to fix but leave a poor impression if they are not caught before submission. Work through the areas below in order, checking each one completely before moving to the next.

Header, Title, and Identifying Information

Does the paper have the correct identifying information? For MLA format, does the first page include the student's name, instructor's name, course name and number, and date — in the correct order, aligned left, before the centered title? For APA format, is the title page formatted correctly with all required fields? Is the paper title in title case, not italicized, not in quotation marks, and not followed by a period?

Page Numbers and Running Head

Are page numbers present on every page? Are they in the correct location for the assigned style — upper right for MLA and APA, upper right or centered bottom for Chicago? If a running head is required, does it appear correctly? Have you used the word processor's header function rather than typing numbers manually?

Margins, Spacing, and Font

Are all margins set to one inch? Check this in your word processor's page setup or layout settings, not by estimating visually. Is the paper double-spaced throughout, including the reference list or works cited page? Is there extra spacing after paragraphs that should not be there? Is the correct font used throughout at the correct size? Has a different font accidentally appeared in headings, the title, or footnotes?

Paragraph Formatting, Block Quotations, and Headings

Is each body paragraph's first line indented correctly? Are block quotations formatted and indented correctly for the assigned style? Does one space (not two) follow each period? If headings are used, are they formatted consistently at each level? Are parallel headings parallel in grammatical structure? Is any heading immediately followed by another heading without intervening text? If so, add a brief transitional sentence between them.

Tables, Figures, and Appendices

Are all tables and figures numbered in order of appearance? Do all tables have labels and titles above them? Do all figures have labels and captions below them? Is every table and figure referenced explicitly in the body text before or at the point it appears? Are table and figure notes present where needed for explanation or attribution? If appendices are included, do they appear after the reference list? Is each appendix labeled correctly? Is each appendix referenced in the body of the paper?

Print Preview and File Submission

Before final submission, view the paper in print preview mode or print a physical copy. Visual problems that are easy to miss on a scrolling screen — stray page breaks, headings stranded at the bottom of a page without any following text, tables that split awkwardly across pages — are often more visible in print format. Check the assignment submission instructions for required file formats (PDF, DOCX) and file naming conventions before uploading.

Application — Build a Formatting Template

Many formatting errors in student papers come from word processor default settings that do not match academic requirements. A practical solution: at the beginning of each term, create one properly formatted document with correct margins, font, spacing, and header settings. Save it as a template. Open that file — never the program default — when beginning a new paper. This practice eliminates most common formatting errors before they occur, and it takes less than fifteen minutes to set up once per term.

Chapter 42 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 42.1Formatting is the visual layer of academic communication. It signals professionalism, helps readers navigate documents, and ensures that good ideas are received clearly. Formatting is a finishing task, completed after revision, though requirements should be understood before drafting.
  • Sec 42.2Paper titles should be specific, informative, and formatted in title case — not italicized, not underlined, not in quotation marks. Headings create visual hierarchy and must be parallel, descriptive, and consistently formatted within each level. No heading should be immediately followed by another heading without intervening text.
  • Sec 42.3Standard academic formatting uses one-inch margins on all sides, double-spacing throughout, first-line paragraph indentation of one-half inch, and a readable 12-point font such as Times New Roman. Word processor defaults do not always match academic requirements and must be adjusted manually.
  • Sec 42.4Page numbers appear in the upper right corner of each page in MLA and APA styles. MLA includes the writer's last name before the number. APA student papers require only the page number. Use the word processor's header function, not manual typing. Running heads are required in APA professional papers but not in APA student papers as of the 7th edition.
  • Sec 42.5MLA papers typically do not require a title page; identifying information appears on the first page of text. APA and Chicago papers require title pages. Abstracts (150–250 words) summarize purpose, methods, findings, and significance in a single block paragraph; they are required in APA professional papers and many research-based courses.
  • Sec 42.6Tables and figures appear in academic papers only when visual presentation is genuinely clearer than prose. Tables carry their label and title above the table; figures carry their label and caption below. Every visual element must be referenced explicitly in the surrounding text. Prose should interpret visuals, not simply repeat their data.
  • Sec 42.7Appendices contain supplementary material (surveys, raw data, extended transcripts) that supports but does not fit within the body. Multiple appendices are labeled A, B, C in the order mentioned in the text. Each appendix must be referenced in the body of the paper and appears after the reference list.
  • Sec 42.8The final submission check reviews all formatting elements systematically: title and identifying information, page numbers, margins, spacing, font, indentation, headings, tables, figures, appendices, and reference list. Print preview catches visual errors missed on screen. Confirm file format and naming requirements before uploading.

Key Terms Review

abstract
A brief, self-contained summary of a paper's purpose, methods, findings, and significance; typically 150–250 words in APA format; appears on its own page after the title page.
appendix
A section at the end of a paper containing supplementary material (surveys, raw data, extended tables) that supports but does not fit within the main text.
block quotation
A long quotation set off from the main text by indentation rather than quotation marks; used for prose quotations exceeding four lines (MLA) or forty words (APA).
caption
A brief descriptive label placed below a figure identifying its content; includes the figure number, a description, and a source note when applicable.
double-spacing
A line spacing setting in which each line of text is separated by one blank line; the standard throughout most academic paper formats including references.
figure
Any visual element in a paper that is not a table — graphs, charts, diagrams, photographs, illustrations; numbered separately from tables and captioned below the image.
header
Text that appears at the top of every page of a document, typically containing the page number and, in MLA format, the writer's last name.
heading
A labeled title identifying a section or subsection of a longer paper; must be formatted consistently within a hierarchy of levels and parallel in structure within each level.
margin
The blank space between the text area and the edge of the page; standard academic margin width is one inch on all sides.
running head
A shortened version of the paper's title appearing in the header of every page; required in APA professional papers; not required in APA 7th edition student papers.
table
Data organized into rows and columns; labeled with a number and descriptive title above the table; uses horizontal rules but typically not vertical lines in APA style.
title case
A capitalization convention in which all major words in a title are capitalized; articles, prepositions, and coordinating conjunctions are lowercase unless they begin the title.
title page
A separate opening page presenting the paper's title, author, course, instructor, and date; required in APA and Chicago formats; identifying information appears on the first text page in MLA.
font
The typeface used throughout a document; Times New Roman 12-point is the traditional standard; must be consistent throughout the paper without decorative variation.

Chapter Checklist

Use this checklist before submitting any academic paper.

Title and Identifying Information
  • Paper title is present, descriptive, and formatted in title case
  • Title is not italicized, underlined, or placed in quotation marks
  • Identifying information (name, course, instructor, date) is present and correctly formatted
  • If a title page is required, it is included and correctly formatted for the assigned style
Page Numbers and Header
  • Page numbers appear on every page using the word processor's header function
  • Page numbers are in the correct location for the assigned style (MLA: upper right with last name; APA student: upper right only; Chicago: upper right or centered bottom)
  • Running head is included if required by the style or instructor
Margins, Spacing, and Font
  • All margins are set to one inch on all sides (confirmed in page setup, not estimated visually)
  • Paper is double-spaced throughout, including all quotations and the reference list
  • No extra spacing appears between paragraphs where it does not belong
  • Correct font is used throughout at the correct size (Times New Roman 12pt or as assigned)
  • Font is consistent throughout — no accidental changes in headings, title, or footnotes
  • One space, not two, follows each period
Paragraph Formatting
  • Each body paragraph's first line is indented one-half inch
  • Block quotations are formatted and indented correctly for the assigned style
Headings
  • Headings are used only if appropriate for the paper type and length
  • Headings at the same level are formatted consistently
  • Headings within each level are parallel in grammatical structure
  • No heading is immediately followed by another heading without intervening text
Abstract (if required)
  • Abstract is included on its own page if required by the style or assignment
  • Abstract is 150–250 words and covers purpose, methods, findings, and significance
  • Abstract paragraph has no first-line indentation
Tables and Figures
  • Every table and figure is numbered in order of appearance, in separate sequences
  • Tables have labels and titles above them
  • Figures have labels and captions below them
  • Every table and figure is referenced explicitly in the body text
  • Table and figure notes appear where explanation or source attribution is needed
Appendices
  • Appendices are labeled correctly (Appendix A, Appendix B, etc.) with descriptive titles
  • Each appendix is referenced in the body of the paper
  • Appendices appear after the reference list, each beginning on a new page
References
  • Reference list, works cited, or bibliography begins on a new page
  • Reference list is double-spaced with hanging indentation for each entry
  • Entries are alphabetized correctly
  • Citation format is correct for the assigned style
Final Review
  • Paper has been viewed in print preview mode for visual formatting problems
  • File is saved in the required format (DOCX, PDF, or as specified)
  • File is named according to any assignment requirements
  • All assignment-specific formatting requirements have been reviewed and followed
Looking Ahead
Chapter 43: Revision as Re-Seeing

Chapter 42 addressed the final presentation layer of a finished paper — the system of conventions that ensures your work reaches readers clearly and professionally. Chapter 43 turns back to the content itself, asking a more fundamental question: how do writers revisit a draft not just to polish it, but to genuinely rethink it? Revision is not proofreading. It is re-seeing — returning to a draft with fresh eyes and the willingness to change not just sentences, but structure, argument, and evidence. Part VIII of this book addresses the full revision process in depth, and Chapter 43 begins by drawing a clear line between what revision is and what it is not.

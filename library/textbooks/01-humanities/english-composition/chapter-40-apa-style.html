Chapter 40 — Front Matter

Chapter Overview and Learning Objectives

When researchers write for publication, they do not simply hand over their findings in whatever form they choose. They follow agreed-upon rules for organizing information, citing sources, and formatting documents. These rules exist so that readers from the same discipline can navigate any paper efficiently, locate original sources with confidence, and evaluate the quality of evidence without wasting time deciphering an unfamiliar system.

APA style is one of the most widely used documentation systems in the world. Developed by the American Psychological Association, it was created to meet the specific needs of researchers in psychology, education, nursing, social work, communications, and related fields. Today it is required in hundreds of disciplines and thousands of institutions. Whether you are writing a research paper for a psychology course, a literature review for a sociology class, or a report for a public health program, there is a good chance your instructor expects APA documentation.

This chapter teaches APA style from the ground up. It explains what APA is and why it was designed the way it was. It walks through in-text citations in detail, showing how to handle sources with one author, multiple authors, no author, organizations as authors, and direct quotations. It then explains the APA References page, covering the most common source types students encounter. Finally, it addresses general APA paper formatting.

Why This Chapter Matters

APA is the required format in most social science, health, education, and behavioral science courses. Mastering it early means every paper you submit in those fields will meet professional standards. More importantly, understanding why APA works the way it does helps you use it correctly in situations no reference sheet can anticipate.

Section 40.1
What APA Style Is
Documentation Systems · Author-Date Format · Disciplinary Context

The Purpose of a Documentation System

Before learning any specific formatting rules, it helps to understand what documentation systems are trying to accomplish. Academic writing is a conversation. When researchers publish their findings, they build on the work of other researchers who came before them. They respond to existing theories, challenge previous conclusions, add new data, and refine old models. Because so much academic writing depends on prior work, readers need a reliable way to trace ideas back to their original sources.

A documentation system is a standardized set of rules for acknowledging those sources. It tells writers exactly how to record the essential information about every source they use, and it tells readers exactly where to look to find original material. When everyone in a discipline follows the same rules, research becomes more transparent, more verifiable, and more trustworthy.

Documentation SystemDeveloped ByPrimary FieldsIn-Text Format
APAAmerican Psychological AssociationSocial sciences, education, nursing, healthAuthor, Year
MLAModern Language AssociationLiterature, language, humanitiesAuthor, Page
ChicagoUniversity of Chicago PressHistory, humanities, some social sciencesFootnotes or Author, Year

Why APA Was Designed the Way It Was

APA style was first published in 1929 as a brief article in the journal Psychological Bulletin, offering guidelines for the preparation of manuscripts in psychology. It has been revised many times since then. The current edition, the seventh, was published in 2019 and introduced several updates to simplify and modernize the system.

The most distinctive feature of APA documentation is its author-date citation format. Every in-text citation includes the author's last name and the year of publication. This reflects the priorities of scientific and social-scientific research. In these fields, the date of a study matters enormously. A reader encountering a citation can immediately see who conducted the research and when — allowing them to judge how current the evidence is.

Compare this to literature scholarship, where a 1958 critical essay on Shakespeare may be just as relevant as one written last year. In literary studies, the date of a secondary source is usually less important than who wrote it and what they argued. MLA style, which does not include the year in its in-text citations, reflects that different set of priorities.

Study Note — Why the Year Matters in APA

In social science research, findings can be overturned by new studies. A reader seeing "(Smith, 1995)" knows immediately to check whether newer research has updated or revised that finding. The date is not just a formality — it is information the reader needs to evaluate the quality and currency of the evidence.

Section 40.2
APA In-Text Citations
Paraphrase · Quotation · Signal Phrases · Multiple Authors · Special Cases

The Logic of Author-Date Citations

Every time you use information from a source in APA style, you must acknowledge that source within the sentence or passage where you use it. This acknowledgment takes the form of a brief parenthetical note or a signal phrase containing the author's last name and the year of publication. This in-text citation connects directly to the full entry on the References page at the end of your paper.

The relationship between in-text citations and References entries is essential. An in-text citation is never complete by itself — it is a pointer. Every source you cite in the body of your paper must appear in the References list. Every source in your References list must be cited somewhere in the body of your paper. There should be no orphaned citations and no uncited references.

The Core Rule of APA In-Text Citation

Paraphrase: Author's last name, year

Direct quotation: Author's last name, year, p. page number

Both can appear as parenthetical citations or be integrated through a signal phrase. The year always follows the author's name in parentheses.

Paraphrase Citations

When you paraphrase a source — expressing the author's ideas in your own words — you cite the author's last name and the year of publication. The citation typically appears in parentheses at the end of the sentence, before the period.

Example 40.2.1
Paraphrase — Parenthetical Citation
  • ExampleCognitive load can be reduced significantly when information is presented in smaller, manageable units rather than as a single dense block (Sweller, 1988).
  • StructureAuthor's last name, comma, year — all in parentheses before the period.
Example 40.2.2
Paraphrase — Signal Phrase Citation
  • ExampleSweller (1988) argued that cognitive load can be reduced significantly when information is presented in smaller, manageable units.
  • StructureAuthor's name is part of the sentence; year immediately follows in parentheses. No second parenthetical needed.
Both formats are equally correct. Signal phrases are useful when you want to emphasize who made a claim.

Direct Quotation Citations

When you quote a source word for word, APA style requires three pieces of information: the author's last name, the year of publication, and the page number. The page number is preceded by p. for a single page or pp. for a range of pages.

Example 40.2.3
Direct Quotation with Page Number
  • Parenthetical"The limits of my language mean the limits of my world" (Wittgenstein, 1922, p. 74).
  • Signal phraseWittgenstein (1922) famously observed that "the limits of my language mean the limits of my world" (p. 74).
  • NoteThe period comes after the closing parenthesis. The citation is part of the sentence.

Block Quotations

A quotation of forty words or more is formatted as a block quotation. Block quotations are indented half an inch from the left margin, have no quotation marks around them, and are followed by the citation in parentheses after the final period — which is the reverse of normal sentence punctuation.

Common Mistake — Block Quotation Punctuation

In a block quotation, the period comes before the parenthetical citation, not after. This is the opposite of how regular sentence citations work: ". . . more capable peers. (Vygotsky, 1978, p. 86)" — the period is inside the quote, and the citation follows the closing period of the block.

Sources with Multiple Authors

Number of AuthorsIn-Text FormatExample
One authorLast name, year(Kahneman, 2011)
Two authorsLast name & Last name, year(Kahneman & Tversky, 1979)
Three or more authorsFirst author's last name et al., year(Bandura et al., 1977)

For two authors, always cite both names, connected by an ampersand (&) in a parenthetical citation or by the word and in a signal phrase: Kahneman and Tversky (1979) demonstrated that...

For three or more authors, cite only the first author's name followed by et al. (Latin for "and others") from the very first citation. APA 7 simplified this from the previous edition, which required listing up to five authors before using et al.

Student Warning — & vs. "and" in Signal Phrases

Use the ampersand (&) only inside parenthetical citations: (Kahneman & Tversky, 1979). When authors' names appear in the flow of a sentence, use the word "and": "Kahneman and Tversky (1979) found that..." Using & in the sentence itself is incorrect in APA style.

Introduce the abbreviation on first use; use it alone after that
  • First cite(World Health Organization [WHO], 2020)
  • Subsequent(WHO, 2020)

Section 40.3

What the References Page Is

The References page appears at the end of your paper. It contains a complete, alphabetically organized list of every source you cited in your paper. The purpose of the References page is to give readers everything they need to locate each source independently: the author, the date, the title, the publication information, and — where applicable — a link or database identifier.

The APA References page is not the same as a bibliography. A bibliography may include sources you read but did not cite. A References list includes only the sources you actually cited in the paper. Every entry must correspond to at least one in-text citation, and every in-text citation must correspond to an entry.

References Page Formatting Rules

• Begin on a new page after the final page of your paper.

• Center the word References at the top in bold.

• List all entries alphabetically by the first author's last name.

• Use a hanging indent: first line flush left, all subsequent lines indented half an inch.

• Double-space all entries with no extra space between them.

• Use sentence case for article and chapter titles; use title case for journal names and book titles.

The General Structure of Any APA Reference Entry

Every APA entry follows this pattern: Author, A. A. (Year). Title of work. Publisher/Source information. The who, when, what, where structure is consistent — only the specific contents change for different source types.

Section 40.4

Journal Articles and DOIs

APA style was developed in a research context where journal articles are the primary medium for sharing findings. Knowing how to cite a journal article accurately is the most important citation skill in APA style.

Author, A. A., & Author, B. B. (Year). Title of article in sentence case. Title of Journal in Title Case, volume(issue), page–page. https://doi.org/xxxxx
Example 40.4.1
Journal Article with DOI
Twenge, J. M., Joiner, T. E., Rogers, M. L., & Martin, G. N. (2018). Increases in depressive symptoms, suicide-related outcomes, and suicide rates among U.S. adolescents after 2010 and links to increased new media screen time. Clinical Psychological Science, 6(1), 3–17. https://doi.org/10.1177/2167702617723376

Understanding DOIs

A Digital Object Identifier, or DOI, is a permanent alphanumeric code assigned to a piece of scholarly content. It functions like a digital address that never changes, even if the document moves to a different server or website. In APA 7, DOIs are formatted as hyperlinks:

https://doi.org/10.1177/2167702617723376

When a DOI is available, always include it. When no DOI is available, include the URL of the journal's home page or a freely available version online. If the article comes from a subscription database and has no DOI, end the entry after the page range.

Example 40.4.2
Journal Article Without a DOI
Loftus, E. F., & Palmer, J. C. (1974). Reconstruction of automobile destruction: An example of the interaction between language and memory. Journal of Verbal Learning and Verbal Behavior, 13(5), 585–589.
When no DOI exists and no stable URL is available, the entry ends after the page range.
Common Mistake — Capitalization in Journal Articles
Increases In Depressive Symptoms Among U.S. Adolescents. Clinical psychological science, 6(1), 3–17. Increases in depressive symptoms among U.S. adolescents. Clinical Psychological Science, 6(1), 3–17.

The article title is in sentence case (only first word and proper nouns capitalized). The journal name is in title case and italicized. The volume number is also italicized; the issue number in parentheses is not.

Section 40.5

Books and Book Chapters

Author, A. A. (Year). Title of book in sentence case. Publisher.
Example 40.5.1
Entire Book
Kahneman, D. (2011). Thinking, fast and slow. Farrar, Straus and Giroux.
Example 40.5.2
Book with Edition Information
American Psychological Association. (2020). Publication manual of the American Psychological Association (7th ed.). https://doi.org/10.1037/0000165-000
When author and publisher are the same organization, list the organization in the author position and omit it from the publisher position.
Example 40.5.3
Edited Book
Miller, J. D., & Lynam, D. R. (Eds.). (2019). The handbook of trait narcissism: Key advances, research methods, and controversies. Springer.

Chapter in an Edited Book

When you use a specific chapter from an edited book, you must cite the chapter author and title separately from the book's editor and title. This format is more complex than other entries but follows a consistent logic: the chapter author appears in the author position, and the book editor and title appear in the container section.

Author, A. A. (Year). Title of chapter. In E. E. Editor (Ed.), Title of edited book (pp. xx–xx). Publisher.
Example 40.5.4
Chapter in an Edited Book
Baumeister, R. F. (2019). The self. In R. F. Baumeister & E. J. Finkel (Eds.), Advanced social psychology: The state of the science (pp. 139–175). Oxford University Press.
Book Chapter Format — Details to Remember

The chapter title is in sentence case and has no quotation marks or italics. The word In precedes the editor's name. The editor's name appears in normal order (initials first, last name second) — unlike the chapter author. The abbreviation Ed. appears in parentheses. The book title is italicized and in sentence case. Page range appears in parentheses after the book title.

Section 40.6

Reports and Organizational Authors

Research reports published by government agencies, research institutes, nonprofits, and professional organizations are common sources in social science, public health, education, and policy writing. These reports often have organizational authors rather than individual authors.

Author, A. A. (Year). Title of report (Report No. xxx). Publisher. URL
Example 40.6.1
Government or Institutional Report
National Institute of Mental Health. (2021). Mental illness (NIH Publication No. 21-MH-8088). U.S. Department of Health and Human Services. https://www.nimh.nih.gov/health/statistics/mental-illness
When the author and publisher are the same organization, list it in the author position and omit it from the publisher position.
Section 40.7

Websites and Online Sources

The web presents particular challenges for documentation. Websites may lack clear authorship, publication dates, or stable URLs. Content may change or disappear after you access it. APA 7 introduced updates to handle online sources with the general principle: give as much identifying information as you can, and prioritize permanence where possible.

Author, A. A. (Year, Month Day). Title of page or document. Site Name. URL
Example 40.7.1
Webpage — Organization as Author
Centers for Disease Control and Prevention. (2023, January 10). About mental health. https://www.cdc.gov/mentalhealth/learn/index.htm
Example 40.7.2
News Article Online
Kolata, G. (2020, March 12). Coronavirus tests: Who has them and who does not. The New York Times. https://www.nytimes.com/2020/03/12/health/coronavirus-testing.html
The publication name (The New York Times) is italicized because it functions as the container. The article title is in sentence case, not italicized.
Missing ElementSolution
No authorBegin the entry with the title of the page in the author position.
No dateUse n.d. in the date position. Add a retrieval date if content changes frequently.
No titleUse a description in square brackets: [Description of the content].
Section 40.8
APA Paper Formatting
Page Setup · Title Page · Abstract · Headings · Tables and Figures

The way your paper looks on the page communicates something to your reader. A correctly formatted paper signals that you understand the conventions of the discipline and have given attention to presentation as well as content. APA formatting guidelines create consistency across documents, make papers easier to read, and ensure that submission-ready manuscripts look the same regardless of the word processor used to create them.

General Page Setup

ElementAPA Specification
MarginsOne inch on all sides
FontTimes New Roman 12pt, Calibri 11pt, Arial 11pt, or Georgia 11pt
Line spacingDouble-spaced throughout — including title page, abstract, body, and References
Paragraph indentationHalf-inch indent for first line of each paragraph (use Tab key, not spacebar)
AlignmentLeft-aligned; do not use full justification
Page numbersUpper right corner of every page; begins with title page as page 1

Title Page

The APA title page for a student paper includes the paper title (centered, bold, title case), the author name(s), institutional affiliation, course information, instructor name, and assignment due date — all centered. The title is in the upper half of the page. The title page is its own page (page 1); the paper begins on page 2.

Student Papers vs. Manuscript Submissions

Papers submitted for journal publication include a running head — a shortened version of the title in all capitals, placed in the left side of the header. For most student papers submitted in courses, the running head is not required. Check with your instructor before adding one.

Abstract

An abstract is a brief summary of the paper, typically between 150 and 250 words. It appears on its own page (page 2) after the title page. The word Abstract is centered and bold at the top. The abstract itself is not indented and is written as a single paragraph. Not all student papers require an abstract — your instructor will specify. Below the abstract, you may be asked to include 3 to 5 keywords:

Keywords: cognitive development, working memory, academic performance, self-regulation

Headings

APA uses a five-level heading system. Most student papers use only two or three levels. Do not use the word "Introduction" as a heading — the opening section is understood to be the introduction without labeling.

LevelFormatUse
Level 1Centered, bold, title caseMajor sections
Level 2Left-aligned, bold, title caseSubsections within Level 1
Level 3Left-aligned, bold italic, title caseSubsections within Level 2

Tables and Figures

Each table and figure is numbered sequentially (Table 1, Table 2; Figure 1, Figure 2) and includes a title and explanatory note. The word Table or Figure followed by the number appears in bold above the table or figure. A descriptive title in italics appears on the line below. A note appears below the table in regular type, beginning with the word Note in italics followed by a period.

Student Warning — Alphabetizing the References List

Alphabetize letter by letter, ignoring spaces. Mc and Mac are treated as spelled. When the same author has multiple entries, list them from earliest to most recent. When the same author has multiple publications in the same year, alphabetize by title and add letters to the year: 2018a, 2018b. When no author is listed, alphabetize by the first significant word of the title.

Chapter 40 — Back Matter

Key Terms, Summary, and Review

Key Terms

APA Style
The documentation system developed by the American Psychological Association, used widely in social sciences, education, nursing, and related fields.
Author-Date Citation
The in-text citation format used in APA style, which includes the author's last name and year of publication.
Block Quotation
A direct quotation of forty or more words, formatted as an indented block without quotation marks. Period precedes the citation.
DOI
Digital Object Identifier — a permanent alphanumeric code assigned to scholarly content, formatted as https://doi.org/... in APA 7.
Et al.
Latin abbreviation meaning "and others," used in APA citations when a source has three or more authors.
Hanging Indent
A formatting style in which the first line of a reference entry is flush with the left margin and subsequent lines are indented half an inch.
In-Text Citation
A brief citation within the body of a paper — author and year — that directs the reader to the full entry in the References list.
References List
The alphabetically organized list of all sources cited in an APA paper, placed at the end of the document on its own page.
Running Head
A shortened version of the paper title in all capitals, placed in the header of manuscripts submitted for journal publication. Not required for most student papers.
Sentence Case
Capitalization style in which only the first word, the first word after a colon, and proper nouns are capitalized. Used for article and chapter titles in APA.
Signal Phrase
A phrase that introduces a source by naming the author as part of the sentence, rather than in a parenthetical citation at the end.
Title Case
Capitalization style in which all major words are capitalized. Used for journal names and book titles in APA references.
Abstract
A 150–250 word summary of a paper's purpose, methods, key findings, and conclusions, appearing on its own page after the title page.
Paraphrase
Expressing a source's ideas in your own words. APA paraphrase citations include the author's last name and year but no page number.

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 40.1APA style uses an author-date format because publication year matters in research-oriented fields. It was developed by the American Psychological Association and is now standard in social sciences, health, and education. The seventh edition (2019) introduced key simplifications.
  • Sec 40.2In-text citations include author and year for paraphrases; author, year, and page number for direct quotations. Two authors are always named; three or more use et al. from the first citation. Special rules handle organizations, missing authors, missing dates, and multiple sources.
  • Sec 40.3The References page is not a bibliography — it lists only sources actually cited. It begins on a new page, uses hanging indents, is double-spaced, and orders entries alphabetically by first author's last name. Every entry follows the author-date-title-source pattern.
  • Sec 40.4Journal articles require author(s), year, article title in sentence case, journal name in title case and italics, volume in italics, issue in parentheses, page range, and DOI formatted as a hyperlink. When no DOI exists, include a URL or end after the page range.
  • Sec 40.5Books use author, year, italicized title in sentence case, and publisher name (without corporate identifiers). Chapters in edited books must credit the chapter author, chapter title, book editor(s), italicized book title, page range, and publisher. Translated books include the translator and original publication year.
  • Sec 40.6Reports published by organizations follow a similar pattern to books. When the author and publisher are the same organization, list it only once in the author position. Include report numbers when available and a URL for online reports.
  • Sec 40.7Websites require author or organization, date, title, site name, and URL. When elements are missing, use n.d. for no date or begin with the title for no author. News articles include the publication name as a container, italicized. Videos follow the same logic with the platform name included.
  • Sec 40.8APA papers use one-inch margins, double-spacing, an accepted font, half-inch paragraph indents, and page numbers in the upper right. The title page lists the paper title, author, affiliation, course, instructor, and date. Abstracts appear on page 2 for longer papers. Three levels of headings organize most student papers.

Discussion and Writing Prompts

  1. APA style requires authors to include the year of publication in every in-text citation. Why do you think this is particularly important in fields like psychology, sociology, and public health? Think of an example in which the date of a source would significantly affect how a reader evaluates a claim.
  2. One of the most common mistakes students make is treating documentation as a last-minute task rather than an ongoing part of the research process. What problems can arise from waiting until the paper is finished to begin formatting citations? What habits would help you avoid those problems?
  3. The shift from MLA to APA style can feel disorienting because many of the rules are similar but not identical. Without looking at any reference guide, write out an in-text citation for a fictional source in both MLA and APA style. Then compare them. What is the same? What is different? What does the comparison reveal about the priorities of each system?
  4. APA 7 updated several rules from the previous edition, including simplifying author rules for sources with three or more authors and eliminating the running head requirement for most student papers. Why might a major documentation system update its rules over time? What forces might drive those changes?

Chapter Checklist

Use this checklist before submitting any paper that requires APA documentation.

Chapter 40 Submission Checklist
Review all items before turning in an APA-formatted paper
In-Text Citations
  • Every paraphrase includes the author's last name and year of publication.
  • Every direct quotation includes the author's last name, year, and page number.
  • Works with two authors use an ampersand (&) in parenthetical citations and "and" in signal phrases.
  • Works with three or more authors use et al. from the first citation onward.
  • Organizational authors are identified correctly, with abbreviations introduced on first use.
  • Sources with no author use the title (italicized for books; in quotes for articles) in the author position.
  • Sources with no date use n.d. in the year position.
References Page
  • The References page begins on a new page after the body of the paper.
  • The word References appears centered and in bold at the top of the page.
  • All entries are alphabetized by the first author's last name.
  • All entries use hanging indents (first line flush left; subsequent lines indented half an inch).
  • The entire list is double-spaced with no extra space between entries.
  • Article and chapter titles are in sentence case (not title case).
  • Journal names and book titles are in title case and italicized.
  • Volume numbers are italicized; issue numbers in parentheses are not.
  • DOIs are formatted as hyperlinks (https://doi.org/...) when available.
  • Every in-text citation has a corresponding References entry, and every entry has a corresponding in-text citation.
Paper Formatting
  • Margins are one inch on all sides.
  • The entire paper is double-spaced with no extra spacing between paragraphs.
  • An acceptable font (Times New Roman 12pt, Calibri 11pt, etc.) is used throughout.
  • Page numbers appear in the upper right corner of every page, beginning with page 1 on the title page.
  • The first line of each paragraph is indented half an inch.
  • The title page includes the paper title (bold), author name, affiliation, course information, instructor name, and due date.
  • Headings follow correct APA levels where applicable (centered bold for Level 1; left-aligned bold for Level 2).
  • Tables and figures, if included, are numbered sequentially and include titles and notes.
Looking Ahead
Chapter 41: Chicago Style

Chapter 40 introduced APA style, the documentation system of the social and behavioral sciences. Chapter 41 turns to Chicago style — the system most commonly required in history, classical studies, and certain humanities fields. Chicago style offers two documentation options: notes-bibliography style (which uses footnotes or endnotes rather than in-text citations) and author-date style (which resembles APA). Understanding when and why each system is used will give you the documentation versatility to write in any academic field.

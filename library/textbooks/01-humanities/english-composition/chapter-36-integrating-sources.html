Chapter 36 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Using sources well is one of the most important skills in academic writing, and also one of the most commonly misunderstood. Many students assume that the main challenge of research is finding sources—that once the investigation is done, the writing will follow naturally. In practice, the harder work is what comes after: deciding how to bring source material into your writing in a way that strengthens your argument without replacing it.

This chapter teaches source integration. Integration is not simply about inserting quotations or paraphrases at the right moments. It is about maintaining your voice as the author of the paper while using source material to build, support, and deepen your reasoning. A well-integrated source does not speak for itself—it speaks in service of your ideas.

The chapter covers six interconnected skills: treating sources as evidence rather than answers, using the source sandwich structure, introducing sources with effective signal phrases, explaining what source evidence means after you present it, comparing sources when they offer different perspectives, and synthesizing multiple sources into a unified argument. Together, these skills give you control over research writing instead of letting your sources control you.

Opening Question

Think of a research paper you have written or read. Did the writer seem to be using sources to build an argument—or did the sources seem to be doing the arguing themselves? What is the difference? By the end of this chapter, you will be able to answer that question precisely and apply the distinction to your own writing.

Sections 36.1 – 36.2
The Fundamental Principle: Evidence, Not Conclusions
Sources as Evidence · The Source Sandwich
Section 36.1

Sources as Evidence

The most important principle in this chapter is also the simplest: sources are evidence, not conclusions. A source does not write your argument for you. A source gives you material—facts, expert opinions, data, interpretations, documented observations—that you as the writer must evaluate, explain, and connect to your own reasoning.

This may sound obvious, but many students write research papers in a way that accidentally reverses the relationship. They find a source that seems to say what they want to say, quote it, and then move on, as though the source has done the arguing for them. The result is a paper where the writer has essentially disappeared. Paragraphs consist of one quoted passage after another, loosely connected by phrases like "Another study says…" and "According to another expert…" The student's own thinking is missing.

Think of sources the way a lawyer thinks of evidence. A lawyer does not walk into a courtroom, drop a stack of documents on the table, and say nothing. Documents alone do not make the argument. The lawyer presents the evidence, explains what it means, connects it to the larger case, and anticipates how the other side might interpret it differently. The evidence matters because the lawyer frames it, explains it, and uses it toward a specific conclusion.

Your job as a research writer is exactly that. You are the lawyer. Your sources are your evidence. Every time you bring a source into your paper, your job is not just to present it—it is to show your reader what it means, why it matters, and how it supports the point you are building.

This principle also explains why it is possible to have too many sources. A paper packed with quotations and paraphrases from ten different scholars may look thorough, but if the writer's own analysis is absent, the paper has no argument—it has only a collection of other people's arguments. Strong research writing requires that your voice remain at the center of the work, directing the evidence toward your own conclusions.

Study Note

The test for good source integration is simple. After reading a paragraph in your draft, ask: Whose thinking drives this paragraph—mine or my source's? Your reasoning should be doing most of the work. The source should be reinforcing or illustrating it.

Section 36.2

Source Sandwiches

One of the most practical frameworks for integrating sources is a structure sometimes called the source sandwich. The name describes a three-part pattern: you introduce the source, present the source material, and then explain what it means in relation to your argument. Like a sandwich, the source goes in the middle, held in place by your own writing on both sides.

The Three Layers of the Source Sandwich

Layer 1: Introduction — You establish who or what the source is, why it is relevant, and what role it will play in your paragraph's reasoning.

Layer 2: Source Material — You present the borrowed content: a quotation, a paraphrase, or a summary.

Layer 3: Explanation — You explain, analyze, or interpret what the source material means and connect it to your argument.

Why All Three Layers Matter

Beginning writers often skip the first or third layer, treating only the source material itself as the essential part. Both omissions weaken the writing in distinct ways.

When the introduction layer is missing, quotations and paraphrases appear without context. The reader does not know who is being quoted, why this person has authority, or how the material connects to the paragraph's purpose. A quotation that arrives without preparation is disorienting—it forces the reader to figure out where it came from rather than focusing on what it means.

When the explanation layer is missing, the source appears to speak the final word on the subject. The writer presents the evidence and stops, as though evidence explains itself. But evidence never entirely explains itself. A quotation from a researcher, a statistic from a report, or a paraphrase of a scholarly argument always requires interpretation. Why does it matter? What does it prove? How does it support this specific claim? The explanation layer is where you answer those questions.

Model Source Sandwich
A fully developed three-layer paragraph on climate science

[Introduction Layer] Climate scientists have documented accelerating changes in polar ice coverage over the past several decades. Glaciologist Marika Holland, whose research focuses on Arctic sea ice trends, notes that the rate of summer ice loss has increased significantly since the 1970s.

[Source Material] As Holland explains, "The Arctic is losing sea ice at a pace that exceeds what most climate models predicted even a decade ago, and the consequences for global weather patterns are only beginning to be understood."

[Explanation Layer] This acceleration matters for more than environmental reasons. As ice reflects less solar energy back into the atmosphere, the warming effect compounds—meaning that the longer-term projections for temperature rise may themselves be underestimates. The question is not simply whether ice is melting, but whether our models can keep pace with how quickly the conditions are changing.

Application

Notice that the explanation layer does not restate the quotation—it draws a further implication from it, moving the argument forward. The source provided the material; the writer provided the thinking. That division of labor is what source integration looks like in practice.

Sections 36.3 – 36.4
Introducing and Explaining: Signal Phrases and Analysis
Signal Phrases · Reporting Verbs · Explaining Source Evidence
Section 36.3

Introducing Sources

The introduction layer of the source sandwich depends on what are called signal phrases: short introductory constructions that tell the reader who is speaking, what role the source plays, and how the borrowed material relates to the paragraph's argument. Signal phrases almost always include the author's name, a relevant credential, and a reporting verb that characterizes what the source is doing.

The choice of verb is not neutral. Different verbs signal different relationships between the source and your argument. Using the word says for every source is technically acceptable but stylistically weak and analytically imprecise. A verb like argues tells the reader that this source is making a contestable claim—not merely reporting a fact. A verb like challenges tells the reader the source is entering a debate. These distinctions help the reader follow the intellectual relationships between sources and between the sources and your argument.

Reporting VerbWhat It SuggestsWhen to Use It
arguesThe source takes a debatable positionWhen the source makes a contestable claim
explainsThe source clarifies or defines somethingWhen the source breaks down a concept or process
demonstratesThe source shows evidence supporting a claimWhen the source presents data or proof
notesThe source makes an observation, often secondaryWhen the point is supplementary, not central
contendsThe source asserts something you may later challengeWhen you will complicate or refute the source
suggestsThe source points toward a conclusion without certaintyWhen the evidence is tentative or preliminary
challengesThe source disputes a prevailing viewWhen the source counters an established position
concludesThe source arrives at a finding based on analysisWhen the source presents the result of a study
reportsThe source presents factual information or findingsWhen the source is describing data or events
emphasizesThe source stresses a particular point as importantWhen the source returns to or underscores a key idea

Varying Signal Phrase Placement

Signal phrases do not always have to appear before source material. They can be placed before, within, or after a quotation, though placement before is most common and most accessible for developing writers.

Signal Phrase Placement — Three Options
Using the same source with three different placements

Before: Economist Paul Krugman argues that austerity measures in the aftermath of a recession often delay recovery rather than accelerate it.

Within: "The evidence," Krugman argues, "consistently shows that cutting public spending during a downturn contracts the economy at exactly the moment it needs stimulus."

After: In cases where fiscal response fails to stabilize markets, austerity measures often prolong economic weakness—or so Krugman contends in his analysis of post-recession policy.

Establishing Source Credibility

Your introduction layer should do more than name the source. It should give the reader enough information to understand why this source has relevant authority. A brief credential—a professional role, an institutional affiliation, an area of expertise, or the nature of a study—helps the reader evaluate the evidence appropriately. You do not need a full biography. A phrase or clause is usually sufficient.

Weak — No credential or context
According to Smith, traffic fatalities increased in states that reduced highway speed limit enforcement.
Stronger — Credential and study scope included
According to transportation safety researcher James Smith, whose longitudinal study tracked fatality rates across thirty-two states over a fifteen-year period, traffic fatalities increased in states that reduced highway speed limit enforcement.
Common Mistake

Introducing a source only by last name the first time it appears—without any signal of who the person is or why they are relevant—leaves the reader without the context to evaluate the claim. The first time you introduce a source, give enough background for the reader to understand the source's authority. After that first introduction, the last name alone is generally sufficient.

Section 36.4

Explaining Source Evidence

Placing a quotation or paraphrase into a paragraph is not enough. After you present source material, you must explain it. This step—the explanation layer of the source sandwich—is where most of the analytical work in a research paper actually happens, and it is the step most commonly skipped by developing writers.

Explanation after a source serves several functions. It tells the reader what the evidence means in the context of your argument. It prevents the source from appearing to make your entire point for you. It shows the reader that you have thought carefully about the evidence. And it keeps your analytical voice at the center of the paragraph.

What Explanation Is Not

Explanation is not repetition. Restating the quotation in slightly different words is not analysis—it is redundant summary. If you quote a researcher saying that urban green spaces reduce residents' stress levels, then explain by writing "In other words, green spaces in cities help people feel less stressed," you have added nothing. The reader understood the quotation.

Explanation is not validation either. Writing "This shows how important green spaces are" after a quotation is not analysis—it is a vague endorsement. It tells the reader you found the evidence impressive without explaining what it actually demonstrates about your argument.

What Real Explanation Does

Genuine explanation does at least one of the following:

· Identifies what the evidence specifically proves in relation to your paragraph's claim

· Draws out an implication not immediately obvious from the evidence itself

· Connects the evidence to the larger argument of the paper

· Qualifies or limits the claim the evidence makes

· Anticipates how the evidence might be questioned and addresses that challenge

Before and After — Explanation Layer
The same source presented with and without genuine explanation

Without explanation (weak):

Linguist Deborah Tannen has argued that men and women often use language for different purposes in conversation, with men more often using talk to convey information and establish status, while women more often use talk to build connection. "For many men, talk is primarily a means to preserve independence and negotiate and maintain status in a hierarchical social order." This shows that gender plays a role in communication.


With genuine explanation (strong):

Linguist Deborah Tannen has argued that men and women often use language for different purposes in conversation, with men more often using talk to convey information and establish status, while women more often use talk to build connection. "For many men, talk is primarily a means to preserve independence and negotiate and maintain status in a hierarchical social order." What this distinction reveals is that communication breakdown between people of different genders is often not a failure of language itself but a failure of assumed shared purpose. When two people enter a conversation with fundamentally different expectations about what conversation is supposed to accomplish, misunderstanding does not require bad intentions—it is built into the structure of the exchange itself.

The explained version uses Tannen's claim as a launching point for a further argument. The inference—that communication breakdown is structural, not intentional—is the writer's contribution. The source provided the material; the writer provided the thinking.

As a general rule, your explanation should be at least as long as the source material you are explaining, and usually longer. A three-sentence quotation warrants at least three sentences of analysis, often more. A brief paraphrase still requires at least one or two sentences of genuine follow-through. If your paragraphs consist mainly of long quotations with brief explanatory tags, your own thinking has gone missing from the paper.

Sections 36.5 – 36.6
Working with Multiple Sources: Comparison and Synthesis
Comparing Sources · Synthesizing Multiple Sources
Section 36.5

Comparing Sources

Research writing often involves working with multiple sources that address the same question, sometimes in agreement and sometimes in tension. When two or more sources speak to the same point, you have the opportunity to do something more intellectually interesting than simply presenting them in sequence—you can compare them.

Comparing sources means showing the reader explicitly how two or more sources relate to each other. They might agree on the facts but disagree on interpretation. They might use different methods to arrive at similar conclusions. They might approach the same problem from different disciplines, offering complementary insights. Or they might contradict each other outright, forcing you to evaluate which view is better supported or to explain why the contradiction exists.

Presenting a single source per point can make a research paper feel like a list: one source supports point A, a different source supports point B, another source supports point C. When you compare sources, you demonstrate that you have thought about the relationships between ideas, not just the ideas themselves. You show the reader that the academic conversation around your topic is complex, that researchers disagree and debate, and that you are an active participant in that conversation.

Language for Comparing Sources

RelationshipUseful Transitional Phrases
Agreement"Similarly, [Author B] finds that…" / "This view is supported by…" / "Both [A] and [B] conclude that…" / "Consistent with [A]'s findings, [B] demonstrates that…"
Contrast"While [A] argues that…, [B] contends that…" / "In contrast to [A]'s position, [B] suggests…" / "[B], however, reaches a different conclusion…" / "Not all researchers agree: [B] challenges this view…"
Complementary"Where [A] examines the economic dimensions, [B] focuses on the social ones, together suggesting that…" / "[A]'s historical analysis and [B]'s sociological study converge on…"
Model Comparison Paragraph
Two sources compared and synthesized around a shared conclusion

Researchers who study decision-making under uncertainty have found that people consistently overestimate their ability to predict outcomes they cannot actually control. Psychologist Daniel Kahneman describes this tendency as the "illusion of validity"—a persistent confidence in one's own judgments even when feedback demonstrates that those judgments are unreliable. Behavioral economist Richard Thaler reaches a complementary conclusion through a different approach: studying how investors respond to market downturns, he finds that people double down on failing strategies rather than revise their expectations, a behavior he attributes to the same overconfidence Kahneman documents in laboratory settings. Together, these findings suggest that overconfidence is not a quirk of inexperienced decision-makers—it appears across contexts, from psychology experiments to financial markets, and is resistant even to repeated evidence of failure. This has significant implications for how organizations design feedback systems: if confidence does not naturally calibrate in response to experience, feedback must be structured in ways that force recalibration.

Notice how the paragraph uses both sources not merely to stack up evidence but to build a stronger claim through their relationship. Kahneman and Thaler study different phenomena using different methods, but their convergence makes the writer's argument more persuasive. The final sentences use both sources together to arrive at a practical implication neither source stated explicitly—and that implication belongs to the writer.

Section 36.6

Synthesizing Multiple Sources

Comparison involves placing two sources in relationship. Synthesis takes this further: it means drawing multiple sources together into a unified argument that reflects your own thinking about the topic as a whole. Synthesis is the highest-level source integration skill, and it is what distinguishes genuinely strong research writing from mere reporting.

In a synthesized argument, you do not walk the reader through your sources one at a time, explaining each in sequence. Instead, you identify the threads, patterns, tensions, and conclusions that run across your sources, and you weave those threads into an argument that belongs to you—one that could not have been constructed from any single source alone.

What Synthesis Is Not

Synthesis is often confused with summary, especially summary of multiple sources. A common error is what researchers in composition education call the source-by-source structure: the writer devotes one paragraph to source A, the next to source B, the next to source C, and then writes a brief conclusion noting that "all three sources agree." This structure is not synthesis—it is multiple summaries placed in sequence.

True synthesis does not organize by source. It organizes by idea. You are not writing a tour of your sources. You are building an argument, and the sources are evidence you draw on as needed to support it. Different sources may appear in the same paragraph because they both speak to the same aspect of your argument. A single source may appear in multiple places in your paper because it bears on multiple dimensions of your analysis. The question is never "What does each of my sources say?" but "What does my argument need to establish at this point, and which sources help me make it?"

Annotated vs. Synthesized Approach
The same three sources handled in two different ways

Research question: To what extent do standardized testing policies affect student motivation?


Annotated approach (source-by-source): Jones (2018) argues that standardized tests reduce intrinsic motivation by shifting students' focus from learning to performance. Smith (2020) reports that students in high-stakes testing environments show lower engagement with challenging material. Williams (2022) notes that the effects vary significantly by demographic and prior academic achievement.


Synthesized approach (organized by idea): The research on standardized testing and motivation consistently identifies a common mechanism: when assessment is high-stakes and externally imposed, students shift their orientation from mastering material to managing their performance on a specific measure. Jones (2018) documents this shift in motivation patterns, while Smith (2020) traces its behavioral consequences in classroom engagement. Importantly, Williams (2022) complicates this picture by showing that the effects are not uniform—students with stronger prior achievement appear more resilient to motivational decline, which suggests that the testing environment interacts with existing academic identity in ways that simple policy comparisons often miss. The research does not simply tell us that standardized testing harms motivation. It tells us that testing harms motivation for specific students in specific conditions, and that identifying those conditions is essential to designing more equitable assessments.

Strategies for Building Synthesis

  1. 1Group sources by idea, not by identity. Before drafting, sort your notes by the sub-questions or sub-claims of your argument. Which sources address causes? Which address effects? Which complicate the main claim? This sorting prepares you to write about ideas rather than writing about sources.
  2. 2Look for patterns and tensions. When multiple sources arrive at similar conclusions, that agreement supports your claim. When they disagree, you have a tension to explain—and explaining that tension usually advances your argument more than summarizing either position alone.
  3. 3Let the argument ask the questions. Instead of asking "What does each source say?" ask "What does my argument need to establish at this point, and which sources speak to that need?" This reversal keeps the writing centered on your reasoning.
  4. 4Use connective language that signals synthesis. Phrases like "taken together," "across these studies," "the pattern that emerges," and "what these findings collectively suggest" signal that you are synthesizing, not just accumulating evidence.
Common Mistake

Writing "All three sources agree that…" as a synthesis move is too simple. Real synthesis does not just note agreement—it explains what the agreement means, what it supports in your argument, and what questions it does or does not resolve. Agreement is material for synthesis; the synthesis itself is what you do with that material.

Sections 36.7 – 36.8
Balance, Voice, and Revision
Balancing Source Material and Analysis · Revising Source Integration
Section 36.7

Balancing Source Material and Original Analysis

One of the most important judgments in research writing is knowing how much of your paper should come from sources and how much should come from your own analysis. Getting this balance right is the difference between a paper that reports and a paper that argues.

There is no universal formula. Different assignments, disciplines, and genres have different conventions. An empirical science paper relies heavily on data sources and may involve relatively brief analysis sections. A literary analysis essay may quote frequently from a primary text while relying almost entirely on the writer's own interpretation. A policy analysis paper may draw on statistics and expert opinion while spending most of its pages on the writer's reasoning.

Despite these variations, a general principle holds: in most academic writing, the majority of the intellectual work should come from the writer. Sources are tools. If your paper is dominated by direct quotation, the sources have overwhelmed the argument.

Signs That Source Material Has Taken Over

  • ·Block quotations everywhere. Long block quotations—especially when they appear frequently—signal that the writer is using source length to fill space rather than selecting evidence precisely. A block quotation should appear only when the exact wording is crucial and cannot be adequately captured in a shorter excerpt or paraphrase.
  • ·Quotation stacking. Two or more consecutive quotations without intervening analysis—sometimes called quotation stacking—gives the reader a series of voices without knowing what to do with them. Each source should be followed by explanation before the next source is introduced.
  • ·Sources answering questions the writer should answer. If you pose a research question in your introduction and then spend the body of your paper quoting scholars' answers to it, you have written a literature review, not an argument. Your voice needs to appear in every section, directing evidence toward your own conclusions.
  • ·Thin transitions. Transitions like "Another study found…" and "According to yet another expert…" connect sources to each other but not to any overarching argument. If your transitions primarily announce sources rather than advance ideas, your structure may be source-driven rather than idea-driven.

Signs That Analysis Has Replaced Evidence

The opposite problem—a paper that makes large, confident claims without adequate source support—is less common but equally serious. In some academic contexts, this appears as unsupported generalizations: broad statements about what "most people believe" or "all experts agree" that are not backed by documented evidence. In others, it appears as pure speculation—the writer reasons carefully but does not ground that reasoning in sources.

Good research writing needs both. Sources provide the evidential grounding that makes your claims credible. Your analysis provides the thinking that makes the evidence meaningful. Neither can substitute for the other.

Practical Guideline

A useful rough guideline for most undergraduate research essays: aim for roughly one-third source material and two-thirds original writing, measured across the whole paper. If you read through your draft and find that more than a third of every paragraph consists of direct quotations or close paraphrases, you likely need to reduce your quotation, strengthen your analysis, or both.

Application — The Highlighter Audit

Print out a draft of a research essay and use two highlighters: one for source material (quotations, paraphrases, summaries) and one for your own writing (introductions, transitions, explanations, arguments, conclusions). If the first color covers significantly more of the page than the second, revise by strengthening your explanation layers and replacing unnecessary direct quotations with paraphrase where appropriate.

Section 36.8

Revising Source Integration

The skills covered in this chapter are most easily applied during revision. In a first draft, many writers get through the paper by inserting sources wherever they seem relevant and promising to improve the integration later. That approach is reasonable as long as the revision actually happens. This section gives you a systematic method for checking and improving source integration in any research draft.

  1. Step 1Identify every source entry point. Mark every place where a quotation, paraphrase, or summary enters the paper. For each entry point, ask: Is there an introduction layer? Does the reader know who is speaking and why the source is relevant before encountering the source material? Is the signal phrase specific and accurate?
  2. Step 2Check every exit point. For each entry point, locate the exit: the moment after the source material ends and your own writing must resume. Ask: Does the explanation tell the reader what the evidence means in the context of this paragraph's claim? Does it do more than restate the source? Does it connect the source to the larger argument? Is it long enough?
  3. Step 3Audit source distribution. Look at the overall distribution of sources through the paper. Is any single source being used too heavily? Are there sections with no sources where evidence would strengthen the argument? Are there sections so densely sourced that your voice has disappeared?
  4. Step 4Check for synthesis. Look at sections where you use two or more sources. Are they being synthesized or merely listed? Is there language that draws the sources into relationship with each other? Is your argument driving the section, or are the sources driving it?
  5. Step 5Read for voice. Read the entire paper with one question in mind: Whose paper is this? Your voice should be the dominant presence throughout—directing evidence, building reasoning, arriving at conclusions. If the paper sounds like a collection of other people's ideas loosely connected by your transitions, revision is needed.
Revision Lab
Strengthening a source sandwich in a real draft paragraph

Take a paragraph from your current draft that uses at least one source. Copy it onto a separate page. Then revise it explicitly using the source sandwich structure: strengthen the introduction layer, adjust the signal phrase to use a precise reporting verb, and write at least three sentences of genuine explanation after the source material. Compare the original and revised versions. The revised version should feel more analytical and more clearly authored by you.

Key Terms Review

source integration
The process of incorporating borrowed material into a piece of writing in a way that supports the writer's argument while maintaining the writer's voice.
source sandwich
A three-part structure for presenting source material: introduction of the source, presentation of the source material, and explanation of what the source material means.
signal phrase
A phrase that introduces source material, typically including the author's name, a relevant credential, and a reporting verb.
reporting verb
A verb that characterizes what a source does: argues, explains, demonstrates, suggests, challenges, concludes, and so on. Word choice conveys the intellectual role of the source.
explanation layer
The portion of a source sandwich that follows the source material, where the writer analyzes what the evidence means and connects it to the argument.
quotation stacking
The practice of placing two or more quotations or paraphrases in sequence without intervening analysis. Generally a significant weakness in research writing.
source-by-source structure
An organization pattern in which the writer covers one source at a time rather than organizing by idea. The opposite of synthesis.
synthesis
The practice of drawing multiple sources together into a unified argument organized by the writer's ideas rather than by the sources themselves.
comparison of sources
The explicit identification of agreements, disagreements, complementary perspectives, or tensions between two or more sources.
source balance
The proportion of source material to original analysis in a piece of writing. In most academic essays, original analysis should predominate.
patchwriting
A form of problematic source use in which a writer closely follows the language or structure of a source without genuine paraphrase or attribution. Discussed further in Chapter 35.

  3. Find a paragraph in any academic article or textbook—not this one—that uses a source. Analyze it: Is there an introduction layer? A signal phrase? An explanation layer? How long is the explanation relative to the source material? What does the explanation accomplish?
  4. The chapter distinguishes between a paper that reports and a paper that argues. In your own experience as a student, how have teachers drawn—or failed to draw—this distinction? How is it similar to or different from how this chapter describes it?
  5. Synthesis requires organizing by idea rather than by source. This is harder than it sounds. What makes it difficult? What preparation strategies might help a writer build toward synthesis rather than falling into a source-by-source structure?

Practice Activities

Activity 36ASignal Phrase Revision

Each of the following sentences introduces a source poorly. Revise each one to include an effective signal phrase that names the source, establishes its relevance, and uses an appropriate reporting verb. You may invent plausible but realistic details for author names and credentials.

  1. "According to a source, poverty rates in rural areas have increased since 2000."
  2. "A study says that sleep deprivation affects cognitive performance."
  3. "An expert thinks that social media use is linked to anxiety in teenagers."
  4. "According to another scholar, the interpretation of the poem has changed over time."
  5. "Someone found that bilingual students outperform monolingual students on certain cognitive tasks."
Activity 36BWriting the Explanation Layer

For each of the following quotations, write at least three sentences of genuine explanation. Your explanation should not simply restate the quotation. It should draw out an implication, connect the evidence to a broader argument, or identify what the evidence means in context. The topic and argument context are provided for each.

  1. Topic: The value of community libraries in urban neighborhoods.
    Quotation: "Public libraries consistently rank as the municipal service residents trust most, a finding that holds across income levels, education levels, and political affiliation."
    Your explanation should develop what this pattern of trust means for arguments about library funding and public investment.
  2. Topic: How learning a second language affects cognitive development.
    Quotation: "Bilingual children have been shown to perform better on tasks requiring focused attention and the suppression of irrelevant information, even when their vocabulary scores are lower than their monolingual peers."
    Your explanation should address what this paradox suggests about how language learning shapes cognitive development.
  3. Topic: The effects of urban noise pollution on residents' health.
    Quotation: "Long-term exposure to traffic noise above certain decibel thresholds is associated with increased rates of cardiovascular disease, independent of air pollution exposure."
    Your explanation should connect this finding to arguments about how cities prioritize health in urban planning decisions.
Activity 36CSource Sandwich Construction

Using any legitimate source—an article from a library database, a textbook chapter, or a credible news organization—find a passage that could serve as evidence for a paragraph about a topic of your choice. Write a complete source sandwich paragraph: introduce the source with context and a precise signal phrase; present the source material as either a direct quotation or a paraphrase; write at least four sentences of explanation that analyze the evidence and connect it to a specific claim. Then label the three layers of the source sandwich in your finished paragraph.

Activity 36DSynthesis Practice

Below are three brief claims drawn from imaginary sources on the topic of urban noise pollution and public health. Your task is to synthesize them into a single coherent paragraph organized by idea, not by source. Do not simply summarize each claim in sequence. Draw the claims into relationship with each other and build toward a unified argument about what cities should do.

Source Claims for Synthesis

Source A (public health researcher): Long-term exposure to traffic noise above certain decibel levels is associated with increased rates of cardiovascular disease, independent of air pollution exposure.

Source B (urban planning scholar): The neighborhoods with the highest noise exposure are disproportionately low-income neighborhoods, where residents have less access to private green space and sound insulation.

Source C (behavioral psychologist): Chronic background noise degrades residents' ability to concentrate and recover cognitively from stress, compounding the physical health effects with mental health consequences.

Your synthesis paragraph should make an argument about what these findings collectively suggest for how cities should think about noise—not just describe what each study says.

Activity 36ESource Balance Audit

Take a research essay draft you have already written—for this class or another—and complete the following steps. First, highlight all source material in one color (quotations, paraphrases, summaries). Second, highlight all original writing in a second color (your introductions, transitions, explanations, arguments, conclusions). Third, calculate the approximate ratio. Fourth, identify the two paragraphs where source material is most out of proportion. Fifth, revise those two paragraphs using the source sandwich structure, strengthening the explanation layers and reducing unnecessary direct quotations with paraphrase where appropriate. Finally, write a brief reflection (one paragraph) on what changed and why the revised version is stronger.

Chapter Checklist

Before moving on, verify that you can do the following.

  • ☐Explain the source sandwich structure and describe the purpose of each of its three layers
  • ☐Write a signal phrase that names a source, establishes its relevance, and uses a precise reporting verb

  • ☐Write a genuine explanation layer after a quotation or paraphrase—one that analyzes rather than restates
  • ☐Identify quotation stacking in a draft and revise it by inserting explanation between sources

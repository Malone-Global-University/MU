Chapter 24 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Every academic essay you write serves a purpose. Sometimes you are asked to explain what a text says. Sometimes you are asked to evaluate what it means. Sometimes you are asked to defend a position, compare two ideas, solve a problem, or synthesize research from multiple sources. These different purposes call for different approaches, and academic writing has developed recognizable structures — essay types — that help writers organize their thinking and help readers follow their ideas.

Learning to recognize essay types is not about memorizing formulas. It is about understanding what different kinds of intellectual work look like when put on the page. A student who understands the difference between a summary-response essay and an analytical essay will not make the mistake of simply retelling a text when the instructor expects interpretation. A student who understands what an argument essay demands will not settle for presenting balanced information when the assignment calls for a defended position.

This chapter introduces the major essay types you will encounter and produce throughout your academic life: the summary-response essay, the analytical essay, the comparison and contrast essay, the argument essay, the problem-solution essay, the literary and textual analysis essay, and the research essay. Each type is explained in terms of its purpose, its typical structure, its essential features, and the common mistakes students make when writing it.

These essay types are not rigid boxes. Real academic writing often blends elements from more than one type. An argument essay might include analysis. A research essay might make a comparative claim. A literary analysis might involve a degree of personal response. What matters is understanding the core logic of each type so you can use that logic deliberately, adjust it when needed, and recognize what any given assignment is actually asking you to do.

Section 24.1

Why Essay Types Matter

The question of why essay types matter is worth pausing on before moving into the specifics. Students sometimes resist the idea of essay types, viewing them as artificial constraints on self-expression. This concern is understandable, but it misses something important about how academic writing actually works.

Academic writing is a form of communication, and like all communication it depends on shared conventions. When a reader picks up an argument essay, they expect to encounter a debatable claim supported by evidence and reasoning. When they read a comparison essay, they expect both subjects to receive substantial treatment and the comparison to serve some interpretive or evaluative purpose. When they read a research essay, they expect to find evidence from multiple credible sources integrated into an original line of reasoning. These are not arbitrary expectations. They are the result of centuries of academic practice, and they exist because they help knowledge circulate efficiently and reliably.

Learning essay types is therefore not about following rules for their own sake. It is about learning to participate in academic conversation with competence and credibility. Writers who understand essay types produce work that readers can follow. Writers who ignore these conventions — not out of intentional innovation, but out of confusion about what is expected — often produce essays that frustrate readers, even when the underlying ideas are good.

There is also a practical benefit. Understanding essay types gives you a framework for reading assignments. When an instructor asks you to "analyze the causes of World War I," you are not being asked to tell the story of the war. When an instructor asks you to "argue for or against a policy," you are not being asked to present both sides neutrally. Understanding what different assignment verbs mean — analyze, argue, compare, evaluate, respond, synthesize — puts you in control of your writing from the very start.

Finally, essay types reflect different kinds of intellectual work. Summary asks you to understand. Analysis asks you to interpret. Argument asks you to reason and persuade. Research asks you to investigate and synthesize. Each of these is a real cognitive skill, and developing fluency with each makes you a stronger thinker, not just a stronger writer.

Summary and Response Essays

Section 24.2

What a Summary-Response Essay Does

The summary-response essay is one of the most common types assigned in early composition courses, and one of the most frequently misunderstood. The essay has two distinct parts: a summary of a source text, and a response to that text. Each part requires different skills, and confusing the two is one of the most predictable problems in student writing.

A summary presents what the source text says — accurately, fairly, and in the writer's own words — without adding interpretation, evaluation, or personal reaction. A response presents the writer's thinking about the text: whether its argument is convincing, whether its evidence is strong, whether its conclusions are important, where the writer agrees or disagrees, and why.

The summary-response format teaches a foundational academic skill: the ability to represent another writer's ideas accurately before engaging with them. This skill matters beyond composition class. Every discipline requires its practitioners to read carefully, summarize fairly, and respond thoughtfully. In a history course, you summarize a historian's argument before evaluating it. In a science course, you summarize the findings of a study before analyzing their implications. In a business course, you summarize a case analysis before recommending a course of action. The summary-response essay is training for all of these.

The Summary Section

A strong summary accomplishes three things. First, it identifies the source clearly — the author's name, the title of the text, and often its publication context. Second, it presents the source's main claim or thesis. Third, it covers the key supporting points without getting lost in minor details.

The summary should be proportionate to the response. In most summary-response essays, the summary occupies roughly one-quarter to one-third of the total length. A summary that goes on for most of the essay leaves no room for the more demanding intellectual work of response.

One of the most important rules for summary writing is this: do not let your own opinions enter the summary. The summary section is not the place for phrases like "the author is wrong to say" or "I disagree with this point." The summary's job is to represent the source as the author intended it to be understood. A reader who only read your summary should come away with a fair picture of the original text.

Model — Summary Opening

In her essay "The Myth of Multitasking," Dr. Christine Rosen argues that the modern habit of simultaneously performing multiple digital tasks does not improve productivity but instead degrades attention, retention, and the quality of all the tasks involved. Rosen supports this claim by drawing on cognitive science research, historical analysis of the concept of attention, and observations of how digital devices have changed everyday behavior.

Study Note — What the Model Does Well

The summary above identifies the author, the text, and the main claim, then briefly indicates the kind of support the author uses. It makes no judgment about whether Rosen is right or wrong. That judgment belongs in the response. Notice that the summary uses a reporting verb ("argues") to signal that these are the author's ideas, not the writer's own conclusions.

The Response Section

The response section is where the writer's voice and reasoning take center stage. A strong response is not simply a record of emotional reactions ("This essay made me think about my own phone use"). It is an intellectually engaged evaluation of the source's ideas.

There are several legitimate directions a response can take. You might agree with the source's main argument while questioning a specific piece of evidence. You might disagree with the premise and explain why. You might agree in principle but identify important exceptions the author overlooked. You might explore the implications of the author's argument — what would follow if their conclusions are correct? You might connect the author's ideas to your own experience or to other texts you have read.

What a response should not be is vague or purely personal. "I found this essay interesting and it gave me a lot to think about" is not a response. A response makes a claim about the source and supports that claim with reasoning.

Common Mistake — The Summary That Never Ends

Many students write summary-response essays that are almost entirely summary. They summarize the source thoroughly, then add a final paragraph that begins with "In conclusion, I agree with the author's points." This is not a response. If your response consists of one undeveloped paragraph, revise. The response section needs its own development, its own reasoning, and its own specific references to the text.

Analytical Essays
What Analysis Means · The Analytical Thesis · Evidence and Interpretation
Section 24.3

What Analysis Means

Analysis is one of the most important words in academic writing, and it is also one of the most misunderstood. Students often hear "analyze this" and think they are being asked to describe or summarize more carefully. But analysis is a different kind of work entirely. Analysis means breaking something apart to understand how it works, why it is the way it is, or what it means.

An analytical essay does not simply report what happened or what a text says. It asks questions like: What are the parts of this thing, and how do they relate? What causes produced this effect? What does this mean or represent? What assumptions underlie this position? What patterns appear across these examples? The analytical essay answers such questions through close reading, careful reasoning, and specific evidence.

Analysis appears everywhere in academic work. A student analyzing a speech is not summarizing the speech. They are examining how the speaker's choices of language, structure, and evidence work together to create a particular effect or argument. A student analyzing a historical event is not retelling what happened. They are explaining the forces, decisions, and conditions that made the event possible. A student analyzing data from a scientific experiment is not describing the numbers. They are interpreting what the numbers reveal about the phenomenon under study.

The Analytical Thesis

Because analytical essays involve interpretation, the thesis of an analytical essay is an interpretive claim — a claim that answers the question "What does this mean?" or "How does this work?" or "Why did this happen?" An analytical thesis is not simply a fact. It can be supported, tested, and debated. It tells the reader what the essay will argue, not merely what it will be about.

An analytical thesis should be specific enough to be meaningful and broad enough to require development. A thesis that is too narrow can be exhausted in a paragraph. A thesis that is too vague gives the essay no direction.

Weak"Advertising affects how people think about themselves."
Stronger"Cosmetic advertising directed at teenage consumers systematically connects product use to social acceptance, creating an association between purchasing and belonging that shapes how young people understand their own worth."

The stronger thesis makes a specific claim that can be developed with evidence and reasoning. It tells the reader what kind of analysis to expect and commits to an interpretable position rather than a vague generality.

Building an Analytical Essay

An analytical essay typically opens with a brief context-setting introduction that leads to the analytical thesis. The body paragraphs each develop one part of the analysis, using specific evidence — quotations, examples, data, details — to support the analytical claims. Each body paragraph should do more than present evidence; it must explain what the evidence reveals, why it matters, and how it supports the larger analytical thesis.

The conclusion of an analytical essay does not simply restate the thesis. It reflects on the significance of the analysis: why does this interpretation matter? What does understanding this thing in this way help us see that we could not see before?

Study Note — Analysis Is Not Opinion

Students sometimes confuse analytical writing with personal opinion writing. The difference is crucial. An analytical claim is supported by evidence from the text or subject being analyzed, not by the writer's feelings or preferences. "I think the ending of this story is sad" is a personal response. "The ending of this story deliberately withholds the emotional release the reader has been led to expect, creating a sense of irreversible loss" is an analytical claim. One reflects the reader's experience; the other explains how the text works.

Common Mistake — Surface Description Instead of Analysis

Many students describe what happens in a text or event rather than explaining what it means or how it works. If your body paragraphs read like a retelling, you are describing, not analyzing. Ask yourself after each paragraph: "So what? Why does this detail matter? What does it reveal?" If you cannot answer those questions, the paragraph needs revision.

Section 24.4

The Purpose of Comparison

A comparison and contrast essay examines two or more subjects in relation to each other. But comparison in academic writing is not simply a list of similarities and differences. The most important question for any comparison essay is: why does this comparison matter? What do we learn by putting these two things side by side that we could not learn by studying them separately?

Comparison reveals relationships, patterns, and distinctions that are invisible when subjects are examined in isolation. Comparing two historical revolutions might reveal shared causes that explain revolutionary behavior across contexts. Comparing two scientific theories might reveal underlying assumptions about what counts as evidence. Comparing two literary texts might expose how different narrative techniques produce different effects on readers. In each case, the comparison is a tool for understanding something deeper than the subjects themselves.

This means that a strong comparison essay always has an analytical or argumentative purpose. It is not enough to note that Subject A and Subject B share certain features and differ in others. The comparison must be in service of a larger claim: that one approach is more effective, that both subjects reflect the same underlying cause, that apparent similarities hide important differences, or that examining the two together reveals a pattern worth understanding.

Organizing a Comparison Essay

There are two main organizational strategies for comparison essays, and each has advantages depending on the material and the purpose.

The point-by-point method organizes the essay by the points of comparison. For each point, the essay addresses both subjects before moving to the next point. This structure is well-suited for complex comparisons where showing the direct relationship between subjects on each point is important. It keeps the comparison active throughout the essay, and the reader never loses sight of how the two subjects relate.

The block method addresses one subject fully before addressing the other. This structure works well when the subjects are complex enough to require extended treatment and when the comparison is straightforward enough that readers can hold both subjects in mind across the essay. The block method requires stronger transitions and more explicit comparative language in the second block, since the two subjects are treated separately.

MethodStructureBest Used When
Point-by-PointEach paragraph covers one comparison point for both subjectsMultiple complex points of comparison; when direct contrast is the main goal
BlockFirst half covers Subject A fully; second half covers Subject B, referring back to ASubjects require extended individual treatment; fewer, clearer comparison points

Writing a Comparative Thesis

The thesis of a comparison essay should state both subjects and the analytical or evaluative claim the comparison is meant to support.

Weak"The American and French Revolutions had both similarities and differences."
Stronger"Although the American and French Revolutions were both fueled by Enlightenment principles of liberty and popular sovereignty, their divergent outcomes reveal how deeply the social and economic conditions preceding each revolution shaped whether those principles could be sustained in practice."
Common Mistake — Comparison Without a Purpose

Listing similarities and differences without a thesis is not a comparison essay — it is a list. Before drafting, ask: "What do I want the reader to understand about these two subjects after reading my comparison?" A comparison that produces no insight wastes both writer and reader. The comparison must be in service of an interpretive or evaluative claim.

Argument Essays

Section 24.5

What Academic Argument Is

The argument essay is perhaps the most fundamental type of academic writing. At its core, an argument essay makes a debatable claim and supports that claim with evidence and reasoning. It then addresses opposing viewpoints seriously, either refuting them or acknowledging their merits while explaining why the primary argument is still more persuasive.

It is important to understand what academic argument is not. It is not the same as conflict or confrontation. It is not the same as expressing a strong opinion. And it is not the same as simply presenting information on both sides of an issue. Academic argument is disciplined reasoning in service of a position the writer genuinely defends.

The claim of an argument essay must be debatable. A debatable claim is one that a reasonable person could disagree with. "Water is a chemical compound" is not debatable; it is a fact. "The United States should adopt a federal minimum wage of fifteen dollars per hour" is debatable; reasonable people disagree about it, and the disagreement can be informed by evidence and reasoning. Part of writing an argument essay is being clear about what kind of claim you are making — factual, causal, evaluative, or policy — because different kinds of claims require different kinds of support.

The Components of Argument

A well-built argument essay has several essential components working together.

The claim is the thesis of the argument. It states the position the essay will defend. It should be specific, debatable, and supportable with evidence.

Reasons explain why the claim should be accepted. They answer the question "Why should I believe this?" Each main reason typically becomes a section or set of paragraphs in the body of the essay.

Evidence supports the reasons. Evidence can take many forms: statistics, research findings, examples, expert testimony, historical precedent, and logical deduction. The quality of evidence matters enormously.

Explanation and analysis connect the evidence to the claim. Evidence does not speak for itself. A strong argument essay explains what the evidence shows, why it is relevant to the claim, and how it strengthens the argument.

Counterargument and rebuttal address opposing viewpoints. A credible argument acknowledges that other reasonable positions exist and explains why, despite those positions, the writer's claim is more persuasive. Ignoring counterarguments does not make them disappear; it makes the argument seem incomplete.

Concession and qualification acknowledge the limits of the claim. Strong academic arguers do not pretend their position is perfect or that every piece of evidence falls in their favor. They acknowledge complexity, limit the scope of their claim where necessary, and earn the reader's trust through intellectual honesty.

Application — Recognizing an Argument Assignment

Assignment prompts that call for argument essays often use verbs like: argue, defend, take a position on, persuade, evaluate whether, recommend, support or oppose. When you see these verbs, you know the assignment expects a thesis that makes a claim, not a balanced report. The difference matters: a balanced report presents multiple perspectives without taking a position; an argument essay commits to a position and defends it.

Common Mistake — Stating Opinion Without Evidence

An argument essay is not an opinion essay. "I believe" and "In my opinion" are not substitutes for evidence and reasoning. Your position needs to be supported by something beyond your own conviction. Likewise, avoid words like "always," "never," "everyone," and "obviously" unless you can genuinely support such sweeping assertions. Qualified language — "in many cases," "evidence suggests," "under these conditions" — is often more accurate and more persuasive.

Problem-Solution Essays
Problem Analysis · Connecting Solutions to Causes · Objections and Responses
Section 24.6

What Problem-Solution Essays Do

A problem-solution essay identifies a specific problem, analyzes its causes and significance, and proposes and defends one or more solutions. This essay type is especially common in courses related to public policy, business, health sciences, social science, and applied fields, but it appears across the curriculum wherever practical questions arise.

Problem-solution essays require analytical and argumentative skills working together. The analysis dimension involves examining the problem carefully: what is it, how serious is it, what causes it, who is affected, and why have previous attempts to solve it fallen short? The argumentative dimension involves proposing a solution and defending its effectiveness, feasibility, and superiority to alternatives.

A problem-solution essay is not simply a complaint. Students sometimes write problem sections that are detailed and forceful but then offer solutions that are vague, undeveloped, or naive. The solution section must receive as much careful development as the problem section. A proposed solution that cannot survive basic scrutiny — that ignores costs, overlooks practical obstacles, or fails to explain how it addresses the causes of the problem — is not a successful argument.

Identifying a Real Problem

The problem in a problem-solution essay should be specific, significant, and genuine. "Social media has problems" is not a problem claim that can anchor an essay. "The algorithm-based design of major social media platforms prioritizes engagement over accuracy, contributing to the rapid spread of health misinformation" is a specific problem claim that can be analyzed and addressed.

A strong problem section typically covers four things: what the problem is, how serious it is (evidence of scope and impact), what causes it, and why it persists despite existing responses. This last point is important. If a simple obvious solution already existed and was working, the problem would not still be a problem. Good problem analysis examines why the situation is genuinely difficult.

Proposing and Defending Solutions

The solution section proposes a specific response to the problem and defends it. A strong solution proposal typically explains what the solution is, how it directly addresses the causes of the problem, what evidence or reasoning suggests it would be effective, what obstacles might arise and how they could be addressed, and why it is preferable to alternative approaches.

Common Mistake — The Unconnected Solution

A solution that does not directly address the causes identified in the problem section is a logical flaw. If your problem analysis concludes that a health crisis is caused by lack of access to preventive care, a solution that focuses only on improving treatment for late-stage illness does not address the root cause. Make sure your solution connects explicitly to the causes your analysis has identified.

Section 24.7

The Purpose of Literary Analysis

Literary and textual analysis essays examine how texts — literature, film, speeches, historical documents, media, and other forms of communication — work and what they mean. They ask not just "what does this text say?" but "how does it say it, why does it say it this way, and what does that reveal?"

Literary analysis is a form of close reading. It requires careful attention to specific elements of a text — language, structure, imagery, characterization, narrative voice, argument, evidence, rhetorical appeals, cultural context — and the ability to make interpretive claims about how those elements work together to create meaning.

Literary analysis essays are common in courses in literature, writing, history, communication, cultural studies, and philosophy. They are also valuable in any context where you are expected to think critically about how texts shape understanding, arguments, and experience.

Close Reading as the Foundation

Literary analysis begins with close reading: reading slowly, carefully, and with attention to detail. Close reading means noticing not just what a text says but how it says it. You ask questions like: What words does the author choose, and why might those specific words matter? How is the text structured, and how does that structure affect meaning? What images, metaphors, or symbols appear, and what do they suggest? Whose perspective is represented, and whose is absent? What assumptions does the text make about its reader or audience?

Close reading often produces more interpretive material than any single essay can use. Part of the skill is learning to select the most significant details — the ones that most directly support your analytical thesis — and develop them fully rather than cataloguing every interesting feature of the text.

The Literary Analysis Thesis

A literary analysis thesis makes a specific, interpretive, arguable claim about the text. It does not simply identify a feature of the text; it argues for what that feature means or does.

Observation"In 1984, Orwell uses the image of the telescreen throughout the novel."
Thesis"In 1984, the telescreen functions not merely as a surveillance device but as a symbol of the way totalitarian systems work to make self-censorship automatic, ultimately replacing external control with internal compliance."

The observation identifies something in the text. The thesis makes a claim about what that something means and what it reveals about the text's larger argument. A literary analysis thesis should be specific enough to guide an essay and arguable enough to require support.

Integrating Textual Evidence

Literary analysis depends on textual evidence — quotations, paraphrases, and specific references to the text. Evidence should be chosen carefully, introduced clearly, quoted accurately where quotation is used, and explained thoroughly.

The principle of context-evidence-explanation is especially important in literary analysis. Before a quotation, establish the context: where in the text does this appear, and what is happening? After the quotation, provide analysis: what do these specific words, images, or choices reveal? What does this evidence show about your thesis?

Study Note — Don't Let Quotations Do Your Work

A common weakness in literary analysis essays is using quotations as if they speak for themselves. They do not. Even the most striking passage from a text requires analysis. What specific words are significant? What effect do they create? How do they relate to the essay's larger claim? The analysis you provide around the quotation is where the intellectual work happens.

Common Mistake — Plot Summary Instead of Analysis

Perhaps the most common problem in literary analysis essays is substituting plot summary or text summary for analysis. Plot summary tells what happens; analysis explains what it means or how it works. A literary analysis essay should contain minimal summary — just enough to orient the reader to the specific moment or passage being analyzed — and maximum interpretation. A practical test: if you could delete a paragraph from your essay and it would have no effect on your argument, it is probably summary that needs to be replaced with analysis.

Section 24.8

What Makes Research Essays Distinctive

The research essay is perhaps the most comprehensive academic essay type, drawing on skills from every other type covered in this chapter. A research essay requires you to investigate a question or problem using sources — books, journal articles, reports, data sets, primary documents — and to synthesize what you find into an original argument or analysis supported by that evidence.

The word original is important here. A research essay is not a report that collects and reports what others have said. It is a piece of writing in which your own thinking — your thesis, your analysis, your interpretation — is at the center, supported by evidence from sources. Sources serve your argument. Your argument does not simply follow from sources.

Research essays are the dominant form of academic writing in most upper-level undergraduate and graduate courses. The skills developed in academic research essays transfer directly to professional contexts as well: policy analysts, scientists, journalists, lawyers, business consultants, and scholars all produce forms of research writing regularly.

From Question to Thesis

Research essays begin with a research question — a genuine question about a topic that requires investigation to answer. The research question is not the same as the thesis. The research question is what you set out to investigate; the thesis is what you conclude after that investigation.

A research question should be focused, genuine, and answerable through available sources. As you research, you develop and test a tentative answer — a working thesis — that becomes more refined as your research deepens. The research process sometimes changes your initial hypothesis. You may discover evidence that undermines your original direction. You may find that the question leads somewhere unexpected. This is a sign that research is working, not failing. A research essay is a genuine inquiry, and genuine inquiry is open to surprise.

Synthesizing Sources

The most challenging skill in research writing is synthesis: bringing together ideas, findings, and evidence from multiple sources to support your own line of reasoning. Synthesis is different from summary and different from citation. Summary presents what one source says. Citation acknowledges where an idea comes from. Synthesis integrates material from multiple sources around your own analytical framework.

Strong synthesis requires you to see relationships between sources: where they agree, where they disagree, where one fills in a gap left by another, where one challenges the assumptions of another. These relationships become the intellectual substance of a research essay.

Application — What Synthesis Looks Like

Patchwork (not synthesis): Smith argues that urban green spaces improve mental health (Smith, 2019). Jones found that property values near parks increase over time (Jones, 2021). The City Planning Association reports that parks reduce heat island effects (CPA, 2020).

Synthesis: Multiple lines of evidence converge on the conclusion that urban green spaces generate compounding benefits. Research in environmental psychology demonstrates improvements in mental health outcomes for residents near parks (Smith, 2019), while real estate studies document corresponding increases in surrounding property values (Jones, 2021). These findings align with urban planning research showing that parks also reduce the heat island effect that disproportionately affects lower-income urban neighborhoods (CPA, 2020). Together, they suggest that green space investment is not simply an aesthetic preference but a public health and economic equity strategy.

In the synthesis version, the writer's thinking is in control. The sources are working together in service of an argument the writer is constructing, not simply being listed.

Common Mistake — The Research Essay That Is Really a Report

A report presents information. A research essay argues or analyzes. The distinction is in the thesis. If your thesis says "this essay will discuss X," you have a report structure. If your thesis says "the evidence suggests that X because of Y and Z," you have a research essay structure. Make sure your research essay takes an actual position and defends it.

Documentation in Research Essays

Because research essays rely on sources, documentation is essential. You must give credit to the sources of your ideas, quotations, paraphrases, and data. The specific documentation format — MLA, APA, Chicago, or another style — will depend on your discipline and assignment. What is universal is the requirement to cite. Documentation is covered in full in Parts VI and VII of this textbook. For the purposes of planning a research essay, remember that source tracking begins during the research phase, not the writing phase. Keep careful records of your sources so that documentation is not a crisis at the end of the process.

  • Sec 24.2The summary-response essay has two distinct parts: a neutral, accurate summary of a source, and a reasoned evaluative response. Summary represents what the source says; response makes a claim about it and develops that claim with reasoning. The two must be kept distinct and proportionate.
  • Sec 24.3Analysis means interpreting how something works or what it means, not summarizing or describing it. An analytical thesis is an interpretive, arguable claim. Body paragraphs must do more than present evidence — they must explain what the evidence reveals and how it supports the thesis.
  • Sec 24.4Comparison essays examine two or more subjects in relation to each other in service of an analytical or evaluative purpose. Point-by-point and block are the two main organizational methods. A strong comparative thesis states both subjects and makes a specific claim about what the comparison reveals.
  • Sec 24.5Argument essays make debatable claims and defend them with evidence, reasoning, and counterargument. The claim must be genuinely debatable. Counterargument should be engaged honestly. Qualified, precise language earns more trust than sweeping assertions.
  • Sec 24.6Problem-solution essays combine analysis (problem examination) and argument (solution defense). The solution must address the specific causes identified in the problem section. The solution section requires as much development as the problem section.
  • Sec 24.7Literary and textual analysis essays use close reading to make interpretive claims about how texts work and what they mean. The thesis must be arguable and interpretive, not merely observational. Textual evidence must be explained, not left to speak for itself. Plot summary is not analysis.
  • Sec 24.8Research essays begin with a research question and develop an original thesis supported by synthesized evidence from multiple sources. Synthesis integrates sources around the writer's own reasoning. Documentation is essential and begins during the research phase.

Key Terms Review

analytical essay
An essay that interprets, explains, or evaluates a subject by examining its parts, patterns, causes, or meanings, rather than simply reporting what is known about it.
argument essay
An essay that makes a debatable claim and defends it with evidence, reasoning, and responses to counterarguments.
block method
An organizational strategy for comparison essays in which one subject is treated fully before the other subject is introduced.
claim
A statement that can be debated, supported with evidence, and that forms the central position of an argument essay.
close reading
The careful, detailed examination of a text's language, structure, and rhetorical choices in order to support literary or textual analysis.
comparison essay
An essay that examines two or more subjects in relation to each other in order to reveal something meaningful about both, in service of an analytical or evaluative purpose.
counterargument
A position or argument that opposes the writer's claim, which the writer must acknowledge and respond to in an argument essay.
literary analysis essay
An essay that examines how a text works — through its language, structure, imagery, and choices — and what it means.
plot summary
A retelling of events in a narrative. It is different from analysis and should be minimized in literary analysis essays.
point-by-point method
An organizational strategy for comparison essays in which the essay moves through each comparison point, addressing both subjects under each point before moving to the next.
problem-solution essay
An essay that identifies a specific problem, analyzes it, and proposes and defends a solution that directly addresses the problem's causes.
research essay
An essay that investigates a question through multiple sources and synthesizes the evidence into an original argument or analysis.
research question
A genuine question that guides the research process, distinct from the thesis, which is the answer the research supports.
summary
A neutral, accurate presentation of a source's main ideas in the writer's own words, without evaluation or personal response.
summary-response essay
An essay composed of a neutral summary of a source followed by the writer's developed, evaluative response to that source.
synthesis
The integration of ideas and evidence from multiple sources around a writer's own analytical framework, as distinct from simple summarization or citation.
textual evidence
Specific quotations, paraphrases, or references to a text used to support analytical or argumentative claims.
working thesis
A preliminary thesis formed early in the writing or research process, expected to be refined and revised as the work develops.

Chapter Checklist

Use this checklist before submitting any essay to confirm that your work reflects the standards discussed in this chapter.

For All Essay Types
  • I have identified the essay type required by my assignment.
  • My thesis reflects the intellectual work appropriate to the essay type (interpretation, argument, comparison, proposal, synthesis, or response).
  • My thesis is specific, focused, and supportable with evidence.
  • Each body paragraph develops one idea and supports the thesis.
  • I have used specific evidence in every body paragraph.
  • I have explained my evidence rather than simply presenting it.
  • My introduction establishes context and leads clearly to the thesis.
  • My conclusion reflects on significance rather than simply restating the thesis.
  • My transitions connect ideas smoothly within and between paragraphs.
For Summary-Response Essays
  • My summary is accurate, fair, and written in my own words.
  • My summary does not include my own opinions or reactions.
  • My response makes a specific evaluative or analytical claim.
  • My response is developed with reasoning and specific references to the text.
For Analytical and Literary Analysis Essays
  • My thesis makes an interpretive claim, not just an observation.
  • I am analyzing (interpreting, explaining) rather than summarizing or describing.
  • Every quotation or reference is followed by analysis that explains its significance.
  • I have minimized summary and maximized interpretation.
For Argument Essays
  • My claim is genuinely debatable.
  • I have supported my claim with evidence from credible sources.
  • I have addressed at least one significant counterargument.
  • My evidence is explained and connected to my claim.
  • My language is fair, precise, and free of logical fallacies.
For Comparison Essays
  • I have a clear analytical or evaluative purpose for the comparison.
  • Both subjects receive substantive, balanced treatment.
  • My essay explicitly compares the subjects rather than describing them separately.
  • I have chosen a clear organizational method (point-by-point or block) appropriate to the material.
For Problem-Solution Essays
  • My problem analysis is specific and supported by evidence.
  • My solution directly addresses the causes identified in the problem section.
  • I have addressed likely objections to my solution.
  • My proposed solution is realistic and developed in detail.
For Research Essays
  • My thesis is an original analytical or argumentative claim, not a restatement of what sources say.
  • I have used at least the minimum number of required sources.
  • I have synthesized sources around my own reasoning rather than summarizing them separately.
  • All sources are properly cited according to the required documentation style.
  • My research question genuinely guided my investigation.

Chapter 8 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Every sentence you write makes a choice. That choice is not just about what to say — it is about how to say it, how much weight to give an idea, and how to show the relationship between one thought and the next. The sentence is the smallest complete unit of meaning in academic writing, and yet it is also one of the most flexible tools available to a writer. A single idea can be expressed as a short, direct statement, forceful and unqualified. It can be joined with another idea to show contrast or connection. It can be made to depend on something else, to qualify, to expand, or to emphasize. The structure of a sentence does more than package meaning — it shapes meaning.

This chapter introduces the four main sentence types in English: simple, compound, complex, and compound-complex. It explains how each type is built, what each type communicates, and how academic writers use all four in combination to create writing that is clear, varied, and effective. The chapter also addresses coordination and subordination — the two fundamental tools that allow writers to connect ideas — and explains how sentence variety and deliberate pattern choices improve the quality of academic prose.

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to identify any sentence by type, construct each type correctly, understand what each type communicates, and make informed choices about which sentence pattern best serves your purpose in academic writing.

Opening Question

Consider these two passages about the same historical event. Both are grammatically correct. Both contain accurate information. Why does one feel more analytical and mature than the other?

"The printing press was invented in the fifteenth century. Books became cheaper. Literacy rates began to rise. Ideas spread more quickly across Europe."

"When the printing press was invented in the fifteenth century, books became cheaper, and literacy rates began to rise as ideas spread more quickly across Europe."

The answer lies in sentence structure. By the end of this chapter, you will understand exactly what makes the second passage more effective — and you will have the tools to write that way deliberately.

Section 8.1
What a Complete Sentence Needs
Subject · Predicate · Complete Thought · The Foundation for All Sentence Types

Before examining sentence types, it is worth revisiting what makes a sentence complete in the first place. A sentence must do three things: it must have a subject, it must have a predicate (which contains a finite verb), and it must express a complete thought. These three requirements are not just grammatical rules to memorize — they reflect something fundamental about meaning. Every sentence answers, at minimum, two questions: who or what is the sentence about, and what is happening or what is being said about that subject?

The Subject

The subject is the noun, noun phrase, or pronoun that the sentence is about. It is the agent, the topic, or the thing being described, acted upon, or identified. Subjects can be simple — a single noun or pronoun — or they can be extended noun phrases with modifiers and qualifiers. What matters is that the sentence is organized around the subject, and everything else in the sentence relates to it.

The student revised her thesis three times before submitting the final draft. A careful analysis of the data revealed several unexpected patterns. She argued that the evidence supported a different conclusion.

The Predicate

The predicate is the part of the sentence that says something about the subject. At the heart of every predicate is a finite verb — a verb that is marked for tense and that agrees with the subject in number and person. The predicate may also include objects, complements, and various modifying phrases, but the verb is essential. The predicate does the active work of the sentence. It tells us what the subject does, what happens to it, what it is, or what state it is in. Without a predicate containing a finite verb, there is no sentence — there is only a fragment.

Complete Thought

The third requirement is perhaps the most important for understanding how sentences work: a sentence must express a complete thought. This is what distinguishes a complete sentence from a fragment. Some groups of words have a subject and even a verb-like word but still feel incomplete because they leave the reader waiting for more information.

Contrast: Fragment vs. Complete Sentence
The word although signals that a thought is unfinished
  • FragmentAlthough the research was extensive. — Has a subject and verb, but the word although signals that something more is coming. The thought is unfinished.
  • CompleteAlthough the research was extensive, the conclusion remained uncertain. — The dependent clause is completed by an independent clause. The thought is now whole.
Why This Matters for Sentence Types

Once you understand what a complete sentence requires, you can understand sentence types as different ways of combining and arranging complete and incomplete clauses. The four sentence types are not arbitrary categories — they represent genuinely different structural choices, each with its own effect on meaning, rhythm, and emphasis.

Section 8.2

Simple Sentences

The simple sentence is the most fundamental sentence structure. A simple sentence contains one independent clause and no dependent clauses. An independent clause is a group of words that has a subject, a finite verb, and expresses a complete thought — meaning it can stand alone as a sentence.

At its most reduced form, a simple sentence can be extraordinarily brief. Rivers flow. Language changes. Evidence matters. Each is complete: subject, verb, complete thought. However, the term "simple" refers to the clause structure, not the length or complexity of the content. A simple sentence can contain compound subjects, compound predicates, multiple modifying phrases, and considerable detail — as long as it contains only one independent clause and no dependent clauses.

The professor and the graduate students analyzed the survey data and presented their findings to the research committee.

This sentence is simple in structure: one independent clause, no dependent clauses. But it contains a compound subject, a compound predicate, and several noun phrases. What makes it simple is not its brevity but its single-clause structure.

What Simple Sentences Communicate

Simple sentences are direct and declarative. They make statements without qualification, without condition, without dependent relationships. This directness is a strength in the right context — when you want to state a fact clearly, announce a key point, or give emphasis to a finding, a simple sentence does the job with force. The results were inconclusive. This argument has three major weaknesses. Language is not neutral. In each case, the simple sentence creates a kind of finality. The thought is complete, unhedged, and stands on its own.

Common Mistake — Compound Subject ≠ Compound Sentence

A sentence with two subjects sharing one verb, or one subject performing two verbs, is still a simple sentence — not a compound one. History and geography overlap in many research questions is simple (compound subject, one verb). The committee reviewed the proposal and approved the budget is simple (one subject, compound predicate). Knowing this distinction matters when analyzing and constructing compound sentences.

Study Note — Simple Sentences in Academic Writing

Simple sentences are valuable tools, but they should not be overused. A paragraph built entirely from simple sentences can feel choppy, disconnected, or immature, because no sentence shows how the ideas relate to one another. Reserve simple sentences for direct claims, key conclusions, and moments of deliberate emphasis.

Section 8.3

Compound Sentences

A compound sentence contains two or more independent clauses joined together. Each independent clause could stand alone as a complete sentence — but the writer has chosen to connect them, indicating that the ideas belong together and are of equal grammatical weight. There are three standard ways to join independent clauses in a compound sentence.

Three Ways to Form a Compound Sentence

Method 1 — Coordinating conjunction preceded by a comma: The experiment produced surprising results, but the researchers could not explain them immediately.

Method 2 — Semicolon without a conjunction (closely related ideas): The population increased dramatically over the decade; infrastructure did not keep pace.

Method 3 — Semicolon + conjunctive adverb + comma: The evidence was compelling; however, the argument contained a significant logical gap.

The FANBOYS Conjunctions

The seven coordinating conjunctions can be remembered with the acronym FANBOYS: For, And, Nor, But, Or, Yet, So. The most important thing to understand about these conjunctions is that each one signals a different logical relationship between the clauses it joins. Choosing a conjunction is not a neutral grammatical act — it is a rhetorical choice about what you are claiming.

ConjunctionRelationship SignaledExample
AndAddition, continuationThe study was thorough, and the conclusions were well-supported.
But / YetContrast, contradictionThe argument was persuasive, but the evidence was weak.
Or / NorAlternative, negative alternativeThe author must revise the thesis, or the argument will remain unclear.
ForReason, cause (formal)The results were unexpected, for the original hypothesis had been widely accepted.
SoConsequence, resultThe data was incomplete, so the researchers delayed publication.

Substituting one conjunction for another changes what the sentence means. Replacing but with and removes the contrast. Replacing so with but removes the causal relationship. These are not interchangeable words; they carry distinct logical signals that your reader will rely on.

Conjunctive Adverbs

Conjunctive adverbs — words like however, therefore, moreover, consequently, furthermore, nevertheless, and in contrast — join independent clauses with a semicolon before them and a comma after. Unlike coordinating conjunctions, conjunctive adverbs cannot join clauses with a comma alone; that produces a comma splice.

The study involved a large sample size; therefore, the results can be generalized with greater confidence. The evidence was compelling; however, the argument contained a significant logical gap.
Common Mistake — The Comma Splice

A comma splice joins two independent clauses with only a comma and no coordinating conjunction. "The argument was persuasive, the evidence was weak." This is incorrect. The sentence needs a coordinating conjunction (but), a semicolon, or restructuring. The comma alone is never strong enough to join two complete clauses.

Common Mistake — Overusing Compound Sentences

If you join every pair of ideas with and or but, your writing will feel flat and undifferentiated. Not all ideas are equal. Some ideas are causes; some are conditions; some are consequences; some are elaborations. When you treat unequal ideas as grammatically equal, you lose precision. This is why complex sentences, discussed next, are such an important tool.

Section 8.4

Complex Sentences

A complex sentence contains one independent clause and one or more dependent clauses. A dependent clause — also called a subordinate clause — has a subject and a finite verb, but it cannot stand alone as a sentence. It depends on the independent clause to complete its meaning. Dependent clauses are introduced by subordinating conjunctions or relative pronouns.

This is where the power of subordination becomes available to writers. While compound sentences give equal weight to each clause, complex sentences create hierarchy: they signal that one idea is primary and another is secondary, conditional, causal, or contextual. Academic writing constantly needs to express these kinds of relationships, and the complex sentence is the primary vehicle for doing so.

Introducing Dependent Clauses

RelationshipCommon Subordinating Conjunctions
Cause / Reasonbecause, since, as
Conditionif, unless, as long as, provided that
Contrast / Concessionalthough, though, even though, while, whereas
Timewhen, after, before, until, once, whenever
Purposeso that, in order that
Relative clauseswho, whom, which, that, whose

The subordinating conjunction you choose is a logical commitment, not just a grammatical choice. When you write because, you are claiming a causal relationship. When you write although, you are acknowledging a contrast or concession. These are distinct claims about how ideas relate, and they cannot be used interchangeably without changing the meaning.

Placement and Emphasis

In a complex sentence, the dependent clause can appear before or after the independent clause, and its position affects rhythm and emphasis.

Example 8.4.1
Dependent clause placement changes emphasis
  • BeforeAlthough the methodology was rigorous, the sample size was too small to support broad conclusions. — The concession appears first; emphasis falls on the main claim at the end.
  • AfterThe sample size was too small to support broad conclusions, although the methodology was rigorous. — The main claim comes first; the concession qualifies it as an afterthought.
  • EmbeddedThe study, which examined housing patterns in three metropolitan areas, found no significant correlation between income level and school attendance. — A relative clause embedded in the middle adds detail without interrupting the main claim.

When the dependent clause comes first, it provides context before the main idea arrives, which tends to give emphasis to the independent clause at the end. When the independent clause comes first, the dependent clause functions as a qualification or expansion after the main point has already been received. Neither placement is universally better — the choice depends on what you want the reader to focus on.

Example 8.4.2
Subordination makes causal claims explicit
  • Two simpleThe country faced a severe economic recession. The government introduced austerity measures. — Two events stated; no relationship claimed.
  • ComplexBecause the country faced a severe economic recession, the government introduced austerity measures. — The causal relationship is now explicit: the recession caused the policy response.
Common Mistake — Dependent Clause as Fragment

Students sometimes punctuate a dependent clause as though it were a complete sentence. "Although the research was extensive. The findings remained inconclusive." The first group of words is a fragment — it has a subject and a verb, but the word although leaves it incomplete. It must be attached to an independent clause: "Although the research was extensive, the findings remained inconclusive."

Section 8.5

Compound-Complex Sentences

A compound-complex sentence combines both previous structures: it contains two or more independent clauses and one or more dependent clauses. It is the most structurally elaborate of the four sentence types, and when used well, it can express multi-part arguments in a single, well-organized sentence.

The Structure of a Compound-Complex Sentence

Requirement 1: at least two independent clauses (like a compound sentence).

Requirement 2: at least one dependent clause (like a complex sentence).

Example: Because the budget was reduced significantly, the program lost several key staff members, and the quality of services declined noticeably.

Dependent clause: Because the budget was reduced significantly — Independent clause 1: the program lost several key staff members — Independent clause 2: the quality of services declined noticeably

Compound-complex sentences are capable of expressing multi-part arguments efficiently. They allow the writer to state a condition or reason, then connect two related consequences or observations. In academic writing, this can be effective when two outcomes share the same cause, or when a concession is paired with two contrasting points.

While economic conditions improved for some segments of the population, wages for working-class families stagnated, and access to affordable housing continued to decline.
A Note of Caution

Compound-complex sentences can easily become unwieldy. Because they are the most structurally demanding type, they are also the most prone to confusion. A compound-complex sentence should only be used when the ideas genuinely require that degree of connection. If the sentence becomes difficult to parse on first reading, it should probably be broken into two sentences. The goal of sentence complexity is always clarity — not the appearance of sophistication.

Section 8.6

Coordination and Subordination

Now that all four sentence types have been introduced, it is worth examining the two structural principles that produce them: coordination and subordination. These are not merely grammatical categories — they are ways of thinking about how ideas relate to one another. Every time you choose between a compound and a complex sentence, you are implicitly choosing between coordination and subordination. That choice has consequences for meaning.

What Coordination Does

Coordination places two or more elements in a grammatically equal relationship. The fundamental message of coordination is: these things are equal, parallel, or of the same logical type. In compound sentences, coordination joins independent clauses. But coordination also operates within clauses: compound subjects, compound predicates, and compound objects are all forms of coordination.

Coordination is especially valuable when you want to show parallelism — when two or more ideas have the same logical status or belong to the same category.

The study examined income, employment, and housing access. [coordinated noun phrases — three equal elements] The argument was original, but the evidence was insufficient. [coordinated clauses — contrast between equal-weight claims]

What Subordination Does

Subordination places one element in a position of dependence on another. The fundamental message of subordination is: this idea depends on, is caused by, is limited by, or provides context for the main claim. Subordination is especially valuable in academic writing because academic arguments frequently involve conditional statements, causal claims, concessions, and temporal sequences.

Because the sample was small, the results cannot be generalized. [reason — the sample size is context for the conclusion] Although the evidence was limited, the researchers drew cautious conclusions. [concession — the limitation is acknowledged before the main point]

Faulty Coordination and Faulty Subordination

Two common structural errors involve misapplying these principles.

Example 8.6.1
Faulty coordination — treating unequal ideas as equal
  • FaultyThe economy weakened, and poverty increased, and crime rates rose, and the government called an emergency session. — Four events presented as equal, with no relationship between them stated.
  • RevisedBecause poverty increased as the economy weakened and crime rates rose, the government called an emergency session. — Subordination makes the causal relationships explicit.
Example 8.6.2
Faulty subordination — placing the main idea in the dependent clause
  • FaultyAlthough the study found a significant correlation, the researchers published their findings. — The word although signals concession, but a significant correlation is presumably the reason they published, not something to concede.
  • RevisedBecause the study found a significant correlation, the researchers published their findings. — The conjunction now matches the actual logical relationship.
Key Principle

Choosing between coordination and subordination is, at bottom, a question of logical precision: what is the actual relationship between these ideas? If they are equal and parallel, coordinate. If one depends on, causes, qualifies, or provides context for the other, subordinate.

Section 8.7

Sentence Variety

Understanding the four sentence types is necessary but not sufficient. What distinguishes competent academic writing from truly effective academic writing is the writer's ability to move across sentence types fluidly — using the right structure for the right purpose, creating rhythm, and keeping the reader engaged. Reading is a rhythmic experience. Sentence length and structure create a kind of cadence, and when that cadence becomes monotonous, readers disengage.

Example 8.7.1
The cost of monotony — all simple sentences
  • BeforeUrbanization affects public health. Cities have higher population density. Disease spreads more quickly in dense areas. Air pollution is also worse in cities. Mental health outcomes are affected too. Urban residents report higher stress levels. — Grammatically correct. Analytically weak. No relationships stated.
  • AfterUrbanization has significant effects on public health, and the consequences are felt both physically and psychologically. Because population density is higher in cities, infectious disease spreads more rapidly among urban residents. Air quality also deteriorates in heavily populated areas, exposing residents to elevated pollution levels. Even mental health is affected: urban residents consistently report higher rates of stress and anxiety, suggesting that the built environment shapes psychological well-being in ways that are only beginning to be understood.

The revised passage uses the same information but makes relationships visible. The compound sentence in the first line signals a two-part claim. The complex sentence with because makes a causal argument. The final complex sentence builds toward an interpretive conclusion. The rhythm also varies — some sentences are shorter and more direct, others build and accumulate.

The Short Sentence as Emphasis

Sentence length is its own kind of variation. Long sentences slow the reader down and convey complexity. Short sentences stop the reader and create emphasis. The most dramatic emphasis comes from a short sentence that follows a long one — a technique available to any writer who understands sentence structure.

For much of the nineteenth century, reformers argued that access to education would be sufficient to address persistent economic inequality, that giving workers the tools to compete in a changing labor market would naturally produce a more equitable distribution of wealth and opportunity. It did not.
A Word of Caution About Variety

Sentence variety should never become variety for its own sake. The goal is not to appear sophisticated. The goal is to serve the meaning. Every structural choice should ask: does this sentence structure help the reader understand this idea? If the answer is no, simplify. Academic writing values clarity above all. Variety that serves clarity is an asset; variety that undermines it is a liability.

Section 8.8

Choosing Sentence Patterns for Meaning

The final section of this chapter brings all the previous material together. How do you, as a writer, choose the right sentence pattern for what you are trying to say? The answer depends on three factors: what the idea is, what relationship it has to surrounding ideas, and where the emphasis should fall. The following table summarizes when each sentence type serves the writer best.

When to UseSentence TypeReason
Stating a fact, conclusion, or key claim with forceSimpleDirectness and emphasis; the idea stands alone
Showing contrast, addition, or consequence between two equal thoughtsCompoundEqual-weight coordination; relationship named by conjunction
Making a causal, conditional, or concessive relationship explicitComplexSubordination shows hierarchy; one idea depends on the other
Two outcomes share one cause; multi-part argument in one sentenceCompound-ComplexCombines both structures; use with care for clarity

When revising your writing, do not ask first what type of sentence is this? Ask first what am I trying to say, and what relationship exists between these ideas? The sentence type follows from the answer. If you are stating a conclusion clearly, a simple sentence may be best. If you are contrasting two equal findings, use a compound sentence with but or yet. If you are explaining why something happened, use a complex sentence with because. If you are acknowledging a counterpoint, use a complex sentence with although. When in doubt, choose clarity over complexity. A simple sentence that communicates your idea precisely is always preferable to a compound-complex sentence that confuses.

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 8.1A complete sentence requires a subject, a predicate with a finite verb, and a complete thought. A dependent clause has a subject and verb but fails the third requirement; it cannot stand alone.
  • Sec 8.2Simple sentences contain one independent clause and no dependent clauses. They communicate directness and emphasis. They may have compound subjects and predicates and still be simple. Overuse produces choppy, disconnected writing.
  • Sec 8.3Compound sentences contain two or more independent clauses joined by a coordinating conjunction (FANBOYS + comma), a semicolon, or a semicolon plus conjunctive adverb plus comma. The conjunction chosen signals the logical relationship. Comma splices and run-ons are the characteristic errors.
  • Sec 8.4Complex sentences contain one independent clause and one or more dependent clauses introduced by subordinating conjunctions or relative pronouns. They express hierarchical relationships — cause, condition, time, concession, contrast. Subordinating conjunction choice is a logical commitment.
  • Sec 8.5Compound-complex sentences contain two or more independent clauses and one or more dependent clauses. They can express multi-part arguments efficiently, but they are the most prone to confusion. Clarity must always take priority.
  • Sec 8.6Coordination places ideas in grammatically equal relationships. Subordination places one idea in dependence on another. The choice between them is a matter of logical precision, not just style preference. Faulty coordination treats unequal ideas as equal; faulty subordination places the main idea in the dependent clause.
  • Sec 8.7Sentence variety improves academic prose by creating rhythm, preventing monotony, and allowing the writer to use the right structure for each logical relationship. Varying sentence length — including deliberate use of short sentences for emphasis — is a powerful tool. Variety must serve clarity.
  • Sec 8.8Sentence pattern selection should follow from the ideas and their relationships, not from a desire to appear sophisticated. Ask what relationship exists between the ideas, then choose the structure that expresses that relationship most clearly and precisely.

Key Terms

simple sentence
A sentence containing one independent clause and no dependent clauses. May have compound subjects, predicates, or many modifiers.
compound sentence
A sentence containing two or more independent clauses joined by a coordinating conjunction, a semicolon, or a conjunctive adverb after a semicolon.
complex sentence
A sentence containing one independent clause and one or more dependent clauses introduced by a subordinating conjunction or relative pronoun.
compound-complex
A sentence with two or more independent clauses and one or more dependent clauses — combining the structures of compound and complex sentences.
independent clause
A group of words with a subject, finite verb, and complete thought that can stand alone as a sentence.
dependent clause
Also: subordinate clause. Has a subject and verb but cannot stand alone; introduced by a subordinating conjunction or relative pronoun.
coordination
A structural relationship placing two or more elements in grammatically equal positions. Used in compound sentences and compound elements within clauses.
subordination
A structural relationship placing one clause in dependence on another. Used in complex sentences to express cause, condition, time, and concession.
coordinating conjunction
One of the FANBOYS words (for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so) used to join independent clauses in compound sentences.
subordinating conjunction
A word or phrase (because, although, when, if, unless, since, etc.) that introduces a dependent clause and signals its relationship to the main clause.
conjunctive adverb
A word (however, therefore, moreover, consequently, etc.) used after a semicolon to join independent clauses. Requires a comma after it.
relative pronoun
A word (who, whom, which, that, whose) that introduces a relative clause modifying a noun within a sentence.
sentence variety
The deliberate use of different sentence types, lengths, and structures to improve rhythm, emphasis, and precision in academic prose.
comma splice
An error in which two independent clauses are joined by only a comma, with no coordinating conjunction.
run-on sentence
An error in which two independent clauses are fused without any punctuation or conjunction between them.
fragment
An incomplete sentence — typically a dependent clause or phrase punctuated as though it were a full sentence.

Discussion and Writing Prompts

Does the writer appear to use sentence type deliberately to create emphasis or show logical relationships? Write a brief analysis of what you find.
  2. Take a paragraph you have written recently — for this class or another — and rewrite it twice: once using only simple sentences, and once using a variety of types. Discuss the differences. Which version communicates the ideas more clearly? Which reads more naturally?

Others argue that style should follow from logic — that you vary structure because the ideas require it, not for effect. Which position do you find more compelling, and why? Use what you have learned in this chapter to support your answer.

The printing press was invented in the fifteenth century. It changed the distribution of information permanently. Books became cheaper. Literacy rates began to rise. The printing press was one of the most consequential inventions in human history.

  • I can correctly place a comma before a coordinating conjunction in a compound sentence.
  • I can correctly use a semicolon before a conjunctive adverb and a comma after it.
  • I can introduce a dependent clause with an appropriate subordinating conjunction that matches the logical relationship.

  • I understand how the placement of a dependent clause — before or after the main clause — affects emphasis.

  • I can choose a sentence pattern based on the logical relationship between ideas, not just for stylistic effect.
  • I understand why a simple sentence that communicates clearly is always preferable to a complex sentence that confuses.

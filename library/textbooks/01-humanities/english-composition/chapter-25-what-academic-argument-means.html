Chapter 25 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Most students come to academic writing with a clear idea of what an argument is. An argument is a fight. It is raised voices, competing opinions, and someone trying to make someone else admit they are wrong. It happens at dinner tables, in comment sections, and on talk shows. It is uncomfortable, exhausting, and rarely changes anyone's mind.

Academic argument is something different. It is not louder than ordinary conversation. It is not more aggressive. In fact, academic argument is often more careful, more respectful, and more intellectually honest than most everyday disagreements. This is because academic argument is not primarily about winning. It is about thinking clearly, explaining reasoning honestly, and contributing to a larger conversation about ideas that matter.

This chapter introduces academic argument as a form of disciplined inquiry. You will learn what separates argument from opinion, what separates argument from aggression, and what it means to participate seriously in the intellectual conversations that happen inside universities, academic disciplines, and written scholarship. You will also learn how argument connects to curiosity and research, why some questions are genuinely debatable and others are not, and how intellectual honesty and fairness make arguments stronger rather than weaker.

Part V of this textbook is dedicated to argument, rhetoric, and reasoning. This chapter is the foundation. Everything that follows depends on understanding what academic argument actually is and why it matters.

Section 25.1

Argument as Reasoned Conversation

The word argument carries baggage. In everyday speech, it almost always signals conflict. When two people are having an argument, we understand that something has gone wrong — feelings are running high, communication has broken down, and someone is going to be upset when it is over. This ordinary sense of argument is so powerful that many students feel uncomfortable being asked to argue in academic writing. Arguing feels rude. It feels like attacking someone else's ideas.

Academic argument is better understood as reasoned conversation. The word conversation is important here. A conversation involves at least two parties. It involves listening, not just speaking. It involves responding to what others have said, not just repeating your own position. And it involves some openness to changing your mind if someone presents a better idea. Academic argument has all of these features. When you write an academic argument, you are not starting a fight. You are joining a conversation that may have been going on for years, decades, or centuries, and you are contributing your own reasoned, evidence-based perspective to that conversation.

Consider how scholars in any field actually work. A historian studying the causes of World War I does not simply announce her conclusion and dare someone to disagree. She reads what other historians have said, considers their evidence and reasoning, identifies where she agrees and where she thinks the existing accounts are incomplete or mistaken, and then writes her own analysis — explaining what she thinks the evidence shows and why her interpretation is more accurate or complete than competing accounts. She expects other historians to respond, to challenge her evidence, to offer alternative interpretations. That exchange of argument and counterargument is how historical knowledge grows.

This same pattern appears in every academic field. Scientists argue about experimental results and their interpretations. Philosophers argue about the logical structure of ethical claims. Literary scholars argue about the meaning of texts. Economists argue about the causes of recessions. Even in fields that feel settled, there is ongoing argument at the edges of knowledge — about what the evidence means, about which explanations are most complete, about what questions have not yet been answered. Academic argument is the engine that moves knowledge forward.

For a student, academic argument serves a different but related purpose. When you write an argumentative essay, you are practicing the skills that scholars use: forming a clear position, finding evidence, reasoning carefully, considering alternatives, and presenting your thinking in a way that readers can evaluate. You may not be contributing to a centuries-long scholarly debate, but you are learning how to think within that tradition.

Why This Matters for Your Writing

Understanding argument as reasoned conversation changes how you approach your essays. Instead of trying to sound forceful or certain at all costs, you will learn to sound honest, careful, and thoughtful. Instead of treating your thesis as a position you must defend at any price, you will learn to treat it as a claim you are prepared to support with evidence and reasoning — and to revise if the evidence demands it.

Section 25.2

Opinion Versus Claim

One of the most important distinctions in academic argument is the difference between an opinion and a claim. Students sometimes use these words interchangeably, but in academic writing they describe different things, and the difference matters enormously.

An opinion is a personal preference, feeling, or belief that is not necessarily supported by evidence or reasoning. Opinions are entirely valid in personal life. You are entitled to prefer one kind of music over another, to feel that a particular movie was boring, or to believe that your city has the best food in the country. But opinions, in the strict sense, are not really arguable. If someone finds a film tedious and you find it brilliant, there is no fact of the matter to appeal to, no evidence that could settle the question, no reasoning that could prove one person right and the other wrong. You simply have different responses.

A claim, in academic writing, is a statement that asserts something about the world — about causes, effects, meanings, patterns, relationships, values, or interpretations — in a way that can be supported by evidence and reasoning. Claims can be evaluated. They can be defended. They can be challenged. And crucially, they can be wrong — which means that the person making them has something at stake intellectually.

Example 25.2.1
The difference between opinion and claim — on the same topic
  • OpinionI think the minimum wage is too low.
  • ClaimResearch from multiple economic studies suggests that moderate increases in the minimum wage reduce poverty rates without causing significant increases in unemployment in most regional economies.
  • Why it mattersThe opinion expresses a feeling. The claim makes an assertion about the world — about what research shows and what effects minimum wage increases produce — that can be examined, challenged, evaluated, and supported or undermined by evidence.

This distinction is not meant to dismiss opinions. In fact, many academic arguments begin with strong feelings or intuitions. A student who feels strongly that something is unjust, inefficient, or misunderstood has a starting point. But that feeling needs to be transformed into a claim — a specific, evidence-based assertion about the world — before it can become academic argument.

Type of ClaimWhat It AssertsEvidence Required
Factual claimSomething that can be verified through observation or established dataData, records, empirical sources
Interpretive claimA particular meaning or significance of a text, event, or phenomenonTextual evidence, historical context, analysis
Evaluative claimA judgment about quality, value, or importanceCriteria, evidence of meeting/failing criteria
Causal claimA relationship between cause and effectStatistical data, studies, historical patterns
Policy claimThat something should be done or changedEvidence of need, feasibility, anticipated effects
Common Mistake

Many students treat their thesis as though it were simply their opinion — as though the job of the essay is to announce how they feel and then repeat it emphatically. Academic argument is not about intensity of feeling. A student can feel very strongly about something and still be wrong. What matters in academic argument is not how strongly you believe something but how well you can support it with reasoning and evidence.

Section 25.3

Argument Versus Aggression

Academic argument requires a particular kind of discipline: the willingness to pursue the best answer rather than to win at any cost. This discipline separates argument from aggression.

Aggression in argument means trying to defeat the other person — embarrassing them, dismissing their perspective without engaging with it, using emotional pressure or personal attacks to force them to back down, or presenting your own position in such overwhelming language that disagreement seems impossible. Aggression prioritizes victory over truth.

Argument, in the academic sense, means trying to find or defend the most accurate, well-supported answer — even if that means revising your own position. Academic argument treats the person who disagrees with you not as an enemy to be defeated but as a reader whose objections deserve honest engagement.

This does not mean academic argument is timid. You can make strong, direct claims. You can disagree clearly and forcefully with existing scholarship. You can point out errors in reasoning or gaps in evidence. What it means is that the force of your argument comes from your reasoning and evidence, not from your tone of certainty or your willingness to attack those who disagree.

Aggressive Response Academic Response
"Anyone who thinks this policy is effective is obviously ignoring the basic facts. This interpretation is absurd."
"While some scholars argue that this policy produces significant economic benefits, the data from the most recent decade suggests that those benefits are more modest than previously claimed, and that earlier studies may not have accounted for several confounding variables."

Both responses disagree with an existing position. But only the second one actually argues. It acknowledges the competing view, specifies why the writer disagrees, points to evidence, and explains the problem with earlier studies. A reader can evaluate this response. The first response gives the reader nothing to evaluate. It simply announces contempt.

Aggression in academic writing often masks a lack of argument. When a writer does not have strong evidence or clear reasoning, there is a temptation to compensate with forceful language — to sound so certain that the reader assumes the case has been made. Experienced academic readers are not fooled by this. The best academic writers know that their case is stronger, not weaker, when they state it precisely and support it carefully rather than asserting it aggressively.

Section 25.4

Argument and Inquiry

Here is one of the most important ideas in this chapter: academic argument does not always begin with a firm conclusion. It often begins with a question.

This surprises many students. They assume that to write an argument, they must first decide what they believe and then find evidence to support that belief. In reality, the best academic arguments often emerge from genuine inquiry — from a writer who is honestly curious about a question, who does not yet know the answer, who reads widely and thinks carefully and then arrives at a position that the evidence and reasoning support.

Example 25.4.1
Two approaches to the same topic
  • Conclusion-first"I believe that social media is harmful to teenagers. I will now find evidence to prove this."
  • Inquiry-first"There is a great deal of public concern about social media and teenage mental health. What does the research actually show? What are the strongest arguments on different sides? What do the best-designed studies find? What are the limitations of the available evidence? Having examined all of this, what position is most defensible?"
  • The differenceThe first approach risks confirmation bias — selecting only evidence that confirms a pre-existing belief. The second produces a stronger, more honest argument because the position has actually been tested against the full range of available evidence.

The first approach risks confirmation bias — the well-documented tendency to seek out and accept evidence that supports what we already believe, while dismissing or ignoring evidence that challenges it. When you start with your conclusion and then go looking for support, you are not really inquiring. You are building a brief for a position you have already committed to. This makes for weak academic argument, because readers who know the evidence well will notice what you have left out.

The second approach is harder, because it requires genuine intellectual openness. It means being willing to be surprised by the evidence, to discover that your initial intuition was wrong, to end up arguing for something more nuanced or more complicated than your original feeling. But it produces stronger arguments, because the position you arrive at has actually been tested against the available evidence.

This inquiry-based approach to argument also explains why research and argument are so closely connected in academic writing. Research is not just a technical skill. It is a habit of mind: the habit of looking honestly at what is known before forming a conclusion, and of remaining open to revising your conclusion if new evidence warrants it.

Study Note — The Working Thesis

The connection between argument and inquiry means that your thesis statement does not need to be your very first thought. It is often more honest — and more accurate — to treat your early thesis as a working thesis: a provisional position that you will refine, sharpen, or even significantly revise as you research and draft. A thesis is not a decree you issue at the start of writing. It is a conclusion you work toward through a process of honest inquiry. Chapter 20 of this textbook covers thesis statements in depth.

Section 25.5

Debatable Questions

Not every question is worth arguing about in an academic essay. Academic argument works best when it addresses a genuinely debatable question — a question to which there is not already a simple, universally accepted answer.

Some questions look like they might be debatable but actually are not. These are settled questions: questions that have been answered through evidence so overwhelmingly that no reasonable informed person disputes the answer. The question of whether the Earth is round is not debatable. The question of whether vaccines cause autism is not debatable — large, rigorous studies have consistently shown no such link. Writing an academic argument defending positions that contradict settled science would not be genuine inquiry; it would be pretending that settled questions are open when they are not.

Other questions are matters of pure personal preference and therefore not genuinely arguable in an evidence-based sense. Whether chocolate ice cream is better than vanilla is not a question that academic argument can resolve. There is no evidence to cite, no reasoning that could settle it, because it depends entirely on individual taste.

The questions that support strong academic argument are those that fall between these extremes: questions where the evidence is real and relevant, but where the evidence does not point to one simple, obvious answer — where interpretation, weighing of competing values, or analysis of complex causation is genuinely required.

Question TypeDescriptionAppropriate for Academic Argument?
Settled questionAnswered by overwhelming evidence; no reasonable dispute among informed observersNo — present established knowledge instead
Personal preferenceDepends entirely on individual taste; no evidence could settle itNo — no basis for evidence-based argument
Genuinely debatableEvidence is real but mixed, or interpretation of evidence is legitimately disputed among informed scholarsYes — this is where academic argument belongs
Examples of Genuinely Debatable Academic Questions

Does mandatory minimum sentencing reduce violent crime rates? How should historians interpret the role of economic factors in causing the First World War? To what extent does Shakespeare's Hamlet reflect Elizabethan attitudes toward revenge and justice? Is there sufficient evidence that urban green space significantly improves mental health outcomes in dense cities? Each of these questions has mixed or incomplete evidence, competing scholarly interpretations, or requires weighing values — which is what makes them genuinely worth arguing about.

A useful test for debatability is sometimes called the So What / Who Cares test. When you settle on a thesis, ask yourself: Is there any reasonable, informed person who would disagree with this? If not, you probably do not have a debatable claim; you have a summary of established knowledge.

Another useful test: Could someone write a strong opposing argument? If a thoughtful person could construct a serious, evidence-based argument against your thesis, then your thesis is genuinely debatable.

Important Caution

Debatable does not mean any position is equally valid. Academic argument is not relativism — it is not the view that all perspectives are equally well-supported. Some arguments are better than others. They have stronger evidence, cleaner reasoning, fewer logical errors, and more honest engagement with complications. The fact that a question is debatable means it requires genuine intellectual work to determine the most defensible answer — not that all answers are equally good.

Section 25.6

Academic Disagreement

One of the most valuable skills in academic writing is the ability to disagree — clearly, specifically, and honestly — with existing scholarship, conventional wisdom, or common assumptions. This skill makes many students uncomfortable. Who are you to disagree with a published scholar, an established expert, or a widely held view?

The answer is that you are a careful reader who has examined the evidence. Academic disagreement is not about status or authority. It is about reasoning and evidence. If a published scholar has made a claim that the evidence does not support — or that more recent evidence complicates — a careful argument to that effect is valuable, regardless of who makes it.

Academic disagreement has several important features that distinguish it from ordinary disagreement:

It is specific. Academic disagreement does not merely assert that someone is wrong. It identifies precisely what the problem is. Is the evidence insufficient? Is the reasoning flawed? Does the claim depend on assumptions that are not supported? Has important evidence been ignored? Effective academic disagreement targets a specific weakness, not a general dismissal.

It is fair. Before you disagree with a position, you need to understand it accurately. This means reading and representing an opposing view as charitably as possible — stating the strongest version of the argument you are challenging, not a simplified or distorted version. Disagreeing with a weak caricature of a position is called a straw man argument, and it is considered a serious intellectual failure. Chapters 28 and 29 of this textbook cover counterargument and logical fallacies in detail.

It is evidence-based. Academic disagreement appeals to evidence, not to personal authority or emotional intensity. You might feel very strongly that an existing interpretation is wrong, but that feeling is not an argument. What matters is what the evidence shows.

It is proportionate. The strength of your disagreement should match the strength of your evidence. If the evidence against an existing position is overwhelming, your disagreement can be direct and confident. If the evidence is suggestive but not conclusive, your disagreement should be appropriately qualified: "This evidence suggests that the existing account may be incomplete" rather than "This evidence proves the existing account is false."

It is generative. Good academic disagreement does not merely tear down. It offers something better. When you argue that an existing explanation is inadequate, you should also suggest what a more adequate explanation would look like. Disagreement that only criticizes, without contributing a more accurate or complete understanding, is less valuable than disagreement that both critiques and improves.

Application

When you write an essay that challenges a common assumption or an established view, make sure you can answer this question: What am I claiming instead, and why is my claim better supported? If you can answer that question clearly, with evidence, you have the beginning of strong academic disagreement.

Section 25.7

Fairness and Intellectual Honesty

Academic argument depends on a set of ethical commitments. These are not just stylistic preferences or arbitrary rules. They are the foundation of a system in which knowledge can actually be built, tested, and improved over time. When writers violate these commitments, the entire enterprise of academic inquiry is damaged.

The most important of these commitments are fairness and intellectual honesty.

Fairness means representing the positions you are disagreeing with accurately and charitably. It means not cherry-picking only the evidence that supports your position while ignoring evidence that complicates it. It means acknowledging limitations in your own argument. It means giving credit where it is due — when someone else's research or reasoning has informed your thinking, you say so explicitly through citation and acknowledgment. Fairness does not require you to be neutral or to avoid taking strong positions. It requires you to take those strong positions honestly, on the basis of what the evidence actually shows.

Intellectual honesty means several related things in practice:

Acknowledging uncertainty. When the evidence on a question is genuinely mixed, saying so — rather than pretending it all points one way. Academic writing that overstates the certainty of its conclusions is not stronger; it is less trustworthy.

Engaging seriously with complications. Every interesting argument has complications — evidence that does not quite fit, alternative interpretations, studies with mixed findings. Intellectual honesty means engaging with those complications rather than pretending they do not exist. Addressing complications often strengthens an argument rather than weakening it, because it shows readers that you have thought carefully about the full picture.

Changing your mind when the evidence warrants it. One of the most intellectually honest things a writer can say is: "I began this inquiry believing one thing, but the evidence led me to a more complicated conclusion." This does not signal weakness. It signals that you have actually engaged with the evidence rather than just confirming your initial assumption.

Citing your sources. Academic argument draws on the work of others, and intellectual honesty requires acknowledging that work. Plagiarism — presenting others' ideas as your own — is not just a rule violation. It is a form of intellectual dishonesty that corrupts the academic conversation. Part VII of this textbook covers citation in full detail.

Common Mistake

Many students believe that admitting any uncertainty or complexity in their argument will make it look weak. In fact, the opposite is often true. Readers trust writers who acknowledge complications because those writers seem to have actually grappled with the question. Readers distrust writers who seem to oversimplify, ignore contrary evidence, or claim more certainty than the evidence warrants. A well-qualified, honest argument is usually more persuasive than an overconfident one, because it is more credible.

There is also an important difference between legitimate persuasion and manipulation. Legitimate persuasion uses honest evidence, sound reasoning, and fair representation of opposing views to help a reader see why a particular conclusion is well-supported. Manipulation uses selective evidence, distorted representations of opposing views, emotional pressure, or misleading framing to push a reader toward a conclusion regardless of whether the evidence supports it. Academic writing commits to legitimate persuasion. The goal is to help your reader think well about a question — not to trick them into agreeing with you.

The Distinction That Defines Academic Argument

Legitimate persuasion: Uses honest evidence, sound reasoning, and fair representation to help a reader reach a well-supported conclusion.

Manipulation: Uses selective evidence, distorted representations, or misleading framing to push a reader toward agreement regardless of what the evidence shows.

Academic writing always commits to legitimate persuasion. The goal is to help your reader think well — not to engineer a predetermined conclusion.

Section 25.8

Argument Across Disciplines

One of the things that surprises students when they move across different university courses is that argument does not look the same everywhere. A history essay, a psychology research paper, a philosophy analysis, a chemistry lab report, and a business case study all involve argument in the broad sense — they all make claims and support those claims with evidence and reasoning — but they do so in quite different ways.

Understanding these disciplinary differences will help you as you write in different courses. It will also help you understand why the principles introduced in this chapter are foundational: they apply across all of these disciplines, even though the specific conventions vary.

In the humanities — fields like history, literature, philosophy, religious studies, and cultural studies — argument often involves interpretation. The claim is usually about meaning, significance, or explanation. A literary scholar argues that a particular reading of a poem is the most defensible interpretation of its imagery and structure. A historian argues that a particular set of factors best explains a historical event. Evidence in the humanities tends to include primary texts, historical documents, works of art, and scholarly secondary sources. The writing is often more explicitly argumentative, with the author's voice and reasoning visible throughout.

In the social sciences — fields like psychology, sociology, political science, economics, and anthropology — argument is often organized around research findings. A claim about human behavior or social phenomena needs to be supported by empirical evidence: data from studies, surveys, experiments, observations, or statistical analyses. The author's argument is still present, but it tends to be organized around questions of what the evidence shows and what it means. Social scientists also pay careful attention to methodology — the design of studies — because how a study was conducted affects how trustworthy its results are.

In the natural sciences — biology, chemistry, physics, and related fields — argument takes a somewhat different form. Scientific writing tends to be more structured and less explicitly argumentative in the humanities sense. The goal is to report findings clearly and to place them in the context of existing knowledge. But scientific writing still involves argument: the discussion section of a research paper, for instance, argues for a particular interpretation of the data, acknowledges alternative explanations, and explains why the findings matter. Scientific argument is heavily constrained by rules of evidence — claims must be supported by data, and the methods used to collect that data must be transparent and replicable.

In professional and technical fields — business, law, engineering, nursing, and similar programs — argument often takes the form of analysis and recommendation. A business case argues for a particular strategic decision, supported by market analysis and financial projections. A legal brief argues for a particular interpretation of the law, supported by precedents and statutes. A nursing care plan argues for a particular treatment approach, supported by clinical evidence and patient data. These arguments tend to be practical rather than theoretical, but they still require the same basic skills: clear claims, relevant evidence, honest engagement with complications, and transparent reasoning.

What all disciplinary forms of argument share is this: they require the writer to make a specific claim, support it with relevant evidence, reason clearly, and engage honestly with the complexity of the question. The vocabulary, the types of evidence, the conventional structure, and the expected level of formality vary significantly — but the intellectual core of argument is the same everywhere.

Academic Transfer

As you take courses across your program of study at MGU, pay attention to how argument works in each field. When you read a scholarly article, notice how the author states the main claim. Notice what counts as evidence in that field. Notice how the author addresses complications and opposing views. Reading like a writer — as Chapter 4 of this textbook explains — is especially valuable for understanding disciplinary argument conventions.

Chapter 25 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 25.1Academic argument is best understood as reasoned conversation — a disciplined, evidence-based, intellectually honest practice that is fundamentally different from ordinary disagreement or conflict. It is not about winning. It is about contributing the most accurate, well-supported understanding of a question that the evidence allows.
  • Sec 25.2Academic argument requires claims — specific, evidence-based assertions about the world that can be supported, challenged, and evaluated — rather than mere opinions, which are personal preferences not grounded in evidence. Claims may be factual, interpretive, evaluative, causal, or policy-focused; each type requires a different kind of evidence.
  • Sec 25.3Academic argument is distinct from aggression. Aggressiveness prioritizes winning; argument prioritizes truth. The force of academic argument comes from reasoning and evidence, not from forceful language or the dismissal of those who disagree. Aggression often masks a lack of real argument.
  • Sec 25.4The best academic arguments begin with inquiry — genuine curiosity and honest engagement with evidence — rather than a fixed conclusion. Starting with a conclusion risks confirmation bias. A working thesis is a provisional position refined through research and honest engagement with the full range of evidence.
  • Sec 25.5Academic argument requires genuinely debatable questions — questions where the evidence is real but does not point to one simple, universally accepted answer. Settled questions and matters of pure personal preference are not appropriate subjects for academic argument. Debatable does not mean equally valid; some arguments are better supported than others.
  • Sec 25.6Academic disagreement is not a matter of status or authority but of reasoning and evidence. Effective academic disagreement is specific, fair, evidence-based, proportionate, and generative. The straw man fallacy — misrepresenting a position in simplified form before disagreeing with it — is a serious intellectual failure.
  • Sec 25.7Fairness and intellectual honesty are not optional virtues in academic writing; they are ethical commitments essential to inquiry. Intellectual honesty means acknowledging uncertainty, engaging with complications, being open to revising your position, and citing your sources. Legitimate persuasion is fundamentally different from manipulation.
  • Sec 25.8Argument looks different across academic disciplines — humanists interpret texts and events; social scientists analyze empirical evidence; natural scientists report and discuss experimental findings; professional fields produce analysis and recommendation. But the intellectual core of argument — specific claims, relevant evidence, clear reasoning, honest engagement — is consistent across all fields.

Key Terms

academic argument
A form of reasoned, evidence-based communication in which a writer makes a specific claim, supports it with evidence and reasoning, and engages honestly with complexity and alternative views.
claim
A statement that asserts something about the world — about causes, effects, meanings, patterns, values, or interpretations — in a way that can be supported by evidence and reasoning.
opinion
A personal preference, feeling, or belief not necessarily grounded in evidence or subject to logical evaluation. Valid in personal life, but insufficient for academic argument.
inquiry
A process of honest investigation that begins with a question rather than a fixed conclusion. Inquiry-based argument starts with curiosity, examines evidence carefully, and arrives at a defensible position through research and reasoning.
debatable question
A question to which the available evidence does not point to one simple, universally accepted answer, and which therefore requires analysis, interpretation, and reasoned judgment to address.
settled question
A question answered through overwhelming evidence to the satisfaction of all informed, reasonable observers. Settled questions are not appropriate subjects for genuine academic debate.
intellectual honesty
The commitment to represent evidence fairly, acknowledge uncertainty and complications, engage seriously with alternative views, and revise one's position when the evidence warrants it.
fairness
In academic argument, the practice of representing opposing views accurately and charitably, acknowledging limitations in one's own argument, and avoiding selective use of evidence.
confirmation bias
The tendency to seek out, accept, and remember evidence that supports a pre-existing belief, while dismissing or ignoring evidence that challenges it.
straw man argument
A logical fallacy in which a writer misrepresents an opposing view in simplified or distorted form, then argues against that distorted version rather than the actual position.
legitimate persuasion
Argumentation that uses honest evidence, sound reasoning, and fair representation to help a reader see why a particular conclusion is well-supported; distinguished from manipulation.
disciplinary conventions
The field-specific norms, forms of evidence, structural expectations, and stylistic practices that govern academic writing in particular academic disciplines.

Review Questions

ReviewComprehension and Application
  1. In your own words, explain why academic argument is better understood as "reasoned conversation" than as a form of conflict. What features of conversation does academic argument share?
  2. What is the difference between an opinion and a claim? Give an original example of each on the same general topic, and explain what makes the claim arguable in a way the opinion is not.
  3. Explain the connection between argument and inquiry. Why is starting with a question often better than starting with a conclusion when writing an academic argument?
  4. What is a debatable question? Give two examples of questions that would be genuinely debatable in an academic context, and explain what makes them debatable rather than settled.
  5. Why is fairness important in academic argument? What does it mean to represent an opposing view fairly, and what is the alternative called when this is not done?
  6. What is intellectual honesty in academic argument? Describe at least three specific practices that demonstrate intellectual honesty in writing.
  7. Describe one significant way that argument works differently in the humanities compared to the natural sciences. What stays the same, and what changes?
  8. What is confirmation bias, and why is it a problem for academic argument? How does inquiry-based thinking help address it?
  9. Is it possible for a question to be debatable but still have a better-supported answer than other possible answers? Explain your response.
  10. Why might acknowledging complications and uncertainty in your argument actually make it stronger rather than weaker?

Discussion Prompts

DiscussionCritical Thinking and Application
  1. From Opinion to Claim. Think of a topic you feel strongly about — something in your community, your field of study, or current events. How would you describe your current position on this topic? Is it closer to an opinion or a claim? What would you need to do to turn it into a specific, arguable academic claim?
  2. Argument in Your Field. If you have a sense of what field or profession you are interested in, think about what kinds of questions that field argues about. What counts as good evidence in that field? What does a strong argument look like? If you are unsure, discuss what you have noticed about argument in courses you have already taken.
  3. Academic Disagreement and Confidence. Many students feel uncomfortable disagreeing with experts, established scholars, or widely held views. Why do you think this discomfort exists? Is it ever appropriate to challenge expert opinion in academic writing? Under what conditions? What would make such a challenge intellectually responsible?
  4. Inquiry Versus Advocacy. What is the difference between writing as an inquiry (trying to figure out what is true) and writing as advocacy (trying to persuade others of a position you already hold)? Can good academic writing be both? What would that look like?
  5. Fairness in Public Disagreement. Think about arguments you have seen in public life — in politics, on social media, in journalism. How often do you see the features of academic disagreement described in this chapter: specific claims, fair representation of opposing views, evidence-based reasoning, intellectual honesty? What do you notice about the difference between public argument and the academic argument described in this chapter?

Practice Activities

Activity 1Opinion or Claim?

Read each statement below and decide whether it is an opinion (personal preference or feeling) or a claim (an arguable assertion about the world). For each claim, briefly explain what evidence might be used to support or challenge it. For each opinion, rewrite it as an academic claim.

  1. School uniforms are ugly and uncomfortable.
  2. Studies suggest that students in schools with uniform policies show higher attendance rates in some contexts, though findings vary by demographic and school type.
  3. I think social media companies should be regulated more strictly.
  4. Classical music is boring.
  5. Extended prison sentences do not appear to reduce recidivism rates more effectively than shorter sentences combined with rehabilitation programs, based on available comparative data.
  6. History classes are more important than math classes.
Activity 2Is This Question Debatable?

For each question below, decide whether it is genuinely debatable, a settled question, or a matter of pure personal preference. Briefly explain your reasoning.

  1. Did the Holocaust occur?
  2. Does increased screen time in children correlate with decreased academic performance?
  3. Is pizza better than sushi?
  4. Did economic factors play a significant role in causing the American Civil War?
  5. Does the death penalty deter violent crime more effectively than life imprisonment?
  6. Is the Earth approximately 4.5 billion years old?
  7. Should universities require students to take courses outside their major?
  8. Do stricter gun laws reduce overall rates of gun violence?
Activity 3From Inquiry to Working Thesis

Choose one of the following broad topics. Spend five minutes writing down everything you already think you know about it and what your initial inclination or feeling about it is. Then write three genuine questions you would need to investigate in order to form a well-supported position. Finally, write a working thesis — a provisional claim based on your current understanding, which you acknowledge might change with more research.

  1. The effects of remote work on employee productivity
  2. The role of social media in political polarization
  3. The relationship between income inequality and educational achievement
  4. The long-term effects of standardized testing on student learning
Activity 4Analyzing Fairness

Read the following two passages. Both disagree with the same opposing position. Analyze which one demonstrates better academic fairness and explain why. What specific features of the fairer passage make it more academically credible? Then rewrite the weaker passage to match the standards of fairness demonstrated in the stronger one.

Passage A
A disagreement with an opposing view
  • Text"Some people claim that increasing the minimum wage helps low-income workers, but this argument is obviously wrong. Anyone who understands economics knows that raising wages simply causes businesses to fire workers. The people making this argument clearly have not thought through the consequences."
Passage B
A disagreement with the same opposing view
  • Text"Proponents of minimum wage increases argue that higher wages provide meaningful income gains for low-wage workers without significantly affecting employment levels, and point to studies from states that have implemented increases without obvious negative employment effects. While these findings are real, critics note that regional economic conditions vary significantly, and that some research — particularly studies examining rapid or large increases — does find employment effects in specific sectors. The impact of minimum wage increases appears to depend substantially on the size of the increase, the regional labor market, and the affected industry."
Activity 5Academic Disagreement in Practice

Find a short article, editorial, or essay on a topic related to your field of study or a topic you find genuinely interesting. Read it carefully. Then write a short response (roughly one paragraph) in which you disagree with at least one claim the author makes. Your response should:

  1. Clearly state what specific claim you are disagreeing with.
  2. Represent that claim fairly before disagreeing with it.
  3. Explain specifically why you disagree — what the problem with the claim or its evidence is.
  4. Suggest, even briefly, what a more accurate or complete claim might look like.
  5. Use an academic tone throughout.

Be prepared to discuss your response with a partner or small group, focusing on whether your disagreement is specific, fair, evidence-based, and constructive.

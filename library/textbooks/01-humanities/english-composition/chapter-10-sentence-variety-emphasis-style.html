Chapter 10 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Every sentence you write makes a small claim on your reader's attention. When that sentence ends, another begins. Then another. If those sentences all sound alike — the same length, the same rhythm, the same opening word — something happens that you may not notice at first: your reader grows tired. Not because the ideas are uninteresting, but because the sound of the writing has become predictable. Predictability in prose is a quiet enemy. It does not announce itself. It simply makes reading harder than it should be.

This chapter teaches you how to control sentence variety, emphasis, and style. These are not decorative concerns. They are fundamental tools for making your meaning land clearly and for keeping your reader engaged with your ideas from one sentence to the next.

Sentence variety does not mean making writing complicated. It means making deliberate choices about how long your sentences are, how they begin, where the important words appear, and how ideas relate to one another. Good academic writers think about these things — not constantly, and not anxiously, but as a natural part of writing and revision.

Opening Question

Read this paragraph: "The economy affects education. Students need money for school. Many students take out loans. Loans must be repaid. Repayment is difficult. Some students cannot find jobs. They default on their loans. This creates financial problems. The government is affected too."

Nothing there is grammatically wrong. But something is deeply wrong with it as writing. By the end of this chapter, you will be able to explain exactly what — and to fix it.

Section 10.1

Why Sentence Variety Matters

To understand what sentence variety actually does, compare two versions of the same paragraph. The first version was shown in the chapter overview: short, repetitive, identical in structure. The second version, presented below, contains the same ideas — but organized into sentences that vary in length and structure.

Without Variety
With Variety
The economy affects education. Students need money for school. Many students take out loans. Loans must be repaid. Repayment is difficult. Some students cannot find jobs. They default on their loans. This creates financial problems. The government is affected too.
The economy affects education in ways that reach far beyond tuition costs. Because many students must borrow money to attend school, they enter the workforce already carrying debt — and when those students cannot find stable employment after graduation, the resulting loan defaults create financial problems not only for individuals but for the government programs that backed their borrowing.

The second version is not more intelligent than the first. It contains essentially the same information. But it is dramatically easier to read, and it communicates a clearer sense of how these ideas are connected. That clarity comes partly from the way the sentences are built: their varied lengths, their different structures, the way important ideas appear in positions of emphasis.

Three Reasons Variety Matters

Why Sentence Variety Serves Academic Writing

1. Engagement. When sentences sound the same, reading becomes mechanical. The mind processes words without registering meaning. Variety in rhythm and structure keeps the reader alert.

2. Logical relationships. When you combine ideas into a single sentence, you signal that they are connected. When you break ideas into separate sentences, you signal that they should be considered individually. Sentence structure is a form of meaning-making, not just arrangement.

3. Quality of thinking. A writer who can produce only one type of sentence is a writer whose thinking is still developing. As your ideas grow more complex and your reasoning more sophisticated, your sentences naturally need to become more flexible. Controlling sentence structure is, in part, learning to express nuance.

What Variety Is Not

Sentence variety is not about using long, complicated sentences to sound impressive. Some of the most admired academic writers write quite plainly. Clarity is always more important than complexity.

Sentence variety is not about using one of every sentence type — one simple sentence, one compound, one complex — in some kind of mechanical rotation. That would be variety in form but not in purpose.

And sentence variety is not about adding words just to change rhythm. Every word in a sentence should earn its place.

Core Principle

Your sentences should be as long as they need to be — no longer and no shorter — and their structure should reflect the logical relationships among your ideas. When you get that right, variety follows naturally.

Section 10.2

Varying Sentence Length

The most basic form of sentence variety is length. Academic writing typically benefits from a mix of short, medium, and long sentences. Each length has a different effect on the reader, and understanding those effects gives you precise control over your writing's rhythm and emphasis.

Short Sentences

Short sentences are powerful. They land hard. They create emphasis. They slow the reader down.

A short sentence placed after several longer ones draws the reader's attention because it breaks the expected rhythm. It says: stop here. This matters.

Example — Short Sentence as Emphasis

The study tracked five hundred participants over a period of ten years, monitoring their diet, exercise habits, sleep quality, and stress levels through a combination of self-reported surveys and biometric data. Researchers expected to find a correlation between sleep and cardiovascular health. They found something more alarming.

The third sentence — "They found something more alarming" — is short. It follows two much longer sentences. Its brevity creates suspense and emphasis. The reader feels the weight of that sentence in a way they would not if it were buried in a longer construction. However, a paragraph made entirely of short sentences feels choppy and thin. Short sentences need the company of longer ones to work properly.

Medium Sentences

Most sentences in academic writing fall in the middle range — roughly fifteen to thirty words. Medium-length sentences carry the bulk of most academic paragraphs. They allow enough complexity to express connected ideas without becoming hard to follow. A medium sentence can hold a main idea and a qualifying detail, a cause and an effect, an observation and its significance. It is the workhorse of academic prose.

Long Sentences

Long sentences are useful when ideas are genuinely connected and need to be held together in one thought. A well-constructed long sentence can show how multiple factors relate to a single outcome, how a process unfolds step by step, or how a writer is carefully qualifying an important claim.

The danger with long sentences is that they can collapse under their own weight. A sentence that runs to sixty or seventy words is not automatically a good sentence. If the reader must reread it twice to understand what it is saying, it is doing more harm than good. Long sentences work best when the ideas genuinely belong together, when the sentence is clearly organized so the reader can track it, when the key point appears at the end in a position of emphasis, and when they follow or precede shorter sentences that provide relief.

LengthApproximate RangeBest Used For
Short1–12 wordsEmphasis, conclusions, turning points, key facts
Medium13–30 wordsMost explanatory prose; carrying the main argument forward
Long31+ wordsComplex relationships; carefully qualified claims; flowing narrative
Revision Strategy — Read Aloud

One of the best ways to test whether your sentences have appropriate variety is to read your writing aloud. Your ear will catch what your eye misses. If you find yourself reading in a monotone, your sentences are probably too uniform. If you run out of breath before a sentence ends, it is probably too long. If your reading sounds like a series of small punches with no flow connecting them, your sentences are probably too short and too uniform. Read aloud. Fix what sounds wrong. Read again.

Section 10.3

Varying Sentence Openings

One of the most common patterns that makes student writing sound repetitive is not sentence length — it is sentence openings. Many student writers begin nearly every sentence the same way: with a subject followed immediately by a verb.

Monotonous Opening Pattern
The study shows that diet affects memory. The author argues for stronger regulation. The data suggests a significant correlation. The government decided to act. The results indicate meaningful change.

There is nothing wrong with any individual sentence that opens this way. Subject-first sentences are the foundation of English prose. But when every sentence in a paragraph begins with the same grammatical structure, the paragraph sounds mechanical and flat, regardless of how strong the ideas are. The solution is to vary your sentence openings — not randomly, but strategically, so that each opening reflects where the sentence fits in the flow of your paragraph.

Opening with a Transitional Word or Phrase

Transitional words and phrases show logical relationships between sentences. They are natural sentence openers that also improve coherence. Before the reader even reaches the main idea, they know how this sentence relates to the one before it.

Transition TypeExamples
ContrastHowever, Nevertheless, On the other hand, In contrast
Cause / ResultAs a result, Therefore, Consequently, For this reason
AdditionFurthermore, In addition, Moreover, Similarly
IllustrationFor example, For instance, To illustrate, Specifically
ConcessionAdmittedly, Of course, It is true that, While this is the case

Opening with a Dependent Clause

A dependent clause placed at the beginning of a sentence creates variety and signals an important logical relationship — especially cause and effect, condition, or contrast. The dependent clause provides context before the main idea arrives, creating a natural rhythm: setup, then payoff. The reader receives the conditions first and is prepared for the conclusion.

Dependent Clause Openings
Although the treatment showed early promise, the long-term outcomes were disappointing.

Because the budget had been cut significantly, the program was forced to scale back its services.

While critics praised the novel's structure, many readers found its pacing difficult.

Opening with a Participial Phrase

A participial phrase begins with a verb form (usually ending in -ing or -ed) and modifies the subject of the main clause. Participial phrases are common in academic writing because they pack contextual information efficiently into the sentence opening without requiring a separate clause.

Participial Phrase Openings
Faced with rising costs, the university announced a tuition increase.

Recognizing the limits of earlier research, the team designed a more controlled study.

Established in the early twentieth century, the organization has grown steadily for decades.
Common Mistake — Dangling Modifiers

Participial phrases must refer clearly to the subject of the main clause. If they do not, the result is a dangling modifier. Example of the error: "Recognizing the limits of earlier research, the study was redesigned." The study cannot recognize anything. The phrase dangles because the implied subject of recognizing is not the grammatical subject of the sentence. Corrected: "Recognizing the limits of earlier research, the team redesigned the study."

Opening with a Prepositional Phrase

Prepositional phrases establish time, place, condition, or context at the start of a sentence. They are among the most natural and unobtrusive ways to vary sentence structure — they slip into place without drawing attention to themselves, which is exactly what a sentence opening should do.

Prepositional Phrase Openings
In the decades following World War II, economic growth reshaped American society.

Under these conditions, any agreement would be difficult to enforce.

Throughout the novel, the narrator's reliability remains in question.

Opening with a Single Adverb

Occasionally, a single adverb placed at the start of a sentence creates emphasis or marks a logical turn. Use this sparingly — one or two per page is appropriate. More than that begins to feel like a stylistic habit rather than a deliberate choice.

Single Adverb Openings (Use Sparingly)
Unfortunately, the data was collected inconsistently.

Significantly, the response rate exceeded ninety percent.

Ultimately, the question is not whether the program worked, but whether it was worth the cost.
Key Reminder — Avoid Over-Using Any Single Strategy

The goal is variety, not substitution. Some writers learn that beginning sentences with transitions is good — and then begin every sentence with a transition. The point is not to replace one pattern with another. The point is to have multiple options available and to choose the one that best fits the sentence's place in the paragraph.

Section 10.4

Emphasis at the Beginning and End

Where you place information inside a sentence affects how strongly the reader registers it. In English, the two positions of greatest emphasis are the beginning and the end of a sentence. The middle of a long sentence is the weakest position — the place where information is most likely to be absorbed without being fully noticed. This is not a rule; it is an observation about how readers process language. Understanding it gives you a powerful tool for controlling what your reader pays attention to.

The End Position: The Most Emphatic Spot

The end of a sentence is the position of greatest emphasis. Readers naturally lean forward as a sentence progresses and feel a small sense of arrival when it concludes. Whatever words occupy the final position carry the most weight. When you revise your sentences, ask yourself: what do I most want the reader to register from this sentence? Then ask: is that information at the end?

Key Idea Buried
Key Idea at End
Despite years of advocacy and repeated calls for reform, the policy was not changed.
Despite years of advocacy and repeated calls for reform, the policy survived unchanged.

The first version ends on changed, a negative construction — the reader's attention lands on the action of changing. The second ends on survived unchanged, which is more precise and carries a sharper irony. Both are grammatically correct, but the second is more effective because it places the most significant information in the strongest position.

The Beginning Position and the Given-New Principle

The beginning of a sentence also carries emphasis, though of a different kind. The opening orients the reader and establishes what the sentence is going to be about. In a principle of prose style sometimes called the given-new principle, effective sentences typically begin with information the reader already knows — a connection to something previously established — and move toward new information by the end. This creates a natural sense of flow, because the reader is never left without a foothold. Each sentence picks up where the last one left off and carries the reader forward.

Example — Given-New Principle in Action

Education policy reform has been a subject of intense political debate for decades. That debate has produced numerous proposals, most of which have failed to achieve broad legislative support. The failure of these proposals reflects not only political disagreement but also deep uncertainty about what education reform should accomplish.

Each sentence begins by echoing something from the previous sentence — debate connects to debate, proposals connects to proposals, failure connects to failed. This is not accidental repetition. It is the given-new principle at work, creating coherent flow while moving toward new information with each sentence.

The Middle: Where Information Gets Lost

The middle of a long sentence is not the place for your most important idea. It is where information is most easily overlooked. Important actions, key facts, and critical conclusions all lose force when buried in the center of a sentence loaded with modifying phrases and subordinate clauses.

Main Idea Buried in Middle
Main Idea Moved to End
The committee, after months of review and extensive consultation with department heads, faculty representatives, and external experts whose views were compiled in a report submitted last spring, decided to approve the proposal.
After months of review and consultation with department heads, faculty representatives, and external experts, the committee decided to approve the proposal.

Section 10.5

Parallel Structure

Parallel structure means giving related ideas the same grammatical form. When you list items, when you join two or more ideas with a coordinating conjunction, or when you create a series of phrases or clauses, the items in the series should be grammatically equivalent. Parallel structure does two things at once: it creates clarity by making it obvious that the listed ideas are of the same type or at the same level, and it creates emphasis by establishing a rhythm that reinforces the connection among the ideas.

Parallel Structure in Lists

When you list three or more items, all items should be in the same grammatical form. Inconsistency in a list is not merely a stylistic awkwardness — it creates genuine confusion about whether all items are truly at the same level, or whether one item is different in kind from the others.

Not Parallel
Parallel
The program offers students mentorship opportunities, to develop research skills, and guidance from faculty.
The program offers students mentorship opportunities, research skill development, and faculty guidance.
(Mixed: noun phrase + infinitive phrase + noun phrase)
(All noun phrases — consistent and clear)

Parallel Structure with Coordinating Conjunctions

When you join two ideas with and, but, or, nor, for, yet, or so, both ideas should be in the same grammatical form. This is easy to overlook because the conjunctions themselves are so familiar that the mismatch in the joined elements can go unnoticed in a first draft.

Not Parallel
Parallel
The author argues that poverty causes crime and for stricter enforcement of existing laws.
The author argues that poverty causes crime and that stricter enforcement of existing laws is needed.

Parallel Structure with Correlative Conjunctions

Correlative conjunctions come in pairs: both...and, either...or, neither...nor, not only...but also. The elements joined by these pairs must be parallel. The easiest check: identify the grammatical form of the element following the first conjunction, then ensure the element following the second conjunction is in the same form.

Not Parallel
Parallel
The research both challenges existing assumptions and is raising new questions.
The research both challenges existing assumptions and raises new questions.
Common Mistake — Faulty Parallelism

Faulty parallelism is one of the most frequent sentence-level errors in academic writing, partly because it often sounds almost right. The ear catches something off, but the writer may not be able to identify exactly what is wrong. A reliable revision strategy: whenever you write a list or a paired construction, identify the grammatical form of the first item, then check each subsequent item against that form. If any item is in a different form, revise it.

Parallelism as Deliberate Emphasis

In addition to preventing error, parallel structure is a deliberate stylistic tool. Writers use parallelism to create rhythm, build intensity, and emphasize the relationship among ideas. Used with intention, parallelism is one of the most powerful tools for creating memorable, emphatic prose.

Parallelism as Rhetorical Emphasis

We read. We discuss. We write.

Three short parallel sentences. The parallelism creates a drumbeat effect — each sentence lands with equal weight. The structure itself communicates that these three activities are equivalent in importance.

Section 10.6

Combining Sentences

Many student writers, especially in early drafts, produce strings of short sentences that state ideas separately when those ideas actually belong together. Combining sentences is one of the most powerful revision strategies available, because it forces you to identify the relationships among your ideas and express those relationships directly rather than leaving them implicit.

Before Combining (Fragmented)
After Combining (Cohesive)
Maria Montessori was an Italian educator. She developed a new approach to childhood education. The approach emphasized independent learning. Children were allowed to move freely. They chose their own activities. The teacher served as a guide. The teacher did not direct every action.
Maria Montessori, an Italian educator, developed an approach to childhood education that emphasized independent learning. Rather than directing every action, teachers served as guides, while children were free to move through the classroom and choose their own activities.

Two sentences instead of seven. The ideas are now connected through sentence structure itself. The subordinate clause (that emphasized independent learning) shows the relationship between the approach and its defining characteristic. The contrast between teacher and student roles is expressed through the sentence's own architecture rather than left for the reader to infer.

Strategies for Combining Sentences

StrategyBeforeAfter
Subordinating conjunction (logical relationship) The funding was cut. The program continued. Although the funding was cut, the program continued.
Relative clause (attach detail to subject) James Baldwin was an American novelist. He wrote Go Tell It on the Mountain. James Baldwin, the American novelist who wrote Go Tell It on the Mountain, was also a celebrated essayist.
Participial phrase (condense background) The researchers reviewed all previous studies. They then designed their experiment. Having reviewed all previous studies, the researchers designed their experiment.
Coordinating conjunction (join equal ideas) The results were unexpected. They were also significant. The results were unexpected but significant.
Semicolon (closely related clauses) Reading builds vocabulary. It also develops critical thinking. Reading builds vocabulary; it also develops critical thinking.

Knowing When Not to Combine

Not every pair of short sentences should be combined. Sometimes two separate sentences are the right choice — especially when you want to create emphasis through brevity, or when the ideas are genuinely distinct and do not need to be held together grammatically. The question to ask is: do these ideas belong together because they are part of the same thought, or are they separate thoughts that happen to appear near each other? If they are part of the same thought, combine. If they are distinct, leave them separate.

Section 10.7

Breaking Long Sentences

The opposite problem from fragmented, choppy writing is writing in which too many ideas are packed into sentences that run too long. Long sentences are not inherently bad — as discussed in section 10.2, they are sometimes exactly the right choice. But sentences that overload the reader with multiple ideas, multiple clauses, and multiple parenthetical details can make reading exhausting and can bury your most important points under their own weight.

Signs That a Sentence Needs to Be Broken

Warning Signs — Sentences That Need Breaking

You have to reread the sentence to understand what it is saying.

It contains more than one main idea that could stand independently.

It includes so many clauses and phrases that the subject and verb are hard to find.

Reading it aloud makes you run out of breath before it ends.

It contains several embedded qualifications that interrupt the flow of the main idea.

How to Break Long Sentences

The strategy is to identify the main ideas inside the long sentence and give each one its own sentence or clause — or to find the natural break point where one thought ends and another begins.

Overloaded Sentence
After Breaking
The organization, which was founded in the 1970s by a group of community activists who were concerned about the lack of affordable housing in the city and who had previously worked together on a series of neighborhood improvement projects that had received only limited attention from local government despite their documented success, has since expanded its mission and currently operates in twelve cities across three states.
The organization was founded in the 1970s by community activists concerned about affordable housing. These activists had previously collaborated on neighborhood improvement projects that, despite their documented success, received limited attention from local government. Since then, the organization has expanded its mission and now operates in twelve cities across three states.

Three sentences instead of one. Each sentence carries its own idea. The reader can process each idea before moving on to the next. The information is identical — but the revised version is dramatically easier to follow.

Balance Is the Goal

Breaking up long sentences is important, but overcorrecting by breaking everything into very short sentences creates the choppy, fragmented problem discussed in section 10.2. Most of your sentences should be in the middle range, with short sentences used for emphasis and long sentences used when ideas genuinely belong together. The goal is balance, not uniformity in the other direction.

Section 10.8

Style Choices in Academic Writing

Everything discussed in this chapter — sentence length, sentence openings, emphasis, parallelism, combination, and breaking — is a matter of style. In academic writing, style is not decoration or personality. Style is how you make meaning. Your choices about sentence structure are inseparable from your choices about what you are saying. This section brings those choices together and offers practical guidance about what kind of style serves academic writing best.

Clarity Is the Foundation

Every style choice in academic writing must be evaluated first against the question: does this make my meaning clearer, or does it make it harder to understand? A sentence can be stylistically interesting but still unclear. A sentence can be plain and simple and be exactly right.

Do not sacrifice clarity for variety. If varying a sentence opening makes the sentence harder to follow, keep the straightforward construction. If combining two sentences creates confusion about which idea is primary, leave them separate. Variety and emphasis are tools in service of clarity, not alternatives to it.

Concision Is a Virtue

Academic writing does not reward length for its own sake. A shorter, clearer sentence is almost always better than a longer one that says the same thing. When you revise, cut words that are not doing work.

Common Source of WordinessWordy VersionConcise Version
There is / There are constructionsThere is evidence that suggests a correlation.Evidence suggests a correlation.
Weak verb-noun combinationsThe committee made a decision to proceed.The committee decided to proceed.
Redundant phrasesEach and every student must submit past history.Every student must submit their history.
Empty intensifiersThe results were very significant and really important.The results were significant.

Active and Passive Voice

Active voice means the subject of the sentence performs the action: The committee approved the proposal. Passive voice means the subject receives the action: The proposal was approved by the committee. Active voice is generally preferred in academic writing because it is clearer and more direct — it is immediately obvious who is doing what.

However, passive voice has legitimate uses in academic writing. Use it when the actor is unknown (The manuscript was discovered in the nineteenth century), when the actor is irrelevant (The samples were collected over a six-month period), in scientific writing where the focus is on the process rather than the people, or when you want to emphasize the recipient of an action. The issue is not passive voice itself but overuse of it, which can make writing feel evasive, impersonal, or unnecessarily complicated.

Passive (Often Weaker)
Active (Often Stronger)
The study was conducted by researchers at the university, and important findings were reported in the journal.
Researchers at the university conducted the study and reported important findings in the journal.
The samples were collected. The data was analyzed. The report was submitted.
(Passive is appropriate here — process focus, actor irrelevant)

First Person in Academic Writing

Many students have been told never to use I in academic writing. This is an oversimplification that does not reflect actual academic practice. In many disciplines and many types of writing, first person is entirely appropriate. When you are describing your own argument, your own interpretation, your own reasoning process, or your own research decisions, I is often the clearest and most honest choice.

First Person in Academic Writing — Appropriate Uses

I argue that this interpretation overlooks key historical evidence.

In this essay, I will examine three factors that contributed to the policy's failure.

These uses of first person are direct and clear. The alternative — "This paper will argue..." or "It will be shown that..." — is often more awkward and less accurate, since papers do not argue and things do not show themselves.

That said, context matters. Check the conventions of your discipline, the expectations of your instructor, and the requirements of the assignment. Some fields, some assignment types, and some instructors prefer less use of first person. When in doubt, ask.

Academic Style Is Not Stuffy

A common misconception is that academic writing should sound formal to the point of being difficult. This is wrong. The best academic writing is clear, precise, and readable. It uses formal vocabulary where formal vocabulary is called for, but it does not use complicated language just to sound scholarly.

If a plain word works, use it. If a technical term is necessary, define it and use it consistently. If a sentence sounds awkward when you read it aloud, revise it. Your goal is to be understood by intelligent, educated readers — not to impress them with complexity. Style serves your reader; your reader does not serve your style.

Key Terms

sentence variety
The deliberate use of different sentence lengths, structures, and openings to create rhythm, emphasis, and clarity.
emphasis
The placement of important information in a sentence where readers are most likely to register it — typically at the beginning or end.
parallel structure
A grammatical principle requiring that items in a series, paired ideas, or related phrases and clauses take the same grammatical form.
given-new principle
A principle of sentence flow in which sentences begin with familiar information (given) and move toward new information, creating coherent forward movement.
participial phrase
A phrase beginning with a participle (verb form ending in -ing or -ed) that modifies the subject of the main clause.
sentence combining
A revision strategy that joins shorter sentences to express logical relationships between ideas more directly.
active voice
A sentence construction in which the grammatical subject performs the action: The committee approved the proposal.
passive voice
A sentence construction in which the grammatical subject receives the action: The proposal was approved.
concision
The quality of expressing ideas in as few words as necessary without sacrificing clarity or completeness.
faulty parallelism
An error in which items in a list or paired construction are expressed in different grammatical forms, creating inconsistency and awkwardness.
dangling modifier
A phrase or clause that does not clearly and logically relate to the word or words it is intended to modify, typically occurring at the beginning of a sentence.

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 10.1Sentence variety matters for three reasons: reader engagement, expression of logical relationships between ideas, and demonstration of sophisticated thinking. Variety means sentences that are as long as they need to be, structured to reflect the logic of your ideas.
  • Sec 10.2Short sentences create emphasis and work best when surrounded by longer ones. Medium sentences carry the bulk of academic prose. Long sentences suit complex, genuinely connected ideas. Reading aloud is the most reliable way to detect problems with sentence length.
  • Sec 10.3Sentence openings should vary to prevent monotony and to reflect logical flow. Effective alternatives to subject-first openings include transitional phrases, dependent clauses, participial phrases, prepositional phrases, and single adverbs. No single strategy should dominate.
  • Sec 10.4The end of a sentence is its most emphatic position. The beginning orients the reader. The middle is the weakest position for important information. The given-new principle creates coherent flow by beginning sentences with familiar information and moving toward new information.
  • Sec 10.5Parallel structure requires that items in a list, paired construction, or series take the same grammatical form. Faulty parallelism is a common and often subtle error. Parallelism used deliberately creates rhythm, clarity, and memorable emphasis.
  • Sec 10.6Sentence combining is a revision strategy that reveals and expresses logical relationships hidden in fragmented writing. Key strategies include subordinating conjunctions, relative clauses, participial phrases, coordinating conjunctions, and semicolons. Not every short sentence should be combined.
  • Sec 10.7Long sentences should be broken when they contain multiple main ideas, when key information is buried, or when the reader must reread them to follow the meaning. The goal is balance between the two extremes of fragmentation and overloading.
  • Sec 10.8Every style choice should be evaluated against clarity first. Concision is a virtue; cut words that are not doing work. Active voice is generally preferred; passive voice has legitimate uses. First person is appropriate in many academic contexts. Good academic style is clear and precise, not artificially difficult.

  4. Why is the end of a sentence considered the most emphatic position? How can a writer use this knowledge during revision?
  5. What is parallel structure? Define it and explain why faulty parallelism is considered an error, not just a stylistic weakness.
  6. Under what circumstances is passive voice appropriate in academic writing? Give two specific examples of situations where passive voice is the better choice.
  7. What is a dangling modifier? Explain what causes one and how to correct it. Write a sentence that contains a dangling modifier, then revise it correctly.
  8. What is the difference between revising for sentence variety and simply making sentences longer or more complicated? Where is the line between appropriate complexity and overloading the reader?

Discussion Questions

  1. Read a paragraph from a textbook in another one of your courses. Analyze the sentence variety in that paragraph: Are there short, medium, and long sentences? Do the sentences begin differently? Are there examples of parallel structure? What do you notice about the style choices the author makes?
  2. Many students have been taught to avoid first person in academic writing. Based on what you read in section 10.8, what is your view of this rule? When is first person appropriate, and when might it be less appropriate?
  3. Think about a time when you had difficulty following a piece of writing — an article, a textbook chapter, or an essay. Looking back with the concepts from this chapter in mind, can you identify any sentence-level factors that contributed to your difficulty? What might the writer have done differently?
  4. This chapter argues that style is not decoration — it is part of how meaning is made. Do you agree? Can you think of a case where two sentences contain the same information but communicate different meanings because of how they are structured?

Practice Activities

Activity 1Diagnosing Sentence Variety

Copy two paragraphs from a paper you have recently written. For each sentence, mark its approximate length (short: 1–10 words; medium: 11–25 words; long: 26+ words) and note how the sentence begins (subject-first, transitional word, dependent clause, participial phrase, prepositional phrase, or other). Then answer: What patterns do you notice? Do you rely heavily on one type of opening? Are your sentences mostly the same length? What changes would improve the variety? Revise the paragraphs to introduce more variety where it is appropriate.

Activity 2Sentence Combining

Combine each group of sentences into one or two well-constructed sentences. Focus on expressing the relationships among ideas through sentence structure.

  1. The company was founded in 1998. It began with five employees. It now employs over three thousand people. It operates in nine countries.
  2. The study had several limitations. The sample size was small. The participants were not randomly selected. The results may not apply to the general population.
  3. The author grew up in rural Mississippi. She later moved to New York. She drew on both experiences in her fiction. Her fiction explores themes of displacement and belonging.
Activity 3Emphasis and Placement

Revise each sentence so that the most important information appears in the position of greatest emphasis (typically the end).

  1. The committee eventually approved the proposal, after months of review and debate.
  2. In terms of long-term health outcomes, the study found that regular exercise was the single most significant factor.
  3. The program failed to meet its goals in its third year, according to the evaluators.
Activity 4Correcting Faulty Parallelism

Identify the error in each sentence and revise it to correct the faulty parallelism.

  1. The internship helped students develop professional skills, to build their networks, and gaining real-world experience.
  2. The professor was known for her clear explanations, her accessibility during office hours, and she always returned papers quickly.
  3. The review committee will evaluate the proposal based on its feasibility, innovation, and whether the budget is realistic.
Activity 5Finding and Placing Emphasis

Write three original sentences on any academic topic — one in which the most important information is correctly placed at the end, one in which it is incorrectly buried in the middle, and one in which it is correctly placed at the beginning for orientation purposes. Label each sentence to identify what you were aiming for and explain briefly why that placement was appropriate.

Activity 6Breaking a Long Sentence

Divide the following overloaded sentence into two or three cleaner sentences. Preserve all the original information.

Sentence to Break

The policy, which had been developed over a period of several years through a process that involved extensive consultations with stakeholders including nonprofit organizations, government agencies, and community representatives from affected neighborhoods, was ultimately rejected by the city council in a vote that surprised many observers who had assumed, based on the preliminary indications from council members during earlier hearings, that it would pass with a comfortable majority.

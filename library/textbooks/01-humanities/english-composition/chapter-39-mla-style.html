Chapter 39 — Front Matter

Chapter Overview

When you write a research paper, an analytical essay, or any academic work that draws on outside sources, you take on a responsibility to your readers. That responsibility is documentation: you must tell your readers where your information and ideas came from, give them enough detail to find those sources themselves, and make clear which ideas are yours and which belong to others.

MLA style is one of the most widely used documentation systems in academic writing, particularly in the humanities — courses in literature, writing, language, history, philosophy, film, and the arts. MLA stands for Modern Language Association, the professional organization of scholars and teachers who study and teach language and literature. The MLA publishes guidelines that help writers document sources consistently, ethically, and clearly.

This chapter introduces MLA documentation from the ground up. You will learn what MLA style is and why it exists, how to format in-text citations, how to build a Works Cited page, how to document the most common source types, and how to format a finished paper according to MLA standards. By the end of the chapter, you should be able to document most sources you will encounter in general humanities coursework and understand the principles behind MLA so that you can figure out less common situations yourself.

Documentation may seem like a mechanical task — a matter of commas and italics and page numbers — but it is deeply connected to intellectual honesty, reader trust, and the larger goals of academic writing. This chapter will help you see documentation not as an obstacle but as a professional skill.

Opening Question

You have just finished a draft of a literary analysis essay. You quoted a novelist, paraphrased a critic, and summarized a historical overview you found on an academic database. You know you need to document your sources — but how? Where do the citations go in the paper? What information do you need? What does the final list of sources look like? This chapter answers all of those questions and shows you why the answers matter as much as the rules themselves.

Section 39.1

What MLA Style Is

MLA style is a set of guidelines for formatting academic papers and documenting sources. It was developed by the Modern Language Association and is published in the MLA Handbook, which has been updated through multiple editions over several decades. Most instructors who teach in the humanities — writing, literature, language, film, philosophy — use MLA as their default documentation system. If you are enrolled in a general composition or academic writing course, there is a good chance your instructor expects MLA.

What Documentation Systems Do

Before looking at MLA specifically, it helps to understand what any documentation system is trying to accomplish. When you write a research paper, you draw on sources: books, articles, websites, databases, interviews, videos, and more. Those sources contain ideas, data, quotations, arguments, and information that belong to other writers, researchers, and thinkers. When you use that material — even when you put it in your own words — you are borrowing intellectual property. Documentation systems provide a standard, recognized method for giving credit for that borrowed material.

Documentation also serves a second purpose: it gives your readers a trail. A reader who finds your paper interesting should be able to follow your citations back to your sources, read those sources in their full context, and evaluate whether you have used them accurately and fairly. Without documentation, your reader has no way to check your work, verify your claims, or learn more about your topic from the same materials you used.

A third purpose is clarity. When every writer in a field follows the same documentation conventions, readers know exactly what to expect. An MLA in-text citation tells a reader, "There is an entry in the Works Cited that gives you full information about this source." Each system has its own logic, and readers who know the system can navigate a paper efficiently.

Why MLA Is Used in the Humanities

Different academic fields use different documentation systems because different fields have different priorities. Sciences and social sciences tend to use APA style, which emphasizes the year of publication because the currency of research matters greatly in those fields. The humanities tend to use MLA because the identity of the text — who wrote it, what the exact words were, and where in the text a passage appears — matters more than when the text was published. A Shakespeare play published today contains the same language it contained four hundred years ago, so the publication date of the edition is less important than knowing which edition, which page, and which act and scene.

This is why MLA in-text citations use author and page number rather than author and year. In literary analysis, you may refer to the same text dozens of times, and your reader needs to be able to find every specific passage you discuss.

The Container System

One of the most important ideas in modern MLA style is the concept of containers. A container is any larger thing that holds a source. A short story is contained in an anthology. A journal article is contained in a journal. A television episode is contained in a series. A webpage is contained in a website.

Many sources exist inside more than one container. A journal article that you access through an academic database exists inside the journal (first container) and inside the database (second container). MLA style asks you to document both containers when relevant. Understanding the container concept helps you build Works Cited entries more logically. Instead of memorizing a separate rule for every possible source type, you can ask yourself: What is this source? What is it contained in? What is that container contained in? The answers guide your entry.

Study Note — Container Logic

When you are unsure how to document an unfamiliar source, ask these three questions: (1) What is the specific thing I am citing? (2) What larger work contains it? (3) What contains that? Your answers map directly onto the MLA core elements for title of source, title of container, and second container.

Section 39.2

MLA In-Text Citations

An in-text citation is a brief reference placed directly in the body of your paper, immediately after any material you have borrowed from a source — a quotation, a paraphrase, or a summary. In MLA style, the in-text citation is parenthetical, meaning it appears inside parentheses at the end of the sentence or passage. It is not a footnote and is not placed in the middle of a sentence unless the sentence structure clearly calls for it.

The purpose of the in-text citation is simple: it alerts your reader that borrowed material ends here, and it identifies which source in your Works Cited holds the full information. The in-text citation and the Works Cited entry work together as a pair. You cannot have one without the other.

The Basic Format

The standard MLA in-text citation contains two pieces of information: the author's last name and the page number where the material appears. There is no comma between the author's name and the page number, and there is no "p." or "pp." before the page number. The period that ends the sentence comes after the closing parenthesis, not before it.

Example 39.2.1
Standard parenthetical citation
  • PatternBorrowed material (Author Page).
  • ExampleOne scholar argues that the novel's ending resists easy interpretation (Morrison 142).
  • NoteNo comma between Morrison and 142. No "p." before 142. Period falls after the closing parenthesis.

If you introduce the author's name in your own sentence — a practice called using a signal phrase — you only need the page number in the parentheses. Both formats are correct. Experienced academic writers vary between them to keep their prose from becoming repetitive. Using signal phrases also allows you to identify the author's credentials or perspective, which can strengthen the integration of source material into your argument.

Example 39.2.2
Citation with a signal phrase
  • PatternAuthor Name [verb] that borrowed material (Page).
  • ExampleMorrison argues that the novel's ending resists easy interpretation (142).
  • NoteBecause the author's name appears in the sentence, only the page number is needed in the parentheses.

Common Mistake — Indirect Sources

An indirect source is a source you encountered not in the original but through someone else's citation. For example, you read a book by Kaplan that quotes a speech by Brown. Ideally, find and read Brown's speech directly. When that is not possible, use "qtd. in" to indicate that you are citing the source indirectly. Never present a secondhand source as if you read the original.

Placement of In-Text Citations

In-text citations should appear as close as possible to the borrowed material, at a natural pause in the sentence — usually at the end. For a block quotation (a quotation set off from the main text because it is longer than four lines of prose or three lines of poetry), the citation comes after the final period, not before it. This is the reverse of the rule for regular quotations, which can confuse students. The reason for the difference is that a block quotation stands visually apart as its own unit of text, so the citation follows the quotation's own concluding punctuation.

Example 39.2.3
Block quotation citation placement
  • RuleIn block quotations, the citation goes after the final period.
  • ExampleThe narrator reflects at length:

    I had come to understand that the house was not simply a place but a kind of argument — a claim staked on a particular piece of earth by people who had once believed, fiercely and without apology, that the earth could be claimed. (Okafor 204)
  • NoteThe citation (Okafor 204) comes after the period that ends the quotation, and no additional period follows the parentheses.

Section 39.3

The MLA Works Cited Page

The Works Cited page is a list of all the sources you actually cited in your paper. It appears at the end of your paper on its own page. The Works Cited page is the partner of your in-text citations: every in-text citation should correspond to an entry on the Works Cited page, and every entry on the Works Cited page should correspond to at least one in-text citation in the paper. If a source appears on the Works Cited page but was never cited in the body of the paper, remove it. If a source was cited in the paper but does not appear on the Works Cited page, add it immediately.

A Works Cited page is not the same as a bibliography, which lists all sources consulted whether or not they were cited. Unless your instructor specifically asks for a bibliography, a Works Cited page is what you need.

General Format of the Works Cited Page

Works Cited Page — Formatting Requirements
  1. Begin on a new page after the last page of your paper.
  2. Center the heading Works Cited at the top. Do not bold, italicize, underline, or put it in quotation marks.
  3. List entries in alphabetical order by the author's last name. If no author, alphabetize by the first significant word of the title (ignore A, An, and The).
  4. Use a hanging indent: the first line of each entry is flush left; all subsequent lines are indented one-half inch.
  5. Double-space all entries. Do not add extra space between entries.
  6. Do not number the entries.

The Nine Core Elements of an MLA Entry

Modern MLA style is organized around nine core elements that apply across source types. Not every element applies to every source, but understanding the elements helps you build entries for sources that do not fit a familiar pattern. You do not need to include an element if it does not apply to your source or if it would not help your reader find the source. When an element does apply, include it in the order listed.

#ElementDescriptionFollowed By
1AuthorPerson or organization responsible for the workPeriod
2Title of sourceThe specific work being citedPeriod
3Title of containerThe larger work that holds the sourceComma
4Other contributorsEditors, translators, directorsComma
5VersionEdition or version, if relevantComma
6NumberVolume and issue numbers; season and episodeComma
7PublisherOrganization that produced the workComma
8Publication dateWhen the work was publishedComma
9LocationPage numbers, URLs, DOIs, or physical locationsPeriod

Each element is followed by its own punctuation. The author element ends with a period. The title of the source ends with a period (unless it already ends with a question mark or exclamation point). The title of the container ends with a comma. Subsequent elements are separated by commas, and the entire entry ends with a period.

Study Note — Using the Core Elements

The core elements approach is more useful than memorizing separate formats for every source type. When you encounter an unfamiliar source, work through the nine elements in order, skipping those that do not apply. The result will be a workable MLA entry even for sources you have never documented before.

Section 39.4

Books and Edited Collections

Books are among the most common sources in humanities research, and they represent some of the most straightforward MLA entries. The examples below illustrate the key variations you are likely to encounter.

A Book by a Single Author

The basic format for a single-author book is: Last Name, First Name. Title of Book. Publisher, Year. The book title is italicized. The publisher's name is given without corporate designations such as "Inc." or "Ltd." The year is the year of publication of the specific edition you used.

Achebe, Chinua. Things Fall Apart. Anchor Books, 1994.

A Book by Two or More Authors

For two authors, reverse only the first author's name. The second author's name appears in normal order. For three or more authors, list the first author's name followed by "et al."

Strunk, William, and E. B. White. The Elements of Style. Pearson, 2019. Kaplan, Justin, et al. The Norton Anthology of American Literature. Norton, 2017.

An Edited Collection as a Whole

When citing an edited collection as a whole, the editor's name comes first, followed by "editor" or "editors."

Gates, Henry Louis, Jr., and Nellie Y. McKay, editors. The Norton Anthology of African American Literature. Norton, 2004.

A Chapter or Essay Within an Edited Collection

When you are citing a specific essay, chapter, or story within an edited collection, the author of that piece comes first, followed by the title of the piece in quotation marks, followed by the title of the collection. This is a classic two-container situation: the essay is in the collection, and the collection is the container.

Baldwin, James. "Notes of a Native Son." The Price of the Ticket: Collected Nonfiction, edited by James Baldwin, St. Martin's Press, 1985, pp. 127–145.

A Book with a Translator

Kafka, Franz. The Trial. Translated by Mike Mitchell, Oxford University Press, 2009.

A Book in a Later Edition

Gibaldi, Joseph. MLA Handbook for Writers of Research Papers. 7th ed., Modern Language Association, 2009.
Common Mistake — Publisher Names

Do not include corporate designations (Inc., Ltd., Co.) in publisher names. Do not abbreviate "University Press" to "UP" unless your instructor specifically requires it. When a publisher uses two names joined by an ampersand, include both as they appear: "Simon & Schuster."

Section 39.5

Articles and Database Sources

Academic journals contain peer-reviewed scholarship — work that has been evaluated by other experts before publication — and they are among the most credible sources for humanities research. Most journal articles are now accessed through library databases rather than in print. When you use a database, you are working with two containers: the journal is the first container and the database is the second.

A Journal Article in Print

Kim, Jae Hoon. "Revisiting the Canon: Postcolonial Readings of Victorian Literature." Victorian Studies, vol. 58, no. 2, 2016, pp. 245–270.

A Journal Article Accessed Through a Database

When you find an article through a library database, add the database name (italicized) as a second container and include the URL or DOI at the end. A DOI (digital object identifier) is a permanent identifier for academic content and is preferable to a URL when available.

Kim, Jae Hoon. "Revisiting the Canon: Postcolonial Readings of Victorian Literature." Victorian Studies, vol. 58, no. 2, 2016, pp. 245–270. JSTOR, www.jstor.org/stable/10.2979/victorianstudies.58.2.245.

A Magazine Article

Magazine articles do not typically have volume and issue numbers. Use the full publication date instead.

Lee, Andrea. "On the Road to Timbuktu." The New Yorker, 15 May 2023, pp. 40–48.

A Newspaper Article

Osei, Kofi. "University Enrollment Trends in Sub-Saharan Africa." The Guardian, 3 Apr. 2024, p. A4.

An Article with No Author

When no author is given, begin with the title of the article. Alphabetize the entry on the Works Cited page by the first significant word of the title.

"The Case for Open Access Publishing." Nature, vol. 598, no. 7882, 2021, pp. 543–544.
Tip — Months in MLA

MLA style abbreviates months in Works Cited entries: Jan., Feb., Mar., Apr., May, June, July, Aug., Sept., Oct., Nov., Dec. Note that May, June, and July are not abbreviated. Use these abbreviations consistently in all date references.

Section 39.6

Websites and Online Sources

Websites present some of the most variable and challenging documentation situations in MLA. Website content ranges from peer-reviewed scholarship to personal blogs, from government reports to social media posts. Before documenting a website, be sure you have evaluated whether the source is appropriate for academic use. Chapter 33 of this textbook covers source evaluation in detail.

For websites, gather as much of the following information as is available: the author's name, the title of the specific page or article, the title of the website as a whole, the sponsoring organization (if different from the website name), the publication or last-updated date, and the URL. When some of this information is missing, work with what is available and move on.

A Page on a Website

Brody, Jane. "The Hidden Costs of Social Isolation." The New York Times, 12 Feb. 2022, www.nytimes.com/article-url.

A Website with No Individual Author

If the author of the webpage is an organization or institution rather than an individual, list the organization as the author.

World Health Organization. "Mental Health at Work." WHO, 28 Sept. 2022, www.who.int/news-room/fact-sheets/detail/mental-health-at-work.

When No Publication Date Is Available

When no publication or update date is available, you may note the date you accessed the page. This is especially important for content that may change over time. Add "Accessed" followed by the date before the period that closes the entry.

United Nations Educational, Scientific and Cultural Organization. "Education for Sustainable Development." UNESCO, www.unesco.org/en/education-sustainable-development. Accessed 14 Mar. 2024.

An Online Video

For videos published on platforms such as YouTube, list the creator's name or channel name, the title of the video in quotation marks, the platform name in italics, the upload date, and the URL.

Crash Course. "The Civil War, Part I — Crash Course US History #20." YouTube, 21 June 2013, www.youtube.com/watch?v=watch-id.
Common Mistake — Assuming Websites Are Not Citable

Many students assume that websites cannot be used as sources, or that they do not need to be cited. Both assumptions are wrong. Credible websites — government sites, university repositories, established news organizations, professional associations — are legitimate sources that must be documented just as carefully as printed materials. What matters is the quality and credibility of the source, not its format.

Section 39.7

Literary and Primary Texts

Literary and primary texts require special attention in MLA documentation because they are often studied in multiple editions and translations, and because writers in the humanities frequently cite them many times within a single paper. The goals in documenting literary texts are to identify the specific edition you used and to help your reader find the passage in any edition — not just the one you happen to have.

A Classic Novel in a Modern Edition

Austen, Jane. Pride and Prejudice. Edited by James Kinsley, Oxford University Press, 2008.

A Play

Plays can be cited much like books. For in-text citations of plays, use act, scene, and line numbers rather than page numbers so that readers using different editions can locate the passage.

Shakespeare, William. Hamlet. Edited by Harold Jenkins, Methuen, 1982.
Example 39.7.1
In-text citation for a play
  • Pattern(Act.Scene.Lines)
  • ExampleHamlet's famous hesitation is visible in his soliloquy: "To be, or not to be, that is the question" (3.1.56).
  • NoteAct 3, scene 1, line 56. Use periods between numbers, not commas or slashes.

A Poem in a Collection

Dickinson, Emily. "Because I could not stop for Death." The Complete Poems of Emily Dickinson, edited by Thomas H. Johnson, Little, Brown, 1960, pp. 350–351.

For in-text citations of poems, cite line numbers rather than page numbers. Use the abbreviation "line" or "lines" the first time you cite the poem, then use just the numbers for subsequent citations.

A Translation

When studying a work in translation, include the translator's name because translation is a significant intellectual act that shapes how you read the work. Different translations of the same novel can read very differently; your reader deserves to know which version you are using.

Dostoevsky, Fyodor. Crime and Punishment. Translated by Richard Pevear and Larissa Volokhonsky, Vintage Books, 1993.

A Sacred or Classical Text

The Holy Bible: New International Version. Zondervan, 2011.

For in-text citations of the Bible, identify the book, chapter, and verse rather than a page number: (John 3:16). If you use more than one translation in a paper, identify the translation in each citation: (John 3:16, New International Version).

Section 39.8

MLA Paper Formatting

Beyond citation, MLA style includes recommendations for how your entire paper should look. These formatting requirements create a consistent, professional appearance and make your work easier to read and evaluate. An instructor who reads dozens of student papers in a semester depends on consistent formatting to navigate efficiently. Learning these conventions also prepares you for professional and scholarly writing environments where formatting expectations are taken seriously.

Basic Paper Format

ElementMLA Requirement
Paper and marginsStandard white paper, 8.5 × 11 inches; one-inch margins on all sides
FontA clear, readable serif font (e.g., Times New Roman) at 12-point size
SpacingDouble-space everything throughout — header, title, body paragraphs, block quotations, Works Cited page
Paragraph indentationFirst line of each paragraph indented one-half inch from the left margin
Title pageNot required for most student papers; use the first-page header instead

The First-Page Header

MLA style does not require a title page for most student papers. Instead, use a four-line header in the upper left corner of the first page. The header contains your name, your instructor's name, the course name and number, and the date in day-month-year format. The title appears centered below the header, double-spaced from the last line of the header. Do not italicize, bold, underline, or put your title in quotation marks unless it contains the name of a work that would normally receive those treatments.

First-Page Header Format

Your Full Name

Instructor's Name

Course Name and Number

Date (Day Month Year: 15 April 2024)

Then, centered: Your Paper Title (in regular font, no special formatting)

Page Numbers and Running Head

Every page of your paper should have a running head in the upper right corner. The running head contains your last name and the page number, with no comma or other punctuation between them: Smith 1. Use your word processor's header function to insert this automatically so it appears consistently on every page. Page numbers appear in the upper right, not at the bottom center as in some other formats.

Block Quotations

A block quotation is used when a prose quotation is longer than four lines in your paper, or when a poetry quotation is longer than three lines. For block quotations: begin the quotation on a new line; indent the entire quotation one inch from the left margin; do not use quotation marks (the indentation signals that this is quoted material); place the in-text citation after the final period; and continue your own text afterward if needed.

Block quotations should be used sparingly. They are appropriate when the exact wording of a long passage is essential to your analysis, or when you are doing a close reading that requires the full passage in front of the reader. Do not block-quote material simply because it is long or to fill space. Heavy use of block quotations suggests that a writer is relying on sources rather than developing an original argument.

Study Note — Formatting with a Word Processor

Most formatting errors on student papers happen because students set options manually rather than using their word processor's built-in tools. Use the Styles menu for consistent headings, the Header function for running heads, the Paragraph settings for hanging indents, and the Line Spacing settings for double-spacing. These tools apply formatting consistently and save time. Avoid pressing Enter twice between paragraphs — it creates uneven spacing that does not meet MLA standards.

Common Mistakes in MLA Documentation

Mistake 1 — Missing in-text citations
Quotation or paraphrase appears with no parenthetical citation following it.

Every quotation, paraphrase, and summary needs an in-text citation. Including a source in the Works Cited does not substitute for citing it in the paper body.

Mistake 2 — Period before the closing parenthesis
The results were significant. (Morrison 42)

Correct: The results were significant (Morrison 42). In regular citations (not block quotations), the period comes after the closing parenthesis, not before it.

Mistake 3 — Using "p." or "pp." before page numbers
(Chen p. 44) or (Chen, 44)

Correct: (Chen 44). MLA in-text citations use no abbreviation for page and no comma between the author's name and the page number.

Mistake 4 — Reversed hanging indent
First line indented; subsequent lines flush left (the opposite of a hanging indent).

In a hanging indent, the first line is flush with the left margin and all following lines are indented one-half inch. Set this in your paragraph formatting dialog, not by pressing Tab manually.

Mistake 5 — Alphabetizing by first name
Works Cited entries listed under the author's first name (e.g., "James Baldwin" filed under J).

Alphabetize Works Cited entries by the author's last name. "James Baldwin" is filed under B: Baldwin, James.

Mistake 6 — Confusing Works Cited with bibliography
A Works Cited page that includes sources the writer consulted but never cited.

A Works Cited page lists only sources actually cited in the paper. A bibliography includes all sources consulted. Use Works Cited unless your instructor specifies otherwise.

Key Terms

APA style
A documentation system used primarily in social sciences and sciences, using author-date in-text citations. Covered in Chapter 40.
block quotation
A long quotation (more than four prose lines or three poetry lines) formatted by indenting the entire passage one inch from the left margin, without quotation marks. The citation follows the final period.
container
In MLA terminology, the larger work that holds a source. A journal contains an article; a database contains a journal; a website contains a webpage.
core elements
The nine categories of information used to build MLA Works Cited entries: author, title of source, title of container, other contributors, version, number, publisher, publication date, and location.
DOI
Digital object identifier; a permanent numerical code assigned to online academic content that provides a stable location and is preferable to a URL in Works Cited entries.
hanging indent
A formatting convention in Works Cited entries in which the first line is flush with the left margin and all subsequent lines are indented one-half inch.
in-text citation
A brief parenthetical reference placed within the body of a paper after borrowed material, pointing the reader to the full entry in the Works Cited.
MLA
Modern Language Association; the professional organization that publishes MLA documentation guidelines for the humanities.
MLA style
A set of guidelines for formatting academic papers and documenting sources, commonly used in humanities disciplines such as literature, writing, and philosophy.
running head
The author's last name and page number placed in the upper right corner of each page of a paper, inserted using the word processor's header function.
signal phrase
A phrase that introduces a source by naming the author within the sentence rather than only in a parenthetical citation (e.g., "Morrison argues that...").
Works Cited
The list of all sources cited in a paper, formatted according to MLA guidelines, alphabetized by author's last name, and placed at the end of the paper on its own page.

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 39.1MLA style is the documentation system most commonly used in humanities disciplines. It exists to give credit to sources, provide readers with enough information to locate those sources, and establish shared formatting conventions. The container system helps writers document unfamiliar sources by asking what work contains the source and what contains that container.
  • Sec 39.2The MLA in-text citation uses author and page number in parentheses at the end of borrowed material. No comma separates the author's name from the page number; no "p." or "pp." precedes the page number. Variations account for sources with no page numbers, no identified authors, multiple authors, indirect sources, and literary works such as plays and poems.
  • Sec 39.3The Works Cited page lists every source cited in the paper, alphabetized by the author's last name, with a hanging indent and consistent double-spacing. Modern MLA style organizes entries around nine core elements rather than requiring separate memorized formats for every source type.
  • Sec 39.4Books are documented with the author, italicized title, publisher, and year. Variations cover multiple authors, edited collections, chapters within collections, translators, and later editions. When citing a work within a collection, the author of the specific piece comes first.
  • Sec 39.5Journal articles require volume number, issue number, year, and page range. Articles accessed through databases include both the journal and the database as containers, with a DOI or URL at the end. Magazine and newspaper articles follow similar patterns with appropriate date formats.
  • Sec 39.6Websites require the author (individual or organization), the title of the specific page in quotation marks, the website title in italics, the publication or update date, and the URL. When no date is available, note the access date. Online videos follow a similar pattern with the platform as the container.
  • Sec 39.7Literary and primary texts are documented to identify the specific edition used. In-text citations for plays use act, scene, and line numbers; citations for poems use line numbers. Translations include the translator's name because the translator's choices significantly shape the reading experience.
  • Sec 39.8MLA paper formatting requirements include one-inch margins, double-spacing throughout, a first-page header with student and course information, a centered title without special formatting, a running head with last name and page number, and proper block quotation formatting for long quotations.

Chapter Checklist

MLA Documentation Checklist
Use before submitting any paper that uses MLA documentation
In-Text Citations
  • Every quotation, paraphrase, and summary has an in-text citation.
  • Each citation includes the author's last name and the page number (or an acceptable substitute when page numbers are unavailable).
  • There is no comma between the author's name and the page number.
  • There is no "p." or "pp." before page numbers.
  • The period comes after the closing parenthesis in regular citations.
  • Block quotations have the citation after the final period of the quotation.
  • Signal phrases are used to introduce sources smoothly in the prose.
  • Indirect sources use "qtd. in."
  • Play citations use act, scene, and line numbers (e.g., 3.1.56).
  • Poetry citations use line numbers, with "line" or "lines" on first reference.
Works Cited Page
  • The page is titled "Works Cited" — centered, no special formatting.
  • All entries are double-spaced with no extra space between entries.
  • Each entry uses a hanging indent (first line flush left, subsequent lines indented).
  • Entries are alphabetized by the author's last name.
  • Every source cited in the paper appears on the Works Cited page.
  • Every entry on the Works Cited page corresponds to at least one in-text citation.
  • Book, journal, and website titles are italicized.
  • Article, chapter, and webpage titles are in quotation marks.
  • Database articles include both the journal and the database as containers, with a DOI or URL.
Paper Formatting
  • One-inch margins on all sides.
  • Double-spacing throughout, including the header and Works Cited page.
  • Four-line first-page header: student name, instructor name, course, date.
  • Title centered below the header, without italics, bold, underlining, or quotation marks.
  • Running head with last name and page number in the upper right corner of every page.
  • First line of each paragraph indented one-half inch.
  • Block quotations indented one inch and set without quotation marks.

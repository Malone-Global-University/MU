Chapter 37 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Every academic writer, at every level, works within a system of shared trust. That system depends on one fundamental agreement: when you submit your writing, the ideas, words, and arguments in it are honestly represented. The work attributed to you is actually yours. The sources you cite are real and accurately used. The help you received is disclosed appropriately. When writers honor that agreement, academic writing can do what it is supposed to do — help people learn, advance knowledge, and participate seriously in intellectual life.

Plagiarism is the violation of that agreement. It means presenting someone else's work, words, ideas, or creative effort as your own without proper credit. Plagiarism is not simply a rule invented to make writing harder. It is a form of dishonesty that damages the trust on which education depends. It also damages the writer, because a student who submits work that is not honestly theirs learns less, grows less, and builds less of the actual skill the assignment was meant to develop.

This chapter explains plagiarism clearly and practically. It covers the full range of what plagiarism means — from deliberate copying to accidental misuse of source material — and it explains what separates honest, skilled source use from dishonest or careless practice. It also addresses the specific challenges that today's writers face: the temptation to patchwrite, confusion about proper paraphrase, uncertainty about the limits of collaboration, and the increasingly complicated questions raised by digital tools and artificial intelligence.

Understanding academic integrity is not just about avoiding punishment. It is about understanding what kind of writer and thinker you want to become.

Section 37.1

What Academic Integrity Means

Academic integrity is the commitment to honesty, fairness, and responsibility in all academic work. It covers writing, research, testing, collaboration, and the representation of your own effort and ideas. In the context of writing, it means that your submitted work honestly represents who did the thinking, the researching, and the writing.

The concept rests on a simple but important idea: in a learning community, people need to be able to trust each other's work. When a professor reads a student's essay, they are assuming that the ideas in it came from the student's thinking and the words came from the student's writing — except where sources are clearly credited. When a researcher reads a journal article, they are assuming the data was honestly collected and the cited sources were actually read and accurately represented. When an institution grants a degree, it is certifying that the student completed the required learning honestly.

This system of trust is not naive. It exists because education only works if the work is real. A student who copies an essay learns nothing from the assignment. A researcher who fabricates data produces false knowledge that may mislead others for years. A writer who presents borrowed ideas as their own deprives the original thinker of credit while building a false reputation for themselves. The damage is real, even when it is invisible.

Why Academic Integrity Matters to You Specifically

It is easy to think of academic integrity rules as something imposed from outside — punishments designed to catch students who cut corners. But integrity actually serves your interests directly. Writing assignments exist to develop your ability to think, analyze, organize, and communicate. If you complete them dishonestly, you miss the development they were designed to produce. A student who regularly submits work they did not actually write cannot write well. The ability does not develop on its own.

Academic dishonesty also creates compounding problems. A student who submits a plagiarized essay may avoid detection, but they have also avoided developing the skills that course was meant to build. When the next course assumes those skills, the student is more likely to struggle — and more likely to be tempted again. Dishonesty in academic work tends to build on itself rather than stay contained.

The professional and academic world eventually tests what you actually know and can do. A degree or transcript is only valuable if it honestly reflects your learning. Employers, graduate schools, and professional licensing boards all eventually discover whether the credential corresponds to real ability. Academic integrity, in other words, is not just about following rules. It is about taking your own education seriously.

The Components of Academic Integrity in Writing

Representing your own words and ideas honestly — not claiming borrowed work as original.

Giving proper credit for sources, ideas, and assistance you received.

Completing your own work rather than having someone else complete it for you.

Being honest about collaboration when it occurs and disclosing it as required.

Using sources accurately and fairly, without misrepresenting what they say.

Submitting original work rather than recycling previous assignments without permission.

Section 37.2

Direct Plagiarism

Direct plagiarism is the clearest and most serious form of academic dishonesty. It means copying someone else's words, sentences, or passages and presenting them as your own without quotation marks and without a citation. The source material is taken without credit and without acknowledgment.

Direct plagiarism can take several forms: copying a paragraph or section from a book, article, or website and pasting it into a paper without quotation marks or citation; submitting an essay that someone else wrote entirely, whether purchased, borrowed, or downloaded; copying another student's paper; or reproducing substantial portions of a published source without attribution. The most obvious version — downloading and submitting someone else's completed essay — is also the most likely to be detected. Instructors are familiar with their students' writing, and abrupt changes in voice, vocabulary, or argument quality are noticeable. Plagiarism detection tools are widely used and can identify copied passages even when slightly altered.

But direct plagiarism does not require copying a whole essay. Even copying a single distinctive sentence without quotation marks and a citation is direct plagiarism. The test is not how much was copied, but whether the words were presented as the writer's own when they were not.

Common Rationalizations — and Why They Fail

Some students rationalize direct plagiarism by arguing that the source is publicly available, that the copied portion is minor, or that the original author's phrasing was too precise to improve. None of these rationalizations hold up.

Making source material publicly available does not give readers the right to claim it as their own work. The original author owns their words and their expression of ideas. Taking them without credit violates that ownership regardless of how accessible the source is. Similarly, the proportion of copied material in a paper does not change whether copying occurred — a paragraph copied without attribution is plagiarized whether it appears in an otherwise original paper or a mostly copied one.

As for the idea that an original author's phrasing is "too good to change" — that is precisely the situation that quotation marks and citations are designed for. When you genuinely need to preserve an author's exact words, you quote them, identify them clearly, and cite the source. The problem is not using another writer's exact words when necessary; the problem is using them without acknowledgment.

Common Mistake

Students sometimes believe that adding a citation at the end of a paragraph excuses copying the paragraph's sentences verbatim without quotation marks. It does not. If you reproduce another writer's exact words, those words must appear in quotation marks, regardless of whether a citation is present. A citation identifies the source; quotation marks signal that the words are borrowed. Both are required when reproducing another writer's language.

Section 37.3

Accidental Plagiarism

Not all plagiarism is intentional. Accidental plagiarism occurs when a writer unintentionally fails to credit a source — not from dishonest intent, but from careless note-taking, poor research organization, misunderstanding citation requirements, or simple oversight. Accidental plagiarism is still plagiarism. The failure to give credit harms the original author and misrepresents the writer's work regardless of whether the oversight was intentional. But it is important to understand accidental plagiarism separately because it has different causes — and therefore different solutions.

CauseDescriptionPrevention
Inadequate note-takingLosing track of which phrases came from sources vs. original thinking; mixing source quotes into notes without marking themAlways mark direct quotations in notes; record source with every note
Forgotten citationsIntending to add a citation but submitting before doing soInsert citations as you draft, not afterward
Citation misunderstandingBelieving only direct quotations require citations, not paraphrases or summariesCite any borrowed idea, regardless of how it is expressed
Misjudging common knowledgeTreating specific research findings or interpretations as "general knowledge" not requiring citationAsk: did a specific person or study produce this? If yes, cite it
Rushed paraphraseParaphrasing too quickly and staying too close to the original wording without realizing itRead, set source aside, write from memory, then verify accuracy
Study Note — What Counts as Common Knowledge

Common knowledge is widely shared, broadly known information that is not the product of a specific individual's original research. The dates of a well-known historical event, the name of a country's capital, or a basic scientific principle appearing in every textbook can generally be stated without a source. But specific claims, statistics, research findings, interpretations, and arguments are not common knowledge even when widely discussed. If a specific person or study produced the information, it requires a citation.

The prevention of accidental plagiarism is largely a matter of developing disciplined habits during research and drafting. Always record the full source information when you take any note from any source. Never copy a phrase into your notes without clearly marking it with quotation marks and the source. When you write your draft, assume that any idea appearing in your research notes came from a source unless you can identify it as clearly your own. Check your draft against your notes before submitting. These habits do not require extraordinary effort — they require consistent care throughout the process rather than rushed work at the last moment.

Section 37.4

Patchwriting

Patchwriting is one of the most common forms of academic dishonesty, and also one of the most misunderstood. Patchwriting occurs when a writer takes a source's sentences and makes small changes — replacing some words with synonyms, rearranging phrases, or slightly altering the structure — while keeping the basic sentence pattern and ideas of the original. The result looks superficially different from the source but is not genuinely the writer's own work.

Patchwriting is often the result of misunderstanding what paraphrase means. A student who thinks that "putting something in your own words" means swapping out individual words for synonyms is likely to produce patchwriting. Real paraphrase means genuinely understanding an idea and then explaining it in your own sentence structures and your own voice — not merely altering individual words in someone else's sentence.

Example 37.4.1 — Recognizing Patchwriting
Original passage, patchwritten version, and genuine paraphrase compared
  • Original "Regular physical exercise has been shown to improve cognitive function in adults, including enhanced memory, better concentration, and reduced symptoms of anxiety and depression."
  • Patchwritten "Consistent physical activity has been demonstrated to enhance mental performance in grown adults, including improved recall, stronger focus, and decreased symptoms of worry and low mood." Notice: every word is replaced with a synonym, but the sentence structure is identical, the sequence of ideas is identical, and the thought pattern belongs entirely to the original source. Even with a citation, this is not acceptable paraphrase.
  • Genuine paraphrase "Adults who exercise regularly appear to benefit mentally as well as physically. Research has linked consistent physical activity to sharper memory, improved focus, and lower rates of anxiety and depression (Author, year)." This version takes the same idea but expresses it in genuinely different sentence structures. The writer has understood the content and rebuilt it in their own language — and still cites the source, because the idea came from it.
The difference: patchwriting borrows the structure. Paraphrase rebuilds from understanding.

Why Patchwriting Is a Problem — and What to Do Instead

Patchwriting is harmful for two related reasons. First, it is a form of dishonesty: the writer is taking credit for a way of thinking and expressing ideas that actually belongs to the original author. Second, and perhaps more important for student development, patchwriting is a sign that the writer has not fully understood the source. Real paraphrase requires comprehension. If you can only swap synonyms rather than genuinely restate an idea, you may be working with source material you have not yet fully understood.

Instructors frequently recognize patchwriting even without running detection software, because the sentence structures and thought patterns of a patchwritten passage do not match the writer's own voice elsewhere in the paper.

The solution to patchwriting is not simply to change more words. The solution is to read a passage, set it aside, think about what it actually says, and then write from your understanding without looking at the original. Then compare what you wrote to the original to make sure you have represented it accurately — and still cite it, because the idea came from a source.

Tip — The Cover Method for Genuine Paraphrase

When paraphrasing, read the passage carefully until you feel you understand it. Close or cover the source. Write an explanation of the idea using your own words and sentence structures — as if explaining it to a classmate who has not read the source. Then uncover the original and compare: have you represented the meaning accurately? If yes, add a citation. If you find your version too close to the original's structure, revise before submitting.

Section 37.5

Improper Paraphrase

Closely related to patchwriting is the broader problem of improper paraphrase — using source material in ways that fail to accurately or honestly represent what the source actually says. Improper paraphrase includes patchwriting, but it also includes several other failures that writers should understand: distorting a source's meaning, paraphrasing without a citation, and cherry-picking from sources.

Paraphrase That Distorts the Source's Meaning

A paraphrase can stay entirely within your own sentence structures and still misrepresent a source by changing its meaning. This can happen accidentally when a writer paraphrases too quickly, without fully understanding the nuance of the original. It can also happen when a writer wants a source to support a point the source does not actually make, and shapes the paraphrase to match the desired argument rather than the actual content.

Source Statement
"Some studies suggest a possible correlation between social media use and increased anxiety in teenagers, though the evidence remains mixed and causation has not been established."
Distorting Paraphrase (Improper)
"Research has proven that social media causes anxiety in teenagers."

This paraphrase claims the source said something the source explicitly did not say. The source used "possible correlation," "mixed evidence," and noted causation was not established. The paraphrase erases all qualification. This is dishonest representation, not paraphrase.

Accurate Paraphrase
"While some research has found an association between teenage social media use and higher anxiety levels, the evidence is inconclusive, and researchers have not demonstrated that social media actually causes this anxiety (Author, year)."

Paraphrase must represent the source's actual meaning, including its qualifications, limitations, and uncertainty. A source that says "may be related" cannot be paraphrased as "proves" without falsifying what the source actually claims.

Paraphrase Without Citation

Even a perfect paraphrase — genuinely restated in your own language, accurately representing the source — requires a citation. The citation is for the idea, not just for the quoted words. If the idea came from a source, the source must be credited, regardless of whether any of the original wording was preserved. This is one of the most common misunderstandings about citation: many students believe that paraphrase does not require a citation because they "put it in their own words." The words may be theirs, but the idea still belongs to the source.

Cherry-Picking

Another form of improper paraphrase involves taking only the parts of a source that support your argument and ignoring contradictory information the same source presents. This is sometimes called cherry-picking. A source may acknowledge evidence on both sides of an issue, but a selective paraphrase that extracts only the supportive portions gives readers a false impression of what the source actually argues.

Academic writing requires honest representation of sources. If a source presents a more complex picture than your paraphrase suggests, you are misrepresenting that source — even if each individual sentence you use is technically accurate.

Common Mistake — Selective Quotation

Cherry-picking applies to quotations as well as paraphrase. Quoting only the sentence from a paragraph that supports your claim — while omitting the surrounding sentences that qualify or contradict it — misrepresents the source. Always read the full context of any passage you quote or paraphrase, and represent that context honestly.

Section 37.6

Missing or Incomplete Citations

A citation that is present but incomplete or inaccurate creates problems even when the writer had honest intentions. Missing or incomplete citations occur when a writer acknowledges a source in a general way but fails to provide enough information for readers to locate and verify it, or when a citation is present in one place but missing elsewhere in the paper where the same source is used.

When a Citation Is Missing

A citation is missing when source material — whether quoted, paraphrased, or summarized — appears in the paper with no attribution. This is a clear form of plagiarism, even if the writer intends to add the citation later and simply forgot. A citation is also missing when a writer cites a source in one paragraph but then continues using material from that same source in the next several paragraphs without any additional attribution. A citation at the beginning of a section does not cover all subsequent uses of the same source. Each distinct use — each quotation, each paraphrase, each piece of borrowed data or reasoning — needs its own attribution.

When a Citation Is Incomplete

An incomplete citation provides some information about a source but not enough to meet the requirements of the documentation style being used or to allow a reader to locate the original source. For example, citing an author's last name without a publication date (in APA style), or listing a website URL without an author or access date, is incomplete.

Incomplete citations are often the result of hasty or disorganized research. Writers who do not record full source information when they first encounter a source often find themselves unable to reconstruct all the necessary details later. This is one more reason why careful research organization, as covered in Chapter 34, matters from the very beginning of the research process.

When a Citation Is Inaccurate

An inaccurate citation attributes material to a source that does not actually contain it, provides incorrect page numbers, misidentifies the author, or otherwise misrepresents where the information came from. Inaccurate citations can result from confusion between multiple sources, careless transcription of source details, or attempts to manufacture the appearance of research support that does not actually exist. An intentionally inaccurate citation — listing a source that the writer knows does not contain the cited information — is a form of fabrication and a serious breach of academic integrity.

Tip — Citation Habits That Prevent Problems

Record full citation information every time you encounter a source you might use. Keep that information organized alongside your notes from each source. When you draft, insert citations as you write rather than planning to add them later. Before submitting, verify that every piece of source material in your paper has a corresponding, complete, and accurate citation. Do not rely on citation-generating tools to produce accurate entries automatically — always verify the results against the original source.

Section 37.7

Collaboration, Help, and Authorship

Academic writing takes place in a social context. Students discuss ideas with classmates, receive feedback from tutors, consult writing centers, and work on group projects. Most of this collaboration is not only acceptable but encouraged. The challenge is understanding where appropriate collaboration ends and a violation of academic integrity begins.

What Collaboration Typically Is Acceptable

Discussing ideas before writing is generally acceptable and often valuable. Brainstorming with a classmate about possible thesis directions, asking a friend to explain a concept you do not understand, or talking through your argument with a family member are all forms of intellectual engagement that do not compromise your authorship of the resulting writing.

Receiving feedback on a draft is also generally acceptable. When a writing center tutor reads your draft and points out where your argument is unclear, or when a classmate tells you that your introduction is confusing, you are receiving the kind of response that helps writers improve. The writing remains yours. The revision remains yours. You are getting the kind of feedback a writer would normally receive from an editor or a reader — guidance and response, not replacement of your own work.

Type of HelpGenerally AcceptableGenerally Problematic
DiscussionTalking through ideas before writing; explaining a difficult conceptHaving someone outline your argument for you in detail
FeedbackReceiving comments on clarity, structure, or argumentHaving someone rewrite your sentences or paragraphs
EditingGetting help identifying grammar errors to fix yourselfHaving someone correct all your errors for you without your engagement
AuthorshipAll ideas and writing are substantially yoursWork is substantially produced by someone else and submitted as yours

Where Collaboration Becomes a Problem

Collaboration becomes a problem when it crosses from feedback and discussion into producing the work for you. If a classmate writes a paragraph because yours was unclear, that paragraph is not your work, even if you revise it afterward. If someone else substantially rewrites your essay — not just comments on it but actually produces new sentences and arguments — the rewritten version is no longer fully yours. If you submit a paper that a tutor, family member, or paid editor has extensively rewritten, you are misrepresenting the work as your own.

The test is not who physically typed the final version. The test is whose thinking and whose language constitutes the work. If the ideas, arguments, and sentences substantially belong to someone other than the writer named on the submission, the submission misrepresents authorship.

Group Work and Shared Credit

When assignments are explicitly collaborative — group projects, co-authored papers, team presentations — the rules about individual authorship relax in ways the assignment defines. Work that is intended to be shared and submitted jointly is not plagiarism when submitted jointly. But in group work, other integrity issues can arise: one student doing nearly all the work while others claim equal credit, or one student copying another's contribution and presenting it as their own. Fair representation of contribution is part of academic integrity even in collaborative contexts.

Contract Cheating

The clearest violation in this area is having someone else — a tutor, a professional writing service, a friend, or a family member — complete your assignment for you and then submitting it under your name. This is sometimes called contract cheating when a paid service is involved. It is a serious form of academic dishonesty regardless of whether money changed hands. You are presenting as your own work something you did not actually produce.

When to Ask Your Instructor

If you are uncertain whether a specific form of help is appropriate for a given assignment, the best approach is to ask your instructor before you receive that help. Different courses have different policies about collaboration, outside feedback, and group work. When the assignment instructions are not clear about what is and is not permitted, asking is always preferable to guessing. Instructors generally appreciate students who want to understand the rules rather than work around them.

Section 37.8

Integrity in the Age of Digital and AI Tools

Technology has created new possibilities in academic writing — and new forms of academic dishonesty. Today's writers have access to tools that did not exist a generation ago, and the questions those tools raise about integrity are not always simple. This section addresses the most significant of those questions.

Search Engines, Databases, and Digital Sources

The availability of digital sources has not changed the fundamental rules of academic integrity. Whether a source is a physical library book, a scanned journal article, or a website, the rules are the same: use it honestly, represent it accurately, and cite it properly. The fact that a source is freely accessible online does not change who owns it or whether it must be cited.

One specific challenge with online sources is the prevalence of unreliable, anonymous, or unclear material. A student who copies text from a website that itself copied from another source may be plagiarizing the original source without knowing it. This is one more reason why careful source evaluation — covered in Chapter 33 — matters before using any source in academic writing.

AI Writing Tools and Academic Integrity

Artificial intelligence writing tools — tools that can generate text, complete sentences, summarize sources, answer questions, and produce extended passages of prose — present the most significant new challenge to academic integrity in recent years. The central question with AI tools is the same question that governs all academic integrity: whose work is this, and is it being honestly represented?

When a student submits text generated by an AI tool as their own writing without disclosure, they are misrepresenting the work. The words were not theirs. The sentences were not composed by their thinking. The arguments may not reflect their understanding. Presenting AI-generated text as the student's own writing is a form of academic dishonesty for the same reason that submitting a purchased essay is: the work does not represent the student's actual learning, thinking, or writing.

This is not a statement that AI tools are inherently harmful or that they have no place in academic work. The question is how they are used and whether their use is honestly disclosed.

Acceptable and Unacceptable Uses of AI Tools

Policies on AI tool use vary significantly across institutions, departments, and individual courses, and those policies are still evolving. Some instructors and courses prohibit AI assistance entirely. Others permit limited and disclosed use for specific purposes. Others may allow broader use if properly acknowledged. The specific policies that apply to your work come from your institution and your instructor, and you should know those policies before using any AI tool for academic work.

Regardless of specific course policies, several principles remain consistent. Using AI-generated text as your own submitted writing is a form of academic dishonesty. If your submitted essay contains sentences and paragraphs generated by an AI and presented as your writing, you are misrepresenting the work — whether the text was generated all at once or assembled from multiple interactions.

Using AI to understand material you are responsible for learning is a different matter. Asking an AI tool to explain a difficult concept so that you can understand it, or to give you examples of something you are studying, is more analogous to using a textbook or consulting a tutor. The understanding you build from that explanation is yours, even if the explanation came from a tool. However, the writing you produce from that understanding must still be your own.

If your institution or instructor permits any use of AI in your writing process, that use must be disclosed according to the applicable guidelines. Concealing the use of AI tools when disclosure is required is dishonest regardless of whether the AI-generated content appears in the final submission.

Important — AI Tools Can Produce Errors

AI writing tools sometimes generate inaccurate facts, false citations, and misleading claims with apparent confidence. A student who submits AI-generated content is responsible for the accuracy of that content. Discovering after submission that the AI invented a citation or misrepresented a fact does not transfer responsibility away from the student who submitted it. Always verify every factual claim and citation in your paper, regardless of how it was produced.

Other Digital Integrity Concerns

Online essay mills and file-sharing sites offer previously submitted student papers, purchased essays, and shared homework answers. Using material from these sources violates academic integrity in the same way as any other form of submitting work you did not produce. Digital citation tools and reference managers can be genuinely useful for organizing sources and generating citations, but they sometimes produce errors. A citation generated automatically by a website or database tool may be incomplete or formatted incorrectly. You are responsible for verifying the accuracy of every citation in your paper, regardless of whether you generated it manually or with a tool.

Developing Your Own Integrity Framework

The landscape of digital tools will continue to change. New tools will emerge, policies will evolve, and specific rules will be updated regularly. What will not change is the underlying principle: your submitted academic work should honestly represent your own thinking, learning, and writing. Tools and resources can support that work, but they cannot replace it without compromising its integrity.

The most reliable guide is not a specific list of prohibited tools but a genuine commitment to honest representation. If you are uncertain whether a particular use of a tool is appropriate, ask yourself honestly: does this represent my own thinking and learning, and am I representing it honestly to my instructor? Then, if still uncertain, ask your instructor before proceeding.

Widely known information not produced by a specific individual's original research, which generally does not require citation.
cherry-picking
Selectively quoting or paraphrasing only the parts of a source that support an argument while ignoring contradictory information in the same source.
contract cheating
Paying or arranging for another person or service to complete an assignment and submitting that work as one's own.
self-plagiarism
Submitting work from a previous assignment as if it were new work, without permission from the relevant instructors.
citation
The formal acknowledgment of a source, providing enough information to locate the original and to distinguish the writer's ideas from borrowed material.
authorship
The honest representation of who produced the thinking, research, and writing in a submitted piece of work.

Activity 1Identifying Plagiarism Types

Read the following scenarios and identify which form of plagiarism (if any) is present in each case. Explain your reasoning in two or three sentences.

  1. A student reads a magazine article about climate policy, takes notes without marking which phrases are directly copied, and later includes those phrases in an essay without quotation marks or citation.
  2. A student writes a paper on the causes of the First World War. One paragraph summarizes the main argument of a history book using different sentences from the original, but includes no citation.
  3. A student has a classmate explain a difficult passage from an assigned reading. The student then writes a paragraph responding to the reading using their own understanding of what the classmate explained.
  4. A student copies three sentences from a website, changes several words in each sentence for synonyms, and includes a citation at the end of the paragraph.
  5. A student submits a paper they wrote for a different class two semesters ago for a new assignment, without notifying the instructor.
  6. A student asks an AI writing tool to generate an outline for their essay, then writes all of the essay themselves based on their own research and thinking.

Activity 3Citation Audit

Take a paragraph you have written recently for any class that includes at least one reference to a source. Conduct a citation audit using the steps below.

Chapter Checklist

Use this checklist before submitting any academic writing assignment. Every item should be checked before you submit.

Pre-Submission Integrity Checklist
Complete before submitting any academic writing assignment
Source Documentation
  1. Every idea, fact, and argument I took from a source is acknowledged with a citation, whether I quoted, paraphrased, or summarized it.
  2. Every direct quotation has quotation marks and a complete citation.
  3. My paraphrases are genuinely in my own sentence structures — I set the source aside and rebuilt the idea from understanding, not synonym substitution.
  4. I have represented each source's meaning accurately and completely, including its qualifications and limitations.
  5. I have not cherry-picked from sources to misrepresent their overall claims.
  6. All citations are complete and accurate according to the documentation style required for this assignment.
Research and Note-Taking
  1. I recorded full citation information for every source I used during research.
  2. My notes clearly distinguish direct quotations from my own summaries and ideas.
  3. I can trace every piece of source material in my draft back to a specific, identifiable source.
Authorship and Collaboration
  1. The ideas, arguments, and sentences in this paper are substantially my own.
  2. Any feedback I received from tutors, classmates, or other readers helped me improve my own writing rather than replacing it.
  3. I did not submit this paper or any substantial portion of it for another assignment without permission.
  4. I have not used tools or assistance that my instructor prohibits for this assignment.
Digital and AI Tools
  1. I understand my instructor's and institution's policies on AI writing tools and have followed them.
  2. Any use of AI tools I made has been disclosed according to the applicable guidelines.
  3. I have verified the accuracy of all citations and factual claims, including any that may have been generated by automated tools.
Final Check
  1. I am confident that this submission honestly represents my own learning and writing.
  2. I am ready to discuss and explain any part of this paper in conversation with my instructor.

Chapter 43 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Most students finish a draft and feel relief. The writing is done, the ideas are on the page, and the deadline is approaching. It is tempting at that point to read through once for obvious errors, fix a few commas, and submit. That instinct is understandable. But it misunderstands what revision is and what it can do.

Revision is not the final polish on a finished piece of writing. It is the process through which writing becomes finished. For most writers — student writers, professional writers, academic writers, and published authors — the first draft is a starting point, not a destination. It is the raw material that revision shapes into something clear, organized, purposeful, and convincing.

The word revision comes from Latin roots meaning "to see again." That etymology is worth taking seriously. To revise is not merely to look over what you have written. It is to look at your writing differently — to step back from the page, examine what is actually there rather than what you intended, and make the changes necessary to close the gap between the two.

This chapter introduces revision as a way of thinking, not just a set of tasks. It explains the difference between revision, editing, and proofreading — three distinct activities that students often collapse into one. It describes what global revision involves and why it must come before paragraph or sentence work. It explains how to use feedback productively without losing your voice or judgment. And it offers a practical framework for building a revision plan that turns a rough draft into a strong final paper.

Opening Principle

The first draft is raw material. Revision is the work that turns it into something worth reading. Every decision in this chapter — about what to revise first, how to read your own work honestly, how to use feedback — reflects one guiding idea: revision is where writing becomes serious.

Understanding Revision: What It Is and What It Isn't
What Revision Means · Revising vs. Editing · Why Order Matters
Section 43.1

What Revision Means

Revision is the process of rethinking and reshaping a draft in order to communicate more clearly, accurately, and effectively with a reader. It is the work of making sure that what the page says matches what the writer intended to say — and that what the writer intended to say is actually worth saying, and worth saying in the way it was said.

Rethinking means more than proofreading. It means asking whether the paper's central idea is clear and correct, whether the argument holds together, whether the evidence is sufficient, whether the organization makes sense for the reader, and whether the paper does what the assignment actually asked. Rethinking can lead to significant changes — a new thesis, a reorganized structure, a paragraph cut entirely, a new section added. Students who approach revision only as surface correction never engage in this deeper work.

Reshaping means changing the structure of the writing, not just the words. A paper can be grammatically correct and still be poorly organized. It can have strong sentences and still have a weak argument. Reshaping involves moving paragraphs, expanding underdeveloped sections, cutting material that wanders from the main idea, and rebuilding introductions and conclusions that do not yet do their jobs.

Communicating with a reader is the standard against which all revision decisions should be measured. Revision is not about making the paper sound smarter, longer, or more impressive. It is about making the paper clearer and more useful for the specific reader it is trying to reach. Every revision decision should be tested against the question: Does this change make the paper more useful and clear for the reader?

One of the most important shifts a writer can make is to move from thinking like a writer to thinking like a reader. When you write a first draft, you know everything you intended. You know what you meant by that ambiguous sentence. You know why you jumped from one idea to the next. Your reader does not know any of those things. Your reader only has the words on the page. Revision is the process of making sure those words actually carry the meaning you intended them to carry.

This is harder than it sounds, because writers are often the worst readers of their own work. You have read the paper so many times, and you are so close to the ideas, that you tend to read what you meant rather than what you wrote. A sentence that makes complete sense to you may be genuinely confusing to a reader encountering it for the first time. A paragraph that seems focused and developed to you may be thin and circular when read without the benefit of everything you know but did not write down. Learning to revise well means learning to read your own writing with some of the distance of a stranger.

Study Note

Strong revision requires time and distance. If you write a draft on Monday night and try to revise it on Monday night, you will read what you meant rather than what you wrote. Whenever possible, give yourself at least a full day — ideally longer — between finishing a draft and beginning revision. Distance makes honest reading possible.

Section 43.2

Revising Versus Editing

Students frequently use the words revising and editing interchangeably. In everyday speech, this is harmless. In writing practice, conflating the two leads to a common and serious mistake: students edit first, spending their energy on sentences and grammar, and then have no energy left to revise the ideas, structure, and argument that actually determine whether the paper succeeds.

Revision and editing are different activities, aimed at different levels of the writing, performed at different stages of the process.

Revision works at the level of ideas, structure, argument, and development. When you revise, you ask large-scale questions: Is my thesis clear, specific, and arguable? Does my paper do what the assignment asked? Is my argument logically organized? Does each paragraph develop one clear idea? Is my evidence sufficient and relevant? Have I explained my evidence or just dropped it on the page? These are not questions about word choice or punctuation. They are questions about whether the paper works.

Editing works at the level of sentences, word choice, grammar, and punctuation. When you edit, you ask smaller but still important questions: Is this sentence clear? Is this word the right word? Is this sentence too long? Is this comma correct? Editing is real work and matters, but it is always secondary to revision — because there is no point in polishing the sentences of a paragraph that needs to be cut, reorganized, or completely rethought.

Think of it this way: you do not paint the walls of a room that still needs to be replumbed. Editing is painting. Revision is replumbing. Both matter. But order matters too. Proofreading is different from both: it is the final check for mechanical errors — typos, spelling mistakes, missing words, inconsistent formatting — and it happens last.

Activity Focus Questions It Asks When to Do It
Revision Ideas, structure, argument, evidence Does this paper work? First
Editing Sentences, words, grammar, punctuation Is this writing clear and correct? Second
Proofreading Typos, spelling, surface errors Is this paper clean and error-free? Last
Common Mistake

Many students think they are revising when they are actually editing. If your revision process consists mainly of changing words and fixing commas, you are editing, not revising. True revision requires stepping back from the sentence level and asking whether the paper as a whole is making a clear and well-supported argument.

The MGU Revision Order

Meaning first. Structure second. Paragraphs third. Sentences fourth. Grammar, citation, and formatting last. Students who proofread a draft before revising it are wasting time on the wrong problem. Students who edit sentences before fixing their argument are building on a foundation that may still need to be torn up.

Sections 43.3 – 43.6
The Three Levels of Revision

Section 43.3

Global Revision

Global revision is the first and most important phase of the revision process. It is called global because it addresses the whole paper — the large-scale elements of thesis, purpose, organization, argument, and evidence — before attending to individual paragraphs or sentences.

Global revision requires a particular kind of reading. You need to pull back from individual sentences and paragraphs and ask, as objectively as you can: What is this paper actually doing? Does it succeed? One practical technique is to read the paper through once without stopping to fix anything. Do not correct a typo when you spot one. Just read, and pay attention to your experience as a reader. Where do you feel confused? Where does the argument seem to jump? Where does a paragraph stall? Those impressions are diagnostic information — they tell you where to focus your revision work.

Key Questions for Global Revision
What to ask before touching individual paragraphs or sentences
  • ThesisIs the thesis specific enough? Does it make a claim the body actually develops? Is it arguable, or does it state an obvious fact? Does the paper deliver what the thesis promises?
  • PurposeIs the paper doing what the assignment asked? If it asked for analysis, is the paper analyzing — or mostly summarizing? Does the paper take a clear position when one is required?
  • OrganizationDoes the paper move from idea to idea in a logical sequence? Does each section grow naturally from what came before? Are sections out of place or interrupting the argument's flow?
  • EvidenceIs each major point supported with sufficient evidence? Is that evidence explained rather than merely presented? Are there dropped quotations — evidence that appears without introduction or analysis?
  • GapsIs anything essential missing? Is there a counterargument the paper ignores? Is one section disproportionately long or short in a way that distorts the paper's argument?

A useful technique during global revision is to make a reverse outline — not an outline of what you planned to write, but an outline of what you actually wrote. Read each paragraph and write one sentence summarizing its main point. Then look at the sequence of those sentences. Do they form a logical progression? Do any repeat each other? Do any seem disconnected from the thesis? A reverse outline reveals the actual structure of a draft, which is often different from the intended structure.

Global revision is the hardest phase because it may require making changes that feel large and costly — cutting a long section you worked hard to write, adding new material that requires more research, restructuring an organization you planned carefully. These changes can feel discouraging. But a paper that has been globally revised is fundamentally stronger than one that has only been edited at the sentence level. The time invested in global revision is the most valuable time spent in the entire writing process after the initial drafting.

Technique: The Reverse Outline

After reading through your draft once, write a one-sentence summary of each paragraph's actual main point — not what you intended it to say, but what it says. Read those sentences in sequence. If they don't build a logical argument, you have found your most important global revision work.

Section 43.4

Paragraph-Level Revision

After global revision has addressed the paper's thesis, organization, argument, and evidence, the next phase moves to the paragraph level. Paragraph-level revision asks whether each individual paragraph is doing its job effectively. This is the phase that often produces the most visible improvements in a draft.

Focus and unity. Does each paragraph have a clear topic — a single, focused idea that all its sentences develop? A paragraph that covers two or three unrelated ideas, or that wanders from its opening topic to a different point by its end, lacks unity. These paragraphs often need to be split, with each part focused on one idea.

Topic sentences. Does each paragraph open with a clear topic sentence that announces the paragraph's main idea and connects it to the paper's thesis? A paragraph without a clear topic sentence leaves the reader to guess what the paragraph is about and why it appears at this point in the paper.

Development and evidence. Does each paragraph develop its topic sentence with sufficient evidence, explanation, and analysis? A paragraph that consists of a topic sentence, one supporting detail, and a closing sentence is almost certainly underdeveloped. Academic paragraphs need to do intellectual work — not just state a claim but demonstrate it, explain it, and analyze it in enough depth that the reader is genuinely persuaded or informed.

Explanation of evidence. After every piece of evidence — a quotation, a statistic, a paraphrase, an example — the paragraph should explain what the evidence means and how it supports the paragraph's claim. Students often assume that strong evidence speaks for itself. It rarely does. The writer's job is to bridge the gap between the evidence and the claim it is meant to support.

Coherence and flow. Does the paragraph move smoothly from sentence to sentence? Are the connections between ideas clear, or does the paragraph jump from one point to another without showing the relationship? Coherence involves logical order, effective transitions, consistent pronoun reference, and the repetition of key terms that link sentences to each other and to the paragraph's main idea.

Paragraph length. Is the paragraph long enough to develop its topic fully? Is it too long, trying to cover ground that should be distributed across several paragraphs? Neither extreme serves the reader well. Underdeveloped paragraphs feel thin and unconvincing. Overly long paragraphs exhaust the reader without providing the white space and structural clarity that paragraph breaks offer.

Section 43.5

Sentence-Level Editing

Sentence-level editing comes after revision at the global and paragraph levels. Once the paper's ideas, structure, and paragraphs are sound, editing the individual sentences for clarity, concision, correctness, and variety makes the paper as readable as possible.

Clarity. Is each sentence clear on first reading? Clarity problems at the sentence level include tangled syntax, ambiguous pronoun references, misplaced modifiers, vague language, and overly complex sentences that lose the reader before they arrive at the main point.

Concision. Is each sentence as direct as it can be? Concision does not mean brevity for its own sake — a long sentence can be perfectly concise if every word earns its place. Concision means eliminating words that do not contribute to meaning: redundant phrases, hedging language that weakens claims, and elaborate constructions where simple ones will serve.

Grammar and mechanics. Are there errors in subject-verb agreement, tense consistency, pronoun reference, parallel structure, or other grammatical categories? These errors distract the reader from the paper's ideas and undermine the credibility that careful revision worked to build.

Sentence variety. Does the paper's prose have a natural rhythm, or does it march through a monotonous series of identically structured sentences? A mix of short and long sentences, varied sentence openings, and a range of patterns makes academic prose more readable.

Word choice. Are the words specific and precise? Academic writing rewards precision. The difference between many and most, between suggests and demonstrates, between affects and determines is not trivial. Each word choice makes a slightly different claim about the world.

Sentence-level editing is satisfying work because its results are immediately visible. But this satisfaction should not mislead you into treating it as the primary revision activity. Polished sentences in the service of a weak argument do not save the paper. The argument comes first.

Section 43.6

Proofreading

Proofreading is the final step in the revision process. It is the careful, systematic search for the surface-level errors that survived the editing process — typos, misspellings, missing or doubled words, inconsistent formatting, errors in citation format, and similar mechanical problems.

Because proofreading is done last, it is also the phase most likely to be rushed. Students who waited too long to begin writing often find themselves proofreading hastily in the final minutes before a deadline. Surface errors in a final submission suggest to an academic reader that the writer did not care enough about the paper to give it careful attention. They undermine the credibility that revision and editing worked to build.

Several strategies make proofreading more effective. Read slowly, word by word. Moving a finger along each line prevents your eye from skipping ahead. Read aloud — your ear will often catch mistakes your eye misses, including a repeated word, a missing article, or a sentence that ends abruptly. Print the paper: many writers catch errors on paper that they miss on screen. Use spell-check carefully: it will not catch correctly spelled but wrongly used words — form for from, their for there, pear for pair. Spell-check should supplement careful human proofreading, not replace it.

Common Mistake

Students sometimes assume that spell-check and grammar-check software have proofread their papers for them. These tools catch some errors and miss many others. There is no substitute for careful, slow, attentive reading of the final draft.

Section 43.7

Using Feedback

Most academic writing in MGU courses involves receiving feedback of some kind — from an instructor, from peers in a writing workshop, from a writing center tutor, or from a combination of sources. Using feedback productively is a skill in itself, and one that many students struggle with.

The first challenge with feedback is emotional. When you have worked hard on a paper, feedback that identifies weaknesses can feel like personal criticism. This reaction is natural and understandable. But feedback is aimed at the writing, not at you. An instructor who identifies a weak thesis or an underdeveloped paragraph is not saying that you are a poor thinker. They are saying that this draft — this particular set of words on this particular page — is not yet doing its job as effectively as it could.

The most useful way to receive feedback is to treat it as diagnostic information: information that tells you where the paper needs work and why. Feedback is most valuable when it identifies the places where the reader experienced confusion, skepticism, or a need for more. Those are exactly the places that need the most revision attention.

Reading Instructor Feedback
A productive approach to written comments
  • Read all firstRead all comments through before responding to any individual one. Look for patterns. If several comments address similar issues — vague thesis, insufficient evidence, unclear transitions — that pattern tells you the paper's most significant weaknesses.
  • Global before localPay particular attention to comments about the thesis, argument, and organization. These are global issues and should be addressed in the first phase of revision. Comments about sentence clarity can wait until sentence-level editing.
  • No dismissalDo not dismiss comments you disagree with. If a reader found a passage confusing, the passage confused that reader — regardless of what you intended. Ask what in the writing might have caused the misunderstanding, rather than concluding the reader misunderstood.
  • Peer feedbackPeers read as students — genuine data about how the paper is communicating. Be somewhat selective: if one peer says the paper is fine and another says it is confusing, the discrepancy itself is informative. If multiple peers identify the same problem, take it seriously.

The goal of using feedback is not to respond to every comment but to make the paper stronger. Sometimes responding to a comment is as simple as adding a transition or clarifying a sentence. Sometimes it requires rethinking a paragraph or restructuring an argument. The standard is always the same: does the revision make the paper clearer and more effective for the reader?

Study Note

Not all feedback requires revision. If you receive a comment suggesting a change and, after careful thought, you believe the original choice was stronger, you may decide not to make that change. The goal is good judgment, not mechanical compliance. However, if you choose not to act on feedback, be honest with yourself about why. Make sure the reason is intellectual — that you genuinely believe the original is better — and not defensive.

Section 43.8

Building a Revision Plan

Knowing the theory of revision is not the same as having a practical plan for revising a specific draft. A revision plan — a structured, ordered approach to improving a draft — keeps revision work focused and prevents the overwhelm that comes from trying to fix everything at once.

A revision plan should be built in this order: global first, then paragraph-level, then sentence-level, then proofreading. Do not skip levels, do not reverse the order, and do not try to do everything simultaneously. Moving from large to small keeps you from investing effort in sentences and paragraphs that may later be cut or restructured.

The Seven-Step Revision Plan

Step 1: Read the draft as a reader. Read the complete draft from beginning to end without stopping to make corrections. Notice where you feel confused, where the argument jumps, where a section feels thin.

Step 2: Make a reverse outline. Write a one-sentence summary of each paragraph's actual main point. Read those summaries in sequence to reveal the paper's existing structure.

Step 3: Address global issues. Work through thesis, purpose, organization, evidence, and gaps before touching individual paragraphs.

Step 4: Revise at the paragraph level. Once the global structure is sound, work through each paragraph: focus, topic sentences, development, evidence explanation, and coherence.

Step 5: Edit at the sentence level. With ideas and paragraphs strong, edit for clarity, concision, grammatical correctness, variety, and precise word choice.

Step 6: Proofread. In a final, slow, careful pass, look for surface-level mechanical errors. Read aloud if possible. Check citation formatting.

Step 7: Check against the assignment. Before submitting, read the assignment description once more and verify that the paper meets all requirements — length, format, citation style, specific questions addressed.

Building this kind of structured revision plan protects you from two common failures: the failure to revise at all (simply editing for surface errors and submitting) and the failure to revise efficiently (trying to fix everything at once and becoming overwhelmed). Revision done in a clear order, from global to local, is revision done well.

Key Terms Review

revision
The process of rethinking and reshaping a draft to improve its ideas, argument, structure, development, and effectiveness for a reader.
editing
The process of improving individual sentences for clarity, concision, grammatical correctness, and word choice. Comes after revision.
proofreading
The final review of a draft for surface-level mechanical errors, including typos, spelling mistakes, and formatting inconsistencies. Comes last.
global revision
Revision at the level of the whole paper, addressing thesis, purpose, organization, argument, and evidence before individual paragraphs.
paragraph-level revision
Revision at the level of individual paragraphs, addressing focus, topic sentences, development, evidence explanation, and coherence.
sentence-level editing
Editing that improves individual sentences for clarity, concision, grammatical accuracy, variety, and precise word choice.
reverse outline
A revision tool in which the writer summarizes the actual main point of each paragraph to reveal the paper's existing structure — as opposed to its intended structure.
feedback
Comments, suggestions, and evaluations from instructors, peers, or tutors that identify strengths and areas for revision in a draft.
diagnostic reading
Reading a draft with the goal of identifying where and why the writing is not yet working, rather than correcting errors in the moment.
re-seeing
The fundamental act of revision: stepping back from a draft to read it as a reader would, with distance from the writer's intentions and investment.

Practice Activities

Activity 1
Read for Global Revision

Take a recent draft of your own — or the sample draft your instructor provides — and read it through once from beginning to end without stopping to make corrections. As you read, keep a separate sheet of paper where you note:

  1. The moment (if any) when you felt confused about what the paper was arguing
  2. Any place where the argument seemed to jump without explanation
  3. Any section that felt thin or underdeveloped
  4. The place where you felt most convinced by the paper's argument
  5. Any gap in the argument you noticed as a reader

After reading, summarize the paper's thesis in one sentence using only what the paper actually says. Compare that sentence to the actual thesis in the paper. Are they the same?

Activity 2
Make a Reverse Outline

Using the draft from Activity 1, write one sentence for each paragraph describing what its main point actually is — not what you intended, but what it says. Arrange those sentences in a numbered list and read through them as if they were an outline.

Answer these questions:

  1. Does the sequence form a logical argument?
  2. Are any points repeated?
  3. Do any points seem unconnected to the thesis?
  4. Are any essential points missing?

Based on this reverse outline, identify the two or three most important global revisions the paper needs.

Activity 3
The Three-Level Revision Log

Take one paragraph from your draft — one that you sense is not quite working — and revise it three times, once at each level.

First revision: Address the paragraph at the global level. Does it belong in this position in the paper? Does it support the thesis?

Second revision: Address the paragraph at the paragraph level. Does it have a clear topic sentence? Is it developed with sufficient evidence? Is the evidence explained?

Third revision: Address the paragraph at the sentence level. Edit each sentence for clarity, concision, and grammatical correctness.

After all three revisions, compare the original paragraph to the final version. Write a brief explanation (two to three sentences) of the most significant change you made at each level.

Activity 4
Practicing Feedback

Exchange a draft with a classmate. Read your classmate's draft as a reader — attentively and honestly. Write feedback that addresses:

  1. What is the paper's thesis? Is it clear, specific, and arguable?
  2. What is the paper's strongest section, and why does it work well?
  3. Where is the paper least convincing, and what is missing?
  4. Identify one paragraph that needs more development and explain what kind would help.
  5. Identify one sentence that is unclear and explain what makes it confusing.

When you receive feedback from your classmate, read it through once without responding or defending. Then identify the one piece of feedback that, if acted on, would most improve your paper. Write a brief explanation of how you would act on it.

Activity 5
Build Your Revision Plan

Take a current draft you are working on and build a complete revision plan using the seven-step model from Section 43.8. For each step, write two to three sentences describing specifically what you plan to do — not the general step, but what you will actually do with this particular draft.

Complete this plan before beginning your revision, and then follow the plan in order. After completing your revision, write a short reflection (one paragraph) describing which step produced the most significant improvement in the draft.

  • Build a complete, ordered revision plan for a paper draft
  • Distinguish between revising defensively (making surface changes to look responsive) and revising genuinely (making changes that improve the paper)

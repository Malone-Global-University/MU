Chapter 21 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Most writing problems that show up in a finished essay were actually created much earlier — not during proofreading, not during drafting, but before the student wrote a single sentence of the actual paper. The essay that wanders without focus, that buries its main point in the third body paragraph, that repeats itself without realizing it, or that suddenly runs out of things to say — these are not drafting failures. They are planning failures.

Planning is not a preliminary activity that serious writers skip in favor of getting straight to the real work. Planning is real work. It is the stage where a writer transforms a vague topic or confusing assignment prompt into a clear, purposeful structure that can actually be written. Without it, students often find themselves staring at a blank page for too long, writing in circles without direction, or discovering midway through a draft that they do not actually know what they want to say.

This chapter teaches you how to move from an assignment to a finished plan. It explains how to understand what an assignment is really asking, how to generate and focus ideas, how to organize your thinking into a logical structure, and how to create outlines — formal and informal — that guide your writing without locking you into a rigid script. It also addresses one of the most common planning errors students make: spending so much time planning that they never actually write.

By the end of this chapter, you will understand planning not as a chore that comes before the real writing, but as a form of thinking that makes the writing itself faster, clearer, and more effective.

Opening Question

Think of the last time you sat down to write an essay and felt genuinely lost — unsure where to begin, unsure what you actually wanted to argue, unsure whether your ideas were organized. Now ask: at what point did that confusion begin? In most cases, the answer is not the drafting stage. The confusion was already present before the first sentence appeared on the page. This chapter is about what to do in that space — before drafting begins — so that the writing itself can proceed with direction and purpose.

Section 21.1

Understanding the Assignment Prompt

Before you brainstorm a single idea, before you write a thesis, before you decide on structure or evidence — you need to understand exactly what you have been asked to do. This sounds obvious, but a surprisingly large number of weak essays begin not with weak ideas or poor writing, but with a misreading of the assignment.

An assignment prompt is not just a topic suggestion. It is a set of instructions that specifies the kind of thinking the writer must perform, the kind of writing that must result, the audience who will read it, and often the specific evidence or materials that must be used. A student who reads the prompt quickly, picks out a few key words, and charges directly into drafting is likely to produce an essay that is well-written but does not answer the question — which is worse than a rough draft that answers it imperfectly.

Reading for the Task Verb

The most important word or phrase in most assignment prompts is the task verb — the action word that tells you what to do with the topic. Common task verbs include: analyze, argue, compare, contrast, describe, discuss, evaluate, explain, explore, identify, reflect, summarize, and synthesize. Each of these verbs describes a different kind of intellectual operation, and they are not interchangeable.

If the prompt asks you to analyze a text, you are being asked to examine its parts and explain how they work together or what they reveal. If the prompt asks you to summarize a text, you are being asked to report the main ideas without adding your own interpretation. If the prompt asks you to argue, you are expected to take a position and defend it with evidence. Confusing these tasks is a fundamental error. A student who writes a descriptive summary when asked for an analysis has misunderstood the assignment no matter how well-written the summary is.

Task VerbWhat It RequiresCommon Mistake
AnalyzeExamine parts; explain how they function or what they revealDescribing rather than interpreting
ArgueTake a position; support with evidence; address counterargumentsPresenting both sides without committing to a claim
Compare/ContrastExamine similarities and differences in structured formDescribing subjects separately without actual comparison
SummarizeReport main ideas accurately without personal interpretationAdding opinion or evaluation
EvaluateMake a judgment based on stated criteria and evidenceDescribing without reaching a reasoned conclusion
SynthesizeBring multiple sources or ideas together into a unified argumentSummarizing sources one by one without integrating them

Reading for Topic, Focus, Audience, and Format

Once you understand the task, identify the topic — what the essay is about — and any focus requirements that narrow the topic. Some prompts are broad: "Write an essay discussing the causes of a significant historical event." Others are narrow: "Write an essay analyzing how the author uses imagery in the first three chapters to establish the narrator's emotional state." The narrower the prompt, the more precisely your planning must match its requirements.

Sometimes a prompt names specific texts, data sets, time periods, or theoretical frameworks you must use. Note these before you begin planning. An essay that ignores a required text or substitutes your own examples when the prompt specifies particular ones is incomplete regardless of its other qualities.

Many prompts also specify audience, length, formatting style, and citation requirements. Audience shapes tone and the assumed level of background knowledge. Length shapes how much you can develop each point — a four-page essay requires a narrower focus than a ten-page essay. Make note of all of these before planning begins.

Practical Strategy — Prompt Deconstruction

Before beginning any planning, rewrite the assignment in your own words. Answer these questions in writing: What kind of writing am I being asked to produce? What is the topic? What specific materials, texts, or evidence must I use? What does success look like for this assignment? What is the reader expecting? This exercise takes fewer than ten minutes and protects you from wasting hours planning the wrong essay.

Common Mistake

Students often read assignment prompts looking for permission — searching for evidence that what they already want to write is acceptable. Read prompts to understand requirements, not to validate assumptions. If you are unsure what a prompt is asking, ask your instructor before you write three pages in the wrong direction.

Section 21.2

Generating Ideas

Once you understand the assignment, you need something to say. For many students, this is where anxiety begins. The blank page seems to demand perfect ideas delivered immediately, and when perfect ideas do not appear on demand, the student freezes or starts writing an introduction before knowing what the essay will actually argue.

The solution is prewriting — a set of informal writing and thinking strategies designed to generate raw material before drafting begins. Prewriting is private, low-stakes writing. It is not the essay. It is the mess you make before you find what you want to say. Its purpose is not to produce polished prose but to produce usable material — ideas, connections, questions, examples, arguments, and observations that you can sort and develop into an essay.

Brainstorming

Brainstorming is the most basic prewriting strategy. Set a timer for five to ten minutes, write your topic or assignment question at the top of the page, and then write down every idea, word, phrase, question, or connection that comes to mind. Do not evaluate ideas as you write them. Do not cross anything out. Do not stop to think whether an idea is good enough. The goal is volume, not quality.

Brainstorming works because it bypasses the critical self-monitoring that tends to shut down idea generation. When you allow yourself to write without judgment, connections emerge that careful, deliberate thinking would have suppressed. After the timer stops, review what you have written. Some ideas will be useless. Others will surprise you with their usefulness. A few will suggest directions for your essay that you had not considered before you started.

Example 21.2.1
What a brainstorm list looks like in practice
  • AssignmentWrite an essay analyzing how social media affects adolescent self-esteem.
  • Brainstorm likes and validation · comparison to others · curated images · fear of missing out cyberbullying · identity performance · echo chambers · comment sections anonymous cruelty · anxiety · depression statistics · positive community creative expression · influencer culture · parent monitoring · screen time body image · followers as social currency · highlight reel vs. reality
  • ResultThat list is not an essay — but it is rich material from which an essay can be planned. Several clusters are already visible: comparison and self-image, cyberbullying and mental health, identity and performance. Each cluster could anchor a focused essay.

Freewriting

Freewriting is continuous writing without stopping, editing, or evaluating. Set a timer for five to fifteen minutes, keep your pen or keyboard moving the entire time, and write about your topic in any direction that occurs to you. If you run out of things to say, write "I don't know what to say next" until another idea arrives. Do not stop.

Freewriting is particularly useful for students who have a general sense of what they think but cannot yet articulate it. The act of continuous writing forces the mind to produce and commit to ideas rather than holding them as comfortable vague impressions. After freewriting, read what you have written and highlight anything that seems like the seed of an actual argument, an interesting example, or a question worth exploring.

Clustering and Concept Mapping

Clustering, also called mind mapping or concept mapping, is a visual prewriting method. Write your main topic in the center of a blank page and circle it. Then draw lines outward from the center and write related ideas at the end of each line, circling them too. From each of those ideas, draw further lines toward smaller, more specific subtopics. Continue branching outward until you run out of connections.

Clustering is especially useful for visual thinkers and for complex topics with many interrelated parts. It lets you see the relationship between ideas spatially rather than in a linear list. When you look at a completed cluster map, you may notice that certain branches are much fuller than others — which often indicates where you have the most to say. You may also notice connections between branches that you had not anticipated.

Questioning

The questioning strategy uses a set of prompting questions to generate ideas systematically. The most commonly used framework is the journalist's six questions: Who? What? When? Where? Why? How? A more academically useful questioning framework applies these prompts to your topic:

QuestionWhat It Generates
What is this?Definition and description of the topic
What causes this, or what does this cause?Causal relationships and chains of consequence
What are the parts or components?Breakdown and structural analysis
How does this compare to related ideas?Similarities, differences, and contrasts
What are the arguments for and against?Claims, counterclaims, and the landscape of debate
What is the significance?Implications, stakes, and why the topic matters
What do I not yet understand?Gaps in knowledge that require further inquiry
Study Note — Combining Strategies

Different prewriting strategies work for different writers and different assignments. Try more than one strategy before settling on the approach that works best for you. Many experienced writers combine strategies: they brainstorm first to generate a wide range of ideas, then freewrite on the most promising ones to develop them further. Talking through your topic with a classmate or even out loud to yourself can also surface ideas that written prewriting alone misses.

Section 21.3

Finding a Focus

Prewriting generates material. The next challenge is focus: deciding, from among all the material you have generated, what your essay will actually be about. Focus is one of the most important — and most frequently avoided — tasks in essay planning. Students who skip it tend to write essays that are too broad, that shift between disconnected topics, or that reach the page limit without ever developing a central idea with depth.

The Problem with Broad Topics

A broad topic is not an essay. "Social media" is a topic. "Social media affects teenagers" is a slightly narrower topic. "Social media platforms that prioritize image-sharing are associated with increased appearance-related anxiety in adolescent girls" is a focused claim that can anchor an essay. The difference between these three versions is the difference between a subject area, a general observation, and a specific, arguable, developable idea.

The broader a topic is, the less the essay can say that is specific, interesting, or useful. An essay about "the causes of climate change" will almost certainly be shallow, because climate change has many causes and they interact in complex ways that cannot be meaningfully addressed in a student essay of typical length. An essay about "how methane emissions from cattle agriculture contribute to atmospheric warming" is narrow enough to allow real development, specific evidence, and substantive analysis. This does not mean every topic must be tiny or technical. It means the scope of the topic must match the scope of the essay.

From Topic to Question to Thesis

One of the most reliable paths from a broad topic to a focused essay runs through a question. Instead of beginning with a topic and asking "what should I say about this?", begin with a topic and ask "what question about this do I want to answer?" A question gives the essay a purpose, implies a focus, and sets up the thesis — which is the writer's answer to the question.

Broad TopicFocused QuestionWorking Thesis
Climate changeHow does individual consumer behavior compare to industrial emissions as a driver of climate change?While individual behavior changes are often emphasized in public campaigns, a small number of corporations account for a disproportionate share of global emissions, suggesting that systemic policy change is more impactful than consumer-level action.
Shakespeare's MacbethHow does Shakespeare use the witches to develop the theme of ambition?The witches in Macbeth function not as supernatural causes of Macbeth's downfall but as mirrors that reveal and amplify ambitions already present within him.
Social mediaWhat effect does social comparison on image-based platforms have on adolescent body image?Research consistently links heavy use of image-based social media to increased body dissatisfaction among adolescents, suggesting a need for digital literacy education in secondary schools.

Notice that in each case, the thesis is a specific answer to a specific question — not a general statement about the broad topic, and not a simple fact. A working thesis may not be perfectly phrased when you first write it down, and it may change as you develop your essay. That is normal. What matters at the planning stage is that you have a focused direction: a claim you intend to develop and support.

Checking Your Focus

Before moving into formal planning, test your focus by working through these questions:

  • →Is this claim specific enough to be developed in the space I have?
  • →Is this claim something I can actually support with available evidence?
  • →Does this claim respond directly to the assignment prompt?
  • →Is this claim arguable — or is it so obvious that no one would disagree?
  • →Am I genuinely interested in developing this idea, or am I choosing it because it seems easy?

If your answer to any of these questions suggests a problem, revise your focus before moving forward. Changing direction at the planning stage costs minutes. Changing direction midway through a draft costs hours.

Section 21.4

Organizing Main Points

Once you have a focused claim or working thesis, you need to figure out what your essay will actually say — not just what it is about, but what its main points of development will be, and in what order those points should appear.

Main Points and the Thesis

The main points of an essay are the major claims or ideas that together develop, support, or explain the thesis. They are what the body of the essay is built from. In a typical academic essay, each main point receives at least one body paragraph, and more complex points may require two or three paragraphs to develop properly.

Identifying your main points begins with asking: What does a reader need to understand or believe in order to accept my thesis? Each necessary step in that reasoning process is a potential main point. For a thesis arguing that Shakespeare's witches in Macbeth reveal rather than create Macbeth's ambition, the main points might be: (1) the witches only confirm desires Macbeth has already expressed; (2) Macbeth's responses reveal ambition that predates any supernatural encounter; (3) Lady Macbeth, not the witches, provides the decisive push toward action; and (4) by the end of the play, Macbeth acts entirely independently of the witches. These four points work together to support the thesis, each addressing a different aspect of the argument.

Principles of Organization

Having identified your main points, you must arrange them in the order that will be clearest and most persuasive for the reader. Several organizational patterns are available, and the right choice depends on the essay's purpose and topic.

Organizing PrincipleDescriptionBest Used For
Order of ImportanceMoves from least to most significant (or reverse), building a cumulative effectArgument essays; persuasive writing where the strongest point benefits from final emphasis
Chronological OrderArranges points according to time sequenceNarrative essays, process explanations, historical analyses where sequence is meaningful
Cause-and-Effect OrderArranges points according to causal relationshipsAnalytical and argumentative essays explaining why something happens or what consequences follow
Comparison OrderExamines two or more subjects either subject-by-subject or point-by-pointComparison essays; analytical writing requiring direct juxtaposition
Problem-Solution OrderPresents a problem and then proposes, analyzes, or argues for a solutionPolicy writing, social science essays, persuasive academic papers
Analytical GroupingClusters related points based on conceptual relationship rather than external sequenceLiterary analysis, philosophical argument, interdisciplinary essays

Whatever organizing principle you choose, test it by asking: Does the order I have chosen help the reader follow my reasoning? Would a different arrangement make the argument clearer or more persuasive? Are any of my main points redundant or out of place? Some essays use more than one organizing principle in combination — what matters is that every choice serves clarity and logical development.

Section 21.5

Formal Outlines

With a focused thesis and an organized set of main points, you have the raw material for a formal outline. A formal outline is a structured, hierarchical document that maps the full content of an essay before drafting begins. It uses a consistent system of numbers and letters to show the relationship between main points, supporting points, and specific evidence.

Why Formal Outlines Are Worth the Effort

Students sometimes resist formal outlines because they seem like extra work — one more document to produce before writing "the real thing." This resistance is understandable but mistaken. A formal outline is an investment that pays off in drafting speed, organizational coherence, and overall essay quality.

When you build a formal outline, you are making dozens of small decisions — about what to include, what to omit, how to order ideas, how to balance sections — that would otherwise have to be made in the middle of drafting. Making those decisions while drafting is expensive: it interrupts the writing process, creates patchwork organization, and often produces paragraphs that do not connect logically because the writer was figuring out the essay's direction one paragraph at a time. With a strong outline, drafting becomes largely a process of expansion and articulation — you are filling in a structure you have already designed, which is considerably easier than building the structure and the content simultaneously.

Standard Formal Outline Format

The traditional formal outline uses Roman numerals for main sections, capital letters for major supporting points within each section, Arabic numerals for specific evidence or sub-points, and lowercase letters for the most specific supporting details. Each level of the outline should be more specific than the level above it.

Outline Structure
Standard hierarchical format
  • Format Thesis: [State your thesis here] I. Introduction A. Opening context or hook B. Background information C. Thesis statement II. First main body point A. Topic sentence / main claim of this section B. Supporting evidence or sub-point 1. Specific detail, example, or quotation 2. Explanation or analysis C. Additional supporting evidence 1. Specific detail, example, or quotation 2. Explanation or analysis D. Transition or connection to next section III. Second main body point A. Topic sentence / main claim of this section ... IV. Third main body point (if applicable) ... V. Conclusion A. Restatement of thesis in new terms B. Summary of main points or synthesis C. Final implication or significance

A Worked Example

The following is a formal outline for the Macbeth essay introduced in Section 21.3. Notice how specific each entry is — specific enough that a reader who had not yet seen the essay could understand exactly what it will argue and how it will be supported.

Formal outline — witches in Macbeth as mirrors of ambition
  • ThesisThe witches in Macbeth function not as supernatural causes of Macbeth's downfall but as mirrors that reveal and amplify ambitions already present within him.
  • Outline I. Introduction A. Common interpretation of the witches as causes of Macbeth's actions B. Complexity of the relationship between supernatural suggestion and human choice C. Thesis: witches as mirrors of pre-existing ambition, not its source II. The witches confirm desires Macbeth already holds A. Macbeth is described in terms suggesting violent ambition before he meets the witches (the Captain's speech, Act I) B. His reaction to the prophecy about kingship is disproportionate — suggesting the idea was already present 1. Analysis of his "aside" speeches immediately after the prophecy 2. Broader implication: the play's tragic structure depends on human agency — a victim cannot be a tragic protagonist C. Final thought: Shakespeare uses the supernatural to show the human capacity for self-deception and moral corruption

Parallel Structure in Formal Outlines

A well-constructed formal outline uses parallel structure — entries at the same level should be grammatically and logically similar. If Roman numeral II begins with a noun phrase, Roman numeral III should also begin with a noun phrase. If capital letter entries are written as complete sentences, all capital letter entries in that section should be complete sentences.

Parallel structure in an outline is not merely cosmetic. When you construct parallel entries, you force yourself to think about whether the content at each level is truly equivalent in scope and function. Outlines that lack parallel structure often reveal planning problems — sections that are wildly different in size or scope, main points that are not actually at the same level of the argument, or evidence that has been mislabeled as a main point.

Common Mistake — Vague Outline Entries

Many students write outlines that are too vague to be useful. "Main point about Macbeth" is not a useful outline entry. "The witches confirm rather than create Macbeth's ambition, as shown by his disproportionate reaction to the prophecy" is. Your outline should be specific enough that someone else could read it and understand what your essay will argue at each stage.

Section 21.6

Informal and Visual Outlines

Formal outlines are valuable, but they are not the only form of planning, and they are not always the best form for every writer or every essay. Some students find that the rigid structure of a formal outline works against their thinking process, particularly in the early stages of planning. Others work well with formal outlines for long, complex essays but prefer lighter planning tools for shorter, more exploratory writing.

Informal Outlines

Informal outlines are simplified planning documents that capture the main structure of an essay without requiring full parallel structure, complete sentences, or multilevel hierarchies. An informal outline for the Macbeth essay might look like this:

Example 21.6.1
Informal outline — compact version of the same essay plan
  • Structure Intro — context about witch interpretation, lead to thesis Thesis — witches as mirrors, not causes Body 1 — Macbeth already has ambition before meeting the witches → Captain's description, asides after prophecy, letter to LM Body 2 — Lady Macbeth is the real driver of action → her soliloquy, the persuasion scene before the murder Body 3 — By Act IV, Macbeth acts on his own → seeks out witches himself; kills Macduff's family without instruction Conclusion — tragic protagonist, not victim; human agency is the point
This informal outline takes five to ten minutes to create and is sufficient guidance for drafting a five-to-seven-page essay.

Visual Outlines and Box Diagrams

Visual outlines move planning off the linear page and onto a spatial diagram. The most common visual outline format uses a central thesis box, with major body sections arranged around it and evidence branching from each section. Unlike a brainstorming cluster, a visual outline is organized and deliberate — it shows the structure of the essay, not just a collection of ideas.

Another visual planning approach is the box diagram, in which each major section of the essay is represented as a labeled box with brief notes about what that section will argue and what evidence it will use. Boxes are arranged in sequence. This method is particularly useful for seeing the proportions of an essay — whether one section is far larger or smaller than others, and whether the overall balance is appropriate.

Choosing the Right Planning Tool

There is no single correct planning method. What matters is that you use some planning tool consistently and that the tool you use actually serves your drafting process.

Choosing Your Planning Tool

Use a formal outline when: the essay is long (more than eight to ten pages); the argument is complex and multilayered; the assignment requires submitting an outline; you have a history of organizational problems; the essay must synthesize many sources.

Use an informal outline when: the essay is shorter and the structure is clear; you have worked through ideas sufficiently through prewriting; you are an experienced writer; a rigid outline format tends to block rather than assist your drafting.

Use a visual outline when: you think spatially and find linear lists frustrating; the essay involves many interconnected ideas hard to place in a hierarchy; you want to see the proportions of the essay before drafting.

Section 21.7

Reverse Outlining

All of the planning techniques discussed so far are applied before drafting — they help you build a structure to write toward. Reverse outlining is a planning and revision technique applied after you have already written a draft. Instead of creating an outline to guide the writing, you create an outline from what you have already written, in order to evaluate whether the draft actually has the structure it needs.

How to Create a Reverse Outline

To create a reverse outline, read through your draft and, for each paragraph, write a single sentence that states what the paragraph actually says — not what you intended it to say, but what it actually argues or explains. Number these sentences in order. The resulting list is your reverse outline.

Once you have the reverse outline, examine it carefully. Does each entry state a clear claim, or is some of your writing descriptive or unfocused? Does the sequence of entries form a logical progression, or do points appear in a confusing or arbitrary order? Are there repetitions — points that appear more than once without adding new development? Are there gaps — logical steps that the essay assumes without actually arguing? Does the opening entry establish the essay's topic and purpose? Does the final entry complete the essay's thinking?

Example 21.7.1
What a reverse outline reveals
  • DraftA student submits a draft of a literary essay. After reading it, she creates a reverse outline by summarizing each paragraph.
  • Reverse Outline 1. Introduction: The witches are often blamed for Macbeth's actions. 2. Macbeth meets the witches and receives a prophecy. 3. The witches have a long history in folklore and drama. 4. Macbeth immediately writes to Lady Macbeth after the prophecy. 5. Lady Macbeth convinces Macbeth to act. 6. The witches appear again later in the play. 7. Macbeth makes his own decisions by Act IV. 8. Conclusion: The witches are important to the play.
  • DiagnosisEntry 3 (history of witches in folklore) is off-topic — it does not support the thesis. Entry 6 ("witches appear again") is descriptive rather than analytical. Entry 8 (conclusion) does not restate the thesis or reach a meaningful conclusion. These problems are now concrete and fixable.
A reverse outline turns the abstract complaint "my essay doesn't flow" into a specific list of fixable problems.

Why Reverse Outlining Is Powerful

Reverse outlining is one of the most powerful revision tools available because it makes the abstract problem of "structure" concrete and visible. When a student says "my essay doesn't flow," what they usually mean is that the structure has problems — but "flow" is too vague a concept to diagnose or fix. A reverse outline translates that vague sense of disorder into a specific list of claims and their relationships, which makes problems identifiable and solvable.

Reverse outlining is particularly valuable for longer essays and research papers, where the structure is complex enough that writers frequently lose track of their overall organization while drafting. After revising a draft substantially, many experienced writers create a second reverse outline to check whether their revisions improved the structure or introduced new problems.

Application

Take a draft of any essay you have recently written. Create a reverse outline by summarizing each paragraph in one sentence. Then evaluate the outline: Does the sequence make sense? Are there redundant paragraphs? Are there gaps in the argument? Use your findings to create a specific revision plan — not just "improve the essay" but "move paragraph 3 after paragraph 5, cut paragraph 4, and add an analytical paragraph between 6 and 7."

Section 21.8

Planning Without Overplanning

There is a planning failure on the opposite end of the spectrum from insufficient planning: overplanning. Some students use planning as a form of avoidance. They outline extensively, revise the outline, create a new outline, organize their notes, reorganize their notes, and continue refining their plan indefinitely — because planning, unlike drafting, can never fail. You can always keep adding to it, and no one will judge an outline the way they will judge an essay.

Overplanning is a form of procrastination with extra steps. A perfect outline does not write the essay. At some point, planning must give way to drafting.

When Is Planning Complete?

Planning is complete enough to begin drafting when you can answer yes to each of the following:

  • →Do I understand what the assignment is asking me to do?
  • →Do I have a working thesis that focuses my essay and responds to the prompt?
  • →Do I know what my main points will be?
  • →Do I have a sense of what evidence or support I will use for each point?
  • →Do I know roughly what order my points will follow and why?

You do not need to know every sentence before you write it. You do not need to have found every source before you draft sections that use sources. You do not need a flawless outline before beginning. You need enough structure to give your drafting a direction and a purpose.

Letting the Draft Discover What Planning Cannot

Even with excellent planning, drafting often surprises the writer. You may begin drafting a section and discover that you have more to say about it than you expected, requiring you to split it into two sections. You may realize, midway through the body, that your thesis needs to be more specific. You may reach a point where your planned third main point no longer seems relevant because the second one developed in a direction that rendered it redundant.

These surprises are not signs that your planning failed. They are signs that writing is a form of thinking, and thinking continues during drafting. A good plan accommodates revision. A formal outline is not a contract — it is a guide. If the essay needs to develop differently than the outline suggested, let it.

Adaptive Planning

When you discover midway through drafting that your plan needs to change, stop and revise the plan before continuing. Do not simply abandon the outline and draft freely from that point forward — that is how structurally disorganized drafts are produced. Instead, update the outline to reflect what the essay has become, and use the revised outline to guide the remainder of the draft.

This practice of adaptive planning — revising the plan in response to discoveries made during drafting — is one of the habits that distinguishes experienced writers from beginners. Beginners tend to treat the plan as either a rigid script (refusing to deviate even when the essay needs to) or as irrelevant after the first few paragraphs. Experienced writers treat the plan as a living document that evolves with the writing while continuing to serve its fundamental purpose: keeping the essay organized and purposeful.

Study Note — The Productive Middle

The most productive writers are usually neither the ones who plan the most rigorously nor the ones who plan the least. They are the ones who plan enough to draft with direction, draft with enough flexibility to discover what the essay needs, and then revise with enough discipline to reshape what drafting produced.

Chapter 21 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 21.1Effective planning begins with close reading of the assignment prompt — particularly the task verb, the topic requirements, and any specific materials, formats, or audiences specified. Rewriting the prompt in your own words before planning begins prevents the most common planning failure: misunderstanding the assignment.
  • Sec 21.2Prewriting strategies — brainstorming, freewriting, clustering, and questioning — generate usable material before drafting begins. They bypass self-criticism that shuts down idea generation. Using more than one strategy produces richer and more varied raw material.
  • Sec 21.3Finding a focus means moving from a broad topic to a specific question and then to a working thesis that answers that question. The scope of the topic must match the scope of the essay. A working thesis may be imperfect at the planning stage but must provide enough direction to guide the drafting process.
  • Sec 21.4Main points are the major claims that together develop the thesis. They should be identified by asking what a reader needs to understand or believe in order to accept the thesis. Organizing principles — order of importance, chronological, cause-and-effect, comparison, problem-solution, analytical grouping — provide frameworks for arranging main points logically.
  • Sec 21.5Formal outlines use Roman numerals, capital letters, Arabic numerals, and lowercase letters to show hierarchical relationships between sections, main points, sub-points, and evidence. Parallel structure at each level is essential and reveals planning problems when it breaks down. The more specific the outline, the more useful it is for drafting.
  • Sec 21.6Informal outlines capture the same essential structure more briefly. Visual outlines and box diagrams serve writers who think spatially. The right planning tool depends on essay length, complexity, and the writer's preferred cognitive style. Any consistent planning method is better than none.
  • Sec 21.7Reverse outlining is applied after drafting: summarizing each paragraph in one sentence produces a document that reveals structural problems — gaps, repetitions, unfocused paragraphs, and weak conclusions — in concrete, fixable form. It makes abstract complaints about "flow" into specific, actionable revision tasks.
  • Sec 21.8Planning is complete when the writer has a focused thesis, organized main points, and a general sense of evidence to be used. Overplanning is a form of avoidance. Adaptive planning — revising the outline in response to discoveries made while drafting — allows the plan to serve the essay throughout the writing process, not only before it begins.

Key Terms Review

Task Verb
The action word in an assignment prompt that identifies the kind of intellectual operation required (analyze, argue, compare, summarize, etc.).
Prewriting
Informal writing and thinking strategies used before drafting to generate ideas and raw material. Includes brainstorming, freewriting, clustering, and questioning.
Brainstorming
A prewriting strategy in which the writer generates ideas rapidly and without evaluation, aiming for volume over quality within a set time limit.
Freewriting
Continuous writing without stopping, editing, or judging, used to generate ideas and surface commitments the writer did not know they had.
Clustering
A visual prewriting strategy in which ideas are arranged spatially around a central topic, with lines connecting related concepts. Also called mind mapping.
Working Thesis
A preliminary thesis statement used to focus planning and drafting. It is expected to be revised as the essay develops and should not be treated as final during the planning stage.
Main Points
The major claims or ideas that together develop, support, or explain the essay's thesis. Each main point typically receives at least one body paragraph.
Formal Outline
A hierarchical planning document using Roman numerals, capital letters, and Arabic numerals to show the relationships between sections, supporting points, and specific evidence.
Parallel Structure
Grammatical consistency among entries at the same level in an outline, ensuring logical equivalence and revealing structural imbalances when it breaks down.
Informal Outline
A simplified planning document that captures essay structure without requiring full parallel structure or a complete hierarchical format.
Visual Outline
A spatial representation of an essay's structure, suited to writers who think associatively or who want to see proportions before drafting.
Reverse Outline
A post-drafting technique in which each paragraph is summarized in one sentence to evaluate whether the draft's structure is coherent, complete, and free of gaps and repetitions.
Overplanning
The practice of refining a plan indefinitely as a form of avoidance, preventing the writer from beginning the drafting stage.
Adaptive Planning
The practice of revising the outline in response to discoveries made during drafting, so that the plan continues to guide the writing without constraining it artificially.
Analytical Grouping
An organizing principle that clusters related points based on conceptual relationship rather than time, importance, or causation. Common in literary analysis and interdisciplinary writing.

Discussion Questions and Writing Prompts

Section BDiscussion and Reflection
  1. Think of an essay you have written in the past that had structural or organizational problems — paragraphs in the wrong order, a thesis that did not quite match the body, an introduction that did not lead to the actual argument, or a conclusion that did not complete the essay's thinking. Looking back, at what stage of the process did those problems originate? Could better planning have prevented them? Explain your reasoning with specifics.
  2. Some writers argue that planning too rigorously kills creativity — that the best writing happens when writers discover what they want to say through the act of writing itself, without a predetermined structure. Others argue that structure enables rather than constrains good writing. Where do you stand on this question, and why? What evidence from your own experience do you have to support your position?
  3. Compare a formal outline and an informal outline for the same hypothetical essay. What information is present in the formal outline that is absent from the informal one? Under what circumstances is the additional detail of the formal outline worth the additional time it takes to create? Under what circumstances is it unnecessary?
  4. The reverse outline is a technique applied to a draft that already exists. Why might a writer who has already written a full draft need to step back and outline what they have produced? What does this suggest about the relationship between planning and revising in the overall writing process?

Practice Activities

Activity 1Prompt Deconstruction

Read the following assignment prompt and answer each question below it in writing.

Assignment Prompt

"Select a significant environmental policy change from the past decade and write an analytical essay examining its effectiveness. Your essay should consider both the intended goals of the policy and the evidence for or against its success. Support your analysis with credible sources, and organize your essay around a clear, arguable claim."

  1. What is the task verb? What kind of writing does it require?
  2. What is the topic, and what scope does the prompt specify?
  3. What specific requirements or constraints does the prompt include?
  4. What would a weak thesis for this essay look like, and what would a strong one look like? Write one example of each.
  5. Rewrite the prompt in your own words as a set of clear instructions to yourself.
Activity 2Prewriting in Three Methods

Choose any topic from a course you are currently studying. Use all three of the following prewriting strategies on that topic, spending five to eight minutes on each: brainstorming, freewriting, and clustering. After completing all three, write a brief reflection (one paragraph) addressing: Which method generated the most useful material? Which method produced ideas you had not thought of before? Which would you be most likely to use in the future, and why?

Activity 3From Topic to Thesis

For each of the following broad topics, complete the three-step movement: identify a focused question about the topic, then write a working thesis that answers that question. The thesis should be specific, arguable where appropriate, and focused enough to anchor a five-to-eight-page academic essay.

  1. The use of standardized testing in education
  2. The role of the narrator in a novel you have read
  3. The causes of economic inequality in any country or region
  4. Social media platforms and public discourse
  5. A scientific or technological development you have studied
  6. A historical event covered in a course you are taking

Compare your theses with a classmate. Discuss: Are the theses specific? Are they arguable? Could they be developed in an essay of the specified length?

Activity 4Building a Formal Outline

Using the thesis you developed for one of the topics in Activity 3, construct a complete formal outline for a five-to-seven-page essay. The outline should include: the thesis statement at the top; an introduction section with at least three entries (A, B, C); at least three body sections, each with a topic sentence entry (A) and at least two supporting entries (B, C) with sub-entries; and a conclusion section with at least three entries. Check your outline for parallel structure at each level. After completing the outline, write a brief reflection on what decisions the outlining process forced you to make that you had not worked out before you began.

Activity 5Reverse Outline and Diagnosis

Take a completed essay you have previously written — from this course or another — and create a reverse outline by summarizing each paragraph in one complete sentence. Number the sentences in order. Then evaluate the reverse outline by answering the following questions in writing:

  1. Does each sentence state a clear claim, or do some paragraphs lack a clear purpose?
  2. Does the sequence make logical sense, or do points appear in a confusing or arbitrary order?
  3. Are there any paragraphs that repeat an earlier point without adding new development?
  4. Are there any gaps — places where an important logical step seems to be missing?
  5. Does the conclusion actually conclude, or does it simply stop?

Based on your evaluation, write a brief revision plan identifying the three most important structural changes the essay would need — not vague goals ("improve the organization") but specific actions ("move paragraph 4 after paragraph 6, cut paragraph 3, and add a transitional paragraph between paragraphs 5 and 6 explaining the connection between the two arguments").

Chapter Checklist

Understanding the Assignment
  • I have identified the task verb and understand what kind of writing is required.
  • I know the topic and any specific focus, material, or format requirements.
  • I know the audience, length, and citation requirements.
  • I have rewritten the prompt in my own words as a set of clear instructions to myself.
Generating Ideas
  • I have used at least one prewriting strategy to generate material.
  • I have reviewed my prewriting and identified the most promising ideas.
  • I have not skipped prewriting in favor of writing the introduction first.
Finding a Focus
  • I have moved from a broad topic to a focused question.
  • I have written a working thesis that is specific, arguable, and responsive to the prompt.
  • I have checked that the scope of my thesis matches the scope of the assignment.
Organizing Main Points
  • I have identified the main points that will develop my thesis.
  • I have chosen an organizing principle suited to my essay's purpose.
  • I have arranged my main points in a logical, defensible order.
Creating a Planning Document
  • I have created a formal or informal outline suited to the essay's length and complexity.
  • My outline is specific enough to guide drafting — not just section labels, but content notes.
  • My formal outline (if used) has parallel structure at each level.
Readiness to Draft
  • My planning document gives me enough direction to begin drafting.
  • I know what my thesis is and how each main point develops it.
  • I have enough evidence or material identified for each main section.
  • I have not overplanned — I am ready to begin drafting.

Chapter 15 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

A paragraph with a strong topic sentence but nothing behind it is like a claim without a case. The topic sentence names what the paragraph will do. The rest of the paragraph must actually do it.

This chapter is about that work — the work of development. Development is the process of building a paragraph so that it genuinely supports, explains, and demonstrates its controlling idea. Underdeveloped writing is one of the most common problems in academic writing at every level, from first-year composition to graduate seminars. Students often know what they want to say but have not yet learned how to say it fully, how to support it convincingly, or how to explain what their evidence actually means.

By the end of this chapter, you will understand what counts as support in academic writing, how different kinds of evidence function, how to explain evidence rather than simply present it, and how to revise paragraphs that are thin, vague, or incomplete.

Central Principle of This Chapter

Evidence does not speak for itself. Every piece of support you bring into a paragraph requires introduction, explanation, connection, and analysis from you.

Chapter Connections

This chapter works closely with Chapter 14 (Topic Sentences and Controlling Ideas) and Chapter 16 (Coherence, Transitions, and Flow). If you understand how a paragraph begins and how its sentences connect, this chapter teaches you how to fill what comes between. Strong development is what makes coherence possible — you cannot create flow in a paragraph that has nothing in it worth connecting.

What Support Is: Evidence and the Work It Does
Defining Support · Examples · Details · Concreteness
Section 15.1

What Counts as Support

When writing teachers say a paragraph needs more support, they mean it needs material that demonstrates, proves, illustrates, or develops the controlling idea. But what exactly qualifies as support? Many students are uncertain. They sense that their paragraphs feel thin, but they are not sure what they are missing or what they are allowed to bring in.

Support in academic writing is any material that helps a reader understand, believe, or engage with a claim. That material takes many forms. It can be an example drawn from experience or observation. It can be a specific detail that makes a general statement concrete. It can be a fact or piece of data gathered from research. It can be a quotation from a primary text, a study, or a relevant authority. It can be a comparison that clarifies a difficult idea. It can be an explanation of a process, a cause, or a consequence. Sometimes support comes from a logical chain of reasoning rather than from external sources at all.

The form of support that is appropriate depends on the kind of writing you are doing, the discipline you are writing in, and the nature of the claim you are developing. A paragraph arguing that a character in a novel experiences grief will draw on quotations and close readings from the text itself. A paragraph claiming that students who sleep fewer than six hours per night perform worse on memory tasks will draw on research findings. A paragraph explaining how a democratic system handles judicial appointments will draw on factual descriptions of institutional processes. Knowing what kind of support fits your claim and your discipline is part of becoming a skilled academic writer.

What All Forms of Support Share

Every type of support — examples, details, facts, data, quotations, reasoning — makes the abstract concrete and the general specific.

A topic sentence states something. The support shows it, demonstrates it, or proves it. Without support, you have stated a position but given readers no reason to accept it and no material to think with.

There is a common misunderstanding worth naming here: students sometimes believe that longer paragraphs are automatically more developed. Length is not development. A paragraph can stretch to a full page and still be underdeveloped if every sentence repeats the same claim in slightly different words. Development means new material — new evidence, new reasoning, new connection — that genuinely advances the reader's understanding. The goal is not to fill space. The goal is to build a complete unit of thought.

Section 15.2

Examples and Details

Examples are among the most powerful tools an academic writer has. A well-chosen example can do in three sentences what a paragraph of abstractions cannot accomplish in twelve. Examples give readers something real to hold. They show rather than merely tell. They anchor general claims in specific, recognizable situations.

An example is a specific instance of a general idea. If you claim that urban planning decisions often favor wealthier neighborhoods over lower-income ones, an example might describe a specific city that invested heavily in parks and transit infrastructure in its downtown business district while leaving residential neighborhoods on the outskirts without reliable bus service or sidewalks. The example does not prove the general claim on its own, but it makes the claim visible and plausible. It gives the reader something concrete to examine.

A detail is a specific, precise piece of information that makes a statement more exact. Details can be part of examples, or they can function more simply to sharpen a claim. Instead of writing that a particular scientific study was influential, you might specify that the study was published in a leading peer-reviewed journal, involved a sample of more than two thousand participants across multiple countries, and was later replicated by three independent research teams. Those details do not change the basic claim, but they make it far more credible and informative.

Practical Strategy

After writing any general or abstract sentence, ask yourself a simple question: What does this look like? The answer to that question is usually an example or a detail waiting to be written. If you cannot answer the question, that is a signal to think more carefully before continuing.

The difference between a paragraph that uses examples and details and one that does not is the difference between writing that teaches and writing that merely gestures. Consider these two versions of the same claim about social media and teenagers.

Underdeveloped — No Examples

Social media has negative effects on teenagers. Many young people use social media constantly, and this affects how they feel about themselves. The pressure to look good online can be damaging.

Developed — With Specific Example

Social media use among teenagers has been linked to increased rates of social comparison — a process in which individuals evaluate their own lives, appearances, and achievements against those of others. When adolescents scroll through feeds populated by carefully curated images, photos taken, filtered, and selected to project idealized versions of daily life, they frequently encounter what researchers describe as upward comparisons, measuring themselves against representations that are more flattering than reality. A teenager who sees dozens of such images in a single session may find it difficult to maintain a realistic sense of what ordinary life looks like, which can contribute to dissatisfaction with their own circumstances.

The second version is longer, but it is longer because it is doing more work. It defines the mechanism (social comparison), gives a specific example of how the mechanism operates (curated images in a feed), names the particular type of comparison that is most damaging (upward comparison), and explains the cognitive and emotional consequence (distorted expectations). Each sentence advances the thought rather than restating it.

Section 15.3

Facts and Data

Facts and data are particularly important in academic writing because academic argument depends on evidence that can be verified, challenged, and examined. While examples often come from observation or a single case, facts and data represent information that has been documented, measured, or established through systematic inquiry.

A fact in academic writing is a piece of information that is verifiable and not reasonably contested. That a particular law was passed in a specific year, that a country's population reached a certain number at a certain census date, that a scientific element has a particular atomic number — these are facts. Writers use facts to establish context, to anchor claims in reality, and to show that they have engaged with the actual record of events or findings.

Data refers to information gathered through research, measurement, or systematic collection — typically numbers, percentages, rates, or results from studies. Data is particularly common in scientific, social scientific, and policy writing. When you cite a statistic — that unemployment in a given region increased by a particular percentage during a specific period, for instance — you are using data to support a claim.

Three Requirements for Using Facts and Data Well

Accuracy: Facts and data must be correct. An inaccurate statistic or a misremembered date damages the credibility of everything around it. Academic readers check claims, and a verifiable error signals carelessness.

Sourcing: Facts and data must come from credible, traceable sources. Vague appeals to "studies show" are not the same as citing specific research. Chapters 31–34 address finding and evaluating sources in detail.

Context and explanation: A number alone does not make an argument. Data without explanation is raw material, not finished argument. The writer's job is to interpret the data, not merely report it.

Common Mistake — Evidence Dumping

A common error is stacking facts, statistics, and findings without doing the analytical work of explaining them. A paragraph built almost entirely of data, with only thin sentences to hold it together, is not a well-developed paragraph. It is an unprocessed collection of information. The writer's job is to interpret the data, not merely report it.

Evidence dumping often comes from a mistaken belief that more information automatically means a stronger argument. In fact, three pieces of evidence that are carefully explained are almost always more effective than eight pieces of evidence dropped in without comment.

Section 15.4

Quotations and Source Evidence

Quotations are a specialized form of evidence. When you quote a source, you are borrowing the exact language of another writer or speaker to support your argument. Quotations serve several distinct purposes in academic writing: they preserve specific language that cannot be fairly paraphrased, they demonstrate that you have engaged directly with primary or scholarly texts, they allow readers to examine the original evidence themselves, and they give authority and texture to an argument.

But quotations are among the most misused forms of evidence in student writing, largely because students often drop them into paragraphs without sufficient introduction or explanation. A quotation should never arrive without context, and it should never be left to speak for itself.

The Source Sandwich

The source sandwich is a three-part structure for integrating quotations into academic paragraphs:

Top slice (introduction): Establish who is speaking or writing and why their words are being brought in. Set up what the reader should expect to encounter.

Filling (the quotation): The exact language of the source, properly enclosed in quotation marks and attributed.

Bottom slice (explanation): Your analysis of what the quotation means, what it shows, and how it connects to the paragraph's controlling idea.

Example 15.4.1
A dropped quotation versus a sandwiched quotation
  • Dropped Many scholars have written about the power of language. "Language is not a neutral medium that passes freely and easily into the private property of the speaker's intentions; it is populated — overpopulated — with the intentions of others." This shows that language is complicated. The quotation is strong but surrounded by almost nothing. The introduction is vague; the explanation adds no value.
  • Sandwiched Mikhail Bakhtin's theory of language challenges the common assumption that writers simply choose words to express their own meanings. For Bakhtin, every word a writer or speaker uses arrives already carrying the traces of previous uses: "Language is not a neutral medium that passes freely and easily into the private property of the speaker's intentions; it is populated — overpopulated — with the intentions of others." What Bakhtin means is that words do not belong to writers the way tools belong to craftspeople. Instead, each word has already been used — in arguments, in everyday conversation, in literature, in political speech — and those prior uses cling to it. When a writer reaches for a word, they are not picking up a blank instrument; they are handling something already shaped by history and use. This insight matters for literary analysis because it means that understanding a text requires looking not just at what an author intended, but at what cultural and linguistic meanings the author's language was already carrying. The opening sentence identifies the theoretical context. The introduction names the theorist and sets up the idea. The explanation unpacks the quotation precisely and connects it to the purpose of the analysis.
Key difference: every sentence in the sandwiched version earns its place by doing analytical work the reader could not do alone.

Additional Principles for Quotation Use

Quote selectively. Use quotations when the exact language matters. If you are summarizing a general point from a source, a paraphrase is usually better than a long quotation. Reserve direct quotation for moments when the specific words are irreplaceable — when rephrasing would distort meaning, when the original phrasing is itself the subject of analysis, or when the authority of the source is what your argument needs.

Do not begin or end a paragraph with a quotation. Opening with a quotation means the paragraph begins with someone else's voice before you have established your own. Ending with a quotation means the paragraph's final impression is the source's language, not yours. Academic paragraphs should begin and end with your voice — your claim, your conclusion, your analysis.

Keep quotations appropriately brief. Long block quotations can be appropriate in literary analysis or historical scholarship, but in many academic contexts, short, targeted quotations are more effective than long excerpts. If you are quoting more than four lines, ask whether all of it is necessary or whether a tighter selection would be stronger.

Integrate quotations grammatically. The sentence that contains a quotation should work as a single grammatical unit. If it does not read naturally aloud, revise the integration. A quotation that does not fit the grammar of its surrounding sentence is disorienting for readers.

Section 15.5

Explanation and Analysis

The Most Important Principle in This Chapter

Explaining your evidence is not optional. It is the core intellectual work of academic writing.

Evidence — examples, facts, data, quotations — does not carry self-evident meaning. Readers can look at a piece of evidence and draw different conclusions from it, depending on their background, assumptions, and interpretive frameworks. Your job as the writer is to guide readers through your reasoning: to show them how you are reading the evidence, what you see in it, and why it supports your claim.

Explanation tells the reader what the evidence means. Analysis goes further: it examines how and why, breaks the evidence down, draws connections, considers implications, and evaluates significance. Both are essential in academic writing, though analysis tends to be more valued in higher-level academic work because it demonstrates that the writer is not merely reporting but genuinely thinking.

A useful way to distinguish between thin and developed academic paragraphs is to look at the proportion of evidence to explanation. A paragraph that consists almost entirely of evidence with little explanation is underdeveloped, even if the evidence is strong. A paragraph that consists almost entirely of explanation with little actual evidence is unsupported. Strong academic paragraphs maintain a balance: evidence is presented, and explanation proportional to the complexity of that evidence follows.

Type of WritingWhat It DoesExample Move
ReportingStates what the evidence says or shows; no interpretation"The study found that 68% of students reported feeling unprepared."
ExplanationTells readers what the evidence means and why it matters"This suggests that writing instruction in high school may not adequately prepare students for college-level expectations."
AnalysisExamines how and why; evaluates implications; connects to larger questions"The gap between students' confidence in high school and their experience of college writing demands points to a systemic mismatch — not in student ability, but in what each setting asks students to do."
Example 15.5.1
Underdeveloped vs. Developed: The Same Fact, Two Different Paragraphs
  • Underdeveloped Homelessness is a major problem. In 2023, hundreds of thousands of people were experiencing homelessness in the United States on any given night. There were more people without homes in large cities. This is a serious issue. The paragraph contains a fact, but nothing is done with it. The final sentence simply restates that the topic is serious, which readers already knew.
  • Developed Homelessness in the United States is not only widespread but unevenly distributed in ways that reflect deeper structural inequalities. Point-in-time counts conducted annually by local agencies estimated that on a single night in 2023, several hundred thousand people across the country were experiencing homelessness — a number that captures only individuals visible to count teams and likely undercounts those staying in informal arrangements or doubled up in others' homes. The concentration of homelessness in urban areas is not primarily a product of individual choices; it reflects the convergence of several structural conditions: the loss of affordable housing stock in cities experiencing economic growth, insufficient public mental health infrastructure following decades of deinstitutionalization, and labor market conditions that have left certain segments of the workforce without income sufficient to cover rising rents. Understanding homelessness as the outcome of these intersecting systems, rather than as a collection of individual failures, is essential for evaluating which policy responses are most likely to be effective. This version uses facts but moves directly into explanation and analysis. It questions what the data includes and excludes, identifies structural causes, and ends with an argument about why this analytical framework matters.
The developed version does not have more facts — it has more thinking about the facts it does have.

Section 15.6

Connecting Evidence to Claims

Even well-chosen evidence, skillfully explained, can fail if it is not clearly connected to the paragraph's controlling idea. Connection is the explicit link between the evidence you have presented and the claim you made in your topic sentence. Without this link, readers are left to make the connection themselves — and they may not make the connection you intended.

New academic writers often assume that connection is obvious: if the evidence and the claim are both in the same paragraph, surely readers understand that they are related. But this assumption places too much burden on readers. Academic writing does not ask readers to figure out what you mean. It demonstrates what you mean, step by step.

The clearest form of connection is a sentence — sometimes called a clincher sentence or closing analytical sentence — that names the relationship between evidence and claim. This sentence might state what the evidence shows in relation to the topic sentence, draw a conclusion from the evidence, name the significance of what the evidence reveals, acknowledge a limitation and explain why the evidence is still meaningful, or connect the paragraph's conclusion to the larger argument of the essay.

The "So What?" Strategy

After introducing and explaining evidence, ask yourself: So what? So what does this show? Why does this matter for the claim I'm making? The answer is your connection sentence.

If you cannot answer the "so what" question, that is a signal that either the evidence does not actually support your claim, or that you have not yet done the analytical thinking the paragraph requires.

Example 15.6.1
Writing a clincher sentence
  • Topic Sentence Frederick Douglass's Narrative uses the act of learning to read as a symbol of intellectual liberation that cannot be undone by continued physical enslavement.
  • Evidence Douglass describes his slaveholder's alarm upon discovering that Douglass had learned to read, noting that the slaveholder immediately forbade further instruction and declared that a literate enslaved person would be unmanageable.
  • Weak close This shows that literacy was important in the slave narrative. Vague, restates the obvious.
  • Strong clincher Douglass's careful attention to his slaveholder's reaction makes visible something the slaveholder already understood and feared — that literacy and subjugation are incompatible, and that a man who can read has already begun to escape, regardless of whether his body is still in chains. Draws a conclusion from the relationship between the evidence and the topic sentence. Names what the slaveholder's reaction reveals about the logic of the text.
The clincher sentence does not repeat the topic sentence or the evidence. It draws a conclusion from their combination.

Section 15.7

Avoiding Underdeveloped Paragraphs

Underdevelopment is the most widespread structural problem in academic writing at the introductory level. Because it is so common, it is worth examining specifically: what does underdevelopment look like, and why does it happen?

Five Common Patterns of Underdevelopment

Pattern 1
The Assertion Paragraph
  • What it isThe topic sentence makes a claim, and every sentence that follows simply restates that claim in slightly different language. No evidence is introduced. No examples are given. The paragraph says the same thing over and over in different words and then ends.
  • ExampleReading is very important for students. Students who read a lot tend to do better in school. Reading helps people learn more and understand things better. Good students always read regularly. This is why reading matters in academic life.
  • ProblemEvery sentence says the same thing. There is no evidence, no example, no detail. The paragraph has a controlling idea but no development at all.
Pattern 2
The Evidence-Only Paragraph
  • What it isThe paragraph introduces evidence — sometimes quite a lot of it — but provides almost no explanation or analysis. Quotations, facts, and examples appear back to back, followed only by a thin closing sentence.
  • ProblemThe reader receives information but not argument. The writer has gathered material but has not done the intellectual work of interpreting it. This is the evidence-dumping pattern described in Section 15.3.
Pattern 3
The Too-Short Paragraph
  • What it isThe paragraph contains a topic sentence and one or two supporting sentences before ending. This is almost never sufficient in academic writing.
  • ProblemA paragraph of two or three sentences has declared a claim and made the barest gesture toward supporting it, but has not developed the idea fully enough to be convincing. In academic writing, a paragraph is not a list item. It is a unit of argument that needs room to build.
Pattern 4
The Vague-Example Paragraph
  • What it isThe writer uses phrases like "for example" and "for instance" but follows them with examples that are themselves vague or hypothetical rather than specific.
  • ExampleFor example, a student might feel nervous before a test. Another student might not prepare well enough. These are examples of how anxiety can affect performance.
  • ProblemWriting that "a student might feel nervous" does not count as a specific example — it is still a generalization. Real examples name specific situations, specific people, specific events, or specific research findings. Hypothetical generalizations are not evidence.
Pattern 5
The Narrative-Drift Paragraph
  • What it isThe writer begins with a topic sentence, introduces an example, and then narrates the example in such detail that the original claim is forgotten. The paragraph becomes a story. The connection between the story and the controlling idea disappears.
  • ProblemBy the end, readers remember the narrative but not the argument. Examples should illuminate the topic sentence, not replace it. The story is in service of the claim — not the other way around.

Understanding why underdevelopment happens helps writers address it. Often, students write short, thin paragraphs because they have not yet fully worked out what they think. The topic sentence states a claim the writer intends to develop, but the writer has not yet done the thinking necessary to develop it. In these cases, the solution is not to write more words; it is to think more carefully about the claim, look for evidence that actually supports it, and work through what that evidence means.

Other times, students have thought carefully about the content but underestimate how much explanation readers need. A writer who has done substantial research may feel that certain points are obvious and need only brief mention. But readers do not have the same background as the writer. What seems obvious after weeks of research may not be clear to someone encountering the material for the first time. Academic writing asks you to build the bridge for your reader, even if the destination seems obvious from where you are standing.

Section 15.8

Revising for Stronger Development

Recognizing that a draft paragraph is underdeveloped is the first step toward fixing it. Revision is where development problems are corrected. This section offers a practical six-step process for revising underdeveloped paragraphs, followed by a model revision that shows the process in action.

Six Steps for Revising Underdeveloped Paragraphs

Step 1: Identify your controlling idea precisely. Read your topic sentence and ask: What specific claim am I making? The more specific the claim, the easier it is to gather appropriate evidence. If the topic sentence is vague, clarify it before adding development.

Step 2: Ask what a skeptical reader would need. After reading your topic sentence, imagine a thoughtful, skeptical reader who says: "Interesting claim. Can you show me?" What would you need to present to convince that reader?

Step 3: Add specific, concrete evidence. Review what you have written and look for places where you have made general statements that could be replaced or followed by specific ones. Specificity is the engine of development.

Step 4: Explain each piece of evidence. After each piece of evidence, write at least one explanatory sentence. Ask yourself: What does this evidence show? What does it mean in relation to my claim?

Step 5: Connect back to the controlling idea. After explanation, make sure readers can see the relationship between your evidence and your topic sentence. Add a sentence that names the connection explicitly or draws a conclusion.

Step 6: Read the paragraph aloud and test its logic. Reading aloud is a reliable revision tool. You will notice gaps — places where the argument jumps without explanation, places where a transition is missing, places where a sentence sounds thin or incomplete.

Example 15.8.1
A complete revision — draft to developed paragraph
  • Draft Education is important for economic mobility. People with college degrees earn more money than people without them. This shows that education helps people improve their lives. Three sentences: a claim, one fact, a restatement. The fact is not explained or analyzed. No connection is drawn.
  • Revised Access to higher education remains one of the most significant factors affecting economic mobility in contemporary society, though the relationship between education and earnings is more complex than simple comparisons suggest. Workers who hold four-year college degrees earn substantially more over the course of their careers, on average, than workers without degrees — a gap that has widened significantly in recent decades as the labor market has shifted toward knowledge-based work and away from manufacturing jobs that once provided middle-class wages without requiring formal credentials. However, the relationship is not simply that education causes higher earnings. Education credentials also function as signals to employers, gatekeeping entry into certain occupations and professional networks regardless of the specific skills a degree represents. What this means for economic mobility is that access to education is not only about what students learn in classrooms; it is also about what social and professional doors a credential opens. For students from lower-income backgrounds, who may face financial barriers to completing degrees or may attend institutions with fewer employer relationships, the mobility-producing effects of education are therefore not automatic — they depend on a set of structural conditions that are themselves unequally distributed. The revision complicates the original claim, provides specific context for the data, distinguishes between two different mechanisms by which education affects earnings, and draws an analytical conclusion about what this means for economic mobility.
The revised paragraph is longer — but every sentence carries analytical weight. It is not padding; it is thinking.

Chapter 15 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 15.1Support is any material that helps a reader understand, believe, or engage with a claim. All support makes the abstract concrete. Length is not development; new material that advances the thought is development.
  • Sec 15.2Examples show a specific instance of a general idea. Details make statements more precise. Together they transform general claims into visible, examinable arguments. Asking "What does this look like?" surfaces examples waiting to be written.
  • Sec 15.3Facts and data must be accurate, sourced, and explained. Data without context and interpretation is raw material, not argument. Evidence dumping — stacking facts without analysis — is a form of underdevelopment, not strength.
  • Sec 15.4Quotations require the source sandwich: introduction, quotation, and explanation. Paragraphs should not begin or end with quotations. Quote selectively when exact language matters; paraphrase when it does not.
  • Sec 15.5Explanation tells readers what evidence means. Analysis examines how and why, evaluates implications, and draws connections. Both are required in academic writing. Strong paragraphs balance evidence and explanation proportionally.
  • Sec 15.6The clincher sentence connects evidence explicitly to the paragraph's controlling idea. The "so what?" test generates this sentence: what does this evidence show, and why does it matter for the claim you are making?
  • Sec 15.7Five patterns of underdevelopment: the assertion paragraph, the evidence-only paragraph, the too-short paragraph, the vague-example paragraph, and the narrative-drift paragraph. Underdevelopment often reflects incomplete thinking, not incomplete writing.
  • Sec 15.8Revision for development follows six steps: clarify the controlling idea, identify what a skeptical reader needs, add specific evidence, explain each piece, connect back to the claim, and read aloud to test logic. Development is not adding length — it is adding thought.

Key Terms Review

development
The process of building a paragraph through evidence, explanation, and analysis so that the controlling idea is fully supported and demonstrated.
evidence
Any material — examples, facts, data, quotations, comparisons, or reasoning — that supports or demonstrates a claim.
example
A specific instance of a general idea, used to make abstract claims visible and concrete.
detail
A precise piece of information that makes a general statement more specific, accurate, or vivid.
data
Quantitative or systematically gathered information, often used in scientific and social scientific writing to support claims.
quotation
The exact language of another writer or speaker, used to support an argument, preserve specific phrasing, or engage with a primary or scholarly text.
explanation
Writing that tells readers what evidence means in relation to a claim, interpreting and clarifying rather than simply reporting.
analysis
A deeper form of explanation that examines how and why, evaluates implications, draws connections, and assesses significance.
source sandwich
The three-part structure for integrating quotations: introduction of the source, the quotation itself, and explanation that connects it to the paragraph's claim.
clincher sentence
A closing analytical sentence that makes the connection between evidence and the paragraph's controlling idea explicit, often by drawing a conclusion.
underdevelopment
A structural problem in which a paragraph fails to provide sufficient evidence, explanation, or analysis to fully support its controlling idea.
evidence dumping
Stacking multiple pieces of evidence without adequate explanation or analysis between them — a form of underdevelopment despite apparent information density.
patchwriting
A form of source misuse in which a writer substitutes a few words in a source's phrasing rather than genuinely paraphrasing. Discussed further in Chapters 35 and 37.

Chapter 15 Checklist

Chapter 15 Checklist
Use before submitting any academic paragraph — check each item carefully
Evidence
  • My paragraph contains at least one specific, concrete piece of evidence (example, fact, data, quotation, or other support)
  • My evidence is specific rather than vague or hypothetical
  • I have not merely asserted my claim — I have supported it with actual material
Introduction of Evidence
  • I have introduced each piece of evidence with a signal phrase or context-setting sentence
  • Quotations are attributed to their source before they appear
  • My paragraph does not begin with a quotation or piece of data dropped in without setup
Explanation and Analysis
  • After each piece of evidence, I have written at least one explanatory sentence
  • My explanation tells readers what the evidence means, not just what it says
  • For complex evidence, I have gone beyond basic explanation into analysis
Connection
  • My paragraph ends with a sentence that explicitly connects the evidence to the topic sentence
  • Readers do not have to infer the relationship between my evidence and my claim — I have stated it
Development
  • My paragraph is not simply a restatement of the topic sentence in different words
  • My paragraph does not consist only of evidence without explanation (evidence dumping)
  • Each sentence in my paragraph advances the thought rather than repeating it
Quotations (if used)
  • My quotation is introduced, used, and followed by explanation (source sandwich structure)
  • My paragraph does not begin or end with a quotation
  • My quotation is grammatically integrated into the surrounding sentence
Overall
  • If a skeptical reader asked "Can you show me?" after reading my topic sentence, my paragraph genuinely answers that question
  • This paragraph is a complete unit of thought — it says something, shows it, explains it, and connects it

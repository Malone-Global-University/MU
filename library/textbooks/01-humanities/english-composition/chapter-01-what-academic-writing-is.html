Chapter 1 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Most students arrive in a writing course with years of writing experience already behind them. They have written text messages, personal essays, journal entries, social media posts, and perhaps five-paragraph essays for school. They know what writing feels like. What many students have not yet had to think about carefully is what academic writing specifically requires — and why those requirements exist.

This chapter introduces academic writing not as a set of rules to memorize but as a way of thinking, communicating, and participating in the intellectual life of a university or learning community. Academic writing has particular features — clarity, organization, evidence, and reasoned argument — not because professors enjoy formality for its own sake, but because those features make communication more reliable, more useful, and more honest.

Understanding what academic writing is, why it exists, and how it differs from other kinds of writing is the foundation on which every skill in this textbook rests. Before you can write strong sentences, develop focused paragraphs, build coherent essays, or document sources correctly, you need a clear sense of what you are actually trying to do when you write for academic purposes. This chapter builds that foundation.

Opening Question

Imagine two students are asked to respond to the same question: "Does social media harm young people's mental health?" The first student writes two pages describing their own experience and expressing their opinion. The second student writes two pages drawing on research studies, psychological findings, and specific evidence from credible sources, organized around a clear central claim. Both students spent the same amount of time. Which response is stronger academic writing — and why? By the end of this chapter, you will be able to answer that question precisely, and more importantly, you will understand the reasoning behind the answer well enough to apply it to any writing task you encounter.

Section 1.1

What Academic Writing Means

The term academic writing gets used frequently in schools and universities, but it is rarely explained directly. Students are often told to "write academically" without being shown what that actually means in practice. Before anything else, it is worth defining the term carefully.

Academic writing is purposeful, organized, evidence-based communication produced within a learning or scholarly community. That definition contains several important ideas, and each one deserves attention.

Core FeatureWhat It MeansWhy It Matters
PurposefulEvery piece of writing has a specific goal — to explain, analyze, argue, report, compare, or evaluateWithout purpose, writing has no direction and gives readers nothing to hold onto
OrganizedIdeas are arranged in a logical sequence that readers can follow, with each section building on the lastStructure is not decoration; it communicates the relationship between ideas and guides reader understanding
Evidence-basedClaims are supported by research, data, examples, expert knowledge, textual evidence, or logical reasoningEvidence makes claims accountable — verifiable by others rather than merely asserted
Community-situatedWriting is produced for and within a specific community of readers with shared expectations and conventionsWriting is always social; understanding your community's norms allows you to communicate reliably within it

Taken together, these features explain why academic writing is distinct from many other forms of writing a student encounters in daily life. A text message to a friend is purposeful but not organized for a formal audience. A diary entry may be deeply thoughtful but carries no obligation to be evidence-based. A news article may be clear and organized but may not engage in the kind of sustained reasoning and analysis that academic writing requires. Academic writing occupies a specific role in a specific context, and understanding that context is the first step toward writing effectively within it.

What Academic Writing Is Not

Academic writing is not defined by long words, complicated sentences, passive voice, or technical jargon. Many students believe that making writing sound complicated is what makes it sound academic. This is one of the most damaging misconceptions in writing education. Academic writing should be as clear as the complexity of the subject allows. The goal is precision and honesty in communication — not the performance of intelligence through obscurity.

Section 1.2

Academic Writing as Clear Thinking

One of the most important things to understand about academic writing is that it is not simply the recording of thoughts that have already been completed. Writing is not a passive container into which finished ideas are poured. Writing is itself a process of thinking.

This idea surprises many beginning writers. When you sit down to write a paper, it can feel as though you are supposed to already know what you want to say, and the job of writing is just to express those ideas in proper sentences. But this is not how most good writing actually works. Most writers — students, scholars, professionals, and creative writers alike — discover what they think by writing. The act of putting ideas into words forces the writer to test those ideas, develop them, connect them, and refine them.

Consider what happens when you try to explain a complicated idea out loud to someone else. Often, in the middle of your explanation, you realize that part of what you said does not quite make sense, or that you are missing a step in your reasoning, or that your explanation leads somewhere you had not expected. Explaining a concept to another person forces you to encounter its difficulties. Writing works the same way, but more rigorously. When you write down an argument, you can see it — examine it, question it, and improve it in ways that pure mental reflection does not always allow.

This means that a confusing piece of writing usually reflects confusing thinking, and that improving the writing usually means improving the thinking first. A student who produces an unclear essay is not always struggling with language. Often, the student has not yet thought clearly enough about what they are trying to say. The solution is not just editing sentences but returning to the ideas themselves and thinking them through more carefully.

Study Note — Writing Before You Are Ready

Do not begin a paper by immediately trying to write the first sentence. Begin by thinking: What question am I answering? What do I know about this topic? What is my main point? What evidence supports it? What complications might arise?

Only when you have a working sense of what you want to think and say should you begin drafting. And as you draft, be ready for the writing itself to reveal new questions and complications. That is not a problem — that is writing doing its job.

Academic writing, then, is a discipline of thinking as much as it is a discipline of language. When your professor asks you to write an analytical essay, they are not simply asking you to demonstrate that you can produce grammatical sentences. They are asking you to think carefully about a question, develop a position or explanation, support it with evidence and reasoning, and communicate that thinking clearly to a reader. The writing is evidence of the thinking. This is why this textbook consistently addresses ideas, arguments, and evidence before addressing sentences, grammar, and formatting. Fixing commas in a paper whose main idea is not yet clear is spending effort in the wrong place.

Section 1.3

Writing for Readers

All writing is written for someone. Most writing is written for other people, and academic writing is almost always written for readers who are not the writer. This seems obvious, but beginning writers often forget it in practice. Writing that makes complete sense to the writer — because the writer knows what they meant, what they were thinking, and what background knowledge they were drawing on — may make no sense at all to a reader who lacks that context. The reader cannot read the writer's mind. The writer must provide everything the reader needs in the text itself.

This is one reason academic writing tends to be explicit rather than implicit. Academic writers do not assume their readers will fill in gaps or infer connections. If an argument depends on a connection between two ideas, that connection must be stated and explained in the text. If a claim rests on evidence, the evidence must be provided and analyzed, not merely gestured at. If a term has a specialized meaning, it must be defined before it is used. Explicitness is not condescension toward the reader. It is respect for the reader's time and intelligence, and it is also intellectual accountability on the part of the writer.

Writing for readers also means making choices about what to include and how to organize it. Readers read linearly — they encounter information in the order the writer presents it. A writer who buries crucial background information in the middle of a paper, after the argument that depends on it, has failed the reader. A writer who introduces technical vocabulary before defining it has made the reader's work unnecessarily difficult. Good academic writing imagines the reader's experience as the text unfolds and arranges information so that the reader always has what they need when they need it.

Example 1.3 — Explicit vs. Implicit Writing
How explicitness protects reader understanding
  • Implicit "The policy failed. Studies confirmed what critics had been saying all along."This version assumes readers know which policy, which studies, and what critics said. A reader without that background is left with almost nothing.
  • Explicit "The city's 2018 curfew policy, designed to reduce youth crime, failed to produce measurable results. A 2021 study published in the Journal of Criminal Justice found no statistically significant difference in juvenile arrest rates in the three years following the policy's implementation — a finding that confirmed the concerns raised by child welfare advocates during the original policy debate."This version provides the reader with context, evidence, and connection — everything needed to understand and evaluate the claim.
Principle: Give readers what they need, when they need it. Do not rely on shared background that only the writer possesses.

Audience awareness is a fundamental academic writing skill. The concept of audience will receive extensive treatment in Chapter 3, but it is worth introducing here because it shapes every writing decision — from the structure of the whole essay down to individual word choices. Who is going to read this? What do they already know? What do they need to know? What are their expectations for how writing in this field or course looks and sounds? Asking these questions before and during writing will improve almost any draft.

At MGU, your academic readers include professors, teaching assistants, and sometimes classmates in peer review contexts. These are educated readers with specific expectations: that writing gets to the point without unnecessary padding; that claims are supported with evidence; that sentences are grammatically clear; and that sources are properly documented. Meeting these expectations is not about impressing a professor — it is about communicating effectively within a shared system of academic conventions.

Section 1.4

Writing with Evidence

At the center of academic writing is a commitment to evidence. This commitment is what most clearly separates academic writing from personal opinion, casual conversation, and many forms of public discourse.

In everyday life, people make claims based on feelings, impressions, or the authority of a single experience. Someone might say, "I know this approach works because it helped me." Someone else might argue, "I think things are getting worse because everything seems harder than it used to be." These claims may or may not be accurate, but they rest on individual experience rather than systematic evidence. In casual conversation, such claims are often acceptable. In academic writing, they are not sufficient on their own.

Academic writing requires that claims be supported by appropriate evidence, and that evidence must be presented, explained, and connected to the claim. What counts as appropriate evidence varies by discipline.

DisciplineCommon Forms of Evidence
Natural SciencesExperimental data, reproducible results, peer-reviewed studies, controlled observations
HumanitiesClose reading of primary texts, historical documents, scholarly interpretation, archival sources
Social SciencesStatistical data, survey results, qualitative interviews, case studies, empirical studies
Philosophy / MathematicsLogical reasoning, formal proof, conceptual analysis, thought experiments

Despite these disciplinary differences, core principles hold across academic writing. Evidence must be relevant — it must actually address the claim being made, not merely touch the same general topic. Evidence must be reliable — it should come from credible, well-established sources rather than unverified or anonymous ones. And evidence must be interpreted — it cannot simply be dropped into a paragraph and left to speak for itself. The writer must explain what the evidence means, why it matters, and how it supports the specific claim being made.

This last point is one of the most commonly misunderstood aspects of academic writing. Many students believe that finding good evidence is the hard part, and that once found, the job is mostly done. In fact, the analysis and explanation of evidence is often the most intellectually demanding part of writing. Raw data, a quotation from a source, or a statistical finding does not automatically prove anything. It becomes evidence only when a writer connects it to a specific argument and explains its significance.

Example 1.4 — Citing vs. Analyzing Evidence
The difference between dropping evidence and using it
  • Weak "Unemployment rates rose significantly during the period in question. According to the Bureau of Labor Statistics, the unemployment rate reached 9.8 percent in 2010. This shows that the economy was struggling."The evidence is cited, but the analysis stops at restating the obvious. The connection between the number and its significance is left entirely to the reader.
  • Stronger "Unemployment data from the period reveals the scale of the economic disruption. The unemployment rate peaked at 9.8 percent in 2010 — the highest level recorded in decades, according to the Bureau of Labor Statistics. This figure does not simply represent individuals without paychecks; it reflects a systemic contraction in hiring across multiple sectors that simultaneously reduced consumer spending, diminished tax revenue, and eroded confidence in financial institutions. The number itself becomes meaningful only when read against what it cost in terms of household stability and public trust."Here the evidence is cited, analyzed, and connected to a broader line of reasoning. The writer explains what the figure means and why that meaning matters to the argument.
Principle: Evidence requires analysis. Cite it, explain it, connect it — in that order.
Core Principle

Academic writing is always accountable to evidence, and evidence must always be analyzed — not merely cited. Finding evidence is not the end of the job. Explaining what it means and how it supports your argument is where the real intellectual work happens.

Section 1.5

Writing in a Formal Learning Community

Academic writing does not exist in isolation. Every piece of writing you produce at MGU — every essay, research paper, lab report, or analytical response — is produced within a learning community that has shared values, expectations, and conventions.

Understanding this context helps explain features of academic writing that might otherwise seem arbitrary. Why must you cite sources? Because the learning community is built on shared access to ideas, and citing sources lets readers trace where ideas came from and evaluate them in context. Why does academic writing require a degree of formality? Because formal conventions signal that the writer is participating in a shared intellectual framework, not simply expressing private feeling. Why does structure matter? Because organized writing allows complex ideas to be communicated reliably across differences in background knowledge and perspective.

The concept of academic writing as participation in a conversation is one of the most useful frameworks you can adopt. Scholars, researchers, and thinkers produce work that builds on, responds to, questions, and extends other work. When you write a paper, even as a student, you are entering that conversation. You are reading what others have argued, building on or questioning their ideas, and contributing your own analysis. Your writing does not need to be original in the sense of having never been said before, but it must show that you understand the conversation you are entering and that you are contributing to it meaningfully.

Academic Transfer — The Conversation Metaphor

Think of your academic field as an ongoing conversation that has been happening for decades or centuries. When you walk into a room where a conversation is already in progress, you do not immediately start talking. You listen first. You learn who has said what, and what the important disagreements are. Then, when you have something to contribute, you speak — and you speak in a way that acknowledges what has already been said. Academic writing works the same way. You read the existing conversation (your sources) before contributing your own voice (your paper).

This conversational model also explains why the use of other people's ideas without acknowledgment — plagiarism — is such a serious violation. Plagiarism is not simply an infraction of a bureaucratic rule. It is a form of dishonesty that corrupts the academic conversation. It misrepresents who contributed what, undermines the trust that makes intellectual communities function, and cheats both the original author and the reading audience. In an academic community built on the honest exchange of ideas, misrepresenting the source of those ideas is a fundamental breach. Academic integrity will be covered in depth in Chapter 37.

The formal dimension of academic writing — its conventions about structure, citation, tone, and evidence — should be understood as the grammar of a larger communication system. Just as grammatical conventions in sentences allow readers to parse meaning reliably, academic writing conventions allow readers across courses, institutions, and disciplines to engage with each other's ideas reliably. Learning these conventions is not simply learning to please professors. It is gaining access to a larger intellectual conversation.

Section 1.6

Academic Writing Across Subjects

One of the more surprising things students discover as they progress through a university is that academic writing looks somewhat different in different disciplines. A history paper does not look exactly like a chemistry lab report. A literary analysis does not look exactly like a business case study or a psychology research paper. These differences are real and significant.

Different disciplines value different kinds of evidence, use different citation systems, follow different structural conventions, and employ different characteristic vocabularies. A biologist writing a methods section is expected to use the passive voice in ways that a literary scholar is not. A historian citing primary documents follows different conventions from a sociologist citing interview data. A mathematician's proof has a structure unlike anything in an economics paper. Learning to write in a discipline means learning to write the way that discipline thinks — and this kind of deep disciplinary writing develops gradually over years of study in a field.

DisciplineCharacteristic StructurePrimary Citation StyleTypical Evidence
Literature / HumanitiesThesis-driven analysis; close textual readingMLAQuotations from primary texts; scholarly interpretation
HistoryNarrative and argumentative; footnote-heavyChicagoPrimary documents; archival sources; secondary scholarship
Psychology / Social SciencesIMRaD (Introduction, Methods, Results, Discussion)APAEmpirical studies; statistical data; qualitative research
Natural SciencesLab report format; hypothesis-drivenAPA or field-specificExperimental data; peer-reviewed studies
BusinessExecutive summary; recommendation-drivenAPA or field-specificMarket data; case studies; financial analysis

At the same time, the fundamental characteristics of academic writing — purpose, organization, evidence, and clarity — remain consistent across all of these disciplines. What changes is how those principles are expressed in each field's particular conventions.

This textbook is designed primarily for students in the early stages of academic writing, before deep specialization in any one discipline. Its goal is to teach the transferable core of academic writing: how to construct a clear thesis, how to develop and support ideas, how to use evidence honestly and effectively, how to revise for clarity and depth, and how to document sources properly. These skills will apply wherever you write academically, even when the specific conventions of a particular discipline require adaptation. Chapter 48, the final chapter of this textbook, returns to this theme of writing across the curriculum and shows in concrete terms how the skills you develop here transfer across your entire MGU program of study.

Section 1.7

Common Myths About Good Writing

Before discussing how this textbook builds writing skill, it is worth clearing away several widespread myths about writing that can seriously mislead students. These myths are not harmless. They lead writers to make poor choices, feel unnecessary discouragement, and spend effort on the wrong things. Each myth below is followed by the reality that should replace it.

Myth 1
Good writers get it right on the first draft.

This is perhaps the most damaging myth in writing education. Many students believe that if they have to revise, they failed. In reality, revision is how good writing is made. Professional writers, scholars, and journalists revise extensively — often through multiple complete drafts. A first draft is not a failure; it is a necessary beginning. The writer's job is not to produce perfect prose on the first pass but to get ideas onto the page where they can be examined, shaped, and improved. This textbook will return to revision throughout, and Part VIII is dedicated entirely to revision as a craft.

Myth 2
Big words make writing better.

Academic writing requires precision and clarity, not complexity for its own sake. Using a long or technical word when a simpler word communicates more clearly is not sophisticated writing — it is obstruction. A writer who uses "utilize" when "use" is clearer, or "in the event that" when "if" works perfectly well, has not made their writing more academic. They have made it harder to read. The best academic writers choose words with precision, not those who choose the most impressive-sounding words available. Vocabulary should serve communication, not decorate it.

Myth 3
Longer is better.

Academic assignments have minimum length requirements for a reason: a two-page response cannot develop an argument as thoroughly as a ten-page paper. But within any given length, more words do not automatically produce a better paper. A student who pads sentences, repeats points unnecessarily, and fills paragraphs with vague generalities in order to reach a word count is not writing a better paper — they are writing a longer one. Length should follow from the depth of the thinking, not from the desire to appear thorough. Professors who read student writing regularly are very good at identifying padding, and it does not help a grade.

Myth 4
Academic writing must sound impersonal and distant.

Students sometimes believe they should purge all sense of a thinking human being from their writing. This leads to sentences that are awkward, passive, and unclear. While academic writing does require a degree of formality and objectivity, it does not require the writer to disappear. A first-person sentence — "I argue that..." or "In this essay, I will examine..." — is appropriate in many academic contexts and is often clearer than convoluted passive constructions designed to avoid saying "I." Academic tone and voice will be addressed fully in Chapter 47.

Myth 5
Grammar is the most important part of writing.

Grammar matters. Errors distract readers, undermine credibility, and sometimes cause genuine confusion. But grammar is not the foundation of good writing — it is the final layer. A paper with perfect grammar but no clear thesis, no developed argument, and no evidence is a far weaker piece of writing than a paper with a minor grammatical error or two but a compelling, well-supported central argument. This textbook teaches grammar and correctness carefully, but it follows a deliberate philosophy: address meaning first, structure second, paragraphs third, sentences fourth, and grammar last. Fixing commas in a paper whose central idea is not yet clear is spending effort in exactly the wrong place.

Myth 6
Good writers are born, not made.

Writing is a skill, and like all skills, it develops through practice, instruction, and feedback. Some students arrive at college with stronger writing backgrounds than others, and those differences are real. But writing ability is not a fixed trait you either have or do not have. Students who struggle with writing at the beginning of a course regularly become significantly stronger writers by the end of it — not because they discovered hidden talent, but because they practiced, revised, received feedback, and applied it. This textbook is built on the conviction that every student who engages seriously with the material and does the work will become a better writer.

Section 1.8

How This Textbook Builds Writing Skill

Understanding the structure of this textbook will help you use it more effectively. The book is organized to move from smaller writing units to larger ones, gradually building the skills you need to produce complete academic writing projects. The overarching movement of the book is this:

The Progression

Sentences → Paragraphs → Essays → Argument → Research → Citation → Revision → Academic Style

Each part builds on the skills developed in the part before it. A student who controls sentences writes better paragraphs. A student who understands paragraphs writes better essays. A student who understands essays and argument produces better research. And a student who understands revision can improve all of the above.

PartChaptersFocus
Part I1–5What academic writing is; writing as a process; audience and purpose; reading like a writer; habits and confidence
Part II6–12Sentences, grammar, parts of speech, sentence structure, clarity, variety, common errors, punctuation

Throughout the textbook, each chapter includes model examples, before-and-after revision demonstrations, practice activities, common mistake warnings, and checklists. These features are not decorations; they are tools. Use them actively.

Writing skill develops through doing, not through reading about writing passively. Every practice activity in this book asks you to write, revise, or analyze actual writing — because that is the only way writing ability actually grows. A student who reads every chapter carefully but never completes a practice activity will improve far less than a student who reads thoughtfully and writes regularly. The reading teaches you what to do; the writing teaches you how to do it.

It is also worth noting that the skills taught in this textbook are not confined to an English class. They are tools you will use in every academic course you take at MGU and in nearly every professional context beyond the university. History papers, psychology research reports, business proposals, policy memos, grant applications — all of these are forms of academic or professional writing that draw on exactly the skills this textbook develops. Treating this course as preparation for English class alone would be a serious underestimate of what you are actually learning.

How to Use This Textbook

Read each chapter at least once before completing the practice activities. As you read, mark passages that raise questions or that connect to writing challenges you have personally encountered.

When you do the practice activities, treat them as real writing tasks, not exercises to complete mechanically. The goal of each activity is to develop a habit, not to produce a correct answer. Writing requires the same kind of deliberate practice that learning a musical instrument or a sport does — and it responds to that practice in exactly the same way.

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 1.1Academic writing is purposeful, organized, evidence-based communication produced within a learning or scholarly community. It is defined not by complexity or jargon but by clarity, accountability, and intellectual honesty.

Key Terms

academic writing
Purposeful, organized, evidence-based written communication produced within a learning or scholarly community.
audience
The intended reader or readers for a piece of writing, whose expectations, knowledge, and needs shape the writer's choices at every level.
claim
A statement the writer asserts to be true and is prepared to support with reasons and evidence.
discipline
A field of study with its own methods, conventions, vocabulary, and standards for evidence — such as history, biology, economics, or literature.
evidence
Information, data, examples, expert opinion, or logical reasoning that supports a claim in academic writing.
evidence analysis
The writer's explanation of what evidence means, why it matters, and how it connects to the specific argument being made.
explicitness
The quality of stating connections, context, and reasoning directly in the text rather than assuming readers will infer them from background knowledge.
learning community
The network of scholars, students, teachers, and institutions that share conventions and expectations for intellectual communication.
plagiarism
The presentation of another person's words or ideas as one's own without proper acknowledgment — a form of intellectual dishonesty that violates academic community trust.
purpose
The specific goal a piece of writing is meant to accomplish — to explain, analyze, argue, reflect, report, compare, and so on.
revision
The process of rethinking, reshaping, and improving a draft — a fundamentally different activity from simple error correction.
thesis
The central claim or main argument of an academic essay or paper, which the rest of the piece develops and supports.

Review Questions

  1. In your own words, define academic writing. What are its three or four most important characteristics? Why does each characteristic matter?
  2. What does it mean to say that writing is a form of thinking, not just the recording of completed thought? Can you think of a personal experience in which writing helped you discover or change an idea?
  3. Why does academic writing tend to be explicit rather than implicit? What happens to a reader when important context or connections are left unstated?
  4. What is the difference between citing evidence and analyzing evidence? Why does that difference matter for the quality of an academic argument?
  5. What does it mean for academic writing to exist within a "learning community"? How does that community context shape the expectations and conventions of academic writing?
  6. Academic writing looks different in different disciplines. Why does this happen? What remains consistent across disciplines despite those differences?
  7. Select one of the six common writing myths from Section 1.7. Explain specifically how a student who believed that myth might make different — and worse — writing decisions than a student who did not believe it.
  8. Why does this textbook prioritize meaning before grammar and correctness in the revision process? Do you agree with this priority? What is the reasoning behind it?

Discussion Prompts

Discussion 1
Your Writing History

Think about your most memorable experience with writing — either positive or negative. What was the situation? What made it memorable? What did it teach you about writing, or about yourself as a writer? Share this with a partner or in a small group and discuss: what assumptions about "good writing" did your experience reveal? Which of those assumptions hold up in light of this chapter, and which do not?

Discussion 2
Comparing Writing Types

Find an example of academic writing (a paragraph from a textbook, an article from an academic journal, or an academic essay) and an example of informal writing on the same topic (a blog post, a social media thread, or a popular magazine article). Compare the two. How do they differ in tone, evidence, organization, and purpose? Which do you find easier to read? Which do you find more reliable? What explains the differences?

Discussion 3
The Role of Evidence in Everyday Life

In everyday life, do people typically require evidence before accepting claims? What forms of evidence do people find persuasive in everyday contexts — and how do those forms compare to the kinds of evidence academic writing requires? What are the implications of those differences for how academic communities and general public discourse differ in their reliability?

Discussion 4
Writing Myths You Have Encountered

As a class, brainstorm additional myths about writing beyond the ones discussed in Section 1.7. Where did those myths come from — school, family, popular culture, prior teachers? What is the cost of each myth? How would believing or disbelieving each myth change a student's concrete approach to a writing assignment?

Practice Activities

Activity 1.1Identifying Academic Writing Characteristics
  1. Find a short passage of academic writing — from a textbook, a journal article, or an academic essay. Annotate it by identifying at least three of the following: a clear claim, a use of evidence, an organizational signal, a transition between ideas, a moment of analysis or explanation, or a citation. Write two or three sentences explaining how each feature you identified contributes to the effectiveness of the writing as a whole.
Activity 1.2Comparing Writing Contexts
  1. Write two short paragraphs on the same subject — any subject you know well. Write the first paragraph as if you were explaining the subject to a close friend in a casual text message or social media post. Write the second paragraph as if you were writing an academic explanation for an educated reader who is unfamiliar with the topic.
  2. Then write a third short paragraph reflecting on what changed between the two versions and why. Which choices did you make differently, and what drove those choices?
Activity 1.3Evidence and Analysis
  1. Consider the following claim: Urban green spaces improve mental health outcomes for city residents. First, identify what kinds of evidence you would need to support this claim in an academic paper. What data would be useful? What sources might you consult? What kinds of studies would be relevant?
  2. Second, write a short paragraph of five to eight sentences that incorporates at least one piece of specific evidence — real or hypothetical — and analyzes it rather than simply citing it. Practice the technique: cite the evidence, explain what it means, and connect it explicitly to the claim.
Activity 1.4Myth Examination
  1. Select one of the writing myths discussed in Section 1.7. Write a response of one to two paragraphs that does two things: first, explain specifically how that myth could damage a student's writing in concrete terms; and second, describe the writing practice or belief that should replace it, and explain why the replacement is more accurate and more useful.
Activity 1.5Writing for an Academic Audience
  1. Choose a topic you know something about from a previous course or from personal experience. Write one paragraph of approximately 150 to 200 words on that topic, writing as if for an academic reader who has general education but no specific expertise in your subject.
  2. After writing the paragraph, review it against the four core characteristics of academic writing: Is it purposeful? Organized? Evidence-based? Written with a reader in mind? Write two or three sentences of honest self-assessment identifying where your paragraph succeeds and where it could be strengthened. This is your first revision practice of the course.

Academic Transfer Activity

Academic Transfer — Writing Across Your Courses

Academic writing skills are not confined to English class. Choose one of your other current courses — history, science, mathematics, psychology, philosophy, economics, or another subject — and identify an assignment or written task from that course.

Answer the following questions in writing: (1) What kind of writing does that assignment require — primarily explanatory, analytical, argumentative, or something else? (2) What kind of evidence does that assignment require — data, textual evidence, historical sources, mathematical reasoning, or experimental results? (3) What does your professor in that course expect from a strong piece of writing? (4) How do the principles of purposeful, organized, evidence-based writing apply to that specific assignment?

This activity asks you to begin thinking of writing skills as transferable tools rather than as techniques specific to one class. The goal is not to produce a complete piece of writing but to build the habit of analyzing writing tasks carefully before beginning them — a habit that will serve you in every course you take.

Looking Ahead
Chapter 2: Writing as a Process

Chapter 1 established what academic writing is: purposeful, organized, evidence-based communication within a learning community. Chapter 2 will explain how academic writing happens — as a repeatable process rather than a one-time performance. You will learn why strong writing almost always develops through planning, drafting, feedback, revision, editing, and proofreading; how to understand an assignment before beginning to write; and how to manage writing time so that revision is always possible. The process framework introduced in Chapter 2 will shape your approach to every writing task in every chapter that follows.

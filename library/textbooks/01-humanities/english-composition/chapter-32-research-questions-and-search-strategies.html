Chapter 32 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Every research project begins with a question. That statement sounds simple, but it carries a significant implication: research is not about confirming what you already believe. It is not about finding sources that agree with your opinion and ignoring the rest. Research begins when a writer looks at a topic and asks, honestly and openly, What do I actually want to know about this?

This chapter teaches you how to move from a broad, vague topic to a focused, workable research question. It then teaches you how to search for sources effectively, systematically, and critically. Many students assume that research is just typing words into a search engine and clicking on whatever appears first. That approach can work for casual curiosity, but it fails in academic writing, where the quality of your sources determines the quality of your argument.

By the end of this chapter, you will understand the difference between a topic and a research question, know how to narrow and focus your inquiry, build effective search strategies using keywords and databases, and revise your research question as your investigation develops. These skills are not specific to one course or discipline. They are foundational tools you will use in every academic subject you study.

Opening Question

Imagine you have been asked to write a research paper on the effects of social media. Where do you begin? Social media touches psychology, politics, economics, public health, and culture. The topic is enormous. This chapter is about how you move from that enormous, open territory to a question specific enough to actually investigate — and how you then find the evidence that lets you answer it.

Section 32.1

Moving from Topic to Question

When a professor assigns a research paper, students are often given a topic area rather than a specific question. You might be told to write about climate change, the civil rights movement, public health policy, economic inequality, or artificial intelligence. These are topics. They are not yet research questions.

A topic is a broad subject area. A research question is a specific question about that subject that your research will attempt to answer. The difference between the two is not just a matter of detail. The difference is a matter of intellectual purpose. A topic gives you a general region to explore. A research question gives you a destination.

Consider the topic of social media. Social media is an enormous subject. Billions of people use social media platforms. Researchers in communication, psychology, sociology, business, public health, political science, and education have all studied different aspects of social media. If you simply write a paper "about social media," you will have no way to know what sources to look for, what argument to make, or when you have gathered enough information. The topic alone gives you no direction.

Now consider a narrower formulation: How does social media use affect mental health in adolescents? This is still broad, but it has moved significantly toward a research question. It specifies a population (adolescents), a potential relationship (social media use and mental health), and an implied problem worth investigating. Even narrower would be: Is there a relationship between the amount of time teenagers spend on image-focused social media platforms and rates of depression and anxiety? This question specifies the type of social media, the population, and the outcome of interest, and invites a direct investigative response.

The progression from topic to broad question to focused research question is not always linear. You may circle back several times as you learn more about your subject. But understanding this progression is essential because your research question controls everything that follows. It determines which sources are relevant, what evidence you need to collect, and what claim your paper will ultimately make.

Why This Matters: Reports Versus Research Papers

Students who skip the step of developing a research question often end up writing what composition instructors call a report rather than a research paper. A report simply assembles facts about a topic: social media was invented in the early 2000s, here are the most popular platforms, here are some statistics about usage. A research paper uses evidence to answer a specific question and make a specific claim. Both types of writing have their place, but academic writing in most disciplines requires the focused, question-driven approach of a research paper.

Developing a focused research question also prevents wasted effort. If you search for sources with nothing more specific than "social media" in mind, you will find thousands of articles, many of them irrelevant to anything you actually want to say. A clear research question functions as a filter. It helps you recognize immediately whether a source is useful.

Characteristics of a Strong Research Question

Specific — Narrow enough to be answerable within the scope of your assignment.

Open-ended — Not a yes/no question with an obvious answer; genuinely investigable.

Researchable — Evidence exists that could be found and used to address it.

Significant — The answer matters to someone; it connects to larger ideas or real concerns.

Study Note — The Question Controls the Paper

Your research question is not just a formality. It determines which sources are relevant, what kind of evidence you need, how you organize the paper, and what your thesis will claim. Time spent developing a strong research question is time saved at every later stage of the project.

Section 32.2

Broad, Narrow, and Focused Questions

Understanding the spectrum from too broad to too narrow will help you find the productive middle range where most strong research questions live. Learning to recognize questions that are out of range — and to adjust them — is a skill that takes practice but pays off throughout your academic career.

Too Broad

A question is too broad when it cannot be answered within the scope of your assignment, when it would require reading hundreds of sources to even partially address, or when it is so general that almost any source about the topic would technically be relevant. Consider the question: What are the effects of technology on society? This question is nearly unanswerable as a research paper topic. Technology encompasses everything from smartphones to irrigation systems to pharmaceuticals. Society encompasses the entire range of human organization. Even a team of scholars writing a multivolume encyclopedia would not fully answer this question.

Too-broad questions often emerge when students have not yet spent enough time thinking about what specifically interests them within a topic, or when they are trying to avoid commitment to any particular angle. The problem is that avoiding commitment at the question stage means the paper will lack direction at every later stage.

Too Narrow

A question can also be too narrow. Questions become too narrow when they are so specific that very little research exists on the exact topic, when they reduce to a simple factual lookup rather than genuine inquiry, or when they cannot be connected to broader ideas or conversations that matter to readers. A question asking for a very precise data point from a single study, for instance, may be technically answerable but is probably not significant enough to support a full research paper, and the existing literature is unlikely to address it independently.

The Productive Middle

The best research questions sit in the productive middle ground: specific enough to be focused and answerable, but broad enough to connect to significant ideas and to have a body of research you can actually find and use. Here is an example of how a question can be refined from too broad to too narrow to appropriately focused:

StageExample QuestionAssessment
Too broadWhat is the relationship between diet and health?Encompasses all of nutrition science; no possible scope
Still too broadHow does nutrition affect disease?Millions of diseases; still no focus
Getting closerDoes dietary sugar intake affect the risk of type 2 diabetes?Better, but still quite wide
Focused (workable)What does recent research suggest about added sugar consumption and type 2 diabetes risk in adults under 40?Specific population, substance, disease, and scope
Too narrowHow many grams of added sugar did participants in one 2019 study consume per day?A single data point; not a research question

Finding Your Focus Through Background Reading

One reliable way to find a focused research question is to do some background reading before committing to a question. Background reading means reading broadly and generally about your topic area without immediately trying to find sources for your paper. You might read encyclopedia articles, introductory chapters, magazine overviews, or summary articles that give you a landscape view of what is known and debated in the field.

As you read, pay attention to what surprises you, what seems contested, what gaps in knowledge researchers mention, and what questions keep appearing without clear answers. These are the productive sites where your research question might live. Background reading is not wasted time. It is an investment that makes every later stage of research more efficient.

Tip — Start with an Encyclopedia

Academic encyclopedias and reference databases (such as those available through your library) are excellent starting points for background reading. They provide a summary of current knowledge, major debates, and key terms in a field — exactly what you need to identify a promising research question.

Section 32.3

Keywords and Search Terms

Once you have a focused research question, the next step is translating that question into keywords — the terms you will actually type into databases and search engines to find relevant sources. Keywords are not the same as your research question. You would not type your full research question into a database search box. Instead, you identify the core concepts in the question and search for terms related to each one.

Consider the research question: How has the increased availability of smartphones affected reading habits among middle school students? The core concepts are smartphones, reading habits, and middle school students. These are your primary keywords. From each, you can generate related terms — synonyms, broader terms, and narrower terms that might appear in sources addressing your question.

Example 32.3.1
Building a keyword map
  • QuestionHow has the increased availability of smartphones affected reading habits among middle school students?
  • Core ConceptsSmartphones · Reading habits · Middle school students
  • Smartphones mobile devices, cell phones, digital devices, screen time, handheld technology
  • Reading habits reading comprehension, literacy, recreational reading, print reading, digital reading
  • Middle school students adolescents, tweens, young adolescents, ages 11–14, grades 6–8, secondary school students
Result: A flexible keyword map with multiple search options for each concept.

Building this kind of keyword map before you begin searching gives you flexibility. If your first search with one set of terms returns too few results, you can try synonyms. If your first search returns too many irrelevant results, you can add more specific terms. The map also ensures you are not limited by your own vocabulary — researchers writing about your topic may use different terminology than you do.

Thinking Like a Database

Databases and search engines do not think the way humans do. They cannot infer what you mean; they search for what you type. A database does not know that "cell phones" and "smartphones" are related unless you tell it. It does not know that "tweens" are middle school students unless that connection appears in the text of the articles it indexes. This is why generating multiple related terms is so valuable: researchers in different fields use different terminology, and if you search only for the terms most familiar to you, you may miss important sources entirely.

Phrase Searching

When you want a database to treat multiple words as a single phrase rather than as separate keywords, most databases allow you to put the phrase in quotation marks. Searching for "reading habits" will return results where those two words appear together as a phrase, rather than results where "reading" appears somewhere and "habits" appears somewhere else in the same document. Phrase searching is especially useful for technical terms, proper names, and established concepts that only mean something when the words appear together.

Common Mistake — Searching Only Your Own Words

If you search only with the vocabulary most familiar to you, you will miss sources that use synonymous terms. A health researcher might write "adolescents" while an education researcher writes "middle school students." A paper on "screen time" might be directly relevant to your question about "smartphone use." Build your keyword map broadly before you begin.

Section 32.4

Library Catalogs and Databases

Once you have identified your keywords, you need to know where to search. Different tools are designed to find different types of sources, and using the right tool for the right purpose makes your research significantly more efficient.

The Library Catalog

A library catalog is a searchable index of everything a library owns or has access to, including books, ebooks, journals, DVDs, and other materials. Most library catalogs are now digital and available online. Library catalogs are the right tool when you are looking for books on your topic, textbooks and reference works, government publications and reports, and theses or dissertations. They are generally not the right tool for finding individual journal articles. When you search a catalog for an article topic, you may find that your library subscribes to a journal that publishes articles on that topic, but you will not find the individual articles themselves. For that, you need a database.

Article Databases

An article database is a searchable index of published journal articles, magazine articles, and sometimes news articles. Some databases cover many fields; others specialize in one discipline. Using an article database is the standard way to find scholarly, peer-reviewed research articles on academic topics.

Different databases serve different fields. A student in psychology might use PsycINFO. A student in medicine or health sciences might use PubMed or MEDLINE. A student in history or literature might use JSTOR or MLA International Bibliography. A student working on a broad social sciences topic might use EBSCO's Academic Search Complete or ProQuest Central, both of which cover many disciplines simultaneously. Your library's website almost certainly provides access to multiple databases. Many libraries also provide a discovery layer — a single search interface that searches multiple databases at once and returns combined results. Discovery layers are convenient for early, exploratory searches, but for serious, comprehensive research, going directly into subject-specific databases usually produces better results.

What Peer Review Means

When professors ask you to find peer-reviewed or scholarly sources, they are asking you to find articles that have gone through the peer review process. In peer review, before an article is published in a journal, other experts in the same field read it critically and evaluate whether the research is sound, the methods are appropriate, the conclusions are supported by the evidence, and the contribution to knowledge is genuine. Articles that do not pass this review are rejected or returned for revision.

Peer review is not a guarantee that an article is correct — researchers make mistakes, and science is always subject to revision. But peer review is a quality filter that distinguishes articles that have been vetted by expert readers from articles that have not. Most academic databases allow you to filter search results to show only peer-reviewed articles. Using this filter when your professor requires scholarly sources is a simple but important step.

Tip — Always Check the Filter

Most article databases have a checkbox or dropdown that limits results to peer-reviewed sources only. Look for it before you start reading through hundreds of results. Filtering first saves time and ensures you are working with sources appropriate for academic assignments.

Section 32.5

Search Engines and Academic Search Tools

Search engines like Google are not designed for academic research, but they are not entirely useless for it either. The key is knowing what search engines can and cannot do, and knowing which search tools are designed with academic use in mind.

General Search Engines

A general search engine indexes an enormous range of web content: news articles, product pages, personal blogs, social media posts, government websites, university websites, propaganda, entertainment, and some academic content. The algorithm that decides what to show you first is based partly on popularity and relevance, but it is also influenced by advertising and by how websites are structured. A search engine cannot reliably distinguish a peer-reviewed study from a well-designed website making unsupported claims.

This does not mean you should never use a general search engine in academic research. General search engines are useful for initial background reading and orientation, finding government and organizational websites, locating news coverage of events, and checking a basic fact quickly. They are not reliable for finding peer-reviewed research articles or for conducting systematic literature searches.

Google Scholar

Google Scholar occupies a middle position between a general search engine and a proper academic database. Google Scholar indexes scholarly literature: journal articles, books, theses, conference papers, and technical reports from many fields. It is free, accessible without a library login, and very broad in its coverage. It often shows how many times an article has been cited by other researchers (citation counts are one rough measure of an article's influence), and it frequently provides links to free, full-text versions of articles.

Google Scholar also has real limitations. Its indexing is not always consistent or complete. It does not always distinguish peer-reviewed from non-peer-reviewed work. It lacks the advanced filtering options available in dedicated academic databases. For many undergraduate research projects, Google Scholar is a reasonable starting point, but it should not be your only search tool for serious research. Think of it as a way to find relevant articles quickly, which you then verify and access through your library's more controlled resources.

Discipline-Specific Search Tools

Many academic disciplines have developed specialized search tools that are better than Google Scholar for finding work in that field. These tools often index sources that Google Scholar misses, and they use subject-specific terminology and indexing that makes searches more precise. When you are researching a topic in a specific academic field, identifying whether a specialized database exists for that field is worth the small additional effort. Your librarian can help you identify the most appropriate tool for any given subject area.

Section 32.6

Boolean Operators and Search Filters

Once you understand where to search, the next level of skill is learning how to search with precision. Boolean operators are logical connectors that tell a database how to combine your search terms. The three primary Boolean operators used in academic research are AND, OR, and NOT. Most databases recognize these operators when you type them in capital letters between your search terms.

AND

AND narrows a search by requiring that both terms appear in the results. If you search for social media AND adolescents, the database will return only results that contain both terms. Adding more AND connections makes a search progressively more specific. Searching for social media AND adolescents AND depression will return only results that mention all three concepts. AND is your primary tool for focusing an overly broad set of results.

OR

OR broadens a search by accepting results that contain either term. If you search for teenagers OR adolescents, the database will return results that contain either word. OR is especially useful when you have identified synonyms for a concept. Searching for smartphones OR "mobile devices" OR "cell phones" tells the database to find articles using any of those related terms. This ensures you do not miss relevant sources simply because their authors used different vocabulary than you did.

NOT

NOT excludes a term from results. If you search for depression NOT clinical, you might exclude highly technical clinical studies if you are interested in a more general treatment of the topic. NOT is a useful precision tool but should be used carefully, because you might accidentally exclude relevant sources. Reserve NOT for situations where a specific term is consistently pulling in irrelevant material.

Combining Operators

You can combine Boolean operators in a single search for greater precision. When combining AND and OR, it is important to use parentheses to group the OR terms together, so the database interprets your logic correctly.

Example 32.6.1
A combined Boolean search query
  • GoalFind articles about smartphones or mobile devices AND adolescents or teenagers AND reading.
  • Query(smartphones OR "mobile devices") AND (adolescents OR teenagers) AND reading
  • LogicThe database finds results containing at least one term from the smartphone group AND at least one from the adolescent group AND the word reading.
  • Without ParenthesesWithout grouping OR terms in parentheses, the database may interpret the operators in an unexpected order and return unreliable results.
Use parentheses to group OR synonyms; connect concept groups with AND.

Search Filters

Most academic databases provide search filters that allow you to narrow your results without changing your search terms. Using filters before you start reading through results can save you significant time.

Common filters include: Date range, which limits results to articles published within a specific period (useful for requiring current research or targeting a historical period); Source type, which allows you to limit results to peer-reviewed journals, books, newspapers, or other specific source types; Language, which limits results to articles published in a particular language; and Full text availability, which limits results to articles immediately available in full through your library (a practical filter, though using it may mean missing important articles that require additional steps to access).

Common Mistake — Skipping Filters

Many students set up a search and then scroll through hundreds of results without using any filters. This wastes time and often produces a mix of peer-reviewed, non-peer-reviewed, dated, and irrelevant sources. Set your filters before you start reading. A well-filtered search of 30 results is almost always more useful than an unfiltered list of 3,000.

Section 32.7

Tracking Search Results

Effective research requires keeping records. This is not glamorous advice, but it is among the most practically important things this chapter can teach you. Students who do not track their searches and their sources frequently encounter a cluster of problems: they cannot find an article again when they want to cite it; they cannot remember which database they used; they do not know which search terms they have already tried; they accidentally search the same territory twice; or, most seriously, they lose track of which ideas in their notes came from their own thinking and which came from a source. This last problem, when it leads to uncited source material appearing in a paper, is a form of accidental plagiarism.

A Simple Research Log

A research log is a simple record of your research activity. It does not need to be complicated. A document, a notebook page, or a spreadsheet can serve this purpose. What matters is that you record the following each time you search: the date of the search, the database or tool you used, the search terms you used (including Boolean operators and any filters applied), the number of results returned, and brief notes on what you found or why the search was or was not useful.

Example 32.7.1
A sample research log entry
  • DateCurrent week of research
  • DatabaseAcademic Search Complete (EBSCO)
  • Search Terms(smartphones OR "mobile devices") AND (adolescents OR teenagers) AND "reading habits"
  • FiltersPeer-reviewed only; date range: last 10 years
  • Results47 articles returned
  • NotesMany results focused on reading comprehension; fewer on recreational reading. Found three promising articles. Will try "screen time" instead of "smartphones" to see if different results emerge.
A log entry takes 5 minutes to write and saves hours of repeated or disorganized searching.

Saving and Organizing Sources

As you find potentially useful sources, save them in a way that allows you to find them again and identify them fully later. Most academic databases allow you to create a free account and save articles to a folder within the database. You can also export citations in standard formats (MLA, APA, Chicago) directly from most databases, which saves significant time when you build your bibliography later. Some researchers use citation management software — programs that store source information, generate citations automatically, and help organize sources by topic. Whether you use a formal system or a simple personal folder, the core principle is the same: do not rely on your memory to keep track of sources.

What to Record for Each Source

For every source you save, record enough information to cite it properly and find it again. Record at minimum: the full author name or names, the full title of the article or chapter, the full title of the journal or book, the volume and issue number for journal articles, the date of publication, page numbers, a DOI (Digital Object Identifier) or stable URL if available, the database where you found it, and brief personal notes on why the source seemed useful. Recording this information at the time you find the source is much easier than trying to reconstruct it later, when you may no longer be able to locate the article again.

Tip — Export Citations Immediately

As soon as you identify a source as potentially useful, export its citation information from the database directly into your citation manager or reference document. Doing this at the moment of discovery takes seconds. Trying to reconstruct a citation from memory — or finding the article again to retrieve the DOI — can take much longer and sometimes fails entirely.

Section 32.8

Revising the Research Question

The research process is rarely perfectly linear. When you begin searching and reading, you will almost certainly discover that your original research question needs adjustment. This is not a sign that you did something wrong. It is a sign that you are learning.

When Revision Is Necessary

You should consider revising your research question in several situations. First, if the question is too broad for the available assignment length, your early searches may reveal that a topic is far more extensively researched than you expected — a signal to focus more specifically. Second, if the question is too narrow for the available evidence, searching multiple databases with multiple keyword strategies and finding almost nothing suggests your question may be so specific that researchers have not yet studied it in the way you imagined. Third, as you read background material, you may discover that the question you started with rests on a false assumption, or that a more interesting question has emerged from the material you have read. Following genuine intellectual interest into a revised question is not losing direction; it is how serious inquiry works.

Most importantly, if the evidence points toward a different claim than you expected, your research question — and your eventual thesis — should reflect what the evidence actually shows. If you began your research expecting to find that smartphones unambiguously harm reading habits, but the evidence you find is more mixed or more nuanced, revising your question to remain honest to the evidence is a mark of intellectual integrity, not weakness.

How to Revise Without Starting Over

When you revise your research question, you do not always need to discard all the work you have done. Often, revision means adjusting the scope, adding a specification, changing the population, shifting the time frame, or changing the relationship you are investigating. Sources you have already found may remain relevant to your revised question. Your keyword map will likely need some additions or modifications, but the core terms often remain useful.

It helps to think of your initial research question as a working question rather than a final commitment. A working question is good enough to get started, to guide initial searches, and to begin reading — but it is openly provisional, subject to revision as you learn more. Only after significant reading and searching will you have enough knowledge of the topic to write a strong, refined final research question, from which your thesis will develop.

Connection to Academic Integrity

Revising a research question in response to evidence is not the same as abandoning your argument whenever you find a challenging source. Strong academic writing engages with all the significant evidence, including evidence that complicates a simple claim. Revising your question to reflect what you have actually found is how honest inquiry works — and it produces better papers than forcing evidence to fit a predetermined conclusion.

Imagine you have been researching the question Do violent video games cause aggressive behavior in teenagers? and you discover that the evidence is much more mixed than you expected — some studies find a relationship, others do not, and many researchers debate the methodology. How should you respond? Should you change your question? Your argument? Both? Write a short paragraph explaining your reasoning.
Chapter 32 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 32.1A topic is a broad subject area; a research question is a specific, focused, open-ended question that guides investigation. Strong research questions are specific, open, researchable, and significant. Without a research question, research produces a report rather than an argument.
  • Sec 32.2Research questions can be too broad, too narrow, or appropriately focused. Background reading — early, general reading before committing to a question — helps writers identify what is debated, contested, and worth investigating in a field.
  • Sec 32.3Translating a research question into keywords requires identifying core concepts and generating related terms (synonyms, broader terms, narrower terms) for each. Phrase searching using quotation marks is useful for multi-word technical terms. Databases search for what you type, not what you mean.
  • Sec 32.4Library catalogs find books and reference works; article databases find scholarly journal articles. Peer-reviewed sources have been evaluated by expert reviewers before publication. Most databases allow you to filter results to show only peer-reviewed articles.
  • Sec 32.5General search engines are useful for background reading and orientation but not for systematic academic research. Google Scholar offers broad scholarly coverage but lacks advanced filters. Subject-specific databases provide the best coverage for research in specialized fields.
  • Sec 32.6Boolean operators AND, OR, and NOT give writers control over how search terms are combined. Parentheses group OR synonyms so that AND logic applies correctly between concept groups. Search filters (date range, source type, peer-reviewed only) refine results without changing search terms.
  • Sec 32.7A research log records databases used, search terms, results, and notes for every search session. Saving full bibliographic information for each source at the time of discovery prevents lost citations and accidental plagiarism. Consistent record-keeping makes complex research manageable.
  • Sec 32.8Research questions should be treated as working questions, openly provisional and subject to revision as evidence accumulates. Revising a question in response to evidence is a sign of intellectual honesty, not failure. Sources already found often remain useful after a question is revised.

Key Terms Review

topic
A broad subject area that provides a general domain for investigation but does not specify a question or direction for research.
research question
A specific, focused, open-ended question that a research project investigates and attempts to answer.
background reading
Early, general reading done to gain familiarity with a topic area before committing to a specific research question or beginning systematic source searches.
keyword
A word or phrase that captures a core concept in a research question, used to search databases and catalogs for relevant sources.
related term
A synonym, broader term, or narrower term associated with a keyword, used to expand or refine searches and capture sources that use different vocabulary.
phrase searching
Searching for a specific combination of words as an intact phrase, typically by placing the phrase in quotation marks in a database search.
library catalog
A searchable index of materials owned or accessible by a library, typically best suited for finding books, reference works, and government publications.
article database
A searchable index of published journal articles, used to find scholarly and research literature on a topic.
peer review
A quality evaluation process in which experts in a field review a manuscript before publication, assessing the soundness of the research and the validity of its conclusions.
Boolean operators
Logical connectors (AND, OR, NOT) used in database searches to combine or exclude search terms and control the scope and precision of results.
search filter
A database option that allows the user to limit search results by criteria such as date range, source type, language, or peer-review status.
research log
A personal record of search activity, including databases searched, terms used, results found, and notes on the usefulness of each search session.
citation management software
A digital tool designed to store source information, organize references, and generate properly formatted citations automatically.
working question
A preliminary research question used to guide initial investigation, openly provisional and subject to revision as research and reading develop.

Practice Activities

Activity 1Topic to Question Progression

Choose one of the following broad topics: renewable energy, immigration policy, mental health in college students, artificial intelligence in education, or the history of public libraries.

  1. Write the topic as given above.
  2. Write two questions about the topic — one that you consider too broad and one that you consider too narrow. Explain briefly why each falls outside the productive range.
  3. Write a focused research question that falls in the productive middle range.
  4. In a short paragraph (four to six sentences), explain what changes you made to move from too broad and too narrow to appropriately focused. What specifications did you add? What did you cut?
Activity 2Keyword Mapping

Take the focused research question you developed in Activity 1, or use this provided question: How does air pollution from vehicle traffic affect respiratory health in urban children?

  1. Identify the core concepts in the research question. List each concept as a heading.
  2. Under each heading, list at least three related terms (synonyms, broader terms, or narrower terms).
  3. Write two different database search queries using Boolean operators: one using AND to connect all three core concepts, and one using AND combined with OR terms in parentheses to incorporate your related terms.
  4. In one or two sentences, explain what each query is designed to find and how the two queries differ in scope.
Activity 3Evaluating Search Results

Conduct a real search using your research question and keywords. Use your library's database access, Google Scholar, or another academic search tool your instructor approves.

  1. Record in your research log: the database used, the search terms (with Boolean operators), and the number of results returned.
  2. Apply at least one search filter (such as date range or peer-reviewed only) and note how the results change. Record this in your log.
  3. Identify three results that appear potentially useful. For each, record the author, title, publication, and date.
  4. Write a brief note (two to four sentences) for each source explaining why it might be relevant to your research question and whether it appears to be peer-reviewed.
Activity 4Reflecting on Source Quality

Locate two sources on the same topic — one from a general web search and one from an academic database. The sources should address the same subject but may differ in type and quality.

  1. Write out the full bibliographic information for each source (author, title, publication, date, URL or DOI if applicable).
  2. Answer the following questions for each source: Who wrote or published it? What evidence does it provide? Who is the intended audience? Does it appear to be peer-reviewed, professionally reviewed, or neither?
  3. Write a short comparison (one paragraph) explaining which source you consider more appropriate for an academic research paper and why. Be specific about what differences between the two sources inform your judgment.
Activity 5Research Log Practice

For a topic assigned by your instructor or chosen from your own coursework, keep a research log for three separate search sessions conducted across different days. Each log entry should include: date and time of search, database or tool used, search terms and operators, whether any filters were applied, number of results, and brief notes on the usefulness of the search.

  1. Complete three research log entries following the format described above.
  2. After your three sessions, write a short reflection (one to two paragraphs) describing how your search strategy changed across sessions. What did you learn from your first session that improved your second? What adjustments did you make?
  3. Based on your three sessions, write a revised version of your working research question if the evidence or search results suggest revision is needed. If no revision is needed, explain why your original question held up well.

Looking Ahead
Chapter 33: Finding and Evaluating Sources

Chapter 32 gave you the tools to develop a focused research question and search for sources systematically. Chapter 33 will teach you how to judge the quality of the sources you find. Not every source that appears in a database search is appropriate for academic use, and not every source that appears credible at first glance will hold up to careful evaluation. Chapter 33 covers credibility, relevance, accuracy, authority, bias, currency, and the difference between popular, professional, and scholarly sources — the criteria that let you move from a list of search results to a set of genuinely useful, defensible evidence for your paper.

Chapter 22 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Every essay has a beginning and an end. This sounds obvious, but what those two positions actually require is something most writers underestimate. The introduction and the conclusion are not decoration. They are not formalities to be dispatched quickly so that the "real" writing can begin or end. They are functional parts of the essay, each with specific work to do, and when either one fails, the entire essay suffers.

This chapter explains what introductions and conclusions are supposed to accomplish, how to build them effectively, and what mistakes to avoid. It also confronts some of the most common habits that weaken these two critical sections of academic writing: the empty hook, the vague opening that delays the essay's real subject, the mechanical conclusion that merely lists what the essay already said, and the final paragraph that trails off without giving the reader any sense of completion or meaning.

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to write introductions that genuinely prepare readers for what is coming and conclusions that genuinely complete the essay's work. You will also understand why these skills matter not just for English class but for every academic course that asks you to write.

Chapter Question

Think about the last essay you submitted. Did your introduction actually prepare your reader for what followed — or did it delay the real subject? Did your conclusion genuinely complete the argument — or did it merely list what you had already said? Most writers, even experienced ones, struggle with these two endpoints. This chapter explains why they are difficult and how to get them right.

Section 22.1

What Introductions Must Do

When a reader picks up an essay, the first paragraph makes several silent promises. It suggests what the essay is about. It establishes the writer's tone and level of seriousness. It signals how the writer thinks about the subject. And, in academic writing, it leads to a thesis statement that tells the reader what the essay will argue, analyze, or explain.

An introduction that fulfills all of these responsibilities is doing real work. An introduction that merely takes up space at the beginning of the page is failing the reader and weakening the essay before it has really begun.

It helps to think about the introduction from the reader's perspective. When you begin reading a new essay, you bring questions with you. You want to know: What is this about? Why does it matter? What is the writer's position or focus? What should I be paying attention to as I read? A strong introduction answers all of these questions before the body of the essay begins. A weak introduction leaves the reader uncertain, confused, or worse, uninterested.

Academic readers — professors, instructors, writing center tutors, and educated peers — are experienced readers. They have read many essays, good and bad. They notice almost immediately whether a writer is in control of the material or struggling to find a way in. An introduction that delays the topic, makes empty gestures toward importance, or arrives at a vague thesis tells an experienced reader something unflattering about the draft. On the other hand, an introduction that establishes context efficiently and leads to a clear, focused thesis creates confidence. It tells the reader: this writer knows what the essay is doing.

The Three Core Functions of an Introduction

An effective academic introduction performs three distinct functions, and it performs them in roughly this order.

Three Core Functions

First: Establish context. The reader needs to know what territory the essay is entering. This means providing some background, framing the topic, or identifying the question or problem the essay will address. The context should be relevant and proportionate — enough to orient the reader, not so much that the introduction becomes its own essay.

Second: Narrow toward the specific focus. A well-constructed introduction does not stay in broad territory. It moves from general context toward the specific subject of the essay. This movement gives the reader a sense of direction. Each sentence or group of sentences brings the reader closer to the thesis.

Third: State the thesis. The thesis is the central claim, argument, or controlling idea of the essay. In most academic essays, the thesis appears at or near the end of the introduction. It is the destination that the introduction has been preparing the reader to reach.

These three functions work together. Context without narrowing leaves the reader floating. Narrowing without context feels abrupt. A thesis without context feels unexplained and unsupported before it has even been developed. When all three work together, the introduction feels logical, purposeful, and readable.

Study Note

Think of the introduction as a funnel. The wide opening represents the broader context. As the funnel narrows, the introduction moves toward the specific topic and problem. The narrow end — the point at which everything exits — is the thesis. The reader should feel pulled through this funnel, not dropped into it.

Section 22.2

Context and Background

The opening sentences of an introduction are among the most important sentences in the entire essay. They set the tone, establish the subject, and either earn or lose the reader's attention. Many students waste this opportunity by writing sentences that are too broad, too vague, or too disconnected from the actual essay.

Context means giving the reader enough background to understand why the topic matters and what problem or question the essay will address. The right amount of context depends on the assignment, the topic, and the audience. A short in-class essay may need only a sentence or two of background. A longer research essay may need several sentences or even a full paragraph of context before the narrowing begins.

The key principle is that context should be relevant. Every sentence in the introduction should be pulling the reader toward the thesis. If a sentence does not contribute to that movement, it probably does not belong in the introduction.

What Good Context Looks Like

Good context grounds the reader in the topic quickly and meaningfully. Consider an essay analyzing the causes of the Great Fire of London in 1666. A useful contextual opening might identify the historical period, the city's conditions, and the significance of the fire as an event worth examining. This gives the reader orientation without telling the entire story before the essay has begun.

StrategyDescriptionUseful For
Name the problem or questionState directly what issue the essay will addressAnalytical and research essays
Identify a tension or contradictionPresent two things that conflict or a belief that evidence complicatesArgumentative and analytical essays
Provide relevant backgroundGive the reader the historical, scientific, or cultural context needed to understand the topicResearch essays, historical analysis
Identify the subject of analysisName the text, event, policy, or phenomenon the essay will examineLiterary analysis, policy essays
Describe a situationSketch a recognizable scenario that raises the essay's central questionSocial science, education, policy writing

All of these strategies have something in common: they help the reader understand what the essay is for. Context is not decoration. It is preparation.

What Weak Context Looks Like

Many student introductions begin with context that is too broad, too vague, or too disconnected from the actual argument. The most common version of this error is the "since the beginning of time" opening, where the writer begins at the broadest possible level before trying to work down toward the topic.

Weak Context

Throughout human history, people have faced many challenges. Society has always struggled with difficult problems. One of the most important issues today is climate change.

Effective Context

Over the past three decades, the scientific consensus on human-caused climate change has strengthened considerably. Yet public debate about climate policy remains divided, often along political and economic lines. This essay examines why the gap between scientific evidence and public policy has persisted, and what role misinformation has played in widening that gap.

The weak version fails on several levels. The first two sentences say almost nothing — every essay could open that way. The third sentence finally names the topic but arrives without useful preparation. The effective version uses its sentences efficiently: the first sentence establishes existing consensus, the second identifies the tension, and the third narrows to the specific focus. The reader knows where the essay is going before the thesis has even been fully stated.

Common Mistake — Broad Opening Sentences

Resist the urge to open with statements like "Since the beginning of time," "Throughout human history," or "In today's modern world." These phrases say nothing specific and signal to experienced readers that the writer is stalling. Begin with something that belongs specifically to your topic.

Section 22.3

Introducing the Topic and Problem

Many academic essays are not just about a topic — they are about a problem, question, debate, or gap in understanding related to that topic. The difference matters. A topic is a subject area. A problem or question is what makes the essay worth writing.

Think about the distinction this way. "Urban transportation" is a topic. "Why public transit ridership has declined in mid-sized American cities despite increased investment" is a problem. An essay about the topic might wander through a variety of observations. An essay about the problem has direction, purpose, and something to argue or explain.

Introducing the problem — not just the topic — is one of the most important things an introduction can do. When a reader understands the problem the essay is addressing, they understand why the essay exists. They read with a purpose: they want to see how the writer addresses the problem.

Four Strategies for Framing the Problem

Strategy 1
Name the question directly
  • What It IsState the research or analytical question explicitly. This approach is direct and works well for analytical and research essays.
  • ExampleThis essay asks whether the economic benefits of urban tourism justify the social costs experienced by long-term residents of tourist-heavy neighborhoods.
Strategy 2
Identify a tension or contradiction
  • What It IsPresent two things that conflict, or a widely held belief that evidence complicates. Introducing this tension early gives the reader something to think about.
  • ExampleAlthough access to digital information has increased dramatically in recent decades, studies suggest that many students struggle more, not less, with evaluating source credibility.
Strategy 3
Describe a situation that needs explanation
  • What It IsSketch a situation the reader can recognize but that raises a genuine question. This is especially effective in social science and policy writing.
  • EffectThe reader finishes the opening thinking: why is this happening? The essay promises to answer that question.
Strategy 4
Identify the conversation and the gap
  • What It IsIn research essays, identify what has already been written about the topic, then identify what is missing or contested. Common in academic journal articles and longer college research essays.
  • EffectThe reader understands both where the essay fits in the larger scholarly conversation and why it needs to exist.
The Reader's Question

Whatever strategy you use, the goal is the same: give the reader a reason to keep reading. When you finish your introduction, ask yourself honestly: Does a reader who finishes this paragraph want to see what comes next? If the answer is no, the problem has not been introduced effectively.

Section 22.4

Leading to the Thesis

The thesis statement is the most important sentence in most academic essays. It states the main claim, argument, or controlling idea. It tells the reader what the essay will demonstrate, analyze, or argue. And in most academic introductions, it appears at or near the end of the introductory paragraph or section.

The introduction's job is to lead the reader to the thesis. Every sentence in the introduction should be building toward the moment when the thesis arrives. When this works well, the thesis feels like the natural destination of the introduction — almost inevitable. The reader finishes the introduction thinking: yes, that is exactly the question this essay should be asking, or yes, I can see why that is the argument.

When the introduction does not do this work, the thesis arrives like an interruption. The reader has been moving in one direction, and the thesis pulls them somewhere else. This disconnect is a sign that the introduction needs revision.

Thesis Placement

In most college-level academic essays, the thesis appears at or near the end of the introduction. This placement is conventional for good reasons. It allows the writer to establish context before stating the claim. It allows the reader to understand the territory before being told where the essay is going to stand within that territory.

However, the thesis does not always need to be the absolute last sentence of the introduction. In some essays, the thesis is followed by a brief sentence that indicates the structure or main points of the essay — sometimes called a "roadmap" sentence. A roadmap sentence can be useful in longer essays where the structure is complex. In shorter essays, it is often unnecessary and can feel mechanical.

What matters most is not whether the thesis is the last sentence or the second-to-last sentence. What matters is that the thesis is positioned at the end of the introduction's movement — after context has been established and the topic has been narrowed — and that it says something clear and specific.

Weak Thesis in Introduction

This essay is about social media and mental health in teenagers.

Stronger Thesis in Introduction

While social media platforms offer genuine opportunities for connection among teenagers, research suggests that heavy daily use correlates with increased rates of anxiety and depression, particularly among adolescent girls.

The weak version merely announces a topic. The stronger version makes a specific, arguable claim. A reader who finishes the stronger introduction knows exactly what the essay will argue and is ready to evaluate the evidence that follows.

Common Mistake — Announcing Instead of Arguing

Thesis statements that begin with "This essay will discuss..." or "In this paper I will examine..." are mechanical and flat. They tell the reader what the writer will do, but they do not make a claim. Replace announcements with arguments. Instead of "This essay will discuss the effects of social media," try "Social media use among teenagers is reshaping how young people experience both connection and loneliness."

Section 22.5

What Conclusions Must Do

If the introduction's job is to prepare the reader for what is coming, the conclusion's job is to complete what the essay has done. The conclusion is not a summary. It is not a list of what the essay already said. It is not a place to introduce entirely new arguments. It is the place where the essay arrives.

Think of an essay as a journey. The introduction tells the reader where the journey is going. The body paragraphs carry the reader through the terrain. The conclusion tells the reader what the journey meant. This is why conclusions that simply repeat the thesis and list the main points feel so unsatisfying — they tell the reader where they started, not where they arrived.

A strong conclusion does several things. It returns to the central argument and shows how the essay has developed or enriched that argument. It synthesizes — meaning it brings together the threads of the essay and shows how they connect. It suggests the implications or significance of the argument. And it gives the reader a sense of completion: the feeling that the essay has done what it set out to do.

The Difference Between Summary and Synthesis

The distinction between summary and synthesis is one of the most important concepts in this chapter. Understanding this distinction will change how you write every conclusion going forward.

Example 22.5.1
Summary conclusion vs. synthesis conclusion
  • TopicAn essay arguing that urban green spaces benefit city residents in multiple ways.
  • SummaryIn this essay, I discussed three reasons why urban green spaces are important. First, they improve air quality. Second, they provide recreational opportunities. Third, they reduce urban heat. These three points show that green spaces are valuable.
  • Why It FailsThis conclusion tells the reader nothing they do not already know. It does not add meaning or perspective. It does not show the writer thinking about the larger significance of the argument.
  • SynthesisTaken together, the environmental, social, and thermal benefits of urban green spaces point toward a larger truth about city planning: that prioritizing human well-being and ecological health does not conflict with urban development — it makes development more sustainable. Cities that invest in green infrastructure are not trading economic growth for sentiment; they are making a rational long-term investment in livable, resilient urban environments.
  • Why It WorksThe synthesis conclusion does not merely list the three points. It connects them and draws a larger conclusion from their relationship. It gives the reader a reason to care about the argument.
The Key Question

Before writing your conclusion, ask yourself: What do all of these arguments, examples, and pieces of evidence add up to? What should the reader understand now that they could not have understood at the beginning? Your answer to these questions is the foundation of your synthesis.

Section 22.6

Synthesis Instead of Repetition

Synthesis is the intellectual heart of a strong conclusion. To synthesize means to combine separate things into a unified whole. In an academic essay, synthesis means showing how the essay's individual arguments, pieces of evidence, and analytical moves add up to something larger and more meaningful than any single part.

This is not as difficult as it might sound, but it does require a shift in thinking. When you have finished writing the body of an essay, you have assembled a collection of ideas, evidence, and arguments. The conclusion's job is to step back from that collection and ask: What does all of this mean? What does it add up to? What should the reader walk away understanding?

Four Strategies for Achieving Synthesis

StrategyHow It WorksWhat It Adds
Return to the thesis with greater depthRestate the thesis, but now with the full weight of the body's evidence behind it. This is not repetition — it is the thesis enriched.Shows that the essay has proven what it set out to prove
Connect the specific to the larger contextStep back from the essay's specific argument and show how it relates to a broader issue, debate, or field of inquiry.Shows the essay's relevance beyond its immediate scope
Answer the "so what" question directlyState explicitly why the argument matters — what it means for readers, for the field, or for the problem introduced at the beginning.Gives the reader a reason to care about the argument
Acknowledge complexityRecognize what the essay cannot resolve, where further inquiry is needed, or where the argument has limits — without undermining the thesis.Shows intellectual honesty and maturity

Notice that none of these strategies involves listing the essay's main points again. Every strategy requires the writer to think about the essay's argument in a new way — to step above it and see its shape from the outside.

What Synthesis Does Not Mean

Synthesis does not mean inventing entirely new arguments in the conclusion. One of the most common conclusion errors is introducing a major claim or piece of evidence that never appeared in the body of the essay. This creates a jarring effect for the reader, who expected the essay to be winding down. New arguments introduced in the conclusion feel undeveloped and unsupported because they are — the essay never made room to develop them.

Synthesis also does not mean being vague or abstract. Some students, trying to write a "big" conclusion, drift into language so general that it says nothing. Statements like "This shows that many things are important in the world today" are not synthesis. They are empty filler. Good synthesis is specific, grounded in the essay's actual content, and clear about what the writer means.

Common Mistake — New Arguments in the Conclusion

If you find yourself writing about something in your conclusion that never appeared in your body paragraphs, stop. You have two options: move the material into the body where it can be properly developed, or cut it from the conclusion. A conclusion cannot do justice to an argument the essay has not developed.

Section 22.7

Final Implications

One of the most effective ways to conclude an essay is to articulate its implications — to tell the reader what follows from the argument that has been made.

Implications are the logical extensions of an argument. If an essay has argued that a certain policy causes a specific harm, the implication might be that the policy should be reformed or reconsidered. If an essay has analyzed a literary text and shown that a particular reading is more supported by the evidence, the implication might be that previous interpretations have missed something important. If an essay has examined a historical event, the implication might involve what the event reveals about the period or what lessons it offers.

Implications give the reader a sense that the essay's argument matters beyond the page. They answer the question: If you accept what this essay has argued, what follows from that?

How to Write Implications Without Overreaching

Writing about implications requires care. There is a real risk of overreaching — making claims that go far beyond what the essay's evidence can support. An essay that analyzes two short stories by a single author should not conclude by claiming to reveal universal truths about all human literature. An essay about traffic patterns in one city should not claim to have solved urban planning for the entire world.

The key is to scale your implications to your evidence. Ask yourself: What can I reasonably claim based on what this essay has actually shown? Small, precise implications are more persuasive than large, vague ones.

Overreaching

This essay proves that technology is destroying human relationships and society must change immediately.

Appropriately Scaled

The evidence examined in this essay suggests that the design of certain social media platforms may actively discourage the kinds of deep, sustained attention that healthy relationships require — a finding that has implications for how platforms are regulated and how users, particularly young people, are educated about their use.

Both versions are drawing implications, but the second version is grounded in what the essay actually argued. It is specific about what kind of technology, what kind of relationship, and what kinds of implications follow. This precision makes it more credible and more persuasive.

Application — Across Disciplines

The skill of articulating implications transfers to every academic subject. A history essay might conclude by addressing what a past event reveals about patterns that persist today. A science report might conclude by suggesting what its findings mean for future research. A business analysis might conclude by addressing what its findings recommend for practice. Wherever you write, implications answer the "so what" question.

Section 22.8

Revising Openings and Closings

Even experienced writers rarely get introductions and conclusions right on the first draft. These are among the hardest parts of an essay to write well because they require a clear understanding of the whole essay — and that understanding often only develops after the body has been written.

One of the most practical pieces of advice in this chapter is this: write your introduction last, or at least revise it substantially after the body is complete. Many writers discover that the essay they finished writing is not exactly the essay they planned to write. The argument evolved, the evidence led somewhere unexpected, or the thesis shifted during drafting. If the introduction was written at the beginning and never revised, it may no longer accurately represent the essay that follows.

The same applies to conclusions. A conclusion written before the body paragraphs have been fully developed cannot synthesize what the essay has actually done. Conclusions often need to be written or rewritten once the body is in good shape.

Diagnosing Introduction Problems

When revising an introduction, ask yourself the following questions. These questions will identify the most common problems and suggest what needs to change.

Introduction Revision Checklist
  • Does the introduction actually lead to the thesis, or does it go in a different direction and then suddenly land on the thesis without preparation? If the thesis feels disconnected from the opening, rework the movement from context to thesis so it is logical.
  • Does the context give the reader enough to understand why the topic matters — or is it either too broad (starting at the level of all human history) or too narrow (jumping immediately into technical detail the reader cannot yet process)?
  • Is the thesis specific and clear? If the thesis is vague, the introduction will feel purposeless, because the reader cannot tell where the essay is going.
  • Does every sentence in the introduction contribute to leading the reader toward the thesis? If a sentence could be cut without damaging the introduction, it probably should be.

Diagnosing Conclusion Problems

When revising a conclusion, similar diagnostic questions help identify what is not working and why.

Conclusion Revision Checklist
  • Does the conclusion simply repeat what was in the introduction and body, or does it add meaning? If you can read the introduction and the conclusion and they say essentially the same thing, the conclusion needs to be reworked.
  • Does the conclusion answer the "so what" question? A reader who finishes your conclusion should feel that the argument mattered — that the essay was worth reading.
  • Does the conclusion introduce new arguments that were never developed in the body? If so, either move the argument into the body where it can be developed, or cut it from the conclusion.
  • Does the conclusion end with a strong final sentence, or does it trail off? The last sentence of the conclusion is the last thing the reader reads. It should leave an impression.

Chapter 22 — Common Mistakes

Common Mistakes in Introductions and Conclusions

The Empty Hook

Perhaps the most common introduction error is the "hook" gone wrong. Students are often taught that introductions should begin with a hook — a striking sentence that grabs the reader's attention. In practice, many hooks are empty: they create the form of a dramatic opening without delivering real content.

Error Type 1 — The Dictionary Definition Opening
"According to Merriam-Webster, justice is defined as the quality of being just, impartial, or fair."

This approach signals to the reader that the writer could not find a more interesting way into the topic. Dictionary definitions can occasionally be useful, but only when the definition itself is genuinely at issue in the essay — not as a generic opener.

Error Type 2 — The Rhetorical Question That Goes Nowhere
"Have you ever wondered about climate change?"

This kind of question creates the impression of engagement but delivers nothing. It asks the reader a question that the essay may or may not answer, and it substitutes a conversational gesture for actual content.

Error Type 3 — The Sweeping Historical Generalization
"Since the dawn of civilization, humans have struggled with inequality."

This kind of opening is too broad to be useful. It says something technically true but meaninglessly vast. It could serve as the opening of any essay on any subject related to human society.

Error Type 4 — The Statement of Intent Without Substance
"In this essay, I will discuss three reasons why social media is harmful to teenagers."

This kind of opening is mechanical. It tells the reader what the essay will do without doing it. It announces rather than argues. In some contexts, this kind of signposting is appropriate — but as an opening move, it tends to flatten the introduction.

The "In Conclusion" Problem

Beginning a conclusion with "In conclusion," "To summarize," or "As I have shown in this essay" is a weak habit. These phrases are not forbidden, but they are usually unnecessary. The reader knows the conclusion is the conclusion because it is the last paragraph. Announcing it redundantly is a mild form of padding.

More importantly, "in conclusion" openings often lead into mere summary, not synthesis. The phrase trains the writer to list what was already said rather than to think about what it all means. A stronger concluding paragraph opens with a sentence that moves the argument forward. Instead of marking time, open the conclusion by naming the central insight the essay has developed, or by returning to the problem introduced at the beginning — now with the understanding the essay has built.

The Abrupt Conclusion

Some conclusions end too quickly. They synthesize briefly, offer one implication, and then stop. The reader is left feeling that the essay ran out of energy before the end. A conclusion should feel complete — not long for its own sake, but genuinely landed.

Abrupt

Overall, urban green spaces are important and cities should build more of them.

Complete

Urban planning that makes room for trees, parks, and natural corridors is not a luxury or a concession to sentiment — it is an investment in the resilience, equity, and health of cities whose long-term success depends on livable environments as much as on economic infrastructure.

The complete version does more work. It is specific, grounded in the essay's argument, and gives the reader a clear, memorable articulation of the essay's central claim. It sounds like the end of something — which is exactly what a conclusion should sound like.

The opening section of an essay that establishes context, narrows to the topic and problem, and presents the thesis statement.

The closing section of an essay that synthesizes the argument, addresses implications, and provides a sense of completion.

Background information that helps the reader understand what territory the essay is entering and why the topic matters.

  6. What does it mean to address implications in a conclusion? What question do implications answer, and how should they be scaled?
  7. Why is it common advice to write or substantially revise the introduction after the body of the essay is complete? What problems arise when writers do not do this?
  8. Identify three common introduction errors discussed in this chapter. For each one, explain why it weakens the introduction and what the writer should do differently.
  9. Why is the dictionary definition opening generally considered a weak hook? In what narrow circumstances might it actually be appropriate?
  10. What should the final sentence of a conclusion accomplish? How does a strong final sentence differ from an abrupt or trailing one?

Discussion and Writing Prompts

  1. The Opening Problem. Think of a book, article, or essay you have read recently — in any course — that had an introduction you found engaging. What made it work? Did it use context, tension, a problem, or something else? Write a short reflection of two to three paragraphs analyzing why that introduction succeeded.
  2. The "So What" Question. Choose an essay you have already written — in this course or another. Read only the conclusion. Does it answer the "so what" question? Does it synthesize or merely summarize? Write a one-paragraph evaluation of your own conclusion, then rewrite the conclusion using what you have learned in this chapter.
  3. Introduction Failure Modes. Look at the following two introductions. Identify which type of error each one makes and explain in a paragraph what each writer should do differently.

    Introduction A: From the very beginning of time, people have always argued about politics. Politics is everywhere in modern society. This essay will talk about the Electoral College.

    Introduction B: The Electoral College is the system by which American presidents are elected. Many people have different opinions about it. Some people think it should be kept. Others think it should be abolished. There are many interesting points on both sides of this debate.
  4. Synthesis Practice. Take a topic you know well from any course — a historical event, a scientific concept, a literary theme, a policy issue. Write a one-paragraph synthetic conclusion for an imaginary essay on that topic. Your conclusion should not summarize fake body paragraphs. Instead, write a conclusion that synthesizes a central insight and addresses its implications.

Activity 22CThe Synthesis Challenge

Take the following list of essay body points and write a conclusion that synthesizes them — do not merely list them again. Your conclusion should be at least four to six sentences. It should not begin with "In conclusion." It should end with a strong final sentence.

Essay Topic: The effects of urbanization on local bird populations

Body points made in the essay: Urbanization reduces natural habitat and nesting sites. Some bird species adapt to urban environments while others decline significantly. Light and noise pollution disrupt migration and breeding behavior. Urban green spaces partially offset the negative effects of development.

Your conclusion should connect these points to a larger insight about the relationship between urban development and ecological health.

Activity 22DPeer Review — Introductions and Conclusions

Exchange a current essay draft with a classmate. Read only the introduction and conclusion. Do not read the body. Write your feedback in full sentences, not one-word answers. Give your classmate something actionable to work with.

  1. For the introduction: What context does the introduction establish? Does it narrow effectively toward a thesis? Is the thesis specific and clear? Does anything feel missing, vague, or disconnected?
  2. For the conclusion: Does the conclusion synthesize or merely summarize? Does it address implications? Does it feel complete? What is the final sentence, and does it have weight?

Chapter Checklist

Chapter 22 Checklist
Use before submitting any essay — evaluates introduction and conclusion against chapter standards
Introduction
  • The opening sentence establishes context rather than making an empty or overly broad claim
  • The introduction narrows from context toward the specific topic and problem
  • The thesis statement is specific, focused, and positioned at or near the end of the introduction
  • Every sentence in the introduction contributes to leading the reader toward the thesis
  • The introduction does not make promises the essay does not keep
  • The opening does not use a dictionary definition, a sweeping generalization, or an empty rhetorical question unless the choice is genuinely effective and justified
Conclusion
  • The conclusion synthesizes rather than simply repeats the essay's main points
  • The conclusion returns to the thesis and shows how the essay has developed or enriched it
  • The conclusion addresses the implications of the argument — it answers "so what"
  • The conclusion does not introduce major new arguments that were never developed in the body
  • The conclusion does not begin with "In conclusion," "To summarize," or similar mechanical phrases
  • The final sentence has weight and gives the reader a genuine sense of completion
Revision Check
  • The introduction was revised after the body was complete to ensure it accurately reflects the essay's actual argument
  • The conclusion was written or revised after the body was complete to ensure it synthesizes what the essay actually built
  • The introduction and conclusion feel like they belong to the same essay — there is no significant gap between the thesis the introduction promises and the argument the conclusion delivers

Chapter 9 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

A sentence can be grammatically correct and still confuse its reader. It can contain the right words, in roughly the right order, with proper punctuation at the end — and still require a reader to stop, back up, and read it again. When that happens, the problem is almost never the reader's fault. The problem is clarity.

Sentence clarity is the quality that allows a reader to understand what a sentence means on the first pass, without rereading, guessing, or puzzling over what the writer intended. Clarity is not the same as simplicity. Clear sentences can be long, complex, and sophisticated. What they cannot be is confusing. They must move forward in a way that makes sense — subject before explanation, cause before effect, actor before action — so that the reader's mind is always guided, never lost.

This chapter focuses on the craft of writing sentences that are genuinely clear. It begins with the most important clarity principle — that every sentence needs a recognizable human actor doing a clear action — and works outward through word order, modifier placement, pronoun reference, unnecessary interruptions, and sentence focus. It ends with a practical revision method for transforming confusing sentences into ones that communicate reliably.

Sentence clarity is a skill that improves with deliberate practice. Many clarity problems in student writing are not careless errors; they are habits of writing that were never consciously examined. This chapter asks you to examine those habits, understand why they cause problems, and replace them with techniques that reliably produce clear prose.

Opening Question

Read the following sentence: "There was a study conducted by researchers at the university, which found that students who had been exposed to the new curriculum, which replaced the old one in the previous year, showed improvement in their performance on standardized assessments, and this was especially notable."

The sentence is grammatically defensible. But how many times did you have to read it? By the end of this chapter, you will know exactly why that sentence is difficult and how to revise it into something a reader can follow on the first try.

Section 9.1

What Sentence Clarity Means

Clarity in a sentence is not decoration. It is the basic obligation of academic writing. When you write a sentence, you are asking a reader to follow a path from the first word to the last. If that path is well-marked, the reader moves smoothly through. If the path twists unexpectedly, has unmarked forks, or doubles back on itself, the reader stumbles — and when readers stumble repeatedly, they stop trusting the writer.

Clarity Is About the Reader

The most important thing to understand about sentence clarity is that it is not about you, the writer. It is about the reader. When you compose a sentence, you already know what you mean. The meaning lives inside your thinking before the words even arrive. This is why so many unclear sentences pass through a writer's first draft unnoticed: the writer reads what they meant, not what they wrote. The reader has no access to the writer's private intention. The reader has only the words on the page.

This distinction matters enormously. Asking yourself "Does this sentence make sense to me?" is not a reliable test for clarity. The sentence probably does make sense to you — you wrote it. The better question is: "If a careful reader encountered this sentence for the first time, would it be immediately clear what I am saying, who is doing what, and how this relates to my point?"

Academic writing asks this question constantly, because academic readers are not passive. They are evaluating your reasoning, following your evidence, and judging whether your ideas hold together. A sentence that makes a reader pause and decode interrupts that evaluative process — and in academic contexts, that interruption costs you credibility.

Clarity Is Not Simplicity

Clarity and simplicity are related, but they are not the same thing. A simple sentence is short and contains only basic information. A clear sentence can be long, layered, and intellectually demanding — and still guide the reader through without confusion. The difference lies in structure and sequence, not in length or vocabulary.

Clear (though long)
"Although the committee reviewed all three proposals before the deadline, only the first — the one submitted by the research team in January — contained the full financial documentation required for preliminary approval."
Confusing (though shorter)
"The proposal they reviewed before the deadline, which the committee did, was the one with the financial documentation that was required."

The first version contains a subordinate clause, a parenthetical, and a relative clause. But it is clear: the reader always knows what the main point is, who is involved, and how the pieces relate. The second version is shorter and uses simpler vocabulary — yet it is much harder to follow. Clarity is about arrangement, not length.

Key Principle

Clarity and grammar are not the same. A sentence can follow every rule of standard English grammar and still be genuinely difficult to understand. Clarity is a higher-order concern than correctness: it ensures that correctly written sentences actually communicate.

Section 9.2

Clear Subjects and Strong Verbs

The most reliable foundation for a clear sentence is a specific, recognizable subject paired with a direct, meaningful verb. This pairing — subject and verb — is the core of every English sentence. When the subject is clear and the verb is strong, the reader immediately understands who is doing what. When the subject is vague or buried and the verb is weak, the reader's attention drifts before the sentence even finishes.

Subjects Should Name Actors

In academic writing, subjects tend to drift away from real actors — the people, groups, institutions, or forces actually doing things. Instead, abstract nouns take over: "factors," "considerations," "aspects," "issues," "situations," "elements." When these abstract nouns become subjects, sentences become passive and difficult to follow.

Weak Subject
"Significant factors in the consideration of economic policy include the influence of inflationary pressures on the decision-making processes of governmental bodies."
Clear Subject
"Governments must consider how inflation affects economic policy decisions."

The weak version uses abstract nouns ("factors," "consideration," "influence," "processes") as its main structural elements. The clear version puts the real actor — governments — at the front, with a real verb — "must consider" — immediately after. The reader immediately knows who is doing what.

This does not mean you should always use simple words or avoid abstraction. Academic writing necessarily deals with abstract ideas. But even when the ideas are abstract, you can often find a subject that represents an actor or a doer — a researcher, a government, a theory, an argument — and make that actor the grammatical subject of your sentence.

Nominalization: Hiding Verbs Inside Nouns

Nominalization is the pattern of converting verbs and adjectives into nouns, then using those nouns where verbs should be. This is one of the most common sources of academic writing that sounds impressive but reads as vague. Nominalizations turn verbs into nouns: "failure" from "fail," "consideration" from "consider," "recommendation" from "recommend." When a nominalization sits where a verb should be, the sentence loses energy and direction.

Nominalized (Weak)Revised (Strong)
The committee made a recommendation that...The committee recommended that...
There was a failure on the part of administrators to communicate...Administrators failed to communicate...
The study offers an explanation of why...The study explains why...
The team reached a consensus that...The team agreed that...
There was an improvement in outcomes after the intervention.Outcomes improved after the intervention.

Passive Voice and Clarity

Related to weak subjects is passive voice. In a passive construction, the object of an action becomes the subject of the sentence, and the real actor either disappears or moves to a secondary "by" phrase at the end.

Example 9.2.1
Active vs. Passive Voice
  • ActiveThe research team conducted the experiment. — Actor is the subject; verb is direct.
  • PassiveThe experiment was conducted by the research team. — Object is the subject; actor is demoted.
  • Passive (actor deleted)The experiment was conducted. — Actor has disappeared entirely. Who conducted it? Unknown.

Passive voice is not always wrong. In scientific writing, passive voice is sometimes preferred because it emphasizes the process or result rather than the individual researcher. In legal and policy writing, passive constructions serve specific purposes. But in general academic writing, especially in the humanities and social sciences, overuse of passive voice creates vague, evasive sentences in which responsibility and action are unclear.

The strongest test for passive voice is not grammatical but communicative: if your reader cannot tell who is responsible for an action, consider whether active voice would make the sentence clearer and more honest.

Common Mistake — Overusing Passive Voice

Many students adopt passive voice in academic writing because it sounds "formal" or "objective." But passive voice is only appropriate when the actor genuinely does not matter or is truly unknown. When you know who did something and it matters, name them as the subject.

Section 9.3

Logical Word Order

Sentences are not just collections of words; they are sequences of words in which order carries meaning. English word order is not entirely free. Readers have expectations about where information will appear in a sentence, and when those expectations are violated — even within grammatical rules — the result can be confusion.

The Basic English Pattern

The fundamental word order pattern in English declarative sentences is: Subject → Verb → Object or Complement. This pattern is so deeply ingrained in English that readers process it almost automatically. When sentences depart significantly from this pattern — when the verb is far from the subject, when heavy modifiers precede rather than follow what they modify, when multiple clauses interrupt before the main verb arrives — readers must work harder to extract meaning.

This does not mean every sentence must follow the simple pattern exactly. Skilled writers vary sentence structure for emphasis and rhythm. But when variation creates confusion, the variation is working against clarity rather than for it.

Front-Loading and End-Loading

Where information appears in a sentence determines how much emphasis it receives. English readers instinctively pay most attention to two positions: the beginning of a sentence and the end. Information placed in the middle of a long sentence tends to receive less attention.

Front-loading means placing the most important orienting information at the beginning — usually the subject and context. This tells the reader immediately what or who the sentence is about.

End-loading means placing the most important new or surprising information at the end. The end position carries natural emphasis, much like the end of a spoken sentence carries slightly more stress in the voice.

Example 9.3.1
Controlling Emphasis Through Word Order
  • Strong order"Despite strong criticism from the scientific community, the government approved the policy." — Context up front; main point at the end.
  • Reversed emphasis"The policy was approved by the government, despite strong criticism from the scientific community." — Main point leads; concession trails. Neither is wrong — the choice depends on what you want the reader to notice.

Information Should Move Forward, Not Backward

Another key word order principle is that sentences should generally move forward — from familiar to new, from context to claim, from cause to effect. When sentences move backward — introducing a conclusion before the context, or explaining significance before the fact — readers must reassemble the logic in reverse order.

Backward Movement (Confusing)
"The policy was reversed after the funding collapse, which happened because external donors withdrew support in response to the accounting irregularities."
Forward Movement (Clear)
"External donors withdrew support after accounting irregularities were discovered, causing a funding collapse that ultimately forced the program to reverse its policy."

The revised version follows the logical and chronological sequence: irregularities → withdrawal → collapse → reversal. The reader follows the chain naturally. The original version gives the conclusion first and explains causes in reverse, requiring extra cognitive work.

Section 9.4

Modifier Placement

A modifier is a word, phrase, or clause that describes, limits, or adds information to another element in the sentence. Modifiers are essential tools for adding precision and detail to academic writing. But they are also one of the most common sources of sentence confusion, because they must be placed close to — and clearly connected to — the element they modify.

When a modifier is placed too far from the word it modifies, or when it could logically modify more than one element, the result is ambiguity or absurdity. The two main types of modifier problems are misplaced modifiers and dangling modifiers.

Misplaced Modifiers

A misplaced modifier has been placed in a position where it appears to modify the wrong element in the sentence. Misplaced modifiers most often involve limiting words like only, just, nearly, almost, even, barely, and merely. These small words should be placed immediately before the word or phrase they modify.

Example 9.4.1
Misplaced Limiting Word
  • Ambiguous"The professor only teaches graduate students on Thursdays." — Does "only" modify "teaches graduate students" or "on Thursdays"?
  • Meaning A"The professor teaches graduate students only on Thursdays." — She teaches them on no other day.
  • Meaning B"On Thursdays, the professor teaches only graduate students." — She teaches no undergraduates on Thursdays.

Modifying phrases placed too far from their noun also cause misplacement problems.

Example 9.4.2
Misplaced Prepositional Phrase
  • Confusing"She found an article about the research in the library." — Was the research in the library, or did she find the article there?
  • Corrected"In the library, she found an article about the research." — "In the library" now clearly modifies where she was, not where the research was.

Dangling Modifiers

A dangling modifier is a modifying phrase or clause that has no clear, logical connection to any element in the sentence. The most common dangling modifiers are participial phrases at the beginning of a sentence. When the subject of the main clause is not the person or thing performing the action in the participial phrase, the modifier dangles.

Example 9.4.3
Dangling Modifier: Participial Phrase
  • Dangling"Having reviewed the data carefully, the conclusion seems clear." — Who reviewed the data? Not the conclusion.
  • Corrected"Having reviewed the data carefully, the researchers concluded that the pattern was clear." — Now "the researchers" are both the subject of the main clause and the implied reviewers.
Dangling ModifierCorrected Version
By analyzing the survey results, several patterns were identified.By analyzing the survey results, the team identified several patterns.
After considering the evidence, the argument remains unconvincing.After considering the evidence, I find the argument unconvincing.
Walking down the hall, the noise was overwhelming.Walking down the hall, she found the noise overwhelming.
Common Mistake — Rephrasing the Phrase Instead of Fixing the Clause

Students sometimes try to fix a dangling modifier by rewording the introductory phrase rather than correcting the main clause. But if the actor does not appear in the main clause as the subject, the modifier is still dangling — regardless of how carefully the opening phrase is worded. The fix must be in the main clause.

Section 9.5

Pronoun Reference

Pronouns — he, she, it, they, this, that, these, those, which, who — are among the most useful words in the English language. They allow writers to refer back to previously named subjects without repeating those subjects every time. In academic writing, where ideas build on each other across multiple sentences, pronouns help create coherence and flow.

But pronouns only work when their antecedent — the word or idea they refer to — is clear and unambiguous. When a pronoun could refer to more than one possible antecedent, or when the antecedent is missing entirely, the sentence loses clarity. This is called vague pronoun reference, and it is one of the most common sources of confusion in academic writing.

Every Pronoun Must Have a Clear Antecedent

Every pronoun should refer clearly and directly to a specific noun or noun phrase that appeared recently in the text. The reader should never have to guess which noun the pronoun is replacing.

Example 9.5.1
Ambiguous Pronoun Reference
  • Ambiguous"When the director spoke to the journalist, she was not prepared for the controversy." — Who was not prepared? The director or the journalist?
  • Corrected A"When the director spoke to the journalist, the director was not prepared for the controversy."
  • Corrected B"When she spoke to the journalist, the director found herself unprepared for the controversy."

The Problem with This and That

Among the most problematic pronouns in academic writing are this and that used alone to refer to an entire idea, argument, or situation from the previous sentence. When used this way, these pronouns become so vague that the reader cannot be sure exactly what they are pointing to.

Vague Demonstrative
"The study found that low-income students had significantly less access to academic support resources than their higher-income peers. This has major implications for educational equity."

What does "this" refer to? The study? The finding? The access gap? The comparison?
Summary Noun Added
"The study found that low-income students had significantly less access to academic support resources than their higher-income peers. This disparity has major implications for educational equity."

"This disparity" is precise. The reader immediately knows what the writer is pointing to.
The Summary Noun Technique

Whenever you use this, that, these, or those to refer to a broad idea or situation, follow the demonstrative with a summary noun that specifies exactly what you are pointing to. This single habit will dramatically reduce vague pronoun reference in your academic writing.

Vague DemonstrativeImproved with Summary Noun
This is a serious problem.This pattern is a serious problem.
This suggests that...This evidence suggests that...
That led to significant changes.That decision led to significant changes.
This has been studied extensively.This phenomenon has been studied extensively.
Section 9.6

Avoiding Unnecessary Interruptions

A sentence is a continuous act of communication. The reader follows it from subject to verb to object, accumulating meaning along the way. When the sentence is interrupted — when long phrases, asides, or parenthetical clauses separate the subject from its verb or the verb from its object — the reader must hold part of the sentence in memory while waiting for the rest. Occasionally, a brief interruption adds useful emphasis or qualification. Frequently, unnecessary interruptions make sentences genuinely difficult to process.

When Interruptions Cause Problems

An interruption becomes a problem when it is so long or so dense that the reader loses track of the main clause while wading through it.

Overloaded Interruption
"The committee, after months of deliberation and numerous consultations with external advisors, financial analysts, and members of the general public who had submitted comments during the open review period, decided to reject the proposal."

Twenty-seven words separate "committee" from "decided."
Interruption Moved to Front
"After months of deliberation and extensive public consultation, the committee decided to reject the proposal."

The main clause — "the committee decided to reject the proposal" — runs without interruption.

Strategies for Reducing Interruptions

StrategyBeforeAfter
Move to frontThe committee, having reviewed all the testimony, recommended approval.Having reviewed all the testimony, the committee recommended approval.
Move to endThe study, funded by three agencies, confirmed the findings.The study confirmed the findings, having been funded by three separate government agencies.
Split into two sentencesThe proposal, which had been submitted three months earlier and revised twice, was approved.The research team submitted the proposal three months earlier. After two rounds of revisions, it was finally approved.
The Read-Aloud Test

Read the sentence aloud and skip the interrupting material. If the main clause still makes complete sense on its own, the interruption is functioning as a modifier. If the interruption contains essential information the main clause depends on, consider whether it belongs before or after the main clause rather than in the middle.

Section 9.7

Sentence Focus and Emphasis

Every sentence has a focal point — the part that the reader understands as the most important piece of information. In a well-crafted sentence, the writer controls where that focal point lands. In a poorly structured sentence, the focal point drifts to wherever the sentence happens to end, or it disappears into the middle where emphasis is naturally weakest.

The End of a Sentence Carries Natural Emphasis

English readers instinctively treat the end of a sentence as the place where the most significant information lands. This is partly because of spoken language — we naturally emphasize the final element of a spoken utterance, and that last word or phrase lingers in the mind. Written prose follows the same pattern. Whatever appears at the end of a sentence receives the most attention.

This principle has a practical implication: avoid ending sentences with weak words. A sentence that ends with "however," "though," "also," "as well," or "too" places its emphasis on a word with almost no content. A sentence that ends with a specific noun, a precise verb, or a key term places its emphasis where it belongs.

Weak Ending
"The study produced important results, surprisingly enough."

The sentence ends on a filler phrase. "Surprisingly enough" carries no meaning.
Strong Ending
"The study produced results that challenge the dominant theoretical framework."

The sentence ends on the key concept. The reader's attention falls on "dominant theoretical framework."

The Beginning of a Sentence Establishes Context

If the end carries the most emphasis, the beginning carries the responsibility of orientation. The first few words of a sentence tell the reader what the sentence is going to be about. If the opening is vague, abstract, or delayed by unnecessary preamble, the reader must wait longer than necessary to understand what is coming.

Example 9.7.1
Removing Empty Openings
  • Vague opening"It is interesting to note that recent studies on sleep deprivation have shown significant effects on cognitive performance."
  • Direct opening"Recent studies on sleep deprivation show significant effects on cognitive performance." — "It is interesting to note that" carries no information. Its removal makes the sentence cleaner and more confident.
Common Empty Openings to Avoid

It is important to note that... · It should be mentioned that... · It is interesting that... · There is / There are (when this buries the real subject) · The fact that... — These constructions are not always wrong, but when they appear out of habit as a way of starting a sentence without committing to a subject, they should be revised.

The Known-to-New Principle

A related principle of sentence focus is that sentences work best when they move from known information to new information. Known information is what the reader already has from context: the subject just discussed, the concept just introduced. New information is what the sentence is adding. When a sentence opens with new, unfamiliar information and ends with something the reader already knows, it feels backward.

Reversed (New to Known)
"A significant contributor to the dropout rates seen in the study was the lack of mentorship — something researchers had long suspected."
Forward (Known to New)
"Researchers had long suspected that mentorship played a role in retention. The study confirmed this suspicion, finding that lack of mentorship was a significant contributor to student dropout rates."

In the revised version, the first sentence opens with known context (what researchers suspected), and the second sentence opens with that context again ("The study confirmed this suspicion") before delivering the new finding. The reader is always guided from what they know to what they are learning.

Section 9.8

Revising Confusing Sentences

No writer produces clear sentences on the first try, every time. First drafts are written at the speed of thinking, and when thought moves fast, clarity often suffers. The solution is revision — and revision of sentence-level problems works best when it is systematic rather than random. The following six-step process gives you a reliable way to approach any confusing sentence.

The Six-Step Revision Process

Step 1 — Read aloud. The ear catches confusion the eye overlooks. If you stumble while reading aloud, the reader will too.

Step 2 — Find the subject and verb. Who or what is the subject? What is the main verb? Are they close together, or separated by a long interruption?

Step 3 — Check the modifiers. Is every modifying phrase close to what it modifies? Could any modifier be read as modifying the wrong element? Are participial phrases followed by the correct subject?

Step 4 — Check pronoun references. Does every pronoun have a clear, specific antecedent? Could any pronoun refer to more than one candidate? Do any uses of this or that need a summary noun?

Step 5 — Check word order. Does the sentence open with helpful orienting information? Does it end with the most important point? Does it move from known to new?

Step 6 — Simplify if needed. If the sentence is still difficult after steps 1–5, it may be trying to carry too many ideas. Divide it into two shorter sentences.

Before-and-After Revision Example

The following passage contains multiple clarity problems. The revision applies the six-step process.

Before Revision
There was a study conducted by researchers at the university, which found that students who had been exposed to the new curriculum, which replaced the old one in the previous year, showed improvement in their performance on standardized assessments, and this was especially notable.
Revision Analysis
Applying the Six Steps
  • Step 1–2Subject: buried ("a study"). Verb: "was conducted" — passive and weak. Forty-eight words with no clean main clause.
  • Step 3Two relative clauses ("which found..." and "which replaced...") stack on top of each other, creating an interrupted, tangled structure.
  • Step 4"This was especially notable" — what does "this" refer to? The study? The improvement? The comparison? A summary noun is needed.
  • Step 5–6Sentence is too long and too tangled for a single restructure. Break it into two sentences with clear actors and verbs.
After Revision
A university research team found that the new curriculum, introduced the previous year, significantly improved students' performance on standardized assessments. The improvement was especially notable among students who had struggled under the old curriculum.

The revision addresses all the major problems at once: "There was a study conducted" becomes the clear active subject-verb "A university research team found"; the tangled relative clauses are separated into two clean sentences; "this was especially notable" becomes "The improvement was especially notable" (summary noun replaces vague demonstrative); and the result is two clear, readable sentences in place of one long, confused sentence.

Notice that the revised passage says exactly what the original tried to say. No information is lost. The revision makes meaning accessible — it does not reduce it.

Key Terms, Summary, and Review

Key Terms

antecedent
The word or phrase that a pronoun refers to. Every pronoun should have a clear, specific antecedent nearby in the text.
dangling modifier
A modifying phrase or clause that has no clear, logical connection to the subject of the main clause. Most common with opening participial phrases.
end emphasis
The principle that the final position of a sentence receives the most natural emphasis in English prose. Key information belongs at the end.
known-to-new movement
A sentence structure principle in which familiar information appears early in the sentence and new information appears at the end.
misplaced modifier
A modifier placed in a position where it appears to modify the wrong element in the sentence, creating ambiguity or confusion.
modifier
A word, phrase, or clause that describes, limits, or qualifies another element in a sentence. Must be placed close to what it modifies.
nominalization
The conversion of a verb or adjective into a noun (e.g., "fail" → "failure"). Overuse weakens sentences by hiding action inside abstract nouns.
passive voice
A construction in which the object of an action becomes the grammatical subject and the real actor is absent or placed in a secondary "by" phrase.
pronoun reference
The relationship between a pronoun and the noun it replaces. Clear pronoun reference means the pronoun has one obvious, unambiguous antecedent.
sentence clarity
The quality of a sentence that allows a reader to understand its meaning on the first reading, without rereading or guesswork.
summary noun
A noun added after a demonstrative pronoun (this, that, these, those) to make vague reference specific — e.g., "this finding," "this disparity."
vague pronoun reference
A pronoun whose antecedent is unclear, missing, or capable of referring to more than one element in the surrounding text.

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 9.1Sentence clarity is the quality that allows a reader to understand a sentence on the first reading. It is not the same as simplicity or grammatical correctness. Clarity is fundamentally about the reader, not the writer — and requires imagining how the sentence will land for someone who has not yet read it.
  • Sec 9.2Clear sentences pair specific, identifiable subjects (real actors) with strong, direct verbs. Nominalizations — hiding verbs inside nouns — weaken sentences by moving action into abstract nouns. Passive voice is appropriate in some academic contexts but should not be used by default when it obscures who is responsible for an action.
  • Sec 9.3Logical word order follows the basic Subject → Verb → Object pattern and moves information forward — from familiar to new, from cause to effect, from context to claim. Front-loading provides orientation; end-loading delivers emphasis. Backward-moving sentences require readers to mentally reassemble the logic.
  • Sec 9.4Modifiers must be placed close to what they modify. Misplaced modifiers appear to modify the wrong element; dangling modifiers have no clear connection to the subject of the main clause. The solution for dangling modifiers is to name the correct actor as the subject of the main clause.
  • Sec 9.5Every pronoun needs a clear, unambiguous antecedent. Vague use of this and that to refer to broad ideas creates confusion; adding a summary noun (e.g., "this finding," "this disparity") resolves the problem precisely and directly.
  • Sec 9.6Long interruptions between the subject and verb — especially those running more than ten to fifteen words — prevent readers from following the main clause. These interruptions can usually be moved to the front or back of the sentence, or split into a separate sentence entirely.
  • Sec 9.7The end of a sentence carries the most natural emphasis; important information belongs there. The beginning establishes context and should not be wasted on empty preamble. Sentences should move from known information to new information, guiding the reader forward rather than asking them to work backward.
  • Sec 9.8The six-step revision process — read aloud, locate subject and verb, check modifiers, check pronouns, evaluate word order, simplify — provides a reliable method for diagnosing and repairing confusing sentences. Clarity is achieved through revision, not through getting every sentence perfect on the first attempt.

  4. Why does the end position of a sentence carry special importance in English prose? How should this principle change the way you construct sentences that communicate your most important ideas?

  6. Explain the "known-to-new" principle of sentence construction. Why does this principle help readers follow complex academic arguments more easily than the reverse pattern would?
  7. When is an interruption in the middle of a sentence acceptable, and when does it become a clarity problem? Describe three strategies for handling a problematic interruption.
  8. Why is reading aloud one of the most useful revision tools for sentence clarity? What kinds of problems is it especially effective at catching that silent reading might miss?

There is a general tendency among students who are new to academic writing to engage in the production of sentences that are excessively long and which contain many ideas that are connected by various phrases and clauses, and this is something that has been noted by instructors, which leads to confusion on the part of readers. Chapter 9 Writer's Checklist

Use this checklist when revising any academic writing for sentence clarity. Work through it in order — global issues (subjects, verbs) before local issues (punctuation, word choice).

Subjects and Verbs
  • Every sentence has a clear, identifiable subject that names a real actor or agent.
  • Main verbs are strong and carry real meaning — not hidden inside nominalizations.
  • Passive voice is used intentionally and purposefully, not by default or out of habit.
  • Abstract nouns do not serve as subjects when a real actor could take that position instead.
Word Order
  • Sentences generally move from familiar information to new information.
  • The most important information is positioned at the end of the sentence, not buried in the middle.
  • Sentences move logically forward — cause before effect, context before claim, actor before action.
  • No sentence ends with an empty or weak word where a meaningful term should appear.
Modifiers
  • All modifying phrases and clauses are placed close to the elements they modify.
  • No modifier could reasonably be read as modifying the wrong element.
  • All introductory participial phrases are followed by the correct subject in the main clause.
  • Limiting words (only, just, nearly, almost) are placed immediately before the word they modify.
Pronoun Reference
  • Every pronoun has a clear, specific antecedent that appears nearby in the text.
  • No pronoun could refer to more than one possible antecedent.
  • This, that, these, and those are followed by summary nouns when they refer to broad ideas or situations.
Interruptions and Focus
  • No sentence has an interruption so long that the main clause becomes difficult to follow.
  • Long modifying material has been moved to the beginning or end of the sentence where possible.
  • Sentences do not begin with empty preambles (It is important to note that... · There is/are... when used without purpose).
Revision Process
  • Confusing sentences have been read aloud.
  • The subject and verb have been located in each difficult sentence.
  • Overly long sentences carrying too many ideas have been divided into two shorter sentences.
  • Every revision has been checked to confirm that no meaning was accidentally changed or lost.

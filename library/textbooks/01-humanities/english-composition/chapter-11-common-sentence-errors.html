Chapter 11 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Every writer, at every level of experience, makes sentence errors. This is not a sign of carelessness or low intelligence. It is a sign that writing is difficult, that the rules of written English are sometimes complex, and that the distance between what we mean and what we actually write is often wider than we realize. The good news is that the most common sentence errors follow predictable patterns. Once you learn to recognize those patterns, you can find and correct them reliably in your own work.

This chapter covers the eight sentence errors that appear most frequently in student academic writing: sentence fragments, run-on sentences, comma splices, subject-verb agreement errors, pronoun agreement and reference errors, shifts in verb tense, shifts in point of view, and faulty parallelism. For each error, this chapter explains what it is, why it happens, how to find it, and how to fix it. It also explains why each error matters — not as an arbitrary rule to memorize, but as a real problem that interferes with clear communication.

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to look at a paragraph you have written and identify sentence-level problems with confidence. That skill is essential not just for this course but for every piece of writing you do in your academic and professional life.

Writing Skill Focus

Identifying and correcting the eight most common sentence-level errors in academic writing: fragments, run-ons, comma splices, subject-verb disagreement, pronoun errors, tense shifts, point-of-view shifts, and faulty parallelism.

Section 11.1
Why Sentence Errors Matter
Communication · Credibility · Errors as Meaning Problems

Before diving into individual errors, it is worth taking a moment to understand why sentence correctness matters in academic writing. Some students assume that readers should look past sentence errors if the ideas are strong enough. This view is understandable, but it misses something important about how communication actually works.

When a reader encounters a fragment, a run-on, or a pronoun that seems to refer to two different things at once, the reader has to stop and figure out what the writer meant. That interruption breaks concentration, erodes confidence in the writer, and sometimes causes the reader to misunderstand the point entirely. In academic writing, sentence errors carry additional weight. Instructors, editors, and professional readers interpret serious or frequent errors as evidence that the writer has not revised carefully. The assumption — fair or not — is that a writer who submits a paper full of fragments and run-ons has not taken the work seriously enough to review it. This perception affects grades, evaluations, and professional credibility.

There is also a deeper reason to care about sentence correctness. The grammatical structure of a sentence is not decoration. Structure is meaning. When you write a sentence fragment, you are not just breaking a rule — you are leaving an idea incomplete. When you write a run-on sentence, you are forcing two distinct ideas together in a way that makes their relationship unclear. When you use a pronoun with no clear antecedent, you are asking your reader to guess. These are failures of communication, not just failures of grammar.

Study Note

This chapter addresses unintentional sentence errors — problems that weaken writing because they confuse readers or signal incomplete revision. Experienced writers sometimes use fragments or other nonstandard structures deliberately for rhetorical effect. That kind of intentional choice is a different matter entirely and requires the writer to have first mastered the standard forms.

Section 11.2
Sentence Fragments
Missing Subjects · Missing Verbs · Dependent-Clause Fragments · Finding and Fixing

What a Fragment Is

A sentence fragment is an incomplete sentence that has been punctuated as if it were complete. A complete English sentence requires three things: a subject, a verb, and an independent thought. When one or more of these elements is missing, the result is a fragment.

This sounds simple, and often it is. But fragments can be surprisingly difficult to spot, especially in your own writing, because your mind fills in the missing parts automatically. You know what you meant to say, so the fragment looks fine to you. A reader who does not already know what you meant will notice the gap immediately.

Three Types of Fragments

Fragments generally fall into three categories: those missing a subject, those missing a verb, and those that have both a subject and a verb but do not express a complete thought.

Type 1 — Missing-Subject Fragment
A sentence that begins with a verb but has no subject
  • FragmentStudied late into the night before the exam. (Who studied?)
  • FixAdd a subject: She studied late into the night before the exam.
Type 2 — Missing-Verb Fragment
A participial phrase mistaken for a complete sentence
  • FragmentThe professor standing at the front of the room. (No main verb — "standing" is a participle, not a predicate.)
  • Fix AAdd a verb: The professor was standing at the front of the room.
  • Fix BAttach to a main clause: The professor standing at the front of the room handed out the syllabus.
Type 3 — Dependent-Clause Fragment
A subordinate clause punctuated as a complete sentence
  • FragmentBecause the library closes early on Sundays. (Has a subject and verb, but "because" makes it dependent — it raises the question: so what?)
  • FixAttach a main clause: Because the library closes early on Sundays, students should plan their research accordingly.

The dependent-clause fragment is the trickiest type because it contains both a subject and a verb. The problem is the subordinating conjunction — words like because, although, when, if, since, while, unless, after, before, and even though. These words signal that the clause depends on another clause to complete its meaning. Without the main clause, the sentence is unfinished.

Dependent-clause fragments are particularly common because subordinating conjunctions feel like sentence starters. Students hear because at the beginning of sentences frequently in casual speech. In formal academic writing, a because clause must always be attached to a main clause to be complete.

Common Mistake

Writers often create fragments when they separate a piece of reasoning from the sentence it belongs with. The committee reviewed the proposal. And rejected it unanimously. The second "sentence" is a fragment — it is the continuation of the first sentence, incorrectly separated by a period. Correct: The committee reviewed the proposal and rejected it unanimously.

How to Find and Fix Fragments

Fragment Detection Strategy

Read your paper backward — from the last sentence to the first, one sentence at a time. This breaks the forward momentum of reading and forces you to evaluate each sentence independently.

For each sentence, ask:

  • Does this sentence have a subject?
  • Does this sentence have a complete verb?
  • Can this sentence stand alone as a complete thought?

If the answer to any question is no, you have a fragment.

Section 11.3
Run-On Sentences
Definition · Causes · Four Correction Strategies

What a Run-On Sentence Is

A run-on sentence occurs when two or more independent clauses are joined without any punctuation or conjunction to signal where one clause ends and another begins. The clauses simply collide, leaving the reader to sort out where the first thought stops and the second begins.

The term run-on sometimes confuses students who assume it simply means a very long sentence. Length alone does not make a sentence a run-on. A sentence can be long and perfectly correct. What makes a run-on is the absence of proper connection between independent clauses.

Run-On
The experiment produced unexpected results the researchers decided to repeat the trial.

This example contains two independent clauses: The experiment produced unexpected results and the researchers decided to repeat the trial. They have been fused together with no punctuation and no conjunction. The reader hits the end of the first clause and must guess where it ends and the second begins.

Run-ons typically occur when a writer is thinking quickly and moving through ideas faster than the punctuation is being applied. They are also common in early drafts, when writers are focused on capturing ideas rather than polishing sentences. The problem arises when a writer submits the early draft without revision.

Four Strategies for Correcting Run-Ons

Strategy 1 — Use a Period

Separate the two independent clauses into two complete sentences. This is the simplest solution and often the best one.

The experiment produced unexpected results. The researchers decided to repeat the trial.
Strategy 2 — Use a Semicolon

A semicolon connects two closely related independent clauses while signaling they are distinct thoughts. Use it when the two clauses are closely connected in meaning and roughly equal in grammatical weight.

The experiment produced unexpected results; the researchers decided to repeat the trial.
Strategy 3 — Use a Comma + Coordinating Conjunction

The seven coordinating conjunctions — for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so — join two independent clauses when preceded by a comma.

The experiment produced unexpected results, so the researchers decided to repeat the trial.
Strategy 4 — Use a Subordinating Conjunction

Turn one independent clause into a dependent clause. This works especially well when one idea explains or conditions the other.

Because the experiment produced unexpected results, the researchers decided to repeat the trial.
Choosing the Right Strategy

If the two ideas are equally important, a semicolon or coordinating conjunction works well. If one idea explains or conditions the other, a subordinating conjunction often produces a clearer sentence. The choice should reflect the relationship between the ideas, not convenience.

Section 11.4
Comma Splices
Why the Comma Alone Fails · Correction Strategies · Distinguishing from Correct Sentences

What a Comma Splice Is

A comma splice occurs when two independent clauses are joined with only a comma — without a coordinating conjunction. Unlike the run-on, which has no punctuation at all, the comma splice has punctuation; the problem is that the comma alone is not strong enough to connect two independent clauses.

Comma Splice
The report was due on Friday, the student submitted it on Sunday.

Both The report was due on Friday and the student submitted it on Sunday are independent clauses. A comma alone cannot hold them together.

The comma splice is probably the most frequent punctuation error in student writing. Many students sense that their two clauses are related — which they often are — and the comma feels like an appropriate way to connect them. The instinct to connect related ideas is a good one; the tool (the comma alone) is simply not strong enough for the job. A comma signals a short pause within a sentence, not the junction of two complete sentences.

How to Fix Comma Splices

The corrections for comma splices are identical to those for run-ons. The difference is that the comma splice already has a comma, so one fix involves building on that comma.

StrategyCorrected Version
Period — separate into two sentencesThe report was due on Friday. The student submitted it on Sunday.
Coordinating conjunction — add after the commaThe report was due on Friday, but the student submitted it on Sunday.
Semicolon — replace the commaThe report was due on Friday; the student submitted it on Sunday.
Subordination — make one clause dependentAlthough the report was due on Friday, the student did not submit it until Sunday.
Test: Comma Splice or Correct Sentence?

Find the comma in question. Check what comes immediately after it. If a coordinating conjunction (for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so) follows the comma, the sentence is correctly formed. If no conjunction follows and both parts could stand alone, you have a comma splice.

Section 11.5
Subject-Verb Agreement
Intervening Phrases · Compound Subjects · Indefinite Pronouns · Collective Nouns · Inverted Order

What Subject-Verb Agreement Means

Subject-verb agreement is the principle that a verb must match its subject in number: a singular subject takes a singular verb, and a plural subject takes a plural verb. This is one of the fundamental conventions of English grammar, and violations frequently attract the attention of academic readers.

In many sentences, agreement is automatic. The dog barks and The dogs bark sound right and wrong without any conscious effort. The difficulty arises in sentences where the subject is harder to identify, where intervening words obscure the subject-verb relationship, or where the rules are genuinely counterintuitive.

Five Common Agreement Errors

Error Type 1 — Intervening Phrases
The verb agrees with the nearest noun instead of the actual subject
  • ErrorThe stack of papers on the instructor's desk were graded.
  • SubjectThe subject is stack (singular), not papers. Remove the intervening phrase mentally: The stack … was graded.
  • CorrectedThe stack of papers on the instructor's desk was graded.
Key: Phrases beginning with "of," "with," "along with," "together with," and similar words do not change the number of the subject.
Error Type 2 — Compound Subjects
Subjects joined by "or" or "nor" follow the nearest-subject rule
  • And → PluralThe professor and the teaching assistant grade the exams.
  • Or/Nor → NearestNeither the students nor the professor was prepared for the power outage.
  • Or/Nor → NearestNeither the professor nor the students were prepared for the power outage.
When subjects are joined by "or" or "nor," the verb agrees with the subject closest to it.
Error Type 3 — Indefinite Pronouns
Many indefinite pronouns are singular despite referring to groups
  • Always Singularanyone, everyone, someone, no one, anybody, everybody, somebody, nobody, each, either, neither, one, another, much, little
  • ErrorEveryone in both sections have submitted their assignment.
  • CorrectedEveryone in both sections has submitted their assignment.
  • Context-DependentAll, any, none, some, most can be singular or plural depending on what they refer to: Most of the water has evaporated. / Most of the students have arrived.
Error Type 4 — Collective Nouns
Words like "team," "committee," and "faculty" are typically singular
  • SingularThe committee has reached a decision. (Acting as a unit)
  • SingularThe team is preparing for the tournament.
Treat collective nouns as singular in American English unless the sentence specifically emphasizes members acting independently.
Error Type 5 — Subject After the Verb
In "There is / There are" constructions, the subject follows the verb
  • ErrorThere is several reasons for the delay.
  • SubjectThe subject is reasons (plural). "There" is never the subject.
  • CorrectedThere are several reasons for the delay.

Section 11.6
Pronoun Agreement and Reference
Pronoun-Antecedent Agreement · Ambiguous Reference · Vague Reference · Missing Antecedents

Two Related Problems

This section addresses two distinct but related pronoun problems: pronoun agreement (when a pronoun does not match its antecedent in number or person) and pronoun reference (when it is unclear which noun a pronoun refers to). Both problems confuse readers and weaken the clarity of academic prose.

A pronoun is a word that stands in for a noun. The noun it refers to is called its antecedent. Pronoun agreement requires that the pronoun match its antecedent in number (singular or plural), person (first, second, or third), and sometimes gender.

Pronoun Agreement

Agreement with Indefinite Pronouns
The most common pronoun agreement error
  • ErrorEach student must submit their essay by midnight. (Each is singular; their is plural.)
  • Option AEach student must submit his or her essay by midnight. (Grammatically correct but can feel awkward in repeated use.)
  • Option B — BestRewrite to use a plural subject: All students must submit their essays by midnight.
The singular "they" is now accepted by major style guides for reference to individuals when gender is unknown or irrelevant. The cleanest fix, however, is often to revise the subject to plural.

Three Types of Reference Errors

Reference Error 1 — Ambiguous Reference
The pronoun could refer to more than one antecedent
  • AmbiguousWhen the professor met with the student, she seemed unprepared. (Who seemed unprepared — the professor or the student?)
  • ClarifiedWhen the professor met with the student, the student seemed unprepared.
Reference Error 2 — Vague Reference
"This," "that," "which," or "it" refers to a general idea rather than a specific noun
  • VagueThe data were incomplete, the methodology was unclear, and the conclusions were not well-supported. This undermined the study's credibility.
  • ClarifiedThe data were incomplete, the methodology was unclear, and the conclusions were not well-supported. These weaknesses undermined the study's credibility.
Replace vague "this," "that," and "which" with a specific noun phrase that names what is being summarized.
Reference Error 3 — Missing Antecedent
The pronoun has no clear referent at all
  • MissingIn the article, they argue that climate change is accelerating. (Who is "they"?)
  • CorrectedIn the article, the authors argue that climate change is accelerating.

Section 11.7
Tense and Point-of-View Shifts
Inconsistent Tense · Literary Present · Point of View · Avoiding "You"

Inconsistent Verb Tense

Verb tense tells readers when an action or state occurred. Academic writing typically establishes a tense at the outset — often past tense for narrating completed events, present tense for discussing texts or findings — and then maintains that tense consistently unless there is a logical reason to shift.

An inconsistent tense shift occurs when a writer changes tense without a meaningful reason, confusing the reader about the timeline of events.

Inconsistent Tense
The committee reviewed the proposal and decides to table it until further evidence was available.
Corrected
The committee reviewed the proposal and decided to table it until further evidence was available.
The Literary Present Tense

In literary analysis, academic convention calls for the literary present tense: authors and characters speak and act in the present tense even if the work was written centuries ago.

Conventional: In Great Expectations, Dickens uses Pip's first-person narration to examine class mobility in Victorian England.

Switching between literary present and past tense within the same literary analysis is a very common error and should be corrected during revision.

Shifts in Point of View

Point of view in academic writing refers to the grammatical person — first person (I, we), second person (you), or third person (he, she, they, one). Academic writing generally maintains a consistent point of view throughout a piece.

An inconsistent shift in point of view occurs when the writer moves between grammatical persons without a clear reason.

Mixed Point of View
A student who manages time well will succeed in this program. You need to set clear priorities and make a schedule. They also benefit from regular check-ins with advisors.
Corrected — Consistent Third Person
Students who manage time well tend to succeed in this program. They should set clear priorities, make a schedule, and meet regularly with advisors.
Avoiding the Second-Person "You" in Academic Writing

Academic prose generally avoids you because it makes claims about the reader that the writer cannot verify and can feel presumptuous or informal.

Informal: When you read this study, you can see that the results are surprising.
Academic: The results of the study are surprising.
Academic: Readers who examine this study will find the results surprising.

Section 11.8
Faulty Parallelism
Parallel Structure in Lists · Correlative Conjunctions · Thesis Statements · Finding and Correcting

What Parallelism Is

Parallelism — also called parallel structure — is the principle that items in a series or paired elements in a sentence should be grammatically equivalent. When sentences list things or compare ideas, all the items in the list or comparison should be in the same grammatical form. Nouns should be paired with nouns, verbs with verbs, phrases with phrases, and clauses with clauses.

Parallelism is one of the most powerful tools in academic writing because it creates clarity, balance, and rhythm. A parallel sentence is easier to read because its structure signals relationships between ideas. When parallelism breaks down, readers sense the imbalance even if they cannot immediately name what is wrong.

Faulty parallelism errors typically occur when a writer builds a list phrase by phrase without returning to check that all items are grammatically consistent. The writer may add a final item in a different form because they are thinking about the content rather than the structure.

Three Types of Faulty Parallelism

Type 1 — Items in a Simple List
All items in a series must use the same grammatical form
  • FaultyThe course requires reading primary texts, writing analytical essays, and to participate in weekly discussion.
  • ProblemThe first two items are gerund phrases (reading, writing). The third shifts to an infinitive (to participate).
  • CorrectedThe course requires reading primary texts, writing analytical essays, and participating in weekly discussion.
Type 2 — Correlative Conjunctions
"Both...and," "either...or," "not only...but also," "neither...nor," "whether...or"
  • FaultyThe professor emphasized not only that students read the assignment but also taking notes during lecture.
  • ProblemThat students read is a noun clause. Taking notes is a gerund phrase. They are not equivalent.
  • CorrectedThe professor emphasized not only that students read the assignment but also that they take notes during lecture.
Rule: Both halves of a correlative conjunction pair must connect grammatically equivalent structures.
Type 3 — Thesis Statements and Formal Claims
All claims in a list must be grammatically equivalent
  • FaultyThis paper argues that immigration policy needs reform, the economy benefits from diverse labor, and discrimination in hiring practices.
  • ProblemThe first two items are that-clauses making complete claims. The third is a noun phrase with no verb — it expresses no actual claim.
  • CorrectedThis paper argues that immigration policy needs reform, that the economy benefits from diverse labor, and that discrimination in hiring must be addressed through stronger enforcement.
How to Find and Fix Parallelism Errors

Identify every series and every paired comparison in your writing. For each one, write out the items separately and check whether they are in the same grammatical form. If they are not, revise them until they match. The simplest fix is to choose the grammatical form of the first item and rewrite all others to match it.

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 11.1Sentence errors matter because they interfere with communication. Structure is meaning: a fragment leaves an idea incomplete; a run-on obscures relationships between ideas; a vague pronoun forces readers to guess. Errors also signal inadequate revision, affecting academic credibility.
  • Sec 11.2Sentence fragments are incomplete sentences punctuated as complete ones. The three types are missing-subject fragments, missing-verb fragments (often participial phrases), and dependent-clause fragments. Corrections involve adding the missing element or attaching the fragment to an adjacent sentence.
  • Sec 11.3Run-on sentences fuse two or more independent clauses without any punctuation or conjunction. The four correction strategies are: period, semicolon, comma plus coordinating conjunction, and subordinating conjunction. The best choice depends on the logical relationship between the ideas.
  • Sec 11.4Comma splices join two independent clauses with only a comma. Corrections are identical to those for run-ons, with the added option of inserting a coordinating conjunction after the existing comma. A correctly formed compound sentence uses a comma followed by a coordinating conjunction.
  • Sec 11.5Subject-verb agreement errors occur when a verb does not match its subject in number. Common causes include intervening phrases, compound subjects joined by or/nor, singular indefinite pronoun subjects, collective nouns, and subjects that follow the verb in "there is/are" constructions.
  • Sec 11.6Pronoun agreement errors occur when a pronoun does not match its antecedent in number or person. Pronoun reference errors occur when the antecedent is ambiguous, vague, or missing. Both are corrected by naming the antecedent explicitly or restructuring the sentence so the reference is unambiguous.
  • Sec 11.7Inconsistent tense shifts disrupt the reader's sense of timeline. Literary analysis uses the literary present tense throughout. Point-of-view shifts move inconsistently between first, second, and third person. Academic prose generally avoids the second-person "you" and maintains consistent grammatical person.
  • Sec 11.8Faulty parallelism occurs when items in a series or paired comparison are not in equivalent grammatical forms. Parallel structure must be maintained in lists, correlative conjunction pairs, and thesis statements. The fix is to identify the grammatical form of the first item and rewrite all others to match it.

Key Terms Review

antecedent
The noun or noun phrase to which a pronoun refers. Every pronoun must have a clear, identifiable antecedent.
comma splice
A punctuation error in which two independent clauses are joined with only a comma and no coordinating conjunction.
dependent clause
A group of words with a subject and a verb but unable to stand alone as a sentence because it begins with a subordinating conjunction or relative pronoun.
faulty parallelism
A structural error in which items in a series or paired comparison are not in equivalent grammatical forms.
sentence fragment
An incomplete sentence that has been punctuated as if it were complete; missing a subject, a verb, or an independent thought.
independent clause
A group of words with a subject and a verb that can stand alone as a complete sentence.
parallel structure
The use of grammatically equivalent forms for items in a series or paired comparisons. Also called parallelism.
point of view
The grammatical person used in writing: first person (I/we), second person (you), or third person (he/she/they/one).
pronoun agreement
The requirement that a pronoun match its antecedent in number, person, and (where relevant) gender.
pronoun reference
The clarity with which a pronoun points to its antecedent. A reference error occurs when the antecedent is missing, ambiguous, or too distant.
run-on sentence
Two or more independent clauses joined without appropriate punctuation or a conjunction.
subject-verb agreement
The grammatical requirement that a verb match its subject in number: singular subjects take singular verbs; plural subjects take plural verbs.
subordinating conjunction
A word (because, although, when, since, unless, while, etc.) that introduces a dependent clause and signals its relationship to the main clause.
tense shift
An inconsistent change in verb tense that does not reflect a meaningful change in time frame.
literary present tense
The convention in literary analysis of discussing authors and characters in the present tense, regardless of when the work was written.
correlative conjunction
Paired conjunctions (both…and, either…or, not only…but also, neither…nor, whether…or) that must connect grammatically equivalent structures.

Chapter 11 Checklist

Use this checklist when reviewing any piece of academic writing for sentence-level errors.

Fragments
  • Does every sentence have a subject?
  • Does every sentence have a complete verb?
  • Does every sentence express a complete thought that can stand alone?
  • Have I checked sentences beginning with because, although, when, since, while, and other subordinating conjunctions to make sure they are attached to main clauses?
Run-On Sentences and Comma Splices
  • Have I made sure that every junction of two independent clauses uses appropriate punctuation (period, semicolon, or comma plus coordinating conjunction)?
  • Have I checked every comma to confirm that when it joins two independent clauses, a coordinating conjunction follows?
Subject-Verb Agreement
  • Have I identified the subject and verb of each sentence and confirmed they match in number?
  • Have I checked sentences with phrases between the subject and verb to make sure I agreed with the actual subject, not the nearest noun?
  • Have I applied the correct rules for compound subjects, indefinite pronouns, and collective nouns?
  • In "there is/are" constructions, have I checked what the actual subject is and matched the verb to it?
Pronoun Agreement and Reference
  • Does every pronoun agree with its antecedent in number and person?
  • Does every pronoun have a clear, identifiable antecedent?
  • Have I replaced vague uses of this, that, which, and it with specific noun phrases where the reference was unclear?
Tense and Point of View
  • Does my paper maintain consistent verb tense, with shifts only when there is a logical reason to change time frame?
  • Does my paper maintain a consistent grammatical person (first, second, or third) throughout?
  • If I am writing about a literary text, have I used the literary present tense consistently?
  • Have I avoided inappropriate use of the second-person you?
Parallel Structure
  • Have I checked all lists and series to confirm that every item is in the same grammatical form?
  • Have I checked all correlative conjunction pairs (not only…but also, both…and, etc.) to confirm that the elements they connect are grammatically equivalent?
  • Have I checked my thesis statement and formal claims for parallel structure?

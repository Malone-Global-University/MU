Chapter 28 — Front Matter

Chapter Overview and Learning Objectives

One of the most reliable signs of a strong academic argument is not the confidence with which a writer states a position. It is the willingness to engage honestly with positions that challenge it. Writers who acknowledge only the evidence and reasoning that supports their own claims produce arguments that feel thin, one-sided, and ultimately untrustworthy. Writers who take opposing views seriously, represent them fairly, and respond to them thoughtfully produce arguments that feel intellectually rigorous and worth reading.

This chapter teaches the skill of counterargument: identifying the views, objections, and evidence that complicate or oppose your argument, and then responding to them in a way that strengthens your position rather than weakening it. It also teaches related skills — concession, rebuttal, qualification, and synthesis — that allow writers to handle complexity without abandoning their central claims.

Counterargument is not a sign of weakness. It is a sign that a writer understands how academic thinking works: through debate, challenge, revision, and the honest examination of competing ideas. Learning to use counterargument well will make every argument you write more credible, more complete, and more persuasive.

Sections 28.1 – 28.2
The Case for Counterargument and How to Find It
Why It Matters · Identifying Opposing Views · The Skeptical Reader · Complicating Evidence
Section 28.1

Why Counterargument Matters

Academic argument exists in a world of multiple perspectives. Almost any question worth arguing about has been considered by many people, and those people have not always reached the same conclusions. When you write an academic argument, you are not writing in isolation. You are entering a conversation that is already in progress, and that conversation includes voices that disagree with your position.

Ignoring those voices does not make your argument stronger. It makes your argument incomplete. Readers who are familiar with a topic will notice when you have overlooked important objections. Readers who are not familiar with a topic will trust you less when they eventually discover that your presentation was one-sided. Either way, avoiding counterargument costs you credibility.

There is a deeper reason counterargument matters, beyond the practical concern of how readers respond. Academic thinking is built on the principle that ideas improve through challenge. When a scientist publishes a finding, other scientists look for errors, alternative explanations, and disconfirming evidence. When a historian proposes an interpretation, other historians test it against different sources and frameworks. When a philosopher develops an argument, other philosophers probe its premises and look for internal contradictions. This process of challenge and response is how knowledge advances.

Writing that takes counterargument seriously participates in this process. It shows that you have genuinely thought about the problem, not just assembled evidence that confirms what you already believed. It shows that you understand the difference between a question and an answer, between a claim and a proof, between one interpretation and the only possible interpretation.

From a purely persuasive standpoint, counterargument also works. Rhetoric and composition scholarship has long observed that writers who acknowledge opposing views are often more persuasive, not less, because they appear reasonable, fair, and trustworthy. A reader who might have objected to your argument is more likely to reconsider if you have already acknowledged the objection and addressed it thoughtfully. A reader who agrees with you is more confident in your position when they see that you have considered the alternatives.

Why This Matters

Counterargument is not a concession to defeat. It is evidence of intellectual honesty. When you engage fairly with opposing views, you demonstrate that your argument has survived scrutiny — and that makes it worth taking seriously.

Section 28.2

Identifying Opposing Views

Before you can respond to opposing views, you need to find them. This requires a kind of active intellectual honesty: you must genuinely ask yourself what someone who disagrees with you might think, and you must take that question seriously rather than dismissing it.

There are several productive ways to identify the opposing views relevant to your argument.

Question to Ask YourselfWhat It Helps You Find
What would a skeptical reader question?Unsupported assumptions, missing evidence, weak reasoning — places where the argument is most vulnerable
What evidence points in a different direction?Data, cases, or studies that complicate the picture your argument presents
Who disagrees, and why?Established scholarly or public disagreements; the strongest version of the opposing position
What does my argument assume?Premises that some readers will not share, and that therefore invite challenge
What am I least certain about?The thinner or less well-supported parts of your argument — the natural landing zones for opposing views

The imagined skeptical reader is one of the most useful tools a writer has. Imagining a thoughtful person who has not yet been persuaded — and asking what they would question — helps you identify the gaps and vulnerabilities in your argument before your actual readers do. This is not the same as imagining an unreasonable adversary who rejects everything you say. The skeptical reader is intellectually honest, expects evidence, and asks fair questions.

Not every counterargument you identify will need to appear in your essay at full length. Some objections are too minor to address extensively; others are central to the argument and require careful treatment. Part of the skill of writing counterargument is judgment: knowing which opposing views are significant enough to address and how much space they deserve.

Academic Transfer

In history, identifying opposing views means recognizing that other historians have interpreted the same evidence differently. In science, it means acknowledging alternative hypotheses. In social science, it means engaging with studies that produced different results. The skill of finding counterarguments is discipline-specific in its details but universal in its purpose.

Section 28.3
Representing Opposing Views Fairly
Accuracy · Signal Phrases · Context · Avoiding Caricature · The Strongest Version
Section 28.3

Representing Opposing Views Fairly

Once you have identified the opposing views relevant to your argument, you face a critical responsibility: you must represent those views accurately. This means presenting the opposing position as its actual supporters would recognize it — not as a simplified or distorted version that is easy to dismiss.

Fair representation begins with understanding. You cannot accurately represent a view you have not genuinely tried to understand. This means reading and considering the strongest version of the opposing argument, not just the weakest or most extreme formulation you can find. If you are arguing against a position that many thoughtful people hold, those thoughtful people presumably have reasons that make sense to them. Your job is to understand those reasons well enough to state them clearly.

Practices That Support Fair Representation

Use accurate language. Do not characterize an opposing view with language its supporters would reject. If critics of a policy argue that it causes economic instability, do not say they argue that the policy is reckless or incompetent unless those are their actual words and they are being used in context. Loaded language distorts opposing views and signals to readers that you are not engaging honestly.

Present the strongest form of the opposing argument. There is usually a weaker version and a stronger version of any position. The weaker version has obvious flaws; the stronger version has genuine force. Academic writers are expected to engage with the stronger version. Engaging only with the weakest formulation of an opposing view is a form of intellectual dishonesty, and careful readers will notice.

Use signal phrases that attribute views accurately. When presenting an opposing view, use phrases that make clear you are describing a position, not endorsing it. Signal phrases allow you to present opposing views clearly without confusing them with your own position.

Give opposing views appropriate context. A view lifted out of context can look different from what its proponents intend. When representing an opposing position, give enough context for the reader to understand what the argument is actually claiming and why its supporters find it convincing.

Do not caricature. A caricature is a distorted, exaggerated version of a view designed to make it look ridiculous. Caricatures are intellectually dishonest and rhetorically counterproductive. Readers who know the subject will spot the distortion. Readers who do not will learn a false version of the opposing position, which does not serve academic inquiry.

Signal Phrases for Introducing Opposing Views

The following phrases are useful for presenting opposing views without endorsing them. Choose the one that fits your context most accurately.

Some scholars argue that…
Critics of this position contend that…
A common objection to this view holds that…
Opponents of this policy suggest…
It might be argued that…
One counterargument worth addressing is…
Those who disagree often point to…
From a different perspective, one might argue…

Fair representation is not only an ethical requirement. It is a rhetorical advantage. When you represent opposing views accurately and generously, you demonstrate that you understand the full scope of the issue. That demonstration builds your credibility with readers who might otherwise suspect you of cherry-picking.

Sections 28.4 – 28.5
Concession, Rebuttal, and the Full Counterargument Move
Types of Concession · Rebuttal Strategies · The Acknowledge-Concede-Rebut Structure
Section 28.4

Concession

Concession is the act of acknowledging that an opposing view has genuine merit. It is the intellectual honesty of saying: this point is fair, this evidence is real, this concern deserves acknowledgment. Concession does not mean abandoning your argument. It means acknowledging what is true or reasonable in an opposing position before explaining why your argument still holds.

Concession is one of the most powerful tools in academic argument because it is unexpected. Readers approach arguments defensively, anticipating that the writer will dismiss everything that challenges their position. When a writer instead says, in effect, "you have a point here, and I want to acknowledge it honestly," that acknowledgment disarms the reader's defenses and builds trust.

Type of ConcessionWhat You Are AcknowledgingExample
Conceding a factThe opposing view rests on accurate information you cannot dispute"It is true that implementation costs exceeded initial projections."
Conceding a logical pointThe opposing argument is coherent, even if you disagree with its conclusion"This line of reasoning is internally sound, but it applies only under limited conditions."
Conceding a valueThe concern motivating the opposing position reflects genuine and legitimate values"The concern for individual liberty here is legitimate and deserves acknowledgment."
Conceding uncertaintyThe evidence is genuinely mixed or the question is genuinely difficult"The research on this question is not conclusive, and reasonable disagreement remains."

Concession typically appears before the rebuttal that follows it. A common structure is: acknowledge the strength of the opposing view → grant what is true or fair in it → explain why your argument remains stronger despite this concession. This structure shows readers that you have engaged honestly with the challenge and that your argument has survived it.

Example — Concession in Academic Prose
Acknowledging a genuine problem without abandoning the argument

"Critics of this policy argue, with some justification, that its implementation costs have been higher than initial projections suggested. This is a fair point, and the data on implementation expenses are not easily dismissed."

Notice that this passage does not abandon the argument. It acknowledges a genuine problem before the writer goes on to explain why the policy remains worth supporting despite that problem. The concession is specific ("implementation costs"), not vague ("there are some concerns").

Section 28.5

Rebuttal

Rebuttal is the response to an opposing view. After identifying and fairly representing an opposing argument, and after conceding whatever is genuinely fair in it, the writer explains why the opposing view does not ultimately undermine the main argument.

Rebuttal is not simply denial. Saying "this view is wrong" is not a rebuttal. A genuine rebuttal provides reasons, evidence, and reasoning that demonstrate why the opposing view is less convincing than it might appear — or why it does not apply to the specific case being argued, or why it leads to conclusions that are less defensible than the writer's own position.

Rebuttal Strategies

Challenging the evidence. An opposing view may rest on evidence that is incomplete, outdated, methodologically flawed, or misinterpreted. If you can show that the evidence does not actually support the conclusion drawn from it, you have offered a substantive rebuttal. This requires engaging with the evidence carefully rather than dismissing it.

Challenging the reasoning. Even if the evidence is accurate, the reasoning that connects evidence to conclusion may be flawed. An opposing view might assume a causal relationship that has not been established, or it might generalize from a limited sample, or it might ignore relevant alternative explanations. Identifying and explaining the flaw in the reasoning is a form of rebuttal.

Challenging the scope of the claim. An opposing view might be valid in some contexts but not in the specific context you are discussing. Explaining the limited scope of the opposing argument — that it applies to general patterns but not to the specific case at hand — is a legitimate rebuttal strategy.

Offering counterevidence. The most direct rebuttal is evidence that points in the opposite direction from what the opposing view claims. If an opposing argument rests on the premise that a particular cause produces a particular effect, evidence that the effect occurs without the cause — or that the cause occurs without the effect — directly challenges that premise.

Showing that the concession does not undermine the main argument. Sometimes the most effective rebuttal is simply explaining why the point you have conceded, while genuinely fair, does not change the overall conclusion. The opposing view might identify a real problem or a real consideration, but one that the main argument can accommodate or that does not outweigh the evidence on the other side.

The Acknowledge-Concede-Rebut Structure

1. Introduce the opposing view with a fair signal phrase.

2. Represent the opposing view fully and accurately.

3. Concede what is genuinely valid in the opposing view.

4. Rebut what is not sufficient to overturn your argument — with evidence and reasoning.

5. Reconnect to your main claim.

Example — Full Counterargument Move
Acknowledge-concede-rebut in a complete paragraph

"Some researchers contend that the evidence linking early reading programs to long-term academic achievement is weaker than advocates suggest, pointing to studies where gains made in structured reading instruction during early elementary school diminished significantly by the third or fourth grade. This concern deserves serious consideration, and the studies in question are not negligible."

"However, the research these critics cite primarily examines programs that were discontinued after the initial intervention period, without ongoing support or reinforcement. When early reading programs are paired with consistent follow-up instruction, the evidence of lasting benefit is considerably stronger. The concern is legitimate, but it argues for better program design and continuity, not for abandoning early reading instruction altogether."

Examine what this example accomplishes at each stage. It introduces the opposing view without distortion ("some researchers contend"). It acknowledges the seriousness of the objection ("this concern deserves serious consideration"). It offers a specific, evidence-based rebuttal rather than a simple dismissal. And it ends not by restating the original position unchanged, but by refining it — the argument now includes an important qualification about program design. This is how genuine engagement with counterargument strengthens an argument rather than weakening it.

Signal Phrases for the Rebuttal Turn

However…
Nevertheless…
This concern is genuine, but…
While this point deserves acknowledgment…
Even granting this…
The evidence suggests, however, that…
This objection applies to a different set of conditions…
A closer examination reveals…

Section 28.6

Qualification and Nuance

Qualification is the practice of limiting or narrowing a claim to make it more accurate. Academic claims are often qualified because the full, unqualified version would be an overstatement. Qualification is a sign of intellectual honesty, not weakness.

Writers qualify claims in several ways. Using qualifying language — words like often, frequently, typically, in many cases, under certain conditions, and for the most part — acknowledges that a claim does not apply universally. Phrases like the evidence suggests, it appears that, one interpretation is, and this analysis indicates qualify the certainty with which a conclusion is stated. These are not signs of vagueness. They are signs of precision.

Limiting the scope of a claim is another form of qualification. Rather than arguing that something is true in all cases, you might argue that it is true in a particular set of cases, during a particular historical period, in a particular cultural context, or under a particular set of conditions. Limiting the scope makes the claim more accurate and harder to refute.

Acknowledging exceptions is closely related. A qualified claim explicitly notes that exceptions exist. If you argue that a particular pattern appears consistently in the research, you might add that several studies have found exceptions in specific populations or circumstances. Acknowledging exceptions shows that you have engaged with the full range of the evidence.

Sometimes the most useful qualification is explaining what you are not arguing. If readers might assume your argument is stronger than it is, clarifying the actual scope of your claim prevents misunderstanding. "This argument does not claim that X is always the case; it argues that X is more likely under the conditions described here" is a useful move when overinterpretation is a risk.

Common Mistake — Confusing Qualification with Weakness

Many students assume that a writer who qualifies claims is uncertain or uncommitted. The opposite is usually true. Unqualified claims are often overstatements that knowledgeable readers will reject. Qualified claims are more defensible and, paradoxically, often more persuasive — because they signal that the writer knows the limits of the evidence.

Example — Unqualified vs. Qualified Claims
How qualification improves precision without weakening the argument

Unqualified: "Social media causes depression in teenagers."

Qualified: "Research consistently links heavy social media use to elevated rates of depressive symptoms among adolescents, particularly among users who engage primarily in passive consumption rather than active communication."

The qualified version is more accurate, more defensible, and more informative. It does not abandon the core claim; it sharpens it.

Section 28.7

Avoiding the Straw Man Fallacy

The straw man fallacy occurs when a writer distorts, exaggerates, or misrepresents an opposing view to make it easier to dismiss. The term comes from the image of building a figure out of straw — something that looks like an opponent but has none of the substance of the real position — and then defeating it triumphantly while the actual argument stands untouched.

Straw man arguments are a form of intellectual dishonesty. They misrepresent the actual debate, mislead readers, and fail to address the genuine challenge posed by opposing views. They are also rhetorically self-defeating: readers who know the subject will recognize the distortion and lose confidence in the writer's credibility.

Form of Straw ManWhat It DoesExample
Exaggerating the opposing viewAmplifies the opposing position beyond what its supporters actually claimA critic notes limitations of a policy; the writer responds as if the critic called the policy entirely useless
Selecting the weakest versionIgnores the strongest objections and addresses only minor or fringe criticismsEngaging with an extreme critic while ignoring the mainstream scholarly objection
Attributing extreme positions to moderate criticsTreats cautious objection as radical oppositionA critic urges gradual reform; the writer accuses them of opposing reform entirely
Using loaded languageCharacterizes the opposing view negatively before engaging with it"Those who foolishly believe that…" rather than "Those who argue that…"

Avoiding the straw man fallacy requires returning to the principles of fair representation discussed in Section 28.3. Before responding to an opposing view, ask yourself: would the people who hold this view recognize my description of it? If the answer is no, revise your characterization before proceeding with your response.

This practice is sometimes called the principle of charitable reading: before critiquing a view, interpret it in its strongest and most reasonable form. Engaging with the strongest version of an opposing argument rather than the weakest is both the ethical and the strategically sound choice. If you can defeat the strongest version of the opposing position, your argument is genuinely stronger. If you can only defeat a distorted version, the real challenge remains unaddressed.

Study Note

The straw man fallacy is one of the most common errors in student argument essays. It often appears not because students are trying to deceive, but because misrepresenting an opposing view is easier than genuinely engaging with it. Taking the time to understand and accurately represent the strongest version of an opposing argument is one of the most important habits academic writers can develop.

Section 28.8

Integrating Counterargument into Essays

Knowing how to handle counterargument in principle is one thing. Knowing where and how to integrate it into an actual essay is another. Counterargument does not belong in just one place. It can appear at multiple points in an essay, and the best placement depends on the essay's structure, the nature of the opposing views, and the rhetorical effect the writer is aiming for.

Placement Options

Before the thesis. Some writers introduce and respond to the most significant counterargument in the introduction, before stating their own thesis. This approach acknowledges the complexity of the issue immediately and frames the essay as a response to an existing debate. It works particularly well when the counterargument is widely held or likely to be the reader's initial position. The structure moves from: establish the issue → note a widely held opposing view → acknowledge what is legitimate about it → transition to a more specific or different argument → state the thesis.

After the thesis, before the main argument. Some essays introduce the main counterargument early, right after the thesis, to signal immediately that the writer is aware of the challenge and plans to address it. This approach prepares the reader for a more nuanced argument and establishes the writer's credibility before the evidence has been fully laid out.

Within body paragraphs. The most flexible approach integrates counterargument at the specific points where it is most relevant. When a particular claim in the argument is most vulnerable to a particular objection, address that objection in or immediately after the paragraph making the claim. This approach keeps counterargument close to the reasoning it complicates, which is often the clearest presentation for the reader.

In a dedicated section. Longer essays — especially research essays and argumentative papers — sometimes include a full section or cluster of paragraphs devoted to counterargument. This approach works well when the opposing view is substantial and requires extended treatment. The section typically appears after the main argument has been developed, so readers are already familiar with the writer's position when the counterargument is introduced.

In the conclusion. Some writers return to counterargument in the conclusion to acknowledge remaining complexity, note what questions are still open, or suggest that the debate continues beyond the scope of the essay. This approach is honest about the limits of any single argument and positions the essay within a larger ongoing conversation.

Placement Principle

There is no single correct place for counterargument. The most important principle is that it should appear where it is most logically connected to the argument it complicates, and where it will be most rhetorically effective for the intended reader. Let the logic of your argument guide the placement.

A Model Counterargument Passage

The following passage demonstrates a complete counterargument move in an essay about the value of requiring students to study a foreign language in secondary school. Read it carefully, then examine how each stage of the acknowledge-concede-rebut structure functions.

Model Passage — Counterargument in Academic Writing

Not everyone agrees that mandatory foreign language study is the best use of limited instructional time. Some educators and policy analysts argue that the resources devoted to language instruction would produce greater academic benefit if redirected toward subjects more directly connected to university preparation and career readiness, such as additional mathematics, laboratory science, or technical writing. This argument reflects a genuine concern. Students face serious academic pressures, and there is legitimate debate about how a finite school day can best serve their development. The concern deserves a direct response rather than dismissal.

However, the argument that foreign language instruction crowds out more valuable subjects rests on a false premise: that language learning is separable from the cognitive and communicative development students need across all subjects. Research in applied linguistics and educational psychology has consistently found that students who develop proficiency in a second language demonstrate measurable advantages in working memory, metalinguistic awareness, and the ability to monitor their own comprehension — skills that transfer to mathematics, reading, and analytical writing. The question, then, is not whether language instruction competes with rigorous academic preparation, but whether we recognize that language learning is itself a form of rigorous cognitive training. Redirecting language instruction hours would not solve the problem of limited instructional time; it would simply exchange one form of intellectual development for another while closing off capacities that the other subjects cannot as reliably cultivate.

Examine what this passage does at each stage. It introduces the opposing view clearly and without distortion ("some educators and policy analysts argue"). It presents the opposing argument in its own terms, giving it genuine weight. It concedes the genuine concern behind it ("this argument reflects a genuine concern…the concern deserves a direct response"). Its rebuttal challenges not the evidence the opposing view uses but the premise it rests on — an especially effective move because it shifts the ground of the debate rather than simply disputing facts. And it ends by restating the main argument with greater specificity than before, incorporating what the counterargument revealed about the real nature of the question.

Chapter 28 — Back Matter

Key Terms, Summary, and Review

Key Terms

counterargument
An opposing view, objection, or challenge that complicates or contradicts a writer's main argument; addressing counterargument is essential to credible academic writing.
concession
The acknowledgment that an opposing view has genuine merit or that a point raised against one's argument is fair; concession builds trust without abandoning the main claim.
rebuttal
The response to an opposing view that explains, with reasons and evidence, why it does not ultimately undermine the writer's main argument.
qualification
The practice of limiting or narrowing a claim to make it more precise and accurate; a mark of intellectual honesty, not weakness.
straw man fallacy
A reasoning error in which a writer distorts or misrepresents an opposing view to make it easier to dismiss; undermines credibility and fails to address the genuine challenge.
acknowledge-concede-rebut
A framework for integrating counterargument that moves from presenting the opposing view, to granting what is fair in it, to explaining why the main argument remains stronger.
signal phrase
A phrase used to attribute a view to a particular source or group, making clear the writer is describing rather than endorsing the position.
charitable reading
The practice of interpreting an opposing view in its strongest and most reasonable form before critiquing it; the foundation of fair representation.

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 28.1Counterargument strengthens academic writing by demonstrating intellectual honesty, building credibility, and showing that an argument has been tested against genuine challenges. Writers who engage opposing views are often more persuasive, not less.
  • Sec 28.2Identifying opposing views requires imagining the skeptical reader, seeking complicating evidence, locating disagreement in the scholarly conversation, examining your own assumptions, and acknowledging what you are least certain about.
  • Sec 28.3Fair representation means presenting opposing views in language their supporters would recognize, engaging with the strongest version of the opposing argument, using signal phrases that attribute without endorsing, and giving appropriate context. Caricature is both dishonest and self-defeating.
  • Sec 28.4Concession acknowledges what is genuinely valid in an opposing view — a fact, a logical point, a value, or an area of genuine uncertainty. Concession builds trust and disarms defensive readers without requiring the writer to abandon the main argument.
  • Sec 28.5Rebuttal responds to an opposing view with reasons and evidence, not mere assertion. Strategies include challenging the evidence, challenging the reasoning, challenging the scope, offering counterevidence, or explaining why the concession does not change the overall conclusion. The acknowledge-concede-rebut structure organizes the full counterargument move.
  • Sec 28.6Qualification limits or narrows a claim to make it more accurate and defensible. Qualifying language, limited scope, acknowledged exceptions, and clarification of what the argument is not claiming all strengthen rather than weaken academic writing.
  • Sec 28.7The straw man fallacy distorts or exaggerates an opposing view to make it easier to dismiss. It takes several forms: exaggeration, selecting the weakest version, attributing extreme positions to moderate critics, and loaded characterization. Charitable reading — engaging with the strongest version of the opposing argument — prevents it.
  • Sec 28.8Counterargument can be placed before the thesis, after the thesis, within body paragraphs, in a dedicated section, or in the conclusion. The best placement is the one that connects the counterargument most logically to the argument it complicates and serves the reader most effectively.

Review Questions

  1. Why do academic writers include counterargument in their essays? Explain at least two reasons.
  2. What is the difference between concession and rebuttal? Can a writer use both in response to the same opposing view? Explain.
  3. What does it mean to represent an opposing view "fairly"? What practices support fair representation?
  4. Describe the straw man fallacy. Give an example of how a straw man might appear in a student essay, and explain why it is a problem.
  5. What is qualification, and why is it a sign of intellectual precision rather than weakness?
  6. Describe the acknowledge-concede-rebut structure. What does each stage accomplish in the argument?
  7. At what points in an essay can counterargument be effectively placed? What factors should guide this decision?
  8. Why is it important to engage with the strongest version of an opposing argument rather than the weakest?

Discussion and Writing Prompts

  1. Prompt 1: Think of an argument you hold about a topic in your field of study or in current public life. Write a paragraph identifying the two or three strongest objections someone might make to your position. Do not yet respond to them — just identify and describe them as fairly as you can.
  2. Prompt 2: Find a short opinion piece or editorial. Identify how the writer handles opposing views. Does the writer concede anything? Does the writer rebut? Does the writer commit any straw man moves? Write a one-page analysis of how counterargument is handled in the piece.
  3. Prompt 3: Consider the following claim: "Students should be required to take at least one philosophy course before graduating from secondary school." Write two paragraphs: one presenting the strongest counterargument to this position, and one rebutting that counterargument using the acknowledge-concede-rebut structure.
  4. Prompt 4: Choose a topic from one of your other courses where two interpretations or positions are in genuine disagreement. Summarize each position fairly, and then write a paragraph that concedes a key point to each side. Reflect in writing on what this exercise reveals about the complexity of the question.
  5. Prompt 5: Describe a situation in which you changed your mind about something after hearing a strong opposing argument. What made the argument persuasive? How does this experience connect to what you have learned in this chapter about counterargument?

When finished, mark the following in your revised draft: (1) where you introduce the opposing view, (2) where you concede, and (3) where you rebut.

Activity 28-EPeer Review — Evaluating Counterargument

Exchange a short argumentative essay or paragraph with a partner. Read your partner's draft and answer the following questions in writing:

  1. What opposing views does the writer address? Are there important opposing views that are missing?
  2. Does the writer represent the opposing views fairly, or does the description seem distorted?
  3. Is the concession genuine, or does it feel dismissive?
  4. Is the rebuttal supported with reasons and evidence, or does it simply assert that the opposing view is wrong?
  5. What is the most important revision you would suggest for the counterargument section?

Chapter 28 Checklist

Chapter 28 Checklist
Use before submitting any argumentative writing
Identifying Opposing Views
  • I have identified the most significant opposing views relevant to my argument.
  • I have considered what a skeptical reader would question.
  • I have looked for evidence that complicates my argument, not just evidence that supports it.
  • I have identified the assumptions my argument makes that some readers might challenge.
Representing Opposing Views
  • I have represented the opposing view in language its supporters would recognize.
  • I have engaged with the strongest version of the opposing argument, not the weakest.
  • I have used signal phrases that clearly attribute the opposing view without endorsing it.
  • I have avoided distorting, exaggerating, or caricaturing the opposing position.
Concession
  • I have acknowledged what is genuinely valid or fair in the opposing view.
  • My concession is specific rather than vague.
  • My concession does not abandon my main argument.
Rebuttal
  • My rebuttal provides specific reasons, evidence, or reasoning — not just assertion.
  • I have explained why the opposing view does not defeat my main argument.
  • I have reconnected to my main argument after the rebuttal.
Qualification
  • I have used qualifying language where my claims are not universal.
  • I have limited the scope of my claims where appropriate.
  • I have acknowledged exceptions or areas of genuine uncertainty.
Avoiding Straw Man
  • I have not exaggerated or distorted the opposing view.
  • I have not selected only the weakest version of the opposing argument.
  • I have not used loaded language to characterize the opposing position.
Integration
  • Counterargument appears in a logical place in my essay.
  • The transition into counterargument is smooth and clearly signaled.
  • The transition out of counterargument reconnects clearly to my main argument.
Looking Ahead
Chapter 29: Logic, Fallacies, and Weak Reasoning

Chapter 28 equipped you with the tools to engage honestly with opposing views. Chapter 29 extends that work by examining what happens when reasoning breaks down entirely. You will learn to recognize the most common logical fallacies — hasty generalization, false cause, false dilemma, ad hominem, straw man, slippery slope, circular reasoning, and appeal to popularity — and to understand why each one weakens an argument. Recognizing fallacies in others' reasoning, and auditing your own argument for them, is the natural next step after learning to handle counterargument well. The two skills reinforce each other: writers who understand counterargument know what genuine challenge looks like; writers who understand fallacies know what false challenge looks like.

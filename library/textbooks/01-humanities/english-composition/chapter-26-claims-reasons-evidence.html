Chapter 26 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Every academic argument rests on a foundation of three interlocking parts: a claim that states what the writer believes to be true, reasons that explain why the claim is defensible, and evidence that gives those reasons concrete support. These three elements are not interchangeable, and they are not optional. An argument that has a claim but no reasons is little more than an assertion. An argument that has reasons but no evidence is little more than personal opinion dressed up in formal language. An argument that piles on evidence without connecting it to a clear claim leaves the reader to wonder what the point was.

This chapter teaches you to build arguments that work — arguments that are logically connected from beginning to end, that earn a reader's trust through disciplined reasoning, and that demonstrate the kind of thinking academic writing requires. You will learn what each element does, how the elements fit together, and how to avoid the most common mistakes writers make when they try to use them.

The goal is not to teach you to argue more aggressively. Academic argument is not a competition to see who can sound most certain. The goal is to teach you to argue more clearly, more fairly, and more convincingly, by understanding how the architecture of a good argument actually works.

Opening Question

Consider this statement: "Schools should require students to study a second language." Is this an argument? Not yet — it is only a claim. What makes it an argument is the work that follows: the reasons that explain why such a requirement is defensible, the evidence that supports those reasons, and the analysis that connects the evidence to the claim. By the end of this chapter, you will be able to distinguish between asserting a position and arguing one — and you will understand why that distinction matters in every piece of academic writing you produce.

The Architecture of Argument: Claims, Reasons, and Structure
The Three Elements · Main Claims and Subclaims · Building Genuine Reasons
Section 26.1

The Structure of an Argument

When most people hear the word argument, they think of disagreement — raised voices, competing opinions, someone trying to win. Academic argument is something different. In academic writing, an argument is a structured attempt to support a claim with reasons and evidence so that a thoughtful reader can follow your thinking, evaluate your support, and decide whether you have made a convincing case.

This distinction matters enormously. Academic argument is not about dominance. It is about demonstration. You are not trying to overpower your reader; you are trying to show your reader that your position is reasonable, well-supported, and worth considering. This requires a different kind of discipline than simply asserting what you believe. It requires you to slow down, organize your thinking, and lay out the logic step by step — not because readers are hostile, but because clear reasoning is a form of respect for the reader's intelligence.

The basic structure of any academic argument can be described with three elements:

Claim
What you are arguing — the position you are asking the reader to accept
Reason
Why the claim is defensible — the explanation that connects the claim to something the reader might already accept
Evidence
What supports the reason — specific, concrete material that grounds the reason in observable reality

In practice, academic arguments are rarely this neat. A paper may have one central claim supported by three or four main reasons, each reason supported by multiple pieces of evidence, and each piece of evidence requiring explanation and analysis before it genuinely supports anything. But the basic logic always reduces to the same pattern: you say something, you explain why it is true, and you show the evidence that backs up your explanation.

Understanding this structure is the first step toward controlling it. Writers who do not understand the difference between claims, reasons, and evidence often produce papers that feel unfocused or unsupported — not because the writer lacks ideas, but because the ideas are not organized into the logical relationship that argument requires.

Example 26.1.1
A three-part argument structure in practice
  • ClaimStudents who read widely outside of class perform better on academic writing assignments.
  • ReasonWide reading exposes students to more sentence structures, vocabulary, and organizational patterns than they would encounter through coursework alone.
  • EvidenceResearch on language acquisition consistently shows that exposure to varied texts increases vocabulary depth and syntactic flexibility, both of which are linked to measurable improvements in writing quality.
  • NoteEach element is doing different work. The claim states the position. The reason explains the mechanism — it answers the question why is the claim true? The evidence gives the reason something concrete to stand on. Remove any one of the three, and the argument weakens significantly.
Study Note — Argument vs. Assertion

An assertion is a statement that simply declares something to be true: "Social media is harmful to teenagers." An argument is an assertion plus the reasoning and evidence required to make a thoughtful reader believe it. Academic writing requires arguments, not assertions. The moment you state a claim, you have taken on the obligation to support it.

Section 26.2

Main Claims and Subclaims

A claim is a statement that asserts something to be true and that a reasonable person could, in principle, question or dispute. Not every statement is a claim in this sense. A statement of obvious fact — "Water boils at 100 degrees Celsius at sea level" — is not a claim an academic argument would need to support, because no informed reader disputes it. A claim is a statement where the writer has a position and must support it.

In academic writing, claims exist at different levels of generality. At the top of the argument sits the main claim, which is usually the thesis statement of the paper. The main claim states the overall position the paper will defend. Below the main claim sit subclaims — narrower, more specific assertions that, together, add up to the main claim.

Think of it this way. If the main claim is the roof of a building, the subclaims are the walls. The roof cannot stand without the walls beneath it, and the walls would be purposeless without the roof they are meant to support. Subclaims are not digressions. They are the structured support for the main argument.

Example 26.2.1
A main claim with three supporting subclaims
  • Main ClaimMany college students struggle with academic writing not because they lack intelligence, but because they have not been taught to revise.
  • Subclaim 1Most writing instruction emphasizes producing a first draft rather than improving it.
  • Subclaim 2Students who receive explicit instruction in revision techniques show measurable improvement in writing quality.
  • Subclaim 3Revision requires strategies that are distinct from the strategies used in drafting and that must be taught separately.
  • NoteEach subclaim is a claim in its own right — it can be questioned, and it needs its own support. Each one is also connected to the main claim. If you prove all three subclaims convincingly, the main claim becomes much more persuasive.

One common structural mistake in student writing is arguing for a subclaim as though it were the main claim, then moving to a completely different subclaim without connecting both of them back to the thesis. The result is an essay that feels like a list of separate points rather than a unified argument. Every subclaim should be introduced in a way that makes its relationship to the main claim explicit.

A useful test: after writing a body paragraph or body section, ask yourself, "How does this point support my thesis?" If you cannot answer that question clearly, the section may not be doing the argumentative work you think it is.

Strong Claims vs. Weak Claims

It is also worth noting the difference between a strong claim and a weak claim. A weak claim is vague, overly general, or so obvious that it barely needs argument. "Air pollution is a serious problem" is technically a claim, but it is so broad and so widely agreed upon that it does not give an argument much to do. A stronger version demands more specific reasoning and evidence.

Weak ClaimWhy It Is WeakStronger Version
Air pollution is a serious problem.Too broad; too obvious; no specific position to argueMunicipal governments in rapidly growing cities must prioritize emissions regulation over economic incentives for industrial development, even when doing so reduces short-term tax revenue.
Education is important.No one disputes this; no direction for an argumentFirst-generation college students are more likely to complete their degrees when institutions provide structured peer mentorship programs in their first semester.
Social media affects teenagers.Vague; the direction and nature of the effect are unstatedPassive social media consumption — scrolling without posting or interacting — is more strongly associated with adolescent anxiety than active use, suggesting that the type of engagement matters more than the amount of time spent.
Common Mistake — The Thesis That Announces Instead of Argues

A common weak claim disguises itself as a thesis statement: "In this essay, I will discuss three reasons why schools should require students to study a second language." This is an announcement, not an argument. It describes what the essay will do without committing to a position. A genuine thesis states what the essay will argue: "Schools should require students to study a second language because bilingualism strengthens cognitive flexibility, broadens cultural understanding, and improves economic opportunity — benefits that outweigh the time and resource costs of implementation."

Section 26.3

Reasons

A reason answers the question: Why should the reader accept the claim? A reason is the explanatory bridge between what you are asserting and why it is true. Without reasons, a claim is merely an assertion — a statement that demands to be believed without explaining itself.

Many students confuse reasons with restatements. If your claim is "Students should be required to take at least one writing course regardless of their major," and your "reason" is "because writing is important for all students," you have not actually given a reason — you have restated the claim in slightly different words. A genuine reason explains why the claim follows from something the reader might already accept. The logic needs to move forward, not in a circle.

A better reason for the same claim might be: "Because all professional fields require some form of written communication, and students who cannot write clearly are disadvantaged in the workplace regardless of their technical skills." Now there is a genuine explanatory connection. The claim is defensible not just because it sounds reasonable but because it follows from an observable feature of how professional life works.

Two Tests for a Genuine Reason

Test 1: Does this reason actually explain why the claim is true, or does it just restate the claim? If your reason could be replaced by your claim without much loss of meaning, it is probably a restatement, not a reason.

Test 2: Is this reason something that itself could be supported with evidence? A reason that cannot be supported with any concrete evidence is, at best, a strong opinion. Reasons need to be grounded in something verifiable.

Example 26.3.1
Restatement versus genuine reason
  • ClaimPublic libraries play a crucial role in reducing educational inequality in communities with limited school funding.
  • Weak "Reason"Because libraries are important for education. (This is just a vague restatement of the claim.)
  • Genuine ReasonBecause public libraries provide free access to books, internet, educational programs, and tutoring services that low-income families cannot afford through private means, compensating for some of the resource gaps created by inequitable school funding systems.
  • NoteThe stronger reason explains the mechanism — the how and why — rather than simply agreeing with the claim in different words. It points toward specific, verifiable things (books, internet access, educational programs) that can be supported with evidence.

Academic arguments typically involve multiple reasons, each of which becomes the main claim of a body section or body paragraph. Together, the reasons form the logical backbone of the essay. When you outline an essay, you are largely organizing its reasons into a sequence that builds the overall argument step by step.

One further consideration: reasons should be independent enough to provide genuine support. If all your reasons are simply variations on the same underlying idea, your argument will feel repetitive rather than thorough. A well-constructed argument covers the major logical angles of a claim — perhaps addressing effectiveness, fairness, practicality, and long-term consequences as four distinct and genuinely different types of reasons for the same position.

Application — Reasons as an Essay Map

Your reasons, stated together, should function as a preview of your paper's body structure. If your thesis is "Schools should require a second language," and your three reasons are cognitive, cultural, and economic, you have also identified your three body sections. The reasons do not just support the claim — they organize the entire essay.

Section 26.4

Evidence

Evidence is the specific, concrete material that supports a reason. If reasons explain why a claim is true, evidence demonstrates that the reasons themselves are grounded in reality rather than mere opinion. Evidence is what distinguishes academic argument from personal conviction. Without evidence, you are asking the reader to take your word for it. With evidence, you are inviting the reader to examine what grounds your position.

Evidence takes many forms, and the appropriate type of evidence varies by discipline, assignment, and argument type. Choosing the right kind of evidence for the right context is part of what it means to write for a specific academic audience.

Type of EvidenceDescriptionCommon Disciplinary Use
Factual evidenceVerifiable facts, statistics, data, documented events, or established scientific findingsSocial science, science, policy writing, history
Expert testimonyQuotations, paraphrases, or summaries from qualified authorities in a fieldAll disciplines; requires careful source evaluation
ExamplesSpecific cases or instances that illustrate a broader pattern or claimAll disciplines; especially valuable for making abstract claims concrete
Textual evidenceQuotations or references from primary texts used to support interpretive claimsLiterary analysis, history, philosophy, law
Logical reasoningStructured argument such as analogy or deduction, where the evidence is a logical relationship between ideasPhilosophy, mathematics, some humanities disciplines

In practice, strong arguments often use more than one type of evidence. A single quotation from an expert might be supported by data that shows the pattern the expert describes, followed by a specific example that makes the data tangible for the reader. Layering types of evidence strengthens a reason by approaching it from multiple angles.

Using evidence well requires understanding that evidence does not speak for itself. This is one of the most important principles in academic argument, and it is one that beginning writers consistently underestimate. A common and significant mistake in student writing is what instructors sometimes call evidence dropping — inserting a quotation or statistic into a paragraph and then moving on, as though the evidence has automatically made the point. It has not. Evidence becomes part of an argument only when it is explained, connected to the reason it supports, and tied back to the claim. The explanation is not optional. It is where the actual intellectual work of argument happens.

Example 26.4.1
Evidence dropping versus evidence that works
  • DroppedMany students struggle with revision. "Writers who revise tend to produce higher-quality final drafts" (Smith, 2019). Students should be taught revision skills.
  • ExplainedMany students struggle with revision because they have never been taught that it is a distinct cognitive skill, not simply a matter of rereading for typos. Research supports the significance of this gap: writing scholars have found that writers who engage in substantive revision — reconsidering structure, evidence, and argument — tend to produce measurably higher-quality final drafts than those who limit their post-draft work to surface corrections (Smith, 2019). This finding suggests that the problem is not student ability but student preparation. If students understood revision as a learnable process with specific techniques, they would be better equipped to use it.
  • DifferenceThe second version introduces the evidence with context, names what the evidence shows, and connects that finding back to the larger argument about student preparation. The evidence now has a job to do, and it is doing it.
Common Mistake — Evidence Dropping

Evidence dropping occurs when a writer presents a quotation, statistic, or example and then immediately moves to the next point without explaining what the evidence means or why it matters. The reader is left to make the connection themselves — and often cannot. Strong academic writers never drop evidence without analysis. If you cannot explain why a piece of evidence supports your reason, it probably does not belong in the paragraph.

Section 26.5

Analysis of Evidence

Analysis is the process of explaining what evidence means in the context of your argument. It is the intellectual move that transforms evidence from raw material into genuine support. Without analysis, your argument is a collection of quotations and facts. With analysis, it becomes reasoning.

To analyze evidence, you must ask and answer several questions. What does this evidence show? Why is it relevant to my reason and my claim? What does it mean in the context of this specific argument? What does it not show — are there limitations I should acknowledge? How does it connect to other evidence or reasons in my paper?

Analysis is distinct from summary. Summary tells the reader what the evidence says. Analysis tells the reader what it means. A paragraph that only summarizes its evidence has not done its argumentative work. This distinction is not subtle — it marks the difference between writing that presents information and writing that makes an argument.

Example 26.5.1
Summary versus analysis of the same evidence
  • Summary OnlyA study conducted over three years followed two hundred first-year college students who received explicit revision instruction and compared them to a control group that did not. The study found that students in the revision instruction group scored significantly higher on end-of-semester writing assessments.
  • With AnalysisA three-year study following two hundred first-year college students found that those who received explicit revision instruction scored significantly higher on end-of-semester writing assessments than a comparable control group (Johnson & Park, 2021). This result is significant for more than the obvious reason that instruction improved outcomes. What it reveals is that revision is learnable — it responds to teaching in measurable ways. If revision were simply a matter of talent or effort, the instruction group would not have outperformed the control group so consistently. The study therefore supports the argument that institutions that fail to teach revision explicitly are not simply leaving a skill undeveloped; they are leaving a teachable skill untaught.
  • DifferenceThe analytical version draws out the implication of those findings for the argument being made. It does not just report the study's findings — it thinks through what they mean and says so explicitly for the reader.

A useful technique for developing analytical writing is to practice completing the following prompts after introducing any piece of evidence. Over time, these analytical moves become natural. In early drafts, using them explicitly can help you break the habit of evidence dropping.

Analytical Sentence Starters

"This shows that..." — Use this to name what the evidence demonstrates.

"This matters because..." — Use this to connect the evidence to the larger argument.

"This suggests that..." — Use this to draw an inference from the evidence.

"What this means for my argument is..." — Use this to make the connection to your claim explicit.

"This does not mean that..." — Use this to acknowledge what the evidence does not prove, strengthening your credibility.

Academic Transfer — Analysis Across Disciplines

The analytical move required in English — explaining what a quotation means for your argument — is the same move required in history (explaining what a primary source reveals about the period), in biology (explaining what experimental data shows about the hypothesis), and in economics (explaining what statistical trends imply about market behavior). Analysis is a transferable skill, not a writing-class trick.

Sections 26.6 – 26.7
Assumptions, Warrants, and the Quality of Evidence
Hidden Assumptions · When to Defend Assumptions · Evaluating Credibility and Relevance
Section 26.6

Assumptions and Warrants

Every argument, even a careful one, contains assumptions — unstated beliefs that the argument takes for granted in order to move from a reason to a claim. The term warrant is sometimes used in argumentation theory to describe the assumption that connects a reason to a claim. Understanding assumptions is important because hidden assumptions are one of the most common sources of weakness in academic arguments. A reader who spots an assumption the writer never addressed has found a logical gap.

Here is how assumptions work in practice:

Claim
Schools should require students to study a second language.
Reason
Students who speak more than one language perform better on measures of cognitive flexibility.
Assumption
Cognitive flexibility is a worthwhile goal of schooling, AND school-mandated language study is an effective way to develop it.

The argument moves from its reason to its claim by assuming two things: that cognitive flexibility is something schools should care about, and that language study in schools actually produces meaningful cognitive benefits. If either assumption is false or contestable, the argument needs to address it. A reader who questions whether school-based language study actually produces the cognitive benefits attributed to it has found a legitimate gap in the argument's reasoning.

Assumptions are not always wrong. Often they are shared values or widely accepted facts that do not need to be argued for. If your essay argues that clean water access is a public health priority, you do not need to prove that public health matters — that assumption is broadly shared. But when an argument rests on assumptions that a reasonable reader might question, those assumptions need to be stated and defended.

A Method for Examining Your Own Assumptions

Ask yourself: "What does my argument have to be true in order to hold together?" Each answer identifies an assumption. You can then decide whether the assumption is safe — shared by your reader and well-grounded — or whether it is something you need to address in the paper.

Addressing assumptions does not mean abandoning your argument. Often, acknowledging an assumption and briefly defending it strengthens the argument by demonstrating that the writer has thought carefully about the logical structure, not just the surface content.

Example 26.6.1
Identifying and addressing an assumption
  • ArgumentLocal governments should prioritize funding for after-school arts programs because research shows that participation in structured arts activities improves academic performance in core subjects.
  • AssumptionThis argument assumes that local governments have an obligation to support activities that improve academic performance, and that the academic benefits of arts programs outweigh other possible uses for the same funding.
  • Safe?The first assumption (that governments should support academic improvement) is broadly shared. The second (that arts programs are the best use of funding) is contestable and should be addressed — perhaps by citing the cost-effectiveness of arts programs compared to other academic intervention programs.
  • Stronger VersionAddressing the second assumption directly makes the argument more persuasive: "While there are many ways to invest in student academic success, after-school arts programs are particularly cost-effective per student served and have the added benefit of reducing dropout rates — making them a higher-value investment than many tutoring programs of similar cost."
Common Mistake — The Invisible Assumption

Arguments that rest on unstated assumptions a reasonable reader would question invite easy dismissal. If your argument seems persuasive to you but not to others, the problem is often a hidden assumption — something that seems obvious to you but is actually contested, discipline-specific, or culturally located. Identifying your own assumptions is difficult precisely because they feel obvious from the inside. Practicing with a reader who can ask "But why does that follow?" is one of the best ways to surface them.

Section 26.7

Evidence Quality

Not all evidence is equally good. Academic writers must evaluate the quality of the evidence they use, not just its existence. Using poor evidence is often worse than using no evidence at all, because poor evidence can mislead readers or undermine a writer's credibility when the source's weaknesses become apparent. Careful source selection is not merely a citation formality — it is an ethical and intellectual responsibility.

CriterionWhat to AskWhy It Matters
CredibilityWho wrote this? What are their qualifications? What standards does the publication apply?Peer-reviewed research carries more weight than anonymous or unvetted sources; credibility transfers to the argument.
CurrencyIs this source current enough for the field? Does it reflect the present state of knowledge?Outdated evidence can misrepresent current understanding, especially in fast-moving fields like medicine, technology, or policy.
RelevanceDoes this evidence speak to my specific claim, or just to the general topic?Evidence relevant to a related topic may not support the specific argument being made; relevance must be tested, not assumed.
RepresentativenessDoes this evidence reflect the range of available research, or does it cherry-pick a convenient outlier?Honest argument acknowledges the weight of evidence, not just the pieces that support a desired conclusion.
Fairness of UseAm I using this source in a way its author would recognize as accurate? Is the context preserved?Misrepresenting sources is a serious ethical failure; careful reading prevents inadvertent misuse.

A useful practice when gathering evidence is to ask yourself: "If I were arguing the opposite position, could I use this source against my argument?" If the answer is yes — if the source actually undermines your claim when read carefully — you need to either address that tension directly or choose better evidence. Selectively ignoring contradictory evidence is not just a logical weakness; it is an ethical problem. Academic integrity requires engaging honestly with what the evidence shows, including its complications.

Study Note — Peer-Reviewed Sources

Peer-reviewed academic articles are reviewed by other experts in the field before publication. This process does not guarantee that a study is correct, but it does mean that the methodology and conclusions have been scrutinized by qualified readers. For most academic arguments, peer-reviewed sources carry substantially more credibility than news articles, blogs, opinion pieces, or general reference websites — though those sources may sometimes be appropriate for other purposes, such as establishing that a debate exists or demonstrating what a general audience believes.

Section 26.8

Building a Chain of Reasoning

In a short argument, the connection between claim, reason, and evidence is relatively simple. In a full academic paper, those connections multiply. A thesis statement may have three major supporting reasons. Each reason may require two or three pieces of evidence. Each piece of evidence requires analysis. And all of it must connect back to the claim. The result is not three separate arguments but a single chain of reasoning — a logically connected sequence of ideas that builds from beginning to end.

The metaphor of a chain is useful because it highlights two key requirements. First, every link must be present. A gap in the chain — a reason without evidence, evidence without analysis, a subclaim that does not connect back to the thesis — breaks the logical sequence and gives a reader a reason to stop trusting the argument. Second, every link must be connected to the ones beside it. Even if all the individual elements are strong, an argument falls apart if the connections between them are not made explicit.

Building a chain of reasoning requires thinking about argument at three levels simultaneously.

Three Levels of Logical Connection

The level of the whole paper: Does the thesis control the argument from beginning to end? Do all the major sections genuinely support the thesis, or do some of them drift into related but separate territory?

The level of the section or paragraph: Does each section or paragraph begin with a clear subclaim that announces what point it will develop? Does the evidence in the paragraph actually support that subclaim, and is it analyzed in relation to it?

The level of the sentence: Are the transitions between sentences explicit enough that the reader can follow the logical movement from one idea to the next? Does each sentence's connection to the argument make sense, or does the prose jump between ideas without explanation?

Writers who master all three levels produce arguments that feel not merely correct but inevitable — arguments where each step follows naturally from the previous one, and where the reader arrives at the conclusion feeling persuaded rather than commanded. That quality of inevitability is not magic; it is the result of meticulous attention to connection.

The Reverse Outline

One practical tool for checking a chain of reasoning is the reverse outline. After drafting an essay, write a one-sentence summary of what each paragraph actually argues — not what you intended it to argue, but what it actually says. Then ask yourself: Does each summary sentence connect clearly to the thesis? Do the summary sentences, read in order, tell a logical story that moves from introduction to conclusion? If they do not, the chain of reasoning has gaps that revision needs to address.

The reverse outline is especially useful for catching a common structural problem: the paragraph that discusses something related to the topic but not clearly connected to the thesis. This paragraph may contain interesting material, but without a clear logical relationship to the main claim, it weakens the overall argument by diverting the reader's attention. The reverse outline surfaces this problem quickly, because the summary sentence for such a paragraph will not clearly follow from the thesis.

Example 26.8.1
Applying the reverse outline
  • ThesisMany college students struggle with academic writing not because they lack intelligence, but because they have not been taught to revise.
  • Para 1 SummaryMost high school writing instruction focuses on producing first drafts rather than improving them. → Connects clearly to thesis.
  • Para 2 SummaryThe five-paragraph essay formula, while useful for learning structure, discourages substantive revision because the structure is predetermined. → Connects: explains a specific mechanism behind the pattern named in Para 1.
  • Para 3 SummaryMany famous writers have talked about the importance of revision in their creative process. → Does NOT connect clearly: this is about professional creative writers, not the teaching of revision in school settings. The thesis concerns college students and instruction, not professional creative practice.
  • ActionPara 3 needs to be revised to connect to the thesis, or replaced with evidence about revision instruction in educational settings. Its interesting material does not belong in this argument as currently written.

The Therefore / Because Test

Another useful check is the therefore / because test. Try reading your argument forward and backward:

The Therefore / Because Test

Forward (because): "The main claim is true because reason A, because reason B, because reason C."

Backward (therefore): Reason A leads therefore to the subclaim about A, which leads therefore to the main claim.

If the because and therefore connections feel strained or require you to insert large unstated assumptions to make them work, the chain needs strengthening. The connections that feel natural to you as the writer may not be obvious to a reader who does not already know your argument — and argument is written for readers, not for the writer.

The therefore / because test is most useful at the sentence and paragraph level, where logical connections are most often left implicit. Writers tend to know what they mean from sentence to sentence and assume the reader can fill in the gaps. Often the reader cannot. Practicing the test trains you to make the logical connections visible — to say not just what comes next but why it follows.

Common Mistakes in Argument Construction

Mistake 1 — Asserting Instead of Arguing
Many claims stated confidently but never supported with reasons or evidence.

A strong argument does not assume its conclusion; it builds toward it. Every claim requires a reason, and every reason requires evidence.

Mistake 2 — Using Evidence Without Analysis
A quotation or statistic inserted into a paragraph, followed immediately by the next point.

Evidence that is not explained is evidence that has not been made to work. Always analyze; never drop and run.

Mistake 3 — Confusing Reasons with Restatements
Claim: "Students learn better in smaller classes." Reason: "Because small class sizes improve student learning."

A reason that is simply a paraphrase of the claim does not add to the argument. Every reason should introduce a genuinely new explanatory element.

Mistake 4 — Selecting Evidence That Does Not Actually Support the Claim
Using a quotation because it sounds impressive, without checking whether it applies to the specific claim being made.

Always verify that your evidence is relevant to your specific argument, not just to the general topic area.

Mistake 5 — Failing to Connect Subclaims to the Main Claim
Body paragraphs that develop interesting points but never explicitly connect those points back to the thesis.

An essay whose body sections do not visibly advance the main claim is an essay without a unified argument. Every section must be doing work that supports the thesis.

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 26.1Academic argument is structured demonstration, not assertion. Every argument rests on three interlocking elements: a claim (what you argue), a reason (why it is defensible), and evidence (what grounds the reason in reality). All three must be present and connected.
  • Sec 26.2A claim is an arguable statement that requires support. Main claims state the thesis; subclaims are narrower assertions that support it, forming the structural skeleton of the essay's body. Weak claims are vague, obvious, or merely announcements; strong claims are specific and arguable.
  • Sec 26.3A reason explains why a claim is true — it is the explanatory bridge between assertion and evidence. Reasons must be genuine explanations, not restatements. They must pass two tests: they must explain the claim rather than repeat it, and they must be supportable with evidence.
  • Sec 26.4Evidence is specific, concrete material — facts, expert testimony, examples, textual quotations, or logical reasoning — that supports a reason. Evidence does not speak for itself; evidence dropping (presenting evidence without analysis) is one of the most common weaknesses in undergraduate argument.
  • Sec 26.5Analysis explains what evidence means in the context of the argument. It is distinct from summary (what the evidence says) and is not optional. Analytical sentence starters help develop the habit: "This shows that...", "This matters because...", "What this means for my argument is..."
  • Sec 26.6Assumptions are unstated beliefs that allow an argument to move from reasons to claims. Safe assumptions are widely shared and need not be defended. Contestable assumptions must be identified and addressed. Examining assumptions demonstrates intellectual rigor and closes logical gaps.
  • Sec 26.7Evidence quality matters as much as evidence quantity. Credible, current, relevant, and representative evidence strengthens an argument. Using evidence fairly — accurately representing what sources say and do not say — is an ethical and intellectual requirement.
  • Sec 26.8A chain of reasoning is the logically connected sequence from thesis to conclusion. It must be maintained at three levels: the whole paper, the paragraph, and the sentence. The reverse outline and the therefore / because test are practical revision tools for checking whether the chain holds together.

Key Terms Review

argument
In academic writing, a structured effort to support a claim with reasons and evidence so that a thoughtful reader can evaluate and be persuaded by the reasoning — not a personal dispute or a competition to win.
claim
A statement that asserts something to be true and that a reasonable person might question or dispute, making it the kind of statement that requires support through reasoning and evidence.
main claim
The central, overarching position of an essay or paper, usually expressed in the thesis statement, which all subclaims and evidence must support.
subclaim
A narrower, more specific assertion that supports the main claim and serves as the central point of a section or paragraph; a claim in its own right, requiring its own support.
reason
An explanatory statement that answers the question why is the claim true? — the bridge between a claim and the evidence that grounds it. Reasons must be genuine explanations, not restatements.
evidence
Specific, concrete material — facts, data, examples, expert testimony, textual quotations, or logical reasoning — that supports a reason and grounds it in observable reality.
analysis
The writer's explanation of what evidence means in the context of the argument; distinct from summary and essential for turning evidence into genuine argumentative support.
assumption
An unstated belief that an argument takes for granted in order to move from reason to claim. Assumptions that a reasonable reader might question must be identified and addressed.
warrant
The logical connection or underlying assumption that allows a reason to support a claim; the "because" that links evidence to its conclusion in a formal model of argument.
evidence dropping
The mistake of inserting evidence into a paragraph without analyzing it or connecting it explicitly to the argument — one of the most common weaknesses in undergraduate academic writing.
chain of reasoning
The logically connected sequence of claims, reasons, evidence, and analysis that runs from the thesis of a paper through to its conclusion, maintained at the levels of the whole paper, the paragraph, and the sentence.
reverse outline
A revision technique in which the writer summarizes what each paragraph actually argues (not what was intended), then checks whether those summaries connect logically to the thesis and to each other.

Review Questions

  1. What is the difference between a claim and an assertion? Why does academic writing require claims to be supported rather than simply stated? Give an example of each.
  2. In your own words, explain the relationship between a main claim and a subclaim. How do subclaims support the thesis of an essay? What happens when subclaims are not connected back to the thesis?
  3. What is the difference between a reason and a restatement of a claim? Write an original claim, then write both a weak restatement and a genuine reason that explains why the claim is true.
  4. What is evidence dropping, and why does it weaken an argument? What should a writer do after presenting any piece of evidence?
  5. Explain the difference between summary and analysis of evidence. Why is analysis not optional in academic argument?
  6. What is an assumption in the context of academic argument? Give an original example of an argument and identify one assumption it makes. Is that assumption safe or contestable?
  7. What does it mean to evaluate evidence quality? Name and explain at least three criteria for judging whether evidence is appropriate for an academic argument.
  8. What is a chain of reasoning? At what three levels must logical connections be maintained in an academic paper? What happens when connections at one of these levels break down?
  9. Explain the reverse outline as a revision technique. What problem is it specifically designed to surface?
  10. Describe the therefore / because test in your own words. How would you apply it to a draft essay you have already written?

Discussion and Writing Prompts

Prompt 1Analyzing an Argument You Have Read

Find a short persuasive piece — an opinion column, an editorial, or a posted statement on a topic you care about. Identify the main claim, at least two reasons, and one or two pieces of evidence. Then evaluate: Does the writer analyze the evidence or simply drop it? Are there hidden assumptions a skeptical reader might question? Is the evidence quality strong? Write a paragraph — 150 to 250 words — explaining what makes the argument effective or weak, using the vocabulary from this chapter.

Prompt 2Building an Argument from a Claim

Choose one of the following claims and build an argument sketch: a main claim, three reasons, and at least one piece of evidence per reason. After each piece of evidence, write a two-to-three-sentence analytical note explaining what it means in the context of the argument.

(a) Regular sleep schedules improve academic performance in college students.   (b) Cities should invest in public transportation before road expansion.   (c) Reading literary fiction develops skills that are valuable in professional life.

Prompt 3Examining Your Own Assumptions

Take the thesis statement from a paper you have written or plan to write. List the three most important assumptions your argument makes — the beliefs your argument takes for granted in order to work. For each assumption, ask: Is this assumption safe (widely shared and well-grounded), or does it need to be addressed in your paper? Write a paragraph on each assumption explaining your answer and, if the assumption needs to be addressed, how you might do so.

Prompt 4Analyzing Evidence Quality

Find three sources related to a topic you are researching or might write about. For each source, evaluate its credibility, currency, relevance, and representativeness using the criteria from Section 26.7. Write a brief paragraph on each source explaining whether you would use it in an academic argument and why. Note any limitations the source has, even if you decide to use it.

Passage for Analysis

The transition from high school to college writing represents one of the most significant challenges first-year students face. High school writing often rewards students for following formulas — five paragraphs, clear topic sentences, and a summary conclusion — without requiring them to make original analytical moves. College writing, by contrast, typically requires students to develop a complex claim, work with primary and secondary sources, and respond to genuine intellectual questions rather than assigned prompts with predetermined answers. Studies of first-year college students have found that the majority report feeling underprepared for the analytical demands of college writing, despite having performed well on high school writing assessments. This gap suggests that high school writing instruction, however effective at teaching formula, may not be developing the kind of transferable analytical skill that college courses require.

Chapter 26 Checklist

Chapter 26 Revision Checklist
Use this checklist as you draft and revise any argumentative writing
Claims
  • My paper has a clear main claim that is specific, arguable, and appropriately scoped — not vague, obvious, or merely an announcement.
  • My main claim is stated clearly in a thesis statement early in the paper.
  • Each body section or paragraph develops a subclaim that supports the main claim.
  • Each subclaim is explicitly connected to the main claim, not merely related to the general topic.
Reasons
  • Each major section develops a genuine reason — an explanatory statement that answers why is the claim true?
  • My reasons are not simply restatements of the claim in different words.
  • My reasons are distinct from each other and together cover the major logical dimensions of the argument.
  • Each reason is something that can be supported with concrete evidence.
Evidence
  • Each reason is supported by at least one concrete piece of evidence.
  • My evidence is credible, current, relevant, and representative of the available research.
  • I have not selected evidence based solely on what supports my conclusion while ignoring contradictory evidence.
  • I have not dropped evidence without explanation in any paragraph.
Analysis
  • I analyze each piece of evidence rather than simply presenting it.
  • After each piece of evidence, I explain what it means in the context of my argument.
  • I connect each piece of evidence explicitly to the reason and claim it supports.
  • I distinguish between what my evidence shows and what it does not show.
Assumptions
  • I have identified the most important assumptions my argument relies on.
  • I have addressed any assumptions that a reasonable reader might question.
  • I have not left significant logical gaps that a reader could use to dismiss the argument.
Chain of Reasoning
  • The argument holds together from thesis to conclusion without unexplained gaps at the paper level.
  • The therefore / because test works in both directions through my argument.
  • I have completed (or could complete) a reverse outline, and the summary sentences connect logically to the thesis.
  • Transitions between sentences and paragraphs make the logical movement explicit, not implicit.
Looking Ahead
Chapter 27: Rhetorical Appeals and Persuasion

Chapter 26 gave you the structural architecture of academic argument: claim, reason, evidence, analysis, and the chain of reasoning that connects them. Chapter 27 extends this framework by examining the rhetorical dimension of argument — how writers use ethos (credibility), pathos (emotional appeal), logos (logical structure), and kairos (timing and occasion) to shape the relationship between their argument and their reader. You will learn that effective argument is not only logically correct; it is also rhetorically appropriate — calibrated to the audience, occasion, and purpose that define every writing situation. Chapter 28 will then teach you to handle opposing viewpoints through concession, rebuttal, and qualification, completing the full toolkit of academic argument.

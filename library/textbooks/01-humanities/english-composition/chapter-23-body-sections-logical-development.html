Chapter 23 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

An essay's thesis makes a promise to the reader. It says, in effect: this is what I am claiming, and this is what I will show you. The body of the essay is where that promise gets kept. Every paragraph, every section, every piece of evidence exists to fulfill the commitment the thesis established. When the body works well, readers move through an essay feeling that each new paragraph answers a logical question, that the argument is building toward something, and that the writer is in control of the material. When the body fails, readers feel confused, lost, or unconvinced — not because the thesis was wrong, but because the development did not do its job.

This chapter teaches the skills required to develop the body of an academic essay with clarity, purpose, and logical force. You will learn what main points and subpoints are, how to choose the organizational order that best fits your purpose, how to divide longer essays into sections, and how to use transitions to keep readers oriented. You will also learn two of the most common problems that weaken essay bodies — repetition and drift — and how to revise for both.

The skills in this chapter build directly on what you learned about thesis statements in Chapter 20, outlining in Chapter 21, and introductions and conclusions in Chapter 22. If the thesis is the map, the body is the terrain. This chapter teaches you how to move through that terrain efficiently, purposefully, and in a way that makes sense to readers who are following you for the first time.

Central Question

You have a thesis and a plan. You have gathered evidence and developed ideas. Now you face the hardest organizational question in essay writing: How do I arrange this material so that my argument is not just present, but convincing? The answer requires understanding main points, logical order, section structure, and the transitions that hold them together. This chapter provides the tools.

Section 23.1

What the Body of an Essay Must Do

Every academic essay has a central idea — a thesis, claim, or main argument — that needs support, development, and elaboration. A thesis stated without evidence or reasoning is simply an opinion. A claim made without explanation is simply an assertion. The body of an essay is the space where claims become arguments, where assertions become reasoned positions, and where the reader is given the material needed to evaluate, understand, and accept what the writer is saying.

This may sound straightforward, but many student writers misunderstand what the body is supposed to do. A common mistake is to think of the body as a container for information — a place to deposit relevant facts, examples, and quotations gathered during research or thinking. This view treats the body as storage. The problem is that information, on its own, does not make an argument. A paragraph full of relevant facts is not the same as a paragraph that uses those facts to build a case. Evidence must be selected, arranged, explained, and connected to the thesis in order to do its work.

A better way to think about the body is as a series of answers to a single question: Why should the reader believe what the thesis claims? Each major section of the body answers part of that question. Each paragraph within those sections provides the specific reasoning and evidence that make the answer convincing. When you write the body of an essay with this model in mind, every decision — which point to make first, how much evidence to include, when to transition — becomes easier, because every decision is guided by the same underlying principle: serve the thesis, serve the reader.

Writer's Principle

The body of an essay is not a display of everything you know about the topic. It is a carefully selected and ordered argument for a specific thesis. Every paragraph that does not advance the thesis is a paragraph that weakens the essay's focus.

Three Jobs the Body Must Accomplish

The body of an essay has three interrelated jobs. The first is to develop the central claim by providing the reasons, evidence, and analysis that make the claim worth believing. The second is to organize that development so the reasoning is easy to follow. The third is to create a sense of movement and building, so that by the end of the essay the reader feels that something has been established — that the essay has gone somewhere meaningful.

These three jobs explain why organization is not merely a cosmetic concern. Organization is not simply about making an essay look neat or following a formula. Organization determines whether an essay's reasoning actually reaches the reader in a usable form. Even the best evidence, presented in the wrong order or without adequate connection to the thesis, can fail to persuade. The body of an essay is not just the container for ideas; it is the structure that gives those ideas force.

Consider what happens when one of these jobs is missing. An essay that develops its claims extensively but presents them without logical order leaves readers with the frustrating sense that the material is good but they cannot quite grasp the argument. An essay that is organized clearly but does not develop its claims with sufficient evidence or analysis feels thin and unconvincing. An essay that develops and organizes well but lacks a sense of forward movement — that returns to the same ideas, covers the same ground, or loses its connection to the thesis — leaves readers feeling the essay has not actually arrived anywhere. All three jobs are required.

Study Note
The body in relation to the thesis

Think of the relationship between the thesis and the body like this: the thesis is a claim made in advance, before all the evidence has been presented. The body is the argument that earns that claim retrospectively — it gathers and organizes the reasoning that makes the thesis not just a statement, but a demonstrated position. A reader who reaches the end of a well-organized body should feel that the thesis was not just stated, but proved, at least to the degree any essay proves anything.

This is why revision of the body almost always involves checking back to the thesis: every major decision in the body should be traceable back to the question of whether this paragraph, this evidence, this order — serves the specific claim the thesis makes.

Section 23.2

Distinguishing Main Points from Subpoints

Before you can organize the body of an essay, you need to know what the body is actually trying to establish. This requires distinguishing between main points and subpoints — two levels of development that serve different functions in an argument.

A main point is a major reason, claim, or area of development that directly supports the thesis. If the thesis makes a central claim, each main point advances one significant part of that claim. Think of main points as the primary divisions of your argument — the large-scale answers to the question of why the thesis is true or worth accepting. Main points are big enough to carry their own sections or groups of paragraphs.

A subpoint is a more specific idea, piece of evidence, or line of reasoning that supports one of the main points. Subpoints belong inside the sections or paragraphs built around their main points. They are the details, examples, quotations, data, or analytical steps that make each main point credible and fully developed.

Example 23.2.1
Main points derived from a thesis

Suppose a student is writing an argumentative essay with the following thesis:

Public libraries are essential civic institutions because they provide free access to information, create inclusive community spaces, and support literacy development for all age groups.

This thesis identifies three main points: (1) free access to information, (2) inclusive community spaces, and (3) literacy development for all ages. Each of these main points would become a major section or group of paragraphs in the essay's body. Under each main point, the writer would develop subpoints: specific usage statistics, examples of programs offered, evidence of outcomes for library patrons, and so on.

Notice what happens when main points and subpoints are clearly distinguished. The writer knows what each section is responsible for. The reader can track the argument. The essay does not wander or repeat itself, because each section has a defined job and stays within it.

Identifying Your Main Points

The process of identifying main points is closely connected to the thesis. In many thesis statements, the main points are explicitly named, as in the library example above. In others, they must be inferred from the nature of the claim being made. Ask yourself: What are the major reasons, areas of evidence, or lines of reasoning that would convince a skeptical reader that my thesis is correct? Those reasons and lines of reasoning are your main points.

A standard academic essay typically has two to four main points, though longer research essays may have more. Having too many main points can make an essay feel scattered — the writer moves through so many areas that none receives adequate development. Having only one main point can make the body feel underdeveloped, as if the essay arrived at its thesis but could only find one reason to believe it.

Characteristics of Strong Main Points
  • Each main point is distinct — it addresses a genuinely different aspect of the thesis, not a slight variation on the same idea.
  • Each main point is directly relevant to the thesis — it cannot be interesting without also supporting what the thesis claims.
  • Each main point is substantial enough to develop fully — a main point that can be handled in a single sentence is not really a main point.
  • Taken together, the main points cover the thesis — they provide enough cumulative support to make the thesis convincing.

Identifying and Managing Subpoints

Once you know your main points, you can identify the subpoints that will develop each one. Subpoints are the specific supporting material — evidence, examples, data, reasoning, analysis — that make each main point convincing rather than simply asserted.

A useful test for subpoints is to ask: Does this detail, example, or piece of evidence directly support this main point? If the answer is yes, it belongs in the section built around that main point. If the answer is uncertain, you may be dealing with a subpoint that belongs elsewhere, or with material that does not yet serve a clear function in the essay.

During the planning and drafting stages, writers often generate more subpoints than they need. This is healthy. It is better to have abundant material and select the most useful subpoints than to have too little material and pad paragraphs with weak evidence. Part of organizing the body of an essay is making decisions about which subpoints to include, which to cut, and how many are needed to make each main point convincing without overloading it.

Planning Tip

As you plan, write each main point on a separate line, then list potential subpoints beneath it. If subpoints cluster naturally and abundantly under two or three main points but leave a third main point sparse, that may signal either that the thin main point needs more research, or that it is not a true main point for this thesis and should be reconsidered.

Section 23.3

Choosing the Right Organizational Order

Once you know your main points, you face a question that many writers underestimate: In what order should these points appear? The order of main points is not arbitrary. Different types of essays, different subjects, and different arguments call for different kinds of order. Choosing the right order makes an essay feel logical and inevitable. Choosing the wrong order — even when individual paragraphs are strong — makes an essay feel random, difficult to follow, or less persuasive than it could be.

Academic writing uses several standard logical orders. Understanding each one — what it does, when it works, and what it requires — gives you a practical set of tools for organizing the body of any essay.

Logical Order How It Works Best For
Order of Importance Points move from least to most important (climactic) or most to least (anticlimactic) Argumentative and persuasive essays
Chronological Order Points follow time sequence — either events as they occurred or steps in a process Historical analysis, narrative, process explanation
Cause-and-Effect Order Points move from causes to effects, or from observed effects back to their causes Analytical, explanatory, and social science writing
Comparison Order Points examine two or more subjects by criteria, using block or point-by-point structure Comparative analysis, evaluative writing
Problem-Solution Order Points move from establishing a problem to arguing for one or more solutions Policy, proposal, and applied writing
Analytical Grouping Points are organized by conceptual categories determined by the writer's analytical framework Literary analysis, philosophical argument, interdisciplinary writing

Order of Importance

Order of importance arranges main points according to the strength, significance, or persuasive weight of the evidence and reasoning behind each one. The most common form is climactic order, in which the essay builds from the least important point to the most important point. This arrangement creates a sense of momentum. The reader moves through a series of increasingly powerful reasons, and by the end of the essay, the argument has built to its strongest, most compelling point.

Climactic order is often the best choice for argumentative essays, because it ends on the most persuasive note. Readers are left with the most forceful reason to accept the thesis as the last thing they encounter before the conclusion. This is not manipulation; it is a recognition of how persuasion works. People tend to remember the last thing they read, and ending strongly uses that tendency to the argument's advantage.

The reverse — beginning with the most important point and moving to less important ones — is sometimes called anticlimactic order. This can be effective when the audience needs to be captured immediately, when the most important point is also the most arresting or surprising, or when the writer wants to establish the central claim forcefully before developing supporting details. It is less common in sustained academic argument but has its uses.

Example 23.3.1
Climactic order in an argumentative essay

A student writing about why a historical agricultural policy failed might organize three main points climactically: beginning with a relatively minor implementation problem — inadequate training for local administrators — moving to a more significant funding issue — grants released too late in the growing season — and ending with the most damaging factor: a fundamental misunderstanding of the soil conditions in the region the policy was designed to serve.

By the time the reader reaches the final main point, the argument feels fully supported. Each preceding point has established that the policy was flawed at multiple levels, and the final point delivers the most serious charge. The reader finishes the body convinced.

Chronological Order

Chronological order arranges main points according to time — either the sequence in which events occurred, or the sequence in which a process unfolds. It is especially effective for narrative essays, historical analyses, and any argument whose evidence is rooted in change over time. It respects the natural logic of cause and effect: what happened first may have caused what happened later, and presenting events in sequence allows readers to follow that causal relationship clearly.

A student writing about the development of environmental legislation in a particular country might organize the essay chronologically: discussing early regulatory attempts in one period, legislative achievements in a later period, and current challenges in the present. The chronological order does not just organize the information — it also implies an argument about development, progress, or continuity over time.

Chronological order should not be confused with simple storytelling. Even in an analytically organized chronological essay, the writer's job is to explain the significance of events, not simply to report their sequence. Each section should make a clear analytical or argumentative claim about what the period or stage means, not merely describe what happened.

Cause-and-Effect Order

Cause-and-effect order organizes an essay around the relationship between causes and their outcomes. An essay might move from causes to effects — explaining the factors that produced a result — or from effects back to causes — beginning with an observed outcome and investigating its origins. This order is common in analytical writing, social science essays, and historical argument.

Consider a student writing about rising rates of food insecurity in a specific region. The thesis might argue that food insecurity results from three interacting causes: agricultural disruption, economic inequality, and policy failure. The body develops each cause in turn, explaining the evidence for it and showing how it connects to the larger problem. This organization builds a multi-part causal explanation rather than merely listing factors.

Common Mistake — Post Hoc Reasoning

A common error in cause-and-effect essays is post hoc reasoning — assuming that because one thing happened before another, the first thing must have caused the second. Students using cause-and-effect order should make sure their evidence actually supports a causal relationship, not merely a temporal sequence. Correlation is not causation, and strong analytical writing distinguishes between the two.

Comparison Order

Comparison order organizes an essay around the similarities and differences between two or more subjects. There are two primary structural options. In block structure, the essay covers all aspects of one subject first, then all aspects of the other. In point-by-point structure, the essay alternates between the subjects, comparing them on one criterion at a time.

Block structure works well when subjects are short enough to discuss fully in a few paragraphs and when the contrast between them is the essay's main focus. Point-by-point structure works better for longer essays with multiple criteria of comparison, because it keeps the comparison active at each stage rather than requiring readers to hold all of one subject's characteristics in memory until the second half.

A student comparing two governmental approaches to public health policy might use point-by-point structure, examining both governments' funding models in one section, their implementation strategies in another, and their measurable outcomes in a third. Each section maintains clear analytical focus while keeping both subjects visible throughout the essay.

Problem-Solution Order

Problem-solution order organizes an essay around the identification of a problem and the development of one or more solutions. The body typically devotes early sections to establishing the existence, significance, and nature of the problem, then moves to arguing for solutions. This order is common in policy essays, proposal writing, and applied fields such as public health, urban planning, and business.

An important consideration in problem-solution order is proportionality. The problem section should be developed fully enough that readers understand why the problem is serious and why the proposed solution is necessary. But the essay should not spend so much time diagnosing the problem that it leaves insufficient space to argue for the solution convincingly. The balance between diagnostic and prescriptive work matters.

Analytical Grouping

Analytical grouping organizes an essay around conceptual categories or analytical frameworks rather than time, cause, importance, or comparison. The writer divides the subject into meaningful analytical components and examines each one in turn. This order is especially common in literary analysis, philosophical argument, and interdisciplinary writing.

A student analyzing a novel might organize an essay around three analytical categories — imagery, narrative structure, and characterization — examining how each contributes to the novel's central themes. The order is not chronological, causal, or comparative; it is determined by the writer's analytical framework for understanding the subject. When analytical grouping works well, it gives an essay an intellectually distinctive quality. It shows that the writer is not merely reporting information but is actively shaping the way the subject is understood.

Choosing Your Order

The best order for your essay is the one that makes your specific argument clearest and most convincing. If you are unsure which order to choose, ask: What does my thesis assume about how these main points relate to each other? If the thesis implies that some reasons are stronger than others, consider climactic order. If it implies that events produced each other, consider chronological or cause-and-effect order. If it implies that two things can be evaluated against the same standards, consider comparison order. Let the relationship between your ideas guide the choice.

Section 23.4

Sectioning Longer Essays

Short essays of two to four pages can often be organized as a sequence of individual paragraphs, each developing one main point. But longer essays — those of five pages or more — typically benefit from being divided into sections: distinct divisions of the essay that group related paragraphs around a single major point or area of development.

Sections serve several important functions. They make longer essays easier to navigate. They signal to the reader that the essay is moving from one major area of argument to another. They allow the writer to develop complex main points through multiple paragraphs without losing focus. And they help the writer maintain control over the proportion and balance of the essay as a whole.

When to Use Sections

Not every essay needs explicit section divisions. Short essays, personal reflective pieces, and some analytical essays work better as continuous prose with strong paragraph-level transitions. But when an essay is developing multiple substantial main points, when each main point requires several paragraphs of development, or when the subject naturally divides into major areas that need distinct treatment, sections become an important organizational tool.

Academic writing in many disciplines uses section headings as a standard convention. Scientific and social science writing almost universally uses section headers — Introduction, Literature Review, Methods, Results, Discussion, Conclusion. Humanities writing more often uses sections without explicit headers, relying on transitions to signal movement from one section to another. When in doubt, check your assignment instructions and ask your instructor. But even when explicit headings are not used, the logic of sectioning — grouping related paragraphs around distinct main points — is essential to every well-organized longer essay.

How to Divide an Essay into Sections

Dividing an essay into sections is not the same as arbitrarily breaking it into chunks. Each section should be built around one main point, and that main point should be clearly stated — usually in a topic sentence near the beginning of the section's first paragraph, or in a transition that signals the section's focus.

A useful way to think about sections is to ask: If a reader could only read one section of this essay, what would they come away understanding? Each section should be coherent enough to be understood on its own terms, while also fitting into the larger logic of the essay as a whole. The first paragraph of a section typically introduces the main point and begins developing it. Subsequent paragraphs deepen the development, add evidence, address complications, or extend the analysis. The final paragraph of a section usually completes the argument for that main point and often includes a brief transition toward the next section.

Example 23.4.1
Sectioning a longer analytical essay

Consider a longer analytical essay arguing that rapid urbanization in developing countries presents three interconnected challenges: inadequate housing, overstrained infrastructure, and environmental degradation. Each of these challenges is substantial enough to require multiple paragraphs of development. Rather than writing a sprawling sequence of paragraphs that readers must hold together mentally, the writer divides the essay into three body sections:

Section 1: The Housing Crisis — Opens by establishing the connection between urbanization and housing shortages, develops the main point through statistical evidence and case examples, examines the social consequences of inadequate housing, and closes with a brief transition to the infrastructure problem.

Section 2: Infrastructure Under Pressure — Opens by linking housing pressure to infrastructure strain, develops through examples of transportation, water, and sanitation systems, analyzes the compounding effects of rapid growth on existing systems, and closes with a transition toward environmental concerns.

Section 3: Environmental Costs of Rapid Growth — Opens by broadening the analysis to environmental consequences, develops through evidence on land use, pollution, and resource depletion, and closes with a synthesis that connects all three sections to the essay's central argument.

This sectioned structure allows the writer to develop complex material fully, gives the reader clear signposts throughout the essay, and maintains the larger argument's coherence across a substantial piece of writing.

Section 23.5

Transitions Between Sections

Transitioning effectively between sections is one of the most important — and most underestimated — skills in academic essay writing. A transition is not just a sentence that announces the next topic. A good transition does three things simultaneously: it closes the current section by summarizing what has been established, it connects the current section to the next by showing how the two main points relate, and it opens the next section by signaling what is coming.

When all three of these functions work together, readers never feel lost. They understand where they have been, why the essay is moving forward in this direction, and what they should expect next. When transitions are missing or weak, readers experience the essay as a series of disconnected blocks — paragraphs that seem to share a topic but do not feel like a unified argument.

The Three Functions of a Strong Section Transition
  • Close — Complete the argument for the current main point. A sentence or two drawing the section's reasoning together is usually sufficient.
  • Connect — Show how the two main points relate. This is the most demanding function and the one most writers omit. Simply listing "Another important point is…" is not a connection; it is a list.
  • Open — Introduce the new main point clearly enough that the reader can orient to it before it is fully developed.
Example 23.5.1
Weak transition versus strong transition

Returning to the urbanization essay, a transition between the housing section and the infrastructure section is needed. Consider the difference between these two versions:

Weak transition:

Housing shortages are a serious problem. Another problem is infrastructure.

This transition announces a new topic but does nothing to close the previous section or show why the two main points are related. It reads like a list.

Strong transition:

The housing crisis does not exist in isolation. As millions of new urban residents crowd into informal settlements without secure housing, they also arrive in cities whose roads, water systems, and public transit networks were designed for far smaller populations. The pressure of unplanned growth does not stop at the walls of individual homes; it radiates outward into every system the city depends on.

This transition completes the thought about housing, establishes a causal relationship between housing pressure and infrastructure strain, and opens the infrastructure section with a clear sense of what is at stake. The reader is not just told that the next topic is infrastructure; they understand why infrastructure comes next.

Common Transition Mistakes

Abrupt transitions move from one section to the next without any connection. The reader feels a sudden shift — one moment the essay is discussing one topic, and the next moment it is discussing something else without explanation. These are usually caused by drafting sections independently and failing to stitch them together.

Mechanical transitions use formulaic phrases — "Now we will look at...," "The second point is...," "Moving on to..." — that announce movement without creating connection. These transitions are not wrong, but they are weak. They fulfill the minimum requirement of signaling a new section without doing the intellectual work of showing how the sections relate to each other.

Repetitive transitions summarize the entire preceding section at length before introducing the new one. Brief closure is effective; full recapitulation is not. Transitions should create forward movement, not send readers backward through material they have just read.

Section 23.6

Balance and Proportion

A well-organized essay body is not only logically ordered; it is proportionally balanced. Balance and proportion refer to the distribution of space, development, and emphasis across the body's sections and paragraphs. When an essay is proportionally balanced, every main point receives the development it requires — no more and no less.

An essay that devotes four paragraphs to its first main point and half a paragraph to its third sends the reader an unintentional message: the third main point is either less important or less supported than the first. If that is the writer's actual view, the essay's thesis may need to be revised to reflect it. If it is not the writer's view — if the third main point is genuinely as significant as the first — then the disproportion represents a development problem that revision needs to address.

Balance does not mean that every section must be exactly the same length. Different main points may genuinely require different amounts of development. A main point supported by a single, highly concentrated piece of evidence may be fully developed in two paragraphs. A main point that depends on multiple types of evidence, or that involves a more complex analytical argument, may require four or five paragraphs. What balance means is that the length and depth of each section should be justified by the complexity and importance of the main point it develops — not by the quantity of evidence the writer happened to collect or an unconscious preference for certain parts of the argument.

Diagnosing Proportion Problems

Proportion problems often reveal themselves in the draft stage. The writer who begins with strong energy on the first main point and runs out of steam by the third has created a proportion problem. So has the writer who discovers a fascinating sub-topic in the middle of the essay and spends three paragraphs developing it at the expense of the essay's actual main points. When revising a draft, read through the body with proportion in mind.

Questions for Diagnosing Proportion
  • Does any section feel rushed or underdeveloped relative to the others?
  • Does any section feel padded or overlong — as if the writer is repeating evidence or analysis already made?
  • Are the most important main points given the most substantial development?
  • Is there a section that could be cut or consolidated without weakening the overall argument?
  • Is there a section that needs more evidence, more analysis, or a stronger connection to the thesis?
Section 23.7

Avoiding Repetition and Drift

Two of the most common problems in essay bodies are repetition and drift. Both weaken essays significantly, and both are difficult for writers to detect in their own drafts. Understanding them clearly is important for any serious revision process.

Repetition

Repetition occurs when an essay restates a point, cites evidence already used, or re-covers ground already developed — without adding new reasoning, deeper analysis, or a new angle on the material. A small amount of deliberate repetition is useful. Recalling a key term, briefly restating a central claim to anchor a new section, or returning to an established idea in order to extend it — these are not problems. The problem is unintentional repetition: when the writer makes the same point twice in different words without realizing it, or when the same evidence appears in two different sections as if it were two separate pieces of support.

Repetition typically has one of two causes. The first is a planning problem: the writer did not clearly distinguish the main points before drafting, so two sections end up developing the same idea from slightly different angles. The solution is to revise the outline — clarify each main point and make sure the points are genuinely distinct before revising the draft. The second cause is a development problem: the writer runs out of material in the middle of a section and returns to earlier evidence or reasoning to fill the space. The solution here is either to find additional material that genuinely advances the argument or to reduce the scope of the section to match the available evidence.

Drift

Drift occurs when the body of an essay gradually moves away from the thesis — when the writing begins to develop interesting ideas that are related to the topic but do not actually serve the central claim. Drift is subtle and can feel, while writing, like good development.

Imagine a student writing about the effectiveness of early childhood education programs who encounters an interesting section in a source about the history of public education funding. The student includes a paragraph, then two, then a full section on this history. The paragraphs are well-written and the information is genuinely related to the general topic. But the essay's thesis is about effectiveness, not history, and the historical section does not actually support the effectiveness claim. The essay has drifted.

The test for drift is always the same: Does this paragraph directly support the thesis? If the answer is "not really, but it's interesting" or "sort of, in a general way," the paragraph may be drifting. The material may be genuinely useful in a different essay, or it may need to be refocused so that its connection to the thesis is made explicit.

Revision Tool — The Reverse Outline
Using the reverse outline to catch repetition and drift

A reverse outline is one of the most effective tools for identifying both repetition and drift. After writing a draft, go through it paragraph by paragraph and write a one-sentence summary of what each paragraph actually argues. Then compare those summaries to the thesis.

Any paragraph whose actual content does not directly support the thesis is either drifting or needs to be revised so that its connection to the thesis is made explicit. Any two paragraphs whose summaries are nearly identical are likely repeating each other. The reverse outline makes these problems visible in a way that simply re-reading the essay often does not, because re-reading keeps the writer focused on local flow rather than global structure.

Section 23.8

A Systematic Approach to Revising Organization

Revision is where good essays are made. The organizational principles covered in this chapter are not just planning tools; they are revision tools. When you return to a draft to revise its body, you are asking whether the organizational choices you made — consciously or unconsciously — are actually serving the essay's thesis and the reader's needs.

Many student writers approach revision by reading through their draft and correcting errors as they go. This is editing, not revision. True revision of essay organization requires stepping back from the draft to assess its global structure before working at the sentence level. The following seven steps provide a systematic approach to revising the body of an academic essay.

  1. Step 1Identify your actual main points. Read through the draft and identify, for each body section or group of paragraphs, what main point is actually being developed. Write these down in a list. This is your reverse outline.
  2. Step 2Compare actual main points to intended main points. Does the list of actual main points match the main points your thesis implied? Are there sections that drift away from the thesis? Are there main points the thesis promised that are missing or underdeveloped?
  3. Step 3Evaluate the order. Given the type of essay you are writing and the nature of your argument, is the order of main points logical? Would a different order — building to the most important point, or establishing cause before examining effect — make the argument clearer or more convincing?
  4. Step 4Check balance and proportion. Does each main point receive the development it requires? Are any sections overlong, padded, or repetitive? Are any sections rushed, thin, or underdeveloped?
  5. Step 5Evaluate transitions. Does each major transition close the preceding section, connect it to the next, and open the new section clearly? Are there abrupt or mechanical transitions that need to be deepened?
  6. Step 6Check for repetition and drift. Using the reverse outline, identify any paragraphs that repeat material already developed elsewhere or that drift from the thesis. Decide whether to cut, consolidate, or refocus each problematic section.
  7. Step 7Revise. With a clear picture of what needs to change, revise the body — cutting what does not serve the argument, strengthening transitions, expanding underdeveloped sections, and reordering main points if necessary.

This process takes time, but it is the process that separates a rough draft from a polished essay. It treats revision not as correction but as re-seeing — a genuine reassessment of whether the essay's body is doing what the thesis requires. Writers who revise at the global level before working at the sentence level consistently produce stronger final essays, because they ensure the argument is sound before they refine its expression.

Sequencing Your Revisions

As the textbook's writing philosophy states: revise for meaning first, structure second, paragraphs third, sentences fourth, and grammar and formatting last. This sequence keeps you from wasting time polishing sentences in sections that will ultimately be cut or substantially reorganized. Always work from the largest scale to the smallest.

Chapter 23 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 23.1The body of an essay is not a storage space for information; it is the structured argument that makes the thesis convincing. Its three jobs are to develop the central claim, organize that development, and create a sense of forward movement.
  • Sec 23.2Main points are the major claims that directly support the thesis. Subpoints are the specific evidence and reasoning that support each main point. Distinguishing clearly between these two levels allows writers to keep sections focused and avoid mixing different areas of argument.
  • Sec 23.3Logical order — the arrangement of main points in a meaningful sequence — is not arbitrary. Six standard orders are available: order of importance (climactic and anticlimactic), chronological, cause-and-effect, comparison (block and point-by-point), problem-solution, and analytical grouping. The best order for any essay is the one that makes the specific argument clearest and most convincing.
  • Sec 23.4Longer essays benefit from being divided into sections — distinct groups of paragraphs each built around one main point. Each section should be coherent enough to stand on its own terms while fitting into the larger logic of the essay.
  • Sec 23.5Effective section transitions perform three functions: closing the preceding section, connecting it to the next, and opening the new section. Abrupt, mechanical, and repetitive transitions all fail to create the connection that keeps readers oriented in a longer essay.
  • Sec 23.6Balance and proportion require that each main point receives the development its importance and complexity demands. Balance does not mean equal length; it means each section's length is justified by the weight of its main point.
  • Sec 23.7Repetition is the unintentional restatement of already-developed material. Drift is the gradual movement away from the thesis into related but unsupporting content. Both can be identified and corrected through the reverse outline and systematic revision.
  • Sec 23.8Revising essay organization requires a seven-step process that moves from identifying actual main points, to evaluating order, to checking proportion, transitions, repetition, and drift — always before editing at the sentence level.

Key Terms

main point
A major reason, claim, or area of development that directly supports the essay's thesis. Main points are the primary divisions of an essay's body.
subpoint
A specific piece of evidence, example, or line of reasoning that supports one of the main points.
logical order
The arrangement of main points in a sequence determined by the relationship between ideas — importance, time, cause, comparison, or conceptual grouping.
climactic order
A form of order of importance that builds from the least to the most important main point, ending the essay on its strongest note.
anticlimactic order
A form of order of importance that presents the most important main point first, then moves to supporting points of lesser weight.
chronological order
An organizational structure that arranges main points according to time sequence, useful for historical analysis and process explanation.
cause-and-effect order
An organizational structure that arranges main points around the relationship between causes and their outcomes.
comparison order
An organizational structure that arranges main points around similarities and differences between two or more subjects, using block or point-by-point structure.
block structure
A comparison organization method in which all aspects of one subject are covered before all aspects of the other.
point-by-point structure
A comparison organization method in which the two subjects are compared alternately on one criterion at a time.
problem-solution order
An organizational structure that moves from establishing and analyzing a problem to developing and defending a solution.
analytical grouping
An organizational structure that divides a subject into meaningful conceptual categories determined by the writer's analytical framework.
section
A distinct division of a longer essay that groups related paragraphs around a single major point or area of development.
transition
Writing that closes one section, connects it to the next, and opens the new section. Effective transitions perform all three functions simultaneously.
balance and proportion
The distribution of space, development, and emphasis across sections, justified by the complexity and importance of each main point.
repetition
The unintentional restatement of points, evidence, or reasoning already developed earlier in the essay.
drift
The gradual movement of an essay's body away from the thesis into related but thesis-unsupporting material.
reverse outline
A revision technique in which the writer summarizes each paragraph of a completed draft to assess how well the actual content matches the intended argument.

Chapter 23 Checklist

Main Points and Development
  • I have identified the main points that support my thesis, and each one is distinct from the others.
  • Each main point is directly relevant to the thesis — not merely related to the topic in general.
  • I have identified the subpoints (evidence, examples, reasoning) that will develop each main point.
  • Taken together, my main points cover the thesis — they provide enough cumulative support to make it convincing.
Logical Order
  • I have chosen a logical order for my main points based on the type of essay and the nature of my argument.
  • The order I have chosen makes the argument as clear and convincing as possible.
  • If I am using order of importance, I have considered whether climactic or anticlimactic order better serves my purpose.
  • If I am using comparison order, I have chosen between block and point-by-point structure deliberately.
Sections and Transitions
  • If my essay is longer than four or five pages, I have divided the body into sections, each built around one main point.
  • Each section has a clear main point stated at or near its beginning.
  • My transitions between sections close the preceding section, connect the two sections, and open the new section.
  • I have avoided abrupt, mechanical, and repetitive transitions.
Balance and Proportion
  • Each main point receives the development its importance and complexity requires.
  • No section is significantly longer or shorter than what the main point justifies.
  • I have distributed evidence and analysis proportionally — not concentrated in one area of the body.
Repetition and Drift
  • I have checked the draft for unintentional repetition of points, evidence, or reasoning.
  • I have checked the draft for drift — material that is interesting but does not directly support the thesis.
  • I have used or considered using a reverse outline to identify organizational problems.
Revision Sequence
  • I have revised the body for global organization before editing at the sentence level.
  • I have confirmed that the body's actual content matches the argument the thesis promises.
  • The body ends in a position of strength — the argument is fully developed and ready for the conclusion.
Looking Ahead
Chapter 24: Common Academic Essay Types

Chapter 23 has given you the tools to organize the body of any academic essay — regardless of type or discipline. Chapter 24 applies these tools directly to the major essay forms you will encounter across your courses: summary-response, analytical, comparison, argument, problem-solution, literary analysis, and research essays. Each type has its own conventions for how the body develops, but all of them depend on the same principles of main points, logical order, sectioning, and proportion that this chapter has taught. Understanding the specific demands of each essay type will make you a more flexible and effective academic writer.

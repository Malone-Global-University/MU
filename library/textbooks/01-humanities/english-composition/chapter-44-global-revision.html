Chapter 44 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Most student writers revise too small and too soon. They finish a draft and immediately begin hunting for comma errors, fixing word choices, and smoothing sentences that may not even belong in the final paper. The result is a carefully polished version of a draft that still has serious problems at its core. A paper can have elegant sentences and still fail to make a clear argument. It can have perfect punctuation and still be organized in a way that confuses the reader. It can be grammatically correct from start to finish and still present evidence that does not actually support its thesis.

Global revision is the practice of stepping back from a draft and examining its largest, most fundamental elements before attending to smaller ones. In global revision, you are not yet a copyeditor working line by line. You are a reader and thinker asking the hardest questions about your own work: Does this paper have a clear, defensible thesis? Are its main points organized in the most effective order? Does the evidence actually support the claims being made? Does the reasoning hold together? Have I addressed the assignment's requirements?

This chapter teaches you how to approach a draft from the top down — starting with the whole and working inward toward the parts. Students who learn to revise globally write papers that improve substantially from draft to final. Students who skip global revision and move straight to sentence-level editing may spend hours on a paper and still submit work that falls short of its potential.

The Core Problem This Chapter Solves

Imagine spending two hours polishing the sentences in your introduction — only to realize, at the end, that your thesis is unclear and your introduction needs to be rewritten from scratch. Global revision prevents exactly this kind of wasted effort. By examining the biggest structural elements first, you ensure that the work you do at the sentence level is worth doing.

Section 44.1

Starting with the Whole Draft

When you sit down to revise a draft, the most natural impulse is to begin reading from the first sentence and fix things as you go. This approach feels productive because it generates visible activity — words get changed, sentences get rewritten, errors get caught. The problem is that it focuses your attention on the surface of the writing before you have confirmed that the writing's foundation is sound.

Think of a draft as a building under construction. Before painters and tile workers arrive, a building inspector checks the foundation, the framing, and the structural supports. If those are wrong, no amount of surface work will save the building. The same logic applies to a draft. Before you work on sentences, you need to confirm that the draft's central idea is clear and defensible, that the main points are arranged logically, that the evidence genuinely supports the argument, and that the paper fulfills what the assignment asked for.

The Core Principle of Global Revision

Starting with the whole draft means reading it as a reader, not as a writer. This is a discipline that takes practice. When you read your own writing, you tend to see what you intended to say rather than what is actually on the page. Your memory fills in gaps, your familiarity with your own ideas prevents you from noticing where a reader might be confused, and your attachment to sentences you worked hard on makes it difficult to cut them even when they do not belong.

One of the most effective techniques for beginning global revision is to put the draft away for at least several hours — ideally a full day — before reading it again. The distance makes it easier to read the draft as a stranger would. Another technique is to read the draft aloud. When you hear your writing rather than simply reading it silently, awkward organization, weak transitions, and missing logic become much harder to overlook. A third technique is to write a brief summary of what the draft actually says — not what you intended it to say, but what it says on the page. If that summary does not match your original intention, the gap tells you exactly where global revision needs to happen.

The Sequence Matters

In global revision, you ask large questions first: What is this paper arguing? How is it organized? Is the evidence strong? Only when you are satisfied with those answers does it make sense to move to paragraph-level revision and, after that, to sentence editing and proofreading. Working in this order prevents spending an hour polishing a section that you later realize needs to be cut or completely rewritten.

Section 44.2

Revising the Thesis

The thesis statement is the most important sentence in most academic essays. It tells the reader what the paper argues, what it explains, or what position it takes. It gives the writer a fixed point to organize around. A strong thesis creates a clear promise to the reader; the body of the paper exists to fulfill that promise.

In global revision, your first major task is to evaluate whether your thesis is doing its job. Many drafts contain thesis statements that seemed strong during the planning stage but, once a full draft exists, turn out to be too vague, too broad, too obvious, or misaligned with what the paper actually ended up saying.

What Makes a Thesis Strong

QualityDescriptionWeak ExampleStronger Example
Specific Makes a focused assertion, not a topic announcement or question "This paper is about climate change." "Agricultural communities in the American Midwest face water access crises intensified by climate change that existing federal policy has not adequately addressed."
Arguable Asserts something a thoughtful reader could question or want supported "Climate change is affecting the environment." "Voluntary corporate emissions pledges have consistently underdelivered because they lack enforceable accountability mechanisms."
Focused Covers territory the paper can genuinely develop in its given length "The history of American health care is complicated." "The defeat of universal health insurance legislation in the 1990s had lasting consequences for how Americans came to accept employer-based coverage as a norm."
Aligned Matches what the paper's body paragraphs actually argue Thesis argues economic causes; body focuses on political ones Thesis and body address the same central question with consistent framing

Common Thesis Problems in First Drafts

The following problems appear frequently in first drafts and require attention during global revision.

Problem 1 — The Too-Obvious Thesis
States something most readers would agree with immediately, leaving the paper with no real argument to make. A strong thesis makes a specific, arguable claim. The difference between "Education is important for young people" and "Students from under-resourced schools face structural disadvantages in standardized testing that test-prep programs alone cannot address" is the difference between an obvious statement and a thesis that can drive a paper.
Problem 2 — The Announcement Rather Than the Argument
"In this essay, I will argue that..." or "This paper examines three reasons why..." These phrasing patterns are weak because they shift the reader's attention from the argument itself to the process of making it. In most academic contexts, the thesis should state the argument, not announce that an argument is coming.
Problem 3 — The Thesis That Doesn't Match the Paper
The draft has evolved away from its starting thesis during drafting. Writers frequently discover, after drafting, that their thinking evolved and produced a better argument in the body than the thesis reflects. When this happens, revise the thesis to match what the paper actually argues — the evolution of your thinking is usually an improvement worth keeping.

A Practical Thesis Revision Sequence

How To
Revising Your Thesis During Global Revision
  • Step 1Locate your thesis statement in the introduction. If you cannot find a sentence that clearly states the paper's main claim, that is itself a significant problem — some papers need a thesis added during revision.
  • Step 2Write out your thesis as it currently stands. Then, without looking at the draft, write a separate sentence capturing what the paper actually argues based on the body's evidence and reasoning. Compare these two sentences. If they do not match, you have identified your first revision task.
  • Step 3Ask whether your thesis is specific, appropriately focused, and — if the essay type calls for argument — genuinely arguable. If it is too vague, sharpen it by adding a specific claim. If it is too broad, narrow it by specifying a context, time period, or particular mechanism.
  • Step 4Check whether your thesis tells the reader not just what you claim but, at least generally, why or how. A thesis like "Remote work policies increase employee productivity because they reduce commute-related fatigue and allow workers to control their environment" is stronger than "Remote work policies increase productivity" because it previews the reasoning the paper will develop.

Section 44.3

Revising Organization

Once you are satisfied that your thesis is clear and appropriately specific, the next global revision task is to examine the organization of the draft. Organization is the sequence and arrangement of the paper's main points, evidence, and sections. Even a paper with a strong thesis and good evidence can fail if the material is arranged in a way that is confusing, repetitive, illogical, or mismatched to the paper's purpose.

Organization is not decoration or convention. It exists to serve the reader's understanding. A well-organized essay creates a reading experience in which each part of the paper builds on what came before and prepares the reader for what comes next. The reader should always have a sense of where the paper is going and why each section belongs where it is. Poor organization forces the reader to do extra cognitive work — to hold things in memory waiting for connections that may never arrive, to go back and reread sections that were unclear because necessary information appeared too late, or to wonder whether two separate sections are making the same point or different points.

Common Organizational Structures

StructureHow It WorksBest Used For
Order of ImportanceMain points move from least to most important (or most to least)Argumentation; building toward the strongest point last
Chronological OrderMaterial arranged by sequence of events over timeHistorical processes, narrative, cause-and-effect sequences
Cause-and-Effect OrderExamines causes, effects, or both in relation to an event or outcomeAnalytical essays asking why something happened or what resulted from it

Problem-Solution OrderStates and analyzes a problem before proposing and defending a solutionPolicy argument, practical and applied writing

The Reverse Outline

The reverse outline is one of the most valuable techniques in global revision. Unlike a planning outline — which describes what you intend to write — a reverse outline describes what you have actually written. The process is straightforward: read your draft paragraph by paragraph and write a one-sentence summary of each paragraph's main point. When you are finished, read the list of summaries as a document in its own right.

This list reveals the actual structure of your draft with remarkable clarity. It strips away your prose style and the texture of your sentences and shows you, in skeletal form, what the paper is really doing.

Example 44.3.1
Reading a Reverse Outline
  • SituationA student writes a reverse outline of their five-page argumentative essay. The summary list reads: (1) Introduction with thesis; (2) Historical background on the policy; (3) More historical background; (4) Economic argument for the thesis; (5) Economic argument — similar point; (6) Social argument; (7) Counterargument acknowledged; (8) Conclusion.
  • Problems VisibleParagraphs 2 and 3 duplicate each other's purpose — both provide historical background. Paragraphs 4 and 5 appear to make overlapping points about economics. The counterargument appears very late (paragraph 7 of 8), giving it little room to be adequately addressed.
  • Revision PlanConsolidate the two historical paragraphs into one. Determine whether the two economic paragraphs make genuinely different points or whether one should be cut or merged. Move the counterargument earlier so there is space to address it fully before the conclusion.

As you read through your reverse outline, ask several questions. Does each item serve the thesis — or does some material address a tangential topic that doesn't connect to the paper's central claim? Is the sequence logical — does each main point build on the previous one? Are any items repetitive — does the same point appear in two different places? Are there any gaps — is there a step in the argument, a piece of context, or a necessary piece of evidence that the paper never provides?

The reverse outline also helps you see the proportions of your essay. If three of your eight body paragraphs are devoted to one minor supporting point while your central argument receives only a single paragraph, the balance needs to be corrected. Proportion communicates to the reader which ideas matter most, and an essay should devote the most space to its most important material.

Study Note — Proportion as Argument

The amount of space you devote to an idea implicitly communicates how important that idea is. If you spend two pages on background context and only one page developing your actual argument, you are telling the reader — without intending to — that the background matters more than the argument. Revision for proportion means consciously deciding how much space each idea deserves and then adjusting the draft to match that decision.

Section 44.4

Revising Evidence

A thesis makes a promise. The body of the essay must fulfill that promise by providing evidence — examples, data, quotations, factual details, analysis of sources — that genuinely supports the claims being made. In global revision, examining your evidence means asking not just "Is evidence present?" but "Does this evidence actually do what the paper needs it to do?"

Evidence in academic writing is not decoration. It is not included simply to make a paper look researched or to fill out required length. Evidence exists to give readers a reason to accept the paper's claims. It answers the reader's implicit question: How do you know that? What makes you say so?

Three Qualities of Strong Evidence

Relevant — The evidence actually connects to the specific claim it is meant to support, not just to the general topic.

Sufficient — The evidence provides enough support for the weight of the claim. Big claims require substantial evidence. A single anecdote is rarely sufficient for a broad generalization.

Accurate — The evidence is factually correct, properly attributed, and not distorted by being taken out of context.

Common Evidence Problems

Problem 1 — Insufficient Evidence
Claims are made without adequate support. A paragraph asserts a major point and then provides only a single vague example before moving on. During global revision, identify every major claim and ask whether you have provided enough evidence to justify it for a skeptical reader. If not, you need to either add evidence or qualify the scope of the claim.
Problem 2 — Irrelevant Evidence
A paper arguing that a policy failed due to poor implementation cites statistics about the policy's unpopularity — but popularity and effectiveness are not the same thing. Check each piece of evidence against the specific claim it is meant to support, not just against the paper's general topic. Evidence can appear logically related to the topic while actually proving something different from what the argument requires.
Problem 3 — Unexamined Evidence
Quotations, statistics, or examples are dropped into a paragraph without explanation. The paragraph consists mainly of source material with little surrounding analysis. Evidence does not speak for itself. After presenting evidence, a writer needs to explain what the evidence shows, why it matters, and how it connects to the claim. A writer who only quotes and never analyzes has hidden behind sources rather than doing the intellectual work the paper requires.
Example 44.4.1
Underdeveloped vs. Developed Evidence
  • Underdeveloped"Remote learning negatively affected students. Studies showed drops in grades. Students also reported feeling isolated. Clearly, remote learning cannot replace in-person education."
  • ProblemEach piece of evidence is dropped in without explanation. The logical path from "grade drops" and "reported isolation" to the conclusion that remote learning "cannot replace" in-person education is never established. What specific studies? How large were the grade drops? Were the effects uniform across all students? Is the conclusion proportionate to the evidence?
  • DevelopedA stronger paragraph would name specific findings, explain what they demonstrate, acknowledge any complexity (such as variation by student age or subject area), and explicitly connect the evidence to the paper's argument about the limitations of remote instruction — rather than letting the reader infer the connection.
Section 44.5

Revising Reasoning

Evidence does not carry an argument by itself. Between evidence and conclusion, there is always reasoning — the logical steps that connect what a piece of evidence shows to what the paper claims it proves. Weak reasoning is one of the most common problems in student writing, and it is one of the most important things to examine during global revision.

Every argument, at its core, involves a claim, evidence that bears on the claim, and reasoning that explains the logical relationship between the evidence and the claim. When reasoning is weak, a reader can see that evidence exists but cannot follow the logical path from that evidence to the conclusion. The connection feels assumed rather than demonstrated.

Example 44.5.1
Identifying Missing Reasoning
  • Argument"Professor Harris gives too much homework. Last week alone, I had three assignments due in her class."
  • The GapThe claim is that the professor gives too much homework. The evidence is that three assignments were due in one week. But the reasoning — the step that would connect those three assignments to the conclusion that the workload is excessive — is missing. How many assignments per week is reasonable? How does this compare to other courses? What harm does this workload cause?
  • What's NeededReasoning that addresses those questions: for example, citing institutional norms for expected study time, comparing this course's workload to similar courses, or documenting concrete negative effects on student performance. Without that reasoning, the evidence merely sits alongside the claim without supporting it.

Common Reasoning Problems

ProblemDescriptionExample
Logical Leap A gap between evidence and conclusion that the writer has not filled in; the connection is assumed, not demonstrated Citing a policy's unpopularity as evidence that it failed, without explaining the relationship between public opinion and policy outcomes
Hasty Generalization Drawing a broad conclusion from limited evidence Observing two examples of a pattern and asserting that the pattern holds universally
False Cause Assuming that because one event preceded another, it caused the other "The city installed bike lanes. Congestion increased. Therefore, bike lanes cause congestion."
Circular Reasoning Using the conclusion as evidence for the conclusion "This argument is strong because it makes a compelling case." (The claim and the evidence are the same assertion.)
Ignored Counterevidence Presenting reasoning that avoids or dismisses evidence that would complicate or contradict the argument Arguing that a treatment is effective without acknowledging studies showing mixed or negative results
Technique — Argue Against Yourself

One of the most effective ways to check your reasoning during global revision is to deliberately argue against your own paper. Ask: What are the strongest objections a skeptical reader could raise to my thesis? What evidence might someone cite against my argument? Where could my logic be challenged? If you find places where you cannot answer these objections, you have identified where your reasoning needs to be strengthened, qualified, or supplemented with additional evidence.

Common Mistake — Mistaking Style for Argument

Confident, assertive prose can mask weak reasoning. A sentence that sounds certain does not necessarily contain a well-reasoned claim. During global revision, read your paper's reasoning as if the confident tone were not there — as if every assertion needed to justify itself with logic and evidence. This habit reveals gaps that the surface confidence of your prose might otherwise conceal.

Section 44.6

Revising for Assignment Requirements

Every piece of academic writing exists in a specific context created by the assignment. An essay may be excellent as a piece of writing and still miss the assignment because it addresses the wrong question, uses the wrong number of sources, ignores a required section, or fails to use the required citation style. Global revision is the appropriate time to check, systematically, whether your draft fulfills the terms of the assignment.

This check may seem mechanical, but it matters enormously. Many student papers lose credit not because the writing is poor but because the writer lost track of what was actually required. Writers who become deeply absorbed in a topic can drift away from the original prompt without realizing it. Writers who were uncertain about the requirements from the start sometimes avoid rereading the prompt because they do not want to discover problems.

What to Check Against the Assignment

Assignment Audit Checklist
Questions to Ask While Holding the Assignment Prompt and Your Draft Side by Side
  • Question 1Does the paper address the assigned question or task? Compare the assignment's central question to your paper's thesis and main points. If the assignment asks you to analyze the causes of a historical event and your paper focuses primarily on the effects, you have not fulfilled the assignment regardless of how well you have written about effects.
  • Question 2Does the paper use the required types and number of sources? If the assignment specifies scholarly sources, check whether your sources qualify. If a minimum number of sources is required, count them. If certain source types are prohibited, check that none appear in your draft.
  • Question 3Does the paper meet the length requirements? A paper significantly shorter than required may not be developing its ideas adequately. A paper that far exceeds the limit may need to be focused more tightly.
  • Question 4Does the paper use the required citation style? Check that your in-text citations and works cited or reference page comply with the correct format — MLA, APA, Chicago, or whatever the assignment specifies.
  • Question 5Are all required structural elements present? Some assignments require a counterargument section, a summary of a specific source, a reflection component, or a particular kind of introduction. Check that all required elements are present and adequately developed.
A Common Pattern to Avoid

A particularly common mistake is allowing an interesting idea that emerges during drafting to pull the paper away from the assignment. A student writing an analysis of a poem might become fascinated by the poem's historical context and spend half the essay on biographical and historical background, leaving the actual analysis underdeveloped. The history may be interesting, but the assignment asked for analysis of the poem. Global revision is the time to identify this kind of drift and correct it before it affects your grade.

Section 44.7

Cutting, Moving, and Adding Sections

Once you have evaluated your thesis, organization, evidence, reasoning, and assignment alignment, you will likely have identified material that needs to be cut, moved, or added. These are among the most powerful global revision moves, and they are moves that many student writers resist because they involve significant changes to a draft they have already invested time in producing.

The resistance is understandable. Cutting material you spent hours writing feels like losing work. Moving sections means rebuilding transitions. Adding new material means more drafting. But these moves are often what separate a good paper from an adequate one, and they are far easier to make during global revision — before you have polished every sentence — than after.

Cutting

Material should be cut when it does not serve the paper's thesis or when it is genuinely redundant. In global revision, the test for any section or paragraph is simple: if this were removed, would the paper's argument be weakened? If the answer is no, the material should probably be cut.

Common candidates for cutting include introductory background that continues too long before reaching the thesis, digressions into interesting material that does not connect to the argument, paragraphs that repeat a point already made elsewhere, and content that addresses a topic tangential to the thesis. A useful practice when cutting significant material is to save it in a separate document rather than deleting it entirely. Knowing that the material still exists somewhere often makes cutting easier psychologically, and occasionally material cut from one paper turns out to be useful in a different context.

Moving

Material should be moved when it is in the wrong place — when it would serve the paper better if it appeared earlier or later in the sequence. Common situations that call for moving include background information that appears late in the paper when it would be more useful earlier, counterargument that is addressed before the main argument is fully established, and evidence that is presented before the claim it supports has been clearly stated.

When you move a section, you will need to revise the transitions around the section's new location. The sentences that connected it to its original neighbors will no longer apply, and new connections will need to be built. This is additional work, but it is worth doing when the move significantly improves the paper's logic and flow.

Adding

Material should be added when the paper has identified a gap — a place where the argument depends on a step that has not been taken, where a claim is made without evidence, where the reader would need information that the paper does not provide, or where a counterargument is raised but not adequately addressed.

Adding material during global revision requires returning to drafting mode. Before doing so, be specific about what you need. Write a brief note identifying exactly what is missing: "Need a paragraph here explaining the historical context for the policy change" or "The third main point has no evidence — need at least two sources." Specificity makes the additional drafting more efficient and prevents you from writing material that does not address the actual gap.

Study Note — The Paradox of Cutting

Experienced writers often say that the best revision move is usually cutting — and that cuts make papers stronger, not weaker. When you remove material that doesn't serve the argument, you clear the way for the material that does. A tighter, more focused paper almost always reads as more confident and more intelligent than a longer paper padded with tangential material. Learning to cut without regret is one of the marks of a mature academic writer.

Section 44.8

Global Revision Checklist

The following checklist brings together the key questions for global revision into a single reference. Work through this checklist with your draft before moving on to paragraph-level or sentence-level revision. Each item should be considered carefully — not simply checked off as a formality, but genuinely answered by looking at your actual draft.

Thesis

  • I can identify a clear thesis statement in the introduction.
  • The thesis makes a specific, focused claim rather than merely announcing a topic or asking a question.
  • For argumentative essays: the thesis is genuinely arguable, not a statement of the obvious.
  • The thesis matches what the paper actually argues in the body paragraphs.
  • The thesis is appropriately focused for the paper's length — not too broad and not too narrow.
  • The thesis gives the reader at least a general sense of the paper's reasoning, not just its conclusion.

Organization

  • I have created a reverse outline of the draft by summarizing each paragraph in one sentence.
  • The sequence of main points follows a logical order suited to the paper's purpose.
  • Each section and paragraph serves the thesis; no section addresses a tangential topic.
  • There is no repeated content that should be consolidated or cut.
  • There are no gaps in the argument — no missing steps, missing context, or missing connections.
  • The proportions of the paper are appropriate — the most important material receives the most space.
  • Transitions between sections help the reader follow the movement from point to point.

Evidence

  • Each major claim is supported by relevant, specific evidence.
  • Each piece of evidence actually supports the specific claim it accompanies, not just the general topic.
  • Each piece of evidence is adequately explained and analyzed after it is presented.
  • The evidence is sufficient in scale for the scale of the claims being made.
  • All sources are accurately and fairly represented in context.

Reasoning

  • At each major point, I can trace the logical path from evidence to conclusion.
  • I have avoided logical leaps that assume connections I have not demonstrated.
  • I have avoided hasty generalization — drawing broad conclusions from limited evidence.
  • I have addressed the strongest objections to my argument.
  • My conclusions match what the evidence actually demonstrates — I have not overstated my case.

Assignment Requirements

  • The paper addresses the assigned question or task, not a related but different question.
  • The paper uses the required types and number of sources.
  • The paper meets length requirements.
  • The paper uses the required citation style consistently.
  • All required sections or structural elements are present and adequately developed.

Cutting, Moving, and Adding

  • I have identified any material that does not serve the thesis and should be cut.
  • I have identified any material in the wrong place that would work better moved elsewhere.
  • I have identified any gaps that need new material added.
  • I have a specific plan for completing each revision move before I begin sentence-level editing.
Chapter 44 — Back Matter

Key Terms, Summary, and Review

Key Terms

global revision
The process of examining and improving a draft's largest, most fundamental elements — thesis, organization, evidence, and reasoning — before attending to sentence-level and grammatical issues.
thesis revision
The process of evaluating a thesis statement for clarity, specificity, argumentative strength, and alignment with the paper's actual argument, and rewriting it as needed.
reverse outline
A technique in which a writer reads a completed draft and writes a one-sentence summary of each paragraph, producing an outline of the draft as it actually exists rather than as it was planned.
logical leap
A place in an argument where the reasoning between evidence and conclusion is missing or incomplete, requiring the reader to make an inferential jump the writer has not provided.
counterevidence
Evidence that complicates, qualifies, or contradicts the paper's main argument, and which a thorough argument should acknowledge and address rather than ignore.
proportionality
The principle that a paper should devote the most space and development to its most important claims and evidence, so that the structure of the essay reflects the structure of the argument.
redundancy
The presence of repeated content — the same point made in two different places — that weakens organization and wastes the reader's attention.
assignment alignment
The degree to which a paper fulfills the specific requirements, questions, and constraints established by the assignment prompt.

Review Questions

  1. What is global revision, and how does it differ from sentence-level editing and proofreading? Why does the order of these revision activities matter?
  2. Describe three concrete techniques a writer can use to gain enough distance from a draft to read it as a reader rather than as the writer. Why is this distance important?
  3. List at least four qualities that distinguish a strong thesis statement from a weak one. Give a brief example for each quality.
  4. What is a reverse outline, and how does it help a writer evaluate the organization of a draft? What specific problems can a reverse outline reveal that reading the draft straight through might miss?
  5. What does it mean for evidence to be "relevant" in academic writing? Give an example of evidence that might be topically related to an argument but not actually relevant to the specific claim it is meant to support.
  6. Explain the difference between presenting evidence and analyzing evidence. Why does academic writing require both, and what does underdeveloped evidence look like in practice?
  7. Identify and explain three common reasoning problems that global revision should check for. For each, give an example of how that problem might appear in a student paper.
  8. Why should a writer check a draft against the assignment requirements during global revision rather than simply trusting their memory of what the assignment asked for? What is "topic drift" and how does it happen?
  9. What is the difference between cutting, moving, and adding material in global revision? Describe a situation that would call for each move.
  10. Why might a writer's thesis drift away from the paper's actual argument during drafting? When this happens, should the writer revise the thesis to match the body or revise the body to match the original thesis? Explain your reasoning.

Discussion and Writing Prompts

Prompt 1
The Revision You Resist
Think about a piece of writing you have revised in the past. What was the hardest revision move you had to make — cutting a section, restructuring your organization, rethinking your thesis? Why was it difficult, and what would have happened if you had not made that change? Write a paragraph reflecting on what that experience taught you about revision.
Prompt 2
The Reverse Outline in Practice
Find a draft of a paper you have written recently. Create a full reverse outline of the draft by writing one sentence for each paragraph summarizing its main point. Then read through your list of summaries and write a brief assessment identifying what works, what is redundant, what is out of order, and what might be missing. What does the reverse outline reveal that you did not notice while writing?
Prompt 3
Arguing Against Yourself
Choose a thesis statement from a paper you have written. Write three specific, reasonable objections a skeptical reader might raise against that thesis. For each objection, explain whether your paper addresses it, partially addresses it, or ignores it entirely. What would need to change to make the paper more responsive to those objections?
Prompt 4
Evidence Under Scrutiny
Examine a body paragraph from a draft you have written. Identify each piece of evidence in that paragraph and ask, for each: Is this evidence relevant to the specific claim? Is it adequately explained? Is it sufficient for the size of the claim? Write a revised version of the paragraph that strengthens the evidence and analysis based on what you find.
Prompt 5
The Assignment Audit
Retrieve the prompt for a paper you have recently submitted or are currently working on. Read the prompt carefully and then read your paper or draft. Create a checklist of every specific requirement in the prompt and mark each as fully met, partially met, or not met. Write a brief reflection on what the audit reveals.

Practice Activities

Activity 44AThesis Diagnosis

Below are five thesis statements from student drafts. For each, identify the problem (too vague, too broad, too obvious, announcement rather than argument, or likely misaligned with a paper) and write a revised version that corrects the problem. Be specific about why the original thesis is weak and what makes your revision stronger.

  1. "Social media has had a big impact on society."
  2. "In this essay, I will argue that the minimum wage should be increased."
  3. "Shakespeare wrote many famous plays that are still read today."
  4. "This paper will examine the relationship between exercise and mental health."
  5. "The decline of local newspapers is a serious problem with multiple causes and effects."
Activity 44BBuilding a Reverse Outline

Take a draft of an essay you are currently working on or have recently completed. Read through it paragraph by paragraph and write a one-sentence summary of each paragraph's main point. When you have finished, read through your outline and answer the following questions in writing. Then identify the three most important organizational changes the draft needs.

  1. Does the sequence of main points follow a logical order suited to the paper's purpose? If not, what order would work better and why?
  2. Are there any paragraphs without a clear main point? What should be done with them?
  3. Are any main points repeated across two different paragraphs? Should they be consolidated or is one version stronger than the other?
  4. Are the proportions of the paper appropriate to the importance of each point? Which sections receive too much space? Too little?
  5. Are there any gaps — places where a necessary step is missing from the argument?
Activity 44CEvidence Analysis

Read the following paragraph and evaluate its evidence. Identify: (a) what claim the paragraph is making, (b) what evidence is provided, (c) whether the evidence is relevant and sufficient, and (d) where analysis is missing or inadequate. Then write a revised version of the paragraph that strengthens both the evidence and the analysis.

Remote learning during extended school closures had significant negative effects on students. Many students struggled with motivation. Studies showed drops in grades. Students also reported feeling isolated. One student said she missed her friends. Clearly, remote learning cannot replace in-person education.
Activity 44DReasoning Check

The following argument contains a reasoning problem. Identify the specific problem (logical leap, hasty generalization, false cause, circular reasoning, or ignored counterevidence), explain why it weakens the argument, and then rewrite the argument to correct the reasoning.

The city installed new bike lanes on Main Street in the spring. By the following summer, traffic congestion on Main Street had increased significantly. The bike lanes are clearly responsible for the congestion, and the city should remove them.
Activity 44EGlobal Revision in Practice

Select a draft you are working on — ideally one you will submit for a grade — and complete the full Global Revision Checklist from Section 44.8. For every checklist item you mark as incomplete or problematic, write a specific note describing what needs to be done. Then prioritize your revision tasks by importance and create a revision plan: a brief ordered list of the changes you will make, starting with the most significant global concerns and ending with the smallest. Use this plan to guide your actual revision session.

Looking Ahead
Chapter 45: Paragraph-Level Revision

Chapter 44 addressed the largest elements of a draft — thesis, organization, evidence, reasoning, and assignment alignment. Once those foundational concerns have been addressed, revision moves inward to the paragraph level. Chapter 45 will teach you how to examine individual paragraphs for focus, development, coherence, and effective use of evidence. You will learn to revise topic sentences, strengthen development, improve transitions, and make decisions about splitting, combining, or repositioning paragraphs within the larger essay structure. The work of global revision you have done in this chapter ensures that the paragraphs you revise in Chapter 45 are the right paragraphs, in the right order, making the right points — so that your effort at the paragraph level is fully productive.

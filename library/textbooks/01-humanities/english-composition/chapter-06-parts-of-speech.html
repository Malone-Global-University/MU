Chapter 6 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Every sentence you write is built from smaller units — words that play specific roles in creating meaning. These roles are what grammarians call parts of speech. You already use all of them naturally when you speak or write, even if you cannot name them. The purpose of learning their names and functions is not to memorize definitions for a quiz. The purpose is to give you a vocabulary for understanding how sentences work, so that when a sentence fails — when it is unclear, weak, or awkward — you can diagnose the problem and fix it.

This chapter introduces the eight traditional parts of speech in English: nouns, pronouns, verbs, adjectives, adverbs, prepositions, conjunctions, and interjections. For each part of speech, the chapter explains what it is, what it does in a sentence, and how academic writers use it deliberately. The chapter also introduces an important distinction that will serve you throughout Part II: the difference between a word's grammatical form and its function in any particular sentence.

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to identify the parts of speech in academic sentences, understand how each contributes to meaning, and begin to see how strong academic sentences are built by choosing words that carry exactly the weight and function the sentence needs.

Opening Question

Consider this sentence: The government's rapid response to the economic crisis ultimately prevented a deeper recession. How many different kinds of work are being done in that single sentence? Something names the subject. Something describes how quickly it acted. Something identifies the action taken. Something names what was being responded to. Something shows the result. Every one of those jobs is performed by a different part of speech — and understanding which word does which job is the first step toward writing sentences like this deliberately, not just by accident.

Section 6.1

Why Parts of Speech Matter

Grammar instruction sometimes suffers from a reputation problem. Students are taught to label sentences — to circle the noun, underline the verb, identify the adjective — and the exercise feels disconnected from the work of actually writing. The labels seem like ends in themselves, not tools for anything.

This chapter takes a different approach. Parts of speech are not labels for their own sake. They are a vocabulary for describing what words do in sentences — and that vocabulary is enormously useful the moment you are trying to understand why a sentence is not working. Consider a sentence that feels vague and weak: There was a significant amount of concern expressed by the committee about the proposal. Something is wrong with it, but naming the problem requires understanding that there was is an existential construction that buries the real subject, that expressed by the committee is a passive construction that hides the agent, and that a significant amount of concern is a noun phrase that converts an action into a thing. In other words: diagnosing the sentence requires a working knowledge of verbs, nouns, and sentence structure. The revision — The committee expressed serious concerns about the proposal — is shorter, clearer, and more direct. The improvement is possible because the writer understands what the words are doing.

Parts of speech matter for a second reason: precision. Academic writing requires not just correct sentences but exact ones. When you choose a word, you are choosing not only a meaning but a grammatical role. The word rapid is an adjective that modifies a noun; rapidly is an adverb that modifies a verb or adjective. Investigate is a verb that places an agent in action; investigation is a noun that names the action as a thing. These are not interchangeable. Understanding what role a word plays helps you make the choices that produce sentences that say exactly what you mean.

Study Note

As you work through this chapter, resist the impulse to treat it as a memorization exercise. Instead, practice identifying parts of speech in sentences you read and write. The goal is not to name every word in a sentence before you write it — the goal is to build an intuition for what words do, so you can diagnose and revise effectively.

Section 6.2

Nouns and Pronouns

A noun is a word that names a person, place, thing, or idea. Nouns are among the most fundamental building blocks of English sentences because they supply the subjects, objects, and complements around which sentences are organized. When you name what you are writing about — a concept, a person, an event, an institution — you are using nouns.

Nouns come in several categories, each with different implications for academic writing.

Type of NounDescriptionAcademic Examples
Common nounNames a general person, place, or thinggovernment, theory, researcher, policy, institution
Proper nounNames a specific, unique person, place, or organization; always capitalizedCongress, Darwin, Harvard University, the Renaissance
Abstract nounNames an idea, quality, or concept that cannot be perceived by the sensesjustice, inequality, democracy, consciousness, validity
Concrete nounNames something that can be perceived by the sensesdata, document, laboratory, text, sample
Collective nounNames a group treated as a unitcommittee, jury, faculty, population, workforce
Count nounCan be counted; has singular and plural formsargument / arguments; study / studies; variable / variables
Non-count nounCannot be counted as individual units; typically has no plural formevidence, research, information, knowledge, feedback

Non-count nouns cause frequent errors in student writing. Words like evidence, research, and information are singular and cannot take an indefinite article in the same way count nouns can. You write a study but not an evidence; you write pieces of evidence or simply evidence without an article. Paying attention to whether a noun is a count or non-count noun will help you avoid these errors.

Common Mistake — Non-Count Nouns

Non-count nouns should not be pluralized or preceded by a/an as if they were count nouns. Writing researches, evidences, or an information is incorrect. When you need to refer to a unit of a non-count noun, use a partitive phrase: a piece of evidence, a body of research, a piece of information.

Pronouns

A pronoun is a word that takes the place of a noun or noun phrase. Pronouns prevent the tedious repetition of the same noun throughout a passage, but they introduce a specific responsibility: every pronoun must refer clearly to a specific noun, called its antecedent. Unclear pronoun reference — using a pronoun when it is not clear which noun it refers to — is one of the most common sources of confusion in student writing.

Type of PronounExamplesRole in Academic Writing
Personal pronounsI, you, he, she, it, we, they, me, him, her, us, themRefer to specific people or things; agree with their antecedent in number and gender
Relative pronounswho, whom, which, that, whoseIntroduce relative clauses; who/whom for people, which/that for things
Demonstrative pronounsthis, that, these, thosePoint to nearby or previously mentioned nouns; require a clear antecedent
Indefinite pronounsanyone, everyone, each, none, some, manyRefer to nonspecific persons or things; agreement errors are common
Reflexive pronounsmyself, yourself, itself, themselvesRefer back to the subject; should not substitute for I or me
Example 6.2.1
Pronoun reference — clear versus unclear
  • UnclearThe researcher interviewed the administrator after she completed the report.Who completed the report — the researcher or the administrator? The pronoun she could refer to either noun.
  • RevisedAfter the researcher completed the report, she interviewed the administrator.
  • Or:The researcher, having completed the report, then interviewed the administrator.Restructuring the sentence eliminates the ambiguity entirely.
Principle: Every pronoun must have a clear, single, unambiguous antecedent.

Section 6.3

Verbs and Verb Phrases

If nouns name the world, verbs animate it. A verb is a word that expresses action, occurrence, or a state of being. It is the most essential part of any sentence — without a finite verb, there is no sentence, only a fragment. But the choice of verb matters as much as its presence. Weak verbs produce flat, lifeless sentences; strong, precise verbs produce sentences with energy and clarity.

Verbs in English fall into three main categories: action verbs, linking verbs, and helping verbs.

Action Verbs

Action verbs express what the subject does, physically or mentally. They are the workhorse verbs of academic writing, and choosing precise action verbs is one of the most reliable ways to improve the clarity and force of a sentence.

Weak Verb ConstructionStronger Verb ChoiceWhat Changes
made an investigation ofinvestigatedNominalization removed; verb is direct
gave consideration toconsideredVerb restored from buried noun
had an effect onaffectedVerb is simpler and more direct
put forward an argumentarguedAction expressed directly
came to the conclusionconcludedOne word replaces four

The pattern illustrated in the table — converting a noun phrase back into a verb — is one of the most common and effective revision strategies in academic writing. When nouns are formed from verbs (a process called nominalization), the result is often wordy and weak. The original verb, restored, makes the sentence more direct and easier to read.

Linking Verbs

Linking verbs connect the subject to a complement — a word or phrase that identifies or describes the subject. The most common linking verb is be in all its forms (is, are, was, were, will be, has been, etc.). Other common linking verbs include seem, appear, become, remain, feel, look, sound, taste, smell.

Linking verbs are not inherently weak — they are appropriate when you are defining, classifying, or describing a subject. But overusing is/are/was as the main verb in every sentence can drain energy from academic prose. When a linking verb sentence could be converted to an action verb sentence without losing precision, the action verb is usually the better choice.

Example 6.3.1
Linking verbs — appropriate and overused
  • AppropriateDemocracy is a system of government in which power is held by elected representatives.The linking verb defines the subject — appropriate use.
  • OverusedThere was a growing awareness among economists that the model was flawed.The existential there was buries the subject and the action.
  • RevisedEconomists increasingly recognized that the model was flawed.One strong action verb replaces the weak existential construction.

Helping Verbs and Verb Phrases

Helping verbs (also called auxiliary verbs) combine with a main verb to form a verb phrase. The helping verb modifies the meaning of the main verb by indicating tense, aspect, voice, or modality.

The primary helping verbs are forms of be, have, and do. The modal helping verbs — can, could, will, would, shall, should, may, might, must — are especially important in academic writing because they express degrees of certainty, obligation, and possibility. Choosing the right modal is a matter of precision: may indicates possibility; must indicates necessity; should indicates recommendation; will indicates prediction or certainty.

Modal Verbs and Academic Precision

may / might — possibility: This finding may suggest a causal relationship.

can / could — ability or conditional possibility: Future research could clarify these results.

must — necessity or strong inference: Participants must provide informed consent.

should — recommendation or expectation: Writers should cite all borrowed ideas.

will — prediction or certainty: The next section will address this objection.

Academic Transfer — Verbs in Different Disciplines

Different academic disciplines use different verb registers. History and literary analysis favor active verbs in simple past: argued, proposed, rejected, described. Scientific writing favors passive constructions for methods: were collected, were analyzed, was measured. Social science writing uses modal verbs to hedge claims: may indicate, could suggest, appears to support. Pay attention to the verb conventions in the discipline you are writing for.

Verb Tense in Academic Writing

Tense consistency is a significant issue in academic writing. Different disciplines have different tense conventions, and students often shift tense within a paragraph without meaning to. The most common conventions are as follows: use simple present when discussing ideas that are generally true or when discussing a text's content (Orwell argues that language corrupts thought); use simple past when describing completed historical events or completed studies (The researchers conducted the experiment in 2019); use present perfect when discussing research whose relevance extends to the present (Several studies have demonstrated a link between diet and cognitive function).

The most important rule is consistency within a piece: do not shift tense without a logical reason for the shift.

Section 6.4

Adjectives and Adverbs

Both adjectives and adverbs are modifiers — words that limit, describe, or qualify the meaning of another word. The difference between them is what they modify: adjectives modify nouns and pronouns, while adverbs modify verbs, adjectives, and other adverbs. Getting this distinction right is not just a grammatical nicety; it affects how sentences are constructed and what they communicate.

Adjectives

An adjective answers the questions Which one? What kind? How many? How much? when applied to a noun. Adjectives can appear directly before the noun they modify (called the attributive position) or after a linking verb as a subject complement (called the predicative position).

Example 6.4.1
Adjective positions
  • AttributiveThe preliminary findings suggest a significant correlation.Preliminary modifies findings; significant modifies correlation.
  • PredicativeThe findings were preliminary, and the correlation appeared significant.Both adjectives follow linking verbs and modify the subject.

Academic writing tends to favor precise, specific adjectives over vague, evaluative ones. The word interesting is almost never the right choice in academic prose — it tells the reader that you have a reaction but does not describe what makes the subject interesting. Replace vague adjectives with ones that convey the specific quality you mean: unexpected, counterintuitive, methodologically rigorous, statistically significant, ideologically motivated.

A second caution: in academic writing, adjectives should be used when they genuinely add information, not as filler. Adding very, really, quite, or other weak intensifiers before adjectives typically weakens rather than strengthens a sentence. If a concept is genuinely extreme, choose a stronger adjective rather than modifying a weak one: not very large but enormous; not quite significant but substantial.

Common Mistake — Vague and Overqualified Adjectives

Avoid stacking multiple weak adjectives in front of a noun. A very important, highly significant, truly remarkable finding is less convincing than a remarkable finding. Each adjective you add should do new work. If it does not add specific meaning, cut it.

Adverbs

An adverb answers the questions How? When? Where? To what extent? How often? Adverbs can modify verbs (The study conclusively demonstrated a link), adjectives (a statistically significant result), or other adverbs (The results were remarkably consistently strong across all groups).

In academic writing, adverbs serve several important functions. Adverbs of degree indicate intensity or extent and are particularly useful for hedging claims (partially, largely, substantially, marginally). Adverbs of frequency indicate how often something occurs (consistently, rarely, frequently, typically). Sentence adverbs modify an entire clause and indicate the writer's attitude toward the claim (arguably, notably, significantly, importantly, surprisingly).

Type of AdverbFunctionAcademic Examples
MannerDescribes how an action is performedrigorously, carefully, systematically, convincingly
DegreeIndicates intensity or extentsubstantially, marginally, largely, partially, significantly
FrequencyIndicates how often something occurstypically, consistently, rarely, frequently, invariably
TimeIndicates when something occurssubsequently, previously, initially, ultimately, recently
Sentence adverbsModify the entire clause; signal the writer's stancearguably, notably, surprisingly, unfortunately, importantly
Adverbs and Hedging in Academic Writing

Academic writers use adverbs of degree to hedge claims — to indicate that a conclusion holds under certain conditions or with a certain level of confidence. This is not weakness; it is intellectual honesty. The data largely supports this interpretation is more accurate than The data proves this interpretation if the evidence, while strong, is not absolute. Learning to hedge precisely is a mark of academic maturity.

Section 6.5

Prepositions and Prepositional Phrases

A preposition is a word that shows the relationship between a noun or pronoun (called the object of the preposition) and another element in the sentence. Prepositions typically indicate spatial, temporal, or logical relationships. A prepositional phrase consists of a preposition plus its object and any modifiers of that object.

Common prepositions include: about, above, across, after, against, among, at, because of, before, behind, between, by, during, except, for, from, in, in spite of, into, like, near, of, off, on, onto, out of, over, through, to, toward, under, until, upon, with, within, without.

Prepositional phrases function as adjective phrases (modifying nouns) or adverb phrases (modifying verbs, adjectives, or other adverbs) within a sentence. A single sentence can contain several prepositional phrases, each adding a layer of information about location, time, cause, manner, or relationship.

Example 6.5.1
Prepositional phrases in an academic sentence
  • SentenceResearchers at the university published a report on income inequality in urban areas during the post-war period.
  • PP 1at the university — adjective phrase modifying researchers (which university?)
  • PP 2on income inequality — adjective phrase modifying report (what kind of report?)
  • PP 3in urban areas — adverb phrase modifying the noun phrase income inequality or specifying the context
  • PP 4during the post-war period — adverb phrase modifying published (when?)
Note: Removing any of these phrases changes the precision of the claim. Prepositional phrases add specificity.

Prepositional phrases are useful, but they can be overused. Student writing frequently becomes clogged with chains of prepositional phrases that could be simplified by converting them into single adjectives or adverbs, or by restructuring the sentence. This pattern is sometimes called preposition stacking:

Preposition Stacking
The members of the board of directors of the organization expressed concerns about the direction of the policy of the government.

Six prepositional phrases create a sentence that is difficult to parse. Revise by converting nouns to adjectives where possible, cutting where possible, and restructuring.

Revision of Preposition Stacking
Converting and restructuring
  • OriginalThe members of the board of directors of the organization expressed concerns about the direction of the policy of the government.
  • RevisedThe organization's board of directors expressed concerns about the government's policy direction.Four prepositional phrases replaced by two possessive constructions. Significantly cleaner.

Section 6.6

Conjunctions and Connections

A conjunction is a word that joins words, phrases, or clauses. Conjunctions are the most explicitly logical of all the parts of speech — their job is to signal the relationship between the elements they connect. When you choose a conjunction carelessly, you obscure the logic of your argument. When you choose it precisely, you make the relationship between ideas visible and clear.

There are three types of conjunctions in English, each with a distinct role: coordinating conjunctions, subordinating conjunctions, and correlative conjunctions.

Coordinating Conjunctions

Coordinating conjunctions join grammatically equal elements: two words, two phrases, or two independent clauses. There are seven of them, remembered by the acronym FANBOYS: For, And, Nor, But, Or, Yet, So. Each one signals a different logical relationship.

ConjunctionRelationshipAcademic Example
andAddition, continuationThe study examined income levels and educational attainment.
butContrast, contradictionThe argument was compelling, but the evidence was limited.
orAlternativeThe government must raise taxes or reduce spending.
norNegative alternativeThe hypothesis was neither tested nor supported by the data.
forReason (formal)The policy failed, for it addressed symptoms rather than causes.
yetContrast (often stronger than but)The evidence was suggestive, yet not conclusive.
soConsequence, resultThe sample was small, so generalization requires caution.
Common Mistake — Comma Splice

When a coordinating conjunction joins two independent clauses, a comma must appear before the conjunction: The sample was small, so generalization requires caution. If you use a comma without a conjunction, the result is a comma splice — one of the most common sentence errors in academic writing: The sample was small, generalization requires caution. [error] Always include the conjunction or restructure the sentence.

Subordinating Conjunctions

Subordinating conjunctions introduce dependent clauses and signal how the dependent clause relates to the independent clause. Unlike coordinating conjunctions, which join equals, subordinating conjunctions create hierarchy: the clause they introduce depends on the main clause to be complete.

Subordinating conjunctions are among the most precise logical connectors available to writers. Choosing the wrong one changes the meaning of the sentence fundamentally. The word because asserts cause; although asserts concession; if asserts condition; when asserts time. These are not interchangeable.

RelationshipSubordinating ConjunctionsExample
Cause / Reasonbecause, since, asBecause the data was incomplete, the study was delayed.
Conditionif, unless, provided that, as long asUnless the sample size increases, the results will remain inconclusive.
Contrast / Concessionalthough, though, even though, while, whereasAlthough the theory was widely accepted, evidence against it accumulated.
Timewhen, after, before, until, once, wheneverOnce the data was collected, analysis could begin.
Purposeso that, in order thatParticipants were kept anonymous so that responses would be candid.

Correlative Conjunctions

Correlative conjunctions come in pairs and join parallel elements of equal grammatical weight. The most common pairs are: both…and, either…or, neither…nor, not only…but also, whether…or.

A key rule for correlative conjunctions is parallel structure: the elements joined by each half of the pair must be the same grammatical type. If the first element is a noun phrase, the second must also be a noun phrase. If the first is an infinitive, the second must also be an infinitive.

Example 6.6.1
Correlative conjunctions and parallel structure
  • ErrorThe policy not only failed to reduce poverty but also an increase in inequality occurred.The first element (failed to reduce poverty) is a verb phrase; the second (an increase in inequality occurred) is an independent clause. Not parallel.
  • RevisedThe policy not only failed to reduce poverty but also contributed to an increase in inequality.Both elements are now verb phrases: failed to reduce… and contributed to…. Parallel.
Rule: With correlative conjunctions, what follows the first conjunction must be grammatically parallel to what follows the second.

Conjunctions Versus Conjunctive Adverbs

Students sometimes confuse conjunctions with conjunctive adverbs — words like however, therefore, moreover, consequently, furthermore, nevertheless, in contrast. These words look similar in function: they seem to join ideas. But they are not conjunctions and cannot join two independent clauses the way coordinating conjunctions can. A conjunctive adverb requires a semicolon before it and a comma after it when it connects two independent clauses.

Conjunction (needs comma before)Conjunctive Adverb (needs semicolon before, comma after)
The sample was small, but the results were suggestive.The sample was small; however, the results were suggestive.
The policy was controversial, so it was modified.The policy was controversial; consequently, it was modified.
Evidence was gathered, and conclusions were drawn.Evidence was gathered; furthermore, conclusions were drawn.

Section 6.7

Function Versus Form

One of the most important ideas in this chapter is also one that many grammar courses skip: a word's form — what category it appears to belong to based on its spelling and typical use — is not always the same as its function — the role it actually plays in a particular sentence. In English, many words can serve as more than one part of speech depending on how they are used.

This is not a complication for its own sake. Understanding that function determines part of speech is essential for accurate sentence analysis, and it explains why simply memorizing lists of nouns or adjectives can only take you so far. The part of speech a word belongs to must be determined by looking at the sentence it is in, not by looking at the word in isolation.

WordAs a NounAs a VerbAs an Adjective
researchThe research was extensive.She will research the topic.a research question
studyThe study was published.We will study this chapter.the study group
complexan apartment complex(rarely used as verb)a complex argument
fast(uncommon)She chose to fast.a fast reader
governmentThe government acted.(not used as verb)a government policy

The same principle applies to words that might be categorized as adverbs when they function differently in a specific sentence. The word just can be an adverb of degree (just barely), an adverb of time (I just arrived), an adjective (a just outcome), or even a noun in some contexts. The only way to determine its part of speech is to look at what it modifies or what role it plays in the sentence.

Key Principle — Function Determines Part of Speech

To identify the part of speech of a word in a sentence, ask: What job is this word doing? Is it naming something (noun function)? Is it expressing action or being (verb function)? Is it modifying a noun (adjective function)? Is it modifying a verb, adjective, or adverb (adverb function)? Is it connecting elements (conjunction function)? Is it showing a relationship between a noun and another word (preposition function)? The answer to those questions determines the part of speech in that sentence.

Example 6.7.1
The word "light" across sentence roles
  • NounThe light in the laboratory was insufficient for accurate reading.Light is the subject noun — it names a thing.
  • VerbThe researchers decided to light the workspace differently for the second round of tests.Light is the main verb — it expresses an action.
  • AdjectiveThe panel recommended a light revision of the original policy.Light modifies the noun revision — it functions as an adjective.
Same word, three different parts of speech — determined entirely by function in the sentence.

Interjections

For completeness, the eighth traditional part of speech should be mentioned: interjections are words or phrases that express emotion or exclamation and are grammatically independent of the rest of the sentence (Oh, Well, Indeed, Ah). In academic writing, interjections are extremely rare and should generally be avoided. The closest academic equivalent is the sentence adverb used to signal the writer's attitude (notably, unfortunately, remarkably), which does not function as an interjection but performs some of the same signaling work in a far more appropriate register.

(b) A government report was released yesterday. (c) She was asked to report her findings to the committee. (d) The report contained three recommendations.

Section 6.8

Parts of Speech in Academic Sentences

Everything in this chapter has been building toward a practical skill: the ability to look at an academic sentence and understand what every word is doing. This skill matters for two reasons. First, when you read academic texts, being able to identify how the parts of a sentence work together helps you understand complex sentences more quickly and accurately. Second, when you write, being able to think about what each word is doing allows you to revise with precision — to identify exactly what is creating vagueness, weakness, or confusion in your own sentences.

Let us apply the chapter's concepts to a real academic sentence and trace how the parts of speech work together to produce clear, specific meaning.

Example 6.8.1
Full sentence analysis
  • SentenceAlthough economic growth accelerated significantly during this period, persistent income inequality undermined the long-term stability of the region.
  • AlthoughSubordinating conjunction — introduces the dependent clause and signals a concession (contrast)
  • economicAdjective — modifies growth
  • growthNoun — subject of the dependent clause
  • acceleratedVerb (action) — main verb of the dependent clause
  • significantlyAdverb (degree/manner) — modifies accelerated
  • during this periodPrepositional phrase — adverb phrase modifying accelerated (when?)
  • persistentAdjective — modifies income inequality
  • income inequalityNoun phrase — subject of the independent clause (income here functions as an adjective modifying inequality)
  • underminedVerb (action) — main verb of the independent clause; strong, precise, active verb
  • the long-term stabilityNoun phrase — direct object of undermined; long-term is a hyphenated adjective modifying stability
  • of the regionPrepositional phrase — adjective phrase modifying stability (which stability?)
Observation: The sentence works because its parts are doing precise jobs. The subordinating conjunction signals concession. The action verb is strong and specific. The adjectives and prepositional phrases narrow the nouns precisely.

Now consider how this analysis can inform revision. Suppose a student had written the sentence differently: There was economic growth in the period, but inequality was still there and stability was affected. The nouns are vague (growth, inequality, stability), the verbs are weak (was, was, was affected), the adjectives are absent, and the logical relationship between the two clauses is underspecified. The coordinating conjunction but signals contrast, but it does not tell us what kind of contrast — that the growth was real but the inequality persisted and caused damage. The analysis of parts of speech reveals exactly what is missing and points toward the revision.

This is the payoff of grammatical awareness: not the ability to label every word in a sentence, but the ability to diagnose why sentences fail and to revise them into sentences that succeed.

Writer's Strategy — Parts of Speech as a Revision Lens

When a sentence feels weak or vague, ask yourself: (1) Is the main verb an action verb or a weak linking verb / nominalization? (2) Are the nouns specific, or could they be more precise? (3) Are the adjectives doing real work, or are they vague or redundant? (4) Does the conjunction I used accurately represent the logical relationship between the ideas? These four questions, applied quickly during revision, can transform a flat sentence into a precise one.

Chapter 6 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 6.1Parts of speech are a vocabulary for understanding what words do in sentences. This knowledge is practical: it allows writers to diagnose why sentences fail and to revise them with precision.
  • Sec 6.2Nouns name persons, places, things, and ideas. Non-count nouns cannot be pluralized or used with a/an. Pronouns replace nouns; every pronoun must have a clear, unambiguous antecedent.
  • Sec 6.3Verbs express action, occurrence, or state. Action verbs are often stronger than linking verbs or nominalized constructions. Modal verbs express degrees of certainty, obligation, and possibility. Tense should be consistent within a piece of writing.
  • Sec 6.4Adjectives modify nouns and pronouns; adverbs modify verbs, adjectives, and other adverbs. Precise, specific modifiers strengthen academic sentences; vague adjectives and weak intensifiers weaken them.
  • Sec 6.5Prepositional phrases add information about location, time, cause, manner, and relationship. Preposition stacking creates unnecessary complexity; possessives and adjective forms can often replace chains of prepositional phrases.
  • Sec 6.6Coordinating conjunctions join equals; subordinating conjunctions introduce dependent clauses; correlative conjunctions come in pairs and require parallel structure. Conjunctions signal logical relationships and must be chosen precisely. Conjunctive adverbs require semicolons, not commas, to join independent clauses.
  • Sec 6.7Function determines part of speech, not form alone. Many words can serve as different parts of speech depending on how they are used. Always determine a word's part of speech by examining its role in the specific sentence.
  • Sec 6.8Analyzing the parts of speech in academic sentences reveals how precision is achieved. Applying this analysis to your own writing — asking whether verbs are strong, nouns are specific, adjectives are earning their place, and conjunctions are logically accurate — is one of the most powerful revision strategies available.

Key Terms Review

noun
A word that names a person, place, thing, or idea. Nouns serve as subjects, objects, and complements in sentences.
pronoun
A word that replaces a noun or noun phrase. Pronouns must agree with and clearly refer to their antecedents.
antecedent
The noun that a pronoun refers to or replaces. Every pronoun must have a clear, unambiguous antecedent.
non-count noun
A noun that names an uncountable quantity; it cannot be pluralized or preceded by a/an. Examples: evidence, research, information.
action verb
A verb that expresses something the subject does, physically or mentally. Strong action verbs produce clearer, more energetic academic sentences.
linking verb
A verb that connects the subject to a complement describing or identifying it. The most common linking verb is be in all its forms.
helping verb
An auxiliary verb that combines with a main verb to form a verb phrase, indicating tense, aspect, voice, or modality.
modal verb
A helping verb expressing certainty, possibility, ability, necessity, or recommendation. Examples: may, must, should, will, could.
nominalization
The conversion of a verb or adjective into a noun form. Excessive nominalization weakens sentences; restoring the original verb often strengthens them.
adjective
A word that modifies a noun or pronoun, answering the questions Which one? What kind? How many? How much?
adverb
A word that modifies a verb, adjective, or other adverb, answering the questions How? When? Where? To what extent? How often?
preposition
A word that shows the relationship between its object and another element in the sentence, typically indicating location, time, cause, or manner.
prepositional phrase
A phrase consisting of a preposition, its object, and any modifiers. Functions as an adjective or adverb within the sentence.
coordinating conjunction
One of seven connectors (FANBOYS: for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so) that join grammatically equal elements.
subordinating conjunction
A word or phrase that introduces a dependent clause and signals its logical relationship to the independent clause.
correlative conjunction
A pair of conjunctions that join parallel elements. Examples: both…and, either…or, not only…but also.
conjunctive adverb
A word like however, therefore, moreover that signals a logical relationship between clauses. Requires a semicolon before it and a comma after when joining independent clauses.
function vs. form
The distinction between what grammatical category a word typically belongs to (form) and the role it actually plays in a specific sentence (function). Function always determines part of speech.

Error Analysis

Each item below contains an error related to parts of speech. Identify the error and rewrite the sentence correctly.

Error 1 — Non-count noun
The researchers gathered many evidences before publishing their findings.

What is wrong with evidences? Rewrite the sentence correctly.

Error 2 — Pronoun reference
The senator met with the economist before she announced the new tax policy.

Who announced the policy? Rewrite to make the reference unambiguous.

Error 3 — Conjunctive adverb punctuation
The methodology was rigorous, however the sample size was a significant limitation.

Identify the punctuation error and rewrite the sentence correctly.

Error 4 — Faulty parallelism with correlative conjunction
The policy not only reduced carbon emissions but also an improvement in air quality occurred.

What parallel structure rule is being violated? Rewrite the sentence correctly.

Error 5 — Nominalization weakening the verb
The committee made a decision to conduct an investigation of the allegations.

Identify the two nominalizations in this sentence and rewrite it using direct verbs.

Error 6 — Wrong subordinating conjunction
Because the argument was persuasive, the evidence was insufficient.

The logical relationship between these two clauses is contrast, not cause. Which conjunction should replace because? Rewrite correctly.

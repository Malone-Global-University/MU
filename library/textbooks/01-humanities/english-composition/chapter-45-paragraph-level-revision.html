Chapter 45 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Most student writers make the same mistake when they revise: they go straight to sentences. They fix commas, swap out a few words, clean up spelling, and call the paper revised. The result is a draft that looks cleaner on the surface but still has the same structural problems underneath. Weak topic sentences stay weak. Underdeveloped paragraphs stay thin. Evidence that was never explained still floats without connection to any claim.

Paragraph-level revision is the work that happens after you have examined the whole draft — after you have confirmed that your thesis is clear, your organization makes sense, and your evidence is substantial — and before you go line by line fixing grammar and punctuation. It is the middle layer of the revision process, and it is where many of the most important improvements happen.

A paragraph is a unit of thought. Each paragraph in an academic essay has a job: to advance the essay's central argument or explanation by developing one focused idea. When paragraphs are doing their jobs well, the essay flows. When they are not, the essay stalls, repeats itself, or confuses the reader — even if every individual sentence is grammatically correct.

This chapter teaches you to look at paragraphs as working units. You will learn how to evaluate each paragraph for focus, development, coherence, evidence, and proportion. You will practice revising topic sentences, strengthening development, improving transitions, and making decisions about when to split or combine paragraphs. By the end of this chapter, you will have a practical set of revision skills that work across essays, research papers, and writing assignments in all academic subjects.

Opening Question

Think of the last paper you submitted. Did you revise it by going through each paragraph as a unit — asking whether each one was focused, developed, and coherent? Or did you revise by reading through and fixing what seemed wrong at the sentence level? Most writers do the latter. This chapter teaches you to do the former, and explains why the order matters.

Section 45.1

Revising Paragraph Focus

What Focus Means in a Paragraph

When a paragraph is focused, every sentence in it is doing the same job. Every sentence relates to the paragraph's controlling idea. There are no detours, no distractions, no sentences that would fit better somewhere else. The reader can move from the first sentence to the last and feel that the whole paragraph has been about one clear thing.

Focus is not the same as length. A short paragraph can be perfectly focused. A long paragraph can lose focus completely. Focus is about unity: the degree to which all the parts of a paragraph belong together and point in the same direction.

When a paragraph loses focus, it usually happens in one of two ways. The first is topic drift, where the paragraph starts with one idea and slowly migrates toward a different one without the writer noticing. The second is overcrowding, where the writer tries to address too many separate ideas in a single paragraph. Both problems make the paragraph harder to follow, and both are common in first drafts.

Why Focus Matters

Academic readers expect each paragraph to deliver on the promise made in its topic sentence. When a paragraph begins by claiming it will discuss one thing and then wanders into another, the reader loses confidence in the writer's organization. They also lose track of how any particular idea connects to the essay's thesis. The paragraph becomes noise rather than signal.

Focus also matters for the writer's own clarity. When you sit down to revise a paragraph and find that it is pulling in two directions at once, that is often a sign that you have not fully separated two ideas in your own thinking. Revising for focus is not just fixing writing — it is also a form of thinking through a problem more carefully.

How to Check a Paragraph for Focus

There is a simple test for paragraph focus. Read the paragraph and then ask: What is this paragraph really about? If you can answer that question in one phrase — "this paragraph is about the financial costs of urban sprawl" — the paragraph is probably focused. If you find yourself saying "well, it is partly about the financial costs, but also about environmental effects, and it touches on community planning issues," the paragraph has a focus problem.

A related test is to read each sentence in the paragraph individually and ask: Does this sentence develop the controlling idea I identified? If a sentence relates to something else — even something important to the essay — it belongs in a different paragraph or a different location in the essay.

A Model Revision

Before Revision — Unfocused
Urban sprawl creates significant financial burdens for municipalities. When cities expand outward rather than inward, they must build new roads, water lines, sewer systems, and schools at great expense. Property taxes from new suburban developments often fail to cover the infrastructure costs they generate. Many environmentalists have also raised concerns about the ecological impact of sprawl. Wetlands and farmland are lost when developers build on the urban fringe. The loss of green space affects local air quality and reduces biodiversity. Some urban planners have proposed smart growth policies to address these issues, though implementation varies widely from city to city.
After Revision — Focused
Urban sprawl creates significant financial burdens for municipalities. When cities expand outward rather than inward, they must build new roads, water lines, sewer systems, and schools to serve the new developments. These infrastructure investments are costly, and the property tax revenue generated by suburban expansion often falls short of covering them. As a result, many municipalities that permitted aggressive outward expansion in previous decades now face budget shortfalls tied directly to the infrastructure demands of those decisions.

The revised paragraph stays on one idea: the financial costs. The environmental sentences have been removed and belong in a separate paragraph. The revised version is sharper, easier to follow, and more persuasive because every sentence contributes to a single point.

Common Mistake

Do not confuse mentioning multiple aspects of a topic with developing a single idea. A focused paragraph does not mean a shallow paragraph. A focused paragraph means all the depth is pointed in the same direction.

Section 45.2

Revising Topic Sentences

What a Topic Sentence Is and Is Not

A topic sentence is not simply the first sentence of a paragraph. A topic sentence is the sentence that states or implies the controlling idea of the paragraph — the idea that all the other sentences will develop, explain, or support.

Many effective paragraphs do begin with their topic sentences, and for academic writing, this is usually the strongest approach. Placing the topic sentence first tells the reader immediately what the paragraph will be about. It makes the writer's organization visible. But not all topic sentences are good topic sentences. There is a large difference between a topic sentence that simply announces a general subject and one that makes a specific, controlling claim that the paragraph can actually develop.

What Makes a Topic Sentence Weak

WeaknessDescriptionExample
The announcementStates that the paragraph will discuss something, rather than making a claim about it"This paragraph will discuss the effects of deforestation."
The vague statementUses language so broad that almost anything could serve as evidence"Deforestation has many effects."
The factual statementStates a fact rather than an interpretive or analytical claim"Deforestation has been occurring for centuries."
Topic without commentNames a subject but makes no controlling claim about it"The Amazon rainforest."

How to Revise a Weak Topic Sentence

When you find a weak topic sentence during revision, ask yourself three questions: What is this paragraph actually arguing or explaining? What specific claim does the evidence in this paragraph support? How does this paragraph's main point connect to the essay's thesis?

Your answers will help you write a stronger topic sentence. Often, the best topic sentence you can write during revision is one that captures what the paragraph is really doing — even if your original first sentence didn't say that clearly. Student writers often draft topic sentences before they fully know what the paragraph will contain. During revision, you can write the topic sentence you should have written, now that you know what the paragraph actually says.

It also helps to read the paragraph from the bottom up: look at the evidence you used and the explanation you provided, and then write a topic sentence that those details actually support.

Before Revision — Weak Topic Sentence
There are many effects of social media on teenagers. Studies show that social media use is connected to mental health. Many teenagers spend hours each day on platforms like Instagram and TikTok. Some researchers have found connections to anxiety, depression, and poor sleep. Parents and educators have expressed concern. On the other hand, some research shows positive social connections for isolated teens.
After Revision — Specific Controlling Claim
Excessive social media use has been linked to significant mental health challenges among teenagers, particularly increased rates of anxiety and sleep disruption. Research has found that teens who spend more than three hours per day on social platforms report higher levels of anxiety and depressive symptoms than those with more limited use. Sleep is particularly affected because many teenagers use devices late at night, disrupting sleep cycles essential for cognitive development. While some researchers note that social media can provide positive connections for socially isolated young people, the weight of current evidence suggests that high-volume, unregulated use presents real psychological risks during a critical developmental period.
Study Note

During revision, try writing a one-sentence summary of what each paragraph argues. If the summary you write is more specific and interesting than your actual topic sentence, rewrite the topic sentence to match your summary.

Section 45.3

Revising Development

What Development Means

Development is the work a paragraph does to make its controlling idea convincing, clear, and substantive. A developed paragraph does not simply state a claim and leave it sitting there. It provides evidence, offers explanation, gives examples, shows reasoning, or draws connections. It earns the claim it makes.

Underdevelopment is one of the most common problems in student writing, and it is a problem that clean sentences cannot fix. A paragraph can be grammatically flawless and still be completely underdeveloped. Development is not about sentence-level correctness — it is about the depth of thinking inside the paragraph.

Signs of an Underdeveloped Paragraph

Four Patterns of Underdevelopment
  • The assertion-only paragraph. Makes a claim but offers no evidence or reasoning. Tells the reader what to believe but gives no reason to believe it.
  • The evidence-only paragraph. Presents evidence but never explains what it means or how it supports the claim. Evidence is described and then abandoned.
  • The list paragraph. Lists several pieces of evidence without developing any of them. Each point gets one sentence, then the paragraph moves on — wide but shallow.
  • The definition-then-nothing paragraph. Defines a term and then stops, as if defining is the same as analyzing.

The Explanation Gap

The most common form of underdevelopment in academic writing is the explanation gap: the writer provides evidence and then assumes the connection between that evidence and the claim is obvious. It often is not obvious to the reader, even when it seems obvious to the writer.

Every piece of evidence in an academic paragraph should be followed by at least a sentence — usually more — of explanation that answers the question: So what? Why does this evidence matter? What does it show? How does it connect to the controlling idea of this paragraph and the thesis of the essay?

This explanation is where the writer's own thinking appears. Without it, the paragraph is just a collection of borrowed material with someone else's name on top.

Before Revision — Underdeveloped
Social media companies profit from users' attention. The longer users stay on a platform, the more advertisements they see. This generates revenue for the company. Teenagers are frequent users.
After Revision — Fully Developed
Social media companies have a direct financial incentive to keep users engaged for as long as possible, and that incentive shapes the design of the platforms themselves. The longer a user stays on a platform, the more advertisements they encounter, and advertising revenue is the primary source of income for companies like Meta and ByteDance, the parent company of TikTok. This economic model means that the platforms are not designed primarily to serve users' wellbeing — they are designed to maximize time spent on the platform. Teenagers, whose developing brains are particularly responsive to social feedback and novelty, are especially susceptible to the engagement mechanisms these platforms employ, including infinite scroll, notification systems, and algorithmic content feeds calibrated to reward continued viewing. Understanding this structural incentive is essential for evaluating the mental health debate around social media, because the design choices that drive engagement are not neutral or accidental — they are deliberate features of a business model.
Application

Take any paragraph from a recent draft. Underline the topic sentence. Then highlight every sentence that provides explanation or analysis rather than just stating evidence. If very little of the paragraph is highlighted, you need more development.

Section 45.4

Revising Evidence and Explanation

Evidence and Explanation Work Together

Evidence and explanation are not the same thing, and academic paragraphs need both. Evidence is the material — the data, quotation, example, statistic, case, or detail — that gives the claim something concrete to stand on. Explanation is the writer's own reasoning about what that evidence means and how it connects to the claim.

Student writers often treat evidence as the endpoint of a paragraph's job. They find a good source, quote or paraphrase it, and consider the paragraph finished. But evidence does not speak for itself. Evidence is always open to interpretation. The writer's job is to show the reader what the evidence means — from the writer's perspective, in the context of this particular argument.

Common Evidence Problems

ProblemWhat It Looks Like
The dropped quotationA quotation placed in the paragraph without introduction or explanation; the reader does not know who is speaking or what the quotation means in context.
The unexplained statisticA number appears but is not interpreted. What does it mean? Is it large or small relative to a comparison? What does it reveal about the claim?
The mismatched exampleThe example does not actually illustrate the claim the writer is making; the connection is absent or invisible to the reader.
The over-quotationSo many quotations fill the paragraph that the writer's own voice disappears. The paragraph becomes a wall of other people's words.
The redundant evidenceMultiple sources repeat the same point without adding new angles; the paragraph widens instead of deepening.
Before Revision — Evidence Without Explanation
"Screen time has increased dramatically for teenagers in recent years. According to Common Sense Media, teenagers now spend an average of seven hours and twenty-two minutes per day on screens, not including school-related use." This shows that screen time is a major issue.
After Revision — Evidence With Full Explanation
Screen time among teenagers has reached levels that researchers and public health advocates consider significant. According to a report by Common Sense Media, teenagers in the United States now spend an average of more than seven hours per day on screens for entertainment purposes alone — a figure that does not include the screen-based schoolwork that became standard during and after the COVID-19 pandemic. To put this number in context: seven hours represents nearly half of a typical teenager's waking day. The extent of this exposure matters not only because of the total time involved but because of what that time displaces — physical activity, in-person social interaction, unstructured play, and sleep. When screen use consistently crowds out these activities, the consequences extend well beyond the question of what content teenagers are viewing.
Common Mistake

"This shows that" followed by a restatement of the claim is not explanation — it is repetition. Explanation answers the question how or why, not just what. Explanation is where your own thinking lives.

Section 45.5

Revising Coherence

What Coherence Is

Coherence refers to the quality of a paragraph that allows a reader to move smoothly from one sentence to the next, always understanding how each new sentence connects to what came before it. A coherent paragraph has an internal logic that is visible to the reader — not just to the writer.

Writers sometimes produce paragraphs that make sense in their own heads but are difficult for readers to follow. The writer knows the connections between ideas because they have been living inside the argument for days. The reader has no such advantage. Coherence revision means looking at a paragraph from the outside — imagining a reader who does not already know where the paragraph is going — and checking that the path from sentence to sentence is clearly marked.

Sources of Incoherence

SourceDescription
Illogical orderSentences are not arranged in the order that makes the most sense. Ideas jump forward and backward.
Missing transitionsConnections between sentences are assumed rather than stated. The reader cannot tell whether the second idea is an example, a contrast, a consequence, or a qualification of the first.
Pronoun reference problemsPronouns like it, they, this, and these are used without a clear antecedent.
Sudden shifts in focusA sentence appears that seems to come from a different paragraph — a different argument or time period — without any connecting signal.
Key term inconsistencyDifferent words refer to the same concept without signaling that they are synonyms; the reader may wonder if the shift signals a change in meaning.

Strategies for Improving Coherence

Check logical order. Read the paragraph and ask: Does each sentence follow logically from the sentence before it? If not, try reordering sentences until the logic becomes visible.

Add transitions. Transitions signal the relationship between one idea and the next. Common academic transitions include furthermore, however, as a result, in contrast, for example, this suggests, nevertheless, and in other words. Transitions should reflect the actual relationship between ideas — do not add a transition just to have one.

Repeat key terms deliberately. Strategic repetition of key terms is not a writing weakness — it is a coherence technique. When you use the same precise term consistently to refer to the same concept, the reader does not have to guess whether you are still talking about the same thing.

Use the known-to-new pattern. Each sentence should typically begin with information the reader already knows from the previous sentence — something familiar — and then move toward new information. This pattern creates a chain of connected sentences where each new idea grows from what preceded it.

Clarify pronoun references. Pay particular attention to sentences that begin with This, These, It, or They. Ask: What does this pronoun refer to? If the answer is not completely clear, either replace the pronoun with a specific noun or add a noun after the pronoun for clarity — "This pattern of displacement" rather than just "This."

Before Revision — Incoherent
Urban heat islands affect cities significantly. Trees help reduce urban temperatures. Many cities lack green space. High-density development is often prioritized over parks. Climate change is making summers hotter. Pavement and buildings absorb heat. Low-income neighborhoods often have less tree cover.
After Revision — Coherent Chain
Urban areas experience significantly higher temperatures than surrounding rural regions, a phenomenon researchers call the urban heat island effect. This warming occurs largely because pavement, buildings, and other hard surfaces absorb solar heat during the day and radiate it back at night, rather than allowing the cooling effect that vegetation and soil provide. In cities where green space has been sacrificed for high-density development, this problem is intensified — there are fewer trees and parks to moderate temperatures. The effect is not distributed evenly across a city: low-income neighborhoods frequently have significantly less tree cover than wealthier areas, which means that residents who already face economic stress also bear a disproportionate share of the health risks associated with heat exposure. As climate change pushes average summer temperatures higher across many regions, this existing inequity is likely to become more severe.
Section 45.6

Revising Transitions

Transitions Between Paragraphs

Transitions between sentences within a paragraph are part of coherence revision, as discussed above. But transitions also operate between paragraphs, and this is where many student writers need additional work.

Between-paragraph transitions are the moments where the essay makes its structure visible. They are the places where the writer signals: I have finished one major point and I am now moving to the next. Done well, these transitions make an essay feel unified and forward-moving. Done poorly — or omitted entirely — they make the essay feel like a series of disconnected pieces rather than a connected argument.

Types of Between-Paragraph Transitions

The simplest form of between-paragraph transition is a transitional word or phrase at the opening of a new paragraph: Furthermore, However, In addition, By contrast, As a result. These are useful and workable, but they are also the minimum. A reader can understand from these signal words that a paragraph is adding, contrasting, or concluding — but they do not necessarily understand what the connection is to the specific content of the previous paragraph.

A stronger form creates an explicit bridge between the idea that just finished and the idea that is about to begin. This can be done by opening the new paragraph with a sentence that refers back to the previous point before introducing the new one:

Example of a Bridge Transition

"While the financial costs of urban sprawl clearly place pressure on municipal budgets, the environmental consequences may ultimately prove even more difficult to address."

This sentence ends one topic (financial costs) and introduces the next (environmental consequences), while also signaling a relationship: the environmental problem may be larger. The reader knows exactly where they are in the essay.

Another strong technique is to end a paragraph with a sentence that looks forward to the next point — a forward-pointing bridge sentence at the end of the paragraph rather than the beginning of the next one. Either approach works; the goal is to make the movement from one point to the next feel intentional rather than abrupt.

Study Note

Read your essay's topic sentences in sequence, paragraph by paragraph. If the topic sentences alone form a readable, logical progression of the essay's main argument, your transitions and organization are working well. If the topic sentences read as disconnected or random, your organization needs work at a level above paragraph revision.

Section 45.7

Splitting and Combining Paragraphs

When to Split a Paragraph

Sometimes a paragraph contains two distinct controlling ideas that each deserve their own paragraph. This often happens because a writer was not sure, during drafting, exactly where to separate one idea from another. The result is a paragraph that is either very long or that begins with one controlling idea and quietly transitions into a different one halfway through.

The clearest signal that a paragraph should be split is when you can identify two distinct topic sentences — two claims that the paragraph is trying to develop simultaneously. In that case, the solution is usually straightforward: find the natural division point, make the clearest sentence of each argument into the new topic sentence for each paragraph, and develop both paragraphs properly.

Situations That Call for Splitting
  • A paragraph begins with one point and ends with a substantially different one, with a visible seam in the middle where the shift happens.
  • A paragraph is very long — more than a page — and contains multiple distinct moves of reasoning.
  • A paragraph contains two pieces of evidence that support two different (though related) claims, and the paragraph never fully commits to either.
  • A paragraph makes a general claim and then introduces a specific example that actually raises a new, distinct point worth developing on its own.

When to Combine Paragraphs

The opposite problem is also common: two paragraphs in a draft are so closely related that they feel like halves of one idea rather than two distinct ideas. Treating them as separate paragraphs fragments the argument and disrupts the flow.

Situations That Call for Combining
  • The two paragraphs share essentially the same controlling idea; dividing them weakens rather than clarifies.
  • One paragraph is very short — two or three sentences — and does not develop a complete idea, but is really a continuation of the paragraph before it.
  • Two paragraphs are developing two sides of a comparison or contrast, and separating them loses the connection between the two sides.
  • The essay has too many short, choppy paragraphs that give the impression of superficial treatment rather than sustained analysis.

The Unit-of-Thought Test

The decision to split or combine should be driven by one question: What does a single paragraph represent in this essay? A paragraph is a unit of thought. If two ideas are genuinely distinct enough to require separate development, they belong in separate paragraphs. If two ideas are two parts of the same thought — two aspects of one claim — they may belong in one paragraph.

There is no rigid rule about paragraph length. A very short paragraph can be effective if it delivers a sharp, complete point. A very long paragraph can work if it is genuinely developing one sustained idea. But if a paragraph is very short because it is underdeveloped, or very long because it contains two ideas, those are revision problems.

Before Revision — One Overcrowded Paragraph
The economic impact of the 2008 financial crisis was severe and lasting. Unemployment rose sharply, peaking at over ten percent nationally, and many workers who lost jobs during the recession remained unemployed or underemployed for years afterward. Entire industries contracted dramatically, and small businesses that depended on consumer spending were particularly vulnerable. The psychological impact on affected households was also significant. Research on long-term unemployment consistently finds elevated rates of depression, anxiety, and stress-related illness among displaced workers. Children in households that experienced financial hardship during the recession showed measurable effects on academic performance and social development that persisted well into the following decade. The crisis, in other words, did not just damage household finances — it disrupted the developmental trajectories of an entire generation of children.
After Revision — Two Focused Paragraphs
The economic impact of the 2008 financial crisis was severe and lasting. Unemployment rose sharply, peaking at over ten percent nationally, and many workers who lost jobs during the recession remained unemployed or underemployed for years afterward. Entire industries contracted dramatically, and small businesses that depended on consumer spending were particularly vulnerable. The material losses were substantial and, for many households, permanent — not temporary disruptions but permanent reductions in lifetime earnings, savings, and economic security.
The crisis also produced significant psychological and developmental harm that extended well beyond household finances. Research on long-term unemployment consistently finds elevated rates of depression, anxiety, and stress-related illness among displaced workers, particularly among those who experienced extended periods without employment. Children in households that experienced financial hardship during the recession showed measurable effects on academic performance and social development that persisted well into the following decade. The financial crisis did not merely damage balance sheets — it disrupted the developmental trajectories of an entire generation of children who had no direct relationship to the financial instruments that caused the collapse.
Common Mistake

Do not split a paragraph just because it is long. Length alone is not a reason to divide. Split only when there are genuinely two separate controlling ideas that each deserve independent development.

Section 45.8

Paragraph Revision Checklist

Using the Checklist

A paragraph revision checklist is a structured tool for examining each paragraph in a draft systematically. Working through a checklist slows you down enough to notice problems you might otherwise overlook when reading for general impression. It also ensures that you apply consistent standards to every paragraph, not just the ones you happen to be worried about.

Some writers prefer to go through the draft once looking only at topic sentences, then again looking only at development, and so on. The order does not matter. What matters is that you address each dimension before moving to sentence-level editing.

MGU Paragraph Revision Checklist

Focus and Unity
  • The paragraph develops one clear controlling idea from beginning to end.
  • No sentences are in this paragraph that belong somewhere else.
  • The paragraph does not begin with one topic and gradually shift to another.
Topic Sentence
  • The paragraph has a clear topic sentence that makes a specific, analytical, or explanatory claim.
  • The topic sentence is not merely an announcement or a statement of topic without a controlling comment.
  • The topic sentence is as specific as it needs to be to guide the development of the paragraph.
  • If the topic sentence is implied rather than stated, the implied claim is unmistakably clear.
Development
  • The paragraph does more than state a claim — it develops, supports, and explains it.
  • The evidence or examples provided are appropriate and sufficient for the claim being made.
  • Every piece of evidence is followed by explanation that connects it to the paragraph's controlling idea.
  • The paragraph would be convincing to a careful, skeptical reader.
Evidence and Explanation
  • Every quotation or cited material is properly introduced with a signal phrase or context.
  • Every statistic or data point is placed in context and explained.
  • The explanation of evidence occupies at least as much space as the evidence itself.
  • The writer's own analytical voice is present in the paragraph, not buried under sources.
Coherence and Flow
  • The sentences in the paragraph are arranged in a logical order.
  • Each sentence connects clearly to the sentence before it and the sentence after it.
  • Transitions between sentences accurately signal the relationships between ideas.
  • All pronouns have clear, specific antecedents.
  • Key terms are used consistently throughout the paragraph.
Transitions
  • The paragraph connects clearly to the paragraph before it.
  • The paragraph connects clearly to the paragraph after it.
  • The transition between this paragraph and adjacent paragraphs accurately signals the relationship.
Length and Proportion
  • The paragraph is neither too short to develop its idea nor so long that it should be split.
  • If the paragraph is very short, it is either intentionally brief or needs to be developed or merged.
  • If the paragraph is very long, it either develops one sustained idea or should be split.
Chapter 45 — Back Matter

Key Terms

coherence
The quality of a paragraph in which each sentence connects clearly and logically to the sentences around it, making the paragraph easy to follow for a reader.
controlling idea
The specific, focused claim or interpretive point that a topic sentence makes about a subject, guiding the development of the entire paragraph.
development
The process by which a paragraph's claim is made convincing, specific, and substantive through evidence, examples, explanation, and analysis.
explanation gap
The space in a paragraph between a piece of evidence and the writer's analysis of what that evidence means; failing to fill this gap is a common form of underdevelopment.
focus
The degree to which all sentences in a paragraph relate to and advance one controlling idea without drifting into unrelated territory.
known-to-new pattern
A coherence strategy in which each sentence begins with information already familiar from the preceding sentence and then introduces new information, creating a chain of connected sentences.
paragraph-level revision
The stage of the revision process that examines individual paragraphs for focus, development, coherence, evidence, transitions, and appropriate length.
topic drift
A focus problem in which a paragraph begins with one idea and gradually moves toward a different, unrelated idea without explicitly signaling the shift.
topic sentence
The sentence in a paragraph, usually the first, that states the paragraph's controlling idea and guides all subsequent development.
transition
A word, phrase, or sentence that signals the logical relationship between one idea and the next, either within a paragraph or between paragraphs.
unity
A quality of a paragraph in which all sentences belong to and contribute to the same controlling idea; closely related to focus.

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 45.1Paragraph focus means every sentence in a paragraph develops one controlling idea. Topic drift and overcrowding are the two main focus problems. A focused paragraph is not a shallow paragraph — it means all its depth is pointed in the same direction.
  • Sec 45.2A strong topic sentence makes a specific, analytical claim rather than announcing a topic or stating a vague generalization. During revision, write the topic sentence that reflects what the paragraph actually does, not what you hoped it would do when you first drafted it.
  • Sec 45.3Development is the work that makes a claim convincing. The most common form of underdevelopment is the explanation gap: evidence is presented but not explained. Every piece of evidence needs the writer's own analytical follow-through.
  • Sec 45.4Evidence and explanation work together. Dropped quotations, unexplained statistics, and over-quotation weaken paragraphs. Explanation should occupy at least as much space as the evidence itself. "This shows that" is not explanation.
  • Sec 45.5Coherence is achieved through logical sentence order, accurate transitions, consistent key terms, clear pronoun references, and the known-to-new information pattern. Incoherence results from illogical order, missing transitions, and unclear pronoun references.
  • Sec 45.6Between-paragraph transitions make the essay's structure visible. A strong bridge transition refers to the previous point while introducing the new one. Reading topic sentences in sequence is a reliable test of whether transitions are working.
  • Sec 45.7Paragraphs should be split when they contain two distinct controlling ideas. They should be combined when two short, closely related paragraphs are actually two halves of one thought. The unit-of-thought test drives both decisions.
  • Sec 45.8The MGU Paragraph Revision Checklist provides a systematic tool for applying revision standards to every paragraph. Paragraph-level revision comes after global revision and before sentence-level editing.

Looking Ahead
Chapter 46: Sentence-Level Editing and Proofreading

Chapter 45 gave you the tools to revise paragraphs as units of thought — checking their focus, development, evidence, coherence, and structure before anything else. Chapter 46 will take you to the next layer: sentence-level editing and proofreading. Once you are confident that every paragraph is doing its job well, you are ready to move through each sentence for clarity, concision, grammar, punctuation, and correctness. The order matters: editing sentences in a paragraph that still needs structural revision is working in the wrong direction. Now that the paragraphs are sound, the sentences can be refined.

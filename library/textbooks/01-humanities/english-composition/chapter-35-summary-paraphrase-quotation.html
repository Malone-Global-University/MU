Chapter 35 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Every time you write an academic paper, you will work with sources. You will read articles, books, reports, and studies. You will find ideas that support your argument, challenge your thinking, or provide essential background information. The question is not whether to use those sources — the question is how.

This chapter teaches you the three fundamental techniques for incorporating source material into your writing: summary, paraphrase, and quotation. These are not interchangeable tools that you choose randomly. Each one serves a different purpose, fits different situations, and creates a different relationship between your voice and the source's voice. A skilled academic writer knows when to use each technique, how to execute it correctly, and how to keep their own thinking at the center even while drawing on other people's work.

Used well, these three techniques make your writing stronger. They allow you to join an academic conversation, support your claims with credible evidence, and show readers that you understand your subject deeply. Used poorly, they can make your writing feel scattered, dishonest, or hollow — a collection of other people's words with nothing of your own holding it together.

This chapter also addresses patchwriting, one of the most common and serious problems in student academic writing. Patchwriting happens when a writer changes a few words in a source passage without genuinely restating the idea in their own language. It looks like paraphrase, but it is not. Understanding why patchwriting is a problem — and how to avoid it — will make you a more careful, more ethical, and more capable writer.

Opening Question

You have found a research article that makes exactly the point you need. The author explains the idea clearly, precisely, and with authority. How do you bring that idea into your own paper? Do you copy the author's words? Do you rewrite them? Do you just describe what the article said? The answer depends on what your paper needs — and on which technique fits the situation. By the end of this chapter, you will know how to make that decision confidently every time.

Section 35.1
Three Ways to Use Sources
Defining Each Technique · What Each Preserves · The Central Principle

When you bring source material into your writing, you have three choices: you can summarize it, you can paraphrase it, or you can quote it directly. These three techniques represent different levels of distance from the original source text, and each one asks something different of you as a writer.

Understanding the difference between these techniques starts with understanding what each one preserves and what each one changes.

Technique What It Does Length Relative to Original Best Used When
Summary Captures the main point in the writer's own words Significantly shorter The broad idea matters more than specific details
Paraphrase Restates a specific passage entirely in the writer's own words Approximately the same The specific idea matters but the exact wording does not
Quotation Reproduces exact words of the source in quotation marks Same as selected passage The exact wording is itself significant
The Central Principle

Source material should support your argument, not replace it. Your writing must be driven by your thinking — your thesis, your claims, your analysis. Sources function as evidence. When a paper consists mostly of quoted or paraphrased passages strung together by a few transitional sentences, there is no real writer at the center. The goal is not to collect other people's ideas. The goal is to develop your own thinking, and to use sources as tools that help you do that.

Citation Always Required

Every use of source material — in summary, paraphrase, or quotation — requires a citation. This is not optional. Failing to cite is plagiarism regardless of whether you used the original words or rewrote them. The ideas belong to their original author even when you express them in your own language. Citation is covered fully in Part VII of this textbook; this chapter focuses on the writing techniques themselves.

Section 35.2
Summary
Definition · When to Use · How to Write · Common Mistakes · Example

What Summary Is

A summary is a condensed restatement of someone else's work, written in your own words. A good summary captures the essential idea, argument, or information of a source without reproducing its structure, its specific details, or its original language.

Summaries come in different scales. You might write a one-sentence summary of a short article, a paragraph-long summary of a book chapter, or several paragraphs summarizing a long, complex report. In each case, the defining characteristic is the same: you are compressing the source to its core meaning while expressing that meaning entirely in your own words.

Summary is the most common form of source use in academic writing, partly because it is the most flexible. It allows you to bring in ideas from many different sources efficiently, giving your reader the benefit of a broad conversation without forcing them to read every source you read. It also lets you control the level of detail. You can summarize an entire book in two sentences when all you need from it is its general argument. You can summarize a single section of an article when only that section is relevant to your point.

When to Use Summary

Use summary when you need the overall idea of a source but not its specific details. This is common at the beginning of an essay when you are introducing background information or explaining the existing conversation your paper enters. It is also common when you are surveying multiple sources that all support a similar point — rather than quoting or paraphrasing each one individually, you can summarize them together.

Ask yourself: does my reader need to know the specific evidence, examples, or reasoning from this source? Or do they just need to understand what the source generally argues? If the answer is the latter, summary is the right choice.

Summary is also useful when a source is long, complex, or technical, and the specific details would distract from your own argument. If you are writing about educational policy and you want to mention that a sociological study found a general relationship between school funding and student outcomes, you probably do not need to reproduce the study's methodology, statistical tables, or specific coefficients. A well-placed summary gives your reader enough to understand why the source is relevant without overwhelming them with detail.

How to Write a Good Summary

Writing a strong summary requires genuine comprehension. You cannot summarize a text you have not understood. Before you try to write a summary, make sure you have read the source carefully, identified the main claim or central argument, and understood how the supporting points relate to that central argument.

Summary Writing Process

Step 1. Read the source completely, without taking notes on your first pass. Your goal on first reading is understanding.

Step 2. Set the source aside and write down, from memory, what you think the main point was. Do not look at the source while you write this.

Step 3. Return to the source and check whether your memory was accurate. Identify anything you missed or misrepresented.

Step 4. Revise your notes until they accurately reflect the source's main idea.

Step 5. Write the summary using your notes, not the original text directly in front of you.

The technique of writing from memory before returning to the source is especially valuable because it forces you to process the material rather than copy it. If you summarize while looking at the original text, you are more likely to drift toward the source's language without noticing. Writing from memory makes your own language feel more natural.

A good summary is significantly shorter than the original, written entirely in the writer's own words, accurately represents the original's main idea, does not add the writer's own opinions or interpretations, and includes a signal phrase and citation.

Example 35.2 — Summary
Reducing a 120-word passage to a two-sentence summary
  • Original
    "The shift from handwritten to digital note-taking in schools has generated considerable debate among educators and researchers. Proponents of digital note-taking point to speed, searchability, and ease of sharing as significant advantages. Critics, however, argue that the physical act of writing by hand engages different cognitive processes than typing, including processes associated with encoding information into long-term memory. Several experimental studies have attempted to compare recall and comprehension across note-taking modalities, with mixed results. Some studies suggest that students who write by hand show stronger retention of conceptual information, while others find no significant difference when the quality of notes — rather than the method — is controlled for. The debate remains active and unresolved."
  • Summary
    Researchers and educators continue to debate whether handwritten or digital note-taking is more effective for student learning. While some argue that handwriting supports better memory and comprehension, others suggest that the quality of notes matters more than the method used to produce them.
  • Analysis The summary is less than a third the length of the original. It captures the essential tension — the debate between handwriting and digital note-taking — without reproducing the specific points about speed, searchability, or individual studies. It uses the writer's own language throughout and maintains the original's nuance by acknowledging both sides.
Common Mistakes in Summary

Including too much detail. A summary that lists all the supporting points and examples from the original is not really a summary — it is a compressed reproduction. Ask: if I removed this detail, would my summary still capture the main point? If yes, remove it.

Misrepresenting the source. Do not exaggerate, simplify unfairly, or remove qualifications that were important in the original. If an author argued that something "may be helpful under certain conditions," you should not summarize it as "the author proved this approach works."

Letting the source's language creep in. Even experienced writers sometimes produce summaries that include phrases borrowed directly from the original without quotation marks. Either place borrowed phrases in quotation marks or replace them entirely.

Section 35.3
Paraphrase
Definition · When to Use · How to Write Genuinely · Patchwriting Preview · Example

What Paraphrase Is

Paraphrase is the restatement of a specific passage from a source in your own words, at approximately the same length as the original. Unlike summary, paraphrase does not compress. It preserves all of the meaning of the original passage — including its details, its reasoning, and its qualifications — but expresses that meaning entirely in new language.

Paraphrase is more demanding than summary because it requires you to capture not just the main idea but the complete content of a specific passage. It also requires you to do this in entirely your own words, which means you cannot borrow the source's sentence structure, vocabulary, or phrasing.

When to Use Paraphrase

Use paraphrase when a specific passage from a source contains information or reasoning that is directly relevant to your argument, but when the exact wording of that passage is not what matters. Most source use in academic writing should be paraphrase, not quotation. Quotation is appropriate only in specific circumstances, which Section 35.4 addresses. When neither summary nor quotation is the right fit, paraphrase is usually the correct choice.

Paraphrase is particularly useful when a source passage is technical, dense, or written in a style that does not suit your paper. By paraphrasing, you translate the source into the voice and register of your own writing, making the information more accessible to your reader while preserving its accuracy. Paraphrase is also useful when you want to show that you understand a source thoroughly enough to explain it in your own words — this demonstrates genuine engagement rather than passive reproduction.

How to Write a Good Paraphrase

Like summary, good paraphrase begins with genuine comprehension. You cannot accurately paraphrase something you do not understand. If you find yourself uncertain about what a passage means, the solution is to read more carefully, look up unfamiliar terms, or seek additional context — not to begin paraphrasing and hope the meaning reveals itself.

Paraphrase Writing Process

Step 1. Read the passage carefully until you are confident you understand it fully.

Step 2. Set the source aside and write the passage's meaning from memory, in your own words.

Step 3. Return to the source and compare your version to the original. Check for accuracy: Did you preserve all the key ideas? Did you miss any important qualifications?

Step 4. Check for language: does your version contain any phrases borrowed from the original? If so, replace them or put them in quotation marks.

Step 5. Revise until your paraphrase is both accurate and genuinely your own.

What does "genuinely your own" mean? It means more than swapping synonyms. Replacing a few words while keeping the sentence structure and overall phrasing of the original is not paraphrase — it is patchwriting, which Section 35.8 addresses in full. True paraphrase means the sentence structure, the vocabulary, and the organizational sequence are all your own.

Example 35.3 — Paraphrase vs. Patchwriting
Three versions of the same passage — spot the difference
  • Original
    "Adolescents who report higher levels of sleep deprivation consistently demonstrate poorer performance on standardized assessments of attention and working memory, suggesting that insufficient sleep during developmental years may compromise cognitive functions essential for academic achievement."
  • Patchwriting ✗
    Teenagers who report higher amounts of sleep deprivation consistently show worse performance on standardized tests of attention and working memory, which suggests that not getting enough sleep during the developmental years may harm cognitive functions that are important for academic success.
  • Paraphrase ✓
    Research indicates that teenagers who are chronically underslept tend to perform worse on tests that measure focus and short-term memory, pointing to the possibility that inadequate rest during adolescence interferes with the mental capacities students need to succeed in school.
  • Analysis The patchwritten version substitutes individual words — "teenagers" for "adolescents," "amounts" for "levels" — while keeping the original sentence structure nearly intact. The genuine paraphrase restructures the sentence entirely, uses different organizational logic, and expresses the same idea in language that is naturally and completely the writer's own.
Common Mistakes in Paraphrase

Patchwriting. Changing a few words while keeping the original structure is not genuine paraphrase. It is a form of source misuse addressed fully in Section 35.8.

Changing the meaning. If the original said that a relationship was "possible" or "suggested," you cannot paraphrase it as "proved" or "demonstrated." Qualifications are part of the meaning.

Paraphrasing without citing. You must cite paraphrased material. The words are yours; the idea is not. Many students mistakenly believe that because they have rewritten something in their own words, no citation is needed. This is incorrect.

Section 35.4
Quotation
Definition · When to Quote · When Not to Quote · Mechanics · Common Mistakes

What Quotation Is

A direct quotation reproduces the exact words of a source, enclosed in quotation marks. Every word inside those quotation marks must match the original exactly — including spelling, capitalization, and punctuation — with one exception: you may use brackets to indicate minor changes and ellipses to indicate omissions.

Of the three source techniques, quotation is the most powerful — and the most frequently misused. Many student writers quote too much, too often, and for the wrong reasons. Understanding when quotation is genuinely appropriate, and when it is not, is one of the most important lessons in this chapter.

When to Use Quotation

Quotation is appropriate when specific conditions apply. It is important to understand these conditions before reaching for quotation marks.

Use Quotation When…Explanation
The exact wording is itself significant If you are analyzing a text — a poem, speech, legal document, or philosophical argument — the precise language is the subject of your analysis. You must quote because what you are studying is the words themselves.
The source is being discussed directly If your paper is about a specific author's argument, quoting anchors your analysis in the actual text rather than in your characterization of it.
The original wording is unusually precise or powerful Occasionally a source states something so specifically or economically that any paraphrase would lose something essential. In these cases, the exact language adds value.
The speaker's words carry special authority A policy statement from an organization, a formal definition from an authoritative body, or a public commitment may need to be quoted to carry its full weight.
When Quotation Is NOT Appropriate

Quotation is not appropriate simply because a passage sounds impressive, because you are unsure how to paraphrase it, or because quoting is faster than processing the material yourself. These are extremely common reasons student writers over-quote, and none of them serve the writing. If a passage is hard to paraphrase, that difficulty is a signal that you need to understand it more deeply — not a reason to copy it.

How to Use Quotation Correctly

Every direct quotation must be enclosed in quotation marks and cited. Beyond these absolute requirements, strong quotation use follows several additional conventions.

Quotation Mechanics

Keep quotations short. A quotation longer than two or three sentences usually suggests that the source is carrying your argument rather than supporting it.

Introduce every quotation. A quotation should never appear at the beginning of a sentence without an introduction. A "dropped quotation" — one that begins at the start of a sentence with no signal phrase — disorients readers and suggests the writer does not know how to integrate sources.

Explain every quotation. After a quotation, your job is not finished. You need to explain what the quotation means in context, why you chose it, and what the reader should understand from it. Never assume a quotation speaks for itself.

Use brackets for necessary changes. If you need to change a capital letter or clarify a pronoun's reference, use square brackets: "She argued that [the policy] would have unintended consequences."

Use ellipses for omissions. If you omit words from the middle of a quotation, use three spaced periods: "The committee noted that the proposal . . . required further review."

Do not distort meaning through omission. You may trim irrelevant material, but you may not use omission to change what the author was actually saying.

Example 35.4 — Quotation Used Well
A quotation with context, signal phrase, and follow-up explanation
  • Context The question of who benefits from city renewal projects has long divided urban planners and community advocates.
  • Quotation Sociologist Linda Farrow captures the central tension clearly:
    "Revitalization narratives almost always emphasize what is gained — new construction, rising property values, cleaner streets — while systematically obscuring what is lost: the networks, histories, and affordable spaces that low-income communities depend on."
  • Explanation This observation points to a fundamental limitation in how success is measured in urban policy: if displacement is not counted as a cost, then projects that displace thousands of residents can appear, statistically, to be unqualified successes.
  • Analysis Notice the three-part structure: context prepares the reader, the quotation is introduced with a signal phrase, and explanation follows to connect the evidence to the paper's argument. The quotation does not appear and disappear — it is surrounded by the writer's own analytical work.
Common Mistakes in Quotation

Over-quoting. Papers that consist primarily of quotations are not academic arguments — they are collections of other people's words. As a rough guideline, quoted material should make up a small fraction of your total word count.

Dropped quotations. A quotation that appears without an introduction — beginning at the start of a sentence with no signal phrase — disorients readers. Every quotation needs a lead-in.

Under-explaining quotations. Introducing a quotation and immediately moving on without explaining it leaves the reader to do interpretive work that is the writer's responsibility.

Misquoting. Changing a word inside a quotation without using brackets, or combining two non-adjacent sentences as one, is misquotation. It is a serious academic integrity issue even when changes seem minor.

Section 35.5

Signal Phrases

A signal phrase is the introductory language that tells your reader you are about to present someone else's idea. It typically includes the author's name and a verb that characterizes what the author is doing. Signal phrases work for all three forms of source use — summary, paraphrase, and quotation.

Signal phrases serve several important functions. They tell the reader that a source is coming before it arrives. They distinguish the source's voice from your own. They can also do subtle analytical work by characterizing the source's stance — whether the author argues, concedes, suggests, demonstrates, questions, or warns.

How to Construct Signal Phrases

The simplest signal phrase follows this pattern: [Author's name or identifying description] + [verb] + [source material].

Example 35.5 — Signal Phrase Variations
Three ways to position a signal phrase
  • Front
    Johnson argues that the policy failed primarily because of implementation problems at the local level.
  • Mid-sentence
    The policy, Johnson argues, failed primarily because of implementation problems at the local level.
  • End
    The policy's failures were rooted in implementation rather than design — a conclusion supported by Johnson's analysis of municipal records from the period.

Choosing Signal Verbs

The verb you choose in a signal phrase matters. Different verbs convey different relationships between the author and their claim. Choosing the right signal verb is not a mechanical task — it requires you to understand what the author is actually doing in the passage you are drawing from. Using "demonstrates" when an author is actually speculating misrepresents the source.

A common weakness in student writing is using the same signal verb repeatedly — most often "says" or "states." These verbs are technically correct but carry no analytical weight. Training yourself to choose signal verbs thoughtfully is a meaningful step toward more sophisticated academic writing.

argues
The author is making a contestable claim
demonstrates
The author is showing something through evidence
suggests
The author is proposing something tentatively
acknowledges
The author concedes a point, possibly against their main position

Section 35.6

Context Before Source Material

Every piece of source material — whether summary, paraphrase, or quotation — needs context before it arrives. Context is the analytical framing that tells your reader why the source is relevant and how it connects to your argument.

Think of it this way: your reader is following your argument, not the source's. When you introduce source material, you are briefly pulling your reader into someone else's work. Without context, that transition is jarring. With context, it is smooth and purposeful.

Context does not need to be long. In many cases, one or two sentences is enough. The essential question is: does my reader know why this source material is coming? Do they understand what it is supposed to show? If not, more context is needed.

Without Adequate Context With Context
Research has found that urban green spaces reduce stress. "Access to parks and natural environments within urban areas is associated with measurable reductions in cortisol levels and self-reported anxiety among adult residents" (Rivera, 2019, p. 44). This is important for city planning.
One line of research supporting green space in urban design focuses on measurable health outcomes. Rivera's study of adult urban residents found that proximity to parks correlated with reduced cortisol levels and lower self-reported anxiety — evidence that green space may serve a public health function as well as an aesthetic one.

The second version connects the source to the surrounding argument before introducing it. The reader understands why this evidence is being brought in and what it is meant to establish. Notice also that it uses paraphrase rather than a dropped quotation, and includes a brief explanation that moves the argument forward.

Section 35.7

Explanation After Source Material

Introducing source material with a signal phrase and context is half the work. The other half is explaining the source material after you present it.

Explanation is where your analysis lives. It is where you tell the reader what the source means in the context of your argument, why you chose this evidence, how it supports your claim, and what the reader should take from it.

A common shortcut student writers take is to present a quotation or paraphrase and immediately begin a new point. This treats source material as self-evidently meaningful, but it is not. A quotation that sits in the middle of a paragraph without follow-up analysis asks readers to do interpretive work that is actually the writer's responsibility.

The Source Sandwich Model

Every piece of source material builds a three-part structure:

1. Context — analytical framing that prepares the reader for the source

2. Source material — the summary, paraphrase, or quotation itself

3. Explanation — your analysis connecting the source to your argument

Example 35.7 — Full Source Sandwich
A complete paragraph showing all three components
  • Context The relationship between physical activity and academic performance has been documented across multiple age groups.
  • Source One longitudinal study tracking elementary students over three years found that children who participated in structured physical activity during the school day showed sustained improvements in classroom attention and task completion compared to students who did not (Chen, 2021, p. 112).
  • Explanation This finding matters for school administrators making decisions about physical education budgets: treating recess and gym class as peripheral to academic achievement misreads the evidence. Physical activity appears not to compete with learning time — it may actually support it.
The reader knows exactly why this source is here and what to understand from it. The source supports the writer's reasoning; it does not replace it.
Section 35.8
Avoiding Patchwriting and Source Misuse
What Patchwriting Is · Why It Happens · How to Recognize It · Detailed Comparison · Other Forms of Misuse

What Patchwriting Is

Patchwriting is one of the most widespread and least well-understood problems in academic writing. It occurs when a writer takes a passage from a source and makes surface-level changes — swapping synonyms, rearranging a few phrases, changing the grammatical form of a word — without genuinely rewriting the passage in their own language.

Patchwriting looks like paraphrase. The passage appears to be in the writer's own words because the original text is not reproduced exactly. But underneath the substitutions, the sentence structure, the organizational logic, and the conceptual framing are all borrowed from the source. The writer has not processed the material and expressed it genuinely — they have made cosmetic changes to someone else's prose.

Patchwriting is a problem for several reasons. It is a form of academic dishonesty because it gives a false impression of the writer's engagement with the material. It also produces awkward writing, because patchwritten passages often do not fit smoothly into the writer's own prose — they were never truly translated into the writer's voice. And it is a missed learning opportunity: genuinely paraphrasing material requires you to understand it well enough to explain it, which deepens your comprehension in ways that patchwriting does not.

Why Patchwriting Happens

Understanding why patchwriting happens helps writers recognize and break the habit. It usually occurs for one of three reasons.

First, the writer does not fully understand the source passage and is reluctant to admit that uncertainty. Faced with complex material, they do the minimum necessary to make it look like their own words without actually processing it. Second, the writer does not trust their own language. They feel that the source's vocabulary is more authoritative than their own, so they stay as close to the original as possible. Third, the writer is working under time pressure and taking shortcuts.

In all three cases, the solution is the same: deeper engagement with the source material and more confidence in your own ability to express ideas in your own words.

How to Recognize Patchwriting in Your Own Work

One of the challenges of patchwriting is that it can be difficult to recognize in your own writing. When you have spent time reading a source, its language stays in your memory, and your "own" version of its ideas may echo the original more closely than you realize.

Diagnostic Questions for Paraphrase

Ask yourself these questions when reviewing a paraphrase:

If I covered the original and read only my paraphrase, would my version feel natural? Or does it feel stiff?

If I read my version aloud, does it sound like me — like my usual way of writing?

Did I write this while looking at the source, or did I look away first?

Have I kept any phrases that appear in the original, even short ones, without quotation marks?

Is my sentence structure essentially the same as the original's, even if the words are different?

Example 35.8 — Patchwriting vs. Genuine Paraphrase
Side-by-side comparison with analysis
  • Original
    "Contemporary research in behavioral economics suggests that individuals frequently make financial decisions not on the basis of rational calculation but in response to cognitive shortcuts, emotional triggers, and social comparisons, often leading to outcomes that contradict their own stated goals."
  • Patchwriting ✗
    Current research in behavioral economics indicates that people often make financial choices not based on rational computation but in response to mental shortcuts, emotional cues, and social comparisons, frequently leading to results that contradict their own stated objectives.
  • Paraphrase ✓
    Studies in behavioral economics have found that when people make money-related decisions, they typically do not work through the problem rationally. Instead, they rely on mental rules of thumb, react emotionally, and compare themselves to others around them — a pattern that often pushes them toward choices they would not endorse on reflection.
  • Analysis The patchwritten version changes the vocabulary while keeping the sentence structure and logical sequence nearly identical. The genuine paraphrase restructures the idea completely — it breaks one long sentence into two, reorganizes the logical flow, uses entirely different language, and ends with an interpretive phrase ("choices they would not endorse on reflection") that shows the writer's own understanding of what the research means.

Other Forms of Source Misuse

Beyond patchwriting, there are several other ways that source use can go wrong. Chapter 37 addresses academic integrity comprehensively; it is worth naming a few issues here in the context of source technique.

Out-of-context quotation occurs when a writer selects words from a source in a way that changes or distorts their original meaning. Even if every word in the quotation is accurate, using it to support a point the original author did not intend to make is a serious misrepresentation.

Selective paraphrase occurs when a writer paraphrases only the parts of a passage that support their argument while omitting qualifications, counterarguments, or limiting conditions that the author included. A faithful paraphrase must preserve the spirit of the original, including its qualifications.

Fabricated signal phrases are a subtler form of distortion: using a signal verb that misrepresents what the author is actually doing. If an author tentatively suggests a possibility, writing "the author proves" is inaccurate even if the paraphrase of the content is correct.

All of these problems share a common root: they treat sources as material to be mined for convenient support rather than as genuine contributions to a conversation. Academic integrity in source use requires representing your sources honestly — even when their full meaning complicates your argument.

Chapter 35 — Back Matter

Key Terms, Summary, and Review

Key Terms

summary
A condensed restatement of the main idea or overall argument of a source, written in the writer's own words and significantly shorter than the original.
paraphrase
A complete restatement of a specific passage from a source in the writer's own words, at approximately the same length as the original, preserving all of the original's meaning.
quotation
The reproduction of exact words from a source, enclosed in quotation marks and cited. Every word inside must match the original exactly.
signal phrase
Introductory language that tells the reader a source is about to be introduced; typically includes the author's name and a characterizing verb.
patchwriting
A form of inadequate paraphrase in which the writer makes surface-level word substitutions without genuinely rewriting the passage; not acceptable in academic writing.
source sandwich
An informal model for source integration: context before the source material, the source material itself, and explanation after.
dropped quotation
A quotation that appears without an introduction or signal phrase; a common error in student academic writing.
attribution
The acknowledgment of where source material comes from, achieved through signal phrases and formal citation.
ellipsis
Three spaced periods used within a quotation to indicate that words have been omitted from the original.
brackets
Square symbols used within a quotation to indicate that the writer has made a minor, necessary change to the original text.

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 35.1Academic writers use three main techniques — summary, paraphrase, and quotation — each serving a different purpose. All three require citation. Sources should support, not replace, the writer's own argument.
  • Sec 35.2Summary compresses a source to its main idea in the writer's own words. Use it when the broad idea matters more than specific details. Write from memory to avoid borrowing the source's language. A good summary is shorter than the original and does not add the writer's opinions.
  • Sec 35.3Paraphrase translates a specific passage into the writer's own words at approximately the same length. It requires genuine comprehension and complete replacement of the source's language and sentence structure. Patchwriting — changing a few words while keeping the structure — is not genuine paraphrase.
  • Sec 35.4Quotation reproduces exact words from a source. Use it only when the precise wording is significant, not simply because a passage is well written. Every quotation must be introduced, cited, and followed by explanation. Over-quotation weakens academic writing.
  • Sec 35.5Signal phrases introduce source material and characterize the author's stance. The verb choice matters — "argues" carries a different meaning than "suggests" or "demonstrates." Vary sentence structure to avoid formulaic attribution.
  • Sec 35.6Every piece of source material needs context before it arrives — analytical framing that tells the reader why the source is relevant and how it connects to the argument. Even one or two sentences of context significantly improves integration.
  • Sec 35.7Explanation after source material is where analysis lives. The source sandwich model — context, source material, explanation — keeps the writer's argument at the center and ensures that sources serve reasoning rather than replace it.
  • Sec 35.8Patchwriting substitutes words while keeping the original structure. It is not genuine paraphrase and misrepresents the writer's engagement with sources. Other misuse forms include out-of-context quotation, selective paraphrase, and fabricated signal phrases. Honest source use requires representing sources fairly, even when their full meaning complicates your argument.

Review Questions

  1. What is the essential difference between summary and paraphrase? In what writing situation would you choose one over the other?
  2. Explain the difference between a paraphrase and a patchwritten passage. Why is patchwriting considered a form of academic dishonesty even when none of the original's exact words are reproduced?
  3. Name three situations in which direct quotation is an appropriate choice in academic writing. Name two situations in which quotation is not appropriate, even if the passage you want to use is well written.
  4. What is a signal phrase, and what work does it do in academic writing? How does the choice of signal verb affect the way readers understand a source?
  5. Why is citation required even when you paraphrase or summarize, not just when you quote directly?
  6. What does it mean to say that sources should "support your argument rather than replace it"? What does a paper look like when this principle is violated?
  7. Explain the source sandwich model. What are the three components? Why is each one necessary?
  8. What is a dropped quotation? What effect does it have on a reader, and how can it be corrected?
DiscussionDiscussion and Writing Prompts
  1. Find a short article on a topic you are currently studying. Choose one paragraph and write three versions of its main idea: first as a summary, then as a paraphrase, then as a brief direct quotation with context and explanation. Compare the three versions. Which technique felt most natural? Which was most challenging, and why?
  2. Consider this statement: "Students quote too much because they do not trust their own voices." Do you agree? What pressures might lead writers to over-rely on quotation? How might a writer develop the confidence to use their own language more?
  3. This chapter argues that patchwriting is a form of academic dishonesty even when the writer does not realize they are doing it. Do you think it is fair to hold writers responsible for a problem they may not recognize in their own work? What does this suggest about the writer's responsibility?
  4. What is the relationship between understanding a source and being able to paraphrase it well? If paraphrase is difficult, what does that difficulty tell a writer about their comprehension of the source?
ActivitiesPractice Activities
Activity 1 — Identifying Techniques
Read each passage and identify whether it is summary, paraphrase, quotation, or patchwriting. Explain your reasoning.
Passage A
  1. According to Wen (2020), the history of public transportation in mid-sized American cities during the postwar period is largely a story of deliberate disinvestment — a process driven less by declining ridership than by political decisions that prioritized highway construction and suburban development over urban transit systems.
Passage B
  1. Wen (2020) argues that the decline in public transit in postwar American cities was not inevitable but resulted from political choices favoring highways and suburbs over urban systems.
Passage C
  1. "The dismantling of urban transit networks in American cities during the postwar decades was not an organic response to consumer preference but an engineered outcome produced by specific legislative decisions, funding mechanisms, and lobbying interests that favored the automobile and the construction of federal highway systems" (Wen, 2020, p. 73).
Passage D
  1. Wen (2020) contends that the dismantling of urban transit networks in American cities during the postwar decades was not an organic response to consumer preference but rather an engineered outcome produced by certain legislative decisions, funding mechanisms, and lobbying interests that favored the automobile and federal highway construction.
Activity 2 — Writing Practice: Summary
Choose any article or textbook section of at least four paragraphs on a subject you are currently studying.
Instructions
  1. Write a summary in no more than five sentences that captures the source's main claim and most important supporting point.
  2. Do not look at the source while you write — set it aside and write from memory.
  3. Return to the source and revise for accuracy.
  4. Write two to three sentences explaining what you chose to leave out and why.
Activity 3 — Writing Practice: Paraphrase
Find a dense or technical passage of three to five sentences from a source in your area of study.
Instructions
  1. Write a genuine paraphrase of the passage.
  2. Then evaluate your paraphrase: Is every sentence in a different structure from the original?
  3. Have you avoided all phrases from the original (other than technical terms with no alternative)?
  4. Does your paraphrase preserve all the qualifications and conditions the original included?
  5. Does your version read naturally in your own voice? If any answer is no, revise until all answers are yes.
Activity 4 — Building Source Sandwiches
Choose a paragraph you have written recently for any class that uses at least one source.
Instructions
  1. Evaluate whether each source in the paragraph has a context sentence or phrase before it.
  2. Evaluate whether each source has a clear signal phrase introducing it.
  3. Evaluate whether each source has an explanation sentence or sentences after it.
  4. If any source is missing one of these three elements, revise the paragraph to include all three.
  5. Compare the original and revised versions and describe how the revision changes how the paragraph reads.
Activity 5 — Signal Verb Practice
For each situation, write a signal phrase that accurately characterizes what the source is doing. Choose your signal verb carefully.
Situations
  1. A researcher tentatively proposes that sleep disruption may be linked to certain metabolic changes, but notes the evidence is preliminary.
  2. A historian makes a strong claim that the conventional account of a particular event is incorrect, supported by newly discovered documents.
  3. A government report acknowledges that a previous policy created unintended consequences.
  4. An economist notes a pattern in the data without drawing a causal conclusion.
  5. A philosopher disputes a widely held assumption about the nature of moral responsibility.

Chapter Checklist

  • I can define summary, paraphrase, and quotation clearly and explain the difference between them
  • I know when to use each technique and can make an appropriate choice in context
  • I can write an accurate summary that is substantially shorter than the original and entirely in my own words
  • I can write a genuine paraphrase that preserves meaning, changes sentence structure and vocabulary, and avoids patchwriting
  • I can identify patchwriting in a passage and revise it to produce a genuine paraphrase
  • I can use direct quotation correctly, with introduction, accurate reproduction, and follow-up explanation
  • I can construct effective signal phrases with accurate, varied signal verbs
  • I understand the source sandwich model and can apply it to integrate sources into paragraphs
  • I understand that all three techniques require citation regardless of whether the original words are reproduced
  • I can explain why sources should support, not replace, the writer's own argument

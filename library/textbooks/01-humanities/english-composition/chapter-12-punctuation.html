Chapter 12 — Front Matter

Chapter Overview

Punctuation is not decoration. It is not a set of arbitrary rules that teachers invented to make writing more difficult. Punctuation is a system — a shared set of signals that writers use to guide readers through meaning. When punctuation works well, readers move through a sentence without noticing it. When punctuation fails, readers stumble, misread, or stop entirely.

This chapter treats punctuation as a tool for communication, not a test of memorization. You will learn what each major punctuation mark does, how it works inside academic sentences, and how to use it confidently in your own writing. You will also learn what goes wrong when punctuation is misused — not to embarrass you for past errors, but to help you understand why the rules exist and how to apply them purposefully.

Good punctuation will not make weak writing strong. But even well-reasoned academic writing loses authority when it is poorly punctuated. Readers in academic contexts judge careless punctuation as careless thinking. The good news is that the core punctuation system of academic English is learnable, logical, and consistent. By the end of this chapter, you will be able to punctuate academic sentences with confidence.

Opening Question

Read this sentence without punctuation: students who study in groups often perform better on exams however they need structured environments and clear goals not just conversation. Now read the punctuated version: Students who study in groups often perform better on exams; however, they need structured environments and clear goals, not just conversation. The ideas are identical. The punctuation changes nothing about what is being said — only how clearly and comfortably a reader can receive it. That is the work of this chapter.

Section 12.1
What Punctuation Does
Pause · Relationship · Academic Authority · Rules and Judgment

Before examining specific marks, it helps to understand what punctuation is doing in the first place. At its most basic level, punctuation manages two things: pause and relationship.

Pause refers to the timing signals punctuation sends to readers. A comma signals a brief pause. A period signals a full stop. A semicolon signals something between the two. These timing signals help readers process sentences in manageable chunks. Without them, even grammatically correct sentences become difficult to follow.

Relationship refers to how punctuation signals the connection between words, phrases, and clauses. A colon says: what follows explains or lists what came before. A dash says: what follows is worth special attention. A hyphen says: these two words function together as a single modifier. Without these relationship signals, readers must guess how parts of a sentence connect — and guessing takes effort that should be going toward understanding your ideas.

Without Punctuation
students who study in groups often perform better on exams however they need structured environments and clear goals not just conversation
With Punctuation
Students who study in groups often perform better on exams; however, they need structured environments and clear goals, not just conversation.

The ideas are identical. The punctuation does not change what is being said — it changes how clearly and comfortably a reader can receive it. That is the core function of punctuation: to serve the reader.

This is worth stating plainly because many students approach punctuation as a set of restrictions rather than as a set of resources. Every punctuation mark you use is a choice you are making as a writer. That choice can be made well or poorly, consciously or accidentally. This chapter will help you make those choices deliberately.

Punctuation and Academic Authority

In academic writing specifically, punctuation carries additional weight. Readers in academic contexts — professors, peers, editors, researchers — expect writing to demonstrate careful control. A paper that shows consistent punctuation errors signals that the writer did not attend carefully to the final product. Even when the ideas are strong, punctuation errors erode the reader's confidence.

This does not mean that a single comma error will ruin a paper. It means that developing real control over punctuation is part of developing authority as an academic writer. You want your reader focused on your argument, not distracted by questions about what you meant.

Core Principle

Punctuation is not about following rules for their own sake. It is about serving your reader. Every mark you place — or omit — shapes how clearly and confidently your ideas arrive.

A Note on Punctuation Rules

Some punctuation rules are absolute: you must use a period or equivalent at the end of every sentence. Others are matters of convention that vary across style guides, disciplines, and regions: whether to use the Oxford comma, for instance, is handled differently by different style guides. Still others are judgment calls that depend on context, rhythm, and what you want to emphasize.

This chapter teaches the core rules that apply across academic writing. Where significant variation exists, that variation will be noted. When a choice is genuinely stylistic, you will be given guidance for making that choice wisely rather than arbitrarily.

Section 12.2
Commas
Introductory Elements · Compound Sentences · Series · Nonessential Elements · Coordinate Adjectives

The comma is the most frequently used and most frequently misused punctuation mark in academic writing. Many students either use too few commas, producing sentences that rush past necessary pauses, or too many, scattering them through sentences without clear purpose. Both errors create confusion.

The key to using commas correctly is understanding that commas are not simply placed wherever you feel like pausing. They mark specific grammatical structures and relationships. Learning those structures will make comma placement reliable rather than guesswork.

Commas After Introductory Elements

When a sentence begins with an introductory word, phrase, or clause before the main subject and verb, a comma follows that introductory element. This helps the reader identify where the main clause begins.

Introductory TypeExample
Transitional adverbNevertheless, the data supported the original hypothesis.
Prepositional phraseIn many developing nations, access to clean water remains a significant challenge.
Participial phraseHaving reviewed the literature carefully, the research team identified three major gaps.
Dependent clauseAlthough the experiment yielded inconclusive results, the methodology itself was sound.
Useful Test

If you can move the introductory element to the end of the sentence and it still makes sense, a comma was needed before the main clause. These openings establish context, cause, time, or condition before the main point. The comma marks the transition between that context and the central idea.

Common Mistake — Omitting the Comma After Short Introductory Phrases

Many students omit the comma after a short introductory phrase, reasoning that the phrase is "too small" to need one. While very brief prepositional phrases represent a genuine gray area, as a general rule of academic writing, using the comma is the safer and more professional choice. When in doubt, include it.

Commas in Compound Sentences

A compound sentence joins two independent clauses — two complete thoughts that could each stand alone as sentences — using a coordinating conjunction. The coordinating conjunctions are: for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so (often remembered with the acronym FANBOYS). When these conjunctions join two independent clauses, a comma is placed before the conjunction.

Example 12.2.1
Coordinating conjunctions joining independent clauses
  • CorrectThe government proposed new environmental regulations, but the industry coalition challenged them in court.
  • CorrectThe study was widely cited, and its conclusions influenced policy for decades.
  • CorrectShe completed the internship with distinction, so the company offered her a permanent position.
Common Mistake — Comma Before Shared Verb

Do not add a comma before and when it joins two verbs sharing the same subject rather than two full independent clauses.

Incorrect: The committee reviewed the proposal, and rejected it unanimously.

Correct: The committee reviewed the proposal and rejected it unanimously.

The test: does the element after the conjunction have its own subject and verb, making it a complete thought? If yes, use the comma. If no, omit it.

Commas in a Series — The Oxford Comma

When three or more items appear in a list within a sentence, commas separate them. The question of whether to include a comma before the final and or or — the Oxford comma, also called the serial comma — depends on your style guide, but it is strongly recommended for academic writing.

Example 12.2.2
Oxford comma preventing ambiguity
  • AmbiguousThe documentary honored the researchers, Jane Goodall and David Attenborough.
    This could mean Goodall and Attenborough ARE the researchers — a two-item list.
  • ClearThe documentary honored the researchers, Jane Goodall, and David Attenborough.
    Three separate honorees, clearly distinguished.
The Oxford comma is required by APA style and most academic house styles. Use it consistently.

Commas with Nonessential (Nonrestrictive) Elements

Some phrases and clauses add information to a sentence but are not essential to identifying the noun they modify. These nonessential elements are set off with commas on both sides. An essential (restrictive) clause, by contrast, is necessary to identify which noun is meant and receives no commas.

TypeExampleRule
NonessentialProfessor Chen, who has published extensively on urban policy, will lead the seminar.Use commas — clause could be removed without changing who is meant
EssentialThe students who submitted their assignments on time received full credit.No commas — clause is needed to identify which students
The Removal Test

If you can remove the phrase or clause and the sentence still identifies the noun clearly, the element is nonessential — use commas. If removing it changes the meaning or leaves the identification unclear, it is essential — no commas.

Commas with Coordinate Adjectives

When two or more adjectives independently modify a noun and could be separated by and, they are coordinate adjectives and should be separated by a comma: a thorough, well-documented analysis. However, when the first adjective modifies the combination of the second adjective and the noun, no comma is needed: three different statistical methods (where three modifies different statistical methods as a unit).

The test: can you insert and between the adjectives and reverse their order without awkwardness? If yes, use a comma.

Other Standard Comma Uses

UseExample
Full dates mid-sentenceThe treaty was signed on November 11, 1918, ending four years of conflict.
AddressesThe organization is located at 450 Fifth Avenue, New York, NY 10018.
Direct addressProfessor Martinez, could you clarify the assignment requirements?
Transitional expressions mid-sentenceThe evidence, however, points in the opposite direction.
Section 12.3
Semicolons and Colons
Joining Independent Clauses · Transitional Adverbs · Complex Lists · Introducing Elaboration

Semicolons and colons are two of the most powerful punctuation marks in academic writing, and also two of the most frequently misunderstood. Many students avoid them entirely, defaulting to periods and commas. This leads to writing that feels monotonous or choppy. Used correctly, semicolons and colons give academic prose a sense of precision and control.

The Semicolon

A semicolon is a punctuation mark that sits between a comma and a period in terms of the strength of pause it signals. It indicates a stronger separation than a comma but a less complete break than a period. Its primary use is joining two closely related independent clauses when the writer wants to emphasize their connection.

Three Legitimate Uses of the Semicolon

Use 1 — Join two related independent clauses: The data clearly supported the hypothesis; the researchers proceeded to the next stage.

Use 2 — Before transitional adverbs joining two clauses: The committee approved the proposal; however, implementation was delayed.

Use 3 — Separate complex list items that contain internal commas: Lagos, Nigeria; Nairobi, Kenya; Cairo, Egypt.

Example 12.3.1
Semicolon before a transitional adverb
  • IncorrectThe experiment produced unexpected results, therefore, the researchers revised their model.
    A comma before "therefore" creates a comma splice — two independent clauses joined only by a comma.
  • CorrectThe experiment produced unexpected results; therefore, the researchers revised their model.
Conjunctive adverbs (however, therefore, consequently, furthermore, nevertheless, moreover, thus) are NOT coordinating conjunctions. A semicolon is required before them when they join two independent clauses.
Example 12.3.2
Semicolons in a complex list
  • ConfusingThe study drew participants from Lagos, Nigeria, Nairobi, Kenya, Cairo, Egypt, and Accra, Ghana.
  • ClearThe study drew participants from Lagos, Nigeria; Nairobi, Kenya; Cairo, Egypt; and Accra, Ghana.
When list items themselves contain commas, semicolons replace the commas between items to prevent reader confusion.
What Semicolons Cannot Do

A semicolon cannot join a complete clause to a fragment. It cannot replace a colon. It cannot follow an introductory element the way a comma can. It cannot join more than two elements the way a comma does in a series. Each side of a semicolon must be a complete independent clause when the semicolon is functioning as a clause connector.

The Colon

A colon signals that what follows directly explains, illustrates, or lists what came before. It says to the reader: here comes the elaboration. The colon creates anticipation — it promises that what follows will clarify or fulfill what just ended. The words before the colon must form a complete independent clause. This is the rule most commonly violated in student writing.

Example 12.3.3
Correct and incorrect colon use
  • IncorrectThe three main causes of the conflict were: territorial disputes, economic competition, and historical resentment.
    "The three main causes of the conflict were" is not a complete sentence — "were" requires a complement to be complete.
  • CorrectThe conflict had three main causes: territorial disputes, economic competition, and historical resentment.
  • CorrectThe committee's conclusion was unambiguous: the project could not proceed without additional funding.
  • CorrectThe report's opening statement was stark: "Access to clean water is not a privilege. It is a human right."
Test: read the words before the colon. Can they stand alone as a complete sentence? If not, restructure the sentence so that a full independent clause precedes the colon.
Capitalization After a Colon

Whether the first word after a colon is capitalized depends on what follows and which style guide you are using. APA capitalizes the first word after a colon when what follows is a complete sentence. Most style guides do not capitalize after a colon when what follows is a list or a phrase. Be consistent within any single document.

Section 12.4
Apostrophes
Possession · Contractions · Its versus It's · Academic Style

Apostrophes serve two distinct functions in English: they mark possession and they mark omitted letters in contractions. These are different functions governed by different rules. Confusing them is among the most common errors in written English.

Apostrophes for Possession

Noun TypeRuleExample
Singular nounAdd apostrophe + sthe student's essay, the committee's decision
Plural noun ending in sAdd apostrophe onlythe students' essays, the committees' decisions
Irregular plural (not ending in s)Add apostrophe + sthe children's textbooks, the women's organization
Singular noun ending in sAdd apostrophe + s (most guides)Charles's argument, the thesis's conclusion
Joint possessionApostrophe on second noun onlyMarcus and Aisha's collaborative project
Individual possessionApostrophe on each nounMarcus's paper and Aisha's paper

Apostrophes in Contractions

Contractions join two words by omitting one or more letters, and the apostrophe marks the location of the omission. Most academic writing avoids contractions in formal papers. This is a matter of register, not grammar: contractions are not wrong, but they carry an informal tone that typically does not suit formal academic essays. Spell out full forms in academic work unless your course or style guide specifies otherwise.

ContractionFull FormContractionFull Form
don'tdo notit'sit is / it has
can'tcannotthey'rethey are
won'twill notyou'reyou are
I'veI haveshe'dshe would / she had

Its Versus It's

This distinction causes more confusion than almost any other apostrophe question. The confusion arises because apostrophes mark possession for nouns (the student's), so students assume it's must be the possessive form of it. But possessive pronouns — his, her, its, their, our, your — never take apostrophes. They have their own forms that already indicate possession.

Example 12.4.1
Its versus It's — the substitution test
  • It's= it is or it has (apostrophe marks the omission)
    It's clear that the policy had unintended consequences. (= It is clear…)
    It's been a decade since the regulation was revised. (= It has been…)
  • Its= the possessive pronoun (no apostrophe)
    The organization published its annual report.
    The theory has its critics, but most scholars accept its core claims.
Test: if you can substitute "it is" or "it has" and the sentence still makes sense, use it's. If you cannot, use its.
Possessive Pronouns Never Take Apostrophes

None of these possessive pronouns ever use an apostrophe: its, their, theirs, your, yours, our, ours, his, her, hers, whose. If you write it's, you are always writing a contraction for it is or it has. This rule has no exceptions.

Section 12.5
Quotation Marks
Direct Quotations · Punctuation Placement · Titles · Scare Quotes

Quotation marks serve several purposes in academic writing: marking direct speech, indicating borrowed exact language from sources, identifying titles of short works, and occasionally signaling that a word is being used in a special or questioned sense. Each purpose has its own conventions.

Direct Quotations from Sources

When you reproduce exact words from a source, those words must be enclosed in quotation marks (unless the quotation is long enough to be formatted as a block quotation — typically more than four lines in MLA or forty words in APA). Quotation marks signal to the reader: these exact words belong to someone else. They are a fundamental component of academic honesty. Presenting another writer's exact words without quotation marks — even if you cite the source — is a form of plagiarism, because you are implying the words are yours when they are not.

Punctuation Placement with Quotation Marks (American Convention)

Punctuation MarkPlacementExample
Comma and periodInside closing quotation markThe report described it as "an unexpected success," which surprised analysts.
Semicolon and colonOutside closing quotation markThe author called this approach "fundamentally misguided"; scholars have largely agreed.
Question mark (part of quotation)Inside closing quotation markThe pamphlet asked: "Who benefits from this policy?"
Question mark (part of sentence)Outside closing quotation markDid the report describe the results as "statistically insignificant"?
British Convention

British English reverses some of these placement rules, placing periods and commas outside the closing quotation mark in many cases. If you are writing for a publication with British conventions, adjust accordingly. In American academic writing, commas and periods always go inside the closing quotation mark.

Quotation Marks with Titles

In academic writing, the distinction between quotation marks and italics for titles signals the size and independence of the work. Short or contained works take quotation marks; larger, independent works are italicized.

Use Quotation Marks ForUse Italics For
Article titles (in journals, magazines, newspapers)Book titles
Chapter titles in booksJournal, magazine, and newspaper publication titles
Short story titlesFilm and television series titles
Poem titlesFull album titles
Episode titlesLong plays and epic poems
Song titlesNamed artworks and exhibits
Example 12.5.1
Titles in academic prose
  • CorrectThe article "Social Capital and Educational Outcomes" appears in the journal Sociology of Education.
  • CorrectIn the chapter "Reading Like a Writer," the textbook English Composition and Academic Writing shows students how to annotate sources.

Scare Quotes

Sometimes writers place quotation marks around a word to signal irony, skepticism, or a specific use they want to question. These are called scare quotes. Use them sparingly in academic writing. Overusing them makes writing feel evasive or rhetorically aggressive. If you disagree with a term or want to question its meaning, you can often do so more powerfully in full sentences rather than by placing quotes around a single word.

Scare Quote — Used Sparingly
What the administration called "efficiency measures" were, in practice, significant service cuts.
Section 12.6
Parentheses and Dashes
Supplementary Information · Emphasis · Tone · Em Dash · En Dash · Overuse Warnings

Parentheses and dashes are both used to set off additional information within a sentence, but they carry different tones and signal different relationships to the main content. The choice between them is both grammatical and rhetorical.

Parentheses

Parentheses enclose material that is supplementary — useful but not central — to the main sentence. They signal to the reader: this is extra information; the sentence works without it, but you may find it helpful. In academic writing, parentheses are also the standard container for in-text citations in many style guides.

Example 12.6.1
Parentheses — supplementary information and citations
  • AsideThe study (conducted over a period of seven years) tracked changes in educational attainment across five socioeconomic groups.
  • CitationEducational outcomes improved significantly in regions that received the intervention (Martinez & Chen, 2019).
  • PunctuationThe committee approved the budget (with some reservations), and implementation began the following month.
    Punctuation for the surrounding sentence goes outside the closing parenthesis.

Em Dashes

The em dash (—) is a longer dash that creates emphasis and signals a more dramatic interruption or addition than parentheses. Where parentheses lower the volume of inserted information, the dash raises it. Use dashes when you want the reader to pause and pay special attention to the material being set off.

Example 12.6.2
Em dashes — emphasis and sharp turns
  • EmphasisThe committee's decision — unanimous and unequivocal — surprised most observers.
  • Sharp turnThe evidence pointed in one direction — and it was not the direction the researchers had expected.
  • Strong elaborationThere is one conclusion the data cannot support — that the program had no effect at all.
ParenthesesEm Dash
ToneQuiet, supplementaryEmphatic, dramatic
EffectLowers the information's prominenceHighlights the information
Typical useCitations, brief asides, definitionsSurprising turns, strong emphasis, appositives
Caution — Overusing Dashes

Em dashes are rhetorically powerful precisely because they are relatively rare. Academic writing that overuses dashes begins to feel breathless and dramatic. Reserve dashes for moments that genuinely deserve emphasis. If you find yourself using a dash in every paragraph, reconsider which instances are truly earning the mark.

En Dashes

The en dash (–) is shorter than the em dash and longer than a hyphen. It is used primarily to indicate ranges — pages 45–78, the 1990–2000 period — and to indicate relationships or connections between terms: the teacher–student relationship, the New York–London flight. In most student writing, the en dash is less frequently needed than the em dash or hyphen, but you should recognize it when you see it.

Section 12.7
Hyphens and Compound Modifiers
Before-Noun Rule · After Linking Verb · Compound Nouns · Prefixes · Style Guide Variation

The hyphen is one of the smaller punctuation marks in appearance but one of the more important for precision. Its primary function in academic writing is joining words that work together as a single modifier before a noun — what are called compound modifiers or compound adjectives.

Hyphenating Compound Modifiers

When two or more words work together to modify a single noun and they appear before the noun, they are typically joined with a hyphen. The hyphen signals that these words should be read together as a single unit of meaning, not as two separate modifiers.

When the same modifying words come after a linking verb, they are not hyphenated, because there is no ambiguity about what they modify.

Before the Noun (Hyphenate)After the Linking Verb (No Hyphen)
a well-documented argumentThe argument was well documented.
a peer-reviewed journalThe journal is peer reviewed.
a five-year studyThe study lasted five years.
an evidence-based approachThe approach was evidence based.
a long-term solutionThe solution was long term.
Why the Distinction Matters

Consider a small-business owner versus a small business owner. With the hyphen, small modifies business (meaning a business that is small), and small business together modifies owner. Without the hyphen, a reader might briefly read small as independently modifying owner. In formal academic writing, hyphenating compound modifiers before nouns demonstrates control and eliminates potential ambiguity.

Hyphens in Compound Nouns and with Prefixes

Some compound words are hyphenated as established conventions: self-esteem, well-being, follow-up (as a noun), cross-examination. Others that were once hyphenated have become closed compounds over time: database, notebook, website. Still others remain open (two separate words): high school, social media. When uncertain, consult a current dictionary or your required style guide.

Hyphens with prefixes are sometimes required to prevent misreading or awkward letter combinations: anti-inflammatory, co-author, re-examine. Style guides vary: APA tends toward closed forms (pretest, posttest), while other editors prefer hyphens for clarity (pre-test). Check your specific style guide and remain consistent within any single document.

Section 12.8
Punctuation in Academic Contexts
Long Sentences · Source Integration · Ellipsis · Brackets · Style Guide Variation

Having learned the individual marks, it helps to step back and consider how punctuation functions as a system within the specific demands of academic writing. Academic writing has its own characteristic rhythms, sentence structures, and punctuation patterns.

Long Sentences and Internal Punctuation

Academic prose often uses longer, more complex sentences than casual writing. These long sentences depend heavily on internal punctuation — commas, semicolons, dashes, parentheses — to keep readers oriented. A long sentence that is punctuated correctly is fully navigable; the same sentence without internal punctuation becomes dense and confusing.

Internal Punctuation in a Complex Academic Sentence
The theory, which had been widely accepted in the literature for nearly two decades, faced new scrutiny when a series of replication studies — several of them conducted by researchers at major institutions — returned results that contradicted its central predictions.

The commas around the nonrestrictive clause and the dashes around the parenthetical note keep a structurally complex sentence fully readable. Remove all internal punctuation and the sentence becomes a wall of words.

Punctuation and Source Integration

When you incorporate quotations and paraphrases from sources, punctuation plays a critical role in how smoothly those sources blend with your own prose. Signal phrases that introduce quotations require careful punctuation.

Signal Phrase TypePunctuationExample
Verb like says, argues, notesComma after the signal phraseSmith argues, "The reform effort failed on multiple fronts."
Complete introductory sentenceColon after the sentenceSmith's assessment is blunt: "The reform effort failed on multiple fronts."
Grammatically integrated quotationNo punctuation before the quotationSmith describes the effort as one that "failed on multiple fronts."

Ellipsis and Brackets

Ellipsis (three spaced periods: . . .) indicates that material has been omitted from within a quotation. Use it only to remove genuinely unnecessary material, never to change the meaning of the original.

Brackets (square brackets [ ]) allow you to insert a word or short clarification into a quotation without misrepresenting the original. The brackets indicate that the inserted word was not in the original but has been supplied for clarity.

Example 12.8.1
Ellipsis and brackets in quotations
  • EllipsisThe committee concluded that "the project . . . required fundamental restructuring before it could proceed."
  • BracketsThe report noted that "[the organization] had consistently underreported its administrative costs."
    Brackets signal that "the organization" was not the exact original word — perhaps it was "it" or an acronym — but has been supplied for reader clarity.

Punctuation Across Style Guides

Depending on your course and discipline, you may be required to follow MLA, APA, Chicago, or another documentation style. These style guides agree on the fundamental rules covered in this chapter. However, they differ on specific details: capitalization after a colon, the use of serial commas, formatting for ellipses, and the handling of certain compound words, among others.

The rules in this chapter represent core academic English conventions. When you have a specific style guide requirement, defer to that guide for its specific rules while using this chapter as your foundation.

Key Terms Review

apostrophe
A punctuation mark (') used to show possession in nouns or to mark omitted letters in contractions.
clause
A grammatical unit containing a subject and a verb. An independent clause can stand alone; a dependent clause cannot.
colon
A punctuation mark (:) that introduces a list, explanation, elaboration, or quotation — but only after a complete independent clause.
comma splice
The error of joining two independent clauses with only a comma and no coordinating conjunction.
compound modifier
Two or more words working together as a single adjective before a noun, typically joined by a hyphen.
contraction
A shortened form of two words in which an apostrophe marks the omitted letter(s), e.g., don't for do not.
coordinating conjunction
One of seven conjunctions (for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so) that can join independent clauses when preceded by a comma.
em dash
A long dash (—) used to signal emphasis, interruption, or a strong appositive. More dramatic in tone than parentheses.
en dash
A medium dash (–) used primarily to indicate ranges (pages 45–78) and connections (teacher–student relationship).
hyphen
A short dash (-) used to join words in compound modifiers before nouns and in certain compound words and prefix combinations.
nonrestrictive clause
A clause that adds supplementary information to a sentence but is not essential to identifying the noun; set off with commas.
Oxford comma
The comma placed before the final conjunction in a series of three or more items. Also called the serial comma.
parentheses
Curved brackets () used to enclose supplementary or explanatory information that is useful but not central to the sentence.
restrictive clause
A clause essential to identifying the noun it modifies. Not set off with commas because removing it would change the sentence's meaning.
scare quotes
Quotation marks used to indicate irony, skepticism, or a questioned or specialized use of a term. Use sparingly.
semicolon
A punctuation mark (;) used to join closely related independent clauses or to separate complex list items that contain internal commas.
signal phrase
An introductory phrase that identifies the source of a quotation or paraphrase, typically including the author's name and a reporting verb.

Problem 1
2(x + 3) + 4x = 2x + 3 + 4x = 6x + 3

Wait — wrong subject. See the writing version below:

Problem 1 — Comma Error
The study lasted three years, and produced results that challenged the existing model.

What is the error? The comma before "and" is incorrect here. "And" does not join two independent clauses — "produced results" shares the subject "The study" with the first verb. Correct: The study lasted three years and produced results that challenged the existing model.

Problem 2 — Comma Splice
The data clearly supported the hypothesis, however, the team remained cautious.

Error: "however" is a conjunctive adverb, not a coordinating conjunction. A comma before it creates a comma splice. Correct: The data clearly supported the hypothesis; however, the team remained cautious.

Problem 3 — Colon After Incomplete Clause
The three main causes of the conflict were: territorial disputes, economic competition, and historical resentment.

Error: "The three main causes of the conflict were" is not a complete sentence — "were" requires a complement. Correct: The conflict had three main causes: territorial disputes, economic competition, and historical resentment.

Problem 4 — Its / It's
The organization published it's annual report last month.

Error: it's means "it is" — substituting "it is" here gives "The organization published it is annual report," which is wrong. The word needed is the possessive pronoun its. Correct: The organization published its annual report last month.

Problem 5 — Missing Comma After Introductory Clause
Although the initial findings were promising the replication study produced different results.

Error: no comma after the introductory dependent clause "Although the initial findings were promising." Correct: Although the initial findings were promising, the replication study produced different results.

Chapter 12 Checklist

Use this checklist when proofreading a draft for punctuation.

Commas
  • I have placed a comma after every introductory element (word, phrase, or clause) that precedes the main clause.
  • I have used a comma before every coordinating conjunction joining two independent clauses.
  • I have used commas to separate three or more items in a series and included the Oxford comma before the final item.
  • I have set off all nonessential (nonrestrictive) phrases and clauses with commas on both sides.
  • I have not placed commas between subjects and verbs or before "and" when it joins two verbs sharing a subject.
Semicolons and Colons
  • Every semicolon I used joins two complete independent clauses or separates complex list items with internal commas.
  • I have placed a semicolon before conjunctive adverbs (however, therefore, nevertheless, etc.) when they join two independent clauses.
  • Every colon I used follows a complete independent clause that can stand alone as a sentence.
  • I have used a colon to introduce lists and explanations only when a full sentence precedes it.
Apostrophes
  • I have added apostrophe + s to singular nouns to show possession.
  • I have added only an apostrophe (no s) to plural nouns already ending in s to show possession.
  • I have checked every instance of its and it's using the substitution test (it is / it has).
  • I have not used apostrophes with possessive pronouns (its, their, your, our, whose).
Quotation Marks
  • All exact words borrowed from sources are enclosed in quotation marks.
  • Titles of short works (articles, chapters, poems, songs) are in quotation marks; titles of long works are italicized.
  • Commas and periods are placed inside closing quotation marks (American convention).
  • Semicolons and colons are placed outside closing quotation marks.
Parentheses and Dashes
  • I have used parentheses for supplementary, lower-priority information and citations.
  • I have used em dashes sparingly, only for material that genuinely deserves emphasis or creates a meaningful dramatic pause.
  • Neither parentheses nor dashes appear more than two or three times per page.
Hyphens
  • I have hyphenated compound modifiers that appear before a noun.
  • I have removed hyphens from compound modifiers that appear after a linking verb.
  • I have checked any words with common prefixes (self-, co-, anti-, pre-) against my style guide.
General
  • I have read my draft aloud, listening for sentences where punctuation may be missing or misplaced.
  • I have checked all source integrations for correct punctuation of signal phrases, quotations, and citations.
  • My punctuation choices are consistent throughout the document.

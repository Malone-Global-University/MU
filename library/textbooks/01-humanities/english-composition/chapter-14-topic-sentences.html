Chapter 14 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Every well-developed academic paragraph has a job to do. It must introduce a point, develop that point with evidence and explanation, and connect logically to the paragraphs around it. The topic sentence is the tool that makes all of this possible. It tells the reader what the paragraph is about, what the writer thinks about that subject, and what direction the evidence and explanation will take.

Despite how important topic sentences are, many students underestimate them. A weak topic sentence is one of the most common causes of unfocused, underdeveloped paragraphs. Students who skip topic sentences, write vague ones, or mistake factual statements for controlling ideas often find that their paragraphs wander, repeat themselves, or fail to support their thesis. Learning to write strong topic sentences is one of the highest-leverage skills in academic writing.

This chapter teaches you how topic sentences work, what makes them strong or weak, and how to write them with precision and purpose. It explains the key distinctions between a topic, a controlling idea, a claim, and a factual statement. It shows how topic sentences function in different kinds of writing across academic disciplines. It addresses implied topic sentences and gives you practical strategies for revising weak ones.

Central Question of This Chapter

Does this paragraph have a focused, specific, analytical topic sentence that tells the reader exactly what this paragraph will argue or explain? By the end of Chapter 14, you will be able to answer that question with confidence — and to rewrite any topic sentence that falls short.

Section 14.1

What Topic Sentences Do

A topic sentence is a sentence, usually placed near the beginning of a paragraph, that states the paragraph's main point. It functions as a kind of contract between the writer and the reader. It promises that the rest of the paragraph will develop, support, and explain the idea it introduces. When the paragraph keeps that promise, the reader follows easily. When the paragraph breaks that promise — by drifting off topic, introducing too many ideas, or failing to develop what the topic sentence announced — the reader becomes confused or frustrated.

Topic sentences do several things at once. They signal to the reader what the paragraph is about, so the reader does not have to guess. They limit the paragraph to a manageable scope, so the writer does not try to cover everything at once. They establish the paragraph's relationship to the essay's thesis, making the larger argument feel organized and coherent. And they give the writer a standard to measure the paragraph against: every sentence in the paragraph should connect to the topic sentence in some clear way.

Think of a topic sentence as a small thesis statement for a single paragraph. Just as a thesis tells the reader what the entire essay will argue or explain, a topic sentence tells the reader what one paragraph will argue or explain. The two work together. The thesis states the essay's main claim, and each topic sentence states the claim of one piece of evidence, one line of reasoning, or one analytical point that builds toward the thesis.

Without topic sentences, paragraphs collapse into shapeless collections of related sentences. The reader may be able to sense what the paragraph is about, but reading becomes effortful. The writer, meanwhile, often has no anchor to keep the paragraph focused. Many of the most common paragraph problems — wandering off topic, underdevelopment, repetition, lack of transitions — trace back directly to weak or missing topic sentences.

Study Note — Placement

A topic sentence does not have to be the very first sentence in a paragraph. In some styles and disciplines, it comes after a brief transition or context sentence. However, it should always appear early — no later than the second or third sentence — so that the reader knows the point before encountering the evidence.

The Four Jobs of a Topic Sentence

1. Signals to the reader what the paragraph is about.

2. Limits the scope so the paragraph stays focused on one idea.

3. Connects the paragraph to the essay's thesis and overall argument.

4. Gives the writer a standard — every sentence in the paragraph should connect to it.

Section 14.2

Topics and Controlling Ideas

One of the most important distinctions in academic writing is the difference between a topic and a controlling idea. Many students write topic sentences that state a topic without adding a controlling idea. The result is a sentence that announces a subject but makes no claim about it. This kind of sentence cannot actually guide a paragraph, because it leaves the direction of development completely open.

A topic is the subject the paragraph is about. It answers the question: What is this paragraph discussing? A topic can be a person, an event, a concept, a problem, a text, a process, or any other subject. By itself, however, a topic is not yet a topic sentence. A topic alone gives the reader no information about what the writer thinks, argues, or claims about the subject.

A controlling idea is the writer's specific claim, position, observation, or analytical point about the topic. It answers the question: What does this paragraph say about the topic? The controlling idea is what makes a topic sentence useful. It gives the paragraph direction, limits the scope of development, and tells the reader what to expect.

Example 14.2.1
Topic only versus topic with controlling idea
  • Topic OnlyThis paragraph is about climate change policy.
    The reader knows the general subject but has no idea what the writer will argue, conclude, or explain about it. Any paragraph touching climate change policy could follow.
  • With Controlling IdeaInternational climate change agreements have repeatedly failed to produce binding commitments because wealthy nations prioritize economic growth over environmental obligation.
    The reader now knows the subject (international agreements), the claim (they fail), and the reason the writer will develop (wealthy nations' priorities). The paragraph has a clear direction.

This is why the controlling idea is not optional. Without it, a topic sentence is just a label. With it, a topic sentence becomes a guide. A student writing from the first version above would not know what evidence to select, what to explain, or how to conclude. A student writing from the second version knows precisely what to demonstrate: evidence of wealthy nations choosing economic priorities over environmental commitments in the context of international agreements, and an explanation of how this pattern blocks binding commitments.

The controlling idea is also what determines scope — the range of material the paragraph will cover. A controlling idea that is too broad will try to do too much in one paragraph and end up superficial. A controlling idea that is too narrow may not generate enough material for genuine development. The goal is a controlling idea specific enough to guide one paragraph and substantial enough to require real explanation and evidence.

Finding the Controlling Idea

When drafting a paragraph, the controlling idea usually emerges from thinking about the evidence you plan to use. Ask yourself: Why does this evidence matter? What does it prove? What pattern does it illustrate? What argument does it support? The answer to one of these questions is often your controlling idea.

For example, if you are writing about the causes of a historical event and you have evidence about economic instability, political corruption, and foreign interference, you need to decide what your paragraph argues about those factors. Does it argue that economic instability was the primary cause? That all three factors worked together in a specific way? That one cause triggered the others? Your answer becomes your controlling idea, and the evidence becomes the development of that idea.

Section 14.3

Claims Versus Announcements

A second important distinction is the difference between a claim and an announcement. Many students write topic sentences that announce what the paragraph will do rather than stating what the writer thinks. Announcements are not topic sentences. They are descriptions of topic sentences — and they waste the space where the actual point should be.

An announcement is a sentence that tells the reader you are about to say something without actually saying it. It shifts focus away from the subject and toward the writer's process. It makes writing feel mechanical and evasive. Most importantly, it provides no analytical content: no claim, no controlling idea, no direction for development.

Common Announcement Patterns to Avoid

"This paragraph will discuss..." / "In this section, I will explain..." / "There are several reasons for..." / "The following evidence shows..." / "This paper will look at..." — Each of these describes a paragraph rather than writing one.

A claim is a statement that takes a position, makes an argument, offers an analysis, or asserts a relationship. It can be agreed with or disagreed with. It requires evidence to be convincing. It gives the paragraph a purpose beyond simply recounting information.

Example 14.3.1
Converting announcements into claims
  • Announcement There are several effects of social media use on adolescent mental health.
    This tells the reader only that effects exist. It gives no direction.
  • Claim Heavy social media use is associated with increased rates of anxiety and depression among adolescents, particularly among girls in early to mid-adolescence.
    This tells the reader what the effects are and who is most affected. The paragraph now has a clear, arguable direction.
  • Announcement This paragraph will explain the symbolism in George Orwell's Animal Farm.
    Describes the task rather than performing it.
  • Claim The pigs in Animal Farm represent the corrupting nature of unchecked political power, gradually adopting the behaviors of the human oppressors they displaced.
    Performs the analytical work directly — identifies the symbol, what it represents, and how the representation works.

It is worth understanding why students write announcements. Often, the writer is not yet certain what the paragraph's claim is and uses an announcement as a placeholder while drafting. This is understandable. If you find yourself writing "This paragraph will discuss..." in a draft, treat it as a signal to pause and ask: What exactly do I think about this topic? What is my actual point? The answer to that question is your topic sentence. The announcement is scaffolding — useful during planning, but it must be replaced before the final draft.

Common Mistake

Writing "There are many reasons why..." or "There are several factors that..." as a topic sentence. These phrases announce that reasons or factors exist without saying what they are or what they mean. Replace them with a sentence that identifies the most important reason or factor and says something specific about it.

Section 14.4

Specific Topic Sentences

Specificity is one of the most important qualities of a strong topic sentence. Vague topic sentences lead to vague, underdeveloped paragraphs. When a topic sentence is specific, development becomes much easier because the writer knows exactly what to explain and what evidence to select.

Vague topic sentences often fail in one of two ways. They use language that is too general — words like many, several, various, some, things, aspects, factors, and issues — without defining what those things actually are. Or they make claims so broad they could apply to virtually any subject, so they say nothing meaningful about the actual topic.

Vague Topic SentenceSpecific Topic Sentence
Many aspects of the American healthcare system are problematic. The lack of universal insurance coverage in the American healthcare system forces millions of low-income patients to delay treatment for serious conditions until those conditions become medical emergencies.
Shakespeare uses many literary devices in his plays. In Hamlet, Shakespeare uses dramatic irony to create sustained tension between what the audience knows about King Hamlet's murder and what most of the court believes to be true.
Social media has changed the way people communicate. The algorithmic design of social media platforms prioritizes content that generates emotional reactions, gradually training users to associate engagement with outrage rather than with genuine connection.

Specificity comes from asking more precise questions about your topic. Instead of asking What is this paragraph about?, ask What specifically happens, and why does it matter? Instead of What is the problem?, ask What is the specific mechanism by which this problem causes harm? These more precise questions produce more precise topic sentences.

It is also worth noting that specificity does not require complexity. A specific topic sentence can be a single, clear sentence. What it needs is a precise subject and a precise controlling idea. Precision comes from careful thinking, not from adding more words.

Specificity and Evidence Selection

One practical test for whether a topic sentence is specific enough is to ask: Could this topic sentence introduce any of several different paragraphs, or does it point clearly to one set of evidence? If your topic sentence could introduce three entirely different paragraphs with completely different evidence, it is probably too vague.

For example, Many factors contributed to the outbreak of World War I could introduce a paragraph about nationalism, the alliance system, economic competition, or the assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand. It gives no direction. By contrast, The rigid European alliance system transformed what might have been a regional conflict into a continent-wide war by obligating nations to honor mutual defense agreements regardless of their own strategic interests points clearly to one line of argument and one set of evidence.

Writer's Checklist — Specificity Test

After writing a topic sentence, ask: Could this sentence open three or more completely different paragraphs? If yes, add a more specific controlling idea. The topic sentence should lead naturally to one focused set of supporting evidence.

Section 14.5

Analytical Topic Sentences

In many academic courses — especially in literature, history, social sciences, and philosophy — students are expected to write analytical topic sentences rather than merely informational ones. An analytical topic sentence does not simply report what happened or what exists. It interprets, evaluates, connects, or explains significance.

The distinction between analytical and informational topic sentences reflects two different writing tasks. An informational paragraph tells the reader what. An analytical paragraph tells the reader what and why — or what it means, or how it works, or what its significance is. Most academic writing beyond the introductory level calls for the analytical approach.

Example 14.5.1
Informational versus analytical — literature
  • Informational In Chapter 3, the narrator describes the green light at the end of Daisy's dock.
    Reports an event in the text. A paragraph built on this sentence will likely just retell what happens.
  • Analytical The green light that the narrator describes at the end of Daisy's dock functions as a symbol of Gatsby's larger relationship to the American Dream: perpetually visible, perpetually out of reach, and ultimately hollow when approached.
    Interprets the event, making a claim about what it means in the context of the novel's themes. A paragraph built on this sentence will explore the symbol, connect it to evidence, and develop an interpretation.
Example 14.5.2
Informational versus analytical — history
  • Informational The New Deal introduced several economic programs during the Great Depression.
    Reports the existence of programs without making a claim about what they accomplished or why.
  • Analytical Although New Deal programs provided essential relief to millions of Americans, they ultimately fell short of ending the economic crisis because they were designed to stabilize the existing system rather than restructure its fundamental inequalities.
    Evaluates the programs, makes a specific claim about their limitations, and identifies the reason. The writer now has a complex, arguable point to develop.

Writing analytically at the topic sentence level does not mean being cynical, contrarian, or artificially complicated. It means asking deeper questions: What does this evidence mean? Why did this happen in this way and not another? What is the significance of this pattern? How do these elements relate to each other? The answers to these questions are analytical claims, and they produce analytical topic sentences.

Study Note — Science and Technical Writing

In science and technical writing, topic sentences are often more informational and less interpretive than in humanities writing. A biology lab report paragraph might appropriately begin: The results of the enzyme activity experiment showed a direct correlation between temperature increase and reaction rate up to 37 degrees Celsius. This is informational and precise, which is appropriate for the discipline. Context always shapes what a strong topic sentence looks like.

Section 14.6

Topic Sentences in Different Disciplines

One of the most useful things a writer can understand is that different academic disciplines have different expectations for topic sentences, just as they have different expectations for evidence, structure, tone, and citation. A topic sentence that works well in a literary analysis paper may not be the right model for a topic sentence in a psychology research paper or a chemistry lab report.

Understanding how topic sentences vary across disciplines helps you adapt your writing to the expectations of specific courses without abandoning the underlying principle: a paragraph needs a clear, focused main point.

Humanities

In humanities courses — literature, history, philosophy, art history, cultural studies — topic sentences are typically analytical and argumentative. They make claims about meaning, significance, causation, pattern, or interpretation. They are expected to go beyond summarizing and to demonstrate original thinking about the material.

In a literature course, topic sentences often introduce interpretive claims about texts: what a symbol means, how a narrative technique functions, what a character's choices reveal about a theme, or how a passage reflects historical or cultural context. In a history course, topic sentences often make causal claims, evaluative claims, or comparative claims. The analytical voice is expected and rewarded.

Social Sciences

In social sciences — psychology, sociology, economics, political science — topic sentences often introduce findings, relationships, or patterns that the paragraph will explain and support with data or research. They tend to be more precise and less overtly argumentative than humanities topic sentences, though they still require a controlling idea.

A psychology paper might use a topic sentence like: Children who experience inconsistent attachment in early infancy display measurably different stress response patterns in adolescence compared to children with secure early attachments. This is specific, based in research evidence, and controlled — it identifies the relationship the paragraph will explain.

Sciences

In sciences, topic sentences often state the purpose, finding, or claim of a section of a report or paper. Laboratory reports, scientific reviews, and research papers use topic sentences that signal what the paragraph will demonstrate or explain, often with precision and technical vocabulary appropriate to the field. The interpretive layer is usually lighter, but the need for a focused main point remains.

Professional and Applied Fields

In business, nursing, education, and other professional fields, topic sentences often introduce practical claims, analyses, or recommendations. They may be slightly more direct and action-oriented than humanities topic sentences, reflecting the applied nature of professional writing. A business analysis might begin: The company's declining customer retention rate can be attributed primarily to the gap between customer expectations established during marketing and the product experience delivered at purchase.

DisciplineTypical Topic Sentence CharacteristicsExample
LiteratureAnalytical; interpretive; claims about meaning, symbol, techniqueSethe's act of infanticide in Beloved functions not as an act of despair but as an extreme assertion of maternal ownership in the face of a system that treated enslaved children as property.
HistoryCausal, evaluative, or comparative; argues significance or relationshipThe Marshall Plan succeeded where earlier relief efforts had failed because it tied economic aid to coordinated policy reform, giving recipient nations incentive to cooperate rather than compete.

What Stays the Same Across All Disciplines

Every paragraph, in every discipline, needs a clear main point. That point should be visible to the reader near the beginning of the paragraph. How formal, how interpretive, how argumentative, and how technical the topic sentence is will vary by field — but the underlying function does not change.

Section 14.7

Implied Topic Sentences

Not every paragraph in academic writing has an explicit topic sentence — a sentence that directly states the main point. Some paragraphs use an implied topic sentence, where the main point emerges from the accumulation of sentences rather than being stated directly at the beginning.

Implied topic sentences are more common in certain kinds of writing — narrative essays, literary personal essays, some descriptive writing, journalistic prose — and less common in analytical, argumentative, and research writing. In academic writing, implied topic sentences are generally appropriate only when the writer is confident that the main point is clear enough from context and development that no explicit statement is needed.

Example 14.7.1
An implied topic sentence in narrative writing
  • Paragraph The train was already moving when she reached the platform. She pressed her hand against the window as the last car passed, but the glass was cold and streaked with rain. She did not run. She stood and watched until the train disappeared around the bend in the track, and then she picked up her bag and walked back the way she had come.
  • Implied Point The character missed the train and made no effort to stop it — suggesting resignation or defeat. The implied topic sentence might read: She arrived too late and chose not to fight it. The reader infers this from the accumulation of details.

However, for most academic writing — especially essays, research papers, analytical responses, and argumentative papers — implied topic sentences are risky. They rely on the reader to infer the main point correctly, which requires the writer to be highly skilled and the development to be exceptionally clear. Beginning and intermediate academic writers are usually safer with explicit topic sentences, which prevent misunderstanding and keep paragraphs focused.

Common Mistake — Implied vs. Missing

Do not confuse an implied topic sentence with a missing one. An implied topic sentence is a deliberate choice that leaves the main point clear through skillful development. A missing topic sentence is an oversight that leaves the paragraph without focus. If your paragraph seems to lack a topic sentence, ask honestly: Is the main point truly clear from what I have written, or am I hoping the reader will figure it out? If you are hoping — write the sentence explicitly.

Section 14.8

Revising Weak Topic Sentences

Even experienced writers often draft weak topic sentences and revise them later. Knowing how to identify and fix weak topic sentences is one of the most practical revision skills in academic writing. Weak topic sentences typically fail in one of five ways: they are too vague, they are announcements, they are factual statements rather than claims, they try to cover too many points at once, or they have no controlling idea at all.

The Revision Process

When you revise topic sentences, work through each paragraph systematically. Read the first sentence of each paragraph and ask five questions:

Five Revision Questions for Every Topic Sentence

1. Does this sentence state a specific point or just a general topic?

2. Does it make a claim, or does it just announce that a topic will be discussed?

3. Is it something that could be agreed with or disagreed with, or is it simply a fact?

4. Does it give the reader a clear sense of what direction the paragraph will take?

5. Does every sentence in the paragraph connect to this topic sentence?

Revision Examples by Failure Type

Problem 1 — Topic only, no controlling idea
This paragraph discusses immigration policy in the United States.

Revision strategy: Add a specific claim about what the writer will argue about immigration policy.

The United States' current immigration policy creates prolonged legal uncertainty for undocumented residents who have lived and worked in the country for decades, generating significant economic and social costs for both individuals and communities.
Problem 2 — Announcement
In this paragraph, I will explain the causes of the French Revolution.

Revision strategy: State the causes directly and make a claim about their significance or relationship.

The French Revolution was triggered not by a single cause but by the convergence of fiscal collapse, aristocratic resistance to reform, and widespread popular anger at a monarchy that seemed indifferent to widespread suffering.
Problem 3 — Factual statement with no analytical claim
The Great Barrier Reef is the world's largest coral reef system and is located off the coast of Queensland, Australia.

Revision strategy: Add a controlling idea that makes a claim about the reef rather than simply identifying it.

The Great Barrier Reef, though it remains the world's largest coral reef system, is now experiencing unprecedented rates of bleaching and ecosystem loss as a direct consequence of rising ocean temperatures driven by climate change.
Problem 4 — Too many points at once
The novel explores themes of identity, loss, colonialism, language, memory, family loyalty, and the immigrant experience.

Revision strategy: Choose one theme or relationship for this paragraph and write a focused controlling idea about it.

The novel uses the protagonist's experience of language loss — her gradual shift from Tagalog to English — as a central metaphor for the broader erosion of cultural identity that accompanies immigration.
Problem 5 — Vague language with no substance
There are many important aspects of effective leadership that good leaders possess.

Revision strategy: Identify one specific aspect and make a claim about why it matters.

Effective leaders distinguish themselves primarily through their willingness to make difficult decisions under uncertainty, even when those decisions carry significant personal or professional risk.

Reverse Outlining

One of the most effective revision techniques for topic sentences is called reverse outlining. After drafting an essay or section, read through each paragraph and write a one-sentence summary of what each paragraph actually does — not what you intended it to do, but what it actually says. Then compare your reverse outline to your topic sentences.

If the topic sentences match the summaries, your paragraphs are focused and your promises are kept. If they do not match, you have identified paragraphs that need revision — either the topic sentence needs to be rewritten to match what the paragraph actually argues, or the paragraph needs to be revised to develop what the topic sentence promises.

Reverse outlining is especially useful for longer papers where it is easy to lose track of individual paragraph focus over the course of drafting. A reverse outline of a ten-paragraph essay takes less than fifteen minutes and reliably surfaces the most serious structural problems.

How to Reverse Outline

After drafting, number each paragraph. Read each one and write one sentence capturing what it actually argues. Compare these sentences to your topic sentences. Where they match, you have a focused paragraph. Where they diverge, you have a revision task — either rewrite the topic sentence to match the paragraph, or revise the paragraph to match the topic sentence.

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 14.1Topic sentences are contracts with the reader. They signal the paragraph's subject, limit scope, connect to the thesis, and give the writer a standard every sentence must meet. Missing or weak topic sentences are among the most common causes of unfocused paragraphs.
  • Sec 14.2A topic is a subject; a controlling idea is the writer's specific claim about that subject. Both are required. The controlling idea determines direction, scope, and evidence selection. It emerges from asking what the evidence means or proves.
  • Sec 14.3Announcements describe what a paragraph will do rather than doing it. They provide no analytical content. Claims take positions, assert relationships, and can be agreed with or disagreed with. Every announcement should be converted to a claim before the final draft.
  • Sec 14.4Vague topic sentences produce vague, underdeveloped paragraphs. Specificity comes from asking precise questions: not just what the topic is, but what specifically happens, why it matters, and what the mechanism is. A specific topic sentence points naturally to one set of evidence.
  • Sec 14.5Analytical topic sentences interpret, evaluate, or explain significance. They go beyond reporting what exists or happened. In humanities and social science writing, analytical topic sentences are expected; they demonstrate the original thinking that academic writing requires.
  • Sec 14.6Topic sentence conventions vary by discipline. Humanities writing calls for interpretive, argumentative sentences; social sciences for precise, evidence-based ones; sciences for informational and technical ones; professional fields for direct and practical ones. The function — a clear main point near the beginning — remains constant across all fields.
  • Sec 14.7Implied topic sentences are appropriate in narrative and literary prose where the main point is unmistakably clear. In analytical, argumentative, and research writing, explicit topic sentences are the safer and more effective choice. Missing topic sentences and implied topic sentences are different things.
  • Sec 14.8Weak topic sentences fail in five ways: too vague, announcement-style, pure factual statement, too many points at once, or no controlling idea. Each has a specific revision strategy. Reverse outlining — summarizing each paragraph after drafting and comparing to the topic sentence — is the most reliable method for identifying mismatches.

Key Terms Review

topic sentence
A sentence near the beginning of a paragraph that states the paragraph's main point and gives the reader a controlling idea to follow.
topic
The subject that a paragraph discusses. A topic alone, without a controlling idea, is not yet a topic sentence.
controlling idea
The writer's specific claim, analytical point, or position about the topic. The part of a topic sentence that gives the paragraph its direction and scope.
claim
A statement that takes a position, makes an argument, or asserts a relationship that can be supported with evidence and could be agreed with or disagreed with.
announcement
A sentence that describes what a paragraph will discuss without actually making a claim. A common weakness in student writing that must be revised into a claim.
analytical topic sentence
A topic sentence that goes beyond reporting information to interpret, evaluate, connect, or explain the significance of evidence or events.
implied topic sentence
A main point not stated directly but emerging clearly from the development of a paragraph. More appropriate in narrative prose than in analytical academic writing.
scope
The range of material a topic sentence and its paragraph will cover. A topic sentence should be specific enough to guide one paragraph without being so narrow it lacks substance.
reverse outlining
A revision strategy in which a writer summarizes what each completed paragraph actually argues, then compares those summaries to the intended topic sentences to identify mismatches.
factual statement
A statement that reports a verifiable fact without making a claim or offering interpretation. Not appropriate as a standalone topic sentence in analytical writing.

Discussion and Writing Prompts

  1. Find a paragraph from a textbook, article, or essay in any of your current courses. Identify the topic sentence (or decide whether it is implied). Write a one-paragraph analysis of how well the topic sentence functions: Does it state a clear controlling idea? Is it specific? Is it analytical? Is it an announcement? What could be improved?
  2. Take a position on any topic you care about — a current event, a social issue, a question in a course you are taking. Write five different topic sentences on that topic, each representing a different argument or analytical angle. Then choose the strongest one and explain in writing why it works better than the others.
  3. Read two paragraphs from a research article in any field and compare how topic sentences function in that discipline compared to what you have studied in this chapter. What similarities do you notice? What differences? What explains the differences?
  4. Look at a paragraph you have written for any class in the past month. Identify its topic sentence. Does it have a controlling idea? Is it an announcement? Is it specific? Rewrite the topic sentence and explain in one paragraph what you changed and why the revision is stronger.
  5. Why do you think many student writers write announcements instead of claims? What habits or assumptions might lead to this pattern? What practice could most effectively help a writer shift from announcements to claims?

Activity 1Topic, Controlling Idea, or Announcement?

For each sentence below, identify whether it is (a) a topic only, (b) a topic with a controlling idea, or (c) an announcement. If it is a topic only or an announcement, rewrite it as a complete topic sentence with a focused controlling idea.

  1. The Industrial Revolution changed the way people worked.
  2. This paragraph will explain the causes of World War I.
  3. Renewable energy is a topic that many people are discussing today.
  4. The shift from coal to renewable energy sources in Germany has demonstrated that large industrial economies can reduce carbon emissions without sacrificing economic productivity.
  5. There are many aspects of social media that affect teenagers.
  6. Sleep deprivation among college students is linked to measurable declines in academic performance, immune function, and emotional regulation.
  7. Animal testing is used in many industries.
  8. This section will discuss the importance of early childhood education.
Activity 2From Vague to Specific

Each topic sentence below is too vague to guide a focused paragraph. Rewrite each one to include a specific topic and a specific controlling idea. You may need to invent a particular claim, angle, or position.

  1. Many things affect a person's health.
  2. Shakespeare's plays are very interesting and have many themes.
  3. Technology has changed society in many ways.
  4. The environment is an important issue in today's world.
  5. Historical events have had various effects on the present.
  6. Different cultures have different perspectives on education.
Activity 3Analytical vs. Informational

For each pair of topic sentences below, identify which is analytical and which is informational. Then explain in one or two sentences what the analytical sentence does that the informational one does not.

  1. a. The United States passed the Civil Rights Act in 1964.
    b. The Civil Rights Act of 1964 transformed American law by outlawing the explicitly discriminatory policies that had maintained racial segregation in public life, though its implementation remained contested for decades afterward.
  2. a. Mary Shelley's Frankenstein was published in 1818 and tells the story of a scientist who creates life.
    b. In Frankenstein, Shelley uses the relationship between the creature and his creator to critique the Romantic glorification of ambition, suggesting that the unchecked pursuit of knowledge without moral responsibility produces not triumph but abandonment and suffering.
  3. a. The brain releases dopamine in response to pleasurable experiences.
    b. The brain's release of dopamine in response to pleasurable stimuli provides the neurological basis for understanding addictive behavior, as repeated exposure trains the reward system to associate certain actions with relief rather than genuine pleasure.
Activity 4Reverse Outlining

Choose a piece of writing you have completed recently — an essay, a response paper, a paragraph assignment, or any written work for school. Read each paragraph carefully and write a one-sentence reverse-outline summary of what each paragraph actually argues. Then compare your reverse outline to the topic sentences you actually wrote. Answer the following questions in writing.

  1. Do your topic sentences match what the paragraphs actually do?
  2. Are any paragraphs unfocused, covering more than one main point?
  3. Are any topic sentences announcements that you could replace with claims?
  4. Which topic sentences are most specific and analytical?
  5. Rewrite at least two topic sentences based on what you discovered. Explain what changed and why the revision is stronger.
Activity 5Building Topic Sentences from Evidence

Below are three sets of evidence. For each set, write a topic sentence that could introduce a paragraph using that evidence. Your topic sentence should make a specific claim that the evidence could logically support.

  1. Evidence Set 1:
    — A 2022 survey found that 68% of college students report high levels of anxiety related to academic performance.
    — Students who reported high anxiety also reported more frequent procrastination and lower grade point averages.
    — Counseling center visits at many universities increased by over 30% in the past decade.
  2. Evidence Set 2:
    — The Amazon rainforest absorbs approximately two billion tons of carbon dioxide per year.
    — Deforestation has reduced the Amazon's carbon-absorbing capacity by an estimated 20% since 1970.
    — Scientists project that continued deforestation could push the Amazon past a tipping point within decades, releasing more carbon than it absorbs.
  3. Evidence Set 3:
    — In Of Mice and Men, Lennie repeatedly asks George to describe their dream farm.
    — The dream is never realized, and Lennie dies before the end of the novel.
    — Other characters also carry unfulfilled dreams — Curley's wife, Candy, and Crooks each describe visions of a better life that remain permanently out of reach.

Chapter Checklist

Before moving to the next chapter, confirm that you can do each of the following.

  • Explain what a topic sentence does and why it is essential to a well-developed paragraph.
  • Distinguish between a topic and a controlling idea and explain why both are required.
  • Identify announcements in your own and others' writing and replace them with claims.
  • Write topic sentences that are specific rather than vague.
  • Write analytical topic sentences that interpret or evaluate rather than simply report.
  • Recognize how topic sentence expectations vary across academic disciplines.
  • Evaluate when an implied topic sentence is appropriate and when an explicit one is necessary.
  • Use reverse outlining to check whether your topic sentences match what your paragraphs actually argue.
  • Revise weak topic sentences by identifying the source of weakness and applying an appropriate strategy.

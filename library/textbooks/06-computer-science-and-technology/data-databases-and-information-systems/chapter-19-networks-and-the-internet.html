Networks and the Internet

Every message, web page, video stream, and voice command travels across a network, following precise rules through physical infrastructure built by computer scientists and engineers over several decades. This chapter explains how computers communicate, from packets and addresses to the global internet and the cloud services built on top of it.

Every time you send a message, load a web page, stream a video, play an online game, or ask a voice assistant a question, your device is communicating across a network. That communication does not happen by magic. It follows precise rules, travels through physical infrastructure, and depends on systems designed by computer scientists and engineers over several decades.

This chapter explains how computers communicate. You will learn what a network is, how information travels across networks in small packets, how devices are identified by IP addresses, how names are translated into addresses, and how the global internet connects billions of devices into a single infrastructure. You will also learn why networks sometimes fail, how engineers think about performance and reliability, and how cloud computing uses networked infrastructure to deliver services.

By the end of this chapter, you should understand how a message gets from one device to another on the other side of the world, why that process is more complicated than it looks, and why the internet works as well as it does, given how many things could go wrong.

Note for Students

This chapter builds the conceptual map for everything networked you will study later, including the web in Chapter 20. You do not need any prior networking experience. You do need patience with new vocabulary, since this chapter introduces many terms that will reappear throughout the rest of the textbook.

19.1

What a Network Is

A network is a collection of devices that can communicate with each other by sending and receiving data.

Networks can be small. A home network might include a laptop, a phone, a tablet, a printer, and a smart television, all connected to a wireless router. Networks can also be enormous. The internet connects billions of devices across every country on earth.

What makes something a network is not size. It is the ability to send and receive information between devices.

Why Networks Matter

Before networks existed, computers were isolated. Each machine processed its own data and stored its own files. Moving information between machines required physically copying files to removable storage: floppy disks, tapes, or later USB drives.

Networks changed everything. Once computers could communicate, they could share data without physical transfer, use shared resources like printers and storage, run software that depends on other machines, communicate in real time across large distances, pool computing power for larger problems, and deliver services to many users simultaneously.

Today, almost every useful computing system depends on a network. The isolated computer is the exception, not the rule.

Three Things Every Network Needs

Every network, regardless of size, needs three basic things.

Devices

The endpoints that communicate, including computers, phones, servers, printers, sensors, and cameras. In network vocabulary, these endpoints are often called nodes or hosts.

Links

The physical or wireless connections between devices, such as cables, fiber optic lines, radio waves, or satellite links.

Protocols

The shared rules that govern communication. Devices cannot communicate without agreeing on how to format messages, address each other, and handle errors.

All Three Together

A network with no links is just a collection of isolated machines. A network with no protocols has links but no shared language. All three components must exist for a network to function.

19.2

Nodes, Links, and Packets

Nodes

A node is any device connected to a network. Nodes include end-user devices such as laptops, phones, tablets, and desktop computers, servers that respond to requests from other nodes, routers that direct traffic between networks, switches that connect multiple nodes within a local network, and printers, cameras, smart speakers, and other connected devices.

Not all nodes serve the same role. Some nodes send requests, some respond to them, and some help move data from one place to another. Understanding which nodes play which roles helps make sense of how networks are organized.

Links

A link is the connection between nodes. Links come in several forms. Wired links use physical cables, including Ethernet cables, which carry electrical signals, and fiber optic cables, which carry pulses of light and can transmit data much faster and farther than copper cables. Wireless links use radio waves, including Wi-Fi, used in homes, offices, and public spaces, cellular networks such as 4G and 5G, used by mobile phones, and satellite links, used in remote areas or for global coverage.

Each type of link has different characteristics for speed, distance, cost, and reliability. Fiber optic cables are very fast but expensive to install. Satellite links can reach remote locations but introduce delays, since signals must travel thousands of kilometers to a satellite and back.

Packets

Networks do not send information as a single continuous stream. Instead, they break information into small, manageable pieces called packets.

A packet is a small bundle of data that travels independently across a network.

To understand why packets are useful, imagine sending a long letter across a city. You could hire a single courier to carry the whole letter, but that courier might be delayed. Alternatively, you could cut the letter into ten pieces, put each piece in a separate envelope, and send each envelope by a different route. The pieces might arrive at different times and through different paths, but the recipient can reassemble them in order.

Packets work the same way. A large file, say a video, is broken into thousands of packets. Each packet travels independently. Different packets from the same video might take different routes through the network. When they all arrive, the receiving device reassembles them into the complete video.

What a Packet Contains

Each packet contains two main sections.

The header contains control information: the source address, indicating where the packet came from, the destination address, indicating where the packet is going, a sequence number, so packets can be reassembled in the right order, error-checking information, so the receiver can detect corrupted packets, and protocol information, indicating what kind of data the packet carries.

The payload contains the actual data being transmitted, such as a piece of the video, part of an email, or a fragment of a web page.

The header is overhead. It does not contain content, but without it, the packet could not be routed or reassembled. Network engineers design headers to be as small as possible while carrying enough information to make delivery reliable.

Packet Switching

The technique of breaking data into packets and routing each packet independently is called packet switching.

Packet switching has several advantages over older approaches that tried to reserve a dedicated path for the entire duration of a communication. Multiple conversations can share the same links simultaneously. If one route is congested, packets can take alternate paths. The network can recover from node failures by rerouting packets. Links are used efficiently rather than sitting idle during pauses in transmission.

Central Idea

Packet switching is one of the fundamental design decisions that makes the internet scalable and resilient. By breaking data into small, independently routed pieces, the network can share capacity efficiently and recover from failures without losing the entire communication.

19.3

Local Networks and Wide-Area Networks

Networks are often classified by their geographic scale.

Local Area Networks

A local area network, or LAN, connects devices within a limited area, such as a home, an office building, a school, or a campus. LANs are typically fast, because the distances are short and the infrastructure is controlled by a single organization.

Home Wi-Fi networks are LANs. The computers in a school computer lab form a LAN. The devices in a hospital wing connected to the hospital's internal network form a LAN.

Devices on a LAN can communicate directly with each other without going through the broader internet. A printer on a home LAN can receive a print job from a laptop on the same LAN without either device touching the internet.

Wide-Area Networks

A wide-area network, or WAN, connects devices across large geographic distances, such as across cities, countries, or continents. WANs use leased telecommunications lines, fiber optic cables, satellite links, or combinations of these.

The internet is the largest WAN in the world, but organizations also build private WANs. A corporation with offices in multiple cities might connect those offices using a private WAN, so that employees can share resources across locations without routing traffic through the public internet.

Other Network Classifications

Several other terms describe networks by scale or purpose.

Personal area network (PAN): a very small network connecting personal devices within a few meters. Bluetooth connections between a phone and wireless headphones form a PAN.

Metropolitan area network (MAN): a network that spans a city or metropolitan area, often operated by telecommunications companies or city governments.

Campus network: a network connecting buildings across a campus, such as a university, a corporate campus, or a hospital complex. Campus networks typically use both wired and wireless infrastructure.

Understanding these classifications helps make sense of how networks are designed, owned, and managed at different scales.

19.4

The Internet as a Network of Networks

The internet is not a single network. It is a vast collection of networks, operated by thousands of different organizations, all interconnected and able to exchange data using shared protocols.

How the Internet Is Structured

At the smallest scale, you have home networks and office LANs. These connect to larger networks operated by Internet Service Providers, or ISPs, companies such as cable providers or telephone companies that sell internet access. ISPs connect to even larger regional and national networks. Those connect to tier-1 networks, the largest backbone providers that form the core of the internet's infrastructure and span continents.

This hierarchy can be visualized as layers:

At every level, different organizations own and operate the infrastructure. The internet works because all of these organizations agree to use common protocols that allow their networks to exchange packets with each other.

Autonomous Systems

Each independently managed network on the internet is called an autonomous system, or AS. An autonomous system is a network under a single administrative authority, such as an ISP, a university, a corporation, or a government.

There are tens of thousands of autonomous systems. They connect to each other through peering agreements, arrangements where two networks agree to exchange traffic, either freely or for payment.

Why the Internet Is Resilient

The internet's design assumes that individual components will fail. Routers malfunction. Cables break. Data centers go offline. The internet is designed to route around these failures automatically.

Because there are many possible paths between any two points on the internet, packets can be rerouted when a path becomes unavailable. No single point of failure brings down the entire network.

This resilience was a deliberate design goal. Early research on packet-switched networks was motivated in part by the desire to build communication systems that could survive disruptions and continue functioning even when parts of the network were damaged.

19.5

IP Addresses

Every device on a network needs an identifier, so that packets can be addressed to it. On the internet, this identifier is called an IP address.

An IP address is a numerical label assigned to each device on a network. It serves two purposes: identifying the device and indicating its location within the network structure.

IPv4 Addresses

The original version of IP addressing, called IPv4, uses 32-bit addresses. These are typically written as four numbers separated by dots, where each number is between 0 and 255.

Examples of IPv4 addresses:

  • 192.168.1.1
  • 10.0.0.1
  • 172.217.10.46

Each number between the dots represents 8 bits. Because 232 equals approximately 4.3 billion, IPv4 allows for about 4.3 billion unique addresses.

When the internet was designed in the 1970s and 1980s, 4.3 billion addresses seemed more than sufficient. As the internet grew far beyond initial expectations, the available pool of IPv4 addresses was exhausted. This created pressure to develop a new addressing system.

IPv6 Addresses

IPv6 uses 128-bit addresses, written as eight groups of four hexadecimal digits separated by colons.

Example — IPv6 Address
2001:0db8:85a3:0000:0000:8a2e:0370:7334

IPv6 allows for approximately 3.4 × 1038 unique addresses, an astronomically large number that is sufficient for every device on earth to have many unique addresses.

The internet is gradually transitioning from IPv4 to IPv6, but both systems are currently in widespread use simultaneously.

Public and Private Addresses

Not every device has a unique public IP address. Home networks use a technique called Network Address Translation, or NAT, that allows multiple devices on a LAN to share a single public IP address.

Your home router has one public IP address assigned by your ISP. All devices in your home, including your laptop, phone, tablet, and smart TV, communicate with the internet through that single address. The router keeps track of which internal device each connection belongs to and directs incoming packets to the right device.

Private IP address ranges are reserved for internal use within LANs and are not routable on the public internet. Common private ranges include addresses starting with 192.168, 10, and 172.16 through 172.31.

Dynamic and Static Addresses

IP addresses can be assigned in two ways.

Dynamic addresses are assigned automatically each time a device connects to the network, using a protocol called DHCP, or Dynamic Host Configuration Protocol. The address might change each time. Most home devices and mobile phones use dynamic addresses.

Static addresses are manually configured and remain constant. Servers and network infrastructure typically use static addresses, so that clients can reliably locate them.

19.6

Domain Names and DNS

IP addresses are effective for machines, but they are difficult for people to remember. Nobody types 172.217.10.46 into a browser to visit a website. Instead, people type names like www.example.com.

The system that translates human-readable names into IP addresses is called the Domain Name System, or DNS.

Domain Names

A domain name is a human-readable label that maps to an IP address. Domain names are organized hierarchically.

Consider the name mail.university.edu. The portion edu is the top-level domain, or TLD, a category that indicates educational institutions. The portion university is the second-level domain, the specific organization. The portion mail is a subdomain, a specific service or server within the organization.

Common top-level domains include .com for commercial organizations, .org for nonprofits and organizations, .net, originally for network infrastructure, .edu for educational institutions, .gov for government entities, and country-code TLDs such as .uk, .de, .jp, and .ca.

How DNS Works

When you type a domain name into your browser, your computer cannot immediately connect to the server. It must first find out the IP address corresponding to that name. The process unfolds in steps.

  1. Check the local cache. Your device stores recent DNS lookups in a local cache. If you visited the same site recently, the IP address may already be stored, and no DNS query is needed.
  2. Query a recursive resolver. If the address is not cached, your device queries a recursive resolver, a DNS server operated by your ISP or a third-party DNS service. The recursive resolver does the work of finding the answer.
  3. Query the root nameservers. The recursive resolver queries one of thirteen sets of root nameservers, the authoritative servers at the top of the DNS hierarchy. The root nameserver does not know the IP address for mail.university.edu, but it knows which nameservers are responsible for .edu addresses.
  4. Query the TLD nameservers. The recursive resolver queries the .edu nameservers, which know which nameservers are responsible for university.edu.
  5. Query the authoritative nameserver. The recursive resolver queries university.edu's authoritative nameserver, which returns the IP address for mail.university.edu.
  6. Return the answer. The recursive resolver returns the IP address to your device and caches it for future use.

This entire process typically takes milliseconds, though it involves communication between multiple servers around the world.

Why DNS Matters

DNS is a critical piece of internet infrastructure. If DNS fails, domain names cannot be resolved, and most internet services become unreachable, even if the underlying network is functioning. For this reason, DNS is designed with redundancy at every level.

19.7

Protocols

A protocol is a set of rules that governs how devices communicate. Protocols define the format of messages, meaning how data is structured, the sequence of exchanges, meaning what happens first and what happens next, error handling, meaning what to do when something goes wrong, and addressing, meaning how devices identify each other.

Without protocols, two devices could be physically connected but unable to communicate meaningfully, like two people trying to have a conversation in languages neither understands.

The Protocol Stack

Networks do not use a single protocol for everything. Instead, communication is organized in layers, with each layer responsible for a different aspect of communication.

This layered approach is called a protocol stack, or network stack. Each layer provides services to the layer above it and uses services from the layer below it.

A widely used model for understanding network layers is the TCP/IP model, which has four layers:

Diagram — The TCP/IP Protocol Stack
┌─────────────────────────────┐
│     Application Layer       │  HTTP, HTTPS, DNS, SMTP, FTP
├─────────────────────────────┤
│     Transport Layer         │  TCP, UDP
├─────────────────────────────┤
│     Internet Layer          │  IP
├─────────────────────────────┤
│     Network Access Layer    │  Ethernet, Wi-Fi
└─────────────────────────────┘
Network Access Layer

Handles the physical transmission of bits across a link, such as electrical signals in a cable, the radio waves of Wi-Fi, or light pulses in fiber optic cables.

Internet Layer

Handles addressing and routing packets across multiple networks. The primary protocol at this layer is IP, which puts source and destination addresses in packet headers and routes packets from network to network.

Transport Layer

Handles reliable delivery between two endpoints, using TCP for guaranteed ordering and error checking, or UDP for faster but unreliable delivery.

Application Layer

Handles the specific tasks that applications need, such as loading web pages, sending email, resolving domain names, and transferring files.

The Two Main Transport Protocols

TCP, or Transmission Control Protocol, provides reliable delivery, guaranteed ordering, and error checking. It is used when every byte must arrive correctly.

UDP, or User Datagram Protocol, is faster but unreliable, with no guaranteed ordering. It is used when speed matters more than perfection, for example in live video or gaming.

Key Protocols at the Application Layer

HTTP, or Hypertext Transfer Protocol, is the protocol used to request and deliver web pages. When your browser loads a web page, it sends an HTTP request to a web server, which responds with HTTP containing the page's content.

HTTPS, or HTTP Secure, is HTTP with encryption added. HTTPS ensures that the content of communication between your browser and a server is encrypted, making it unreadable to anyone intercepting the traffic. The padlock icon in a browser's address bar indicates HTTPS.

SMTP, or Simple Mail Transfer Protocol, is used to send email between servers.

FTP, or File Transfer Protocol, is used to transfer files between computers.

SSH, or Secure Shell, is used to securely connect to and control remote computers via a command-line interface.

Why Layering Matters

Layering keeps each protocol focused on a specific job. When engineers improve one layer, for example developing a faster networking technology at the physical layer, the layers above it do not need to change. HTTP works the same whether the physical connection is a copper cable or a fiber optic line. Layering also allows different protocols to be swapped in at each layer, so a web application using HTTP does not need to know or care whether the transport layer is using a wired or wireless connection.

19.8

Routers and Packet Paths

A router is a network device that forwards packets from one network to another. Routers are the traffic directors of the internet.

How Routers Work

When a packet arrives at a router, the router examines the destination IP address in the packet's header. The router then consults its routing table, a list of known networks and the best path to reach each of them.

Based on the routing table, the router forwards the packet to the next router on the path toward the destination. This process repeats at each router until the packet reaches its destination.

Consider a simplified example. You send a message from your laptop in one city to a server in another country. The message is broken into packets. Each packet leaves your laptop and reaches your home router, your home router forwards it to your ISP's network, your ISP's router forwards it to a larger network, several more routers forward it across national backbone networks, it crosses undersea cables to another continent, several more routers forward it through networks in the destination country, and it reaches the destination server.

Each step is called a hop. A packet typically passes through ten to twenty routers on a journey across the internet, though this varies considerably.

Routing Tables and Routing Protocols

Routers do not have hardcoded knowledge of the entire internet's structure. Instead, they use routing protocols to discover network topology and build their routing tables dynamically.

When a network path becomes unavailable, because a router fails or a cable is cut, routing protocols propagate the information to neighboring routers, which update their tables and begin routing around the failure.

This dynamic routing is why the internet can survive localized failures without going down entirely. Packets simply take different paths.

The traceroute Tool

A tool called traceroute, or tracert on Windows, shows the path packets take from your device to a destination. Running traceroute example.com reveals each router the packets pass through, along with the time each hop takes.

Here is an example of what traceroute output might look like:

Terminal — traceroute Output
traceroute to example.com (93.184.216.34)
  1  192.168.1.1          2 ms     (home router)
  2  10.0.0.1             8 ms     (ISP gateway)
  3  72.14.204.97        12 ms     (ISP backbone)
  4  108.170.246.34      15 ms     (larger backbone)
  5  209.85.255.93       85 ms     (transatlantic link)
  6  93.184.216.34       88 ms     (destination)

Each line is one hop. The time shown is the round-trip time, how long a test packet took to travel to that router and back. Large jumps in time often indicate a long-distance link, like a transatlantic fiber cable.

19.9

Bandwidth, Latency, and Reliability

Two of the most important measures of network performance are bandwidth and latency. They are different things, and they affect user experience differently.

Bandwidth

Bandwidth is the maximum rate at which data can be transferred across a network link. It is typically measured in bits per second, or larger multiples.

Table 19.1 — Common units of bandwidth.
UnitAbbreviationSize
KbpsKilobits per second1,000 bps
MbpsMegabits per second1,000,000 bps
GbpsGigabits per second1,000,000,000 bps

A home internet connection might have a download bandwidth of 100 Mbps. A data center might connect to the internet at 10 Gbps or higher.

Bandwidth determines how much data can flow simultaneously. Higher bandwidth means more data can be transferred in a given time.

An Analogy

Bandwidth is like the number of lanes on a highway. More lanes mean more cars can travel simultaneously.

Latency

Latency is the time it takes for a packet to travel from its source to its destination. It is typically measured in milliseconds.

Latency is determined primarily by physical distance and the speed at which signals travel through the medium. Light through fiber optic cables travels at about 200,000 km/s, fast, but not instantaneous. A packet traveling from New York to London and back, approximately 12,000 km round trip, requires at minimum about 60 ms just for the signal to travel, plus additional time for processing at each router.

An Analogy

Latency is like the time it takes to drive from one city to another, regardless of how many lanes the highway has. You can have a very wide highway, meaning high bandwidth, but it still takes time to travel the distance.

Bandwidth vs. Latency: Different Problems

Bandwidth and latency affect different types of applications differently.

Table 19.2 — Which applications depend most on bandwidth, latency, or both.
ApplicationLimited ByWhy
Streaming videoBandwidthRequires continuous high data rate
Large file downloadsBandwidthTotal data transferred is large
Online gamingLatencyPlayer actions must reach server quickly
Video callsBothRequires both speed and responsiveness
Loading a web pageLatency, then bandwidthFirst request must complete before content begins loading

A fast internet connection with high bandwidth but high latency, like a satellite connection from a remote area, might download large files quickly but feel sluggish for gaming and interactive tasks. A low-latency connection might feel responsive even if bandwidth is limited.

Throughput vs. Bandwidth

Throughput is the actual rate at which data is transferred in practice, which is often lower than the theoretical maximum bandwidth. Throughput is affected by congestion, meaning many devices sharing the same link, packet loss, meaning packets that must be retransmitted, protocol overhead, meaning header data consuming bandwidth, and processing delays at routers.

Bandwidth is a capacity. Throughput is the actual performance achieved.

Reliability and Packet Loss

Networks are not perfectly reliable. Packets can be lost due to congestion, where routers drop packets when their buffers are full, hardware failures, signal interference, especially in wireless networks, and corrupted packets detected and discarded by error-checking.

The TCP protocol handles packet loss by requiring acknowledgment of received packets and retransmitting any that are not acknowledged. This makes TCP reliable, but retransmission takes time and reduces throughput.

UDP does not retransmit lost packets. For some applications, such as live audio or video, a small amount of packet loss is acceptable, because retransmitting old data is pointless when newer data has already arrived.

Network Congestion

Congestion occurs when more packets are sent across a link than it can carry. Routers have limited buffer memory. When a router's buffer fills up, it discards incoming packets. Those discards cause retransmissions, which can create a feedback loop of worsening congestion.

TCP includes congestion control mechanisms that detect packet loss and automatically slow down transmission to reduce congestion. This is one reason internet performance degrades gracefully, rather than collapsing completely, when many users are active simultaneously.

19.10

Cloud Computing and Connected Services

The modern internet has enabled an entirely new model of computing: cloud computing, in which computational resources are delivered as services over a network rather than running on local hardware.

What Cloud Computing Means

The term "cloud" refers to computing infrastructure operated by a third party and accessed over the internet. Instead of running software on your own computer or buying servers for your organization, you access computing resources provided by companies such as Amazon Web Services, Google Cloud, and Microsoft Azure.

Cloud computing encompasses several kinds of services.

Infrastructure as a Service, or IaaS, is a model where the cloud provider offers virtual machines, storage, and networking. The customer installs their own operating systems and software. This is closest to owning a server, but the hardware is managed by the provider.

Platform as a Service, or PaaS, is a model where the cloud provider offers a managed environment for running applications, including databases, web servers, and development tools, without the customer needing to manage the underlying infrastructure.

Software as a Service, or SaaS, is a model where the cloud provider delivers a complete application over the internet. Email services, document editors, video conferencing, and customer relationship tools are examples. The customer uses the software without knowing or controlling any of the infrastructure.

Why Cloud Computing Became Important
Scalability

Cloud providers operate enormous data centers with vast computing resources. A startup can begin with small resources and scale to handle millions of users without building its own infrastructure.

Economics

Buying, housing, cooling, and maintaining physical servers is expensive. Cloud services allow organizations to pay only for what they use, often on a per-hour or per-request basis.

Reliability

Major cloud providers operate multiple data centers in different geographic regions. If one data center fails, services can continue from another.

Accessibility

Cloud services are available from any device with an internet connection, so users can access their data and applications from anywhere.

Content Delivery Networks

Delivering content quickly to users around the world is a challenge because of latency. A server in North America takes measurably longer to respond to a user in Southeast Asia than to a user nearby.

A Content Delivery Network, or CDN, addresses this by distributing copies of content to servers located in many geographic regions. When a user requests content, they are served from a nearby CDN server rather than the original source, reducing latency.

Major websites, streaming services, and software downloads typically use CDNs to ensure fast delivery, regardless of where a user is located.

Edge Computing

A newer trend is edge computing, moving computation closer to the devices that generate and consume data, rather than sending all data to distant data centers.

Smart cameras, industrial sensors, autonomous vehicles, and connected devices generate enormous amounts of data. Sending all of it to a distant data center for processing introduces latency and requires significant bandwidth. Edge computing runs processing on local devices or nearby small servers, sending only processed results or important events to central systems.

Edge computing is increasingly important for applications where real-time response is critical, such as factory automation, traffic management, and medical monitoring.

How Everything Connects

A modern web application illustrates how all the pieces of this chapter work together.

  1. A user types a domain name into a browser.
  2. DNS resolves the name to an IP address.
  3. The browser sends an HTTPS request, which is broken into TCP packets.
  4. IP routes those packets across multiple networks and routers.
  5. The packets arrive at a server, possibly a cloud-hosted virtual machine.
  6. The server processes the request and sends a response.
  7. The response travels back across the network via the same mechanism.
  8. The browser reassembles the packets and renders the web page.
  9. Some resources, such as images and videos, are delivered from CDN servers closer to the user.
Bringing It Together

Each step in this process involves the concepts covered in this chapter. Networks, packets, IP addresses, DNS, protocols, routers, bandwidth, latency, and cloud infrastructure all participate in delivering a single web page.

Chapter Summary
  • A network is a collection of devices connected so they can communicate. Networks require nodes, meaning devices, links, meaning connections, and protocols, meaning rules. Information travels across networks in small bundles called packets, each containing a header with addressing and control information and a payload with actual data.
  • Networks vary in scale, from personal area networks to local area networks, metropolitan networks, wide-area networks, and the internet itself. The internet is not a single network but a global collection of interconnected networks operated by thousands of organizations, all using common protocols to exchange data.
  • Every device on an internet-connected network has an IP address, a numerical identifier that allows packets to be addressed and routed. IPv4 uses 32-bit addresses, while IPv6 uses 128-bit addresses to accommodate the enormous and growing number of connected devices.
  • The Domain Name System translates human-readable domain names into IP addresses through a hierarchical lookup process involving root nameservers, top-level domain nameservers, and authoritative nameservers.
  • Protocols are the rules governing communication. The TCP/IP protocol stack organizes protocols into layers: the network access layer handles physical transmission, the internet layer handles addressing and routing, the transport layer handles reliable delivery through TCP or fast delivery without guarantees through UDP, and the application layer handles tasks like web browsing, email, and file transfer.
  • Routers direct packets through the network by consulting routing tables and forwarding packets hop by hop toward their destination. Routing protocols allow routers to discover network topology and route around failures dynamically.
  • Network performance is measured by bandwidth, the maximum data rate, and latency, the delay. These affect different applications differently, and throughput is the actual performance achieved in practice, typically lower than theoretical bandwidth.
  • Cloud computing delivers computing resources over the internet as services, enabling scalability, flexibility, and global accessibility. Content delivery networks distribute content geographically to reduce latency, and edge computing moves processing closer to devices for time-sensitive applications.
Key Terms
Autonomous System (AS)

An independently managed network on the internet, operated by a single organization such as an ISP, university, or company.

Bandwidth

The maximum rate at which data can be transferred across a network link, typically measured in bits per second, megabits per second, or gigabits per second.

CDN (Content Delivery Network)

A distributed network of servers that stores copies of content in multiple geographic locations to deliver it quickly to users nearby.

Cloud Computing

A model of delivering computing resources, including processing, storage, and software, as services over the internet, operated by a third party.

Congestion

A condition in which more packets are sent across a network link than the link can carry, causing packets to be discarded and performance to degrade.

DHCP (Dynamic Host Configuration Protocol)

A protocol that automatically assigns IP addresses to devices when they join a network.

DNS (Domain Name System)

A distributed system that translates human-readable domain names into IP addresses.

Domain Name

A human-readable label such as example.com that maps to an IP address through DNS.

Edge Computing

A computing model in which processing occurs on or near the devices that generate data, rather than in distant data centers.

Header

The control section of a packet, containing addressing, sequencing, and error-checking information.

HTTPS (HTTP Secure)

An encrypted version of HTTP that protects communication between a browser and a web server.

IP Address

A numerical identifier assigned to each device on a network, used to address packets and identify the device's location within the network.

IPv4

The original version of the Internet Protocol, using 32-bit addresses, supporting approximately 4.3 billion unique addresses.

IPv6

The current version of the Internet Protocol, using 128-bit addresses, supporting an astronomically large number of unique addresses.

ISP (Internet Service Provider)

A company that provides internet access to homes, businesses, and other organizations.

Latency

The time delay for a packet to travel from its source to its destination, typically measured in milliseconds.

LAN (Local Area Network)

A network connecting devices within a limited geographic area, such as a home, office, or campus.

Link

A physical or wireless connection between network nodes.

NAT (Network Address Translation)

A technique allowing multiple devices on a local network to share a single public IP address.

Node

Any device connected to a network, including computers, servers, routers, printers, and connected devices.

Packet

A small bundle of data transmitted across a network, containing a header with control information and a payload with actual data.

Packet Switching

The technique of breaking data into packets and routing each packet independently across a network.

Payload

The data content section of a packet, containing the actual information being transmitted.

Protocol

A set of rules governing how devices communicate, defining message formats, sequences, addressing, and error handling.

Protocol Stack

A layered set of protocols, each responsible for a different aspect of network communication.

Router

A network device that forwards packets from one network to another based on routing tables.

Routing Table

A data structure stored in a router containing information about known networks and the best paths to reach them.

TCP (Transmission Control Protocol)

A transport-layer protocol that provides reliable, ordered delivery of packets between two endpoints.

Throughput

The actual rate of data transfer achieved in practice, typically lower than theoretical bandwidth.

Top-Level Domain (TLD)

The rightmost portion of a domain name, such as .com, .org, .edu, or .gov, indicating a category or country.

UDP (User Datagram Protocol)

A transport-layer protocol that provides fast but unreliable packet delivery, used when speed matters more than guaranteed delivery.

WAN (Wide-Area Network)

A network connecting devices across large geographic distances, such as across cities, countries, or continents.

Worked Example Review
Example 1 — Tracing a Web Request

A student in Nairobi opens a browser and types https://example.com. The web server for example.com is located in Virginia, United States. Walk through what happens, step by step, from the moment the student presses Enter until the web page begins to appear.

Step-by-Step Walkthrough

  1. The browser checks its DNS cache for example.com. Assume no cached entry exists.
  2. The browser sends a DNS query to the recursive resolver, which the student's ISP provides.
  3. The recursive resolver queries the root nameserver, and learns that .com TLD nameservers handle .com domains.
  4. The recursive resolver queries the .com TLD nameserver, and learns which nameservers are authoritative for example.com.
  5. The recursive resolver queries example.com's authoritative nameserver. The authoritative nameserver returns the IP address, 93.184.216.34.
  6. The recursive resolver caches the result and returns 93.184.216.34 to the browser.
  7. The browser initiates a TCP connection to 93.184.216.34 on port 443, the standard HTTPS port. This involves a handshake exchange of several packets to establish the connection.
  8. The browser sends an HTTPS request for the web page. The request is broken into TCP packets. IP routes those packets through Nairobi's ISP, across Africa, through undersea cables on the seabed of the Indian Ocean and Atlantic Ocean, through networks in Europe or the US, and into Virginia.
  9. The web server receives the request, prepares the page, and sends the response as a series of packets routed back to Nairobi.
  10. The browser reassembles the packets, decrypts the HTTPS content, and renders the page.

The total time for steps 1 through 10 is typically under a second for a simple web page, though the round-trip distance adds latency at every stage.

Example 2 — Understanding Bandwidth and Latency

A student has a home internet connection with 100 Mbps download bandwidth and 50 ms latency to a server in a nearby city.

Question A: How long does it take to download a 50 MB file from that server?

  • 50 MB equals 50 × 8, or 400 Megabits, since 1 byte equals 8 bits.
  • At 100 Mbps, 400 Mbps divided by 100 Mbps equals 4 seconds for the data to transfer.
  • Add latency for the initial connection setup, a few round trips of 50 ms each, roughly 150 to 200 ms of setup overhead.
  • Total: approximately 4.15 to 4.2 seconds.

Question B: The student switches to a satellite internet connection with 25 Mbps bandwidth but 600 ms latency. How does this affect the file download and online gaming?

  • File download: 400 Mbps divided by 25 Mbps equals 16 seconds, plus latency overhead. The download takes about four times as long, due to lower bandwidth.
  • Online gaming: a latency of 600 ms means actions take 600 ms to reach the server. At this latency, real-time games become essentially unplayable, because player actions are delayed by over half a second before the server registers them.
  • Conclusion: high bandwidth but high latency is acceptable for downloading files, where total time matters, but not for gaming or interactive applications, where responsiveness matters.

Coming Next — Chapter 20: The World Wide Web

Chapter 19 explained how the internet works, the infrastructure that carries packets between devices. Chapter 20 introduces the World Wide Web: the system of pages, documents, applications, and services that runs on top of the internet.

The internet and the web are not the same thing, even though they are often confused. The internet is the infrastructure, the cables, routers, protocols, and addressing systems. The web is one set of services built on that infrastructure, using HTTP to request and deliver linked documents.

Chapter 20 will explain how browsers communicate with web servers, what URLs contain, how HTML, CSS, and JavaScript work together to build what you see on a web page, the difference between web pages and web applications, how search engines work, and what web accessibility means. By the end of Chapter 20, you will understand what happens technically from the moment you type an address to the moment a page appears on your screen.

Every time you search for a book in a library catalog, every time a school records your grade, every time a hospital updates your patient file, every time you log into a website and it remembers your preferences, a database is at work. Databases are not exotic technology reserved for large corporations and government agencies. They are one of the most ordinary and essential tools in modern life, operating quietly beneath the surfaces of systems people use every hour of every day.

This chapter opens the door to everything that follows in this textbook. It introduces data as the raw recorded facts that systems collect, store, and retrieve. It introduces databases as organized structures designed to hold data reliably and make it retrievable on demand. It introduces information systems as larger combinations of people, processes, software, hardware, data, rules, and decisions that accomplish real organizational goals.

By the time you finish this chapter, you will understand why this field of study exists, what problems it solves, and what kinds of questions you will be equipped to answer after working through all twenty-eight chapters. You will also understand something that many beginners miss: the difference between data that simply exists and information that genuinely helps someone decide or act.

Note for New Students

This chapter is not yet technical. It does not teach SQL syntax or database design rules. It builds the conceptual foundation that will make every technical topic that follows meaningful rather than mechanical. Take time to read it carefully.

1.1

What Data Studies

Data is everywhere, but the word itself deserves careful attention. In everyday conversation, people use "data" and "information" as if they mean the same thing. In this field, the distinction matters.

Data refers to raw recorded facts, observations, measurements, or values that have been captured and stored. Data is the raw material of information systems. On its own, a single piece of data may mean very little. It is a number, a name, a date, a code, a reading, a count, or a category. Without context, without organization, and without interpretation, data does not answer questions. It simply exists.

Consider a few examples of raw data:

  • 47
  • Nairobi
  • 2024-03-15
  • TRUE
  • BIO201
  • 19.95
  • Absent

Each of these values is a piece of data. Alone, they are nearly meaningless. Is 47 a temperature, an age, a student number, a quantity, or a test score? Is Nairobi a birthplace, the location of a branch office, or the home city of a library user? Is 19.95 a price, a weight, a distance, or a time in seconds?

Data becomes meaningful only when it is placed in a structure, connected to context, and organized for use. The study of data is the study of how these raw facts are captured, represented, typed, organized, validated, stored, retrieved, and eventually used to support real decisions.

Central Idea

What data studies is not just the facts themselves, but the entire life cycle of those facts: how they are collected, what form they take, how they are organized into records and tables, how they are validated and cleaned, how they are stored securely, how they are queried and retrieved, how they are combined with other data, how they are presented to people who need them, and how they are eventually updated, archived, or deleted.

Data in Real Systems

Data is generated constantly in modern organizations. A school generates data about students, courses, instructors, grades, attendance, and resources. A hospital generates data about patients, appointments, diagnoses, treatments, medications, and staff. A library generates data about books, users, loans, reservations, fines, and catalog records. A store generates data about products, suppliers, customers, orders, payments, and inventory. A government agency generates data about citizens, services, transactions, budgets, and regulations.

None of these organizations could function without reliable ways to store and retrieve their data. Before databases, organizations used filing cabinets, index cards, ledger books, and paper forms. These systems worked at small scales, but they broke down under the weight of growth, multiple users, complex queries, and the need for reliable, consistent records. Databases exist to solve these problems.

1.2

What Databases Do

A database is an organized collection of data stored so that it can be accessed, managed, and updated efficiently. This definition is simple, but what it implies is substantial. A database is not just a pile of data. It is a structured system designed with specific goals: to store data reliably, to retrieve it accurately, to allow multiple users to work with it simultaneously, to protect it from unauthorized access, and to maintain consistency even when many changes are happening at once.

Before Databases: File Cabinets and Spreadsheets

Imagine a small school with two hundred students. The school keeps student records in paper folders stored in a filing cabinet. Each folder contains a student's name, address, enrollment date, courses, and grades. This system works at small scale, but it has serious problems.

If you want to find all students enrolled in a specific course, you must open every folder and check manually. If a student's address changes, someone must find the folder and update it, but if the address also appears in separate attendance records, those records must also be updated, and if any copy is missed, the records become inconsistent. If two staff members need to access the same folder at the same time, one must wait. If the cabinet is damaged, all records may be lost permanently.

Spreadsheets improve on paper files, but they also have serious limitations. They are designed for one user at a time. They have no built-in way to enforce rules like "every student must have a unique identifier" or "a grade must belong to a real course." They are easy to corrupt accidentally. They do not handle complex connections between tables well. They do not control who can see or change different parts of the data.

A database solves all of these problems.

What Databases Actually Do
Storage

Stores data on disk in an organized structure so that it persists across time, sessions, and users, with saving handled automatically by the system.

Structure Enforcement

Imposes structure through defined columns, data types, and constraints that prevent invalid data from entering the system.

Retrieval

Retrieves exactly the records that match a specified condition, even from tables with millions of rows, using queries rather than manual browsing.

Concurrency

Allows many users to read and write data at the same time without corrupting the data or producing inconsistent results.

Security

Controls who can access which data and what they can do with it, with different permissions for different roles and users.

Integrity

Maintains consistency within and across tables, preventing records from becoming orphaned or contradictory.

Database Management Systems

When this textbook refers to a database, it usually means both the stored data and the database management system (DBMS) that manages it. The DBMS is the software that controls how data is stored, indexed, retrieved, and protected. Examples include SQLite, PostgreSQL, MySQL, Microsoft SQL Server, Oracle Database, and MariaDB. This textbook uses SQLite for examples because it is lightweight, requires no separate server, and runs on nearly any computer. The SQL you learn here will transfer directly to other systems.

1.3

What Information Systems Do

A database is a technical structure for storing and retrieving data. An information system is something broader: an organized combination of people, processes, data, software, hardware, and rules working together to collect, store, process, and distribute information that supports decisions and activities within an organization.

This distinction is important. A database is one component of an information system, but it is rarely the whole story. The information system also includes the people who enter data, the forms they use to do it, the validation rules that check their input, the reports that display results, the dashboards that summarize trends, the workflows that define what happens next, the permissions that control access, the policies that govern retention and deletion, and the decisions that the system is ultimately meant to support.

A Simple Example: A Library

Consider a library. The library has a database that stores records about books, users, and loans. But the library's information system is much more than that database.

It includes librarians who add new books to the catalog. It includes the intake forms used to record a new user's name, contact information, and library card number. It includes the checkout interface used when a user borrows a book. It includes automated notices sent when a book is due for return. It includes the search interface that users employ to find books by title, author, or subject. It includes the reports that administrators use to see which books are most popular, which users have overdue items, and how many new books were added in a given period. It includes access controls that determine which staff members can mark a book as lost and which can see a user's full borrowing history. It includes the backup plan that protects the catalog against loss.

All of these elements together form the library information system. The database is the core storage and retrieval engine. The rest of the system makes the data useful to people with real needs.

Why This Distinction Matters

Students who study only the database layer often encounter a gap when they work on real systems. They can write excellent SQL queries, but they do not know how data gets into the database in the first place, or how the results of their queries get displayed to the people who need them, or what happens when a user enters invalid data into a form, or who is responsible for deciding how long records should be kept before deletion. This textbook covers both layers.

1.4

Data, Software, People, and Processes

Information systems involve four essential components working together: data, software, people, and processes. Understanding how these components relate helps explain why information systems can succeed or fail.

Data

Data is the content that the system stores, retrieves, and processes. The quality of the data determines much of the usefulness of the system. A database that contains inaccurate, incomplete, or inconsistent data will produce misleading reports even if the software is perfectly written. This is why data quality, cleaning, and validation occupy an entire chapter early in this textbook.

Software

Software is the application code and database management system that store, retrieve, process, and display data. Software includes the database engine, the forms and interfaces users interact with, the query logic that retrieves and summarizes records, the validation checks that reject bad input, the reporting tools that generate summaries, and the security layer that controls access.

People

People design systems, enter data, use reports, make decisions, maintain databases, set policies, and bear responsibility for outcomes. A well-designed database with well-written software can still fail if the people who use it enter data carelessly, ignore validation warnings, or make poor decisions based on its output. Information systems exist to serve people, but they also depend on people to work correctly.

Processes

Processes are the sequences of steps that an organization follows to accomplish repeated tasks. A course enrollment process involves steps: a student searches available courses, selects sections, submits an enrollment request, the system checks prerequisites and capacity, the enrollment is confirmed or rejected, the student receives a confirmation, and the instructor's roster is updated. An information system is designed around processes like this.

Understanding the process is essential before designing the database. A database designed without understanding the real workflow it supports will inevitably be missing tables, missing fields, or structured in ways that make the process harder rather than easier.

How They Work Together

These four components are interdependent. Poor data quality undermines even the best software. Software that fails to support the real process frustrates users and leads to workarounds that introduce further data problems. People who do not understand the system's purpose enter data incorrectly. Processes that are not well understood cannot be supported by a database design that serves them. Studying data, databases, and information systems means learning to think about all four components together.

1.5

Tables, Records, and Relationships

The central organizing structure in a relational database is the table. Understanding what a table is, what it contains, and how tables relate to each other is the foundation of everything else in this course.

Tables

A table is a structured set of data organized into rows and columns. Each table represents one kind of thing: students, courses, books, products, users, orders, or appointments. A table is sometimes called a relation in formal database theory, but the everyday term "table" is used throughout this textbook.

Here is a simple example of a table that stores information about students:

Table 1.1 — The students table: each row is one student, each column is one kind of information.
student_id last_name first_name enrollment_date department
1001OkaforAmara2023-09-01Biology
1002MendesRafael2023-09-01Mathematics
1003TanakaYuki2024-01-15History
1004SvenssonLena2024-01-15Biology
Records and Fields

Each row in a table is called a record. A record represents one instance of the thing the table describes. In the students table above, each record represents one student. Each column in a table is called a field or attribute. A field represents one piece of information stored for every record in the table. The last_name field stores the last name of every student. The enrollment_date field stores the date each student enrolled.

Relationships

Most databases contain many tables. The power of a relational database comes from the fact that these tables are connected to each other through relationships.

Suppose the school also has a table of courses:

Table 1.2 — The courses table.
course_id course_name department credits
BIO201Cell BiologyBiology3
MAT301Linear AlgebraMathematics4
HIS101World History IHistory3
BIO305GeneticsBiology4

And a third table that records which students are enrolled in which courses:

Table 1.3 — The enrollments table connects students to courses using shared identifier values.
enrollment_id student_id course_id term
50011001BIO2012024-Spring
50021001BIO3052024-Spring
50031002MAT3012024-Spring
50041003HIS1012024-Spring

The enrollment table connects students to courses. It does not repeat all the student information or all the course information. Instead, it stores just the identifying codes that link to the full records in the other tables. This is the relational approach: store each piece of information once, in the table where it belongs, and connect tables through shared identifiers.

This design means that if Amara Okafor's last name changes, it is updated in exactly one place: the students table. The enrollment records remain correct without any changes because they refer to the student by identifier, not by name.

1.6

Queries and Questions

One of the most important ideas in this textbook is that a query is a question asked of a database. When you write a query, you are not manipulating data by hand. You are describing what you want, and the database management system finds and returns it.

What a Query Is

A query is a formal instruction written in a query language. The standard query language for relational databases is SQL, which stands for Structured Query Language. SQL is read by a database engine, which finds the matching records, applies any filters and sorting rules, computes any summaries, and returns a result.

Here is a simple example. Suppose you want to know which students are enrolled in the Biology department. In plain language, the question is: "Show me the names of all students in the Biology department." In SQL, this question looks like:

SQL — Example Query
SELECT last_name, first_name
FROM students
WHERE department = 'Biology';

The database reads this query and returns:

Result — Students in the Biology department.
last_namefirst_name
OkaforAmara
SvenssonLena

You do not need to read or write SQL yet. Chapter 10 introduces SQL carefully and builds your skills through the SQL chapters in Part III. But it is important to understand right now that a query is simply a question expressed in a formal language that the database can interpret and answer.

Queries Answer Real Questions

The questions that databases answer are not abstract. They are the real questions that organizations need to answer to do their work.

A school might need to ask: How many students are enrolled in each department? Which students have not yet enrolled in a required course? A library might need to ask: Which books are currently on loan? Which users have had overdue items in the past six months? A hospital might need to ask: Which patients have a scheduled appointment tomorrow? Which medications were prescribed more than three hundred times in the past quarter?

Every one of these questions can be answered with a query, as long as the data has been stored in a well-designed database. Learning to write those queries, and more importantly learning to design databases that can answer those questions, is a central goal of this textbook.

1.7

Forms, Reports, and Dashboards

Databases store and retrieve data, but most people who use databases never see the tables directly. Instead, they interact with systems through three kinds of interfaces: forms for entering data, reports for reading organized summaries, and dashboards for viewing current conditions at a glance.

Forms

A form is an interface that allows a user to enter or edit data in a database. When a librarian adds a new book to the catalog, she likely fills in a form that asks for the title, author, subject, ISBN, and other details. When a student registers for a new account on a learning platform, she fills in a form asking for her name, email address, student number, and password.

Forms are important for two reasons. First, they make data entry easier by presenting one record's worth of fields in a readable, organized layout. Second, they support data quality by validating input before it reaches the database. A well-designed form will not allow a user to submit a date in an unrecognized format, leave a required field empty, or enter a value outside the allowed range.

Reports

A report is an organized presentation of data retrieved from a database, usually formatted for reading, printing, or sharing. Reports summarize, filter, sort, and present data in ways that help readers understand patterns, check details, or make decisions.

A school might produce a report that shows all students enrolled in each course, sorted alphabetically. A hospital might produce a report showing all patients scheduled for appointments in the next seven days, with the appointment time, the doctor's name, and the room number. A library might produce a report showing all overdue items, with the user's name, the title, the due date, and the number of days overdue.

Dashboards

A dashboard is a visual display that shows current conditions, key metrics, or important summaries, usually in real time or near real time. Dashboards are designed for quick reading rather than detailed analysis. They use numbers, charts, and indicators to show the most important information at a glance.

A school administrator might use a dashboard that shows total enrollment this term compared to the previous term, the number of students with outstanding holds, and the number of courses at capacity. A library director might use a dashboard that shows how many items are currently on loan, how many users registered this month, and which subject areas have the highest circulation numbers. Dashboards are connected to the database, and the numbers and charts they show are generated from queries.

1.8

Benefits, Risks, and Responsibilities

Databases and information systems are powerful tools, but power comes with consequences. Studying data is not only a technical subject. It is also a subject with ethical dimensions, legal implications, and human responsibilities.

Benefits

The benefits of well-designed information systems are substantial. They allow organizations to serve more people more efficiently. They make information accessible to those who need it, when they need it, without requiring manual searching through paper files. They enable organizations to see patterns and trends that would be invisible in unorganized data. They support consistency, so that every user's information is treated the same way by the same rules. They protect organizations and the people they serve by maintaining accurate records.

Risks

The risks of poorly designed or poorly managed data systems are equally substantial. A database that contains inaccurate data about a patient's medication can contribute to a dangerous medical error. A system that stores sensitive personal information without adequate security can expose that information to theft or misuse. A database that lacks proper backups can lose years of records in a single hardware failure.

Beyond technical failures, data systems also carry risks of bias and unfairness. When data is collected selectively, or when systems are designed without considering the populations they affect, the information those systems produce can reflect and reinforce existing inequalities. A hiring system that scores applicants based on historical data may systematically disadvantage groups that were underrepresented in the past. These risks are not hypothetical. They have occurred and continue to occur in real systems around the world.

Responsibilities

Everyone who works with data carries responsibilities. These responsibilities do not fall only on database administrators or software engineers. They fall on anyone who designs a system, enters data, uses data to make decisions, sets policies about who can access data, or communicates data to others.

Accuracy

Build validation rules, flag errors promptly, and make data as accurate as possible at every point of entry and maintenance.

Privacy

Protect data from unauthorized access and collect only the data that is genuinely needed for the purpose at hand.

Fairness

Consider whether decisions based on data are fair, whether the data is representative, and whether the system produces consistent results across groups.

Transparency

Represent data honestly, acknowledge uncertainty, and avoid presenting summaries that mislead even if the underlying numbers are technically correct.

1.9

How to Study Data and Database Systems Successfully

This textbook covers a wide range of topics, from raw data representation to SQL querying to information system design to data ethics. Learning all of it well requires good habits from the beginning.

  • Read Tables and Diagrams Carefully

    When you encounter a sample table, read every column header and every row. Ask yourself what each field represents, what data type it stores, and whether the values make sense. Database learning is highly visual. Students who read diagrams carefully advance much faster than those who skip them.

  • Read Queries Before Writing Queries

    SQL chapters always introduce queries for reading before asking you to write them. When you encounter a SQL example, read through each clause and ask yourself what that clause does and what the result table should look like. Predict the result before it is shown. Check your prediction.

  • Connect New Ideas to Real Examples

    Every concept in this textbook applies to real systems. When you learn about primary keys, think about the student number, the library card number, the patient record number. When you learn about transactions, think about what happens if a payment and a delivery record must both succeed or both fail together.

  • Practice Data Quality Thinking

    When you see a dataset, ask: Are there missing values? Could there be duplicate records? Are the values in each field consistent with what the field is supposed to store? Are there outliers that seem unlikely to be correct? Good database professionals are always alert to data that does not look right.

  • Understand Design Before Implementation

    Resist the temptation to jump straight to SQL without spending enough time on design. The design chapters come before the SQL chapters for good reason. A database that is poorly designed will produce problems that no amount of clever querying can fix.

  • Reflect on Consequences

    Throughout this course, ask: Who is affected by this system? What happens if this data is wrong? Who can see this information, and who should not? These questions are not separate from the technical work. They are part of it. The most effective data professionals are thoughtful about what their work means for the people their systems touch.

Key Terms
Data

Raw recorded facts, observations, measurements, or values that have been captured and stored. Data becomes meaningful when it is organized, contextualized, and interpreted.

Database

An organized collection of data stored so that it can be accessed, managed, and updated efficiently. A database enforces structure, supports queries, controls access, and maintains consistency.

Database Management System (DBMS)

The software that controls how data is stored, indexed, retrieved, and protected within a database. Examples include SQLite, PostgreSQL, MySQL, and Microsoft SQL Server.

Information System

An organized combination of people, processes, data, software, and hardware that works together to collect, store, process, and distribute information in support of organizational goals.

Table

The central organizing structure of a relational database. A table stores data about one kind of thing in rows (records) and columns (fields).

Record

A single row in a table, representing one instance of the thing the table describes.

Field

A single column in a table, representing one kind of information stored for every record in the table. Also called an attribute.

Relationship

A connection between two tables, established through shared identifier values. Relationships allow data from multiple tables to be combined in queries without duplicating the data itself.

Query

A formal instruction written in a query language (usually SQL) that asks a database to retrieve, filter, sort, summarize, or modify data.

SQL (Structured Query Language)

The standard language used to query and manage relational databases.

Form

An interface that allows a user to enter or edit data in a database, usually including validation rules that protect data quality.

Report

An organized presentation of data retrieved from a database, formatted for reading, printing, or sharing.

Dashboard

A visual display that shows current conditions, key metrics, or important summaries, usually generated from database queries and updated automatically.

Constraint

A rule that a database enforces automatically to protect the validity and consistency of its data.

Relational Database

A database that organizes data into tables connected by relationships, using shared identifiers to link records across tables.

Situations
  • A public library allows users to search for books by title, author, or subject, place holds on items, and renew borrowed items from a website.

Common Misconceptions
Misconception 1
A database is just a spreadsheet.

A spreadsheet stores data in rows and columns, but it lacks most of what makes a database powerful: relational connections between tables, enforced rules and constraints, multi-user access with concurrency control, built-in security and permissions, query languages, and robust backup and recovery. Chapter 23 examines the important differences in detail.

Misconception 2
Data automatically speaks for itself.

Data does not automatically tell you what it means. The same number can be a temperature, an age, a distance, a count, or a code, depending on context. A table of data without column headers, without documentation, and without knowledge of how it was collected cannot be safely interpreted. Chapter 2 addresses this misconception directly.

Misconception 3
A database is the whole information system.

In reality, the database is the storage and retrieval engine. The information system also includes the people who use it, the forms they enter data through, the validation rules that protect quality, the reports and dashboards that present results, the workflows that govern processes, the permissions that control access, and the policies that define responsibilities. Chapters 19 through 22 expand on this substantially.

Misconception 4
SQL is the only thing to learn.

SQL is an important and powerful skill, and this textbook teaches it seriously across five chapters. But SQL is only part of the picture. You also need to understand how to design a database worth querying (Part II), how to manage and protect data over time (Part IV), how databases fit into larger systems (Part V), how they connect to other tools and platforms (Part VI), and what ethical and governance responsibilities come with working with data (Part VII). SQL without design thinking produces poorly structured systems.

Misconception 5
Good software compensates for bad data.

No amount of good software can fix bad data. If the data stored in a database is inaccurate, incomplete, duplicated, or inconsistent, the results of every query, every report, and every dashboard will be wrong, no matter how well the queries are written. Data quality is a precondition for everything else the system tries to do.

How does information become knowledge? How does knowledge support decisions? And when can data mislead even careful readers?

Chapter 2 will also introduce the idea of uncertainty in data: the fact that data is always a partial and imperfect record of reality, collected under particular conditions by particular people for particular purposes. Understanding this does not mean distrusting all data. It means becoming a more careful and effective user of it. The habits of interpretation you develop in Chapter 2 will support everything that follows, from evaluating the accuracy of a dataset in Chapter 4 to reading a query result critically in Chapter 12 to thinking about the ethics of a data-driven decision in Chapter 27.

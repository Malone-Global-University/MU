Business Processes and Workflow

No database sits by itself. Every database that matters to an organization exists inside a larger system of repeated activity, where people follow steps, wait for approvals, hand work from one role to the next, and leave records of what happened and when. This chapter connects the tables and queries you have already learned to the real workflows they are built to support.

Every database that matters to an organization exists inside a larger system of repeated activity. People do not simply open a database, type values, and walk away. They follow steps, they wait for approvals, they hand work from one person or role to the next, they receive notifications, check statuses, handle exceptions, and leave records of what happened and when.

This chapter introduces the idea of a business process: a repeated sequence of steps that an organization follows to accomplish a goal. It connects business processes to the information systems that support them, and it shows how databases store the data that makes those processes trackable, searchable, auditable, and improvable.

By the end of this chapter, you should understand what a workflow is, how status fields track the progress of a process through its stages, how approvals and handoffs move work from one role to another, how notifications and reminders keep things moving, how bottlenecks and exceptions arise, what audit trails are and why they matter, and how process improvement begins with careful observation and measurement. You should also be able to read a simple workflow diagram and identify the tables, fields, and queries that would support a described process.

Note for New Students

This chapter does not introduce new SQL clauses. It teaches you to think about the data you already know how to store and query, and to ask a new question: what process is this data actually supporting? That question will sharpen every design decision you make from here forward.

21.1

What a Business Process Is

The word "business" in "business process" can be misleading. It suggests that only commercial organizations with customers, revenue, and profit have processes worth studying. In fact, any organization that does anything repeatedly has business processes. Schools enroll students, assign instructors, record grades, and issue transcripts. Libraries catalog resources, manage checkouts, send overdue notices, and process returned items. Hospitals admit patients, schedule procedures, record diagnoses, generate bills, and file insurance claims. Government offices accept applications, review documentation, make decisions, send notifications, and maintain records. Research labs collect samples, run experiments, record results, and archive data.

A business process is a repeated, structured sequence of activities that an organization performs to accomplish a defined goal. The key properties are that it is repeated, meaning it happens more than once, often many times per day, that it is structured, meaning there is a recognized order or pattern to the steps, and that it has a goal, meaning it produces some meaningful output or result.

Business processes typically involve multiple people or roles, multiple steps, multiple decisions, and multiple records. The student enrollment process, for example, involves the student submitting an application, the institution verifying eligibility, the enrollment system checking course capacity, an advisor or system approving the enrollment, the registration being recorded, a confirmation being sent, and the enrollment appearing on the student's schedule and the instructor's roster. That is not one action. It is a process with many connected steps, each of which depends on the previous ones.

Central Idea

Information systems exist largely to support business processes. The database stores the data that each step generates, checks, or requires. The interface provides the forms and screens that people use to move through the steps. The queries retrieve the right records at the right moment. The reports summarize what is happening across many process instances at once.

When students understand business processes, they understand why databases are designed the way they are, and why so many design decisions that seemed arbitrary actually reflect the structure of real organizational work.

21.2

Workflow Steps

A workflow is the specific sequence of steps that a business process follows from its beginning to its end. Every workflow has a starting point, a series of intermediate steps or stages, and an ending point or final state.

Consider the process of a student submitting an assignment and receiving a grade. The workflow might look like this:

  1. Student submits an assignment through the course system.
  2. The system records the submission with a timestamp.
  3. The instructor is notified that a submission is waiting.
  4. The instructor reviews the submission.
  5. The instructor enters a score and written feedback.
  6. The system records the grade and marks the submission as graded.
  7. The student is notified that feedback is available.
  8. The student views the grade and feedback.

Each of those steps is a distinct action performed by a specific actor, at a specific point in the sequence, that produces a specific record or state change. The database must store enough information to support each step: the submission itself, the timestamp, the current status, the instructor's grade and feedback, and the notification history.

Workflows can be simple or complex. A simple workflow has a linear sequence where every instance follows the same steps in the same order. A complex workflow has branches, decisions, parallel paths, loops, and exceptions. Most real organizational workflows are somewhere in between. They have a main path and some branching points where different things happen depending on conditions.

Questions to Ask

When designing a database to support a workflow, it helps to list all the steps explicitly and ask, for each step: What data does this step need to read? What data does this step produce or change? Who performs this step? How does the system know this step is complete? What happens next?

Those questions reveal the tables, fields, relationships, and constraints that the database must contain.

21.3

Status Tracking

One of the most important patterns in workflow-supporting databases is the status field. A status field records what stage a process instance is currently in. It answers the question: where is this item in its journey through the workflow?

Consider a library loan. A resource can be:

  • Available
  • On hold
  • Checked out
  • Overdue
  • Returned
  • Lost
  • Under repair

Each of those values is a status. The current status of a loan record tells the library what has happened so far and what should happen next. If a resource is checked out and its due date has passed, the status should update to overdue and a notice should be sent. If the resource is returned, the status should update to returned and the next hold, if any, should be activated.

Status fields should be designed carefully. They should use a controlled vocabulary, meaning a fixed set of allowed values, rather than free text. Free text status fields quickly become inconsistent: one staff member types "Returned," another types "returned," another types "Ret.," and another types "Item has been returned to the shelf." None of those four values is equivalent in a query, even though they all mean the same thing. Using a controlled set of allowed values, enforced by a constraint or a lookup table, keeps the data clean and queryable.

A simple status field in a loans table might look like this:

Loans
------
loan_id        INTEGER   PRIMARY KEY
resource_id    INTEGER   FOREIGN KEY references Resources(resource_id)
user_id        INTEGER   FOREIGN KEY references Users(user_id)
checkout_date  DATE
due_date       DATE
return_date    DATE
status         TEXT      CHECK (status IN ('checked_out', 'overdue', 'returned', 'lost'))

With this design, a query to find all overdue loans is straightforward:

SQL — Find Overdue Loans
SELECT loan_id, resource_id, user_id, due_date
FROM Loans
WHERE status = 'overdue';

And a query to update a returned loan is equally clear:

SQL — Record a Return
UPDATE Loans
SET status = 'returned', return_date = '2025-09-14'
WHERE loan_id = 4471;

Status tracking also allows reporting. An administrator can ask: how many loans are currently checked out? How many are overdue? What percentage of loans are returned on time? Those questions require clean, consistent status data to answer meaningfully.

21.4

Approvals and Handoffs

Many workflows require one person or role to complete a step before another person or role can proceed. This is called a handoff. Handoffs move work from one actor to the next in the sequence.

Some handoffs require an explicit approval: a decision by an authorized person that the previous step was completed correctly and that the next step may proceed. An application cannot advance to the review stage until the applicant has submitted all required documents. A purchase order cannot be fulfilled until it has been approved by a manager. A course cannot be offered until the curriculum committee has reviewed and approved the proposal.

Approvals introduce a new kind of data: decision records. When someone approves or rejects a request, the system should record who made the decision, when they made it, and sometimes why. This creates accountability and makes it possible to audit decisions later.

An approval record might look like this:

Approvals
-----------
approval_id     INTEGER   PRIMARY KEY
request_id      INTEGER   FOREIGN KEY references Requests(request_id)
reviewer_id     INTEGER   FOREIGN KEY references Users(user_id)
decision        TEXT      CHECK (decision IN ('approved', 'rejected', 'returned'))
decision_date   DATE
notes           TEXT

The decision field records the outcome. The reviewer_id records who made it. The decision_date records when. The notes field allows the reviewer to explain a rejection or request additional information.

Handoffs also raise questions about visibility and permissions. When work is handed from one role to the next, the new actor may need to see records that were not previously visible to them. A library circulation desk staff member might need to see patron loan records that are ordinarily restricted. An instructor grading an assignment might need to see student submission details that are otherwise private. Information systems must manage not only what the current status is, but also who can see and act on records at each stage.

Design Reminder

Designing for approvals and handoffs means thinking through the complete cast of actors in a workflow, the permissions each actor has at each stage, the decisions each actor can make, and what data is created or updated by each decision.

21.5

Notifications and Reminders

Workflows do not run on their own. People forget, deadlines pass, and steps that should happen automatically require someone to initiate them. Information systems reduce the cost of staying on top of a complex workflow by generating notifications and reminders at the right moments.

A notification is a message sent to a user to inform them of something that has happened: your application has been received, your loan is due in three days, your request has been approved, your assignment has been graded. A reminder is a message sent to prompt action before a deadline passes: this task has been waiting for your review for four days, this resource is due back tomorrow, you have an unread notification.

Notifications are often generated by changes in status. When a loan moves from checked_out to overdue, the system should notify the patron. When an application moves from submitted to under_review, the system might notify the applicant. When a review decision is recorded, the system should notify whoever submitted the request.

From a database perspective, notifications are often stored as records, not just sent as emails. A notifications table might look like this:

Notifications
--------------
notification_id   INTEGER   PRIMARY KEY
user_id           INTEGER   FOREIGN KEY references Users(user_id)
message           TEXT
notification_type TEXT
sent_at           TIMESTAMP
read_at           TIMESTAMP
related_id        INTEGER
related_table     TEXT

Storing notifications allows the system to display a list of unread notifications to a user, track whether notifications have been seen, query notification history, and measure response times. The related_id and related_table fields allow the notification to link back to the specific record it refers to, such as a specific loan, application, or assignment.

Reminders require the system to check, usually on a schedule, whether any upcoming deadlines are approaching. A query to find loans that are due within two days might look like this:

SQL — Find Loans Due Soon
SELECT loan_id, user_id, resource_id, due_date
FROM Loans
WHERE status = 'checked_out'
  AND due_date <= DATE('now', '+2 days');

A system running this query each morning could automatically generate reminder notifications for all the matching patrons.

21.6

Bottlenecks

A bottleneck is a point in a workflow where work accumulates because it cannot move forward as fast as it arrives. Bottlenecks slow down the entire process, create backlogs, frustrate the people waiting, and reduce the organization's ability to serve its users.

Bottlenecks often occur at approval steps, at resource-constrained steps, or at steps that require a single person or role to handle a large volume of work. An instructor with a large class and a short grading window becomes a bottleneck in the assignment workflow. A single administrator responsible for reviewing all course registration exceptions becomes a bottleneck in the enrollment workflow.

Databases and information systems help identify bottlenecks by making the work visible. If the system stores timestamps for each stage of the process, it becomes possible to measure how long work sits at each step. A query to find the average time applications spend in each status might look like this:

SQL — Average Time Waiting by Status
SELECT status, AVG(JULIANDAY('now') - JULIANDAY(entered_at)) AS avg_days_waiting
FROM ApplicationHistory
GROUP BY status
ORDER BY avg_days_waiting DESC;

If that query shows that applications sit in under_review for an average of eleven days but in every other status for less than two days, the review stage is likely a bottleneck.

Making bottlenecks visible is the first step toward addressing them. The solution might be adding more reviewers, simplifying the review process, automating part of the review, or changing the policy that requires review in the first place. The database does not solve bottlenecks on its own, but it provides the evidence that reveals them and the data that measures whether solutions are working.

21.7

Exceptions and Special Cases

No workflow covers every possible situation perfectly. Real organizational work always involves exceptions: cases that do not fit the normal path, require special handling, or trigger a deviation from the standard procedure.

A student who is enrolled in a course but then becomes ill and needs a late withdrawal is an exception. A library resource that is reported damaged at return requires a different process than a clean return. A purchase order that arrives without a matching authorization requires manual review rather than automatic fulfillment. A patient with multiple overlapping diagnoses may not fit neatly into the standard treatment protocol.

Exceptions are expensive. They require human judgment, interrupt normal workflow, take more time, and are often handled inconsistently because they happen rarely, and the person handling them may not remember exactly what the correct procedure is. Well-designed information systems try to reduce the cost of exceptions in several ways.

First, the system can flag records that appear to be exceptions so they are routed to the appropriate handler rather than silently stalling. A loan that has been renewed three times and is being requested for a fourth renewal might be flagged for librarian review rather than automatically approved or rejected.

Second, the system can store the exception and its resolution, creating a history that helps handle similar cases consistently in the future. An exception log table might look like this:

ExceptionLog
--------------
exception_id    INTEGER   PRIMARY KEY
process_name    TEXT
record_id       INTEGER
exception_type  TEXT
description     TEXT
handler_id      INTEGER   FOREIGN KEY references Users(user_id)
resolution      TEXT
logged_at       TIMESTAMP
resolved_at     TIMESTAMP

Third, the system can provide clear guidance to the person handling the exception, either through documentation embedded in the interface or through a decision tree that walks them through the correct steps.

Design Principle

Designing for exceptions means accepting that they will happen, building the data model to accommodate them, and creating processes that handle them gracefully rather than treating them as failures.

21.8

Audit Trails

An audit trail is a record of what happened to data, who changed it, and when. Audit trails answer the question: how did this record come to be in its current state?

Audit trails matter for accountability, error investigation, compliance, and trust. When a patient's record is updated, the hospital needs to know who made the change and why. When an exam grade is revised, the institution needs a record of the original grade, the revised grade, and the authorization for the change. When a financial transaction is recorded, the organization needs to be able to reconstruct the sequence of events that led to it.

Audit trails are implemented in different ways, depending on the system's needs.

A Simple Approach: Metadata Fields

The simplest approach is to add metadata fields to every table that record who created a record and when, and who last modified it and when:

Resources
----------
resource_id       INTEGER   PRIMARY KEY
title             TEXT
...
created_by        INTEGER   FOREIGN KEY references Users(user_id)
created_at        TIMESTAMP
last_modified_by  INTEGER   FOREIGN KEY references Users(user_id)
last_modified_at  TIMESTAMP

This approach records only the most recent change. It does not preserve the full history of modifications.

A More Complete Approach: A Separate Audit Log

A more complete approach uses a separate audit log table that records every change as a new row:

AuditLog
----------
log_id        INTEGER   PRIMARY KEY
table_name    TEXT
record_id     INTEGER
field_name    TEXT
old_value     TEXT
new_value     TEXT
changed_by    INTEGER   FOREIGN KEY references Users(user_id)
changed_at    TIMESTAMP
action        TEXT      CHECK (action IN ('INSERT', 'UPDATE', 'DELETE'))

With this design, every insert, update, and delete produces a new row in the audit log. The history of any record can be reconstructed by querying all the log entries for that record ID and table name.

A query to see the full history of changes to resource number 1042 might look like this:

SQL — Full History of a Record
SELECT log_id, field_name, old_value, new_value, changed_by, changed_at, action
FROM AuditLog
WHERE table_name = 'Resources'
  AND record_id = 1042
ORDER BY changed_at ASC;

Audit trails serve purposes beyond investigation. They also provide data for analysis. By examining the audit log, an administrator can see which records are changed most frequently, which users make the most modifications, and when the heaviest periods of activity occur.

Not Optional

The decision to implement an audit trail adds complexity and storage requirements to a system. But for any system that handles sensitive, consequential, or regulated data, the audit trail is not optional. It is a fundamental responsibility.

21.9

Process Improvement

Databases and information systems do not just record what is happening. They make it possible to measure what is happening, and measurement is the foundation of improvement.

Process improvement means identifying aspects of a workflow that are slow, inconsistent, error-prone, or unnecessarily expensive, and making changes that lead to better outcomes. Better outcomes might mean faster processing, fewer errors, lower cost, more consistent results, or greater user satisfaction.

The data that supports process improvement comes from the workflow itself. If the system records timestamps for each stage transition, the time spent in each stage can be measured. If the system records exceptions and their resolutions, the frequency and types of exceptions can be analyzed. If the system records error rates, duplicate records, rejected submissions, and returned items, the places where things go wrong can be identified.

Consider the following query, which measures how long course enrollment requests spend in each status stage:

SQL — Average Days Between Status Transitions
SELECT
    from_status,
    to_status,
    COUNT(*) AS transitions,
    AVG(JULIANDAY(transitioned_at) - JULIANDAY(previous_at)) AS avg_days
FROM EnrollmentStatusHistory
GROUP BY from_status, to_status
ORDER BY avg_days DESC;

If this query shows that the transition from submitted to reviewed takes an average of nine days while every other transition takes less than one day, the organization knows exactly where to focus its improvement effort.

Process improvement is a continuous activity, not a one-time event. An organization that collects good process data can run this kind of analysis regularly, test whether changes have the intended effect, and catch new problems as they emerge. The database provides the institutional memory that makes this possible.

Data Describes, Judgment Decides

It is important to remember that data describes what has happened, not what should have happened. Process improvement requires human judgment to interpret the data, identify root causes, design changes, and evaluate results. The database supports that work, but it does not substitute for it.

21.10

Modeling a Workflow

A workflow diagram is a visual representation of the steps in a process, the decisions that determine which path a process instance takes, and the actors responsible for each step. Workflow diagrams help communicate how a process works, identify missing steps or ambiguous decision points, and guide the design of the database and interface that will support the process.

A simple workflow diagram for a library checkout process might show:

Workflow Diagram — Library Checkout and Return
[Patron requests resource]
        |
        v
[System checks availability]
        |
     Available? --No--> [Place on hold] --> [Notify patron when available]
        |
       Yes
        |
        v
[Record checkout: patron, resource, date, due date]
        |
        v
[Send confirmation to patron]
        |
        v
[Set status: checked_out]
        |
       ...
        |
[Patron returns resource]
        |
        v
[Staff checks condition]
        |
     Damaged? --Yes--> [Record damage, flag for review]
        |
        No
        |
        v
[Record return: return date]
        |
        v
[Set status: returned]
        |
        v
[Check for holds on this resource]
        |
    Hold exists? --Yes--> [Notify next patron on hold list]
        |
        No
        |
        v
[Set status: available]

This diagram is not a formal notation. It is a thinking tool. It helps identify the data the system must store, such as availability, checkout date, due date, condition, and hold list, the status transitions the system must support, the actors involved, including the patron, the staff member, and the automated notification system, and the decision points that require branching logic.

Questions for Modeling a Workflow

What is the first thing that happens? What are all the possible paths from beginning to end? Who is responsible for each step? What data does each step need? What data does each step produce? What decisions must be made, and who makes them? What happens when something goes wrong? How does the organization know the process is complete? What records must be kept for accountability or compliance?

The answers to those questions become the requirements for the database and the information system that supports it.

Chapter Summary
  • Business processes are the repeated, structured sequences of activities that organizations use to accomplish their goals. Every meaningful database exists inside a larger system of process activity: enrollments, applications, loans, orders, approvals, reviews, grades, registrations, and records.
  • Workflow steps are the discrete actions in a process, each performed by a specific actor, that produce a specific record or state change.
  • Status fields track where a process instance currently sits in its journey from start to finish, and should use a controlled vocabulary rather than free text.
  • Approvals and handoffs move work from one role to the next and generate decision records that create accountability.
  • Notifications and reminders help people and systems stay on top of deadlines and pending actions, and are often stored as records, not just sent as messages.
  • Bottlenecks are accumulation points where work moves slower than it arrives, and databases can reveal them through timestamp analysis.
  • Exceptions are cases that do not fit the normal path and require special handling, documentation, and consistency mechanisms.
  • Audit trails are records of what changed, who changed it, and when, providing the historical evidence needed for accountability, investigation, and compliance.
  • Process improvement uses the data that workflows generate to measure performance, identify problems, test solutions, and build institutional knowledge.
  • Workflow diagrams are thinking tools that communicate how a process works, guide database design, and expose missing steps and ambiguous decisions.

A database designed without thinking about process is a container. A database designed with the workflow in mind is a tool.

Key Terms
Business Process

A repeated, structured sequence of activities that an organization performs to accomplish a defined goal.

Workflow

The specific sequence of steps that a business process follows from its beginning to its end, including the actors, decisions, data, and state transitions at each step.

Status Field

A field in a database record that records what stage of a workflow the record is currently in, using a controlled vocabulary of allowed values.

Controlled Vocabulary

A fixed, enforced set of allowed values for a field, such as a status field. Using a controlled vocabulary prevents inconsistency and makes data queryable.

Handoff

The point in a workflow where responsibility for a task or record moves from one actor or role to the next.

Notification

A message generated by a system to inform a user of something that has happened, such as a status change or a decision.

Reminder

A message generated by a system to prompt a user to take action before a deadline or pending task is missed.

Bottleneck

A point in a workflow where work accumulates because it cannot proceed as fast as it arrives. Bottlenecks slow the entire process and are often revealed by measuring how long records spend in each status.

Exception

A case that does not follow the normal path through a workflow and requires special handling, human judgment, or deviation from standard procedure.

Audit Trail

A record of changes made to data, including who made each change, when it was made, and what the previous value was. Audit trails support accountability, error investigation, and compliance.

Process Improvement

The ongoing practice of identifying and addressing aspects of a workflow that are slow, inconsistent, error-prone, or unnecessarily costly, using data collected during normal workflow operation.

Workflow Diagram

A visual representation of the steps, decisions, actors, and paths in a workflow, used to communicate, analyze, and guide the design of supporting systems.

Worked Example: Designing Tables for a Workflow
Problem

A small college uses a paper-based process for students to request course enrollment exceptions. Students fill out a form, submit it to a departmental administrator, the administrator reviews it, routes it to the instructor for a signature, and then forwards it to the registrar for final approval and processing. The college wants to digitize this process.

  1. Identify the actors.

    Student (requestor), departmental administrator (initial reviewer), instructor (signature step), and registrar (final approver and processor).

  2. Identify the stages.

    Submitted by student, under review by departmental administrator, pending instructor signature, pending registrar approval, approved and processed, or rejected.

  3. Identify the data each stage needs.

    Student identity, course requested, reason for exception, review notes from the administrator, instructor decision and signature date, registrar decision and process date, current status at any moment, and a full history of status changes.

Step 4: Design the Tables
SQL — Workflow Table Design
CREATE TABLE EnrollmentExceptionRequests (
    request_id      INTEGER PRIMARY KEY,
    student_id      INTEGER NOT NULL REFERENCES Students(student_id),
    course_id       INTEGER NOT NULL REFERENCES Courses(course_id),
    term_id         INTEGER NOT NULL REFERENCES Terms(term_id),
    reason          TEXT NOT NULL,
    status          TEXT NOT NULL DEFAULT 'submitted'
                    CHECK (status IN (
                        'submitted',
                        'under_review',
                        'pending_instructor',
                        'pending_registrar',
                        'approved',
                        'rejected'
                    )),
    submitted_at    TIMESTAMP NOT NULL DEFAULT CURRENT_TIMESTAMP,
    resolved_at     TIMESTAMP
);

CREATE TABLE ExceptionRequestHistory (
    history_id      INTEGER PRIMARY KEY,
    request_id      INTEGER NOT NULL REFERENCES EnrollmentExceptionRequests(request_id),
    from_status     TEXT,
    to_status       TEXT NOT NULL,
    actor_id        INTEGER REFERENCES Users(user_id),
    notes           TEXT,
    transitioned_at TIMESTAMP NOT NULL DEFAULT CURRENT_TIMESTAMP
);
Step 5: Write Queries to Support the Workflow

All requests currently waiting for administrator review:

SQL
SELECT r.request_id, s.full_name AS student, c.title AS course, r.submitted_at
FROM EnrollmentExceptionRequests r
JOIN Students s ON r.student_id = s.student_id
JOIN Courses c ON r.course_id = c.course_id
WHERE r.status = 'under_review'
ORDER BY r.submitted_at ASC;

Average time from submission to resolution for the current term:

SQL
SELECT AVG(JULIANDAY(resolved_at) - JULIANDAY(submitted_at)) AS avg_days_to_resolve
FROM EnrollmentExceptionRequests
WHERE resolved_at IS NOT NULL
  AND term_id = 14;

Full history of a specific request:

SQL
SELECT h.from_status, h.to_status, u.full_name AS actor, h.notes, h.transitioned_at
FROM ExceptionRequestHistory h
LEFT JOIN Users u ON h.actor_id = u.user_id
WHERE h.request_id = 287
ORDER BY h.transitioned_at ASC;
Practice Activities
Activity 21-A — Workflow Diagram Practice: Read the Workflow

A public library uses the following process when a patron reports a missing item from their account. Read the description and answer the questions below.

  1. The patron contacts the library and reports that a loan record on their account is for an item they never received.
  2. A circulation staff member reviews the loan record and checks the physical location of the item.
  3. If the item is found on the shelf, the staff member closes the report and updates the loan record.
  4. If the item is not found, the report is escalated to a supervisor.
  5. The supervisor investigates: checks the last return scan, reviews any security camera timestamps if available, and interviews staff.
  6. If the supervisor determines the item was never properly checked out, the loan is voided and a note is added to the patron's record.
  7. If the supervisor determines the item is genuinely lost, the patron is notified and a replacement cost is assessed.
  8. If the patron disputes the assessment, the case is escalated to the library director.
  9. The director makes a final decision and documents the outcome.
Questions
  • List all the actors in this workflow.
  • List all the status values a report in this workflow might have.
  • What data would a report record need to store to support this workflow?
  • At which step is there a bottleneck risk, and why?
  • What should the audit trail record at each step?

Common Errors

The following are mistakes that students often make when designing workflow-supporting databases. Study each one and the explanation of why it is a problem.

Error 1 — Using Free Text for Status Fields
-- Problematic design
status TEXT  -- Allows 'returned', 'Returned', 'RETURNED', 'item returned', 'ret.'

This makes filtering unreliable. Use a CHECK constraint with a fixed list of allowed values.

Error 2 — Only Storing the Current Status, Not the History

If you only store the current status, you cannot answer questions like: How long has this been in this state? Who moved it to this state? What was it before? Always consider whether you need a separate history table.

Error 3 — Recording Decisions Without Recording the Decision-Maker
-- Missing accountability
decision TEXT,
decision_date DATE
-- No record of who made the decision

Always record which user made an approval, rejection, or modification. Without this, audit trails are incomplete.

Error 4 — Treating Exceptions as Errors

Designing a system as if every instance will follow the normal path perfectly means the system will be fragile when exceptions inevitably arise. Design for exceptions explicitly, with an exception log and clear routing rules.

Error 5 — Not Indexing Status Fields That Are Frequently Filtered

If your application constantly queries records by status, such as all overdue loans, all pending approvals, or all submitted requests, the status field should be indexed for performance. Unindexed status fields on large tables cause slow queries.

Data Ethics, Privacy, Governance, and Risk

Every table, query, permission setting, and backup plan exists inside a broader world of human lives, organizational responsibilities, and social consequences. This chapter teaches students to think beyond the technical mechanics of databases and consider who is affected by the systems they build, what can go wrong, and what responsible data practice requires.

27.10Risk Management and Responsible Practice

Every database is built by people, used by people, and affects people. This is easy to overlook when you are writing SQL queries, designing schemas, or thinking about index performance. The technical work is absorbing. The logic is clean and testable. A query either returns the right rows or it does not. A primary key either prevents duplicates or it does not.

But behind every table row is a real situation in the world. A row in a students table represents a person attending a school. A row in a medical_appointments table represents a person seeking care. A row in a loan_applications table represents a person trying to borrow money. A row in a user_accounts table represents a person who trusted a company with their email address, password, and possibly their home address, payment information, and browsing history.

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to explain why data privacy matters and what consent requires, classify sensitive data and apply appropriate protections, apply data minimization and retention principles to real system designs, recognize bias and fairness problems in data collection and use, understand transparency and explainability as values in data-driven decisions, describe what data governance means and why organizations need it, identify common legal and regulatory frameworks at an introductory level, and practice basic risk identification and responsible data design.

A Different Kind of Chapter

This chapter does not require advanced statistics, legal expertise, or organizational management experience. It requires careful thinking, honest reasoning, and the willingness to consider the human consequences of technical choices. Responsible data work is not an optional topic added at the end of a database course. It is woven into every design decision from the beginning.

27.1

Data Systems Have Consequences

When data systems work well, they serve people efficiently and fairly. Records are accurate. Queries return correct information. Permissions are set correctly so that only authorized people can see sensitive data. Backups protect records against loss. Reports give decision-makers accurate information.

When data systems work badly, the consequences are not just technical. Inaccurate records can deny someone a job, a loan, or medical care. Poorly secured databases can expose personal information to criminals or hostile actors. Biased data can cause automated systems to treat some people unfairly. Retained data that should have been deleted can be used to harm people years later. Systems with no audit trail allow abuse without accountability.

This Is Not a Theoretical Concern

Large-scale data breaches, discriminatory algorithmic decisions, unauthorized surveillance, and accidental exposure of sensitive records are documented and recurring events. They happen in healthcare systems, financial institutions, social media platforms, government databases, educational institutions, and retail companies. The engineer who designed the database, the analyst who ran the query, the administrator who set the permissions, and the manager who authorized the system all share responsibility for what that system does.

Shared Responsibility

Responsibility for data systems does not fall only on database administrators or software engineers. It falls on anyone who designs a system, enters data, uses data to make decisions, sets policies about who can access data, or communicates data to others. The most effective data professionals are thoughtful about what their work means for the people their systems touch.

This chapter introduces the principles and practices that make data work responsible. Some of these principles are ethical, rooted in how people ought to be treated. Some are organizational, rooted in how institutions need to manage information over time. Some are legal, rooted in the requirements that governments and regulators have established. All of them are practical: they apply to real decisions made during the design, development, and operation of real systems.

27.2

Privacy and Consent

Privacy is the principle that people have a right to control information about themselves. This includes the right to decide who collects information about them, what is collected, how it is used, how long it is kept, and who can access it.

Privacy is not simply about secrecy. A person might share their home address with a delivery company because they want a package delivered. That does not mean they consent to having their address sold to other companies, used to send unwanted advertising, or retained indefinitely in a database they cannot access.

What Genuine Consent Requires

Consent means that people knowingly and voluntarily agree to the collection and use of their personal data. Genuine consent has four properties.

Informed

The person understands what data is being collected, why it is being collected, how it will be used, who will have access to it, and how long it will be kept. Consent buried in pages of legal language that most people will never read is, at best, formal rather than genuine.

Specific

Agreeing to one use of data does not imply agreement to all uses. A user who consents to their purchase history being used to personalize recommendations has not necessarily consented to that history being shared with third-party advertisers, employers, or insurance companies.

Revocable

People should be able to withdraw consent and request deletion of their data. A person who closes an account should be able to have their personal records removed, not retained indefinitely.

Freely Given

People should not be coerced or misled into giving consent. A service that requires users to accept all data collection as a condition of use at all offers a weaker form of consent than one that provides genuine choice.

Privacy as a Design Principle

For database designers and information systems professionals, privacy and consent translate directly into design decisions. These include building systems that collect only what is necessary, storing sensitive data securely, making it possible to delete records when consent is withdrawn, keeping records of what consents have been given and when, and designing interfaces that explain data practices clearly rather than obscuring them.

Privacy is not a feature added to a finished system. It must be considered when the first table is designed, when the first form is sketched, and when the first permission rule is written.

27.3

Sensitive Data

Not all data carries the same risk. A person's favorite color is personal but not particularly sensitive. Their medical diagnosis, bank account number, immigration status, mental health history, sexual orientation, religious beliefs, or home address carries much higher risk if exposed or misused.

Sensitive data is information that, if disclosed, could cause meaningful harm to a person. This includes harm from discrimination, financial loss, physical danger, social stigma, legal consequences, identity theft, or damage to relationships and reputation.

Identifying Sensitivity During Design

A common database design mistake is treating all data as equally sensitive or equally unimportant. A students table might contain names and student ID numbers, which are relatively low risk, alongside academic records, accommodation needs, financial aid status, and disciplinary history, all of which require substantially more protection.

Sensitive data requires special handling. This includes stronger access controls, encryption at rest and in transit, more restrictive permissions, tighter audit logging, shorter retention periods, and careful attention to who can run which queries. Sensitive data should be identified during the design phase, not discovered after a breach.

27.4

Data Minimization

Data minimization is the practice of collecting only the data that is actually necessary for a stated purpose, and not collecting additional data just because it might be convenient or interesting to have.

The temptation to collect more data is real. Storage is inexpensive. Data might be useful someday. Richer records enable richer analysis. But data that has been collected can be exposed, stolen, subpoenaed, sold, misused, or retained beyond its useful life. Data that was never collected cannot cause these problems.

Three Questions for Every Data Field

Data minimization asks three questions about every piece of data a system collects.

Is this data necessary for the system's stated purpose? A library checkout system needs to know who borrowed which book and when. It probably does not need to record the patron's age, employer, political interests, or how frequently they visit the building.

Is there a less sensitive way to accomplish the same goal? A system that needs to verify that a person is over eighteen years old needs confirmation of age, not necessarily the person's exact birthdate, ID number, or full legal name. Aggregate or anonymized information often serves analytical purposes without requiring records that identify individuals.

How long must this data be kept? Data that is no longer needed for its original purpose should be deleted. A company that retains customer records indefinitely creates more risk than one that deletes records after an appropriate retention period.

Minimization Is Not Restriction

Data minimization is not a barrier to building useful systems. It is a discipline that produces systems that are easier to secure, easier to maintain, and less risky to operate. In practical database design, it means being deliberate about which columns belong in a table, considering whether derived or aggregated data can substitute for raw individual records, and avoiding the habit of adding columns to a table because they might be useful in the future.

27.5

Retention and Deletion

Data that is kept longer than necessary creates risk without proportionate benefit. Old records can be exposed in breaches, discovered in legal proceedings, used for purposes that were never consented to, or subject to regulations that require their deletion.

Retention refers to how long a system keeps records after they are created or last used. Deletion refers to the process of permanently removing records when they are no longer needed. A retention policy is a documented rule that specifies how long different types of data are kept and when they must be deleted or anonymized. Retention policies are not only an ethical good. In many industries and jurisdictions they are a legal requirement.

Retention Policies Vary by Data Type

Financial transaction records may need to be kept for several years to satisfy audit and tax requirements. Access logs may need to be kept for a shorter period sufficient for security investigation purposes. A user's personal profile data may need to be deleted promptly when the user requests account closure. Designing for these differences requires knowing the relevant requirements before the database is built.

Designing for Deletion

Designing for deletion is harder than it sounds. In relational databases with foreign key constraints, deleting a record may cascade to dependent records in other tables, or may be blocked if those constraints prevent deletion. A system that was designed without deletion in mind often makes deletion difficult or incomplete.

Anonymization is sometimes offered as an alternative to deletion. An anonymized record has had its identifying fields removed or transformed so that the individual can no longer be identified from it. Truly anonymous data may be retained for analytical purposes even after the original identifying record has been deleted. However, anonymization is harder to achieve completely than it appears, and supposedly anonymous records have been re-identified by combining them with other available data.

Students designing databases should ask during the design phase how old records will be managed, what the retention periods are for each data type, whether the schema and application can execute deletions reliably, and whether any audit requirements affect what must be kept and for how long.

27.6

Bias and Fairness

Data systems do not merely reflect the world. They often reinforce and amplify existing patterns in it, including patterns of discrimination and inequality. Bias in data means that a dataset systematically over-represents or under-represents certain groups, outcomes, or situations in ways that produce inaccurate or unfair results when that data is used for analysis or automated decision-making.

How Bias Enters Data Systems
Historical Bias

Data reflects past practices that were themselves discriminatory. An automated hiring system trained on historical decisions that favored certain groups will reproduce those patterns, even if it never explicitly references protected characteristics.

Collection Bias

The collection mechanism consistently favors certain groups. A health survey reaching only people with reliable internet access will under-represent populations with lower technology access, who may also have different health characteristics.

Measurement Bias

The methods used to measure or record a characteristic are less accurate for some groups than others. A facial recognition system trained primarily on lighter-skinned faces performs less accurately on darker-skinned faces.

Labeling Bias

Human judgments used to create training data or evaluate outcomes are themselves influenced by bias. Medical images labeled by physicians with implicit biases will carry that bias into any system trained on those labels.

Feedback Loop Bias

A biased system produces decisions that change the underlying data in ways that reinforce the original bias. Flagging certain neighborhoods for increased patrols generates more arrests there, producing more data confirming high crime rates, justifying more patrols.

Proxy Bias

A variable that appears neutral is actually correlated with a protected characteristic. Using ZIP codes as a proxy for creditworthiness can embed geographic and economic disparities that track closely with race or ethnicity.

Fairness

Fairness in data systems means that the system's outcomes are equitable across the groups it affects. There are several different formal definitions of fairness, and they cannot all be satisfied simultaneously in general. This is a genuine technical and ethical challenge, not merely a matter of good intentions.

For students at this level, the practical lesson is this: data is not objective simply because it is stored in a database. It was collected by people, labeled by people, cleaned by people, and interpreted by people. Any of those steps can introduce bias. A responsible data practitioner asks who collected this data, how it was collected, who might be over-represented or under-represented, what purposes it was used for before, and whether those purposes embedded assumptions that should not be carried forward.

27.7

Transparency and Explainability

Transparency means that the people affected by a data system can understand, at an appropriate level of detail, what data is being collected about them, how it is being used, and what decisions are being made on the basis of it.

Explainability is the related principle that decisions made using data, especially automated or algorithmic decisions, can be explained in terms that the affected person can understand. If a system denies someone a loan, rejects a job application, flags an account for fraud, or assigns a student to a lower academic track, the person affected deserves to know why. Transparency and explainability have become increasingly important as automated decision-making systems have become more powerful and more widely used. They are required by law in some jurisdictions for certain types of decisions.

Practical Dimensions of Transparency
Documentation

The system is described clearly, including what data it collects, how it processes that data, and what its outputs mean. Good documentation supports transparency to users and accountability within the organization.

Audit Trails

If a user's account is flagged, blocked, or scored in a way that affects them, it should be possible to review the data and logic that produced that outcome.

Legible Outputs

Reports, scores, recommendations, and classifications are described in terms that affected parties can understand, not just in terms that are technically precise but practically opaque.

Recourse Mechanisms

People who believe a decision affecting them was wrong have a way to challenge it, request review, and have errors corrected.

Opacity Is a Design Choice

A data system that makes important decisions while being opaque, unexplainable, and non-correctable is not merely technically flawed. It is a system that removes human agency from situations that matter. Choosing not to provide transparency is still a choice, and it carries ethical consequences.

27.8

Data Governance

Data governance is the set of policies, roles, responsibilities, processes, and standards that an organization uses to manage its data well over time. Governance answers questions like these: who is responsible for the accuracy of the student enrollment records? Who decides how long medical records are retained? Who can approve adding a new column to a table that includes personal information? Who is notified when a breach is detected?

Without governance, data systems tend toward entropy. Different parts of an organization maintain conflicting records. No one knows who last updated a particular field or whether the information in it is current. Access permissions drift over time as roles change and old accounts are never deactivated. Retention policies that exist on paper are not implemented in practice. When problems occur, no one is clearly responsible.

Components of Good Governance

Data ownership means that specific people or roles are designated as responsible for the accuracy, currency, and appropriate use of specific data assets.

Data stewardship means that people with day-to-day responsibility for managing data are held accountable for following policies and maintaining quality.

Policies and standards are documented rules that specify how data should be collected, stored, protected, used, shared, retained, and deleted.

Metadata management means that data about the data, such as what each field means, what values are valid, when a record was last updated, and who updated it, is maintained and accessible.

Change management means that changes to data structures, policies, or systems are reviewed, documented, and communicated rather than made informally.

Review and audit means that governance policies are periodically evaluated to check whether they are being followed and whether they are adequate for the current environment.

Beyond the Technical Layer

The technical work of building a database is only part of what makes a database system work well over time. The organizational, procedural, and human dimensions are equally important. A perfectly designed schema operated without governance will drift into inconsistency. A mediocre schema operated with strong governance and clear ownership will remain reliable and trustworthy.

27.9

Compliance at an Introductory Level

Many organizations that handle personal data operate under legal and regulatory requirements that govern how that data must be collected, stored, protected, used, and deleted. These requirements vary by industry, geography, and data type. Students are not expected to be legal experts, but they should be aware that compliance frameworks exist, understand their general purposes, and know enough to ask appropriate questions when working on real systems.

Examples of Compliance Frameworks

General data protection regulations exist in many jurisdictions to protect individuals' rights over their personal data. The European Union's General Data Protection Regulation, often called the GDPR, is among the most comprehensive and influential. It establishes rights for individuals including the right to access their data, the right to correction, the right to deletion under certain conditions, and the right to object to certain types of processing. It requires organizations to document their data practices, obtain appropriate consent, protect data against breaches, and report significant breaches to regulators.

Sector-specific regulations govern data practices in particular industries. In the United States, the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act, known as HIPAA, governs the handling of protected health information. The Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act, known as FERPA, governs educational records. The Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard, known as PCI DSS, governs how payment card data is handled.

Data breach notification laws exist in many jurisdictions and require organizations that suffer a breach affecting personal data to notify affected individuals and relevant regulators within specified timeframes.

Compliance Shapes Design

Compliance is not an afterthought. Systems handling regulated data must be designed from the beginning with the relevant requirements in mind. Access controls, encryption, audit logging, data minimization, retention policies, and breach detection capabilities are not optional features for regulated systems. They are required.

Compliance also creates documentation obligations. Organizations typically need to be able to demonstrate what data they hold, why they hold it, how they protect it, and who has accessed it. This is another reason why good governance and careful system documentation matter, and why both must begin at design time rather than after launch.

27.10

Risk Management and Responsible Practice

Risk management in the context of data systems means identifying the things that could go wrong, assessing how likely and how serious those problems would be, and taking steps to reduce their likelihood or mitigate their consequences. Risk is not something that can be eliminated entirely. Every system that stores and processes data carries some risk. But risk can be understood, reduced, and managed.

Common Risks in Data Systems

Data breach occurs when unauthorized parties gain access to protected data. Breaches can happen through external attacks, insider threats, misconfigured permissions, stolen credentials, or physical theft. The consequences can include financial harm to affected individuals, regulatory penalties, reputational damage to the organization, and legal liability.

Data loss occurs when records are destroyed, corrupted, or made inaccessible. Causes include hardware failure, software bugs, human error, ransomware attacks, and natural disasters. Good backup and recovery practices reduce but do not eliminate this risk.

Data inaccuracy occurs when the records in a database do not reflect reality correctly. Decisions based on inaccurate data can cause serious harm. Data quality practices, validation rules, and regular data audits reduce this risk.

Unauthorized use occurs when data collected for one purpose is used for a different purpose without appropriate consent or authorization. This can violate privacy, breach trust, and break legal requirements.

Bias-driven harm occurs when a data system produces unfair or discriminatory outcomes because of biased data or problematic design.

What Responsible Practice Looks Like

Responsible data practice is the integration of all the principles in this chapter into the actual work of building, operating, and maintaining data systems. It means designing with privacy in mind, not adding privacy protections later as patches. It means collecting only the data that is actually needed. It means treating sensitive data with appropriate care. It means setting permissions by least privilege. It means building systems that can execute deletions reliably when records must be removed. It means documenting what is built and why. It means maintaining audit trails. It means testing whether systems are fair. It means planning for backups and recovery. It means establishing clear governance.

None of these practices is particularly exotic. They require the same careful thinking, patient design, and attention to detail that good technical database work requires. The difference is that they also require holding in mind, throughout the work, the reality that the records in the database represent people, and that those people are affected by how their information is handled.

Central Idea

A responsible data practitioner approaches risk management not as a bureaucratic exercise but as a genuine attempt to protect the people the system affects. Ask, during design: what are the worst realistic failures of this system, who would be harmed by those failures, and what design choices and operational practices would reduce those risks?

Chapter Summary
  • Data systems have real consequences for real people. Inaccurate records, security failures, biased algorithms, and unauthorized data use can all cause serious harm. Everyone who builds or operates a data system shares responsibility for its effects.
  • Privacy is the right of individuals to control their own information. Consent is the mechanism through which that control is exercised, and it must be informed, specific, revocable, and freely given to be genuine.
  • Sensitive data, including health, financial, identity, location, authentication, communications, and political or social information, requires heightened protection and careful design. Sensitivity should be identified during the design phase, not after a breach.
  • Data minimization means collecting only what is necessary for the stated purpose. Retention policies specify how long data is kept and when it must be deleted or anonymized. Designing for deletion must happen at design time, not after launch.
  • Bias enters data systems through historical patterns, collection methods, measurement practices, labeling, feedback loops, and proxy variables. Bias can produce unfair outcomes even without discriminatory intent. Fairness requires that outcomes be equitable across affected groups.
  • Transparency means that affected people can understand how their data is used. Explainability means that automated decisions can be explained in terms the affected person can understand. Recourse mechanisms allow errors to be challenged and corrected.
  • Data governance is the set of policies, roles, and processes that keeps data systems well-managed over time. It includes data ownership, stewardship, policy documentation, metadata management, change management, and regular audit.
  • Compliance frameworks, including the GDPR, HIPAA, FERPA, and PCI DSS, establish legal requirements for regulated data. Compliance must be built into a system from the start, not added afterward.
  • Risk management means identifying and reducing the likelihood and impact of data breaches, data loss, inaccuracy, unauthorized use, and bias-driven harm. Responsible data practice integrates all of these principles into the actual work of designing, operating, and maintaining real systems.
Key Terms
Anonymization

The transformation of personal data so that individuals can no longer be identified from it. Truly effective anonymization is technically challenging and should not be assumed to be complete.

Audit Trail

A record of who accessed, changed, or acted on data, and when. Supports accountability and enables review of decisions that affected users or the organization.

Bias

Systematic patterns in data or processes that produce inaccurate or unfair results, often by over-representing or under-representing certain groups or outcomes.

Collection Bias

Bias that enters data because the collection method consistently favors certain groups or situations over others.

Compliance

Adherence to legal, regulatory, and contractual requirements governing data collection, use, protection, and deletion. Examples include the GDPR, HIPAA, FERPA, and PCI DSS.

Consent

Knowing and voluntary agreement by an individual to the collection and use of their personal data. Genuine consent is informed, specific, revocable, and freely given.

Data Governance

The policies, roles, responsibilities, and processes an organization uses to manage its data well over time, including ownership, stewardship, standards, and audit.

Data Minimization

The practice of collecting only the data that is necessary for a stated purpose, and not retaining data beyond that purpose.

Data Stewardship

Responsibility for the day-to-day management of data according to governance policies, including maintaining accuracy and following access and retention rules.

Explainability

The property of a system or decision that allows it to be explained in terms an affected person can understand, enabling meaningful recourse when errors occur.

Fairness

The principle that a data system's outcomes are equitable across the groups it affects. Multiple formal definitions of fairness exist and cannot all be satisfied simultaneously in general.

Feedback Loop Bias

Bias that is reinforced over time because a biased system produces decisions that alter the underlying data in ways that support and amplify the original bias.

Historical Bias

Bias that enters data because it reflects past practices that were themselves discriminatory, encoding those patterns into systems trained or built on that data.

Informed Consent

Consent given by someone who genuinely understands what data is being collected, how it will be used, who will have access, and what rights they retain.

Labeling Bias

Bias that enters data because the human judgments used to create or evaluate it are themselves influenced by bias, producing skewed labels that train biased systems.

Measurement Bias

Bias that enters data because the measurement or recording method is less accurate for some groups than others, producing systematically worse data quality for those groups.

Personally Identifiable Information (PII)

Data that can be used, alone or in combination with other information, to identify a specific individual.

Privacy

The principle that people have a right to control information about themselves, including who collects it, how it is used, how long it is kept, and who can access it.

Retention Policy

A documented rule specifying how long different types of data are kept before they must be deleted or anonymized. Often legally required in regulated industries.

Risk Management

The process of identifying, assessing, and reducing the likelihood or impact of things that could go wrong in a data system.

Sensitive Data

Information that, if disclosed, could cause meaningful harm to a person, including harm from discrimination, financial loss, physical danger, social stigma, or legal consequences.

Transparency

The property of a system or organization that allows affected people to understand how their data is collected, used, and acted upon.

Ethics Scenario Practice

For each scenario below, identify the ethical principles at stake, what went wrong or what risks are present, and what a more responsible approach would look like. These scenarios are designed for written response, small-group discussion, or whole-class analysis.

Scenario A
The Retained Records

A small online tutoring company asks students to create accounts and collects their names, email addresses, home addresses, ages, and academic performance records. When a student stops using the service, their account shows as inactive, but all records are retained indefinitely in the database. The company has no deletion process and no retention policy. Three years after the company closes, its database is discovered on an unsecured server and published online.

Consider:

What data should have been collected in the first place? What retention policy should have existed from the beginning? What deletion process was needed, and at what points should it have been triggered? Who bears responsibility for the outcome, and how is that responsibility distributed?

Scenario B
The Hiring Algorithm

A company builds a tool that screens job applications automatically. The tool is trained on ten years of the company's historical hiring decisions. Over those ten years, the company hired predominantly men for technical roles, partly because of implicit bias among hiring managers. The tool learns to de-prioritize applications from candidates whose resumes match patterns associated with female applicants. The company deploys the tool believing it is objective because it uses data rather than human judgment.

Consider:

What type of bias does this represent? Why is the fact that it uses historical data not sufficient to make it objective? What steps should the company have taken before deploying the tool? What recourse mechanisms should candidates have?

Scenario C
The Medical Record Leak

A hospital uses a database system to track patient diagnoses, treatments, and medications. A staff member who has administrative access, but whose role only requires access to appointment scheduling, runs a query that exports the full patient records for several thousand patients. The staff member shares this data with a journalist investigating a public figure. The exported data includes mental health diagnoses and addiction treatment records.

Consider:

What access control failures allowed this to happen? What would the least privilege principle have changed about the staff member's permissions? What audit mechanisms might have detected or prevented this? What compliance obligations were likely violated?

Scenario D
The Opaque Academic Flag

A university uses an automated system to flag students for academic intervention based on their attendance, grade history, and assignment submission patterns. Students whose accounts are flagged receive an email saying they have been identified for academic support. The email does not explain what data was used, what threshold triggered the flag, how accurate the system is, or how students can challenge the determination if they believe it is wrong.

Consider:

What transparency obligations are unmet here? What would an explainable version of this system look like? What recourse mechanisms should exist? What could go wrong if the system's flagging is inaccurate and no correction process exists?

Governance Questions

Answer the following questions based on the principles discussed in this chapter.

  • What is the difference between data ownership and data stewardship in an organization? Give an example of each role in a school setting.
  • A company has a privacy policy posted on its website. Is having a privacy policy the same as having data governance? What does governance include that a policy statement alone does not?
  • A hospital's IT department can technically access all patient records because they manage the database servers. Should they have access to all patient records? What principle applies here, and how should access be designed?
  • A research team wants to keep anonymized survey response data indefinitely for possible future research purposes. What questions should they ask before deciding whether to retain it? What risks does long-term retention of supposedly anonymized data carry?
  • An organization has a retention policy stating that user records should be deleted after two years of inactivity. Describe what must be true of the database schema, the application logic, and the operational processes for this policy to actually be carried out reliably.
Risk Analysis

For each database system description below, identify at least two specific risks, explain what harm could result from each risk, and describe one design or operational choice that would reduce each risk.

  • A hospital manages electronic patient records. The database has two user types: administrator, who can read and write all tables, and viewer, who can only read all tables. All clinical staff use viewer accounts. There are no role distinctions within clinical staff.
  • A small nonprofit organization stores donor records including full names, home addresses, email addresses, and donation histories in a cloud database. The database is backed up once a year. The executive director and three staff members all share a single database account with the same password.
  • A school district uses an automated attendance tracking system. The system records every student's daily attendance and location check-ins throughout the building. Records are retained indefinitely. No student, parent, or guardian can request access to or deletion of these records.
  • An e-commerce company stores customer purchase histories for marketing analysis. The database includes full names, shipping addresses, email addresses, phone numbers, and detailed product and category purchase histories. The data is shared with third-party analytics partners under terms that allow those partners to combine it with data from other sources.

Review Questions
Comprehension Questions
  • Why is it insufficient to say that a database is ethically designed simply because it stores data accurately and returns correct query results?
  • What are four properties that genuine consent should have? Give an example of a data practice that fails to meet each property.
  • Name four categories of sensitive data and give a specific example of a harm that could result from each category being improperly disclosed.
  • Explain the principle of data minimization in your own words. Give an example of a database design that violates this principle and describe how it should be corrected.
  • What is the difference between anonymization and deletion? When might anonymization be appropriate, and what limitations does it have?
Analysis Questions
  • Describe three different ways that bias can enter a data system. For each, give an example of how that type of bias could produce an unfair outcome in a real context.
  • What does it mean for a data-driven decision to be explainable? Why does explainability matter for the people affected by such decisions? What happens when an unexplainable system makes an error?
  • What is data governance? Name four components of a data governance program and explain what each one does to keep a data system reliable and trustworthy over time.
  • What is a retention policy? Why is designing for deletion important, and why can it be technically difficult in relational databases?
  • Describe two specific risks in a data system and explain, for each one, a design or operational practice that would reduce that risk.
Reflection Questions
  • Think of a data system you use regularly, such as a school portal, a library catalog, a store account, or a social media platform. Who might have access to the data it holds about you? What retention policy, if any, do you think it follows? What would you want to know about how your data is used that you currently cannot find out?
  • The chapter argues that responsible data practice must be built into a system from the design stage, not added afterward as a patch. Why is it harder to add privacy protections, audit trails, or deletion capabilities to an existing system than to include them from the start? What specific design choices would be harder to change later?

Coming Next — Chapter 28: Capstone — Designing a Small Information System

Chapter 28, the capstone chapter, guides you through designing a complete small information system from beginning to end. You will define a problem, identify users and requirements, design the data model, build the schema, create sample data, write useful queries, plan forms and reports, plan permissions and backups, and document the system for a real audience.

Everything in this textbook has been building toward this chapter. The design skills from Part II, the SQL skills from Part III, the data management knowledge from Part IV, the information systems thinking from Part V, the integration knowledge from Part VI, and the ethical principles from this chapter all come together in the capstone project.

Chapter 28 does not introduce major new technical concepts. Its goal is to demonstrate that a complete, thoughtful, and responsible database-backed information system can be designed with the knowledge and habits you have developed across this course. The final reflection in Chapter 28 returns to the question this chapter asks: who is affected by this system, and what does responsible design require of the people who build it?

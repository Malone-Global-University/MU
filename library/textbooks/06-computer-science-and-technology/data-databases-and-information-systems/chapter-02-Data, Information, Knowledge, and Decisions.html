Data, Information, Knowledge, and Decisions

Part I — Foundations of Data and Information
Data does not speak for itself. People collect it, organize it, clean it, interpret it, and act on it. Understanding how raw facts become useful decisions is one of the most important ideas in this entire course.

Imagine a thermometer placed outside a building. Every hour, a sensor records the temperature. After a full day, there are twenty-four numbers sitting in a file: 14, 13, 12, 11, 11, 10, 9, 9, 10, 12, 15, 18, 21, 23, 24, 25, 24, 23, 21, 19, 17, 16, 15, 14.

Are those numbers useful on their own? To someone who happens to know the units, the location, the date, and the purpose, they might be. But to most people glancing at them, those twenty-four numbers are just numbers. They could be temperatures, ages, scores, or distances. They carry no meaning by themselves.

Now add a label: outdoor temperature in degrees Celsius, measured hourly at the MGU campus weather station, Thursday, March 7. Suddenly the numbers begin to make sense. A facilities manager reads them and decides not to run the building's heating system in the afternoon. A biology student notices the temperature pattern and realizes it matches the expected daily cycle for late winter. A professor uses the data to illustrate a lesson about climate monitoring.

The same twenty-four numbers produced three different useful conclusions for three different people with three different purposes. That progression, from raw recorded facts to organized and labeled information to knowledge applied in context to action taken as a result, is what Chapter 2 is about.

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to distinguish data from information from knowledge, explain how context gives data meaning, identify the kinds of summaries and patterns that appear in data systems, understand what makes a decision data-driven or evidence-informed, and recognize several common ways that data can mislead.

2.1 Data as Recorded Facts

Data is the raw material of information systems. A piece of data is a recorded fact: something observed, measured, collected, or generated, and then stored in some form so it can be retrieved later.

Data can take many forms. A temperature reading is data. A student's name is data. The number of books borrowed from a library in a week is data. A photograph, a sound recording, and a GPS coordinate are all data. Even a survey response of "Somewhat Agree" is data.

What makes something data is not the form it takes, but the fact that it has been recorded and can be retrieved. An observation that exists only in someone's memory is not data in the technical sense, because it cannot be shared, verified, or used reliably by a system.

Three Properties of Data

Data in the context of information systems generally has three important properties.

Data is recorded. It has been captured in some persistent form, whether in a file, a table, a database, a spreadsheet, a log, or a piece of paper. The act of recording is not trivial. Choices made during recording, what to measure, how to measure it, what units to use, how often to record, and what to ignore, shape the data before anyone ever interprets it.

Data is discrete. Each piece of data represents a specific observation or measurement at a specific moment, about a specific thing. A row in a student records table represents one student. A row in a weather log represents one temperature reading at one time. The discreteness of data is what makes it possible to organize, sort, filter, count, and query.

Data can contain errors. Because data is recorded by people, machines, or software that are not perfect, data can be wrong. A number can be entered incorrectly. A sensor can malfunction. A category can be misapplied. A record can be duplicated. This is why data quality, introduced in Chapter 4, is so important. Raw data is not automatically accurate.

Data Versus Documents

It is worth distinguishing data from unstructured documents. A spreadsheet of student grades is data: structured, discrete, queryable. A written essay is a document: unstructured, continuous, not easily queried. Many modern information systems work with both, but databases are primarily designed for structured data. When this textbook uses the word "data," it generally means structured, recorded facts that can be organized into fields and records.

A Brief Taxonomy of Data Types

Data comes in several broad types. You will study these more carefully in Chapter 3, but it is useful to introduce them here.

Quantitative data is numeric. It can be counted or measured. Height, weight, temperature, grade, price, and distance are quantitative. Quantitative data can be added, averaged, and compared mathematically.

Qualitative data is descriptive. It represents categories, qualities, or identities. A student's major, a book's genre, a country's name, and a survey response of "Strongly Agree" are qualitative. Some qualitative data has a natural order (like a rating scale from 1 to 5), and some does not (like a list of color names).

Temporal data is time-based. Dates, times, timestamps, and durations are temporal. Temporal data enables systems to record when something happened, sort records chronologically, measure intervals, and track change over time.

Identifier data is used to label and distinguish records. Student ID numbers, ISBN codes for books, order numbers, and employee codes are identifiers. They are not usually meaningful as quantities but are essential for linking records together.

2.2 Information as Organized Data

Information is data that has been organized, labeled, and placed in a context that gives it meaning and makes it useful to someone with a purpose.

The difference between data and information is not a difference in the underlying facts. It is a difference in organization, context, and usefulness. The same set of numbers can be raw data in one situation and clear information in another, depending on what has been added around them.

What Organization Adds

When data is organized into information, several things are typically added.

Labels and field names tell readers what each piece of data represents. A number becomes a "birth year" or a "final exam score" when a label is attached to it.

Structure arranges data into rows and columns, or some other recognizable form, so that readers know where to look for what.

Units and formats make values interpretable. A temperature is meaningless without a unit (Celsius, Fahrenheit, Kelvin). A date is ambiguous without a format: is 03/04/25 March 4 or April 3?

Relationships connect pieces of data to each other. A student record becomes more informative when it is linked to enrollment records, course records, and grade records.

Selection and filtering remove irrelevant data, so that what remains is focused on the question at hand.

An Example of the Transformation

Consider the following raw data from a library system.

7842, 7843, 7844, 7845, 7846
1, 0, 1, 1, 0
14, 0, 5, 22, 0

Without context, these numbers are nearly meaningless. Now add structure and labels.

The same numbers, organized into a labeled table, become readable and actionable.

Now the same numbers tell a story. Resource 7845 has not been borrowed in 22 days and is not currently available, which might mean it has been checked out for a long time. Resources 7843 and 7846 are available and were never borrowed, or were returned today. A librarian looking at this table can take meaningful action.

Information Requires a Reader

It is also worth noting that information is not purely a property of data. It is a relationship between data and a reader with a purpose. Information systems are designed with specific readers, users, and purposes in mind. A well-designed system makes the right information available to the right people in the right form.

2.3 Knowledge and Interpretation

Knowledge is what a person builds when they combine information with prior experience, judgment, and understanding of how things work in the world.

Information tells you what the data says. Knowledge tells you what it means, why it matters, and what to do about it.

A database can store information. A report can present information. But knowledge exists in people, not in systems. A system can support and extend human knowledge, but it cannot replace the interpretive work that human minds do.

The Gap Between Information and Knowledge

Consider a simple example. A school database produces a report showing that 34 percent of students in a particular course received a failing grade in the first exam. That is information: clear, organized, labeled.

But knowing what to do about that information requires knowledge. An experienced instructor might recognize that the first exam in this course has historically been difficult, and that students who fail the first exam often pass by the end of the term when they adjust their study habits. The information and the knowledge together suggest a particular response: offer extra help early, not panic and change the entire curriculum.

Neither interpretation is simply a matter of reading the data correctly. Both require bringing prior understanding, experience, and judgment to bear on the information. That is what makes knowledge different from information.

Tacit and Explicit Knowledge

Knowledge comes in two broad forms that are relevant to information systems.

Explicit knowledge can be written down and shared: formulas, procedures, rules, policies, and reference materials. A database can store some forms of explicit knowledge, such as a table of tax rates, a list of approved suppliers, or a set of validation rules.

Tacit knowledge is harder to write down. It includes expertise, intuition, experience, and judgment, the kind of knowledge an experienced doctor uses when interpreting a patient's unusual combination of symptoms, or a librarian uses when helping a student narrow down a research topic. Information systems can support tacit knowledge by giving experts better information, but they cannot replace it.

Domain Knowledge

Domain knowledge is expertise in a particular field: medicine, finance, education, logistics, environmental science, or any other area. Domain knowledge is essential for interpreting data correctly.

Without domain knowledge, even excellent information can be misread. This is one reason why data analysts often work closely with domain experts: the analyst knows how to work with data, and the domain expert knows what the data means in context.

2.4 Decisions Based on Information

The reason organizations invest in databases, information systems, reports, dashboards, and data management is ultimately that they want to make better decisions. Data and information are tools in service of decisions.

The Decision Cycle

Decisions in organizations typically follow a cycle.

A question arises. A manager wants to know which products are selling poorly. A school wants to identify students at risk of not completing a course. A hospital wants to track whether a new protocol is reducing wait times.

Data is collected and organized. The question determines what data is needed, how it should be collected, and what time period is relevant. Not all questions can be answered with existing data; sometimes new data must be gathered.

The data is analyzed and summarized. Raw records are aggregated, filtered, sorted, and compared. Patterns and anomalies are identified. The results are organized into reports, tables, charts, or dashboards.

The information is interpreted. People with domain knowledge review the results and form conclusions. This step requires judgment, not just reading.

A decision is made. Based on the interpreted information, a course of action is chosen.

The decision is evaluated. After action is taken, new data is collected to see whether the decision achieved its purpose. This feeds back into the cycle.

Decision Types

Not all decisions are the same. Information systems support different kinds of decisions.

Operational decisions are routine and frequent. Did a student submit their assignment? Is a book available? Is an order ready to ship? These decisions are often automated in modern systems, with the database checking conditions and triggering an action.

Tactical decisions are periodic and moderately complex. Which courses should be added to next semester's schedule? How should library resources be distributed across departments? These decisions require reviewing summaries, comparing options, and applying judgment.

Strategic decisions are long-term and high-stakes. Should a university invest in a new campus building? Should a hospital adopt a new records management system? These decisions involve uncertainty, trade-offs, and values that go beyond what any single dataset can resolve.

2.5 Context and Meaning

One of the most important ideas in data literacy is that data does not carry its own meaning. Meaning comes from context.

Context is everything that surrounds a piece of data and makes it interpretable: the source, the time, the place, the purpose, the units, the definitions, the comparisons, and the people involved.

Context Changes Interpretation

The same piece of data can mean very different things depending on context.

A student's score of 62 on a test means something very different if the class average was 85 versus if the class average was 55. The number 62 has not changed. The context has changed.

A response time of 4 hours for a customer service request is excellent in some industries and unacceptably slow in others. A drop of 10 percent in monthly sales could be alarming, expected (if the previous month was unusually strong), or even a sign of a planned seasonal adjustment. The number alone cannot tell you which interpretation is correct.

Metadata: Data About Data

Metadata is information about data. It is the context that makes data interpretable.

For a database table, metadata includes the table name, column names, data types, units, source, collection date, validation rules, and any notes about how the data was gathered or what it represents.

Metadata is not optional or decorative. It is essential. Data without metadata is often useless or misleading. A column of numbers with no label, no units, and no source cannot be trusted or used.

In the Database

In databases, metadata is typically stored in the database schema: the definitions of tables, columns, data types, and constraints. In some systems, additional metadata is stored in documentation, data dictionaries, or catalog tables.

The Importance of Consistent Definitions

A subtle but important form of context is the definition of the data itself. What exactly does a field represent?

Consider a field called enrollment_count in a university database. Does it count all students who registered for courses this term? Does it exclude students on leave? Does it count only full-time students? Does it count students who later withdrew?

Different people or departments might answer these questions differently. If two analysts calculate enrollment using different definitions, they will get different numbers from the same underlying data, and a meeting between them can become confusing and unproductive.

Consistent, documented field definitions are part of responsible data management. This is one reason why data governance, introduced in Chapter 27, matters: organizations need shared agreements about what their data means.

2.6 Signals, Noise, and Uncertainty

Real data is almost never perfectly clean or perfectly clear. Most datasets contain a mix of meaningful patterns and meaningless variation. Understanding the difference between signal and noise is essential for interpreting data honestly.

Signal and Noise

In data analysis, signal refers to genuine patterns, trends, and relationships in the data that reflect something real about the world. Noise refers to random variation, measurement error, and irrelevant fluctuations that do not reflect any real pattern.

There is genuine variation week to week, some weeks better and some worse. That variation is partly noise. But across the ten weeks, there is also a real signal: the scores are gradually improving. Separating signal from noise typically requires looking at enough data over a long enough period, using summaries rather than individual data points, and applying domain knowledge to judge what kind of variation is normal.

Uncertainty

Uncertainty is the recognition that data-based conclusions are almost never certain. They are estimates, inferences, or probabilities, not absolute truths.

Even with good data and careful analysis, conclusions carry uncertainty for several reasons.

Sampling: Much data is collected from a sample, not the entire population. A survey of 500 students cannot tell you with certainty what all 20,000 students in a university think. It gives an estimate with some margin of error.

Measurement error: All measurement involves some imprecision. A temperature sensor may be accurate to within half a degree. A self-reported survey response depends on the respondent's memory and honesty.

Incomplete data: Records may be missing. Not everyone responds to a survey. Not every transaction gets recorded. Gaps in data create gaps in knowledge.

Changing conditions: Data from the past may not accurately predict the future if conditions have changed.

Responsible Practice

Presenting conclusions as more certain than they are is a form of misleading communication, even if it is done unintentionally. Responsible use of data acknowledges uncertainty rather than hiding it.

2.7 Summaries and Patterns

One of the most important things databases and information systems do is convert large quantities of raw records into summaries and patterns that people can actually use. No one can read hundreds of thousands of rows and draw useful conclusions directly. But a summary that shows the total number of loans per month, organized by resource category, can be read and understood in a few minutes.

Common Types of Summaries

Counts tell you how many records meet a condition. How many students enrolled in a course? How many books are currently on loan?

Totals sum a numeric field. What is the total revenue from ticket sales? What is the total number of pages borrowed across all digital resources this month?

Averages give a typical value. What is the average grade in this course? What is the average response time for support tickets?

Minimums and maximums show the range of values. What is the lowest recorded temperature this winter? What is the highest-selling product this quarter?

Distributions show how values are spread. Are grades clustered tightly around the average, or spread out? Are most loans short, or do some go on for months?

Trends show how something changes over time. Is enrollment growing or shrinking? Are response times improving or getting worse?

Comparisons show differences between groups. Do students who attend office hours perform better on exams? Do resources in one subject area get borrowed more often than resources in another?

Patterns and Anomalies

Patterns are regularities that appear consistently across the data. Library loans peak at the beginning and end of each term. Customer orders are higher on weekdays than weekends. Student scores in a particular course tend to be lower in the first exam than in later ones.

Anomalies are values or records that stand out as unusual compared to the pattern. A resource that has not been borrowed in two years while similar resources are borrowed weekly. A student whose scores dropped sharply in one week when they had been stable for months.

Why Anomalies Matter

Anomalies are worth investigating. Sometimes they reveal errors in the data. Sometimes they reveal genuine exceptions that require action. Sometimes they reveal interesting realities that the pattern obscures: that unborrowed resource may be in a format students don't know how to access.

2.8 Data-Driven and Evidence-Informed Decisions

You will encounter the phrase "data-driven decision-making" frequently in modern organizations and educational institutions. It is worth understanding both what this phrase means and what its limits are.

What Data-Driven Means

A data-driven decision is one based on evidence gathered from data, rather than on intuition, tradition, or assumption alone. The idea is that decisions made with systematic evidence are likely to be more accurate and effective than decisions made without it.

Data-driven approaches have genuine advantages. They force decision-makers to look at what is actually happening rather than what they assume is happening. They create accountability: decisions can be evaluated against data afterward. They allow patterns to be detected across many cases, which individuals cannot do through observation alone.

The Limits of Data-Driven Thinking

However, "data-driven" can become a misleading phrase if it is taken too literally. Several important limits apply.

Data reflects the past, not the future. Decisions affect what happens next, not what has already happened. Past data can inform predictions, but it cannot guarantee them.

Data reflects what was measured, not everything that matters. If a school system measures test scores and uses those scores to evaluate teachers, it may optimize for test performance while neglecting other aspects of learning that matter equally or more but are harder to measure.

Values cannot come from data. Data can tell you what is happening. It cannot tell you what matters, what is fair, or what trade-offs are worth making. Those are questions of values and judgment that require human deliberation, not just calculation.

Evidence-Informed Decisions

A more accurate and honest phrase is evidence-informed decision-making. This means that data and analysis are important inputs to decisions, taken seriously and examined carefully, but that decisions also incorporate domain knowledge, values, context, and judgment.

Evidence-informed decision-making treats data as a powerful tool that must be used thoughtfully, not as an automatic answer machine. This is the attitude this textbook tries to build throughout.

2.9 When Data Can Mislead

Even careful, well-collected data can lead to wrong conclusions if it is interpreted carelessly. Understanding the ways data can mislead is part of data literacy.

Missing Context

Data presented without context is one of the most common sources of misleading conclusions. A chart showing that a hospital's patient mortality rate is higher than the regional average sounds alarming. But if that hospital is the regional center for complex surgical cases and trauma care, its patient population is systematically sicker than that of community hospitals. The comparison is not meaningful without that context.

Correlation and Causation

Correlation means that two things tend to occur together. Ice cream sales and drowning rates both rise in summer. Students who eat breakfast tend to have higher grades. These correlations are real in the data.

Causation means that one thing causes another. Ice cream does not cause drowning; both are caused by hot weather and increased outdoor activity. Confusing correlation with causation is one of the most common errors in data interpretation. A database can show you that two things are related. It generally cannot, by itself, tell you why they are related.

Selection Bias

Selection bias occurs when the data you have does not represent the full population you are trying to understand. A survey of library users does not represent non-users. A dataset of reported crimes does not represent all crimes, only those that were reported. If decisions are made on the basis of selected data as if it represented the full picture, the conclusions may be systematically wrong.

Aggregation Problems

Summaries can hide important variation. The average grade in a course might be 75, but if half the students scored above 90 and half scored below 60, the average is a misleading picture of what is happening.

A related problem is called the ecological fallacy: drawing conclusions about individuals from group-level data. A school district might have strong average test scores overall while some individual schools are struggling significantly. Acting on the average without examining variation can mean missing the problems that matter most.

Outdated Data

Data that was accurate when it was collected may become inaccurate over time. A student's contact information from three years ago may no longer be correct. A product price recorded last year may have changed. Using outdated data as if it were current is a common and consequential error.

Misleading Presentation

Even accurate data can be presented in ways that lead to wrong conclusions. A graph that starts its vertical axis at a high number rather than zero can make a small change look dramatic. A table that selects only favorable time periods can make a trend look more positive than it is. These presentation choices, whether intentional or not, affect what conclusions people draw.

Chapter Summary

Chapter 2 introduced the journey from raw recorded facts to useful decisions, a journey that passes through several stages: data, information, knowledge, and action.

Data

Raw, discrete, recorded facts. Data alone is not inherently meaningful. It requires organization, labels, units, and context to become useful.

Information

Data that has been organized, labeled, and placed in context so that it communicates something to someone with a purpose.

Knowledge

Understanding built by combining information with experience, domain expertise, and judgment. Knowledge resides in people, not systems.

Context

The key ingredient that makes data interpretable. Metadata, field definitions, and documentation make context explicit and shareable.

Summaries and Patterns

Counts, totals, averages, trends, and comparisons convert large datasets into forms that can be read, discussed, and acted upon.

Misleading Data

Even well-collected data can mislead through missing context, false causal inference, selection bias, aggregation problems, and outdated records.

These ideas are not just background theory. When you write a query, you are turning data into information for a specific purpose. When you design a database, you are making choices that will shape what contexts are captured and what questions can be answered. When you build a report, you are creating a summary that someone will use to make a decision. Understanding the full journey from data to decision makes you a more thoughtful database designer and a more responsible information systems practitioner.

Common Misconceptions

Misconception

More data automatically means better decisions.

More data can improve decisions, but only if the additional data is relevant, accurate, and correctly interpreted. A very large dataset of low-quality data, or data that measures the wrong things, does not improve decision-making. It may actually make it harder by adding noise and creating false confidence.

Misconception

If data shows a correlation, we know the cause.

Correlation shows that two things tend to occur together. It does not explain why. The cause could be a direct relationship, a common third factor, a coincidence in the particular dataset, or an artifact of how data was collected. Determining causation requires more than showing that a correlation exists.

Misconception

Averages tell you what is typical.

Averages can be misleading when data is not evenly distributed. A dataset where most values are very low and a few are extremely high can have an average that is not typical of most observations. Looking at distributions, medians, and ranges alongside averages gives a more accurate picture.

Misconception

Data is objective, so it cannot be biased.

Data is collected and organized by people making choices about what to measure, how to measure it, who to include, and how to present results. Those choices reflect values and assumptions that can introduce bias. Working with data responsibly requires thinking critically about its sources and limitations.

Misconception

If something cannot be measured, it is not important.

Measurement makes things visible to information systems, but unmeasured things do not stop being real or important. Student well-being, institutional culture, the quality of a mentoring relationship, and community trust are all difficult to measure but profoundly important. An information system that only tracks measurable outcomes can lead to decisions that optimize for what is easy to count at the expense of what matters most.

It introduces the fundamental structures of organized data: fields, records, tables, rows, columns, and data types. You will learn how different kinds of data, including text, numbers, dates, Boolean values, and categories, are represented in database systems, and why choosing the right type for each piece of data matters. Where Chapter 2 asked what data is and why it matters, Chapter 3 asks how data is structured so that a computer can store, retrieve, and use it reliably.

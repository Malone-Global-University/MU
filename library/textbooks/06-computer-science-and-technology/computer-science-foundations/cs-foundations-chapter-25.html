Chapter 25 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Every day, enormous quantities of information are recorded, transmitted, and stored. Hospitals record patient measurements and treatment outcomes. Retailers track purchases and browsing behavior. Weather stations measure temperature, humidity, and wind speed every few minutes. Social platforms log every post, like, and scroll. All of this recorded information is called data — but raw data does not automatically answer questions. Data science is the discipline that turns raw data into useful knowledge.

This chapter introduces data science as the study of how to collect, organize, clean, explore, analyze, visualize, model, and interpret data to answer questions, discover patterns, and support decisions. You will learn what datasets are, how data is collected and cleaned, how visualization reveals patterns, how descriptive statistics summarize data, what correlation and causation mean, how prediction works at an introductory level, how bias enters data and models, and how to interpret data responsibly.

No prior statistics or calculus is required. You should be comfortable with arithmetic, ratios, percentages, tables, and basic graph reading. This chapter prepares you for later courses in databases, data science, machine learning, and artificial intelligence.

Opening Question

A school district wants to understand which students are most at risk of falling behind in reading. They have access to attendance records, past test scores, course enrollment data, and socioeconomic indicators collected from surveys. How would you turn that data into a useful decision? What would you need to clean, measure, visualize, and watch out for? This chapter gives you the vocabulary and habits to answer those questions carefully.

Section 25.1

What Data Science Studies

Data science is the study of how to collect, organize, clean, explore, analyze, visualize, model, and interpret data to answer questions, discover patterns, and support decisions. Data scientists work across medicine, business, science, government, education, journalism, sports, environmental monitoring, and many other fields.

Data science is not a single subject. It sits at the intersection of three disciplines. Computing provides the tools for storing, retrieving, processing, and programming with data. Statistics provides methods for measuring, summarizing, testing, and reasoning under uncertainty. Domain knowledge tells a data scientist which questions matter, what the data actually represents, and whether an answer makes sense.

A data scientist studying hospital readmissions needs all three: programming knowledge to work with the database, statistical reasoning about patient populations, and medical knowledge to interpret what the findings mean in practice.

What Data Science Is Not

Data science is not the same as machine learning or artificial intelligence, though all three are related. Machine learning (Chapter 26) is one technique data scientists sometimes use. Data science is the broader practice of working with data from beginning to end — collecting it, understanding it, cleaning it, summarizing it, visualizing it, modeling it, and interpreting results carefully. It is also not magic: data can be incomplete, biased, mismeasured, or simply irrelevant to the question at hand.

Section 25.2

Data, Datasets, and Features

In computer science, data refers to recorded information — numbers, text, dates, images, audio, sensor readings, or any other representation of something in the world. Before data can be analyzed, it must be organized. The most common way to organize data for analysis is a dataset — a structured collection of related data organized as a table.

The Structure of a Dataset

A dataset table has rows, also called records or observations — each row represents one thing being studied. It also has columns, also called features, variables, or attributes — each column represents one characteristic that was measured or recorded.

Why Feature Types Matter

Recognizing the type of each feature is one of the first steps in any data science project. The type of feature determines which operations make sense, which visualizations are appropriate, and which statistical methods apply. Treating a categorical feature like a number — for example, treating grade levels (9, 10, 11, 12) as if their differences are meaningful quantities — leads to incorrect analysis.

Section 25.3

Data Collection

Before a dataset exists, someone must collect data. How data is collected has enormous consequences for what can and cannot be learned from it.

Methods of Collection

MethodDescriptionExample
SurveysAsking people questions and recording answersAnnual census, customer satisfaction poll
Sensors and instrumentsDevices that automatically record physical measurementsWeather stations, fitness trackers, traffic counters
Transaction recordsEvents recorded as they happenPurchases, hospital visits, web clicks
ExperimentsDeliberately varying conditions and measuring outcomesClinical drug trials, A/B website tests
Administrative recordsData from normal institutional operationsTax records, school enrollment, court records

Populations and Samples

Suppose a data scientist wants to understand the reading habits of teenagers in a country of 50 million people. It would be impossible to survey every teenager. Instead, data scientists typically study a sample — a subset of the full population of interest.

Representativeness is the key property a good sample needs. A representative sample accurately reflects the diversity of the population. A sample of teenagers from one wealthy neighborhood is not representative of all teenagers in a country. When a sample is unrepresentative, the conclusions drawn from it will not apply to the broader population — this is called sampling bias, explored further in Section 25.9.

Observational Studies vs. Experiments

ApproachDescriptionStrengthLimitation
Observational studyObserve things as they naturally occur without interventionCan study situations where experiments are impossibleCannot easily establish causation — confounders remain
ExperimentDeliberately vary one condition; randomly assign subjects to groupsRandom assignment distributes confounders equally; causation more justifiedMay be impossible or unethical in many real situations
What Gets Left Out

Data collection always involves choices about what to measure and what to ignore. A hospital dataset might record treatments but not whether patients had strong family support at home — which could also affect outcomes. What is not in a dataset is often as important as what is. A data scientist must always ask: what information is missing, and could it change the conclusions?

Section 25.4

Data Cleaning

Real-world data is rarely perfect when collected. Before analysis, data almost always requires data cleaning — the process of finding and correcting problems so the data accurately represents what it is supposed to represent.

Data cleaning is unglamorous but essential. Experienced data scientists estimate that cleaning consumes a majority of time on most data projects. A small number of errors in a dataset can distort the results of an entire analysis.

Common Data Problems

ProblemDescriptionExamplePossible Handling
Missing valuesData was not collected for some observationsSurvey respondent skipped a questionRemove row, or impute using average of known values
Duplicate recordsSame observation appears more than onceForm submitted twiceRemove the duplicate; keep one copy
Inconsistent formattingSame value represented multiple waysDates as "2024-03-15" and "March 15, 2024"Standardize all values to one format
OutliersValue far from the typical rangeWeight recorded as 1500 kg instead of 150 kgInvestigate: genuine extreme (keep) or error (correct/remove)
Incorrect typesValues stored as wrong typeA number column stored as textConvert to the correct type
Data Cleaning Example
Cleaning a small delivery-time dataset
  • Original Customer ID | Delivery Time (min) ------------|-------------------- 2001 | 32 2002 | 28 2003 | 9999 ← sensor error 2004 | 31 2005 | ← missing 2006 | 29 2001 | 32 ← duplicate
  • ProblemsValue 9999 is clearly a sensor error. Row 2005 is missing a value. Customer 2001 appears twice — the second row is a duplicate.

Section 25.5

Data Visualization

Numbers alone are difficult to interpret, especially in large quantities. Data visualization is the practice of representing data graphically so that patterns, trends, outliers, and relationships become easier to see. A good visualization communicates information more efficiently than a table of numbers. A poor one obscures or distorts information.

Anscombe's Quartet — Why Visualization Matters

One of the most famous examples in data science, created by statistician Francis Anscombe in 1973, consists of four small datasets with nearly identical descriptive statistics: the same mean, variance, correlation, and regression line. Yet when plotted, the four datasets look completely different — one is linear, one is curved, one has an outlier, and one forms a vertical cluster. This demonstrates that summary statistics alone can miss important features of data. Visualization catches what numbers summarize away.

Line Charts — Trends Over Time

Line charts connect data points in sequence, usually over time. Use them when a single quantitative feature is measured at multiple ordered points. The line makes seasonal patterns, trends, and sudden changes easy to see.

Pie Charts and Their Limits

Pie charts represent parts of a whole using wedge-shaped slices. They are familiar but frequently misleading. Human eyes are poor at accurately judging the sizes of different-shaped wedges, especially when slices are similar. For most purposes, a bar chart communicates the same information more accurately. Pie charts work reasonably well only when there are a small number of categories and the differences are large and obvious.

HonestyThe chart should show what the data actually saysShowing only the time period where a trend looks favorable
Appropriate choiceThe chart type should match the data and the questionUsing a line chart for unordered categories; a pie chart with 15 slices

Section 25.6

Descriptive Statistics at an Introductory Level

Descriptive statistics are numerical summaries that describe the main features of a dataset. Rather than examining every row individually, descriptive statistics compress a dataset's key properties into a small number of meaningful numbers.

Measures of Center

A measure of center describes the typical or central value in a dataset.

Mean, Median, and Mode — Side by Side
Dataset: scores 78, 91, 65, 83, 54
  • Mean Add all values and divide by count.
    Mean = (78 + 91 + 65 + 83 + 54) / 5 = 371 / 5 = 74.2 The mean is sensitive to outliers. Adding a score of 0 (absent student):
    Mean = (78 + 91 + 65 + 83 + 54 + 0) / 6 = 371 / 6 = 61.8 A single extreme value drops the mean by more than 12 points.
  • Median The middle value when sorted. If even count, average the two middle values.
    Sorted: 54, 65, 78, 83, 91 Middle value (3rd of 5): 78 The median is resistant to outliers. Adding the 0:
    Sorted: 0, 54, 65, 78, 83, 91 Two middle values: 65 and 78 Median = (65 + 78) / 2 = 71.5 Median shifted only from 78 to 71.5 — a much smaller change than the mean's shift.
  • Mode The value that appears most often. Most useful for categorical data or data with repeated values. In this dataset, every value is unique — no mode. If scores were A, B, B, C, B, A, C, B — the mode would be B (appears 4 times).
SituationBetter MeasureReason
Data roughly symmetric, no extreme outliersMeanUses all values; most mathematically precise
Data contains extreme outliersMedianResistant to pull from extreme values
Income, housing prices, wealthMedianThese distributions are heavily skewed by high earners
Categorical dataModeMean and median are not meaningful for categories

Measures of Spread

Knowing the center of a dataset is not enough. Two datasets can have the same mean but very different distributions. Dataset A: 50, 50, 50, 50, 50 — mean = 50, no variation. Dataset B: 10, 30, 50, 70, 90 — mean = 50, values spread widely. A measure of spread describes how much variation exists.

MeasureHow to CalculateSensitivity to Outliers
RangeMaximum − MinimumVery sensitive — one outlier changes it completely
Interquartile Range (IQR)Q3 (75th percentile) − Q1 (25th percentile)Resistant — ignores the top and bottom 25%
Standard DeviationAverage distance of each value from the mean (roughly)Sensitive to outliers, but captures all values
Standard Deviation — Conceptual Idea

The standard deviation is a measure of how far typical values are from the mean. A small standard deviation means values cluster closely around the mean. A large standard deviation means values are spread out widely. Students who continue to statistics or data science courses will learn to compute it precisely. For now: small std dev = tightly clustered; large std dev = widely spread.

Section 25.7

Correlation and Causation

One of the most important ideas in data science — and one of the most frequently misunderstood — is the relationship between correlation and causation. Two features are correlated when changes in one feature tend to accompany changes in another. Causation means that one thing directly produces a change in another. These are not the same.

The Correlation Coefficient

Correlation is measured by the correlation coefficient, written as r, which ranges from −1 to +1.

r valueInterpretation
+1.0Perfect positive correlation — as one increases, the other always increases proportionally
+0.7Strong positive correlation
+0.3Weak positive correlation
0.0No linear correlation
−0.3Weak negative correlation
−0.7Strong negative correlation
−1.0Perfect negative correlation — as one increases, the other always decreases proportionally

Correlation Does Not Imply Causation

Consider a famous example: cities that sell more ice cream tend to have more drowning deaths. Ice cream sales and drowning deaths are positively correlated. Does eating ice cream cause drowning? Obviously not.

The explanation is a confounding variable: hot weather. When the weather is hot, people buy more ice cream and people swim more, so more drowning deaths occur. Hot weather causes both. Ice cream and drowning are correlated not because one causes the other, but because both are caused by a third variable.

A confounding variable (also called a lurking variable) is a variable related to both the feature being studied and the outcome, creating a spurious appearance of correlation between them.

REMEMBER: A scatter plot or correlation coefficient tells you
          whether A and B tend to vary together.
          It does NOT tell you WHY they vary together.
Worked Example
Identifying a confounding variable
  • ObservationCities with more hospitals tend to have higher rates of disease. A researcher notes a strong positive correlation between hospital count and disease rates.
  • False claim"Hospitals cause disease."
  • ConfoundPopulation size is the confounding variable. Larger cities have more people — which means more hospitals are needed and more disease cases occur in total simply because there are more people.
  • Correct interpretationWhen you look at disease rates per capita rather than total counts, the correlation may weaken or disappear. The appropriate conclusion is not "hospitals cause disease" but "larger cities have more of both because they have more people."
When Can We Claim Causation?

The strongest evidence comes from randomized controlled experiments, where researchers randomly assign subjects to a treatment group or a control group. Random assignment distributes confounding variables equally between groups, so differences in outcomes can be attributed to the treatment. Where experiments are impossible or unethical, scientists use careful observational studies, multiple independent datasets, and accumulated evidence over time. A correlation in data is a clue worth investigating — not a conclusion.

Section 25.8

Prediction

One of the most powerful uses of data science is prediction — using patterns found in historical data to estimate unknown or future values. The feature being predicted is called the target or label. The features used to make the prediction are called inputs or predictors.

Inputs (known features)Target (to be predicted)
Study hours, practice tests takenExam score
Patient age, weight, symptomsDiagnosis
Historical temperature, humidityTomorrow's weather
Transaction amount, time, locationFraud or not fraud
House size, location, ageSale price

Linear Prediction — Trend Lines

For two quantitative features with a linear relationship, prediction can be done with a trend line (also called a line of best fit or regression line). The trend line passes through the scatter plot in the direction that best represents the overall relationship.

If the trend line for study hours and scores gives an equation like Predicted score ≈ 45 + 2.5 × (Study Hours), then for a student who studied 16 hours: Predicted score ≈ 45 + 2.5 × 16 = 85. This is an estimate — not a guarantee. A student who studies 16 hours might actually score 79 or 92.

Underfitting and Overfitting

ProblemDescriptionEffect
UnderfittingModel is too simple to capture real patternsPerforms poorly on both training data and new data
OverfittingModel learns the training data too precisely, memorizing noise instead of patternsPerforms well on training data but fails on new data
Good fitModel captures real patterns without memorizing random variationPerforms well on both training data and new data
Prediction Is Not Certainty

Even a good prediction model produces estimates, not guarantees. Presenting model outputs as certainties is one of the most common and harmful mistakes in data science communication. A model that predicts a 73% chance of hospital readmission means exactly that — a 73% probability, not a guaranteed outcome.

Section 25.9

Bias in Data

Data science can produce conclusions that are unfair, inaccurate, or harmful when the data itself — or decisions made about it — introduce bias. Bias in data science refers to systematic errors that skew results in a consistent direction.

Type of BiasHow It ArisesExample
Sampling biasThe sample does not represent the intended populationA medication study conducted only on men whose findings do not apply to women
Historical biasTraining data accurately reflects the past, but the past was unfairA hiring algorithm trained on historical decisions learns to reject women in technical roles because fewer were historically hired
Measurement biasThe process of collecting data produces systematic errorsA face-detection system trained mostly on lighter-skinned faces performs poorly on darker-skinned faces
Label biasLabels in training data reflect biased human judgmentsA recidivism prediction model trained on data where some judges were systematically harsher toward certain groups
Confirmation biasThe analyst focuses on patterns that support preexisting beliefsReporting only findings that support a preferred teaching method, ignoring contradictory evidence in the same dataset
Historical Bias Is Especially Difficult

Historical bias is particularly hard to address because the data is technically accurate — it really does reflect what happened. The problem is that what happened was unfair. A system that learns from historical patterns and replicates them is not "neutral" just because it is based on real data. Data encodes the choices, inequalities, and judgments of those who collected it.

Recognizing and Addressing Bias

Recognizing bias requires asking careful questions at every stage of a project. Who collected this data, and who is represented — and who might be missing? Does the training data reflect historical patterns that should not be perpetuated? Could the measurement process systematically favor certain groups? Are the labels produced by reliable, unbiased judgments? Am I looking for evidence that challenges my assumptions, or only evidence that supports them?

No dataset or analysis is perfectly free of bias. Responsible data science involves making bias visible, addressing it where possible, and being honest about its effects on conclusions.

Section 25.10

Responsible Data Interpretation

Data science has significant power to influence decisions — in medicine, criminal justice, hiring, education, and finance. With that power comes responsibility.

Principles of Honest Interpretation

PrincipleWhat It Means
Report the full pictureDo not select only results that support a preferred conclusion. Report what the data shows overall, including inconvenient findings.
Distinguish statistical from practical significanceA 0.2-point average score difference might be statistically detectable in a huge dataset but have no practical meaning for real decisions.
State limitations clearlyEvery analysis has limitations: unrepresentative samples, missing variables, models that may not generalize. Stating these is not weakness — it is honesty.
Separate description from prescriptionDescribing what data shows is different from recommending what to do. Decisions require values, context, and judgment that data alone cannot supply.

Privacy and Consent

When data involves people, ethical responsibilities include privacy — data about individuals should be collected and used in ways that protect them. Even data that seems anonymous can sometimes be re-identified when combined with other information. This is called re-identification and is a serious privacy risk.

Consent means people should know what data is being collected about them and have meaningful choices about how it is used. Data minimization — collecting only the data needed for a specific purpose — reduces the risk of misuse or breach.

Chapter 25 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 25.1Data science combines computing, statistics, and domain knowledge to turn raw data into useful knowledge. It is distinct from machine learning and from basic programming, and it is not a substitute for human judgment about values and context.
  • Sec 25.2A dataset is a structured table of observations (rows) and features (columns). Features may be quantitative, categorical, ordinal, or temporal. Recognizing feature types is the first step in any analysis because it determines which operations and visualizations are appropriate.
  • Sec 25.3Data is collected through surveys, sensors, records, and experiments. Samples must be representative of the population to produce valid conclusions. Observational studies reveal patterns but cannot easily establish causation; experiments provide stronger causal evidence. What is not measured can be as important as what is.
  • Sec 25.4Real-world data requires cleaning before analysis. Common problems include missing values, duplicates, inconsistent formatting, outliers, and incorrect data types. The right approach to each problem depends on why it occurred and how it would affect the analysis.
  • Sec 25.5Data visualization reveals patterns that numbers alone obscure. Line charts show trends over time; bar charts compare categories; histograms show distributions; scatter plots reveal relationships between two features. Good visualizations are accurate, clear, honest, and appropriately matched to the data and question.
  • Sec 25.6Descriptive statistics summarize key dataset properties. Mean, median, and mode describe center; range, IQR, and standard deviation describe spread. The median is more resistant to outliers than the mean. Choosing the right measure depends on the shape of the data and the question being asked.
  • Sec 25.7Correlation describes how two features tend to vary together, measured by the correlation coefficient r from −1 to +1. Correlation does not imply causation. Confounding variables can produce spurious correlations. Establishing causation requires more evidence than correlation data alone can provide — ideally a randomized experiment.
  • Sec 25.8Prediction uses patterns in historical data to estimate target values from input features. Trend lines model linear relationships. Underfitting occurs when a model is too simple; overfitting occurs when it memorizes training data too precisely. Predictions are estimates, not certainties.
  • Sec 25.9Bias enters data through unrepresentative sampling, unfair historical patterns, systematic measurement errors, and biased human labels. No dataset is perfectly free of bias. Responsible data science makes bias visible, addresses it where possible, and is honest about its effects on conclusions.
  • Sec 25.10Responsible data interpretation requires reporting results honestly, stating limitations clearly, distinguishing description from prescription, recognizing what data cannot answer, respecting privacy and consent, and communicating findings accurately for non-specialist audiences. Ethical responsibility runs through every stage of a data science project — not only the final report.

Key Terms Review

bias
A systematic error that consistently skews data or analysis in a particular direction, causing inaccurate or unfair conclusions.
categorical feature
A feature representing groups or categories, not numerical quantities. Arithmetic on categorical features is usually not meaningful.
confounding variable
A variable related to both a feature being studied and an outcome, creating a misleading appearance of a direct relationship. Also called a lurking variable.
correlation
A statistical relationship where changes in one feature tend to accompany changes in another, measured by the correlation coefficient r (−1 to +1).
data cleaning
The process of finding and correcting errors, missing values, and inconsistencies in a dataset before analysis.
data science
The discipline that uses data, computing, statistical reasoning, and domain knowledge to answer questions, discover patterns, and support decisions.
dataset
A structured collection of related data, typically organized as a table of observations (rows) and features (columns).
descriptive statistics
Numerical summaries that describe the main features of a dataset, including measures of center and spread.
feature
A column in a dataset representing one characteristic measured for each observation. Also called a variable or attribute.
histogram
A chart showing the distribution of a single quantitative feature by grouping values into bins and showing observation counts per bin.
historical bias
Bias that occurs when training data accurately reflects historical patterns that are unfair and should not be replicated in future systems.
imputation
Filling in missing values using a reasonable estimate, such as the mean or median of the known values in that feature.
IQR
Interquartile range — the difference between Q3 (75th percentile) and Q1 (25th percentile). Resistant to outliers.
mean
The arithmetic average: sum of values divided by count. Sensitive to outliers; pulled by extreme values.
median
The middle value in an ordered dataset. Resistant to outliers; preferred when data is skewed.
mode
The value appearing most often in a dataset. Most useful for categorical data or data with repeated values.
observation
A single row in a dataset representing one instance of the thing being studied. Also called a record.
outlier
A value far from the typical range. May represent a genuine extreme or a data collection error; must be investigated before deciding how to handle it.
overfitting
A modeling problem where a model learns training data too precisely, capturing noise instead of real patterns and failing to generalize to new data.
population
The complete group a data scientist wants to learn about. Samples are drawn from populations.
prediction
Using patterns in historical data to estimate unknown or future target values from input features.
quantitative feature
A feature representing a measurable number. Arithmetic operations are meaningful. Examples: height, temperature, score.
range
Maximum value minus minimum value. The simplest measure of spread; very sensitive to outliers.
re-identification
Combining supposedly anonymous data with other sources to identify specific individuals. A serious privacy risk even after names are removed.
sample
A subset of the population actually studied. Must be representative to produce conclusions that generalize.
sampling bias
Bias occurring when a sample does not represent the intended population, skewing results toward over-represented groups.
scatter plot
A chart placing dots on a grid where each axis is a quantitative feature and each dot is one observation, used to explore relationships.
standard deviation
A measure of how far typical values are from the mean. Small = tightly clustered; large = widely spread.
target
The feature a prediction model estimates. Also called the label or outcome.
trend line
A line drawn through a scatter plot representing the direction and strength of the relationship between two features. Used for simple prediction. Also called line of best fit or regression line.
underfitting
A modeling problem where a model is too simple to capture real patterns, performing poorly on both training and new data.

Worked Example 25-1
Calculating Descriptive Statistics
  • DatasetDelivery times (minutes) after cleaning: 23, 31, 28, 19, 34, 27, 31, 25, 42, 22
  • Mean Sum = 23+31+28+19+34+27+31+25+42+22 = 282 Count = 10 Mean = 282 / 10 = 28.2 minutes
  • Median Sorted: 19, 22, 23, 25, 27, 28, 31, 31, 34, 42 Even count — average the 5th and 6th values: Median = (27 + 28) / 2 = 27.5 minutes
  • Mode31 appears twice — all others appear once. Mode = 31 minutes
  • Range42 − 19 = 23 minutes
  • InterpretThe mean (28.2) is slightly higher than the median (27.5), suggesting the value 42 pulls the average upward. A data scientist should investigate whether 42 minutes is a genuine extreme or an error in this context.
Mean: 28.2 min · Median: 27.5 min · Mode: 31 min · Range: 23 min
Worked Example 25-2
Identifying Correlation vs. Causation
  • FindingA researcher analyzes city-level data and finds that cities with more hospitals have higher rates of disease. Strong positive correlation is observed.
  • False claim"Hospitals cause disease." This is a causal claim made from a correlation without ruling out confounders.
  • ConfounderPopulation size. Larger cities need more hospitals and have more disease cases in total — simply because they have more people. Both are effects of the same cause.
  • Correct approachExamine disease rates per capita. Examine what happens to disease rates when hospital capacity changes over time in specific communities. Control for other variables (poverty, age demographics, pollution). Even then, establishing causation from observational data is difficult.
Correlation is real. Causation is not supported. Population size is the confounding variable.

Debugging and Error Analysis

Error 1 — Data Cleaning
A data scientist reports mean delivery time as 147 minutes; other analysts find 29 minutes after cleaning.

What type of data problem most likely explains the discrepancy? What should the data scientist have done before computing the mean?

Error 2 — Misleading Visualization
A bar chart comparing three schools' test scores (97, 98, 99) uses a y-axis starting at 95. School C's bar appears 3× as tall as School A's.

What is wrong with this chart? How does the y-axis choice affect the viewer's perception? What would the chart look like if drawn honestly?

Error 3 — Causation Claim
"Ice cream sales are strongly correlated with violent crime (r = +0.78). We recommend restricting ice cream sales to reduce crime."

Identify two errors in reasoning in this recommendation. What is the likely confounding variable?

Error 4 — Overfitting
A prediction model achieves 98% accuracy on training data but only 61% accuracy on a separate test set.

What problem has occurred? What does it mean, and what should the data scientist do?

Error 5 — Sampling Bias
A researcher surveys 1,000 readers of a fitness magazine about weekly exercise and concludes: "Most adults exercise at least 5 days per week."

Identify the flaw in this conclusion. What population does the sample actually represent?

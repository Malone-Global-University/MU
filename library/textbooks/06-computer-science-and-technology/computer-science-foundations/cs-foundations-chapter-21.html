Chapter 21 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Every connected system faces risk. Data moves across networks. Programs run on shared machines. Millions of people use devices that hold private information, financial records, personal messages, medical histories, and professional secrets. Keeping those systems, networks, and data safe is the work of cybersecurity.

This chapter introduces cybersecurity as a discipline — not as a collection of alarming news stories, not as hacker mythology, and not as a catalog of tricks. Cybersecurity is the disciplined practice of protecting systems, networks, devices, and data from unauthorized access, damage, disruption, and misuse. It involves understanding how attacks work, why systems are vulnerable, and how defenders design systems that resist harm.

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to name major categories of cybersecurity threats, explain the difference between vulnerabilities and attacks, describe common defensive strategies, understand how authentication and authorization protect systems, follow the conceptual logic of encryption, and reason carefully about security tradeoffs in real systems. This chapter will not teach students to attack systems. It will teach students to think like defenders.

Opening Question

A hospital's patient records system goes offline. Surgeries are delayed. Staff cannot access medical histories. Nurses write notes on paper. A ransom note appears on every screen demanding cryptocurrency payment. How did this happen? What could have prevented it? What should the hospital do now? By the end of this chapter, you will have the vocabulary and reasoning to answer those questions.

Section 21.1

What Cybersecurity Protects

Cybersecurity protects four things: systems, networks, devices, and data. A system is any organized arrangement of hardware, software, and processes designed to perform a function. A hospital's patient record system, a bank's transaction processing system, and a city's traffic management system are all examples. Each one holds information and performs actions that matter to real people — disrupting or corrupting them causes real harm. A network is the infrastructure connecting systems and devices. A device is any physical computing hardware. Data is the information stored, processed, and transmitted across all of these.

Security professionals organize their thinking around three core properties, often called the CIA triad. These three properties define what it means for a system to be secure, and every significant cybersecurity incident can be described as a failure of one or more of them.

Confidentiality
Information is accessible only to those who are authorized to see it. Unauthorized parties cannot read, copy, or intercept sensitive data.
Integrity
Information is accurate and complete. It has not been altered, corrupted, or destroyed by unauthorized parties.
Availability
Systems, networks, and data are accessible when authorized users need them. Services remain operational and responsive.

The CIA triad is not a formula — it is a checklist and a way of framing tradeoffs. A system that is perfectly available but has no confidentiality controls is not secure. A system with perfect confidentiality but no integrity checking can be quietly corrupted. Real security requires balancing all three properties, and the right balance depends on the system's purpose and the value of the data it holds.

Example — Ransomware and the CIA Triad

A ransomware attack encrypts a hospital's patient files and demands payment for the decryption key. This is an availability failure — authorized staff cannot access the data they need. If attackers also copy the data before encrypting it, it is also a confidentiality failure. If the attack corrupts records, it is also an integrity failure. Most serious attacks violate more than one property at once.

Practice 21.1Concept Check

  3. An attacker modifies financial records to change account balances by small amounts, hoping no one will notice. Which property does this primarily attack?

Section 21.2

Threats, Vulnerabilities, and Risk

Three concepts form the foundation of security thinking. A threat is any potential event, person, or circumstance that could cause harm to a system. A vulnerability is a weakness in a system that a threat could exploit. Risk is the combination of the likelihood that a threat will exploit a vulnerability and the potential impact of that exploitation. Having a vulnerability does not guarantee that harm will occur — it means a system has a weakness that could be exploited if a threat is present and capable.

ConceptDefinitionExample
ThreatAny potential source of harm to a systemRansomware groups targeting hospitals; disgruntled employees; natural disasters
VulnerabilityA weakness that could be exploitedUnpatched software, default passwords, misconfigured permissions, untrained staff
Threat actorAny entity that might exploit a vulnerabilityOutside attackers, malicious insiders, nation-state groups, automated bots
RiskLikelihood × Impact of exploitationHigh-likelihood + high-impact = high priority for immediate action
Attack Surface

Attack surface describes the total set of points in a system where an attacker could attempt to gain access — every open port, every running service, every user account, every piece of software with known vulnerabilities. Reducing the attack surface is a fundamental security strategy. Every service turned off, every unnecessary account deleted, and every unneeded piece of software removed makes the attack surface smaller and the system harder to compromise.

Practice 21.2Threat Analysis
  1. A company uses software that hasn't been updated in two years. A security researcher has publicly documented an exploit for a vulnerability in that software version. Classify the threat, the vulnerability, and explain why this represents high risk.
  2. A small business has fifty computers. Ten are running software that hasn't been updated. Five share a login with the password "admin." Three have antivirus software disabled. How do each of these contribute to the attack surface?
  3. Explain why the concept of attack surface means that adding more features to software always comes with a security cost.

Section 21.3

Malware

Malware — from "malicious software" — is software designed to cause harm. It is one of the most common tools attackers use, and it takes many forms. Understanding the major categories helps defenders recognize risks and choose appropriate protections.

Virus
Attaches to legitimate files or programs and spreads when those files are executed or copied. Requires human action to spread — opening an infected file, running an infected program.
Worm
Spreads across networks without human action by exploiting vulnerabilities. Self-replicates rapidly. The WannaCry worm infected hundreds of thousands of computers across 150 countries within hours.
Trojan Horse
Disguised as legitimate or useful software. When installed, the malware activates alongside or instead of the promised program. Does not self-replicate — relies on tricking users.
Ransomware
Encrypts the victim's files and demands payment (usually cryptocurrency) for the decryption key. Has attacked hospitals, schools, city governments, and critical infrastructure.
Spyware
Secretly monitors activity and sends information to the attacker. May record keystrokes, capture screenshots, track browsing, or access the camera and microphone.
Rootkit
Designed to hide itself and other malware from system monitoring tools by modifying operating system components. Extremely difficult to detect and remove.
Botnet
A network of compromised computers (bots or zombies) controlled remotely. Used to send spam, launch attacks, mine cryptocurrency, or distribute more malware — all without the owners' knowledge.
Adware
Displays unwanted advertising. May collect browsing data without consent and serve as a delivery vehicle for more serious malware.

How Malware Reaches Systems

Entry PathwayDescription
Email attachmentsMalicious files sent as attachments; opened by the recipient
Malicious linksLinks in email, messages, or web pages that trigger a download when clicked
Drive-by downloadsMalware that downloads automatically when visiting a compromised web page
Infected USB drivesPhysical media containing malware, inserted into a computer
Untrusted softwareMalware bundled with pirated software, fake tools, or unofficial app stores

Supply chain attacksMalware inserted into legitimate software by compromising a developer or update system
Defenses Against Malware

Keep operating systems and software updated. Use antivirus and antimalware software with current signatures. Avoid opening attachments or clicking links from unknown or untrusted sources. Download software only from official sources. Back up data regularly so ransomware cannot permanently destroy access to important files. Use least-privilege accounts to limit the damage a compromised account can cause.

Section 21.4

Phishing and Social Engineering

Many attacks do not exploit software vulnerabilities at all — they exploit human psychology. This category is called social engineering: manipulating people into doing something that helps the attacker. Social engineering is often the first step in sophisticated attacks on well-defended systems, precisely because humans are harder to patch than software.

Forms of Phishing

TypeDescription
PhishingMass emails impersonating a trusted entity (bank, tech company, government) to steal credentials or install malware
Spear phishingTargeted phishing crafted for a specific individual or organization using researched personal details
WhalingSpear phishing aimed at high-value targets: executives, directors, senior officials
SmishingPhishing conducted through text messages (SMS)
VishingPhishing conducted through phone calls

Other Social Engineering Techniques

TechniqueHow It Works
PretextingAttacker creates a fabricated scenario to justify requesting information — e.g., calls pretending to be IT support
BaitingLeaves a malware-infected USB drive where a curious person will find and plug it in
TailgatingPhysically follows an authorized person through a secure door by appearing to belong there
Quid pro quoOffers something (technical help, information) in exchange for credentials or access
Why Social Engineering Works

Social engineering exploits real human tendencies: the desire to be helpful, the instinct to obey apparent authority, the response to urgency and fear, the tendency to trust people who seem familiar, and reluctance to embarrass someone who appears to belong. These are not character flaws — they are normal human responses that attackers have learned to exploit systematically. Urgency is almost always a manipulation tactic; slow down and verify before acting.

Recognizing a Phishing Email

Sample Phishing Email — Read Carefully
From: support@paypa1.com   [note: "paypa1" not "paypal"]
Subject: URGENT — Your PayPal account has been limited

Dear Valued Customer,

We have detected unusual activity on your account. To restore full access, you must verify your identity within 24 hours or your account will be permanently suspended.

[Verify My Account Now]

PayPal Customer Security Team
Red Flags in That Email

The sender domain is "paypa1.com" (number one, not letter L). Legitimate organizations do not threaten permanent suspension after 24 hours. Generic greeting "Valued Customer" rather than your name. Urgency designed to prevent careful thought. The link destination may differ from what it appears to be. When in doubt, navigate directly to the official site rather than clicking any link in the email.

  3. A physical security guard lets a person in a delivery uniform through a secure door without checking their badge because the person's arms are full of boxes. What social engineering technique is being used? What procedure should the guard follow instead?

Section 21.5

Authentication

Authentication is the process of verifying that someone or something is who or what it claims to be. Before a system grants access, it must verify identity. Authentication relies on one or more of three factors.

FactorWhat It MeansExamples
Something you knowA secret the user has memorizedPassword, PIN, security question answer
Something you haveA physical object in the user's possessionHardware token, smartphone, smart card, authenticator app
Something you areA biological characteristic of the userFingerprint, face scan, voice pattern, retina scan

Single-factor authentication uses only one factor — typically a password. It is vulnerable because if that one factor is compromised (stolen, guessed, leaked in a data breach), the attacker has everything they need. Multifactor authentication (MFA) requires two or more independent factors. Even if a password is stolen, an attacker still cannot authenticate without also having the second factor — a code sent to the user's phone, or a fingerprint. MFA is one of the single most effective defenses against unauthorized account access.

Passwords

Password AttackHow It Works
Brute forceSystematically trying every possible combination of characters until one works
Dictionary attackTrying common words, phrases, and known passwords from lists
Credential stuffingUsing username/password pairs leaked from one service to try access at other services
PhishingTricking the user into entering their password on a fake login page
Shoulder surfingPhysically watching someone type their password
Worked Example 21.5.1
Evaluating Password Strength
  • Passwordpassword — Extremely weak. The most common password in leaked databases. First entry on every dictionary attack list.
  • PasswordP@ssw0rd — Still weak. Common character substitutions (@ for a, 0 for o) are well-known to attackers and appear in wordlists.
  • PasswordFluffy2015! — Moderately weak. Combines a real word (probably a pet's name) with a predictable year. Attackers research personal details for targeted attacks.
  • Passwordcorrect-horse-battery-staple — Much stronger. Long passphrase of random words. Length provides significant resistance to brute force. Easier to remember than complex short passwords.
  • PasswordXk9#mP2$vL7@nQ — Very strong. Random, long, mixes all character types. Nearly impossible to guess; only practical attack is obtaining it through phishing or a breach of the stored hash.
Key principle: length matters more than complexity alone. A 20-character passphrase of random words is significantly stronger than an 8-character "complex" password. Use a password manager to generate and store strong, unique passwords for every account.
Password Hashing

Well-designed systems never store passwords in plain text. Instead, they store a hash — the result of running the password through a one-way mathematical function. When a user logs in, the system hashes the entered password and compares the result to the stored hash. If an attacker steals the database, they get hashes, not passwords. Reversing a strong hash is computationally very difficult. Weak passwords can still be cracked using precomputed tables called rainbow tables, which is why strong, unique passwords remain important even when hashing is in use.

Practice 21.5Authentication

  3. Explain why storing a password as a hash is better than storing it in plain text, but why hashing alone is not sufficient protection for weak passwords.
  4. Why would biometric authentication (fingerprint or face scan) be considered a "something you are" factor? What is one limitation of biometric authentication compared to a password?
Section 21.6

Authorization and Permissions

Authentication asks "Who are you?" Authorization asks "What are you allowed to do?" A user can be fully authenticated — the system is certain of their identity — and still be restricted from certain resources, files, or actions. Authorization enforces those restrictions.

The principle of least privilege states that users and programs should have only the permissions they need for their legitimate function — and no more. An employee who processes expense reports does not need access to payroll records. A web application should not run with administrator rights. A mobile app does not need access to the user's contacts if it is a simple calculator. Least privilege limits the damage that results from an account being compromised or a program being subverted. If an attacker gains access to a limited account, they can only do what that account is authorized to do.

Access Control Models

ModelHow It WorksWhere It's Used
DAC (Discretionary)Resource owners decide who has access and in what wayPersonal computer file systems; most everyday software
RBAC (Role-Based)Permissions are assigned to roles (doctor, nurse, accountant); users are assigned to rolesHospitals, corporations, enterprise software
MAC (Mandatory)A central authority assigns classification levels; access is determined by clearanceMilitary and government systems; high-security environments
Worked Example 21.6.1
Applying Least Privilege — Medical Clinic Database
  • ScenarioA small medical clinic's database holds patient contact information, medical records, billing data, insurance details, and employee payroll records. Assign appropriate permissions to each role.
  • ReceptionistRead access to patient names and contact information only. No access to medical records, billing details, or payroll. They need names to book appointments — nothing more.
  • NurseRead/write access to patient names, contact information, and medical history. No access to billing details or payroll. They need to record clinical notes, not process payments.
  • Billing SpecialistAccess to patient names, billing information, and insurance details. No access to medical history or payroll. They need financial records, not clinical records.
  • IT AdminSystem-level access for maintenance; ideally, access controls can be designed so IT can maintain the database structure without reading the actual patient data inside it.
Principle: each role accesses only what its legitimate function requires. If a receptionist's account is compromised, the attacker gains contact information — not complete medical histories. Least privilege contains the damage of any single breach.
Practice 21.6Authorization
  1. A software developer at a tech company is given administrator rights on every server in the company so they can do their work more easily. What principle does this violate? What risks does it create?
  2. A mobile app for tracking daily step counts requests access to the user's contacts, microphone, camera, and location. What principle does this appear to violate? What should a security-aware user do?
  3. Explain in your own words why the principle of least privilege reduces the damage caused by a security breach, even if it does not prevent the breach itself.
Section 21.7

Encryption at an Introductory Level

Encryption transforms readable data (plaintext) into an unreadable form (ciphertext) using a mathematical algorithm and a key. Only someone with the correct key can reverse the process (decryption) and recover the original plaintext. Encryption is one of the most important tools in information security. It protects data in transit across networks and data at rest on storage devices. Even if an attacker intercepts or steals encrypted data, the data is useless without the key.

Symmetric Encryption

In symmetric encryption, the same key is used to encrypt and decrypt. Both parties must have the key, and they must keep it secret. Symmetric encryption is fast and suitable for large amounts of data. The challenge is key distribution: how do two parties securely share a key before they have a secure channel to communicate through?

Asymmetric Encryption

Asymmetric encryption (also called public-key cryptography) uses two mathematically related keys: a public key and a private key. Data encrypted with the public key can only be decrypted with the private key. The public key is shared freely. The private key is kept secret. This solves the key distribution problem — anyone can encrypt a message to you using your public key, but only you can decrypt it with your private key.

How HTTPS Uses Both

HTTPS uses asymmetric encryption to establish a secure channel and exchange a symmetric session key. Then it switches to symmetric encryption for the actual data transfer. This combines the key-distribution strength of asymmetric encryption with the speed of symmetric encryption — a practical solution to a real engineering tradeoff.

Data in Transit vs. Data at Rest

StateDescriptionProtection Method
Data in transitData moving across a network — from browser to server, between email servers, across a VPNHTTPS, encrypted email, VPN; prevents eavesdroppers from reading intercepted packets
Data at restData stored on a disk, database, or cloud serverDisk encryption, database encryption; protects data if storage is stolen or accessed without authorization
What Encryption Does Not Do

Encryption protects the content of data — not who is communicating, when, or the fact that communication is occurring. It does not protect data from someone who already has the key, and it does not prevent access if an authorized user's credentials are compromised. Encryption is an essential tool, not a complete solution.

Section 21.8

Backups and Recovery

Data backups are copies of data stored separately from the original, so that if the original is lost, corrupted, or held for ransom, the data can be recovered. Backups are one of the most reliable defenses against data loss — including ransomware. An organization with current, tested, offline backups can recover from a ransomware attack without paying the ransom.

The 3-2-1 Backup Rule
  • Keep 3 copies of the data (the original plus two backups)
  • On 2 different types of storage media (for example, an internal drive and an external drive)
  • With 1 copy stored offsite or in the cloud, disconnected from the primary network

The offsite or offline requirement is critical: backups stored on the same network as the original can be encrypted by the same ransomware attack. Offline and offsite copies cannot.

Backup PrincipleWhy It Matters
Regular backupsThe gap between backups is data that could be lost. A 24-hour backup cycle means up to 24 hours of work is at risk.
Tested backupsA backup is only as useful as its ability to actually restore data. Untested backups have failed organizations at the worst possible moment.
Offline or offsite copiesBackups on the same network can be compromised alongside the primary data. Physical separation prevents this.
Practice 21.8Backups
  1. A school backs up its student records every Sunday night to an external drive that stays connected to the school's server. Ransomware attacks the school on a Friday afternoon and encrypts all files, including the backup drive. What went wrong? How should the backup strategy be improved?
  2. Apply the 3-2-1 rule to design a backup strategy for a small business that processes customer orders. Where would each of the three copies be stored?
  3. Why is it not sufficient to simply make a backup — why must backups be tested?
Section 21.9

Updates and Patch Management

Software contains bugs. Some bugs cause crashes or unexpected behavior. Others create security vulnerabilities — weaknesses that attackers can exploit to gain unauthorized access, escalate privileges, or execute malicious code. When a vulnerability is discovered, the software developer typically releases a patch — an update that fixes it. The process of systematically applying patches is called patch management.

Between the time a vulnerability is discovered and the time a patch is applied, systems are exposed. If the vulnerability becomes public knowledge before patches are applied, attackers can target unpatched systems at scale. This window is called the exposure window. Some of the most damaging attacks in history exploited known vulnerabilities for which patches already existed — organizations that had applied the patch were protected; those that had not were compromised.

WannaCry — A Lesson in Patch Management

The WannaCry ransomware attack of 2017 exploited a Windows vulnerability for which Microsoft had released a patch approximately two months earlier. Organizations that had applied the patch were unaffected. Those that had not — including many hospitals in the United Kingdom's National Health Service — had their systems encrypted and were unable to access patient records for days. The cause was not an unknown, undefended vulnerability. It was a known, patchable vulnerability that had not been patched.

Update TypePurpose
Security patchFixes a specific vulnerability that could be exploited by attackers
Bug fixCorrects non-security errors that cause crashes or incorrect behavior
Feature updateAdds new functionality
Performance updateImproves speed or efficiency
End-of-Life Software

Software eventually reaches end of life (EOL) — the point at which the developer stops releasing updates, including security patches. New vulnerabilities discovered in EOL software will never be fixed. Systems running EOL software become increasingly vulnerable over time as new exploits are discovered and published. Replacing or upgrading EOL software is an important and often overlooked security responsibility.

Practice 21.9Patch Management
  1. A hospital's IT staff delays applying security patches for three months because they are worried updates might cause problems with their specialized software. A known vulnerability is exploited during that window. What could they have done differently? What does this tradeoff reveal about real-world security decisions?
  2. Explain the concept of an "exposure window." Why does the exposure window create urgency around applying security patches quickly?
  3. A company continues to use an operating system that reached end of life two years ago. The software still works fine. What security risk does this create, and why does that risk grow over time?
Section 21.10

Defense in Depth

No single security measure is sufficient. A determined attacker who encounters one defense will look for another way in. The strategy of using multiple independent, overlapping layers of security — so that bypassing one does not grant full access — is called defense in depth. The term comes from military strategy: if one defensive line fails, others remain to limit the advance. In computing, the goal is similar: even if one control is bypassed, others should slow or stop the attack and limit the damage.

01 Physical Security Locked rooms, access badges, security cameras, biometric entry. If an attacker can physically reach a device, digital defenses become irrelevant.
02 Network Security Firewalls, intrusion detection systems, network segmentation. Controls and filters traffic entering and moving through the network.
03 Endpoint Security Antivirus/antimalware, host-based firewalls, disk encryption, automatic screen locks on individual devices.
04 Application Security Input validation, secure coding practices, security testing before deployment, keeping applications updated.
05 Identity & Access Management Strong authentication, multifactor authentication, least privilege, regular auditing of who has access to what.
06 Data Security Encryption of sensitive data at rest and in transit, data classification, controlled export, secure disposal.
07 Human Layer Training users to recognize phishing, report suspicious activity, follow security policies, and use strong authentication. Often the weakest link.
08 Monitoring & Response Logging events, monitoring for anomalies, incident response procedures, practicing recovery. Detects and limits damage when prevention fails.

Threat Modeling

Thinking like a defender means systematically asking adversarial questions about every system and process. This practice is called threat modeling. A basic threat model asks:

Threat Modeling Questions
  • What could go wrong with this system?
  • Who might want to cause this harm, and why?
  • How would they try to do it? What vulnerabilities could they exploit?
  • What defenses exist? Which are strongest and which are weakest?
  • If those defenses fail, what then? What is the fallback?
  • How would we detect that something has gone wrong?
  • How would we recover from an incident?
Worked Example 21.10.1
Classifying a Security Incident — Full Analysis
  • ScenarioA university student receives an urgent email appearing to come from IT, warning that their account will be disabled in 24 hours unless they verify their identity. They click the link, enter their credentials on a realistic-looking fake page, and receive a reassuring confirmation. Two days later their account is used to send thousands of spam emails.
  • Attack typePhishing — a fraudulent email impersonating a trusted entity directed to a fake login page to steal credentials.
  • What attacker gainedThe student's username and password — their authentication credentials. The account was then enrolled in a spamming botnet.
  • CIA triadConfidentiality violated (credentials stolen). Availability threatened (account may be suspended for spam). Integrity violated (account used to send content the student never created).
  • PreventionsMultifactor authentication would have blocked the attacker even with the password. Training to recognize urgency as a phishing tactic. Navigating to the official login page directly rather than clicking email links. Email filtering to quarantine phishing attempts before they reach the inbox.
No single prevention is complete. MFA would have stopped account access. Training might have prevented the click. Email filtering might have blocked the delivery. This is defense in depth working across multiple layers.

  2. A company invests heavily in a state-of-the-art firewall but does nothing to train employees about phishing. Chapter 21 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • 21.1Cybersecurity protects systems, networks, devices, and data. The CIA triad — confidentiality, integrity, and availability — defines what security means and how failures are classified.
  • 21.2Threats are potential sources of harm; vulnerabilities are weaknesses that could be exploited; risk combines likelihood and impact. Reducing the attack surface reduces risk.
  • 21.3Malware — including viruses, worms, Trojans, ransomware, spyware, rootkits, and botnets — causes harm through various entry pathways. Defenses include updates, antimalware, careful downloads, and backups.
  • 21.4Social engineering attacks human psychology rather than software. Phishing and its variants (spear phishing, whaling, smishing, vishing) impersonate trusted entities. Urgency is a manipulation tactic; verification is the defense.
  • 21.5Authentication uses something you know, something you have, or something you are. Multifactor authentication requires two or more factors. Strong, unique passwords and password managers significantly reduce risk.
  • 21.6Authorization controls what authenticated users can do. The principle of least privilege limits permissions to what each role actually needs, containing the damage from any breach.
  • 21.7Encryption converts plaintext to ciphertext using a key. Symmetric encryption uses one key; asymmetric encryption uses a public/private key pair. Encryption protects both data in transit and data at rest.
  • 21.8Backups provide recovery when data is lost or held hostage. The 3-2-1 rule — three copies, two media types, one offsite — ensures backups survive the same event that destroys the original.
  • 21.9Patch management — systematically applying software updates — closes known vulnerabilities. The exposure window between disclosure and patching is a high-risk period. End-of-life software receives no patches and grows permanently more vulnerable.
  • 21.10Defense in depth uses multiple overlapping layers — physical, network, endpoint, application, identity, data, human, and monitoring — so no single failure causes total compromise. Threat modeling asks adversarial questions to expose weaknesses before attackers do.

Key Terms Review

CIA triad
The three foundational properties of security: confidentiality, integrity, and availability.
attack surface
The total set of points in a system where an attacker could attempt to gain access or cause harm.
threat
Any potential event, person, or circumstance that could cause harm to a system.
vulnerability
A weakness in a system that could be exploited by a threat.
risk
The combination of the likelihood that a threat will exploit a vulnerability and the impact of the resulting harm.
malware
Software designed to cause harm; includes viruses, worms, Trojans, ransomware, spyware, rootkits, and botnets.
ransomware
Malware that encrypts the victim's files and demands payment for the decryption key.
worm
Malware that self-replicates and spreads across networks without requiring human action.
botnet
A network of compromised computers controlled remotely by an attacker, often without the owners' knowledge.
social engineering
Attacks that manipulate human psychology rather than exploiting software vulnerabilities.
phishing
A social engineering attack that impersonates a trusted entity to steal credentials or install malware.
spear phishing
Targeted phishing crafted for a specific individual or organization using researched personal details.
authentication
The process of verifying that a person or system is who or what it claims to be.
multifactor auth
Authentication requiring two or more independent factors: something you know, have, or are.
authorization
The process of determining what an authenticated user or system is permitted to do.
least privilege
The principle that users and programs should have only the permissions they need — no more.
encryption
Converting plaintext into ciphertext using an algorithm and a key so that only authorized parties can read it.
symmetric encryption
Encryption using the same key to encrypt and decrypt. Fast but requires secure key sharing.
asymmetric encryption
Encryption using a public key to encrypt and a private key to decrypt. Solves the key distribution problem.
data in transit
Data actively moving across a network. Protected by HTTPS, VPNs, encrypted messaging.
data at rest
Data stored on a disk, database, or cloud server. Protected by disk and database encryption.
patch
A software update that fixes a bug or security vulnerability.
patch management
The systematic process of applying software updates to keep systems secure.
end of life (EOL)
The point at which a vendor stops releasing updates for software, including security patches.
defense in depth
A security strategy using multiple independent, overlapping layers of protection.
threat modeling
Systematically asking adversarial questions about a system to identify vulnerabilities before attackers do.
firewall
A system that monitors and filters network traffic based on security rules.
3-2-1 backup rule
Keep 3 copies of data, on 2 different media types, with 1 copy offsite or offline.

Debugging and Error Analysis

Each statement contains a misconception, error, or incomplete reasoning. Identify the problem and explain what is correct.

Error 1
"Our database was breached, but it's fine — we hash all our passwords. Even if attackers have the hashes, they can't log in without the plain-text passwords."

What is wrong with this reasoning? What risk remains even with hashed passwords?

Error 2
"We use HTTPS on our website, so our users' data is completely secure."

Identify at least two things HTTPS does not protect against. What does it specifically protect?

Error 3
"We haven't been attacked in five years. Our current security must be sufficient."

Why does an absence of known attacks not confirm that security is sufficient?

Error 4
"We back up our data every week to an external drive that stays plugged into our server. We're fully protected from ransomware."

What critical backup principle is being violated here? How could ransomware defeat this backup strategy?

Error 5
"Using a complicated 8-character password like 'X7k!2mP@' is always more secure than a long simple phrase like 'correct-horse-battery-staple'."

Evaluate this claim. What does research on password strength actually show about length versus complexity?

Error 6
"Our most important security measure is our antivirus software. As long as we run regular antivirus scans, we're protected from most attacks."

What attacks does antivirus software not protect against? What does this error say about single-layer security thinking?

Looking Ahead
Chapter 22: Privacy, Identity, and Digital Safety

Chapter 21 introduced the technical side of cybersecurity: threats, malware, authentication, encryption, backups, updates, and defense in depth. These are the tools and strategies used to protect systems and data. Chapter 22 turns to the human side. Technical security measures protect systems — but privacy concerns what happens to the information those systems collect about people, and what the consequences are for individuals when that information is misused, exposed, or exploited. In Chapter 22, students will learn what personal data and metadata are, how digital systems track behavior and location, how online identity and digital reputation work, how to evaluate privacy settings and make informed consent decisions, and how to recognize scams, manipulation, and online threats to personal safety. Chapter 22 connects the technical systems introduced throughout Part V to the real human lives those systems affect.

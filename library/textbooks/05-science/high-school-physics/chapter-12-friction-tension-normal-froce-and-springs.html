Physics: Foundations of Physical Science

Part II — Motion and Forces · Chapter 12

Friction, Tension, Normal Force, and Springs

12.1 Why Specific Force Models Matter

Newton’s second law, Fnet = ma, is universally true, but it is only as useful as your knowledge of the forces on the right side. In Chapter 10, you learned what forces are and what Newton’s laws say about them. In Chapter 11, you learned to draw them. This chapter teaches you the quantitative rules that govern the most common mechanical forces: friction, tension, normal force, and spring force.

Each force type has its own model — a rule that specifies its magnitude and direction under given conditions. These models are empirical: they are simplifications that capture the dominant behavior of real forces to within the precision needed for most problems. They are not exact at the atomic level, but they are powerful and reliable within their domains.

Key Point: A force model is a rule connecting a force’s magnitude to measurable quantities such as surfaces, geometry, and displacement. Knowing the model means you can calculate the force from the situation, not just identify that a force exists.

12.2 Contact Forces vs. Constraint Forces

The forces in this chapter fall into two broad categories. Contact forces arise from direct physical interaction between surfaces or objects at a point of contact. They include friction and the normal force. They exist only when surfaces are in contact and disappear when contact is broken.

Constraint forces are forces that enforce a geometric or mechanical constraint on motion. Tension in a rope constrains an object to move with the rope. The normal force from a surface constrains an object to not pass through the surface. Springs constrain objects to remain connected to the spring’s equilibrium point. These forces take whatever magnitude is needed to maintain the constraint — up to the constraint’s breaking point.

Table 12.1 Contact and constraint force categories.
Force Category What It Enforces
Normal force Constraint / Contact Object stays on the surface; no penetration
Tension Constraint Object stays connected to the rope; rope does not stretch
Static friction Contact Object stays stationary relative to surface
Kinetic friction Contact Opposes sliding between surfaces in motion
Spring force Constraint / Contact Object stays connected to spring; spring resists deformation

12.3 The Nature of Friction

Friction is a contact force that opposes relative motion, or the tendency toward relative motion, between two surfaces. It acts parallel to the contact surface and, unlike the normal force, has a component along the direction of motion.

Friction arises from electromagnetic interactions at the atomic level. Real surfaces, no matter how polished, are microscopically rough. When two surfaces are pressed together, their irregularities interlock and form tiny adhesive bonds. Breaking these bonds requires a force. The friction we measure is the macroscopic result of billions of these microscopic bond-breaking events.

Friction depends on the types of surfaces and on how hard they are pressed together, which is the normal force. It does not depend on the area of contact — a wide box and a narrow box of the same mass on the same surface experience the same friction force. This result, which surprises many students, follows from the microscopic model.

Historical Note: The laws of friction were studied empirically by Leonardo da Vinci in the 1490s and systematized by Guillaume Amontons in 1699. The independence of friction from contact area was the key insight that made the simple coefficient model work. Charles-Augustin de Coulomb, famous for electrostatics, also contributed significantly to the theory in the 1780s.

12.4 Static Friction

Static friction acts on an object that is not sliding relative to the surface. It is a constraint force: it takes whatever magnitude is needed to prevent sliding, up to a maximum value. Static friction is variable — it adjusts to balance the applied forces and keep the object stationary.

When the applied force along the surface is small, static friction is small and exactly cancels it. As the applied force increases, static friction increases to match. When the applied force exceeds the maximum static friction, the object begins to slide and kinetic friction takes over.

fs ≤ μsN

The direction of static friction opposes the tendency to slide: it points in the direction that would prevent sliding. Ask: if friction disappeared, which way would the object start to move? Static friction acts opposite to that direction.

Example — Static friction below maximum

A 10.0 kg box on a surface with μs = 0.50 is pushed with 20.0 N. Maximum static friction = 0.50 × 98.1 = 49.1 N. Since 20.0 N < 49.1 N, the box does not move. Static friction = exactly 20.0 N, not 49.1 N.

Common Mistake: Static friction equals the applied force, not μsN, as long as the object has not started sliding. It only reaches μsN at the moment of impending motion. Many students incorrectly always use μsN as the friction value even when the object is in equilibrium with a smaller applied force.

12.5 Kinetic Friction

Kinetic friction, also called sliding friction, acts on an object that is already sliding relative to the surface. Unlike static friction, kinetic friction has a fixed magnitude, for given surfaces and normal force, that does not depend on how fast the object is moving or the applied force.

fk = μkN

Because kinetic friction is constant for constant N, it produces a constant deceleration in the absence of other horizontal forces. This allows the kinematic equations from Chapter 8 to be applied directly: the deceleration from kinetic friction is a = fk/m = μkg for a horizontal surface with no other horizontal forces.

Example — Kinetic friction in action

A 5.0 kg box slides on a surface with μk = 0.30. Normal force N = mg = 49.1 N. Kinetic friction fk = 0.30 × 49.1 = 14.7 N, opposing motion. Deceleration = 14.7 / 5.0 = 2.94 m/s².

Key Point: Kinetic friction is typically less than maximum static friction (μk < μs). This is why it takes more force to start an object sliding than to keep it sliding — you must overcome maximum static friction to initiate sliding, but only kinetic friction to maintain it.

12.6 Coefficients of Friction

The coefficient of friction is a dimensionless number that characterizes how rough a pair of surfaces is relative to each other. It is always defined for a surface pair — not for a single surface alone — because friction depends on both surfaces in contact.

Table 12.2 Representative coefficients of static and kinetic friction.
Surface Pair Coefficient of Static Friction (μs) Coefficient of Kinetic Friction (μk)
Rubber on dry concrete0.7–0.80.6–0.7
Steel on steel (dry)0.740.57
Wood on wood0.25–0.500.20–0.40
Ice on ice0.100.03
Steel on ice0.100.05

Coefficients of friction are measured empirically. They are not exact physical constants — they depend on surface cleanliness, temperature, humidity, and surface finish. The values in Table 12.2 are representative ranges; problems in this book will always specify the coefficient to use.

Worked Example 12.1 — Friction on a Horizontal Surface

Problem: A 20.0 kg box rests on a wooden floor (μs = 0.40, μk = 0.30). A horizontal force of 70.0 N is applied. Does the box slide, and if so, what is its acceleration?

  1. Normal force: N = mg = 20.0 × 9.81 = 196.2 N.
  2. Maximum static friction: fs,max = μsN = 0.40 × 196.2 = 78.5 N.
  3. Compare applied force: 70.0 N < 78.5 N — the box does not slide.
  4. Static friction value: fs = 70.0 N, exactly equal to the applied force; a = 0.

Now apply 90.0 N instead:

  1. 90.0 N > 78.5 N: the box slides.
  2. Kinetic friction: fk = μkN = 0.30 × 196.2 = 58.9 N.
  3. Net force: Fnet = 90.0 − 58.9 = 31.1 N.
  4. Acceleration: a = 31.1 / 20.0 = 1.56 m/s².

12.7 Tension in Ropes and Cables

Tension is the internal force transmitted through a rope, cable, chain, or rod under pulling, or tensile, stress. When you pull one end of a rope attached to an object, the rope transmits that pulling force to the object as tension. The tension acts along the rope and pulls — never pushes.

For an ideal rope, massless and inextensible, the tension is the same at every point along the rope. This is because each infinitesimally small section of the rope has zero mass, so any net force on it would produce infinite acceleration. Newton’s second law then requires zero net force at every point, meaning the tension must be equal throughout.

When a rope is attached to multiple objects or passes over a pulley, the tension determines how forces are transmitted. Drawing an FBD of each object and a segment of the rope separately reveals how tension propagates.

Example — Tension in a hanging system

A 3.0 kg block hangs from rope A, which is tied to rope B, which is tied to the ceiling. Analyzing the block: TA = mg = 29.4 N upward. Analyzing the junction between A and B: TB = TA = 29.4 N. Both rope segments carry the same tension for massless ropes.

12.8 Ideal Rope and Pulley Assumptions

Most rope-and-pulley problems in this book use three idealizations. Stating them explicitly at the start of each problem is good practice.

Table 12.3 Ideal rope and pulley assumptions.
Assumption What It Means What It Simplifies
Massless rope Rope has zero mass Tension is the same throughout the rope
Inextensible rope Rope does not stretch Objects at each end move with the same speed and acceleration
Frictionless, massless pulley Pulley redirects the rope with no energy loss Tension magnitude is unchanged across the pulley; only direction changes

In an Atwood machine, two masses hanging from a single rope over a pulley, the inextensible rope means both masses have the same acceleration magnitude but opposite directions. The massless pulley means the tension in the rope is the same on both sides.

Worked Example 12.2 — Atwood Machine

Problem: Masses m1 = 4.0 kg and m2 = 6.0 kg hang from a massless, frictionless pulley. Find the acceleration and tension.

  1. FBD of m1: T − m1g = m1a, with m1 accelerating upward.
  2. FBD of m2: m2g − T = m2a, with m2 accelerating downward.
  3. Add equations: (m2 − m1)g = (m1 + m2)a → a = (6.0 − 4.0) × 9.81 / (6.0 + 4.0) = 19.62 / 10.0 = 1.96 m/s².
  4. Find T: T = m1(g + a) = 4.0(9.81 + 1.96) = 47.1 N.
  5. Check: T = m2(g − a) = 6.0(9.81 − 1.96) = 47.1 N.

12.9 The Normal Force

The normal force is the contact force a surface exerts on an object perpendicular to the surface, preventing the object from passing through. “Normal” means perpendicular. It is always directed away from the surface and into the object.

The normal force is a constraint force: it takes whatever value is needed to keep the object on the surface. On a horizontal surface with only gravity and the normal force acting vertically, N = mg by Newton’s second law. In other situations, N takes a different value.

The normal force arises from the electromagnetic repulsion between the electron clouds of the surface atoms and the object’s atoms. When you push two surfaces together, their electron clouds resist interpenetration. The macroscopic normal force is the sum of billions of these repulsive interactions.

12.10 Why Normal Force Is Not Always Equal to Weight

The common shortcut “N = mg” applies only in the specific case of a horizontal surface with no other vertical forces and zero vertical acceleration. In every other case, N must be calculated from Newton’s second law applied to the perpendicular direction.

Table 12.4 Normal force in common situations.
Situation Normal Force Why N ≠ mg
Object at rest on horizontal surface N = mg This is the one case where they are equal.
Object on ramp at angle θ N = mg cos θ Gravity’s perpendicular component is less than mg.
Elevator accelerating upward at a N = m(g + a) Must also provide upward acceleration.
Elevator accelerating downward at a N = m(g − a) Net force partially uses weight; less support needed.
Rope pulls upward on object on surface N = mg − T sin θ Rope partially lifts object; surface need not support full weight.
Additional downward force N = mg + F Extra force adds to what surface must support.

Worked Example 12.3 — Normal Force on an Incline

Problem: A 5.0 kg box sits on a 35° frictionless ramp. Find the normal force.

  1. Resolve gravity perpendicular to ramp: mg cos 35° = 5.0 × 9.81 × 0.819 = 40.2 N.
  2. Perpendicular equilibrium: N − mg cos 35° = 0 → N = 40.2 N.
  3. Check: N = 40.2 N < mg = 49.1 N, as expected on a ramp.

Worked Example 12.4 — Normal Force in an Accelerating Elevator

Problem: A 70.0 kg person rides an elevator accelerating upward at 2.5 m/s². What does the scale read?

  1. FBD: N upward and W = 686.7 N downward.
  2. Newton’s second law, upward positive: N − W = ma → N = W + ma = 686.7 + 70.0 × 2.5 = 861.7 N.
  3. Interpretation: the scale reads 861.7 N, about 87.9 kg-force. The person feels heavier because the elevator must both support their weight and provide upward acceleration.

12.11 Spring Forces and Elastic Deformation

Many materials deform elastically: when deformed, they exert a restoring force that tends to return them to their original shape. Coil springs, rubber bands, and even steel rods behave this way within their elastic limits. The restoring force model for springs, derived from experiment, is one of the most useful relationships in mechanics.

Elastic deformation is reversible: remove the applied force and the material returns to its original shape. Beyond the elastic limit, or yield point, permanent plastic deformation occurs. The spring models in this chapter assume elastic deformation throughout.

Springs appear throughout physics: in mechanical devices, seismometers, shock absorbers, atomic models of crystalline solids, and as conceptual models for chemical bonds. The simple linear spring is the prototype for a vast class of oscillatory and restoring-force phenomena.

12.12 Hooke’s Law

Hooke’s Law states that for small deformations within the elastic limit, the restoring force exerted by a spring is proportional to the displacement from equilibrium and directed opposite to that displacement.

Fspring = −kx

In most mechanics problems, we specify the direction explicitly rather than relying on the sign. The rule is: a stretched spring pulls the object back toward equilibrium; a compressed spring pushes the object back toward equilibrium. Either way, the force magnitude is k|x| and the direction is toward equilibrium.

Table 12.5 Spring state, displacement, force, and direction.
Spring State Displacement x Spring Force on Object Direction
Natural length (equilibrium) 0 0 No force
Stretched by x x > 0 F = kx Toward equilibrium; restoring, pulls in
Compressed by x x < 0, or |x| F = k|x| Toward equilibrium; restoring, pushes out

The spring constant k characterizes the stiffness of a spring: a large k means a stiff spring that requires a large force for a small deformation. A small k means a soft spring easily deformed by small forces.

Table 12.6 Typical spring constants.
Spring / Elastic Object Typical k (N/m)
Car suspension spring20,000–50,000
Garage door spring2,000–4,000
Retractable pen spring20–100
Lab physics spring10–200
Soft rubber band30–150
C—C bond (molecular)~400,000

Worked Example 12.5 — Hooke’s Law Calculation

Problem: A spring with k = 250 N/m is compressed by 8.0 cm. What force does it exert?

  1. Convert: x = 8.0 cm = 0.080 m.
  2. Apply Hooke’s Law: F = kx = 250 × 0.080 = 20.0 N.
  3. Direction: the spring is compressed, so it pushes outward — away from equilibrium in the direction the object would move if released.

Worked Example 12.6 — Finding k from Data

Problem: A spring stretches 12.0 cm when a 3.0 kg mass hangs from it in equilibrium. Find k.

  1. FBD of mass: T = kx upward; W = mg downward. In equilibrium: T = W.
  2. Apply: k × 0.120 = 3.0 × 9.81 = 29.43 N.
  3. Solve: k = 29.43 / 0.120 = 245 N/m.

Bridge Note: Springs become a foundation for oscillations, waves, molecular bonding, and many forms of energy storage. Hooke’s Law is one of the first examples of a linear restoring force model.

12.13 Combining Multiple Forces in One System

Most real problems involve several force types simultaneously. The approach is always the same: draw the FBD for each object, identify the magnitude rule for each force (N = mg cos θ, f = μN, T from constraint, Fspring = kx), then apply Newton’s second law.

The key discipline is to derive each force from its model rather than guessing. Normal force comes from the perpendicular equilibrium condition. Friction comes from the coefficient times the normal force. Tension comes from the constraint equation. Spring force comes from Hooke’s Law.

Worked Example 12.7 — Block on a Rough Incline with a Spring

Problem: A 4.0 kg block rests on a 25° rough incline (μs = 0.35) connected to a horizontal spring (k = 200 N/m). How much must the spring stretch to hold the block in equilibrium?

  1. FBD: along slope, positive up: spring component + static friction − mg sin 25° = 0.
  2. Perpendicular to slope: N = mg cos 25° = 4.0 × 9.81 × 0.906 = 35.5 N.
  3. Maximum static friction: fs ≤ μsN = 0.35 × 35.5 = 12.4 N, acting up slope if the block tends to slide down.
  4. Gravity along slope: mg sin 25° = 4.0 × 9.81 × 0.423 = 16.6 N, down slope.
  5. Spring needed: Fspring = mg sin 25° − fs = 16.6 − 12.4 = 4.2 N, minimum needed.
  6. Stretch: x = F/k = 4.2 / 200 = 0.021 m = 2.1 cm.

12.14 Solving Multi-Force Newton’s Law Problems

A systematic method prevents errors when multiple force types interact.

The Six-Step Method

  1. Draw the FBD for each object. Label every force with its type and symbol.
  2. Choose a coordinate system aligned with the acceleration direction where possible.
  3. Identify the magnitude rule for every force: N from perpendicular equilibrium, f = μN, T from constraint, Fspring = kx.
  4. Write Newton’s second law for each direction: ΣFx = max and ΣFy = may.
  5. Solve the system of equations for the unknowns, using algebra before numbers.
  6. Check units, signs, and reasonableness.

Worked Example 12.8 — Box on Rough Horizontal Surface with Rope at Angle

Problem: A 12.0 kg box is pulled by a rope at 30° above horizontal with tension T = 65.0 N. μk = 0.25. Find the acceleration.

  1. FBD: W = 117.7 N down; N up; T at 30°; fk to the left.
  2. y-direction: N + T sin 30° − W = 0 → N = 117.7 − 65.0 × 0.500 = 85.2 N.
  3. Friction: fk = μkN = 0.25 × 85.2 = 21.3 N.
  4. x-direction: T cos 30° − fk = ma → 65.0 × 0.866 − 21.3 = 12.0 × a.
  5. Solve: 56.3 − 21.3 = 12.0a → a = 35.0 / 12.0 = 2.92 m/s².

12.15 Common Mistakes in Force Modeling

Mistake 1: Using N = mg when the situation does not warrant it

On an incline, N = mg cos θ. In an elevator, N ≠ mg. With an angled applied force, N changes. Derive N from Newton’s second law in the perpendicular direction every time.

Mistake 2: Using maximum static friction in equilibrium problems

fs = μsN only when the object is on the verge of sliding, or impending motion. In general, fs is whatever is needed for equilibrium, up to the maximum. If the problem says the object is in equilibrium, find fs from the equilibrium condition, then check that it is ≤ μsN.

Mistake 3: Applying kinetic friction when the object is at rest

Static friction acts on stationary objects. Kinetic friction acts on sliding objects. Determine whether the object is moving first, then choose the appropriate friction model.

Mistake 4: Forgetting that tension changes direction across a pulley

For a massless, frictionless pulley, tension magnitude is the same on both sides. Only the direction changes. If the pulley has mass or friction, the tension differs on each side — a more advanced situation not covered in this chapter.

Mistake 5: Forgetting the minus sign in Hooke’s Law when setting up equations

Fspring = −kx means the force is opposite to the displacement. When setting up Newton’s second law, the spring force acts toward equilibrium. Draw it in the correct direction on the FBD before writing the equation.

Mistake 6: Confusing spring stretch with spring displacement

x in Hooke’s Law is the displacement from the natural, or equilibrium, length. If a spring has natural length 20 cm and is stretched to 28 cm, then x = 8 cm = 0.08 m. Do not use the total length.

Common Mistake: The most common multi-force error is finding friction before finding the normal force. Friction = μN requires N to be known first. Always solve the perpendicular direction for N before using it to compute friction.

Chapter Summary

Specific force models link force magnitudes to measurable quantities. The four major contact and constraint forces in mechanics — friction, tension, normal force, and spring force — each obey a quantitative rule.

  • Static friction: fs ≤ μsN; equals applied force up to maximum; opposes tendency to slide.
  • Kinetic friction: fk = μkN; fixed magnitude for given surfaces; opposes sliding direction.
  • μk < μs for the same surface pair; starting sliding requires more force than maintaining it.
  • Tension in an ideal rope has the same magnitude throughout and acts along the rope, pulling toward the attachment.
  • An ideal pulley redirects tension without changing its magnitude.
  • Normal force is perpendicular to the surface and away from it. N = mg only on horizontal surfaces with zero vertical acceleration and no other vertical forces. Derive N from Newton’s second law.
  • Spring force follows Hooke’s Law: F = kx toward equilibrium; k is in N/m; x is displacement from natural length.
  • To solve multi-force problems: draw the FBD, find N first from perpendicular equilibrium, compute friction, then apply Fnet = ma.

Key Terms

static friction
The friction force on a stationary object that prevents it from sliding; variable, ranging from 0 to μsN; direction opposes tendency to slide.
kinetic friction
The friction force on a sliding object; fixed magnitude fk = μkN; direction opposes sliding.
coefficient of static friction (μs)
Dimensionless ratio characterizing maximum static friction relative to normal force for a surface pair; μs ≥ μk.
coefficient of kinetic friction (μk)
Dimensionless ratio characterizing kinetic friction relative to normal force for a surface pair; always less than μs.
tension
The pulling force transmitted through a rope or cable; acts along the rope toward the attachment; cannot push.
ideal rope
A massless, inextensible rope; transmits tension unchanged in magnitude throughout its length.
ideal pulley
A massless, frictionless pulley; redirects the rope without changing the tension magnitude.
normal force
The contact force perpendicular to and away from a surface; a constraint force that prevents penetration; not always equal to weight.
spring constant (k)
The stiffness of a spring in N/m; larger k means stiffer spring; determined experimentally.
Hooke’s Law
The linear relationship between spring force and displacement: F = −kx, a restoring force toward equilibrium.
elastic limit
The maximum deformation beyond which a material no longer returns to its original shape; permanent deformation occurs and Hooke’s Law fails.
Atwood machine
A simple pulley system with two masses hanging from a single rope over a pulley; used to study tension and Newton’s second law.

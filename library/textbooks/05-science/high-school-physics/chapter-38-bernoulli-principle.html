Chapter 38: Bernoulli's Principle

38.1 Why Fluid Speed and Pressure Are Connected

Consider a horizontal pipe that narrows. Chapter 37 showed that by continuity (A₁v₁ = A₂v₂), the fluid must speed up in the narrow section. What causes this acceleration? The fluid element entering the narrow section must be pushed from behind and not fully supported from in front: the pressure behind it (upstream) must be greater than the pressure ahead of it (downstream, in the narrow section). The pressure drops wherever the fluid accelerates. This is Newton’s second law for a fluid: a fluid parcel accelerates only if there is a net pressure force acting on it. Acceleration requires a pressure gradient in the direction of acceleration. When a fluid speeds up, pressure decreases in the direction of flow. When it slows down, pressure increases in the direction of flow. Bernoulli’s equation makes this precise by tracking the energy of a fluid element as it moves along a streamline. The three forms of energy — pressure energy (stored in the compressed fluid), kinetic energy (due to motion), and gravitational potential energy (due to elevation) — trade off with each other, with their sum remaining constant Physics: Foundations of Physical Science Chapter 38 — Bernoulli's Principle along a streamline in ideal flow.

38.2 Review of Continuity in Fluid Flow

Before deriving Bernoulli’s equation, recall the continuity result from Chapter 37: for

steady, incompressible flow, the volume flow rate Q = Av is constant along any

unbranched streamtube.

A₁ v₁ = A₂ v₂

Continuity tells us how speed changes with geometry. Bernoulli’s equation will tell us how pressure changes with speed and elevation. The two equations together allow complete analysis of ideal fluid flow: given the geometry and flow conditions at one point, both speed and pressure at any other point along the streamline can be determined.

38.3 Energy in Moving Fluids

Consider a small parcel of fluid with volume ΔV, density ρ, and mass Δm = ρΔV,

moving along a streamline. The fluid parcel possesses three forms of energy:

Kinetic energy: KE = ½(Δm)v² = ½ρΔV v². A fluid parcel moving at speed v has kinetic

energy proportional to the square of its speed.

Gravitational potential energy: PE = (Δm)gh = ρΔV g h. A fluid parcel at elevation h has

gravitational potential energy proportional to h. Pressure energy (flow work): The pressure in a fluid represents the ability of the fluid to do work by pushing against other fluid parcels. The work done by the fluid at pressure P to push the parcel through area A a distance Δx is P × A × Δx = PΔV. This is often called flow work or pressure energy per unit volume.

38.4 Pressure Energy, Kinetic Energy, and Gravitational Potential Energy

The derivation of Bernoulli’s equation follows from applying the work-energy theorem to a fluid parcel moving along a streamline. Imagine tracking a small parcel from position 1 (wide, lower section) to position 2 (narrow, higher section). The work done by pressure forces on the parcel as it moves equals the change in its kinetic plus gravitational potential energy. At position 1, the pressure P₁ pushes the parcel forward; at position 2, the pressure P₂ pushes it backward (opposing further entry into the narrow section). Working through the algebra of force times displacement and using the incompressibility condition (so

that the volume ΔV of the parcel is conserved), one obtains:

P₁ + ½ρv₁² + ρgh₁ = P₂ + ½ρv₂² + ρgh₂

38.5 Bernoulli's Principle

The Rule:

Bernoulli's Principle: In steady, incompressible, frictionless (ideal) flow, the sum of static pressure, dynamic pressure, and hydrostatic pressure is constant along a streamline. Where flow speed is high, static pressure is low; where flow speed is low, static pressure is high. This is the qualitative statement. The quantitative form is Bernoulli's equation (Section Physics: Foundations of Physical Science Chapter 38 — Bernoulli's Principle 38.6). The principle is most strikingly illustrated in horizontal flow (h constant), where it reduces to: higher speed →lower pressure; lower speed →higher pressure. This is the form most often quoted and most often misapplied. The key word is ‘along a streamline.’ Bernoulli's equation is a statement about the energy of a fluid parcel as it travels its path. It does not compare pressures at different elevations in a static fluid (that is the hydrostatic equation, Chapter 35). It does not compare pressures on different streamlines unless the flow is irrotational (a more advanced concept). Applied within its scope, it is exact for ideal flow.

38.7 Fast Flow and Low Pressure

The most quoted form of Bernoulli’s principle — fast flow has low pressure — is the

horizontal (constant h) special case. Setting h₁ = h₂ and rearranging:

P₁ −P₂ = ½ρ(v₂² −v₁²)

This explains the operation of many devices. A Venturi meter (Section 38.10) constricts a pipe to speed up flow and measures the resulting pressure drop to infer flow rate. An atomiser nozzle blows air over the top of a liquid-filled tube; the fast air creates low pressure at the tube opening, and atmospheric pressure in the reservoir pushes liquid up and out. Carburettors work the same way — air speeds through a venturi throat, pressure drops, and fuel is drawn in from a bowl maintained at atmospheric pressure.

The pressure ½ρv² is called the dynamic pressure or velocity pressure. It represents the

kinetic energy per unit volume of the flowing fluid and is the pressure associated with the directed motion of the fluid. It is always additive to static pressure when the fluid is brought to rest (stagnated), which is the basis of Pitot tube speed measurement.

38.8 Flow Through Narrow Openings: Torricelli's Theorem

Consider a large open tank with a small opening (orifice) in its side at a depth h below the surface. What is the speed of water flowing out? Apply Bernoulli’s equation from the surface (point 1) to the orifice (point 2). At the surface, the pressure is P₀ (atmospheric) and the speed is approximately zero (large

tank). At the orifice, the pressure is also P₀ (open to atmosphere). Setting h₂ = 0 (orifice

as datum) and h₁ = h:

P₀ + 0 + ρgh = P₀ + ½ρv² + 0

v = √(2gh)

surface. v = √(2 × 9.81 × 3.0) = √58.86 = 7.67 m/s. If the hole has area 0.5 cm² = 5 × Physics: Foundations of Physical Science Chapter 38 — Bernoulli's Principle 10 ⁵ m², the flow rate is Q = Av = 5 × 10 ⁵ × 7.67 = 3.84 × 10 ⁴ m³/s = 0.384 L/s.

Historical Note:

Evangelista Torricelli (1608–1647), a student of Galileo, discovered this result and also invented the mercury barometer. He correctly identified that atmospheric pressure supports a column of mercury about 76 cm tall, and by analogy recognised that air has weight and exerts pressure. Torricelli ’s theorem was one of the first quantitative results in fluid dynamics and predates Newton’s mechanics by several decades.

38.9 Lift and Airfoils

An airfoil (the cross-sectional shape of a wing) is designed so that air flows faster over the upper curved surface than over the lower flatter surface. By Bernoulli’s principle, faster flow over the top creates lower pressure on the upper surface than on the lower surface. The pressure difference produces a net upward force — lift. The lift force per unit span on an airfoil is given (in ideal flow) by the Kutta-Joukowski theorem, which involves the flow speed and the circulation (a measure of the airfoil’s tendency to induce rotation in the flow). For a first approximation: if the pressure

above the wing is P_upper and below is P_lower, and the wing area is A_wing:

F_lift = (P_lower −P_upper) × A_wing

A common misconception is that lift arises purely because the upper path is longer and air must “meet up” with air taking the lower path (the “equal transit time” fallacy). This is incorrect: air over the upper surface does travel faster, but not because of equal transit times. The correct explanation involves the angle of attack (the angle between the wing chord and the oncoming airflow), the wing’s camber (curvature), and the Kutta condition (that the flow must leave smoothly from the trailing edge). Bernoulli’s equation correctly relates the speed increase to the pressure decrease; what requires more careful analysis is why the flow speeds up. Lift also depends on angle of attack: tilting the wing leading edge upward increases lift (up to the stall angle, beyond which the flow separates from the upper surface and lift collapses dramatically). This is why aircraft rotate (pitch up) on takeoff — increasing angle of attack to generate sufficient lift at the available speed. Key Point:

Aerodynamic lift is real and quantitatively described by Bernoulli’s principle (for the pressure-speed relationship) combined with circulation theory (for the explanation of why the speed difference arises). The "equal transit time" explanation commonly taught at elementary level is incorrect and should be discarded. Air over the upper surface arrives at the trailing edge before air over the lower surface, not at the same time.

38.10 Venturi Effect

The Venturi effect is the pressure drop that occurs when a fluid is forced through a constricted section of pipe. It is named after Giovanni Battista Venturi (1746–1822), who demonstrated the effect experimentally. The Venturi meter exploits this pressure drop to measure flow rate. In a Venturi meter, a fluid flows through a converging section (reducing area), a throat (minimum area), and a diverging section (expanding area). By continuity, flow speed is highest at the throat. By Bernoulli’s equation (horizontal flow), pressure is lowest at the throat. The pressure difference between the inlet and the throat is measured (typically with a U-tube manometer or a differential pressure transducer). This pressure difference can be directly related to the flow rate:

ΔP = P₁ −P₂ = ½ρ(v₂² −v₁²)

Q = A₁ A₂ √(2ΔP / (ρ(A₁² −A₂²)))

38.11 Applications in Medicine, Engineering, and Weather

Bernoulli’s principle appears across many fields wherever fluids flow through varying geometries. Medicine: The Bernoulli effect in blood flow is clinically important. Atherosclerotic plaques narrow arterial cross-sections, increasing blood flow speed through the constriction (by continuity) and decreasing pressure (by Bernoulli). The reduced pressure can cause the artery walls to be drawn inward, potentially worsening the narrowing — a positive feedback effect called the Venturi effect in haemodynamics.

Echocardiography uses the modified Bernoulli equation (ΔP = 4v², where v is the peak

flow speed in m/s and ΔP is in mmHg) to non-invasively estimate pressure gradients

across narrowed heart valves. Engineering: Carburettors mix fuel and air using a Venturi throat. Aircraft Pitot-static systems measure airspeed using the stagnation pressure at a forward-facing orifice

minus the static pressure at a side-facing orifice: ΔP = ½ρv², giving v = √(2ΔP/ρ).

Injectors in diesel engines use high-speed fuel jets to entrain (suck in) air. Ejector pumps in submarines and industrial equipment use fast jets to draw in and move surrounding fluid with no moving parts. Weather: Pressure variations in the atmosphere are driven partly by Bernoulli effects. Wind blowing over a hill speeds up over the crest (reduced cross-sectional area of the air column above terrain), creating a pressure drop. In extreme cases (mountain passes, building corners), localised high-speed flow and low pressure can strip roofs, overturn vehicles, or accelerate erosion. The Venturi effect between closely spaced buildings creates dangerous wind corridors in urban environments — a key consideration in architectural fluid dynamics.

38.12 Limits of Bernoulli's Principle

Bernoulli's equation is derived under four assumptions: steady flow, incompressible flow, inviscid (frictionless) flow, and application along a single streamline. Each assumption fails in certain situations: Unsteady flow: When flow conditions change with time (pumps starting, valves closing, pulsatile blood flow), the steady-flow Bernoulli equation is inapplicable. The unsteady Bernoulli equation includes an additional term involving the time derivative of velocity, which accounts for the energy stored in accelerating the fluid mass. Compressibility: At high speeds (Mach > 0.3 for gases), density changes significantly with pressure. Bernoulli's equation for compressible flow has a more complex pressure- Physics: Foundations of Physical Science Chapter 38 — Bernoulli's Principle velocity relationship, and at supersonic speeds, shock waves create sudden pressure jumps that the equation cannot describe at all. Viscosity and friction: Real fluids lose energy to viscous friction, especially near walls (boundary layers) and in turbulent eddies. This energy is not recovered — it is converted to heat. A more general energy equation includes a loss term (head loss) that accounts for friction. In a long pipe, most of the pressure drop is due to friction, not to Bernoulli effects. Ignoring friction in a long pipe and applying Bernoulli gives dramatically incorrect results. Different streamlines: Bernoulli's equation relates conditions on the same streamline. In rotational flows (where fluid elements spin as they move), conditions on different streamlines cannot be compared using Bernoulli's equation without additional assumptions. In irrotational flows (common around streamlined bodies at high Reynolds number), the Bernoulli constant is the same on all streamlines, and inter- streamline comparison is valid.

Common Mistake:

Do NOT apply Bernoulli's equation: (1) across a pump, fan, or turbine (which add or remove energy from the flow), (2) when significant friction losses occur (long pipes, rough surfaces, sharp bends), (3) at supersonic conditions, or (4) to compare points on different streamlines in rotational flow. In all these cases, Bernoulli gives the wrong answer.

38.13 Common Mistakes in Bernoulli Problems

• Applying Bernoulli across a pump or turbine. Bernoulli conserves energy along a streamline; pumps and turbines add or remove energy. If the flow path includes

a pump, use the extended Bernoulli equation: P₁ + ½ρv₁² + ρgh₁ + W_pump =

P₂ + ½ρv₂² + ρgh₂ + W_losses.

• Using gauge pressure in Bernoulli's equation when absolute pressure is needed. Bernoulli's equation is consistent whether you use absolute or gauge pressure, as long as you are consistent: the atmospheric P₀ appears on both sides and cancels. The mistake is mixing absolute pressure at one point with gauge pressure at another. Use one system throughout. •

Forgetting to include the elevation term ρgh when height changes are

significant. For water at height changes of more than ∼0.5 m, the gravitational term is comparable to typical dynamic pressure changes and cannot be neglected. • Not applying continuity first. Bernoulli’s equation has two unknowns (P and v) at the second point if the speed is not known. Continuity gives the speed. Physics: Foundations of Physical Science Chapter 38 — Bernoulli's Principle Forgetting to use continuity before Bernoulli leads to under-determined problems. •

Using the wrong fluid density. Bernoulli's ρ is the flowing fluid density, not the

density of a manometer fluid used to measure the pressure difference. If a

mercury manometer measures ΔP, use ρ_Hg g h to find ΔP, then use this ΔP in

Bernoulli with ρ_water (or whichever fluid is flowing).

• Confusing total pressure with static pressure. Static pressure P is the pressure measured by a gauge moving with the fluid (or a side-facing orifice). Total

(stagnation) pressure is P_total = P + ½ρv², measured at a point where the flow

is brought to rest. Bernoulli’s equation says total pressure is constant along a streamline (in horizontal flow with no elevation change). A Pitot tube measures stagnation pressure; a static port measures static pressure; their difference is the dynamic pressure.

Common Mistake:

Bernoulli's equation is a statement about energy conservation in ideal flow. It is not a magical rule that 'fast flow always causes low pressure everywhere.' The pressure reduction requires the flow to be along the same streamline, with no energy additions, subtractions, or losses. Always check whether the ideal flow conditions are met before applying Bernoulli. Physics: Foundations of Physical Science Chapter 38 — Bernoulli's Principle

Momentum and Impulse

16.1 Why Momentum Matters

Momentum is a conserved quantity: the total momentum of an isolated system never changes, no matter how complex the internal interactions. This makes it a powerful tool for analyzing collisions, explosions, and interactions where forces are too brief or too variable to treat with Newton’s laws directly.

The work–energy theorem connects force and displacement to changes in kinetic energy. Momentum connects force and time to changes in motion. Together, these two perspectives — energy and momentum — give a complete picture of how interactions transfer both energy and motion between objects.

Momentum is particularly valuable when the collision time is very short and forces are not known precisely; when we know initial and final states but not the details of the interaction; and when multiple objects interact and we want to track the combined behavior of the system without analyzing every internal force.

Historical Note

René Descartes and Gottfried Leibniz debated whether the correct conserved quantity of motion was mv, momentum, or mv2, what we now call twice the kinetic energy. Both are conserved, but under different conditions. Momentum is conserved in all closed-system interactions; kinetic energy is conserved only in elastic collisions.

16.2 Defining Linear Momentum

The linear momentum of an object is the product of its mass and its velocity:

p = mv

The SI unit of momentum is kg·m/s, which is equivalent to N·s. Both are correct and used in different contexts.

Newton’s second law can be restated in terms of momentum. The net force on an object equals the rate of change of its momentum:

Fnet = Δp / Δt

Bridge Note

In the advanced series, Newton’s second law is always written F = dp/dt. This more general form remains valid even when mass changes, such as in rockets, and forms the foundation for relativistic mechanics.

16.3 Momentum as a Vector Quantity

Momentum is a vector: it has both magnitude and direction. The direction of momentum is the same as the direction of velocity. In one-dimensional problems, positive and negative signs encode direction. In two-dimensional problems, momentum must be analyzed by components.

The total momentum of a system of objects is the vector sum of the momenta of all individual objects:

ptotal = p1 + p2 + p3 + … = m1v1 + m2v2 + …

When two objects move in opposite directions, their momenta partially or fully cancel. When they move in the same direction, their momenta add. This vector nature is crucial for understanding collisions and conservation of momentum.

Example — Vector Nature of Momentum

Car A, with mass 1000 kg, moves east at 20.0 m/s: pA = +20,000 kg·m/s. Car B, with mass 800 kg, moves west at 25.0 m/s: pB = −20,000 kg·m/s, taking east as positive. Total momentum = 20,000 + (−20,000) = 0 kg·m/s. The system has zero total momentum even though both cars are moving.

Common Mistake

When adding momenta of objects moving in opposite directions, assign signs carefully. Momentum is a vector; magnitudes do not add. An object moving east with p = 500 kg·m/s and an object moving west with p = 500 kg·m/s have a combined momentum of zero, not 1000 kg·m/s.

16.4 Impulse and Change in Momentum

When a net force acts on an object for a time interval Δt, the object’s momentum changes. The quantity that measures this change is called impulse.

Impulse is defined as the product of the average net force and the time interval over which it acts:

J = Favg Δt

Impulse is particularly useful when the force is large and brief, as in a collision, making the average force a more practical quantity than the instantaneous force history.

Graphically, impulse is the area under a force-time graph. For a constant force, this is simply F × Δt. For a variable force, the area must be estimated.

Key Point

The units of impulse, N·s, and momentum, kg·m/s, are equivalent: 1 N·s = 1 kg·m/s. This reflects the connection between impulse and momentum change.

16.5 The Impulse–Momentum Theorem

The impulse–momentum theorem states that the impulse acting on an object equals its change in momentum:

J = Δp = pf − pi = mvf − mvi

This theorem follows directly from Newton’s second law. It is useful because it connects observable quantities, such as initial and final velocities or force and time, without requiring knowledge of acceleration at every instant.

Worked Example 16.1 — Finding Impulse from Velocity Change

A 0.145 kg baseball traveling at −35.0 m/s is hit and leaves at +50.0 m/s. Find the impulse on the ball.

  1. Initial momentum: pi = 0.145 × (−35.0) = −5.075 kg·m/s.
  2. Final momentum: pf = 0.145 × (+50.0) = +7.250 kg·m/s.
  3. Impulse: J = Δp = pf − pi = 7.250 − (−5.075) = +12.3 N·s.
  4. The bat delivered 12.3 N·s of impulse in the direction the ball was hit.

Worked Example 16.2 — Finding Average Force from Impulse

If the ball-bat contact lasted 1.20 ms, then Favg = J/Δt = 12.3 / 0.00120 = 10,250 N ≈ 10.3 kN.

16.6 Force-Time Relationships

In a real collision, the force is not constant but varies with time: it rises from zero to a peak value and then falls back to zero as the objects deform and separate. The impulse is the area under this force-time curve.

For a given impulse, there is a trade-off between force and time: a larger contact time results in a smaller average force, and vice versa.

16.7 Why Increasing Collision Time Reduces Force

The impulse–momentum theorem J = Favg Δt = Δp shows that for a fixed change in momentum, force and time are inversely related.

  • Airbags extend the time over which a driver’s momentum changes during a crash.
  • Gymnasts and martial artists bend their knees on landing to increase contact time.
  • Crumple zones in cars deform progressively, increasing crash duration.
  • Catching a ball with soft hands extends the stopping time and reduces force.
  • Foam padding, helmets, and shock-absorbing soles all work by increasing Δt for a given Δp.

Example — Airbag Calculation

A 70.0 kg driver decelerates from 15.0 m/s to 0. Δp = 70.0 × 15.0 = 1050 kg·m/s. Without an airbag, with Δt = 0.005 s, F = 210,000 N. With an airbag, with Δt = 0.050 s, F = 21,000 N. The airbag reduces the force by a factor of 10.

16.8 Momentum in Isolated Systems

A system is isolated, or closed, if no net external force acts on it. In an isolated system, the total momentum is constant, regardless of what happens inside the system.

Internal forces are forces between objects within the system; they always come in Newton’s third law pairs and cancel when summed over the whole system. External forces are forces from outside the system; they change the total momentum.

ΣFexternal = 0 ⇒ ptotal = constant

The Rule

To use conservation of momentum: define the system to include all interacting objects; check that no net external force acts on the system, or that external forces are negligible during the interaction; then apply ptotal before = ptotal after.

Key Point

In most collision problems, the collision time is so short that external forces have negligible impulse compared with the internal collision forces. We treat the system as isolated during the collision itself.

16.9 Conservation of Momentum

In an isolated system, the total momentum before an interaction equals the total momentum after the interaction:

ptotal,i = ptotal,f

m1v1i + m2v2i = m1v1f + m2v2f

Conservation of momentum is one of the most fundamental laws in physics. It holds in classical mechanics, quantum mechanics, special relativity, and nuclear physics.

Worked Example 16.3 — Two Objects Colliding in One Dimension

A 2.0 kg ball moving at +4.0 m/s collides with a 3.0 kg ball at rest. After the collision, the 2.0 kg ball moves at +1.0 m/s. Find the velocity of the 3.0 kg ball.

  1. Total initial momentum: pi = 2.0(4.0) + 3.0(0) = 8.0 kg·m/s.
  2. Total final momentum: pf = 2.0(1.0) + 3.0vf = 2.0 + 3.0vf.
  3. Conservation: 8.0 = 2.0 + 3.0vf, so vf = +2.0 m/s.

Worked Example 16.4 — Perfectly Inelastic Collision

A 1500 kg car moving at 20.0 m/s east collides with a 2000 kg van at rest. They lock together. Initial momentum is 30,000 kg·m/s. Final momentum is (1500 + 2000)vf = 3500vf. Thus vf = 30,000 / 3500 = +8.57 m/s east.

16.10 Recoil and Propulsion

When two objects that are initially at rest push apart, momentum is conserved. The total momentum before was zero, so the total momentum after must also be zero. The momenta of the two objects are equal in magnitude and opposite in direction.

m1v1 + m2v2 = 0 ⇒ m1v1 = −m2v2

This is the principle behind recoil, rocket propulsion, and jet propulsion. The lighter object acquires greater speed, but the momenta are equal and opposite.

Example — Gun Recoil

A 0.010 kg bullet is fired at 900 m/s from a 3.0 kg rifle initially at rest. Conservation of momentum gives 0 = 0.010(900) + 3.0vrifle, so vrifle = −3.0 m/s. The rifle recoils in the opposite direction.

Example — Rocket Propulsion

A rocket in space, with mass 500 kg and initially at rest, expels 10.0 kg of exhaust at 2000 m/s backward. Conservation of momentum gives 0 = (−10.0)(2000) + 490vrocket, so vrocket = +40.8 m/s forward.

16.11 Explosions and Separation Events

An explosion is the reverse of a perfectly inelastic collision: a single object separates into multiple parts, with internal energy converted to kinetic energy. Momentum is still conserved. If the system starts at rest, the total final momentum is zero.

Worked Example 16.5 — Explosion / Separation

A 5.0 kg cart at rest explodes into a 2.0 kg piece moving at +12.0 m/s and a 3.0 kg piece. Initial momentum is zero. Final momentum is 2.0(+12.0) + 3.0v = 24.0 + 3.0v. Conservation gives 0 = 24.0 + 3.0v, so v = −8.0 m/s.

In an explosion, kinetic energy increases because internal energy is converted to kinetic energy. Total momentum is conserved, but kinetic energy is not.

16.12 Common Mistakes in Momentum Analysis

Mistake 1: Forgetting Momentum Is a Vector

Objects moving in opposite directions have momenta of opposite signs. Total momentum is an algebraic sum, not a sum of magnitudes.

Mistake 2: Not Checking Whether the System Is Isolated

Conservation of momentum applies only when the net external force is zero, or when external impulses are negligible during a short interaction.

Mistake 3: Confusing Impulse with Force

Impulse is J = FΔt, not force. A small force acting for a long time can have the same impulse as a large force acting briefly.

Mistake 4: Assuming Kinetic Energy Is Conserved in All Collisions

Kinetic energy is conserved only in perfectly elastic collisions. Momentum is conserved for isolated systems.

Mistake 5: Using Speed Instead of Velocity

Momentum is a vector, so use signed velocity values. Direction must be included.

Mistake 6: Treating a Perfectly Inelastic Final State as Two Separate Objects

When objects stick together, they move as one combined mass with one final velocity: pf = (m1 + m2)vf.

Common Mistake

Sign errors are the most common source of wrong answers in momentum problems. Draw the situation, label positive and negative directions, assign signed velocities, and carry signs through every calculation.

Chapter Summary

Momentum is the product of mass and velocity: p = mv. It is a conserved vector quantity in isolated systems. The impulse–momentum theorem connects force, time, and momentum change.

  • Linear momentum: p = mv; SI unit kg·m/s = N·s; vector quantity.
  • Newton’s second law in momentum form: Fnet = Δp/Δt.
  • Impulse: J = FavgΔt = Δp; equals change in momentum and the area under an F-t graph.
  • For fixed Δp, longer collision time means smaller average force.
  • In an isolated system, total momentum before equals total momentum after.
  • Elastic collisions conserve both momentum and kinetic energy. Inelastic collisions conserve only momentum. Explosions conserve momentum but increase kinetic energy.

Key Terms

linear momentum
The product of an object’s mass and velocity: p = mv; a vector quantity.
impulse
The product of average net force and the time interval over which it acts: J = FavgΔt; equals change in momentum.
impulse–momentum theorem
The impulse acting on an object equals its change in momentum.
isolated system
A system on which the net external force is zero.
conservation of momentum
The total momentum of an isolated system remains constant.
elastic collision
A collision in which both momentum and kinetic energy are conserved.
inelastic collision
A collision in which momentum is conserved but kinetic energy decreases.
perfectly inelastic collision
An inelastic collision in which objects stick together and move with a common final velocity.
explosion
A separation event in which one object or system separates into parts; momentum is conserved but kinetic energy increases.
recoil
The backward motion of an object when it expels mass or exerts a force forward.

Sound Waves and Resonance

25.1 What Sound Is

Sound is a mechanical disturbance that propagates through a material medium by the transmission of energy via oscillations of the medium’s particles. It requires a material medium — air, water, steel, rock — and cannot travel through a vacuum. This was confirmed dramatically when Robert Boyle in 1660 showed that a bell ringing inside an evacuated glass vessel becomes inaudible as the air is pumped out.

What humans perceive as sound is a very small subset of all acoustic waves. The human auditory system detects pressure oscillations in air with frequencies from roughly 20 Hz to 20 000 Hz (20 kHz). Below 20 Hz is infrasound, inaudible to humans but produced by earthquakes, large machinery, and some animals (elephants, whales).

Above 20 kHz is ultrasound, used in medical imaging, sonar, and industrial cleaning.

Sound is ultimately the energy of a vibrating source (vocal cords, a speaker diaphragm, a vibrating string) transferred to the surrounding medium and propagated outward.

When these pressure oscillations reach the eardrum, they cause it to vibrate at the same frequencies, initiating the complex process of hearing.

Key Point: In space, no one can hear you scream — this is physically correct. Space is

essentially a vacuum, and sound cannot travel without a medium. Explosions in space Physics: Foundations of Physical Science films are cinematic fiction. Spacecraft can transmit radio waves (electromagnetic), but not sound.

25.2 Sound as a Longitudinal Mechanical Wave

Sound is a longitudinal mechanical wave. The medium’s particles (air molecules, for instance) oscillate back and forth parallel to the direction of sound propagation, not perpendicular to it. This distinguishes sound from waves on a string or from light, which are transverse.

When a loudspeaker cone moves forward, it pushes the air molecules immediately in front of it together, creating a local region of higher pressure and density called a compression. When the cone moves backward, it pulls the air molecules apart, creating a region of lower pressure and density called a rarefaction. The speaker alternates rapidly between these two motions, creating an alternating sequence of compressions and rarefactions that travels outward as a sound wave.

Each individual air molecule does not travel with the sound — it oscillates back and forth around its original position, displaced only a tiny distance from equilibrium (typically much less than 1 mm, even for loud sounds). The pattern of compressions and rarefactions is what travels. This is exactly the wave/matter distinction from Chapter 24 applied to sound.

The Rule:

Sound is longitudinal: medium particles oscillate parallel to the wave’s direction of travel. The compressions and rarefactions are the wave — the medium particles merely oscillate and return to equilibrium.

25.3 Compression and Rarefaction

A compression is a region in a longitudinal wave where the medium’s particles are pushed closer together than normal — a region of higher-than-normal pressure and density. A rarefaction is a region where particles are more spread out than normal — a region of lower-than-normal pressure and density.

In a sound wave in air, the pressure oscillates sinusoidally around atmospheric pressure.

The amplitude of this pressure variation is typically extremely small compared to atmospheric pressure. A sound at the threshold of hearing (0 dB) has a pressure amplitude of only 2 × 10 ⁵ Pa — about 2 × 10 ¹⁰ times atmospheric pressure. A sound that is painfully loud (120 dB) has a pressure amplitude of about 20 Pa — still only about 0.02% of atmospheric pressure. The ear is extraordinarily sensitive.

25.5 Frequency, Pitch, and Human Hearing

The frequency of a sound wave is the number of complete oscillation cycles per second, measured in hertz (Hz). Pitch is the subjective human perception of frequency. In general, higher frequency produces a higher perceived pitch. However, the relationship between frequency and pitch is not linear — pitch perception is logarithmic: doubling the frequency raises the pitch by one musical octave, regardless of the starting frequency.

The human auditory range spans from about 20 Hz (felt as much as heard) to about 20 000 Hz (a very high hiss). This range narrows with age; most adults over 40 cannot hear above 14 000–16 000 Hz. The ear is most sensitive in the range 1000–4000 Hz, which corresponds to the range of frequencies most important for speech intelligibility.

25.6 Amplitude, Intensity, and Loudness

The amplitude of a sound wave is the maximum pressure variation above and below atmospheric pressure. Intensity is the power per unit area of the wave, and it is proportional to the square of the amplitude, just as for all waves: I ∝A². The SI unit of intensity is watts per square meter (W/m²).

Loudness is the subjective human perception of intensity.

Like pitch, loudness perception is not linear in intensity — it is approximately logarithmic. An increase by a factor of 10 in intensity corresponds to roughly a doubling of perceived loudness (a very approximate rule, since loudness also depends on frequency).

25.8 Reflection and Echoes

Sound reflects from surfaces following the same law of reflection that governs all waves: the angle of reflection equals the angle of incidence. When the reflected sound reaches a listener separately from the direct sound, it is perceived as an echo.

An echo is distinguishable from the original sound when the reflected sound arrives more than about 0.1 seconds after the direct sound — because the auditory system

corresponds to a round-trip distance of 34.3 m, so an echo requires a reflecting surface at least 17 m away.

Reverberation is the persistence of sound in an enclosed space due to multiple reflections from walls, floor, and ceiling. Unlike an echo, reverberation is a blending of many closely-spaced reflections that prolongs the sound without producing distinct repeated events. The reverberation time (RT60) is the time for the sound intensity to drop by 60 dB after the source is turned off.

Reverberation time is critically important in architectural acoustics. Concert halls for orchestral music require relatively long reverberation times (1.8–2.2 s) that enhance Physics: Foundations of Physical Science richness and blend. Speech auditoria and recording studios require shorter times (0.4–0.8 s) for clarity and intelligibility. Churches and cathedrals often have very long reverberation times (4–8 s or more), creating the reverberant quality associated with sacred choral music.

25.9 Standing Sound Waves

Just as standing transverse waves can form on a string, standing longitudinal waves can form in air columns inside tubes. When sound reflects back and forth between the ends of a tube, the incident and reflected waves superimpose. At specific resonant frequencies, this produces a stable standing wave pattern.

The boundary conditions at the ends of the tube determine the resonant frequencies.

At a closed end, the air cannot move (the wall constrains it), creating a displacement node. At an open end, the air pressure is fixed at atmospheric pressure (the pressure wave meets the external air), creating a pressure node — equivalently, a displacement antinode.

25.10 Resonance in Air Columns and Strings

Resonance in acoustics is the dramatic increase in vibration amplitude when a system is driven at one of its natural frequencies.

For air columns and strings, the natural frequencies are the harmonic series derived in Section 25.9 and in Chapter 24.

A tuning fork held over a tube will cause the air column to resonate when the tube length is adjusted so that one of the column’s natural frequencies matches the fork’s frequency. This is a simple but precise method for measuring the speed of sound: measure the tube length L at resonance, note the frequency f of the fork, and apply v = fλ = f × 4L (for a closed-end tube at its fundamental).

Acoustic resonance shapes every musical instrument. The resonance of an instrument body amplifies specific frequencies from the vibrating source (string, reed, lips) and suppresses others.

The combination of amplified frequencies — the instrument’s resonance spectrum — determines its characteristic sound quality. This is why a violin and a viola playing the same note sound different: they have different bodies with different resonant characteristics.

Resonance in buildings and structures can be dangerous. Earthquakes produce seismic waves at frequencies of 0.1–10 Hz. Buildings of different heights have different natural frequencies — tall buildings resonate at lower frequencies than short ones. When the earthquake frequency matches the building’s natural frequency, resonance amplifies the oscillation and can cause collapse. The 1985 Mexico City earthquake was particularly devastating partly because it produced waves near 2 Hz, matching the natural frequency of medium-rise buildings (7–15 stories) common in the city.

25.12 Musical Instruments and Sound Production

Musical instruments are carefully engineered acoustic resonators. They transform the energy of a player’s input (bowing, blowing, striking) into sound by exciting and sustaining resonant oscillations at specific frequencies. Understanding the physics of instruments reveals why they are built as they are.

Tuned percussion (marimba, glockenspiel) is carefully shaped so that the first few resonant modes are in near-integer ratios, giving clear pitch.

25.13 Beats and Interference in Sound

When two sound waves of slightly different frequencies are superimposed, they alternate between constructive and destructive interference as their relative phase shifts cyclically.

The result is a periodic variation in amplitude called beats. The beat frequency is the difference between the two source frequencies:

guitar strings are tuned to 440 Hz and 443 Hz, the combined sound will throb 3 times per second.

Musicians use beats to tune instruments: when the beat frequency approaches zero, the two sources are converging on the same pitch. When beats disappear entirely, the instruments are in tune.

Beats also appear between a note and any of its harmonics, and between harmonics of different notes. This is partly responsible for the sense of consonance and dissonance in music: note combinations whose harmonics have few near-coincidences sound consonant (harmonious); combinations with many near-coincidences produce rapid beats perceived as rough or dissonant.

Constructive and destructive interference of sound are audible in many real Physics: Foundations of Physical Science environments. In a large room with hard walls, standing at different positions relative to speakers can produce dramatically louder or quieter sound as path-length differences shift between constructive and destructive interference. Sound engineers exploit this to design speaker arrangements that produce uniform coverage; they avoid positions of systematic destructive interference called nulls.

25.14 The Doppler Effect (Introductory)

The Doppler effect is the change in observed frequency of a wave when the source or the observer is in motion relative to the medium. When source and observer approach each other, the observed frequency is higher than the emitted frequency. When they move apart, the observed frequency is lower.

The physical explanation is straightforward for a moving source. If a car honks its horn while moving toward you, each successive wavefront is emitted slightly closer to you than the previous one (because the car has moved forward). The wavefronts are

When the car passes and moves away, the wavefronts are stretched, lengthening the wavelength and lowering the observed frequency. This is the familiar drop in pitch as a vehicle passes.

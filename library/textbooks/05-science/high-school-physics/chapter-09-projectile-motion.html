9.1 What Makes Motion a Projectile

A projectile is any object that is launched into the air and then moves under the influence of gravity alone, with no propulsion. Once the object leaves the launching mechanism — whether a hand, a cannon, or a ramp — it becomes a projectile. The word “projectile” does not imply a weapon; it applies to a thrown baseball, a water droplet from a fountain, a long-jumper in the air, or a package dropped from an airplane.

The defining feature of ideal projectile motion is that the only force acting on the object after launch is gravity. This means the vertical acceleration is a constant 9.81 m/s², downward if upward is chosen as positive, and the horizontal acceleration is exactly zero. These two facts, taken together, completely determine the trajectory.

Key Point: Real-world projectiles also experience air resistance, spin, and the Coriolis effect from Earth’s rotation. We ignore all of these in this chapter. Section 9.13 discusses when those corrections matter and how large they are.

9.2 Assumptions of Ideal Projectile Motion

The analysis in this chapter rests on three idealizations. State them explicitly at the start of every projectile problem.

  • No air resistance: the only force after launch is gravity. This is accurate for dense, compact objects at moderate speeds and breaks down for light, wide, or fast objects.
  • Constant gravitational acceleration: g = 9.81 m/s² downward, uniform everywhere during the flight. This is valid as long as the height change is much smaller than Earth’s radius, about 6400 km.
  • Flat Earth approximation: the curvature of Earth’s surface is negligible over the range of the projectile. This is valid for ranges much less than 8 km.

Historical Note: In 1638, Galileo derived the parabolic trajectory of a projectile by separating motion into independent horizontal and vertical components — precisely the method used today. His Two New Sciences established both kinematics and the independence principle decades before Newton published his laws.

9.3 Horizontal and Vertical Motion Are Independent

The foundational principle of projectile analysis is that the horizontal and vertical components of motion are completely independent. Each obeys its own set of kinematic equations. They share only one thing: time. The time elapsed is the same for both components.

This independence follows directly from Newton’s second law applied separately to each direction. Gravity acts only downward; it has zero horizontal component. No force acts horizontally under the assumptions of ideal projectile motion, so there is zero horizontal acceleration. The two directions do not communicate with each other.

The Rule: To solve any projectile problem: decompose the launch velocity into x and y components; solve the horizontal problem as constant velocity; solve the vertical problem as constant acceleration or free fall; use time as the link between the two.

9.4 Launch Velocity and Vector Components

The launch velocity is a vector with magnitude v_i, the launch speed, and direction θ above the horizontal. As in Chapter 4, we decompose it into horizontal and vertical components:

v_{x0} = v_i cosθ

v_{y0} = v_i sinθ

These components are the starting values for the two independent kinematic analyses. The horizontal component v_{x0} stays constant throughout the flight. The vertical component v_{y0} changes every instant due to gravitational acceleration.

Table 9.1 Launch angle and velocity components
Launch Angle θ v_{x0} = v_i cosθ v_{y0} = v_i sinθ Character of Motion
0° (horizontal) v_i (full) 0 Horizontal launch; drops immediately
30° 0.866v_i 0.500v_i More horizontal than vertical
45° 0.707v_i 0.707v_i Equal horizontal and vertical; maximum range with no air resistance
60° 0.500v_i 0.866v_i More vertical than horizontal; higher, shorter range
90° (vertical) 0 v_i (full) Straight up; zero horizontal range

Common Mistake: The angle θ is always measured from the horizontal, not from the vertical. If a problem gives an angle from the vertical, subtract it from 90° first.

9.5 Horizontal Motion in Projectile Problems

Because horizontal acceleration is zero, horizontal motion is uniform: constant velocity throughout the flight. The only equation needed for the horizontal direction is:

x = v_{x0}t, where v_{x0} = v_i cosθ

The horizontal velocity v_{x0} never changes. It is the same at launch, at the peak, and just before landing. There is no horizontal force to speed it up or slow it down under the ideal assumptions.

Example — Horizontal motion only: A ball is launched at 20.0 m/s horizontally, so θ = 0 and v_{x0} = 20.0 m/s. After 3.0 s, x = 20.0 × 3.0 = 60.0 m. The ball has moved 60.0 m horizontally regardless of what it does vertically.

Key Point: The horizontal component of velocity is unchanged even at the very peak of the trajectory. At the peak, v_y = 0, but v_x = v_{x0} throughout. The speed at the peak equals v_{x0}, not zero.

9.6 Vertical Motion in Projectile Problems

Vertical motion is free fall: constant downward acceleration g = 9.81 m/s². Using upward as positive, a_y = −9.81 m/s². The kinematic equations from Chapter 8 apply directly:

v_y = v_{y0} + a_y t

y = v_{y0}t + ½a_y t²

v_y² = v_{y0}² + 2a_y Δy

The vertical and horizontal analyses share time t. Finding t from one component and substituting into the other is the most common technique for linking the two equations.

Example — Vertical motion: A ball is launched at 20.0 m/s at 30.0° above horizontal. v_{y0} = 20.0 sin 30° = +10.0 m/s. After t = 3.0 s, v_y = 10.0 + (−9.81)(3.0) = −19.4 m/s, downward. Its vertical position is y = 10.0(3.0) + ½(−9.81)(3.0)² = −14.1 m, so the ball is 14.1 m below the launch point.

9.7 Time of Flight

The time of flight is the total time the projectile is in the air from launch to landing. For a projectile launched from and returning to the same height, time of flight is found by setting y = 0 in the vertical kinematic equation and solving for t.

0 = v_{y0}t + ½a_y t² = t(v_{y0} + ½a_y t)

This equation has two solutions: t = 0, the launch instant, and t = 2v_{y0}/g, the landing instant. The time of flight is:

T = 2v_{y0}/g = 2v_i sinθ/g

Example — Time of flight: A ball is launched at 25.0 m/s at 40.0°. v_{y0} = 25.0 sin 40° = 16.07 m/s. T = 2(16.07)/9.81 = 3.28 s.

Common Mistake: This formula for T applies only when landing height equals launch height. For elevated launches or drops, set up the full equation y = v_{y0}t + ½a_y t² with the correct final y value and solve for t using the quadratic formula.

9.8 Maximum Height

Maximum height is reached when the vertical velocity is zero, v_y = 0. At that instant, the projectile is moving purely horizontally. Using the vertical kinematic equation without time:

0 = v_{y0}² + 2a_yH

H = v_{y0}²/(2g) = (v_i sinθ)²/(2g)

Example — Maximum height: For v_i = 25.0 m/s and θ = 40.0°, v_{y0} = 16.07 m/s. Then H = (16.07)²/(2 × 9.81) = 13.2 m.

The time to reach maximum height is exactly half the total time of flight for symmetric trajectories:

t_{peak} = v_{y0}/g

9.9 Horizontal Range

The horizontal range R is the total horizontal distance from launch to landing. It is found by substituting the time of flight into the horizontal equation:

R = v_{x0}T = v_i cosθ(2v_i sinθ/g)

R = v_i² sin(2θ)/g

Example — Range: For v_i = 25.0 m/s and θ = 40.0°, R = (25.0)² sin(80°)/9.81 = 62.8 m.

Key Point: Complementary angles, such as 30° and 60°, give the same range because sin(2 × 30°) = sin(60°) and sin(2 × 60°) = sin(120°). This symmetry is a direct consequence of the range formula.

9.10 Launches from Elevated Positions

When a projectile is launched from a height h above the landing point, or below it, the simple formulas for time of flight, maximum height, and range no longer apply directly. Instead, set up the vertical equation with the correct displacement and solve the quadratic.

Worked Example 9.1 — Horizontal Launch from a Cliff

Problem: A ball is launched horizontally, v_i = 15.0 m/s and θ = 0°, from a cliff 45.0 m above the ground. Find the time of flight, horizontal range, and velocity at impact.

  1. Setup: v_{x0} = 15.0 m/s; v_{y0} = 0; a_y = −9.81 m/s²; Δy = −45.0 m.
  2. Time: −45.0 = 0 + ½(−9.81)t², so t² = 9.174 and t = 3.03 s.
  3. Range: x = 15.0 × 3.03 = 45.4 m.
  4. Velocity at impact: v_x = 15.0 m/s; v_y = −29.7 m/s. Speed = √(15.0² + 29.7²) = 33.3 m/s.
  5. Direction: tanα = |v_y|/v_x = 29.7/15.0 = 1.98, so α = 63.3° below horizontal.

Worked Example 9.2 — Angled Launch from a Height

Problem: A ball is launched at 20.0 m/s at 30.0° above horizontal from a platform 10.0 m above the ground. Find the horizontal range.

  1. Components: v_{x0} = 20.0 cos 30° = 17.32 m/s; v_{y0} = 20.0 sin 30° = 10.0 m/s.
  2. Vertical equation: −10.0 = 10.0t + ½(−9.81)t².
  3. Rearrange: 4.905t² − 10.0t − 10.0 = 0.
  4. Solve: t = [10.0 ± √(100 + 4×4.905×10.0)]/(2×4.905), giving t = 2.776 s from the positive root.
  5. Range: x = 17.32 × 2.776 = 48.1 m.

9.11 Symmetry in Projectile Motion

For a projectile launched and landing at the same height, the trajectory is perfectly symmetric about the peak. This symmetry produces several useful relationships:

  • The time to reach the peak equals the time to fall from the peak back to launch height: t_{up} = t_{down} = T/2.
  • The speed at any point on the way up equals the speed at the corresponding point on the way down at the same height.
  • The launch angle below the horizontal at impact equals the launch angle above the horizontal at launch, in magnitude.
  • At the peak, v_y = 0 and v_x = v_{x0}; the speed is at its minimum value of v_i cosθ.
  • The vertical velocity at impact has the same magnitude as v_{y0} but opposite sign: v_{yf} = −v_{y0}.

Tip: Use symmetry as a check. If your calculated impact speed differs from the launch speed when landing at the same height, something is wrong. The magnitudes must match, though the direction changes. Similarly, landing time should be exactly twice the peak time.

Four graphical representations capture different aspects of projectile motion.

Trajectory (y vs. x) The actual curved path in space Parabola; peak at T/2; symmetric for equal heights
x-t graph (horizontal position) Horizontal position vs. time Straight line; slope = v_{x0} (constant)
y-t graph (vertical position) Vertical position vs. time Downward parabola; peak at t = v_{y0}/g
v_y vs. t graph Vertical velocity vs. time Straight line with slope −9.81 m/s²; crosses zero at peak
v_x vs. t graph Horizontal velocity vs. time Horizontal line at v_{x0} (constant)

The trajectory is a parabola. This can be shown by eliminating t between x = v_{x0}t and y = v_{y0}t + ½a_y t². Substituting t = x/v_{x0} into the vertical equation gives y = (v_{y0}/v_{x0})x + ½a_y(x/v_{x0})², which is a quadratic in x: the equation of a parabola.

9.13 Real-World Deviations: Air Resistance and Drag

The ideal projectile model predicts that range is maximized at 45°, complementary angles give identical ranges, and the trajectory is a perfect parabola. Real projectiles deviate from all three predictions whenever air resistance is significant.

Air resistance, or drag, acts opposite to the velocity vector and depends on speed — typically as F_{drag} ∝ v² for fast objects. This force is not constant, so the constant-acceleration equations do not apply. The effects are:

  • Maximum range angle drops below 45°, typically 30–40° for balls and less for bullets.
  • Complementary angles no longer give equal ranges; shallow angles outperform steep ones.
  • The trajectory is no longer symmetric: the descent is steeper than the ascent.
  • Impact speed is less than launch speed because energy is lost to drag.

For dense, compact objects at moderate speeds, air resistance produces corrections of order 10–20% and the ideal model is a reasonable approximation. For lightweight objects, air resistance dominates and the ideal model fails badly.

Table 9.3 Air resistance and ideal projectile model quality
Projectile Effect of Air Resistance Ideal Model Quality
Steel shot put Small (1–3%) Excellent
Baseball (50 m) Moderate (1–15%) Good
Golf ball (full drive) Significant (1–40%) Rough estimate only
Table tennis ball Very large (dominant) Poor; spin effects also matter

9.14 Common Mistakes in Projectile Problems

Mistake 1: Mixing x and y components

Never use a horizontal velocity in a vertical equation or vice versa. Keep a strict two-column layout: horizontal equations on the left, vertical on the right. Clearly label every variable with a subscript x or y.

Mistake 2: Assuming the speed at the peak is zero

Only the vertical component of velocity is zero at the peak. The horizontal component v_{x0} persists throughout. The speed at the peak equals v_{x0} = v_i cosθ, which is non-zero for any θ < 90°.

Mistake 3: Forgetting to decompose the launch velocity

Substituting the full launch speed v_i directly into horizontal or vertical equations without using cosθ or sinθ is one of the most common errors. Always decompose first.

Mistake 4: Using the range formula for elevated launches

R = v_i² sin(2θ)/g applies only when the landing height equals the launch height. For any other situation, use the general vertical equation and solve for t with the quadratic formula.

Mistake 5: Getting the sign of the vertical displacement wrong

Choose upward positive and apply it consistently. If a projectile is launched from a cliff, the landing point has y < 0 relative to the launch point. Substituting that negative vertical displacement into the vertical equation gives the correct result.

Common Mistake: Draw a coordinate system with labeled positive directions before writing any equations. Mark the launch point as the origin. Write the y-coordinate of the landing point as positive or negative based on whether it is above or below the launch point.

Chapter Summary

A projectile moves in two dimensions under gravity alone. The key to every projectile problem is separating motion into independent horizontal and vertical components, linked only by the shared time variable.

  • Horizontal: a_x = 0; v_x = v_i cosθ = constant; x = v_x t.
  • Vertical: a_y = −9.81 m/s²; v_y = v_{y0} + a_y t; y = v_{y0}t + ½a_y t².
  • At the peak: v_y = 0; v_x = v_{x0}; speed = v_i cosθ.
  • Time of flight for same-height launches: T = 2v_{y0}/g = 2v_i sinθ/g.
  • Maximum height: H = v_{y0}²/(2g).
  • Range for same-height launches: R = v_i² sin(2θ)/g; maximized at θ = 45°.
  • For elevated launches, use the quadratic formula on the full vertical equation.
  • The trajectory is a parabola and is symmetric about the peak for equal-height launches.
  • Air resistance reduces range, lowers the optimal angle below 45°, and breaks symmetry.

Key Terms

projectile
Any object launched into the air and subsequently moving under gravity alone.
trajectory
The curved path followed by a projectile through space; a parabola under ideal conditions.
independence principle
The horizontal and vertical components of projectile motion are independent; each obeys its own kinematic equations.
v_{x0} (initial horizontal velocity)
The constant horizontal component of velocity throughout the flight: v_i cosθ.
v_{y0} (initial vertical velocity)
The initial vertical component of velocity at launch: v_i sinθ; changes during flight due to gravity.
time of flight
The total time a projectile is in the air from launch to landing.
maximum height
The greatest height above the launch point reached during the trajectory; occurs when v_y = 0.
range
The horizontal distance from the launch point to the landing point.
launch angle
The angle above the horizontal at which a projectile is launched; determines the mix of horizontal and vertical velocity.
air resistance
A drag force opposing motion through air; causes real trajectories to deviate from the ideal parabolic path.

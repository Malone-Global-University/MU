Chapter 53: Physics in Engineering

53.1 What Engineering Does with Physics

Physics describes how the natural world works: it discovers laws, measures constants, and tests theories against experiment. Engineering uses those laws to build things: structures, machines, circuits, systems. The engineer 's question is not "What does nature do?" but "How do we make nature do what we want?" This shift from description to prescription changes the way physics is used. In physics, we often solve idealised problems: frictionless surfaces, ideal gases, point masses, perfect conductors. In engineering, friction matters, real gases deviate from ideal behaviour, masses have size and shape, and no conductor is perfect. Engineering requires not just applying the right formula, but choosing the right model, knowing when a simplification is justified, and understanding the consequences when a model fails. Engineering disciplines map onto areas of physics. Civil and structural engineering draw heavily on statics, mechanics, and materials science. Mechanical engineering uses Physics: Foundations of Physical Science Chapter 53 — Physics in Engineering dynamics, thermodynamics, fluid mechanics, and materials. Electrical engineering is built on electromagnetism, circuit theory, and quantum mechanics (for semiconductors). Aerospace engineering combines fluid mechanics, thermodynamics, structural mechanics, and control theory. Chemical engineering uses thermodynamics and reaction kinetics. Biomedical engineering applies mechanics, fluid dynamics, and electromagnetism to biological systems. 53.2 Design Constraints and Tradeoffs

Every engineering design exists within a set of constraints: requirements that limit what solutions are acceptable. Understanding, quantifying, and satisfying constraints is the core of engineering design. Physics defines many of these constraints. Physical constraints are the most fundamental: the laws of thermodynamics set limits on efficiency; stress limits in materials set maximum loads; electrical breakdown strength sets maximum voltages; diffraction limits optical resolution. These cannot be negotiated. Physics: Foundations of Physical Science Chapter 53 — Physics in Engineering Practical constraints include cost, manufacturability, weight, size, power consumption, and maintenance requirements. A material with twice the strength at ten times the cost may not be the right choice. An engine with 5% higher efficiency but 20% greater mass may lose net performance in a vehicle. Engineering design is fundamentally about managing tradeoffs. The design process typically involves specifying requirements, generating candidate solutions, analysing each against the physical constraints and practical constraints, prototyping and testing the most promising solutions, and iterating. Physics analysis is the tool that prunes the enormous space of possible solutions to those that are physically feasible.

The Rule:

Tradeoff principle: In engineering, improving one performance parameter usually worsens another. Stronger materials are often heavier or more expensive. Higher efficiency engines often require higher temperatures, which require more expensive materials. Lighter structures are often less stiff. Recognising these tradeoffs is a core engineering competence.

Example — Bridge design tradeoff

A suspension bridge can span much longer distances than a beam bridge with the same material, but it requires expensive cables and anchorages. A truss bridge is stiffer but heavier. A cable-stayed bridge is a compromise. The choice depends on the required span, traffic load, site conditions, cost, and aesthetic requirements. There is no universally optimal bridge type.

53.3 Forces, Loads, and Structural Design

Every structure must support loads without failing. A load is any force or moment applied to a structure. Dead loads are permanent (the structure's own weight). Live loads are variable (people, vehicles, wind, snow). Dynamic loads vary with time and can cause fatigue or resonance. For a structure to be in static equilibrium, the net force and net moment (torque) on every part must be zero. Structural engineers use these conditions — the same conditions introduced in Chapter 4 — to calculate internal forces in beams, columns, trusses, and cables. These internal forces are characterised as: • Tension: a pulling force tending to elongate a member. Cables and rods in suspension bridges carry tension. • Compression: a pushing force tending to shorten a member. Columns and arches carry compression. • Shear: a force parallel to a surface, tending to cause sliding. Beams under transverse loads experience shear. Beams under loads experience bending moments that create tension on one face and compression on the other. The relationship between internal forces and the resulting stresses and strains is given

Failure modes are the specific ways a structure or component can cease to function. Common failure modes include: • Yielding: the material permanently deforms. For ductile materials like steel, yielding is visible and progressive — it is a warning, not a sudden catastrophe. Engineers design structures to yield rather than fracture under extreme load (ductility is a safety feature). • Fracture: the material breaks. Brittle materials (glass, cast iron, some ceramics) fracture without warning. Fracture mechanics predicts when a crack will grow unstably. • Fatigue: repeated cyclic loading below the yield strength causes slow crack growth that eventually leads to fracture. Fatigue failure caused the de Havilland Comet crashes in 1954 and the Aloha Airlines fuselage failure in 1988. Engineers use fatigue analysis (S-N curves) to predict service life. • Buckling: a slender column under compression fails by bending sideways suddenly, even before the material yields. Buckling is a geometric instability, not a material failure — a thin steel ruler buckles at a fraction of its compressive strength.

Historical Note:

The Tacoma Narrows Bridge collapsed in 1940, just four months after opening, due to aerodynamic flutter — a resonance between its natural oscillation frequency and the shedding of vortices by wind. The bridge was not overloaded statically; it failed dynamically. The collapse prompted major advances in bridge aerodynamics and led to modern wind tunnel testing of bridge designs before construction.

converts one form of energy to another, the efficiency η is the fraction of input energy

that appears as useful output:

η = E_useful_output / E_input = W_out / W_in

Thermodynamic efficiency limits are set by the laws of thermodynamics. For a heat

engine operating between a high-temperature source T_H and a low-temperature sink

T_C (in kelvin), the maximum possible efficiency is the Carnot efficiency (Chapter 29):

η_Carnot = 1 −T_C / T_H

Raising operating temperature to improve efficiency is a persistent theme in engineering. Modern gas turbine engines operate at combustor temperatures above 1700°C — above the melting point of the turbine blade material — and achieve this through active cooling systems where air from the compressor flows through channels inside the blades, keeping the metal below its melting point.

53.6 Motion, Forces, and Machine Design

Machines convert forces and motions into other forces and motions. The ratio of output force to input force is the mechanical advantage (MA). For an ideal (lossless) machine, energy is conserved: a large force over a small distance equals a small force over a large distance. Real machines lose some energy to friction, so actual mechanical advantage is always less than ideal. Simple machines (levers, pulleys, inclined planes, screws, wheels and axles) are the Physics: Foundations of Physical Science Chapter 53 — Physics in Engineering fundamental building blocks of complex mechanisms. Every complex machine — a car engine, a printing press, a robotic arm — is a combination of simple machines. The principles of torque (Chapter 8) and power (Chapter 6) govern their design. Rotating machinery introduces additional considerations. For rotating components, the

relevant quantities are angular velocity ω, torque τ, and rotational power P = τω. A

gear system converts rotational speed and torque: a gear pair with ratio N (output teeth / input teeth) multiplies torque by N and divides angular velocity by N, conserving power (in the ideal case).

53.7 Fluids in Engineering Systems

Fluid mechanics (Part VII, Chapters 35–38) is central to an enormous range of engineering systems. Fluid flows carry energy, transport heat, transmit forces, generate lift, and drive turbines. The key principles are continuity (conservation of mass in flow), Bernoulli's equation (energy conservation in flow), and momentum (Newton's second law applied to fluids). Hydraulic systems use incompressible fluids (usually oil or water) to transmit forces and power. Pascal's principle (Chapter 35) underlies hydraulic brakes, lifts, presses, and

actuators. The power in a hydraulic system is P = ΔPq, where ΔP is pressure difference

and q is volumetric flow rate. Pneumatic systems use compressed gas. Because gases are compressible, pneumatic Physics: Foundations of Physical Science Chapter 53 — Physics in Engineering systems store energy in compressed form and are used where actuator forces are moderate and spring-like behaviour is acceptable (air tools, pneumatic cylinders, air suspension). Aerodynamics is the branch of fluid mechanics dealing with air flows around objects. Bernoulli's principle explains lift qualitatively: an aerofoil is shaped so that air flows faster over the upper surface, creating lower pressure above and higher pressure below. The net upward pressure difference provides lift. Modern aerofoil analysis is more nuanced (inviscid potential flow, boundary layer theory, computational fluid dynamics), but Bernoulli's principle provides the correct physical intuition. Pipe flow in water distribution, chemical plant piping, and HVAC systems requires analysis of pressure drop due to friction. The Hagen-Poiseuille equation for laminar flow and empirical friction factor correlations for turbulent flow (both derived from fluid mechanics principles) determine the required pipe diameter, pump power, and pressure ratings.

Example — Pump selection

A water supply system requires 0.05 m³/s of flow

through a pipe of length 500 m, diameter 0.1 m, with a 30 m elevation gain and pipe

friction losses of 25 m head. Total head required: 30 + 25 = 55 m. Pump power: P =

ρgQh = 1000 × 9.81 × 0.05 × 55 = 26.9 kW (ideal). Real pump would require about

35–40 kW to account for pump efficiency of 70–75%.

53.8 Heat Transfer and Thermal Design

Thermal engineering involves managing the flow of heat into, through, and out of systems. The three mechanisms of heat transfer — conduction, convection, and radiation (Chapter 30) — all appear in engineering systems, often simultaneously.

Thermal resistance is the engineering analogue of electrical resistance. Just as V = IR

relates voltage, current, and resistance, the heat flow rate Q_dot through a conductor is

Q_dot = ΔT / R_th, where ΔT is the temperature difference and R_th is the thermal

resistance. Thermal resistances in series add directly, just like electrical resistances in series. This allows complex thermal systems (insulated walls, heat exchangers, electronic cooling systems) to be analysed with simple circuit-like diagrams. Electronics cooling is a major thermal engineering challenge. Microprocessors generate

50–150 W of heat from a chip area of a few cm² — heat fluxes comparable to a rocket

nozzle. Without effective cooling, chips overheat within milliseconds. Solutions include heat sinks (extended surfaces to increase convective area), heat pipes (phase-change cooling), liquid cooling loops, and thermoelectric coolers. Thermal management is a limiting factor in computer performance: the "power wall" that halted clock speed Physics: Foundations of Physical Science Chapter 53 — Physics in Engineering increases around 2004 was fundamentally a thermal problem. Buildings require thermal design to maintain comfortable interior temperatures without excessive energy consumption. R-value is the thermal resistance per unit area of an

insulating material (m²·K/W). Higher R-values mean better insulation. The total heat loss

from a building is Q_dot = AΔT/R_total, where the sum includes walls, roof, windows,

and air infiltration. Building energy codes specify minimum R-values to limit energy consumption.

Key Point:

The Carnot limit and the laws of thermodynamics define the efficiency ceiling for any heat engine: no device can have efficiency greater than 1

T_C/T_H.

Refrigerators and heat pumps obey analogous efficiency limits (COP limits). These are hard physical constraints that no engineering innovation can circumvent; they can only be approached, never exceeded.

53.9 Electricity, Circuits, and Control Systems

Electrical engineering translates the principles of electromagnetism (Part VI) into power systems, electronics, communications, and control. Every electrical system obeys Ohm's

law (V = IR), Kirchhoff's laws (current and voltage conservation), and the principles of

electromagnetic induction. Power Systems Electrical power is transmitted over long distances as high-voltage AC (alternating current) because transformers can step voltage up and down efficiently — high voltage

means low current, which means low resistive losses (P_loss = I²R). The national grid

transmits at 100–500 kV; distribution to homes steps down to 230 V (UK/Europe) or 120 V (North America). The frequency is maintained at 50 or 60 Hz — deviating by even a fraction of a Hz causes equipment damage and grid instability. Electronics Transistors (Chapter 48) are the fundamental switching and amplifying element of all modern electronics. A smartphone contains approximately billion transistors. Moore's Law — the observation that transistor density roughly doubles every two years — has driven computing performance for decades by making transistors smaller, faster, and cheaper. Quantum mechanical tunnelling, however, sets a fundamental limit to how small transistors can be made before electrons tunnel through the barrier that should switch them off. Control Systems Physics: Foundations of Physical Science Chapter 53 — Physics in Engineering A control system uses sensors, feedback, and actuators to maintain a desired output. A thermostat is a simple control system: it senses temperature, compares it to a setpoint, and switches the heater on or off. A cruise control system uses vehicle speed as the feedback signal and adjusts engine throttle to maintain target speed. Modern aircraft fly-by-wire systems replace mechanical linkages with electronic control systems: the pilot's stick inputs are processed by computers that calculate the control surface deflections needed to achieve the desired aircraft motion, checking hundreds of physical and safety constraints simultaneously. Control theory uses the mathematics of feedback to analyse stability, response time, and precision. A poorly designed feedback system can oscillate or become unstable — a consequence directly analogous to mechanical resonance. The physics of the process being controlled determines what control strategies are feasible.

53.10 Waves, Sound, and Optical Technologies

Wave physics (Part V) underlies a wide range of engineering technologies in communications, sensing, imaging, and manufacturing. Communications Radio, microwave, cellular, WiFi, and satellite communications all use electromagnetic waves. The electromagnetic spectrum is a finite resource: different frequency bands are allocated for different uses and licensed by governments. Signal encoding, antenna design, and receiver sensitivity are all governed by electromagnetic wave physics and information theory. Fibre optic communications transmit data as light pulses through glass fibres using total internal reflection (Chapter 24), achieving data rates of terabits per second over transoceanic distances. Ultrasonic Technology Ultrasonic waves (sound above 20 kHz) are used for non-destructive testing (detecting cracks and voids in materials without cutting them open), sonar (ranging in water), medical imaging (ultrasound), and cleaning. The physics of reflection, transmission, and refraction at material interfaces (Chapter 22) determines what ultrasound can detect and with what resolution. Optical Engineering Cameras, telescopes, microscopes, laser systems, and optical fibres all apply the principles of geometric and wave optics (Chapters 23–25). The diffraction limit (Chapter 25) sets the minimum resolvable feature size for any optical instrument: smaller wavelengths achieve higher resolution. Electron microscopes use the de Broglie Physics: Foundations of Physical Science Chapter 53 — Physics in Engineering wavelength of electrons (Chapter 45) to resolve features far smaller than visible light allows. Extreme ultraviolet lithography uses 13.5 nm wavelength EUV light to pattern transistors on computer chips. RADAR and LIDAR RADAR (Radio Detection And Ranging) uses the Doppler effect (Chapter 22) and reflection of radio waves to detect and locate objects, measure their speed, and map terrain. Weather RADAR detects precipitation by the reflection of radio waves from raindrops. Traffic speed cameras use the Doppler shift of reflected radio waves to measure vehicle speed. LIDAR (Light Detection And Ranging) uses laser pulses for high- resolution three-dimensional mapping and is a key sensor technology in autonomous vehicles.

53.11 Modelling, Prototyping, and Testing

Engineering design does not move directly from equations to manufactured product. Between theoretical analysis and production lies an iterative cycle of modelling, prototyping, testing, and refinement. Mathematical and Computational Modelling Engineers build mathematical models of proposed designs to predict performance before physical construction. Analytical models (closed-form equations) are used where the geometry is simple enough. Computational models — Finite Element Analysis (FEA) for stress and deformation, Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) for flow problems, circuit simulation for electronics — are used for complex geometries and coupled phenomena. All computational models are approximations: their accuracy depends on mesh quality, material model correctness, and boundary condition specification. Validating computational models against physical measurements is a critical engineering practice. Prototyping A prototype is a physical realisation of a design at some stage of development — from a rapid 3D-printed concept model to a full-scale functional prototype. Physical prototypes reveal interactions between components that are difficult to model computationally: assembly tolerances, unexpected resonances, thermal gradients, and manufacturing variability. Testing a prototype under realistic conditions is the most reliable way to identify design flaws before full production. Testing and Measurement Physics: Foundations of Physical Science Chapter 53 — Physics in Engineering Testing verifies that a product meets its specifications under real conditions. Mechanical testing measures strength, stiffness, fatigue life, and failure mode. Environmental testing exposes products to temperature extremes, humidity, vibration, and shock. Electrical testing checks performance across voltage and temperature ranges. Non- destructive testing (NDT) — X-ray, ultrasound, eddy current inspection — verifies internal quality without destroying the part. Every test is ultimately a physics experiment, and test results are meaningful only if the measurement uncertainty is understood and controlled.

53.12 Safety Factors and Reliability

Engineering design always includes margins to account for uncertainty. A safety factor (also called a factor of safety, FS) is the ratio of the material's strength (or a structure's load-carrying capacity) to the expected maximum applied stress (or load):

FS = Strength / Applied_stress

(or Load capacity / Design load) Safety factors exist because of uncertainties: uncertainty in the actual loads (what if the maximum earthquake is larger than predicted?), uncertainty in material properties (batch-to-batch variability, effects of corrosion and aging), uncertainty in analysis methods (simplified models miss some failure modes), and uncertainty in construction quality (workmanship defects). Reliability engineering quantifies the probability that a system will perform its function over a specified time period. Probabilistic analysis replaces single-number safety factors with distributions of load and strength, calculating the probability of failure rather than assuming failure occurs only when the mean load exceeds the mean strength. For high -consequence applications (aircraft, nuclear plants, life-critical medical devices), failure probabilities as low as 10 ⁹ per hour of operation are required. Common Mistake:

A safety factor does not make a design infinitely safe — it reduces the probability of failure to an acceptable level. An unexpected failure mode not covered by the analysis (fatigue, corrosion, an unforeseen load combination) can cause failure even at high safety factors. Engineering safety requires identifying all relevant failure modes, not just calculating a number.

53.13 Common Mistakes in Applying Physics to Engineering

• Treating idealised models as exact descriptions: Assuming a structure is rigid, a fluid is inviscid, or a gas is ideal when the real system deviates significantly from the idealisation. Always check whether the model's assumptions are satisfied in the actual application. • Ignoring units and dimensional consistency: Mixing units (pounds and newtons, inches and metres, gauge and absolute pressure) causes catastrophic errors. The 1999 Mars Climate Orbiter was lost because one engineering team used pound-

force·seconds and another used newton·seconds for impulse — a $327 million

unit conversion error. • Neglecting dynamic loads and fatigue: Static analysis finds the stress under steady loading. But real structures experience fluctuating, dynamic, and impact loads that can cause fatigue failure at stresses well below the static yield strength. A design that is safe statically can still fail in service if fatigue is not analysed. • Confusing thermal and electrical analogues incorrectly: Thermal and electrical systems have analogous mathematics (Fourier's law mirrors Ohm's law; thermal resistance mirrors electrical resistance), but they differ in important ways. Heat Physics: Foundations of Physical Science Chapter 53 — Physics in Engineering capacity has no perfect electrical analogue, and thermal interfaces have contact resistance that electrical contacts do not. • Applying Bernoulli's equation outside its domain: Bernoulli's equation applies to steady, inviscid, incompressible flow along a streamline. Applying it to highly viscous flows, compressible flows (high-speed air), or flows with large velocity gradients gives incorrect results. Know the limits of every equation. • Over-relying on safety factors without identifying failure modes: A safety factor is only as good as the analysis that produced it. If the failure mode is wrong (using tensile yield strength when buckling is the actual mode, for example), the safety factor is meaningless. Failure mode analysis must precede safety factor application. • Ignoring manufacturing tolerances and variability: A design that works perfectly in analysis may fail in production because parts are not made to exact dimensions. Engineers specify tolerances and verify that performance is acceptable across the entire tolerance range, not just at the nominal (target) dimension.

Historical Note:

The Challenger space shuttle disaster of 1986 was caused by O-ring seals that became stiff and failed to seal properly at the low temperature (approximately 2°C) at launch. Engineers at the O-ring manufacturer had warned against launching at such temperatures, but organisational pressure overrode the technical objection. The disaster illustrates that engineering safety requires both correct physics and the institutional culture to act on technical concerns without organisational pressure to ignore them.

It uses the laws of mechanics, thermodynamics, electromagnetism, fluids, and waves to design systems that must work safely and reliably under real-world constraints. • Engineering design is constrained by physical laws (non-negotiable) and practical requirements (tradeoffs).

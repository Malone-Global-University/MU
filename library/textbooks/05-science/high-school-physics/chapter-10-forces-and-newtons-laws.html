Chapter 10 — Forces and Newton's Laws

This chapter introduces force as a vector interaction, explains net force and inertia, develops Newton's three laws, and applies them to everyday mechanics, weight, action-reaction pairs, and common conceptual mistakes.

Physics: Foundations of Physical Science Chapter 10 Motion and Forces

10.1 What a Force Is

A force is a push or a pull exerted on an object by another object or by a field. Forces are interactions: they always involve two objects, and they always come in pairs, as described by Newton's third law. Forces are vector quantities: they have magnitude, direction, and a point of application.

The SI unit of force is the newton (N). One newton is defined as the force required to accelerate a 1 kg mass at 1 m/s².

\[1\,\text{N} = 1\,\text{kg}\cdot\text{m/s}^2\]

This unit emerges directly from Newton's second law and ensures dimensional consistency throughout mechanics.

A force does not have to cause motion. A book resting on a table has gravity pulling it down and the table pushing it up. These forces are equal and opposite, so the book does not accelerate. The forces are real and significant; they just cancel. What matters for motion is the net force, the vector sum of all forces acting on an object.

10.2 Contact Forces and Field Forces

Forces are divided into two categories based on how they are transmitted between objects.

Contact forces require physical contact between the objects. They act only when surfaces touch. Examples include friction, normal force, tension, applied force, and air resistance. These forces vanish immediately when the objects separate.

Field forces, also called non-contact forces or action-at-a-distance forces, act between objects that are not in contact, through a field that permeates space. The field carries the force across the gap. Examples include gravity through a gravitational field, electric force through an electric field, and magnetic force through a magnetic field. Gravity acts between Earth and a falling apple even though they never touch; the gravitational field transmits the interaction.

Key Point: At the atomic level, all contact forces are fundamentally electromagnetic. When two surfaces touch, it is really the electrons in their atoms repelling each other. Gravity is the only truly distinct force at everyday scales. The four fundamental forces of nature — gravity, electromagnetism, strong nuclear, and weak nuclear — underlie every force encountered in this book.

10.3 Net Force and Balanced vs. Unbalanced Forces

The net force, sometimes called the resultant force, on an object is the vector sum of all forces acting on it. Because forces are vectors, they must be added using the component method from Chapter 4.

\[F_{\text{net}} = F_1 + F_2 + F_3 + \cdots\]

When the net force is zero, forces are balanced. A balanced object does not accelerate; it remains either at rest or moving with constant velocity. When the net force is non-zero, forces are unbalanced, and the object accelerates in the direction of the net force.

Common Mistake: A moving object can have zero net force. Motion does not require a net force; only changes in motion do. A hockey puck sliding on frictionless ice has zero net force and continues moving at constant velocity forever. This is one of the most important conceptual distinctions in mechanics.

10.4 Newton's First Law of Motion

Newton's first law, the law of inertia, states that an object at rest stays at rest, and an object in uniform motion, meaning constant velocity, continues in uniform motion unless acted upon by a non-zero net external force.

\[\text{If } F_{\text{net}} = 0,\text{ then } \Delta v = 0\]

The first law tells us two things. First, the natural state of an object with no net force is constant velocity, not rest. Rest is just the special case of constant velocity where the velocity happens to be zero. Second, forces are required to change velocity, not to maintain it.

Newton's first law overturned the older assumption that continued motion required continued force.

Before Newton, Aristotle's framework held that a continuous force was required to maintain motion and that objects naturally came to rest. Newton's first law overturned this: objects naturally maintain whatever motion they have. Friction is what brings earthly objects to rest; on a perfectly frictionless surface, they would slide forever.

Historical Note: Galileo effectively discovered the first law before Newton. Through inclined plane experiments, he showed that a ball rolling on a perfectly smooth horizontal surface would continue indefinitely. Newton generalized and formalized this idea into the first law and embedded it in a complete mathematical framework.

10.5 Inertia and Mass

Inertia is the tendency of an object to resist changes in its state of motion. An object with more inertia is harder to accelerate: it takes more force to produce the same change in velocity.

Mass is the quantitative measure of inertia. The greater the mass, the greater the inertia, and the harder it is to accelerate the object. Mass is measured in kilograms and is an intrinsic property of matter. It does not depend on location, orientation, or the presence of other objects.

Mass is not the same as weight. Mass is inertia, or resistance to acceleration. Weight is the gravitational force on the mass. An astronaut in space has the same mass as on Earth but is nearly weightless. A 70 kg person has the same mass on the Moon as on Earth, but their weight on the Moon is about one-sixth their weight on Earth.

10.6 Newton's Second Law of Motion

Newton's second law, the law of acceleration, states that the acceleration of an object is directly proportional to the net force acting on it and inversely proportional to its mass. The acceleration is in the same direction as the net force.

\[F_{\text{net}} = ma\]

This is the central equation of classical mechanics. It connects the kinematic quantity, acceleration, to the dynamic quantity, force, with mass as the proportionality constant.

Three important consequences follow immediately:

  • For a fixed mass, doubling the net force doubles the acceleration.
  • For a fixed net force, doubling the mass halves the acceleration.
  • If \(F_{\text{net}} = 0\), then \(a = 0\). Newton's first law is a special case of the second law.
\[a = \frac{F_{\text{net}}}{m}\]

In one-dimensional problems, the vector equation reduces to signed scalars: positive force produces positive acceleration, and negative force produces negative acceleration, consistent with the sign convention chosen for the problem.

Bridge Note: Newton's second law links the description of motion from kinematics to the causes of motion in dynamics.

10.7 Force, Mass, and Acceleration Relationships

The second law \(\vec{F}=m\vec{a}\) encodes a proportional relationship: fix one quantity and the other two scale linearly. These relationships can be summarized graphically and numerically.

Example — \(F=ma\) Applied

A net force of 15.0 N acts on a 3.0 kg box. The acceleration is:

\[a = \frac{15.0}{3.0} = 5.0\,\text{m/s}^2\]

The acceleration is in the direction of the force. If mass doubles to 6.0 kg with the same force, then:

\[a = \frac{15.0}{6.0} = 2.5\,\text{m/s}^2\]

If force doubles to 30.0 N with the original 3.0 kg mass, then:

\[a = \frac{30.0}{3.0} = 10.0\,\text{m/s}^2\]

10.8 Applying \(F=ma\)

Applying Newton's second law systematically requires the same discipline as applying kinematic equations: identify the system, identify all forces, find the net force, then solve for the unknown.

The Newton's Second Law Problem-Solving Method

  1. Identify the object, or system, to analyze.
  2. Choose a positive direction and, in two-dimensional problems, choose a coordinate system.
  3. List all forces on the object with their magnitudes and signs.
  4. Find the net force: \(F_{\text{net}} = \Sigma F\), the sum of all forces with signs.
  5. Apply \(F_{\text{net}} = ma\); solve for the unknown acceleration, force, or mass.
  6. Check units, sign, and physical reasonableness.

Worked Example 10.1 — Finding Acceleration

A 5.0 kg box is pushed right with 20.0 N and pulled left with 8.0 N. Find the acceleration.

  1. System: the 5.0 kg box.
  2. Positive direction: rightward.
  3. Forces: \(F_1 = +20.0\,\text{N}\); \(F_2 = -8.0\,\text{N}\).
  4. Net force: \(F_{\text{net}} = +20.0 + (-8.0) = +12.0\,\text{N}\).
  5. Apply \(F=ma\): \(a = F_{\text{net}}/m = 12.0/5.0 = +2.4\,\text{m/s}^2\).
  6. Check: positive acceleration is rightward, and units are \(\text{N/kg} = \text{m/s}^2\).

Worked Example 10.2 — Finding Force

What net force is needed to accelerate a 1200 kg car at 3.5 m/s²?

  1. Apply \(F=ma\): \(F_{\text{net}} = m \times a = 1200 \times 3.5 = 4200\,\text{N}\).
  2. Check: units are \(\text{kg}\cdot\text{m/s}^2 = \text{N}\). A force of 4200 N, or 4.2 kN, is reasonable for a car.

10.9 Newton's Third Law of Motion

Newton's third law, the law of interaction, states that whenever object A exerts a force on object B, object B simultaneously exerts an equal and opposite force on object A.

\[F_{A\text{ on }B} = -F_{B\text{ on }A}\]

Third-law force pairs always have these characteristics: they are equal in magnitude, opposite in direction, act simultaneously, are of the same type, and act on different objects. The third law is always true, without exception.

Common Mistake: Third-law pairs act on different objects. They cannot cancel each other for the purpose of Newton's second law because \(F=ma\) applies to one object at a time. Forces that cancel, or balance, are forces on the same object from different sources.

10.10 Action-Reaction Force Pairs

The term action-reaction pair is sometimes misunderstood to imply a time sequence: first an action, then a reaction. This is wrong. Newton's third-law forces are simultaneous. There is no primary action and secondary reaction; neither force causes the other. They are both part of the same interaction.

Example — Why Rockets Work

A rocket engine expels exhaust gas downward. By Newton's third law, the exhaust pushes the rocket upward with an equal force. The rocket does not push against air; it works in vacuum. The interaction is between the rocket body and the exhaust gas it ejects.

10.11 Weight and Gravitational Force

The weight of an object is the gravitational force exerted on it by a nearby massive body, usually Earth. Weight is a force, a vector pointing toward Earth's center, and is measured in newtons, not kilograms.

\[W = mg\]

On Earth's surface, \(g = 9.81\,\text{m/s}^2\). On the Moon, \(g_{\text{Moon}} \approx 1.62\,\text{m/s}^2\). On Mars, \(g_{\text{Mars}} \approx 3.71\,\text{m/s}^2\). An astronaut with mass 70.0 kg has weight 687 N on Earth, 113 N on the Moon, and 260 N on Mars, but the same mass everywhere.

The third-law pair of weight, Earth pulling an object down, is the object pulling Earth upward with the same force. Earth's enormous mass means this force produces negligible acceleration of Earth, so we do not notice it.

Example — Weight vs. Mass

A student has mass 60.0 kg. Her weight is:

\[W = 60.0 \times 9.81 = 589\,\text{N}\]

In everyday speech, people say they "weigh 60 kg." In physics, that is mass, not weight. Weight is always a force measured in newtons.

Common Mistake: Never express weight in kilograms. Weight is a force, measured in newtons. The confusion between mass and weight is one of the most persistent errors in introductory physics.

10.12 Common Force Interactions in Everyday Life

Newton's laws operate in every physical situation, though we rarely think about them explicitly in daily life. The following scenarios can be analyzed in terms of forces and net force.

10.13 Misconceptions About Motion and Force

Misconception 1: Moving objects need a force to keep moving

No. Objects maintain their velocity without any net force, as stated by the first law. Friction is what slows most real objects; in the absence of friction, they would continue indefinitely. A hockey puck on ice, a spacecraft in deep space, and a marble in a frictionless tube all demonstrate this.

Misconception 2: A larger force always produces a larger acceleration

Acceleration depends on net force divided by mass. A very large force on a very massive object may produce less acceleration than a small force on a tiny object.

Misconception 3: The action force is the "real" force; the reaction is just a response

Both forces in a Newton's third-law pair are equally real, simultaneous, and equal in magnitude. Neither causes the other; they are both aspects of a single interaction.

Misconception 4: If an object accelerates, there must be more forces in the direction of motion than against it

This is correct only when speaking about the net force. Students sometimes think there must be a specific "accelerating force" acting in the direction of motion. A single unbalanced force produces acceleration; no separate mover force is needed.

Misconception 5: Heavier objects fall faster than lighter ones

In a vacuum, all objects fall with the same acceleration \(g\), regardless of mass. Galileo demonstrated this, and it follows directly from \(F=mg\) and \(a=F/m=g\), which is independent of mass. Air resistance is what causes feathers to fall slower than hammers. On the Moon, where there is no air, they fall together.

10.14 Limits of Newtonian Mechanics

Newton's laws provide an extraordinarily accurate description of the physical world for the vast majority of situations encountered in engineering and everyday life. However, they have well-defined limits.

  • Very high speeds: when objects move at a significant fraction of the speed of light, such as \(v \gtrsim 0.1c\), special relativity must be used. Newton's laws give increasingly inaccurate predictions.
  • Very small scales: at the scale of atoms and subatomic particles, quantum mechanics governs. Newton's second law does not describe the behavior of electrons orbiting a nucleus or photons interacting with matter.
  • Very strong gravity: near a black hole or neutron star, where spacetime curvature is extreme, general relativity is required. Newtonian gravity is an approximation to the full theory.

Within their domain of validity — objects of everyday size, moving at speeds much less than light, in moderate gravitational fields — Newton's laws are exquisitely accurate. GPS satellites, bridge engineering, spacecraft trajectory planning, and crash-safety design all rely on Newtonian mechanics to extraordinary precision.

Bridge Note: Newtonian mechanics remains the practical foundation for most everyday and engineering-scale motion, even though deeper theories are required outside its domain.

Chapter Summary

Forces are vector interactions that cause changes in motion. Newton's three laws provide the complete causal framework of classical mechanics.

  • A force is a push or pull; forces are vector quantities measured in newtons, where \(1\,\text{N}=1\,\text{kg}\cdot\text{m/s}^2\).
  • Contact forces require physical contact; field forces act at a distance through fields.
  • Net force is the vector sum of all forces on an object; zero net force means no acceleration.
  • Newton's First Law: an object maintains constant velocity unless acted on by a non-zero net force. Motion does not require a net force; change in motion does.
  • Inertia is resistance to changes in motion; mass, in kilograms, is its quantitative measure.
  • Newton's Second Law: \(F_{\text{net}} = ma\). Acceleration is proportional to net force and inversely proportional to mass; the direction of acceleration is the direction of the net force.
  • Newton's Third Law: for every force A exerts on B, B exerts an equal and opposite force on A — simultaneously, on different objects, and of the same type.
  • Weight \(W=mg\) is the gravitational force on a mass; it is measured in newtons, not kilograms.
  • Newton's laws apply for everyday objects at everyday speeds; they break down at relativistic speeds, atomic scales, and extreme gravity.

Key Terms

force
A push or pull exerted on an object by another object or field; a vector quantity; SI unit: newton, \(\text{N}=\text{kg}\cdot\text{m/s}^2\).
contact force
A force that requires direct physical contact between objects; examples include normal force, friction, tension, and applied force.
field force
A force that acts at a distance through a field without physical contact; examples include gravity, electric force, and magnetic force.
net force
The vector sum of all forces acting on a single object; determines acceleration via Newton's second law.
Newton's First Law
An object at rest or in uniform motion remains in that state unless acted upon by a non-zero net force; the law of inertia.
inertia
The tendency of an object to resist changes in its state of motion; quantified by mass.
mass
The quantitative measure of inertia; an intrinsic property of matter; SI unit: kilogram; does not depend on location.
Newton's Second Law
The net force on an object equals its mass times its acceleration: \(F_{\text{net}}=ma\); the central equation of classical mechanics.
Newton's Third Law
For every force A exerts on B, B exerts an equal and opposite force on A; forces always occur in action-reaction pairs on different objects.
action-reaction pair
A pair of forces described by Newton's third law: equal magnitude, opposite direction, same type, acting on different objects, and simultaneous.
weight
The gravitational force on an object: \(W=mg\); a vector directed toward the center of the attracting body; measured in newtons.
newton (N)
The SI unit of force: \(1\,\text{N}=1\,\text{kg}\cdot\text{m/s}^2\); the force needed to accelerate 1 kg at 1 m/s².

Concept Review Questions

  1. What is the difference between a contact force and a field force? Give two examples of each.
  2. A book sits on a table. List all the forces acting on the book. Is the net force zero or non-zero? How do you know?
  3. State Newton's first law in your own words. Give a real-world example where you can observe it.
  4. Why is it incorrect to say that a moving object needs a force to keep it moving?
  5. Explain the difference between mass and weight. A 10 kg box is on the Moon. What is its mass? What is its weight if \(g_{\text{Moon}} = 1.62\,\text{m/s}^2\)?
  6. State Newton's second law in words and as an equation. Explain what each symbol represents.
  7. If the net force on an object doubles and the mass stays the same, what happens to the acceleration?
  8. State Newton's third law. Why do the two forces in an action-reaction pair not cancel each other?
  9. A rocket expels gas. Explain using Newton's third law why the rocket moves.
  10. Under what conditions do Newton's laws break down? Name two physical regimes where they fail.

Newton's Laws Practice Problems

  1. A 2.0 kg ball experiences a net force of 10.0 N to the right. Find its acceleration.
  2. A net force of 500 N acts on a 1200 kg car. Find the acceleration.
  3. A 0.150 kg baseball accelerates at 4000 m/s² during a pitch. What net force acts on it?
  4. What mass will accelerate at 6.0 m/s² under a net force of 30.0 N?
  5. A 70.0 kg person stands on a scale in an elevator. The elevator accelerates upward at 2.0 m/s². What does the scale read in newtons?
  6. Same person, same elevator, now decelerating at 1.5 m/s² while moving upward. What does the scale read?

Force and Acceleration Problems

  1. A 4.0 kg block is pushed with 30 N to the right and 18 N to the left simultaneously. Find: (a) net force; (b) acceleration.
  2. A 55.0 kg skier accelerates down a slope. If the net force along the slope is 110 N, find the acceleration.
  3. A 10,000 kg rocket has a thrust of 200,000 N. Neglecting gravity, find the acceleration. If gravity acts, with \(W = 98,100\,\text{N}\), find the net upward force and the actual acceleration.
  4. A 0.50 kg toy car accelerates from rest to 2.0 m/s in 4.0 s. What net force acted on it?
  5. Two horizontal forces act on an object: 40 N east and 30 N north. The mass is 5.0 kg. Find: (a) the magnitude of the net force; (b) the direction of the net force; (c) the magnitude of the acceleration.

Action-Reaction Analysis

  1. A 60 kg person pushes a 5.0 kg skateboard with a force of 20 N. (a) What force does the skateboard exert on the person? (b) Find the acceleration of the skateboard. (c) Why do the person and skateboard accelerate differently if the forces are equal?
  2. Earth pulls the Moon with a gravitational force of about \(2.0 \times 10^{20}\,\text{N}\). (a) What force does the Moon exert on Earth? (b) Given Earth's mass of \(5.97 \times 10^{24}\,\text{kg}\), find Earth's acceleration toward the Moon. (c) Why don't we notice Earth moving?
  3. A hammer drives a nail into wood. (a) Identify the action-reaction force pair. (b) Both forces are equal, so why does the nail move but the hammer does not?
  4. A swimmer pushes the pool wall backward with 80 N. (a) What force does the wall exert on the swimmer? (b) If the swimmer has mass 60 kg, find her initial acceleration.

Applied Mechanics Problems

  1. A 15.0 kg box rests on a frictionless surface. Two ropes attached to it pull with 50.0 N at 0° and 30.0 N at 90°. Find the magnitude and direction of the acceleration.
  2. A car of mass 1500 kg decelerates from 25.0 m/s to rest in 6.0 s. Find: (a) acceleration; (b) net braking force.
  3. An elevator of mass 800 kg and occupants of total mass 400 kg accelerates upward at 1.5 m/s². Find the tension in the cable.
  4. Challenge: Two blocks are connected by a string on a frictionless table. Block A, with mass 3.0 kg, is pulled by a 12 N force. Block B, with mass 1.0 kg, trails behind. Treating the two-block system: (a) find the system acceleration; (b) find the tension in the string between A and B.

Free-Body Diagrams

11.1 Why Free-Body Diagrams Matter

A free-body diagram (FBD) is a sketch that shows a single object in isolation, with every external force acting on it represented as a labeled arrow. The diagram makes the force analysis concrete, visual, and systematic, preventing the most common errors in Newton’s second law problems: missing a force, double-counting a force, or assigning the wrong direction.

The discipline of drawing FBDs before writing equations is one of the clearest markers that separates expert problem-solvers from novices. Students who draw FBDs make fewer errors, catch mistakes earlier, and solve more complex problems correctly. The FBD is not a preliminary sketch you do quickly before the real work; it is the real work.

Tip: Draw a free-body diagram for every mechanics problem, every time. Even for problems that seem simple, the FBD confirms your force identification and prevents sign errors.

11.2 Isolating a System or Object

The first step in drawing an FBD is choosing the system: the single object, or group of objects, you want to analyze with Newton’s second law. This choice determines which forces are relevant.

To isolate the object, imagine removing it from its environment by cutting every physical contact and every field interaction. Whatever was attached to the object at those cut points exerts a force on the object that must be represented in the FBD. What the object exerts on others is not shown; only forces on the chosen object appear.

The Rule: One object, one FBD. If a problem involves two objects, draw two separate FBDs, one for each. Forces on object A go on A’s FBD; forces on object B go on B’s FBD. Newton’s third law pairs appear in separate diagrams.

Example — Choosing the system: A block hangs from a rope tied to the ceiling. If you analyze the block, the FBD shows weight W down and tension T up. If you analyze the ceiling hook, the FBD shows the tension of the rope pulling down and the ceiling attachment pulling up. Different objects require different FBDs.

11.3 Internal vs. External Forces

External forces act on the chosen object from outside the system boundary. These are the only forces that affect the acceleration of the object or system, and they are the only ones that appear in the FBD.

Internal forces are forces between parts within a system boundary. When you treat a system of two or more objects as a single unit, forces between those objects are internal and cancel in pairs. They do not appear in the system’s FBD and do not affect the system’s acceleration.

11.4 Drawing Objects as Simplified Bodies

In an FBD, the object itself is drawn as a simple shape, almost always a dot, square, or rectangle. Detail is irrelevant. What matters is the point of application of each force and its direction.

  • Use a filled circle for a point mass when rotation can be neglected.
  • Use a square or rectangle for an extended object when shape affects force distribution.
  • Draw the object in a clear central position with space on all sides for force arrows.
  • Do not draw the environment in the FBD; draw only the isolated object and its force vectors.

Tip: The simplest FBD has the object as a dot in the center. Each arrow is labeled with its force symbol, such as W, N, T, or f, and its magnitude if known.

11.5 Representing Forces with Vectors

Each force in an FBD is represented as an arrow with direction, magnitude, and label. Direction shows the physical direction of the force. Arrow length represents the force’s strength. The label identifies the force type and, when known, its magnitude and unit.

  • Arrow direction: Gravity points down; normal force is perpendicular to the surface; tension pulls along the rope toward the attachment.
  • Arrow length: Longer arrows represent larger forces. Use a consistent scale within one diagram.
  • Label: Write the force symbol next to the arrow, such as W, N, T, f, or Fapp.

Common Mistake: Every arrow in an FBD must have a label. An unlabeled force arrow is incomplete.

11.7 Weight Force

Weight is the gravitational force Earth, or another body, exerts on an object. It always points straight downward, toward Earth’s center, regardless of the object’s shape, orientation, or motion. Its magnitude is W = mg, where g = 9.81 m/s² near Earth’s surface.

Weight acts at the object’s center of mass. For a uniform object, this is the geometric center. For FBD purposes, it is drawn from the center of the body representation, pointing straight down.

Example — Weight in an FBD: A 5.0 kg box has weight W = 5.0 × 9.81 = 49.05 ≈ 49.1 N downward. Draw a downward arrow from the center of the box symbol, labeled W = 49.1 N.

11.8 Normal Force

The normal force is the contact force that a surface exerts on an object perpendicular to, and away from, the surface. “Normal” means perpendicular. The normal force is a surface’s mechanical response to compression; it prevents objects from passing through each other.

The normal force is not always equal to the object’s weight. It equals the weight only on a horizontal surface with no vertical acceleration and no additional vertical forces. On an incline, in an elevator, or with an applied force at an angle, the normal force differs from the weight.

Table 11.3 Normal force in common situations.
SituationNormal Force Magnitude
Object at rest on horizontal surface with no other vertical forcesN = mg = W
Object on horizontal surface; rope pulls upward with force TN = mg − T
Elevator accelerating upward at aN = m(g + a)
Elevator accelerating downward at aN = m(g − a)
Object on ramp inclined at angle θN = mg cosθ

Common Mistake: The normal force is always perpendicular to the contact surface, not perpendicular to the direction of motion.

11.9 Tension Force

Tension is the pulling force transmitted through a rope, string, cable, or chain. Tension always pulls; it cannot push. The force on the object at one end of a rope is directed along the rope, away from the object, toward the other end.

For an ideal massless, inextensible rope, the tension magnitude is the same at every point along the rope. When a rope passes over an ideal massless, frictionless pulley, the tension magnitude is unchanged; only the direction changes.

Example — Tension in an FBD: A 4.0 kg block hangs from a rope. The tension T acts upward on the block. The FBD of the block shows W = 39.2 N down and T up. If the block is in equilibrium, T = W = 39.2 N.

11.10 Friction Force

Friction is a contact force parallel to the surface, opposing relative motion or the tendency for relative motion. Kinetic friction opposes sliding motion. Static friction opposes the tendency to slide.

A common error is placing friction in the wrong direction. Ask which way the surface would slide if friction disappeared. Friction opposes that direction.

Example — Friction direction: A box slides to the right across a floor. Kinetic friction acts to the left on the box. A box is pushed to the right but has not yet moved; static friction acts to the left, opposing the tendency to slide.

11.11 Applied Forces and Contact Forces

Applied forces are forces exerted directly by an agent, such as a hand, motor, or person. Their direction is given in the problem. If an applied force is not along a coordinate axis, it must be resolved into components.

When an applied force is at an angle θ above the horizontal, its horizontal component F cosθ and vertical component F sinθ both appear in the force analysis. The vertical component affects the normal force; the horizontal component drives horizontal acceleration.

Example — Applied force at an angle: A rope pulls a sled at 25° above the horizontal with force 60.0 N. The vertical component is T sin 25° = 25.4 N, reducing N. The horizontal component is T cos 25° = 54.4 N, driving horizontal acceleration.

11.12 Net Force from a Free-Body Diagram

Once all forces are drawn and labeled in the FBD, apply Newton’s second law to find the net force and acceleration. Resolve all forces into x and y components, then sum each component separately.

The FBD-to-Equation Procedure

  1. Draw the FBD with all forces labeled.
  2. Choose a coordinate system aligned with the expected acceleration when possible.
  3. Resolve forces not along a coordinate axis into components.
  4. Sum forces in x: Fnet,x = ΣFx = max.
  5. Sum forces in y: Fnet,y = ΣFy = may.
  6. Solve for the unknown.

Worked Example 11.1 — Block on a Horizontal Surface: A 10.0 kg block is pushed right with 40.0 N while friction acts left with 15.0 N. Horizontally, Fnet,x = +40.0 − 15.0 = +25.0 N. Vertically, N − W = 0, so N = 98.1 N. The acceleration is ax = 25.0 / 10.0 = +2.50 m/s².

11.13 Free-Body Diagrams on Inclines

Inclined surface problems require a rotated coordinate system: one axis along the slope and one axis perpendicular to the slope. This makes the normal force entirely perpendicular to the surface and gravity partially along the slope and partially into the surface.

Gravity has components in the rotated system: along the slope, mg sinθ; perpendicular to the slope, mg cosθ. For a frictionless incline, the acceleration down the slope is a = g sinθ.

Worked Example 11.2 — Block on a Frictionless Incline: A 6.0 kg block slides down a frictionless 30° incline. Weight is 58.9 N. The component down the slope is 58.9 sin 30° = 29.4 N. The perpendicular component is 58.9 cos 30° = 51.0 N. Thus N = 51.0 N and a = g sin 30° = 4.91 m/s² down the slope.

Worked Example 11.3 — Block on an Incline with Friction: With μk = 0.20, N = 51.0 N, so fk = 10.2 N up the slope. Net force down the slope is 29.4 − 10.2 = 19.2 N, giving a = 19.2 / 6.0 = 3.2 m/s² down the slope.

11.14 Multi-Object Free-Body Analysis

When two or more objects are connected by a rope, rod, or direct contact, draw a separate FBD for each object. Then apply Newton’s second law to each FBD. Connected objects often share the same acceleration magnitude, which acts as a constraint.

Worked Example 11.4 — Two Blocks Connected by a String: Block A, 3.0 kg, and Block B, 2.0 kg, are connected by a string on a frictionless horizontal surface. A 15.0 N force pulls Block A. As a system, the total mass is 5.0 kg, so a = 15.0 / 5.0 = 3.0 m/s². For Block B alone, tension is the only horizontal force, so T = mBa = 2.0 × 3.0 = 6.0 N.

11.15 Common Free-Body Diagram Mistakes

  1. Including forces the object exerts on others: An FBD shows only forces acting on the chosen object.
  2. Drawing the net force as a force arrow: Net force is computed from the diagram; it is not an additional force.
  3. Wrong direction for the normal force: Normal force is perpendicular to the contact surface and points away from it.
  4. Assuming N = mg on inclines or in elevators: Normal force must be derived from the FBD equations.
  5. Friction in the wrong direction: Kinetic friction opposes sliding; static friction opposes the tendency to slide.
  6. Forgetting a force: Scan for gravity, contact, friction, tension, applied forces, and other given forces.

Common Mistake: After finishing an FBD, check for weight downward, normal forces perpendicular to contact surfaces, tension along ropes away from the object, friction parallel to the surface, all given forces, and no forces exerted by the object on something else.

Chapter Summary

A free-body diagram isolates a single object and shows every external force acting on it as a labeled vector arrow. It is the essential first step for applying Newton’s second law to any mechanics problem.

  • An FBD shows one object with all external forces as labeled arrows.
  • Forces the object exerts on others are not shown.
  • External forces appear in the FBD; internal forces within a chosen system do not.
  • Common forces include weight, normal force, tension, friction, and applied force.
  • Normal force is not always equal to weight; derive it from the FBD equations.
  • On an incline, N = mg cosθ and the gravity component along the slope is mg sinθ.
  • For connected objects, draw one FBD per object and use the shared acceleration as a constraint.
  • The net force is computed from the FBD; it is never drawn as an additional force arrow.

Key Terms

free-body diagram (FBD)
A sketch showing a single isolated object with every external force represented as a labeled arrow.
external force
A force acting on the chosen object from outside the system boundary.
internal force
A force between parts within the chosen system boundary.
normal force
The contact force perpendicular to a surface and away from it.
tension
The pulling force transmitted through a rope, string, or cable.
system
The object or group of objects chosen for analysis.
incline problem
A mechanics problem involving a surface tilted at an angle.
constraint
A condition that restricts motion of connected objects.

Applied Newton’s Law Problems

  1. Two blocks are stacked: Block A, 5.0 kg, on top of Block B, 8.0 kg, on a frictionless floor. A 26.0 N force pushes B to the right. The surfaces between A and B are rough with μs = 0.40. Do A and B move together or does A slide? Find the acceleration and the friction force on A.
  2. Block A, 4.0 kg, and Block B, 6.0 kg, hang from either side of a pulley. Find the acceleration of the system and the tension in the rope. Assume a massless, frictionless pulley and massless rope.
  3. A 2.0 kg block and a 3.0 kg block are connected by a rope on a horizontal frictionless surface. The 3.0 kg block is pulled by a 15.0 N force. Find the acceleration and the tension in the rope.
  4. A 70.0 kg person stands on a scale in an elevator. The scale reads 840 N. Is the elevator accelerating up or down? What is the magnitude of the acceleration?
  5. A 6.0 kg block on a 30° frictionless incline is connected by a string over a pulley to a hanging 4.0 kg block. Find the acceleration and tension.

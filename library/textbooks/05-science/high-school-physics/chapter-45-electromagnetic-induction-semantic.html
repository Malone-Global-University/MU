Chapter 45: Electromagnetic Induction

45.1 The Discovery of Electromagnetic Induction

By 1820, Hans Christian Ørsted had shown that an electric current produces a magnetic field (Chapter 44). The natural next question was whether the reverse was true: can a magnetic field produce an electric current? For over a decade, many physicists tried to produce a current using a static magnet placed near a wire. None succeeded. The crucial insight — that the magnetic field must be changing, not merely present — was discovered independently and nearly simultaneously by Michael Faraday in England and Joseph Henry in the United States in 1831. Physics: Foundations of Physical Science Chapter 45 — Electromagnetic Induction

Historical Note:

Faraday's original experiment used a ring of soft iron wound with two separate coils of wire. When he connected a battery to one coil (the primary), a current appeared briefly in the other coil (the secondary) — but only at the moment of connection or disconnection. A steady current in the primary produced no effect. It was the change in the magnetic field threading the secondary coil that drove the current. Faraday had no mathematical framework for this; he thought in terms of "lines of force" filling space. His physical intuition was so acute that he reached the correct conclusion anyway, and James Clerk Maxwell later gave it the mathematical form we use today. Henry made a similar discovery at about the same time but published later, losing priority. He did, however, discover self-inductance (a coil inducing a voltage in itself due to its own changing current) somewhat before Faraday, and the SI unit of inductance, the henry (H), commemorates him. The unifying idea is this: a changing magnetic environment can push electric charges into motion. Whether the magnetic field changes because a magnet moves, because a current ramps up or down, or because a conductor moves through a steady field, the result is the same — charges experience a force and current can flow if a circuit is present.

45.2 Magnetic Flux

To state the law of induction precisely, we need the concept of magnetic flux. Magnetic flux (Φ, phi) through a surface is a measure of how many magnetic field lines pass through that surface. It combines the field strength, the area of the surface, and the angle between the field lines and the surface.

Φ = B × A × cos(θ)

When the field is perpendicular to the surface (θ = 0°), cosθ = 1 and the flux is

maximum: Φ = BA. When the field is parallel to the surface (θ = 90°), cosθ = 0 and the

flux is zero — the field lines skim along the surface without passing through it. This makes physical sense: flux counts lines passing through the area, not lines running alongside it. Think of it this way: if you hold a wire loop and dip it into a pool of water, the flux is analogous to the volume of water flowing through the loop per unit time — maximum when the loop is horizontal (perpendicular to flow), zero when the loop is vertical (parallel to flow). Key Point:

Flux can be positive or negative depending on the direction of the field through the surface, but only the change in flux matters for induction. A large steady flux produces no current; a small but rapidly changing flux can produce a large current.

45.3 What Causes Induced Current

An induced current appears in a closed conducting loop whenever the magnetic flux through the loop changes. The flux can change in three distinct ways: • The magnetic field B changes in strength while the loop stays fixed. (Example: a nearby electromagnet is switched on or off, or a current in an adjacent coil is varied.) • The area A of the loop changes while the field stays constant. (Example: a loop is deformed, stretched, or compressed inside a uniform field.) • The angle θ between the field and the loop changes. (Example: the loop rotates inside a uniform field — the operating principle of every electric generator.)

In each case, the flux Φ = BA cosθ changes over time, and that changing flux is what

drives the induced current. A static situation — strong field, large loop, but nothing changing — produces no induced current at all. The current depends on the rate of change of flux, not on the flux itself. The mechanism by which changing flux drives current is an induced electric field. A changing magnetic field creates an electric field in the surrounding space even in the absence of any conductor. When a conductor is present, the induced electric field exerts forces on the free charges in the conductor, driving a current. When no conductor is present, the induced electric field still exists — it just has nothing to Physics: Foundations of Physical Science Chapter 45 — Electromagnetic Induction accelerate into a steady flow. This is one of Maxwell's most profound insights: changing magnetic fields and changing electric fields are not independent; each can generate the other.

45.4 Faraday's Law

Faraday's Law states the quantitative relationship between the rate of change of magnetic flux and the induced electromotive force (EMF). In conceptual form:

The Rule:

The induced EMF in a loop is proportional to the rate at which the magnetic flux through the loop is changing. The faster the flux changes, the larger the induced EMF.

ε = −N × (ΔΦ / Δt)

Several consequences follow immediately. First, a coil of N turns is N times more effective than a single loop because each turn contributes an independent EMF that adds to the others. This is why transformer coils and generator windings contain many

turns of wire. Second, the induced EMF is proportional to ΔΦ/Δt, the rate of change of

flux — not the amount of flux. Third, the negative sign tells us the direction of the induced EMF opposes the change, a constraint known as Lenz's Law (Section 45.5).

Example — Faraday's Law calculation

A 200-turn coil experiences a change in

magnetic flux from 0.05 Wb to 0.12 Wb in 0.20 s. ΔΦ = 0.07 Wb. ε = N(ΔΦ/Δt) = 200

× 0.07/0.20 = 70 V. This EMF drives a current through whatever external circuit is

connected to the coil. Faraday's Law is one of the four Maxwell equations that govern all classical electromagnetic phenomena. It tells us that electricity and magnetism are not independent: you cannot have a changing magnetic field without a corresponding electric effect. This mutual dependence is ultimately what allows electromagnetic waves to propagate through empty space (Chapter 46). Physics: Foundations of Physical Science Chapter 45 — Electromagnetic Induction

45.5 Lenz's Law and Direction of Induced Current

Faraday's Law gives the magnitude of the induced EMF. Lenz's Law gives its direction: the induced current always flows in the direction that opposes the change in flux that caused it.

The Rule:

Lenz's Law: The induced current creates a magnetic field that opposes the change in flux through the loop. If the flux is increasing, the induced current's field opposes the increase (reduces the flux). If the flux is decreasing, the induced current's field opposes the decrease (adds to the flux). Lenz's Law is a consequence of energy conservation. If the induced current reinforced the change that caused it, the resulting magnetic field would cause more induction, which would increase the current further, which would increase the field further, producing energy from nothing. Lenz's law prevents this runaway: the induced current always costs energy to drive, and that energy must come from the agent that is changing the flux (e.g., the hand pushing a magnet, or the turbine turning a generator). A helpful mnemonic: the induced current is always fighting the change. Push a north pole toward a loop: the loop becomes an electromagnet with its own north pole facing the approaching magnet, repelling it. Pull the north pole away: the loop becomes a south pole facing the retreating magnet, attracting it. In both cases, you must do work against the magnetic force, and that work becomes electrical energy in the circuit.

Example — Lenz's Law direction

A horizontal loop lies flat. A bar magnet is held above the loop with its north pole pointing downward toward the loop. As the magnet falls closer, the downward flux through the loop increases. Lenz's Law: the induced current must create a field opposing the increase — that is, an upward field through the loop. By the right-hand rule, when the right-hand fingers curl in the direction of current flow and the thumb points upward (through the loop), the current flows counterclockwise as seen from above.

Common Mistake:

Students often confuse the direction of the induced magnetic field with the direction of the induced current. First determine the required field direction (Lenz's Law), then apply the right-hand rule to find the current direction. Always do these as two separate steps. 45.6 Induced EMF

An electromotive force (EMF) is not actually a force — it is a voltage (energy per unit charge) that drives charges around a circuit. Induced EMF plays the same role as the voltage of a battery: it pushes current through the circuit. Unlike a battery, which converts chemical energy to electrical energy, an induced EMF converts mechanical energy (the work done to change the flux) to electrical energy.

The induced EMF drives a current through the loop according to Ohm's Law: I = ε/R,

where R is the total resistance of the circuit. The greater the rate of flux change, the greater the EMF, and the greater the current. The greater the resistance, the smaller the current for the same EMF.

Example — Induced current

A single circular loop of wire (radius 0.10 m, resistance

2.0 Ω) lies perpendicular to a magnetic field that decreases from 0.80 T to 0.20 T in

0.50 s. Area A = π(0.10)² = 0.0314 m². ΔΦ = AΔB = 0.0314 × (0.80

0.20) = 0.0314 ×

0.60 = 0.01885 Wb. ε = ΔΦ/Δt = 0.01885/0.50 = 0.0377 V. I = ε/R = 0.0377/2.0 =

0.019 A 19 mA. By Lenz's Law, since the flux is decreasing, the current flows in the direction that tries to maintain it (right-hand rule determines the actual direction).

The power delivered by the induced EMF is P = εI = ε²/R. This power comes from the

mechanical energy of whatever agent is changing the flux. An electric generator, for example, is mechanically driven by a turbine or engine, and the mechanical power delivered equals (in the ideal case) the electrical power produced. Real generators have resistive losses in the windings and friction in the bearings, reducing efficiency below 100%, but modern large generators achieve efficiencies above 98%. 45.7 Moving Conductors in Magnetic Fields

When a straight conductor of length L moves with velocity v perpendicular to a uniform magnetic field B, an EMF is induced across the conductor. This is sometimes called motional EMF. It arises because the magnetic field exerts a force on the moving

positive charges in the conductor (F = qv × B), pushing them to one end and creating

a charge separation — a voltage.

ε = BLv

This is the basis for railgun technology, MHD (magnetohydrodynamic) propulsion, and some types of electric generators. In a generator, the rotating coil has conductors that sweep through the magnetic field; the motional EMF in each conductor adds up to drive the total generator output. Note that if the conductor is not part of a closed circuit, charges accumulate at the ends until the electric force due to the charge separation exactly cancels the magnetic

force. No current flows, but a voltage ε = BLv exists across the ends. This is analogous

to an open-circuit battery: the EMF is present but no current flows because there is no complete path.

Example — Motional EMF

A 0.50 m copper rod slides along two parallel rails separated by 0.50 m in a 0.30 T magnetic field perpendicular to the rails. The rod

moves at 2.0 m/s. ε = BLv = 0.30 × 0.50 × 2.0 = 0.30 V. If the rails are connected by a

1.5 Ω resistor, the current is I = 0.30/1.5 = 0.20 A. To maintain constant velocity, an

external force must push the rod; that force does work at the rate P = Fv = εI = 0.30

× 0.20 = 0.060 W = 60 mW, which is exactly the power dissipated in the resistor.

45.8 Electric Generators

An electric generator converts mechanical energy into electrical energy by rotating a coil of wire inside a magnetic field (or equivalently, rotating a magnet inside a coil). As the coil rotates, the angle between the field and the coil's normal changes continuously,

causing the flux to oscillate: Φ = BA cos(ωt), where ω is the angular speed in radians

per second. Because the flux changes sinusoidally, so does the induced EMF:

ε(t) = NBAω sin(ωt) = ε₀ sin(ωt)

₀ The output is alternating current (AC): the current changes direction twice per revolution. For a generator spinning at 50 Hz (50 revolutions per second, as in European power systems) or 60 Hz (as in American power systems), the current reverses direction 100 or 120 times per second. To produce direct current (DC), a device called a commutator is used in place of slip rings. The commutator reverses the connection to the external circuit every half turn, so the output voltage always has the same polarity (though it still pulsates). Practical DC generators use multiple coils offset by small angles to smooth out the pulsation. 45.9 Transformers

A transformer uses electromagnetic induction to change the voltage (and correspondingly the current) of an alternating electrical signal. It consists of two coils — the primary and the secondary — wound around a common iron core. The iron core greatly amplifies the magnetic flux linkage between the coils. When an alternating current flows in the primary coil, it creates an alternating magnetic field in the iron core. That alternating field threads the secondary coil and induces an EMF in it by Faraday's Law. The ratio of the output (secondary) voltage to the input (primary) voltage equals the ratio of the number of turns:

V₂ / V₁ = N₂ / N₁

Energy conservation requires that, in an ideal transformer (no losses), the power input

equals the power output: P₁ = P₂, so V₁I₁ = V₂I₂. Therefore:

I₂ / I₁ = N₁ / N₂

Example — Transformer

A step-up transformer has 500 primary turns and 10 000

secondary turns. Primary voltage is 240 V. Secondary voltage: V₂ = V₁ × N₂/N₁ = 240

× 10000/500 = 4800 V. If primary current is 20 A, secondary current: I₂ = I₁ × N₁/N₂ =

20 × 500/10000 = 1.0 A. Primary power: 240 × 20 = 4800 W. Secondary power: 4800

× 1.0 = 4800 W. Confirmed — power is conserved.

Key Point:

Transformers work only with alternating current. A DC current creates a steady magnetic field; a steady field produces no change in flux; no change in flux means no induced EMF in the secondary. This is why the world's power grids use AC: it is the only form of electricity that can be transformed cheaply and efficiently.

45.10 Power Transmission Systems

One of the most important practical uses of transformers is efficient long-distance electrical power transmission. Power is lost as heat in transmission lines due to the

resistance of the wires: P_loss = I²R. To minimise this loss, the current I must be kept as

small as possible.

Since power P = VI, transmitting a given amount of power at very high voltage allows

the current to be very low, dramatically reducing transmission losses. Step-up transformers at power stations raise voltage from the generator output (typically 10–25 kV) to very high transmission voltages (110–765 kV in different countries). Near consumers, step-down transformers reduce the voltage to safe levels for distribution (typically 11 kV at local substations, then again to 240 V or 120 V at the pole-mounted transformers near homes).

Example — Transmission losses A power station generates 100 MW at 20 kV. A step

-up transformer raises this to 400 kV for long-distance transmission. (a) Transmission

current: I = P/V = 100×10⁶/400×10³ = 250 A. (b) If the transmission line has resistance

10 Ω, power lost: P_loss = I²R = 250² × 10 = 625 000 W = 0.625 MW. Loss = 0.625%.

(c) Without the transformer, at 20 kV: I = 100×10⁶/20×10³ = 5000 A. Power lost: 5000²

× 10 = 250 MW — more than the power being transmitted! High-voltage transmission

makes long-distance power delivery practical. The modern electrical grid is a hierarchy of voltage transformations: generation (10–25 kV) →transmission (110–765 kV) →sub-transmission (33–66 kV) →distribution (11 kV) →service (240 V or 120 V). Each step-down uses transformers. 45.11 Induction in Everyday Technology

Electromagnetic induction is not confined to power stations. It underlies a remarkable range of everyday technologies. Wireless Charging Wireless chargers for smartphones, electric toothbrushes, and electric vehicles use inductive coupling. The charger contains a primary coil carrying alternating current at moderate frequency (typically 100–200 kHz for phone chargers, higher for industrial systems). The device contains a secondary coil. Changing current in the primary creates a changing magnetic field that induces an EMF in the secondary, which is rectified and used to charge the battery. No electrical contact is required. Induction Cooktops An induction cooktop contains a coil carrying high-frequency AC (20–50 kHz). When a steel or cast-iron pan is placed on the cooktop surface, the alternating magnetic field induces large eddy currents in the pan's base. These eddy currents, flowing in a

resistive material, heat the pan directly by the I²R effect. The cooktop surface itself

does not heat up (it is glass-ceramic and non-magnetic); only the pan heats. Response is faster than gas or radiant electric, and the surface is safe to touch immediately after removing the pan. Metal Detectors A metal detector contains a transmitter coil and a receiver coil. The transmitter sends AC magnetic flux into the ground. Metal objects in the ground carry induced eddy currents, which create their own magnetic fields. The receiver coil detects the disturbance in the total field. The frequency and phase of the distortion can identify metals (ferrous vs. non-ferrous) and sometimes estimate depth. Magnetic Braking Moving a conducting plate through a magnetic field induces eddy currents in the plate. By Lenz's Law, these currents create forces opposing the motion — a braking force proportional to velocity. Magnetic brakes require no contact and no wear. They are Physics: Foundations of Physical Science Chapter 45 — Electromagnetic Induction used in high-speed rail systems, roller coasters, laboratory instruments, and equipment that must be stopped smoothly and controllably without friction. RFID and NFC Radio-frequency identification (RFID) tags and near-field communication (NFC) chips in contactless cards and phones operate through inductive coupling. A reader creates an alternating magnetic field; the tag's antenna coil harvests energy from this field (via induction), powers the chip, and transmits data back by modulating its own inductance. Passive RFID tags require no battery: they are powered entirely by the induced EMF from the reader.

45.12 Common Mistakes in Induction Analysis

•

Confusing flux and field. Flux (Φ = BA cosθ) is not the same as the magnetic

field B. A large field threading a tiny area gives the same flux as a small field threading a large area. Induced EMF depends on the change in flux, not the change in field alone. • Thinking a large flux induces a current. Only a changing flux induces a current. A coil sitting inside a strong, steady magnetic field has zero induced EMF. Students must always ask: is the flux changing? If not, there is no induction. •

Ignoring the number of turns N. Faraday's Law is ε = NΔΦ/Δt. A 1000-turn coil

experiencing the same rate of flux change as a 1-turn loop produces 1000 times the EMF. Always include N. • Getting Lenz's Law direction backwards. The induced current opposes the change, not the flux itself. If the flux is increasing, the induced field opposes the increase. If the flux is decreasing, the induced field tries to maintain it. Use two steps: (1) determine required field direction from Lenz's Law; (2) use right-hand rule to find current direction. • Applying transformer equations to DC. Transformers work only with changing (AC) currents. A DC current produces a steady field, no change in flux, no induced EMF, no transformer action. Using the turns ratio with a DC input gives a wrong (and physically impossible) result. • Forgetting that step-up voltage means step-down current. Power conservation:

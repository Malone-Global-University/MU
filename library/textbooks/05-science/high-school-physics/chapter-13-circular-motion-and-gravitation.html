13.1 Why Circular Motion Is Different from Linear Motion

In linear motion, acceleration changes the magnitude of velocity. In circular motion at constant speed, acceleration changes the direction of velocity while the magnitude stays constant. Both are accelerations — both require a net force by Newton’s second law — but they feel very different and require different analysis.

A key insight: an object moving in a circle at constant speed is always accelerating, even though its speedometer would read constant. The velocity vector is always tangent to the circle, and since the direction of a tangent changes continuously as the object moves around, the velocity is constantly changing. Any change in velocity is acceleration.

The acceleration required to maintain circular motion points toward the center of the circle at every instant. This is called centripetal acceleration. The corresponding force is the centripetal force.

Key Point

Centripetal force is not a new type of force. It is simply the name for whatever force, or net force, is directed toward the center of curvature and provides the centripetal acceleration. Gravity, tension, friction, and normal force can all serve as centripetal force in different situations.

13.2 Velocity in Circular Motion

For an object moving in a circle of radius r with constant speed v, the velocity vector is always tangent to the circle — perpendicular to the radius at every point. The speed v is the magnitude of this velocity vector and remains constant for uniform circular motion.

The period T is the time to complete one full circle. The speed is the circumference divided by the period:

v = 2πr / T

The frequency f is the number of complete circles per second, measured in hertz. Frequency and period are reciprocals:

f = 1 / T

Example — Circular Speed

A satellite orbits Earth at radius r = 7.0 × 106 m with period T = 5820 s. Speed = 2π × 7.0 × 106 / 5820 = 7.56 × 103 m/s = 7.56 km/s.

13.3 Centripetal Acceleration

Even at constant speed, an object in circular motion has acceleration because its velocity direction changes. This centripetal acceleration always points toward the center of the circle.

ac = v2 / r

Equivalent expressions using period T or frequency f are:

ac = 4π2r / T2 = 4π2rf2

Centripetal acceleration increases with speed squared and decreases with increasing radius. Double the speed, and the centripetal acceleration becomes four times greater. Double the radius, and the centripetal acceleration becomes half as large.

Example — Centripetal Acceleration

A ball on a 0.80 m string moves at 3.0 m/s. ac = (3.0)2 / 0.80 = 9.0 / 0.80 = 11.25 m/s2 toward the center.

13.4 Centripetal Force

By Newton’s second law, the centripetal acceleration requires a net force directed toward the center of the circle. This is the centripetal force:

Fc = mv2 / r

This equation is not a new force law; it is Newton’s second law applied to circular motion. Whatever real force provides the centripetal acceleration — gravity, tension, friction, normal force, or any combination — its inward component must equal mv2/r for the object to maintain circular motion.

The Rule

To solve any circular motion problem: identify what force, or component of force, points toward the center; set that force equal to mv2/r; solve for the unknown.

In different physical situations, different forces supply the centripetal acceleration. Recognizing the source of centripetal force is the first step in any circular motion problem.

Worked Example 13.1 — Ball on a String, Horizontal Plane

A 0.25 kg ball swings on a 0.60 m string in a horizontal circle at 4.0 m/s. Find the tension.

  1. Centripetal force needed: Fc = mv2/r = 0.25 × (4.0)2 / 0.60 = 6.67 N.
  2. Source of centripetal force: tension.
  3. Set equal: T = 6.67 N.

Worked Example 13.2 — Car Rounding a Flat Curve

A 1200 kg car rounds a flat curve of radius 80.0 m at 20.0 m/s. Find the friction force needed and check if it is possible with μs = 0.50.

  1. Centripetal force needed: Fc = mv2/r = 1200 × 400 / 80.0 = 6000 N.
  2. Source: static friction.
  3. Maximum available friction: fs,max = μsN = 0.50 × 1200 × 9.81 = 5886 N.
  4. Compare: 6000 N > 5886 N, so the car cannot make this curve at 20 m/s with μs = 0.50.

13.6 Banking and Circular Motion Applications

A banked curve is tilted at an angle θ inward so that the horizontal component of the normal force provides centripetal acceleration, reducing or eliminating the need for friction. At the ideal banking angle for a given speed, no friction is needed at all.

tanθ = v2 / (rg)

Example — Banking Angle

A highway curve of radius 120 m is to be banked so cars at 25 m/s need no friction. tanθ = (25)2 / (120 × 9.81) = 625 / 1177 = 0.531, so θ = 28.0°.

Vertical circular motion introduces an additional complication: the gravitational force changes its contribution to centripetal force depending on position in the loop. At the bottom of a loop, the normal force must exceed weight to provide centripetal acceleration upward. At the top of a loop, weight and normal force both point toward the center; the minimum speed occurs when N = 0.

Worked Example 13.3 — Minimum Speed at Top of a Loop

A roller coaster loop has radius 12.0 m. What is the minimum speed at the top to maintain contact?

  1. At the top, centripetal direction is downward: mg + N = mv2/r.
  2. At minimum speed, N = 0: mg = mvmin2/r.
  3. vmin = √(gr) = √(9.81 × 12.0) = √117.7 = 10.9 m/s.

13.7 Universal Gravitation

Gravity is universal: every object in the universe with mass attracts every other object with mass. This was Newton’s great insight, published in the Principia in 1687: the same force that causes an apple to fall causes the Moon to orbit Earth, and the same force governs planets orbiting the Sun.

Before Newton, gravity and the celestial motions of planets were considered entirely separate phenomena. Newton unified them with a single mathematical law, showed that the inverse-square relationship produced Kepler’s elliptical orbits, and demonstrated that the force causing free fall on Earth was exactly sufficient to keep the Moon in its orbit. This unification was one of the greatest intellectual achievements in scientific history.

Historical Note

Newton reportedly said that the idea of universal gravitation came to him when he saw an apple fall in his garden in 1666, during a forced retreat from Cambridge due to plague. Whether the story is literally true is disputed, but the timing — his “annus mirabilis” — is well documented. He did not publish his results until 1687, spending years verifying the quantitative predictions.

13.8 Newton’s Law of Gravitation

The gravitational force between two masses is attractive, acts along the line joining them, and has magnitude:

Fg = Gm1m2 / r2

Key features of the law: the force is always attractive; it is proportional to each mass separately; it is inversely proportional to the square of the separation.

Worked Example 13.4 — Force Between Two Objects

Find the gravitational force between two 70.0 kg people separated by 1.0 m.

  1. Fg = Gm1m2/r2 = 6.674 × 10−11 × 70.0 × 70.0 / (1.0)2.
  2. Fg = 6.674 × 10−11 × 4900 = 3.27 × 10−7 N.
  3. This is about 0.33 micronewtons — negligible in everyday life.

13.9 Gravitational Fields

Rather than thinking of gravity as an action-at-a-distance force, physicists often describe it using the concept of a gravitational field. The gravitational field g at any point in space is defined as the gravitational force per unit mass that would act on a small test mass placed there:

g = Fg / m = −GM / r2 (pointing toward M)

Near Earth’s surface, the gravitational field is approximately uniform with magnitude g = 9.81 m/s2, directed downward. This is the g used throughout Chapters 6–12. As altitude increases, g decreases:

g(r) = GMEarth / r2

Example — g at Altitude

At 400 km above Earth’s surface: r = 6.37 × 106 + 4.0 × 105 = 6.77 × 106 m. g = 6.674 × 10−11 × 5.97 × 1024 / (6.77 × 106)2 = 8.69 m/s2. Gravity at the ISS altitude is only about 11% less than at the surface.

13.10 Weight vs. Mass Revisited

We can now revisit the weight-mass distinction with greater precision. The weight of an object is the gravitational force on it: W = mg, where g is the local gravitational field strength. Mass m is constant; weight varies with location because g varies.

An astronaut on the ISS, at 400 km altitude, experiences g ≈ 8.69 m/s2. Their weight is W = m × 8.69 N — not zero. The astronaut appears weightless because the station and the astronaut are in the same free-fall orbit. Both accelerate toward Earth at the same rate, so there is no contact force between them. The normal force is zero, giving the sensation of weightlessness.

Key Point

Astronauts in orbit are not in zero gravity. They are in continuous free fall. The sensation of weightlessness is the absence of a normal force, not the absence of gravity. The gravitational force on ISS astronauts is about 89% of their surface weight.

13.11 Orbital Motion

An orbit is a continuous free fall around a body. The satellite falls toward Earth due to gravity, but moves so fast horizontally that Earth’s surface curves away at the same rate the satellite falls. The result is a circular or elliptical path that never intersects the surface.

For a circular orbit, the gravitational force provides exactly the centripetal force needed:

GMm / r2 = mv2 / r

Solving for the orbital speed:

vorbit = √(GM / r)

All objects at the same orbital radius orbit at the same speed, regardless of mass.

Worked Example 13.5 — Orbital Speed of the ISS

The ISS orbits at r = 6.77 × 106 m. Find its orbital speed.

  1. v = √(GMEarth/r) = √(6.674 × 10−11 × 5.97 × 1024 / 6.77 × 106).
  2. Numerator: 6.674 × 10−11 × 5.97 × 1024 = 3.984 × 1014.
  3. Divide by r: 3.984 × 1014 / 6.77 × 106 = 5.885 × 107.
  4. v = √(5.885 × 107) = 7672 m/s ≈ 7.67 km/s.

13.12 Satellites and Planetary Motion

The orbital period T is related to orbital radius r by combining v = 2πr/T with v = √(GM/r):

T2 = (4π2 / GM) r3

This relationship, first discovered empirically by Johannes Kepler in 1619 from astronomical observations, was later derived theoretically by Newton from his gravitational law. It is one of the great confirmations that Newton’s mechanics and gravity are correct.

13.13 Escape Velocity and Orbital Concepts

Escape velocity is the minimum speed required for an object to escape a planet’s gravitational pull entirely — to reach infinite distance without further propulsion. It is found by setting the object’s kinetic energy equal to the work needed to escape the gravitational well:

vescape = √(2GM / r)

Note that vescape = √2 × vorbit: escape velocity is about 1.41 times the circular orbital speed at the same radius. For Earth’s surface, vescape ≈ 11.2 km/s.

Geosynchronous orbit is the radius at which the orbital period equals Earth’s rotation period, 24 hours. A satellite in geosynchronous orbit above the equator appears stationary from the ground — this is the basis for communication and weather satellites. Geosynchronous radius is about 42,200 km from Earth’s center.

13.14 Common Mistakes in Circular Motion Analysis

Mistake 1: Inventing a “centrifugal force” pointing outward

In an inertial reference frame, there is no outward centrifugal force. The sensation of being pushed outward in a turning car is inertia — your body tends to continue in a straight line while the car turns. Centrifugal force is a fictitious force that appears only in rotating reference frames.

Common Mistake

Never add a centrifugal force pointing outward to a free-body diagram for circular motion. The centripetal force is the net inward force. If you draw something outward, you are double-counting or inventing a fictitious force.

Mistake 2: Setting centripetal force equal to a single force when multiple forces act

The centripetal force is the net inward force. Write Newton’s second law in the radial direction and sum all inward force components. Only then set that sum equal to mv2/r.

Mistake 3: Confusing centripetal acceleration with tangential acceleration

Centripetal acceleration changes the direction of velocity. Tangential acceleration changes the speed. Uniform circular motion has centripetal acceleration only; non-uniform circular motion has both.

Mistake 4: Using weight for centripetal force in orbital problems

In orbital problems, gravity is the centripetal force: set Fg = mv2/r. The satellite’s weight is not zero; it equals Fg, which provides the centripetal acceleration.

Mistake 5: Not converting units before using gravitational formulas

G = 6.674 × 10−11 N·m2/kg2 requires mass in kilograms and distance in meters. Convert all distances and masses before substituting.

Chapter Summary

Circular motion requires a net inward, or centripetal, force. Gravity is the centripetal force for orbits. Newton’s law of universal gravitation governs all gravitational interactions.

  • Uniform circular motion: constant speed, continuously changing velocity direction; always accelerating.
  • Centripetal acceleration: ac = v2/r, directed toward center of circle.
  • Centripetal force: Fc = mv2/r, directed toward center; provided by real forces such as tension, friction, gravity, and normal force.
  • Centripetal force is not a new force type — it is the label for whatever net inward force maintains circular motion.
  • Banking angle for a curve: tanθ = v2/(rg) for friction-free cornering.
  • Newton’s law of gravitation: Fg = Gm1m2/r2; attractive; inverse-square law.
  • Gravitational field g = GM/r2; decreases with altitude; near Earth’s surface, about 9.81 m/s2.
  • Orbital speed: v = √(GM/r); depends only on M and r, not on satellite mass.
  • Kepler’s Third Law: T2 = (4π2/GM)r3.
  • Escape velocity: vesc = √(2GM/r) = √2 × vorbit.
  • Orbiting astronauts are weightless in sensation but not in gravity — they are in continuous free fall.

Key Terms

uniform circular motion
Motion at constant speed along a circular path; velocity is always tangent to the circle; acceleration is always toward the center.
centripetal acceleration
The acceleration directed toward the center of a circular path: ac = v2/r; responsible for changing the direction of velocity.
centripetal force
The net inward force required to maintain circular motion: Fc = mv2/r; not a new force type but a label for the net inward real force.
period (T)
The time for one complete revolution; T = 2πr/v for uniform circular motion.
frequency (f)
The number of complete revolutions per second; f = 1/T; units: Hz.
centrifugal force
A fictitious outward force that appears in rotating reference frames; does not exist in inertial frames and should not be included in FBDs.
universal gravitation
Newton’s law that every mass attracts every other mass: Fg = Gm1m2/r2.
gravitational constant (G)
The universal constant in Newton’s gravity law: G = 6.674 × 10−11 N·m2/kg2.
gravitational field
The gravitational force per unit mass at a point in space; g = GM/r2 directed toward M; units m/s2.
orbital speed
The speed required to maintain a circular orbit at radius r: v = √(GM/r).
Kepler’s Third Law
The orbital period squared is proportional to the orbital radius cubed: T2 = (4π2/GM)r3.
escape velocity
The minimum speed to escape a gravitational field: vesc = √(2GM/r).
geosynchronous orbit
The orbital radius at which the period equals Earth’s rotation period; about 42,200 km from Earth’s center; satellite appears stationary from the ground.

Circular Motion Problems

  1. A 0.40 kg ball on a 0.75 m string swings in a horizontal circle at 5.0 m/s. Find: (a) centripetal acceleration; (b) tension in the string.
  2. A car of mass 1100 kg rounds a flat curve (r = 50.0 m) at 15.0 m/s. (a) Find the centripetal force. (b) What is the minimum μs needed?
  3. A highway curve of radius 200 m is banked at 12°. At what speed can a car round it without relying on friction?
  4. A roller coaster loop has radius 15.0 m. (a) What minimum speed at the top keeps the car on the track? (b) What normal force does a 70.0 kg rider experience at the bottom if traveling at 22.0 m/s?
  5. A 1.5 kg mass swings on a 1.2 m rope in a vertical circle. (a) What is the tension at the bottom of the swing when moving at 4.0 m/s? (b) What is the tension at the top if moving at 3.5 m/s?
  6. A satellite travels in a circular orbit of radius 8.0 × 106 m around Earth. Find: (a) centripetal acceleration; (b) orbital speed; (c) period.

Gravitation Problems

  1. Find the gravitational force between Earth (m = 5.97 × 1024 kg) and the Moon (m = 7.35 × 1022 kg) separated by 3.84 × 108 m.
  2. Two identical 5.0 kg balls are 0.50 m apart. Find the gravitational force between them.
  3. At what height above Earth’s surface does g = 4.905 m/s2, half of surface value? Earth radius = 6.37 × 106 m.
  4. Compare the gravitational force on a 70 kg person at Earth’s surface to the force at 400 km altitude.
  5. What is the weight of a 50.0 kg astronaut on the Moon (gMoon = 1.62 m/s2)? On Mars (gMars = 3.71 m/s2)?

Orbital Mechanics Exercises

  1. Find the orbital speed and period of a satellite at r = 7.0 × 106 m from Earth’s center. MEarth = 5.97 × 1024 kg.
  2. The Moon orbits Earth in T = 27.3 days at r = 3.84 × 108 m. Verify this is consistent with g = 9.81 m/s2 at Earth’s surface using the inverse-square law.
  3. Find the radius of geosynchronous orbit (T = 86,400 s). MEarth = 5.97 × 1024 kg.

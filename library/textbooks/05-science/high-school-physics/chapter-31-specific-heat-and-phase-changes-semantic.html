Specific Heat and Phase Changes

31.1 Why Different Materials Heat Differently

When you apply the same amount of thermal energy to equal masses of different materials, their temperatures rise by different amounts. This is not mysterious: it reflects the different internal structures of the materials and the different ways in which their atoms and molecules can absorb and store energy. In a monoatomic gas (like helium), molecules can only absorb energy as translational kinetic energy — three independent directions of motion. In a diatomic gas (like nitrogen), molecules can also absorb energy as rotational kinetic energy (two independent rotational axes at ordinary temperatures) and vibrational energy. More ways to store energy means more energy is required to raise the temperature by one degree. In solids, atoms vibrate in three dimensions. According to the Dulong-Petit law (which works well for many metals at room temperature), each atom has six modes of energy storage: three translational kinetic and three potential energy modes for vibration. This

gives metals a characteristic molar heat capacity of about 3R = 24.9 J/(mol·K) — and

indeed most metals cluster around this value. Water is exceptional. Each water molecule has translational motion, rotational motion in three dimensions, and complex intermolecular hydrogen bonding interactions. These multiple energy storage modes give water one of the highest specific heats of any common substance — 4186 J/(kg·K) — far higher than metals. This is why oceans are such effective thermal reservoirs, moderating coastal climates by absorbing and releasing vast amounts of energy with relatively small temperature changes.

31.2 Heat Capacity vs. Specific Heat

Heat capacity (C) is the energy required to raise the temperature of a specific object (not a unit of material) by 1°C (or 1 K). It depends on both the material and the amount of material. Units: J/K or J/°C. Specific heat capacity (c), usually called simply specific heat, is the energy required to raise the temperature of one kilogram of a material by 1°C (or 1 K). It is a material property, independent of the amount. Units: J/(kg·K) or J/(kg·°C). Molar heat capacity (C_m) is the energy per mole per kelvin: J/(mol·K). It is used in chemistry and statistical mechanics, where amounts are counted in moles. The

relationship: C_m = c × M, where M is the molar mass in kg/mol.

The heat capacity of a specific object is simply C = mc, where m is the mass and c is

the specific heat of the material. A 2.0 kg copper pan (c_Cu = 387 J/kg·K) has heat

capacity C = 2.0 × 387 = 774 J/K. This means every joule added to the pan raises its

temperature by 1/774 °C 0.0013°C. 31.3 The Specific Heat Equation

The heat Q required to change the temperature of a mass m of material by ΔT, given specific heat c, is:

Q = mcΔT

The sign convention: Q > 0 when heat is added to a substance (temperature rises, ΔT > 0). Q < 0 when heat is removed (temperature falls, ΔT < 0). Always check the sign of your answer: if a substance is heated and you get Q < 0, something is wrong.

Step 1: Identify: m (kg), c (J/kg·K from table), T_i and T_f.

Step 2: Compute: ΔT = T_f T_i. Note sign: positive if heating, negative if cooling.

Step 3: Apply: Q = mcΔT. State the result with units and sign.

Example — Heating water Heat 0.250 kg of water from 20.0°C to 80.0°C. ΔT =

60.0°C. Q = 0.250 × 4186 × 60.0 = 62 790 J 62.8 kJ.

Example — Cooling iron Cool 1.50 kg of iron from 200°C to 25°C. ΔT = 175°C. Q =

1.50 × 449 × ( 175) = 117 862 J 118 kJ. (Negative: heat is removed from the

iron.)

31.4 Interpreting Specific Heat Physically

A high specific heat means the material resists temperature change: it absorbs a large amount of energy per degree of temperature rise. Water’s specific heat of 4186 J/(kg·K) is about 10 times higher than most metals. This means that a kilogram of water absorbs 10 times more energy than a kilogram of iron to achieve the same temperature rise. This has profound consequences for climate and biology. The ocean covers 71% of Earth’s surface and has enormous mass and high specific heat. It absorbs solar energy during summer, moderating temperature rises; it releases stored energy in winter, moderating temperature drops. Coastal cities have milder climates than inland cities at the same latitude because of this thermal buffering by the ocean. The human body is about 60% water by mass, giving it a high specific heat. This helps maintain a stable body temperature despite large fluctuations in metabolic heat production (exercise generates 5–20 times more heat than rest) and environmental temperature. A person exercising vigorously produces about 500 W of heat; without the high heat capacity of body tissue, their core temperature would skyrocket. Low specific heat materials (metals) change temperature quickly for a given energy input. This is why metal pots and pans heat up quickly on a stove. The relatively low c of iron and copper means a small amount of energy rapidly raises the cooking surface temperature. This is useful for cooking, but it also means metal surfaces cool quickly when removed from the heat source.

31.6 Calorimetry

Calorimetry is the experimental measurement of heat exchange. A calorimeter is an insulated container designed to minimize heat loss to the surroundings, so that all heat exchanged within the system stays within it. The calorimeter may be a simple expanded polystyrene cup (coffee-cup calorimeter) or a precision bomb calorimeter for measuring combustion energies. The calorimetry method for measuring specific heat: place a known mass of hot material into a known mass of water (or other calorimeter fluid) at a known temperature. Measure the equilibrium temperature. Apply energy conservation. The heat lost by the hot material equals the heat gained by the water and calorimeter:

-Q_hot = Q_cold ⇒ Σ mcΔT = 0

In a real calorimeter, the calorimeter itself (container, thermometer, stirrer) absorbs some heat. This is accounted for by the calorimeter ’s water equivalent — the mass of water that would absorb the same heat as the calorimeter itself. In school-level problems, the calorimeter ’s heat capacity is often given directly or treated as negligible.

Example — Finding specific heat by calorimetry A 0.200 kg unknown metal at

95.0°C is dropped into 0.500 kg of water at 15.0°C in a calorimeter (negligible heat

capacity). Equilibrium temperature = 18.8°C. Metal: m_m c_m (18.8 95.0) = water:

0.500 × 4186 × (18.8 15.0). 0.200 c_m ( 76.2) = 2093 × 3.8 = 7953.4. c_m = 7953.4

/ (0.200 × 76.2) = 521 J/kg·K 520 J/kg·K. This is close to the specific heat of

chromium (448) or titanium (520), so the metal is probably titanium.

31.7 Thermal Equilibrium in Mixing Problems

When two or more substances at different temperatures are mixed and allowed to exchange heat until they reach a common temperature, the equilibrium temperature is determined by energy conservation. The total thermal energy of the system is conserved (assuming no heat exchange with the environment and no phase changes).

Setting up mixing problems: write Q_i = m_i c_i (T_f T_i) for each substance, where

T_f is the unknown final temperature. Apply ΣQ_i = 0. Solve for T_f. This gives:

T_f = Σ(m_i c_i T_i) / Σ(m_i c_i)

The equilibrium temperature is always between the two extreme initial temperatures — never higher than the hottest initial temperature or lower than the coldest. If your answer violates this, an arithmetic error has been made. The equilibrium temperature is closer to the temperature of the component with the larger heat capacity (mc), which changes temperature the least.

Study Tip: Check: In a mixing problem, T_f must lie between T_lowest and T_highest.

If it does not, a sign error has been made. Also check: the substance that starts hotter ends up with Q < 0 (it released heat); the substance that starts cooler ends up with Q > 0 (it gained heat).

31.8 States of Matter and Molecular Energy

Matter exists in several states: solid, liquid, gas, and plasma. The state of a substance depends on the competition between the kinetic energy of its molecules (which drives them apart) and the intermolecular attractive forces (which hold them together). Higher temperatures give molecules more kinetic energy, favoring less ordered, more energetic states. In a solid, molecules are locked into a regular lattice by strong intermolecular forces. They vibrate about fixed positions but cannot move past each other. Solids have definite shape and volume. The molecules have both kinetic (vibrational) energy and potential energy stored in the distorted intermolecular bonds. In a liquid, molecules have enough kinetic energy to move past each other, giving the liquid fluidity and allowing it to take the shape of its container. But the molecules are still close enough to experience strong attractive forces, giving the liquid definite volume. The molecular arrangement is disordered but still dense. In a gas, molecules have sufficient kinetic energy to overcome intermolecular attractions almost entirely. They move freely and rapidly, separated by large distances compared to the molecular size. Gases have no definite shape or volume — they expand to fill any container. Most of the gas’s energy is translational kinetic energy.

31.9 Phase Changes

A phase change (also called a phase transition) is a change in the state of matter: solid to liquid (melting), liquid to solid (freezing), liquid to gas (vaporization/boiling), gas to liquid (condensation), and solid to gas (sublimation). Each phase change involves a change in the intermolecular potential energy of the substance, not its kinetic energy. The most important feature of phase changes: they occur at constant temperature. During a phase change, heat is being added (or removed) but the temperature does not change. For example, water at 100°C at atmospheric pressure will absorb heat from a flame and boil — the steam produced is also initially at 100°C. The added energy breaks intermolecular bonds, changing the state, not raising the temperature. Why is there no temperature change during a phase change? Temperature measures the average kinetic energy of the particles. During a phase change, the energy goes into (or comes from) changing the potential energy of the particles — breaking or forming intermolecular bonds. Kinetic energy (and hence temperature) remains constant throughout. This is a clear example of the distinction between temperature and energy.

The Rule: During a phase change: temperature is constant. Heat is being absorbed or

released. The energy goes into changing the state of matter (breaking or forming intermolecular bonds), not into changing the kinetic energy of molecules.

31.10 Latent Heat

Latent heat is the energy per unit mass required to complete a phase change at constant temperature. The word ‘latent’ means hidden — the energy seems to disappear from view during the phase change because temperature does not change. There are two main types: Latent heat of fusion (L_f): the heat per unit mass required to melt a solid (or released

when a liquid freezes) at constant temperature. For water: L_f = 3.34 × 10⁵ J/kg = 334

kJ/kg. Latent heat of vaporization (L_v): the heat per unit mass required to vaporize a liquid

(or released when a gas condenses) at constant temperature. For water: L_v = 2.26 ×

10⁶ J/kg = 2260 kJ/kg.

Note: L_v for water (2260 kJ/kg) is about 6.8 times larger than L_f (334 kJ/kg). It takes

far more energy to vaporize water than to melt ice. This is because vaporization must completely overcome all intermolecular attractive forces, while melting only disrupts the regular crystal lattice while maintaining molecular proximity. 31.11 Heating Curves and Cooling Curves

A heating curve is a graph of temperature vs. heat added (or time, if heat is added at constant rate) for a substance heated from a low temperature through phase changes to high temperature. It reveals the specific heat and latent heat properties of the substance visually. For a substance such as water, the heating curve has five distinct regions. In region 1 (ice below 0°C), the curve is a rising slope: the temperature of ice rises as energy is added. The slope equals 1/(mc_ice). In region 2 (ice melting at 0°C), the curve is a horizontal plateau: the temperature remains at 0°C while all the ice melts. The length of

the plateau represents the latent heat of fusion, Q = mL_f. In region 3 (water from 0°C

to 100°C), the temperature rises again with slope 1/(mc_water). In region 4 (water boiling at 100°C), another horizontal plateau represents the latent heat of vaporization,

Q = mL_v. In region 5 (steam above 100°C), the temperature rises with slope

1/(mc_steam). The cooling curve is the mirror image: starting from steam and cooling to ice, the same plateaus appear at 100°C (condensation, releasing L_v) and 0°C (freezing, releasing L_f). Latent heat is always released during the reverse phase change. The relative lengths of the regions reveal the relative magnitudes of the thermal quantities. For water, the vaporization plateau is much longer than the fusion plateau because L_v ≫ L_f. The rising temperature regions for liquid water are steep (low slope

= large mc → slow temperature rise per joule).

31.12 Energy During Melting, Boiling, and Condensation

Solving problems involving phase changes requires identifying all the thermal processes involved and adding their heat requirements. A substance may need to: (1) change

temperature in a single phase (use Q = mcΔT), (2) change phase at constant

temperature (use Q = mL), or (3) do both in sequence.

For complex problems, draw the heating curve first and identify all segments. Calculate Q for each segment separately, then add them.

Step 1: List phases: Identify all thermal segments (heating ice, melting, heating water,

boiling, heating steam).

Step 2: Compute each Q: Use Q = mcΔT for temperature-change segments; Q =

±mL for phase-change segments.

Step 3: Sum: Q_total = Q_1 + Q_2 + Q_3 + …

Example — Melting and heating ice to steam Find total heat to convert 0.100 kg of

ice at 20°C to steam at 120°C.

  1. Heat ice 20°C → 0°C: Q₁ = 0.100 × 2090 × 20 = 4 180 J
  2. Melt ice at 0°C: Q₂ = 0.100 × 334 000 = 33 400 J
  3. Heat water 0°C → 100°C: Q₃ = 0.100 × 4186 × 100 = 41 860 J
  4. Boil water at 100°C: Q₄ = 0.100 × 2 260 000 = 226 000 J
  5. Heat steam 100°C → 120°C: Q₅ = 0.100 × 2010 × 20 = 4 020 J

Total: Q = 4 180 + 33 400 + 41 860 + 226 000 + 4 020 = 309 460 J 309 kJ

Notice that boiling (Q₄ = 226 kJ) accounts for about 73% of the total energy

requirement. This is why steam is such an effective carrier of thermal energy — it carries a large latent heat that it releases when it condenses, making it ideal for industrial heating, sterilization, and power generation. Condensation is particularly important in heat pumps and refrigerators. The refrigerant in a refrigerator absorbs latent heat as it evaporates (cooling the interior) and releases that latent heat as it condenses (at the exterior coils). The large latent heat values make phase-change refrigeration far more efficient than sensible heat refrigeration would be.

31.13 Common Mistakes in Thermal Calculations

  • Forgetting to include phase-change steps. When a substance crosses a melting

or boiling point, the latent heat Q = mL must be added before the temperature

can change on the other side of the transition. Skipping this step gives a large error.

  • Using the wrong latent heat. L_f (fusion) applies to melting/freezing; L_v

(vaporization) applies to boiling/condensing. Do not use L_v for melting or vice versa.

  • Using the wrong specific heat for the wrong phase. Water, ice, and steam have

different specific heats (4186, 2090, and 2010 J/kg·K respectively). Use c_ice for ice, c_water for liquid water, c_steam for steam.

  • Getting the sign of Q wrong in calorimetry. The substance that loses heat has

negative Q; the substance that gains heat has positive Q. If Q_hot + Q_cold = 0,

and the hot substance cools, Q_hot < 0. Setting up Q_hot = Q_cold (without the

negative sign) overcounts by a factor of 2.

  • Mixing up ΔT and T in the specific heat equation. Q = mcΔT uses the change in

temperature, not the final or initial temperature alone. ΔT = T_f T_i.

  • Assuming the equilibrium temperature is the arithmetic average. T_f = (T_1 +

T_2)/2 only when the two substances have equal heat capacities (m₁c₁ = m₂c₂).

Otherwise, T_f is a weighted average that depends on both masses and specific heats.

  • Thinking temperature rises during a phase change. During melting, boiling,

freezing, or condensation, the temperature is constant. If you calculate Q =

mcΔT through a phase boundary without including the latent heat, your answer is wrong.

Common Mistake: Always check your heating curve. Every time a phase boundary is

crossed, a latent heat term Q = ±mL must appear in the calculation before the

temperature resumes changing. Missing a phase transition is the most common error in multi-stage thermal problems.

Chapter Summary

Different materials require different amounts of heat to achieve the same temperature change because of differences in their molecular structure and the number of ways they store energy.

  • Specific heat c: heat per unit mass per degree. Q = mcΔT. High c means slow

temperature change per joule (water: 4186 J/kg·K; iron: 449 J/kg·K).

  • Calorimetry: energy conservation in a thermally isolated system: ΣmcΔT = 0 or

Q_lost = Q_gained.

  • Equilibrium temperature: T_f = Σ(m_i c_i T_i) / Σ(m_i c_i). Lies between extreme

initial temperatures, closest to the component with largest mc.

  • Phase changes: melting, boiling, condensation, freezing, sublimation. Occur at

constant temperature. Energy goes into changing molecular potential energy (bonds), not kinetic energy.

  • Latent heat Q = mL. Fusion L_f (melting/freezing). Vaporization L_v

(boiling/condensing). Water: L_f = 334 kJ/kg; L_v = 2260 kJ/kg.

  • Heating curves have rising segments (Q = mcΔT) and flat plateaus (Q = mL) at

each phase change. Total heat is sum of all segments.

  • Steam is a highly effective heat carrier because it carries large latent heat of

vaporization that is released on condensation.

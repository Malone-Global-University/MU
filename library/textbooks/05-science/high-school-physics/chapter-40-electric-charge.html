Chapter 40: Electric Charge

40.1 What Electric Charge Is

Electric charge is a fundamental intrinsic property of certain subatomic particles. Like mass, it is not explained by any more fundamental property — it simply is. Unlike mass, charge comes in two varieties (positive and negative) and can cancel: equal amounts of positive and negative charge in the same region produce no net charge effect. Charge is quantised: it comes only in integer multiples of the elementary charge e = 1.602 × 10 ¹⁹ C. The SI unit of charge is the coulomb (C). One coulomb is an enormous amount of charge — the charge on about 6.24 × 10¹¸ elementary particles. The charge of a single electron is e =

1.602 × 10 ¹⁹ C; a proton carries +e = +1.602 × 10 ¹⁹ C.

No free particle with a charge smaller than e has ever been observed (quarks have fractional charge, but they are always confined in groups whose total charge is an integer multiple of e). Electric charge creates an electric force. Two charged objects exert forces on each other: like charges (both positive, or both negative) repel; unlike charges (one positive, one negative) attract. The strength of this force depends on the amount of charge and the distance between the charges, following Coulomb’s Law (Section 40.10). Key Point:

Electric charge is analogous to mass in classical mechanics: both are fundamental properties of matter, both create forces (gravitational for mass; electric for charge), and both are conserved. But charge can be negative, creating repulsion as well as attraction, while mass is always positive and gravity is always attractive.

40.2 Positive and Negative Charge

The labelling of charge as “positive” and “negative” is a convention introduced by Benjamin Franklin in the 18th century. Franklin did not know about electrons and protons; he simply observed that there were two kinds of charge that cancelled each other. He chose to call the charge acquired by a glass rod rubbed with silk “positive” and the charge acquired by rubber rubbed with fur “negative.” We still use this convention. The choice of sign for which type of particle to call positive was arbitrary, but it has consequences: in a metal wire carrying conventional current, current is defined as the direction positive charges would flow, which is opposite to the direction electrons (the actual charge carriers) move. This historical accident occasionally causes confusion but is deeply embedded in all engineering and physics convention. The key physical rules are: like charges repel, unlike charges attract. Two positive charges push each other apart; two negative charges push each other apart; a positive and a negative charge pull toward each other. The magnitude of this force depends on how much charge is present and the distance between them.

40.3 Protons, Electrons, and Ions

In ordinary matter, the charge of an atom is determined by the numbers of protons (positive, in the nucleus) and electrons (negative, in orbitals around the nucleus). A neutral atom has equal numbers of protons and electrons, giving zero net charge. The nuclear charge of a proton is +1.602 × 10 ¹⁹ C; each electron has charge 1.602 × 10 ¹⁹ C. If an atom gains one or more electrons, it has more negative charge than positive and becomes a negative ion (anion). If it loses electrons, it becomes a positive ion (cation). Chemical bonds, electrochemistry, and the behaviour of salts and acids in solution all involve the transfer and sharing of electrons between atoms. When we say an object has been charged electrically, we mean that electrons have been added to or removed from it — protons, being locked in the nucleus, do not move in ordinary chemistry or contact electricity. 40.4 Conductors and Insulators

Materials differ enormously in their ability to allow charge to move through them. This difference determines almost everything about how they behave electrically. Conductors A conductor is a material in which electrons can move freely throughout the material. Metals are the most important class of conductors: in a metal, the outer electrons of each atom are not tightly bound and form a “sea” of mobile electrons that can flow throughout the material. When an external electric force is applied (for example, by a battery), these free electrons drift, creating an electric current. Common conductors include copper, aluminium, silver, gold, iron, and saltwater. Insulators An insulator (or dielectric) is a material in which electrons are tightly bound to individual atoms or molecules and cannot flow freely. Applying an electric force moves electrons only tiny distances from their equilibrium positions before restoring forces balance the applied force. Charge placed on an insulator stays where it is placed rather than spreading over the surface. Common insulators include glass, plastic (polyethylene, PVC), rubber, dry wood, and air. Physics: Foundations of Physical Science Chapter 40 — Electric Charge Semiconductors Semiconductors (silicon, germanium, gallium arsenide) have electrical conductivity between metals and insulators. Their conductivity can be altered by adding small amounts of impurities (doping) or by applying heat, light, or electric fields. This tunability makes semiconductors the basis of all modern electronics: transistors, integrated circuits, LEDs, solar cells, and computer chips. Superconductors Superconductors are materials that, below a critical temperature, allow electric current to flow with literally zero electrical resistance. The charge carriers in a superconductor form quantum-mechanical “Cooper pairs” that propagate without scattering. Superconductors have applications in MRI machines (superconducting magnets), particle accelerators, and are the subject of intense research for energy transmission. Class

40.5 Charging by Friction

When two different materials are rubbed together, electrons transfer from one material to the other. The material that tends to give up electrons becomes positively charged; the material that gains electrons becomes negatively charged. The charge acquired by each material is equal in magnitude and opposite in sign — total charge is conserved. The triboelectric series ranks materials by their tendency to give up electrons (positive Physics: Foundations of Physical Science Chapter 40 — Electric Charge end) or gain electrons (negative end) when rubbed: Positive end (loses electrons): human skin, glass, hair, wool, nylon, lead, silk, aluminium, paper — neutral materials in the middle — cotton, steel, wood, amber, rubber, polyethylene (negative end: gains electrons). When you rub a glass rod with silk, the glass loses electrons to the silk. The glass rod becomes positively charged and the silk negatively charged. When you rub rubber with fur, the rubber gains electrons and becomes negatively charged. The carpet-and-shoe combination that causes static shocks involves your shoe soles (rubber or synthetic) gaining electrons from the carpet fibres.

Historical Note:

Benjamin Franklin (1706–1790) demonstrated in 1752 that lightning is an electrical discharge using his famous (and extremely dangerous) kite experiment. He proposed the single-fluid model of electricity and introduced the terms “positive” and “negative.” Franklin also invented the lightning rod, saving countless buildings from fire by providing a low-resistance path to ground for lightning strikes.

40.6 Charging by Contact

A charged object can transfer charge to a neutral conductor by direct contact. When a negatively charged rod is touched to a neutral metal sphere, electrons flow from the rod onto the sphere (since like charges repel and electrons spread to minimise repulsion). The sphere acquires a net negative charge. When contact is broken, the sphere retains this charge. The amount of charge transferred by contact depends on the sizes and geometries of the objects and the initial charge. In general, charge distributes itself so that the potential is equalised between the two touching conductors — a concept we will formalise in Chapter 41. After contact, each object may have different total charge, but the potential at their surfaces is the same. An important special case is grounding: connecting an object to the Earth. Because the Earth is an enormous conductor, it acts as an infinite charge reservoir. Grounding a positively charged sphere allows electrons from the Earth to flow onto the sphere, neutralising it. Grounding a negatively charged sphere allows its excess electrons to flow into the Earth. After grounding, the object is neutral. This is why electrical grounding is essential for safety: it prevents dangerous charge build-up on equipment and provides a path for fault currents.

40.7 Charging by Induction

Physics: Foundations of Physical Science Chapter 40 — Electric Charge Induction charges an object without direct contact by rearranging the charges already in it. The mechanism relies on the fact that conductors have free electrons that can move in response to external electric forces. Process for charging a conductor by induction:

Step 1: Approach:

Bring a charged object (say, negatively charged) near but not touching a neutral conducting sphere.

Step 2: Redistribution:

The charged object’s electric field repels free electrons in the sphere. Electrons migrate to the far side of the sphere. The near side of the sphere becomes positively charged (electron deficit); the far side becomes negatively charged (electron surplus). The sphere as a whole is still neutral but has separated charge.

Step 3: Ground: While the charged object is still near, touch the sphere briefly with a

finger or ground wire. Electrons repelled to the far side escape to ground (or to your body). The near side retains its positive charge.

Step 4: Remove ground:

Break the ground connection first, before removing the charged rod.

Step 5: Remove rod:

Remove the original charged rod. The positive charge on the sphere redistributes uniformly. The sphere is now positively charged without ever having touched the charged rod. Notice: the charged rod that did the inducing retains its original charge throughout. Charging by induction does not discharge the inducing object. Also notice: the sign of the induced charge is opposite to the sign of the inducing charge (the sphere becomes positive when approached by a negative rod).

Key Point:

Induction differs from contact charging in two important ways: (1) no charge is transferred between the two objects; and (2) the induced charge is opposite in sign to the inducing charge, while contact charging produces charge of the same sign as the charging object.

40.8 Conservation of Charge

Conservation of charge is one of the most fundamental and precisely tested laws of physics: the total electric charge of an isolated system never changes. Charge can be transferred between objects, separated, or recombined, but it cannot be created from nothing or destroyed.

When an electron and a positron (antielectron) annihilate, they produce photons (which carry no

charge) — the total charge before ( 1e + 1e = 0) equals the total charge after (0). In

every nuclear reaction, decay, and particle collision ever observed, the total charge is conserved. Conservation of charge is a consequence of a deep symmetry in the laws of physics called gauge invariance. Unlike conservation of energy (which follows from time- translation symmetry) or conservation of momentum (from space-translation symmetry), charge conservation follows from the gauge symmetry of the electromagnetic field. This is a connection to advanced physics, but the practical result is simple and absolute: you cannot create or destroy charge.

The Rule: In any charging process — friction, contact, induction, ionisation, or nuclear

reaction — the net charge gained by all objects involved sums to zero. If object A gains charge +Q, object B (or the environment) gains charge Q.

40.9 Electric Force Between Charges

Charges exert forces on each other without contact. This “action at a distance” puzzled physicists for centuries. The modern understanding is that each charge creates an electric field in the space around it, and that field exerts force on any other charge placed in it (Chapter 41). For now, we treat the interaction directly. The key qualitative rules are: like charges repel, unlike charges attract. The force acts along the line connecting the two charges. The force on each charge is equal in magnitude and opposite in direction (Newton’s third law). The force decreases with distance — specifically, it follows an inverse-square law identical in mathematical form to Newton’s law of gravitation. The force between two point charges was quantified by Charles-Augustin de Coulomb in 1785 through torsion balance experiments, giving Coulomb’s Law.

40.11 Attraction, Repulsion, and Distance Effects

Physics: Foundations of Physical Science Chapter 40 — Electric Charge

The inverse-square dependence of Coulomb’s Law (force ∝1/r²) means the electric

force falls off rapidly with distance, but never reaches zero. Doubling the separation reduces the force to one-quarter; tripling it reduces the force to one-ninth. This is the same mathematical form as gravity, and it arises from the same geometric reason: the

force spreads over the surface area of a sphere, which grows as r².

Multiple charges exert forces independently. The net force on a charge due to several others is the vector sum of all the individual Coulomb forces. This principle of superposition is exact and allows complex charge distributions to be analysed by summing the contributions of individual charges. Even neutral objects can be attracted to charged objects. When a charged rod is brought near a neutral conductor, the nearer face develops induced charge of opposite sign (Section 40.7). The attractive force to the near face is stronger than the repulsive force from the far face (which is farther away and therefore the force is weaker). The net result is attraction. This is why a charged comb picks up small pieces of paper, even though the paper carries no net charge.

40.12 Electric Charge in Everyday Technology

Electric charge and its manipulation underlie almost all modern technology. Photocopiers and laser printers use electrostatic attraction: a photosensitive drum is charged uniformly, then selectively discharged by a laser beam (printer) or light image (copier). Charged toner powder sticks to the remaining charged areas of the drum and is then transferred to paper by further electrostatic attraction, then fused by heat. Electrostatic precipitators remove dust, soot, and particulate matter from industrial flue gases. Particles passing through a high-voltage region acquire charge, then are attracted to and deposited on oppositely charged collector plates. Modern power plants and cement factories use precipitators to remove over 99% of particulates. Capacitive touchscreens (smartphones, tablets) detect the position of a conducting finger by measuring the change in capacitance at the touch point. The finger, being a conductor, disturbs the electric field maintained between the screen’s transparent conducting layers. The device maps these disturbances to screen positions. Physics: Foundations of Physical Science Chapter 40 — Electric Charge Spray painting with electrostatic guns charges paint droplets before they leave the nozzle. The charged droplets are attracted to the grounded object being painted, wrapping around edges and covering surfaces that a direct spray would miss. Electrostatic painting achieves higher transfer efficiency (less wasted paint) and more uniform coverage than conventional spraying. Van de Graaff generators build up large static charges by mechanically carrying charge on a moving belt to a hollow conducting sphere, which stores the charge on its outer surface (all charge on a conductor resides at the surface). They produce voltages of hundreds of thousands of volts and are used in physics demonstrations and as particle accelerators for nuclear research.

40.13 Common Mistakes in Charge Problems

• Forgetting to convert microcoulombs to coulombs. Charges are often given in

microcoulombs (μc = 10 ⁶ C) or nanocoulombs (nC = 10 ⁹ C). Substituting these

directly into Coulomb’s Law without converting gives answers off by factors of 10⁶ or 10⁹. Always convert to SI (coulombs) before calculating. • Confusing the direction of force. Coulomb's Law gives only the magnitude. You must determine direction separately from the rule: like charges repel (force points away from the other charge); unlike charges attract (force points toward the other charge). In superposition problems, assign positive and negative signs to forces in your chosen coordinate direction before summing. • Applying Coulomb’s Law to non-point charges incorrectly. Coulomb’s Law applies to point charges and to uniformly charged spheres (where all charge can be treated as concentrated at the centre). For other extended charge distributions, integration or superposition of differential elements is required. • Thinking that inducting a charge transfers charge from the inducing object. Induction does not transfer charge from the inducing rod to the object. The rod retains its original charge throughout. Only contact charging transfers charge from one object to another. • Incorrectly applying conservation of charge in contact problems. When two identically sized conducting spheres touch, the total charge distributes equally. But if one sphere is larger, charge distributes in proportion to capacitance (roughly surface area). For identical spheres: each gets (q₁ + q₂)/2 after contact. • Forgetting that Coulomb forces are vectors, not scalars. In problems with three or more charges, compute each Coulomb force as a vector (magnitude and Physics: Foundations of Physical Science Chapter 40 — Electric Charge direction), then add the vectors. Simply adding magnitudes ignores direction and gives wrong answers whenever forces are not all in the same direction.

Common Mistake:

Always convert charge units to coulombs before using Coulomb's Law. A charge of 3.0 μc is 3.0 × 10 ⁶ C, not 3.0 C. Substituting 3.0 C instead of 3.0 ×

10 ⁶ C gives an answer 10¹² times too large — a physically impossible result.

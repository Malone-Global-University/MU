Chapter 15: Potential Energy and Conservation of Energy

15.1 What Potential Energy Means

Potential energy is stored energy: energy associated with the position or configuration of an object or system, available to be converted into kinetic energy when the system is released. 'Potential' here means 'waiting to do work' — the energy is real but not yet expressed as motion. Every form of potential energy is associated with a force. Gravitational potential energy is associated with the gravitational force. Elastic potential energy is associated with the spring (elastic restoring) force. Electrical potential energy is associated with electric forces. In every case, the potential energy is stored by virtue of the configuration of the system relative to some reference point — and can be released by allowing the relevant force to act. Potential energy is always defined relative to a reference level or configuration. The absolute value of potential energy is not physically meaningful; only changes in potential energy matter. We choose the reference to make the problem most convenient — and the physics never depends on that choice. Key Point: Potential energy belongs to the system, not to a single object. Gravitational PE is shared between the object and Earth. Elastic PE is stored in the spring. This distinction becomes important when analyzing which forces are conservative and which systems obey conservation of mechanical energy. 15.2 Gravitational Potential Energy

Near Earth's surface, where gravitational field strength g is approximately constant, the gravitational potential energy of an object of mass m at height h above the chosen reference level is:

U_g = mgh

The reference level (h = 0) is chosen by the problem-solver. Common choices: the floor,

the lowest point of the motion, the ground level. The choice does not affect any

calculated change in PE or any predicted speed, because ΔU_g = mgΔh depends only

on the change in height.

When an object rises, h increases, U_g increases, and work done against gravity is

stored as gravitational PE. When an object falls, h decreases, U_g decreases, and

gravitational PE converts to kinetic energy (or other forms).

Example — Gravitational PE

A 5.0 kg book is lifted from the floor (reference) to a

shelf 2.0 m high. U_g = 5.0 × 9.81 × 2.0 = 98.1 J. If it falls off the shelf, that 98.1 J

converts entirely to kinetic energy (ignoring air resistance). v at floor = √(2 × 98.1 /

5.0) = 6.26 m/s.

Common Mistake:

Gravitational PE depends on height above the reference level, not on the path taken to reach that height. An object lifted straight up 3 m has the same

U_g as one carried on a 45° ramp to the same height. Only the vertical height change

matters.

15.3 Elastic Potential Energy

Elastic potential energy is the energy stored in a deformed spring (or any elastic material) due to the work done against the spring's restoring force. It is stored in the elastic deformation itself — in the stretched or compressed molecular bonds of the spring material. Unlike gravitational PE, elastic PE is always non-negative: whether you stretch or compress a spring, you store positive energy in it. The reference configuration is the

spring at its natural (undeformed) length, where U_s = 0.

U_s = ½ k x²

Because U_s depends on x², the stored energy grows rapidly with deformation:

doubling the compression quadruples the stored energy. This quadratic relationship means springs store disproportionately more energy at large deformations. Physics: Foundations of Physical Science Chapter 15 — Potential Energy and Conservation of Energy

Example — Elastic PE

A spring with k = 400 N/m is compressed 0.15 m. U_s = ½ ×

400 × (0.15)² = 200 × 0.0225 = 4.5 J.

15.4 Energy Stored in Springs

The work done by an external agent to compress or stretch a spring from its natural length by displacement x is equal to the elastic potential energy stored:

W_{on spring} = U_s = ½ k x²

This result requires integration (since the spring force is variable), but the geometric argument gives the same answer: the work equals the area of the triangle under the F-

x graph from 0 to x, which is ½ × (kx) × x = ½ kx².

When the spring is released, it converts stored elastic PE entirely to kinetic energy of the object attached to it (on a frictionless surface):

U_s →KE:

½ k x² = ½ m v²

⇒

v = x√(k/m)

Worked Example 15.1 — Spring Launch

A spring (k = 300 N/m) is compressed 0.10 m against a 0.200 kg ball on a frictionless

surface. Find the ball's speed when it leaves the spring.

Step 1: Stored energy: U_s = ½ × 300 × (0.10)² = 1.5 J

Step 2: Energy conservation: U_s = KE ⇒1.5 = ½ × 0.200 × v² ⇒v² = 15.0

Step 3: Speed: v = √15.0 = 3.87 m/s

Step 4: Check: Units: J = kg·m²/s² ⇒v in m/s ✔

15.5 Mechanical Energy

The mechanical energy of a system is the sum of its kinetic energy and all its potential energies:

E_mech = KE + U_g + U_s

(+ any other PE forms) Mechanical energy is the total energy associated with the motion and configuration of objects in a mechanical system. It does not include thermal energy, chemical energy, or other non-mechanical forms. When only conservative forces do work on a system, mechanical energy is conserved: it remains constant as the system evolves. Energy moves between KE and PE forms but the total stays the same. When nonconservative forces (friction, drag) act, mechanical Physics: Foundations of Physical Science Chapter 15 — Potential Energy and Conservation of Energy energy decreases — it is converted to thermal or other forms.

15.6 Conservative vs. Nonconservative Forces

A conservative force is one for which the work done depends only on the starting and ending positions — not on the path taken between them. Equivalently, a conservative force does zero work on any closed path (a path that returns to its starting point). A nonconservative force is one for which the work done depends on the path. Friction is the most common example: sliding along a longer, winding path involves more friction work than a straight path between the same two points. Force Conservative? 15.7 Conservation of Mechanical Energy

Physics: Foundations of Physical Science Chapter 15 — Potential Energy and Conservation of Energy When only conservative forces do work on a system, the total mechanical energy is conserved:

E_mech = KE + U = constant

KE_i + U_i = KE_f + U_f

The power of this principle is that it replaces multiple force and kinematic equations with a single energy equation. We do not need to know how an object moved along its path; we only need to know its kinetic and potential energies at the start and end.

15.8 General Conservation of Energy

The law of conservation of energy, in its most general form, states that the total energy of an isolated system is constant. Energy can transform between forms — kinetic, potential, thermal, chemical, electromagnetic, nuclear — but the total never changes.

E_total = KE + U_g + U_s + E_thermal + E_chemical + … =

The mechanical energy 'lost' appears as thermal energy (heat from friction), sound, or deformation. No energy disappears; it just changes form.

E_{mech,i} = E_{mech,f} + E_{thermal}

The thermal energy produced by friction over displacement d with kinetic friction force

ΔE_thermal = f_k × d

15.9 Energy Losses and Dissipation

In real systems, some mechanical energy is always converted to thermal energy through friction, air resistance, inelastic deformation, or viscosity. This process is called energy dissipation. The dissipated energy is not destroyed; it raises the temperature of the materials involved and eventually radiates into the environment. Energy dissipation is irreversible: thermal energy does not spontaneously convert back to organized mechanical energy. This directionality — from ordered motion to disordered thermal energy — is one aspect of the second law of thermodynamics, which Chapter 33 explores. 15.10 Solving Problems with Energy Methods

The energy method replaces the step-by-step force and kinematic analysis with a global accounting of energy at two states. The procedure is:

Step 1: Choose the system (object + Earth for gravity; object + spring for elastic PE).

Step 2: Identify initial and final states (when the problem begins and ends).

Step 3: List all energy forms at each state: KE, U_g, U_s.

Step 4:

Check for nonconservative forces (friction, applied forces not from conservative fields). If none, use conservation of mechanical energy. If present, include

ΔE_thermal = f_k d.

Step 5: Write the energy equation and solve for the unknown.

Step 6: Check units, sign, and whether the answer is physically reasonable.

The Rule:

Energy methods eliminate the need to find acceleration, normal force, or tension — unless those are specifically asked for. When the problem asks for a speed or height and gives enough information about energies, energy methods are almost always faster than force-kinematic analysis.

15.12 Common Mistakes in Energy Conservation Problems

Mistake 1: Applying conservation of mechanical energy when friction acts

Conservation of E_mech requires that only conservative forces do work. If friction, air

resistance, or applied forces are present, E_mech is not conserved. Use E_mech,i =

E_mech,f + ΔE_thermal, including the dissipated energy term.

Mistake 2: Choosing an inconvenient reference level

While any reference level gives the correct ΔU and the correct final speed, a poorly

chosen reference can make the algebra messy. Choose the reference at the lowest point of the motion so that no potential energy values are negative.

Mistake 3: Forgetting to square v in ½mv²

KE = ½mv²: the velocity is squared. Students sometimes write ½mv instead of ½mv²,

especially when rearranging. Solve for v² first, then take the square root.

Mistake 4: Treating the spring equation as F = kx instead of U = ½kx²

The spring force is F = kx. The energy stored is U_s = ½kx² (the area under the force-

displacement graph). Using F = kx in an energy equation gives the wrong answer.

Mistake 5: Forgetting that reference height cancels in Δh

If you choose h = 0 at the starting point, U_g,i = 0 and U_g,f = mgh_f. If you choose h

= 0 at the end, U_g,i = mgh_i and U_g,f = 0. Either gives the same equation; only Δh

Common Mistake:

Energy conservation is about the total system. Include potential energy for all conservative forces in the system — both gravitational PE and spring PE if a spring is involved. Leaving out one term gives a wrong answer, not a simpler one.

Chapter Summary

Potential energy is stored energy associated with the configuration of a system. Conservation of mechanical energy — valid when only conservative forces act — is one of the most powerful tools in physics. •

Gravitational PE: U_g = mgh; reference at chosen height; only Δh matters.

•

Elastic PE: U_s = ½kx²; always ≥0; reference at natural spring length.

•

Mechanical energy: E_mech = KE + U_g + U_s; conserved when only

conservative forces do work. • Conservative force: work is path-independent; has an associated potential energy.

Examples: gravity, spring force. Nonconservative: friction, drag.

•

When friction acts: E_mech,i = E_mech,f + f_k d (dissipated thermal energy).

• General conservation: total energy (including thermal, chemical, etc.) is always conserved. • Energy method procedure: identify states, list energies, apply energy equation, solve for unknown. • Energy methods are preferred when speed or height is unknown and path details are not needed.

Chapter 37: Fluid Flow

37.1 What It Means for a Fluid to Flow

In the previous chapters, we treated fluids at rest (hydrostatics). We now turn to fluids in motion (fluid dynamics). A fluid flows when there is a net force driving it — typically a pressure difference. Fluid flows from regions of higher pressure toward regions of lower pressure, just as objects accelerate from regions of higher to lower potential energy. Flow can be described at two levels: macroscopic and microscopic. At the macroscopic level, we treat the fluid as a continuous medium and describe properties like velocity, pressure, and density as functions of position and time. This is the continuum assumption, valid whenever the fluid parcel under consideration contains enormous numbers of molecules (essentially always true for liquids, and true for gases at ordinary pressures). At the microscopic level, individual molecules are tracked — this level of detail is only needed for rarefied gases or very small-scale flows (microfluidics). In this chapter we adopt the macroscopic view and further simplify by considering ideal fluid flow: the fluid is incompressible (density is constant, valid for liquids and slow gas flows), inviscid (frictionless — no internal friction or viscosity), and the flow is Physics: Foundations of Physical Science Chapter 37 — Fluid Flow steady (conditions at every point do not change with time). These idealisations allow clean mathematical results; the deviations from ideal behaviour (viscosity, compressibility, turbulence) are discussed at the end of the chapter.

Key Point:

Ideal fluid flow is a useful approximation for: water in pipes at moderate speeds, air around slowly moving objects, and preliminary design calculations. Corrections for viscosity (internal friction) and turbulence become important at high flow speeds or in narrow channels.

37.2 Flow Rate and Volume Flow Rate

Volume flow rate (also called volumetric flow rate) Q is the volume of fluid passing a cross-section per unit time. It is the most natural description of “how fast” a fluid is moving through a pipe, channel, or duct:

Q = V / t

Flow rate can also be expressed in terms of the flow speed v and the cross-sectional area A perpendicular to the flow direction. In time Δt, the fluid moves a distance vΔt. The volume that passes the cross-section in this time is:

Q = A v

Mass flow rate is important in thermodynamics (energy carried by flowing fluids), engineering (combustion and reaction rates, which depend on mass not volume), and anywhere the fluid density changes significantly. For the incompressible liquids considered in this chapter, we can work with volume flow rate Q and convert to mass flow rate when needed. For a compressible gas (not treated in this chapter), density changes with pressure and temperature, so volume flow rate alone is ambiguous — you must specify pressure and temperature. Mass flow rate is unambiguous because it directly counts molecules. This is why gas flow is often specified in kg/s or standard cubic metres per second (where “standard” refers to a defined reference pressure and temperature).

37.4 Streamlines and Flow Paths

A streamline is a curve that is everywhere tangent to the velocity vector of the fluid at a given instant. In steady flow, streamlines are also the paths that individual fluid elements follow: a particle released into the flow at a given point will travel along the streamline through that point. Streamlines have several important properties. They never cross each other in steady flow: if two streamlines crossed, a fluid element at the crossing point would have two different velocities simultaneously, which is impossible. Streamlines are denser (closer together) where the flow speed is higher and spaced farther apart where the flow speed is lower. This visual property of streamline patterns is extremely useful for qualitative analysis. A stream tube is a bundle of streamlines forming a tube-like region. No fluid crosses the walls of a stream tube (by definition, since streamlines are parallel to velocity). Fluid entering one end of a stream tube must exit the other end. This concept is the geometric basis for the continuity equation.

Key Point:

In unsteady (time-varying) flow, streamlines change from moment to moment and no longer coincide with particle paths. In that case, particle paths are called pathlines, and are distinct from streamlines. 37.5 Steady vs. Unsteady Flow

Flow is steady if the velocity, pressure, and density at every fixed point in the flow do not change with time. A tap running at constant rate produces approximately steady flow in the pipe. Flow is unsteady if these properties change with time at fixed points — as when a tap is turned on or off, or when waves propagate through a fluid. Most practical pipe and duct flows reach a quasi-steady state a short time after startup. The transient period (when flow is establishing itself) involves unsteady effects. Once steady state is reached, the simpler steady-flow equations apply. Natural flows are more often unsteady: the heartbeat creates pulsatile (periodic unsteady) blood flow; wind is inherently unsteady; ocean waves are unsteady surface waves. The steady-flow assumption is an idealisation that gives useful results when the time scale of flow changes is much longer than the transit time of fluid through the system.

37.6 Laminar vs. Turbulent Flow

Laminar flow (also called streamline flow or viscous flow) occurs when fluid layers slide smoothly past each other in parallel paths, without lateral mixing. The streamlines are smooth, orderly, and nearly parallel. Laminar flow occurs at low speeds in narrow channels or with viscous fluids. Turbulent flow occurs at high speeds or in wide channels. The flow breaks into chaotic eddies and swirls; fluid elements mix laterally and the velocity at any point fluctuates irregularly with time. Turbulent flow carries much greater energy in its random motions, produces greater friction losses, and is much harder to model mathematically. The transition between laminar and turbulent flow is predicted by the Reynolds number Re, a dimensionless ratio of inertial to viscous forces:

37.7 Continuity in Fluid Flow

Continuity is the principle that matter is conserved in fluid flow. No fluid is created or destroyed inside a flow system (no sources or sinks). This means that whatever mass enters a pipe or channel per unit time must also leave it per unit time. Consider fluid flowing through a pipe that changes cross-sectional area from A₁ at

position 1 to A₂ at position 2. In a small time interval Δt, the fluid at position 1 moves

a distance v₁Δt, sweeping out a volume A₁v₁Δt. The mass entering the wide section in

this time is ρ A₁ v₁ Δt. The mass leaving the narrow section is ρ A₂ v₂ Δt. For an

incompressible fluid with no accumulation:

ρ A₁ v₁ = ρ A₂ v₂

The continuity equation is the fluid equivalent of conservation of mass (or, for incompressible fluids, conservation of volume). It constrains the kinematics of the flow — the speed at any point is determined by the geometry (area) of the flow path at that point, given the flow rate Q. When a pipe branches into two or more branches, continuity applies to the total flow: the flow rate entering equals the sum of flow rates leaving. If a pipe of area A₀ splits into two branches of areas A₁ and A₂:

Q₀ = Q₁ + Q₂

⇒

A₀ v₀ = A₁ v₁ + A₂ v₂

37.9 Flow Through Pipes and Constrictions

The continuity equation directly explains several observable phenomena in everyday pipe flow. When you partially block a garden hose nozzle with your thumb, you reduce the cross-sectional area A. Since Q is fixed by the pump or water mains pressure, v = Q/A must increase — the water sprays farther. The same principle governs spray nozzles, showerheads, and firefighting hoses. In a river, water flows slowly in wide, shallow sections (floodplains) and rapidly in narrow gorges. The volume flow rate (discharge) of the river is the same throughout; the product Av is constant. The narrower the channel (smaller A), the faster the water (larger v). In the human cardiovascular system, the aorta (large artery, diameter ∼2.5 cm) carries blood away from the heart. Blood then branches into progressively smaller arteries, arterioles, and capillaries. Although each individual capillary is tiny (diameter ∼8 μm), there are so many capillaries in parallel that their total cross-sectional area is vastly Physics: Foundations of Physical Science Chapter 37 — Fluid Flow greater than the aorta. Continuity therefore requires blood to flow much more slowly in the capillaries than in the aorta — which is physiologically important because the slow capillary flow allows time for oxygen and nutrient exchange with surrounding tissues.

37.10 Real-World Flow Systems

The ideal pipe flow model (steady, incompressible, inviscid) gives useful intuition, but real engineering flow systems involve additional considerations. Four of the most important are: pipe friction losses, entrance and exit effects, flow measurement, and system branching. Pipe friction: Real fluids are viscous. Viscosity causes friction between the fluid and pipe walls, which dissipates energy and causes a pressure drop along the pipe. The Darcy- Weisbach equation (beyond this chapter) quantifies this pressure drop as a function of flow speed, pipe diameter, length, and friction factor. In general, higher speed flow experiences greater friction losses. Flow measurement: Several practical devices exploit the continuity equation and pressure changes to measure flow rate. The Venturi meter constricts the pipe to speed up the flow, then measures the pressure difference between the wide and narrow sections. The orifice plate does the same with a simpler (but less efficient) constriction. The Pitot tube measures local flow speed from the pressure difference between a forward-facing orifice (stagnation pressure) and a side-facing orifice (static pressure). All of these are discussed further in Chapter 38 under Bernoulli applications. System branching: Municipal water distribution networks, natural gas grids, and biological vascular systems all involve complex branching pipe networks. Continuity applies at every junction, and pressure must be consistent at each node. Solving such networks requires simultaneous application of continuity (flow conservation at junctions) and pressure equations (energy conservation along paths).

37.11 Limits of Ideal Flow Models

The ideal flow model (incompressible, inviscid, steady) is a powerful tool but has well- Physics: Foundations of Physical Science Chapter 37 — Fluid Flow defined limits. Understanding these limits helps identify when more sophisticated models are needed. Compressibility becomes important when fluid speeds approach the speed of sound. For air, this occurs at Mach 0.3 or above (about 100 m/s at sea level). Aircraft flying at Mach 0.8–1.2 (transonic) or above (supersonic) cannot use incompressible flow equations. Shockwaves form when local speeds exceed the sound speed, creating sudden pressure jumps that the ideal model cannot predict. Viscosity is always present in real fluids. Its effects are negligible at high Reynolds numbers (turbulent flows in wide pipes) for most macroscopic purposes, but dominate at low Reynolds numbers (microfluidics, creeping flow). The no-slip condition — that the fluid velocity at a solid wall must equal the wall velocity (zero for stationary walls) — is a viscous effect that the ideal model ignores. In reality, a thin boundary layer near every wall has a velocity gradient from zero (at the wall) to the free-stream value. Boundary layer theory is a major subject in fluid mechanics. Turbulence: Even when the bulk flow is well modelled by the steady equations, local instabilities can trigger turbulent fluctuations. Turbulence dramatically increases effective friction and mixing but is extremely difficult to model from first principles. Most engineering design uses empirical correlations for turbulent friction, established by experiments.

•

Using diameter instead of radius (or area) in Q = Av. Area = πr² = π(d/2)². Using

d directly instead of d/2 gives an area four times too large. • Forgetting to square the radius when computing circular cross-sectional area. If r doubles, A quadruples (A ∝r²). Students often apply a linear ratio where a squared ratio is needed. •

Applying continuity to the wrong cross-section. Continuity A₁v₁ = A₂v₂ applies

between any two points in the same unbranched streamtube. If there is a branch between the two points, total flow conservation must be used instead. • Confusing flow rate Q with flow speed v. Q = Av has units m³/s; v has units m/s. Or the same flow rate but very different speeds. • Assuming that a larger pipe always means faster flow. By continuity, a larger area pipe has slower speed at the same flow rate. The water main in your street has a much larger diameter than your household pipes and flows much more slowly, even though it supplies a higher total flow rate. • Applying ideal flow (continuity only) to flows where viscosity matters significantly. Chapter Summary

Fluid flow is described by velocity fields, flow rate, and streamlines. In ideal (steady, incompressible, inviscid) flow, the continuity equation expresses conservation of mass. • Volume flow rate: Q = Av (m³/s). Mass flow rate: ṁ= ρQ = ρAv (kg/s). • Streamlines are everywhere tangent to velocity; they never cross in steady flow. • Laminar flow is smooth and ordered; turbulent flow is chaotic. The Reynolds number Re = ρvD/μ predicts the transition. • Continuity equation: A₁v₁ = A₂v₂ (Q constant along an unbranched streamtube). At a branch: Q_in = ΣQ_out. • Smaller cross-section →faster flow; larger cross-section →slower flow (at constant Q). • Applications: pipe networks, cardiovascular flow, river channels, nozzles, flow meters. • Ideal flow limits: compressibility important at Mach > 0.3; viscosity important at low Re; turbulence requires empirical models.

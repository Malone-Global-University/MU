Chapter 54: Physics in Medicine

54.1 Why Medicine Depends on Physics

Modern medicine is inseparable from physics. Every major diagnostic tool — stethoscope, X-ray, CT scanner, MRI, ultrasound, ECG, pulse oximeter — operates on physical principles. Every treatment technology — radiation therapy, laser surgery, lithotripsy, pacemaker, defibrillator — is designed using physics. The human body itself is a physical system: it obeys Newton's laws, conservation of energy, fluid mechanics, electrostatics, thermodynamics, and wave physics. Medical physics is the branch of applied physics concerned with the application of physical concepts, methods, and techniques to human health. It spans four broad areas: medical imaging (creating pictures of internal structures), radiation therapy (using radiation to destroy tumours), biomechanics (applying mechanics to bones, joints, muscles, and circulation), and bioelectronics (understanding and manipulating the body's electrical signals). Historically, physics drove medical revolutions. Wilhelm Röntgen's discovery of X-rays in 1895 gave physicians their first non-invasive window into the human body. The development of nuclear magnetic resonance in the 1940s led directly to MRI scanners Physics: Foundations of Physical Science Chapter 54 — Physics in Medicine in the 1970s and 1980s. The physics of ultrasound, developed for submarine detection during World War II, became obstetric and cardiac ultrasound within two decades. Physics discoveries almost always find medical applications within a generation. Historical Note: Röntgen discovered X-rays on November 1895 while experimenting with cathode ray tubes. Within weeks he had produced the first X-ray image — of his wife's hand, showing her wedding ring and finger bones. Within a year, X-ray machines were being used in hospitals across Europe and North America. The time from physics discovery to medical deployment was less than twelve months.

54.2 Mechanics of the Human Body

The skeleton, muscles, and joints form a mechanical system. Bones act as levers, muscles provide forces, and joints act as fulcrums. Newton's laws apply: the body accelerates when net forces act on it and is in static equilibrium when the sum of forces and torques is zero. Levers in the body are classified by the position of the fulcrum relative to the force and load. The elbow is a third-class lever: the bicep muscle (force) acts between the elbow joint (fulcrum) and the hand holding a load. Third-class levers sacrifice mechanical advantage for speed and range of motion — a small muscle contraction produces a large hand displacement.

This is why hip implant design requires understanding of biomechanical forces, not just material strength.

54.3 Biomechanics of Movement and Force

Biomechanics applies the principles of mechanics to biological systems, particularly the study of human movement, posture, gait, and sport. It combines Newtonian mechanics, material science, and anatomy to understand how forces are generated, transmitted, and absorbed by the body. During walking, the centre of mass follows an inverted-pendulum path: the body vaults over the stance leg, converting kinetic energy to potential energy and back again. Running is a spring-mass system: the tendons (particularly the Achilles tendon and plantar fascia) store elastic potential energy during foot contact and release it during push-off, improving efficiency. The Achilles tendon stores and releases roughly 35% of the mechanical energy of each running stride. Ground reaction forces (GRFs) are measured using force plates. During walking, the vertical GRF is approximately 1.2 times body weight at heel strike, drops to about 0.8 times body weight at midstance, and rises again to 1.2 times body weight at toe-off. During running, peak GRFs are 2–3 times body weight. These data inform rehabilitation, prosthetic design, and sports injury prevention. 54.4 Pressure and Fluid Flow in Circulation

The cardiovascular system is a closed-loop hydraulic circuit. The heart is a dual pump: the right side pumps deoxygenated blood to the lungs (pulmonary circulation, low pressure); the left side pumps oxygenated blood to the body (systemic circulation, high pressure). Blood pressure is the gauge pressure exerted by blood on vessel walls. Normal systemic blood pressure is reported as systolic / diastolic: typically 120 / 80 mmHg (16 / 10.7 kPa). Systolic pressure (120 mmHg) is the peak pressure during ventricular contraction. Diastolic pressure (80 mmHg) is the minimum pressure during ventricular relaxation. Blood pressure is measured with a sphygmomanometer (pressure cuff) and stethoscope, which detects the Korotkoff sounds caused by turbulent blood flow as the cuff pressure is released.

54.5 Heat Transfer in Human Physiology

The human body is a thermodynamic system that generates heat through metabolism and must transfer it to the environment to maintain a stable core temperature of 37°C. The four mechanisms of heat transfer — conduction, convection, radiation, and evaporation — all operate simultaneously. Conduction transfers heat through contact. The body's subcutaneous fat layer (poor conductor, κ 0.2 W/m°C) provides thermal insulation. Clothing traps a layer of air (even poorer conductor, κ 0.025 W/m°C), dramatically reducing conductive heat loss. In cold water, the body loses heat 25 times faster than in air at the same temperature because water is a much better conductor (κ 0.6 W/m°C) and also forces convection over the skin surface. Convection — heat loss through moving air or liquid — accounts for about 25–30% of heat loss at rest and increases with wind speed (wind chill) and exercise. Radiation accounts for about 40–60% of heat loss at rest — the body radiates as a near-perfect

blackbody at infrared wavelengths. The power radiated is P = εσAT⁴, where ε

0.97 for human skin regardless of pigmentation. Evaporation of sweat is the dominant cooling mechanism during vigorous exercise, contributing up to 80% of heat loss. 54.6 Electricity in the Nervous System and Heart

Neurons transmit information as electrical signals called action potentials. The resting membrane potential of a neuron is approximately 70 mV (inside negative relative to outside), maintained by the sodium-potassium pump and selective ion permeability. When stimulated, sodium ions rush in, depolarising the membrane; the action potential propagates along the axon as a self-regenerating wave at speeds of 1–100 m/s depending on axon diameter and myelination. The heart has its own electrical pacemaker: the sinoatrial (SA) node generates spontaneous action potentials at about 60–80 times per minute, triggering a coordinated wave of contraction through the atria and ventricles. The ECG (electrocardiogram) records this electrical activity from electrodes on the skin. The characteristic P-QRS-T waveform maps to: P wave — atrial depolarisation; QRS complex — ventricular depolarisation (contraction); T wave — ventricular repolarisation. Defibrillators deliver a brief high-energy electrical shock (200–360 J) to reset the heart's electrical activity during ventricular fibrillation (chaotic, uncoordinated contractions). The shock simultaneously depolarises all heart muscle cells, briefly stopping all electrical activity; the SA node (which has the fastest intrinsic rate) then resumes control and restores normal rhythm. Automated external defibrillators (AEDs) analyse rhythm and instruct even untrained users through the process.

54.7 Medical Imaging with X-Rays

X-rays are high-energy electromagnetic radiation (wavelength 0.01–10 nm, frequency 3

× 10¹³ – 3 × 10¹⁶ Hz). They are produced by bombarding a tungsten target with high-

speed electrons accelerated through 30–150 kV. X-rays penetrate soft tissue but are absorbed by denser materials (bone, metal). The transmitted X-rays darken Physics: Foundations of Physical Science Chapter 54 — Physics in Medicine photographic film or activate a digital detector, producing a shadow image.

X-ray absorption follows Beer's law: I = I₀ e^( μx), where I₀ is incident intensity, x is the

thickness of material, and μ is the linear attenuation coefficient. Different tissues have

different μ values: bone (high μ, absorbs strongly, appears white on film), lung tissue

(low μ, appears dark), and soft tissue (intermediate μ, grey). Contrast agents — iodine

compounds injected into blood vessels, or barium swallowed to outline the

gastrointestinal tract — increase contrast by raising μ in specific regions.

₀ Computed Tomography (CT) uses a rotating X-ray source and detector array to acquire hundreds of shadow projections around the patient. Computer algorithms (filtered back -projection or iterative reconstruction) combine these projections to compute a three- dimensional map of X-ray attenuation coefficients throughout the body, which can be visualised as cross-sectional slices or three-dimensional volume renders. CT greatly improves the detection of soft-tissue structures compared to plain X-ray. The biological risk of X-rays arises from ionisation: X-ray photons have enough energy to eject electrons from atoms, breaking chemical bonds and potentially damaging DNA.

The radiation dose is measured in gray (Gy, 1 Gy = 1 J/kg absorbed) and effective dose

in sievert (Sv), which weights absorbed dose by tissue sensitivity. A chest X-ray delivers about 0.1 mSv effective dose; a CT scan of the abdomen delivers about 10 mSv — comparable to the annual natural background dose.

Common Mistake:

X-ray and CT scans deliver ionising radiation. The risk is small for individual scans but accumulates with repeated exposure. Clinicians use the ALARA principle (As Low As Reasonably Achievable) to minimise dose while obtaining diagnostically adequate images.

54.8 MRI and Magnetic Field Applications

Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) exploits nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR): the tendency of atomic nuclei with non-zero spin to align with an external magnetic field and resonate at a characteristic frequency when disturbed. In clinical MRI, hydrogen nuclei (protons) in water and fat are the signal source. A patient is placed inside a superconducting magnet generating a uniform field of 1.5–3.0 T (about 30 000–60 000 times Earth's field). Protons align with this field. No ionising radiation is involved: MRI uses RF radiation, which is non-ionising and considered biologically safe at clinical field strengths. MRI is contraindicated in patients with ferromagnetic implants (certain pacemakers, cochlear implants, surgical clips, metal fragments) because the strong magnetic field exerts forces and torques on ferromagnetic objects, and the RF pulse can induce dangerous currents in conducting implants. The MRI suite must be a controlled zone with strict metallic object exclusion.

Doppler ultrasound uses the Doppler shift of reflected sound to measure blood flow velocity and direction — blood moving toward the transducer shifts the frequency up; blood moving away shifts it down. Ultrasound is safe (no ionising radiation), portable, inexpensive relative to MRI/CT, and provides real-time imaging. It is the first-choice modality for obstetric imaging, cardiac imaging (echocardiography), guiding needle biopsies and injections, and imaging soft- tissue structures such as the liver, kidneys, thyroid, and tendons. Its main limitations are poor penetration through gas and bone, and operator dependence.

54.10 Radiation Therapy and Nuclear Medicine

Radiation therapy uses ionising radiation to destroy cancer cells. High-energy X-rays

(4–25 MV, produced by a linear accelerator), gamma rays (from ¹³·Cs or ¶⁰Co sources),

protons, or heavy ions are directed at tumours. The radiation damages DNA, preventing cancer cells from dividing. Because cancer cells divide rapidly and have impaired DNA repair mechanisms, they are more radiosensitive than most normal tissues. Modern radiation therapy uses intensity-modulated radiation therapy (IMRT) and image -guided radiation therapy (IGRT) to deliver shaped beams from multiple angles that converge on the tumour while sparing surrounding tissue. Stereotactic radiosurgery (Gamma Knife, CyberKnife) delivers hundreds of low-intensity beams that intersect only at the tumour, achieving surgical precision without a physical incision. Nuclear medicine uses radioactive isotopes administered to the patient (as tracers or therapeutic agents). Diagnostic nuclear medicine: a gamma-emitting isotope (typically ⁹ m Tc, technetium-99m, with a 6-hour half-life) is attached to a biologically active molecule and injected. The molecule preferentially accumulates in specific organs or tumours. A gamma camera detects the emitted photons and produces a functional image showing where the tracer has concentrated. Positron Emission Tomography (PET) uses positron-emitting isotopes (¹⁸F is most common, with a 110-minute half-life). Emitted positrons immediately annihilate with electrons, producing two back-to-back 511 keV gamma photons. 54.11 Prosthetics and Biomedical Engineering

Biomedical engineering applies engineering principles to medicine and biology. Prosthetics — artificial replacement limbs — require careful biomechanical analysis. A lower-limb prosthetic must replicate the mechanical function of the missing joint and limb segment, including load bearing, energy storage and return during gait, and proprioceptive feedback. Modern running prosthetics (the Cheetah® blade used by para-athletes) are made from carbon-fibre composites designed to store elastic strain energy during stance and release it during push-off, mimicking the Achilles tendon. The spring stiffness is tuned to the athlete's mass and running speed. Osseointegration — direct anchoring of the prosthetic to a titanium implant that fuses with the bone — improves load transfer compared to socket-based attachment. Physics: Foundations of Physical Science Chapter 54 — Physics in Medicine Cochlear implants bypass damaged hair cells in the inner ear. An external microphone captures sound; a signal processor decomposes it into frequency channels; an implanted electrode array delivers current pulses to different locations along the spiral cochlea (which encodes frequency tonotopically — high frequencies at the base, low frequencies at the apex). The timing and pattern of pulses encode speech and music information directly to the auditory nerve. Biomedical sensors are ubiquitous in modern healthcare. Pulse oximeters use the different optical absorption spectra of oxygenated and deoxygenated haemoglobin at two wavelengths (660 nm red and 940 nm infrared) to measure blood oxygen saturation. Continuous glucose monitors use electrochemical sensors implanted subcutaneously. Implantable cardiac monitors record ECG data continuously for months to detect arrhythmias.

54.12 Safety and Ethics in Medical Technology

Physical technologies in medicine carry risks that must be rigorously managed. Ionising radiation causes DNA damage; cumulative doses are tracked and limited for patients, radiation workers, and the general public. International Commission on Radiological Protection (ICRP) guidelines set dose limits: 20 mSv/year for occupationally exposed workers and 1 mSv/year for members of the public from artificial sources. MRI strong magnetic fields can accelerate ferromagnetic objects into the magnet bore (projectile risk), induce currents in conducting implants, and heat metal near the field. Ultrasound can cause bioeffects (cavitation and local heating) at high intensities; diagnostic ultrasound operates well below these thresholds. Lasers can damage the eyes and skin. Electrical devices must be electrically isolated to prevent microshock

(leakage current as low as 10 μA can induce ventricular fibrillation if a catheter carries

it directly to the heart). Ethical considerations include equitable access to imaging technology (MRI scanners are concentrated in wealthy countries and urban centres), the use of AI-based image interpretation (accuracy, bias, accountability), genetic data from nuclear medicine and imaging biobanks, and the environmental disposal of radioactive waste from nuclear medicine. Physics students who become healthcare workers, engineers, or policymakers will face these questions directly.

54.13 Common Mistakes in Medical Physics Interpretation

• Confusing ionising and non-ionising radiation. X-rays and gamma rays are ionising (enough energy per photon to eject electrons). UV light, visible light, infrared, microwaves, and RF (including MRI pulses) are non-ionising. Many students incorrectly assume all medical radiation is ionising and dangerous. MRI and ultrasound do not use ionising radiation. • Assuming higher magnetic field is always better in MRI. Higher field (3 T vs 1.5 T) improves signal-to-noise ratio but also increases artefacts, cost, contraindications, and specific absorption rate (RF heating). 1.5 T remains the most widely used clinical field strength for most applications. •

Forgetting the round-trip factor in ultrasound depth calculations. Depth = v × t

/ 2. The echo must travel to the reflector and back, so dividing by 2 is required. Omitting it gives double the actual depth. • Thinking Doppler ultrasound measures absolute velocity. It measures the component of velocity along the beam direction. If the beam is perpendicular to flow, the Doppler shift is zero regardless of speed. Clinicians correct for the angle between the beam and the flow direction. • Confusing gray (Gy) and sievert (Sv). Gray measures absorbed dose (energy per mass); sievert measures effective dose (weighted by radiation type and tissue

sensitivity). For X-rays and gamma rays, 1 Gy = 1 Sv. For alpha particles (more

biologically damaging), 1 Gy = 20 Sv. Do not interchange units.

• Applying Poiseuille's law uncritically to blood flow. Blood is non-Newtonian (its viscosity changes with shear rate), the vessels are elastic (not rigid), and flow is pulsatile (not steady). Poiseuille's law is a useful approximation but must not be applied as an exact description of real circulatory physics.

Common Mistake:

Radiation dose units are frequently confused in medical contexts. Gray (Gy) is absorbed dose; sievert (Sv) is effective dose. For gamma rays and X-rays

the numerical values are equal, but for alpha radiation 1 Gy = 20 Sv. Using the wrong

unit can misrepresent risk by a factor of 20.

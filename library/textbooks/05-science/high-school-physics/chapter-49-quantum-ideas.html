49.1 What "Quantum" Means

The word quantum comes from the Latin for "how much" or "a discrete amount." In physics, a quantum is the smallest possible discrete unit of a physical quantity. Quantum physics is the physics of systems where physical quantities are quantised — restricted to specific discrete values rather than varying continuously. In everyday life, most things appear continuous. Temperature, velocity, position, electrical current — all seem to vary smoothly and can take any value within a range. This is a correct description at large scales. But at atomic and subatomic scales, many quantities are found to be discrete. Energy, angular momentum, electric charge — all come in specific minimum units. You cannot have half a unit of charge. You cannot have 2.5 units of angular momentum. You cannot have an electron in a hydrogen atom with energy of 6.0 eV. Quantum physics was developed between roughly 1900 and 1930 through contributions from Planck, Einstein, Bohr, de Broglie, Heisenberg, Schrödinger, Dirac, and many others. Its predictions have been confirmed to extraordinary precision — the anomalous magnetic moment of the electron is predicted and measured to within one part in 10¹. No prediction of quantum mechanics has ever been proven wrong by experiment. It is, by the measure of experimental verification, the most successful physical theory ever devised.

Key Point: Quantum physics does not replace classical physics — it contains classical

physics as a limiting case. For objects much larger than atoms, quantum effects average out and classical physics is an excellent approximation. The transition from quantum to classical behaviour is called the correspondence principle: in the limit of large quantum numbers, quantum predictions approach classical predictions. 49.2 Quantised Energy

The first clear indication that energy is quantised came from Max Planck's work on blackbody radiation in 1900. A blackbody is an idealised object that absorbs all electromagnetic radiation falling on it and emits radiation based only on its temperature. The spectrum of blackbody radiation (the distribution of emitted power across wavelengths) was well-measured but could not be explained by classical physics, which predicted that a hot object should emit infinite energy at short wavelengths — an absurd result called the "ultraviolet catastrophe." Planck found that he could explain the observed spectrum only by assuming that the

energy of each oscillator in the blackbody is quantised: E = nhf, where n is a non-

negative integer, f is the oscillator's frequency, and h is a new fundamental constant now called Planck's constant.

E = nhf

(n = 0, 1, 2, 3, …)

Planck himself was uncomfortable with this idea and tried to find a classical interpretation. He could not. The quantisation was real, and it led directly to all of quantum physics. Planck's constant h is the fundamental scale of quantum effects: when h can be neglected (i.e. when energies are much larger than hf), classical physics applies. At atomic scales, hf is comparable to typical energies, and quantum effects dominate.

Historical Note:

Max Planck (1858–1947) described his introduction of energy quantisation as "an act of desperation." He had tried every classical approach and none worked. He was a deeply conservative physicist and expected someone to find a classical explanation. No one ever did. Planck received the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1918 for this discovery. 49.3 Photons and Light Quanta

In 1905, Albert Einstein took Planck's quantisation further. Planck had quantised the energies of the oscillators emitting and absorbing radiation, but still treated light itself as a continuous wave. Einstein proposed something more radical: light itself is composed of discrete packets of energy called photons (a term introduced later by Gilbert Lewis in 1926). Each photon carries energy proportional to the frequency of the light:

E = hf = hc / λ

Photons have no rest mass. They always travel at c in vacuum. Each photon carries

both energy E = hf and momentum p = h/λ = hf/c. Despite having zero rest mass,

photons have momentum — a fact confirmed by the radiation pressure exerted by light on surfaces.

Example — Photon energy at different wavelengths

Red light (λ = 700 nm): E =

hc/λ = (6.626 × 10 ³⁴ × 3.00 × 10⁸)/(700 × 10 ⁹) = 2.84 × 10 ¹⁹ J = 1.77 eV. Blue light

(λ = 400 nm): E = (6.626 × 10 ³⁴ × 3.00 × 10⁸)/(400 × 10 ⁹) = 4.97 × 10 ¹⁹ J = 3.10 eV.

A blue photon carries 1.75× more energy than a red photon.

49.4 The Photoelectric Effect Revisited

The photoelectric effect (introduced in earlier chapters) is the emission of electrons from a metal surface when light shines on it. Classical physics expected: (1) any frequency of light should work, given enough intensity; (2) brighter light should produce more energetic electrons; (3) energy should build up gradually, so there should be a time delay before emission begins. Experiments showed exactly the opposite: (1) only light above a threshold frequency causes emission, regardless of intensity; (2) brighter light produces more electrons but not more energetic ones — the electron energies depend only on frequency; (3) emission begins instantaneously, with no time delay even at very low intensities. Einstein's photon model explains all three observations. Each electron is ejected by a

single photon. The photon must have enough energy to overcome the work function φ

(the binding energy of the electron to the metal). If hf < φ, no electron is ejected

regardless of intensity (there are many low-energy photons but each individual one

cannot liberate an electron). If hf > φ, the excess energy becomes the electron's kinetic

energy:

KE_max = hf −φ

Einstein received the 1921 Nobel Prize in Physics specifically for this explanation of the photoelectric effect, not for relativity. It was the decisive experimental confirmation of the photon concept. Photoelectric cells, solar panels, and photomultiplier tubes all rely on this principle.

Sodium has work function φ = 2.28 eV. Light of

wavelength 400 nm shines on it. Photon energy: E = hc/λ = 1240 eV·nm / 400 nm =

49.5 Matter Waves

If light — classically a wave — can behave like a particle (a photon), could particles — classically objects with definite positions and momenta — behave like waves? In 1924, Louis de Broglie proposed exactly this. He suggested that every particle with momentum p has an associated wavelength:

λ = h / p = h / (mv)

For macroscopic objects, the de Broglie wavelength is negligibly small. A 1 kg ball

moving at 1 m/s has λ = h/mv = 6.626 × 10 ³⁴ m — far smaller than any atom,

completely undetectable. But for an electron moving at typical atomic speeds (∼10⁶

m/s), λ

10 ¹⁰ m 0.1 nm, which is comparable to the size of an atom and the spacing of atoms in a crystal. This should produce detectable wave effects. In 1927, Davisson and Germer fired electrons at a nickel crystal and observed diffraction patterns — exactly as expected for waves with the de Broglie wavelength. This was direct experimental proof that electrons have wave properties. Shortly after, G. P. Thomson (son of J. J. Thomson, who established the particle nature of electrons) independently observed electron diffraction through thin metal films. Father proved electrons are particles; son proved they are also waves.

49.6 Wave-Particle Duality

Quantum objects — electrons, photons, atoms, even molecules — exhibit both wave and particle behaviour. This is wave-particle duality. It is not that quantum objects are sometimes waves and sometimes particles; it is that they are neither, in the classical sense, but something new that has aspects of both. The double-slit experiment illustrates this beautifully. Fire electrons one at a time at a barrier with two slits. Each electron arrives as a localised dot on the detector — particle -like. But over time, the dots accumulate into an interference pattern of alternating bright and dark bands — wave-like. The interference pattern appears even when electrons pass through one at a time, meaning each electron's probability of landing at a point is determined by the wave nature of the electron — it "interferes with itself" by passing through both slits simultaneously. If you try to determine which slit the electron went through by placing a detector at one slit, the interference pattern disappears. The act of measurement disturbs the system. This is not a failure of instrumentation — it is a fundamental feature of quantum mechanics.

The Rule: Wave-particle duality: quantum objects do not have a definite nature (wave

or particle) independent of measurement. The experimental setup determines which aspect is revealed. Both aspects are real; neither alone is the complete picture. This duality is not limited to electrons and photons. Neutrons, protons, atoms, and even molecules as large as C₆₀ (buckminsterfullerene, containing 60 carbon atoms) have been shown to produce interference patterns in double-slit experiments. Wave behaviour is universal in quantum systems. Physics: Foundations of Physical Science Chapter 49 — Quantum Ideas

49.7 Electron Probability Clouds

The modern quantum mechanical description of an electron in an atom replaces Bohr's fixed circular orbits with probability distributions. The electron does not travel on a

definite path; instead, its state is described by a wave function ψ (psi), and the

probability of finding the electron in a small volume dV at position r is proportional to

|ψ|&#x00B2; dV.

The probability distribution

|ψ|&#x00B2;

describes an electron cloud: a three- dimensional region where the electron is likely to be found. For the ground state of

hydrogen (n = 1), the probability is highest at the Bohr radius (0.053 nm) from the

nucleus and decreases exponentially with distance. The electron is most likely to be near the nucleus but can be found at any distance with non-zero probability — including, in principle, on the other side of the universe, though the probability becomes vanishingly small.

Different energy levels correspond to different orbital shapes. The s-orbitals (l = 0) are

spherically symmetric. The p-orbitals (l = 1) are dumbbell-shaped with two lobes. The d

-orbitals (l = 2) have four lobes. These orbital shapes determine how atoms bond to

form molecules and are the basis of all chemistry.

Key Point: The electron cloud is not a picture of an electron smeared out in space —

when you measure the position of an electron, you always find it at a single, specific location. The cloud shows where that location is likely to be. Before measurement, the electron does not have a definite position; it has a probability distribution. After measurement, it has a definite (if temporary) position. 49.8 The Uncertainty Principle

In 1927, Werner Heisenberg derived one of the most profound results in all of physics: the Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle. It states that certain pairs of physical quantities — called conjugate variables — cannot both be known precisely at the same time. The more precisely one is known, the less precisely the other can be known. The most important conjugate pair is position and momentum:

Δx

Δp_x ≥ħ/2

There is also an energy-time uncertainty relation: ΔE

Δt ≥ħ/2. This has an important

consequence: a quantum state that exists for a short time Δt has an intrinsic energy

uncertainty ΔE ≥ħ/(2Δt). This is why spectral lines have finite widths rather than being

perfectly sharp — excited states have finite lifetimes Δt, giving an intrinsic energy

spread that broadens the emitted spectral line. The uncertainty principle is not due to clumsy measurement. It is not a statement about the limitations of instruments. It is a fundamental feature of nature: conjugate variables do not simultaneously have definite values. An electron does not have both a definite position and a definite momentum. Asking for both is like asking for the north edge of a cloud.

49.9 Energy Levels and Quantum States

In the full quantum mechanical treatment, an electron in an atom is described by four quantum numbers: the principal quantum number n (energy level), the angular

momentum quantum number l (orbital shape), the magnetic quantum number m_l

(orbital orientation), and the spin quantum number m_s (electron spin, either +1/2 or

1/2). Each unique set of quantum numbers specifies a quantum state. The Pauli exclusion principle (Wolfgang Pauli, 1925) states that no two electrons in the same atom can have the same set of four quantum numbers. This means each quantum state can hold at most one electron. The filling of quantum states according to the Pauli exclusion principle, with states filled from lowest energy upward, explains the structure of the periodic table. Hydrogen

has one electron; helium fills the n = 1 shell; lithium begins the n = 2 shell; neon fills

the n = 2 shell; and so on. The chemical properties of elements — valence, reactivity,

49.10 Quantum Transitions

A quantum transition is an abrupt change of a system from one quantum state to another. Unlike classical transitions, which can be gradual, quantum transitions are instantaneous: the system is in one state, then suddenly in another. There is no in- between state. Transitions between energy levels in atoms occur by the absorption or emission of photons (or by other processes such as electron collisions). The selection rules of quantum mechanics specify which transitions are allowed and which are forbidden. Not every transition between any two levels is permitted; the angular momentum quantum number l must change by exactly ±1 in an electric dipole transition (the most common type). Forbidden transitions are not truly impossible but are much less likely. Stimulated emission is a type of quantum transition with enormous practical importance. In spontaneous emission, an excited atom emits a photon at a random time and in a random direction. In stimulated emission, a passing photon with energy equal to the transition energy triggers the atom to emit an identical photon — same energy, same direction, same phase. This is the mechanism behind the laser (Light Amplification by Stimulated Emission of Radiation). Quantum tunnelling is another type of quantum behaviour with no classical analogue. A particle encountering a potential energy barrier higher than its kinetic energy would be completely reflected classically. In quantum mechanics, the wave function extends into and through the barrier, giving a non-zero probability that the particle will appear on the other side. Tunnelling underlies alpha decay, the operation of tunnel diodes, the scanning tunnelling microscope, and the nuclear fusion reactions in the Sun. 49.12 Quantum Physics in Modern Technology

Quantum physics is not merely abstract theory. It underlies virtually all modern technology. Semiconductors and transistors The operation of transistors — the building blocks of all computers, smartphones, and digital devices — is entirely quantum mechanical. The band structure of semiconductors (allowed and forbidden energy bands for electrons in a crystal lattice) arises from quantum mechanics. The behaviour of n-type and p-type doping, the p-n junction, and the field-effect transistor all rely on quantum energy levels and probability distributions. Lasers Lasers produce coherent, monochromatic, directional light through stimulated emission of photons (Section 49.10). A laser medium (gas, liquid, solid, or semiconductor) is pumped to create a population inversion (more atoms in an excited state than in the lower state), allowing stimulated emission to dominate. Lasers are used in surgery, communications, manufacturing, CD/DVD/Blu-ray players, barcode scanners, range- finding, and scientific instruments. MRI and NMR Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) exploits the quantum spin of protons (hydrogen nuclei) in the body. Proton spin has two quantised states in a magnetic field; radio- frequency photons with energy matching the energy difference between states cause transitions whose characteristics reveal tissue structure. MRI provides detailed images of soft tissue without ionising radiation. LED and solar cells Light-emitting diodes (LEDs) emit photons when electrons cross the band gap of a semiconductor: each photon energy equals the band gap energy, giving a specific colour. Solar cells work in reverse: photons with energy above the band gap excite electrons, creating electrical current. Both devices are direct applications of photon- electron energy level interactions. Atomic clocks Atomic clocks use the precisely defined transition frequency between two hyperfine energy levels of caesium-133 atoms (9,192,631,770 Hz, by definition) as a frequency standard. The GPS system depends on atomic clocks; without quantum-mechanical precision, GPS position errors would accumulate at kilometres per day. 49.13 Common Misunderstandings About Quantum Physics

• The uncertainty principle is not about measurement disturbance. The principle is often explained as "measuring position disturbs momentum." While some disturbance does occur, the principle is deeper: position and momentum are not simultaneously defined properties of a quantum system, independent of measurement. The uncertainty is fundamental, not instrumental. • Wave-particle duality does not mean objects switch between wave and particle. It means quantum objects have properties of both simultaneously. The apparent contradiction disappears when you recognise that "wave" and "particle" are classical concepts that do not perfectly map onto quantum reality. • Quantum randomness is not the same as ignorance. Classical randomness (a coin flip) arises because we do not know the initial conditions precisely enough. Quantum randomness (the exact moment an excited atom decays) is fundamental: there is no hidden information that, if known, would allow prediction. This was established by Bell's theorem and confirmed by experiment. • "Observer" in quantum mechanics does not mean a conscious being. Any physical interaction that correlates the quantum system with the measuring apparatus constitutes a "measurement." Consciousness is irrelevant. A rock interacting with an electron is an "observer" in the quantum sense. • Schrödinger's cat is a thought experiment illustrating the absurdity of applying quantum superposition to macroscopic objects, not a serious proposal about cat physics. Real cats are macroscopic, classical objects; decoherence prevents the superposition from surviving to macroscopic scales. Schrödinger intended his thought experiment as a reductio ad absurdum critique of naive quantum measurement theory. • Quantum mechanics does not say "anything is possible." Quantum tunnelling has a calculable, often extremely small probability. The uncertainty principle gives ranges, not permission for arbitrary behaviour. Quantum physics is precisely quantitative, not a licence for mysticism.

The ray model of light (geometric optics) treats light as straight-line rays that reflect and refract at boundaries. It works beautifully for mirrors, lenses, and prisms — systems where wavelength is much smaller than any relevant dimension. 28.2 Review of Superposition in Waves

Wave optics is built on the superposition principle established in Chapter 24: when two or more waves overlap, the resultant displacement is the algebraic sum of the individual displacements at every point. This is exact for linear media (which includes all optical materials for normal light intensities).

Two key concepts govern the outcome of superposition: phase and coherence. Phase describes where in its oscillation cycle a wave is at a given point. Two waves are in phase if their oscillations align (crest with crest, trough with trough). They are out of phase if one’s crest aligns with the other’s trough.

Coherence is the property of two waves that enables stable interference. Two waves are coherent if they maintain a fixed phase relationship over time. A laser produces highly coherent light; an incandescent bulb produces incoherent light (random phase relationships between different atomic emissions).

Incoherent sources can produce interference, but only over extremely short path-length differences; for the interference patterns studied here, coherent sources (lasers, or light from the same narrow source passed through both slits) are required.

The Rule:

For stable interference patterns, the sources must be coherent: they must have the same frequency and a fixed (not random) phase relationship. In practice, this means using the same source for both interfering beams.

28.3 Constructive and Destructive Interference

When two coherent waves of the same frequency and amplitude meet, the nature of their interference depends entirely on the path-length difference δ (delta) between the waves: the difference in distance each wave traveled from its source to the observation point.

Physics: Foundations of Physical Science Constructive interference occurs when the waves arrive in phase: crest aligns with crest, trough with trough. This happens when the path-length difference is a whole number of wavelengths. The waves reinforce, producing amplitude equal to the sum of the individual amplitudes (and intensity proportional to the square of amplitude).

Destructive interference occurs when the waves arrive exactly out of phase: crest aligns with trough. This happens when the path-length difference is a half-integer number of wavelengths. The waves cancel, producing zero amplitude (and zero intensity for equal- amplitude waves).

28.5 Young's Double-Slit Experiment

Young's double-slit experiment (1801) provided decisive evidence that light behaves as

coherent light source. On a screen beyond the slits, instead of the two bright strips predicted by particle/ray optics, he observed a regular pattern of alternating bright and dark bands: interference fringes.

The explanation: each slit acts as a coherent source of circular (cylindrical in 3D) wave fronts. These two sets of wave fronts overlap beyond the slits and interfere. At points on the screen where the path-length difference from the two slits is a whole number of wavelengths, constructive interference produces a bright fringe. Where the path-length difference is a half-integer of wavelengths, destructive interference produces a dark fringe.

Babinet’s Principle: the diffraction pattern from an opaque obstacle has the same intensity distribution as the pattern from an aperture of the same shape and size (except at the exact forward direction). This is why the shadow of a circular disk has a bright spot at its very center (Poisson’s spot or Arago spot) — the diffraction from the circular edge constructively interferes on-axis. This was experimentally predicted as a consequence of Fresnel’s wave theory and confirmed by Arago in 1818, converting many remaining wave-theory skeptics.

28.9 Polarization of Light

Polarization is a property of transverse waves in which the direction of oscillation is restricted.

For a transverse wave, the oscillation can occur in any direction perpendicular to propagation. Unpolarized light (like sunlight or incandescent light) has its electric field vectors oscillating in all perpendicular directions with equal probability and random phase. Polarized light has its electric field oscillating preferentially in one direction.

axis). Circular polarization: the electric field rotates uniformly around the propagation direction as the wave travels. Elliptical polarization is the general case, of which linear and circular are special cases.

Three mechanisms produce polarized light: selective absorption (polarizing filters), reflection at Brewster’s angle, and scattering. In everyday life, reflected light from non- metallic surfaces (water, glass, road, snow) is partially polarized parallel to the surface.

Polarizing sunglasses exploit this by transmitting only vertically polarized light, blocking the horizontally polarized reflected glare.

28.11 Evidence That Light Is Transverse

The existence of polarization is definitive proof that light is a transverse wave, not a longitudinal one.

A longitudinal wave — like sound — oscillates parallel to its propagation direction; there is only one possible oscillation direction, and polarization is impossible. A transverse wave oscillates perpendicular to propagation, allowing oscillations in many directions — and thus allowing polarization.

Sound cannot be polarized; light can. This experimental fact establishes that light must be transverse. The direction of the electric field’s oscillation is the polarization direction of light. Electromagnetic wave theory confirms this: the electric and magnetic fields in a light wave are always perpendicular to the direction of propagation and to each other.

Brewster’s angle (also called the polarizing angle) provides another demonstration.

28.12 Real-World Applications of Wave Optics

Wave optics underpins an enormous range of practical technologies, many of which would be impossible to engineer without understanding interference, diffraction, and Physics: Foundations of Physical Science polarization.

Anti-reflection coatings Camera lenses, microscope objectives, and eyeglasses are coated with thin dielectric films (typically MgF₂ or multiple layers of other materials) designed so that reflected

reflection from 4% to less than 0.5% for a specific wavelength. Multi-layer coatings (10–50 layers) can reduce reflection to below 0.1% across the entire visible spectrum.

Holography A hologram records the interference pattern between a reference beam and a beam scattered from an object. When the hologram is illuminated by a laser, it reconstructs the wavefronts from the object, producing a three-dimensional image. Credit card security holograms, medical imaging, and data storage systems all use holographic principles.

Interferometers Interferometers split a beam into two paths, send them along different routes, and recombine them to measure path-length differences with sub-wavelength precision.

The Michelson interferometer (1887) was used to show that the speed of light is the same in all directions, contributing to the development of special relativity. The LIGO (Laser Interferometer Gravitational-Wave Observatory) uses two 4 km arms; when a gravitational wave passes, one arm stretches by 10 ¹⁸ m — less than one-ten- thousandth the diameter of a proton — which is detected as a change in the interference pattern.

Optical data storage (CD, DVD, Blu-ray) CDs use pits and lands (0.83 μm wide) etched in a reflective aluminum layer. A laser reads the disc: when the beam crosses a pit edge, reflected light from the pit and the surrounding land interfere destructively, signaling a bit transition. DVD uses smaller features (0.74 μm) and a shorter-wavelength laser for higher density. Blu-ray uses a 405 nm blue laser for even finer features (0.32 μm) and higher capacity.

X-ray crystallography X-rays have wavelengths (0.01–1 nm) comparable to interatomic spacings in crystals.

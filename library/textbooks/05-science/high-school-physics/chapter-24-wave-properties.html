Wave Properties

24.1 What a Wave Is

A wave is a traveling disturbance that carries energy from one place to another without permanently transporting matter. This definition has three parts, each important.

Traveling disturbance: a wave is not a static pattern. It moves. The disturbance at one location propagates to neighboring locations over time, creating a moving pattern of some physical quantity (displacement, pressure, electric field, etc.).

Carries energy: waves transport energy from their source to distant locations. This is not incidental — it is the defining function of a wave in physics and engineering. The energy in sunlight that warms the Earth traveled across 150 million kilometers of space as electromagnetic waves. The energy that shatters a wine glass traveled from a speaker as sound waves.

Without permanently transporting matter: this distinguishes waves from particle flow.

In a water wave, individual water molecules move in small circles and return to their original positions — they do not travel across the ocean. In a sound wave, air molecules vibrate back and forth but do not stream outward. The pattern moves; the medium does not.

Key Point:

Exceptions exist: ocean currents do transport water. But the wave pattern on the surface is separate from the underlying current. It is the wave pattern — not the current — that carries the energy toward the shore.

24.2 Waves vs. Oscillations

Chapter 23 described oscillations — local, repetitive motion of a system around an equilibrium. Waves are what happen when that oscillation propagates. The distinction is critical:

pendulum, a vibrating string element. It occurs at a point in space and involves the same material moving back and forth repeatedly.

A wave is a spatial phenomenon: it involves many locations, each oscillating, with the phase of the oscillation shifting from one location to the next. The wave pattern is the set of all these oscillations arranged in space and time.

When you pluck a guitar string, you create a local disturbance. That disturbance travels along the string — the string element you plucked starts oscillating, then the adjacent element starts oscillating (slightly later), then the next, and so on. The wave is the propagating collection of all these oscillations, staggered in phase. At any instant, the string forms a spatial pattern of displacements. Over time, this pattern moves.

The Rule:

Oscillation: one location, repeating in time. Wave: many locations, each oscillating, with the oscillation propagating from one to the next in space and time.

24.3 Mechanical vs. Electromagnetic Waves

All waves fall into two broad categories: mechanical waves and electromagnetic waves.

Mechanical waves require a physical medium — a material substance through which the oscillation propagates. Sound waves travel through air, water, and solid rock, but cannot travel through a vacuum. Seismic waves travel through the Earth. Water waves travel along the water surface. The medium provides the restoring force and inertia that sustain the oscillation and allow it to propagate. Remove the medium, and the wave cannot exist.

24.4 Transverse and Longitudinal Waves

Within mechanical waves, there is a second important classification based on the relationship between the direction of oscillation and the direction of wave propagation.

In a transverse wave, the medium oscillates perpendicular to the direction the wave travels. A wave on a taut string is transverse: the string oscillates up and down while the wave pattern moves horizontally along the string. Electromagnetic waves are also transverse: the electric and magnetic fields oscillate perpendicular to the direction of propagation.

In a longitudinal wave, the medium oscillates parallel to the direction the wave travels.

The oscillations are compressions and rarefactions along the wave’s path. Sound waves in air are longitudinal: air molecules oscillate back and forth in the same direction the sound travels. A slinky pushed at one end creates a longitudinal wave as compressions travel along its length.

Some wave types have both transverse and longitudinal components. Surface waves on water are an example: water particles move in ellipses that combine horizontal (longitudinal) and vertical (transverse) motion. Seismic S-waves (secondary waves) are transverse; seismic P-waves (primary waves) are longitudinal. The different speeds and behavior of these wave types allow seismologists to determine the location and depth of earthquakes.

24.5 Amplitude, Wavelength, Frequency, and Period

A traveling wave is described by four key quantities. Three of these — amplitude, frequency, and period — are shared with oscillations (Chapter 23). The fourth, wavelength, is unique to waves.

Amplitude (A) is the maximum displacement of the medium from its equilibrium value.

For a transverse string wave, it is the maximum vertical deflection. For a sound wave, it is the maximum pressure variation above or below atmospheric. Amplitude is set by the source and determines how much energy the wave carries (energy is proportional to A²).

24.7 Energy Transport in Waves

Waves carry energy from their source to distant locations. This is the primary function of waves in nature and technology. Understanding how much energy a wave carries and how that energy is distributed in space is central to wave physics.

The intensity of a wave is the power delivered per unit area perpendicular to the wave’s direction of travel: I = P/A, measured in watts per square meter (W/m²).

Intensity is the key measurable quantity for energy transport.

For a wave spreading outward from a point source in three dimensions (like sound from a speaker, or light from a lamp), the wave energy spreads over a sphere whose area increases as 4πr². Since total power is conserved, intensity decreases with the square of distance: I ∝1/r². This is the inverse-square law for wave intensity. Doubling the distance from a source reduces the intensity to one-quarter; tripling the distance reduces it to one-ninth.

I ∝1/r²

Wave intensity is proportional to the square of the amplitude: I ∝A². This is the same relationship as for oscillator energy in Chapter 23. Physically, a wave with twice the amplitude delivers four times the energy per unit area per unit time.

For waves confined to two dimensions (like surface waves on water), energy spreads over a circle of circumference 2πr, and intensity decreases as 1/r. For waves confined to one dimension (like a wave on a string or in a tube), intensity does not decrease with distance in the ideal case (no damping).

24.8 Wave Fronts and Direction of Propagation

A wave front is a surface connecting all points in a medium that are at the same phase of oscillation — for example, the crest of a wave or the leading edge of a disturbance.

Wave fronts propagate outward from the source at the wave speed.

For a point source in a uniform medium, wave fronts are concentric spheres (in three dimensions) or concentric circles (in two dimensions, such as on a water surface). These are spherical waves. The direction of propagation is always perpendicular to the wave front surface, pointing radially outward.

Far from a point source, the curved wave fronts become nearly flat over any small Physics: Foundations of Physical Science region. These nearly flat wave fronts are called plane waves. Plane waves have parallel rays and constant amplitude across the wave front. Many physical waves can be treated as plane waves in the regions where they are analyzed — this is a useful simplification that greatly reduces the mathematical complexity of wave problems.

The ray is an imaginary line perpendicular to the wave front, indicating the direction of wave propagation and energy transport. Ray diagrams are used extensively in optics and acoustics to trace the paths of waves through systems with boundaries, mirrors, and lenses. The ray model is a simplification valid when the wavelength is much smaller than any obstacles or apertures the wave encounters.

24.9 Reflection of Waves

Reflection occurs when a wave encounters a boundary between two media and is at least partly redirected back into the original medium. All wave types — mechanical and electromagnetic — reflect from boundaries.

The law of reflection states that the angle of reflection equals the angle of incidence, both measured from the normal (perpendicular) to the reflecting surface. This law applies to all waves when the reflecting surface is smooth relative to the wavelength (specular reflection).

When the surface is rough (bumpy on the scale of the wavelength), reflection is diffuse and scattered in many directions.

(Law of Reflection) When a transverse wave on a string reflects from a fixed end (a wall), the reflected wave is inverted — a crest becomes a trough. This is a phase change of 180° on reflection from a denser medium. When the wave reflects from a free end (an end that can move), the reflected wave is not inverted. This distinction matters greatly for standing wave formation (Section 24.14).

Partial reflection and partial transmission occur at most real boundaries. When sound hits a wall, most is reflected and some is transmitted into the wall material. When light hits a glass surface, about 4% is reflected and 96% is transmitted. The fractions depend on the wave type and the properties of the two media.

24.12 Interference of Waves

Interference is the combination of two or more waves at the same location in space and time. It is, along with diffraction, the most distinctive wave behavior — one that has no counterpart in particle motion.

When two waves meet, the resulting displacement at each point is the sum of the individual displacements (this is the superposition principle, Section 24.13). Interference can be constructive (the waves reinforce each other, producing larger amplitude) or destructive (the waves partially or completely cancel each other).

Constructive interference occurs when two waves arrive at a point in phase — that is, when their crests arrive simultaneously. The displacements add, producing amplitude equal to the sum of the individual amplitudes.

Destructive interference occurs when two waves arrive out of phase — when the crest of one coincides with the trough of the other.

The displacements partially or completely cancel. For two waves of equal amplitude arriving exactly 180° out of phase, the result is zero displacement: complete cancellation.

Interference is responsible for the iridescent colors in soap bubbles and oil films (thin- film interference), the noise-canceling effect in noise-canceling headphones (destructive interference of sound), the bright and dark bands in double-slit experiments (Young’s experiment), and the operating principle of radio antennas, interferometric telescopes, and laser-based sensors.

24.13 Superposition Principle

The superposition principle states that when two or more waves overlap in a linear medium, the resultant displacement at any point at any time is the algebraic sum of the displacements of the individual waves. Each wave passes through the other unchanged; after the waves separate, each continues as if the interaction never occurred.

This principle is fundamental and far-reaching. It holds exactly for linear media — media in which the restoring force is proportional to displacement, including most real media for small amplitudes. It fails for very large amplitudes (nonlinear behavior), where waves can interact in more complex ways (a field called nonlinear optics or nonlinear acoustics).

Superposition underlies interference and all complex wave behavior. The shape of a wave on a guitar string at any instant is the superposition of all the normal modes (harmonics) simultaneously present. The signal in a telephone wire is the superposition of thousands of simultaneously transmitted conversations. The light from a fluorescent lamp is the superposition of many individual atomic emission frequencies. Fourier

24.14 Standing Waves (Introductory)

When a wave reflects from a boundary and returns along the same path, the original (incident) wave and the reflected wave coexist and superimpose.

For specific frequencies, this superposition produces a standing wave: a pattern that appears stationary rather than traveling.

A standing wave has nodes — points that never move — and antinodes — points that oscillate with maximum amplitude. Nodes occur where the incident and reflected waves always cancel (destructive interference). Antinodes occur where they always reinforce (constructive interference). Unlike a traveling wave, a standing wave does not transport energy along the medium; instead, energy is stored in the oscillating pattern.

Standing waves form when the length of the medium fits a whole number of half- wavelengths. For a string fixed at both ends (length L), standing waves occur at frequencies: Physics: Foundations of Physical Science

Musical instruments produce standing waves: the vibrating string of a guitar, the air column in a flute, the membrane of a drum — all resonate at their characteristic harmonic frequencies. The combination of harmonics present and their relative amplitudes determines the timbre (tone quality) that distinguishes one instrument from another playing the same note.

Historical Note: Thomas Young performed his famous double-slit experiment in 1801,

demonstrating that light produces interference fringes consistent with wave behavior.

This was decisive evidence that light is a wave — contradicting Newton’s corpuscular theory. Young’s experiment is now reproduced routinely with electrons and even individual photons, revealing the quantum wave-particle duality at the heart of modern physics.

26.1 What Light Is

Light is a form of electromagnetic radiation — a self-sustaining oscillation of electric and magnetic fields that propagates through space at a speed that depends on the

called the speed of light. This is one of the fundamental constants of nature, the same for all observers regardless of their motion — a fact that underpins Einstein’s special theory of relativity.

Visible light is the portion of the electromagnetic spectrum detectable by the human eye, spanning wavelengths from approximately 380 nm (violet) to 700 nm (red). The full electromagnetic spectrum extends from radio waves (wavelengths of kilometers) to gamma rays (wavelengths smaller than an atomic nucleus), all traveling at the same speed c in vacuum and differing only in frequency and wavelength.

Light exhibits a dual nature: under some conditions it behaves as a wave (producing interference and diffraction patterns), and under others as a stream of discrete energy packets called photons. For this chapter, the wave and ray models are sufficient. The photon model is needed for quantum optics and the photoelectric effect, covered in later chapters.

26.3 Propagation of Light in Vacuum and Media

In a uniform, isotropic medium (one with the same properties in all directions), light travels in straight lines. This is the basis of the ray model of light: we represent a beam of light as a geometric ray — a directed line segment pointing in the direction of propagation. Ray optics (also called geometric optics) is an excellent approximation when the wavelength is much smaller than any obstacles or apertures the light encounters, which is true for most everyday optical situations.

In vacuum, the ray model is exact: light always travels in a straight line at speed c. In a homogeneous medium (uniform composition and density), light also travels in straight

In a non-uniform medium (such as the atmosphere with temperature gradients, or glass with stress patterns), the index of refraction varies from point to point. Rays follow curved paths through such media, continuously bending toward regions of higher n (slower speed). This is the physics behind mirages, the twinkling of stars, and the atmospheric bending that keeps radio signals from escaping into space.

26.4 Reflection of Light

Reflection is the redirection of light back into the medium from which it came when it strikes a surface or interface. All surfaces reflect some light; the fraction reflected depends on the optical properties of both media and the angle of incidence.

Specular reflection occurs from smooth, polished surfaces (mirrors, still water, polished metal). Parallel incoming rays reflect as parallel outgoing rays, producing a clear, undistorted image. The surface appears shiny.

Diffuse reflection occurs from rough surfaces (matte paper, walls, skin, unpolished wood). Parallel incoming rays scatter in many directions because each small patch of the surface faces a slightly different direction. No coherent image is formed. The surface appears dull or matte. Most surfaces in everyday life produce diffuse reflection, which is why we can see them from many viewing angles without them appearing as mirrors.

Partial reflection occurs at the interface of two transparent media (air–glass, water–air).

Some light is reflected back, and some is transmitted into the second medium. The Physics: Foundations of Physical Science fraction reflected depends on the index of refraction difference and the angle of incidence. For normal incidence at an air–glass interface (n_glass 1.5), about 4% of the light is reflected and 96% transmitted. This partial reflection is why windows act as both transparent surfaces and weak mirrors.

26.5 The Law of Reflection

The law of reflection governs the direction of specular reflection. It states that the angle of reflection equals the angle of incidence, both measured from the normal to the reflecting surface at the point of incidence.

The normal is an imaginary line perpendicular to the surface at the point where the ray strikes. It is always measured from the normal, not from the surface. A common mistake is to measure angles from the surface; this gives the complement of the correct angle.

The incident ray, the reflected ray, and the normal at the point of incidence all lie in the same plane, called the plane of incidence. The reflected ray is on the opposite side of the normal from the incident ray, in the same plane. This coplanarity is part of the law of reflection.

26.6 Plane Mirrors and Image Formation

A plane mirror is a perfectly flat, specularly reflecting surface. When an object is placed in front of a plane mirror, reflected rays diverge from the mirror in a way that appears to an observer as if they are coming from a point behind the mirror. This apparent source location is the image.

The image formed by a plane mirror has four key properties that follow directly from the law of reflection applied geometrically. First, the image is virtual: the reflected rays Physics: Foundations of Physical Science diverge and do not actually converge at the image location; the image exists only where rays appear to originate when extended backward. Second, the image is upright (not inverted). Third, the image is the same size as the object (magnification = 1).

Fourth, the image distance behind the mirror equals the object distance in front of it: d_image = d_object.

Plane mirrors produce left–right reversal, more precisely described as a front–back reversal (the image’s depth axis is reversed relative to the object’s). When you raise your right hand, the image appears to raise its left hand, because the axis pointing toward the mirror is reversed while the lateral axes are unchanged.

Key Point:

A virtual image cannot be projected onto a screen because the light rays do not actually pass through the image location. A real image, formed by converging rays, can be projected (as in a movie projector or camera). The mirror in a bathroom forms a virtual image.

26.7 Refraction of Light

Refraction is the change in direction of a ray of light as it crosses the boundary between two media with different speeds of light. When light moves from a medium in which it travels fast into one where it travels slower, the ray bends toward the normal.

When it moves into a faster medium, the ray bends away from the normal.

Refraction occurs because different parts of the wave front reach the boundary at

new medium first slows down (or speeds up) while the rest of the front continues at the original speed. This differential speed change rotates the wave front, changing the direction of propagation. (This is the wave explanation of Snell’s Law.) Everyday examples of refraction: a spoon appears bent in a glass of water (the underwater portion is displaced toward the surface in your perception because rays from it bend as they leave the water); a pool appears shallower than it is (light from the bottom bends away from the normal as it enters air, making the bottom appear higher); the apparent position of the setting Sun is slightly above the horizon even after the Sun has geometrically set (atmospheric refraction bends the rays over the horizon).

Refraction is simultaneously transmission: when light is refracted, it is transmitted into Physics: Foundations of Physical Science the second medium. The refracted ray and reflected ray coexist at any real interface; their relative intensities depend on the materials and the angle.

26.8 Why Light Bends Between Media

The physical reason for refraction is the change in wave speed. When a wave enters a medium where it travels more slowly, each part of the wave front that crosses the boundary first is slowed before the rest of the front reaches the boundary. The wave front pivots around the point of contact, changing the direction of propagation.

An analogy: imagine a column of soldiers marching in a line across a field, hitting a muddy region at an angle. The soldiers who reach the mud first slow down while the others continue at full speed. The formation rotates — the direction of the column’s advance changes. This is exactly what happens to a wave front entering a slower medium.

26.12 Dispersion and Prisms

Dispersion is the variation of the speed of light (and therefore the index of refraction) with wavelength within a medium. Most transparent materials have higher indices of refraction for shorter wavelengths (violet) than for longer wavelengths (red). This means violet light bends more than red light when refracted.

When white light (a mixture of all visible wavelengths) passes through a prism, each wavelength refracts by a slightly different angle. After two refractions (entering and exiting the prism), the wavelengths are spread out into a spectrum. Violet light emerges at the largest angle from the original direction; red light at the smallest. The result is the familiar rainbow of colors produced by a glass prism.

Natural rainbows arise from the same physics. Sunlight enters raindrops, refracts at the entry surface, reflects off the back of the drop (TIR or partial reflection), and refracts again on exit. Each wavelength exits at a slightly different angle. Red light exits at about 42° from the anti-solar point (the point directly opposite the Sun from the observer); violet at about 40°. The observer sees a circular arc with red on the outside and violet on the inside.

Dispersion is a practical challenge in optical systems: a simple lens refracts different colors to slightly different focal points,

colored fringing around images (chromatic aberration). Telescope and camera lens designers use combinations of glasses with different dispersion characteristics (achromatic doublets) to correct this, bringing at least two wavelengths to the same focus.

26.13 Atmospheric Refraction and Mirages

The atmosphere is not uniform: air density (and therefore index of refraction) decreases with altitude. Rays from astronomical objects outside the atmosphere are continuously

atmospheric refraction makes celestial objects appear higher above the horizon than they actually are.

The effect is largest near the horizon, where the air path is longest. The Sun and Moon Physics: Foundations of Physical Science appear about 0.5° higher than their actual positions, which equals almost exactly one solar or lunar diameter. This means that when you see the bottom of the Sun touching the horizon, the entire Sun has actually set geometrically — you are seeing it above its true position due to atmospheric refraction.

Mirages are optical illusions caused by atmospheric refraction in non-standard temperature gradients. In a hot desert (or over a hot road), the air near the surface is much hotter and less dense than the air above, creating a gradient in which the index of refraction decreases toward the ground. Rays from the sky travel downward and then curve back upward due to this gradient, creating TIR-like behavior at the hot surface layer. The observer sees an image of the sky below the horizon — which the brain interprets as a pool of water reflecting the sky.

An inferior mirage (the most common type, seen on hot roads) shows an image below the actual object. A superior mirage occurs when cold air lies below warmer air (a temperature inversion): rays curve downward, bringing images of objects over the horizon into view. Mirages of ships and coastlines at extreme distances have been observed in the Arctic and over large cold lakes.

Chapter 36: Buoyancy

36.1 Why Objects Float or Sink

The everyday intuition about floating and sinking is roughly this: light things float, heavy things sink. This is wrong as stated. A steel aircraft carrier floats; a grain of sand sinks. The relevant quantity is not weight alone but the relationship between the weight of an object and the weight of fluid that object displaces. When an object is placed in a fluid, the fluid exerts forces on every surface of the object. These forces are pressure forces — they act perpendicular to each surface element. Because pressure increases with depth (Chapter 35), the pressure on the bottom of the object is greater than the pressure on the top. The net result of all these pressure forces is an upward force called the buoyant force. Whether the object floats or sinks depends on the competition between two forces: its weight (downward) and the buoyant force (upward). If the buoyant force equals or exceeds the weight, the object floats. If the weight exceeds the maximum possible buoyant force, the object sinks. The maximum possible buoyant force is the weight of fluid the object would displace if fully submerged.

Key Point:

The tendency to float or sink is determined by density, not by total mass or weight. An object whose average density is less than the fluid will float; an object denser than the fluid will sink. This is why a hollow steel ship (average density less Physics: Foundations of Physical Science Chapter 36 — Buoyancy than water) floats while a solid steel sphere (density 7870 kg/m³, much greater than water) sinks.

36.2 Pressure Differences in Fluids

Consider a rectangular block submerged in a fluid of density ρ_f. The block has height

h, horizontal cross-sectional area A, and its top face is at depth d below the surface. Its bottom face is at depth d + h.

The pressure on the top face is P_top = P₀ + ρ_f g d. The pressure on the bottom face

is P_bottom = P₀ + ρ_f g(d + h). The difference in pressure between the bottom and

top faces is:

ΔP = P_bottom −P_top = ρ_f g h

The downward force on the top face is F_top = P_top × A (pushing down). The upward

force on the bottom face is F_bottom = P_bottom × A (pushing up). The net upward

force is:

F_b = F_bottom −F_top = ΔP × A = ρ_f g h A

Note that d, the depth of the top face, cancels out entirely. The buoyant force does not depend on how deep the object is submerged — only on its volume and the fluid density. A submerged cube experiences the same buoyant force at 1 m depth as at 100 m depth (assuming the fluid density is uniform, which is an excellent approximation for liquids).

36.3 Origin of the Buoyant Force

The derivation above was for a rectangular block, but the result is completely general. For any shape, the net upward pressure force equals the weight of fluid that would occupy the volume of the object. This can be shown by imagining the object removed and replaced by an identical volume of fluid: that fluid volume would be in equilibrium, so the net pressure force on it (from the surrounding fluid) must equal its weight. The same pressure forces act on any object of the same shape and volume, regardless of what the object is made of. Physics: Foundations of Physical Science Chapter 36 — Buoyancy The buoyant force arises purely from the pressure gradient in the fluid, not from anything special about the object. It acts even on objects much denser than the fluid — the buoyant force on a steel block submerged in water is the same as the buoyant force on an identically shaped block of wood. The difference in behaviour (steel sinks, wood floats) comes from the different weights of the two blocks, not from different buoyant forces.

Historical Note:

Archimedes of Syracuse (287–212 BC) is credited with discovering the relationship between buoyancy and displaced fluid volume. According to legend, he was asked by King Hiero II to determine whether a gold crown had been adulterated with silver without melting it down. By noticing the water rising when he entered a full bath, he realised that displaced volume provides the key. He allegedly ran through the streets shouting “Eureka” (I have found it). The method works because gold and silver have different densities: equal masses of gold and silver displace different volumes of water.

36.4 Archimedes' Principle

Archimedes' principle is the formal statement of what the pressure-difference argument shows:

The Rule:

Archimedes' Principle: The buoyant force on an object immersed in a fluid equals the weight of the fluid displaced by the object.

F_b = ρ_f V_displaced g

Archimedes' principle applies to all fluids — liquids and gases alike. A helium balloon displaces air; the buoyant force on it equals the weight of air displaced. The same principle that makes steel ships float in water makes hot-air balloons and dirigibles

float in air. The only difference is the magnitude of the fluid density (ρ_air

1.2 kg/m³

vs ρ_water = 1000 kg/m³), which is why air buoyancy is negligible for most solid

objects but dominant for lightweight gas-filled envelopes.

36.6 Apparent Weight in Fluids

Neutral buoyancy is the condition where the average density of an object exactly matches the fluid density, so the object neither rises nor sinks. Submarines achieve neutral buoyancy by adjusting the amount of water in ballast tanks. Fish use a gas- filled swim bladder to regulate their average density. Scuba divers wear weighted belts and buoyancy control devices to achieve near-neutral buoyancy at chosen depths. The average density of a hollow object includes the air or gas inside it. A steel ship has an enormous internal air volume. Its average density (total mass divided by total enclosed volume including internal air) is less than water, so it floats. If the hull is breached and water floods in, average density increases until it exceeds seawater density, and the ship sinks.

36.9 Submerged vs. Floating Objects

For a fully submerged object, the displaced volume is fixed (equal to the object’s total volume), so the buoyant force is fixed regardless of depth. If the object’s weight exceeds this fixed buoyant force, it sinks; if the buoyant force exceeds the weight, it accelerates upward and will surface. For a floating object, the system self-adjusts: if the object is pushed down, more fluid is

displaced, increasing F_b until equilibrium is restored. If the object bobs up, less fluid is

displaced, decreasing F_b until equilibrium is restored again. This is why floating

equilibrium is stable (for a correctly shaped object) — any small perturbation generates a restoring force. A fully submerged sinking object does not experience this self-correction: as it sinks deeper, the buoyant force remains constant (because displaced volume is fixed), so the net downward force is constant and it continues sinking at a rate determined by drag.

Key Point:

An object will reach floating equilibrium only if its density is less than the

fluid density. If ρ_obj > ρ_f, no stable floating equilibrium exists: the object will sink to

the bottom regardless of shape (unless its shape allows it to capture air and reduce average density, as a ship’s hull does).

36.10 Stability of Floating Bodies

A floating object in equilibrium may be stable (returns to upright after a small tilt) or unstable (capsizes when tilted). Stability depends on the relationship between the centre of gravity (G) and the metacentre (M), a geometric point determined by the Physics: Foundations of Physical Science Chapter 36 — Buoyancy shape of the waterplane. The centre of buoyancy (B) is the centroid of the displaced fluid volume. When a ship tilts, B shifts to one side because the shape of the displaced volume changes. The point at which the vertical line through the new B intersects the original vertical axis of the ship is called the metacentre M. If M is above G, the ship is stable: the buoyant force creates a righting moment that restores upright. If M is below G, the ship is unstable and will capsize. This is why ship designs are tested for metacentric height (GM = distance from G to M). Cargo ships must be loaded carefully to keep the centre of gravity low enough. Overloading upper decks or uneven cargo distribution raises G toward or above M, creating capsize risk. The 1994 Estonia ferry disaster and many other maritime casualties involved loss of stability.

Study Tip:

For high school physics, the key stability rule is: low centre of gravity improves stability. Ships, racing cars, and double-decker buses are all designed to keep heavy components as low as possible for the same reason.

36.11 Applications of Buoyancy in Engineering and Nature

Buoyancy governs an enormous range of engineering systems and natural phenomena. In naval architecture, the design of ships, submarines, and offshore platforms requires precise calculation of buoyant forces, stability, and load distribution. A submarine surfaces by blowing compressed air into ballast tanks to displace water; it submerges by flooding those tanks to increase average density above seawater. In aviation and meteorology, atmospheric buoyancy drives convection: warm air, being less dense than cool air, rises. Thunderstorms, sea breezes, and global atmospheric circulation are all buoyancy-driven. Hot-air balloons heat the air inside the envelope to reduce its density below ambient air density, generating an upward buoyant force. In medicine, hydrostatic weighing uses buoyant force to determine body volume and hence body density, from which body fat percentage is estimated (since fat is less dense than lean muscle or bone). More recently, underwater weighing has been supplemented by air displacement plethysmography (Bod Pod), which uses the same principle in air rather than water. In geology and geophysics, isostasy is the buoyancy of Earth’s crust floating on the

denser mantle (density ρ_mantle

3300 kg/m³; ρ_crust

2700 kg/m³). Mountains have deep crustal roots that extend downward into the mantle, just as icebergs extend below water. Ice ages load continents with ice, depressing the crust; after glaciers melt, the crust slowly rebounds — this post-glacial rebound is still occurring in Scandinavia Physics: Foundations of Physical Science Chapter 36 — Buoyancy at several millimetres per year. In biology, swim bladders in fish, pneumatophores in some marine invertebrates, and the gas-filled chambers of nautiluses all exploit buoyancy for depth control. The nautilus fills its chambers with a gas (mostly nitrogen) at less than atmospheric pressure and regulates buoyancy by controlling the amount of fluid inside those chambers via an osmotic pump.

36.12 Common Mistakes in Buoyancy Problems

•

Using object density instead of fluid density in the buoyant force formula. F_b =

ρ_fluid V g, not ρ_object V g. The buoyant force depends on the fluid being

displaced, not the object doing the displacing. • Using total volume instead of submerged volume for a floating object. If an

object is only 60% submerged, V_displaced = 0.60 V_total. Using V_total

overestimates F_b by a factor of 1/0.60. • Forgetting that buoyant force is constant with depth for a fully submerged incompressible object. Students sometimes think a deeper object experiences greater buoyancy because pressure is greater. The net upward force from pressure is the same at any depth because both top and bottom pressures increase equally. •

Confusing weight and mass in F_b = ρ V g. The buoyant force is already a force

(in newtons). Do not multiply again by g when comparing to weight. Weight W = mg; F_b = ρ_f V g. Compare forces directly. • Incorrectly applying the floating fraction formula to a sinking object. V_sub/V_total = ρ_obj/ρ_f applies only when the object is floating at equilibrium. If ρ_obj > ρ_f, the object sinks and there is no floating equilibrium. • Ignoring the buoyancy of air for gas-filled objects. For balloons, blimps, and

similar objects, the relevant fluid is air, not water. Use ρ_air

1.204 kg/m³.

Common Mistake:

Always identify which fluid is being displaced before writing the buoyant force formula. A balloon rising in air displaces air; a ship floating on water displaces water; a submarine hull experiences the buoyancy of seawater. Using the wrong fluid density is the most common buoyancy calculation error.

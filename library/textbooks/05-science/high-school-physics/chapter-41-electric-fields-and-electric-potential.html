Chapter 41: Electric Fields and Electric Potential

41.1 Why Physicists Use Field Models

Before the field concept, electric and gravitational forces were described as “action at a distance”: one body exerted force directly on another with nothing between them. Newton himself was uncomfortable with this idea but could not explain how gravity propagated across empty space. Michael Faraday (1791–1867), who had no formal mathematical training but extraordinary physical intuition, introduced the field concept. He imagined that a charged object modifies the space around it in a way that is physically real and that other charges respond to this modification — not to the distant charge directly. The field exists at every point in space and carries both energy and momentum. The field concept is not merely a bookkeeping device. It resolves the action-at-a- distance problem by specifying that charge A creates a field that travels outward at the speed of light, and that field (not charge A directly) exerts force on charge B. If charge A is suddenly moved, the field change propagates at the speed of light, and charge B “finds out” about the change only after time r/c has elapsed. This is physically correct: Physics: Foundations of Physical Science Chapter 41 — Electric Fields and Electric Potential nothing in electromagnetism acts instantaneously over distance. The field concept is also mathematically powerful. Instead of tracking every pairwise interaction between charges, we can (1) compute the field created by one charge distribution, then (2) find the force on any other charge by multiplying charge × field. The two steps are independent, which greatly simplifies complex problems.

Historical Note:

Michael Faraday (1791–1867) came from a poor background and received almost no formal schooling, yet became one of the greatest experimental physicists ever. He introduced the concepts of electric and magnetic fields, discovered electromagnetic induction (the basis of all electric generators), invented the electric motor concept, and made fundamental contributions to electrochemistry. James Clerk Maxwell later provided the mathematical framework for Faraday’s field ideas in Maxwell’s equations (1865).

41.2 What an Electric Field Is

The electric field E at a point in space is defined as the electric force per unit positive test charge that would be experienced by a small positive test charge placed at that point:

E = F / q

The test charge is assumed to be infinitesimally small so that it does not disturb the field it is measuring. In practice, “small positive test charge” means a charge small enough that its presence does not significantly alter the charge distribution creating the field. The electric field is a vector field: at every point in space there is a vector (an arrow) specifying the strength and direction of the electric influence at that location. The SI unit of electric field is newtons per coulomb (N/C), or equivalently, volts per metre (V/m) — two names for the same unit, as we will show in Section 41.12. Once the electric field at a point is known, the force on any charge q placed at that point is:

F = qE

41.3 Field Direction and Field Lines

The direction of the electric field at a point is the direction of the force that would be exerted on a small positive test charge placed at that point. Since like charges repel, the field of a positive charge points away from the charge (a positive test charge would be pushed away). The field of a negative charge points toward the charge (a positive test charge would be attracted inward). Electric field lines (or lines of force) are a visual representation of the electric field. They are drawn so that: • The direction of the field line at any point is tangent to the line at that point. • The density of lines (number of lines per unit area crossing a surface perpendicular to the lines) is proportional to the magnitude of the field. • Field lines begin on positive charges and end on negative charges (or travel to/from infinity). • Field lines never cross each other. (If they did, the field would have two directions at that point, which is impossible.) For an isolated positive point charge, field lines radiate outward uniformly in all directions. For an isolated negative charge, they converge inward. For two opposite charges (a dipole), lines leave the positive charge and curve to arrive at the negative charge. For two like charges, the lines bend away from the region between the charges and no lines cross the midpoint plane.

Key Point:

Field lines are not paths that charges travel. They show the direction a positive charge would accelerate at each point, not the trajectory it would follow (which depends on its initial velocity and on all subsequent forces).

41.6 Electric Force from an Electric Field

The electric field provides a compact way to describe the effect of a charge distribution on any other charge placed in the field. Given the field E at a location, the force on a charge q placed there is simply:

F = qE

This relationship is why the field is defined per unit positive charge: it gives the force

per unit charge, so the actual force on any charge q is always F = qE, regardless of the

sign or magnitude of q. For a charged particle of mass m in an electric field, Newton’s second law gives its acceleration:

a = F/m = qE/m

This relationship is exploited in particle accelerators (protons and electrons are accelerated in electric fields), cathode ray tubes (electrons are deflected by electric fields to steer the beam), mass spectrometers (ions are separated by the ratio of charge to mass, q/m), and inkjet printers (charged droplets are steered by electric fields).

41.7 Uniform Electric Fields

A uniform electric field has the same magnitude and direction at every point in a region. The field lines are parallel and equally spaced. The most common way to create a uniform field is with two large, parallel, conducting plates carrying equal and opposite charges (a parallel-plate capacitor). Between the plates of a parallel-plate capacitor separated by distance d with potential difference V (voltage), the uniform field has magnitude:

E = V / d

In a uniform field, the force on a charged particle is constant (same magnitude and direction everywhere). This means a charged particle in a uniform field undergoes constant acceleration, analogous to projectile motion in a gravitational field. The kinematic equations from Chapter 3 apply directly with the electric acceleration a = Physics: Foundations of Physical Science Chapter 41 — Electric Fields and Electric Potential qE/m replacing g.

but electrons have very small mass.

41.8 Electric Potential Energy

Just as gravity is a conservative force (meaning work done by gravity depends only on start and end positions, not on the path), the electric force is also conservative. This means we can define an electric potential energy (U) that depends on position. When a charge q moves from point A to point B in an electric field, the work done by the electric field is:

W_AB = −ΔU = −(U_B −U_A) = U_A −U_B

For two point charges Q and q separated by distance r, the electric potential energy is:

U = k Q q / r

⁹

The reference point (where U = 0) is chosen at r = ∞(infinite separation). This means

two repelling charges have positive potential energy — they would accelerate away from each other, converting potential energy to kinetic energy. Two attracting charges have negative potential energy — energy must be supplied to separate them. Physics: Foundations of Physical Science Chapter 41 — Electric Fields and Electric Potential

41.9 Electric Potential (Voltage)

Electric potential V (voltage) is the electric potential energy per unit charge. Like the electric field is force per charge, potential is energy per charge:

V = U / q

The electric potential due to a single point charge Q at distance r is:

V = k Q / r

⁹ Potential is a scalar, which makes it easier to work with than the vector electric field in many problems. The potential due to multiple charges is the algebraic (scalar) sum of

the potentials due to each charge: V_total = V₁ + V₂ + V₃ + … No vector addition is

required. The potential energy of a charge q at a location where the potential is V is U = qV. The

work done moving a charge q through a potential difference ΔV is:

W = q ΔV = q(V_B −V_A)

Example — Potential of a point charge

Find the electric potential 0.30 m from a charge of +6.0 μc. V = kQ/r = (8.99 × 10⁹)(6.0 × 10 ⁶)/0.30 = 1.80 × 10⁵ V = 180 kV.

Note: if we asked for the potential energy of a 2.0 μc charge placed at this point, U =

qV = (2.0 × 10 ⁶)(1.80 × 10⁵) = 0.36 J.

41.11 Equipotential Surfaces

An equipotential surface (or equipotential line in two dimensions) is a surface (or curve) on which the electric potential is the same at every point. Moving a charge along an

equipotential surface requires no work because ΔV = 0 and W = qΔV = 0.

Equipotential surfaces and electric field lines are always perpendicular to each other. This follows because the electric field has no component along an equipotential surface (if it did, moving a charge along the surface would require work). The field is entirely perpendicular to the equipotential surface. For a positive point charge, equipotential surfaces are concentric spheres centred on the charge. Field lines are radial lines crossing these spheres at right angles. For a uniform field between parallel plates, equipotential surfaces are planes parallel to the plates, equally spaced; field lines are perpendicular to the plates. Conductors in electrostatic equilibrium are equipotential surfaces: all points on or inside a conductor (when no current flows) are at the same potential. This is why Physics: Foundations of Physical Science Chapter 41 — Electric Fields and Electric Potential charge distributes on the outer surface of a conductor and why the field inside a conductor in electrostatic equilibrium is zero. (Any interior field would drive currents, which would redistribute charge until the field vanishes.)

Key Point:

The relationship between equipotentials and field lines is the electrostatic

analogy of contour lines on a topographic map: contour lines (equal height = equal

gravitational potential energy) are always perpendicular to the direction of steepest

descent (the direction a ball would roll = the direction of gravitational force). Electric

potential plays the role of height; field lines play the role of slope direction.

41.12 Relationship Between Field and Potential

The electric field and electric potential are two ways of describing the same physical situation. They are related: the field is the negative rate of change of potential with distance (the negative gradient of potential). In one dimension:

E_x = −ΔV / Δx

This relationship shows that (a) the units V/m and N/C are equivalent (both are SI units of electric field), and (b) the field is stronger where the equipotentials are more closely spaced (steeper potential gradient), just as gravity is stronger where topographic contours are more closely spaced (steeper slope). This relation also explains why the electric field inside a conductor in electrostatic

equilibrium is zero: the conductor is an equipotential (no change in V), so ΔV/Δx = 0,

so E = 0. Conversely, this is why high fields are concentrated at sharp points and edges

of conductors: the potential must change rapidly near a sharp tip, producing a large gradient and therefore a large field. Lightning rods exploit this: the very strong field at the tip of the rod ionises the surrounding air and provides a low-resistance discharge path for lightning.

Example — Field from potential

A potential difference of 300 V exists between two

parallel plates 6.0 mm apart. E = ΔV/d = 300/0.006 = 50 000 V/m = 50 kV/m, directed

from the high-potential plate to the low-potential plate. Physics: Foundations of Physical Science Chapter 41 — Electric Fields and Electric Potential

41.13 Applications of Electric Fields and Potential

The electric field and potential framework has applications across all of science and technology. Cathode Ray Tubes (CRT) The first electronic displays (oscilloscopes, early TVs) used cathode ray tubes: an electron gun accelerates electrons through a large potential difference, and deflecting plates (uniform fields) steer the beam. The electron enters the deflecting region at high speed and is deflected sideways by the electric field, tracing a path analogous to projectile motion but in an electric field rather than a gravitational one. Electrocardiograms and Electroencephalograms The electrical activity of the heart (ECG) and brain (EEG) creates potential differences measurable at the skin surface. The heart’s depolarisation wave creates a dipole-like potential pattern that varies with time as the wave propagates through cardiac muscle. Electrodes on the skin measure these potential differences (typically millivolts), and the pattern allows diagnosis of cardiac rhythm, infarction, and conduction abnormalities. Electrostatic Shielding A conductor enclosing a region shields the interior from external electric fields. Because the conductor is an equipotential, any external field terminates on its outer surface; the interior field is zero. This is a Faraday cage: metal mesh or solid enclosures protect sensitive electronics from external electromagnetic interference. The metal body of a car also acts as a Faraday cage — passengers in a car struck by lightning are relatively safe because the charge flows around the outside of the conducting shell. Particle Accelerators All particle accelerators use electric fields to give charged particles kinetic energy. Linacs (linear accelerators) accelerate particles through a series of potential differences. Cyclotrons and synchrotrons use both electric and magnetic fields — the electric field increases the particles’ speed, while the magnetic field bends their path in a circle. The

Large Hadron Collider (LHC) accelerates protons to 6.5 TeV = 6.5 × 10¹² eV by passing

them through millions of volts of potential difference many times around a 27-km ring. Capacitors A capacitor is a device that stores energy in an electric field. The simplest is the parallel -plate capacitor: two conducting plates separated by an insulator. The stored energy is

U = ½CV², where C is the capacitance (a geometric property, Chapter 42). Capacitors in

flash units store the energy needed for a bright brief light pulse. Supercapacitors Physics: Foundations of Physical Science Chapter 41 — Electric Fields and Electric Potential (electrolytic double-layer capacitors) store enough energy for regenerative braking in hybrid vehicles.

41.14 Common Mistakes in Electrostatics Analysis

• Treating electric potential as a vector. Potential is a scalar. To find the potential at a point due to multiple charges, simply add the values algebraically (with signs). This is much simpler than adding electric field vectors, and students sometimes incorrectly try to add potentials as vectors. •

Confusing potential and potential energy. Potential V = U/q is energy per charge;

it is a property of the location in the field. Potential energy U = qV is the energy

of a specific charge q at that location. The same point in space has one value of V but different values of U depending on the charge placed there. •

Getting the sign wrong on work. W = qΔV = q(V_B

V_A). A positive charge

moving from low to high potential gains potential energy (work done against the field is positive). A positive charge moving from high to low potential loses potential energy and gains kinetic energy (the field does positive work). Negative charges behave oppositely. • Forgetting that equipotentials and field lines are perpendicular. When sketching field line diagrams, many students draw field lines and equipotentials at arbitrary angles. They must be perpendicular everywhere. •

Using E = V/d for non-uniform fields. E = V/d applies only to uniform fields

(parallel-plate geometry). For point charges and other geometries, E = kQ/r² and

the field varies with position. •

Ignoring the sign of charges when computing potential. V = kQ/r requires the

sign of Q. A +3 μc charge gives positive V at all surrounding points; a

3 μc

charge gives negative V. When superimposing, keep these signs: V_total = V₁ +

V₂ = k(+3μc)/r₁ + k( 3μc)/r₂.

•

Confusing the two uses of E = V/d and E = F/q. E = F/q defines the field in

terms of force on a test charge. E = V/d computes the magnitude of a uniform

field from the potential difference and plate spacing. Both give E in V/m, but they apply in different situations.

Common Mistake:

The most common electrostatics error: adding electric field vectors when potentials should be added, or vice versa. Use fields (vectors) when finding forces; use potentials (scalars) when working with energy or when superimposing contributions from multiple charges.

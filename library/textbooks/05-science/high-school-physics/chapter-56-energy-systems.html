56.1 What an Energy System Is

An energy system is the complete chain of processes that extracts energy from nature, converts it into useful forms, stores it where needed, transmits it to where it is needed, and delivers it for final use. Energy systems involve primary energy sources (the forms in which energy exists in nature), energy carriers (intermediate forms used to transport or store energy), and end-use technologies (devices that deliver the services users actually want: heat, light, mechanical work, communication). The key physical constraint on all energy systems is the laws of thermodynamics. The first law (energy conservation) requires that energy input equals useful output plus all losses. The second law requires that some energy is always degraded to low-grade heat in any real conversion process; the Carnot efficiency sets the upper bound for thermal machines. No energy system delivers 100% useful output; real systems have cascading losses at every conversion step. Energy is measured in joules (J), but practical energy systems use larger units. 1 kWh

56.2 Primary vs Secondary Energy Sources

Primary energy sources are found in nature before any human conversion. They include fossil fuels (coal, oil, natural gas — stored solar energy from ancient photosynthesis), nuclear fuels (uranium, thorium — nuclear binding energy), and renewable flows (solar radiation, wind, falling water, geothermal heat, tidal energy). Primary energy is characterised by energy density (joules per kilogram or joules per litre) and by whether it is a stock (finite quantity that is depleted) or a flow (continuously replenished). Secondary energy carriers are derived from primary sources through conversion: electricity (from generators driven by thermal, hydraulic, wind, or nuclear power), hydrogen (from electrolysis or steam methane reforming), refined petroleum products (from crude oil), and district heat (from co-generation plants). Secondary carriers are more useful than primary sources — they are portable, controllable, and deliverable through infrastructure networks — but conversion always involves losses. 56.3 Mechanical Energy Systems

The simplest energy systems convert gravitational or kinetic energy to mechanical work directly. Waterwheels, windmills, and sailing ships are ancient examples; their modern descendants are hydroturbines, wind turbines, and wave energy converters.

Mechanical energy systems are governed by conservation of energy: E_k + E_p =

constant (no losses), with real systems losing energy to friction, turbulence, and

material vibration. The power available from a mechanical energy flow is P = Fv (linear

motion) or P = τω (rotational motion), where τ is torque and ω is angular velocity.

Flywheels store rotational kinetic energy: E = ½ Iω², where I is the moment of inertia.

Modern flywheel energy storage systems use high-strength composite rotors spinning at 20 000–60 000 rpm in vacuum enclosures, storing tens of kWh with round-trip efficiencies of 90–95%. They are used for frequency regulation in electrical grids and for uninterruptible power supplies.

56.4 Thermal Power Generation

The majority of the world's electricity is generated by thermal power plants, which convert heat from combustion (fossil fuels, biomass) or nuclear fission into electrical energy via a heat engine driving a generator. The working fluid in most thermal power plants is steam (water vapour), cycled through the Rankine cycle: heating at constant pressure, expansion through a turbine (work output), condensation back to liquid, and pumping back to high pressure.

η_Carnot = 1 −T_C / T_H

A combined cycle gas turbine (CCGT) plant uses a gas turbine (Brayton cycle) at high temperature and recovers waste heat in a steam turbine (Rankine cycle), achieving efficiencies of 55–60%. All thermal power plants must reject waste heat to a cold reservoir — typically a river, lake, ocean, or cooling tower. This waste heat is not a design failure but a thermodynamic necessity. A 1 GW electrical plant at 40% efficiency must reject 1.5 GW

of waste heat (since P_in = P_out + P_waste; P_waste/P_out = (1 η)/η = 1.5 at 40%).

Cooling towers are concrete hyperbolic structures that transfer this heat to the atmosphere by evaporation. The white plume from a cooling tower is water vapour, not pollution.

56.5 Hydroelectric and Fluid Power Systems

Hydroelectric power converts the gravitational potential energy of elevated water into

electrical energy. Water falling through height h releases potential energy E = mgh per

unit mass. A turbine converts this kinetic energy of the water flow into rotational mechanical energy, which drives a generator.

P = η ρ g Q h

Hydroelectric plants range from small run-of-river systems (no significant reservoir, head 2–10 m, power tens of kW) to large impoundment facilities (reservoir stores months of flow, head hundreds of metres, power gigawatts). The Three Gorges Dam in China (22.5 GW capacity) is the world's largest hydroelectric facility, generating about 100 TWh/year — enough to supply roughly 15 million average Chinese households. Pumped-storage hydroelectricity (PSH) is the dominant form of large-scale electrical energy storage worldwide. During periods of excess generation (low demand, high renewable output), electricity pumps water uphill to a reservoir. During peak demand, water is released to generate electricity. Round-trip efficiency is approximately 75–85%. PSH accounts for about 94% of the world's installed grid energy storage capacity. Tidal energy exploits the potential and kinetic energy of ocean tides, which arise from the gravitational forces of the Moon and Sun on the oceans. Tidal barrages (La Rance, France, 240 MW) capture tidal flow through sluice gates and turbines. Tidal stream generators (analogous to underwater wind turbines) extract kinetic energy from tidal currents where flow is fast (e.g., the Pentland Firth, UK, with currents up to 4 m/s).

56.6 Solar Energy Systems

Solar energy reaches Earth's surface at a mean irradiance of about 170 W/m² globally

(averaged over day/night, latitude, and weather). Peak irradiance on a horizontal

surface can reach 1000 W/m² under full sun in the tropics. Two main technologies

convert solar radiation to useful energy: photovoltaics (PV, converting light directly to electricity) and solar thermal (concentrating heat for hot water, space heating, or electricity generation). Photovoltaic cells use the photoelectric effect in semiconductor p-n junctions. Incident

photons with energy E = hf ≥E_gap excite electrons across the band gap, creating

electron-hole pairs that are separated by the built-in electric field of the junction and collected as direct current. Silicon single-crystal cells have band gaps of 1.12 eV; this matches the solar spectrum well, but photons with energy less than 1.12 eV (infrared) are not absorbed, and photons with energy much greater than 1.12 eV (UV) deliver most of their energy as waste heat. These losses account for the theoretical Shockley- Queisser efficiency limit of about 33% for a single-junction silicon cell.

E_{photon} = hf = hc / λ

(minimum for PV absorption:

E_{photon} ≥E_{gap})

The capacity factor of solar PV (fraction of time at peak output) is 10–25% in most locations, determined by solar resource and local weather. The cost of solar PV has fallen by over 90% since 2010, making it the cheapest source of new electricity generation in much of the world as of the mid-2020s. Concentrated solar power (CSP) uses mirrors or lenses to concentrate sunlight onto a receiver, generating high-temperature heat that drives a conventional steam turbine. CSP plants can incorporate thermal storage (molten salt at 390–560°C) that allows electricity generation for hours after sunset. Parabolic trough, power tower (heliostat field), and linear Fresnel designs are the main CSP configurations. Physics: Foundations of Physical Science Chapter 56 — Energy Systems

56.7 Wind Energy Systems

Wind turbines extract kinetic energy from moving air. The power available in a wind

stream passing through area A at speed v is P_wind = ½ρAv³ (kinetic energy flux). No

turbine can extract all of this: the Betz limit (derived from conservation of momentum applied to the actuator disc model) states that the maximum extractable fraction is 16/27 59.3%.

P_{extracted} = ½ ρ A v³ × C_p

(C_p ≤16/27 ≈0.593)

Modern onshore wind turbines have rotor diameters of 100–175 m and rated capacities of 3–6 MW. Offshore wind turbines reach 15–22 MW (rotor diameters up to 280 m). Capacity factors for onshore wind are typically 25–40%; offshore 40–60%. Wind turbines operate between cut-in speed ( 3 m/s, below which the blades do not generate useful power) and cut-out speed ( 25 m/s, above which the turbine is shut down to prevent damage).

56.8 Nuclear Energy Systems

Nuclear fission releases energy when heavy nuclei (uranium-235, plutonium-239) absorb a neutron and split into two smaller nuclei plus 2–3 fast neutrons and energy.

The energy per fission event is about 200 MeV = 3.2 × 10 ¹¹ J. This is about 50 million

times more energy per reaction than chemical combustion; 1 kg of U-235 undergoing complete fission releases the equivalent energy of about 2700 tonnes of coal.

E = Δm c²

⁶ In a nuclear reactor, a controlled chain reaction is maintained. Each fission event

produces 2–3 neutrons; exactly one must on average cause another fission (k = 1,

criticality) for a sustained reaction. Moderators (water, heavy water, graphite) slow fast neutrons to thermal energies where U-235 fission cross-section is much higher. Control rods (boron, hafnium, cadmium) absorb neutrons and allow the reaction rate to be regulated. The heat produced heats coolant (water, heavy water, sodium, helium), which drives a steam turbine through a Rankine cycle. Nuclear power plants produce no CO₂ during operation. Life-cycle emissions (including construction, fuel mining, and decommissioning) are 5–15 gCO₂/kWh, comparable to wind and lower than solar PV. The main challenges are: high capital cost and long construction time, management of spent fuel (high-level radioactive waste with long- lived isotopes), and public concern about safety (following accidents at Three Mile Island 1979, Chernobyl 1986, and Fukushima 2011). Nuclear fusion (Chapter 53) remains a long-term research goal. 56.9 Batteries and Energy Storage

Energy storage is essential for balancing supply and demand in electrical grids (especially with variable renewables), for mobile applications (electric vehicles), and for backup power. Batteries convert chemical energy to electrical energy through electrochemical reactions at two electrodes separated by an electrolyte. The voltage of a battery cell is determined by the electrochemical potential difference between the anode and cathode materials. Energy density (Wh/kg or Wh/L) determines how much energy can be stored per unit mass or volume. Power density (W/kg) determines how fast energy can be delivered. Cycle life is the number of charge- discharge cycles before capacity degrades significantly. 56.10 Electrical Transmission and Distribution

Electrical power is transmitted from generating stations to consumers through a network of transmission lines (high voltage, long distance) and distribution lines (lower

voltage, local). Power loss in a transmission line is P_loss = I²R, where R is the line

resistance and I is the current. For fixed power P = IV, increasing voltage reduces

current in proportion, reducing losses by I².

P_{loss} = I² R = P^2 R / V^2

Transformers (Chapter 48) step voltage up at the generating station (e.g., from 11 kV to 400 kV for long-distance transmission) and step it down progressively for distribution and final use. A typical UK domestic supply is 230 V (single-phase); US is 120/240 V. Industrial supplies are 11 kV or 33 kV. The efficiency of modern power transformers is very high (99.5–99.7%), making them one of the most efficient energy conversion devices.

A power station generates 500 MW and

transmits it through a 400 kV, 300 km line with resistance 0.06 Ω/km (total R = 18 Ω).

High-voltage direct current (HVDC) transmission uses power electronics (rectifiers and inverters) to convert AC to DC for long-distance transmission and back to AC for distribution. HVDC has lower line losses than HVAC for distances over 600 km (overhead) or 100 km (underground/submarine cable), because capacitive charging losses in AC cables are eliminated. HVDC is preferred for long submarine cables (e.g., interconnectors between countries) and for integrating distant renewable resources.

56.11 Efficiency and Energy Losses in Infrastructure

Every stage of an energy system loses some energy, typically as heat. The overall efficiency of a system is the product of the efficiencies of each stage. A typical electricity-to-heat

chain in an old electric resistance heater: power plant (40%) ×

transmission (95%) × distribution (97%) × heater (100%) = 36.9% primary energy to

useful heat. Replacing the resistance heater with a heat pump (COP 3.0) gives: 40% ×

95% × 97% × 300% = 110.7% — more useful heat than the primary energy input to

the power plant, because the heat pump also draws heat from the environment.

η_{system} = η_1 × η_2 × η_3 × …

The coefficient of performance (COP) of a heat pump is the ratio of useful heat output

to electrical work input: COP = Q_H / W. For a Carnot heat pump: COP_max = T_H /

(T_H

T_C). A ground-source heat pump with T_C = 7°C (280 K) and T_H = 35°C (308

K) has COP_max = 308/28 = 11.0; practical COPs are 3–5.

Electric vehicles (EVs) are substantially more energy-efficient than internal combustion engine (ICE) vehicles. An ICE vehicle converts about 20–25% of fuel energy to wheel motion (the rest is heat). An EV converts about 85–90% of battery energy to wheel motion. Even accounting for power-plant losses in generating the electricity, EVs typically deliver 2–3 times more useful mobility per unit of primary energy than ICE vehicles, and 4–6 times more when powered by renewable electricity. 56.12 Grid Stability and Energy Demand

An electrical grid must balance supply and demand instantaneously — electricity cannot easily be stored in large quantities, and if supply exceeds demand, frequency Physics: Foundations of Physical Science Chapter 56 — Energy Systems rises; if demand exceeds supply, frequency falls. Grid frequency (50 Hz in Europe, 60 Hz in North America) is the real-time indicator of this balance. Generators slow down if they are overloaded (demand > supply); they speed up if underloaded (supply > demand). Grid stability is maintained by a hierarchy of responses: primary frequency response (automatic, within seconds, from generator governor systems); secondary frequency control (automatic generation control, minutes timescale); and tertiary control (manual dispatch of reserve generation, tens of minutes). Large inertia (from spinning generator masses) provides the system with time to respond before frequency deviates dangerously. As fossil-fuel synchronous generators are replaced by variable-speed inverter-based renewable generators, traditional rotational inertia is reduced — requiring new approaches such as virtual inertia from grid-scale batteries. Demand variability follows predictable daily patterns (morning and evening peaks), seasonal patterns (winter heating demand, summer cooling demand), and unpredictable short-term fluctuations (e.g., large simultaneous kettle use at the end of a popular TV show — a real phenomenon that UK grid operators must plan for). Demand response programmes incentivise large electricity consumers to shift or curtail usage during peak demand periods. Integrating large amounts of variable renewable generation (wind and solar) increases the challenge of balancing because their output depends on weather. Solutions include: flexible backup generation (gas peakers, hydro), demand response, energy storage (batteries, pumped hydro, hydrogen), and interconnection with neighbouring grids so surplus renewable generation in one region can serve demand in another. Long- duration energy storage (8–100 hours) is an active area of technology development.

Key Point: The term "baseload" — generation that runs continuously at steady output

— becomes less meaningful in a grid with high renewable penetration. What matters is flexibility: the ability to ramp generation up and down quickly in response to changes in renewable output and demand. Physics determines the ramp rates: hydro and gas can ramp in seconds to minutes; coal plants take tens of minutes to hours.

Common Mistake:

Never use efficiency without specifying the input and output energy forms. Thermal efficiency, electrical efficiency, exergy efficiency, and round-trip efficiency all measure different things and can give very different numbers for the same system. Always state: efficiency of converting what, to what, under what conditions.

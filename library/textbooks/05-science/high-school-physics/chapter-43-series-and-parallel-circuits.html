Chapter 43: Series and Parallel Circuits

43.1 Why Circuit Arrangement Matters

A circuit is not just a list of components — it is a system in which the arrangement of those components determines how current flows and how voltage distributes. The same three resistors wired in series, in parallel, or in a combination behave as completely different circuits. The physical laws governing each arrangement follow directly from two fundamental principles: charge cannot accumulate at a junction (conservation of charge, giving Kirchhoff's Current Law), and energy must be conserved around any closed loop (conservation of energy, giving Kirchhoff's Voltage Law). Recognising the arrangement — series, parallel, or mixed — is always the first step in circuit analysis. Once you identify the configuration, a small set of rules gives you all the currents and voltages.

43.3 Series Circuits

Components are connected in series when they are joined end-to-end so that the only path for current is through each component in succession. There is exactly one current loop; no branching occurs. If any component in a series circuit is removed or fails open -circuit, the entire circuit breaks and no current flows anywhere. The defining characteristic of a series circuit is that there is only one path for current. Because charge cannot accumulate at any point, the same current must flow through every component. This single-path property is the key to all series circuit rules.

Key Point: A series circuit has exactly one path for current. All components share the

same current. Remove any one component and the circuit is broken. Physics: Foundations of Physical Science Chapter 43 — Series and Parallel Circuits

43.4 Current in Series Circuits

In a series circuit, the current is the same through every component. This follows directly from conservation of charge: charge does not accumulate at any junction, and since there are no junctions in a series circuit, the current that enters one component must exit the other side and immediately enter the next.

I₁ = I₂ = I₃ = … = I

(series)

Example — Series current

Three resistors (100 Ω, 220 Ω, 330 Ω) are connected in

series to a 12 V battery. A current meter in any part of the circuit reads the same value.

If I = 18.5 mA, then I through each resistor = 18.5 mA. (The calculation of I comes

from the equivalent resistance, covered in section 43.6.)

43.5 Voltage in Series Circuits

In a series circuit, the total voltage supplied by the source is distributed among the components. Each component has a voltage drop (also called potential difference)

across it, determined by Ohm's Law: V = IR. The sum of all these voltage drops equals

the source voltage.

V_source = V₁ + V₂ + V₃ + …

Each resistor "uses up" a portion of the available voltage proportional to its resistance. A larger resistor has a larger voltage drop across it (for the same current). The voltage drops add up to the full supply voltage — none is lost and none is created.

Example — Voltage distribution

A 12 V battery drives a series circuit with R₁ = 100

Ω, R₂ = 200 Ω. Total R = 300 Ω. I = 12/300 = 0.040 A. V₁ = IR₁ = 0.040 × 100 = 4.0 V.

V₂ = IR₂ = 0.040 × 200 = 8.0 V. Check: 4.0 + 8.0 = 12 V = V_source. ✓The larger

resistor has the larger share of the voltage. Physics: Foundations of Physical Science Chapter 43 — Series and Parallel Circuits

43.6 Equivalent Resistance in Series

A set of series resistors can be replaced by a single equivalent resistor (R_eq) that

draws the same current from the source and has the same voltage across it. The equivalent resistance of series resistors is simply the sum of their individual resistances.

R_eq = R₁ + R₂ + R₃ + …

Physically, connecting resistors in series is like extending the length of a wire — each resistor adds more length of obstructing material. More length means more resistance

(recall R = ρL/A).

Example — Equivalent resistance Four resistors: 47 Ω, 100 Ω, 150 Ω, 220 Ω in series.

R_eq = 47 + 100 + 150 + 220 = 517 Ω. If the source voltage is 24 V: I = 24/517 =

0.0464 A = 46.4 mA. This single current flows through all four resistors.

Study Tip:

Check: R_eq for series must always be larger than the largest individual

resistor. If your calculated R_eq is smaller than any single resistor in the group, you

have made an error.

43.7 Parallel Circuits

Components are connected in parallel when both terminals of each component are connected to the same two nodes. This creates multiple paths for current. Each component has a direct connection to both the positive and negative terminals of the source (or the same two nodes in a more complex circuit). Removing one parallel component leaves the others unaffected — they still have their direct connections and current still flows through them. The defining characteristic of a parallel circuit is that all components share the same two endpoints. As a result, they all experience the same voltage across their terminals. If any component is removed, the others continue operating at the same voltage.

Key Point:

A parallel circuit has multiple paths for current. All components share the same voltage. Remove any one component and the others are unaffected. Physics: Foundations of Physical Science Chapter 43 — Series and Parallel Circuits

43.8 Voltage in Parallel Circuits

In a parallel circuit, every component has the same voltage across it, equal to the voltage of the source (assuming ideal connecting wires with negligible resistance).

V₁ = V₂ = V₃ = … = V_source

(parallel) This is why household devices are wired in parallel: each device receives the full mains voltage (120 V or 230 V) regardless of what other devices are connected. A lamp, a television, and a heater plugged into the same circuit each receive the same voltage and operate independently.

43.9 Current in Parallel Circuits

In a parallel circuit, the total current from the source splits among the parallel branches.

Each branch carries a current determined by Ohm's Law and its own resistance: I = V/R.

The total current from the source is the sum of the individual branch currents.

I_total = I₁ + I₂ + I₃ + …

A lower-resistance branch carries more current (because I = V/R and V is the same for

all branches). Adding more parallel branches provides more paths for current, so the total current increases and the effective resistance decreases.

Example — Parallel current distribution

Two resistors, R₁ = 60 Ω and R₂ = 120 Ω,

are connected in parallel across a 12 V source. I₁ = 12/60 = 0.20 A. I₂ = 12/120 = 0.10

A. I_total = 0.20 + 0.10 = 0.30 A. Note: R₁ carries twice the current of R₂ because it

has half the resistance, yet both are connected to the same 12 V. Physics: Foundations of Physical Science Chapter 43 — Series and Parallel Circuits

43.10 Equivalent Resistance in Parallel

Parallel resistors can be replaced by a single equivalent resistor (R_eq) that draws the

same total current from the source. The reciprocal of the equivalent resistance equals the sum of the reciprocals of the individual resistances.

1/R_eq = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ + 1/R₃ + …

Adding more resistors in parallel always decreases R_eq. Physically, each new parallel

branch provides an additional path for current — like adding more lanes to a highway, which increases total flow and decreases effective resistance.

Example — Equivalent resistance (two resistors)

R₁ = 60 Ω, R₂ = 120 Ω in parallel.

R_eq = (60 × 120)/(60 + 120) = 7200/180 = 40 Ω. Check: 40 Ω is less than both 60 Ω

and 120 Ω. ✓Source current = 12/40 = 0.30 A, confirming the branch current sum

from section 43.9.

43.11 Combined Series-Parallel Circuits

Most real circuits are neither purely series nor purely parallel. They contain groups of series-connected components and groups of parallel-connected components combined together. The strategy is to reduce the circuit step by step, simplifying the most nested sub-circuits first, until a single equivalent resistance remains.

Step 1: Identify:

Find groups of resistors that are clearly in series or clearly in parallel with each other. Start with the most nested (innermost) group.

Step 2: Simplify:

Replace each identified group with its equivalent resistance using the appropriate formula.

Step 3: Repeat:

Redraw the simplified circuit. Identify the next group to simplify. Repeat until one equivalent resistor remains.

Step 4: Find I and V: With R_eq and the source voltage known, find the total current:

I = V/R_eq. Then work backwards through the circuit, applying Kirchhoff's rules at each

junction and Ohm's Law across each component to find individual currents and voltages.

43.13 Practical Circuit Design Considerations

Real circuit design uses series and parallel arrangements for specific purposes. Understanding the physics behind each motivates the engineering decisions. Voltage Dividers (Series) Two resistors in series with a power supply form a voltage divider. The output voltage is taken from the junction between the two resistors. If R₁ and R₂ are in series with

supply voltage V_s, the voltage across R₂ is: V_out = V_s × R₂/(R₁ + R₂). This

relationship allows precise output voltages to be produced from a fixed supply. Fuses and Circuit Breakers (Series) Fuses and circuit breakers are always placed in series with the circuit they protect. When current exceeds the fuse rating, the fuse element melts (or the breaker trips), opening the circuit. Because fuses are in series with the load, opening the fuse stops current through the entire load. A fuse in parallel would not work — it would simply provide a low-resistance bypass and bypass current would increase when the fuse blew. Household Wiring (Parallel) All household outlets are wired in parallel from the main distribution panel. This ensures each appliance receives the full mains voltage (120 V or 230 V) regardless of how many others are plugged in. It also allows individual control: unplugging one device does not affect others. The tradeoff is that adding more parallel loads increases total current from the source, which is why circuit breakers have current ratings.

Historical Note:

Thomas Edison's original DC power distribution (1882) and Nikola Tesla's AC distribution (1888–1893) both used parallel connections to supply customers with a standard voltage. The "War of Currents" was not about series vs. parallel but about DC vs. AC — both systems connected loads in parallel to maintain a constant voltage for each customer. 43.14 Common Mistakes in Circuit Analysis

•

Adding resistances in parallel instead of using 1/R_eq = Σ 1/R. Parallel

resistances combine as reciprocals, not directly. The result must be smaller than the smallest individual resistor. • Assuming current is the same everywhere in a parallel circuit. Current splits in parallel circuits. Only voltage is the same for all parallel branches. Current equality applies only in series circuits. • Assuming voltage splits in a parallel circuit. All parallel branches share the same voltage. Only in series circuits does voltage split across components. • Forgetting to take the reciprocal at the end of the parallel formula. After

computing 1/R₁ + 1/R₂, many students forget to invert the sum. R_eq = 1 / (1/R₁

+ 1/R₂), not 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ itself. • Misidentifying series vs. parallel in a combined circuit. Two components are in series only if the same current must pass through both. Two components are in parallel only if both terminals of one are connected to both terminals of the other. Redraw the circuit if necessary to clarify the topology. • Not checking the answer with Kirchhoff's laws. Voltage drops around any loop must sum to zero (when accounting for the source). Current into any junction must equal current out. These checks catch arithmetic errors before they propagate.

Common Mistake:

When working through a combined circuit, always redraw the circuit after each simplification step. Drawing it out prevents mistakes that come from trying to track the simplification mentally. Chapter Summary

Series and parallel are the two fundamental circuit arrangements. All multi-component circuits are built from combinations of these two configurations. •

Series: one path; I same everywhere; V splits (V_T = ΣV); R_eq = ΣR (larger than

any individual R). •

Parallel: multiple paths; V same everywhere; I splits (I_T = ΣI); 1/R_eq = Σ(1/R)

(smaller than any individual R). • Kirchhoff's Voltage Law: sum of voltage drops around any closed loop equals the source voltage. • Kirchhoff's Current Law: sum of currents entering any junction equals sum leaving. • Combined circuits: reduce innermost sub-circuits first; work outward to a single

R_eq; then work backwards to find individual V and I.

• Applications: series for voltage dividers, fuses, and switches; parallel for household wiring and independent loads. • Always verify answers using KVL and KCL checks. Physics: Foundations of Physical Science Chapter 43 — Series and Parallel Circuits

Key Terms

series circuit
Circuit arrangement with a single current path; all components carry the

same current; voltages add; R_eq = sum of individual R values.

parallel circuit
Circuit arrangement where both terminals of each component share

the same two nodes; all components have the same voltage; currents add; 1/R_eq =

sum of 1/R.

equivalent resistance (R_eq)
A single resistance that produces the same total current

from the source as the entire combination it replaces.

Kirchhoff's Voltage Law (KVL)
The sum of all voltage drops around any closed loop

equals the source voltage (equivalently, net voltage change around any closed loop is zero).

Kirchhoff's Current Law (KCL)
The sum of currents entering any junction equals the

sum of currents leaving that junction; a consequence of conservation of charge.

voltage divider
Two resistors in series used to produce an output voltage that is a
fraction of the supply voltage

node (junction)
A point in a circuit where two or more components connect. In

parallel circuits, all components share the same two nodes.

branch
A single path through a component or group of components in a parallel

open circuit

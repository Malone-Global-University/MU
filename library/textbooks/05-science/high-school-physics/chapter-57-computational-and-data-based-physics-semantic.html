57.1 Why Physics Uses Computation

The equations of physics are often simple to write but impossible to solve by hand for

realistic systems. Newton's law F = ma is three lines of algebra for one object. For ten

objects interacting with each other gravitationally, it becomes ten coupled differential equations with thirty unknowns that have no closed-form solution — the famous n-

body problem. The atmosphere of Earth contains roughly 10²⁵ molecules, each obeying

the same gas laws studied in Chapter 32, but predicting tomorrow's weather from first principles requires tracking their collective behaviour through billions of coupled interactions. Computation enters wherever the gap between the known laws and the tractable solution is too wide to cross by algebra alone. Three categories of problem drive this need. The first is complexity: systems with too many interacting parts (weather, protein folding, galaxy formation, financial markets). The second is geometry: domains with irregular shapes, boundaries, or internal structure that cannot be reduced to a symmetric idealisation (blood flow through the branching arteries of a specific patient's heart; acoustic vibrations of a concert hall). The third is extreme scale: processes too fast, too small, too large, or too energetic to study by direct observation, from femtosecond laser pulses to the evolution of the universe over billions of years. Computation also plays a role in the other direction: analysing data that experiments produce. Modern detectors generate millions of measurements per second. A particle physics experiment at CERN records petabytes of collision data annually. A genomics laboratory sequences billions of base pairs. A gravitational wave observatory monitors laser interference fringes at kilohertz frequencies. In every case, the physical signal of interest is buried in noise, and extracting it requires systematic, automated data analysis rather than manual inspection.

Key Point:

Computation does not replace physical understanding — it amplifies it. A simulation is only as meaningful as the physical model it implements. Garbage in, garbage out: a simulation based on wrong assumptions produces wrong predictions, often presented with misleading precision. Understanding what a model assumes is as important as knowing how to run it. Physics: Foundations of Physical Science Chapter 57 — Computational and Data-Based Physics

57.2 Models, Inputs, Outputs, and Assumptions

Every computational physics problem begins with a model: a mathematical description of the physical system, specifying which laws apply, which variables are tracked, and which interactions are included. The model is always a simplification of reality — the question is whether the simplification is appropriate for the question being asked. A model has four components. Inputs are the quantities that describe the initial or boundary conditions of the system: positions and velocities of objects, temperature and pressure at the boundaries, material properties. Equations are the physical laws (Newton's laws, Maxwell's equations, thermodynamic relations) written in a form the computer can solve numerically. Outputs are the computed quantities that answer the question: final positions, temperatures, pressures, energies, images. Assumptions are the simplifications made to make the model tractable: treating air as an ideal gas, ignoring radiation losses, assuming surfaces are perfectly smooth, treating Earth as a perfect sphere. A sensor produces an analogue electrical signal (a continuously varying voltage or current) proportional to the physical quantity. An analogue-to-digital converter (ADC) samples this signal at regular intervals and converts each sample to a binary number. The sampling rate (samples per second) determines the highest frequency the system Physics: Foundations of Physical Science Chapter 57 — Computational and Data-Based Physics can faithfully record (Nyquist theorem: sampling rate must be at least twice the signal frequency). The bit depth (number of bits per sample) determines the precision of each measurement: 8-bit resolution gives 256 discrete levels; 16-bit gives 65 536 levels. Sensor calibration — establishing the relationship between the sensor output and the true physical value — is essential before any measurement is meaningful. A thermistor may produce 2.3 V when immersed in 25°C water, but this number means nothing without a calibration curve that maps volts to degrees. Calibration is performed by exposing the sensor to known reference values (e.g., ice water at 0°C, boiling water at 100°C) and recording the corresponding outputs.

57.4 Tables, Graphs, and Digital Measurements

Raw data from an experiment is a table of numbers: values of the independent variable (usually time, position, or temperature) and the corresponding values of one or more dependent variables. The table is the primary record of the experiment. Before any analysis, data should be inspected for outliers (values far from the expected range that may indicate sensor failure, transcription errors, or genuine anomalies), missing values, and units. Graphs are the most powerful tool for initial data analysis. Plotting data before fitting any model allows the experimenter to see the overall shape of the relationship, identify whether it is linear or curved, spot outliers, and check whether scatter is uniform or systematic. A graph that looks like a straight line suggests a linear relationship; a curve suggests power-law or exponential behaviour, which can be identified by plotting on logarithmic axes. 57.5 Numerical Models

A numerical model replaces an exact mathematical solution with a sequence of approximate calculations performed step by step. The core idea is discretisation: instead of solving a differential equation for a continuous function, we approximate it by computing the function at a finite set of discrete points, stepping forward in small increments. The simplest numerical method for solving equations of motion is Euler's method. Given the state of a system at time t (position x, velocity v), we approximate the state

at time t + Δt using:

v(t + Δt) ≈v(t) + a(t) × Δt

x(t + Δt) ≈x(t) + v(t) × Δt

The process is then repeated: use the new v and x to compute the force at the new time, compute the next step, and so on. Each step introduces a small error; these errors accumulate over time, so the accuracy of Euler's method degrades for long simulations. More accurate methods (Runge-Kutta, Verlet, leapfrog) reduce the per-step error and are preferred in practice, but Euler's method captures the essential idea.

57.6 Simulations of Motion and Forces

Orbital mechanics provides one of the clearest examples of computational physics. For two bodies (Earth and Moon, or Earth and Sun), the equations of motion have exact analytical solutions — Kepler's ellipses. For three or more bodies, no general analytical solution exists. The trajectories of spacecraft, the long-term stability of the solar system, and the orbital evolution of binary star systems all require numerical simulation. NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory uses numerical integrations of the equations of motion for all solar system bodies, updated with radar ranging data, to compute spacecraft trajectories with metre-level precision at distances of billions of kilometres. The Voyager 1 probe, launched in 1977, is now beyond the solar system — its trajectory was computed numerically decades in advance. Collision and impact simulations use numerical methods to model what happens when objects collide at high speed: the deformation of metal in a car crash, the formation of craters when meteorites strike planets, the behaviour of protective armour under projectile impact. These simulations use finite element analysis (FEA), which divides the object into millions of tiny elements, each governed by stress-strain laws, and tracks the deformation of every element through time. Molecular dynamics (MD) simulation models matter at the atomic level. Each atom is treated as a classical particle subject to forces from neighbouring atoms (described by empirical potential energy functions). Integrating the equations of motion for systems of millions of atoms over nanoseconds reveals how proteins fold, how materials fracture, how drug molecules bind to receptors, and how heat conducts through semiconductors. MD is computationally intensive: a simulation of one microsecond of a protein with 100 000 atoms may require weeks of supercomputer time.

Historical Note:

The first computer simulation in physics was performed in 1953 at Los Alamos National Laboratory by Enrico Fermi, John Pasta, Stanislaw Ulam, and Mary Tsingou. They simulated the vibrations of a one-dimensional chain of particles coupled by nonlinear springs, expecting energy to distribute evenly across all vibrational modes as classical thermodynamics predicted. Instead, the energy kept returning to the initial mode — the Fermi-Pasta-Ulam-Tsingou recurrence problem, which remained unexplained for over a decade and eventually led to the discovery of solitons and the foundations of chaos theory. 57.7 Simulations of Fluids, Heat, and Weather

Fluid dynamics simulations solve the Navier-Stokes equations — the governing equations of fluid motion derived from Newton's second law applied to fluid parcels — on a three-dimensional grid. The fluid domain is divided into a mesh of small cells; velocity, pressure, temperature, and density are computed at each cell centre and updated at each time step. This approach is called computational fluid dynamics (CFD). CFD is now essential in aerospace engineering. Every major commercial aircraft is extensively simulated before physical prototypes are built, reducing the number of wind tunnel tests required. The aerodynamic drag and lift of the entire aircraft, the flow over engine inlets, and the thermal loads on turbine blades are all computed via CFD. The same methods are used to design Formula 1 racing cars, optimise wind turbine blade shapes, simulate blood flow through cardiac prostheses, and predict the dispersal of pollutants in urban air. Heat transfer simulations solve the heat equation — a partial differential equation describing how temperature diffuses through a material over time — on a spatial grid. Applications include: designing the thermal management systems of computer chips (which must remove heat generated by billions of transistors switching billions of times per second), modelling the heat shield of a spacecraft re-entering the atmosphere, and predicting the temperature distribution in a nuclear reactor core. Numerical weather prediction (NWP) is one of the most computationally intensive applications of physics. A global weather model divides the atmosphere into a three- dimensional grid (horizontal resolution now reaching 10 km; vertical resolution of tens to hundreds of metres in layers) and solves the coupled equations of fluid dynamics, thermodynamics, and radiation transfer at each grid point for each time step. A 10-day

global forecast requires roughly 10¹³ floating-point operations and runs on the world's

most powerful supercomputers in about an hour. Without this computation, done twice daily, modern weather forecasts would be impossible. Climate models extend weather models to run for decades or centuries, but with coarser resolution and simplified representations of processes too small to resolve (clouds, ocean eddies, sea ice dynamics). Climate projections — such as those produced for the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) — are the product of dozens of independent models from research groups worldwide, whose agreement (or disagreement) on specific outcomes provides a measure of confidence in the predictions.

57.8 Data Analysis in Experiments

After data is collected, the goal of data analysis is to extract physical meaning from numbers. This involves several stages: cleaning (removing bad data points), exploration (graphing to understand the shape of the data), fitting (finding the parameters of a model that best match the data), and inference (drawing conclusions about physical quantities, with stated uncertainties). 57.9 Error, Uncertainty, and Noise in Data

Every measurement contains uncertainty. This is not a failure of equipment or experimenter; it is an unavoidable feature of the physical world (at the quantum level) and of the limitations of any finite measurement apparatus. Understanding and quantifying uncertainty is essential for interpreting data correctly. Random error (noise) is unpredictable variation in repeated measurements of the same quantity. It arises from thermal fluctuations in electronics, vibrations in the environment, photon shot noise in optical measurements, and countless other sources. Random error can be reduced by averaging many measurements: if the noise in each measurement

has standard deviation σ, the standard error on the mean of n measurements is σ/√n.

Doubling the number of measurements halves the standard error. Systematic error is a consistent bias that shifts all measurements in the same direction. A miscalibrated thermometer that reads 1°C too high introduces a systematic error that averaging cannot remove. Systematic errors are eliminated by careful calibration, cross- checking with independent methods, and blind analysis (analysing data without knowing which measurements come from which condition, to avoid confirmation bias).

57.10 Pattern Recognition and Prediction

One of the most powerful roles of computation in modern physics is finding patterns in large datasets that no human analyst could detect by inspection. This includes spectral analysis (finding periodic signals in noisy time series), clustering (grouping similar objects in a multidimensional dataset), classification (labelling objects as belonging to one of several known categories), and anomaly detection (identifying unusual events that do not match the expected pattern). Spectral analysis — specifically, the Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) — decomposes a time- varying signal into its constituent frequencies. Given a signal measured over time, the FFT produces a spectrum showing how much power is present at each frequency. Periodic signals appear as sharp peaks; noise appears as a broad, flat baseline. The FFT is used in audio engineering, seismology, medical imaging (MRI, ultrasound), communications, and mechanical vibration analysis. Machine learning (ML) methods train algorithms to recognise patterns by exposure to labelled examples. In physics, ML is used to: classify particle tracks in detectors (identify electrons from muons from pions from background noise); identify gravitational wave signals in LIGO data; find exoplanets in telescope photometry data (the Kepler mission used ML to identify planetary transits — brief dips in starlight as a planet crosses the star's face); and accelerate simulations by replacing expensive physical computations with fast neural network approximations. Predictive modelling uses a fitted model to forecast values at inputs not yet observed. This is only valid when the model correctly captures the underlying physics and is used within its range of validity. Weather forecasting is predictive modelling: the model is fitted (calibrated) against historical observations and then run forward in time. A critical concept is extrapolation risk: using a model beyond the range of data it was fitted to. A linear fit to data from 0–10°C that is extrapolated to 1000°C will give physically meaningless results if the relationship is not truly linear over that range.

Study Tip:

When you see a graph of data with a fitted curve, always ask: (1) What physical model does the curve represent? (2) Is the fit good (are residuals random and small)? (3) Is the curve being extrapolated beyond the data range? (4) What are the uncertainties on the fitted parameters? A curve that passes through all data points and has no stated uncertainties is as likely to be over-fitted noise as a genuine physical law. 57.11 Computational Physics in Engineering, Medicine, and Astronomy

The applications of computational physics span every domain of modern science and technology. A survey of three fields illustrates the breadth. Engineering Structural engineering uses finite element analysis to compute stresses and deformations in bridges, buildings, aircraft, and dams under load. The 1940 collapse of the Tacoma Narrows Bridge in Washington State (driven into oscillation by aerodynamic forces) would now be detected in CFD simulations before construction. Every modern skyscraper is modelled for wind loads, seismic response, and thermal expansion. The Boeing 787 Dreamliner was designed almost entirely in simulation before any physical prototype was built, saving an estimated billions of dollars in development costs. Semiconductor device simulation models the quantum mechanical behaviour of electrons in transistors, enabling chip designers to predict whether new transistor architectures will function before fabrication. Modern transistors are a few nanometres in size — so small that quantum tunnelling effects must be included in the model. Without these simulations, the rapid progress of computing power described by Moore's Law would have stalled decades ago. Medicine Medical imaging relies on computational physics at every stage. X-ray computed tomography (CT) takes hundreds of two-dimensional projections of the body from different angles, then uses the Radon transform (an inverse problem solved computationally) to reconstruct a three-dimensional density map. MRI uses the Fourier transform to convert raw frequency-space data (k-space) into anatomical images. Ultrasound imaging uses beamforming algorithms to construct images from arrays of piezoelectric elements. None of these images would exist without the computational steps that convert raw detector data into interpretable pictures. Radiation therapy treatment planning uses computational models of how X-ray beams deposit energy in tissue to optimise the directions and intensities of beams from many angles, maximising dose to a tumour while minimising dose to surrounding healthy tissue. Monte Carlo simulation — using random sampling to model the probabilistic trajectories of millions of individual photons through tissue — is the gold standard for dose calculation. Astronomy and Cosmology N-body simulation models the gravitational interaction of thousands to billions of particles, tracking the formation of galaxies, galaxy clusters, and the large-scale structure of the universe. The Millennium Simulation (2005) followed 10 billion particles of dark matter through 13 billion years of cosmic evolution, producing a synthetic universe whose statistical properties match the observed distribution of galaxies. The IllustrisTNG project (2018) extended this to include gas dynamics, star formation, and black hole feedback. Gravitational wave astronomy, opened by LIGO's first detection in 2015, is entirely computational. The waveforms of gravitational waves from merging black holes and neutron stars are computed by solving Einstein's equations of general relativity numerically (numerical relativity) — a problem that required decades of algorithm development before the first stable simulations were achieved in 2005. Detecting the signal requires matched filtering: correlating the observed data with a bank of millions of pre-computed waveform templates to find the best match. 57.12 Limits and Risks of Computer Models

Computational models are tools, not oracles. They are only as good as the physics they encode, the data they are given, and the assumptions they make. Uncritical trust in model outputs — sometimes called model worship or garbage-in-garbage-out blindness — is one of the most dangerous failure modes in applied science and engineering. Sensitivity to initial conditions is a fundamental limit. In chaotic systems — systems governed by the same deterministic equations but exhibiting exponential sensitivity to starting conditions — even infinitesimally different inputs produce completely different outputs after sufficient time. The atmosphere is a chaotic system: this is the physical basis of the two-week predictability horizon for weather forecasting. No amount of computational power or measurement precision can extend reliable daily weather forecasts much beyond about two weeks because the unavoidable uncertainty in initial conditions amplifies exponentially. Numerical errors accumulate. Every arithmetic operation in a computer is performed with finite precision (floating-point representation). Small rounding errors in each step accumulate over millions of steps. For well-designed algorithms and appropriate step sizes, these errors are controllable; for poorly designed simulations or too-large step sizes, numerical errors can dominate the output and produce physically meaningless results that look superficially plausible. Unresolved scales: a simulation grid cannot resolve features smaller than the grid spacing. Atmospheric models with 10 km horizontal resolution cannot directly simulate individual thunderstorms (which are 1–10 km across) and must instead use parameterisations — statistical representations of the average effect of many unresolved storms. If the parameterisation is wrong, the large-scale simulation is wrong. This is the main source of uncertainty in climate projections. Model overfitting is a risk whenever a model has more free parameters than the data can constrain. An overfit model describes the noise in the training data perfectly but fails to predict new data. The antidote is cross-validation: fitting the model on a portion of the data and testing its predictions on the remainder.

Common Mistake: A simulation result is not experimental evidence. A simulation that

reproduces one set of observations does not prove the model is correct — it only shows that the model is consistent with those observations. The same data may be consistent with multiple models based on different physical assumptions. Independent experimental validation, ideally by a different group with different apparatus, is required before a model should be trusted for high-stakes predictions.

57.13 Common Mistakes in Data-Based Physics

• Plotting data without first inspecting it for errors. Outliers, sign errors, unit inconsistencies, and instrument glitches produce obviously wrong data points that should be identified and investigated before fitting any model. • Fitting a linear model to non-linear data. If the data has a visible curve, a

straight-line fit gives wrong slope and intercept values and misleading R². Always

check residuals: a curved residual plot indicates a non-linear relationship. • Confusing correlation with causation. A computed correlation coefficient tells you that two quantities vary together; it says nothing about which causes which, or whether both are caused by a third factor. Correlation is a mathematical property of the data; causation requires a physical mechanism. • Ignoring uncertainty. Reporting a fitted value without its uncertainty (e.g., g =

9.81 m/s² with no stated uncertainty) is incomplete. A measurement of g = 9.81

± 0.05 m/s² and g = 9.81 ± 2.0 m/s² have very different physical meaning, even

though the central values are identical. • Extrapolating beyond the data range. A model fitted to data from 0–50°C should not be used to predict values at 200°C unless there is independent physical justification that the same relationship holds. The validity of the model is only established within the range of the data. • Using too-large a time step in numerical simulations. Euler's method with a large

Δt introduces large errors per step that accumulate rapidly. As a check, run the

simulation with half the time step: if the results change significantly, the original step size was too large. • Treating simulation output as ground truth. A simulation result is only as reliable as the model that produced it. Comparing simulation outputs to independent experimental data is always required before using a simulation for prediction or decision-making. • Omitting units from graphs and tables. Every axis label, every column header, every reported result must include the SI unit (or clearly stated non-SI unit). A table of temperatures with no unit could mean anything from Kelvin to Fahrenheit; a plot of "current" with no unit tells the reader nothing.

Common Mistake:

The most dangerous outputs of computational physics are those that look precise but are based on an invalid model or bad data. Computers report numbers to many decimal places regardless of whether those decimal places are meaningful. Numerical precision (how many digits the computer calculates) is entirely separate from physical accuracy (how close the result is to reality). Chapter Summary

Computational physics extends physical reasoning into domains of complexity, scale, and precision that are beyond analytical or manual methods. It encompasses numerical modelling, simulation, data collection, and data analysis. • Models specify inputs, equations, outputs, and assumptions. Validation (does the model match reality?) and verification (is the code correct?) are both essential. • Sensors convert physical quantities to digital signals. Calibration, sampling rate, and bit depth determine measurement quality. • Graphing and linearisation are the first steps of data analysis. Residuals reveal whether a model is appropriate. • Euler's method approximates equations of motion by stepping forward in small

time increments Δt. Accuracy increases as Δt decreases.

• Simulations of motion (orbital mechanics, FEA, molecular dynamics), fluids (CFD), heat (heat equation), and weather (NWP) all use discretised differential equations solved on grids or for particle ensembles. •

Least-squares regression finds the best-fit model parameters. R² and residual

plots assess fit quality. •

Random error is reduced by averaging (uncertainty ∝1/√n). Systematic error

requires calibration and experimental design. • Pattern recognition (FFT, machine learning, matched filtering) extracts signals from noisy data at scales impossible for human inspection. • Limits of computational models: chaos limits weather forecasting; numerical errors accumulate; unresolved scales require parameterisation; overfitting risks extrapolation failure. • Simulation output is not evidence. Independent experimental validation is always required.

(model explains none of the variance) to 1 (perfect fit). R² > 0.99 indicates excellent

linear fit.

random error
Unpredictable, zero-mean variation in repeated measurements arising

from noise; reduced by averaging many measurements (uncertainty ∝1/√n).

systematic error
A consistent bias affecting all measurements in the same direction;

not reduced by averaging; eliminated by calibration and careful experimental design.

signal-to-noise ratio (SNR)
The ratio of signal amplitude to noise amplitude; higher

SNR means the signal is more easily detected and measured.

Fast Fourier Transform (FFT)
An efficient algorithm for decomposing a time-domain

signal into its constituent frequency components; fundamental to spectral analysis.

overfitting
The condition in which a model has too many free parameters relative to

the data, causing it to fit noise rather than physical signal; identified by poor performance on new data.

extrapolation
Using a fitted model to predict values outside the range of data used to

fit it; inherently risky unless the physical model is known to hold beyond the data range.

matched filtering
A signal processing technique that detects a known waveform in

noisy data by correlating the data with a template; used in gravitational wave detection and communications.

Monte Carlo simulation
A computational method using random sampling to model

probabilistic physical processes; used in radiation dose calculation, statistical mechanics, and financial modelling.

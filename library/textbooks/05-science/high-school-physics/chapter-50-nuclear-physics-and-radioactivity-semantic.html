50.1 Inside the Atomic Nucleus

The nucleus is the dense, positively charged core of the atom, occupying a sphere of

radius approximately r = r_0 A^{1/3}, where r_0

1.2 × 10 ¹⁵ m and A is the mass

number. For a typical nucleus (A

50), the radius is about 4.5 × 10 ¹⁵ m = 4.5 fm

(femtometres). This is roughly 100,000 times smaller than the atom itself. Nuclear matter is extraordinarily dense. If the nucleus of a hydrogen atom were scaled up to the size of a marble (1 cm), the atom would be 1 km across. The density of

nuclear matter is approximately 2.3 × 10¹⁷ kg/m³ — about 230 million tonnes per cubic

centimetre. A teaspoon of nuclear material would weigh more than a mountain. Despite this density, nuclei do not simply crush together under gravity or fly apart under electrostatic repulsion. They are held together by the strong nuclear force, one of the four fundamental forces of nature. Understanding this force, and why some nuclei are stable while others are not, is the central problem of nuclear physics. 50.2 Protons, Neutrons, and Nuclear Forces

The nucleus contains protons (charge +e, mass 1.6726 × 10 ²⁷ kg

1.007 u) and

neutrons (no charge, mass 1.6749 × 10 ²⁷ kg

1.009 u), collectively called nucleons. The atomic mass unit u is defined as 1/12 of the mass of a carbon-12 atom: 1 u =

1.6605 × 10 ²⁷ kg.

Protons repel each other through the electromagnetic force (like charges repel). In a large nucleus, where many protons are packed together, this repulsion is enormous. Yet nuclei hold together. The explanation is the strong nuclear force: an attractive force between all nucleons (proton-proton, neutron-proton, and neutron-neutron) that is much stronger than the electromagnetic force at short distances (< 2–3 fm) but falls off extremely rapidly and becomes negligible beyond about 3 fm. The strong force acts equally between all nucleon pairs (charge-blind) and dominates at nuclear distances, easily overcoming electromagnetic repulsion. But because it has such a short range, each nucleon can only "feel" its nearest neighbours. For large nuclei (many protons), the long-range electromagnetic repulsion of protons at opposite ends of the nucleus accumulates without a compensating increase in strong-force attraction, eventually making large nuclei unstable. 50.3 Isotopes

The atomic number Z determines the element (number of protons). The mass number

A = Z + N, where N is the number of neutrons. Atoms of the same element (same Z)

but different mass numbers (different N) are called isotopes. Most elements have several naturally occurring isotopes. Carbon has three: carbon-12

(¹²C, 6 protons, 6 neutrons, 98.9% of natural carbon), carbon-13 (¹³C, 6 protons, 7

neutrons, 1.1%), and carbon-14 (¹⁴C, 6 protons, 8 neutrons, trace amounts from cosmic-

ray production). Hydrogen has three isotopes: ¹H (protium, 99.98%), ²H (deuterium,

0.015%), and ³H (tritium, radioactive, trace amounts).

Isotopes are written as X^A_Z or, more commonly, as the element name followed by

the mass number: carbon-14, uranium-238, iodine-131. Since Z is implied by the

element symbol, the mass number alone is usually sufficient: ¹⁴C, ²³⁸U, ¹³¹I.

Key Point:

Isotopes of an element have identical chemical properties (because chemical behaviour depends on electron structure, not the nucleus) but different physical properties, particularly density and radioactive behaviour. This is why isotope separation requires physical methods (centrifuges, diffusion) rather than chemical reactions. 50.4 Nuclear Stability

Not all combinations of protons and neutrons form stable nuclei. The stable nuclides cluster along a narrow band in the Z-N plane called the valley of stability. For light nuclei (Z ≤20), the stable ratio is approximately N/Z 1 (equal numbers of protons and neutrons). For heavier nuclei, the stable ratio increases to about N/Z 1.5 at Z = 80, because extra neutrons are needed to dilute the growing proton-proton repulsion with additional strong-force binding. Binding energy is the energy required to completely separate a nucleus into its constituent protons and neutrons. It is the measure of nuclear stability: a larger binding energy means a more tightly bound, more stable nucleus. The binding energy per nucleon peaks at around 8.7 MeV for iron-56 (⁵⁶Fe), which is the most stable nucleus per nucleon. Nuclei lighter than iron can release energy by fusion; nuclei heavier than iron can release energy by fission. Both processes move toward the iron peak. The mass defect is the difference between the actual nuclear mass and the total mass

of the separate nucleons. By E = mc², this mass defect equals the binding energy: a

bound nucleus is lighter than the sum of its parts because energy was released when it formed.

E_binding = Δm × c² = (Z m_p + N m_n −m_nucleus) × c²

50.5 Radioactive Decay

A radioactive nucleus is one that spontaneously transforms into a different nuclear configuration, emitting particles or radiation in the process. This is radioactive decay. It is a quantum mechanical process: each unstable nucleus has a fixed probability of decaying in any given time interval, independent of its past history, the temperature, the chemical environment, or any external influence. Radioactive decay is governed by the law of radioactive decay:

N(t) = N₀ e^(−λt)

or

A(t) = A₀ e^(−λt)

50.6 Alpha Decay

Alpha decay occurs when a heavy nucleus emits an alpha particle (α particle), which is a helium-4 nucleus: 2 protons and 2 neutrons, charge +2e, mass 4 u. Alpha decay reduces the atomic number by 2 and the mass number by 4:

²⁴ₐX

→

²´₈₂Y

+

⁴₂He

Alpha particles are emitted with kinetic energies typically in the range 4–9 MeV and travel at about 5–7% of the speed of light. Despite their high energy, alpha particles are relatively heavy and doubly charged, so they lose energy rapidly through ionisation of surrounding matter. Alpha particles from nuclear decay are stopped by a few centimetres of air or a thin sheet of paper. Alpha decay occurs by quantum tunnelling: classically, the alpha particle is trapped inside the nucleus by the potential energy barrier created by the strong force. Quantum mechanically, the alpha particle's wave function extends through the barrier, giving a non-zero probability of escape. The probability of escape determines the

decay constant λ and hence the half-life. This explains why alpha emitters have half-

lives ranging from microseconds to billions of years depending on the nuclear energy and barrier width.

50.7 Beta Decay

Beta decay involves the transformation of a nucleon via the weak nuclear force. There are two types: beta-minus (β ) and beta-plus (β ). Beta-minus decay (β ) A neutron in the nucleus transforms into a proton, emitting an electron (the beta

(β decay) Beta-minus decay occurs in neutron-rich nuclei (too many neutrons relative to protons). It moves the nucleus toward the valley of stability by converting a neutron to a proton. The electron emitted has kinetic energy up to the maximum allowed by energy conservation, but shares the energy with the antineutrino, so the electron energy spectrum is continuous (not discrete, as it would be without the neutrino). ν_e

50.8 Gamma Decay

Gamma decay occurs when a nucleus in an excited nuclear energy state (a metastable state produced by a previous alpha or beta decay, or by neutron capture) drops to a

lower energy state by emitting a gamma photon (γ). The gamma ray is a high-energy

photon with energy typically in the range 0.1–10 MeV.

ᴮX* →ᴮX + γ

Gamma rays are the most penetrating form of ionising radiation: they have no charge and no mass, so they interact rarely with matter. They are attenuated (reduced in intensity) exponentially as they pass through material, but a significant fraction can pass through many centimetres of lead or metres of concrete. Gamma shielding requires dense, thick materials. Gamma-emitting isotopes are used extensively in medicine (technetium-99m for diagnostic imaging, cobalt-60 for cancer radiotherapy), in industry (radiographic inspection of welds and pipelines), and in food irradiation (sterilisation). Gamma cameras detect gamma photons emitted from inside the body to produce images of organ function. 50.9 Half-Life

The half-life T½ of a radioactive nuclide is the time required for exactly half of any

given number of radioactive nuclei to decay. It is a fixed property of each nuclide,

ranging from 10 ²² seconds (some nuclear isomers) to 10²² years (bismuth-209, the

rarest stable nuclide). Importantly, the half-life does not depend on how many nuclei are present: whether you have one gram or one tonne of a substance, the fraction that decays in one half-life is always exactly 1/2.

After n half-lives: N(t) = N₀ × (1/2)^n, where n = t/T½. The fraction remaining is always

(1/2)^n regardless of N₀. The activity (decay rate) also halves every T½.

Example — Half-life calculation

Iodine-131 has T½ = 8.02 days. A hospital receives

a 5.0 GBq sample. Activity after 24 days (3 half-lives): A = 5.0 × (1/2)³ = 5.0/8 = 0.625

GBq. Activity after 40.1 days (5 half-lives): A = 5.0 × (1/2)⁵ = 5.0/32 = 0.156 GBq.

50.10 Nuclear Fission

Nuclear fission is the splitting of a heavy nucleus into two lighter nuclei (fission fragments) plus several neutrons and a large amount of energy. Fission can occur spontaneously (spontaneous fission) but is much more commonly induced by neutron absorption. For uranium-235, neutron-induced fission is the key reaction in nuclear reactors and

uranium bombs. When ²³₅U absorbs a slow (thermal) neutron, it becomes the highly

unstable ²³⁶U*, which splits in one of many possible ways. A typical fission:

¹₀n + ²³₅₉²U →¹⁴₆₆Ba + ₉°³₆Kr + 3 ¹₀n + energy

The energy released per fission event of ²³₅U is about 200 MeV

3.2 × 10 ¹¹ J.

Burning a single carbon atom in chemical combustion releases about 4 eV. Nuclear fission of uranium releases about 50 million times more energy per atom. The key to a sustained fission reaction is the chain reaction: each fission event releases

2–3 neutrons, which can induce further fissions in other ²³₅U nuclei. If on average at

least one neutron from each fission causes another fission, the chain reaction is self- sustaining (critical). In a nuclear reactor, control rods (made of neutron-absorbing materials such as boron or cadmium) regulate the neutron population to maintain criticality at a controlled, steady rate. In a nuclear bomb, the chain reaction is allowed to become supercritical, amplifying exponentially in microseconds.

Key Point: Natural uranium is 99.3% ²³⁸U (which does not fission with slow neutrons)

and only 0.71% ²³₅U (which does). Reactor-grade uranium is typically enriched to 3–5%

²³₅U. Weapons-grade uranium is enriched to > 90% ²³₅U. This difference in enrichment

level makes the civilian and weapons programmes physically distinct. Physics: Foundations of Physical Science Chapter 50 — Nuclear Physics and Radioactivity

50.11 Nuclear Fusion

Nuclear fusion is the combination of two light nuclei to form a heavier nucleus. Because the binding energy per nucleon increases as nuclei become heavier (up to iron), fusion releases energy for nuclei lighter than iron. Fusion releases more energy per unit mass than fission: the deuterium-tritium fusion reaction releases 17.6 MeV for nuclei with total mass number 5, compared to 200 MeV for fission of a mass-235 nucleus — about 3.5 times more energy per nucleon.

²₁H + ³₁H →⁴₂He + ¹₀n + 17.6 MeV

Fusion powers the Sun and all main-sequence stars. In the Sun's core (temperature

1.5 × 10⁷ K, density

150,000 kg/m³), the dominant process is the proton-proton

chain: four protons fuse (in several steps) to produce a helium-4 nucleus, releasing 26.7 MeV plus neutrinos. The Sun converts 600 million tonnes of hydrogen to helium every second, with a mass-to-energy conversion of about 4 million tonnes per second

via E = mc².

Achieving controlled fusion on Earth requires confining a plasma at temperatures of 10⁸ K or higher for long enough for the reaction rate to produce net energy. Current approaches include magnetic confinement (tokamak reactors, such as ITER under construction in France) and inertial confinement (high-power laser compression). As of 2025, sustained net energy gain remains a major engineering challenge, though scientific breakeven has been achieved.

Historical Note:

The first thermonuclear weapon (hydrogen bomb) was tested by the United States in 1952 (Ivy Mike test). It used a fission bomb to trigger fusion of lithium deuteride, releasing 10.4 megatons of TNT equivalent — about 450 times more powerful than the Hiroshima bomb. The destructive power of fusion weapons comes primarily from the energy released in the fusion reaction, supplemented by fast-

neutron fission of a ²³⁸U tamper in most designs.

50.12 Mass-Energy Equivalence: E = mc²

Einstein's special theory of relativity (1905) showed that mass and energy are

equivalent: a body with rest mass m at rest contains energy E = mc², where c = 3.00 ×

10⁸ m/s is the speed of light in vacuum.

E = mc²

In nuclear physics, E = mc² explains where the energy in nuclear reactions comes from.

In fission and fusion, the total mass of the products is slightly less than the total mass of the reactants. This mass difference (mass defect) is converted to kinetic energy of

the products, gamma radiation, and neutrinos. The conversion factor is c² = (3 × 10⁸)²

= 9 × 10¹⁶ J/kg: even a small mass defect corresponds to an enormous energy release.

50.13 Nuclear Technology and Applications

Nuclear power generation Nuclear power plants use controlled fission chain reactions to generate heat, which produces steam to drive turbines connected to electrical generators. Most operating

reactors use enriched uranium fuel (²³₅U) in a moderator (usually water, which slows

neutrons to thermal energies where fission is more likely) controlled by neutron- absorbing control rods. As of 2025, nuclear power generates about 10% of the world's electricity from around 440 operating reactors. France generates about 70% of its electricity from nuclear power. Nuclear medicine: diagnostics Single-photon emission computed tomography (SPECT) and PET scanning use radioactive tracers administered to patients. SPECT uses gamma emitters such as technetium-99m; PET uses positron emitters such as fluorine-18 (FDG, a glucose analogue). The spatial distribution of the tracer reveals metabolic activity — cancer cells metabolise glucose at elevated rates, making FDG-PET effective for tumour detection and staging. Iodine-131 is used both diagnostically and therapeutically for thyroid disorders, because the thyroid gland selectively concentrates iodine. Nuclear medicine: therapy Radiation therapy uses high-energy radiation (X-rays, gamma rays, proton beams, or electron beams) to destroy cancer cells by damaging their DNA. External beam radiotherapy uses a linear accelerator to direct a shaped beam at the tumour from multiple angles. Brachytherapy implants radioactive seeds directly into or near the tumour. Proton beam therapy deposits most of its energy at a precise depth (the Bragg peak), minimising damage to surrounding tissue. Radiocarbon dating Carbon-14 is produced continuously in the atmosphere by cosmic-ray neutrons

reacting with nitrogen-14: ¹⁴N + n →¹⁴C + p. Living organisms continuously exchange

carbon with the atmosphere, maintaining a constant ¹⁴C/¹²C ratio. At death, exchange

ceases and ¹⁴C decays with T½ = 5730 years. Measuring the residual ¹⁴C ratio allows

the age of organic material to be determined up to about 50,000 years with ±100 year precision. For older materials, longer-lived isotopes (potassium-40, uranium-238, rubidium-87) are used. Physics: Foundations of Physical Science Chapter 50 — Nuclear Physics and Radioactivity

50.14 Radiation Safety and Biological Effects

Ionising radiation — radiation energetic enough to remove electrons from atoms — can damage biological tissue by breaking chemical bonds, generating free radicals, and directly damaging DNA. The biological effects depend on the type of radiation, the energy deposited, the organs irradiated, and the time over which the dose is received. The absorbed dose (D) measures energy deposited per unit mass of tissue: 1 gray (Gy) = 1 J/kg. But equal doses of different radiation types cause different biological damage: alpha particles, being heavy and highly ionising, cause about 20 times more biological damage per unit absorbed dose than gamma rays or X-rays. 50.15 Common Mistakes in Nuclear Physics

•

Confusing mass number and atomic number. Mass number A = protons +

neutrons; atomic number Z = protons only. In decay equations, both A and Z

must balance separately. A balanced decay equation satisfies ΣA_reactants =

ΣA_products AND ΣZ_reactants = ΣZ_products simultaneously.

• Forgetting that gamma decay does not change Z or A. Gamma emission changes only the energy state of the nucleus, not its proton or neutron count. The daughter nuclide is the same element with the same mass number as the parent. •

Confusing activity and number of nuclei. Activity A = λN — they are

proportional but not equal. A nucleus can have a large N but low activity (small

λ, long half-life) or small N but high activity (large λ, short half-life). Units: N is

dimensionless; A is in becquerel (Bq = decays/second).

•

Applying n = t/T½ when t is not a whole multiple of T½. For non-integer n, use

the exponential formula N = N₀ e^(-λt). Using (1/2)^n only works cleanly when

n is an integer. •

Using the wrong mass-energy conversion factor. The conversion is: 1 u = 931.5

MeV/c². A mass defect of Δm in unified mass units gives energy E = Δm × 931.5

MeV. If Δm is in kg, use E = Δm × c² with c in m/s. Mixing units gives errors of

many orders of magnitude. • Thinking radioactive decay is triggered or accelerated by conditions. It is not. Heating, pressurising, dissolving, or chemically reacting a radioactive substance does not change its half-life. Decay is a quantum probability inherent to the nucleus, independent of external conditions (with the tiny exception of electron capture in exotic environments).

Common Mistake:

In decay equation problems, check both Z and A balance separately. A common mistake is balancing A correctly but forgetting that the daughter nucleus must also have the correct Z (and therefore be the right element in the periodic table). Write out the full nuclide notation — symbol, mass number superscript, atomic number subscript — for every particle to make the balance check explicit.

Chapter 19: Torque and Rotational Motion

Part IV — Rotation, Equilibrium, and Materials · Chapter 19

Extending Newton’s laws into the world of spinning, turning, and rotating objects.

A wrench loosens a bolt that your fingers could never budge, even if your fingers exerted the same force — because the wrench applies that force far from the bolt’s center, where it is much more effective at causing rotation. A figure skater pulls her arms inward and spins faster, even though no new force acts. A door swung from its hinges obeys rules strikingly similar to those governing a cart pushed across a floor. Rotation follows laws just as precise as those governing linear motion — laws that are the direct rotational analogues of Newton’s three laws and the kinematics of Chapters 6 through 8.

19.1 Why Rotation Requires New Tools

Everything in Chapters 6 through 18 dealt with objects treated as point masses or as objects that translate without rotating. A box sliding down a ramp, a ball launched from a cliff, a car braking on a highway — in all of these, every part of the object moves in the same way. The entire object translates.

But most real objects also rotate. A tire rolls without slipping. A planet spins on its axis while orbiting the Sun. A gymnast executes a somersault. A wrench turns a bolt. In all of these, different parts of the object move differently — points farther from the rotation axis move faster than points closer to it.

To describe and predict rotational motion, we need new quantities. The analogue of position becomes angular position. The analogue of velocity becomes angular velocity. The analogue of acceleration becomes angular acceleration. The analogue of force becomes torque. The analogue of mass becomes moment of inertia. And Newton’s second law \(F = ma\) becomes its rotational counterpart \(\tau = I\alpha\).

This parallelism is not a coincidence. It is the mathematical expression of a deep physical fact: rotational mechanics is not a separate subject from translational mechanics. It is the same mechanics, expressed in the language most natural for rotating systems.

Key Point

Every quantity in linear mechanics has a rotational analogue, and every law of linear mechanics has a rotational counterpart. As you learn each rotational concept, identify its linear counterpart. This parallelism is the key to mastering rotational motion efficiently.

19.2 Translational vs. Rotational Motion

Before introducing new quantities, it is worth making the distinction precise.

Translational (linear) motion occurs when all parts of an object move in the same direction with the same velocity at any given instant. The position of the whole object is described by the position of its center of mass.

Rotational motion occurs when an object spins about an axis. Each particle in the object moves in a circular arc around that axis. Particles at different distances from the axis trace circles of different radii, but all particles complete one revolution in the same time — they all have the same angular velocity.

Combined motion is the most general case: an object translates and rotates simultaneously. A rolling wheel is the classic example. Its center of mass moves forward (translational), while the wheel spins about its own axis (rotational). In this chapter, we focus primarily on pure rotation about a fixed axis. Chapter 19 builds the tools; Chapters 20 and 21 apply them to equilibrium and to objects in complex settings.

19.3 Angular Position and Angular Displacement

When a rigid object rotates about a fixed axis, every point on it traces a circular arc. We describe the object’s orientation using an angular position \(\theta\), measured from a chosen reference line. The reference line is usually chosen to be along the positive \(x\)-axis, and angles are measured counterclockwise as positive (following the standard mathematical convention).

The SI unit of angular position is the radian (rad). One radian is the angle subtended at the center of a circle by an arc length equal to the radius.

\[ \theta = \frac{s}{r} \]

where \(s\) is the arc length and \(r\) is the radius. This definition makes radians dimensionless — the units of arc length divide out. Nonetheless, we write “rad” as a reminder that we are using the radian measure rather than degrees.

Conversion: \(360° = 2\pi \text{ rad}\), so \(1 \text{ rad} = 57.3°\) and \(1° = \pi/180 \text{ rad}\).

The angular displacement is the change in angular position:

\[ \Delta\theta = \theta_f - \theta_i \]

Angular displacement is positive for counterclockwise rotation and negative for clockwise rotation (when viewed from the positive axis direction). Like linear displacement, it can be positive, negative, or zero, and it does not depend on the path — only on the initial and final orientations.

Common Mistake

Students sometimes confuse angular position \(\theta\) (where the object is pointing, relative to a reference) with arc length \(s\) (how far a point on the rim has traveled). They are related by \(s = r\theta\), but they are different quantities with different units. Always keep them distinct.

19.4 Angular Velocity

Angular velocity \(\omega\) (lowercase Greek omega) is the rate of change of angular position:

\[ \omega = \frac{\Delta\theta}{\Delta t} \]

For instantaneous angular velocity (at one specific moment), this becomes the limit as \(\Delta t \to 0\) — conceptually the same as the definition of instantaneous linear velocity.

The SI unit of angular velocity is radians per second (rad/s). Angular velocity is positive for counterclockwise rotation and negative for clockwise rotation.

Angular velocity is also related to the period \(T\) (time for one complete rotation) and frequency \(f\) (rotations per second, in Hz):

\[ \omega = \frac{2\pi}{T} = 2\pi f \]

Example

A CD spinning at 500 rpm (revolutions per minute):

\[\omega = 500 \text{ rpm} \times \frac{2\pi \text{ rad}}{1 \text{ rev}} \times \frac{1 \text{ min}}{60 \text{ s}} = \frac{500 \times 2\pi}{60} \approx 52.4 \text{ rad/s}\]

19.5 Angular Acceleration

Angular acceleration \(\alpha\) (lowercase Greek alpha) is the rate of change of angular velocity:

\[ \alpha = \frac{\Delta\omega}{\Delta t} \]

The SI unit is radians per second squared (rad/s²). Angular acceleration is positive when the angular velocity is increasing (speeding up counterclockwise, or slowing down clockwise) and negative when the angular velocity is decreasing.

The rotational kinematic equations for constant angular acceleration are direct analogues of the linear kinematic equations from Chapter 8:

\[ \omega_f = \omega_i + \alpha t \]

\[ \theta = \omega_i t + \tfrac{1}{2}\alpha t^2 \]

\[ \omega_f^2 = \omega_i^2 + 2\alpha\Delta\theta \]

\[ \Delta\theta = \tfrac{1}{2}(\omega_i + \omega_f)t \]

These four equations apply only when angular acceleration is constant — the rotational analogue of the condition required for the linear kinematic equations in Chapter 8. Every method, strategy, and caution from Chapter 8 applies here with the substitutions: \(x \to \theta\), \(v \to \omega\), \(a \to \alpha\).

Worked Example 19.1 — Rotational Kinematics

A motor starts from rest and reaches an angular velocity of 240 rad/s in 8.0 s at constant angular acceleration. Find: (a) the angular acceleration; (b) the total angle rotated during the 8.0 s; (c) the number of complete revolutions.

(a) Angular acceleration:

\[ \alpha = \frac{\Delta\omega}{\Delta t} = \frac{240 - 0}{8.0} = 30 \text{ rad/s}^2 \]

(b) Total angle rotated (use equation 2, with \(\omega_i = 0\)):

\[ \Delta\theta = \omega_i t + \tfrac{1}{2}\alpha t^2 = 0 + \tfrac{1}{2}(30)(8.0)^2 = 15 \times 64 = 960 \text{ rad} \]

(c) Number of revolutions:

\[ N = \frac{\Delta\theta}{2\pi} = \frac{960}{2\pi} \approx 152.8 \text{ rev} \]

19.6 Tangential vs. Angular Quantities

A point at distance \(r\) from the rotation axis moves along a circular arc. Its motion can be described either in terms of angular quantities (how many radians per second it sweeps through) or in terms of linear (tangential) quantities (how fast it actually moves along the arc). The two descriptions are linked by the radius \(r\).

Tangential (linear) speed of a point on a rotating object:

\[ v_t = r\omega \]

Tangential acceleration (rate of change of speed along the arc):

\[ a_t = r\alpha \]

Centripetal acceleration (directed inward, toward the axis — this is the acceleration from Chapter 13 required to keep the point moving in a circle):

\[ a_c = \frac{v_t^2}{r} = r\omega^2 \]

The total acceleration of a point on a rotating, accelerating object has two components: \(a_t\) (tangential, along the arc, changing the speed) and \(a_c\) (centripetal, perpendicular to the arc, changing the direction). These add as perpendicular vectors:

\[ a_\text{total} = \sqrt{a_t^2 + a_c^2} \]

Key Point

Points farther from the rotation axis have greater tangential speed and greater tangential acceleration for the same \(\omega\) and \(\alpha\). The angular velocity \(\omega\) and angular acceleration \(\alpha\) are the same for every point on a rigid body, but the tangential (linear) quantities \(v_t\) and \(a_t\) increase with \(r\).

Worked Example 19.2 — Tangential and Centripetal Acceleration

A disk of radius 0.50 m accelerates from rest at \(\alpha = 4.0\) rad/s². Find the tangential speed, tangential acceleration, and centripetal acceleration of a point on the rim at \(t = 3.0\) s.

Step 1 — Angular velocity at \(t = 3.0\) s:

\[ \omega = \omega_i + \alpha t = 0 + 4.0 \times 3.0 = 12.0 \text{ rad/s} \]

Step 2 — Tangential speed:

\[ v_t = r\omega = 0.50 \times 12.0 = 6.0 \text{ m/s} \]

Step 3 — Tangential acceleration:

\[ a_t = r\alpha = 0.50 \times 4.0 = 2.0 \text{ m/s}^2 \]

Step 4 — Centripetal acceleration:

\[ a_c = r\omega^2 = 0.50 \times (12.0)^2 = 0.50 \times 144 = 72 \text{ m/s}^2 \]

Step 5 — Total acceleration:

\[ a_\text{total} = \sqrt{(2.0)^2 + (72)^2} = \sqrt{4 + 5184} \approx 72.0 \text{ m/s}^2 \]

At this moment the centripetal component dominates because the disk is spinning fast. The tangential component is relatively small.

19.7 Radius and Rotational Kinematics

The relationship \(s = r\theta\) (arc length equals radius times angle in radians) is the bridge between the rotational kinematic quantities and their linear equivalents. It applies at every level: position, velocity, and acceleration.

\[ s = r\theta \qquad v_t = r\omega \qquad a_t = r\alpha \]

These three relations are all you need to translate between the rotational description (\(\theta\), \(\omega\), \(\alpha\)) and the tangential linear description (\(s\), \(v_t\), \(a_t\)) for any point at radius \(r\) on a rotating body.

A critically important application is rolling without slipping. When a wheel of radius \(r\) rolls without slipping on a flat surface, the contact point is instantaneously at rest, and the center of the wheel moves forward at speed:

\[ v_\text{cm} = r\omega \]

The wheel’s rotation and its translation are locked together. If the wheel spins faster (larger \(\omega\)), the center moves faster (larger \(v_\text{cm}\)) by the same factor. This constraint — \(v_\text{cm} = r\omega\) — connects the rotational and translational descriptions of rolling motion.

19.8 What Torque Is

When you push open a door, the door rotates about its hinges. Push at the edge of the door (far from the hinge) and it swings open easily. Push at a point close to the hinge and it is much harder to open. Push directly at the hinge and the door does not rotate at all, regardless of how hard you push.

The quantity that captures “how effective a force is at causing rotation” is called torque (symbol \(\tau\), lowercase Greek tau). Torque is the rotational analogue of force. Just as a net force causes linear acceleration, a net torque causes angular acceleration.

Torque depends on three things: 1. The magnitude of the force \(F\) 2. The distance from the pivot (rotation axis) to the point where the force is applied, called the moment arm or lever arm \(r\) 3. The angle between the force vector and the line from the pivot to the application point

A large force applied far from the axis, at a right angle to the radius, produces the largest possible torque. The same force applied closer to the axis, or at a non-perpendicular angle, produces less torque.

Key Point

Torque is not simply force. A large force that passes directly through the pivot point produces zero torque, because it has no tendency to cause rotation. Location and direction of the force relative to the axis are just as important as the force’s magnitude.

19.9 Lever Arm and Perpendicular Distance

The lever arm (also called the moment arm) is the perpendicular distance from the rotation axis to the line of action of the force. The line of action is the straight line along which the force vector acts, extended in both directions if necessary.

The lever arm \(d_\perp\) is related to the distance \(r\) from pivot to application point and the angle \(\theta\) between the force and the radius vector by:

\[ d_\perp = r\sin\theta \]

This is the key geometric identity in torque problems. When \(\theta = 90°\) (force perpendicular to the radius), the lever arm equals \(r\) — maximum torque. When \(\theta = 0°\) or \(180°\) (force parallel to the radius, pointing toward or away from the pivot), the lever arm is zero — no torque.

Example

A 0.40 m wrench is used to tighten a bolt. If you push perpendicular to the wrench (\(\theta = 90°\)), the lever arm is 0.40 m. If you push at 30° to the wrench (\(\theta = 30°\)), the lever arm is \(0.40 \sin 30° = 0.20\) m — half as effective. If you push directly along the wrench (\(\theta = 0°\)), the lever arm is zero — no torque at all, no matter how hard you push.

19.10 Calculating Torque

The magnitude of the torque produced by a force \(F\) applied at distance \(r\) from the pivot, at angle \(\theta\) between the force and the radius vector, is:

\[ \tau = rF\sin\theta \]

Equivalently, since the lever arm is \(d_\perp = r\sin\theta\):

\[ \tau = F \cdot d_\perp \]

Both forms are correct and useful. The first is easier to apply when \(r\) and \(\theta\) are given. The second is easier when the perpendicular distance to the line of action has been identified geometrically.

The SI unit of torque is the newton-meter (N·m). Note that this is the same unit as energy (joule = N·m), but torque is not energy — it is a different physical quantity that happens to have the same dimensions. In practice, torques are always written as N·m (never joules) to avoid confusion.

Sign convention for torque: Following the standard convention, torques that tend to produce counterclockwise rotation are positive; torques that tend to produce clockwise rotation are negative. (This matches the sign convention for angles and angular velocity.)

Worked Example 19.3 — Computing Torques

Three forces act on a disk that can rotate about its center: - Force \(F_1\) = 10.0 N applied at \(r_1\) = 0.30 m, perpendicular to the radius (tends to rotate counterclockwise). - Force \(F_2\) = 15.0 N applied at \(r_2\) = 0.20 m, at 40° to the radius (tends to rotate clockwise). - Force \(F_3\) = 8.0 N applied along the radius (\(\theta_3 = 0°\), passing through the center).

Torque from \(F_1\):

\[ \tau_1 = r_1 F_1 \sin 90° = 0.30 \times 10.0 \times 1 = +3.00 \text{ N·m (counterclockwise, positive)} \]

Torque from \(F_2\):

\[ \tau_2 = r_2 F_2 \sin 40° = 0.20 \times 15.0 \times 0.643 = -1.93 \text{ N·m (clockwise, negative)} \]

Torque from \(F_3\):

\[ \tau_3 = r_3 F_3 \sin 0° = 0.25 \times 8.0 \times 0 = 0 \text{ N·m (passes through pivot)} \]

Net torque:

\[ \tau_\text{net} = +3.00 + (-1.93) + 0 = +1.07 \text{ N·m (net counterclockwise)} \]

Common Mistake

The most common error in torque calculations is using \(r\) instead of \(r\sin\theta\) — treating the distance from pivot to application point as the lever arm, when in fact the lever arm is the perpendicular distance from the pivot to the line of action of the force. Draw the line of action and the perpendicular to it from the pivot before computing the lever arm.

19.11 Net Torque and Rotational Acceleration

Just as the net force determines translational acceleration (\(F_\text{net} = ma\)), the net torque determines rotational acceleration. Newton’s second law for rotation states:

\[ \tau_\text{net} = I\alpha \]

where: - \(\tau_\text{net}\) is the net torque about the rotation axis (in N·m) - \(I\) is the moment of inertia of the object about that axis (in kg·m²) - \(\alpha\) is the angular acceleration (in rad/s²)

The net torque is the algebraic (signed) sum of all torques acting on the object:

\[ \tau_\text{net} = \sum_i \tau_i = \sum_i r_i F_i \sin\theta_i \]

If the net torque is positive (counterclockwise), the object gains positive angular acceleration (spins faster counterclockwise or slows down if spinning clockwise). If the net torque is zero, the object has zero angular acceleration — it may be rotating, but at constant angular velocity.

Worked Example 19.4 — Finding Angular Acceleration

A disk of moment of inertia \(I = 0.80\) kg·m² experiences a net torque of \(\tau_\text{net} = 3.2\) N·m. Find the angular acceleration.

\[ \alpha = \frac{\tau_\text{net}}{I} = \frac{3.2 \text{ N·m}}{0.80 \text{ kg·m}^2} = 4.0 \text{ rad/s}^2 \]

Check: Units: \(\frac{\text{N·m}}{\text{kg·m}^2} = \frac{\text{kg·m}^2/\text{s}^2}{\text{kg·m}^2} = \text{s}^{-2} = \text{rad/s}^2\) ✓

19.12 Moment of Inertia (Conceptual Introduction)

The moment of inertia \(I\) is the rotational analogue of mass. Just as mass measures resistance to translational acceleration (a larger mass requires a larger force to achieve the same acceleration), the moment of inertia measures resistance to rotational acceleration — how hard it is to spin an object faster or slower.

However, the moment of inertia differs from mass in one crucial way: it depends not just on how much mass an object has, but on how that mass is distributed relative to the rotation axis. Mass concentrated far from the axis contributes more to the moment of inertia than the same mass concentrated close to the axis.

For a single point mass \(m\) at distance \(r\) from the axis:

\[ I = mr^2 \]

For a collection of point masses:

\[ I = \sum_i m_i r_i^2 \]

For continuous objects, the sum becomes an integral — a topic handled fully in Volume I of the advanced series. For this book, we use the results of those integrals, listed in Table 19.4 as standard formulas.

Table 19.4: Moments of inertia for common shapes about their most natural axes. Note that for the same mass and radius, a hollow cylinder ( \(I = mR^2\) ) has twice the moment of inertia of a solid cylinder ( \(I = \frac{1}{2}mR^2\) ) — its mass is all at maximum distance from the axis.
Object Rotation Axis Moment of Inertia
Point mass \(m\) Distance \(r\) from mass \(I = mr^2\)
Thin rod, length \(L\), mass \(m\) Through center, perpendicular to rod \(I = \frac{1}{12}mL^2\)
Thin rod, length \(L\), mass \(m\) Through one end, perpendicular to rod \(I = \frac{1}{3}mL^2\)
Solid cylinder (or disk), radius \(R\) Through center, along axis \(I = \frac{1}{2}mR^2\)
Hollow cylinder (thin ring), radius \(R\) Through center, along axis \(I = mR^2\)
Solid sphere, radius \(R\) Through center \(I = \frac{2}{5}mR^2\)
Hollow sphere (thin shell), radius \(R\) Through center \(I = \frac{2}{3}mR^2\)

Key Point

A figure skater pulling her arms inward decreases her moment of inertia (mass is brought closer to the spin axis). By conservation of angular momentum (\(L = I\omega = \text{constant}\), discussed in Chapter 22), a smaller \(I\) requires a larger \(\omega\) — she spins faster. Conversely, stretching her arms outward increases \(I\) and slows her rotation. No external torque acts; the change comes entirely from redistributing her mass.

Bridge Note

In Volume I of the advanced series, moment of inertia is derived from first principles using integration for continuous mass distributions. The parallel-axis theorem (\(I = I_\text{cm} + md^2\)) allows calculation of the moment of inertia about any axis once the center-of-mass moment is known. These tools make the table above derivable rather than memorized.

19.13 Rotational Analogies to Newton’s Laws

The three laws of Newton that govern translational motion each have a precise rotational counterpart. The analogy is exact — every concept maps directly.

Newton’s First Law — Rotational Version

A rotating object continues to rotate at constant angular velocity unless acted upon by a net external torque.

An object spinning freely in space (no friction, no external torques) will spin forever at the same rate. This is the rotational inertia of the object. A gyroscope demonstrates this dramatically: once spinning, its axis resists any torque that tries to change its orientation.

Newton’s Second Law — Rotational Version

\[ \tau_\text{net} = I\alpha \]

The net torque on an object equals the moment of inertia times the angular acceleration. The direction of \(\alpha\) is the same as the direction of \(\tau_\text{net}\) (using the sign convention: positive = counterclockwise).

Newton’s Third Law — Rotational Version

If object A exerts a torque on object B, then object B exerts an equal and opposite torque on object A.

When you use a wrench to tighten a bolt, the bolt exerts an equal torque back on the wrench (and therefore on your hand). When an engine applies torque to a car’s axle to spin the wheels, the wheels apply an equal and opposite torque back on the engine (engine braking). When a helicopter rotor spins one way, the body of the helicopter tends to spin the opposite way — which is why most helicopters have a tail rotor to provide an opposing torque.

Table 19.5: Linear and rotational Newton’s laws side by side.
Newton’s Law Linear Version Rotational Version
First Law \(\vec{F}_\text{net} = 0 \Rightarrow \vec{v} = \text{constant}\) \(\tau_\text{net} = 0 \Rightarrow \omega = \text{constant}\)
Second Law \(\vec{F}_\text{net} = m\vec{a}\) \(\tau_\text{net} = I\alpha\)
Third Law \(\vec{F}_{AB} = -\vec{F}_{BA}\) \(\tau_{AB} = -\tau_{BA}\)

Worked Example 19.5 — Applying the Rotational Second Law

A 2.0 kg solid disk (radius \(R = 0.40\) m) is initially at rest. A rope wrapped around its rim exerts a constant tangential force of 6.0 N. Find: (a) the torque exerted by the rope; (b) the moment of inertia of the disk; (c) the angular acceleration; (d) the angular velocity after 5.0 s; (e) the tangential speed of the rim at \(t = 5.0\) s.

(a) Torque — the rope pulls tangentially at radius \(R\), so \(\theta = 90°\):

\[ \tau = RF\sin 90° = 0.40 \times 6.0 \times 1 = 2.4 \text{ N·m} \]

(b) Moment of inertia for a solid disk:

\[ I = \tfrac{1}{2}mR^2 = \tfrac{1}{2}(2.0)(0.40)^2 = 0.16 \text{ kg·m}^2 \]

(c) Angular acceleration:

\[ \alpha = \frac{\tau}{I} = \frac{2.4}{0.16} = 15 \text{ rad/s}^2 \]

(d) Angular velocity after 5.0 s (starting from rest):

\[ \omega = \omega_i + \alpha t = 0 + 15 \times 5.0 = 75 \text{ rad/s} \]

(e) Tangential speed of rim:

\[ v_t = R\omega = 0.40 \times 75 = 30 \text{ m/s} \]

19.14 Common Mistakes in Rotational Analysis

Mistake 1: Using degrees instead of radians in calculations

Angular velocity, angular acceleration, and the relations \(s = r\theta\), \(v_t = r\omega\), \(a_t = r\alpha\) all require angles in radians. If \(\omega = 180°/\text{s}\), convert to \(\pi\) rad/s before using any of these formulas. Mixing degrees into radian-based calculations produces errors by factors of \(\pi/180 \approx 0.0175\).

Mistake 2: Forgetting that torque depends on the perpendicular lever arm, not \(r\)

The torque is \(\tau = rF\sin\theta = F \cdot d_\perp\). Using \(r\) instead of \(r\sin\theta\) gives the maximum possible torque, not the actual torque. Identify the angle between the force and the radius, apply the sine, or find the perpendicular distance from the pivot to the line of action directly.

Mistake 3: Assuming all points on a rotating object have the same speed

All points on a rigid rotating body share the same \(\omega\) and \(\alpha\), but points at different radii have different tangential speeds (\(v_t = r\omega\)) and different tangential accelerations (\(a_t = r\alpha\)). A point at the rim moves much faster than a point halfway to the center.

Mistake 4: Confusing torque and force

Torque and force are different quantities with different units. A large force can produce zero torque (if its line of action passes through the pivot), and a small force can produce a large torque (if applied at a large lever arm). Always compute torque using \(\tau = r F \sin\theta\), not just \(\tau = F\).

Mistake 5: Applying \(\tau = I\alpha\) with the wrong axis

The equation \(\tau_\text{net} = I\alpha\) applies about a specific rotation axis. The torque must be calculated about the same axis as the moment of inertia. If you switch the axis (e.g., use the moment of inertia about the center but calculate torques about the edge), you get the wrong answer.

Mistake 6: Treating moment of inertia as independent of axis position

The moment of inertia of an object changes with the choice of rotation axis. A rod rotating about its center has \(I = \frac{1}{12}mL^2\); the same rod rotating about one end has \(I = \frac{1}{3}mL^2\) — four times larger. Always use the formula for the axis specified in the problem.

Mistake 7: Forgetting to include the sign of each torque

When computing net torque, assign positive signs to counterclockwise torques and negative signs to clockwise torques before summing. A common error is to add all magnitudes and then guess the direction. Add algebraically: \(\tau_\text{net} = \sum_i \tau_i\) with signs.

Common Mistake

A student computes the “torque on a wrench” by multiplying the force times the length of the wrench. This gives the correct answer only if the force is perpendicular to the wrench. If the force is applied at an angle, the torque is \(\tau = \ell F \sin\theta\), where \(\ell\) is the wrench length and \(\theta\) is the angle between the force and the wrench. Always include the \(\sin\theta\) factor.

Chapter Summary

Rotational motion is governed by the same physical principles as translational motion, expressed through rotational analogues of every linear quantity and law.

Angular kinematics (constant \(\alpha\)):

\[ \omega_f = \omega_i + \alpha t \qquad \Delta\theta = \omega_i t + \tfrac{1}{2}\alpha t^2 \qquad \omega_f^2 = \omega_i^2 + 2\alpha\Delta\theta \]

Connecting angular and tangential quantities (at radius \(r\)):

\[ s = r\theta \qquad v_t = r\omega \qquad a_t = r\alpha \qquad a_c = r\omega^2 \]

Torque:

\[ \tau = rF\sin\theta = F \cdot d_\perp \]

Units: N·m. Positive = counterclockwise; negative = clockwise.

Rotational Newton’s second law:

\[ \tau_\text{net} = I\alpha \]

Core principles: - Angles must be in radians for all kinematic and dynamic calculations. - Torque depends on the lever arm (perpendicular distance from pivot to line of action), not just the distance \(r\) to the application point. - All points on a rigid body share the same \(\omega\) and \(\alpha\); tangential speed and acceleration scale with \(r\). - Moment of inertia \(I\) depends on both total mass and the distribution of that mass relative to the axis. - The rotational kinematic equations are direct analogues of the linear equations from Chapter 8 — use the same four-step method (list quantities, identify missing variable, select equation, solve).

Key Terms

angular position (\(\theta\))
The orientation of a rotating body measured from a reference line, in radians. Positive counterclockwise.
radian (rad)
The SI unit of angle. One radian is the angle subtended by an arc equal in length to the radius. \(2\pi\) rad = \(360°\).
angular displacement (\(\Delta\theta\))
The change in angular position: \(\Delta\theta = \theta_f - \theta_i\). A vector-like signed quantity.
angular velocity (\(\omega\))
The rate of change of angular position: \(\omega = \Delta\theta/\Delta t\). SI unit: rad/s.
angular acceleration (\(\alpha\))
The rate of change of angular velocity: \(\alpha = \Delta\omega/\Delta t\). SI unit: rad/s².
tangential speed (\(v_t\))
The linear speed of a point on a rotating object along its circular arc: \(v_t = r\omega\).
tangential acceleration (\(a_t\))
The component of acceleration along the arc (changing the speed): \(a_t = r\alpha\).
centripetal acceleration (\(a_c\))
The component of acceleration directed inward toward the axis (changing the direction): \(a_c = r\omega^2 = v_t^2/r\).
torque (\(\tau\))
The rotational effect of a force; the product of force, lever arm, and \(\sin\theta\): \(\tau = rF\sin\theta\). SI unit: N·m. The rotational analogue of force.
lever arm (moment arm)
The perpendicular distance from the rotation axis to the line of action of the force. Equal to \(r\sin\theta\).
line of action
The infinite straight line along which a force vector acts, extended in both directions.
moment of inertia (\(I\))
The rotational analogue of mass; measures resistance to angular acceleration; depends on total mass and its distribution relative to the axis: \(I = \sum m_i r_i^2\). SI unit: kg·m².
rotational Newton’s second law
\(\tau_\text{net} = I\alpha\); the net torque on an object equals its moment of inertia times its angular acceleration.

Concept Review Questions

Answer in complete sentences. No calculation required.

  1. Every linear quantity in mechanics has a rotational analogue. List five pairs of analogous quantities and state what each member of the pair measures.

  2. Why must angles be expressed in radians rather than degrees when using the relations \(v_t = r\omega\) and \(s = r\theta\)? What goes wrong if degrees are used?

  3. A large disk and a small disk are both rotating at the same angular velocity. Which has the greater tangential speed at its rim? Explain using \(v_t = r\omega\).

  4. Explain why a force applied directly through the pivot point of a rotating object produces zero torque, regardless of the magnitude of the force.

  5. A wrench is used to tighten a bolt. Explain in terms of lever arm why pushing perpendicular to the wrench handle produces more torque than pushing at an angle.

  6. Two objects have the same mass: a solid cylinder and a hollow cylinder of the same outer radius. Which has the larger moment of inertia? Explain physically why, without using the formulas.

  7. A figure skater spins and then pulls her arms inward. Her moment of inertia decreases. What happens to her angular velocity? What physical principle governs this? (You do not need to know angular momentum formally — reason from \(\tau = I\alpha\) and the absence of external torques.)

  8. State Newton’s three laws in their rotational form. For each one, give a physical example that illustrates it.

  9. What is the difference between angular velocity and tangential velocity? Can two points on the same rotating rigid body have different angular velocities? Can they have different tangential velocities?

  10. Explain why the moment of inertia of a rod rotating about its end (\(I = \frac{1}{3}mL^2\)) is greater than the moment of inertia of the same rod rotating about its center (\(I = \frac{1}{12}mL^2\)).

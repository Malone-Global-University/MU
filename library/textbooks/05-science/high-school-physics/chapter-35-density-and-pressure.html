Chapter 35: Density and Pressure

35.1 What a Fluid Is

A fluid is any substance that can flow and deform continuously under the application of a shear stress (a force applied parallel to a surface). Both liquids and gases are fluids. Solids, by contrast, resist shear deformation — they can be pushed without flowing. The defining property of a fluid is the inability to sustain a static shear stress: apply a sideways force to a layer of fluid and it will flow, no matter how small the force. This distinguishes water from ice (a solid that resists shear), and air from glass (which behaves like an extremely viscous fluid over geological timescales, despite seeming solid on human timescales). Fluids do resist compression and tension (normal stresses), which is why you cannot push your hand through concrete even though concrete technically flows over centuries. But for all practical purposes of this chapter and the next two, fluids are treated as substances that transmit pressure forces in all directions and deform without limit when sheared.

35.2 Liquids vs. Gases

Liquids and gases share the property of being fluids, but they differ in important ways Physics: Foundations of Physical Science Chapter 35 — Density and Pressure that arise from their microscopic structure. A liquid has a definite volume but no definite shape. Its molecules are close together and interact through intermolecular forces, but they can slide past each other (unlike a solid). Liquids are nearly incompressible: changing the pressure by a factor of two changes the volume by much less than 1%. This makes liquids excellent for hydraulic systems, where pressure must be transmitted with minimal energy storage in compression. A gas has neither definite volume nor definite shape. Its molecules are widely separated and interact very weakly. Gases are highly compressible — halving the volume roughly doubles the pressure (Boyle’s Law, Chapter 32). 35.5 Relative Density and Specific Gravity

Specific gravity (SG), also called relative density, is the ratio of the density of a substance to the density of a reference substance, usually water at 4°C:

SG = ρ_substance / ρ_water = ρ_substance / 1000

(where ρ is in kg/m³)

Specific gravity is convenient because its numerical value in the metric system equals the density in g/cm³ and makes comparisons

immediate: SG = 0.92 means the

substance is 92% as dense as water (ice floats); SG = 7.87 means iron is 7.87 times

denser than water (iron sinks). No unit conversion is needed. Specific gravity is measured by a hydrometer: a weighted float that sinks deeper in denser liquids. Hydrometers are used to check battery acid concentration, the sugar content of wine musts (brix measurement), antifreeze concentration, and the salinity of aquaria.

35.6 Pressure

Pressure is the force exerted perpendicular to a surface per unit area of that surface. It is one of the most important quantities in fluid mechanics and connects the macroscopic behaviour of fluids directly to the forces they exert. Physics: Foundations of Physical Science Chapter 35 — Density and Pressure

P = F / A

The same force applied over a smaller area produces greater pressure. This is why a stiletto heel sinks into soft ground while a flat boot does not: the same body weight (force) is concentrated into a much smaller area by the heel. Why a sharp knife cuts more easily than a dull one: the same force acts over a much smaller edge area, producing very high pressure. Why snowshoes allow walking on snow: the body weight is spread over a large area, keeping the pressure below the snow’s yield strength.

11.8 MPa. This is comparable to the yield strength of some soft materials — hence the

damage stiletto heels cause to wooden floors.

35.8 Fluid Pressure and Depth

In a static fluid (one not in motion), pressure increases with depth because the weight of the fluid above presses down on the fluid below. The pressure at any depth h below

the free surface of a fluid of density ρ is:

P = P₀ + ρgh

₀ This equation has several key consequences. First, pressure depends only on depth, not on the shape of the container or the horizontal position. A point 5 m below the surface of a narrow well and 5 m below the surface of the ocean both have the same pressure (if the same fluid and same surface pressure). This is a non-obvious result that puzzled natural philosophers until Pascal systematised it. Second, pressure increases linearly with depth. Every additional metre of water adds

35.9 Atmospheric Pressure

Atmospheric pressure is the pressure exerted by the weight of the column of air above a given point in the atmosphere. At sea level, standard atmospheric pressure P_atm =

101 325 Pa = 101.325 kPa

1 atm 760 mmHg

14.7 psi.

Atmospheric pressure decreases with altitude because the column of air above is shorter and less dense. At 5500 m altitude (approximately the height of the summit of Kilimanjaro), atmospheric pressure is roughly half of sea-level value — each breath contains half as many oxygen molecules as at sea level. At 10 000 m (typical cruising altitude for aircraft), pressure is about 26 kPa, which is why aircraft cabins are pressurised. Atmospheric pressure varies with weather: high-pressure systems (anticyclones) have slightly above-average P_atm; low-pressure systems (cyclones, storms) have below- average P_atm. A falling barometric pressure reading signals approaching unsettled weather. The variation is typically 1–2% around the standard value, significant for aviation and precision measurement. The weight of the entire atmosphere acts on every surface. A horizontal flat surface of area 1 m² has about 101 325 N 10 tonnes of air weight pressing down on it at sea level. This force is balanced by the atmospheric pressure acting upward on the underside of any real surface, so we do not feel it as a net force. When pressure is removed from one side (as in a vacuum seal or suction cup), the net force becomes apparent.

35.10 Gauge Pressure vs. Absolute Pressure

Absolute pressure is pressure measured from zero (a perfect vacuum). It is always non- negative. Gauge pressure is pressure measured relative to atmospheric pressure: P_gauge = P_abs P_atm. Gauge pressure can be positive (pressure above atmospheric, as in an inflated tyre) or negative (pressure below atmospheric, as in a partial vacuum or a suction cup). Gauge pressure is zero when the absolute pressure equals atmospheric. Physics: Foundations of Physical Science Chapter 35 — Density and Pressure

P_abs = P_gauge + P_atm

Most everyday pressure gauges read gauge pressure: a flat tyre reads 0 psi gauge, not

14.7 psi absolute. A tyre inflated to “32 psi” has gauge pressure 32 psi and absolute

pressure 32 + 14.7 = 46.7 psi. Always clarify which type of pressure is being used;

using gauge where absolute is required (or vice versa) causes errors in gas law and fluid pressure calculations.

Study Tip:

Gas law calculations (Chapters 32–34) require absolute pressure. Fluid

depth-pressure calculations using P = P₀ + ρgh require absolute pressure at the

surface (P₀ = P_atm for an open container). Most real-world gauges read gauge

pressure. Always convert before applying formulas.

35.11 Pascal's Principle

Pascal's Principle states: a pressure change applied to a confined fluid is transmitted undiminished to every part of the fluid and to the walls of the container.

This follows directly from the hydrostatic pressure equation P = P₀ + ρgh. If P₀ changes

(for example, by applying a force to a piston), then P changes by the same amount at

every point in the fluid (since ρgh is unchanged for any fixed depth h). The pressure

change propagates instantaneously throughout the entire fluid volume. Pascal's principle makes fluids ideal for transmitting forces over distances and around corners without mechanical linkages. It is the operating principle behind every hydraulic system: brakes, lifts, presses, and jacks.

Example — Pascal's principle

A pump increases the pressure at one end of a hydraulic line by 500 kPa. The pressure at a cylinder 10 m away (same elevation) increases by exactly 500 kPa. The 10 m distance does not reduce the transmitted pressure change.

35.12 Hydraulic Systems

A hydraulic system transmits force from one piston to another through an incompressible fluid. The key insight is that pressure is the same throughout the fluid (Pascal’s principle), but the force exerted by the fluid depends on the area of the piston. A small force applied to a small piston can be amplified into a much larger force at a large piston.

P = F₁/A₁ = F₂/A₂

⇒

F₂ = F₁ × A₂/A₁

Physics: Foundations of Physical Science Chapter 35 — Density and Pressure The mechanical advantage is A₂/A₁. If the output piston is 10 times larger in area than the input piston, the output force is 10 times the input force. But energy is conserved:

the output piston moves a shorter distance. Specifically, A₁d₁ = A₂d₂ (fluid volume is

conserved), so d₂ = d₁ A₁/A₂. Force is amplified by A₂/A₁; distance is reduced by the

same factor. Work done in = work done out (in the ideal case).

Example — Hydraulic lift

A car lift has a small input piston (area 5.0 cm²) and a large output piston (area 200 cm²). A mechanic pushes the input piston with 150 N.

Output force: F₂ = 150 × 200/5.0 = 6000 N. This is enough to lift a car of mass

6000/9.81 612 kg. The mechanical advantage is 200/5 = 40. Hydraulic systems power car brakes (master cylinder amplifies foot force to disc/drum pressure), aircraft control surfaces (pilots move controls against minimal resistance; hydraulics drive the actual surface), construction machinery (excavators, bulldozers), and industrial presses (forging, stamping, injection moulding). The combination of Pascal’s principle and incompressibility makes hydraulic force transmission essentially lossless at practical engineering speeds.

35.13 Common Mistakes in Density and Pressure Problems

•

Confusing mass and weight. Density = mass/volume, not weight/volume. The

weight of a fluid does appear in the pressure formula P = ρgh (as ρg = weight

per unit volume), but density itself is a mass property. • Using gauge pressure when absolute pressure is required. The hydrostatic

formula P = P₀ + ρgh gives absolute pressure. If P₀ is gauge (zero for open

containers), you must add P_atm to get absolute. Failing to include P_atm leads to underestimating pressure by about 101 kPa. • Forgetting to convert units consistently. If density is given in g/cm³ and depth in

metres, convert before applying ρgh. Either convert ρ to kg/m³ and use g in

m/s², or convert h to cm and use g in cm/s² (inconvenient). Easiest: always use SI units (kg, m, Pa). • Thinking pressure depends on the shape of the container (the hydrostatic paradox). The pressure at a given depth depends only on the fluid density, the depth, and the surface pressure — not on whether the container is wide, narrow, tilted, or U-shaped. A narrow tube and a wide tank containing the same fluid have the same pressure at the same depth. Physics: Foundations of Physical Science Chapter 35 — Density and Pressure • Misapplying hydraulic MA: confusing area ratio with force ratio. The force ratio

equals the area ratio (F₂/F₁ = A₂/A₁), but the distance ratio is the inverse (d₂/d₁

= A₁/A₂). Both must be stated consistently or the energy conservation check fails. • Using diameter instead of area. Hydraulic problems often give piston diameters.

Area = πr² = π(d/2)². Using diameter directly instead of computing area first

gives the wrong force ratio.

Common Mistake: Whenever you use P = P₀ + ρgh, check: is P₀ the absolute surface

pressure (P_atm for an open container) or a gauge pressure that must be corrected? This is the most common source of error in hydrostatic pressure problems.

Chapter Summary

Density (ρ = m/V) measures mass per unit volume. Pressure (P = F/A) measures force

per unit area. Both are foundational to understanding all fluid behaviour. •

Density SI unit: kg/m³. Specific gravity = ρ/ρ_water (dimensionless; equals

density in g/cm³). •

Pressure SI unit: pascal (Pa = N/m²). Acts equally in all directions at a point in a

static fluid. •

Hydrostatic pressure: P = P₀ + ρgh. Increases linearly with depth; independent of

container shape. • Atmospheric pressure 101 325 Pa at sea level; decreases with altitude. •

Absolute = gauge + atmospheric. Gas laws and depth-pressure formulas require

absolute pressure. • Pascal's principle: pressure change in a confined fluid is transmitted undiminished throughout. •

Hydraulic systems: F₂ = F₁(A₂/A₁). Force amplified by area ratio; distance reduced

by same factor; work conserved.

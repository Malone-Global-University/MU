Chapter 11 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Every substance in chemistry has a name and a formula. These are not decorative labels. They are precise communications — a kind of language chemists use to tell each other exactly what a substance is made of, how many of each atom it contains, and how those atoms are charged and arranged.

When a doctor prescribes sodium chloride, a geologist identifies calcium carbonate, or an engineer specifies dinitrogen tetroxide as a rocket propellant, they are using a naming system that chemists around the world have agreed upon. A chemist in Brazil, Japan, or Kenya can read those names and immediately know what compound is being described, because the naming rules are universal.

This chapter teaches you that language. Chemical nomenclature — the system of naming compounds — is not simply a list of terms to memorize. It is a rule-based system built on the same atomic and bonding knowledge you developed in Chapters 9 and 10. Once you understand the logic behind the rules, you can name thousands of compounds you have never seen before, and you can write a formula from a name you have never encountered.

Opening Question

A chemistry student reads a laboratory procedure calling for "iron chloride." She goes to the stockroom and finds two bottles: one labeled FeCl₂ and another labeled FeCl₃. Which one should she use? Without a Roman numeral in the name, she cannot tell. By the end of this chapter, you will understand why "iron(II) chloride" and "iron(III) chloride" are not the same compound, and you will be able to name any ionic or molecular compound precisely enough that no one could ever be confused about what you mean.

Section 11.1

Why Chemical Names and Formulas Matter

Many substances have common names that have been used for centuries. Table salt, baking soda, chalk, lye, and laughing gas are all familiar to people outside of chemistry. These names are useful in daily conversation, but they create serious problems in science. The name "lye" might refer to sodium hydroxide (NaOH) or potassium hydroxide (KOH) depending on context. The name "muriatic acid" — still found on hardware store labels for hydrochloric acid — tells you nothing about which atoms are present or how they are combined.

Systematic chemical names solve these problems. A systematic name is built according to rules. It tells you what elements are present, how many of each atom are in the formula, and whether the compound is ionic or molecular. The name carries information that any trained chemist in the world can decode.

What a Formula Communicates

The formula NaCl tells you sodium and chlorine are present in a 1:1 ratio. MgCl₂ tells you magnesium and chlorine are in a 1:2 ratio. Ca₃(PO₄)₂ tells you calcium, phosphorus, and oxygen are combined in a 3:2 ratio of calcium to phosphate groups. Formulas are essential for equation writing, stoichiometry, and laboratory work.

Two Types of Compounds, Two Sets of Rules

Ionic compounds are made of metal cations and nonmetal anions held together by electrostatic attraction. Names reflect the charges and identities of those ions.

Molecular compounds are made of nonmetal atoms sharing electrons in covalent bonds. Names use prefixes to indicate how many atoms of each element are present.

Acids are a special category treated separately in Section 11.8.

Section 11.2

Naming Binary Ionic Compounds

A binary compound contains exactly two elements. A binary ionic compound contains exactly two elements — one metal and one nonmetal — bonded ionically. The metal forms a cation; the nonmetal forms an anion.

The Naming Rule for Binary Ionic Compounds

Part 1: The name of the metal cation, written exactly as the element's name.

Part 2: The name of the nonmetal anion — take the element's name and replace the ending with the suffix -ide.

The formula subscripts are not stated in the name. The name gives element identity. The formula gives the ratio.

The -ide Suffix

ChlorineChlorideOxygenOxide
BromineBromideSulfurSulfide

Example 11.2.1
Naming binary ionic compounds from formulas
  • NaClMetal: sodium. Nonmetal: chlorine → chloride. Name: sodium chloride
  • MgOMetal: magnesium. Nonmetal: oxygen → oxide. Name: magnesium oxide
  • CaBr₂Metal: calcium. Nonmetal: bromine → bromide. Name: calcium bromide (the subscript 2 does not appear in the name)
  • Al₂O₃Metal: aluminum. Nonmetal: oxygen → oxide. Name: aluminum oxide (subscripts 2 and 3 do not appear in the name)

Writing Formulas: The Criss-Cross Method

To write a formula from a name, determine the charge of each ion, then find the subscripts that make the compound electrically neutral. The total positive charge must equal the total negative charge — this is called charge balance.

Example 11.2.2
Writing formulas from binary ionic names
  • Calcium bromideCa²⁺ and Br⁻. One Ca²⁺ (+2) needs two Br⁻ (−2 total). Formula: CaBr₂
  • Aluminum oxideAl³⁺ and O²⁻. Criss-cross: subscript on Al = 2, subscript on O = 3. Check: 2(+3) = +6 and 3(−2) = −6 → balanced Formula: Al₂O₃
  • Calcium oxideCa²⁺ and O²⁻. Criss-cross gives Ca₂O₂ — reduce to lowest ratio. Formula: CaO (not Ca₂O₂)
Always Reduce to Lowest Ratio

After applying the criss-cross method, always check whether the subscripts share a common factor. Ca₂O₂ reduces to CaO. Na₂Cl₂ reduces to NaCl. The formula of an ionic compound must be written in the lowest whole-number ratio.

Section 11.3

Naming Ionic Compounds with Transition Metals

Most main-group metals have a fixed, predictable ion charge — sodium always forms Na⁺, calcium always forms Ca²⁺. Transition metals are different. Many can form ions with more than one possible charge. Iron can form Fe²⁺ or Fe³⁺. Copper can form Cu⁺ or Cu²⁺. Without additional information in the name, a reader cannot know which iron ion is present.

The Roman Numeral System (IUPAC)

When naming a compound with a transition metal that has variable charge, write the metal's ion charge as a Roman numeral in parentheses immediately after the metal's name.

Format: Metal name (Roman numeral) + anion name

Roman NumeralIon ChargeRoman NumeralIon Charge
(I)1+(III)3+
(II)2+(IV)4+
Example 11.3.1
Naming transition metal ionic compounds
  • FeCl₂Chloride is Cl⁻. Two chloride ions = −2 total. Iron must be +2. Iron(II). Name: iron(II) chloride
  • FeCl₃Three chloride ions = −3 total. Iron must be +3. Iron(III). Name: iron(III) chloride
  • CuOOxide is O²⁻. One oxide = −2. Copper must be +2. Copper(II). Name: copper(II) oxide
  • Cu₂OOne oxide = −2. Two copper ions share +2, so each copper = +1. Copper(I). Name: copper(I) oxide

Metals That Always Have Fixed Charges — No Roman Numeral Needed

Metal or GroupAlways FormsExamples
Group 1 metals+1Na⁺, K⁺, Li⁺, Rb⁺, Cs⁺
Group 2 metals+2Mg²⁺, Ca²⁺, Ba²⁺, Sr²⁺

Do Not Add Unnecessary Roman Numerals

"Sodium(I) chloride" is incorrect. Main-group metals with fixed charges never need Roman numerals. Adding one implies the metal could have other charge states — which is misleading and wrong. The correct name is simply "sodium chloride."

Section 11.4

Polyatomic Ions

A polyatomic ion is a charged group of covalently bonded atoms that behaves as a single unit in ionic compounds. The atoms within the group are bonded together with covalent bonds, but the group as a whole carries an overall ionic charge. Polyatomic ions appear in minerals, fertilizers, medicines, industrial chemicals, biological molecules, cleaning products, and foods. Learning them is essential for naming and formula writing throughout the rest of your chemistry course.

The -ate and -ite Pattern

When two related ions differ only in oxygen content, the one with more oxygen ends in -ate (nitrate: NO₃⁻) and the one with less oxygen ends in -ite (nitrite: NO₂⁻). This pattern applies to sulfate/sulfite, chlorate/chlorite, phosphate, and others. These polyatomic ions must be memorized — there is no shortcut for deriving them from rules at this level.

Section 11.5

Naming Compounds with Polyatomic Ions

The Naming Rule

When a polyatomic ion appears in a compound, its name is used directly — exactly as it appears on the reference list — without adding any suffix or modifying the name.

Format: Cation name (+ Roman numeral if needed) + polyatomic ion name

The subscript on a polyatomic ion is never spoken in the name.

Example 11.5.1
Naming compounds with polyatomic ions
  • NaNO₃Sodium (Na⁺, fixed). Nitrate (NO₃⁻). Name: sodium nitrate
  • Ca(OH)₂Calcium (Ca²⁺, fixed). Hydroxide (OH⁻). Name: calcium hydroxide — the subscript 2 is not "dihydroxide"
  • (NH₄)₂SO₄Ammonium (NH₄⁺). Sulfate (SO₄²⁻). Name: ammonium sulfate
  • Fe₂(SO₄)₃Three sulfate ions = −6 total. Two iron ions must provide +6, so each Fe = +3. Iron(III). Name: iron(III) sulfate
  • Mg(NO₂)₂Magnesium (Mg²⁺). Nitrite (NO₂⁻). Name: magnesium nitrite
Example 11.5.2
Writing formulas from names with polyatomic ions
  • Calcium phosphateCa²⁺ and PO₄³⁻. Criss-cross: 3 Ca²⁺ and 2 PO₄³⁻. Check: 3(+2) = +6 and 2(−3) = −6 → balanced Formula: Ca₃(PO₄)₂
  • Ammonium sulfateNH₄⁺ (+1) and SO₄²⁻ (−2). Two ammonium needed. Formula: (NH₄)₂SO₄
Use Parentheses for Polyatomic Ions with Subscripts Greater Than 1

When a polyatomic ion needs a subscript of 2 or more, the entire ion must be enclosed in parentheses. Writing Ca(OH)₂ is correct. Writing CaOH₂ is incorrect because it suggests the subscript applies only to hydrogen, not to the entire hydroxide group.

Section 11.6

Naming Binary Molecular Compounds

Binary molecular compounds contain two nonmetal elements bonded covalently. Unlike ionic compounds, they do not form from charges — they form from shared electrons. Because there are no charges to balance, the names of molecular compounds must explicitly state how many atoms of each element are in the molecule. This is done using Greek numerical prefixes.

The Greek Prefix System

NumberPrefixNumberPrefix
1mono-6hexa-
2di-7hepta-
3tri-8octa-
4tetra-9nona-
5penta-10deca-
The Naming Rule for Binary Molecular Compounds

Part 1: First element is named with its element name, with a prefix for the count. Mono- is not used for the first element.

Part 2: Second element is named with an -ide suffix, with a prefix for the count. Mono- is used for the second element if there is only one atom.

When a prefix ending in a vowel precedes an element name beginning with a vowel, drop the prefix's final vowel: mono + oxide → monoxide (not monooxide).

Example 11.6.1
Naming binary molecular compounds
  • COCarbon (no mono for first element) + one oxygen = monoxide. Name: carbon monoxide
  • CO₂Carbon + two oxygens = dioxide. Name: carbon dioxide
  • N₂O₄Two nitrogens = dinitrogen. Four oxygens = tetroxide. Name: dinitrogen tetroxide
  • PCl₃Phosphorus (no mono) + three chlorines = trichloride. Name: phosphorus trichloride
  • PCl₅Phosphorus + five chlorines = pentachloride. Name: phosphorus pentachloride
Note: PCl₃ and PCl₅ are different compounds. Without prefixes, you could not tell them apart — which is exactly why the prefix system exists.
Example 11.6.2
Writing formulas from molecular names
  • Dinitrogen monoxideDi = 2 nitrogens. Mono = 1 oxygen. Formula: N₂O
  • Sulfur hexafluorideNo prefix on sulfur = 1. Hexa = 6 fluorines. Formula: SF₆
  • Tetraphosphorus decoxideTetra = 4 phosphorus. Deca = 10 oxygens. Formula: P₄O₁₀
Section 11.7

Writing Formulas from Names

Writing formulas from names requires you to first identify which type of compound the name describes, and then apply the appropriate procedure. Reading the name carefully always comes first.

Step 1 — Identify the Compound Type

If the name ends in -ide and contains a metal name with no Greek prefix → likely a binary ionic compound.

If the name contains a polyatomic ion name (nitrate, sulfate, hydroxide, phosphate, ammonium, etc.) → ionic compound with a polyatomic ion.

If the name contains Greek prefixes (di-, tri-, tetra-…) or both elements are clearly nonmetals → molecular compound.

If the name ends in acid with a hydro- prefix or ends in -ic acid / -ous acid without hydro- → acid (see Section 11.8).

Example 11.7.1
Mixed formula-writing practice
  • Potassium sulfideBinary ionic. K⁺ and S²⁻. Two K⁺ balance one S²⁻. Formula: K₂S
  • Iron(III) phosphateIonic with polyatomic ion. Fe³⁺ and PO₄³⁻. One of each. Formula: FePO₄
  • Ammonium carbonateIonic with polyatomic ions. NH₄⁺ (+1) and CO₃²⁻ (−2). Two ammonium needed. Formula: (NH₄)₂CO₃
  • Diphosphorus pentoxideMolecular. Di = 2, penta = 5. Formula: P₂O₅
  • Copper(II) nitrateCu²⁺ and NO₃⁻ (−1). Two nitrate needed to balance Cu²⁺. Formula: Cu(NO₃)₂

Section 11.8

Naming Acids

Acids are compounds that release hydrogen ions (H⁺) when dissolved in water. In their formulas, hydrogen is written first. Acids have their own naming rules that are distinct from both ionic and molecular compounds. At this level, you will learn to name two types: binary acids (hydrogen + one nonmetal) and oxyacids (hydrogen + a polyatomic ion containing oxygen).

Binary Acids

Binary Acid Naming Rule

1. Start with the prefix hydro-

2. Take the nonmetal's name, change the ending to -ic

3. Add the word acid

Note: HCl gas is named "hydrogen chloride." HCl dissolved in water — labeled (aq) — is "hydrochloric acid."

Oxyacid Naming Rule

If the polyatomic anion ends in -ate → the acid name ends in -ic acid

Section 11.10

Avoiding Naming Errors

Nomenclature errors are among the most common mistakes in introductory chemistry. Most fall into a small number of recognizable categories.

Error 1 — Mixing up ionic and molecular naming rules
CO₂ named as "carbon(IV) oxide"

Correct: "carbon dioxide." CO₂ is molecular — two nonmetals. Use Greek prefixes, not Roman numerals.

Error 2 — Using prefixes on ionic compounds
MgCl₂ named as "dimagnesium chloride"

Correct: "magnesium chloride." Ionic compounds never use Greek prefixes. Subscripts are determined by charge balance, not named.

Error 3 — Forgetting the Roman numeral for variable-charge metals
FeCl₃ named as "iron chloride"

Correct: "iron(III) chloride." When a transition metal with variable charge appears, you must determine and include the Roman numeral.

Error 4 — Adding an unnecessary Roman numeral
NaCl named as "sodium(I) chloride"

Correct: "sodium chloride." Main-group metals with fixed charges never need Roman numerals. Adding one is misleading.

Error 5 — Forgetting parentheses for polyatomic ions with subscripts
Ca(OH)₂ written as CaOH₂

Correct: Ca(OH)₂. Parentheses group the entire polyatomic ion so the subscript applies to the whole group, not just the last element.

Error 6 — Confusing -ate and -ite ions
Calling NaNO₂ "sodium nitrate" (it is sodium nitrite)

Correct: Nitrate is NO₃⁻; nitrite is NO₂⁻. The -ate/-ite distinction must be memorized carefully for every pair.

Error 7 — Confusing -ic and -ous acid names
Calling both HNO₃ and HNO₂ "nitric acid"

Correct: HNO₃ is nitric acid (-ate anion → -ic acid). HNO₂ is nitrous acid (-ite anion → -ous acid). The acid naming system depends on the -ate/-ite distinction.

Error 8 — Not reducing formulas to lowest whole-number ratios
Calcium oxide written as Ca₂O₂

Correct: CaO. After applying the criss-cross method, always check whether subscripts share a common factor and reduce them.

Chapter 11 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 11.1Systematic chemical names are built by rules that communicate which elements are present, their ratios, and whether the compound is ionic or molecular. Common names lack this structural information.
  • Sec 11.2Binary ionic compounds are named: metal cation name + nonmetal anion name with -ide suffix. Subscripts are determined by charge balance using the criss-cross method, always reduced to lowest ratio. Subscripts are not stated in the name.
  • Sec 11.3Transition metals with variable charge require a Roman numeral in parentheses after the metal's name. The Roman numeral is determined by working backward from the anion's charge and the formula's subscripts. Fixed-charge metals never need Roman numerals.
  • Sec 11.4Polyatomic ions are charged groups of covalently bonded atoms that act as a single unit. They must be memorized by name, formula, and charge. The -ate/-ite pattern indicates relative oxygen content within related pairs.
  • Sec 11.5Polyatomic ion names are used directly in compound names without modification. Subscripts greater than 1 require parentheses around the entire polyatomic ion. Subscripts are determined by charge balance.
  • Sec 11.6Binary molecular compounds use Greek prefixes to state the number of each atom. Mono- is skipped for the first element but used for the second. Subscripts in the formula translate directly to prefixes in the name.
  • Sec 11.7Formula writing always begins by identifying compound type from the name. Binary ionic, polyatomic ionic, and molecular compounds each follow distinct procedures. Compound type must be identified before any rule is applied.
  • Sec 11.8Binary acids use the hydro- prefix and -ic acid suffix. Oxyacids are named based on the anion: -ate anions give -ic acid names; -ite anions give -ous acid names. Hydro- is never used for oxyacids.
  • Sec 11.9Many compounds retain historical common names. Knowing both common and systematic names — and the formulas they represent — is necessary for working in any chemistry context.
  • Sec 11.10Eight recurring errors are identified: mixing ionic and molecular rules, using prefixes on ionic compounds, missing or adding unnecessary Roman numerals, forgetting parentheses, confusing -ate/-ite, confusing -ic/-ous acids, and failing to reduce formulas.

Key Terms Review

acid
A compound that produces hydrogen ions (H⁺) when dissolved in water. Named using conventions distinct from ionic and molecular compounds.
binary acid
An acid containing hydrogen and one nonmetal. Named using the prefix hydro- and the suffix -ic acid. Requires (aq) label to distinguish from the pure gas.
binary compound
A compound containing exactly two elements.
binary ionic compound
An ionic compound containing exactly two elements — one metal and one nonmetal — bonded ionically.
binary molecular compound
A molecular compound containing exactly two nonmetal elements bonded covalently. Named using Greek prefixes.
charge balance
The condition in which total positive charge equals total negative charge in an ionic compound, giving it no net charge.
chemical formula
A representation of a compound using element symbols and subscript numbers to show the types and ratios of atoms present.
chemical nomenclature
The systematic, rule-based method used to name chemical compounds so that any trained chemist can determine composition from the name.
criss-cross method
A shortcut for finding ionic formula subscripts by swapping the absolute values of the ion charges, then reducing to lowest ratio.
-ide suffix
The ending added to a nonmetal's name when it forms a simple anion. Chlorine → chloride; oxygen → oxide; sulfur → sulfide.
ionic compound
A compound formed from the attraction between positively charged cations and negatively charged anions, typically metal and nonmetal.
molecular compound
A compound formed when nonmetal atoms share electrons through covalent bonds. Named using Greek prefixes rather than charge-based rules.
oxyacid
An acid containing hydrogen, oxygen, and another element (as a polyatomic oxyanion). Named based on whether the anion ends in -ate or -ite.
polyatomic ion
A charged group of covalently bonded atoms that acts as a single unit in ionic bonding. Must be memorized by name, formula, and charge.
Roman numeral system
The IUPAC method of indicating the charge of a variable-charge metal cation by placing the charge in Roman numerals in parentheses after the metal's name.
systematic name
A chemically precise name built according to IUPAC rules, as opposed to a common or trivial name.
trivial name
A traditional or informal name for a compound that does not follow systematic naming rules (for example, "baking soda" for sodium hydrogen carbonate).
variable charge
The property of transition metals that allows them to form cations of more than one charge, requiring Roman numerals in compound names.

Naming Rule Review

Binary Ionic Compounds — fixed-charge metal

Name: metal name + nonmetal name with -ide suffix

Formula: determine subscripts from charge balance; always reduce to lowest ratio

Binary Ionic Compounds — variable-charge metal

Name: metal name + (Roman numeral) + nonmetal name with -ide suffix

Roman numeral: determined by working backward from the anion's total charge

Ionic Compounds with Polyatomic Ions

Name: cation name (+ Roman numeral if needed) + polyatomic ion name (unchanged)

Formula: charge balance; use parentheses when polyatomic ion subscript > 1

Binary Molecular Compounds

Name: (prefix)first element + (prefix)second element with -ide suffix

Prefixes: mono, di, tri, tetra, penta, hexa, hepta, octa, nona, deca

Drop mono- for the first element; keep mono- for the second element

Binary Acids

Name: hydro- + nonmetal stem + -ic acid

Formula: H followed by the nonmetal symbol, labeled (aq)

Oxyacids

Anion ends in -ate → acid ends in -ic acid

Anion ends in -ite → acid ends in -ous acid

No hydro- prefix for oxyacids

Name a compound with a polyatomic ion — Pb(NO₃)₂
  • Step 1Lead (Pb) is a transition metal with variable charge.
  • Step 2Nitrate is NO₃⁻ (charge −1). Two nitrate ions = −2 total.
  • Step 3Lead must be +2 to balance: Pb²⁺. Roman numeral: (II).
Answer: lead(II) nitrate
Worked Example 4
Name a binary molecular compound — N₂O₅
  • Step 1Two nonmetals: nitrogen and oxygen. Binary molecular compound.
  • Step 2Two nitrogens = di-; five oxygens = penta-.
Answer: dinitrogen pentoxide
Worked Example 5
Name an oxyacid — H₂SO₃
  • Step 1Remove hydrogens to identify the anion: SO₃²⁻.
  • Step 2Anion name: sulfite (ends in -ite).
  • Step 3-ite anion → -ous acid.
Answer: sulfurous acid

Looking Ahead
Chapter 12: Molecular Geometry and Intermolecular Forces

In Chapter 11, you learned to name and write formulas for ionic and molecular compounds. You now know the symbols and ratios — but molecules are not flat. They occupy three-dimensional space. The arrangement of atoms in space determines a molecule's shape, and shape determines properties.

Chapter 12 will show you why molecules are three-dimensional, how electron pairs push atoms into predictable shapes, and how molecular shape connects to polarity, boiling point, solubility, biological function, and material design. The Lewis structures you practiced in Chapter 10 will become the starting point for understanding molecular geometry in Chapter 12.

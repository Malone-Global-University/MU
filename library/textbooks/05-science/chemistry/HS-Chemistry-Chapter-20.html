Chapter 20 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Every chemical reaction involves energy. A candle releases heat and light as it burns. A cold pack absorbs heat when its chemicals dissolve. A car engine converts chemical energy in fuel into mechanical motion. A battery stores chemical energy and releases it as electricity. Thermochemistry is the branch of chemistry that studies these energy changes — measuring them, explaining them, and predicting them.

In this chapter, you will learn how chemists think about energy, heat, and temperature. You will explore what it means for a reaction to be exothermic or endothermic, how scientists measure energy changes using calorimeters, and how enthalpy is used to describe the heat content of chemical systems. You will also practice calculations involving specific heat and calorimetry, and connect thermochemical ideas to fuels, food, industry, and environmental chemistry.

Opening Question

When you hold a piece of ice, your hand gets cold and the ice begins to melt. When you light a match, heat rushes outward. Both events involve energy moving between chemicals and their surroundings — but in opposite directions. By the end of this chapter, you will know how to measure that energy flow, calculate how much heat is involved, and explain why some reactions release energy while others absorb it.

Section 20.1

Energy in Chemical Systems

What Energy Is

Energy is the capacity to do work or transfer heat. It is not a substance you can hold or see — it is a property of matter in motion or matter in certain arrangements. A stretched rubber band has energy. A moving car has energy. A chemical bond between two atoms has energy. A warm cup of water has more energy than a cold cup of the same water.

Chemists care about energy because chemical reactions always involve changes in energy. Bonds break and form during reactions, and these events release or absorb energy. Understanding these energy changes allows chemists to predict how reactions behave, design efficient industrial processes, develop better fuels, understand metabolism, and assess environmental consequences.

Forms of Energy Relevant to Chemistry

Form of EnergyDescriptionChemistry Example
Kinetic energyEnergy of motion; at the particle level, atoms and molecules vibrate, rotate, and translate through spaceFaster-moving particles = higher temperature
Chemical potential energyEnergy stored in chemical bonds and in the arrangement of atoms relative to one anotherEnergy stored in a molecule of glucose or methane
Thermal energyTotal kinetic energy of all particles in a sample; depends on both temperature and amount of matterA bathtub of warm water holds more thermal energy than a cup of boiling water
Law of Conservation of Energy

Energy cannot be created or destroyed. It can only be converted from one form to another or transferred from one place to another. This is sometimes called the first law of thermodynamics. When a reaction releases energy, that energy does not disappear — it flows into the surroundings. Whatever energy the chemicals lose, the surroundings gain, and vice versa. The total energy of the universe remains constant.

Section 20.2

Heat and Temperature

Two of the most commonly confused concepts in chemistry are heat and temperature. They are related, but they are not the same thing.

Critical Distinction

Temperature is a measure of the average kinetic energy of particles — how fast they move on average. It is an intensive property (does not depend on amount). Heat is the transfer of thermal energy between objects at different temperatures. It is an extensive property (depends on amount and temperature change). A small flame and a bonfire can be at the same temperature, yet the bonfire contains enormously more total energy.

PropertyTemperatureHeat
What it measuresAverage kinetic energy of particlesTransfer of thermal energy between objects
Type of propertyIntensive (independent of amount)Extensive (depends on mass and ΔT)
SI unitKelvin (K)Joule (J)
DirectionNot directionalAlways flows from warmer to cooler

Units of Heat Energy

In chemistry, heat is measured in joules (J) or kilojoules (kJ). One kilojoule equals 1,000 joules. An older unit, the calorie (cal), is the amount of heat needed to raise the temperature of one gram of water by one degree Celsius. The relationship is:

1 cal = 4.184 J     1 kcal = 1,000 cal = 4,184 J

The kilocalorie (kcal), or "Calorie" (capital C) on food labels, equals 1,000 small calories. A food label listing 200 Calories means 200 kilocalories — approximately 836,800 joules of chemical energy. In chemistry class, you will work primarily in joules and kilojoules.

Kelvin Conversion

The Kelvin scale begins at absolute zero — the theoretical temperature at which particle motion essentially stops. Absolute zero is −273.15°C. All Kelvin temperatures are positive numbers.

Section 20.3

Systems and Surroundings

To analyze energy changes clearly, chemists draw a conceptual boundary around the part of the universe they are studying. The system is the specific chemicals or objects being studied — the reactants and products of a reaction, for example. Everything outside the system is the surroundings: the reaction vessel, the air, the lab bench, the water in a calorimeter — essentially everything else.

This distinction is not just bookkeeping. It allows chemists to track energy flow precisely: energy leaving the system enters the surroundings, and energy entering the system comes from the surroundings.

System TypeExchanges Matter?Exchanges Energy?Example
Open systemYesYesPot of boiling soup — steam escapes, heat flows to room
Closed systemNoYesSealed flask being heated
Isolated systemNoNoPerfectly insulated thermos (theoretical ideal)
Sign Convention for Energy Transfer

Chemists use the system's perspective to assign signs to energy changes:

Positive (+) — energy flows into the system. The system gains energy.

Negative (−) — energy flows out of the system. The system loses energy.

If a reaction releases heat into the surroundings, the reaction system loses energy: ΔH is negative.

Section 20.4

Exothermic and Endothermic Processes

Exothermic Processes

A process is exothermic if it releases energy to the surroundings. In an exothermic process, the system loses energy, and that energy flows into the surroundings as heat. The result: the surroundings get warmer. If you touch a flask where an exothermic reaction is occurring, you will feel it heat up.

In an exothermic process, the chemical potential energy stored in the bonds of the reactants is greater than the energy stored in the bonds of the products. The excess energy is released as heat.

Exothermic ProcessWhat Releases EnergyWhere You Encounter It
Combustion of fuelsBreaking C–H and C–C bonds; forming CO₂ and H₂O bondsStoves, furnaces, engines, campfires
Rusting of ironSlow oxidation of iron by oxygenCorroding metal surfaces
Neutralization reactionsAcid + base → salt + waterAntacids, industrial pH control
Condensation of water vaporGas → liquid phase transitionSteam burns, dew formation
Cellular respirationBreakdown of glucose in living cellsBody heat, muscle energy
Dissolving calcium chlorideIon–water interactions release energyInstant hand warmers

Endothermic Processes

A process is endothermic if it absorbs energy from the surroundings. In an endothermic process, the system gains energy, and the surroundings lose energy. The result: the surroundings get cooler. The flask will feel cold to the touch.

In an endothermic process, the chemical potential energy stored in the products is greater than in the reactants. The extra energy must come from the surroundings.

Endothermic ProcessWhat Absorbs EnergyWhere You Encounter It
Melting of iceBreaking hydrogen bonds in ice crystal latticeIce packs, cooling drinks
Evaporation of waterBreaking intermolecular attractions in liquidSweating, evaporative cooling
PhotosynthesisLight energy absorbed to convert CO₂ + H₂O → glucoseAll green plants
Dissolving ammonium nitrateBreaking ion–ion attractions requires more energy than ion–water interactions releaseInstant cold packs
Cooking an eggProtein denaturation requires continuous energyCooking and baking
Decomposition reactionsBreaking stable bonds requires energy inputElectrolysis of water, thermal decomposition

Comparing Exothermic and Endothermic

FeatureExothermicEndothermic
Energy flow directionSystem → SurroundingsSurroundings → System
Temperature of surroundingsIncreases (surroundings feel warm)Decreases (surroundings feel cool)

Common Misconception

Students often think that endothermic processes are "cold" and that exothermic processes are "hot." The temperature change you observe is in the surroundings, not the system itself. What defines a process as exo- or endothermic is the direction of energy flow. A reaction can be endothermic even at very high temperatures — what matters is whether it absorbs energy from its surroundings as it proceeds.

Section 20.5

Specific Heat

Not all substances warm up at the same rate when you add heat. A metal spoon left in a pot of hot soup heats up very quickly and can burn your hand. The soup itself takes much longer to reach the same temperature, even though the spoon and soup are in contact with the same heat source. This difference arises because different materials have different specific heats.

The specific heat capacity (usually called specific heat, written c) of a substance is the amount of heat required to raise the temperature of one gram of that substance by one degree Celsius (or one Kelvin). Specific heat is measured in:

c = J / (g · °C)
Why Water Is Special

Water has an unusually high specific heat: 4.184 J/g·°C. It takes more than ten times as much energy to warm water as to warm the same mass of gold. This is why oceans and large lakes moderate climate — they absorb enormous amounts of heat without changing temperature dramatically. It is also why water is used as a coolant in car engines and industrial reactors.

SubstanceSpecific Heat (J/g·°C)

The Specific Heat Equation

q = mcΔT

q = heat transferred (joules, J)

m = mass of the substance (grams, g)

c = specific heat capacity (J/g·°C)

ΔT = change in temperature = Tfinal − Tinitial (°C)

If ΔT is positive, the substance was heated and q is positive (gained heat). If ΔT is negative, the substance was cooled and q is negative (lost heat).

Worked Example 20.1
Calculating Heat Absorbed by Water
  • ProblemHow much heat is required to warm 250.0 g of water from 20.0°C to 85.0°C?
  • Givenm = 250.0 g  |  c = 4.184 J/g·°C (water)  |  Tinitial = 20.0°C  |  Tfinal = 85.0°C
  • Step 1 ΔT = T_final − T_initial = 85.0°C − 20.0°C = 65.0°C
  • Step 2 q = mcΔT q = (250.0 g)(4.184 J/g·°C)(65.0°C) q = 68,000 J = 68.0 kJ
  • CheckHeating 250 g of water through 65°C is a large change — 68 kJ is reasonable. The sign is positive because the water is gaining heat. ✓
Answer: 68.0 kJ of heat must be added to warm the water.
Worked Example 20.2
Calculating Final Temperature
  • ProblemA 150.0 g piece of iron (c = 0.449 J/g·°C) at 25.0°C absorbs 2,500 J of heat. What is its final temperature?
  • Givenm = 150.0 g  |  c = 0.449 J/g·°C  |  Tinitial = 25.0°C  |  q = 2,500 J
  • Step 1Solve for ΔT by rearranging q = mcΔT: ΔT = q / (mc) = 2500 J / [(150.0 g)(0.449 J/g·°C)] ΔT = 2500 / 67.35 = 37.1°C
  • Step 2 T_final = T_initial + ΔT = 25.0°C + 37.1°C = 62.1°C
  • CheckIron has a low specific heat, so 2,500 J raises it by 37°C — much more than water would be raised by the same heat. Reasonable. ✓
Answer: The iron reaches a final temperature of 62.1°C.
Worked Example 20.3
Finding Specific Heat from Experimental Data
  • ProblemA 200.0 g sample of an unknown metal is heated to 95.0°C and placed in 100.0 g of water at 22.0°C. The final temperature of both metal and water is 31.4°C. What is the specific heat of the metal? (Assume no heat loss to surroundings.)
  • ReasoningThe hot metal loses heat; the cool water gains heat. Since no heat escapes: qmetal = −qwater
  • Water q_water = m_w · c_w · ΔT_w = (100.0 g)(4.184 J/g·°C)(31.4 − 22.0)°C = (100.0)(4.184)(9.4) = 3,933 J
  • Metal q_metal = −3,933 J −3,933 = (200.0)(c_metal)(31.4 − 95.0) −3,933 = (200.0)(c_metal)(−63.6) c_metal = −3,933 / −12,720 = 0.309 J/g·°C
  • CheckThe value 0.309 J/g·°C falls between copper (0.385) and gold (0.128). This is close to the specific heat of several metals. More precise measurements would allow identification. ✓
Answer: The specific heat of the metal is approximately 0.309 J/g·°C.

Section 20.6

Calorimetry

A calorimeter is a device used to measure heat transfer during chemical or physical processes. In calorimetry, the heat released or absorbed by a process is measured by observing the temperature change of a surrounding substance — usually water or aqueous solution.

The central assumption in calorimetry is that all the heat released by the reaction is absorbed by the calorimeter contents, and no heat is lost to the outside environment. Real calorimeters are imperfect insulators, but simple coffee cup calorimeters used in high school chemistry give good approximate measurements.

The Coffee Cup Calorimeter

The simplest educational calorimeter is two nested polystyrene (foam) cups with a loose lid and a thermometer. Polystyrene is a good heat insulator. This design is used for reactions in aqueous solution — dissolving a salt, neutralizing an acid with a base, or running a simple double-replacement reaction. These reactions occur at constant atmospheric pressure, making this type of calorimetry compatible with measuring enthalpy changes.

Coffee Cup Calorimeter Procedure

1. Measure the initial temperature of the solution.

2. Add the second reactant or solute and stir carefully.

3. Record the maximum (or minimum) temperature reached.

4. Calculate: q_solution = mcΔT (using total mass of solution)

5. Since the solution gains what the reaction releases: q_rxn = −q_solution

Worked Example 20.4
Coffee Cup Calorimetry — Neutralization
  • Problem50.0 mL of 1.0 M HCl and 50.0 mL of 1.0 M NaOH are mixed in a coffee cup calorimeter. Both solutions start at 22.5°C. After mixing, the temperature rises to 29.3°C. Density = 1.00 g/mL; c = 4.18 J/g·°C. Calculate the heat released by the neutralization reaction.
  • GivenTotal volume = 100.0 mL  |  m = 100.0 g  |  c = 4.18 J/g·°C  |  Ti = 22.5°C  |  Tf = 29.3°C
  • Step 1 ΔT = 29.3°C − 22.5°C = 6.8°C
  • Step 2 q_solution = mcΔT = (100.0 g)(4.18 J/g·°C)(6.8°C) = 2,842 J ≈ 2.84 kJ
  • Step 3The solution temperature increased — the solution absorbed heat from the reaction. Therefore the reaction released heat. q_rxn = −q_solution = −2.84 kJ
  • InterpretThe negative sign confirms the reaction is exothermic. The neutralization of HCl and NaOH releases energy to the solution. ✓
Answer: q_rxn = −2.84 kJ. The reaction is exothermic.

The Bomb Calorimeter

For combustion reactions and other high-energy processes, chemists use a bomb calorimeter (constant-volume calorimeter). The reaction occurs inside a sealed, heavy-walled metal container (the "bomb") submerged in a known mass of water. Bomb calorimeters can withstand the high pressures generated by combustion. They are used to measure the caloric content of fuels and foods — the kilocalorie values on nutrition labels were originally determined by burning food samples in bomb calorimeters.

The calculation uses the heat capacity of the calorimeter (Ccal, in J/°C or kJ/°C), determined by calibration with a substance of known energy content:

q_rxn = −C_cal × ΔT

Section 20.7

Enthalpy Change

In chemistry, most reactions occur in open containers at constant atmospheric pressure. Chemists use a quantity called enthalpy (H) to describe the heat content of a system at constant pressure. Enthalpy itself cannot be directly measured, but the change in enthalpy (ΔH) can be measured through calorimetry:

Enthalpy Change

ΔH = H_products − H_reactants

ΔH negative (−) → Products have less enthalpy than reactants → heat was released → exothermic

ΔH positive (+) → Products have more enthalpy than reactants → heat was absorbed → endothermic

Units: kJ or kJ/mol (kilojoules per mole of reaction)

Thermochemical Equations

A thermochemical equation is a balanced chemical equation that includes the enthalpy change for the reaction. The ΔH value is written after the equation.

ReactionThermochemical EquationInterpretation
Combustion of methaneCH₄(g) + 2O₂(g) → CO₂(g) + 2H₂O(l)   ΔH = −890 kJExothermic; 890 kJ released when 1 mol CH₄ burns
Decomposition of water2H₂O(l) → 2H₂(g) + O₂(g)   ΔH = +572 kJEndothermic; 572 kJ must be supplied to decompose 2 mol H₂O
Dissolving ammonium nitrateNH₄NO₃(s) → NH₄⁺(aq) + NO₃⁻(aq)   ΔH = +25.7 kJEndothermic; solution cools; used in instant cold packs
Formation of water from elements2H₂(g) + O₂(g) → 2H₂O(l)   ΔH = −572 kJExothermic; reverse of decomposition
Enthalpy as a State Function

Enthalpy is a state function — its value depends only on the current state of the system, not on the path taken to reach that state. This means if a reaction occurs by two different routes ending at the same products, the total ΔH is the same. This property (known as Hess's Law) allows chemists to calculate ΔH for reactions that are difficult to measure directly by combining ΔH values of reactions that can be measured.

Standard Enthalpy of Formation

The standard enthalpy of formation (ΔH°f) of a compound is the enthalpy change when one mole of that compound is formed from its elements in their standard states at 25°C and 1 atm. By convention, ΔH°f for any element in its standard state is zero.

CompoundΔH°f (kJ/mol)
H₂O(l)−285.8
H₂O(g)−241.8
CO₂(g)−393.5
NaCl(s)−411.2
NH₃(g)−46.1

Section 20.8

Energy Diagrams

An energy diagram (also called an enthalpy diagram or reaction coordinate diagram) is a graph that shows how the energy of a system changes as a reaction proceeds. The horizontal axis represents the reaction coordinate — the progress of the reaction from reactants to products. The vertical axis represents potential energy or enthalpy.

Features of an Energy Diagram

FeatureLocation on DiagramWhat It Tells You
ReactantsLeft side, at a certain energy levelStarting energy of the system
ProductsRight side, at a different energy levelEnding energy of the system
Transition statePeak of the energy curveHighest-energy arrangement of atoms during the reaction; exists momentarily
Activation energy (Ea)Height from reactant level to peakMinimum energy particles must have to react; determines reaction speed
ΔHDifference between reactant and product levelsOverall energy change; negative = exothermic, positive = endothermic

Exothermic Reaction Diagram

In an exothermic reaction, the products are at a lower energy level than the reactants. The curve rises (activation energy hump) and then drops to a lower level. The difference in height between reactants and products is |ΔH|, and that energy is released to the surroundings.

Energy (kJ) | | /\ ← transition state (peak) | / \ |______/ \________ ← products (lower than reactants) |↑ reactants | |__________________________ Reaction Progress → ΔH is NEGATIVE (energy released) E_a = height from reactants to peak Products are LOWER than reactants

Endothermic Reaction Diagram

In an endothermic reaction, the products are at a higher energy level than the reactants. The curve still shows an activation energy hump, but the final product level is higher than the starting reactant level. Energy must flow in from the surroundings for the reaction to proceed.

Energy (kJ) | /\ ← transition state (peak) | / \_____ ← products (HIGHER than reactants) | / |____________/ ← reactants | |__________________________ Reaction Progress → ΔH is POSITIVE (energy absorbed) E_a = height from reactants to peak Products are HIGHER than reactants
Reading an Energy Diagram — Four Questions

1. Exo or endo? Products lower than reactants → exothermic. Products higher → endothermic.

2. How large is ΔH? Vertical distance between reactant and product energy levels.

3. How large is Ea? Vertical distance from reactant level to peak. Large Ea → slow reaction.

4. Effect of a catalyst? A catalyst lowers the peak (reduces Ea) without changing the reactant or product energy levels. ΔH is unchanged; only the height of the barrier changes.

Section 20.9

Thermochemistry in Fuels, Food, and Industry

Fuels and Combustion Energy

Fuels are substances that release large amounts of energy through combustion. The energy density of a fuel — the amount of energy released per gram — determines how useful it is. All complete combustion reactions of hydrocarbon fuels (containing only carbon and hydrogen) produce carbon dioxide and water:

fuel + O₂(g) → CO₂(g) + H₂O + energy

Incomplete combustion — when oxygen supply is insufficient — also produces carbon monoxide (CO) and soot (carbon particles), both of which are harmful to human health and the environment.

Why Hydrogen Is Attractive — and Challenging

Hydrogen has the highest energy density by mass of any fuel. This makes it attractive for space exploration and fuel cell vehicles. However, hydrogen is a gas at room temperature, making it difficult to store and transport safely in large quantities. Gasoline and natural gas are much easier to handle, which is why they dominate transportation and heating despite their environmental consequences.

Food as Chemical Fuel

Your body runs on the same thermochemical principles as an engine, just at lower temperatures and with far more sophisticated control. Food contains chemical potential energy stored in the bonds of carbohydrates, fats, and proteins. When you digest and metabolize food, your cells break these molecules down through cellular respiration — releasing energy to power muscle contractions, nerve signals, protein synthesis, and temperature regulation.

The energy content of food is measured in kilocalories (kcal), called "Calories" (capital C) on nutrition labels. These values are determined by burning food samples in bomb calorimeters.

NutrientEnergy per GramChemical Storage Form
Carbohydrates4 kcal/g (≈17 kJ/g)Sugars and starches; glucose is the primary cellular fuel
Proteins4 kcal/g (≈17 kJ/g)Amino acid chains; used primarily for building, not fuel
Fats (lipids)9 kcal/g (≈38 kJ/g)Long-chain fatty acids; most efficient long-term energy storage
Alcohol (ethanol)7 kcal/g (≈29 kJ/g)Metabolized primarily by the liver

Fats store more than twice as much energy per gram as carbohydrates or proteins. This is why fat is an efficient long-term energy storage molecule — you can store a lot of energy in relatively little mass. Your basal metabolic rate is the minimum energy your body needs to maintain basic functions at rest — roughly 1,500–2,000 kcal per day for an average adult. Physical activity requires additional energy beyond this baseline.

Industrial Thermochemistry

Industrial chemistry depends heavily on thermochemical principles. Designing a manufacturing process requires knowing not just whether a reaction occurs, but how much energy it requires or releases. Energy costs are often the largest expense in industrial chemical production.

Energy Storage and Climate

As society moves toward renewable energy sources, energy storage becomes critically important — the sun doesn't always shine and the wind doesn't always blow. Thermochemistry is central to developing better batteries, hydrogen fuel systems, and thermal storage materials.

Combustion of fossil fuels releases CO₂ that has been stored in the earth for millions of years, returning it to the atmosphere. The thermochemistry of CO₂ in the atmosphere — how it absorbs and re-emits infrared radiation — is the chemical basis of the greenhouse effect and climate change. Understanding thermochemistry does not resolve political debates about energy policy, but it gives you the chemical literacy to evaluate evidence, understand tradeoffs, and think clearly about real constraints and possibilities.

Chapter 20 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 20.1Energy is the capacity to do work or transfer heat. Chemical reactions always involve energy changes because bonds break and form. Kinetic energy is energy of motion; chemical potential energy is stored in bonds; thermal energy is the total kinetic energy of all particles in a sample. The law of conservation of energy states that energy cannot be created or destroyed.
  • Sec 20.2Temperature measures the average kinetic energy of particles (intensive). Heat is the transfer of thermal energy between objects at different temperatures (extensive). Heat is measured in joules (J) or kilojoules (kJ). The Kelvin scale begins at absolute zero: K = °C + 273.15. 1 cal = 4.184 J; 1 kcal = 1,000 cal (the "Calorie" on nutrition labels).
  • Sec 20.3The system is the chemicals being studied; the surroundings are everything else. Energy flowing into the system is positive (+); energy flowing out is negative (−). Open systems exchange both matter and energy; closed systems exchange only energy; isolated systems exchange neither.
  • Sec 20.4Exothermic processes release energy to the surroundings (surroundings get warmer; ΔH negative). Examples include combustion, rusting, neutralization, condensation, and cellular respiration. Endothermic processes absorb energy from the surroundings (surroundings get cooler; ΔH positive). Examples include melting, evaporation, photosynthesis, and dissolving ammonium nitrate.
  • Sec 20.5Specific heat capacity (c) is the heat required to raise the temperature of one gram of a substance by one degree Celsius. Water's high specific heat (4.184 J/g·°C) makes it an exceptional coolant and climate regulator. The equation q = mcΔT relates heat (q), mass (m), specific heat (c), and temperature change (ΔT = T_final − T_initial).
  • Sec 20.6Calorimetry measures heat transfer by observing temperature changes in a surrounding substance. A coffee cup calorimeter is used for aqueous reactions at constant pressure. A bomb calorimeter measures combustion reactions at constant volume. In both cases: q_rxn = −q_calorimeter, because heat released by the reaction equals heat absorbed by the calorimeter contents.
  • Sec 20.7Enthalpy (H) is the heat content of a system at constant pressure. ΔH = H_products − H_reactants: negative for exothermic, positive for endothermic. Thermochemical equations include ΔH values. Enthalpy is a state function (path-independent). Standard enthalpies of formation (ΔH°_f) provide a reference for calculating ΔH for any reaction; ΔH°_f = 0 for elements in their standard states.
  • Sec 20.8Energy diagrams show how system energy changes during a reaction. Key features: reactants and products at different energy levels, activation energy barrier (E_a), and transition state at the peak. Exothermic diagrams show products lower than reactants; endothermic diagrams show products higher. Catalysts lower E_a without changing ΔH.
  • Sec 20.9Fuels release energy through combustion; energy density (kJ/g) compares their usefulness. Food is chemical fuel: carbohydrates and proteins supply 4 kcal/g; fats supply 9 kcal/g. Industrial thermochemistry affects production costs and design (Haber process, steelmaking, refrigeration). Combustion of fossil fuels releases CO₂, driving climate change; thermochemistry underlies both the problem and potential solutions.

Worked Example Review

Example A
Basic specific heat calculation
  • ProblemA 50.0 g piece of aluminum (c = 0.897 J/g·°C) is warmed from 15.0°C to 65.0°C. Find q.
  • Solveq = mcΔT = (50.0 g)(0.897 J/g·°C)(65.0 − 15.0)°C = (50.0)(0.897)(50.0) = 2,243 J = 2.24 kJ
Answer: q = 2.24 kJ (positive: aluminum gained heat)
Example B
Calorimetry calculation
  • ProblemA reaction in a coffee cup calorimeter raises the temperature of 200.0 g of solution from 21.3°C to 28.7°C. Given c = 4.18 J/g·°C, find q_rxn.
  • Solveq_solution = (200.0)(4.18)(28.7 − 21.3) = (200.0)(4.18)(7.4) = 6,187 J q_rxn = −6,187 J = −6.19 kJ
Answer: q_rxn = −6.19 kJ (exothermic: negative sign; reaction released heat to solution)
Example C
Interpreting a thermochemical equation
  • EquationC₃H₈(g) + 5O₂(g) → 3CO₂(g) + 4H₂O(l)   ΔH = −2220 kJ
  • InterpretExothermic (ΔH < 0). 2220 kJ of heat is released when one mole of propane burns completely. Products have less enthalpy than reactants. In an energy diagram, products appear at a lower energy level than reactants.
The combustion of propane is strongly exothermic — this is why propane is used as a fuel for grills and heaters.

Key Terms Review

activation energy
The minimum energy that colliding particles must have for a reaction to occur; the height of the energy barrier in a reaction coordinate diagram.
calorimeter
A device used to measure the heat transferred during a chemical or physical process.
calorimetry
The experimental technique of measuring heat flow by observing temperature changes in a calorimeter.
chemical potential energy
Energy stored in chemical bonds and in the arrangement of atoms within molecules; changes when bonds break and form.
endothermic process
A process that absorbs heat from the surroundings; the surroundings get cooler; ΔH is positive.
energy
The capacity to do work or transfer heat; measured in joules (J) or kilojoules (kJ). Cannot be created or destroyed.
energy density
The amount of energy stored per unit mass of a fuel (kJ/g); used to compare fuel efficiency.
energy diagram
A graph showing how the potential energy or enthalpy of a system changes as a reaction proceeds from reactants to products; also called a reaction coordinate diagram.
enthalpy (H)
A thermodynamic quantity representing the heat content of a system at constant pressure.
enthalpy change (ΔH)
ΔH = H_products − H_reactants. Negative for exothermic reactions; positive for endothermic reactions. Units: kJ or kJ/mol.
exothermic process
A process that releases heat to the surroundings; the surroundings get warmer; ΔH is negative.
heat (q)
The transfer of thermal energy between objects at different temperatures; always flows from warmer to cooler objects.
joule (J)
The SI unit of energy. 1 kJ = 1,000 J. 1 cal = 4.184 J.
kilocalorie (kcal)
1,000 calories; the unit used on nutrition labels (written "Calorie" with capital C). 1 kcal = 4,184 J.
kinetic energy
Energy of motion; at the particle level, related to temperature (faster particles = higher temperature).
law of conservation of energy
Energy cannot be created or destroyed; it can only be converted between forms or transferred between objects. Also called the first law of thermodynamics.
potential energy
Stored energy associated with position or arrangement; in chemistry, primarily stored in chemical bonds.
specific heat capacity (c)
The amount of heat required to raise the temperature of one gram of a substance by one degree Celsius; measured in J/g·°C. Water's value is 4.184 J/g·°C.
standard enthalpy of formation
ΔH°_f: enthalpy change when one mole of a compound forms from its elements in their standard states at 25°C and 1 atm. Zero for elements in their standard states.
surroundings
Everything outside the defined system; typically the container, solution, calorimeter, or environment around the reaction.
system
The specific part of the universe being studied; typically the chemicals or reaction of interest.
temperature
A measure of the average kinetic energy of particles in a substance; an intensive property measured in °C, K, or °F.
thermal energy
The total kinetic energy of all particles in a sample; depends on both temperature and amount of matter.
thermochemical equation
A balanced chemical equation that includes the enthalpy change (ΔH) for the reaction.
thermochemistry
The branch of chemistry that studies the energy changes associated with chemical and physical processes.
transition state
The highest-energy arrangement of atoms reached during a reaction; corresponds to the peak of the activation energy barrier in an energy diagram.

Chapter 21: Reaction Rates

Chapter 20 established how much energy is released or absorbed in a chemical reaction. Chapter 21 asks a different question: how fast does that release happen? You already know from energy diagrams that reactions must overcome an activation energy barrier. In Chapter 21, you will learn how collision theory explains why not every particle collision leads to a reaction, what factors control how quickly reactions proceed — concentration, temperature, surface area, and catalysts — and how chemists describe and measure reaction rates. The energy diagrams you studied here will reappear as tools for understanding reaction pathways, and the concept of activation energy will become central to explaining why some reactions happen instantly while others take years. The thermochemistry of Chapter 20 and the kinetics of Chapter 21 together describe not just whether a reaction releases energy, but how quickly that energy is released — a distinction with enormous practical consequences in industry, medicine, and environmental chemistry.

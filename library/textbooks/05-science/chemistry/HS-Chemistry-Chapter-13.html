Chapter 13 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Every time iron rusts, wood burns, bread rises, or a battery powers a flashlight, a chemical reaction is happening. Atoms are rearranging. Substances are transforming. Old bonds are breaking and new bonds are forming.

For centuries, chemists could observe these transformations, but they had no compact way to write them down and share them precisely. A description like "burn carbon in air and you get a colorless gas that suffocates a flame" is accurate, but it is also cumbersome. It does not tell you how much carbon, how much air, how much gas, or what the atoms are actually doing.

Chemical equations solve this problem. A chemical equation is a symbolic statement that describes a chemical reaction: what substances enter the reaction, what substances exit the reaction, and how the atoms are arranged before and after. Learning to read, write, and balance chemical equations is one of the central skills of chemistry. Every stoichiometry calculation, every prediction about reactions, every industrial chemical process, and every biological pathway you encounter later in this course will depend on understanding equations. This chapter builds that foundation carefully, from meaning to symbol to balance.

Opening Question

When hydrogen gas and oxygen gas react, they produce water — a substance with completely different properties than either reactant. How would you write a description of this transformation that is precise enough for any chemist in the world to read? How could you communicate not just what substances are involved, but exactly how many atoms of each kind are rearranging? By the end of this chapter, you will be able to write, read, and balance the symbolic statement that captures all of this: 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O.

Section 13.1

What Chemical Equations Represent

When chemists write a chemical equation, they are doing three things at once. First, they are identifying the substances involved — the reactants that enter the reaction and the products that form. Second, they are communicating composition through chemical formulas, which show exactly which atoms are present and in what ratios. Third, they are making a quantitative statement about amounts through the coefficients that describe the ratios in which substances react and form.

A chemical equation is therefore a compact, information-rich description of a chemical change. Every symbol and number carries meaning.

Standard Format

Reactants are written on the left side, products on the right. An arrow — the reaction arrow — separates them, pointing left to right and meaning "react to form" or "yield." Multiple reactants or products are separated by plus signs on their respective sides.

ComponentPositionMeaningExample
ReactantsLeft of arrowSubstances present before the reactionH₂ + O₂
Reaction arrow (→)Center"React to form" or "yield"→
ProductsRight of arrowSubstances formed by the reactionH₂O
Plus signsBoth sidesSeparates multiple substancesCO₂ + H₂O
CoefficientsBefore formulasRelative number of units of each substance2H₂
State symbolsAfter formulasPhysical state of each substanceH₂O(l)
The Arrow Is Not an Equals Sign

The reaction arrow (→) means transformation, not equality. The left side does not "equal" the right side the way 2 + 2 = 4. The arrow says the substances on the left are chemically converted into the substances on the right. In some reversible reactions, a double arrow (⇌) is used — you will study those in Chapter 22.

Particle-Level Meaning

Chemical equations do not just describe what you can see. They are models of events at the level of individual atoms and molecules. When hydrogen and oxygen react, H–H bonds and the O=O double bond break, atoms rearrange, and new O–H bonds form to produce water molecules. Every formula in every equation is a window into that invisible world.

Section 13.2

Reactants and Products

Reactants are the starting materials of a chemical reaction — the substances present before the reaction occurs, always on the left side of the equation. A reaction may have one reactant or several. Products are the substances formed by the reaction, appearing on the right side. Reactants and products are chemically different substances with different formulas, different structures, and often completely different observable properties.

Example 13.2.1
Identifying reactants and products
  • EquationCH₄ + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O
  • ReactantsMethane (CH₄) and oxygen (O₂) — the substances present before the reaction.
  • ProductsCarbon dioxide (CO₂) and water (H₂O) — the substances formed as a result of the reaction.
  • Key PointIron metal (Fe) is shiny and magnetic. Oxygen gas (O₂) is invisible. Their product, iron(III) oxide (Fe₂O₃), is reddish-brown and brittle — completely different properties than either reactant.
Atoms Follow the Arrow

Atoms are not created or destroyed in a chemical reaction. Every atom in the reactants must appear somewhere in the products. Atoms rearrange — bonds break on the left, new bonds form on the right — but the count of each type of atom never changes. This is why chemical equations must be balanced.

Section 13.3

Chemical Symbols and Formulas in Equations

Chemical equations use the universal symbolic language of chemistry: element symbols and chemical formulas. If you are uncertain about reading a chemical formula, review Chapters 9, 10, and 11. This section focuses on the ideas that matter most for writing and balancing equations.

The Seven Diatomic Elements

When an element symbol appears in an equation representing a pure element, it usually means that element in its most common structural form. However, seven elements do not exist as single atoms under normal conditions — they exist as diatomic molecules (two atoms bonded together). These seven must always be written with the subscript 2 when they appear as pure elements in any equation.

ElementSymbolNameState at Room Temperature
HydrogenH₂Hydrogen gasGas
NitrogenN₂Nitrogen gasGas
OxygenO₂Oxygen gasGas
FluorineF₂Fluorine gasGas
ChlorineCl₂Chlorine gasGas
BromineBr₂BromineLiquid
IodineI₂IodineSolid
Memory Device for Diatomic Elements

The seven diatomic elements can be remembered with the phrase "BrINClHOF" (bromine, iodine, nitrogen, chlorine, hydrogen, oxygen, fluorine) or arranged into the mnemonic "I Have No Fear Of Ice Cold Beer" — one diatomic element per word. Writing O instead of O₂ for pure oxygen gas in an equation is a significant error.

Coefficients Versus Subscripts — A Critical Distinction

One of the most common errors in equation work is confusing coefficients with subscripts. They are fundamentally different and must never be mixed up.

Subscripts vs. Coefficients

Subscript — Part of the chemical formula. Tells you the composition of one molecule or formula unit. Changing a subscript changes the identity of the substance. H₂O and H₂O₂ are entirely different compounds.

Coefficient — A number in front of the formula. Tells you how many units of that substance are present. Coefficients can be changed freely when balancing because they do not change the identity of any substance.

To represent two water molecules: write 2H₂O, not H₄O₂. Never change a subscript to balance an equation.

Never Change Subscripts to Balance

Subscripts are locked. They are part of the chemical formula for that specific substance, determined by its actual molecular or ionic structure. Only coefficients may be adjusted during balancing. Changing H₂O to H₂O₂ does not give you two water molecules — it gives you one molecule of hydrogen peroxide, a completely different compound.

Section 13.4

Subscripts and Coefficients

To find the total number of atoms of a given element in any expression, multiply the coefficient by the subscript for that element. This is the fundamental atom-counting skill that makes balancing equations possible.

Example 13.4.1
Counting atoms using coefficients and subscripts
  • Problem 1How many oxygen atoms are in 4CO₂?
  • SolveSubscript for O in CO₂ = 2 Coefficient = 4 Total O atoms = 4 × 2 = 8
  • Problem 2How many hydrogen atoms are in 3NH₃?
  • SolveSubscript for H in NH₃ = 3 Coefficient = 3 Total H atoms = 3 × 3 = 9
  • Problem 3How many oxygen atoms are in 2Ca(NO₃)₂?
  • SolveInside parentheses: NO₃ has 3 oxygens Subscript outside parentheses = 2 Oxygens per formula unit = 2 × 3 = 6 Coefficient = 2 Total O atoms = 2 × 6 = 12
Answers: 8 O atoms | 9 H atoms | 12 O atoms
Section 13.5

Conservation of Atoms

The law of conservation of mass states that in any chemical reaction, the total mass of the reactants equals the total mass of the products. This law was established through the careful experimental work of Antoine Lavoisier in the late 1700s. Lavoisier performed reactions in sealed containers and demonstrated that no mass was gained or lost. Before his work, some chemists believed that burning released a mysterious substance called phlogiston. Lavoisier's measurements showed instead that burning was combination with oxygen — and that mass was always conserved.

At the particle level, conservation of mass makes perfect sense: atoms are not created or destroyed in chemical reactions. They are only rearranged. Because atoms have mass, and because the same atoms that enter a reaction also leave it (in new arrangements), the total mass cannot change.

This is why unbalanced equations are scientifically incorrect. An equation showing more atoms on the product side is claiming atoms were created from nothing. An equation showing fewer atoms is claiming atoms vanished. Neither is possible. Every correctly written equation must have equal numbers of each type of atom on both sides.

Example 13.5.1
Checking for atom balance
  • EquationIs H₂ + O₂ → H₂O balanced?
  • Left sideH: 2 (from H₂) O: 2 (from O₂)
  • Right sideH: 2 (from H₂O) O: 1 (from H₂O)
  • VerdictHydrogen is balanced (2 = 2). Oxygen is NOT balanced (2 ≠ 1). This equation must be corrected.
Not balanced — oxygen atoms are not conserved. Correction follows in Section 13.6.
A Useful Mental Image

Imagine building with colored blocks: 4 red and 6 blue. No matter how you rearrange them into new structures, you must still have exactly 4 red and 6 blue at the end. Chemical reactions work exactly this way. Atoms rearrange into new substances, but the count of each type of atom never changes.

Section 13.6

Balancing Chemical Equations

Balancing a chemical equation means adjusting the coefficients in front of chemical formulas so that every type of atom has the same count on both sides. Balancing is not optional. An unbalanced equation is an incorrect equation. Every stoichiometry calculation in Chapters 15 and 16 will depend on correctly balanced equations — if the equation is wrong, every calculation built on it will be wrong.

The One and Only Rule

You may only change coefficients — never subscripts. Subscripts are part of the chemical formula and fixed by the identity of the substance. Coefficients indicate quantity and may be freely adjusted. An implied coefficient of 1 is never written.

The Inspection Method (Trial and Error)

At this level, the standard approach is inspection: adjust coefficients and recheck atom counts until all elements balance. With practice, this becomes systematic. Follow these steps:

  1. Write the unbalanced equation with correct formulas for all reactants and products.
  2. Count the atoms of each element on both sides.
  3. Start with the most complex formula, or with an element that appears in only one reactant and one product.
  4. Adjust a coefficient to balance that element. Recount all atoms.
  5. Continue adjusting one element at a time, rechecking each time.
  6. Balance hydrogen and oxygen last — they often appear in multiple substances.
  7. Check that all elements are balanced and all coefficients are in their lowest whole-number ratio.
Worked Example 13.6.1
Formation of water from hydrogen and oxygen
  • StartH₂ + O₂ → H₂O (unbalanced)
  • CountLeft: 2 H, 2 O Right: 2 H, 1 O ← oxygen not balanced
  • Fix OPlace coefficient 2 in front of H₂O to get 2 oxygens on the right: H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O New count: Left: 2 H, 2 O | Right: 4 H, 2 O ← oxygen balanced; hydrogen not
  • Fix HPlace coefficient 2 in front of H₂ to get 4 hydrogens on the left: 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O
  • CheckLeft: 4 H (2×2), 2 O Right: 4 H (2×2), 2 O (2×1) ✓ balanced
Balanced equation: 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O
Worked Example 13.6.2
Combustion of methane
  • StartCH₄ + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O (unbalanced)
  • CountLeft: 1 C, 4 H, 2 O Right: 1 C, 2 H, 3 O
  • Fix HCarbon is balanced. Place 2 in front of H₂O to match the 4 hydrogens on the left: CH₄ + O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O New count: Left: 1 C, 4 H, 2 O | Right: 1 C, 4 H, 4 O
  • Fix ONow 2 oxygens on the left, 4 on the right. Place 2 in front of O₂: CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O
  • CheckLeft: 1 C, 4 H, 4 O (2×2) Right: 1 C, 4 H, 4 O (2+2) ✓ balanced
Balanced equation: CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O
Worked Example 13.6.3
Reaction of iron with oxygen — finding a common multiple
  • StartFe + O₂ → Fe₂O₃ (unbalanced)
  • CountLeft: 1 Fe, 2 O Right: 2 Fe, 3 O
  • Fix FePlace 2 in front of Fe: 2Fe + O₂ → Fe₂O₃ New count: Left: 2 Fe, 2 O | Right: 2 Fe, 3 O — Fe balanced; O not
  • Fix ONeed equal oxygens: LCM of 2 and 3 is 6. Place 3 in front of O₂ (giving 6 O left) and 2 in front of Fe₂O₃ (giving 6 O right): 2Fe + 3O₂ → 2Fe₂O₃ Now right side has 2×2 = 4 Fe, but left side has only 2 Fe.
  • Fix Fe againAdjust Fe coefficient to 4: 4Fe + 3O₂ → 2Fe₂O₃
  • CheckLeft: 4 Fe, 6 O (3×2) Right: 4 Fe (2×2), 6 O (2×3) ✓ balanced
Balanced equation: 4Fe + 3O₂ → 2Fe₂O₃
Tips for Challenging Equations

Polyatomic ions: If a polyatomic group like SO₄²⁻ or NO₃⁻ appears unchanged on both sides, treat it as a single unit when counting. This simplifies the process considerably.

Fractions: If balancing produces a fractional coefficient (like ½), multiply all coefficients by the denominator to convert everything to whole numbers.

Start with the most complex formula or with an element that appears in only one reactant and one product.

Section 13.7

States of Matter in Equations

A chemical equation can include symbols to show the physical state of each substance. These state symbols appear in parentheses immediately after the chemical formula. They are not decorative — they describe actual conditions and help predict what will happen during the reaction.

SymbolStateMeaningExample
(s)SolidThe substance is in solid formFe(s)
(l)LiquidThe substance is in liquid formH₂O(l)
(g)GasThe substance is in gaseous formO₂(g)
(aq)AqueousThe substance is dissolved in waterNaCl(aq)
Full Example with State Symbols

CH₄(g) + 2O₂(g) → CO₂(g) + 2H₂O(l)

This tells you: methane and oxygen are gases that react, producing carbon dioxide gas and liquid water. If the reaction occurs at very high temperature and water is produced as vapor, you would write H₂O(g) instead.

The (aq) symbol is especially important in chemistry. It means the substance is dissolved in water — individual ions or molecules spread throughout a solution. Table salt NaCl dissolves to give Na⁺(aq) and Cl⁻(aq) ions floating freely through water, surrounded by water molecules. This is chemically different from solid NaCl(s), where ions are locked in a rigid lattice. You will use (aq) extensively in Chapter 17 when writing ionic equations for reactions in aqueous solution.

Knowing that a product forms as a gas tells you it may escape as bubbles if the reaction occurs in a liquid — a visible sign of reaction. Knowing a product forms as a solid in aqueous solution tells you it will precipitate out. State symbols in an equation carry all of this predictive information.

Section 13.8

Evidence of Chemical Reactions

A chemical equation is written in the world of symbols, but it represents events that happen in the physical world. One of the important skills in chemistry is connecting what you observe to what the symbolic equation describes. When you observe a chemical change, you are seeing the result of atoms rearranging. The equation translates those observations into a precise particle-level description.

Common Signs of a Chemical Reaction

  • PrecipitateWhen two aqueous solutions mix and a solid forms and settles (or clouds the solution), a precipitate has formed. The equation will show a product marked (s) in an otherwise aqueous system. Example: mixing silver nitrate and sodium chloride solutions produces white silver chloride solid.
  • Gas FormationBubbles forming during a reaction (when heat is not involved) indicate gas production. The equation will show a product marked (g). Example: zinc reacting with hydrochloric acid releases hydrogen gas (H₂) as visible bubbles.
  • Color ChangeMany reactions produce a color change as substances with different light-absorbing properties form. A dramatic, sudden color change is strong evidence of a chemical reaction. Example: adding iodine solution to a starchy food turns it dark blue-black.
  • Temperature ChangeReactions that release energy warm their surroundings; reactions that absorb energy cool them. A noticeable temperature change that cannot be explained by simple mixing is evidence of a chemical reaction. You will study energy in reactions in Chapter 20.
  • Light ProductionSome reactions release energy as visible light — burning substances glow, glow sticks produce light through chemical reactions, and fireflies produce bioluminescence through enzyme-catalyzed chemistry. Light production is dramatic evidence of a chemical event.
  • New OdorChemical reactions often produce substances with new and distinctive odors. The formation of gases like hydrogen sulfide (rotten egg smell) or ammonia (sharp, irritating) signals that new substances have formed.
Not Every Change Is a Chemical Reaction

Dissolving sugar in water changes appearance but forms no new substance. Ice melting is a physical change — water's chemical identity is unchanged. Carbon dioxide fizzing from a soda is a physical change — gas was dissolved under pressure and is now escaping, not being produced by a reaction. Chemists look for multiple lines of evidence and check whether the original substance can be recovered by reversing the process. If it can (like re-freezing melted ice), the change is physical, not chemical.

Section 13.9

Reading Equations as Particle Models

Every chemical equation describes a story at the level of individual atoms and molecules. Learning to visualize that story — not just to count atoms abstractly — deepens your understanding of chemistry in ways that pay dividends throughout this course.

Example 13.9.1
The particle-level story of 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O
  • Symbolic2 molecules of H₂ and 1 molecule of O₂ react to produce 2 molecules of H₂O.
  • Particle levelTwo H–H molecules and one O=O molecule collide and interact. The H–H bonds and the O=O double bond break. Each oxygen atom bonds to two hydrogen atoms, forming two H–O–H water molecules.
  • Why these coefficients?The oxygen molecule supplies 2 oxygen atoms. Each water molecule needs 1 oxygen and 2 hydrogens. To use one O₂ and produce 2 water molecules, you need 4 hydrogen atoms total — exactly 2 H₂ molecules. The balancing reflects the actual geometry of the atoms involved.

The coefficients in a balanced equation describe the ratio of molecules (or formula units) that react and form. In 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O, the ratio is 2 : 1 : 2. You could read this at any scale: 2 molecules of hydrogen react with 1 molecule of oxygen to produce 2 molecules of water. Or 2 dozen. Or 2 moles. These ratios — given by the coefficients — are the foundation of all stoichiometric calculations, which you will study in Chapter 16.

Particle Diagrams

A particle diagram is a simple sketch showing atoms and molecules on each side of an equation as spheres or symbols. For CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O, a particle diagram would show one carbon atom surrounded by four hydrogen atoms on the left (one CH₄ molecule), two pairs of oxygen atoms (two O₂ molecules), and on the right, one carbon bonded to two oxygens (CO₂) and two oxygen atoms each bonded to two hydrogens (2H₂O). Counting up: 1 C, 4 H, 4 O on each side. The diagram makes conservation of atoms visual and concrete.

What Equations Do Not Show

It is important to know the limits of what chemical equations communicate, so you do not read more into them than is there.

What Equations Do NOT ShowWhere to Find It
The mechanism — the exact sequence of bond-breaking and bond-forming stepsAdvanced organic chemistry and biochemistry courses
The reaction rate — whether it takes milliseconds or centuriesChapter 21: Reaction Rates
Energy released or absorbedChapter 20: Thermochemistry
Whether the reaction actually occurs under ordinary conditionsRequires experimental confirmation or thermodynamic analysis
Despite These Limits

The balanced chemical equation remains one of the most powerful tools in chemistry. It tells you what substances are involved, what substances form, and the precise quantitative ratios in which they participate — ratios that make stoichiometry, production calculations, pharmaceutical dosing, and industrial chemistry possible.

Key Terms Review

reactant
A substance that enters a chemical reaction; written on the left side of the equation, before the reaction arrow.
product
A substance formed by a chemical reaction; written on the right side of the equation, after the reaction arrow.
reaction arrow
The symbol (→) separating reactants from products; means "react to form" or "yield." It is a sign of transformation, not equality.
chemical equation
A symbolic description of a chemical reaction showing the formulas and relative amounts of reactants and products.
coefficient
A whole number placed in front of a chemical formula; indicates the relative number of units of that substance. The only number that may be changed when balancing an equation.
subscript
A number written below and to the right of an element symbol in a formula; indicates the number of atoms of that element in one formula unit. Cannot be changed without changing the substance's identity.
diatomic molecule
A molecule consisting of exactly two atoms of the same element. The seven diatomic elements (H₂, N₂, O₂, F₂, Cl₂, Br₂, I₂) must be written with subscript 2 in equations.
conservation of mass
The principle that in any chemical reaction, total mass of reactants equals total mass of products. Atoms are neither created nor destroyed.
balancing
The process of adjusting coefficients in a chemical equation so that every type of atom has equal counts on both sides of the arrow.
state symbols
Parenthetical notations showing physical state: (s) solid, (l) liquid, (g) gas, (aq) aqueous (dissolved in water).
precipitate
A solid product that forms from a reaction occurring in solution; indicated by the state symbol (s) when the surrounding substances are aqueous.
aqueous (aq)
A state symbol indicating that a substance is dissolved in water — individual ions or molecules spread throughout a water solution.
particle diagram
A visual model using circles or symbols to represent individual atoms or molecules in a chemical equation; used to visualize conservation of atoms concretely.

Error Analysis

Each student has made an error. Identify and explain the error, then write the correct answer.

Problem 1
A student balances H₂ + O₂ → H₂O by writing H₂ + O₂ → H₂O₂

What error did the student make? Why is this wrong? What is the correctly balanced equation?

Problem 2
N₂ + H₂ → N₂H₂ (student claims this is balanced: 2 N and 2 H on each side)

What is wrong with this equation? Consider what the actual product of this reaction is and whether the equation represents a real substance correctly.

Problem 3
3Fe + 4H₂O → Fe₃O₄ + 4H₂ — student says the Fe₃O₄ coefficient should be changed to 3 to balance iron

Explain why this reasoning is incorrect and state whether the original equation is actually balanced. Count all atoms.

Problem 4
C₄H₁₀ + O₂ → 4CO₂ + 5H₂O — student then writes 13O₂ on the left because they count 13 oxygens total on the right

Explain the student's counting error (hint: how many oxygen atoms are in each H₂O?). Then write the correctly balanced equation.

Problem 5
Student says 2H₂O → 2H₂ + O₂ is NOT balanced because there are 2 molecules of H₂O but only 1 molecule of O₂

Is the student correct? Remember: balance is determined by counting atoms, not molecules. Check atom counts on both sides and explain.

Mixed ReviewCumulative Questions
  1. A chemical formula tells you the types and numbers of atoms in one unit of a substance. What does a coefficient in a chemical equation tell you that a formula alone does not?
  2. How many total atoms are represented by the expression 3Ca₃(PO₄)₂?
  3. What is the difference between a chemical change and a physical change? Give one example of each.
  4. The law of conservation of mass was confirmed experimentally long before atoms were directly observed. How could scientists confirm conservation of mass without seeing atoms?
  5. Explain why the equation N₂ + O₂ → NO is not correctly balanced and write the corrected equation.
  6. Silver tarnishes to form silver sulfide (Ag₂S) when silver reacts with hydrogen sulfide gas (H₂S) in the air, also producing water vapor. Write the balanced chemical equation for this reaction, including appropriate state symbols. (Silver: solid; H₂S: gas; Ag₂S: solid; H₂O: gas.)
  7. The balanced equation for fermentation of glucose is: C₆H₁₂O₆(aq) → 2C₂H₅OH(aq) + 2CO₂(g). (a) What are the reactant(s) and product(s)? (b) What gas is released? (c) Verify the equation is balanced by counting all atoms on both sides. (d) What observable evidence might you see?
  8. Chlorine gas reacts with sodium metal to produce sodium chloride. Write the unbalanced equation using correct formulas, then balance it.
  9. A student finds 6 oxygen atoms on the left side of an equation and 4 on the right. Can the student fix this by changing a subscript in a product formula? Explain.
  10. Hydrogen peroxide (H₂O₂) decomposes into water and oxygen gas. Write the balanced equation for this decomposition reaction.

Looking Ahead
Chapter 14: Types of Chemical Reactions

Chapter 13 gave you the tools to write and balance chemical equations — the symbolic language of chemical change. Chapter 14 uses this foundation to explore the patterns that chemical reactions follow. Not all reactions are random or unpredictable. Chemists have found that most reactions belong to recognizable categories: synthesis, decomposition, single-replacement, double-replacement, combustion, and others. Knowing which category a reaction belongs to helps you predict what the products will be — even before you do the experiment. In Chapter 14, you will learn to recognize each major reaction type, predict products from reactant information, and balance equations in a variety of chemical contexts. Every balancing skill you built here will be applied to every new reaction type you encounter.

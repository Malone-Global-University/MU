Chapter 18 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Look around you right now. You are surrounded by a gas. The air filling the room, the breath moving in and out of your lungs, the atmosphere stretching dozens of kilometers above your head — all of it is made of particles in constant, rapid, invisible motion. You cannot see the individual molecules of nitrogen and oxygen that make up the air, but their behavior determines the weather, the pressure in your bicycle tires, the way your lungs work, and the altitude at which airplanes can fly.

Gases behave differently from solids and liquids. They expand to fill any container. They can be compressed into a smaller space. They push outward on every surface they touch. They mix completely with other gases. And their behavior changes in predictable, measurable ways when temperature, pressure, or volume changes.

Chemists and physicists have studied gases for centuries, and from that work emerged a set of gas laws — mathematical relationships that connect pressure, volume, temperature, and amount of gas in ways that can be used to make precise predictions. These laws are not just formulas to memorize. They are windows into the behavior of matter at the particle level.

In this chapter, you will learn what gases are made of, how pressure and temperature relate to particle motion, and how to use the gas laws to solve real problems. You will apply algebra to situations involving weather forecasting, breathing, balloons, diving, scuba tanks, aerosol cans, automobile engines, and laboratory gas collection.

Opening Question

A sealed syringe contains a fixed amount of gas at room temperature. If you push the plunger in and cut the volume in half, what happens to the pressure inside? What if you then heat the syringe — what changes? By the end of this chapter, you will be able to calculate the exact answer to both questions, explain the particle-level reason for each, and apply the same reasoning to weather, biology, and industry.

Section 18.1

Properties of Gases

What Makes a Gas Different

Matter exists in three common states: solid, liquid, and gas. The state a substance occupies depends on how strongly its particles attract one another and how much energy those particles have. In solids, particles are locked tightly together. In liquids, particles flow past each other. In gases, particles move freely and independently, separated from one another by large distances compared to the size of the particles themselves.

This simple difference — large separation between particles — explains nearly every property that gases have.

Gas PropertyExplanationExample
Expand to fill containersNo fixed shape or volume; particles spread in all directionsPerfume diffuses across a room
CompressibleLarge empty space between particles allows them to be pushed closerAir pumped into a bicycle tire
Low densityMost of the gas volume is empty spaceAir: ~1.2 g/L vs. water: ~1000 g/L
Exert pressureParticles collide with container walls billions of times per secondTire pressure; atmospheric pressure
Diffuse and mixRandom, high-speed motion causes gases to mix uniformlyCO₂ and N₂ mixing in air

Section 18.2

Gas Pressure

Pressure is defined as force divided by area. Gas pressure specifically results from the force of gas particle collisions with surfaces. Every time a gas particle strikes a container wall, it exerts a tiny push. Billions of collisions per second across every square centimeter of the container wall add up to a measurable, steady pressure.

Two things increase gas pressure: more collisions and harder collisions. More particles in the same container means more collisions per second, so more particles means higher pressure. Faster-moving particles (higher temperature) means harder collisions, so higher temperature means higher pressure when volume is fixed.

Pressure Unit Conversions

1 atm = 101.3 kPa = 760 mmHg = 760 torr = 101,325 Pa = 14.7 psi

Memorize the relationships to atm. Use them as conversion factors in dimensional analysis.

Section 18.3

Temperature and Particle Motion

The Kelvin Scale

Temperature is a measure of the average kinetic energy of particles in a substance. The faster particles move, the higher the temperature. For the gas laws, temperature must always be expressed using the Kelvin scale — an absolute temperature scale that starts at absolute zero, the theoretical temperature at which particles would have no kinetic energy.

Temperature Conversion — Always Required for Gas Laws

T (K) = T (°C) + 273

Absolute zero = 0 K = −273 °C. The Kelvin scale begins at the true absence of thermal energy, which makes proportional relationships in gas laws mathematically valid. Using Celsius in gas law calculations produces wrong answers — no exceptions.

Reference PointTemperature (°C)Temperature (K)
Absolute zero−273 °C0 K
Melting point of water0 °C273 K
Room temperature (approx.)25 °C298 K
Body temperature37 °C310 K
Boiling point of water100 °C373 K
Worked Example 18.2
Converting temperatures to Kelvin
  • ProblemConvert (a) 37 °C, (b) −78 °C, and (c) 0 °C to Kelvin.
  • Solve (a) 37 + 273 = 310 K (b) −78 + 273 = 195 K (c) 0 + 273 = 273 K
Answers: (a) 310 K (b) 195 K (c) 273 K

The Kinetic Molecular Theory of Gases

The behavior of gases is explained by the kinetic molecular theory, a model based on five key assumptions about ideal gases:

#AssumptionConsequence
1Gas particles are negligibly small compared to the distances between themMost of a gas is empty space; gases are compressible
2Particles move in constant, random, straight-line motionGases expand and diffuse; pressure is exerted in all directions
3Collisions between particles are elastic (no net energy loss)Temperature stays constant unless heat is added or removed
4Particles do not attract or repel each other between collisionsGas behavior is independent of chemical identity (ideal model)
5Average kinetic energy is directly proportional to temperature in KelvinHeating a gas speeds particles; cooling slows them
Keep the Theory in Mind

As you learn each gas law, connect it back to the kinetic molecular theory. Every mathematical relationship has a particle-level explanation. Understanding both levels — the equation and the model — is the goal of this chapter.

Section 18.4

Boyle's Law

Imagine a sealed syringe. The syringe contains a fixed amount of gas at a fixed temperature. When you push the plunger in — reducing the volume — the gas particles are forced into a smaller space. They collide with the walls more frequently. More collisions per second means higher pressure. When volume decreases, pressure increases. Pull the plunger out, and the opposite occurs: more space, fewer collisions per second, lower pressure.

This relationship — pressure and volume change in opposite directions when temperature and amount of gas remain constant — is Boyle's Law, described by Irish scientist Robert Boyle in 1662.

Boyle's Law

At constant temperature and constant amount of gas, pressure and volume are inversely proportional.

P₁V₁ = P₂V₂

When one increases, the other decreases. Their product remains constant. Temperature (T) and moles (n) are held constant.

Worked Example 18.3
Finding new volume when pressure increases
  • GivenP₁ = 1.5 atm, V₁ = 4.0 L, P₂ = 3.0 atm. Temperature constant.
  • FindV₂
  • Solve P₁V₁ = P₂V₂ (1.5)(4.0) = (3.0)(V₂) 6.0 = 3.0 × V₂ V₂ = 6.0 ÷ 3.0 = 2.0 L
  • CheckPressure doubled → volume should halve: 4.0 ÷ 2 = 2.0 L. ✓
Answer: V₂ = 2.0 L
Worked Example 18.4
Finding pressure when volume doubles
  • GivenP₁ = 720 mmHg, V₁ = 250 mL, V₂ = 500 mL. Temperature constant.
  • Solve P₁V₁ = P₂V₂ (720)(250) = P₂(500) 180,000 = P₂ × 500 P₂ = 180,000 ÷ 500 = 360 mmHg
  • CheckVolume doubled → pressure should halve: 720 ÷ 2 = 360 mmHg. ✓
Answer: P₂ = 360 mmHg
Boyle's Law in Everyday Life

Breathing is Boyle's Law in action. Inhaling expands the chest cavity (volume increases), pressure in the lungs drops below atmospheric, and air flows in. Exhaling compresses the cavity (volume decreases), pressure rises above atmospheric, and air flows out. Scuba divers must also understand Boyle's Law: air compressed in a tank expands as pressure decreases during ascent, which is why breath-holding during a rapid ascent can be dangerous.

Section 18.5

Charles's Law

Now keep the pressure on a gas sample constant — use a flexible container like a balloon — but heat the gas. Faster particles hit the walls harder and more often. In a flexible container, the walls move outward until pressure is balanced again. Volume increases when temperature increases. Cool the gas, and the opposite occurs. Volume decreases when temperature decreases.

This relationship — volume and temperature change in the same direction when pressure and amount of gas remain constant — is Charles's Law, named after French scientist Jacques Charles, who described it in 1787.

Charles's Law

At constant pressure and constant amount of gas, volume and temperature (in Kelvin) are directly proportional.

V₁ / T₁ = V₂ / T₂

Temperature must always be in Kelvin. Pressure (P) and moles (n) are held constant.

Worked Example 18.5
Finding new volume when temperature rises
  • GivenV₁ = 3.00 L at 20 °C. Temperature rises to 40 °C. Pressure constant.
  • ConvertT₁ = 20 + 273 = 293 K    T₂ = 40 + 273 = 313 K
  • Solve V₁/T₁ = V₂/T₂ 3.00/293 = V₂/313 V₂ = 3.00 × (313/293) V₂ = 3.00 × 1.068 = 3.20 L
  • NoteTemperature did NOT double (in Kelvin, 293 → 313 is only ~7% increase), so volume did not double. Always use Kelvin.
Answer: V₂ = 3.20 L
Worked Example 18.6
Finding the temperature needed for a volume change
  • GivenV₁ = 500 mL at 27 °C. Find T₂ when V₂ = 750 mL. Pressure constant.
  • ConvertT₁ = 27 + 273 = 300 K
  • Solve 500/300 = 750/T₂ T₂ = 750 × (300/500) T₂ = 450 K
  • Convert back450 − 273 = 177 °C
Answer: T₂ = 450 K = 177 °C
Charles's Law in Everyday Life

Hot air balloons demonstrate Charles's Law directly — heating the air inside the envelope expands its volume, making it less dense than the surrounding cool air, and the balloon rises. Bread dough rises in the oven as CO₂ gas from yeast expands with rising temperature. On cold mornings, car tires may appear slightly low because cooler air occupies less volume (or exerts less pressure in a rigid tire).

Section 18.6

Gay-Lussac's Law

Consider a rigid, sealed container — one that cannot change volume. When temperature increases, gas particles move faster and hit the walls harder and more often. But the walls cannot move. The increased force translates directly into increased pressure. When temperature increases in a sealed rigid container, pressure increases. Cool the gas, and pressure decreases.

This relationship — pressure and temperature change in the same direction when volume and amount of gas remain constant — is Gay-Lussac's Law, named after French chemist Joseph Louis Gay-Lussac, who described it in 1808.

Gay-Lussac's Law

At constant volume and constant amount of gas, pressure and temperature (in Kelvin) are directly proportional.

P₁ / T₁ = P₂ / T₂

Temperature must always be in Kelvin. Volume (V) and moles (n) are held constant.

Worked Example 18.7
Aerosol can left in a hot car
  • GivenP₁ = 1.2 atm at 25 °C. Temperature rises to 60 °C. Volume constant (rigid can).
  • ConvertT₁ = 298 K    T₂ = 333 K
  • Solve P₁/T₁ = P₂/T₂ 1.2/298 = P₂/333 P₂ = 1.2 × (333/298) P₂ = 1.34 atm
Answer: P₂ = 1.34 atm — aerosol cans must not be heated for this reason.
Worked Example 18.8
Finding the temperature that produces a target pressure
  • GivenP₁ = 3.00 atm at 300 K. Find T₂ when P₂ = 4.50 atm. Volume constant.
  • Solve 3.00/300 = 4.50/T₂ T₂ = 4.50 × (300/3.00) = 450 K
  • Convert450 − 273 = 177 °C
Answer: T₂ = 450 K = 177 °C
Section 18.7

Avogadro's Law

The three laws above hold the amount of gas constant. What happens when the amount changes? If you pump more gas into a flexible balloon at constant temperature and pressure, the balloon expands. Volume increases when moles of gas increase. Remove gas, and the balloon shrinks. This is Avogadro's Law, named after Italian scientist Amedeo Avogadro.

Avogadro's Law and Molar Volume

At constant temperature and pressure, volume is directly proportional to moles of gas.

V₁ / n₁ = V₂ / n₂

At STP (0 °C and 1 atm), one mole of any ideal gas occupies exactly 22.4 L.

This molar volume is the same regardless of the chemical identity of the gas.

Worked Example 18.9
Using molar volume at STP
  • ProblemWhat volume does 0.500 mol of nitrogen gas occupy at STP?
  • Solve 0.500 mol × (22.4 L / 1 mol) = 11.2 L
Answer: 11.2 L
Worked Example 18.10
Finding moles from volume at STP
  • ProblemA gas sample occupies 8.96 L at STP. How many moles does it contain?
  • Solve n = 8.96 L × (1 mol / 22.4 L) = 0.400 mol
Answer: 0.400 mol
Avogadro's Law and Stoichiometry

When gases react at the same temperature and pressure, their volume ratios equal their mole ratios in the balanced equation. For 2 H₂(g) + O₂(g) → 2 H₂O(g): 2 volumes of H₂ react with 1 volume of O₂ to produce 2 volumes of H₂O. Volume ratios are mole ratios for gases at constant T and P.

Section 18.8

The Combined Gas Law

Boyle's, Charles's, and Gay-Lussac's laws each hold two variables constant and relate the other two. In practice, more than one variable often changes at the same time. The combined gas law merges all three individual laws into a single equation that handles simultaneous changes in pressure, volume, and temperature, while keeping the amount of gas constant.

The Combined Gas Law

P₁V₁ / T₁ = P₂V₂ / T₂

Amount of gas (n) must remain constant. Temperature must be in Kelvin.

When any variable is constant, it cancels from both sides, reducing the equation to one of the three individual laws.

Worked Example 18.11
Combined gas law — pressure and temperature both change
  • GivenA gas at 2.00 L, 1.00 atm, 25 °C. New conditions: 2.00 atm, 100 °C. Find V₂.
  • ConvertT₁ = 298 K    T₂ = 373 K
  • Solve P₁V₁/T₁ = P₂V₂/T₂ (1.00)(2.00)/298 = (2.00)(V₂)/373 V₂ = (1.00)(2.00)(373) / (298)(2.00) V₂ = 746 / 596 V₂ = 1.25 L
  • CheckPressure doubled (tends to halve volume). Temperature rose ~25% (tends to increase volume). Net effect: slight reduction. 1.25 L is reasonable. ✓
Answer: V₂ = 1.25 L
Worked Example 18.12
Finding pressure using the combined gas law
  • GivenV₁ = 500 mL, P₁ = 0.800 atm, T₁ = 50 °C. New conditions: V₂ = 300 mL, T₂ = 25 °C. Find P₂.
  • ConvertT₁ = 323 K    T₂ = 298 K
  • Solve P₁V₁/T₁ = P₂V₂/T₂ (0.800)(500)/323 = P₂(300)/298 P₂ = (0.800)(500)(298) / (323)(300) P₂ = 119,200 / 96,900 P₂ = 1.23 atm
Answer: P₂ = 1.23 atm
Strategy for Combined Gas Law Problems

Always list P₁, V₁, T₁ and P₂, V₂, T₂. Convert all temperatures to Kelvin first. If one variable is constant (not listed as changing), it cancels — and you recognize which simpler law applies. If two or three variables change, use the full combined equation.

Section 18.9

The Ideal Gas Law

The combined gas law handles changes in P, V, and T for a fixed amount of gas. But what about situations where the amount of gas — the number of moles — is part of the problem? What if you want to calculate how many moles are present in a sample based on its measured pressure, volume, and temperature?

For that, you need a law that connects all four gas variables in a single equation. That equation is the ideal gas law.

The Ideal Gas Law

PV = nRT

P = pressure in atm    V = volume in liters    n = moles of gas    T = temperature in Kelvin

R = ideal gas constant = 0.08206 L·atm / (mol·K)

When pressure is in kPa, use R = 8.314 L·kPa / (mol·K). Match R to the pressure units used.

Worked Example 18.13
Finding moles using the ideal gas law
  • Given5.00 L container, 2.00 atm, 27 °C. Find n.
  • ConvertT = 27 + 273 = 300 K
  • Solve PV = nRT → n = PV / (RT) n = (2.00 atm)(5.00 L) / (0.08206 L·atm/mol·K)(300 K) n = 10.0 / 24.618 n = 0.406 mol
Answer: n = 0.406 mol
Worked Example 18.14
Finding pressure from moles
  • Given0.250 mol CO₂ in a 3.00 L container at 37 °C. Find P.
  • ConvertT = 310 K
  • Solve PV = nRT → P = nRT / V P = (0.250)(0.08206)(310) / (3.00) P = 6.360 / 3.00 P = 2.12 atm
Answer: P = 2.12 atm
Worked Example 18.15
Starting from grams — converting to moles first
  • Given1.50 g of N₂ (M = 28.0 g/mol) in a 500 mL container at 25 °C. Find P.
  • Step 1n = 1.50 g ÷ 28.0 g/mol = 0.0536 mol
  • Step 2V = 500 mL = 0.500 L    T = 298 K
  • Step 3 P = nRT / V P = (0.0536)(0.08206)(298) / (0.500) P = 1.311 / 0.500 P = 2.62 atm
Answer: P = 2.62 atm

Gas Density from the Ideal Gas Law

The ideal gas law can be rearranged to find gas density or molar mass. Starting from PV = nRT and substituting n = m/M (where m is mass in grams and M is molar mass in g/mol), density is:

d = PM / (RT)

where d = density in g/L, P = pressure in atm, M = molar mass in g/mol, R = 0.08206 L·atm/mol·K, and T = temperature in Kelvin. Gas density increases with pressure and molar mass, and decreases with temperature.

Worked Example 18.16
Calculating gas density
  • ProblemWhat is the density of O₂ (M = 32.0 g/mol) at 1.00 atm and 25 °C?
  • Solve d = PM / (RT) d = (1.00)(32.0) / (0.08206)(298) d = 32.0 / 24.45 d = 1.31 g/L
Answer: d = 1.31 g/L
Section 18.10

Real Gases and Limits of Models

The ideal gas law is a powerful and accurate tool for most chemistry problems. But it is based on a model — the kinetic molecular theory — that makes simplifying assumptions. Real gas particles are not perfectly volumeless. They do attract and repel each other. At high pressure and low temperature, these non-ideal behaviors become significant.

ConditionReal Gas BehaviorWhy It Differs
High pressureActual volume is slightly larger than ideal predictsParticle volume becomes a meaningful fraction of total volume
Low temperatureActual pressure is slightly lower than ideal predictsAttractive forces between slow-moving particles reduce wall collision force
Large, polar moleculesGreater deviation from ideal behaviorStronger intermolecular attractions (e.g., H₂O, NH₃)
Small, nonpolar moleculesClosest to ideal behaviorWeak attractions, negligible particle volume (e.g., He, H₂)
Using Models Responsibly

The ideal gas model works beautifully for most gases at ordinary laboratory temperatures and pressures — and in those conditions, using PV = nRT produces answers accurate within the precision of typical measurements. When conditions become extreme (very high pressure, very low temperature) or the gas has strong intermolecular forces, the model should be applied with care. Learning to use a model includes knowing when it applies and when it does not. This is part of scientific thinking.

Important Reminder — Always Use Kelvin

For every gas law calculation in this chapter — Boyle's is the only exception, because temperature is constant and cancels — temperature must be in Kelvin. Forgetting to convert from Celsius to Kelvin is the single most common error in gas law problems. Convert before you set up the equation, every time.

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 18.1Gases expand to fill containers, are compressible, have low density, exert pressure, and diffuse freely. Four variables describe any gas: pressure (P), volume (V), temperature (T in Kelvin), and amount in moles (n).
  • Sec 18.2Gas pressure is force per unit area arising from particle collisions with container walls. Standard units: 1 atm = 101.3 kPa = 760 mmHg = 760 torr. Convert between units using these relationships as conversion factors.
  • Sec 18.3Temperature must always be in Kelvin for gas law calculations: K = °C + 273. Absolute zero (0 K) is the theoretical temperature of zero kinetic energy. The kinetic molecular theory models gases as small, fast-moving particles with elastic collisions and no intermolecular attractions.

  • Sec 18.5Charles's Law: at constant P and n, volume and temperature (in Kelvin) are directly proportional. V₁/T₁ = V₂/T₂. When temperature increases, volume increases proportionally.
  • Sec 18.6Gay-Lussac's Law: at constant V and n, pressure and temperature (in Kelvin) are directly proportional. P₁/T₁ = P₂/T₂. In a rigid sealed container, heating always increases pressure.
  • Sec 18.7Avogadro's Law: at constant T and P, volume is directly proportional to moles. V₁/n₁ = V₂/n₂. At STP (0 °C, 1 atm), one mole of any ideal gas occupies 22.4 L regardless of chemical identity.
  • Sec 18.8The combined gas law, P₁V₁/T₁ = P₂V₂/T₂, handles simultaneous changes in P, V, and T at constant n. Constant variables cancel, reducing to one of the three individual laws when applicable.
  • Sec 18.9The ideal gas law PV = nRT connects all four gas variables. R = 0.08206 L·atm/mol·K. Use it to calculate any unknown variable from the other three, or to find gas density using d = PM/(RT).
  • Sec 18.10Real gases deviate from ideal behavior at high pressure (particle volume matters) and low temperature (intermolecular attractions matter). Ideal gas calculations are accurate for most laboratory conditions. All models have limits.

Worked Example Review

Example A — Boyle's Law
Gas at 1.50 atm, 8.00 L. New pressure: 3.00 atm. Find V₂.
  • SolveP₁V₁ = P₂V₂ → (1.50)(8.00) = (3.00)(V₂) → V₂ = 12.0/3.00 = 4.00 L
Answer: 4.00 L
Example B — Charles's Law
Gas at 1.50 L, 300 K. New temperature: 450 K. Find V₂.
  • SolveV₁/T₁ = V₂/T₂ → 1.50/300 = V₂/450 → V₂ = 1.50 × (450/300) = 2.25 L
Answer: 2.25 L
Example C — Gay-Lussac's Law
Gas at 2.00 atm, 400 K. New temperature: 600 K. Find P₂.
  • SolveP₁/T₁ = P₂/T₂ → 2.00/400 = P₂/600 → P₂ = 2.00 × (600/400) = 3.00 atm
Answer: 3.00 atm
Example D — Combined Gas Law
Gas at 1.00 atm, 300 K, 4.00 L. New conditions: 2.00 atm, 400 K. Find V₂.
  • Solve P₁V₁/T₁ = P₂V₂/T₂ (1.00)(4.00)/300 = (2.00)(V₂)/400 V₂ = (1.00)(4.00)(400) / (300)(2.00) = 1600/600 = 2.67 L
Answer: 2.67 L
Example E — Ideal Gas Law
Gas at 2.50 atm, 10.0 L, 27 °C. Find moles.
  • Solve T = 300 K n = PV/(RT) = (2.50)(10.0) / (0.08206)(300) = 25.0 / 24.618 = 1.02 mol
Answer: 1.02 mol

Key Terms Review

absolute zero
The theoretical temperature at which gas particles would have zero kinetic energy; equal to 0 K or −273 °C.
atmosphere (atm)
A unit of pressure equal to 101.3 kPa or 760 mmHg; defined as standard atmospheric pressure at sea level.
Avogadro's Law
At constant T and P, the volume of a gas is directly proportional to the number of moles present. V₁/n₁ = V₂/n₂.
Boyle's Law
At constant T and n, pressure and volume are inversely proportional. P₁V₁ = P₂V₂.
Charles's Law
At constant P and n, volume is directly proportional to temperature in Kelvin. V₁/T₁ = V₂/T₂.
combined gas law
P₁V₁/T₁ = P₂V₂/T₂ — combines Boyle's, Charles's, and Gay-Lussac's laws to handle simultaneous changes in P, V, and T at constant n.
compressible
Capable of being reduced in volume by applying pressure; a key property of gases due to large spaces between particles.
diffusion
The gradual movement and spreading of gas particles through space until they are uniformly distributed.
elastic collision
A collision in which kinetic energy is conserved overall; assumed in the kinetic molecular theory of gases.
Gay-Lussac's Law
At constant V and n, pressure is directly proportional to temperature in Kelvin. P₁/T₁ = P₂/T₂.
ideal gas
A hypothetical gas that perfectly follows all assumptions of kinetic molecular theory and obeys all gas laws exactly under all conditions.
ideal gas constant (R)
The proportionality constant in the ideal gas law; R = 0.08206 L·atm/(mol·K) when using atmospheres and liters.
ideal gas law
PV = nRT — relates pressure, volume, temperature, and moles for an ideal gas in a single equation.
Kelvin (K)
The SI unit of absolute temperature; calculated as K = °C + 273. Required for all gas law calculations.
kinetic molecular theory
A model of gas behavior: small particles in constant random motion with elastic collisions, negligible volume, no attractions, and average kinetic energy proportional to temperature in Kelvin.
manometer
A laboratory instrument used to measure the pressure of a gas sample in an enclosed container.
molar volume
The volume occupied by one mole of an ideal gas at STP; equal to 22.4 L/mol regardless of chemical identity.
pressure
Force per unit area; for a gas, the collective result of particle collisions with container walls.
real gas
An actual gas whose behavior deviates from ideal behavior, especially at high pressure and low temperature.
STP
Standard temperature and pressure: defined as 0 °C (273 K) and 1 atm; used as a reference point for gas law calculations and molar volume.
torr
A unit of pressure equal to 1 mmHg; 760 torr = 1 atm. Named after Evangelista Torricelli, inventor of the barometer.

Error Analysis

Each problem contains an error. Identify it, explain what went wrong, and show the correct solution.

Problem A — Boyle's Law
Gas at 2.00 atm, 5.00 L. Pressure decreases to 1.00 atm. P₁V₁ = P₂V₂ → (2.00)(5.00) = (1.00)(V₂) → V₂ = 2.50 L

Is the answer reasonable? Pressure decreased, so what should happen to volume? Identify the calculation error and give the correct answer.

Problem B — Charles's Law
Gas at 27 °C, 4.00 L. Heated to 54 °C. Temperature doubled (27 × 2 = 54), so volume doubles: V₂ = 8.00 L.

Explain what is wrong with this reasoning. Convert both temperatures to Kelvin and solve correctly.

Problem C — Ideal Gas Law
Find moles in a 2.00 L container at 1.00 atm and 27 °C. n = PV/RT = (1.00)(2.00) / (0.08206)(27) = 0.902 mol

Identify the error. What temperature should be used? Solve correctly.

Problem D — Combined Gas Law
Gas at 800 mmHg, 300 K, 3.00 L changed to 400 mmHg, 600 K. Student gets V₂ = 12.0 L.

Check all steps. Is the reasoning and arithmetic correct? Verify whether the answer makes physical sense: pressure decreased and temperature increased — which directions do those push volume?

Real-World Applications

  1. Breathing mechanics. When you inhale, the volume of your chest cavity increases. Using Boyle's Law, explain why this causes air to flow into your lungs. What happens to chest volume and lung pressure when you exhale?
  2. Hot air balloons. The burner heats air inside the balloon envelope. Use Charles's Law to explain why this increases volume, and explain how lower density creates lift.
  3. Scuba diving. A scuba diver carries compressed air. As the diver descends, water pressure increases. The regulator delivers air at the pressure of the surrounding water. Why is it dangerous for a breath-holding diver to ascend rapidly? Use Boyle's Law in your explanation.
  4. Tire pressure and temperature. A car tire is inflated to 35.0 psi on a summer day at 35 °C. On a winter morning the temperature is −10 °C. Assuming the volume does not change, use Gay-Lussac's Law to calculate the winter morning pressure. (Work as a ratio directly — you do not need to convert psi to atm for this calculation.)
  5. Aerosol cans. An aerosol can at 22 °C contains gas at 1.30 atm. It is placed in a fire and reaches 400 °C. Using Gay-Lussac's Law, calculate the pressure inside. Does this help explain the warning "Do not expose to heat or flame"?
  6. Weather balloons. A weather balloon is released at sea level with a volume of 1.00 m³ at 1.00 atm and 15 °C. The balloon rises until pressure outside is 0.350 atm and temperature is −45 °C. Using the combined gas law, calculate the balloon's new volume at altitude.
  7. Industrial gas storage. A factory produces 450 L of nitrogen gas at 25 °C and 1.00 atm. The gas is compressed into a 45.0 L steel cylinder at 25 °C. Calculate the pressure in the cylinder in atm and in kPa.
  8. Stoichiometry and gas volumes. Methane burns: CH₄(g) + 2 O₂(g) → CO₂(g) + 2 H₂O(g). At STP, how many liters of O₂ are needed to react with 44.8 L of CH₄? How many liters of CO₂ are produced? Use Avogadro's Law reasoning.

Looking Ahead
Chapter 19: Liquids, Solids, and Phase Changes

In Chapter 18, you studied gases — the state of matter in which particles move most freely. In Chapter 19, you will turn to liquids and solids, where particles are far closer together and far more strongly attracted to each other. You will explore how intermolecular forces determine whether a substance is solid, liquid, or gas at a given temperature and pressure, and you will study the energy changes involved in melting, freezing, vaporization, condensation, and sublimation. Heating curves and cooling curves will let you read these transitions from a graph, and phase diagrams will show you how temperature and pressure together determine which state any substance occupies. You will discover why water boils at a lower temperature on a mountain, why dry ice sublimes without becoming liquid, and how evaporation cools your skin. Chapter 19 connects directly to the gas laws of Chapter 18 and prepares you for the thermochemistry of Chapter 20.

Chapter 6 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

In Chapter 5, you learned that atoms are real particles with a nucleus, and that protons, neutrons, and electrons are their three main components. You learned that the atomic number — the number of protons — is what makes one element different from every other element.

But atomic structure goes deeper than that introduction suggested. Atoms of the same element do not always have the same number of neutrons. Atoms can gain or lose electrons, changing their electrical charge. The mass of an atom can be expressed in special units that make calculations practical. And atomic mass values on the periodic table are not whole numbers — they are weighted averages that reflect the natural mixture of atom types found in any real sample of an element.

This chapter builds that deeper understanding. You will learn to use nuclear symbols to describe any atom precisely. You will learn what isotopes are, why they exist, and why they matter. You will calculate average atomic mass the way chemists do. You will learn what ions are and how charged atoms form. And you will see how isotopes connect to real work in medicine, archaeology, environmental science, and research.

Opening Question

The periodic table lists carbon's atomic mass as 12.011 — not 12. Why not a clean whole number? And why do some carbon atoms behave identically to others in every chemical reaction, yet have different masses? By the end of this chapter, you will understand exactly why, and you will be able to calculate that 12.011 value yourself from scratch.

Section 6.1

The Parts of the Atom

Every atom in the universe is made of three kinds of particles: protons, neutrons, and electrons. These three particles have different locations, different masses, and different electrical charges. Understanding how they work together is the foundation of everything else in chemistry.

The Nucleus

At the center of every atom is the nucleus. The nucleus is extraordinarily small compared to the total size of the atom, but it contains almost all of the atom's mass. If an atom were the size of a football stadium, the nucleus would be roughly the size of a marble on the fifty-yard line.

The nucleus contains two kinds of particles: protons and neutrons. These particles are called nucleons because they live in the nucleus. Protons carry a positive electrical charge of +1. Neutrons carry no electrical charge at all — they are electrically neutral, which is how they got their name.

Electrons

Electrons occupy the space outside the nucleus. They move through a large region surrounding the nucleus called the electron cloud. Each electron carries a charge of −1. That negative charge exactly balances a proton's +1 charge, which means a neutral atom has equal numbers of protons and electrons.

ParticleLocationChargeRelative Mass
ProtonNucleus+11 amu
NeutronNucleus01 amu
ElectronElectron cloud−11/1836 amu (≈ 0)
Key Insight — Where the Mass Lives

Electrons weigh almost nothing compared to protons and neutrons. For all practical purposes, the mass of an atom comes entirely from the protons and neutrons in its nucleus. Electrons contribute so little that they are ignored in almost every mass calculation.

A Neutral Atom — Three Defining Features

Protons — determine what element the atom is. Never changes without a nuclear reaction.

Neutrons — contribute to mass. Can vary among atoms of the same element.

Electrons — equal to protons in a neutral atom, giving zero net charge.

Section 6.2

Atomic Number and Element Identity

The single most important number associated with any atom is its atomic number. The atomic number is the number of protons in the nucleus. No two elements share the same atomic number. Every atom with exactly 6 protons is carbon. Every atom with exactly 79 protons is gold. If you change the number of protons, you change the element — completely.

The periodic table is arranged in order of increasing atomic number. When you look at carbon's entry, the number 6 near its symbol is the atomic number. In a neutral atom, the number of electrons always equals the atomic number, because the positive and negative charges must balance.

ElementAtomic NumberProtons (neutral)Electrons (neutral)
Carbon666
Sodium111111
Chlorine171717
Gold797979
Common Misconception

Gaining or losing electrons does not change what element an atom is — that is set by the proton count alone. Only a nuclear reaction that adds or removes protons can change one element into another.

Section 6.3

Mass Number and Isotopes

The mass number is the sum of the protons and neutrons in the nucleus of a specific atom. Because protons and neutrons each have a mass of approximately 1 amu, the mass number is also a close approximation of the atom's mass in atomic mass units.

Key Formulas

Mass number = Number of protons + Number of neutrons

Number of neutrons = Mass number − Atomic number

Atoms of the same element that have different numbers of neutrons are called isotopes. Isotopes are chemically nearly identical because chemical behavior depends on electrons, and isotopes of the same element have the same number of electrons. They differ only in mass. Isotopes are named by their element name followed by their mass number: carbon-12, carbon-13, carbon-14.

Worked Example 6.1
Finding the number of neutrons
  • ProblemChlorine has atomic number 17. A particular chlorine atom has a mass number of 35. How many neutrons does it have?
  • SolveNeutrons = Mass number − Atomic number Neutrons = 35 − 17 = 18
  • Check17 protons + 18 neutrons = 35 total nucleons = mass number 35. ✓
Answer: This chlorine-35 atom has 17 protons, 18 neutrons, and (if neutral) 17 electrons.
Worked Example 6.2
Identifying isotopes
  • ProblemAtom A: 7 protons, 7 neutrons. Atom B: 7 protons, 8 neutrons. Are they isotopes?
  • ThinkBoth have 7 protons → both are nitrogen. Their neutron counts differ (7 vs. 8), giving mass numbers 14 and 15.
  • SolveSame element, different neutron count → same element, different mass number = isotopes.
Answer: Yes — nitrogen-14 and nitrogen-15 are isotopes of each other.
Section 6.4

Nuclear Symbols

The nuclear symbol is the standardized notation that communicates an isotope's element, mass number, and atomic number in one compact form. The mass number (A) appears as a superscript on the upper left of the element symbol; the atomic number (Z) appears as a subscript on the lower left.

Nuclear Symbol Format

ᴬX → mass number upper-left, atomic number lower-left, element symbol center

ᴢ

Reading the symbol gives you: element identity (symbol), proton count (Z), total nucleons (A), and by subtraction, the neutron count (A − Z).

Worked Example 6.3
Reading a nuclear symbol
  • ProblemA nuclear symbol shows 238 on the upper left, 92 on the lower left, and the symbol U. Identify all atomic information.
  • SolveElement: Uranium (U) Mass number: 238 Atomic number: 92 (protons) Neutrons: 238 − 92 = 146 Electrons: 92 (if neutral)
Answer: Uranium-238. 92 protons, 146 neutrons, 92 electrons (neutral).
Worked Example 6.4
Writing a nuclear symbol
  • ProblemAn oxygen atom has 8 protons and 10 neutrons. Write its nuclear symbol.
  • SolveElement symbol: O Atomic number: 8 Mass number: 8 + 10 = 18 Isotope name: Oxygen-18
  • ResultWrite 18 as the upper-left superscript, 8 as the lower-left subscript, and O as the symbol.
Answer: Oxygen-18 (¹⁸₈O). Mass number 18, atomic number 8.
Section 6.5

Average Atomic Mass

Most elements exist in nature as a mixture of isotopes. The percentages in which each isotope occurs in a natural sample are called its natural abundance. Because a natural sample of any element contains multiple isotopes in specific proportions, the periodic table reports a weighted average mass — not the mass of any single isotope, but the typical mass of an atom in a naturally occurring sample.

Average Atomic Mass Formula

Average atomic mass = (mass₁ × fraction₁) + (mass₂ × fraction₂) + ...

Fractional abundance = percentage ÷ 100. Multiply each isotope's mass by its fractional abundance, then sum all products.

Worked Example 6.5
Calculating average atomic mass — carbon
  • GivenCarbon-12: mass = 12.000 amu, abundance = 98.89%. Carbon-13: mass = 13.003 amu, abundance = 1.11%.
  • Step 1Convert to decimal fractions: 0.9889 and 0.0111.
  • Step 2Carbon-12: 12.000 × 0.9889 = 11.867 amu Carbon-13: 13.003 × 0.0111 = 0.144 amu
  • Step 311.867 + 0.144 = 12.011 amu
Answer: 12.011 amu — matches the periodic table value for carbon.
Worked Example 6.6
Calculating average atomic mass — chlorine
  • GivenChlorine-35: 34.969 amu, 75.77%. Chlorine-37: 36.966 amu, 24.23%.
  • SolveCl-35: 34.969 × 0.7577 = 26.496 amu Cl-37: 36.966 × 0.2423 = 8.957 amu Total: 26.496 + 8.957 = 35.453 amu
Answer: 35.45 amu. Because Cl-35 is ~75% abundant, the average is pulled close to 35 — as expected.
Reasonableness Check

The average atomic mass must always fall between the lightest and heaviest isotopes, and must be pulled toward the more abundant one. If your answer falls outside the range of isotope masses, recheck your arithmetic.

Section 6.6

Ions and Electric Charge

A neutral atom has equal numbers of protons and electrons. When an atom gains or loses electrons, this balance breaks, and the atom acquires a net electrical charge. An atom that has gained or lost electrons is called an ion.

Ion Charge Formula

Charge = Number of protons − Number of electrons

Positive result → positive ion (more protons than electrons)

Negative result → negative ion (more electrons than protons)

Worked Example 6.7
Finding ion charge
  • ProblemA magnesium atom (atomic number 12) loses 2 electrons. What is its charge?
  • SolveProtons: 12 Electrons: 12 − 2 = 10 Charge: 12 − 10 = +2
Answer: The magnesium ion has a charge of 2+. Written Mg²⁺.
Worked Example 6.8
Finding electrons in an ion
  • ProblemA sulfur ion (atomic number 16) has a charge of 2−. How many electrons does it have?
  • SolveCharge = Protons − Electrons −2 = 16 − Electrons Electrons = 16 + 2 = 18
Answer: The sulfide ion has 18 electrons.
Section 6.7

Cations and Anions

Ions are divided into two categories: cations (positively charged, formed when atoms lose electrons) and anions (negatively charged, formed when atoms gain electrons). Metals tend to lose electrons and form cations. Nonmetals tend to gain electrons and form anions.

IonSymbolProtonsElectronsType
Sodium ionNa⁺1110Cation
Magnesium ionMg²⁺1210Cation
Aluminum ionAl³⁺1310Cation

Oxide ionO²⁻810Anion

Naming Convention

Cations keep the element name: the sodium ion, the calcium ion. Anions formed from a single element change their ending to -ide: chloride, fluoride, oxide, sulfide, nitride. You will use this throughout Chapters 9 and 11.

Worked Example 6.9
Identifying cations and anions
  • Problem(a) A fluorine atom (atomic number 9) gains 1 electron. (b) A calcium atom (atomic number 20) loses 2 electrons. Identify each.
  • Solve (a)Gained electron → extra negative charge → anion. Fluoride ion, F⁻.
  • Solve (b)Lost electrons → more protons than electrons → cation. Calcium ion, Ca²⁺.
Answer: F⁻ is an anion. Ca²⁺ is a cation.
Section 6.8

Atomic Mass Units

A proton has a mass of approximately 1.67 × 10⁻²⁴ grams — an inconveniently small number for everyday chemistry. To avoid working with such tiny values, chemists use the atomic mass unit (amu). One atomic mass unit is defined as exactly one-twelfth the mass of a carbon-12 atom. Carbon-12 was chosen as the reference because it is stable, abundant, and precisely measurable.

ParticleMass in amuNote
Proton1.0073 amu≈ 1 amu for most calculations
Neutron1.0087 amu≈ 1 amu for most calculations
Electron0.000549 amuNegligible; ignored in mass calculations
Carbon-1212.000 amu (exact)The reference standard
Why Mass Numbers Are Good Approximations

Because each proton and neutron contributes almost exactly 1 amu, the mass number gives a very close estimate of any atom's mass in amu. The precise atomic masses on the periodic table reflect small corrections and isotope averaging, but mass numbers are excellent working approximations.

Section 6.9

Isotopes in Medicine, Dating, and Research

Isotopes are not just a theoretical concept. They have powerful real-world applications in medicine, archaeology, environmental science, geology, and fundamental research. Some isotopes are stable — they do not change over time. Others are unstable and eventually release energy and particles as their nuclei break apart. These unstable isotopes are called radioactive isotopes, or radioisotopes, and they are especially useful in medicine and dating.

Medical Imaging with Isotopes

In nuclear medicine, radioactive isotopes called tracers can be followed as they move through the body. Technetium-99m is given to patients (usually by injection) and detected by a camera that senses its radiation. Doctors can watch where it travels to identify problems in organs, bones, the heart, and the thyroid gland — millions of scans every year rely on this isotope. Iodine-131 concentrates specifically in the thyroid, making it useful for diagnosing thyroid disorders and treating thyroid cancer. PET scans use fluorine-18 to image metabolic activity in the brain, heart, and other organs.

Carbon-14 Dating

Carbon-14 is a radioactive isotope that forms naturally in the upper atmosphere. As long as a plant or animal is alive, it continuously takes in carbon — including a small, predictable fraction of carbon-14. When the organism dies, it stops taking in new carbon, and the carbon-14 already present begins to decay at a known rate. By measuring how much carbon-14 remains in an ancient sample, scientists can calculate when the organism died. This technique works for organic materials up to about 50,000 years old and has been used to date ancient wooden artifacts, charcoal, bone, fabric, and plant remains.

Radiometric Dating of Rocks

For materials millions or billions of years old, geologists use isotopes with much longer decay times. Uranium-238 decays to lead-206 over billions of years and is used to date ancient rocks and meteorites. Potassium-40 decays to argon-40 and is used to date volcanic rocks. These techniques have established that Earth is approximately 4.5 billion years old.

Stable Isotopes in Ecological Research

Stable isotopes that do not decay are also research tools. Different isotopes of the same element behave slightly differently in physical and chemical processes, allowing scientists to track them through ecosystems, food webs, water cycles, and climate records. The ratio of oxygen-18 to oxygen-16 in ancient ice cores reveals past temperatures. The ratio of nitrogen isotopes in animal tissues reveals what the animal ate. Carbon isotope ratios in ancient human bones have helped archaeologists reconstruct prehistoric diets.

Chapter 6 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 6.1Atoms consist of protons (+1, ≈1 amu) and neutrons (0, ≈1 amu) in the nucleus, and electrons (−1, ≈0 amu) in the electron cloud. Mass is almost entirely in the nucleus.
  • Sec 6.2The atomic number — number of protons — defines the element. No two elements share an atomic number. In a neutral atom, electrons equal protons.
  • Sec 6.3The mass number is protons + neutrons. Isotopes are atoms of the same element with different neutron counts and therefore different mass numbers. Neutrons = mass number − atomic number.
  • Sec 6.4The nuclear symbol places the mass number (upper left) and atomic number (lower left) next to the element symbol, encoding full atomic identity in one compact form.
  • Sec 6.5Average atomic mass is a weighted average of all naturally occurring isotopes, each multiplied by its fractional abundance. It represents the typical mass in a natural sample, not the mass of any single atom.
  • Sec 6.6Ions form when atoms gain or lose electrons. Charge = protons − electrons. Gaining electrons gives a negative charge; losing electrons gives a positive charge. The element identity (proton count) is unchanged.
  • Sec 6.7Cations are positively charged ions (lost electrons). Anions are negatively charged ions (gained electrons). Metals tend to form cations; nonmetals tend to form anions. Anion names end in -ide.
  • Sec 6.8One atomic mass unit is defined as exactly one-twelfth the mass of a carbon-12 atom. Protons and neutrons each mass ≈1 amu; electrons are negligible. Periodic table masses are in amu.
  • Sec 6.9Isotopes have wide real-world use: medical imaging (technetium-99m), cancer treatment (iodine-131), archaeological dating (carbon-14), geological dating (uranium-238), and ecological tracing (stable isotope ratios).

Key Terms Review

atomic number
The number of protons in the nucleus of an atom. Defines the element; never changes for a given element.
mass number
The total number of protons and neutrons in the nucleus of a specific atom. Mass number = protons + neutrons.
isotopes
Atoms of the same element with the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons, giving different mass numbers.
nuclear symbol
Standardized notation showing the element symbol with mass number as upper-left superscript and atomic number as lower-left subscript.
natural abundance
The percentage of each isotope found in a naturally occurring sample of an element.
average atomic mass
The weighted average of the masses of all naturally occurring isotopes, accounting for their relative abundances. Listed on the periodic table.
ion
An atom that has gained or lost electrons and therefore carries a net electrical charge.
cation
A positively charged ion formed when an atom loses one or more electrons. Charge = protons − electrons (positive).
anion
A negatively charged ion formed when an atom gains one or more electrons. Charge = protons − electrons (negative).
atomic mass unit (amu)
Standard unit of atomic mass, defined as exactly one-twelfth the mass of a carbon-12 atom. ≈ 1.66 × 10⁻²⁴ grams.
nucleus
The small, dense, positively charged center of an atom containing protons and neutrons. Contains essentially all of the atom's mass.
proton
A subatomic particle with a charge of +1 and a mass of ≈1 amu, located in the nucleus. The proton count defines the element.
neutron
A subatomic particle with no electrical charge and a mass of ≈1 amu, located in the nucleus. Varies among isotopes.
electron
A subatomic particle with a charge of −1 and negligible mass, found in the electron cloud. Determines chemical behavior.
radioisotope
An unstable isotope whose nucleus decays over time, releasing energy and particles. Used in medicine, dating, and research.

Chapter 6 focused on the nucleus — protons, neutrons, mass, isotopes, and ions. Now the focus shifts decisively to electrons. Electrons are the architects of chemical behavior. They determine how atoms bond, what reactions occur, and why the periodic table has the pattern it does. Chapter 7 introduces electron energy levels, the electron cloud model, orbital regions, electron configurations, valence electrons, and Lewis dot symbols. These ideas form the foundation for everything in Part III: Bonding, Compounds, and Molecular Structure.

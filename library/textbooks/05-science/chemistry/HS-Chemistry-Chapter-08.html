Chapter 8 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Imagine trying to organize a library with hundreds of thousands of books, but you have no system — no alphabetical order, no genres, no subjects. Finding anything would be nearly impossible. Before the periodic table existed, chemists faced something similar. By the mid-1800s, scientists had identified dozens of elements, each with its own properties, its own reactions, its own personality. But there was no map, no order, no way to see relationships at a glance.

The periodic table changed everything. Today it hangs in nearly every chemistry classroom and laboratory on Earth. Scientists in different countries, speaking different languages, all use the same table. It is one of the greatest organizing achievements in the history of science — not because it lists facts, but because it reveals patterns.

In Chapter 7, you learned how electrons are arranged in atoms and why valence electrons matter so much for chemical behavior. Chapter 8 builds directly on that foundation. The periodic table is, at its core, a map of electron arrangements. Once you understand how the table is organized and why, you will be able to use it to predict properties, explain trends, and make sense of chemistry that might otherwise seem like memorization.

Opening Question

Without looking at a periodic table, could you predict how many electrons an element in Group 16 would gain to become stable? Could you predict whether it would be larger or smaller than the element directly above it? By the end of this chapter, you will be able to answer both questions — and many more — using only an element's position on the table.

Section 8.1

Why the Periodic Table Matters

The Problem of Too Many Elements

By 1860, chemists had identified more than sixty elements. Each element had a name, a symbol, an atomic mass, and a set of physical and chemical properties. But the elements seemed disconnected — a long list without a story. Some chemists noticed patterns. Lithium, sodium, and potassium were all soft, reactive metals that reacted violently with water. Fluorine, chlorine, and bromine were all reactive nonmetals with sharp odors. These similarities suggested there might be an underlying order, if only someone could find it.

Mendeleev's Discovery

In 1869, Russian chemist Dmitri Mendeleev arranged the known elements in order of increasing atomic mass and noticed that properties repeated at regular intervals. He called this the periodic law: when elements are arranged by atomic mass, their properties repeat periodically. His genius was in trusting the pattern. When it predicted a gap, Mendeleev left the space empty and predicted the missing element's properties. When gallium (1875) and germanium (1886) were discovered, their properties matched his predictions almost exactly — confirming the table revealed something real about nature.

The Modern Periodic Table

Today's periodic table is arranged by increasing atomic number — the number of protons — rather than atomic mass. This adjustment resolved irregularities in Mendeleev's table and placed all elements in their correct positions. The modern table contains 118 confirmed elements in 18 vertical columns and 7 horizontal rows. Every element has a unique position, and that position reveals a great deal about its behavior. The table is not a list — it is a predictive tool. You will use it in nearly every remaining chapter of this course.

Why the Table Comes First

You will use the periodic table when you write chemical formulas, predict ion charges, balance equations, discuss solubility, study acids and bases, and analyze energy in reactions. Learning to read the table — not memorize it, but read it — is one of the most valuable skills in chemistry.

Section 8.2

Atomic Number and Periodic Order

The number of protons in the nucleus of an atom is its atomic number. Every atom of a given element has the same atomic number. Hydrogen has atomic number 1. Carbon has atomic number 6. Gold has atomic number 79. No two elements share the same atomic number — this is what makes each element unique. If you change the number of protons, you change the element. In the modern periodic table, elements are arranged left to right and top to bottom in order of increasing atomic number.

Reading an Element's Box

Each element on the periodic table appears in a box containing its symbol, atomic number, and atomic mass. The element symbol is a one- or two-letter abbreviation — the first letter is always capitalized, the second (if present) is always lowercase. The atomic number is usually printed above the symbol and tells you how many protons the neutral atom contains. The atomic mass is usually printed below the symbol and reflects the weighted average of all naturally occurring isotopes, expressed in atomic mass units (u). It is not a whole number because it reflects the natural mixture of isotopes.

Worked Example 8.1
Reading an Element Box — Silicon
  • GivenSilicon: atomic number 14, symbol Si, atomic mass 28.09.
  • ProtonsThe atomic number always equals the number of protons: 14 protons.
  • ElectronsIn a neutral atom, electrons equal protons: 14 electrons.
  • MassAverage atomic mass: 28.09 u.
Answer: 14 protons, 14 electrons, average mass ≈ 28.09 u.
Section 8.3

Periods and Groups

Periods and Groups Defined

Period — A horizontal row. There are seven periods. The period number tells you how many electron energy levels an element's atoms use.

Group (Family) — A vertical column. Groups are numbered 1 through 18. Elements in the same group have the same number of valence electrons and similar chemical properties.

Worked Example 8.2
Finding Valence Electrons from Position — Chlorine
  • PositionChlorine is in Period 3, Group 17.
  • LevelsPeriod 3 → electrons occupy 3 energy levels.
  • ValenceGroup 17 → 7 valence electrons. Configuration: 2-8-7.
  • BehaviorWith 7 valence electrons, chlorine is one short of the stable arrangement of 8. It is a reactive nonmetal that tends to gain one electron in reactions.
Answer: 7 valence electrons; tends to gain 1 electron; forms Cl⁻ ions.
The Key Insight

Elements in the same group behave similarly because they have the same valence electron count and the same general electron arrangement in their outermost shell. Valence electrons govern chemical behavior — and the periodic table maps them directly.

Reading the Staircase

On most printed periodic tables, a bold staircase line separates metals on the left from nonmetals on the right. Metalloids sit along this line. Elements to the left: metals. Elements to the right: nonmetals. Elements on the line: metalloids. This is the first thing to check when locating an unfamiliar element.

Mercury — The Metallic Exception

Mercury (Hg) is a metal yet is liquid at room temperature. Some alkali metals are so soft they can be cut with a knife and tarnish almost instantly in air. The periodic table includes many elements that challenge everyday mental images of what a metal looks like.

Section 8.5

Alkali Metals and Alkaline Earth Metals

Group 1: The Alkali Metals

Group 1 contains lithium (Li), sodium (Na), potassium (K), rubidium (Rb), cesium (Cs), and francium (Fr). Hydrogen is also in Group 1 but is a nonmetal and is treated separately. Each alkali metal has exactly 1 valence electron, making them extremely reactive. A single valence electron is easy to lose; these elements very readily give up that electron to form 1+ ions.

Alkali metals are soft enough to cut with a knife, shiny when freshly cut but tarnish quickly in air, lower in density than most metals, and have low melting points. They react violently with water, producing hydrogen gas and a metal hydroxide — with the reaction becoming more vigorous going down the group. Cesium reacts explosively with water. They never exist as free metals in nature.

Real-World Connections — Alkali Metals

Sodium is a component of table salt (NaCl), baking soda (NaHCO₃), and soap. Potassium is essential for human nerve and muscle function. Lithium powers rechargeable batteries in phones and electric vehicles. These familiar substances depend on the chemistry of Group 1.

Group 2: The Alkaline Earth Metals

Group 2 contains beryllium (Be), magnesium (Mg), calcium (Ca), strontium (Sr), barium (Ba), and radium (Ra). Each has 2 valence electrons. Alkaline earth metals are harder and denser than alkali metals and less violently reactive, since losing two electrons requires more energy than losing one. They form 2+ ions and are never found as free elements in nature. Calcium is the most abundant alkaline earth metal in the human body — the primary mineral in bones and teeth. Magnesium is a component of chlorophyll in plants and essential for many biological processes.

Section 8.6

Halogens and Noble Gases

Group 17: The Halogens

Group 17 contains fluorine (F), chlorine (Cl), bromine (Br), iodine (I), and astatine (At). The name "halogen" means "salt-former," describing their tendency to combine with metals to produce salts. Each halogen has 7 valence electrons — one short of the stable arrangement of 8 — making them highly reactive. They readily gain one electron to form 1– ions.

HalogenState at Room TemperatureAppearance
Fluorine (F₂)GasPale yellow
Chlorine (Cl₂)GasYellow-green, sharp bleach-like odor
Bromine (Br₂)LiquidReddish-brown (only nonmetal liquid at room temp)

This progression from gas to liquid to solid going down the group reflects increasing intermolecular forces as atoms become larger and heavier. Halogens react readily with alkali metals and alkaline earth metals to form ionic compounds. Fluorine is the most reactive of all nonmetals. Because of their reactivity, halogens are never found as free elements in nature.

Real-World Connections — Halogens

Chlorine is used in water treatment to kill pathogens. Fluoride compounds strengthen tooth enamel. Iodine is an essential nutrient for thyroid function. Fluorine compounds are used in nonstick cookware (Teflon) and refrigerants.

Group 18: The Noble Gases

Group 18 contains helium (He), neon (Ne), argon (Ar), krypton (Kr), xenon (Xe), and radon (Rn). Noble gases have completely filled outer electron shells — helium has 2 electrons filling its first shell; all others have 8 valence electrons. This complete arrangement is extremely stable, and noble gases have essentially no tendency to gain, lose, or share electrons. All are colorless, odorless gases at room temperature and exist as individual atoms (monatomic) rather than molecules.

The Noble Gas Configuration

The noble gas arrangement — 8 valence electrons (or 2 for helium) — is the stable electron configuration that many other elements attempt to achieve through bonding. This is the foundation of understanding ionic and covalent bonding in Chapters 9 and 10.

Section 8.7

Transition Metals

The transition metals occupy the wide central block (Groups 3–12) and include many familiar and economically important elements: iron (Fe), copper (Cu), zinc (Zn), gold (Au), silver (Ag), nickel (Ni), chromium (Cr), manganese (Mn), titanium (Ti), and cobalt (Co).

The Lanthanides and Actinides

At the bottom of the periodic table, two rows of elements — the lanthanides (elements 57–71) and actinides (elements 89–103) — are displayed separately to keep the main table from becoming too wide. The lanthanides (rare earth metals) are important in powerful magnets, electric motors, wind turbines, cell phones, and lasers. The actinides include uranium and thorium, which are important in nuclear chemistry (Chapter 26). Most actinides beyond uranium are synthetic.

Section 8.8

Periodic Trends

The arrangement of the periodic table reflects measurable trends in the properties of atoms. These periodic trends allow chemists to predict and compare properties without measuring every element independently. Three major trends are atomic radius, ionization energy, and electronegativity.

Atomic Radius

Atomic radius is a measure of the size of an atom — roughly the distance from the nucleus to the outermost electron.

Across a period (left to right): Atomic radius decreases. As atomic number increases, more protons pull the electron cloud closer to the nucleus. The number of energy levels stays the same across a period, but the electrons are drawn in more tightly.

Down a group (top to bottom): Atomic radius increases. Each new period adds another energy level, placing the outermost electrons farther from the nucleus. The atom grows larger even as the nuclear charge increases, because the added shells push the outer electrons out.

Ionization Energy

Ionization energy is the energy required to remove one electron from a neutral atom. It measures how strongly an atom holds onto its electrons.

Across a period (left to right): Ionization energy generally increases. Increasing nuclear charge holds electrons more tightly — it takes more energy to remove one.

Down a group (top to bottom): Ionization energy decreases. Outer electrons are farther from the nucleus and shielded by inner shells — they are easier to remove. This is why cesium is more reactive than lithium: cesium's valence electron is far easier to remove.

Electronegativity

Electronegativity measures how strongly an atom in a bond attracts shared electrons toward itself.

Across a period (left to right): Electronegativity increases — more protons pull shared electrons more strongly.

Down a group (top to bottom): Electronegativity decreases — outer electrons are held less tightly. Fluorine has the highest electronegativity of any element. It is the most powerful electron-attractor in chemistry.

Connecting Trends to Reactivity

Low ionization energy means an element easily loses electrons — reactive metal behavior. High ionization energy means an element holds electrons tightly — nonmetal behavior. All three trends point toward the upper right as the region of small, electron-hungry atoms, and the lower left as the region of large, electron-releasing atoms.

Section 8.9

Predicting Properties from Position

Four-Step Prediction Method

Step 1 — Region: Is the element metal (left), nonmetal (right), or metalloid (border)?

Step 2 — Group: How many valence electrons? What kind of ions does it form?

Step 3 — Period: How many energy levels? Where does it sit relative to others in its group?

Step 4 — Trends: Apply atomic radius, ionization energy, and electronegativity trends to compare with neighbors.

Worked Example 8.3
Predicting Properties from Position — Period 4, Group 16
  • Step 1Group 16 is on the right side — this is a nonmetal.
  • Step 2Group 16 → 6 valence electrons. Two short of 8 → tends to gain 2 electrons → forms 2– ion.
  • Step 3Period 4 → outermost electrons in the fourth energy level.
  • Step 4Compared to oxygen (Period 2, Group 16): larger atomic radius, lower ionization energy, less electronegative — all because going down a group shifts these properties in these directions.
Answer: The element is selenium (Se) — a reactive nonmetal forming 2– ions, larger and less electronegative than sulfur or oxygen above it.
Worked Example 8.4
Comparing Reactivity — Sodium vs. Cesium
  • BothSodium and cesium are in Group 1. Both have 1 valence electron and tend to lose it.
  • TrendGoing down Group 1, ionization energy decreases — the valence electron becomes easier to remove.
  • ConclusionCesium is farther down the group, has a lower ionization energy, and loses its electron more easily.
Answer: Cesium is more reactive than sodium. It reacts explosively with water at room temperature.
Worked Example 8.5
Comparing Atomic Size — Chlorine, Iodine, and Sulfur
  • Cl vs. IBoth in Group 17. Going down a group, atomic radius increases. Iodine (Period 5) is larger than chlorine (Period 3).
  • Cl vs. SBoth in Period 3. Going across a period left to right, atomic radius decreases. Sulfur (Group 16) is to the left of chlorine (Group 17), so sulfur is slightly larger.
Answer: Iodine > Chlorine > (close) Sulfur in terms of atomic radius.
Section 8.10

Using the Periodic Table in Chemistry Problems

Many students view the periodic table as a reference to check when they forget a symbol or atomic mass. That is a limited use of a very powerful tool. Here are the active ways you will use the table in chemistry problems throughout this course.

TaskHow the Table Helps
Finding atomic number and massRead directly from the element's box
Determining electron configurationCount valence electrons from group position; use period for shell count
Predicting ion chargeGroup 1 → 1+; Group 2 → 2+; Group 17 → 1–; Group 16 → 2–; Group 15 → 3–
Writing ionic formulasBalance positive and negative charges based on predicted ion charges
Predicting reactivityApply periodic trends to rank elements by ease of electron loss or gain
Identifying compound typeMetal + nonmetal → ionic; nonmetal + nonmetal → molecular
Worked Example 8.6
Predicting an Ionic Formula — Magnesium and Nitrogen
  • IonsMagnesium (Group 2) → Mg²⁺. Nitrogen (Group 15) needs 3 electrons to reach 8 → N³⁻.
  • BalanceNeed equal total positive and negative charge: 3 × (2+) = 6+; 2 × (3–) = 6–.
  • FormulaMg₃N₂
Answer: Mg₃N₂
Cautions When Using the Table

The atomic mass listed is an average of isotopic masses and is not a whole number. Do not confuse it with the mass number, which is always a whole number and refers to a specific isotope. Transition metals have variable charges that cannot be read directly from their group numbers the way main group charges can — additional context is usually needed. The table reveals a great deal but not everything: color, exact melting point, solubility, and specific reaction products require more detailed reference data.

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 8.1The periodic table arranges elements by atomic number, revealing that properties repeat periodically. Mendeleev's 1869 table was the first systematic organization; the modern table uses atomic number. The table is a predictive tool used throughout all of chemistry.
  • Sec 8.2Atomic number equals the number of protons and uniquely identifies each element. Each element box shows its symbol, atomic number, and atomic mass. Atomic mass is a weighted average of isotopic masses and is not a whole number.
  • Sec 8.3Periods are horizontal rows; the period number equals the number of electron energy levels. Groups are vertical columns; the group number (for main group elements) equals the number of valence electrons. Same group = same valence electrons = similar chemical behavior.
  • Sec 8.4Metals (left/center) are lustrous, conductive, malleable, and tend to lose electrons. Nonmetals (upper right) are poor conductors, brittle as solids, and tend to gain or share electrons. Metalloids (staircase border) have intermediate properties and are semiconductors important in electronics.
  • Sec 8.5Alkali metals (Group 1) have 1 valence electron, are extremely reactive, form 1+ ions, and react violently with water. Alkaline earth metals (Group 2) have 2 valence electrons, are less reactive, and form 2+ ions. Both groups are never found as free elements in nature.
  • Sec 8.6Halogens (Group 17) have 7 valence electrons, are the most reactive nonmetals, and form 1– ions; they progress from gas to liquid to solid going down the group. Noble gases (Group 18) have complete outer shells and are essentially unreactive; they exist as monatomic gases.
  • Sec 8.7Transition metals (Groups 3–12) are dense, hard metals with high melting points, variable oxidation states, brightly colored compounds, and significant catalytic activity. Lanthanides and actinides are displayed below the main table; many are important in technology and nuclear chemistry.
  • Sec 8.8Three major periodic trends: atomic radius increases down a group and decreases across a period. Ionization energy and electronegativity both increase across a period and decrease down a group. All three trends point to the upper right as the region of small, reactive nonmetals and the lower left as large, reactive metals.
  • Sec 8.9Position in the table enables prediction: identify the region (metal/nonmetal/metalloid), group (valence electrons and ion charge), period (energy levels), and then apply trends to compare relative size, reactivity, and electronegativity with neighboring elements.
  • Sec 8.10The periodic table is actively used to find atomic data, determine electron configurations, predict ion charges, write ionic formulas, rank reactivity, and identify compound types. Transition metal charges and exact physical properties require additional data beyond the table itself.

Key Terms Review

Atomic number
The number of protons in the nucleus of an atom. Each element has a unique atomic number. The periodic table is arranged in order of increasing atomic number.
Period
A horizontal row in the periodic table. The period number tells you how many electron energy levels the atoms of that element use.
Group (family)
A vertical column in the periodic table. Elements in the same group have the same number of valence electrons and similar chemical properties.
Metal
An element that is typically lustrous, malleable, ductile, and a good conductor of electricity and heat. Metals tend to lose electrons and form positive ions.
Nonmetal
An element that is typically a poor conductor, lacks metallic luster, and tends to gain or share electrons in reactions. Found in the upper right of the table.
Metalloid
An element with properties intermediate between metals and nonmetals. Found along the staircase border. Examples: silicon and germanium.
Semiconductor
A material that conducts electricity under some conditions but not others. Metalloids are natural semiconductors, fundamental to modern electronics and solar cells.
Alkali metals
Group 1 elements (Li, Na, K, Rb, Cs, Fr). Soft, highly reactive metals with 1 valence electron that react vigorously with water and form 1+ ions.
Alkaline earth metals
Group 2 elements (Be, Mg, Ca, Sr, Ba, Ra). Harder and less reactive than alkali metals, with 2 valence electrons and 2+ ions.
Halogens
Group 17 elements (F, Cl, Br, I, At). Highly reactive nonmetals with 7 valence electrons that form 1– ions and readily combine with metals to form salts.
Noble gases
Group 18 elements (He, Ne, Ar, Kr, Xe, Rn). Essentially unreactive elements with complete outer electron shells; monatomic gases at room temperature.
Transition metals
Elements in Groups 3–12. Dense, hard metals with complex electron configurations, often forming multiple types of ions and brightly colored compounds.
Lanthanides
Elements 57–71, displayed below the main table. Also called rare earth metals; used in powerful magnets, electric motors, and high-tech devices.
Actinides
Elements 89–103, displayed below the lanthanides. Include naturally occurring uranium and several synthetic elements important in nuclear chemistry.
Atomic radius
A measure of the size of an atom. Increases down a group; decreases across a period from left to right.
Ionization energy
The energy needed to remove one electron from a neutral atom. Increases across a period from left to right; decreases down a group.
Electronegativity
A measure of how strongly an atom in a bond attracts shared electrons toward itself. Fluorine has the highest electronegativity. Increases across a period; decreases down a group.
Periodic law
The principle that the properties of elements repeat periodically when elements are arranged in order of increasing atomic number.
Periodic trend
A pattern in properties that repeats regularly across periods or down groups of the periodic table, enabling prediction without measuring each element independently.

Common Misconceptions

Misconception 1 — "The atomic mass is the mass of one atom"
The atomic mass is the exact mass of every atom of that element.

The atomic mass listed is a weighted average of all naturally occurring isotopes. No single atom has exactly this mass. Carbon's listed mass is 12.011 u, but individual atoms are carbon-12, carbon-13, or the very rare carbon-14 — never 12.011 exactly.

Misconception 2 — "Elements in the same group have identical properties"
Same group = same properties in all respects.

Elements in the same group are similar, not identical. They share valence electron count and general chemical behavior but differ in size, reactivity, melting point, and physical state. Fluorine is a gas; iodine is a solid. Lithium reacts moderately with water; cesium explodes. Same family — very different specifics.

Misconception 3 — "Larger atoms are always heavier"
Atomic size and atomic mass increase together across a period.

Atomic radius and atomic mass are related but not the same. Across a period, atoms get smaller even as their mass increases, because increasing nuclear charge pulls electrons in more tightly. A large atom is not necessarily the heaviest in its period.

Misconception 4 — "Noble gases cannot form any compounds"
Noble gases are completely inert and cannot bond under any conditions.

Xenon (Xe) and krypton (Kr) can form compounds under special conditions with fluorine or oxygen. Xenon difluoride (XeF₂) is a real compound that can be purchased and used in chemistry. Noble gases are extremely unreactive but are not absolutely inert.

Misconception 5 — "The periodic table is just a memorization chart"
The table's only purpose is to look up element symbols and masses.

The periodic table is a predictive tool with embedded relationships. Its organization reflects deep physical laws about atomic structure, electron behavior, and chemical properties. Students who treat it as a memorization chart miss its greatest value: the ability to reason from position to behavior without having memorized every element.

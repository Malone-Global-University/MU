Chapter 25 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Almost everything that makes up a living body, a wooden table, a plastic bottle, a strand of nylon, a drop of gasoline, or a tablet of medicine has one thing in common: it is built mostly from carbon. Of the more than one hundred elements on the periodic table, carbon alone forms the backbone of millions of known compounds — more than all other elements combined. This is not an accident. Carbon has a unique set of bonding abilities that allow it to form long chains, branched structures, and rings, often combined with hydrogen, oxygen, nitrogen, sulfur, and other elements.

The branch of chemistry devoted to carbon-containing compounds is called organic chemistry. The name comes from an old — and now outdated — idea that such compounds could only be produced by living organisms. Chemists eventually learned to synthesize organic compounds in the laboratory, but the name remained. Today, organic chemistry includes everything from the simplest natural gas molecule to the most complex protein, and it touches fuels, plastics, fabrics, paints, foods, medicines, pesticides, and the molecules of life itself.

This chapter is an introduction, not a full organic chemistry course. Its goal is to give you the vocabulary, the structural reasoning, and the pattern-recognition skills you need to understand how carbon compounds are built, named, classified, and connected to the world around you.

Opening Question

Two molecules can have the exact same molecular formula — the same number of carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen atoms — yet one is a sweet-smelling flavoring used in food, and the other has completely different properties. How can two molecules made of identical atoms behave so differently? By the end of this chapter, you will be able to answer this question using the idea of structure: not just what atoms a molecule contains, but how those atoms are arranged and connected.

Section 25.1

Why Carbon Is Special

Carbon sits in Group 14 of the periodic table, with the electron configuration 2, 4. That means carbon has four valence electrons — exactly half of a stable octet. Instead of gaining or losing electrons to reach a noble gas configuration (as ionic-bonding elements do), carbon almost always shares electrons, forming four covalent bonds to other atoms. This single fact — four bonds per carbon atom — is the starting point for everything in organic chemistry.

Because carbon can form four bonds, it can bond to four different atoms (such as hydrogen, oxygen, nitrogen, or halogens), to other carbon atoms building chains, to other carbon atoms building branched structures, and to other carbon atoms building closed rings. The ability of an element to form bonds with itself, creating chains and rings, is called catenation. Carbon shows catenation to an extraordinary degree.

PropertyCarbonTypical Comparison
Valence electrons4Oxygen has 6 (forms 2 bonds); nitrogen has 5 (forms 3 bonds)
Maximum covalent bonds4Hydrogen forms only 1 bond
CatenationExtensive — chains, branches, rings of any lengthSilicon forms some Si–Si chains, but they are far less stable
Bond strength (C–C, C–H)Strong and stable under normal conditionsMany element–element bonds are weaker or more reactive
Key Idea

Carbon's combination of four bonds, strong C–C and C–H bonds, and extensive catenation allows it to form an essentially unlimited number of stable molecular "skeletons." Every other atom in an organic molecule — hydrogen, oxygen, nitrogen, sulfur, phosphorus, or a halogen — attaches to this carbon skeleton and gives the molecule its specific identity and behavior.

Drawing Carbon's Four Bonds

When you draw a carbon atom in a structure, count the bonds and lone connections around it. A neutral carbon atom should always have exactly four bonds total — whether that means four single bonds, two single bonds and one double bond, one single bond and one triple bond, or two double bonds. If your drawing shows a carbon with three or five bonds, something is wrong with the structure.

Section 25.2

Hydrocarbons

The simplest organic compounds contain only two elements: carbon and hydrogen. These compounds are called hydrocarbons. Hydrocarbons form the "core" of organic chemistry because every more complicated organic molecule can be thought of as a hydrocarbon skeleton with one or more atoms or groups attached in place of hydrogen atoms.

Hydrocarbons are extracted in enormous quantities from petroleum (crude oil) and natural gas, both of which formed over millions of years from the remains of ancient organisms. Refineries separate crude oil into different hydrocarbon fractions based on the size of the molecules, producing everything from gasoline and diesel fuel to lubricating oils and waxes.

Saturated and Unsaturated Hydrocarbons

ClassificationCarbon–Carbon BondsDescription
Saturated hydrocarbonAll single (C–C) bondsCarbon atoms are bonded to as many hydrogen atoms as possible
Unsaturated hydrocarbonOne or more double (C=C) or triple (C≡C) bondsFewer hydrogen atoms than a saturated hydrocarbon with the same number of carbons

Hydrocarbon Combustion

Hydrocarbons are widely used as fuels because burning them releases a large amount of energy. The general pattern for the complete combustion of a hydrocarbon in oxygen is:

hydrocarbon + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O + energy

For example, the combustion of methane, the main component of natural gas, is:

CH₄ + 2 O₂ → CO₂ + 2 H₂O
Hydrocarbons as a Starting Point

Whenever you encounter an unfamiliar organic molecule, first look for the carbon "skeleton." If the molecule contains only carbon and hydrogen, it is a hydrocarbon. If it contains other elements (oxygen, nitrogen, halogens, and so on), think of it as a hydrocarbon skeleton with one or more hydrogen atoms replaced by a functional group — a concept developed fully in Section 25.6.

Section 25.3

Alkanes, Alkenes, and Alkynes

The simplest hydrocarbons can be sorted into three families based on the type of carbon–carbon bonding they contain: alkanes, alkenes, and alkynes. Each family follows a predictable pattern in its molecular formula and its name.

Alkanes — Single Bonds Only

Alkanes are saturated hydrocarbons containing only single bonds. Their general formula is CnH(2n+2), where n is the number of carbon atoms. Alkane names end in -ane, and the first part of the name (the "stem") tells you how many carbon atoms are in the chain.

Carbons (n)StemAlkane NameMolecular Formula
1meth-methaneCH₄
2eth-ethaneC₂H₆
3prop-propaneC₃H₈
4but-butaneC₄H₁₀
5pent-pentaneC₅H₁₂
6hex-hexaneC₆H₁₄
7hept-heptaneC₇H₁₆
8oct-octaneC₈H₁₈
Learn These Prefixes

These eight stems (meth-, eth-, prop-, but-, pent-, hex-, hept-, oct-) appear throughout organic chemistry — not only in alkane names but also in the names of alkenes, alkynes, alcohols, and many other compound families. Learning this list well makes every later section much easier.

Example 25.3.1
Using the General Formula for Alkanes
  • ProblemWhat is the molecular formula of decane, the alkane with 10 carbon atoms?
  • ThinkUse the general formula CnH(2n+2) with n = 10.
  • Solve Number of hydrogens = 2n + 2 = 2(10) + 2 = 22 Formula: C₁₀H₂₂
  • CheckCompare to octane (n = 8): C₈H₁₈. Decane has 2 more carbons, so it should have 2 × 2 = 4 more hydrogens: 18 + 4 = 22. ✓
Answer: C₁₀H₂₂

Alkenes and Alkynes

Alkenes are unsaturated hydrocarbons containing at least one carbon–carbon double bond (C=C). The general formula for an alkene with one double bond is CnH(2n). Alkene names end in -ene. The two-carbon alkene is ethene (C₂H₄), commonly known as ethylene — an important industrial chemical used to make polyethylene plastic.

Alkynes are unsaturated hydrocarbons containing at least one carbon–carbon triple bond (C≡C). The general formula for an alkyne with one triple bond is CnH(2n−2). Alkyne names end in -yne. The simplest alkyne is ethyne (C₂H₂), better known as acetylene, used in welding torches.

FamilyBond TypeGeneral FormulaName Ending2-Carbon Example
AlkaneSingle bonds only (C–C)CₙH₍₂ₙ₊₂₎-aneethane, C₂H₆
AlkeneAt least one C=CCₙH₍₂ₙ₎-eneethene, C₂H₄
AlkyneAt least one C≡CCₙH₍₂ₙ₋₂₎-yneethyne, C₂H₂
Example 25.3.2
Finding a Formula from a Name
  • ProblemWhat is the molecular formula of propene?
  • Think"Prop-" means 3 carbons. "-ene" means an alkene, so use CnH(2n) with n = 3.
  • Solve Number of hydrogens = 2n = 2(3) = 6 Formula: C₃H₆
  • CheckCompare to propane, C₃H₈. An alkene has 2 fewer hydrogens than the corresponding alkane. 8 − 2 = 6. ✓
Answer: C₃H₆
Example 25.3.3
Classifying a Hydrocarbon from Its Formula
  • ProblemA hydrocarbon has the molecular formula C₅H₈. Classify it as an alkane, alkene, or alkyne.
  • ThinkFind the alkane formula for 5 carbons: CnH(2n+2) = C₅H₁₂. Compare to the given formula.
  • Solve C₅H₁₂ (alkane) has 12 hydrogens C₅H₈ has 8 hydrogens Difference = 12 - 8 = 4 hydrogens "missing" Each triple bond removes 4 H → one triple bond matches
  • CheckFor an alkyne: CnH(2n−2) = C₅H₈. This matches exactly. ✓
Answer: C₅H₈ is consistent with an alkyne (one triple bond). It could also represent a molecule with two double bonds or a ring plus a double bond — full structural information would be needed to decide.
Student Warning — Don't Mix Up the Endings

The endings -ane, -ene, and -yne differ by only one or two letters but represent very different molecules with different formulas, different reactivity, and different properties. Always read the ending carefully and double-check by comparing the hydrogen count to the general formulas above.

Section 25.4

Structural Formulas

A molecular formula such as C₄H₁₀ tells you what atoms are present and how many of each, but it does not tell you how those atoms are connected. For most purposes in organic chemistry, the arrangement of atoms matters just as much as their identity — sometimes even more. Chemists use several types of formulas to represent organic molecules, each suited to a different purpose.

Formula TypeWhat It ShowsExample (butane)
Molecular formulaTotal count of each type of atomC₄H₁₀
Structural formulaEvery atom and every bond, drawn outAll H atoms and bonds explicit
Condensed structural formulaGroups of atoms written together; bonds between groups impliedCH₃CH₂CH₂CH₃
Skeletal (line-angle) formulaCarbon and hydrogen atoms not written explicitly; lines represent bonds and carbon atomsA zig-zag line with 3 segments

Condensed Structural Formulas

A condensed structural formula groups the hydrogen atoms with the carbon atom they are attached to, without drawing each individual C–H bond. For example, butane (C₄H₁₀) is written as CH₃–CH₂–CH₂–CH₃. This tells you there are four carbon atoms in a row (a "backbone"), the two end carbons each have three hydrogens (CH₃ groups), and the two middle carbons each have two hydrogens (CH₂ groups).

Skeletal (Line-Angle) Formulas

For larger molecules, drawing every carbon and hydrogen atom becomes cumbersome. Skeletal formulas simplify this by using line segments to represent carbon–carbon bonds, with the understanding that each end or bend (vertex) of a line represents a carbon atom, and each carbon atom is assumed to have enough hydrogen atoms attached to give it a total of four bonds. Atoms other than carbon and hydrogen are written explicitly.

Example 25.4.1
Converting Between Formula Types
  • ProblemPropane has the molecular formula C₃H₈. Write its condensed structural formula, and describe its skeletal formula.
  • ThinkPropane is an alkane with 3 carbons in a row. End carbons will be CH₃ groups; the middle carbon will be CH₂, bonded to two other carbons.
  • Solve Condensed structural formula: CH₃-CH₂-CH₃ Carbon count: 1 + 1 + 1 = 3 ✓ Hydrogen count: 3 + 2 + 3 = 8 ✓ Skeletal formula: a simple zig-zag line with two segments (3 vertices = 3 carbons)
  • CheckC₃H₈ matches the alkane formula CnH(2n+2) for n = 3: 2(3) + 2 = 8. ✓
Answer: CH₃–CH₂–CH₃; a two-segment zig-zag line.
Counting Hydrogens in a Skeletal Formula

To "fill in" the hydrogens on a skeletal formula, look at each carbon (each vertex or endpoint) and count how many bonds to other atoms are already drawn. Since every carbon must have four bonds total, the remaining bonds are filled with hydrogen atoms. An endpoint carbon with one line needs three hydrogens (CH₃); a middle carbon with two lines needs two hydrogens (CH₂).

Section 25.5

Isomers

Molecular formulas alone do not uniquely identify a compound. Two or more compounds can share the exact same molecular formula but have different structures — different arrangements of the same atoms. These compounds are called isomers.

Key Idea

Isomers have the same molecular formula but different structural formulas, and therefore often have different physical and chemical properties — different boiling points, melting points, densities, reactivities, and uses — even though they are built from identical sets of atoms.

Structural Isomers of Butane

Consider the molecular formula C₄H₁₀. There are two different ways to arrange four carbon atoms:

IsomerStructure DescriptionCondensed Structural Formula
Butane (n-butane)A straight chain of four carbonsCH₃CH₂CH₂CH₃
Isobutane (2-methylpropane)A chain of three carbons with a fourth carbon branching off the middleCH₃CH(CH₃)CH₃

Both molecules have the formula C₄H₁₀ — 4 carbon atoms and 10 hydrogen atoms — but they are different compounds. Butane has a boiling point of about −1 °C, while isobutane boils at about −12 °C. This difference arises purely from the difference in shape: straight-chain molecules pack together and attract one another more effectively than branched molecules of the same size.

Example 25.5.1
Verifying an Isomer's Molecular Formula
  • ProblemVerify that isobutane's structure gives the correct molecular formula C₄H₁₀.
  • ThinkIn isobutane, a central carbon is bonded to three CH₃ groups and one H atom.
  • Solve Central carbon: bonded to 3 CH₃ groups + 1 H = 4 bonds ✓ Carbon count: 1 (central) + 3 (from CH₃ groups) = 4 carbons Hydrogen count: 3 × 3 (from CH₃) + 1 (on central C) = 10 hydrogens Formula: C₄H₁₀ ✓
  • CheckMatches CnH(2n+2) for n = 4: 2(4) + 2 = 10. ✓
Answer: Isobutane correctly gives C₄H₁₀ — confirming the two are isomers.
Isomer Number Increases Quickly

As the number of carbon atoms increases, the number of possible structural isomers increases very rapidly. Butane (C₄H₁₀) has 2 isomers; decane (C₁₀H₂₂) has 75. This rapid growth is one major reason organic chemistry includes so many distinct compounds within a single hydrocarbon family.

Student Warning — Same Formula Does Not Mean Same Substance

Do not assume that two molecules with the same molecular formula are the same compound. And do not assume that two compounds with similar formulas (such as C₄H₁₀ and C₅H₁₂) are isomers. Isomers must have the identical molecular formula — same count of each type of atom — but different structures.

Section 25.6

Functional Groups

If hydrocarbons are the "skeleton" of organic molecules, functional groups are the parts that give a molecule its characteristic chemistry. A functional group is a specific group of atoms within a molecule that is responsible for the molecule's typical chemical reactions and many of its physical properties. The same functional group tends to behave similarly no matter what hydrocarbon skeleton it is attached to — this is one of the most powerful organizing ideas in organic chemistry.

Functional GroupStructureExampleClass Name
Hydroxyl–OHCH₃CH₂OH (ethanol)Alcohol
Carbonyl (mid-chain)C=O on interior carbonCH₃COCH₃ (acetone)Ketone
Carbonyl (end of chain)C=O on end carbon, with HHCHO (formaldehyde)Aldehyde
Carboxyl–COOHCH₃COOH (acetic acid)Carboxylic acid
Amino–NH₂CH₃NH₂ (methylamine)Amine
Ether linkage–O– between carbon groupsCH₃OCH₃ (dimethyl ether)Ether
Halide–F, –Cl, –Br, –ICH₃Cl (chloromethane)Alkyl halide
Ester linkage–COO–CH₃COOCH₃ (methyl acetate)Ester

How Functional Groups Change Properties

Replacing a single hydrogen atom on a hydrocarbon with a functional group can dramatically change the molecule's behavior. For example, ethane (CH₃CH₃) is a nonpolar gas with a very low boiling point (about −89 °C). Ethanol (CH₃CH₂OH) — the same two-carbon backbone, but with one hydrogen replaced by an –OH group — is a polar liquid at room temperature with a boiling point of about 78 °C, and it mixes completely with water. This dramatic change occurs because the –OH group can participate in hydrogen bonding (Chapter 12), giving ethanol much stronger intermolecular attractions than ethane.

Example 25.6.1
Identifying a Functional Group
  • ProblemThe molecule CH₃CH₂COOH contains which functional group, and to which class does it belong?
  • ThinkLook for a recognizable group of atoms from the functional group table. The ending "–COOH" matches the carboxyl group.
  • Solve Functional group present: -COOH (carboxyl group) Class: carboxylic acid
  • CheckCarboxylic acids commonly have names ending in "-oic acid," which matches "propanoic acid." ✓
Answer: Propanoic acid contains a carboxyl group (–COOH) and belongs to the carboxylic acid class.
Functional Groups and Chapter 23

Carboxylic acids are organic acids — they can donate a hydrogen ion (H⁺) in water, connecting directly to the acid-base chemistry of Chapter 23. Vinegar, for example, is a dilute solution of acetic acid (CH₃COOH), a carboxylic acid.

Section 25.7

Polymers

Many of the most important organic materials — both natural and synthetic — are polymers: very large molecules built by linking together many smaller, repeating units called monomers. The word polymer comes from Greek roots meaning "many parts," and monomer means "one part."

PolymerMonomer(s)TypeCommon Use
PolyethyleneEthene (ethylene), C₂H₄SyntheticPlastic bags, bottles, containers
Polyvinyl chloride (PVC)Chloroethene (vinyl chloride)SyntheticPipes, flooring, insulation
PolystyreneStyreneSyntheticFoam packaging, disposable cups
NylonTwo different monomers joined repeatedlySyntheticFabrics, rope, fishing line

CelluloseGlucose units (different linkage)NaturalStructural material in plant cell walls
ProteinsAmino acidsNaturalStructure, enzymes, transport in living things
DNANucleotidesNaturalGenetic information storage

Addition and Condensation Polymerization

Polymers form through reactions called polymerization. In addition polymerization, monomers containing a double bond open up their double bonds and link directly to one another, with no atoms lost. Polyethylene forms this way from ethene molecules. In condensation polymerization, monomers join together with the loss of a small molecule — usually water — at each connection point. Many natural polymers, including proteins and starch, form this way.

The important conceptual point is that polymers are built from repeating smaller units, and that the identity of the monomer determines the properties of the resulting material — its strength, flexibility, melting behavior, and chemical resistance.

Example 25.7.1
Recognizing a Repeating Unit
  • ProblemPolyethylene can be represented by the repeating unit –(CH₂–CH₂)–, repeated thousands of times. What monomer was used to make this polymer?
  • ThinkThe repeating unit –(CH₂–CH₂)– contains 2 carbons and 4 hydrogens. Compare this to the alkenes from Section 25.3.
  • Solve Repeating unit: CH₂-CH₂ → C₂H₄ C₂H₄ matches ethene (the 2-carbon alkene)
  • CheckEthene's double bond (CH₂=CH₂) opens up during addition polymerization, producing the repeating –(CH₂–CH₂)– pattern. ✓
Answer: The monomer is ethene (ethylene), C₂H₄.
Natural Polymers Are Everywhere

Many of the molecules that make up living things are polymers: proteins are polymers of amino acids, starch and cellulose are polymers of glucose, and DNA and RNA are polymers of nucleotides. You will study these natural polymers in detail in Chapter 28.

Section 25.8

Organic Compounds in Fuels, Foods, and Medicines

Fuels

Gasoline, diesel fuel, natural gas, and propane are all mixtures dominated by hydrocarbons. As discussed in Section 25.2, the combustion of these hydrocarbons releases energy that powers engines, heats homes, and generates electricity. The energy content of a fuel is closely related to the number and type of carbon–carbon and carbon–hydrogen bonds present, a topic connected to the bond-energy ideas of Chapter 20.

Foods

The three major classes of organic molecules in food — carbohydrates, lipids (fats), and proteins — are all built primarily from carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen (with nitrogen also present in proteins). Each of these classes will be studied in more chemical detail in Chapter 28.

Food ClassTypical ElementsFunctional Groups PresentExample
CarbohydrateC, H, OMultiple –OH groups, carbonyl groupsGlucose
Lipid (fat)C, H, OEster linkages, long hydrocarbon chainsVegetable oil
ProteinC, H, O, N (often S)Amino (–NH₂) and carboxyl (–COOH) groupsEnzymes, muscle protein

Medicines

Most medicines are organic molecules carefully designed so that their shape and functional groups allow them to interact with specific molecules in the body — often proteins called receptors or enzymes. Even small structural changes — the kind of changes that distinguish isomers (Section 25.5) — can change how a medicine behaves in the body, which is why pharmaceutical chemists pay extremely close attention to molecular structure, not just molecular formula.

Structure Determines Function

Throughout this section, notice a recurring theme: the function of an organic molecule — as a fuel, a nutrient, or a medicine — depends on its structure: the arrangement of its carbon skeleton and the functional groups attached to it. This "structure determines function" idea is one of the most important unifying themes in all of chemistry and biology.

Section 25.9

Organic Chemistry and Living Systems

Every living thing on Earth — from the simplest bacterium to the largest tree or animal — is built primarily from organic molecules: carbohydrates, lipids, proteins, and nucleic acids, along with water and a relatively small number of dissolved ions and minerals. This is sometimes summarized by saying that life is carbon-based.

Why carbon? The same properties introduced in Section 25.1 — four bonds per atom, strong and stable C–C and C–H bonds, and extensive catenation — make carbon uniquely suited to building the enormous variety of large, stable, yet chemically active molecules that living systems require. A protein, for example, may contain thousands of carbon atoms arranged into a precise three-dimensional shape, with functional groups positioned exactly where they are needed to carry out a specific job.

The organic chemistry concepts introduced in this chapter — hydrocarbon skeletons, structural formulas, isomers, functional groups, and polymers — are not separate from biology; they are the chemical vocabulary used to describe biological molecules.

Organic Chemistry ConceptBiological Connection
Hydrocarbon chains and ringsBackbones of fatty acids, sugars, and many hormones

Chapter 25 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 25.1Carbon's four valence electrons allow it to form four covalent bonds, and carbon's extensive catenation — its ability to form stable chains, branches, and rings — explains why organic chemistry includes far more known compounds than the chemistry of any other element.
  • Sec 25.2Hydrocarbons contain only carbon and hydrogen. They are classified as saturated (only single C–C bonds) or unsaturated (containing C=C or C≡C bonds), and hydrocarbon combustion produces carbon dioxide, water, and energy.
  • Sec 25.3The three major hydrocarbon families are alkanes (CnH(2n+2), "-ane"), alkenes (CnH(2n), "-ene"), and alkynes (CnH(2n−2), "-yne"), named using the standard carbon-count prefixes (meth-, eth-, prop-, but-, pent-, hex-, hept-, oct-).
  • Sec 25.4Four types of formulas are used in organic chemistry: molecular, structural, condensed structural, and skeletal (line-angle) formulas — each showing different levels of structural detail.
  • Sec 25.5Isomers have identical molecular formulas but different structures, illustrated by butane and isobutane. Isomers often have different physical and chemical properties because their atoms are connected differently.
  • Sec 25.6Functional groups — hydroxyl, carbonyl, carboxyl, amino, ether, halide, and ester groups — are the parts of organic molecules responsible for characteristic reactivity and properties, including boiling point, solubility, and acidity.
  • Sec 25.7Polymers are large molecules built from repeating monomer units, formed by addition polymerization (no atoms lost) or condensation polymerization (small molecule released at each join). Both synthetic polymers (polyethylene, PVC, nylon) and natural polymers (starch, cellulose, proteins, DNA) follow this pattern.
  • Sec 25.8Organic chemistry connects to fuels (hydrocarbon combustion), foods (carbohydrates, lipids, and proteins), and medicines (structure-dependent interactions with the body). The theme "structure determines function" runs through all three areas.
  • Sec 25.9Life is carbon-based. The vocabulary of this chapter — skeletons, functional groups, isomers, and polymers — is the same vocabulary used to describe biological molecules, and forms a direct bridge to Chapter 28.

Key Terms Review

organic chemistry
The branch of chemistry that studies carbon-containing compounds.
catenation
The ability of an element's atoms to bond to one another, forming chains, branches, and rings. Carbon shows this to an extraordinary degree.
hydrocarbon
A compound made up of only carbon and hydrogen atoms.
saturated hydrocarbon
A hydrocarbon containing only single carbon–carbon bonds, "filled" with as many hydrogen atoms as possible.
unsaturated hydrocarbon
A hydrocarbon containing at least one carbon–carbon double or triple bond.
alkane
A saturated hydrocarbon with the general formula CnH(2n+2), named with the ending "-ane."
alkene
An unsaturated hydrocarbon containing at least one C=C double bond, named with the ending "-ene."
alkyne
An unsaturated hydrocarbon containing at least one C≡C triple bond, named with the ending "-yne."
molecular formula
A formula showing the total number of each type of atom in a molecule, without showing connections.
structural formula
A formula showing every atom and every bond in a molecule.
condensed structural formula
A formula that groups atoms attached to each carbon together, with bonds between groups implied.
skeletal formula
A simplified formula in which carbon and hydrogen atoms are not written explicitly; lines represent the carbon skeleton and bonds.
isomers
Compounds that have the same molecular formula but different structural formulas.
functional group
A specific group of atoms within a molecule responsible for many of its characteristic chemical reactions and properties.
alcohol
An organic compound containing a hydroxyl (–OH) functional group.
carboxylic acid
An organic compound containing a carboxyl (–COOH) functional group.
amine
An organic compound containing an amino (–NH₂ or related) functional group.
polymer
A large molecule made of many repeating smaller units called monomers.
monomer
A small molecule that can join with others through polymerization to form a polymer.
addition polymerization
Polymerization in which monomers with double bonds join directly, without losing any atoms.

Common Misconceptions

Misconception 1 — "Organic means it came from a living thing or a farm."
Organic compounds can only come from living organisms.
Correction: In chemistry, "organic" simply means "containing carbon." Organic compounds can be synthesized in laboratories and factories and do not need to come from living organisms or be related to food labeling.
Misconception 2 — "Same molecular formula = same compound."
C₄H₁₀ must always be the same substance.
Correction: Molecules with the same molecular formula but different structures are isomers — different compounds that can have very different properties, as shown by butane and isobutane.
Misconception 3 — "More carbon atoms always means unpredictable names."
Organic naming has no pattern beyond a few simple molecules.
Correction: The carbon-count prefixes (meth-, eth-, prop-, but-, pent-, hex-, hept-, oct-…) follow a consistent, learnable pattern that applies across alkanes, alkenes, alkynes, alcohols, and many other organic families.
Misconception 4 — "A polymer is just a big version of its monomer."
Polyethylene is just a lot of ethene molecules stuck together with the same properties.
Correction: A polymer's properties (strength, flexibility, melting point, etc.) often differ dramatically from those of the small monomer molecules it is built from. Ethene is a gas; polyethylene is a solid plastic.
Misconception 5 — "Functional groups only matter for naming compounds."
Functional groups are just naming conventions with no chemical significance.
Correction: Functional groups largely determine a molecule's physical properties (such as boiling point and solubility) and chemical reactivity (such as acidity). Naming is simply a way of communicating which functional groups are present.

Chapter 26: Nuclear Chemistry

Chapter 25 explored how carbon atoms bond to one another and to other elements to build the molecules of fuels, materials, foods, medicines, and living things — all of this chemistry takes place among the electrons surrounding atomic nuclei, while the nuclei themselves remain unchanged. Chapter 26 shifts attention to the nucleus itself.

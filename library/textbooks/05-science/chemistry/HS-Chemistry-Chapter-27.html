Chapter 27 — Front Matter

Chapter Introduction

Chemistry does not stay in the laboratory. It flows through rivers, rises into the atmosphere, feeds crops, powers cities, and shapes the products that billions of people depend on every day. Every glass of clean drinking water, every breath of manageable city air, every bag of fertilizer spread across a field, and every piece of recycled plastic represents chemical knowledge applied at an enormous scale.

Chapter 27 connects the principles you have studied throughout this course to the real world of water systems, air quality, soils, fuels, materials, manufacturing, and public health. This chapter is not a chemistry elective. It is chemistry in its most consequential form. When chemists understand the mechanisms behind pollution, contamination, combustion, and corrosion, they gain the ability to design systems that protect people, reduce harm, and use resources wisely.

This chapter approaches these topics through evidence, measurement, mechanism, and tradeoffs. Chemistry does not always produce clean and simple answers to environmental problems. What it does produce is the ability to describe problems precisely, measure them accurately, and evaluate potential solutions honestly.

Why This Chapter Matters

Environmental problems are chemical problems at their core. When a lake becomes too acidic for fish to survive, that is an acid-base problem. When iron bridges corrode in salty air, that is a redox problem. When drinking water contains lead ions, that is an ionic chemistry problem. When the atmosphere traps more heat than it used to, that involves the molecular structure and infrared behavior of gases. Chemists are essential to identifying, measuring, explaining, and mitigating these problems.

Section 27.1

Chemistry at Civilization Scale

Every structure of modern civilization depends on chemistry. The food supply depends on fertilizers, pesticides, soil amendments, and food preservation chemistry. Water supplies depend on treatment processes that remove pathogens, dissolved solids, and toxic compounds. Energy systems depend on the combustion of fuels, the electrochemistry of batteries, and increasingly on photovoltaic and hydrogen-based technology. Materials — from steel to concrete to glass to semiconductors to pharmaceuticals — are products of chemical understanding applied at industrial scale.

When chemists talk about environmental and industrial chemistry, they are studying the intersection of chemical knowledge with large-scale human systems. This is different from studying a single reaction in a beaker. The same principles of bonding, reaction, equilibrium, solubility, acid-base chemistry, and thermochemistry still apply. But the scale changes everything. A reaction that produces a small amount of carbon dioxide in a laboratory produces billions of tons of it each year across the global energy system. A reaction that makes a useful solvent in a factory may also produce a toxic byproduct that contaminates groundwater for decades.

Key Idea — Scale Changes Consequences

A single log burning in a fireplace produces soot and gases. That same combustion chemistry, operating through millions of vehicles and power plants, produces enough particulate matter and nitrogen oxides to change the air quality of entire cities. Understanding chemistry at civilization scale means learning to ask not just "Does this reaction occur?" but "What are the products? Where do they go? What happens to them in the environment? Who is affected? What are the tradeoffs?"

The Chemist's Role in Environmental Questions

Environmental problems are chemical problems at their core. When a lake becomes too acidic for fish to survive, that is an acid-base problem. When iron bridges corrode in salty air, that is a redox problem. When drinking water contains lead ions, that is an ionic chemistry problem. When the atmosphere traps more heat than it used to, that involves the molecular structure and infrared behavior of gases. Chemists are not the only professionals who address these problems, but they are essential to identifying, measuring, explaining, and mitigating them.

Responsible environmental chemistry requires honesty about what is known and what is uncertain. It requires measuring concentrations accurately, reporting data carefully, and distinguishing between chemical facts and policy choices. Policy choices — decisions about acceptable risk, economic tradeoffs, and regulatory standards — involve more than chemistry. But those decisions should be informed by chemistry.

Section 27.2

Water Chemistry and Water Treatment

Water is the most important solvent on Earth. Liquid water covers most of the planet's surface, moves through the atmosphere as vapor, and fills the bodies of every living organism. Water's chemical properties — its polarity, its ability to form hydrogen bonds, its capacity to dissolve ionic compounds and polar molecules, its resistance to temperature change — make it uniquely suited for the roles it plays in chemistry, biology, geology, and engineering.

What Makes Water Effective as a Solvent

Water molecules are polar. Each water molecule has a partial negative charge near the oxygen atom and partial positive charges near the hydrogen atoms. This polarity allows water to dissolve ionic compounds by surrounding and separating their ions. The partial negative oxygen atoms attract cations; the partial positive hydrogen atoms attract anions. Polar covalent compounds also dissolve in water because their charges interact with water's polar structure.

Nonpolar substances do not dissolve well in water. Oils, waxes, and many organic compounds are nonpolar and form separate layers or droplets when added to water. This distinction — polar dissolves in polar, nonpolar dissolves in nonpolar — determines which substances are carried in water systems and which accumulate in fatty tissues or organic sediments.

Natural Water Chemistry

Natural water is never purely H₂O. Rainwater absorbs carbon dioxide from the atmosphere, forming carbonic acid (H₂CO₃), which gives natural precipitation a slightly acidic pH of around 5.6. As water moves over and through rock, it dissolves mineral compounds, picking up calcium ions (Ca²⁺), magnesium ions (Mg²⁺), sodium ions (Na⁺), bicarbonate ions (HCO₃⁻), sulfate ions (SO₄²⁻), and many other dissolved species. Water also picks up organic material from decaying plant and animal matter, suspended sediments, and dissolved gases.

Tip — Eutrophication

Excess nitrogen and phosphorus cause rapid algae growth (an algal bloom). When the algae die and decompose, bacteria consume dissolved oxygen to break down the organic matter, leaving too little oxygen for fish and other aquatic life. This chain of events — nutrients → algae → decomposition → oxygen loss — is called eutrophication.

Municipal Water Treatment

Municipal water treatment systems use a series of chemical and physical processes to make water safe for drinking.

Process 27.2.1
Coagulation and Flocculation
  • PurposeRemove suspended dirt, clay, bacteria, and organic particles from water.
  • MethodAluminum sulfate (Al₂(SO₄)₃) or iron salts are added to water. These react with water to form sticky aluminum hydroxide or iron hydroxide particles called floc, which attract and bind to suspended particles, clumping them into larger aggregates that settle out.
  • Reaction Al₂(SO₄)₃ + 6 H₂O → 2 Al(OH)₃ + 3 H₂SO₄
  • Follow-upThe sulfuric acid produced can lower pH, so treatment plants also add a base such as lime (Ca(OH)₂) to maintain proper pH.
Result: Suspended particles clump into floc and settle from the water column.
StepProcessWhat It Does
1SedimentationFloc-particle aggregates settle to the bottom of large holding tanks and are removed as sludge
2FiltrationWater passes through layers of sand, gravel, and activated charcoal, which adsorbs dissolved organic compounds, chlorine byproducts, and some contaminants onto its enormous surface area
3DisinfectionChlorine, ozone, or UV light kills remaining pathogens
4pH adjustmentBrings treated water to a slightly alkaline pH (about 7.5–8.5) to minimize pipe corrosion
5FluoridationAdds fluoride ions at low concentration (about 0.7 mg/L in the U.S.) to strengthen tooth enamel
Tip — Adsorption vs. Absorption

Activated charcoal works by adsorption — molecules stick to the surface of the charcoal. This is different from absorption, in which molecules are taken into the interior of a material. Activated charcoal's enormous surface area (created by its porous structure) is what makes it effective at removing dissolved organic compounds.

Disinfection Chemistry

Chlorine gas (Cl₂) or sodium hypochlorite (NaOCl) is a powerful oxidizing agent that destroys bacterial cell membranes and inactivates viruses:

Cl₂ + H₂O → HCl + HOCl

Hypochlorous acid (HOCl) is the active disinfecting species — it is more effective than the hypochlorite ion (OCl⁻), which forms in alkaline water. This is why maintaining proper pH in treated water matters.

Disinfection Byproducts (DBPs)

Chlorine treatment can react with naturally occurring organic compounds in water to form disinfection byproducts such as trihalomethanes (THMs), which are of concern at high concentrations. Treatment plants monitor and control DBP levels carefully. Ozone (O₃) and ultraviolet (UV) light are alternatives that avoid chlorinated byproducts, but both require a follow-up residual disinfectant to protect water as it travels through distribution pipes.

Fluoridation strengthens tooth enamel by replacing hydroxide ions in the mineral hydroxyapatite with fluoride ions, forming fluorapatite, which is more resistant to acid attack.

Wastewater Treatment

StageProcess
Primary treatmentRemoves large solids through screening and sedimentation
Secondary treatmentBiological processes — bacteria consume dissolved organic matter, converting it to CO₂, water, and bacterial biomass (sludge)
Tertiary treatmentAddresses remaining nutrients, pharmaceuticals, pathogens, and fine particles using chemical precipitation, activated charcoal, or advanced filtration

The chemical goal throughout wastewater treatment is to return water to a condition safe for the receiving environment, reducing biochemical oxygen demand (BOD), nutrient concentrations, and pathogen levels.

Section 27.3

Air Quality and Atmospheric Chemistry

The atmosphere is a complex chemical system. It contains nitrogen (N₂, approximately 78%), oxygen (O₂, approximately 21%), argon (Ar, about 0.9%), and trace gases including carbon dioxide (CO₂), methane (CH₄), ozone (O₃), water vapor, and many other compounds. Human activities add pollutants to this system, changing its chemistry in ways that affect human health, ecosystems, materials, and climate.

Photochemical Smog

Ground-level ozone forms in the atmosphere when nitrogen oxides and volatile organic compounds react in the presence of sunlight:

NOₓ + VOCs + sunlight → O₃ + other products

The resulting brownish haze — ground-level ozone, nitrogen dioxide, and particulate matter interacting in sunlight — is called photochemical smog.

Two Kinds of Ozone

Ozone in the upper atmosphere (stratosphere) protects life from ultraviolet radiation and is beneficial. Ozone at ground level is a powerful oxidant that irritates lung tissue, damages crops, and degrades rubber and plastics — it is harmful. Do not confuse the two: same molecule, very different consequences depending on location.

Sulfur Dioxide and Acid Rain

Coal, oil, and natural gas contain sulfur compounds. When burned, the sulfur is oxidized to sulfur dioxide, which can then be further oxidized and combine with water to form sulfuric acid.

Process 27.3.1
Formation of Acid Rain
  • Step 1Sulfur in fuel is oxidized during combustion:S + O₂ → SO₂
  • Step 2Sulfur dioxide is further oxidized in the atmosphere:2 SO₂ + O₂ → 2 SO₃
  • Step 3Sulfur trioxide reacts with water vapor to form sulfuric acid:SO₃ + H₂O → H₂SO₄
  • Nitrogen pathwayNitrogen oxides similarly form nitric acid:4 NO + 3 O₂ + 2 H₂O → 4 HNO₃
Result: Precipitation with pH significantly below the natural value of 5.6 — acid rain that acidifies lakes and streams, leaches soil nutrients, damages forests, and corrodes stone, metal, and concrete.
Tip — Scrubbers

Power plants reduce SO₂ emissions using scrubbers, which react flue gases with limestone or lime slurry:

CaCO₃ + SO₂ → CaSO₃ + CO₂   or   Ca(OH)₂ + SO₂ → CaSO₃ + H₂O

The calcium sulfite produced can be further oxidized to calcium sulfate (gypsum), which has commercial uses in construction.

Carbon Monoxide

Carbon monoxide (CO) is produced by incomplete combustion — combustion that occurs without enough oxygen to fully oxidize carbon to carbon dioxide.

Combustion TypeReactionProduct
Complete combustionC + O₂ → CO₂Carbon dioxide
Incomplete combustion2 C + O₂ → 2 COCarbon monoxide
Why CO Is Dangerous

Carbon monoxide binds to hemoglobin in the blood much more strongly than oxygen does. Hemoglobin bound to CO cannot carry oxygen to body tissues. Exposure to high concentrations of CO causes oxygen deprivation, loss of consciousness, and can be fatal. Proper ventilation and combustion engineering minimize CO emissions.

Particulate Matter

Particulate matter (PM) is classified by particle size. PM₁₀ refers to particles less than 10 micrometers in diameter; PM₂.₅ refers to particles less than 2.5 micrometers. Smaller particles penetrate more deeply into lung tissue and are more damaging to health. Particulates come from combustion of solid fuels, diesel engines, construction dust, wildfires, and industrial processes, and are associated with respiratory and cardiovascular disease at elevated concentrations.

The Ozone Layer

The stratospheric ozone layer absorbs ultraviolet (UV) radiation from the sun, preventing much of it from reaching Earth's surface. Without this protection, rates of skin cancer, cataracts, and damage to plant and animal life would increase dramatically.

Natural Ozone Formation

Ozone is created in the stratosphere when UV radiation splits oxygen molecules:

O₂ + UV → 2 O

O + O₂ → O₃

Naturally, ozone is also broken down by UV, maintaining a dynamic balance.

Process 27.3.2
CFCs and the Ozone Destruction Chain Reaction
  • BackgroundChlorofluorocarbons (CFCs) — synthetic compounds once widely used in refrigerants and aerosol propellants — were found to deplete stratospheric ozone.
  • ReleaseIn the stratosphere, UV radiation breaks down CFCs, releasing chlorine atoms:CF₂Cl₂ + UV → CF₂Cl + Cl
  • Chain reactionThe chlorine atom destroys ozone in a repeating cycle: Cl + O₃ → ClO + O₂ ClO + O → Cl + O₂
  • SignificanceA single chlorine atom can destroy thousands of ozone molecules before being removed from the atmosphere. The chlorine is regenerated at the end of each cycle, allowing the chain to continue.
Result: The Montreal Protocol (1987) phased out CFCs internationally. Stratospheric ozone levels have been recovering since then, demonstrating that chemistry-based environmental interventions can work.

Section 27.4

Soil Chemistry and Agriculture

Soil is not simply dirt. It is a complex chemical system containing mineral particles, organic matter, water, gases, and living organisms. Healthy soil chemistry supports plant growth and agricultural productivity. Poor soil chemistry — whether from nutrient depletion, acidification, contamination, or salinization — makes agriculture difficult or impossible.

Essential Plant Nutrients

NutrientRole in the PlantAbsorbed Form
Nitrogen (N)Proteins, nucleic acids, chlorophyllNO₃⁻ or NH₄⁺
Phosphorus (P)ATP, DNA, cell membranesH₂PO₄⁻ or HPO₄²⁻
Potassium (K)Enzyme function, water regulation, ion transportK⁺
Secondary macronutrientsVarious structural and regulatory rolesCa²⁺, Mg²⁺, SO₄²⁻
MicronutrientsNeeded in very small amountsFe, Mn, Zn, Cu, B, Mo

The major macronutrients — nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium — are abbreviated NPK on fertilizer labels. Although N₂ gas makes up most of the atmosphere, plants cannot use gaseous nitrogen directly; it must be converted to nitrate or ammonium ions first.

The Nitrogen Cycle

Four Key Transformations

Nitrogen fixation — converts atmospheric N₂ into NH₃ or NH₄⁺. Carried out by certain bacteria (e.g., Rhizobium species in legume roots) or industrially by the Haber-Bosch process (Section 27.7).

Nitrification — bacterial conversion of NH₄⁺ to NO₂⁻ and then to NO₃⁻, the primary form most crops absorb.

Denitrification — converts NO₃⁻ back to N₂ or N₂O gas in waterlogged, low-oxygen soils, returning nitrogen to the atmosphere but reducing plant-available nitrogen.

Decomposition — organic matter is broken down by soil bacteria and fungi, releasing nitrogen as NH₄⁺ back into the available pool.

Soil pH and Chemistry

Soil pH affects which nutrients are available to plants. Most crops grow best in slightly acidic to neutral soil, roughly pH 6 to 7.

Soil ConditionEffect on Nutrient Availability
Low pH (acidic)Aluminum and manganese become more soluble and can reach toxic concentrations; phosphate becomes less available; calcium and magnesium leach more easily
High pH (alkaline)Iron, manganese, zinc, and copper become less soluble and may cause deficiency even when present in the soil
Tip — Adjusting Soil pH

Farmers raise soil pH by adding lime (calcium carbonate, CaCO₃, or calcium hydroxide, Ca(OH)₂). They lower soil pH by adding elemental sulfur, which oxidizes in soil to form sulfuric acid.

Fertilizers

Commercial fertilizers supply nutrients in forms plants can absorb. Nitrogen fertilizers include ammonia (NH₃), ammonium nitrate (NH₄NO₃), urea (CO(NH₂)₂), and ammonium sulfate ((NH₄)₂SO₄). Potassium fertilizers come mainly from mined deposits of potassium chloride (KCl) or potassium sulfate (K₂SO₄).

Process 27.4.1
The Superphosphate Process
  • PurposeConvert insoluble phosphate rock into a soluble, plant-available form.
  • ReactionCa₃(PO₄)₂ + 2 H₂SO₄ → Ca(H₂PO₄)₂ + 2 CaSO₄
  • ResultThe calcium dihydrogen phosphate, Ca(H₂PO₄)₂, produced is soluble and plant-available.
Answer: Treating phosphate rock with sulfuric acid produces soluble superphosphate fertilizer.
Nutrient Runoff

Excess nitrogen and phosphorus that are not absorbed by crops wash into rivers and lakes, causing eutrophication (Section 27.2). Careful nutrient management — applying the right amount at the right time in the right place — reduces runoff while maintaining crop productivity.

Pesticides

Pesticides are chemicals that kill insects (insecticides), weeds (herbicides), or fungi (fungicides) that reduce crop yield. Some pesticides are biodegradable — they break down through chemical or biological reactions. Others are persistent — they resist breakdown and accumulate in soil, water, and food chains. Organochlorine pesticides such as DDT were found to accumulate in fatty tissues of animals through bioaccumulation, reaching harmful concentrations at higher levels of the food chain (biomagnification, discussed further in Section 27.8). Integrated pest management (IPM) combines targeted pesticide use with biological controls, crop rotation, and resistant plant varieties to minimize chemical use while controlling pests effectively.

Section 27.5

Fuels and Combustion Products

Combustion is one of the most widely used chemical reactions in civilization. Fuels — coal, petroleum, natural gas, wood, and increasingly hydrogen and biofuels — are burned to release energy for heating, electricity generation, transportation, and manufacturing.

Fossil Fuels

Fossil fuels are hydrocarbons and related organic compounds formed over millions of years from the remains of ancient organisms. They store chemical energy in carbon-hydrogen and carbon-carbon bonds.

FuelCompositionCombustion Reaction
Natural gasPrimarily methane (CH₄), with some ethane and propaneCH₄ + 2 O₂ → CO₂ + 2 H₂O
Petroleum (crude oil)Complex mixture of hydrocarbons, refined into gasoline, diesel, jet fuel, etc.2 C₈H₁₈ + 25 O₂ → 16 CO₂ + 18 H₂O (octane, approx.)
CoalMostly carbon, with hydrogen, sulfur, nitrogen, and trace metalsProduces CO₂, H₂O, SO₂, NOₓ, and ash

Combustion Products and Their Effects

Complete combustion of a pure hydrocarbon produces only carbon dioxide and water. In practice, fuels are impure and combustion conditions are imperfect, producing a range of products.

ProductHow It FormsEffect
Carbon dioxide (CO₂)Complete combustion of carbon-containing fuelsPrimary greenhouse gas; absorbs and re-emits infrared radiation, warming the lower atmosphere
Water vapor (H₂O)Combustion of hydrogen-containing fuelsAlso a greenhouse gas

Sulfur dioxide (SO₂)Sulfur impurities in coal and oilPrecursor to acid rain

Alternative Fuels

Hydrogen

Hydrogen burns cleanly, producing only water:

2 H₂ + O₂ → 2 H₂O

Hydrogen is not a fossil fuel — it must be produced from water or other hydrogen-containing compounds using energy. Currently, most hydrogen is produced by steam reforming of methane, which still produces CO₂. Electrolysis of water using renewable electricity can produce hydrogen without carbon emissions.

Tip — Biofuels

Biofuels are derived from plant or animal matter. Ethanol (C₂H₅OH) made from corn or sugarcane can replace or blend with gasoline; biodiesel is made from vegetable oils or animal fats. Biofuels are renewable but still produce CO₂ on combustion. The net carbon effect depends on how much CO₂ was removed from the atmosphere by the plants during growth.

Section 27.6

Plastics, Materials, and Recycling

Plastics are organic polymers — long chains of repeating molecular units called monomers. They are lightweight, durable, chemically resistant, and easily molded. These properties have made plastics central to packaging, construction, medicine, electronics, transportation, and countless other applications.

How Plastics Are Made

Addition Polymerization

Monomers containing double bonds join together without losing any atoms. Ethylene (C₂H₄) polymerizes to form polyethylene:

n CH₂=CH₂ → −(CH₂−CH₂)ₙ−

The subscript n indicates that the unit repeats many thousands of times. Common addition polymers include polyethylene (PE), polypropylene (PP), polyvinyl chloride (PVC), and polystyrene (PS).

Tip — Condensation Polymerization

Condensation polymerization joins monomers with the release of a small molecule, usually water. Nylon is a condensation polymer formed from diamine and diacid monomers. Polyester (used in bottles labeled PET — polyethylene terephthalate) is another condensation polymer.

Properties of Common Plastics

PlasticResin CodeProperties & Uses
PET (polyethylene terephthalate)1Used for beverage bottles; one of the most commonly recycled plastics
HDPE (high-density polyethylene)2Stiff; used in bottles and pipes
PVC (polyvinyl chloride)3Rigid (pipes, window frames) or flexible with plasticizers (flooring, cables); contains chlorine, produces HCl and dioxins if burned
LDPE (low-density polyethylene)4Flexible; used in plastic bags and films
PP (polypropylene)5Heat-resistant; used in food containers, bottle caps, medical equipment
PS (polystyrene)6Used in disposable cups, food trays, packaging foam; brittle, difficult to recycle

Environmental Concerns About Plastics

Plastics persist in the environment because most are not biodegradable — they are not broken down by soil bacteria or other biological processes on human timescales. Instead, plastics fragment into smaller and smaller pieces under UV radiation and physical stress, eventually becoming microplastics — particles smaller than 5 millimeters. Microplastics have been found in oceans, rivers, soil, drinking water, and animal tissues.

Three Chemical Concerns

Plastics may contain additives (plasticizers, flame retardants, stabilizers) that can leach into food, water, or body tissues — bisphenol A (BPA) is one such compound studied for potential hormonal effects. Plastics floating in the ocean concentrate persistent organic pollutants (POPs) from seawater onto their surfaces, which marine organisms can then consume along with the plastic. Burning plastics in open fires or inadequate incinerators releases toxic compounds, including HCl from PVC and polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs) from other plastics.

Recycling Chemistry

MethodProcessLimitation
Mechanical recyclingSorting, cleaning, melting, and re-forming plasticsMost effective for single-resin streams; mixing different plastics produces poor-quality material because melting points and molecular structures are incompatible
Chemical recyclingBreaks polymer chains back into monomers via depolymerization, pyrolysis, or solvolysisCan handle mixed plastics but requires significant energy input

Recycling rates for most plastics remain low in most countries, largely because of sorting challenges, contamination, and economics. Reducing plastic use and designing products for recyclability from the start are complementary strategies.

Section 27.7

Industrial Chemical Processes

Several industrial chemical processes are so large in scale and so important to civilization that every chemistry student should understand their basic chemistry.

Process 27.7.1
The Haber-Bosch Process — Ammonia Synthesis
  • ReactionN₂ + 3 H₂ ⇌ 2 NH₃ ΔH = −92 kJ/mol
  • ChallengeThis reaction is exothermic but has a very large activation energy — it requires breaking the triple bond of N₂, one of the strongest bonds in chemistry. The reaction is also reversible, so it reaches equilibrium before completion.
  • Conditions Temperature: approximately 400–500°C (high enough for reasonable rate, but not so high that equilibrium shifts to disfavor NH₃)
    Pressure: approximately 150–300 atmospheres (high pressure favors the product side because 4 moles of gas become 2)
    Catalyst: iron with potassium and aluminum oxide promoters
  • Le ChâtelierHigh pressure favors the side with fewer moles of gas (products); moderate temperature balances rate and equilibrium; the catalyst speeds the reaction without changing the equilibrium position.
Significance: Approximately half of the nitrogen atoms in the proteins of people alive today came from Haber-Bosch ammonia. Without synthetic nitrogen fertilizers, current agricultural output could not support the world's population.
The Tradeoff

The Haber-Bosch process consumes enormous amounts of energy (mainly natural gas), and the nitrogen it releases into agriculture has large-scale effects on natural ecosystems, including eutrophication of waterways and emission of nitrous oxide (N₂O), a potent greenhouse gas.

Process 27.7.2
The Contact Process — Sulfuric Acid
  • BackgroundSulfuric acid (H₂SO₄) is the industrial chemical produced in the largest quantity globally, used in fertilizer production, metal processing, oil refining, paper manufacture, explosives, and more.
  • Step 1Sulfur is burned to produce sulfur dioxide:S + O₂ → SO₂
  • Step 2Sulfur dioxide is oxidized to sulfur trioxide using a vanadium pentoxide (V₂O₅) catalyst at about 450°C:2 SO₂ + O₂ ⇌ 2 SO₃ This step is also an equilibrium. High pressure and lower temperature favor SO₃ production, but temperature must be high enough for the catalyst to function. About 99.5% conversion is achieved.
  • Step 3Sulfur trioxide is absorbed in concentrated sulfuric acid to form oleum, which is diluted with water to produce concentrated sulfuric acid: SO₃ + H₂SO₄ → H₂S₂O₇ H₂S₂O₇ + H₂O → 2 H₂SO₄
Note: SO₃ cannot be absorbed directly in water because the reaction produces so much heat and acid mist that it cannot be controlled safely — this is why the oleum intermediate step is necessary.
Process 27.7.3
The Chlor-Alkali Process
  • MethodElectrolysis of concentrated sodium chloride solution (brine) produces three important industrial chemicals simultaneously.
  • Cathode2 H₂O + 2 e⁻ → H₂ + 2 OH⁻
  • Anode2 Cl⁻ → Cl₂ + 2 e⁻
  • Overall2 NaCl + 2 H₂O → Cl₂ + H₂ + 2 NaOH
Products: Chlorine (Cl₂) for water disinfection, PVC production, pharmaceuticals, and bleach; sodium hydroxide (NaOH) for paper production, aluminum refining, soap making, and chemical manufacturing; hydrogen (H₂) as a fuel and in chemical synthesis.

Petroleum Refining

Crude oil is a complex mixture of thousands of different hydrocarbons with different chain lengths, structures, and boiling points. Petroleum refining separates and transforms these compounds into useful products.

FractionCarbon RangeUse
Petroleum gasC₁–C₄Fuel gas
GasolineC₅–C₁₂Motor fuel
Kerosene / jet fuelC₁₀–C₁₆Aviation and heating fuel
Diesel fuelC₁₄–C₂₀Diesel engines

Tip — Cracking, Reforming, and Treating

Cracking breaks long-chain hydrocarbons into shorter, more valuable molecules. Thermal cracking uses heat; catalytic cracking uses a zeolite catalyst (fluid catalytic cracking, or FCC, is widely used to increase gasoline yield from heavier fractions). Reforming rearranges molecular structures to produce branched and aromatic hydrocarbons with higher octane ratings. Treating removes sulfur compounds by reacting them with hydrogen over a catalyst to produce hydrogen sulfide (H₂S), which is recovered and converted to elemental sulfur or sulfuric acid.

Section 27.8

Chemical Risk and Toxicity

Every substance is toxic at some dose. Water causes hyponatremia (low blood sodium) if consumed in excessive quantities. Caffeine is lethal at very high doses. The difference between a medicine and a poison often lies in the dose. This foundational principle of toxicology — associated with the 16th-century physician Paracelsus — is sometimes stated as: "The dose makes the poison."

Measuring Toxicity

MeasureDefinitionExample
LD₅₀Lethal dose for 50% of a test population (mg/kg body weight); lower LD₅₀ = more acutely toxicBotulinum toxin ≈ 1–2 ng/kg; table salt (NaCl) ≈ 3,000 mg/kg
LC₅₀Lethal concentration for 50% of a test populationUsed for inhaled gases or dissolved water pollutants
NOAELNo Observed Adverse Effect Level — the highest dose at which no harmful effects are detectedUsed with safety factors to set exposure limits for pesticides, additives, and pollutants

Bioaccumulation and Biomagnification

Some substances are not easily broken down by biological systems. They accumulate in fatty tissues because they are lipid-soluble and nonpolar. As larger animals eat many smaller ones, the concentration in fat tissue increases at each step in the food chain. This is biomagnification.

Case Example 27.8.1
DDT and Biomagnification
  • Base of chainSmall organisms in a lake: DDT concentration ≈ 0.000003 ppm
  • Small fishEating those organisms: DDT concentration ≈ 0.2 ppm
  • Large fishEating small fish: DDT concentration ≈ 2 ppm
  • Fish-eating birdsDDT concentrations reached levels that caused eggshell thinning, preventing successful reproduction in species such as eagles.
Key idea: Understanding biomagnification requires understanding both the chemistry of a substance (Is it fat-soluble? Is it biodegradable?) and the structure of food webs.

Risk Assessment

Hazard + Exposure = Risk

Risk has two components: hazard (how harmful the substance can be) and exposure (how much and how often people are actually exposed). A very hazardous substance with zero exposure poses no risk. A mildly hazardous substance with constant high exposure may pose significant risk.

Regulatory agencies combine hazard assessment (toxicology studies, epidemiology) with exposure assessment (measured concentrations in air, water, food, consumer products) to estimate risk and set standards — often with large safety factors to protect sensitive populations such as children and people with health conditions.

Section 27.9

Chemistry, Sustainability, and Public Health

Chemistry has created enormous benefits for human civilization — fertilizers that feed billions, medicines that prevent and cure disease, materials that build shelter and infrastructure, processes that provide clean water and sanitation. Chemistry has also created problems — persistent pollutants, toxic waste, greenhouse gas emissions, resource depletion. The same knowledge and tools that produce harm can be redirected to prevent it.

Green Chemistry

Selected Principles of Green Chemistry

Developed by Paul Anastas and John Warner, the twelve principles of green chemistry guide the design of chemical products and processes that reduce or eliminate the use and generation of hazardous substances. Selected principles include:

• Prevent waste rather than treating it after the fact
• Design syntheses to use and produce substances with little or no toxicity
• Design chemicals that degrade after use into innocuous products
• Use renewable feedstocks rather than fossil fuels
• Use catalysts rather than stoichiometric reagents to improve efficiency
• Minimize energy use and use renewable energy
• Design processes to minimize the potential for accidents, explosions, and fires

Tip — Green Chemistry Is a Design Challenge

Green chemistry is not a compromise of chemical effectiveness — it is a design challenge. Developing a synthesis that is both effective and clean is harder than developing one that is merely effective. It requires deep understanding of reaction mechanisms, thermodynamics, kinetics, and materials chemistry.

Sustainability in Chemical Systems

Sustainability in chemistry means meeting current needs without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their needs. This requires thinking about resources (are the materials renewable?), energy (where does it come from and at what cost?), waste (where does it go and what does it do?), and toxicity (who is harmed and how?).

AreaChemistry Involved
Renewable energy chemistryPhotovoltaic cells (semiconductor chemistry), wind turbine materials (polymer composites, rare earth magnets), battery chemistry (lithium-ion, solid state), hydrogen fuel cells
Carbon capture and storageChemical and geological methods for capturing CO₂ from combustion and sequestering it underground or converting it to useful materials
Biobased materialsReplacing petroleum-derived polymers with materials from plant biomass, including bioplastics made from polylactic acid (PLA) derived from corn starch or sugarcane
Circular economyDesigning products and industrial systems so materials cycle continuously through use and recovery rather than ending as waste

Chemistry and Public Health

Public health and chemistry are deeply intertwined. Major public health advances of the twentieth century include:

AdvanceChemical Basis
Clean water and sanitationWater treatment chemistry (chlorination, coagulation, filtration) dramatically reduced deaths from waterborne diseases such as cholera and typhoid
Vaccines and pharmaceuticalsDrug development and manufacturing chemistry extended lifespans and reduced suffering from infectious and chronic disease
Pesticide chemistryInsecticide use dramatically reduced malaria transmission through mosquito control (though environmental consequences required careful management)
Food preservationChemical preservatives, controlled atmosphere storage, and refrigeration reduced food waste and food-borne illness
Lead removalRemoving lead — a neurotoxin — from gasoline, paint, and plumbing measurably improved public health outcomes, particularly for children
Making Decisions with Chemical Knowledge

Chemistry gives you a more powerful lens through which to view the world. You can read a report about drinking water quality and understand what the contaminants are and why they matter. You can evaluate a claim about air pollution and assess whether the chemistry behind it is sound. You can read a product label and understand what chemicals it contains. This does not mean every chemical question has a simple answer — many real-world problems involve incomplete information, economic constraints, and genuine disagreement among experts. What chemistry gives you is the ability to recognize what is known, what is uncertain, what requires more evidence, and what the chemical mechanisms actually are.

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 27.1Environmental and industrial chemistry applies the principles of this course at the scale of civilization. Scale changes consequences — a single reaction multiplied across millions of sources produces effects that a single instance never would.
  • Sec 27.2Water's polarity makes it an effective solvent for ionic and polar substances. Natural water carries dissolved minerals and gases. Municipal treatment uses coagulation, sedimentation, filtration, disinfection, pH adjustment, and fluoridation; wastewater treatment uses primary, secondary, and tertiary stages.
  • Sec 27.3Primary pollutants (CO, SO₂, NOₓ, PM, VOCs) are emitted directly; secondary pollutants like ground-level ozone form through atmospheric reactions. SO₂ and NOₓ form acid rain. CFCs catalytically destroy stratospheric ozone through a chain reaction.
  • Sec 27.4Plants absorb nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium as ions. The nitrogen cycle (fixation, nitrification, denitrification, decomposition) governs nitrogen availability. Soil pH affects nutrient solubility. Fertilizers supply nutrients in plant-available forms, but runoff causes eutrophication.
  • Sec 27.5Fossil fuel combustion releases CO₂, H₂O, and — depending on fuel purity and combustion conditions — CO, SO₂, NOₓ, particulates, and unburned hydrocarbons. Hydrogen and biofuels offer alternative emissions profiles.
  • Sec 27.6Plastics form through addition or condensation polymerization. Different resins have different properties and recyclability. Plastics persist in the environment, fragmenting into microplastics. Recycling may be mechanical or chemical.
  • Sec 27.7The Haber-Bosch process (ammonia), the Contact Process (sulfuric acid), the chlor-alkali process (chlorine, NaOH, H₂), and petroleum refining (distillation, cracking, reforming, treating) are large-scale applications of equilibrium, electrochemistry, and organic chemistry.
  • Sec 27.8Toxicity is dose-dependent ("the dose makes the poison"). LD₅₀, LC₅₀, and NOAEL measure toxicity. Acute and chronic toxicity differ in exposure pattern. Bioaccumulation and biomagnification concentrate persistent, fat-soluble chemicals through food chains. Risk combines hazard and exposure.
  • Sec 27.9Green chemistry designs processes that prevent waste and reduce hazard. Sustainability considers resources, energy, waste, and toxicity. Chemistry underlies major public health advances including clean water, vaccines, food preservation, and lead removal.

Key Terms

acid rain
Precipitation with a pH below 5.6, caused by dissolved sulfuric acid and nitric acid formed from sulfur dioxide and nitrogen oxide emissions.
activated charcoal
A highly porous form of carbon used in water and air treatment to adsorb dissolved organic compounds and other contaminants.
bioaccumulation
The buildup of a chemical substance in an organism's tissues over time, especially lipid-soluble, persistent compounds.
biodegradable
Able to be broken down by biological organisms into simpler compounds; many plastics are not biodegradable on human timescales.
biomagnification
The increase in concentration of a persistent chemical substance at successive levels of a food chain.
chlorofluorocarbon (CFC)
A synthetic compound containing carbon, fluorine, and chlorine, formerly used in refrigerants and aerosols; catalytically destroys stratospheric ozone.
chronic toxicity
Harm caused by repeated small exposures to a chemical over a long period.
coagulation
A water treatment process in which chemicals such as aluminum sulfate are added to cause suspended particles to clump together.
Contact Process
The industrial process for manufacturing sulfuric acid by oxidizing sulfur dioxide to sulfur trioxide with a vanadium pentoxide catalyst.
eutrophication
The process by which excess nutrients in water cause algal blooms and oxygen depletion, harming aquatic life.
flocculation
The aggregation of coagulated particles into larger clumps (floc) that settle from water.
green chemistry
The design of chemical products and processes that reduce or eliminate the use and generation of hazardous substances.
Haber-Bosch process
The industrial synthesis of ammonia from nitrogen and hydrogen gases using an iron catalyst at high temperature and pressure.
hard water
Water containing high concentrations of dissolved calcium and magnesium ions.
LD₅₀
The dose of a substance that kills 50% of a test population; a measure of acute toxicity.
microplastics
Plastic particles smaller than 5 millimeters formed by the fragmentation of larger plastic objects.
nitrification
The bacterial conversion of ammonium ions to nitrite and then to nitrate in soil.
nitrogen fixation
The conversion of atmospheric nitrogen (N₂) to ammonia or other nitrogen compounds usable by organisms.
NOAEL
No Observed Adverse Effect Level; the highest dose at which no harmful effects are detected in a toxicology study.
particulate matter (PM)
Tiny solid or liquid particles suspended in air, classified by size (PM₂.₅ and PM₁₀) and associated with respiratory health effects.
persistent organic pollutant (POP)
An organic compound that resists environmental degradation and accumulates in ecosystems and living organisms.
photochemical smog
A brownish haze formed when nitrogen oxides and volatile organic compounds react in sunlight to form ozone and other secondary pollutants.
polymerization
A chemical reaction in which many small monomer molecules join together to form a long polymer chain.
primary pollutant
An air pollutant emitted directly from a source.
risk assessment
The evaluation of the likelihood and magnitude of harm from chemical exposure, combining hazard assessment and exposure assessment.
scrubber
A pollution control device that removes sulfur dioxide from flue gases by reacting it with an alkaline material such as lime.
secondary pollutant
An air pollutant formed by chemical reactions in the atmosphere from primary pollutants and other atmospheric components.
sustainability
Meeting current needs without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their needs; minimizing resource depletion and environmental harm.
toxicology
The scientific study of the adverse effects of chemicals on living organisms.
volatile organic compound (VOC)
An organic compound that readily evaporates at room temperature, contributing to ground-level ozone and photochemical smog.

Environmental Chemistry Case Study

Case Study
The Flint Water Crisis

In 2014, the city of Flint, Michigan changed its water supply from treated Lake Huron water to water from the Flint River. The Flint River water was more corrosive because it had higher chloride concentrations and different chemistry than the previous source water. The new water also was not treated with corrosion inhibitors.

The previous water source had deposited a protective scale of calcium carbonate inside aging lead pipes and brass fixtures throughout the city. The more corrosive Flint River water began dissolving that scale, exposing the underlying lead and brass to direct contact with water. Lead (as Pb²⁺ ions) leached from the pipes into drinking water.

Within months, lead concentrations in the water of many Flint homes exceeded the EPA action level of 15 ppb (micrograms per liter). In some homes, concentrations exceeded 100 ppb. Lead is a neurotoxin with no safe level of exposure for children. Studies found elevated blood lead levels in children throughout the affected area.

  1. What chemical property of the Flint River water made it more corrosive to lead pipes than the previous water supply?
  2. The protective scale was composed of calcium carbonate (CaCO₃). Write a chemical equation showing how acidic water (H⁺ ions) could dissolve this scale.
  3. What is the corrosion inhibitor that should have been added to prevent lead leaching? (Hint: it forms an insoluble coating on the inside of pipes.) Research: orthophosphate treatment forms lead phosphate (Pb₃(PO₄)₂). Write the ionic formula for lead(II) phosphate.
  4. Lead enters the body through ingestion of contaminated water. Once in the bloodstream, Pb²⁺ ions interfere with enzymes and accumulate in bones (substituting for Ca²⁺). Why would children be more severely affected than adults by the same level of lead exposure?
  5. Why is it important that water chemistry decisions be made by people with knowledge of chemical reactions, not just engineering decisions based on cost?

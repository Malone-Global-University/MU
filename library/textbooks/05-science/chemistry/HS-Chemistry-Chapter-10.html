Chapter 10 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

The world is full of substances that do not conduct electricity, do not dissolve easily in water, and melt at temperatures far lower than the compounds you studied in Chapter 9. Air, water, sugar, alcohol, wax, gasoline, plastic, DNA — these substances behave very differently from table salt or magnesium oxide. The reason is that their atoms are not held together by opposite charges. Instead, their atoms share electrons.

Covalent bonding is the sharing of electrons between nonmetal atoms. When two atoms share electrons, both atoms gain stability without either one giving up an electron completely. The result is a molecule — a group of atoms held together by shared electron pairs and behaving as a single particle.

By the end of this chapter, you will understand how covalent bonds form and why sharing produces stability, how to draw Lewis structures for simple molecules, how single, double, and triple bonds differ in strength and length, what the octet rule is and when it has exceptions, and how the covalent nature of a compound determines its physical properties. You will also see how covalent bonding appears in living systems, materials science, medicine, and environmental chemistry.

Opening Question

Oxygen gas keeps us alive; nitrogen gas makes up most of the air we breathe; water is the solvent of life. All three are molecular compounds held together by covalent bonds. Yet nitrogen is almost completely unreactive, oxygen reacts readily with fuels and metals, and water dissolves almost everything. Why do covalently bonded molecules behave so differently from each other? By the end of this chapter, you will have the tools — Lewis structures, bond types, lone pairs, and the octet rule — to begin answering that question.

Section 10.1

What Covalent Bonding Is

In Chapter 9, you learned that ionic bonding occurs when a metal atom transfers one or more electrons to a nonmetal atom. But what happens when two nonmetal atoms meet? Neither atom has a strong tendency to give up electrons. Both want to reach a stable electron configuration. Neither will simply hand its electrons to the other.

The answer is sharing. When two nonmetal atoms approach each other closely, their electron clouds begin to overlap. If both atoms have unpaired valence electrons, those electrons can be shared between the two nuclei simultaneously. Each shared pair of electrons is attracted to both nuclei at once — and this mutual attraction holds the atoms together.

Definition — Covalent Bond

A covalent bond is a chemical bond formed by the sharing of one or more pairs of electrons between two atoms. The word comes from co- (together) and valent (referring to valence electrons). Covalent bonds are partnerships between valence electrons, not transfers.

Consider two hydrogen atoms. Each has one electron and needs two to be stable. When they approach, their electrons begin to occupy the space around both nuclei. Both electrons are now attracted to both protons. The atoms have found a configuration that lowers the total energy of the system. This is the essence of covalent bonding: a shared pair of electrons attracts two nuclei simultaneously, pulling them toward each other and lowering energy.

When atoms are held together by covalent bonds, they form a molecule — the smallest unit of a covalently bonded substance that retains the chemical identity of that substance. Water, carbon dioxide, oxygen gas, and glucose are all molecules.

Key Idea

Ionic bonding involves complete electron transfer. Covalent bonding involves electron sharing. This fundamental difference produces entirely different particle types and entirely different physical properties.

Section 10.2

Molecules and Molecular Compounds

A molecule is a discrete group of atoms held together by covalent bonds, with a definite composition and a definite structure. This stands in contrast to ionic compounds, which exist as extended lattice networks without discrete units. A sodium chloride crystal is not a single molecule; it is a repeating grid of ions. But a water molecule is truly a unit of three specific atoms in a specific arrangement.

Molecular Formulas

The molecular formula of a compound tells you the exact number of each type of atom in one molecule. Examples: H2O (two hydrogen, one oxygen), CO2 (one carbon, two oxygen), NH3 (one nitrogen, three hydrogen), CH4 (one carbon, four hydrogen), C6H12O6 (glucose). Molecular formulas tell you composition but not structure.

The Seven Diatomic Elements

Several elements exist naturally as two-atom molecules. These are called diatomic molecules. When writing chemical equations, always write these elements with the subscript 2 — writing a bare symbol like "O" represents a single atom, not the naturally occurring form.

Student Warning — Diatomic Elements in Equations

In a chemical equation, writing H instead of H₂ or O instead of O₂ is chemically incorrect. It represents a single, unstable atom rather than the stable naturally occurring form. Always write the subscript 2 for these seven elements when they appear as pure substances.

Molecular vs. Empirical Formulas

A molecular formula gives the actual number of atoms in one molecule. An empirical formula gives the simplest whole-number ratio of atoms. For glucose (C6H12O6), the empirical formula is CH2O. For hydrogen peroxide (H2O2), the empirical formula is HO. For water, the two are the same. You will work with empirical formulas when analyzing experimental data.

Section 10.3

Shared Electron Pairs

When two atoms form a covalent bond, one pair of electrons is shared between them. Each atom typically contributes one electron to the shared pair. The shared pair then belongs to both atoms simultaneously.

It is the valence electrons — those in an atom's outermost energy level — that participate in covalent bonding. Inner electrons remain close to their own nucleus and do not participate in bonding.

Consider hydrogen fluoride (HF). Hydrogen has one valence electron; fluorine has seven. They share one electron pair. Hydrogen now counts two electrons — matching helium. Fluorine now counts eight electrons — matching neon. Both atoms achieve stability through sharing.

Lewis Notation for Shared Pairs

In a Lewis structure, a shared pair of electrons between two atoms is written as a line (a bond) between those atoms. Each line represents two electrons being shared. Electrons that are not shared remain on their own atom as lone pairs — shown as pairs of dots.

H — H    Hydrogen molecule: one shared pair (two electrons)

H — F:    Hydrogen fluoride: one shared pair; three lone pairs on fluorine

Lone Pairs — Not Just Leftovers

Electrons not used in bonding are called lone pairs (or nonbonding pairs). They are not passive extras — lone pairs affect molecular shape, polarity, and reactivity. The lone pair on nitrogen in ammonia is what makes ammonia a base. The lone pairs on oxygen in water are responsible for water's bent shape and high boiling point.

Section 10.4

Single, Double, and Triple Bonds

Atoms can share more than one pair of electrons. The number of shared pairs determines whether the bond is single, double, or triple — and has a direct effect on bond strength and bond length.

Bond TypeShared PairsElectrons SharedRelative StrengthRelative LengthSymbol
Single12WeakestLongest—
Double24IntermediateIntermediate=
Triple36StrongestShortest≡

Single bonds are found in H2, HCl, H2O, and CH4 (methane). Double bonds appear in O2 (O=O), CO2 (O=C=O), and formaldehyde. Triple bonds are found in N2 (N≡N) and CO (C≡O). Nitrogen's triple bond is famously strong — this is why N2 is so unreactive despite making up 78% of the atmosphere.

Key Principle — Bond Order and Bond Properties

As the number of shared electron pairs increases between the same two types of atoms: Bond strength increases. More pairs pull the nuclei together more strongly, requiring more energy to break. Bond length decreases. More pairs draw the atoms closer together. These comparisons are valid only between bonds of the same element pair.

Student Warning — Comparing Bond Strengths

Bond strength comparisons (single vs. double vs. triple) are valid only when comparing bonds between the same pair of elements. A C=O double bond is stronger than a C—O single bond. But do not compare, say, a C—C bond to an N=N bond without additional data — different element pairs have different inherent bond strengths.

Section 10.5

Lewis Structures for Simple Molecules

A Lewis structure (also called a Lewis dot structure or electron dot structure) is a diagram showing the arrangement of atoms in a molecule, all bonding pairs of electrons (as lines between atoms), and all lone pairs on each atom (as pairs of dots). Lewis structures help you count electrons, check the octet rule, and predict molecular geometry.

Seven-Step Procedure for Drawing Lewis Structures

Step 1 — Count valence electrons. Add up valence electrons from every atom. This is your total electron budget.

Step 2 — Identify the central atom. Usually the atom that appears once in the formula, or the least electronegative atom (not hydrogen, which is always terminal).

Step 3 — Connect atoms with single bonds. Each single bond uses 2 electrons from your budget.

Step 4 — Complete octets on outer atoms first. Place lone pairs on surrounding atoms (2 electrons for hydrogen, 8 for others). Subtract used electrons from your budget.

Step 5 — Place remaining electrons on the central atom as lone pairs.

Step 6 — Check the central atom's electron count. If it has fewer than 8, convert lone pairs on adjacent atoms into double or triple bonds until the central atom achieves an octet.

Step 7 — Verify the total. Count all electrons in your diagram. This must equal the total from Step 1.

Worked Example 10.5.1
Drawing the Lewis structure of water (H₂O)
  • Step 1Valence electrons: O has 6, each H has 1. Total = 6 + 1 + 1 = 8 electrons.
  • Step 2Central atom: oxygen. Hydrogen is always terminal.
  • Step 3Connect O to each H with single bonds. Used: 4 electrons. Remaining: 4 electrons.
  • Step 4Hydrogen needs only 2 electrons — the O—H bonds already provide that. No additional electrons needed for H atoms.
  • Step 5Place remaining 4 electrons on oxygen as 2 lone pairs.
  • Step 6Oxygen: 2 bonding pairs (4 e⁻) + 2 lone pairs (4 e⁻) = 8 electrons total. Octet satisfied. ✓
  • Step 7Total electrons: 4 (bonds) + 4 (lone pairs) = 8 ✓
Answer: Oxygen at center bonded to two hydrogen atoms, with two lone pairs on oxygen. Structure: H—O—H (with two lone pairs on O)
Worked Example 10.5.2
Drawing the Lewis structure of carbon dioxide (CO₂)
  • Step 1Valence electrons: C has 4, each O has 6. Total = 4 + 6 + 6 = 16 electrons.
  • Step 2Central atom: carbon.
  • Step 3Connect C to each O with single bonds. Used: 4 electrons. Remaining: 12 electrons.
  • Step 4Complete octets on outer O atoms (each needs 6 more electrons → 3 lone pairs each). Used: 12 electrons. Remaining: 0 electrons.
  • Step 5No electrons remain for the central atom.
  • Step 6Carbon has only 2 single bonds = 4 electrons — needs 8. Convert one lone pair on each oxygen into a bonding pair, forming two double bonds. Now each oxygen has 2 lone pairs + 1 double bond = 8 electrons. Carbon has 2 double bonds = 8 electrons. ✓
  • Step 7Total electrons: 4 bonds × 2 e⁻ = 8; 4 lone pairs × 2 e⁻ = 8; Total = 16 ✓
Answer: O=C=O with two lone pairs on each oxygen atom.
Worked Example 10.5.3
Drawing the Lewis structure of ammonia (NH₃)
  • Step 1Valence electrons: N has 5, each H has 1. Total = 5 + 3 = 8 electrons.
  • Step 2Central atom: nitrogen.
  • Step 3Connect N to each H with single bonds. Used: 6 electrons. Remaining: 2 electrons.
  • Step 4Each H already has 2 electrons — complete.
  • Step 5Place remaining 2 electrons on nitrogen as 1 lone pair.
  • Step 6Nitrogen: 3 bonding pairs + 1 lone pair = 8 electrons. Octet satisfied. ✓
  • Step 7Total electrons: 6 (bonds) + 2 (lone pair) = 8 ✓
Answer: Nitrogen bonded to three hydrogen atoms with one lone pair on nitrogen.
Section 10.6

Lone Pairs and Bonding Pairs

Bonding pairs are pairs of electrons shared between two atoms — they create and sustain the covalent bond. Lone pairs (nonbonding pairs) belong to only one atom and do not participate in bonding. Both occupy space around an atom, and both exert repulsive forces on nearby electron pairs. Lone pairs, however, repel more strongly than bonding pairs — a fact that determines the shape of molecules.

How Lone Pairs Affect Molecular Shape

MoleculeBonding Pairs on Central AtomLone Pairs on Central AtomShapeBond Angle
CH₄ (methane)40Tetrahedral109.5°
NH₃ (ammonia)31Trigonal pyramidal~107°
H₂O (water)22Bent~104.5°

Each additional lone pair on the central atom pushes the bonding pairs closer together, compressing the bond angle. This is why water has a bent shape rather than a straight line — the two lone pairs on oxygen force the hydrogen atoms together. You will study molecular geometry in full in Chapter 12.

Lone Pairs and Chemical Reactivity

Lone pairs give molecules the ability to donate electrons to other species. Nitrogen's lone pair in ammonia allows it to act as a base by accepting a hydrogen ion. Oxygen's two lone pairs in water allow water to act as both a base and a hydrogen bond donor. These chemical roles are explored in Chapter 23 (acids and bases) and Chapter 28 (biological chemistry).

Section 10.7

The Octet Rule and Its Limits

The Octet Rule

Atoms tend to bond in ways that give each atom eight electrons in its outer energy level. This configuration matches the electron arrangement of the noble gases, which are chemically stable. Hydrogen is the primary exception — it follows the duet rule, achieving stability with just two electrons (the helium configuration).

The octet rule is a useful guideline, not an absolute law. Several important exceptions exist at the high school level.

Exception TypeExamplesExplanation
Hydrogen and heliumH in any compound; He (no bonds)Stability with 2 electrons (duet rule)

Student Warning — When to Apply the Octet Rule

At the high school level, you will work primarily with molecules where the octet rule holds. When you encounter a Period 3 or higher central atom (S, P, Cl, etc.) or boron, be aware that exceptions may apply. If your Lewis structure requires a Period 3 central atom to have more than 8 electrons to bond correctly with all surrounding atoms, this is expected — not an error.

Section 10.8

Properties of Molecular Compounds

The properties of ionic compounds arise from strong electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions in a crystal lattice. The properties of molecular compounds arise from a different set of forces — the attractions between neutral molecules, called intermolecular forces. These forces are typically much weaker than ionic bonds, and this difference produces all of the characteristic properties of molecular compounds.

Low Melting Points and Boiling Points

Most molecular compounds have relatively low melting and boiling points. Water melts at 0°C. Methane melts at –183°C. Rubbing alcohol boils at only 83°C. This occurs because separating molecules requires overcoming only the weak intermolecular forces between them — not the covalent bonds within them. During melting and boiling, covalent bonds are not broken. The intermolecular attractions are. Ionic compounds, by contrast, require much higher temperatures to disrupt their strong lattice forces.

Poor Electrical Conductivity

Ionic compounds dissolve in water to produce ions that carry electric current. Molecular compounds consist of neutral molecules. When they dissolve or melt, they do not typically produce ions, so they do not conduct electricity. Pure water is a poor conductor because it contains only neutral H2O molecules. Note that some molecular compounds — particularly acids — do produce ions when dissolved in water through a chemical process called ionization. HCl gas dissolves in water and releases H+ and Cl– ions, making hydrochloric acid conductive. But this is a chemical reaction, not simple dissolution of pre-existing ions.

Variable Solubility in Water

The solubility of molecular compounds varies widely and depends largely on polarity. Polar molecular compounds tend to dissolve well in water, because water is also polar — sugar, ethanol, and acetic acid dissolve readily. Nonpolar molecular compounds tend to be insoluble in water but dissolve in nonpolar solvents — oil, wax, and most hydrocarbons do not dissolve in water. The guiding principle is "like dissolves like" — polar solvents dissolve polar solutes; nonpolar solvents dissolve nonpolar solutes. You will study polarity in Chapter 12.

Section 10.9

Covalent Bonding in Life and Materials

Virtually every molecule in a living cell is held together by covalent bonds. Proteins, carbohydrates, lipids, and nucleic acids — the four major classes of biological molecules — are all molecular compounds built on covalent frameworks. Covalent bonding is not merely one type of bonding among many; it is the chemistry of life itself.

Carbon: The Backbone of Life

Carbon's four valence electrons allow it to form four covalent bonds, including bonds to other carbon atoms. This makes carbon capable of forming chains, rings, branched structures, and complex three-dimensional frameworks of extraordinary variety. No other element rivals carbon's structural diversity. Oxygen's double bonds in sugars and fats store chemical energy. Nitrogen's lone pair in amino acids allows proteins to form their characteristic shapes. Hydrogen bonding — a special attraction involving hydrogen covalently bonded to N, O, or F — holds DNA's two strands together and gives proteins their secondary structure.

Covalent Bonds in Materials

Polymers are long-chain molecules made of repeating units called monomers, connected by covalent bonds. Plastics, nylon, rubber, polyester, and many fibers are polymers. The covalent bonds in polymers give them strength, flexibility, and durability.

Diamond is an extreme covalent solid — every carbon atom is bonded to four neighbors in a tetrahedral arrangement extending throughout the entire crystal. This network of covalent bonds makes diamond the hardest natural material known. Graphite — another form of pure carbon — consists of layers of hexagonally bonded carbon atoms. The covalent bonds within each layer are strong; the intermolecular forces between layers are weak, which is why graphite is soft enough to write with.

Silicon dioxide (SiO2), the major component of sand and quartz, is a covalent network solid with a three-dimensional network of covalent bonds — giving it high hardness and a very high melting point.

Covalent Bonds in Medicine and the Environment

Every pharmaceutical drug is a molecular compound. Its three-dimensional shape — determined entirely by its covalent bonds — controls how it interacts with proteins and receptors in the body. Anesthetics, antibiotics, vitamins, and hormones all depend on the precise geometry of their covalent frameworks.

The key molecules of Earth's atmosphere — N2, O2, CO2, H2O, O3 — are all molecular compounds. Ozone formation and destruction, the greenhouse effect, and acid rain all involve covalent reactions. Photosynthesis and respiration are covalent transformations: carbon dioxide and water become glucose and oxygen, then glucose and oxygen become carbon dioxide and water again — driving most of life on Earth.

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 10.1Covalent bonds form when nonmetal atoms share electron pairs rather than transfer them. The shared pair is attracted to both nuclei simultaneously, lowering the system's energy. The result is a molecule. Ionic and covalent compounds differ fundamentally in particle type, bonding, and physical properties.
  • Sec 10.2A molecule is a discrete group of atoms bonded by covalent bonds with a definite composition and structure. Molecular formulas give the exact number of each atom per molecule. Seven elements exist naturally as diatomic molecules (H₂, N₂, O₂, F₂, Cl₂, Br₂, I₂) and must always be written with a subscript 2 in chemical equations.
  • Sec 10.3Valence electrons are shared in covalent bonds. A shared pair belongs to both atoms simultaneously. Electrons not used in bonding are lone pairs. Lone pairs affect molecular shape, polarity, and reactivity — they are chemically significant, not merely leftover electrons.
  • Sec 10.4Single bonds share one pair (2 e⁻); double bonds share two pairs (4 e⁻); triple bonds share three pairs (6 e⁻). As shared pairs increase between the same elements, bonds become shorter and stronger. Nitrogen's triple bond is the strongest common covalent bond, explaining N₂'s exceptional stability.
  • Sec 10.5Lewis structures show all bonding pairs (as lines) and lone pairs (as dot pairs) in a molecule. The seven-step procedure: count valence electrons, identify the central atom, connect with single bonds, complete outer octets, place remaining electrons on the central atom, form multiple bonds if needed, and verify the total electron count.
  • Sec 10.6Bonding pairs hold atoms together; lone pairs repel neighboring pairs more strongly. Each lone pair on a central atom compresses bond angles. Methane (no lone pairs) is tetrahedral; ammonia (one lone pair) is trigonal pyramidal; water (two lone pairs) is bent. Lone pairs also control reactivity — they allow molecules to act as bases and hydrogen bond donors.
  • Sec 10.7The octet rule states atoms tend to bond to achieve 8 electrons. Hydrogen follows the duet rule (2 electrons). Exceptions: boron (often 6 electrons, electron-deficient), Period 3+ elements (expanded octets using d orbitals), and odd-electron free radicals. The octet rule is a guideline, not an absolute law.
  • Sec 10.8Molecular compounds have lower melting and boiling points than ionic compounds (intermolecular forces, not covalent bonds, are overcome during phase changes). Most do not conduct electricity. Solubility depends on polarity — "like dissolves like." Molecular compounds can be gases, liquids, or solids at room temperature depending on intermolecular force strength.
  • Sec 10.9Covalent bonding underlies virtually all biological molecules (proteins, DNA, carbohydrates, lipids), all major materials (polymers, diamond, graphite, silica), all pharmaceutical drugs, and all major atmospheric molecules. Carbon's ability to form four bonds — including bonds to other carbons — makes the structural diversity of life possible.

Key Terms Review

bonding pair
A pair of electrons shared between two atoms, forming a covalent bond. Represented by a line in a Lewis structure.
covalent bond
A chemical bond formed by the sharing of one or more pairs of electrons between two atoms. Occurs primarily between nonmetals.
diatomic molecule
A molecule composed of exactly two atoms of the same element. The seven diatomic elements are H₂, N₂, O₂, F₂, Cl₂, Br₂, and I₂.
double bond
A covalent bond formed by the sharing of two pairs of electrons (four electrons total) between two atoms. Shorter and stronger than a single bond.
duet rule
The principle that hydrogen achieves stability with two electrons, matching the configuration of helium. The hydrogen exception to the octet rule.
electron-deficient molecule
A molecule in which the central atom has fewer than eight electrons (e.g., BF₃ with only 6 electrons on boron). Common for boron compounds.
empirical formula
A formula giving the simplest whole-number ratio of atoms in a compound. May differ from the molecular formula (e.g., CH₂O vs. C₆H₁₂O₆).
expanded octet
A bonding arrangement in which a Period 3 or higher element holds more than eight electrons (e.g., SF₆ with 12 electrons on sulfur).
free radical
A molecule or atom with an unpaired valence electron. Free radicals have an odd number of electrons and are generally very reactive.
Lewis structure
A diagram showing the arrangement of atoms in a molecule along with all bonding pairs (lines) and lone pairs (dot pairs) on each atom.
lone pair
A pair of valence electrons on an atom that is not shared with another atom. Also called a nonbonding pair. Affects molecular shape and reactivity.
molecule
A group of atoms held together by covalent bonds and behaving as a single particle. The smallest unit of a molecular compound that retains its chemical identity.
molecular compound
A compound composed of molecules formed by covalent bonding, typically between nonmetal atoms.
molecular formula
A formula showing the exact number of each type of atom in one molecule of a compound (e.g., H₂O, C₆H₁₂O₆).
octet rule
The principle that atoms tend to bond in ways that give each atom eight electrons in its outer energy level, matching the noble gas configuration.
single bond
A covalent bond formed by the sharing of one pair of electrons between two atoms. The weakest and longest bond type for a given element pair.
triple bond
A covalent bond formed by the sharing of three pairs of electrons (six electrons total). The shortest and strongest bond type for a given element pair.

Common Misconceptions

Misconception 1
"A covalent compound cannot conduct electricity under any circumstances."

Most covalent compounds do not conduct electricity, but acids — which are molecular compounds — ionize in water to produce ions. Hydrochloric acid, sulfuric acid, and nitric acid are all covalent compounds that produce conductive solutions. Conductivity depends on whether ions are present in solution, not strictly on whether the original substance was ionic or molecular.

Misconception 2
"Lone pairs are just leftover electrons with no chemical importance."

Lone pairs are chemically essential. They determine molecular shape, participate in hydrogen bonding, allow molecules to act as acids or bases, and enable reactions with other molecules. The lone pair on nitrogen in ammonia is what makes ammonia a base. The lone pairs on oxygen in water determine its bent shape and high boiling point.

Misconception 3
"Breaking bonds releases energy, so more bonds always means more energy released."

Breaking bonds requires energy input — it is endothermic. Forming bonds releases energy — it is exothermic. A reaction is exothermic overall when more energy is released forming product bonds than is absorbed breaking reactant bonds. This idea is central to thermochemistry in Chapter 20.

Misconception 4
"The octet rule always applies to every atom."

The octet rule is a guideline that works well for Period 2 elements but breaks down for hydrogen (duet rule), boron (often 6 electrons), and Period 3 elements (expanded octets). Recognizing when the octet rule applies and when exceptions are expected is part of chemical reasoning.

Misconception 5
"Molecules and atoms are the same thing."

An atom is the fundamental unit of an element. A molecule is two or more atoms bonded together. Oxygen as an element exists as O₂ molecules, not as individual O atoms under normal conditions. A lone O atom would be an extremely reactive species very different from the stable oxygen gas you breathe.

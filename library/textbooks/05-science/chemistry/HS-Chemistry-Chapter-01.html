Chapter 1 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Every time you breathe, you perform chemistry. Oxygen molecules travel into your lungs, cross into your bloodstream, bind to protein molecules in your red blood cells, and travel to every tissue in your body. When your cells burn glucose for energy, they release carbon dioxide, which your blood carries back to your lungs and you exhale. This cycle happens about fifteen times a minute, every minute of your life, driven entirely by chemistry.

You also perform chemistry when you cook an egg, charge your phone, take a pain reliever, watch iron rust on a fence, mix bleach and water to clean a surface, or smell bread baking in an oven. Chemistry is not something that happens only in laboratories. It is the science of everything physical — the materials the world is made of, the energy stored inside those materials, and the changes those materials undergo.

This chapter is your introduction to what chemistry studies and why it matters. Before you learn to write chemical formulas, balance equations, or calculate molar masses, you should understand what chemistry is, what chemists do, and why thinking like a chemist is a powerful and practical skill.

Opening Question

A silver spoon sits in a drawer for a year and comes out coated in a dull black film. A piece of ice melts in a glass of warm water. Which of these events produced a new substance, and which simply changed the form of the same substance? By the end of this chapter, you will know exactly how to answer that question — and why the distinction matters to every chemist.

Section 1.1

What Chemistry Studies

Chemistry is the study of matter and the changes that matter undergoes. That one sentence covers an enormous amount of ground. Matter is anything that has mass and takes up space — the air, the chair, your body, water, salt, iron, wood, plastic, glass, natural gas, and diamond are all matter. Chemistry studies all of it: what matter is made of, how it behaves, what properties it has, and what happens when one kind of matter interacts with another.

Change is just as important as matter. Chemistry is not only about what things are — it is about what things do. Iron rusts. Wood burns. Bread rises. Milk sours. Medications dissolve and react with molecules in your body. Fossil fuels release energy when ignited. These are chemical changes, and chemistry is the science that explains why they happen and how they can be predicted, controlled, or prevented.

Chemistry as the Central Science

Chemistry is sometimes called the central science because it connects biology, physics, geology, environmental science, and materials engineering. A biologist studying cell membranes needs organic chemistry. A geologist studying rock formation needs mineralogy and thermochemistry. A materials engineer designing a jet engine component needs knowledge of how metals behave at extreme temperatures.

Section 1.2

Matter, Substances, and Materials

Matter

Anything that has mass and occupies space. Gases are matter, even when invisible. Light and sound are not matter — they have no mass and do not occupy space. Electricity is not matter, though the electrons that carry electric current are.

Pure Substance

A type of matter with a definite, uniform composition throughout. Every sample of a pure substance has exactly the same properties as every other sample, regardless of where it came from or how large the sample is. Water, gold, table salt, and carbon dioxide are all pure substances.

Mixture

Matter composed of two or more substances combined without a fixed ratio. The composition of a mixture can vary. Seawater, soil, and air are all mixtures — ocean water in the Arctic has a different salt concentration than water near the equator.

CategoryDefinitionExamples
Pure substanceDefinite, uniform composition; fixed propertiesWater, gold, table salt, carbon dioxide, oxygen gas
MixtureTwo or more substances combined; variable compositionSeawater, air, soil, milk, brass, blood
MaterialsSolid matter used to build or manufacture thingsSteel, glass, rubber, ceramic, concrete, plastic

Section 1.3

Chemical Properties and Physical Properties

Every substance has properties — characteristics that describe it. Chemists divide properties into two types: physical and chemical. The key distinction is whether observing the property changes the chemical identity of the substance.

Physical Properties

A physical property is a characteristic that can be observed or measured without changing the chemical identity of the substance. After you observe a physical property, the substance is still the same kind of matter.

  • ColorIron is silvery gray; sulfur is yellow; copper is reddish-orange
  • DensityGold is very dense (19.3 g/cm³); balsa wood is not
  • Melting pointIce melts at 0°C under standard pressure
  • Boiling pointWater boils at 100°C at sea level
  • HardnessDiamond is the hardest known natural substance
  • ConductivityCopper conducts electricity; rubber does not
  • SolubilityTable salt dissolves in water; sand does not

Chemical Properties

A chemical property describes how a substance reacts with other substances — how it changes its chemical identity under certain conditions. To observe a chemical property, the substance must actually undergo a chemical change and become a different kind of matter.

  • FlammabilityGasoline burns in oxygen; nitrogen does not
  • Reactivity with waterSodium reacts violently with water; gold does not
  • Reactivity with acidLimestone fizzes in hydrochloric acid; glass does not
  • Corrosion tendencyIron rusts in moist air; gold does not corrode
  • Supports combustionOxygen supports burning; carbon dioxide does not
Student Caution

A physical property can be observed without changing what the substance is. A chemical property can only be observed when the substance actually reacts and becomes something new. If measuring the property destroys or transforms the sample, it is a chemical property.

Section 1.4

Chemical Changes and Physical Changes

Type of ChangeWhat HappensNew Substance Formed?Examples
Physical changeForm or state of matter changes; chemical identity preservedNoMelting ice, cutting paper, dissolving salt, crushing a can
Chemical changeNew substances with different properties are producedYesBurning wood, rusting iron, baking a cake, digesting food

Evidence of Chemical Change

Chemists look for observable clues that suggest a chemical change may be occurring. These signs are evidence, not proof — they indicate that further investigation is warranted.

  • Gas formationBubbles appearing when substances react
  • PrecipitateA solid forming when two clear liquids are mixed
  • Color changeA new, permanent color appears
  • Energy changeSignificant heat or light is released or absorbed
  • Odor changeA new smell suggests new molecules are present
Reversibility as a Clue

Physical changes are often reversible — water can be refrozen; dissolved salt can be recovered by evaporation. Chemical changes are generally not reversible by simple means. You cannot un-bake a cake or un-rust iron by simply removing the heat or moisture that caused the change.

Section 1.5

Observations, Evidence, and Explanations

Two Types of Observation

Qualitative — describes qualities without numbers: "The liquid is blue," "gas bubbles appeared," "the solid dissolved completely."

Quantitative — includes numbers and units: "The mass of the solid is 4.52 grams," "the temperature rose from 22.0°C to 31.5°C."

An inference is an interpretation of an observation — a reasoned judgment about what the observation means. If you observe that a white solid disappears when stirred into water, you infer that it dissolved. Inferences can be wrong, and good scientists test them before accepting them.

A good scientific explanation accounts for all available evidence, makes predictions that can be tested, and does not contradict established principles without strong reason. When evidence contradicts an existing explanation, scientists revise the explanation. This is not a weakness of science — it is one of its greatest strengths.

Section 1.6

The Particle View of Matter

One of chemistry's most important achievements is explaining observable properties using invisible particles. When you see iron rust, bend a copper wire, dissolve sugar in coffee, or watch dry ice smoke at room temperature, you are observing the behavior of matter — but the explanation lives at the level of atoms and molecules, particles far too small to see with the naked eye.

The Scale of Atoms

Atoms are incredibly small. A single grain of sand contains more atoms than there are grains of sand on all of Earth's beaches. A single drop of water contains approximately 1.67 × 10²¹ water molecules. The diameter of a hydrogen atom is about 53 picometers — roughly 0.000000000053 meters. We cannot see atoms with the naked eye, or even with most microscopes. We infer their existence and behavior from experimental evidence.

TermDefinitionExample
AtomSmallest unit of an element that retains its chemical propertiesA gold atom; an oxygen atom
MoleculeTwo or more atoms chemically bonded togetherWater (H₂O): 2 hydrogen + 1 oxygen atom
ElementA pure substance made of only one kind of atomGold, oxygen, carbon, iron
CompoundA pure substance made of two or more different elements in a fixed ratioTable salt (NaCl), water (H₂O), carbon dioxide (CO₂)

Two Levels of Description

Throughout this course, you will move between two levels of description. The macroscopic level is what you can observe directly — color, temperature, mass, volume, state of matter. The particle level is the invisible world of atoms and molecules that explains why the macroscopic world behaves the way it does. Connecting these two levels is one of the central skills of chemistry.

Why the Particle View Matters

The particle view explains why different substances have different melting and boiling points; why some dissolve in water and others do not; why gases expand to fill their containers; why some materials conduct electricity; and why chemical reactions release or absorb energy. Almost everything chemistry explains at the observable level traces back to particles.

Chemistry connects to every field where materials, living systems, or energy are involved. A nurse needs to understand drug chemistry. A farmer needs to understand soil and fertilizer chemistry. An environmental engineer needs to understand how pollutants behave in water and air. A materials scientist needs to understand bonding and structure to design better components. The reach of chemistry is as broad as the physical world itself.

Section 1.8

Why Chemical Safety Matters

Chemistry is a powerful science. The same knowledge that allows chemists to design life-saving medications also describes how dangerous substances work. The ability to release chemical energy makes fuels useful and explosives possible. Power over matter requires respect — not as a scare tactic, but as an honest statement about the nature of chemical knowledge.

Safe Handling Principles

Read every label before opening any chemical container.

Know the hazards of a substance before you work with it.

Wear appropriate protective equipment — safety glasses, gloves, and a lab coat or apron when required.

Never mix chemicals unless specifically instructed and never pour waste chemicals down the drain without confirmation that it is safe.

Keep work areas clean, organized, and free of unnecessary materials.

Emergency Preparedness — Know Before You Need It

Before beginning any laboratory work, locate the fire extinguisher and learn which fires it handles; find the eyewash station and emergency shower; identify the evacuation route and assembly point; and know how to report accidents to the teacher. A safe student is an alert, prepared, and honest student — one who reports accidents rather than hiding them.

Section 1.9

How to Study Chemistry Successfully

Chemistry rewards students who approach it thoughtfully. It is not a subject you can learn by memorizing lists the night before a test. Chemistry requires building understanding across time, connecting new ideas to earlier ones, and practicing skills until they feel natural.

StrategyWhy It Works
Understand before memorizingChemistry's facts and formulas are connected by reasoning. Understanding why a concept exists makes it far more durable than isolated memorization.
Practice problems activelyReading about chemistry is not enough. Work through examples before checking answers. When wrong, diagnose the error rather than copying the solution.
Build vocabulary carefullyTechnical terms exist because ordinary language lacks precision. Learn what each term actually refers to — and use terms in your own explanations.
Connect chemistry to the worldNoticing chemistry in daily life — rust, fizz, condensation, browning — builds intuition that makes formal concepts easier to grasp.
Ask for help earlyChemistry builds on itself. Confusion in Chapter 5 makes Chapter 9 harder. Seek help immediately when something is unclear.

Key Terms

atom
The smallest particle of an element that retains the chemical properties of that element.
chemical change
A change that produces one or more new substances with different chemical identities and properties from the original substances.
chemical property
A property that describes how a substance reacts with other substances or transforms into a different substance under certain conditions.
chemistry
The study of matter and the changes that matter undergoes.
compound
A pure substance made of two or more different elements chemically combined in a fixed ratio.
element
A pure substance made of only one kind of atom; cannot be broken down into simpler substances by chemical means.
evidence
Information collected through careful observation and experiment, used to support or test explanations.
inference
A reasoned interpretation of an observation; a conclusion drawn from evidence that goes beyond what was directly observed.
macroscopic level
The level of matter observable directly by the senses or with common instruments — color, mass, temperature, state.
matter
Anything that has mass and occupies space.
mixture
Matter composed of two or more pure substances combined without a fixed ratio.
molecule
A group of two or more atoms chemically bonded together.
observation
Information collected directly from the senses or from measuring instruments.
particle level
The level of atoms and molecules, too small to be seen with the naked eye — the level at which chemistry explains observable properties.
physical change
A change in the form or state of matter that does not alter the chemical identity of the substance.
physical property
A characteristic of matter that can be observed or measured without changing the chemical identity of the substance.
pure substance
Matter with a definite, uniform composition throughout; either an element or a compound.
qualitative observation
An observation that describes characteristics without using numerical measurements.
quantitative observation
An observation that includes numerical measurements and units.

Answer these questions before attempting the review problems. They test understanding, not memorization.

  1. A student heats water in a beaker. The water boils and steam rises into the air. Is this a physical change or a chemical change? Explain how you know.

  5. Explain what it means to say that chemistry connects the macroscopic level to the particle level. Give one example.
  6. A student says: "The copper wire must have reacted with the acid because it lost mass." Identify which part is an observation and which part is an inference.

Practice Set AObservation and Evidence

For each situation: identify at least one qualitative observation and one quantitative observation (if numbers are given); determine whether the change is physical or chemical; and explain what evidence supports your classification.

  1. A piece of magnesium ribbon is placed in hydrochloric acid. Bubbles form immediately, and the ribbon shrinks and disappears. After the reaction, the solution feels warm to the touch.
  2. A student takes a 10.00 gram block of ice from the freezer and sets it on a table. After 20 minutes, the block has melted into 9.98 grams of liquid water.
  3. A slice of bread is toasted in a toaster. It changes from soft and white to firm and brown, and a new smell is produced.
  4. A student dissolves 5.0 grams of table salt in 100 mL of water and stirs until the salt disappears. The solution is clear and colorless.

Practice Set BChemistry in Everyday Life
  1. Cooking chemistry. When an egg is boiled, the clear, runny egg white becomes solid and white. Is this a physical change or a chemical change? What evidence supports your answer?
  2. Battery chemistry. A battery powers a flashlight. When the battery is "dead," the light no longer works. Where did the energy come from, and what does this tell you about what happens inside a battery?
  3. Rust and iron. A brand-new iron nail is shiny silver. After weeks outdoors, it is coated in reddish-brown rust. Identify at least two observations that suggest a chemical change occurred. Then explain why rust is considered a different substance from iron.
  4. Water treatment. Municipal water treatment plants add chlorine to drinking water to kill harmful bacteria. Is chlorine being used here because of its physical properties or its chemical properties? Explain.
  5. Medication design. A pharmaceutical chemist wants to design a drug that reduces inflammation in the body. Should the chemist be more interested in the drug's physical properties or its chemical properties? Explain.
  6. Environmental monitoring. Scientists testing a river for pollution measure the concentration of lead in parts per billion. They also observe that the water has a slight greenish color. Classify each measurement as qualitative or quantitative.

Common Misconceptions

Misconception 1
"If I cannot see it, nothing is happening."

Many important chemical events happen at the particle level and produce no visible sign at first. A gas may be produced by a reaction before bubbles are visible. A new substance may form slowly over many hours. The absence of obvious change does not mean chemistry is not occurring.

Misconception 2
"Dissolving is a chemical change because the solid disappears."

Dissolving a substance in water is almost always a physical change. The substance separates into particles that spread throughout the water, but the particles remain the same substance. When you evaporate the water, the original substance reappears. Salt dissolved in water is still salt — it has separated into ions but has not become something new.

Misconception 3
"If a gas is produced, a chemical reaction must have occurred."

Not necessarily. Dissolved carbon dioxide escaping from a soda when you open the bottle is a physical change — the gas was already dissolved in the liquid. Opening the bottle releases pressure and the gas escapes, but no new substance is formed. Gas formation is evidence to investigate, not automatic confirmation of a chemical change.

Misconception 4
"Physical changes are always reversible."

Most physical changes are reversible, but not all. Cutting a piece of paper into small pieces is a physical change — no new substance is formed — but reassembling the paper is not practical. The word "reversible" describes what is theoretically possible about the substance's chemical identity, not whether the process is convenient or easy.

Misconception 5
"Chemistry only happens in laboratories."

Chemistry happens everywhere, at all times. Rusting, breathing, digestion, combustion, weathering of rock, cooking, and decomposition of organic matter are all chemistry. The laboratory is where chemistry is studied systematically under controlled conditions, but the chemistry itself is universal.

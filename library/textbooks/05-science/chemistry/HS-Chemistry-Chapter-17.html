Chapter 17 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Pour a pinch of table salt into a glass of water and stir. Within seconds, the salt seems to vanish. Nothing dramatic happens — no bubbling, no color change, no flash of light. Yet something chemically significant has occurred. The orderly ionic lattice of sodium chloride has come apart, and billions of sodium ions and chloride ions are now drifting freely through the liquid, surrounded by water molecules.

That invisible transformation — matter dissolving, ions separating, particles moving through solution — is the foundation of this chapter. Most of the chemistry that matters to living organisms, ecosystems, and human technology takes place not in dry flasks or melted solids, but in water. Blood, seawater, groundwater, cell fluid, and rainfall are all aqueous systems where dissolved substances interact constantly.

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to write and interpret equations for reactions in water, predict when precipitates form, and reason about concentration as a measurable and controllable chemical variable.

Opening Question

When you mix two clear aqueous solutions together and a white solid immediately appears, what has happened at the particle level? Where did the solid come from? Which ions formed it, and which ions are still drifting invisibly through the solution? By the end of this chapter, you will be able to predict exactly which combinations of solutions produce solids, name every particle present, and write equations at three levels of detail — from the formula unit level down to the individual ion level.

Section 17.1

Water as a Chemical Environment

Water covers most of Earth's surface, fills the cells of every living organism, and serves as the primary medium for industrial chemistry, environmental chemistry, and laboratory analysis. The dominance of water in chemistry is not accidental. Water has a set of chemical properties that make it exceptionally well suited to hosting chemical reactions.

To understand why water works so well as a chemical environment, you need to recall what you learned about molecular geometry and polarity in Chapter 12. A water molecule consists of one oxygen atom bonded to two hydrogen atoms. The molecule is bent, not linear. Because oxygen is far more electronegative than hydrogen, the electrons in each O–H bond are pulled toward the oxygen end. This makes each O–H bond polar. Because the molecule is bent, the two polar bonds do not cancel each other out — the molecule as a whole is polar. One end of the water molecule, near the oxygen, carries a partial negative charge. The other end, near the two hydrogens, carries a partial positive charge.

Water and Ionic Compounds

Consider what happens when you place an ionic compound like sodium chloride into water. The ionic crystal is held together by the electrostatic attraction between positive sodium ions (Na⁺) and negative chloride ions (Cl⁻). When water contacts the surface of the crystal, the partial negative end of each water molecule is attracted to the Na⁺ ions at the surface, and the partial positive end is attracted to the Cl⁻ ions. These attractions are strong enough to pull ions away from the crystal surface one by one.

As ions leave the surface, water molecules cluster around them in a process called hydration. Hydrated ions are surrounded by a shell of oriented water molecules. This surrounding shell stabilizes the ions in solution and prevents them from simply recombining into the crystal.

Substance TypeWhat Happens When It DissolvesExample
Ionic compoundLattice breaks apart; individual ions enter solution surrounded by water molecules (hydration)NaCl → Na⁺ + Cl⁻ in water
Polar molecular compoundMolecules remain intact; water surrounds and separates individual moleculesSucrose dissolves without ionizing
Nonpolar compoundWater molecules attract each other more than the nonpolar substance; the compound does not dissolveOil does not mix with water
Like Dissolves Like

The general rule from Chapter 12 applies here: polar and ionic substances dissolve in polar solvents like water, while nonpolar substances dissolve better in nonpolar solvents. Water is not merely a passive container — it actively attracts ions and polar molecules, participates in acid-base reactions, and can act as a reactant in certain chemical processes.

Section 17.2

Solutes and Solvents

Key Definitions

Solution — a homogeneous mixture that is uniform throughout at the particle level.

Solvent — the component present in the largest amount; the substance that does the dissolving. In aqueous solutions, water is always the solvent.

Solute — the substance dissolved in the solvent. There may be more than one solute in a single solution.

Solubility — the maximum amount of a solute that can dissolve in a given amount of solvent at a specific temperature.

SolutionSolventSolute(s)
SeawaterWaterNaCl, MgCl₂, SO₄²⁻, and many other ions
Blood plasmaWaterNa⁺, K⁺, Cl⁻, HCO₃⁻, glucose, proteins, gases
VinegarWaterAcetic acid (CH₃COOH)
RainwaterWaterCO₂, H₂CO₃, dissolved gases
IV salineWaterNaCl at 0.9% by mass (≈ 0.154 M)

A saturated solution contains as much dissolved solute as it can hold at a given temperature. Adding more solute will not dissolve it — it sinks to the bottom as undissolved solid. A solution that contains less than the maximum amount is unsaturated. For most solid solutes, solubility increases as temperature increases; for gases dissolved in water, solubility decreases as temperature rises.

Section 17.3

Dissociation of Ionic Compounds

When an ionic compound dissolves in water, it does not simply enter the water as intact formula units. The ionic lattice breaks apart completely, releasing individual ions into the solution. This separation is called dissociation. Dissociation can be written as a chemical equation:

NaCl(s) → Na⁺(aq) + Cl⁻(aq) CaCl₂(s) → Ca²⁺(aq) + 2 Cl⁻(aq) Na₂SO₄(s) → 2 Na⁺(aq) + SO₄²⁻(aq)
Polyatomic Ions

Polyatomic ions such as SO₄²⁻, NO₃⁻, and PO₄³⁻ stay together as a unit when an ionic compound dissolves. They do not break apart further into individual atoms. Always write polyatomic ions intact in dissociation equations.

Writing Dissociation Equations

To write a dissociation equation: identify the ions in the compound, balance so the number of each ion matches the formula, and include (s) for the solid and (aq) for each aqueous ion. Always check that the total charge on each side equals zero.

Example 17.3.1
Writing the dissociation equation for potassium phosphate
  • ProblemWrite the dissociation equation for K₃PO₄.
  • IonsPotassium forms K⁺. Phosphate is PO₄³⁻. The formula K₃PO₄ contains 3 K⁺ ions and 1 PO₄³⁻ ion per formula unit.
  • EquationK₃PO₄(s) → 3 K⁺(aq) + PO₄³⁻(aq)
  • CheckRight side charge: 3(+1) + (−3) = 0. Left side: neutral compound = 0. ✓
Answer: K₃PO₄(s) → 3 K⁺(aq) + PO₄³⁻(aq)
Example 17.3.2
Writing the dissociation equation for aluminum sulfate
  • ProblemWrite the dissociation equation for Al₂(SO₄)₃.
  • IonsAluminum forms Al³⁺. Sulfate is SO₄²⁻. The formula contains 2 Al³⁺ and 3 SO₄²⁻.
  • EquationAl₂(SO₄)₃(s) → 2 Al³⁺(aq) + 3 SO₄²⁻(aq)
  • CheckRight side charge: 2(+3) + 3(−2) = +6 − 6 = 0. ✓
Answer: Al₂(SO₄)₃(s) → 2 Al³⁺(aq) + 3 SO₄²⁻(aq)
Section 17.4

Electrolytes and Nonelectrolytes

Pure water does not conduct electricity well. However, if you dissolve sodium chloride in water, the solution conducts electricity readily. The current is carried by the moving ions — Na⁺ moves toward the negative electrode, Cl⁻ moves toward the positive electrode. A substance that produces ions in aqueous solution and allows the solution to conduct electricity is called an electrolyte. A substance that dissolves without producing ions is a nonelectrolyte.

TypeIons ProducedConductivityExamples
Strong electrolyteComplete dissociation; all or nearly all particles become ionsHigh (bulb glows brightly)NaCl, KBr, CaCl₂, HCl (strong acid), NaOH (strong base)
Weak electrolytePartial dissociation; only a small fraction ionizesLow (bulb glows dimly)Acetic acid (CH₃COOH), ammonia (NH₃)
NonelectrolyteNo ions producedNone (bulb does not glow)Sucrose, ethanol, most organic molecules
Electrolytes and Life

Your body carefully controls the concentrations of Na⁺, K⁺, Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺, Cl⁻, HCO₃⁻, and phosphate in blood and cells. These ions allow nerve cells to transmit signals, muscles to contract, and the kidneys to regulate fluid balance. When a person sweats heavily or loses fluids through illness, they lose electrolytes — which is why sports drinks and medical rehydration solutions contain dissolved ionic salts, not just water.

Section 17.5

Precipitation Reactions

Not all reactions in aqueous solution produce gases, color changes, or temperature changes. Some produce a solid that separates from the solution. This solid is called a precipitate, and the reaction that forms it is a precipitation reaction. Precipitation occurs when two aqueous ionic solutions are mixed and the ions rearrange to form a product that is insoluble in water.

Example 17.5.1
Lead(II) iodide precipitation
  • SetupAqueous lead(II) nitrate, Pb(NO₃)₂, is mixed with aqueous potassium iodide, KI. A bright yellow solid immediately forms.
  • Ions presentPb²⁺, NO₃⁻, K⁺, I⁻ all enter the same solution.
  • ResultPb²⁺ and I⁻ ions combine to form PbI₂, which is insoluble. KNO₃ remains dissolved.
  • EquationPb(NO₃)₂(aq) + 2 KI(aq) → PbI₂(s) + 2 KNO₃(aq)
PbI₂(s) is the bright yellow precipitate. KNO₃ remains in solution.
Example 17.5.2
Silver chloride precipitation
  • SetupAqueous AgNO₃ is mixed with aqueous NaCl.
  • Ions presentAg⁺, NO₃⁻, Na⁺, Cl⁻.
  • ProductsAgCl is insoluble (white precipitate). NaNO₃ is soluble.
  • EquationAgNO₃(aq) + NaCl(aq) → AgCl(s) + NaNO₃(aq)
AgCl(s) forms as a white precipitate. This reaction confirms the presence of Cl⁻ ions in an unknown solution.
The Central Question

The key question in any potential precipitation reaction is: Is the product soluble or insoluble? If insoluble, a precipitate forms. If soluble, no precipitate forms. To answer this, you need the solubility rules in Section 17.6.

Section 17.6

The solubility rules are a set of guidelines that summarize the observed behavior of ionic compounds in water. While they have some exceptions, they are reliable tools for predicting whether a precipitate will form.

Example 17.6.1
Using solubility rules to predict precipitation
  • ProblemWill a precipitate form when aqueous K₂CO₃ is mixed with aqueous CaCl₂?
  • IonsFrom K₂CO₃: K⁺ and CO₃²⁻. From CaCl₂: Ca²⁺ and Cl⁻.
  • ProductsPossible new compounds: CaCO₃ and KCl.
  • SolubilityCaCO₃: carbonate with Ca²⁺ (not alkali metal) → insoluble. KCl: potassium (alkali metal) chloride → soluble.
  • EquationK₂CO₃(aq) + CaCl₂(aq) → CaCO₃(s) + 2 KCl(aq)
Yes — white CaCO₃ precipitates. KCl remains dissolved.
Example 17.6.2
No-reaction case
  • ProblemWill a precipitate form when aqueous NaNO₃ is mixed with aqueous KCl?
  • IonsNa⁺, NO₃⁻, K⁺, Cl⁻.
  • ProductsNaCl (alkali metal chloride — soluble) and KNO₃ (alkali metal nitrate — soluble).
  • ResultBoth possible products are soluble. No precipitate forms.
No reaction (NR). All ions remain dissolved.

Section 17.7

Complete Ionic Equations

The molecular equations you have been writing — such as AgNO₃(aq) + NaCl(aq) → AgCl(s) + NaNO₃(aq) — are useful for summarizing what reacts and what forms. But they hide something important: in aqueous solution, the soluble ionic compounds are not present as intact formula units. They are present as separated ions.

A complete ionic equation shows all soluble ionic compounds in their dissociated form as individual aqueous ions. It gives a more accurate picture of what is actually happening at the particle level.

Writing Complete Ionic Equations — Three Steps

1. Write the balanced molecular equation.

2. Identify every (aq) compound that is a strong electrolyte (soluble ionic compound, strong acid, or strong base) and split it into individual ions with charges and (aq) labels.

3. Leave (s), (l), and (g) substances unchanged. Do not split weak electrolytes or nonelectrolytes.

Example 17.7.1
Complete ionic equation — silver chloride formation
  • MolecularAgNO₃(aq) + NaCl(aq) → AgCl(s) + NaNO₃(aq)
  • Split (aq)AgNO₃ → Ag⁺ + NO₃⁻. NaCl → Na⁺ + Cl⁻. AgCl is (s) — leave unchanged. NaNO₃ → Na⁺ + NO₃⁻.
  • Complete ionicAg⁺(aq) + NO₃⁻(aq) + Na⁺(aq) + Cl⁻(aq) → AgCl(s) + Na⁺(aq) + NO₃⁻(aq)
Example 17.7.2
Complete ionic equation — barium sulfate formation
  • MolecularBaCl₂(aq) + Na₂SO₄(aq) → BaSO₄(s) + 2 NaCl(aq)
  • Complete ionicBa²⁺(aq) + 2 Cl⁻(aq) + 2 Na⁺(aq) + SO₄²⁻(aq) → BaSO₄(s) + 2 Na⁺(aq) + 2 Cl⁻(aq)
Section 17.8

Net Ionic Equations

Look at Example 17.7.1 again. Notice that Na⁺ and NO₃⁻ appear identically on both sides of the complete ionic equation — completely unchanged. They were present before the reaction and are still present after. They did not participate in the actual chemical change. Ions that appear in exactly the same form on both sides of a complete ionic equation are called spectator ions.

When spectator ions are removed from both sides, what remains is the net ionic equation. It shows only the particles that actually undergo a chemical change.

Writing Net Ionic Equations — Four Steps

1. Write the balanced complete ionic equation.

2. Identify spectator ions — any ion that appears identically on both sides.

3. Cancel the spectator ions from both sides.

4. What remains is the net ionic equation. Check that both mass and charge are balanced.

Example 17.8.1
Net ionic equation — silver chloride
  • Complete ionicAg⁺(aq) + NO₃⁻(aq) + Na⁺(aq) + Cl⁻(aq) → AgCl(s) + Na⁺(aq) + NO₃⁻(aq)
  • SpectatorsNa⁺ and NO₃⁻ appear on both sides — they are spectator ions. Cancel them.
  • Net ionicAg⁺(aq) + Cl⁻(aq) → AgCl(s)
  • CheckMass: 1 Ag, 1 Cl on each side ✓. Charge: (+1) + (−1) = 0; AgCl is neutral = 0 ✓.
Net ionic equation: Ag⁺(aq) + Cl⁻(aq) → AgCl(s)
Example 17.8.2
Net ionic equation — iron(III) hydroxide formation
  • MolecularFeCl₃(aq) + 3 NaOH(aq) → Fe(OH)₃(s) + 3 NaCl(aq)
  • Complete ionicFe³⁺(aq) + 3 Cl⁻(aq) + 3 Na⁺(aq) + 3 OH⁻(aq) → Fe(OH)₃(s) + 3 Na⁺(aq) + 3 Cl⁻(aq)
  • SpectatorsNa⁺ and Cl⁻. Cancel them.
  • Net ionicFe³⁺(aq) + 3 OH⁻(aq) → Fe(OH)₃(s)
  • CheckCharge: (+3) + 3(−1) = 0; Fe(OH)₃ neutral = 0 ✓.
Net ionic equation: Fe³⁺(aq) + 3 OH⁻(aq) → Fe(OH)₃(s)
The Power of Net Ionic Equations

Many different molecular reactions can share the same net ionic equation. Any combination of a soluble barium compound with a soluble sulfate compound will always produce the same net ionic equation: Ba²⁺(aq) + SO₄²⁻(aq) → BaSO₄(s). The spectator ions change, but the essential chemistry is always the same.

Common Error — Claiming the Molecular Equation Is the Net Ionic Equation

The molecular equation still shows complete formula units and includes spectator ions. It is not the same as the net ionic equation. Always split soluble ionic compounds into ions first, then identify and remove spectators to arrive at the true net ionic equation.

Section 17.9

Introduction to Molarity

When you dissolve salt in water, knowing that "some salt was dissolved" is not enough information for most purposes. Chemists need to know exactly how much solute is in a given amount of solution. Concentration describes how much solute is dissolved in a given amount of solution. The standard unit of concentration in chemistry is molarity, abbreviated M.

The Molarity Formula

M = moles of solute ÷ liters of solution

Rearrangements:

moles of solute = M × liters of solution

liters of solution = moles of solute ÷ M

M = molarity (mol/L)
moles of solute = amount of dissolved substance (mol)
liters of solution = total volume of the final solution, not the solvent alone (L)
Volume of Solution, Not Solvent

Molarity always uses liters of solution, not liters of solvent. When a solute dissolves, the total volume of the solution may differ from the volume of the water alone. Always use the volume of the final solution in molarity calculations.

Example 17.9.1
Calculating molarity from mass and volume
  • ProblemA student dissolves 5.85 g of NaCl in enough water to make 500. mL of solution. What is the molarity?
  • Step 1Find moles of NaCl. Molar mass: 22.99 + 35.45 = 58.44 g/mol.
    moles = 5.85 g ÷ 58.44 g/mol = 0.1001 mol
  • Step 2Convert volume to liters.
    500. mL × (1 L / 1000 mL) = 0.500 L
  • Step 3Calculate molarity.
    M = 0.1001 mol ÷ 0.500 L = 0.200 mol/L
The NaCl solution is 0.200 M.
Example 17.9.2
Calculating moles from molarity and volume
  • ProblemHow many moles of KNO₃ are in 250 mL of a 0.400 M KNO₃ solution?
  • Step 1250 mL × (1 L / 1000 mL) = 0.250 L
  • Step 2moles = 0.400 mol/L × 0.250 L = 0.100 mol
0.100 mol of KNO₃ is in the sample.
Example 17.9.3
Calculating volume from molarity and moles
  • ProblemWhat volume of a 2.00 M HCl solution contains 0.300 mol of HCl?
  • SolveV = 0.300 mol ÷ 2.00 mol/L = 0.150 L = 150 mL
150 mL of the 2.00 M HCl solution contains 0.300 mol.
Example 17.9.4
Calculating mass from molarity
  • ProblemWhat mass of CaCl₂ is dissolved in 350 mL of a 0.250 M CaCl₂ solution?
  • Step 10.350 L × 0.250 mol/L = 0.0875 mol
  • Step 2Molar mass of CaCl₂: 40.08 + 2(35.45) = 110.98 g/mol
    mass = 0.0875 mol × 110.98 g/mol = 9.71 g
9.71 g of CaCl₂ is dissolved in the solution.

Molarity and Ion Concentration

When an ionic compound dissolves completely, each type of ion enters the solution in the amount given by the dissociation equation. You can calculate the concentration of each ion separately.

Example 17.9.5
Ion concentration in a solution of CaCl₂
  • ProblemWhat is the concentration of each ion in a 0.300 M solution of CaCl₂?
  • DissociationCaCl₂(s) → Ca²⁺(aq) + 2 Cl⁻(aq)
  • Ion conc.Each mole of CaCl₂ produces 1 mol Ca²⁺ and 2 mol Cl⁻.
    [Ca²⁺] = 1 × 0.300 M = 0.300 M[Cl⁻] = 2 × 0.300 M = 0.600 M
[Ca²⁺] = 0.300 M. [Cl⁻] = 0.600 M. Although the solution is 0.300 M in CaCl₂, the chloride concentration is twice that.
Section 17.10

Aqueous Reactions in Nature and Technology

Aqueous reactions shape the natural world at every scale and underlie countless human technologies.

ContextAqueous Chemistry Involved
Caves and limestoneRain absorbs CO₂ to form H₂CO₃, which slowly dissolves CaCO₃: CaCO₃(s) + H₂CO₃(aq) → Ca²⁺(aq) + 2 HCO₃⁻(aq)
Water treatmentAl₂(SO₄)₃ causes suspended particles to coagulate and settle; chlorine compounds kill microorganisms; fluoride ions strengthen tooth enamel. All are dosed by molarity calculations.

Chapter 17 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 17.1Water is an effective solvent because it is a polar molecule. Its partial charges attract ions and polar molecules, pulling them into solution through hydration. Nonpolar substances do not dissolve well in water.
  • Sec 17.2A solution consists of a solvent and one or more solutes. Solubility is the maximum amount that can dissolve at a given temperature. A saturated solution has reached that maximum; an unsaturated solution has not.
  • Sec 17.3Ionic compounds dissolve by dissociation: the lattice breaks apart and individual ions enter solution, surrounded by water molecules. Dissociation equations must be balanced for both atoms and charge. Polyatomic ions remain intact.
  • Sec 17.4Electrolytes produce ions in aqueous solution and allow electrical conduction. Strong electrolytes dissociate completely; weak electrolytes dissociate partially; nonelectrolytes produce no ions. Soluble ionic compounds and strong acids and bases are strong electrolytes.
  • Sec 17.5Precipitation reactions occur when two aqueous solutions are mixed and an insoluble product forms. The precipitate is the product that cannot stay dissolved; other ions remain in solution.
  • Sec 17.6Solubility rules summarize the observed solubility of ionic compounds. Alkali metal, ammonium, nitrate, and acetate compounds are generally soluble. Most carbonates, phosphates, sulfides, and hydroxides (of non-alkali metals) are insoluble.
  • Sec 17.7The complete ionic equation shows all soluble strong electrolytes as individual aqueous ions. Solids, liquids, gases, and weak electrolytes are left as formula units.
  • Sec 17.8Spectator ions appear unchanged on both sides of the complete ionic equation and do not participate in the reaction. Removing them gives the net ionic equation, which shows only the essential chemical change. Charge must balance in the net ionic equation.
  • Sec 17.9Molarity (M) = moles of solute ÷ liters of solution. The formula can be rearranged to find moles or volume. Ion concentrations in solution are found by multiplying the compound's molarity by the number of that ion per formula unit.
  • Sec 17.10Aqueous chemistry operates in rivers, caves, blood, hospitals, water treatment plants, farms, and factories. The concepts of dissociation, electrolytes, precipitation, ionic equations, and molarity underlie all of these systems.

electrolyte

nonelectrolyte

weak electrolyte

molecular equation

Looking Ahead
Chapter 18: Gases and Gas Laws

Chapter 18 moves from aqueous solutions to gases. You have already encountered gases in chemical equations, where they appear as (g). Now you will study the behavior of gases in much more detail — how pressure, volume, temperature, and amount of gas relate to one another through the gas laws. Boyle's law, Charles's law, Gay-Lussac's law, and the ideal gas law are among the most practical and well-tested relationships in all of chemistry. The mole concept and molar calculations you developed in Chapters 15, 16, and 17 continue as essential tools. Gas behavior is also explained through the particle model: molecules in gases are in constant motion, on average far apart from one another, and their collisions with container walls produce the pressure we measure.

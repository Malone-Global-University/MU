Chapter 24 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Every time a flashlight glows, a car starts, a piece of iron rusts, or a surgeon uses a battery-powered instrument, electrons are moving. Not in a chaotic, random way — but in a purposeful, chemical way. Atoms are giving electrons to other atoms, and that transfer of charge is driving the change. This is the subject of Chapter 24.

In earlier chapters, you learned that chemical reactions rearrange atoms and that energy changes accompany chemical changes. In this chapter, you will learn that a special category of reactions — called oxidation-reduction reactions, or redox reactions — involves the transfer of electrons between atoms. These reactions explain how metals corrode, how batteries store energy, how living organisms extract energy from food, how water is purified, and how industrial processes manufacture metals from ores.

Electrochemistry is the branch of chemistry that studies the relationship between chemical reactions and electricity. When electrons move in a controlled way through an external circuit, they can do electrical work — this is how a battery powers your phone. When electricity is used to force a chemical reaction that would not happen on its own, it is called electrolysis — this is how aluminum is produced from ore, how water is split into hydrogen and oxygen, and how metal objects are electroplated.

Opening Question

If you place a strip of zinc metal into a solution containing copper(II) ions, the zinc strip slowly disappears and a reddish-brown coating of copper metal builds up in its place. Nothing was added except the zinc strip — so where did the copper come from, and where did part of the zinc go? By the end of this chapter, you will be able to explain this transformation in terms of electrons changing hands — and you will see how the very same idea, scaled up and rearranged, powers batteries, protects ships from rusting, and produces the aluminum in a soda can.

Section 24.1

What Redox Reactions Are

Reactions That Move Electrons

You have already studied many types of chemical reactions: synthesis, decomposition, single-replacement, double-replacement, combustion, acid-base, and precipitation. Each type describes a different pattern of how atoms rearrange during a reaction.

Oxidation-reduction reactions, commonly called redox reactions, are defined by a different kind of change: the transfer of electrons from one atom or ion to another. In a redox reaction, one participant loses electrons and another participant gains those electrons. The electrons do not disappear — they move. This is the defining feature of every redox reaction: electron transfer.

A Simple Example

Consider what happens when a strip of zinc metal is placed into a solution containing copper(II) ions:

Zn(s) + Cu²⁺(aq) → Zn²⁺(aq) + Cu(s)

Before the reaction, zinc is a neutral metal. After the reaction, zinc has become a 2+ ion — it has lost two electrons. Before the reaction, copper was a 2+ ion dissolved in water. After the reaction, copper is a solid neutral metal — it has gained two electrons. The two electrons lost by zinc were gained by copper. Electrons were transferred.

This is a redox reaction. The zinc strip visibly decreases, and a reddish-brown coating of copper metal deposits on its surface. The color change and solid formation are observable evidence that the reaction is occurring.

Why Redox Reactions Matter

Redox reactions are not exotic or rare. They are among the most common and most important reactions in chemistry and in the natural world. The combustion of fuels — whether wood, gasoline, natural gas, or coal — is a redox reaction. The rusting of iron is a redox reaction. Photosynthesis, which converts carbon dioxide and water into sugar using sunlight, is a redox process. Cellular respiration, which releases energy from glucose in living cells, is a redox process. The charging and discharging of every rechargeable battery involves redox reactions. The extraction of aluminum from bauxite ore, the purification of copper, and the manufacture of chlorine gas from salt water — all redox reactions.

Understanding redox chemistry means understanding an enormous swath of how the material world works.

Two Halves of the Same Reaction

Every redox reaction can be divided into two half-reactions. One half-reaction describes the loss of electrons, and the other describes the gain of electrons. These two processes always occur together. You cannot have one without the other. If electrons are lost by one substance, they must be gained by another. Electrons do not float freely in solution waiting to be picked up later.

The term used for the loss of electrons is oxidation. The term used for the gain of electrons is reduction. Both must happen simultaneously.

Memory Tool

OIL RIG — Oxidation Is Loss, Reduction Is Gain.

The zinc-copper reaction split into half-reactions:

Oxidation half-reaction: Zn → Zn²⁺ + 2e⁻

Reduction half-reaction: Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu

Notice that the electrons appear explicitly in each half-reaction. In the oxidation half-reaction, electrons are produced (lost by zinc). In the reduction half-reaction, electrons are consumed (gained by copper). When the two half-reactions are added together, the electrons cancel out, and the overall reaction is:

Zn + Cu²⁺ → Zn²⁺ + Cu
Section Summary — 24.1

A redox reaction involves the transfer of electrons from one substance to another. Oxidation is the loss of electrons; reduction is the gain of electrons. The two processes always occur together. Redox reactions are responsible for combustion, corrosion, batteries, biological energy, and many industrial processes.

Section 24.2

Oxidation and Reduction

Defining Oxidation

Oxidation is the loss of electrons by an atom, ion, or molecule. When a substance is oxidized, it loses one or more electrons. This causes its charge to become more positive. A neutral atom that loses two electrons becomes a 2+ ion. An ion with a 1+ charge that loses one more electron becomes a 2+ ion.

The term "oxidation" originally referred to reactions with oxygen. When iron reacts with oxygen to form iron oxide, iron is combining with oxygen. Early chemists noticed that substances burned or rusted in oxygen, and they called this process oxidation. Later, chemists realized that oxygen was doing something consistent in all these reactions: it was pulling electrons away from whatever it reacted with. Eventually, the definition broadened from "reaction with oxygen" to "loss of electrons," which is more accurate and more general.

Today, a substance can be oxidized even when oxygen is not present — as long as electrons are being transferred away from it.

Defining Reduction

Reduction is the gain of electrons by an atom, ion, or molecule. When a substance is reduced, it gains one or more electrons. This causes its charge to become more negative (or less positive). A 2+ ion that gains two electrons becomes a neutral atom. A neutral atom that gains one electron becomes a 1− ion.

The term "reduction" comes from early metallurgy. When metal ores were converted to pure metals, chemists said the ore was "reduced" — its mass and complexity were reduced. We now understand that converting a metal ion (like Fe³⁺ in iron ore) to a neutral metal involves gaining electrons. The term has been retained, though its meaning is now specifically about electron gain.

Oxidation and Reduction Always Occur Together

It is tempting to think of oxidation and reduction as separate events, but they are not. They are two sides of the same reaction. If one substance loses electrons, those electrons must go somewhere. They are transferred to another substance. That receiving substance is reduced.

There is no such thing as a reaction where something is oxidized and nothing is reduced, or vice versa. The two halves always balance. This is why they are called a coupled process. Chemists sometimes write the two halves separately for clarity, but they represent one complete reaction.

A Second Example: Magnesium and Oxygen

When magnesium burns in air, it reacts with oxygen gas to form magnesium oxide:

2 Mg(s) + O₂(g) → 2 MgO(s)

This reaction releases a brilliant white light — bright enough that magnesium was used in early photography as a flash powder.

ElementBeforeAfterWhat Happened
MagnesiumMg, neutralMg²⁺ in MgOLoses 2 electrons — oxidized
OxygenO₂, neutralO²⁻ in MgOGains 2 electrons (each O) — reduced

Oxidation half-reaction: Mg → Mg²⁺ + 2e⁻

Reduction half-reaction: O + 2e⁻ → O²⁻ (for each oxygen atom)

The electrons lost by magnesium are exactly the electrons gained by oxygen. The bookkeeping balances.

What "More Oxidized" and "More Reduced" Mean

Sometimes chemists compare the oxidation state of the same element in different compounds. Carbon in carbon dioxide (CO₂) is more oxidized than carbon in methane (CH₄). Carbon in methane has a lower oxidation state — it has more electron density around it.

Fuels are reduced forms of carbon. When they burn, they are oxidized by oxygen. Products like CO₂ and H₂O are the more oxidized forms of carbon and hydrogen after combustion. This language — more oxidized, more reduced — refers to the direction of electron movement. It will become more precise in the next section, where you learn about oxidation numbers.

Section Summary — 24.2

Oxidation is the loss of electrons; reduction is the gain of electrons. Both occur in every redox reaction. Oxidation causes a substance's charge to increase; reduction causes a substance's charge to decrease. The two processes are coupled — electrons lost by one substance are gained by another.

Section 24.3

Oxidation Numbers

A Tool for Tracking Electrons

In simple ionic reactions like zinc reacting with copper(II) ions, it is easy to see which atoms gain or lose electrons — the charges of the ions tell you directly. But many redox reactions involve covalent compounds, where electrons are shared rather than fully transferred. In these cases, it is not immediately obvious who "has" the electrons.

Chemists use a bookkeeping system called oxidation numbers (sometimes called oxidation states) to track electron ownership in any compound, ionic or covalent.

An oxidation number is an assigned charge that an atom would have if all bonding electrons were given entirely to the more electronegative atom. It is a helpful fiction — atoms in covalent bonds do not literally carry the charge that their oxidation number suggests — but it provides a consistent way to track where electrons are in a molecule and how they change during a reaction.

Rules for Assigning Oxidation Numbers

The following rules, applied in order, allow you to assign oxidation numbers to any element in any compound.

RuleStatementNotes / Exceptions
1A pure element in its standard form has oxidation number 0.Na(s), O₂(g), Fe(s), Cl₂(g) → all 0
2A monatomic ion's oxidation number equals its charge.Na⁺ = +1, Ca²⁺ = +2, Cl⁻ = −1, Fe³⁺ = +3
3Fluorine is always −1 in compounds.Most electronegative element; no exceptions
4Oxygen is usually −2 in compounds.Peroxides (H₂O₂): −1. Bonded to F (OF₂): +2
5Hydrogen is usually +1 in compounds.Metal hydrides (NaH, CaH₂): −1
6Sum of oxidation numbers in a neutral compound = 0.Used to solve for unknowns
7Sum of oxidation numbers in a polyatomic ion = ion charge.Used to solve for unknowns

These rules allow you to find the oxidation number of any element in a compound, even if the other elements' oxidation numbers are already known.

Example 24.3.1
Finding the oxidation number of sulfur in H₂SO₄
  • Step 1Assign known oxidation numbers: H = +1 (Rule 5), O = −2 (Rule 4).
  • Step 2Write the sum equation. The compound is neutral, so the sum equals zero (Rule 6). 2(+1) + S + 4(−2) = 0
  • Step 3Solve for S. 2 + S − 8 = 0 S − 6 = 0 S = +6
Answer: The oxidation number of sulfur in H₂SO₄ is +6.
Example 24.3.2
Finding the oxidation number of nitrogen in NO₃⁻
  • Step 1Assign known oxidation numbers: O = −2 (Rule 4).
  • Step 2The ion has a 1− charge, so the sum equals −1 (Rule 7). N + 3(−2) = −1
  • Step 3Solve for N. N − 6 = −1 N = +5
Answer: The oxidation number of nitrogen in NO₃⁻ is +5.
Example 24.3.3
Finding the oxidation number of manganese in KMnO₄
  • Step 1Assign known oxidation numbers: K = +1 (Rule 2, K always forms K⁺), O = −2 (Rule 4).
  • Step 2Write the sum equation for this neutral compound. (+1) + Mn + 4(−2) = 0 1 + Mn − 8 = 0 Mn = +7
Answer: The oxidation number of manganese in KMnO₄ is +7.

Using Oxidation Numbers to Identify Redox Reactions

Once you can assign oxidation numbers, you can identify redox reactions by looking for changes in oxidation number. If the oxidation number of an element increases from reactant to product, that element was oxidized (it lost electrons — its assigned charge became more positive). If the oxidation number of an element decreases, that element was reduced (it gained electrons — its assigned charge became more negative). If no oxidation numbers change, the reaction is not a redox reaction.

Consider the combustion of methane:

CH₄(g) + 2 O₂(g) → CO₂(g) + 2 H₂O(g)
ElementBeforeAfterChange
Carbon−4 (in CH₄)+4 (in CO₂)Increased — oxidized
Oxygen0 (in O₂)−2 (in CO₂, H₂O)Decreased — reduced
Hydrogen+1 (in CH₄)+1 (in H₂O)No change

This confirms that methane combustion is a redox reaction.

Section Summary — 24.3

Oxidation numbers are assigned charges that track electron ownership in compounds and ions. Seven rules allow oxidation numbers to be assigned to any element in any compound or ion. An increase in oxidation number indicates oxidation; a decrease indicates reduction. Oxidation numbers make it possible to identify redox reactions even in covalent compounds.

Section 24.4

Oxidizing Agents and Reducing Agents

Two Roles in Every Redox Reaction

Every redox reaction has two key players: one substance that causes oxidation, and one substance that causes reduction. These substances are given specific names based on what they do during the reaction.

The oxidizing agent is the substance that causes another substance to be oxidized. The oxidizing agent does this by accepting electrons. As it accepts electrons, the oxidizing agent itself is reduced.

The reducing agent is the substance that causes another substance to be reduced. The reducing agent does this by donating electrons. As it donates electrons, the reducing agent itself is oxidized.

Student Warning — A Confusing Pair of Names

The oxidizing agent is itself reduced. The reducing agent is itself oxidized. The agent does the action to something else, and the action the agent does to something else is the opposite of what happens to the agent itself.

The oxidizing agent takes electrons away from the reducing agent — it causes oxidation in the reducing agent, but it gains those electrons itself, so it is reduced. The reducing agent gives electrons to the oxidizing agent — it causes reduction in the oxidizing agent, but it loses those electrons itself, so it is oxidized.

Identifying Oxidizing and Reducing Agents

Return to the zinc-copper reaction:

Zn(s) + Cu²⁺(aq) → Zn²⁺(aq) + Cu(s)
SpeciesOxidation Number ChangeProcessRole
Zn0 → +2OxidizedReducing agent
Cu²⁺+2 → 0ReducedOxidizing agent

The reducing agent (Zn) is the one that gets oxidized. The oxidizing agent (Cu²⁺) is the one that gets reduced.

Common Oxidizing Agents

Some substances are well-known, powerful oxidizing agents because they readily accept electrons. They tend to be electronegative nonmetals or compounds containing elements in high oxidation states.

Reducing agents are substances that readily donate electrons. They tend to be metals, carbon-containing compounds, or substances containing elements in low oxidation states.

Why Strength Matters

Not all oxidizing agents are equally powerful. A stronger oxidizing agent will pull electrons more effectively from a reducing agent. If two substances are both good reducing agents, the stronger one will be oxidized in preference.

This concept underpins the activity series of metals, which you may have encountered in the chapter on single-replacement reactions. More active metals are better reducing agents — they give up electrons more readily. A more active metal can displace a less active metal from its ionic solution, because the more active metal acts as a stronger reducing agent.

Connecting Ideas — The Activity Series

Zinc is above copper in the activity series. This means zinc is a better reducing agent than copper. When zinc contacts copper ions, zinc loses electrons (is oxidized) and copper ions gain electrons (are reduced). The reverse — copper metal reducing zinc ions — does not occur under standard conditions, because copper is a weaker reducing agent than zinc.

Section Summary — 24.4

The oxidizing agent accepts electrons and is itself reduced during the reaction. The reducing agent donates electrons and is itself oxidized during the reaction. Strong oxidizing agents include oxygen, fluorine, chlorine, and hydrogen peroxide. Strong reducing agents include active metals, hydrogen gas, and carbon. The relative strength of oxidizing and reducing agents determines which direction a redox reaction proceeds.

Section 24.5

Redox Reactions in Everyday Life

The principles established in the previous sections — electron transfer, oxidation numbers, oxidizing and reducing agents — are not abstract concepts confined to chemistry classrooms. They describe some of the most important processes in the physical and biological world.

Combustion

The burning of any fuel is a redox reaction. Whether the fuel is wood, natural gas, propane, gasoline, coal, or alcohol, combustion involves the oxidation of carbon and hydrogen atoms by oxygen. In the combustion of propane (C₃H₈), used in camping stoves and gas grills:

C₃H₈(g) + 5 O₂(g) → 3 CO₂(g) + 4 H₂O(g)

Carbon is oxidized from approximately −8/3 in propane to +4 in CO₂. Oxygen is reduced from 0 in O₂ to −2 in CO₂ and H₂O. The energy released as heat and light is the energy associated with this electron transfer — energy that was stored in the chemical bonds of propane and is released when those bonds break and new, more stable bonds form. Every fire — campfire, candle, engine — is powered by the redox chemistry of oxidation.

Respiration

Cellular respiration is a biological process that extracts energy from glucose. The overall reaction is:

C₆H₁₂O₆(aq) + 6 O₂(g) → 6 CO₂(g) + 6 H₂O(l) + energy

This looks similar to combustion, and chemically it is: glucose is being oxidized (carbon goes from approximately zero to +4 in CO₂) and oxygen is being reduced. The difference is that respiration happens in a series of controlled steps inside cells, not in one explosive burst. The energy is captured gradually in the form of ATP (adenosine triphosphate), the cell's energy currency. You are running redox reactions right now to stay alive.

Photosynthesis

Photosynthesis is, in a sense, the reverse of respiration — at least in chemical terms. Plants use light energy to drive the conversion of carbon dioxide and water into glucose:

6 CO₂(g) + 6 H₂O(l) + light energy → C₆H₁₂O₆(aq) + 6 O₂(g)

In this reaction, carbon is reduced (from +4 in CO₂ to approximately zero in glucose) and oxygen in water is oxidized (from −2 in H₂O to 0 in O₂). Plants use light energy to force this electron transfer uphill — moving electrons from water to carbon dioxide, producing glucose and releasing oxygen as a byproduct.

The oxygen in Earth's atmosphere is almost entirely a product of photosynthetic redox chemistry, accumulated over billions of years of plant, algal, and cyanobacterial activity.

Bleaching

When chlorine bleach (sodium hypochlorite, NaOCl) whitens fabric or disinfects surfaces, it is acting as an oxidizing agent. It transfers electrons from the colored organic compounds in stains or from the biological molecules in microorganisms. This electron transfer breaks apart the chemical structures responsible for color or for life. The stain loses its color; the microorganism is destroyed. Hydrogen peroxide (H₂O₂) works similarly when used as a bleaching agent in hair bleach or as a wound disinfectant.

Metal Refining

Producing pure metals from ores requires reducing the metal ions in the ore to neutral metal atoms. Iron ore contains iron in the form of Fe₂O₃ (iron(III) oxide). To make iron, the ore is heated in a blast furnace with coke (a form of carbon). The carbon acts as a reducing agent, donating electrons to the iron ions:

Fe₂O₃(s) + 3 CO(g) → 2 Fe(l) + 3 CO₂(g)

Here, carbon in CO is oxidized to CO₂, and iron is reduced from Fe³⁺ to Fe. The result is liquid iron, which is collected and refined into steel. This industrial process runs on a scale of millions of tons per year. The steel in buildings, bridges, vehicles, and appliances was produced by redox chemistry.

Food Spoilage and Food Preservation

Fats and oils in food can undergo oxidation reactions when exposed to oxygen in the air. The oxidized fat molecules have different structures and produce compounds with unpleasant odors and flavors — this is what "rancid" butter or old cooking oil smells like.

Food manufacturers add antioxidants — substances that are preferentially oxidized in place of the food's own molecules. Common antioxidants include vitamin E (tocopherol), vitamin C (ascorbic acid), and synthetic compounds like BHA and BHT. These molecules sacrifice themselves by donating electrons to reactive oxygen species, protecting the food's fats, proteins, and colors from oxidative damage.

Your body uses antioxidants in the same way, protecting cells from oxidative damage associated with aging, inflammation, and disease.

Section Summary — 24.5

Redox reactions are the chemistry behind combustion, cellular respiration, photosynthesis, bleaching, metal refining, and food oxidation. Recognizing redox reactions in everyday contexts deepens the understanding of why these processes occur and what controls them. Nearly every major energy transformation in the natural and industrial world involves the transfer of electrons.

Section 24.6

Galvanic Cells and Batteries

Harnessing Electron Flow

In the zinc-copper reaction you studied earlier, zinc metal placed in a solution of copper sulfate causes electrons to transfer directly from zinc to copper ions. This reaction produces heat and results in a visible deposit of copper metal on the zinc surface. Energy is released — but it is mostly wasted as heat.

What if you could separate the two half-reactions into different locations and force the electrons to travel through a wire between them? Then the moving electrons would constitute an electric current — useful electrical energy that could power a device.

This is the principle behind a galvanic cell (also called a voltaic cell), named after Italian scientists Luigi Galvani and Alessandro Volta, who pioneered early work on electricity and chemical reactions in the late 1700s. A galvanic cell converts chemical energy into electrical energy through a spontaneous redox reaction.

Components of a Galvanic Cell

Memory Tool

AN OX and a RED CAT — ANode = OXidation; REDuction = CAThode.

ComponentRole
Two electrodesSolid conductors — often metals — that participate in the reaction. The anode is where oxidation occurs; the cathode is where reduction occurs.
Two electrolytesEach electrode sits in a solution containing ions relevant to its half-reaction.

The Zinc-Copper Galvanic Cell

A classic galvanic cell uses the zinc-copper redox reaction: the anode is a zinc strip submerged in a zinc sulfate (ZnSO₄) solution, and the cathode is a copper strip submerged in a copper sulfate (CuSO₄) solution. A wire connects the two metal strips, and a salt bridge connects the two solutions.

Example 24.6.1
Tracing the zinc-copper galvanic cell
  • AnodeZinc atoms in the strip are oxidized. Zn(s) → Zn²⁺(aq) + 2e⁻ They enter solution as Zn²⁺ ions, and two electrons are released into the zinc metal strip. These electrons travel through the external wire toward the copper electrode.
  • CathodeElectrons arriving from the wire combine with Cu²⁺ ions in the copper sulfate solution. Cu²⁺(aq) + 2e⁻ → Cu(s) These atoms deposit on the copper electrode.
  • As it runsThe zinc anode slowly dissolves; the copper cathode slowly grows; electrons flow continuously through the wire from zinc to copper. K⁺ ions migrate through the salt bridge toward the cathode side (where negative charge would build up as Cu²⁺ is removed), and NO₃⁻ ions migrate toward the anode side (where positive charge would build up as Zn²⁺ accumulates).
Result: a steady flow of electrons through the wire — usable electrical current.

Cell Voltage

The driving force of a galvanic cell is measured as voltage, also called electrical potential or electromotive force (EMF). Voltage is measured in volts (V). Voltage reflects how strongly the reaction pulls electrons from the reducing agent and pushes them toward the oxidizing agent. A higher voltage means a greater tendency for electron transfer — a more spontaneous reaction.

The zinc-copper cell produces approximately 1.10 volts under standard conditions.

Different combinations of half-reactions produce different voltages. Chemists have measured and tabulated standard reduction potentials for hundreds of half-reactions. A more positive reduction potential means the half-reaction's oxidizing agent accepts electrons more readily. A more negative reduction potential means the half-reaction's reducing agent donates electrons more readily.

Formula

Cell voltage = (Reduction potential of cathode) − (Reduction potential of anode)

You do not need to memorize standard reduction potential tables at this level, but you should understand that cell voltage depends on the identities of the two half-reactions, not simply on the size of the electrodes or the volume of solution.

Real Batteries

A battery is one or more galvanic cells packaged for practical use. The chemical energy stored in the battery's reagents is converted to electrical energy on demand.

Battery TypeChemistry
Dry cell (alkaline)AA, AAA, C, D batteries. Zinc anode; manganese dioxide cathode; moist paste electrolyte. ~1.5 V.
Lead-acidRechargeable battery in gasoline vehicles. Lead (Pb) anode, lead(IV) oxide (PbO₂) cathode, sulfuric acid electrolyte. A 12 V car battery = six 2 V cells in series.
NiMH / Lithium-ionModern rechargeables for hybrid vehicles, phones, laptops, power tools. Lithium-ion prized for high energy density per unit weight.

Section Summary — 24.6

A galvanic cell converts chemical energy to electrical energy through a spontaneous redox reaction. Oxidation occurs at the anode; reduction occurs at the cathode. Electrons flow through the external wire from anode to cathode. A salt bridge maintains electrical neutrality. Cell voltage depends on the specific half-reactions involved. Batteries are packaged galvanic cells, ranging from simple alkaline cells to sophisticated lithium-ion and fuel cell systems.

Section 24.7

Electrolysis

Running Redox Reactions Backward

In a galvanic cell, a spontaneous redox reaction generates electrical energy. But what if you want to run a reaction that is not spontaneous — one that requires energy input to proceed?

Electrolysis is the process of using electrical energy to drive a nonspontaneous chemical reaction. In electrolysis, an external power source (a battery or power supply) forces electrons to move in a direction they would not naturally go, causing a chemical change that requires energy input.

Cell TypeDirection of Energy Flow
Galvanic cellSpontaneous chemical reaction → produces electricity
Electrolytic cellElectricity is supplied → drives a nonspontaneous chemical reaction

Components of an Electrolytic Cell

An electrolytic cell has the same basic components as a galvanic cell — two electrodes in an electrolyte solution — but the electrodes are connected to an external power source.

The electrode connected to the negative terminal of the power source is the cathode. The negative terminal pushes electrons into this electrode, and the extra electrons reduce the positive ions in solution.

The electrode connected to the positive terminal of the power source is the anode. The positive terminal pulls electrons away from this electrode, forcing the atoms or ions at its surface to be oxidized.

Student Warning — Anode and Cathode Never Switch Definitions

In both galvanic cells and electrolytic cells, oxidation occurs at the anode and reduction occurs at the cathode. The definition of anode and cathode always relates to which reaction is occurring, not to which electrode is positive or negative.

Electrolysis of Water

One of the simplest demonstrations of electrolysis is the splitting of water into hydrogen and oxygen gases:

2 H₂O(l) → 2 H₂(g) + O₂(g)

This reaction is not spontaneous under ordinary conditions — it requires an energy input of approximately 237 kilojoules per mole. But when an electric current is passed through water (with a small amount of dissolved electrolyte to improve conductivity), water molecules are decomposed.

ElectrodeProcessHalf-Reaction
CathodeReduction — H₂ gas bubbles form2 H₂O + 2e⁻ → H₂ + 2 OH⁻
AnodeOxidation — O₂ gas bubbles form2 H₂O → O₂ + 4 H⁺ + 4e⁻

The volume of hydrogen gas produced is twice the volume of oxygen gas, reflecting the 2:1 ratio in the formula of water. Electrolysis of water is being explored as a way to produce hydrogen gas for use as a fuel. If the electricity used for electrolysis comes from a renewable source (solar or wind), then hydrogen produced this way is a carbon-free fuel — burning hydrogen produces only water.

Electroplating

Electroplating uses electrolysis to deposit a thin layer of one metal onto the surface of another. The object to be plated is placed as the cathode. The plating metal (or a compound containing it) serves as the anode or is dissolved in the electrolyte. When current flows, metal ions from the solution are reduced at the cathode and deposit as a thin metallic film on the object's surface.

ApplicationPurpose
Chrome plating on automotive partsShiny, corrosion-resistant surface on steel
Silver plating on cutleryAppearance and durability on cheaper base metals
Gold plating on jewelry and connectorsConductivity and corrosion resistance in a thin layer
Zinc plating (galvanizing) on steelProtects steel from rust — pipes, guardrails, fencing

The thickness of the plating layer depends on the current applied and the duration of electrolysis.

Industrial Electrolysis: Producing Aluminum

Aluminum is the most abundant metal in Earth's crust, but it does not exist naturally as a pure metal. It occurs as Al₂O₃ (aluminum oxide, or alumina) embedded in a rock called bauxite. Extracting aluminum from alumina requires reducing Al³⁺ ions to neutral aluminum atoms — a reaction so nonspontaneous that electrolysis is the only practical method.

The Hall-Héroult process, developed independently by Charles Hall in the United States and Paul Héroult in France in 1886, dissolves aluminum oxide in molten cryolite (Na₃AlF₆) at very high temperatures. A powerful electric current is then passed through the molten mixture. Aluminum ions are reduced at the cathode and collect as liquid aluminum, which is drained off. Oxygen is produced at the carbon anode, where it reacts with the carbon to form carbon dioxide.

Hall-Héroult Process

Cathode: Al³⁺ + 3e⁻ → Al(l)

Anode: C + O²⁻ → CO₂ + electrons (simplified)

The Hall-Héroult process requires an enormous amount of electricity — producing one ton of aluminum requires approximately 15,000 kilowatt-hours of electrical energy. This is why aluminum recycling is so important: recycling aluminum uses only about 5% of the energy required to produce it from ore. The energy savings from recycling one aluminum can is enough to power a television for three hours.

Industrial Electrolysis: Chlor-Alkali Process

Chlorine gas and sodium hydroxide — two of the most important industrial chemicals — are both produced by the electrolysis of concentrated sodium chloride (salt) solution (brine).

ElectrodeHalf-ReactionProduct
Anode2 Cl⁻ → Cl₂(g) + 2e⁻Chlorine gas (oxidation)
Cathode2 H₂O + 2e⁻ → H₂(g) + 2 OH⁻Hydrogen gas + hydroxide ions (reduction)

The sodium ions remain in solution with the hydroxide ions, forming sodium hydroxide (NaOH). This process, called the chlor-alkali process, produces chlorine for water treatment and PVC plastic production, hydrogen for industrial uses, and sodium hydroxide for paper production, soap making, and many other industries. It runs continuously at massive industrial scale around the world.

Section Summary — 24.7

Electrolysis uses electrical energy to drive nonspontaneous chemical reactions. Reduction occurs at the cathode; oxidation occurs at the anode — as in galvanic cells. Electrolysis of water produces hydrogen and oxygen gas. Electroplating deposits metal coatings on surfaces. Electrolysis is essential for producing aluminum from ore and for the industrial chlor-alkali process.

Section 24.8

Corrosion

When Oxidation Is Unwanted

Corrosion is the gradual oxidation of a metal by its environment. From a chemical standpoint, corrosion is a redox reaction — the metal is oxidized by substances like oxygen, water, or acids in the surrounding environment. From a practical standpoint, corrosion is one of the most costly and destructive processes in the material world. The most familiar form of corrosion is the rusting of iron and steel.

The Chemistry of Rusting

Iron rusts when it reacts with oxygen and water. The overall process is more complex than a single balanced equation, but the essential chemistry involves two stages.

Process
The two stages of rust formation
  • Stage 1Iron metal at the surface is oxidized to Fe²⁺ ions. 2 Fe(s) → 2 Fe²⁺(aq) + 4e⁻
  • Stage 2Oxygen dissolved in water is reduced. O₂(g) + 2 H₂O(l) + 4e⁻ → 4 OH⁻(aq)
  • ResultThe Fe²⁺ ions from Stage 1 and the OH⁻ ions from Stage 2 react to form iron(II) hydroxide, Fe(OH)₂. This can be further oxidized by more oxygen to form iron(III) hydroxide, Fe(OH)₃, which dehydrates to give iron(III) oxide, Fe₂O₃·xH₂O — the reddish-brown, flaky substance we call rust.
Note: unlike the oxide layers that form on some other metals, rust does not form a protective barrier. Rust flakes off, exposing fresh iron surface to further attack.

Factors That Speed Up Corrosion

FactorEffect
SaltDissolved NaCl dramatically increases the rate of rusting by improving the conductivity of the water, allowing electrons to move more easily. Vehicles in regions where roads are salted in winter corrode much faster than vehicles in dry climates.
AcidAcidic environments (low pH) increase corrosion by providing H⁺ ions that react with the metal or interfere with any protective oxide layer.
Contact between different metalsWhen two different metals are in contact in the presence of an electrolyte, a galvanic cell is created. The more active metal becomes the anode and corrodes preferentially — this is galvanic corrosion. Steel in contact with copper in a moist environment corrodes faster than steel alone.

Corrosion of Other Metals

While rusting is the most well-known form of corrosion, other metals corrode too — though often differently.

MetalCorrosion Behavior
AluminumOxidizes very rapidly in air, but the Al₂O₃ that forms is hard, tightly bonded, and impermeable — it protects the underlying metal from further attack.
CopperOxidizes slowly to form a green coating called patina (copper(II) carbonate and copper(II) hydroxide), visible on old copper roofs, statues, and gutters. Patina is protective, like aluminum oxide.
SilverTarnishes by reacting with sulfur compounds in the air (H₂S) to form black silver sulfide (Ag₂S) — why silverware requires polishing.
GoldEssentially immune to oxidation under ordinary conditions because gold has a very high reduction potential — it strongly resists losing electrons. Gold remains shiny for thousands of years.

Preventing Corrosion

Because corrosion causes enormous economic damage — estimated in the hundreds of billions of dollars annually worldwide — many strategies have been developed to prevent or slow it.

StrategyHow It Works
Protective coatingsPainting, powder-coating, oiling, greasing, or applying plastic films creates a physical barrier between the metal and the environment.
GalvanizingCoating steel with zinc protects it two ways: zinc physically covers the surface, and if scratched, zinc — a better reducing agent than iron — corrodes preferentially, sacrificing itself.
AlloyingMixing iron with other metals to form corrosion-resistant alloys. Stainless steel contains 10–20% chromium, forming a thin, stable, invisible oxide layer.
Cathodic protectionA more active metal is connected to the metal needing protection. The active metal becomes the anode of an inadvertent galvanic cell and oxidizes, while the protected metal becomes the cathode and is preserved.
Sacrificial Anodes

The sacrificial metal in cathodic protection is called a sacrificial anode. Zinc blocks are bolted to the hulls of steel ships and to the inside of water heaters for exactly this purpose. The zinc dissolves over time (it is "sacrificed"), protecting the steel. When the zinc block is consumed, it is replaced.

Impressed current cathodic protection uses an external power source to push electrons into the structure being protected, preventing it from being oxidized. This method is used for buried pipelines, underground storage tanks, and bridge supports.

The Economic and Safety Importance of Corrosion Control

The failure of metal structures due to corrosion has caused bridges to collapse, pipes to burst, aircraft to fail, and ships to sink. In medicine, the corrosion resistance of metal implants — hip replacements, dental implants, surgical instruments — is critical for patient safety. In electronics, corrosion of circuit connections causes device failures. Understanding the electrochemistry of corrosion is not merely academic. It is an engineering and public safety concern of the highest importance.

Section Summary — 24.8

Corrosion is the oxidation of metals by their environment. Rusting of iron involves the oxidation of iron and the reduction of oxygen in the presence of water. Corrosion is accelerated by salt, acid, and contact between dissimilar metals. Protective coatings, galvanizing, alloying, and cathodic protection are strategies used to prevent or slow corrosion. The economic and safety costs of corrosion are enormous.

Section 24.9

Electrochemistry and Energy Technology

The technologies that define modern civilization — electric vehicles, smartphones, renewable energy grids, satellites, medical devices, and industrial manufacturing — depend on electrochemistry. This final section looks at how the principles of oxidation, reduction, galvanic cells, and electrolysis are being applied to the most pressing energy challenges of the modern era.

Rechargeable Batteries and Energy Storage

A rechargeable battery is a galvanic cell that can be restored to its original chemical state by running current through it in the reverse direction — that is, by electrolysis. During discharge, the battery converts chemical energy to electrical energy (spontaneous redox). During charging, electrical energy is used to reverse the redox reaction and restore the original chemicals (electrolysis).

The lithium-ion battery has become the dominant rechargeable battery technology because of its high energy density, light weight, and ability to undergo hundreds of charge-discharge cycles without significant degradation. Lithium ions move between a graphite anode and a metal oxide cathode (often lithium cobalt oxide, LiCoO₂) through an organic electrolyte.

ProcessWhat Happens
DischargeLithium is oxidized at the anode, releasing electrons to the external circuit, while the cathode is reduced.
ChargingThe external power source reverses the process, re-reducing the anode material and re-oxidizing the cathode material.

Lithium-ion batteries power electric vehicles, portable electronics, cordless tools, and are increasingly being used for grid-scale energy storage — storing electricity generated by solar panels and wind turbines for use when the sun is not shining or the wind is not blowing. The search for better batteries — higher energy density, faster charging, longer cycle life, lower cost, and safer operation — is one of the most active areas of materials chemistry research today.

Fuel Cells

A hydrogen fuel cell generates electricity by the controlled electrochemical reaction of hydrogen and oxygen, producing only water as a product:

2 H₂(g) + O₂(g) → 2 H₂O(l) + electricity
ElectrodeHalf-Reaction
Anode (oxidation)H₂ → 2 H⁺ + 2e⁻
Cathode (reduction)O₂ + 4 H⁺ + 4e⁻ → 2 H₂O

Unlike a battery, a fuel cell does not store energy chemically within itself. It generates electricity continuously as long as hydrogen and oxygen are supplied. Fuel cells are highly efficient — they convert chemical energy to electrical energy more efficiently than combustion engines — and their only exhaust product is water.

Fuel cells have been used in NASA spacecraft since the 1960s and are now being developed for use in vehicles, buses, forklifts, and stationary power generation. The main challenges are producing hydrogen cleanly (most hydrogen today is produced from natural gas, releasing CO₂), storing hydrogen safely, and reducing the cost of fuel cell components.

Solar Energy and Electrochemistry

Solar cells (photovoltaic cells) convert light energy directly to electrical energy without any moving parts or chemical reactions. But electrochemistry plays an important role in solar energy systems in two ways. First, the electricity generated by solar panels is often stored in batteries — electrochemical devices — for use at night or during cloudy periods.

Second, scientists are working on artificial photosynthesis — using solar energy to drive the electrolysis of water, producing hydrogen fuel. This would mimic what plants do: using light energy to store chemical energy in a reduced compound. If successful at scale, artificial photosynthesis could provide a renewable, storable fuel with no carbon emissions.

Electrochemistry in Medicine

TechnologyElectrochemical Role
Implantable pacemakers and defibrillatorsRely on long-lasting batteries (typically lithium-iodide or lithium-silver-vanadium oxide) that operate for years inside the body.
Blood glucose monitorsMeasure the small electric current generated when glucose in a blood sample is oxidized by a reagent on a test strip — a direct redox measurement.
BiosensorsDetect blood oxygen levels (pulse oximetry), ion concentrations, and specific molecules using electrochemical principles.
Drug delivery systemsUse small electrical pulses to control the release of drugs from electrochemically active polymers.

Electrochemistry and Climate

The connection between electrochemistry and climate change is significant and growing. Burning fossil fuels is a redox process that oxidizes carbon to CO₂, releasing stored chemical energy but also releasing a greenhouse gas. Replacing combustion with electrochemical energy conversion — batteries and fuel cells — eliminates or reduces direct CO₂ emissions.

Electrochemical carbon capture is a research area exploring how electric current might be used to convert CO₂ into useful chemicals, effectively running photosynthesis in an industrial setting. Electrolytic production of green hydrogen (using renewable electricity to split water) is a potential pathway to decarbonizing industries like steel production, ammonia synthesis, and long-distance shipping that are difficult to electrify directly.

The chemistry of redox — electron transfer between atoms — is at the center of humanity's search for a sustainable energy future.

Section Summary — 24.9

Electrochemistry is central to modern energy technology. Rechargeable batteries (especially lithium-ion) store and release energy for electric vehicles and portable electronics. Fuel cells convert hydrogen and oxygen to electricity and water. Solar energy systems use electrochemical storage. Medical devices including pacemakers and glucose monitors rely on electrochemical principles. Electrochemistry is a key science in addressing climate change through cleaner energy conversion and storage.

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 24.1A redox reaction involves the transfer of electrons from one substance to another. Oxidation is the loss of electrons; reduction is the gain of electrons. The two processes always occur together and can be written as separate half-reactions.
  • Sec 24.2Oxidation causes a substance's charge to become more positive; reduction causes it to become more negative. The terms originated from reactions with oxygen and from metallurgy, but now refer specifically to electron transfer.
  • Sec 24.3Oxidation numbers track electron ownership using seven rules. An increase in oxidation number signals oxidation; a decrease signals reduction. Oxidation numbers let chemists identify redox reactions even in covalent compounds.
  • Sec 24.4The oxidizing agent accepts electrons and is reduced; the reducing agent donates electrons and is oxidized. Strong oxidizers include O₂, F₂, Cl₂, and KMnO₄. Strong reducers include active metals, H₂, and carbon.
  • Sec 24.5Combustion, respiration, photosynthesis, bleaching, metal refining, and food oxidation are all redox processes that shape everyday life and the natural world.
  • Sec 24.6A galvanic cell converts a spontaneous redox reaction into electrical energy. Oxidation occurs at the anode, reduction at the cathode; electrons flow through a wire and ions migrate through a salt bridge. Batteries are packaged galvanic cells.
  • Sec 24.7Electrolysis uses electrical energy to drive nonspontaneous reactions. It splits water into hydrogen and oxygen, enables electroplating, and powers the industrial production of aluminum and chlorine/sodium hydroxide.
  • Sec 24.8Corrosion is the unwanted oxidation of metals, accelerated by salt, acid, and contact between dissimilar metals. Coatings, galvanizing, alloying, and cathodic protection (including sacrificial anodes) prevent or slow corrosion.
  • Sec 24.9Electrochemistry underlies rechargeable batteries, fuel cells, solar energy storage, and medical devices, and is central to efforts to decarbonize energy production through batteries, green hydrogen, and electrochemical carbon capture.

Key Terms Review

redox reaction
A chemical reaction in which electrons are transferred from one substance to another. Oxidation and reduction occur simultaneously.
oxidation
The loss of electrons by an atom, ion, or molecule. An increase in oxidation number indicates oxidation.
reduction
The gain of electrons by an atom, ion, or molecule. A decrease in oxidation number indicates reduction.
half-reaction
One of the two parts of a redox reaction, showing either the oxidation or the reduction process separately, with electrons written explicitly.
oxidation number
A number assigned to an atom representing the charge it would have if all bonding electrons were assigned to the more electronegative atom. Used to track electron transfer.
oxidizing agent
The substance that accepts electrons from another substance, causing that substance to be oxidized. The oxidizing agent is itself reduced.
reducing agent
The substance that donates electrons to another substance, causing that substance to be reduced. The reducing agent is itself oxidized.
electrochemistry
The branch of chemistry that studies the relationship between chemical reactions and electricity.
galvanic cell (voltaic cell)
An electrochemical cell in which a spontaneous redox reaction generates electrical energy.
electrolytic cell
An electrochemical cell in which electrical energy from an external source drives a nonspontaneous chemical reaction.
anode
The electrode where oxidation occurs. Negative in a galvanic cell; connected to the positive terminal of the power source in an electrolytic cell.
cathode
The electrode where reduction occurs. Positive in a galvanic cell; connected to the negative terminal of the power source in an electrolytic cell.
salt bridge
A tube or connection filled with electrolyte that connects the two half-cells of a galvanic cell, allowing ions to migrate and maintain electrical neutrality.
voltage (cell potential, EMF)
The electrical potential difference between the two electrodes of a galvanic cell, measured in volts (V); represents the driving force of the redox reaction.
electrolysis
The use of electrical energy to drive a nonspontaneous chemical reaction in an electrolytic cell.
electroplating
The use of electrolysis to deposit a thin layer of metal onto the surface of an object.
corrosion
The gradual oxidation of a metal by its environment, driven by redox reactions.
galvanic corrosion
Accelerated corrosion that occurs when two dissimilar metals are in electrical contact in the presence of an electrolyte, forming an unintended galvanic cell.
sacrificial anode
A more active metal deliberately connected to a less active metal to protect it from corrosion. The sacrificial anode corrodes preferentially.

Formula Review

Cell Voltage

Cell voltage = Reduction potential of cathode − Reduction potential of anode

Measured in volts (V). A positive cell voltage indicates a spontaneous reaction (galvanic cell). A negative cell voltage indicates a nonspontaneous reaction (requires electrolysis).

For a neutral compound: sum of all oxidation numbers = 0

For a polyatomic ion: sum of all oxidation numbers = charge of the ion

Worked Example Review

Worked Example 1
Identifying oxidation and reduction in a reaction
  • ProblemIdentify what is oxidized and what is reduced in: 2 Al(s) + 3 Cl₂(g) → 2 AlCl₃(s)
  • ReactantsAl(s): 0 (Rule 1). Cl₂(g): 0 (Rule 1).
  • ProductsIn AlCl₃: Al = +3 (aluminum commonly forms 3+ ions). Cl = −1, since (+3) + 3(−1) = 0. ✓
  • CompareAl: 0 → +3 (increased) — oxidized; aluminum is the reducing agent. Cl: 0 → −1 (decreased) — reduced; chlorine gas is the oxidizing agent.
Answer: Aluminum is oxidized (reducing agent); chlorine is reduced (oxidizing agent).
Worked Example 2
Finding an unknown oxidation number — K₂Cr₂O₇
  • Step 1Assign known oxidation numbers: K = +1 (always); O = −2 (Rule 4).
  • Step 2Write the sum equation for this neutral compound. 2(+1) + 2(Cr) + 7(−2) = 0 2 + 2Cr − 14 = 0 2Cr = 12 Cr = +6
Answer: The oxidation number of chromium in K₂Cr₂O₇ is +6.
Worked Example 3
Describing a galvanic cell — magnesium and silver
  • SetupAnode: Mg(s) in MgCl₂(aq). Cathode: Ag(s) in AgNO₃(aq).
  • Anode (oxidation)Magnesium metal is oxidized; the magnesium strip dissolves as Mg²⁺ ions enter solution, releasing electrons into the external circuit. Mg(s) → Mg²⁺(aq) + 2e⁻
  • Cathode (reduction)Silver ions from solution are reduced; silver metal deposits on the cathode. Ag⁺(aq) + e⁻ → Ag(s)
  • BalancingMg loses 2 electrons, but each Ag⁺ gains only 1 — multiply the silver half-reaction by 2. Mg → Mg²⁺ + 2e⁻ 2 Ag⁺ + 2e⁻ → 2 Ag Overall: Mg(s) + 2 Ag⁺(aq) → Mg²⁺(aq) + 2 Ag(s)
Answer: Magnesium is oxidized at the anode; silver ions are reduced at the cathode.

In Chapter 25, you will study why carbon is uniquely suited to form the enormous variety of compounds found in living organisms, fuels, plastics, and medicines. You will learn about hydrocarbons — compounds containing only carbon and hydrogen — and about functional groups, which are specific arrangements of atoms that give organic molecules characteristic properties. You will explore structural formulas, isomers, polymers, and the connection between organic chemistry and the chemistry of life.

The skills you have built in this chapter — reading oxidation states, recognizing electron transfer, and connecting molecular structure to reactivity — will serve you well as you encounter organic reactions, many of which are themselves redox processes.

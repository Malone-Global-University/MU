Chapter 7 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

You have already learned that atoms contain protons, neutrons, and electrons. Protons and neutrons are packed tightly in the nucleus at the center of the atom. Electrons move in the space surrounding that nucleus. You have also learned that the number of protons defines which element an atom is, and that adding or removing electrons creates charged ions.

But here is a question worth sitting with: if protons determine identity, why do chemists care so much about electrons? The answer is this: protons determine what an atom is, but electrons determine what an atom does.

When two atoms bond to form a compound, electrons are involved. When a metal conducts electricity, electrons are moving. When a substance dissolves in water, electrons are being shared or attracted. When neon glows in a sign, electrons are releasing energy as light. Electrons are the active, social particles of chemistry — they reach out from atoms, interact with other atoms, and make chemical reactions possible.

This chapter builds your understanding of electrons carefully. We will move from energy level ideas to the modern electron cloud model, introduce electron configurations, explain valence electrons, and teach you to draw Lewis dot symbols. By the end of this chapter, you will have the tools needed to understand chemical bonding, periodic trends, and the behavior of real chemical substances.

Opening Question

Sodium metal reacts explosively with water. Neon — the element right after sodium in terms of electron count — barely reacts with anything at all. Both atoms have nuclei. Both have electrons. What explains the difference? By the end of this chapter, you will be able to answer that question precisely, using the language of electron configuration and valence electrons.

Section 7.1

Why Electrons Matter

Recall the basic structure of an atom. At the center sits the nucleus, which contains positively charged protons and neutral neutrons packed tightly together. Around the nucleus, in a region of space enormously larger than the nucleus itself, move the electrons — each carrying a negative charge of −1.

The nucleus is dense and small. If an atom were the size of a football stadium, the nucleus would be approximately the size of a marble at the center. Electrons would move through all that surrounding space like tiny, fast-moving specks. Because electrons occupy the outer regions of atoms, they are the first thing one atom encounters when it approaches another. When two atoms get close enough to interact chemically, it is their electrons that meet first. This is why electrons control bonding.

What Electrons Determine

Bonding — Whether an atom will form chemical bonds, what kinds, and how many.

Ion formation — Whether an atom tends to gain, lose, or share electrons in reactions.

Reactivity — How readily an element reacts with other substances.

Conductivity — Whether electrons can move freely through a material.

Energy — The energy stored in chemical bonds and released in reactions.

Key Idea

Protons determine what an atom is. Electrons determine what an atom does. A complete understanding of chemistry at the atomic level requires understanding electron arrangements.

Section 7.2

Energy Levels

Early models imagined electrons as tiny balls orbiting the nucleus like planets around the sun. This picture was useful but incorrect in important ways. Experiments showed that electrons in atoms can only have certain specific amounts of energy — not just any energy at all. This is the concept of quantized energy. An electron in an atom cannot have any energy it chooses. It must occupy one of a set of allowed energy states called energy levels.

Think of energy levels like the rungs of a ladder. You can stand on the first rung, the second rung, the third rung — but not between rungs. Electrons work the same way. They can have the energy of level 1, level 2, level 3, and so on, but not the energies in between.

Key Terms — Energy Levels

Principal quantum number (n) — the integer that labels an energy level; n = 1, 2, 3 …

Ground state — the lowest-energy arrangement of electrons in an atom; electrons occupy the lowest available levels.

Excited state — an arrangement in which one or more electrons have absorbed energy and moved to a higher level.

2n² rule — the maximum number of electrons in energy level n equals 2n².

Electrons, Light, and Flame Colors

When electrons absorb energy and jump to higher levels, then fall back down, they release that energy as light. Different elements have unique sets of energy levels, so they release light at unique wavelengths. This is why copper produces green flames, sodium produces yellow-orange ones, and neon signs glow red-orange. Each color is a signature of a specific set of electron energy jumps.

Worked Example 7.2.1
Distributing electrons among energy levels
  • ProblemSodium has 11 electrons. How are those electrons distributed among the first three energy levels?
  • Step 1Fill n = 1 first. It holds 2 electrons. Electrons placed: 2    Remaining: 11 − 2 = 9
  • Step 2Fill n = 2 next. It holds 8 electrons. Electrons placed: 8    Remaining: 9 − 8 = 1
  • Step 3The last electron goes into n = 3. 1 electron in n = 3
Answer: Sodium distributes as 2 | 8 | 1 across energy levels n = 1, 2, 3.

Section 7.3

The Electron Cloud Model

Early models, especially Bohr's model from the early 1900s, pictured electrons moving in circular orbits around the nucleus like planets orbiting the sun. Bohr's model was a significant improvement and successfully explained some observations about hydrogen — but it had serious limitations for atoms with more than one electron.

More careful experiments and mathematical work revealed something surprising: electrons do not travel in neat, defined paths. In fact, it is not possible to know exactly where an electron is and exactly how fast it is moving at the same time. This is a fundamental feature of nature at the atomic scale, not a limitation of our instruments.

Because electrons do not have defined paths, chemists describe them in terms of probability. Instead of asking "where is this electron right now?", chemists ask "where is this electron most likely to be found?" The answer defines a region of space around the nucleus called the electron cloud.

Visualizing the Electron Cloud

Imagine shaking a can of spray paint over a sheet of paper many times, starting every shake from the same point. The paint would build up in some areas more than others. If you photographed the result, the dense regions would show where the paint landed most often. The electron cloud works similarly — where the cloud is dense, the probability of finding the electron is high. Where the cloud is thin or absent, the probability is low.

Section 7.4

Orbitals as Regions of Probability

The energy levels described in Section 7.2 are divided into smaller units called sublevels (sometimes called subshells). Within each sublevel, electrons occupy regions of space called orbitals. An orbital is a specific three-dimensional region of space within an atom where there is a high probability of finding an electron. Each orbital can hold a maximum of 2 electrons.

Which Sublevels Belong to Which Energy Level

s orbitals are spherical. An electron in an s orbital is equally likely to be found in any direction around the nucleus, so the probability cloud forms a sphere. Every energy level has exactly one s orbital.

p orbitals have a dumbbell shape — two lobes extending in opposite directions from the nucleus. Because there are three p orbitals in every p sublevel, they point in three perpendicular directions. Together, the three p orbitals cover all directions in three-dimensional space.

For this course, you do not need to memorize the detailed shapes of d and f orbitals. Understanding s and p orbitals well will prepare you for everything in high school chemistry, including molecular geometry in Chapter 12.

Common Confusion — Orbital vs. Energy Level

An orbital is a single region of space that holds at most 2 electrons. An energy level is a collection of sublevels. An sublevel is a collection of one or more orbitals. The three terms are not interchangeable. n = 2 is an energy level; 2p is a sublevel; one of the three 2p regions is a single orbital.

Section 7.5

Electron Configurations

An electron configuration is a written description of how the electrons in an atom are distributed among energy levels and sublevels. Electron configurations follow a set of rules. Once you know those rules, you can write the electron configuration for any element.

Three Rules for Filling Orbitals

Aufbau Principle: Electrons occupy the lowest available energy sublevel before filling higher ones. Build the configuration from the bottom up.

Pauli Exclusion Principle: Each orbital holds a maximum of 2 electrons. No two electrons in the same atom can be in the same orbital with the same spin.

Hund's Rule: When electrons fill orbitals in the same sublevel, they spread out one per orbital before any orbital gets a second electron. Electrons prefer separate orbitals to minimize repulsion.

The Filling Order

Sublevels fill in this order from lowest to highest energy:
1s → 2s → 2p → 3s → 3p → 4s → 3d → 4p → 5s → 4d → 5p …
Notice that 4s fills before 3d. The periodic table is organized to reflect this order — use it as a guide when you need to remember the sequence.

Student Warning — The 4s/3d Order

The 4s sublevel fills before the 3d sublevel because 4s is slightly lower in energy than 3d for neutral atoms. Do not assume that a higher n value always means later filling. When in doubt, use the periodic table as your guide — elements in Period 4 of the s block (potassium, calcium) fill 4s before entering the d block.

Section 7.6

Valence Electrons

You now know how to write a complete electron configuration for an atom. But not all electrons participate equally in chemical reactions. The electrons most involved in bonding and chemical behavior are the ones in the outermost occupied energy level — the level with the highest principal quantum number (n) that actually contains electrons. These outermost electrons are called valence electrons.

For most main-group elements (the s block and p block), valence electrons are the electrons in the outermost s and p sublevels. Electrons that are not valence electrons are called core electrons. They are in lower energy levels, tightly held, and do not participate in bonding under ordinary chemical conditions.

How to Find Valence Electrons

1. Write the electron configuration of the atom.

2. Identify the highest n value that appears in the configuration.

3. Count all electrons in sublevels with that n value.

Worked Example 7.6.1
Valence electrons of sodium (11 electrons)
  • Config1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s¹
  • FindHighest n = 3. Only 3s is present. It contains 1 electron.
Sodium has 1 valence electron.
Worked Example 7.6.2
Valence electrons of sulfur (16 electrons)
  • Config1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s² 3p⁴
  • FindHighest n = 3. Sublevels: 3s² and 3p⁴. Total: 2 + 4 = 6 valence electrons.
Sulfur has 6 valence electrons.

Valence Electrons and the Periodic Table

One of the most powerful features of the periodic table is that elements in the same group have the same number of valence electrons. This is not a coincidence — it is the reason those elements behave similarly in chemical reactions.

Section 7.7

Lewis Dot Symbols

Chemists use a simple visual shorthand to represent the valence electrons of an atom called a Lewis dot symbol. It was developed by American chemist Gilbert N. Lewis in the early twentieth century and remains one of the most widely used tools in chemistry.

A Lewis dot symbol consists of the chemical symbol of the element, surrounded by dots — each dot representing one valence electron.

Rules for Drawing Lewis Dot Symbols

1. Write the element's chemical symbol.

2. Count the number of valence electrons.

3. Place dots one at a time on the four sides of the symbol (top, bottom, left, right). Each side is one position.

4. Once each side has one dot, begin pairing — add a second dot to each position that needs it.

5. Continue until all valence electrons are placed. Follow Hund's rule: spread out before pairing.

What Lewis Dot Symbols Tell Us

Unpaired electrons (single dots) are available to form bonds. An atom with 3 unpaired electrons can form 3 bonds; an atom with 1 unpaired electron can form 1 bond. Lone pairs (paired dots) are not typically used for bonding in the same way, though they influence molecular geometry. The drive to pair unpaired electrons with unpaired electrons from other atoms is the basis of covalent bonding.

Section 7.8

Stable Electron Arrangements

Atoms do not bond randomly. They bond because bonding leads to a more stable, lower-energy arrangement. The most stable electron arrangement for most atoms involves having a full outer energy level. For most atoms, a full outer level means 8 valence electrons. This stability goal is called the octet rule: atoms tend to gain, lose, or share electrons until they have 8 valence electrons in their outermost level.

Three Ways Atoms Reach Stability

Gaining electrons (forming anions) — Nonmetals on the right side often gain electrons. Chlorine has 7 valence electrons; gaining 1 gives it 8, forming Cl⁻ with the argon configuration.

Losing electrons (forming cations) — Metals on the left side often lose electrons. Sodium has 1 valence electron in n = 3; losing it exposes n = 2 with 8 electrons, forming Na⁺ with the neon configuration.

Sharing electrons (covalent bonding) — Nonmetals share electrons with each other. Each atom counts shared electrons toward its own total, helping both atoms reach 8.

SituationApplies ToExampleResult
Duet ruleH, He (n = 1 holds only 2)H + H → H₂Each H counts 2 electrons

Student Warning — The Octet Rule Has Exceptions

The octet rule is a useful guideline, not an absolute law. Hydrogen and helium work with a duet. Boron commonly has only 6 electrons around it in compounds. Phosphorus and sulfur can have more than 8 valence electrons in some compounds. Transition metals often do not follow the rule at all. Use the octet rule as a helpful first prediction, not as an unbreakable rule.

Section 7.9

Electrons and Chemical Behavior

You have now studied energy levels, the electron cloud model, orbitals, electron configurations, valence electrons, Lewis dot symbols, and the octet rule. In this final section, we step back and connect all of these ideas to the larger story of chemistry.

Electrons Explain the Periodic Table

Elements are arranged by atomic number — and as atomic number increases, electrons are added one by one to energy levels and sublevels. When a new energy level begins filling, a new period of the table starts. When the same sublevel fills across a row, elements in that row share the same general electron arrangement pattern. When a column of elements all have the same number of valence electrons, they share similar chemical behavior. The periodic table is essentially a map of electron configurations.

Electrons Explain Bonding

Atoms bond to achieve full outer energy levels. Ionic bonds form when one atom transfers electrons to another, creating oppositely charged ions that attract. Covalent bonds form when atoms share electrons, both counting the shared pair toward their valence totals. The type of bond, the number of bonds, and the geometry of molecules all trace back to electron arrangements.

Electrons Explain Reactivity

Some atoms react violently, some react slowly, and some barely react at all. Alkali metals (Group 1) have 1 valence electron — easily lost, producing a very stable configuration. They react with almost anything. Noble gases have full outer shells and nothing to gain or lose, so they do not react. Halogens (Group 17) have 7 valence electrons — one short of a full shell. They aggressively attract electrons from other atoms. Reactivity follows directly from the electron count.

Electrons Explain Energy

When electrons move between energy levels, energy is absorbed or released. When bonds form, energy is released. When bonds break, energy is absorbed. The energy stored in fuels — gasoline, natural gas, hydrogen — is ultimately stored in the electron arrangements of molecules. Burning releases that energy because the products have lower-energy electron arrangements than the reactants.

The Invisible Made Useful

Electrons cannot be seen directly — they are smaller than any traditional microscope can image. Yet chemists have built an extraordinarily detailed and reliable model of electron behavior, one that accurately predicts the colors of light emitted by atoms, the angles of bonds in molecules, the conductivity of metals, the strength of acids, the solubility of compounds, and countless other properties. This is what makes the electron model one of the great achievements of human understanding.

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 7.1Electrons are the chemically active particles in atoms. They occupy the outer regions of atoms and are the first particles encountered when atoms interact. Electrons determine bonding, ion formation, reactivity, conductivity, and energy changes.
  • Sec 7.2Electrons occupy quantized energy levels labeled by principal quantum number n. The maximum number of electrons in level n is 2n². Electrons fill the lowest levels first (ground state) and can absorb energy to jump to higher levels (excited state).
  • Sec 7.3The electron cloud model replaces the orbit picture with a probability-based description. The electron cloud is a map of where an electron is likely to be found. Atoms have no sharp edges because the cloud thins gradually with distance from the nucleus.
  • Sec 7.4Energy levels contain sublevels (s, p, d, f), and sublevels contain orbitals. Each orbital holds at most 2 electrons. s orbitals are spherical; p orbitals are dumbbell-shaped. The p sublevel holds 6 electrons in 3 orbitals; the s sublevel holds 2 in 1 orbital.
  • Sec 7.5Electron configurations describe the distribution of electrons among sublevels. Three rules govern filling: Aufbau (lowest first), Pauli exclusion (max 2 per orbital), Hund's rule (spread before pairing). The order 4s fills before 3d is an important detail.
  • Sec 7.6Valence electrons are in the outermost occupied energy level and determine chemical behavior. For main-group elements, the group number equals the valence electron count. Core electrons do not participate in ordinary bonding.
  • Sec 7.7Lewis dot symbols represent valence electrons as dots around the element's chemical symbol. Unpaired electrons (single dots) are available for bonding. Lone pairs (paired dots) influence geometry. Elements in the same group have identical Lewis dot patterns.
  • Sec 7.8The octet rule: most main-group atoms gain, lose, or share electrons until they have 8 valence electrons. Hydrogen and helium seek a duet (2 electrons). Noble gases are already stable and rarely react. The octet rule is a guideline with exceptions.
  • Sec 7.9Electron arrangements explain the structure of the periodic table, the nature of chemical bonds, the reactivity of elements, and the energy changes in reactions. All bonding and periodic trend content in the chapters ahead depends on the ideas introduced here.

Key Terms Review

Aufbau principle
The rule that electrons fill the lowest available energy sublevel before occupying higher ones. Build configurations from the bottom up.
core electrons
Electrons in inner energy levels that do not participate in bonding; also called inner electrons. They shield valence electrons from the nucleus.
electron cloud
A probability-based map of where an electron is likely to be found around a nucleus. Dense regions indicate high probability; thin regions indicate low probability.
electron configuration
A notation describing how electrons are distributed among the energy levels and sublevels of an atom. Example: 1s² 2s² 2p⁶.
energy level
One of the allowed, quantized energy states an electron can occupy in an atom. Labeled by the principal quantum number n = 1, 2, 3, …
excited state
An electron arrangement in which one or more electrons have absorbed energy and moved to a higher energy level than the ground state.
ground state
The lowest-energy electron arrangement of an atom; electrons occupy the lowest available energy levels.
Hund's rule
Electrons fill orbitals in the same sublevel one at a time before any orbital gets a second electron. Electrons spread out before pairing.
Lewis dot symbol
A notation showing the chemical symbol surrounded by dots representing valence electrons. Each dot is one valence electron.
lone pair
A pair of valence electrons that is not involved in bonding; also called a nonbonding pair. Lone pairs influence molecular geometry.
octet rule
The tendency of main-group atoms to gain, lose, or share electrons until they have 8 valence electrons. A useful guideline with known exceptions.
orbital
A three-dimensional region of space within an atom where there is a high probability of finding an electron. Each orbital holds at most 2 electrons.
Pauli exclusion principle
No two electrons in the same atom can occupy the same orbital with the same spin. Each orbital holds at most 2 electrons.
principal quantum number (n)
The integer that labels an energy level (n = 1, 2, 3, …). Higher n means higher energy and greater average distance from the nucleus.
quantized energy
Energy that can only take specific, allowed values rather than any value in a continuous range. Electrons in atoms have quantized energies.
sublevel
A subdivision of an energy level, labeled s, p, d, or f. Each sublevel contains a specific number of orbitals and holds a specific maximum number of electrons.
unpaired electron
A valence electron that occupies an orbital by itself; available to form a bond with another atom. Counted as single dots in Lewis dot symbols.
valence electrons
Electrons in the outermost occupied energy level of an atom. These electrons participate in bonding and determine chemical behavior.

Common Misconceptions

Misconception 1 — Electrons orbit the nucleus like planets
"Electrons travel in fixed circular orbits around the nucleus." Correction: Electrons do not follow defined paths. They occupy regions of probability called orbitals. The location of an electron at any moment cannot be precisely determined. The orbit image is a useful introductory picture but does not reflect how electrons actually behave.
Misconception 2 — Higher energy levels are always farther from the nucleus
"An electron in n = 3 is always farther from the nucleus than an electron in n = 2." Correction: Higher n values correspond to greater average distances from the nucleus, but electrons in any level can be found at various distances. The statement is approximately true and useful as a guide, but not precisely exact.
Misconception 3 — Electron configurations never change
"The electron configuration of an element is the same whether it is a neutral atom or part of a compound." Correction: Electron configurations describe neutral atoms. When atoms bond, they gain, lose, or share electrons — so their electron arrangement changes. The neutral atom configuration is a starting point, not a fixed description for all situations.
Misconception 4 — The octet rule is absolute
"All atoms always end up with exactly 8 valence electrons after bonding." Correction: The octet rule is a useful guideline with real exceptions. Hydrogen and helium use a duet (2 electrons). Boron commonly forms compounds with 6 electrons. Phosphorus and sulfur can have more than 8. Transition metals often do not follow the rule at all.
Misconception 5 — Lone pairs are wasted electrons
"Lone pairs are leftover electrons that didn't bond — they don't do anything." Correction: Lone pairs are valence electrons not involved in bonding between atoms, but they are not wasted. They influence molecular geometry, contribute to polarity, and can participate in certain bonding types. They are a full part of the electron arrangement.
Misconception 6 — Valence electrons are weakly held because they are "outer"
"Valence electrons are the outermost electrons, so they must be very loosely held by all atoms." Correction: How tightly valence electrons are held depends on nuclear charge and shielding by core electrons. Fluorine, for example, holds its valence electrons very tightly despite them being outer electrons. This variation is the basis of periodic trends you will study in Chapter 8.

Chapter 12 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

When you draw a Lewis structure on paper, it looks flat. Dots and lines stretch left and right across the page. But molecules are not flat. They exist in three dimensions, and their shapes are just as real as the shapes of anything else you can hold in your hand.

The shape of a molecule is not just an interesting fact. Shape determines how molecules interact with each other, how they dissolve in water, whether they have high or low boiling points, and whether they can fit into a biological receptor the way a key fits a lock. The shape of water explains why ice floats. The shape of DNA's building blocks allows genetic information to be copied. The shape of a drug molecule determines whether it will work in the human body.

In this chapter, you will learn how to predict the three-dimensional shape of a molecule from its Lewis structure, how to determine whether a molecule is polar or nonpolar, and how molecular polarity and shape together determine the forces that act between molecules. These forces — called intermolecular forces — control physical properties such as melting point, boiling point, viscosity, and solubility. By the end of this chapter, you will see molecules not just as flat diagrams but as three-dimensional objects whose geometry shapes the physical world around you.

Opening Question

Water (H₂O) and carbon dioxide (CO₂) are both small molecules made of nonmetals. Yet water is a liquid at room temperature while CO₂ is a gas. Water is the universal solvent of life; CO₂ does not dissolve freely in your cells. A major reason for this difference is not what the molecules are made of — it is what shape they take in three dimensions. By the end of this chapter, you will be able to explain this difference precisely.

Section 12.1

Molecules Have Shape

Consider two molecules: carbon dioxide (CO₂) and water (H₂O). Both are small, simple molecules. Both are compounds of nonmetals. Both appear constantly in Earth's atmosphere and in living things. Yet CO₂ is a gas at room temperature that does not dissolve well in biological fluids, while water is a liquid at room temperature and the universal solvent of life. A major reason for this difference is molecular shape.

Carbon dioxide is linear. Its two oxygen atoms sit on opposite ends of a straight line with carbon in the center. Water is bent. Its two hydrogen atoms are angled away from each other — not opposite, but at about 104.5°. This difference in shape produces dramatic differences in the properties of the two substances.

A bond angle is the angle formed between two bonds that share a central atom. In water, the bond angle is about 104.5°. In carbon dioxide, the bond angle is exactly 180°. In methane (CH₄), all four bond angles are 109.5°. These are not coincidences — they are predictable consequences of how electron pairs arrange themselves around a central atom.

Key Insight

The shape of a molecule is not random or arbitrary. It follows from the arrangement of electrons around the central atom. Understanding this connection — from Lewis structure to three-dimensional shape to physical property — is the central skill of this chapter.

Section 12.2

Electron Pair Repulsion

The VSEPR Principle

Electron pairs in the valence shell of a central atom repel each other and arrange themselves to be as far apart as possible. This arrangement determines the shape of the molecule.

VSEPR stands for Valence Shell Electron Pair Repulsion. Pronounced "vesper."

An electron group is any region of electron density around a central atom — including single bonds, double bonds, triple bonds, and lone pairs. Multiple bonds (double and triple) count as one electron group because all their electrons occupy the same region of space between the bonded atoms.

To use VSEPR, count the total number of electron groups around the central atom. The arrangement that keeps them farthest apart depends on how many groups there are: two groups achieve 180° separation; three groups spread into a flat triangle at 120°; four groups spread into a three-dimensional tetrahedron at 109.5°.

Electron GroupsElectron ArrangementIdeal AngleExample Central Atom
2Linear180°BeCl₂ (Be)
3Trigonal planar120°BF₃ (B)
4Tetrahedral109.5°CH₄ (C), NH₃ (N), H₂O (O)

Lone Pairs vs. Bonding Pairs

Not all electron pairs repel equally. Lone pairs are closer to the central atom than bonding pairs, because bonding pairs are pulled outward — they are shared between two atoms. Lone pairs belong entirely to one atom and spread out more in space. As a result, lone pairs exert stronger repulsive forces than bonding pairs and compress the angles between bonding pairs.

In methane (CH₄), four identical bonding pairs produce bond angles of exactly 109.5°. In ammonia (NH₃), one lone pair pushes the three bonding pairs closer together — angles compress to about 107°. In water (H₂O), two lone pairs compress the bond angle further to about 104.5°.

Student Warning

Do not forget to count lone pairs when determining the total number of electron groups. Lone pairs are invisible in the final molecular shape, but they must be included in the electron group count because they push the bonding pairs into their actual positions.

Section 12.3

Linear, Bent, Trigonal Planar, and Tetrahedral Shapes

Electron geometry describes the arrangement of all electron groups around a central atom, including lone pairs. Molecular geometry describes the arrangement of atoms only — lone pairs are not atoms, so they do not appear in the final shape. When lone pairs are present, the electron geometry and molecular geometry are different.

Electron GroupsLone PairsMolecular GeometryBond AngleExample
20Linear180°CO₂, BeCl₂
30Trigonal planar120°BF₃, SO₃
31Bent~117°SO₂
40Tetrahedral109.5°CH₄, CCl₄
41Trigonal pyramidal~107°NH₃, PH₃
42Bent~104.5°H₂O, H₂S

Linear Molecules

A molecule is linear when two bonding groups and no lone pairs surround the central atom. The electron pairs point in exactly opposite directions, producing a straight molecule with a 180° bond angle. Carbon dioxide (CO₂) is the classic example — two double bonds, no lone pairs on carbon, oxygen atoms on opposite sides. Hydrogen cyanide (HCN), with a triple bond to nitrogen and a single bond to hydrogen, is also linear.

Trigonal Planar Molecules

When three electron groups surround a central atom with no lone pairs, the groups arrange into a flat triangle with 120° angles. All atoms lie in the same plane. Boron trifluoride (BF₃) is trigonal planar. Formaldehyde (H₂C=O) is also trigonal planar — carbon has a double bond to oxygen (one electron group) and two single bonds to hydrogen, giving three groups total. When one lone pair is present among three electron groups, the molecular shape becomes bent, with bond angles slightly less than 120°.

Tetrahedral Molecules

Four electron groups with no lone pairs point toward the corners of a tetrahedron — a three-dimensional shape with four triangular faces. Bond angles are all 109.5°. Methane (CH₄) is the classic tetrahedral molecule: four identical C–H bonds pointing in four different directions in space, not flat. Carbon tetrachloride (CCl₄) and silicon tetrafluoride (SiF₄) are also tetrahedral.

Trigonal Pyramidal Molecules

When four electron groups are present but one is a lone pair, the molecular geometry is trigonal pyramidal. The three bonding pairs point to three corners of a tetrahedron; the lone pair occupies the fourth corner but is not an atom, so it is invisible in the shape. The result looks like a triangular pyramid. Ammonia (NH₃) is the best-known example, with a bond angle of approximately 107°.

Bent Molecules

When four electron groups surround a central atom but two are lone pairs, only two atoms are bonded to the central atom. The molecule is bent (sometimes called V-shaped), resembling a boomerang. Water (H₂O) is the most important example, with a bond angle of approximately 104.5°. Two lone pairs compress the bond angle even more than one does.

Worked Example 12.1
Predicting Molecular Geometry — Phosphorus Trifluoride (PF₃)
  • Step 1Draw the Lewis structure. Phosphorus (Group 15) has 5 valence electrons. Each fluorine has 7. Total: 5 + 3(7) = 26 electrons. Phosphorus forms three single bonds to three fluorine atoms (6 electrons). Each fluorine has 3 lone pairs (18 electrons). Phosphorus has 1 lone pair remaining (2 electrons). Total: 26 electrons. ✓
  • Step 2Count electron groups around the central atom. Phosphorus has 3 bonding pairs + 1 lone pair = 4 electron groups.
  • Step 3Determine electron geometry. 4 electron groups → tetrahedral electron geometry.
  • Step 4Identify lone pairs and determine molecular geometry. 1 lone pair → trigonal pyramidal molecular geometry.
  • Step 5Predict the bond angle. Approximately 107° — less than 109.5° because the lone pair compresses the bond angles.
Answer: PF₃ is trigonal pyramidal with bond angles of approximately 107°.
Worked Example 12.2
Electron Geometry vs. Molecular Geometry — Sulfur Dioxide (SO₂)
  • Step 1The sulfur atom in SO₂ has 2 bonding groups and 1 lone pair. Count electron groups: 2 bonding + 1 lone pair = 3 electron groups.
  • Step 2Electron geometry: 3 electron groups → trigonal planar electron geometry.
  • Step 3Molecular geometry: The lone pair is not an atom. With one lone pair among three electron groups, the molecular geometry is bent — only the two bonded oxygen atoms and central sulfur atom define the shape.
  • Step 4Bond angle: approximately 117° — less than 120° because the lone pair compresses the bonding pairs slightly.
Answer: SO₂ is bent with a bond angle of approximately 117°. Its electron geometry is trigonal planar; its molecular geometry is bent.

Section 12.4

Bond Polarity

Electronegativity is a measure of an atom's ability to attract shared electrons toward itself in a covalent bond. Electronegativity increases across a period (left to right) and decreases down a group. Fluorine is the most electronegative element. Oxygen, nitrogen, and chlorine are also highly electronegative. Carbon, hydrogen, and most metals have lower electronegativity values.

When two atoms with different electronegativities share electrons, the electrons are not shared equally. The more electronegative atom pulls the electrons closer to itself. This creates a polar covalent bond — one end is slightly negative, the other slightly positive. Chemists use the Greek letter delta (δ) to show this: the more electronegative atom is labeled δ−, the other is labeled δ+.

When two identical atoms share a bond — as in H₂, O₂, or Cl₂ — the electronegativity values are exactly equal. The electrons are shared perfectly. This is a nonpolar covalent bond. Bonds between atoms with very similar electronegativity (such as C–H) are also effectively nonpolar.

Electronegativity DifferenceBond TypeExample
≈ 0 (identical atoms)Nonpolar covalentH–H, Cl–Cl, C–C
0 – ~0.4Slightly polar covalent (often treated as nonpolar)C–H
~0.4 – ~1.7Polar covalentO–H, N–H, C–O, H–Cl
> ~1.7IonicNa–Cl, Mg–O
Common Polar Bonds

O–H (very polar), N–H (polar), H–F (very polar), C–O (moderately polar), C–N (moderately polar), H–Cl (polar), C–F (very polar). These patterns appear throughout organic chemistry and biochemistry.

Bond Type Is a Continuum

The boundary between polar covalent and ionic is not a sharp line — it is a gradual transition. Electronegativity differences give approximate guidance, not absolute categories. A difference of 1.7 is a rough threshold, not a chemical law.

Section 12.5

Molecular Polarity

A molecule can have polar bonds but still be nonpolar overall. This happens when the individual bond polarities cancel because of molecular geometry. Think of it this way: if two people pull equally on opposite ends of a rope, there is no net motion in either direction — the forces cancel. A similar cancellation happens in symmetric molecules.

To determine whether a molecule is polar, you must consider two things: (1) whether the individual bonds are polar, and (2) whether the bond polarities cancel or add together based on the shape of the molecule.

Five-Step Decision Process

Step 1: Draw the Lewis structure.

Step 2: Determine the molecular geometry.

Step 3: Identify all polar bonds.

Step 4: Determine whether the bond polarities cancel (symmetric) or add together (asymmetric).

Step 5: If polarities cancel → nonpolar molecule. If they do not cancel → polar molecule (has a dipole moment).

Symmetric Molecules — Nonpolar

Carbon dioxide (CO₂) has very polar C=O bonds, but is linear — the two bond polarities point in exactly opposite directions and cancel perfectly. CO₂ is nonpolar despite having polar bonds. Carbon tetrachloride (CCl₄) has four polar C–Cl bonds, but its perfect tetrahedral symmetry causes them to cancel. Boron trifluoride (BF₃) has three polar B–F bonds in a symmetric trigonal planar geometry — they cancel. All three are nonpolar molecules.

Asymmetric Molecules — Polar

Water (H₂O) is bent, not linear. The two O–H bond polarities point at an angle — they do not cancel. The oxygen end has a net partial negative charge (δ−) and the hydrogen end has a net partial positive charge (δ+). Water is a polar molecule with a significant dipole moment. Ammonia (NH₃) is also polar — three N–H bond polarities all point toward nitrogen, adding together, and the lone pair on nitrogen adds to the asymmetry. Hydrogen chloride (HCl), a simple diatomic molecule, is polar because chlorine is more electronegative than hydrogen.

Worked Example 12.3
Determining Molecular Polarity — Chloroform (CHCl₃)
  • Step 1Lewis structure and geometry: Carbon forms 4 single bonds (one to H, three to Cl). No lone pairs on carbon. Tetrahedral geometry.
  • Step 2Bond polarity: The C–Cl bonds are polar (Cl is significantly more electronegative than C). The C–H bond is only very slightly polar and is often treated as approximately nonpolar.
  • Step 3Symmetry check: In CCl₄, four identical Cl atoms pull equally in all four tetrahedral directions — they cancel. But in CHCl₃, one Cl is replaced by H. This breaks the symmetry. The three C–Cl bond polarities all pull toward the chlorine-rich end.
  • Step 4The bond polarities do not cancel. The chlorine end of the molecule has a net partial negative charge; the hydrogen end has a net partial positive charge.
Answer: CHCl₃ is a polar molecule. This is why chloroform was historically used as a solvent — it is polar enough to dissolve some ionic and polar compounds.

Section 12.6

Intermolecular Forces

In Chapter 10, you studied intramolecular forces — the covalent and ionic bonds that hold atoms together within a compound. Those forces are strong, and breaking them requires large amounts of energy and produces chemical change.

Intermolecular forces are different. They are the attractions between separate molecules. These forces are much weaker than covalent or ionic bonds, but they are enormously important because they determine the physical properties of substances: whether a substance is a solid, liquid, or gas at a given temperature; how high its boiling point is; how easily it dissolves in water; how viscous (thick) it is.

When a liquid boils, covalent bonds within the molecules do not break. The molecules simply gain enough kinetic energy to overcome the intermolecular attractions holding them together, and they fly off as gas-phase molecules. Boiling overcomes intermolecular forces — it does not break intramolecular bonds.

Section 12.7

Hydrogen Bonding

Requirements for Hydrogen Bonding

A hydrogen atom must be covalently bonded directly to a highly electronegative atom — F, O, or N.

That hydrogen is then attracted to a lone pair on another F, O, or N in a neighboring molecule.

Hydrogen bonded to carbon, sulfur, or other elements does not form true hydrogen bonds — those atoms are not electronegative enough to strip enough electron density away from hydrogen.

Water is the most familiar example of hydrogen bonding. Each water molecule can form hydrogen bonds with up to four neighboring water molecules: two through the two hydrogen atoms (each H can interact with an O on a neighbor) and two through the two lone pairs on the oxygen (which can attract H atoms from neighbors). This extensive network of hydrogen bonds is responsible for water's remarkable properties.

Hydrogen bonds are significantly stronger than most other intermolecular forces — typically 5 to 30 kJ/mol — though still much weaker than covalent bonds (hundreds of kJ/mol). This difference is enough to dramatically raise boiling points. Compare water (H₂O, molar mass 18 g/mol, boiling point 100°C) to hydrogen sulfide (H₂S, molar mass 34 g/mol, boiling point −60°C). Despite being smaller and lighter, water's hydrogen-bonding network gives it a much higher boiling point.

Substances That Form Hydrogen Bonds

Substances that form hydrogen bonds include water (H₂O), ammonia (NH₃), hydrogen fluoride (HF), alcohols (contain O–H groups), carboxylic acids (contain O–H groups), amines (contain N–H groups), DNA base pairs (N–H···N and N–H···O), and proteins (N–H···O interactions along the backbone). Hydrogen bonding is essential to life — the double helix structure of DNA is held together between strands by hydrogen bonds, and the three-dimensional shapes of proteins are stabilized partly by internal hydrogen bonds.

Why Hydrogen Bonds Are Unusually Strong

F, O, and N are so electronegative that they pull nearly all the electron density away from the bonded hydrogen, leaving a hydrogen nucleus that is almost bare — a proton with very little electron shielding. This nearly exposed proton is strongly attracted to lone pairs on nearby electronegative atoms, creating a stronger attraction than ordinary dipole-dipole forces.

Section 12.8

Intermolecular Forces and Physical Properties

London Dispersion Forces

London dispersion forces (also called van der Waals forces) are the weakest type of intermolecular attraction, but they are present in all molecules — polar and nonpolar alike. They arise because electrons are in constant motion. At any instant, the electron distribution in a molecule may be slightly uneven, creating a temporary dipole. This temporary dipole induces a dipole in a neighboring molecule, causing a fleeting attraction. Over time these average to zero, but at any moment they create measurable attraction.

London dispersion forces are stronger in molecules with more electrons. A molecule with many electrons has a larger, more polarizable electron cloud — its electrons can shift more easily to create temporary dipoles. This is why larger molecules generally have higher boiling points than smaller ones, even when both are nonpolar.

Among the halogens: F₂ is a gas, Cl₂ is a gas, Br₂ is a liquid, and I₂ is a solid at room temperature. This progression reflects increasing London dispersion forces as the number of electrons increases.

Dipole-Dipole Forces

Polar molecules have permanent dipoles — one end is consistently partially negative and the other consistently partially positive. When polar molecules are near each other, they align so positive ends face neighboring negative ends. This is a dipole-dipole force. These forces are stronger than London dispersion forces between similarly sized molecules. Polar molecules tend to have higher boiling points than nonpolar molecules of similar size because they experience both dipole-dipole forces and London dispersion forces, while nonpolar molecules experience only London dispersion forces.

The "Like Dissolves Like" Rule

Solubility is closely connected to intermolecular forces. Polar solvents — especially water — dissolve polar solutes and ionic compounds well because they can engage in dipole-dipole interactions or hydrogen bonds with those solutes. Nonpolar solvents dissolve nonpolar solutes well through London dispersion forces.

Water and oil do not mix. Oil consists of nonpolar hydrocarbon molecules with only London dispersion forces. Water has strong hydrogen bonds. Water molecules are much more strongly attracted to each other than to the oil molecules, so they exclude the oil. Soap works because its molecules have a polar end (interacts with water) and a nonpolar end (interacts with grease), allowing it to bridge the two.

Stronger Intermolecular Forces Produce…Weaker Intermolecular Forces Produce…
Higher melting pointLower melting point
Higher boiling pointLower boiling point
Higher viscosity (thicker liquid)Lower viscosity (thinner liquid)
Lower vapor pressureHigher vapor pressure
Worked Example 12.4
Comparing Boiling Points — CH₄, HCl, and H₂O
  • MethaneCH₄ is nonpolar. Only London dispersion forces. Very small molecule with few electrons. Weakest intermolecular forces of the three. Boiling point: −161°C.
  • HClHCl is polar. Has both London dispersion forces and dipole-dipole forces. Larger than methane (more electrons). Stronger intermolecular forces than methane. Boiling point: −85°C.
  • WaterH₂O is polar and forms hydrogen bonds. Has London dispersion forces, dipole-dipole forces, AND hydrogen bonds. Despite being smaller than HCl, water's hydrogen bonds are significantly stronger than HCl's dipole-dipole forces. Boiling point: +100°C.
  • RankingCH₄ (−161°C) < HCl (−85°C) < H₂O (+100°C)
The order reflects increasing intermolecular force strength: London dispersion only → London + dipole-dipole → London + dipole-dipole + hydrogen bonds.

Section 12.9

Structure and Function in Chemical Systems

Everything you have studied in this chapter comes together to explain something fundamental: why substances have the physical properties they do, and how molecular structure determines function in the real world.

Water's Unusual Properties

Water's high boiling point (100°C) is due to its extensive hydrogen bonding. When water freezes, the hydrogen bonds lock into a crystal lattice slightly less dense than liquid water — which is why ice floats. If ice sank, lakes would freeze from the bottom up, destroying aquatic ecosystems. Water's polarity also makes it an excellent solvent for ionic and polar substances, earning it the name "universal solvent of life."

DNA and Protein Structure

The two strands of the DNA double helix are held together between complementary base pairs by hydrogen bonds. These bonds are strong enough to maintain the double helix at body temperature but weak enough to be unzipped when the cell needs to copy or read the genetic code. Proteins fold into specific three-dimensional shapes partly because nonpolar regions cluster toward the interior (away from water) while polar and charged regions face outward — a process driven entirely by intermolecular forces.

Drug Design, Polymers, and Fuels

A drug molecule must fit precisely into a biological receptor. Chemists design drugs by carefully engineering their geometry, polarity, and hydrogen-bonding capacity — all concepts from this chapter. Synthetic polymers' physical properties (hard vs. soft, rigid vs. flexible) depend on intermolecular forces between chains: polar polymers like nylon have stronger chain-to-chain attractions and higher melting points than nonpolar polymers like polyethylene. Fuels such as methane and octane are nonpolar hydrocarbons — their low polarity means they do not dissolve in water but vaporize easily enough to mix with air for combustion.

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 12.1Molecules are three-dimensional objects whose shapes are not arbitrary but predictable. Bond angles are the angles between bonds at a central atom and are determined by electron pair arrangement. Shape determines physical properties.
  • Sec 12.2VSEPR theory predicts molecular geometry based on the principle that electron groups repel each other and arrange to be as far apart as possible. Lone pairs repel more strongly than bonding pairs and compress bond angles. An electron group is any region of electron density: single bond, multiple bond, or lone pair.
  • Sec 12.3The five core geometries at the high school level are: linear (2 groups, 0 lone pairs, 180°), trigonal planar (3 groups, 0 lone pairs, 120°), tetrahedral (4 groups, 0 lone pairs, 109.5°), trigonal pyramidal (4 groups, 1 lone pair, ~107°), and bent (3 or 4 groups with 1–2 lone pairs, ~104.5–117°). Electron geometry includes all groups; molecular geometry shows atoms only.
  • Sec 12.4Electronegativity is an atom's ability to attract shared electrons. Bonds between atoms of different electronegativity are polar covalent; those between identical atoms are nonpolar. The more electronegative atom carries a partial negative charge (δ−); the other carries a partial positive charge (δ+). Very large electronegativity differences produce ionic bonds.
  • Sec 12.5A molecule is polar if it has polar bonds arranged asymmetrically so they do not cancel. It is nonpolar if it has no polar bonds or if its polar bonds cancel because of symmetric geometry. Polarity requires both polar bonds AND an asymmetric arrangement. A dipole moment is the measure of molecular polarity.
  • Sec 12.6Intermolecular forces are attractions between separate molecules — distinct from the intramolecular bonds within molecules. Three types exist: London dispersion forces (all molecules), dipole-dipole forces (polar molecules), and hydrogen bonds (H bonded to F, O, or N). Stronger intermolecular forces produce higher melting and boiling points, greater viscosity, and lower vapor pressure.
  • Sec 12.7Hydrogen bonds require H directly bonded to F, O, or N. The resulting attraction between this nearly bare proton and a lone pair on another F, O, or N is the strongest intermolecular force. Hydrogen bonding explains water's anomalously high boiling point and is essential to DNA structure, protein folding, and many biological processes.
  • Sec 12.8London dispersion forces arise from temporary dipoles and increase with molecular size. Dipole-dipole forces arise from the alignment of permanent dipoles in polar molecules. The "like dissolves like" rule states that polar solvents dissolve polar solutes and nonpolar solvents dissolve nonpolar solutes — a direct consequence of intermolecular force compatibility.
  • Sec 12.9Molecular geometry and intermolecular forces explain water's properties, the structure of ice, DNA base pairing, protein folding, drug-receptor interactions, polymer properties, fuel volatility, and the mechanism of anesthetics. These concepts connect to every remaining chapter of this textbook.

Key Terms

VSEPR model
A model that predicts molecular geometry by assuming electron groups around a central atom repel each other and arrange as far apart as possible.
electron group
Any region of electron density around a central atom: single bond, double bond, triple bond, or lone pair. Multiple bonds count as one group.
electron geometry
The three-dimensional arrangement of all electron groups (bonding and lone pairs) around a central atom.
molecular geometry
The three-dimensional arrangement of atoms in a molecule — determined by bonding pairs only; lone pairs are not atoms and do not appear in the shape.
bond angle
The angle formed between two bonds sharing a central atom. Determined by the number of electron groups and lone pairs.
linear
A molecular geometry with all atoms in a straight line; bond angle 180°. Occurs with two electron groups and no lone pairs.
trigonal planar
A molecular geometry in which the central atom and three surrounding atoms lie in the same plane with 120° bond angles.
tetrahedral
A molecular geometry with four atoms bonded to a central atom, pointing toward the corners of a tetrahedron at 109.5°.
trigonal pyramidal
A molecular geometry with three bonding pairs and one lone pair around the central atom; shaped like a triangular pyramid with ~107° bond angles.
bent (V-shaped)
A molecular geometry with two bonding pairs and one or two lone pairs; the molecule resembles a boomerang with bond angles less than 120° or 109.5°.
electronegativity
A measure of an atom's ability to attract shared electrons toward itself; increases across a period and decreases down a group.
nonpolar covalent bond
A bond in which electrons are shared equally between atoms of identical or very similar electronegativity.
polar covalent bond
A bond in which electrons are shared unequally because one atom is more electronegative, creating partial charges on the bonded atoms.
partial charge
A fractional positive (δ+) or negative (δ−) charge on an atom in a polar bond, caused by unequal sharing of electrons.
dipole moment
A measure of molecular polarity; present when polar bonds are arranged asymmetrically and do not cancel.
polar molecule
A molecule with a net dipole moment; has an asymmetric arrangement of polar bonds so one side is partially negative and the other is partially positive.
nonpolar molecule
A molecule with no net dipole moment; either has no polar bonds or has polar bonds arranged symmetrically so they cancel.
intermolecular forces
Attractions between separate molecules; weaker than intramolecular bonds but responsible for determining physical properties.
London dispersion forces
Weak intermolecular forces arising from temporary dipoles due to electron motion; present in all molecules; increase with molecular size.
dipole-dipole forces
Intermolecular attractions between polar molecules caused by the alignment of permanent dipoles.
hydrogen bond
A strong intermolecular attraction between a hydrogen atom bonded to F, O, or N and a lone pair on another F, O, or N in a nearby molecule.
hydrophilic
Attracted to water; describes polar or hydrogen-bonding substances or molecular regions.
hydrophobic
Does not interact favorably with water; describes nonpolar substances or molecular regions.
polarizability
The ease with which an electron cloud can be distorted to create a temporary dipole; increases with molecular size and number of electrons.

Common Misconceptions

Misconception 1
"A molecule with polar bonds is always polar."

Carbon dioxide has very polar C=O bonds but is linear — the two bond polarities point in opposite directions and cancel perfectly. CCl₄ has four polar C–Cl bonds, but tetrahedral symmetry causes them to cancel. Both CO₂ and CCl₄ are nonpolar. You must consider both bond polarity AND geometry to determine molecular polarity.

Misconception 2
"Lone pairs are just extra electrons that don't matter for shape."

Lone pairs have a major effect on molecular geometry because they repel bonding pairs more strongly than bonding pairs repel each other. Without counting lone pairs, you cannot correctly predict the shape of water (bent, not linear) or ammonia (trigonal pyramidal, not trigonal planar). Lone pairs must be included in the electron group count even though they do not appear in the final shape.

Misconception 3
"Intermolecular forces are just weaker versions of covalent bonds."

Intermolecular forces and covalent bonds are fundamentally different in kind, not just strength. Covalent bonds involve orbital overlap and electron sharing. Intermolecular forces arise from electrostatic interactions between partial charges and temporary dipoles. When water boils, no covalent bonds break — only intermolecular forces are overcome.

Misconception 4
"Hydrogen bonds form between any hydrogen atom and any electronegative atom."

Hydrogen bonds require the hydrogen to be bonded directly to F, O, or N — not just any electronegative atom. A C–H bond does not form true hydrogen bonds even when carbon is bonded to electronegative elements. The key is that F, O, and N are electronegative enough to make the hydrogen nearly bare, which is required for hydrogen bond formation.

Misconception 5
"All tetrahedral molecules have bond angles of exactly 109.5°."

The 109.5° angle is the ideal for a perfect tetrahedron with four identical bonding groups and no lone pairs. When lone pairs are present — as in ammonia (~107°) or water (~104.5°) — they compress the bond angles below 109.5°. Only molecules like CH₄ and CCl₄ achieve the ideal 109.5°.

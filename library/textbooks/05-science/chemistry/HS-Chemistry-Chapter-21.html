Chapter 21 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Iron rusts slowly over years. Wood burns in seconds. Hydrogen and oxygen can exist together peacefully in a balloon — until a spark ignites them in an instant. Bread dough rises over an hour; explosives detonate in a millisecond. Milk goes sour overnight if left on the counter, but stays fresh for weeks when refrigerated.

All of these are chemical reactions, yet they happen at wildly different speeds. Why? What controls how fast a reaction goes? And can we do anything to speed a reaction up or slow it down when we need to? These are the questions that chemical kinetics answers. Kinetics is the branch of chemistry that studies reaction rates — how quickly reactants are converted into products and what factors control that speed.

This chapter builds on what you already know about chemical reactions, energy, and thermochemistry. You have seen what happens when reactions occur. Now you will learn how fast they happen — and why.

Opening Questions

Why does refrigeration keep food fresh longer? Why does a campfire need a spark to start? Why do some medicines have to be stored in a cool, dark place? By the end of this chapter, you will be able to answer all of these questions using the same set of chemical principles — collision theory, activation energy, and the factors that control how often and how effectively particles interact.

Section 21.1

What Reaction Rate Means

A reaction rate describes how quickly a chemical reaction proceeds — specifically, how fast the concentration of reactants decreases or the concentration of products increases over time. As a reaction proceeds, reactant particles are consumed and product particles form. The reaction rate describes how fast that change happens.

Chemists express reaction rate in terms of concentration change per unit of time. The typical unit is moles per liter per second (mol/L·s), though other time units — minutes, hours, days — are used depending on the speed of the reaction being studied.

Reaction Rate

Reaction rate = change in concentration ÷ change in time

Units: mol/L·s (or mol/L·min, mol/L·hr, depending on the reaction's speed)

A fast reaction has a high rate: large concentration changes in a short time. A slow reaction has a low rate: small concentration changes over a long period.

Rate Changes Over Time

The rate of most reactions is not constant. At the beginning, when reactant concentrations are high, particles collide frequently and the rate is fastest. As reactants are consumed, collisions become less frequent and the rate slows. Near the end, when reactants are nearly gone, the rate approaches zero.

When scientists report a reaction rate, they typically specify the rate at a particular point in time — most often the initial rate, measured at the very start of the reaction when concentrations are known precisely.

Factors That Affect Rate

Five major factors influence how fast a reaction proceeds: the nature of the reactants, the concentration of reactants, temperature, surface area (for solids), and the presence of a catalyst. Each will be explored in the sections that follow.

Section 21.2

Collision Theory

At the particle level, a chemical reaction can only occur when reactant particles meet. Atoms, molecules, or ions cannot react with each other from a distance; they must come into direct contact. This leads to collision theory, which explains reaction rates in terms of how often particles collide and what happens when they do.

Three Conditions for a Reaction (Collision Theory)

1. Reactant particles must collide with each other. No collision means no reaction.

2. The collision must have sufficient energy to break the bonds that need to be broken.

3. The colliding particles must have correct orientation — the reactive parts must face each other in a way that allows new bonds to form.

Collision Frequency

The more frequently particles collide, the more chances there are for a successful reaction. Collision frequency depends primarily on how many particles are present in a given volume and how fast they are moving. A crowded container full of fast-moving particles will have far more collisions per second than a dilute container of slow-moving particles. This is why concentration and temperature both affect reaction rate — they directly influence how often particles collide.

Effective Collisions

A collision that meets all three conditions — enough energy and correct orientation — is called an effective collision. Only effective collisions lead to products. The reaction rate therefore depends not just on the total number of collisions but on the fraction of those collisions that are effective.

For larger or more complex molecules, orientation becomes increasingly important and explains why some reactions with high concentrations and temperatures still proceed more slowly than expected. Simple atoms are less sensitive to orientation; complex molecules can be very sensitive.

The Core Idea

To speed up a reaction, you need more effective collisions per second. Every factor that affects reaction rate — concentration, temperature, surface area, catalysts — can be understood through its effect on collision frequency or collision effectiveness.

Section 21.3

Activation Energy

Collision theory tells us that particles must collide with enough energy for a reaction to occur. Activation energy (Ea) is the minimum amount of energy that colliding particles must possess for a reaction to occur. It represents an energy barrier that must be overcome before reactants can be converted into products.

Think of activation energy as a hill that the reactants must climb before they can roll down to form products. Even if the products are at a lower energy level than the reactants — even if the reaction is exothermic — the reactants still need an initial push to get over the hill.

The Transition State

Activation energy exists because making new bonds requires first breaking or partially breaking old ones. When two reactant particles approach each other, they begin to interact — electrons repel, old bonds strain and stretch, and new bonds begin to form. This moment, when old bonds are breaking and new bonds are forming, is called the transition state or activated complex. It is the highest-energy point of the process and is not a stable substance — it exists only for an instant.

If the particles have enough energy to reach the transition state, the reaction proceeds forward. If not, the particles bounce apart unchanged.

Important Distinction

Do not confuse activation energy with enthalpy change. A reaction can be strongly exothermic (large negative ΔH) and still have a high activation energy. These are independent quantities. The combustion of methane is strongly exothermic but requires a spark — high Ea — to get started.

Section 21.4

Concentration and Reaction Rate

When reactant concentration increases, there are more particles packed into the same space. By collision theory, more particles in a given volume means more frequent collisions. More frequent collisions means more chances for effective collisions. More effective collisions means a faster reaction rate.

The relationship is straightforward: as the concentration of reactants increases, the reaction rate increases. As reactants are consumed and their concentrations drop, the rate decreases.

Concentration and Rate

Higher concentration → more particles per unit volume → more frequent collisions → faster reaction rate.

Lower concentration → fewer particles per unit volume → less frequent collisions → slower reaction rate.

Because reactants are consumed during a reaction, concentration drops over time — and so does the rate. This is why most reactions start fast and slow down.

Concentration and Gases

For reactions involving gases, concentration is linked to pressure. When the pressure of a gas is increased, its particles are pushed closer together — the effective concentration increases. This is why increasing the pressure of a gaseous reactant tends to speed up reactions that involve gases, just as increasing the concentration of a dissolved reactant speeds up reactions in solution.

Reading Concentration–Time Graphs

A graph of reactant concentration versus time starts with a steep slope (fast rate at high concentration) and gradually flattens as concentration falls. A nearly flat late slope means the reaction has slowed considerably. The slope of this curve at any point is the reaction rate at that moment.

Section 21.5

Temperature and Reaction Rate

Temperature is one of the most powerful factors controlling reaction rate. A modest increase in temperature can dramatically speed up a chemical reaction.

Temperature is a measure of the average kinetic energy of particles in a sample. When temperature increases, particles move faster on average. Faster particles collide more frequently. And crucially, a greater fraction of those particles have enough kinetic energy to exceed the activation energy threshold. Both effects combine to produce a significant increase in reaction rate.

Two Effects of Temperature on Rate

1. More frequent collisions — faster-moving particles collide more often per unit time.

2. Greater fraction of effective collisions — more particles have energy exceeding Ea and can react when they collide. This second effect is usually the more important one.

The 10°C Approximation

A commonly cited rule of thumb is that for many reactions near room temperature, the reaction rate roughly doubles for every 10°C increase in temperature. This is an approximation and does not apply universally, but it illustrates how sensitive reaction rates can be to temperature.

Consider what this means practically: a reaction that takes 60 minutes at 20°C might take only 30 minutes at 30°C, and only 15 minutes at 40°C. The same reaction might take four hours at 10°C.

Section 21.6

Surface Area and Reaction Rate

For reactions that involve a solid reactant, a special factor comes into play: surface area. A chemical reaction between a solid and a liquid or gas can only occur where the two substances are in direct contact — at the surface of the solid. Particles deep inside the solid are shielded by outer layers and cannot participate until those layers are consumed.

If the solid is broken into smaller pieces, more surface area is exposed, and the reaction can proceed faster.

Surface Area and Rate

For reactions involving solid reactants: smaller pieces → greater surface area → more particles exposed to collision → faster reaction rate.

Total volume of reactant is unchanged. Only the surface available for contact is increased.

Industrial Application

Chemical engineers design solid catalysts in porous or granular forms specifically to maximize surface area. The more surface area the catalyst presents, the faster the reaction can proceed — allowing smaller, more efficient reactors.

Section 21.7

Catalysts

A catalyst is a substance that increases the rate of a chemical reaction without being permanently consumed in the reaction. After the reaction is complete, the catalyst can be recovered in the same chemical form as it started. Catalysts increase reaction rate by providing an alternative reaction pathway with a lower activation energy.

What a Catalyst Does — and Does Not — Change

Changes: The activation energy (Ea) — lowers the energy barrier through an alternative pathway.

Does not change: The energy of the reactants. The energy of the products. The overall enthalpy change (ΔH). Whether the reaction is exothermic or endothermic.

Result: A larger fraction of collisions has enough energy to be effective, so the reaction proceeds faster at the same temperature and concentration.

Mountain Tunnel Analogy

The uncatalyzed route goes over a high mountain pass — possible, but demanding. A catalyst is like a tunnel cut through the mountain: same starting point, same destination, but an easier path that far more travelers can use.

Enzymes: Biological Catalysts

Living organisms depend entirely on biological catalysts called enzymes to control the thousands of chemical reactions that keep cells alive. Enzymes are large protein molecules with precisely shaped active sites — regions where specific reactant molecules (substrates) bind and react. Enzymes can lower activation energies dramatically and accelerate reactions by factors of millions or billions compared to uncatalyzed rates.

Enzymes are highly specific — each enzyme typically catalyzes only one reaction or a small family of related reactions. This specificity comes from the shape of the active site, which fits only certain substrate molecules in the way a lock fits a specific key. Without enzymes, most biochemical reactions would proceed far too slowly to sustain life at body temperature.

Inhibitors: The Opposite of Catalysts

A substance that decreases reaction rate by interfering with the catalytic process is called an inhibitor. Inhibitors work by blocking active sites, deactivating catalysts, or removing necessary intermediate species. They are important in medicine (many drugs inhibit specific enzymes), in food preservation (antioxidants inhibit oxidation reactions), and in industrial processes (corrosion inhibitors slow metal oxidation).

Catalyst vs. Inhibitor

A catalyst increases rate by lowering Ea. An inhibitor decreases rate — it does not simply represent the absence of a catalyst. An inhibitor actively interferes with collisions or with the catalytic process, reducing rate below the uncatalyzed baseline.

Section 21.8

Energy Diagrams and Reaction Pathways

Energy diagrams (also called potential energy diagrams or reaction coordinate diagrams) visually summarize the energy story of a chemical reaction. Reading them carefully is an essential skill for understanding both thermochemistry (Chapter 20) and kinetics (this chapter).

Reading a Complete Energy Diagram

FeatureHow to Find ItWhat It Tells You
Activation energy (Ea)Height from reactant energy level to the peakEnergy barrier; controls rate — taller peak = slower reaction
Enthalpy change (ΔH)Height difference between reactant and product energy levelsExothermic (products lower) or endothermic (products higher); independent of Ea
Catalyzed pathwayA second, lower peak on the same diagramSame reactant and product levels; lower barrier = faster rate
Multistep reactionMultiple peaks on the curveEach peak = one step; tallest peak = rate-determining step (slowest)
Ea and ΔH Are Independent

A strongly exothermic reaction can have a very high activation energy (example: combustion of hydrogen — needs a spark). An endothermic reaction can have a low activation energy. Never assume that exothermic means fast, or that endothermic means slow.

The Rate-Determining Step

Many real reactions proceed through a sequence of steps, each with its own transition state and activation energy. For a multistep reaction, the energy diagram shows multiple peaks. The rate of the overall reaction is determined by the slowest step — the one with the highest activation energy barrier. Chemists call this the rate-determining step: the bottleneck through which all reactants must pass. The tallest peak controls how fast the overall reaction proceeds.

Section 21.9

Reaction Rates in Living and Industrial Systems

Reaction rates are not just a laboratory concept. Every chemical process that matters to human life — in the body, in industry, in agriculture, in the environment — is governed by kinetics. Understanding how to control reaction rates is one of the most powerful tools chemistry offers to civilization.

Living Systems

The human body operates under strict temperature and pH conditions. Body temperature is maintained near 37°C — a temperature at which the body's enzyme-catalyzed reactions proceed at appropriate speeds. If body temperature drops too low (hypothermia), metabolic reactions slow dangerously. If temperature rises too high (hyperthermia), enzymes can denature — lose their shape and catalytic ability — disrupting metabolism.

Digestion is a kinetics story: enzymes in saliva (amylase), the stomach (pepsin), and the small intestine break down carbohydrates, proteins, and fats at rates controlled by enzyme concentration, pH, and temperature. Drug design frequently targets reaction rates — many antibiotics work by inhibiting bacterial enzymes; many cancer treatments target enzymes involved in cell division.

Food and Agriculture

Food preservation methods are almost entirely based on controlling reaction rates. Refrigeration slows all chemical reactions, including those carried out by microorganisms and enzymes responsible for spoilage. Freezing slows reactions further. Drying removes water, needed as a solvent for many spoilage reactions. Antioxidants — such as vitamins C and E — inhibit oxidation reactions that cause fats to turn rancid and fruits to brown.

In agriculture, the rate of nitrification in soil (microbially catalyzed conversion of ammonia to nitrate) affects how available nitrogen is to plants. Pesticide breakdown rates determine how long chemicals persist in soil and water.

Environmental Chemistry

Atmospheric chemistry is largely a kinetics problem. The ozone layer is maintained by reactions between ozone molecules and ultraviolet radiation. Ozone-depleting substances such as chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs) act as catalysts for ozone destruction reactions, dramatically increasing the rate at which ozone is broken down. Understanding these rate relationships was critical to diagnosing the ozone hole and to international agreements to phase out CFCs.

The persistence of pollutants in the environment depends on their chemical stability — a kinetics question. A pollutant that breaks down quickly has a very different environmental impact than one that persists for decades. Climate chemistry also involves kinetics: the rate at which carbon dioxide is absorbed by the oceans, the rate of methane oxidation in the atmosphere, and the rates of greenhouse gas reactions all affect how the climate system responds to emissions.

Key Terms Review

activation energy (Ea)
The minimum energy that colliding particles must possess for a reaction to occur; the height of the energy barrier on an energy diagram.
activated complex
The high-energy, unstable arrangement of atoms at the peak of the energy diagram; also called the transition state. Old bonds are breaking and new bonds are forming.
catalyst
A substance that increases the reaction rate by providing an alternative pathway with lower activation energy; not permanently consumed in the reaction.
collision theory
The model that explains chemical reactions in terms of particle collisions; states that reactions require collisions with sufficient energy and correct orientation.
effective collision
A collision between reactant particles that has sufficient energy and proper orientation to result in a chemical reaction.
enzyme
A biological catalyst made of protein; controls reaction rates in living cells by dramatically lowering activation energies for specific biochemical reactions.
heterogeneous catalyst
A catalyst that is in a different phase from the reactants; for example, a solid catalyst used with liquid or gaseous reactants.
homogeneous catalyst
A catalyst that is in the same phase as the reactants; mixes directly with the reactant molecules.
inhibitor
A substance that decreases the rate of a chemical reaction by interfering with the catalytic process or blocking reactant access to active sites.
kinetics
The branch of chemistry that studies reaction rates and the factors that affect them.
rate-determining step
In a multistep reaction, the slowest step — the one with the highest activation energy barrier — which controls the overall reaction rate.
reaction rate
The speed at which a chemical reaction proceeds, expressed as the change in concentration of a reactant or product per unit of time (mol/L·s).
surface area
The total area of a solid exposed and available for contact with other reactants; increasing surface area increases reaction rate for reactions involving solids.
transition state
See activated complex; the highest-energy point in a reaction pathway, representing the moment of maximum atomic rearrangement.

Looking Ahead
Chapter 22: Chemical Equilibrium

Chapter 21 has shown you that reactions proceed at different rates — and that those rates can be controlled. But many reactions do not simply run to completion. Instead, they reach a state where the forward reaction and the reverse reaction proceed at the same rate, producing what appears to be a standstill — a condition chemists call chemical equilibrium.

Chapter 22 will introduce you to reversible reactions and dynamic equilibrium. You will learn that equilibrium is not a static condition but a dynamic balance. You will use Le Châtelier's principle to predict how a system at equilibrium responds to changes in concentration, pressure, and temperature. And you will see how equilibrium is exploited in industrial chemistry — including the Haber process you encountered in this chapter — to maximize the production of desired products. Kinetics tells you how fast a reaction goes. Equilibrium tells you how far it goes. Together, they explain the behavior of virtually every chemical system that matters.

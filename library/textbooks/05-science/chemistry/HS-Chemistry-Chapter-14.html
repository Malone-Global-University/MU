Chapter 14 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

In Chapter 13, you learned how to read and write chemical equations. You learned that equations describe chemical change using symbols, formulas, coefficients, and subscripts, and you learned how to balance equations so that atoms are conserved on both sides. You learned to recognize evidence that a chemical reaction has occurred — gas production, color changes, temperature changes, light, and the formation of a solid in solution.

Now a new question arises: are all chemical reactions random and unpredictable, or do they follow patterns? The answer, discovered through centuries of careful observation, is that chemical reactions do follow patterns. When you look at thousands of different reactions, you begin to see the same general shapes appearing again and again. Certain types of starting materials combine in certain ways to produce certain types of products. Recognizing these patterns gives chemists a powerful tool: the ability to make educated predictions about what will happen before a reaction even occurs.

In this chapter, you will learn to recognize the major categories of chemical reactions. By the end of this chapter, you should be able to look at a chemical equation and say not just "this is a reaction," but "this is a synthesis reaction" or "this is a combustion reaction" — and to understand what that classification tells you about the substances involved and the changes taking place.

Opening Question

When magnesium ribbon burns in air, it produces a blinding white light and leaves behind a white powdery solid. When baking soda meets vinegar, it fizzes vigorously. When iron slowly rusts, it gains mass. These three events look and feel completely different — yet all three are chemical reactions. What, if anything, do they have in common? By the end of this chapter, you will be able to classify each reaction, name its type, and explain why each one behaves the way it does.

Section 14.1

Why Reaction Types Matter

Chemistry involves an almost limitless number of possible reactions. Millions of known chemical reactions have been documented, and chemists discover new ones every year. If every reaction were completely unique and unpredictable, chemistry would be overwhelmingly complex. Every new experiment would require starting from scratch. Fortunately, this is not how chemistry works.

Chemists have noticed that most reactions fall into a small number of recognizable categories. These categories are defined by the pattern of how reactants are rearranged to form products. Learning these patterns lets you do something very useful: make predictions.

Pattern Recognition — An Analogy

When you learn that most English past tenses are formed by adding "-ed" to a verb, you gain the ability to form the past tense of words you have never seen before. Reaction types work the same way. Once you learn that a synthesis reaction follows the pattern A + B → AB, you can recognize that pattern whether you are looking at iron combining with sulfur or sodium combining with chlorine.

Reaction types are a useful organizing framework, but they have limits. Some reactions belong to more than one category. Some reactions that seem like they should occur actually do not, because energy or other factors prevent them. Throughout this chapter, keep two things in mind: reaction types are tools for thinking, not rigid rules, and predicting products always requires checking your work against balanced equations and chemical identity.

Section 14.2

Synthesis Reactions

A synthesis reaction is one in which two or more simpler substances combine to form a single more complex product. The word synthesis means "putting together," which describes the pattern perfectly. Because the reactants are simpler than the product, synthesis reactions are sometimes described as "building" reactions.

General Pattern — Synthesis

A + B → AB

Two or more reactants combine. A single, more complex product forms. The key identifying feature is multiple reactants producing one product.

Carbon dioxide + WaterCO₂ + H₂O → H₂CO₃Two compounds combining; produces carbonic acid in rain

Count the formulas on each side of the arrow. If you see two or more on the left and only one on the right, it is likely a synthesis reaction. Common forms include: element + element, element + compound, and compound + compound — all producing one product.

Section 14.3

Decomposition Reactions

A decomposition reaction is the opposite of a synthesis reaction. In a decomposition reaction, a single compound breaks down into two or more simpler substances. Because the reactant is more complex than the products, decomposition reactions are sometimes described as "breaking apart" reactions. Decomposition usually requires an input of energy — often supplied as heat, light, or electricity. Without an energy input, many compounds are stable and do not spontaneously break apart.

General Pattern — Decomposition

AB → A + B

One reactant, two or more products. The identifying feature is the opposite of synthesis: a single formula on the left producing multiple formulas on the right.

Examples of Decomposition Reactions

CompoundBalanced EquationEnergy Input
Water2 H₂O → 2 H₂ + O₂Electrical current (electrolysis)
Hydrogen peroxide2 H₂O₂ → 2 H₂O + O₂Light or catalyst (enzymes in blood)
Mercury(II) oxide2 HgO → 2 Hg + O₂Strong heat (Lavoisier's historic experiment)
Calcium carbonateCaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂High heat (calcination; lime production)
Ammonium nitrate2 NH₄NO₃ → 2 N₂ + O₂ + 4 H₂OStrong heat; regulated due to explosive hazard
Safety Note — Ammonium Nitrate

Ammonium nitrate is a common agricultural fertilizer that can decompose explosively when heated in large quantities or in the presence of contaminants. Its storage and handling are tightly regulated. Never heat ammonium nitrate in a laboratory setting without specific instructor authorization.

Section 14.4

Single-Replacement Reactions

A single-replacement reaction — also called a single-displacement reaction — is one in which one element displaces another element from a compound. The element that is already part of the compound is replaced, or "displaced," by the free element entering the reaction. One new compound forms and one element is set free.

General Pattern — Single-Replacement

A + BC → AC + B

Element A replaces element B in compound BC. The displaced element B is released as a free element. Whether the replacement occurs depends on the relative reactivity of A and B — check the activity series.

The Activity Series

Not all elements can replace all other elements. Whether a replacement reaction occurs depends on the relative reactivity of the elements involved. Chemists use a tool called the activity series to organize elements by their reactivity. A more reactive element can replace a less reactive element from a compound; a less reactive element cannot replace a more reactive one.

Hydrogen in the Activity Series

Hydrogen is included because many metals react with acids (which contain H⁺ ions) to release H₂ gas. Metals above hydrogen in the activity series react with dilute acids; metals below hydrogen do not. This makes it easy to predict whether a metal will dissolve in acid.

Section 14.5

Double-Replacement Reactions

A double-replacement reaction — also called a double-displacement or metathesis reaction — is one in which two compounds exchange partners to produce two new compounds. The "partners" swap. Double-replacement reactions usually occur in aqueous solution, where the ions of the dissolved compounds are free to move and encounter each other.

General Pattern — Double-Replacement

AB + CD → AD + CB

The positive part of one compound (A) pairs with the negative part of the other compound (D), and vice versa. A reaction proceeds when at least one product is: (1) an insoluble precipitate, (2) a gas that escapes, or (3) a weakly ionized substance such as water.

Examples of Double-Replacement Reactions

Example 14.5.1
Formation of a precipitate — barium chloride and sodium sulfate
  • ReactionTwo aqueous solutions are mixed: BaCl₂(aq) and Na₂SO₄(aq).
  • ExchangeBa²⁺ pairs with SO₄²⁻ → BaSO₄. Na⁺ pairs with Cl⁻ → NaCl.
  • CheckBaSO₄ is insoluble (precipitate forms). NaCl is soluble (stays in solution as spectator ions).
  • EquationBaCl₂(aq) + Na₂SO₄(aq) → BaSO₄(s) + 2 NaCl(aq)
A white precipitate of barium sulfate forms immediately. Sodium and chloride ions remain dissolved.
Example 14.5.2
Formation of water — hydrochloric acid and sodium hydroxide
  • ReactionHCl(aq) reacts with NaOH(aq).
  • ExchangeH⁺ pairs with OH⁻ → H₂O. Na⁺ pairs with Cl⁻ → NaCl.
  • Driving ForceWater forms from H⁺ and OH⁻ — a weak electrolyte that drives the reaction to completion. This is also a neutralization reaction.
  • EquationHCl(aq) + NaOH(aq) → NaCl(aq) + H₂O(l)
Sodium chloride remains dissolved. Water forms, neutralizing both the acid and the base.
Example 14.5.3
Formation of a gas — sodium carbonate and hydrochloric acid
  • ReactionNa₂CO₃(aq) reacts with HCl(aq).
  • ExchangeNa⁺ pairs with Cl⁻ → NaCl. H⁺ pairs with CO₃²⁻ → H₂CO₃ (unstable).
  • Secondary StepCarbonic acid immediately decomposes: H₂CO₃ → H₂O + CO₂↑. The escaping gas drives the reaction forward.
  • EquationNa₂CO₃(aq) + 2 HCl(aq) → 2 NaCl(aq) + H₂O(l) + CO₂(g)
Carbon dioxide gas bubbles out of solution. This is why baking soda fizzes when it meets vinegar.
When No Reaction Occurs

If two soluble ionic compounds are mixed and all possible products are also soluble, there is no driving force. No precipitate forms, no gas escapes, no water is produced, and no observable change occurs. In this case, write: No reaction (NR).

Section 14.6

Combustion Reactions

A combustion reaction is one in which a substance reacts rapidly with oxygen gas, releasing energy in the form of heat and usually light. Combustion is commonly recognized as burning. For the complete combustion of any hydrocarbon — a compound made only of carbon and hydrogen — the products are always carbon dioxide (CO₂) and water (H₂O). Combustion is always exothermic.

General Pattern — Combustion (Hydrocarbon)

Hydrocarbon + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O + Energy

All carbon atoms in the fuel → CO₂. All hydrogen atoms → H₂O. Oxygen comes entirely from O₂ in air. Combustion requires a fuel and oxygen as the two reactants.

Complete Combustion of Common Fuels

FuelFormulaBalanced EquationCommon Use
MethaneCH₄CH₄ + 2 O₂ → CO₂ + 2 H₂ONatural gas; heating
PropaneC₃H₈C₃H₈ + 5 O₂ → 3 CO₂ + 4 H₂OGrills; portable stoves
OctaneC₈H₁₈2 C₈H₁₈ + 25 O₂ → 16 CO₂ + 18 H₂OGasoline component
EthanolC₂H₅OHC₂H₅OH + 3 O₂ → 2 CO₂ + 3 H₂OFuel; antiseptic

Complete Versus Incomplete Combustion

Complete combustion occurs when there is ample oxygen available. It releases maximum energy and produces only CO₂ and H₂O. Incomplete combustion occurs when there is insufficient oxygen. Instead of producing only CO₂ and H₂O, incomplete combustion produces carbon monoxide (CO), soot (carbon particles), or both.

Carbon Monoxide — A Silent Hazard

Carbon monoxide is a colorless, odorless gas produced by incomplete combustion. It binds to hemoglobin in blood far more strongly than oxygen does, preventing cells from receiving oxygen. Carbon monoxide poisoning is a leading cause of indoor death from improperly vented stoves, heaters, and generators. Every home with combustion appliances should have a carbon monoxide detector.

Non-Hydrocarbon Combustion

Combustion is not limited to carbon-hydrogen compounds. Other substances combust as well. Hydrogen burns to produce only water: 2 H₂ + O₂ → 2 H₂O. Magnesium burns to produce magnesium oxide: 2 Mg + O₂ → 2 MgO — also a synthesis reaction. Sulfur burns to produce sulfur dioxide: S + O₂ → SO₂, which contributes to acid rain.

Example 14.6.1
Balancing a combustion equation — ethanol
  • FuelEthanol: C₂H₅OH. Reactants: C₂H₅OH + O₂. Products (complete combustion): CO₂ + H₂O.
  • Carbon2 C atoms → 2 CO₂. Place coefficient 2 before CO₂.
  • Hydrogen6 H atoms → 3 H₂O. Place coefficient 3 before H₂O.
  • OxygenRight side: 2(2) + 3(1) = 7 oxygen atoms. Left side has 1 from ethanol. Need 6 more from O₂ → 3 O₂.
  • BalancedC₂H₅OH + 3 O₂ → 2 CO₂ + 3 H₂O

Section 14.7

Precipitation Reactions

A precipitation reaction is a specific type of double-replacement reaction identified by the formation of an insoluble solid — a precipitate — when two solutions are mixed. Precipitation reactions appear frequently in laboratory work, environmental chemistry, water treatment, and industry. Predicting when precipitation occurs requires knowing solubility rules.

A substance is soluble if it dissolves readily in water to produce an aqueous solution of ions. A substance is insoluble if it does not dissolve significantly in water. When solutions of two soluble ionic compounds are mixed, a precipitation reaction occurs only if the ions can combine to form an insoluble product.

General Solubility Guidelines

Nitrate (NO₃⁻)Nearly all solubleNone

Using State Symbols

In precipitation equations, (aq) indicates a dissolved ion or compound, and (s) indicates an insoluble solid precipitate. Including state symbols makes it immediately clear which product is the precipitate and which ions remain in solution as spectator ions.

Precipitation in the Environment and Industry

Precipitation reactions occur naturally throughout Earth's water systems. Many minerals form through the precipitation of dissolved ions as water chemistry changes. The "hardness" of water — dissolved calcium and magnesium ions — leads to scale deposits (CaCO₃ and Mg(OH)₂) inside pipes and water heaters. In water treatment, precipitation reactions are deliberately used to remove dissolved heavy metals, phosphates, and other contaminants. Kidney stones often involve the precipitation of calcium oxalate or calcium phosphate from body fluids.

Section 14.8

Acid-Base Reactions

An acid-base reaction — also called a neutralization reaction — is a specific type of double-replacement reaction in which an acid and a base react to produce a salt and water. This result is so characteristic and so important that acid-base reactions have their own name. The hydrogen ions from the acid (H⁺) combine with the hydroxide ions from the base (OH⁻) to form water. The remaining ions pair to form the salt.

General Pattern — Acid-Base (Neutralization)

Acid + Base → Salt + Water

Using Arrhenius definitions: an acid produces H⁺ ions in water; a base produces OH⁻ ions in water. The H⁺ and OH⁻ combine to form H₂O. The remaining ions form the salt. Chapter 23 will examine acids and bases in much greater depth.

Examples of Acid-Base Reactions

AcidBaseBalanced EquationSalt Produced
HClNaOHHCl(aq) + NaOH(aq) → NaCl(aq) + H₂O(l)NaCl (table salt)
H₂SO₄KOHH₂SO₄(aq) + 2 KOH(aq) → K₂SO₄(aq) + 2 H₂O(l)K₂SO₄ (potassium sulfate)
HNO₃Ca(OH)₂2 HNO₃(aq) + Ca(OH)₂(aq) → Ca(NO₃)₂(aq) + 2 H₂O(l)Ca(NO₃)₂ (calcium nitrate)
H₃PO₄NaOHH₃PO₄(aq) + 3 NaOH(aq) → Na₃PO₄(aq) + 3 H₂O(l)Na₃PO₄ (sodium phosphate)
Identifying the Salt

In a neutralization reaction, the salt is always formed from the positive ion of the base and the negative ion of the acid. In HCl + NaOH: the positive ion of the base is Na⁺, the negative ion of the acid is Cl⁻, so the salt is NaCl. Apply this pairing rule to any acid-base pair to identify the salt product.

Acid-base reactions occur constantly in daily life. Antacid tablets contain bases — often calcium carbonate or magnesium hydroxide — that neutralize stomach acid (hydrochloric acid) to relieve acid indigestion. Baking involves acids and bases: baking soda (a base) reacts with acidic ingredients to produce CO₂ gas, which causes baked goods to rise. In agriculture, liming of soil uses calcium compounds to neutralize soil acidity and improve conditions for crops. In lakes affected by acid rain, naturally occurring carbonate minerals help neutralize acidity and protect aquatic life.

Section 14.9

Oxidation-Reduction Reactions

An oxidation-reduction reaction — also called a redox reaction — is one in which electrons are transferred from one substance to another. Unlike the other reaction types in this chapter, which focus on rearranging atoms and ions, redox reactions are defined by the movement of electrons. This is an introduction to the category; Chapter 24 will develop oxidation numbers, half-reactions, electrochemical cells, and corrosion in full detail.

Definitions — OIL RIG

Oxidation is the loss of electrons. The substance that loses electrons is oxidized.

Reduction is the gain of electrons. The substance that gains electrons is reduced.

OIL RIG — Oxidation Is Loss · Reduction Is Gain

Oxidation and reduction always occur together. If one substance loses electrons, another must gain them. Electrons are transferred, never created or destroyed.

Oxidizing Agents and Reducing Agents

TermWhat It DoesWhat Happens to It
Oxidizing agentCauses oxidation of the other substance by taking its electronsIs itself reduced (gains electrons)
Reducing agentCauses reduction of the other substance by giving up its electronsIs itself oxidized (loses electrons)
Memory Aid — Agents Do the Opposite

The oxidizing agent gets reduced. The reducing agent gets oxidized. This seems backwards, but it makes sense: the agent acts on the other substance, so it undergoes the complementary process. Think of it this way: the oxidizing agent is "the taker" — it takes electrons and is therefore reduced. The reducing agent is "the giver" — it gives electrons and is therefore oxidized.

Examples of Redox Reactions

Example 14.9.1
Zinc and hydrochloric acid — a single-replacement redox reaction
  • EquationZn + 2 HCl → ZnCl₂ + H₂
  • OxidationZinc (Zn⁰) loses 2 electrons → Zn²⁺. Zinc is oxidized. Zinc is the reducing agent.
  • ReductionEach H⁺ gains 1 electron → H⁰ (in H₂). Hydrogen ions are reduced. HCl is the oxidizing agent.
All single-replacement reactions are redox reactions. The free element loses or gains electrons as it enters or leaves the compound.
Example 14.9.2
Rusting of iron
  • Equation4 Fe + 3 O₂ → 2 Fe₂O₃
  • OxidationIron (Fe⁰) loses 3 electrons → Fe³⁺. Iron is oxidized. Iron is the reducing agent.
  • ReductionEach oxygen atom gains 2 electrons → O²⁻. Oxygen is reduced. O₂ is the oxidizing agent.
Rusting is a slow redox reaction. Iron is oxidized by oxygen, forming iron(III) oxide — the familiar brownish-red corrosion of iron and steel.

Most synthesis reactions involving free elements, all combustion reactions, and all single-replacement reactions are redox reactions. Double-replacement reactions that simply exchange ions between compounds generally are not redox reactions — the elements do not change their charge states, they only switch partners. Redox chemistry is central to batteries, fuel cells, cellular respiration, photosynthesis, electroplating, and corrosion.

Section 14.10

Predicting Products Carefully

One of the most valuable applications of understanding reaction types is the ability to predict what will happen when substances are mixed. This is not a perfect science, but reaction types give you a framework for making educated predictions. The approach differs slightly for each reaction type.

Reaction TypeKey Prediction Steps
SynthesisIdentify all reactants. Determine the compound they would form if combined. Check formula validity. Write one product.
DecompositionIdentify the single reactant. Consider simpler products (elements, oxides, CO₂, H₂O). Note energy input required.
Single-ReplacementIdentify the free element and the compound. Check activity series. If incoming element is more reactive, write the new compound and displaced element. If not, write NR.
Double-ReplacementIdentify both compounds. Swap partners. Check solubility of each product. If a precipitate, gas, or water forms, proceed. If all products are soluble, write NR.
CombustionIdentify the fuel. For hydrocarbons: products are CO₂ and H₂O. Balance C first, then H, then O last.
Acid-BaseIdentify the acid and base. Write water as one product. Pair the positive ion of the base with the negative ion of the acid to get the salt. Balance.

After Predicting: Always Check

After predicting products, check: (1) Are all formulas chemically correct — check charges for ionic compounds. (2) Is the equation balanced — count atoms on both sides. (3) Are states of matter reasonable — precipitates should be labeled (s), escaping gases should be labeled (g). (4) Is there a reason the reaction would actually occur — a driving force, activity series confirmation, energy input.

Example 14.10.1
Predicting products — double-replacement: iron(III) chloride and sodium hydroxide
  • IdentifyFeCl₃(aq): provides Fe³⁺ and Cl⁻. NaOH(aq): provides Na⁺ and OH⁻.
  • Reaction TypeTwo ionic compounds in aqueous solution → double-replacement.
  • New PairsFe³⁺ + OH⁻ → Fe(OH)₃. Na⁺ + Cl⁻ → NaCl.
  • SolubilityFe(OH)₃ is insoluble → precipitate (s). NaCl is soluble → stays in solution (aq).
  • BalancedFeCl₃(aq) + 3 NaOH(aq) → Fe(OH)₃(s) + 3 NaCl(aq)
  • VerifyLeft: 1 Fe, 3 Cl, 3 Na, 3 O, 3 H. Right: 1 Fe, 3 O, 3 H, 3 Na, 3 Cl. ✓
Iron(III) hydroxide precipitates as a rust-brown solid. This is a precipitation reaction (type of double-replacement).
Example 14.10.2
Predicting products — single-replacement decision: copper and silver nitrate
  • IdentifyCu (free element) + AgNO₃(aq) (compound providing Ag⁺ and NO₃⁻).
  • Reaction TypeFree element + ionic compound → single-replacement.
  • Activity SeriesCu is above Ag. Copper is more reactive than silver. Replacement WILL occur.
  • PredictCopper replaces silver. Cu²⁺ pairs with NO₃⁻ → Cu(NO₃)₂. Ag⁺ reduced to Ag metal.
  • BalancedCu + 2 AgNO₃ → Cu(NO₃)₂ + 2 Ag
  • VerifyLeft: 1 Cu, 2 Ag, 2 N, 6 O. Right: 1 Cu, 2 N, 6 O, 2 Ag. ✓
The reaction occurs. Silver metal deposits on the copper; the solution turns blue as Cu²⁺ ions form.
Example 14.10.3
Predicting products — complete combustion: propane
  • FuelC₃H₈. Other reactant: O₂. Products: CO₂ and H₂O (complete combustion).
  • Carbon3 C atoms → 3 CO₂.
  • Hydrogen8 H atoms → 4 H₂O.
  • OxygenRight side: 3(2) + 4(1) = 10 oxygen atoms. Need 5 O₂.
  • BalancedC₃H₈ + 5 O₂ → 3 CO₂ + 4 H₂O
  • VerifyLeft: 3 C, 8 H, 10 O. Right: 3 C, 8 H, 10 O. ✓
Balanced combustion equation: C₃H₈ + 5 O₂ → 3 CO₂ + 4 H₂O

Chapter 14 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 14.1Reaction types reveal patterns in how reactants become products. Classifying reactions allows chemists to make educated predictions. Types are tools for thinking, not rigid rules — some reactions belong to more than one category.
  • Sec 14.2Synthesis reactions follow A + B → AB. Two or more reactants combine into one product. Both elements and compounds can serve as reactants. The key feature is multiple reactants producing a single, more complex product.
  • Sec 14.3Decomposition reactions follow AB → A + B. One compound breaks into simpler products. They typically require energy input — heat, light, or electricity. Examples include electrolysis of water and the decomposition of hydrogen peroxide.
  • Sec 14.4Single-replacement reactions follow A + BC → AC + B. A free element displaces a less reactive element from a compound. The activity series must be checked: replacement only occurs when the incoming element is more reactive than the one being displaced.
  • Sec 14.5Double-replacement reactions follow AB + CD → AD + CB. Two compounds exchange partners. A reaction proceeds when a precipitate, a gas, or water forms. If all products are soluble, no reaction occurs.
  • Sec 14.6Combustion reactions involve a fuel reacting with O₂ to release heat and light. Complete combustion of hydrocarbons produces CO₂ and H₂O. Incomplete combustion produces CO and/or soot. All combustion reactions are exothermic and are also redox reactions.
  • Sec 14.7Precipitation reactions are a specific type of double-replacement in which an insoluble solid forms. Predicting whether a precipitate forms requires applying solubility rules. Spectator ions remain dissolved; the precipitate is labeled (s).
  • Sec 14.8Acid-base reactions (neutralization) produce a salt and water. The H⁺ from the acid and OH⁻ from the base combine to form water. The salt is identified by pairing the positive ion of the base with the negative ion of the acid.
  • Sec 14.9Redox reactions involve electron transfer: OIL RIG. The oxidizing agent gains electrons (is reduced). The reducing agent loses electrons (is oxidized). Combustion, single-replacement, and most reactions involving free elements are redox reactions.
  • Sec 14.10Predicting products requires identifying the reaction type, applying the appropriate pattern, checking driving forces and activity series, verifying formula correctness, and balancing. When conditions prevent a reaction, write NR.

Key Terms Review

synthesis reaction
A + B → AB. Two or more substances combine to form one more complex product.
decomposition reaction
AB → A + B. A single compound breaks down into two or more simpler products. Usually requires an energy input.
single-replacement
A + BC → AC + B. A free element displaces a less reactive element from a compound. Check the activity series first.
double-replacement
AB + CD → AD + CB. Two compounds exchange ions. Also called metathesis or double-displacement.
combustion reaction
Fuel + O₂ → oxides + energy. A substance reacts rapidly with oxygen, releasing heat and usually light.
complete combustion
A hydrocarbon burns in ample oxygen, producing only CO₂ and H₂O.
incomplete combustion
Combustion with insufficient oxygen, producing CO and/or soot (carbon particles) instead of only CO₂ and H₂O.
precipitation reaction
A double-replacement reaction in which an insoluble solid (precipitate) forms when two aqueous solutions are mixed.
precipitate
An insoluble solid that forms during a reaction in solution. Labeled (s) in chemical equations.
solubility
The ability of a substance to dissolve in water. Soluble compounds dissolve readily; insoluble compounds do not dissolve significantly.
neutralization
An acid-base reaction in which H⁺ and OH⁻ combine to form water, eliminating both the acidic and basic character of the reactants.
salt
The ionic compound produced in a neutralization reaction; composed of the positive ion of the base and the negative ion of the acid.
redox reaction
A reaction involving electron transfer from one substance to another. Oxidation and reduction always occur together.
oxidation
The loss of electrons by a substance in a redox reaction. OIL — Oxidation Is Loss.
reduction
The gain of electrons by a substance in a redox reaction. RIG — Reduction Is Gain.
oxidizing agent
The substance that gains electrons (is reduced) and causes the oxidation of another substance.
reducing agent
The substance that loses electrons (is oxidized) and causes the reduction of another substance.
activity series
A ranking of metals and hydrogen by relative reactivity. More reactive elements can displace less reactive ones from compounds.
spectator ions
Ions that are present in solution but do not participate in the actual chemical change; they appear on both sides of the complete ionic equation.
no reaction (NR)
Notation used when a predicted reaction does not actually occur under the given conditions — no driving force exists or the activity series prohibits replacement.

Common Misconceptions

Misconception 1
"Synthesis reactions can only involve two elements."
Correction
Synthesis reactions involve two or more reactants combining into one product. The reactants can be elements, compounds, or a mixture of both. For example, CO₂ + H₂O → H₂CO₃ is a synthesis reaction involving two compounds.
Misconception 2
"Any two metals will replace each other in single-replacement reactions."
Correction
Replacement only occurs when the incoming element is more reactive than the one being displaced. The activity series determines whether replacement occurs. Copper cannot replace zinc from a zinc compound because copper is less reactive than zinc.
Misconception 3
"Double-replacement reactions always occur when two ionic solutions are mixed."
Correction
A double-replacement reaction only produces an observable result when a precipitate, gas, or water forms. If all ions remain dissolved, there is no net reaction and no observable change occurs.
Misconception 4
"Combustion always produces carbon dioxide."
Correction
The products of combustion depend on what is burning. Hydrogen combustion produces only water. Sulfur combustion produces SO₂. Only the combustion of carbon-containing (organic) compounds produces carbon dioxide.
Misconception 5
"Oxidation means reacting with oxygen."
Correction
While reacting with oxygen is a common form of oxidation, the chemical definition of oxidation is the loss of electrons. A substance can be oxidized without directly involving oxygen gas, as in many single-replacement reactions where a metal loses electrons to dissolved ions.

Looking Ahead
Chapter 15: The Mole and Molar Mass

Chapter 14 gave you the ability to classify reactions, write balanced equations for many types of reactions, and predict what products will form. You can now look at a chemical equation and understand not just the symbols, but what kind of chemical event is being described. However, a balanced equation only tells you the ratio of particles involved. It does not yet tell you how many grams of each reactant you need or how many grams of product you will produce. Chapter 15 introduces the mole — the central counting unit of chemistry and the bridge that connects the invisible world of atoms and molecules to the measurable world of grams and liters. With the mole in hand, the quantitative work of stoichiometry in Chapter 16 becomes possible.

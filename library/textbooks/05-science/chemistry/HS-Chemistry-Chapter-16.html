Chapter 16 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Every recipe uses ratios. If you want to make two dozen cookies instead of one dozen, you double every ingredient. If you want to make only half a batch, you cut every ingredient in half. The relationships between ingredients are fixed by the recipe itself. Chemical reactions work the same way.

A balanced chemical equation is a recipe. It tells you exactly how much of each substance reacts and exactly how much of each product forms. The ratios between substances are fixed by the balanced equation. Stoichiometry is the branch of chemistry that reads those ratios and uses them to make quantitative predictions about chemical reactions.

The word stoichiometry comes from Greek words meaning "element" and "measure." Stoichiometry is, quite literally, the measurement of chemical substances in reactions. In this chapter, you will learn to predict how much product a reaction can form, determine which reactant runs out first, and measure how efficient a real reaction is compared to what theory predicts.

Opening Question

A pharmaceutical company synthesizes aspirin by reacting salicylic acid with acetic anhydride. Before running a batch, the chemists must calculate exactly how much of each starting material is needed, which reactant will run out first, and how much product they can expect. If they calculate wrong, they waste expensive materials or fall short of their target. By the end of this chapter, you will have every tool needed to answer these questions for any reaction.

Section 16.1

What Stoichiometry Studies

Chemists do not only ask what happens in a reaction. They ask how much. How many grams of iron are needed to produce a specific mass of iron oxide? How much carbon dioxide forms when a fuel burns? How much aspirin can be synthesized from a given quantity of salicylic acid? These questions are answered by stoichiometry.

Stoichiometry rests on one central idea: balanced chemical equations represent fixed ratios between particles, and those ratios allow chemists to calculate amounts of substances involved in reactions. When you balance a chemical equation, you are not just satisfying conservation of mass. You are establishing a set of mole ratios — the proportions in which reactants combine and products form.

The Four Calculation Types

Section 16.2

Balanced Equations as Ratios

Consider the synthesis of water:

2 H₂ + O₂ → 2 H₂O

The coefficients 2, 1, and 2 can be read at the particle level or the mole level. At the particle level: 2 molecules of hydrogen react with 1 molecule of oxygen to produce 2 molecules of water. At the mole level: 2 moles of hydrogen react with 1 mole of oxygen to produce 2 moles of water. Both readings are correct. The ratios are identical whether you are counting particles or counting in moles.

Reading Mole Ratios from Equations

Consider the synthesis of ammonia:

N₂ + 3 H₂ → 2 NH₃
SubstancesMole Ratio (as fraction)
N₂ to H₂1 mol N₂ / 3 mol H₂
H₂ to N₂3 mol H₂ / 1 mol N₂
N₂ to NH₃1 mol N₂ / 2 mol NH₃
NH₃ to N₂2 mol NH₃ / 1 mol N₂
H₂ to NH₃3 mol H₂ / 2 mol NH₃
NH₃ to H₂2 mol NH₃ / 3 mol H₂
Choosing the Correct Mole Ratio

Always place the unknown substance in the numerator and the given substance in the denominator. This ensures the given units cancel and the unknown units remain after multiplication.

Example 16.2.1
Using a mole ratio — N₂ + 3 H₂ → 2 NH₃
  • ProblemHow many moles of NH₃ form from 6 moles of H₂?
  • Given6 mol H₂  ·  Unknown: mol NH₃
  • Ratio2 mol NH₃ / 3 mol H₂ (unknown on top, given on bottom)
  • Solve6 mol H₂ × (2 mol NH₃ / 3 mol H₂) = 4 mol NH₃
Answer: 4 mol NH₃
Section 16.3

Mole Ratios

The mole ratio serves as a conversion factor between moles of different substances in a reaction — exactly as molar mass converts between moles and grams. When you multiply by a mole ratio, you are converting from one substance to another while preserving the proportional relationship dictated by the equation.

General Setup

mol given × (mol unknown / mol given) = mol unknown

Always write units explicitly and confirm they cancel before doing arithmetic.

Example 16.3.1
Moles of O₂ needed — CH₄ + 2 O₂ → CO₂ + 2 H₂O
  • ProblemHow many moles of O₂ are needed to react completely with 5.0 mol of CH₄?
  • Ratio2 mol O₂ / 1 mol CH₄
  • Solve5.0 mol CH₄ × (2 mol O₂ / 1 mol CH₄) = 10.0 mol O₂
Answer: 10.0 mol O₂
Example 16.3.2
Moles of H₂O produced — same equation
  • ProblemHow many moles of H₂O form from 5.0 mol of CH₄?
  • Ratio2 mol H₂O / 1 mol CH₄
  • Solve5.0 mol CH₄ × (2 mol H₂O / 1 mol CH₄) = 10.0 mol H₂O
Answer: 10.0 mol H₂O
Example 16.3.3
Moles of CH₄ needed to produce a given amount of CO₂
  • ProblemHow many moles of CH₄ are needed to produce 8.4 mol of CO₂?
  • Ratio1 mol CH₄ / 1 mol CO₂
  • Solve8.4 mol CO₂ × (1 mol CH₄ / 1 mol CO₂) = 8.4 mol CH₄
  • CheckThe 1:1 ratio between CH₄ and CO₂ in the equation confirms the answer should equal the given moles. ✓
Answer: 8.4 mol CH₄
Reasonableness Check

After every mole ratio calculation, ask whether the answer makes sense with the coefficients. If the equation shows a 2:1 ratio and you expect more product than reactant, confirm your number is larger, not smaller. Catching direction errors this way saves time on every problem.

Section 16.4

Mole-to-Mole Stoichiometry

Mole-to-mole stoichiometry is the most direct calculation type. You are given moles of one substance and asked to find moles of another. The only conversion factor needed is the mole ratio from the balanced equation. This is the conceptual core of all stoichiometry — every more complex calculation contains a mole-to-mole step at its center.

Mole-to-Mole Pathway mol given × mole ratio → mol unknown
Practice 16.4Mole-to-Mole Problems

Use the equation 4 Fe + 3 O₂ → 2 Fe₂O₃ for all problems.

  1. How many moles of O₂ are needed to react with 12.0 mol of Fe?
  2. How many moles of Fe₂O₃ are produced from 6.0 mol of Fe?
  3. How many moles of Fe are needed to produce 5.0 mol of Fe₂O₃?
  4. How many moles of O₂ are needed to produce 10.0 mol of Fe₂O₃?
Answers: 1. 9.0 mol O₂  ·  2. 3.0 mol Fe₂O₃  ·  3. 10.0 mol Fe  ·  4. 15.0 mol O₂
Section 16.5

Mole-to-Mass Stoichiometry

When you are given moles of one substance and asked for the mass in grams of another, you add one step to the pathway: convert moles of the unknown to grams using its molar mass.

Mole-to-Mass Pathway mol given × mole ratio → mol unknown × molar mass → g unknown
Example 16.5.1
Grams of Fe₂O₃ from moles of Fe — 4 Fe + 3 O₂ → 2 Fe₂O₃
  • ProblemHow many grams of Fe₂O₃ are produced from 2.0 mol of Fe?
  • Molar MassFe₂O₃: 2(55.85) + 3(16.00) = 111.70 + 48.00 = 159.70 g/mol
  • Setup 2.0 mol Fe × (2 mol Fe₂O₃ / 4 mol Fe) × (159.70 g Fe₂O₃ / 1 mol Fe₂O₃)
  • Calculate2.0 × 0.500 × 159.70 = 159.7 g → 160 g (2 sig figs)
Answer: 160 g Fe₂O₃
Example 16.5.2
Grams of O₂ needed to burn propane — C₃H₈ + 5 O₂ → 3 CO₂ + 4 H₂O
  • ProblemHow many grams of O₂ are required to burn 3.0 mol of C₃H₈?
  • Molar MassO₂: 2(16.00) = 32.00 g/mol
  • Setup3.0 mol C₃H₈ × (5 mol O₂ / 1 mol C₃H₈) × (32.00 g O₂ / 1 mol O₂)
  • Calculate3.0 × 5 × 32.00 = 480 g O₂
Answer: 480 g O₂
Significant Figures Reminder

Carry at least four significant figures in molar mass values from the periodic table. Apply significant figures to the final answer only, not to intermediate steps. Rounding too early accumulates error.

Section 16.6

Mass-to-Mass Stoichiometry

In real laboratory work, quantities are almost always measured in grams. Mass-to-mass stoichiometry is therefore the most commonly used form. The key insight is this: you cannot directly convert grams of one substance to grams of another. You must always pass through moles. Moles are the bridge between substances in a chemical equation.

Mass-to-Mass Pathway (Three Steps) g given ÷ molar mass A → mol given × mole ratio → mol unknown × molar mass B → g unknown
Example 16.6.1
Grams of H₂O from grams of glucose — C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6 O₂ → 6 CO₂ + 6 H₂O
  • ProblemHow many grams of water are produced when 36.0 g of glucose undergoes cellular respiration?
  • Molar MassesC₆H₁₂O₆ = 180.16 g/mol  ·  H₂O = 18.02 g/mol
  • Setup 36.0 g C₆H₁₂O₆ × (1 mol / 180.16 g) × (6 mol H₂O / 1 mol C₆H₁₂O₆) × (18.02 g / 1 mol H₂O)
  • Calculate= 0.1998 × 6 × 18.02 = 21.6 g H₂O
  • Check36.0 g of glucose is 0.200 mol. Six moles of water form per mole of glucose, so 1.20 mol H₂O × 18.02 g/mol = 21.6 g. ✓
Answer: 21.6 g H₂O
Example 16.6.2
Grams of AlCl₃ from grams of Al — 2 Al + 3 Cl₂ → 2 AlCl₃
  • ProblemHow many grams of AlCl₃ are produced when 54.0 g of aluminum reacts with excess Cl₂?
  • Molar MassesAl = 26.98 g/mol  ·  AlCl₃ = 26.98 + 3(35.45) = 133.33 g/mol
  • Setup 54.0 g Al × (1 mol Al / 26.98 g) × (2 mol AlCl₃ / 2 mol Al) × (133.33 g AlCl₃ / 1 mol AlCl₃)
  • Calculate= 2.001 mol Al × 1 × 133.33 = 266.9 g → 267 g (3 sig figs)
Answer: 267 g AlCl₃
Never Skip the Mole Bridge

Even when the mole ratio is 1:1, you must still pass through moles. Multiplying grams of one substance by a mass ratio will give wrong answers unless the molar masses of the two substances are identical. The mole ratio is the only valid bridge between substances in a reaction.

Section 16.7

Limiting Reactants

So far, you have assumed that all reactants are available in exactly the right proportions for complete reaction. In real situations, this is rarely the case. Imagine you are assembling sandwiches. Each sandwich requires 2 slices of bread and 1 slice of cheese. If you have 10 slices of bread and 4 slices of cheese, you can make only 4 sandwiches — the cheese runs out first, regardless of how much bread remains. Chemical reactions work exactly the same way.

Definitions

Limiting reactant — the reactant that is completely consumed first. It determines the maximum amount of product that can form. When it is used up, the reaction stops.

Excess reactant — any reactant present in greater amount than needed. Some remains unreacted after the limiting reactant is consumed.

Method: Convert Each Reactant to Moles of Product

For each reactant, calculate how many moles of product it could produce if it were the only limiting factor. The reactant that produces the least product is the limiting reactant. This method is systematic and works for all situations.

Example 16.7.1
Identifying the limiting reactant — N₂ + 3 H₂ → 2 NH₃
  • ProblemYou have 4.0 mol N₂ and 9.0 mol H₂. Which is the limiting reactant? How many moles of NH₃ can form?
  • From N₂4.0 mol N₂ × (2 mol NH₃ / 1 mol N₂) = 8.0 mol NH₃
  • From H₂9.0 mol H₂ × (2 mol NH₃ / 3 mol H₂) = 6.0 mol NH₃
  • CompareH₂ produces fewer moles of NH₃ (6.0 < 8.0). H₂ is the limiting reactant.
Limiting reactant: H₂  ·  Product formed: 6.0 mol NH₃
Example 16.7.2
Identifying the limiting reactant — 2 H₂ + O₂ → 2 H₂O
  • ProblemYou have 6.0 mol H₂ and 4.0 mol O₂. Which is limiting?
  • From H₂6.0 mol H₂ × (2 mol H₂O / 2 mol H₂) = 6.0 mol H₂O
  • From O₂4.0 mol O₂ × (2 mol H₂O / 1 mol O₂) = 8.0 mol H₂O
  • CompareH₂ produces fewer moles of H₂O (6.0 < 8.0). H₂ is the limiting reactant.
Limiting reactant: H₂  ·  Product formed: 6.0 mol H₂O
Common Misconception

The reactant present in the smaller number of moles is not always the limiting reactant. The limiting reactant depends on both the amount present and the mole ratio required by the equation. Always calculate moles of product from each reactant — never simply compare mole quantities.

Section 16.8

Excess Reactants

After the limiting reactant is consumed, some of the excess reactant remains unreacted. Chemists need to know how much in order to recover and reuse it, or to account for it in safety and waste calculations.

Calculating Excess Remaining

mol excess remaining = mol excess starting − mol excess consumed

First identify the limiting reactant. Then calculate how much excess reactant was consumed by the limiting reactant. Subtract from the starting amount.

Example 16.8.1
Excess N₂ remaining — N₂ + 3 H₂ → 2 NH₃
  • SetupFrom Example 16.7.1: 4.0 mol N₂ and 9.0 mol H₂. H₂ is the limiting reactant.
  • N₂ Consumed9.0 mol H₂ × (1 mol N₂ / 3 mol H₂) = 3.0 mol N₂ consumed
  • N₂ Remaining4.0 mol N₂ − 3.0 mol N₂ = 1.0 mol N₂ remaining
1.0 mol N₂ remains unreacted after the reaction.
Example 16.8.2
Mass-based limiting reactant — 2 Al + 3 Cl₂ → 2 AlCl₃
  • ProblemYou have 27.0 g of Al and 106.5 g of Cl₂. Which is the limiting reactant? How many grams of AlCl₃ form?
  • Convert mol Al = 27.0 / 26.98 = 1.00 mol mol Cl₂ = 106.5 / 70.90 = 1.50 mol
  • From Al1.00 mol Al × (2 mol AlCl₃ / 2 mol Al) = 1.00 mol AlCl₃
  • From Cl₂1.50 mol Cl₂ × (2 mol AlCl₃ / 3 mol Cl₂) = 1.00 mol AlCl₃
  • ResultBoth produce equal product — the stoichiometric ratio is exact. Neither is in excess. Product = 1.00 mol AlCl₃ × 133.33 g/mol = 133 g AlCl₃.
Answer: 133 g AlCl₃  ·  No excess reactant (exact stoichiometric ratio)

Section 16.9

Theoretical Yield, Actual Yield, and Percent Yield

Stoichiometric calculations give you the theoretical yield — the maximum amount of product that could form if the reaction were perfectly complete, all substances were pure, no product was lost in handling, and no side reactions occurred. In real laboratory and industrial situations, the amount of product actually collected — the actual yield — is almost always less.

Why Actual Yield Falls Short

ReasonExplanation
Incomplete reactionThe reaction does not go to 100% conversion
Product lossesMaterial lost during transfer between containers
Side reactionsReactants consumed by competing reactions that form different products
EquilibriumSome reactions reach a balance point before completion
Purification lossesSome product lost during filtering, crystallization, or drying
Percent Yield Formula
Percent Yield = (Actual Yield / Theoretical Yield) × 100%

Expressed as a percentage. A value of 100% is theoretically possible but rarely achieved. A value above 100% is impossible and indicates error or contamination.

Example 16.9.1
Percent yield — aspirin synthesis
  • ProblemStoichiometry predicts 14.4 g of aspirin. A student collects 11.7 g. What is the percent yield?
  • SolvePercent Yield = (11.7 g / 14.4 g) × 100% = 81.3%
Answer: 81.3% — the student collected about 81% of the maximum possible product.
Example 16.9.2
Theoretical yield then percent yield — combustion of ethanol
  • EquationC₂H₅OH + 3 O₂ → 2 CO₂ + 3 H₂O
  • ProblemA chemist burns 23.0 g of ethanol and collects 40.5 g of CO₂. Find the theoretical yield and the percent yield.
  • Molar MassesC₂H₅OH = 46.07 g/mol  ·  CO₂ = 44.01 g/mol
  • Theoretical Yield 23.0 g C₂H₅OH × (1 mol / 46.07 g) × (2 mol CO₂ / 1 mol C₂H₅OH) × (44.01 g / 1 mol CO₂) = 0.4993 × 2 × 44.01 = 43.9 g CO₂
  • Percent Yield(40.5 / 43.9) × 100% = 92.2%
Theoretical yield: 43.9 g CO₂  ·  Percent yield: 92.2%
Industrial Significance

In pharmaceutical manufacturing, a five-step synthesis where each step has 90% yield produces a final overall yield of approximately 59% (0.90⁵ ≈ 0.59). Improving even one step significantly increases total production and reduces material waste and cost.

Section 16.10

Stoichiometry in Industry and Laboratories

Every major chemical industry depends on stoichiometric calculations. Without them, manufacturers would have no way to know how much raw material to purchase, how much waste to expect, or how efficiently their processes are running.

ApplicationStoichiometric Role
Haber-Bosch Process (ammonia for fertilizers)Maintains N₂ : H₂ mole ratios; controls limiting reactant; measures percent yield continuously
Pharmaceutical synthesisCalculates batch sizes; multiplies step yields across multi-step syntheses
Combustion engineeringOptimizes fuel-to-air ratio for complete combustion; minimizes CO and unburned fuel
Water treatmentCalculates mass of disinfectant needed per volume of water
Environmental monitoringCalculates CO₂ produced per liter of fuel burned; models atmospheric chemistry
Laboratory planningDetermines how much reactant is needed to produce a target amount of product
Chemistry Connection

The Haber-Bosch process — N₂ + 3 H₂ → 2 NH₃ — is one of the most important chemical reactions in human history. It produces the ammonia used in fertilizers that support the agricultural production feeding billions of people. The precise stoichiometric control of this reaction, developed in the early twentieth century, transformed global food production.

Chapter 16 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 16.1Stoichiometry is the quantitative study of chemical reactions. Balanced equations encode fixed mole ratios that allow chemists to calculate amounts of any substance from any other substance in the reaction.
  • Sec 16.2Coefficients in balanced equations represent particle ratios and mole ratios simultaneously. Every pair of substances in an equation produces a mole ratio usable as a conversion factor. The unknown goes in the numerator; the given goes in the denominator.
  • Sec 16.3Mole ratios convert between moles of different substances. Always write units explicitly and confirm they cancel. Check answers for reasonableness against the equation's coefficients.
  • Sec 16.4Mole-to-mole stoichiometry uses the mole ratio directly. It is the conceptual core of all stoichiometric calculations.
  • Sec 16.5Mole-to-mass stoichiometry adds conversion of mol unknown to g unknown using molar mass. The pathway is: mol given → mol unknown → g unknown.
  • Sec 16.6Mass-to-mass stoichiometry is the most common type. The pathway is: g given → mol given → mol unknown → g unknown. Moles are the required bridge; never convert grams directly to grams.
  • Sec 16.7The limiting reactant is completely consumed first and sets the maximum product yield. To identify it, calculate moles of product each reactant could produce and choose the one giving the least. Do not simply compare mole quantities.
  • Sec 16.8Excess reactant remaining = moles starting − moles consumed. Use the limiting reactant to calculate how much of the excess reactant was consumed, then subtract. Mass-based problems require converting grams to moles first.
  • Sec 16.9Theoretical yield is the maximum product predicted by stoichiometry. Actual yield is what is collected. Percent yield = (actual / theoretical) × 100%. Values above 100% are impossible; they indicate measurement error or contamination.
  • Sec 16.10Stoichiometry is used in every branch of chemistry and chemical industry — from the Haber-Bosch process and pharmaceutical manufacturing to combustion engineering, water treatment, and environmental monitoring.

Key Terms

stoichiometry
The branch of chemistry that uses balanced equations to calculate quantitative relationships between reactants and products in a chemical reaction.
mole ratio
A ratio derived from the coefficients of a balanced chemical equation; used as a conversion factor between moles of different substances.
limiting reactant
The reactant that is completely consumed first and determines the maximum amount of product that can form. Also called the limiting reagent.
excess reactant
A reactant present in greater quantity than the stoichiometric ratio requires; some of it remains unreacted after the limiting reactant is consumed.
theoretical yield
The maximum amount of product calculated from stoichiometry, assuming complete reaction with no losses and no side reactions.
actual yield
The amount of product actually collected from a reaction in practice; always less than or equal to the theoretical yield.
percent yield
The ratio of actual yield to theoretical yield, multiplied by 100%; a measure of reaction efficiency. Values above 100% are impossible.

Formula Review

Mole-to-Mole Conversion
mol unknown = mol given × (coefficient of unknown / coefficient of given)
Mass-to-Mass Conversion (Full Chain)
g unknown = g given × (1 mol given / MM given) × (mol unknown / mol given) × (MM unknown / 1 mol unknown)
Percent Yield

2 H₂ + O₂ → 2 H₂O  ·  How many grams of water form from 8.0 g of H₂?

  • Excess S4.0 mol − 3.0 mol = 1.0 mol S remaining

Each problem contains a stoichiometric error. Identify the error and correct it.

Equation for Problems A and B: 2 HCl + Ca → CaCl₂ + H₂

Problem A
4.0 mol HCl × (2 mol H₂ / 1 mol HCl) = 8.0 mol H₂

The mole ratio is inverted and uses incorrect coefficients. What should it be?

Problem B
6.0 mol HCl × (110.98 g CaCl₂ / 1 mol HCl) = 665.9 g CaCl₂

The mole ratio step was skipped entirely. What is the correct calculation?

Problem C
Percent yield = (24.0 / 18.0) × 100% = 133%

The formula was set up upside down. Correct it and explain why a value above 100% is impossible.

Looking Ahead
Chapter 17: Reactions in Aqueous Solution

Chapter 17 moves stoichiometry into the aqueous world. Most chemical reactions in biology, medicine, geology, and environmental science take place in water. When ionic compounds dissolve, they separate into individual ions that can react with other ions. You will learn about dissociation, electrolytes, precipitation reactions, net ionic equations, and concentration expressed as molarity — and you will apply the stoichiometric skills from this chapter to reactions in solution. Understanding how ions behave in water prepares you for acid-base chemistry, titration, and environmental chemistry in later chapters.

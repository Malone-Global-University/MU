Chapter 28 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

You are made of chemistry. Every breath you take is a gas exchange governed by the same principles you studied in Chapter 18. Every meal you eat is processed by reactions as precise as anything that happens in a laboratory. Every medicine a doctor prescribes works because its molecules interact with specific proteins in your body at the atomic level. The cells in your body run on electrochemical gradients, pH balance, enzyme catalysis, and molecular recognition — all of which are chemistry.

Biology is chemistry at an extraordinary level of organization. Living systems did not invent new rules for matter. They evolved to use the same rules — the same bonding, polarity, acid-base behavior, and reaction kinetics — that you have studied throughout this course. Understanding chemistry is therefore a genuine foundation for understanding life.

This chapter introduces the chemical foundations of living systems and medicine. You will learn how water makes life possible, how carbohydrates and lipids store and supply energy, how proteins carry out the work of the cell, how nucleic acids store and transmit information, how enzymes accelerate reactions with precision, how the body maintains pH, and how medicines act by interacting with molecules at the atomic scale.

This is the final chapter of the course. It brings together nearly everything you have studied — bonding, polarity, intermolecular forces, reaction types, acids and bases, thermochemistry, equilibrium, and organic chemistry — and shows how those ideas apply in the most complex chemical system we know: a living organism.

Opening Question

Right now, your cells are maintaining a blood pH near 7.4, storing energy in molecules built by the same condensation reactions you studied in organic chemistry, and moving electrons in ways that echo the redox reactions of Chapter 24. As you read this final chapter, watch for ideas you have already learned — bonding, polarity, equilibrium, acids and bases, reaction rates — appearing again in a new context: the chemistry of you. By the end of the chapter, you should be able to look at your own body and explain, in chemical terms, how it works.

Section 28.1

Chemistry in Living Systems

Life Requires Chemistry

A living cell is a chemical system. It takes in matter from the environment, transforms that matter through thousands of coordinated reactions, uses released energy to do work, responds to its surroundings, reproduces, and maintains internal conditions within narrow ranges. All of this depends on chemistry.

The chemistry of living systems operates under constraints that a test tube does not face. Reactions must happen at body temperature — roughly 37°C for humans — and at nearly neutral pH. They must be precisely controlled, running fast when needed and stopping when not. They must be organized so that different reactions happen in different compartments without interfering with each other. Living chemistry achieves all of this through the properties of a small number of chemical families: water, carbohydrates, lipids, proteins, and nucleic acids.

The Elements of Life

Only a small subset of the periodic table makes up the bulk of living matter. Carbon, hydrogen, oxygen, nitrogen, phosphorus, and sulfur account for the vast majority of biological molecules by mass. These six elements — sometimes abbreviated CHNOPS — form the backbone of all four major biomolecule families.

Element CategoryExamplesBiological Role
Major elements (CHNOPS)Carbon, Hydrogen, Oxygen, Nitrogen, Phosphorus, SulfurForm the structural backbone of carbohydrates, lipids, proteins, and nucleic acids
Trace elementsIron, Calcium, Magnesium, Potassium, Sodium, Chlorine, Zinc, Copper, IodinePresent in small amounts but chemically essential — e.g., iron in hemoglobin, calcium in muscle contraction, potassium and sodium in nerve signals

Why Carbon Is Central

As you learned in Chapter 25, carbon forms four covalent bonds and can bond to itself in chains, rings, and branched structures of enormous variety. This capacity for structural diversity is why carbon-based molecules can encode the information needed for heredity, catalyze thousands of different reactions, and form structures ranging from rigid cell walls to flexible membranes.

No other element comes close to carbon's combination of bonding flexibility, stability in water, and ability to form both polar and nonpolar functional groups.

Organization of Living Systems

Chemistry operates at every level of biological organization. Atoms bond to form molecules. Small molecules combine to form large polymers. Polymers fold and assemble into structures. Structures work together in cells. Cells work together in tissues and organs. The chemical principles you have studied — bonding, polarity, intermolecular forces, acid-base chemistry, reaction kinetics, equilibrium — operate at every level.

Reading This Chapter

As you move through this chapter, you will move from molecule to function: understanding not just what biological molecules are, but why their chemistry makes life possible. Whenever you encounter a new biomolecule, ask: what is its structure, and how does that structure determine what it can do?

Section 28.2

Water and Life

Water Is Not a Passive Solvent

You studied water throughout this course — as a polar molecule, as a solvent for ionic compounds, as a medium for acid-base reactions, and as a participant in hydration reactions. In biology, water is not simply a background solvent. It is an active participant in nearly every reaction and structural feature of living chemistry.

Life as we know it depends on liquid water. This is not a coincidence. Water's unusual combination of properties — high heat capacity, cohesion, adhesion, density anomaly near freezing, and extraordinary solvent power — make it uniquely suited to support living systems.

Polarity and Hydrogen Bonding in Biology

Water is a polar molecule, and this polarity creates hydrogen bonds. Each water molecule can form up to four hydrogen bonds with neighboring water molecules — two through its hydrogen atoms and two through its lone pairs. This extensive hydrogen bonding network gives water properties that are exceptional among small molecules.

Water's high specific heat means that large inputs of energy produce only modest temperature increases. This buffers the temperature of living cells against rapid fluctuations. Ocean water moderates Earth's climate by absorbing enormous quantities of heat with relatively little temperature change.

Connection — Sweating as a Cooling System

Water's heat of vaporization is high, which means evaporating sweat carries substantial heat away from the body's surface. This makes sweating an effective cooling mechanism — a direct application of the intermolecular force concepts from Chapter 12 and the energy concepts from Chapter 20.

Water as a Universal Solvent

Water dissolves an extraordinary range of substances because of its polarity. Ionic compounds dissociate in water when the attraction between water molecules and ions exceeds the lattice energy holding the crystal together. Polar covalent compounds dissolve because their polar groups interact favorably with water. This is why blood, cytoplasm, saliva, and urine are all aqueous solutions — they carry dissolved ions, nutrients, gases, and waste products throughout the body.

Nonpolar substances do not dissolve well in water. Oils and fats separate from water because their nonpolar carbon-hydrogen bonds cannot form favorable interactions with polar water molecules. This property, called hydrophobicity, turns out to be essential for the structure of cell membranes, as you will see in Section 28.4.

Water as a Reactant

Water participates directly in many biological reactions. Hydrolysis reactions break large biological polymers into smaller pieces by adding a water molecule across a bond. The reverse reaction, condensation (also called dehydration synthesis), links small molecules together by releasing a water molecule.

These two reactions — hydrolysis and condensation — are the basic tools by which cells build and break down carbohydrates, proteins, lipids, and nucleic acids.

The Two Fundamental Reactions of Biochemistry

Hydrolysis (breaking a bond by adding water): A—B + H₂O → A—OH + H—B

Condensation (forming a bond by releasing water): A—OH + H—B → A—B + H₂O

Digestion is largely a process of hydrolysis: breaking polymers in food into monomers that can be absorbed. Biosynthesis reverses this, using condensation reactions to build the body's own polymers.

Water's Ionization

Pure water ionizes to a small extent, as you saw in Chapter 23:

Autoionization of Water

H₂O ⇌ H⁺ + OH⁻

At 25°C, the concentration of H⁺ ions in pure water is 1 × 10⁻⁷ mol/L, giving a pH of 7. This autoionization is the foundation of the pH scale and is essential for understanding biological acid-base chemistry. Living systems maintain pH within very narrow ranges because even small changes in H⁺ concentration significantly affect the behavior of proteins and enzymes.

Section 28.3

Carbohydrates

What Carbohydrates Are

Carbohydrates are organic compounds made of carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen, typically in a ratio that approximates CH₂O. They serve as the primary energy source for most living cells, as structural materials in plants and bacteria, and as molecular signals on cell surfaces.

The name "carbohydrate" reflects the historical observation that these compounds appeared to be hydrates of carbon — that is, carbon combined with water. While this description is not a precise chemical definition, it remains a useful memory aid.

Monosaccharides: The Building Blocks

The simplest carbohydrates are monosaccharides, single sugar units that cannot be broken down into smaller sugars by hydrolysis. Common monosaccharides include glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆), fructose (C₆H₁₂O₆), and galactose (C₆H₁₂O₆).

Monosaccharides contain multiple hydroxyl groups (—OH), which makes them highly polar and readily soluble in water. They also contain either an aldehyde group (—CHO) or a ketone group (C=O), which affects their reactivity.

Glucose, the most important monosaccharide in human biology, is the primary fuel for cellular respiration. The brain consumes glucose almost exclusively.

Connection — Isomers and Taste

Glucose, fructose, and galactose have identical molecular formulas. They are structural isomers — the same atoms arranged differently. This difference in arrangement gives them different shapes and different interactions with enzymes and receptors. Your tongue detects fructose as sweeter than glucose, even though they have identical compositions, because the differently shaped molecules interact differently with taste receptor proteins.

Disaccharides: Two Units Joined

When two monosaccharides join through a condensation reaction, they form a disaccharide. The bond connecting them is called a glycosidic bond.

DisaccharideComposed OfNotes
Sucrose (table sugar)Glucose + FructoseThe form in which most plants transport sugar from leaves to other tissues
Lactose (milk sugar)Glucose + GalactoseLactose intolerance results when the body lacks sufficient lactase enzyme to hydrolyze lactose
Maltose (malt sugar)Glucose + GlucoseForms during the digestion of starch

Polysaccharides: Chains of Many Units

Polysaccharides are large polymers built from many monosaccharide units linked by glycosidic bonds. Their function depends on the identity of the monomer, the type of glycosidic bond connecting units, and whether the chain is branched or linear.

PolysaccharideMonomerRole and Notes
StarchGlucosePrimary energy storage in plants; chain coils into a helix; digested by amylase enzymes into glucose
GlycogenGlucoseEnergy storage in animals (liver and muscle); more branched than starch, allowing faster glucose mobilization
CelluloseGlucoseStructural material in plant cell walls; glycosidic bond geometry differs from starch, so human enzymes cannot hydrolyze it — provides dietary fiber
ChitinModified glucose (with acetyl-amine group)Structural material in insect exoskeletons, crustacean shells, and fungal cell walls

Carbohydrates as Energy

When cells break down glucose through cellular respiration, they release energy stored in the carbon-carbon and carbon-hydrogen bonds:

Cellular Respiration

C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6 O₂ → 6 CO₂ + 6 H₂O + energy

This is a combustion reaction at the cellular level — the same type of reaction as burning wood or gasoline, but carried out in small, controlled steps by enzymes rather than all at once. The energy released is used to synthesize ATP (adenosine triphosphate), the molecule cells use to power virtually all their energy-consuming activities.

One mole of glucose releases approximately 2,803 kJ of energy upon complete combustion. Cells capture about 40% of this energy as ATP; the rest is released as heat, which maintains body temperature.

Section 28.4

Lipids

What Lipids Are

Lipids are a chemically diverse group of biological molecules that share one defining property: they are largely nonpolar and therefore insoluble in water. This hydrophobic character is not a limitation; it is precisely what makes lipids useful for energy storage, membrane structure, insulation, and chemical signaling.

Unlike carbohydrates, proteins, and nucleic acids, lipids are not true polymers built from repeating monomer units. They are instead a collection of structurally related but chemically varied molecules unified by their water insolubility.

Fatty Acids: The Building Blocks of Fats

Many lipids are built from fatty acids — long-chain carboxylic acids with the general structure CH₃—(CH₂)ₙ—COOH. The long hydrocarbon chain is nonpolar and hydrophobic. The carboxylic acid group (—COOH) at one end is polar and can interact with water. This combination of a polar head and a nonpolar tail gives fatty acids amphipathic character — a property that is essential for membrane structure.

Fatty acids are classified as saturated or unsaturated based on whether their hydrocarbon chains contain double bonds.

Saturated fatty acids have no carbon-carbon double bonds. Their chains are straight and can pack tightly together. Fats that are solid at room temperature — butter, lard, coconut oil — are rich in saturated fatty acids. The tight packing of saturated chains creates strong London dispersion forces, raising the melting point.

Unsaturated fatty acids contain one or more carbon-carbon double bonds, which introduce kinks into the chain. These kinks prevent tight packing, reducing intermolecular attraction and lowering the melting point. Oils that are liquid at room temperature — olive oil, vegetable oil, fish oil — are rich in unsaturated fatty acids. Polyunsaturated fatty acids contain multiple double bonds.

Trans Fats

Most naturally occurring unsaturated fatty acids have cis double bonds, which create sharp kinks. Artificial trans fats result from partial hydrogenation of vegetable oils, which converts some cis double bonds to trans. Trans double bonds produce less kinking, allowing the chains to pack more closely — raising the melting point and giving the fat a more solid texture. Trans fats have been largely phased out of food production because of their association with cardiovascular disease.

Triglycerides: Energy Storage Lipids

Triglycerides (also called triacylglycerols) are the main form of energy storage in animals. A triglyceride is formed when three fatty acid molecules bond to a glycerol backbone through ester bonds, releasing three water molecules (condensation).

Glycerol is a three-carbon alcohol with a hydroxyl group on each carbon. Each hydroxyl reacts with the carboxylic acid group of a fatty acid to form an ester linkage.

Energy Density Comparison

Triglycerides release about 37 kJ of energy per gram — more than twice the energy density of carbohydrates (about 17 kJ per gram). This is because fatty acid chains are highly reduced (rich in C—H bonds), and oxidizing those bonds releases substantial energy. The hydrophobic nature of triglycerides also means they store without the water of hydration that carbohydrates require, making them even more compact. Adipose (fat) tissue stores triglycerides as long-term energy reserves, insulation, and cushioning for organs.

Phospholipids and Cell Membranes

Phospholipids are structurally similar to triglycerides, but one fatty acid is replaced by a phosphate group attached to a small polar molecule such as choline. This gives phospholipids a distinctive structure: two nonpolar fatty acid tails and one polar phosphate head.

This amphipathic structure — hydrophilic head, hydrophobic tails — is the key to membrane formation. When phospholipids are placed in water, they spontaneously arrange themselves into a bilayer: two sheets of phospholipids with their tails pointing inward (away from water) and their heads pointing outward (toward water). This arrangement, driven purely by the chemistry of intermolecular forces, forms the structural foundation of every cell membrane.

The phospholipid bilayer creates a selectively permeable barrier. Small nonpolar molecules like oxygen and carbon dioxide cross easily. Large or charged molecules cannot cross without the assistance of protein channels embedded in the membrane. This selectivity allows cells to maintain a different internal chemical environment from their surroundings.

Steroids

Steroids are lipids with a distinctive four-ring carbon skeleton. Despite looking very different from triglycerides, steroids share the property of being largely nonpolar and hydrophobic.

Cholesterol is the most abundant steroid in animal cell membranes. It is embedded between phospholipids and modulates membrane fluidity — preventing membranes from becoming too rigid at low temperatures or too fluid at high temperatures.

Steroid hormones — including testosterone, estrogen, progesterone, and cortisol — are derived from cholesterol. Because steroids are nonpolar, they can cross cell membranes directly and interact with receptor proteins inside the cell, triggering changes in gene expression. This distinguishes steroid hormones from most protein-based hormones, which must bind to surface receptors and signal through second-messenger systems.

Waxes

Waxes are esters formed from long-chain fatty acids and long-chain alcohols. They are highly nonpolar and water-repellent. Waxes coat plant leaves (reducing water loss), insect cuticles, feathers, and human ear canals (where earwax traps debris). Beeswax, carnauba wax, and spermaceti (from whale oil) are examples.

Proteins

Proteins Are the Working Molecules of Life

If carbohydrates are the fuel and lipids are the membranes, proteins are the machinery. Proteins carry out more diverse functions than any other class of biological molecule. They catalyze reactions (as enzymes), transport molecules (hemoglobin carries oxygen), provide structure (collagen and keratin), regulate gene expression (transcription factors), defend against pathogens (antibodies), generate movement (myosin and actin in muscle), and transmit signals (many hormones and receptors are proteins).

This versatility comes from structure: proteins can fold into an enormous variety of three-dimensional shapes, and shape determines function at the molecular level.

Amino Acids: The Building Blocks

Proteins are polymers of amino acids. There are twenty standard amino acids used in biological proteins. All share a common core structure: a central carbon (the alpha carbon) bonded to an amino group (—NH₂), a carboxylic acid group (—COOH), a hydrogen atom, and a variable side chain called the R group.

General Amino Acid Structure

H₂N—CH(R)—COOH

The R group is what distinguishes one amino acid from another. Some R groups are nonpolar and hydrophobic (alanine, leucine, valine). Some are polar and uncharged (serine, threonine). Some are charged — either positively (lysine, arginine, histidine) or negatively (aspartate, glutamate) at physiological pH. Cysteine has a sulfhydryl group (—SH) that can form disulfide bonds; proline has a cyclic R group that creates kinks in protein chains; glycine's R group is just a hydrogen atom, making it the smallest and most flexible amino acid.

The chemical diversity of R groups — ranging from nonpolar hydrocarbons to charged acids and bases — gives proteins their range of structures and functions.

Peptide Bonds: Linking Amino Acids

Amino acids link together through peptide bonds, formed by condensation reactions between the carboxylic acid group of one amino acid and the amino group of the next, releasing a water molecule.

A chain of two amino acids is a dipeptide. A chain of three is a tripeptide. Longer chains are polypeptides. A protein is typically a polypeptide (or assembly of polypeptides) that has folded into a specific three-dimensional shape and performs a biological function.

The sequence of amino acids in a polypeptide is called the primary structure. This sequence is determined by genetic information in DNA.

Levels of Protein Structure

Protein structure is described at four levels, each building on the previous.

LevelDescriptionStabilized By
PrimaryThe linear sequence of amino acids in the chainCovalent peptide bonds
SecondaryLocal folding patterns such as the alpha helix and beta sheetHydrogen bonds between backbone atoms (C=O and N—H groups)
TertiaryThe overall three-dimensional shape of the entire polypeptide chainInteractions among R groups: hydrophobic clustering, hydrogen bonds, ionic interactions, and disulfide bonds

Connection 28.5.1
A single change in primary structure: sickle cell disease
  • BackgroundEven a single change in the amino acid sequence — one amino acid substituted for another — can dramatically alter a protein's function.
  • The ChangeSickle cell disease results from a single amino acid change in hemoglobin: valine replaces glutamate at position 6 of the beta chain.
  • The EffectThis single substitution changes the protein's surface chemistry — glutamate is charged and polar, while valine is nonpolar and hydrophobic — causing hemoglobin molecules to aggregate under low-oxygen conditions and distorting red blood cells into a sickle shape.
One amino acid out of hundreds can change a protein's chemistry — and a person's health.

Hemoglobin, for example, consists of four polypeptide subunits held together by noncovalent interactions in its quaternary structure. The interaction among subunits allows hemoglobin to undergo cooperative conformational changes that improve its efficiency in picking up and releasing oxygen.

Protein Denaturation

Protein function depends on structure. If the three-dimensional shape of a protein is disrupted, the protein loses its function — a process called denaturation.

Denaturation Is Often Permanent

Denaturation can be caused by heat (which disrupts hydrogen bonds and hydrophobic interactions), extreme pH (which changes the charges on ionizable R groups), certain chemicals (such as urea or detergents), and heavy metals (which interfere with disulfide bonds). Cooking an egg denatures the proteins in egg white, converting the transparent, runny liquid into a white, firm solid. Once denatured, most proteins cannot return to their original structure under physiological conditions.

Section 28.6

Nucleic Acids

Information Storage at the Molecular Level

Every living cell contains a chemical library. Encoded in the sequence of nucleotides in DNA is the information needed to build every protein the cell can make. This information is copied, read, translated, and inherited. The molecules that store and transmit this information are nucleic acids: deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA) and ribonucleic acid (RNA).

Nucleotides: The Building Blocks

Nucleic acids are polymers of nucleotides. Each nucleotide has three parts: a five-carbon sugar (deoxyribose in DNA, ribose in RNA), a phosphate group, and a nitrogenous base.

Base CategoryBasesStructure
PurinesAdenine (A), Guanine (G)Double-ring structures
PyrimidinesCytosine (C), Thymine (T — DNA only), Uracil (U — RNA only)Single-ring structures

Nucleotides link together through phosphodiester bonds between the phosphate of one nucleotide and the sugar of the next, forming a long chain with a repeating sugar-phosphate backbone. The sequence of bases along this backbone encodes genetic information.

DNA Structure

DNA (deoxyribonucleic acid) carries the genetic information for all cellular life. James Watson, Francis Crick, Rosalind Franklin, and Maurice Wilkins contributed to the discovery of DNA's structure in 1953.

DNA consists of two antiparallel polynucleotide chains wound around each other in a double helix. The two chains are held together by hydrogen bonds between complementary base pairs. This base-pairing specificity means that if you know the sequence of one strand, you automatically know the sequence of the other.

Base Pairing Rules

Adenine (A) pairs with Thymine (T) — two hydrogen bonds

Guanine (G) pairs with Cytosine (C) — three hydrogen bonds

The sugar-phosphate backbone of each strand is on the outside of the helix (facing water); the base pairs are in the interior (facing away from water, like stacked hydrophobic rings). This arrangement is stabilized by both hydrogen bonding between base pairs and hydrophobic stacking interactions between adjacent base pairs. Base-pair complementarity is what makes DNA replication possible: each strand serves as a template for building a new complementary strand, so both daughter cells receive an accurate copy of the genetic information.

RNA Structure and Function

RNA (ribonucleic acid) differs from DNA in three ways: it contains ribose instead of deoxyribose, it uses uracil instead of thymine, and it is usually single-stranded rather than double-stranded (though it can fold back on itself to form local double-stranded regions).

RNA TypeRole
Messenger RNA (mRNA)Carries the genetic sequence from DNA in the nucleus to the ribosomes in the cytoplasm, where proteins are made
Transfer RNA (tRNA)Reads the mRNA sequence and brings the correct amino acid to the ribosome; each tRNA has a complementary anticodon that matches a three-nucleotide codon in the mRNA
Ribosomal RNA (rRNA)Forms the structural and catalytic core of ribosomes; catalyzes the formation of peptide bonds during translation

The flow of information — from DNA to RNA to protein — is called the central dogma of molecular biology. The chemistry of nucleic acids — hydrogen bonding, base stacking, backbone flexibility — makes this information transfer possible.

ATP: A Special Nucleotide

Adenosine triphosphate (ATP) is built from the nucleotide adenine + ribose + three phosphate groups. It is not primarily an information storage molecule but an energy currency molecule. The bonds between phosphate groups in ATP store chemical energy. Hydrolysis of ATP releases this energy:

ATP Hydrolysis

ATP + H₂O → ADP + Pᵢ + energy

This is the reaction that powers virtually all energy-consuming processes in living cells: muscle contraction, active transport, biosynthesis, and cell signaling. The energy of cellular respiration (breaking down glucose) is used to synthesize ATP from ADP and inorganic phosphate.

Section 28.7

Enzymes and Reaction Rates

What Enzymes Do

You studied reaction rates in Chapter 21 and learned that catalysts speed up reactions without being consumed. Enzymes are biological catalysts — proteins (and in some cases RNA molecules) that accelerate chemical reactions in living systems by lowering activation energy.

Without enzymes, most biochemical reactions would proceed too slowly to sustain life at normal temperatures. The conversion of carbon dioxide to carbonic acid in blood, for example, happens about ten million times faster with the enzyme carbonic anhydrase than without it. The digestion of a meal, the copying of DNA, and the synthesis of every protein in your body all depend on enzyme catalysis.

The Active Site

Enzymes are not general-purpose catalysts. Each enzyme catalyzes one specific reaction or type of reaction. This specificity comes from the active site — a precisely shaped pocket or groove on the enzyme's surface where the substrate (the molecule being converted) binds.

The active site is formed by the three-dimensional folding of the protein. Specific R groups from amino acids in the active site interact with the substrate through hydrogen bonds, ionic interactions, and hydrophobic contacts. The specific geometry and chemical character of these interactions determines which molecule can bind — a concept called enzyme-substrate complementarity.

The induced fit model describes how binding is not completely rigid: when the substrate enters the active site, the enzyme's shape adjusts slightly to embrace the substrate more tightly, orienting reactive groups for catalysis.

Once bound, the enzyme may stress or distort the substrate's bonds, lower the energy required for the reaction to proceed, or bring two substrates close together in the correct orientation. After the reaction is complete, the products leave the active site, and the enzyme is free to catalyze another reaction.

Factors That Affect Enzyme Activity

FactorEffect on Enzyme Activity
TemperatureIncreases rate up to a point, like any reaction. Temperatures that are too high denature the enzyme, destroying the active site. Each enzyme has an optimal temperature — about 37°C for human enzymes, but 70–80°C or higher for enzymes from heat-loving bacteria.
pHAffects the charges on amino acid R groups in the active site. Most human enzymes work best near neutral pH. Pepsin (stomach) works best at pH 2; intestinal enzymes work best around pH 7–8. Extreme pH can disrupt ionic and hydrogen bonds, reducing activity or causing denaturation.
Substrate concentrationAt low concentration, increasing substrate increases rate (more frequent collisions with the active site). At very high concentration, active sites become saturated and rate cannot increase further without adding more enzyme.
InhibitorsCompetitive inhibitors resemble the substrate and bind the active site, blocking substrate access. Noncompetitive inhibitors bind elsewhere and change the enzyme's shape, reducing catalytic efficiency.
Cofactors and coenzymesNon-protein molecules required for activity. Some enzymes need metal ion cofactors (zinc, iron, magnesium); others need organic coenzymes, many derived from vitamins (B₁₂, niacin, riboflavin).
Connection — Vitamins and Enzymes

Many pharmaceutical drugs work by inhibiting specific enzymes. Aspirin inhibits an enzyme involved in prostaglandin synthesis, reducing fever and inflammation. Vitamin deficiencies cause metabolic disorders precisely because, without the vitamin-derived coenzyme, the enzyme that depends on it cannot function.

Worked Example 28.7.1
Identifying factors in an enzyme experiment
  • ProblemA student tests the rate of an enzyme-catalyzed reaction at pH 2, pH 7, and pH 12. Results show the highest rate at pH 7, and very low rates at pH 2 and pH 12. The student concludes the enzyme is active only at pH 7.
  • EvaluateThe conclusion is partially correct but could be improved. The enzyme shows optimal activity at pH 7, but activity at other pH values should be described as reduced, not absent, unless confirmed by the data.
  • ConsiderThe student should also consider whether the activity loss at extreme pH values is reversible (inhibition) or irreversible (denaturation).
Better conclusion: "This enzyme shows optimal activity near pH 7. Activity decreases at pH 2 and pH 12, possibly due to changes in the ionization of active site residues or enzyme denaturation."
Section 28.8

pH and Buffers in the Body

Why pH Must Be Controlled

You studied pH and buffers in Chapter 23. In living systems, pH control is not merely convenient — it is critical for survival. Even small changes in blood pH outside the range of 7.35 to 7.45 can interfere with enzyme function, disrupt the balance of charged biomolecules, and impair organ function. A blood pH below 7.2 or above 7.6 is life-threatening.

The body maintains pH through chemical buffers, the respiratory system, and the kidneys — all working together in a remarkable coordinated system.

The Bicarbonate Buffer System

The most important buffer in blood is the bicarbonate buffer system, based on the equilibrium between carbon dioxide, carbonic acid, and bicarbonate ion:

The Bicarbonate Buffer Equilibrium

CO₂ + H₂O ⇌ H₂CO₃ ⇌ H⁺ + HCO₃⁻

When blood pH drops (more H⁺ present), the equilibrium shifts left, consuming H⁺ ions by converting them to H₂CO₃, which then breaks down to CO₂. Faster breathing expels CO₂ from the lungs, driving the equilibrium further left and removing acid from the blood. When blood pH rises (fewer H⁺ ions), the equilibrium shifts right, releasing H⁺ ions to restore pH.

This buffer system connects chemistry to physiology: breathing rate is a chemical control mechanism for blood pH. Hyperventilation (breathing too fast) can lower CO₂ levels enough to raise blood pH, causing alkalosis. Hypoventilation (breathing too slowly) can allow CO₂ to build up, lowering pH and causing acidosis.

Phosphate and Protein Buffers

Inside cells, the phosphate buffer system (H₂PO₄⁻ ⇌ H⁺ + HPO₄²⁻) helps maintain intracellular pH. Proteins also act as buffers: amino acid R groups with ionizable side chains can accept or donate protons depending on pH, helping resist pH changes in both blood and cells.

Hemoglobin itself is an important buffer in blood. As hemoglobin releases oxygen in tissues where CO₂ is high, it picks up protons (becomes protonated), helping buffer the increase in H⁺ caused by CO₂ dissolving in blood.

Acidosis and Alkalosis

When the body's pH-regulating systems are overwhelmed, pH moves outside the normal range.

Respiratory acidosisCO₂ builds up in blood — from lung disease, airway obstruction, or depressed breathing (e.g., sedative overdose)Excess CO₂ generates excess H⁺, lowering pH

Treating the Underlying Chemistry

Treatment depends on restoring the underlying chemical balance, not just neutralizing the pH, because the buffer and respiratory systems are interrelated.

Section 28.9

Medicines and Molecular Interactions

How Drugs Work at the Molecular Level

A drug is a molecule that alters biological function by interacting with specific target molecules in the body. Understanding how drugs work requires understanding bonding, shape, polarity, and the chemistry of molecular recognition.

Most drugs work by binding to specific proteins — usually receptors, enzymes, or ion channels — and either activating or inhibiting those proteins. The selectivity of a drug comes from the complementarity between the drug molecule's shape and chemical properties and the binding site on its target protein.

Lock and Key: Molecular Shape and Binding

Drug-target interactions are often described using the lock-and-key analogy: the drug (key) must fit precisely into the binding site (lock) on its target protein. The binding is typically noncovalent — held together by hydrogen bonds, ionic interactions, London dispersion forces, and hydrophobic contacts.

Because these interactions are noncovalent, they are reversible. Most drugs bind their targets temporarily and then dissociate, which is important for controlling drug action. A drug that bound permanently to its target would be much harder to use safely.

Chirality Matters — Thalidomide

Shape and chirality matter enormously in drug design. Many drug molecules contain chiral centers — carbon atoms bonded to four different groups — that create mirror-image structures called enantiomers. The two enantiomers of a drug often have very different effects because they fit differently into the asymmetric binding sites of biological proteins. Thalidomide, a drug used for morning sickness in the 1950s, was sold as a mixture of enantiomers. One enantiomer had the intended effect; the other caused severe birth defects.

Enzyme Inhibitors as Drugs

Many important drugs work by inhibiting specific enzymes. Understanding the enzyme's active site and reaction mechanism guides the design of molecules that block it.

Aspirin (acetylsalicylic acid) inhibits cyclooxygenase enzymes (COX-1 and COX-2) by forming a covalent bond with a serine residue in the active site, permanently blocking the enzyme. COX enzymes catalyze the synthesis of prostaglandins — lipid signaling molecules involved in inflammation, pain, and fever. By blocking prostaglandin synthesis, aspirin reduces all three.

Statins are drugs that reduce blood cholesterol by competitively inhibiting HMG-CoA reductase, a key enzyme in cholesterol biosynthesis. Statins resemble the natural substrate closely enough to bind the active site but are not converted by the enzyme.

Penicillin and related antibiotics inhibit enzymes involved in building bacterial cell walls. Human cells lack cell walls, so penicillin selectively kills bacteria without affecting human cells — an example of selective toxicity. This selectivity is based on the difference in chemistry between bacterial and human cells, not on any particular kindness of the antibiotic molecule.

HIV protease inhibitors block the protease enzyme that HIV uses to cut its long polyprotein into functional pieces during replication. By inhibiting this enzyme, the drugs prevent HIV from making new viral particles inside infected cells.

Receptor Agonists and Antagonists

Many drugs act on receptor proteins — proteins that normally bind a natural signaling molecule (such as a hormone or neurotransmitter) and generate a cellular response.

Agonist vs. Antagonist

An agonist is a drug that binds the receptor and activates it — mimicking the natural signaling molecule. An antagonist binds the receptor but does not activate it, blocking the natural signal.

Beta-blockers (antagonists at beta-adrenergic receptors) block epinephrine from binding, reducing heart rate and blood pressure. Opioid pain medications (agonists at opioid receptors) suppress pain signals and produce sedation — but the same binding also slows breathing, which is why opioid overdose causes respiratory failure. Antihistamines (antagonists at histamine receptors) block histamine, relieving allergy symptoms such as itching, swelling, and mucus production.

Drug Absorption, Distribution, and Elimination

A drug that cannot reach its target is useless. The journey of a drug from administration to action involves absorption into the bloodstream, distribution to target tissues, metabolism (chemical transformation), and elimination.

Most drugs are absorbed in the small intestine when taken orally. Their rate of absorption depends on the drug's polarity, molecular size, and ionization state. Nonpolar drugs cross membranes by diffusion more easily than polar ones. The stomach's acidic environment affects ionization of drugs with ionizable groups, which in turn affects absorption.

The liver metabolizes most drugs, often converting them into more polar, water-soluble products that can be excreted by the kidneys. This metabolic transformation sometimes converts an inactive prodrug into an active form, or a therapeutic drug into a toxic metabolite.

Acetaminophen and the Liver

Acetaminophen (Tylenol) is normally metabolized safely, but in overdose, a toxic metabolite accumulates in the liver, causing liver failure. This is a direct example of how drug metabolism — a chemical transformation — can determine whether a medicine is safe or dangerous depending on dose.

Section 28.10

Chemistry as a Bridge to Biology

The Chemical Basis of Life's Complexity

You began this course by learning that chemistry links visible observations to invisible particles. You end it at the frontier where chemistry becomes biology. The living cell is not a mystery that chemistry cannot explain — it is chemistry organized at a level of complexity that continues to astonish scientists.

The same bonding principles that explain table salt explain the ionic interactions holding proteins together. The same polarity that explains why oil and water do not mix explains why cell membranes form spontaneously. The same acid-base equilibria that describe vinegar and baking soda describe the buffer systems keeping your blood at pH 7.4.

Biochemistry and Medicine

The molecular understanding of disease has transformed medicine. Before the discovery of DNA structure, infectious disease was fought primarily through hygiene, surgery, and broad-spectrum drugs. Today, drugs are designed by examining the three-dimensional structure of target proteins, modeling how candidate molecules will bind, and optimizing them computationally before synthesis. Vaccines are designed at the molecular level, targeting specific viral proteins. Gene therapy targets the chemistry of DNA itself.

Understanding the chemistry of enzymes led to modern antibiotics. Understanding the chemistry of hemoglobin led to the molecular understanding of sickle cell disease. Understanding the chemistry of DNA led to PCR, genetic sequencing, and modern forensics. Understanding the chemistry of receptors has enabled the rational design of hundreds of drugs.

Biochemistry and Nutrition

Nutrition is applied biochemistry. The energy values of foods — measured in Calories (kilocalories) — reflect the thermochemistry of oxidizing carbohydrates, fats, and proteins. The essential amino acids your body cannot synthesize must come from food. The fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K) are absorbed with dietary fat and stored in fatty tissues. The water-soluble vitamins (B complex, C) dissolve in blood and are not stored as effectively, making regular intake important.

Understanding the chemistry of lipids explains why excessive saturated fat raises LDL cholesterol: saturated fats reduce the clearance of LDL particles from blood by downregulating LDL receptors in the liver. Understanding the chemistry of glucose and insulin explains diabetes and guides dietary recommendations for managing it.

Biochemistry and the Environment

The chemistry of living systems interacts with the chemistry of Earth systems. Photosynthesis, carried out by plants, algae, and cyanobacteria, uses solar energy to convert carbon dioxide and water into glucose and oxygen — the reverse of cellular respiration. These two reactions together drive the global carbon cycle.

Nitrogen fixation by bacteria converts atmospheric nitrogen (N₂) into ammonia (NH₃), making nitrogen available for building amino acids and nucleotides in all living things. The chemistry of nitrogen fixation — breaking the extremely stable triple bond of N₂ — is one of the most energetically demanding reactions in biology and in industrial chemistry (the Haber process, which you encountered in Chapter 22).

The chemistry of decomposition — bacteria and fungi breaking down dead organic matter — cycles nutrients back into the soil and atmosphere. Agricultural chemistry depends on understanding these cycles to maintain soil fertility and avoid environmental damage from excess nutrient runoff.

Looking Forward

This course has introduced you to chemistry as a way of thinking: a way of asking why matter behaves as it does, of connecting the invisible particle world to observable properties and transformations, and of applying quantitative reasoning to real problems.

Students who continue into biology will find that biochemistry — the chemistry of life — makes much more sense with a foundation in chemical bonding, reaction types, equilibrium, and thermochemistry. Students who continue into physics will find that thermodynamics, electromagnetic interactions, and wave-particle duality connect to topics you have studied in chemistry. Students who continue into medicine, environmental science, engineering, materials science, or agriculture will find chemical thinking indispensable.

Chemistry does not stop at the boundaries of this course. It is the science of matter and change, and matter and change are everywhere.

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 28.1Living systems are chemical systems built mainly from carbon, hydrogen, oxygen, nitrogen, phosphorus, and sulfur (CHNOPS), plus essential trace elements. Carbon's bonding versatility makes it central to biological molecules. Chemistry operates at every level of biological organization, from atoms to organisms.
  • Sec 28.2Water's polarity, hydrogen bonding, and extraordinary solvent properties make it the medium of life. Hydrolysis and condensation reactions build and break down biological polymers. Water's autoionization (H₂O ⇌ H⁺ + OH⁻) is the foundation of biological pH regulation.
  • Sec 28.3Carbohydrates serve as energy sources (glucose, starch, glycogen) and structural materials (cellulose, chitin). Monosaccharides are the monomers; disaccharides and polysaccharides are built by condensation reactions. Cellular respiration oxidizes glucose to release energy as ATP.
  • Sec 28.4Lipids include triglycerides (energy storage), phospholipids (membrane structure), steroids (hormones and cholesterol), and waxes. Lipids are defined by their hydrophobicity. Phospholipid bilayers form spontaneously because of the amphipathic structure of phospholipids.
  • Sec 28.5Proteins are polymers of amino acids linked by peptide bonds. The twenty amino acids differ in their R groups. Protein structure is organized at four levels — primary, secondary, tertiary, and quaternary — and function depends entirely on structure. Denaturation destroys function by disrupting structure.
  • Sec 28.6Nucleic acids — DNA and RNA — are polymers of nucleotides. DNA stores genetic information in base sequence; complementary base pairing (A—T, G—C) enables replication. RNA transfers information from DNA to ribosomes, where it directs protein synthesis. ATP, built from a nucleotide, is the energy currency of the cell.
  • Sec 28.7Enzymes are protein catalysts that lower activation energy and accelerate reactions with great specificity. Active sites are precisely shaped for substrate binding, refined by induced fit. Temperature, pH, substrate concentration, inhibitors, and cofactors all affect enzyme activity.
  • Sec 28.8The body maintains blood pH in the narrow range of 7.35 to 7.45 using the bicarbonate buffer system, supplemented by phosphate and protein buffers and coordinated with respiratory rate and kidney function. Failure to maintain pH causes acidosis or alkalosis.
  • Sec 28.9Drugs work by binding to specific proteins — enzymes, receptors, or ion channels — through noncovalent interactions that depend on molecular shape, polarity, and charge complementarity. Enzyme inhibitors, receptor agonists, and antagonists all exploit molecular-level interactions to alter biological function. Drug design, absorption, metabolism, and elimination all depend on chemical principles.
  • Sec 28.10Chemistry is the foundation of biology, medicine, nutrition, and environmental science. Mastering the principles of this course prepares students to understand living systems, interpret medical science, and address real-world problems at the molecular level.

Key Terms Review

active site
The specific region on an enzyme where the substrate binds and the catalytic reaction occurs; shaped precisely for the enzyme's specific substrate.
adenosine triphosphate (ATP)
A nucleotide carrying three phosphate groups that serves as the primary energy currency of cells; hydrolysis of ATP releases energy for cellular work.
amino acid
The monomer unit of proteins; consists of an amino group, a carboxylic acid group, and a variable R group attached to a central alpha carbon.
amphipathic
Describes a molecule with both a hydrophilic (water-loving) region and a hydrophobic (water-fearing) region; characteristic of phospholipids.
agonist
A drug or molecule that binds a receptor and activates it, mimicking the natural signaling molecule.
antagonist
A drug or molecule that binds a receptor without activating it, blocking the natural signal.
carbohydrate
A biological molecule made of carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen in approximately a CH₂O ratio; serves as an energy source and structural material.
cellular respiration
The metabolic process by which cells break down glucose in the presence of oxygen to produce carbon dioxide, water, and ATP.
central dogma
The flow of genetic information from DNA to RNA to protein.
coenzyme
An organic molecule required for enzyme activity, often derived from vitamins.
competitive inhibitor
A molecule that inhibits an enzyme by binding to the active site, competing directly with the substrate.
denaturation
The disruption of a protein's three-dimensional structure by heat, extreme pH, or other agents, resulting in loss of function.
disaccharide
A carbohydrate formed by the condensation of two monosaccharides joined by a glycosidic bond.
DNA (deoxyribonucleic acid)
The double-stranded nucleic acid that stores genetic information in the sequence of its bases.
enzyme
A biological catalyst, usually a protein, that speeds up a specific chemical reaction by lowering activation energy.
fatty acid
A long-chain carboxylic acid that forms the building blocks of triglycerides and phospholipids; may be saturated (no double bonds) or unsaturated (one or more double bonds).
glycosidic bond
The covalent bond connecting two monosaccharide units in a disaccharide or polysaccharide; formed by condensation.
hydrolysis
The chemical breakdown of a compound by reaction with water; used by cells to break down polymers.
hydrophobic
Describing a nonpolar molecule or region that does not interact favorably with water; literally "water-fearing."
induced fit model
A description of enzyme-substrate binding in which the enzyme's shape adjusts upon substrate binding to optimize the interaction.
lipid
A diverse group of nonpolar biological molecules including fats, oils, waxes, phospholipids, and steroids; defined by insolubility in water.
monomer
A small molecule that joins with others to form a polymer.
monosaccharide
The simplest carbohydrate; a single sugar unit (e.g., glucose, fructose, galactose).
nucleotide
The monomer unit of nucleic acids; consists of a five-carbon sugar, a phosphate group, and a nitrogenous base.
peptide bond
The covalent bond linking the carboxylic acid group of one amino acid to the amino group of the next; formed by condensation.
phospholipid
A lipid with two fatty acid tails and a polar phosphate-containing head; the primary structural component of cell membranes.
phospholipid bilayer
The double-layer arrangement of phospholipids forming cell membranes, with hydrophobic tails facing inward and hydrophilic heads facing outward toward water.
polypeptide
A long chain of amino acids linked by peptide bonds.
polysaccharide
A carbohydrate polymer made of many monosaccharide units (e.g., starch, glycogen, cellulose).
primary structure
The linear sequence of amino acids in a polypeptide chain; determined by genetic information.
protein
A biological polymer of amino acids that folds into a specific three-dimensional structure and performs a specific function.
quaternary structure
The assembly of two or more polypeptide chains in a functional protein complex.
R group
The variable side chain on an amino acid that determines its chemical properties and identity.
RNA (ribonucleic acid)
A single-stranded nucleic acid involved in protein synthesis; types include mRNA, tRNA, and rRNA.
saturated fatty acid
A fatty acid with no carbon-carbon double bonds; solid at room temperature.
secondary structure
Local folding patterns in a polypeptide, such as alpha helices and beta sheets, stabilized by hydrogen bonds between backbone atoms.
selective toxicity
The ability of a drug to harm a pathogen or target cell without harming the host; depends on differences in chemistry between the target and host.
steroid
A lipid with a characteristic four-ring carbon skeleton; includes cholesterol and steroid hormones.
substrate
The molecule that binds to an enzyme's active site and is chemically transformed by the enzyme.
tertiary structure
The overall three-dimensional shape of a polypeptide chain, determined by interactions among R groups.
triglyceride
A lipid formed from glycerol and three fatty acids joined by ester bonds; the primary form of energy storage in animals.
unsaturated fatty acid
A fatty acid containing one or more carbon-carbon double bonds; liquid at room temperature.

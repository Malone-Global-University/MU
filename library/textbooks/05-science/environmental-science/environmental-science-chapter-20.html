Chapter 20 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Every day you experience weather. The temperature when you step outside. Whether you need an umbrella. Whether roads are icy or skies are clear. Weather affects what you wear, how you travel, whether planes fly, whether crops grow, and whether communities face danger.

Weather is not random. It is the result of energy, moisture, pressure differences, and air movement interacting in the atmosphere. When you understand how those ingredients combine, weather stops being a mystery and starts being a system you can analyze, interpret, and predict.

This chapter explains what weather is, how scientists measure and map it, how major storm systems form, and how forecasting works — including what it means when a forecast is uncertain.

Opening Question

A forecaster says there is a 40% chance of thunderstorms tomorrow afternoon. Should you cancel your outdoor plans? What does that number actually mean? And how did the forecaster arrive at it — from satellite images, radar data, and computer models running equations that describe the atmosphere? By the end of this chapter, you will be able to read a weather map, explain how a thunderstorm forms, and interpret a probabilistic forecast with scientific understanding rather than guesswork.

Section 20.1

What Weather Is

Weather is the short-term condition of the atmosphere at a specific place and time. It includes temperature, air pressure, humidity, wind speed, wind direction, precipitation, and cloud cover. Weather is always changing because the atmosphere is always moving. Heat from the Sun drives the movement of air. Differences in temperature and pressure create wind. Water constantly evaporates, condenses, and falls. These processes interact continuously, producing the variety of conditions we call weather.

Weather is different from climate, which describes long-term average patterns of temperature, precipitation, and seasonal conditions in a region. Climate describes what is typical; weather describes what is actually happening right now.

Weather happens in the troposphere, the lowest layer of the atmosphere, extending from the ground to about 12 kilometers above Earth's surface. Almost all water vapor, clouds, precipitation, and weather systems are found in the troposphere. The troposphere is where temperature generally decreases with altitude, creating instability that drives vertical air movement — the engine of weather.

Weather vs. Climate — The Essential Distinction

Weather — what the atmosphere is doing right now at a specific place: "It is raining in Denver today."

Climate — the long-term average of what the atmosphere typically does in a region: "Denver averages 300 sunny days per year."

A single cold winter does not disprove a warming climate. A single hot summer does not prove one. Climate is about decades-long patterns; weather is about today.

Section 20.2

Temperature, Pressure, Humidity, and Wind

Four variables describe atmospheric conditions at any location. Understanding each one is essential before understanding how weather systems form and move.

Temperature

Temperature is the measure of the average kinetic energy of air molecules. Warm air has molecules moving faster; cold air has molecules moving more slowly. Temperature varies with latitude, elevation, season, time of day, proximity to oceans, and cloud cover. The unequal heating of Earth's surface by the Sun drives almost all atmospheric motion. Air over a sun-heated desert heats faster than air over a cool ocean; the resulting temperature differences create pressure differences; pressure differences drive wind.

Air Pressure

Air pressure is the weight of air pressing down on a surface. At sea level, air pressure averages about 1013 millibars (mb). Pressure decreases with altitude because less air is above you. Pressure also varies horizontally because of temperature differences: warm air expands, becomes less dense, and rises, creating low pressure at the surface. Cold air contracts, becomes denser, and sinks, creating high pressure at the surface. These pressure differences are what drive wind.

Humidity

Humidity measures the amount of water vapor in the air. Relative humidity compares how much water vapor is present to the maximum the air could hold at that temperature, expressed as a percentage. When relative humidity reaches 100%, the air is saturated — it cannot hold more water vapor. Dew point is the temperature at which air becomes saturated and condensation begins. When air temperature equals the dew point, clouds, fog, or dew form. Warm air can hold more water vapor than cold air, which is why cooling air often leads to condensation and precipitation.

Wind

Wind is the horizontal movement of air from areas of higher pressure to areas of lower pressure. The strength of wind depends on the pressure gradient — the difference in pressure between two locations divided by the distance between them. A steep pressure gradient produces strong winds. Wind direction is described by where the wind comes from: a north wind blows from the north toward the south.

Earth's rotation deflects moving air through the Coriolis effect, causing winds to curve to the right in the Northern Hemisphere and to the left in the Southern Hemisphere. This shapes large-scale wind patterns and determines the rotation direction of storms — counterclockwise for low pressure systems in the Northern Hemisphere.

Pressure and Weather — A Quick Rule

Falling barometer pressure → rising air → clouds and precipitation likely. Rising barometer pressure → sinking air → clearing skies and fair weather likely. Experienced mariners and farmers have used this relationship for centuries; modern meteorology explains exactly why it works.

Section 20.3

Air Masses

Weather across North America is largely controlled by large bodies of air called air masses — enormous volumes of air that have acquired relatively uniform temperature and humidity characteristics from the surface they form over. An air mass may span thousands of kilometers and extend through much of the troposphere. When an air mass moves into a region, it carries its temperature and moisture characteristics with it, and the weather changes accordingly.

How Air Masses Form and Move

An air mass forms when air remains over a surface region long enough — days to weeks — to take on that region's temperature and moisture properties. A stagnant air mass over a cold, dry Canadian landmass in January becomes cold and dry. One that develops over the warm, humid Gulf of Mexico becomes warm and moist. Once formed, air masses are pushed by upper-level winds and pressure differences into new regions, where they clash with resident air masses and produce frontal weather.

Reading Air Mass Codes

The first letter tells you moisture: c = continental (dry), m = maritime (moist). The second letter tells you temperature: A = Arctic (extremely cold), P = Polar (cold), T = Tropical (warm). So mT = moist and warm — the air mass most responsible for summer thunderstorms in the eastern United States.

Section 20.4

Fronts

When two different air masses meet, they do not mix easily — they differ too much in density and temperature. The boundary between them is called a front. Most of the significant weather events in the middle latitudes — storms, precipitation, temperature changes, wind shifts — occur along fronts. The key to all frontal weather is lifting: fronts force air upward, and rising air produces clouds and precipitation.

Cold Fronts

A cold front forms when cold, dense air pushes into warmer air. Because cold air is denser, it acts as a wedge, sliding under the warm air and forcing it upward sharply. Rapidly rising warm air cools quickly, producing tall cumulonimbus clouds and often intense, short-duration precipitation — frequently thunderstorms along an active cold front. After a cold front passes, temperatures drop, pressure rises, and winds shift, typically from south or southwest to north or northwest. Cold fronts move faster than warm fronts and often produce a narrow but intense band of weather.

Warm Fronts

A warm front forms when warm air advances and rides up gradually over cooler, denser air. Because warm air rises slowly over a long ramp of cold air, clouds and precipitation extend hundreds of kilometers ahead of the surface front. Precipitation ahead of a warm front is typically steady and long-lasting rather than intense and brief. A sequence of cloud types — high cirrus first, then altostratus, then thick nimbostratus — often signals an approaching warm front many hours before it arrives. After it passes, temperatures rise and air becomes more humid.

Stationary and Occluded Fronts

A stationary front forms when neither air mass advances significantly. Clouds and precipitation can persist for several days along stationary fronts, sometimes causing flooding when persistent rain falls over the same area. An occluded front forms when a faster-moving cold front overtakes a warm front, lifting the warm air completely off the surface. Occluded fronts are associated with complex weather patterns and mark the late stage of a mid-latitude storm system.

Section 20.5

Clouds and Precipitation

Clouds are visible evidence of condensation in the atmosphere. They form when moist air rises, expands, and cools to the dew point temperature. At that point, water vapor condenses onto tiny particles called condensation nuclei — dust, pollen, sea salt, and pollution particles that provide surfaces for droplet formation. The resulting droplets are microscopic, far too small to fall as rain. Millions of them together form a cloud visible from the surface.

Cloud Classification

Clouds are classified by altitude and form. The altitude tells you roughly where they are in the troposphere; the form tells you about the air motion that created them.

Reading the Sky — Cloud Sequence at a Warm Front

Watch for this sequence as a warm front approaches from the west: thin wispy cirrus (6–12 hours before) → spreading cirrostratus that creates a halo around the Sun or Moon → thickening altostratus (Sun dims through a gray sheet) → dark nimbostratus with steady rain. Each cloud type tells you that the front is closer.

How Precipitation Forms

Cloud droplets are far too small to fall — they float on the slightest updraft. Precipitation requires droplets to grow large enough that gravity overcomes air resistance. Two main processes accomplish this.

In collision-coalescence, common in warm tropical clouds, droplets of slightly different sizes fall at different rates, collide, and merge. Growing droplets sweep up smaller ones as they fall, eventually becoming large enough to reach the surface as rain.

In the ice crystal process (Bergeron process), common in colder clouds containing both ice crystals and supercooled water droplets, water vapor deposits preferentially onto ice crystals, which grow at the expense of the surrounding droplets. When ice crystals become large enough, they fall — reaching the surface as snow if air below remains below freezing, or melting into rain if they pass through warmer air. Hail forms in strong thunderstorm updrafts when ice pellets cycle repeatedly through supercooled water, adding layers of ice until they are too heavy to be supported.

Doppler Radar

Doppler radar transmits pulses of radio waves that reflect off precipitation particles. By measuring how the frequency of the returning waves shifts — higher frequency from precipitation moving toward the radar, lower from precipitation moving away — the system detects not just where precipitation is, but how fast it is moving and in what direction. This velocity data is what makes it possible to detect the rotating wind patterns inside a supercell thunderstorm, providing tornado warnings before a funnel cloud is even visible. Without Doppler radar, modern tornado warning lead times would be a fraction of what they are today.

Weather Balloons and Satellites

Radiosondes carried by weather balloons provide the vertical profile of the atmosphere — temperature, humidity, and pressure at each altitude — that is essential for forecasting. The National Weather Service launches balloons twice daily from about 100 stations across the U.S. Geostationary satellites orbit at 35,786 km, matching Earth's rotation to remain stationary above one location, providing continuous imagery of weather systems. Polar-orbiting satellites circle the globe at lower altitude, providing higher-resolution data from different locations as they pass over.

Section 20.7

Weather Maps

Weather maps (also called surface analysis maps or synoptic charts) display current atmospheric conditions across a large area simultaneously. Reading a weather map is a fundamental Earth science skill — it allows you to synthesize data from hundreds of observation stations and identify the large-scale patterns shaping weather across a region.

Station Models

At each observation station, data is plotted using a station model — a standardized arrangement of symbols and numbers around a circle. The circle fill indicates cloud cover (empty = clear, fully filled = overcast). A line extends from the circle in the direction the wind is coming from; barbs on this line indicate speed (each full barb = 10 knots, each half barb = 5 knots, a pennant = 50 knots). Temperature appears upper left, dew point lower left. When the two are close together, the air is near saturation and precipitation or fog is likely.

Isobars and Pressure Systems

Isobars are lines connecting points of equal air pressure, drawn at regular intervals (usually every 4 mb). Like contour lines on a topographic map, closely spaced isobars indicate a steep pressure gradient — and stronger winds. Widely spaced isobars indicate gentle pressure gradients and lighter winds.

High pressure systems (H) are centers of relatively high pressure where air sinks and spreads outward. Sinking air warms and dries, generally bringing clear skies and calm conditions. In the Northern Hemisphere, surface winds spiral clockwise out of a high. Low pressure systems (L) are centers where air converges and rises. Rising air cools and forms clouds and precipitation. Northern Hemisphere surface winds spiral counterclockwise into a low.

Worked Example 20.7.1
Reading and interpreting a weather map
  • SituationA surface analysis map shows a low pressure center (992 mb) over the Great Lakes, with isobars at 996, 1000, 1004, and 1008 mb extending outward. A cold front extends southwest from the low; a warm front extends east.
  • PressureThe isobars are closely spaced near the low — steep pressure gradient — indicating strong winds near the storm center. Farther from the low, wider isobar spacing suggests lighter winds.
  • Wind directionIn the Northern Hemisphere, winds spiral counterclockwise into a low. South of the low: winds from the south (warm sector). North of the low: winds from the north (cold air behind cold front).
  • Frontal weatherAlong the cold front to the southwest: intense, narrow band of thunderstorms likely. East of the warm front: steady rain or drizzle extending well ahead of the frontal boundary, with a sequence from cirrus to nimbostratus.
  • ForecastAs the system moves east, a city ahead of the cold front will first experience warm, humid southerly winds — then a line of intense storms as the cold front passes — then clearing and cooler northwest winds behind it.
Key insight: The position of the low, the fronts, and the isobar spacing together reveal the current weather and the sequence of changes to come as the system moves.

Section 20.8

Thunderstorms and Severe Weather

How Thunderstorms Form

Thunderstorms form when warm, moist, unstable air rises rapidly. Three conditions are required: sufficient moisture (water vapor in the lower atmosphere), a lifting mechanism (a front, daytime surface heating, mountains, or converging winds), and atmospheric instability (air that continues to rise once lifted because it remains warmer than the surrounding environment).

As air rises in a thunderstorm, water vapor condenses and releases latent heat, warming the rising air further and accelerating the updraft. This self-reinforcing process builds the storm rapidly. A mature cumulonimbus cloud may extend from near the surface to 15 kilometers or more, with the top spreading into a characteristic anvil shape as it hits the stable stratosphere and can no longer rise.

Three Conditions for Thunderstorm Development

1. Moisture — sufficient water vapor in the lower atmosphere to fuel latent heat release

2. Lift — a mechanism to force air upward: fronts, surface heating, terrain, or converging winds

3. Instability — atmospheric profile where a lifted parcel remains warmer (less dense) than surrounding air and continues to rise on its own

Remove any one of these and the storm cannot develop or sustain itself.

Lightning and Thunder

Inside a thunderstorm, collisions between ice crystals and water droplets in the churning updraft separate electrical charges. Negative charges accumulate near the cloud base; positive charges concentrate near the top and on the ground below. When the electrical potential difference becomes large enough, a sudden discharge — lightning — equalizes the charge. A single lightning bolt can heat the surrounding air to about 30,000°C — five times hotter than the surface of the Sun — in a fraction of a second. The explosive expansion of that superheated air produces the sound wave we hear as thunder. Because light travels nearly instantaneously but sound travels about 340 meters per second, you can estimate the distance to a lightning strike by counting the seconds between the flash and the thunder, then dividing by 3 to get approximate kilometers.

Hail and Flash Floods

Hail forms only in strong thunderstorms with powerful updrafts that can keep ice pellets aloft. As they cycle through regions of supercooled liquid water, layers of ice accumulate around a central nucleus. When hailstones grow too heavy for the updraft to support, they fall. Large hailstones have concentric rings of clear and opaque ice, recording each cycle through the cloud. Hailstones larger than 2.5 cm (golf ball-size) can cause serious damage to crops, vehicles, and structures.

Flash floods are among the deadliest thunderstorm hazards — often more deadly than lightning or hail. Heavy rainfall over a short period overwhelms stream channels and drainage systems. Flash floods can develop within minutes in valleys, canyons, and urban areas where pavement prevents infiltration. Moving water only 15 cm deep can knock a person off their feet; 60 cm can carry away a vehicle. The danger is speed: flash flood waters can rise several meters in minutes.

Severe Thunderstorm Criteria

A thunderstorm is classified as severe by the National Weather Service if it produces hail 2.5 cm (1 inch) in diameter or larger, wind gusts of 93 km/h (58 mph) or greater, or a tornado. Severe thunderstorm warnings provide critical safety information for people outdoors, driving, or in vulnerable structures.

Section 20.9

Hurricanes, Tornadoes, and Winter Storms

Hurricanes

A hurricane (called a typhoon in the western Pacific or tropical cyclone in the Indian Ocean) is a large rotating storm system that forms over warm tropical ocean water. Hurricanes require warm ocean water of at least 26–27°C to depths of at least 50 meters, high humidity in the lower and middle atmosphere, and low wind shear (little change in wind speed or direction with altitude) so the storm can organize vertically without being torn apart.

As warm ocean water evaporates intensely and rises, it releases enormous amounts of latent heat — the energy that fuels the storm. Air spirals inward at the surface in a counterclockwise direction (Northern Hemisphere), rises rapidly near the center, and spreads outward at high altitude. The storm is a heat engine, converting the thermal energy of the warm ocean into organized kinetic energy of wind.

Storm Surge — The Deadliest Hurricane Hazard

Storm surge is a dome of ocean water pushed ashore by hurricane winds. It is the leading cause of hurricane deaths. A Category 4 storm approaching a gently sloping coastline can push a wall of water 4–6 meters above normal tide level inland for kilometers. Storm surge is not proportional to wind category alone — storm size, forward speed, coastline shape, and angle of approach all matter. A slow-moving Category 2 can produce worse flooding than a fast-moving Category 4.

Tornadoes

A tornado is a violently rotating column of air extending from a thunderstorm to the ground. Most significant tornadoes are produced by supercell thunderstorms — large, long-lived organized thunderstorms with a persistent rotating updraft called a mesocyclone.

Tornadoes form when wind shear in the atmosphere — winds that increase in speed and/or change direction with altitude — causes horizontal spinning air tubes to be tilted vertically by a thunderstorm updraft. If conditions are right, this rotation tightens and intensifies into a visible funnel cloud extending from the cloud base. When the funnel contacts the ground, it becomes a tornado. Doppler radar can detect the rotation signatures of mesocyclones, enabling tornado warnings to be issued before a tornado touches down — on average, 13 minutes of lead time with modern technology.

Tornado Alley — spanning parts of Texas, Oklahoma, Kansas, and Nebraska — has the world's highest frequency of strong tornadoes due to the frequent collision of warm moist Gulf air with cold dry continental polar air across a flat landscape that allows both air masses to interact freely without terrain interference.

Winter Storms

Winter storms bring hazardous combinations of snow, ice, and wind. Nor'easters are powerful low pressure systems that develop along the eastern coast of North America, drawing moisture from the Atlantic Ocean and cold air from the north, sometimes producing heavy snow from the Mid-Atlantic states into New England. A blizzard is defined by the National Weather Service as a storm with sustained winds or gusts of 56 km/h or greater, considerable falling or blowing snow, and visibility frequently reduced to 400 meters or less for three or more hours. Wind chill — the combined cooling effect of cold temperatures and wind — accelerates heat loss from the body and poses serious hypothermia risk even at temperatures above freezing.

Ice storms occur when rain falls through a layer of below-freezing air near the surface, creating freezing rain that coats surfaces with ice. Accumulation of 6 mm or more causes significant damage to trees and power lines and makes roads extremely hazardous. Lake-effect snow forms when cold air moves over relatively warm Great Lakes water, picking up moisture and heat and depositing intense, narrow bands of snow downwind of the lakes — sometimes several meters of snow in a single season at downwind locations.

Section 20.10

Weather Forecasting and Uncertainty

How Modern Forecasting Works

Modern weather forecasting relies on massive amounts of observational data collected from surface stations, weather balloons, aircraft, ships, ocean buoys, Doppler radar networks, and satellites around the world. This data is fed into numerical weather prediction (NWP) models — complex computer programs that divide the atmosphere into a three-dimensional grid and solve mathematical equations describing the physics of atmospheric motion: fluid dynamics, thermodynamics, radiative transfer, and water vapor phase changes.

Major models include the U.S. Global Forecast System (GFS), which provides global coverage updated four times daily, and the European Centre for Medium-Range Weather Forecasts (ECMWF) model, widely regarded as the most accurate for medium-range forecasting. Human forecasters analyze model output, compare multiple models, consult local observations, and apply knowledge of local terrain effects to produce the final forecast.

Why Forecasts Have Limits

Weather forecasting has improved dramatically over the past 50 years — a 5-day forecast today is roughly as accurate as a 3-day forecast was in 1980. But it cannot achieve perfect accuracy, for reasons that are built into the nature of the atmosphere itself.

The atmosphere is a chaotic system: small differences in initial conditions grow into large differences in outcomes over time. This is sometimes called the butterfly effect — in theory, the atmospheric disturbance from a butterfly flapping its wings could eventually affect the path of a storm thousands of kilometers away weeks later. In practice, errors in measuring initial atmospheric conditions, no matter how small or how dense the observation network, grow until they overwhelm the forecast. This imposes a hard practical limit on deterministic forecasting of roughly 10–14 days.

Additional uncertainty comes from observation gaps over oceans and remote regions, and from model limitations — even the best NWP models approximate the real atmosphere, and processes smaller than the model grid spacing must be estimated rather than directly calculated.

Probability in Forecasting

A probability of precipitation (PoP) — "40% chance of rain" — does not mean it will rain 40% of the time, or that 40% of the forecast area will receive rain. It means there is a 40% chance that at least a measurable amount of rain will fall at any given point in the forecast area during the forecast period. A 40% chance is not a dismissal — it means rain is nearly as likely as not, and preparation is reasonable.

Ensemble forecasting improves on single-model forecasts by running many slightly different versions of a model, each starting with slightly different initial conditions. The spread of the ensemble results gives forecasters a direct measure of forecast confidence. When all ensemble members converge on the same solution, confidence is high and the forecast is reliable. When they diverge widely — different storm tracks, different temperature outcomes — uncertainty is high and the forecast should be interpreted cautiously.

Worked Example 20.10.1
Interpreting a probability of precipitation forecast
  • Scenario"Tuesday afternoon: 60% chance of thunderstorms. Tuesday night: 20% chance of showers."
  • Tuesday PMA 60% PoP means that at any given point in the forecast area, there is a 60% probability of measurable rainfall during the afternoon. This is not a near-certain event (that would be 80–90%), but it is more likely than not. An outdoor event should have a contingency plan.
  • Tuesday nightA 20% PoP means that at any given point, there is only a 20% chance of measurable rain overnight — this is possible but unlikely. An outdoor event late Tuesday night is probably fine but not guaranteed.
  • Key pointNeither a 60% forecast nor a 20% forecast is the forecaster "guessing." Both are probabilistic statements grounded in numerical model output and forecaster experience. The 60% could verify as rain or not rain — and either outcome is consistent with the forecast.
Interpreting PoP correctly: it is the probability of measurable precipitation at a specific point — not the fraction of time, not the fraction of area, and not a certainty threshold.

Weather Forecasting and Society

Improved forecasting saves lives. Average tornado warning lead times increased from near zero in the 1970s to about 13 minutes today. Hurricane track forecasts have improved so much that evacuation orders can now be issued for the right areas several days in advance. Flash flood guidance helps emergency managers decide when to issue warnings before flooding begins.

Beyond life safety, weather forecasting serves agriculture (frost warnings, planting decisions), aviation (routing around storms, turbulence avoidance), shipping (routing forecasts for vessels), construction (wind and precipitation planning), utilities (demand forecasting), and countless daily decisions. Understanding that forecasts carry inherent uncertainty — and knowing how to interpret probabilistic language accurately — is a basic component of scientific and civic literacy in the modern world.

Chapter 20 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 20.1Weather is the short-term condition of the atmosphere at a specific place and time, distinct from climate (long-term averages). Weather occurs in the troposphere, driven by unequal solar heating of Earth's surface.
  • Sec 20.2Four variables describe atmospheric conditions: temperature (average molecular kinetic energy), air pressure (weight of air; creates pressure gradients that drive wind), relative humidity (water vapor as a percentage of maximum), and wind (air movement from high to low pressure, deflected by the Coriolis effect).
  • Sec 20.3Air masses are large volumes of air with uniform temperature and humidity. They are classified by source region temperature (Arctic, Polar, Tropical) and moisture (continental = dry, maritime = moist). When air masses move into new regions, weather changes accordingly.
  • Sec 20.4Fronts are boundaries between air masses. Cold fronts produce rapid lifting and intense but short storms. Warm fronts produce gradual lifting and widespread steady precipitation. Stationary fronts persist for days. Occluded fronts mark the late stage of a storm cycle. All frontal weather is driven by lifting that cools air to its dew point.
  • Sec 20.5Clouds form when rising air cools to the dew point and water vapor condenses on condensation nuclei. Cloud classification uses altitude (high, middle, low, vertical) and form (cumulus, stratus, cirrus, nimbus). Precipitation grows via collision-coalescence in warm clouds or the ice crystal process in cold clouds.
  • Sec 20.6Standard instruments measure each atmospheric variable. Doppler radar detects not just precipitation location but wind motion inside storms, enabling tornado warnings. Radiosondes carried by weather balloons measure the vertical atmospheric profile. Satellites provide continuous large-scale coverage of cloud systems.
  • Sec 20.7Weather maps use station models (local observations), isobars (equal-pressure lines), and front symbols to display conditions across large areas. High pressure brings sinking, clear air; low pressure brings rising, cloudy air. Isobar spacing indicates pressure gradient and wind strength.
  • Sec 20.8Thunderstorms require moisture, lift, and instability. Latent heat from condensation powers updrafts. Charge separation causes lightning; thunder is the acoustic signature. Hail forms in strong updrafts; flash floods are among the deadliest thunderstorm hazards. Severe criteria: hail ≥2.5 cm, wind ≥93 km/h, or tornado.
  • Sec 20.9Hurricanes are fueled by warm ocean evaporation; storm surge is their deadliest hazard. Tornadoes form in supercell mesocyclones; Doppler radar provides warning. The EF scale rates tornado damage. Winter storms include nor'easters, blizzards (high winds, low visibility, heavy snow), ice storms (freezing rain), and lake-effect snow.
  • Sec 20.10Numerical weather prediction models feed global observational data into physics equations to forecast the atmosphere. Forecasts are highly reliable at 1–3 days; uncertainty grows beyond 7–10 days because the atmosphere is chaotic. Ensemble forecasting quantifies uncertainty. Probability of precipitation is a point-specific probability, not a fraction of time or area.

Key Terms Review

weather
Short-term atmospheric conditions (temperature, pressure, humidity, wind, precipitation) at a specific place and time.
climate
Long-term average pattern of weather conditions in a region over decades.
air pressure
Weight of air above a unit of surface area; decreases with altitude; drives horizontal wind when it varies.
relative humidity
Ratio of actual water vapor in the air to the maximum it could hold at that temperature; expressed as a percentage.
dew point
Temperature at which air becomes saturated and condensation begins; air temperature = dew point means 100% relative humidity.
Coriolis effect
Deflection of moving air to the right in the Northern Hemisphere and left in the Southern Hemisphere, caused by Earth's rotation.
pressure gradient
Difference in pressure between two locations divided by the distance between them; steeper gradient = stronger wind.
air mass
Large volume of air with relatively uniform temperature and humidity acquired from its source region over days to weeks.
front
Boundary between two air masses with different temperature and humidity properties; most significant weather occurs along fronts.
cold front
Boundary where advancing cold air undercuts warmer air; produces rapid lifting, intense short-duration storms, and post-frontal temperature drop.
warm front
Boundary where advancing warm air rises gradually over cooler air; produces widespread clouds and steady precipitation extending hundreds of km ahead.
condensation nuclei
Tiny particles (dust, salt, pollen) on which water vapor condenses to form cloud droplets.
isobar
Line on a weather map connecting points of equal atmospheric pressure; closely spaced = steep gradient = strong winds.
station model
Standardized set of symbols at a weather observation point on a synoptic chart; records temperature, dew point, wind, pressure, cloud cover, and current weather.
high pressure system
Region of above-average pressure with sinking, diverging air; brings clear skies and calm weather in the Northern Hemisphere; winds spiral clockwise outward.
low pressure system
Region of below-average pressure with rising, converging air; associated with clouds and precipitation; winds spiral counterclockwise inward (Northern Hemisphere).
Doppler radar
Radar system that detects precipitation location, intensity, and the velocity of precipitation — enabling detection of storm rotation and tornado warning.
latent heat
Energy absorbed or released when water changes phase; condensation releases heat that powers thunderstorm and hurricane updrafts.
flash flood
Rapid flooding caused by heavy rainfall in a short period; develops in minutes to hours; one of the deadliest weather hazards.
hurricane
Large rotating tropical storm with sustained winds above 119 km/h, fueled by warm ocean evaporation; also called typhoon or tropical cyclone.
storm surge
Dome of ocean water pushed ashore by hurricane winds; the leading cause of hurricane deaths; not proportional to wind category alone.
tornado
Violently rotating column of air extending from a thunderstorm to the ground; most formed in supercell mesocyclones; rated EF0–EF5.
supercell
Large, long-lived organized thunderstorm with a persistent rotating updraft (mesocyclone); primary producer of significant tornadoes.
mesocyclone
Rotating updraft within a supercell thunderstorm; precursor to tornado formation; detectable by Doppler radar velocity products.
blizzard
Winter storm with sustained winds ≥56 km/h, considerable snow, and visibility ≤400 m for three or more hours.
numerical weather prediction
Use of mathematical models and computers to solve atmospheric physics equations and forecast future weather conditions.
ensemble forecasting
Running multiple model versions with slightly different initial conditions; spread of results measures forecast confidence and uncertainty.
probability of precipitation
Chance that measurable precipitation will occur at a specific point during the forecast period; not a fraction of time or area.
wind shear
Change in wind speed and/or direction with altitude; important for tornado formation in supercells; destructive to hurricanes.
lake-effect snow
Intense localized snowfall downwind of large lakes; cold air picks up moisture and heat from warm lake water and drops it as snow on the downwind shore.

Map Review

Isobar map interpretation. On a sample isobar map, practice: identifying high and low pressure centers; estimating wind direction at multiple locations (counterclockwise into lows, clockwise out of highs in the Northern Hemisphere); comparing isobar spacing to identify where winds are strongest; locating frontal boundaries and predicting weather on either side.

Hurricane track and uncertainty cone maps. The National Hurricane Center issues forecast track maps showing the predicted storm center path and a widening cone of possible positions — not a cone of damage. The cone represents forecast uncertainty, which grows with time. Hazards extend well beyond the cone: storm surge, rainfall flooding, and tornadoes can affect areas entirely outside the predicted path. Practice: given a track map, identify the most likely landfall location, the range of possible landfall locations, and what hazards coastal areas outside the cone might still experience.

Radar interpretation. On a radar reflectivity image, higher-intensity colors (yellows, reds, purples in standard coloring) indicate heavier precipitation. Storm motion vectors show where cells are moving. On velocity (Doppler) images, opposing inbound and outbound velocity zones close together inside a storm indicate rotation — a signature of mesocyclone activity requiring immediate attention from forecasters.

Worked Example Review

Example 20.A
Calculating pressure gradient and estimating wind strength
  • ProblemLocation A has a pressure of 1020 mb. Location B, 400 km away, has a pressure of 1000 mb. Calculate the pressure gradient and assess wind strength relative to a gradient of 0.02 mb/km.
  • Calculate Pressure gradient = (1020 − 1000 mb) ÷ 400 km = 20 ÷ 400 = 0.05 mb/km
  • Interpret0.05 mb/km is 2.5 times steeper than the comparison value of 0.02 mb/km. This pressure gradient would produce substantially stronger winds — likely gusty to strong winds rather than light breezes.
  • CheckThe steeper the pressure gradient (more mb per km), the stronger the wind. This relationship holds across all scales from sea breezes to hurricane-force winds.
Pressure gradient = 0.05 mb/km — a steep gradient associated with strong wind conditions.
Example 20.B
Reading a station model
  • ProblemA station model shows: temperature = 18°C (upper left), dew point = 16°C (lower left), wind barb pointing from the southwest with 2 full barbs (upper right of circle shows 1016 mb), circle is half filled.
  • ReadTemperature: 18°C. Dew point: 16°C. Temperature–dew point spread = 2°C — very small, indicating air is nearly saturated. Wind: from the southwest at approximately 20 knots (2 full barbs × 10 knots each). Pressure: 1016 mb. Cloud cover: half the sky covered.
  • InterpretThe near-equal temperature and dew point suggest fog, low clouds, or precipitation is possible soon. A southwest wind suggests warm, moist air advancing — possibly ahead of an approaching warm front.
Near-saturation conditions; southwest wind at 20 knots; half-cloud cover; approaching weather change likely.

Common Misconceptions

Chapter 20 — Common Misconceptions
"A Category 5 hurricane is always more dangerous than a Category 3."

Corrected: Wind category is one factor, not the only one. Storm size, forward speed, track angle relative to the coastline, and bathymetry (seafloor depth) all affect storm surge height. A slow-moving Category 2 can produce worse rainfall flooding and higher surge than a fast-moving Category 4 at certain locations. Evacuation decisions must consider all hazards, not just the wind category.

"The Coriolis effect determines which direction water drains in a bathtub — north of the equator it drains clockwise."

Corrected: The Coriolis effect operates at large scales — hundreds to thousands of kilometers. On the scale of a bathtub (less than 2 meters), the Coriolis effect is negligible — billions of times smaller than the forces from water agitation, the shape of the drain, or how the tub was filled. Bathtub drain direction is determined by local conditions, not hemisphere. Only large weather systems like hurricanes and high/low pressure centers rotate due to Coriolis deflection.

"Lightning never strikes the same place twice."

Corrected: Lightning frequently strikes the same location repeatedly, especially tall structures. The Empire State Building is struck about 20–25 times per year. Lightning follows the path of least electrical resistance, and features that attract one strike — height, conductivity, isolation — attract subsequent ones. Lightning rods work precisely by repeatedly conducting lightning safely to the ground.

"Tornadoes are attracted to mobile home parks and avoid sturdy brick buildings."

Corrected: Tornadoes do not seek out targets. Mobile homes are more frequently hit in statistical records because they are common in tornado-prone areas of the central United States and because their light construction is far less tornado-resistant than permanent structures. Brick buildings can and do suffer catastrophic damage from strong tornadoes. The perceived pattern reflects geography and building vulnerability, not tornado preference.

"Opening windows during a tornado equalizes pressure and protects the house."

Corrected: This is false and potentially dangerous. The primary cause of building damage in tornadoes is not pressure difference but the direct force of extreme winds on the structure and flying debris impact. Time spent opening windows is time not spent taking shelter in the lowest, most interior part of the building — which is where you need to be. Go to shelter immediately; forget the windows.

Chapter 22 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

This chapter explains the relationships among Earth, the Moon, and the Sun. These relationships are not abstract astronomy — they are the direct causes of day and night, the year, the seasons, ocean tides, Moon phases, and the supply of solar energy that drives every weather system, ocean current, and ecosystem on Earth. Students who understand how Earth moves, why seasons occur, and why the Moon's gravity raises tides will be better equipped to understand climate, ecology, and the energy systems studied throughout this textbook.

Chapter 21 examined climate and climate change. Part of that story depended on how solar energy reaches Earth and how orbital geometry shapes long-term temperature patterns. Chapter 22 builds the astronomical foundation behind those ideas. Chapter 23 will extend the view outward to the full solar system.

Opening Question

If someone asked you why it is cold in January and warm in July, you might say “because Earth is farther from the Sun in winter.” That explanation is wrong — and it is the most common misconception in astronomy. Earth is actually slightly closer to the Sun in January than in July. So why are Northern Hemisphere winters cold? By the end of this chapter, you will be able to explain the real answer, connect it to latitude, sunlight angle, and day length, and apply that reasoning to any point on Earth in any season.

Earth in Motion: Rotation, Revolution, and Seasons
Earth in Motion · Day-Night Cycles · The Year · Why Seasons Occur
Section 22.1

Earth in Motion

Earth moves in two distinct ways: it spins on its own axis, and it travels in a nearly circular path around the Sun. These two motions — rotation and revolution — are the foundation of nearly every pattern studied in this chapter.

Rotation is the spinning of Earth on its own axis. Earth's axis is an imaginary line running from the North Pole through the center of the planet to the South Pole. Earth completes one full rotation approximately every 24 hours, and this rotation is responsible for day and night. Rotation also controls wind patterns, ocean currents, and the Coriolis effect studied in earlier chapters.

Revolution is Earth's orbital motion around the Sun. Earth completes one full orbit in approximately 365.25 days — the basis of the calendar year. The orbit is an ellipse, meaning Earth is not always at the same distance from the Sun. The point of closest approach (around January 3) is called perihelion, roughly 147 million km from the Sun. The farthest point (around July 4) is called aphelion, roughly 152 million km away. The difference is only about 3%, and as the opening question established, this distance variation is not the cause of the seasons.

Earth's axis is not perpendicular to its orbital plane. It is tilted at approximately 23.5° from vertical relative to that plane. This tilt is the key fact that causes seasons, and it remains pointed in the same direction in space (toward the North Star, Polaris) throughout the entire year. As Earth orbits the Sun, the Northern Hemisphere is tilted toward the Sun during half the year and away from it during the other half.

The combination of rotation and revolution creates the rhythmic patterns of day, year, season, and climate that all living systems have adapted to over billions of years.

Two Motions of Earth

Rotation — spinning on the axis — period: ~24 hours — causes: day and night, Coriolis effect.

Revolution — orbiting the Sun — period: ~365.25 days — causes: the year, seasons (combined with axial tilt).

Axial tilt — 23.5° from perpendicular to orbital plane — direction: fixed toward Polaris throughout year — causes: seasons.

Section 22.2

Rotation and Day-Night Cycles

As Earth rotates from west to east, different parts of its surface face the Sun at different times. The half of Earth facing the Sun is in daylight; the half facing away is in darkness. The boundary between these two regions is called the circle of illumination.

A solar day is the time from one solar noon to the next — the time it takes for the Sun to return to the same position in the sky. This averages 24 hours, though it varies slightly across the year due to Earth's elliptical orbit. A sidereal day is the time it takes Earth to rotate exactly 360° relative to distant stars — approximately 23 hours and 56 minutes. The 4-minute difference exists because Earth is also moving in its orbit, so it must rotate slightly more than 360° for the Sun to return to the same apparent position.

The rate of Earth's rotation means that the Sun appears to move across the sky from east to west at roughly 15° per hour. This apparent motion is entirely due to Earth's rotation, not the Sun's movement. Observers in the Northern Hemisphere see the Sun cross the southern part of the sky; those in the Southern Hemisphere see it cross the northern sky; observers near the equator see it pass nearly overhead.

At the poles, the geometry of day and night becomes extreme. For six months of the year, one pole is in continuous daylight while the other is in continuous darkness. At the Arctic and Antarctic Circles (66.5°N and 66.5°S), at least one day per year has either 24 hours of daylight or 24 hours of darkness. These patterns have profound effects on ecosystems at high latitudes.

Why Sidereal and Solar Days Differ

Think of it this way: Earth must rotate slightly more than one full turn each day to bring the Sun back to the same sky position, because during that rotation Earth has also moved along its orbit. That extra ~4 minutes per day adds up to one extra complete rotation over the course of a year — which is why there are 366 sidereal days in a year but only 365 solar days.

Section 22.3

Revolution and the Year

Earth's orbital period — the time to complete one full revolution around the Sun — is 365.2422 days. Because the calendar must use whole days, we use a 365-day year and add a leap year (366 days) every four years to account for the accumulated quarter-day. This calendar correction, introduced by Julius Caesar and refined by Pope Gregory XIII, is the basis of the Gregorian calendar used worldwide today.

The shape of Earth's orbit is an ellipse with the Sun at one focus, as described by Kepler's First Law. However, the orbit is very nearly circular — the eccentricity is only about 0.017, meaning the farthest and closest points differ by roughly 5 million km out of 150 million km. For most Earth-science purposes, the orbit can be treated as circular.

Earth's revolution speed is not constant. When Earth is closer to the Sun (around perihelion in January), it moves faster — about 30.3 km/s. When it is farther away (around aphelion in July), it moves slightly slower — about 29.3 km/s. This variation, described by Kepler's Second Law, means that the seasons in the Northern Hemisphere are not exactly equal in length. Northern Hemisphere summer (from March equinox to September equinox) is slightly longer than Northern Hemisphere winter because Earth moves more slowly when it is at aphelion during Northern summer.

Section 22.4

Why Seasons Occur

Seasons are caused by the tilt of Earth's axis relative to its orbital plane, combined with Earth's revolution around the Sun. The tilt remains fixed in direction throughout the year, so as Earth orbits, first one hemisphere and then the other is tilted toward the Sun.

When the Northern Hemisphere is tilted toward the Sun: the Sun is higher in the Northern Hemisphere sky and its rays strike at a steeper angle; a steeper angle means more solar energy per square meter; days are longer in the Northern Hemisphere, giving more hours of sunlight. Both effects combine to produce summer in the Northern Hemisphere. At the same time, the Southern Hemisphere is tilted away from the Sun, receiving sunlight at a shallower angle and experiencing shorter days — producing Southern Hemisphere winter.

Angle of incidence is the key concept. When sunlight strikes a surface at a high angle (close to perpendicular), the energy is concentrated in a small area and heating is intense. When sunlight strikes at a low angle, the same amount of energy spreads over a larger area and heating is less intense. The insolation — the amount of solar radiation received per unit area — is much higher when the Sun is high in the sky than when it is low.

The Tropics (between 23.5°N and 23.5°S) are the only latitudes where the Sun can appear directly overhead. Solar heating is most intense in this zone throughout the year, driving the tropical circulation patterns that distribute heat toward the poles through winds and ocean currents studied in Chapters 19 and 20.

The Arctic and Antarctic Circles (at 66.5°N and 66.5°S) mark the boundaries of polar day and polar night. North of the Arctic Circle, the Sun does not rise on the winter solstice and does not set on the summer solstice.

Common Misconception — Distance and Seasons

Students frequently believe seasons are caused by varying distance from the Sun. The evidence against this is direct: (1) Earth is closest to the Sun in January — Northern Hemisphere winter, not summer. (2) Both hemispheres experience opposite seasons simultaneously — if distance were the cause, both would have summer at the same time. (3) The 3% variation in Earth-Sun distance produces only a small change in solar energy received, far less than the 50% or more variation in insolation caused by tilt.

Worked Example 22.4.1
Reasoning about seasonal insolation by angle of incidence
  • ProblemAt the equinox, the Sun is directly overhead at the equator (angle of incidence = 0° from vertical). At 60°N, sunlight arrives at 60° from vertical. Compare the intensity of sunlight per square meter at 60°N relative to the equator.
  • ThinkSunlight arriving at an angle spreads over a larger area. The intensity ratio equals the cosine of the angle from vertical (the zenith angle).
  • Calculate Equator (0° from vertical): cos(0°) = 1.00 → 100% of maximum 60°N (60° from vertical): cos(60°) = 0.50 → 50% of maximum
  • InterpretAt 60°N on the equinox, each square meter of ground receives only half as much solar energy per unit time as the equator does. This is why high latitudes are fundamentally cooler, even before accounting for day length.
Answer: Insolation at 60°N is 50% of equatorial insolation on the equinox.

Section 22.5

The Moon's Orbit

The Moon is Earth's only natural satellite and the largest relative to its planet of any moon in the solar system. Its average distance from Earth is about 384,400 km, though this varies because the Moon's orbit is elliptical. The closest point (perigee) is about 357,000 km; the farthest (apogee) is about 406,000 km.

The Moon orbits Earth in the same direction that Earth rotates — counterclockwise when viewed from above the North Pole. Its orbital period is approximately 27.3 days relative to the stars (sidereal period). However, because Earth is also moving in its orbit around the Sun, the Moon must travel a bit more than 360° to return to the same phase relative to the Sun. The synodic period — the time from one new moon to the next — is approximately 29.5 days. This is the basis of the lunar month.

Synchronous rotation is one of the most important features of the Moon's motion. The Moon rotates on its own axis in exactly the same time it takes to orbit Earth — about 27.3 days. As a result, the same hemisphere of the Moon always faces Earth. An observer on Earth never sees the Moon's far side with the naked eye. This synchronized rotation is not a coincidence; it results from billions of years of tidal friction. Earth's gravitational pull on the Moon gradually slowed the Moon's rotation until it matched its orbital period — a state called tidal locking.

The Moon's orbit is tilted about 5° relative to Earth's orbital plane (the ecliptic). This tilt is why eclipses do not occur every month — the Moon usually passes slightly above or below the Sun-Earth line. Eclipses only occur when the Moon crosses the ecliptic plane near a new or full moon.

Section 22.6

Moon Phases

The Moon does not generate its own light — it reflects sunlight. As the Moon orbits Earth, different amounts of its sunlit half are visible from Earth, producing the cycle of lunar phases. The phases are not caused by Earth's shadow on the Moon (that is an eclipse). They are caused by the changing geometry of the Moon's position relative to Earth and the Sun. At any given time, exactly half the Moon is lit by the Sun. What changes is how much of that lit half faces Earth.

Common Error — Moon Phases vs. Eclipses

Moon phases occur because we see different portions of the Moon's sunlit half as the Moon orbits Earth. They have nothing to do with Earth's shadow. A lunar eclipse occurs when Earth's shadow falls on the Moon — which only happens at full moon and only when the Moon is aligned at an orbital node.

Waxing vs. Waning

Waxing means the illuminated portion is growing (new moon toward full moon). Waning means it is shrinking (full moon toward new moon). In the Northern Hemisphere, a waxing crescent shows light on the right (western) side; a waning crescent shows light on the left (eastern) side. A useful memory aid: the letter D looks like a waxing crescent; the letter C looks like a waning crescent. The full cycle of phases takes approximately 29.5 days — the synodic month.

Section 22.7

Solar and Lunar Eclipses

An eclipse occurs when one celestial body moves into the shadow of another. Because the Moon's orbit is tilted 5° relative to Earth's orbital plane, the Moon usually passes slightly above or below the Sun-Earth line. Eclipses only occur when the Moon crosses the plane of Earth's orbit (at points called nodes) during new moon or full moon. Eclipse seasons occur approximately every 173 days, when the nodes align with the Sun.

Solar Eclipses

A solar eclipse occurs during a new moon when the Moon passes directly between Earth and the Sun, casting a shadow on Earth's surface. The shadow has two regions:

The umbra is the cone of complete shadow. Observers within the umbra experience a total solar eclipse — the Moon completely blocks the Sun's disk. Totality lasts only a few minutes at most and is visible only within a narrow path (often less than 270 km wide) as Earth rotates beneath the shadow.

The penumbra is the region of partial shadow surrounding the umbra. Observers in the penumbra see a partial solar eclipse — only part of the Sun is blocked.

When the Moon is near apogee (its farthest point from Earth), it appears slightly smaller than the Sun and cannot completely cover it, even when perfectly aligned. This produces an annular eclipse — a ring of sunlight remains visible around the Moon's silhouette. Total solar eclipses are among the most dramatic natural events visible from Earth, occurring because of a remarkable coincidence: the Sun is about 400 times larger than the Moon, but also about 400 times farther away, making the two appear nearly identical in angular size from Earth's surface.

Lunar Eclipses

A lunar eclipse occurs during a full moon when Earth passes between the Sun and the Moon, casting Earth's shadow onto the Moon. Lunar eclipses are visible from any location where the Moon is above the horizon — roughly half of Earth's surface simultaneously. During a total lunar eclipse, the Moon moves entirely into Earth's umbra and turns a deep red or orange color. This red color is caused by sunlight refracted through Earth's atmosphere onto the Moon — essentially, the Moon is lit by the light of all of Earth's sunrises and sunsets simultaneously. During a partial lunar eclipse, only part of the Moon enters the umbra.

Section 22.8

Tides and the Moon

Tides are the periodic rise and fall of sea level caused primarily by the Moon's gravitational pull on Earth. The Sun also influences tides but with less than half the tidal force of the Moon, because tidal force depends on the difference in gravitational pull across an object — and the Moon, though much less massive than the Sun, is close enough to produce a greater differential pull across Earth's diameter.

The Moon's gravity pulls on every part of Earth, but it does not pull equally. The side of Earth closest to the Moon feels a stronger pull than Earth's center, and Earth's center feels a stronger pull than the far side. This difference in gravitational force across Earth's diameter is the tidal force. The result is that ocean water on the Moon-facing side is pulled toward the Moon, creating a tidal bulge on that side. On the opposite side, the solid Earth is pulled toward the Moon slightly more than the ocean water there, leaving that water behind — creating a second tidal bulge on the far side.

Earth therefore has two tidal bulges — one facing the Moon and one facing away. As Earth rotates beneath these bulges (which remain pointed roughly toward and away from the Moon), most coastal locations experience two high tides and two low tides per day. Because the Moon also moves in its orbit during that day, the tidal cycle is approximately 24 hours and 50 minutes rather than exactly 24 hours, so high tides occur about 50 minutes later each day.

Spring and Neap Tides

Spring tides occur when the Sun, Earth, and Moon are aligned — at new moon and full moon. During spring tides, the solar and lunar tidal forces reinforce each other, producing the highest high tides and the lowest low tides — the greatest tidal range. Spring tides have nothing to do with the spring season.

Neap tides occur when the Moon is at first or third quarter — when the Sun, Earth, and Moon form a right angle. The solar and lunar tidal forces partially cancel each other, producing smaller tidal ranges.

Case Study 22.8
The Bay of Fundy — Extreme Tidal Range
  • SituationThe Bay of Fundy in Nova Scotia, Canada, has the world's highest tidal range — up to 17 meters. The open Atlantic Ocean nearby has tides of about 1 meter. Both locations are subject to the same lunar and solar tidal forces.
  • ExplanationTidal range depends not only on gravitational forces but on the resonant geometry of the water body. The Bay of Fundy is shaped so that its natural period of oscillation (the time for a wave to travel from the open end to the closed end and back) closely matches the 12.4-hour tidal period. This resonance amplifies the tide, much as pushing a swing at its natural frequency amplifies its motion.
  • SignificanceThe Bay of Fundy's extreme tides have shaped a unique ecosystem. Vast mudflats are exposed at low tide, providing critical shorebird feeding habitat. Tidal currents mix nutrients throughout the water column, supporting highly productive marine food webs. It also illustrates that tidal range is a local geographic property, not just an astronomical one.
Tidal Friction and Earth-Moon Evolution

Tidal friction gradually slows Earth's rotation. The energy of tidal flexing is transferred from Earth's rotation to the Moon's orbit, slowly pushing the Moon farther from Earth — currently about 3.8 cm per year. Billions of years ago, the Moon was significantly closer, producing much stronger tides. Earth's day has lengthened over geologic time as a result of this tidal braking. The same process that produced the Moon's synchronous rotation (tidal locking) continues to slowly alter Earth's rotation rate today.

Section 22.9

Solar Energy and Earth Systems

The Sun is the ultimate energy source for nearly all processes at Earth's surface. Solar energy drives evaporation, atmospheric circulation, ocean currents, photosynthesis, and the water cycle. Understanding how solar energy reaches Earth — and how its distribution varies with latitude and season — is essential for understanding weather, climate, and ecosystems.

The solar constant is the average solar energy flux arriving at the top of Earth's atmosphere: approximately 1,361 watts per square meter (W/m²). This value varies slightly as Earth moves between perihelion and aphelion and as the Sun's own output fluctuates over its 11-year cycle.

However, the solar constant refers to energy perpendicular to the Sun's rays. The actual energy received per square meter at any location on Earth's surface depends on three factors.

Three Factors Controlling Surface Insolation

Angle of incidence — sunlight striking at a low angle spreads over a larger surface area, reducing intensity per unit area. This is why the tropics are warm and the poles are cold year-round, independent of season.

Day length — more hours of sunlight means more total energy received over the course of a day. Longer summer days amplify the heating effect at mid-latitudes.

Atmospheric path length — low-angle sunlight must travel through more atmosphere, increasing absorption and scattering by aerosols, water vapor, and clouds. This further reduces surface insolation at high latitudes and low Sun angles.

The combination of these three factors means that the equatorial tropics receive roughly 2.5 times more solar energy annually than the polar regions. This fundamental energy gradient drives every major circulation system on Earth.

Connecting Solar Energy to Earth's Systems

Weather: Differential solar heating drives atmospheric pressure differences and winds. The tropics receive surplus solar energy; the poles receive a deficit. Atmospheric and ocean circulation systems continuously redistribute this energy from the equator toward the poles — the fundamental driver of global weather patterns studied in Chapters 19 and 20.

Climate zones: The latitude-dependent variation in insolation is the primary reason that climate zones are distributed roughly parallel to latitude lines, as studied in Chapter 21. Superimposed on this are the effects of topography, ocean currents, and monsoon circulation.

Ecosystems: Photosynthesis rates depend directly on available solar energy. Tropical ecosystems receive intense, year-round solar energy and support the highest rates of plant productivity on Earth. Polar ecosystems experience extreme seasonal swings from near-total darkness to continuous summer daylight. These patterns shape biome distributions as studied in Chapter 25.

Ocean circulation: Differential solar heating creates density differences in ocean water. Solar energy evaporates water at the tropics, increasing salinity and altering density. Wind-driven surface currents transport heat from tropical oceans to higher latitudes, moderating the climates of regions like Western Europe, as examined in Chapter 18.

The carbon cycle and energy balance: Earth absorbs solar energy and re-emits it as infrared radiation. Greenhouse gases in the atmosphere intercept some of this outgoing infrared energy, warming the surface — the greenhouse effect examined in Chapters 19 and 21. The balance between incoming solar energy and outgoing infrared radiation determines Earth's average surface temperature.

Solar Energy Variations Over Geologic Time: Milankovitch Cycles

Over millions to billions of years, several astronomical factors change the distribution of solar energy reaching Earth's surface. These three orbital cycles — known as Milankovitch cycles — were introduced in Chapter 21 in the context of ice ages and long-term climate change. They represent the astronomical pacing of Earth's ice age cycles over the past several million years.

  • Sec 22.6Moon phases result from the changing geometry of how much of the Moon's sunlit half faces Earth. The eight phases complete a 29.5-day synodic cycle. Phases are not caused by Earth's shadow.
  • Sec 22.7Solar eclipses occur at new moon when the Moon's shadow falls on Earth. Lunar eclipses occur at full moon when Earth's shadow falls on the Moon. Both require alignment at an orbital node. The Moon's 5° tilt prevents eclipses from occurring every month.
  • Sec 22.8The Moon's tidal force creates two tidal bulges, producing two high and two low tides per day in most locations. Spring tides (maximum range) occur at new and full moon; neap tides (minimum range) occur at quarter phases. Tidal friction is slowly lengthening Earth's day.
  • Sec 22.9Solar energy delivery to Earth's surface depends on angle of incidence, day length, and atmospheric path length. Latitude controls the fundamental pattern of insolation, driving weather, climate zones, ocean circulation, and ecosystem productivity. Milankovitch cycles pace long-term climate variation over thousands to hundreds of thousands of years.

Key Terms

angle of incidence
The angle at which solar radiation strikes a surface, measured from the perpendicular. Lower angles spread energy over a larger area and reduce heating per unit area.
aphelion
The point in Earth's orbit where it is farthest from the Sun, occurring around July 4; approximately 152 million km.
apogee
The point in the Moon's orbit where it is farthest from Earth; approximately 406,000 km.
axis of rotation
The imaginary line through Earth's center around which it spins; tilted 23.5° from perpendicular to the orbital plane.
eclipse
The blocking or shadowing of one celestial body by another; solar eclipses occur at new moon; lunar eclipses occur at full moon.
equinox
Either of two points in Earth's orbit where the Sun is directly overhead at the equator and day and night are approximately equal in length everywhere; March 20 and September 22.
full moon
The lunar phase when the entire sunlit hemisphere of the Moon faces Earth; occurs when the Moon is on the opposite side of Earth from the Sun.
insolation
Incoming solar radiation received per unit area at a surface; varies with latitude, season, and angle of incidence.
lunar eclipse
An eclipse occurring when Earth's shadow falls on the Moon; only possible during full moon; visible from the entire night side of Earth.
neap tide
A tide of reduced range occurring at first and third quarter moon, when the gravitational forces of the Moon and Sun act at right angles to each other.
new moon
The lunar phase when the Moon is between Earth and the Sun; the sunlit side faces away from Earth and the Moon is not visible at night.
orbital period
The time for one complete revolution around a larger body; Earth's orbital period around the Sun is approximately 365.25 days.
penumbra
The outer region of a shadow where only partial blocking of the light source occurs; observers in Earth's penumbra during a solar eclipse see a partial eclipse.
perigee
The point in the Moon's orbit where it is closest to Earth; approximately 357,000 km.
perihelion
The point in Earth's orbit where it is closest to the Sun, occurring around January 3; approximately 147 million km.
precession
A slow wobble of Earth's rotational axis that completes a cycle approximately every 26,000 years; one of the three Milankovitch cycles.
revolution
The orbital motion of Earth around the Sun; Earth completes one revolution in approximately 365.25 days.
rotation
The spinning of Earth on its own axis; one rotation takes approximately 24 hours (solar day) and causes day and night.
solar constant
The average solar energy flux received at the top of Earth's atmosphere, approximately 1,361 W/m².
solar eclipse
An eclipse occurring when the Moon passes between Earth and the Sun; only possible during new moon; visible only within the Moon's narrow shadow on Earth.
solstice
Either of two points in Earth's orbit where the Sun reaches its greatest angular distance from the equator; the June solstice has the longest Northern Hemisphere day; the December solstice has the shortest.
spring tide
A tide of maximum range occurring at new and full moon, when the gravitational forces of the Moon and Sun are aligned and reinforce each other.
synchronous rotation
The condition of a body whose rotation period equals its orbital period, so that the same face always points toward the body it orbits; the Moon is tidally locked to Earth.
tidal bulge
The elongation of Earth's ocean water along the Moon-Earth axis, caused by the differential gravitational pull of the Moon across Earth's diameter; two bulges exist simultaneously.
tidal force
The differential gravitational force across a body due to a nearby mass; the Moon's tidal force on Earth produces the ocean tides.
tidal range
The difference in water level between high tide and low tide at a given location; varies from less than 1 m in the open ocean to over 17 m in the Bay of Fundy.
umbra
The central region of a shadow where light is completely blocked; observers within the Moon's umbra during a solar eclipse experience totality.
waning
The phase of the Moon during which the illuminated portion visible from Earth is decreasing, from full moon toward new moon.
waxing
The phase of the Moon during which the illuminated portion visible from Earth is increasing, from new moon toward full moon.

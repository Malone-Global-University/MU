Chapter 6 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Every continent on Earth is built on a foundation that was once molten. The granite beneath mountain ranges, the basalt of ocean floors, the volcanic islands rising from the sea — all of them began as magma, liquid rock generated deep inside Earth, that cooled and solidified into stone. Igneous rocks are the most abundant rock type on Earth by volume, and they connect directly to some of the most powerful processes our planet produces: volcanic eruptions, the formation of ocean basins, mountain building, and the slow cycling of rock from Earth's interior to its surface and back again.

In Chapter 5, you learned what minerals are and how they form. Igneous rocks are, in a sense, aggregates of minerals crystallized from melt. Understanding how those minerals grow — how fast the melt cools, what it is made of, where it solidifies — explains nearly everything visible in an igneous rock: its color, its texture, its grain size, and what it tells you about where it came from.

This chapter connects igneous rocks to the interior heat engine that drives them upward, to the plate tectonic boundaries where most volcanic activity occurs, and to the hazards, resources, and landscape features that igneous processes create. By the end of this chapter, you should be able to look at an igneous rock and read its history — and understand what it means for the living landscape around it.

Opening Question

In 1980, Mount St. Helens in Washington State erupted with an explosion that leveled 600 square kilometers of forest and sent a plume of ash 24 kilometers into the atmosphere. Yet volcanoes in Hawaii erupt frequently and tourists walk within meters of lava flows without danger. Both are volcanoes. Both are fed by magma from deep inside Earth. Why are they so different — and what controls whether a volcano explodes or flows? By the end of this chapter, you will know how to answer that question using magma composition and the physics of volcanic gases.

Section 6.1

How Igneous Rocks Form

Igneous rocks form when molten rock cools and solidifies. The word igneous comes from the Latin ignis, meaning fire. All igneous rocks share a common origin: they were once liquid, and they became solid as heat was lost.

The key variable in igneous rock formation is where cooling happens. If molten rock cools deep inside Earth, surrounded by other rock that conducts heat slowly, cooling takes a very long time — thousands to millions of years. If molten rock erupts onto Earth's surface and is exposed to air or water, cooling happens quickly — sometimes in hours or days.

That difference in cooling rate controls the texture of the resulting rock, which means the size and arrangement of the mineral crystals it contains. Slow cooling allows large crystals to grow. Fast cooling produces small crystals. Very rapid cooling can prevent crystals from forming at all, producing a glassy rock with no visible crystal structure.

Two Dimensions of Igneous Rock Classification

Origin — Did the rock cool inside Earth (intrusive) or at the surface (extrusive)?

Composition — What minerals and elements does the rock contain? This is controlled by the composition of the original melt.

These two variables — origin and composition — give igneous rocks most of their distinguishing characteristics.

Section 6.2

Magma and Lava

Magma is molten rock located beneath Earth's surface. Lava is molten rock that has reached the surface. The same material has different names depending on where it is. Once lava reaches the surface and cools into solid rock, that rock is called extrusive igneous rock or volcanic rock.

Magma is not simply melted solid rock in the same way ice melts into water. The melting of rock inside Earth is controlled by three factors.

Melting MechanismDescriptionWhere It Occurs
Temperature increaseHigher temperatures cause minerals to melt. Earth's temperature increases with depth.Deep mantle; hottest regions
Decompression meltingDecreasing pressure as rock moves upward lowers its melting point, causing melting even without added heat.Mid-ocean ridges; hot spots
Flux meltingWater and other volatiles lower the melting point of rock when introduced from a subducting plate.Subduction zones

What Magma Contains

Magma is a complex mixture of melt (liquid silicate material), dissolved gases (primarily water vapor, carbon dioxide, and sulfur dioxide), and sometimes mineral crystals already suspended in the liquid if cooling has partially begun.

The dissolved gases in magma are important because they drive eruptions. As magma rises toward the surface and pressure decreases, dissolved gases come out of solution — much as carbon dioxide bubbles out of a carbonated drink when the cap is removed. If gas escapes gradually, eruptions tend to be relatively calm. If gas cannot escape easily because the magma is thick and viscous, pressure builds until eruptions become explosive.

Earth Systems Connection

The gases released during volcanic eruptions — water vapor, carbon dioxide, sulfur dioxide — enter the atmosphere and affect weather, climate, and air chemistry. Volcanic outgassing over geologic time is thought to have contributed significantly to Earth's early atmosphere and oceans. This connects Chapter 6 directly to Chapters 19, 21, and 24.

Section 6.3

Intrusive and Extrusive Igneous Rocks

Intrusive Igneous Rocks

Intrusive igneous rocks (also called plutonic rocks) form when magma cools and solidifies below Earth's surface. Because the surrounding rock acts as insulation, cooling is slow and crystals have time to grow large enough to see with the naked eye. Bodies of intrusive igneous rock have specific names based on their size and shape.

Intrusive BodyShape and SizeExample
BatholithVery large (>100 km²), often the core of mountain rangesSierra Nevada, California
StockSmaller than a batholith, similar shapeCommon in eroded mountain roots
DikeSheet cutting across existing rock layersVisible in canyon walls and cliffs
SillSheet running parallel to existing rock layersPalisades Sill, New York/New Jersey
LaccolithLens-shaped; domes up overlying rockHenry Mountains, Utah

Intrusive rocks are not visible at Earth's surface while forming. They become exposed when the rock above them is removed by erosion over millions of years, or when tectonic forces lift them upward.

Extrusive Igneous Rocks

Extrusive igneous rocks (also called volcanic rocks) form when magma reaches Earth's surface and cools rapidly. Because cooling is fast, crystals remain small — often too small to see without a microscope — or the rock is glassy. Lava can erupt from central vents, fissures, or submarine vents on the ocean floor.

Extrusive rocks can also form from material ejected explosively from volcanoes: tephra (volcanic ash, cinders, and bombs) that falls from the air and accumulates on the surface.

Section 6.4

Crystal Size and Cooling Rate

The relationship between cooling rate and crystal size is one of the most important concepts in igneous petrology. When magma cools, mineral crystals begin to form through a process called nucleation — atoms arrange themselves into ordered crystal structures. As temperature continues to drop, crystals grow by adding more atoms to their edges.

The longer this process continues without interruption, the larger the crystals become. When cooling is slow, atoms have time to migrate through the melt and join growing crystals. When cooling is fast, many small crystals nucleate nearly simultaneously and have little time to grow before the rock solidifies.

Textures of Igneous Rocks

TextureCrystal SizeCooling RateExample Rock
Coarse-grained (phaneritic)Visible to naked eyeVery slow (underground)Granite, gabbro

Worked Example 6.4.1
Reading a rock's cooling history from its texture
  • ObservationA geologist finds an igneous rock sample that contains large, well-formed crystals of feldspar (2–3 cm across) surrounded by a very fine-grained dark groundmass in which no individual crystals are visible.
  • AnalysisThe large crystals (phenocrysts) indicate a period of slow cooling deep underground, during which the crystals had time to grow. The fine-grained surrounding material indicates that the magma was then transported rapidly toward the surface and cooled quickly, freezing the smaller crystals in place.
  • ConclusionThis rock has a porphyritic texture and records a two-stage cooling history: slow cooling at depth followed by rapid cooling near or at the surface.
  • InterpretationPorphyritic texture is common in lavas and shallow intrusions and is a direct piece of evidence about magma movement — the rock is recording its own journey from depth to surface.
Common Misconception

Students sometimes think a rock with many different minerals must be intrusive, or that a dark rock must be extrusive. Neither is reliable. Texture — crystal size — is the primary evidence for cooling rate and origin. A dark fine-grained rock is likely extrusive; a dark coarse-grained rock (like gabbro) is intrusive. Always look at crystal size first.

Section 6.5

Composition of Igneous Rocks

Not all magmas are alike. The composition of a magma — what elements and minerals it contains — controls its viscosity, gas behavior, the minerals that crystallize from it, the color of the resulting rock, and the style of volcanic eruption it produces. The most important compositional variable is silica content — the proportion of silicon dioxide (SiO₂).

TypeSilica ContentIntrusive RockExtrusive RockColorWhere It Forms
FelsicHigh (~70%)GraniteRhyoliteLight (pink, gray, white)Continental crust, subduction zones
IntermediateMedium (~60%)DioriteAndesiteMedium graySubduction zones, volcanic arcs
MaficLow (~50%)GabbroBasaltDark (black, dark gray)Ocean crust, hot spots, rifts
UltramaficVery low (~40%)PeridotiteKomatiiteVery dark green/blackUpper mantle

Viscosity and Eruption Style

Viscosity is resistance to flow. High-silica (felsic) magmas are very viscous — they flow slowly and resist the escape of dissolved gases. When gas pressure builds, felsic magmas can produce explosive eruptions. Low-silica (mafic) magmas are much less viscous, gases escape more easily, and lava flows more freely, producing less explosive eruptions.

This is why basaltic volcanoes (mafic) tend to have gently sloping sides and produce lava flows, while volcanoes made of andesite or rhyolite (intermediate to felsic) tend to have steeper sides and produce explosive eruptions. Eruption style is controlled more by magma composition than by anything else.

Key Relationship

High silica → high viscosity → gases trapped → explosive eruption

Low silica → low viscosity → gases escape → effusive lava flows

Section 6.6

Basalt, Granite, Obsidian, and Pumice

Four igneous rocks deserve special attention because they are so common, economically important, or illustrative of igneous processes.

Rock Profile
Basalt — the most abundant surface rock on Earth
  • TypeFine-grained, dark gray to black; mafic; extrusive
  • MineralsPyroxene, olivine, calcium-rich plagioclase feldspar
  • Forms whereMid-ocean ridges, oceanic hot spots, continental flood basalt provinces
  • Notable featureColumnar jointing — hexagonal columns that form as basalt contracts during cooling. Examples: Giant's Causeway (Northern Ireland), Devils Postpile (California).
  • UsesCrushed stone for roads and construction, fiber insulation, railroad ballast
Rock Profile
Granite — the foundation of continental crust
  • TypeCoarse-grained, light-colored (pink, gray, white); felsic; intrusive
  • MineralsQuartz (glassy, gray), feldspar (white or pink), biotite mica (black) or hornblende
  • Forms whereDeep underground in continental crust; exposed after overlying rock is eroded away
  • Notable landscapesYosemite Valley, Sierra Nevada, Swiss Alps, Canadian Shield
  • UsesBuilding stone, countertops, monuments, curbing — extremely hard and durable
Rock Profile
Obsidian — volcanic glass
  • TypeGlassy texture, typically black; felsic in composition; extrusive
  • Why no crystals?Lava cooled so rapidly that atoms had no time to organize into crystal structures.
  • Notable featureConchoidal fracture — smooth, curved breaks that produce extremely sharp edges. Before metals, obsidian was used for surgical-quality cutting tools and weapons.
  • Archaeological useGeochemical fingerprinting of obsidian artifacts reveals ancient trade routes — different volcanic sources have distinct chemical signatures.
Rock Profile
Pumice — the rock that floats
  • TypeHighly vesicular, light gray to white; felsic to intermediate; extrusive
  • Why does it float?The rock is so full of gas-bubble voids (vesicles) that its bulk density is less than water. The vesicles were frozen in place as frothy lava was rapidly quenched.
  • UsesAbrasive in cleaning products and cosmetics, skin care, polishing metals and glass, lightweight aggregate in concrete

Section 6.7

Volcanic Landforms

Volcanoes are the most dramatic surface expression of igneous activity, but the landforms they produce are diverse. The shape of a volcano reflects the composition and viscosity of the magma, the style and duration of eruptions, and the volume of material erupted.

Shield Volcanoes

Shield volcanoes are broad, gently sloping volcanoes built almost entirely from basaltic lava flows. Named for their resemblance to a warrior's shield lying on the ground, they have gentle slopes (typically 2°–10°) because basaltic magma is fluid enough for lava to travel far from the vent before solidifying. Shield volcanoes erupt frequently but relatively quietly. The Hawaiian Islands are classic examples. Mauna Loa, measured from the ocean floor, is the tallest mountain on Earth by that measure.

Composite Volcanoes (Stratovolcanoes)

Composite volcanoes (also called stratovolcanoes) are steep-sided, cone-shaped volcanoes built from alternating layers of hardened lava, ash, and other volcanic material. They form from magmas of intermediate to felsic composition — andesite is most common — which are more viscous and gas-rich than basaltic magma. Slopes of 25°–35° are typical. They produce more explosive eruptions and are found primarily at subduction zone boundaries. Mount St. Helens, Mount Fuji, and Mount Rainier are examples.

Cinder Cones

Cinder cones are small, steep-sided hills built from cinders and other pyroclastic material ejected from a central vent. They rarely grow taller than a few hundred meters and have very steep sides (30°–40°) because loose cinders pile up at the angle of repose. A bowl-shaped crater typically marks the top. Paricutín in Mexico, which grew from a farmer's field in 1943, is a famous example of a cinder cone growing from scratch in historical time.

Calderas

A caldera is a large, roughly circular depression formed when a volcanic structure collapses inward after a massive eruption empties the underlying magma chamber. Calderas can be several to tens of kilometers across. They should not be confused with craters, which are relatively small openings at the top of a volcanic vent. Crater Lake in Oregon occupies a caldera formed about 7,700 years ago when Mount Mazama collapsed. Yellowstone is underlain by a supervolcano caldera system.

Other Volcanic Features

Lava tubes are tunnels formed when the surface of a lava flow cools and hardens while lava continues flowing through the interior. When flow stops, the tube is left empty and can be explored on foot in places like Hawaii and Iceland. Pillow lavas form when basaltic lava erupts underwater — rapid quenching by seawater produces rounded, pillow-shaped masses abundant on the ocean floor. Fumaroles emit volcanic gases without producing lava. Geysers and hot springs form where groundwater is heated by magma or hot rock below.

Section 6.8

Igneous Rocks and Plate Boundaries

The distribution of volcanoes and igneous intrusions on Earth follows the pattern of plate tectonics almost exactly. Different types of plate boundaries produce different types of magma, different igneous rocks, and different volcanic landforms.

Divergent Boundaries: Basalt and New Ocean Crust

At divergent plate boundaries, plates pull apart and the mantle rises to fill the gap. Decreasing pressure causes decompression melting of mafic mantle rock, generating basaltic magma that erupts at mid-ocean ridges to become new ocean floor. The Mid-Atlantic Ridge and East Pacific Rise are active examples. Divergent boundaries can also occur within continents, forming continental rift zones — the East African Rift is currently splitting Africa apart.

Convergent Boundaries: Andesite and Explosive Volcanism

At convergent boundaries, water and volatiles released from a subducting oceanic plate trigger flux melting in the mantle above. The resulting magma is intermediate to felsic in composition — more silica-rich and gas-rich than basaltic magma — and erupts as andesite, dacite, or rhyolite. These magmas produce the explosive composite volcanoes characteristic of volcanic arcs.

Subduction SettingLandformExamples
Oceanic plate beneath oceanic plateIsland arcAleutian Islands, Japan, Mariana Islands
Oceanic plate beneath continental plateContinental volcanic arcCascades (Pacific NW), Andes (South America)

The Ring of Fire — the arc of intense volcanic and seismic activity surrounding the Pacific Ocean — marks the subduction zones where Pacific oceanic plates are descending beneath surrounding plates. About 75% of Earth's active and dormant volcanoes are found within the Ring of Fire.

Hot Spots: Intraplate Volcanism

Hot spots are locations of unusually high volcanic activity not at plate boundaries. They are thought to result from mantle plumes — columns of anomalously hot mantle rock rising from depth. Hot spot volcanoes produce primarily basaltic magma. Because tectonic plates move over stationary hot spots, they leave chains of progressively older volcanic islands. The Hawaiian-Emperor Seamount Chain traces the Pacific Plate's path over the Hawaiian hot spot for more than 75 million years.

Worked Example 6.8.1
Reading a volcanic island chain as a record of plate motion
  • ObservationThe Hawaiian Islands decrease in age from the Big Island (active volcanoes today) to the northwest, where older islands are more eroded and eventually submerged as seamounts.
  • ReasoningIf the hot spot is stationary and the Pacific Plate moves northwest over it, each new volcano forms above the hot spot, then moves away as the plate continues moving. The island directly above the hot spot is youngest; older islands to the northwest were once above the hot spot and have since moved away and begun to erode.
  • ConclusionThe age progression of the Hawaiian chain is direct evidence of Pacific Plate motion over a fixed hot spot — and by measuring the ages and distances of islands, geologists can calculate the speed of plate movement.
  • ConnectionThis connects to Chapter 12 (Plate Tectonics), where plate motion and hot spots are covered in full.

Section 6.9

Igneous Processes and Natural Hazards

Volcanic eruptions are among the most powerful natural events on Earth. Understanding igneous processes is directly relevant to volcanic hazard assessment, community planning, and emergency response.

Hazardous Volcanic Products

HazardDescriptionNotable Example
Lava flowsStreams of molten rock; move slowly enough for evacuation but destroy everything in their pathHawaii: neighborhoods destroyed over weeks

Volcanic ashFine pulverized rock/glass; collapses roofs, contaminates water, damages aircraft, travels thousands of kmEyjafjallajökull 2010: European air travel halted for weeks

Volcano Monitoring

Scientists monitor active volcanoes using a variety of tools to detect signs of unrest before an eruption occurs.

Volcano Monitoring Methods

Seismometers — detect small earthquakes caused by magma moving through rock. Earthquake swarms often precede eruptions.

GPS and tiltmeters — measure ground deformation. Swelling of the surface as magma accumulates below indicates approaching eruption.

Gas sensors — measure SO₂ and other emissions. Increasing gas output often signals rising magma.

Thermal imaging — detects changes in surface temperature near vents.

Satellite imagery — tracks ground deformation, gas plumes, and surface changes over time.

Despite advances in monitoring, volcanic eruption prediction remains imperfect. Scientists can often identify increased probability and sometimes provide hours to days of warning, but exact timing and intensity are difficult to predict.

Benefits of Volcanic Activity

Volcanic activity also brings significant benefits. Fertile soils: volcanic ash and weathered basalt produce some of the world's most productive agricultural soils — Java, Iceland, and the Pacific Northwest farm extensively on volcanic soils. Geothermal energy: Iceland generates the majority of its heating from geothermal sources. Mineral deposits: hydrothermal fluids associated with igneous activity concentrate copper, gold, silver, zinc, and lead. New land: volcanic eruptions literally create new land, as the ongoing growth of the Hawaiian Islands demonstrates.

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 6.1Igneous rocks form when molten rock cools and solidifies. Cooling location (inside Earth vs. at the surface) controls texture; composition controls mineralogy. Both dimensions together classify igneous rocks.
  • Sec 6.2Magma is molten rock below the surface; lava is magma at the surface. Melting is caused by temperature increase, decompression, or flux from volatiles. Dissolved gases drive explosive eruptions when they cannot escape easily.
  • Sec 6.3Intrusive (plutonic) rocks cool slowly underground and have coarse-grained textures. Extrusive (volcanic) rocks cool rapidly at or near the surface and have fine-grained, glassy, or vesicular textures. Major intrusive bodies include batholiths, dikes, sills, and laccoliths.
  • Sec 6.4Slow cooling → large crystals (phaneritic). Rapid cooling → small crystals (aphanitic). Extremely rapid → glassy. Gas-trapped → vesicular. Two-stage cooling → porphyritic. Explosive ejection → pyroclastic. Texture is a direct record of cooling history.
  • Sec 6.5Silica content is the key compositional variable. Felsic magmas (high silica) are viscous and explosive; mafic magmas (low silica) are fluid and effusive. Color roughly tracks composition: light = felsic; dark = mafic.
  • Sec 6.6Basalt: fine-grained, mafic, most abundant surface rock. Granite: coarse-grained, felsic, common continental crust. Obsidian: volcanic glass with conchoidal fracture, once used for cutting tools. Pumice: vesicular and light enough to float.
  • Sec 6.7Shield volcanoes are broad and basaltic with gentle slopes. Composite volcanoes are steep, explosive, and intermediate in composition. Cinder cones are small and short-lived. Calderas form by collapse after large eruptions. Lava tubes, pillow lavas, fumaroles, and geysers are associated volcanic features.
  • Sec 6.8Divergent boundaries produce basalt via decompression melting. Convergent boundaries produce andesite to rhyolite via flux melting and explosive volcanic arcs. Hot spots produce basalt from mantle plumes and leave age-progressive island chains as evidence of plate motion.
  • Sec 6.9Volcanic hazards include lava flows, pyroclastic flows, ash fall, lahars, volcanic gases, and tsunamis — with very different risk profiles. Monitoring uses seismometers, GPS, gas sensors, thermal imaging, and satellites. Volcanic activity also produces fertile soils, geothermal energy, and ore deposits.

Fine-grained, dark, mafic extrusive igneous rock; the most abundant rock on Earth's surface.
granite
Coarse-grained, light-colored, felsic intrusive igneous rock; common in continental crust.
obsidian
A dark volcanic glass formed by extremely rapid cooling of felsic lava; breaks with conchoidal fracture.
pumice
A highly vesicular, lightweight igneous rock from frothy, gas-rich lava; can float on water.
shield volcano
A broad, gently sloping volcano built from fluid basaltic lava flows. Example: Mauna Loa, Hawaii.
composite volcano
A steep, cone-shaped volcano of alternating lava and pyroclastic layers; typically at subduction zones.
cinder cone
A small, steep-sided volcano built from pyroclastic cinders and fragments around a central vent.
caldera
A large circular depression formed by the collapse of a volcano after a massive eruption.
volcanic arc
A chain of volcanoes that forms above a subduction zone.
hot spot
A location of unusually high volcanic activity above a mantle plume, not at a plate boundary.
pyroclastic flow
A fast-moving avalanche of hot gas, ash, and rock fragments produced by a volcanic eruption.
lahar
A volcanic mudflow formed when volcanic material mixes with water.
tephra
Fragments of volcanic rock and ash ejected during an eruption.
columnar jointing
A pattern of hexagonal or pentagonal columns that form in basalt as it contracts during cooling.

Common Misconceptions

Misconception — "Volcanoes are not very dangerous because lava flows slowly"

While many lava flows move slowly enough for evacuation, volcanic eruptions produce many hazards far more lethal: pyroclastic flows can reach 700°C and travel over 100 km/h; lahars can bury entire towns; ash can collapse buildings and travel thousands of kilometers; toxic gases can suffocate people. The 79 CE eruption of Vesuvius killed thousands in minutes — from pyroclastic flows and ash surges, not lava flows.

Misconception — "Volcanoes only occur at plate boundaries"

While most of Earth's volcanic activity does occur at plate boundaries, a significant fraction occurs at hot spots far from any boundary. Hawaii, the Galápagos Islands, Iceland (partly), and Yellowstone are underlain by mantle plumes, not plate boundaries. These intraplate volcanoes demonstrate that plate tectonics is not the only mechanism generating volcanic activity.

Misconception — "Granite must be volcanic because it contains so many minerals"

Granite is intrusive, not extrusive. It forms deep underground, which is precisely why it has large crystals — slow cooling allows mineral crystals to grow to visible sizes. Volcanic rocks typically have fine-grained or glassy textures because they cool too quickly. The number of different minerals in a rock does not indicate whether it is volcanic or plutonic; texture and composition do.

Misconception — "A volcano that hasn't erupted in a long time is extinct and safe"

Many volcanoes quiet for decades, centuries, or thousands of years have reactivated. Mount Pinatubo had no recorded eruptions for 500 years before its catastrophic 1991 eruption. Mount St. Helens was considered dormant before 1980. Geologists distinguish active, dormant, and extinct categories — but these involve significant uncertainty, particularly for volcanoes with long repose periods.

Looking Ahead
Chapter 7: Sedimentary Rocks and Surface Processes

Chapter 7 introduces sedimentary rocks — rocks formed at Earth's surface from accumulated particles, dissolved minerals, and the remains of organisms. Where Chapter 6 followed heat and pressure from Earth's interior outward to the surface, Chapter 7 follows gravity and water in the opposite direction: breaking down surface materials, moving them across the landscape, and depositing them in layers that become rock. You will learn how sedimentary rocks preserve evidence of ancient rivers, oceans, deserts, swamps, and climates — making them the primary archive of Earth's surface history and the record in which fossils are found.

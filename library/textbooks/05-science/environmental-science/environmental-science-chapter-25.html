Chapter 25 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

You have studied how Earth's rock, water, atmosphere, and energy systems interact. You have traced water through watersheds, followed carbon through the atmosphere, and watched how plate tectonics reshapes the land over millions of years. Now you turn to the living layer of Earth — the biosphere — and ask: how do organisms fit into these systems, and how do Earth systems shape the communities of life?

An ecosystem is not simply a list of organisms. It is a web of relationships: between species, between organisms and their physical environment, between nutrients and the soil that holds them, between rainfall and the forests it sustains. Ecosystems are dynamic. They change with seasons, recover from disturbances, respond to climate shifts, and — like all Earth systems — can be pushed past the point of easy recovery.

This chapter introduces ecosystems as components of Earth systems. You will learn how matter cycles and energy flows through living communities, how different regions of Earth support distinctive communities called biomes, why biodiversity matters beyond its ethical dimensions, and how human activity is reorganizing ecosystems at a scale with no clear precedent in the geologic record.

Opening Question

In 1995, wildlife managers reintroduced gray wolves to Yellowstone National Park after a 70-year absence. Within a decade, not only had elk populations changed — so had the vegetation along riverbanks, the populations of beavers and songbirds, and even the flow patterns of streams. How could the return of a single predator alter plants, rivers, and dozens of species it never directly touched? This chapter will give you the tools to answer that question — and many others like it.

Ecosystem Structure: Components, Roles, and Energy Flow

Section 25.1

Ecosystems in Earth Systems

An ecosystem is a community of living organisms interacting with each other and with their nonliving physical environment — the soil, water, air, sunlight, temperature, and nutrients of a particular place. This definition contains two essential halves. The living community is called the biotic component. The nonliving physical environment is the abiotic component. Neither half functions without the other.

Plants draw carbon dioxide from the air and minerals from the soil. Animals depend on plants or other animals for energy. Decomposers break organic material back into inorganic nutrients that plants can use again. Every organism both shapes and is shaped by its physical environment.

Ecosystems are open systems. They exchange matter and energy with their surroundings. Sunlight enters. Rainwater flows in and out. Wind carries seeds, spores, and insects across boundaries. Rivers carry nutrients from upland forests to coastal wetlands. These connections mean that what happens in one ecosystem can affect ecosystems far away.

Key Distinction: Biotic vs. Abiotic

Biotic — all living components: plants, animals, fungi, bacteria, protists, and other microorganisms.

Abiotic — all nonliving physical and chemical components: temperature, light, water, soil minerals, pH, salinity, and gases.

In every ecosystem, biotic and abiotic components interact continuously. Organisms alter abiotic conditions (trees shade soil; roots hold water). Abiotic conditions determine which organisms can survive.

All of Earth's ecosystems together form the biosphere — the thin zone of life stretching from the deep ocean floor to the upper atmosphere, wherever liquid water, energy, and nutrients make life possible. The biosphere interacts continuously with the geosphere (rock and soil), hydrosphere (water), and atmosphere (air). The carbon cycle, nitrogen cycle, water cycle, and phosphorus cycle all cross the boundary between the living and nonliving worlds. Organisms are not passengers in Earth's systems; they are active participants that help regulate atmospheric composition, soil chemistry, ocean chemistry, and surface temperatures.

Systems Connection

Ecosystems exist at many scales. A temporary pond in a meadow is an ecosystem. So is the Amazon basin. The processes — energy flow, matter cycling, predator-prey interactions — are similar in kind but vastly different in extent and consequence. Scale matters when predicting how disturbances will propagate.

Section 25.2

Habitats and Niches

A habitat is the physical place where an organism lives — the specific combination of temperature, moisture, soil type, light, elevation, and other conditions that an organism requires. A marsh warbler lives in freshwater marshes. A cactus wren lives in Sonoran Desert scrub. Each organism has specific habitat requirements shaped by millions of years of evolution.

Habitats are not static. They change with seasons, with succession (Section 25.7), with climate, and with human land use. A wetland drained for agriculture is a destroyed habitat. A warming ocean is a shifting one — species that depended on cold-water temperatures may find their habitat contracting even as a physical ocean remains.

A niche is broader than a habitat. An organism's niche describes its complete role in the ecosystem: what it eats, what eats it, where and when it is active, what physical conditions it tolerates, and how it interacts with other species — as competitor, predator, prey, pollinator, decomposer, or host. Two species may share the same habitat but occupy different niches if they use different food resources or are active at different times.

Case Study 25.2
Sea Otters and Kelp Forests

Sea otters on the Pacific coast of North America eat sea urchins. When otters were hunted nearly to extinction in the 19th century, sea urchin populations exploded and consumed the kelp forests that hundreds of other species depended on. When otter populations recovered in protected areas, kelp forests returned. A single species — not the most abundant, not the largest — controlled the structure of an entire marine ecosystem. This is the defining characteristic of a keystone species: its effect on the ecosystem is far out of proportion to its numbers.

Section 25.3

Food Chains and Food Webs

Energy and Trophic Levels

Every ecosystem requires an energy source. In most ecosystems, that source is sunlight. Producers (also called autotrophs) capture solar energy through photosynthesis and use it to build organic molecules from carbon dioxide and water. Plants, algae, and photosynthetic bacteria are the primary producers of most ecosystems.

In some ecosystems — deep-sea hydrothermal vents, for example — there is no sunlight. Producers there are chemotrophs, organisms that capture energy from chemical reactions rather than light. These ecosystems demonstrate that life does not absolutely require sunlight, only an energy source of some kind.

Trophic LevelDescriptionExamples
ProducersCapture energy from sunlight or chemical reactions; make organic matter from inorganic compoundsGrasses, trees, algae, phytoplankton
Primary consumersEat producers directly (herbivores)Caterpillars, deer, grasshoppers, zooplankton
Secondary consumersEat primary consumersBirds eating insects, wolves eating deer, small fish eating zooplankton
Tertiary consumersEat secondary consumersEagles eating birds, orcas eating seals
DecomposersBreak down dead organic matter at all levels; return nutrients to soil and waterBacteria, fungi, earthworms
The 10 Percent Rule

On average, only about 10 percent of the energy stored at one trophic level is transferred to the next. The rest is lost as heat during metabolism, used in movement and reproduction, or passed in forms consumers cannot digest. This means an ecosystem can support far more producers than primary consumers, and far more primary consumers than secondary consumers.

Consequence: Large predators require enormous territories and are rare relative to their prey. Energy flows in only one direction — upward from producers — unlike matter, which cycles repeatedly through the system.

Food Webs and Trophic Cascades

A food chain traces a single pathway of energy transfer: grass → grasshopper → frog → heron. Real ecosystems are not chains; they are webs. Most organisms eat several different species and are eaten by several different predators. These overlapping feeding relationships form a food web.

Food webs are more stable than simple chains because if one species declines, others can shift their diet. But food webs have limits. If a keystone species is removed, the web may collapse in unpredictable ways. These rippling effects are called trophic cascades.

Case Study 25.3
Wolves and the Trophic Cascade in Yellowstone

When wolves were reintroduced to Yellowstone National Park in 1995, their presence changed where elk grazed — elk avoided valleys and riverbanks where they could be cornered. With less grazing pressure, willows and aspens regrew along streams. Beavers returned because their food supply recovered. Beaver ponds slowed stream flow, raised water tables, and created wetland habitats for songbirds and fish. This chain of indirect effects triggered by the reintroduction of a predator changed plants, rivers, and dozens of species the wolves never directly touched.

Decomposers and Nutrient Cycles

Decomposers are often overlooked but are essential to every ecosystem. Without bacteria and fungi breaking down dead organic matter, nutrients would be locked up in dead tissue and producers would run out of the raw materials for growth. Decomposition returns carbon to the atmosphere (as carbon dioxide), nitrogen to the soil, and releases phosphorus, potassium, and other nutrients that plants need.

The rate of decomposition depends on temperature and moisture. Decomposition is fastest in warm, wet tropical forests and slowest in cold, dry arctic tundra. This is one reason arctic soils store enormous quantities of organic carbon in permafrost — organic matter accumulates faster than it decomposes. As temperatures rise and permafrost thaws, decomposition rates increase, releasing carbon dioxide and methane — connecting ecosystem science directly to climate science.

Systems Connection: Carbon and Ecosystems

Chapter 21 introduced the carbon cycle and climate change. Permafrost thaw is one of the most important feedback loops in the climate system: warming → decomposition accelerates → CO₂ and CH₄ release → additional warming. The living ecosystem is a direct participant in Earth's climate feedback system.

Section 25.4

Biomes

A biome is a large geographic region characterized by a distinctive climate and a distinctive community of plants and animals adapted to that climate. Biomes are defined primarily by temperature and precipitation — the two climatic variables that most control what organisms can survive in a region.

The same biome type can occur on multiple continents. Temperate grasslands appear in North America (prairies), South America (pampas), Central Asia (steppes), and South Africa (highveld). The grasses differ by species on each continent, but the structure of the ecosystem is remarkably similar — reflecting convergent evolution: different lineages evolving similar strategies in response to similar environmental pressures.

The boundaries between biomes are rarely sharp. Transitional zones called ecotones — where forest grades into grassland, or grassland into desert — often support species from both adjacent biomes and some found only in the transition zone. Ecotones are frequently among the most productive and biologically rich areas on Earth.

Aquatic Biomes

Aquatic systems are categorized by salinity, depth, light penetration, temperature, and flow. Freshwater systems include rivers, streams, lakes, ponds, and wetlands. Wetlands — marshes, swamps, bogs, and fens — are among the most productive ecosystems on Earth: they filter water, store carbon, buffer floods, recharge groundwater, and support enormous biodiversity. They have also been drained and altered more extensively than almost any other ecosystem type.

Estuaries occur where rivers meet the sea, mixing fresh and salt water. They are among the most biologically productive systems on Earth and serve as nursery habitat for many commercial fish species. Coral reefs — sometimes called the rainforests of the sea — are built by coral polyps that maintain symbiotic photosynthetic algae within their tissues. They are highly sensitive to temperature: warming of even 1–2°C above normal summer temperatures can cause coral bleaching, in which the symbiotic algae are expelled, leaving coral white and vulnerable to starvation and disease.

Section 25.5

Biodiversity

Biodiversity refers to the variety of life at multiple levels: the diversity of genes within a species, the diversity of species in an ecosystem, and the diversity of ecosystems across a region or the planet. When people speak of protecting biodiversity, they usually mean all three levels simultaneously.

Species richness — the simple count of how many species are present — is the most commonly measured component of biodiversity. But richness alone does not capture the full picture. Evenness — how equally individuals are distributed among species — is equally important. Two forests might each have 200 species, but if one is dominated by a single species, its functional diversity is far lower than the count suggests.

Genetic diversity within populations is critical for long-term resilience. Populations with high genetic diversity contain a wider range of traits and are better able to adapt to changing conditions — new diseases, climate shifts, new predators. Small, isolated populations often suffer from low genetic diversity, increasing their vulnerability to extinction.

Why Biodiversity Matters — Ecological Evidence

Ecologists have found, through experiments and long-term observations, that ecosystems with more species tend to:

· Produce more biomass over the long term

· Use nutrients and resources more efficiently

· Recover more quickly from disturbances

· Maintain their functions more stably across varying conditions

The mechanism is partly redundancy (multiple species perform similar roles; loss of one is buffered by others) and partly complementarity (species use slightly different resources, preventing waste).

Biodiversity is not evenly distributed. Tropical regions — especially tropical rainforests, tropical mountains, and coral reefs — support far more species per unit area than temperate or polar regions. Biodiversity hotspots are regions of especially high species richness that are also under severe threat. The world's 36 recognized hotspots cover less than 3 percent of Earth's land area but contain more than half of all plant species and about 43 percent of terrestrial vertebrate species found nowhere else on Earth. They are also regions where habitat loss has already been severe.

The Sixth Mass Extinction

Earth has experienced five mass extinctions in its geologic history. Many ecologists argue that Earth is currently experiencing a sixth, driven primarily by human activity. Current extinction rates are estimated to be 100 to 1,000 times the background rate in the fossil record. Unlike previous extinctions driven by asteroids or volcanic eruptions, the primary causes are habitat destruction, overexploitation, invasive species, pollution, and climate change — all products of human activity. Not all scientists agree on every estimate, but the direction of the trend is not disputed.

Section 25.6

Ecosystem Services

Ecosystem services are the benefits that functioning ecosystems provide to people. This framework was developed partly to make the value of ecosystems visible in policy and economic discussions where natural systems are often treated as having no value until converted to a use that generates money.

CategoryDescriptionExamples
Provisioning servicesDirect products ecosystems provideFood (fish, game, pollenated crops), fresh water, timber, fiber, pharmaceutical compounds, genetic resources
Regulating servicesWays ecosystems regulate environmental conditionsClimate regulation (carbon absorption), water purification (wetlands), flood control (floodplains), pollination, disease regulation, pest control
Cultural servicesNonmaterial benefitsRecreation, tourism, spiritual values, education, aesthetic experience, sense of place
Supporting servicesUnderlying processes that make all other services possibleNutrient cycling, soil formation, primary production, water cycling — not consumed directly but foundational to everything else
The Hidden Cost of Ecosystem Conversion

When a wetland is drained for farmland, the market value of crops grown there may be measured. What is rarely measured is the loss of flood control, water filtration, waterfowl habitat, carbon storage, and storm buffering. When these values are counted, many conversions of natural land to agricultural or urban use are economically counterproductive — even before any noneconomic values are considered.

Section 25.7

Ecological Succession

Ecosystems are not static. They change in predictable ways following disturbances — fire, flood, landslide, volcanic eruption, hurricane, or human clearing. The process by which an ecosystem recovers or develops over time is called ecological succession.

Primary Succession

Primary succession begins on bare rock or other substrate that has never supported a community — newly cooled lava flows, exposed rock after a glacier retreats, freshly deposited sandbars. There is no soil, no organic matter, no seed bank.

The first colonizers are called pioneer species — organisms tolerant of harsh physical conditions and capable of establishing on bare substrate. Lichens (a symbiosis of fungi and algae or cyanobacteria) are classic pioneers on bare rock. They secrete acids that slowly break down rock surfaces, and their decomposed bodies add the first traces of organic matter. As thin soil accumulates, mosses and small herbaceous plants can establish. As soil develops further, shrubs, then small trees, eventually large trees can establish. Each successive community changes the physical environment in ways that make it more suitable for the next community — the fundamental mechanism of succession.

Secondary Succession

Secondary succession occurs on land where a community has been disturbed or removed but where soil still exists and a seed bank may be intact. Abandoned farmland, logged forests, burned grasslands, and river floodplains after floods all undergo secondary succession. Secondary succession is faster than primary succession because the foundational infrastructure — soil, organic matter, root networks, dormant seeds — survives the disturbance.

Within a few years of field abandonment in the eastern United States, grasses and annual weeds colonize. Within 10–20 years, shrubs and fast-growing trees (pines, aspens, birches) dominate. Within 50–100 years, a mixed forest may develop that eventually transitions to a more shade-tolerant community.

Dynamic Equilibria, Not Static Endpoints

Early ecologists proposed that succession always proceeds toward a stable climax community — a fixed, self-sustaining endpoint. This view has been substantially revised. Many ecosystems exist in continuous, partial disturbance — patches at different successional stages across the landscape. Fire-dependent grasslands and forests never reach a static endpoint; periodic fires reset parts of the community to earlier successional stages. The current understanding is that ecosystems are dynamic equilibria — fluctuating around a range of conditions. This means protecting ecosystems may require allowing disturbance processes (fire, flood, grazing) to continue rather than suppressing them.

Section 25.8

Habitat Loss and Fragmentation

The single greatest cause of current species extinctions is habitat loss — the conversion of natural land to agriculture, urban areas, roads, mines, and other human uses. Since the industrial revolution, humans have transformed more than half of Earth's ice-free land surface. Tropical forests have been cleared at particularly high rates in the 20th and 21st centuries.

Even when the total area of a habitat type is maintained, dividing it into smaller, isolated pieces can dramatically reduce its biodiversity. Habitat fragmentation is the process by which a large, continuous habitat is broken into smaller patches separated by different land uses — farmland, roads, suburbs.

Three Problems Caused by Fragmentation

Reduced patch size. Smaller habitat patches cannot support species that require large territories — large predators, migratory species, species with naturally low population densities.

Edge effects. The boundary between a habitat patch and the surrounding landscape has different conditions than the interior: higher light, lower humidity, greater temperature extremes, more invasive species and predators. As patches shrink, the ratio of edge to interior increases.

Isolation. Species in isolated patches cannot move between patches to find mates, track resources, or recolonize after local extinction. Small, isolated populations are more vulnerable to random events — disease, drought, fire — because there is no nearby source population to repopulate the habitat.

The study of how species numbers relate to habitat area was formalized in the theory of island biogeography, developed by ecologists Robert MacArthur and E.O. Wilson in the 1960s. Working initially with oceanic islands, they showed that the number of species an island supports is a predictable function of its area and its distance from a source of colonizers. The same principles apply to habitat fragments surrounded by human-altered land. This framework predicts, and field studies confirm, that fragmentation drives species loss even when some habitat remains.

Wildlife corridors — strips of habitat connecting patches — can partially offset fragmentation by allowing movement between patches. Road underpasses and overpasses have been built in many regions to allow wildlife to cross highways safely, with documented success in reducing roadkill and maintaining genetic diversity in isolated populations.

Section 25.9

Invasive Species

Throughout Earth's history, species have colonized new regions naturally — carried by wind and water, crossing land bridges during periods of lower sea level. The current era is different in scale. Human transportation has connected every region of the world, enabling species to move across barriers that would have taken thousands of years to cross naturally — or that would never have been crossed at all.

An invasive species is a non-native species that establishes a self-sustaining population and spreads in a way that causes ecological or economic harm. Not all introduced species become invasive — the vast majority fail to establish. But a small fraction spread rapidly and displace native species. Invasive species are the second-largest cause of species extinction globally after habitat loss.

Why Some Species Become Invasive

MechanismExplanation
Release from natural enemiesA species arriving in a new environment may leave behind its predators, parasites, and diseases. In the new environment, it faces a native community that has not co-evolved with it and may lack effective defenses.
Competitive advantagesInvasive plants often grow faster, reproduce earlier, or tolerate a wider range of conditions than native species. Invasive animals may be more aggressive competitors for food or nesting sites.
DisturbanceHuman disturbance — clearing, grazing, urban development — often creates conditions that favor invasives over native species adapted to the predisturbance environment.
Case Study 25.9A
Brown Tree Snakes and Guam's Forest Birds

Brown tree snakes were introduced accidentally to Guam after World War II. They eliminated most of the island's native forest bird species — 9 of 12 native forest birds are now extinct on Guam. Having evolved on an island with no native snake predators, the birds had no effective defenses. In the absence of the birds, insect pest populations have grown and seed dispersal has declined, altering the structure of the forest. The cascading effects from a single introduced predator continue to reshape the entire ecosystem decades later.

Case Study 25.9B
Cheatgrass and Fire Cycles in the American West

Cheatgrass, introduced from Europe, has spread across sagebrush steppe ecosystems of the American West. Unlike native grasses, cheatgrass grows and dries early in the season, creating dry fuel by early summer. This has dramatically increased fire frequency in a region where fire was historically rare. Native sagebrush and the animals that depend on it — including greater sage-grouse — are not adapted to frequent fire. Post-fire recovery favors cheatgrass over natives, creating a self-reinforcing cycle: cheatgrass invades → fire frequency increases → natives decline further → cheatgrass expands. Prevention — blocking introduction — is far more effective than control after establishment.

Section 25.10

Ecosystem Resilience and Conservation

Ecosystem resilience is the capacity of an ecosystem to absorb disturbance and reorganize while undergoing change, returning to essentially the same state after disturbance. Resilient ecosystems bounce back from fire, flood, drought, or disease. Less resilient ecosystems may be pushed into a different state — a different community structure, a different set of functions — from which they recover slowly or not at all.

Factor That Increases ResilienceHow It Works
BiodiversityMultiple species performing similar ecological roles provide functional redundancy — if one species is disrupted, others compensate
ConnectivityEcosystems connected to source populations of native species can be replenished after disturbance; isolated patches cannot
Physical stabilityStable soils, reliable water supply, and climate within the historical range of variation reduce background stress
Intact ecological processesNatural fire, flooding, and grazing operating within historical ranges maintain structures that support resilience; suppressing these often reduces resilience even while appearing to increase short-term stability

Some ecosystems can exist in two or more alternative stable states and can shift abruptly from one to another when a threshold is crossed. A shallow lake maintained in a clear-water state by aquatic plants can shift to a turbid, algae-dominated state when nutrient loading exceeds a threshold — and this shift can be very difficult to reverse even if nutrient loading is then reduced. Coral reefs can shift from coral-dominated to algae-dominated states following bleaching events or overfishing of herbivorous fish. Understanding these thresholds is critical for conservation: managing ecosystems to keep them well within their thresholds — rather than allowing conditions to approach the tipping point — is a core principle of resilience management.

Economic Tools in Conservation

One increasingly influential approach is finding economic mechanisms that make intact ecosystems more valuable than converted ones. Payments for ecosystem services programs pay landowners to maintain or restore natural land for services such as carbon storage, water purification, or watershed protection. Conservation easements — legal agreements in which landowners voluntarily restrict development rights in exchange for tax benefits or payments — have protected millions of acres of private land in North America. These approaches recognize that most land is privately owned and that conservation will require working with private landowners, not just protecting public lands.

No Single Tool Is Sufficient

Conservation biologists broadly agree that meeting the challenges of the current biodiversity crisis requires addressing all major drivers simultaneously: reducing habitat loss rates, expanding and connecting protected areas, addressing climate change, managing invasive species, and reducing overexploitation of wild species — all at scale and over the long term. No single approach, by itself, is adequate.

Chapter 25 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 25.1Ecosystems consist of biotic (living) and abiotic (nonliving) components that interact continuously. They are open systems exchanging matter and energy with their surroundings. The biosphere includes all of Earth's ecosystems and interacts with the geosphere, hydrosphere, and atmosphere.
  • Sec 25.2A habitat is the physical place where an organism lives. A niche is the organism's complete ecological role — what it eats, what eats it, and all its environmental tolerances and interactions. Resource partitioning allows multiple species to coexist in the same habitat. Keystone species have disproportionately large effects on their ecosystems relative to their abundance.
  • Sec 25.3Energy flows from producers through primary, secondary, and tertiary consumers. Approximately 10 percent of energy is transferred at each trophic level; the rest is lost as heat. Food webs are more stable than simple chains. Trophic cascades occur when the addition or removal of a predator triggers indirect effects through multiple trophic levels. Decomposers return nutrients to the environment, enabling continued production.
  • Sec 25.4Biomes are large geographic regions defined primarily by temperature and precipitation. Major terrestrial biomes include tropical rainforest, savanna, desert, chaparral, temperate grassland, temperate deciduous forest, boreal forest, and tundra. Aquatic biomes include freshwater, wetland, estuarine, and marine systems. Ecotones are transitional zones between biomes. Convergent evolution produces similar ecosystem structures in similar climates on different continents.
  • Sec 25.5Biodiversity encompasses genetic, species, and ecosystem levels of variety. More diverse ecosystems tend to be more productive, more efficient, and more resilient. Biodiversity is concentrated in tropical regions and biodiversity hotspots. Current extinction rates are estimated to be 100 to 1,000 times background rates, driven primarily by human activity.
  • Sec 25.6Ecosystem services are the benefits functioning ecosystems provide: provisioning (food, water, timber), regulating (climate regulation, water purification, flood control), cultural (recreation, education), and supporting (nutrient cycling, soil formation). Many conversions of natural land to agricultural or urban use are economically counterproductive when ecosystem service losses are counted.
  • Sec 25.7Ecological succession is the predictable sequence of community changes following disturbance. Primary succession begins on bare substrate; secondary succession follows disturbance of existing ecosystems where soil remains. Pioneer species initiate primary succession. Ecosystems are dynamic equilibria, not static climax endpoints — allowing natural disturbances is often essential to maintaining ecosystem structure.
  • Sec 25.8Habitat loss — conversion of natural land to human uses — is the primary driver of biodiversity loss. Habitat fragmentation reduces patch size, increases edge effects, and isolates populations. The theory of island biogeography predicts that larger, better-connected habitat patches support more species. Wildlife corridors can partially offset fragmentation by allowing movement between patches.
  • Sec 25.9Invasive species are non-native species that establish and spread in new regions, causing ecological or economic harm. They often succeed through release from natural enemies, competitive advantages, or by exploiting human-disturbed habitats. Invasive species are the second-largest cause of extinction globally after habitat loss. Prevention is far more effective than control after establishment.
  • Sec 25.10Ecosystem resilience — the capacity to absorb disturbance and return to essentially the same state — depends on biodiversity, connectivity, physical stability, and intact ecological processes. Some ecosystems can shift abruptly between alternative stable states when thresholds are crossed. Conservation approaches operate at species, habitat, landscape, and restoration scales. Economic tools including payments for ecosystem services and conservation easements extend conservation beyond public lands.

Key Terms Review

Abiotic
The nonliving physical components of an ecosystem: water, minerals, temperature, light, and gases.
Biodiversity
The variety of life at genetic, species, and ecosystem levels.
Biodiversity hotspot
A region of high species richness and endemism that has also experienced severe habitat loss.
Biome
A large geographic region characterized by a distinctive climate and community of organisms.
Biomass
The total mass of living organic material in a defined area or trophic level.
Biosphere
The zone of Earth where life exists; all of Earth's ecosystems combined.
Biotic
The living components of an ecosystem: plants, animals, fungi, bacteria, and other organisms.
Coral bleaching
The expulsion of symbiotic algae from coral tissue, usually caused by elevated water temperature, leaving coral white and vulnerable.
Decomposer
An organism (primarily bacteria and fungi) that breaks down dead organic matter and returns nutrients to the environment.
Ecological succession
The process of community change in an ecosystem over time following disturbance.
Ecotone
The transitional zone between two adjacent biomes or habitat types; often biologically rich.
Ecosystem
A community of living organisms interacting with each other and with their nonliving physical environment.
Ecosystem resilience
The capacity of an ecosystem to absorb disturbance and return to essentially the same state.
Ecosystem services
The benefits that functioning ecosystems provide to people: provisioning, regulating, cultural, and supporting services.
Edge effect
Ecological conditions at the boundary between two habitat types; different from interior conditions, often harsher for interior-adapted species.
Food chain
A linear sequence of feeding relationships tracing energy flow from producers to consumers.
Food web
The network of overlapping food chains in an ecosystem; more realistic than a simple chain.
Habitat
The physical place where an organism lives; the combination of environmental conditions it requires.
Habitat fragmentation
The division of a continuous habitat into smaller, isolated patches by roads, agriculture, or development.
Invasive species
A non-native species that establishes and spreads in a new region, causing ecological or economic harm.
Keystone species
A species whose impact on its ecosystem is disproportionately large relative to its abundance.
Niche
The complete role of an organism in its ecosystem: what it eats, what eats it, how it interacts with other species, and what conditions it tolerates.
Permafrost
Permanently frozen soil, characteristic of arctic tundra and some alpine regions; stores vast quantities of organic carbon.
Pioneer species
The first organisms to colonize bare or disturbed substrate during primary succession.
Primary producer
An organism that captures solar or chemical energy to produce organic matter from inorganic compounds; an autotroph.
Primary succession
Succession beginning on bare substrate where no ecosystem has previously existed.
Resource partitioning
The division of ecological roles among species in the same habitat, reducing direct competition and allowing coexistence.
Secondary succession
Succession following disturbance of an existing ecosystem; faster than primary succession because soil remains.
Species richness
The number of different species present in an area; the most commonly measured component of biodiversity.
Ten percent rule
The approximation that about 10 percent of energy stored at one trophic level is transferred to the next; the rest is lost as heat.
Trophic cascade
Indirect effects in a food web triggered by the addition or removal of a predator, propagating through multiple trophic levels.
Trophic level
A feeding level in a food web: producers, primary consumers, secondary consumers, and so on.
Wildlife corridor
A strip of habitat connecting isolated habitat patches, allowing organisms to move between them.

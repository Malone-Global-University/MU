Chapter 7 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Sedimentary rocks are Earth's memory. Every layer of sandstone, limestone, and shale preserves a record of an environment that once existed — a beach, a reef, a river delta, a shallow sea. In this chapter you will learn how sediment forms, how it moves, how it becomes rock, and what it tells scientists about the past. You will also learn why fossils occur almost exclusively in sedimentary rocks and how geologists use rock layers to reconstruct ancient landscapes and climate conditions.

Sedimentary rocks cover roughly 75 percent of Earth's land surface and nearly all of the shallow ocean floor. They are the primary source of fossil fuels, groundwater, building stone, and dozens of industrial minerals. Understanding how they form and what they record is essential for reading Earth's history, managing natural resources, and interpreting how environments change over time.

Opening Question

If you picked up a piece of red sandstone from a canyon wall in Utah, you would be holding rock made from ancient sand dunes — a desert that existed roughly 180 million years ago. The dunes are gone. The desert is gone. But the cross-bedded layers inside the rock still record the direction the wind blew on a day when dinosaurs walked nearby. How does a pile of sand become a rock that preserves that level of detail? And what other secrets can sedimentary rocks reveal about a planet's past?

Section 7.1

Sediments and Surface Processes

Walk along any river bank and you will see sand, gravel, and mud. Walk across a desert and you will find dunes of fine-grained silt. Look at the bottom of a quiet lake and you will find a soft layer of clay and organic material. All of these loose, unconsolidated particles are sediment.

Sediment is not random debris. It is the product of surface processes — the physical and chemical forces that break down existing materials at Earth's surface and move them from one place to another. Those forces include water, wind, ice, gravity, and the chemical reactions between minerals and the atmosphere or rainwater.

Surface processes are constantly at work. A raindrop striking bare soil dislodges individual particles. A river picks up those particles and carries them downstream. A wave breaks against a cliff and slowly grinds rock into sand. A glacier drags boulders across bedrock and leaves behind a layer of mixed debris. In every case, material is being broken down, transported, and eventually deposited somewhere new.

When sediment comes to rest and accumulates in sufficient quantities, it can eventually become sedimentary rock through a process called lithification. Lithification means turning loose sediment into solid rock through compaction and cementation — processes explained in Section 7.3.

Sedimentary rocks make up only about 5 percent of Earth's crust by volume, but they cover roughly 75 percent of the land surface and nearly all of the shallow ocean floor. Because sedimentary rocks form at Earth's surface and preserve the environments in which they were deposited, they are the primary source of information about Earth's past climate, geography, and life.

Section 7.2

Weathering, Erosion, Transport, and Deposition

Before sediment can become sedimentary rock, it must go through a sequence of stages: weathering, erosion, transport, and deposition. These four processes work together as a system, and each stage influences what kinds of sediment eventually end up in any given location.

Weathering

Weathering is the process by which rocks and minerals at Earth's surface are broken down into smaller pieces or altered chemically. Weathering does not move material from one place to another — it only breaks it apart or changes its composition.

Mechanical weathering breaks rock into smaller pieces without changing the minerals' chemical composition. Frost wedging — water freezing in cracks and expanding — is a common example. Chemical weathering actually changes the minerals themselves through reactions with water, oxygen, carbon dioxide, and acids. Feldspars react with slightly acidic rainwater to form clay minerals. Quartz is far more resistant to chemical weathering, which is why quartz grains are so common in sand. The products of weathering become the raw material for sediment.

Erosion

Erosion is the process by which weathered material is loosened and removed from its original location. Erosion requires energy — typically from moving water, wind, or ice. The rate of erosion depends on the energy of the erosional agent, the resistance of the surface, and how much protective cover such as vegetation exists.

Transport

Once sediment has been eroded, it enters transport — movement away from the source. The medium of transport determines a great deal about the sediment's final character. Water sorts sediment by size; heavy particles settle out first, lighter ones travel farther. Wind is even more selective. Ice carries everything together regardless of size — large boulders and fine clay mixed indiscriminately.

During transport, sediment particles collide with each other and with the streambed. This abrasion rounds the particles and makes them smoother over time. Sand grains reworked by waves for thousands of years are nearly spherical; freshly broken rock fragments are angular and irregular.

Deposition

Deposition occurs when the energy of the transporting medium drops below what is needed to keep particles moving. A river slowing as it enters a flat delta drops its sediment load. Wind losing speed drops its sand. The environment where deposition occurs — the depositional environment — strongly influences what kind of sedimentary rock will eventually form.

Section 7.3

Compaction and Cementation

Loose sediment does not automatically become rock just because it is deposited. Two processes must occur to transform sediment into solid sedimentary rock. Together they are called lithification.

Compaction

As sediment accumulates in layers over time, the weight of newer layers presses down on the layers below. This pressure squeezes water out of the pore spaces between grains and pushes the grains closer together. The process is called compaction. It reduces the volume of the sediment, increases its density, and begins to make it more rigid. Compaction is most effective for fine-grained sediment like clay and silt, which can lose more than half their original volume. Compaction alone rarely produces true sedimentary rock; a second process is required.

Cementation

Cementation is the process by which dissolved minerals precipitate in the pore spaces between grains and bind them together. Groundwater moving through the sediment carries dissolved ions — commonly calcium carbonate (calcite), silicon dioxide (quartz), or iron oxide. As these ions precipitate, they form cement that glues grains together into solid rock.

The type of cement affects the rock's color and strength. Calcite cement gives rocks a white or light tan color. Iron oxide cement — essentially rust — produces the red and orange hues common in many sandstones. Quartz cement produces some of the hardest sandstones. Lithification typically takes thousands to millions of years, usually at depths where pressure is sufficient.

Systems Connection

Lithification connects the rock cycle to water systems. Groundwater is the agent of cementation — it carries dissolved minerals through sediment and deposits them in pore spaces. Without the movement of water through Earth's crust, most sediment would remain loose and unconsolidated indefinitely.

Section 7.4

Clastic Sedimentary Rocks

Clastic sedimentary rocks (sometimes called detrital rocks) form from fragments — clasts — of pre-existing rock that have been weathered, transported, deposited, and lithified. What makes them clastic is that they consist of physical fragments rather than minerals that precipitated in place. Clastic rocks are classified primarily by grain size.

Grain SizeSediment NameRock Name
Greater than 2 mmGravel, pebbles, bouldersConglomerate (rounded) / Breccia (angular)
0.0625–2 mmSandSandstone
0.004–0.0625 mmSiltSiltstone
Less than 0.004 mmClayShale (layered) / Mudstone

Conglomerate and Breccia

Conglomerate consists of rounded gravel- and pebble-sized clasts cemented together. The rounded shape indicates significant transport distance — enough time for abrasion to smooth the clasts. Conglomerates form in high-energy environments such as fast-moving mountain streams, beaches, and alluvial fans.

Breccia is similar but contains angular rather than rounded clasts. Angular fragments have not been transported far enough to become rounded. Breccias can form at the base of cliffs, in fault zones, or near volcanic eruptions.

Sandstone

Sandstone is one of the most common and economically important sedimentary rocks. It forms from sand-sized grains — usually quartz — cemented together. Texture tells geologists much about the depositional environment: well-sorted, well-rounded sandstone with cross-bedding indicates desert dunes or a beach; poorly sorted, angular sandstone indicates a river closer to the sediment source. Sandstone is important as a reservoir rock for groundwater and petroleum because its pore spaces can hold fluids.

Siltstone and Shale

Siltstone forms from silt-sized particles and typically forms in environments with moderate energy — river floodplains, tidal flats, and shallow lake floors.

Shale is the most common sedimentary rock. It forms from clay-sized particles compacted and cemented in quiet, low-energy environments: deep ocean floors, lake bottoms, and river floodplains. Shale has a distinctive property called fissility — it splits along flat planes parallel to the original depositional layers. Mudstone is similar but breaks into irregular blocky chunks rather than flat layers.

Shale is scientifically invaluable. Because it accumulates slowly in quiet water, it can preserve microfossils, organic material, and thin chemical layers that record ancient ocean conditions with remarkable detail.

Worked Example 7.4.1
Interpreting a clastic rock's history
  • ObservationA geologist finds a coarse-grained rock with well-rounded pebbles of granite, quartzite, and basalt, all cemented together in a sandy matrix.
  • ThinkLarge grain size means high-energy environment. Rounded pebbles mean significant transport and abrasion. Mixed rock types (granite, quartzite, basalt) suggest multiple source rocks — a varied landscape nearby.
  • InterpretThe rounded pebbles and coarse grain size point to conglomerate. The most likely depositional environment is a high-energy river or a beach with strong wave action. The rounding indicates the pebbles traveled a substantial distance from their source.
  • AnswerThis rock is a conglomerate. It formed in a high-energy depositional environment, most likely a fast-moving river or wave-washed beach, with material derived from a landscape containing multiple rock types.
Section 7.5

Chemical Sedimentary Rocks

Chemical sedimentary rocks form when minerals precipitate directly from water — either because the water becomes saturated and minerals can no longer stay dissolved, or because water evaporates and leaves minerals behind.

Limestone

Limestone is the most common chemical sedimentary rock, composed primarily of calcite (calcium carbonate, CaCO₃). Most limestone forms in warm, shallow marine environments where calcium carbonate precipitates from seawater or accumulates as the hard parts of marine organisms. Chemically precipitated limestone can form tiny rounded grains called ooids or thin layers of calcite mud.

Limestone is critically important in Earth systems. It stores enormous amounts of carbon, connecting the rock cycle to the carbon cycle and to long-term climate regulation. Slightly acidic groundwater dissolves limestone over time, creating caves, sinkholes, and springs in what are called karst systems. (Chapter 17 explores groundwater and karst in detail.)

Chalk is a soft, white limestone made of microscopic calcite shells of marine organisms called coccolithophores. Dolostone is similar to limestone but contains magnesium carbonate — it typically forms when magnesium-rich fluids later replace some calcium in existing limestone.

Chert

Chert is a very hard, fine-grained rock made of silicon dioxide (SiO₂). It can form from the accumulated silica shells of microscopic marine organisms, or it can precipitate directly from silica-rich water on the ocean floor. Chert is extremely resistant to weathering. Some of the earliest evidence of microbial life on Earth is found in ancient chert over 3 billion years old. Prehistoric peoples used chert and the related rock flint to make sharp cutting tools because the rock breaks with a razor-sharp edge.

Evaporites

Evaporites form when a body of water evaporates and leaves its dissolved minerals behind. As water evaporates, it becomes more concentrated until minerals begin to precipitate in sequence: calcium carbonate first, then calcium sulfate, then sodium chloride (halite), and finally potassium and magnesium salts. Rock salt (halite) and rock gypsum are the most common evaporites, found in thick beds formed in ancient evaporating seas and lakes. Large evaporite deposits beneath the Gulf of Mexico and the Mediterranean Sea record episodes when those ocean basins nearly dried up millions of years ago.

Section 7.6

Organic Sedimentary Rocks

A third category of sedimentary rock forms from the accumulation and transformation of organic material — the remains of once-living organisms. These are sometimes called biochemical sedimentary rocks because biological processes play a central role.

Bioclastic Limestone

Many limestones are built largely from the shells, skeletons, and hard parts of marine organisms — corals, clams, oysters, brachiopods, sea urchins, and microscopic plankton. When these organisms die, their remains accumulate on the seafloor and lithify. Coquina is a loose, crumbly limestone made almost entirely of shell fragments. Fossiliferous limestone contains clearly visible fossil fragments throughout. Reef limestone preserves ancient coral reef structure in remarkable detail, recording ancient ocean temperatures, water depths, and sea levels.

Coal

Coal is an organic sedimentary rock formed from the compressed remains of plant material — mostly trees, ferns, and other land plants — that accumulated in ancient swamps. When plants die and fall into oxygen-poor swamp water, they decompose only partially to form soft, spongy peat. With burial, pressure, and heat, peat is gradually transformed:

Coal Formation Sequence

Peat → Lignite (brown coal) → Bituminous coal (soft coal) → Anthracite (hard coal)

Each stage involves greater compression, loss of water, and concentration of carbon. Anthracite is more than 90 percent carbon and burns at very high temperatures.

Most of Earth's great coal deposits formed during the Carboniferous Period (approximately 300–360 million years ago), when vast tropical forests covered large portions of the continents. Coal's importance as a fuel source — and the carbon dioxide released when it is burned — directly connects organic sedimentary rocks to today's energy systems and climate discussions. (These connections are explored further in Chapters 21 and 26.)

Oil Shale

Oil shale is a fine-grained sedimentary rock containing organic material called kerogen — the waxy remains of marine organisms and algae that accumulated in low-oxygen lake or ocean floor environments. With enough heat and time, kerogen transforms into oil and natural gas. Most conventional petroleum originates in organic-rich shale (the source rock), where heat converts kerogen into hydrocarbons that then migrate into porous reservoir rocks such as sandstone or limestone.

Section 7.7

Fossils and Sedimentary Layers

One of the most important features of sedimentary rocks is that they preserve fossils — the remains, impressions, traces, and chemical signatures of once-living organisms. Fossils occur almost exclusively in sedimentary rocks because:

Why Fossils Are Found in Sedimentary Rocks

Sedimentary environments bury organisms rapidly, protecting them from scavengers and decay.

The low-temperature, low-pressure conditions of sediment burial can preserve organic material rather than destroying it.

Igneous and metamorphic processes — high heat, pressure, and melting — destroy organic remains.

How Fossils Form

A fossil usually begins when an organism — or part of one — is buried in sediment shortly after death. Soft tissues decay, but hard parts such as shells, bones, and teeth may survive long enough to be replaced by minerals. In permineralization, groundwater moving through the sediment slowly deposits minerals in the pores of bone or shell, gradually converting the organic structure into rock.

Trace fossils are preserved evidence of behavior: footprints, burrows, feeding marks, and trails. They reveal not just what organisms existed but how they moved and lived. Mold and cast fossils form when a shell or bone dissolves away, leaving a cavity (the mold) that may later fill with minerals (the cast). Carbon films — thin residues of carbon — can preserve remarkable detail in flat organisms such as leaves and soft-bodied animals.

Index Fossils

Some fossils are particularly useful for establishing the age of rock layers. An index fossil comes from an organism that lived during a short, well-defined period of geologic time, had a wide geographic distribution, and is easily recognized. When the same index fossil appears in rock layers at widely separated locations, geologists conclude both layers are approximately the same age. This technique — fossil correlation — is a key method in reconstructing Earth's geologic history. Chapter 10 will explore correlation and geologic time in detail.

Section 7.8

Sedimentary Structures

The structures formed during deposition — the internal arrangement of layers, surface features, and patterns of bedding — are preserved in sedimentary rocks and carry important information about the energy, direction, and environment of deposition.

Bedding

The most fundamental sedimentary structure is bedding (also called stratification) — the parallel layers visible in most sedimentary rock. Each layer represents a period of deposition. Thick beds indicate sustained, high-energy deposition. Thin beds called laminae indicate slow, gentle deposition or seasonal variation. In an undisturbed sequence, younger layers lie on top of older ones — the law of superposition, introduced fully in Chapter 10.

Cross-Bedding

Cross-bedding occurs when sediment layers are deposited at an angle to the main bedding surface — typically by a current of water or wind forming dunes or ripples. As sediment moves over a dune crest and cascades down the downwind side, it builds thin inclined layers called foresets. Cross-bedding is a powerful environmental indicator: large-scale cross-bedding with consistent compass direction suggests ancient sand dunes; herringbone cross-bedding (alternating directions) suggests a tidal flat; small-scale irregular cross-bedding suggests a river channel.

Ripple Marks

Ripple marks are small, regular ridges on the surface of a sediment layer formed by moving water or wind. Asymmetric ripples form in a one-directional current — the steep face points downstream or downwind. Symmetric ripples form where water oscillates back and forth, as in wave action on a shallow seafloor. Ripple size is roughly proportional to current strength.

Graded Bedding

Graded bedding is a pattern in which grain size changes from coarse at the bottom to fine at the top within a single layer. It forms when a turbulent flow of water carrying mixed sediment decelerates: heavy particles settle first, light particles settle later. Graded beds are characteristic of turbidites — sediment deposited by turbidity currents, which are dense, gravity-driven underwater avalanches that rush down the continental slope and spread across the deep ocean floor.

Mud Cracks and Sole Marks

Mud cracks form when wet, fine-grained sediment dries out and contracts, leaving a polygonal network of cracks that can be filled by the next sediment layer and preserved. Mud cracks indicate periods of exposure to air — a tidal flat that dried at low tide, or a lake margin that periodically dried out.

Sole marks are impressions on the bottom surfaces of beds formed by currents scouring the underlying soft sediment. They indicate the direction of ancient current flow, allowing geologists to reconstruct paleoflow — which direction ancient water or wind currents were moving.

Section 7.9

Sedimentary Rocks as Environmental Records

Sedimentary rocks are not passive containers for minerals and fossils. They are dense with information about the physical, chemical, and biological conditions at the time of deposition. When geologists read sedimentary rocks carefully, they can reconstruct past environments in remarkable detail — a practice called paleoenvironmental reconstruction.

Case Study 7.9.1
Reading a Canyon Wall Sequence
  • Lowest LayerConglomerate — gravel-sized, rounded clasts → high-energy river or beach environment
  • Next LayerWell-sorted sandstone with large-scale cross-bedding; medium sand, good rounding → ancient desert dune field
  • Next LayerLimestone with coral and shell fossils → warm, shallow marine environment (the sea advanced over the land)
  • Top LayerBlack shale with only microscopic fossils → low-oxygen, deep or quiet water (the sea deepened or became stratified)
  • InterpretationOver millions of years, the location changed: first a river, then a desert, then a sea, then a deeper quieter body of water. Sedimentary sequences record the migration of environments across the landscape driven by sea level change, climate, tectonics, and subsidence.

Sedimentary Rocks and Climate History

The chemistry of sedimentary rocks is a powerful climate record. The ratio of two oxygen isotopes — oxygen-16 and oxygen-18 — in carbonate fossils changes predictably with ancient ocean temperature. Scientists have used oxygen isotope ratios in microscopic marine fossils from deep-sea sediment cores to reconstruct ocean temperatures over millions of years, producing a detailed record of ice ages and warm periods. Carbon isotope ratios in carbonate rocks record changes in the amount of organic material being buried — a connection to ancient atmospheric carbon dioxide levels.

Sedimentary Rocks and Natural Resources

Many of humanity's most important natural resources come from sedimentary rocks. Petroleum and natural gas originate in organic-rich shale and migrate into porous sandstone and limestone reservoirs. Coal is an organic sedimentary rock burned for energy. Groundwater is stored in porous sandstone and limestone aquifers. Building stone — limestone, sandstone, and travertine — has been used in construction for thousands of years. Industrial minerals such as gypsum (for drywall) and halite (for food and chemical production) come from evaporite deposits. Phosphate, used in fertilizers, comes from marine sedimentary deposits.

Systems Connection — Sedimentary Rocks and the Carbon Cycle

Limestone stores carbon in solid rock, removing it from the atmosphere for millions of years. When limestone is weathered by slightly acidic rain, that carbon is released back into solution and eventually into the ocean or atmosphere. When fossil fuels (coal, oil, gas) are burned, carbon stored in organic sedimentary rock for hundreds of millions of years is released as CO₂ in decades. Sedimentary rocks are therefore one of Earth's largest long-term carbon reservoirs — central to understanding both natural climate cycles and current climate change.

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 7.1Sediment is loose, unconsolidated material produced by surface processes. Sedimentary rocks cover 75 percent of Earth's land surface despite being only about 5 percent of crust by volume. Lithification converts sediment to rock through compaction and cementation.
  • Sec 7.2Sediment production follows four stages: weathering (breakdown), erosion (removal), transport (movement), and deposition (settling). Water sorts particles by size; ice does not. Transport rounds and smooths particles. The depositional environment determines the character of the sediment and ultimately the rock.
  • Sec 7.3Compaction squeezes out pore water and reduces volume under the weight of overlying sediment. Cementation precipitates minerals in pore spaces to bind grains into solid rock. Both processes together are called lithification and typically take thousands to millions of years.
  • Sec 7.4Clastic rocks form from transported fragments classified by grain size: conglomerate (rounded gravel), breccia (angular gravel), sandstone (sand), siltstone (silt), and shale/mudstone (clay). Grain size reflects the energy of the depositional environment. Shale is the most common sedimentary rock.
  • Sec 7.5Chemical sedimentary rocks form by mineral precipitation from water. Limestone consists of calcite and connects to the carbon cycle and karst water systems. Chert consists of silica and is extremely resistant to weathering. Evaporites such as rock salt and gypsum form when bodies of water evaporate.
  • Sec 7.6Organic (biochemical) sedimentary rocks form from accumulated biological material. Bioclastic limestone forms from marine shells and skeletons. Coal forms through the progressive burial and compression of plant material: peat → lignite → bituminous → anthracite. Oil shale contains kerogen that can transform into petroleum with heat and time.
  • Sec 7.7Fossils occur almost exclusively in sedimentary rocks because burial in sediment protects organic material from destruction. Types include body fossils (permineralized), trace fossils, molds and casts, and carbon films. Index fossils — short-lived, geographically widespread species — allow geologists to correlate rock layers across distances.
  • Sec 7.8Sedimentary structures record depositional conditions. Bedding reflects layers of deposition. Cross-bedding records ancient current direction. Asymmetric ripples indicate one-directional current; symmetric ripples indicate wave oscillation. Graded bedding indicates turbidity currents. Mud cracks indicate exposure to air and drying.
  • Sec 7.9Combining rock type, fossils, and sedimentary structures allows reconstruction of ancient environments — rivers, deserts, seas, swamps. Oxygen isotope ratios record ancient ocean temperatures. Transgressive and regressive sequences record sea level change. Sedimentary rocks are the source of petroleum, coal, groundwater, building stone, and industrial minerals.

Mudstone
Fine-grained clastic rock similar to shale but breaking into blocky chunks rather than flat layers.
Organic sedimentary rock
Rock formed from accumulated biological material; includes coal, oil shale, and bioclastic limestone.
Ripple marks
Small, regular ridges preserved on a sedimentary layer surface, formed by moving water or wind.
Sandstone
Clastic rock made of sand-sized grains (usually quartz) cemented together; important reservoir rock for groundwater and petroleum.
Sediment
Loose, unconsolidated particles of mineral, rock, or organic material deposited by water, wind, ice, or gravity.
Shale
The most common sedimentary rock; fine-grained, clay-sized particles that split along flat planes (fissility).
Trace fossil
Preserved evidence of an organism's behavior, such as footprints, burrows, or feeding marks.
Transport
Movement of eroded sediment from its source to a depositional environment by water, wind, ice, or gravity.
Turbidite
Sedimentary deposit from an underwater turbidity current; characterized by graded bedding.
Weathering
The physical and chemical breakdown of rock and minerals at Earth's surface.

Common Misconceptions

Misconception 1 — "All sand becomes sandstone; all mud becomes shale."
Deposited sediment automatically becomes rock over time.
Becoming sedimentary rock requires burial, compaction, and cementation — not just deposition. Most sediment is reworked or re-eroded and never lithifies. Burial to sufficient depth and the movement of mineral-rich groundwater are usually necessary.
Misconception 2 — "Fossils form from any dead organism buried in sediment."
Burial guarantees fossilization.
Most organisms do not fossilize. Preservation requires rapid burial before scavengers destroy remains, the presence of hard parts (or rare conditions that preserve soft tissue), and the right chemistry for mineralization. Only a tiny fraction of organisms that ever lived appear in the fossil record.
Misconception 3 — "Limestone only forms from shells."
All limestone must contain visible shell fragments.
Limestone can form biochemically (from accumulated shells), chemically (direct precipitation from water), or both. Micrite — very fine carbonate mud — precipitates chemically or from the breakdown of shells into microscopic particles. Not all limestone contains visible fossils.
Misconception 4 — "Cross-bedding means the layers were tilted after deposition."
Cross-bedded layers must have been moved by tectonics.
Cross-bedding forms during deposition — it is the natural inclined arrangement of sediment on the downstream face of a dune or ripple. It is an original depositional structure, not a product of later deformation. Geologists must distinguish between original cross-bedding and tectonic tilting of the entire sequence.
Misconception 5 — "Coal is a mineral."
Coal is a mineral like halite or quartz.
Coal is a rock, not a mineral. Minerals have specific chemical formulas and crystal structures. Coal is a mixture of organic compounds formed from plant material and does not have a fixed chemical formula. It is classified as an organic sedimentary rock.

Chapter 8: Metamorphic Rocks and Rock Transformation

Chapter 7 showed that sedimentary rocks record what happens at Earth's surface. Chapter 8 moves deeper — into the conditions created by Earth's internal heat and the immense pressures that build during tectonic activity. When existing rocks are subjected to high temperature, high pressure, and chemically active fluids without fully melting, they transform into a new class of rocks called metamorphic rocks. Chapter 8 will explore how metamorphic rocks form, what they look like, what minerals they contain, and what they reveal about the deep history of mountain building and tectonic activity. The connection between the rock cycle and Earth's internal energy — only briefly touched on here — becomes central in Chapter 8.

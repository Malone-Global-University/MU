Appendix A

Mathematics and Data Skills for Earth Science

Earth and environmental science depends on quantitative reasoning at every scale. This appendix reviews the mathematical skills used throughout the textbook. No calculus or advanced algebra is required, but fluency with ratios, rates, percentages, unit conversions, graph reading, and scale interpretation is essential.

A.1 — Ratios and Percentages
Comparing Quantities and Parts of Wholes
Ratio

A ratio compares two quantities of the same type. Example: a rock sample has 3 parts quartz and 1 part feldspar. The ratio of quartz to feldspar is 3:1, or 3/1 = 3. Ratios can compare any two related quantities.

Percentage

A percentage expresses a part out of 100. If 42 of 200 water samples exceeded contamination limits, the percentage is: (42 ÷ 200) × 100 = 21%. To find a percentage change: [(new − old) ÷ old] × 100.

A.3 — Unit Conversion
Changing Units Without Changing Meaning
Conversion Procedure

Multiply by a conversion factor — a fraction equal to 1 — so the unwanted unit cancels. Example: convert 5 km to meters.

5 km × (1,000 m / 1 km) = 5,000 m

The km units cancel because they appear in both numerator and denominator.

A.4 — Rates of Change
How Fast Things Change
Rate Formula

Rate = Change in quantity ÷ Change in time

Example: a glacier retreated 2.4 km in 30 years. Rate = 2.4 ÷ 30 = 0.08 km/yr = 80 m/yr.

Rates in Earth science: erosion rate (mm/yr), sea-level rise rate (mm/yr), plate velocity (cm/yr), temperature change (°C/decade).

A.5 — Map Scale
Connecting Map Distance to Real Distance
Scale Types

Representative fraction (RF): 1 : 24,000 means 1 unit on the map = 24,000 units in reality.

Bar scale: A drawn segment labeled with real-world distance. Measure the segment, then proportion.

Formula: Real distance = Map distance × Denominator of RF

Example: 3.5 cm on a 1 : 50,000 map = 3.5 × 50,000 = 175,000 cm = 1.75 km.

Graph Reading Habit

Before reading a graph: (1) read the title, (2) read both axis labels and units, (3) note the scale on each axis, (4) look for trends before reading specific values. Never read a value from a graph without first checking the axis units.

A.7 — Averages and Variation
Summarizing Data Sets
Mean (Average)

Mean = Sum of all values ÷ Number of values

Example: Monthly precipitation (cm): 5, 8, 12, 18, 22, 25, 30, 28, 20, 14, 8, 4. Sum = 194. Mean = 194 ÷ 12 = 16.2 cm/month. Annual total = 194 cm.

Range = maximum − minimum. Tells you how spread out the data are.

A.8 — Models, Timelines, and Geometry
Spatial and Temporal Reasoning

Using Models Responsibly

  • A model simplifies reality — it leaves things out by design. A good model captures the most important features for a specific purpose.
  • All models have assumptions. Question those assumptions before accepting a model's output.
  • Compare model predictions to real data. If they disagree substantially, either the model or the data measurement needs scrutiny.

Timeline Interpretation

  • Geologic timelines are often logarithmic or compressed. Equal distances on the page may not represent equal time intervals.
  • Check the scale: is each tick mark 1 million years? 100 million? 1 billion?
  • Identify where you are on the timeline relative to major events (mass extinctions, plate collisions, ice ages).

Basic Geometry for Earth Science

  • Area of a rectangle: A = length × width (used in watershed area, farm field area)
  • Area of a circle: A = π × r² (used in impact crater size, lake area)
  • Volume of a rectangular prism: V = length × width × depth (used in reservoir volume)
  • Slope = rise ÷ run (used in topographic maps, river gradient)

Projection Misconception

No flat map can perfectly represent a sphere. Every map projection involves tradeoffs. The Mercator projection, commonly used in classrooms, makes Greenland appear similar in size to Africa — but Africa is about 14 times larger. Always check what a projection preserves before using it to compare areas or distances.

B.3 — Topographic Maps
Reading Elevation and Landform
Contour Line Rules
  • Every point on a contour line is at the same elevation.
  • Contour lines never cross each other.
  • Closely spaced contour lines indicate steep slopes; widely spaced indicate gentle slopes.
  • Contour lines form closed loops around hilltops.
  • Hachure marks (inward tick marks) indicate a depression (closed low point).
  • Contour lines that cross a stream form a V-shape pointing upstream (uphill).
  • The contour interval is the elevation difference between adjacent contour lines.
  • Index contours (every 5th line, usually darker and labeled) help read elevation quickly.

Reading an Elevation Profile

  • Draw a line across the topographic map where you want the profile.
  • Mark where each contour line intersects your profile line.
  • Transfer each intersection point to a graph, plotting elevation on the vertical axis and horizontal distance on the horizontal axis.
  • Connect the points to reveal the shape of the land surface.

C.5 — The Rock Cycle
How Rocks Transform Over Time
Rock Cycle Pathways
  • Magma cools → Igneous rock (intrusive if slow, extrusive if fast)
  • Igneous or metamorphic rock weathers → Sediment → buried and compressed → Sedimentary rock
  • Any rock subjected to heat and pressure (without melting) → Metamorphic rock
  • Any rock melted → Magma → eventually cools → Igneous rock
  • Sedimentary rock subducted or buried deeply → Metamorphic → if heated further → Magma

D.2 — Absolute Dating Basics
Radioactive Decay and Numerical Ages
Half-Life Concept

A half-life is the time required for half of a radioactive parent isotope to decay into a stable daughter isotope. After one half-life, 50% parent remains. After two half-lives, 25% remains. After three, 12.5%. The ratio of parent to daughter in a rock sample, combined with the known half-life, gives the age of the rock.

G.6 — The Greenhouse Effect and Carbon Cycle
Mechanisms of Climate Regulation
Greenhouse Effect (Natural)

Solar radiation (mostly visible light) passes through the atmosphere and warms Earth's surface. Earth's surface radiates energy back as infrared (heat) radiation. Greenhouse gases — water vapor, CO₂, methane, nitrous oxide — absorb infrared radiation and re-emit it in all directions, including back toward the surface. This keeps Earth ~33°C warmer than it would be without an atmosphere. The greenhouse effect is natural and essential for life.

10% Energy Rule

On average, only about 10% of the energy stored at one trophic level is transferred to the next. The remaining 90% is lost as heat, used for metabolism, or not consumed. This is why food chains rarely have more than four or five levels, and why it takes much more land and resources to produce animal protein than plant protein.

I.3 — Emergency Preparedness Basics
What Every Student Should Know

Earthquake Safety

  • During shaking: Drop, Cover, and Hold On — take cover under a sturdy table or against an interior wall away from windows
  • Do not run outside during shaking — falling debris is a major hazard
  • After shaking: check for injuries and structural damage before moving; expect aftershocks
  • If near the coast after a large earthquake: move to high ground immediately without waiting for official warning

Flood and Hurricane Safety

  • Never drive through flooded roads — even shallow fast-moving water can sweep away a vehicle
  • Follow official evacuation orders; do not wait to "see what happens"
  • Know your flood zone status and have an evacuation route planned in advance
  • Have an emergency kit: water (1 gallon per person per day for 3+ days), food, medications, documents, flashlight, radio

Wildfire Safety

  • Monitor local alerts and be ready to leave immediately if evacuation is ordered
  • Create defensible space around structures in fire-prone areas
  • Know multiple evacuation routes; wildfires can block roads quickly

5All models simplify reality. A strength is that they allow prediction and testing of hypotheses. A limitation is that any model omits some features and may fail under conditions outside those it was designed for.

1Earth's energy sources: solar radiation (drives weather, water cycle, photosynthesis), Earth's internal heat (drives plate tectonics, volcanic activity), and gravity (drives erosion, ocean tides, compaction).
3If water remains in the deep ocean for an average of ~3,000 years before returning to the surface, its residence time in that reservoir is approximately 3,000 years.
Chapter 5 — Minerals and Earth Materials
1Quartz: hardness 7, no cleavage, conchoidal fracture, vitreous luster. Calcite: hardness 3, rhombohedral cleavage, fizzes in HCl, double refraction. These properties allow unambiguous identification.
3A fingernail (~2.5) can scratch gypsum (hardness 2) but not calcite (hardness 3). A copper coin (~3.5) can scratch calcite.
Chapter 6 — Igneous Rocks and Volcanic Processes
1Granite: coarse-grained (slow cooling underground). Basalt: fine-grained (rapid cooling at surface). Crystal size is determined by cooling rate, not composition alone.
3Obsidian has no visible crystals because it cooled too rapidly for crystals to grow — it is volcanic glass.
Chapter 7 — Sedimentary Rocks and Surface Processes
1Conglomerate (large rounded gravel) → sandstone (sand) → shale (mud) indicates a decrease in energy from high-energy to low-energy depositional environment over time or distance from source.
3Ripple marks preserved in sandstone indicate a shallow water or windblown environment at the time of deposition. Cross-bedding often indicates dunes or strong current direction.
Chapter 10 — Geologic Time and Earth History
1Sequence from oldest to youngest (law of superposition): layer D → C → B → A. If a fault cuts layers A through D, the fault is younger than all four layers (cross-cutting relationship).
3After two half-lives, 25% of the parent isotope remains. After three half-lives, 12.5% remains. If the half-life is 1.25 billion years and 25% parent remains, the rock is approximately 2.5 billion years old.
Chapter 12 — Plate Tectonics
1Mid-ocean ridges are young and high (newly formed hot crust); ocean trenches occur at subduction zones where old, cold, dense oceanic crust descends into the mantle.
3Evidence for plate tectonics includes: matching continental shapes, matching rock types and fossils across oceans, magnetic striping on seafloor, and direct GPS measurement of plate motion.
Chapter 16 — The Water Cycle and Freshwater Systems
1Approximately 97.5% of Earth's water is salt water. Of the 2.5% freshwater, about 69% is in glaciers and ice sheets, 30% is groundwater, and less than 1% is in surface water and the atmosphere.
3If annual precipitation is 800 mm and evapotranspiration removes 550 mm, then the remaining 250 mm must either recharge groundwater or become runoff (800 − 550 = 250 mm).
Chapter 19 — The Atmosphere
1The atmosphere is 78% nitrogen, 21% oxygen, ~0.93% argon, and ~0.04% carbon dioxide, plus water vapor and trace gases. Oxygen and nitrogen together make up 99% of dry air.
3Temperature increases with altitude through the stratosphere because ozone absorbs UV radiation from the Sun. Temperature decreases with altitude in the troposphere because the surface is the primary heat source.
Chapter 21 — Climate and Climate Change
1Natural climate variations include orbital cycles (Milankovitch cycles), volcanic eruptions, and solar output changes. These cannot explain the rapid post-1950 warming trend, which correlates with greenhouse gas increases.
3Mitigation reduces future emissions (e.g., transitioning to renewable energy). Adaptation adjusts to unavoidable impacts (e.g., building seawalls, developing drought-resistant crops). Both strategies are needed.
Chapter 25 — Ecosystems, Biomes, and Biodiversity
1If a top predator is removed, herbivore populations may explode, overgrazing vegetation, which can increase erosion and reduce habitat complexity for other species — a trophic cascade.
3Ecosystem services include: clean water filtration (wetlands), pollination (bees and other insects), carbon storage (forests), flood buffering (floodplains), and coastal protection (mangroves and coral reefs).
Chapter 28 — Natural Hazards, Risk, and Resilience
1Risk = Hazard × Exposure × Vulnerability. A community with low exposure (not in a floodplain) has low flood risk even if the hazard is high. A community with high vulnerability (poor-quality construction) has high earthquake risk even for moderate seismicity.
3A 100-year flood has a 1% annual probability of being exceeded — not that it happens once every hundred years. Two 100-year floods can occur in the same decade.

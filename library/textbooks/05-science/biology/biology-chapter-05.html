Chapter 5 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Life is chemistry, but chemistry organized into something that can grow, respond, remember, and reproduce. The molecules studied in this chapter are the raw material of that organization.

Everything alive is made of molecules. Your muscles contract because of proteins. Your cells store energy in carbohydrates and fats. Your genetic information is written in nucleic acids. Every signal your body sends, every structure it builds, every reaction it runs depends on the chemistry of a small set of molecular families.

This chapter introduces the four major families of biological molecules — carbohydrates, lipids, proteins, and nucleic acids — together with the one substance that makes all of them functional: water. These are sometimes called the molecules of life or biomolecules because they are found in every living system, from bacteria to blue whales.

Understanding biomolecules is not about memorizing lists of terms. It is about learning a fundamental biological principle: molecular structure determines biological function. The shape of a molecule, the way its atoms are arranged, the chemical groups it carries — all of these determine what that molecule can do inside a living cell. Change the structure, and you change the function. This principle will appear again and again throughout your study of biology.

Opening Question

A loaf of bread and a wooden board are both made largely of glucose — the same simple sugar molecule. Yet one feeds you and the other does not. How can the same building block produce materials with such completely different properties? By the end of this chapter, you will understand how the way monomers are connected determines what a molecule can do.

Section 5.1

Molecules of Life

Living things are made primarily of four elements: carbon (C), hydrogen (H), oxygen (O), and nitrogen (N). Smaller but essential amounts of phosphorus (P), sulfur (S), and other elements also appear in living systems. These elements are not rare — they are among the most common atoms on Earth. What makes living matter distinctive is not the elements themselves but the way they are assembled.

The molecules that make up life fall into four major categories. All four groups are often called the macromolecules of life because they tend to be large molecules built by linking smaller units together. The smaller units are called monomers. The large molecules built from monomers are called polymers.

Macromolecule FamilyMonomer UnitPrimary Biological Roles
CarbohydratesMonosaccharide (e.g., glucose)Energy fuel; structural support (cell walls, exoskeletons)
LipidsGlycerol + fatty acids (not a true monomer–polymer system)Energy storage; cell membranes; hormones; waterproofing
ProteinsAmino acidEnzymes, structure, transport, immunity, signaling, movement
Nucleic acidsNucleotideStoring and transmitting genetic information; protein synthesis
Why Carbon?

Carbon can form four covalent bonds and link with other carbon atoms in chains, branches, and rings. This structural flexibility gives organic chemistry its extraordinary variety. Life exploits that variety to build the molecular tools it needs. All four macromolecule families are organic molecules — they are all built on a carbon framework.

Section 5.2

Water as the Medium of Life

Before examining any of the four macromolecule families, it is important to understand water. Water is not one of the four macromolecule groups, but it is the substance that makes all of them functional. Every biological process on Earth takes place in or near water. Cells are mostly water by mass. Reactions in cells happen in water. Nutrients are carried in water. Wastes are removed in water.

The Structure of Water

A water molecule consists of one oxygen atom bonded to two hydrogen atoms (H₂O). Oxygen attracts electrons more strongly than hydrogen does — the electrons in the O–H bonds are pulled toward the oxygen side. This creates a slight negative charge (δ−) at the oxygen end and a slight positive charge (δ+) at the hydrogen ends. A molecule with this kind of charge separation is called a polar molecule.

Because water is polar, the slightly positive hydrogen of one molecule is attracted to the slightly negative oxygen of a neighboring molecule. This attraction is called a hydrogen bond. Any individual hydrogen bond is weak, but water molecules form enormous numbers of them simultaneously. Collectively, hydrogen bonds give water a set of properties that make life possible.

PropertyExplanationBiological Importance

Hydrophilic and Hydrophobic
  • Hydrophilic (water-loving): polar molecules and ions that dissolve readily in water because they interact with water's polarity. Examples: glucose, salt, amino acids.
  • Hydrophobic (water-fearing): nonpolar molecules that do not dissolve in water and tend to cluster away from it. Examples: fats and oils.

This distinction is crucial for understanding cell membranes, protein folding, and lipid behavior.

Section 5.3

Carbohydrates

Carbohydrates are molecules made of carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen, typically in roughly a 1:2:1 ratio (CₙH₂ₙOₙ). Sugars are the simplest carbohydrates. Carbohydrates serve two primary roles in living systems: energy (simple sugars are a cell's most readily available fuel) and structure (some carbohydrates form the rigid frameworks that support cells, tissues, and organisms).

Monosaccharides: The Simplest Sugars

The monomer unit of carbohydrates is the monosaccharide. Monosaccharides are simple sugars that cannot be broken down into smaller sugar units. The most important monosaccharide in biology is glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆) — the primary fuel molecule for cellular respiration. Glucose, fructose, and galactose all share the same molecular formula (C₆H₁₂O₆) but have different arrangements of atoms, which means cells treat them differently.

Disaccharides: Two Sugars Joined

When two monosaccharides are joined together, the product is a disaccharide. The bond between them is called a glycosidic bond, and a water molecule is released (a condensation reaction). Disaccharides can be broken back into monosaccharides by adding water — a hydrolysis reaction.

Lactose Intolerance — A Molecular Explanation

The enzyme lactase breaks lactose into glucose and galactose. People who do not produce enough lactase cannot break down milk sugar efficiently. This is a direct example of how enzyme structure determines function: without the right enzyme shape, the chemical reaction cannot proceed effectively.

Polysaccharides: Long Chains of Sugars

Polysaccharides are polymers built from many monosaccharide monomers. The function of a polysaccharide depends on the specific way its monomers are connected — not merely what the monomers are.

PolysaccharideMonomerFunctionFound In

Common Misconception — Cellulose Is Just Starch

Cellulose and starch are both made of glucose, but the bonds between glucose units are oriented differently. This structural difference means starch is digestible by most organisms and cellulose is not. The same building block, assembled in a different way, produces entirely different materials — this is structure determining function in one of its clearest forms.

Section 5.4

Lipids

Lipids are defined not by a common structure but by a common property: they do not dissolve in water. Lipids are hydrophobic — nonpolar molecules that are repelled by water. Unlike carbohydrates, proteins, and nucleic acids, lipids are not polymers built from repeating monomer units. They are a collection of related hydrophobic molecules that biology uses for a variety of purposes.

Fats and Oils: Triglycerides

A triglyceride consists of one glycerol molecule linked to three fatty acid chains. Each fatty acid is a long hydrocarbon chain with an acid group at one end. The long hydrocarbon tails are strongly nonpolar, which is why triglycerides do not mix with water. Triglycerides store more energy per gram than carbohydrates, making them excellent long-term energy reserves.

TypeBond StructurePhysical StateExamples
Saturated fatNo double bonds; straight chains pack tightlySolid at room temperatureButter, lard, coconut oil
Unsaturated fatOne or more double bonds; kinked chains cannot pack tightlyLiquid (oil) at room temperatureOlive oil, canola oil, fish oil

Phospholipids: The Foundation of Cell Membranes

Phospholipids are structurally similar to triglycerides, but one fatty acid chain is replaced by a phosphate-containing group. This creates a molecule with two distinct regions: a hydrophilic head (the glycerol + phosphate group) and a hydrophobic tail (the two fatty acid chains). A molecule with both water-loving and water-fearing regions is called amphipathic.

When phospholipids are placed in water, they spontaneously organize into a phospholipid bilayer: the hydrophobic tails face inward (away from water) and the hydrophilic heads face outward (toward the surrounding water). This bilayer is the structural basis of every cell membrane on Earth — and it forms spontaneously, driven by the physical properties of the molecule itself, without any additional instructions.

Steroids

Steroids have a distinctive structure of four fused carbon rings. All steroids share this framework, but different functional groups give different steroids different roles. Cholesterol is found in animal cell membranes where it affects fluidity and stability; it also serves as the starting material for steroid hormones such as testosterone, estrogen, and cortisol. Because steroids are hydrophobic, they can pass directly through cell membranes and enter cells to influence gene activity.

Section 5.5

Proteins

Proteins are the most functionally diverse of the four macromolecule families. They serve as structural components, catalysts, transporters, regulators, receptors, immune defenders, motors, and signals. Nearly every biological process depends on proteins.

All proteins are polymers built from monomers called amino acids. There are 20 different amino acids used by living organisms. Each has the same basic structure — a central carbon bonded to an amino group (–NH₂), a carboxyl group (–COOH), a hydrogen atom, and a distinctive side chain called an R group. The R group is what makes each amino acid different. Some R groups are hydrophobic; others are hydrophilic. Some carry charges. Some contain sulfur. These differences ultimately determine the shape and function of every protein.

Peptide Bonds and Polypeptides

Amino acids are joined by peptide bonds. A peptide bond forms when the carboxyl group of one amino acid reacts with the amino group of the next, releasing a water molecule (condensation). A chain of amino acids linked by peptide bonds is called a polypeptide. Most proteins consist of one or more polypeptide chains that fold into a specific three-dimensional shape. The sequence of amino acids — the primary structure — is determined by genetic information and is the starting point from which all protein properties emerge.

Key Idea — Shape Determines Function

The function of a protein depends critically on its three-dimensional shape. A protein that loses its shape loses its function. The shape of a protein is the sum of all interactions among its amino acids and between the protein and the surrounding water environment.

Denaturation

When a protein is exposed to extreme heat, strong acids or bases, or certain chemicals, it can lose its three-dimensional shape — a process called denaturation. A denatured protein cannot perform its normal function. Cooking an egg denatures its proteins; the white shifts from clear and fluid to white and solid. Very high fevers can denature proteins in the body, which is one reason they are medically dangerous. In many cases, if conditions return to normal quickly enough, some proteins can refold and regain function, but severe denaturation is often irreversible.

Why Such Diversity?

The diversity of protein functions reflects the enormous variety of shapes that can be produced from sequences of just 20 amino acids. Because the number of possible amino acid sequences is astronomically large, the number of possible protein shapes — and therefore functions — is correspondingly vast.

Section 5.6

Nucleic Acids

Nucleic acids are the molecules of heredity and information. They store genetic instructions, transmit those instructions from parents to offspring, and carry information within cells to direct protein synthesis. The two types are DNA (deoxyribonucleic acid) and RNA (ribonucleic acid).

Nucleic acids are polymers built from monomers called nucleotides. Each nucleotide consists of three components: a five-carbon sugar (deoxyribose in DNA; ribose in RNA), a phosphate group (negatively charged; links nucleotides in a chain), and a nitrogenous base (the information-carrying part).

DNA: The Double Helix

DNA consists of two strands of nucleotides wound around each other in a double helix. The backbone of each strand alternates phosphate groups and deoxyribose sugars. The nitrogenous bases project inward, where they pair with bases on the opposite strand according to strict rules — complementary base pairing: A pairs with T, and C pairs with G. The two strands are held together by hydrogen bonds between the paired bases.

The sequence of bases along a DNA strand encodes genetic information, much as the sequence of letters on a page encodes language. Different sequences of bases specify different instructions, which ultimately direct the assembly of different proteins. This sequence is what is passed from parents to offspring.

RNA: The Molecular Messenger

RNA is similar to DNA but differs in three important ways: it is single-stranded; it contains ribose sugar instead of deoxyribose; and it uses uracil instead of thymine. RNA serves as an intermediary between the genetic instructions in DNA and the proteins built by ribosomes.

ATP: Nucleotide as Energy Currency

Not all nucleotides carry genetic information. Adenosine triphosphate (ATP) is a nucleotide that serves as the primary energy currency of cells. ATP consists of adenine, ribose, and three phosphate groups. When the bond between the second and third phosphate groups is broken — releasing one phosphate group and producing ADP (adenosine diphosphate) — energy is released and made available for cellular work. You will study ATP in depth in the chapters on photosynthesis and cellular respiration.

DNA vs. RNA — Key Differences
  • DNA is double-stranded; RNA is single-stranded
  • DNA contains deoxyribose sugar; RNA contains ribose sugar
  • DNA uses thymine (T); RNA uses uracil (U) in its place
  • DNA stores genetic information long-term; RNA carries and interprets that information

Section 5.7

Monomers and Polymers

Three of the four macromolecule families — carbohydrates, proteins, and nucleic acids — share a common organizational pattern: large molecules are built by joining many smaller subunits. The small subunits are monomers; the large assembled molecules are polymers. (Lipids are hydrophobic biological molecules but do not fit the strict monomer-polymer pattern.)

Building Polymers: Condensation Reactions

Polymers are assembled by condensation reactions (also called dehydration synthesis). In a condensation reaction, two monomers are joined and a water molecule is released. This type of reaction requires an input of energy. Every peptide bond in a protein and every glycosidic bond in a polysaccharide was formed this way.

Breaking Polymers: Hydrolysis Reactions

Polymers are broken apart by hydrolysis. In hydrolysis, a water molecule is added across the bond between monomers, breaking the bond and releasing the individual monomers. Digestion is largely a process of hydrolysis — your digestive system breaks the polymers in food into monomers small enough to absorb into the bloodstream.

Condensation (dehydration synthesis)Monomers → PolymerReleased (one molecule per bond formed)Linking amino acids to build a protein

Section 5.8

Molecular Structure and Biological Function

The theme running through this entire chapter is one of the most important principles in all of biology: structure determines function. The properties and abilities of any biological molecule depend entirely on its structure — on the specific arrangement of its atoms, the types of chemical groups it carries, and the shape it adopts in three dimensions.

Examples of Structure Determining Function

Cellulose vs. Starch

Both are made of glucose units. But the bonds linking those glucose units are oriented differently — producing straight, rigid chains in cellulose and coiled, branching chains in starch. Starch is digestible by most organisms. Cellulose is not. The same monomer, connected differently, produces entirely different materials.

Saturated vs. Unsaturated Fatty Acids

A single double bond in a fatty acid chain creates a kink that prevents tight packing. Saturated fatty acids (no double bonds, straight chains) pack tightly and are solid at room temperature. Unsaturated fatty acids (one or more double bonds, kinked chains) cannot pack as tightly and are liquid. One structural difference — the presence of a double bond — explains why butter is solid and olive oil is liquid.

Protein Shape and Enzyme Specificity

Each enzyme has a precisely shaped region called an active site that fits only certain molecules (its substrates). Change the shape of the active site — by mutating an amino acid, changing the temperature, or shifting the pH — and the enzyme no longer works. Protein shape and protein function are inseparable.

Complementary Base Pairing in DNA

The specific shapes of adenine and thymine allow them to form exactly two hydrogen bonds with each other. The shapes of cytosine and guanine allow exactly three. This precise geometry — determined by molecular structure — is the basis of genetic fidelity: when DNA is copied, complementary base pairing ensures the sequence is reproduced accurately.

The Phospholipid Bilayer

The amphipathic structure of phospholipids — hydrophilic head, hydrophobic tails — dictates how they organize in water. This self-organization produces cell membranes without any additional instructions. The structure of the molecule creates the structure of the membrane.

Section 5.9

Biomolecules in Food, Cells, and Medicine

Biomolecules in Food

When you eat, you consume a mixture of carbohydrates, lipids, and proteins. Your digestive system breaks macromolecules into monomers through hydrolysis. Those monomers are then absorbed and either reassembled into new polymers your body needs or broken down further to release energy. Nutritional labels reflect macromolecule categories: carbohydrates and proteins provide about 4 kilocalories per gram; fats provide about 9 kilocalories per gram, making them the most energy-dense macromolecule. Dietary fiber — mostly cellulose and other plant polysaccharides that human enzymes cannot break — provides little energy but plays important roles in digestive health.

Biomolecules in Medicine

Insulin is a protein hormone produced today through genetic engineering: the human insulin gene is inserted into bacteria, which produce human insulin in large quantities. Before the 1980s, insulin for people with diabetes was extracted from animal pancreases.

Antibiotics often work by targeting specific biomolecules in bacteria. Penicillin interferes with enzymes that bacteria use to build cell walls. Because human cells lack those enzymes, penicillin harms bacteria without harming human cells — a direct application of structural differences between organisms.

Cholesterol is an essential lipid that every cell membrane needs, but excess cholesterol in the blood can contribute to plaque formation in arteries. Understanding cholesterol as a hydrophobic lipid helps explain why it can accumulate in blood vessel walls rather than remaining dissolved in blood plasma.

Biomolecules in Industry and Technology

Enzymes are used industrially in enormous quantities: enzymes that break down lactose are added to dairy products; proteases are used in meat tenderizers and laundry detergents; amylases are used in baking and brewing. Carbohydrates from plant material (cellulose and starch) are the starting point for biofuels and biodegradable plastics. Understanding the chemistry of plant biomolecules is increasingly important as society looks for alternatives to petroleum-based products.

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 5.1Living things are made primarily of carbon, hydrogen, oxygen, and nitrogen assembled into four macromolecule families: carbohydrates, lipids, proteins, and nucleic acids. Three families follow a monomer-to-polymer pattern. All are organic molecules built on carbon frameworks.
  • Sec 5.2Water is a polar molecule that forms hydrogen bonds, giving it high specific heat, strong solvent ability, cohesion and adhesion, and the ability to participate in condensation and hydrolysis reactions. Hydrophilic substances dissolve in water; hydrophobic substances do not.
  • Sec 5.3Carbohydrates are made from monosaccharide monomers linked by glycosidic bonds. Simple sugars (glucose) provide energy. Polysaccharides — starch and glycogen for energy storage; cellulose and chitin for structure — illustrate how the same monomer assembled differently produces different functions.
  • Sec 5.4Lipids are defined by hydrophobicity. Triglycerides store energy; saturated fats are solid and unsaturated fats are liquid. Phospholipids are amphipathic and spontaneously form the bilayer basis of cell membranes. Steroids including cholesterol serve membrane and hormonal functions.
  • Sec 5.5Proteins are polymers of amino acids connected by peptide bonds. Their shape — determined by primary, secondary, tertiary, and quaternary structure — determines their function. Denaturation destroys shape and function. Proteins serve as enzymes, structural components, transporters, hormones, immune proteins, and receptors.
  • Sec 5.6Nucleic acids (DNA and RNA) are polymers of nucleotides. DNA stores genetic information in its base sequence; its double helix is held together by complementary base pairing (A–T, C–G). RNA is single-stranded and carries/interprets genetic information. ATP is a nucleotide that serves as cellular energy currency.
  • Sec 5.7Condensation reactions build polymers by joining monomers and releasing water. Hydrolysis reactions break polymers by adding water across bonds. Digestion depends on hydrolysis; cellular construction depends on condensation. Enzymes catalyze both.
  • Sec 5.8Molecular structure determines biological function. The same glucose monomer produces starch or cellulose depending on how bonds are oriented. Protein shape determines enzyme specificity. Complementary base pairing geometry ensures faithful DNA copying. Phospholipid amphipathic structure produces self-assembling membranes.
  • Sec 5.9Biomolecules underlie nutrition (macronutrient categories), medicine (insulin production, antibiotic mechanisms, cholesterol), and technology (industrial enzymes, biofuels). Understanding molecular structure and function is essential for all these applications.

Key Terms Review

amino acid
The monomer of proteins; consists of a central carbon bonded to an amino group, a carboxyl group, a hydrogen, and a variable R group (side chain).
amphipathic
Having both hydrophilic and hydrophobic regions in the same molecule; characteristic of phospholipids and the basis of membrane formation.
carbohydrate
A biomolecule made of carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen in roughly a 1:2:1 ratio; includes monosaccharides, disaccharides, and polysaccharides.
cellulose
A structural polysaccharide forming plant cell walls; made of glucose units connected by bonds that most organisms cannot break.
chitin
A structural polysaccharide (modified glucose with nitrogen) found in arthropod exoskeletons and fungal cell walls.
cholesterol
A steroid lipid in animal cell membranes that affects fluidity and serves as the starting material for steroid hormones.
complementary base pairing
The rule that adenine pairs with thymine (in DNA) or uracil (in RNA), and cytosine pairs with guanine; the basis of genetic information storage and copying.
condensation reaction
A chemical reaction in which monomers are joined and a water molecule is released; also called dehydration synthesis. Builds polymers.
denaturation
The loss of a protein's three-dimensional structure due to heat, extreme pH, or chemicals; typically results in loss of function.
disaccharide
A carbohydrate consisting of two monosaccharides joined by a glycosidic bond; examples include sucrose, lactose, and maltose.
DNA
Deoxyribonucleic acid; the double-stranded nucleic acid that stores genetic information in the sequence of its nitrogenous bases.
fatty acid
A long hydrocarbon chain with a carboxyl group at one end; the main energy-storing component of fats and oils.
glycogen
The energy-storage polysaccharide in animals; stored primarily in the liver and muscles; more highly branched than plant starch.
glycosidic bond
The covalent bond linking monosaccharides in a carbohydrate polymer; formed by a condensation reaction.
hydrolysis
A chemical reaction in which a polymer is broken into monomers by the addition of water across a bond. Digestion relies on hydrolysis.
hydrophilic
Attracted to water; characteristic of polar molecules and ions. From Greek hydro (water) and philos (loving).
hydrophobic
Repelled by water; characteristic of nonpolar molecules such as lipids. From Greek hydro (water) and phobos (fear).
hydrogen bond
A weak attraction between a partially positive hydrogen atom and a partially negative atom in a neighboring molecule; responsible for many of water's properties.
lipid
A hydrophobic biomolecule; includes fats (triglycerides), phospholipids, steroids, and waxes. Defined by insolubility in water, not by a shared structure.
macromolecule
A very large molecule; in biology, refers collectively to carbohydrates, lipids, proteins, and nucleic acids — the four families of biomolecules.
monomer
A small molecular subunit that serves as the building block of a polymer. Examples: monosaccharide, amino acid, nucleotide.
monosaccharide
The monomer of carbohydrates; a simple sugar such as glucose, fructose, galactose, or ribose. Cannot be broken into smaller sugars.
nitrogenous base
The information-carrying component of a nucleotide; in DNA: A, T, C, G; in RNA: A, U, C, G.
nucleic acid
A polymer of nucleotides; includes DNA (double-stranded, stores genetic info) and RNA (single-stranded, transmits and interprets genetic info).
nucleotide
The monomer of nucleic acids; consists of a five-carbon sugar, a phosphate group, and a nitrogenous base.
peptide bond
The covalent bond that joins amino acids in a polypeptide chain; formed by a condensation reaction between an amino group and a carboxyl group.
phospholipid
An amphipathic lipid with a hydrophilic phosphate head and two hydrophobic fatty acid tails; the main structural component of cell membranes.
phospholipid bilayer
The double layer of phospholipids forming the basis of cell membranes; hydrophilic heads face outward, hydrophobic tails face inward. Forms spontaneously.
polar molecule
A molecule with an uneven distribution of charge, resulting in slightly positive and slightly negative regions. Water is the most biologically important polar molecule.
polymer
A large molecule made of many monomers joined together by condensation reactions. Examples: starch, protein, DNA.
polypeptide
A chain of amino acids joined by peptide bonds; the primary structure of a protein. Most proteins consist of one or more folded polypeptide chains.
polysaccharide
A carbohydrate polymer made of many monosaccharide units; examples include starch, glycogen, cellulose, and chitin.
primary structure
The linear sequence of amino acids in a polypeptide; determined by the gene encoding that protein; the foundation for all higher-order protein structure.
protein
A polymer of amino acids; the most functionally diverse macromolecule family, serving as enzymes, structural components, transporters, hormones, antibodies, receptors, and more.
RNA
Ribonucleic acid; a single-stranded nucleic acid involved in protein synthesis. Types: mRNA (messenger), rRNA (ribosomal), tRNA (transfer).
saturated fat
A triglyceride with no double bonds in its fatty acid chains; straight chains pack tightly; tends to be solid at room temperature.
starch
The energy-storage polysaccharide in plants; made of glucose units; digestible by most organisms. Amylose is unbranched; amylopectin is branched.
steroid
A lipid with a four-ring carbon structure; includes cholesterol, testosterone, estrogen, progesterone, and cortisol.
tertiary structure
The overall three-dimensional shape of a single polypeptide chain; results from interactions between R groups including hydrogen bonds, ionic bonds, hydrophobic clustering, and disulfide bonds.
triglyceride
A lipid consisting of glycerol bonded to three fatty acid chains; the primary form of fat used for long-term energy storage in organisms.
unsaturated fat
A triglyceride with one or more double bonds in its fatty acid chains; kinked chains prevent tight packing; tends to be liquid (an oil) at room temperature.

Structure-and-Function Practice

  1. Both cellulose and starch are made of glucose. Explain why one serves as a structural material and the other as an energy storage molecule.
  2. A scientist adds acid to a solution containing an enzyme. The acid changes the shape of the enzyme's active site. Predict what happens to the enzyme's ability to catalyze its reaction, and explain your reasoning.
  3. Phospholipids are described as amphipathic. Explain what this means and how it leads to the formation of a cell membrane.
  4. DNA and RNA both carry genetic information in the form of nucleotide sequences. Identify two structural differences between DNA and RNA and explain how those differences might relate to their different roles.
  5. A protein that normally transports oxygen has its structure disrupted by extreme heat. What do you predict happens to its oxygen-carrying ability? What does this tell you about the relationship between structure and function in proteins?

Common Misconceptions

Misconception 1
"Fats are always harmful."
Lipids are essential for life. Phospholipids form every cell membrane. Cholesterol is necessary for membrane function and hormone production. Triglycerides store energy the body uses during exercise or periods of low food intake. The concern about certain dietary fats in human health is a question of quantity, type, and context — not a reason to classify all fats as harmful.
Misconception 2
"Proteins are only for muscle."
Muscles are rich in structural proteins such as actin and myosin, but proteins perform a vast range of other functions: enzymes, hormones, immune proteins (antibodies), transport molecules (hemoglobin), receptor proteins, regulatory proteins, and more. Most cellular activity involves proteins in some way.
Misconception 3
"DNA and RNA are the same thing."
DNA and RNA are both nucleic acids made of nucleotides, but they differ in sugar type (deoxyribose vs. ribose), one base (thymine vs. uracil), and structure (double-stranded vs. single-stranded). These structural differences correspond to different biological roles: DNA stores genetic information long-term; RNA carries and interprets that information.
Misconception 4
"Cellulose is just starch."
Cellulose and starch are both glucose polymers, but the bonds between glucose units are oriented differently. This structural difference means starch is digestible by most organisms and cellulose is not. Calling them the same thing would be like saying two different buildings made of the same bricks are identical — what matters is how the bricks are assembled.
Misconception 5
"Denatured proteins are destroyed."
Denaturation changes a protein's shape but does not necessarily break the peptide bonds holding amino acids together. A denatured protein may be able to refold if conditions return to normal, though severe or prolonged denaturation is often irreversible. The amino acid chain remains intact even when the three-dimensional structure is lost.
Looking Ahead
Chapter 6: Cell Structure and Function

Chapter 5 introduced the molecular building blocks of life — water and the four macromolecule families. The next chapter moves up one level of biological organization to examine cells: the smallest units capable of carrying out all the essential activities of life.

You will see how the biomolecules studied here come together to form the structures inside cells. The phospholipids you learned about form the membranes that enclose every cell. The proteins you studied appear as enzymes in the cytoplasm, as structural components of organelles, and as receptors embedded in membranes. The DNA you read about is packaged inside the nucleus and carries the genetic information that directs every cellular activity.

Chapter 6 introduces the cell theory, the distinction between prokaryotic and eukaryotic cells, and the functions of the major organelles in plant and animal cells. Understanding what cells are and how they are organized is essential before studying how cells obtain energy (Chapters 9 and 10), divide (Chapter 11), or use genetic information (Chapters 12 and 13).

Chapter 17 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Every living thing alive today is the descendant of something that survived. Every trait that appears in modern populations — a bird's beak shape, a bacterium's resistance to antibiotics, the coloring of a moth's wings — exists because organisms carrying those traits left more offspring than organisms that did not.

This is evolution by natural selection: the process by which populations change over generations because some heritable traits improve survival and reproduction in a given environment. It is not a matter of intention, desire, or progress toward a goal. It is the predictable outcome of variation, inheritance, and differential survival playing out across generations.

Evolution is the unifying framework of biology. It explains why organisms are built the way they are, why life is so diverse, why diseases become resistant to drugs, why some species go extinct and others thrive, and why the human body carries structures that made sense in ancestral environments. Understanding natural selection means understanding not just how life changes, but why living things are the way they are right now.

Opening Question

Bacteria exposed to an antibiotic mostly die — but a few survive and reproduce. Within weeks, the surviving population is almost entirely resistant. No scientist designed this resistance. No bacterium chose to change. Yet the outcome is predictable. By the end of this chapter, you will understand exactly why this happens, and why the same logic applies to moth wings, finch beaks, giraffe necks, and the human immune system.

Section 17.1

What Evolution Means

The word evolution is sometimes used casually to mean almost any kind of change. In biology, it has a precise meaning.

Biological evolution is change in the heritable traits of a population over generations.

This definition has several important parts worth examining closely.

Change in heritable traits. Evolution involves traits — features of organisms — that can be passed from parent to offspring through genes. A population member that develops a scar during its lifetime has changed, but that change is not heritable. Evolution requires genetic change, not just individual change.

Of a population. Individuals do not evolve. A single organism lives out its life with the traits it has. Evolution is something that happens to populations — groups of individuals of the same species living in a shared area — over many generations.

Over generations. Evolution is a generational process. It is not something that happens within a single lifetime. Some evolutionary changes are rapid; others take millions of years. In all cases, evolution operates across reproductive generations.

Key Idea

Evolution explains both the enormous diversity of life on Earth and the deep similarities connecting all living things — shared DNA, shared genetic code, shared cellular organization — which reflect common ancestry. The diversity reflects divergence as populations adapted to different environments over billions of years.

Section 17.2

Variation Within Populations

Natural selection requires variation. If all individuals in a population were identical, selection would have nothing to act on. In any real population, individuals differ from one another in countless ways — in coloration, body size, disease resistance, behavior, developmental timing, and more.

This variation is observable and measurable. If you collected data on beak length in a population of finches, you would find a range of values distributed around a mean. Most individuals cluster near the average, with fewer individuals at the extremes. This continuous variation is found in most measurable traits.

Sources of Variation in Populations

SourceHow It Produces VariationNotes
MutationChanges in the DNA sequence; the original source of all new genetic variantsMost mutations are neutral or harmful; occasionally one improves fitness in a particular environment
Sexual reproduction and recombinationMeiosis shuffles and recombines alleles, producing offspring with novel trait combinationsDoes not create new alleles, but generates new combinations of existing variation
Gene flowIndividuals migrate between populations and reproduce, introducing alleles from elsewhereCan introduce variation that was absent from the receiving population
Why Variation Matters

Variation is the raw material of evolution. Without differences in heritable traits, natural selection has nothing to act on. The more variation exists in a population, the greater the potential for selection to shift the population's trait composition over generations.

Section 17.3

Heritable Traits

Not all variation is heritable. A tree that grows in a sunny location may be taller than the same species growing in shade. But if seeds from both are transplanted to identical conditions, the offspring will not consistently differ in height — that difference was caused by the environment, not by genes.

For natural selection to drive evolution, the traits that affect survival and reproduction must be heritable — they must have a genetic basis that can be passed from parent to offspring.

Heritability refers to the proportion of trait variation in a population that is due to genetic differences rather than environmental ones. Most biologically important traits — body size, coloration, disease resistance, behavioral tendencies — have genetic components that can respond to selection.

Example 17.3.1
Heritability and natural selection in a beetle population
  • SetupIn a population of beetles, shell color ranges from light brown to dark brown. Shell color is heritable: dark-shelled parents tend to produce dark-shelled offspring.
  • SelectionThe local environment is dark soil. Bird predators more easily spot light-colored beetles. Dark beetles survive predation more often and leave more offspring.
  • OutcomeOver generations, alleles associated with dark shell color become more common in the population. The frequency of light-shell alleles declines.
  • ConclusionThis is natural selection working on a heritable trait. Because color is heritable, the population's trait composition shifts. If color were not heritable, no shift would occur.
Key Point: Selection can only cause evolutionary change when the favored trait is heritable.
Important Distinction

A trait acquired during an organism's lifetime — a scar, a learned behavior, a muscle developed through exercise — is not heritable and therefore cannot be selected for. Evolution requires genetic variation, not just any variation.

Section 17.4

Overproduction and Competition

In 1798, the economist Thomas Malthus observed that human populations tend to grow faster than food supplies, leading to scarcity and competition. Charles Darwin read Malthus and recognized a biological principle: virtually all species produce far more offspring than can possibly survive.

Overproduction is the tendency of organisms to reproduce at rates that would generate far more offspring than the environment can support. A single oak tree may produce tens of thousands of acorns in a good year. A pair of rabbits could theoretically generate hundreds of descendants within a few years if all survived. A bacterium can divide every twenty minutes — in theory producing billions of descendants within a day. Yet populations do not spiral without limit. Most offspring die before reproducing.

Resources That Limit Survival and Reproduction

ResourceWho Competes For ItEffect of Scarcity
Food and nutrientsAll heterotrophs; also plants competing for soil nutrientsStarvation, reduced reproduction, growth failure

Connection to Natural Selection

Because more individuals are born than can survive, and because individuals vary in their traits, some variants will survive and reproduce better than others. Overproduction creates the competitive pressure that makes differential survival meaningful. Without it, there would be no filter — all individuals would survive equally and no selection would occur.

Section 17.5

Natural Selection

With variation, heritability, and overproduction established, natural selection follows as a logical consequence. Natural selection occurs when individuals with certain heritable traits survive and reproduce at higher rates than individuals without those traits, causing those traits to become more common in the population over generations.

Darwin's Four Conditions for Natural Selection

1. Variation exists. Individuals in a population differ in heritable traits.

2. Struggle for existence. More offspring are produced than can survive; resources are limited.

3. Differential survival and reproduction. Individuals with certain traits survive and reproduce at higher rates than others.

4. Inheritance. Traits are passed from parent to offspring.

When all four conditions are met, the composition of the population changes over time. Traits associated with higher survival and reproduction become more common; others become rarer.

Example 17.5.1
The peppered moth — natural selection documented in real time
  • BeforeBefore industrial pollution darkened tree bark with soot in England during the 1800s, most peppered moths (Biston betularia) were light-colored. Light coloration provided camouflage against pale lichen-covered bark. Dark-colored moths were rare — they were easily spotted and eaten by birds.
  • ChangeAs pollution killed lichens and blackened bark, the environment changed. Light moths became conspicuous against dark bark; dark moths were now camouflaged. Birds ate light moths at higher rates. Dark moths survived and reproduced more successfully.
  • OutcomeWithin a few decades, dark moths became the dominant form in polluted industrial areas. In unpolluted areas, light moths remained more common.
  • AnalysisThis satisfies all four conditions: heritable variation in wing color; overproduction and predation pressure; differential survival (dark survived better in sooty areas); inheritance of wing color. The population evolved.
Key Point: Natural selection is not random. It is the predictable, non-random outcome of variation, differential survival, and inheritance.
Natural Selection Is Not Random

Students sometimes confuse natural selection with random mutation. Mutation is random — it occurs without respect to whether it will be useful. Natural selection is not random: individuals with traits that improve survival in the current environment consistently leave more offspring. Selection has a direction determined by the environment.

Section 17.6

Adaptation

A trait that improves an organism's survival or reproduction in its environment is called an adaptation. Adaptations are features — structural, behavioral, or physiological — that have been favored by natural selection because they increase fitness in a particular environment. Adaptations are always relative to a particular environment.

TypeExampleProblem It SolvesEnvironment

Adaptation Does Not Mean Perfection

Natural selection cannot produce a perfectly designed organism. It can only work with existing variation in the population. It cannot anticipate future conditions. Many organisms carry structural compromises, evolutionary remnants, and solutions that work well enough without being optimal. A feature that is adaptive in one environment may be neutral or harmful in another — when environments change, which traits are adaptive changes too.

Section 17.7

Fitness in Biological Context

In everyday language, "fitness" suggests health, strength, or athletic ability. In biology, the word has a precise and narrower meaning.

Biological fitness is the relative reproductive success of an individual — how many offspring it contributes to the next generation relative to other individuals in the population.

An individual that is large, fast, and healthy but never reproduces has zero fitness. An individual that is small and slow but leaves many surviving, reproducing offspring has high fitness. Fitness is measured in offspring, not in strength. Fitness is always relative and always context-dependent.

Component of FitnessDescription
Survival to reproductive ageAn organism must survive long enough to reproduce; premature death = zero contribution
Mating successIn sexually reproducing species, finding and attracting a mate is necessary; the basis of sexual selection
Reproductive outputProducing offspring contributes to fitness; offspring quality matters alongside quantity
Offspring survivalOffspring must themselves survive to reproduce; parental care that improves offspring survival increases the parent's fitness
Example 17.7.1
Sexual selection — the peacock's tail
  • ObservationPeacock tail feathers are large, elaborate, brightly colored, and energetically expensive to grow and carry. They appear to reduce survival by making the bird more visible to predators and slower in flight.
  • ExplanationFemale peafowl prefer mates with larger, more elaborate tails. Males with more impressive tails mate more often and leave more offspring — even if they die slightly younger.
  • PrincipleThis is sexual selection: a form of natural selection in which traits that increase mating success are favored, even at some cost to survival. Fitness is not about surviving longest — it is about leaving the most descendants.
Key Point: Traits that reduce survival can still be favored by selection if they sufficiently increase mating success.
Section 17.8

Populations Change Over Time

Natural selection operates in different ways depending on which variants survive best. Three patterns of selection describe how the trait distribution of a population shifts over generations.

Mode of SelectionWhat Is FavoredEffect on PopulationExample
Directional selectionOne extreme of the trait distributionPopulation mean shifts in one direction over generationsAntibiotic resistance; finch beak size during drought
Stabilizing selectionIntermediate values; extremes selected againstMean stays the same; variation is reducedHuman birth weight — very low and very high both have higher infant mortality
Disruptive selectionBoth extremes; intermediates selected againstPopulation splits toward two peaks in trait distributionBeak sizes in some bird species where small and large exploit different food sources

Other Forces That Change Populations

Genetic drift is change in allele frequencies due to random chance, especially in small populations. In a small population, chance events — which individuals happen to reproduce, which are eaten by a predator, which survive a drought — can dramatically change allele frequencies regardless of fitness. Genetic drift is a form of evolution that is not driven by natural selection.

Gene flow — the movement of alleles between populations through migration — can change allele frequencies by introducing variants from elsewhere. Speciation occurs when populations diverge sufficiently through selection, drift, and reproductive isolation that they can no longer interbreed. Over long timescales, this process has produced the millions of species alive today.

Genetic Drift vs. Natural Selection

Both natural selection and genetic drift change allele frequencies, but through very different mechanisms. Selection changes allele frequencies because some alleles improve reproductive success. Drift changes allele frequencies by chance — some alleles become more common simply because those individuals happened to reproduce in that generation. Drift is most powerful in very small populations.

Section 17.9

Natural Selection in Medicine and Agriculture

Natural selection is not a process that only occurred in the distant past. It is happening continuously, including in contexts that directly affect human welfare.

Antibiotic Resistance

Antibiotic resistance is one of the most serious public health challenges in the world, and it is a direct product of natural selection.

When a person takes an antibiotic, the drug kills most bacteria in the infection. But in any large bacterial population, there is variation in how well individuals resist the drug. A few individuals may carry mutations that allow them to survive antibiotic exposure. These individuals survive and reproduce. Within a short time, the surviving population consists largely of antibiotic-resistant descendants.

Understanding resistance as a product of natural selection has direct practical implications: using antibiotics improperly — stopping a course early, taking them for viral infections, or using them in low doses — selects for resistant strains. The more antibiotics are misused, the faster resistance evolves.

Example 17.9.1
Antibiotic resistance — tracing natural selection step by step
  • Step 1A bacterial population infects a patient. Within that population, there is variation: most bacteria are sensitive to the antibiotic, but a few carry a mutation conferring resistance.
  • Step 2The patient is prescribed the antibiotic. Most bacteria die. The resistant few survive.
  • Step 3The surviving resistant bacteria reproduce rapidly. Within days, the infection is dominated by resistant cells.
  • Step 4Resistance genes can spread to other bacteria through horizontal gene transfer — even across different species.
  • AnalysisAll four conditions were met: variation (sensitive vs. resistant), overproduction (rapid bacterial reproduction), differential survival (resistant survived), inheritance (resistance is heritable). The population evolved.
Key Point: The antibiotic did not cause the mutation. It selected for mutations that already existed in the population.

Pesticide Resistance

The same logic applies to pest control. When a pesticide is applied to a population of insects, most die. But individuals with heritable resistance survive and reproduce. Over generations, the resistant variants become common and the pesticide loses effectiveness. Resistance to many classes of insecticides has evolved in hundreds of pest species worldwide. Resistance to DDT, organophosphates, and pyrethroids are well-documented examples.

Artificial Selection in Agriculture

Humans have been practicing a form of directed selection — called artificial selection — for thousands of years. By selectively breeding organisms with desirable traits, humans have dramatically changed domesticated species.

Modern corn (maize) descends from a wild grass called teosinte that bore only a few small seeds. Over thousands of years of selecting seeds from the most productive plants, humans produced corn with large cobs bearing hundreds of starchy kernels — a plant that looks almost nothing like its ancestor. Cattle, chickens, dogs, wheat, rice, and virtually every domesticated plant and animal have been transformed by artificial selection. The mechanism is identical to natural selection: heritable variation, differential reproduction, trait change over generations. The only difference is that humans, rather than the environment, are doing the selecting.

Natural SelectionArtificial Selection
Selecting agent is the environmentSelecting agent is human preference
Favors traits that improve survival and reproductionFavors traits that humans find desirable (yield, flavor, behavior)

Evolution of Viruses

Influenza viruses evolve rapidly because they have high mutation rates and short generation times. Each year, the influenza virus population changes enough that vaccines must be reformulated. This is directional selection in action: immune pressure from vaccines and prior infections selects for viral variants that evade recognition. The COVID-19 pandemic provided a highly visible, real-time demonstration of how a viral population can rapidly produce new variants as it spreads through a population under immune pressure.

Section 17.10

Common Misunderstandings About Evolution

Evolution is one of the most misunderstood topics in biology. Clearing up common errors is part of understanding the science correctly.

Misconception 1 — "Individuals evolve."

Individuals do not evolve. Individual organisms live, change through growth and experience, and die with the traits they had. Evolution is change in the trait composition of populations across generations.

Correction

When we say a species evolved larger brains, we mean that alleles associated with larger brain size became more common in the population over many generations. No individual's brain grew because of natural selection within that lifetime.

Misconception 2 — "Evolution has a direction or goal."

Natural selection has no foresight, intention, or goal. It does not push populations toward complexity, perfection, or "higher" forms.

Correction

Selection favors traits that happen to improve survival and reproduction in the current environment. If the environment changes, what is favored changes. Bacteria are not "lower" forms of life than mammals — they are extremely well-adapted organisms that have been evolving for billions of years.

Misconception 3 — "Organisms evolve because they need to."

No organism evolves because it needs to or wants to. An individual bacterium does not become antibiotic resistant because it needs to survive the drug.

Correction

Variation arises through mutation and recombination, not through desire or need. The drug selects for individuals that happen to have resistance — a trait that was already present before the drug was introduced. Need plays no role. Existing variation does.

Misconception 4 — "Evolution is only a theory."

In everyday language, "theory" sometimes means a guess or unproven idea. This causes confusion when applied to scientific theories.

Correction

In science, a theory is a well-tested explanation supported by extensive evidence from multiple independent lines of inquiry. The atomic theory, the germ theory of disease, and the theory of gravity are all "theories" in this sense. Evolution is supported by fossil evidence, comparative anatomy, molecular biology, direct observation of evolutionary change, and genetics. The fact that scientists call it a theory reflects its scientific strength, not weakness.

Misconception 5 — "Survival of the fittest means the strongest survive."

"Fitness" in everyday language suggests physical strength or health. This makes "survival of the fittest" sound like a competition of brute force.

Correction

Fitness in biology means reproductive success. The "fittest" organisms are those that leave the most offspring — which may require being fast, or cryptically colored, or drought tolerant, or resistant to disease, or skilled at parental care. A small, slow organism that leaves many surviving descendants is more fit, in the biological sense, than a large, powerful organism that leaves none.

Misconception 6 — "Evolution explains the origin of life."

Students sometimes confuse the origin of life with evolution by natural selection.

Correction

Evolution by natural selection explains how life changes and diversifies once it exists. It does not explain how life originally arose from nonliving chemistry. The origin of life is a separate and active area of scientific research. Natural selection requires reproducing organisms with heritable variation — it cannot operate on systems that do not yet reproduce.

Key Terms Review

evolution
Change in the heritable traits of a population over generations.
population
A group of individuals of the same species living in a shared geographic area and capable of interbreeding; the unit that evolves.
variation
Differences in heritable traits among individuals in a population; the raw material for natural selection.
mutation
A change in the DNA sequence of a gene; the original source of all new genetic variation in populations.
heritability
The proportion of trait variation in a population attributable to genetic differences rather than environmental ones.
overproduction
The tendency of organisms to produce more offspring than the environment can support, creating competition for limited resources.
natural selection
The process by which individuals with certain heritable traits survive and reproduce at higher rates, causing those traits to become more common over generations.
adaptation
A heritable trait that improves an organism's survival or reproduction in its current environment, having been favored by natural selection.
biological fitness
The relative reproductive success of an individual; how many offspring it contributes to the next generation compared to others in the population.
sexual selection
A form of natural selection in which traits that increase mating success are favored, even at some cost to survival.
directional selection
Natural selection that favors one extreme value of a trait, shifting the population mean over generations.
stabilizing selection
Natural selection that favors intermediate trait values and selects against extremes, reducing variation without shifting the mean.
disruptive selection
Natural selection that favors extreme trait values at both ends and selects against intermediate values.
genetic drift
Changes in allele frequency due to random chance events, especially influential in small populations.
gene flow
The movement of alleles between populations through the migration and reproduction of individuals.
speciation
The evolutionary process by which one species diverges into two or more distinct species, typically through reproductive isolation and accumulated genetic differences.
reproductive isolation
The condition in which two populations cannot successfully interbreed; a key step in the formation of new species.
artificial selection
The selective breeding of organisms by humans to produce populations with desired heritable traits; the same mechanism as natural selection with humans as the selecting agent.
antibiotic resistance
The ability of bacteria to survive antibiotic exposure; the result of natural selection favoring resistant variants in antibiotic-exposed populations.
allele
A variant form of a gene. Different alleles in a population produce heritable variation in traits; allele frequencies change as populations evolve.

Looking Ahead
Chapter 18: Evidence for Evolution

Chapter 17 explained the mechanism of evolution — how natural selection works, what drives it, and what it produces over generations. But how do we know that evolution has actually occurred? Chapter 18 examines the evidence for evolution from multiple independent sources: the fossil record, comparative anatomy, molecular biology, embryology, biogeography, and directly observed evolutionary change. Each line of evidence, gathered independently, points to the same conclusion — that all living things share common ancestors and that populations change over time through evolutionary processes. Understanding this evidence moves evolution from a logical argument about what should happen to a documented record of what has happened across Earth's history.

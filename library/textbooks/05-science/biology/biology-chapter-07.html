Chapter 7 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Every living cell exists in a world full of molecules it needs and molecules it must exclude. Nutrients, water, oxygen, hormones, and waste products are constantly moving through the environment around a cell — but the cell cannot simply open itself to everything. It must select what enters, what leaves, and when.

The cell membrane makes this possible. It is a thin, flexible boundary that separates the inside of the cell from the outside world. But calling it just a boundary understates what it does. The cell membrane is selective, dynamic, and responsive. It controls traffic in both directions, responds to signals, and helps the cell maintain the internal conditions it needs to survive.

This chapter explains how the cell membrane is built, how it controls the movement of molecules, and why membrane transport is essential to homeostasis, health, and life itself.

Opening Question

Imagine a city without walls, gates, or borders. Goods, people, and vehicles would flow without any direction or control — the city could not concentrate resources, remove waste, or protect what matters. A cell without a functioning membrane faces the same problem. By the end of this chapter, you will understand exactly how the membrane works as a dynamic gatekeeper, and why disrupting it even slightly causes serious disease.

Section 7.1

Why Cell Membranes Matter

All the molecules the cell has carefully assembled — its enzymes, its DNA, its ATP, its structural proteins — would simply diffuse away into the surrounding environment if the cell had no boundary. The cell would lose its identity, its organization, and its ability to function.

The cell membrane solves this problem while doing far more than simply holding things together. It controls which molecules can enter and which can leave, maintains the chemical conditions the cell needs for metabolism, allows the cell to communicate with its environment, and helps the cell respond to signals from hormones, nutrients, and neighboring cells.

Five Essential Functions of the Cell Membrane

1. Containment — holds the cell's contents together and maintains cell shape

2. Selective transport — controls the movement of molecules into and out of the cell

3. Communication — receives and transmits signals from hormones and other cells

4. Homeostasis — maintains stable internal chemical conditions

5. Recognition — identifies the cell to other cells through surface carbohydrates

Think About Scale

The cell membrane is only about 7–10 nanometers thick — roughly 10,000 times thinner than a human hair. Yet this ultra-thin layer controls everything that enters and leaves the cell. Its power comes entirely from the chemistry of its molecules, not its thickness.

Section 7.2

The Phospholipid Bilayer

The cell membrane is built from a class of molecules called phospholipids. Each phospholipid has two distinct regions: a hydrophilic head (water-loving) that is attracted to water, and two hydrophobic tails (water-fearing) made of fatty acid chains that repel water.

When phospholipids are placed in water, they spontaneously arrange themselves into a bilayer — two layers of phospholipids with their hydrophilic heads facing outward toward the watery environment and their hydrophobic tails facing inward, away from water. This arrangement is not forced by any external process. It is thermodynamically favorable — the molecules settle into the most stable configuration available.

Additional Membrane Components

ComponentLocationFunction
PhospholipidsEntire bilayerForm the structural foundation; create a selectively permeable barrier
Transmembrane proteinsSpan the entire membraneAct as channels, carriers, or pumps for specific molecules
Peripheral proteinsInner or outer surfaceServe as receptors, enzymes, or structural anchors
CholesterolBetween phospholipid tails (animal cells)Regulates membrane fluidity; buffers against temperature changes
CarbohydratesOuter surface onlyForm the glycocalyx; enable cell recognition and immune function

The Fluid Mosaic Model

The modern understanding of membrane structure is called the fluid mosaic model. "Fluid" means that phospholipids are not locked in place — they drift and exchange positions with neighboring phospholipids, giving the membrane flexibility. Proteins can also move laterally within the membrane, though some are anchored to the cytoskeleton. "Mosaic" means the membrane is a patchwork of different molecules — phospholipids, proteins, cholesterol, and carbohydrates — distributed in a complex, nonuniform pattern.

Worked Example 7.2.1
Why phospholipids form a bilayer — reasoning from molecular structure
  • QuestionA phospholipid has one head and two tails. Why does it form a double layer (bilayer) instead of a single layer in water?
  • Key factThe hydrophilic heads are attracted to water. The hydrophobic tails are repelled by water. Both sides of the cell are watery (extracellular fluid outside; cytoplasm inside).
  • ReasoningIn a single layer, one set of tails would face water — an unstable arrangement. By forming two layers, tails from both layers face each other inward, shielded from water on both sides. Heads face outward into water on each side.
  • ConclusionThe bilayer is the only arrangement that satisfies the chemical preferences of all parts of the molecule simultaneously. It forms spontaneously because it is the lowest-energy, most stable configuration.
Answer: The bilayer places hydrophilic heads toward water on both sides and shields hydrophobic tails from water in the interior — the only stable arrangement in an aqueous environment.
Common Misconception

Students sometimes think the cell membrane is solid or rigid, like a wall. In fact, the membrane is fluid at normal body temperatures. Phospholipids constantly move sideways, and so do many membrane proteins. This fluidity is essential — without it, the membrane could not flex, cells could not divide, and vesicles could not fuse with the membrane surface.

Section 7.3

Selective Permeability

The cell membrane does not let everything through equally. It is selectively permeable, meaning it allows some substances to pass freely, restricts others, and blocks some entirely. Whether a molecule can cross the membrane depends mainly on three properties: its size, its polarity, and its charge.

Small nonpolar molecules (like oxygen and carbon dioxide) dissolve in the fatty interior of the bilayer and cross easily. Polar molecules and ions are repelled by the hydrophobic core and generally cannot cross without transport proteins.

SubstanceCan cross freely?Reason
O₂, CO₂ (small nonpolar gases)YesSmall and nonpolar; dissolve in the lipid interior
Water (H₂O)Mostly yesVery small; also uses aquaporin protein channels for faster transport
Small polar molecules (ethanol)SomewhatSmall enough to pass slowly despite polarity
Glucose (large polar molecule)NoToo large and polar; requires carrier proteins
Ions (Na⁺, K⁺, Cl⁻, Ca²⁺)NoCharged; strongly repelled by the hydrophobic interior
Large molecules (proteins, DNA)NoFar too large for any passive crossing; require vesicle-based mechanisms
A Useful Rule of Thumb

Ask two questions about any molecule: Is it small? Is it nonpolar? If the answer to both is yes, it can probably cross the membrane by simple diffusion. If the answer to either question is no, the cell likely needs a transport protein or vesicle to move it.

Section 7.4

Diffusion

Molecules in solution are always in motion. They bump into each other, bounce off surfaces, and move in every direction at random. This random motion produces a predictable result: over time, molecules spread out from areas where they are concentrated to areas where they are less concentrated. This spontaneous movement of molecules from an area of high concentration to an area of low concentration is called diffusion.

Diffusion requires no energy input — it is driven entirely by the random kinetic energy of molecules. The difference in concentration across a membrane is called the concentration gradient. Diffusion always moves molecules down (with) the concentration gradient.

Four Factors That Affect Diffusion Rate

Concentration gradient — a steeper gradient (larger concentration difference) produces faster diffusion

Temperature — higher temperature means more kinetic energy and faster movement

Molecule size — smaller molecules diffuse faster than larger ones

Distance — diffusion is only effective over very short distances; this is one reason cells are microscopic

Worked Example 7.4.1
Predicting the direction of diffusion
  • ScenarioA muscle cell is actively respiring. Inside the cell, CO₂ concentration is 0.08%. In the surrounding capillary blood, CO₂ concentration is 0.04%. In which direction will CO₂ diffuse, and why?
  • IdentifyCO₂ is small and nonpolar — it can cross the membrane by simple diffusion. Concentration inside cell (0.08%) is higher than outside (0.04%).
  • Apply ruleDiffusion moves molecules from high concentration to low concentration — down the gradient. CO₂ will diffuse from the cell into the blood.
  • EquilibriumDiffusion will continue until concentrations equalize on both sides. Because the blood is flowing, fresh blood continuously carries CO₂ away, maintaining the gradient and keeping diffusion going.
Answer: CO₂ diffuses out of the muscle cell into the blood, moving from higher to lower concentration. Continuous blood flow maintains the gradient.
Common Misconception

Students sometimes say molecules "want" to move toward where they are needed. Diffusion is not purposeful — molecules do not have goals or intentions. Diffusion happens because of random molecular motion. The net movement toward lower concentration is a statistical outcome of many random collisions, not a directed process. Cells can take advantage of diffusion, but they do not cause it.

Section 7.5

Osmosis

Water is the most abundant molecule in every living cell, and its movement across the membrane is critically important. The diffusion of water across a selectively permeable membrane is called osmosis. Water moves from areas of lower solute concentration (more water, fewer dissolved particles) to areas of higher solute concentration (less water, more dissolved particles).

A useful way to think about osmosis: water moves toward where there is more stuff dissolved. The dissolved particles attract and hold water molecules, effectively pulling water in that direction.

Tonicity

The term tonicity describes the relative solute concentration of a solution compared to the inside of a cell. It predicts how a cell will behave in a given environment.

Solution TypeSolute Concentration OutsideWater MovementEffect on Animal CellEffect on Plant Cell
IsotonicEqual to cell interiorEqual in and out — no net movementNormal; cell maintains volumeNormal; slightly less firm than turgid
HypotonicLower than cell interiorWater moves into cellCell swells; may lyse (burst)Cell swells; becomes turgid (firm, healthy)
HypertonicHigher than cell interiorWater moves out of cellCell shrinks (crenation)Cell loses water; plasmolysis (wilting)
Worked Example 7.5.1
Predicting osmotic behavior — potato experiment
  • DataPotato cubes placed in saltwater solutions of varying concentrations for 30 minutes show the following mass changes: 0% → +8%; 1.0% → 0%; 2.0% → −9%
  • Analyze 0%Pure water has a lower solute concentration than potato cells. Water enters cells by osmosis. Mass increases. The 0% solution is hypotonic to potato cells.
  • Analyze 1.0%No mass change means no net water movement. Solute concentration inside potato cells equals 1.0%. This is the isotonic concentration for potato cells.
  • Analyze 2.0%Solution has higher solute concentration than potato cells. Water exits cells by osmosis. Mass decreases. The 2.0% solution is hypertonic to potato cells.
Answer: The isotonic concentration for these potato cells is approximately 1.0% salt. Below this, cells gain water; above this, cells lose water.
Real-World Osmosis

Osmosis explains why intravenous (IV) fluids must be isotonic with blood (0.9% NaCl — called "normal saline"), why wilting plants recover when watered, why salted food resists bacterial growth, and why freshwater fish produce large volumes of dilute urine. Whenever water moves predictably across a membrane, osmosis is at work.

Section 7.6

Facilitated Diffusion

Many molecules that cells need — including glucose, amino acids, and many ions — cannot cross the phospholipid bilayer by simple diffusion. They are too large, too polar, or too charged. Yet they must enter and leave cells constantly. The solution is facilitated diffusion: diffusion assisted by transport proteins embedded in the membrane.

Like simple diffusion, facilitated diffusion moves molecules down the concentration gradient and requires no ATP. Unlike simple diffusion, it requires a specific protein.

Protein TypeHow It WorksExample
Channel proteinForms an open pore; molecules that fit the channel pass through freelyIon channels (K⁺, Na⁺, Cl⁻ channels)
AquaporinSpecialized channel protein for rapid water transportKidney tubule cells; red blood cells
Carrier proteinBinds a specific molecule, changes shape, releases molecule on the other sideGlucose transporters (GLUT proteins)
Two Key Properties of Facilitated Diffusion

Specificity — each transport protein works only with one type of molecule or ion; glucose carriers transport glucose, not fructose; sodium channels transport Na⁺, not K⁺

Saturation — if all transport proteins are occupied, the transport rate cannot increase even if the concentration gradient steepens; this is unlike simple diffusion, which has no such ceiling

Worked Example 7.6.1
Distinguishing simple diffusion from facilitated diffusion using a graph
  • ScenarioA graph shows transport rate (y-axis) vs. concentration gradient (x-axis) for two molecules. Curve A rises steeply and then levels off at a maximum rate. Curve B rises steadily without leveling off.
  • Identify ACurve A levels off because all transport proteins become occupied (saturated). This is the hallmark of facilitated diffusion — a maximum rate limited by protein availability.
  • Identify BCurve B has no maximum — rate keeps rising with gradient. No protein is involved; the molecule crosses directly. This is simple diffusion.
  • PredictionAdding more transport proteins to the membrane would raise the plateau of Curve A (higher maximum rate), but would not affect Curve B.
Answer: Curve A = facilitated diffusion (saturation plateau); Curve B = simple diffusion (no plateau). Saturation distinguishes protein-dependent from protein-independent transport.

Section 7.7

Active Transport

Diffusion and facilitated diffusion only move molecules from high to low concentration. But cells often need to maintain concentrations very different from those in their surroundings — sometimes far higher than outside, sometimes far lower. To do this, they must move molecules against (up) the concentration gradient. This process is called active transport, and it requires cells to spend energy in the form of ATP.

Active transport uses carrier proteins that act as pumps. The protein binds the molecule on the low-concentration side, uses ATP to change its shape, and releases the molecule on the high-concentration side — pumping it uphill.

The Sodium-Potassium Pump

The most important active transport pump in animal cells is the sodium-potassium pump (Na⁺/K⁺ pump). Each cycle pumps 3 Na⁺ out of the cell and 2 K⁺ in, consuming one ATP molecule. The result: sodium is kept high outside, potassium is kept high inside.

Worked Example 7.7.1
Tracing the sodium-potassium pump cycle
  • Step 1Three Na⁺ ions inside the cell bind to the pump protein on the cytoplasmic side.
  • Step 2One ATP molecule is hydrolyzed (broken down). The energy released causes the pump protein to change shape.
  • Step 3The shape change exposes the Na⁺ binding sites to the outside. The Na⁺ ions are released into the extracellular fluid. The pump now has low affinity for Na⁺ and high affinity for K⁺.
  • Step 4Two K⁺ ions from outside the cell bind to the pump protein.
  • Step 5The pump returns to its original shape, releasing K⁺ into the cytoplasm. One cycle is complete.
Net result per cycle: 3 Na⁺ pumped out, 2 K⁺ pumped in, 1 ATP consumed. Both ions move against their concentration gradients.
ComparisonPassive Transport (diffusion/facilitated)Active Transport
Direction of movementDown concentration gradient (high → low)Against concentration gradient (low → high)
Energy requirementNone — driven by concentration gradientATP required
Protein required?Not always (simple diffusion needs none)Always — pump proteins required
Reaches equilibrium?Yes — stops at equilibriumNo — maintains concentration differences indefinitely
ExamplesO₂, CO₂, glucose (via GLUT), waterNa⁺/K⁺ pump, Ca²⁺ pump, proton pumps
Important Distinction

Active transport is not simply "faster" than diffusion. They are fundamentally different processes. Diffusion can only move molecules from high to low concentration. Active transport moves them the other way. Speed is not the distinction — direction against the gradient, requiring ATP, is what defines active transport.

Section 7.8

Endocytosis and Exocytosis

Simple diffusion, facilitated diffusion, and active transport all move individual molecules through transport proteins. But some substances are too large for this — whole bacteria, large protein complexes, and clumps of material cannot pass through protein channels one molecule at a time. Cells handle large-scale transport using a completely different strategy: the membrane itself engulfs or releases material by folding and fusing.

Endocytosis: Bringing Material In

Endocytosis is the process by which the cell membrane folds inward around material outside the cell, forming a membrane-enclosed sac called a vesicle that is brought inside the cell. There are three types.

TypeWhat Is Taken InBiological Example
Phagocytosis ("cell eating")Solid particles — bacteria, cellular debris, large objectsWhite blood cells (macrophages) destroying pathogens
Pinocytosis ("cell drinking")Small droplets of liquid and dissolved materialsIntestinal cells sampling extracellular fluid; general sampling
Receptor-mediated endocytosisSpecific molecules that bind to receptor proteins on the cell surfaceCells taking in LDL cholesterol; cells absorbing hormones; iron uptake

Exocytosis: Releasing Material Out

Exocytosis is the reverse process: a vesicle inside the cell moves to the membrane, fuses with it, and releases its contents to the outside. Exocytosis is how cells secrete digestive enzymes, release hormones into the bloodstream, export antibodies from immune cells, release neurotransmitters from nerve cells, and export waste materials that cannot cross the membrane individually.

Worked Example 7.8.1
Classifying transport — phagocytosis vs. receptor-mediated endocytosis
  • Scenario AA macrophage encounters a bacterium. The macrophage membrane wraps around the entire bacterium and engulfs it in a large vesicle. Lysosomal enzymes then digest the bacterium.
  • Classify AThis is phagocytosis. The target is a large solid particle (the bacterium). The uptake is nonspecific in terms of binding — it targets the physical presence of the bacterium, not a specific receptor-molecule pair.
  • Scenario BA liver cell has receptor proteins for LDL particles on its surface. When LDL binds to these receptors, the membrane folds inward specifically at those sites, taking in the LDL particles.
  • Classify BThis is receptor-mediated endocytosis. Uptake is triggered by a specific molecular binding event — LDL binds to its receptor, then and only then does the membrane respond.
Answer: Scenario A = phagocytosis (large solid particle, general engulfment); Scenario B = receptor-mediated endocytosis (specific binding triggers targeted uptake).

Section 7.9

Transport and Cellular Homeostasis

All the transport processes in this chapter work together to maintain homeostasis within the cell — the stable internal conditions that allow biochemical reactions to proceed correctly. No single transport process does this alone. The cell membrane coordinates all these mechanisms simultaneously, adjusting their rates as internal and external conditions change.

What the Cell Must RegulateTransport Mechanisms Involved
Water balanceOsmosis; aquaporins regulate rate of water movement
Ion concentrationsActive transport pumps (Na⁺/K⁺ pump, Ca²⁺ pump) maintain ion gradients
Nutrient supply (glucose, amino acids)Facilitated diffusion; receptor-mediated endocytosis
Waste removal (CO₂, urea, H⁺)Simple diffusion; active transport; exocytosis
pH (H⁺ concentration)Proton pumps and ion channels regulate H⁺ movement
Signal reception (hormones, nutrients)Receptor-mediated endocytosis; membrane receptor proteins

Transport Failure and Disease

Cystic Fibrosis
Defective CFTR chloride channel protein

When the CFTR channel fails, chloride ions cannot exit lung and digestive cells normally. Water follows chloride by osmosis — without chloride exiting, water stays inside cells. The result is thick, sticky mucus that clogs airways, traps bacteria, and causes progressive lung damage. A single defective transport protein disrupts the water balance of entire tissues.

Cholera
Bacterial toxin locks chloride channels permanently open

Cholera toxin forces chloride channels in intestinal cells to remain open continuously. Chloride floods out; water follows by osmosis — now in the wrong direction. The result is massive, life-threatening diarrhea. Cholera kills through dehydration caused by uncontrolled osmosis triggered by a single malfunctioning transport protein.

Section 7.10

Membrane Transport in Health and Disease

Understanding membrane transport is not just abstract science — it is the foundation for understanding how medications work, how the body responds to illness, and how living cells manage their complex chemistry.

Medications and the Membrane

Drug or TherapyTransport Mechanism TargetedHow It Works
Diuretics (blood pressure)Ion transporters in kidney cellsBlock Na⁺ reabsorption; water follows by osmosis into urine; blood pressure drops
Lidocaine (local anesthetic)Sodium channels in nerve cellsBlocks Na⁺ channels; prevents nerve signals from firing; eliminates pain sensation
Proton pump inhibitors (acid reflux)H⁺ pumps in stomach cellsBlock proton pumps in stomach lining; reduce acid production

Biology in Everyday Life — Oral Rehydration Therapy

Diarrheal diseases kill hundreds of thousands of children per year, mostly in low-income countries. The treatment for most of these cases is remarkably simple: oral rehydration solution — water mixed with specific concentrations of sodium, potassium, and glucose. The glucose activates coupled transporter proteins in intestinal cells that bring both glucose and sodium into the cell together. Water follows by osmosis. The solution works precisely because its composition is matched to the transport mechanisms of intestinal epithelial cells. This life-saving treatment is entirely based on the biology of facilitated diffusion and osmosis.

Chapter 7 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 7.1The cell membrane holds the cell together, controls molecular traffic, enables communication, maintains homeostasis, and allows cell recognition. Without a functioning membrane, no cell can survive.
  • Sec 7.2The cell membrane is a phospholipid bilayer — two layers with hydrophilic heads facing outward toward water and hydrophobic tails facing inward. Embedded proteins, cholesterol, and surface carbohydrates complete the structure, described by the fluid mosaic model.
  • Sec 7.3The membrane is selectively permeable. Small nonpolar molecules cross freely. Polar molecules and ions require transport proteins. Large molecules use vesicle-based processes. Size, polarity, and charge determine permeability.
  • Sec 7.4Diffusion is the movement of molecules from high to low concentration driven by random kinetic energy. It requires no ATP. Rate increases with steeper gradients, higher temperature, and smaller molecule size. Diffusion stops at equilibrium.
  • Sec 7.5Osmosis is the diffusion of water across a selectively permeable membrane from lower to higher solute concentration. Tonicity (isotonic, hypotonic, hypertonic) predicts whether cells will maintain volume, swell, or shrink.
  • Sec 7.6Facilitated diffusion uses channel or carrier proteins to move polar molecules and ions down their concentration gradients without ATP. It shows specificity (each protein is selective) and saturation (a maximum rate determined by protein number).
  • Sec 7.7Active transport uses ATP to move molecules against their concentration gradients via pump proteins. The sodium-potassium pump (3 Na⁺ out, 2 K⁺ in per ATP) is essential for nerve signals, muscle contraction, and nutrient uptake.
  • Sec 7.8Endocytosis (phagocytosis, pinocytosis, receptor-mediated endocytosis) brings large material into the cell in vesicles. Exocytosis releases vesicle contents outside the cell. These bulk processes handle substances too large for protein channels.
  • Sec 7.9All transport mechanisms together maintain cellular homeostasis — stable internal conditions. Transport failures cause specific diseases: cystic fibrosis results from a defective chloride channel; cholera results from a toxin that locks chloride channels open.
  • Sec 7.10Many medications work by targeting membrane transport proteins: diuretics, anesthetics, antacids, and statins all alter specific channels, pumps, or receptors. Dialysis and oral rehydration therapy are also built on transport biology.

Key Terms Review

active transport
Movement of molecules against their concentration gradient using ATP and pump proteins.
aquaporin
A protein channel specialized for rapid water transport across the membrane.
carrier protein
A membrane protein that binds a specific molecule and changes shape to transport it across the membrane.
channel protein
A protein that forms a pore through the membrane through which specific molecules or ions can pass.
cholesterol
A lipid found in animal cell membranes that buffers membrane fluidity against temperature changes.
concentration gradient
The difference in concentration of a substance between two regions; the driving force for diffusion.
diffusion
The movement of molecules from high to low concentration driven by random molecular motion; requires no energy.
endocytosis
A process in which the cell membrane folds inward to engulf material in a vesicle and bring it into the cell.
exocytosis
A process in which vesicles fuse with the cell membrane and release their contents outside the cell.
facilitated diffusion
Diffusion aided by transport proteins; moves molecules down their gradient without ATP.
fluid mosaic model
The model of membrane structure: a fluid phospholipid bilayer with a mosaic of embedded proteins, cholesterol, and carbohydrates.
glycocalyx
The carbohydrate coat on the outer membrane surface, involved in cell recognition and immune function.
homeostasis
The maintenance of stable internal conditions in a cell or organism despite external changes.
hydrophilic
Water-loving; describes polar or charged molecules that interact favorably with water.
hydrophobic
Water-fearing; describes nonpolar molecules that do not interact with water and are repelled by it.
hypertonic
A solution with higher solute concentration than the cell interior; causes water to move out of the cell.
hypotonic
A solution with lower solute concentration than the cell interior; causes water to move into the cell.
isotonic
A solution with the same solute concentration as the cell interior; no net water movement occurs.
osmosis
The diffusion of water across a selectively permeable membrane from lower to higher solute concentration.
phagocytosis
A type of endocytosis in which the cell engulfs large solid particles such as bacteria or debris.
phospholipid
A molecule with a hydrophilic head and two hydrophobic tails that forms the structural basis of cell membranes.
phospholipid bilayer
The double layer of phospholipids forming the core of the membrane, with heads facing outward and tails facing inward.
pinocytosis
A type of endocytosis in which the cell takes in small droplets of liquid and dissolved materials.
receptor-mediated endocytosis
Endocytosis triggered by specific molecules binding to receptor proteins on the cell surface.
selective permeability
The property of the membrane that allows some substances to cross while restricting others.
sodium-potassium pump
An active transport protein that pumps 3 Na⁺ out and 2 K⁺ in per ATP cycle; essential for nerve and muscle function.
tonicity
The relative solute concentration of a solution compared to the interior of a cell; determines direction of osmosis.
transmembrane protein
A protein that spans the entire width of the cell membrane; typically serves as a channel, carrier, or receptor.
vesicle
A small membrane-enclosed sac used to transport materials into or out of the cell during endocytosis or exocytosis.
concentration gradient
The difference in solute concentration across a space or membrane; the driving force for passive transport.

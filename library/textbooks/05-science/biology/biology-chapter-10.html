Chapter 10 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Every living cell needs energy to do work. At every moment, a cell is importing materials, exporting wastes, synthesizing proteins, repairing damage, sending signals, and maintaining the conditions necessary for life. That energy comes from food — but cells cannot use food directly. They must first convert the chemical energy stored in food molecules into a form they can actually spend. That form is a small molecule called ATP.

This chapter explains how cells extract energy from food through a process called cellular respiration: a coordinated set of chemical reactions that breaks down glucose and captures its energy as ATP. You will trace the fate of a glucose molecule through three major stages — glycolysis, the Krebs cycle, and the electron transport chain — and see how fermentation allows cells to keep producing ATP even when oxygen is unavailable. You will also see how cellular respiration connects to photosynthesis, exercise, nutrition, and life at every scale.

Opening Question

After a hard sprint, your leg muscles may feel as though they are on fire. Your breathing becomes rapid and labored even after you stop running. What is happening inside your muscle cells? Why does your body demand so much oxygen — and what happens when it cannot get enough fast enough? By the end of this chapter, you will be able to answer these questions in molecular terms, tracing the path from a glucose molecule to a burning muscle and back again.

Section 10.1

Why Cells Need ATP

Living cells are not passive. At every moment, a cell is importing materials, exporting wastes, synthesizing proteins, replicating DNA, repairing damage, sending and receiving signals, and maintaining the conditions necessary for life. All of this activity requires energy.

The energy currency of the cell is ATP, or adenosine triphosphate. ATP is a small organic molecule that stores energy in its chemical bonds and releases it quickly when needed. When a cell needs to do work — whether contracting a muscle fiber, pumping an ion across a membrane, or linking amino acids into a protein — it uses ATP to power that work.

ATP is not a fuel cells store in large quantities. Instead, cells continuously produce ATP by breaking down food molecules, regenerating it almost as fast as they spend it. A single human cell may cycle through more than a million ATP molecules per minute during high activity. The entire human body produces its own weight in ATP each day.

ATP vs. Breathing

Cellular respiration is not the same as breathing. Breathing is the mechanical process of moving air in and out of the lungs. Cellular respiration is the chemical process inside cells that produces ATP from food molecules. They are connected — breathing supplies the oxygen that cellular respiration needs — but they are not the same thing.

Key Idea — ATP as Energy Currency

Just as money is not itself food or shelter but can be exchanged for them, ATP is not itself energy but carries energy that can be spent for cellular work. Cells "earn" ATP by breaking down food and "spend" it to power every biological activity.

Section 10.2

Food Molecules and Stored Energy

The food that organisms eat contains energy stored in chemical bonds. When those bonds are broken in a controlled way, the energy they contain can be captured and used to make ATP. The primary fuel molecule for cellular respiration is glucose, a six-carbon sugar with the molecular formula C₆H₁₂O₆.

Cells can also extract energy from fats and proteins, but glucose is the primary starting point for the pathways described in this chapter. Fats are an especially rich energy source — they contain more than twice the energy per gram as carbohydrates — and they feed into the same pathway at different entry points.

The key idea: food molecules contain stored chemical energy. Cellular respiration is the controlled process by which cells release that stored energy and use it to build ATP.

Section 10.3

The Overall Equation for Cellular Respiration

The process of cellular respiration can be summarized in a single overall equation:

Overall Equation — Cellular Respiration
C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + ATP (energy)
Glucose + Oxygen → Carbon dioxide + Water + Energy

This equation tells a clear story: glucose is the fuel being broken down, oxygen is required, carbon dioxide and water are the waste products released, and ATP is the useful energy product captured. This equation looks similar to combustion — burning a sugar releases CO₂, water, and heat. But cellular respiration is not burning. It happens at body temperature, step by step, through dozens of controlled reactions. Rather than releasing all the energy at once as heat, cells capture a significant portion of it in the bonds of ATP molecules.

The Overall Equation Is a Summary, Not a Description

The equation C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + ATP conceals enormous complexity. Cellular respiration actually involves three major stages, each happening in a different part of the cell, through dozens of enzyme-controlled steps. The overall equation tells you what goes in and what comes out — not how it happens.

Section 10.4

Glycolysis

The first stage of cellular respiration is glycolysis — from the Greek for "sugar splitting." Glycolysis takes place in the cytoplasm of the cell, not inside any organelle. It does not require oxygen and occurs in nearly all living organisms, making it one of the oldest metabolic pathways in the history of life.

In glycolysis, one molecule of glucose (a 6-carbon molecule) is broken into two molecules of pyruvate (each with 3 carbons) through a series of enzyme-controlled steps. Along the way, the cell first invests a small amount of ATP to start the process, then harvests a larger amount in return.

Glycolysis — Energy Accounting

ATP invested to start glycolysis: 2 ATP used

ATP produced during glycolysis: 4 ATP produced

Net ATP gain: 2 ATP per glucose molecule

Electron carriers produced: 2 NADH (carry high-energy electrons to the ETC)

Glucose (6C)
↓  [2 ATP invested to begin]
↓  [series of enzyme-controlled steps in cytoplasm]
2 Pyruvate (3C each) + 2 ATP (net) + 2 NADH
Glycolysis Is Universal

Glycolysis occurs in the cytoplasm of virtually every living cell — bacteria, archaea, plants, fungi, and animals all use it. This universality suggests that glycolysis evolved very early in the history of life, before oxygen was abundant in Earth's atmosphere. It is the one metabolic pathway shared by nearly all life on Earth.

The two pyruvate molecules produced by glycolysis are not waste. If oxygen is available, they enter the mitochondria and are prepared for the next stage. Glycolysis alone produces only a small fraction of the total ATP available from glucose — the larger payoff requires oxygen and the stages that follow.

Section 10.5

The Krebs Cycle

When oxygen is present, the pyruvate molecules produced by glycolysis enter the mitochondria. Each pyruvate is converted to a 2-carbon molecule called acetyl-CoA, releasing one CO₂ and producing more NADH in the process. Acetyl-CoA then enters the Krebs cycle, also called the citric acid cycle.

The Krebs cycle is a series of enzyme-controlled reactions that take place in the matrix — the interior space — of the mitochondrion. The 2-carbon acetyl group combines with a 4-carbon molecule to form a 6-carbon compound called citric acid. Through a series of reactions, the carbons are progressively broken off as CO₂, which is released as waste. This CO₂ is the same carbon dioxide you exhale.

Krebs Cycle — What Each Turn Produces (per pyruvate)

CO₂ released: 2 molecules (carbons from glucose returned to atmosphere)

Electron carriers: 3 NADH + 1 FADH₂ (carry electrons to the ETC)

ATP produced directly: 1 ATP

Turns per glucose: 2 (once per pyruvate; glycolysis yields 2 pyruvate)

Figure 10.2 — Krebs Cycle Summary
Pyruvate (3C) → Acetyl-CoA (2C) + CO₂ + NADH
↓  [Acetyl-CoA + 4C molecule → Citric acid (6C)]
↓  [series of reactions in mitochondrial matrix]
2 CO₂ released per turn + 3 NADH + 1 FADH₂ + 1 ATP per turn
[4C molecule regenerated → cycle continues]
The Krebs Cycle's Main Product Is Electron Carriers, Not ATP

The Krebs cycle produces only 2 ATP directly per glucose (one per turn). Its most important output is the large number of NADH and FADH₂ electron carrier molecules — which deliver high-energy electrons to the electron transport chain, where most of the ATP is actually made.

Section 10.6

Electron Transport Chain

The electron transport chain (ETC) is the third and most productive stage of cellular respiration. It takes place in the inner membrane of the mitochondrion and generates the vast majority of ATP from a glucose molecule.

The NADH and FADH₂ molecules produced by glycolysis and the Krebs cycle carry high-energy electrons to the ETC. Here is how the chain works:

How the Electron Transport Chain Works
Step-by-step: from electron carriers to ATP
  • Step 1NADH and FADH₂ deliver high-energy electrons to the first protein complexes in the inner mitochondrial membrane.
  • Step 2Electrons pass from one protein complex to the next along the chain. At each step, energy is released.
  • Step 3That released energy is used to pump hydrogen ions (H⁺) from the matrix into the intermembrane space, creating a concentration gradient.
  • Step 4H⁺ ions flow back into the matrix through the enzyme ATP synthase. This flow of ions powers the production of ATP — a process called chemiosmosis.
  • Step 5At the end of the chain, electrons combine with oxygen (O₂) and H⁺ to form water (H₂O). Oxygen is the final electron acceptor — this is why aerobic respiration requires oxygen.
Without oxygen, the chain stops. Electrons have nowhere to go, the H⁺ gradient cannot be maintained, and ATP synthesis ceases.

Why the ATP Count Is Approximate

Textbooks sometimes list 36, 38, or even 30–32 ATP per glucose. The actual yield varies because some ATP is used to transport pyruvate into the mitochondria, proton gradients can leak, and efficiency varies by cell type and condition. The important point is that the ETC produces the overwhelming majority of ATP — far more than glycolysis or the Krebs cycle alone.

Section 10.7

Aerobic and Anaerobic Pathways

Cellular respiration can proceed along different pathways depending on whether oxygen is available.

Aerobic respiration uses oxygen. It includes all three stages — glycolysis, the Krebs cycle, and the electron transport chain — and produces the full yield of approximately 36–38 ATP per glucose. This is the pathway used by most animals, plants, and fungi under normal conditions.

Anaerobic pathways proceed without oxygen. When oxygen is absent or limited, cells cannot run the Krebs cycle or the electron transport chain. They rely on glycolysis alone — but glycolysis quickly uses up all available NAD⁺. Without oxygen to accept electrons at the end of the ETC, NADH cannot be converted back to NAD⁺, and glycolysis stalls.

The NAD⁺ Problem

Glycolysis requires NAD⁺ as an electron acceptor. When NADH is produced, NAD⁺ is used up. To keep glycolysis running, the cell must regenerate NAD⁺. Under aerobic conditions, the ETC does this. Without oxygen, the cell must use fermentation to recycle NAD⁺ and allow glycolysis to continue.

Section 10.8

Fermentation

Fermentation is not a separate form of respiration. It is a process that regenerates NAD⁺ so that glycolysis can continue in the absence of oxygen. Fermentation itself does not produce ATP — the ATP still comes from glycolysis. But without fermentation, glycolysis would stop and no ATP would be made at all.

There are two major types of fermentation:

Lactic Acid Fermentation

In lactic acid fermentation, pyruvate (from glycolysis) accepts electrons from NADH and is converted into lactic acid. This regenerates NAD⁺ and allows glycolysis to continue. Lactic acid fermentation occurs in human muscles during intense exercise when oxygen delivery cannot keep up with demand, and in bacteria used to produce yogurt, cheese, and sauerkraut.

Alcoholic Fermentation

In alcoholic fermentation, pyruvate is first converted to acetaldehyde (releasing CO₂), then acetaldehyde accepts electrons from NADH to form ethanol. NAD⁺ is regenerated. This occurs in yeast under low-oxygen conditions and is used in bread, beer, and wine production. The CO₂ released by yeast during alcoholic fermentation makes bread dough rise and creates bubbles in fermented beverages.

Worked Example 10.8
Tracing what happens in a sprinting muscle cell
  • SituationA sprinter pushes maximum effort for 100 meters. Oxygen cannot be delivered to leg muscles fast enough to sustain aerobic respiration at the required rate.
  • What HappensMuscle cells switch to lactic acid fermentation. Glycolysis continues at high speed, producing 2 ATP per glucose. Pyruvate is converted to lactic acid, regenerating NAD⁺ so glycolysis does not stop.
  • Short-term resultMuscle cells keep making ATP. Lactic acid accumulates, contributing to the burning sensation in muscles and eventually limiting performance.
  • After the sprintHeavy breathing continues to supply oxygen. The liver converts lactic acid back to glucose. Normal aerobic respiration resumes. The "oxygen debt" is repaid.
Key insight: Fermentation is a short-term, low-efficiency emergency strategy — it keeps ATP production alive when oxygen is not available fast enough.
Section 10.9

Photosynthesis and Respiration Together

Photosynthesis and cellular respiration are often described as opposites, and the comparison is useful and accurate:

Photosynthesis
6CO₂ + 6H₂O + light energy → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂
Cellular Respiration
C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + ATP

Photosynthesis builds glucose and releases oxygen. Respiration breaks down glucose and consumes oxygen. The two processes use opposite sets of reactants and products. Together, they form a cycle of matter and energy that connects producers and consumers across ecosystems. The CO₂ released by respiration is used again by photosynthetic organisms. The oxygen released by photosynthesis is used again by aerobic respirers.

This cycle does not mean that energy is recycled. Matter (carbon, oxygen, hydrogen) does cycle through organisms, the atmosphere, oceans, and soils. Energy, however, flows in one direction: sunlight enters the system through photosynthesis, is stored and spent, and eventually leaves as heat. Life requires a continuous input of energy from the sun.

Figure 10.3 — The Photosynthesis-Respiration Connection
PHOTOSYNTHESIS  (in chloroplasts of plants, algae, cyanobacteria)
CO₂ + H₂O + light energy → Glucose + O₂

↕   glucose and oxygen cycle between the two processes

CELLULAR RESPIRATION  (in mitochondria of all living cells)
Glucose + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O + ATP
Common Misconception

"Plants photosynthesize; animals respire."

Correction: All living cells — including plant cells — carry out cellular respiration. Plants both photosynthesize and respire. During daylight hours, photosynthesis typically produces more oxygen than respiration consumes, so the plant is a net absorber of CO₂. At night, without photosynthesis, the plant is a net releaser of CO₂ from respiration alone.

Section 10.10

Respiration in Exercise and Health

Exercise and Energy Demand

During rest, cells have enough oxygen to run aerobic respiration and produce ATP efficiently. During light to moderate exercise, increased breathing and heart rate deliver more oxygen to muscle cells, which continue aerobic respiration. During intense exercise — a sprint, a heavy lift, a fast climb — muscles need ATP faster than aerobic respiration can supply it. They switch to lactic acid fermentation for quick ATP. This works for short bursts but cannot be sustained, because lactic acid accumulates and interferes with muscle function. After intense exercise, heavy breathing continues even after activity stops. This "oxygen debt" is the body's process of clearing lactic acid and restoring normal conditions.

Food, Fuel, and Nutrition

When you eat food, you consume molecules containing stored energy. Carbohydrates are broken down into glucose, which enters glycolysis directly. Fats are broken down into fatty acids and glycerol, which enter cellular respiration at different points. Proteins, when used for energy, yield amino acids that also enter the pathway at various stages. The Calorie listed on food labels represents the amount of energy stored in food that can be released through cellular respiration. Fats provide about 9 Calories per gram; carbohydrates and proteins each provide about 4 Calories per gram.

Mitochondria and Health

Because mitochondria produce most of a cell's ATP, they are essential to the health of cells with high energy demands — heart muscle cells, neurons, and skeletal muscle cells are especially dependent on healthy mitochondria. Mitochondrial dysfunction is connected to a range of diseases and to the biology of aging. Regular aerobic exercise increases the number and efficiency of mitochondria in muscle cells.

ATP

cellular respiration

glucose

Common Misconceptions

Misconception

"Cellular respiration is the same as breathing."

Correction: Breathing is the mechanical process of moving air into and out of the lungs. Cellular respiration is the chemical process inside cells that produces ATP from food molecules. Breathing supplies the oxygen that cellular respiration needs and removes the CO₂ it produces — but they are distinct processes operating at entirely different scales.

Misconception

"Fermentation produces no ATP."

Correction: Fermentation itself does not produce ATP — the ATP still comes from glycolysis. Fermentation's role is to regenerate NAD⁺ so that glycolysis can continue. Without fermentation to recycle NAD⁺, glycolysis would stop and no ATP at all would be produced. Fermentation keeps the 2 ATP per glucose flowing even without oxygen.

Misconception

"Plants photosynthesize; animals respire."

Correction: All living cells carry out cellular respiration — including plant cells. Plants both photosynthesize and respire. During daylight, photosynthesis typically outpaces respiration, and the plant is a net absorber of CO₂. At night, only respiration continues, and the plant releases CO₂.

Misconception

"Cellular respiration destroys energy."

Correction: Cellular respiration converts energy from one form to another. The chemical energy stored in glucose bonds is converted into the chemical energy of ATP bonds, with some energy released as heat. Energy is neither created nor destroyed — this is a consequence of how all energy transformations work. Cells are not perfectly efficient, but "inefficiency" is not destruction.

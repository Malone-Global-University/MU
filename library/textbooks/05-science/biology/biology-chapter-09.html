Chapter 9 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Every meal you eat traces back to sunlight. The sandwich, the rice, the apple, the burger — all of it started as light energy captured by a plant and stored in chemical form. You are, in a very real sense, running on stored sunlight.

Photosynthesis is the biological process that makes this possible. It is one of the most important chemical events on Earth. Without it, there would be no atmospheric oxygen, no food for animals, no fossil fuels, and no life as we know it. Photosynthesis connects the sun to nearly every living thing on Earth through food webs, energy flow, and the cycling of carbon.

In this chapter, you will learn how plants, algae, and some bacteria capture light energy and use it to build sugar molecules. You will trace the path of energy from sunlight to chemical bonds, see how the chloroplast is organized to carry out this work, and explore how photosynthesis connects to ecosystems, agriculture, climate, and the future of energy on Earth.

Opening Question

A plant sits in a windowsill. It has soil in its pot, water from the last watering, and sunlight streaming through the glass. By the end of the day, the plant has grown slightly heavier — new cells have been added. Where did the mass come from? The soil? The water? The light? By the end of this chapter, you will know the answer — and it may surprise you.

Section 9.1

Why Photosynthesis Matters

Life Requires Energy

Living things need a continuous supply of energy to grow, move, reproduce, repair damage, and maintain internal order. Energy does not appear on its own — it must come from somewhere. Most of the energy available to living systems on Earth originates from one source: the sun.

The sun radiates enormous amounts of energy as light, but light energy on its own cannot power a cell. Cells run on chemical energy stored in molecules like ATP and glucose. Photosynthesis is the biological process that bridges this gap: it converts light energy into chemical energy that living things can use.

TermDefinitionExamples
AutotrophAn organism that builds its own food from inorganic raw materials using an external energy source (usually light)Plants, algae, cyanobacteria
HeterotrophAn organism that cannot photosynthesize; must consume other organisms for energy and carbonAnimals, fungi, most bacteria

Autotrophs are the foundation of nearly every food web on Earth. Without them, there would be no food for heterotrophs, no oxygen in the atmosphere, and no carbon-based molecules to build life's structures.

What Photosynthesis Produces

Photosynthesis takes in carbon dioxide and water and, using light energy, produces two outputs: glucose — a sugar molecule that stores chemical energy — and oxygen, released into the atmosphere as a byproduct. The oxygen in Earth's atmosphere is almost entirely the product of photosynthesis carried out over billions of years.

Who Performs Photosynthesis

GroupExamplesWhere Found
PlantsTrees, grasses, mosses, ferns, flowering plantsLand (primarily)
AlgaeMicroscopic single-celled algae, seaweedsOceans, lakes, rivers
CyanobacteriaPhotosynthetic bacteriaWater, soil, land crusts
Did You Know?

Algae in the ocean may be responsible for more than half of all photosynthesis on Earth. The trillions of microscopic algae floating near the ocean surface are constantly absorbing sunlight and producing oxygen — including a significant fraction of every breath you take.

Section 9.2

Chloroplasts and Pigments

The Organelle of Photosynthesis

In plant and algal cells, photosynthesis takes place inside organelles called chloroplasts. Chloroplasts are double-membrane organelles with a complex internal structure built specifically for capturing light and running the chemical reactions of photosynthesis.

StructureDescriptionFunction
Outer & inner membranesDouble membrane surrounding the organelleControls what enters and exits the chloroplast
ThylakoidFlattened, membrane-bound sac inside the chloroplastContains pigments and proteins for light-dependent reactions
Granum (pl. grana)Stack of thylakoidsIncreases surface area for light capture
Thylakoid lumenFluid-filled space inside the thylakoid networkSite where H⁺ ions accumulate during chemiosmosis
StromaFluid surrounding the thylakoids inside the chloroplastSite of the Calvin cycle; contains enzymes and DNA
Key Structural Principle

The chloroplast's two-compartment design — thylakoids embedded in stroma — is not accidental. It allows the two major stages of photosynthesis to occur in separate locations, with products from one stage flowing directly to the next. Structure enables function.

Chlorophyll and Other Pigments

Photosynthesis begins with the absorption of light. Chloroplasts contain specialized molecules called pigments that absorb certain wavelengths of light and reflect others. The primary photosynthetic pigment in plants is chlorophyll, which absorbs red and blue wavelengths strongly but reflects green — which is why leaves appear green.

PigmentLight AbsorbedColor Reflected / VisibleRole
Chlorophyll aRed, blue-violetGreenPrimary pigment; directly involved in light reactions
Chlorophyll bBlue, orangeYellow-greenAccessory pigment; passes energy to chlorophyll a
CarotenoidsBlue-green, blueYellow, orange, redBroaden light capture; protect against excess light
Fall Colors Explained

In summer, abundant green chlorophyll masks carotenoids. In autumn, as days shorten and temperatures drop, plants break down chlorophyll. The yellow and orange carotenoids that were always present become visible — this is the biological source of fall leaf color.

Section 9.3

The Overall Equation for Photosynthesis

The Overall Equation for Photosynthesis 6 CO₂  +  6 H₂O  +  light energy  →  C₆H₁₂O₆  +  6 O₂

In words: six molecules of carbon dioxide plus six molecules of water, powered by light energy, are converted into one molecule of glucose and six molecules of oxygen gas. This equation shows what goes in and what comes out — but not how. The "how" involves dozens of chemical reactions organized into two major stages.

MoleculeRoleWhere It Comes From / Goes
CO₂ (carbon dioxide)Provides carbon atoms for glucoseAbsorbed from air through stomata in leaves
H₂O (water)Provides hydrogen for glucose; oxygen is releasedAbsorbed through roots; transported to leaves
Light energyPowers the chemical reactionsCaptured by chlorophyll in thylakoid membranes
C₆H₁₂O₆ (glucose)Stores chemical energy; building block for other moleculesProduced in the stroma; used or converted to starch, cellulose, lipids
O₂ (oxygen)Byproduct of water splittingReleased through stomata into the atmosphere
Common Misconception — Source of Released Oxygen

Many students assume the oxygen released in photosynthesis comes from CO₂. Experiments using isotope-labeled oxygen atoms showed that the O₂ released comes from the splitting of water molecules, not from carbon dioxide. The oxygen in CO₂ ends up incorporated into glucose, not released as gas.

Tracking Atoms

The photosynthesis equation is balanced — the same atoms appear on both sides, just rearranged. Matter is conserved. Photosynthesis does not create new matter; it uses light energy to rearrange existing atoms. Count the carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen atoms on each side of the equation to confirm they are equal.

Section 9.4

Light Energy and Chemical Energy

The Nature of Light

Light is a form of electromagnetic radiation that travels as waves. Different wavelengths correspond to different colors. Visible light — the portion captured by photosynthetic pigments — ranges from violet (short wavelength, more energy) to red (long wavelength, less energy). Light also behaves as if it comes in discrete packets called photons.

When a photon strikes a chlorophyll molecule and is absorbed, it excites electrons in the pigment to a higher energy state. The photosynthetic system captures this energy and uses it to do chemical work — rather than simply releasing the energy as heat or light.

Energy CarrierWhat It CarriesProduced InUsed In
ATPChemical energy (phosphate bonds)Light-dependent reactionsCalvin cycle (and cellular work generally)
NADPHHigh-energy electronsLight-dependent reactionsCalvin cycle (reduction of CO₂)
GlucoseLong-term chemical energy storageCalvin cycleCellular respiration; building other molecules
Why Glucose?

Glucose is an especially useful energy-storage molecule because its bonds store a large amount of chemical energy, it is stable enough to be transported within the plant, and it can be converted into other molecules — starch for long-term storage, cellulose for cell walls, lipids for seeds. When you eat a potato, you are consuming starch assembled from glucose produced using sunlight months earlier.

Section 9.5

Light-Dependent Reactions

Where They Occur

The light-dependent reactions take place in the thylakoid membranes. The pigments, proteins, and electron carriers needed for this stage are embedded in or associated with these membranes. The light-dependent reactions have three major jobs: absorb light energy using pigment molecules; use that energy to split water molecules, releasing oxygen; and produce ATP and NADPH to power the Calvin cycle.

Photosystems

Chlorophyll and other pigments are organized into clusters called photosystems. Each photosystem contains hundreds of pigment molecules arranged around a central reaction center. The surrounding pigments act like an antenna — they absorb photons and funnel the energy toward the reaction center, where it excites a specific chlorophyll molecule and releases a high-energy electron.

There are two photosystems. Photosystem II (named in order of discovery, but acts first) and Photosystem I work in sequence.

The Sequence of Events

Worked Summary
The light-dependent reactions, step by step
  • Step 1Light absorption in Photosystem II. Photons are absorbed by antenna pigments and their energy is funneled to the reaction center. Electrons in chlorophyll are excited to a high-energy state.
  • Step 2Splitting of water (photolysis). To replace the electrons that leave Photosystem II, water molecules are split: H₂O → H⁺ + electrons + O. The oxygen atoms pair up and are released as O₂. This is the source of the oxygen you breathe.
  • Step 3Electron transport chain. High-energy electrons pass through a series of proteins in the thylakoid membrane. As they move, they release energy, which is used to pump H⁺ ions across the thylakoid membrane, creating a concentration gradient.
  • Step 4ATP production via chemiosmosis. H⁺ ions flow back across the membrane through the protein ATP synthase. This flow drives the synthesis of ATP from ADP and phosphate.
  • Step 5Light absorption in Photosystem I. Lower-energy electrons arrive at Photosystem I. More light energy is absorbed and used to re-energize these electrons.
  • Step 6Production of NADPH. Re-energized electrons are used to reduce NADP⁺ to NADPH. NADPH carries high-energy electrons to the Calvin cycle.
Outputs: Oxygen (O₂) released · ATP produced · NADPH produced
Summary — Outputs of the Light-Dependent Reactions

Oxygen (O₂) — released into the atmosphere as a byproduct of water splitting.

ATP — produced by ATP synthase using the energy of chemiosmosis.

NADPH — electron carrier with stored energy; carries high-energy electrons to the Calvin cycle.

Student Warning — Photosystem Order

Photosystem II is named "II" because it was discovered second, but it acts first in the sequence of the light reactions. Photosystem I acts second. This naming convention confuses many students. Remember: II acts first, I acts second.

Section 9.6

The Calvin Cycle at an Introductory Level

Where It Occurs

The second stage of photosynthesis takes place in the stroma — the fluid surrounding the thylakoids. Unlike the light reactions, this stage does not directly require light. It requires the products of the light reactions (ATP and NADPH) and a supply of carbon dioxide from the air.

This stage is called the Calvin cycle, named after Melvin Calvin, who worked out its steps in the 1950s using radioactively labeled carbon dioxide to track where the carbon went.

The Three Phases

PhaseWhat HappensKey Molecules
1. Carbon FixationCO₂ is attached to RuBP by the enzyme rubisco. The product immediately splits into two 3-carbon molecules.CO₂, RuBP, rubisco
2. ReductionATP and NADPH are used to convert 3-carbon molecules into G3P, a three-carbon sugar with stored energy.ATP, NADPH, G3P
3. Regeneration of RuBPSome G3P molecules are used (with more ATP) to regenerate RuBP so the cycle can continue.G3P, ATP, RuBP
Key Definitions — Calvin Cycle

Carbon fixation — incorporating inorganic CO₂ into organic molecules (organic = carbon-containing compounds that are part of living things).

RuBP — the five-carbon molecule that accepts CO₂ at the start of each cycle turn.

Rubisco — the enzyme that catalyzes carbon fixation; one of the most abundant enzymes on Earth.

G3P — the three-carbon sugar produced; the first stable sugar from the Calvin cycle. Building block for glucose and many other molecules.

Worked Example 9.6.1
Counting Calvin cycle inputs and outputs
  • QuestionHow many turns of the Calvin cycle are needed to produce one glucose molecule? What does each turn consume?
  • ThinkGlucose has 6 carbons. Each turn of the cycle fixes 1 CO₂. So 6 turns are needed to fix enough carbon. Each turn uses 3 ATP and 2 NADPH.
  • Solve 6 turns × 1 CO₂ each = 6 CO₂ fixed 6 turns × 3 ATP each = 18 ATP consumed 6 turns × 2 NADPH each = 12 NADPH consumed
  • CheckThis matches the overall equation: 6 CO₂ → C₆H₁₂O₆. The light reactions must produce 18 ATP and 12 NADPH to support one full round of glucose synthesis. ✓
6 turns needed. Per turn: 1 CO₂ fixed, 3 ATP and 2 NADPH consumed.

The Fate of G3P

G3P produced by the Calvin cycle is a versatile building block. The plant uses it to make glucose (and from glucose, sucrose for transport through the phloem); starch for long-term energy storage in roots, seeds, and tubers; cellulose for cell walls; lipids used in membranes and energy storage in seeds; and amino acids when combined with nitrogen from the soil. Photosynthesis is therefore not just an energy-capturing process — it is the source of nearly every organic molecule in a plant's body, and through food webs, in the bodies of most other living things on Earth.

Common Misconception — Does the Calvin Cycle Need Light?

The Calvin cycle is sometimes called the "dark reactions," which leads students to believe it only runs in the dark. This is misleading. The Calvin cycle does not directly use light, but it runs best in the light — because it depends on ATP and NADPH from the light reactions, which require light to operate. When the light goes off, ATP and NADPH supplies drop, and the Calvin cycle slows and stops.

Section 9.7

Factors That Affect Photosynthesis

The rate of photosynthesis — how fast glucose is produced — changes depending on environmental conditions. Three major factors affect it.

FactorEffect on RateWhy It MattersPractical Example
Light intensityIncreases rate up to a plateau; levels off at high intensityMore photons drive more light reactions; enzymes become the limit at high lightPlants in shade grow more slowly than those in full sun
CO₂ concentrationIncreases rate up to a limitCO₂ is the raw material for the Calvin cycle; higher CO₂ allows more fixationGreenhouse growers add CO₂ to increase crop yield
TemperatureIncreases rate up to an optimum; drops sharply above itEnzymes work faster as temperature rises, but denature at high temperaturesDrought conditions or heat waves reduce crop productivity
The Law of Limiting Factors

The rate of photosynthesis at any given moment is determined by whichever factor is currently most limiting. Increasing a non-limiting factor will not increase the rate. To increase photosynthesis, you must address the actual limiting factor.

Example: A plant with plenty of light and water but very low CO₂ will not benefit from adding more light. The CO₂ supply is the limiting factor. Fix the CO₂ first.

The Light Compensation Point

The light compensation point is the light intensity at which photosynthesis rate exactly equals cellular respiration rate. Below this point, the plant consumes more energy than it produces. Above this point, net photosynthesis — and therefore growth — occurs. Shade-tolerant plants have lower compensation points than sun-loving plants.

Student Warning — Temperature and Photosynthesis

A common error is to assume that higher temperature always increases photosynthesis. This is true only up to the enzyme's optimal temperature. Above the optimum, enzymes begin to denature — they lose their shape and stop working. At very high temperatures, photosynthesis drops to zero even if light and CO₂ are abundant.

Section 9.8

Photosynthesis and Food Webs

Autotrophs as the Foundation

Organisms that carry out photosynthesis are called producers (or primary producers) because they produce organic molecules from inorganic raw materials. In nearly every ecosystem on Earth, photosynthetic organisms form the base of the food web. Every consumer traces its energy back to a producer. Even a wolf eating a deer is consuming energy that originally came from grass — which came from sunlight.

Energy Flow and the 10% Rule

Energy captured by photosynthesis does not flow through food webs efficiently. At each step from producer to consumer, most energy is lost as heat — used for the organism's own metabolism, movement, and maintenance. On average, only about 10% of the energy stored at one trophic level is available to the next. This is why ecosystems can support many more herbivores than carnivores, and why plant-based diets require less photosynthetic productivity than meat-based diets.

Worked Example 9.8.1
Applying the 10% energy rule
  • ProblemA meadow food web: grass → grasshopper → frog → hawk. Grass captures 10,000 kJ through photosynthesis. How much energy is available at each level?
  • Apply Grass (producers): 10,000 kJ Grasshoppers (1°consumers): 10,000 × 0.10 = 1,000 kJ Frogs (2° consumers): 1,000 × 0.10 = 100 kJ Hawks (3° consumers): 100 × 0.10 = 10 kJ
  • InterpretOnly 10 kJ — 0.1% of the original solar energy stored by grass — is available to the hawk. This is why large predators are rare and require vast territories.
Grasshoppers: 1,000 kJ. Frogs: 100 kJ. Hawks: 10 kJ.

Aquatic Food Webs and Phytoplankton

In oceans, lakes, and rivers, photosynthesis is carried out primarily by microscopic algae called phytoplankton. Though individually invisible, phytoplankton exist in vast numbers and collectively fix an enormous amount of carbon. The entire ocean food web — including whales, sharks, tuna, and seabirds — ultimately runs on phytoplankton photosynthesis.

Connection — Agriculture

Agriculture is, at its core, a human effort to direct and maximize photosynthesis toward food production. When farmers grow corn, wheat, soybeans, or rice, they are managing the conditions — water, light, soil nutrients, spacing — to maximize photosynthetic output in the form of edible plant products. Understanding photosynthesis is therefore directly relevant to food security, crop development, and agricultural responses to climate change.

Section 9.9

Photosynthesis, Oxygen, and Earth Systems

A Planet Transformed

Earth's early atmosphere contained very little oxygen. The Great Oxygenation Event — beginning roughly 2.4 billion years ago — was caused by the accumulation of oxygen released by photosynthetic cyanobacteria. This oxygen transformed the atmosphere and enabled the evolution of aerobic organisms, including the ancestors of all plants and animals. The approximately 21% oxygen in today's atmosphere is maintained by the continuous photosynthesis of plants, algae, and cyanobacteria.

Photosynthesis and the Carbon Cycle

Photosynthesis plays a central role in the carbon cycle — the movement of carbon between the atmosphere, living organisms, soil, and oceans. Plants pull CO₂ out of the atmosphere and incorporate its carbon into organic molecules. When organisms die and decompose, or are eaten and their carbon passes through food webs, carbon is eventually released back to the atmosphere as CO₂ through cellular respiration and decomposition.

Over millions of years, some organic carbon was buried before it could decompose, forming coal, oil, and natural gas — the fossil fuels. When humans burn these fuels, we release carbon stored millions of years ago, adding CO₂ to the atmosphere much faster than the carbon cycle can reabsorb it. This is a primary driver of current climate change.

ConceptDefinitionConnection to Photosynthesis
Carbon sinkA location that absorbs and stores more carbon than it releasesForests and oceans absorb CO₂ via photosynthesis; deforestation reduces this capacity
Fossil fuelsCoal, oil, gas formed from ancient organic carbonAncient photosynthesis stored the carbon; burning releases it as CO₂
DeforestationLarge-scale removal of forestsReduces carbon sink capacity and adds CO₂ to the atmosphere
Looking Ahead — Artificial Photosynthesis

Photosynthesis has inspired researchers working on sustainable energy. If artificial systems could mimic photosynthesis — capturing sunlight to split water or fix carbon — they could produce clean fuels without burning fossil fuels. This field remains challenging, but understanding natural photosynthesis is the essential foundation for developing it.

Key Terms Review

autotroph
An organism that produces its own food from inorganic raw materials using an external energy source (usually light). Also called a producer.
heterotroph
An organism that must obtain energy and carbon by consuming other organisms. Cannot photosynthesize.
chloroplast
The organelle in plant and algal cells where photosynthesis occurs. Contains thylakoids embedded in stroma; enclosed by a double membrane.
thylakoid
Flattened, membrane-bound sac inside the chloroplast where the light-dependent reactions occur. Stacked thylakoids form grana.
stroma
The fluid-filled space surrounding the thylakoids inside the chloroplast. Site of the Calvin cycle.
chlorophyll
The primary photosynthetic pigment in plants. Absorbs red and blue light; reflects green. Chlorophyll a directly participates in the light reactions; chlorophyll b is an accessory pigment.
carotenoid
Accessory photosynthetic pigments (yellow, orange, red) that absorb blue-green light and pass energy to chlorophyll. Also protect against excess light energy.
photosystem
A cluster of pigment molecules and proteins in the thylakoid membrane that absorbs light and transfers energy to a reaction center.
photolysis
The splitting of water molecules using light energy, releasing oxygen gas, hydrogen ions (H⁺), and electrons. Occurs in Photosystem II.
electron transport chain
A series of proteins in the thylakoid membrane through which electrons flow, releasing energy used to pump H⁺ ions and drive ATP production.
chemiosmosis
The movement of hydrogen ions through ATP synthase across a membrane, used to generate ATP. Occurs during both the light reactions and cellular respiration.
ATP synthase
The protein embedded in the thylakoid membrane that synthesizes ATP from ADP and phosphate, powered by the flow of H⁺ ions.
NADPH
An electron carrier molecule produced during the light reactions. Carries high-energy electrons to the Calvin cycle for use in reducing CO₂.
Calvin cycle
The second stage of photosynthesis, occurring in the stroma. Uses ATP and NADPH to fix CO₂ into organic molecules through three phases.
carbon fixation
The process of incorporating inorganic carbon (CO₂) into organic molecules. The first phase of the Calvin cycle, catalyzed by rubisco.
RuBP
Ribulose bisphosphate. The five-carbon molecule to which CO₂ is attached in the first step of the Calvin cycle. Regenerated at the end of each cycle.
rubisco
The enzyme that catalyzes carbon fixation in the Calvin cycle — attaching CO₂ to RuBP. One of the most abundant proteins on Earth.
G3P
Glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate. The three-carbon sugar produced by the reduction phase of the Calvin cycle. Used to build glucose and other organic molecules.
stomata
Tiny pores on the surface of leaves through which CO₂ enters and O₂ and water vapor exit. Regulated by guard cells.
light compensation point
The light intensity at which the rate of photosynthesis exactly equals the rate of cellular respiration. Net photosynthesis occurs above this point.
producer
An autotroph that forms the base of a food web by capturing energy from the environment (usually sunlight) and storing it in organic molecules.
phytoplankton
Microscopic photosynthetic organisms (algae and cyanobacteria) floating in aquatic environments. Major producers in ocean food webs; responsible for roughly half of global photosynthesis.
carbon sink
A location or system that absorbs and stores more carbon than it releases (e.g., forests, oceans). Carbon sinks help regulate atmospheric CO₂.
fossil fuels
Coal, oil, and natural gas formed from ancient organic carbon buried and transformed over millions of years. Burning them releases long-stored CO₂, driving climate change.

Looking Ahead
Chapter 10: Cellular Respiration

Photosynthesis stores energy in glucose. Chapter 10 explores cellular respiration — the process by which cells break down glucose and release its stored chemical energy as ATP. You will trace glucose through glycolysis, the Krebs cycle, and the electron transport chain, and you will see why oxygen is needed for efficient energy release. You will also learn about fermentation, anaerobic pathways, and how respiration connects to exercise, metabolism, and body systems.

Photosynthesis and cellular respiration are complementary and opposite processes. Together, they form the engine of energy flow through living systems. Notice the structural parallels: both use electron transport chains in membranes; both produce ATP through chemiosmosis; both involve carefully connected stages. Chapter 10 will complete this picture and reveal how deeply these two processes are linked.

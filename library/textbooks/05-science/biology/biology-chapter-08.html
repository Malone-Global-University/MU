Chapter 8 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Every second, thousands of chemical reactions are happening inside your cells. Your body is breaking down food molecules, building new proteins, repairing DNA, and releasing energy — all at once, all without you thinking about it. How does all of this chemistry happen fast enough to keep you alive?

The answer is enzymes. Enzymes are proteins that speed up chemical reactions in living systems. Without them, the chemistry of life would happen far too slowly to support any organism. A reaction that takes an enzyme a fraction of a second might take years without one.

But enzymes are only part of the story. To understand how cells manage their chemistry, we also need to understand metabolism — the complete network of chemical reactions that keeps a cell alive. This chapter explains what metabolism is, how enzymes work, what shapes enzyme activity, how cells control their chemical pathways, and why enzymes matter in medicine, digestion, and industry.

Opening Question

Hydrogen peroxide (H₂O₂) sits in a bottle in your medicine cabinet without breaking down much at all. Yet when you pour it on a cut, it bubbles vigorously within seconds. The enzyme catalase, released from damaged cells, is breaking the hydrogen peroxide into water and harmless oxygen gas. The same reaction — the same molecules — behaves completely differently depending on whether an enzyme is present. By the end of this chapter, you will understand exactly why, and why that difference is the difference between living and nonliving chemistry.

Section 8.1

What Metabolism Means

The word metabolism comes from a Greek word meaning "change." In biology, metabolism refers to all of the chemical reactions that take place in a living organism. It is not one reaction — it is a vast, interconnected network. Every cell is constantly performing hundreds of reactions simultaneously, breaking some molecules apart while building others up.

Two Directions of Metabolism

TypeDirectionEnergyExample
CatabolismBreaks molecules downReleases energyBreaking glucose into CO₂ and water during respiration
AnabolismBuilds molecules upRequires energy inputJoining amino acids to build a protein

Catabolism and anabolism are always happening at the same time. Energy released by catabolic reactions is captured and used to power anabolic reactions. The cell is a dynamic chemical system that never completely stops.

Memory Aid

Catabolism = catastrophe (breaking down). Anabolism = anabolic (building up, like in muscle building). Both are always running simultaneously in every living cell.

Left to themselves, most biological molecules are remarkably stable. Glucose sitting in a jar does not spontaneously break down into carbon dioxide and water — at least not quickly. The same reactions that happen inside cells in milliseconds would take years or centuries without help. Cells solve this problem with enzymes, making biological chemistry fast enough to sustain life.

Section 8.2

Chemical Reactions in Cells

In any chemical reaction, starting materials called reactants are converted into ending materials called products. The atoms involved are rearranged, but they are not created or destroyed. Chemical reactions in cells involve energy, and the direction of that energy determines what kind of reaction it is.

Reaction TypeEnergy DirectionProducts vs. ReactantsBiological Example
ExergonicReleases energyProducts have less stored energyBreaking down glucose in cellular respiration
EndergonicRequires energy inputProducts have more stored energyBuilding proteins from amino acids
ATP: Energy Currency of the Cell

In cells, energy released by exergonic reactions is captured in a molecule called ATP (adenosine triphosphate). ATP carries that energy to endergonic reactions that need it. Think of ATP as a rechargeable battery — it stores energy from catabolic reactions and delivers it to anabolic reactions. You will study ATP in detail in Chapters 9 and 10.

For any reaction to occur, reactants must collide with enough energy and in the right orientation. Not every collision leads to a reaction — only those with sufficient energy will cause bonds to break and reform. The minimum energy needed to start a reaction is called the activation energy.

Section 8.3

Activation Energy

Imagine a boulder sitting at the top of a hill. The boulder has potential energy, but it will not roll down unless something pushes it over the edge. Once it clears the peak, it rolls downhill and releases energy. Chemical reactions work similarly. Even if a reaction releases energy overall, the reactants must first gain enough energy to overcome the activation energy barrier before the reaction can proceed.

Worked Example 8.3.1
Tracing activation energy in a reaction diagram
  • ScenarioA reaction diagram shows reactants at an energy level of 100 units. The peak of the diagram (transition state) is at 160 units. The products settle at 40 units.
  • IdentifyActivation energy = peak energy − reactant energy = 160 − 100 = 60 energy units.
  • DirectionTotal energy released = reactant energy − product energy = 100 − 40 = 60 energy units net released. This is an exergonic reaction.
  • Enzyme effectIf an enzyme is added, the peak drops to, say, 120 units. Now activation energy = 120 − 100 = 20 energy units. The reaction is far easier to start, but the net energy released (100 − 40 = 60) is unchanged.
Key point: Enzymes lower activation energy; they do not change the net energy of the reaction.

Why Cells Cannot Simply Use Heat

One way to speed up reactions is to add heat. Higher temperatures give molecules more kinetic energy, increasing the chance that collisions will be forceful enough to overcome the activation energy barrier. But cells cannot simply raise their temperature. High temperatures denature (unfold and destroy) proteins and damage cell structures. A cell that heated itself to speed up reactions would destroy its own enzymes in the process.

Instead, cells use enzymes to lower activation energy — not by adding heat, but by providing a more favorable chemical environment for the reaction to proceed. With a lower activation energy barrier, more reactant molecules can clear the barrier at normal biological temperatures.

Section 8.4

Enzymes as Biological Catalysts

A catalyst is any substance that speeds up a chemical reaction without being consumed by the reaction. Enzymes are biological catalysts — they are made by living cells and they facilitate the chemical reactions that keep those cells alive.

Key Properties of Enzymes

Enzymes are proteins. Almost all enzymes are proteins — folded chains of amino acids. (A small number of RNA catalysts called ribozymes exist but are rare.)

Enzymes are not consumed. An enzyme participates in a reaction and is released unchanged, ready to catalyze again — thousands or millions of times per second.

Enzymes are specific. Each enzyme catalyzes only one reaction or a narrow group of related reactions.

Enzymes lower activation energy. They do not change whether a reaction is energetically favorable; only how fast it happens.

Enzymes work at biological temperatures. They allow fast reactions at the mild temperatures inside living cells.

Naming Enzymes

Many enzymes are named by adding the suffix -ase to the name of the molecule they act on (called the substrate).

LipaseLipidsBreaks down fats into fatty acids and glycerol
ProteaseProteinsBreaks down protein chains into amino acids
AmylaseStarch (amylose)Breaks down starch into sugars
DNA polymeraseDNA nucleotidesAssembles new DNA strands
PepsinProteinsOlder name; works in the acidic stomach

Section 8.5

Enzyme Shape and Specificity

Why can each enzyme only catalyze one type of reaction? The answer lies in shape. Each enzyme has a specific three-dimensional region called the active site — the part of the enzyme where the substrate binds and where the reaction takes place. The shape of the active site is complementary to the shape of the substrate, much like a lock and a key. Only a substrate with the right shape can fit and react.

Induced Fit

Early models described enzyme-substrate binding as a rigid lock-and-key relationship. Modern biology has refined this idea. The current model, called induced fit, recognizes that both the enzyme and the substrate change shape slightly as they come together. The enzyme molds itself around the substrate to create a tighter, more precise fit — like a hand fitting into a glove. This shape change helps stress certain bonds in the substrate, making them easier to break, or brings reactive groups into close proximity to help form new bonds.

Worked Example 8.5.1
Tracing the enzyme-substrate cycle
  • Step 1The enzyme floats in the cell's cytoplasm, its active site open and unoccupied.
  • Step 2A substrate molecule with the complementary shape approaches and binds to the active site. Both the enzyme and substrate shift shape slightly (induced fit). This forms the enzyme-substrate complex.
  • Step 3Within the complex, the reaction occurs. Chemical bonds in the substrate are broken or formed. The substrate is converted into products.
  • Step 4The products are released from the active site. The enzyme returns to its original shape, ready to accept the next substrate molecule.
Summary: Enzyme + Substrate → Enzyme-Substrate Complex → Enzyme + Products
Lock-and-Key vs. Induced Fit

The lock-and-key model treats the active site as a rigid, fixed shape. The induced fit model is more accurate — it recognizes that the enzyme actively adjusts its shape when the substrate arrives, and this adjustment is part of what makes the reaction happen efficiently.

Section 8.6

Factors That Affect Enzyme Activity

Enzyme activity is not constant — it responds to conditions in the cell's environment. Understanding what increases or decreases enzyme activity explains how cells regulate their chemistry, and why disease, extreme temperatures, or chemical exposures can interfere with life processes.

Temperature

Increasing temperature generally increases enzyme activity up to a point. Higher temperatures mean faster-moving molecules, more frequent collisions, and more successful reactions. However, every enzyme has an optimal temperature — the temperature at which it works most efficiently. Beyond that optimum, activity drops sharply. At high temperatures, the enzyme's three-dimensional shape unfolds in a process called denaturation. A denatured enzyme has lost its functional shape, including the active site, and cannot bind its substrate.

Worked Example 8.6.1
Interpreting a temperature-activity graph
  • GivenA graph shows enzyme activity rising from 10°C to 37°C, peaking at 37°C, then dropping sharply to near zero by 55°C.
  • Optimal temp37°C. This matches human body temperature — not a coincidence. Human enzymes are adapted to function best at body temperature.
  • Above 50°CActivity collapses. The enzyme is being denatured — its protein structure unfolds. The active site loses its shape and can no longer bind substrate.
  • ImplicationCooling the enzyme back to 37°C will NOT restore its activity if it was severely denatured — unfolding at extreme temperatures is often irreversible.
Key: Optimal temperature ≠ maximum temperature. Above the optimum, denaturation destroys function.
Common Error — Denaturation is Often Irreversible

Many students assume that cooling a denatured enzyme back to normal temperature will restore its function. This is sometimes possible for mild denaturation, but at extreme temperatures, the protein unfolds irreversibly and cannot refold into its functional shape. Think of cooking an egg: you cannot "uncook" it by cooling it down.

pH

Enzymes also have an optimal pH — the pH at which they function most efficiently. Changing pH alters the electrical charges on the amino acids that make up the enzyme, which affects the enzyme's three-dimensional shape and therefore its active site.

EnzymeOptimal pHLocationReason
Pepsin~2 (very acidic)StomachStomach is highly acidic from hydrochloric acid
Salivary amylase~6.8–7MouthSaliva is nearly neutral
Trypsin~8 (slightly basic)Small intestinePancreatic secretions neutralize and slightly raise pH
Most cellular enzymes~7–7.4Cytoplasm / bloodBody maintains near-neutral internal pH

Substrate Concentration and Saturation

If enzyme concentration stays the same, increasing substrate concentration initially increases reaction rate — more substrate means more successful collisions with active sites. However, reaction rate eventually levels off as all active sites become occupied. This upper limit is called saturation. At saturation, adding more substrate has no additional effect because no more enzyme is available.

Inhibitors

Enzyme inhibitors are molecules that reduce or stop enzyme activity. Inhibition is one of the cell's most important regulatory tools — and one of medicine's most powerful targets.

Inhibitor TypeBinding LocationMechanismCan Substrate Overcome It?
CompetitiveActive siteResembles substrate; blocks substrate accessYes — high substrate concentration can outcompete the inhibitor
NoncompetitiveAllosteric site (not active site)Changes enzyme shape, distorting active siteNo — substrate cannot overcome this type
Worked Example 8.6.2
Distinguishing competitive from noncompetitive inhibition
  • Scenario AA molecule that looks like the substrate binds to the active site and blocks the real substrate. When more substrate is added, activity recovers. → Competitive inhibition.
  • Scenario BA regulatory molecule binds to a different part of the enzyme. The active site's shape changes so the substrate no longer fits. Adding more substrate does not help. → Noncompetitive inhibition.
  • Key testDoes increasing substrate restore activity? Yes → competitive. No → noncompetitive.
The location of binding and the substrate's ability to compete determine inhibition type.

Section 8.7

Enzyme Regulation

Cells do not let metabolism run at full speed all the time. Producing more products than needed wastes energy and materials. Using up a substrate completely might deprive other pathways of needed molecules. Regulation keeps metabolism precisely balanced — a remarkable feat given how many reactions are running simultaneously.

Allosteric Regulation

Many enzymes have allosteric sites — regions separate from the active site where regulatory molecules can bind. When a molecule binds to an allosteric site, it changes the enzyme's overall shape, which changes the activity of the active site. Some allosteric molecules are activators (they increase activity); others are inhibitors (they decrease it). Allosteric regulation is fast and reversible, making it ideal for moment-to-moment adjustment.

Worked Example 8.7.1
Feedback inhibition in an amino acid synthesis pathway
  • SetupA cell uses a five-step pathway to produce the amino acid isoleucine. The first enzyme in the pathway has an allosteric site that binds isoleucine.
  • Product builds upAs isoleucine accumulates (more than the cell needs), it binds to the allosteric site on the first enzyme. This changes the enzyme's shape, reducing or stopping its activity.
  • EffectThe entire pathway slows. Less starting material enters the sequence, so less isoleucine is made.
  • RecoveryAs isoleucine is used up by the cell, its concentration drops. The inhibitor dissociates from the allosteric site, restoring enzyme activity. Production resumes.
Feedback inhibition: the end product of a pathway inhibits an enzyme earlier in that same pathway — a self-regulating loop.
Why Feedback Inhibition Is Elegant

Feedback inhibition requires no external control system. The pathway monitors and regulates itself. When the product is plentiful, production slows. When the product is scarce, production resumes. This self-regulation appears throughout metabolism — in amino acid synthesis, nucleotide synthesis, and many other pathways.

Some inhibitors bind permanently to enzymes, inactivating them irreversibly. These include certain toxins and nerve agents. Unlike competitive or allosteric inhibition, irreversible inhibition cannot be overcome by changing concentrations. The enzyme is permanently destroyed.

Section 8.8

Metabolic Pathways

Individual enzyme-catalyzed reactions rarely work in isolation. In cells, reactions are organized into metabolic pathways — sequences of reactions in which the product of one reaction becomes the substrate of the next. Each step is catalyzed by a specific enzyme, and each intermediate molecule in the sequence is called a metabolic intermediate.

Structure of a Metabolic Pathway

Starting molecule → Enzyme A → Intermediate 1 → Enzyme B → Intermediate 2 → Enzyme C → Final Product

Pathways allow cells to break or build complex molecules step by step, capturing or spending energy at each stage, and to branch toward different products depending on the cell's needs.

Energy Coupling

Many anabolic reactions require energy. Cells often couple endergonic reactions to exergonic ones — the energy released by an exergonic reaction is captured in ATP and immediately used to drive an endergonic reaction. This coupling makes otherwise unfavorable reactions proceed. Metabolism is built on coupling: energy from food breakdown is not lost as heat, but channeled into the reactions that build, repair, and maintain the cell.

Pathway NameTypeLocationSummary
GlycolysisCatabolicCytoplasm of all cellsGlucose (6C) is broken into two 3-carbon molecules; small ATP release. First stage of cellular respiration.
Protein synthesisAnabolicRibosomesAmino acids are joined in sequence to build a protein. ATP drives each addition step.

Section 8.9

Enzymes in Digestion, Medicine, and Industry

Enzymes are not just important inside cells. They have widespread applications in medicine, industry, and everyday life. Understanding enzyme function has opened up entire fields of biotechnology and pharmacology.

Enzymes in Digestion

Digestion is largely a story of enzymes. When you eat, your body must break large food molecules into small enough pieces to absorb through the intestinal wall. Enzymes in saliva, the stomach, and the small intestine catalyze the breakdown of carbohydrates, proteins, and fats. You will study the digestive system in detail in Chapter 26.

EnzymeLocationSubstrateProducts
Salivary amylaseMouth (saliva)StarchSugars; digestion begins as you chew
PepsinStomachProteinsShorter peptide chains; requires pH ~2
Pancreatic lipaseSmall intestineFatsFatty acids and glycerol
Pancreatic proteasesSmall intestineProteins / peptidesAmino acids; continue protein digestion
LactaseSmall intestineLactoseGlucose and galactose
Lactose Intolerance and Enzyme Deficiency

Lactose intolerance occurs when the body produces insufficient lactase. Without lactase, lactose is not digested in the small intestine and passes to the large intestine, where bacteria ferment it — producing gas and discomfort. Lactase enzyme supplements allow people with lactose intolerance to digest dairy products by supplying the missing enzyme.

Enzymes in Medicine

Many medications work by targeting enzymes. Understanding enzyme function has made possible entire classes of life-saving treatments.

Drug / TreatmentTarget EnzymeMedical Application
StatinsHMG-CoA reductaseLower cholesterol by inhibiting the enzyme that synthesizes it
ACE inhibitorsAngiotensin-converting enzymeLower blood pressure by blocking enzyme that constricts blood vessels
PenicillinBacterial cell wall synthesis enzymesKills bacteria by blocking enzymes bacteria need but humans lack
HIV protease inhibitorsHIV proteaseBlock viral enzyme HIV needs to assemble new virus particles

Defective enzymes also cause disease. Many inherited metabolic disorders result from mutations that produce a nonfunctional enzyme, causing metabolic pathways to fail or back up with harmful intermediates. Phenylketonuria (PKU), for example, results from a mutation in the gene for an enzyme that processes the amino acid phenylalanine. Without this enzyme, phenylalanine accumulates to toxic levels, causing neurological damage if untreated.

Enzymes in Industry

Industry uses enzymes extensively because they are efficient, specific, biodegradable, and work under mild conditions — far more sustainable than many industrial chemicals.

IndustryEnzyme UsedFunction
Laundry detergentsProteases, lipasesBreak down protein and fat stains in clothing
Cheese makingProteases (rennet)Curdle milk; develop texture and flavor
Bread bakingAmylasesBreak down starch to feed yeast, aiding rise and flavor
BiofuelsCellulasesBreak down plant material into fermentable sugars
Biotechnology (PCR)Taq polymeraseCopy DNA at high temperatures; essential for PCR
Taq Polymerase and the Discovery at Yellowstone

One of the most important industrial enzymes came from a hot spring at Yellowstone National Park. Scientists isolated a bacterium called Thermus aquaticus that lives at near-boiling temperatures. Its DNA polymerase — an enzyme that copies DNA — is stable at high temperatures. This heat-stable enzyme, called Taq polymerase, is now used in PCR (polymerase chain reaction), a technique that allows scientists to make millions of copies of a DNA segment in a few hours. PCR is used in medicine, forensics, evolutionary biology, and genetic research. You will learn more about PCR in Chapter 16.

Key Terms Review

metabolism
The complete network of chemical reactions in a living organism, including both catabolism and anabolism.
catabolism
Metabolic reactions that break down molecules and release energy. Example: breaking glucose into CO₂ and water.
anabolism
Metabolic reactions that build up molecules and require energy input. Example: assembling amino acids into a protein.
reactant
A starting material in a chemical reaction that is converted into products.
product
An ending material produced by a chemical reaction.
exergonic reaction
A reaction that releases energy; products have less stored energy than reactants.
endergonic reaction
A reaction that requires a net input of energy; products have more stored energy than reactants.
activation energy
The minimum energy required to start a chemical reaction — the energy barrier reactants must overcome.
catalyst
A substance that speeds up a chemical reaction without being consumed by it.
enzyme
A biological catalyst, almost always a protein, that speeds up chemical reactions in living systems by lowering activation energy.
substrate
The specific molecule or molecules that an enzyme binds to and acts upon.
active site
The region of an enzyme where the substrate binds and the reaction takes place. Its shape is complementary to the substrate.
induced fit
The model of enzyme-substrate binding in which both enzyme and substrate change shape slightly to achieve a precise, functional fit.
enzyme-substrate complex
The combined structure formed when a substrate binds to an enzyme's active site, just before the reaction occurs.
denaturation
The loss of a protein's functional three-dimensional shape due to heat, extreme pH, or other disruptive conditions. Often irreversible.
optimal temperature
The temperature at which an enzyme functions most efficiently. Above this temperature, denaturation occurs.
optimal pH
The pH at which an enzyme functions most efficiently. Deviations from this pH reduce or eliminate activity.
competitive inhibitor
A molecule that competes with the substrate for the active site. Can be overcome by increasing substrate concentration.
noncompetitive inhibitor
A molecule that binds to an allosteric site and reduces activity by changing the enzyme's shape. Cannot be overcome by more substrate.
allosteric site
A regulatory site on an enzyme, separate from the active site, where inhibitors or activators can bind to change enzyme activity.
feedback inhibition
A regulatory mechanism in which the end product of a metabolic pathway inhibits an enzyme earlier in that same pathway, preventing overproduction.
metabolic pathway
A sequence of enzyme-catalyzed reactions in which the product of one step becomes the substrate of the next.
metabolic intermediate
A molecule produced partway through a metabolic pathway that serves as the substrate for the next reaction in the sequence.

Common Misconceptions

Misconception

Enzymes change the energy of a reaction. Enzymes do not change whether a reaction is exergonic or endergonic. They only change the speed by lowering activation energy. A reaction that is energetically impossible without an enzyme is equally impossible with one — the enzyme merely makes possible reactions happen faster.

Misconception

Denatured enzymes can always be restored. Many students assume that cooling a heated enzyme back to normal temperature will restore its function. This is sometimes true for mild denaturation, but at high temperatures the protein unfolds irreversibly — like cooking an egg. You cannot "uncook" a denatured enzyme by cooling it.

Misconception

All enzymes in the human body work at the same pH. Enzymes are adapted to the environment in which they work. Pepsin in the acidic stomach works best at pH 2, while enzymes in the blood work best near pH 7.4. The same enzyme outside its optimal pH range would be largely inactive.

Misconception

Competitive inhibitors destroy enzymes. Competitive inhibitors bind reversibly to the active site. They block the substrate temporarily, but the enzyme itself is not damaged. If the inhibitor leaves (which happens naturally as concentrations change), the enzyme can function normally again.

Misconception

Metabolism only means burning calories. "Metabolism" is often used informally to mean the rate at which a body burns energy. In biology, metabolism includes all chemical reactions — both breakdown (catabolism) and building up (anabolism). Every living cell performs both at the same time, all the time.

Looking Ahead
Chapter 9: Photosynthesis

Enzymes are the molecular machines that make life's chemistry possible. In Chapter 9, you will see enzymes at work in one of biology's most important processes: photosynthesis. Plants, algae, and some bacteria use enzyme-powered reactions to capture energy from sunlight and store it in glucose — the very sugar molecule that cellular respiration will later break down to power life. Understanding enzymes here prepares you to understand how living systems capture, store, and transfer energy across the entire biosphere. The concepts of activation energy, enzyme regulation, and metabolic pathways will appear throughout Chapters 9 and 10 as you study the two great energy transformations of living systems.

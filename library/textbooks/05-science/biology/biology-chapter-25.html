Chapter 25 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Every cell in your body needs oxygen and nutrients to survive. Every cell also produces waste — carbon dioxide, heat, and other byproducts that must be removed or neutralized. And at any moment, microscopic organisms are attempting to enter your body and use it as a food source. Three systems handle these challenges together: the circulatory system, the respiratory system, and the immune system.

By the end of this chapter, you should understand how the heart pumps blood through two connected loops, how oxygen and carbon dioxide are exchanged in the lungs, how the blood carries those gases to and from every cell, and how the body defends itself through both rapid general defenses and slow but highly targeted adaptive immunity. You will see how these systems are integrated — not independent — and how their coordination is an expression of homeostasis at the organ system level.

Opening Question

When you exercise vigorously, your breathing deepens and your heart beats faster — changes that begin within seconds. What signal triggers this response? The answer is not "low oxygen" but "high carbon dioxide." By the end of this chapter, you will understand the feedback loop that coordinates breathing rate, heart rate, blood pH, and cellular energy demand — and why a single molecule, CO₂, is at the center of it all.

Section 25.1

Transport and Defense in the Body

A single cell survives by exchanging materials directly with its surroundings — oxygen diffuses in, carbon dioxide diffuses out, nutrients arrive, wastes leave. Diffusion alone is sufficient when the distance between the cell and its environment is small. A human body cannot work this way. With trillions of cells, many of them far from any surface in contact with the outside world, diffusion across such distances is far too slow to meet cellular demands.

Large multicellular organisms solve this problem with bulk flow — the movement of fluid through a network of vessels — to carry materials rapidly across long distances. Diffusion then covers only the final, short leg of the journey: from the vessel to the individual cell. The circulatory system provides that bulk-flow network. The respiratory system provides the gas-exchange interface. The immune system uses the circulatory network to deploy defenses wherever threats appear.

Three Systems, One Mission

Circulatory system — provides the bulk-flow network: heart, blood vessels, and blood.

Respiratory system — provides the gas-exchange surface (lungs) where O₂ enters blood and CO₂ leaves.

Immune system — uses the circulatory network to deploy defenses wherever threats appear.

Scale

If all the blood vessels in a human body were laid end to end, they would extend approximately 100,000 km — enough to circle Earth more than twice. Blood completes a full circuit in roughly one minute at rest. The heart beats about 100,000 times per day, every day of a human lifetime.

Section 25.2

Blood and Its Components

Blood is a liquid tissue — a mixture of cells, cell fragments, proteins, and dissolved substances suspended in a watery fluid. An adult human body contains approximately five liters of blood.

Plasma

About 55 percent of blood volume is plasma — a pale yellow liquid that is roughly 90 percent water. Dissolved in plasma are proteins (including antibodies, clotting factors, and albumin), glucose and other nutrients, hormones, waste products such as urea, oxygen and carbon dioxide, and ions including sodium, potassium, and calcium. Plasma is the medium through which dissolved substances travel and in which cells are suspended.

Red Blood Cells

Red blood cells (erythrocytes) make up most of the cellular content of blood. In mature human red blood cells, the nucleus is absent — an unusual feature that creates more interior space for oxygen-binding proteins. The key protein is hemoglobin, an iron-containing molecule that binds oxygen in regions of high oxygen concentration (the lungs) and releases it in regions of low oxygen concentration (active tissues). Each hemoglobin molecule can carry up to four oxygen molecules. Red blood cells live approximately 120 days before being replaced; the body produces millions of new ones every second.

White Blood Cells

White blood cells (leukocytes) make up less than one percent of blood volume under normal conditions and are the cellular arm of the immune system. Neutrophils engulf bacteria through phagocytosis. Lymphocytes (B cells and T cells) carry out adaptive immunity. Monocytes differentiate into macrophages — large cells that engulf pathogens and present antigen fragments to the adaptive immune system. Eosinophils and basophils respond to parasites and allergic reactions.

Platelets

Platelets are small cell fragments that participate in blood clotting. When a blood vessel is damaged, platelets clump at the injury site and trigger a clotting cascade — converting fibrinogen to fibrin threads that trap red blood cells and seal the wound. This is one of the few cases in the body where positive feedback serves a clearly beneficial function: the rapid amplification of the clotting signal results in a firm clot within minutes.

Section 25.3

The Heart

The heart is a muscular pump about the size of a fist, located slightly left of center in the chest cavity. It beats continuously throughout life, contracting approximately 60 to 100 times per minute at rest. The heart has four chambers arranged in two functional pairs, separated by a thick muscular wall called the septum — the right and left sides do not share blood.

Why the Left Ventricle Has Thicker Walls

The left ventricle must generate enough pressure to push blood throughout the entire body — a much longer circuit than the lungs. The right ventricle only needs to push blood the short distance to the lungs. Greater distance requires greater pressure, which requires more muscular wall thickness.

Valves and the Cardiac Cycle

Four valves prevent backflow. Atrioventricular (AV) valves separate atria from ventricles; semilunar valves are at the exits of the ventricles. The familiar "lub-dub" of a heartbeat is these valves closing in sequence. The heart's rhythm originates in the sinoatrial (SA) node — the natural pacemaker in the right atrium — which sends electrical impulses that spread across the atria and then to the ventricles after a brief delay at the AV node. One heartbeat consists of systole (ventricular contraction) and diastole (ventricular relaxation and filling). Blood pressure is measured as systolic pressure over diastolic pressure; a typical healthy reading is approximately 120/80 mmHg.

Two Loops of Circulation

Pulmonary circulation: right ventricle → pulmonary arteries → lungs → pulmonary veins → left atrium. Blood picks up O₂ and releases CO₂.

Systemic circulation: left ventricle → aorta → arteries → capillaries → veins → right atrium. Blood delivers O₂ and nutrients; collects CO₂ and wastes.

The two loops are serial — blood passes through the lungs on every circuit.

Section 25.4

Blood Vessels and Circulation

Vessel TypeDirectionWall ThicknessKey Features
ArteriesAway from heartThick, muscular, elasticWithstand high pressure; arterioles regulate blood flow to tissues
CapillariesBetween arteries and veinsOne cell thickSite of all gas, nutrient, and waste exchange with tissues
VeinsToward heartThinContain valves to prevent backflow; hold ~60–70% of blood volume at rest

At capillaries, the real work of circulation takes place. Walls are only one cell thick, allowing easy diffusion of oxygen and nutrients from blood to tissue, and carbon dioxide and wastes from tissue to blood. Nearly every cell in the body is within a few cell-widths of a capillary.

Blood in veins returns to the heart at low pressure, aided by one-way valves in the vein walls, skeletal muscle contractions that squeeze veins during movement, and pressure changes in the chest during breathing. The lymphatic system collects fluid that leaks from capillaries into surrounding tissues, returns it to the bloodstream, and filters it through lymph nodes — connecting fluid return to immunity.

Common Confusion — Arteries vs. Veins and Oxygen Content

Arteries always carry blood away from the heart; veins always carry blood toward the heart — regardless of oxygen content. The pulmonary artery carries deoxygenated blood; the pulmonary veins carry oxygenated blood. The oxygen-content distinction applies only to systemic circulation.

Section 25.5

The Respiratory System

Cellular respiration — the process by which cells extract energy from glucose — requires oxygen and produces carbon dioxide. These gases must be exchanged with the external environment continuously. In large organisms like humans, a specialized gas-exchange surface — the lungs — brings air into close contact with the circulatory system.

Path of Air

Air enters through the nose and mouth, where it is warmed, moistened, and filtered by mucus and cilia. It then passes through the pharynx (throat), larynx (voice box), and into the trachea (windpipe) — a rigid tube reinforced by cartilage rings. The trachea branches into two bronchi, one entering each lung. Inside the lungs, bronchi branch repeatedly into smaller bronchioles. This branching pattern dramatically increases surface area. At the ends of bronchioles are clusters of tiny air sacs called alveoli — the site of actual gas exchange.

Why Alveoli Are Effective Gas Exchange Surfaces

Enormous number: 300–500 million alveoli, total surface area ~70 m² — about the size of one side of a tennis court.

Extremely thin walls: one cell thick — the shortest possible diffusion distance.

Rich capillary network: each alveolus is surrounded by capillaries, minimizing distance to blood.

Moist surface: gases dissolve into the fluid lining before crossing; gas exchange requires dissolved molecules.

Breathing Mechanics

The lungs have no muscle — they expand and contract passively as the chest cavity changes volume. Inhalation: the diaphragm contracts and flattens downward; intercostal muscles lift the rib cage; chest cavity volume increases; lung pressure drops below atmospheric; air flows in. Exhalation at rest is largely passive: muscles relax; elastic lungs recoil; chest cavity volume decreases; lung pressure rises; air flows out. The breathing control center in the brainstem (medulla oblongata) monitors CO₂ levels in the blood — not oxygen — and adjusts breathing rate automatically. Rising CO₂ triggers faster, deeper breathing; this is why you cannot hold your breath indefinitely.

The Brainstem Monitors CO₂, Not O₂

A common assumption is that the urge to breathe is triggered by falling oxygen. In reality, the primary signal is rising CO₂ (and the resulting fall in blood pH). This distinction matters: a person who hyperventilates blows off CO₂, reducing the drive to breathe even though their oxygen level has not changed significantly.

Section 25.6

Gas Exchange in the Lungs

Oxygen Loading

Oxygen diffuses from the alveolus into the surrounding capillary and immediately binds to hemoglobin in red blood cells, forming oxyhemoglobin. This binding removes oxygen from solution, maintaining a steep diffusion gradient that continues pulling more oxygen across the alveolar wall. In the lungs: high oxygen partial pressure → hemoglobin loads oxygen. In active tissues: low oxygen partial pressure → hemoglobin releases oxygen. This sensitivity to local oxygen concentration makes hemoglobin a load-and-deliver system — picking up oxygen where it is abundant and releasing it where it is scarce.

Carbon Dioxide Transport

CO₂ is transported from tissues to the lungs in three ways: dissolved directly in plasma (~7–10%), bound to hemoglobin (~20–23%), and converted to bicarbonate ions (HCO₃⁻) in red blood cells by the enzyme carbonic anhydrase (~70%). At the lungs, these processes reverse: bicarbonate is converted back to CO₂, which diffuses from capillary into the alveolus and is exhaled.

Worked Example 25.6.1
Predicting gas exchange direction from partial pressure gradients
  • ScenarioAlveolar air has an oxygen partial pressure (pO₂) of 105 mmHg. Blood arriving at the pulmonary capillary from the body has pO₂ of 40 mmHg.
  • QuestionIn which direction does oxygen move? What drives this movement?
  • SolveGases diffuse from higher concentration (higher partial pressure) to lower concentration. Oxygen partial pressure is higher in the alveolus (105) than in the incoming blood (40). Therefore oxygen diffuses from alveolus into the capillary.
  • NoteCO₂ moves in the opposite direction: pCO₂ is higher in the blood arriving from active tissues (~45 mmHg) than in alveolar air (~40 mmHg), so CO₂ diffuses from capillary into alveolus and is exhaled.
Oxygen moves from alveolus → capillary. CO₂ moves from capillary → alveolus. Both driven by diffusion down concentration gradients.
Section 25.7

Oxygen, Carbon Dioxide, and Homeostasis

Carbon dioxide does not simply pass harmlessly through the blood. When CO₂ dissolves in blood plasma, it reacts with water to form carbonic acid, which dissociates into bicarbonate ions and hydrogen ions (H⁺). Rising CO₂ therefore lowers blood pH — makes it more acidic. Normal blood pH is approximately 7.35 to 7.45, a very narrow range. Small deviations impair enzyme function and disrupt nerve signaling.

The CO₂–pH–Breathing Feedback Loop

Disturbance: Muscles produce CO₂ during exercise → blood CO₂ rises → pH falls.

Sensor: Brainstem detects falling pH (rising CO₂).

Response: Brainstem signals faster, deeper breathing.

Correction: More CO₂ exhaled → blood CO₂ falls → pH rises back toward normal.

This is negative feedback: the deviation triggers a response that reverses the deviation.

The Exercise Response

During vigorous exercise, CO₂ rises in the blood, signaling the brainstem to increase breathing rate and depth. The nervous system simultaneously increases heart rate and cardiac output. Arterioles in working muscles dilate; arterioles elsewhere constrict — blood is redirected to where demand is highest. More blood passes through the lungs per minute, picking up more oxygen. When exercise stops, CO₂ falls, breathing slows, and heart rate decreases. The response is not delayed — it begins within seconds of the increase in demand and resolves within minutes of rest.

Sections 25.8 – 25.10
The Immune System: Defending the Body
Innate Defenses · Adaptive Immunity · Vaccines and Memory
Section 25.8

Innate Defenses

The immune system operates in layers. The outermost layers are general and fast — they respond to any threat without needing to identify it specifically. These are innate defenses. Deeper layers are slower but highly specific and long-lasting — these are adaptive defenses.

Physical and Chemical Barriers

The body's first line of defense is its outer surface. Skin is a physical barrier whose outermost layer of dead, keratinized cells resists penetration by most pathogens. Mucus in the respiratory, digestive, and urinary tracts traps pathogens before they reach cells. Cilia sweep mucus upward toward the throat. Stomach acid (pH ~2) destroys most swallowed pathogens. Antimicrobial proteins in tears and saliva inhibit microorganisms. The body's normal microbiota — trillions of resident bacteria — compete with pathogens for space and resources.

Inflammation

When barriers are breached, inflammation is the body's immediate response. It is not a disease but a defense mechanism. The classic signs — redness, warmth, swelling, and pain — reflect a coordinated physiological response. Damaged cells release chemical signals (histamine, cytokines). Blood vessels near the site dilate and become more permeable, increasing blood flow (redness, warmth). Fluid and proteins leak into tissue (swelling). Neutrophils and monocytes migrate out of blood vessels and begin destroying bacteria by phagocytosis. Macrophages engulf debris and present antigen fragments to the adaptive immune system. Pain results from swelling pressing on nerve endings and from chemical signals that sensitize pain receptors — alerting the organism to protect the injured area.

Fever, Phagocytosis, and Natural Killer Cells

Fever — a rise in core body temperature above 37°C — is triggered by chemical signals (pyrogens) released by immune cells during infection. Fever appears to impair growth of many bacteria and viruses, accelerate some immune reactions, and reduce iron availability for bacterial reproduction. Mild fever is generally useful; very high fever (>40°C / 104°F) can be dangerous because high temperatures begin to denature human proteins.

Natural killer (NK) cells patrol the body looking for cells that display abnormal surface proteins — signs of viral infection or cancer. Normal cells display surface markers that signal "healthy self-cell." Cells that fail to display these markers are destroyed by NK cells, which release proteins that trigger cell death.

Innate vs. Adaptive at a Glance

Innate immunity: fast (minutes to hours), nonspecific, no memory formed. Adaptive immunity: slow to start (days), highly specific to a particular antigen, leaves behind memory cells. Most infections are cleared by innate defenses alone; adaptive immunity is recruited when innate responses are insufficient or when the pathogen requires targeted antibodies.

Section 25.9

Adaptive Immunity

Adaptive immunity is specific, slow to start, powerful, and long-lasting. An antigen is any molecule — typically a protein or polysaccharide on a pathogen's surface — that the immune system recognizes and targets. Lymphocytes are the central cells of adaptive immunity. B cells produce antibodies. T cells include helper T cells (which coordinate the response), cytotoxic T cells (which kill infected cells), and memory T cells (which persist after infection). Each B cell and T cell has surface receptors specific to a particular antigen, and the body generates an enormous diversity of these cells before any infection occurs.

Clonal Selection and the Adaptive Response

  • 1Antigen presentation: Macrophages engulf pathogens, process their proteins, and display antigen fragments on their surface using MHC proteins.
  • 2Helper T cell activation: A helper T cell whose receptor matches the displayed antigen binds to the macrophage and becomes activated. It releases cytokines that signal other immune cells.
  • 3Clonal selection and expansion: Activated helper T cells stimulate B cells and cytotoxic T cells whose receptors match the pathogen's antigen. These selected cells divide rapidly, producing many identical copies — a clone — all targeting the same antigen.
  • 4Effector cells: Most cloned B cells become plasma cells that produce large quantities of antibodies. Cloned cytotoxic T cells kill infected and cancerous cells.
  • 5Memory cells: Some cloned cells become long-lived memory cells that remain in the body after infection, ready to respond rapidly to future exposure.

Antibodies

Antibodies are Y-shaped proteins. Their variable region (the "arms") binds specifically to one antigen. Their constant region (the "stem") determines function. Antibodies defend the body by neutralization (blocking pathogens from entering cells), agglutination (clumping pathogens so phagocytes can engulf them more easily), complement activation (triggering proteins that punch holes in bacterial membranes), and flagging (coating pathogens to signal phagocytes to destroy them).

Worked Example 25.9.1
Primary vs. secondary immune response
  • ScenarioA student gets chickenpox at age 8. At age 35, they are exposed to the same virus again. Compare the two immune responses.
  • First exposureNo memory cells exist for this antigen. Adaptive response takes several days to develop. Antibody levels rise slowly. The student becomes ill while the immune system builds its response. Memory cells are formed as the infection clears.
  • Second exposureMemory B cells and T cells specific to the chickenpox antigen are already present. Response begins within hours. Antibody production is faster and reaches much higher levels. The virus is typically cleared before significant symptoms develop.
  • ConclusionThe biological basis of immunity is the persistence of memory cells. The second response is faster, larger, and more effective — the pathogen is often neutralized before the person becomes ill.
Second exposure → faster response (hours vs. days), higher antibody levels, milder or no illness — all due to surviving memory cells from the first exposure.
Section 25.10

Vaccines and Immune Memory

A vaccine trains the immune system to recognize a specific pathogen without causing the disease itself. Vaccines introduce antigens in a form that does not cause serious illness, triggering an immune response and generating memory cells. If the vaccinated person later encounters the actual pathogen, their immune system responds with a fast, targeted secondary response — often clearing the pathogen before symptoms develop.

Herd Immunity

When a sufficiently large proportion of a population is immune — through infection or vaccination — the pathogen cannot find enough susceptible hosts to sustain transmission. Even individuals who cannot be vaccinated (due to age, allergy, or immune disorders) are protected indirectly because transmission chains are broken. This population-level protection is called herd immunity. The coverage needed depends on contagiousness. Measles (highly contagious) requires approximately 95% population immunity; polio requires roughly 80–85%. When vaccination rates fall below the threshold, previously controlled diseases can re-emerge.

Misconception — Vaccines Give You the Disease

Vaccines do not give you the disease. Some live attenuated vaccines can occasionally cause very mild symptoms in healthy individuals, but they cannot cause the full disease. Inactivated vaccines, protein subunit vaccines, and mRNA vaccines contain no living pathogen whatsoever and cannot cause infection. Side effects like soreness or mild fever are signs that the immune system is responding — which is exactly the goal.

When Immunity Misfires

Allergies occur when the immune system mounts a response against harmless antigens — pollen, pet dander, certain foods. The response involves IgE antibodies and histamine release from mast cells, producing symptoms from runny nose to, in severe cases, life-threatening anaphylaxis. Autoimmune conditions occur when the immune system attacks the body's own healthy tissues — joints in rheumatoid arthritis, myelin sheaths in multiple sclerosis, insulin-producing cells in Type 1 diabetes. Treatment typically involves suppressing immune activity, which must be balanced against increased vulnerability to infection.

Section 25.11

Circulation, Respiration, and Immunity Working Together

These three systems do not operate independently. They are integrated into a single coordinated response to the body's needs.

Integration During Infection

  • 1Damaged tissue releases inflammatory signals → blood vessels near the wound dilate (circulatory system).
  • 2Increased blood flow delivers more neutrophils and macrophages to the site (circulatory + immune).
  • 3Neutrophils and macrophages engulf bacteria by phagocytosis (innate immunity).
  • 4Fever develops, raising metabolism and oxygen demand → breathing deepens (respiratory + immune).
  • 5Macrophages present antigens → helper T cells activate → B cells produce antibodies (adaptive immunity).
  • 6Antibody-coated bacteria are engulfed more efficiently by phagocytes (adaptive + innate).
  • 7Memory cells remain after infection is cleared (immune).
  • 8Inflammation resolves; heart rate and breathing return to baseline (all three systems + homeostasis).

Integration During Exercise

  • 1Muscles consume O₂ and produce CO₂ → blood CO₂ rises → pH drops slightly.
  • 2Brainstem signals increased breathing rate and depth → more CO₂ exhaled (respiratory).
  • 3Heart rate and stroke volume increase → more blood per minute through lungs and muscles (circulatory).
  • 4Arterioles dilate in working muscles, constrict elsewhere → blood redirected to highest demand (circulatory).
  • 5Immune surveillance continues throughout exercise (immune).
  • 6Exercise ends → CO₂ falls → heart rate slows → breathing slows → all variables return to resting set points via negative feedback.
Homeostasis as the Unifying Theme

The circulatory, respiratory, and immune systems together maintain oxygen levels, CO₂ and blood pH, blood pressure and flow distribution, body temperature, internal sterility, and blood volume and composition. These variables are coupled — changes in one affect the others, and the body responds to multiple signals simultaneously. The result, under normal conditions, is a stable internal environment in which cells can function and life can continue. This is homeostasis at the organ-system level.

  • Sec 25.7Rising CO₂ lowers blood pH because CO₂ reacts with water to form carbonic acid. The brainstem detects falling pH and increases breathing rate — a negative feedback loop. During exercise, increased CO₂, heart rate, and breathing rate are coordinated to match oxygen delivery to demand.
  • Sec 25.8Innate defenses include physical barriers (skin, mucus, cilia, stomach acid), inflammation (vasodilation, phagocyte recruitment), fever (elevated temperature impairs pathogens), phagocytes (engulf and destroy bacteria and debris), and NK cells (destroy infected and cancerous cells). Innate responses are fast and nonspecific.
  • Sec 25.9Adaptive immunity is specific to a particular antigen. B cells become plasma cells that produce antibodies; T cells include helper T cells (coordinate the response via cytokines), cytotoxic T cells (kill infected cells), and memory T cells (enable rapid secondary response). Clonal selection amplifies lymphocytes that match the pathogen. The secondary response is faster and larger than the primary response because memory cells already exist.
  • Sec 25.10Vaccines introduce antigens safely, triggering immune memory without disease. Types include live attenuated, inactivated, subunit, mRNA, and toxoid vaccines. Herd immunity occurs when enough of a population is immune to interrupt pathogen transmission. Allergies and autoimmune conditions represent misdirected immune responses against harmless substances or self-tissue.
  • Sec 25.11The circulatory, respiratory, and immune systems are integrated — not independent. They respond together during infection and exercise, and together maintain the stable internal conditions (oxygen, pH, blood pressure, temperature, sterility) that homeostasis requires.

Key Terms Review

adaptive immunity
The specific, slow-developing, long-lasting branch of immunity involving lymphocytes, antibodies, and immune memory; responds to particular antigens.
alveoli
Tiny air sacs at the ends of bronchioles in the lungs; the sites of gas exchange between air and blood.
antibody
A Y-shaped protein produced by plasma cells that binds specifically to one antigen to neutralize it or mark it for destruction.
antigen
A molecule (usually a protein or polysaccharide on a pathogen's surface) that the immune system recognizes and targets.
aorta
The body's largest artery; carries oxygenated blood from the left ventricle to the systemic circulation.
arteries
Blood vessels that carry blood away from the heart; have thick, muscular, elastic walls to withstand high pressure.
B cells
Lymphocytes that produce antibodies; become plasma cells when activated; leave memory B cells after infection.
capillaries
The smallest blood vessels, one cell thick; the site of gas, nutrient, and waste exchange between blood and tissues.
cardiac cycle
One complete sequence of heart contraction (systole) and relaxation (diastole).
clonal selection
The process by which a lymphocyte whose receptor matches a specific antigen is activated and multiplies, producing a clone of identical antigen-specific cells.
diaphragm
The dome-shaped muscle below the lungs; contraction drives inhalation by increasing chest cavity volume.
hemoglobin
The iron-containing protein in red blood cells that binds oxygen in the lungs and releases it in active tissues.
herd immunity
Population-level protection that occurs when enough individuals are immune to prevent sustained pathogen transmission, indirectly protecting those who cannot be vaccinated.
inflammation
A localized innate response to tissue damage or infection; characterized by vasodilation, increased permeability, phagocyte recruitment, redness, warmth, swelling, and pain.
innate immunity
The rapid, nonspecific layer of internal defense; includes phagocytes, NK cells, fever, and inflammation; does not form memory.
memory cells
Long-lived B and T lymphocytes that remain after an infection, enabling a faster and larger secondary immune response on re-exposure.
natural killer (NK) cells
Innate immune cells that destroy virus-infected cells and cancer cells by recognizing abnormal surface signals.
oxyhemoglobin
Hemoglobin bound to oxygen; formed in the lungs where oxygen partial pressure is high; releases oxygen in active tissues.
phagocytosis
The process by which immune cells (neutrophils, macrophages) engulf and digest pathogens, dead cells, or cellular debris.
plasma
The liquid component of blood (~55% of volume); water with dissolved proteins, nutrients, hormones, gases, and wastes.
plasma cells
Differentiated B cells that produce large quantities of antibodies during an immune response.
platelets
Small cell fragments in blood that initiate clotting by clumping at damaged vessel sites and triggering the fibrin-forming cascade.
pulmonary circulation
The loop of circulation between the heart and the lungs; blood picks up O₂ and releases CO₂.
secondary immune response
The rapid, amplified immune response on re-exposure to a previously encountered antigen; results from activation of existing memory cells.
sinoatrial (SA) node
The heart's natural pacemaker; a cluster of cells in the right atrium that generates electrical impulses initiating each heartbeat.
systemic circulation
The loop of circulation between the heart and the rest of the body (excluding lungs); delivers O₂ and nutrients, collects CO₂ and wastes.
T cells
Lymphocytes that mature in the thymus; types include helper T cells, cytotoxic T cells, and memory T cells.
trachea
The windpipe; a cartilage-reinforced tube carrying air from the larynx to the bronchi.
vaccine
A preparation that introduces antigens in a safe form to stimulate immune memory without causing disease.
veins
Blood vessels that carry blood toward the heart; thinner walls than arteries; contain valves that prevent backflow.

Common Misconceptions

Misconception — Veins and Arteries and Oxygen

The accurate statement is: arteries carry blood away from the heart; veins carry blood toward the heart — regardless of oxygen content. The pulmonary artery carries deoxygenated blood; the pulmonary veins carry oxygenated blood. The oxygen-content generalization applies only to systemic vessels.

Misconception — Blood Is Blue Inside the Body

Blood is never blue inside the body. Deoxygenated blood (hemoglobin without oxygen bound) is dark red or maroon. Veins appear bluish through skin because of the way different wavelengths of light penetrate tissue — not because of the blood's actual color.

Misconception — Breathing Is Triggered by Low Oxygen

The primary breathing-rate signal is rising CO₂ and the resulting fall in blood pH. Oxygen levels also matter, but CO₂ sensitivity is the primary fast-response driver. This is why hyperventilation — which lowers CO₂ — can temporarily reduce the urge to breathe even when oxygen is adequate.

Misconception — Immunity Is Permanent

Memory cells are long-lived but not always permanent. Immunity can wane over time for some diseases. Additionally, pathogens evolve — influenza changes surface antigens frequently, so prior immunity to one strain may not protect against a significantly mutated new strain. This is why annual flu vaccination is recommended.

Misconception — Vaccines Give You the Disease

Vaccines do not cause the full disease. Some live-attenuated vaccines can cause very mild symptoms in healthy individuals but cannot cause the disease itself. Inactivated, subunit, and mRNA vaccines contain no replicating pathogen whatsoever. Soreness and mild fever after vaccination are signs of immune activation — which is the goal.

Looking Ahead
Chapter 26: Digestion, Excretion, and Reproduction

Chapter 26 examines three more body systems that work with the circulatory system to maintain homeostasis. The digestive system breaks food molecules into forms that can be absorbed into the blood — glucose, amino acids, and fatty acids that travel through the circulatory system to cells throughout the body. The liver, connected to both the digestive and circulatory systems, regulates blood chemistry before it reaches other organs. The excretory system — centered on the kidneys — filters the blood, removing metabolic wastes and adjusting pH and blood pressure in coordination with the respiratory system. Reproduction introduces a different biological challenge: not maintaining the individual, but producing new individuals. Together, Chapters 23 through 26 build a picture of the human body as an integrated system maintaining the conditions life requires.

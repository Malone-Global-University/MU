Chapter 22 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Animals are the most behaviorally complex organisms on Earth. They move, sense, hunt, hide, communicate, compete, reproduce, and die. They inhabit nearly every environment — ocean trenches, arctic tundra, tropical rainforests, freshwater rivers, desert sand, and urban sidewalks. Despite this variety, all animals share a common thread: they are multicellular organisms that must find and consume food, move energy through their bodies, respond to the environment, and reproduce.

This chapter does not attempt to catalog every animal group. Instead, it asks a more useful question: how do animals solve common biological problems? Problems like: how do you get food into your body and break it down? How do you hold your body up and move through the world? How do you sense what is happening around you and respond quickly? How do you reproduce and ensure offspring survive? How do you fit into the ecosystems around you? By the end of this chapter, you will be able to compare major animal groups not as lists to memorize, but as different evolutionary solutions to these shared challenges.

Opening Question

A jellyfish and a chimpanzee are both animals. So are a tapeworm, a blue whale, a honeybee, and a Venus flytrap's prey — the spider. What do these organisms have in common that places them all in kingdom Animalia? And what structural and functional differences explain why they live such different lives? By the end of this chapter, you will understand both the shared blueprint and the remarkable variety of solutions animals have evolved.

Section 22.1

What Makes Animals Distinctive

Animals belong to the domain Eukarya and the kingdom Animalia. They share several defining characteristics that set them apart from plants, fungi, and other eukaryotes.

Animals are multicellular. Every animal body is built from many cells, organized into tissues that carry out specialized functions. This is in contrast to single-celled eukaryotes such as amoebas, which must perform all life functions within a single cell.

Animals are heterotrophs. Unlike plants, animals cannot make their own food through photosynthesis. They must consume other organisms to obtain organic molecules and energy. This single fact shapes almost everything about animal body plans, behavior, and ecology.

Animals lack cell walls. Plant cells have rigid cell walls made of cellulose. Animal cells do not. This gives animal cells flexibility, allowing them to change shape — a property critical for muscle contraction, immune cell movement, and the formation of complex body shapes.

Animals reproduce sexually. Most animals reproduce through sexual reproduction, combining genetic material from two parents to produce offspring with genetic variation. Many animals also have the capacity for asexual reproduction, but sexual reproduction is the rule across the kingdom.

Animals develop from a blastula. In early embryonic development, animal embryos pass through a characteristic stage called a blastula — a hollow ball of cells. This shared developmental pattern reflects the common ancestry of all animal life.

Animals are typically mobile. Most animals can move during at least one stage of their life cycle. Mobility is connected to feeding — animals must find food rather than make it.

Connection to Heterotrophy

Almost every defining feature of animals links back to heterotrophy. Because animals must find and consume food rather than make it, they need mobility (to reach prey or plants), flexible cells (for muscle contraction), nervous tissue (to detect and respond to food sources), and complex digestive systems. Heterotrophy is the organizing constraint of animal evolution.

Section 22.2

Animal Body Plans

A body plan is the overall structural organization of an animal — its symmetry, the arrangement of its tissues, the presence or absence of an internal body cavity, and how its major organs are positioned relative to each other.

Symmetry

Symmetry TypeDescriptionExamplesEcological Context
AsymmetryNo organized symmetrical arrangement; irregular body shapeSpongesSessile filter feeders; shape unimportant for movement
RadialBody divided into equal halves by multiple planes through a central axisJellyfish, sea stars, sea anemonesFloat, drift, or are attached; encounter food from any direction
BilateralDistinct left/right mirror sides; defined front, back, top, bottomInsects, fish, frogs, humansDirectional movement; sensory organs concentrated at front
Cephalization

Bilateral symmetry is closely linked to cephalization — the concentration of sensory organs and nerve tissue at the anterior (front) end, forming a head. An animal that moves forward benefits from detecting stimuli in the direction of travel before the rest of the body arrives. Nearly all bilaterally symmetrical animals have some degree of cephalization.

Body Cavities

A coelom is a fluid-filled body cavity that surrounds the internal organs, cushions them from physical shock, and provides space for organ development and movement. The type of body cavity is a key feature used in classifying animals.

TypeBody Cavity?Cavity LiningExamples

Segmentation

Many animals have bodies divided into repeating units called segments. Segmentation is prominent in annelid worms, arthropods, and vertebrates (where it appears in the vertebral column and repeated muscle blocks). Segmentation allows for specialization — different segments can be modified for feeding, locomotion, reproduction, or sensing.

Section 22.3

Tissues, Organs, and Organ Systems

All but the simplest animals have their cells organized into tissues, tissues organized into organs, and organs working together as organ systems. The four fundamental tissue types in animals are found across the kingdom.

The Four Tissue Types
Tissue, role, and examples
  • EpithelialCovers body surfaces and lines internal cavities. Acts as a barrier, controls what passes through, and produces secretions. Examples: skin surface, gut lining, lung lining.
  • ConnectiveSupports and connects other tissues. Examples: bone, cartilage, blood, tendons, fat. Bone stores calcium, produces blood cells in marrow, and supports the body.
  • MuscleCapable of contracting and generating force. Enables whole-body movement and internal movement (pumping blood, moving food through the digestive system).
  • NervousConducts electrical signals. Allows animals to detect stimuli and coordinate responses rapidly. Makes up the brain, spinal cord, and peripheral nerves.
Complexity Varies Enormously

A simple animal like a flatworm has primitive organs and a diffuse nervous system. A vertebrate like a mammal has highly differentiated organs organized into more than a dozen distinct systems. Both are animals — the difference reflects hundreds of millions of years of divergent evolution from a common ancestor.

Animal Diversity: Invertebrates and Vertebrates

Section 22.4

Invertebrate Diversity

The vast majority of animal species — roughly 97 percent — are invertebrates: animals without a backbone. Rather than surveying every group exhaustively, this section focuses on major phyla and the key structural solutions each represents.

Sponges (Phylum Porifera)

Sponges are the oldest animal lineage. They are asymmetrical, lack true tissues, and have no nervous system or muscle tissue. They are filter feeders: water flows through pores in their body wall, and specialized cells called choanocytes trap bacteria and organic particles. Despite their simplicity, sponges play important ecological roles, especially on coral reefs, and some sponge compounds have been developed into anticancer drugs.

Cnidarians (Phylum Cnidaria)

Cnidarians include jellyfish, corals, sea anemones, and hydra. They have radial symmetry and two tissue layers. Their defining feature is the cnidocyte — a specialized stinging cell used for capturing prey and defense. Cnidarians exist in two body forms: the polyp (attached, like a sea anemone) and the medusa (free-swimming, like a jellyfish). Corals are colonial polyps that secrete calcium carbonate skeletons; coral reef ecosystems support enormous biodiversity.

Flatworms (Phylum Platyhelminthes)

Flatworms are bilaterally symmetrical and acoelomate — they have no body cavity. Their flat, ribbon-like shape allows gas exchange directly through the body surface without a dedicated respiratory system. Some flatworms (like planarians) are free-living predators in freshwater. Others (like tapeworms and flukes) are parasites that live inside host organisms, including humans, and can cause serious disease.

Roundworms (Phylum Nematoda)

Roundworms, or nematodes, are among the most abundant animals on Earth. They live in soil, freshwater, saltwater, and inside the bodies of other organisms. A single handful of garden soil may contain thousands of nematodes. Most are free-living, feeding on bacteria and recycling nutrients. Others are parasitic — roundworm infections in humans affect hundreds of millions of people globally.

Annelids (Phylum Annelida)

Annelids are the segmented worms: earthworms, marine polychaetes, and leeches. They have a true coelom and a closed circulatory system. Earthworms improve soil by burrowing, aerating the soil and increasing drainage. Marine polychaetes are ecologically important in ocean floor sediments. Leeches are still used in controlled medical situations to reduce blood clotting after microsurgery.

Mollusks (Phylum Mollusca)

Mollusks are one of the largest animal phyla, including snails, clams, squids, octopuses, and nautiluses. Despite their variety, most share a basic body plan: a soft body, often with a muscular foot for movement, a mantle that may secrete a shell, and a visceral mass containing the organs. Cephalopods (squids and octopuses) are the most behaviorally complex invertebrates, with sophisticated nervous systems and remarkable camouflage abilities.

Echinoderms (Phylum Echinodermata)

Echinoderms include sea stars, sea urchins, sea cucumbers, and sand dollars. They are radially symmetrical as adults but develop from bilaterally symmetrical larvae, indicating their evolutionary relationship to vertebrates. Their most distinctive feature is a water vascular system — a network of fluid-filled canals connected to tube feet, used for movement, feeding, and gas exchange. Sea stars are keystone predators in many rocky intertidal communities.

Arthropods (Phylum Arthropoda)

Arthropods are the most species-rich animal phylum, making up more than three-quarters of all known animal species. They include insects, spiders, crustaceans (crabs, lobsters, shrimp), and myriapods (centipedes and millipedes). All arthropods share three defining features.

Three Defining Features of Arthropods

Exoskeleton — a hard external covering made of chitin that supports the body, protects against injury, and reduces water loss.

Jointed appendages — limbs used for walking, swimming, feeding, sensing, and reproduction (the name arthropod means "jointed foot").

Segmented body — often fused into distinct regions such as head, thorax, and abdomen in insects.

The Molting Constraint

The exoskeleton does not grow. Arthropods must periodically molt — shed their exoskeleton and secrete a new, larger one — to grow. During molting, the body is temporarily soft and vulnerable to predation, desiccation, and injury. This is a significant cost of the exoskeletal body plan.

Section 22.5

Vertebrate Diversity

Vertebrates are animals with a backbone — a column of vertebrae that protects the spinal cord and provides structural support. Vertebrates belong to the phylum Chordata and share several features during development: a notochord (a flexible rod that becomes the backbone), a dorsal hollow nerve cord (which becomes the brain and spinal cord), pharyngeal slits (present at least during embryonic development), and a post-anal tail at some stage. Vertebrates make up only about 3 percent of animal species, but they dominate in body size, ecological influence, and human familiarity.

Fishes

Fishes are the most ancient and diverse vertebrates. They are aquatic, breathe through gills, and are typically ectothermic — their body temperature is regulated by the environment, not internally generated. Jawless fishes (lampreys and hagfishes) are the most primitive vertebrates. Cartilaginous fishes (sharks, rays) have a skeleton made entirely of cartilage. Bony fishes, with more than 30,000 species, are by far the most diverse vertebrate group; many have a swim bladder — a gas-filled organ that allows them to adjust buoyancy and hover at a given depth without continuous swimming.

Amphibians

Amphibians (frogs, salamanders, caecilians) represent the evolutionary transition from aquatic to terrestrial life. They typically have moist, permeable skin that allows gas exchange directly through the body surface — but also means they cannot live in dry environments for long. Most amphibians lay eggs in water, and their larvae (tadpoles) are aquatic before metamorphosing into terrestrial adults with lungs. Amphibian populations worldwide have declined dramatically due to habitat loss, disease, climate change, and pollution.

Reptiles

Reptiles include lizards, snakes, turtles, crocodilians, and the tuatara. They have dry, scaly skin that resists water loss, allowing them to live in arid environments where amphibians cannot survive. Most reptiles lay amniotic eggs — eggs with a protective membrane and shell that allow development on land without drying out. Most reptiles are ectothermic and regulate body temperature through behavior — basking on warm rocks or retreating to shade — making their energy requirements far lower than those of birds and mammals of similar size.

Birds

Birds are characterized by feathers, hollow lightweight bones, a four-chambered heart, and endothermy — the ability to maintain a constant body temperature through internal metabolic heat production. Birds evolved from theropod dinosaurs; their evolutionary relationship to reptiles is reflected in their scales (on feet and legs), amniotic eggs, and molecular genetics. Their lightweight skeleton, powerful flight muscles, and feathers represent integrated adaptations for aerial life. Birds have diversified into an enormous range of feeding strategies, reflected in beak shape.

Mammals

Mammals are characterized by hair or fur, mammary glands that produce milk to nourish offspring, endothermy, a four-chambered heart, and a diaphragm that assists breathing. Mammals include three major groups: monotremes (platypus and echidnas, which lay eggs), marsupials (kangaroos, koalas, opossums, which give birth to very immature young that complete development in an external pouch), and placental mammals (which nourish the developing embryo through a placenta and are by far the most diverse group).

Section 22.6

Feeding and Digestion Strategies

All animals must consume organic molecules to obtain energy and building materials. The strategies they use to find, capture, and process food are among the most varied aspects of animal biology.

Digestive Systems

Simple animals like cnidarians have a gastrovascular cavity — a sac with one opening that serves as both mouth and anus. Food enters, digestion occurs, and waste exits through the same opening. Most animals have a complete digestive system — a tube running from mouth to anus, with different regions specialized for different stages of digestion. This allows continuous feeding and more complex processing. The length and complexity of the digestive tract often reflects diet: herbivores typically have longer intestines than carnivores, providing more time and surface area for breaking down plant material.

Section 22.7

Movement and Support

Three Types of Skeletons
Comparing hydrostatic, exoskeleton, and endoskeleton
  • HydrostaticUses fluid pressure. Circular and longitudinal muscles act against a fluid-filled coelom to generate movement. Found in soft-bodied animals: earthworms, sea anemones. Advantage: works well for burrowing. Constraint: cannot support large masses out of water.
  • ExoskeletonHard external covering (chitin in arthropods, calcium carbonate in mollusk shells). Advantage: excellent protection and firm muscle attachment. Constraint: does not grow; requires molting. Limits maximum body size on land.
  • EndoskeletonInternal hard structure (bone or cartilage in vertebrates). Advantage: grows continuously; can support very large body masses. Also protects organs, produces blood cells, and stores minerals. Constraint: heavier than exoskeleton relative to volume.

Locomotion Strategies

Animals have evolved a remarkable array of locomotion strategies: walking and running (on two, four, six, or eight legs), swimming (using fins, paddles, tails, or undulating the entire body), flying (using wings made of feathers, membranes, or skin folds), burrowing, climbing, and jet propulsion (forcing water out rapidly, as squids and scallops do). Each locomotion mode is associated with particular body proportions, musculature, and skeletal adaptations.

Section 22.8

Reproduction and Development

Asexual Reproduction

Some animals reproduce asexually. Budding occurs in hydra and some corals: a new individual grows from the parent's body and separates. Fragmentation occurs in sea stars and some worms: a body fragment regenerates into a complete individual. Parthenogenesis — development from an unfertilized egg — occurs in aphids, some lizards, and other species. Asexual reproduction produces genetically identical offspring rapidly, which is advantageous in stable environments, but generates no genetic variation, making populations vulnerable to disease or environmental change.

Metamorphosis

Many animals undergo metamorphosis — a dramatic transformation of body form during development. In insects, complete metamorphosis (holometabolism) includes four distinct stages: egg, larva, pupa, and adult. The larva (caterpillar, maggot, grub) and adult use completely different resources and occupy different ecological roles, reducing competition between young and adults of the same species. Incomplete metamorphosis (hemimetabolism) involves egg, nymph, and adult stages; the nymph resembles a small adult but lacks wings and reproductive maturity. Grasshoppers, dragonflies, and cockroaches develop this way.

Section 22.9

Animal Behavior

Behavior is any action an organism takes in response to its environment. Animal behavior is shaped by genetics, learning, and development, and it can be studied at multiple levels — from neural mechanisms to evolutionary function.

Behavior TypeOriginFlexibilityExamples
InnateGenetically programmed; appears without learningConsistent within species; not easily modifiedNewborn sea turtle heading to ocean; spider web building; bird song from birth
LearnedAcquired through experienceCan be modified by environment and individual historyHabituation; tool use in crows; problem-solving in primates
Most Behavior Is Both

The distinction between innate and learned is a useful starting point, but most behavior involves a combination of both. A songbird has an innate tendency to learn songs, but it learns the specific song it will sing by listening to adults early in life. Innate tendencies create the framework; experience fills in the details.

Communication

Animals communicate through visual signals (color patterns, postures, bioluminescence), acoustic signals (songs, calls, clicks, ultrasound), chemical signals (pheromones that attract mates, mark territories, or signal alarm), and tactile signals (grooming, touching, substrate vibrations). Communication serves reproduction, social cohesion, defense, and competition.

Social Behavior and Migration

Social living has both costs (competition for food, disease transmission) and benefits (cooperative defense, cooperative hunting, shared care of young, information transfer). Eusociality — the most extreme form of social organization — is found in bees, ants, wasps, and termites, with reproductive queens, reproductive males, and large numbers of sterile workers. Migration allows animals to exploit seasonal food or climate: arctic terns make a round trip of roughly 70,000 kilometers; monarch butterflies travel thousands of kilometers between summer breeding grounds and winter refuges.

Section 22.10

Animals in Ecosystems and Human Life

Animals do not exist in isolation. They are embedded in ecosystems and interact with one another, with plants, fungi, and microorganisms, and with physical environments. These interactions have profound effects on the structure and function of ecosystems.

Ecological Roles

Ecological Role — Worked Case
Trophic cascade: wolves in Yellowstone
  • SituationWolves were reintroduced to Yellowstone National Park after being absent for decades. Researchers observed a chain of effects far beyond what anyone predicted.
  • Effect 1Wolves reduced elk numbers and changed elk behavior — elk avoided grazing in open areas near rivers where wolves could ambush them.
  • Effect 2Reduced grazing pressure allowed streamside vegetation — willows and aspens — to recover along riverbanks.
  • Effect 3Stabilized plant roots reduced stream bank erosion; rivers narrowed and flowed in more defined channels.
  • Effect 4Recovered vegetation increased habitat for beavers, songbirds, and fish. Beaver dams created new wetland habitats.
  • ConceptThis chain of indirect effects — one species influencing many others through food web connections — is called a trophic cascade.
Trophic cascade: a change in one species triggers cascading effects through multiple levels of a food web — not just the immediately adjacent predator-prey pair.
Ecological RoleWhat Animals DoEcosystem Effect
HerbivoreConsume plant biomassControl plant populations; shape vegetation structure
PredatorRegulate prey populationsTrigger trophic cascades; maintain community structure
PollinatorTransfer pollen between flowersEnable plant reproduction; critical for agriculture
Seed disperserCarry seeds away from parent plantsEnable plant colonization of new areas; maintain forest diversity
Decomposer/DetritivoreBreak down dead organismsRecycle nutrients; maintain soil fertility
Connection to Agriculture

An estimated 75 percent of flowering plant species depend on animal pollination. Bees, butterflies, moths, hummingbirds, and many other animals transfer pollen between flowers, enabling plant reproduction. Agriculture, which feeds human civilization, depends heavily on pollinators. Declines in bee populations due to habitat loss, pesticides, and disease are a serious concern for global food security.

Humans have relied on animals throughout history for food, clothing, labor, companionship, and materials. Domestication of animals began thousands of years ago and remains central to food production. Animals are also essential to medicine: research using model organisms — mice, rats, zebrafish, fruit flies, roundworms — has produced fundamental discoveries about genetics, development, cancer, immunity, and neuroscience.

At the same time, human activities threaten animal populations worldwide. Habitat destruction, overhunting, invasive species, pollution, and climate change have driven many species to extinction and placed many more at risk. Conservation biology works to understand these threats and develop strategies to protect animal diversity and the ecosystems it sustains.

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 22.1Animals are multicellular, heterotrophic eukaryotes without cell walls. They typically reproduce sexually, develop from a blastula, and can move during at least part of their life cycle. Heterotrophy is the organizing constraint that shapes most other animal features.
  • Sec 22.2Animal body plans are organized by symmetry (asymmetrical, radial, bilateral), type of body cavity (acoelomate, pseudocoelomate, coelomate), and segmentation. Bilateral symmetry is associated with directional movement and cephalization.
  • Sec 22.3Animal tissues — epithelial, connective, muscle, and nervous — are organized into organs and organ systems of varying complexity, from the simple organs of flatworms to the highly differentiated systems of mammals.
  • Sec 22.4Roughly 97 percent of animal species are invertebrates. Major phyla include sponges, cnidarians, flatworms, roundworms, annelids, mollusks, echinoderms, and arthropods. Each represents a distinct structural solution to shared biological challenges.
  • Sec 22.5Vertebrates share four chordate features. Vertebrate groups — fishes, amphibians, reptiles, birds, and mammals — differ in body covering, temperature regulation (ecto- vs. endothermy), reproductive strategy, and degree of terrestrial adaptation.
  • Sec 22.6Animals feed as herbivores, carnivores, omnivores, filter feeders, detritivores, or parasites. Most have a complete digestive system (mouth to anus); herbivores typically have longer guts than carnivores to digest cellulose-rich plant material.
  • Sec 22.7Animals support and move their bodies using hydrostatic skeletons (fluid pressure), exoskeletons (hard external covering, requires molting), or endoskeletons (internal bone or cartilage, grows continuously). Each type has distinct advantages and constraints.
  • Sec 22.8Animals reproduce sexually (most) or asexually (some). External fertilization involves releasing gametes into water; internal fertilization protects gametes and increases efficiency. Development is oviparous, viviparous, or ovoviviparous. Many animals undergo complete or incomplete metamorphosis.
  • Sec 22.9Animal behavior is innate (genetically programmed) or learned (acquired through experience), often both. Animals communicate through visual, acoustic, chemical, and tactile signals. Eusociality is the most extreme form of social organization; migration allows exploitation of seasonal resources.
  • Sec 22.10Animals play irreplaceable ecological roles as herbivores, predators, pollinators, seed dispersers, and decomposers. Predators can trigger trophic cascades. Pollinators sustain agriculture. Human activities threaten animal diversity and the ecosystem services it provides.

Key Terms

acoelomate
An animal lacking a true body cavity; organs are embedded in solid tissue. Example: flatworms.
amniotic egg
An egg with internal membranes and often a shell, allowing development on land without drying out; found in reptiles, birds, and monotremes.
bilateral symmetry
A body plan in which the left and right sides mirror each other; associated with directional movement and cephalization.
blastula
A hollow ball of cells produced early in animal embryonic development; shared by all animals.
cephalization
The concentration of sensory organs and neural tissue at the anterior end of a bilaterally symmetrical animal.
coelom
A fluid-filled body cavity completely lined with mesodermal tissue; found in coelomate animals including vertebrates.
complete metamorphosis
Insect development through four distinct stages — egg, larva, pupa, adult — where larval and adult forms occupy different ecological roles.
ectotherm
An animal that regulates body temperature primarily through external heat sources; most fishes, amphibians, and reptiles.
endoskeleton
An internal skeleton; found in vertebrates. Grows continuously and can support large body masses.
endotherm
An animal that regulates body temperature internally through metabolic heat production; birds and mammals.
eusociality
An extreme form of social organization with reproductive division of labor, cooperative care of young, and overlapping generations; found in ants, bees, wasps, and termites.
exoskeleton
A hard external skeleton made of chitin in arthropods. Provides protection and muscle attachment but requires molting for growth.
filter feeding
A feeding strategy in which an animal strains small food particles from water; used by baleen whales, clams, and flamingos.
heterotroph
An organism that obtains energy and carbon by consuming other organisms; the defining feeding mode of all animals.
hydrostatic skeleton
A body-support system that uses fluid pressure within a body cavity; found in soft-bodied animals such as earthworms and sea anemones.
innate behavior
A genetically programmed behavior that appears without prior learning; consistent within a species.
invertebrate
An animal without a backbone; approximately 97 percent of all animal species.
metamorphosis
A dramatic change in body form during animal development; complete (4 stages) or incomplete (3 stages) in insects.
molt
To shed an exoskeleton, allowing growth; required periodically by all arthropods.
oviparous
Describes animals that lay eggs in which the embryo develops outside the mother's body.
parthenogenesis
Development of an unfertilized egg into a new individual; a form of asexual reproduction.
radial symmetry
A body plan in which the body can be divided into equal halves by multiple planes through a central axis; found in cnidarians and adult echinoderms.
trophic cascade
An indirect ecological effect in which a change in one species triggers effects through multiple levels of a food web.
vertebrate
An animal with a backbone (vertebral column); a subgroup of the phylum Chordata.
viviparous
Describes animals that give birth to live young nourished through a placenta or uterine secretions.
water vascular system
The fluid-filled canal network of echinoderms, connected to tube feet used for movement, feeding, and gas exchange.

Structure-and-Function Practice

Focus on the relationship between structure and function — not just what a structure is, but what it does and why that design matters.

  1. A shark has a skeleton made entirely of cartilage rather than bone. Cartilage is lighter and more flexible than bone but provides less rigid support. Propose two ways this skeletal material might benefit a large, active aquatic predator. What functions of a skeleton does cartilage still fulfill?
  2. Earthworms use a hydrostatic skeleton. When circular muscles contract, the worm's body narrows and lengthens. When longitudinal muscles contract, it shortens and widens. Explain how these two muscle groups working in sequence allow an earthworm to move forward through soil.
  3. The exoskeleton of an arthropod is made of chitin, which does not stretch or expand. Explain two consequences of this design for arthropod biology — one advantage and one constraint — and describe what arthropods must do to grow.
  4. Baleen whales are the largest animals on Earth, yet they feed on some of the smallest organisms — krill and small fish. How does filter feeding make it energetically possible to sustain such a large body? What physical features enable effective filter feeding in a whale?
  5. Birds have hollow bones with internal struts rather than solid bones. Explain why reducing skeletal mass is particularly important for flying animals, and identify one other adaptation in birds that supports the demands of flight.
  6. Compare the skin of an amphibian and the skin of a reptile. How does each suit the lifestyle and habitat of each group? What does this comparison reveal about the evolutionary challenges of moving from water to land?

Adaptation Questions

For each scenario, identify the adaptation, explain the biological problem it solves, and evaluate what tradeoffs the adaptation might involve.

  1. Arctic foxes have small, rounded ears and short limbs compared to their desert-dwelling relatives, which have very large ears. Propose an explanation for this difference in terms of heat exchange and survival.
  2. A mantis shrimp has specialized club-like appendages capable of striking with the force of a bullet. It uses this to break open the shells of hard-shelled prey. What problem does this solve? What structural features would you expect this appendage to require?

Common Misconceptions

Misconception — "Fish are primitive animals"

Fish are not primitive or simple — they are exquisitely adapted for aquatic life and have diversified over hundreds of millions of years into more than 30,000 species. In biology, "primitive" means earlier-branching on the tree of life, not poorly designed. Many so-called primitive traits are highly functional for the environments in which those animals live.

Misconception — "Cold-blooded means an animal's blood is actually cold"

Ectothermic animals can have warm blood — sometimes warmer than mammals — when basking in the sun. "Cold-blooded" refers to the source of body heat, not the temperature itself. Many desert reptiles maintain body temperatures as high as or higher than mammals during active periods.

Misconception — "Insects are not animals"

Insects are animals — members of the kingdom Animalia and the phylum Arthropoda. The casual use of "animals" to mean only vertebrates (or only mammals) is biologically inaccurate. Insects account for the majority of all animal species on Earth.

Misconception — "Evolution always produces more complex animals over time"

Evolution does not have a built-in direction toward greater complexity. Many lineages have become simpler over time, losing structures that were no longer needed. Parasites frequently lose organs and systems present in their free-living ancestors. Natural selection favors whatever traits improve survival and reproduction in the current environment — complexity is sometimes advantageous, but not always.

Misconception — "Social insects work for the good of the colony"

Social insects work in ways that maximize the spread of their own genes, which they share with their relatives. Worker bees and ants do not sacrifice themselves out of altruism; their behavior is explained by the fact that they share a large proportion of genes with their sisters, making helping raise siblings reproductively equivalent to raising their own offspring. Evolutionary biology explains this through kin selection.

Looking Ahead
Chapter 23: Tissues, Organs, and Body Systems

Chapter 22 introduced animals as organisms that solve common biological challenges — feeding, moving, sensing, reproducing — in diverse ways across invertebrate and vertebrate groups. Chapter 23 shifts focus from animal diversity to the human body as a single, integrated biological system. It begins with the organizational hierarchy of the human body — cells, tissues, organs, organ systems, and the whole organism — and then introduces the central concept that ties all of human biology together: homeostasis. The human body maintains stable internal conditions despite constant changes in the external environment, doing so through feedback loops, particularly negative feedback. Understanding homeostasis in Chapter 23 will prepare you for the detailed study of specific human body systems in Chapters 24, 25, and 26.

Chapter 27 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

A single tree does not exist alone. It draws water through soil shared with fungi. Its leaves shelter insects that feed birds. Its seeds disperse by wind and by animals. Bacteria on its roots convert nitrogen from the air into forms the tree can use. When it dies, decomposers break it down and return its materials to the soil, where the next generation of plants will absorb them.

This is ecology: the study of how living things interact with each other and with the physical environments they inhabit. Ecology works at many levels — from a single organism's relationship with its surroundings, to populations of thousands of individuals, to communities of interacting species, to ecosystems that link biology to chemistry, geology, and climate.

Ecologists ask questions like: Why does a species live here and not there? What happens to a community when a predator is removed? How does a forest recover after a wildfire? What limits the size of a population? These questions matter not only for understanding the natural world but for managing it — for conservation, agriculture, public health, fisheries, climate, and human survival.

Opening Question

When wolves were removed from Yellowstone National Park in the early twentieth century, elk populations rose — but so did something unexpected: streamside vegetation declined, beavers disappeared from many areas, and streams changed course. How could removing a single predator alter rivers? By the end of this chapter, you will understand the ecological mechanisms that connect wolves, elk, willows, beavers, and streams into a single living system.

Section 27.1

What Ecology Studies

Ecology is the scientific study of the relationships between organisms and their environments. The word comes from the Greek oikos, meaning home or household. Ecology, in a sense, is the study of nature's household — how it is organized, how energy and materials move through it, and how the living and nonliving parts interact.

Ecology is not simply the study of nature as something beautiful or wild. It is a rigorous science that uses observation, experiments, models, and data analysis to understand how living systems work at large scales.

Levels of Ecological Study

LevelDefinitionExample Question
OrganismAn individual living thingHow does a cactus conserve water in the desert?
PopulationA group of individuals of the same species in the same areaWhat limits the size of a wolf pack in Yellowstone?
CommunityAll populations of different species interacting in the same areaHow do predators affect species diversity in a tide pool?
EcosystemA community plus its nonliving physical environmentHow does nitrogen move between soil, plants, and animals?
BiosphereThe global sum of all ecosystemsHow do forests affect atmospheric carbon dioxide levels?

Abiotic and Biotic Factors

Every environment has two kinds of components. Abiotic factors are nonliving physical and chemical characteristics — temperature, light, water, soil chemistry, pH, salinity, wind, and mineral nutrients. They set the physical conditions in which life must operate.

Biotic factors are the living components of an environment — all the organisms present and their effects on one another. Predators, competitors, parasites, mutualists, and decomposers are all biotic influences on any organism's survival. Ecology studies how these factors combine to determine where organisms can live, how many can survive, and how communities are organized.

Remember the Distinction

Abiotic = absent of life (physical and chemical). Biotic = biological (living organisms and their effects). A river's temperature is abiotic; the fish in the river are biotic. Both influence each other and must be studied together.

Section 27.2

Organisms and Their Environments

Habitat

An organism's habitat is the physical environment where it lives — the place that provides the conditions, resources, and shelter it needs. A red-tailed hawk's habitat is open grassland and woodland edge. A largemouth bass's habitat is warm, vegetated freshwater. Habitat is not just a location; it is a set of conditions that must be met. When habitat is destroyed or altered, species that depend on it can decline or disappear.

Ecological Niche

A niche is more than a habitat. While habitat describes where an organism lives, the niche describes how it lives — its role in the community, its use of resources, its timing of activity, its interactions with other species, and its tolerance for environmental conditions.

Habitat vs. Niche

Think of the habitat as an organism's address and the niche as its profession. Two species can share an address but have different professions — meaning they can live in the same area while using different resources or being active at different times.

The fundamental niche is the full range of conditions a species could potentially use with no competition. The realized niche is the actual, often smaller, range it uses in the presence of competitors and predators.

The Competitive Exclusion Principle

When two species compete for exactly the same resources in exactly the same way, one will eventually outcompete the other. This is the competitive exclusion principle. The inferior competitor either goes locally extinct or is pushed into a different niche. This principle explains why ecologically similar species tend to differ in at least one important way — the size of prey they hunt, the depth of water they use, or the time of day they are active.

Common Confusion

The competitive exclusion principle does not mean two similar species can never coexist. It means two species occupying exactly the same niche cannot coexist indefinitely. Similar species often coexist by partitioning resources and occupying slightly different realized niches.

Section 27.3

Populations

Population Size and Density

A population is a group of individuals of the same species living in the same geographic area and potentially interacting with one another. Population size is the total number of individuals. Population density is the number of individuals per unit area or volume — for example, 12 deer per square kilometer. Density matters because it affects how individuals interact: at high densities, competition intensifies and disease spreads more easily.

What Drives Population Change?

Population Growth Equation

Change in population = (Births + Immigration) − (Deaths + Emigration)

When births and immigration exceed deaths and emigration, the population grows. When deaths and emigration exceed births and immigration, the population shrinks.

Exponential Growth

Under ideal conditions — unlimited food, space, and no disease or predation — a population can grow exponentially. Each new individual becomes a reproducer, so the population grows by a larger and larger absolute number each time period even though the growth rate stays constant. A population growing exponentially produces a characteristic J-shaped curve on a graph.

Exponential growth is possible in the real world — bacteria in a new nutrient broth, insects arriving in a habitat with no predators. But it cannot continue forever. Eventually, something limits it.

Logistic Growth and Carrying Capacity

Real populations do not grow exponentially indefinitely. As a population increases in size, resources become scarcer, competition intensifies, and disease spreads more easily. These pressures slow growth as the population gets larger.

Logistic growth describes what happens when growth rate slows as the population approaches the limits of its environment. The population initially grows rapidly, then slows, and finally levels off at a maximum called the carrying capacity, often written as K. Carrying capacity is set by the availability of food, water, shelter, and other critical resources. Logistic growth produces a characteristic S-shaped curve.

Worked Example 27.3.1
Reading a population growth curve
  • ScenarioA graph shows a population of rabbits growing from 50 individuals. The curve rises steeply at first, slows around year 4, and levels off near 200 individuals by year 6. What type of growth is this?
  • IdentifyThe curve is S-shaped: rapid early growth that slows and levels off. This is logistic growth.
  • InterpretThe population levels off near 200 because carrying capacity has been reached. Resources — food, space, or shelter — are now limiting further growth.
  • ContrastIf the curve were J-shaped and still rising steeply by year 6 with no sign of leveling off, it would suggest exponential growth and that resources have not yet become limiting.
Section 27.4

Limiting Factors and Carrying Capacity

What Sets the Limit?

A limiting factor is any resource or environmental condition that constrains population growth. Common limiting factors include food and nutrients, water, space and shelter, light (for plants and algae), temperature, disease and parasites, and predation.

TypeDefinitionExamplesEffect on Population
Density-dependentEffect intensifies as population density increasesDisease, competition, predation, waste accumulationRegulates growth; pushes rate down when population is large
Density-independentAffects population regardless of its sizeFrost, drought, wildfire, volcanic eruption, floodCan cause dramatic crashes even in healthy populations

Boom-and-Bust Cycles

Some populations do not stabilize near a carrying capacity. Instead, they grow rapidly, overshoot the carrying capacity, crash when resources are depleted, then grow again. This boom-and-bust pattern is common in small organisms with fast reproduction rates — aphids, lemmings, and some fish populations.

Predator-prey relationships can also produce cycling populations. The relationship between snowshoe hares and lynx in northern Canada is one of the most well-studied examples: hare populations peak roughly every ten years, followed by lynx population peaks. When prey is abundant, predators reproduce more; as predators rise, prey decline; with less prey, predators decline too, allowing prey to recover — and the cycle repeats.

Graphing Tip — J vs. S Curves

Exponential growth = J-shaped curve (keeps rising steeply). Logistic growth = S-shaped curve (levels off near K). On any population graph, look first at whether the curve continues upward or flattens to identify the growth type.

Section 27.5

Communities

Structure and Diversity

A community is all the populations of different species that live and interact in the same area. Communities have structure: some species are far more numerous than others; some play more influential roles. Species richness is simply the number of different species present. Species diversity takes into account both the number of species and their relative abundance. More diverse communities tend to be more stable and more productive — if one species declines, others can fill similar roles.

Keystone Species

Some species have effects on communities far larger than their abundance would suggest. A keystone species is one whose presence strongly influences the structure and diversity of the community. Removing it causes dramatic changes — other species may decline, increase, or disappear.

Ecologist Robert Paine first developed the concept in the 1960s by studying tide pools in Washington State. Removing sea stars (top predators of mussels) caused mussels to take over the rock surface and crowd out most other species — dramatically reducing community diversity. The sea star was a keystone whose predation had kept mussels in check.

Case Example — Wolves in Yellowstone
A trophic cascade
  • BackgroundWolves were absent from Yellowstone National Park for most of the twentieth century. Without a top predator, elk populations grew and grazed streamside willows and aspens intensively. Beaver populations — dependent on willows — declined, reducing ponds and wetlands.
  • ReintroductionIn 1995, wolves were reintroduced. Elk were not only reduced in number — they also changed their behavior, avoiding exposed areas like riverbanks where wolves could easily catch them.
  • CascadeIn some areas, willows and aspens began recovering. Beaver activity increased. Stream banks stabilized as plant roots returned. The predator's effects rippled down through the food web to plants, soil, and water.
  • ConceptThis is a trophic cascade — a chain of effects that ripples through a food web when a top predator is added or removed. Wolves are a keystone species in this system.
Section 27.6

Niches

Resource Partitioning

When similar species live in the same community, they often reduce competition by resource partitioning — using resources in different ways so they are not competing directly for the same thing at the same time. Ecologist Robert MacArthur studied five closely related warbler species in spruce forests and found each fed at different heights and different parts of the tree canopy. By partitioning the resource, similar species can coexist without excluding one another.

StrategyDescriptionExample
Spatial partitioningDifferent species use different physical locationsWarblers feeding at different heights in the same tree
Temporal partitioningDifferent species are active at different timesDesert lizards active at different times of day
Dietary partitioningDifferent species eat different food types or sizesCoral reef fish feeding on different prey

Ecological Equivalents

Sometimes species in different parts of the world occupy similar niches in their respective communities even though they are not closely related. These are called ecological equivalents. Wolves and thylacines (Tasmanian tigers, now extinct) were both large predators of medium-sized prey in their respective regions. Cacti and African euphorbias both evolved thick water-storing tissues for dry environments — not because they are related, but because natural selection acts on the same functional challenges.

Section 27.7

Competition and Predation

Competition

Competition occurs when two or more organisms need the same limited resource. Intraspecific competition occurs between individuals of the same species; interspecific competition occurs between different species. Competition reduces the fitness of both competitors. The worse competitor may eventually be excluded from the area or forced into a narrower niche. Most ecological competition is indirect and invisible — trees competing for light never "fight," but the effect is real.

Predation

Predation is a relationship in which one organism (the predator) kills and eats another (the prey). Predation drives an evolutionary arms race: prey evolve defenses; predators evolve counter-adaptations; each side is under selection pressure to improve, driving evolutionary change in both lineages.

Section 27.8

Symbiosis

Symbiosis is a close, long-term interaction between individuals of two different species. Symbiotic relationships vary in their effects on the participants. The three main categories are mutualism, commensalism, and parasitism.

Mutualism — Both Partners Benefit

In mutualism, both species benefit. Mutualism is common and ecologically important. Pollination is mutualism: flowers provide food to pollinators, and pollinators transfer pollen between flowers. Mycorrhizal relationships are mutualism: fungi extend into soil and provide water and minerals to plant roots; the plant provides the fungus with sugars from photosynthesis. Most plant species — including most crop plants — depend on mycorrhizal fungi. Nitrogen-fixing bacteria in legume root nodules convert atmospheric nitrogen into usable forms while receiving carbohydrates from the plant.

Commensalism — One Benefits, One Unaffected

In commensalism, one species benefits and the other is neither significantly helped nor harmed. Barnacles attaching to whale skin are commonly cited — barnacles gain transportation and access to new feeding areas; the whale appears unaffected. Epiphytic plants grow on the surface of trees, benefiting from elevated light without harming their hosts. True commensalism may be rarer than it appears, since most close relationships probably affect both partners at least slightly.

Parasitism — One Benefits at Another's Expense

In parasitism, the parasite benefits at the expense of the host. Unlike predation, the parasite typically does not immediately kill its host — it needs the host alive to continue feeding or reproducing. Parasites are extraordinarily diverse: viruses, bacteria, fungi, protists, roundworms, tapeworms, ticks, fleas, and parasitic plants. Nearly every living species is host to multiple parasites. Parasites can dramatically affect host populations, cause disease, reduce reproduction, and drive population cycles.

Common Misconception — Symbiosis ≠ Mutualism

Symbiosis is a general term for any close, long-term interaction between two species — including parasitism, which harms one partner. Not all symbiosis is beneficial to both parties. Always specify which type of symbiosis you mean.

Section 27.9

Ecosystems

From Community to Ecosystem

When we add the nonliving environment to a community — the soil, water, air, sunlight, temperature, and mineral nutrients — we get an ecosystem: a biological system consisting of all the organisms in a community together with their abiotic environment and the flows of energy and materials between them. Ecosystems can be any size: a small pond, a vast forest, a rotting log, or the entire biosphere.

Energy Flows Through Ecosystems

All life requires energy. In most ecosystems, energy enters as sunlight and is captured by photosynthesis. Producers (autotrophs) — plants, algae, and photosynthetic bacteria — convert light energy into chemical energy stored in organic molecules. Primary consumers eat producers. Secondary consumers eat primary consumers. Tertiary consumers eat secondary consumers. Decomposers — fungi and bacteria — break down dead organic matter and return nutrients to the environment.

Energy does not cycle in ecosystems — it flows in one direction, entering as light and leaving as heat. At each step in the food chain, most energy is lost as heat from metabolism. Only a fraction — typically around 10% — is converted into new biomass that the next level can consume. This inefficiency is why food chains rarely have more than four or five steps.

Energy Flow vs. Nutrient Cycling

Energy flows in one direction through ecosystems: in as sunlight, out as heat. It does not cycle. This is why ecosystems require a continuous input of solar energy.

Nutrients (carbon, nitrogen, water, phosphorus) cycle. They move between organisms and the physical environment, used and reused by many generations of living things. Decomposers are essential to cycling — they release nutrients from dead organisms back into forms that producers can use again.

Ecosystem Services

Ecosystems provide enormous benefits to human civilization. These are called ecosystem services. Clean air is maintained by plant photosynthesis. Water is filtered through soil and wetlands. Nutrients that support agriculture are cycled by soil bacteria and decomposers. A large fraction of the human food supply depends on insect pollination. Forests absorb carbon dioxide and influence climate. Wild species provide food, building materials, and the compounds from which many drugs are derived. The value of ecosystem services is enormous — protecting ecosystems is not only a matter of conservation ethics but of practical human survival.

Section 27.10

Ecological Succession

Communities Change Over Time

Communities are not static. They change over time in a process called ecological succession — the gradual change in the species composition of a community after a disturbance or following the development of a new, bare habitat. Succession occurs because organisms modify their environment. The plants that colonize a bare area change the soil, provide shade, and create organic matter. These changes make conditions favorable for different species, which then replace the first colonizers. The process continues until a relatively stable community develops.

Primary Succession

Primary succession begins in a place that has no soil and no remnant community — a bare rock surface, a new volcanic island, or an area scraped clean by a glacier. Because there is no soil, primary succession is slow — it may take hundreds or thousands of years.

The first organisms to colonize are pioneer species — typically lichens and mosses. Lichens are mutualistic associations of fungi and photosynthetic algae or bacteria. They tolerate extreme conditions and produce acids that slowly break down rock into mineral particles. As lichens and mosses live and die, organic matter accumulates and thin soil begins to form. Small plants establish, then shrubs, then fast-growing trees. Over time, a relatively stable climax community develops — its composition determined by climate, soil type, and regional factors.

Secondary Succession

Secondary succession begins after a disturbance removes or destroys the existing community but leaves the soil intact. Abandoned agricultural fields, burned forests, logged land, and areas after floods all undergo secondary succession. Because soil already exists, secondary succession is much faster than primary succession. Seeds, spores, and root fragments may already be present in the soil.

An abandoned farm field in the eastern United States follows a fairly predictable sequence: annual weeds first, then perennial grasses and herbs, then shrubs, then fast-growing trees like aspens and pines, then slower-growing shade-tolerant trees that eventually overtop and replace the early colonizers.

FeaturePrimary SuccessionSecondary Succession
Starting conditionsNo soil; no remnant community (bare rock, new volcanic island)Soil present; community removed by disturbance
SpeedVery slow — centuries to millenniaFaster — decades to centuries
First colonizersPioneer species (lichens, mosses)Fast-growing weedy plants; seeds from soil or nearby

Is the Climax Community Really Stable?

Ecologists once thought succession always proceeded toward a fixed, stable climax community that would remain unchanged indefinitely. The modern view is more nuanced. Many communities are maintained in an intermediate successional state by regular disturbances — fire, windstorms, flooding, herbivory. Prairies are maintained by periodic fires; without fire, they succeed to woodland. Lodgepole pine forests are adapted to fire — their cones open and release seeds only after intense heat.

The intermediate disturbance hypothesis suggests that communities at intermediate levels of disturbance have the highest species diversity. Too little disturbance allows competitive dominants to exclude other species. Too much leaves only fast-reproducing weedy species. Intermediate disturbance maintains a mix of early and late successional species.

Real-World Application

Forest managers, conservation biologists, and rangeland managers use successional principles in practice. Prescribed burns in prairies and fire-adapted forests mimic natural disturbance, maintaining habitat for species that depend on early successional conditions while preventing the accumulation of fuel that could cause catastrophic wildfires.

Key Terms

abiotic factor
A nonliving physical or chemical component of an environment, such as temperature, water, light, or soil chemistry.
biotic factor
A living component of an environment, including all organisms and their effects on one another.
ecology
The scientific study of the relationships between organisms and their environments at multiple levels of organization.
habitat
The physical environment where an organism lives and finds the conditions it needs to survive and reproduce.
niche
An organism's functional role in its community, including its use of resources, timing of activity, and relationships with other species.
fundamental niche
The full range of environmental conditions and resources a species could potentially use in the absence of competition or predation.
realized niche
The actual, often smaller, range of conditions and resources a species uses in the presence of competitors and predators.
competitive exclusion
The principle that two species competing for exactly the same resources cannot coexist indefinitely in the same place — one will displace the other.
population
A group of individuals of the same species living in the same area at the same time and potentially interacting.
population density
The number of individuals per unit area or volume in a population.
exponential growth
Population growth in which the rate stays constant but the number added each period increases, producing a J-shaped curve.
logistic growth
Population growth in which the rate slows as population size approaches carrying capacity, producing an S-shaped curve.
carrying capacity (K)
The maximum population size that an environment can sustainably support given available resources. Not a fixed number — it changes as resources change.
limiting factor
Any resource or environmental condition that constrains population growth, such as food, water, space, light, or temperature.
density-dependent factor
A limiting factor whose effect intensifies as population density increases, such as disease, competition, or predation.
density-independent factor
A limiting factor that affects population size regardless of density, such as a drought, wildfire, or severe freeze.
boom-and-bust cycle
A pattern of rapid population growth followed by a sharp decline, often caused by overuse of resources or predator-prey dynamics.
community
All the populations of different species that live and interact in the same area.
species richness
The total number of different species present in a community.
species diversity
A measure of community composition that accounts for both the number of species and their relative abundance.
keystone species
A species whose removal causes disproportionately large changes in community structure and diversity relative to its abundance.
trophic cascade
A chain of effects that ripples through a food web when a top predator is added or removed, altering multiple lower levels of the community.
resource partitioning
The division of resources among species, reducing direct competition and allowing ecologically similar species to coexist.
ecological equivalents
Species in different parts of the world that occupy similar niches despite not being closely related.
competition
An interaction between organisms that need the same limited resource, reducing the fitness of each competitor.
predation
An interaction in which a predator kills and eats a prey organism, directly reducing the prey population.
symbiosis
Any close, long-term interaction between individuals of two different species, including mutualism, commensalism, and parasitism.
mutualism
A symbiotic relationship in which both species benefit from the interaction.
commensalism
A symbiotic relationship in which one species benefits and the other is neither significantly helped nor harmed.
parasitism
A symbiotic relationship in which one species (the parasite) benefits at the expense of the other (the host).
ecosystem
A community of organisms together with their nonliving physical environment and the flows of energy and materials between them.
producer
An organism that captures energy from sunlight or chemicals to produce organic molecules; also called an autotroph. The base of most food chains.
decomposer
An organism (typically a fungus or bacterium) that breaks down dead organic matter and returns nutrients to the environment.
ecosystem services
The benefits that healthy ecosystems provide to human civilization, including clean air, water filtration, pollination, climate regulation, and food production.
ecological succession
The gradual, directional change in the species composition of a community after a disturbance or in a newly formed habitat.
primary succession
Ecological succession that begins in an area with no soil and no remnant community, such as bare rock after a glacial retreat.
secondary succession
Ecological succession that begins after a disturbance removes the community but leaves the soil intact, such as an abandoned field.
pioneer species
The first organisms to colonize a disturbed or barren area, beginning the process of ecological succession. Often lichens or mosses in primary succession.
climax community
A relatively stable community that develops at the end of succession under a given set of environmental conditions.
intermediate disturbance hypothesis
The hypothesis that communities at intermediate levels of disturbance maintain the highest species diversity — neither too little nor too much disturbance.

Population Graph Practice

Use the following exercises to practice interpreting and constructing population growth curves.

  1. A population of bacteria is placed in a nutrient broth. The population doubles every 30 minutes. After 0 minutes there are 100 bacteria. Calculate the population size at 30, 60, 90, 120, and 150 minutes. Plot your results. What shape is this curve, and what type of growth does it represent?

Ecosystem Case Study: Wolves in Yellowstone

When wolves were eliminated from Yellowstone National Park by the mid-twentieth century, elk populations grew without their primary predator. Elk grazed intensively on streamside willows and aspens, which declined. Beaver populations — which depend on willows for food and dam building — also declined, reducing the number of ponds and wetlands in the park.

When wolves were reintroduced in 1995, their effects were complex. Elk were not only reduced in number; they also changed their behavior, avoiding areas where they were vulnerable to wolf predation. In some areas, willows and aspens began recovering. Beaver activity increased in certain streams. Stream banks stabilized as plant roots returned. Ecologists call this a trophic cascade — the predator's effects rippled down through the food web, ultimately affecting plants, soil, and water.

Common Misconceptions

Misconception

"Predators are harmful to ecosystems."

Predators regulate prey populations, prevent overgrazing, and maintain species diversity. Their removal often leads to ecosystem disruption rather than improvement. Predators play an essential role in healthy community structure — as the Yellowstone wolf example illustrates.

Misconception

"Carrying capacity is a fixed number."

Carrying capacity is not permanent. It changes as resources change. A drought reduces carrying capacity; restoration of vegetation increases it. Carrying capacity is a dynamic property of the environment, not a biological constant of the species.

Misconception

"Competition always involves fighting."

Most ecological competition is indirect — organisms competing for the same resource without ever interacting directly. Trees competing for light never fight. The competition is real, but invisible and gradual. Direct contest competition does occur but is not required for competitive effects.

Misconception

"Succession always produces a stable final state."

Many communities are maintained by recurring disturbances that prevent the community from ever reaching a "final" state. Fire-maintained prairies, flood-maintained riparian forests, and storm-maintained coastal habitats are all examples where disturbance is part of the ecosystem's normal functioning.

Misconception

"Symbiosis means mutualism."

Symbiosis is a general term for any close, long-term interaction between two species — including parasitism, which harms one partner. Mutualism, commensalism, and parasitism are all forms of symbiosis. Always specify which type you mean.

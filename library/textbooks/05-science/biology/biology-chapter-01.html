Chapter 1 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

This chapter opens the study of biology by explaining what the science of life actually is. Biology is not a list of names to memorize or a catalog of creatures to identify. It is the study of how living systems work — how they are organized, how they use energy, how they store and transmit information, how they respond to their environments, how they change over generations, and how they interact across every scale from a molecule inside a cell to a forest spanning a continent.

By the end of this chapter, you should understand why biology is unified despite the staggering diversity of life, how living things are organized into levels that connect atoms to ecosystems, what questions biologists ask and why those questions matter, and how biology connects to health, agriculture, medicine, conservation, climate, food, and daily life. This chapter lays the conceptual foundation that every later chapter builds upon.

Opening Question

A single oak tree is home to hundreds of insect species, dozens of bird species, and thousands of fungi and bacteria that live in and on its bark, roots, and leaves. The tree captures sunlight and converts it to sugar. Its roots draw water from the soil. Its acorns carry genetic instructions to build another oak. A disease carried by a beetle can kill a tree that has stood for two hundred years. Where does biology begin and end in this picture? The answer is: biology is all of it. By the end of this course, you will be able to explain each of those processes from the molecular level to the ecosystem level.

Section 1.1

What Biology Studies

Biology is the scientific study of life and living systems. That definition sounds simple, but its scope is enormous. Life includes single bacteria smaller than a grain of dust, blue whales stretching longer than a school bus, and old-growth redwood trees taller than a twenty-story building. It includes the chemical reactions happening inside a cell at this moment, the migration of birds across continents, and the slow drift of species across millions of years of evolution.

What unifies all of this is that biologists ask the same fundamental kinds of questions regardless of which organism or system they study: How is this living thing organized? How does it get and use energy? How does it grow and reproduce? How does it sense and respond to its environment? How does it interact with other living things and with the physical world? How did it come to exist in its current form?

Major Area of BiologyWhat It StudiesExample Questions
Cell BiologyThe structure and function of cells, the basic units of lifeHow does a cell membrane control what enters and exits? How does a cell divide?
GeneticsHeredity, DNA, genes, and the transmission of traitsHow are traits inherited from parents? How does a gene direct the building of a protein?
EvolutionHow populations of organisms change over generationsWhy do organisms in different environments have different adaptations?
EcologyHow organisms interact with each other and with their environmentsWhat limits the size of a deer population in a forest? How does energy move through a food web?
PhysiologyHow the body systems of organisms functionHow do the lungs deliver oxygen to the blood? How does the nervous system coordinate movement?
Biology Is Not Memorization

The goal of this course is not to learn the names of every organelle, every organism, or every hormone. The goal is to understand how living systems work and why they work that way. Names and terms are tools for thinking, not the destination. Every definition you learn should help you reason about something, not just label it.

Section 1.2

Life as a System

A system is a set of parts that interact with one another to produce behaviors or properties that none of the individual parts could produce alone. Living things are systems — extraordinarily complex ones. A cell is not just a collection of molecules. The molecules interact, and those interactions allow the cell to grow, respond to signals, divide, and survive in ways that no single molecule can do by itself. An ecosystem is not just a list of species. The species interact, and those interactions produce nutrient cycles, food webs, and population dynamics that none of the individual organisms controls or directs.

This idea — that the whole is more than the sum of its parts — is called emergence. Biological systems have emergent properties at every level of organization. Understanding biology means learning to see systems, not just parts.

Key Idea — Emergence

Emergent properties are characteristics that appear at a given level of organization but are not present in the individual components below that level. A single water molecule is not wet. A single neuron does not think. A single heart muscle cell does not pump blood. These properties emerge from the organized interactions of many components working together.

Example 1.2.1
Emergence in a living system
  • QuestionWhy can a flock of starlings perform complex, coordinated aerial movements — called a murmuration — when no single bird is directing the group?
  • ComponentsIndividual starlings, each following simple local rules: stay close to neighbors, match their direction, avoid collision.
  • InteractionEach bird responds to the birds immediately around it. No central control exists. No bird has a plan for the whole flock's shape.
  • EmergenceThe coordinated wave patterns of the flock emerge from thousands of local interactions. The behavior is a property of the system, not of any individual bird.
Takeaway: Complex biological behaviors often emerge from simpler rules operating at a lower level. This is a pattern you will see again and again in biology.
Practice 1.2Systems Thinking

  2. The human immune system involves many cell types, proteins, and organs. Why is it useful to think of immunity as a system rather than as a list of separate defenses?
  3. Describe an emergent property you have observed in a living thing or in nature. What parts interact to produce it?
Section 1.3

Organisms, Cells, and Ecosystems

Biologists organize life into levels. These levels do not exist as walls that biology works within — living systems connect across them constantly. But the levels help us see how organization works and how complexity builds from simpler foundations. Every level has its own questions, its own methods, and its own emergent properties.

TissueA group of similar cells that work together to perform a functionMuscle tissue; leaf epidermis; nervous tissue

The Cell Rule

The cell is the fundamental unit of biology. Every living thing is either a single cell or made of cells. Viruses, which are not cells, are not considered alive by most biologists — a point explored in Chapter 2. Understanding cells is the foundation of understanding everything else in biology.

Section 1.4

Structure and Function

One of the most powerful ideas in all of biology is that structure supports function. What something does depends on what it is made of and how it is arranged. This principle operates at every level — from the shape of a protein molecule to the architecture of a leaf to the body plan of a fish. Whenever you encounter a biological structure you have never seen before, asking "What does its shape allow it to do?" will often take you a long way toward understanding it.

StructureKey Features of Its FormFunction Enabled
Red blood cellBiconcave disc shape; flexible; no nucleus; packed with hemoglobinMaximizes surface area for gas exchange; fits through narrow capillaries; carries oxygen efficiently
LeafFlat and broad; thin; internal air spaces; tiny pores on undersideMaximizes light capture; allows gas exchange for photosynthesis; minimizes water loss
Neuron (nerve cell)Long, branching extensions; electrically excitable membraneReceives signals from many sources; transmits electrical signals over long distances
Root hair cellTiny hair-like projection; thin wall; no chloroplastsGreatly increases surface area for water and mineral absorption from soil
Enzyme proteinSpecific three-dimensional shape with active siteBinds only its particular target molecule; speeds a specific chemical reaction
The Central Theme

Throughout this textbook — from cell organelles to human organ systems to ecosystems — the principle of structure and function connects every topic. Ask it always: How does the shape, arrangement, or composition of this biological structure make its function possible?

Example 1.4.1
Applying structure-and-function thinking
  • StructureThe human small intestine is about 6 meters long and its inner surface is covered in millions of tiny finger-like projections called villi, each of which is covered in even smaller projections called microvilli.
  • QuestionWhy does the small intestine have this elaborate folded and projected structure instead of being a simple smooth tube?
  • ReasoningThe surface area of the small intestine determines how much digested food can be absorbed per unit of time. A smooth tube 6 meters long would have limited surface area. The villi and microvilli increase the total surface area dramatically — estimates suggest by a factor of 600 or more — maximizing the rate of nutrient absorption.
  • PrincipleThe folded, projected structure directly enables the function of efficient absorption. Structure supports function.
Takeaway: Biological structures are not random. Their form is connected to their role. When you see an unusual shape or arrangement in biology, ask what function it serves.
Practice 1.4Structure and Function

  2. A bird's bones are hollow and filled with air pockets rather than solid throughout. Apply the structure-and-function principle to explain why this matters for a bird's survival.
  3. Cactus stems are thick, fleshy, and green, while cactus leaves have been reduced to spines. What functions do these structural features serve? Think about the environment in which cacti live.
Section 1.5

Energy, Matter, and Information in Life

All living systems require three things to function: energy to drive processes, matter as raw material for building and maintaining structure, and information to direct the processes and pass traits from one generation to the next. These three can be thought of as the three currencies of life — every biological process involves all three in some way.

Currency of LifeWhat It IsWhere It Comes FromHow It Is Used
EnergyThe capacity to do work or drive changeUltimately from sunlight (for most life) or from chemical bonds in foodDrives chemical reactions, movement, growth, reproduction, signaling, and all other processes
MatterThe physical substance of living things: atoms and moleculesFrom food, water, air, and soil; matter is recycled through ecosystemsBuilding blocks for cells, tissues, organs, and all biological molecules
InformationInstructions encoded in DNA; the recipe for building and operating a living organismInherited from parent organisms; copied during cell divisionDirects protein synthesis; controls development; enables heredity across generations
Energy Flows; Matter Cycles

There is an important difference between how energy and matter behave in living systems. Energy flows through ecosystems in one direction — from sunlight into organisms and eventually out as heat. It is not recycled. Matter, by contrast, cycles continuously. The carbon atoms in your body have been part of countless other organisms before you, and they will be part of others after you. This distinction between the flow of energy and the cycling of matter runs through all of ecology.

Practice 1.5Energy, Matter, and Information
  1. In your own words, explain why all three of the following are essential to life: energy, matter, and information. Give a specific biological example for each.
  2. When a plant makes sugar from carbon dioxide and sunlight, which of the three currencies is being transformed? Which is being incorporated as raw material?
  3. When a cell divides, which currency must be copied accurately and passed on to both daughter cells? What happens to a cell if that copying fails?
  4. A fire also uses energy and consumes matter. Why is a fire not alive, even though it shares some of these properties with living systems?
Section 1.6

Biology Across Scale

One of the most striking features of biology is the range of scales it spans. A DNA base pair is about 0.34 nanometers — smaller than a wavelength of visible light. The biosphere — the total zone on Earth where life exists — stretches from miles below the ocean surface to miles above it. Between these extremes, biology operates at every scale, and events at one scale influence events at all others.

A single mutation in a DNA sequence can change the shape of a protein, alter the function of a cell, change the physiology of an organism, and — if it affects reproduction — shift the makeup of an entire population over generations. Direction can also run the other way: a change in climate affects ecosystems, which affects populations, which places selective pressure on individual organisms, which shapes the survival of cells carrying particular genes.

Cell1–100 micrometersThe fundamental unit of life; metabolism; cell division
TissueMillimetersCoordinated cell function; support, transport, communication
OrganCentimetersIntegrated physiological functions: pumping, filtering, sensing

EcosystemKilometers to continentalEnergy flow, nutrient cycling, species interactions

Common Misconception — Scale

Biology is not "just about organisms you can see." The vast majority of living things on Earth are microorganisms — bacteria, archaea, and protists — that are invisible to the naked eye. And the molecules inside cells, invisible even to most microscopes, direct every function of every organism. Biology at the smallest scales is not less important than biology at the visible scale. It is often more fundamental.

  3. A pesticide kills insects in a field. Describe effects that might occur at the organism level, the population level, and the ecosystem level as a result of this single human action.

Section 1.7

Biology in Health, Agriculture, and Technology

Biology is not only an academic discipline. It is one of the most practically consequential sciences in human history. The food you eat, the medicines you take, the vaccines that protect you from disease, the crops that feed billions of people, and the technologies emerging from genomics and genetic engineering — all of these trace their foundations directly to biological knowledge.

FieldWhat Biology ContributesExamples
MedicineUnderstanding disease at the cellular and molecular level; developing treatments and vaccines; understanding the immune systemAntibiotics targeting bacterial cell walls; vaccines training the immune system; cancer therapies targeting cell division signals
Public HealthUnderstanding how diseases spread through populations; designing interventions; monitoring pathogen evolutionContact tracing; vaccine campaigns; tracking antibiotic-resistant bacteria
AgricultureImproving crop yields, disease resistance, and nutritional content; understanding plant physiology and geneticsDisease-resistant wheat varieties; nitrogen-fixing crops; precision irrigation based on plant water-use science
BiotechnologyUsing biological systems or molecules as tools for human purposesInsulin produced by engineered bacteria; genetic testing; CRISPR-based therapies
ForensicsUsing DNA and biological evidence to identify individuals or reconstruct eventsDNA fingerprinting in criminal cases; identifying human remains
NutritionUnderstanding how food molecules are digested, absorbed, and used by the bodyDietary guidelines based on metabolic research; understanding how fiber affects gut bacteria
Biology Changes Fast

More biological knowledge has been generated in the last thirty years than in all previous human history combined. Technologies like DNA sequencing, gene editing, and high-resolution microscopy have transformed what is possible. As you study biology, pay attention to how recent many of the discoveries are. Some of the most important ideas in this textbook were unknown when your parents were in school.

Section 1.8

Biology and the Environment

Living things and their environments are inseparable. No organism exists apart from its physical and biological surroundings. Biology and environmental science are deeply intertwined because life shapes the environment and the environment shapes life — simultaneously, continuously, and at every scale.

The oxygen in the atmosphere was produced by photosynthetic organisms over billions of years. The fertility of soil depends on the work of bacteria, fungi, earthworms, and plant roots. The stability of coastlines depends partly on mangrove roots and seagrass beds. And the disruption of ecosystems — through habitat loss, invasive species, pollution, or climate change — threatens the biological systems that humans depend on.

Environmental IssueBiological DimensionWhy Biology Matters Here
Climate changeShifting species ranges; coral bleaching; altered timing of migration and flowering; ecosystem disruptionPredicting and responding to biological impacts requires understanding physiology, ecology, and evolution
Habitat lossPopulation fragmentation; reduced genetic diversity; species extinctionConservation requires understanding minimum viable population sizes, habitat requirements, and species interactions
Invasive speciesOutcompeting native species; disrupting food webs; spreading diseaseUnderstanding competition, predation, and ecosystem dynamics informs management decisions
Water qualityAlgal blooms from nutrient overload; declining fish populations; threats to drinking waterBiological processes drive water quality; biology informs restoration and monitoring
Biodiversity lossReduction in genetic variation; collapse of ecosystem services; unknown species extinctionBiodiversity supports ecosystem stability, medicine, and food security — protecting it requires biological knowledge
Biology as Stewardship

Understanding biology is not only a matter of scientific curiosity. It is increasingly a civic responsibility. Decisions about land use, agriculture, medicine, energy, and climate will shape the living world for centuries. People who understand how biological systems work are better equipped to evaluate evidence, participate in public decisions, and contribute to solutions. Biology education is, in this sense, a form of preparation for citizenship in the biological world.

  2. Some people argue that protecting biodiversity is not economically practical. How would a biologist respond to this argument? What biological knowledge supports the value of biodiversity?
  3. In what ways does human agriculture depend on biological systems? Consider soil, pollinators, water cycles, and pest control in your answer.
Section 1.9

How to Study Biology Successfully

Biology is a demanding subject not primarily because it requires memorizing large numbers of facts, but because it requires integrating knowledge from many levels simultaneously. A question about why a patient's cells cannot absorb glucose might require you to think about the structure of a cell membrane, the function of a receptor protein, the role of a hormone, the genetics of a disease, and the physiology of digestion — all at once. The study habits that work for biology are habits that build connections, not just lists.

Six Habits That Work in Biology

1. Read for understanding, not recognition. After reading a section, close the book and try to explain the main idea in your own words. If you cannot, you recognized words but did not learn the concept.

2. Use diagrams actively. When you see a diagram, try to redraw it from memory. Label it. Explain what each part does. Diagrams in biology carry as much information as text.

3. Connect ideas across levels. Ask how the concept you just learned connects to something at a smaller scale and something at a larger scale. Biology is always vertical — the levels are connected.

4. Apply structure-and-function thinking. For every new biological structure, ask: what does its shape or composition allow it to do? This question is almost always productive.

5. Use evidence, not just authority. Ask why biologists believe what they believe. What evidence supports the claim? This is the scientific approach, and it helps you remember and evaluate knowledge rather than just absorbing it.

6. Review before the next class, not just before the test. Biology builds on itself. The cell division in Chapter 11 makes much more sense if you understood the DNA structure in Chapter 12. Reviewing regularly prevents concepts from becoming isolated.

Common Study Error

The most common mistake students make in biology is confusing recognition with understanding. Reading a term and feeling like you know it is not the same as being able to explain what it means, why it matters, and how it connects to other ideas. Before any assessment, test yourself actively: cover your notes, and explain the concept aloud or in writing without looking. If you cannot, keep studying.

On Biology Vocabulary

Biology has an enormous vocabulary, much of it derived from Greek and Latin roots. Learning these roots pays dividends throughout the course. Phago- means to eat; cyto- refers to cells; photo- means light; -lysis means breakdown. When you encounter an unfamiliar term, look for root words you recognize. A vocabulary guide appears in Appendix I of this textbook, and a root word reference is available as a companion resource.

Chapter 1 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 1.1Biology is the scientific study of life and living systems. It spans areas including cell biology, genetics, evolution, ecology, and physiology. Biologists ask how living things are organized, how they use energy, how they reproduce, and how they interact — not how to name them.
  • Sec 1.2Living things are systems: sets of interacting parts that produce emergent properties not found in the parts alone. Understanding biology requires thinking about systems, interactions, and wholes — not just individual components in isolation.
  • Sec 1.3Life is organized into levels: atoms, molecules, macromolecules, organelles, cells, tissues, organs, organ systems, organisms, populations, communities, ecosystems, and the biosphere. The cell is the fundamental unit of all living things.
  • Sec 1.4Structure supports function at every level of biological organization. The form of a biological structure — its shape, composition, and arrangement — directly enables what it does. This principle applies from protein molecules to organism body plans.
  • Sec 1.5All living systems require energy (to drive processes), matter (as raw material for structure), and information (stored in DNA to direct processes and enable heredity). Energy flows through ecosystems; matter cycles. Both depend on biological information to be used correctly.
  • Sec 1.6Biology spans scales from nanometers (DNA) to planetary (biosphere). Events at one scale affect all others. Understanding biology requires moving fluidly between molecular, cellular, organismal, and ecological levels of analysis.
  • Sec 1.7Biology has transformed medicine, public health, agriculture, biotechnology, nutrition, and forensics. Biological knowledge underlies vaccines, antibiotics, crop improvement, genetic medicine, and environmental management.
  • Sec 1.8Living things and their environments are inseparable. Biology illuminates environmental problems including climate change, habitat loss, invasive species, water quality, and biodiversity loss. Understanding biology is a foundation for responsible environmental stewardship.
  • Sec 1.9Successful biology study requires active engagement: explaining concepts in your own words, redrawing and labeling diagrams, connecting ideas across levels, applying structure-and-function thinking, evaluating evidence, and reviewing consistently rather than only before tests.

Key Terms Review

biology
The scientific study of life and living systems, including how they are organized, how they function, how they reproduce, and how they interact with their environments.
system
A set of parts that interact with one another to produce behaviors or properties that none of the individual parts could produce alone.
emergent property
A characteristic that appears at a given level of organization but is not present in the individual components below that level. Life itself is an emergent property of organized chemistry.
cell
The basic structural and functional unit of all living things. Every organism is either a single cell or built from cells.
organism
An individual living thing capable of carrying out all the functions of life, including metabolism, homeostasis, response to stimuli, growth, and reproduction.
population
All individuals of the same species living in the same area at the same time and capable of interbreeding.
community
All the populations of different species that live in the same area and interact with one another.
ecosystem
A community of organisms together with the nonliving physical environment they inhabit and interact with.
biosphere
All living organisms on Earth together with the environments they inhabit; the global zone of life on our planet.
structure and function
The central biological principle that the physical form of a biological structure — its shape, composition, and arrangement — directly enables and supports what it does.
energy
The capacity to do work or drive change. All living processes require a continuous input of energy, ultimately derived from sunlight or chemical bonds.
matter
Physical substance made of atoms; the raw material from which organisms build themselves. Matter cycles through ecosystems and is neither created nor destroyed.
information
In biology, instructions encoded in DNA that direct the building and operation of living organisms and are passed from generation to generation through reproduction.
DNA
Deoxyribonucleic acid; the molecule that carries hereditary information in all cellular life. DNA stores instructions for building proteins and is copied when cells divide.
evolution
Change in the heritable characteristics of populations over successive generations; the process by which species arise, change, and become adapted to their environments over time.
ecology
The branch of biology that studies how organisms interact with one another and with their physical environments at the levels of populations, communities, ecosystems, and the biosphere.
biodiversity
The variety of life on Earth — including the diversity of genes, species, and ecosystems — which supports ecosystem stability, resilience, and many human needs.
biotechnology
The application of biological systems and organisms to develop products and technologies for medicine, agriculture, industry, and other human purposes.

Structure-and-Function Practice

For each structure described, explain what function its form enables. Then explain what would happen to function if the structure were altered as described.

  1. Lung air sacs (alveoli): The lungs contain approximately 480 million microscopic air sacs, each surrounded by a dense network of tiny blood vessels. The walls of each sac are extremely thin — just one cell thick. What function does this structure serve? What would happen if the walls thickened, as occurs in some lung diseases?
  2. Shark skin: A shark's skin is covered in tiny tooth-like scales called denticles, all aligned to point toward the tail. Rather than increasing drag, this alignment actually reduces it. How does this structure serve the shark's survival?
  3. Tendons: Tendons connect muscle to bone. They are made of tightly packed parallel protein fibers (collagen) and are nearly inelastic — they do not stretch. Why would elasticity be a problem in this structure? What function does rigidity serve?
  4. Liver cells: Liver cells are packed with smooth membrane structures (smooth endoplasmic reticulum) and are loaded with enzymes that break down drugs, alcohol, and toxins. What does this structure suggest about the liver's function? What would happen if liver cells lacked this equipment?

Biology in Everyday Life

Each scenario involves biology you experience directly. Explain the biological connection.

  1. You eat a meal and feel tired shortly afterward, then more energetic an hour later. What biological processes explain these changes? What energy currency is involved?
  2. A friend gets a strep throat infection and is prescribed an antibiotic. Why does the antibiotic not harm your friend's own cells? What biological knowledge makes targeted antibiotics possible?
  3. You notice that plants in a shaded corner of a room grow tall and spindly, leaning toward the window, while plants near the window are shorter and fuller. What biological principle explains this response? What does it tell you about the relationship between structure and environment?
  4. After a wildfire destroys a forest, biologists predict that the forest will regrow — slowly at first with grasses and shrubs, then with pioneer tree species, and eventually with mature forest species. What levels of biological organization are involved in this recovery process?
  5. Two patients have the same disease. One recovers quickly; one struggles. The difference may be genetic, environmental, or both. What does this suggest about the relationship between information, environment, and biological outcome?

Common Misconceptions

Each statement contains a misconception or error. Identify what is wrong and explain the correct understanding.

Misconception 1
"Biology is mostly about memorizing the names of animals, plants, and body parts."

What is wrong with this view of biology? What is biology actually studying, and why does that matter?

Misconception 2
"Viruses are the smallest living things."

What is wrong with calling viruses "living things"? What features do viruses lack that living cells have? (Hint: think about the definition of life from Sections 1.2 and 1.3, and see Chapter 2 for more detail.)

Misconception 3
"Energy is recycled in ecosystems, just like matter is."

What is the difference between how energy and matter behave in ecosystems? Which one flows in one direction, and which one cycles?

Misconception 4
"Structure and function are separate things. A structure is just the physical part; function is what you do with it."

Why is this separation misleading in biology? Give an example that shows how structure and function are inseparable.

Misconception 5
"Biology only matters to people who want to be doctors or veterinarians."

List at least four fields outside medicine and veterinary science where biological knowledge is essential. Explain why biological literacy matters for citizens in general, not only for professionals.

Review Questions

Part AShort Answer
  1. Define biology in one sentence. Then explain what that definition means using two examples — one at the cellular scale and one at the ecosystem scale.
  2. What is an emergent property? Give a biological example and a non-biological example of emergence.
  3. A biologist says that "a heart cannot be understood without understanding the circulatory system, and the circulatory system cannot be understood without understanding the organism." What view of biological organization does this reflect?
  4. Name and describe three levels of biological organization that exist between the cell and the ecosystem.
  5. Explain the difference between energy flowing through an ecosystem and matter cycling through an ecosystem. Give a concrete example of each.
Part BReasoning and Analysis
  1. A student claims that a bird's wing and a bat's wing are "the same thing" because they are both wings used for flying. A biologist points out that they evolved independently from different ancestral structures. Why would a biologist care about this distinction? What does it reveal about the difference between function and evolutionary history?
  2. Biologists discovered that a single gene mutation causes cells to grow and divide uncontrollably. Describe the chain of events from the molecular level to the organism level that could follow from this mutation. What human condition does this describe?
  3. You are shown a cross-section of an unknown organ. It has many layers of folded tissue with an enormous surface area, a rich supply of blood vessels running close to the surface, and walls only one cell thick. What function do you predict this organ performs? Justify your prediction using structure-and-function reasoning.
  4. A food crop has been genetically engineered to resist a specific fungal disease. Critics argue this is unnatural and potentially dangerous. Supporters argue it will reduce pesticide use and food insecurity. What biological knowledge would you need to evaluate both sides of this argument? What questions would a biologist ask before recommending the crop for use?
  5. Imagine you are a biologist studying the effects of a new pollutant that prevents cells from using oxygen. Trace the effects of this pollutant from the cellular scale to the ecosystem scale. At what point would you expect ecological effects to become visible?
Part CSynthesis and Application
  1. Choose one organism that you find interesting — an animal, plant, fungus, or microorganism. Describe it at five different levels of biological organization: molecular, cellular, organ or tissue, organism, and population or ecosystem. What questions does biology ask at each level?
  2. The phrase "living system" appears throughout this chapter. Using what you have learned, write a paragraph that explains what makes a system "living." What properties must it have? What distinguishes a living system from a complex non-living system like a computer or a hurricane?
  3. A newspaper article states that scientists have found the "gene for intelligence" in humans. Using what you know about biological complexity, emergence, and the relationship between genes and traits, write a response explaining what is likely wrong or misleading about this claim.

Looking Ahead
Chapter 2: The Nature of Life

Chapter 1 introduced biology as the study of life and laid out the conceptual framework — systems, levels of organization, structure and function, energy, matter, information, and scale — that will guide the entire course. Chapter 2 goes deeper into the question this chapter raised: what exactly makes something alive? You will learn the major characteristics shared by all living things, why no single feature defines life on its own, how biologists use these characteristics to recognize living systems, and why viruses sit at the boundary between living and nonliving. Chapter 2 is where the definition of life becomes scientifically serious.

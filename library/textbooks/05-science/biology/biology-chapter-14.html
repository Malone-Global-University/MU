Chapter 14 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

"In science the credit goes to the man who convinces the world, not to the man to whom the idea first occurs."

— Francis Darwin  ·  Gregor Mendel's results sat unread for decades. When the world finally caught up, genetics was born.

Every person you have ever seen is genetically unique. Even identical twins, who begin from the same fertilized egg, accumulate small differences over a lifetime. The features that make you recognizable—your eye color, blood type, the shape of your earlobes, the way your hair grows—were partly determined before you were born, by information inherited from your parents.

How does that information pass from one generation to the next? How can two brown-eyed parents have a blue-eyed child? Why do some traits skip generations? These questions haunted naturalists for centuries. Farmers had long known that careful breeding could produce animals and plants with desirable traits, but no one understood the rules underneath—until a monk in Austria started counting pea plants.

Gregor Mendel spent eight years crossbreeding peas in a monastery garden, keeping records of thousands of plants and their offspring. He counted. He compared. He applied mathematics. What he found were patterns so consistent and so precise that they revealed something fundamental about how life passes traits from parent to child. Mendel published his results in 1866. They were ignored for thirty-four years.

When other scientists rediscovered his work in 1900, they immediately understood what he had found. Mendel had uncovered the basic rules of heredity. His work became the foundation of genetics—the science of how biological information is inherited. This chapter introduces Mendel's experiments, his conclusions, and the vocabulary and models that grew from his work.

Opening Question

Two tall pea plants are crossed. Their offspring include three tall plants and one short plant. Where did the short offspring come from? Neither parent was short. The answer lies in hidden information—information that was present but invisible. By the end of this chapter, you will know exactly why this happens, and how to predict it.

Section 14.1

Heredity and Inheritance

Heredity is the transmission of traits from parents to offspring. A trait is any observable or measurable characteristic of an organism—its eye color, height, blood type, flower color, seed shape, or thousands of other features. Traits are inherited through genes, which are specific segments of DNA that carry instructions for building and operating the body. Every organism inherits two copies of most genes—one from each parent. Those two copies interact to produce the traits we observe.

Heredity is not random. It follows patterns. The science of genetics exists because those patterns are consistent enough to study, model, and predict.

Note on Complexity

Mendel's model is elegant and powerful, but also simplified. Not all traits follow the clean patterns he discovered. Some traits are controlled by many genes. Some genes influence many traits. Some alleles interact in complicated ways. Some traits are strongly shaped by environment. This chapter teaches Mendelian genetics as a foundational model; Chapter 15 extends it to human genetics, and Chapter 16 explains how mutations and recombination add variation.

Section 14.2

Mendel's Experiments

Gregor Mendel (1822–1884) chose the common garden pea (Pisum sativum) for his experiments. Peas were ideal: easy to grow in large numbers, available in clearly distinct trait forms, capable of both self-fertilization and hand-controlled crossing, and fast-growing enough to study multiple generations in a few years.

Pure-Breeding Lines

Before experimenting with crosses, Mendel spent two years establishing pure-breeding lines—populations of plants that, when self-fertilized for many generations, always produced offspring identical to themselves for a given trait. A pure-breeding round-seed plant always produced round-seed offspring. A pure-breeding wrinkled-seed plant always produced wrinkled-seed offspring. These stable lines gave Mendel a clean starting point.

Fortunate Design

Each of Mendel's seven traits turned out to be controlled by a single gene, and each gene turned out to be on a different chromosome. This meant the traits sorted independently, making the patterns clear. If Mendel had studied traits linked on the same chromosome, the results would have been far messier.

The P, F₁, and F₂ Generations

LabelNameDescription

F₁ generationFirst filial generationOffspring of the P cross; filial from the Latin for "son" or "daughter"

Mendel's Key Finding
The disappearing and reappearing trait
  • P CrossRound-seed plant × wrinkled-seed plant (both pure-breeding)
  • F₁ ResultAll offspring had round seeds. The wrinkled trait had vanished entirely — not some, all of them.
  • F₂ ResultWhen F₁ plants self-fertilized: about 3/4 had round seeds, about 1/4 had wrinkled seeds. The trait came back.
  • CountsMendel counted 5,474 round and 1,850 wrinkled seeds in the F₂ — a ratio of 2.96:1, extremely close to 3:1. He observed the same ratio for all seven traits.
Conclusion: The recessive trait was not destroyed — it was hidden. It reappeared in the F₂ generation at a consistent 3:1 ratio.

Section 14.3

Genes, Alleles, and Traits

To explain his results, Mendel proposed that traits are determined by discrete particles of inheritance — one inherited from each parent. These particles are what we now call genes. Mendel's key insight was that these particles did not blend together. A round-seed particle and a wrinkled-seed particle did not produce a medium-seed plant. Instead, one particle could hide the other — and the hidden particle could reappear in later generations.

Genes and Alleles Defined

Gene — A segment of DNA that influences one or more biological traits. Each gene occupies a specific location called a locus on a chromosome.

Alleles — The different versions of a gene. Most organisms carry two copies of each gene, one from each parent, so each organism carries two alleles for each gene.

Representing alleles — Capital letters represent dominant alleles (e.g., R = round). Lowercase letters represent recessive alleles (e.g., r = wrinkled).

Section 14.4

Dominant and Recessive Alleles

When an organism carries two different alleles for a gene — one dominant and one recessive — the dominant allele is expressed. The recessive allele is present but does not affect the organism's appearance. This is why all F₁ plants in Mendel's crosses showed only one form of each trait: they carried both alleles, but only the dominant allele was visible in the phenotype.

Critical Clarification

Dominant does not mean "better," "more common," or "stronger." Dominant simply means one copy is enough to produce that trait. Recessive means two copies are required for the trait to appear. A recessive allele is not weaker or damaged — it carries its information intact. When an organism carries two copies of a recessive allele, that trait is fully expressed.

Section 14.5

Genotype and Phenotype

The genotype of an organism is the actual combination of alleles it carries for a given gene. The genotype describes the genetic information — what is written in the DNA. The phenotype is the observable trait produced by the genotype — what you can see, measure, or detect.

GenotypeDescriptionPhenotype (seed shape)
RRHomozygous dominant — two copies of the dominant alleleRound seeds
RrHeterozygous — one copy of each alleleRound seeds
rrHomozygous recessive — two copies of the recessive alleleWrinkled seeds
Homozygous and Heterozygous

Homozygous — Two identical alleles for a gene. RR is homozygous dominant; rr is homozygous recessive.

Heterozygous — Two different alleles for a gene. Rr is heterozygous.

Carrier — A heterozygous organism that carries the recessive allele without expressing the recessive trait. The recessive allele can be passed to offspring.

The Key Insight

Two different genotypes (RR and Rr) produce the same phenotype (round seeds). You cannot always determine an organism's genotype just by looking at its phenotype. A round-seeded pea plant might be RR or Rr — the seed alone cannot tell you which. This is why test crosses (Section 14.9) are so important.

Section 14.6

Punnett Squares

A Punnett square is a diagram used to predict the possible genotypes of offspring from a genetic cross. It was developed by Reginald Crundall Punnett in the early 1900s, shortly after Mendel's work was rediscovered. A Punnett square is a probability model. It shows all the possible ways parental alleles can combine in offspring — not a guarantee for any individual, but the probability distribution across many offspring.

How to Set Up a Punnett Square

Step 1 — Write parent genotypes. Place one parent's alleles across the top, one allele per column. Place the other parent's alleles down the left side, one allele per row.

Step 2 — Fill in each cell. Each cell represents one possible allele combination. Fill each cell by combining the column allele (top parent) with the row allele (left parent).

Step 3 — Read the results. Count the genotypes in the cells. The ratio of genotypes reflects the probability of each outcome across many offspring.

Example 14.6.1
Homozygous cross — PP × pp (Purple flower × White flower)
  • SetupP = purple (dominant), p = white (recessive). Cross: PP × pp
  • Punnett Square
    pp
    PPpPp
    PPpPp
  • ResultsGenotype: 100% Pp (all heterozygous). Phenotype: 100% purple flowers.
This matches Mendel's observation — all F₁ offspring show only the dominant trait. The recessive allele is present but hidden.
Example 14.6.2
Heterozygous cross — Pp × Pp (the F₁ × F₁ cross)
  • SetupBoth parents are heterozygous purple (Pp). This is Mendel's F₁ × F₁ cross.
  • Punnett Square
    Pp
    PPPPp
    pPppp
  • Genotype Ratio1 PP : 2 Pp : 1 pp
  • Phenotype Ratio3 purple (PP or Pp) : 1 white (pp) — the famous 3:1 ratio Mendel observed in every F₂ generation.
The white-flowered trait reappears because some offspring inherit two copies of the recessive allele (pp). The recessive allele was present — hidden — in every F₁ plant.
Probability, Not Prediction

The 3:1 ratio is what you expect on average across many offspring. Among any four actual plants from this cross, you might see 4 purple, or 3 and 1, or 2 and 2. The 3:1 ratio becomes reliable only with large sample sizes. This is why Mendel counted thousands of plants.

Section 14.7

Probability in Genetics

Punnett squares use the logic of probability. When a parent with genotype Rr forms gametes (sperm or eggs), each gamete carries exactly one allele. With equal probability, half the gametes carry R and half carry r. When two gametes combine, the outcome depends on which gamete from each parent happened to be involved. Each combination is equally likely.

Two Probability Rules in Genetics

Product Rule — The probability of two independent events both occurring equals the product of their individual probabilities. If the probability of inheriting R from the mother is 1/2, and from the father is 1/2, then: P(RR) = 1/2 × 1/2 = 1/4.

Sum Rule — The probability of either of two mutually exclusive events equals the sum of their individual probabilities. In a Pp × Pp cross: P(purple) = P(PP) + P(Pp) = 1/4 + 2/4 = 3/4.

Example 14.7.1
Using the product and sum rules
  • CrossRr × Rr (round-seed heterozygotes). What is the probability of a round-seed offspring?
  • Product Rule P(RR) = 1/2 × 1/2 = 1/4 P(Rr) from father R, mother r = 1/2 × 1/2 = 1/4 P(Rr) from father r, mother R = 1/2 × 1/2 = 1/4 P(rr) = 1/2 × 1/2 = 1/4
  • Sum RuleP(round) = P(RR) + P(Rr) = 1/4 + 1/4 + 1/4 = 3/4    [both Rr outcomes combined: 2/4]
  • Answer3/4 of offspring are expected to have round seeds; 1/4 wrinkled seeds.
This matches the 3:1 phenotype ratio from the Punnett square — confirming that the square is a visual representation of these probability calculations.
Probability and Individual Offspring

Probability describes what to expect on average across many outcomes. It does not determine what will happen in any individual case. A cross expected to produce a 3:1 ratio might give 4 purple plants and 0 white among only four offspring — all by chance. Each offspring is an independent event, like a coin flip.

Section 14.8

Monohybrid Crosses

A monohybrid cross is a genetic cross that examines the inheritance of a single trait. "Mono" means one; "hybrid" refers to offspring of parents with different forms of the trait. Mendel's original crosses — round × wrinkled, purple × white, tall × short — were all monohybrid crosses. The table below shows all the major cross combinations and their results.

Section 14.9

Test Crosses

A major challenge in genetics is that you often cannot determine an organism's genotype just from its phenotype. A tall pea plant might be TT or Tt. Both genotypes produce the same tall phenotype. The test cross solves this problem.

A test cross is a cross between an organism of unknown genotype and a homozygous recessive organism. Because the homozygous recessive parent contributes only recessive alleles, any dominant alleles in the offspring must have come from the unknown parent. The phenotypes of the offspring reveal the genotype of the unknown parent.

Example 14.9.1
Test cross — Is the tall plant TT or Tt?
  • SetupCross the unknown tall plant (T?) with a known short plant (tt). Examine the offspring phenotypes.
  • Case 1: Unknown is TT
    TT
    tTtTt
    tTtTt
    All offspring are Tt → all offspring are tall.
  • Case 2: Unknown is Tt
    Tt
    tTttt
    tTttt
    1/2 offspring are Tt (tall), 1/2 are tt (short).
  • InterpretIf all offspring are tall → unknown parent was TT. If approximately half are short → unknown parent was Tt.
The phenotype ratio of offspring reveals the genotype of the unknown parent. Short offspring can only appear if the tall parent carried a t allele (was Tt).
Why Test Crosses Matter

Test crosses allow geneticists and breeders to determine genotypes from offspring phenotypes. In agriculture, a breeder might want to know whether a prize bull carries a recessive disease allele. A test cross — mating the bull with females known to be homozygous recessive — can reveal the answer from the phenotypes of the calves.

Section 14.10

Limits of Simple Mendelian Models

Mendel's model applies cleanly to single genes with two alleles, one dominant and one recessive. Many traits in biology are more complicated. Understanding these limits prepares you for the more complex inheritance patterns you will encounter in Chapter 15.

Incomplete Dominance

In some cases, neither allele is fully dominant. When a red-flowered plant (RR) is crossed with a white-flowered plant (rr), the F₁ offspring might be pink. The heterozygote (Rr) shows an intermediate phenotype — a blend between the two homozygous extremes. This is called incomplete dominance. The F₂ cross (Rr × Rr) produces a 1 red : 2 pink : 1 white phenotype ratio — not the 3:1 ratio Mendel observed.

Codominance

In codominance, both alleles are fully expressed in the heterozygote. Neither masks the other; both appear simultaneously. Blood type AB is a well-known example: a person with one allele for type A and one for type B expresses both — both proteins are present on red blood cells at the same time.

Multiple Alleles

Many genes exist in more than two versions in a population. The ABO blood type gene has three alleles: IA, IB, and i. Any individual carries two of these three alleles, producing four possible blood types (A, B, AB, O). The Mendelian logic still applies — each individual carries two alleles and the rules of dominance and probability still operate — but the variety of possible outcomes is greater.

Polygenic Traits

Many traits are controlled by multiple genes. Height, skin color, and body weight are all polygenic traits. Because many genes contribute, these traits show continuous variation — a range of values rather than distinct categories. Skin color in humans is influenced by at least six different genes. You cannot draw a simple Punnett square and predict an outcome the way you can for seed shape.

Gene-Environment Interaction

The phenotype is always the product of genotype plus environment. A plant with genes for tall growth may stay short if it receives too little water or light. A person's actual height depends on both genetic inheritance and nutrition during development. For some traits, the genetic influence is so strong that environment barely matters. For others — like body weight, behavior, or health — environment plays an enormous role.

What Punnett Squares Can and Cannot Do

Excellent for: Predicting genotype and phenotype ratios in simple dominant-recessive crosses; determining probability of a specific offspring genotype; analyzing test cross results; teaching allele segregation logic.

Not designed for: Polygenic traits; incomplete dominance or codominance (though modified versions help); gene-environment interactions; linked genes; predicting the outcome for any specific individual offspring.

Mendelian genetics is not the whole story — but it is the essential first chapter.

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 14.1Heredity is the transmission of traits from parents to offspring through genes. Traits are inherited through discrete alleles — not by blending.
  • Sec 14.2Mendel chose pea plants for their distinct trait forms and short generations. He established pure-breeding lines, performed controlled crosses, and counted thousands of offspring. He found a consistent 3:1 phenotype ratio in F₂ generations for all seven traits he studied.
  • Sec 14.3Traits are controlled by genes; different versions of a gene are alleles. Organisms carry two alleles for each gene. Alleles are represented by capital letters (dominant) and lowercase letters (recessive).
  • Sec 14.4A dominant allele is expressed when one or two copies are present. A recessive allele is expressed only when two copies are present. Dominant does not mean better, healthier, or more common — it describes expression, not quality or frequency.
  • Sec 14.5Genotype is the allele combination an organism carries. Phenotype is the observable trait. Two genotypes (homozygous dominant and heterozygous) can produce the same dominant phenotype. Homozygous organisms carry identical alleles; heterozygous organisms carry different alleles.
  • Sec 14.6Punnett squares are probability models for predicting offspring genotype and phenotype ratios. A heterozygous × heterozygous cross produces a 1:2:1 genotype ratio and a 3:1 phenotype ratio.
  • Sec 14.7The product rule calculates the probability of two independent events both occurring. The sum rule calculates the probability of either of two mutually exclusive events. Probability predicts ratios across many offspring — not outcomes for individual offspring.
  • Sec 14.8A monohybrid cross examines one trait. The five major cross combinations produce predictable genotype and phenotype ratios, ranging from 100% dominant (AA × aa) to a 1:1 split (Aa × aa) to a 3:1 ratio (Aa × Aa).
  • Sec 14.9A test cross — between an organism of unknown genotype and a homozygous recessive organism — reveals hidden alleles. All dominant offspring indicate TT; a 1:1 split indicates Tt.
  • Sec 14.10Mendelian models do not cover all biological inheritance. Incomplete dominance, codominance, multiple alleles, polygenic traits, gene-environment interactions, and linked genes all require modifications beyond simple Punnett squares. The Mendelian foundation remains essential.

The combination of alleles an organism carries for a given gene or genes.
heredity
The transmission of traits from parents to offspring through genes.
heterozygous
Having two different alleles for a given gene (e.g., Aa). Also called a hybrid for that gene.
homozygous
Having two identical alleles for a given gene (e.g., AA or aa).
incomplete dominance
A relationship in which the heterozygote shows a phenotype intermediate between the two homozygous phenotypes — a blend.
locus
The specific location of a gene on a chromosome. Plural: loci.
monohybrid cross
A genetic cross examining the inheritance of a single trait.
multiple alleles
The existence of more than two alleles for a given gene in a population, though each individual still carries only two.
P generation
The parental generation; the pure-breeding organisms used to begin a cross.
phenotype
The observable characteristic produced by an organism's genotype. Shaped by genotype, environment, and their interaction.
polygenic trait
A trait influenced by two or more genes. Polygenic traits typically show continuous variation across a range of values.
probability
The likelihood that a particular outcome will occur. In genetics, probability describes expected ratios across many offspring.
product rule
The probability of two independent events both occurring equals the product of their individual probabilities.
Punnett square
A diagram used to predict the possible genotypes and phenotype ratios of offspring from a genetic cross.
pure-breeding
Describing a line that, when self-fertilized, consistently produces offspring identical to the parents for a given trait. Also called true-breeding.
recessive allele
An allele expressed in the phenotype only when two copies are present (homozygous recessive). Represented by a lowercase letter.
sum rule
The probability of either of two mutually exclusive events occurring equals the sum of their individual probabilities.
test cross
A cross between an organism of unknown genotype and a homozygous recessive organism, used to determine the unknown's genotype from offspring phenotypes.
trait
Any observable or measurable characteristic of an organism.

Error Analysis

Each statement contains a mistake in genetics reasoning. Identify the error and explain what is correct.

Error 1
"A dominant allele is stronger than a recessive allele and will always outcompete it."

What does "dominant" actually mean? What would be a more accurate description?

Error 2
"If two parents both have brown eyes, all of their children must also have brown eyes."

Under what condition could this be false? What genotypes would the parents need to have to produce a blue-eyed child?

Error 3
"A plant with genotype Rr will show a blended phenotype — somewhere between round and wrinkled seeds."

What inheritance pattern would produce a blended phenotype? What does actually happen in a standard dominant-recessive situation?

Error 4
"A Punnett square tells me exactly what my offspring will be. If it shows a 3:1 ratio, I should get exactly 3 dominant offspring for every 1 recessive offspring."

What does the 3:1 ratio actually represent? How does this differ from a guaranteed outcome?

Error 5
"If a recessive trait skips a generation, it is lost and cannot reappear."

What is actually happening to the recessive allele when a trait "skips" a generation? Under what condition can it reappear?

Common Misconceptions

Misconception 1
"Dominant means more common."

Dominant refers to the relationship between alleles — a dominant allele is expressed when one copy is present. It says nothing about frequency in a population. Some dominant alleles are rare (polydactyly — having six fingers — is dominant in some forms but uncommon). Some recessive alleles are extremely common in a population.

Misconception 2
"A dominant trait is the normal or healthy trait."

Many serious genetic diseases are caused by dominant alleles. Huntington's disease is caused by a dominant allele — being affected requires only one copy. Recessiveness is not a sign of disease, and dominance is not a sign of health. The words describe expression patterns, not biological quality.

Misconception 3
"Offspring are a blend of their parents' traits."

Mendel showed that alleles do not blend. Each allele is passed intact to offspring. A cross between a pure-breeding round-seed plant and a pure-breeding wrinkled-seed plant does not produce medium-seed offspring — it produces round-seed offspring that carry the wrinkled allele hidden within them. The wrinkled trait can reappear precisely because it was not destroyed. Blending inheritance was the dominant pre-Mendelian theory; Mendel's results disproved it.

Misconception 4
"A 3:1 ratio guarantees exactly 3 dominant offspring for every 1 recessive."

The 3:1 ratio is a probability prediction, not a guarantee. Individual offspring are like coin flips — each one is independent. With small numbers, observed ratios can differ substantially from the expected ratio by chance alone. The expected ratio becomes reliable only with large sample sizes, which is why Mendel counted thousands of plants.

Biology in Everyday Life

Genetics and Agriculture

Plant and animal breeders have applied the principles of heredity for thousands of years — long before Mendel made the rules explicit. Modern plant breeding still uses Mendelian logic at its foundation. When developing a new crop variety that is both disease-resistant and drought-tolerant, breeders perform controlled crosses and track traits through multiple generations, using test crosses to verify that plants are pure-breeding for the desired traits before investing in large-scale seed production.

  1. A plant breeder wants to develop a tomato variety homozygous for both large fruit size (LL) and disease resistance (DD). She starts with two pure-breeding plants: one that is large-fruited but susceptible to disease (LLdd) and one that is small-fruited but disease-resistant (llDD). Describe the crosses she would need to perform to eventually obtain plants that are LLDD. What would she look for in each generation?
  2. Two parents are each carriers for a recessive condition (both Aa). They have four children. (a) What is the probability that all four children are unaffected? (b) What is the probability that exactly one child is affected? (c) Why might a real family of four children not match these predictions?
Looking Ahead
Chapter 15: Human Genetics and Inheritance

Chapter 14 introduced Mendelian genetics using pea plants — organisms with cleanly dominant and recessive traits that follow simple patterns. Real heredity, including human heredity, is more varied. Chapter 15 extends genetics to human inheritance. You will learn to read pedigrees — diagrams that trace traits across family generations. You will study sex-linked traits and why some conditions appear more often in males than females. You will encounter codominance and incomplete dominance in human examples, explore blood type as a multiple-allele system, and examine chromosomal disorders that result not from allele inheritance but from errors in chromosome number or structure. Human genetics also raises ethical questions about genetic testing, privacy, and medical decision-making. The foundation you built in Chapter 14 — genes, alleles, genotype, phenotype, dominant, recessive, Punnett squares, probability, and test crosses — is the essential toolkit for everything that follows.

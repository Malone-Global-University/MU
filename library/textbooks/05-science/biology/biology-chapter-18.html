Chapter 18 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

"The evidence for evolution is overwhelming, and it comes from multiple independent lines of inquiry. No single piece of evidence makes the case; together, they converge on a single explanation for the diversity of life."

In Chapter 17, you learned how natural selection works: populations contain variation, some variation is heritable, and individuals whose traits help them survive and reproduce tend to leave more offspring. Over many generations, populations change. This is evolution.

But how do we know it happened? How can we study something that has been occurring for hundreds of millions of years, much of it long before any human observer existed? This chapter answers that question by examining the evidence — the multiple, independent, and converging lines of scientific support that together make evolution one of the most thoroughly supported explanations in all of science.

The evidence comes from radically different fields: paleontology, anatomy, embryology, genetics, geography, and direct observation. None of these fields was invented to prove evolution. Each produces its own kind of data. The remarkable fact is that all of them tell the same story.

Connection to Chapter 17

Chapter 17 explained the mechanism of evolution — how natural selection works on variation within populations. This chapter asks the complementary question: what evidence shows that evolution has actually occurred? The two chapters together address both the how and the how do we know of evolutionary biology.

Section 18.1

How Evidence Supports Evolution

In science, evidence is not simply a single observation or experiment that proves something once and for all. Evidence accumulates from many different investigations, using different methods, conducted by different researchers in different places and times. When independent lines of evidence all point to the same conclusion, confidence in that conclusion increases.

Evolution is supported by exactly this kind of convergent evidence. The fossil record, discovered by geologists and paleontologists, shows organisms changing over time. Comparative anatomy reveals shared structural patterns across very different species. Embryology shows surprising similarities among vertebrate embryos at early stages of development. Molecular biology reveals shared DNA sequences across all living things. Biogeography shows that the distribution of species matches patterns predicted by common ancestry and continental history. Direct observation documents evolutionary change in living populations.

Each of these lines of evidence was developed independently, using different tools and methods. All support the same underlying conclusion: life on Earth shares common ancestry and has changed over time through natural processes.

What Is a Scientific Theory?

In everyday speech, "theory" often means a guess or hunch. In science, a theory is a well-tested, well-supported explanation that accounts for a large body of evidence. Scientists do not begin by assuming evolution is true and then look for supporting facts. They ask questions, gather evidence, and look for patterns. The pattern that emerged — from fossils, anatomy, genetics, and geography — consistently pointed toward common ancestry and descent with modification. Evolution is a theory in this scientific sense: a coherent explanation that organizes a great deal of biological knowledge and has been tested extensively.

Think About It

Consider how unusual it would be for six completely independent fields — geology, anatomy, embryology, genetics, geography, and ecology — to all reach the same conclusion by mistake. When unrelated lines of evidence converge, that convergence itself is evidence.

Section 18.2

The Fossil Record

A fossil is any preserved remnant, impression, or trace of an organism from the past. Fossils form when organisms or their traces are buried in sediment that slowly hardens into rock. Over millions of years, minerals may replace the original organic material, preserving the shape — sometimes in remarkable detail. Fossils are not equally likely for all organisms. Hard structures — bones, shells, teeth, woody plant tissue — fossilize much more readily than soft bodies.

What the Fossil Record Shows

PatternDescriptionSignificance
Life has changed over timeOrganisms in older rock layers differ from those in younger layersSpecies are not fixed and unchanging
Older organisms are often simplerMicrobial life appears first; fish before amphibians; reptiles before mammalsSequence matches evolutionary predictions

Reading Rock Layers

Geologists and paleontologists use the position of fossils in rock layers, called strata, to determine relative age. Older layers are buried beneath younger layers in undisturbed rock — a principle called the law of superposition. Fossils found in lower strata are generally older than fossils in upper strata. Radiometric dating techniques, which measure the decay of radioactive isotopes, allow scientists to assign actual ages in years to rocks and fossils. These methods have confirmed the relative sequence revealed by stratigraphy and provided absolute dates spanning billions of years.

The Incompleteness Problem

No one expects the fossil record to be complete. Fossilization requires very specific conditions, and most organisms die without leaving any trace. What is remarkable is not the gaps but how consistent the sequence is. Whenever new fossils are discovered, they fit within the existing pattern. Paleontologists have never found a mammal fossil in Precambrian rock, or a human fossil alongside a dinosaur. The sequence is consistent everywhere it has been studied.

Section 18.3

Transitional Forms

A transitional form is a fossil that shows anatomical features intermediate between an ancestral group and a descendant group. Transitional forms are direct fossil evidence of evolutionary change — they document the gradual accumulation of new features over time. Critics of evolution sometimes ask, "Where are the missing links?" The answer is that many transitional forms have been discovered, and new ones are found regularly.

Transitional Form 1
Fish to Tetrapod: Tiktaalik roseae
  • Age and LocationDiscovered in Arctic Canada in 2004; approximately 375 million years old
  • Fish FeaturesGills and scales, like a fish
  • Tetrapod FeaturesA neck (fish have none); flat head; fins with internal bones arranged like tetrapod limbs — corresponding to the humerus, radius, and ulna found in all land vertebrate forelimbs
  • SignificanceTiktaalik could likely prop itself up on its fins — a motion impossible for a typical fish. Its discovery was predicted: paleontologists knew what age of rock to search and found exactly what evolutionary theory anticipated.
A classic example of a predicted discovery: the evolutionary model identified the time period and rock type where such a fossil should exist, and a search confirmed it.
Transitional Form 2
Dinosaur to Bird: Archaeopteryx and Feathered Dinosaurs
  • Age and DiscoveryArchaeopteryx first discovered in Germany in 1861 (two years after Darwin's On the Origin of Species); approximately 150 million years old
  • Bird FeaturesFeathers and wings
  • Dinosaur FeaturesTeeth, a long bony tail, and clawed fingers — typical of certain theropod dinosaurs
  • SignificanceMany additional feathered dinosaur fossils have since been found in China, showing a gradual accumulation of bird-like features. Modern birds are living theropod dinosaurs — a branch that survived the mass extinction that killed non-avian dinosaurs approximately 66 million years ago.
Transitional Form 3
Land Mammal to Whale: Pakicetus and Relatives
  • Starting PointPakicetus, found in Pakistan, lived about 53 million years ago — a land-dwelling mammal with four limbs, but with skull and ear bones that share features with modern whales
  • The SeriesAmbulocetus → Rodhocetus → Basilosaurus and others: each form shows progressively reduced hind limbs, increasingly streamlined bodies, and more adapted limbs for swimming
  • SignificanceThis is one of the most complete transitional series in the fossil record, documenting large-scale evolutionary change across tens of millions of years in substantial detail. Modern whales retain vestigial hip bones as a reminder of their terrestrial ancestors.
Three independent transitional series — each from a completely different lineage — all show the same pattern: gradual anatomical change from an ancestral form to a descendant form over millions of years.

Section 18.4

Comparative Anatomy

If life on Earth shares common ancestry, we would expect related organisms to share structural features inherited from common ancestors. Comparative anatomy — the study of structural similarities and differences among organisms — provides this evidence. Anatomical comparison reveals patterns that are difficult to explain any other way. Why would organisms that live very different lives, in different environments, and use their body parts for entirely different purposes share the same underlying skeletal architecture? The answer that best explains the evidence is common descent.

Structure and Function

In biology, structure and function are deeply linked — but comparative anatomy reveals a twist: structures with the same underlying architecture can perform entirely different functions. This only makes sense if the structures were inherited from a common ancestor and then modified. Design from scratch would produce more efficient, purpose-built structures with no resemblance to each other.

Section 18.5

Homologous and Analogous Structures

Homologous structures are structures in different organisms that share the same underlying anatomy, derived from a common ancestor, even if they are now used for different functions. The forelimbs of vertebrates are a classic example. A human arm, a cat's front leg, a whale's flipper, a bat's wing, and a bird's wing are all structurally built from the same set of bones: one upper bone (the humerus), two lower bones (radius and ulna), a group of wrist bones, and then finger bones.

OrganismForelimb FunctionSame Basic Bone Arrangement?
HumanGrasping, manipulatingYes — humerus, radius, ulna, wrist bones, finger bones
CatWalking, runningYes — same bones, different proportions
BatFlyingYes — elongated finger bones support wing membrane
WhaleSwimmingYes — flattened and embedded in flipper tissue
BirdFlyingYes — fused bones with different proportions
FrogJumping, supportYes — same basic plan

This shared plan is not what you would design from scratch for each function. A wing and a flipper look very different. But they share the same bones because both were inherited and modified from a common ancestral limb. This pattern — same structure, different function — is the signature of common descent.

Analogous Structures and Convergent Evolution

Analogous structures are structures in different organisms that have similar functions but very different underlying anatomy. They evolved independently in separate lineages as solutions to similar problems. The wing of a butterfly and the wing of a bird are analogous: both enable flight, but a butterfly wing has no bones and evolved through an entirely independent pathway. They look functionally similar because they solve the same problem, not because they share a common ancestor. This independent evolution of similar features is called convergent evolution.

Key Distinction

Homologous structures reflect shared ancestry — evidence for common descent. Analogous structures reflect convergent evolution — independent solutions to similar problems. Always ask: do these structures share the same underlying anatomy, or only similar external appearance and function? The answer determines which category applies.

Practice 18.5Homologous vs. Analogous
  1. A shark and a dolphin have similar streamlined body shapes. Internally, however, sharks have cartilage skeletons and breathe through gills, while dolphins have bony skeletons and breathe air. Are their body shapes homologous or analogous? Explain.
  2. The forelimbs of a mole, a human, and a whale all contain a humerus, radius, and ulna. Are these forelimbs homologous or analogous? What does this suggest about the ancestry of these three species?
  3. The eye of an octopus and the eye of a vertebrate both form focused images. However, in the vertebrate eye, photoreceptor cells face away from incoming light; in the octopus eye, they face toward it. Are these eyes homologous or analogous? Explain your reasoning.
  4. Cacti in the Americas and certain euphorbs in Africa both have thick, fleshy stems for water storage and spines for protection, yet they are unrelated plants. Is this similarity an example of homology or analogy? What term describes this pattern?
Section 18.6

Vestigial Structures

A vestigial structure is a structure that has been reduced in size or function in a descendant species compared to its functional form in an ancestor. Vestigial structures are not necessarily completely useless — some retain minor roles — but they are clearly reduced relative to equivalent structures in species where the function is still important. They are inherited from ancestors that used them and have gradually been reduced as selection pressure for their original function decreased.

Why Vestigial Structures Matter

Vestigial structures make complete sense in the context of evolution: they are inherited from ancestors and gradually reduced when selection pressure for their function disappears. Without common ancestry, vestigial structures are puzzling — why would a whale have hip bones with no limbs? With common descent, they are exactly what we would expect to find.

Section 18.7

Embryological Evidence

Early embryologists made a striking discovery: the embryos of very different vertebrate animals — fish, amphibians, reptiles, birds, and mammals, including humans — look remarkably similar at early stages of development. At an early embryonic stage, all vertebrate embryos possess pharyngeal arches (gill pouches), a post-anal tail, and a similar body plan.

In fish, the pharyngeal arches develop into functional gills. In humans and other mammals, those same structures are present in the embryo but develop into entirely different structures — parts of the jaw, middle ear bones, and throat. The tail is present in human embryos and then reabsorbed before birth. These shared developmental features suggest that vertebrates inherited a common developmental program from a common ancestor.

Developmental Genes: Hox Genes

Molecular biology has extended and refined the embryological evidence. Researchers have discovered that many genes controlling development — called Hox genes — are shared across extremely different animals, from worms to flies to fish to humans. These genes determine the basic body plan: which end is the head, which is the tail, and how body regions are organized. The fact that such fundamental genetic instructions are shared across such distantly related animals provides powerful evidence that all animals share ancestry and that core developmental mechanisms were established very early in animal evolution.

Key Idea: Shared Developmental Programs

The same Hox gene that determines which end of a fruit fly becomes its head performs the same positional function in a mouse, a fish, and a human. These genes are so ancient and so fundamental to body organization that they have been conserved — with some modifications — across hundreds of millions of years of evolution. This is evidence of deep common ancestry across the entire animal kingdom.

Section 18.8

Molecular Evidence

Every living organism carries DNA. Because DNA is inherited from ancestors, it carries a record of evolutionary history. Organisms that share a more recent common ancestor are expected to have more similar DNA sequences than organisms that diverged longer ago. Modern molecular biology has allowed scientists to compare DNA sequences across species with extraordinary precision. The results are consistent and powerful: the pattern of molecular similarity exactly mirrors the pattern predicted by anatomy, embryology, the fossil record, and biogeography.

Greater molecular similarity between more closely related organisms is exactly what evolution predicts. It is very difficult to explain any other way.

Cytochrome c: A Classic Molecular Comparison

Cytochrome c is a protein involved in cellular respiration, found in essentially all aerobically respiring organisms. Comparing its amino acid sequence across species reveals a striking pattern: human and chimpanzee cytochrome c are identical; human and rhesus monkey differ by 1 amino acid; human and horse differ by 12; human and fish differ by about 18; human and yeast differ by about 44. The closer the evolutionary relationship, the more similar the protein — exactly the signature of molecules inherited from common ancestors and modified over time through mutation and selection.

Pseudogenes

A pseudogene is a DNA sequence that resembles a functional gene but has accumulated mutations that prevent it from producing a working protein. One compelling example involves the gene for synthesizing vitamin C. Most mammals can make vitamin C internally. The gene for the final enzyme in this pathway exists in humans, chimpanzees, and gorillas — but it is broken by the same mutation in the same location in all three. This shared broken gene makes no sense unless these species inherited it from a common ancestor in whom the mutation originally occurred.

Endogenous Retroviruses

Retroviruses can insert their DNA into the genome of a host cell. These inserted sequences — called endogenous retroviruses (ERVs) — are then inherited by the host's descendants. Humans and other great apes share many of the same ERV insertions in the same chromosomal locations. The probability that identical viral insertions would occur at the same location by chance in multiple independent lineages is essentially zero. These shared insertions are inherited from common ancestors, exactly as evolution predicts.

Example 18.8
Reasoning from molecular evidence — the shared broken vitamin C gene
  • ObservationHumans, chimpanzees, and gorillas all carry a non-functional copy of the gene for the last enzyme in the vitamin C synthesis pathway. The same mutation disables the gene in the same location in all three species.
  • QuestionHow did three different species end up with the same broken gene at the same location?
  • Evolutionary ExplanationA mutation disabled the gene in the common ancestor of humans, chimpanzees, and gorillas. All three lineages then inherited the non-functional gene from that ancestor.
  • Alternative Explanation?The same specific mutation would have to occur independently in the same location in three separate lineages — an event so improbable it is effectively impossible.
The shared broken gene is strong evidence of common ancestry. It is a molecular "scar" inherited from a common ancestor.

Section 18.9

Biogeography

Biogeography is the study of the geographic distribution of species. If evolution is true, we expect species to be distributed in patterns that reflect their ancestry, the history of landmasses, and the pathways of dispersal available to their ancestors. This is exactly what biogeographers find.

Islands and Continental Relatives

Islands that have never been connected to a continent generally have species that are clearly related to species on the nearest mainland — but modified for island life. The Galápagos Islands, located about 1,000 kilometers from South America, have finches, iguanas, tortoises, and other animals that are clearly related to South American species but have diversified into many distinct forms. They did not arrive from Africa or Asia, which are much farther away. They arrived from South America and then evolved in isolation into distinct species.

Madagascar, which separated from Africa millions of years ago, has lemurs and other unique species found nowhere else. The species on Madagascar reflect its isolation: their ancestors arrived before the island separated or crossed the water gap, then evolved in isolation for millions of years.

Continental Distributions

Many groups of organisms are distributed across multiple continents in ways that reflect ancient connections. Marsupials (pouched mammals) are found today mainly in Australia and the Americas. The fossil record shows they were once more widespread, and their current distribution reflects both ancient continental connections and later extinction. The same environment in different parts of the world is often occupied by ecologically similar but anatomically very different organisms — a pattern called convergent evolution: marsupial wolves in Australia, placental wolves in the Northern Hemisphere; cactus-like plants in the Americas and cactus-like euphorbs in Africa, evolved independently from different ancestors because similar environments favor similar adaptations.

Biogeography and Island Evolution

The Galápagos finches famously observed by Darwin are a textbook biogeographic example. The roughly 15 species of finches on the islands are all descended from a single ancestral finch population that arrived from South America. Isolated populations on different islands evolved different beak shapes suited to different food sources — a process called adaptive radiation. The islands provided a natural experiment in evolution by isolation and selection.

Section 18.10

Evolution Observed Today

Some people assume that evolution is only about the distant past — something that happened to dinosaurs and ancient organisms but is no longer occurring. This is incorrect. Evolution is happening now, and scientists have documented it directly in living populations.

Antibiotic Resistance

The evolution of antibiotic resistance in bacteria is one of the most documented and consequential examples of natural selection in real time. When a bacterial population is exposed to an antibiotic, most bacteria are killed. But if any individuals carry mutations that allow them to survive, those individuals reproduce and pass on their resistance. In relatively short time — sometimes days or weeks — the bacterial population becomes resistant. Antibiotic resistance is not something bacteria "choose" to develop. It is natural selection acting on variation that already exists or arises through mutation — exactly the process Darwin described, occurring at a rate humans can directly observe. Understanding evolution is essential to addressing this ongoing public health crisis.

The Peppered Moth

In industrial England during the 1800s, pollution darkened tree trunks with soot. The peppered moth (Biston betularia) came in two forms: a light speckled form and a dark form. Before industrialization, the light form was common because it blended with light-colored lichen-covered bark; the dark form was rare and easily spotted by predators. After industrialization, dark trunks favored dark moths; the dark form increased dramatically in frequency. When pollution controls were later introduced and tree trunks lightened again, the frequency of the light form increased. The population changed and changed back in response to changing selection pressure — exactly as evolutionary theory predicts.

Darwin's Finches

Scientists Peter and Rosemary Grant and their colleagues studied finch populations on the Galápagos Islands for decades. During a severe drought, large hard seeds became the primary food source. Birds with larger beaks were better able to crack these seeds and survived at higher rates. Beak size in the population increased measurably within a single generation. When rainfall returned and food conditions changed, beak size distributions shifted again. This was natural selection documented in real time in a natural population — evolution happening fast enough to measure directly.

Evolution Is Not Only History

Every case of observed evolution reinforces the same core principles from Chapter 17: there must be heritable variation, selection must act on that variation, and some variants must survive and reproduce at different rates. These are not abstract ideas — they are processes occurring right now in bacterial populations in hospitals, insect populations in agricultural fields, and bird populations on remote islands.

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 18.1Evolution is supported by multiple independent lines of evidence from different scientific fields, each using different methods. When independent lines of evidence converge on the same conclusion, confidence increases. A scientific theory is a well-tested explanation, not a guess.
  • Sec 18.2The fossil record shows that life has changed over time, that older organisms differ from modern ones, and that organisms appear in the sequence predicted by evolutionary theory. Despite being incomplete, the fossil record is consistent everywhere it has been studied.
  • Sec 18.3Transitional forms document evolutionary change in progress. Tiktaalik shows the transition from fish to tetrapods; feathered dinosaurs document the transition to birds; the pakicetid-to-whale series documents a terrestrial-to-aquatic transition in considerable detail.
  • Sec 18.4Comparative anatomy reveals that related organisms share structural features inherited from common ancestors. The same underlying skeletal plan appearing in organisms with very different lifestyles is the signature of common descent.
  • Sec 18.5Homologous structures share underlying anatomy from a common ancestor, even if used for different functions. Analogous structures perform similar functions but evolved independently from different ancestry — reflecting convergent evolution, not shared descent.
  • Sec 18.6Vestigial structures are reduced remnants of structures functional in ancestors. The human coccyx, whale hip bones, and snake pelvic vestiges all make sense as inherited structures no longer under strong selection — puzzling without evolution, expected with it.
  • Sec 18.7Vertebrate embryos share developmental features at early stages, reflecting inherited developmental programs. Hox genes controlling body plan organization are shared across animals as distantly related as worms and humans, providing evidence of deep common ancestry.
  • Sec 18.8Molecular evidence is among the most powerful support for evolution. DNA sequence comparisons track evolutionary relationship with precision. Shared pseudogenes (including the broken vitamin C gene) and shared endogenous retroviruses in the same chromosomal locations confirm common ancestry.
  • Sec 18.9Biogeography shows that organisms are distributed geographically in ways reflecting ancestry, continental history, and dispersal pathways. Islands are populated by modified relatives of mainland species. Convergent forms in isolated regions reflect similar selection pressures on different lineages.
  • Sec 18.10Evolution is not only history; it is an ongoing process. Antibiotic resistance, pesticide resistance, beak size changes in Galápagos finches, and color frequency changes in peppered moths have all been documented directly by scientists in living populations.

Key Terms Review

fossil
Any preserved remnant, impression, or trace of an organism from the past, typically formed when organisms are buried in sediment that hardens into rock.
stratigraphy
The study of rock layers (strata) and their sequence, used to determine the relative ages of fossils. Older layers lie beneath younger layers in undisturbed rock.
transitional form
A fossil showing anatomical features intermediate between an ancestral group and a descendant group, documenting gradual evolutionary change.
comparative anatomy
The study of structural similarities and differences among organisms, used to infer evolutionary relationships based on shared or divergent features.
homologous structures
Structures in different organisms that share the same underlying anatomy, derived from a common ancestor, even if now used for different functions.
analogous structures
Structures in different organisms that perform similar functions but have different underlying anatomy, having evolved independently in separate lineages.
convergent evolution
The independent evolution of similar features in unrelated lineages, typically in response to similar environmental conditions or selection pressures.
vestigial structure
A structure that is reduced in size or function in a descendant species compared to its functional form in the ancestral lineage.
Hox genes
A family of genes that control body plan organization during animal development; found in similar forms across very different animal species, reflecting deep common ancestry.
pseudogene
A DNA sequence resembling a functional gene that has been disabled by mutations and no longer produces a working protein; inherited from a common ancestor.
endogenous retrovirus (ERV)
A viral DNA sequence that has been inserted into a host genome and is then inherited by that host's descendants; shared ERV insertions are evidence of common ancestry.
biogeography
The study of the geographic distribution of species and how those distributions reflect evolutionary history, continental movement, and dispersal pathways.
molecular evidence
Evidence for evolutionary relationships derived from comparisons of DNA, RNA, or protein sequences across species; shows greater similarity among more closely related organisms.
antibiotic resistance
The ability of bacterial populations to survive exposure to antibiotics, arising through natural selection on existing or newly arisen genetic variation.
cytochrome c
A protein involved in cellular respiration found in almost all aerobic organisms; used as a molecular clock to compare evolutionary distances between species.

Common Misconceptions

Misconception — "Evolution is just a theory — scientists aren't sure if it's true"

In everyday speech, "theory" often means a guess or hunch. In science, a theory is a well-tested, well-supported explanation that accounts for a large body of evidence. Gravity is also "just a theory." The theory of evolution has been tested through fossil evidence, molecular biology, genetics, biogeography, direct observation, and many other independent lines of inquiry. It is one of the most thoroughly supported explanations in all of science.

Misconception — "There are no transitional fossils"

There are many documented transitional fossils. Tiktaalik (fish to tetrapod), Archaeopteryx and feathered dinosaurs (dinosaur to bird), and the pakicetid-to-whale series are among the best known. New transitional fossils are discovered regularly. The sequence of fossils in rock layers is consistent with evolutionary relationships everywhere it has been studied.

Misconception — "Humans evolved from chimpanzees"

Humans did not evolve from modern chimpanzees. Humans and chimpanzees share a common ancestor — an extinct species from which both lineages descended. The two lineages diverged millions of years ago and have each been evolving independently ever since. Modern chimpanzees are our evolutionary cousins, not our ancestors.

Misconception — "Organisms evolve because they need to"

Evolution has no purpose or direction. Organisms do not sense what mutations they need and then produce them. Mutations occur randomly. Natural selection then favors mutations that happen to improve survival and reproduction in the current environment. There is no foresight, no need, and no direction in the process — only selection acting on existing variation.

Misconception — "Analogous structures are evidence for common ancestry"

Analogous structures — like the wings of butterflies and birds — are evidence of convergent evolution, not common ancestry. They evolved independently in separate lineages as solutions to similar problems. Homologous structures, which share underlying anatomy inherited from a common ancestor, are the evidence for common descent.

Looking Ahead
Chapter 19: Classification and the Tree of Life

Chapter 18 has shown you the evidence for evolution — the multiple, independent, and converging lines of support that establish that life on Earth shares common ancestry and has changed over time. Chapter 19 asks the next question: if life has diversified through evolution, how do biologists organize and make sense of that diversity?

In Chapter 19, you will learn taxonomy, binomial nomenclature, the domains of life, cladograms, and phylogenetic trees. You will see that classification is not simply a naming system — it is a map of evolutionary history, continually updated as new evidence, including molecular data, improves our understanding of the relationships among living things. The evidence for evolution you studied in this chapter is exactly the kind of evidence that biologists use to build and revise those maps.

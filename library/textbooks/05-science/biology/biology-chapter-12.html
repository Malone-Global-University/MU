Chapter 12 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Every living cell carries a complete set of instructions for building and running itself. Those instructions are written in a molecule called DNA. In this chapter, you will learn what DNA is, how it is structured, how it stores information, and how cells copy it before dividing. You will also learn how DNA is organized into chromosomes inside the nucleus and how the same basic system of genetic information appears across all life on Earth.

Understanding DNA is the foundation of all modern genetics. The experiments that identified DNA as the hereditary molecule, the discovery of its double-helix structure, and the working out of how it copies itself are among the most important achievements in the history of biology. These discoveries connect the visible traits of organisms to the molecular instructions that produce them.

Opening Question

A human cell is about six micrometers across — far too small to see without a microscope. Yet inside its nucleus, it contains about two meters of DNA. That DNA carries the instructions for building and running every cell in your body. How does so much information fit into such a small space? How is it copied accurately, billions of base pairs at a time, every time a cell divides? And how do a skin cell and a nerve cell — both carrying identical DNA — end up doing completely different jobs? By the end of this chapter, you will be able to answer all three questions.

Section 12.1

DNA as Hereditary Material

Long before scientists knew what DNA was made of, they knew that something inside cells passed traits from parents to offspring. For much of the early twentieth century, many scientists assumed that proteins were the molecules of heredity — proteins were known to be complex and varied, which seemed to make them good candidates for storing biological information. DNA, by contrast, was thought to be too simple and repetitive to carry meaningful instructions. A series of key experiments overturned that assumption.

Key Experiments Establishing DNA as the Hereditary Molecule

  • 1928 The Griffith Experiment. Frederick Griffith studied two strains of Streptococcus pneumoniae — a smooth disease-causing strain and a rough harmless strain. When he mixed heat-killed smooth bacteria with living rough bacteria, some of the rough bacteria transformed into disease-causing smooth bacteria. Something from the dead cells had entered the living cells and permanently changed them. Griffith called this the transforming principle, but did not identify what it was.
  • 1944 The Avery, MacLeod, and McCarty Experiment. Oswald Avery and colleagues systematically destroyed different molecules from the heat-killed smooth bacteria — proteins, carbohydrates, RNA — and tested whether transformation still occurred. Only one treatment stopped transformation: destroying the DNA. This pointed directly to DNA as the transforming principle.
  • 1952 The Hershey-Chase Experiment. Alfred Hershey and Martha Chase worked with bacteriophages — viruses that infect bacteria. They labeled the DNA of the phages with radioactive phosphorus and the protein coats with radioactive sulfur, then let the phages infect bacteria. When they tracked where each label ended up, the DNA label entered the bacterial cells while the protein label stayed outside. The hereditary material injected to take over the cell was DNA, not protein.
Why the Hershey-Chase Experiment Was More Convincing

The Avery experiment showed that DNA could transform bacteria, but some scientists still doubted the result. Hershey and Chase directly showed which part of the virus — DNA or protein — entered the cell to redirect its machinery. Tracking two different radioactive labels made the conclusion hard to dispute: the genetic information of a virus is carried in its DNA.

Section 12.2

The Structure of DNA

In 1953, James Watson and Francis Crick, building on X-ray crystallography data produced by Rosalind Franklin and Maurice Wilkins, proposed the double helix model of DNA. This model explained both the structure of DNA and how it could carry and copy information. DNA stands for deoxyribonucleic acid. It is a long polymer made of repeating units called nucleotides.

Three Parts of a DNA Nucleotide
  • A sugar called deoxyribose (five-carbon sugar)
  • A phosphate group (contains phosphorus and oxygen atoms)
  • A nitrogenous base — one of four: Adenine (A), Thymine (T), Guanine (G), or Cytosine (C)

Nucleotides connect to each other through covalent bonds between the sugar of one and the phosphate of the next, forming a long sugar-phosphate backbone with bases projecting inward. Two such strands wind around each other to form the famous double helix — resembling a twisted ladder. The backbones form the outer rails; the paired bases form the rungs.

The two strands run in opposite directions — they are antiparallel. One strand runs 5' to 3', the other 3' to 5'. These labels refer to which carbon of the deoxyribose sugar is at each end. The directionality matters because enzymes that copy or read DNA can only work in one direction along a strand.

FeatureDescription
Sugar-phosphate backboneThe outer rails of the helix; identical in all nucleotides; provides structural support
Nitrogenous basesThe inner rungs of the helix; four types (A, T, G, C); their sequence carries information
Double helixTwo antiparallel strands wound around a central axis; held together by hydrogen bonds between bases
Antiparallel orientationOne strand runs 5'→3'; the other runs 3'→5'; the strands go in opposite directions
Common Confusion — DNA vs. Nucleotide

A nucleotide is a single monomer unit. DNA is the polymer made from thousands to billions of nucleotides chained together. When we say the nitrogenous bases differ among nucleotides, we mean they differ within the same strand of DNA. The sugar and phosphate are the same in every nucleotide of DNA.

Section 12.3

Base Pairing

The two strands of a DNA double helix are held together by hydrogen bonds between nitrogenous bases. But not just any base can pair with any other — the pairing follows strict rules called complementary base pairing.

The Base-Pairing Rules
  • Adenine (A) always pairs with Thymine (T) — held by 2 hydrogen bonds
  • Guanine (G) always pairs with Cytosine (C) — held by 3 hydrogen bonds

These pairings are specific because of molecular shape — A and T fit together, G and C fit together, and no other combinations form stable pairs. The consequence is powerful: if you know the sequence of bases on one strand, you can predict the sequence on the complementary strand.

Example 12.3.1
Writing a complementary DNA strand
  • Problem Write the complementary strand for the following DNA sequence, in the correct antiparallel orientation:
    5' — A T G C C T A G — 3'
  • Think Apply the base-pairing rules base by base: A pairs with T, T pairs with A, G pairs with C, C pairs with G. The complementary strand runs antiparallel, so its 3' end aligns with the original strand's 5' end.
  • Solve Original strand (given): 5' — A T G C C T A G — 3' Complementary strand: 3' — T A C G G A T C — 5'
  • Check Verify each pair: A–T ✓, T–A ✓, G–C ✓, C–G ✓, C–G ✓, T–A ✓, A–T ✓, G–C ✓. The complementary strand is written 3'→5' because it runs antiparallel to the original 5'→3' strand.
Answer: 3' — T A C G G A T C — 5'
Example 12.3.2
Using base percentages to apply Chargaff's rules
  • Problem A double-stranded DNA molecule contains 22% guanine. What percentage of its bases are cytosine? What percentage are adenine?
  • Think Because G pairs with C, the percentage of G always equals the percentage of C in a double-stranded DNA molecule. The remaining percentage is split equally between A and T (since A pairs with T).
  • Solve G = 22% → C = 22% G + C = 44% A + T = 100% − 44% = 56% A = T = 56% ÷ 2 = 28%
  • Check A + T + G + C = 28 + 28 + 22 + 22 = 100% ✓
Answer: Cytosine = 22%. Adenine = 28%.
Why Base Pairing Is So Important

Base pairing does more than hold the two strands together. It is the mechanism that allows DNA to be copied accurately — each strand serves as a template for building a new complementary strand. It is also how genetic information is read to make RNA, which in turn guides protein synthesis. Without specific, reliable base pairing, neither replication nor protein synthesis would work.

Section 12.4

Chromosomes

A cell does not store its DNA as a loose, tangled mass. DNA is organized into compact structures called chromosomes. A chromosome is a single, continuous DNA molecule together with the proteins that help organize and support it. In eukaryotic cells, chromosomes are housed in the nucleus.

ConceptDescriptionHuman Example
ChromosomeOne DNA molecule packaged with histone proteinsHumans have 46 chromosomes per cell
Homologous pairTwo chromosomes carrying genes for the same traits; one from each parentHumans have 23 pairs of homologs
AutosomeChromosome not involved in sex determination22 pairs (44 of the 46 chromosomes)
Sex chromosomeChromosome involved in sex determinationX and Y; XX = typically female, XY = typically male
ChromatinLoosely organized DNA–protein complex in non-dividing cellsPresent during interphase; genes are accessible

For most of a cell's life, DNA is loosely organized as chromatin, spread throughout the nucleus and accessible to the cellular machinery that reads genes. When a cell prepares to divide, chromatin condenses dramatically into the shorter, thicker, visible chromosomes that appear in textbook diagrams. This condensation is why chromosomes are easier to count during cell division than at other times.

Chromosome number varies across species and has no simple relationship to biological complexity. Dogs have 78 chromosomes, fruit flies have 8, and some ferns have more than a thousand. What matters for complexity is not chromosome count but the information carried on those chromosomes.

Common Misconception
"More chromosomes means a more complex organism."

Chromosome number does not indicate biological complexity. Humans have 46 chromosomes, while some ferns have more than 1,000 and some insects have fewer than 10. Complexity arises from which genes are present and how they are regulated — not from the count of chromosomes carrying them.

Section 12.5

Genes and Genomes

A gene is a specific segment of DNA that contains the instructions for building a functional product — usually a protein, but sometimes an RNA molecule that performs its own function. Genes are located at specific positions on chromosomes; the location of a gene is called its locus (plural: loci). The complete set of DNA in an organism — all chromosomes together — is its genome.

Not all DNA encodes genes. In humans, only about 1.5 to 2 percent of the total DNA directly codes for proteins. The rest includes regulatory sequences that control when genes are active, structural sequences that help organize chromosomes, and regions whose functions are still being studied. Mapping the entire human genome was the goal of the Human Genome Project, completed in 2003. The human genome contains approximately 3 billion base pairs and an estimated 20,000 to 25,000 protein-coding genes.

Every cell in a multicellular organism carries essentially the same genome. A liver cell, a skin cell, and a neuron all contain the same DNA sequence. What differs is which genes are active in each cell type. This regulation of gene expression — which genes are read and when — is what produces the enormous variety of specialized cell types from a single genome.

Genome Size ≠ Complexity

Some salamanders and many plants have genomes far larger than the human genome — yet humans are not "less complex" organisms. Much of the extra DNA in large genomes consists of repeated sequences and non-coding regions. The number of genes is often similar. What matters most for biological complexity is how genes are regulated and combined, not how much total DNA is present.

Section 12.6

DNA Replication

Before a cell can divide, it must copy all of its DNA so each daughter cell receives a complete genome. This process is called DNA replication. It is one of the most accurate copying operations in all of chemistry — the error rate is roughly one mistake per billion base pairs. Replication relies entirely on complementary base pairing: each strand serves as a template for building a new partner strand.

Major Steps of DNA Replication
  • Unwinding: The enzyme helicase breaks the hydrogen bonds between base pairs and separates the two strands at the replication fork
  • Base pairing: Free nucleotides in the nucleus pair with exposed bases on each template strand (A–T, G–C)
  • Joining: DNA polymerase links new nucleotides together, synthesizing a new complementary strand in the 5'→3' direction
  • Proofreading: DNA polymerase detects and corrects most mismatched bases as it works; repair enzymes fix remaining errors
Example 12.6.1
Tracing semiconservative replication
  • Scenario A DNA molecule has two strands, labeled OLD-1 and OLD-2. It undergoes one round of replication. Describe what each of the two resulting molecules looks like.
  • Think In semiconservative replication, each original strand is kept and used as a template for a new partner strand. The original strands are not destroyed.
  • Solve Molecule 1: OLD-1 strand + NEW strand (complementary to OLD-1) Molecule 2: OLD-2 strand + NEW strand (complementary to OLD-2)
  • Result Both new molecules contain one original (conserved) strand and one newly synthesized strand. This is why the process is called semiconservative — each product conserves half of the original molecule.
Each daughter molecule contains one original strand and one new strand — neither original strand is lost.

Replication does not begin at one end of a chromosome and proceed to the other. It starts simultaneously at many points along the chromosome, called origins of replication, and proceeds in both directions from each origin. Thousands of origins firing at once allow the human genome — over 6 billion base pairs total (two sets per cell) — to be replicated in a matter of hours.

Semiconservative Does Not Mean "Half Destroyed"

Students sometimes think semiconservative replication means half the old DNA is broken down and replaced. It does not. "Semiconservative" means each new double helix conserves one of the two original strands intact. Both original strands survive — they end up in separate daughter molecules, each paired with a brand-new strand.

Section 12.7

DNA Packaging in Cells

If you stretched out all the DNA from a single human cell, it would be about 2 meters long. Yet a typical human cell nucleus is only about 6 micrometers in diameter — roughly 300,000 times smaller. Cells accomplish the necessary packing through multiple levels of organization, each level folding the DNA more tightly than the last.

LevelStructureDescription
Level 1NucleosomeDNA wraps around a cluster of 8 histone proteins; looks like beads on a string under the electron microscope
Level 2Solenoid fiberThe nucleosome chain coils into a thicker fiber (~30 nm diameter), shortening the chain further
Level 3Looped scaffoldThe fiber loops and coils around protein scaffolds, producing the compact condensed chromosomes visible during cell division

This packaging is not merely storage. Cells actively regulate which regions of chromatin are accessible. When a gene needs to be read, the relevant chromatin loosens up. When a gene must be silenced, the chromatin in that region compacts tightly. Histone proteins carry chemical modifications that influence local packing density — one component of what biologists call epigenetic regulation, which shapes gene expression without changing the DNA sequence itself.

Packaging Controls Access

Think of DNA packaging as a filing system. Tightly packed chromatin is like files locked in a cabinet — the information is there, but it cannot be read. Loosely packed chromatin is like files spread open on a desk. Cells actively move genes between "locked" and "accessible" states depending on which proteins are needed at any given moment.

Section 12.8

DNA in Prokaryotes and Eukaryotes

All living cells contain DNA, but its organization differs significantly between prokaryotes and eukaryotes. Understanding these differences connects to the broader question of how cells evolved and how different life forms are related.

FeatureProkaryotesEukaryotes
Chromosome shapeTypically circularLinear
Chromosome numberUsually one main chromosomeMultiple chromosomes (varies by species)
LocationNucleoid region (no membrane)Membrane-enclosed nucleus
Histone proteinsAbsent (or simpler versions in some archaea)Present; essential for packaging
Extra DNAPlasmids commonOrganelle DNA in mitochondria and chloroplasts
Genome sizeGenerally much smaller (millions of base pairs)Generally much larger (hundreds of millions to billions)

Eukaryotic cells also contain small amounts of DNA in mitochondria and (in plant cells) in chloroplasts. This organelle DNA is circular and resembles prokaryotic DNA — a key piece of evidence supporting the endosymbiotic theory, which proposes that these organelles evolved from ancient free-living bacteria that were incorporated into ancestral eukaryotic cells. You will explore this theory further in the evolution chapters.

Section 12.9

DNA as Biological Information

DNA is both a chemical molecule and an information-carrying system. Understanding both aspects together is essential to understanding how life works.

As a molecule, DNA is a polymer of nucleotides held together by covalent bonds in the backbone and hydrogen bonds between base pairs. It has physical properties that can be measured, copied, cut, sequenced, and compared between organisms.

As an information system, DNA stores instructions using a four-letter chemical alphabet: A, T, G, and C. The sequence of these bases encodes the information needed to build proteins. Differences in sequence — even a single-base change — can alter the protein produced, change its function, and affect the organism's traits.

DNA is remarkably stable. Ancient DNA has been extracted from mammoths, Neanderthals, and other organisms that died long ago. It is also faithfully copied generation after generation, allowing traits to pass reliably from parents to offspring. Yet DNA is not completely unchanging. Mutations — changes in the base sequence — occur naturally at a low rate, can be caused by radiation or certain chemicals, and can arise from replication errors. Most mutations have little effect; some are harmful; occasionally one is beneficial. Variation in DNA sequence across individuals is the raw material for evolution by natural selection.

The relationship between DNA sequence and organism traits is often complex. A single gene can affect multiple traits. A single trait can be influenced by dozens of genes. Environment shapes how genes are expressed. The same DNA sequence can produce different outcomes depending on which genes are active. DNA, then, is not a rigid blueprint but a flexible information system — one that interacts with cellular processes, environmental signals, and chance to produce the living organisms we observe.

Connection to Evolution and Genetics

The sequence variation in DNA among individuals is what Mendelian genetics tracks as alleles, what molecular biologists study as mutations, and what evolutionary biologists identify as the raw material of natural selection. When you study inheritance in Chapter 14 and evolution in Chapter 17, the molecular foundation underlying those patterns is always the base sequence of DNA and how it varies.

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 12.1DNA was established as the molecule of heredity through three landmark experiments: Griffith (1928) identified a transforming principle; Avery, MacLeod, and McCarty (1944) showed it was DNA; Hershey and Chase (1952) confirmed it using labeled bacteriophages.
  • Sec 12.2DNA is a polymer of nucleotides, each consisting of deoxyribose, a phosphate group, and one of four nitrogenous bases (A, T, G, C). Two antiparallel strands wind into a double helix, with the sugar-phosphate backbones on the outside and paired bases on the inside.
  • Sec 12.3Complementary base pairing governs DNA structure: A pairs with T (2 hydrogen bonds) and G pairs with C (3 hydrogen bonds). The sequence of one strand determines the sequence of the other. Base pairing is also the mechanism of replication and gene reading.
  • Sec 12.4Chromosomes are single DNA molecules packaged with histone proteins. Human cells have 46 chromosomes in 23 homologous pairs, including two sex chromosomes (X and Y). DNA exists as loose chromatin in non-dividing cells and condenses into visible chromosomes before division.
  • Sec 12.5A gene is a DNA segment encoding a functional product, located at a specific locus on a chromosome. The genome is an organism's complete DNA. Only about 2% of human DNA encodes proteins. All cells of an organism carry the same genome; gene expression differs by cell type.
  • Sec 12.6Before division, helicase unwinds the double helix; DNA polymerase adds nucleotides to each template strand, following base-pairing rules. Replication is semiconservative — each new molecule contains one original and one new strand. Replication starts at multiple origins simultaneously.
  • Sec 12.7DNA is packaged at multiple levels: DNA wraps around histones to form nucleosomes; nucleosomes coil into a solenoid fiber; fibers loop further to form condensed chromosomes. Packaging also regulates gene expression — tightly packed regions are gene-silenced regions.
  • Sec 12.8Prokaryotes have a single circular chromosome in an unenclosed nucleoid region, plus plasmids. Eukaryotes have multiple linear chromosomes in a membrane-enclosed nucleus, packaged with histones. Mitochondrial and chloroplast DNA resembles prokaryotic DNA, supporting endosymbiotic theory.
  • Sec 12.9DNA is both a molecule with measurable physical properties and an information system using a four-letter alphabet of bases. Mutations in DNA sequence cause variation; variation is the raw material for evolution. Gene expression — which genes are active — determines cell identity despite identical genome content.

Common Misconceptions

Common Misconception
"DNA and a chromosome are the same thing."

DNA is the molecule. A chromosome is a structure that contains one DNA molecule plus histone proteins and organizational components. The chromosome is the packaged, organized form of the DNA — not the DNA itself.

Common Misconception
"In semiconservative replication, the original DNA strands are destroyed and replaced by two new ones."

Replication is semiconservative, meaning each new double helix conserves one original strand intact. Both original strands survive — they end up in separate daughter molecules, each paired with a newly synthesized strand.

Common Misconception
"Having more chromosomes means an organism is more complex."

Chromosome number has no simple relationship to complexity. Humans have 46 chromosomes; some ferns have over 1,000; some insects have fewer than 10. What matters is which genes are present and how they are regulated, not the count of chromosomes carrying them.

Common Misconception
"Only some cells in the body contain DNA."

Nearly every cell in the human body carries the full genome. Mature red blood cells, which lose their nucleus as they mature, are the notable exception. All other typical body cells — skin, muscle, liver, nerve — contain 46 chromosomes and the full DNA sequence.

Common Misconception
"Mutations are always harmful."

Most mutations have little to no effect. Some are harmful, some are neutral, and occasionally one is beneficial in a particular environment. Mutations are the source of genetic variation that drives evolution — if all mutations were lethal, evolution could not occur.

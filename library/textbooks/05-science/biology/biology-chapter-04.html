Chapter 4 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Living things are made of matter and run on chemistry. Every time a cell divides, a muscle contracts, a leaf captures sunlight, or a nerve sends a signal, chemical reactions are happening. Biology cannot be understood without some understanding of chemistry — not because biologists need to memorize the periodic table, but because the molecules inside living things obey chemical rules, and those rules explain how life works.

This chapter introduces the chemical ideas you need for the rest of this course. You will not need advanced chemistry. You will need enough chemistry to understand why water is so important to cells, why proteins can change shape, why some molecules carry energy, and why the acid-base balance in your blood has to stay within a very narrow range. These ideas will come back in nearly every chapter that follows.

Opening Question

Your body is about 60–70% water. Your DNA is a long chain of carbon-containing molecules held together partly by weak attractions called hydrogen bonds. The enzymes that speed up every chemical reaction in your cells are proteins that can stop working if the pH of your blood shifts by even a fraction of a unit. How does all of this chemistry connect to being alive? By the end of this chapter, you will have the foundation to answer that question — and to understand every biological system this course covers.

Section 4.1

Why Biology Needs Chemistry

When you eat a meal, your digestive system breaks large molecules into smaller ones that your cells can absorb. When your cells use oxygen to release energy from sugar, they are running a carefully controlled set of chemical reactions. When DNA is copied before a cell divides, chemical bonds are being formed and broken with remarkable accuracy. When a hormone travels through the bloodstream and changes the behavior of a distant cell, it works by fitting into a protein receptor the way a key fits a lock — chemistry explaining biology.

Biology is the study of living systems, but living systems are made of chemicals, and their processes are chemical processes. A cell is not a bag of mystery. It is a highly organized collection of molecules doing specific jobs, following the same laws of chemistry that govern reactions in a laboratory or a kitchen.

What You Need From Chemistry in This Course

· Why some molecules dissolve in water and others do not

Section 4.2

Atoms, Elements, and Compounds

Atoms

All matter — whether living or nonliving — is made of atoms. An atom is the smallest unit of an element that retains the chemical properties of that element. Atoms are extraordinarily small: a single cell contains trillions of them. Every atom has a nucleus at its center, containing protons (positively charged) and neutrons (no charge). Surrounding the nucleus is a region where electrons (negatively charged) move. The number of protons defines what element an atom is — an atom with six protons is always carbon; an atom with eight protons is always oxygen.

The Four Key Biological Elements

ElementSymbolApprox. % of living matter (by mass)Key roles in biology
OxygenO~65%Water, cellular respiration, many biological compounds
CarbonC~18%Backbone of all biological molecules
HydrogenH~10%Water, organic molecules, acids and bases
NitrogenN~3%Proteins, DNA, RNA, chlorophyll
Memory Tool — CHON

Carbon, Hydrogen, Oxygen, and Nitrogen make up about 96% of living matter by mass. The acronym CHON helps you remember the four most abundant elements in life. Other elements — phosphorus, sulfur, calcium, potassium, iron — are also essential but present in smaller amounts.

Compounds and Molecules

When atoms of different elements bond together, they form a compound. Water (H₂O) is a compound of hydrogen and oxygen. Carbon dioxide (CO₂) is a compound of carbon and oxygen. The biological molecules that carry out life's functions — sugars, fats, proteins, DNA — are all compounds. A molecule is two or more atoms bonded together. The properties of a compound are different from the properties of its individual elements: hydrogen and oxygen are both colorless gases, but combined as water they form a liquid that is essential for life.

Section 4.3

Chemical Bonds in Living Systems

Atoms bond to one another through forces of attraction between their electrons. The type of bond formed affects how easily it can be broken, how much energy it stores, and how molecules behave in water.

Bond TypeHow It FormsRelative StrengthBiological Importance
Covalent (nonpolar)Two atoms share electrons equallyStrongCarbon-hydrogen bonds in organic molecules; stable backbones of proteins, DNA, fats
Covalent (polar)Two atoms share electrons unequally; one end is slightly negativeStrongO–H bonds in water; creates water's polarity and solvent properties
IonicOne atom transfers electrons to another; opposite charges attractModerate (weaken in water)Salt ions in body fluids; mineral nutrition; cell membrane charges
HydrogenWeak attraction between partially positive H and partially negative O or NWeak individually; strong collectivelyHolds DNA strands together; determines protein shape; gives water its properties
Key Idea 4.3
Why bond strength matters for living systems
  • Strong bondsCovalent bonds hold large biological molecules (proteins, DNA, fats, carbohydrates) together. They are stable enough to store biological information and structural integrity.
  • Weak bondsHydrogen bonds can be formed and broken quickly and precisely. When DNA strands are separated before replication, hydrogen bonds between the two strands break. When an enzyme grabs and releases a molecule, hydrogen bonds help. Flexibility and reversibility are essential for life.
  • Both togetherLiving systems use strong bonds for stable structure and weak bonds for controlled, reversible interactions. Neither type alone would work.
Common Misconception

"Breaking bonds releases energy." This is a common half-truth. Breaking bonds actually requires energy. Energy is released when new, stronger bonds form in the products. In an exergonic reaction, the bonds formed in the products are collectively stronger than the bonds broken in the reactants — so the net result is energy release. This concept will reappear in Chapter 8 (Enzymes) and Chapters 9–10 (Photosynthesis and Respiration).

Section 4.4

Water and Its Special Properties

Of all the molecules that matter for biology, water is the most important. Life as we know it originated in water, depends on water, and in most organisms consists mostly of water. A human body is roughly 60–70% water by mass. A bacterial cell may be 70–90% water. All of water's special properties trace back to one feature: its polarity.

A water molecule (H₂O) has two hydrogen atoms bonded to one oxygen atom. Oxygen pulls shared electrons strongly toward itself, making the oxygen end slightly negative (δ−) and the hydrogen ends slightly positive (δ+). The molecule is bent, not linear, so the partial charges are not symmetrically arranged. Water is a polar molecule, and this polarity explains everything below.

PropertyCauseBiological Significance
CohesionHydrogen bonds between water moleculesWater clings together; allows water to be pulled up tall plants in continuous columns; creates surface tension
AdhesionWater is attracted to other polar surfacesWater clings to walls of vessels in plants and blood vessels; assists upward movement of water against gravity
High specific heatHydrogen bonds resist temperature changeBody temperature is stabilized; blood resists rapid heating or cooling; coastal climates are moderated
Evaporative coolingHigh-energy water molecules escape as vapor, leaving cooler liquid behindSweating cools the body; plants release water vapor through leaves to cool tissues
Excellent solventPolarity allows water to surround and dissolve polar and ionic substancesNutrients, wastes, gases, and signals dissolve in body fluids and move through organisms
Ice floatsFrozen water forms a less-dense crystal lattice than liquid waterIce forms on the surface of lakes and insulates liquid water below, allowing aquatic life to survive winter
Worked Example 4.4
Tracing a property back to polarity
  • QuestionWhy can water dissolve table salt (NaCl), but not cooking oil?
  • Step 1Table salt is an ionic compound. It is made of positively charged sodium ions (Na⁺) and negatively charged chloride ions (Cl⁻). Water molecules, with their partial charges, surround these ions and pull them apart. The ionic solid dissolves.
  • Step 2Cooking oil is made of nonpolar molecules. Nonpolar molecules have no partial charges. Water molecules are attracted to each other (by hydrogen bonds) more than they are attracted to nonpolar molecules. Oil is excluded and does not dissolve.
  • ConclusionHydrophilic (water-loving) substances have charges or polarity and dissolve in water. Hydrophobic (water-fearing) substances are nonpolar and do not.
The polarity of water determines what it can dissolve. This distinction is critical for cell membranes, protein folding, and fat digestion.
Why Ice Floating Matters

If ice sank, lakes would freeze solid from the bottom up each winter. Fish and other aquatic organisms would have no liquid water refuge. Because ice is less dense than liquid water — an unusual property traceable to water's hydrogen bonding — it floats on the surface and acts as an insulating layer, keeping the water beneath above freezing.

Section 4.5

Solutions and Mixtures in Biology

A solution is a uniform mixture of two or more substances. The substance that does the dissolving is the solvent; the substance that is dissolved is the solute. In biology, water is almost always the solvent. The concentration of a solution describes how much solute is dissolved in a given volume of solvent.

The fluids inside and outside your cells are solutions. Blood plasma, cytoplasm, the fluid in your digestive tract, and the liquid in your eyes are all aqueous (water-based) solutions containing dozens of dissolved substances — ions, gases, nutrients, proteins, hormones, and waste products. The concentration of these dissolved substances affects nearly every biological process.

Mixture typeParticle sizeBehaviorBiological example
SolutionVery small (ions, small molecules)Uniform; particles do not settleSalt dissolved in blood plasma; glucose dissolved in cytoplasm
ColloidIntermediateParticles dispersed but too large for true solutionCytoplasm (gel-like interior of cells)
SuspensionLarge (visible particles)Particles may settle over timeRed blood cells suspended in plasma
Why Concentration Matters

If the concentration of ions inside a cell changes, the cell's chemistry changes. If blood glucose rises too high (as in uncontrolled diabetes) or falls too low (hypoglycemia), serious health problems result. Homeostasis at the molecular level is largely about maintaining the right concentrations of dissolved substances in cells and body fluids. You will see this again in Chapters 7 (osmosis and transport), 24 (blood sugar regulation), and 26 (kidney function).

Section 4.6

Acids, Bases, and pH

Water molecules occasionally break apart spontaneously, releasing a hydrogen ion (H⁺) and a hydroxide ion (OH⁻). The concentration of hydrogen ions in a solution determines whether it is an acid or a base.

An acid is a substance that releases H⁺ when dissolved in water, increasing hydrogen ion concentration. A base is a substance that accepts H⁺ or releases OH⁻, decreasing hydrogen ion concentration. The pH scale measures H⁺ concentration on a scale from 0 to 14. The scale is logarithmic — each step represents a tenfold change in H⁺ concentration.

Reading the pH Scale

· pH below 7 = acidic (higher H⁺ concentration)

· pH 7 = neutral (pure water)

· pH above 7 = basic / alkaline (lower H⁺ concentration)

· Each step of 1 pH unit = a 10-fold change in H⁺ concentration. A solution at pH 4 has 100 times more H⁺ than one at pH 6.

SubstanceApproximate pHAcidic / Neutral / Basic
Stomach acid1.5 – 3.5Strongly acidic
Lemon juice2Strongly acidic
Black coffee5Weakly acidic
Urine4.5 – 8Varies
Pure water7Neutral
Blood7.35 – 7.45Slightly basic
Pancreatic fluid8 – 8.3Weakly basic
Seawater8.1Weakly basic

Buffers

A buffer is a solution that resists changes in pH when small amounts of acid or base are added. Buffers work by absorbing excess H⁺ when pH drops and releasing H⁺ when pH rises. The bicarbonate buffer system in human blood uses carbonic acid (H₂CO₃) and bicarbonate ions (HCO₃⁻) to maintain blood pH within the narrow range of 7.35–7.45. A drop below 7.35 is called acidosis; a rise above 7.45 is called alkalosis. Both are medical emergencies.

Worked Example 4.6
Interpreting pH changes
  • QuestionA lake has a normal pH of 7.2. Acid rain lowers it to 5.2. How many times more acidic has the lake become?
  • Step 1Calculate the difference in pH units: 7.2 − 5.2 = 2 units.
  • Step 2Because the scale is logarithmic, each unit is a 10-fold change. Two units = 10 × 10 = 100 times change.
  • ConcludeThe lake is now 100 times more acidic than normal. This is far more than the two-unit difference suggests.
The lake is 100 times more acidic. This would significantly disrupt enzyme function, shell formation, and many biological processes in the ecosystem.
pH and Enzyme Function

Enzymes are proteins, and their shapes depend on weak interactions (hydrogen bonds, ionic attractions) that are sensitive to H⁺ concentration. If pH shifts outside the enzyme's normal range, its shape changes and it stops working. Stomach enzymes work best at pH 1.5–2; intestinal enzymes work best near pH 7–8. Chapter 8 will cover this in depth.

Section 4.7

Carbon as the Backbone of Life

Of the approximately 118 known elements, carbon has a special role in biology. Almost every important molecule in living things — sugars, fats, proteins, DNA — is built on a backbone of carbon atoms. Chemistry that uses carbon as a framework is called organic chemistry.

Why carbon? Because a carbon atom has four electrons available for bonding, meaning it can form four covalent bonds simultaneously. Carbon atoms can bond to other carbon atoms (forming long chains and rings), to hydrogen, oxygen, nitrogen, phosphorus, and sulfur. This versatility allows carbon to form an enormous variety of stable molecular structures. Life has taken advantage of this diversity over billions of years of evolution.

Carbon Skeleton ShapeExampleBiological significance
Straight chainFatty acid chains in fatsLong nonpolar chains are hydrophobic — key to membrane structure
Branched chainSome amino acid side chainsBranching creates three-dimensional shapes that determine protein function
RingGlucose (6-carbon ring), DNA basesRigid, compact structure; important for sugar chemistry and genetic information

Functional Groups

Attached to carbon skeletons are clusters of atoms called functional groups. Functional groups are specific arrangements of atoms that give molecules particular chemical properties — making them acidic, basic, reactive, hydrophilic, or hydrophobic. The chemical behavior of a large biological molecule depends significantly on which functional groups it carries.

Functional GroupCompositionPropertyFound in
Hydroxyl−OHPolar, hydrophilicAlcohols, sugars
Carboxyl−COOHActs as an acid; releases H⁺Amino acids, fatty acids
Amino−NH₂Acts as a base; accepts H⁺Amino acids, DNA bases
Phosphate−PO₄Charged, hydrophilic; stores energy in bondsDNA, RNA, ATP
CarbonylC=OPolar; reactiveSugars, some lipids
Concept, Not Memorization

You do not need to memorize this functional group table. What matters is the concept: the chemical behavior of a large biological molecule depends on what functional groups are attached to its carbon skeleton. The same carbon backbone with different functional groups is an entirely different molecule with entirely different properties.

Section 4.8

Chemical Reactions in Cells

A chemical reaction is a process in which chemical bonds are broken, formed, or rearranged, converting reactants into products. Every biological process — from digesting a meal to copying DNA — is a chemical reaction or a linked sequence of them.

Two Types of Reactions by Energy

Exergonic reactions — release energy. Products have less stored energy than reactants. Cellular respiration is exergonic: glucose + oxygen → CO₂ + water + energy released.

Endergonic reactions — require energy input. Products have more stored energy than reactants. Building proteins, DNA, and other large molecules is endergonic — it costs energy.

In cells, exergonic and endergonic reactions are often coupled: energy released by breaking down food powers the synthesis of ATP, which then powers endergonic reactions elsewhere.

Activation Energy

Even reactions that ultimately release energy require an initial input to get started. This initial energy is called activation energy. Think of pushing a boulder off a hilltop: you must push it up a small rise before it rolls down. In cells, molecules called enzymes lower activation energy, making reactions fast enough to sustain life. Without enzymes, most biological reactions would occur far too slowly. Enzymes are covered fully in Chapter 8.

Worked Example 4.8
Reading a reaction: cellular respiration
  • EquationC₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + energy
  • ReactantsGlucose (C₆H₁₂O₆) and oxygen (O₂) are the starting materials.
  • ProductsCarbon dioxide (CO₂), water (H₂O), and energy are the outputs.
  • Reaction typeThis is exergonic — energy is released as chemical bonds in glucose are broken and new bonds form in CO₂ and H₂O. Cells capture this released energy in ATP molecules.
  • ScaleThis is not a single-step reaction. Cellular respiration is a metabolic pathway with dozens of steps, each catalyzed by a different enzyme. The overall equation summarizes the net result.
Cellular respiration is an exergonic metabolic pathway that releases energy from glucose. The summary equation does not show the many intermediate steps or enzymes involved.

Metabolism as a Network

Metabolism is the total of all chemical reactions in a living organism. Metabolic reactions are organized into pathways — sequences in which the product of one reaction becomes the reactant for the next. Anabolic pathways (anabolism) build complex molecules from simple ones and require energy input. Catabolic pathways (catabolism) break complex molecules into simpler ones and release energy. Living things maintain life by constantly running and regulating both types.

Pathway typeDirectionEnergyExample
AnabolicSmall → large moleculesRequires energy (endergonic)Building proteins from amino acids; making DNA; photosynthesis building glucose
CatabolicLarge → small moleculesReleases energy (exergonic)Cellular respiration breaking down glucose; digestion breaking down proteins

Section 4.9

Chemistry in Health and the Environment

pH and Human Health

The body maintains careful chemical balance. When this regulation fails, illness follows. Acid reflux occurs when stomach acid (pH ~2) escapes into the esophagus, which lacks the protective lining of the stomach. Kidney disease can disrupt regulation of ion concentrations and pH in the blood. Diabetes involves disrupted regulation of glucose concentration in the blood. Each of these is a chemical problem with biological consequences.

Water Quality and Ecosystems

Acid rain — rainfall made acidic by sulfur dioxide and nitrogen oxide pollution — can lower the pH of lakes and soils, harming aquatic organisms that cannot survive outside a narrow pH range. Ocean acidification — caused by the ocean absorbing excess carbon dioxide from the atmosphere — threatens shellfish and coral reefs, whose calcium carbonate structures dissolve in more acidic water.

Carbon Chemistry and Climate

Living things are made of carbon compounds. When organisms die and decompose, or when fossil fuels (the remains of ancient organisms) are burned, carbon dioxide is released into the atmosphere. Carbon dioxide is a greenhouse gas — it traps heat. Understanding the carbon cycle, covered in Chapter 28, requires understanding how carbon moves between living organisms, the atmosphere, the oceans, and Earth's crust.

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 4.1Biology cannot be understood without chemistry. Every cellular process — from DNA replication to muscle contraction — is a chemical reaction. This chapter provides the chemical foundation for the rest of the course.
  • Sec 4.2All matter is made of atoms. The number of protons defines the element. Four elements — oxygen, carbon, hydrogen, nitrogen (CHON) — make up about 96% of living matter. Compounds form when atoms of different elements bond together.

  • Sec 4.7Carbon's four bonding sites allow it to form chains, rings, and branched skeletons — the backbone of all biological molecules.   • Sec 4.8Chemical reactions convert reactants to products. Exergonic reactions release energy; endergonic reactions require energy.   • Sec 4.9Chemistry connects biology to health and the environment. Acid-base imbalances cause disease. Acid rain and ocean acidification disrupt aquatic ecosystems. Carbon chemistry links living organisms to the atmosphere and climate. Agriculture and medicine depend on understanding biological chemistry.

A uniform mixture of a solvent and one or more solutes; in biology, the solvent is almost always water.
solvent
The substance in which solutes are dissolved. Water is the solvent of life.
specific heat
The energy required to raise the temperature of a substance by one degree. Water's unusually high specific heat stabilizes temperature in living systems.

Common Misconceptions

Misconception — Breaking bonds releases energy
"Breaking chemical bonds releases energy."

Correction: Breaking bonds requires energy. Energy is released when new, stronger bonds form in the products. In an exergonic reaction, the bonds formed in the products are collectively stronger than those broken in the reactants — so the net result is energy release. Confusing breaking with releasing is a very common error in biology and chemistry.

Misconception — Water is just a passive medium
"Water is just the medium — chemistry happens in spite of water."

Correction: Water is not a passive container. Its polarity, hydrogen bonding, high specific heat, and solvent properties actively participate in biological chemistry. Many reactions in cells would not occur at all without water.

Misconception — Acidic means dangerous
"Acidic means dangerous; basic means safe."

Correction: Both acids and bases can be harmful at extremes. Stomach acid (pH ~2) is highly acidic and essential for digestion. Bleach (pH ~13) is highly basic and corrosive. What matters is concentration, context, and what tissues are exposed — not whether something is acidic or basic in general.

Misconception — Carbon is only in living things
"Carbon is only found in living organisms."

Correction: Carbon is found throughout the nonliving world — in carbon dioxide, limestone, diamonds, and coal. What is special about carbon in living things is the remarkable variety of carbon-containing molecules that organisms build, not that carbon is exclusive to life.

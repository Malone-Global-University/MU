Chapter 21 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Walk through almost any outdoor space and plants are everywhere — covering the ground, climbing walls, arching over paths, floating on water. They are so familiar that it is easy to overlook how remarkable they are. Plants make their own food from sunlight, water, and air. They anchor soil, filter water, regulate temperature, and produce the oxygen that nearly every organism on Earth depends on. They are the foundation of most food webs on land.

But plants are more than a backdrop for other life. They are sophisticated organisms with internal systems for transporting water and nutrients, detecting light and gravity, timing their reproduction to the season, and responding to damage, drought, and competition. A redwood tree moving hundreds of liters of water from roots to canopy every day, a Venus flytrap snapping shut on an insect, a sunflower tracking the sun across the sky — these are not passive events. They are the work of biological systems.

This chapter explores what makes plants distinctive as a group of organisms, how their cells and tissues are organized, how their roots, stems, and leaves serve interconnected functions, how water and nutrients move through vascular tissue, how plants reproduce, and how plants respond to their environments. It also examines the deep connections between plants and the rest of life on Earth.

Opening Question

A large oak tree may move several hundred liters of water from its roots to its leaves on a hot summer day — with no pump, no heart, and no muscle. How is this possible? By the end of this chapter, you will understand the physical and biological mechanisms that make this feat routine for plants, and why it depends on a simple property of water molecules clinging to one another.

Section 21.1

What Makes Plants Distinctive

Plants are multicellular, eukaryotic organisms that carry out photosynthesis using chlorophyll. They are the primary producers in most land ecosystems — organisms that capture energy from sunlight and convert it into organic molecules that other organisms consume. Several features set plants apart from all other groups of living things.

Cell walls made of cellulose. Every plant cell is surrounded by a rigid cell wall composed largely of cellulose, a carbohydrate polymer. Cell walls give plant cells structural support, help plants maintain shape without a skeleton, and allow plants to build upright stems and tall trees.

Chloroplasts. Plant cells that carry out photosynthesis contain chloroplasts — organelles that capture light energy and use it to produce sugar from carbon dioxide and water. Chlorophyll, the green pigment inside chloroplasts, absorbs primarily red and blue light while reflecting green light, which is why most plants appear green.

Alternation of generations. Plants have a life cycle that alternates between two distinct stages: a diploid stage called the sporophyte and a haploid stage called the gametophyte. In familiar flowering plants and trees, the sporophyte is the large, visible plant. The gametophyte stage is reduced and, in many plants, microscopic — pollen grains and the structures inside ovules are gametophytes.

Stationary lifestyle. Unlike animals, plants cannot move from place to place to find resources, escape stress, or locate mates. Their biology reflects this: they evolved extensive root systems to find water and nutrients, chemical signaling systems to respond to threats, and reproduction strategies that allow their offspring to disperse.

Adaptation to land. Plants evolved from aquatic algal ancestors. The transition to land required solving major challenges: preventing water loss, supporting upright structures against gravity, transporting water from soil to tissues, and reproducing without surrounding water to carry reproductive cells.

Key Distinction

Angiosperms enclose their seeds within a fruit — the mature ovary wall. Gymnosperms produce "naked seeds" on the surface of cone scales. This distinction is one of the most important in plant classification. Angiosperm means "enclosed seed"; gymnosperm means "naked seed."

Plant bodies are built from several distinct cell types, each specialized for particular functions, organized into three tissue systems that run throughout the plant body.

Three Plant Tissue Systems

Dermal tissue — the outer covering; epidermis, cuticle, root hairs. Protects against water loss and physical damage; absorbs water in roots.

Ground tissue — the bulk of most plant organs; parenchyma, collenchyma, sclerenchyma. Carries out photosynthesis, storage, and structural support.

Vascular tissue — xylem and phloem running through roots, stems, and leaves as continuous bundles. Transports water, minerals, and sugars throughout the plant body.

Section 21.3

Roots

Roots serve three primary functions: anchoring the plant in soil, absorbing water and dissolved minerals, and storing nutrients. In many plants, roots also house beneficial microorganisms and play a role in vegetative reproduction.

Root Zones

At the tip of each root is the root cap — a thimble-shaped mass of cells that covers and protects the root apical meristem, a zone of actively dividing cells responsible for root growth. As the root pushes through the soil, the root cap is worn away and continuously replaced.

Just behind the root tip is the zone of cell division, where the meristem produces new cells. These cells then enter the zone of elongation, where they lengthen dramatically, pushing the root tip forward. Further back is the zone of maturation, where cells differentiate into the specialized cell types of functional root tissue.

The epidermis of the zone of maturation is studded with root hairs — thin extensions of individual epidermal cells that vastly increase surface area available for water and mineral absorption. A single plant may have billions of root hairs. Water and dissolved minerals enter through root hairs largely by osmosis and active transport, then move through the cortex to the vascular tissue at the root's core.

Worked Example 21.3.1
Why root hairs dramatically increase absorption
  • QuestionA root without hairs has a surface area of 2 cm². Root hairs increase surface area by a factor of 10. How does this increase affect the plant's ability to absorb water?
  • ThinkWater absorption depends on the area of contact between the root surface and soil water. More surface area means more simultaneous contact and higher absorption rate.
  • Calculate Surface area with hairs = 2 cm² × 10 = 20 cm² Absorption capacity increases by the same factor — approximately 10×
  • ApplyThis is why disturbing or cutting root hairs (when transplanting seedlings) causes wilting — the plant temporarily loses most of its absorption surface until new root hairs regenerate.
Answer: Root hairs increase absorption capacity approximately in proportion to their surface area increase — a critical adaptation for water uptake.

Legume roots form nodules housing bacteria that convert atmospheric nitrogen gas (N₂) into forms the plant can use. This is why farmers rotate legume crops with others — legumes enrich soil nitrogen, reducing the need for nitrogen fertilizer. This natural symbiosis is one of the most agriculturally important ecological relationships on Earth.

Section 21.4

Stems

Stems serve multiple functions: they support leaves and flowers, position photosynthetic surfaces to receive light, and provide the main transport pathway connecting roots to leaves. Some stems also carry out photosynthesis and store water or nutrients.

In monocots (grasses, lilies, corn, and their relatives), vascular bundles are scattered throughout the ground tissue of the stem. In eudicots (most broadleaf plants, including beans, tomatoes, and oaks), vascular bundles form a ring near the stem's outer edge, arranged around a central pith.

Primary and Secondary Growth

Primary growth lengthens the plant. It originates from apical meristems at the tips of roots and stems — zones of cell division that add new cells that elongate and differentiate, extending the plant body upward and downward.

Secondary growth increases girth. It occurs in most gymnosperms and in eudicot trees and shrubs, but not in monocots. The vascular cambium (a lateral meristem) produces new xylem inward and new phloem outward. The xylem produced by secondary growth is wood. Annual tree rings form because xylem produced in spring (larger, lighter cells) differs from xylem produced in late summer (smaller, darker cells).

Primary vs. Secondary Growth

Primary growth: Lengthens the plant. Originates at apical meristems (tips of roots and shoots). Occurs in all vascular plants.

Secondary growth: Increases girth; produces wood and bark. Originates at lateral meristems (vascular cambium, cork cambium). Occurs in most gymnosperms and eudicot trees and shrubs. Does NOT occur in monocots — explains why palm trees, grasses, and corn do not produce wood rings.

Section 21.5

Leaves

Leaves are the primary photosynthetic organs of most plants. Their structure reflects an evolutionary solution to a design challenge: how to maximize light capture and gas exchange while minimizing water loss.

Leaf Structure

A leaf is typically a flat, thin blade — a shape that maximizes surface area for capturing light while keeping diffusion distances for gases small. The outer surfaces of a leaf are covered by the epidermis, coated with a waxy cuticle that reduces evaporative water loss. The underside of most leaves (and sometimes both sides) is punctuated by stomata — microscopic pores that allow the exchange of carbon dioxide and oxygen with the atmosphere. Each stoma is flanked by two guard cells whose shape changes to open or close the pore.

The interior of the leaf is called the mesophyll. In many eudicot leaves, the palisade mesophyll near the upper surface consists of elongated, tightly packed cells densely loaded with chloroplasts — this is where most photosynthesis occurs. Below it, the spongy mesophyll contains cells with large air spaces between them, facilitating the movement of gases to and from photosynthetic cells. Vascular bundles — the veins visible in leaves — branch throughout the mesophyll, delivering water and minerals and collecting the sugars produced by photosynthesis.

Worked Example 21.5.1
Analyzing the stomatal trade-off
  • ScenarioA plant faces a dry, hot day. Its guard cells detect low water availability and trigger stomatal closure. Analyze the costs and benefits of this response.
  • BenefitClosed stomata reduce transpiration — the evaporation of water from leaves. This conserves water and prevents wilting and dehydration.
  • CostClosed stomata block the entry of carbon dioxide, the raw material of photosynthesis. Without CO₂ entering, the rate of photosynthesis falls, limiting the plant's ability to produce sugar and grow.
  • ConclusionThis is a biological trade-off: the plant sacrifices photosynthetic rate to preserve water. Both responses are regulated by the hormone abscisic acid (ABA), which rises during drought stress.
Answer: Stomatal closure conserves water but reduces photosynthesis — a trade-off between survival and growth.
Leaf Adaptations in Extreme Environments

Desert plants have thick cuticles, sunken stomata, and reduced leaf surfaces to minimize transpiration. CAM plants (cacti, agaves) open stomata only at night, fixing CO₂ when temperatures are lower and water loss is reduced. Conifer needles have a small surface area and thick cuticle — adaptations for cold, dry conditions where liquid water may be unavailable for months.

Section 21.6

Xylem and Phloem

The vascular system of a plant is its internal plumbing — a continuous network of tubes running from root tips to leaf edges, connecting every part of the plant body. It moves two separate streams: water and minerals moving upward through xylem, and sugars moving in both directions through phloem.

Xylem: Moving Water Upward

Xylem conducts water and dissolved minerals from roots to the rest of the plant. Water movement through xylem is driven by transpiration-cohesion-tension:

Transpiration-Cohesion-Tension — Step by Step

Step 1 — Transpiration: Water evaporates from leaf cells and exits through stomata. This creates negative pressure (tension) in the xylem of the leaf.

Step 2 — Cohesion: Water molecules cling to one another (cohesion) and to xylem walls (adhesion), forming a continuous water column through the xylem tubes.

Step 3 — Tension: The tension created by transpiration at the leaf pulls the water column upward throughout the xylem — all the way from roots to leaf tips. No pump is needed.

Step 4 — Replacement: As water is pulled up, new water is drawn in at the roots by osmosis from surrounding soil.

How Tall Can This Work?

This passive mechanism can move water more than 100 meters to the tops of the world's tallest trees — coast redwoods and mountain ash. On a warm summer day, a single large tree may transpire hundreds of liters. No energy input beyond the sun evaporating water from leaves is required.

At the root, water crossing the cortex must pass through the endodermis — a layer of cells with a waxy band called the Casparian strip in their cell walls. This band blocks water from moving between endodermal cells; water must pass through the interior of endodermal cells instead. This selective passage allows the plant to control which solutes enter the vascular system — functioning like a gateway.

Phloem: Moving Sugars

Phloem transports dissolved sugars — primarily sucrose — produced by photosynthesis from wherever they are produced or stored (sources) to wherever they are consumed or stored (sinks). Growing shoot tips, roots, and developing fruits are sinks; mature leaves carrying out active photosynthesis are sources. Unlike xylem, phloem flow is bidirectional.

Phloem transport operates by pressure flow. At a source, sugar is actively loaded into phloem sieve tube cells, raising solute concentration and drawing in water by osmosis — creating high pressure. At a sink, sugar is unloaded, water follows by osmosis, and pressure drops. The pressure difference drives flow from source to sink through the interconnected phloem.

Section 21.7

Plant Reproduction

Plants can reproduce both sexually and asexually. Sexual reproduction involves the union of gametes and produces genetically variable offspring — important for adaptation and evolution. Asexual reproduction produces genetically identical offspring (clones) from a single parent — a rapid, reliable way to multiply when conditions are favorable.

Asexual Reproduction

Many plants reproduce vegetatively — producing new individuals without gametes. This occurs naturally through runners (strawberries), rhizomes (many grasses and ferns), bulbs (tulips and onions), and fragmentation. Humans exploit plant asexual reproduction extensively in agriculture through cuttings, grafting, and layering to produce genetically identical plants.

Alternation of Generations in Seed Plants

The Two-Stage Plant Life Cycle

Sporophyte (diploid, 2n): The large, visible plant you see. Produces spores by meiosis.

Gametophyte (haploid, n): Produces gametes (eggs and sperm). In flowering plants, the gametophyte is microscopic and dependent on the sporophyte. Male gametophyte = pollen grain. Female gametophyte = small structure within the ovule.

Fertilization: Sperm from pollen fertilizes egg in ovule → diploid embryo (new sporophyte generation).

Pollination and Double Fertilization

In flowering plants, reproduction centers on the flower. A typical flower includes sepals (protective outer structures), petals (often colorful, attracting pollinators), stamens (male reproductive structures bearing pollen-producing anthers), and carpels (female reproductive structures with stigma, style, and ovary containing ovules).

Pollination is the transfer of pollen from anther to stigma. A pollen grain that lands on a compatible stigma germinates, producing a pollen tube that grows down the style to the ovule. Two sperm travel through the pollen tube. One fertilizes the egg to produce a diploid embryo; the other fuses with other cells to produce the endosperm — tissue that will nourish the embryo. This process of double fertilization is unique to flowering plants.

Worked Example 21.7.1
Following a pollen grain to fertilization
  • Step 1A bee visits a flower and brushes against the anther. Pollen grains (male gametophytes, each containing sperm cells) adhere to the bee's body.
  • Step 2The bee visits another flower of the same species. A pollen grain is deposited on the stigma — the sticky upper surface of the carpel. Pollination has occurred.
  • Step 3The pollen grain germinates: it grows a pollen tube down through the style, guided by chemical signals, toward the ovule inside the ovary.
  • Step 4Two sperm travel through the pollen tube. One fertilizes the egg → diploid zygote (future embryo). The other fuses with two other nuclei → triploid endosperm nucleus. This is double fertilization.
  • Step 5The zygote develops into an embryo. The endosperm provides nutrition. The ovule wall hardens into the seed coat. The ovule becomes a seed; the ovary develops into a fruit.
Answer: Pollination → pollen tube growth → double fertilization → embryo + endosperm → seed inside fruit.
Coevolution: Flowers and Their Pollinators

Flower color, shape, fragrance, and timing often reflect the sensory abilities and behaviors of specific pollinators. Red tubular flowers attract hummingbirds (which see red well but lack a strong sense of smell). Pale, heavily scented flowers attract night-flying moths. Landing platforms and guide markings direct bees to nectar and pollen. These are the results of millions of years of coevolution — mutual adaptation between flowers and their pollinators.

Section 21.8

Flowers, Seeds, and Fruits

Seeds

A seed contains three key components: the embryo (the developing sporophyte), endosperm (nutrient tissue), and a seed coat (protective outer layer derived from the ovule wall). Seeds protect the embryo, provide it with stored nutrients for germination, and allow it to survive unfavorable conditions — sometimes for years.

Germination — the resumption of embryo growth — is typically triggered by water absorption, appropriate temperature, and sometimes light or a period of cold (stratification). As the embryo emerges, it draws on the endosperm for nutrients until its leaves begin photosynthesis.

Fruits and Dispersal

A fruit is a mature ovary, often with associated structures. Fruits develop from the ovary wall after fertilization and typically contain seeds. Their structure reflects how their seeds travel.

Common Misconception — Fruits vs. Vegetables

The distinction between fruits and vegetables is culinary, not botanical. Botanically, a fruit is any mature ovary containing seeds. By this definition, tomatoes, cucumbers, peppers, squash, beans, and corn kernels are all fruits — they develop from fertilized ovaries. Roots (carrots, beets), stems (celery), and leaves (spinach, lettuce) are the structures a biologist would classify as non-fruit plant organs.

Section 21.9

Plant Responses and Hormones

Plants cannot move from place to place, but they are far from passive. They respond to light, gravity, touch, water availability, temperature, day length, and damage. Without neurons or a nervous system, plants coordinate these responses through chemical signals — hormones — and through electrical signals.

Plant hormones are organic molecules produced in one part of the plant that travel to target tissues and alter their growth or activity. The same hormone can have different effects in different tissues or at different concentrations.

Auxin and phototropism

  • Step 2When light hits from one side, auxin migrates to the shaded side of the shoot.

Photoperiodism and Flowering

Many plants flower in response to the length of the dark period each night — a response called photoperiodism. Short-day plants (more accurately long-night plants) flower when nights exceed a critical length — they bloom in late summer or fall. Chrysanthemums, poinsettias, and soybeans are short-day plants. Long-day plants flower when nights fall below a critical length — they bloom in spring or early summer. Spinach, lettuce, and many grain crops are long-day plants. Day-neutral plants flower regardless of day length.

Agricultural Application: Controlling Flowering

Florists and growers use controlled lighting to trigger or prevent flowering out of season. Keeping chrysanthemums under extended artificial darkness during summer induces them to flower in time for winter holidays. Briefly interrupting long dark periods with flashes of light prevents "short-day" plants from flowering prematurely — a technique used in commercial greenhouses worldwide.

Section 21.10

Plants in Ecosystems and Human Life

In most terrestrial ecosystems, plants are the primary producers — the foundation of the food web. Through photosynthesis, they convert solar energy into chemical energy stored in organic molecules. Every heterotroph that eats plants, and every predator that eats herbivores, ultimately depends on this energy. Plants also fix carbon from atmospheric carbon dioxide into organic matter and release the oxygen that aerobic organisms require.

Plants are central participants in nutrient cycles. They absorb carbon dioxide from the atmosphere and incorporate carbon into their tissues. When they die and decompose, that carbon returns to the atmosphere or soil. Their roots stabilize soil, reducing erosion and keeping nutrients in place. Forests, grasslands, and wetlands — all dominated by plants — are major reservoirs of carbon. The clearing and burning of forests releases stored carbon, contributing to atmospheric carbon dioxide accumulation.

Plants and Human Civilization

No other organism group has shaped human civilization as fundamentally as plants. Human settlements became possible when people began cultivating plants — grains (wheat, rice, corn), legumes (beans, lentils, soybeans), roots and tubers (potatoes, cassava, yams), and fruits and vegetables. Agriculture made food surpluses possible, which enabled cities, specialization of labor, and the development of complex societies.

Connection to Ecology — Chapter 27 and 28

Plants do not merely participate in ecosystems — they largely create them. The physical structure of a forest, grassland, or wetland is defined by its plant community. These ecosystems provide what ecologists call ecosystem services: flood prevention, water filtration, climate regulation, soil building, pollination, and carbon storage. Chapter 21 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 21.1Plants are multicellular, photosynthetic eukaryotes with cellulose cell walls, chloroplasts, and an alternation of generations life cycle. Major groups — nonvascular plants, seedless vascular plants, gymnosperms, and angiosperms — represent successive evolutionary innovations in water transport, structural support, and reproduction.
  • Sec 21.2Plant cell types include parenchyma (metabolism and storage), collenchyma (flexible support), sclerenchyma (rigid support), xylem cells (water conduction, dead at maturity), phloem cells (sugar transport, living), and guard cells (stomatal regulation). These are organized into dermal, ground, and vascular tissue systems.
  • Sec 21.3Roots anchor the plant, absorb water and minerals, and store nutrients. Root zones (division, elongation, maturation) and root hairs (massively increasing surface area) serve these functions. Taproot and fibrous root systems represent different structural strategies. Modified roots include prop roots, storage roots, and nitrogen-fixing nodule-bearing roots.
  • Sec 21.4Stems support and connect, conducting materials between roots and leaves. Monocots have scattered vascular bundles; eudicots have bundles in a ring. Primary growth lengthens the plant from apical meristems; secondary growth increases girth through lateral meristems, producing wood in most gymnosperms and eudicot trees. Many stems are modified for storage, spread, or photosynthesis.
  • Sec 21.5Leaves maximize photosynthetic surface area while cuticle and regulated stomata limit water loss. Palisade mesophyll (dense chloroplasts) carries out most photosynthesis; spongy mesophyll facilitates gas diffusion. Guard cells regulate the stomatal trade-off: open stomata allow CO₂ in but also allow water out. Leaf diversity reflects adaptation to different environments.
  • Sec 21.6Water and minerals move upward through xylem by transpiration-cohesion-tension — evaporation at leaves creates tension that pulls a cohesive water column upward from roots. The Casparian strip in the root endodermis controls what enters the vascular system. Sugars move through phloem by pressure flow from sources (mature leaves) to sinks (growing tissues, roots, fruits) in both directions.
  • Sec 21.7Plants reproduce sexually through alternation of generations. In flowering plants, pollen grains (male gametophytes) germinate on the stigma and grow a pollen tube to the ovule. Double fertilization produces both the diploid embryo and the triploid endosperm. Coevolution between flowers and pollinators has produced extraordinary diversity in flower form, color, and fragrance.
  • Sec 21.8Seeds contain an embryo, endosperm, and seed coat; they protect and nourish the embryo and allow dormancy and dispersal. Fruits develop from ovary walls and enclose seeds; their structure — fleshy, winged, hooked, or explosive — reflects their dispersal mechanism. All structures developing from a fertilized ovary are botanically fruits, regardless of culinary usage.
  • Sec 21.9Plant hormones coordinate responses to environmental cues: auxin drives phototropism and apical dominance; cytokinins promote cell division; gibberellins promote elongation and germination; abscisic acid signals drought stress and closes stomata; ethylene promotes ripening and senescence. Tropisms (phototropism, gravitropism, thigmotropism) and photoperiodism (day-length flowering response) allow plants to orient growth and time reproduction.
  • Sec 21.10Plants are the primary producers of most terrestrial ecosystems, fix carbon, cycle nutrients, stabilize soil, and produce atmospheric oxygen. They underpin human agriculture, medicine, fiber, timber, and fuel. Plant-dominated ecosystems provide ecosystem services — water filtration, climate regulation, flood control, and biodiversity habitat — essential to human welfare.

Key Terms Review

abscisic acid (ABA)
A plant hormone that promotes stomatal closure during water stress and maintains seed dormancy.
alternation of generations
A plant life cycle alternating between diploid sporophyte and haploid gametophyte stages.
angiosperm
A flowering plant that produces seeds enclosed within fruit; the most diverse plant group.
apical meristem
A region of cell division at the tip of a root or shoot; responsible for primary (lengthening) growth.
auxin
A plant hormone produced in shoot tips that promotes cell elongation and drives phototropism and apical dominance.
Casparian strip
A waxy band in the walls of root endodermal cells that forces water through cell interiors, allowing selective control of what enters the vascular system.
cohesion
The attraction of water molecules to one another; allows a continuous water column to be maintained in xylem.
cuticle
A waxy coating on leaf and stem epidermis that reduces water loss by evaporation.
double fertilization
A process unique to flowering plants in which one sperm fertilizes the egg (producing an embryo) and another fuses with other cells (producing endosperm).
endodermis
A layer of root cells with a waxy Casparian strip that controls water and solute entry into the vascular cylinder.
endosperm
Nutrient tissue produced during double fertilization in flowering plants; nourishes the developing embryo and seedling.
ethylene
A gaseous plant hormone that promotes fruit ripening, leaf and flower senescence, and responses to mechanical stress.
fruit
A mature ovary that contains seeds and aids in their dispersal; includes all structures developing from a fertilized ovary.
gametophyte
The haploid stage of the plant life cycle that produces gametes; microscopic and dependent on the sporophyte in seed plants.
gibberellin
A plant hormone that promotes stem elongation, seed germination, and flowering; used commercially to increase fruit size.
gravitropism
Directional plant growth in response to gravity; roots grow downward (positive) and shoots grow upward (negative).
guard cell
One of a pair of cells flanking a stoma; changes in turgor pressure regulate stomatal opening and closing.
gymnosperm
A seed plant whose seeds are not enclosed in fruit; includes conifers, cycads, and ginkgos.
lateral meristem
Meristematic tissue responsible for secondary (girth) growth; includes the vascular cambium and cork cambium.
meristem
A region of undifferentiated plant cells capable of continuous division; the source of all new plant growth.
mesophyll
The photosynthetic ground tissue of a leaf; includes palisade (densely chloroplast-packed) and spongy (air-spaced) layers.
parenchyma
The most common plant cell type, living at maturity; carries out photosynthesis, storage, and general metabolic functions.
phloem
Vascular tissue that transports dissolved sugars and other organic molecules from sources to sinks in both directions.
photoperiodism
The response of a plant to the relative lengths of day and night; often regulates the timing of flowering.
phototropism
Directional plant growth in response to light; shoots grow toward light, driven by unequal auxin distribution.
pollen tube
A tube produced by a germinating pollen grain that carries two sperm through the style to the ovule.
pressure flow
The mechanism of phloem transport: high sugar concentration at sources creates osmotic pressure that pushes solution toward sinks with lower pressure.
primary growth
Growth that lengthens the plant, originating from apical meristems at root and shoot tips.
root cap
A protective mass of cells covering the root apical meristem; continuously replaced as the root pushes through soil.
root hair
A thin extension of a root epidermal cell that dramatically increases surface area for water and mineral absorption.
secondary growth
Growth that increases the girth of roots and stems, originating from lateral meristems; produces wood and bark.
seed
A structure containing a plant embryo, endosperm, and protective seed coat; an adaptation enabling dormancy and dispersal.
sporophyte
The diploid stage of the plant life cycle that produces spores by meiosis; the large visible plant in most species.
stoma (stomata)
A pore in leaf or stem epidermis through which gas exchange occurs; regulated by guard cells.
thigmotropism
Directional plant growth in response to touch or mechanical contact; causes tendrils to wrap around supports.
transpiration
The evaporation of water from plant surfaces, primarily through stomata; drives upward water movement through xylem.
transpiration-cohesion-tension
The mechanism driving water movement upward through xylem: evaporation at leaves creates tension that pulls a cohesive water column from roots to leaves.
tropism
A directional growth response to an environmental stimulus such as light, gravity, or touch.
vascular cambium
A lateral meristem that produces secondary xylem (wood) inward and secondary phloem outward.
vascular tissue
The internal transport system of plants; includes xylem (water and minerals) and phloem (sugars).
xylem
Vascular tissue that conducts water and dissolved minerals from roots to the rest of the plant; cells are dead at functional maturity.

Common Misconceptions

Misconception — Plants breathe in only CO₂ and breathe out only O₂

Correction: Plants perform cellular respiration continuously, consuming oxygen and releasing carbon dioxide just as animals do. During daylight, the rate of photosynthesis in green cells exceeds respiration, so the net exchange is CO₂ uptake and O₂ release. But at night — or in non-photosynthetic tissues such as roots — plants consume oxygen and release carbon dioxide.

Misconception — Plants absorb food from the soil through their roots

Correction: Plants make their own food through photosynthesis. Roots absorb water and dissolved minerals — not organic food molecules. The sugars that fuel plant growth are produced in leaves and distributed through the plant via phloem. This is why plants are called producers: they produce their own organic matter.

Misconception — Flowers are primarily for the plant's beauty

Correction: Flowers are reproductive structures. Their colors, shapes, fragrances, and structures are adaptations that attract specific pollinators and facilitate pollen transfer. Their visual appeal to humans is incidental to their biological function — a happy coincidence of pollinator and human sensory preferences.

Misconception — Larger leaves always mean more photosynthesis

Correction: The rate of photosynthesis depends on light, water, carbon dioxide, temperature, and the amount of photosynthetic tissue — not simply on leaf size. Desert plants with small or reduced leaves minimize water loss at the cost of photosynthetic surface area, which is adaptive where water rather than light is the limiting resource.

Misconception — Short-day plants flower because days are short

Correction: "Short-day plants" actually respond to the length of the uninterrupted dark period each night, not to day length itself. A long-night plant would be a more accurate name. A single brief flash of light in the middle of an otherwise long night can prevent flowering in these plants — demonstrating that it is darkness, not day length, that matters.

Chapter 23 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

You are made of trillions of cells. A single red blood cell, a neuron stretching from your spine to your toe, a muscle fiber in your heart, a bone-building cell buried in your femur — each is a living unit carrying out its own chemistry. Yet somehow, all of these cells work together. They coordinate. They communicate. They maintain conditions inside your body that remain remarkably stable even as the world around you changes constantly.

How does that happen? How does a collection of microscopic cells become a person who can run, think, digest a meal, fight an infection, and regulate body temperature — all at the same time? The answer lies in organization. Living cells are not scattered randomly. They are arranged into tissues, tissues into organs, organs into systems, and systems into a whole organism capable of sustaining life. Each level of organization adds new capabilities that individual cells alone cannot achieve.

This chapter introduces that organization. It explains how the human body is structured from the inside out, how different tissues and organs serve different functions, and how the body uses feedback loops to maintain the internal balance that living systems require. Every chapter in Part V builds on these ideas.

Opening Question

Your body temperature right now is close to 37°C. In an hour, whether you sit still or run a mile, it will still be close to 37°C. How does a biological system composed of billions of independent cells maintain such precise control over its own internal conditions — and what happens when that control breaks down? This chapter explains the mechanisms behind that remarkable stability.

Section 23.1

Levels of Organization in the Human Body

Biology studies life across many scales. A single molecule of glucose, a mitochondrion, a muscle cell, a bicep muscle, the muscular system, and a sprinting athlete — all are part of biology, and all are connected. Understanding any one level requires knowing how it relates to the levels above and below it. In the human body, six levels of organization stack on top of one another, each adding emergent capabilities that the level below cannot provide alone.

LevelDescriptionExample
Atoms & MoleculesThe chemical foundation; elements combine into biomolecules (carbohydrates, lipids, proteins, nucleic acids)Glucose, DNA, hemoglobin
CellsThe basic units of life; hundreds of specialized types shaped for particular jobsRed blood cell, neuron, muscle fiber
TissuesGroups of similar cells working together to perform a shared functionCardiac muscle tissue, nervous tissue
OrgansStructures of two or more tissue types working together; new functional capabilities emergeHeart, stomach, kidney
Organ SystemsGroups of organs collaborating on a major body functionCardiovascular system, digestive system
OrganismThe whole living person; survival depends on all lower levels functioning togetherYou
Key Idea — Emergent Function

At each level of organization, new capabilities appear that did not exist at the level below. A single muscle cell can contract, but it cannot move your arm. A muscle organ (bicep) coordinates thousands of fibers to produce directed force. A muscular system coordinates dozens of muscles to produce coordinated movement. Biology is not just about parts — it is about what those parts can do together.

Section 23.2

Tissues

A tissue is a group of cells with similar structure, origin, and function. The human body contains four fundamental tissue types. Everything in your body — every organ, every structure — is built from combinations of these four.

Epithelial Tissue

Epithelial tissue forms sheets of tightly packed cells that cover surfaces and line cavities. Your skin is epithelial tissue. So is the inner lining of your lungs, your intestines, your blood vessels, and your kidneys. Epithelial tissue also forms glands — structures that produce and secrete substances.

Epithelial cells are arranged in layers and are anchored to a non-cellular base called the basement membrane. Because they cover surfaces exposed to the outside world or to body fluids, epithelial cells experience considerable wear and are replaced rapidly. The cells lining your intestine, for example, are replaced every few days. Epithelial tissue performs many functions depending on location: protection (skin), absorption of nutrients (intestinal lining), secretion (glands), gas exchange (lung lining), filtration (kidney), and sensation (specialized sensory epithelium in the nose and inner ear).

Connective Tissue

Connective tissue is the most widespread tissue type in the body. Its defining feature is an extracellular matrix — a non-cellular material surrounding the cells, made of protein fibers and fluid or solid ground substance. Connective tissue connects, supports, and protects other tissues and organs. It comes in several distinct forms:

Dense connective tissueTightly packed, parallel collagen fibers; great tensile strengthTendons (muscle to bone), ligaments (bone to bone)

Muscle Tissue

Muscle tissue is specialized for contraction, containing proteins (primarily actin and myosin) that slide past each other to shorten the cell and produce force. The human body contains three types:

Muscle TypeLocationVoluntary?Striated?Key Feature
SkeletalAttached to bonesYesYesLong, multinucleate cells; produces movement of skeleton
CardiacHeart wallNoYesBranched cells with gap junctions; contracts rhythmically
SmoothWalls of hollow organs (digestive tract, blood vessels)NoNoSlow, sustained contractions; moves materials through organs

Nervous Tissue

Nervous tissue is specialized for generating and transmitting electrical signals. It consists of two cell types. Neurons are the signal-carrying cells — they receive input, integrate information, and send electrical signals (nerve impulses) rapidly across long distances. A single neuron can extend from the spinal cord all the way to a muscle in your foot. Glial cells support, protect, insulate, and nourish neurons. Far from being passive scaffolding, glial cells actively participate in brain function. Nervous tissue is found in the brain, spinal cord, and peripheral nerves throughout the body.

Figure 23.2 — Four Tissue Types. Illustrations of epithelial, connective, muscle, and nervous tissue labeled to show characteristic features: tight cell packing in epithelium; extracellular matrix in connective tissue; striations in skeletal and cardiac muscle; neuron cell body and long axon in nervous tissue.

The Unifying Feature of Connective Tissue

All connective tissues — loose, dense, cartilage, bone, blood, and adipose — share one defining feature: an extracellular matrix surrounding the cells. The composition and consistency of that matrix (fluid, gel, firm, or mineralized) determines the specific tissue's properties. Blood's matrix is liquid plasma; bone's matrix is calcified and rigid. Both are connective tissues.

Section 23.3

Organs

An organ is a structure composed of two or more tissue types that work together to perform a specific function. Organs represent a major jump in biological capability. A single tissue type has limits; an organ that combines tissues can accomplish things no single tissue could do alone.

Consider the stomach. Its inner surface is lined with epithelial tissue folded into deep pits, where cells secrete hydrochloric acid and digestive enzymes. The stomach wall contains three layers of smooth muscle oriented in different directions, allowing powerful churning of food in multiple axes. Nervous tissue in the stomach wall detects stretch and chemical conditions, sending signals that regulate digestion. Connective tissue holds everything together and carries blood vessels that supply the organ. No single tissue type could churn, secrete, sense, and supply a food-processing organ simultaneously.

Worked Example 23.3.1
Analyzing an organ by its tissue composition
  • OrganThe skin — the largest organ in the human body.
  • EpithelialThe epidermis (outer layer) is stratified epithelium. Provides waterproofing, protection from pathogens and UV radiation, and contains cells that produce melanin pigment. Outer cells are dead and filled with the protein keratin.
  • ConnectiveThe dermis (beneath the epidermis) is dense and loose connective tissue containing collagen and elastin fibers, which give skin strength and elasticity. The dermis also contains hair follicles, sweat glands, oil glands, and blood vessels.
  • NervousSensory nerve endings throughout the dermis detect touch, pressure, temperature, and pain. This sensory function is essential for detecting danger and maintaining homeostasis.
  • ConclusionThe skin's functions — protection, temperature regulation, sensation, vitamin D synthesis, and fluid retention — emerge from the integration of all three tissue types working together.
Principle: An organ's function is always a product of its tissue composition. Structure and function are inseparable at the organ level.

Section 23.4

Organ Systems

Organ systems are groups of organs that collaborate to carry out a major physiological function. No single organ, however capable, can manage a major body function alone. The heart pumps blood, but blood needs vessels to travel through, lungs to exchange gases, and kidneys to filter it. Body functions require teams of organs.

System Boundaries Are Artificial

The boundaries between organ systems are somewhat artificial. The nervous and endocrine systems both regulate and coordinate body functions — they are so intertwined that physiologists often call them the neuroendocrine system. The lymphatic system overlaps substantially with the immune system. When studying each system individually in Chapters 24–26, remember that these are conceptual divisions of convenience, not true biological walls.

Section 23.5

Homeostasis in the Body

Homeostasis is the maintenance of a relatively stable internal environment despite changing external conditions. The prefix homeo- means "same" or "similar"; stasis means "standing still." The body never literally stands still — it is constantly adjusting. But the adjustments keep key variables within ranges that cells can tolerate.

Why does this matter? Because cells are chemically sensitive. Enzymes work best within specific temperature and pH ranges. Neurons fire correctly only when ion concentrations across their membranes are properly maintained. Proteins fold and function correctly only within a narrow thermal range. If these variables drift too far from their set points, cells malfunction and the organism suffers.

VariableNormal RangeConsequences if Too LowConsequences if Too High
Body temperature~37°C (98.6°F)Chemical reactions slow; hypothermiaEnzymes denature; hyperthermia
Blood glucose70–100 mg/dL (fasting)Cells starve; brain function impairedBlood vessel damage over time
Blood pH7.35–7.45Acidosis; enzyme failure; cardiac arrhythmiaAlkalosis; muscle spasms; seizures
Blood pressure~120/80 mmHgOrgans underperfused; faintingVessel damage; heart strain; stroke
Blood oxygen95–100% saturationHypoxia; organ damage; deathOxidative cell damage (less common)
Homeostasis is Active, Not Passive

Homeostasis is not a passive condition — the body does not simply stay stable by doing nothing. It actively monitors variables, detects deviations, and responds with corrections. This active process requires a surveillance and response system: the feedback loop. Think of homeostasis as the goal, and feedback loops as the mechanism.

Section 23.6

Feedback Loops

A feedback loop is a regulatory circuit in which a change in a monitored variable triggers a response that acts back on that variable. Every feedback loop has three essential components:

Three Components of Every Feedback Loop

Receptor (sensor) — detects the value of a variable and sends information to a control center.

Control center — receives the signal, compares it to a set point, and determines the appropriate response.

Effector — carries out the response. Effectors are typically muscles or glands.

Information flow: receptor → control center → effector → change in variable → receptor again. The loop continues as long as the variable remains off its set point.

Analogy — The Thermostat System

A home thermostat monitors air temperature (receptor function), compares it to the set temperature (control center function), and switches the heater on or off (effector function). The body uses the same logic — but with dozens of variables monitored simultaneously, often by the same organ system, and with responses measured in milliseconds rather than minutes.

Section 23.7

Negative Feedback

Negative feedback is a feedback loop in which the response opposes or reverses the original change. The response moves in the opposite direction from the deviation. Negative feedback is the dominant regulatory mechanism in the human body — it is the primary mechanism of homeostasis.

The word "negative" does not mean harmful. It means that the response is in the negative (opposing) direction relative to the change. If the variable rises, the response drives it down. If the variable falls, the response drives it up. The result: the variable oscillates around a set point rather than drifting uncontrollably.

Worked Example 23.7.1
Body temperature regulation — negative feedback
  • StimulusBody temperature rises above 37°C during exercise.
  • ReceptorThermoreceptors in the skin and hypothalamus detect the temperature increase.
  • ControlThe hypothalamus receives the signal and activates cooling responses.
  • EffectorsSweat glands produce sweat that evaporates and cools skin. Blood vessels near the skin surface dilate, bringing warm blood closer to the surface so heat radiates away.
  • ResultBody cools back toward 37°C. As temperature falls, the stimulus decreases, the hypothalamus reduces its signal, and sweating and dilation diminish.
  • Negative?Yes — the response (cooling) opposes the original change (warming). The loop resists the deviation and restores the set point.
The same logic operates in reverse when you are cold: temperature drops → hypothalamus activates warming (shivering, vasoconstriction) → temperature rises → stimulus decreases → warming responses diminish.
Worked Example 23.7.2
Blood glucose regulation — negative feedback
  • After a mealBlood glucose rises. Beta cells in the pancreas detect rising glucose and secrete the hormone insulin. Liver, muscle, and fat cells absorb glucose. Blood glucose falls back toward the set point; insulin secretion decreases.
  • Between mealsBlood glucose falls. Alpha cells in the pancreas detect low glucose and secrete glucagon. Glucagon signals the liver to break down stored glycogen and release glucose back into the blood. Blood glucose rises; glucagon secretion decreases.
  • Negative?Yes — in both cases, the response opposes the deviation. Rising glucose triggers insulin (which lowers it); falling glucose triggers glucagon (which raises it).
Result: Blood glucose fluctuates after meals and during fasting but does not spiral out of control in either direction. Negative feedback keeps it within a functional range.

Figure 23.5 — Negative Feedback: Blood Glucose. Diagram showing two opposing loops: (1) high glucose → beta cells → insulin → glucose uptake → glucose falls → less insulin. (2) Low glucose → alpha cells → glucagon → glycogen breakdown → glucose rises → less glucagon. Arrows emphasize that responses oppose deviations.

Section 23.8

Positive Feedback

Positive feedback is a feedback loop in which the response amplifies or intensifies the original change, pushing the variable further in the same direction rather than restoring it toward a set point. Positive feedback is not used for stable homeostatic regulation. Instead, it is used in situations where the body benefits from a rapid, self-amplifying response that continues until a defined end point is reached, then shuts off completely.

Worked Example 23.8.1
Blood clotting — positive feedback
  • StimulusA blood vessel is damaged; platelets begin to gather at the wound.
  • AmplificationAs platelets gather, they release chemical signals that attract more platelets and activate clotting factors. Those activated factors activate still more — a cascade where each step amplifies the next.
  • End PointThe clot forms and seals the wound. Once bleeding stops, the stimulus (vessel damage) is resolved and the cascade halts.
  • Why positive?If clotting used negative feedback, the response would slow itself before completing — exactly the wrong outcome for a wound that needs sealing. Positive feedback is essential here.
Positive feedback is appropriate because the goal is not a stable set point — it is complete sealing of the vessel, a defined end point that terminates the loop.
Worked Example 23.8.2
Childbirth contractions — positive feedback
  • StimulusThe baby's head presses against the cervix; pressure receptors send signals to the hypothalamus.
  • AmplificationThe hypothalamus releases oxytocin → uterine contractions → baby pressed further against cervix → more pressure → more oxytocin → stronger contractions.
  • End PointBirth. Once the baby is delivered, the cervical pressure is removed and the loop ends immediately.
Like blood clotting, labor uses positive feedback to rapidly complete an all-or-nothing event. A gradual, self-limiting process would not produce the sustained, escalating force needed for delivery.
FeatureNegative FeedbackPositive Feedback
Response directionOpposes the original changeAmplifies the original change
OutcomeStabilizes the variable at a set pointDrives the variable toward a defined end point
Primary roleHomeostasis; everyday regulationRapid completion of events; all-or-nothing responses
Common examplesTemperature, blood glucose, blood pressure, blood pHBlood clotting, labor contractions, some hormonal surges
End point required?No — set point is ongoing targetYes — without a termination point, the loop spirals
Positive Feedback Without an End Point

Positive feedback loops must have a defined end point that terminates the cycle. Without termination, a positive feedback loop could spiral without limit — which occurs in certain pathological conditions: uncontrolled hemorrhage (clotting factors consumed faster than produced), runaway fever, or cardiac arrhythmias from cascading electrical signals. The end point is what makes positive feedback functional rather than destructive.

Section 23.9

Body Systems Working Together

No organ system operates in isolation. Every system depends on others for materials, signals, structural support, or waste removal. Understanding this interconnection is as important as understanding each system individually. Consider what happens when you start running.

Case Study — Running
How every organ system responds to a single physical event
  • MuscularIncreases workload, demanding more oxygen and glucose, and producing more CO₂ and heat as byproducts.
  • CardiovascularHeart beats faster and harder. Blood vessels in muscles dilate; vessels in less active areas (digestive tract) constrict slightly, redirecting blood to where it is needed most.
  • RespiratoryBreathing rate and depth increase to bring more oxygen into the lungs and expel rising CO₂.
  • NervousSensory neurons detect oxygen levels, CO₂ levels, muscle stretch, and blood pressure. Motor neurons increase heart rate, adjust breathing, and coordinate muscle contraction patterns.
  • EndocrineEpinephrine (adrenaline) from adrenal glands amplifies cardiovascular and muscular responses, mobilizes stored energy, and prepares the body for sustained effort.
  • IntegumentarySweat glands open to dissipate heat generated by muscle contraction.
  • ExcretoryKidney filtration adjusts to manage fluid balance as sweating and increased breathing alter water content.
A single act of running mobilizes every major organ system in coordinated fashion. Homeostasis here is not a passive steady state — it is an active, continuously adjusted balance maintained by systems that communicate and collaborate in real time.

Section 23.10

Health as System Balance

Understanding the body as an integrated system of feedback-regulated tissues and organs reframes how we think about health and disease. In biological terms, health is the condition in which organ systems are functioning within normal ranges, feedback loops are operating effectively, and the body is successfully maintaining homeostasis across its monitored variables. Disease often represents a failure of homeostasis — or a challenge that overwhelms the body's regulatory mechanisms.

Disease as Homeostatic Failure — Four Cases
Connecting feedback loop concepts to real clinical conditions
  • Type 1 DiabetesInsulin-producing beta cells are destroyed. Without insulin, the blood glucose negative feedback loop is broken. Glucose cannot be driven down after meals; it accumulates. Consequences cascade: glucose damages blood vessels; cells metabolize fats and proteins instead; chemical byproducts acidify the blood. One broken link in a feedback loop produces system-wide consequences.
  • HypertensionChronically high blood pressure — the cardiovascular pressure regulation loop cannot maintain a healthy set point. Contributing factors include kidney disease, hormonal imbalance, arterial stiffness, and excess sodium. Over time, damage accumulates in blood vessels, heart, kidneys, and brain.
  • FeverWhen pathogens invade, immune cells release molecules signaling the hypothalamus to raise the temperature set point. The body mounts responses (shivering, vasoconstriction, seeking warmth) to reach the new, higher set point. At 38–39°C, many bacteria grow less effectively and immune responses operate more vigorously. Fever is homeostasis — at a temporarily elevated set point, with a biological purpose.
  • AgingA gradual decline in regulatory precision. Feedback loops become slower, less sensitive, and less accurate. The elderly thermoregulate less effectively, heal more slowly, and recover from illness more gradually — reflecting accumulated cellular damage and reduced communication between system components.
Understanding health through the lens of homeostasis and feedback regulation is the conceptual framework underlying medicine, pharmacology, and many personal health decisions.
Why Lifestyle Matters — A Homeostatic Explanation

Exercise improves cardiovascular health because it trains the heart, blood vessels, and regulatory systems to function more efficiently. A healthy diet provides raw materials for cellular repair, enzyme function, and homeostatic regulation. Sleep is when many restorative and regulatory processes occur — hormonal cycling, immune function, tissue repair. Biology is not a list of facts; it is the study of systems, and every lifestyle factor that promotes health does so through its effects on biological regulation.

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 23.1The human body is organized across six hierarchical levels — atoms and molecules, cells, tissues, organs, organ systems, and the whole organism. Each level adds emergent capabilities that the level below cannot provide alone.
  • Sec 23.2Four fundamental tissue types build every structure in the body. Epithelial tissue covers surfaces and forms glands. Connective tissue supports and connects, defined by its extracellular matrix. Muscle tissue contracts in three specialized forms (skeletal, cardiac, smooth). Nervous tissue generates and transmits electrical signals via neurons and glial cells.
  • Sec 23.3Organs are structures of two or more tissue types working together. Their function is always a product of their tissue composition — structure and function are inseparable at the organ level. No single tissue type could accomplish what an organ does.
  • Sec 23.4The human body contains eleven major organ systems, each a group of organs collaborating on a major physiological task. System boundaries are somewhat artificial; every system depends on others. No system functions in isolation.
  • Sec 23.5Homeostasis is the active maintenance of a stable internal environment despite changing conditions. Key variables — temperature, blood glucose, blood pressure, pH, water balance — are continuously monitored and adjusted. Homeostasis is dynamic regulation, not a fixed frozen state.
  • Sec 23.6Feedback loops are the regulatory circuits of homeostasis. Every loop includes three components: a receptor that detects the variable, a control center that processes the signal and determines a response, and an effector that carries out the response.
  • Sec 23.7Negative feedback opposes deviations and restores set points — this is the dominant regulatory mechanism in the body. Body temperature and blood glucose regulation are classic examples. "Negative" means opposing, not harmful.
  • Sec 23.8Positive feedback amplifies deviations toward a defined end point. It is used for rapid, self-completing processes like blood clotting and labor contractions. Positive feedback requires a termination point; without one, the loop spirals dangerously.
  • Sec 23.9Body systems work together constantly. A single event like exercise mobilizes the muscular, cardiovascular, respiratory, nervous, endocrine, integumentary, and excretory systems simultaneously. No system is independent.
  • Sec 23.10Health reflects successful homeostasis across systems. Disease often represents homeostatic failure or challenge — a broken feedback loop, a regulatory system overwhelmed, or a set point altered by disease. This framework underlies medicine, pharmacology, and health decisions.

Key Terms Review

adipose tissue
Connective tissue specialized for energy storage as fat; also provides insulation and cushioning of organs.
blood
A liquid connective tissue consisting of plasma (liquid matrix) and formed elements: red blood cells, white blood cells, and platelets.
cardiac muscle
Involuntary, striated muscle tissue forming the heart wall; electrically connected cells allow synchronized contraction.
cartilage
A firm, flexible connective tissue lacking blood vessels; found at bone ends, in the nose and ear, and between vertebrae.
connective tissue
The most widespread tissue type, defined by an extracellular matrix; includes loose, dense, cartilage, bone, blood, and adipose subtypes.
control center
The component of a feedback loop that receives information from the receptor, compares it to a set point, and directs a response.
effector
The muscle or gland that carries out the response in a feedback loop.
epithelial tissue
Tissue forming sheets that cover body surfaces and line internal cavities; performs protection, absorption, secretion, and gas exchange.
extracellular matrix
The non-cellular material surrounding cells in connective tissue, composed of protein fibers (such as collagen) and a ground substance.
feedback loop
A regulatory circuit in which a change in a variable triggers a response that feeds back on that variable.
glial cells
Non-neuronal cells in nervous tissue that support, protect, insulate, and nourish neurons; active participants in brain function.
homeostasis
The active maintenance of a relatively stable internal environment despite changing conditions; required for cell survival.
negative feedback
A feedback loop in which the response opposes the original change, restoring the variable toward a set point; the primary mechanism of homeostasis.
nervous tissue
Tissue specialized for generating and transmitting electrical signals; composed of neurons and glial cells.
neuron
A specialized cell of nervous tissue that receives, integrates, and transmits electrical signals.
organ
A structure composed of two or more tissue types working together to perform a specific function.
organ system
A group of organs that collaborate to carry out a major physiological function.
positive feedback
A feedback loop in which the response amplifies the original change, driving a variable toward an end point; used for rapid, self-completing events.
receptor (feedback)
The sensor component of a feedback loop; detects the value of a variable and sends information to the control center.
set point
The target value around which a variable is regulated in negative feedback.
skeletal muscle
Voluntary, striated muscle tissue attached to bones; responsible for body movement and posture.
smooth muscle
Involuntary, non-striated muscle tissue lining hollow organs; produces slow, sustained contractions.
tissue
A group of similar cells working together to perform a shared function; the level of organization between cells and organs.

Common Misconceptions

Misconception — Homeostasis means the body never changes

Misconception: Homeostasis means the body stays exactly the same at all times.

Correction: Homeostasis means the body actively maintains key variables within functional ranges — not that they never change. Blood glucose fluctuates throughout the day; body temperature varies slightly by time of day and activity level. Homeostasis is dynamic regulation, not a fixed frozen state. What homeostasis prevents is uncontrolled drift outside functional limits.

Misconception — Negative feedback is harmful

Misconception: "Negative" feedback means something bad is happening in the body.

Correction: In feedback terminology, "negative" means the response opposes the change — not that the process is harmful. Negative feedback is the primary mechanism of homeostasis and is essential for survival. Without it, body temperature, blood glucose, blood pressure, and pH would all drift uncontrollably. Negative feedback is the body doing exactly what it should.

Misconception — Positive feedback is always dangerous and pathological

Misconception: Positive feedback is a sign something has gone wrong.

Correction: Positive feedback is used in normal, healthy processes such as blood clotting and childbirth. What makes positive feedback functional rather than dangerous is a defined end point that terminates the loop once its purpose is achieved. Pathology arises when positive feedback lacks a termination mechanism — but the mechanism itself is a legitimate regulatory strategy.

Misconception — Organ systems work independently

Misconception: Each body system does its own job without depending on other systems.

Correction: No organ system operates in isolation. Every system exchanges signals, materials, structural support, or waste removal with other systems. The cardiovascular, respiratory, nervous, and endocrine systems in particular function as overlapping networks of mutual dependence. Studying them separately is a convenience — not a description of how the body actually works.

Misconception — Smooth muscle is voluntary

Misconception: Smooth muscle can be controlled consciously, like skeletal muscle.

Correction: Smooth muscle is involuntary — not under conscious control. The movement of food through the digestive tract, the dilation of blood vessels, and the emptying of the bladder all occur without conscious direction, mediated by the autonomic nervous system and local hormones. Unlike skeletal muscle, smooth muscle is also non-striated — it lacks the banding pattern visible in skeletal and cardiac muscle.

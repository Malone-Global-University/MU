Appendix A
Mathematics and Data Skills for Biology
Quantitative tools used throughout the course — ratios, percentages, scientific notation, graphing, probability, and data interpretation

Ratios and Percentages

A ratio compares two quantities of the same kind and can be written as a fraction, with a colon, or in words. In biology, ratios appear in genetics (3:1 offspring ratios), ecology (predator-to-prey counts), and physiology (surface-area-to-volume relationships).

A percentage expresses a quantity as a fraction of 100. To convert a ratio to a percentage, divide the part by the whole and multiply by 100. Example: if 45 out of 60 seeds germinate, the germination rate is (45 ÷ 60) × 100 = 75%.

Key Formulas
Percentage = (Part ÷ Whole) × 100 Part = (Percentage ÷ 100) × Whole Ratio A:B means A units for every B units

Scientific Notation

Very large and very small numbers appear constantly in biology — the number of cells in the human body (~37 trillion = 3.7 × 10¹³), the size of a red blood cell (~8 micrometers = 8 × 10⁻⁶ m), and the mass of a DNA nucleotide (~330 daltons). Scientific notation expresses a number as a value between 1 and 10 multiplied by a power of ten.

Graph Reading

Graphs in biology show relationships between variables over time, across conditions, or across populations. Before reading a graph: (1) identify the title; (2) read both axis labels and units; (3) note the scale of each axis; (4) identify what each line, bar, or data point represents; (5) then look for patterns, trends, peaks, and comparisons.

Basic Probability

Probability in biology is central to genetics. The probability of an event is the number of favorable outcomes divided by the total number of equally likely outcomes. Probabilities range from 0 (impossible) to 1 (certain) and can also be expressed as percentages.

When two events are independent, the probability that both occur equals the product of their individual probabilities. In genetics, the probability that a child inherits a specific allele from the father AND a specific allele from the mother equals P(allele from father) × P(allele from mother).

Probability Rules for Genetics
P(event) = favorable outcomes ÷ total equally likely outcomes P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B) [if A and B are independent] P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B) [if A and B are mutually exclusive]

Averages and Variation

The mean (average) is the sum of all values divided by the number of values. The range is the highest value minus the lowest. In biological data, knowing the mean and range together gives a much better picture than the mean alone — a population average height of 1.7 m means something very different if the range is 0.1 m versus 0.6 m.

Reading Tables

Tables organize data into rows and columns. To read a biology table: identify what the rows represent, identify what the columns represent, read column headers and units, then find specific values by locating the row and column intersection. Watch for footnotes indicating special conditions.

Interpreting Experimental Data

When examining experimental results: (1) identify the independent variable (what was changed), the dependent variable (what was measured), and the controlled variables (what was held constant); (2) look for a clear pattern in the data; (3) consider whether the result supports or contradicts the hypothesis; (4) identify any sources of error or confounding factors; (5) determine whether the results are general or specific to these conditions.

Key Habit

Never extract a conclusion from data without first checking whether the graph scale, sample size, and control condition have been reported. A result from 5 organisms may not generalize to a population of millions.

Biology Laboratory Reference
Safe and effective laboratory practice — equipment use, observation, experimental design, data recording, and specimen handling

Lab Safety

  • Wear safety goggles and a lab apron whenever working with chemicals, heat, or sharp instruments.
  • Never pipette by mouth. Use mechanical pipetting devices only.
  • Know the location of the eyewash station, fire extinguisher, and emergency exit before beginning any lab.
  • Wash hands thoroughly before and after every laboratory session.
  • Report all accidents, spills, and broken glass to the instructor immediately.
  • Do not eat, drink, or apply cosmetics in the laboratory.
  • Dispose of biological materials, chemicals, and sharps according to posted disposal procedures.
  • Long hair must be tied back; loose clothing and dangling jewelry must be secured.

Calculating Total Magnification
Total magnification = eyepiece magnification × objective magnification Example: 10× eyepiece × 40× objective = 400× total

Slide Preparation

  • Wet mount: Place a small sample on a clean slide, add one drop of water or stain, and lower a coverslip at a 45° angle to minimize air bubbles.
  • Staining: Common biological stains include iodine (starch and plant cells), methylene blue (animal cells), and crystal violet (bacteria). Apply stain at one edge of the coverslip and draw it through with paper towel at the opposite edge.
  • Cell size estimation: Estimate the diameter of the field of view at a known magnification, then count how many cells fit across, and divide.

Experimental Design

A well-designed biology experiment contains: a clearly stated hypothesis (testable prediction); an independent variable (one factor the investigator changes); a dependent variable (what is measured in response); controlled variables (all other factors held constant); a control group (receives no treatment, as a baseline); an experimental group (receives the treatment); and repeated trials or adequate sample size to account for natural variation.

Experimental Design Checklist

☐ One independent variable only

☐ Measurable dependent variable defined

☐ Control group identified

☐ Controlled variables listed

☐ Sample size adequate for the type of organism and variation expected

☐ Data recording plan prepared before starting

Graphing Biological Data

Convention: the independent variable goes on the x-axis (horizontal); the dependent variable goes on the y-axis (vertical). Always label both axes with the variable name and units. Use an appropriate scale that spreads data across most of the graph area. Give the graph a title that states the relationship being shown, not just the name of the experiment.

Responsible Specimen Handling

  • Handle living organisms with care; minimize stress and injury whenever possible.
  • Preserved specimens must be handled with gloves; fixatives contain hazardous chemicals.
  • Return organisms to appropriate environments at the end of observations whenever the protocol allows.
  • Do not release organisms collected in one region into another; this can introduce invasive species.
  • Follow all ethical guidelines for vertebrate animal use; most high school labs avoid procedures that cause pain or distress.

pH and Enzyme Activity

Most human enzymes work best near pH 7. Pepsin in the stomach is an exception, working best near pH 2. Changing the pH changes the charge on the enzyme's active site and can denature the enzyme permanently.

The Genetic Code — Codon Reference

A codon is a triplet of mRNA bases that codes for one amino acid or a stop signal. There are 64 possible codons and 20 amino acids, so most amino acids are coded by more than one codon (the code is redundant but not ambiguous — each codon codes for only one amino acid). AUG is the start codon and also codes for methionine. UAA, UAG, and UGA are stop codons.

Reading a Codon

To find what amino acid a codon specifies: read the first base (row), then the second base (column), then the third base (sub-row). Example: GUC → Valine (Val)

Punnett Square Setup

A Punnett square models the possible genotypes of offspring from a cross. The alleles of one parent are written across the top (one allele per column); the alleles of the other parent are written down the left side (one allele per row). Each box is filled by combining the column allele and the row allele. The result shows the probability of each genotype, not a guarantee for any individual offspring.

Evolution and Classification Reference
Natural selection, evidence for evolution, taxonomy, and phylogenetics — a reference for Chapters 17, 18, and 19

Natural Selection — Four Conditions

Required Conditions for Natural Selection

1. Variation: Individuals in a population differ from one another in heritable traits.

2. Heritability: At least some variation is passed from parents to offspring.

3. Overproduction: More offspring are produced than the environment can support.

4. Differential survival and reproduction: Individuals with advantageous traits are more likely to survive and reproduce, passing those traits to more offspring.

Binomial Nomenclature Rules

Scientific names are written in italics (or underlined in handwriting). The genus is capitalized; the species is lowercase. Example: Homo sapiens. After first use, the genus may be abbreviated: H. sapiens.

Reading Cladograms

A cladogram is a branching diagram that shows evolutionary relationships based on shared derived traits. Each branch point (node) represents a common ancestor shared by all organisms on branches stemming from it. Organisms that share a more recent common ancestor are more closely related. Cladograms do not show time or the amount of change — only the branching pattern of ancestry.

Homeostasis and Feedback Loops

Homeostasis is the maintenance of relatively stable internal conditions in spite of a changing external environment. Most homeostatic systems use negative feedback: when a variable moves away from its set point, a response is triggered that brings it back. Blood glucose regulation and body temperature regulation are classic examples.

Positive feedback amplifies a response rather than reversing it. It is used in processes that need to be completed quickly and completely, then switched off — childbirth contractions and blood clotting are biological examples.

Negative Feedback Loop
Stimulus → Receptor detects change → Control center processes signal → Effector responds → Variable returned toward set point → Receptor detects return → Response reduced or stopped

Ten Percent Rule

On average, only about 10% of the energy at one trophic level is available to the next. The other 90% is lost as heat (through metabolism) or in waste. This is why food chains rarely exceed four or five trophic levels, and why there is far more biomass at lower levels than at higher levels.

Part I — Foundations of Biology
Chapters 1 – 5

Chapter 1: What Biology Studies

Q1
Biology is the scientific study of life and living systems. It encompasses organisms, cells, heredity, evolution, ecology, and all the processes that characterize living things.
Q3
The unifying themes of biology include: cell theory (all life is made of cells), evolution by natural selection (all life shares common ancestry and changes over time), energy flow (living systems require and transform energy), information (DNA stores and transmits hereditary information), and homeostasis (living systems maintain internal balance).
Q5
Structure and function are related because the physical shape, composition, and organization of a biological structure determines what it can do. Example: the shape of an enzyme's active site determines which substrate it can bind.

Chapter 2: The Nature of Life

Q1
The eight characteristics of life are: cellular organization, metabolism, homeostasis, growth and development, reproduction and heredity, response to stimuli, adaptation, and evolution.
Q3
Viruses are considered to occupy the boundary of life because they contain genetic information and can reproduce, but only inside a host cell. They have no metabolism, no cellular organization, and cannot independently maintain homeostasis.

Chapter 3: Scientific Thinking

Q2
An observation is a direct record of what is perceived (what you see, measure, or detect). An inference is an interpretation or explanation of that observation based on reasoning and prior knowledge. Observations are the data; inferences are conclusions drawn from the data.
Q4
The independent variable is what the experimenter deliberately changes. The dependent variable is what is measured in response. Controlled variables are all other factors held constant to ensure that any change in the dependent variable is due only to the independent variable.
Q7
Correlation does not imply causation. Two variables may be correlated because one causes the other, because both are caused by a third factor, or by coincidence. Only a controlled experiment that manipulates one variable while holding others constant can establish causation.

Chapter 4: Chemistry of Life

Q1
Water's polarity causes hydrogen bonding, which gives it unusually high specific heat (resists temperature change), high heat of vaporization (cools through evaporation), cohesion (surface tension), and excellent solvent properties for ions and polar molecules.
Q3
A solution with pH 5 is 100 times more acidic than a solution with pH 7. The pH scale is logarithmic: each unit represents a 10-fold change in hydrogen ion concentration.

Chapter 5: Biomolecules and Water

Q1
The four major classes of biomolecules are carbohydrates, lipids, proteins, and nucleic acids. Carbohydrates provide energy and structural support; lipids form membranes and store energy; proteins carry out most cellular functions; nucleic acids store and transmit genetic information.
Q3
A monomer is a single subunit molecule; a polymer is a long chain of monomers linked together. Example: glucose (monomer) → starch (polymer). Polymerization involves dehydration reactions (water is removed); hydrolysis breaks polymers apart by adding water.
Part II — Cells and Energy
Chapters 6 – 11

Chapter 7: Cell Membranes and Transport

Q3
Osmosis is the diffusion of water specifically across a selectively permeable membrane. Water moves from the side with lower solute concentration (higher water concentration) to the side with higher solute concentration. If a cell is placed in a hypertonic solution, it loses water and shrinks (plasmolysis in plant cells). In a hypotonic solution, water enters and the cell swells.
Q5
Active transport differs from passive transport in two ways: active transport moves substances against their concentration gradient (from low to high concentration), and it requires energy (ATP). Passive transport moves substances down the gradient and requires no energy input.

Chapter 9: Photosynthesis

Q1
Overall equation: 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + light energy → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂. Carbon dioxide and water are the raw materials; glucose and oxygen are the products; light provides the energy.
Q3
The light-dependent reactions occur in the thylakoid membranes. They use light energy to split water, release oxygen as a byproduct, and produce ATP and NADPH. These energy carriers then power the Calvin cycle.

Chapter 10: Cellular Respiration

Q1
Overall equation: C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + ATP (energy). This is essentially the reverse of photosynthesis in terms of inputs and outputs, though the mechanism is completely different.
Q5
Fermentation allows glycolysis to continue when oxygen is absent. The purpose is to regenerate NAD⁺ from NADH so that glycolysis can keep producing ATP. In yeast, the byproduct is ethanol and CO₂; in animal muscle cells, the byproduct is lactate.

Chapter 11: Cell Growth and Division

Q1
Cells divide to allow growth, to replace damaged or worn-out cells, and in sexual reproduction to produce gametes. DNA must be replicated before division so each daughter cell receives a complete copy of the genome.
Q3
Cancer is the result of uncontrolled cell division caused by mutations in cell cycle regulatory genes. Tumor suppressor genes (which normally stop the cycle if something is wrong) may be inactivated; proto-oncogenes (which promote division) may be mutated into oncogenes that permanently activate division.
Part III — Genetics and Biological Information
Chapters 12 – 16

Chapter 12: DNA and Chromosomes

Q1
The Hershey-Chase experiment (1952) provided strong evidence that DNA, not protein, is the hereditary material. They showed that only the DNA from bacteriophage entered host bacteria and directed the production of new phages.
Q3
Base pairing rule for DNA: A pairs with T; G pairs with C. If one strand reads 5'-ATCGGA-3', the complementary strand reads 3'-TAGCCT-5'. In RNA transcription, T in the DNA template is read as A in mRNA (and DNA's A becomes U in mRNA).

Chapter 14: Mendelian Genetics

Q1
A cross between two heterozygotes (Aa × Aa) produces the genotypic ratio 1 AA : 2 Aa : 1 aa and the phenotypic ratio 3 dominant : 1 recessive. The 3:1 phenotypic ratio arises because both AA and Aa individuals show the dominant phenotype.
Q3
A test cross determines whether an organism showing the dominant phenotype is homozygous dominant (AA) or heterozygous (Aa). The unknown is crossed with a homozygous recessive (aa). If all offspring show the dominant phenotype, the unknown is likely AA. If approximately half show the recessive phenotype, the unknown is Aa.

Chapter 16: Mutation, Variation, and Biotechnology

Q1
A frameshift mutation (insertion or deletion of a non-multiple-of-three number of bases) shifts the reading frame of all codons downstream of the mutation, usually producing a completely non-functional protein. A point substitution changes only one amino acid (or may be silent if the new codon codes for the same amino acid).
Part IV — Evolution and Diversity
Chapters 17 – 22

Chapter 17: Evolution by Natural Selection

Q1
Four conditions required for natural selection: (1) variation — individuals differ in heritable traits; (2) heritability — traits are passed to offspring; (3) overproduction — more offspring are born than survive; (4) differential reproductive success — individuals with advantageous traits leave more offspring.
Q5
Individuals do not evolve — populations do. Natural selection acts on individuals (some survive and reproduce more), but the change in allele frequency that results occurs at the population level across generations.

Chapter 19: Classification and the Tree of Life

Q1
The three domains of life are Bacteria, Archaea, and Eukarya. Bacteria and Archaea are prokaryotes (no nucleus); Eukarya includes all organisms with membrane-bound nuclei (protists, fungi, plants, animals). Archaea are more closely related to Eukarya than to Bacteria.
Part V — Human Biology
Chapters 23 – 26

Chapter 24: Nervous, Endocrine, and Sensory Systems

Q1
An action potential is a brief reversal of electrical charge across a neuron's membrane, caused by the rapid flow of Na⁺ ions inward followed by K⁺ ions outward. It travels down the axon and triggers neurotransmitter release at the synapse.
Q3
The nervous system responds in milliseconds using electrical signals to specific targets. The endocrine system acts over seconds to hours using hormones carried in the blood to widespread targets. The two systems coordinate: the hypothalamus links them by controlling pituitary hormone release.

Chapter 25: Circulation, Respiration, and Immunity

Q3
Gas exchange in the lungs occurs by diffusion across the thin, moist alveolar walls. Oxygen moves from the air (high concentration) into the blood; carbon dioxide moves from the blood (high concentration) into the air. The large surface area and thin walls of alveoli maximize the rate of diffusion.
Q5
Vaccines work by introducing the immune system to a harmless antigen (or antigen fragment) before infection. The adaptive immune system mounts a primary response and forms memory B and T cells. If the real pathogen appears later, the memory response is rapid and strong enough to prevent disease.
Part VI — Ecology and Earth Systems
Chapters 27 – 28

Chapter 27: Populations, Communities, and Ecosystems

Q1
Carrying capacity is the maximum population size an environment can sustainably support. When a population exceeds carrying capacity, limiting factors (food, water, space, disease) increase mortality and decrease birth rates until the population falls back to or below K.
Q3
Ecological succession is the predictable change in species composition over time. Primary succession begins on bare rock with pioneer species (like lichens) that break down rock and begin soil formation. Secondary succession follows a disturbance that leaves soil intact; it proceeds faster because soil and seed banks remain.

Chapter 28: Energy Flow, Nutrient Cycles, and Human Impacts

Q1
Only about 10% of energy at one trophic level passes to the next. The other 90% is lost as heat through cellular respiration and in waste. This is why energy pyramids narrow steeply upward, why biomass decreases at higher trophic levels, and why food chains rarely exceed four to five links.
Q3
The carbon cycle is closely linked to both photosynthesis and respiration. Plants absorb CO₂ during photosynthesis, converting it to organic carbon. Respiration by organisms releases CO₂ back to the atmosphere. Burning fossil fuels releases carbon that was stored for millions of years, increasing atmospheric CO₂ concentrations rapidly.
Q5
Biodiversity supports ecosystem stability because more species means more functional redundancy — if one species declines, others can fill its role. Biodiversity also directly supports humans through food, medicine, clean water, air, and materials. Loss of biodiversity reduces these services and makes ecosystems more vulnerable to collapse.

High School Biology — Library Textbook Edition

This textbook is the biology volume of the MGU Science Series, designed to follow Physical Science and to serve students studying biology before, during, or after High School Chemistry. It does not assume advanced chemistry, calculus, or prior anatomy.

The series is built on the principle that understanding comes before procedure. Students who understand why a process works — why cells need ATP, why populations evolve, why homeostasis matters — are better prepared for advanced study, for making health decisions, for engaging with environmental issues, and for appreciating the living world around them.

The 28 chapters are grouped into six parts: Foundations of Biology (Chapters 1–5), Cells and Energy (Chapters 6–11), Genetics and Biological Information (Chapters 12–16), Evolution and Diversity of Life (Chapters 17–22), Human Biology and Homeostasis (Chapters 23–26), and Ecology and Earth Systems (Chapters 27–28). Appendices A through L support the chapters as a reference library students can return to throughout their studies.

Typography: Playfair Display (headings), Source Serif 4 (body), DM Mono (labels and data). The MGU Science Series also includes Physical Science, High School Chemistry, and High School Physics.

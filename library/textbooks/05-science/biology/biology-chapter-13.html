Chapter 13 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Every living cell carries a library of instructions written in DNA. But DNA stays locked in the nucleus. How does genetic information travel from a gene to a finished, working protein? This chapter answers that question step by step. You will learn how DNA is copied into RNA, how ribosomes read that message, and how a chain of amino acids folds into a protein that can do something useful — catalyze a reaction, carry oxygen, build a structure, or signal a response.

Understanding protein synthesis means understanding how information becomes biology. The connection between genes and traits runs directly through this chapter. By the time you finish, you will be able to trace a single gene from its nucleotide sequence all the way to a functional protein — and explain what can go wrong at every step.

Opening Question

A human liver cell and a human muscle cell contain identical DNA. Yet they look different, behave differently, and carry out completely different functions. If the DNA is the same, how can the cells be so different? By the end of this chapter, you will understand how cells read the same instructions selectively — using some genes, silencing others — and why that selective reading is the key to all biological diversity within a single organism.

Section 13.1

From Genes to Traits

A gene is a segment of DNA that contains instructions for building a protein. Proteins are the working molecules of the cell. They act as enzymes, structural materials, transport molecules, signals, and regulators. When a gene is active, the instructions it carries are first copied into RNA and then used to build a specific protein. That protein then does something in the cell or body that we may observe as a trait.

The path from gene to trait is indirect. DNA does not directly build proteins. Instead, there is a two-step process: first, the information in a gene is transcribed into a molecule called messenger RNA; second, the message in that RNA is translated into a sequence of amino acids that form a protein. The protein then folds into its functional shape and carries out its biological role.

This sequence — DNA to RNA to protein — is so central to biology that it has a name: the central dogma of molecular biology. Francis Crick proposed it in 1958, and it remains one of the most important organizing principles in all of science. The central dogma describes the direction information normally flows in living systems.

The Central Dogma of Molecular Biology

DNA → RNA → Protein

DNA stores the information. RNA carries the message. Protein does the work. Information flows from DNA outward. It does not normally flow back from protein to DNA.

A useful analogy: imagine DNA as a master recipe book stored in a library archive. The library does not let you take the original book home. Instead, a librarian makes a handwritten copy of the recipe you need and gives you that copy. You take the copy to the kitchen and follow it to make the dish. In this analogy, the original recipe book is DNA, the handwritten copy is messenger RNA, and the finished dish is the protein.

Why does the cell use RNA as an intermediate? DNA is precious and must be protected. Having an RNA copy allows the cell to produce many copies of the same protein from a single gene, and to control when and how much of each protein is made. This system also allows different cell types to use different parts of the same genome to build the specific proteins each cell type needs.

Connecting to Chapter 12

In Chapter 12, you learned that DNA is a double-stranded molecule with a specific base-pairing code: A pairs with T, and G pairs with C. Transcription uses this same base-pairing principle, but now the DNA strand is used as a template to build an RNA strand. The base-pairing rules carry over — with one important change you will see in Section 13.3.

Section 13.2

RNA and Its Roles

RNA stands for ribonucleic acid. Like DNA, RNA is made of nucleotides. But RNA differs from DNA in three important ways. First, RNA uses the sugar ribose instead of deoxyribose. Second, RNA uses the base uracil (U) where DNA uses thymine (T). Third, RNA is single-stranded rather than double-stranded.

These differences matter. Because RNA is single-stranded, it can fold into many three-dimensional shapes. Some of those shapes allow RNA molecules to perform specific jobs inside the cell. There are three major types of RNA involved in protein synthesis, each with a different role.

Type of RNAAbbreviationRole in Protein Synthesis
Messenger RNAmRNACarries the genetic message from the nucleus to the ribosome; sequence of codons determines the protein's amino acid sequence
Transfer RNAtRNADelivers the correct amino acid to the ribosome during translation; carries an anticodon on one end and an amino acid on the other
Ribosomal RNArRNACombines with ribosomal proteins to form the ribosome — the molecular machine where translation occurs

Messenger RNA (mRNA) is the molecular copy of a gene. It carries the code from the nucleus out to the cytoplasm where ribosomes can read it. Each mRNA molecule is a linear sequence of nucleotides, and every three nucleotides form a unit called a codon. Each codon corresponds to a specific amino acid or a stop signal.

Transfer RNA (tRNA) is a small, folded RNA molecule that acts as an adapter between the mRNA code and the amino acids that form proteins. One end of a tRNA carries an anticodon — a three-nucleotide sequence that pairs with a codon on the mRNA. The other end carries the specific amino acid that matches that codon. tRNA molecules are essential for the accuracy of protein synthesis.

Ribosomal RNA (rRNA) combines with ribosomal proteins to form the ribosome. The ribosome is the molecular machine that assembles proteins by reading mRNA and linking amino acids together. Ribosomes can be found free in the cytoplasm or attached to the rough endoplasmic reticulum.

FeatureDNARNA
SugarDeoxyriboseRibose
BasesA, T, G, CA, U, G, C (uracil replaces thymine)
StrandsDouble-strandedSingle-stranded
LocationMostly in nucleusNucleus and cytoplasm
RoleLong-term storage of genetic informationCarries and uses genetic information
Student Warning — Confusing the Three RNA Types

All three RNA types participate in protein synthesis, but they do different jobs. mRNA carries the message. tRNA delivers amino acids. rRNA is part of the ribosome structure. These are not interchangeable. A common error is to describe tRNA as "carrying the message" — it does not. It carries amino acids and matches them to the mRNA message.

Section 13.3

Transcription

Transcription is the process by which a segment of DNA is used as a template to build a complementary strand of mRNA. The word transcription means to copy, and that is exactly what happens: genetic information is copied from DNA into RNA language.

Transcription happens in the nucleus of eukaryotic cells, where the DNA is located. It involves an enzyme called RNA polymerase, which reads the DNA strand and builds the mRNA strand one nucleotide at a time.

Steps of Transcription

The Three Steps of Transcription
Initiation → Elongation → Termination
  • Step 1 — InitiationRNA polymerase binds to a specific region of DNA called the promoter — a sequence of nucleotides that marks the beginning of a gene. When RNA polymerase binds, it separates the two strands of DNA, exposing the template strand.
  • Step 2 — ElongationRNA polymerase moves along the DNA template strand, reading it from 3′ to 5′ and building the mRNA strand in the 5′ to 3′ direction. Free RNA nucleotides pair with their complementary DNA nucleotides: A with U, T with A, G with C, C with G. As RNA polymerase adds each nucleotide, the mRNA grows longer.
  • Step 3 — TerminationRNA polymerase reaches a sequence in the DNA called the terminator. At this signal, RNA polymerase detaches from the DNA, and the newly made mRNA molecule is released. The DNA strands rejoin.
Base Pairing in Transcription

DNA template strand: 3′ — T A C G G A A T C G T A — 5′

mRNA strand: 5′ — A U G C C U U A G C A U — 3′

Notice: A in DNA becomes U in mRNA. All other base pairs follow normal rules (T→A, G→C, C→G).

In eukaryotic cells, the mRNA produced by transcription is not immediately ready to be used. It must be processed before leaving the nucleus. The cell removes non-coding segments called introns and splices together the coding segments called exons. The mRNA also receives a protective cap on its 5′ end and a poly-A tail on its 3′ end. These modifications protect the mRNA from being broken down and help it be recognized by ribosomes. The mature mRNA then travels through nuclear pores into the cytoplasm, where translation will occur.

Memory Tip — Introns vs. Exons

Introns are interruptions — non-coding sequences that are cut out. Exons are the expressed sequences — the parts that remain in the final mRNA and are used to build the protein. Introns are removed; exons are kept.

Section 13.4

The Genetic Code

The genetic code is the set of rules that connects codons to amino acids. A codon is a sequence of three consecutive nucleotides in mRNA. Because there are four possible nucleotides (A, U, G, C) and codons are three nucleotides long, there are 4 × 4 × 4 = 64 possible codons.

There are only 20 amino acids used to build proteins, so 64 codons is more than enough. This means the genetic code is redundant: most amino acids are coded for by more than one codon. For example, both UUA and UUG code for the amino acid leucine. This redundancy provides a degree of protection against mutations.

Three codons — UAA, UAG, and UGA — do not code for any amino acid. They are called stop codons because they signal the ribosome to end translation. One codon, AUG, has two roles: it codes for the amino acid methionine and it also serves as the start codon, the signal that begins translation. Nearly every protein begins with methionine, though this first amino acid is often removed after the protein is assembled.

Key Features of the Genetic Code

· The code is read in triplets (codons) from mRNA

· 64 codons encode 20 amino acids plus stop signals

· The code is redundant — multiple codons can specify the same amino acid

· AUG is the start codon and codes for methionine

· UAA, UAG, and UGA are stop codons — they end translation

· The genetic code is nearly universal — the same codons mean the same amino acids in almost every organism on Earth

Connection to Evolution

The near-universality of the genetic code is one of the strongest pieces of evidence for common ancestry. From bacteria to oak trees to humans, the same AUG means the same amino acid. This shared code tells us that all living things trace back to common ancestors that used this same molecular system billions of years ago. The code was established so early in life's history that it has been conserved ever since.

Section 13.5

Translation

Translation is the process by which a ribosome reads the codons on an mRNA strand and assembles a corresponding chain of amino acids. The word translation reflects the idea that information is being translated from one molecular language (the nucleotide language of RNA) into another (the amino acid language of proteins).

Translation takes place at ribosomes in the cytoplasm. Three types of molecules cooperate: the mRNA provides the sequence information, tRNA molecules deliver amino acids, and the ribosome catalyzes the peptide bonds that link amino acids together.

The Three Steps of Translation
Initiation → Elongation → Termination
  • Step 1 — InitiationThe ribosome assembles around the mRNA near the start codon (AUG). A tRNA carrying methionine (anticodon 3′–UAC–5′) binds to the AUG codon. The ribosome is now positioned and ready to begin.
  • Step 2 — ElongationThe ribosome moves along the mRNA, reading each codon in turn. For each codon, a tRNA with the matching anticodon carries the corresponding amino acid into the ribosome. The ribosome forms a peptide bond between the new amino acid and the growing chain. The tRNA then releases and leaves. This cycle — read, deliver, bond, release, advance — repeats for each codon.
  • Step 3 — TerminationWhen the ribosome reaches a stop codon (UAA, UAG, or UGA), no tRNA matches it. Instead, a protein called a release factor binds to the stop codon. The ribosome releases the completed polypeptide chain. The ribosome disassembles from the mRNA.
Translation at a Glance

mRNA codon: 5′ — AUG — UUU — GGC — UAA — 3′

tRNA anticodon: UAC AAA CCG (stop — no tRNA)

Amino acid: Met Phe Gly (chain released)

The result is a three-amino-acid polypeptide: Met–Phe–Gly

Once a ribosome begins translating an mRNA, other ribosomes can attach and begin translating the same mRNA simultaneously. A cluster of ribosomes all translating the same mRNA at the same time is called a polyribosome (or polysome). This allows the cell to produce many copies of the same protein quickly from a single mRNA molecule.

Transcription vs. Translation — A Comparison

Transcription makes mRNA from DNA, happens in the nucleus, and uses RNA polymerase. Translation makes a polypeptide from mRNA, happens in the cytoplasm at ribosomes, and uses tRNA, rRNA, and ribosomal proteins. Both involve reading a template and building a new molecule one unit at a time.

Section 13.6

Codons and Amino Acids

The genetic code table matches each codon to its amino acid. Being able to read a codon table is an important biology skill. The table is organized by the first, second, and third bases of the codon. To find what amino acid a codon specifies, locate the first base on the left, then the second base across the top, and then the third base on the right.

The order of codons in the mRNA directly determines the order of amino acids in the protein. Change one nucleotide in a codon, and you may change the amino acid at that position — or you may not, if the new codon codes for the same amino acid. This is why the redundancy of the genetic code provides partial protection against small mutations.

Section 13.7

Proteins and Biological Function

A protein begins as a linear chain of amino acids called a polypeptide. The sequence of amino acids is determined by the mRNA sequence, which is determined by the DNA sequence of the gene. But a chain of amino acids is not yet a functional protein. The chain folds into a specific three-dimensional shape, and that shape determines what the protein can do. Change the shape, and you change or destroy the function.

Levels of Protein Structure

LevelDescriptionExample
PrimaryThe linear sequence of amino acids; determined directly by the geneMet–Phe–Gly–Ala–…
SecondaryLocal folding patterns — alpha helices and beta sheets — held by hydrogen bondsCoiled sections in keratin
TertiaryThe overall 3-D shape of a single polypeptide chain; produced by interactions among amino acid side groupsGlobular shape of myoglobin
QuaternaryArrangement of two or more polypeptide chains together; not all proteins have this levelHemoglobin (4 chains)

Proteins serve an enormous range of biological functions. Enzymes are proteins that catalyze chemical reactions. Structural proteins such as collagen and keratin provide support in skin, hair, nails, and connective tissue. Transport proteins such as hemoglobin carry molecules through the body. Receptor proteins on cell surfaces receive signals from hormones and other molecules. Antibodies are proteins that recognize and mark foreign substances for destruction. Motor proteins are involved in muscle contraction and cellular movement.

One Gene, One Protein, Many Consequences

The gene for insulin codes for a single protein, but that one protein regulates blood sugar throughout the entire body. The gene for hemoglobin codes for a protein that carries oxygen in every red blood cell. Mutations in either gene can have serious consequences. The connection between genes, proteins, and biological function is direct and consequential — which is why understanding protein synthesis is fundamental to understanding disease.

Section 13.8

Gene Expression

Not every gene is active in every cell all the time. A human liver cell and a human muscle cell both contain the same DNA, but they look different, function differently, and build different sets of proteins. This is because different genes are expressed in different cell types. Gene expression refers to whether a gene is being transcribed into RNA and whether that RNA is being translated into protein.

Cells regulate gene expression in several ways. Transcription factors are proteins that bind to DNA near a gene and either promote or block RNA polymerase from beginning transcription. When a transcription factor promotes transcription, the gene is switched on. When transcription is blocked, the gene is switched off. This is the most common and important level of gene regulation.

Gene regulation is also influenced by the chemical environment of the cell. Signals from hormones, nutrients, stress, neighboring cells, and the external environment can activate or deactivate transcription factors, changing which genes are expressed. This is how a single cell type can respond differently to different situations: a liver cell processes alcohol differently than it processes sugar, because different genes are switched on or off depending on what molecules are present.

Cells also regulate gene expression after transcription. Some mRNA molecules are more stable than others and survive longer before being broken down, allowing more protein to be produced. Cells can also regulate whether ribosomes begin translating a particular mRNA. And even after a protein is built, cells can modify it, activate it, or target it for destruction. Gene expression involves control at many stages, not just at the DNA.

Section 13.9

Mistakes in Protein Synthesis

Protein synthesis is remarkably accurate, but errors do occur. Mistakes can happen during transcription (a wrong nucleotide is added to the mRNA) or during translation (a wrong amino acid is delivered). The consequences of a mistake depend on where it occurs in the protein and what change it causes.

Error TypeWhat HappensConsequence
Silent errorA codon changes but still specifies the same amino acid (due to redundancy)The protein is unchanged; no effect
Missense errorA codon changes to specify a different amino acidThe protein has one wrong amino acid; effect varies — may be mild or severe depending on the location
Nonsense errorA codon changes to a stop codon prematurelyTranslation ends early; truncated protein that is usually nonfunctional
Case Study
Sickle Cell Disease — One Amino Acid Change, Major Consequences

Hemoglobin is the protein in red blood cells that carries oxygen. It consists of four polypeptide chains. In the beta-globin chain, the sixth amino acid is normally glutamic acid (Glu), which is negatively charged and water-attracted.

In sickle cell disease, a single nucleotide change in the beta-globin gene converts the codon GAG to GUG. In the mRNA, the corresponding codon changes from GAG (Glu) to GUG (Val). Valine is uncharged and water-repelled. This single amino acid swap causes the hemoglobin molecules to clump together under low-oxygen conditions. The clumped hemoglobin deforms red blood cells into a rigid sickle shape. These misshapen cells cannot carry oxygen effectively and can block small blood vessels, causing pain and tissue damage.

The sickle cell case illustrates one amino acid change — out of hundreds — fundamentally altering a protein's behavior. It also illustrates how a missense mutation in a structural gene can cascade into a systemic disease affecting the entire body.

Section 13.10

Gene Expression in Health and Disease

The connection between gene expression and health is direct. Many diseases involve proteins that are absent, malformed, overproduced, or underproduced. Understanding protein synthesis allows scientists and clinicians to identify the molecular cause of a disease and sometimes to design treatments that address it.

Genetic Disorders

Some genetic disorders result from mutations in a single gene that causes a nonfunctional or missing protein. Cystic fibrosis is caused by mutations in the gene encoding a chloride channel protein in lung and digestive cells. Without this functional protein, mucus builds up in the lungs. Phenylketonuria (PKU) results from a missing enzyme that processes the amino acid phenylalanine; without treatment, the buildup damages brain development.

Cancer and Uncontrolled Gene Expression

Cancer often involves genes that regulate cell division. Proto-oncogenes are normal genes that promote controlled cell growth. When mutations cause them to be permanently switched on, they become oncogenes that drive uncontrolled division. Tumor suppressor genes normally halt cell division when needed; mutations that disable them can allow damaged cells to keep dividing. Understanding these regulatory failures helps scientists develop targeted cancer therapies.

Infectious Disease

Many bacteria cause disease by secreting proteins that damage host tissues. Viruses hijack the cell's own protein synthesis machinery to produce viral proteins, turning the infected cell into a factory for more viruses. Antibiotics work in part by targeting bacterial ribosomes or other components of protein synthesis that differ enough from human ribosomes to allow selective disruption.

Biotechnology Applications

Understanding protein synthesis has enabled biotechnology. Scientists can insert a human gene into bacterial cells and cause those bacteria to produce the human protein. Insulin for diabetes treatment, growth hormone, and clotting factors for hemophilia are all now produced this way. Gene therapy attempts to correct missing or nonfunctional genes by inserting working copies into a patient's cells. mRNA vaccines, like some COVID-19 vaccines, deliver mRNA molecules that instruct cells to produce a specific protein that triggers an immune response.

mRNA Vaccines and the Central Dogma

mRNA vaccines deliver messenger RNA into cells. Ribosomes translate that mRNA and produce a piece of a viral protein — such as the spike protein of SARS-CoV-2. The immune system recognizes this protein and builds a response. If the person later encounters the real virus, the immune system is already prepared. The mRNA from the vaccine is broken down within days and does not enter the nucleus or interact with DNA. No permanent change is made to the cell's genetic material.

Chapter 13 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 13.1Genes are DNA sequences that code for proteins. Information flows from DNA to RNA to protein — the central dogma. DNA does not directly make proteins; RNA serves as an intermediate messenger, allowing controlled, selective protein production.
  • Sec 13.2RNA differs from DNA in using ribose, uracil, and a single strand. The three types of RNA in protein synthesis are mRNA (carries the message), tRNA (delivers amino acids), and rRNA (forms the ribosome). A codon is a three-nucleotide unit on mRNA; an anticodon on tRNA matches it.
  • Sec 13.3Transcription uses RNA polymerase to build mRNA from a DNA template in three stages: initiation at the promoter, elongation, and termination. In eukaryotes, the pre-mRNA is processed — introns removed, exons joined, cap and poly-A tail added — before leaving the nucleus.
  • Sec 13.4The genetic code uses 64 codons to specify 20 amino acids and three stop signals. The code is redundant, with most amino acids specified by more than one codon. AUG is the start codon. The code is nearly universal across all life, providing strong evidence for common ancestry.
  • Sec 13.5Translation occurs at ribosomes in three stages: initiation at AUG, elongation as each codon is read and an amino acid is added, and termination at a stop codon. mRNA, tRNA, and rRNA all participate. Polyribosomes allow multiple proteins to be made from one mRNA.
  • Sec 13.6Reading a codon table connects mRNA sequences to amino acid sequences. The order of codons in mRNA directly determines the order of amino acids in the protein. Redundancy provides partial protection — many single-nucleotide changes produce the same amino acid.
  • Sec 13.7A polypeptide chain folds into a protein with primary, secondary, tertiary, and sometimes quaternary structure. Shape determines function. Proteins serve as enzymes, structural materials, transport molecules, receptors, antibodies, and motor molecules.
  • Sec 13.8Gene expression is regulated at multiple levels: transcriptional control by transcription factors, RNA processing, mRNA stability, translational control, and post-translational modification. Different cell types express different genes despite carrying identical DNA.
  • Sec 13.9Errors in protein synthesis can be silent (no change), missense (different amino acid), or nonsense (premature stop). Even a single amino acid change can have severe consequences, as seen in sickle cell disease. Proofreading mechanisms catch most errors.
  • Sec 13.10Gene expression connects directly to health and disease. Genetic disorders arise from nonfunctional proteins. Cancer involves regulatory gene failures. Viruses exploit host protein synthesis. Biotechnology uses the central dogma to produce medicines, vaccines, and engineered proteins.

Key Terms Review

central dogma
The principle that biological information normally flows from DNA to RNA to protein — the foundational rule of molecular biology.
transcription
The process of copying a gene from DNA into mRNA using RNA polymerase, occurring in the nucleus.
translation
The process of reading mRNA codons at a ribosome to assemble a chain of amino acids into a polypeptide.
mRNA
Messenger RNA — carries the genetic message from DNA in the nucleus to ribosomes in the cytoplasm.
tRNA
Transfer RNA — delivers a specific amino acid to the ribosome; carries an anticodon that pairs with mRNA codons.
rRNA
Ribosomal RNA — combines with ribosomal proteins to form the ribosome, the site of translation.
codon
A sequence of three consecutive nucleotides in mRNA that specifies one amino acid or a stop signal during translation.
anticodon
The three-nucleotide sequence on a tRNA molecule that pairs with a complementary codon on mRNA.
genetic code
The set of rules connecting mRNA codons to the amino acids they specify; nearly universal across all living organisms.
start codon
The codon AUG, which signals the beginning of translation and codes for the amino acid methionine.
stop codon
The codons UAA, UAG, or UGA — they signal the end of translation; no tRNA matches them.
RNA polymerase
The enzyme that reads the DNA template strand and builds the mRNA strand during transcription.
promoter
A DNA sequence upstream of a gene that marks where RNA polymerase binds to begin transcription.
polypeptide
A chain of amino acids linked by peptide bonds; the primary product of translation before folding into a functional protein.
gene expression
Whether a gene is actively being transcribed and translated in a given cell at a given time.
transcription factor
A protein that binds near a gene and either promotes or blocks RNA polymerase from beginning transcription.
intron
A non-coding segment of pre-mRNA in eukaryotes that is removed during mRNA processing before translation.
exon
A coding segment of pre-mRNA that remains in the mature mRNA after processing and is used in translation.
missense mutation
A mutation that changes one amino acid codon to a different amino acid codon, producing a protein with one altered amino acid.
nonsense mutation
A mutation that converts an amino acid codon to a stop codon, causing translation to end prematurely and producing a truncated protein.

Protein Synthesis Flowchart

Trace the complete pathway from gene to functioning protein. Each step builds on the previous one.

1Gene in DNA — Specific nucleotide sequence on a chromosome in the nucleus; the original source of the instructions
2RNA Polymerase Binds to Promoter — Recognizes the start of the gene; DNA unwinds to expose the template strand
3Transcription — Elongation — mRNA is assembled complementary to the DNA template strand; uracil pairs with adenine where thymine would in DNA
4Transcription — Termination — RNA polymerase reaches the terminator; pre-mRNA is released; DNA strands rejoin
5mRNA Processing (eukaryotes) — Introns removed; exons joined; 5′ cap and poly-A tail added to protect the mRNA
6mRNA Exits the Nucleus — Mature mRNA travels through nuclear pores into the cytoplasm
7Ribosome Assembles at Start Codon — Small and large ribosomal subunits join around the mRNA at AUG; first tRNA (carrying Met) binds
8Translation — Elongation — tRNA molecules deliver amino acids matching each codon; ribosome forms peptide bonds linking amino acids together; polypeptide grows
9Translation — Termination — Stop codon reached; release factor binds; polypeptide chain released; ribosome disassembles
10Protein Folding — Polypeptide folds into its specific three-dimensional shape; determined by amino acid interactions
11Protein Function — Protein performs its biological role as enzyme, structural component, transport molecule, signal, antibody, or motor protein

Common Misconceptions

Misconception 1

Claim: DNA directly makes proteins.

Correction: DNA does not directly build proteins. There is a two-step process: transcription produces mRNA from a DNA template, and then translation uses mRNA to assemble a protein at a ribosome. DNA itself never leaves the nucleus in eukaryotic cells.

Misconception 2

Claim: Each gene codes for one and only one protein.

Correction: In eukaryotes, a single gene can produce more than one protein through alternative splicing — different combinations of exons are joined together, producing multiple different mRNA molecules and therefore multiple different proteins from the same gene. The human genome has around 20,000 genes but can produce far more than 20,000 proteins.

Misconception 3

Claim: A mutation always causes disease.

Correction: Most mutations have no effect on health. If a mutation occurs in a non-coding region of DNA, or if it is a silent mutation (the new codon specifies the same amino acid), the protein is unchanged. Even mutations that do change an amino acid may not affect protein function if the change occurs in a less critical region of the protein. Only a small fraction of mutations produce seriously nonfunctional proteins.

Misconception 4

Claim: mRNA vaccines change your DNA.

Correction: mRNA vaccines deliver messenger RNA, not DNA. The mRNA enters the cytoplasm and is translated by ribosomes. It never enters the nucleus and cannot interact with DNA. The mRNA from a vaccine is broken down by the cell within a few days, just like any other mRNA molecule. No permanent change is made to the cell's genetic material.

Chapter 11 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

You cannot visit Earth's interior. No drill has ever gone deeper than about 12 kilometers, and the distance to Earth's center is more than 6,300 kilometers. Between the thin shell you stand on and the planet's iron heart lies a world of layered rock and metal under temperatures and pressures that would crush and vaporize anything humans could send down. Yet scientists know a great deal about what lies beneath — its composition, its temperature, its structure, and even how it moves. That knowledge comes almost entirely from indirect evidence.

This chapter explains how Earth's interior is organized into layers of increasing density, what each layer is made of, how scientists discovered this structure without ever going there, and why the interior matters for everything at the surface — from earthquakes and volcanoes to the magnetic field that shields life from solar radiation and the slow movement of tectonic plates that reshapes continents over millions of years.

Opening Question

When an earthquake strikes somewhere in the world, seismograph stations everywhere on Earth detect the vibrations — even on the opposite side of the planet. But some seismographs detect certain kinds of waves while others do not. There are regions of Earth's surface where specific wave types never arrive, no matter how powerful the earthquake. Why would waves travel completely through a planet 12,700 kilometers in diameter but fail to reach certain parts of the surface? The answer to that question reveals the hidden structure of Earth's interior — including a liquid layer thousands of kilometers deep that you will never see but that shapes nearly everything about your planet.

Section 11.1

How Scientists Study Earth's Interior

If you wanted to understand what is inside a sealed container without opening it, you would need indirect methods. You might shake it and listen. You might weigh it carefully. You might use X-rays. Scientists face exactly this challenge when studying Earth's interior. The planet is far too large and far too hot to examine directly, so geoscientists have developed a suite of indirect methods that reveal the interior's structure with remarkable precision.

Seismic waves are the most powerful tool. When an earthquake or a large controlled explosion occurs, it sends waves of energy through Earth in all directions. Those waves travel through different materials at different speeds, and they bend, reflect, or stop when they encounter boundaries between layers. By recording how long seismic waves take to reach stations around the world, scientists can determine the size, depth, and physical properties of Earth's internal layers.

Meteorites provide a second line of evidence. Many meteorites formed from the same material as Earth's interior during the early solar system. Iron meteorites, for example, suggest that Earth's core is rich in iron and nickel, consistent with the density measurements obtained from seismic data.

Laboratory experiments allow researchers to compress and heat rock samples to the pressures and temperatures of Earth's deep interior. These experiments reveal how minerals behave, which phases are stable, and what physical properties to expect at depth.

Volcanic samples from deep-source eruptions carry fragments of mantle rock called xenoliths to the surface, where scientists can analyze their chemistry directly.

No single method provides a complete picture. Earth scientists combine seismic evidence, laboratory data, meteorite analysis, volcanic samples, gravity measurements, and magnetic field data into a model of the interior that has been tested, debated, and refined for more than a century.

Scientific Practice — Indirect Evidence

The inability to visit Earth's interior does not make knowledge about it unreliable. Many scientific conclusions rest on indirect evidence — the orbits of electrons, the structure of DNA, the distance to galaxies. What matters is whether multiple independent lines of evidence point consistently to the same answer. For Earth's interior, they do.

Section 11.2

Density and Layering

Earth is not uniform throughout. It is organized into concentric layers, with denser materials concentrated toward the center and less dense materials forming the outer layers. Understanding why requires thinking about how the planet formed.

Density is the amount of mass packed into a given volume. Iron and nickel — the metals that dominate Earth's core — are much denser than silicate rocks, which are in turn denser than water. When Earth formed roughly 4.5 billion years ago, much of the planet was molten. In this early liquid state, a process called differentiation occurred: denser materials, primarily iron and nickel, sank toward the center under the pull of gravity, while lighter silicate materials rose toward the surface. The result is the layered structure we observe today.

Differentiation also explains something that puzzled early geologists. If you calculate Earth's average density — about 5.5 g/cm³ — and compare it to the density of rocks at Earth's surface (roughly 2.7–3.0 g/cm³), there is a significant gap. Surface rocks are far too light to account for Earth's overall density. The heavy material must be hidden far below, in the core. Seismic data later confirmed this, revealing a core dominated by iron and nickel with a density exceeding 10 g/cm³.

Section 11.3

The Crust

The crust is the outermost solid layer of Earth — the layer you live on and the one most accessible to direct study. It is also the thinnest layer. If Earth were the size of a basketball, the crust would be about as thick as a single sheet of paper.

There are two fundamentally different types of crust, and they differ in thickness, density, age, and composition.

Continental crust underlies the continents and their shallow surrounding shelves. It ranges from about 25 to 70 kilometers thick, with the thickest sections beneath major mountain ranges. Continental crust is made primarily of granite and other silica- and aluminum-rich rocks — what geologists sometimes group under the term felsic rock. It is less dense than oceanic crust and, in many places, very old. Some continental crust is more than 4 billion years old, representing some of the oldest material preserved at Earth's surface.

Oceanic crust forms the floors of the ocean basins. It is thinner (typically 5–10 km) and denser than continental crust. Oceanic crust is composed primarily of basalt and gabbro — dark, iron- and magnesium-rich rocks that geologists call mafic. Because oceanic crust is denser, it sinks beneath continental crust when the two collide — a process called subduction that you will study in Chapter 12. Oceanic crust is also geologically young: most of it is less than 200 million years old, because it is continuously created at mid-ocean ridges and destroyed at subduction zones, recycled back into the mantle.

The boundary between the crust and the mantle below it is called the Mohorovičić discontinuity, or simply the Moho. It was discovered in 1909 by Croatian seismologist Andrija Mohorovičić, who noticed that seismic waves suddenly sped up at a certain depth during an earthquake in Zagreb. The speed increase indicated a sharp change in rock composition — from lighter crustal rock to denser mantle rock below. The Moho is found at roughly 5–10 km depth beneath oceans and 25–70 km depth beneath continents.

Section 11.4

The Mantle

The mantle is Earth's largest layer by volume, extending from the base of the crust down to about 2,900 kilometers. If you could slice Earth open, the mantle would make up about 84% of its volume. Despite being almost entirely solid rock, the mantle is not rigid in the way surface rock seems. Over geological timescales — thousands to millions of years — mantle rock can flow very slowly, like an extremely thick, hot solid that deforms under sustained pressure. This slow movement is critical to plate tectonics, volcanism, and the long-term cycling of Earth's materials.

The mantle is composed primarily of a dense, dark rock called peridotite, which is rich in the minerals olivine and pyroxene. These minerals contain more iron and magnesium than the granitic rocks of the crust. Peridotite brought to the surface by deep-source volcanoes (as xenoliths) gives scientists direct samples of mantle composition.

Scientists recognize several regions within the mantle based on their seismic properties. The upper mantle lies just below the Moho and extends to about 410 km depth. Its uppermost portion, combined with the crust above, forms the rigid lithosphere — the layer of tectonic plates. Below that, the asthenosphere (discussed in detail in Section 11.6) is a zone of slightly weaker rock that allows slow flow. The lower mantle extends from about 660 km to 2,900 km. Although hotter than the upper mantle, it is actually stronger: the enormous pressure at those depths prevents as much flow as occurs in the asthenosphere above. At the very base of the mantle, just above the outer core, a complex region called the D″ layer (D double-prime) shows unusual seismic characteristics. This zone may contain ancient oceanic crust that subducted hundreds of millions of years ago and sank all the way to the core-mantle boundary.

Concept Note — Solid but Flowing

How can the mantle be both solid and capable of flowing? Think of the difference between hitting a block of wax with a hammer (it shatters — it is brittle on short timescales) and leaving that same block in warm temperatures for a year (it slowly sags under its own weight). The mantle behaves like the wax over geological time. Over millions of years, even solid rock can flow slowly in response to sustained forces. This is not the same as melting — the rock remains solid throughout.

Section 11.5

The Outer Core and Inner Core

At a depth of about 2,900 km, Earth transitions abruptly from the rocky mantle to a very different type of material: the outer core, a layer of liquid metal approximately 2,250 km thick. The outer core is composed primarily of iron and nickel, with smaller amounts of lighter elements — possibly sulfur, oxygen, or silicon. The exact lighter-element composition remains an active research question.

The discovery that the outer core is liquid came from seismic evidence. A particular type of seismic wave — the S-wave — cannot travel through liquids. When scientists analyzed earthquake records and found that S-waves do not pass through Earth's deep interior, the conclusion was clear: the outer core must be liquid. This discovery is discussed further in Section 11.7.

The liquid state of the outer core has enormous consequences. The liquid iron circulates in large convection patterns, driven by heat rising from the inner core and shaped by Earth's rotation. Because iron is electrically conductive, these moving currents generate electric fields, which produce Earth's magnetic field — a process called the geodynamo, explained in detail in Section 11.9.

At about 5,150 km depth, the liquid outer core gives way to the inner core, a solid sphere roughly 2,440 km in diameter — approximately the size of the Moon. The inner core is composed primarily of iron and nickel. Its temperature is estimated at 5,000–6,000°C — comparable to the surface of the Sun — and yet it is solid. This seems paradoxical: the inner core is hotter than the outer core, so why is the outer core liquid while the inner core is solid?

The answer is pressure. As depth increases, so does the weight of all the material above — and this pressure raises the melting point of iron. At the extreme pressures of the inner core (approaching 360 gigapascals), iron requires enormously high temperatures to melt. The temperature at the inner core, while very high, is not high enough to melt iron at those pressures. So the inner core stays solid. The outer core, at slightly lower pressure, sits in a range where the temperature does exceed the melting point of iron — and so it remains liquid.

Worked Example 11.5.1
Reasoning about inner core state of matter
  • QuestionThe inner core is estimated to be hotter than the outer core. Why is the inner core solid while the outer core is liquid?
  • IdentifyThis appears contradictory: solid material that is hotter than liquid material nearby. The key variable must be something other than temperature alone.
  • ReasonThe state of matter — solid or liquid — depends on both temperature and pressure. Pressure raises the melting point of most materials, including iron. The inner core is under extreme pressure (estimated ~360 GPa), which raises iron's melting point well above the local temperature. The outer core is at lower pressure, so its temperature exceeds iron's melting point at that depth.
  • AnalogyThis is similar to how water can remain liquid above 100°C if placed under high pressure (as in a sealed pressure cooker). Pressure changes the temperature at which a substance changes phase.
  • ConclusionThe inner core is solid not because it is cooler, but because extreme pressure raises iron's melting point above the local temperature. The outer core, at lower pressure, is liquid because the temperature there exceeds the lower melting point of iron at that pressure.
Answer: Pressure — not temperature alone — determines the state of matter at depth. The inner core's extreme pressure keeps iron solid even at temperatures that would melt it near Earth's surface.

Section 11.6

Lithosphere and Asthenosphere

The layers described so far — crust, mantle, outer core, inner core — are defined primarily by composition: what they are made of. But Earth's interior can also be divided by mechanical behavior: how materials respond to stress over time. Two of the most important mechanical layers are the lithosphere and the asthenosphere.

The lithosphere is Earth's rigid outer shell. It includes the entire crust (both continental and oceanic) plus the uppermost portion of the mantle. The lithosphere is cold enough and stiff enough to behave as a brittle solid — when stressed, it tends to crack and break rather than bend slowly. It is typically about 100 km thick under ocean basins and up to 200 km thick under old, cold continental interiors. The lithosphere is broken into large pieces called tectonic plates, whose movement is the subject of Chapter 12.

The asthenosphere lies just below the lithosphere, extending from roughly 100 to 350 km depth within the upper mantle. The asthenosphere is neither liquid nor particularly soft in everyday terms — it is still mostly solid rock. But at the temperatures and pressures of that depth, the rock is weak enough to deform slowly under sustained stress. Given enough time (thousands to millions of years), asthenospheric rock flows like an extremely viscous fluid. This slow flow is what allows tectonic plates to move: the rigid lithosphere essentially slides over the more pliable asthenosphere below.

Key Distinction — Compositional vs. Mechanical Layers

The lithosphere and asthenosphere are not defined by composition. Both are made mostly of peridotite — the same mantle rock. What differs is mechanical behavior. The lithosphere is rigid and brittle. The asthenosphere is weak and capable of slow plastic flow. Temperature is the main factor: the lithosphere is cool enough to be rigid; the asthenosphere is warm enough to deform slowly.

Section 11.7

Seismic Waves as Evidence

The most powerful evidence for Earth's internal structure comes from seismic waves — vibrations that radiate outward through Earth from earthquakes, volcanic events, or controlled explosions. When a major earthquake occurs, seismograph stations around the world record the arriving waves. By comparing arrival times, wave patterns, and wave types at thousands of stations, scientists can reconstruct the path those waves took through Earth — and from those paths, deduce the interior's structure.

There are two main types of body waves (waves that travel through Earth's interior).

P-waves (primary waves, or compressional waves) move by alternately compressing and expanding the material they travel through, similar to the motion of a spring being pushed and pulled from one end. P-waves can travel through solids, liquids, and gases. They are the fastest seismic waves and always arrive at seismograph stations first.

S-waves (secondary waves, or shear waves) move by shaking material from side to side, perpendicular to the direction of travel — like shaking a rope or guitar string. S-waves can travel only through solids. They cannot pass through liquids because liquids have no resistance to shear stress. S-waves are slower than P-waves and always arrive at stations after P-waves from the same earthquake.

Shadow Zones — The Key Evidence

The most revealing observations involve regions where certain waves do not arrive at all.

The S-wave shadow zone is the region of Earth's surface — roughly beyond 103° of arc from the earthquake's origin — where S-waves never arrive directly. No matter how powerful the earthquake, seismograph stations in this zone receive no direct S-waves. The explanation: S-waves encounter the liquid outer core and stop, because liquids cannot support shear motion. The outer core casts a "shadow" where S-waves cannot penetrate. This shadow zone is one of the strongest pieces of evidence that Earth's outer core is liquid.

There is also a P-wave shadow zone, roughly between 103° and 142° from the earthquake, where P-wave arrivals are unexpectedly weak or absent. P-waves can travel through the liquid outer core, but the liquid causes them to refract (bend) sharply as they cross the mantle-core boundary. This bending steers P-waves away from certain parts of the surface, creating a shadow zone. Beyond 142°, P-waves arrive again — but later and from unexpected directions, because they have bent through the core and emerged on the far side. The exact geometry of these shadow zones precisely determines the depth of the core-mantle boundary at about 2,900 km.

Worked Example 11.7.1
Interpreting seismic wave arrivals
  • SituationA seismograph station located 120° from an earthquake receives P-waves but no S-waves. A station at 60° receives both P-waves and S-waves. What do these observations reveal about Earth's interior?
  • AnalyzeThe 60° station is within the range where both P-waves and S-waves travel normally through the mantle. Both types arrive, confirming the mantle is solid and supports both wave types.
  • AnalyzeThe 120° station is within the S-wave shadow zone but not in the P-wave shadow zone. P-waves arrive (having bent through the outer core). S-waves do not arrive — they were blocked by a liquid layer.
  • ConcludeThe data confirm that there is a liquid layer in Earth's deep interior — the outer core — that blocks S-waves but allows P-waves to pass through (though P-waves are bent in the process).
Answer: The absence of S-waves at 120° and their presence at 60° indicates that a liquid layer — the outer core — lies in the path between those two stations. S-waves cannot travel through liquid; P-waves can.

Section 11.8

Temperature and Pressure Inside Earth

As depth increases inside Earth, two conditions change dramatically: temperature rises and pressure increases. Both have profound effects on the behavior of Earth's materials.

Temperature increases with depth at a rate called the geothermal gradient. In the crust and uppermost mantle, temperature typically rises about 25–30°C for every kilometer of depth — though this varies significantly by location. Near active volcanoes or mid-ocean ridges, the gradient is steeper; in the interiors of old, cold continents, it is shallower. Deeper in the planet, the gradient is smaller per kilometer, but temperatures continue to climb. The core-mantle boundary is estimated at roughly 3,500–4,000°C. At Earth's center, the estimated temperature is approximately 5,000–6,000°C — comparable to the surface temperature of the Sun.

This heat comes from two sources. Primordial heat is energy left over from Earth's formation: the kinetic energy of infalling material during accretion and the heat released during differentiation. Radioactive decay provides a continuous source of new heat as unstable isotopes of uranium, thorium, and potassium break down in the mantle and crust, releasing energy over billions of years. Both sources contribute to Earth's internal heat budget, though their relative contributions are still debated.

Pressure increases with depth because of the cumulative weight of everything above. Pressure is measured in pascals (Pa) or, at Earth's interior scales, gigapascals (GPa). At Earth's surface, atmospheric pressure is about 0.0001 GPa — negligible compared to deep Earth. At the base of the crust, pressure may be around 1 GPa. At the core-mantle boundary, about 136 GPa. At Earth's center, the estimated pressure is approximately 360 GPa — more than 3.5 million times atmospheric pressure.

Worked Example 11.8.1
Calculating temperature at depth using the geothermal gradient
  • ProblemIf the geothermal gradient averages 27°C per kilometer in the upper crust, and the surface temperature is 15°C, what is the estimated temperature at 10 km depth?
  • IdentifySurface temperature: 15°C. Gradient: 27°C/km. Depth: 10 km.
  • Calculate Temperature increase = gradient × depth = 27°C/km × 10 km = 270°C Temperature at 10 km = surface + increase = 15°C + 270°C = 285°C
  • CheckThis is well above the boiling point of water but plausible for crustal rock at 10 km — consistent with what is measured in deep boreholes. ✓
  • NoteThis calculation assumes a constant gradient, which is an approximation. The gradient changes with depth, rock type, and location. At greater depths, the gradient decreases.
Answer: Approximately 285°C at 10 km depth, assuming a constant gradient of 27°C/km and a surface temperature of 15°C.

Section 11.9

Earth's Magnetic Field

Earth is surrounded by an invisible magnetic field that extends from deep inside the planet out into space, forming a protective bubble called the magnetosphere. This field deflects most of the charged particles streaming from the Sun — the solar wind — away from Earth's surface and atmosphere. Without it, solar wind would gradually strip away atmospheric gases and expose the surface to intense radiation. Mars, which lost much of its global magnetic field billions of years ago, likely lost most of its original thick atmosphere to this process.

The magnetic field is generated by the geodynamo: the circulation of electrically conductive liquid iron in the outer core. As the liquid iron moves — driven by convection currents from heat below and organized by Earth's rotation — it generates electric currents. Those currents produce a magnetic field. The magnetic field, in turn, influences how the conductive iron moves — creating a self-sustaining feedback system. The geodynamo requires the outer core to be both electrically conductive and in motion; a solid outer core would not sustain it.

Earth's magnetic field resembles, roughly, the field of a giant bar magnet tilted about 11° relative to Earth's rotational axis. Because of this tilt, magnetic north — where a compass needle points — is not the same as geographic north, which is the location of Earth's rotational pole. The angular difference between the two at any given location on Earth is called magnetic declination. Declination varies by location and changes slowly over decades as the magnetic field shifts. Accurate navigation requires knowing the local declination.

Magnetic Reversals and Paleomagnetism

Earth's magnetic field is not constant over geological time. Evidence preserved in rocks demonstrates that the field has reversed hundreds of times in the past — meaning what is now magnetic north has repeatedly shifted to become magnetic south, and vice versa. These are called geomagnetic reversals. They are not sudden events; reversals unfold over thousands of years, during which the field weakens, becomes disorganized, and then rebuilds in the opposite orientation. The most recent complete reversal — called the Brunhes-Matuyama reversal — occurred about 780,000 years ago.

The evidence for past reversals comes from paleomagnetism: the study of ancient magnetic fields recorded in rocks. When lava erupts and cools, magnetic minerals in the rock — primarily magnetite — align with the prevailing magnetic field before being locked in place as the rock solidifies. These minerals preserve a "fossil" record of the magnetic field's direction and strength at the time of cooling. By analyzing the paleomagnetic signatures of rocks of different ages and locations, scientists can reconstruct the history of field reversals.

Paleomagnetic data played a central role in confirming plate tectonics. When scientists mapped the seafloor in the mid-20th century, they discovered symmetric stripes of alternating normal and reversed magnetism extending away from mid-ocean ridges. These stripes — matching on both sides of each ridge — provided direct evidence that seafloor spreads outward from the ridges and that the magnetic field has reversed repeatedly over time. You will revisit this evidence in Chapter 12.

Section 11.10

Earth's Interior and Surface Change

Earth's interior is not passive scenery beneath the surface. It is an active engine that drives most of the large-scale processes shaping the planet — from the movement of continents to the composition of the atmosphere to the chemical conditions that sustain life.

Mantle convection is perhaps the most important interior-to-surface connection. Heat flowing from the deep mantle and core drives slow circulation of mantle rock: hot material rises, spreads horizontally at shallower depths, cools, and eventually sinks back down. This convection exerts forces on the lithosphere above it, dragging plates along and driving the movements of plate tectonics. Without internal heat, plate motion would cease, volcanoes would go dormant, and the geological processes that renew Earth's surface would stop.

Volcanism brings material from the mantle to the surface, releasing gases, building new rock, and supplying nutrients to soils. Over Earth's history, volcanic outgassing — the release of water vapor, carbon dioxide, nitrogen, and other gases from cooling magma — contributed to building Earth's oceans and atmosphere. The oceans and atmosphere we depend on are partly the product of Earth's deep interior exhaling over billions of years.

The magnetic field, sustained by the geodynamo, protects Earth's atmosphere from being eroded by solar wind. Mars, which has only a weak remnant magnetic field, has an atmosphere about 100 times thinner than Earth's. The magnetic field also provides a reference direction that organisms — including many birds, fish, and insects — use for navigation.

Isostasy is the principle that the lithosphere floats in gravitational balance on the asthenosphere, much as a block of wood floats on water. When mountain ranges build up, the crust thickens and sinks deeper into the mantle. As erosion wears mountains down over millions of years, the reduced load causes the crust to rise — a process called isostatic rebound. The same process applies to glaciers: when ice sheets grow, the crust beneath depresses; when ice melts, the crust slowly rebounds. Scandinavia and parts of Canada are still rebounding from the melting of ice sheets that ended roughly 10,000 years ago. You will encounter isostasy again in Chapter 15.

Understanding Earth's interior, then, is not separate from understanding surface processes, climate, ecosystems, or hazards. The interior and the surface are coupled through energy, matter, and time across all scales.

Systems Connection — Interior to Surface

Earth's interior influences the surface through multiple pathways: heat drives convection and plate motion; volcanism releases gases that build the atmosphere; the geodynamo shields the atmosphere from erosion; mantle flow causes isostatic adjustments. In the language of Earth systems, the geosphere's interior is an active driver of conditions in the geosphere's surface, hydrosphere, atmosphere, and even biosphere.

Chapter 11 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 11.1Earth's interior is studied almost entirely through indirect methods: seismic waves, meteorite analysis, laboratory experiments at high pressure, volcanic samples, and gravity and magnetic measurements. No direct sampling has penetrated beyond the crust.
  • Sec 11.2Earth is layered by density, a result of differentiation during its early molten state. Denser iron-nickel metals sank to the center; lighter silicate rocks rose to the surface. Earth's average density (~5.5 g/cm³) far exceeds surface rock density, confirming heavy material is concentrated in the interior.
  • Sec 11.3Continental crust is thicker (25–70 km), less dense (~2.7 g/cm³), and granite-rich; oceanic crust is thinner (5–10 km), denser (~3.0 g/cm³), and basalt-rich. The Moho — discovered seismically — marks the boundary between crust and mantle.
  • Sec 11.4The mantle (10–2,900 km) is composed mainly of peridotite and constitutes most of Earth's volume. Though solid, it can flow plastically over geological timescales. It is divided into the upper mantle (including the asthenosphere), the lower mantle, and the D″ layer at its base.
  • Sec 11.5The outer core (2,900–5,150 km) is liquid iron-nickel; its liquid state is confirmed by the inability of S-waves to pass through it. The inner core (5,150–6,370 km) is solid iron-nickel despite being hotter, because extreme pressure raises iron's melting point above the local temperature.
  • Sec 11.6The lithosphere (crust + uppermost rigid mantle, ~100–200 km thick) behaves as a brittle solid; the asthenosphere (~100–350 km depth) is weak enough to flow slowly. Both are mantle rock compositionally; they differ in mechanical behavior. Tectonic plates are lithospheric slabs moving over the asthenosphere.
  • Sec 11.7P-waves (compressional, travel through solids and liquids) and S-waves (shear, travel only through solids) are the two main body wave types. The S-wave shadow zone (beyond ~103°) confirms a liquid outer core. The P-wave shadow zone (103°–142°) results from refraction at the core-mantle boundary. Wave velocity changes reveal layer boundaries.
  • Sec 11.8Temperature increases with depth at a geothermal gradient of ~25–30°C/km near the surface, reaching ~5,000–6,000°C at the center. Pressure increases to ~360 GPa at the center. Heat comes from primordial formation energy and radioactive decay. Pressure controls the state of matter, keeping the inner core solid despite extreme temperature.
  • Sec 11.9The geodynamo — convection of liquid iron in the outer core — generates Earth's magnetic field, which deflects solar wind and protects the atmosphere. Magnetic north differs from geographic north by the magnetic declination angle. Geomagnetic reversals, recorded in rocks as paleomagnetism, have occurred hundreds of times; the last full reversal was ~780,000 years ago.
  • Sec 11.10Earth's interior drives surface change: mantle convection powers plate tectonics; volcanic outgassing built the atmosphere and oceans; the magnetic field shields against solar wind; isostasy governs the balance between crustal mass and mantle support. Interior and surface are coupled through energy, matter, and time.

Key Terms Review

Asthenosphere
The zone in the upper mantle (~100–350 km deep) where rock is weak enough to flow slowly; the layer on which lithospheric plates move.
Continental crust
Thicker (25–70 km), less dense (~2.7 g/cm³), granite-rich crust that underlies continents; geologically old and not subducted.
D″ layer
The complex transition zone at the base of the mantle just above the outer core; shows unusual seismic properties and may contain ancient subducted slabs.
Differentiation
The process during Earth's early molten phase in which denser materials (iron, nickel) sank to the center and lighter materials (silicates) rose, producing Earth's layered structure.
Geodynamo
The process by which convection of liquid iron in the outer core generates Earth's magnetic field through the interaction of motion and electrical conductivity.
Geothermal gradient
The rate at which temperature increases with depth inside Earth; approximately 25–30°C per kilometer in the upper crust.
Inner core
The solid iron-nickel sphere at Earth's center (~2,440 km diameter); solid despite extreme temperature because pressure raises iron's melting point above local temperature.
Isostasy
The gravitational balance between the lithosphere and asthenosphere; crust rises or sinks in response to changes in surface mass (erosion, glacial loading, mountain building).
Lithosphere
Earth's rigid outer shell comprising the crust and uppermost mantle (~100–200 km thick); broken into tectonic plates that move over the asthenosphere.
Magnetic declination
The angle between magnetic north and geographic north at a given location; varies by location and changes slowly over time.
Magnetic reversal
A change in Earth's magnetic field direction so that magnetic north and south switch positions; occurs over thousands of years and is recorded in rocks as paleomagnetism.
Mantle
The thick rocky layer (~30–2,900 km depth) between the crust and outer core; composed mainly of peridotite; capable of slow plastic flow over geological time.
Mohorovičić discontinuity
The seismic boundary between Earth's crust and mantle, identified by a sharp increase in seismic wave velocity; named for its discoverer, Andrija Mohorovičić.
Oceanic crust
Thinner (5–10 km), denser (~3.0 g/cm³), basalt-rich crust forming ocean floors; geologically young because it is created at ridges and destroyed at subduction zones.
Outer core
The liquid iron-nickel layer (~2,900–5,150 km depth) surrounding the inner core; its convective motion drives the geodynamo and generates Earth's magnetic field.
P-waves
Primary (compressional) seismic waves that travel through solids, liquids, and gases; faster than S-waves; compress and expand the material they pass through.
Paleomagnetism
The record of past magnetic field directions preserved in igneous rocks as magnetic minerals align with the field during cooling and are locked in place upon solidification.
Peridotite
The dense, dark rock making up most of the mantle; composed primarily of the minerals olivine and pyroxene; rich in iron and magnesium.
S-waves
Secondary (shear) seismic waves that travel only through solids; slower than P-waves; shake material perpendicular to the direction of travel; blocked by Earth's liquid outer core.
Seismograph
An instrument that detects, records, and measures seismic waves; networks of seismographs worldwide allow scientists to map Earth's interior structure.
Shadow zone
A region of Earth's surface where certain seismic waves do not arrive directly from an earthquake, because they have been blocked or refracted by layers in Earth's interior.
Solar wind
The stream of charged particles emitted by the Sun; deflected by Earth's magnetic field, protecting the atmosphere from erosion.

Common Misconceptions

Misconception 1 — The mantle is liquid magma
"The mantle is a layer of molten rock — a huge underground ocean of magma."
The mantle is almost entirely solid. It can flow slowly over millions of years — like an extremely viscous solid — but it is not liquid. Magma forms only in limited zones where specific conditions cause partial melting, typically near plate boundaries or hot spots. The liquid layer in Earth's interior is the outer core, not the mantle.
Misconception 2 — Earth's interior has been directly sampled to great depth
"Scientists have drilled into the mantle and taken direct samples."
The deepest borehole ever drilled — the Kola Superdeep Borehole in Russia — reached about 12 km, barely penetrating the crust. The Moho (crust-mantle boundary) lies 35 km or more beneath most continental surfaces. Everything scientists know about the mantle and core comes from indirect evidence, primarily seismic waves. Some mantle xenoliths (fragments carried up by volcanoes) provide limited direct samples from shallow mantle depths.
Misconception 3 — Magnetic north and geographic north are the same
"A compass always points to the North Pole."
Magnetic north is currently located in the Canadian Arctic, not at the geographic North Pole (Earth's rotational axis). The angular difference — magnetic declination — varies by location and changes slowly over decades as the magnetic field shifts. Precise navigation requires accounting for local declination.
Misconception 4 — Geomagnetic reversals are catastrophic sudden events
"When the magnetic field reverses, the poles flip overnight and cause mass extinctions."
Geomagnetic reversals are gradual processes unfolding over thousands of years. During a reversal, the field weakens, becomes disorganized, and slowly rebuilds in the opposite orientation. While a weakened field could increase radiation exposure at the surface, there is no compelling evidence that reversals have caused mass extinctions. Life has survived hundreds of reversals over Earth's history.
Misconception 5 — The inner core is solid because it is cooler than the outer core
"The inner core is solid because it is colder — it has given off its heat."
The inner core is actually hotter than the outer core, not cooler. It is solid because of extreme pressure — approximately 360 GPa at Earth's center — which raises iron's melting point well above the local temperature. The outer core is liquid not because it is hotter in absolute terms, but because the pressure there is lower, keeping iron's melting point below the local temperature.

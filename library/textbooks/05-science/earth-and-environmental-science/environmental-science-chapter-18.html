Chapter 18 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Stand at the edge of the ocean and the scale of what you are looking at is almost impossible to hold in your mind. The Pacific Ocean alone covers more area than all of Earth's land combined. The average depth of the world's oceans is about 3,800 meters — nearly four kilometers of water sitting above seafloor that most humans have never seen and that was largely unmapped until the mid-twentieth century.

But oceans are not just large. They are active. They absorb heat from the sun and redistribute it around the planet. They store carbon dioxide and release it back into the atmosphere. They drive weather systems, feed billions of people, support a staggering variety of life from microscopic plankton to blue whales, and shape the coastlines where much of humanity lives.

Oceans connect to every other Earth system. Changes in ocean temperature affect hurricanes. Changes in ocean chemistry affect coral reefs. Changes in ocean circulation affect rainfall patterns on land. Pollution carried by rivers eventually reaches the sea. Sea level rise driven by a warming climate threatens coastal cities around the world. In this chapter, you will learn the physical structure of ocean basins, the chemistry of seawater, the forces that move water as waves, tides, and currents, and the ecological systems that oceans support. You will also examine how human activity is changing the oceans and what those changes mean for Earth's systems as a whole.

Opening Question

The North Atlantic Ocean carries warm water from the tropics toward Europe — water that releases heat to the atmosphere and keeps northwestern European cities far warmer than their latitudes would otherwise allow. This circulation depends on cold, dense water sinking in the North Atlantic to replace the warm water flowing in. What happens if large amounts of freshwater from melting ice sheets dilute that cold water, making it less dense? Could a change in ocean chemistry in one place alter the climate of an entire continent? By the end of this chapter, you will be able to trace exactly how this process works — and why scientists are watching it carefully.

Section 18.1

Why Oceans Matter

Oceans Dominate Earth's Surface

About 71 percent of Earth's surface is covered by liquid water, and nearly all of that water is found in the oceans. Earth's oceans form a single, connected body of water conventionally divided into named regions: the Pacific, Atlantic, Indian, Southern, and Arctic Oceans. These divisions are geographic conveniences — the water itself moves continuously among them.

The sheer volume of ocean water — roughly 1.335 billion cubic kilometers — gives oceans an enormous capacity to store heat, dissolved gases, and dissolved materials. This storage capacity makes oceans one of the most important regulators of Earth's climate.

Oceans Regulate Climate

Water has a high specific heat capacity. This means it takes a large amount of energy to raise the temperature of water by even one degree Celsius, and water releases that stored energy slowly as it cools. Because oceans hold such vast amounts of water, they absorb solar energy during warm periods and release it slowly during cool periods. Coastal areas therefore experience more moderate temperatures than inland regions at the same latitude — warmer winters and cooler summers.

Oceans also move heat from the tropics toward the poles through surface currents and deep-water circulation. Without this heat transport, equatorial regions would be far hotter and polar regions far colder than they are today.

Oceans Are Part of the Carbon Cycle

Oceans absorb carbon dioxide from the atmosphere. Some of this CO₂ dissolves directly into seawater. Some is taken up by photosynthetic marine organisms — particularly phytoplankton — and incorporated into organic matter. When marine organisms die, their carbon-containing remains sink to the ocean floor, where much of it is stored in sediments for millions of years. This process, called the biological pump, moves carbon from the atmosphere to the deep ocean. Oceans currently absorb roughly 25 to 30 percent of the CO₂ that humans release each year, slowing atmospheric accumulation — but at a cost we will return to in Section 18.10.

Oceans in Earth Systems

Climate: Ocean water stores and redistributes heat, moderating temperature extremes worldwide.

Carbon cycle: Oceans absorb ~25–30% of annual human CO₂ emissions through dissolution and the biological pump.

Water cycle: The vast majority of global evaporation occurs at the ocean surface, supplying atmospheric moisture.

Oxygen: Marine phytoplankton produce roughly half of Earth's atmospheric oxygen through photosynthesis.

Food: Fisheries and aquaculture provide critical protein for billions of people worldwide.

Section 18.2

Ocean Basins and Seafloor Features

The Shape of the Seafloor

The seafloor is not flat. Like the land surface, it has mountain ranges, deep valleys, plains, and elevated plateaus. These features formed through plate tectonics, volcanic activity, erosion, and sediment deposition — the same processes that shape landscapes on land, operating under kilometers of water. Understanding seafloor topography required technology that did not exist until the twentieth century. Echo sounding, which measures the time it takes for sound pulses to bounce off the seafloor and return to the ship, allowed systematic mapping of ocean basins beginning in the 1920s and 1930s. Satellite altimetry later enabled near-complete global mapping by measuring tiny variations in sea surface height caused by the gravitational pull of underwater features.

Continental Margins

Where land meets ocean, the continent does not immediately drop away into the deep sea. Instead, a gently sloping underwater extension of the continent, called the continental shelf, extends from the shoreline beneath shallow water. Continental shelves are typically less than 200 meters deep and may extend hundreds of kilometers from shore before reaching the continental slope, where the seafloor drops much more steeply toward the deep ocean.

At the base of the continental slope, sediment accumulates in a gentler slope called the continental rise, formed by debris that slides and flows down from the slope above. Together, the shelf, slope, and rise make up the continental margin. Continental shelves are ecologically and economically important. Most marine fisheries operate there, where sunlight reaches the bottom and nutrients are abundant. Much of the world's offshore oil and natural gas is extracted from rocks beneath continental shelves.

The Deep Ocean Floor

Beyond the continental margin lies the abyssal plain, a nearly flat surface covered in fine sediment that has settled slowly from above over millions of years. The average depth of the abyssal plain is around 3,000 to 6,000 meters — among the flattest surfaces on Earth.

Rising from the abyssal plain are mid-ocean ridges — enormous underwater mountain ranges formed at divergent plate boundaries where new oceanic crust is created. The Mid-Atlantic Ridge runs nearly the full length of the Atlantic Ocean and is part of a continuous global ridge system more than 65,000 kilometers long. At the crest of mid-ocean ridges, volcanic activity and hydrothermal vents release heat and chemicals from Earth's interior into the ocean.

At convergent plate boundaries, oceanic crust plunges downward into the mantle in subduction, forming the deepest features on the ocean floor: ocean trenches. The Mariana Trench in the Pacific Ocean reaches about 11,000 meters — deep enough to submerge Mount Everest with more than two kilometers of water to spare. Scattered across the ocean floor are seamounts — underwater volcanoes that have not reached the surface — and oceanic islands such as Hawaii, which are the tops of volcanic mountains that have grown tall enough to break the surface.

Section 18.3

Salinity, Temperature, and Density

What Makes Seawater Salty

Salinity is a measure of the mass of dissolved salts in a given mass of seawater, typically expressed in grams per kilogram (g/kg) or parts per thousand (‰). Average ocean salinity is about 35 g/kg. The principal dissolved salt is sodium chloride, but seawater also contains magnesium, sulfate, calcium, potassium, and many other ions in smaller amounts.

Where evaporation is high and precipitation is low — as in the subtropics — salinity tends to be higher because water evaporates but salts remain behind. Where precipitation is heavy or rivers deliver large freshwater inputs, salinity is lower. The Baltic Sea, nearly enclosed and fed by many rivers, has a salinity of only about 10 g/kg. The Red Sea, nearly enclosed and very arid, exceeds 40 g/kg.

Salts come from two main sources: chemical weathering of rocks on land, which releases ions carried to the sea by rivers, and hydrothermal vents on the seafloor, which release minerals dissolved from oceanic crust. Salinity has remained relatively stable for hundreds of millions of years because ions are also removed through evaporation deposits, sediment incorporation, and hydrothermal reactions.

Ocean Temperature with Depth

At the surface, ocean temperatures range from below 0°C near the poles (seawater freezes at about −1.8°C due to dissolved salts) to above 30°C in tropical seas. Temperature decreases with depth. In tropical and subtropical waters, warm surface water sits above a sharp transition called the thermocline, below which temperature drops rapidly. Below the thermocline, water is cold — typically 2°C to 4°C — throughout the deep ocean regardless of latitude. In polar regions, the surface is already cold, so the thermocline is weak or absent.

Density and Its Consequences

The density of seawater is determined primarily by temperature and salinity. Cold water is denser than warm water. Saltier water is denser than fresher water. Density differences drive whether ocean water layers mix or remain separate. If surface water is less dense than the water below, it floats and the layers remain stratified. Strong stratification limits the mixing of nutrients from depth into the sunlit surface layer where photosynthesis occurs. If surface water becomes denser — by cooling or increasing salinity — it sinks, driving vertical circulation and bringing deep-water nutrients toward the surface.

The densest ocean water is found in the deep ocean: cold and moderately salty. Water near the poles that is cold and not freshened by meltwater tends to sink and fill the deep ocean basins.

Section 18.4

Waves

What Waves Are and How They Form

A wave is the transfer of energy through a medium. Ocean waves carry energy across the water's surface, but in open water, the water itself does not move forward with the wave. Instead, water particles move in roughly circular orbits, returning close to their starting point as each wave passes. This is why a floating object bobs up and down as waves pass beneath it rather than being carried continuously in the wave's direction.

Most ocean waves are generated by wind blowing across the water surface. The wind transfers energy to the water through friction and pressure. Larger waves require stronger winds, winds blowing for longer periods, and a longer fetch — the uninterrupted distance over which wind blows across the water. This is why waves on large oceans can grow much larger than waves on small lakes or enclosed seas. Waves generated by distant storms can travel thousands of kilometers across open ocean as swell — long, regular waves with smooth shapes.

What Happens at Shore

As waves approach shore, they enter shallower water. When depth becomes less than about half the wavelength, the circular orbits of water particles begin to interact with the seafloor. Friction causes the wave to slow at the bottom while the top continues forward — the wave steepens. Eventually, the top outruns its base, the wave breaks, and its energy is released in turbulent surf. Waves approaching at an angle generate a longshore current parallel to the shoreline and move sediment along the beach in longshore drift, shaping beaches, sandbars, and coastal landforms over time.

Tsunamis

Tsunamis are a special category of ocean wave generated not by wind but by sudden disturbances that displace large volumes of water — underwater earthquakes along subduction zones, submarine landslides, or volcanic eruptions. In the open ocean, tsunami waves have very long wavelengths (sometimes hundreds of kilometers) and low wave heights. Ships at sea may not even notice them. But as the wave approaches shore and water shallows, wavelength decreases, wave height increases dramatically, and the wave can arrive as a wall of water tens of meters tall. The 2004 Indian Ocean tsunami, triggered by a magnitude 9.1 earthquake off Sumatra, killed more than 220,000 people across fourteen countries. Tsunami warning systems in both the Indian Ocean and Pacific now use sensors and warning centers to detect tsunamis and issue alerts before they reach populated coasts.

Section 18.5

Tides

The Cause of Tides

Tides are the periodic rise and fall of sea level caused by the gravitational pull of the Moon and, to a lesser extent, the Sun on Earth's oceans. Gravity decreases with distance — the side of Earth facing the Moon feels a slightly stronger gravitational pull than the opposite side. This differential pull causes the ocean to bulge slightly toward the Moon on the near side, and also slightly away from the Moon on the far side due to inertia and differential force. As Earth rotates, different locations pass through these bulges, producing two high tides and two low tides each day at most locations — though timing and size vary with geography and the shape of ocean basins.

Spring and Neap Tides

When the Sun, Earth, and Moon are aligned — at new moon and full moon — the gravitational forces of the Sun and Moon act together, producing larger tidal ranges called spring tides. When the Moon is at a right angle to the Sun-Earth line — at quarter moon — the forces partially cancel, producing smaller tidal ranges called neap tides. Tidal ranges vary greatly by location: less than one meter in the open ocean, but exceeding 16 meters in the Bay of Fundy in eastern Canada, where the bay's geometry creates resonance with the natural oscillation of water inside it.

The Intertidal Zone

The area between the highest high tide and lowest low tide lines is the intertidal zone — one of the most biologically dynamic environments on Earth. Organisms there must tolerate regular cycles of submersion and exposure, temperature extremes, and wave energy. Communities are often visibly zoned by elevation, with different species occupying distinct bands according to their tolerance for air exposure.

Section 18.6

Surface Currents

Wind, the Coriolis Effect, and Ocean Gyres

Surface currents are large-scale flows of ocean water driven primarily by wind. Global patterns of prevailing winds — trade winds in the tropics, westerlies in the mid-latitudes — drag the ocean surface and set water in motion. These currents typically extend from the surface to depths of a few hundred meters.

However, surface currents do not flow exactly in the direction of the wind. The rotation of Earth causes moving fluids to deflect — to the right in the Northern Hemisphere and to the left in the Southern Hemisphere. This is the Coriolis effect, and it causes ocean currents to curve rather than flow in straight lines. The combination of wind stress and the Coriolis effect produces large circular current systems called gyres. In each ocean basin there is a major subtropical gyre — rotating clockwise in the Northern Hemisphere and counterclockwise in the Southern Hemisphere.

The Gulf Stream and Other Major Currents

The Gulf Stream is one of the most powerful and well-studied ocean currents in the world. It originates in the Gulf of Mexico, flows northeast along the eastern coast of North America, and crosses the North Atlantic toward Europe, transporting an enormous volume of warm water at speeds up to 2.5 meters per second. The warmth of the Gulf Stream has a significant moderating effect on the climates of northwestern Europe, keeping temperatures milder than the latitude would suggest.

Other important surface currents include the Kuroshio Current in the North Pacific (analogous to the Gulf Stream), the Humboldt Current flowing north along the west coast of South America, the Benguela Current along southwestern Africa, and the Antarctic Circumpolar Current — the only current that encircles the entire globe without being interrupted by a continent.

Currents and Climate

Cold currents flowing from high latitudes toward the tropics cool the coasts they flow along and suppress precipitation, contributing to coastal deserts. The Atacama Desert on the west coast of South America and the Namib Desert in southwestern Africa both form partly because cold ocean currents suppress moisture in coastal air. Warm currents flowing toward higher latitudes deliver heat and increase evaporation, supplying moisture to the atmosphere and influencing precipitation patterns downwind.

Section 18.7

Deep Ocean Circulation

Thermohaline Circulation

Wind-driven surface currents affect only the upper few hundred meters of the ocean. The deep ocean — about 90 percent of the ocean's total volume — also moves, but it is driven by differences in density rather than by wind. Thermohaline circulation (from the Greek words for heat and salt) is the global circulation pattern driven by density differences caused by temperature and salinity variations.

Deep circulation begins where surface water becomes dense enough to sink. The primary sites of deep water formation are the North Atlantic (especially near Greenland, Iceland, and Norway) and around Antarctica. In the North Atlantic, warm surface water carried by the Gulf Stream loses heat to the cold atmosphere, becomes denser, and sinks to the deep ocean. This sinking water is replaced at the surface by more warm water flowing in from lower latitudes — helping to maintain the Gulf Stream.

The sinking dense water spreads slowly through the deep ocean, mixing with surrounding water and gradually rising toward the surface over hundreds to thousands of years. This slow movement through the deep ocean, combined with surface currents, forms what is sometimes called the global ocean conveyor belt or the Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation (AMOC) — though these terms simplify a system that is more complex and three-dimensional than any single conveyor.

Why Deep Circulation Matters

Thermohaline circulation distributes heat, oxygen, and nutrients throughout the ocean. When deep water eventually rises to the surface, it brings dissolved nutrients from depth, fertilizing surface waters and supporting biological productivity. It also plays a role in the carbon cycle: surface water absorbs CO₂ from the atmosphere, and when this water sinks, it carries dissolved carbon with it, removing CO₂ from the atmosphere for centuries to millennia.

Climate scientists are monitoring thermohaline circulation carefully because freshwater inputs from melting ice sheets can reduce the salinity and density of surface water in the North Atlantic, potentially weakening the sinking that drives AMOC. Evidence suggests AMOC has weakened compared to past centuries, with potential consequences for European and North American climate.

Common Misconception

The "ocean conveyor belt" is a useful mental image but an oversimplification. Real thermohaline circulation is not a single loop but a complex, three-dimensional system with multiple branches, variable speeds, and feedbacks. The diagram helps students grasp the concept; the real system requires models to understand fully.

Section 18.8

Upwelling and Marine Productivity

What Upwelling Is

Upwelling occurs when wind-driven surface currents move water away from a coast or region, and deeper water rises to replace it. Upwelling brings cold, nutrient-rich water from depths of 100 to 300 meters up to the sunlit surface. Persistent upwelling occurs along the western coasts of continents in the mid-latitudes, where prevailing winds blow surface water offshore. The California Current system, the Humboldt Current system off western South America, and the Benguela Current system off southwestern Africa are all associated with strong coastal upwelling.

Why Upwelling Feeds the Ocean

In the deep ocean, sunlight is absent and photosynthesis cannot occur. Organic material produced at the surface sinks when organisms die, carrying nutrients downward — a one-way trip. If nutrients were not returned to the surface, the sunlit zone would eventually be depleted and biological productivity would collapse. Upwelling is one of the primary mechanisms for returning nutrients to the surface. When nutrient-rich deep water rises into the sunlit zone, it triggers explosive growth of phytoplankton, which feed zooplankton, which feed fish, which feed seabirds, marine mammals, and humans. Upwelling zones cover a small fraction of the total ocean area but are extraordinarily productive — the Humboldt Current system off Peru and Chile has historically been one of the most productive fisheries in the world.

El Niño and Upwelling Disruption

The El Niño–Southern Oscillation (ENSO) — a periodic shifting of wind patterns and sea surface temperatures in the tropical Pacific — can dramatically weaken upwelling along the west coast of South America. During El Niño events, trade winds weaken and warm water spreads eastward, suppressing the cold Humboldt Current and the upwelling associated with it. Surface waters off Peru and Ecuador become warmer and poorer in nutrients. Fish populations decline. Seabirds and marine mammals may face starvation. The economic impacts on fishing communities can be severe. El Niño events also affect weather patterns worldwide, influencing rainfall, drought, and storm tracks across North America, South America, Africa, Asia, and Australia — making ENSO one of the most important sources of year-to-year climate variability on Earth.

Connection to Chapter 21

ENSO is part of a larger pattern of ocean-atmosphere interaction that influences global climate. In Chapter 21, you will see how ENSO and other ocean-atmosphere patterns contribute to natural climate variability, separate from the long-term warming trend driven by greenhouse gas accumulation.

Section 18.9

Marine Ecosystems and Resources

Zones of the Ocean

Marine ecologists divide the ocean into zones based on light, depth, and distance from shore, because these factors determine which organisms can live where. The photic zone is the upper layer where there is enough sunlight to support photosynthesis — about 200 meters in clear tropical water, but only a few meters in turbid coastal water. All marine primary production occurs here. Below the photic zone lies the aphotic zone, where no sunlight penetrates. Life exists there, depending on organic material sinking from above (marine snow) or, at hydrothermal vents, on chemosynthesis — the use of chemical energy rather than sunlight.

By distance from shore, the ocean is divided into the neritic zone (over the continental shelf) and the oceanic zone (beyond the shelf, over the deep ocean). Neritic waters are generally shallower, more nutrient-rich, and more productive than oceanic waters.

Phytoplankton and Marine Food Webs

Phytoplankton — microscopic photosynthetic organisms including diatoms, dinoflagellates, and cyanobacteria — are responsible for roughly half of all photosynthesis on Earth. Despite their small individual size, their collective abundance and productivity make them the foundation of nearly all marine food webs and a major contributor to atmospheric oxygen and the carbon cycle. Phytoplankton are consumed by zooplankton — microscopic animals including copepods and krill. Zooplankton are consumed by small fish, which are consumed by larger fish, marine mammals, and seabirds. This energy pyramid, with less energy available at each higher level, determines the carrying capacity of the ocean for large animals.

Major Marine Ecosystems

Coral reefs are among the most diverse ecosystems on Earth. Reef-building corals are colonial animals that secrete calcium carbonate skeletons, constructing the physical structure of the reef. Within their tissues, corals harbor photosynthetic algae called zooxanthellae, which provide the coral with food through photosynthesis. Reefs occupy warm, shallow, clear tropical waters and support extraordinary biodiversity — estimated at one-quarter of all marine species despite covering less than one percent of the ocean floor.

Kelp forests grow along cool, nutrient-rich coastlines, forming underwater forests of giant brown algae that can reach 30 meters in length. Like terrestrial forests, they provide habitat structure and support diverse communities of fish, invertebrates, and marine mammals. Sea otters play a key ecological role in some kelp forest ecosystems by consuming sea urchins, which would otherwise overgraze the kelp.

Mangroves are salt-tolerant trees and shrubs growing at the boundary between land and sea in tropical and subtropical coastal zones. Their dense root systems stabilize sediment, protect coastlines from erosion and storm surge, filter pollutants, and provide nursery habitat for fish and invertebrates. Mangroves also store large amounts of carbon in their soils.

Deep-sea hydrothermal vent ecosystems, discovered only in 1977, support communities of organisms that derive energy from hydrogen sulfide and other chemicals released from the seafloor — with no dependence on sunlight. Tube worms, clams, crabs, and shrimp thrive around vents. These ecosystems expanded scientific understanding of where life can exist and raised new questions about life's potential elsewhere in the universe.

Section 18.10

Human Impacts on Oceans

Overfishing

Commercial fishing technology — large factory ships, sonar, satellite tracking — allows fleets to locate and catch fish with unprecedented efficiency. When fish are removed faster than populations can reproduce, stocks collapse. The Atlantic cod fishery off New England and eastern Canada collapsed in the late twentieth century after decades of intensive fishing. The Grand Banks, which had supported cod fishing for five centuries, could no longer sustain commercial harvests. Cod populations have been slow to recover even decades after catch restrictions were imposed. Bycatch — the accidental capture of non-target species including sea turtles, dolphins, seabirds, and juvenile fish — adds to the ecological impact of fishing. Bottom trawling, which drags heavy nets across the seafloor, can destroy benthic habitat over large areas.

Pollution and Dead Zones

Ocean pollution comes from point sources (industrial discharge, sewage outfalls, oil spills) and non-point sources (agricultural runoff, urban stormwater, atmospheric deposition). Excess nitrogen and phosphorus from agricultural runoff causes eutrophication in coastal waters: massive phytoplankton blooms form, then die and decompose. Bacteria consuming the dead material deplete dissolved oxygen, creating hypoxic zones (dead zones) where most aerobic marine life cannot survive. The dead zone at the mouth of the Mississippi River in the Gulf of Mexico is one of the largest in the world, expanding and contracting seasonally with nutrient inputs and wind patterns.

Plastic Pollution and Microplastics

Plastics do not biodegrade readily; instead, they break into smaller and smaller fragments called microplastics. Ocean gyres concentrate floating debris into accumulation zones sometimes called ocean garbage patches — areas with elevated concentrations of small plastic particles mixed through the water. Plastics are ingested by marine animals ranging from zooplankton to whales. Microplastics have been found in marine organisms, seafood consumed by humans, deep-sea sediments, and Arctic sea ice.

Ocean Acidification

As atmospheric CO₂ concentrations have risen, oceans have absorbed more of it. When CO₂ dissolves in seawater, it forms carbonic acid (H₂CO₃), which releases hydrogen ions. More hydrogen ions mean lower pH. Since the beginning of the industrial era, average ocean pH has decreased from about 8.2 to about 8.1. This small-looking number represents about a 26 percent increase in hydrogen ion concentration, because pH is a logarithmic scale. Ocean acidification threatens marine organisms that build shells and skeletons from calcium carbonate, including corals, mollusks, echinoderms, and some plankton. As water becomes more acidic, it becomes harder for these organisms to build their structures, and in some cases existing structures begin to dissolve. The pace of current ocean acidification is faster than any documented in Earth's geologic past except in the immediate aftermath of asteroid impacts or massive volcanic eruptions, giving marine ecosystems little time to adapt.

Coral Bleaching and Reef Degradation

When water temperature rises even 1–2°C above the normal summer maximum for several weeks, corals expel their zooxanthellae — the algae that give them color and most of their nutrition. The corals turn white (bleach). If temperatures return to normal quickly, corals can recover. If elevated temperatures persist, the corals starve and die, leaving behind white calcium carbonate skeletons. Mass coral bleaching events were rare before the 1980s. They have become more frequent and severe as ocean temperatures have risen. The Great Barrier Reef experienced mass bleaching in 2016, 2017, 2020, and 2022, each event killing portions of the reef. Scientists project that without significant reductions in greenhouse gas emissions, conditions that trigger bleaching will occur annually for most tropical reefs by mid-century, making recovery between events increasingly unlikely.

Sea Level Rise

As climate warms, sea level rises for two reasons. First, warmer water expands — thermal expansion. Second, melting ice sheets and glaciers on land transfer water into the ocean. (Sea ice does not raise sea level when it melts, because it is already floating; it is land ice — the ice sheets of Greenland and Antarctica — that adds water to the ocean.) Global average sea level rose about 20 centimeters during the twentieth century and is currently rising at about 3–4 millimeters per year, a rate that has accelerated. Even modest sea level rise increases the frequency and severity of coastal flooding, salinizes freshwater aquifers, and threatens coastal infrastructure.

Looking Forward

Some marine ecosystems have demonstrated remarkable resilience when human pressures are reduced. Fish stocks can recover when fishing is limited. Marine protected areas show higher fish biomass and spillover benefits to nearby fishing areas. Reefs have recovered from bleaching in some locations when local water quality and other stressors were reduced. Recovery requires time — and understanding ocean systems is the foundation for making decisions that give that time to the largest and most essential system on the planet.

Chapter 18 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 18.1Oceans cover about 71 percent of Earth's surface and form a single, connected body of water that regulates climate, participates in the carbon cycle, produces about half of Earth's atmospheric oxygen, and supports fisheries for billions of people. The ocean's large heat capacity moderates coastal climates worldwide.
  • Sec 18.2Ocean basins contain a rich topography produced by plate tectonics: continental margins (shelf, slope, rise), abyssal plains, mid-ocean ridges at divergent boundaries, ocean trenches at convergent boundaries, and seamounts. The seafloor is blanketed in sediment that preserves a record of past ocean conditions.
  • Sec 18.3Salinity (~35 g/kg on average) varies with evaporation and freshwater input. Temperature decreases with depth below the thermocline. Seawater density is controlled by temperature and salinity: cold, salty water is densest. Density differences drive stratification and vertical circulation.
  • Sec 18.4Wind transfers energy to the ocean surface, generating waves in which water particles orbit in place rather than traveling with the wave. As waves enter shallow water, they slow, steepen, and break. Tsunamis are generated by sudden seafloor displacement and become dangerous as they compress in shallow water near shore.
  • Sec 18.5Tides are caused by the differential gravitational pull of the Moon (and secondarily the Sun) on Earth's oceans. Spring tides occur when Sun, Earth, and Moon align; neap tides occur at quarter moon. Tidal range varies greatly by location. The intertidal zone is one of the most biologically dynamic environments on Earth.
  • Sec 18.6Surface currents are driven by wind and deflected by the Coriolis effect into large clockwise gyres in the Northern Hemisphere and counterclockwise gyres in the Southern Hemisphere. The Gulf Stream and similar currents redistribute heat, influencing coastal climates. Cold currents suppress precipitation; warm currents enhance it.
  • Sec 18.7Thermohaline circulation drives the deep ocean through density differences caused by temperature and salinity variations. Dense water sinks in the North Atlantic and around Antarctica, spreads through the deep ocean, and slowly upwells elsewhere over centuries to millennia. This circulation distributes heat, oxygen, and nutrients, and moves carbon from the surface to the deep ocean.
  • Sec 18.8Upwelling occurs when winds drive surface water away from a coast, pulling cold, nutrient-rich deep water up to the sunlit surface. This triggers phytoplankton blooms and supports highly productive fisheries. El Niño events weaken upwelling along western South America, reducing biological productivity and causing economic hardship for fishing communities.
  • Sec 18.9Ocean zones are defined by light availability (photic/aphotic) and distance from shore (neritic/oceanic). Phytoplankton underpin all marine food webs. Major ecosystems include coral reefs, kelp forests, mangroves, open ocean communities, and hydrothermal vent ecosystems that depend on chemosynthesis rather than sunlight.
  • Sec 18.10Human impacts on the ocean include overfishing, bycatch, pollution, eutrophication and hypoxic dead zones, plastic and microplastic contamination, ocean acidification from rising CO₂, coral bleaching from warming, and sea level rise from thermal expansion and melting land ice. Some ecosystems show capacity for recovery when pressures are reduced.

Key Terms Review

abyssal plain
The flat, sediment-covered floor of the deep ocean, typically at depths of 3,000 to 6,000 meters.
aphotic zone
The deep ocean region below the photic zone where insufficient light penetrates to support photosynthesis.
AMOC
Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation — a major component of Atlantic ocean circulation driven by thermohaline processes, moving warm surface water northward and cold deep water southward.
biological pump
The process by which marine organisms move carbon from the surface ocean to the deep ocean through photosynthesis, consumption, and sinking of organic matter.
bycatch
The unintended capture of non-target species in fishing operations.
continental margin
The underwater edge of a continent, including the continental shelf, continental slope, and continental rise.
continental shelf
The gently sloping underwater extension of a continent from the shoreline to the continental slope, typically shallower than 200 meters.
Coriolis effect
The deflection of moving objects caused by Earth's rotation; deflects to the right in the Northern Hemisphere and to the left in the Southern Hemisphere.
dead zone
An area of ocean with very low dissolved oxygen — caused by eutrophication — where most aerobic marine life cannot survive. Also called a hypoxic zone.
El Niño
A periodic warming of surface waters in the central and eastern tropical Pacific associated with weakened trade winds, affecting weather worldwide and suppressing upwelling off South America.
fetch
The uninterrupted distance over which wind blows across water; a longer fetch allows larger waves to develop.
gyre
A large, circular ocean current system formed by the interaction of global wind patterns and the Coriolis effect.
longshore current
A current flowing parallel to the shoreline, generated when waves approach the shore at an angle.
microplastics
Tiny plastic fragments less than 5 mm formed when larger plastic items break down; widespread in marine environments and organisms.
mid-ocean ridge
An underwater mountain range formed at a divergent plate boundary where new oceanic crust is created by volcanic activity.
neap tide
A tide with a smaller tidal range, occurring when the Moon is at a right angle to the Sun-Earth line (quarter moon), so tidal forces partially cancel.
neritic zone
The relatively shallow ocean zone over the continental shelf; generally nutrient-rich and highly productive.
ocean acidification
The decrease in ocean pH caused by absorption of atmospheric CO₂, which forms carbonic acid in seawater, threatening shell- and skeleton-building marine organisms.
ocean trench
A deep, narrow depression in the ocean floor formed at a convergent plate boundary where oceanic crust subducts into the mantle.
photic zone
The upper layer of the ocean where sunlight is sufficient to support photosynthesis; all marine primary production occurs here.
phytoplankton
Microscopic photosynthetic organisms that form the base of most marine food webs and produce roughly half of Earth's atmospheric oxygen.
salinity
The concentration of dissolved salts in seawater, typically measured in grams per kilogram (g/kg); average ocean salinity is about 35 g/kg.
seamount
An underwater volcano that does not reach the ocean surface.
spring tide
A tide with an especially large tidal range, occurring when the Sun, Earth, and Moon are aligned at new moon or full moon.
stratification
The layering of water into distinct zones of different density, which resists vertical mixing of nutrients and gases.
thermocline
A layer in the ocean where temperature decreases rapidly with depth, separating warmer surface water from cold deep water.
thermohaline circulation
Deep ocean circulation driven by differences in water density caused by variations in temperature and salinity.
tsunami
A series of ocean waves with very long wavelengths generated by sudden large displacement of water — typically caused by an undersea earthquake, landslide, or volcanic eruption.
upwelling
The rising of cold, deep, nutrient-rich water to the ocean surface, often caused by wind-driven displacement of surface water offshore.
zooxanthellae
Photosynthetic algae that live within coral tissues and provide the coral with nutrients through photosynthesis; lost during coral bleaching events.

Common Misconceptions

Misconception
"Ocean acidification means the ocean is becoming acidic — it will soon have a pH below 7."
Correction
Ocean pH has decreased from about 8.2 to 8.1 — it is still alkaline (above 7), not acidic. "Acidification" describes the direction of change (toward lower pH), not the current state. However, even this small change represents a 26 percent increase in hydrogen ion concentration, because pH is a logarithmic scale, and that change is significant for organisms that build calcium carbonate structures.
Misconception
"Melting Arctic sea ice will raise sea level significantly."
Correction
Sea ice is already floating on the ocean. When floating ice melts, it does not raise sea level (the same way ice melting in a glass does not cause the water to overflow). It is land ice — the ice sheets of Greenland and Antarctica, and mountain glaciers — that raises sea level when it melts, because that water is added to the ocean rather than already being part of it.
Misconception
"The open ocean is the most productive part of the marine environment."
Correction
The open ocean (oceanic zone) covers most of the ocean's area but is often nutrient-poor and relatively unproductive. Most marine biological productivity occurs in nutrient-rich areas: coastal upwelling zones, continental shelves where rivers deliver nutrients, and polar seas where seasonal mixing brings nutrients to the surface. The center of large ocean gyres can be among the most nutrient-depleted environments on Earth.

Chapter 21 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Weather changes from day to day. Climate shapes how species evolve, where civilizations form, and which crops grow in which valleys. When climate changes — slowly, over centuries, or rapidly, over decades — everything from sea level to wildfire season to coral reef health changes with it. This chapter explains what climate is, what controls it, how scientists reconstruct past climates, what causes climate to vary naturally, what is driving the current warming trend, and how societies can respond. The goal is not alarm and not dismissal. The goal is understanding: how evidence is gathered, how mechanisms work, and how decisions are made under uncertainty.

By the end of this chapter you should understand how climate differs from weather, what controls regional climate, how the greenhouse effect works, how carbon cycles through Earth's systems, how scientists read past climates from proxies, what causes natural climate variation, what the evidence shows about human-caused warming, what impacts are observed and projected, and how mitigation and adaptation differ as responses. You will also practice reading climate graphs, evaluating evidence claims, and reasoning about uncertainty.

Opening Question

A classmate says: "It was the coldest winter in twenty years, so climate change must be fake." How would you respond? By the end of this chapter, you will have the tools to explain exactly why a single cold winter cannot confirm or disprove a long-term climate trend — and to describe what evidence actually does and does not show.

Section 21.1

Weather and Climate

Weather is what is happening in the atmosphere right now, or over the next few days. It is the temperature at noon on a Tuesday, the rainstorm that arrives Friday evening, the wind that makes November feel colder than the thermometer shows. Weather is measured, forecasted, and highly variable.

Climate is the long-term pattern of weather in a region. It is not any single day or storm. Climate describes what kinds of temperatures and precipitation a place typically receives across months, seasons, and years — averaged and characterized over decades, usually at least thirty years of data.

The classic distinction is this: weather is your mood; climate is your personality. One cold summer does not disprove warming, just as one warm winter does not prove it. Climate requires long records, wide geographic coverage, and careful statistical analysis. Scientists who study climate analyze trends — changes in averages and in the frequency or severity of extremes — across decades and centuries.

FeatureWeatherClimate
TimescaleHours to daysDecades to centuries
Spatial scaleLocal to regionalRegional to global
PredictabilityDays to roughly two weeksStatistical patterns over long periods
ExampleA blizzard in MarchAverage winter snowfall for a region
Statistical Thinking in Climate Science

Understanding climate requires thinking statistically. A single unusually warm year matters less than a consistent upward trend across thirty or forty years. Climate scientists look at distributions — how averages shift, how the frequency of extremes changes — not just single events. A cold month in a warming world is like a step down on a staircase that is still moving up overall.

Section 21.2

Climate Zones

Earth's surface is not uniformly warm or cold, wet or dry. Sunlight arrives at different angles at different latitudes. Continents and oceans store and release heat differently. Mountain ranges force air upward and block moisture. Ocean currents carry warm or cold water thousands of kilometers. These factors combine to create distinct climate zones. The most widely used classification system, developed by the German-Russian climatologist Wladimir Köppen, divides Earth into five major climate types based on temperature and precipitation patterns.

TypeNameKey CharacteristicsExamples
ATropicalWarm year-round; abundant rainfallAmazon basin, Congo, Southeast Asia
BDryEvaporation exceeds precipitationSahara, Australian outback, Atacama
CTemperateMild winters; warm to hot summers; ocean influenceMediterranean coast, Pacific Northwest, SE United States
DContinentalCold winters; large seasonal swings; interior locationsCanada, Russia, northern United States
EPolar / HighlandCold year-round; high latitude or high elevationGreenland, Antarctica, Tibetan Plateau
Climate Zones Are Not Fixed

Climate zones are not rigid boundaries. They shift over time as climate changes. During glacial periods, climate zones moved toward the equator. During warmer periods, they shifted toward the poles. Where climate zones sit — and how they may shift — has enormous implications for agriculture, biodiversity, and human settlement.

Section 21.3

Latitude, Elevation, Oceans, and Topography

Climate does not depend on latitude alone. Four major factors interact to produce local and regional climate patterns.

Latitude

The most powerful control on climate is latitude — the distance from the equator. Near the equator, sunlight strikes Earth's surface at a steep angle, concentrating solar energy in a small area. Near the poles, sunlight strikes at a shallow angle, spreading the same amount of energy over a larger area. The result is that tropical regions are far warmer than polar regions. Latitude also controls the length of days across seasons, producing large seasonal temperature swings at high latitudes.

Elevation

Temperature decreases with altitude at an average rate of about 6.5°C per 1,000 meters — called the environmental lapse rate. A mountain at 3,000 meters elevation may be 15–20°C cooler than the surrounding lowlands. Elevation also affects precipitation: when air rises over mountains it cools and water vapor condenses, producing rain or snow on the windward side. On the leeward side — the rain shadow — descending air warms and dries, creating arid conditions.

Proximity to Oceans

Water heats and cools more slowly than land because of its higher heat capacity. Coastal (maritime) climates are moderated by the ocean: winters are milder and summers are cooler than inland locations at the same latitude. Interior continental climates are more extreme. Ocean currents amplify these effects: the Gulf Stream moderates western European climates; cold currents along western coastlines produce cool, foggy conditions and support productive fisheries.

Topography

Mountain ranges create rain shadows, channel winds, and trap cold air in valleys. Topography interacts with all other factors. Two locations at the same latitude and elevation can have dramatically different climates depending on their position relative to mountains and the ocean.

The Four Climate Controls — Summary

Latitude — controls angle of sunlight and seasonal day length. More poleward = colder and more seasonal.

Elevation — temperature drops ~6.5°C per 1,000 m. Mountains force precipitation on windward sides and create rain shadows on leeward sides.

Ocean proximity — oceans moderate temperature swings. Coastal = mild; interior = extreme seasons.

Topography — shapes airflow, precipitation, and local temperature patterns at finer scales.

Section 21.4

The Greenhouse Effect

Earth is warmed by the sun but also cooled by radiating energy back to space. The balance between incoming solar energy and outgoing heat energy determines Earth's average temperature. The greenhouse effect is the mechanism by which certain gases in the atmosphere slow the loss of heat to space, keeping Earth warmer than it would otherwise be.

How the Greenhouse Effect Works

The sun emits energy primarily as visible light and ultraviolet radiation — short-wavelength, high-energy radiation. Most of this passes through the atmosphere and reaches Earth's surface. Earth's surface absorbs solar radiation and warms. It then re-radiates energy as infrared radiation — long-wavelength, lower-energy heat radiation. Greenhouse gases absorb this outgoing infrared radiation and re-emit it in all directions, including back toward Earth's surface. The result is that the surface and lower atmosphere are warmer than they would be if all the outgoing infrared radiation escaped directly to space.

This process is analogous to a blanket. A blanket does not generate heat; it slows the loss of heat from your body. Greenhouse gases do not generate energy; they slow the loss of heat from Earth's surface to space.

Natural vs. Enhanced Greenhouse Effect

Natural greenhouse effect — without any greenhouse gases, Earth's average surface temperature would be approximately −18°C instead of the actual +15°C. The natural greenhouse effect provides 33°C of warming, making Earth habitable.

Enhanced greenhouse effect — human activities have increased greenhouse gas concentrations, intensifying the effect and pushing the energy balance toward warming. Not a new mechanism — an intensification of a natural one.

Section 21.5

The Carbon Cycle and Climate

Carbon moves continuously through Earth's systems. It is stored in the atmosphere, oceans, land (soils, rocks, living organisms), and the deep Earth. The movement of carbon among these reservoirs is the carbon cycle, and it is deeply connected to climate.

Carbon Reservoirs

ReservoirApproximate SizeKey Notes
Atmosphere~900 Gt CPrimarily as CO₂; concentration now over 420 ppm
Oceans~38,000 Gt CLargest active reservoir; exchanges CO₂ at surface
Land biosphere~2,000–3,000 Gt CLiving plants, soils, organic matter
Fossil fuelsLargest accessible reserveStored over millions of years; removed from active cycle by burial
Deep EarthEnormous but slowVolcanoes release CO₂ from mantle over geologic time

Gt C = gigatons of carbon; one gigaton = one billion metric tons.

Carbon Flows and the Long-Term Thermostat

Over millions of years, the carbon cycle has acted as a long-term climate thermostat. When CO₂ rises (from volcanic activity), temperatures rise, the water cycle accelerates, weathering rates increase, and more CO₂ is drawn down into carbonate rocks — eventually cooling the climate. This negative feedback has kept Earth's temperature within a range compatible with liquid water across billions of years. However, this thermostat operates on timescales of hundreds of thousands to millions of years. It cannot compensate for rapid human emissions over decades to centuries — the geological processes are far too slow.

Current human CO₂ emissions are approximately 36–40 gigatons per year — more than one hundred times the natural rate of volcanic outgassing. Atmospheric CO₂ has risen from about 280 ppm before industrialization to over 420 ppm today, a level not seen in at least 800,000 years.

Section 21.6

Climate Records and Evidence

How do scientists know about past climates? Instrumental records — thermometers, rain gauges, satellites — are accurate but only cover roughly the past 150–200 years. To understand climate variation over centuries, millennia, and millions of years, scientists use proxy records — natural archives that preserve information about past temperature, precipitation, and atmospheric composition.

Instrumental Records

Since the mid-nineteenth century, systematic temperature records have been collected from weather stations and from ships and buoys at sea. These records show a clear warming trend from the late nineteenth century to the present. Satellite records since 1979 confirm tropospheric warming consistent with surface records. When examining temperature records, scientists calculate anomalies — departures from a baseline average — to remove geographic and elevation differences between stations and show whether temperatures are above or below the historical norm.

Ice Cores

Ice cores drilled from Greenland and Antarctica preserve a remarkable record of past climate. Each year's snowfall leaves a layer. As layers compact into ice, air bubbles are trapped — preserving a sample of the actual atmosphere at the time the ice formed. From ice cores scientists can measure atmospheric CO₂ and methane concentrations going back 800,000 years, temperature (from oxygen isotope ratios), volcanic eruptions (ash layers), dust, and sea salt. The Vostok and EPICA Dome C cores from Antarctica are among the most important paleoclimate records ever recovered, showing that CO₂ concentration and temperature have tracked each other closely through glacial-interglacial cycles.

Tree Rings, Ocean Sediments, and Other Proxies

Trees grow a new ring each year. Wide rings indicate favorable growing conditions; narrow rings indicate stress. By analyzing ring widths, scientists can reconstruct temperature or precipitation records going back thousands of years. Ocean sediment cores contain shells of foraminifera whose chemistry records the temperature and salinity of past seawater, extending climate reconstruction back millions of years. Coral annual bands, pollen in lake sediments, cave formations, and historical documents provide additional overlapping evidence. No single proxy is perfect; climate scientists use many overlapping proxies from different regions, cross-validating them against each other and against instrumental records.

Key Finding from Proxy Evidence

The current warming trend is occurring faster than natural variability alone can explain, and the atmospheric CO₂ concentration is higher than at any time in the 800,000-year ice core record — and likely higher than at any time in several million years.

Section 21.7

Natural Climate Variation

Earth's climate has never been static. Long before humans existed, climate shifted between glacial periods with ice sheets covering large parts of the continents and warmer interglacial periods. Understanding natural climate variation helps scientists distinguish it from human-caused changes.

Milankovitch Cycles

Earth's orbit around the sun and the orientation of its axis are not perfectly constant. They vary over long cycles, changing the distribution of solar energy across the planet and seasons. These are called Milankovitch cycles, after the Serbian mathematician Milutin Milanković.

ParameterWhat VariesTimescalePrimary Effect
EccentricityShape of Earth's orbit (circular to elliptical)~100,000 yearsChanges difference in solar energy at closest vs. farthest point from sun
Axial tilt (obliquity)Tilt of axis from 22.1° to 24.5°~41,000 yearsGreater tilt = stronger seasonal contrast
PrecessionWobble of Earth's axis~26,000 yearsChanges which hemisphere points toward sun at perihelion

Milankovitch cycles explain the pacing of ice ages over the past 800,000 years. However, the current orbital configuration would, if anything, produce slight cooling over the next several thousand years — the opposite of what is observed.

Solar Output, Volcanic Eruptions, and Internal Variability

Solar output varies in an approximately 11-year cycle (the sunspot cycle) and shows longer-term changes. However, satellite measurements since 1978 show that solar output has not increased — in fact, it has very slightly decreased — while Earth has warmed significantly. Solar variation is not a driver of the current warming trend.

Volcanic eruptions inject sulfate aerosols into the stratosphere, reflecting sunlight and causing temporary cooling of 0.3–0.5°C lasting one to three years. The 1991 Pinatubo eruption cooled global temperatures for about two years. Volcanic cooling is real but temporary. It cannot explain long-term warming trends.

Internal variability such as El Niño–Southern Oscillation (ENSO) redistributes heat regionally and can raise or lower global average temperatures for a year or two. These patterns do not produce a long-term directional trend.

Common Misconception — "Climate Changed Before, So It's Natural Now"

Natural climate variation is real and well documented. But knowing that past climate changes had natural causes does not tell you the cause of any specific change. Attribution science compares observed patterns against what models predict with natural forcings alone vs. with human forcings added. Natural forcings alone cannot match the observed warming record — human forcings are required.

Section 21.8

Human-Caused Climate Change

The overwhelming scientific evidence is that the current warming of Earth's climate system is primarily caused by human activities, especially the burning of fossil fuels, which has substantially increased the concentration of greenhouse gases in the atmosphere.

Positive Feedbacks That Amplify Warming

An initial warming from greenhouse gases triggers several positive feedbacks that amplify the original effect.

Three Major Amplifying Feedbacks

Water vapor feedback — as temperature rises, more water evaporates and warmer air holds more water vapor. Water vapor is itself a greenhouse gas, trapping more heat and causing further warming. This is the most powerful amplifying feedback.

Ice-albedo feedback — as ice melts, darker land and ocean are exposed. They absorb more solar energy (lower albedo), causing further warming and further melting. This is why the Arctic is warming faster than the global average.

Permafrost feedback — thawing permafrost releases stored methane and CO₂ as organic matter decomposes, potentially accelerating warming.

Climate Sensitivity

Equilibrium climate sensitivity (ECS) is the amount of global average warming expected from a doubling of atmospheric CO₂, after the climate system fully adjusts. The current best estimate from the scientific community is likely in the range of 2.5°C to 4°C, with 3°C as a central estimate. The uncertainty comes primarily from the difficulty of quantifying cloud feedbacks. Even at the lower end, a doubling of CO₂ would produce substantial warming with serious consequences.

Section 21.9

Climate Impacts and Risks

Warming does not affect all places equally or all aspects of Earth systems equally. Climate change involves a complex set of interacting impacts on physical systems, ecosystems, and human societies.

Impact CategoryKey Observations and Projections
Temperature extremesHeat waves more frequent, intense, and longer-lasting; cold extremes less frequent; heat-related illness and death risks increase
Precipitation shiftsMore intense precipitation events in many regions; longer dry periods between events in others; earlier snowpack peak reduces late-season water availability

Ocean acidificationpH dropped ~0.1 units since pre-industrial (~26% more acidic); threatens shell-forming organisms (oysters, corals, foraminifera) and marine food webs

Human healthHeat illness; expanded range of mosquito- and tick-borne diseases; wildfire smoke; food and water security impacts
Differential Vulnerability

Climate impacts are not evenly distributed. Low-income communities, small island nations, communities in dry or coastal regions, and communities that depend on climate-sensitive resources face greater risks with less capacity to adapt. This raises questions of fairness and environmental justice discussed further in Chapter 29.

Section 21.10

Mitigation and Adaptation

Human societies can respond to climate change through two broad strategies: mitigation — reducing the emissions that cause climate change — and adaptation — adjusting to the changes that are already occurring or are expected. Both are necessary. Mitigation limits the severity of future change. Adaptation manages risks from change that has already occurred or is locked in by past emissions.

Mitigation Approaches

ApproachDescription
Transitioning energy systemsSolar, wind, hydropower, geothermal, and nuclear energy replace fossil fuels in electricity generation
Electrifying transportationElectric vehicles powered by low-carbon electricity reduce transportation emissions
Energy efficiencyBetter building insulation, efficient appliances and vehicles, improved industrial processes reduce demand
Protecting forestsHalting deforestation and replanting forests preserves and increases carbon storage
Reducing non-CO₂ emissionsCapturing methane from landfills and oil and gas operations; reducing fertilizer overuse
Carbon captureTechnologies to capture CO₂ from power plants or from the air; currently expensive and limited in scale
Cumulative Emissions and the Emissions Pathway

Warming is largely proportional to the cumulative total of CO₂ emissions since industrialization — not just the current rate. Every ton of CO₂ emitted contributes to warming. Stopping emissions does not immediately stop warming, but it does stop warming from continuing to increase. The faster emissions fall, the less total warming occurs and the more time societies have to adapt.

Adaptation Approaches

Adaptation means adjusting systems to manage climate risks. Examples include: coastal protection (sea walls, managed retreat, wetland restoration); improved water management (drought-resistant crops, efficient irrigation, desalination); heat preparedness (cool spaces, urban tree canopy, early warning systems); infrastructure design that anticipates more extreme precipitation and heat; and ecosystem management that protects habitat corridors and reduces other stressors.

Tradeoffs and Limits

Both mitigation and adaptation involve costs and tradeoffs. Transitioning energy systems requires investment and can displace workers in fossil fuel industries. Adaptation costs fall unevenly across communities. Some risks — ecosystem extinctions, ice sheet loss, community displacement — have adaptation limits beyond which adjustment is no longer possible. Science provides information about what is physically possible. Decisions about which responses to pursue involve economics, politics, values, and ethics — not science alone.

Section 21.11

Climate Data, Uncertainty, and Decision-Making

Climate science is one of the most intensively studied topics in modern science. The core conclusions — that Earth is warming, that the primary cause is human greenhouse gas emissions, and that continued emissions will cause further warming — rest on multiple independent lines of evidence. But climate science is not free of uncertainty, and understanding what is uncertain — and what is not — matters for sound reasoning.

What Is Well Established

Robust, Multi-Source Evidence

Earth's average surface temperature has risen approximately 1.1–1.2°C since pre-industrial times.

Atmospheric CO₂ has risen from 280 ppm to over 420 ppm — the highest level in at least 800,000 years.

The isotopic signature of the added CO₂ confirms it comes from fossil fuels.

Human greenhouse gas emissions are the dominant cause of the warming observed since the mid-twentieth century.

The ocean is warming, sea level is rising, glaciers and ice sheets are losing mass, and Arctic sea ice extent is declining.

Where Uncertainty Remains

Climate sensitivity — the precise amount of warming expected from a given CO₂ increase has a range (roughly 2.5°C to 4°C per CO₂ doubling), not a single value.

Regional projections — global warming is projected with greater confidence than regional changes in precipitation, drought, and extreme events.

Ice sheet dynamics — the stability of the Greenland and West Antarctic ice sheets, and therefore the upper end of sea level rise projections, involves processes not fully understood.

Tipping points — the levels of warming at which Earth systems cross thresholds producing large, rapid, irreversible changes are uncertain.

Future emissions — climate projections are scenarios based on different possible emission pathways — not predictions, but projections of what would happen under different assumptions.

Using Uncertainty Responsibly

Uncertainty does not mean "we don't know anything." It means there is a range of possible outcomes. When potential impacts are severe and irreversible, and when the cost of action is manageable relative to the cost of inaction, the reasonable response is to act rather than wait for perfect certainty. This is the same logic used in public health, engineering design, financial planning, and emergency preparedness — all fields where decisions are made under uncertainty. Uncertainty cuts both ways: if the outcome could be worse than central estimates, uncertainty does not support complacency.

Worked Example 21.1
Reading a Temperature Anomaly Graph
  • ContextGlobal average temperature anomaly graphs show annual or monthly temperature departure from a 30-year baseline. A value of +0.8°C means the year's average was 0.8°C above the baseline average.
  • Step 1Identify the baseline period. Common baselines: 1951–1980 (NASA), 1981–2010 (NOAA). All anomalies are measured relative to this baseline — not to absolute temperature.
  • Step 2Read individual years. Note that year-to-year variation is large. Some years are cooler than recent years even during an overall warming trend — this is expected natural variability within the larger trend.
  • Step 3Identify the long-term trend. Draw a smooth trend line through the data. Notice whether the trend is upward, flat, or downward across decades.
  • Step 4Identify notable features. Major volcanic eruptions cause brief dips (e.g., 1992 after Pinatubo). Strong El Niño events cause brief peaks (e.g., 1998, 2016). These short-term signals can be separated from the long-term trend by examining multi-year averages.
Key insight: The long-term trend and short-term variability are both real and must be distinguished. A single anomalous year — hot or cold — tells you about weather variability, not climate trend direction.
Worked Example 21.2
Calculating a Warming Rate
  • ProblemIf global average temperature was approximately 13.8°C in 1880 and 15.0°C in 2020, what is the average rate of warming per decade?
  • Step 1Calculate total warming:15.0°C − 13.8°C = 1.2°C
  • Step 2Calculate time elapsed and convert to decades:2020 − 1880 = 140 years = 14 decades
  • Step 3Calculate rate:1.2°C ÷ 14 decades ≈ 0.086°C per decade
  • InterpretNote: Actual warming has accelerated. The rate over recent decades is higher than this 140-year average. The average over 1980–2020 alone is approximately 0.18–0.20°C per decade — roughly double the long-period average.
Average rate: ~0.086°C per decade over 1880–2020. More recent decades show a significantly higher rate, indicating acceleration.
Worked Example 21.3
Interpreting the Keeling Curve
  • ContextThe Keeling Curve shows atmospheric CO₂ concentration measured at Mauna Loa Observatory since 1958. It shows both a long-term rising trend and a regular seasonal cycle.
  • Step 1Identify the long-term trend. CO₂ was approximately 315 ppm in 1958. By 2020 it reached approximately 413 ppm. Total increase: 413 − 315 = 98 ppm over 62 years (≈ 6.2 decades). Rate: 98 ÷ 6.2 ≈ 15.8 ppm per decade.
  • Step 2Identify the seasonal cycle. CO₂ dips in northern hemisphere summer when photosynthesis by plants draws carbon from the atmosphere. It rises in winter when photosynthesis slows and decomposition and respiration continue. The annual oscillation is approximately 6–8 ppm.
  • Step 3Calculate the percentage increase from pre-industrial levels. Pre-industrial: 280 ppm. 2020: ~413 ppm. Increase: (413 − 280) ÷ 280 × 100 ≈ 48%.
  • InterpretThe seasonal cycle reflects the breathing of the land biosphere — the largest in the Northern Hemisphere. The long-term trend reflects human emissions accumulating in the atmosphere faster than natural sinks can absorb them.
CO₂ has increased from 315 ppm (1958) to 413 ppm (2020) — a rise of 98 ppm at ~15.8 ppm/decade. Pre-industrial to 2020 represents approximately a 48% increase.
Chapter 21 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 21.1Climate is the long-term statistical pattern of weather in a region, averaged over at least 30 years. A single cold winter does not disprove a warming trend any more than a single hot day proves one. Climate thinking is statistical.
  • Sec 21.2The Köppen climate classification divides Earth into five major types (tropical, dry, temperate, continental, polar) based on temperature and precipitation. Climate zones shift as climate changes, with large implications for agriculture, biodiversity, and settlement.
  • Sec 21.3Four factors control regional climate: latitude (solar angle and day length), elevation (lapse rate and rain shadows), ocean proximity (maritime moderation), and topography (airflow and precipitation patterns). These factors interact at every scale.
  • Sec 21.4The greenhouse effect warms Earth's surface by slowing the escape of infrared radiation to space. Without it, Earth would average −18°C instead of +15°C. The enhanced greenhouse effect is an intensification of this natural mechanism caused by human emissions.
  • Sec 21.5Carbon cycles through the atmosphere, oceans, land biosphere, and deep Earth. The carbon cycle acts as a long-term thermostat over geologic time but cannot offset rapid human emissions on decadal timescales. Human CO₂ emissions (~37–40 Gt/yr) are approximately 100× the natural volcanic outgassing rate.
  • Sec 21.6Scientists reconstruct past climates using proxy records — ice cores (800,000 years of CO₂ and temperature), tree rings (drought and temperature), ocean sediments (millions of years), corals, pollen, and historical documents. Multiple overlapping proxies from different regions are cross-validated for reliability.
  • Sec 21.7Natural climate variation occurs through Milankovitch orbital cycles, solar output changes, volcanic eruptions, and internal variability (ENSO). None of these factors can explain the warming trend observed since the mid-twentieth century. The current orbital configuration would produce slight cooling, not warming.
  • Sec 21.8Multiple independent lines of evidence confirm human causation: rising CO₂, isotopic fingerprinting of fossil-fuel carbon, temperature trends, asymmetric warming patterns (stratosphere cooling while troposphere warms), ocean warming, and physical changes. Positive feedbacks (water vapor, ice-albedo, permafrost) amplify the initial greenhouse warming.
  • Sec 21.9Climate impacts include more frequent and intense heat waves, altered precipitation, sea level rise (thermal expansion + ice melt), ocean acidification, ecosystem disruption, and threats to food, water, and health. Impacts are unevenly distributed, with lower-income and coastal communities facing greater risk.
  • Sec 21.10Mitigation (reducing emissions) limits future warming; adaptation (adjusting to change) manages unavoidable risks. Warming is proportional to cumulative emissions, so emission reductions have a direct, proportional effect on limiting future warming. Both strategies involve costs, tradeoffs, and limits.
  • Sec 21.11Core findings (warming, human causation, physical changes) rest on robust evidence. Genuine uncertainty remains in climate sensitivity, regional projections, ice sheet stability, and tipping points. Sound reasoning uses the full range of evidence, accounts for uncertainty appropriately, and recognizes that uncertainty cuts both ways.

Key Terms Review

adaptation
Adjusting systems, infrastructure, and practices to manage risks from climate change that is already occurring or expected.
albedo
The fraction of incoming solar radiation reflected by a surface. Ice and snow: high albedo. Dark ocean and land: low albedo.
carbon cycle
The continuous movement of carbon among Earth's reservoirs: atmosphere, oceans, land biosphere, soils, and the deep Earth.
climate
The long-term statistical pattern of weather conditions in a region, typically characterized by averages and variability over at least 30 years.
climate sensitivity
The expected long-term global average temperature increase resulting from a doubling of atmospheric CO₂; likely 2.5°C–4°C with a central estimate of ~3°C.
El Niño–Southern Oscillation (ENSO)
Periodic warming (El Niño) and cooling (La Niña) of tropical Pacific surface water that shifts global temperature and precipitation patterns.
enhanced greenhouse effect
The intensification of the natural greenhouse effect caused by human additions of greenhouse gases to the atmosphere.
environmental lapse rate
The average rate at which temperature decreases with altitude in the atmosphere: approximately 6.5°C per 1,000 meters.
feedback (positive/negative)
A process that amplifies (positive) or dampens (negative) an initial change. Water vapor and ice-albedo feedbacks amplify warming.
greenhouse gas
A gas that absorbs and re-emits infrared radiation, slowing heat loss to space. Major examples: water vapor, CO₂, methane, nitrous oxide.
ice-albedo feedback
A positive feedback in which melting ice exposes darker surfaces that absorb more solar energy, causing further warming and melting.
infrared radiation
Heat radiation emitted by Earth's surface and atmosphere at wavelengths longer than visible light; absorbed by greenhouse gases.
Köppen classification
A widely used system of climate zones based on temperature and precipitation: tropical (A), dry (B), temperate (C), continental (D), polar/highland (E).
Milankovitch cycles
Slow, predictable changes in Earth's orbital eccentricity, axial tilt, and precession that drive glacial-interglacial cycles over tens of thousands of years.
mitigation
Reducing greenhouse gas emissions to limit the severity of future climate change.
ocean acidification
Decrease in ocean pH caused by CO₂ absorption from the atmosphere, forming carbonic acid; threatens shell-forming marine organisms.
permafrost
Ground that remains frozen year-round; thawing permafrost releases stored methane and CO₂, creating a positive feedback.
proxy record
Natural archive — ice cores, tree rings, coral, ocean sediments, pollen — that preserves information about past climate conditions.
rain shadow
The dry area on the leeward side of a mountain range where descending air warms and moisture is lost on the windward side.
sea level rise
Increase in ocean surface height driven by thermal expansion of warming water and addition of water from melting ice; ~20–25 cm since 1900.
temperature anomaly
Departure of temperature from a baseline average period; used to compare climate trends across stations and eliminate geographic differences.
tipping point
A threshold in a climate or Earth system beyond which a large, rapid, and potentially irreversible change can occur.
water vapor feedback
A positive feedback in which warming increases atmospheric water vapor, which is itself a greenhouse gas, causing further warming.
weather
Short-term atmospheric conditions including temperature, precipitation, humidity, and wind — variable from day to day.

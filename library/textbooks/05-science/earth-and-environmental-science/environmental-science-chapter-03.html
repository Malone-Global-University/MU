Chapter 3 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Earth and environmental scientists rarely work in a single place at a single moment in time. They study mountain ranges from satellites, reconstruct ancient climates from ice cores, track hurricanes across ocean basins, and model the movement of groundwater through rock that no one will ever see directly. To do any of this well, scientists need tools — not just instruments in the lab, but systems of representation that allow them to organize, share, and reason about information that is too large, too distant, too slow, or too deep to observe all at once.

This chapter introduces those tools. You will learn how maps represent Earth's curved surface on flat pages, how topographic maps capture the shape of the land, how scientists collect and display data, how remote sensing extends human vision into space and time, and how models and simulations allow scientists to explore systems they cannot directly manipulate. You will also examine how measurement uncertainty is handled honestly — because in Earth science, knowing the limits of your data is as important as knowing the data itself.

These are not merely technical skills. They are ways of thinking. A person who can read a topographic map can look at a drainage pattern and know where water flows. A person who can interpret a graph of temperature records can evaluate a claim about climate change. A person who understands how models are built can ask the right questions about what a model can and cannot tell us. That kind of thinking is what Earth and environmental science is built on.

Opening Question

A geologist stands at the rim of the Grand Canyon looking at rock layers stretching down nearly 1.8 kilometers to the Colorado River. She knows those layers record nearly 2 billion years of Earth history — yet she can hold a piece of that rock in her hand. How does she connect what she sees in front of her to a global map of ancient sea floors? How does she date a rock layer she cannot directly observe forming? And how does she share what she has learned with scientists on the other side of the planet who have never visited this canyon? The answer to each question is the same: scientific tools — maps, models, data, and carefully quantified measurements.

Section 3.1

Why Earth Scientists Use Tools

The Problem of Scale

Earth is large. Its surface covers about 510 million square kilometers. Its interior extends more than 6,000 kilometers to the center. Its atmosphere stretches hundreds of kilometers above the surface. Its history spans 4.6 billion years.

No single scientist, standing in one place at one time, can observe all of this directly. To study Earth, scientists must extend their reach in space and time. They use instruments to measure what eyes cannot detect. They use maps to represent spaces too large to see at once. They use records, cores, fossils, and instruments to look backward through time. They use models and simulations to project forward or explore systems under conditions that do not yet exist.

Earth science faces an especially wide range of scales: from the atomic structure of a mineral grain to the global circulation of the oceans; from the seconds of an earthquake to the billions of years of geologic time. Scientific tools are what make that range navigable.

Direct and Indirect Evidence

Some Earth science observations are direct. A geologist can pick up a rock, describe its texture, measure its mineral composition, and test its hardness with a steel file. A weather observer can read a thermometer, check a rain gauge, and record cloud cover from the surface.

But many important Earth science questions require indirect evidence — measurements and observations that allow scientists to infer what they cannot directly see or touch.

The interior of Earth has never been directly sampled below about 12 kilometers. Scientists know what lies thousands of kilometers below the surface because of the way earthquake waves travel through different materials. Ocean floor spreading was inferred from magnetic patterns in seafloor rocks before any direct sampling confirmed it. The composition of Earth's early atmosphere is inferred from chemical signatures preserved in ancient rocks and minerals.

Key Idea

Indirect evidence is not weaker than direct evidence. It is simply evidence gathered through a longer chain of reasoning. The quality of that reasoning — and the tools used to support it — is what matters.

Tools as Extensions of Human Perception

Scientific tools extend human perception in four main directions:

Section 3.2

Maps and Map Projections

What a Map Is

A map is a scaled representation of some portion of Earth's surface drawn on a flat surface. Every map involves four fundamental choices: what to show, how to project, what scale to use, and what symbols and colors to use.

Because Earth's surface is curved, any attempt to represent it on a flat surface requires a projection — a mathematical transformation that converts the curved surface to a plane. Every projection distorts something: area, shape, distance, or direction. The choice of projection determines which distortions are minimized for a given purpose.

Map Projections and Distortion

Projection TypeWhat It PreservesWhat It DistortsBest Use
MercatorShape (local), directionArea at high latitudes (Greenland appears ~14× too large relative to Africa)Navigation, web maps
Equal-area (e.g., Mollweide)Relative areaShape, especially near edgesComparing sizes of regions; biome or population maps
Azimuthal equidistantDirection from centerShape and area away from centerPolar maps; flight route maps from a hub
Conic (e.g., Albers)Area or shape within a mid-latitude bandDistortion increases away from the standard parallelRegional maps of mid-latitude areas
Common Misconception

Many students believe Greenland is nearly as large as Africa because that is how it appears on Mercator maps. In reality, Africa is about 14 times larger than Greenland. Mercator projections preserve shape and direction but severely distort area at high latitudes. When comparing the size of regions, always use an equal-area projection.

Reading a Map Systematically

Every map should be read in order: (1) read the title — what does this map show and for what time period? (2) read the legend — what do colors, symbols, and line styles mean? (3) check the scale; (4) note the projection and its distortions; (5) identify spatial patterns; (6) ask what the pattern suggests about underlying processes.

Section 3.3

Latitude, Longitude, and Location

The Coordinate System

Latitude measures angular distance north or south of the equator. The equator is 0°; the North Pole is 90°N; the South Pole is 90°S. Lines of latitude run east–west and are parallel to each other — they are sometimes called parallels. All points at the same latitude experience the same angle of sunlight at a given time of year, making latitude one of the most important controls on climate.

Longitude measures angular distance east or west of the prime meridian — the line of 0° longitude passing through Greenwich, England. Lines of longitude run north–south and converge at the poles — they are called meridians. Longitude runs from 0° to 180°E and 0° to 180°W.

Together, latitude and longitude form a grid that uniquely specifies any location on Earth's surface. New York City is approximately 40.7°N, 74.0°W. The summit of Mount Everest is approximately 27.99°N, 86.93°E.

Key Distances

One degree of latitude ≈ 111 km everywhere on Earth.

One degree of longitude ≈ 111 km at the equator, decreasing to 0 km at the poles (meridians converge).

GPS and Earth Science Applications

The Global Positioning System (GPS) uses a network of satellites to determine location by measuring radio-signal travel times from multiple satellites simultaneously. A receiver detecting four or more satellites achieves accuracy of a few meters; scientific-grade GPS instruments achieve centimeter-level accuracy.

GPS has transformed Earth science: geologists use GPS networks to measure tectonic plate motion of just a few centimeters per year in real time. Volcanologists detect ground deformation before eruptions. Hydrologists measure groundwater level changes by tracking tiny surface elevation shifts.

Scale and Distance

Three Ways to Express Scale

Large Scale vs. Small Scale — A Common Confusion

A large-scale map has a large RF numerically (e.g., 1:10,000) and shows a small area in great detail. A small-scale map has a small RF (e.g., 1:10,000,000) and shows a large area with little detail. Think of it this way: on a large-scale map, everything looks large.

Example 3.4.1
Calculating real distance from map distance
  • ProblemOn a 1:50,000 map, you measure 4.5 cm between two points. What is the real distance in kilometers?
  • ThinkReal distance = map distance × scale denominator. Convert centimeters to kilometers at the end.
  • Calculate Real distance = 4.5 cm × 50,000 = 225,000 cm 225,000 cm ÷ 100,000 = 2.25 km
  • CheckA distance of 2.25 km is reasonable for a 4.5-cm span on a moderately detailed map. ✓
Answer: 2.25 km
Example 3.4.2
Calculating rate of tectonic motion
  • ProblemA tectonic plate has moved 200 km in 10 million years. What is the average rate of motion?
  • ThinkRate = Distance ÷ Time. Convert to convenient units.
  • Calculate Rate = 200 km ÷ 10,000,000 yr = 0.00002 km/yr 0.00002 km/yr × 100,000 cm/km = 2 cm/yr
  • Interpret2 cm/year is typical of real plate motion rates — slow by human standards, but enough to build mountain ranges over geologic time. ✓
Answer: 2 cm per year
Section 3.5

Topographic Maps

Why Topography Matters

The shape of Earth's surface — its hills, valleys, slopes, ridges, and plains — is called topography. Topography influences where water flows, where erosion is rapid, where roads and buildings can be safely placed, how cold air pools in valleys, where glaciers form, and how lava moves during a volcanic eruption.

A topographic map represents three-dimensional terrain on a two-dimensional surface using contour lines — lines that connect all points of equal elevation above a reference level (usually mean sea level).

Reading a Topographic Map

FeatureDefinitionWhat to Look For
Contour lineA line connecting all points at the same elevationWalking along a contour = staying at constant elevation
Contour intervalElevation difference between adjacent contour linesAlways stated in the map legend; smaller interval = more terrain detail
Index contourEvery 4th or 5th contour line, drawn thicker and labeled with elevationUse to quickly read elevation without counting every line
Closed contourA contour line forming a complete loopLoops with increasing elevation inward = hill; with hachure marks = depression
Hachure marksShort tick marks pointing inward on a closed depression contourIndicates the enclosed area is lower than surroundings

Topographic Patterns and What They Mean

Section 3.6

Contour Lines and Elevation

Five Rules of Contour Lines

1. Contour lines never cross. A single point cannot have two elevations.

2. Every contour line is a closed loop. Lines that appear to end at a map edge continue beyond the boundary.

3. Contour lines point upstream (uphill) in valleys. The V-shape always points toward higher elevation.

4. The steepest slope is perpendicular to contour lines. Water runs directly downslope, crossing contours at right angles.

5. Contour lines do not branch or fork on a normal continuous surface.

Example 3.6.1
Reading elevation from a contour map
  • ProblemThe nearest index contour is labeled 200 m. There are 3 regular contour lines between the index contour and a point uphill. The contour interval is 20 m. What is the elevation of the point?
  • ThinkStart from the index contour and count up, multiplying by the interval.
  • Calculate Elevation = 200 m + (3 × 20 m) = 200 + 60 = 260 m
  • CheckEach regular contour adds one interval. Three intervals above 200 m = 260 m. ✓
Answer: 260 meters

Constructing an Elevation Profile

An elevation profile (topographic cross-section) shows the shape of the land along a straight line, viewed from the side. To construct one: (1) draw a straight line from point A to point B on the topographic map; (2) mark where each contour line intersects the line; (3) on a separate grid, plot each intersection at the correct elevation; (4) connect the points with a smooth curve. Elevation profiles are used to plan road routes, visualize slopes, and study rock layer exposure.

Reading Graphs Critically

When reading any graph in Earth science, ask: What does the x-axis represent (units? scale?)? What does the y-axis represent (does the scale start at zero — if not, small changes can appear large)? What is the data source and time period? What is the overall trend? Are there anomalies? What uncertainty is shown by error bars?

Graph Reading Warning — Truncated Axes

A graph with a y-axis that does not start at zero can make a small change appear dramatic. Always check where the y-axis starts before interpreting the magnitude of a trend. Compare, for example, a temperature graph from 13.5°C to 15°C versus one from 0°C to 20°C — the same data will look very different.

Example 3.7.1
Calculating a rate from a data table
  • ProblemSea level was approximately 0 cm (relative to a 1990 baseline) in 1990, and approximately 9 cm above baseline in 2020. What is the average rate of sea-level rise?
  • ThinkRate = ΔValue ÷ ΔTime, where Δ means "change in."
  • Calculate Rate = 9 cm ÷ 30 years = 0.3 cm/year = 3 mm/year
  • InterpretThis matches observed global average sea-level rise — a small but consistent and measurable trend with significant long-term consequences for low-lying coasts.
Answer: 3 mm per year
Section 3.8

Remote Sensing and Satellite Data

What Remote Sensing Is

Remote sensing is the collection of information about Earth from a distance — typically from aircraft or satellites — without direct physical contact. Remote sensing instruments detect electromagnetic radiation (visible light, infrared, radar, and other wavelengths) reflected or emitted from Earth's surface, atmosphere, or interior.

Remote sensing has transformed Earth science because it enables global observations at regular intervals, reaches regions impossible to access directly, and produces enormous datasets that reveal patterns invisible to ground-based observation.

Remote Sensing Technologies

RadarEmits radio waves and measures return; penetrates cloudsTopographic mapping; ground deformation; soil moisture; sea surface roughness
LidarEmits laser pulses and measures return; very preciseHigh-precision topography; coastal erosion; glacier surface change; forest canopy height

Long-Term Satellite Records

Some satellite programs have collected data continuously for decades, creating irreplaceable records of Earth system change. The Landsat program has produced continuous land surface imagery since 1972. The GRACE satellite pair measured tiny variations in Earth's gravity field to track changes in groundwater and ice sheet mass. These long-term records are among the most valuable datasets in Earth science for separating natural variability from systematic trends.

Section 3.9

Models and Simulations

What a Scientific Model Is

In science, a model is a simplified representation of a real system used to understand, explain, or predict how the system works. All models simplify. A model that included every detail of the real system would be the system itself. The value of a model lies precisely in what it leaves out — simplifying enough to be tractable while retaining enough to capture the behavior of interest.

The goal of a model is not to be a perfect replica of reality. The goal is to be useful: to allow scientists to understand mechanisms, test hypotheses, explore what-if scenarios, and communicate insights.

Types of Models in Earth Science

Model TypeDescriptionEarth Science Example
ConceptualDiagram, flowchart, or word description showing how a system worksWater cycle diagram; Earth system sphere interaction chart
Physical3D object representing a system at reduced scaleStream table for studying river processes; seismic wave tank
MathematicalEquations relating variables in a systemTemperature vs. depth equations; sea-level response to ice volume change
Computational (simulation)Mathematical model implemented in software, run over timeGeneral circulation models (GCMs) simulating global climate; volcanic ash dispersal models

Evaluating Models

Because models are simplifications, they have limitations. Every model involves assumptions about which processes to include, how to represent them mathematically, and what boundary conditions to apply. Evaluating a model means asking:

Questions for Evaluating Any Scientific Model

What does the model include, and what does it leave out? Omitted processes cannot influence output.

How well does the model reproduce known observations? A model that cannot reproduce past records likely misses something important.

What are the model's uncertainties? Running a model many times with slightly different parameters reveals the range of possible outcomes.

What questions can the model answer, and what can it not? Using a model outside its intended range produces unreliable results.

Models and Scientific Progress

Some of the most important advances in Earth science have come from new models. The theory of plate tectonics required a model of how rigid lithospheric plates move on a weaker asthenosphere. Climate science advanced through general circulation models. Earthquake hazard assessment depends on models combining fault geometry, slip rate, and soil properties. Models are not the final word — they are tools for generating and testing understanding. When models and observations disagree, something interesting is happening.

Section 3.10

Measurement, Error, and Uncertainty

Why Measurement Is Never Perfect

Every measurement contains some uncertainty. No instrument is infinitely precise. No sample is perfectly representative of the whole. Understanding measurement uncertainty is not a sign of scientific weakness — it is a mark of scientific honesty and rigor.

In Earth science, where phenomena span vast scales, measurement uncertainty is often large. A temperature record reconstructed from tree rings carries more uncertainty than one measured with a modern thermometer. The age of a rock calculated from radioactive decay carries uncertainty depending on how precisely isotope ratios can be measured.

Accuracy and Precision

PropertyDefinitionAffected By
AccuracyHow close a measured value is to the true valueSystematic errors — consistent biases in a measurement process
PrecisionHow consistent repeated measurements are with each otherRandom errors — small, unpredictable variations in a measurement process
Accuracy and Precision Are Not the Same

A thermometer that consistently reads 2°C too high is precise but not accurate. A thermometer that varies widely around the true temperature is accurate on average but not precise. Scientists aim for both — but must identify which type of error affects each instrument and method. Systematic errors are corrected by improving calibration; random errors are reduced by repeating measurements.

Types of Error in Earth Science

Error TypeSourceExample
Instrument errorLimits of the measuring deviceA rain gauge with 1-mm resolution cannot detect finer amounts; a seismograph may saturate during very large earthquakes
Sampling errorSample is not perfectly representative of the wholeMeasuring soil pH at 10 points to characterize an entire field
Observer errorVariation in how people interpret and record observationsTwo geologists using slightly different descriptive terms for the same rock sample
Model errorMathematical model does not perfectly represent the real processRadioactive dating assumes no daughter isotopes were present initially; if violated, the calculated age is inaccurate

Significant Figures and Error Bars

Significant figures indicate how precisely a measurement is known. A measurement reported as 142.3 m is known to the nearest 0.1 m (four significant figures). In Earth science it is common to report measurements with explicit uncertainty bounds:

Reporting Uncertainty

Example: "The average temperature was 14.7°C ± 0.3°C."

This means the true value is likely between 14.4°C and 15.0°C. The ± notation gives the range of uncertainty. On graphs, this range is shown as an error bar — a vertical line through each data point.

Uncertainty Does Not Mean Ignorance

Consider two statements: "Global temperatures have risen." versus "Global average surface temperature increased by 1.1°C ± 0.1°C between 1850 and 2020." The second statement is more useful precisely because it quantifies how much and how certain. Scientists who quantify uncertainty know something very specific — they know the bounds within which their measurement is likely correct. Acknowledging uncertainty is what allows data to be trusted, compared, and built upon.

Chapter 3 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 3.1Earth scientists use tools to extend observation across spatial, temporal, quantitative, and communicative dimensions. Indirect evidence — inferred rather than directly observed — is central to Earth science and is not inherently weaker than direct observation.
  • Sec 3.2Maps represent Earth's curved surface on flat planes using projections. Every projection distorts area, shape, distance, or direction. Mercator preserves direction but distorts area; equal-area projections preserve area but distort shape. No projection is universally best — the choice depends on purpose.
  • Sec 3.3Latitude measures angular distance north or south of the equator; longitude measures angular distance east or west of the prime meridian. Together they form a global grid. GPS allows real-time, centimeter-precision location measurement, enabling scientists to track plate motion, volcanic deformation, and groundwater changes.
  • Sec 3.4Map scale is the ratio of map distance to ground distance, expressed as a representative fraction, verbal statement, or graphic bar. Large-scale maps show small areas in detail; small-scale maps show large areas with less detail. Rates are calculated by dividing distance by time.
  • Sec 3.5Topographic maps use contour lines to represent three-dimensional terrain. Contour interval, index contours, and the spacing and shape of lines allow readers to identify hills, valleys, ridges, depressions, steep slopes, and stream directions.
  • Sec 3.6Five rules govern contour lines: they never cross, always form closed loops, point upstream in valleys, are crossed at right angles by the steepest slope, and do not branch. Elevation profiles (cross-sections) translate contour information into a side view of terrain.
  • Sec 3.7Line graphs, bar graphs, scatter plots, pie charts, and histograms each serve different purposes. Reading graphs carefully requires attention to axis labels, scale origins, data sources, trends, anomalies, and uncertainty (error bars). Rates are extracted by dividing changes in a variable by elapsed time.
  • Sec 3.8Remote sensing collects information about Earth from a distance using electromagnetic radiation. Visible, infrared, radar, and lidar instruments each reveal different Earth properties. Long-term satellite records — some spanning more than 40 years — are among the most valuable datasets in Earth science.
  • Sec 3.9Scientific models — conceptual, physical, mathematical, and computational — simplify reality to make it understandable and predictable. Every model makes assumptions and has limitations. Evaluating a model means understanding what it includes, what it omits, how well it reproduces observations, and what questions it can and cannot address.
  • Sec 3.10Every measurement contains uncertainty. Accuracy refers to closeness to the true value; precision refers to consistency among repeated measurements. Instrument, sampling, observer, and model errors affect results in different ways. Reporting uncertainty honestly — with ± bounds and error bars — is a mark of scientific rigor, not weakness.

Key Terms Review

accuracy
How close a measured value is to the true value. Affected by systematic errors and calibration of instruments.
azimuthal projection
A map projection centered on a point that preserves true direction from that point. Used for polar and hub-and-spoke maps.
bar graph
A graph comparing discrete categories using bars of different heights or lengths.
conformal projection
A map projection that preserves the shape of small regions at the cost of distorting area at large scales.
contour interval
The difference in elevation between adjacent contour lines on a topographic map. Always stated in the map legend.
contour line
A line on a topographic map connecting all points at the same elevation. Contour lines never cross.
coordinate system
A reference framework for specifying location. On Earth's surface, this is the grid of latitude and longitude.
elevation profile
A diagram showing variation in elevation along a straight line across a topographic map; also called a cross-section.
equal-area projection
A map projection that preserves the relative areas of regions, at the cost of shape distortion. Best for comparing sizes.
error bar
A line through a data point on a graph showing the range of measurement uncertainty associated with that value.
GPS
Global Positioning System — a satellite network allowing receivers to determine latitude, longitude, and elevation with meter-level or better accuracy.
hachure marks
Short tick marks on closed contour lines indicating that the enclosed area is a depression (lower than surroundings).
index contour
Every 4th or 5th contour line, drawn thicker and labeled with its elevation, for easy reading of topographic maps.
latitude
Angular distance north or south of the equator, measured in degrees. Parallels run east–west; 1° ≈ 111 km.
lidar
Light Detection and Ranging — a remote sensing technology using laser pulses to measure distances and map topography with centimeter precision.
line graph
A graph showing how a variable changes over time or how two continuous variables are related, using a connected line of data points.
longitude
Angular distance east or west of the prime meridian (Greenwich), measured in degrees. Meridians run north–south; 1° varies from 111 km at the equator to 0 km at the poles.
map projection
A mathematical method for representing Earth's curved surface on a flat plane. Every projection distorts at least one spatial property.
map scale
The ratio of distance on a map to the corresponding distance on the ground, expressed as a representative fraction, verbal statement, or graphic bar.
Mercator projection
A cylindrical conformal projection that preserves shape and direction but severely distorts area at high latitudes. Not appropriate for comparing region sizes.
model
A simplified representation of a real system — conceptual, physical, mathematical, or computational — used to understand, explain, or predict system behavior.
multispectral imagery
Satellite imagery combining data from multiple wavelength regions of the electromagnetic spectrum to distinguish features invisible in visible light alone.
precision
How consistent repeated measurements are with each other. High precision means low random variation. Precision does not guarantee accuracy.
radar
Radio Detection and Ranging — a remote sensing technology using radio wave pulses to detect surfaces, topography, and deformation even through cloud cover.
remote sensing
The collection of information about Earth from a distance, typically from aircraft or satellites, using electromagnetic radiation.
representative fraction
A map scale expressed as a ratio (e.g., 1:24,000) meaning 1 unit on the map equals 24,000 of the same units on the ground.
scatter plot
A graph showing the relationship between two continuous variables, with each observation plotted as a single point.
significant figures
Digits in a measurement that convey meaningful precision. A measurement of 142.3 m is known to ±0.1 m (four significant figures).
systematic error
A consistent, repeatable bias in a measurement process that affects accuracy. Corrected by calibration, not by averaging more measurements.
topographic map
A map using contour lines to represent the three-dimensional shape of Earth's surface, including elevation, slope, valleys, ridges, and plains.
topography
The shape and relief of Earth's surface, including hills, valleys, slopes, ridges, and plains.
uncertainty
The range within which a measured or modeled value is expected to fall. Reported as ± bounds or shown as error bars on graphs.

Common Misconceptions

Misconception 1 — "Greenland is almost as large as Africa"
The Mercator map shows Greenland nearly the size of Africa, so they must be similar in area.

Africa is approximately 14 times larger than Greenland. The Mercator projection severely distorts area at high latitudes. Greenland appears inflated because it is near the pole. Always use an equal-area projection when comparing the sizes of regions.

Misconception 2 — "Large-scale maps show large areas"
A large-scale map must show more of Earth's surface because "large" means big.

A large-scale map (e.g., 1:10,000) shows a small area in great detail — the ratio is large numerically. A small-scale map (e.g., 1:10,000,000) shows a large area with less detail. The term refers to the size of the ratio, not the size of the area shown.

Misconception 3 — "Uncertainty means scientists don't know"
When scientists report ±0.1°C, it shows they are uncertain and the data cannot be trusted.

Reporting uncertainty is a mark of rigor, not ignorance. A scientist who reports "14.7°C ± 0.1°C" knows the value lies between 14.6°C and 14.8°C — that is very specific knowledge. A scientist who reports no uncertainty may simply be hiding information others need to evaluate the claim.

Misconception 4 — "Contour lines point downstream in valleys"
The V-shape of contours in a valley must point toward lower elevation (downstream), because that's where the water goes.

Contour lines always point upstream (uphill) when crossing a valley or stream. This is because the valley floor is lower than the surrounding hillsides. The V opens toward lower elevation — the vertex points uphill, toward the stream's source.

Misconception 5 — "Indirect evidence is weaker evidence"
Scientists who study Earth's interior are just guessing because no one has been there. Direct observation is always better.

Indirect evidence — inferred through a chain of reasoning — is not inherently weaker than direct evidence. The composition and structure of Earth's deep interior is known through seismic wave analysis with extraordinary detail and consistency. The quality of the reasoning and the convergence of multiple lines of evidence determine reliability, not whether the measurement was direct or indirect.

Chapter 28 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Earth is an active planet. Earthquakes shake the ground, volcanoes erupt, rivers flood, winds strengthen into storms, slopes fail, droughts dry out landscapes, and wildfires consume forests. These are natural processes — they do not become hazards until people, communities, and the things we value are in their path. This chapter brings together nearly everything you have studied in Earth and environmental science: geology, plate tectonics, water systems, weather, climate, ecosystems, and human land use — all focused through the lens of risk, preparedness, and the choices communities make to protect themselves.

Understanding natural hazards is one of the most important things Earth science can offer any citizen. The knowledge in this chapter can save lives — and has already saved hundreds of thousands of them through better monitoring, mapping, warning systems, and community preparedness.

This chapter is also a synthesis. Nearly every major concept from this textbook reappears here: plate tectonics explains where earthquakes and volcanoes cluster; the water cycle explains where floods form; atmospheric science explains where and why storms intensify; climate science explains how drought and wildfire risk are changing. Systems thinking reveals that the greatest risks often arise where multiple hazards overlap — and that the most effective resilience strategies address Earth systems together, not individual hazards in isolation.

Opening Question

Two earthquakes of similar magnitude strike. One kills fewer than 100 people; the other kills more than 80,000. Both are powerful, both are sudden, both occur in geologically similar settings. What explains the difference? By the end of this chapter, you will have the scientific framework to answer that question — and to understand why the answer matters for every community on Earth.

Section 28.1

Natural Events and Natural Hazards

The Difference Between a Process and a Hazard

An earthquake in an uninhabited mountain range shakes the ground, shifts rock, and may trigger a landslide into a valley stream. It is a geological event. The same magnitude earthquake beneath a densely populated city collapses buildings, ruptures water mains, and kills thousands. Both are the same kind of natural event. Only one is a disaster.

This distinction matters enormously. A natural event becomes a natural hazard when it has the potential to harm people, damage infrastructure, disrupt economies, or damage ecosystems. A natural event becomes a disaster when those potential harms are actually realized at significant scale.

Key Distinction

Natural event — a geological, hydrological, atmospheric, or biological process occurring in nature

Natural hazard — a natural event with the potential to harm people or things people value

Natural disaster — a hazard event that actually causes significant loss of life, property, or ecosystem function at scale

Natural events are driven by Earth's systems. They are not rare exceptions — they are what Earth does. The geosphere moves, shifts, and cracks; the hydrosphere flows, rises, and surges; the atmosphere circulates, heats, and intensifies; the biosphere dries and ignites. Often, all four systems interact in a single event: a hurricane brings storm surge, heavy rain, flooding, and landslides at the same time.

The fact that these events keep happening is not a failure of nature. It is nature functioning. The question Earth scientists and communities must ask is: how do we understand these events well enough to protect ourselves and prepare for them?

How Earth's Systems Generate Hazards

Earth SystemProcessesAssociated Hazards
GeospherePlate movement, fault rupture, volcanic activity, slope failure, subsidenceEarthquakes, volcanic eruptions, landslides, liquefaction
HydrospherePrecipitation, runoff, storm surge, wave action, tsunamisRiver floods, flash floods, coastal floods, tsunamis

A Brief History of Hazard Science

For much of human history, natural disasters were understood as acts of divine will or fate — things that happened to people with no predictable pattern. As Earth science developed in the 19th and early 20th centuries, scientists began mapping where earthquakes and volcanoes occurred. They noticed patterns: earthquakes cluster along fault lines, volcanoes concentrate at plate boundaries, floods tend to follow river valleys. Pattern recognition was the first step toward prediction.

Today, hazard science integrates geology, hydrology, meteorology, oceanography, ecology, engineering, public health, social science, and planning. A seismologist can calculate the probability of a large earthquake — but protecting communities from that earthquake also requires architects, civil engineers, emergency managers, community leaders, and governments working together. Understanding natural hazards is therefore both a science and a civic responsibility.

Section 28.2

Hazard, Exposure, Vulnerability, and Risk

To think carefully about natural hazards, scientists use four key concepts. These are not the same thing, and confusing them leads to poor decisions about where to build, how to prepare, and where to invest in protection.

Hazard

A hazard is the natural process or event itself — the earthquake, flood, hurricane, landslide, or wildfire. Hazard is characterized by its type, magnitude, frequency, duration, spatial extent, and speed of onset. Does it strike suddenly (earthquake) or develop gradually (drought)? Hazard is a property of the natural environment. It exists whether or not people are present.

Exposure

Exposure is the presence of people, buildings, infrastructure, crops, ecosystems, or anything of value in areas where a hazard can reach them. A city built on a floodplain is exposed to flooding. A town on a volcanic island is exposed to eruption. Exposure is largely a function of where people choose — or are compelled — to live and build. Over time, human settlement has tended to concentrate in places that offer resources (rivers, coasts, fertile volcanic soils) that also happen to be hazard-prone.

Vulnerability

Vulnerability is the degree to which people and systems can be harmed by a hazard. It is shaped by multiple factors:

Dimension of VulnerabilityDescription and Examples
PhysicalQuality of buildings and infrastructure; a reinforced concrete building performs very differently than an unreinforced brick building in an earthquake
SocialIncome, age, disability, language barriers, access to healthcare, social networks — all affect how badly people are harmed and how quickly they recover
EconomicBusinesses, farms, and governments with fewer financial resources recover more slowly from disaster
InstitutionalWeak governance, poor planning, and inadequate emergency management increase harm; strong institutions reduce it

Risk

The Risk Framework

Risk = Hazard × Exposure × Vulnerability

This is a framework, not a precise equation. It means that risk can be reduced by addressing any of its three components: reduce exposure by not building in floodplains; reduce vulnerability by constructing earthquake-resistant buildings; understand the hazard better so warnings can be issued in time. Risk is not destiny — it can be measured, mapped, communicated, and reduced.

Risk Misperception

One of the most consistent findings in hazard science is that people often misjudge risk. People tend to fear dramatic, sudden events (earthquakes, volcanic eruptions) more than slow-onset events (drought, heat waves) — even when the slow-onset events kill far more people over time. Heat waves are among the deadliest hazards globally, but they rarely feel as frightening as a volcano. Effective hazard communication must account for how people perceive and respond to risk information, not only what the data shows.

Section 28.3

Earthquake and Volcano Risk

Earthquake Risk

Earthquakes are among the most geographically predictable hazards on Earth. Because they occur almost exclusively along plate boundaries and major fault zones, scientists can map regions of elevated seismic hazard with considerable accuracy. Seismic hazard maps display the probability that ground shaking of a given intensity will occur within a specified time period — typically 50 years. These maps combine historical earthquake records, geological fault mapping, geophysical measurements of fault activity, and statistical models of earthquake recurrence.

Seismic hazard is not evenly distributed. The western United States, Japan, Turkey, Chile, Indonesia, and many other regions face very high seismic hazard. But hazard alone does not determine risk. A densely populated city on an active fault with many unreinforced masonry buildings faces far higher risk than a sparsely populated region on the same fault.

Key Factors in Earthquake Damage

FactorDescription
Ground shaking intensityDepends on earthquake magnitude, distance from the fault, and local geology. Loose, water-saturated sediments amplify shaking dramatically — a phenomenon called site amplification
LiquefactionWater-saturated sandy soils can temporarily behave like a liquid when shaken, causing buildings to sink or tip; common in river deltas, bay fills, and coastal areas
Building qualityThe single most important factor in survival; unreinforced masonry collapses, while properly designed reinforced structures flex and survive
Secondary hazardsFires from broken gas lines, landslides from shaking, and tsunamis from undersea fault rupture can cause as much or more damage than the initial shaking
Earthquake Risk Reduction

Building codes requiring earthquake-resistant construction, retrofitting older at-risk buildings, landslide and liquefaction hazard mapping to guide land use, early warning systems (providing seconds to tens of seconds of warning), and public education about protective actions during and after shaking are all proven strategies for reducing earthquake risk.

Volcano Risk

Volcanic hazards are diverse and depend heavily on the type of volcano, the type of eruption, and topography and wind patterns. Volcanoes can often be monitored for warning signs before eruption — increased seismic activity, ground deformation, changes in gas emissions — allowing time-critical evacuations that save lives.

Volcanic HazardDescription and Risk
Lava flowsStreams of molten rock; can destroy structures but generally move slowly enough for evacuation
Pyroclastic flowsFast-moving avalanches of hot gas, ash, and rock fragments; among the most deadly volcanic hazards — travel at highway speeds with no warning
Ash fallCan collapse roofs, contaminate water, disrupt aviation, damage crops, and cause respiratory illness far from the eruption
LaharsVolcanic mudflows triggered when eruption interacts with ice, snow, or heavy rain; travel far down river valleys and can strike hours after an eruption
Volcanic gasesSulfur dioxide, carbon dioxide, and hydrogen sulfide can be lethal at high concentrations near the vent
TsunamisEruptions at or near coastlines, or volcanic island collapses, can generate destructive ocean waves
Case Study
Mount Pinatubo, Philippines, 1991

The 1991 eruption of Mount Pinatubo was one of the largest volcanic eruptions of the 20th century. Scientists monitoring the volcano detected increasing seismic activity, ground deformation, and rising sulfur dioxide emissions weeks before the climactic eruption. This allowed the evacuation of tens of thousands of people from the highest-risk zones. While the eruption was devastating — depositing ash across a wide area and triggering massive lahars that buried towns — the advance warning and evacuation prevented what could have been catastrophic loss of life. The Pinatubo case is a landmark example of how volcanic monitoring directly saves lives.

Section 28.4

Floods and Coastal Hazards

Floods

Flooding is the most widespread and costly natural hazard on Earth. It occurs when water overflows normal channels or drainage systems and inundates land that is typically dry. River floods occur when precipitation or snowmelt exceeds the capacity of a river channel. Flash floods develop from intense rainfall over a short time in steep terrain and can arrive with little warning. Urban floods occur when impervious surfaces prevent infiltration, overwhelming storm drains. Coastal floods push ocean water inland via storm surge, tsunamis, or sea level changes.

Floodplains — the flat areas adjacent to rivers naturally inundated during high-flow events — are among the most fertile and densely settled lands on Earth. This creates inherent tension: the same features that make floodplains attractive for settlement make them hazardous.

Recurrence Intervals and Flood Probability

A "100-year flood" does not mean a flood that happens once every century. It means a flood with a 1% probability of occurring in any given year. In any 30-year period, there is approximately a 26% chance of experiencing at least one 100-year flood. These statistics are often misunderstood by the public and by policymakers — which leads to underestimating flood risk over time.

Flood risk has been increasing in many areas because of urban development that replaces permeable land with impervious surfaces, removal of wetlands that naturally absorb floodwaters, changes in precipitation associated with climate change, subsidence of coastal cities due to groundwater withdrawal, and sea level rise.

Coastal Hazards

Storm surge — the abnormal rise in sea level caused by a hurricane's winds and low pressure — causes the majority of hurricane deaths along coastlines, not wind. Surge height depends on storm intensity, size, track angle relative to the coast, and coastal geometry. Funnel-shaped bays concentrate surge to extreme heights.

Tsunamis, generated by seafloor earthquakes, volcanic eruptions, or submarine landslides, are most hazardous in the Pacific Ocean basin, which is ringed by subduction zones. Warning systems, public education about natural warning signs (ground shaking, unusual sea withdrawal), and well-planned evacuation routes are the primary tools for reducing tsunami deaths.

Coastal erosion is a persistent long-term challenge. Rising sea levels, more intense storms, and reduced sediment supply (often due to dams on upstream rivers) are accelerating shoreline retreat in many regions, placing structures built at the shoreline at growing risk.

Section 28.5

Severe Weather Hazards

Thunderstorms and Tornadoes

Thunderstorms produce lightning, heavy rain, hail, strong winds, and can spawn tornadoes. Tornadoes are rotating columns of air extending from a thunderstorm to the ground, rated on the Enhanced Fujita (EF) scale from EF0 (light damage) to EF5 (catastrophic damage, estimated winds above 200 mph). Tornadoes are most frequent in the central United States but occur on every continent except Antarctica. Tornado warning times have improved significantly with Doppler radar, but the average lead time remains about 13 minutes — making community preparedness and accessible shelter locations essential.

Hurricanes

Hurricanes are large, rotating storm systems that form over warm tropical ocean water, requiring sea surface temperatures of at least 26–27°C to form and sustain. The Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Wind Scale rates intensity from Category 1 (dangerous) to Category 5 (catastrophic). However, wind category alone is a poor predictor of damage: a slower-moving, lower-category hurricane can produce more rainfall and flooding than a faster-moving major hurricane. Storm surge, as noted above, is typically the deadliest element. Climate science indicates that as ocean temperatures rise, hurricanes that form are likely to become more intense and to produce more rainfall, even if the total number of storms does not increase dramatically.

Winter Storms

Winter storms present hazards through snow, ice storms (freezing rain), sleet, and blizzards. Ice storms are particularly damaging — coating surfaces in freezing rain, collapsing trees and power lines, and making roads treacherous. Blizzards combine heavy snow with sustained high winds, creating dangerous wind chills and near-zero visibility. Winter storm deaths are often overlooked in hazard discussions because they are distributed across many smaller incidents — car accidents, falls, hypothermia, cardiac events from shoveling — rather than concentrated in a single dramatic event.

Compounding Hazards

Hurricanes rarely arrive as a single hazard. A landfalling hurricane typically brings storm surge at the coast, heavy inland rainfall causing river flooding, tornadoes spawned in outer bands, and landslides on steep terrain after prolonged saturation. Planning for any single hazard in isolation misses how these events interact. Systems thinking is essential.

Section 28.6

Drought, Heat, and Wildfire

Drought

Drought is a period of abnormally dry conditions that persists long enough to cause problems for people, agriculture, ecosystems, or water supplies. It develops slowly, often over months to years, and ends gradually — making it easy to underestimate its severity until it is well established. Drought is one of the most economically damaging of all natural hazards globally.

Drought TypeDescription
Meteorological droughtBelow-average precipitation over a defined period; the foundation from which other drought types develop
Agricultural droughtSoil moisture deficits that reduce crop yields and stress livestock, even if streams are not yet affected
Hydrological droughtReduced streamflow, lake levels, and groundwater as storage reserves are depleted
Socioeconomic droughtWater supply can no longer meet societal demand; affects cities, industries, and ecosystems simultaneously

Drought is intensified by high temperatures (which increase evaporation and plant water demand), poor land management, and over-allocation of water resources. Climate change is projected to intensify droughts in many already-dry regions. The Palmer Drought Severity Index (PDSI) and the U.S. Drought Monitor are widely used tools for tracking conditions and triggering responses.

Extreme Heat

Heat waves — extended periods of abnormally high temperatures — are the deadliest weather-related hazard in many high-income countries. They are also one of the least visible. Heat wave deaths tend to occur quietly, among elderly people, those with chronic health conditions, outdoor workers, people without air conditioning, and urban residents exposed to the urban heat island effect: the phenomenon in which cities replace vegetation (which cools through evapotranspiration) with pavement and rooftops that absorb and retain heat. Urban areas can be 2–5°C warmer than surrounding rural areas, especially at night — when temperatures need to drop for the human body to recover from daytime heat stress.

Wildfire

Wildfires are fires that spread through natural vegetation — forests, shrublands, grasslands. They are natural processes in many ecosystems, with some plants requiring fire to reproduce. But as human development expands into fire-prone landscapes — the wildland-urban interface (WUI) — wildfire risk to people and property increases dramatically. Wildfire behavior is controlled by three factors: fuel (the amount and type of vegetation), weather (heat, low humidity, and wind), and topography (fires move faster uphill and terrain channels wind).

Rising temperatures, earlier snowmelt, and prolonged drought are drying out vegetation and extending the fire season. The area burned annually in the western United States has increased dramatically in recent decades. Wildfire smoke is a growing public health hazard: fine particles (PM2.5) in smoke travel thousands of miles from fires and degrade air quality across large regions, with disproportionate impacts on children and older adults.

Section 28.7

Landslides and Mass Movement Hazards

Types of Mass Movement

Mass movement — also called mass wasting — refers to the downslope movement of rock, soil, mud, or debris under the influence of gravity. It occurs on a wide spectrum from imperceptibly slow creep to catastrophically fast rock avalanches.

TypeMaterialTypical SpeedWater Content
RockfallRock fragmentsVery fastLow
Rock avalancheLarge rock massVery fastLow
Landslide (slide)Rock or soilSlow to fastVariable
Debris flowRock, soil, organic materialFastHigh
MudflowFine sedimentFastVery high
EarthflowSoilSlow to moderateModerate–high
CreepSoilExtremely slowVariable

Triggers of Landslides

Slopes fail when the forces driving movement (primarily gravity) exceed the forces resisting it (primarily friction and cohesion within the material). Triggers include heavy rainfall that saturates soil and reduces friction, rapid snowmelt, earthquakes that shake slopes momentarily, volcanic activity that destabilizes hillsides, and human activity — road cuts, construction, deforestation, irrigation, and adding weight to slopes.

Debris flows deserve special attention. These fast-moving mixtures of water, rock, and soil can travel at highway speeds, move miles from their source, and strike with virtually no warning. They are especially common in steep, recently burned landscapes after intense rainfall — a pattern that has become more frequent in western states as wildfires grow larger and more intense.

Post-Fire Debris Flow Hazard

When wildfire burns through steep terrain, it removes the vegetation that stabilizes soil and creates a water-repellent layer in the soil surface. Heavy rainfall on a recently burned slope can rapidly generate debris flows that carry enormous volumes of rock, mud, and woody material into populated valleys. Communities below recently burned areas should be aware of elevated debris flow risk during the following rainy season.

Section 28.8

Risk Mapping and Warning Systems

Hazard and Risk Maps

Hazard maps show where natural events of a given type and magnitude can be expected based on geological, meteorological, and historical evidence. Common types include seismic hazard maps (probability of ground shaking), volcano hazard zone maps, FEMA flood insurance rate maps (100-year and 500-year floodplains), landslide susceptibility maps, wildfire risk maps, and storm surge inundation maps.

Risk maps go further, combining hazard with exposure and vulnerability data to show where potential losses to people and property are highest. They are essential tools for emergency managers planning responses, city planners making land use decisions, insurance companies pricing risk, governments prioritizing infrastructure investment, and individuals evaluating where to live.

Limitations of Hazard Maps

Hazard maps represent probabilities, not certainties. They are based on historical records that may not capture all possible events. Natural systems change — climate change is shifting flood and wildfire hazard zones. A line on a map is not a sharp boundary in nature. And political and economic pressures can sometimes distort how maps are drawn and used. Understanding what a hazard map shows, and what it does not show, is an important scientific literacy skill.

Warning Systems

Warning systems provide time for people to take protective action before a hazard strikes. Their effectiveness depends on four elements: detection (sensors, satellites, networks), analysis (rapid data processing to assess severity and likely impact), communication (getting accurate warnings to the right people in time), and response (people who know what the warning means and what to do).

Lead Time Shapes Preparedness Strategy

The difference in lead time between earthquake warnings (seconds) and drought warnings (months) illustrates why different hazards require fundamentally different preparedness approaches. For earthquakes, the priority is immediate automatic protective action and earthquake-resistant buildings. For drought, the priority is long-term water management planning and early social support for affected farmers and communities. There is no single "natural hazard preparedness" strategy — it must be tailored to the hazard type.

Section 28.9

Preparedness, Mitigation, Response, and Recovery

Effective hazard management requires action before, during, and after a hazardous event. Emergency managers organize these actions into four phases, often called the emergency management cycle.

Mitigation

Mitigation refers to actions taken to reduce the likelihood of a disaster or to reduce its impacts when it does occur. Mitigation can be structural (seismic retrofitting, levee construction, fire-resistant roofing), non-structural (zoning laws restricting development in floodplains, building codes, insurance requirements), or ecosystem-based (restoring wetlands that buffer floods, preserving forests that slow runoff, managing fuels to reduce wildfire intensity).

Mitigation is often underfunded because its benefits are invisible — we do not see the disasters that did not happen. Research consistently shows that every dollar spent on hazard mitigation saves multiple dollars in disaster recovery costs.

Preparedness

Preparedness involves planning, training, equipping, and educating people so that they can protect themselves and respond effectively when a hazard strikes. At the household level this means emergency supply kits, family communication plans, knowledge of local hazards, and signing up for local alert systems. At the community level it means emergency operations plans, regular drills, trained emergency responders, and mutual aid agreements between neighboring jurisdictions. At the institutional level it means continuity of operations plans for hospitals, schools, and utilities.

In the immediate aftermath of a major disaster, outside help may take days to arrive. Communities that can care for themselves during that initial window survive better.

Response

Response is the immediate action taken during and just after a hazardous event: search and rescue, evacuation support, emergency shelter, emergency medical care, utility restoration, and damage assessment. Effective response depends on preparedness. Communities that have planned, trained, and equipped themselves respond more effectively than those that have not.

Recovery

Recovery is the long-term process of restoring communities, economies, and ecosystems after a disaster. It can take months, years, or decades. Recovery is not simply returning to the status quo. "Build back better" is the principle that recovery should improve resilience — repairing infrastructure to higher standards, relocating structures away from the most hazard-prone areas, and strengthening community social networks.

Recovery is profoundly unequal. Communities with more financial resources, stronger social networks, stable housing, and access to government programs recover faster. Inequalities that exist before a disaster tend to be amplified by it.

Section 28.10

Resilient Communities

What Resilience Means

Resilience is the ability of a system — whether a person, a household, a community, or an ecosystem — to absorb a disturbance, adapt, and recover. Resilient communities are not communities that never experience disasters. They are communities that experience disasters and come back.

Dimension of ResilienceDescription
Physical resilienceBuildings and infrastructure that withstand hazard impacts without catastrophic failure
Economic resilienceBusinesses and livelihoods that can continue or restart relatively quickly after a disaster
Social resilienceStrong community networks that enable people to help each other during and after a disaster
Institutional resilienceGovernments and organizations that can continue to function, lead recovery, and coordinate across agencies
Ecological resilienceEcosystems that can recover and continue to provide services like flood buffering and water purification

Equity and Resilience

One of the most consistent findings in disaster science is that disasters are not equally distributed. Low-income communities, communities with large elderly populations, communities where people lack English fluency, and communities that lack political power often suffer greater losses and recover more slowly. This is primarily because vulnerability is greater — not only because physical hazards are higher in these communities, though they sometimes are, but because the social and economic resources needed to prepare, respond, and recover are fewer.

Building resilient communities therefore requires addressing the social and economic conditions that create vulnerability — not only building better levees or stronger buildings. Engineering alone is not enough.

Community Resilience in Practice

Resilient communities tend to share several characteristics: informed populations who understand local hazards and know what to do; strong social networks where neighbors know each other and help each other; functional institutions — local government, emergency services, schools, and hospitals that operate effectively; economic diversity that reduces the impact of sector-specific disasters; consistent investment in preparedness before disasters occur; and political will and resources directed toward genuine recovery, not just temporary relief.

The Role of Earth Science in Community Resilience

Earth scientists contribute to community resilience by mapping hazards and communicating risk; monitoring geological, hydrological, and meteorological systems; providing data for engineering and land use decisions; improving warning systems; studying past disasters to understand what works; and communicating uncertainty honestly — acknowledging what is known and what is not. Science cannot eliminate natural hazards. But it can inform the decisions that make communities safer.

Chapter 28 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 28.1Natural events are normal products of Earth's systems. They become natural hazards when they threaten people and things people value, and natural disasters when significant harm is realized. Earth's four systems — geosphere, hydrosphere, atmosphere, and biosphere — each generate characteristic hazards, and the greatest risks often arise where systems interact.
  • Sec 28.2Risk is the combination of hazard (the event itself), exposure (whether people and valued things are in harm's way), and vulnerability (how susceptible they are to damage and how difficult recovery will be). Risk can be reduced by addressing any of these three components. People frequently misperceive risk, overestimating sudden dramatic hazards and underestimating slow-onset ones.
  • Sec 28.3Earthquake and volcano hazards are concentrated along plate boundaries. Earthquake damage depends on ground shaking, site amplification, liquefaction, building quality, and secondary hazards. Volcanic hazards include lava flows, pyroclastic flows, ash fall, lahars, and gases. Both can be anticipated and managed through monitoring, mapping, and early warning.
  • Sec 28.4Flooding is the most widespread and costly natural hazard globally. Flood risk is increasing due to urban development, wetland loss, and climate change. Recurrence intervals describe probability, not scheduling. Storm surge is the deadliest hurricane hazard. Coastal communities face compounding risks from surge, tsunamis, and erosion.
  • Sec 28.5Severe weather hazards include tornadoes (EF0–EF5), hurricanes (Saffir-Simpson Cat 1–5), and winter storms. Wind category alone is a poor predictor of hurricane damage; storm surge and rainfall are often deadlier. Hazards compound: a single hurricane can bring surge, flooding, tornadoes, and landslides simultaneously.
  • Sec 28.6Drought, extreme heat, and wildfire are slow-onset hazards that are often underappreciated despite high death tolls and economic losses. The urban heat island effect amplifies heat risk in cities. Wildfire behavior is controlled by fuel, weather, and topography. Climate change is extending fire seasons and intensifying drought in many regions.
  • Sec 28.7Mass movement ranges from creep to catastrophic rockfalls and debris flows. Landslides are triggered by heavy rainfall, rapid snowmelt, earthquakes, volcanic activity, and human activity. Debris flows from recently burned slopes are a growing hazard as wildfires intensify. Steep terrain, weak rock, intense rainfall, and seismicity all elevate landslide risk.
  • Sec 28.8Hazard maps show the probability of events; risk maps combine hazard with exposure and vulnerability. Both have important limitations. Warning systems provide protective lead time and depend on detection, analysis, communication, and response. Lead time varies from seconds (earthquakes) to months (drought), shaping what preparedness strategies are possible.
  • Sec 28.9Effective hazard management requires action in four phases: mitigation (reducing risk before events), preparedness (planning and training), response (acting during and immediately after events), and recovery (restoring and improving communities afterward). Mitigation consistently saves far more money than it costs. Recovery is unequal and tends to amplify pre-existing inequalities.
  • Sec 28.10Resilient communities are those that can absorb disturbances and recover effectively. Resilience has physical, economic, social, institutional, and ecological dimensions. Building resilience requires addressing the social and economic conditions that create vulnerability, not only engineering better infrastructure. Earth science contributes by mapping hazards, monitoring systems, improving warnings, and communicating risk honestly.

Key Terms Review

natural hazard
A natural event with the potential to harm people, damage infrastructure, disrupt economies, or damage ecosystems.
natural disaster
A natural hazard event that causes significant loss of life, property, or ecosystem function at scale.
hazard
The natural process or event itself — characterized by type, magnitude, frequency, duration, spatial extent, and speed of onset.
exposure
The presence of people, buildings, infrastructure, or ecosystems in locations where a natural hazard can cause harm.
vulnerability
The degree to which people, infrastructure, and ecosystems are susceptible to harm from a hazard, shaped by physical, social, economic, and institutional factors.
risk
The combination of hazard magnitude and frequency, exposure, and vulnerability — the probability and potential consequences of harm from a natural event.
site amplification
The increase in ground shaking intensity that occurs when seismic waves travel through loose, water-saturated sediments compared to solid bedrock.
liquefaction
A phenomenon in which water-saturated, loose sediment temporarily behaves like a liquid when shaken during an earthquake, causing structures to sink or tip.
lahar
A volcanic mudflow composed of volcanic ash, rock fragments, and water, often generated when eruption melts ice or snow or mixes with heavy rainfall.
pyroclastic flow
A fast-moving current of hot gas, ash, and volcanic rock fragments that races down volcanic slopes during an eruption — among the deadliest volcanic hazards.
floodplain
The relatively flat area adjacent to a river that is naturally inundated during high-flow events, where fertile sediment is deposited.
recurrence interval
The average time between events of a given magnitude; a 100-year flood has a 1% probability of occurrence in any given year, not a guaranteed 100-year schedule.
storm surge
The abnormal rise in sea level caused by a tropical storm or hurricane's winds and low pressure — often the most deadly component of a landfalling hurricane.
debris flow
A fast-moving mass of water-saturated rock, soil, and organic material moving down a slope, often following stream channels at highway speeds.
landslide
The downslope movement of a mass of rock, soil, or debris under the influence of gravity, triggered by rainfall, earthquakes, or human activity.
urban heat island
The phenomenon in which cities are significantly warmer than surrounding rural areas due to the replacement of vegetated surfaces with heat-absorbing pavement and buildings.
wildland-urban interface (WUI)
The zone where human development meets or intermingles with undeveloped wildland vegetation, creating elevated wildfire risk to structures and people.
hazard map
A map showing areas where a natural hazard of a given type and intensity can be expected based on geological, meteorological, and historical evidence.
mitigation
Actions taken before a disaster to reduce its likelihood or severity, including structural improvements, land use regulations, and ecosystem restoration.
preparedness
Planning, training, and education undertaken before a hazardous event to improve the ability to protect people and respond effectively.
response
The immediate actions taken during and just after a hazardous event, including search and rescue, evacuation, emergency shelter, and medical care.
recovery
The long-term process of restoring community functions, infrastructure, economies, and ecosystems after a disaster.
resilience
The capacity of a person, community, infrastructure system, or ecosystem to absorb a disturbance, recover, and adapt while maintaining essential functions.
community resilience
The ability of a community to absorb, adapt to, and recover from a disaster while maintaining essential functions and social connections.

Worked Example Review

Worked Example 28.1
Calculating Flood Probability
  • ProblemA river has a 100-year flood recurrence interval. A homeowner has lived in the floodplain for 20 years and says: "We haven't flooded in 20 years, so we're safe for another 80." What is wrong with this reasoning?
  • ThinkThe recurrence interval describes annual probability, not a fixed schedule. Each year is independent — past years without a flood do not reduce the probability in future years.
  • Calculate Annual probability of a 100-year flood = 1/100 = 1% = 0.01 Probability of at least one flood in 30 years: P = 1 − (1 − 0.01)³⁰ = 1 − (0.99)³⁰ ≈ 1 − 0.74 ≈ 0.26 (about 26%) Probability of at least one flood in 20 years: P = 1 − (0.99)²⁰ ≈ 1 − 0.818 ≈ 0.18 (about 18%)
  • CheckThe homeowner's reasoning treats the flood as a scheduled event. But probability does not accumulate and "run out." In any given year, the probability is always 1%, regardless of recent history.
  • InterpretOver the 30 years a family might hold a mortgage on a floodplain property, there is approximately a 26% chance of experiencing at least one 100-year flood. That is not a negligible risk — it is comparable to many other risks people take seriously.
The homeowner's reasoning is incorrect. Each year independently carries a 1% flood probability; 20 "safe" years do not reduce future probability. The 30-year probability of at least one such flood is about 26%.
Worked Example 28.2
Analyzing Earthquake Risk
  • ProblemTwo cities sit on the same active fault with similar seismic hazard. City A has 200,000 people, with most buildings constructed before 1975 of unreinforced brick masonry. City B has 100,000 people, with most buildings constructed since 2000 to current seismic codes, built on solid granite bedrock. Which city faces higher earthquake risk?
  • ThinkApply the risk framework: compare hazard, exposure, and vulnerability separately before drawing a conclusion.
  • Analyze Hazard: Approximately equal — both cities sit on the same fault and face similar shaking probabilities Exposure: Higher in City A — twice the population is in the hazard zone Vulnerability: Significantly higher in City A — unreinforced masonry collapses rather than flexes; older construction predates modern seismic codes; no mention of bedrock (likely softer sediments amplifying shaking). City B has newer reinforced buildings on solid granite with less site amplification.
  • CheckRisk = Hazard × Exposure × Vulnerability. Equal hazard, higher exposure AND higher vulnerability in City A = considerably higher overall risk in City A despite having twice the population of City B.
City A faces considerably higher earthquake risk. Equal hazard × higher exposure × much higher vulnerability = greater expected losses. Priority risk reduction action: retrofit or replace at-risk unreinforced masonry buildings in City A.

Chapter 12 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

"The present is the key to the past — but it took plate tectonics to unlock the door."

Look at a world map long enough, and something starts to nag at you. The eastern bulge of South America fits, almost like a puzzle piece, against the western coast of Africa. The coastlines line up too well to be coincidence. People noticed this for centuries, but for most of that time, no one could explain it. The continents seemed fixed. Solid ground is solid ground.

It turns out the ground is not as fixed as it looks. Earth's outer shell is broken into massive slabs called tectonic plates, and those plates have been moving for hundreds of millions of years — grinding past each other, pulling apart, colliding, and diving beneath one another. This motion shapes nearly everything you see in the geology of the world: the location of mountain ranges, ocean trenches, earthquakes, volcanoes, island chains, and even the distribution of ancient fossils.

Plate tectonics is not just a chapter in Earth science. It is the central organizing theory of all of geology. Once you understand it, rocks, mountains, volcanoes, earthquakes, ocean basins, and the shapes of continents begin to make sense as part of one connected story.

Opening Question

If the continents were once joined together in a single supercontinent, what evidence would you expect to find on opposite sides of an ocean today? By the end of this chapter, you will know exactly what scientists found — and how those discoveries changed geology forever.

Section 12.1

The Big Idea of Plate Tectonics

Plate tectonics is the theory that Earth's lithosphere — the rigid outer shell made up of the crust and uppermost mantle — is divided into a set of large and small slabs called tectonic plates. These plates move slowly across Earth's surface, driven by heat flowing from Earth's interior.

The motion is slow by human standards. Most plates move between 2 and 10 centimeters per year — roughly the rate your fingernails grow. Over millions of years, however, even slow movement adds up to thousands of kilometers. Continents drift. Oceans open and close. Mountain ranges rise and erode away.

What Plate Tectonics Explains
  • Why earthquakes and volcanoes cluster in specific belts rather than occurring randomly
  • How mountains form and why they appear where they do
  • Why the ocean floor is young compared to the continents
  • Why identical fossils appear on continents now separated by thousands of kilometers of ocean
  • Why some coastlines seem to fit together like puzzle pieces
Systems Connection

Plate tectonics connects to every part of Earth's system. Moving plates affect the atmosphere (through volcanic gases), the hydrosphere (by opening and closing ocean basins), the biosphere (by separating and joining continents), and the geosphere (through earthquakes, volcanoes, and mountain building). As you read this chapter, watch for connections to systems you have already studied.

Section 12.2

Continental Drift

The story of plate tectonics begins with Alfred Wegener, a German meteorologist and polar explorer. In 1912, Wegener proposed the hypothesis of continental drift: the idea that the continents were once joined together in a single supercontinent — which he called Pangaea (from Greek, meaning "all land") — and had since drifted apart. He proposed that Pangaea began breaking apart roughly 200 million years ago.

Wegener's Evidence

Type of EvidenceObservationWhat It Suggested
Fit of Coastlines The eastern coastline of South America matches the western coast of Africa, especially along the continental shelves The two continents were once joined and have since separated
Fossil Distributions Mesosaurus (freshwater reptile) found only in South America and Africa; Glossopteris (seed fern) found on five continents including Antarctica These organisms could not have crossed open ocean; the continents must once have been connected
Rock Correlations Same age and type of rock formations on opposite sides of the Atlantic; the Appalachians match sequences in Scotland and Scandinavia These rock sequences formed as part of the same mountain belt before the ocean opened
Climate Evidence Glacial deposits found in tropical Africa, India, South America, and Australia; ancient coal swamps near the Arctic These regions were once in different climate zones — only possible if they were in different locations
Why Wegener Was Rejected

Despite strong evidence, most geologists of Wegener's time rejected continental drift because he could not explain how continents moved. He proposed that continents plowed through the ocean floor like ships through water, but no known force could move entire continents through solid rock. Without a mechanism, the hypothesis remained unacceptable to the scientific community. Wegener died in 1930 on a polar expedition — before his central idea was vindicated.

Section 12.3

Evidence from the Ocean Floor

The real breakthrough came from mapping the ocean floor. For most of human history, the ocean floor was unknown. In the mid-twentieth century, ships began systematically mapping the seafloor using sonar — and what they found changed geology forever.

Four Lines of Ocean-Floor Evidence
How the seafloor revealed plate tectonics
  • Mid-Ocean Ridges Sonar mapping revealed a continuous underwater mountain range running through every major ocean basin — the longest mountain range on Earth, over 65,000 km long. A rift valley runs down its center, where eruptions are clearly adding new material.
  • Sediment Patterns Ocean sediment is thickest far from the ridge and nearly absent at the ridge itself. This means the seafloor near the ridge is youngest, and sediment accumulates as the floor ages and moves away.
  • Heat Flow The ocean floor near mid-ocean ridges is significantly hotter than the rest of the seafloor, consistent with hot mantle material rising from below at the ridge.
  • Magnetic Stripes Magnetic surveys revealed alternating bands of normal and reversed magnetic polarity running parallel to the ridge — symmetric on both sides like a mirror image. These stripes match the known history of Earth's magnetic field reversals, proving the ocean floor was being created at the ridge and spreading outward in both directions.
Why Magnetic Stripes Are So Convincing

When lava erupts at the ridge and cools, magnetic minerals in the rock lock in the direction of Earth's magnetic field at that moment. Earth's magnetic field periodically reverses (north becomes south and vice versa). As new seafloor spreads away from the ridge, it records the current field direction. The result: a symmetric record of reversals on either side of the ridge, like a tape recorder of geological time. This is direct, measurable, falsifiable evidence that the seafloor is spreading.

Section 12.4

Seafloor Spreading

In the early 1960s, geologist Harry Hess proposed the mechanism that Wegener lacked. He called it seafloor spreading. The idea: new ocean floor is continually created at mid-ocean ridges, where hot mantle material rises, melts, and erupts as lava. The new rock is pushed outward as more material rises behind it, like two slow conveyor belts moving in opposite directions away from the ridge.

Seafloor spreading explained all four lines of ocean-floor evidence simultaneously. It also solved a long-standing puzzle: why the ocean floor is so much younger than the continents. Continental rocks are billions of years old. Ocean floor rocks are rarely older than about 200 million years. If ocean floor is continuously created at ridges and subducted — pulled down into the mantle — at the edges of plates, old ocean floor would not survive. The ocean floor is young because it is constantly recycled.

From Drift to Plate Tectonics

By the late 1960s, continental drift, seafloor spreading, and subduction were unified into the comprehensive theory of plate tectonics. The scientific community, once skeptical, accepted it rapidly. The evidence had become overwhelming. This is how science works: hypotheses are tested against multiple independent lines of evidence, and when the evidence converges, even revolutionary ideas gain acceptance.

Earth's lithosphere is broken into about a dozen major plates and several smaller ones. Plates vary greatly in size and carry different types of crust.

Why Ocean Floor Subducts

Oceanic crust is denser (about 3.0 g/cm³) and thinner (5–10 km) than continental crust (about 2.7 g/cm³, 25–70 km thick). When an oceanic plate meets a continental plate, the denser oceanic plate sinks — subducts — beneath the less dense continental plate. Denser material sinks under less dense material, just as in any other density system you have studied. This difference in density is the key to understanding which plate goes where at convergent boundaries.

Section 12.6

Divergent Plate Boundaries

A divergent plate boundary is where two plates move apart. As plates separate, hot mantle material rises to fill the gap, melts, and erupts — creating new ocean floor at mid-ocean ridges or, on continents, rift valleys.

Two Settings for Divergent Boundaries
Where plates pull apart
  • Mid-Ocean Ridges Most divergent boundaries occur on the ocean floor. As magma wells up and solidifies, it creates new oceanic crust. The Atlantic Ocean widens by about 2.5 cm per year. Features include a central rift valley with frequent eruptions, hydrothermal vents where mineral-rich superheated water escapes, and unique ecosystems that depend on chemical energy rather than sunlight.
  • Continental Rifts When divergence occurs within a continent, it creates a continental rift. The East African Rift Valley is the world's most active example — the African continent is slowly pulling apart along a system of rift valleys from Ethiopia south through Kenya, Tanzania, and Mozambique. In tens of millions of years, a new ocean may form where the rift now exists. Iceland sits directly on the Mid-Atlantic Ridge — one of the few places where a divergent boundary is visible above sea level, actively pulling apart and producing frequent eruptions.
Section 12.7

Convergent Plate Boundaries

A convergent plate boundary is where two plates move toward each other. The result depends on which types of crust are colliding. There are three distinct cases.

Case 1: Oceanic–Continental Convergence

When an oceanic plate meets a continental plate, the denser oceanic plate subducts. As it descends into the mantle, it carries seawater and hydrated minerals. The water lowers the melting point of the overlying mantle rock, generating magma that rises through the continental plate and erupts as volcanoes — forming a volcanic arc inland from the coast. A deep ocean trench forms where the plate bends downward.

FeatureLocationReal-World Example
Ocean trenchAt the subduction zonePeru-Chile Trench
Coastal mountain rangeOn the overriding plateAndes Mountains
Volcanic arcInland from the coastCascade Range volcanoes
Earthquake zoneAlong the subducting plateCascadia Subduction Zone

Case 2: Oceanic–Oceanic Convergence

When two oceanic plates converge, the older, colder, denser plate subducts. Volcanoes erupt on the ocean floor and eventually build chains of volcanic islands — called island arcs. The Aleutian Islands of Alaska and the Mariana Islands of the Pacific are examples. The Mariana Trench — the deepest point on Earth's surface at over 11 km below sea level — formed by this type of subduction.

Case 3: Continental–Continental Convergence

When two continental plates collide, neither is dense enough to subduct easily. The crust crumples, thickens, and is pushed upward — building the world's highest mountain ranges. The Himalayas formed when the Indo-Australian Plate collided with the Eurasian Plate roughly 50 million years ago, after India drifted northward across what was then a wide ocean. Mount Everest is still rising by a few millimeters per year. The Alps formed when Africa collided with Europe. The ancient Appalachians record a similar collision hundreds of millions of years ago, now heavily eroded.

Reading the Rocks at Collision Zones

When continental plates collide and close an ancient ocean, the sedimentary rocks that accumulated on the ocean floor get squeezed and folded between the two continents. Marine fossils — shells of sea creatures — are sometimes found high in the Himalayas, recording the ocean that once existed where mountains now stand. The rocks remember the history that the landforms now obscure.

Section 12.8

Transform Plate Boundaries

A transform plate boundary is where two plates slide horizontally past each other. Unlike divergent and convergent boundaries, transform boundaries neither create nor destroy lithosphere. Plates simply grind past one another along faults — fractures in the crust where motion occurs.

The movement is not smooth. Plate edges are rough and irregular. Stress builds as the plates lock together, then releases suddenly as one side lurches past the other — producing earthquakes. The most famous transform boundary in the world is the San Andreas Fault in California, where the Pacific Plate grinds northwestward past the North American Plate at roughly 5 cm per year. Los Angeles is gradually moving toward San Francisco.

Boundary TypePlate MotionEarthquakes?Volcanoes?Landforms
DivergentMoving apartYes (moderate)YesMid-ocean ridges, rift valleys
ConvergentMoving togetherYes (strong)Yes (most types)Mountains, trenches, island arcs

Why No Volcanoes at Transform Boundaries?

Volcanoes require a source of magma — either from mantle material rising at a rift, or from a subducting plate carrying water into the mantle. At transform boundaries, plates slide sideways. There is no rifting and no subduction. No magma is generated, so no volcanoes form. The San Andreas Fault system produces major earthquakes but no volcanic eruptions.

Section 12.9

Mantle Convection and Plate Motion

What drives tectonic plates across Earth's surface? The answer is heat flow from Earth's interior, expressed through a process called mantle convection.

Convection is the movement of material caused by differences in temperature and density. Hot material is less dense and rises; cool material is denser and sinks. You can observe convection in a pot of soup: hot soup rises in the center, spreads toward the sides, cools, and sinks. The same process operates in Earth's mantle — on a vast scale and over millions of years. The mantle is solid on short timescales but flows very slowly over long periods, like an extremely thick fluid.

Three Drivers of Plate Motion
Why plates move
  • Mantle Convection Heat from Earth's core and from radioactive decay within the mantle drives convection currents. Hot mantle rises beneath mid-ocean ridges, spreads sideways, cools, and sinks at subduction zones. Plates ride on top of this circulating material.
  • Slab Pull When an old, cold, dense oceanic plate begins to subduct, its weight pulls the rest of the plate behind it. This slab pull may be the strongest driver of plate motion. Plates attached to large subducting slabs (like the Pacific Plate) tend to move faster than plates that are not subducting.
  • Ridge Push At mid-ocean ridges, newly formed oceanic crust is hot and elevated. As it moves away from the ridge, it cools and becomes denser, sliding downhill. This creates a force called ridge push that pushes the plate away from the ridge. Ridge push is generally considered weaker than slab pull.

Hot Spots

Not all volcanic activity occurs at plate boundaries. Some volcanoes sit in the middle of plates, far from any boundary — associated with hot spots, plumes of unusually hot mantle material that rise from deep within the mantle and burn through the overlying plate.

The Hawaiian Islands are the best-known example. The Pacific Plate moves slowly northwest over a stationary hot spot. Each island was built by volcanic eruptions as it passed over the plume, then went dormant as the plate moved it away. The youngest and most active island — Hawai'i — is currently over the hot spot. The older islands to the northwest (Maui, O'ahu, Kaua'i) are progressively older and more eroded. A new seamount called Lō'ihi is building on the ocean floor southeast of Hawai'i, not yet reaching the surface.

The chain of Hawaiian Islands and seamounts allows geologists to trace both the direction and rate of Pacific Plate motion over millions of years — a fossil record of the plate's journey.

Section 12.10

Plate Tectonics as a Unifying Theory

Plate tectonics is sometimes called the unifying theory of geology — the way that evolution is the unifying theory of biology. Once you understand it, most major features and patterns of Earth's surface begin to make sense as expressions of the same underlying process.

Feature or PatternTectonic Explanation
Mountain rangesContinental collision (Himalayas, Alps) and subduction (Andes, Cascades) both build mountains; ancient ranges like the Appalachians record ancient collisions
EarthquakesCluster along plate boundaries; the "Ring of Fire" traces the edges of the Pacific Plate and surrounding boundaries
VolcanoesNearly all occur at subduction zones or divergent boundaries; their global distribution maps plate boundaries directly
Ocean basinsOcean basins open as continents diverge and close as oceanic plates subduct; the Atlantic is young and growing, the Pacific old and shrinking
Fossil distributionsMatching fossils on different continents record when those continents were joined
Mineral depositsCopper, gold, molybdenum, and chromium deposits form through tectonic processes at subduction zones, rifts, and spreading centers

What Plate Tectonics Does Not Yet Fully Explain

Science is ongoing. While plate tectonics explains broad patterns, several important questions remain active areas of research: the exact nature of deep mantle plumes and hot spots; how and when plate tectonics first began on early Earth; whether other planets have plate tectonics, or only Earth; and the precise relative contributions of ridge push, slab pull, and mantle convection. A theory explaining most of the evidence is not a finished theory — it is a framework for asking better questions.

Earth's Tectonic Future

The plates will continue to move. Over the next 50 million years, East Africa is predicted to separate from the rest of the continent. The Atlantic will continue to widen. The Pacific will continue to shrink. Some models predict that in roughly 250 million years, the continents may reassemble into a new supercontinent — sometimes called Pangaea Ultima or Amasia, depending on the model. Earth's geological story is not finished. It is a planet still in motion.

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 12.1Plate tectonics is the theory that Earth's lithosphere is divided into moving plates. It explains the distribution of earthquakes, volcanoes, mountains, ocean basins, and many other geological features as expressions of plate motion driven by Earth's internal heat.
  • Sec 12.2Alfred Wegener proposed continental drift in 1912, supported by matching coastlines, fossil distributions, rock correlations, and climate evidence. His hypothesis was rejected because he could not provide a mechanism — no known force could move continents through solid ocean floor.
  • Sec 12.3Seafloor mapping revealed mid-ocean ridges, sediment thickness patterns, heat flow anomalies, and symmetric magnetic stripes — all of which pointed to ocean floor being created at ridges and spreading outward. The magnetic stripe evidence was especially powerful because it was symmetric, measurable, and matched the known history of magnetic field reversals.
  • Sec 12.4Harry Hess proposed seafloor spreading: new ocean floor is continuously created at mid-ocean ridges and destroyed at subduction zones. This explained why ocean floor is much younger than continental crust and provided the missing mechanism for continental drift.
  • Sec 12.5Earth's lithosphere is broken into about a dozen major plates. Oceanic crust is denser and thinner than continental crust, which is why oceanic plates subduct beneath continental plates at convergent boundaries.
  • Sec 12.6Divergent boundaries form where plates pull apart. At mid-ocean ridges, new ocean floor is created continuously. Continental rifts can eventually split continents and form new oceans. Iceland and the East African Rift Valley are visible examples.
  • Sec 12.7Convergent boundaries produce different features depending on the crust types involved. Oceanic-continental convergence creates trenches, volcanic arcs, and mountains. Oceanic-oceanic convergence creates deep trenches and island arcs. Continental-continental convergence builds the world's highest mountain ranges.
  • Sec 12.8Transform boundaries form where plates slide horizontally past each other. They produce earthquakes but no volcanic activity, because there is no subduction and no rifting — no source of magma. The San Andreas Fault is the most studied example.
  • Sec 12.9Mantle convection, slab pull, and ridge push all drive plate motion. Hot spots — stationary mantle plumes — produce volcanic chains like the Hawaiian Islands as plates move over them, recording both the direction and rate of plate motion over millions of years.
  • Sec 12.10Plate tectonics is the unifying theory of geology, explaining mountains, earthquakes, volcanoes, ocean basins, fossil distributions, and mineral deposits. Open questions remain, and Earth's plates will continue to rearrange the continents far into the future.

Key Terms Review

asthenosphere
The partially molten, weak layer of the upper mantle beneath the lithosphere, on which tectonic plates move.
continental drift
Wegener's hypothesis that continents were once joined and have moved apart over time; later incorporated into plate tectonics.
convergent boundary
A plate boundary where two plates move toward each other; results in subduction, mountain building, or both.
divergent boundary
A plate boundary where two plates move apart; results in new ocean floor formation or continental rifting.
hot spot
A location of unusually hot mantle material that produces volcanic activity in the interior of a tectonic plate, not at a boundary.
island arc
A chain of volcanic islands formed above a subduction zone where two oceanic plates converge.
lithosphere
Earth's rigid outer shell, consisting of the crust and the uppermost mantle; broken into tectonic plates.
mantle convection
Slow, heat-driven circulation of rock within Earth's mantle; one of the primary drivers of tectonic plate motion.
mid-ocean ridge
An underwater mountain range at a divergent plate boundary where new ocean floor is continuously produced.
Pangaea
The supercontinent that existed approximately 200–300 million years ago, from which present-day continents separated.
plate tectonics
The theory that Earth's lithosphere is divided into moving plates whose interactions shape Earth's surface features and drive geological processes.
ridge push
The force generated when newly formed, elevated oceanic crust cools and slides downward away from a mid-ocean ridge.
rift valley
An elongated valley formed by the pulling apart of Earth's crust at a divergent boundary.
Ring of Fire
The zone of intense earthquake and volcanic activity surrounding the Pacific Ocean, tracing the edges of the Pacific Plate.
seafloor spreading
The process by which new ocean floor is created at mid-ocean ridges as tectonic plates diverge; proposed by Harry Hess.
slab pull
The force generated when a cold, dense oceanic plate sinks into the mantle at a subduction zone, pulling the rest of the plate behind it.
subduction
The process by which a denser oceanic plate descends beneath another plate at a convergent boundary and sinks into the mantle.
subduction zone
The region along a convergent boundary where one plate descends beneath another into the mantle.
tectonic plate
One of the large, rigid slabs that make up Earth's lithosphere; moves slowly driven by heat from Earth's interior.
transform boundary
A plate boundary where two plates slide horizontally past each other; produces earthquakes but not significant volcanic activity.
volcanic arc
A chain of volcanoes formed on the overriding plate above a subduction zone where water from the descending plate lowers rock melting points.

Common Misconceptions

Misconception 1
"Continents float on liquid rock."

The mantle is not liquid. It is solid rock that flows extremely slowly over millions of years — too slow to observe on human timescales, but fast enough to move plates over geologic time. Plates do not float on a liquid; they slide on the weak, partially molten asthenosphere.

Misconception 2
"Plate tectonics only happened in the past."

Plate motion is happening right now. GPS measurements confirm that plates move 2 to 10 centimeters per year. The Himalayas are still rising. The Atlantic is still widening. East Africa is still slowly splitting apart. The past and present are the same process.

Misconception 3
"Earthquakes happen everywhere."

Most earthquakes concentrate at plate boundaries, particularly subduction zones and transform boundaries. Intraplate earthquakes do occur but are much less common. A world earthquake map is essentially a map of plate boundaries.

Misconception 4
"Continental drift and plate tectonics are the same theory."

Continental drift was Wegener's original hypothesis — it correctly identified that continents move but could not explain how. Plate tectonics is the fuller, mechanistic theory developed after Wegener's time, providing seafloor spreading, subduction, and mantle convection as the explanations for both the motion and the geological features it creates.

Misconception 5
"Subduction only happens because plates are pushed apart at ridges."

Ridge push plays a role, but slab pull — the weight of a cold, dense sinking plate pulling the rest of the plate with it — is likely the more important driver. Plates attached to large subducting slabs tend to move faster, suggesting the sinking slab leads, not the pushing ridge.

  1. Building Codes and Earthquake Risk. Cities near active plate boundaries — Seattle, San Francisco, Tokyo, Istanbul — must design buildings to withstand earthquakes. How does knowing which plate boundary lies nearby, and its history of earthquakes, inform building codes, emergency planning, and land use decisions?
  2. Volcanic Soils and Agriculture. Volcanic soils formed by weathered lava and ash are often extremely fertile. The island of Java in Indonesia, which sits above a subduction zone, supports some of the densest agricultural populations on Earth. Explain the connection between plate tectonics, volcanic activity, and soil fertility.
  3. Mineral Resources and Subduction. Porphyry copper deposits — the world's major source of copper — form in the volcanic arcs above subduction zones. The copper deposits of Chile and the American Southwest are examples. Why are subduction zones associated with the formation of ore deposits?
  4. Geothermal Energy. Iceland generates a large portion of its electricity and heat from geothermal energy — heat from Earth's interior accessed through volcanic and hydrothermal activity. Iceland sits directly on the Mid-Atlantic Ridge. Explain why Iceland's location makes it especially well-suited for this energy source.
  5. Plate Tectonics and Biodiversity. Slow plate motion has repeatedly joined and separated continents, creating barriers and corridors for species movement. The joining of North and South America about 3 million years ago allowed a major exchange of animals between the two continents. How might future tectonic changes — East Africa separating, the Atlantic widening — affect the distribution of species millions of years from now?

Mixed Review — Cross-Chapter Connections

These questions require you to connect plate tectonics to concepts from earlier chapters.

  1. (Chapter 11) Seismic waves travel at different speeds through different materials and are refracted at boundaries between layers. How do geologists use seismic wave behavior to detect the existence of Earth's core, mantle, and lithosphere? How does this evidence support the plate tectonics model?
  2. (Chapter 10) Using the principles of relative dating, explain how geologists can determine that rocks in a subduction zone have experienced multiple tectonic events. What evidence in the rocks — folding, faulting, metamorphism, unconformities — would you expect to find?
  3. (Chapter 9) Plate tectonics produces mountain ranges. How do mechanical and chemical weathering then act on those mountains over millions of years? What happens to the eroded material, and where does it ultimately go?
  4. (Chapter 7) When two continents collide and build a mountain range, predict the sequence of sedimentary rock types that might accumulate in an adjacent basin as the mountains erode. What clues in those sedimentary rocks would record the tectonic event that produced the mountains?
  5. (Chapter 4) Subduction carries water and carbon from the ocean floor down into the mantle, while volcanic arcs return these materials to the atmosphere. How does the subduction-volcanism cycle connect to the long-term carbon cycle and to Earth's long-term climate regulation?

Looking Ahead
Chapter 13: Earthquakes

Chapter 12 established the framework of plate tectonics: why plates move, where they interact, and what happens at boundaries. Chapter 13 takes a close look at one of the most dramatic consequences of plate interaction — earthquakes. You will learn what earthquakes are, how stress builds and releases along faults, what seismic waves are and how they carry energy through Earth, how seismographs detect and record earthquakes, how the location and magnitude of earthquakes are measured, what hazards earthquakes produce (including tsunamis), and how communities prepare for and reduce earthquake risk. The concepts from Chapter 12 — plate boundaries, faults, subduction zones, transform faults — provide the framework. Chapter 13 brings earthquakes into sharp focus: where they occur, how they are studied, and what they mean for the people who live near plate boundaries.

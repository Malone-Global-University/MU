Chapter 17 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

In Chapter 16, you learned that water moves through Earth systems in a continuous cycle — evaporating from oceans and land surfaces, condensing into clouds, falling as precipitation, and flowing or soaking into the ground. Chapter 17 looks closely at what happens after that water arrives on land.

Some water runs across the surface, gathering into streams and rivers. Some soaks into the soil and rock beneath your feet, becoming part of enormous underground water stores. Some collects in lakes and wetlands. All of it — on the surface and underground — is connected. It flows through the landscape according to gravity, geology, and land cover, and it eventually finds its way back to the ocean or atmosphere.

This chapter explains how freshwater systems are organized, how scientists study them, what makes them healthy or degraded, and why the decisions people make about land and water use ripple across entire regions. Understanding freshwater systems is one of the most practically important areas of Earth and environmental science, because clean, available freshwater is not unlimited — and the systems that provide it can be damaged, depleted, or restored depending on how they are managed.

Opening Question

A city depends on a river for its drinking water. A farmer 80 kilometers upstream applies fertilizer to fields that border that river. A developer proposes paving a large wetland at the edge of town. Are these three situations connected? By the end of this chapter, you will understand why the answer is yes — and what that connection means for the people who depend on the river.

Section 17.1

Freshwater Systems as Networks

Freshwater systems are not a collection of separate features. They are networks — connected systems in which water flows from higher elevations to lower ones, moves through the ground from areas of high pressure to low pressure, and eventually reaches the ocean, a large lake, or the atmosphere.

Think of a region after a rainstorm. Rainwater falls on hillsides, forests, farms, and parking lots. Some evaporates immediately. Some soaks into the soil. The rest runs downhill as surface water, joining small channels that join larger ones, eventually forming streams and rivers. Underground, water that soaked in continues moving slowly through rock and sediment, feeding springs, wells, and streamflow from below.

Every part of this network is connected. A decision to pave a parking lot affects how much water infiltrates versus how much runs off. A drought affects both surface streams and the underground water stores that feed them. A factory that discharges chemicals upstream affects water quality downstream. Freshwater systems are not isolated — they are integrated networks that link land, groundwater, surface water, ecosystems, and human communities.

Surface Water and Groundwater Together

During wet seasons, rivers can lose water to surrounding sediments, recharging groundwater. During dry seasons, groundwater seeps into riverbeds from below, sustaining streamflow even when rain has not fallen for weeks. Wetlands often sit exactly at the boundary between surface and groundwater, receiving water from both. This exchange works in both directions, which is why groundwater depletion can reduce streamflow and surface water pollution can eventually contaminate wells.

Section 17.2

Aquifers and Groundwater

Groundwater is water stored in the spaces within rock and sediment below Earth's surface. These spaces include pores between grains of sand and gravel, cracks and fractures in rock, and spaces dissolved into soluble rock like limestone. Groundwater makes up the vast majority of Earth's unfrozen liquid freshwater — far more than all rivers and lakes combined.

Below the surface, there are typically two zones. The zone of aeration (also called the unsaturated zone or vadose zone) is the region where pore spaces contain a mixture of air and water. Below it is the zone of saturation, where all pore spaces are filled with water. The upper boundary of the zone of saturation is the water table.

Two Key Properties of Aquifer Materials

Porosity — the percentage of a material's volume that consists of open spaces. High porosity means a material can store more water. Clay has many small pores but they do not connect well, limiting water movement.

Permeability — how easily fluids can move through a material. Gravel and coarse sand have high permeability. Clay and unfractured granite have low permeability. A good aquifer needs both sufficient porosity to store water and sufficient permeability to yield it to wells.

Artesian Wells

When a well is drilled into a confined aquifer, water may rise above the top of the aquifer due to pressure — and in some cases push to the surface without pumping. This is an artesian well. The pressure reflects the elevation of the recharge zone, which may be in a mountain range many miles away.

The Ogallala Aquifer

The Ogallala (High Plains) Aquifer underlies approximately 174,000 square miles beneath eight U.S. states, from South Dakota to Texas, and supports massive agricultural irrigation across the Great Plains. However, water is being withdrawn far faster than it is being recharged — in some areas the water table has dropped many tens of feet over recent decades. This long-term depletion is a serious resource concern for agriculture and communities across the region.

Section 17.3

Water Tables, Recharge, and Wells

The water table is the upper surface of the saturated zone — the boundary between saturated rock or sediment below and unsaturated material above. The water table is not flat. It tends to follow the shape of the land surface, rising under hills and descending toward valleys and streams. It rises when more water enters the groundwater system through recharge and falls when water is removed by evapotranspiration, springs, streamflow, or pumping.

Recharge is the process by which water enters the groundwater system, replenishing aquifers. Recharge occurs primarily through infiltration — water moving downward through the unsaturated zone until it reaches the saturated zone. Human activity can both enhance and reduce recharge. Urbanization — replacing soil and vegetation with impervious surfaces like roads, parking lots, and rooftops — dramatically reduces infiltration and therefore recharge. Preserving or restoring natural land cover maintains infiltration and supports aquifer recharge.

Cones of Depression and Groundwater Overdraft

When a well is pumped, water is drawn from the surrounding aquifer faster than it can immediately be replaced. This causes the water table around the well to drop, forming a cone-shaped depression called a cone of depression. If many wells in an area all pump heavily, their cones of depression can overlap, dropping the regional water table significantly. This is called groundwater overdraft.

Overdraft can cause wells to run dry, increase pumping costs, reduce streamflow, and — in some coastal or low-elevation areas — cause the ground to sink through a process called land subsidence. Parts of California's San Joaquin Valley have sunk by several meters over the past century due to groundwater removal.

Section 17.4

Springs and Groundwater Flow

A spring is a place where groundwater flows naturally to the surface. Springs form wherever the water table intersects the land surface, or where groundwater under pressure finds an opening to emerge. They are found along hillsides where a permeable layer sits above a less permeable one, at the bases of cliffs, along canyon walls, at the edges of valleys, and in some cases at the bottom of lakes or ocean floors.

Groundwater moves — slowly — from areas of higher elevation and higher pressure to areas of lower elevation and pressure. The driving force is gravity. The velocity of groundwater flow is typically very slow — from a fraction of a meter per day to several meters per year, depending on permeability. In coarse gravel, groundwater can move tens of meters per day. In clay, it may barely move at all.

Why Slow Flow Matters for Contamination

Because groundwater moves so slowly, contaminants that enter the groundwater system can persist for decades — even after the source is removed. A chemical spilled onto the ground today may not reach a well a kilometer away for years or decades, and cleanup (remediation) is extremely difficult once groundwater is contaminated. This is why preventing contamination is far more effective than attempting to clean it up afterward.

Section 17.5

Rivers and Streamflow

Rivers are channels that carry water — and the sediment, nutrients, and materials mixed with it — across the land surface from higher to lower elevations. They are powered by gravity. Every river begins somewhere (its headwaters), receives water from the landscape around it, and eventually empties into another water body (its mouth).

Rivers are not just moving water. They are systems that interact continuously with the land they flow through. Rivers erode their banks and beds, transport sediment, deposit sediment in new locations, reshape valleys, and change course over time. The character of a river — its speed, depth, width, sediment load, channel pattern, and seasonal variation — reflects the geology, vegetation, climate, and land use of its watershed.

Discharge and Streamflow

Discharge Formula

Discharge = Cross-sectional area × Velocity

Discharge is the volume of water flowing past a point per unit time, expressed in cubic meters per second (m³/s). It equals the cross-sectional area of the channel (in m²) multiplied by the average water velocity (in m/s). Discharge varies enormously — from liters per second in small streams to hundreds of thousands of cubic meters per second in major rivers like the Amazon.

Worked Example 17.1
Calculating Stream Discharge
  • ProblemA stream gauge shows that a river channel has a cross-sectional area of 45 m² and an average water velocity of 2.3 m/s. What is the discharge at this station?
  • RepresentDischarge = Cross-sectional area × Velocity
  • CalculateDischarge = 45 m² × 2.3 m/s = 103.5 m³/s
  • CheckUnits: m² × m/s = m³/s. ✓ This is a plausible discharge for a medium-sized river.
  • InterpretThis river is passing more than 100 cubic meters of water per second through this point — roughly equivalent to filling a 50-meter competition swimming pool every 25 seconds.
Answer: Discharge = 103.5 m³/s

River Channel Patterns

Meandering rivers develop wide, sweeping S-shaped curves. On the outside of each curve, water flows faster and erodes the bank, forming a cut bank. On the inside of each curve, water flows slower, depositing sediment in a curved bar called a point bar. Over time, meanders migrate across the floodplain. When the neck of a meander is cut off, an isolated curved lake called an oxbow lake is left behind.

Braided rivers develop where a river carries a large load of coarse sediment but lacks enough energy to transport it all efficiently. The channel breaks into multiple interconnected channels separated by sediment bars. Braided rivers are common below glaciers, which produce enormous amounts of coarse sediment.

The longitudinal profile of a river — a plot of elevation against distance from headwaters to mouth — is typically a smooth curve that is steep near the headwaters and flatter near the mouth. Waterfalls and rapids are irregularities in this profile, indicating places where the river is actively eroding to smooth itself out over geologic time.

Section 17.6

Lakes and Wetlands

Lakes

A lake is a body of standing freshwater occupying a depression in the landscape. Lakes form through many processes: glacial erosion (many of the Great Lakes), volcanic calderas, tectonic basin formation, river meander cutoffs (oxbow lakes), landslide dams, and human construction (reservoirs).

Lakes have inputs (rivers, direct precipitation, groundwater) and outputs (rivers, evaporation, groundwater). The residence time of water in a lake — the average time a water molecule spends in the lake before leaving — ranges from days in small, high-throughput lakes to thousands of years in very deep, slowly draining ones.

Worked Example 17.2
Estimating Lake Residence Time
  • ProblemA lake has a volume of 120 million cubic meters and an average outflow of 4 m³/s. Estimate the average residence time of water in the lake.
  • ConvertOutflow = 4 m³/s × 86,400 s/day = 345,600 m³/day
  • CalculateResidence time = Volume ÷ Outflow rate = 120,000,000 m³ ÷ 345,600 m³/day ≈ 347 days ≈ about 1 year
  • InterpretWater entering this lake spends about one year before exiting. If a pollutant entered today, it would take many years to flush out even after the source stopped — mixing is gradual, not instantaneous.
Answer: Residence time ≈ 347 days (approximately 1 year)

Thermal Stratification

In temperate climates, lakes stratify thermally during summer. The sun warms surface water, making it less dense than deeper water. This creates three layers: a warm, well-mixed upper layer called the epilimnion; a zone of rapid temperature change called the thermocline; and a cold, dense lower layer called the hypolimnion. These layers resist mixing, which can reduce dissolved oxygen in deep water and concentrate nutrients near the bottom. In autumn, as surface water cools and becomes denser, the temperature difference breaks down and the lake mixes — a process called lake turnover.

Eutrophication

Eutrophication is the enrichment of a lake with nutrients — particularly nitrogen and phosphorus — that stimulates rapid growth of algae and aquatic plants. Natural eutrophication occurs slowly over thousands of years. Human-accelerated eutrophication (called cultural eutrophication) occurs when excess nutrients from fertilizers, sewage, and animal waste run into lakes. Dense algal blooms can block sunlight, and when the algae die and decompose, bacteria consume oxygen, creating hypoxic (low oxygen) or even anoxic (no oxygen) conditions that kill fish and other aquatic life.

Wetlands

Wetlands are areas where the land is saturated with water or covered by shallow water long enough to support vegetation and soils adapted to wet conditions. Wetlands include marshes, swamps, bogs, fens, vernal pools, and riparian zones along rivers.

Wetland Loss

Despite their ecological and hydrological value, wetlands have historically been drained, filled, and converted for agriculture, development, and mosquito control. The United States has lost more than half its original wetland area since European settlement. When wetlands are removed, the flood control, filtration, and habitat functions they provided must often be replaced by expensive engineered systems — if they can be replaced at all.

Section 17.7

Watersheds

A watershed (also called a drainage basin or catchment) is the entire area of land from which precipitation drains to a common outlet — a stream, river, lake, or ocean. Every point on land belongs to exactly one watershed, defined by the topographic divides (ridgelines and high points) that separate one drainage basin from another.

Watersheds come in all sizes. A small stream in a forested hillside has a watershed of a few hectares. The Mississippi River drains a watershed of over 3.2 million square kilometers — roughly 40% of the contiguous United States. Watersheds nest inside each other: small sub-watersheds drain into larger ones, which drain into still larger ones, ultimately reaching the ocean.

Why the Watershed Concept Is Central to Water Management

Everything that happens within a watershed eventually affects the water body at its outlet. Pollution deposited anywhere in the watershed can be carried to the outlet by runoff and streams. Changes in land cover anywhere affect streamflow patterns. Soil erosion anywhere contributes sediment to streams. Thinking in watersheds connects decisions made on land to outcomes in water — even when the land and the water appear far apart.

Section 17.8

Floods and Floodplains

A flood occurs when water rises beyond normal channel boundaries and spreads over adjacent land. Floods are natural events — rivers have always flooded — and they are among the most common and destructive natural hazards globally. Floods occur when the rate of water input to a river system exceeds the capacity of the channel to carry it.

A hydrograph is a graph showing discharge in a stream or river over time. For a given rainfall event, the hydrograph starts at the baseflow level, rises as runoff reaches the channel, peaks at the peak discharge, and then falls as water drains away. The time between peak rainfall and peak discharge is called the lag time.

Figure 17-D Hydrographs Comparing Forested and Urbanized Watersheds
This diagram shows two hydrographs displayed side by side for the same rainfall event applied to two neighboring watersheds of equal size: one 65% forested and one 65% impervious urban surface. The forested watershed hydrograph shows a gradual rise, a relatively low and rounded peak, a long lag time between rainfall and peak, and a slow recession. The baseflow contribution is clearly visible as a substantial portion of total flow. The urban watershed hydrograph shows a rapid, steep rise, a much higher and sharper peak, a short lag time, and a fast recession, with low baseflow. Study Questions

  1. Why does the urbanized watershed have a higher, faster flood peak for the same rainfall event?
  2. What explains the higher pre-storm baseflow in the forested watershed?
  3. How might converting a forest watershed to agriculture change the hydrograph shape?

A floodplain is the flat land adjacent to a river that is built by river deposition and subject to periodic flooding. The 100-year flood (also called the 1% annual chance flood) is a statistical concept: a flood level that has a 1% probability of being equaled or exceeded in any given year. It is not a prediction that such a flood occurs exactly once per century.

Human Amplification of Flood Risk

Urbanization reduces infiltration, increases runoff volume and speed, and raises peak discharge. Studies consistently show that urbanized watersheds produce larger, faster floods than forested or agricultural ones with similar rainfall. Floodplain development puts people and property in harm's way. Loss of wetlands removes natural storage capacity. Channelization — straightening and lining streams with concrete — reduces flood peaks locally but often increases them downstream. Climate change is altering precipitation patterns in ways that affect flood risk in many regions.

Section 17.9

Water Quality

Water quality refers to the chemical, physical, and biological characteristics of water that determine its suitability for particular uses — drinking, irrigation, aquatic life support, swimming, industrial processes. It is not a single number; it is a profile of measurements compared against standards.

Biological Indicators

Coliform bacteria, especially fecal coliform and E. coli, indicate contamination from animal or human waste. Their presence in drinking water is a public health emergency.

Macroinvertebrates — insects, worms, crustaceans, and mollusks living in and around stream sediments — are widely used as biological indicators of water quality. Because different species tolerate different levels of pollution, the composition of a macroinvertebrate community reflects water quality conditions over time. A stream with abundant sensitive species like stoneflies and mayflies is typically in good ecological condition. A stream dominated by pollution-tolerant species like aquatic worms and midges likely has water quality problems.

Why Biological Indicators Matter

A single water chemistry sample tells you what was in the water at one moment. Macroinvertebrates tell you what conditions have been like over weeks or months — because their communities reflect the cumulative quality of the stream, not just the snapshot at the moment of sampling. Biological and chemical data together provide a more complete picture than either approach alone.

Section 17.10

Watershed Management

Watershed management is the coordinated planning and stewardship of the land, water, and ecosystems within a watershed to achieve goals related to water quality, water quantity, flood risk, ecosystem health, and community well-being. It recognizes that water systems cannot be managed effectively by focusing only on rivers and lakes — the entire watershed must be considered.

Best Management Practices

PracticeDescriptionPrimary Benefit
Riparian buffersStrips of natural vegetation maintained along stream banksFilters runoff, stabilizes banks, shades water, provides habitat

New York City's Watershed Protection Program

Rather than building an enormously expensive filtration plant, New York City negotiated land protection agreements and best management practices with landowners in the Catskill Mountains watershed that supplies the city. The program has saved billions of dollars in avoided infrastructure costs while protecting one of the largest unfiltered municipal water supplies in the world — a widely cited example of watershed protection as a cost-effective alternative to end-of-pipe treatment.

Effective watershed management also depends on systematic monitoring — the ongoing collection of data on water quality, streamflow, groundwater levels, land cover, and biological communities — and on adaptive management, an approach that treats interventions as experiments, adjusts strategies based on results, and accepts that complex watershed systems often behave in ways that are difficult to predict precisely.

Key Terms Review

aquifer
A body of rock or sediment that is sufficiently porous and permeable to store and yield significant amounts of groundwater.
artesian well
A well drilled into a confined aquifer where water pressure causes water to rise above the top of the aquifer without pumping.
baseflow
The portion of streamflow contributed by groundwater seeping into the channel; sustains rivers during dry periods.
braided river
A river pattern with multiple interconnected channels separated by sediment bars; common where sediment supply is high.
confined aquifer
An aquifer bounded above by a low-permeability confining layer, trapping groundwater under pressure.
cone of depression
The funnel-shaped decline in the water table that forms around a pumping well.
cultural eutrophication
Human-accelerated enrichment of a water body with nutrients, particularly nitrogen and phosphorus, leading to algal blooms and oxygen depletion.
cut bank
The outer bank of a river meander, where erosion is concentrated due to faster water velocity.
discharge
The volume of water flowing past a cross-section of a river per unit time; calculated as area × velocity, expressed in m³/s.
dissolved oxygen
Oxygen gas dissolved in water; essential for aquatic organisms. Levels below 5 mg/L begin to stress fish.
epilimnion
The warm, well-mixed upper layer of a thermally stratified lake.
eutrophication
The process by which a water body becomes enriched in nutrients, stimulating algal growth and often reducing dissolved oxygen.
floodplain
The flat land bordering a river, formed by river deposition and subject to periodic flooding.
groundwater
Water stored in the pores, cracks, and spaces within rock and sediment below Earth's surface.
groundwater overdraft
Withdrawal of groundwater at a rate faster than it is naturally recharged.
hydrograph
A graph showing how discharge in a river or stream changes over time in response to precipitation or other inputs.
hypolimnion
The cold, dense bottom layer of a thermally stratified lake.
infiltration
The process by which water soaks into the soil and subsurface from the surface.
land subsidence
Sinking of the ground surface due to compaction of sediments caused by groundwater removal.
macroinvertebrates
Invertebrate animals visible to the naked eye that live in or near stream sediments; widely used as biological water quality indicators.
meander
A sweeping curve or bend in a river channel, formed by differential erosion on the outside and deposition on the inside of curves.
oxbow lake
A curved lake formed when a meander is cut off from the main river channel.
permeability
A measure of how easily fluids can move through a material; high permeability means fluids move easily.
point bar
A deposit of sediment on the inside of a river meander, where water velocity is lower.
porosity
The percentage of a material's volume that consists of open spaces; determines how much fluid a material can store.
recharge
The process by which water enters and replenishes an aquifer, primarily through infiltration.
recurrence interval
The statistical average time between flood events of a given size; a 100-year flood has a 1% probability in any given year.
residence time
The average length of time a water molecule spends in a particular reservoir before moving on.
riparian buffer
A strip of natural vegetation maintained along a stream bank that filters runoff, stabilizes banks, and provides habitat.
spring
A place where groundwater flows naturally to the surface.
thermal stratification
The layering of a lake into temperature zones that resist vertical mixing; typically develops during warm seasons.
thermocline
The zone of rapid temperature change between the warm epilimnion and the cold hypolimnion in a stratified lake.
turbidity
A measure of water clarity based on the amount of suspended material that reduces light penetration; measured in NTU.
unconfined aquifer
An aquifer directly below a permeable layer, with a water table that rises and falls in response to recharge and withdrawal.
water quality
The chemical, physical, and biological characteristics of water that determine its suitability for particular uses.
water table
The upper surface of the saturated zone; the boundary between saturated and unsaturated subsurface material.
watershed
The entire area of land that drains to a common outlet; also called a drainage basin or catchment.
watershed management
The coordinated planning and stewardship of land, water, and ecosystems within a watershed to achieve water quality, quantity, flood, and ecological goals.
wetland
An area where land is saturated or covered by shallow water long enough to support vegetation adapted to wet conditions; includes marshes, swamps, bogs, and fens.
zone of aeration
The unsaturated zone above the water table where pores contain both air and water.
zone of saturation
The zone below the water table where all pore spaces are filled with water.

Error Analysis

Identify the error or misconception in each statement and write a corrected version.

Statement 1
"Groundwater is stored in large underground lakes and rivers."

What is the error? Where is groundwater actually stored? Write a corrected explanation.

Statement 2
"A 100-year flood only happens once every hundred years."

What is the error in this interpretation? What does the 100-year flood designation actually mean?

Statement 3
"Since the water table just means where water starts underground, its depth never changes."

What is wrong with this description? Under what conditions does the water table rise or fall?

Statement 4
"Rivers always flow faster and carry more sediment in the lower part of their course where they are widest."

What is inaccurate here? How do velocity and sediment transport actually change along a river's longitudinal profile?

Statement 5
"Wetlands should be drained and filled because they are swampy, unproductive land that serves no useful purpose."

Identify at least four ecological or hydrological functions that this statement ignores.

Statement 6
"If a contamination source is removed from a confined aquifer's recharge zone, the aquifer will be clean again within a few months."

What does this statement misunderstand about groundwater flow velocity and residence time? How long might contamination persist?

Chapter 18 turns from freshwater to the vast saltwater systems that cover more than 70% of Earth's surface.

Chapter 13 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

On January 17, 1994, at 4:31 in the morning, the ground beneath Northridge, California began to shake. The earthquake lasted less than a minute, but in that time it collapsed freeways, buckled apartment buildings, ruptured gas lines, and knocked out power across the Los Angeles region. Fifty-seven people died. Thousands were injured. Property damage exceeded $20 billion.

What caused it? A fault — a fracture in Earth's crust — had been storing energy for decades as tectonic plates slowly ground past each other. When the stress finally exceeded the strength of the rocks, the fault slipped. Seismic waves radiated outward in all directions. The ground moved.

Earthquakes are not random. They are the predictable result of forces that have been operating on Earth for billions of years. This chapter explains those forces — where they originate, how energy builds and releases, how waves travel through rock, how scientists measure and locate earthquakes, what secondary hazards follow, and how communities can reduce their risk.

Opening Question

A fault near a major city has not produced a large earthquake in more than 300 years. A geologist says it is "overdue." What does that mean scientifically? How do scientists estimate earthquake probability when the last event happened before modern instruments existed? By the end of this chapter, you will understand the evidence and reasoning behind such estimates — and why the distinction between prediction and forecasting matters.

Section 13.1

What Earthquakes Are

An earthquake is a sudden release of energy stored in Earth's crust. That energy travels outward as seismic waves, causing the ground to shake.

To understand earthquakes, think of the crust not as a rigid, unchanging shell, but as a collection of rock masses under constant stress. Tectonic plates move. Rock bends, compresses, stretches, and twists. Most of the time, friction holds rocks in place even as forces push and pull on them. But rock has limits. When stress builds to a breaking point, rock fractures or slips along an existing crack. The energy stored in strained rock releases all at once.

Earthquakes happen most often near plate boundaries, where tectonic forces are strongest. But they also occur in plate interiors, where ancient faults can reactivate. The New Madrid Seismic Zone in the central United States — far from any active plate boundary — produced some of the largest earthquakes in North American history in 1811 and 1812.

Earthquakes range enormously in size. Most are too small to feel. Others crack pavement, topple buildings, and trigger tsunamis that travel across entire ocean basins. The same basic process — stress, rupture, wave energy — explains all of them.

Systems Connection

Earthquakes are an output of the plate tectonic system introduced in Chapter 12. Energy inputs (Earth's internal heat, mantle convection) drive plate motion, which applies stress to crustal rocks at boundaries — the same boundaries where most earthquakes occur. Earthquake energy, in turn, drives other systems: tsunamis affect the hydrosphere; ash and gas from triggered volcanic activity affect the atmosphere.

Section 13.2

Stress, Strain, and Faults

Three Types of Crustal Stress

Stress is a force applied to a material per unit of area. In Earth's crust, stress comes primarily from tectonic plate movement — plates colliding, pulling apart, or sliding past each other — as well as from the weight of overlying rock.

Stress TypeWhat It Does to RockAssociated Plate BoundaryFault Type Produced
CompressionSqueezes rock together; shortens and thickens crustConvergentReverse / thrust faults
TensionPulls rock apart; stretches and thins crustDivergentNormal faults
ShearPushes rock in opposite horizontal directionsTransformStrike-slip faults

Elastic Strain and Brittle Failure

Strain is the change in shape or volume that results from stress. Rock can absorb a certain amount of strain elastically — like a bent rubber eraser, it springs back if the stress is removed. This is called elastic strain. But when stress exceeds the elastic limit of the rock, it either deforms permanently (plastic strain, common in deeper, hotter rocks) or fractures suddenly (brittle failure). Earthquakes result from brittle failure.

The Elastic Rebound Theory

Proposed by geologist Harry Fielding Reid after the 1906 San Francisco earthquake, the elastic rebound theory explains the earthquake mechanism. Rocks on opposite sides of a fault are slowly moving in different directions due to tectonic forces. Friction locks the fault, preventing immediate slip. As rocks continue to be pushed or pulled, they deform elastically — storing energy like a compressed spring. When stress finally exceeds the frictional resistance, the fault suddenly slips. Stored elastic energy releases in an instant; the rocks snap back toward their original shape. This "elastic rebound" produces seismic waves.

The longer a fault remains locked while tectonic forces continue building stress, the more energy accumulates — and the larger the eventual earthquake.

Section 13.3

Focus and Epicenter

Two Key Locations

Focus (hypocenter) — the point inside Earth where fault rupture begins and seismic energy is first released.

Epicenter — the point on Earth's surface directly above the focus. This is what news reports mean when they say an earthquake "struck" a location.

Why Shallow Earthquakes Are Most Dangerous

Imagine two identical earthquakes releasing the same total energy. One has a focus 10 km deep; the other has a focus 200 km deep. The shallower earthquake's energy reaches the surface before spreading far, delivering a more concentrated punch. The deeper earthquake's energy spreads over a much larger volume of rock before reaching the surface, arriving with less intensity at any one location. Shallow earthquakes produce the most destructive surface shaking for a given magnitude.

Section 13.4

Seismic Waves

When a fault ruptures, it releases energy that travels through Earth as seismic waves. These waves are the shaking you feel in an earthquake. They also carry information that scientists use to locate earthquakes, measure their size, and image Earth's interior.

Seismic waves come in two main families: body waves, which travel through Earth's interior, and surface waves, which travel along Earth's surface.

Body Waves

The S-Wave Shadow Zone — Evidence of Earth's Interior

S-waves cannot travel through liquids. Seismographs on the far side of Earth from a large earthquake detect P-waves but not S-waves — they lie in an "S-wave shadow zone." This is not because the waves were blocked by distance; it is because they entered Earth's liquid outer core and could not continue. This observation — made decades before any direct drilling could reach such depths — is one of the strongest pieces of evidence that Earth's outer core is liquid. (Chapter 11 explains this in detail.)

Example 13.4.1
Calculating P-S time interval
  • ProblemA seismic station is located 700 km from an earthquake epicenter. P-waves travel at approximately 6 km/s. S-waves travel at approximately 3.5 km/s. What is the P-S time interval — the difference in arrival times — at this station?
  • ThinkCalculate travel time for each wave type separately, then find the difference. Time = Distance ÷ Speed.
  • Calculate P-wave travel time = 700 km ÷ 6 km/s ≈ 116.7 seconds S-wave travel time = 700 km ÷ 3.5 km/s = 200 seconds P-S interval = 200 − 116.7 ≈ 83.3 seconds (about 1 min 23 s)
  • CheckThe P-S gap increases with distance — this is the physical basis for locating earthquakes. A larger gap means a more distant earthquake. Does 83 seconds seem reasonable for 700 km? Yes — consistent with typical P-S curves. ✓
  • InterpretIf a seismologist observed an 83-second P-S gap on a seismogram, they could use a travel-time graph to estimate the station is approximately 700 km from the earthquake source.
Answer: P-S time interval ≈ 83 seconds at a distance of 700 km.

Section 13.5

Measuring Earthquakes

How large was an earthquake? This simple question has a complicated answer, because earthquakes can be measured in more than one way — by the energy released at the source, or by the shaking experienced at a given location.

Magnitude — Energy at the Source

Magnitude measures the energy released at the earthquake source. It is a property of the earthquake itself, not of any particular location. The original Richter scale, developed by Charles Richter in 1935, measured the amplitude of seismic waves recorded on a specific type of seismograph at a standard distance. Modern seismology uses the moment magnitude scale (Mw), which accounts for the area of the fault that ruptured, the average slip distance, and the stiffness of the rock. The moment magnitude scale is the current standard in scientific reporting.

Each whole-number increase in magnitude represents approximately a 32-fold increase in energy released. A magnitude 7 earthquake releases about 32 times more energy than a magnitude 6, and about 1,000 times more energy than a magnitude 5.

Intensity — Shaking at a Location

Intensity measures the strength of shaking at a specific location on the surface. The same earthquake has different intensities at different places — stronger shaking near the epicenter, weaker shaking farther away.

The Modified Mercalli Intensity Scale (MMI) uses Roman numerals from I to XII to describe the observed effects of shaking:

MMI LevelTypical Observable Effects
INot felt except under special conditions.
IIIFelt indoors by some; hanging objects swing; resembles a passing truck.
VFelt by nearly everyone; small objects displaced; some dishes broken; pendulum clocks may stop.
VIIDifficult to stand; significant damage to poorly built structures; chimneys collapse.
IXDamage to well-built structures; ground cracks; buildings shifted off foundations; underground pipes broken.
XIITotal destruction; objects thrown into the air; ground moves in visible waves; essentially all structures damaged or destroyed.
Common Misconception — Magnitude vs. Intensity

Magnitude is a single number for the whole earthquake. Intensity varies by location. A magnitude 7.0 earthquake might produce MMI IX shaking at the epicenter but only MMI IV shaking 300 km away. These are not contradictions — they measure different things. A news report saying "Intensity VII shaking struck the city" and a seismologist reporting "Magnitude 6.2" are both correct and compatible.

Section 13.6

Locating Earthquakes

Finding the epicenter and focus of an earthquake requires data from multiple seismograph stations. The process uses the difference in P-wave and S-wave travel times — a technique called triangulation (more precisely, trilateration).

The Locating Process — Three Steps

Step 1: Measure the P-S time interval at each seismic station. Use a travel-time graph to convert each interval to an epicentral distance (radius).

Step 2: Draw a circle around each station with radius equal to its epicentral distance. The epicenter lies somewhere on each circle.

Step 3: The epicenter is the point where all three circles intersect. Two circles give two possible points; a third circle resolves the ambiguity to one location.

Example 13.6.1
Interpreting P-S data to find epicentral distance
  • ProblemA seismic station in Denver records a P-S time interval of 4 minutes and 20 seconds (260 seconds). Using approximate wave speeds (P = 6 km/s, S = 3.5 km/s), estimate the epicentral distance. Then explain what further data would be needed to locate the actual epicenter.
  • ThinkIf the P-S interval is 260 seconds, set up an equation. Let d = distance. P travel time = d/6. S travel time = d/3.5. Their difference = 260 s.
  • Calculate d/3.5 − d/6 = 260 d(1/3.5 − 1/6) = 260 d(0.2857 − 0.1667) = 260 d × 0.119 = 260 d ≈ 2,185 km
  • InterpretDenver can draw a circle of radius ~2,185 km. The epicenter lies somewhere on that circle. To find the single intersection point, data from at least two more stations with their own P-S intervals and calculated radii are needed.
Answer: Epicentral distance ≈ 2,185 km from Denver. At least two additional stations are needed to pinpoint the epicenter.
Global Seismographic Network

The Global Seismographic Network (GSN) includes over 150 permanently operating seismic stations around the world, providing continuous real-time data. Most earthquakes are located within minutes of occurrence. Regional networks in California, Japan, Chile, and New Zealand provide even denser coverage in high-risk areas, enabling rapid ShakeMap generation and early warning alerts.

Section 13.7

Earthquake Hazards

Ground shaking is the most obvious earthquake hazard, but earthquakes produce a cascade of secondary effects that can be equally or more deadly. Effective hazard assessment requires understanding all of these pathways.

Ground Shaking and Site Amplification

The direct shaking of the ground damages and destroys buildings, bridges, roads, and pipelines. Damage depends on shaking intensity, duration, distance from the epicenter, and — crucially — the type of soil or sediment at the surface.

Site amplification occurs when soft sediments — river deposits, bay mud, fill material — amplify shaking dramatically compared to solid bedrock. In the 1989 Loma Prieta earthquake, neighborhoods in San Francisco and Oakland built on Bay mud experienced far more damage than bedrock areas at the same distance from the epicenter. Some soil types can amplify shaking by a factor of ten or more.

Section 13.8

Tsunamis

A tsunami is a series of ocean waves generated by a sudden vertical displacement of a large volume of water. The most common trigger is a large submarine earthquake at a subduction zone, though underwater landslides and volcanic eruptions can also generate tsunamis.

The Drawback Warning — Never Walk Out on an Exposed Seafloor

A characteristic warning sign of an approaching tsunami is the drawback — the ocean visibly recedes, sometimes exposing hundreds of meters of seafloor. This happens because the trough of the leading wave arrives before the crest. People who recognize this sign have a few minutes to move to high ground immediately. People who walk out to collect stranded fish on the exposed seafloor are in the most dangerous position possible when the crest arrives.

Tsunami Warning Systems

The Pacific Tsunami Warning Center and the National Tsunami Warning Center monitor seismic activity and sea-level sensors throughout the Pacific. When a large submarine earthquake occurs, warnings give coastal communities minutes to hours of advance notice depending on distance from the source.

Deep-ocean DART buoys (Deep-ocean Assessment and Reporting of Tsunamis) use pressure sensors to confirm tsunami generation in real time, reducing false alarms that cause evacuation fatigue.

The 2004 Indian Ocean tsunami (M9.1 off Sumatra) killed approximately 230,000 people in 14 countries — in part because the Indian Ocean had no warning system at the time. Warning infrastructure has been substantially expanded globally since then.

Example 13.8.1
Tsunami travel time estimation
  • ProblemA magnitude 9.0 earthquake occurs in the Aleutian Islands off Alaska. Honolulu, Hawaii is approximately 4,000 km away. If the tsunami travels at an average speed of 700 km/h across the deep Pacific, how much warning time does Honolulu have between the earthquake and tsunami arrival?
  • Calculate Time = Distance ÷ Speed Time = 4,000 km ÷ 700 km/h ≈ 5.7 hours

Section 13.9

Earthquake Prediction, Forecasting, and Preparedness

Prediction vs. Forecasting

Earthquake prediction refers to specifying the time, location, and magnitude of a future earthquake with enough precision to be useful for action — for example, "a magnitude 7 earthquake will strike near Parkfield, California, between 2:00 and 3:00 PM on March 15." Despite decades of research, reliable short-term earthquake prediction remains beyond current scientific capability. Earthquakes do not consistently produce measurable precursory signals that could enable reliable warnings.

Earthquake forecasting is different. Scientists can estimate the probability of an earthquake of a given magnitude occurring in a particular region over a given time period — decades, not hours. This is analogous to the difference between predicting tomorrow's weather (difficult) and estimating the climate of a region (more tractable).

Probabilistic Earthquake Forecasting — An Example

The Uniform California Earthquake Rupture Forecast (UCERF) estimates there is approximately a 60% probability of a magnitude 6.7 or greater earthquake in the San Francisco Bay Area within any given 30-year window. This type of probabilistic forecast guides building codes, insurance rates, emergency planning, and infrastructure investment — even though it cannot say when, exactly, the next large earthquake will occur.

Earthquake Early Warning — Not Prediction

Earthquake early warning is not the same as prediction. It uses the speed difference between P-waves (fast, little damage) and S-waves (slower, more damaging) to send electronic alerts ahead of the destructive shaking.

When a seismic network near a fault detects P-waves, it calculates the earthquake's likely magnitude and location and transmits warnings at the speed of light — ahead of the slower, damaging S-waves and surface waves. The ShakeAlert system, operational in California, Oregon, and Washington, can provide seconds to tens of seconds of warning.

Example 13.9.1
Calculating early warning time
  • ProblemA school is located 40 km from an active fault. A magnitude 7.2 earthquake occurs. Using P-wave speed ≈ 6 km/s and S-wave speed ≈ 3.5 km/s: (a) How long after the earthquake do P-waves arrive at the school? (b) How long before S-waves arrive? (c) If the early warning system detects the earthquake 5 seconds after it begins and sends an alert at the speed of light, how many seconds of warning does the school get before S-waves arrive?
  • Calculate P-wave travel time = 40 ÷ 6 ≈ 6.7 s S-wave travel time = 40 ÷ 3.5 ≈ 11.4 s Warning sent at: t = 5 s after quake (detection delay) Warning arrives at school at: ~5 s (light speed ≈ instantaneous at 40 km) Warning time before S-waves: 11.4 − 5 = 6.4 seconds
  • InterpretThe school receives about 6 seconds of warning before damaging S-waves arrive. This is enough time for students to drop, cover, and hold on — a meaningful reduction in injury risk from falling objects and collapsing furniture, even if it is not enough to evacuate a building.
Answer: ~6.4 seconds of early warning before S-waves arrive at a school 40 km from the epicenter.
Why Early Warning Cannot Help Locations Near the Fault

Early warning systems need time to detect P-waves and process the signal before sending an alert. This takes several seconds at minimum. For locations very close to the fault — within 10–15 km — the S-waves may arrive before or nearly simultaneously with the warning, leaving no usable alert time. Early warning provides the most benefit for areas 30–200 km from large faults, where the travel time difference between detection, alert transmission, and S-wave arrival creates a useful window.

Drop, Cover, and Hold On — Why Doorways Are Not Safer

The old advice to stand in a doorway during an earthquake is no longer recommended by emergency managers. In older unreinforced adobe construction, doorframes were sometimes the strongest part of the structure. In modern buildings, doorways offer no special protection — and standing in one near the door opening means risk from a swinging door and exposure to falling objects. The current recommendation is universal: Drop to the ground; take cover under a sturdy table or desk, or against an interior wall; protect your head and neck with your arms; hold on until shaking stops.

Section 13.10

Earthquake Risk and Resilience

Hazard refers to the natural phenomenon itself — the earthquake. Risk depends on three factors: hazard, exposure (who and what is in the affected area), and vulnerability (how susceptible people and structures are to damage).

Two communities might face the same earthquake hazard. One — with well-built structures, enforced codes, prepared residents, functioning emergency services, and economic resources to invest in preparedness — will experience far less damage and recover faster. The other — with older construction, limited resources, dense informal settlement, or inadequate emergency capacity — may experience a catastrophe from the same earthquake.

The Risk Equation

Risk = f(Hazard × Exposure × Vulnerability)

Reducing any one of these three factors reduces risk. Earthquakes are natural hazards that cannot be eliminated, but exposure can be reduced through land-use planning, and vulnerability can be reduced through building codes, retrofitting, preparedness, and accessible emergency information.

Disproportionate impacts. Earthquake damage is not distributed equally across a community. Communities with older housing stock, inadequate building codes, limited insurance, or constrained government resources experience greater losses. Renters often have fewer options than homeowners to assess or influence building safety. Low-income neighborhoods may have higher concentrations of unreinforced masonry buildings or buildings on problematic soils. Disability, language barriers, and lack of access to warning information can slow evacuation.

Recognizing these patterns is essential for designing earthquake preparedness and mitigation programs that actually reach the most vulnerable populations — not just those with the resources to seek out safety improvements independently.

Evidence-based resilience. Resilient communities use earthquake science — seismic hazard maps, building code research, loss estimation models, liquefaction and landslide hazard maps, and post-earthquake reconnaissance — to make decisions before disasters happen. Evidence gathered patiently before an event is what allows communities, engineers, planners, and individuals to reduce harm. Earthquakes are not fully preventable, but deaths and losses from earthquakes are substantially preventable — and the scientific knowledge to prevent them exists.

Chapter 13 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 13.1Earthquakes result from the sudden release of elastic energy stored in stressed rocks. They occur most often near plate boundaries but also in plate interiors where ancient faults remain active.
  • Sec 13.2Three types of stress — compression, tension, and shear — produce three main fault types: reverse, normal, and strike-slip. The elastic rebound theory explains how stress accumulates and releases suddenly when rocks fracture along faults.
  • Sec 13.3The focus (hypocenter) is the point inside Earth where rupture begins; the epicenter is the surface point directly above it. Shallow earthquakes cause the most surface damage because their energy reaches the surface before spreading widely.
  • Sec 13.4Seismic waves include P-waves (compressional, fastest, travel through all materials) and S-waves (shear, slower, travel through solids only) as body waves, plus Love and Rayleigh waves as surface waves. S-wave shadow zones reveal Earth's liquid outer core.
  • Sec 13.5Magnitude measures the energy released at the source (moment magnitude scale, Mw); intensity measures shaking effects at a specific location (Modified Mercalli Scale, I–XII). The same earthquake has one magnitude but many intensities.
  • Sec 13.6P-S time intervals allow calculation of epicentral distance. Data from three or more seismic stations are used in triangulation to pinpoint the epicenter. The Global Seismographic Network locates most earthquakes within minutes.
  • Sec 13.7Beyond ground shaking, earthquake hazards include liquefaction, landslides, fires, ground rupture, and aftershocks. Site amplification in soft sediments can dramatically increase shaking intensity compared to bedrock areas at equal epicentral distance.
  • Sec 13.8Tsunamis are generated by sudden vertical seafloor displacement. In the open ocean they travel fast (~800 km/h) but are nearly undetectable. Shoaling near shore slows waves and concentrates energy into devastating heights. DART buoys and tsunami warning centers provide advance notice for distant coastlines.
  • Sec 13.9Short-term earthquake prediction remains unreliable. Probabilistic forecasting estimates earthquake probability over decades and guides building codes and planning. Early warning systems exploit the P-S speed difference to provide seconds to tens of seconds of alert before damaging waves arrive.
  • Sec 13.10Earthquake risk depends on hazard, exposure, and vulnerability — not just magnitude. Damage and death are not equally distributed; social, economic, and built-environment factors determine who is harmed and how severely. Evidence-based resilience reduces risk substantially even when the hazard itself cannot be eliminated.

Key Terms Review

earthquake
A sudden release of energy stored in stressed rocks, usually along a fault, producing seismic waves that cause ground shaking.
stress
Force applied to a material per unit of area; in Earth's crust, produced primarily by tectonic plate motion.
compression
Stress that squeezes rock together; associated with convergent plate boundaries; produces reverse and thrust faults.
tension
Stress that pulls rock apart; associated with divergent plate boundaries; produces normal faults.
shear
Stress that pushes rock in opposite horizontal directions; associated with transform boundaries; produces strike-slip faults.
elastic strain
Temporary, reversible deformation of rock under stress; rock returns to original shape if stress is removed.
fault
A fracture in Earth's crust along which rock masses have moved relative to each other.
normal fault
A fault formed under tension where the hanging wall moves downward relative to the footwall.
reverse fault
A fault formed under compression where the hanging wall moves upward relative to the footwall.
thrust fault
A low-angle reverse fault; common at convergent boundaries where one plate overrides another.
strike-slip fault
A fault in which rock masses move horizontally past each other; formed under shear stress.
elastic rebound theory
The explanation that earthquakes result from sudden release of elastic energy accumulated in rocks deformed by tectonic stress.
focus (hypocenter)
The point inside Earth where fault rupture begins and seismic energy is first released.
epicenter
The point on Earth's surface directly above the earthquake focus.
seismic wave
A wave of energy that travels through Earth following an earthquake or other disturbance.
P-wave
Primary seismic body wave; compresses and expands material in the direction of travel; fastest; travels through solids, liquids, and gases.
S-wave
Secondary seismic body wave; shakes material perpendicular to travel direction; slower than P-waves; cannot travel through liquids.
Love waves
Surface seismic waves that move the ground horizontally, perpendicular to the direction of wave travel.
Rayleigh waves
Surface seismic waves that move the ground in an elliptical rolling motion, producing both vertical and horizontal shaking.
magnitude
A measure of the total energy released by an earthquake; reported using the moment magnitude scale (Mw).
moment magnitude (Mw)
The modern scientific scale for measuring earthquake energy, accounting for fault area, slip distance, and rock stiffness.
intensity
The strength of earthquake shaking at a specific location; described using the Modified Mercalli Intensity Scale.
Modified Mercalli Scale
A scale from I to XII describing the observed effects of earthquake shaking at a location.
site amplification
The increase in ground shaking in areas underlain by soft sediments compared to bedrock at equal distance from an earthquake.
liquefaction
The temporary behavior of saturated, loosely packed sediment as a liquid during intense shaking; buildings sink, pipes float.
aftershock
A smaller earthquake following a mainshock, as stress redistributes along the fault zone; can continue for months or years.
tsunami
A series of ocean waves generated by a large, sudden vertical displacement of water, most commonly by a submarine earthquake.
shoaling
The process by which a tsunami wave slows and increases dramatically in height as it enters shallow coastal water.
runup
The maximum elevation above sea level reached by tsunami water on land.
DART system
Deep-ocean Assessment and Reporting of Tsunamis; pressure-sensor buoys used for real-time tsunami detection in open ocean.
ShakeAlert
The earthquake early warning system operating in California, Oregon, and Washington; provides seconds to tens of seconds of warning.
hazard
A natural phenomenon with the potential to cause harm (e.g., the earthquake itself).
exposure
The people, structures, and systems located in areas that could be affected by a hazard.
vulnerability
The degree to which people, structures, or systems are susceptible to harm from a hazard event.
triangulation
The use of data from three or more seismic stations — each providing an epicentral distance — to determine earthquake location.

Common Misconceptions

Misconception 1 — Magnitude equals danger
"A magnitude 7 earthquake is always more dangerous than a magnitude 5."

Correct reasoning: A magnitude 5 earthquake directly beneath a densely populated city with unreinforced buildings can be far more destructive than a magnitude 7 in a remote, uninhabited area. Danger (risk) depends on exposure and vulnerability, not magnitude alone. The 1989 Loma Prieta (M6.9) and 2010 Haiti (M7.0) earthquakes both illustrate how social and structural factors determine death tolls more than raw magnitude.

Misconception 2 — Earthquakes can be predicted
"Scientists know when the next big earthquake will happen."

Correct reasoning: Scientists can forecast the probability of earthquakes over decades, but reliable short-term prediction (specific time, place, magnitude) remains beyond current capability. Probabilistic forecasts are scientifically valid and practically useful; they are not the same as prediction. Anyone claiming to predict a specific earthquake with a specific date should be treated with extreme skepticism.

Misconception 3 — Standing in a doorway is safest
"The safest place during an earthquake is in a doorway."

Correct reasoning: This advice applied to older unreinforced adobe buildings where doorframes were sometimes the strongest structural element. In modern buildings, doorways offer no special protection. The recommended action is Drop, Cover, and Hold On — under a sturdy desk or table if possible, against an interior wall if not. Doorways expose people to swinging doors and falling objects.

Misconception 4 — Tsunamis are giant waves in the open ocean
"A ship at sea would be destroyed by a tsunami."

Correct reasoning: In deep water, tsunami waves are barely detectable — wave height may be under a meter with wavelengths of hundreds of kilometers. Ships at sea are in no danger. Tsunamis become dangerous only as they enter shallow coastal water, where shoaling concentrates their enormous energy into dramatically reduced water depth, causing wave heights to increase dramatically.

Misconception 5 — The epicenter is the most dangerous location
"The worst damage is always directly at the epicenter."

Correct reasoning: While shaking is often intense near the epicenter, site amplification on soft sediments can produce greater damage at locations farther away. In the 1989 Loma Prieta earthquake, some of the worst damage occurred in San Francisco neighborhoods on bay mud — nearly 100 km from the epicenter — rather than at the epicenter itself in the sparsely populated Santa Cruz Mountains.

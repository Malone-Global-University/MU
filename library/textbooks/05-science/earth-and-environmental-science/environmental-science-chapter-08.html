Chapter 8 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Rocks do not stay the same forever. Deep inside Earth's crust, where temperatures soar and pressures crush, rocks that formed at the surface are slowly transformed into something entirely different — without ever fully melting. These transformed rocks are called metamorphic rocks, and they are some of the most dramatic evidence we have that Earth is an active, changing planet.

In this chapter, you will learn what metamorphism means, what forces drive it, what kinds of metamorphic rocks result, and how metamorphic processes connect to plate tectonics, mountain building, and the rock cycle. You will also practice reading rock descriptions, classifying metamorphic rocks by texture and composition, and tracing rock transformation across Earth systems.

Opening Question

Deep beneath the Himalayas, rock that was once seafloor sediment is being squeezed between two colliding continents. Temperatures are hundreds of degrees. Pressures are equivalent to carrying billions of tons above your head. The rock cannot escape and it cannot melt — so what happens to it? By the end of this chapter, you will know the answer, and you will be able to read the record of that transformation in the minerals and textures of the rock itself.

The Meaning and Mechanics of Metamorphism
What Metamorphism Means · Heat · Pressure · Chemically Active Fluids
Section 8.1

What Metamorphism Means

The word metamorphism comes from Greek roots meaning "to change form." In geology, metamorphism is the process by which existing rocks are changed in mineralogy, texture, or both — as a result of heat, pressure, or chemically active fluids — while remaining solid throughout. This last point is critical: if a rock melts completely, it becomes magma, and any rock that crystallizes from that magma is an igneous rock, not a metamorphic one. Metamorphism happens below the melting point.

Metamorphic rocks form from parent rocks, also called protoliths (from Greek protos, meaning "first"). Any rock type can serve as a protolith — igneous, sedimentary, or even a previously metamorphosed rock. The kind of metamorphic rock that results depends on two things: the composition of the protolith and the conditions of metamorphism.

Why Do Rocks Change?

Rocks are stable under the conditions in which they form. Granite is stable at the surface. Limestone is stable on a shallow seafloor. But when rocks are pushed deep into Earth's crust — by burial, tectonic collision, or intrusion of hot magma — conditions change drastically. Temperature increases. Pressure increases. Hot fluids move through cracks. Under these new conditions, the minerals that made up the original rock may no longer be stable. Atoms rearrange. New minerals grow. Crystal structures flatten or align. The rock changes — often profoundly — while staying solid.

This transformation can happen slowly, over thousands to millions of years. The end product is a metamorphic rock that tells a story about the conditions it experienced.

What Metamorphism Is — and Is Not

It is: transformation of rock by heat, pressure, and/or fluids at depth, without melting.

It is not melting. Rocks that melt completely become igneous rocks, not metamorphic ones.

It is not weathering. Weathering breaks rocks down at Earth's surface. Metamorphism transforms rocks at depth.

It is not erosion. Erosion moves particles. Metamorphism changes mineralogy and texture inside the rock.

Section 8.2

Heat, Pressure, and Chemically Active Fluids

Three agents drive metamorphism: heat, pressure, and chemically active fluids. Each plays a distinct role, and they often act together.

Heat

Heat provides the energy for chemical reactions. When temperature rises, atoms move faster and are more likely to rearrange into new mineral structures. Most metamorphism occurs between about 150°C and 800°C. Below 150°C, reactions are too slow to produce significant change in geologic time. Above 800°C, rocks begin to melt.

Heat reaches rocks in two main ways. First, the geothermal gradient means that as rock is buried deeper into the crust, it encounters higher temperatures — on average about 25–30°C per kilometer of depth. Second, magmatic intrusion occurs when magma pushes into the crust, heating the surrounding rock dramatically — far exceeding what burial alone would produce.

Pressure

Pressure also increases with depth because the weight of overlying rock bears down. Confining pressure (also called lithostatic pressure) pushes equally in all directions, like the pressure you feel underwater. It compacts rock and can trigger the formation of denser minerals.

A different kind of pressure — directed pressure, also called differential stress — pushes more strongly in one direction than another. This happens when tectonic plates collide and squeeze rock from one side. Directed pressure is responsible for the alignment of minerals in a preferred direction, which gives many metamorphic rocks their characteristic layered or banded appearance.

Chemically Active Fluids

Water and other fluids play a surprisingly large role in metamorphism, even though metamorphic rocks are solid. Fluids — mostly hot water with dissolved ions — move through tiny pores and cracks in rock. They speed up chemical reactions, transport ions from place to place, and can introduce or remove elements from the rock, changing its composition. This process is called metasomatism.

Fluids are particularly important in contact metamorphism near igneous intrusions, where hot magmatic water can dramatically alter surrounding rock. They are also important along subduction zones, where water is driven out of descending oceanic crust and rises into the overlying mantle and crust.

Confining Pressure Compacts rock; promotes denser mineral structures Deep burial under thick crust
Directed Pressure Aligns minerals; produces foliation Tectonic collision zones

Systems Thinking Connection

Metamorphism is driven by inputs of energy (heat) and force (pressure) into the geosphere. The results — new minerals, new textures, new rock types — are outputs that connect back to the rock cycle, plate tectonics, and even soil formation millions of years later when these rocks are eroded at the surface.

Section 8.3

Regional Metamorphism

Regional metamorphism is the most widespread type of metamorphism. It affects large volumes of rock — sometimes hundreds of thousands of square kilometers — and is driven primarily by the intense heat and pressure of mountain building at convergent plate boundaries.

When continental plates collide, the crust thickens and rocks are buried deep. Over millions of years, rocks that were once at the surface are pushed 10, 20, or even 30 kilometers below ground. At these depths, temperatures and pressures are extreme. The rocks undergo intense metamorphism, recrystallizing into new minerals and developing strong planar textures due to directed pressure from the collision.

The rocks that form during regional metamorphism include slate, phyllite, schist, and gneiss — a sequence that reflects increasing intensity of metamorphism with increasing depth and temperature. These rocks make up the cores of mountain ranges around the world. When the mountains erode over millions of years, the metamorphic rocks that were once deep underground become exposed at the surface.

Real-World Connection

The Appalachian Mountains of eastern North America, the Alps of Europe, and the Himalayas of Asia all contain extensive metamorphic cores formed during ancient or ongoing plate collisions. When you hike on exposed bedrock in the Appalachians, you are often walking on rocks that formed 10 or more kilometers underground, hundreds of millions of years ago.

Section 8.4

Contact Metamorphism

Contact metamorphism occurs when rock is heated by direct contact with magma — either a volcanic intrusion (like a dike or sill) or a larger pluton, such as a batholith. The heat bakes the surrounding rock, transforming it in a zone called a contact aureole (from the Latin word for "crown" or "halo").

Unlike regional metamorphism, contact metamorphism is driven primarily by heat rather than pressure. The affected rock is heated but not necessarily subjected to strong directed pressure. As a result, the metamorphic rocks that form near intrusions often lack the strong foliation characteristic of regional metamorphic rocks. Instead, they tend to be hard, dense, and fine-grained.

The Contact Aureole

A contact aureole is a zone of metamorphosed rock surrounding an igneous intrusion. The rock closest to the intrusion experienced the most intense heat and therefore the most dramatic transformation. Farther away, the rock experienced lower temperatures and shows less metamorphic change. The result is a series of concentric zones of progressively lower-grade metamorphism radiating outward from the intrusion.

The size of a contact aureole depends on the size and temperature of the intrusion. A small dike may produce a contact aureole only centimeters wide. A large pluton can produce an aureole kilometers across.

Economic Connection

Contact metamorphism is important economically. Hot magmatic fluids moving through contact zones can precipitate valuable minerals, concentrating copper, gold, silver, and other metals. Many significant ore deposits worldwide are associated with contact metamorphic zones.

Section 8.5

Foliated Metamorphic Rocks

One of the most distinctive features of many metamorphic rocks is foliation — a planar texture created when minerals are aligned in parallel layers or bands. Foliation develops when a rock is subjected to directed pressure that forces platy or elongated minerals (like micas) to grow perpendicular to the direction of compression. The result is a rock with a strongly layered appearance that can split along those layers.

The degree of foliation and the size of visible crystals depend on the intensity of metamorphism — that is, how much heat and pressure the rock experienced. The progression from slate through phyllite to schist and gneiss records increasing metamorphic intensity.

Slate

Slate forms from shale under low-grade metamorphic conditions. It has very fine crystal grains — invisible to the naked eye — and a smooth, flat cleavage that allows it to split into thin, even sheets. Its color is typically gray, black, green, or red, depending on its mineral content. Slate is the least altered of the foliated sequence: it has been transformed, but not dramatically. For centuries it has been used for roofing tiles, blackboards, flooring, and paving.

Phyllite

Phyllite forms under slightly higher temperatures and pressures than slate, also derived from shale. Its minerals have grown slightly larger — too small to identify individually, but large enough to give the rock a silky or shiny sheen on its foliation surfaces. This luster is caused by the growth of tiny mica and chlorite crystals aligned along the foliation planes. Phyllite represents an intermediate stage between slate and schist.

Schist

Schist forms under medium-grade metamorphic conditions. Its crystals are large enough to be seen and identified with the naked eye. It is dominated by platy minerals like micas (muscovite and biotite), and its foliation is coarse and well-developed. It often sparkles due to the reflective surfaces of its abundant mica crystals. Schist frequently contains other mineral grains — including garnets, staurolite, kyanite, or hornblende — that grew during metamorphism. These are called index minerals (see Section 8.7).

Gneiss

Gneiss (pronounced "nice") forms under high-grade metamorphic conditions — near the upper range of metamorphic temperatures before melting begins. It has large, visible crystals and a characteristic banded texture called gneissic banding, in which light-colored minerals (feldspar and quartz) alternate with dark-colored minerals (mica and hornblende) in distinct layers. Unlike slate or schist, gneiss does not split easily along its foliation because the banding is compositional rather than a simple alignment of flat minerals. Much of the ancient bedrock underlying the continents is gneiss, some of it billions of years old.

Section 8.6

Nonfoliated Metamorphic Rocks

Not all metamorphic rocks have foliation. When the protolith is composed of minerals that do not align easily under directed pressure — particularly minerals that are equidimensional (similar in all directions) rather than platy or elongated — the resulting metamorphic rock lacks foliation. These nonfoliated metamorphic rocks are massive, hard, and dense, with a uniform texture throughout. They are especially common in contact metamorphism, where heat is the dominant agent and directed pressure is weak or absent.

Marble

Marble forms when limestone or dolostone is metamorphosed. The original calcite or dolomite crystals recrystallize into larger, interlocking grains. Marble is soft enough to carve (hardness 3 on the Mohs scale), takes a high polish, and is often beautifully colored by trace minerals — swirls of pink, green, gray, or gold. Marble does not show foliation because calcite crystals are equidimensional and do not align preferentially under pressure. The Parthenon in Athens, Michelangelo's David, and the Lincoln Memorial were all built from marble.

Quartzite

Quartzite forms when sandstone is metamorphosed. The original quartz sand grains recrystallize and fuse together into an extremely hard, dense rock. Quartzite is harder than the sandstone it came from — it breaks through grains rather than around them because the grains are so tightly bonded. It lacks foliation because quartz crystals are equidimensional. When quartzite is exposed at the surface, it often forms ridges and hills that stand above the surrounding landscape because it resists erosion so effectively.

Hornfels

Hornfels is a fine-grained, hard, dense rock that forms when shale, basalt, or other fine-grained rocks are baked by contact with igneous intrusions. It forms at relatively low pressures but high temperatures. It lacks foliation because heat — not directed pressure — is the primary agent. It is dark-colored and extremely tough, typically found in the inner zones of contact aureoles closest to the igneous intrusion. The name comes from the German word for "horn rock," referring to its tough, compact texture.

Section 8.7

Metamorphic Grade

Metamorphic grade refers to the intensity of metamorphism that a rock has experienced — essentially a measure of how much heat and pressure the rock encountered. Low-grade metamorphic rocks formed at relatively low temperatures and pressures; high-grade rocks formed under extreme conditions approaching partial melting.

Grade is not directly visible in a rock the way color or grain size is. Instead, geologists infer metamorphic grade from the mineral assemblage of the rock — which minerals are present, and which are absent. Different minerals are stable only within specific ranges of temperature and pressure. By identifying the minerals in a metamorphic rock, geologists can estimate the conditions under which it formed.

Index Minerals and the Barrovian Sequence

Index minerals are specific minerals that are stable only within a limited range of temperature and pressure. British geologist George Barrow, working in the Scottish Highlands in the 1890s, first mapped zones of index minerals in metamorphic rocks. He identified a sequence of minerals — chlorite, biotite, garnet, staurolite, kyanite, and sillimanite — each appearing at progressively higher metamorphic grades. These zones are called Barrovian zones. The boundary marking the first appearance of each index mineral is called an isograd (meaning "equal grade").

Index Mineral Metamorphic Grade Approximate Temperature What It Tells Geologists
Chlorite Very low ~300°C Earliest stages of metamorphism; low burial depth
Biotite Low ~400°C Moderate burial; beginning of schist formation
Garnet Low–medium ~500°C Significant burial depth; classic schist zone
Staurolite Medium ~550°C Deeper burial; medium-grade regional metamorphism
Kyanite Medium–high ~600°C High pressure and temperature; deep burial
Sillimanite High ~650°C+ Near-melting conditions; deep in mountain roots
A Thermometer Built into the Rock

Index minerals work like a thermometer and pressure gauge that stayed inside the rock during metamorphism. When geologists find garnet but not staurolite, they can estimate the rock formed at roughly 500–550°C. This allows reconstruction of the history of a mountain range — how deep rocks were buried, how hot they got, and how tectonic forces operated — all from evidence preserved in the minerals themselves.

Section 8.8

Metamorphism and Plate Tectonics

Metamorphism does not happen randomly across Earth's surface. It is concentrated in specific tectonic settings where rocks are subjected to the right combination of heat and pressure. Plate tectonics explains where and why metamorphism occurs.

Subduction Zones: High-Pressure, Low-Temperature Metamorphism

At subduction zones, oceanic crust descends into the mantle. The descending slab carries cold oceanic rock into progressively higher-pressure environments. Because the rock descends relatively quickly (in geologic terms) and the surrounding rock is also cool at shallow depths, the subducting slab experiences high pressure before it has time to warm significantly. This produces a distinctive type of metamorphism called blueschist-facies metamorphism, named for the blue mineral glaucophane that forms under these conditions. Where blueschist rocks are preserved at the surface, they mark ancient subduction zones.

Continental Collision Zones: High-Temperature, High-Pressure Metamorphism

When two continents collide, neither plate subducts easily because both are too buoyant. Instead, the crust thickens enormously — sometimes to twice its normal thickness — as the two plates are compressed together. Rocks are buried under the growing mountain range and subjected to both high temperature and high pressure. This setting produces the full Barrovian sequence: slate, phyllite, schist, and gneiss, arranged in bands reflecting increasing depth and temperature. The cores of major mountain ranges around the world are composed of such regionally metamorphosed rocks.

Divergent Boundaries and Hydrothermal Metamorphism

At divergent plate boundaries, where the crust is being pulled apart, magma rises toward the surface. On mid-ocean ridges, seawater infiltrates the hot oceanic crust and drives chemical alteration — a process called ocean-floor metamorphism or seafloor hydrothermal metamorphism. This transforms oceanic rock significantly and influences ocean chemistry, even though it is not the high-grade metamorphism of mountain belts.

Section 8.9

Metamorphic Rocks in the Rock Cycle

Metamorphic rocks are not the end of any rock's journey — they are one stage in an ongoing cycle of transformation. The rock cycle is the set of processes by which rocks are formed, changed, broken down, and reformed over geologic time. Metamorphic rocks participate in this cycle in several important ways.

Formation

Metamorphic rocks form when igneous or sedimentary rocks (or previously metamorphosed rocks) are subjected to heat, pressure, and fluids at depth. The deeper a rock is buried and the longer it remains at high temperature and pressure, the more completely it is transformed.

Exposure at the Surface

Metamorphic rocks formed deep in the crust are eventually exposed at Earth's surface through the combined effects of uplift and erosion. As mountain ranges are built by tectonic collision, rocks from great depths are raised. As the mountains are eroded by water, wind, and ice over millions of years, the overlying material is stripped away. Eventually, the metamorphic rocks that formed at the heart of the mountain range are exposed at the surface — sometimes billions of years after they formed. The Canadian Shield, the Appalachian highlands, and the core of the Rocky Mountains all expose ancient metamorphic rocks that were once kilometers underground.

Weathering, Melting, and Re-Metamorphism

Once exposed, metamorphic rocks are subject to weathering and erosion. Physical and chemical weathering break them down into sediment — particles of quartz, mica flakes, and clay minerals that can eventually become sedimentary rock, beginning the cycle again. If metamorphic rocks are taken to still greater depths, they may eventually melt completely, becoming magma that crystallizes as igneous rock. Rocks that partially melt produce migmatite — a rock showing both the foliated texture of metamorphic rock and the crystalline structure of igneous rock, formed where melting began but was not complete.

The rock cycle should not be imagined as a simple one-way loop. A metamorphic rock can be re-metamorphosed if it is buried again. It can be partially melted, exposed, weathered, turned into sediment, and metamorphosed again. Earth's rocks have complex, multi-stage histories, and metamorphic rocks often record the most intense and dramatic episodes of those histories.

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 8.1Metamorphism transforms rocks at depth by heat, pressure, and fluids without melting. Any rock type can serve as a protolith. Metamorphism is not melting, not weathering, and not erosion — it happens underground, in the solid state.
  • Sec 8.2Three agents drive metamorphism: heat (from burial or magma), pressure (confining or directed), and chemically active fluids (which accelerate reactions and can alter rock composition via metasomatism). They usually work together.
  • Sec 8.3Regional metamorphism occurs across large areas during mountain building at convergent plate boundaries. It produces the full foliated rock sequence — slate, phyllite, schist, gneiss — reflecting increasing metamorphic intensity with depth.
  • Sec 8.4Contact metamorphism is driven by heat from nearby magmatic intrusions. It produces a contact aureole of progressively lower-grade metamorphic rock away from the intrusion. Hornfels, marble, and quartzite are typical products.
  • Sec 8.5Foliated rocks — slate, phyllite, schist, gneiss — are defined by the alignment of platy minerals under directed pressure. Grain size and foliation intensity increase with metamorphic grade from slate (lowest) to gneiss (highest).
  • Sec 8.6Nonfoliated rocks — marble, quartzite, hornfels — form from protoliths whose minerals are equidimensional and do not align under pressure, or from contact settings where directed pressure is absent. They are massive, hard, and dense.
  • Sec 8.7Metamorphic grade measures the intensity of metamorphism. Index minerals — chlorite through sillimanite in the Barrovian sequence — are stable only within specific temperature-pressure ranges and allow geologists to reconstruct metamorphic conditions from the rock record.
  • Sec 8.8Plate tectonics controls where metamorphism occurs. Subduction zones produce high-pressure, low-temperature metamorphism. Continental collisions produce high-T, high-P regional metamorphism. Intrusions drive contact metamorphism. Mid-ocean ridges produce hydrothermal alteration.
  • Sec 8.9Metamorphic rocks participate in the rock cycle. They can be uplifted and eroded, producing sediment. They can melt, producing igneous rock. They can be re-metamorphosed. Migmatite forms when partial melting occurs at the highest metamorphic grades.

Common Misconceptions

Misconception 1 — Metamorphic rocks were once melted
Metamorphic rocks formed from molten material. Metamorphic rocks are defined by the fact that they transformed without melting. If a rock melts completely, the resulting rock is igneous, not metamorphic. The key to metamorphism is that the rock remains solid throughout — atoms rearrange within solid crystals, and new minerals grow, but the rock never becomes liquid.
Misconception 2 — Foliation is the same as sedimentary layering
The layers in a metamorphic rock are just old sedimentary beds. Foliation is created by the alignment of minerals under directed pressure during metamorphism. Sedimentary layering (bedding) is created by deposition at Earth's surface. They may look superficially similar, but their origins are entirely different. A foliated metamorphic rock has no original bedding — its layers are a product of the forces it experienced deep in the crust.
Misconception 3 — Marble is always white
All marble is pure white. Pure marble, formed from very pure limestone, is white. But most marble contains trace amounts of other minerals — iron oxides, chlorite, graphite, silicates — that produce colors ranging from pink and red to green, gray, black, and gold. The familiar swirling patterns in decorative marble are caused by these trace minerals folded into the rock during metamorphism.
Misconception 4 — Metamorphism only happens at great depth
You need to be very deep underground for metamorphism to occur. Contact metamorphism can occur at relatively shallow depths wherever magma intrudes into the crust. A shallow sill or dike can bake adjacent rock at depths of just a few kilometers. Temperature — not depth alone — is the critical factor in contact metamorphism.
Misconception 5 — Metamorphic rocks are always older than other rock types
Metamorphic rocks are always the oldest rocks because of what they have been through. The age of a metamorphic rock depends on when metamorphism occurred, not on what type of rock it is. Metamorphism is actively occurring today in subduction zones and mountain belts. A metamorphic rock formed in the Himalayas in the last few million years is younger than many sedimentary rocks and even some igneous rocks.

Chapter 9: Weathering, Erosion, Soil, and Landscapes

In Chapter 8, you studied how rocks deep within Earth's crust are transformed by heat and pressure into metamorphic rocks, and how those rocks eventually make their way back to the surface. In Chapter 9, you will learn what happens to rocks once they are exposed at the surface. Weathering — the physical and chemical breakdown of rock — begins immediately.

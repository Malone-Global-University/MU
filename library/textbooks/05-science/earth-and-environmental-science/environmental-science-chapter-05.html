Chapter 5 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

You have probably held a mineral without thinking much about it. Table salt is a mineral. So is the graphite in a pencil, the quartz in a sidewalk, and the copper wire inside a phone charger. The gemstones in jewelry, the iron in a skillet, the calcium in a tooth — all of these trace back to minerals formed inside Earth or at its surface over millions of years.

Minerals are the building blocks of rocks, and rocks are the building blocks of Earth's solid surface. Before we can understand how mountains form, how volcanoes erupt, how rocks change over time, or how natural resources are extracted, we need to understand minerals: what they are, how they form, and how we tell them apart.

This chapter introduces minerals as naturally occurring substances with specific compositions and internal structures. You will learn how to identify minerals by their physical properties, which minerals are most common in Earth's crust, which minerals are economically important, and how mining and mineral use create environmental challenges that Earth scientists help to study and address.

Opening Question

A jeweler calls a stone a diamond. A geologist wants to verify the claim. Without destroying the stone, what tests could identify it? By the end of this chapter, you will know a systematic approach — using crystal structure, hardness, luster, cleavage, streak, and density — that distinguishes diamond from every other clear mineral. The same approach identifies the rock-forming minerals that make up almost every rock on Earth.

Section 5.1

What Minerals Are

Walk along a rocky beach and you will collect dozens of different materials — pebbles of different colors, textures, and weights. Look closely at a single pebble under a magnifying glass and you may see smaller, interlocking grains of different substances. Those individual substances are minerals.

A mineral is a naturally occurring, inorganic solid with a definite chemical composition and an ordered internal structure. Each part of that definition matters.

Part of the DefinitionWhat It MeansWhy It Matters
Naturally occurringForms through natural geological processes, not in factoriesSynthetic diamonds are not minerals; salt from a mine is
InorganicNot made by living organismsExcludes coal, amber, pearl, bone
SolidFixed shape and volume at surface conditionsLiquid water is not a mineral; ice is
Definite chemical compositionSpecific chemical formula or predictable rangeQuartz is always SiO₂; halite is always NaCl
Ordered internal structureAtoms in a regular, repeating 3-D pattern (crystal lattice)Controls physical properties: shape, cleavage, hardness

What Minerals Are Not

A rock is a naturally occurring solid made of one or more minerals. Granite is a rock made of quartz, feldspar, and mica. A single mineral is not a rock, though rocks made of only one mineral type do exist (marble is mostly calcite; quartzite is mostly quartz).

Glass looks like a mineral but is not one because it lacks an ordered internal structure. Atoms in glass are arranged randomly. Volcanic glass (obsidian) forms when lava cools so rapidly that atoms cannot arrange into a crystal lattice.

Organic materials — coal, wood, bone, shell — are not minerals because they contain carbon compounds produced by living organisms or come from biological processes.

How Many Minerals?

Earth scientists have identified more than 5,000 minerals. However, only about a dozen mineral families — especially the silicates — make up the vast majority of all rocks. A few hundred additional minerals are economically important. The rest are rare, found only in specific geologic environments.

Crystal Structure

All minerals have an ordered internal arrangement of atoms — a crystal structure. When minerals grow slowly in open space, they form crystals — geometric solids with flat faces, straight edges, and defined angles that reflect the internal atomic arrangement. Even irregular or dull-looking mineral samples have this internal order.

Key Principle — Structure Controls Properties

A mineral's crystal structure determines its physical properties — how it breaks, how it reflects light, how hard it is, and what shape it grows into. Two minerals can have identical chemical compositions but completely different structures and therefore very different properties.

Diamond vs. Graphite: Both are pure carbon. Diamond's atoms form a tight three-dimensional network → the hardest natural substance (Mohs 10). Graphite's atoms form flat sheets that slide past each other easily → soft enough to leave marks on paper (Mohs 1–2).

Formation Environment as Evidence

A mineral's identity reflects the conditions under which it formed. Geologists work in reverse: by identifying minerals in a rock, they reconstruct the temperatures, pressures, and chemical environments of that rock's origin. This is how Earth's history is read from rock.

Section 5.4

Color, Streak, and Luster

Color

Color is the most obvious property but often the least reliable for identification. Many minerals occur in several colors depending on trace impurities. Quartz is normally clear or white but can be purple (amethyst), yellow (citrine), pink (rose quartz), or gray (smoky quartz). Some minerals do have a consistent, diagnostic color — malachite is always green; sulfur is always yellow — but for most minerals, color alone should not be the deciding factor.

Streak

Streak is the color of a mineral's powder, produced by rubbing the mineral across an unglazed porcelain tile called a streak plate. The powder's color is more consistent than the mineral's surface color because impurities that affect surface appearance do not always affect the bulk powder.

Streak in Action — Pyrite vs. Gold

Pyrite (fool's gold) has a shiny golden surface. Its streak, however, is greenish-black — nothing like real gold, which leaves a yellow streak. The streak test instantly resolves the confusion. Very hard minerals like quartz may scratch the streak plate instead of leaving a streak, making the test less useful for hard minerals.

Luster

Luster describes how a mineral's surface reflects light — not its color, but the quality of reflection.

Hardness, Cleavage, and Fracture

Hardness and the Mohs Scale

Hardness is a mineral's resistance to being scratched. It is controlled by the strength of chemical bonds in the crystal structure. The Mohs Hardness Scale runs from 1 (softest) to 10 (hardest) using ten reference minerals.

Hardness vs. Breakage

Hardness tests involve scratching, not breaking. A mineral that chips when you try to scratch it may appear softer than it really is. Always look for a true scratch mark, not a white powder residue left by the tool. If the unknown mineral leaves powder on the glass but the glass is unmarked, the glass is the harder material.

Cleavage

Cleavage is a mineral's tendency to break along flat, smooth, parallel planes — where chemical bonds are weakest in the crystal structure. Cleavage surfaces are flat, shiny, and reflect light in sheets. Cleavage is described by (1) the number of directions and (2) the quality (perfect, good, or poor).

Cleavage PatternDescriptionMineral Example
1 direction, perfectPeels into flat sheetsMica
2 directions, 90°Breaks into rectangular blocksFeldspar
3 directions, 90°Breaks into perfect cubesHalite, galena
3 directions, not 90°Breaks into rhombohedraCalcite
4 directionsBreaks into octahedral fragmentsFluorite

Fracture

When a mineral breaks in directions that are not along cleavage planes, the break is called a fracture. Fracture surfaces are irregular or curved.

Conchoidal fracture produces curved, shell-like surfaces with smooth ripples — like the inside of a clamshell. Quartz and obsidian both break conchoidally. Quartz has no cleavage at all; it always fractures this way.

Figure 5-D — Illustrator Brief
Cleavage vs. Fracture

Two side-by-side illustrations. Left: mica splitting into flat, parallel sheets labeled "perfect one-directional cleavage" with arrows showing flat reflective planes. Right: a quartz crystal broken with curved conchoidal surfaces labeled "conchoidal fracture." Caption: cleavage produces flat, mirror-like surfaces that flash uniformly when the specimen is rotated under light; fracture surfaces are irregular and reflect light unevenly.

Cleavage vs. Crystal Faces

Crystal faces form as a crystal grows outward. Cleavage surfaces form when a crystal breaks. A natural crystal face that coincides with a cleavage direction will look similar to a cleavage surface, but the two form differently and for different reasons. Recognizing this distinction comes with practice — rotate the specimen and look for fresh, flat surfaces that reflect uniformly as a group.

Section 5.6

Density and Special Properties

Density

Density is mass per unit volume, expressed in grams per cubic centimeter (g/cm³). Most silicate minerals have densities between 2.6 and 3.3 g/cm³. Minerals containing heavy metals are much denser: galena (PbS) has a density of about 7.6 g/cm³; gold is 19.3 g/cm³.

In the field, geologists estimate density by heft — the subjective sense of heaviness relative to size. A mineral that feels unusually heavy for its size probably contains heavy elements. In the lab, density is measured precisely by water displacement.

Section 5.7

Common Rock-Forming Minerals

A small group of minerals makes up the overwhelming majority of rocks in Earth's crust. These rock-forming minerals must be identifiable quickly and confidently by any Earth scientist.

The Feldspars — Most Abundant Minerals in Earth's Crust

The feldspar group makes up roughly 60% of all crustal rocks. All feldspars contain silicon, aluminum, and oxygen; they differ in whether they also contain potassium, sodium, or calcium. Both types have a hardness of 6 and two cleavage directions at right angles.

Orthoclase (potassium feldspar) is typically pink or salmon-colored. Common in granite.

Plagioclase (sodium-calcium feldspar) is usually white or gray, and often shows fine parallel striations (lines) on cleavage surfaces caused by crystal twinning. Common in both granite and basalt.

Quartz

Quartz (SiO₂) is extremely hard (Mohs 7), has no cleavage (only conchoidal fracture), has a glassy luster, and is chemically very resistant to weathering. It can be clear, white, gray, pink, or purple. Because quartz resists weathering so well, it accumulates in sand — most beach sand is largely quartz.

The Micas

Micas are sheet silicates with perfect cleavage in one direction, allowing them to split into thin, flexible, transparent flakes. Pearly luster. Hardness 2.5–4.

Muscovite: colorless to light brown; found in many rock types. Biotite: dark brown to black; contains iron and magnesium.

Pyroxenes and Amphiboles

Both groups are dark-colored silicates found in dark igneous rocks like basalt and gabbro. They contain iron, magnesium, and calcium. The most reliable way to tell them apart is cleavage angle: pyroxenes cleave at nearly 90°; amphiboles cleave at about 60° and 120°.

Augite is the common pyroxene — dark green to black. Hornblende is the common amphibole — black with the distinctive angled cleavage.

Olivine, Calcite, and Clay Minerals

Olivine is a green silicate mineral with iron and magnesium; it is very common in Earth's upper mantle and in dark igneous rocks. No cleavage; fractures irregularly. Gem-quality olivine is called peridot.

Calcite (CaCO₃) is the main mineral in limestone and marble. Mohs 3; three cleavage directions forming a rhombohedron; fizzes in acid; often clear to white.

Clay minerals are sheet silicates that form when feldspars and other silicates weather chemically. Extremely fine-grained, earthy luster. Critical components of soil. Kaolinite, montmorillonite, and illite are common types.

Practical Identification Strategy

Work through properties in a logical sequence: (1) observe luster — metallic or nonmetallic? (2) test hardness — can a fingernail, coin, knife, or glass scratch it? (3) observe cleavage or fracture; (4) test streak; (5) observe color; (6) consider special properties; (7) consider the rock context — what kind of rock was this found in? No single property identifies most minerals; the combination narrows the possibilities.

Ores and Useful Minerals

Ore minerals contain elements — usually metals — in concentrations high enough to be worth extracting economically. A mineral deposit becomes an ore when three conditions are met: it contains a useful material; the concentration (ore grade) is high enough to make extraction practical; and the cost of extraction is less than the value of what is recovered.

Gemstone Minerals

Some minerals are prized for beauty and rarity. Diamond is pure carbon in a cubic crystal structure — the hardest natural material. Corundum forms ruby (red, from chromium impurities) and sapphire (blue, from titanium and iron). Beryl forms emerald (green, from chromium) and aquamarine (blue). Others include tourmaline, topaz, garnet, opal, and turquoise.

Rare Earth Elements — Not as Rare as They Sound

Despite their name, most rare earth elements are not especially rare in Earth's crust overall. The challenge is that they are rarely concentrated into easily mineable deposits. China currently produces the majority of the world's rare earth supply, which creates geopolitical and supply-chain concerns for countries that depend on these materials for clean energy technology.

Minerals as System Connectors

Every mineral on this list had to be found, extracted, processed, and transported. Each step has environmental consequences. Understanding minerals as Earth materials means also understanding them as parts of larger human systems — economic, technological, political, and environmental. These connections are explored further in Chapters 26 and 27.

Environmental Impacts

Acid Mine Drainage — One of the Most Persistent Mining Problems

When sulfide minerals like pyrite (FeS₂) are exposed to air and water, they react to form sulfuric acid. This acidic water dissolves heavy metals from surrounding rock and drains into nearby streams and groundwater — making water highly acidic and toxic to aquatic life. Acid drainage can continue for decades or centuries after a mine closes because the chemical reactions do not stop when mining does.

Reclamation, Remediation, and Recycling

Modern mining regulations in many countries require reclamation — reshaping the land, replacing topsoil, revegetating, and managing drainage after mining is complete. Remediation addresses contamination that has already occurred: treating acid drainage, stabilizing tailings, and removing contaminated soil.

The effectiveness of reclamation varies. Some landscapes recover substantially; others remain altered for generations. Abandoned historical mines — especially from periods before environmental regulation — remain sources of contamination worldwide.

Recycling reduces, though cannot eliminate, pressure on new mining. Recycling aluminum uses about 5% of the energy required to refine it from bauxite. Recovering rare earth elements from electronic waste is technically challenging but increasingly important. Growing populations and new technologies continue to generate new demand for primary mineral extraction even as recycling rates improve.

Resource Economics and Environmental Justice

Mineral extraction often occurs in communities that receive economic benefits (jobs, tax revenue) but also bear environmental costs (water contamination, air quality, land disturbance). These impacts are not always equally distributed across society — an issue that Earth and environmental scientists, planners, and policymakers all must consider. The growing global demand for technology minerals (lithium, cobalt, copper, rare earth elements) driven by clean energy transition creates new pressure on mineral deposits and new questions about where extraction will occur and under what conditions.

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 5.1A mineral is a naturally occurring, inorganic solid with a definite chemical composition and an ordered internal crystal structure. This definition distinguishes minerals from rocks, glasses, organic materials, and synthetic substances. More than 5,000 minerals are known; roughly a dozen families make up most of Earth's crustal rocks.
  • Sec 5.2Minerals are classified by chemical composition into major groups: silicates (most abundant, ~90% of crust), oxides, carbonates, sulfides, sulfates, halides, and native elements. Crystal structure — the 3-D arrangement of atoms — controls physical properties. Diamond and graphite are both pure carbon but have completely different structures and properties.
  • Sec 5.3Minerals form through crystallization from cooling magma, precipitation from water solutions (evaporation, hydrothermal veins), metamorphic recrystallization under heat and pressure, and surface or biological processes. The formation environment leaves a signature in the mineral's identity, allowing geologists to reconstruct Earth's history from rock evidence.
  • Sec 5.4Color is the most visible but least reliable identification property. Streak — the color of a mineral's powder on a streak plate — is more consistent. Luster describes the quality of light reflection: metallic or nonmetallic (glassy, pearly, silky, earthy, adamantine). These three properties together begin to narrow identification possibilities.
  • Sec 5.5Hardness (resistance to scratching) is measured on the Mohs scale from 1 to 10. Field tools — fingernail (~2.5), copper coin (~3), steel blade (~5.5), glass (~5.5) — allow practical testing. Cleavage is breaking along flat planes of weakness in the crystal structure; fracture is irregular breaking in other directions. Recognizing cleavage angle and number of directions is essential for mineral identification.
  • Sec 5.6Density (mass per unit volume) reflects the masses of atoms and how tightly they are packed. Most silicates fall between 2.6–3.3 g/cm³; sulfide ore minerals are much denser. Special properties — magnetism (magnetite), acid fizzing (calcite), double refraction (calcite), fluorescence (fluorite) — provide immediate identification clues for specific minerals.
  • Sec 5.7The major rock-forming minerals — feldspars (orthoclase and plagioclase), quartz, micas (muscovite and biotite), pyroxenes (augite), amphiboles (hornblende), olivine, calcite, and clay minerals — make up the vast majority of crustal rocks. Systematic identification using luster, hardness, cleavage, streak, and context allows consistent identification.
  • Sec 5.8Ore minerals contain useful materials — primarily metals — at concentrations worth extracting. Key ore minerals include hematite and magnetite (iron), chalcopyrite (copper), galena (lead), sphalerite (zinc), and bauxite (aluminum). Industrial minerals (gypsum, halite, calcite, talc, phosphate) serve essential purposes in construction, agriculture, and manufacturing.
  • Sec 5.9Technology minerals including lithium, cobalt, rare earth elements, silicon, and copper are critical for electronics, batteries, and clean energy systems. Minerals also underpin construction (sand, cement), agriculture (phosphate, potassium), and medicine (calcium, fluoride). Every mineral used connects geology to economics, technology, and environmental science.
  • Sec 5.10Mining creates significant environmental impacts: habitat destruction, acid mine drainage from sulfide mineral oxidation, tailings contamination, water use, and land subsidence. Modern regulations require reclamation; remediation addresses existing contamination. Recycling metals reduces but cannot eliminate demand for new extraction. Environmental and economic impacts of mining are not always distributed equally across communities.

Key Terms Review

acid mine drainage
Acidic water formed when sulfide minerals exposed to air and water produce sulfuric acid, which dissolves heavy metals into streams and groundwater.
cleavage
A mineral's tendency to break along flat, smooth planes of weakness in its crystal structure, described by direction count and quality.
conchoidal fracture
A curved, shell-like break pattern in minerals that lack cleavage, such as quartz and obsidian.

The regular, repeating three-dimensional arrangement of atoms inside a mineral that controls its physical properties.
crystal structure
The specific pattern in which atoms are arranged in a mineral, determining its physical properties, cleavage, hardness, and crystal form.
density
The amount of mass per unit volume, expressed in g/cm³; controlled by the masses of atoms and how tightly they are packed.
feldspar
The most abundant mineral group in Earth's crust; silicates containing Al, O, and K, Na, or Ca. Includes orthoclase and plagioclase.
fracture
The way a mineral breaks when it does not break along cleavage planes; produces irregular or curved surfaces.
hardness
A mineral's resistance to being scratched; measured on the Mohs Hardness Scale from 1 (talc) to 10 (diamond).
heft
The subjective sense of a mineral's heaviness relative to its size, used as a field estimate of density.
luster
The quality of light reflection from a mineral's surface; described as metallic or nonmetallic (glassy, pearly, silky, earthy, adamantine).
mineral
A naturally occurring, inorganic solid with a definite chemical composition and an ordered internal crystal structure.
Mohs Hardness Scale
A relative scale from 1 (softest) to 10 (hardest) used to compare mineral hardness, with talc at 1 and diamond at 10.
native element
A mineral composed of a single element in pure form — gold, silver, copper, graphite, or diamond.
ore mineral
A mineral that contains a useful material — typically a metal — in concentrations high enough to be economically extracted.
ore grade
The concentration of a valuable element in an ore deposit; determines whether extraction is economically practical.
quartz
A very common silicate mineral (SiO₂) with hardness 7, conchoidal fracture, no cleavage, and high chemical resistance to weathering.
rare earth elements
A group of 17 elements used in electric motors, screens, and lighting; rarely concentrated into easily mineable deposits despite not being scarce in Earth's crust overall.
reclamation
The process of restoring mined land as much as possible after mining operations are complete.
silicate
The largest mineral group (~90% of Earth's crust), containing silicon-oxygen tetrahedra; includes feldspar, quartz, mica, pyroxene, amphibole, and olivine.
silicate tetrahedron
The fundamental structural unit of silicate minerals: one silicon atom surrounded by four oxygen atoms arranged in a pyramid shape.
streak
The color of a mineral's powder, produced by rubbing the mineral across an unglazed porcelain streak plate; more reliable than surface color.
tailings
The crushed rock material remaining after ore minerals have been extracted; may contain toxic metals and processing chemicals.

Common Misconceptions

Misconception — "Fool's gold looks just like gold"
"Pyrite and gold look exactly the same — you can't tell them apart."

Fact: Pyrite does have a golden color and metallic luster. However, pyrite has a greenish-black streak, a hardness of 6–6.5 (much harder than gold at ~2.5), and forms cubic crystal faces. Real gold has a yellow streak, is very soft, and has a density of 19.3 g/cm³ — about four times denser than pyrite. A single streak test or hardness test eliminates the confusion immediately.

Misconception — "All minerals are rare and valuable"
"You have to be lucky to find a mineral."

Fact: The most abundant minerals — feldspar, quartz, mica, calcite — make up most of the rocks at Earth's surface. Most beach sand is largely quartz. Rarity makes some minerals valuable, but the most geologically important minerals are the ordinary, widespread ones.

Misconception — "Rocks and minerals are the same thing"
"Rocks are just big minerals."

Fact: A mineral is a specific substance with a defined composition and crystal structure. A rock is a mixture of one or more minerals — or sometimes organic material. Granite is a rock; the quartz, feldspar, and mica inside it are minerals. The distinction matters because rocks and minerals have different properties, form differently, and are studied differently.

Misconception — "Hardness means toughness"
"Diamond is the hardest mineral, so it can't be broken."

Fact: A hard mineral resists scratching; a tough material resists breaking. Diamond is the hardest mineral — nothing scratches it — but diamond can be broken or shattered by a sharp blow because its crystal structure has cleavage planes. Jade is much less hard than diamond but exceptionally tough because of its interlocking crystal microstructure.

Misconception — "Mining is always bad for the environment"
"Any mining operation is harmful and should be opposed."

Fact: Mining does cause significant environmental impacts, including habitat destruction, water contamination, and land disturbance. However, the minerals extracted by mining are essential for infrastructure, technology, medicine, and agriculture — including for clean energy systems that reduce other environmental impacts. Evaluating mining decisions requires weighing specific environmental costs against specific social and economic benefits — a judgment that depends on evidence, not blanket approval or disapproval.

Looking Ahead
Chapter 6: Igneous Rocks and Volcanic Processes

Chapter 6 studies igneous rocks — rocks formed by the cooling and solidification of molten material. You already know from Section 5.3 that minerals crystallize from magma; Chapter 6 examines how the rate of cooling, the composition of magma, and the location of cooling (underground or at the surface) determine which minerals form and what kind of rock results. You will connect igneous rocks to plate tectonics, volcanic landforms, and natural hazards. The minerals you identified in Chapter 5 — especially quartz, feldspar, mica, pyroxene, amphibole, and olivine — are the building blocks of nearly every igneous rock. Knowing them well will make igneous rock identification straightforward.

Chapter 4 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Everything you studied in Chapters 1 through 3 — Earth's four spheres, the idea of systems, the tools scientists use — depends on something more fundamental: matter and energy. Matter is the stuff that makes up Earth. Energy is what moves that matter, transforms it, and drives the processes that shape the planet. Without energy, nothing on Earth would change. Rock would never weather. Water would never evaporate. Wind would never blow. Volcanoes would go silent and oceans would freeze.

In this chapter, you will learn where Earth's energy comes from, how it moves through systems, and how matter cycles continuously through the geosphere, hydrosphere, atmosphere, and biosphere. These ideas connect directly to the rock cycle, the water cycle, the carbon cycle, weather, climate, ecosystems, and almost every environmental problem humans face. Think of this chapter as building the engine beneath everything else you will study.

Opening Question

Rain falls on a mountain, soaks into the soil, seeps through rock, feeds a spring, flows into a river, and eventually reaches the ocean — where it evaporates and falls again as rain. A carbon atom in the air is absorbed by a tree, eaten by a deer, returned to the soil by decomposers, and washed into the ocean where it may eventually be locked into limestone for millions of years. What drives all of this movement? What makes matter cycle rather than simply accumulate in one place? By the end of this chapter, you will be able to answer these questions — and explain why they matter for understanding pollution, climate, and nearly every environmental challenge society faces.

Section 4.1

Matter in Earth Systems

What Matter Is

Matter is anything that has mass and takes up space. Rocks, water, air, soil, organisms, ice, and magma are all matter. Earth is an enormously complex collection of matter — billions of tons of rock, water, gas, and living material organized into interacting systems.

Matter exists in three common states: solid, liquid, and gas. In Earth systems, you encounter all three constantly. The geosphere is mostly solid rock and partly molten rock. The hydrosphere includes liquid water, solid ice, and water vapor. The atmosphere is a mixture of gases. Living organisms contain matter in all forms.

Fourth State of Matter

A fourth state — plasma — exists in the Sun and in Earth's outer core as electrically charged particles, but it is less common in everyday Earth science at this level.

Elements and Compounds in Earth Materials

Matter is made of elements — pure substances that cannot be broken into simpler substances by ordinary chemical means. Earth's crust is dominated by just a few: oxygen, silicon, aluminum, iron, calcium, sodium, potassium, and magnesium make up more than 98 percent of the crust by mass. Oxygen alone accounts for about 46 percent.

Elements combine to form compounds — substances made of two or more elements bonded together. Water (H₂O) is a compound of hydrogen and oxygen. Quartz (SiO₂) is a compound of silicon and oxygen. Carbon dioxide (CO₂) is a compound of carbon and oxygen. Most Earth materials are compounds, not pure elements.

CompoundFormulaWhere Found in Earth Systems
WaterH₂OOceans, rivers, glaciers, groundwater, atmosphere
QuartzSiO₂Granite, sandstone, sand grains
Carbon dioxideCO₂Atmosphere, dissolved in ocean water
CalciteCaCO₃Limestone, chalk, coral shells
Iron oxide (hematite)Fe₂O₃Red desert soils, rust on iron-bearing rocks

Mixtures in Earth Systems

Most of what you encounter in Earth systems is not a pure element or compound but a mixture — two or more substances combined without bonding. Soil is a mixture of mineral particles, organic matter, water, and air. Seawater is a mixture of water and dissolved salts. The atmosphere is a mixture of nitrogen, oxygen, argon, carbon dioxide, and other gases. Magma is a complex mixture of silicate melts, dissolved gases, and mineral crystals.

Mixtures matter in Earth science because their properties — density, viscosity, reactivity, conductivity — influence how they behave in Earth systems.

The Law of Conservation of Matter

Matter is neither created nor destroyed in ordinary chemical or physical processes. It changes form, moves from one place to another, and enters different parts of systems, but the total amount of matter remains constant. This is why Earth scientists speak of cycles — the water cycle, the carbon cycle, the nitrogen cycle, the rock cycle. Matter moves continuously through these cycles, changing state and chemical form, but always persisting.

Energy in Earth Systems

Forms of Energy

Form of EnergyDescriptionEarth Science Example
Thermal (heat)Kinetic energy of moving particles in matterHot magma transferring heat to surrounding rock
RadiantEnergy carried by electromagnetic wavesSunlight traveling from the Sun to Earth's surface
ChemicalEnergy stored in bonds between atomsCarbon stored in coal; organic matter in soil

The Law of Conservation of Energy

Energy is never created or destroyed, only converted from one form to another. When sunlight hits rock, radiant energy converts to thermal energy. When water evaporates, thermal energy converts to latent energy stored in water vapor. Energy budgets for Earth systems must always balance: energy in equals energy out plus any energy stored.

Heat Transfer: Conduction, Convection, and Radiation

Three Mechanisms of Heat Transfer
How energy moves through Earth's systems
  • Conduction Transfer of thermal energy through direct contact between materials — particle to particle. Rocks are generally poor conductors, which is why Earth's interior retains heat for billions of years. When a hot rock body contacts cooler surrounding rock, conduction slowly transfers heat across the boundary.
  • Convection Transfer of thermal energy by the bulk movement of a fluid. Hot fluid is less dense and rises; cooler fluid is denser and sinks, creating convection cells. Convection drives mantle movement beneath Earth's crust, ocean currents, atmospheric circulation, and weather patterns.
  • Radiation Transfer of energy by electromagnetic waves, which can travel through empty space. The Sun transfers energy to Earth by radiation across 150 million kilometers of vacuum. Earth's surface absorbs solar radiation and re-emits it as infrared radiation, some of which is absorbed by greenhouse gases in the atmosphere.
Figure 4.1 — Illustrator Brief

Each panel labeled with name and one Earth science example.

Which Mechanism Dominates?

In solid rock: primarily conduction. In the mantle (solid but flows over geologic time): primarily convection. From the Sun to Earth across space: radiation. In the atmosphere and oceans: primarily convection, with radiation also important near the surface.

Section 4.3

Solar Energy

Earth's Primary Energy Source

The Sun is Earth's dominant external energy source. Nearly all the energy that drives Earth's surface systems — weather, climate, photosynthesis, ocean currents, evaporation, erosion — originates with sunlight. The Sun produces energy through nuclear fusion in its core, where hydrogen atoms combine to form helium under extreme pressure and temperature, releasing energy that travels outward as radiation.

The amount of solar energy reaching the top of Earth's atmosphere is approximately 1,361 watts per square meter — a value called the solar constant. This does not mean every square meter of Earth's surface receives that much; the value varies with latitude, season, time of day, and cloud cover.

What Happens to Incoming Solar Energy

Fate of Solar EnergyApproximate FractionWhat Causes It
Reflected back to space~30%Clouds, ice, snow, and light-colored surfaces (albedo)
Absorbed by the atmosphere~23%Clouds, water vapor, ozone, and dust
Absorbed by Earth's surface~47%Land, water, vegetation, and dark-colored surfaces
What Is Albedo?

Albedo is the fraction of incoming solar radiation that is reflected by a surface. Fresh snow has an albedo of 0.80–0.90 (reflects 80–90% of sunlight). Dark ocean water has an albedo of about 0.06 (absorbs 94%). Changes in albedo — from ice melting, land use change, or urbanization — alter how much energy Earth absorbs.

Uneven Heating and Its Consequences

The Sun does not heat Earth evenly. Equatorial regions receive sunlight at nearly perpendicular angles, concentrating energy over smaller areas. Polar regions receive sunlight at low angles, spreading the same amount of energy over much larger surface areas. This difference in solar concentration with latitude is the primary reason tropical regions are warm and polar regions are cold.

This uneven heating drives almost everything in Earth's fluid systems. Temperature differences create pressure differences in the atmosphere, generating wind. Temperature differences in ocean water drive surface currents. The redistribution of solar energy from equator to poles through atmospheric and oceanic circulation is one of the most important processes in Earth science — and you will encounter it repeatedly in chapters on weather, climate, and oceans.

Section 4.4

Earth's Internal Heat

A Second Energy Source

While the Sun drives surface processes, Earth has its own internal energy source: geothermal heat, energy generated within the planet itself. This heat drives volcanic activity, plate tectonics, mantle convection, and earthquakes. Without it, Earth's surface would be geologically dead — no volcanoes, no mountain building, no seafloor spreading.

Two Sources of Internal Heat

SourceDescriptionRelative Contribution Today
Primordial heatLeftover thermal energy from Earth's formation ~4.5 billion years ago. Countless small bodies collided and merged; kinetic energy of impacts converted to heat. Early Earth was largely molten.~50% of current heat flow
Radioactive decayUnstable isotopes (uranium-238, uranium-235, thorium-232, potassium-40) decay into stable forms, continuously releasing energy in Earth's mantle and crust.~50% of current heat flow

The Geothermal Gradient

Temperature increases with depth inside Earth. This rate of increase is called the geothermal gradient — on average about 25–30°C per kilometer in the crust, though it varies with location. At the base of the crust, temperatures reach several hundred degrees Celsius. At Earth's center, temperatures may exceed 5,000°C.

This heat flows outward from core toward surface, mainly through conduction in solid rock and through convection in the mantle. Where heat flow is especially concentrated — at mid-ocean ridges, volcanic hotspots, and subduction zones — it produces dramatic surface features.

Worked Example 4.4.1
Estimating temperature at depth using the geothermal gradient
  • ProblemIf the surface temperature is 15°C and the geothermal gradient is 28°C/km, estimate the temperature at 8 km depth.
  • ThinkTemperature increases with depth at a known rate. Multiply the gradient by the depth and add the surface temperature.
  • CalculateTemperature = surface temperature + (gradient × depth) Temperature = 15°C + (28°C/km × 8 km) Temperature = 15°C + 224°C Temperature = 239°C
  • CheckAt 8 km, we're still in the upper crust. A temperature of about 240°C is consistent with known values from deep mines and boreholes. Reasonable. ✓
Answer: Approximately 239°C at 8 km depth. (Actual values vary by region and rock type.)

Section 4.5

Gravity and Movement

Gravity is a force of attraction between masses. Earth's gravity pulls all objects toward Earth's center. In Earth systems, gravity does enormous work:

Role of GravityEarth System Process
Pulls water downhillDrives rivers, glaciers, and groundwater flow toward the ocean
Pulls dense materials inwardAllowed Earth to differentiate into layers — dense iron core, lighter crust on top
Pulls rock and soil downslopeDrives landslides, creep, and other mass movements
Compresses deep rockGenerates pressure that influences metamorphism and melting
Holds the atmosphere in placeKeeps gases close to Earth's surface, making the atmosphere possible
Potential and Kinetic Energy

Gravity gives objects at height gravitational potential energy. Water stored in a mountain snowpack has enormous potential energy. As it melts and flows downhill, that potential energy converts to kinetic energy — the energy of motion. Kinetic energy in flowing water does mechanical work: it erodes channels, transports sediment, and deposits material wherever flow slows. This connection between gravity, slope, and erosion shapes landscapes across Earth.

Earth's Three Fundamental Energy Drivers

Every process you study in this course is driven by one or more of three energy sources: solar energy (from the Sun), geothermal energy (from Earth's interior), and gravitational potential energy (from Earth's mass and the height of matter within it). Keep these three in mind throughout the course.

Section 4.6

Phase Changes and Water

The Three Phases of Water

Water is unique among Earth's common substances in that it exists naturally in all three states — solid (ice), liquid (water), and gas (water vapor) — across the range of temperatures found on Earth's surface. This versatility makes water central to an enormous number of Earth processes.

Energy and Phase Changes

Changing water from one phase to another requires or releases energy. The energy involved in these transitions is called latent heat — "hidden" heat because it changes the phase of water without changing its temperature. When water evaporates from the ocean surface, it absorbs a large amount of latent heat. When that water vapor condenses to form clouds and rain elsewhere, it releases that same latent heat to the atmosphere. This latent heat release is a major driver of storms, including thunderstorms and hurricanes.

Phase ChangeTransitionEnergyEarth Science Significance
MeltingSolid → liquidAbsorbedGlaciers melt, feeding rivers and sea level rise
FreezingLiquid → solidReleasedIce formation in cracks fractures rock (frost wedging)
EvaporationLiquid → gasAbsorbedOcean and lake surfaces lose water to atmosphere
CondensationGas → liquidReleasedCloud formation; latent heat fuels storm systems
SublimationSolid → gasAbsorbedSnow and ice disappear without melting in cold, dry conditions
DepositionGas → solidReleasedFrost formation on cold surfaces; some snowflake growth

Why Water's Properties Matter

Water has a high specific heat — it takes a lot of energy to warm it, and it releases a lot of energy as it cools. This is why oceans moderate coastal climates: they absorb heat in summer and release it slowly in winter. Water also has a high latent heat of evaporation — evaporating water carries enormous amounts of energy into the atmosphere, which is why evaporation cools surfaces and fuels storms when the vapor later condenses.

Common Misconception — Phase Changes and Temperature

During a phase change, temperature does not change — all the energy goes into changing the arrangement of molecules (the phase), not into increasing their average kinetic energy (temperature). A pot of boiling water stays at 100°C even as it continues to absorb heat from the stove. This is what makes latent heat "latent" — it is hidden from a thermometer.

Section 4.7

Chemical Weathering and Reactions

Chemical Reactions in Earth Systems

Chemical reactions change the composition of matter by breaking and forming chemical bonds. Earth systems depend on chemical reactions continuously:

ReactionWhat HappensEarth System Role
Chemical weatheringMinerals in rock are broken down and alteredReleases nutrients, forms soil, shapes landscapes
PhotosynthesisSolar energy + CO₂ + H₂O → organic compounds + O₂Builds living matter, removes CO₂ from atmosphere
Respiration and decayOrganic compounds → CO₂ + H₂O + energyReturns carbon and nutrients to environment
Carbonate formationDissolved calcium + bicarbonate → calcium carbonateRemoves CO₂ from water; forms limestone and shells
OxidationIron minerals + oxygen → iron oxidesRusts minerals; creates red/orange colors in rock and soil
CarbonationCO₂ + H₂O → H₂CO₃ → reacts with silicate mineralsMost important chemical weathering process on Earth

Chemical Weathering in Detail

When rain falls through the atmosphere, it dissolves carbon dioxide to form carbonic acid (H₂CO₃) — a weak acid but effective over geologic time. This slightly acidic water reacts with silicate minerals in rock, breaking them down into clay minerals, dissolved ions, and silica. This process is called carbonation and is one of the most important chemical weathering reactions on Earth. The same process that slowly dissolves granite also carves limestone caves when acidic groundwater dissolves calcium carbonate.

Oxidation is another common reaction: iron-bearing minerals react with oxygen in air or water, forming iron oxides. The reddish and orange colors of many desert rocks and soils come from oxidized iron — essentially, rust on rock.

Factors Controlling Weathering Rate

FactorEffect on Chemical Weathering RateReason
TemperatureWarmer = fasterHigher temperatures speed up chemical reaction rates
Water availabilityMore water = fasterWater is both a reactant and a transport medium for dissolved ions
Rock compositionVaries by mineralSome minerals (olivine, feldspar) weather much faster than others (quartz)
Surface areaMore broken = fasterSmaller fragments expose more surface area to water and air
Biological activityMore life = fasterPlant roots, fungi, and bacteria produce acids that accelerate weathering
Why Tropical Soils Are Deep

Tropical rainforests near the equator have deep, intensely weathered soils because they combine high temperature, abundant rainfall, and dense biological activity — the three factors that maximize chemical weathering rates. Cold, dry polar regions weather very slowly by comparison.

Section 4.8

The Water, Carbon, Nitrogen, and Rock Cycles

Because matter is conserved — neither created nor destroyed — it must cycle. Elements and compounds that are used by one part of a system must eventually return to be used again. Earth's major biogeochemical cycles describe the paths that matter takes as it moves through Earth's spheres. All these cycles are driven by energy: solar energy, geothermal heat, gravity, and chemical energy. Without energy inputs, the cycles would stop.

The Water Cycle

The water cycle (also called the hydrological cycle) describes the continuous movement of water through the atmosphere, land surface, oceans, glaciers, and groundwater. It connects all four of Earth's spheres, delivers freshwater to land, shapes landscapes, transports nutrients and sediment, regulates temperature, and sustains life.

ProcessWhat HappensEnergy Driver
EvaporationWater molecules leave ocean, lake, and moist surfaces as vaporSolar energy
TranspirationPlants release water vapor through leaves (combined with evaporation = evapotranspiration)Solar energy
CondensationWater vapor cools and forms liquid droplets — clouds and fogLatent heat release
PrecipitationWater falls as rain, snow, sleet, or hailGravity
RunoffWater flows over the surface into streams and rivers toward the oceanGravity
InfiltrationWater soaks into the ground and may become groundwaterGravity
Glacier/snowpack storageWater is stored as ice for years to thousands of years before meltingSolar energy (for melting)

The Carbon Cycle

Carbon is the backbone of life. It is also the key element in the greenhouse gases — carbon dioxide (CO₂) and methane (CH₄) — that regulate Earth's temperature. The carbon cycle describes how carbon moves through the atmosphere, oceans, land, organisms, and rocks. Understanding the carbon cycle is essential to understanding climate change.

Carbon ReservoirHow Carbon Is StoredSize (relative)
AtmosphereCO₂ and CH₄ gasSmall, but critically important
OceansDissolved CO₂ and bicarbonate ionsVery large
Living organismsCarbon in organic moleculesModerate
Soil and dead organic matterDecomposing plant and animal materialLarge
Fossil fuelsCoal, oil, and natural gas (carbon stored over millions of years)Large
Carbonate rocksLimestone and chalk (CaCO₃)Largest long-term reservoir
Fast vs. Slow Carbon Cycling

The carbon cycle has fast components — photosynthesis, respiration, ocean exchange — operating over years to decades. It also has slow components — rock weathering, volcanic outgassing, burial — operating over millions of years. Human burning of fossil fuels is moving carbon from the slow cycle into the fast cycle at an unprecedented rate, which is the fundamental mechanism behind human-caused climate change. You will study this in depth in Chapter 21.

The Nitrogen Cycle

Nitrogen makes up about 78 percent of the atmosphere — but in its atmospheric form (N₂), most organisms cannot use it. The nitrogen cycle describes how nitrogen is converted into forms that living things can use and then returned to the atmosphere.

ProcessWhat HappensWho or What Does It
Nitrogen fixationN₂ is converted to ammonia or nitrate — forms plants can absorbBacteria (and lightning; industrial fertilizers)
AssimilationPlants take up nitrogen compounds; build proteins and DNAPlants and other producers
ConsumptionAnimals eat plants, obtaining nitrogenAnimals and other consumers
DecompositionDead organisms' nitrogen compounds are broken downBacteria and fungi
NitrificationAmmonia is converted to nitrite, then nitrateSoil bacteria
DenitrificationNitrates are converted back to N₂, returned to atmosphereSoil bacteria in low-oxygen conditions
Human Disruption of the Nitrogen Cycle

Human addition of nitrogen fertilizers to agricultural land has significantly altered the natural nitrogen cycle. Excess nitrogen causes water pollution (algal blooms and dead zones in coastal waters), soil acidification, and emissions of nitrous oxide (N₂O), a potent greenhouse gas about 265 times more effective than CO₂ at trapping heat over a century.

The Rock Cycle

The rock cycle describes how rocks transform from one type to another — igneous to sedimentary to metamorphic and back — driven by Earth's internal heat, gravity, and surface processes. The rock cycle operates over millions to hundreds of millions of years and connects to the water cycle (water drives weathering and erosion) and the carbon cycle (carbon is stored in limestone and released by volcanic outgassing).

The Rock Cycle — Key Pathways
How rocks transform from one type to another
  • Path 1Magma cools (intrusive or extrusive) → igneous rock forms. Energy driver: geothermal heat from Earth's interior.
  • Path 2Igneous rock is exposed at surface, weathers, and erodes → sediment forms. Energy drivers: solar energy drives weathering; gravity transports sediment.
  • Path 3Sediment is deposited, compacted, and cemented → sedimentary rock forms. Energy driver: gravity drives compaction; groundwater carries cementing minerals.
  • Path 4Rocks are buried, heated, and pressurized → metamorphic rock forms. Energy driver: geothermal heat and pressure from overlying rock (gravity).
  • Path 5Metamorphic or other rocks melt → magma forms again. Energy driver: geothermal heat, often at plate boundaries.

Section 4.9

Residence Time and Flow Through Systems

Residence time is the average length of time that a molecule or atom spends in a particular reservoir before moving on to another. Different parts of Earth's cycles hold matter for very different amounts of time — ranging from days to billions of years.

ReservoirApproximate Residence TimeImplication
Water in the atmosphere~9–10 daysFast response to changes in evaporation
Water in a riverDays to weeksRivers respond quickly to rainfall and drought
Water in shallow groundwaterYears to decadesContamination takes years to reach wells
Water in deep groundwaterHundreds to thousands of yearsDeep aquifer contamination is very hard to reverse
Water in glaciersDecades to thousands of yearsGlacier loss takes centuries to reverse naturally
Carbon in the atmosphereYears to decadesCO₂ persists long after emissions stop
Carbon in deep oceanHundreds to thousands of yearsSlow ocean uptake limits how fast CO₂ leaves atmosphere
Carbon in limestoneMillions to hundreds of millions of yearsOnce buried in rock, carbon is removed from surface cycle
Nitrogen in soilMonths to yearsSoil nitrogen is accessible to plants on growing-season timescales
Why Residence Time Matters

Slow-cycling reservoirs are not easily corrected. Carbon released from fossil fuels into the atmosphere will remain for centuries, even after emissions stop, because ocean and rock uptake is slow. Fast-cycling reservoirs respond quickly. Atmospheric water vapor cycles every ten days, so changes in evaporation rates affect atmospheric moisture on short timescales. Pollution persists based on residence time. A contaminant in deep groundwater may remain for centuries even after the source is removed.

Section 4.10

Matter and Energy in Environmental Problems

Almost every significant environmental problem can be understood through the lens of matter cycles and energy flows. The matter and energy framework from this chapter is not abstract — it connects directly to the most pressing challenges people and societies face.

Case Study 1
Air Pollution — Matter Entering a Fast-Cycling Reservoir
  • ProblemAir pollutants enter the atmosphere from combustion engines, industrial processes, wildfires, and agriculture.
  • CycleThe atmosphere is a fast-cycling reservoir. Residence times range from hours (coarse particles, removed by rain) to years (fine aerosols, transported thousands of kilometers).
  • ImplicationUnderstanding where pollutants come from, how they move (conduction? convection? wind transport?), and where they go (deposition, chemical reactions) is fundamental to reducing air pollution. Chapter 27 covers this in depth.
Case Study 2
Groundwater Contamination — A Slow-Cycling Reservoir
  • ProblemToxic substances from industrial leaks, agricultural runoff, or improper waste disposal enter an aquifer.
  • CycleDeep groundwater residence time: decades to thousands of years. The contamination persists in the slow-cycling reservoir long after the source is removed.
  • ImplicationRestoring contaminated aquifers is extremely difficult and expensive. This is a direct consequence of residence time: matter enters faster than it can leave the reservoir naturally.
Case Study 3
Carbon Dioxide and Climate — Moving Matter Between Cycles
  • ProblemBurning fossil fuels moves carbon from the long-term geologic reservoir (coal, oil, gas — residence time: millions of years) into the atmosphere (residence time: years to decades).
  • CycleThe slow carbon cycle — ocean uptake and rock weathering — cannot absorb CO₂ as fast as it is being emitted, so it accumulates in the atmosphere, where it acts as a greenhouse gas.
  • ImplicationThe climate responds gradually because the ocean is a large but slow reservoir. Full stabilization requires sustained reduction in emissions, not just a brief pause. Chapter 21 covers this in full.
Case Study 4
Nutrient Pollution and Dead Zones — A Disrupted Cycle
  • ProblemExcess nitrogen from fertilizers enters the fast cycling of soil and water, moving through runoff into rivers and eventually into coastal oceans.
  • CycleHigh nitrogen loads feed explosive algal growth. When algae die and decompose, microbial respiration depletes dissolved oxygen — creating dead zones where fish and other organisms cannot survive.
  • ImplicationThe fundamental problem is a nutrient cycle accelerated and overloaded by human inputs. The Gulf of Mexico dead zone, fed by Mississippi River agricultural runoff, covers thousands of square kilometers annually. Chapter 27 covers water pollution in depth.
The Connection Forward

Every chapter in this textbook connects back to matter cycles and energy flows. Rocks form and transform because of energy from magma and the Sun. Water shapes landscapes because gravity pulls it downhill and solar energy evaporates it back up. Weather forms because solar energy heats the surface unevenly. Climate changes because the carbon cycle has been altered. Ecosystems function because energy moves from sunlight through producers to consumers and decomposers. Environmental decisions — about energy use, land management, pollution control, and climate — all ultimately involve questions about matter cycles and energy flows.

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 4.1Matter — anything with mass and volume — exists in solid, liquid, and gas phases and is found as elements, compounds, and mixtures in Earth's systems. The law of conservation of matter explains why matter cycles rather than accumulates: it is never created or destroyed, only transformed and moved.
  • Sec 4.2Energy takes several forms in Earth systems — thermal, radiant, chemical, mechanical, and gravitational potential. Energy is also conserved. Heat moves through Earth systems by conduction (particle to particle), convection (bulk fluid movement), and radiation (electromagnetic waves through space).
  • Sec 4.3The Sun is Earth's dominant external energy source. About 30% of incoming solar energy is reflected (albedo), 23% is absorbed by the atmosphere, and 47% is absorbed by Earth's surface. Uneven heating — concentrated near the equator, spread out near the poles — drives wind, ocean currents, and weather.
  • Sec 4.4Earth's internal heat comes from two sources: primordial heat left from formation 4.5 billion years ago, and ongoing radioactive decay in the mantle and crust. The geothermal gradient averages ~25–30°C/km in the crust. Geothermal heat drives plate tectonics, volcanism, and mantle convection.
  • Sec 4.5Gravity is Earth's third fundamental energy driver. It pulls water downhill, drives mass movements, compresses rocks under pressure, and organized Earth's interior into layers by density. Gravitational potential energy in elevated water converts to kinetic energy as water flows downhill, driving erosion and sediment transport.
  • Sec 4.6Water exists in all three phases on Earth's surface. Phase changes absorb or release latent heat without changing temperature. Evaporation absorbs enormous energy; condensation releases it — fueling storms. Water's high specific heat and latent heat values make it a key regulator of Earth's climate and energy transfer.
  • Sec 4.7Chemical reactions — especially carbonation and oxidation — transform minerals in rock at Earth's surface. Chemical weathering rates increase with temperature, water, biological activity, and surface area. These reactions supply nutrients, form soils, and connect the geosphere to the biosphere and water systems.
  • Sec 4.8The water cycle, carbon cycle, nitrogen cycle, and rock cycle are Earth's major biogeochemical cycles. Each is driven by energy and operates across a range of timescales. The carbon cycle has both fast components (photosynthesis, ocean exchange) and slow components (rock weathering, burial). Human activities alter these cycles in significant ways.
  • Sec 4.9Residence time is the average time a substance spends in a reservoir. Fast reservoirs (atmospheric water: ~10 days) respond quickly to change; slow reservoirs (deep groundwater: thousands of years; limestone carbon: millions of years) persist long after conditions change. Residence time determines how quickly environmental systems respond and how long pollution lingers.
  • Sec 4.10Air pollution, groundwater contamination, climate change, and nutrient pollution are all analyzable through matter cycling and residence time. Burning fossil fuels moves carbon from slow geologic storage to the fast atmospheric cycle. Groundwater contamination persists because deep aquifers are slow-cycling reservoirs. These connections reappear throughout this course.

Key Terms

albedo
The fraction of incoming solar radiation reflected by a surface back into space without being absorbed. Snow and ice have high albedo; dark ocean water has low albedo.
biogeochemical cycle
A cycle in which matter moves through living (biological) and nonliving (geological and chemical) parts of Earth systems. Examples: water cycle, carbon cycle, nitrogen cycle, rock cycle.
carbon cycle
The biogeochemical cycle tracking the movement of carbon through the atmosphere, oceans, land, organisms, soil, and rocks. Has both fast and slow components.
chemical weathering
The breakdown of rock through chemical reactions that change its mineral composition. Key processes include carbonation and oxidation.
compound
A substance made of two or more elements bonded together in fixed proportions. Water (H₂O) and quartz (SiO₂) are examples found in Earth systems.
condensation
The phase change from gas to liquid — water vapor becoming liquid water. Releases latent heat to the surrounding atmosphere, fueling weather systems.
conduction
The transfer of thermal energy through direct contact between materials, particle to particle. Dominant heat transfer mechanism in solid rock.
convection
The transfer of thermal energy by the bulk movement of a fluid. Warm fluid rises, cool fluid sinks, creating convection cells. Drives mantle movement, ocean currents, and atmospheric circulation.
element
A pure substance that cannot be broken into simpler substances by ordinary chemical means. Oxygen, silicon, and iron are examples dominant in Earth's crust.
evaporation
The phase change from liquid to gas at the surface of a liquid. Absorbs latent heat from the surface, cooling it, and transfers energy into the atmosphere.
geothermal gradient
The rate at which temperature increases with depth inside Earth, averaging about 25–30°C per kilometer in the crust.
geothermal heat
Heat energy generated within Earth from primordial heat (leftover from formation) and ongoing radioactive decay. Drives plate tectonics, volcanism, and mantle convection.
latent heat
Energy absorbed or released during a phase change, without a change in temperature. Evaporation absorbs latent heat; condensation releases it.
matter
Anything that has mass and takes up space. Rocks, water, air, soil, organisms, and magma are all forms of matter in Earth systems.
nitrogen cycle
The biogeochemical cycle tracking nitrogen through the atmosphere, soil, water, and living organisms. Key processes include nitrogen fixation, assimilation, decomposition, and denitrification.
phase change
A change in the physical state of matter (solid, liquid, or gas) caused by the absorption or release of energy. The six phase changes are melting, freezing, evaporation, condensation, sublimation, and deposition.
radiation
The transfer of energy by electromagnetic waves, which can travel through a vacuum. Solar energy reaches Earth by radiation across 150 million km of space.
radioactive decay
The spontaneous transformation of an unstable atomic nucleus into a more stable form, releasing energy. Uranium, thorium, and potassium isotopes heat Earth's interior continuously through this process.
residence time
The average length of time a substance spends in a particular reservoir within a cycle. Ranges from days (atmospheric water vapor) to millions of years (carbon in limestone).
rock cycle
The continuous set of processes by which rocks are formed, transformed, and recycled through the geosphere. Driven by internal heat, gravity, and surface processes.
solar constant
The average amount of solar energy arriving at the top of Earth's atmosphere per unit area per unit time — approximately 1,361 watts per square meter.
specific heat
The amount of energy required to raise the temperature of one gram of a substance by one degree Celsius. Water's high specific heat moderates coastal climates.
water cycle
The continuous movement of water through the atmosphere, land surface, oceans, glaciers, and groundwater, driven by solar energy and gravity. Also called the hydrological cycle.

Real-World Applications

  1. Geothermal Energy: Iceland generates a large fraction of its electricity and heat from geothermal energy. Using what you know about Earth's internal heat and the geothermal gradient, explain why some regions are better suited for geothermal energy than others. What Earth system processes concentrate geothermal heat near the surface in certain places?
  2. Agricultural Nitrogen: Farmers apply synthetic nitrogen fertilizers to crops to boost yields. After application, this nitrogen can follow several pathways — into plants, into soil bacteria, into groundwater, or into rivers and coastal waters. Use the nitrogen cycle to trace two potential pathways for fertilizer nitrogen and explain the environmental consequences of each.
  3. Urban Albedo: Cities are often several degrees warmer than surrounding rural areas — a phenomenon called the "urban heat island effect." Using what you know about albedo and energy absorption, explain how the replacement of vegetation and soil with asphalt and concrete contributes to this effect. What changes to urban surfaces could reduce it?
  4. Glacial Meltwater: As mountain glaciers melt due to warming temperatures, rivers fed by glacial meltwater initially carry more water than usual. Eventually, if the glacier disappears, river flow during summer droughts decreases. Use the water cycle and the concept of residence time to explain this sequence of events.
  5. Cave Formation: Limestone caves form when acidic groundwater (carbonated water, containing carbonic acid) dissolves limestone (CaCO₃). (a) Name the phase of the water cycle in which this water entered the ground. (b) Name the chemical weathering reaction that is dissolving the rock. (c) Explain what happens to the dissolved calcium and carbonate ions after they move through the groundwater system.

Error Analysis

Each statement below contains an error. Identify the error and write a correct version.

Statement 1
"Solar energy is the source of Earth's volcanic activity and plate tectonic movement."

What is the error? Which energy source actually drives these processes? Write a corrected statement.

Statement 2
"When water evaporates from the ocean surface, it releases latent heat into the atmosphere, warming the air."

Is this the correct direction of latent heat transfer during evaporation? Explain what actually happens and when the latent heat is released to the atmosphere.

Statement 3
"The carbon cycle is a fast cycle — carbon moves from fossil fuels to the atmosphere and back within a few years."

Identify the error. What is the distinction between the fast and slow carbon cycles? How long does carbon actually remain in the atmosphere after being released?

Statement 4
"Convection in Earth's mantle works because solid rock rises when it heats up and sinks when it cools."

The statement says "solid rock" — is this accurate? How do Earth scientists describe mantle behavior over geologic timescales? Write a corrected statement.

Statement 5
"Earth's albedo refers to the amount of solar energy absorbed by Earth's surface."

What does albedo actually measure? A surface with high albedo absorbs more or less energy? Write a precise corrected definition.

Chapter 5: Minerals and Earth Materials

Chapter 4 introduced the matter and energy framework underlying all of Earth science. You learned that matter cycles continuously through Earth's spheres and that energy from the Sun, Earth's interior, and gravity drives every process you will study in this course. Chapter 5 takes the next step: examining Earth's most fundamental solid materials — minerals. Minerals are the natural compounds that make up rocks, and rocks are the foundation of the geosphere. To understand the rock cycle you encountered in this chapter, you need to understand the building blocks — what minerals are, how they form, how they are identified, and why they matter for geology, technology, agriculture, and the environment. As you read Chapter 5, you will see the matter and energy framework from Chapter 4 at work: minerals form through chemical processes driven by geothermal heat and surface conditions, and their properties reflect the elements and bonding structures they contain.

Chapter 13 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

When people picture the solar system, they tend to picture the eight planets arranged in neat orbits around the Sun. But this picture leaves out most of what is actually there. The solar system contains hundreds of moons, millions of asteroids, billions of comets, and an enormous population of icy and rocky objects extending far beyond Neptune. These smaller bodies are not afterthoughts. They are, in many ways, the oldest and most scientifically valuable objects in the solar system.

Small bodies preserve conditions that the planets have long since destroyed. A chunk of rock from the asteroid belt may contain material that formed within the first few million years of the solar system's history — material that has never melted, never been reshaped by plate tectonics, never been altered by weather. Comets carry ices that may have formed in the cold outer reaches of the early solar system and have remained largely unchanged ever since. Meteorites recovered from Earth's surface have told us the age of the solar system with precision that no other evidence could match.

Beyond their scientific value, small bodies also affect planets in direct and sometimes dramatic ways. Impacts have reshaped every solid surface in the solar system. An impact likely contributed to the extinction of the non-avian dinosaurs 66 million years ago. Impacts may have delivered water and organic molecules to the early Earth.

Opening Question

If the eight planets formed from the same disk of gas and dust 4.6 billion years ago, why do small bodies like asteroids and comets still exist? Why weren't they swept up into planets? What do they tell us about how and where planets form — and about conditions in the early solar system that no planet can preserve?

Section 13.1

Why Small Bodies Matter

The solar system formed from a rotating disk of gas and dust about 4.6 billion years ago. As material in this disk clumped together under gravity, most of it was eventually swept up into the planets. But not all of it. An enormous amount of material — rocks, ices, dust, and gas — was left over after the planets formed. This leftover material is what we call small bodies.

Small bodies matter for at least four reasons. They preserve the past. The giant and terrestrial planets have experienced billions of years of change — melting, differentiation, volcanism, erosion, and impacts. Small bodies, especially asteroids and comets in the outer solar system, have experienced far less change. They carry chemical and physical records of the early solar system that no planet can provide.

They tell us how planets formed. The process by which small particles stuck together to build planets — called accretion — is not directly observable today. But asteroids and comets are survivors of that process. By studying them, scientists gain evidence about the building blocks that existed, how they were distributed, and what conditions were like during planet formation.

They affect planets. Impacts by asteroids and comets have left their mark on every solid body in the solar system. Some impacts have been catastrophic. Others may have delivered material important for life. They are destinations. Spacecraft have visited asteroids and comets, and in some cases returned samples to Earth. These missions have transformed what looked like featureless points of light into ancient, battered, complex worlds.

Section 13.2

Moons as Worlds

A moon, or natural satellite, is any object in orbit around a planet or other body that is not the Sun. Earth has one moon. Mars has two small moons. Jupiter has at least 95 known moons. Saturn has even more. The giant planets, with their strong gravity, have captured or accumulated large numbers of natural satellites over the history of the solar system.

The word "moon" might suggest a small, barren, cratered rock. But the solar system's moons are extraordinarily diverse. Some are among the most geologically and astrobiologically interesting objects in the solar system.

Why Europa Matters

Europa's subsurface ocean stays liquid not because it is close to the Sun, but because tidal heating from Jupiter continuously warms its interior. This means liquid water can exist far from a star's warmth. This insight has expanded scientists' understanding of where habitable environments might exist — in our solar system and beyond.

Saturn's Major Moons

Titan is the only moon in the solar system with a dense atmosphere — mostly nitrogen, with methane playing a role that water plays on Earth. Titan has methane rain, rivers, and lakes. The Cassini spacecraft and the Huygens probe, which landed on Titan in 2005, revealed a world with ongoing atmospheric and geological processes.

Enceladus erupts plumes of water vapor and ice particles from a liquid water ocean beneath its icy crust. The Cassini spacecraft flew through these plumes and detected water, salts, organic molecules, and molecular hydrogen — chemical signatures consistent with reactions that support life in hydrothermal vent environments on Earth. Enceladus is one of the most compelling astrobiology targets in the solar system.

Triton and Earth's Moon

Neptune's largest moon, Triton, orbits in the retrograde direction — opposite to Neptune's rotation. This strongly suggests Triton was captured, probably from the Kuiper Belt. It has geysers that erupt nitrogen gas, and it is slowly spiraling inward.

Earth's Moon is unusually large relative to Earth — the Moon-to-planet mass ratio is much larger than for any other planet-moon pair in the inner solar system. The most widely accepted explanation is the giant impact hypothesis: early in Earth's history, a Mars-sized body collided with Earth, and debris from this collision eventually coalesced into the Moon. Lunar rocks returned by the Apollo missions support this model.

Tidal Heating

Tidal heating arises because gravity does not pull equally on every part of a moon — the side closer to the planet is pulled more strongly. This unequal pull stretches the moon slightly. If the moon's orbit is not perfectly circular, the stretching changes as the moon moves. Repeated flexing and un-flexing generates heat through friction — just as repeatedly bending a piece of metal makes it warm. For Io, this process is so intense that the interior is kept partially molten, driving relentless volcanism. For Europa and Enceladus, it is enough to maintain subsurface liquid water oceans.

Key Idea — Tidal Heating

Tidal heating = friction from repeated gravitational flexing of a moon's interior. It does not require proximity to a star. It makes liquid water possible far from the Sun — expanding what astronomers consider the potential habitable zone in any planetary system.

Section 13.3

Ring Systems

All four giant planets — Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune — have ring systems. Saturn's rings are the most spectacular: wide, bright, and easily visible through a small telescope. The rings of the other giant planets are much fainter and were not discovered until spacecraft or powerful ground-based telescopes could detect them.

A planetary ring is a disk of particles orbiting a planet within its equatorial plane. Ring particles range from tiny dust grains to chunks of rock and ice several meters across. The particles are not solid — a ring is a vast swarm of individual pieces, each in its own orbit around the planet.

Saturn's Rings

Saturn's rings extend from roughly 7,000 kilometers above Saturn's cloudtops to about 80,000 kilometers, but they are remarkably thin — most of the main rings are only tens to hundreds of meters thick. The total mass of all Saturn's rings is much less than you might expect: roughly equivalent to a small moon only a few hundred kilometers across. The rings are composed mostly of water ice, with some rocky material.

The most prominent gap, the Cassini Division, is maintained by gravitational interactions with Saturn's moon Mimas — a process called orbital resonance. When a ring particle's orbital period is a simple fraction of a moon's orbital period, repeated gravitational nudges push particles out of that region, creating a gap.

The Roche Limit

The Roche limit is the distance from a planet within which tidal forces are stronger than the self-gravity that would hold a solid body together. Inside the Roche limit, a moon or large body cannot hold itself together — it would be torn apart into ring particles. Most planetary rings lie within or near the Roche limit of their respective planets.

Are Saturn's Rings Ancient?

Saturn's rings may be relatively young on astronomical timescales — perhaps only a few hundred million years old. One hypothesis is that a moon or comet came too close and was torn apart by tidal forces. Another is that the rings are primordial but continuously refreshed. The question of the rings' age and origin is still actively investigated.

Section 13.4

Asteroids and the Asteroid Belt

Asteroids are rocky or metallic bodies that orbit the Sun, mostly smaller than planets and moons — sometimes called minor planets. Most asteroids in the inner solar system are found in the asteroid belt, a region between the orbits of Mars and Jupiter roughly 2.2 to 3.2 astronomical units from the Sun.

The asteroid belt is not densely packed. The total mass of all its asteroids is less than 4% of the Moon's mass. If you were flying through it, you could travel without ever coming close to a large asteroid — the belt is vast and the asteroids are spread across an enormous volume of space.

Why Is There an Asteroid Belt?

The asteroid belt is not a failed planet. Jupiter's strong gravitational influence set the belt's material into orbits with enough variation in speed and direction that collisions between objects resulted in fragmentation rather than accretion. Rather than growing larger, objects in the belt have been grinding each other down over billions of years. Many present-day asteroids are fragments of larger bodies that broke apart.

Near-Earth Asteroids and Planetary Defense

Gravitational interactions, collisions, and the slow drift caused by the sunlight-driven Yarkovsky effect can push asteroids into orbits that cross the inner solar system. Near-Earth asteroids (NEAs) are those whose orbits bring them close to Earth's orbit. Tracking NEAs to assess potential impact hazards is an active area of planetary science. In 2022, NASA's DART (Double Asteroid Redirection Test) mission demonstrated that a spacecraft could alter the orbit of a small asteroid by deliberately colliding with it — a proof of concept for planetary defense.

Section 13.5

Meteoroids, Meteors, and Meteorites

Three Terms — Three Precise Meanings

Meteoroid — A small particle or chunk of rock or metal in space. Ranges from sub-millimeter dust to objects meters across.

Meteor — The streak of light produced when a meteoroid enters Earth's atmosphere and heats up through friction. Commonly called a "shooting star."

Meteorite — A meteoroid that survives its passage through the atmosphere and lands on Earth's surface.

Common Confusion

These three terms describe the same object in three different situations — in space, during atmospheric entry, and on the ground. A student who calls a rock on Earth's surface a "meteor" is using the wrong term. The object becomes a meteorite only after it lands.

Meteor Showers

Most meteors are caused by tiny particles shed by comets along their orbital paths. When Earth passes through this particle trail every year, many particles enter the atmosphere simultaneously, creating a meteor shower. The meteors in a shower all appear to radiate from the same point — the radiant — an effect of perspective. Meteor showers are named for the constellation where the radiant lies: the Perseids (mid-August) appear to come from Perseus; the Leonids (November) from Leo.

Carbonaceous Chondrites and the Origin of Life

Carbonaceous chondrites contain organic molecules, including amino acids. Their existence in the early solar system raises the question of whether impacts by these meteorites could have contributed to the chemical inventory that eventually gave rise to life on Earth — a question that connects planetary science to astrobiology.

Section 13.6

Comets

Comets are small bodies composed of ice, dust, and rocky material. When a comet passes close enough to the Sun, solar heat causes the ice to sublimate — transform directly from solid to gas — releasing dust and gas that form the comet's characteristic glowing atmosphere and tails.

A comet in its dormant state, far from the Sun, is a nucleus: a solid, irregularly shaped body typically a few kilometers to tens of kilometers across. Cometary nuclei are dark and covered with organic compounds and dust. The popular nickname "dirty snowball" captures the idea of an icy-dusty mix, though spacecraft images have shown them to be more complex. Some scientists now prefer "snowy dirtball" because rocky and dusty material appears to dominate.

Why Two Tails Point in Different Directions

The dust tail is pushed by radiation pressure — the gentle push of sunlight on dust particles. Because this force is weak, it curves and lags. The ion tail is swept by the solar wind — a stream of charged particles flowing outward from the Sun at high speed. The solar wind always blows radially outward, so the ion tail always points directly away from the Sun, regardless of the comet's direction of travel.

Short-Period and Long-Period Comets

Short-period comets have orbital periods under 200 years and originate in the Kuiper Belt. Long-period comets have periods of more than 200 years — sometimes millions of years — and originate in the Oort Cloud. Comet Halley, with its ~75–76 year period, is the most famous short-period comet. The European Space Agency's Rosetta mission orbited Comet 67P/Churyumov-Gerasimenko for two years and deployed the Philae lander onto its surface in 2014 — the first spacecraft to land on a comet's nucleus.

Section 13.7

Dwarf Planets

A dwarf planet is a body that orbits the Sun and has enough mass and gravity to pull itself into a roughly spherical shape, but has not cleared other objects from its orbital neighborhood. This last criterion distinguishes planets from dwarf planets.

The concept was formalized by the International Astronomical Union (IAU) in 2006, partly in response to the discovery of Eris, a trans-Neptunian object comparable in size to Pluto. The IAU created the dwarf planet category — simultaneously reclassifying Pluto.

Why Pluto Was Reclassified

Pluto was not demoted because scientists discovered it was small — its small size was known long before 2006. It was reclassified because it is one of many similar objects in the Kuiper Belt. Unlike the eight planets, Pluto shares its orbital neighborhood with thousands of comparable objects and has not gravitationally dominated its zone. This reflects a real physical distinction about how Pluto relates to its surroundings.

Section 13.8

The Kuiper Belt

The Kuiper Belt is a broad region of the outer solar system extending from the orbit of Neptune (at about 30 AU) to roughly 50 AU from the Sun. It is somewhat analogous to the asteroid belt but much larger and composed primarily of icy objects rather than rocky and metallic ones. Kuiper Belt objects (KBOs) — also called trans-Neptunian objects (TNOs) — are icy bodies left over from solar system formation that did not accrete into a planet because Neptune's gravity kept them too stirred up.

Short-period comets originate in the Kuiper Belt or in the related scattered disk — a region of highly eccentric and inclined orbits extending outward from the Kuiper Belt. Gravitational interactions with Neptune can nudge KBOs onto orbits that carry them into the inner solar system, where they become active comets.

Pluto and many similar objects are members of the Kuiper Belt. The New Horizons spacecraft, after its Pluto flyby, continued outward and in 2019 flew past Arrokoth, a Kuiper Belt object that turned out to be a contact binary — two lobes gently stuck together, preserved nearly unchanged since the earliest years of the solar system. Arrokoth's gentle, undisturbed shape provides direct evidence of the slow, low-energy accretion process that built up early solar system bodies.

Section 13.9

The Oort Cloud at an Introductory Level

Beyond the Kuiper Belt lies a hypothetical, much larger structure called the Oort Cloud — a vast, roughly spherical shell of small icy bodies surrounding the solar system at distances from roughly 2,000 AU to perhaps 100,000 AU, nearly halfway to the nearest star.

The Oort Cloud has never been directly observed. Its existence is inferred from the orbits of long-period comets. When a long-period comet arrives in the inner solar system for the first time, its orbit can be traced back to a source at enormous distances, and the directions from which these comets arrive are essentially random — consistent with a spherical, not disk-shaped, reservoir.

Evidence for the Oort Cloud

Long-period comets arrive from all directions — not concentrated in the plane of the solar system like short-period comets. Their orbits, when traced backward, indicate sources at distances of tens of thousands of AU. A spherical shell of distant icy objects is the simplest model that explains these observations.

The Oort Cloud is thought to contain icy objects ejected to the outer solar system early in its history by gravitational interactions with the giant planets. Passing stars, giant molecular clouds, or the gravitational field of the Milky Way can nudge these distant objects onto orbits that send them falling toward the inner solar system — where we observe them as new, brilliant long-period comets.

Science from Inference

The Oort Cloud is an excellent example of scientific inference. Astronomers cannot see it directly because its objects are too distant and too faint. But the behavior of long-period comets — their orbital periods, directions of approach, and orbital shapes — cannot be explained without a vast, distant, spherical reservoir. The Oort Cloud is inferred from evidence in the same way scientists infer the existence of Earth's interior or subatomic particles: by reasoning carefully from effects that can be observed.

Section 13.10

Impacts and Planetary History

Perhaps no feature of small bodies has more dramatically shaped the solar system than their tendency to collide with planets and moons. The history of every solid body in the solar system is, in part, a history of bombardment.

The Cratered Record and Surface Ages

The surfaces of the Moon, Mercury, and Mars are covered with impact craters — circular depressions formed when an asteroid or comet strikes a solid surface at high speed. On the Moon, the absence of erosion, volcanism, and plate tectonics means ancient craters are preserved for billions of years. Crater counting provides a method for estimating the relative ages of geological features: older surfaces have more craters; younger surfaces fewer. Calibrated against the actual ages of rocks returned by the Apollo missions, this technique allows scientists to determine the ages of surfaces across the solar system.

The Late Heavy Bombardment

Evidence from the Moon suggests that between roughly 4.1 and 3.8 billion years ago, the inner solar system experienced an intense period of impacts called the Late Heavy Bombardment. Many large impact basins on the Moon formed during this period. One influential model suggests it resulted from the giant planets migrating to their current orbital positions, scattering large numbers of small bodies into the inner solar system in the process.

The Chicxulub Impact and Mass Extinction

The most famous impact in Earth's recent geological history occurred about 66 million years ago, when an asteroid or comet approximately 10 kilometers across struck the Yucatán Peninsula in Mexico, creating the Chicxulub crater. The energy released was billions of times greater than any nuclear weapon ever tested. The impact triggered wildfires, ejected debris that rained back globally, and caused years of reduced sunlight as dust and soot blocked the Sun, disrupting photosynthesis. This event is closely associated with the mass extinction at the end of the Cretaceous period, which eliminated about three-quarters of all species on Earth — including all non-avian dinosaurs.

Constructive Impacts: Delivery of Water and Organics

Not all impacts are destructive in their net effect. Early in Earth's history, impacts by asteroids and comets may have delivered a significant portion of Earth's water and organic molecules. The source of Earth's water is still debated, but isotopic comparisons between Earth's water and the water found in different meteorite and comet types give scientists a way to test hypotheses about delivery.

Planetary Defense

In 2022, NASA's DART (Double Asteroid Redirection Test) mission successfully demonstrated that a spacecraft could alter the orbit of a small asteroid by deliberately crashing into it. The target, Dimorphos (a small moon of the larger asteroid Didymos), had its orbital period changed by about 33 minutes — confirming that kinetic impactor technology could, in principle, deflect a hazardous asteroid if detected far enough in advance. Tracking near-Earth asteroids large enough to cause regional or global damage is an ongoing effort in planetary science.

Key Concepts — Impacts in Planetary History

Older surfaces → more craters → older age estimate. Calibrated by Apollo sample radiometric ages.

Late Heavy Bombardment: ~4.1–3.8 billion years ago. May be linked to giant planet migration.

Chicxulub impact (~66 million years ago): linked to end-Cretaceous mass extinction. Evidence: global iridium layer, shocked quartz, Chicxulub crater itself.

DART (2022): proved asteroid orbit can be changed by spacecraft impact. Planetary defense is feasible in principle.

  • Sec 13.6Comets are icy-dusty bodies that develop a coma and two distinct tails when near the Sun. The dust tail curves away from the comet's path; the ion tail always points directly away from the Sun. Short-period comets come from the Kuiper Belt; long-period comets from the Oort Cloud.
  • Sec 13.7Dwarf planets orbit the Sun, are roughly spherical, but have not cleared their orbital neighborhoods. Pluto's 2006 reclassification reflects the discovery that it is one of many similar Kuiper Belt objects. New Horizons revealed Pluto's geological complexity.
  • Sec 13.8The Kuiper Belt extends from ~30 to 50 AU, contains icy trans-Neptunian objects including Pluto, and is the source of short-period comets. Arrokoth's contact binary shape provides direct evidence of gentle, low-energy accretion in the early solar system.
  • Sec 13.9The Oort Cloud is inferred — never directly seen — from the orbital properties of long-period comets, which arrive from all directions and can be traced to sources ~10,000–100,000 AU from the Sun. It marks the outermost boundary of the solar system.
  • Sec 13.10Impacts have shaped every solid surface in the solar system. Crater counting provides relative surface ages. The Late Heavy Bombardment reshaped the inner solar system ~3.9 billion years ago. The Chicxulub impact ~66 million years ago caused a mass extinction. The 2022 DART mission demonstrated that asteroid orbits can be altered by spacecraft impact.

Key Terms Review

asteroid
A rocky or metallic small body orbiting the Sun; found mostly in the asteroid belt between Mars and Jupiter.
asteroid belt
The region between Mars and Jupiter (2.2–3.2 AU) containing most of the solar system's known asteroids.
coma
The cloud of gas and dust surrounding a comet's nucleus as it approaches and warms near the Sun.
comet
An icy small body that develops a coma and tail when close enough to the Sun to sublimate surface ice.
contact binary
An object consisting of two lobes that came together through gentle accretion; observed in the Kuiper Belt object Arrokoth.
dwarf planet
A body that orbits the Sun, is roughly spherical, but has not cleared its orbital neighborhood of other objects.
Galilean moons
The four large moons of Jupiter discovered by Galileo: Io, Europa, Ganymede, and Callisto.
giant impact hypothesis
The model proposing Earth's Moon formed from debris ejected by a Mars-sized body colliding with early Earth.
Kuiper Belt
A disk-shaped region of icy small bodies beyond Neptune's orbit, extending ~30 to 50 AU; source of short-period comets.
Late Heavy Bombardment
A period ~4.1–3.8 billion years ago during which the inner solar system experienced elevated impact rates.
meteor
The streak of light produced when a meteoroid enters Earth's atmosphere and heats up through friction with air.
meteor shower
A display of many meteors appearing to radiate from a single point, caused by Earth passing through a comet's particle trail.
meteorite
A meteoroid that survives atmospheric entry and lands on Earth's surface.
meteoroid
A small particle or chunk of rock or metal in space; smaller than an asteroid.
moon
Any object in natural orbit around a planet or other body that is not the Sun; also called a natural satellite.
near-Earth asteroid
An asteroid whose orbit brings it close to or into Earth's orbital neighborhood; tracked for potential impact hazard.
nucleus (comet)
The solid, icy, rocky core of a comet; typically a few to tens of kilometers across.
Oort Cloud
A hypothetical vast, spherical shell of icy bodies at ~2,000–100,000 AU; inferred as the source of long-period comets.
orbital resonance
A gravitational interaction between two orbiting bodies whose orbital periods have a simple ratio, causing regular nudges that create gaps or structure in ring systems.
Roche limit
The distance from a planet inside which tidal forces prevent a solid body from holding itself together; most rings lie within or near it.
tidal heating
Internal heating of a moon caused by friction as the moon's interior is repeatedly flexed by the changing gravitational pull during an elliptical orbit.
Yarkovsky effect
A small force on a small body caused by uneven heat emission from sunlight, which can gradually change an asteroid's orbit over long timescales.

Error Analysis

EA1 — The Dense Asteroid Belt
"The asteroid belt is so dense with rocks that it would be impossible for a spacecraft to fly through safely."

Identify the error in this statement.

Correct: The asteroid belt occupies an enormous volume of space. On average, asteroids are separated by hundreds of thousands of kilometers. Multiple spacecraft — including Voyager and New Horizons — have traveled through it without incident. The belt looks dense in diagrams only because all objects are plotted at once in a small image.
EA2 — One Tail
"Comets only have one tail."

Identify the error and describe both tails correctly.

Correct: Active comets have two distinct tails. The dust tail (pushed by radiation pressure) is broad and curved, trailing away from the comet's path. The ion tail (swept by the solar wind) is narrow and straight, always pointing directly away from the Sun. They can point in quite different directions.
EA3 — Pluto's Size
"Pluto was reclassified because scientists discovered it was smaller than they thought."

Is this accurate? What was the actual reason?

Correct: Pluto's small size was known long before 2006. It was reclassified because astronomers discovered it shares its orbital neighborhood with thousands of similar Kuiper Belt objects. Unlike the eight planets, Pluto has not gravitationally dominated its orbital zone — the third criterion for planet classification.
EA4 — Meteorites from Comets
"Meteorites come from comets."

Evaluate this claim. What are most meteorites derived from?

Correct: Most meteorites are fragments of asteroids — rocky or metallic bodies that were broken apart by collisions in the asteroid belt. Some meteorites are fragments of the Moon or Mars. Comets produce meteors (tiny particles that burn up in the atmosphere) but their fragile, icy material rarely survives to reach Earth's surface as meteorites.
EA5 — The Unobservable Oort Cloud
"If we can't see the Oort Cloud, it probably doesn't exist."

Evaluate this reasoning. What is the actual evidence?

Correct: Many well-established scientific conclusions are based on inference rather than direct observation — including Earth's inner core, subatomic particles, and black holes. The Oort Cloud is inferred from the orbital properties of long-period comets, which arrive from all directions and have orbital histories pointing to a vast, distant, spherical source. This is strong scientific evidence even without direct imaging.

Common Misconceptions

Misconception 1 — The Dense Asteroid Belt

The asteroid belt is not densely packed. On average, asteroids are separated by hundreds of thousands of kilometers. Diagrams of the solar system plot all objects at once in a small space, creating a false impression of crowding. Multiple spacecraft have traveled through the belt without incident.

Misconception 2 — "Shooting Stars Are Stars"

Meteors are not stars. They are tiny particles — usually no larger than a grain of sand — burning up in Earth's upper atmosphere. The glow comes from heated air and vaporized material, not from anything happening in distant space.

Misconception 3 — Comets Are Pure Ice

Cometary nuclei contain a complex mixture of water ice, carbon dioxide ice, other frozen compounds, dust, rocky material, and organic molecules. The "dirty snowball" model is a useful starting point but should not suggest that comets are simply frozen water. Spacecraft images have revealed them to be complex, dark, rugged objects.

Misconception 4 — Meteorites Are Hot When They Land

Meteorites are typically cool or cold when they reach Earth's surface. Although a meteoroid heats intensely during atmospheric entry, the burning away of outer layers (ablation) carries most of that heat away. The meteorite's interior has not had time to warm significantly. Some meteorites recovered soon after landing have been reported as frosty or cool to the touch.

Misconception 5 — The Oort Cloud Is Like a Farther Asteroid Belt

The asteroid belt is a flat disk of rocky objects in the inner solar system. The Oort Cloud, if it exists as inferred, is a vast spherical shell at enormous distances, composed primarily of icy objects. The two regions differ in shape, composition, distance, origin, and the types of objects they contain.

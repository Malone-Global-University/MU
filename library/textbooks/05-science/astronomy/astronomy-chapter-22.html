Chapter 22 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

"The universe is under no obligation to make sense to you."

— Neil deGrasse Tyson

For most of human history, people assumed the universe was eternal and unchanging. The stars were fixed. The galaxies — on the rare occasions anyone thought about them at all — were simply part of a static, everlasting backdrop. Even Albert Einstein, when he developed his theory of general relativity in 1915, introduced an artificial mathematical term into his equations specifically to hold the universe still. He assumed it must be static, and he adjusted his equations to match that assumption.

He later called it the greatest mistake of his life.

In the 1920s, astronomers began accumulating evidence that galaxies were not simply sitting still. They were moving — and not randomly, in every direction, as you might expect from objects scattered through space. They were moving away. Almost all of them. And the farther away a galaxy was, the faster it appeared to be receding.

This chapter explains how astronomers discovered the expanding universe, what that expansion actually means, and how it connects to the history, structure, and possible future of the cosmos. The expansion of the universe is not a background fact to memorize — it is a living inference drawn from careful observation, distance measurement, and the interpretation of light.

Opening Question

If you looked out at hundreds of galaxies in every direction and found that nearly all of them had their light shifted toward the red end of the spectrum — and that the farther away they were, the larger the shift — what would you conclude? By the end of this chapter, you will understand exactly what Hubble concluded, and why it changed our picture of the universe forever.

Section 22.1

The Universe Beyond the Milky Way

One Island Among Many

For centuries, astronomers debated whether the faint, fuzzy patches of light visible in telescopes — called nebulae — were clouds of gas within the Milky Way or distant systems of stars far beyond it. The question was not settled until the 1920s.

The critical work was done by American astronomer Edwin Hubble. Using the 100-inch Hooker Telescope at Mount Wilson Observatory in California — the largest telescope in the world at the time — Hubble identified individual Cepheid variable stars inside the Andromeda Nebula.

Cepheid Variable Stars as Distance Tools

Cepheid variable stars pulsate in brightness with regular periods. Astronomer Henrietta Swan Leavitt discovered a precise relationship between a Cepheid's pulsation period and its intrinsic luminosity — a star that takes longer to complete one brightness cycle is genuinely more luminous than one with a shorter cycle.

The method: measure the period → infer the true luminosity → compare to apparent brightness → calculate distance.

When Hubble measured the Cepheids in Andromeda, he found they were far dimmer than their periods suggested — not because they were intrinsically faint, but because they were enormously far away. His calculation placed Andromeda at roughly 900,000 light-years from Earth (the modern measurement is about 2.5 million light-years). This was vastly beyond the size of the Milky Way. Andromeda was not a gas cloud inside our galaxy. It was a separate galaxy in its own right — an island universe, comparable in scale to our own Milky Way.

Subsequent observations confirmed that the universe contains not just two galaxies but hundreds of billions of them, distributed across observable space at distances ranging from hundreds of thousands to billions of light-years.

Why This Matters for Expansion

Once astronomers understood that galaxies were real, distinct structures spread across enormous distances, a new question became possible to ask: Are they moving? And if so, how? To answer that question, they turned to spectra.

Section 22.2

Galaxy Distances and Redshifts

Reading Motion from Light

Recall from Chapter 7 that the Doppler effect shifts the wavelengths of light emitted by a moving source. When a source moves toward an observer, its light is compressed to shorter wavelengths — shifted toward the blue end of the spectrum, called blueshift. When a source moves away, its light is stretched to longer wavelengths — shifted toward the red end of the spectrum, called redshift.

By examining the absorption lines in a galaxy's spectrum and comparing their positions to known laboratory wavelengths, astronomers can measure how much the light has been shifted. This gives them a recession velocity — the speed at which the galaxy appears to be moving away.

Spectral ShiftDirection of MotionWhat Astronomers Observe
RedshiftSource moving away from observerAbsorption lines shifted to longer wavelengths
BlueshiftSource moving toward observerAbsorption lines shifted to shorter wavelengths
No shiftNo motion along line of sightLines at laboratory (rest) wavelengths

What Early Measurements Showed

In the early 1910s and 1920s, astronomer Vesto Slipher at Lowell Observatory systematically measured the spectra of spiral nebulae. The results were puzzling: the vast majority showed redshifts, not blueshifts. Of the dozens of nebulae Slipher measured, roughly 90 percent were receding.

This was not what a static universe would predict. If galaxies were simply moving in random directions through space, you would expect roughly equal numbers of redshifts and blueshifts. Something more systematic was happening.

A Few Exceptions

A few nearby objects — including the Andromeda Galaxy — showed blueshift, indicating approach. These exceptions are real: nearby galaxies can have individual motions that override the general expansion pattern. Hubble's Law applies most reliably to distant galaxies. The more distant a galaxy, the more the expansion signal dominates over its individual motion.

Section 22.3

Hubble's Discovery

A Simple and Astonishing Pattern

Once Hubble established that the spiral nebulae were distant galaxies, and once Cepheid distances gave him reliable measurements for some of them, he was in a position to look for a pattern. Working with his colleague Milton Humason, Hubble compiled a dataset of galaxy distances and recession velocities.

He plotted recession velocity on the vertical axis against distance on the horizontal axis. The result, published in 1929, was a clear, positive relationship: the more distant a galaxy, the faster it was receding.

A galaxy twice as far away receded at roughly twice the speed. A galaxy ten times farther away receded at roughly ten times the speed. The recession velocity appeared to be proportional to distance.

The Core Finding

Recession velocity is proportional to distance. This relationship — now called Hubble's Law — was not predicted by any existing model of the universe. It was not what astronomers expected. It changed cosmology forever.

What This Pattern Means

At first, some astronomers tried to interpret the redshifts as ordinary Doppler motion — galaxies actually flying away through static space. But there was a problem. If galaxies were flying outward from some central point, then the Milky Way would have to occupy a special location at or near the center. There would be a preferred center of the universe.

This is deeply uncomfortable for scientific cosmology, which generally assumes that our location in the universe is not special. This assumption is called the cosmological principle: the universe looks roughly the same from any location, and no observer occupies a privileged position.

The resolution came from a different way of thinking about the data. Rather than interpreting the recession as galaxies moving through space, astronomers recognized that the pattern was exactly what you would expect if space itself is expanding.

Section 22.4

Hubble's Law at an Introductory Level

The Mathematical Relationship

Hubble's observation can be expressed as a simple equation:

Hubble's Law

v = H₀ × d

v = recession velocity of a galaxy (km/s)

d = distance to the galaxy (megaparsecs; 1 Mpc ≈ 3.26 million light-years)

H₀ = the Hubble constant (~67–73 km/s per megaparsec)

The Hubble constant describes the current rate of expansion: for every additional megaparsec of distance, a galaxy recedes approximately 70 km/s faster.

DistanceApproximate Recession Velocity (H₀ = 70)
10 Mpc700 km/s
100 Mpc7,000 km/s
1,000 Mpc70,000 km/s (~23% speed of light)

Using Hubble's Law as a Distance Indicator

Hubble's Law has a practical use: if you can measure a galaxy's redshift (which is relatively straightforward with a spectrum), you can estimate its distance. This is how astronomers measure the distances to very remote galaxies that are too far away for Cepheid variables or other direct methods.

Worked Example 22.4.1
Using Hubble's Law to find distance
  • ProblemA galaxy's spectrum shows its hydrogen absorption lines are shifted to longer wavelengths by 3 percent. This corresponds to a recession velocity of about 9,000 km/s. Using H₀ = 70 km/s/Mpc, find the galaxy's distance.
  • ThinkRearrange Hubble's Law to solve for distance: d = v / H₀.
  • Solve d = v / H₀ d = 9,000 km/s ÷ 70 km/s/Mpc d ≈ 129 Mpc
  • CheckConvert: 129 Mpc × 3.26 million ly/Mpc ≈ 420 million light-years. A large distance — reasonable for a galaxy bright enough to observe its spectrum. ✓
Answer: The galaxy is approximately 129 megaparsecs (about 420 million light-years) away.
Worked Example 22.4.2
Using Hubble's Law to find recession velocity
  • ProblemA galaxy cluster is measured at 500 Mpc. Using H₀ = 70 km/s/Mpc, what is its recession velocity?
  • ThinkApply Hubble's Law directly: v = H₀ × d.
  • Solve v = H₀ × d v = 70 × 500 v = 35,000 km/s
  • Check35,000 km/s is roughly 12% of the speed of light (300,000 km/s). Plausible for a moderately distant galaxy cluster. ✓
Answer: 35,000 km/s, approximately 12 percent of the speed of light.
The Hubble Tension

The precise value of H₀ is actively debated. Different measurement methods — using Cepheids and supernovae in the nearby universe, or using the cosmic microwave background from the early universe — yield values that disagree slightly but persistently. This discrepancy, called the Hubble tension, is one of the open problems of modern cosmology. The values cluster between roughly 67 and 73 km/s/Mpc.

Section 22.5

Expansion of Space

Rethinking What the Recession Means

Hubble's Law describes a precise pattern: recession velocity is proportional to distance. But what does this pattern actually mean physically?

The answer requires a shift in thinking. The recession of galaxies is not primarily galaxies moving through space the way a thrown ball moves through air. It is the expansion of space itself — the fabric of the universe is stretching, and galaxies are being carried apart like raisins in a rising loaf of bread.

The Raisin Bread Analogy

Imagine raisins distributed through bread dough before baking. As the bread rises, the dough expands in all directions. From any single raisin, every other raisin moves away — and raisins that were farther away to begin with recede faster, because there is more expanding dough between them.

This is exactly the pattern Hubble observed. No raisin is the center. Every raisin sees the same pattern. Similarly, no galaxy is the center of the expanding universe.

Cosmological Redshift

This reframing also changes how we interpret the redshift of distant galaxies. The light from a remote galaxy is not Doppler-shifted because the galaxy is flying away through space. It is stretched because the universe was smaller when the light was emitted and has expanded while the light was traveling. By the time the light reaches us, the wavelengths have been stretched — redshifted — in proportion to how much the universe has expanded since the light departed.

This is called cosmological redshift, and it is subtly different from ordinary Doppler redshift. For very distant galaxies — at high redshifts — the distinction becomes important for calculating distances accurately.

Recession Faster Than Light?

Because expansion is the stretching of space — not motion through space — recession velocities can in principle exceed the speed of light for very distant objects. This does not violate the rule that nothing can move through space faster than light. Space itself is not a physical object moving somewhere; it is the arena in which motion occurs, and that arena can expand at any rate. Objects cannot move through space faster than light; space itself has no such restriction.

Section 22.6

The Observable Universe

Why We Cannot See Everything

If the universe is expanding, and if light takes time to travel, then there is a fundamental limit to how far we can observe. We can only see objects from which light has had enough time to reach us since the universe began.

The universe is approximately 13.8 billion years old. Naively, you might think this means we can see out to 13.8 billion light-years in every direction. But because the universe has been expanding while that light was traveling, the actual radius of the observable universe is larger. The current estimate is roughly 46 billion light-years.

Why 46 Billion, Not 13.8 Billion?
Resolving the apparent paradox
  • PuzzleThe universe is 13.8 billion years old, but we can see objects 46 billion light-years away. How is this possible?
  • AnswerThe light from very distant objects left those objects when the universe was much smaller and when those objects were much closer to us. While the light traveled, the universe expanded. The objects were carried to their current positions by expansion. We are seeing them as they were long ago, but they are now much farther away.
  • ImplicationDistance in an expanding universe requires careful definition. The 46-billion-light-year radius is the current distance to objects whose ancient light is just now arriving.

Beyond the Observable Universe

The observable universe is not the same as the entire universe. There may be — almost certainly are — regions of space beyond our observable horizon, but light from those regions has not yet had time to reach us. We have no direct information about them. Cosmologists believe the universe extends far beyond the observable horizon, and may be infinite. We simply cannot know, because we are limited to what light can carry to us.

Section 22.7

Lookback Time and Cosmic History

Seeing the Past

Every time you observe a distant object, you are looking back in time. The light from an object 1 million light-years away left that object 1 million years ago. The light from an object 1 billion light-years away left 1 billion years ago. The light from the most distant detectable galaxies left when the universe was only a few hundred million years old.

This is both a limitation and an extraordinary opportunity. Astronomers cannot watch a single galaxy evolve over billions of years. But they can observe many galaxies at many different distances, and because distance corresponds to lookback time, they can effectively watch cosmic history unfold by studying galaxies across a range of redshifts.

Redshift RangeApproximate Lookback TimeWhat We See
z < 0.5 (nearby)Less than ~5 billion years agoSettled galaxies; organized spiral and elliptical structures; lower star formation rates
z ≈ 1–3 (moderate)Roughly 6–11 billion years agoPeak era of star formation; vigorous galaxy growth and interaction
z ≈ 6–10 (distant)Roughly 12–13 billion years agoEarliest galaxies; smaller, less structured; actively forming stars at high rates
Telescopes as Time Machines

The James Webb Space Telescope, launched in 2021, was designed specifically to observe galaxies at very high redshifts — seeing the universe as it was only a few hundred million years after the Big Bang. In this sense, larger, more powerful telescopes do not just see farther in space; they see farther back in time.

Section 22.8

What Expansion Does and Does Not Mean

The expansion of the universe is one of the most frequently misunderstood concepts in all of science. Several important clarifications deserve careful attention.

Misconception 1 — The Big Bang was an explosion into empty space
"The Big Bang was a giant explosion at a specific point in space, with matter flying outward into a pre-existing void."

Correction: The Big Bang was the beginning of space itself. The entire early universe — not just a central region — was hot and dense. There was no pre-existing empty space for it to expand into. The universe expanded from a hot, dense state everywhere simultaneously. (Chapter 23 develops this further.)

Misconception 2 — Galaxies are flying through space like shrapnel
"Galaxies are actually rocketing outward through space from a central point."

Correction: The recession of galaxies is primarily the stretching of space itself, not individual galaxies moving through static space. This distinction matters especially at large distances, where recession velocities can exceed the speed of light.

Misconception 3 — Expansion means everything is getting bigger
"Since the universe is expanding, atoms, planets, and galaxies are also gradually growing larger."

Correction: Ordinary matter — atoms, planets, stars, and galaxies — is held together by forces much stronger than the large-scale expansion. Gravity, electromagnetism, and nuclear forces keep structures intact. Only on the largest scales, across the voids between galaxy clusters, does expansion dominate. You are not expanding.

Misconception 4 — The Milky Way is at the center
"Because all galaxies are moving away from us, the Milky Way must be at the center of the universe."

Correction: As the raisin bread analogy shows, every raisin (galaxy) sees all others receding. No location is the center. The Milky Way is not special; observers in any galaxy would see the same pattern.

Misconception 5 — There is an edge we could reach
"The observable universe horizon is a physical edge or boundary you could fly toward."

Correction: The cosmic horizon is not a physical boundary. It is simply the limit of what light has had time to bring us. There is very likely more universe beyond the horizon; we just cannot observe it. There is no physical wall at the horizon.

Section 22.9

Evidence for an Expanding Universe

The conclusion that the universe is expanding rests not on a single observation but on multiple independent lines of evidence that converge on the same picture.

#Line of EvidenceWhat It Shows
1Galaxy redshifts and Hubble's LawRecession velocity proportional to distance; confirmed across hundreds of thousands of galaxies spanning billions of light-years
2Cosmic microwave background (CMB)Faint microwave radiation pervading all of space — the cooled remnant of the hot early universe; precisely predicted by the expanding universe model
3Abundance of light elementsThe expanding universe model predicts specific ratios of hydrogen, helium, and lithium formed in the early universe; measured abundances match closely
4Ages of oldest starsThe Hubble constant gives an estimated age for the universe (~13.8 billion years); independent measurements of oldest stars yield consistent ages
5Galaxy evolution with redshiftDistant (high-redshift) galaxies look systematically younger and less evolved than nearby galaxies — exactly as expansion and cosmic aging predict
A Robust and Well-Tested Model

No single piece of evidence would be sufficient to establish the expanding universe. But five or more independent lines of evidence — from different measurement techniques and different physical phenomena — all pointing to the same conclusion: that is the hallmark of a robust scientific model.

The expanding universe is not a speculation. It is one of the best-supported conclusions in all of science.

Key Terms Review

Hubble's Law
The observational relationship stating that recession velocity is proportional to distance: v = H₀ × d.
Hubble constant (H₀)
The proportionality constant in Hubble's Law, expressing the current rate of expansion. Approximately 67–73 km/s per megaparsec.
recession velocity
The speed at which a distant galaxy is moving away from us, inferred from the redshift of its light.
cosmological redshift
The stretching of light to longer wavelengths caused by the expansion of space between the emitting object and the observer; distinct from ordinary Doppler redshift.
Cepheid variable star
A star that pulsates in brightness with a regular period related to its intrinsic luminosity, making Cepheids useful as standard candles for measuring distances.
period-luminosity relationship
Henrietta Swan Leavitt's discovery that the pulsation period of a Cepheid variable is directly related to its intrinsic luminosity.
observable universe
The spherical region from which light has had time to reach Earth since the Big Bang; radius approximately 46 billion light-years.
cosmic horizon
The boundary of the observable universe; not a physical edge, but the limit of what light has had time to bring us.
lookback time
The time in the past at which the light we are observing left its source; equal to the travel time of the light.
cosmological principle
The assumption that the universe is roughly uniform and that no location is special or privileged.
Hubble tension
The discrepancy between different methods of measuring the Hubble constant; an active area of research in modern cosmology.
cosmic microwave background
The faint microwave radiation pervading all of space — the cooled remnant of the hot early universe; key evidence for the Big Bang model.

Real-World and Historical Applications

  1. Henrietta Swan Leavitt's discovery of the period-luminosity relationship was essential to Hubble's measurement of galaxy distances. Leavitt worked as a "human computer" at the Harvard College Observatory, a role often assigned to women who were paid less than the men who used their results. Research the Harvard Computers and write a paragraph connecting Leavitt's scientific contribution to the larger history of who does astronomy and who gets credit.
  2. Edwin Hubble used the Hooker Telescope at Mount Wilson Observatory. The site was chosen partly for its dark skies above Los Angeles. Today, light pollution from the metropolitan area makes Mount Wilson less productive for optical astronomy. What trade-offs do modern observatories face when choosing sites? What solutions have astronomers developed?
  3. The Hubble Space Telescope, launched in 1990, had a flawed primary mirror that required a servicing mission to correct. What does this episode illustrate about the relationship between scientific tools and scientific knowledge?
  4. The Hubble tension — the discrepancy between different measurements of H₀ — is one of the active frontiers of modern cosmology. Different teams using different methods get values that disagree slightly but persistently. What does this disagreement suggest about the current state of cosmological knowledge? Is a discrepancy between careful measurements always a problem, or can it sometimes be productive?

Looking Ahead
Chapter 23: The Big Bang, Cosmic History, and the Cosmic Microwave Background

Chapter 23 develops the full picture that expansion implies. If galaxies are receding and space is growing larger today, then in the past, galaxies were closer together and space was denser. Carried far enough back, the universe was once an almost unimaginably hot and dense state from which it has been expanding and cooling ever since.

Chapter 23 will explain what the Big Bang model actually says — and what it does not say. Students will learn about conditions in the early universe, the formation of the first atomic nuclei, the release of light in recombination, the discovery of the cosmic microwave background, the formation of the first stars and galaxies, and the cosmic timeline. The chapter will walk through this history with careful attention to evidence and appropriate scientific humility.

Chapter 20 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

For most of human history, the Milky Way was the universe. People knew the sky as a dome of stars, planets, and the faint band of our own galaxy stretching overhead. Even early telescopes did not immediately reveal what lay beyond. Scattered across the sky were hazy patches of light — some round, some elongated, some wound into spirals — that astronomers called nebulae, a Latin word simply meaning clouds.

For centuries, a question hung unanswered: were these clouds of gas and dust within our own galaxy, or were they entire systems of stars at enormous distances? The answer did not come until the 1920s, when astronomer Edwin Hubble measured the distance to one of these patches — the Andromeda Nebula — and showed it lay far beyond any star in the Milky Way. It was not a cloud within our galaxy. It was another galaxy entirely, containing hundreds of billions of stars at a distance of more than two million light-years.

That discovery changed astronomy permanently. The Milky Way was not the universe. It was one galaxy among hundreds of billions. This chapter introduces galaxies beyond the Milky Way: what they look like, how astronomers classify them, how far away they are, how they move and interact, and what the most energetic galaxies reveal about supermassive black holes.

Opening Question

When you look at a faint smudge of light in the night sky — even through a small telescope — you may be looking at a galaxy containing hundreds of billions of stars at a distance of millions of light-years. How do astronomers know the difference between a cloud of gas inside the Milky Way and an entire galaxy beyond it? How do they measure the distance to something so remote that no spacecraft could ever reach it? By the end of this chapter, you will understand the evidence and methods that answer these questions.

Section 20.1

Galaxies Beyond the Milky Way

A galaxy is a large, gravitationally bound system containing stars, gas, dust, dark matter, and often a central concentration at its core. Galaxies range from small irregular systems with a few hundred million stars to massive elliptical galaxies containing trillions. Most also contain vast amounts of dark matter, whose gravitational influence shapes the galaxy even though it emits no light.

The Milky Way is a large spiral galaxy, roughly 100,000 light-years across, containing several hundred billion stars. It is part of a small collection of galaxies called the Local Group. Beyond the Local Group, across billions of light-years of space, galaxies extend in every direction. The total number of galaxies in the observable universe is estimated in the hundreds of billions.

When astronomers look at distant galaxies, they are also looking back in time, because the light reaching us left those galaxies billions of years ago. Individual stars are too faint to detect at galactic distances. When you observe another galaxy, you see the combined light of hundreds of billions of stars merged into a single glowing patch. Galaxies are the fundamental units from which astronomers map the large-scale universe.

Lookback Time and Distance

Because light travels at a finite speed, seeing farther into space also means seeing farther back in time. A galaxy 500 million light-years away appears as it was 500 million years ago. Observing many galaxies at different distances lets astronomers piece together a history of how galaxies have changed over cosmic time.

Section 20.2

Spiral Galaxies

Spiral galaxies are among the most visually striking objects in astronomy. From a distance, a spiral galaxy appears as a flattened disk with sweeping arms curving outward from a central bulge. The Milky Way is a spiral galaxy, as is the Andromeda Galaxy — our nearest large galactic neighbor.

Structure of a Spiral Galaxy

Spiral ArmsRegions of slightly higher density within the disk; move through the disk like density wavesCompressed gas clouds, newly forming stars, hot young blue stars

Barred Spiral Galaxies

Many spiral galaxies — including the Milky Way — have a bar of stars stretching across the central bulge, with spiral arms beginning at the ends of the bar rather than at the center. These are called barred spiral galaxies and are denoted SB in the Hubble classification system. Bars form as gravitational instabilities in the disk and channel gas inward toward the center.

Hubble Classification of Spirals

Edwin Hubble developed a classification system for galaxies in the 1920s still used today, sometimes called the Hubble tuning fork diagram. Spiral galaxies are designated S (unbarred) or SB (barred), followed by a letter (a, b, or c) indicating arm tightness and relative bulge size. Sa galaxies have tightly wound arms and large bulges. Sc galaxies have loosely wound arms, smaller bulges, and more gas with active star formation.

The Andromeda Galaxy (M31), roughly 2.5 million light-years away, is a large barred spiral and the most distant object visible to the unaided eye from dark skies. It is currently approaching the Milky Way on a slow collision course and is expected to merge with it billions of years from now.

Common Misconception — Spiral Arms

Spiral arms are not permanent, solid structures rotating with the disk. They are density waves — regions where gas and stars are slightly more compressed, much like a traffic jam on a highway. Stars drift in and out of the arms over time. The arms appear bright and blue because compressed gas in the arms triggers intense new star formation.

Section 20.3

Elliptical Galaxies

Elliptical galaxies look very different from spirals. They have no disk, no spiral arms, and very little gas or dust. In shape they range from nearly spherical to elongated ovals, and their light has a warm, reddish-orange glow because they are dominated by old stars.

Elliptical galaxies contain very little cold gas, so they form very few new stars. Most of their stellar population is old — formed billions of years ago in the galaxy's early history. The stars do not orbit in a flat disk; instead they move in a wide variety of orbital orientations, giving the galaxy its rounded, extended shape.

Giant elliptical galaxies are the most massive galaxies in the universe, containing trillions of stars and spanning hundreds of thousands of light-years. They typically sit at the centers of large galaxy clusters and are thought to have grown by merging with many smaller galaxies over cosmic time.

Lenticular galaxies (S0) sit between spirals and ellipticals: they have a disk and bulge like a spiral but lack prominent arms and show little active star formation. They appear to be spirals that have lost most of their gas, perhaps stripped away during gravitational interactions.

Section 20.4

Irregular Galaxies

Not all galaxies have neat, ordered shapes. Irregular galaxies have no clear symmetry — no disk, no spiral arms, no elliptical form. They tend to be smaller, more chaotic, and often full of gas, dust, and active star formation. Their blue color reflects many hot, young, newly formed stars distributed throughout their disordered structure.

Irregular structure often results from gravitational interactions or collisions with other galaxies. When two galaxies pass close to each other, tidal forces distort their shapes and trigger bursts of star formation.

Case Study 20.4
The Magellanic Clouds
  • What they areThe Large Magellanic Cloud (LMC) and Small Magellanic Cloud (SMC) are two irregular satellite galaxies of the Milky Way, visible to the unaided eye from the Southern Hemisphere.
  • DistanceThe LMC is roughly 160,000 light-years away; the SMC roughly 200,000 light-years. Both are held in orbit by the Milky Way's gravity.
  • CharacterBoth are rich in gas and actively forming stars — good examples of gas-rich, star-forming irregular galaxies that have been gravitationally disturbed by a larger neighbor.
  • SN 1987AIn 1987, a supernova in the LMC — SN 1987A — was the first visible to the unaided eye in nearly four centuries. It gave astronomers a rare close-up view of a massive star's death and confirmed key predictions of stellar evolution theory.
The Magellanic Clouds are the nearest irregular galaxies and among the best-studied examples of satellite galaxy interactions.
Section 20.5

Galaxy Size, Mass, and Brightness

Galaxies vary enormously in size, mass, and luminosity. The most common galaxies in the universe are dwarf galaxies — small, faint systems containing a few hundred million to a few billion stars. Many are satellites of larger galaxies. Because they are faint, they are difficult to detect at large distances and may be systematically undercounted in galaxy surveys.

At the other extreme, giant elliptical galaxies at the centers of large galaxy clusters can contain trillions of stars and span a million light-years or more, having grown by absorbing smaller galaxies over billions of years.

A galaxy's visible mass — its stars and gas — is only part of its total mass. Galaxies are embedded in massive halos of dark matter extending far beyond their visible edges. The total mass including dark matter is typically many times the mass of the visible stars alone.

Luminosity vs. Apparent Brightness

A galaxy's luminosity is the total light it produces. Its apparent brightness is how bright it looks from Earth — which depends on both luminosity and distance. A nearby dim galaxy may appear brighter than a very luminous but distant one. Correcting for distance is essential when comparing galaxies, and requires the distance measurement methods described in Section 20.6.

Section 20.6

Measuring Galaxy Distances

Measuring the distances to galaxies is one of the most challenging and important tasks in astronomy. No single method works across all distances, so astronomers have built a cosmic distance ladder — a series of overlapping techniques, each one calibrated against the previous and extending farther into space.

RungMethodApproximate RangeKey Requirement
1ParallaxUp to a few thousand light-yearsNearby stars only; Earth's orbital baseline
2Cepheid variable starsUp to ~100 million light-yearsPeriod-luminosity relationship
3Type Ia supernovaeUp to billions of light-yearsKnown peak luminosity of supernova
4Redshift / Hubble's lawAcross the observable universeCalibrated from lower rungs

Standard Candles

A standard candle is an object whose true luminosity is known. Because apparent brightness decreases with the square of distance (the inverse-square law), measuring how bright a standard candle appears lets astronomers calculate how far away it must be.

Standard Candle 20.6.1
Cepheid Variable Stars
  • DiscoveryHenrietta Swan Leavitt (early 1900s) discovered that Cepheid stars — which pulsate in brightness with periods of days to weeks — obey a period-luminosity relationship: longer-period Cepheids are more luminous.
  • MethodMeasure the pulsation period → determine true luminosity from the relationship → compare true luminosity to apparent brightness → calculate distance using the inverse-square law.
  • Hubble's useEdwin Hubble used Cepheid stars in 1924 to measure the distance to the Andromeda Galaxy, demonstrating it lay far beyond the Milky Way and resolving the Great Debate.
  • LimitationCepheids become too faint to resolve individually beyond about 100 million light-years. Brighter standard candles — such as Type Ia supernovae — are needed for greater distances.
Cepheid variables are the most important standard candle for calibrating the cosmic distance ladder out to tens of millions of light-years.
Uncertainty in Distance Measurements

Each rung of the distance ladder introduces its own uncertainties, and errors can accumulate as measurements extend to greater distances. Calibrating each step carefully is an ongoing effort in observational astronomy. The ongoing tension between different measurements of the universe's expansion rate — called the Hubble tension — is partly rooted in these accumulated uncertainties.

Section 20.7

Galaxy Redshifts

When astronomers measure the spectrum of light from a distant galaxy, they almost always find that the spectral lines are shifted toward longer wavelengths — toward the red end of the spectrum. This is called cosmological redshift, and it is not caused by galaxies moving away the way a car moves down a road. It is caused by the expansion of space itself.

As light travels across billions of light-years, the expansion of space stretches its wavelength. The longer the journey, the more the light is stretched, and the larger the observed redshift. Redshift is therefore a measure of how much the universe has expanded since the light was emitted.

Redshift Formula

Redshift is denoted by the symbol z and calculated as:

z = (observed wavelength − emitted wavelength) / emitted wavelength

A redshift of z = 0 means no shift — the source is very close. A redshift of z = 1 means wavelengths have been stretched to twice their original value. The most distant galaxies observed have redshifts of z = 10 or higher, meaning their light was emitted when the universe was less than one billion years old.

Example 20.7.1
Calculating a Galaxy Redshift
  • ProblemA galaxy's spectrum shows the hydrogen H-alpha line at an observed wavelength of 820 nm. In the laboratory, H-alpha has a wavelength of 656 nm. Calculate the redshift z.
  • ThinkApply the redshift formula: z = (observed − emitted) / emitted.
  • Solve z = (820 − 656) / 656 z = 164 / 656 z ≈ 0.25
  • InterpretA redshift of z ≈ 0.25 means the wavelength has been stretched by 25%. This galaxy is at a substantial distance — billions of light-years away — and we are seeing it as it appeared hundreds of millions of years ago.
Answer: z ≈ 0.25. The galaxy is at a significant cosmological distance; the universe was smaller when this light was emitted.

Doppler Shift vs. Cosmological Redshift

Cosmological redshift is often confused with the Doppler effect you learned in Chapter 7. The Doppler effect describes how a wave's frequency or wavelength changes when the source and observer move through space relative to each other. Cosmological redshift is different: the galaxies themselves are not necessarily moving through space at extreme speeds. Instead, space between them is expanding, carrying the galaxies apart and stretching the light traveling between them.

A few nearby galaxies — including the Andromeda Galaxy — actually show blueshifts. Their own motion through space toward us is large enough to overpower the small cosmological redshift from their short distances. Over several billion years, Andromeda's approach will lead to a merger with the Milky Way.

Common Misconception — Redshift and Speed

It is tempting to interpret a galaxy's redshift as meaning that galaxy is "moving away from us through space at nearly the speed of light." For most galaxies, cosmological redshift is not primarily a Doppler effect from motion through space — it is the expansion of space itself stretching the light. The two effects are mathematically related but physically distinct. This distinction becomes important in cosmology.

Section 20.8

Galaxy Interactions and Mergers

Galaxies are not isolated objects drifting through empty space. They interact with neighboring galaxies through gravity, and over billions of years these interactions reshape galaxies profoundly.

Tidal Interactions

When two galaxies pass close to each other, each exerts a gravitational pull on the other. Because the near side of each galaxy is pulled more strongly than the far side, the galaxy stretches and distorts. This stretching creates tidal tails — long streamers of stars and gas pulled out from each galaxy into space. Some of the most dramatic images in astronomy show galaxy pairs in the act of mutual distortion, with luminous tails stretching tens of thousands of light-years into space.

Galaxy Mergers

Over long timescales, galaxies that pass close enough eventually fall together and merge. Because stars in galaxies are separated by vast distances, two galaxies merging is not like two solid objects colliding. The stars pass through and around each other, following new orbits determined by the combined gravitational field. The process takes hundreds of millions to billions of years.

During a merger, tremendous amounts of gas are driven toward the center of the combined system. This triggers intense bursts of star formation — astronomers call these starburst galaxies — as compressed gas collapses into new stars. Merging galaxies are often among the most actively star-forming objects in the universe.

The final product of most major mergers is an elliptical galaxy. The organized rotation of a spiral galaxy is destroyed by the chaotic gravitational interactions during the merger. Stars end up moving in many directions, producing the spheroidal shape of an elliptical. This is one reason giant elliptical galaxies at cluster centers are thought to have grown so large — by consuming many smaller galaxies over billions of years.

Case Study 20.8
The Milky Way–Andromeda Collision
  • Current situationAndromeda (M31) is currently moving toward the Milky Way at roughly 110 km/s and is approximately 2.5 million light-years away. Its blueshift (Chapter 7 connection) is evidence of this approach.
  • TimelineModels predict the first close encounter will occur in approximately 4–5 billion years. The two galaxies will pass through each other, distort each other with tidal forces, and eventually — after multiple passes — merge over several billion years more.
  • What to expectEnormous tidal tails of stars; intense bursts of star formation as gas is compressed; gradual loss of organized rotation in both galaxies; eventual formation of a large elliptical galaxy sometimes nicknamed "Milkomeda."
  • Stars and EarthIndividual stars are so sparsely distributed that direct stellar collisions are extremely unlikely. The Sun and Earth are not expected to be destroyed in the merger — though their orbital position within the galaxy will change dramatically.
The Milky Way and Andromeda merger is the best-studied predicted galaxy collision — a future example of the process that has shaped many of the elliptical galaxies observed in the universe today.
Minor Mergers and Galactic Cannibalism

More common than equal-sized mergers are minor mergers, in which a large galaxy gradually absorbs a much smaller satellite galaxy. The Milky Way has absorbed several smaller galaxies in the past — the Sagittarius Dwarf Galaxy is currently being pulled apart by the Milky Way's gravity and will eventually be fully merged. The Large and Small Magellanic Clouds may eventually follow.

Section 20.9

Active Galactic Nuclei and Quasars

Most galaxies, including the Milky Way, have a supermassive black hole at their center. Most of the time, these black holes are relatively quiet — not actively consuming large amounts of material. In some galaxies, however, the central black hole is actively consuming material at a very high rate. Gas and dust fall inward, forming a hot, rotating accretion disk. As material spirals in, it releases enormous amounts of energy — far more than any nuclear process in stars. The region around an actively feeding black hole is called an active galactic nucleus, or AGN.

Why AGN Are So Luminous

The efficiency of converting mass to energy near a black hole's accretion disk is remarkably high — far greater than nuclear fusion in stars. Even a modest rate of mass consumption by a supermassive black hole can outshine the combined light of hundreds of billions of stars. Some AGN are so luminous that their light completely overwhelms the galaxy surrounding them.

The energy source is gravitational potential energy converted to radiation as matter falls inward — not nuclear fusion. This is why AGN can sustain luminosities impossible for any collection of stars.

TypeDescriptionDistinguishing Feature
Seyfert GalaxiesGalaxies with bright, compact nuclei; the host galaxy is otherwise recognizableSpiral or elliptical host visible around the AGN

QuasarsThe most luminous AGN; observed at great distances; host galaxy often difficult to detectPoint-like appearance with enormous redshift; can outshine their host galaxy by factors of 100 or more

Quasars (quasi-stellar objects) were discovered in the 1960s when astronomers noticed point-like radio sources that, on measurement of their spectra, had enormous redshifts indicating they were billions of light-years away. To be visible at such distances, they had to be intrinsically far more luminous than any known star or galaxy. Astronomers now understand quasars as the most energetically active AGN: supermassive black holes in distant galaxies consuming material at very high rates.

Because quasars are so distant, we observe them as they existed when the universe was young. Quasars were far more common in the early universe — a sign that many early galaxies had supermassive black holes in rapid growth. Today, those same black holes still exist at galaxy centers, but most have consumed the available gas and dust and are now much less active. The Milky Way's own central black hole, Sagittarius A*, was likely a quasar billions of years ago.

Key Relationship 20.9
Black Holes and Their Host Galaxies
  • ObservationAstronomers have found a striking relationship: the mass of a galaxy's central supermassive black hole correlates tightly with the mass of the galaxy's central bulge. More massive bulges host more massive black holes.
  • ImplicationThis suggests that the growth of the black hole and the growth of the galaxy are connected — that a galaxy and its central black hole co-evolve over cosmic time, even though the black hole is tiny compared to the galaxy as a whole.
  • MechanismAGN feedback — energy released by the accreting black hole — may heat surrounding gas, suppressing star formation and regulating the galaxy's growth. This feedback loop ties the fates of the black hole and galaxy together.
The co-evolution of black holes and galaxies is one of the most active research areas in modern astronomy, with implications for understanding galaxy formation across cosmic history.
Section 20.10

Galaxies as Building Blocks of the Universe

Individual galaxies are not the largest structures in the universe. They are organized into larger groupings — clusters and superclusters — that in turn form the cosmic web. But the galaxy is the fundamental unit from which this larger structure is built.

Our Milky Way belongs to a modest collection of galaxies called the Local Group. The Local Group contains about 80 known members, dominated by three large galaxies: the Milky Way, the Andromeda Galaxy (M31), and the Triangulum Galaxy (M33). The remainder are mostly dwarf galaxies scattered throughout a region roughly 10 million light-years across.

Beyond the Local Group, galaxies are gathered into clusters containing hundreds to thousands of members bound together by gravity. The nearest large cluster is the Virgo Cluster, containing more than 1,000 galaxies roughly 50 million light-years away. The Local Group, the Virgo Cluster, and many nearby clusters are all part of a larger supercluster called the Laniakea Supercluster, a vast structure several hundred million light-years across. These large-scale structures are explored in Chapter 21.

By observing galaxies at a range of distances and therefore at a range of lookback times, astronomers can trace how galaxies have changed over cosmic history. Early galaxies were often more irregular, more actively star-forming, and more likely to host quasars. Over billions of years, galaxies merged, consumed gas, and built the diversity of forms seen today. The story of galaxies is the story of the universe assembling itself from the bottom up, with smaller structures forming first and gradually building into larger ones.

Connection to Chapter 21

Galaxy clusters contain far more mass than their visible galaxies alone can account for. The motions of galaxies within clusters, and the temperature of hot gas between them, require additional unseen mass — the missing mass problem. This is one of the strongest lines of evidence for dark matter, which is the central topic of Chapter 21.

Key Terms Review

active galactic nucleus
The central region of a galaxy where a supermassive black hole is actively accreting material and releasing enormous energy, often outshining the entire galaxy.
barred spiral galaxy
A spiral galaxy with a bar of stars across the central bulge; spiral arms begin at the bar's ends. The Milky Way is a barred spiral.
cosmic distance ladder
The series of overlapping distance-measurement techniques — parallax, Cepheid variables, Type Ia supernovae, Hubble's law — used to measure distances across the universe.
cosmological redshift
The stretching of light to longer wavelengths caused by the expansion of space during the light's journey from a distant source to the observer.
dwarf galaxy
A small, faint galaxy containing a few hundred million to a few billion stars; the most common type of galaxy in the universe.
elliptical galaxy
A smooth, rounded or elongated galaxy with little gas or dust, no spiral arms, and a stellar population dominated by old, reddish stars.
galaxy
A large, gravitationally bound system of stars, gas, dust, and dark matter, ranging from small dwarfs to giant ellipticals containing trillions of stars.
galaxy cluster
A gravitationally bound group of hundreds to thousands of galaxies; often contains extensive hot gas between galaxies visible in X-rays.
galaxy merger
The gradual gravitational combination of two or more galaxies into a single larger system; often triggers intense star formation and results in an elliptical galaxy.
irregular galaxy
A galaxy with no regular shape; typically rich in gas and actively forming stars; often the result of gravitational interactions with other galaxies.
lenticular galaxy
A galaxy (classified S0) with a disk and bulge like a spiral but lacking prominent arms and containing little gas or active star formation.
Local Group
The small cluster of roughly 80 galaxies that includes the Milky Way, the Andromeda Galaxy, the Triangulum Galaxy, and many dwarf satellites.
lookback time
The amount of time in the past when light now reaching us was emitted; observing distant objects means seeing them as they were in the past.
quasar
An extremely luminous active galactic nucleus powered by rapid accretion onto a supermassive black hole; most common in the early universe at high redshifts.
spiral galaxy
A galaxy with a central bulge, flat disk, and curving spiral arms rich in gas, dust, and young stars. The Milky Way and Andromeda are spiral galaxies.
standard candle
An astronomical object whose true luminosity is known, allowing its distance to be determined by comparing true luminosity to apparent brightness.
supermassive black hole
A black hole at the center of a galaxy with a mass from millions to billions of times the mass of the Sun; present in most large galaxies.
tidal tail
A long streamer of stars and gas pulled from a galaxy by gravitational forces during a close encounter with another galaxy.

Error Analysis

Identify and correct the error in each statement.

Error 1
"Elliptical galaxies are old spirals — spiral galaxies gradually lose their arms and disk as they age, becoming elliptical over time."

What is wrong with this statement? What process does actually transform spiral galaxies into ellipticals?

Error 2
"The redshift of a distant galaxy means it is moving away from us through space at nearly the speed of light."

Identify the physical distinction this statement misses. What is actually causing the redshift?

Error 3
"Quasars are a special type of star in our galaxy that were especially bright in the past."

What are quasars actually? What evidence shows they are not stars in the Milky Way?

Error 4
"The Milky Way cannot be a spiral galaxy because spiral galaxies have visible arms from a distance and we cannot see our own galaxy from outside."

What evidence do astronomers use to establish that the Milky Way is a spiral galaxy, even though we cannot observe it from the outside?

Error 5
"Because the Andromeda Galaxy is approaching us, the universe must be contracting rather than expanding."

Why is Andromeda approaching while the universe is still expanding? What determines whether a nearby galaxy shows a blueshift?

Looking Ahead
Chapter 21: Galaxy Clusters, Dark Matter, and Large-Scale Structure

Chapter 20 showed that galaxies are organized into groups and clusters. Chapter 21 examines how these clusters are themselves organized into superclusters, filaments, and voids — the cosmic web. A central puzzle emerges: when astronomers measure the motions of galaxies within clusters, the gravitational forces required are far greater than the visible matter can explain. This is the missing mass problem, and it leads directly to one of the most important unsolved questions in physics — the nature of dark matter. Chapter 21 introduces galaxy rotation curves, gravitational lensing, and the large-scale structure of the universe on its grandest scales.
